# Anomalies in Particle Physics

> The currently accepted mathematical description of the fundamental constituents and interactions of matter is the Standard Model of particle physics. Its last missing particle, the famous Higgs boson, was observed at the Large Hadron Collider at CERN in 2012. However, it is clear that the Standard Model cannot be the ultimate theory of Nature, and e.g. cannot account for Dark Matter or non-vanishing neutrino masses (and does not include gravity). In fact, searches for physics beyond the SM have been intensified since the Higgs boson discovery. In this article, we review the hints for new physics, called ``anomalies'', obtained in particle physics experiments within the last years. We consider both direct high-energy searches for new resonances at the LHC and indirect low-energy precision experiments. These anomalies range from the nuclear scale (approximately the mass of the proton) to the electroweak scale (i.e. the mass of the Higgs boson) to the TeV scale (the highest scale directly accessible at the LHC), therefore spanning over four orders of magnitude. After discussing the experimental and theoretical status of the anomalies, we summarize possible explanations in terms of new particles and new interactions. In particular, new Higgs bosons and leptoquarks are promising candidates. Discovery prospects and implications for future colliders are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper reviews multiple experimental deviations from Standard Model predictions ("anomalies") that span over four orders of magnitude in energy scale, but emphasizes that while the combined global statistical significance of some anomalies reaches 5.1σ, the reliability of these significances depends on unresolved tensions between different theoretical predictions and on the treatment of systematic uncertainties that cannot be captured by statistical measures alone.

## Method

The authors apply three selection criteria for anomalies: a combined global statistical significance of at least 3σ after trial factors, experimental signatures involving multiple channels or independent experiments, and theoretical robustness against existing constraints. For low-energy precision observables, they employ an effective field theory approach assuming the new physics scale exceeds the experimental energy scale, while for direct searches they use simplified models that add single new fields with relevant couplings to the Standard Model.

## Key numbers

- Muon anomalous magnetic moment (a_μ): deviation from Standard Model prediction at 5.1σ significance, with a preferred positive shift of order 10^{-9}
- X17 anomaly in nuclear decays: statistical significance exceeds 6σ in decays of excited ^8Be, ^4He, and ^{12}C nuclei, with a particle mass of approximately 17 MeV
- MiniBooNE neutrino excess: 4.8σ significance in the neutrino energy range 200 < E_ν < 1250 MeV; combined analysis with MicroBooNE reduces significance to 4.3σ (exclusive) or 3.4σ (inclusive)
- Gallium anomaly: significance of about 5σ deficit in ^71Ge production from inverse beta decay
- CP violation in D decays: ΔA_CP = (-15.4 ± 2.9) × 10^{-4} measured by LHCb, compared to Standard Model estimate |ΔA_CP| < 3.6 × 10^{-4}
- Hadronic B and B_s decays: combined significance of 5.6σ for deviations in total branching ratios of B → D^{(*)}K and B_s → D^{(*)}_sπ
- Neutron lifetime puzzle: beam experiments give τ_n = 888.2 ± 2.0 s, bottle experiments give τ_n = 877.75 ± 0.28_stat +0.22/-0.16_syst s, a tension of approximately 4σ

## Context

This review extends the ongoing search for physics beyond the Standard Model that intensified after the Higgs boson discovery at the LHC in 2012, covering anomalies from the nuclear scale (proton mass) through the electroweak scale (Higgs mass) to the TeV scale. The paper systematically evaluates anomalies including the muon g-2 (Brookhaven E821 and Fermilab experiments), the X17 boson from Atomki, neutrino anomalies from LSND, MiniBooNE, and MicroBooNE, CKM unitarity tests from beta decays, and various B and D meson decay anomalies from LHCb and Belle. The authors identify leptoquarks, new Higgs bosons, Z' bosons, vector-like quarks and leptons, and di-quarks as promising candidates for explaining these anomalies, and note that the high-luminosity LHC and future colliders (FCC-ee, ILC, CEPC, CLIC) will be needed for comprehensive study of any confirmed new physics.
