# QCD Structure of Nuclear Interactions

> This dissertation investigated selected processes involving baryons and nuclei in hard scattering reactions. Through these processes, this work explored the constituent structure of baryons and the mechanisms through which the interactions between these constituents ultimately control the selected reactions. First, hard nucleon-nucleon elastic scattering was studied considering the quark exchange (QE) between the nucleons to be the dominant mechanism of interaction in the constituent picture. It was found that an angular asymmetry exhibited by proton-neutron (pn) elastic scattering data is explained within this framework if a quark-diquark picture dominates the nucleon's structure instead of a more traditional SU(6) model. The latter yields an asymmetry around 90 deg center of mass scattering with a sign opposite to what is experimentally observed. The second process is the hard breakup by a photon of a nucleon-nucleon system in light nuclei. Proton-proton (pp) and pn breakup in 3He, and double Delta-isobars production in deuteron breakup were analyzed in the hard rescattering model (HRM), which in conjunction with the QE mechanism provides a QCD description of the reaction. Cross sections for both channels in 3He photodisintegration were computed without the need of a fitting parameter. The results presented here for pp breakup show excellent agreement with recent experimental data. In double Delta-isobars production in deuteron breakup, HRM angular distributions for the two double Delta channels were compared to the pn channel and to each other. An important prediction from this study is that the Delta++ Delta- channel consistently dominates Delta+Delta0, which is in contrast with models that unlike the HRM consider a double Delta system in the initial state of the interaction. For such models both channels should have the same strength.

## Key claim
The angular asymmetry observed in proton-neutron (pn) hard elastic scattering data is explained by a quark-diquark picture of the nucleon's structure, while the traditional SU(6) three-quark model yields an asymmetry around 90 degrees center-of-mass scattering with a sign opposite to experimental observation. The quark exchange mechanism is considered the dominant interaction mechanism in the constituent picture.

## Method
Hard nucleon-nucleon elastic scattering was analyzed using the quark interchange model (QIM), where the scattering amplitude is constructed from all possible interchanges of quarks between the two nucleons while spectator quarks remain unchanged. For photodisintegration processes in light nuclei (3He and deuteron), the hard rescattering model (HRM) was employed in conjunction with the quark interchange mechanism, using baryonic wave functions expanded in quark-diquark states with a parameter ρ characterizing the relative amplitude of vector diquark to scalar diquark components. Cross sections for pp and pn breakup channels in 3He were computed without fitting parameters.

## Key numbers
- The parameter ρ characterizes the relative strength of the isotriplet diquark radial state to the isosinglet state in nucleon wave functions, with ρ=1 and ρ=0 corresponding to SU(6) and good diquark symmetries respectively
- For Δ-isobar wave functions, σ=0 and ρ=1 in the expansion
- The normalization factor for nucleon wave functions is N(ρ)=1/(3√(1+ρ²))
- The strength factor for ΔΔ channels relative to each other is N_ΔΔ=(1+ρ)²/(1+ρ²), which is independent of ρ
- For pn→Δ⁺⁺Δ⁻ scattering, the helicity amplitude φ₉=0, while for pn→Δ⁺Δ⁰, φ₉=(1/3)N_ΔΔ f(π-θ^N_cm)

## Context
This work extends the quark interchange model previously applied in references Sivers (1975), Farrar et al., and Brodsky et al. to nucleon-nucleon scattering and photodisintegration reactions. The hard rescattering model provides a QCD description of nuclear interactions that contrasts with models considering a ΔΔ system in the initial state, which predict equal strength for Δ⁺⁺Δ⁻ and Δ⁺Δ⁰ channels. The results enable further development of a QCD-based description of the atomic nucleus and provide testable predictions for exclusive reactions at high momentum transfer, particularly at Jefferson Lab facilities.
