# Self-consistent description of the halo nature of 31Ne with continuum and pairing correlations

> Using a Glauber model with our relativistic fully microscopic structure model input, we give a full description of the halo nature of 31Ne that includes a self-consistent use of pairing and continuum contributions that makes predictions consistent with reaction cross section measurements. Our predictions of total reaction and one-neutron removal cross sections of 31Ne on a Carbon target were significantly enhanced compared with those of neighboring Neon isotopes, agreeing well with measurements at 240 MeV/nucleon and consistent with a single neutron halo. Furthermore, our calculations of the inclusive longitudinal momentum distribution of the 30Ne and valence neutron residues from the 31Ne breakup reaction indicate a dilute density distribution in coordinate space, another halo signature.

# Self-consistent description of the halo nature of 31Ne with continuum and pairing correlations

## Key claim

The paper claims that a fully self-consistent combination of the relativistic RAB structure model (which includes pairing correlations and continuum contributions via the ACCC method) with a Glauber reaction model can reproduce all measured reaction-based halo signatures of 31Ne, whereas previous non-relativistic Skyrme Hartree-Fock (SHF) structure inputs to the same Glauber model failed to match the measured cross section increase from 30Ne to 31Ne. The hedge is that the calculated one-neutron removal cross sections and momentum distributions are qualitatively consistent but not in exact quantitative agreement with experimental data.

## Method

The authors used the RAB approach (relativistic mean field theory with BCS pairing approximation and analytical continuation of the coupling constant) to generate core density distributions and valence neutron wave functions for 26-31Ne isotopes. These structure inputs were then fed into a Glauber reaction model to calculate total reaction cross sections, one-neutron removal cross sections, and inclusive longitudinal momentum distributions for 31Ne and neighboring isotopes bombarding a 12C target at 240 MeV/nucleon and 230 MeV/nucleon. The core densities were fitted with a set of 5 Gaussian functions to enable analytical computation of the phase-shift functions.

## Key numbers

- Measured reaction cross section increase from 30Ne to 31Ne: 86 ± 39 mb/amu, a factor of 2.2 ± 1.1 larger than the average increase from 26Ne to 30Ne (39 ± 7 mb/amu)
- Predicted reaction cross section increase from 30Ne to 31Ne: a factor of 3 larger than the average increase from 26Ne to 30Ne
- Predicted one-neutron removal cross section for 31Ne: approximately a factor of 6 larger than for neighboring 29Ne and 27Ne
- Calculated full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the inclusive longitudinal momentum distribution for 30Ne residues from 31Ne breakup: approximately 76 MeV/c
- Measured FWHM of the 30Ne momentum distribution: approximately 120 ± 10 MeV/c
- Reaction cross section for 30Ne + 12C using the Glauber model: approximately 1453 mb
- Using the 30Ne density from Horiuchi et al. (2012) with the authors' valence nucleon wave function gave a calculated cross section of approximately 1435 mb, an increase of 5.7% over the SLy4 result and 3.3% over the SkM* result

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous RAB model prediction of a p-orbital single neutron halo structure for 31Ne (Zhang et al., Phys. Lett. B 730, 30 (2014)), which was based on radius increases and separation energies, by now calculating directly observable reaction quantities. It addresses the failure of previous non-relativistic approaches: Horiuchi et al. (Phys. Rev. C 86, 024614 (2012)) used Skyrme Hartree-Fock (SHF) structure input with SLy4 and SkM* forces in a Glauber model but underestimated the measured cross section increase at 31Ne, likely due to missing pairing and continuum contributions. Sumi et al. (Phys. Rev. C 85, 064613 (2012)) used Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics (AMD) with a Resonating Group Method (RGM) but still fell nearly 1σ below data. The present self-consistent approach enables future determination of halo structures in other exotic nuclei and can be extended to include deformed structures.
