# Production of dark-matter bound states in the early universe by three-body recombination

> The small-scale structure problems of the universe can be solved by self-interacting dark matter that becomes strongly interacting at low energy. A particularly predictive model for the self-interactions is resonant short-range interactions with an S-wave scattering length that is much larger than the range. The velocity dependence of the cross section in such a model provides an excellent fit to self-interaction cross sections inferred from dark-matter halos of galaxies and clusters of galaxies if the dark-matter mass is about 19 GeV and the scattering length is about 17 fm. Such a model makes definite predictions for the few-body physics of weakly bound clusters of the dark-matter particles. The formation of the two-body bound cluster is a bottleneck for the formation of larger bound clusters. We calculate the production of two-body bound clusters by three-body recombination in the early universe under the assumption that the dark matter particles are identical bosons, which is the most favorable case. If the dark-matter mass is 19 GeV and the scattering length is 17 fm, the fraction of dark matter in the form of two-body bound clusters can increase by as much as 4 orders of magnitude when the dark-matter temperature falls below the binding energy, but its present value remains less than 10^(-6). The present fraction can be increased to as large as 10^(-3) by relaxing the constraints from small-scale structure and decreasing the mass of the dark matter particle.

## Key claim

The paper calculates the production of two-body bound clusters (dark deuterons) by three-body recombination in the early universe, assuming dark matter particles are identical bosons with resonant short-range interactions; the fraction of dark matter in dark deuterons can increase by up to 4 orders of magnitude when the dark-matter temperature falls below the binding energy, but the present-day fraction remains less than 10^(-6) for the benchmark parameters that solve small-scale structure problems, and can reach at most 10^(-3) only if those constraints are relaxed.

## Method

The authors model dark matter as identical spin-0 bosons with a large positive S-wave scattering length a, using universal few-body physics in the zero-range limit. They determine the mass m_chi = 19 GeV and scattering length a = 17 fm from a fit to astrophysical self-interaction cross section data from dwarf galaxies, low-surface-brightness galaxies, and galaxy clusters. They then compute the three-body recombination rate constant for identical bosons using universal three-body physics, and solve the Boltzmann equation for the dark deuteron abundance during Hubble expansion in the early universe, accounting for both recombination and breakup reactions.

## Key numbers

* Dark matter mass: m_chi = 19^(+3)_(-2) GeV (best fit to small-scale structure data)
* Scattering length: a = (17 +/- 3) fm (best fit)
* Dark deuteron binding energy: E_2 = 7.1 keV (derived from m_chi and a)
* Present-day dark deuteron fraction for benchmark parameters: less than 10^(-6)
* Maximum possible present-day dark deuteron fraction (relaxed constraints): as large as 10^(-3)
* Enhancement factor when temperature falls below binding energy: up to 4 orders of magnitude
* Universal constant lambda_0 = 22.694 (ratio of successive Efimov trimer binding energies)
* Universal constant s_0 = pi/log(lambda_0) ≈ 1.00624
* Breakup cross section high-energy coefficient: c_2 ≈ 35

## Context

This work extends the universal few-body physics framework developed for atoms with large scattering lengths (Braaten, 2004) to dark matter bound-state formation in cosmology. It builds on the self-interacting dark matter solution to small-scale structure problems (Spergel, 1999; Tulin, 2017) and the specific resonant short-range interaction model proposed by Braaten, Laha, and Kang (2013-2015). The paper directly uses astrophysical data compiled by Kaplinghat, Tulin, and Yu (2015) to fix the dark matter parameters. The calculation shows that three-body recombination cannot produce a significant dark deuteron population under the benchmark parameters that solve small-scale structure problems, implying that larger dark nuclei also cannot form, which contradicts earlier assumptions in dark nucleosynthesis studies (Krnjaic, 2014; Detmold, 2014; Wise, 2014; Hardy, 2014) that relied on two-body radiative fusion reactions.
