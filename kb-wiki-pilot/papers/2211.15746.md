# Precision Studies of QCD in the Low Energy Domain of the EIC

> The manuscript focuses on the high impact science of the EIC with objective to identify a portion of the science program for QCD precision studies that requires or greatly benefits from high luminosity and low center-of-mass energies. The science topics include (1) Generalized Parton Distributions, 3D imagining and mechanical properties of the nucleon (2) mass and spin of the nucleon (3) Momentum dependence of the nucleon in semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering (4) Exotic meson spectroscopy (5) Science highlights of nuclei (6) Precision studies of Lattice QCD in the EIC era (7) Science of far-forward particle detection (8) Radiative effects and corrections (9) Artificial Intelligence (10) EIC interaction regions for high impact science program with discovery potential. This paper documents the scientific basis for supporting such a program and helps to define the path toward the realization of the second EIC interaction region.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a second EIC interaction region optimized for high luminosity at low to medium center-of-mass energies (20 to 60 GeV) is essential for precision QCD studies, particularly for 3D imaging of nucleons and measurement of mechanical properties, but this program requires instantaneous luminosity from 0.5×10³⁴ cm⁻²s⁻¹ up to 1.0×10³⁴ cm⁻²s⁻¹ which is only achievable in the center-of-mass range of 45 to 100 GeV, with significantly lower luminosity at 28 and 140 GeV.

## Method

The authors organized two workshops (March and July 2021) with over 400 international participants to identify high-impact science topics requiring high luminosity at low to medium center-of-mass energies, then formed technical working groups to develop this white paper. The document examines ten science topics including Generalized Parton Distributions (GPDs), gravitational form factors, transverse momentum dependent parton distributions (TMDs), exotic meson spectroscopy, nuclear structure, lattice QCD, far-forward detection, radiative corrections, artificial intelligence applications, and interaction region design. The analysis includes simulations of expected experimental reach at the EIC compared to existing fixed-target data from Jefferson Lab and HERA.

## Key numbers

- Center-of-mass energy range for the EIC: 20 GeV to 140 GeV, with the low-to-medium range of 20 to 60 GeV identified for precision QCD studies
- Instantaneous luminosity target: 0.5×10³⁴ cm⁻²s⁻¹ up to 1.0×10³⁴ cm⁻²s⁻¹ achievable at CM energies of 45 to 100 GeV
- Projected annual integrated luminosity: 100 fb⁻¹ based on 10⁷ seconds of operation per year
- Electron beam energy range: 2.5 GeV to 18 GeV; proton beam energy range: 41 GeV to 275 GeV
- Beam polarization: greater than 70% for both electron and proton beams
- Peak luminosity of L=10³⁴ cm⁻²s⁻¹ at CM energy of 105 GeV for the primary interaction region (IR6)
- Crossing angle: 25 mrad for the primary IR design
- 1160 colliding bunches in each beam for the primary IR design
- Strong luminosity drop from CM energy 44.7 GeV to 28.6 GeV due to increased beam-beam interactions when proton energy is reduced from 100 GeV to 41 GeV

## Context

This white paper extends the science case established in prior EIC documents (Accardi:2012qut, Proceedings:2020eah, AbdulKhalek:2021gbh) and the EIC Conceptual Design Report, specifically focusing on the need for a second interaction region at IP8 that complements the primary detector at IP6. The work builds on Jefferson Lab 12 GeV CEBAF results in the valence quark region and aims to connect to the sea quark and gluon dominated regions at medium and small x_B values (0.01 to 0.1) where chiral symmetry breaking effects are expected. The second IR design with a secondary focus approximately 50 m downstream enables unique forward detection capabilities for diffractive physics and nuclear target studies that go beyond the original EIC motivation.
