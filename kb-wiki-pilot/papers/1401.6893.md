# Analysis of a low-energy correction to the eikonal approximation

> Extensions of the eikonal approximation to low energy (20MeV/nucleon typically) are studied. The relation between the dynamical eikonal approximation (DEA) and the continuum-discretized coupled-channels method with the eikonal approximation (E-CDCC) is discussed. When Coulomb interaction is artificially turned off, DEA and E-CDCC are shown to give the same breakup cross section, within 3% error, of $^{15}$C on $^{208}$Pb at 20MeV/nucleon. When the Coulomb interaction is included, the difference is appreciable and none of these models agrees with full CDCC calculations. An empirical correction significantly reduces this difference. In addition, E-CDCC has a convergence problem. By including a quantum-mechanical correction to E-CDCC for lower partial waves between $^{15}$C and $^{208}$Pb, this problem is resolved and the result perfectly reproduces full CDCC calculations at a lower computational cost.

# Analysis of a low-energy correction to the eikonal approximation

## Key claim

When Coulomb interaction is artificially turned off, the dynamical eikonal approximation (DEA) and the continuum-discretized coupled-channels method with the eikonal approximation (E-CDCC) give the same breakup cross section within 3% error for 15C on 208Pb at 20 MeV/nucleon; however, when Coulomb interaction is included, neither model agrees with full CDCC calculations, though an empirical correction to the impact parameter significantly reduces this difference and a hybrid version of E-CDCC with a quantum-mechanical correction for lower partial waves perfectly reproduces full CDCC at lower computational cost.

## Method

The authors compared DEA and E-CDCC calculations for the breakup of the one-neutron halo nucleus 15C on a 208Pb target at 20 MeV/nucleon, using a three-body model with 14C and n as inert constituents and Woods-Saxon optical potentials for the nuclear interactions. They first performed calculations with the Coulomb interaction turned off to establish equivalence between the two eikonal methods, then included Coulomb interaction and applied an empirical shift to the impact parameter (b' = eta0/K0 + sqrt(eta0^2/K0^2 + b^2)) to correct for Coulomb deflection, and finally implemented a hybrid E-CDCC approach that uses full quantum-mechanical CDCC for low projectile-target partial waves (L < 500 hbar) and shifted E-CDCC for higher partial waves.

## Key numbers

- Breakup cross section of 15C on 208Pb at 20 MeV/nucleon: DEA and E-CDCC agree within 3% error when Coulomb interaction is turned off
- The semi-adiabatic approximation (K_i' + K_i)/(2K_0) ≈ 1 holds within 1.5% error at 20 MeV/nucleon
- E-CDCC model space without Coulomb: l_max = 10, k_max = 1.4 fm^-1, 35 bins per partial wave, 2311 coupled channels, z_max = b_max = 50 fm
- E-CDCC model space with Coulomb: l_max = 6, k_max = 0.84 fm^-1, z_max = 1000 fm, b_max = 150 fm, 589 coupled channels
- DEA with Coulomb: angular mesh up to N_theta x N_phi = 12 x 23 points, radial mesh up to 800 fm with 800 points, b_max = 300 fm, z_max = 800 fm
- Computational time for each b with E-CDCC is about 1/60 of that for each L with full CDCC
- Transition angular momentum L_C = 500 hbar chosen for the hybrid calculation

## Context

This work extends the comparison between DEA and CDCC performed by Capel et al. (2012), which showed that at 68 MeV/nucleon the two models agree well but at 20 MeV/nucleon DEA cannot reproduce CDCC results due to Coulomb deflection. The paper clarifies the formal relation between DEA and E-CDCC, showing they are equivalent when the semi-adiabatic approximation holds and Coulomb is absent. The empirical impact-parameter shift (Eq. 6) was suggested earlier by Broglia and Winther (1981) and Bertulani (2003). The hybrid E-CDCC approach, combining full CDCC for low partial waves with eikonal treatment for high partial waves, was previously developed by Ogata et al. (2003, 2006). This work enables accurate description of nuclear and Coulomb breakup processes at low energies using eikonal-based methods with minimal computational cost, which could be incorporated into other CDCC programs to improve computational efficiency.
