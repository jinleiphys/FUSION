# Electromagnetic Dissociation of 8B and the Rate of the 7Be(p,gamma)8B Reaction in the Sun

> In an effort to better determine the 7Be(p,gamma)8B reaction rate, we have performed inclusive and exclusive measurements of the Coulomb dissociation of 8B. The former was a study of longitudinal momentum distributions of 7Be fragments emitted in the Coulomb breakup of intermediate energy 8B beams on Pb and Ag targets. Analysis of these data yielded the E2 contribution to the breakup cross section. In the exclusive measurement, we determined the cross section for the Coulomb breakup of 8B on Pb at low relative energies in order to infer the astrophysical S factor for the 7Be(p,gamma)8B reaction. Interpreting the measurements with 1st-order perturbation theory, we obtained SE2/SE1 = 4.7 (+ 2.0,- 1.3) times 10^-4 at Erel = 0.6 MeV, and S17(0) = 17.8 (+ 1.4,- 1.2) eV b. Semiclassical 1st-order perturbation theory and fully quantum mechanical continuum-discretized coupled channels analyses yield nearly identical results for the E1 strength relevant to solar neutrino flux calculations, suggesting that theoretical reaction mechanism uncertainties need not limit the precision of Coulomb breakup determinations of the 7Be(p,gamma)8B S factor. A recommended value of S17(0) based on a weighted average of this and other measurements is presented.

## Key claim

The Coulomb dissociation of 8B can be used to infer the astrophysical S factor for the 7Be(p,gamma)8B reaction with precision comparable to direct radiative capture measurements, provided that the E2 contribution is properly accounted for and that theoretical reaction mechanism uncertainties are minimized through appropriate experimental conditions and analysis methods. The authors obtained S17(0) = 17.8 (+1.4, -1.2) eV b, but note that this result assumes the validity of 1st-order perturbation theory and a particular 8B structure model.

## Method

Inclusive measurements of longitudinal momentum distributions of 7Be fragments from Coulomb breakup of 44 and 81 MeV/nucleon 8B beams on Pb and Ag targets were performed using the S800 spectrometer at NSCL to determine the E2 contribution to the breakup cross section. Exclusive measurements of the Coulomb breakup of 83 MeV/nucleon 8B on a Pb target were conducted using multiwire drift chambers and a dipole magnet to reconstruct the 4-momenta of coincident 7Be and proton fragments, from which the relative energy spectrum was obtained. The data were interpreted using both 1st-order perturbation theory and continuum-discretized coupled channels (CDCC) calculations, with a 1.77 degree cut on the reconstructed 8B scattering angle and a 30 fm impact parameter cutoff to minimize nuclear effects and higher-order electromagnetic contributions.

## Key numbers

- S17(0) = 17.8 (+1.4, -1.2) eV b, inferred from the exclusive Coulomb breakup measurement on Pb at 83 MeV/nucleon
- SE2/SE1 = 4.7 (+2.0, -1.3) x 10^-4 at Erel = 0.6 MeV, determined from inclusive longitudinal momentum distribution measurements on Pb
- E2 matrix elements required scaling by factor 0.7 relative to the simple potential model of Esbensen and Bertsch to fit the observed asymmetry in longitudinal momentum distributions
- Relative energy resolution of 55 keV (1 sigma) at Erel = 100 keV in the exclusive measurement
- Geometric detection efficiency for 8B breakups with impact parameter b >= 30 fm was determined, falling off rapidly with increasing relative energy
- Correction for breakup yielding excited 7Be nuclei ranged from 1% at 200 keV to 9% at 2 MeV relative energy
- M1 contribution from the 0.64 MeV 1+ resonance represents a few percent of the measured cross section

## Context

This work extends previous Coulomb dissociation studies of 8B by Motobayashi et al., Kikuchi et al., and Iwasa et al., addressing the complications of multiple electromagnetic multipole contributions, nuclear absorption, and higher-order electromagnetic effects. The measurement provides an independent determination of S17(0) that is of comparable precision to direct radiative capture measurements by Kavanagh, Parker, Filippone, and others, which showed a large spread in absolute normalizations. The results enable more precise calculations of the 8B solar neutrino flux for interpretation of measurements at SNO and other neutrino detectors, with the authors noting that theoretical reaction mechanism uncertainties need not limit the precision of Coulomb breakup determinations of the S factor.
