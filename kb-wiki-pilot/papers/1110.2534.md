# Nuclear physics in the cosmos

> We observe photons and neutrinos from stars. Based on these observations, complemented by measurements of cosmic rays energies and composition, we have been able to constrain several models for the Big Bang and for stellar evolution. But that is not enough. We also need to help this effort with laboratory experiments. We are still far from being able to reproduce stellar environments in a terrestrial laboratory. But in many cases we can obtain accurate nuclear reaction rates needed for modeling primordial nucleosynthesis and hydrostatic burning in stars. The relevant reactions are difficult to measure directly in the laboratory at the small astrophysical energies. In recent years indirect reaction methods have been developed and applied to extract low-energy astrophysical S-factors. These methods require a combination of new experimental techniques and theoretical efforts, which are the subject of this short review.

## Key claim

Indirect reaction methods, combined with new experimental techniques and theoretical efforts, can extract low-energy astrophysical S-factors for nuclear reactions that are difficult to measure directly in the laboratory at the small astrophysical energies relevant for stellar modeling and primordial nucleosynthesis.

## Method

The paper reviews several indirect methods for determining low-energy nuclear reaction rates: the Trojan Horse method (THM) uses breakup reactions to induce reactions at vanishing relative energies, canceling the Coulomb barrier penetration effect; the Asymptotic Normalization Coefficient (ANC) technique extracts the asymptotic normalization coefficients from peripheral transfer reactions or elastic scattering to calculate direct capture cross sections; and the Resonating Group Method (RGM) or Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) coupled with No-Core-Shell-Model (NCSM) wavefunctions provides microscopic calculations of S-factors for light nuclei.

## Context

This review extends the long-standing effort to determine nuclear reaction rates for stellar modeling, addressing reactions such as $^{7}$Be(p,$\gamma$)$^{8}$B (which John Bahcall called the most important reaction in nuclear astrophysics) and $^{12}$C($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{16}$O (which determines whether supernova remnants become black holes or neutron stars). The paper discusses the laboratory screening problem where experimental fusion cross sections disagree with adiabatic theory by a factor of two or more, and describes how indirect methods like THM and ANC techniques can avoid the screening problem entirely. These methods enable the determination of reaction rates for the r-process and other nucleosynthesis pathways where direct measurements are impossible due to extremely low cross sections or short-lived nuclei.
