# Coupled-channels density-matrix approach to low-energy nuclear reaction dynamics

> Atomic nuclei are complex, quantum many-body systems whose structure manifests itself through intrinsic quantum states associated with different excitation modes or degrees of freedom. Collective modes (vibration and/or rotation) dominate at low energy (near the ground-state). The associated states are usually employed, within a truncated model space, as a basis in (coherent) coupled channels approaches to low-energy reaction dynamics. However, excluded states can be essential, and their effects on the open (nuclear) system dynamics are usually treated through complex potentials. Is this a complete description of open system dynamics? Does it include effects of quantum decoherence? Can decoherence be manifested in reaction observables? In this contribution, I discuss these issues and the main ideas of a coupled-channels density-matrix approach that makes it possible to quantify the role and importance of quantum decoherence in low-energy nuclear reaction dynamics. Topical applications, which refer to understanding the astrophysically important collision $^{12}$C + $^{12}$C and achieving a unified quantum dynamical description of relevant reaction processes of weakly-bound nuclei, are highlighted.

## Key claim

The standard coupled-channels approach to low-energy nuclear reactions, which uses complex potentials to represent excluded states, accounts only for absorption (dissipation) but not for quantum decoherence; the coupled-channels density-matrix (CCDM) approach is necessary to quantify whether decoherence effects are manifested in reaction observables, and the paper argues that decoherence should always be explicitly included when modelling low-energy reaction dynamics with a limited set of relevant degrees of freedom.

## Method

The CCDM approach is based on the Liouville-von Neumann master equation with Lindblad dissipative terms, projected onto a product-state basis where one part describes the internuclear separation (grid-basis) and another describes the selected intrinsic collective states of the interacting nuclei. This yields a finite set of coupled equations for the time-dependent density-matrix elements, with the initial density matrix determined when the two nuclei are well-separated in their ground states and a Gaussian wave-packet describes the radial motion. The formalism has been further developed using a coupled-angular-momentum state basis to investigate decoherence effects on asymptotic observables such as angular distributions of inelastic excitations.

## Context

This work extends the author's prior CCDM formalism (Diaz-Torres et al., Phys. Rev. C 78, 064604, 2008) and addresses systematic disagreements between high-precision fusion measurements and predictions of the coherent coupled-channels picture (Dasgupta et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 99, 192701, 2007; Hinde et al., Nucl. Phys. A 834, 117c, 2010). It contrasts with the optical model approach, which the author recently demonstrated cannot describe quantum decoherence (Diaz-Torres, Phys. Rev. C 81, 041603(R), 2010). The approach enables unified quantum dynamical descriptions of weakly-bound nuclei reaction processes (including no-capture breakup, incomplete and complete fusion) and investigations of molecular resonance structures in the $^{12}$C + $^{12}$C fusion excitation function, building on the author's two-center shell model calculations (Diaz-Torres, Phys. Rev. Lett. 101, 122501, 2008).
