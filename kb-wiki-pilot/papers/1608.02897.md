# The neutron within the deuteron as a surrogate for neutron-induced reactions

> We propose the use of neutron poisons in reactions induced by radioactive beams as a test of theoretical models aiming to relate neutron capture in nuclei with neutron surrogate reactions such as (d,p) reactions. We exploit the approximations necessary to obtain a direct relation between the two reactions; surrogate vs. neutron capture. We also show how this is intimately related to the momentum distribution of the neutron within the deuteron. The models we use are based on the theory of inclusive breakup reactions commonly employed in the treatment of incomplete fusion and surrogate method. Such theories were developed in the 80's by Ichimura, Autern and Vincent [Phys. Rev. C 32, 431 (1985)], Udagawa and Tamura [Phys. Rev. C 24, 1348 (1981)] and Hussein and McVoy [Nucl. Phys. A 445, 124 (1985)]. We use these theories to derive an expression for the proton yield in the reaction A(d,p)B. The capture reaction $n + A \rightarrow B$ is then extracted using reasonable approximations. By recalling an old method proposed by Serber [Phys. Rev. 80, 1098 (1950); Nature 166, 709 (1950)] we explain how the momentum distribution of neutrons within the deuteron will depend on the short-range dependence of the nucleon-nucleon force. The relevance of our work to nucleosynthesis in the rapid neutron capture process is emphasized.

## Key claim

The authors propose that neutron poisons (nuclei with extremely large neutron capture cross sections) can be used as benchmarks to test theoretical models relating (d,p) surrogate reactions to neutron capture reactions, but they emphasize that the direct proportionality between surrogate and free-neutron cross sections is "spoiled by the presence of distortion effects within the matrix elements" and that the momentum distribution of neutrons within the deuteron depends on the short-range part of the nucleon-nucleon force, which is not well understood.

## Method

The authors employ the Hussein-McVoy (HM) theory of inclusive breakup reactions, which is based on the distorted wave Born approximation (DWBA) and does not require solving an inhomogeneous equation for the source function, unlike the prior-form theory of Udagawa and Tamura or the post-form theory of Ichimura, Austern and Vincent. They derive an expression for the (d,p) cross section in terms of a modified neutron total reaction cross section that includes a deuteron-wavefunction-modified elastic S-matrix for the proton. They also introduce a "neutron surrogate factor" and a "momentum matching function" using the eikonal approximation to estimate how much of the deuteron wavefunction contains neutrons with thermal energies in the laboratory frame.

## Key numbers

- Thermal neutron capture cross section on $^{135}$Xe: $3 \times 10^{6}$ barns
- Thermal neutron capture cross section on $^{157}$Gd: $2.54 \times 10^{5}$ barns
- Thermal neutron capture cross section on $^{10}$B: $3.80 \times 10^{3}$ barns
- Thermal neutron capture cross section on $^{153}$Cd: $2 \times 10^{4}$ barns
- For a deuteron projectile with 50 MeV, the relative number of neutrons available for capture at thermal energies decreases by a factor $10^{-5}$ to $10^{-3}$ depending on the neutron wavefunction used
- The probability for doorway enhancement of neutron capture cross sections is given by $P(\eta_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\frac{1}{1 + \eta_{0}}$, where $\eta_0 \equiv \Gamma_{D, n}/\Gamma_{q, n}$

## Context

This work extends the inclusive breakup theories developed in the 1980s by Ichimura, Austern and Vincent [Phys. Rev. C 32, 431 (1985)], Udagawa and Tamura [Phys. Rev. C 24, 1348 (1981)], and Hussein and McVoy [Nucl. Phys. A 445, 124 (1985)], and recalls the Serber model [Phys. Rev. 80, 1098 (1950)] for momentum distributions. The authors propose that neutron poisons like $^{135}$Xe provide a perfect benchmarking opportunity for surrogate reaction methods, which could then be applied to determine neutron capture cross sections for the r-process in nuclear astrophysics. The work also connects to recent studies of short-range correlations in nuclei and their relation to the equation of state of neutron stars.
