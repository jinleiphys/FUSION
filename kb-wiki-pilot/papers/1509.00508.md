# Current Status of Nuclear Physics Research

> In this review we discuss the current status of research in nuclear physics which is being carried out in different centers in the World. For this purpose we supply a short account of the development in the area which evolved over the last 9 decades, since the discovery of the neutron. The evolution of the physics of the atomic nucleus went through many stages as more data become available. We briefly discuss models introduced to discern the physics behind the experimental discoveries, such as the shell model, the collective model, the statistical model, the interacting boson model, etc., some of these models may be seemingly in conflict with each other, but this was shown to be only apparent. The richness of the ideas and abundance of theoretical models attests to the important fact that the nucleus is a really singular system in the sense that it evolves from two-body bound states such as the deuteron, to few-body bound states, such as $^4$He, $^7$Li, $^9$Be etc. and up the ladder to heavier bound nuclei containing up to more than 200 nucleons. Clearly statistical mechanics does not work for such finite system, neither does other theories applicable to condensed matter systems. The richness of nuclear physics stems from these restrictions. New theories and models are presently being developed. Theories of the structure and reactions of neutron-rich and proton-rich nuclei, called exotic nuclei, halo nuclei, or Borromean nuclei deal with the wealth of experimental data available in the last 35 years. Further, nuclear astrophysics and stellar and Big Bang nucleosynthesis have become a more mature subject. Due to limited space, this review only covers a few selected topics, mainly those with which the authors have worked with.

## Key claim
This review argues that nuclear physics is uniquely difficult because the nucleus is a finite quantum system with too few particles for statistical mechanics yet too many for exact treatment, and that effective field theories (EFTs) now provide a controlled, improvable framework that resolves the apparent conflicts between earlier models like the shell model and the compound-nucleus model.

## Method
The authors survey the historical development of nuclear structure models (shell model, collective model, interacting boson model, optical model) and reaction theories (compound nucleus, direct reactions, Feshbach formalism). They then describe how modern approaches, lattice QCD for hadron properties, chiral effective field theory for nucleon-nucleon interactions, and mean-field methods (Hartree-Fock, BCS, Bogoliubov) for nuclear masses, are being used to unify these descriptions, with special emphasis on exotic nuclei near the drip lines and on nuclear astrophysics.

## Key numbers
- The triple-alpha resonance in carbon (the Hoyle state) lies at 7.65 MeV above the alpha-emission threshold.
- The stable odd-odd nucleus \(^{180m}\)Ta exists in an isomeric state at \(E^{\star} = 77.1\) keV with spin-parity \(9^{-}\); its abundance in natural ore is only 0.012% (vs. 99.988% for \(^{181}\)Ta).
- The hypernucleus \(^{7}_{\Lambda}\)Li shows a 20% reduction in the radius of \(^{6}\)Li when a \(\Lambda\) hyperon is added.
- The nucleus \(^{11}\)Li has an rms radius as large as that of \(^{208}\)Pb.

## Context
This review extends the tradition of comprehensive nuclear-physics surveys (e.g., Bohr and Mottelson, Feshbach, Wong) by emphasizing the modern synthesis of effective field theory with traditional models. It highlights the resurgence of Bethe's effective-range expansion (ERE) in cold-atom physics and its return to nuclear physics via EFT, and it points to ongoing work on time-dependent many-body techniques and laser-driven nuclear excitation (ELI, IZEST) as the next frontiers. The paper also situates the study of exotic and superheavy nuclei within the global facility projects (RIBRAS in Brazil, and planned facilities in Germany, the USA, China, Japan).
