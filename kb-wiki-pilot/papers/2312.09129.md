# A Vision for the Science of Rare Isotopes

> The field of nuclear science has considerably advanced since its beginning just over a century ago. Today, the science of rare isotopes is on the cusp of a new era with theoretical and computing advances complementing experimental capabilities at new facilities internationally. In this article we present a vision for the science of rare isotope beams (RIBs). We do not attempt to cover the full breadth of the field, but rather provide a perspective and address a selection of topics that reflect our own interests and expertise. We focus in particular on systems near the drip lines, where one often finds nuclei that are referred to as "exotic," and where the role of the "nuclear continuum" is only just starting to be explored. An important aspect of this article is the attempt to highlight the crucial connections between nuclear structure and nuclear reactions required to fully interpret and leverage the rich data to be collected in the next years at RIB facilities. Further, we connect the efforts in structure and reactions to key questions of nuclear astrophysics.

## Key claim

This paper presents a forward-looking perspective on the science of rare isotope beams, focusing specifically on systems near the drip lines where the role of the "nuclear continuum" is only just beginning to be explored, and does not attempt to cover the full breadth of the field but rather reflects the specific topics and expertise of the authors.

## Method

The authors review the current state and future directions of rare isotope science by examining the intersection of nuclear structure, nuclear reactions, and nuclear astrophysics, all connected through effective field theories (EFT) and approaches inspired by EFT concepts that bridge from Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) to emergent phenomena in exotic nuclei. They discuss experimental capabilities at facilities including FRIB, RIBF, SPIRAL2, FAIR, and ARIEL, alongside theoretical advances in ab initio computations, coupled clusters methods, in-medium similarity renormalization group (IMSRG) techniques, and open quantum system frameworks. The paper organizes the discussion around three regions of the nuclear chart: deeply bound nuclei, weakly bound systems near the drip lines, and unbound systems.

## Key numbers

- FRIB began operation in early 2022 and will ultimately provide access to thousands of previously unstudied isotopes
- The FRIB 400 MeV/u energy upgrade will triple or even quadruple the number of isotopic chains for which the neutron drip line is experimentally accessible, extending knowledge from Z=10 (Ne) up to Z=30-60
- Ab initio computations of 208Pb were published in 2022, while in 2015 the state of the art was limited to closed shells below N,Z=30
- The neutron drip line is confirmed experimentally up to Z=10 (neon), taking almost 50 years to reach oxygen (Z=8) and 20 more years to reach neon
- The proton drip line has been crossed up to Z=83 but only stringently established up to Z=13
- The largest isotope ever created, 294Og, has Z=118
- The number of experimentally accessible isotopes is currently estimated at about 3000 and is expected to roughly double with new RIB facilities
- The weakly bound region is reached when one-neutron or one-proton separation energy falls below approximately 1.0 MeV
- The most exotic form of few-body decay observed is the five-proton decay of 9N

## Context

This work extends the long history of rare isotope research that began with the 1951 production of short-lived krypton isotopes using a precursor to the ISOL technique, and builds upon the discoveries of shell evolution, islands of inversion, halo structures (discovered in 1985), and open quantum system descriptions of nuclei. The paper connects to specific prior developments including the USD family of shell-model interactions, the AV18 phenomenological potential, the coupled clusters approach reintroduced in nuclear physics, and the IMSRG method, while looking forward to what new facilities like FRIB will enable in the coming decade for nuclear structure, reactions, and astrophysics.
