# The Lagrange-mesh R-matrix method for inhomogenous equations

> The Lagrange-mesh $R$-matrix method is generalized to inhomogeneous equations. This method is numerically stable and efficient. It can be directly used for transfer reactions with the formalism discussed by Ascuitto and Glendenning [Phys. Rev. 181,1396 (1969)] and for inclusive breakup reactions modeled by Ichimura, Austern, and Vincent [Phys. Rev. C 32, 431 (1985)]. We first present a simple example to assess the method. Then the application to the $^{93}$Nb($d$,$pX$) non-elastic breakup is discussed.

## Key claim

The Lagrange-mesh R-matrix method is generalized to solve inhomogeneous Schrödinger equations, providing a numerically stable and efficient alternative to the Green's function and Numerov methods. The method is directly applicable to transfer reactions using the Ascuitto and Glendenning formalism and to inclusive breakup reactions modeled by Ichimura, Austern, and Vincent, with the caveat that the source term must be negligible at the channel radius for the R-matrix conditions to hold.

## Method

The authors extend the Lagrange-mesh R-matrix formalism to inhomogeneous equations by expanding the internal-region wave function over Lagrange functions based on Legendre polynomials, using Gauss-Legendre quadrature to simplify matrix element calculations. The Bloch operator ensures hermiticity of the Hamiltonian in the internal region and continuity of the derivative at the channel boundary. The S-matrix for the inhomogeneous equation is derived from the continuity condition at the channel radius, and the method is tested against the Green's function method with Gauss-Legendre quadrature and the Numerov method.

## Key numbers

- For the analytical example (mu = 929.4254 MeV, E_cm = 12.74 MeV, s-wave), the R-matrix method with N=20 mesh points and channel radius a=15 fm agrees well with the Numerov method, while the Green's function method requires at least N=80 points for comparable accuracy.
- The R-matrix method is approximately 6 times faster than the Green's function method in CPU time for solving large numbers of inhomogeneous equations.
- For the 93Nb(d,pX) nonelastic breakup at E_lab = 25.5 MeV with outgoing proton energy 14 MeV, a channel radius a >= 80 fm is needed for convergence at small angles.
- The minimum number of mesh points required for the R-matrix method is approximately N ≈ (3-5) * a * k_n / pi, giving N ≈ 48-80 for a = 80 fm and E_n = 8.7 MeV (pi/k_n ~ 5 fm).
- The R-matrix method uses 60 mesh points compared to 1600 points needed by the Numerov method (step size 0.05 fm) for the nonelastic breakup calculation.

## Context

This work extends the Lagrange-mesh R-matrix method, previously applied to homogeneous scattering problems by Descouvemont et al., to inhomogeneous equations relevant to transfer reactions (formalism of Ascuitto and Glendenning, Phys. Rev. 181, 1396 (1969)) and inclusive breakup reactions (model of Ichimura, Austern, and Vincent, Phys. Rev. C 32, 431 (1985)). The method enables efficient computation of nonelastic breakup cross sections in deuteron-induced reactions, where thousands of inhomogeneous equations must be solved, and can be extended to multi-channel problems and non-local interactions.
