# Two-step nuclear reactions: The Surrogate Method, the Trojan Horse Method and their common foundations

> In this Letter I argue that the Surrogate Method, used to extract the fast neutron capture cross section on actinide target nuclei, which has important practical application for the next generation of breeder reactors, and the Trojan Horse Method employed to extract reactions of importance to nuclear astrophysics, have a common foundation, the Inclusive Non-Elastic Breakup (INEB)Theory. Whereas the Surrogate Method relies on the premise that the extracted neutron cross section in a (d,p) reaction is predominantly a compound nucleus one, the Trojan Horse Method, assumes a predominantly direct process for the secondary reaction induced by the surrogate fragment. In general, both methods contain both direct and compound contributions, and I show how theses seemingly distinct methods are in fact the same but at different energies and different kinematic regions. The unifying theory is the rather well developed INEB theory.

# Two-step nuclear reactions: The Surrogate Method, the Trojan Horse Method and their common foundations

## Key claim
The Surrogate Method (SM) and the Trojan Horse Method (THM) share a common foundation in Inclusive Non-Elastic Breakup (INEB) theory, despite the SM assuming predominantly compound nucleus formation and the THM assuming predominantly direct processes; in general both methods contain both direct and compound contributions and are the same method applied at different energies and kinematic regions.

## Method
The author derives the INEB cross section for observing a spectator fragment b from a projectile a = b + x interacting with target A, showing it factorizes into a kinematic factor and a modified total reaction cross section for the x + A subsystem. For the SM, the neutron capture cross section is extracted from the (d,p) reaction by measuring the proton spectrum and using the INEB cross section to obtain the modified reaction cross section, then subtracting the calculated direct component. For the THM, the desired direct reaction x + A → y + B is extracted from the reaction a + A → b + y + B by approximating the off-shell nature of x through adding its binding energy and accounting for Fermi motion of the spectator b.

## Context
This work unifies two previously distinct experimental methods: the Surrogate Method developed by Escher, Dietrich, and collaborators (Phys. Rev. C 74, 054601, 2006; Rev. Mod. Phys. 84, 353, 2012) for reactor applications, and the Trojan Horse Method developed by Baur, Spitaleri, Tumino, and collaborators (Ann. Phys. 305, 228, 2003; Phys. Atomic Nuclei 74, 1725, 2011) for nuclear astrophysics. The unification builds on INEB theory developed by Udagawa and Tamura (1981), Ichimura, Austern, and Vincent (1985), Hussein and McVoy (1985), and Austern et al. (1987). This enables practitioners of each method to benefit from the other's experience and provides a complete theoretical framework for analyzing inclusive deuteron breakup reactions.
