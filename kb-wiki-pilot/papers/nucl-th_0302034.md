# New treatment of breakup continuum in the method of continuum discretized coupled channels

> A new method of pseudo-state discretization is proposed for the method of continuum discretized coupled channels (CDCC) to deal with three-body breakup processes. We propose real- and complex-range Gaussian bases for the pseudo-state wave functions, and show that they form in good approximation a complete set in the configuration space which is important for breakup processes. Continuous S-matrix elements are derived with the approximate completeness from discrete ones calculated by CDCC. Accuracy of the method is tested quantitatively for two realistic examples, d+$^{58}$Ni scattering at 80 MeV and $^{6}$Li+$^{40}$Ca scattering at 156 MeV with the satisfactory results. Possibility of application of the method to four-body breakup processes is also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that real- and complex-range Gaussian basis functions can form an approximate complete set in the configuration space important for breakup processes, enabling accurate interpolation of continuous S-matrix elements from discrete CDCC results. The method is claimed to work for both non-resonant and resonant breakup states, but the accuracy depends on the basis functions reproducing the oscillatory behavior of continuum wave functions up to \(kr \lesssim 35\) for the complex-range case versus \(kr \lesssim 20\) for the real-range case.

## Method

The authors diagonalize the projectile internal Hamiltonian \(H_{\rm bc}\) using either real-range Gaussian functions \(r^\ell \exp[-(r/a_j)^2]\) or complex-range Gaussian functions \(r^\ell \exp[-\eta_j r^2]\) with \(\eta_j = (1+ib)/a_j^2\) and \(b=\pi/2\), producing discrete pseudo-states \(\hat{\Phi}_{i\ell}(r)\) that approximate the continuum. They then solve the standard CDCC coupled differential equations using these pseudo-states as breakup channels, and reconstruct continuous S-matrix elements via the interpolation formula \(S^{(J)}_{\ell L}(k) \approx \sum_i f^{\rm PS}_{i\ell}(k) \hat{S}_{\gamma,\gamma_0}\), where \(f^{\rm PS}_{i\ell}(k) = \langle \Phi_\ell(k,r) | \hat{\Phi}_{i\ell}(r) \rangle\) is the overlap between the exact continuum state and the pseudo-state.

## Key numbers

- For \(d+^{58}\)Ni scattering at 80 MeV: model space \(\ell=0,2\), \(k_{\rm max}=1.3\) fm\(^{-1}\); convergence with 18 breakup channels for real-range Gaussian basis and 16 (s-wave) / 17 (d-wave) for complex-range Gaussian basis; Av method required 30 bins per partial wave
- For \(^{6}\)Li+\(^{40}\)Ca scattering at 156 MeV: model space \(k_{\rm max}=2.0\) fm\(^{-1}\), \(\ell_{\rm max}=2\); convergence with 21 s-wave and 22 d-wave breakup channels for PS methods; Av method required 30 bins for the resonant part alone plus 20 bins each for non-resonant parts
- Complex-range Gaussian basis parameters: \(a_1=1.0\) fm, \(a_n=20.0\) fm, \(2n=40\), \(b=\pi/2\); real-range Gaussian basis parameters: \(a_1=1.0\) fm, \(a_n=30.0\) fm, \(n=30\)
- For \(^{6}\)Li: d-wave resonance at \(\epsilon_{\rm res}=2.96\) MeV with \(\Gamma=0.62\) MeV; resonance peak reproduced by 8 (complex-range) or 12 (real-range) breakup channels versus 30 in the Av method

## Context

This work extends the pseudo-state discretization approach for CDCC, previously limited to virtual breakup processes in elastic scattering and \((d,p)\) reactions, to real breakup reactions by introducing interpolation formulas analogous to those used in the average method by Tostevin. The method improves upon earlier PS methods that required assuming a specific histogram form for the \(k\) distribution of cross sections, and upon the average and midpoint methods which require many bins for convergence, especially near resonances. The complex-range Gaussian basis is shown to produce discrete momentum distributions nearly as uniform as the average method bins, unlike the real-range Gaussian basis which concentrates states at low \(k\). The authors discuss potential extension to four-body breakup reactions (projectile consisting of three particles), where the Av method is impractical because exact three-body wave functions are unavailable.
