# Models of breakup: a final state interaction problem. In memory of Mahir Hussein

> In this paper we discuss the evolution of breakup models from fully quantum mechanical, such as the Ichimura-Austern-Vincent model to semiclassical, to eikonal approximations following the insight on the mechanism first proposed by Hussein and McVoy (HM) for the presently called stripping term. In particular we concentrate on, and stress that, the correct implementation of a quantum mechanical model of breakup requires the use of energy dependent interactions and the energy averaging procedure is a key point to understand the difference among models. On the other hand using fixed energy potentials is one of the steps towards the high energy eikonal limit first proposed by HM. However the intermediate semiclassical transfer to the continuum model (STC) of Bonaccorso and Brink does use an energy dependent nucleon-target optical potential, while fixing the core-target interaction at the incident energy. The relationship between these methods is clarified.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the correct implementation of a quantum mechanical model of breakup requires energy-dependent interactions and energy averaging, and that the differences between fully quantum mechanical (IAV), semiclassical (STC), and eikonal (HM) models can be understood through how they handle the final state interaction of the nucleon with the target, with the STC model serving as an intermediate approach that treats the core-target motion eikonally while keeping the nucleon-target interaction quantum mechanically.

## Method

The authors compare three breakup models: the fully quantum mechanical Ichimura-Austern-Vincent (IAV) model, the Hussein-McVoy (HM) eikonal approximation, and the semiclassical transfer to the continuum (STC) model of Bonaccorso and Brink. They derive the formal relationships between these approaches, showing how the HM model emerges from IAV by assuming eikonal wave functions and decoupling core and nucleon degrees of freedom, while the STC model uses WKB wave functions for distorted waves and Hankel functions for bound and continuum states, with the nucleon-target S-matrix calculated from an energy-dependent optical potential. Numerical calculations are performed for neutron and proton breakup from 14O and 16C on a 9Be target at 53 A.MeV.

## Key numbers

- Incident energy: 53 A.MeV for the reactions 9Be(14O,13O)X and 9Be(14O,13N)X
- The STC model uses the nucleon-target S-matrix calculated for each final nucleon energy according to the optical model with an energy-dependent optical potential including the spin-orbit term
- The eikonal model averages over the whole energy range spanned by the nucleon in the continuum, while STC averages over small intervals according to the optical potential that fits the nucleon-target free particle cross section
- The STC model includes the energy recoil effect of the nucleon via the definitions k1,2 = (εf - εi ∓ 1/2 mv²)/(ℏv) which can be interpreted as the z-components of the nucleon momentum in the initial (core) and final (target) reference frames

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of inclusive breakup reactions originally developed by Hussein and McVoy (1985) for the stripping term, and by Ichimura, Austern, and Vincent (IAV, 1985) for the full quantum mechanical treatment. It clarifies the relationship between these approaches and the semiclassical transfer to the continuum (STC) model, which was developed by Bonaccorso and Brink for heavy-ion reactions at intermediate to high energies. The paper enables a better understanding of when each approximation is valid, particularly for reactions with exotic nuclei where absolute breakup cross sections are used to extract spectroscopic factors, as done by Tostevin and Gade, and by Macfarlane.
