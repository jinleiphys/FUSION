# Random Matrices and Chaos in Nuclear Physics: Nuclear Reactions

> The application of random-matrix theory (RMT) to compound-nucleus (CN) reactions is reviewed. An introduction into the basic concepts of nuclear scattering theory is followed by a survey of phenomenological approaches to CN scattering. The implementation of a random-matrix approach into scattering theory leads to a statistical theory of CN reactions. Since RMT applies generically to chaotic quantum systems, that theory is, at the same time, a generic theory of quantum chaotic scattering. It uses a minimum of input parameters (average S-matrix and mean level spacing of the CN). Predictions of the theory are derived with the help of field-theoretical methods adapted from condensed-matter physics and compared with those of phenomenological approaches. Thorough tests of the theory are reviewed, as are applications in nuclear physics, with special attention given to violation of symmetries (isospin, parity) and time-reversal invariance.

## Key claim

Random-matrix theory (RMT) provides a generic statistical theory of compound-nucleus (CN) reactions that applies to all bombarding energies where resonance scattering is relevant, from isolated resonances (Gamma much less than d) to strongly overlapping resonances (Gamma much greater than d), using only the average S-matrix and mean level spacing as input parameters; the theory simultaneously serves as a generic theory of quantum chaotic scattering.

## Method

The authors combine nuclear scattering theory based on the shell model with a random-matrix approach, using a supersymmetric generating functional borrowed from condensed-matter physics to derive predictions for the statistical properties of the S-matrix. They compare these RMT-based predictions with earlier phenomenological approaches including the Hauser-Feshbach formula, the Weisskopf estimate for average resonance widths, and Ericson's theory of cross-section fluctuations. The theory is tested against experimental data on compound-nucleus reactions, with particular attention to symmetry violations in isospin, parity, and time-reversal invariance.

## Key numbers

- Average spacing d of resonances near neutron threshold is of order 10 eV, while typical spacings near the ground state are of order 100 keV
- The Weisskopf estimate gives Gamma = (d/2pi) * sum_c T_c, so Gamma/d is roughly given by the number of decay channels over 2pi
- At neutron threshold, where d is of order 10 eV and threshold spacing is several 100 keV, the number of resonances N within each energy window is approximately 10^4
- At 15 MeV above neutron threshold (Ericson regime), N is estimated to be one or two orders of magnitude smaller
- In the sd-shell example of 17O, the spacing between the lowest (d5/2) and highest (d3/2) single-particle state is 5.08 MeV

## Context

This review completes the authors' earlier work on random matrices and chaos in nuclear structure (Weidenmueller, 2009, referred to as Part I), extending the RMT framework to nuclear reactions. It builds on the foundational work of Wigner and Dyson from the 1950s, the Hauser-Feshbach formula (1952), Ericson's prediction of statistical fluctuations (1960), and the Bohigas-Giannoni-Schmit conjecture (1984) connecting quantum spectra of classically chaotic systems with RMT. The theory applies generically to chaotic scattering and has been extended to electron transport through disordered mesoscopic samples (Beenakker, 1997; Alhassid, 2000; Imry, 2002) and electromagnetic wave propagation through microwave cavities (Fyodorov, 2005).
