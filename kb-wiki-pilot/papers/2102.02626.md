# Unperturbed inverse kinematics nucleon knockout measurements with a 48 GeV/c carbon beam

> From superconductors to atomic nuclei, strongly-interacting many-body systems are ubiquitous in nature. Measuring the microscopic structure of such systems is a formidable challenge, often met by particle knockout scattering experiments. While such measurements are fundamental for mapping the structure of atomic nuclei, their interpretation is often challenged by quantum mechanical initial- and final-state interactions (ISI/FSI) of the incoming and scattered particles. Here we overcome this fundamental limitation by measuring the quasi-free scattering of 48 GeV/c 12C ions from hydrogen. The distribution of single protons is studied by detecting two protons at large angles in coincidence with an intact 11B nucleus. The 11B detection is shown to select the transparent part of the reaction and exclude the otherwise large ISI/FSI that would break the 11B apart. By further detecting residual 10B and 10Be nuclei, we also identified short-range correlated (SRC) nucleon-nucleon pairs, and provide direct experimental evidence for the separation of the pair wave-function from that of the residual many-body nuclear system. All measured reactions are well described by theoretical calculations that do not contain ISI/FSI distortions. Our results thus showcase a new ability to study the short-distance structure of short-lived radioactive atomic nuclei at the forthcoming FAIR and FRIB facilities. These studies will be pivotal for developing a ground-breaking microscopic understanding of the structure and properties of nuclei far from stability and the formation of visible matter in the universe.

## Key claim
By detecting a bound nuclear fragment (e.g., 11B) in coincidence with knockout protons in high-energy inverse kinematics (p,2p) scattering, the authors show that initial- and final-state interaction (ISI/FSI) distortions are suppressed, enabling access to unperturbed single-nucleon and short-range correlated (SRC) pair properties. The claim is conditional on the fragment detection selecting the "transparent" part of the reaction, and the authors note that this post-selection significantly reduces the measured event rate.

## Method
The experiment used a 48 GeV/c 12C beam from the Nuclotron accelerator incident on a stationary liquid-hydrogen target at the BM@N setup. Two protons from the (p,2p) reaction were detected at large angles (24°–37°) using a two-arm spectrometer, while nuclear fragments (11B, 10B, 10Be) were identified downstream using a dipole magnet, drift chambers, and beam counters measuring rigidity and energy deposition. Single-proton knockout was studied via 12C(p,2p)11B events, and SRC pair breakup was studied via 12C(p,2p)10B and 12C(p,2p)10Be events, with selection cuts on missing momentum (>350 MeV/c for SRC) and missing energy.

## Key numbers
- The 12C(p,2p)11B QE event yield accounts for (43.7 ± 2.4 (stat) +4.9/-5.8 (sys))% of the measured inclusive 12C(p,2p) QE yield.
- 12C(p,2p)10B and 12C(p,2p)10Be QE events account for an additional (7.8 ± 1.0 (stat) +1.3/-1.4 (sys))% and ≤ 2%, respectively.
- 23 12C(p,2p)10B and 2 12C(p,2p)10Be SRC events were measured after selection cuts.
- The GCF-calculated 10B/10Be yield ratio for SRC events is 12.1, consistent with the observed predominance of pn- over pp-SRC pairs.
- The extracted SRC pair center-of-mass momentum distribution has a Gaussian width of σ_c.m. = (156 ± 27) MeV/c.

## Context
This work extends the method of quasi-free proton-induced nucleon knockout in inverse kinematics, previously applied to study single-particle structure of exotic nuclei (e.g., Kobayashi et al., Panin et al., Atar et al.), by demonstrating that detecting the residual bound fragment suppresses ISI/FSI distortions that have historically challenged interpretation. It provides the first direct experimental evidence for the factorization of SRC pair wave-functions from the residual many-body nuclear system, a key feature of modern SRC models (e.g., Ciofi degli Atti, Cruz-Torres et al.), and enables future studies of short-lived radioactive nuclei at FAIR and FRIB.
