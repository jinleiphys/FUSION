# Indirect techniques in nuclear astrophysics. Asymptotic Normalization Coefficient and Trojan Horse

> Owing to the presence of the Coulomb barrier at astrophysically relevant kinetic energies it is very difficult, or sometimes impossible, to measure astrophysical reaction rates in the laboratory. That is why different indirect techniques are being used along with direct measurements. Here we address two important indirect techniques, the asymptotic normalization coefficient (ANC) and the Trojan Horse (TH) methods. We discuss the application of the ANC technique for calculation of the astrophysical processes in the presence of subthreshold bound states, in particular, two different mechanisms are discussed: direct capture to the subthreshold state and capture to the low-lying bound states through the subthreshold state, which plays the role of the subthreshold resonance. The ANC technique can also be used to determine the interference sign of the resonant and nonresonant (direct) terms of the reaction amplitude. The TH method is unique indirect technique allowing one to measure astrophysical rearrangement reactions down to astrophysically relevant energies. We explain why there is no Coulomb barrier in the sub-process amplitudes extracted from the TH reaction. The expressions for the TH amplitude for direct and resonant cases are presented.

## Key claim

The paper argues that indirect techniques, specifically the Asymptotic Normalization Coefficient (ANC) method and the Trojan Horse (TH) method, can overcome the difficulty of measuring astrophysical reaction rates at Gamow energies where the Coulomb barrier makes direct measurements nearly impossible, but the extracted sub-process amplitudes in the TH method are half-off-energy-shell, with the virtuality of the participant particle significantly compensated by the binding energy of the fragments in the projectile rather than by high-momentum components as originally suggested.

## Method

The ANC method determines the normalization of peripheral radiative capture cross sections by measuring the asymptotic normalization coefficient from peripheral transfer reactions (e.g., \(^{14}\mathrm{N}(^{3}\mathrm{He},d)^{15}\mathrm{O}\) at 26.3 MeV) and using it to fix the tail of the bound-state overlap function. The Trojan Horse method selects the quasi-free contribution of a three-body reaction performed above the Coulomb barrier (e.g., \(^{3}\mathrm{He}(^{6}\mathrm{Li},\alpha p)^{4}\mathrm{He}\)) to extract a two-body cross section at astrophysical energies free of Coulomb suppression, with the spectator fragment carrying away excess energy. The R-matrix approach is used to combine resonant and nonresonant amplitudes, with the ANC fixing the normalization of the channel radiative width and nonresonant amplitude simultaneously.

## Context

This work extends the ANC method originally suggested by Mukhamedzhanov and Timofeyuk (1990) and Xu et al. (1994) for peripheral radiative capture, and the Trojan Horse method proposed by Baur (1986) and advanced by the Catania group. The paper applies these techniques to key astrophysical reactions: \(^{14}\mathrm{N}(p,\gamma)^{15}\mathrm{O}\) (the slowest reaction in the CNO cycle, with the new lower S-factor increasing the age of main-sequence turnoff in globular clusters), \(^{11}\mathrm{C}(p,\gamma)^{12}\mathrm{N}\) (an alternative path to produce CNO nuclei bypassing the 3\(\alpha\) process), and \(^{13}\mathrm{N}(p,\gamma)^{14}\mathrm{O}\) (triggering the hot CNO cycle). The results enable more accurate reaction rates for stellar evolution models, particularly for very low-metallicity massive stars and novae, and demonstrate that the ANC can determine the interference sign between resonant and nonresonant amplitudes without direct experimental data.
