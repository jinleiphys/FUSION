# Extension of the ratio method to low energy

> Background: The ratio method has been proposed as a means to remove the reaction model dependence in the study of halo nuclei. Purpose: Originally, it was developed for higher energies but given the potential interest in applying the method at lower energy, in this work we explore its validity at 20 MeV/nucleon. Method: The ratio method takes the ratio of the breakup angular distribution and the summed angular distribution (which includes elastic, inelastic and breakup) and uses this observable to constrain the features of the original halo wave function. In this work we use the Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel method and the Coulomb-corrected Dynamical Eikonal Approximation for the study. Results: We study the reactions of 11Be on 12C, 40Ca and 208Pb at 20 MeV/nucleon. We compare the various theoretical descriptions and explore the dependence of our result on the core-target interaction. Conclusions: Our study demonstrates that the ratio method is valid at these lower beam energies.

## Key claim

The ratio method, previously validated at higher energies (around 70 MeV/nucleon), is shown to be valid at 20 MeV/nucleon for light and medium-mass targets, but the adiabatic approximation assumed within the Recoil Excitation and Breakup (REB) model breaks down for heavy targets (lead) at this lower energy, requiring a non-adiabatic correction.

## Method

The authors performed Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (CDCC) and Coulomb-corrected Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (CC-DEA) calculations for reactions of 11Be on 12C, 40Ca, and 208Pb targets at 20 MeV/nucleon. They computed elastic-scattering and breakup cross sections, then constructed the ratio of the breakup angular distribution to the summed angular distribution (including elastic, inelastic, and breakup contributions). This ratio was compared to the REB model prediction for the form factor |F_{E,0}(Q)|^2, which depends only on the projectile structure.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy: 20 MeV/nucleon for all reactions studied
- Targets studied: 12C, 40Ca, and 208Pb
- Breakup considered to a final core-neutron scattering state of E = 125 keV
- For carbon target, CDCC calculations included partial waves up to l_max = 6, Q_max = 6 multipoles, integration to R_max = 60 fm, matching at R_asym = 1000 fm, and total angular momentum J_max = 20000
- For carbon target, CC-DEA required N_theta x N_phi = 16 x 31 angular mesh points; for calcium, 14 x 27; for lead, 12 x 23
- Radial grid: 600 points extending to 600 fm for carbon and calcium; 800 points to 800 fm for lead
- Impact parameters: 0-100 fm for carbon, 0-160 fm for calcium and lead
- For carbon target, practical measurements estimated possible out to at least 40 degrees
- For lead target, CC-DEA predictions only possible out to 20 degrees due to numerical instabilities

## Context

This work extends the ratio method, originally proposed in Refs. [capel2011, capel2013] for reactions at around 70 MeV/nucleon, to lower beam energies appropriate for facilities such as SPIRAL 2 and FRIB. The study uses CDCC (benchmarked against Faddeev calculations in Ref. [upadhyay2012]) and CC-DEA (Coulomb correction from Ref. [fukui2014]) to test the method. The results enable experimental application of the ratio method at lower-energy facilities, with the finding that light targets (carbon) are preferred over heavy targets (lead) due to breakdown of the adiabatic approximation in Coulomb-dominated processes at low energy.
