# Nuclear Reactions in Evolving Stars

> This chapter will go through the important nuclear reactions in stellar evolution and explosions, passing through the individual stellar burning stages and also explosive burning conditions. To follow the changes in the composition of nuclear abundances requires the knowledge of the relevant nuclear reaction rates. For light nuclei (entering in early stellar burning stages) the resonance density is generally quite low and the reactions are determined by individual resonances, which are best obtained from experiments. For intermediate mass and heavy nuclei the level density is typically sufficient to apply statistical model approaches. For this reason, while we discuss all burning stages and explosive burning, focusing on the reactions of importance, we will for light nuclei refer to the chapters by M. Wiescher, deBoer & Reifarth (Experimental Nuclear Astrophysics) and P. Descouvement (Theoretical Studies of Low-Energy Nuclear Reactions), which display many examples, experimental methods utilized, and theoretical approaches how to predict nuclear reaction rates for light nuclei. For nuclei with sufficiently high level densities we discuss statistical model methods used in present predictions of nuclear reaction cross sections and thermonuclear rates across the nuclear chart, including also the application to nuclei far from stability and fission modes.

## Key claim

This chapter provides a comprehensive overview of nuclear reactions across all stellar burning stages and explosive conditions, but explicitly defers detailed treatment of light-nuclei reactions to other chapters (Wiescher, deBoer & Reifarth for experimental methods; Descouvement for theoretical approaches), focusing instead on statistical model methods applicable to intermediate-mass and heavy nuclei where level densities are sufficiently high.

## Method

The authors derive the formalism for calculating abundance changes via nuclear reaction networks, starting from the basic reaction-rate integral for Maxwell-Boltzmann distributions and extending to photodisintegration, electron capture, neutrino reactions, and decays. They then systematically survey each stellar burning stage (hydrogen burning via PP-cycles and CNO-cycles, helium burning, carbon burning, neon burning, oxygen burning, and silicon burning), identifying the specific reaction sequences and their temperature-density regimes, while noting that for light nuclei the resonance densities are low enough that individual resonances must be determined experimentally.

## Key numbers

- PP-cycle: first reaction $^1$H(p,$e^+\nu$)$^2$H has lifetime 7.9$\times 10^9$ years at $\rho=100$ g cm$^{-3}$, $T=1.5\times 10^7$ K
- Pep reaction: $^1$H(p$e^-,\nu$)$^2$H has lifetime 3.7$\times 10^{12}$ years under same conditions
- Triple-alpha reaction: $^4$He($2\alpha,\gamma$)$^{12}$C with Q=6.445 MeV
- $^{12}$C($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{16}$O with Q=7.161 MeV
- Solar CNO mass fraction: $X_{CNO}=9.33\times 10^{-3}$ (updated from earlier $1.4\times 10^{-2}$)
- CNO-cycles dominate over PP-cycles beyond $T=1.75\times 10^7$ K for solar abundances
- $^8$Be ground state decays on timescale 2.6$\times 10^{-16}$ s via alpha emission

## Context

This chapter serves as a foundational reference for nuclear astrophysics, extending the tradition of reaction-rate compilations by Fowler, Caughlan, and Zimmerman (1967) and subsequent updates by Angulo et al. (1999) and Adelberger et al. (2011). It connects to ongoing experimental efforts in underground laboratories (LUNA, CASPAR, JUNA) that measure cross sections down to stellar energies, and enables nucleosynthesis calculations across all burning stages by providing the theoretical framework for statistical model predictions of reaction rates for nuclei far from stability, including fission modes.
