# Quantum Dynamical Microscopic Approach to Stellar Carbon Burning

> The process of carbon burning is vital to understanding late stage stellar evolution of massive stars and the conditions of certain supernovae. Carbon burning is a complex problem, involving quantum tunnelling and nuclear molecular states. Quantum dynamical calculations of carbon burning are presented, combining the time-dependent wave-packet method and the density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock (DC-TDHF) approach. By limiting the contribution of triaxial molecular configurations to fusion, we demonstrate that the DC-TDHF interaction potential successfully explains the appearance of some resonant structures in the sub-barrier fusion cross-section. This result shows the critical role of nucleon-nucleon interactions in the 12C + 12C fusion resonances observed at astrophysical energies.

## Key claim

By limiting the contribution of triaxial molecular configurations to fusion through a shifted absorptive potential, the DC-TDHF interaction potential can explain some resonant structures in the sub-barrier fusion cross-section of 12C + 12C, but the model underpredicts the experimentally observed S*-factor at energies below 3.5 MeV due to the relatively small oblate deformation of 12C produced by the Skyrme force SkI3.

## Method

The authors combine the time-dependent wave-packet (TDWP) method with density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock (DC-TDHF) theory to compute ion-ion potentials for the 12C + 12C reaction. The TDWP method solves the time-dependent Schrodinger equation with a collective Hamiltonian in a five-dimensional space including internuclear distance and nuclear orientations, while the DC-TDHF method extracts microscopic interaction potentials at 55 distinct orientations using the Sky3D code with the SkI3 Skyrme force. An imaginary Woods-Saxon absorptive potential with an angular dependence term cos(theta1)cos(theta2) is used to simulate fusion, and a shifting parameter r_iw is introduced to control absorption from non-axially symmetric configurations.

## Key numbers

- The static HF calculation for 12C gave quadrupole deformation beta_2 = 0.219 and gamma = 60 degrees (oblate), compared to the experimental value of approximately beta_2 = -0.5
- The DC-TDHF simulations used a grid of 28 fm x 28 fm x 56 fm with 0.5 fm spacing, 15000 time steps of 0.05 fm/c, and initial fragment separation of 25 fm
- The TDWP simulations used a radial grid from 0.5 fm to 1000 fm with spacing 0.55 fm, a Gaussian wave-packet with spatial width sigma_0 = 15 fm centered at R_0 = 300 fm, and time steps of 10^-22 s
- The absorptive potential parameters were strength V_i = 50 fm, diffuseness a_i = 0.3 fm, with shifting parameter values r_iw = 0.0 fm, 0.5 fm, and 0.70 fm tested
- Total angular momentum values contributing to the low-energy region were J = 0, 2, 4, 6
- The numerical error associated with norm and energy conservation was approximately 10^-12
- The model does not predict resonant structures at energies below 3.5 MeV and does not calculate values below E_cm = 2 MeV due to numerical instability

## Context

This work extends previous TDWP calculations by Diaz-Torres (2018) that used macroscopic finite-range liquid-drop potentials with the experimental 12C quadrupole deformation, replacing those with microscopic DC-TDHF potentials that contain dynamical effects including particle exchange, neck formation, nuclear deformations and excitations. The results are compared with experimental data from Spillane (2007), Fruet (2020), and the Trojan Horse Method measurements by Tumino (2018) and Mukhamedzhanov (2019), as well as antisymmetrised molecular dynamics calculations by Taniguchi (2021, 2024). The framework can be extended to study compound nucleus resonances through giant resonance analysis to explore the origin of fusion resonances revealed by THM experiments around the Gamow energy peak.
