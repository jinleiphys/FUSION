# Continuum Effects and the Trojan Horse Mechanism in Halo Nuclei-Induced Reactions: Implications for Heavy Isotope Synthesis

> Nonelastic breakup (NEB) reactions induced by the halo nucleus $^{11}$Be on $^{64}$Zn at 28.7 MeV are investigated using the Ichimura-Austern-Vincent (IAV) model combined with the Continuum Discretized Coupled Channels (CDCC) method. NEB cross sections calculated with full CDCC wave functions (including continuum states), ground-state-only CDCC wave functions, and single-channel calculations are compared. The results indicate that continuum effects are negligible and that NEB cross sections are dominated by the ground-state contribution. This validates the use of simpler models like the distorted wave Born approximation for such reactions. Additionally, by varying the binding energy in a toy model, the feasibility of using halo nuclei in the Trojan Horse Method (THM) for synthesizing heavy isotopes is explored. It is demonstrated that THM significantly enhances sub-barrier fusion cross sections due to the weak binding of halo nuclei, offering a promising approach for the synthesis of new elements.

## Key claim
Continuum effects on nonelastic breakup (NEB) cross sections in the reaction of the halo nucleus $^{11}$Be on $^{64}$Zn at 28.7 MeV are negligible, with NEB dominated by the ground-state contribution; this validates simpler models like the distorted wave Born approximation. The Trojan Horse Method using halo nuclei significantly enhances sub-barrier fusion cross sections due to weak binding, but this conclusion is based on a toy model with manually adjusted binding energies rather than direct experimental measurement.

## Method
The Ichimura-Austern-Vincent (IAV) model was combined with the Continuum Discretized Coupled Channels (CDCC) method to calculate NEB cross sections for $^{64}$Zn($^{11}$Be, $^{10}$Be$X$) and $^{64}$Zn($^{11}$Be, $nX$) at 28.7 MeV laboratory energy. Three types of three-body scattering wave functions were compared: full CDCC wave functions including continuum states, CDCC wave functions using only the ground state component, and single-channel calculations considering only the projectile ground state. A toy model was employed by manually adjusting the binding energy between $^{10}$Be and $n$ inside $^{11}$Be from 0.504 MeV to 2.504 MeV to explore Trojan Horse Method effects.

## Key numbers
- Incident energy: 28.7 MeV in the laboratory frame for $^{11}$Be + $^{64}$Zn
- Actual binding energy of $^{11}$Be: $E_b = 0.504$ MeV; toy model adjusted to $E_b = 2.504$ MeV
- Integrating the NEB cross section over 5 to 10 MeV yields 26 mb
- Direct method at 7 MeV gives cross section on the order of $10^{-6}$ mb
- The indirect method (Trojan Horse) shows enhancement by at least three orders of magnitude below the Coulomb barrier
- Neutron-$^{10}$Be states included up to partial waves $\ell = 0-3$, covering excitation energies up to 10 MeV

## Context
This work extends previous studies of the $^{11}$Be + $^{64}$Zn reaction analyzed by Di Pietro et al. and others, which highlighted strong continuum coupling effects on elastic scattering and substantial inclusive breakup cross sections. The author's prior work (Jin et al., 2019) investigated the breakup-fusion mechanism using the three-body IAV model for deuterons and $^6$Li, finding a mixed nature with the one-step process dominant. The present results enable using simpler DWBA approaches for NEB predictions in similar halo nucleus systems and suggest that halo nuclei in the Trojan Horse Method could open pathways for synthesizing heavy and super-heavy isotopes, potentially including giant halo nuclei as proposed by Meng et al.
