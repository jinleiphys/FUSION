# Real-time calculations of many-body dynamics in quantum systems

> Real-time computation of time-dependent quantum mechanical problems are presented for nuclear many-body problems. Quantum tunneling in nuclear fusion at low energy is described using a time-dependent wave packet. A real-time method of calculating strength functions using the time-dependent Schroedinger equation is utilized to properly treat the continuum boundary condition. To go beyond the few-body models,we resort to the density-functional theory. The nuclear mean-field models are briefly reviewed to illustrate its foundation and necessity of state dependence in effective interactions. This state dependence is successfully taken into account by the density dependence, leading to the energy density functional. Photoabsorption cross sections in 238U are calculated with the real-time method for the time-dependent density-functional theory.

## Key claim

Real-time time-dependent methods can efficiently compute nuclear many-body dynamics (fusion, strength functions, and photoabsorption) without requiring explicit construction of continuum boundary conditions, but the computational advantage depends on the initial wave packet covering the energy region of interest; for halo nuclei, the presence of a weakly-bound neutron suppresses rather than enhances sub-barrier fusion cross sections, contradicting several earlier predictions.

## Method

The time-dependent Schroedinger equation is solved by recursive application of a fourth-order Taylor expansion of the small-time evolution operator \(U(\Delta t) \approx \sum_{n=0}^4 (-i \Delta t H)^n/n!\). For fusion, an imaginary potential \(iW(R)\) inside the Coulomb barrier absorbs flux that penetrates the barrier, and the fusion probability is extracted from the energy distribution of the initial and final wave packets via \(P_L(E) = [f_L(E,t_i)-f_L(E,t_f)]/f_L(E,t_i)\). For strength functions, the initial state is constructed by applying the \(E1\) operator to the ground state, its time evolution is computed, and the Fourier transform of the time-dependent overlap yields the strength distribution. For many-body systems, the time-dependent density-functional theory (TDDFT) is used with Skyrme functionals, extended to superfluid nuclei via the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov framework.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of nuclear matter: \(\rho_0 \approx 0.17\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- Binding energy per particle: \(B/A \approx 16\) MeV (infinite nuclear matter), \(B/A \approx 8\) MeV (finite nuclei)
- Fermi momentum from saturation density: \(k_F \approx 1.36\) fm\(^{-1}\)
- Fermi energy: \(T_F \approx 40\) MeV
- Effective mass from simple mean-field arguments: \(m^*/m \approx 0.4\) (disagrees with empirical values \(0.7 \lesssim m^*/m \lesssim 1\))
- For the two-body fusion model (\(^{10}\)Be + \(^{208}\)Pb, \(L=0\)): initial wave packet centroid at \(K^2/2\mu = 28\) MeV, time evolution up to \(t_f = 10\) MeV\(^{-1} \approx 2000\) fm/c
- For the three-body halo model (\(^{11}\)Be on \(^{209}\)Bi, \(J=0\)): initial wave packet centroid at \(R_0 = 25\) fm, neutron separation energy \(\epsilon_n = -0.6\) MeV
- For the \(E1\) strength calculation in \(^{11}\)Be: imaginary potential active at \(R > 50\) fm, comparison shown for \(2s\) orbital energies of \(-0.5\) MeV and \(-2\) MeV
- For Nd isotopes: intrinsic quadrupole moments calculated with SkM* functional reproduce experimental ground-state deformations deduced from \(B(E2; 2^+ \rightarrow 0^+)\) transition probabilities

## Context

This work extends the real-time wave-packet method previously applied to simple two-body fusion problems (Yabana 1997) to three-body systems with a valence neutron, and to strength function calculations in the continuum. It contradicts earlier coupled-channel predictions (Tolsma-Sierk 1991, Hussein et al. 1992, Takigawa et al. 1993, Hagino et al. 2000, Diaz-Torres 2002) that weakly-bound halo neutrons enhance sub-barrier fusion; the discrepancy is traced to insufficient partial-wave truncation (\(l_{\rm max} \leq 4\)) in those works, while convergence requires \(l_{\rm max} \sim 70\). The time-dependent density-functional approach enables calculation of photoabsorption cross sections in heavy deformed nuclei like \(^{238}\)U, and shape phase transitions in rare-earth Nd isotopes, using the same real-time methodology.
