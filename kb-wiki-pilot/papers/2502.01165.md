# Neutron-transfer induced breakup of the Borromean nucleus $^9$Be

> We address the problem of evaluating neutron-transfer induced breakup cross sections caused by the Borromean nucleus $^{9}$Be, using the reaction $^{197}$Au($^{9}$Be,$^{8}$Be)$^{198}$Au as a test case. This reaction was recently measured over a wide range of incident energies around the Coulomb barrier. To deal with the high density of $^{198}$Au states that can be potentially populated in this reaction, we employ the Ichimura, Austern, Vicent model, in which the spectrum of physical states for this system is replaced by the solutions of a complex n+$^{197}$Au potential, accounting effectively for the fragmentation of single-particle states into physical states. Furthermore, to account for the unbound nature of the emitted $^{8}$Be system, we employ a three-body model of $^{9}$Be. The calculated stripping cross sections are found to be in good agreement with existing data over a wide range of incident energies. The importance of taking into account the energy spread of the single-particle strength of the outgoing $^{8}$Be and the target-like residual nucleus is discussed.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the Ichimura-Austern-Vicent (IAV) model, combined with a three-body description of the Borromean projectile, can quantitatively reproduce measured neutron-transfer induced breakup cross sections for the reaction $^{197}$Au($^{9}$Be,$^{8}$Be)$^{198}$Au over a wide range of incident energies, but only when the energy spread of both the $^{8}$Be and $^{198}$Au single-particle strengths is properly accounted for; neglecting this spread leads to systematic overestimation of the data.

## Method

The authors employed the IAV inclusive breakup formalism, which replaces the dense spectrum of $^{198}$Au states with a complex neutron+$^{197}$Au optical potential whose imaginary part accounts for the fragmentation of single-particle strength into physical states. The $^{9}$Be projectile was described using a three-body ($\alpha+\alpha+n$) model within the hyperspherical harmonics expansion method, with $^{8}$Be continuum states generated via a pseudostate method using transformed harmonic oscillator (THO) basis functions. The double differential cross section for nonelastic breakup was evaluated for each $^{8}$Be pseudostate and summed, using the Sao Paulo potential for the heavy-ion interactions and the Koning-Delaroche global potential for the neutron-target interaction.

## Key numbers

- The $0^+$ ground-state resonance of $^{8}$Be at approximately 0.10 MeV above threshold exhausts about 38% of the total $^{9}$Be norm; the remaining $0^+$ states add up to 53% of the norm
- The $2^+$ states carry 44% of the norm, concentrated around 3 MeV (the nominal energy of the $2_1^+$ resonance in $^{9}$Be); $4^+$ states carry only 1%
- The variational Monte Carlo (VMC) spectroscopic factors used for rescaling are 0.595 for the $0^+$ state and 0.603 for the $2^+$ state
- The neutron separation energy in $^{198}$Au is $S_n = 6.5$ MeV, which defines the bound-state region for integration
- Calculations were performed for incident energies from $E_\mathrm{cm} = 20$ to 50 MeV, with detailed results shown at $E_\mathrm{cm} = 36$ MeV

## Context

This work extends the IAV model, originally developed for inclusive breakup reactions, to the case of a Borromean projectile ($^{9}$Be) where the ejected core ($^{8}$Be) is unbound. It directly addresses the reaction $^{197}$Au($^{9}$Be,$^{8}$Be)$^{198}$Au recently measured by Gollan et al. (2021) and Kaushik et al. (2021). Previous coupled-reaction-channel (CRC) calculations by Gollan et al. limited the $^{198}$Au states to below 700 keV excitation energy and could not describe the inclusive transfer data, while CRC calculations by Kaushik et al. assumed unit spectroscopic factors and omitted higher excited states, potentially producing misleading agreement. The present approach enables a tractable description without arbitrary truncation of the populated state spectrum, and demonstrates that the energy spread of both the $^{8}$Be and $^{198}$Au overlaps is essential for quantitative agreement with experiment.
