# Recent developments in the eikonal description of the breakup of exotic nuclei

> The study of exotic nuclear structures, such as halo nuclei, is usually performed through nuclear reactions. An accurate reaction model coupled to a realistic description of the projectile is needed to correctly interpret experimental data. In this contribution, we briefly summarise the assumptions made within the modelling of reactions involving halo nuclei. We describe briefly the Continuum-Discretised Coupled Channel method (CDCC) and the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA) in particular and present a comparison between them for the breakup of 15C on Pb at 68AMeV. We show the problem faced by the models based on the eikonal approximation at low energy and detail a correction that enables their extension down to lower beam energies. A new reaction observable is also presented. It consists of the ratio between angular distributions for two different processes, such as elastic scattering and breakup. This ratio is completely independent of the reaction mechanism and hence is more sensitive to the projectile structure than usual reaction observables, which makes it a very powerful tool to study exotic structures far from stability.

## Key claim

The eikonal approximation, while accurate at intermediate energies, fails at low beam energies due to its neglect of Coulomb deflection; a semiclassical correction that replaces the transverse impact parameter with the distance of closest approach on a classical Coulomb trajectory can reliably extend the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA) down to lower energies, as shown for the breakup of 15C on Pb at 20A MeV.

## Method

The authors compare the Continuum-Discretised Coupled Channel method (CDCC) and the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA) for the breakup of 15C on a Pb target at 68A MeV and 20A MeV. The DEA factorizes the three-body wave function into a plane wave and a smoothly varying factor, neglecting the second-order derivative of that factor, which leads to a simpler equation than the full coupled-channel set. A semiclassical correction is applied to the DEA at low energy by replacing the transverse component b of the projectile-target relative coordinate with the distance of closest approach b' on a classical Coulomb trajectory. A new observable is also introduced: the ratio of the breakup angular distribution to the summed angular distribution (elastic plus inelastic plus breakup), which is predicted by the Recoil Excitation and Breakup model (REB) to depend only on projectile wave functions.

## Key numbers

* Breakup of 15C on Pb at 68A MeV: CDCC and DEA produce nearly identical energy and angular distributions, with only a tiny shift in the angular distribution.
* Breakup of 15C on Pb at 20A MeV: DEA predicts a breakup cross section too large and too forward-focused compared to CDCC; the semiclassical correction brings the DEA into perfect agreement with CDCC.
* Uncertainty in the core-target optical potential V_cT can lead to up to a factor of 2 uncertainty in the breakup cross section.
* For 11Be on Pb at 69A MeV and on C at 67A MeV, the ratio of breakup to summed cross sections is nearly identical for both targets and in excellent agreement with the REB form factor.
* For 11Be on C at 20A MeV, the ratio method remains valid and results obtained with different V_cT potentials produce nearly identical ratios despite significantly different cross sections.

## Context

This work extends the comparison between CDCC (Kamimura et al., Prog. Theor. Phys. Suppl. 89, 1986; Yahiro et al., Prog. Theor. Exp. Phys. 2012) and DEA (Baye, Capel, Goldstein, Phys. Rev. Lett. 95, 2005; Goldstein, Baye, Capel, Phys. Rev. C 73, 2006) for halo nuclei reactions, building on earlier calculations by Capel, Esbensen, and Nunes (Phys. Rev. C 85, 2012). The semiclassical correction to the eikonal approximation (Fukui, Ogata, Capel, Phys. Rev. C 90, 2014) enables DEA calculations at lower energies where the standard eikonal approximation fails due to neglected Coulomb deflection (Glauber, 1959). The ratio method (Capel, Johnson, Nunes, Phys. Lett. B705, 2011; Phys. Rev. C 88, 2013) provides a reaction-mechanism-independent observable that is more sensitive to projectile structure than usual reaction observables, avoiding the large uncertainties from optical potentials (Capel, Goldstein, Baye, Phys. Rev. C 70, 2004). This enables more reliable extraction of nuclear structure information for exotic nuclei far from stability.
