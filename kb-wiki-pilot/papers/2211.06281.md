# Perspectives on few-body cluster structures in exotic nuclei

> It is a fascinating phenomenon in nuclear physics that states with a pronounced few-body structure can emerge from the complex dynamics of many nucleons. Such halo or cluster states often appear near the boundaries of nuclear stability. As such, they are an important part of the experimental program beginning at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB). A concerted effort of theory and experiment is necessary both to analyze experiments involving effective few-body states, as well as to constrain and refine theories of the nuclear force in light of new data from these experiments. As a contribution to exactly this effort, this paper compiles a collection of ``perspectives'' that emerged out of the Topical Program ``Few-body cluster structures in exotic nuclei and their role in FRIB experiments'' that was held at FRIB in August 2022 and brought together theorists and experimentalists working on this topic.

## Key claim
This paper compiles perspectives from the FRIB Topical Program "Few-body cluster structures in exotic nuclei and their role in FRIB experiments" (August 2022), arguing that a concerted theory-experiment effort is necessary to analyze experiments involving effective few-body states and to constrain theories of the nuclear force, but it does not present new experimental data or theoretical calculations itself.

## Method
The paper is a collection of perspective pieces written by subgroups of participants from a topical program held at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB) in August 2022. Each section discusses specific challenges and opportunities in the study of few-body cluster structures in exotic nuclei, covering topics such as the theory-experiment pipeline, emergence of few-body effects, deformation, and reaction theory. The perspectives synthesize existing knowledge and outline future research directions rather than reporting new results.

## Context
This paper extends discussions from the FRIB Theory Alliance Topical Program "Few-Body Clusters in Exotic Nuclei and Their Role in FRIB Experiments" held at FRIB/Michigan State University in August 2022. It builds on prior work in halo effective field theories (e.g., Bertulani et al. 2002, Ryberg et al. 2014, Ji et al. 2014, Hammer 2017, Capel 2018), continuum shell model approaches (e.g., Rotter 1991, Okolowicz et al. 2003, Volya et al. 2006), and ab initio methods for light nuclei (e.g., Navratil et al. 2016, Quaglioni et al. 2008-2009). The paper aims to guide future research at FRIB by identifying key theoretical and experimental challenges in understanding cluster and halo phenomena near the driplines.
