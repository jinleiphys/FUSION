# Inclusive breakup reaction of a two-cluster projectile on a two-fragment target: A genuine four-body problem

> We develop a four-body model for the inclusive breakup of two-fragment halo projectiles colliding with two-fragment targets. In the case of a short lived projectiles, such as halo nuclei, on a deuteron target, the model allows the extraction of the neutron capture cross section of such projectiles. We supply examples.

## Key claim

The paper develops a four-body model for inclusive breakup reactions where both the projectile and the target consist of two fragments, extending the Inclusive Nonelastic Breakup (INEB) theory beyond previous three-body and three-fragment projectile formulations; the model allows extraction of the medium-modified neutron capture cross section of short-lived radioactive nuclei from measured inclusive proton spectra, but the extracted cross section is not the free capture cross section as several factors come into play owing to the fact that the neutron is bound in the deuteron.

## Method

The authors generalize the INEB theory to treat a two-fragment projectile (e.g., a halo nucleus composed of a core and a valence nucleon) colliding with a two-fragment target (the deuteron), treating the process as two coupled three-body problems: breakup of the projectile without affecting the target and breakup of the target without affecting the projectile. The inclusive nonelastic breakup cross section for the observed spectator proton is expressed as a sum of terms, each containing a medium-modified total reaction cross section of an interacting fragment multiplied by the density of states of the observed proton. The formalism uses the Hussein-McVoy (HM) approach where the source function is the overlap of the distorted wave of the interacting fragment and the total wave function of the incident channel, with the reaction cross section decomposed into partial waves involving the imaginary part of the optical potential.

## Key numbers

- Halo separation energy of $^8$B: 0.137 MeV (one-proton halo)
- Halo neutron separation energy of $^{11}$Be: 0.501 MeV
- Halo neutron separation energy of $^{19}$C: 0.530 MeV
- Proton separation energy of the deuteron: 2.22 MeV
- Lifetime of $^{135}$Xe: 9.8 hours (also stated as 9.6 hours in the text)
- Thermal neutron capture cross section of $^{135}$Xe: 2.5 $\times$ 10$^6$ barns
- Proton separation energy from $^{10}$Be core: 5 MeV

## Context

This work extends the INEB theory previously applied to three-body cases (non-cluster projectile on a two-cluster target) and to three-fragment projectiles (Carlson, Frederico, Hussein 2017) to the genuine four-body case of a two-fragment projectile on a two-fragment target. The method is intended to provide an indirect way to obtain neutron capture cross sections of short-lived radioactive nuclei near the drip line, which are not directly measurable, complementing the Surrogate Method (Escher et al. 2012, 2018) and the Trojan Horse Method. The authors propose using $^{135}$Xe as a benchmark nucleus due to its known huge thermal neutron capture cross section, and suggest applications to halo nuclei such as $^8$B, $^{11}$Be, and $^{19}$C on deuteron targets.
