# Role of neutron transfer in the reaction mechanism of $^9$Be+$^{169}$Tm, $^{181}$Ta, $^{187}$Re and $^{197}$Au systems

> The contribution of one neutron stripping cross section to the total reaction cross section has been studied for $^9$Be projectile incident on $^{169}$Tm, $^{181}$Ta, $^{187}$Re and $^{197}$Au targets around Coulomb barrier energy. The measured one neutron stripping cross sections for these systems have been compared with the coupled channel calculations. The recently developed global set of optical model potential parameters for $^9$Be projectile has been used in the present calculations. The cumulative of measured complete fusion (CF), incomplete fusion (ICF), one neutron stripping and calculated non-capture breakup (NCBU) cross sections is found to explain almost the reaction cross sections for all the targets. A very small contribution from target inelastic states and elastic breakup may contribute to the remaining part. The percentage fraction of cross section for CF, ICF, one neutron stripping, and NCBU over reaction cross section show the dominance of neutron transfer and NCBU at below barrier energies while CF and ICF processes have the major contribution at above barrier energies.

## Key claim
The cumulative cross sections from measured complete fusion (CF), incomplete fusion (ICF), one neutron stripping, and calculated non-capture breakup (NCBU) account for nearly all of the reaction cross section for these systems, with only a small remaining contribution possibly from target inelastic states and elastic breakup. The abstract's claim that these processes "explain almost the reaction cross sections" is qualified by the finding that at above-barrier energies, the reaction cross sections are slightly larger than the cumulative sum, leaving a residual of about 10% unaccounted for.

## Method
Coupled reaction channel (CRC) calculations using the FRESCO code were performed to estimate one neutron transfer cross sections, employing the recently developed global optical model potential parameters for the 9Be projectile. Continuum Discretised Coupled Channel (CDCC) and combined CDCC+CRC calculations were carried out to estimate non-capture breakup (NCBU) and neutron transfer cross sections simultaneously, treating 9Be as a 8Be+n cluster structure. The measured one neutron stripping cross sections from prior work on these four target systems were compared with the CRC and CDCC+CRC calculations across a range of energies around the Coulomb barrier.

## Key numbers
- One neutron stripping cross sections were measured for 9Be+169Tm, 181Ta, 187Re, and 197Au systems around the Coulomb barrier energy
- CRC calculations showed good agreement with measured one neutron stripping data at all energies for all four systems
- CDCC+CRC calculations slightly overpredicted the data at a few energies above the barrier
- The 2+ resonance state of 8Be (E = 3.03 MeV) contributed more to the transfer cross section than the ground state
- ICF contributed between 5-20% of the reaction cross section for all targets
- The cumulative fraction of CF, ICF, 1n transfer, and NCBU left approximately 10% of the reaction cross section unexplained, possibly due to target inelastic excitations
- Neutron transfer and NCBU dominate at below-barrier energies, while CF dominates at above-barrier energies, with the crossover occurring at approximately Vb

## Context
This work extends systematic studies of reaction mechanisms involving weakly bound projectiles, building on prior measurements of one neutron stripping cross sections for these systems by Fang et al. (2016) and Kaushik et al. (2021), as well as CF and ICF measurements by Fang et al. (2015), Zhang et al. (2014), and Kaushik et al. (2020). The results contrast with findings for 6Li and 7Li projectiles, where ICF and CF provide the primary contribution to the reaction cross section even at below-barrier energies, highlighting the unique role of neutron transfer in 9Be reactions due to its 8Be+n cluster structure and low neutron separation energy (Sn=1.67 MeV). The work enables further investigation of neutron transfer systematics across different projectile systems with varying neutron separation energies, including unstable nuclei.
