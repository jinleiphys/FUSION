# Multifractal Dimension Spectrum Analysis for Nuclear Density Distribution

> We present an integral density method for calculating the multifractal dimension spectrum for the nucleon distribution in atomic nuclei. This method is then applied to analyze the non-uniformity of the density distribution in several typical types of nuclear matter distributions, including the Woods-Saxon distribution, the halo structure and the tetrahedral $α$ clustering. The subsequent discussion provides a comprehensive and detailed exploration of the results obtained. The multifractal dimension spectrum shows remarkable sensitivity to the density distribution, establishing it as an effective tool for studying the distribution of nucleons in nuclear multibody systems.

## Key claim

The multifractal dimension spectrum, calculated via the integral density method, is remarkably sensitive to the non-uniformity of nuclear density distributions, but its diagnostic power depends on the specific region examined (center vs. edge) and the scale of the sampling ball, with the spectrum transitioning from monofractal to multifractal behavior as one moves from the uniform central region to the non-uniform edge region.

## Method

The authors developed an integral density method that generalizes the cumulative mass multifractal formalism to continuous nuclear density distributions, treating protons and neutrons separately with their respective masses and binding energies. They applied this method to Woods-Saxon density distributions for 16O, 40Ca, and 208Pb (varying the diffuseness parameter), to a halo density distribution for 11Li, and to a Gaussian-based tetrahedral alpha-cluster density distribution for 16O. Multifractal generalized dimensions Dq were extracted from the slopes of log-log plots of the scaling relation for various q values, using sampling balls of radius R placed at different radial positions rg from the nuclear center.

## Key numbers

- For 208Pb at the central region (rg=0), Dq values are approximately 2.996-2.997 for smaller diffuseness and around 2.987 for larger diffuseness, with a flat (monofractal) trend
- For 16O and 40Ca at the central region, Dq values are smaller than for 208Pb and decrease as q becomes smaller, indicating multifractal behavior
- The Woods-Saxon radius parameters used: R0p = 1.81*Z^(1/3) - 1.12 (protons), R0n = 1.49*N^(1/3) - 0.79 (neutrons)
- The diffuseness parameters: ap = 0.47 - 0.00083*Z (protons), an = 0.47 + 0.00046*N (neutrons)
- For the tetrahedral 16O cluster structure, the Gaussian width parameter delta_alpha = 0.52 was used for each alpha cluster

## Context

This work extends the cumulative mass multifractal method previously applied to particle aggregates (Refs. 10-15) to continuous nuclear density distributions, enabling analysis of realistic nuclear structure models. It builds on prior studies of nuclear density non-uniformity (Ref. 3) and clustering phenomena in weakly bound nuclei (Refs. 1, 2, 18-20). The method enables quantitative comparison of density distributions across different nuclear structure types (stable, halo, cluster) and could be applied to study other exotic nuclear configurations or to extract features for machine learning classification of nuclear density patterns.
