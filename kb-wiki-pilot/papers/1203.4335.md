# Comparing non-perturbative models of the breakup of neutron-halo nuclei

> Breakup reactions of loosely-bound nuclei are often used to extract structure and/or astrophysical information. Here we compare three non-perturbative reaction theories often used when analyzing breakup experiments, namely the continuum discretized coupled channel model, the time-dependent approach relying on a semiclassical approximation, and the dynamical eikonal approximation. Our test case consists of the breakup of 15C on Pb at 68 MeV/nucleon and 20 MeV/nucleon.

## Key claim
At 68 MeV/nucleon, the three non-perturbative models (CDCC, TD, and DEA) agree within 1-3% for energy distributions and DEA reproduces CDCC angular distributions well, but at 20 MeV/nucleon the DEA overestimates the energy distribution by about 10% and shifts the angular distribution by about 10% in angle, primarily due to the absence of Coulomb deflection in the eikonal approximation.

## Method
The authors compare three non-perturbative reaction theories, continuum discretized coupled channel method (CDCC), time-dependent semiclassical approach (TD), and dynamical eikonal approximation (DEA), for the breakup of the one-neutron halo nucleus 15C on a 208Pb target. All three models start from the same three-body Hamiltonian describing the projectile as a valence neutron loosely bound to a 14C core interacting with an inert target, using identical optical potentials for the core-target and neutron-target interactions. Breakup cross sections are computed as functions of the 14C-n relative energy and the scattering angle of the 14C-n center of mass at beam energies of 68 MeV/nucleon and 20 MeV/nucleon.

## Key numbers
- At 68 MeV/nucleon, the three models agree within 1-3% at the peak of the energy distribution
- At 20 MeV/nucleon, the DEA overestimates the CDCC energy distribution by about 10% at the peak
- At 20 MeV/nucleon, the DEA angular distribution is shifted to more forward angles by about 10% in theta compared to CDCC
- At 40 MeV/nucleon, the energy distributions differ by about 5% at the maximum and the angular shift is about 5% in theta
- The 14C-n potential reproduces the 15C ground state at -1.218 MeV (1s1/2) and the excited state at -0.478 MeV (0d5/2)
- Convergence tests ensure accuracy of at least 4% in energy and angular distributions for all three models

## Context
This work provides the first quantitative comparison of non-perturbative breakup models at intermediate energies, extending earlier perturbative approaches that were found insufficient for reliable analysis of Coulomb-breakup measurements (Nunes 1999, Hussein 2006, Esbensen and Bertsch 2002, Capel and Baye 2005). The test case uses 15C on Pb, for which data exist from RIKEN (Nakamura et al. 2009), and the results validate the single-particle nature of 15C. The findings enable experimenters to choose the appropriate computationally-efficient model (TD for angle-integrated observables at all energies studied, DEA only at higher energies) rather than the more computationally intensive CDCC, and point toward a needed correction to DEA to account for Coulomb deflection at lower energies.
