# Systematics of intermediate-energy single-nucleon removal cross sections

> There is now a large and increasing body of experimental data and theoretical analyses for reactions that remove a single nucleon from an intermediate-energy beam of neutron- or proton-rich nuclei. In each such measurement, one obtains the inclusive cross section for the population of all bound final states of the mass A-1 reaction residue. These data, from different regions of the nuclear chart, and that involve weakly- and strongly-bound nucleons, are compared with theoretical expectations. These calculations include an approximate treatment of the reaction dynamics and shell-model descriptions of the projectile initial state, the bound final states of the residues, and the single-particle strengths computed from their overlap functions. The results are discussed in the light of recent data, more exclusive tests of the eikonal dynamical description, and calculations that take input from more microscopic nuclear structure models.

# Systematics of intermediate-energy single-nucleon removal cross sections

## Key claim
The ratio of experimental to theoretical inclusive one-nucleon removal cross sections, R_s = sigma_exp / sigma_th, shows a systematic trend with the neutron-proton separation energy asymmetry parameter Delta S, but this trend is based on calculations that use approximate eikonal reaction dynamics and shell-model structure inputs that may not capture all correlation effects, particularly for strongly-bound nucleons where R_s is significantly suppressed below unity.

## Method
The authors compiled inclusive single-nucleon removal cross section data from intermediate-energy secondary beam experiments (mostly 80-100 MeV/nucleon on Be-9 targets, with some higher-energy data on carbon targets) and compared them to theoretical calculations. The theoretical cross sections were computed using an eikonal model that sums elastic and inelastic breakup contributions, with shell-model spectroscopic factors, Hartree-Fock constrained radial overlaps and optical potentials, and the single-particle cross sections for each bound final state of the A-1 residue. The comparison is quantified by the ratio R_s = sigma_exp / sigma_th, plotted against the asymmetry parameter Delta S.

## Key numbers
- R_s = 0.24(3) for Ar-32(-n) with S_n ≈ 22 MeV and Delta S ≈ +20 MeV (well-bound neutron removal)
- R_s near unity for C-15(-n) with S_n = 1.22 MeV and Delta S ≈ -20 MeV (weakly-bound neutron removal)
- R_s = 0.76(11) for O-14(-p) on carbon target at 305 MeV/nucleon, recalculated with sigma_sp = 27.76 mb and C^2S = 1.55
- R_s = 0.49(4) for C-10(-n) at 1.6 GeV/nucleon, with sigma_sp = 22.8 mb and experimental cross section 21.4(17) mb, confirming the 120 MeV/nucleon value of 0.49(2)
- R_s values from VMC calculations for Be-10(-n) and C-10(-n) became 1.00(5) and 0.75(4) respectively, compared to shell-model values of 0.69(4) and 0.49(2)

## Context
This work updates the systematic compilation first presented in Gade et al. (2008, Phys. Rev. C 77, 044306) with new data from NSCL, RIKEN, HIRFL, and Berkeley measurements, including reactions on Be-9 and C-12 targets. The trend of suppressed R_s for large positive Delta S (removal of well-bound nucleons) extends earlier observations from electron-induced proton knockout studies (reviewed by Dickhoff and Barbieri, 2004) and from analyses of C-12 and O-16 reactions (Brown et al., 2002). The results enable future tests using more microscopic structure models such as variational Monte Carlo (Grinyer et al., 2011) and coupled-cluster calculations (Jensen et al., 2011), and motivate measurements at higher beam energies (above 200 MeV/nucleon) to avoid kinematic cutoff effects for well-bound nucleon removal.
