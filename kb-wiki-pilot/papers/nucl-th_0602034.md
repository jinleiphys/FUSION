# XCDCC: Core Excitation in the Breakup of Exotic Nuclei

> The eXtended Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (XCDCC) method is developed to treat reactions where core degrees of freedom play a role. The projectile is treated as a multi-configuration coupled channels system generated from a valence particle coupled to a deformed core which is allowed to excite. The coupled channels initial state breaks up into a coupled channels continuum which is discretized into bins, similarly to the original CDCC method. Core collective degrees of freedom are also included in the interaction of the core and the target, so that dynamical effects can occur during the reaction. We present results for the breakup of $^{17}$C=$^{16}$C+n and $^{11}$Be=$^{10}$Be+n on $^{9}$Be. Results show that the total cross section increases with core deformation. More importantly, the relative percentage of the various components of the initial state are modified during the reaction process through dynamical effects. This implies that comparing spectroscopic factors from structure calculations with experimental cross sections requires more detailed reaction models that go beyond the single particle model.

## Key claim
The XCDCC method demonstrates that dynamical core excitation/de-excitation during breakup reactions modifies the relative percentages of the various components of the projectile's initial state, implying that comparing spectroscopic factors from structure calculations with experimental cross sections requires reaction models that go beyond the single-particle model.

## Method
The projectile is treated as a multi-configuration coupled channels system generated from a valence particle coupled to a deformed core that is allowed to excite, using a rotational model for the core. The coupled channels initial state breaks up into a coupled channels continuum which is discretized into bins, similarly to the original CDCC method, and core collective degrees of freedom are included in the core-target interaction so that dynamical effects can occur during the reaction. The three-body scattering problem is solved by expanding the wave function over projectile states and reducing it to radial coupled channels equations in the projectile-target coordinate R, with coupling potentials computed from the core-target and valence-target interactions.

## Key numbers
- For $^{17}$C=$^{16}$C+n on $^{9}$Be at ≈60 A MeV: the coupled channels ground state model (deformation β₂=0.55) produces four configurations with relative probabilities: [d⊗0⁺] 0.175, [s⊗2⁺] 0.103, [d⊗2⁺] 0.721, [g⊗2⁺] 0.001
- For $^{11}$Be=$^{10}$Be+n: the coupled channels ground state model (deformation β₂=0.67) produces two configurations with relative probabilities: [s⊗0⁺] 0.883 and [d⊗2⁺] 0.117
- The $^{16}$C core has a 2⁺ state at excitation energy 1.766 MeV and a 4⁺ state at 4.142 MeV
- The rms radius calculated for deformed $^{17}$C is 2.77 fm; the experimental rms radius is 3.04±0.11 fm

## Context
This work extends the Continuum Discretized Coupled Channels (CDCC) method to include core degrees of freedom, addressing limitations of the single-particle model that had shown discrepancies in describing $^{8}$B breakup at higher energies and $^{11}$Be proton scattering. The method enables investigation of dynamical core excitation effects that were previously neglected in both nuclear knockout reactions (where the method of Tostevin adds spectroscopic factors incoherently) and Coulomb dissociation (where Shyam et al. estimated excited core contributions as only a few percent using DWBA and adiabatic models). The XCDCC method provides a fully quantal framework to examine interference between configurations and dynamical (de-)excitation of the core during the reaction process.
