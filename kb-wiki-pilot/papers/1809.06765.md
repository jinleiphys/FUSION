# A Deeply Bound Dibaryon is Incompatible with Neutron Stars and Supernovae

> We study the effect of a dibaryon, S, in the mass range 1860 MeV < m_S < 2054 MeV, which is heavy enough not to disturb the stability of nuclei and light enough to possibly be cosmologically metastable. Such a deeply bound state can act as a baryon sink in regions of high baryon density and temperature. We find that the ambient conditions encountered inside a newly born neutron star are likely to sustain a sufficient population of hyperons to ensure that a population of S dibaryons can equilibrate in less than a few seconds. This would be catastrophic for the stability of neutron stars and the observation of neutrino emission from the proto-neutron star of Supernova 1987A over ~ O(10)s. A deeply bound dibaryon is therefore incompatible with the observed supernova explosion, unless the cross section for S production is severely suppressed.

# A Deeply Bound Dibaryon is Incompatible with Neutron Stars and Supernovae

## Key claim

A deeply bound dibaryon (the "S" sexaquark) with mass between 1860 MeV and 2054 MeV would be produced rapidly in proto-neutron star interiors via on-shell exothermic reactions involving hyperons, leading to catastrophic conversion of baryonic matter into dibaryons within seconds; this conclusion holds unless the dibaryon production cross section is suppressed by approximately 11 orders of magnitude relative to naive expectations.

## Method

The authors solve coupled differential equations for the number densities of nucleons, Lambda hyperons, and S dibaryons in a proto-neutron star core at temperature T=30 MeV and nuclear saturation density n0=0.16 fm^-3. They calculate the thermally averaged cross sections for Lambda production via NN to Lambda N reactions using single-pion exchange with a coupling extracted from the vacuum Lambda lifetime, and for S production via Lambda Lambda to S gamma using wavefunction overlap calculations in the Isgur-Karl and Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone models. The equilibration time t_S for S production is derived analytically and compared to the observed neutrino burst duration of approximately 10 seconds from Supernova 1987A.

## Key numbers

- Mass range of the S dibaryon considered: 1860 MeV < m_S < 2054 MeV, corresponding to binding energies 176.9 MeV < B_S < 361 MeV
- Default Lambda production cross section used: <sigma_{NN to Lambda N} v> = 3 x 10^-30 cm^3/s (conservatively suppressed by 90% from naive estimate of ~10^-27 cm^3/s)
- S production equilibrates at time t_S approximately equal to (1 second) x (4 x 10^-34 cm^3/s) / <sigma_{Lambda Lambda to S gamma} v> when the hierarchy of rates holds
- For <sigma_{Lambda Lambda to S gamma} v> greater than approximately 10^-34 cm^3/s, S production is fatal for the proto-neutron star on timescales shorter than the observed neutrino burst
- The calculated cross section for Lambda Lambda to S gamma is approximately 3 x 10^-23 (g_{Lambda S}^2) (B_S/176.9 MeV) (T/30 MeV) cm^3/s, where g_{Lambda S}^2 can be as small as 10^-11 to 10^-14 only if the S radius is very close to its Compton wavelength of approximately 0.1 fm
- The Lambda strong interaction radius is 0.76 +/- 0.01 fm from experimental data; the constituent quark model gives approximately 0.51 fm
- The minimum S radius from the Compton wavelength bound is r_S >= 0.1 fm x (2054 MeV/m_S) + 0.34x fm, where x is a parameter between 0 and 0.1

## Context

This work extends the long-standing theoretical discussion of the H dibaryon (uuddss state) first proposed by Jaffe in 1976, and specifically addresses the more deeply bound S sexaquark proposed by Farrar (2003, 2017) with binding energy B_S approximately 100-300 MeV. Previous accelerator searches have ruled out dibaryons with masses below approximately 2 GeV, and lattice studies support a weakly bound dibaryon with binding energy of order 10 MeV but cannot rule out the more deeply bound state. The authors show that proto-neutron star conditions provide a stringent astrophysical constraint: the observation of neutrino emission from Supernova 1987A over approximately 10 seconds is incompatible with the existence of such a dibaryon unless its production cross section is severely suppressed, which would require an S radius very close to 0.1 fm. This result is complementary to the cosmological abundance analysis of Kolb and Turner, and to searches for the S in Upsilon decays by Echenard et al.
