# Strangeness Enhancement: Challenges and Successes

> Highly effective conversion of kinetic energy into abundant particle multiplicity is the remarkable feature discovered in high energy heavy ion collisions. This short and pedagogic review addresses topical issues related to the understanding of this phenomenon, originating in the creation of the deconfined quark--gluon plasma phase. I consider in depth the apparently simple, yet sometimes misunderstood, intricate issues: a) statistical hadro-chemistry, chemical parameters, b) strange flavor chemical equilibration in quark--gluon plasma, and c) particle yields and sudden hadronization, in the historic perspective of work and competition with my friend Jozsef Zimanyi.

# Strangeness Enhancement: Challenges and Successes

## Key claim

Strangeness enhancement in high-energy heavy ion collisions is a signature of deconfined quark-gluon plasma formation, but the quantitative success of this signature depends critically on the choice of the strong coupling constant α_s in perturbative QCD calculations; using α_s = 0.6 (appropriate for the thermal energy scale Λ ≈ 3–6T) rather than α_s = 0.2 yields relaxation times of τ ≈ 2 fm/c rather than 15 fm/c, making chemical equilibration of strangeness in QGP achievable within the fireball lifetime.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical development of strangeness production as a QGP signature, focusing on three interconnected topics: (a) statistical hadro-chemistry with chemical parameters λ_i (controlling net quark difference) and γ_i (controlling pair abundance), (b) strange flavor chemical equilibration in QGP via gluon fusion processes GG → s\bar{s} (not just q\bar{q} → s\bar{s}), and (c) particle yields from sudden hadronization where entropy conservation forces the light quark phase space occupancy γ_q above unity (γ_q ≈ 1.6 at T = 140 MeV). The author compares the algebraic coalescence re-hadronization model (ALCOR) of the Budapest group with the SHARE (Statistical HAdronization with REsonances) framework developed by his own group.

## Key numbers

- Strange quark mass in 1980 was believed to be m_s ≈ 280–300 MeV; today the value is about 1/3 as large
- For m_s/T between 1.5 and 2, the function x²K₂(x) varies between 1.3 and 1, giving N_{\bar{s}}/N_{\bar{q}} ≈ 5 in many cases of interest
- The strong coupling constant at the thermal energy scale Λ ≈ 3–6T for T = 200–300 MeV should be ⟨α_s⟩ = 0.6; α_s(0.86 GeV) = 0.60 ± 0.10 ± 0.07 from particle data group
- Using ⟨α_s⟩ = 0.2 instead of 0.6 gives relaxation times longer by an order of magnitude (τ ≈ 15 fm/c vs. τ ≈ 2 fm/c)
- Effective degeneracy near QGP breakup: g ≈ 30, decreasing to g ≈ 28 for equilibrated QGP near T = 260 MeV
- Critical value γ_q = 1.4 found for T ≈ 140 MeV, crossing γ_q = 1 at T ≈ 180 MeV
- Approximate relation: γ_q ≈ 1.6 – 0.015(T – 140) [MeV]
- At LHC, ratio of strangeness pair number to entropy can reach 0.04, with γ_s/γ_q > 2 expected

## Context

This review extends the author's original 1980 proposal that strangeness abundance and strange antibaryon yields offer an opportunity to identify deconfined quark-gluon matter formation, and addresses the historical competition with Jószef Zimányi and the Budapest group. The paper contrasts the Bíró–Zimányi 1981 result (which omitted gluon fusion processes GG → s\bar{s} and found equilibration too slow) with the author's 1982 work with Berndt Müller showing gluon fusion dominates strangeness production. The work enables quantitative use of strangeness enhancement as a deconfinement diagnostic at RHIC and LHC, building on the SHARE program (Torrieri et al., 2004–2006) and the Fermi-2000 hadron production model (Letessier and Rafelski, 2000).
