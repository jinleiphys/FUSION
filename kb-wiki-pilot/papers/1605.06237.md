# Importance of lifetime effects in breakup and suppression of complete fusion in reactions of weakly bound nuclei

> Complete fusion cross sections in collisions of light, weakly bound nuclei and high Z targets show above-barrier suppression of complete fusion. This has been interpreted as resulting from breakup of the weakly bound nucleus prior to reaching the fusion barrier, reducing the probability of complete fusion. This paper investigates how these conclusions are affected by lifetimes of the resonant states that are populated prior to breakup. If the mean life of a populated resonance is much longer than the fusion timescale, then its breakup cannot suppress complete fusion. For short-lived resonances, the situation is more complex. This work includes the mean life of the short-lived 2+ resonance in 8Be in classical dynamical model calculations to determine its effect on energy and angular correlations of the breakup fragments and on predictions of fusion suppression. Coincidence measurements of breakup fragments produced in reactions of 9Be with 144Sm, 168Er, 186W, 196Pt, 208Pb and 209Bi at energies below the barrier are re-analysed. Predictions of breakup observables and of complete and incomplete fusion at energies above the fusion barrier are made using the classical dynamical simulation code PLATYPUS, modified to include the lifetimes of short-lived resonant states. The agreement of the breakup observables is improved when lifetime effects are included. The predicted suppression of complete fusion due to breakup is nearly independent of Z, with an average value of 9%, below the experimentally determined fusion suppression of 30% in these systems. This more realistic treatment of breakup leads to the conclusion that the suppression of complete fusion cannot be fully explained by breakup prior to reaching the fusion barrier. Other mechanisms that can suppress complete fusion must be investigated. A candidate is cluster transfer that produces the same nuclei as incomplete fusion.

## Key claim

The paper concludes that the suppression of complete fusion in reactions of weakly bound nuclei cannot be fully explained by breakup prior to reaching the fusion barrier; only about one third of the observed suppression (an average of 9% predicted versus the experimentally determined ~30%) can be attributed to breakup, with the remaining suppression requiring other mechanisms such as cluster transfer.

## Method

The authors re-analyzed previously performed coincidence measurements of breakup fragments from reactions of 9Be with targets 144Sm, 168Er, 186W, 196Pt, 208Pb and 209Bi at below-barrier energies, using an improved efficiency determination for the BALiN detector array. They then used the classical dynamical simulation code PLATYPUS, modified to explicitly include the lifetimes of short-lived resonant states (specifically the 2+ resonance in 8Be) and excitation energy distributions, to predict breakup observables and complete and incomplete fusion cross sections at above-barrier energies.

## Key numbers

- The 3.03 MeV 2+ state of 8Be has an on-resonance width of 1513 ± 15 keV and a mean life of 0.44 × 10^(-21) s
- The 0+ ground state of 8Be has a width of 5.57 ± 0.25 eV and a mean life of 1.2 × 10^(-16) s
- The predicted suppression of complete fusion due to breakup is nearly independent of Z, with an average value of 9%
- This predicted 9% suppression is below the experimentally determined fusion suppression of approximately 30% in these systems
- The 2.43 MeV 5/2- state in 9Be has a mean life of approximately 8.4 × 10^(-19) s (width 0.78 ± 0.13 keV)

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Rafiei et al. (2010) and Diaz-Torres et al. (2007, 2011) that used the PLATYPUS code without explicit lifetime effects to model breakup and fusion suppression. The inclusion of resonance lifetimes shows that a larger fraction of nuclei remain intact at the fusion barrier radius compared to calculations without lifetime effects, contradicting the earlier interpretation that breakup prior to reaching the fusion barrier fully explains the observed suppression. This enables investigation of alternative mechanisms, with cluster transfer identified as a candidate that produces the same nuclei as incomplete fusion.
