# Low-lying electric-dipole strengths of Ca, Ni, and Sn isotopes imprinted on total reaction cross sections

> Low-lying electric-dipole ($E1$) strength of a neutron-rich nucleus contains information on neutron-skin thickness, deformation, and shell evolution. We discuss the possibility of making use of total reaction cross sections on $^{40}$Ca, $^{120}$Sn, and $^{208}$Pb targets to probe the $E1$ strength of neutron-rich Ca, Ni, and Sn isotopes. They exhibit large enhancement of the $E1$ strength at neutron number $N > 28$, $50$, and $82$, respectively, due to a change of the single-particle orbits near the Fermi surface participating in the transitions. The density distributions and the electric-multipole strength functions of those isotopes are calculated by the Hartree-Fock+BCS and the canonical-basis-time-dependent-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov methods, respectively, using three kinds of Skyrme-type effective interaction. The nuclear and Coulomb breakup processes are respectively described with the Glauber model and the equivalent photon method in which the effect of finite-charge distribution is taken into account. The three Skyrme interactions give different results for the total reaction cross sections because of different Coulomb breakup contributions. The contribution of the low-lying $E1$ strength is amplified when the low-incident energy is chosen. With an appropriate choice of the incident energy and target nucleus, the total reaction cross section can be complementary to the Coulomb excitation for analysing the low-lying $E1$ strength of unstable nuclei.

## Key claim

The total reaction cross section on heavy targets (⁴⁰Ca, ¹²⁰Sn, ²⁰⁸Pb) can serve as a complementary probe to Coulomb excitation for analyzing low-lying E1 strength of neutron-rich unstable nuclei, provided the incident energy and target nucleus are chosen appropriately to amplify the low-lying E1 contribution relative to higher multipoles and the giant resonance region.

## Method

The authors calculated ground-state density distributions of Ca, Ni, and Sn isotopes using the Hartree-Fock+BCS method and electric-multipole strength functions using the canonical-basis-time-dependent-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method, employing three Skyrme-type effective interactions (SkM*, SLy4, SkI3). Nuclear breakup cross sections were computed with the Glauber model in the Nucleon-Target formalism, while Coulomb breakup cross sections were calculated using the equivalent photon method with a finite-charge distribution correction for the target nucleus, including E1, E2, and E3 multipole contributions.

## Key numbers

- For ¹³⁴Sn (N=84), the root-mean-square radii of the outermost 1f₇/₂ orbit are 5.96 fm (SkM*), 6.13 fm (SLy4), and 6.44 fm (SkI3), with corresponding single-particle energies of -3.21, -2.15, and -1.53 MeV
- For ⁸⁰Ni (N=52), the single-particle energies and rms radii of the dominant outermost 1d₅/₂ orbit are -2.72 MeV/5.46 fm (SkM*), -1.62 MeV/5.71 fm (SLy4), and -1.31 MeV/6.05 fm (SkI3)
- For ⁵⁰Ca (N=30), the outermost 1p₃/₂ orbit single-particle energies and rms radii are -5.76 MeV/4.60 fm (SkM*), -6.59 MeV/4.47 fm (SLy4), and -5.16 MeV/4.79 fm (SkI3)
- The finite-charge distribution correction reduces Coulomb breakup cross sections by 1-3% for ⁴⁰Ca target, 3-4% for ¹²⁰Sn target, and 4-5% for ²⁰⁸Pb target at 100-1000A MeV
- For ¹¹⁸Sn+⁴⁰Ca at 77A MeV, calculated σ_R values are 4.20 barn (SkM*), 4.20 barn (SLy4), and 4.18 barn (SkI3), compared to experimental 4.89±0.53 barn
- For ²⁰⁸Pb+⁴⁰Ca at 77A MeV, calculated σ_R values are 5.46 barn (SkM*), 5.43 barn (SLy4), and 5.47 barn (SkI3), compared to experimental 5.33±0.50 barn

## Context

This work extends previous systematic analyses of E1 strength enhancement in neutron-rich nuclei by Ebata et al. (2014) and Papakonstantinou et al. (2014, 2015), which showed that enhancement depends strongly on shell structure and mass region beyond the magic numbers N=28, 50, and 82. The approach contrasts with conventional use of total reaction cross sections on light targets (e.g., ¹H, ¹²C) to study nuclear sizes, as done by Takechi et al. (2010, 2014) and Kanungo et al. (2011), by instead employing heavy targets to amplify Coulomb breakup contributions. The method enables probing low-lying E1 strength of unstable nuclei without requiring final-state wave functions, unlike Coulomb excitation experiments in inverse kinematics used for halo nuclei by Nakamura et al. (2006, 2009).
