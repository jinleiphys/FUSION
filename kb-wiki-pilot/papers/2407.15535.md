# First experimental test of the ratio method for nuclear-reaction analysis

> Nuclear halos are very exotic quantal structures observed far from stability. Because of their short lifetime, they are mostly studied through reactions. The ratio method offers a new observable: the ratio of angular differential cross sections for breakup and scattering. It is predicted to be much more sensitive to the projectile structure than individual cross sections thanks to its independence of the reaction process. We test this new observable experimentally for the first time considering the collision of 11Be on C at 22.8 MeV/nucleon. We extend this analysis to similar data recently measured on Pb at 19.1 MeV/nucleon. Both analyses confirm the theoretical predictions, which opens the door to a new era in the study of nuclear structure near the neutron dripline. This should prove invaluable in conjunction with the start of FRIB. The ratio method could also be extended to other fields of quantum physics beyond nuclear reactions.

## Key claim

The ratio of angular differential cross sections for breakup and scattering is experimentally confirmed to be nearly independent of the optical potential choice and systematic uncertainties, validating the theoretical prediction that this observable is sensitive primarily to projectile structure rather than reaction dynamics, for collisions of 11Be on C at 22.8 MeV/nucleon and on Pb at 19.1 MeV/nucleon.

## Method

The experiment measured inclusive breakup and scattering differential cross sections simultaneously for 11Be impinging on a natural carbon target at 22.8 MeV/nucleon using the BlueSTEAl detector system at Texas A&M University, with particle identification via Si DeltaE-E telescopes and a phoswich detector. The data were analyzed within the dynamical eikonal approximation (DEA) using a three-body model describing 11Be as a 10Be core plus a loosely bound neutron, with the core-target interaction modeled by three different optical potentials (phenomenological ATB and two double-folding potentials from chiral EFT). The analysis was extended to existing 11Be-Pb data at 19.1 MeV/nucleon from Duan et al.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy: 22.8 +/- 0.3 MeV/nucleon for 11Be on C; 19.1 MeV/nucleon for 11Be on Pb
- 11Be beam intensity: about 7.2 x 10^3 particles per second; beam purity about 60%
- Carbon target thickness: 8.7 (3) mg/cm^2 (natural carbon, 99.8% purity)
- Measured cross section uncertainties: 7-19% (1 sigma), including statistical (1-17%), beam intensity (<0.1%), target thickness (3.5%), and solid angle (5.4%)
- At 25 degrees scattering angle, the ratio differs by only 15% between different optical potentials, whereas the individual cross sections change by more than an order of magnitude

## Context

This work provides the first experimental test of the ratio method proposed theoretically by Capel, Johnson, and Nunes (CJN11, CJN13, CJN20), which builds on the recoil excitation and breakup model (REB) of Johnson, Al-Khalili, and Tostevin (JAT97). The results confirm theoretical predictions that the ratio removes the strong dependence on optical potentials that has hampered traditional reaction analyses (CGB04, SNP22, HWL23, CLN23). This validation opens the door to using the ratio method as a spectroscopic tool for studying halo nuclei and other exotic structures near the neutron dripline, with a recently approved FRIB experiment on 19C planned to further test the method under exclusive conditions.
