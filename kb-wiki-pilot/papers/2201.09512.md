# Coupled-channels calculations for nuclear reactions: from exotic nuclei to superheavy elements

> Atomic nuclei are composite systems, and they may be dynamically excited during nuclear reactions. Such excitations are not only relevant to inelastic scattering but they also affect other reaction processes such as elastic scattering and fusion. The coupled-channels approach is a framework which can describe these reaction processes in a unified manner. It expands the total wave function of the system in terms of the ground and excited states of the colliding nuclei, and solves the coupled Shrödinger equations to obtain the $S$-matrix, from which several cross sections can be constructed. This approach has been a standard tool to analyze experimental data for nuclear reactions. In this paper, we review the present status and the recent developments of the coupled-channels approach. This includes the microscopic coupled-channels method and its application to cluster physics, the continuum discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) method for breakup reactions, the semi-microscopic approach to heavy-ion subbarrier fusion reactions, the channel coupling effects on nuclear astrophysics and syntheses of superheavy elements, and inclusive breakup and incomplete fusion reactions of weakly-bound nuclei.

## Key claim

The coupled-channels approach remains a standard tool for analyzing low-energy nuclear reactions, but its reliability depends on approximations such as the isocentrifugal approximation and the use of phenomenological or microscopic coupling potentials; the paper reviews recent developments including the continuum discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) method for breakup reactions and the microscopic coupled-channels approach, which can be applied with greater reliability to unknown regions where no experimental data exist.

## Method

The authors review the coupled-channels formalism, which expands the total wave function of the colliding system in terms of the ground and excited states of the nuclei and solves coupled Schrödinger equations to obtain the S-matrix and cross sections. They describe the construction of coupling potentials using the macroscopic collective model (with vibrational or rotational excitations) and the microscopic double-folding procedure with transition densities from nuclear structure calculations. The paper also covers the CDCC method for continuum states of weakly-bound nuclei, the isocentrifugal approximation to reduce the dimensionality of the coupled equations, and numerical solution methods including direct integration, iterative methods, and the variational method.

## Context

This review extends earlier developments summarized by Tamura (1965), textbooks by Satchler (1983), Fröbrich and Lipperheide (1996), Broglia and Winther (2004), Bertulani and Danielewicz (2004), Thompson and Nunes (2009), and Canto and Hussein (2013), and builds on established computer codes ECIS, FRESCO, and CCFULL. The paper covers new directions including the CDCC method for neutron-rich nuclei (Kamimura 1986, Austern et al. 1987, Yahiro et al. 2012), the microscopic coupled-channels approach (Takashina et al. 2010, Furumoto et al. 2013, Minomo et al. 2016, Kim et al. 2008), and applications to cluster structure of light nuclei (Kanada-En'yo et al. 2019-2021), heavy-ion subbarrier fusion, and synthesis of superheavy elements.
