# Quarkonium Physics at a Fixed-Target Experiment using the LHC Beams

> We outline the many quarkonium-physics opportunities offered by a multi-purpose fixed-target experiment using the p and Pb LHC beams extracted by a bent crystal. This provides an integrated luminosity of 0.5 fb-1 per year on a typical 1cm-long target. Such an extraction mode does not alter the performance of the collider experiments at the LHC. With such a high luminosity, one can analyse quarkonium production in great details in pp, pd and pA collisions at sqrt(sNN)~115 GeV and at sqrt(sNN)~72 GeV in PbA collisions. In a typical pp (pA) run, the obtained quarkonium yields per unit of rapidity are 2-3 orders of magnitude larger than those expected at RHIC and about respectively 10 (70) times larger than for ALICE. In PbA, they are comparable. By instrumenting the target-rapidity region, the large negative-xF domain can be accessed for the first time, greatly extending previous measurements by Hera-B and E866. Such analyses should help resolving the quarkonium-production controversies and clear the way for gluon PDF extraction via quarkonium studies. The nuclear target-species versatility provides a unique opportunity to study nuclear matter and the features of the hot and dense matter formed in PbA collisions. A polarised proton target allows the study of transverse-spin asymmetries in J/psi and Upsilon production, providing access to the gluon and charm Sivers functions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a fixed-target experiment using LHC beams extracted by a bent crystal can provide quarkonium yields 2-3 orders of magnitude larger than at RHIC and about 10 (70) times larger than ALICE in pp (pA) collisions, but this depends on the assumption that such an extraction mode does not alter the performance of the collider experiments at the LHC and that an integrated luminosity of 0.5 fb-1 per year on a 1cm-long target can be achieved.

## Method

The authors propose using bent crystals to extract proton and lead beams from the LHC and direct them onto fixed targets, enabling pp, pd, pA and PbA collisions at center-of-mass energies of sqrt(sNN) ~ 115 GeV for proton beams and sqrt(sNN) ~ 72 GeV for lead beams. They evaluate the expected quarkonium yields per unit of rapidity for various target materials (hydrogen, deuterium, beryllium, copper, tungsten, lead) and compare these yields with those achievable at RHIC and the LHC collider experiments. The analysis considers the physics reach for resolving quarkonium production puzzles, extracting gluon parton distribution functions, studying nuclear matter effects, and measuring transverse-spin asymmetries with a polarised target.

## Key numbers

- Integrated luminosity of 0.5 fb-1 per year on a 1cm-long target
- sqrt(sNN) ~ 115 GeV for pp, pd and pA collisions; sqrt(sNN) ~ 72 GeV for PbA collisions
- In pp (pA) runs, quarkonium yields per unit of rapidity are 2-3 orders of magnitude larger than at RHIC and about 10 (70) times larger than for ALICE
- For a 100cm solid hydrogen target: 5.2 x 10^8 J/psi and 1.0 x 10^6 Upsilon per year per unit of rapidity at y=0
- For a 1cm tungsten target: 1.1 x 10^9 J/psi and 2.3 x 10^6 Upsilon per year per unit of rapidity at y=0
- For a 1cm lead target: 6.7 x 10^8 J/psi and 1.3 x 10^6 Upsilon per year per unit of rapidity at y=0
- For a 100cm liquid deuterium target: 9.6 x 10^8 J/psi and 1.9 x 10^6 Upsilon per year per unit of rapidity at y=0
- For a 1cm copper target: 5.3 x 10^8 J/psi and 1.1 x 10^6 Upsilon per year per unit of rapidity at y=0

## Context

This work extends the physics program of fixed-target quarkonium experiments that began with the co-discovery of the J/psi at BNL in 1974 and the Upsilon at Fermilab, and continues through SPS experiments showing anomalous J/psi suppression in PbPb collisions and the E866 and HERA-B measurements at Fermilab. The proposed experiment, referred to as AFTER, would bridge the energy gap between SPS and RHIC, enabling detailed studies of bottomonium production and dynamics that were previously inaccessible. It would complement LHC collider results by providing high-statistics data at lower energies and accessing the large negative-xF region for the first time, extending previous measurements by HERA-B (which could not go below xF ~ -0.3 for J/psi) and E866. The nuclear target versatility and the possibility of a polarised proton target would enable studies of gluon nPDFs, nuclear matter effects, and transverse-spin asymmetries that are not possible at existing facilities.
