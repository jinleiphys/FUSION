# Reaction mechanism of quasi-free knockout processes in exotic RI beam era

> The quasi-free nucleon knockout reaction has been revealed the single-particle nature of nuclei. Thanks to the advances in experimental techniques and reaction theory, various new aspects of nuclei are being revealed by knockout reactions. In this article, we review the basic concept of the quasi-free knockout reaction, and recent achievements in the SEASTAR project using the MINOS system. We also present our new findings on the low-energy nucleon knockout reaction and the $α$ knockout reaction. The combination of the (microscopic) structure theory, reaction theory and experiments will be the key to a complete understanding of the $α$ formation and its universality in the coming decades. Noble clusters, e.g., $d$, $t$, $^{3}$He, etc. are in the scope of the ONOKORO project. The implementation of the two (and more) nucleon correlation in the reaction theory is essential to connect the properties of such clusters and the reaction observables. A new framework, CDCCIA, is introduced for this purpose, which will also be applicable to the two-nucleon knockout reactions, e.g., $(p,3p)$, $(p,2pn)$, and $(p,p2n)$.

## Key claim

The paper argues that quasi-free knockout reactions, particularly \((p,p\alpha)\) and \((p,pN)\), can probe single-particle and cluster structures in nuclei across the nuclear chart, but the interpretation of observables such as spectroscopic factors and momentum distributions depends critically on the reaction mechanism and kinematics, with the caveat that the extracted spectroscopic factors are not model-independent observables and show systematic discrepancies depending on the reaction type and theoretical framework used.

## Method

The authors review the distorted-wave impulse approximation (DWIA) framework for describing quasi-free knockout reactions, where the transition matrix factorizes into an \(NN\) (or \(p\)-cluster) effective interaction and an overlap wave function representing the struck particle inside the target nucleus. They extend this to cluster knockout reactions such as \((p,p\alpha)\) by replacing the nucleon-nucleon interaction with a \(p\)-\(\alpha\) effective interaction and using cluster optical potentials, and introduce a new combined CDCC-DWIA framework (CDCCIA) for fragile clusters like deuterons. Experimental data from the SEASTAR project at RIKEN RIBF using the MINOS system in inverse kinematics are compared with DWIA, QTC, and other theoretical calculations to extract momentum distributions, spectroscopic factors, and cluster formation amplitudes.

## Key numbers

- The four-neutron resonance observed in \(^{8}\text{He}(p,p\alpha)4n\) has energy \(2.37 \pm 0.38(\text{stat.}) \pm 0.44(\text{sys.})\) MeV and width \(1.75 \pm 0.22(\text{stat.}) \pm 0.30(\text{sys.})\) MeV
- The proton separation energy of \(^{14}\text{O}\) is \(S_p = 4.2\) MeV and the neutron separation energy is \(S_n = 23.2\) MeV, leading to markedly different longitudinal momentum distribution shapes in \((p,2p)\) and \((p,pn)\) reactions at \(\sim 100\) MeV/nucleon
- About 30–40% reduction of spectroscopic factors is expected from missing correlations in the shell model
- The \(\alpha\) knockout cross sections from \(^{112,116,120,124}\text{Sn}\) show an isotope dependence that agrees with theoretical predictions of \(\alpha\) particle formation on the nuclear surface, with suppression due to neutron skin development

## Context

This review extends the long history of quasi-free knockout studies dating to the 1960s–1980s (Jacob, Chant, Roos) and recent SEASTAR project results using MINOS at RIKEN RIBF, which have revealed shell evolution (e.g., \(N=34\) closure in \(^{54}\text{Ca}\)) and \(\alpha\) clustering in both light and medium-mass nuclei. The work enables future systematic studies of cluster formation across the nuclear chart via the ONOKORO project, and introduces the CDCCIA framework for treating fragile clusters (deuteron, triton, \(^{3}\text{He}\)) and two-nucleon knockout reactions \((p,3p)\), \((p,2pn)\), and \((p,p2n)\), connecting reaction observables to nuclear structure models such as THSR, AMD, shell model, VS-IMSRG, SCGF, and HFB.
