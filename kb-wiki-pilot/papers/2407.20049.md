# Systematic study of complete fusion suppression for 6,7 Li nuclei using artificial neural network

> In recent decades, there has been a significant increase in the measurement of complete fusion cross-sections for various reactions, with particular emphasis on the reactions involving weakly bound projectile. It has been well established that the complete fusion cross-section involving weakly bound nuclei is suppressed at above barrier energies due to the breakup effect. Accurate determination of suppression factor is essential to understand the effect of breakup on complete fusion suppression. In this study, Feedforward Artificial Neural Network (ANN) methods based on Multilayer Perceptron is utilized to estimate the complete fusion suppression factor for reactions involving 6,7 Li projectile from the comparison of ANN predicted reduced fusion functions (F (x)) with the Universal Fusion Function(F0 (x)). Average suppression factor has been estimated as 0.68 and 0.74 for 6 Li and 7 Li induced reactions respectively. Normalized Mean Squared Error(NMSE) has been calculated as 1.85% for training and 1.92% for testing data of ANN for 6 Li case, while for 7 Li cases, the same is for 3.73% and 6.48% respectively. Present results have been compared with three other alternative methods Support Vector Regression, Random Forest Regression and Gaussian Process Regression. Relevant performance matrices has been estimated. It has been observed that, ANN algorithm is giving the better accuracy as compared with other. This results indicate that ANN might be an alternative tool for estimation of complete fusion suppression factor for weakly bound projectile.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a feedforward artificial neural network (ANN) can estimate the complete fusion suppression factor for reactions induced by weakly bound 6,7Li projectiles with better accuracy than Support Vector Regression, Random Forest Regression, or Gaussian Process Regression, yielding average suppression factors of 0.68 and 0.74 respectively. The hedge is that the ANN method is presented as a "might be" alternative tool, not a replacement for physical models, and the suppression factors are averages across many target nuclei with the same projectile, not per-reaction predictions.

## Method

Experimental complete fusion cross-section data for 6Li and 7Li projectiles on targets with mass numbers 90 to 209 were collected from existing literature. A feedforward multilayer perceptron with two hidden layers (64 and 32 nodes) was trained on the dimensionless variable x = (E_cm - V_B)/(hbar*omega) as input and the reduced fusion function F(x) as output, using 70% of data for training and 30% for testing. The trained ANN predicted F(x) for finer x values, and the average suppression factor was calculated as the ratio of the ANN-predicted F(x) to the Universal Fusion Function F_0(x). The ANN results were compared with Support Vector Regression, Random Forest Regression, and Gaussian Process Regression using performance metrics including Normalized Mean Squared Error (NMSE) and R-squared.

## Key numbers

* Average complete fusion suppression factor for 6Li-induced reactions: 0.68
* Average complete fusion suppression factor for 7Li-induced reactions: 0.74
* NMSE for 6Li ANN training data: 1.85%; for testing data: 1.92%
* NMSE for 7Li ANN training data: 3.73%; for testing data: 6.48%
* R-squared for 6Li ANN training data: 0.98; for testing data: 0.98
* R-squared for 7Li ANN training data: 0.97; for testing data: 0.95
* ANN network parameters: 2241 total (2144 weights, 97 biases); architecture: 1 input node, 64 nodes in hidden layer 1, 32 nodes in hidden layer 2, 1 output node
* Maximum training iterations: 5000; initial learning rate: 0.0001

## Context

This work extends the method of Canto et al. (J. Phys. G, 2009) for quantifying breakup effects on complete fusion by comparing experimental fusion functions with the Universal Fusion Function, but replaces the traditional coupled-channels approach with machine learning regression. The ANN approach is contrasted with three other machine learning methods (Support Vector Regression, Random Forest Regression, Gaussian Process Regression) and is shown to give the lowest testing NMSE for 6Li (1.92%) and competitive performance for 7Li. The results suggest that machine learning can provide fusion suppression estimates without requiring explicit modeling of breakup coupling mechanisms, potentially enabling rapid predictions for weakly bound projectiles beyond 6,7Li.
