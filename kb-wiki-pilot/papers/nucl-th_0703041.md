# Relating breakup and incomplete fusion of weakly-bound nuclei through a classical trajectory model with stochastic breakup

> A classical dynamical model that treats break-up stochastically is presented for low energy reactions of weakly-bound nuclei. The three-dimensional model allows a consistent calculation of breakup, incomplete and complete fusion cross sections. The model is assessed by comparing the breakup observables with CDCC quantum mechanical predictions, which are found to be in reasonable agreement. Through the model, it is demonstrated that the breakup probability of the projectile as a function of its distance from the target is of primary importance for understanding complete and incomplete fusion at energies near the Coulomb barrier.

# Relating breakup and incomplete fusion of weakly-bound nuclei through a classical trajectory model with stochastic breakup

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the breakup probability of a weakly-bound projectile as a function of its distance from the target, which can be determined from sub-barrier breakup data or CDCC calculations, is of primary importance for understanding complete and incomplete fusion at energies near the Coulomb barrier. The model provides the first quantitative support for the suggested linkage between breakup observed below the barrier and suppression of complete fusion observed at energies above the barrier, though the specific breakup probability function must be determined empirically or from CDCC for each projectile-target combination.

## Method

A three-dimensional classical dynamical model was developed that treats projectile breakup stochastically by sampling a local breakup probability function along classical trajectories. For each projectile angular momentum, the breakup position is Monte Carlo sampled from an exponential distribution, and after breakup the fragments are propagated classically under real central two-body potentials to determine whether zero, one, or two fragments are captured by the target. The model was validated by comparing its no-capture breakup observables with fully quantum mechanical CDCC calculations for a simplified test case of a pseudo-8Be projectile (two alpha particles bound by 0.5 MeV) incident on a 208Pb target at 45 MeV laboratory energy.

## Key numbers

- For the pseudo-8Be + 208Pb test case at 45 MeV: CDCC total absorption cross section = 327 mb, classical model total absorption = 300 mb; CDCC no-capture breakup = 97 mb, classical model no-capture breakup = 93 mb
- The CDCC-derived breakup probability function parameters: A = 1.38, alpha = 0.293 fm^-1
- The empirical breakup probability function from 9Be + 208Pb sub-barrier data: A = 1.68 x 10^4, alpha = 0.922 fm^-1
- For 9Be + 208Pb at energies above the fusion barrier, complete fusion yields were found to be only approximately 2/3 of those expected, with the remaining 1/3 being in incomplete fusion products
- The Coulomb barrier for head-on collisions of the pseudo-8Be projectile with 208Pb: height V_B^PT at separation R_B^PT; the alpha-target fusion radius R_B^alphaT = 10.6 fm
- The classical model calculations used projectile angular momenta up to L_0 = 100 hbar, with breakup sampling using l_max = 4 hbar and epsilon_max = 6 MeV

## Context

This work extends classical trajectory approaches to nuclear reactions, building on a two-dimensional model by Hagino et al. (Nucl. Phys. A 738, 475, 2004) while introducing three-dimensional dynamics and stochastic breakup sampling. It addresses a limitation of quantum mechanical few-body approaches such as CDCC (Hagino et al., Phys. Rev. C 61, 037602, 2000; Diaz-Torres et al., Phys. Rev. C 65, 024606, 2002) and time-dependent wave packet methods (Yabana et al., Nucl. Phys. A 738, 303, 2004), which cannot separate complete and incomplete fusion contributions from their absorptive cross sections. The model enables quantitative predictions of complete fusion, incomplete fusion, and no-capture breakup yields for weakly-bound projectiles, and can be used to interpret future fusion experiments with radioactive nuclei and applications to gamma-ray spectroscopy.
