# Radiative capture cross sections: challenges and solutions

> Radiative capture reactions are one of the main inputs in stellar modelling. In numerous situations, the low energies at which these reactions occur in stars are not accessible with present experimental techniques. Electron screening is one of the major causes of problems in separating the bare cross sections from the screened ones. Indirect experimental techniques have been proposed and are now one of the main tools to obtain these cross sections. I discuss the electron screening problem and the latest progresses obtained with indirect methods.

## Key claim
Indirect experimental techniques (Trojan-Horse, asymptotic normalization coefficients, Coulomb dissociation) can extract low-energy radiative capture cross sections free from the electron screening problem that plagues direct measurements, but the fundamental discrepancy between measured and theoretically predicted laboratory screening energies remains unresolved and may even challenge basic principles like the expected contribution of nuclear recoil to stopping power.

## Method
The paper reviews the electron screening problem in low-energy nuclear reactions, where bound atomic electrons enhance fusion cross sections by reducing Coulomb repulsion. It then describes three indirect methods: the Trojan-Horse method, which uses a breakup reaction to induce the capture process at vanishing relative energy; the asymptotic normalization coefficient (ANC) technique, which extracts the normalization of the tail of the nuclear overlap wavefunction from peripheral transfer reactions; and Coulomb dissociation, where the breakup cross section of a projectile in the Coulomb field of a heavy target is related via time reversal to the radiative capture cross section.

## Key numbers
- The reaction $^{7}$Be(p,$\gamma$)$^{8}$B has an S-factor at low energies uncertain by as much as 30% according to Barker (1980), but recent Coulomb dissociation measurements yield $S_{17}(0) = 19.0$ eV.b, compatible with solar model calculations.
- The $^{12}$C($\alpha$,$\gamma$)$^{16}$O reaction should be known to better than 20% for good stellar modelling, a goal not yet achieved.
- Plasma screening enhances reaction rates like $^{3}$He($^{3}$He,2p)$^{4}$He and $^{7}$Be(p,$\gamma$)$^{8}$B by as much as 20% according to the Debye-Hückel/Salpeter approach.
- The stopping power for proton+hydrogen collisions is proportional to $v^{\alpha}$ with $\alpha = 1.35$ in a coupled-channels calculation, while Andersen-Ziegler tables predict a larger $\alpha$.
- For protons on helium targets in the energy range of 4 keV, Golser and Semrad found stopping power proportional to $v^{3.34}$.
- The Trojan-Horse method applied to $^{7}$Li(p,$\alpha)\alpha$ yields an S-factor free of the screening problem, in contrast to direct measurements which require assuming a screening energy of 350 eV.

## Context
This review extends the long-standing discussion of the electron screening puzzle in low-energy nuclear astrophysics, where experimental screening energies systematically disagree by a factor of two with adiabatic theory (Assenbaum et al. 1987, Engstler et al. 1988, Rolfs 2001). The paper connects this to the stopping power problem, where theoretical calculations (Bertulani and de Paula 2000, 2003) predict lower stopping than extrapolations from Andersen-Ziegler tables, and where nuclear recoil appears to "disappear" from experimental data (Formicola et al. 2000). The indirect methods described enable measurements of key reactions like $^{7}$Be(p,$\gamma$)$^{8}$B and $^{13}$N(p,$\gamma$)$^{15}$O without screening corrections, building on proposals by Baur (1986), Mukhamedzhanov and Timofeyuk (1990), and Baur, Bertulani and Rebel (1986).
