# Fusion and Breakup of Halo Nuclei

> We discuss the effect of the coupling to the break-up channel on the total fusion involving a halo nucleus with a heavy target. We show that there is a competition between the hindrance arising from this coupling, mostly at above barrier energies, and the enhancement which ensues at sub-barrier energies owing to the static effect of the extended matter distribution and the coupling to the soft modes.

# Fusion and Breakup of Halo Nuclei

## Key claim
The coupling to the break-up channel in fusion reactions involving halo nuclei with heavy targets produces a competition between hindrance at above-barrier energies and enhancement at sub-barrier energies, where the latter arises from the static effect of the extended matter distribution and coupling to soft (pygmy) resonances, not from break-up itself.

## Method
The authors develop a model for complete fusion cross-section expressed as a sum over partial waves of the product of a fusion transmission coefficient and a break-up survival probability, derived from general reaction theory. They review experimental data from four measurements on fusion of 11Be and 6He with heavy targets (209Bi and 238U) at near-barrier energies, and critically assess theoretical approaches including Continuum Discretized Coupled Channels (CDCC) calculations and the dynamic polarization potential (DPP) formalism.

## Key numbers
- Break-up threshold for halo nuclei is less than 1 MeV
- For 9Be+208Pb at near-barrier energies, the one-dimensional barrier calculation must be multiplied by 0.68 to account for observed complete fusion suppression
- Incomplete fusion probability for 9Be+208Pb is 0.32 ± 0.07
- For 7Li+209Bi at near-barrier energies, the break-up survival probability effect is 75% reduction in complete fusion
- For 7Li+12C, the predicted reduction is negligible (factor 0.99989) due to the (Z_C/Z_Bi)^2 ≈ 0.005 scaling of the Coulomb DPP

## Context
This review extends earlier work by the authors (Hussein et al., 1992; Canto et al., 1995) on fusion of weakly bound nuclei, and addresses the debate between their model and that of Dasso and Vitturi (1994), who initially found break-up leads to enhancement. The paper reconciles these views by noting that later calculations by Hagino et al. (2000) and Diaz-Torres and Thompson (2002) using CDCC confirm the hindrance at above-barrier energies. The work enables improved treatment of incomplete fusion cross-section calculations using few-body formalisms, and identifies the need for statistical treatment of continuum coupling beyond average CDCC methods.
