# Exposing Novel Quark and Gluon Effects in Nuclei

> The fundamental theory of the strong interaction -- quantum chromodynamics (QCD) -- provides the foundational framework with which to describe and understand the key properties of atomic nuclei. A deep understanding of the explicit role of quarks and gluons in nuclei remains elusive however, as these effects have thus far been well-disguised by confinement effects in QCD which are encapsulated by a successful description in terms of effective hadronic degrees of freedom. The observation of the EMC effect has provided an enduring indication for explicit QCD effects in nuclei, and points to the medium modification of the bound protons and neutrons in the nuclear medium. Understanding the EMC effect is a major challenge for modern nuclear physics, and several key questions remain, such as understanding its flavor, spin, and momentum dependence. This manuscript provides a contemporary snapshot of our understanding of the role of QCD in nuclei and outlines possible pathways in experiment and theory that will help deepen our understanding of nuclei in the context of QCD.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while the EMC effect provides enduring evidence for quark and gluon effects in nuclei beyond the traditional nucleon-meson picture, a complete understanding requires disentangling medium modification of bound nucleons from confinement effects that are well-described by effective hadronic degrees of freedom, with the caveat that several key questions about flavor, spin, and momentum dependence remain unresolved.

## Method

The authors synthesize the current experimental and theoretical status of nuclear QCD studies, reviewing inclusive deep inelastic scattering (DIS) measurements of the EMC effect, nuclear parton distribution function (PDF) fits, Drell-Yan experiments on nuclear targets, spectator tagging in deuteron DIS, deeply virtual Compton scattering (DVCS) on light nuclei, and short-range correlation (SRC) studies. They evaluate existing data from JLab 6 GeV experiments, HERMES, E772, E866, NA3, and NA10, and outline future experimental programs at JLab 12 GeV and a proposed electron-ion collider (EIC), alongside theoretical developments in light-front methods, generalized parton distributions (GPDs), and covariant models such as the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) approach.

## Key numbers

- The minimum Q^2 value for observing the x>1 plateau in A/D cross-section ratios is taken to be about 1.4 GeV^2, illustrated with data from Ref. [Egiyan:2003vg]
- High-momentum nucleons in SRC studies are defined as those with momenta greater than or equal to 300 MeV/c, about a few tens of MeV above the Fermi momentum for nuclei with A>12
- The only existing measurement of the tensor structure function b_1 on the deuteron comes from HERMES [Airapetian:2005cb] and cannot be explained by conventional deuteron convolution models
- Drell-Yan data from E772 [Alde:1990im] show no visible nuclear effects in the anti-shadowing domain, while both E772 and E866 [Vasilev:1999fa] are compatible with DIS data in the onset of the shadowing region
- Pion beam momenta between 100 and 200 GeV/c are noted as available at CERN for proposed Drell-Yan experiments

## Context

This paper extends the long-standing investigation of the EMC effect first observed in DIS experiments, which suggested that a nucleon-meson based picture of the nucleus is incomplete. It builds on prior work including nuclear PDF fits by Paakkinen et al. [Paakkinen:2016wxk], the NJL model calculations of Cloet et al. [Cloet:2009qs, Cloet:2012td] predicting flavor-dependent EMC effects, and SRC studies from JLab experiments [Egiyan:2003vg, Fomin:2011ng]. The work enables future experimental programs including JLab experiments E12-10-008 (EMC effect), E12-06-105 (x>1), E12-10-103 (MARATHON), and E12-14-011 (triton SRCs), as well as the ALERT detector project for nuclear DVCS and potential EIC measurements for lower-x nuclear structure studies.
