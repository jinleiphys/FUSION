# An One Dimensional Adiabatic Model for Fusion Involving Loosely Bound and Halo Nuclei with Heavy Targets

> An one dimensional adiabatic model has been proposed for fusion of loosely bound and halo light nuclei with various heavy targets. It is shown that fusion cross sections at near and sub-barrier energies can be explained using a simple WKB tunneling through an adiabatic barrier without invoking breakup coupling explicitly. The model has been applied successfully to explain fusion cross sections for several systems including recently measured $^{6}Li+^{198}Pt$ system (Phy. Rev. Lett. 103, 232702, 2009) where data exists well above and below the barrier and $^{8}He+^{197}Au$ system (Phy. Rev. Lett. 103, 232701, 2009) where $^{8}He$ is highly neutron rich. Interestingly, the fusion of stable $^{4}He+^{197}Au$ system can not be explained on the basis of this adiabatic model as it requires normal tunneling through the sudden barrier. The requirement of adiabatic potential for the loosely bound and halo nuclei is linked to the fact that for such systems neutron flow leading to neck formation is initiated at a larger distance which modifies the sudden potential.

# An One Dimensional Adiabatic Model for Fusion Involving Loosely Bound and Halo Nuclei with Heavy Targets

## Key claim
Fusion cross sections for loosely bound and halo light nuclei with heavy targets at near- and sub-barrier energies can be explained by a simple WKB tunneling through an adiabatic barrier without explicitly invoking breakup coupling, provided that the adiabatic potential is constructed by adding a neck-formation correction to the sudden potential. The model fails for stable systems like $^{4}He+^{197}Au$, which require normal tunneling through the sudden barrier.

## Method
The author constructs an adiabatic potential by adding a neck-formation energy term to the standard sudden ion-ion nuclear potential (Akuyz-Winther parameterization). The neck is modeled macroscopically as a cylinder connecting the two nuclei, with the neck potential evaluated at its minimum for center-to-center distances less than an adiabatic parameter $R_a$ (typically near the Coulomb barrier radius). Fusion cross sections are then computed using the WKB approximation for sub-barrier energies and the Hill-Wheeler expression for above-barrier energies, with a constant suppression factor applied to match above-barrier data.

## Key numbers
- For $^{6}Li+^{198}Pt$: adiabatic parameter $R_a = 11.0$ fm (Coulomb barrier radius $R_b = 11.2$ fm), suppression factor $f = 0.56$ (compared to $f = 0.58$ for sudden potential), barrier height $V_b = 28.4$ MeV, $\hbar\omega = 5.0$ MeV
- For $^{8}He+^{197}Au$: $R_a = 11.5$ fm ($R_b = 11.5$ fm), $f = 0.71$ (sudden $f = 0.80$), $V_b = 18.7$ MeV, $\hbar\omega = 3.5$ MeV
- For $^{4}He+^{197}Au$: no adiabatic correction needed, $f = 1.0$, $V_b = 19.8$ MeV, $R_b = 10.8$ fm, $\hbar\omega = 5.2$ MeV
- Suppression factors for other systems range from $f = 0.56$ to $0.80$ (adiabatic) and $0.58$ to $0.85$ (sudden)
- Above-barrier suppression is typically $10\%$ to $30\%$ relative to BPM predictions

## Context
This work extends the barrier penetration model (BPM) approach to fusion of loosely bound and halo nuclei, where standard coupled-channel calculations (including CDCC with breakup coupling) explain sub-barrier enhancement but require an energy-independent suppression factor of $10\%$ to $30\%$ to match above-barrier data (Canto et al., Nucl. Phys. A821, 51, 2009; Keeley et al., Prog. Part. Nucl. Phys. 59, 579, 2007). The model successfully reproduces recent high-precision data for $^{6}Li+^{198}Pt$ (Srivastava et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 103, 232702, 2009) and $^{8}He+^{197}Au$ (Lemasson et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 103, 232701, 2009), and suggests that the basic ion-ion potential becomes adiabatic for halo and loosely bound systems due to neutron flow initiating neck formation at larger distances. The approach enables future coupled-channel calculations to use an adiabatic bare potential rather than a sudden one, though above-barrier data would still require a suppression factor.
