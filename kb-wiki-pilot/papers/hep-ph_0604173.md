# Proton and anti-proton production in the forward region of d+Au collisions at RHIC from the color glass condensate

> The power-law tail of high-pt pi^\pm spectra observed in forward d+Au collisions at RHIC can be attributed to the power-law decrease of the dipole forward scattering amplitude appearing in the color glass condensate (CGC) approach. Forward particle production probes the small-x gluon distribution of the target nucleus where its anomalous dimension is rather flat (gamma = 0.6 \sim 0.8) for moderately high pt (\lsim 5 GeV), and where the leading-twist DGLAP approximation is not valid. In the same framework, we examine p and \bar{p} production using baryon fragmentation functions parameterized in the Lund fragmentation scheme. This provides a good description of the forward \bar{p} spectrum while it underestimates the p data by as much as a factor of 2 \sim 3 at pt \lsim 4 GeV. Part of this anomalous baryon excess can be attributed to surviving constituent diquarks from the deuteron projectile. Thus, the contribution from diquark scattering may play an essential role for forward baryon formation.

## Key claim

The CGC formalism with standard parton distribution functions and Lund fragmentation functions describes forward \(\bar{p}\) spectra well but underestimates forward \(p\) data by a factor of \(2\sim 3\) at \(p_t \lesssim 4\) GeV; part of this anomalous baryon excess can be attributed to surviving constituent diquarks from the deuteron projectile, suggesting diquark scattering may play an essential role for forward baryon formation.

## Method

The authors used a collinearly factorized CGC formalism with asymmetric DGLAP\(_\text{proj}\) \(\otimes\) BFKL\(_\text{targ}\) factorization, including mass corrections, to compute single-inclusive hadron production cross sections in d+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=200\) GeV and forward rapidity \(y_h=3.0\). They employed the KKT model for the dipole forward scattering amplitude with an anomalous dimension parameterization, LO CTEQ5 parton distribution functions, and baryon fragmentation functions from the Lund string model (JETSET) rescaled to match the \(Q^2\)-dependence of KKP or AKK fragmentation functions. For diquark contributions, they used the Stockholm diquark model PDFs with a dipole form-factor and modeled diquark fragmentation into hadrons using the Kretzer LO fragmentation functions multiplied by the breakup probability.

## Key numbers

- The CGC formalism with standard PDFs and Lund FFs underestimates forward proton data by a factor of \(2\sim 3\) at \(p_t \lesssim 4\) GeV
- The anomalous dimension \(\gamma\) of the target gluon distribution is \(\gamma = 0.6 \sim 0.8\) for moderately high \(p_t\) (\(\lesssim 5\) GeV)
- The saturation scale is given by \(Q_s^2(y_A,b=0) = A^{1/3} Q_0^2 (x_0/x_A)^\lambda\) with \(Q_0 \simeq 1\) GeV, \(\lambda \simeq 0.3\), and \(x_0 \simeq 3.0 \times 10^{-4}\)
- The diquark form-factor uses \(M^2 = 10\) GeV\(^2\), corresponding to a diquark radius of about 0.2 fm
- The \(p/\pi^+\) ratio at forward rapidity is \(\sim 0.8\) in the range \(1 < p_t \lesssim 3\) GeV
- The proton cross section exceeds that of anti-protons by one order of magnitude for \(1.5 \lesssim p_t \lesssim 3.5\) GeV

## Context

This work extends the CGC formalism previously applied to \(\pi^\pm\) production in forward d+Au collisions at RHIC (Refs. [aaj, aaj2]) to baryon production, addressing the long-standing baryon excess problem observed in p(d)+A collisions at Fermilab (\(\sqrt{s}=27.4, 38.8\) GeV) and RHIC (\(\sqrt{s}=200\) GeV) across central and forward rapidities. The results contradict the expectation from the black-body limit (Ref. [DGS]) that forward baryon number should be strongly suppressed, and instead support the diquark picture (Ref. [diquark_pict]) that successfully described FNAL data near midrapidity. The findings enable quantitative studies of the transition from string-breaking to projectile-fragmentation regimes and the role of saturation physics in baryon production, with the diquark component providing a mechanism to explain the large \(p/\pi^+\) ratio that Pythia simulations underpredict by a factor of \(2\sim 3\).
