# Continuum discretization methods in a composite-particle scattering off a nucleus: the benchmark calculations

> The direct comparison of two different continuum discretization methods towards the solution of a composite particle scattering off a nucleus is presented. The first approach -- the Continumm-Discretized Coupled Channel method -- is based on the differential equation formalism, while the second one -- the Wave-Packet Continuum Discretization method -- uses the integral equation formulation for the composite-particle scattering problem. As benchmark calculations we have chosen the deuteron off \nuc{58}{Ni} target scattering (as a realistic illustrative example) at three different incident energies: high, middle and low. Clear non-vanishing effects of closed inelastic channels at small and intermediate energies are established. The elastic cross sections found in both approaches are very close to each other for all three considered energies.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Wave-Packet Continuum Discretization (WPCD) method and the Continuum-Discretized Coupled Channel (CDCC) method produce nearly identical elastic cross sections for deuteron scattering off a nickel target at high, intermediate, and low incident energies, but only when closed inelastic channels are included in both approaches at the lower energies. The abstract's claim of "very close" agreement glosses over the condition that at intermediate and low energies, closed channels must be incorporated into the CDCC framework using a numerically stable R-matrix method rather than the standard Numerov integration.

## Method

The authors compared two continuum discretization approaches for solving the three-body scattering problem of a deuteron (treated as a neutron-proton composite) off a ⁵⁸Ni target. The CDCC method solves coupled differential equations for the projectile center-of-mass motion using a basis of averaged internal wave functions (momentum bins), while the WPCD method solves a finite-dimensional Lippmann-Schwinger equation using stationary wave packets constructed from energy bins for both the internal and center-of-mass coordinates. For the CDCC calculations at lower energies where closed channels become important, the coupled equations were solved using an R-matrix method instead of the standard Numerov integration to avoid numerical instabilities from exponentially rising asymptotic solutions.

## Key numbers

- Three incident energies studied: \(E_{\rm lab}=80\) MeV (high), 21.6 MeV (intermediate), and 12 MeV (low)
- At 80 MeV: continuum truncated at \(E_{\rm max}=70\) MeV with \(N=20\) bins (CDCC); WPCD used \(N=13\) basis functions for n-p motion and \(M=500\) for c.m. motion, with \(L_{\rm max}=45\) partial waves
- At 21.6 MeV: CDCC required \(N_{\rm open}\simeq 20\) and \(N_{\rm closed}\simeq 15\) bins with \(E_{\rm max}\sim 140\) MeV; WPCD used \(N=15\) basis functions and \(M=150\), with \(L_{\rm max}=25\) partial waves; converged results required \(E_{\rm max}\sim 100\) MeV for closed channels
- At 12 MeV: closed channel effects are even more significant than at 21.6 MeV
- n-p interaction: Gaussian form with \(V_0=66.99\) MeV and \(\beta=0.415\) fm\(^{-2}\)
- Masses used: \(m_n=1.0087\) amu, \(m_p=1.0078\) amu, \(m_{\rm Ni}=57.9353\) amu

## Context

This work extends previous benchmark studies of deuteron scattering off ⁵⁸Ni, which had compared CDCC and Faddeev approaches (showing good agreement in a recent work by Deltuva), by providing a direct comparison between CDCC and the alternative WPCD integral equation method. The study contradicts the common practice in conventional CDCC calculations of neglecting closed channels (as done in references J, R1, A, R2, F, L, C1, C2, P, C4), demonstrating that at intermediate and low energies these contributions are essential and can be incorporated using an R-matrix CDCC formulation (reference Th2). The results enable future applications of the WPCD method to more complex few-body scattering problems, including the possibility of extending the wave-packet technique to solve general Faddeev equations.
