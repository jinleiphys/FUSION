# Prospects for quarkonium studies at the high-luminosity LHC

> Prospects for quarkonium-production studies accessible during the upcoming high-luminosity phases of the CERN Large Hadron Collider operation after 2021 are reviewed. Current experimental and theoretical open issues in the field are assessed together with the potential for future studies in quarkonium-related physics. This will be possible through the exploitation of the huge data samples to be collected in proton-proton, proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions, both in the collider and fixed-target modes. Such investigations include, among others, those of: (i) J/psi and Upsilon produced in association with other hard particles; (ii) chi(c,b) and eta(c,b) down to small transverse momenta; (iii) the constraints brought in by quarkonia on gluon PDFs, nuclear PDFs, TMDs, GPDs and GTMDs, as well as on the low-x parton dynamics; (iv) the gluon Sivers effect in polarised-nucleon collisions; (v) the properties of the quark-gluon plasma produced in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions and of collective partonic effects in general; and (vi) double and triple parton scatterings.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the high-luminosity LHC will enable precision quarkonium measurements that can discriminate between competing theoretical models of in-medium heavy-quark dynamics, but it explicitly notes that the projected integrated luminosities listed for heavy-ion runs are indicative and have not yet been endorsed by the experiments, the LHC, or CERN.

## Method

The authors review the current experimental and theoretical status of quarkonium production in proton-proton, proton-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus collisions, then project the reach of future measurements using expected HL-LHC data samples. They evaluate specific observables including nuclear modification factors (R_AA), elliptic flow (v_2), and excited-to-ground-state yield ratios, comparing them against predictions from semi-classical transport models, open quantum system formalisms, potential NRQCD, and SCET_G-based effective field theories.

## Key numbers

- Binding energies of quarkonium states in vacuum range from 0.05 GeV (psi(2S)) to 1.16 GeV (eta_b), with the QGP peak temperature in PbPb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=2.76 TeV estimated between 0.385 and 0.740 GeV
- Projected integrated luminosities for PbPb runs: 2.3 inverse nb (2021), 3.9 inverse nb (2022), 3.8 inverse nb (2027), 3 inverse nb (2029)
- For the fixed-target mode, LHCb SMOG2 expects 20 inverse nb in one year of PbAr collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=72 GeV, nearly 100 times larger than recorded PbPb at 5 TeV
- The ratio R_AA(chi_b(1P))/R_AA(Upsilon(2S)) is predicted to be about 1/3 in central collisions with correlated recombination, versus larger than unity without it
- Energy-loss calculations overpredict J/psi suppression by a factor of 2 to 3 in the pT > 10 GeV range compared to ATLAS data
- The double ratio R_AA(psi(2S))/R_AA(J/psi) from energy-loss models gives approximately 1.1, while CMS data show psi(2S) suppression 2 to 3 times larger than J/psi

## Context

This review extends the CERN Yellow Report (Working Group 5) prospects for heavy-ion physics at the HL-LHC, building on ten years of LHC Run-1 and Run-2 quarkonium measurements from ALICE, ATLAS, CMS, and LHCb. It addresses open questions in sequential quarkonium suppression, regeneration, and the gluon Sivers effect, and enables future discrimination between transport models (Du et al., Krouppa et al.), statistical hadronisation (Andronic et al.), and EFT-based approaches (Makris et al., Vitev et al.) using high-precision data on excited states like chi_b(1P) and Upsilon(3S).
