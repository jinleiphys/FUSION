# The puzzle of complete fusion suppression in weakly-bound nuclei: a Trojan Horse effect?

> Experimental studies of nuclear collisions involving light weakly-bound nuclei show a systematic suppression of the complete fusion cross section by $\sim$30\% with respect to the expectation for tightly bound nuclei, at energies above the Coulomb barrier. Although it is widely accepted that the phenomenon is related to the weak binding of these nuclei, the origin of this suppression is not fully understood. In here, we present a novel approach that provides the complete fusion for weakly bound nuclei and relates its suppression to the competition between the different mechanisms contributing to the reaction cross section. The method is applied to the $^{6,7}$Li+$^{209}$Bi reactions, where we find that the suppression of complete fusion is mostly caused by the flux associated with non-elastic breakup modes, such as the partial capture of the projectile (incomplete fusion), whereas the elastic breakup mode is found to play a minor role. Finally, we demonstrate that the large $α$ yields observed in these reactions can be naturally explained as a consequence of a {\it Trojan Horse} mechanism.

## Key claim
The authors propose that complete fusion suppression in weakly-bound nuclei (observed as ~30% reduction compared to tightly-bound expectations) is primarily caused by non-elastic breakup modes (such as incomplete fusion) rather than elastic breakup, and that this suppression can be explained through a Trojan Horse mechanism without requiring the commonly assumed two-step breakup-fusion scenario.

## Method
The authors decompose the reaction cross section into complete fusion, inelastic scattering, elastic breakup (EBU), and non-elastic breakup (NEB) components for a two-body projectile model. They compute EBU using the continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) method and NEB using the Ichimura-Austern-Vincent (IAV) inclusive breakup model in its DWBA approximation, then extract complete fusion cross sections by subtraction from the reaction cross section. The method is applied to the $^{6,7}$Li+$^{209}$Bi reactions at energies around and above the Coulomb barrier.

## Key numbers
- Complete fusion suppression of ~30% for $^{6}$Li+$^{209}$Bi and ~25% for $^{7}$Li+$^{209}$Bi relative to single-barrier penetration model expectations
- Coulomb barrier for these systems is approximately $V_b \approx 30$ MeV
- The $d$+$^{209}$Bi and $t$+$^{209}$Bi Coulomb barriers are approximately 10-11 MeV
- For $^{6}$Li at 36 MeV (1.2$V_b$) and $^{7}$Li at 44 MeV (1.5$V_b$), as separation energy increases to ~3-4 MeV, EBU becomes negligible while NEB decreases more slowly
- The $\alpha$ NEB yield at the Coulomb barrier energy is about 5 times larger than EBU for $^{6}$Li and about 4 times larger for $^{7}$Li

## Context
This work extends previous experimental observations of complete fusion suppression by Dasgupta et al. (2002, 2004) and addresses the failure of classical dynamical models (Cook et al. 2016, 2018) and coupled-channels calculations to explain the full suppression magnitude. The authors demonstrate that the Trojan Horse mechanism, originally proposed by Baur (1986) for nuclear astrophysics applications, naturally explains the large $\alpha$ yields without requiring the two-step breakup-fusion picture. This supports the conjecture by Cook et al. (2018) that clustering and weak-binding, rather than breakup in the usual sense, cause the suppression.
