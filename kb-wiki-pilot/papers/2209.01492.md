# First Observation of Large Missing-Momentum (e,e'p) Cross-Section Scaling and the onset of Correlated-Pair Dominance in Nuclei

> We report the first measurement of $x_B$-scaling in $(e,e'p)$ cross-section ratios off nuclei relative to deuterium at large missing-momentum of $350 \leq p_{miss} \leq 600$ MeV/c. The observed scaling extends over a kinematic range of $0.7 \leq x_B \leq 1.8$, which is significantly wider than $1.4 \leq x_B \leq 1.8$ previously observed for inclusive $(e,e')$ cross-section ratios. The $x_B$-integrated cross-section ratios become constant (i.e., scale) beginning at $p_{miss}\approx k_F$, the nuclear Fermi momentum. Comparing with theoretical calculations we find good agreement with Generalized Contact Formalism calculations for high missing-momentum ($> 375$ MeV/c), suggesting the observed scaling results from interacting with nucleons in short-range correlated (SRC) pairs. For low missing-momenta, mean-field calculations show good agreement with the data for $p_{miss}\le k_F$, and suggest that contributions to the measured cross-section ratios from scattering off single, un-correlated, nucleons are non-negligible up to $p_{miss}\approx 350$ MeV/c. Therefore, SRCs become dominant in nuclei at $p_{miss}\approx 350$ MeV/c, well above the nuclear Fermi Surface of $k_F \approx 250$ MeV/c.

## Key claim
The paper reports the first observation of $x_B$-scaling in semi-inclusive $(e,e'p)$ cross-section ratios off nuclei relative to deuterium at large missing momentum, extending the scaling region down to $x_B = 0.7$ from the previously observed $x_B \geq 1.4$ in inclusive $(e,e')$ reactions. However, the onset of full dominance by short-range correlated (SRC) pairs is not simply at the Fermi momentum ($k_F \approx 250$ MeV/c); after accounting for residual mean-field contributions, SRCs become dominant only at a higher missing momentum of $p_{miss} \approx 350$ MeV/c.

## Method
The experiment used a 5.01 GeV electron beam at Jefferson Lab with the CLAS detector to measure the $(e,e'p)$ reaction on deuterium, carbon, aluminum, iron, and lead targets. Events were selected with $Q^2 \ge 1.5$ (GeV/c)$^2$, $350 \le p_{miss} \le 600$ MeV/c, and the knocked-out proton emitted within $25^\circ$ of the momentum transfer, with further cuts on missing mass and angular distributions to suppress inelastic events. The per-nucleon cross-section ratios for each nucleus relative to deuterium were then extracted as a function of $x_B$ and $p_{miss}$ and compared to Generalized Contact Formalism (GCF) calculations for SRC pairs and various mean-field calculations for single-nucleon states.

## Key numbers
- The observed $x_B$-scaling in $(e,e'p)$ cross-section ratios extends over a kinematic range of $0.7 \leq x_B \leq 1.8$, wider than the $1.4 \leq x_B \leq 1.8$ range previously observed for inclusive $(e,e')$ cross-section ratios.
- The $x_B$-integrated cross-section ratios become constant (scale) beginning at $p_{miss} \approx k_F$, the nuclear Fermi momentum ($k_F \approx 220-260$ MeV/c for the nuclei studied).
- Good agreement with Generalized Contact Formalism (GCF) calculations is found for high missing-momentum ($> 375$ MeV/c).
- Mean-field calculations show good agreement with data for $p_{miss} \le k_F$, and suggest contributions from single, un-correlated nucleons are non-negligible up to $p_{miss} \approx 350$ MeV/c.
- The fitted width of the SRC pair center-of-mass momentum distribution, $\sigma_{CM}$, was found to be $160-210$ MeV/c at 68% confidence, agreeing with previous direct measurements.

## Context
This work extends previous inclusive $(e,e')$ scaling measurements (e.g., by Schmookler et al. [2019]) by using the semi-inclusive $(e,e'p)$ reaction to directly tag the knocked-out proton, providing a cleaner probe of short-range correlations (SRCs) over a much broader $x_B$ range. The results confirm the universality of SRC pairs across nuclei and provide a benchmark for theoretical models, showing that while the transition to SRC dominance begins near the Fermi momentum, full dominance requires a higher missing momentum, consistent with the existence of a nuclear Fermi surface that accounts for long-range correlations. This enables future detailed theoretical calculations to quantify the transition region from mean-field to SRC dominance in heavy nuclei.
