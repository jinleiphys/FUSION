# Initial State Dependence of the Breakup of Weakly Bound Carbon Isotopes

> The one-neutron nuclear breakup from the Carbon isotopes $^{19}$C and $^{17}$C, is calculated as an example of application of the theory of transfer to the continuum reactions in the formulation which includes spin coupling. The effect of the energy sharing between the parallel and transverse neutron momentum distributions is taken into account thus resulting in a theory which is more general than sudden eikonal approaches. Both effects are necessary to understand properly the breakup from not too weakly bound $l_i>1$ orbitals. Breakup which leaves the core into an excited state below particle threshold is also considered. The core-target interaction is treated in the smooth cut-off approximation. By comparing to presently available experimental data we show how to make some hypothesis on the quantum numbers and occupancy of the neutron initial state. Possible ambiguities in the interpretation of inclusive cross sections are discussed.

# Initial State Dependence of the Breakup of Weakly Bound Carbon Isotopes

## Key claim

The one-neutron nuclear breakup from $^{19}$C and $^{17}$C is calculated using transfer-to-continuum theory with spin coupling, showing that the reaction mechanism is more general than sudden eikonal approaches because it accounts for energy sharing between parallel and transverse neutron momentum distributions. The theory is necessary to properly understand breakup from not too weakly bound $l_i > 1$ orbitals, and the comparison to experimental data allows hypotheses on the quantum numbers and occupancy of the neutron initial state, though ambiguities in interpreting inclusive cross sections remain.

## Method

The transfer-to-continuum formalism treats the projectile-target relative motion semiclassically with constant velocity and impact parameter, while the neutron transition amplitude is treated quantum mechanically. The neutron breakup probability distribution includes spin coupling factors and an optical model S-matrix for the neutron-target interaction, separating elastic breakup (diffraction) from neutron absorption (stripping). The core-target interaction is treated in a smooth cut-off approximation using a parameterized core survival probability, and the cross section is obtained by integrating over impact parameters with spectroscopic factors for the neutron initial state.

## Key numbers

- For $^{19}$C on $^{9}$Be at 88 A.MeV: cross section $\sigma_{1n} = 150 \pm 40$ mb (MSU experiment), with parallel momentum distribution width $\Gamma_{exp} = 42 \pm 4$ MeV/c
- For $^{19}$C on $^{9}$Be at 910 A.MeV (GSI): $\sigma_{1n} = 233 \pm 51$ mb, width $\Gamma_{exp} = 69 \pm 3$ MeV/c
- For $^{19}$C on $^{12}$C at 67 A.MeV: nuclear elastic breakup cross section $82 \pm 14$ mb (RIKEN experiment)
- For $^{19}$C on $^{208}$Pb at 67 A.MeV: total exclusive breakup cross section $1.34 \pm 0.12$ b (RIKEN experiment)
- For $^{17}$C on $^{9}$Be at 84 A.MeV: $\sigma_{1n} = 60 \pm 20$ mb (MSU exclusive $\gamma$-ray experiment)
- Calculated widths for $^{19}$C d-orbital breakup: $\hbar\Delta k_1 = 142$ MeV/c at 20 A.MeV, $\hbar\Delta k_1 = 177$ MeV/c at 88 A.MeV
- Spectroscopic factor extracted for $^{19}$C: 0.6 for s-state ($0^+ \otimes 2s_{1/2}$) and 0.4 for d-state ($2^+ \otimes d_{5/2}$)
- For $^{17}$C: spectroscopic factors $0.16 \times (2^+ \otimes 2s_{1/2}) + 1.58 \times (2^+ \otimes d_{5/2})$ from literature

## Context

This work extends the transfer-to-continuum theory previously applied to halo breakup in $^{11}$Be (Bonaccorso and Brink, Phys. Rev. C 57, R22 1998; Phys. Rev. C 58, 2864 1998) to the more complex case of carbon isotopes where d-orbitals are important. It contrasts with sudden eikonal approaches used by several authors (Esbensen et al., Typel and Baur) which are best suited for incident energies above 150 A.MeV and very weak neutron binding with low angular momentum states. The paper addresses the ongoing debate about whether $^{19}$C exhibits a halo structure, with conflicting experimental results from MSU (77 and 88 A.MeV), RIKEN (Coulomb breakup), GSI (910 A.MeV), and GANIL (30 A.MeV). The results enable future $\gamma$-ray experiments to discriminate between core excited state contributions and suggest that measuring neutron transverse momentum distributions could help resolve ambiguities in determining initial state angular momenta.
