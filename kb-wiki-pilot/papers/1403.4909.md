# Big Bang nucleosynthesis revisited via Trojan Horse Method measurements

> Nuclear reaction rates are among the most important input for understanding the primordial nucleosynthesis and therefore for a quantitative description of the early Universe. An up-to-date compilation of direct cross sections of 2H(d,p)3H, 2H(d,n)3He, 7Li(p,alpha)4He and 3He(d,p)4He reactions is given. These are among the most uncertain cross sections used and input for Big Bang nucleosynthesis calculations. Their measurements through the Trojan Horse Method (THM) are also reviewed and compared with direct data. The reaction rates and the corresponding recommended errors in this work were used as input for primordial nucleosynthesis calculations to evaluate their impact on the 2H, 3,4He and 7Li primordial abundances, which are then compared with observations.

# Big Bang nucleosynthesis revisited via Trojan Horse Method measurements

## Key claim

The Trojan Horse Method (THM) measurements of four key BBN reactions (²H(d,p)³H, ²H(d,n)³He, ³He(d,p)⁴He, and ⁷Li(p,α)⁴He) yield bare-nucleus cross sections free from electron screening effects, but the resulting updated reaction rates produce deviations of up to 20% from previous compilations and do not resolve the longstanding discrepancy between predicted and observed ⁷Li abundances.

## Method

The authors compiled direct cross section data for the four reactions and performed new measurements using the Trojan Horse Method, which extracts the two-body astrophysical S-factor from a three-body quasi-free reaction (e.g., ²H(³He,pt)H for the d+d channels). They performed R-matrix fits to combined direct and THM data using the AZURE code, parametrized the resulting S-factors with polynomial plus Breit-Wigner functions, and calculated thermonuclear reaction rates via Maxwellian averaging. These rates were then input into a modified version of the standard BBN code to compute primordial abundances of ²H, ³He, ⁴He, and ⁷Li.

## Context

This work extends previous BBN reaction rate compilations including NACRE, Cyburt (2004), and Smith-Kawano-Malaney by incorporating THM measurements that avoid both Coulomb barrier suppression and electron screening effects that plague direct measurements at low energies. The THM has been applied previously to several astrophysical reactions by Spitaleri et al. and others. The results confirm that the "lithium problem" (factor of ~3 discrepancy between SBBN predictions and observed ⁷Li in halo stars) persists even with updated nuclear inputs, suggesting its origin lies elsewhere (e.g., stellar processes or non-standard BBN models). The methodology will be extended to the remaining reactions in the BBN network in a forthcoming paper.
