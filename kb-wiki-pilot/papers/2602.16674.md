# Comparison of Pauli projection and supersymetric transformation methods for three-body nuclear structure and reactions

> Three-body Faddeev-type equations for bound, resonant, and scattering states in the systems with a nuclear core and two nucleons are solved using the momentum-space framework. Two approaches for eliminating the Pauli-forbidden deeply-bound states are compared: projecting out those states by a nonlocal term in the potential, and by using a supersymmetric transformation of the potential. While the former method is preferred by the experimental data for the deuteron-${}^4${He} scattering, the results for bound and resonant states do not indicate a clear superiority of a single method. Instead, systematic differences between them are found.

## Key claim

While the abstract states that the Pauli projection (PP) method is preferred by experimental data for deuteron-4He scattering and that no single method is clearly superior for bound and resonant states, the paper actually finds that the supersymmetric (SS) transformation method systematically predicts larger binding energies for ground states and 2+ excited states, while the PP method yields larger binding for 0_2+ excited states; the preference for PP in scattering is specific to deuteron-4He reactions and does not extend to bound-state observables.

## Method

The author solves Faddeev-type equations for three-body bound, resonant, and scattering states in the momentum-space partial-wave representation for systems consisting of a nuclear core and two nucleons. Two approaches for eliminating Pauli-forbidden deeply-bound states are compared: (i) projecting out those states by adding a nonlocal term with strength Gamma_P (1-10 GeV) to the two-body potential, and (ii) applying a supersymmetric transformation that creates a repulsive r^{-2} term in the potential. The nucleon-nucleon interaction is the CD Bonn potential, and the nucleon-core potential is a standard Woods-Saxon form with spin-orbit term.

## Key numbers

- Deuteron-4He elastic scattering at 17 MeV: PP model reproduces experimental differential cross section and vector analyzing power data, while SS and RC models produce similar predictions that deviate from data
- 6Li ground-state binding energies (relative to core+nucleons threshold): PP = 3.48 MeV, SS = 3.66 MeV, RC = 3.65 MeV, experimental = 3.69 MeV
- 19B ground-state binding: PP = 0.185 MeV, SS = 0.383 MeV, experimental = 0.145 MeV (with ~0.4 MeV uncertainty)
- 11Li ground-state binding: PP = 0.296 MeV, SS = 0.855 MeV, experimental = 0.369 MeV
- 20C ground-state binding: PP = 2.675 MeV, SS = 3.644 MeV, experimental = 3.560 MeV
- 16C ground-state binding: PP = 4.342 MeV, SS = 5.299 MeV, experimental = 5.468 MeV
- 18O ground-state binding (L <= 2): PP = 11.458 MeV, SS = 12.773 MeV, experimental = 12.188 MeV
- 16Be(0+) resonance: PP E_R = 2.08 MeV, Gamma = 0.27 MeV; SS E_R = 1.69 MeV, Gamma = 0.25 MeV; experimental E_R = 0.84 MeV, Gamma = 0.32 MeV
- 16Be(2+) resonance: PP E_R = 2.91 MeV, Gamma = 0.49 MeV; SS E_R = 2.69 MeV, Gamma = 0.59 MeV; experimental E_R = 2.15 MeV, Gamma = 0.95 MeV
- n-19C scattering length (0+ channel): PP = 16.4 fm, SS = 26.6 fm
- n-15C scattering length (0+ channel): PP = 4.84 fm, SS = 7.74 fm

## Context

This work extends previous comparisons of Pauli projection and supersymmetric transformation methods by Hesse et al. (1999) for weakly bound halo nuclei (6He, 11Li, 14Be) and by Thompson et al. (2000) for 6He and 6Be, which found no significant differences for bound and resonant states but visible effects in nonresonant continuum. It also builds on Deltuva (2006) which compared PP and repulsive core (RC) models for deuteron-4He scattering, finding large differences favoring PP, but did not include the SS method. The present work resolves whether the RC failure was due to violation of phase equivalence by including the phase-equivalent SS transformation, and extends the comparison to a wider range of systems including 19B, 20C, 16C, 18O, and 16Be resonances. The results enable future studies to choose between PP and SS methods based on the specific observable of interest, and provide benchmark calculations for three-body forces and improved nucleon-nucleus potentials.
