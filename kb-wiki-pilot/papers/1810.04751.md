# Non-local interactions in the $(d,p)$ surrogate method for $(n,γ)$ reactions

> Single-neutron transfer reactions populating states in the continuum are interesting both for structure and astrophysics. In their description often global optical potentials are used for the nucleon-target interactions, and these interactions are typically local. In our work, we study the effects of nonlocality in $(d,p)$ reactions populating continuum states. This work is similar to that of [1] but now for transfer to the continuum. A theory for computing cross sections for inclusive processes $A (d,p) X$ was explored in [2]. Therein, local optical potentials were used to describe the nucleon-target effective interaction. The goal of the present work is to extend the theory developed in [2] to investigate the effects of including nonlocality in the effective interaction on the relevant reaction observables. We implement the R--matrix method to solve the non--local equations both for the nucleon wavefunctions and the propagator. We then apply the method to systematically study the inclusive process of $(d,p)$ on $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca, $^{48}$Ca and $^{208}$Pb at 10, 20 and 50 MeV. We compare the results obtained when non--local interactions are used with those obtained when local equivalent interactions are included. We find that nonlocality affects different pieces of the model in complex ways. Depending on the beam energy and the target, the non-elastic breakup can either increase or decrease. While the non-elastic transfer cross section for each final spin state can change considerably, the main prediction of the model [2], namely the shape of the spin distributions, remains largely unaltered by nonlocality. [1] L. J. Titus and F. M. Nunes. Testing the Perey effect. Phys. Rev. C, 89:034609, Mar 2014. [2] G. Potel, F. M. Nunes, and I. J. Thompson. Establishing a theory for deuteron-induced surrogate reactions. Phys. Rev. C, 92:034611, Sep 2015.

## Key claim
Nonlocality in nucleon-target interactions affects different components of the inclusive $(d,p)$ reaction model in complex ways, but the shape of the spin distributions, the main prediction of the surrogate method, remains largely unaltered, even though individual non-elastic transfer cross sections for each final spin state can change considerably.

## Method
The authors implemented an R-matrix method to solve the non-local Schrodinger equation for both nucleon wavefunctions and the Green's function propagator, providing three orders of magnitude improvement in computing time over previous iterative methods. They applied this to compute the non-elastic breakup (NEB) cross section for inclusive $(d,p)$ reactions on $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca, $^{48}$Ca and $^{208}$Pb at deuteron beam energies of 10, 20 and 50 MeV, using the Perey-Buck non-local optical potential and comparing with local equivalent potentials (LEP).

## Key numbers
- Effects of nonlocality on heavier targets can be as large as 85%
- For $^{40}$Ca$(d,p)$ at $E_d=50$ MeV, the total NEB cross section changes by +12.21% when nonlocality is included in both neutron and proton channels (from 19.58 mb/MeV with LEP to a value corresponding to that percentage change)
- For $^{208}$Pb$(d,p)$ at $E_d=20$ MeV, the NEB cross section at the peak shows significant partial-wave-dependent effects, with some partial waves changing by over 40%
- The R-matrix method used N=60 Lagrange basis functions with a matching radius of 30 fm, including partial waves up to $l_p=15$ for proton-target motion and $\ell=8$ for neutron-target motion

## Context
This work extends the theory developed by Potel, Nunes and Thompson (Phys. Rev. C, 92:034611, 2015) for inclusive $(d,p)$ surrogate reactions to include nonlocal interactions, and parallels the study by Titus and Nunes (Phys. Rev. C, 89:034609, 2014) on the Perey effect but now for transfer to continuum states rather than bound states. It enables future surrogate method analyses to incorporate nonlocal optical potentials, such as those from dispersive optical model theory or ab initio approaches, and demonstrates that the spin distribution predictions of the surrogate method are robust against nonlocality effects.
