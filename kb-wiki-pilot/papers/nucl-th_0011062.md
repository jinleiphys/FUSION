# Comparison of Transfer-to-Continuum and Eikonal Models of Projectile Fragmentation Reactions

> Spectroscopic properties of nuclei are accessible with projectile fragmentation reactions, but approximations made in the reaction theory can limit the accuracy of the determinations. We examine here two models that have rather different approximations for the nucleon wave function, the target interaction, and the treatment of the finite duration of the reaction. The nucleon-target interaction is treated differently in the eikonal and the transfer-to-continuum model, but the differences are more significant for light targets. We propose a new parameterization with that in mind. We also propose a new formula to calculate the amplitude that combines the better treatment of the wave function in the eikonal model with the better treatment of the target interaction in the transfer-to-continuum model.

## Key claim

The paper concludes that current models for calculating projectile fragmentation cross sections may overestimate the true value, but the error should be of the order of 20% or less, with the caveat that this estimate does not account for target breakup contributions which could account for another 20% of measured cross sections.

## Method

The authors compared the transfer-to-continuum (TC) model and the eikonal model for single-neutron removal reactions, focusing on their different treatments of the neutron-target S-matrix, the nucleon wave function, and the finite duration of the reaction. They performed calculations for neutron removal from s and p states in 12Be projectiles incident on 9Be targets at various energies, using both the asymptotic Hankel function approximation and realistic bound state wave functions, and compared the resulting diffraction and stripping cross sections. A new parameterization of the neutron-9Be optical potential was developed to fit experimental free-particle cross sections in the energy range 10-180 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The sudden eikonal approximation is acceptable for weakly bound neutron orbitals with binding energy less than 6 MeV and beam energies greater than 40A MeV, provided the reaction samples the initial wave function at distances larger than the sum of the radii of the reaction partners.
- For a typical binding energy of 10 MeV or more, the conditions for the sudden eikonal approximation to be valid are satisfied from about 80A MeV.
- The semiclassical condition ak_v >> 1 (where a=0.5 fm is the typical diffuseness) is satisfied starting from about 80 MeV.
- The maximum neutron parallel momentum for a 12Be projectile is k_z^max = 0.96 fm^{-1}.
- The integrated probabilities obtained with the three approximations (TC with Hankel function, TC with realistic wave function, and eikonal) differ by not more than about 10%, leading to about 20% difference in total cross sections after integration over core-target impact parameter.
- The eikonal total cross sections are larger by about 20% than the TC calculations at all energies except 20A MeV, where two effects compensate.
- The new neutron-9Be optical potential gives volume integrals J_W/A approximately 200 MeV fm^3.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of projectile fragmentation models including the eikonal model of Yabana et al. and the transfer-to-continuum model of Bonaccorso and collaborators, and addresses the accuracy of approximations used in both approaches. The paper proposes a new formula (Eq. A10) that combines the better treatment of the wave function in the eikonal model with the better treatment of the target interaction in the transfer-to-continuum model, and suggests that future experiments should detect the final excitation state of the target in coincidence with breakup events, as done by Henri et al. for normal heavy ions.
