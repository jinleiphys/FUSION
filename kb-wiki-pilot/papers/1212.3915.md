# Role of the cluster structure of $^7$Li in the dynamics of fragment capture

> Exclusive measurements of prompt $γ$-rays from the heavy-residues with various light charged particles in the $^7$Li + $^{198}$Pt system, at an energy near the Coulomb barrier (E/$V_b$ $\sim$ 1.6) are reported. Recent dynamic classical trajectory calculations, constrained by the measured fusion, $α$ and $t$ capture cross-sections have been used to explain the excitation energy dependence of the residue cross-sections. These calculations distinctly illustrate a two step process, breakup followed by fusion in case of the capture of $t$ and $α$ clusters; whereas for $^{6}$He + $p$ and $^{5}$He + $d$ configurations, massive transfer is inferred to be the dominant mechanism. The present work clearly demonstrates the role played by the cluster structures of $^7$Li in understanding the reaction dynamics at energies around the Coulomb barrier.

# Role of the cluster structure of $^7$Li in the dynamics of fragment capture

## Key claim
Exclusive particle-gamma coincidence measurements combined with classical dynamical trajectory calculations demonstrate that the dominant mechanism for capture of weakly bound $\alpha$ and $t$ clusters from $^7$Li is breakup followed by fusion, whereas for the more strongly bound $^6$He+$p$ and $^5$He+$d$ cluster configurations, massive transfer from bound states is inferred to be the dominant mechanism rather than breakup fusion.

## Method
Exclusive measurements of prompt $\gamma$-rays from heavy residues were performed in coincidence with light charged particles ($\alpha$, $t$, $d$, $p$) for the $^7$Li + $^{198}$Pt system at a beam energy of 45 MeV (E/$V_b$ $\sim$ 1.6), using four silicon telescope detectors and four Compton-suppressed clover detectors. Off-beam measurements of fusion, $t$-capture, and neutron transfer excitation functions were performed for beam energies from 22 to 45 MeV using radioactive decay of residues. The measured integrated cross-sections for complete fusion, $\alpha$-capture, and $t$-capture were used to constrain the three-dimensional classical trajectory model (PLATYPUS code), which was then coupled with the statistical model code PACE2 to predict evaporation residue cross-sections as a function of excitation energy of the primary composite system.

## Key numbers
- $\alpha$-capture cross-section at 45 MeV: 75 $\pm$ 5 mb (experiment), 77 mb (PLATYPUS calculation)
- $t$-capture cross-section at 45 MeV: 440 $\pm$ 10 mb (experiment), 366 mb (PLATYPUS calculation)
- Complete fusion cross-section at 45 MeV: 1004 $\pm$ 50 mb (experiment), 958 mb (PLATYPUS calculation)
- Breakup threshold for $\alpha$+$t$ cluster in $^7$Li: 2.47 MeV
- Breakup thresholds for $^6$He+$p$ and $^5$He+$d$ clusters: 9.98 MeV and 9.52 MeV respectively
- Ground state Q-values: $\alpha$ transfer +8.9 MeV, $t$ transfer -2.6 MeV, $d$ transfer -2.8 MeV, $p$ transfer +2.4 MeV
- Average excitation energies from PLATYPUS: $^{201}$Au ($t$-capture) 25.5 MeV, $^{202}$Hg ($\alpha$-capture) 28.0 MeV, $^{203}$Hg ($^5$He capture) 38.0 MeV, $^{204}$Hg ($^6$He capture) 42.0 MeV
- Breakup probability function parameters: amplitude $A = 1.68 \times 10^4$, slope $\beta = 0.9$ fm$^{-1}$
- Breakup events with relative energy $E_{rel} \leq 4$ MeV dominate the breakup fusion process

## Context
This work extends previous studies of breakup fusion in weakly bound nuclei such as $^{6,7}$Li (Castaneda et al. 1978, Utsunomiya et al. 1983, Tripathi et al. 2005) by providing the first exclusive experimental demonstration of the role of different cluster structures within a single projectile. The three-dimensional classical trajectory model developed by Diaz-Torres et al. (2007, 2010) and implemented in the PLATYPUS code allows for the first time quantitative interpretation of exclusive particle-gamma coincidence data, which was not possible with earlier models. The results enable future studies of nuclear structure using $^7$Li beams to populate nuclei formed as $^{5,6}$He+target or $t$+target systems, and have implications for predicting production cross-sections of exotic nuclei with radioactive ion beams for high-spin spectroscopy.
