# Importance of the deuteron breakup in the deuteron knockout reaction

> An isoscalar $pn$ pair is expected to emerge in nuclei that have similar proton and neutron numbers and it may be a candidate for a deuteron ``cluster.'' There is, however, no experimental evidence for it. The purpose of this paper is to construct a new reaction model for the ($p,pd$) reaction including the deuteron breakup in the elementary process and the deuteron reformation by the final-state interactions (FSIs). How these processes contribute to the observables of the reaction is investigated. The distorted wave impulse approximation is extended in twofold. The elementary processes of the ($p,pd$), i.e., the $p$-$d$ elastic scattering and $d(p,p)pn$ reaction, are described with an impulse picture employing a nucleon-nucleon effective interaction. The three-body scattering waves in the final state of the ($p,pd$) reaction are calculated with the continuum-discretized coupled-channels method. The triple differential cross section (TDX) of the ($p,pd$) reaction is calculated with the new model. The elementary processes are described reasonably well with the present model. As for the ($p,pd$) reaction, the deuteron reformation can either increase or decrease the TDX height depending on the interference between the elastic and breakup channel of deuteron, while the \textit{back-coupling} effect always decreases it. It is shown that the deuteron reformation significantly changes the TDX of the ($p,pd$) reaction through the interference. It is important to include this process to quantitatively discuss the ($p,pd$) cross sections in view of the deuteron formation in nuclei. For more quantitative discussion regarding the experimental data, further improvement will be necessary.

## Key claim

The deuteron reformation process (where a deuteron broken up in the elementary process reforms via final-state interactions) significantly changes the triple differential cross section of the ($p,pd$) reaction through interference between elastic and breakup channels, but this effect can either increase or decrease the cross section depending on the target nucleus, and the back-coupling effect always decreases it.

## Method

The authors extended the distorted wave impulse approximation (DWIA) by combining it with the continuum-discretized coupled-channels method (CDCC), calling the new model CDCCIA. The elementary processes of the ($p,pd$) reaction (p-d elastic scattering and d(p,p)pn breakup reaction) were described with an impulse picture using a nucleon-nucleon effective interaction, while the three-body scattering waves in the final state of the ($p,pd$) reaction were calculated with CDCC. The triple differential cross section (TDX) was calculated for ($p,pd$) reactions on $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca, and $^{56}$Ni targets at 250 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The correction factor $C_{corr}$ for the p-d elastic scattering cross section ranges from 0.68 to 0.71 across incident energies 120-250 MeV, with an average value of 0.69 used in the ($p,pd$) calculation
- For $^{16}$O($p,pd$)$^{14}$N at 250 MeV, the deuteron reformation process decreases the TDX by about 21% around the peak
- For $^{40}$Ca($p,pd$)$^{38}$K at 250 MeV, the deuteron reformation process increases the TDX by about 11%
- For $^{56}$Ni($p,pd$)$^{54}$Co at 250 MeV, the deuteron reformation process increases the TDX by about 14%
- The deuteron separation energies used are 24.68 MeV ($^{16}$O), 19.60 MeV ($^{40}$Ca), and 19.97 MeV ($^{56}$Ni)
- The $pn$ continuum states included in CDCC have $k_{max} = 2.1$ fm$^{-1}$ with $\ell = 0, 2, 4$

## Context

This work extends the standard DWIA framework for knockout reactions, which was previously used in the $^{16}$O($p,pd$)$^{14}$N analysis by Samanta et al. (1982) but did not include deuteron breakup or reformation processes. The combination of DWIA and CDCC was previously used by Harada et al. (2015) for kaon-induced $^3_\Lambda$He production, but the present study is the first to incorporate deuteron breakup in the elementary process and deuteron reformation by final-state interactions for ($p,pd$) reactions. This model enables more quantitative discussion of deuteron formation in nuclei, though further improvement will be necessary for comparison with experimental data.
