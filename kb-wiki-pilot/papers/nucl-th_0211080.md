# Influence of tunneling on electron screening in low energy nuclear reactions in laboratories

> Using a semiclassical mean field theory, we show that the screening potential exhibits a characteristic radial variation in the tunneling region in sharp contrast to the assumption of the constant shift in all previous works. Also, we show that the explicit treatment of the tunneling region gives a larger screening energy than that in the conventional approach, which studies the time evolution only in the classical region and estimates the screening energy from the screening potential at the external classical turning point. This modification becomes important if the electronic state is not a single adiabatic state at the external turning point either by pre-tunneling transitions of the electronic state or by the symmetry of the system even if there is no essential change with the electronic state in the tunneling region.

## Key claim

Using a semiclassical mean field theory that treats both the classically allowed and tunneling regions consistently, the authors show that the screening potential exhibits a characteristic radial variation inside the tunneling region, contrary to the constant-shift assumption of all previous work; this explicit treatment of the tunneling region yields a larger screening energy than the conventional approach that only studies time evolution in the classical region and estimates the screening energy from the screening potential at the external classical turning point.

## Method

The authors describe the time evolution of electrons by a Schrodinger equation and the relative nuclear motion by classical Newtonian equations, coupled through a variational principle (mean field theory). For the classically allowed region, they solve the coupled equations along the real time axis; when the relative velocity becomes zero, they switch to imaginary time (t = -i tau) and continue the time evolution in the tunneling region using modified equations. The screening potential is defined as the total energy of the electrons at each time (or each separation distance R), and the tunneling probability is calculated via a WKB formula using the resulting effective potential V^(0)(R) + Delta V(R).

## Key numbers

* For the D+d reaction at E_cm = 1 keV, the screening potential at the external turning point is -34.0 eV, matching the average of the binding energies in the lowest gerade state (-54.4 eV) and ungerade state (-13.6 eV) of the united atom ^4He^+.
* The adiabatic limit screening energy for the D+d reaction is 20.4 eV, calculated as the average of (54.4 - 13.6) eV and (13.6 - 13.6) eV.
* The sudden limit screening energy for the D+d reaction is 13.6 eV.
* For the D+d reaction at E_cm = 1.6 keV, the numerical result from the explicit tunneling treatment agrees with the value given by averaging the fusion cross sections for the gerade and ungerade configurations separately.
* For the D+d reaction, the external classical turning point in the presence of an electron at E_K^infinity = 1.0 keV is 0.0142 Angstrom, and the mean square electron radius at that point is 0.688 Angstrom.

## Context

This work extends the dynamical approach of Shoppa, Koonin, Langanke, and Seki (Phys. Rev. C 48, 837, 1993; Nucl. Phys. A 605, 387, 1996), who solved coupled equations for electronic and nuclear motions only in the classical region and estimated the screening energy from the electronic wave function at the external classical turning point. The present paper extends that formalism into the tunneling region using imaginary time evolution. The results show that the conventional method underestimates the screening energy, but the effect is too small to explain the large experimental screening energies reported by Rolfs and Somorjai (Nucl. Instrum. Meth. B99, 297, 1995) that exceed the adiabatic limit for all systems studied experimentally. The large screening energy found for the D+d reaction is attributed to the symmetry special to that system and cannot be generalized to other systems.
