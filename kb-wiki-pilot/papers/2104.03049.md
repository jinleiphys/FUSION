# Single-nucleon removal cross sections on nucleon and nuclear targets

> The eikonal direct-reaction model, as used in spectroscopic studies of intermediate-energy nucleon-removal reactions on light target nuclei, is considered in the case of a proton target and applied to neutron removal from $^{29}$Ne at 240 MeV/nucleon. The computed cross sections and their sensitivities are compared using an earlier detailed analysis of carbon target data. The nuclear structure input, for the $^{29}$Ne ground-state and $^{28}$Ne final states, is that deduced from the carbon target analysis. The comparisons quantify the sensitivity of the two reactions to the angular momenta and binding energies of the active valence orbitals - showing the carbon target to be relatively more efficient for removals from weakly-bound, low-$\ell$, halo-like orbitals. Probing this sensitivity experimentally would provide useful tests of these predictions and of the model's description of the reaction mechanism.

# Single-nucleon removal cross sections on nucleon and nuclear targets

## Key claim

The eikonal direct-reaction model, when applied to neutron removal from 29Ne at 240 MeV/nucleon on a proton target, predicts that the carbon target is relatively more efficient for removals from weakly-bound, low-ℓ, halo-like orbitals, while the proton target shows enhanced sensitivity to higher-ℓ and more bound orbitals; these predicted differences in sensitivity, if confirmed experimentally, would provide useful tests of the model's description of the reaction mechanism.

## Method

The author performed eikonal-model calculations for single-neutron removal from 29Ne on a proton target at 240 MeV/nucleon, using the sudden and eikonal approximations with nucleon-nucleon S-matrices parameterized from experimental NN total cross sections and forward-scattering amplitudes. The nuclear structure input, spectroscopic factors and bound-state wave functions for the 29Ne ground state and 28Ne final states, was taken from the sdpf-m shell-model interaction and Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations, identical to those used in the earlier carbon target analysis of Kobayashi et al. (2016). The computed partial cross sections to each bound shell-model final state of 28Ne were compared with the corresponding carbon target results from that earlier work.

## Key numbers

- Inclusive neutron-removal cross section on proton target: 32.3 mb; on carbon target: 69.0 mb (ratio p:C = 0.47)
- Ground-state to ground-state cross section on proton target: 10.3 mb; on carbon target: 31.6 mb (ratio p:C = 0.33)
- Percentage of inclusive cross section from d+f-wave orbitals: 49% on proton target, 35% on carbon target
- Percentage of inclusive cross section from s+p-wave orbitals: 51% on proton target, 65% on carbon target
- Ground-state to excited-states ratio on proton target: 32:68%; on carbon target: 46:54%
- NN total cross sections used: σ_pp = 22.02 mb, σ_np = 37.14 mb at 240 MeV
- Ratio of p-wave contributions (p:C): 0.36; ratio of d-wave and f-wave contributions (p:C): 0.65
- 29Ne neutron separation energy: 963 keV (with evaluated value 971(196) keV producing cross-section changes smaller than typical measurement errors by a factor of about three)

## Context

This work extends the eikonal direct-reaction model, previously applied to nucleon-removal reactions on light nuclear targets such as carbon and beryllium (Tostevin 2001, Hansen and Tostevin 2003), to the case of a proton target. It builds directly on the carbon and lead target analysis of 29Ne by Kobayashi et al. (2016), which established the 29Ne ground state as a deformed, weakly-bound p-wave neutron-halo system with spin-parity 3/2−. The calculations enable quantitative predictions for future experiments using proton targets (e.g., with the MINOS device) and provide a benchmark for comparing alternative reaction models such as continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) and distorted-waves impulse approximation (DWIA) approaches. The predicted differences in ℓ-value and separation-energy sensitivity between proton and carbon targets could help experimentally identify halo-like states and test the reaction mechanism description.
