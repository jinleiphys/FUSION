# Toward a complete theory for predicting inclusive deuteron breakup away from stability

> We present an account of the current status of the theoretical treatment of inclusive $(d,p)$ reactions in the breakup-fusion formalism, pointing to some applications and making the connection with current experimental capabilities. Three independent implementations of the reaction formalism have been recently developed, making use of different numerical strategies. The codes also originally relied on two different but equivalent representations, namely the prior (Udagawa-Tamura, UT) and the post (Ichimura-Austern-Vincent, IAV) representations. The different implementations have been benchmarked, and then applied to the Ca isotopic chain. The neutron-Ca propagator is described in the Dispersive Optical Model (DOM) framework, and the interplay between elastic breakup (EB) and non-elastic breakup (NEB) is studied for three Ca isotopes at two different bombarding energies. The accuracy of the description of different reaction observables is assessed by comparing with experimental data of $(d,p)$ on $^{40,48}$Ca. We discuss the predictions of the model for the extreme case of an isotope ($^{60}$Ca) currently unavailable experimentally, though possibly available in future facilities (nominally within production reach at FRIB). We explore the use of $(d,p)$ reactions as surrogates for $(n,γ)$ processes, by using the formalism to describe the compound nucleus formation in a $(d,pγ)$ reaction as a function of excitation energy, spin, and parity. The subsequent decay is then computed within a Hauser-Feshbach formalism. Comparisons between the $(d,pγ)$ and $(n,γ)$ induced gamma decay spectra are discussed to inform efforts to infer neutron captures from $(d,pγ)$ reactions. Finally, we identify areas of opportunity for future developments, and discuss a possible path toward a predictive reaction theory.

# Toward a complete theory for predicting inclusive deuteron breakup away from stability

## Key claim

The breakup-fusion formalism for inclusive (d,p) reactions, implemented in three independent codes using both prior (Udagawa-Tamura) and post (Ichimura-Austern-Vincent) representations, can describe elastic and non-elastic breakup contributions across isotopic chains, but the accuracy of predictions for exotic isotopes like 60Ca depends on the assumed asymmetry dependence of the optical potential and awaits experimental confirmation.

## Method

The authors benchmarked three independent numerical implementations of the inclusive breakup formalism, applying them to (d,p) reactions on 40,48,60Ca at two bombarding energies. The neutron-target propagator was described using the Dispersive Optical Model (DOM) framework, and the non-elastic breakup (NEB) contribution was separated into the Udagawa-Tamura (UT) term and the Hussein-McVoy (HM) non-orthogonality correction. For surrogate applications, the compound nucleus formation spin-parity distribution from the (d,p) reaction was combined with a Hauser-Feshbach decay calculation to predict gamma-decay spectra.

## Key numbers

- Energy position of principal single-particle-like peaks reproduced within about 500 keV
- Calculations performed for 40Ca, 48Ca, and 60Ca isotopes at two different bombarding energies
- Experimental data from (d,p) on 40Ca and 48Ca used for validation
- 60Ca identified as nominally within production reach at FRIB
- Spectroscopic factors are not used in the DOM framework; strength is concentrated in the principal peak for the first few excited states and smeared into broad peaks at higher excitation energies

## Context

This work extends the Ichimura-Austern-Vincent (IAV) and Udagawa-Tamura (UT) formalisms for inclusive breakup reactions, building on prior developments by Ichimura et al. (1985), Austern et al. (1987), and Udagawa and Tamura (1981). The three code implementations (Lei:15, Carlson:15, Potel:15b) are benchmarked against each other, and the DOM approach (Dickhoff17) is compared with the global Koning-Delaroche parametrization (Koning:03). The work enables surrogate (d,pγ) reactions to infer (n,γ) cross sections for unstable nuclei, and identifies future development needs including consistent three-body descriptions of the entrance channel and nonlocal implementations of the optical potential.
