# Direct Reactions with Exotic Nuclei, Nuclear Structure and Astrophysics

> Intermediate energy Coulomb excitation and dissociation is a useful tool for nuclear structure and astrophysics studies. Low-lying strength in nuclei far from stability was discovered by this method. The effective range theory for low-lying strength in one-neutron halo nuclei is summarized and extended to two-neutron halo nuclei. This is of special interest in view of recent rather accurate experimental results on the low-lying electric dipole strength in $^{11}$Li. Another indirect approach to nuclear astrophysics is the Trojan horse method. It is pointed out that it is a suitable tool to investigate subthreshold resonances.

## Key claim

The paper argues that effective range theory, which describes low-lying electromagnetic strength in one-neutron halo nuclei using only a few low-energy constants (binding energy, scattering lengths), can be extended to two-neutron halo nuclei like $^{11}$Li, where the dipole strength distribution depends on spectroscopic factors and scattering lengths in a way that can be tested against recent experimental data.

## Method

The authors develop an effective-range expansion for the electromagnetic dipole strength in one-neutron halo nuclei, parameterizing the continuum phase shift by the scattering length and the halo expansion parameter $\gamma = qR$. For two-neutron halo nuclei, they construct a model where the initial bound state is a product of single-particle wave functions with asymptotic normalization constants, the final state consists of two uncorrelated continuum neutrons, and the dipole matrix element separates into a single-particle transition term and an overlap (shake-off) term that accounts for the sudden removal of one neutron.

## Key numbers

- For $^{11}$Be: extracted asymptotic normalization constant $C_{0}=0.724(8)$ fm$^{-1/2}$ from Coulomb dissociation data using cutoff radius $R=2.78$ fm and inverse bound-state decay length $q=0.1486$ fm$^{-1}$
- Spectroscopic factor for $^{11}$Be: $C^{2}S=0.704(15)$
- For $^{11}$Be $p_{1/2}$ channel: binding energy 184 keV, corresponding to $q=0.094$ fm$^{-1}$, with $\gamma^2=0.068$ and scattering length $a_1=210$ fm$^3$
- For $^{11}$Be: dimensionless parameters $c^{3/2}_{1}=-0.41(86,-20)$ and $c^{1/2}_{1}=2.77(13,-14)$, corresponding to scattering length $a^{1/2}_{1}=456(61,-73)$ fm$^3$
- $^{11}$Li binding energy $E_b\sim 300$ keV, with each neutron bound by $\epsilon=E_b/2$

## Context

This work extends the effective-range approach previously applied to the one-neutron halo nucleus $^{11}$Be (Typel and Baur, Phys. Rev. Lett. 93, 142502, 2004) to two-neutron halo nuclei, motivated by recent accurate experimental results on low-lying electric dipole strength in $^{11}$Li (Nakamura et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 96, 25202, 2006). The method is closely related to effective field theories for halo nuclei (Bertulani, Hammer, van Kolck, Nucl. Phys. A 721, 37, 2002) and provides a complementary approach to the more detailed theory of Esbensen and Bertsch (Nucl. Phys. A542, 310, 1992). The paper also discusses the Trojan horse method as a tool for investigating subthreshold resonances, connecting to work on $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ (Brune et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 83, 4025, 1999).
