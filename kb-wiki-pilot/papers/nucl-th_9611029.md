# Nuclear Astrophysics in Storage Rings

> Nuclear reaction cross sections are usually very small in typical astrophysical environments. It has been one of the major challenges of experimental nuclear astrophysics to assess the magnitude of these cross sections in the laboratory. For a successful experiment high luminosity beams are needed. Increasing the target width, one also increases the reaction yields. But, this is of limited use due to multiple scattering in the target. Storage rings are a very good way to overcome these difficulties. In principle, they can be tuned to large luminosities, and have the advantage of crossing the interaction region many times per second (typically one million/s), compensating low density internal gas targets, or low reaction rates in beam-beam collisions. Storage rings are also ideal tools for precise measurements of masses and beta-decay lifetimes of nuclei of relevance for astrophysics.

## Key claim

Storage rings can overcome the fundamental difficulty of measuring very small nuclear reaction cross sections in astrophysical environments by enabling high luminosities through repeated beam crossings (typically one million per second), which compensates for low-density internal gas targets or low reaction rates in beam-beam collisions, though the actual feasibility depends on achieving sufficient luminosity for each specific reaction.

## Method

The paper reviews how storage rings can be applied to nuclear astrophysics through several experimental techniques: bound beta-decay lifetime measurements using fully stripped ions stored in the ring; elastic and inelastic proton scattering with internal gas targets to determine matter distributions; transfer reactions such as (d,p) for spectroscopic factors; the Trojan Horse method using breakup reactions to extract low-energy astrophysical cross sections; asymptotic normalization coefficient extraction from peripheral transfer reactions; charge-exchange (p,n) reactions for Gamow-Teller matrix elements; and Coulomb dissociation to deduce radiative capture cross sections via time reversal.

## Key numbers

- Bound beta-decay half-life of bare 163Dy: 47 +5/-4 days (stable as neutral atom)
- Bound beta-decay half-life of bare 187Re: 34 ± 7 years (neutral atom half-life is 43 Gyr)
- Astrophysical S17(0) factor for 7Be(p,γ)8B derived from asymptotic normalization: 27.4 ± 4.4 eV.b
- Astrophysical S17(0) factor for 7Be(p,γ)8B from Coulomb dissociation method: 16.7 eV.b
- Gamma width of 5.173 MeV 1- resonance in 14O from Coulomb dissociation: 3.1 ± 0.6 eV
- Plasma screening enhances reaction rates for 3He(3He,2p)4He and 7Be(p,γ)8B by as much as 20%
- 30% - 40% uncertainty in primordial 7Li abundance from discrepancies in 4He(3H,γ)7Li cross sections
- 8Li(α,n)11B cross section at E_c.m. ~ 0.5 MeV larger by roughly a factor of 5 than previous inverse-reaction value
- 12C(α,γ)16O cross section should be known to better than 20% for good stellar modeling

## Context

This paper extends the discussion of storage ring applications to nuclear astrophysics following earlier work on bound beta-decay at the ESR at GSI (Jung et al. 1992, Bosch et al. 1995) and proposals for Coulomb dissociation (Baur, Bertulani, Rebel 1986). It addresses persistent discrepancies in key reactions such as 7Be(p,γ)8B (relevant to the solar neutrino problem, Bahcall 1989), 4He(3H,γ)7Li (primordial nucleosynthesis), and 12C(α,γ)16O (stellar evolution), and enables future measurements of reactions inaccessible to direct experiments, particularly for r-process nuclei and inhomogeneous Big Bang scenarios.
