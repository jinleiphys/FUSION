# Direct reactions in/for astrophysics

> Precise nuclear reaction rates are needed for a detailed description of the production of elements in primordial nucleosynthesis and during the hydrostatic burning of stars to constrain the astrophysical models. The relevant reactions are extremely difficult to measure directly in the laboratory at the small astrophysical energies. In recent years direct reactions methods have been developed and applied to extract low-energy astrophysical S factors. The application of these methods requires a combination of experimental and theoretical efforts. This contribution focuses on the underlying reaction theories that have to be well understood in order to assess the precision and limitations of the various approaches.

# Direct reactions in/for astrophysics

## Key claim

Direct reaction methods can extract low-energy astrophysical S-factors for reactions that are extremely difficult to measure directly in the laboratory, but the precision and limitations of these approaches depend critically on understanding the underlying reaction theories, including relativistic effects, higher-order contributions, and nuclear structure inputs.

## Method

The paper reviews several direct reaction techniques applied to nuclear astrophysics: transfer reactions (including the Trojan Horse Method and Asymptotic Normalization Coefficients), intermediate-energy Coulomb excitation, the Coulomb dissociation method, charge-exchange reactions, and knockout reactions. For each method, the author examines the theoretical framework required to extract astrophysical cross sections, including relativistic corrections to Coulomb excitation at intermediate energies (50-100 MeV/nucleon), first-order perturbation theory assumptions in Coulomb dissociation, and the validity of the Taddeucci formula relating charge-exchange cross sections to Gamow-Teller transition strengths.

## Key numbers

- Relativistic corrections increase the scattering angle by up to 6% and the elastic scattering cross section by up to 13% for center-of-mass scattering angles around 0-4 degrees at 100 MeV/nucleon
- At 10 MeV/nucleon, relativistic corrections are important only at the 1% level; at 500 MeV/nucleon, recoil corrections are relevant at the 1% level
- At intermediate energies around 50-100 MeV/nucleon (common at radioactive beam facilities), relativistic and recoil corrections can add up to 50%
- The stopping power for proton+hydrogen collisions is proportional to v^1.35, while Andersen-Ziegler table extrapolations predict a smaller exponent
- For protons on helium targets at 4 keV, the stopping power was found to scale as v^3.34
- The astrophysical S-factor for 7Be(p,gamma)8B is given by S_17(E) = (22.109 eV.b) * (1 + 5.30E + 1.65E^2 + 0.857E^3) / (1 + E/0.1375), where E is the relative energy in MeV
- 8B has a small proton separation energy of approximately 140 keV

## Context

This contribution extends the body of work on indirect methods for nuclear astrophysics, particularly building on the Coulomb dissociation method proposed by Baur, Bertulani, and Rebel (1986) and the first application to 7Be(p,gamma)8B by Motobayashi and collaborators (1994). The paper addresses unresolved problems including the laboratory electron screening discrepancy (where experimental findings disagree systematically by a factor of two with theory), the validity of the Taddeucci formula for charge-exchange reactions, and the need to reconcile nuclear structure calculations (such as the No-Core Shell Model) with reaction theory through methods like the Resonating Group Method. The work enables more reliable extraction of astrophysical reaction rates from experiments at radioactive beam facilities, which is essential for understanding solar neutrino production, the r-process, and stellar evolution.
