# Recent advances in the description of reactions involving exotic nuclei

> In this contribution to the proceedings of the International Nuclear Physics Conference 2019, I review recent developments made in reaction models used to analyse data measured at radioactive-ion beam facilities to study exotic nuclear structures. I focus in particular on reactions like elastic scattering and breakup, which are used to study halo nuclei. Although these peculiar nuclei challenge usual nuclear-structure models, some can now be computed ab initio. This brief review illustrates the progresses made in nuclear-reaction theory in the last few years to improve the description of the projectile within reaction models. I dedicate this contribution to the memory of Mahir Hussein, who has significantly contributed to this field and who passed away in May this year.

## Key claim

While the abstract states that *ab initio* models can now describe halo nuclei, the paper's actual claim is more hedged: these *ab initio* predictions can be tested against reaction data only when coupled to a simplified few-body reaction model via Halo Effective Field Theory (Halo EFT), and even then the agreement is excellent only for peripheral reactions (e.g., Coulomb breakup on Pb) but requires additional three-body forces to reproduce resonant breakup on light targets like carbon.

## Method

The paper reviews three recent developments in reaction theory for halo nuclei. First, the Microscopic Continuum-Discretised Coupled Channel (MCDCC) model embeds a fully microscopic cluster description of the projectile within the CDCC framework, treating the Pauli principle exactly and generating optical potentials via folding from global nucleon-nucleus potentials. Second, the extended CDCC (X-CDCC) framework includes core excitation by treating the core as a collective rigid rotor with explicit low-lying excited states. Third, a Halo EFT description of the projectile (at next-to-leading order, NLO) is coupled to a precise breakup reaction code, where the core-neutron interaction is replaced by a contact interaction regularised by a Gaussian of width sigma, with parameters fitted to binding energies and asymptotic normalisation coefficients (ANCs) from *ab initio* calculations.

## Key numbers

- Elastic scattering of 7Li on Pb at 27 MeV: MCDCC calculations including breakup (solid lines) achieve excellent agreement with data without fitting parameters; calculations considering only bound states (dashed lines) miss the data.
- Elastic scattering of 6He on Pb at 22 MeV: same MCDCC approach reproduces data without fitting parameters.
- Breakup of 19C on proton at 70A MeV: X-CDCC calculations including core excitation reproduce the 5/2+ resonance angular distribution nearly perfectly; a calculation assuming 18C in its 0+ ground state misses the cross section by an order of magnitude.
- Breakup of 11Be on proton at 63.7A MeV: X-CDCC shows that the 5/2+ and 3/2+ resonant states at E approx 1.3 MeV and 3 MeV are mostly populated through dynamical core excitation.
- Breakup of 11Be on Pb at 69A MeV: Halo EFT at NLO reproduces the data excellently with no sensitivity to the Gaussian width sigma, confirming the reaction is purely peripheral.
- Breakup of 11Be on C at 67A MeV: NLO Halo EFT misses breakup strength at the 5/2+ and 3/2+ resonances; adding a three-body force to emulate core dynamical excitation reproduces the resonant peaks.

## Context

This review extends the few-body reaction model framework (e.g., the CDCC model of Austern et al. 1987 and the semiclassical and eikonal approaches reviewed by Baye and Capel 2012) by incorporating more realistic projectile descriptions. It directly builds on the microscopic cluster model of Descouvemont and Dufour (2012) applied to CDCC by Descouvemont and Hussein (2013), the core-excitation X-CDCC work of Summers, Nunes and Thompson (2006) and the Sevilla-Lisbon collaboration (de Diego et al. 2014, Lay et al. 2016, de Diego et al. 2017, Moro 2019), and the Halo EFT framework of Bertulani, Hammer and van Kolck (2002) as reviewed by Hammer, Ji and Phillips (2017). The work enables future tests of *ab initio* nuclear-structure predictions (e.g., Calci et al. 2016, Navratil 2019) against reaction data, and identifies which degrees of freedom (binding energy, ANC, phase shifts, core excitation) are actually probed in different reaction regimes.
