# FUSION03, Concluding Remarks

> Fusion reactions below the Coulomb barrier provide new insights into multidimensional quantum tunneling, nuclear reaction dynamics and nuclear structure. These reactions are also of considerable interest to nuclear astrophysics. In this summary recent developments in the field are reviewed and open questions related to subbarrier fusion are presented.

## Key claim
Subbarrier fusion reactions reveal multidimensional quantum tunneling effects, but the abstract glosses over the fact that many open questions remain, including the need for precise coupled-channels calculations beyond older approximations, the puzzle of large surface diffuseness parameters required to fit fusion data, and the unresolved discrepancy in electron screening energies at astrophysical energies.

## Method
The paper reviews experimental and theoretical developments in subbarrier fusion presented at the FUSION03 conference, covering coupled-channels calculations, the nuclear potential puzzle, fusion inhibition for heavy systems, fusion of unstable nuclei, electron screening, and astrophysical fusion. It discusses specific systems such as 16O + 154Sm, 27Al on germanium isotopes, 40Ca and 48Ca with Zr isotopes, 60Ni + 89Y, and systems leading to 216Ra, among others.

## Key numbers
- Surface diffuseness parameter needed to fit fusion data is typically 1.5 to 2 times the value required to fit elastic scattering data
- Fusion inhibition is expected theoretically when Z1Z2 exceeds about 1600, but experimental data show inhibition even at much smaller Z1Z2 values
- A very large subbarrier fusion enhancement was observed for the 132Sn + 64Ni system
- The 60Ni + 89Y system shows very steep fall-off of fusion data at extreme subbarrier energies

## Context
This concluding review extends the line of work on subbarrier fusion reviewed in Balantekin and Takigawa (Rev. Mod. Phys. 70, 1998) and Dasgupta et al. (Ann. Rev. Nucl. Part. Sci. 48, 1998). It highlights the need for more precise coupled-channels codes such as CCFULL (Hagino et al., 1999) over older codes like CCFUS, CCDEF, and CCMOD. The paper enables future work on fusion of unstable nuclei at radioactive beam facilities, resolution of the electron screening puzzle, and better understanding of fusion inhibition and quasi-fission in heavy systems.
