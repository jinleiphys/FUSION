# Assessing continuum channel importance in continuum-discretized coupled-channels via dynamic polarization potential decomposition

> A recurring question in continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) calculations is which continuum channels carry the breakup coupling, both to interpret the reaction and to decide which channels a model space can safely omit. The standard answer is bin deletion: remove a channel, re-solve the coupled equations, and read the change in the elastic $S$-matrix. We show that this cannot isolate an individual channel's contribution. Deleting a channel forces the surviving model space to reorganize, the neighboring bins rerouting through the off-diagonal Green's function, so the recorded change mixes the channel's own effect with the readjustment of all the others. Working instead from the channel-resolved Feshbach dynamic polarization potential (DPP), whose full-coupling Green's function is a fixed reference shared by every channel, we define an exclusion that removes one channel while holding that reference fixed, returning its contribution to the intact system. The two operations disagree on which continuum bins matter most, for $d$+$^{58}$Ni at 21.6~MeV even reversing their order of importance. The DPP further separates each channel's action into a direct path, virtual excitation and return, and a bridge path, relayed through neighboring bins, a split deletion cannot make; it shows the bins act on the elastic channel mainly as bridges, robustly across angular momentum, and that it is this bridge coupling that reorganizes under deletion. A deletion-based channel importance is therefore best read as the truncated calculation's sensitivity to a channel's removal, not as the channel's intrinsic coupling strength.

# Assessing continuum channel importance in continuum-discretized coupled-channels via dynamic polarization potential decomposition

## Key claim

Standard bin deletion cannot isolate an individual continuum channel's contribution to breakup coupling because removing a channel forces the surviving model space to reorganize, mixing the channel's own effect with the readjustment of all other channels. The channel-resolved Feshbach dynamic polarization potential (DPP), with a fixed full-coupling Green's function as a shared reference, provides an intrinsic measure that disagrees with deletion on which continuum bins matter most, for d+⁵⁸Ni at 21.6 MeV even reversing their order of importance.

## Method

The authors compute the exact, fully nonlocal Feshbach dynamic polarization potential (DPP) for the elastic channel using the full-coupling Green's function from continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) calculations. They decompose the DPP into a bilinear sum over channel pairs, then define an intrinsic importance measure by removing one channel's terms from the DPP while holding the Green's function fixed, and compare this to standard bin deletion (removing the channel from the coupled equations and re-solving). The DPP is further separated into direct (self-coupling) and bridge (inter-channel coherence) contributions for each continuum bin.

## Key numbers

- d+⁵⁸Ni elastic scattering at Elab = 21.6 MeV
- Six l = 2 continuum bins of equal width Δk = 0.087 fm⁻¹, spanning relative energies 0 to 12 MeV (k from 0.02 to 0.54 fm⁻¹)
- At J = 8, intrinsic DPP importance ranks [0.36–0.45] fm⁻¹ bin first (25.9%) while deletion ranks it fourth (10.7%); deletion ranks [0.28–0.36] fm⁻¹ first (22.8%) while intrinsic ranks it fourth (9.3%)
- Ratio of intrinsic to deletion importance at J = 8 ranges from 0.4 ([0.28–0.36] fm⁻¹) to 2.4 ([0.36–0.45] fm⁻¹)
- DPP reconstructs full CDCC elastic S-matrix to better than 0.5%
- Direct CDCC re-solve with U0α = Uα0 = 0 reproduces fixed-g exclusion for every l = 2 bin at J = 8 to within 0.04 percentage points
- At J = 6, intrinsic ranks [0.28–0.36] fm⁻¹ first (45.0%) while deletion also ranks it first (47.3%); at J = 10, intrinsic ranks [0.36–0.45] fm⁻¹ first (6.4%) and deletion also ranks it first (8.6%)

## Context

This work extends the Feshbach projection formalism (Feshbach 1958, 1962) and the breakup-induced DPP first characterized by Sakuragi, Yahiro, and Kamimura (1986), who found it repulsive and weakly absorptive. It builds on the exact, fully nonlocal DPP constructed in the authors' companion work (Liu, Lei, Ren 2026) using the full-coupling Green's function, resolving it down to individual continuum bins and separating direct from bridge pathways, a decomposition impossible with standard bin deletion. The work contradicts the assumption that bin deletion (Hirabayashi 1991; Gomez Camacho 2015, 2016, 2018; Canto 2009) measures a channel's intrinsic coupling strength, showing instead it measures truncation sensitivity. The results enable proper interpretation of which continuum channels carry breakup coupling and guide model-space truncation decisions, with expected strongest effects in halo systems (⁶He, ¹¹Li, ¹¹Be).
