# Dynamical mechanisms for deuteron production at mid-rapidity in relativistic heavy-ion collisions from SIS to RHIC energies

> The understanding of the mechanisms for the production of weakly bound clusters, such as a deuteron $d$, in heavy-ion reactions at mid-rapidity is presently one of the challenging problems which is also known as the "ice in a fire" puzzle. In this study we investigate the dynamical formation of deuterons within the Parton-Hadron-Quantum-Molecular Dynamics (PHQMD) microscopic transport approach and advance two microscopic production mechanisms to describe deuterons in heavy-ion collisions from SIS to RHIC energies: kinetic production by hadronic reactions and potential production by the attractive potential between nucleons. Differently to other studies, for the "kinetic" deuterons we employ the full isospin decomposition of the various $πNN\leftrightarrow πd$, $NNN\leftrightarrow N d$ channels and take into account the finite size properties of the deuteron by means of an excluded volume condition in coordinate space and by the projection onto the deuteron wave function in momentum space. We find that considering the quantum nature of the deuteron in coordinate and momentum space reduces substantially the kinetic deuteron production in a dense medium as encountered in heavy-ion collisions. If we add the "potential" deuterons by applying an advanced Minimum Spanning Tree (aMST) procedure, we obtain good agreement with the available experimental data from SIS energies up to the top RHIC energy.

## Key claim
The paper claims that deuteron production in heavy-ion collisions from SIS to RHIC energies can be described by combining two dynamical mechanisms, kinetic production via hadronic reactions and potential production via attractive nucleon-nucleon interactions, within the PHQMD transport approach, but only when the quantum nature of the deuteron (finite size in coordinate space and projection onto the deuteron wave function in momentum space) is taken into account, which substantially reduces the kinetic production rate in dense media.

## Method
The authors employed the Parton-Hadron-Quantum-Molecular Dynamics (PHQMD) microscopic transport approach, which combines QMD baryon propagation with PHSD hadronic and partonic dynamics. For kinetic deuteron production, they implemented the full isospin decomposition of πNN ↔ πd and NNN ↔ Nd channels using a covariant rate formalism, incorporating an excluded volume condition in coordinate space and projection onto the deuteron wave function in momentum space. For potential deuteron production, they developed an advanced Minimum Spanning Tree (aMST) procedure that stabilizes cluster identification by freezing internal degrees of freedom when clusters are no longer interacting with other hadrons, and requiring negative binding energy (E_B < 0).

## Key numbers
- Binding energy of deuteron: |E_B| ≈ 2 MeV
- MST cluster radius: r_clus = 4 fm, corresponding roughly to the range of the attractive NN potential
- Gaussian width in PHQMD: L = 8.66 fm²
- Saturation density: ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³
- Critical energy density for QGP formation: ε_c ≈ 0.5 GeV/fm³
- Energy range studied: from √s_NN = 2.52 GeV (SIS) to √s_NN = 200 GeV (top RHIC)
- At low (SIS) beam energies: 30% to 50% of protons are bound in deuterons, tritons and ³He
- At LHC energies: fraction of protons bound in clusters decreases to around 1%

## Context
This work extends previous dynamical approaches to deuteron production, particularly the SMASH transport studies by Oliinychenko et al. (2018, 2020) and Staudenmaier et al. (2021) which treated deuterons as point-like particles and approximated catalysis reactions via a fictitious dibaryon resonance. It also improves upon the original MST approach by Aichelin et al. (2019) and the subsequent study by Gläßel et al. (2021), which required choosing an arbitrary time for cluster analysis. The new aMST procedure eliminates this time-dependence by stabilizing cluster identification, enabling a consistent description from SIS to RHIC energies. The combination of kinetic and potential mechanisms enables future studies of hypernuclei and heavier clusters.
