# Theoretical Standard Model Rates of Proton to Neutron Conversions Near Metallic Hydride Surfaces

> The process of radiation induced electron capture by protons or deuterons producing new ultra low momentum neutrons and neutrinos may be theoretically described within the standard field theoretical model of electroweak interactions. For protons or deuterons in the neighborhoods of surfaces of condensed matter metallic hydride cathodes, such conversions are determined in part by the collective plasma modes of the participating charged particles, e.g. electrons and protons. The radiation energy required for such low energy nuclear reactions may be supplied by the applied voltage required to push a strong charged current across a metallic hydride surface employed as a cathode within a chemical cell. The electroweak rates of the resulting ultra low momentum neutron production are computed from these considerations.

# Theoretical Standard Model Rates of Proton to Neutron Conversions Near Metallic Hydride Surfaces

## Key claim

Proton-to-neutron conversion via electron capture can occur at appreciable rates near metallic hydride cathode surfaces when collective plasma modes and surface radiation provide the required ~0.78233 MeV energy, but only if the electron mass is renormalized by strong local electromagnetic fields to a factor β > 2.531 times its vacuum value.

## Method

The authors compute weak interaction transition rates for the reaction e⁻ + p⁺ → n + νₑ using the standard electroweak model, incorporating high-order radiative corrections from surface plasmon modes excited by large cathode currents. They treat the electron mass renormalization via the Hamilton-Jacobi equation in the presence of electromagnetic radiation, and express the neutron production rate in terms of composite fields of charged electrons and effective W-bosons, employing isotopic spin wave formalism for the many-body nucleon system.

## Key numbers

- Energy threshold for proton-to-neutron conversion: Q_in ≈ 0.78233 MeV
- Fermi coupling constant scaled by proton mass: G_F M_p²/(ħc) ≈ 1.02682 × 10⁻⁵
- Fermi coupling constant scaled by electron mass: G_F m²/(ħc) ≈ 3.04563 × 10⁻¹²
- Threshold electron mass renormalization factor: β₀ ≈ 2.531
- Estimated transition rate per (ẽ⁻ p⁺) pair: Γ ∼ 7 × 10⁻⁴ Hz × (β − β₀)²
- Estimated surface density of pairs: n₂ ∼ 10¹⁶/cm² within several atomic layers
- Estimated neutron production rate per unit area: ϖ₂ ∼ (10¹³ Hz/cm²) × (β − β₀)²
- At β ∼ 5 (twice threshold): ϖ₂ ∼ 10¹³ Hz/cm²
- Neutron absorber density: n_a ∼ 10²²/cm³
- Imaginary scattering length: b ∼ 10⁻¹³ cm
- Ultra-low momentum neutron wavelength: λ ∼ 10⁻³ cm
- Neutron absorption mean free path: Λ ∼ 10⁻⁶ cm
- Hard gamma-ray mean free path: L_γ ∼ 3.4 × 10⁻⁸ cm for photon energies 0.5–10 Megavolt
- Vector coupling strength: g_V ≈ 0.9742 (cos θ_C)
- Axial/vector ratio: g_A/g_V ≈ 1.2695

## Context

This work extends prior speculations by Mizuno (1998) and Kozima (1998) on the role of neutrons in low-energy nuclear transmutations in chemical cells, providing a physically plausible electroweak mechanism that those earlier works lacked. The authors build on their own previous work (Widom and Larsen, 2006) on electron mass renormalization in strong radiation fields, and connect to experimental observations of excess heat and nuclear transmutations in metallic hydride cathodes reported by Iwamura (1998), Violante (2002), Dash (2002), and Miley (1996, 1997, 2005). The theory enables a standard-model-based explanation for observed nuclear transmutations in light-water chemical cells without requiring deuteron-deuteron fusion, and predicts that externally detectable neutron and gamma-ray fluxes are negligible due to extremely short absorption mean free paths in the cathode material.
