# Low-energy corrections to the eikonal description of elastic scattering and breakup of one-neutron halo nuclei in nuclear-dominated reactions

> Background: The eikonal approximation is a high-energy reaction model which is very computationally efficient and provides a simple interpretation of the collision. Unfortunately, it is not valid at energies around 10 MeV/nucleon, the range of energy of HIE-ISOLDE at CERN and the future ReA12 at MSU. Fukui etal. [Phys. Rev. C 90, 034617 (2014)] have shown that a simple semiclassical correction of the projectile-target deflection could improve the description of breakup of halo nuclei on heavy targets down to 20 MeV/nucleon. Purpose: We study two similar corrections, which aim at improving the projectile-target relative motion within the eikonal approximation, with the goal to extend its range of validity down to 10 MeV/nucleon in nuclear-dominated collisions, viz. on light targets. The semiclassical correction substitutes the impact parameter by the distance of closest approach of the corresponding classical trajectory. The exact continued $S$-matrix correction replaces the eikonal phase by the exact phase shift. Both corrections successfully describe the elastic scattering of one-neutron halo nuclei. Method: We extend these corrections and study their efficiency in describing the breakup channel. We evaluate them in the case of $^{11}\mathrm{Be}$ impinging on $^{12}\mathrm{C}$ at 20 and 10 MeV/nucleon. Results: Albeit efficient to reproduce the elastic channel, these corrections do not improve the description of the breakup of halo nuclei within the eikonal approximation down to 20 MeV/nucleon. Conclusions: Our analysis of these corrections shows that improving the projectile-target relative motion is not the ultimate answer to extend the eikonal approximation down to low energies. We suggest another avenue to reach this goal.

## Key claim

The semiclassical correction (using a complex distance of closest approach) and the exact continued S-matrix correction, while successful for elastic scattering of one-neutron halo nuclei down to 10 MeV/nucleon, do not improve the eikonal description of breakup of halo nuclei on light targets down to 20 MeV/nucleon, contrary to what might be expected from their performance on elastic scattering.

## Method

The authors performed three-body model calculations for the reaction of a one-neutron halo nucleus (11Be described as a 10Be core plus a valence neutron) impinging on a 12C target at 20 and 10 MeV/nucleon. They compared standard eikonal and Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA) calculations, each with two corrections applied: (1) a semiclassical correction replacing the impact parameter by the complex distance of closest approach from the classical trajectory, and (2) an exact continued S-matrix correction replacing eikonal phases by exact phase shifts. All results were benchmarked against full Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (CDCC) calculations performed with the code FRESCO.

## Key numbers

- The 11Be ground state is bound by 0.5 MeV (s-wave valence neutron)
- The d-wave resonance in the 10Be-n continuum is at E_d = 1.27 MeV with width Gamma_d = 0.16 MeV (close to experimental values E_{5/2+} = 1.274 MeV and Gamma_{5/2+} = 100 keV)
- CDCC model space: c-n orbital angular momentum up to l_max = 6, maximum c-n energy E_max = 10 MeV, total angular momentum up to J_max = 20,000
- The standard eikonal model underestimates breakup cross sections by approximately 30% over the whole energy range at 20 MeV/nucleon
- The semiclassical correction deteriorates the breakup description, reducing cross sections below 1 MeV relative energy with no improvement at higher energy

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Fukui et al. [Phys. Rev. C 90, 034617 (2014)] who showed a semiclassical correction could improve breakup description on heavy targets down to 20 MeV/nucleon, and the authors' own prior work [HC17] showing the semiclassical correction works for elastic scattering on light targets. The paper contradicts the expectation that these corrections would generalize to breakup on light targets, and suggests that the missing couplings between angular momenta (neglected by dropping second-order derivatives in the eikonal approximation) should be addressed instead, possibly through a perturbative approach.
