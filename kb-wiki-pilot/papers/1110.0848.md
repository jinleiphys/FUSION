# Are present reaction theories for studying rare isotopes good enough?

> Rare isotopes are most often studied through nuclear reactions. Nuclear reactions can be used to obtain detailed structure information but also in connection to astrophysics to determine specific capture rates. In order to extract the desired information it is crucial to have a reliable framework that describes the reaction process accurately. A few recent developments for transfer and breakup reactions will be presented. These include recent studies on the reliability of existing theories as well as effort to reduce the ambiguities in the predicted observables.

# Are present reaction theories for studying rare isotopes good enough?

## Key claim

Existing non-perturbative reaction theories (ADWA, CDCC) for transfer and breakup reactions agree with exact Faddeev calculations at around 10 A MeV, but their reliability deteriorates at higher beam energies where interaction ambiguities become significant; the authors do not claim these theories are universally "good enough" but rather quantify the regimes where they are reliable and identify where improvements are needed.

## Method

The authors benchmark the adiabatic distorted wave approximation (ADWA) and continuum discretized coupled channel method (CDCC) against exact Faddeev solutions for (d,p) transfer reactions across target masses A=10 to A=48 and energies E≈5-40 A MeV. For breakup reactions, they compare the dynamical eikonal method (DEA) and time-dependent (TD) semiclassical approach against CDCC for reactions such as ¹⁵C breakup on Pb at 20 and 68 A MeV. They also apply dispersive optical model (DOM) potentials as input to ADWA calculations for (d,p) reactions on doubly magic nuclei including ¹³²Sn, and introduce the ratio method to reduce uncertainties from the core-target interaction in breakup observables.

## Key numbers

- For ¹⁰Be(d,p)¹¹Be at 21.4 MeV, ADWA, CDCC, and Faddeev predict cross sections within a few percent of each other
- Neglecting Coulomb in the ¹⁰Be(d,p)¹¹Be calculation introduces an error of 15%
- DEA performs well for ¹⁵C breakup on Pb at 68 A MeV but produces too large a cross section at 20 A MeV due to inadequate treatment of Coulomb deflection
- TD approach provides reliable angle-integrated cross sections for breakup at 20-70 A MeV but cannot provide detailed angular distributions
- DOM-based spectroscopic factors for ⁴⁹Ca show much less dependence on beam energy compared to standard approaches

## Context

This work extends benchmark studies of reaction theories for rare isotopes (Nunes et al. 2011, Nunes and Deltuva 2011) and applies DOM potentials (Mahaux and Sartor 1991) to transfer reactions, building on earlier studies of Ca isotopes (Mueller et al. 2011). The ratio method for breakup (Capel et al. 2011) is inspired by earlier work on elastic scattering (Johnson et al. 1997). These developments enable more reliable extraction of spectroscopic factors and structure information from reactions with exotic nuclei, and point toward needed extensions for two-particle states and mixed configurations.
