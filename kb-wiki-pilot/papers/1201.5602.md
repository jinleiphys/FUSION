# Thermal and transport properties of the neutron star inner crust

> We review the nuclear and condensed matter physics underlying the thermal and transport properties of the neutron star inner crust. These properties play a key role in interpreting transient phenomena such as thermal relaxation in accreting neutron stars, superbursts, and magnetar flares. We emphasize simplifications that occur at low temperature where the inner crust can be described in terms of electrons and collective excitations. The heat conductivity and heat capacity of the solid and superfluid phase of matter is discussed in detail and we emphasize its role in interpreting observations of neutron stars in soft X-ray transients. We highlight recent theoretical and observational results, and identify future work needed to better understand a host of transient phenomena in neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper argues that at low temperatures, the thermal and transport properties of the neutron star inner crust can be described by a simplified low-energy theory involving only electrons and collective excitations (phonons), with the complex nuclear ground state structure encoded in a few low-energy constants. However, this simplification relies on the condition that temperature is sufficiently low such that single-particle excitations of ions and neutrons are suppressed, which may not hold in all regions of accreting neutron stars where temperatures can reach \(10^8-10^9\) K.

## Method

The authors develop a low-energy effective field theory for the inner crust based on lattice phonons (from the motion of proton clusters) and superfluid phonons (from neutron density oscillations), incorporating the entrainment of neutrons by the moving nuclei through a mixing term in the Lagrangian. They calculate the specific heat contributions from electrons, lattice phonons, normal neutrons, and superfluid phonons, and examine how these depend on density, temperature, and the poorly-constrained nuclear symmetry energy and pairing gap. The composition is modeled for three scenarios: cold catalyzed matter (ground state), and two models of accreting neutron stars with different assumptions about pycno-nuclear fusion rates.

## Key numbers

- Melting temperature of the Coulomb solid: \(T_M \simeq 3.4 \times 10^9 \left(\frac{Z}{40}\right)^{5/3} \frac{\mu_e}{30~\mathrm{MeV}}~\mathrm{K}\), implying the inner crust is solid for \(T \le 10^9\) K
- Ion plasma frequency: \(\omega_p = T_p \simeq 0.3 \sqrt{\frac{Z}{A^*}} \left(\frac{\mu_e}{30~\mathrm{MeV}}\right)^{3/2}~\mathrm{MeV}\)
- Debye temperature: \(T_D \simeq 0.45 T_p\)
- Electron specific heat: \(C^e_v = \frac{1}{3}\mu_e^2 T\)
- Lattice phonon specific heat: \(C^{\mathrm{lph}}_v = n_i \frac{12\pi^4}{5} \left( \frac{T}{T_D}\right)^3\)
- Normal neutron specific heat: \(C^{\mathrm{neutron}}_v = \frac{1}{3} m_n k_{\mathrm{Fn}} T\) for \(T > T_c\)
- Superfluid neutron specific heat (single-particle): \(C^{\mathrm{neutron}}_v \approx \frac{1}{3} m_n k_{\mathrm{Fn}} T \exp\left(\frac{-\Delta_n}{T}\right)\) for \(T \ll T_c\)
- Superfluid phonon specific heat: \(C^{\mathrm{sph}}_v = \frac{2\pi^2}{15} \frac{T^3}{v_\phi^3}\)
- Neutron effective mass from band structure: \(m^*\) typically in range \((3-6) m_n\), can be as large as \(15 m_n\) in some regions
- Conversion factors: \(1~\mathrm{MeV}^2 = 1.07\times 10^{16}~\mathrm{ergs/(cm~s~K)}\) for thermal conductivity; \(1~\mathrm{MeV}^3 = 1.81\times 10^{16}~\mathrm{ergs/(cm^3~K)}\) for specific heat

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on neutron star crust composition by Baym, Bethe and Pethick (1971) and Negele and Vautherin (1973), and the comprehensive review by Chamel and Haensel (2008). It provides a low-energy effective field theory framework that unifies the treatment of lattice phonons and superfluid phonons, incorporating entrainment effects previously studied by Carter, Chamel, and collaborators (2004-2006). The work enables more realistic modeling of transient phenomena in accreting neutron stars (thermal relaxation, superbursts) and magnetar flares by providing the thermal conductivity and heat capacity inputs needed for time-dependent simulations, while identifying the need for better constraints on the nuclear symmetry energy and neutron pairing gap.
