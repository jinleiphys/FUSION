# Heating triangle singularities in heavy ion collisions

> We predict that triangle singularities of hadron spectroscopy can be strongly affected in heavy ion collisions. To do it we examine various effects on the singularity-inducing triangle loop of finite temperature in the terminal hadron phase. It appears that peaks seen in central heavy ion collisions are more likely to be hadrons than rescattering effects under two conditions. First, the flight-time of the intermediate hadron state must be comparable to the lifetime of the equilibrated fireball (else, the reaction mostly happens in vacuo after freeze out). Second, the medium effect over the triangle-loop particle mass or width must be sizeable. When these (easily checked) conditions are met, the medium quickly reduces the singularity: at T about 150 MeV, even by two orders of magnitude, acting then as a spectroscopic filter.

## Key claim

The paper predicts that triangle singularities (kinematic enhancements from rescattering diagrams, not new particles) can be strongly suppressed in heavy ion collisions, but only under two conditions: the flight time of the intermediate hadron state must be comparable to the fireball lifetime, and the medium effect on the triangle-loop particle mass or width must be sizeable. When these conditions are met, the singularity is reduced by up to two orders of magnitude at temperatures around 150 MeV, acting as a spectroscopic filter that makes observed peaks more likely to be genuine hadron resonances rather than rescattering artifacts.

## Method

The authors compute the scalar three-point triangle loop integral in the Matsubara formalism at finite temperature, replacing the continuous energy integral with a sum over Matsubara frequencies and analytically continuing the result back to Minkowski space. They examine five specific triangle diagrams (involving K*Σπ, K*KK, D*D*D, D*DD, and Δpn intermediate states) by evaluating the squared modulus of the loop integral as a function of the final-state invariant mass, both with vacuum parameters and with temperature-dependent masses and widths taken from the literature. The flight time of the intermediate state is estimated from classical kinematics to determine whether the triangle process can occur within the fireball lifetime.

## Key numbers

- At T = 150 MeV, the triangle singularity is reduced by two orders of magnitude (factor ~100) when thermal mass and width corrections are included
- For the K*KK triangle relevant to a1(1420): the K*+ mass drops from 0.89166 GeV (T=0) to 0.508 GeV (T=150 MeV); the K± mass drops from 0.49367 GeV to 0.37 GeV
- For the D*D*D triangle relevant to X(3872) production: the D*- mass drops from 2.0103 GeV to 1.872 GeV; the D*0 mass drops from 2.00685 GeV to 1.868 GeV; the D0 mass drops from 1.86483 GeV to 1.776 GeV
- For the Δpn triangle relevant to d*(2380): the Δ(1232) width increases from 0.1311 GeV (T=0) to 0.159 GeV (T=150 MeV); proton and neutron masses change by less than 1% up to T=150 MeV
- The K*+ width increases from 0.0508 GeV (T=0) to 0.0588 GeV (T=150 MeV)
- The D*- width increases from 83.4 × 10⁻⁶ GeV (T=0) to 0.0359 GeV (T=150 MeV)
- The D*0 width increases from 55 × 10⁻⁶ GeV (T=0) to 0.0237 GeV (T=150 MeV)

## Context

This work extends the ongoing discussion of triangle singularities in hadron spectroscopy, which has been used to explain structures like the a1(1420) as rescattering effects rather than genuine resonances. It directly builds on prior studies of triangle singularities by Guo et al. (2019), Bayar et al. (2017), and Nakamura et al. (2019), and on thermal field theory methods from Weldon (1983) and Laine (2016). The paper enables experimental distinction between genuine hadron resonances and kinematic artifacts in heavy ion collision data, complementing earlier work on using the hot medium to distinguish molecular configurations (which break up) from compact quarkonium states.
