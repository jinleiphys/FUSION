# Theory of the Trojan-Horse Method

> The Trojan-Horse method is an indirect approach to determine the energy dependence of S factors of astrophysically relevant two-body reactions. This is accomplished by studying closely related three-body reactions under quasi-free scattering conditions. The basic theory of the Trojan-Horse method is developed starting from a post-form distorted wave Born approximation of the T-matrix element. In the surface approximation the cross section of the three-body reaction can be related to the S-matrix elements of the two-body reaction. The essential feature of the Trojan-Horse method is the effective suppression of the Coulomb barrier at low energies for the astrophysical reaction leading to finite cross sections at the threshold of the two-body reaction. In a modified plane wave approximation the relation between the two-body and three-body cross sections becomes very transparent. Applications of the Trojan Horse Method are discussed. It is of special interest that electron screening corrections are negligible due to the high projectile energy.

## Key claim

The Trojan-Horse method (THM) can extract the energy dependence of astrophysical S factors for two-body reactions by measuring a related three-body breakup reaction, with the essential feature being that the Coulomb barrier is effectively suppressed at low energies, leading to finite cross sections at the threshold of the two-body reaction; however, the method relies on the surface approximation and a modified plane wave approximation, and its validity must be tested by comparing extracted cross sections with direct measurements of well-studied reactions.

## Method

The theory starts from a post-form distorted wave Born approximation (DWBA) of the T-matrix element for the three-body reaction A + a → C + c + b, where the Trojan horse a is composed of clusters x and b. Using the surface approximation, the overlap integral of the transferred particle is expressed in terms of the S-matrix elements of the two-body reaction A + x → C + c. In a modified plane wave approximation, the three-body cross section factorizes into a kinematical factor, a momentum distribution related to the Trojan horse wavefunction, and a Trojan-Horse cross section that contains the S-matrix elements and Trojan-Horse integrals involving Coulomb wave functions.

## Key numbers

- The three-body cross section behaves as constant (independent of k_Ax) at the threshold of the two-body reaction, in contrast to the two-body cross section which scales as k_Ax^(-2) exp(-2πη_Ax)
- The quasi-free c.m. energy in the A+x system is given by E_Ax^(qf) = E_Aa (1 - μ_Aa/μ_Bb · μ_bx²/m_x²) - ε_a, where ε_a is the binding energy of the Trojan horse
- The surface approximation was validated for the (d,p) reaction at E_d = 26 MeV
- The cutoff radius R defines the region r_Ax ≥ R where the surface approximation applies

## Context

This work extends the theoretical foundation of the Trojan-Horse method introduced by Baur (1986) and builds on earlier studies of transfer reactions into the continuum and inclusive breakup reactions. It formalizes the relation between three-body and two-body cross sections using DWBA with the surface approximation, generalizing the plane-wave impulse approximation used by Jain et al. (1970). The method enables extraction of low-energy astrophysical S factors without the problems of electron screening (which are negligible due to high projectile energy) and extremely low cross sections, and can be tested against direct measurements of well-known reactions such as those studied by Fuchs et al. (1971) and the IAV formalism of Ichimura, Austern, and Vincent.
