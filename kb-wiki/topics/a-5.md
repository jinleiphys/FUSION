## Landscape

### Landscape Synthesis: A ≤ 5

The topic "A ≤ 5" encompasses the physics of the lightest nuclear systems, from single nucleons up to the mass-5 isobars, and their role in fundamental processes. This regime is critical because it bridges few-body nuclear structure with astrophysical phenomena, such as big bang nucleosynthesis and the properties of dense matter in compact stars. Understanding these systems provides a stringent test for nuclear forces derived from quantum chromodynamics and is essential for interpreting observational data from the early universe and neutron stars.

The main approaches include ab initio methods that solve the many-nucleon problem from realistic interactions, as described in the unified ab initio approaches paper, which uses Hamiltonians from chiral effective field theory. The study of light nuclei in dense matter is treated via a cluster mean field approximation, where quasiparticle energy shifts for A=2 to 4 are calculated, accounting for self-energy and Pauli blocking. The thermodynamic model for nuclear multifragmentation uses canonical ensemble calculations to explain observables from heavy ion collisions. Additionally, precision big bang nucleosynthesis predictions for Helium-4 provide a rigid test for the standard cosmological model, while neutrino emission mechanisms in neutron stars, including Urca processes, are reviewed for their role in cooling.

Active open questions include the need for improved precision in primordial abundance predictions to constrain new physics, as highlighted in the big bang nucleosynthesis paper. The possible existence of exotic modes of excitation in weakly-bound nuclei far from stability remains a rapidly growing but experimentally underexplored field. Furthermore, the role of light sterile neutrinos, motivated by experimental anomalies, presents a major open question that connects neutrino physics with astrophysical data.
