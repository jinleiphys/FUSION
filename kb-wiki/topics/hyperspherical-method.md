# Hyperspherical method

**PhySH lineage:** Hyperspherical method

## Landscape

The hyperspherical method is a computational framework for solving the quantum mechanical few-body problem, representing the wavefunction of three or more particles on a hypersphere to handle correlations and continuum states. It matters because it enables precise predictions for exotic nuclear and particle systems, from the production of triply charmed baryons in heavy ion collisions to the structure of halo nuclei and hadronic spectroscopy.

The main approaches include the adiabatic hyperspherical method, used to exclude Pauli forbidden states in three-body halos with phase equivalent potentials, and the correlated Gaussian hyperspherical method, which expands channel functions in correlated Gaussians for robust few-body calculations. The Lagrange mesh technique combined with R matrix theory treats three-body continuum states, as applied to the 6He and 6Be systems. Specific applications include the computation of the Omega_ccc baryon production cross section via quark coalescence and the description of baryon spectroscopy with non-perturbative gluon and pseudoscalar meson exchange potentials.

Active directions visible in recent papers include the extraction of universal coefficients for four fermion systems at unitarity, the development of inverse scattering techniques to recover hyperon nucleon potentials for the Lambda N N three body problem, and the refinement of continuum state calculations to determine collision matrices with high accuracy. These efforts point toward a deeper understanding of three body forces and the discovery of exotic multiquark states in heavy ion collisions.
