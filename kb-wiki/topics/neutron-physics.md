## Landscape

Neutron physics explores the properties of the free neutron and its interactions, serving as a critical probe of fundamental symmetries and nuclear structure. It matters because precision measurements of neutron decay observables and the neutron electric dipole moment are uniquely sensitive to beyond the Standard Model (BSM) tensor, scalar, and CP-violating interactions, as highlighted in the QCDSF/UKQCD/CSSM collaboration paper. Additionally, the systematics of spontaneous neutron emission along the neutron dripline are crucial for nuclear astrophysics, yet remain less understood than proton emission.

The main approaches combine lattice QCD, effective field theory, and advanced simulation tools. The QCDSF/UKQCD/CSSM collaboration calculates matrix elements of low-energy effective operators within neutron states to interpret experimental constraints on BSM physics. Chiral perturbation theory is employed to investigate neutron behavior in strong magnetic fields, with a novel coordinate-space method revealing finite volume effects from charged pions winding around a torus. For applications, the TOUCANS code uses Geant4 for Monte Carlo transport of low-energy neutrons, while comprehensive tabulations of thermal cross sections and astrophysical reaction rates are calculated from evaluated nuclear data libraries.

Active directions include resolving finite volume effects in lattice calculations to extract neutron properties from magnetic field responses, as studied by the chiral perturbation theory work. The interplay between atomic and neutron experiments provides strict upper limits on T-odd, P-even interactions and CP-violation, with atomic parity nonconservation experiments constraining weak matrix elements in heavy nuclei. The ongoing investigation of spontaneous neutron emission from the ground state along the neutron dripline remains a frontier, with its systematics still at an early stage despite its importance for nuclear astrophysics.
