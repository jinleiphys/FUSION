## Landscape

Nuclear engineering applies the principles of nuclear physics to harness nuclear reactions for practical purposes, including energy generation, medical applications, and national security. Its importance stems from the need for reliable, low-carbon energy sources and the safe management of nuclear materials, with fission and neutron-induced reactions forming the core of both power reactors and fuel cycle analysis.

The field employs a range of theoretical and computational methods. The continuum discretized coupled-channels method (CDCC) is reviewed for its applications to nuclear reactions relevant to engineering. Symmetry-restored density functional theory is used to compute fission observables for nuclei like $^{240}$Pu, addressing limitations of symmetry-breaking schemes. Statistical models, such as the Hauser-Feshbach model implemented in TALYS, are applied to predict $(n,\alpha)$ reaction cross sections for Fe and Sn isotopes. The SOURCES code, which quantifies inherent neutron sources from $(\alpha,n)$ reactions and spontaneous fission, is critically examined for its nuclear data assumptions. Additionally, the double-differential cross sections for the $n+^{19}$F reaction are studied to understand energy level effects on charged particle emission.

Active directions include improving predictions for $\beta$-delayed neutron emission and fission rates using relativistic QRPA and statistical models, as uncertainties remain large for r-process simulations. The isotopic dependence of $(n,\alpha)$ reactions is being explored to assess how neutron excess alters cross sections. Updating alpha emission lines and stopping power data in codes like SOURCES represents an ongoing effort to refine neutron source predictions. Finally, addressing gaps in nuclear history education among engineers is recognized as important for contextualizing the field's societal impact.
