## Landscape

Explosive burning in astrophysics refers to the rapid nuclear fusion processes that occur in stellar environments such as novae, supernovae, and X-ray bursts, where extreme temperatures and densities drive nucleosynthesis far from nuclear statistical equilibrium. Understanding these processes is critical because they govern the production of key isotopes like the cosmic gamma-ray emitter $^{26}$Al and shape the chemical evolution of the universe, linking nuclear physics directly to observable astrophysical phenomena.

The field employs two broad methodological threads. One thread focuses on determining nuclear reaction rates through experimental and theoretical nuclear structure studies, as seen in the measurement of single-particle strengths near $^{56}$Ni using stripping reactions and the GRETINA array, and in the use of the $^{25}$Mg(d,p) reaction to constrain resonant rates for $^{25}$Al(p,$\gamma$)$^{26}$Si in nova conditions. The other thread addresses the computational challenge of coupling these reaction networks with hydrodynamics, with recent work developing spectral deferred corrections as an alternative to operator splitting for stiff sources, and implementing nuclear networks within general-relativistic radiation magnetohydrodynamics codes like Gmunu.

Active open questions include the precise determination of key resonant reaction rates that remain experimentally unconstrained, such as the $0^{+}$ and $1^{+}$ resonances in $^{26}$Si for nova burning. Another frontier is the development of first-principles multi-messenger predictions that self-consistently couple nuclear networks with magnetohydrodynamics and neutrino transport, moving beyond simplified coupling schemes to capture the full complexity of explosive environments.
