# Equations of state of nuclear matter

## Landscape

The equation of state (EOS) of nuclear matter describes the relationship between pressure, density, temperature, and other thermodynamic variables in dense nuclear systems. This topic is critical because the EOS governs the structure and stability of neutron stars, the dynamics of core-collapse supernovae, and the evolution of matter created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. Understanding the EOS across a wide range of densities and temperatures is therefore essential for interpreting astrophysical observations and experimental data from particle colliders.

The main approaches to determining the EOS are theoretical modeling and phenomenological extraction from data. Several papers employ microscopic many-body theories, such as the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory and the Dyson-Schwinger quark model, to construct EOSs for hybrid stars containing both nuclear and quark matter, often using a Gibbs construction for the mixed phase. Other works use relativistic mean field models or the three-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model to describe composite baryons. A separate thread uses relativistic hydrodynamics to compare different EOSs, including those based on lattice QCD with a phase transition to a quark-gluon plasma, to experimental spectra from heavy-ion collisions at CERN/SPS. Bayesian methods are also applied to quantify truncation errors in EOSs derived from chiral nucleon-nucleon potentials.

Open questions and active directions include the precise nature of the deconfinement phase transition and the properties of exotic phases like crystalline color superconductivity, which can sustain multipolar deformations. The sensitivity of neutron star oscillations, both radial and non-radial, to the underlying EOS is a key focus, with studies investigating characteristic differences between pure and hybrid stars in their oscillation frequencies and gravitational wave emission. The impact of modified theories of gravity on neutron star mass-radius relations is also being explored, alongside efforts to better constrain the EOS from heavy-ion collision data, particularly regarding the late hadronic stage and the role of Bose-Einstein correlations.
