# Neutron stars & pulsars

## Landscape

The study of neutron stars and pulsars probes the behavior of matter at extreme densities and magnetic field strengths, offering a unique laboratory for nuclear physics and quantum chromodynamics (QCD). These objects are not only fascinating astrophysical phenomena but also provide critical constraints on the equation of state (EOS) of dense baryonic matter, which remains poorly understood. Understanding their internal composition and evolution is essential for interpreting observations from next-generation instruments.

A central approach involves modeling the high-density interior using effective QCD theories, such as the non-local Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, to investigate quark deconfinement in rotating neutron stars. Several papers emphasize that rotation, driven by spin-up or spin-down, can trigger phase transitions in the stellar core, altering its particle composition and structure. For instance, the work on rotation-driven phase transitions focuses on quark matter formation during spin-down, while other studies examine how the equation of state is further restricted by strong magnetic fields, as seen in the comparison of magnetic dipole moments for anomalous X-ray pulsars and soft gamma-ray repeaters.

Open questions center on the precise nature of phase transitions in rotating stars and the role of magnetic fields in modifying the EOS. Recent detections of soft gamma-ray repeaters with low magnetic fields challenge the standard magnetar model, suggesting that some objects might be massive, magnetic white dwarfs. Future surveys from the Square Kilometer Array and other observatories promise to discover tens of thousands of new pulsars, many of which will experience dramatic density changes, enabling tests of these theoretical predictions.
