## Landscape

Nuclear reactions encompass the transformation of atomic nuclei through collisions with particles or other nuclei, a field central to understanding nuclear structure, astrophysical processes, and the properties of dense matter. This topic is critical for interpreting experiments from neutrino oscillation studies to heavy-ion collisions, and for constraining the equation of state of neutron-rich matter relevant to neutron stars and gravitational waves.

The field employs a range of theoretical frameworks. Transport models, such as the Giessen Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (GiBUU) implementation, describe reactions from pion-induced to heavy-ion collisions by incorporating in-medium modifications and off-shell transport. Ab initio approaches like the no-core shell model (NCSM) use realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions for light nuclei, with extensions to reactions via the resonating group method. Specific studies probe the isospin dependence of in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections using radioactive beams, while other work focuses on inclusive charged-current neutrino-nucleus reactions, accounting for long-range correlations and final state interactions.

Active directions include constraining the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy at supra-saturation densities, as probed by isospin diffusion and neutron/proton ratios. Recent papers also explore jet charge modification in dense QCD matter for flavor tagging, and the development of efficient emulators for two-body scattering using eigenvector continuation. Lattice QCD calculations are beginning to connect nuclear matrix elements to electroweak and beyond-Standard-Model processes, offering a bridge to low-energy nuclear physics.
