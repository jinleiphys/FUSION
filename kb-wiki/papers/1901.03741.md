# Neutrino nucleosynthesis: An overview

> Neutrinos produced during a supernova explosion induce reactions on abundant nuclei in the outer stellar shells and contribute in this way to the synthesis of the elements in the Universe. This neutrino nucleosynthesis process has been identified as an important contributor to the origin of $^7$Li, $^{11}$B,$^{19}$F, $^{138}$La, and $^{180}$Ta, but also to the long-lived radionuclides $^{22}$Na and $^{26}$Al, which are both key isotopes for $γ$-ray astronomy. The manuscript summarizes the recent progress achieved in simulations of neutrino nucleosynthesis.

## Key claim

Neutrino nucleosynthesis (the ν-process) contributes significantly to the solar abundances of ⁷Li, ¹¹B, ¹⁹F, ¹³⁸La, and ¹⁸⁰Ta, but modern supernova simulations with lower average neutrino energies severely reduce the predicted production factors compared to earlier studies, and the time dependence of neutrino emission must be explicitly accounted for to obtain realistic yields.

## Method

The authors review neutrino nucleosynthesis studies that combine three essential ingredients: (a) neutrino spectra and luminosities, historically described by Fermi-Dirac distributions with time-independent temperatures but now incorporating full time-dependent emission from modern supernova simulations; (b) neutrino-induced partial nuclear cross-sections calculated using the Random Phase Approximation (RPA) for heavy nuclei and the hyperspherical model or interacting shell model for light nuclei, with decay channels treated by the statistical model; and (c) one-dimensional stellar evolution models (e.g., KEPLER) subjected to artificial piston-driven explosions, coupled with an extensive nuclear reaction network that includes neutrino-induced reactions. Production factors are computed relative to solar abundances and normalized to ¹⁶O for a range of progenitor masses (13–30 M⊙).

## Key numbers

* For a 15 M⊙ progenitor with low neutrino energies (Sieverding et al. 2018), production factors normalized to ¹⁶O are: ⁷Li = 0.083, ¹¹B = 0.280, ¹⁵N = 0.116, ¹⁹F = 0.180, ¹³⁸La = 0.487, ¹⁸⁰Taᵐ = 0.484.
* For the same progenitor with full time-dependent neutrino emission from a modern simulation (Sieverding et al. 2019), production factors increase to: ⁷Li = 0.187, ¹¹B = 0.516, ¹⁵N = 0.141, ¹⁹F = 0.209, ¹³⁸La = 0.824, ¹⁸⁰Taᵐ = 0.636.
* For earlier high-energy spectra (Heger et al. 2005), production factors were: ¹¹B = 1.884, ¹⁵N = 0.487, ¹⁹F = 0.602, ¹³⁸La = 0.974, ¹⁸⁰Taᵐ = 0.964.
* Neutrino temperatures dropped from 8 MeV to 4 MeV for μ/τ neutrinos, 6 MeV to 4 MeV for electron antineutrinos, and 5 MeV to 2.8 MeV for electron neutrinos between Woosley et al. (1990) and Sieverding et al. (2018).
* The ν-process produces about (42 ± 4)% of the solar ¹¹B abundance, with the remainder from cosmic ray irradiation (Austin et al. 2014).
* About 35–39% of ¹⁸⁰Ta survives in its long-lived isomeric state (excitation energy 75 keV) after thermal equilibrium in the O/Ne layer (Mohr et al. 2007, Hayakawa et al. 2010).

## Context

This review summarizes three decades of neutrino nucleosynthesis studies, extending the pioneering work of Woosley et al. (1990) and Heger et al. (2005) by incorporating modern supernova simulations with lower neutrino energies and time-dependent emission spectra. The work shows that the ν-process yields are sensitive to neutrino flavor spectra and luminosities, making isotopes like ¹³⁸La and ¹⁸⁰Ta (produced mainly by charged-current νₑ reactions) potential probes of supernova neutrino properties, including neutrino oscillations and the neutrino mass hierarchy (Yoshida et al. 2004, 2005, 2006, 2008; Mathews et al. 2012; Kajino et al. 2014). The review recommends extending studies to the full range of core-collapse progenitor masses and to multi-dimensional supernova models.
