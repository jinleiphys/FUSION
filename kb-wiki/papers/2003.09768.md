# Axions in neutron star mergers

> Supernovae and cooling neutron stars have long been used to constrain the properties of axions, such as their mass and interactions with nucleons and other Standard Model particles. We investigate the prospects of using neutron star mergers as a similar location where axions can be probed in the future. We examine the impact axions would have on mergers, considering both the possibility that they free-stream through the dense nuclear matter and the case where they are trapped. We calculate the mean free path of axions in merger conditions, and find that they would free-stream through the merger in all thermodynamic conditions. In contrast to previous calculations, we integrate over the entire phase space while using a relativistic treatment of the nucleons, assuming the matrix element is momentum-independent. In particular, we use a relativistic mean field theory to describe the nucleons, taking into account the precipitous decrease in the effective mass of the nucleons as density increases above nuclear saturation density. We find that within current constraints on the axion-neutron coupling, axions could cool nuclear matter on timescales relevant to neutron star mergers. Our results may be regarded as first steps aimed at understanding how axions affect merger simulations and potentially interface with observations.

## Key claim

Axions with coupling strengths at or below the SN1987A bound would free-stream through neutron star merger matter under all thermodynamic conditions, but could still cool the merger on timescales relevant to the merger evolution; however, if the SN1987A bound is relaxed by a factor of about 4, axions could become trapped in the hottest, densest regions.

## Method

The authors calculate the axion mean free path and emissivity from neutron bremsstrahlung (\(n+n \leftrightarrow n+n+a\)) using a relativistic mean field theory (the \(NL\rho\) equation of state) to describe nucleons, accounting for the density-dependent decrease of the nucleon effective mass. They perform the full phase-space integration assuming a momentum-independent matrix element, keeping the relativistic neutron dispersion relation and the axion momentum in the energy-momentum conserving delta function, which extends validity to arbitrary neutron degeneracy. They compare this "constant-matrix-element" calculation to the traditional Fermi surface approximation and to an improved version that corrects the lower integration bound for semi-degenerate conditions.

## Key numbers

* Axion mean free path is longer than 1 km for all thermodynamic conditions in a neutron star merger when the axion-neutron coupling is set to the SN1987A upper bound of \(G_{an} \lesssim 7.9 \times 10^{-10} \text{ GeV}^{-1}\).
* At a density of \(7n_0\) and temperatures typical of mergers, the neutron Dirac effective mass drops to 228 MeV, while the neutron Fermi momentum is 604 MeV.
* In the Fermi surface approximation, the radiative cooling time for a fluid element to reach half its initial temperature is \(\tau_{FS,1/2} \approx 12 \text{ s} \times (m_L / 0.8 m_n) / [(G_{an}/G_{SN1987A})^2 F(y) (T_0/10 \text{ MeV})^4]\).
* The axion mass is related to the coupling by \(m_a = 1.2 \times 10^7 \text{ eV} (G_{an} / \text{GeV}^{-1})\), implying QCD axion masses well below 1 eV for allowed couplings.
* The factor \((1 + m_\pi^2 / k_{\text{typ}}^2)^{-2}\) in the constant matrix element ranges from 0.78 to 0.91 across the relevant density and temperature conditions.

## Context

This work extends the traditional use of supernovae (SN1987A) and cooling neutron stars to constrain axions (Raffelt 1996, Graham 2015) into the new domain of neutron star mergers, following the detection of GW170817. It improves upon prior axion emissivity calculations by Brinkmann and Turner (1988) and Iwamoto (1992) by using a relativistic mean field treatment of nucleons and integrating over the full phase space rather than relying solely on the Fermi surface approximation. The results enable future merger simulations to incorporate axion cooling as a potentially significant energy-loss mechanism, and may ultimately allow gravitational wave and electromagnetic observations of mergers to constrain axion properties.
