# Fast Dynamical Evolution of Hadron Resonance Gas via Hagedorn States

> Hagedorn states are the key to understand how all hadrons observed in high energy heavy ion collisions seem to reach thermal equilibrium so quickly. An assembly of Hagedorn states is formed in elementary hadronic or heavy ion collisions at hadronization. Microscopic simulations within the transport model UrQMD allow to study the time evolution of such a pure non-equilibrated Hagedorn state gas towards a thermally equilibrated Hadron Resonance Gas by using dynamics, which unlike strings, fully respect detailed balance. Propagation, repopulation, rescatterings and decays of Hagedorn states provide the yields of all hadrons up to a mass of m=2.5 GeV. Ratios of feed down corrected hadron multiplicities are compared to corresponding experimental data from the ALICE collaboration at LHC. The quick thermalization within t=1-2 fm\c of the emerging Hadron Resonance Gas exposes Hagedorn states as a tool to understand hadronization.

## Key claim
Hagedorn states (HS) implemented in the UrQMD transport model with full detailed balance can explain the rapid thermalization (within 1-2 fm/c) of hadrons observed in high-energy heavy ion collisions, provided the system starts from a non-equilibrated assembly of HS at hadronization.

## Method
The authors solved a covariant bootstrap equation to obtain the Hagedorn spectrum of mass states up to 8.6 GeV, then implemented these states with their decay widths and branching ratios into the UrQMD microscopic transport model. Box simulations (10x10x10 fm³ with reflecting walls) were performed starting from 200 initial HS with zero net charge at energy densities between 0.3 and 2.0 GeV/fm³, tracking the time evolution via decays, rescatterings, and recreations of HS until chemical equilibrium was reached at t=20 fm/c.

## Key numbers
- Thermalization time of the emerging Hadron Resonance Gas: t = 1-2 fm/c
- Hagedorn spectrum computed up to mass m = 8.6 GeV (computational limit)
- HS total decay widths: Γ ≈ 0.4-3.5 GeV
- Energy density range studied: ε = 0.3-2.0 GeV/fm³ in steps of Δε = 0.1 GeV/fm³
- At ε = 2.0 GeV/fm³: every fourth particle is a HS; >60% of total energy and >70% of total mass are in HS
- Temperature at ε = 1.0 GeV/fm³: T ≈ 0.154 GeV
- K⁻/π⁻ ratio at ε = 1.0 GeV/fm³: 0.193 (compared to ALICE Pb-Pb: 0.149(16))
- Λ/π⁻ ratio at ε = 1.0 GeV/fm³: 0.024 (compared to ALICE Pb-Pb: 0.036(5))
- Ω⁻/π⁻ ratio at ε = 1.0 GeV/fm³: 0.823×10⁻³ (compared to ALICE Pb-Pb: 0.87(17)×10⁻³)

## Context
This work extends the Statistical Bootstrap Model (Hagedorn, 1965) by implementing Hagedorn states dynamically in UrQMD for the first time, providing a microscopic mechanism for the thermal model success (Braun-Munzinger, 2001; Andronic, 2011). It builds on prior rate-equation studies (Greiner, 2004; Noronha-Hostler, 2007-2010) that showed HS accelerate chemical equilibration of strange particles, and on the authors' own covariant bootstrap solution (Beitel, 2014). The results contradict standard hadronic transport approaches (Belkacem, 1998; Bratkovskaya, 2000) that cannot achieve such fast equilibration. Future work can apply this to full heavy ion collision simulations, including finite net baryon densities for NICA and FAIR experiments, and study HS effects on shear viscosity and baryon number fluctuations.
