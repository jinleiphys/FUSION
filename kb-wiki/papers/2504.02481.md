# Revealing the harmonic structure of nuclear two-body correlations in high-energy heavy-ion collisions

> Smashing nuclei at ultrarelativistic speeds and analyzing the momentum distribution of outgoing debris provides a powerful method to probe the many-body properties of the incoming nuclear ground states. Within a perturbative description of initial-state fluctuations in the quark-gluon plasma, we express the measurement of anisotropic flow in ultra-central heavy-ion collisions as the quantum-mechanical average of a specific set of operators measuring the harmonic structure of the two-body azimuthal correlations among nucleons in the colliding states. These observables shed a new light on spatial correlations in atomic nuclei, while enabling us to test the complementary pictures of nuclear structure delivered by low- and high-energy experiments on the basis of state-of-the-art theoretical approaches rooted in quantum chromodynamics.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the mean squared anisotropic flow coefficients measured in ultra-central heavy-ion collisions can be expressed as quantum-mechanical averages of one- and two-body operators acting on the nuclear ground state, specifically the transverse radius operator and a new class of eccentricity operators that probe the harmonic structure of two-body azimuthal correlations among nucleons. This connection is derived within a perturbative treatment of initial-state fluctuations and relies on the approximation that the entropy density of the quark-gluon plasma is proportional to the arithmetic average of the two colliding nuclei's thickness functions.

## Method

The authors start from a standard description of heavy-ion collisions where each nucleus is represented by a thickness function built from nucleon positions sampled from the A-body ground-state wave function. They use a perturbative expansion of the initial entropy density fluctuations around the average, keeping terms up to second order, to express the mean squared spatial anisotropy \(\langle \varepsilon_n^2 \rangle\) in terms of the one- and two-point correlation functions of the entropy density field. By adopting a specific model where the entropy density is the arithmetic average of the two thickness functions, they rewrite these correlation functions as integrals over the nuclear one-body density \(\rho^{(1)}\) and two-body density \(\rho^{(2)}\), and then evaluate the resulting expression in the limit where the nucleon form factor is approximated by a Dirac delta function.

## Key numbers

* For a Gaussian intrinsic density with quadrupole deformation \(\beta_{20}\), the mean squared elliptic anisotropy is \(\langle \varepsilon_2^2 \rangle = \frac{1}{A} + \frac{3}{4\pi} \beta_{20}^2\), where \(A\) is the nuclear mass number.
* For the same model with octupole deformation \(\beta_{30}\), the mean squared triangular anisotropy is \(\langle \varepsilon_3^2 \rangle = \frac{16}{3\pi A} + \frac{2048}{245\pi^3} \beta_{30}^2\).
* The perturbative expression for \(\langle \varepsilon_n^2 \rangle\) in Eq. (6) is accurate at the percent level for collisions of nuclei with \(A \sim 100\).

## Context

This work provides a rigorous quantum-mechanical foundation for interpreting anisotropic flow measurements in heavy-ion collisions as probes of nuclear ground-state correlations, extending earlier phenomenological studies that used the rigid-rotor model (Jia 2021, Giacalone 2021) or independent-source calculations (Bhalerao 2006). The derived eccentricity operators \(\hat{\mathcal{E}}_n\) bear resemblance to the two-body Kumar quadrupole operator (Kumar 1972) but are directly measurable through elliptic and higher-harmonic flow in symmetric nucleus-nucleus collisions. The framework enables future comparisons between collider data and state-of-the-art ab initio nuclear-structure calculations, such as those using projected generator coordinate methods (Frosini 2021, Hagen 2022), to disentangle the contributions of static deformation, shape fluctuations, and non-collective correlations to the measured two-body correlations.
