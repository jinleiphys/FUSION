# Overview: Jet quenching with machine learning

> Jets are suppressed and modified in heavy ion collisions, which serve as powerful probes to the properties of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP). Attributed to the abundant information carried by the jet constituents and reconstructed substructures, plenty of interesting applications of machine learning techniques have been made on a jet-by-jet basis to study the jet quenching phenomena. Here we review recent proceedings on this topic including the tasks of reconstructing jet momentum in heavy ion collisions, classifying quenched jets and unquenched jets, identifying jet energy loss, locating the jet creation points as well as distinguishing between quark- and gluon-initiated jets in the QGP. Such jet-by-jet analyses will allow us to have a better handle on the jet reconstruction and selections to investigate the effects of jet modifications and push forward the long-standing goal of jet tomographic probes of the QGP.

## Key claim
Machine learning techniques applied on a jet-by-jet basis can mitigate selection biases and enable tomographic studies of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP), but the generalizability of these methods to realistic experimental data with correlated fluctuating backgrounds remains to be verified.

## Method
The paper reviews multiple supervised and unsupervised machine learning approaches applied to simulated jet samples from Monte Carlo generators (PYTHIA, JEWEL, HIJING, and a hybrid strong/weak coupling model). Various neural network architectures are employed including convolutional neural networks (CNNs) on jet images, recurrent neural networks (RNNs) on Lund plane sequences, graph neural networks on point clouds, and fully-connected networks on jet observables, for tasks such as jet momentum reconstruction, classification of quenched versus unquenched jets, prediction of jet energy loss, localization of jet creation points, and discrimination between quark- and gluon-initiated jets.

## Key numbers
- Jet momentum reconstruction using machine learning methods is demonstrated down to 20 GeV transverse momentum, outperforming the standard area-based method for jets with pT below 100 GeV and large radius.
- For locating initial jet production positions using a point cloud neural network, validating and testing root-mean-square errors are approximately 2.2 to 2.4 fm.
- Principal component analysis shows that the first 10 principal components explain 90% of the distributions of 31 jet substructure observables for quenched and unquenched jet samples.
- AutoEncoder methods find that 5 latent degrees of freedom capture above 90% of information in jet substructure correlations, while 10 encode almost full information.

## Context
This review extends prior work on jet quenching phenomenology (Mehtar-Tani 2013, Qin 2015) and machine learning applications in high-energy physics (Feickert 2021). It covers recent developments including the ML-based momentum reconstruction method of Haake and Loizides (2019) that was subsequently applied to ALICE experimental data, and the energy loss prediction approach of Du (2020, 2021) that enables initial energy selection to remove selection bias. The work enables future jet tomographic studies of the QGP, including path-length dependence of energy loss and detection of Mach cone signals, while highlighting the need for testing on realistic fluctuating backgrounds and cross-model generalizability.
