# Does the Sun Appear Brighter at Night in Neutrinos?

> We calculate accurately the number of solar neutrino events expected as a function of solar zenith angle, with and without neutrino oscillations, for detectors at the locations of Super-Kamiokande, SNO, and the Gran Sasso National Laboratory. Using different earth models to estimate geophysical uncertainties, and different solar models to estimate solar uncertainties, we evaluate distortions predicted by the MSW effect in the zenith angle distributions of solar neutrino events. The distortions are caused by oscillations and by $ν-e$ interactions in the earth that regenerate $ν_e$ from $ν_μ$ or $ν_τ$. We show that the first two moments of the zenith-angle distribution are more sensitive to the small mixing angle MSW solution than the conventionally studied day-night asymmetry. We present iso-sigma contours that illustrate the potential of Super-Kamiokande, SNO, BOREXINO, ICARUS and HERON/HELLAZ for detecting the earth regeneration effect at their actual locations (and at the equator). MSW solutions favored by the four pioneering solar neutrino experiments predict characteristic distortions for Super-Kamiokande, SNO, BOREXINO, and ICARUS that range from being unmeasurably small to $> 5σ$ (stat) after only a few years of observations.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the first two moments of the zenith-angle distribution of solar neutrino events are more sensitive to the small mixing angle MSW solution than the conventionally studied day-night asymmetry, but the authors note that their analysis uses only statistical errors and that detailed Monte Carlo simulations by experimental collaborations will be necessary to determine systematic uncertainties.

## Method

The authors numerically integrated the differential equations describing neutrino state evolution through accurate earth density models (primarily the Preliminary Reference Earth Model, PREM) to calculate survival probabilities for neutrinos traversing the earth at different zenith angles, avoiding approximations used in prior work. They computed expected event rates as functions of solar zenith angle for detectors at the locations of Super-Kamiokande, SNO, and Gran Sasso National Laboratory, using six different earth models to estimate geophysical uncertainties and the 1995 Bahcall-Pinsonneault standard solar model for solar uncertainties. They performed a chi-squared analysis to determine allowed regions in the Delta m^2 - sin^2 2 theta plane from the four pioneering solar neutrino experiments (Chlorine, Kamiokande, GALLEX, SAGE) including earth regeneration effects.

## Key numbers

- Best-fit small mixing angle (SMA) solution: Delta m^2 = 5.0 x 10^-6 eV^2, sin^2 2 theta = 8.7 x 10^-3, with chi^2_min = 0.25
- Best-fit large mixing angle (LMA) solution: Delta m^2 = 1.3 x 10^-5 eV^2, sin^2 2 theta = 0.63, with chi^2_min = 1.1
- Best-fit LOW solution: Delta m^2 = 1.1 x 10^-7 eV^2, sin^2 2 theta = 0.83, with chi^2_min = 6.9 (acceptable only at 96.5% C.L. when earth regeneration is included)
- For Super-Kamiokande, the fractional shift of the first moment Delta alpha/alpha_0 varies between -0.1% and 3% in the SMA region and between 0.5% and 7% in the LMA region
- For SNO, the fractional shift of the first moment ranges from -0.1% to 4.5% (SMA) and 0.3% to 15% (LMA)
- The undistorted second moment sigma_0 values: 37.9 degrees (Super-Kamiokande), 32.9 degrees (SNO), 34.9 degrees (Gran Sasso), 33.9 degrees (Homestake), 34.5 degrees (Baksan), 47.5 degrees (Equator)
- The fraction of a year during which neutrinos cross the outer core at the Kamioka site is approximately 7%
- The total range of electron number density due to imperfectly known core composition is about 1.5%
- MSW solutions predict distortions ranging from unmeasurably small to greater than 5 sigma (statistical) after only a few years of observations for Super-Kamiokande, SNO, BOREXINO, and ICARUS

## Context

This work extends previous calculations of earth regeneration effects by Baltz and Weneser (various years), Hata (various years), and Lisi and Montanino (various years) by using direct numerical integration rather than approximations, and by systematically evaluating geophysical uncertainties using six different earth models. The paper introduces moments of the zenith-angle distribution as a new diagnostic tool, finding them more sensitive to the small mixing angle MSW solution than the day-night asymmetry studied by the Kamiokande collaboration (1996). The results enable next-generation experiments (Super-Kamiokande, SNO, BOREXINO, ICARUS, HERON/HELLAZ) to test specific MSW solutions and identify the LOW solution, which is only viable when earth regeneration is included.
