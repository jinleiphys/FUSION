# Benchmark calculations of infinite neutron matter with realistic two- and three-nucleon potentials

> We present the equation of state of infinite neutron matter as obtained from highly-realistic Hamiltonians that include nucleon-nucleon and three-nucleon coordinate-space potentials. We benchmark three independent many-body methods: Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone (BBG), Fermi hypernetted chain/single-operator chain (FHNC/SOC), and auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC). We find them to provide similar equations of state when the Argonne $v_{18}$ and the Argonne $v_{6}^\prime$ nucleon-nucleon potentials are used in combination with the Urbana IX three-body force. Only at densities larger than about 1.5 the nuclear saturation density ($ρ_0 = 0.16\,\rm{fm}^{-3}$) the FHNC/SOC energies are appreciably lower than the other two approaches. The AFDMC calculations carried out with all of the Norfolk potentials fitted to reproduce the experimental trinucleon ground-state energies and $nd$ doublet scattering length yield unphysically bound neutron matter, associated with the formation of neutron droplets. Including tritium $β$-decay in the fitting procedure, as in the second family of Norfolk potentials, mitigates but does not completely resolve this problem. An excellent agreement between the BBG and AFDMC results is found for the subset of Norfolk interactions that do not make neutron-matter collapse, while the FHNC/SOC equations of state are moderately softer.

## Key claim

The paper benchmarks three independent many-body methods (BBG, FHNC/SOC, and AFDMC) for infinite neutron matter and finds that they produce similar equations of state when using Argonne potentials with Urbana IX three-body forces, but that FHNC/SOC energies become appreciably lower than the other two methods only at densities above about 1.5 times nuclear saturation density. The Norfolk potentials that fit trinucleon ground-state energies and nd doublet scattering length produce unphysically bound neutron matter, while including tritium beta-decay in the fitting procedure mitigates but does not completely resolve this problem.

## Method

The authors computed the equation of state of infinite neutron matter using three independent many-body methods: Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone (BBG) theory with the hole-line expansion and Bethe-Goldstone equation solved in partial waves up to J_max=9; Fermi hypernetted chain/single-operator chain (FHNC/SOC) with variational wave functions including spin-orbit correlations and up to 29 coupled integral equations; and auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC). They employed the Argonne v18 and Argonne v6' nucleon-nucleon potentials with Urbana IX three-body forces, as well as the first and second generation Norfolk (NV2+3) chiral effective field theory potentials, which differ in how the three-nucleon force low-energy constants c_D and c_E are fitted.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: ρ0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}
- FHNC/SOC energies become appreciably lower than BBG and AFDMC only at densities larger than about 1.5 ρ0
- For NV2 potentials, Class I fits about 2700 data points with χ^2/datum ≤ 1.1; Class II fits about 3700 data points with χ^2/datum ≤ 1.4
- The NV2+3 c_D and c_E values for model Ia: c_D = 3.666, c_E = -1.638; for model Ia*: c_D = -0.635, c_E = -0.090
- The NV2+3 c_D and c_E values for model Ib: c_D = -2.061, c_E = -0.982; for model Ib*: c_D = -4.71, c_E = 0.55
- The NV2+3 c_D and c_E values for model IIa: c_D = 1.278, c_E = -1.029; for model IIa*: c_D = -0.61, c_E = -0.35
- The NV2+3 c_D and c_E values for model IIb: c_D = -4.480, c_E = -0.412; for model IIb*: c_D = -5.25, c_E = 0.05

## Context

This work extends previous benchmark calculations by Piarulli (2019) that only included nucleon-nucleon potentials, now incorporating three-nucleon forces. It addresses the tension between microscopic potentials that accurately describe light nuclear systems (such as AV18+IL7, which fails in neutron matter) and those that work well for infinite matter (such as AV18+UIX, which is less accurate for light nuclei). The results enable better assessment of systematic errors from many-body methods versus uncertainties from the nuclear Hamiltonian, which is critical for constraining nuclear energy density functionals and interpreting neutron star observations from GW170817 and NICER.
