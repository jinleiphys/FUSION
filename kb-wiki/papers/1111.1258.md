# From nuclei to nuclear pasta

> To provide a practitioner's introduction to the study of the nuclear pasta, this article presents a brief review of the evolution of the methods used to study nucleon dynamics. This is followed with a more complete description of the extension of classical molecular dynamics (CMD) to infinite systems. The different techniques used to characterize the nuclear structure, such as cluster recognition algorithms, radial correlation functions and the Minkowski functionals are presented. We reach full functionality of the extended CMD when the Coulomb interaction is introduced through two common methodologies and its effect on the structure is presented. We close the article with some final remarks.

## Key claim

This paper provides a practitioner's introduction to studying nuclear pasta using classical molecular dynamics (CMD), arguing that CMD can describe all limiting behaviors of nuclear matter (non-equilibrium dynamics, hydrodynamic flow, and phase changes) without adjustable parameters, but the authors acknowledge that quantum effects such as Pauli blocking are absent and that at extremely low energies and frozen-like structures, momentum-transferring collisions cease to be an important factor in deciding the stable configuration.

## Method

The authors apply classical molecular dynamics with the Pandharipande potential (a combination of Yukawa potentials) to simulate infinite nuclear matter in a cubic cell with periodic boundary conditions, using systems of 2000 to 3333 nucleons at fixed proton fractions of x=0.3 or 0.5. They cool the systems from T=1.0 MeV to T=0.1 MeV using an Andersen thermostat, then characterize the resulting structures using the Minimum Spanning Tree cluster recognition algorithm (with clusterization radius r_cl=3.0 fm), pair correlation functions, and Minkowski functionals. The Coulomb interaction from the embedding electron gas is incorporated through two separate methods: Thomas-Fermi screened Coulomb potential and Ewald summation.

## Key numbers

- Equilibrium density of the potential: ρ₀=0.016 fm⁻³
- Binding energy at equilibrium: E(ρ₀)=16 MeV/nucleon
- Compressibility values: 250 MeV ("Medium") or 535 MeV ("Stiff")
- Cutoff radius for the nuclear potential: r_c=5.4 fm
- Densities studied range from ρ=0.01 fm⁻³ (≈ρ₀/15) to nearly ρ₀
- Systems studied: 1000 protons with total nucleons A=2000 (for x=0.5) and A=3333 (for x=0.3)
- Final temperature of configurations: T=0.1 MeV, reached by cooling in steps of 0.02 MeV
- Screening length for Thomas-Fermi method: λ=10 fm
- Ewald summation cutoff: n_c=8, giving relative error in energy of O(10⁻⁶)
- Example cluster distribution shown for 3,333 nucleons at x=0.3, T=0.3 MeV, ρ=0.009 fm⁻³
- Example radial correlation function shown for 2,000 nucleons at x=0.5, T=0.1 MeV, ρ≈0.043ρ₀ in a cell of L=28.77 fm

## Context

This review extends the work of Pandharipande and coworkers (1990) who developed the classical molecular dynamics potential for nuclear matter, and complements quantum molecular dynamics approaches (such as those by Watanabe and coworkers, 2003) and static models (liquid-drop, Thomas-Fermi, Hartree-Fock) that have been used to study nuclear pasta. The paper enables direct comparison of two Coulomb treatment methods (screened potential and Ewald summation) within the same dynamical framework, addressing the model dependence noted in prior work by Watanabe and coworkers (2003). The CMD approach has been previously applied to neck fragmentation (Cherno and coworkers, 2002), phase transitions (Barranco and coworkers, 2007), and isoscaling (Dorso and coworkers, 2011).
