# Future directions for probing two and three nucleon short-range correlations at high energies

> We summarize recent progress in the studies of the short-rang correlations (SRC) in nuclei in high energy electron and hadron nucleus scattering and suggest directions for the future high energy studies aimed at establishing detailed structure of two-nucleon SRCs, revealing structure of three nucleon SRC correlations and discovering non-nucleonic degrees of freedom in nuclei.

# Future directions for probing two and three nucleon short-range correlations at high energies

## Key claim

Short-range correlations (SRC) in nuclei can be systematically studied using high-energy probes with large energy-momentum transfer (q0 >= 1 GeV and q >= 1 GeV/c), provided the resolution scale is adjusted as a function of the probed nucleon momentum, thereby circumventing the no-go theorem of Amado which claimed that observing the high-momentum component of the nucleus is in principle impossible.

## Method

The authors review recent experimental results from high-momentum-transfer (e,e') data at Jefferson Lab, (p,2pn) data at BNL, and (e,e'pp) and (e,e'pn) data at Jefferson Lab, analyzing them within the framework of two key concepts: (1) the partial closure approximation for inclusive (e,e') processes at x > 1 and Q^2 >= 1.5 GeV^2, where final state interactions at longitudinal distances <= 1.2 fm cancel in ratios; and (2) hard exclusive processes that instantaneously remove a nucleon from an SRC, probing the nuclear decay function. The authors also develop a generalized eikonal approximation (GEA) to calculate final state interactions at large Q.

## Key numbers

- More than ~90% of all nucleons with momenta k >= 300 MeV/c belong to two-nucleon SRC correlations.
- For a given proton with momenta 600 >= k >= 300 MeV/c, the probability to belong to a pn correlation is ~18 times larger than to belong to a pp correlation.
- The probability for a nucleon to have momentum >= 300 MeV/c in medium nuclei is ~25%.
- 2N SRC are mostly built of two nucleons, not six-quark configurations or Delta N, Delta Delta states, which constitute no more than 20% of the 2N SRCs.
- The proposed kinematics for future studies require q0 >= 1 GeV and q >= 1 GeV/c, with Q^2 = 2 to 4 GeV^2 for electron scattering experiments and proton beam energies starting at 6 GeV at hadron facilities.

## Context

This work extends the authors' own theoretical predictions dating back to Frankfurt and Strikman (1975, 1981, 1988) which argued that previous failed attempts to observe SRC in the 1950s and 1960s were due to insufficient energy and momentum transfer, leading to complicated primary interactions and enhanced final state contributions. The paper builds on recent experimental confirmations from Jefferson Lab (Kim1, Kim2) and BNL (eip2, eip3) that validated these predictions, and it outlines a program for future high-energy studies at JLab, J-PARC, and GSI to establish the detailed structure of two-nucleon SRCs, reveal three-nucleon SRCs, and discover non-nucleonic degrees of freedom in nuclei. The work also connects to calculations of two-nucleon momentum distributions by Schiavilla and Alvioli (2007) for a range of nuclei.
