# What to expect from microscopic nuclear modelling for k$_{\rm eff}$ calculations ?

> Comparisons between predicted and benchmark k$_{\rm eff}$ values from criticality-safety systems are often used as metrics to estimate the quality of evaluated nuclear data libraries. Relevant nuclear data for these critical systems generally come from a mixture of expert knowledge and phenomenological predictions. In the present work, we use solely microscopic nuclear modelling from TALYS to estimate actinides cross sections and angular distributions, and we compare the calculated MCNP k$_{\rm eff}$ values for fast systems between the JEFF-3.3 evaluated library, phenomenological and microscopic modelling. The conclusion is that even if the evaluated library leads to the most adequate results, the microscopic nuclear modelling can reach very similar results for these integral quantities. It demonstrates the remarkable advances in the recent decades of microscopic nuclear reaction ingredients for applied integral observables.

## Key claim

Even though the evaluated JEFF-3.3 library gives the most adequate results for criticality benchmarks, microscopic nuclear modelling (using purely theoretical inputs with no experimental normalization) can achieve very similar integral k_eff values after adjustment of model parameters, demonstrating remarkable recent progress in microscopic reaction theory.

## Method

The authors used the TALYS nuclear reaction code to produce cross sections and angular distributions for actinides and structural materials using two sets of models: phenomenological models (with global systematics) and microscopic models (based on Skyrme-HFB mean-field calculations for masses, level densities, fission barriers, and Gogny D1M HFB+QRPA for photon strength functions). These nuclear data were formatted into ACE files and used in MCNP6.1 to calculate k_eff values for 195 fast-spectrum criticality-safety benchmarks from the ICSBEP database, comparing results against the JEFF-3.3 evaluated library. For the adjusted models, 17 model parameters for 239Pu were sampled and selected to bring average C/E values close to 1.

## Key numbers

- JEFF-3.3 library: average C/E = 1.00175, standard deviation = 530 pcm for 195 benchmarks (all nuclides modified)
- Non-adjusted phenomenological models: average C/E = 0.98300, standard deviation = 6900 pcm for 195 benchmarks
- Non-adjusted microscopic models: average C/E = 0.99069, standard deviation = 4760 pcm for 195 benchmarks
- Adjusted phenomenological models for 239Pu: average C/E = 0.99999, standard deviation = 976 pcm for 90 benchmarks
- Adjusted microscopic models for 239Pu: average C/E = 0.99821, standard deviation = 675 pcm for 90 benchmarks
- JEFF-3.3 for the 90 239Pu-sensitive benchmarks: average C/E = 1.00179, standard deviation = 480 pcm
- Statistical uncertainty for each MCNP k_eff calculation: approximately 50 pcm
- Fission cross sections for 239Pu from all models are within a factor of 2 of JEFF-3.3 values

## Context

This work extends the line of research comparing microscopic nuclear modelling to traditional phenomenological approaches for applied nuclear data, building on prior developments in microscopic nuclear inputs by Goriely (2009, 2013) and Hilaire (2006). It directly compares against the JEFF-3.3 evaluated library (Plompen, 2020) and the TENDL approach (Koning, 2019). The results enable future improvements in microscopic model parameterizations for differential observables, while demonstrating that purely theoretical predictions can now approach the quality of evaluated libraries for integral criticality benchmarks, which is particularly relevant for nuclei where no experimental data exist.
