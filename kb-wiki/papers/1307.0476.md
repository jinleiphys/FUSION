# Shear and bulk viscosities of a photon gas at low temperature

> We explore the viscosities of a photon gas by means of the Euler-Heisenberg effective theory and quantum electrodynamics at zero electron chemical potential. We find parametric estimates that show a very large shear viscosity and an extremely small bulk viscosity (reflecting the very weak coupling simultaneously with a very approximate dilatation invariance). The system is of some interest because it exemplifies very neatly the influence of the breaking of scale invariance on the bulk viscosity.

## Key claim

The shear viscosity of a photon gas is parametrically huge, while the bulk viscosity is extremely tiny; however, the bulk viscosity is not zero as the Euler-Heisenberg effective theory would suggest, because the photon acquires a thermal mass in full QED that breaks scale invariance via an exponential factor \(e^{-m_e/T}\) that is non-analytic at \(T=0\).

## Method

The authors use quasiparticle kinetic theory with the linearized Boltzmann (Uehling-Uhlenbeck) equation for photons, employing the photon-photon scattering amplitude from the Euler-Heisenberg effective Lagrangian for the shear viscosity and from one-loop QED for the bulk viscosity. The shear viscosity is estimated via a relaxation-time approximation using the thermally-averaged elastic cross section \(\gamma\gamma\to\gamma\gamma\); the bulk viscosity is computed by evaluating the source term in the Boltzmann equation that depends on the deviation from conformal invariance, which is controlled by the photon thermal mass \(m_\gamma\) from one-loop QED.

## Key numbers

* Shear viscosity parametric estimate: \(\eta \sim \eta_0 \frac{1}{\alpha^4} \frac{m_e^8}{T^5} \simeq \eta_0 \frac{(1.4\times 10^{19}\ \mathrm{eV})^3}{T^5(\mathrm{K})}\), with \(\alpha \simeq 1/137\).
* Bulk viscosity (inelastic processes, long-time limit): \(\zeta = \zeta_0 \frac{1}{\alpha^4} \frac{m_e^{17}}{T^{14}} e^{-2m_e/T}\).
* Bulk viscosity (elastic processes, short-time limit): \(\zeta = \zeta'_0 \frac{1}{\alpha^2} \frac{m_e^{13}}{T^{10}} e^{-2m_e/T}\).
* Photon thermal mass from one-loop QED: \(m_\gamma^2 = \frac{8\alpha}{\sqrt{2\pi}} m_e^{3/2} T^{1/2} e^{-m_e/T}\).
* Entropy density of the photon gas to order \(\alpha\): \(s(T) = \frac{4\pi^2}{45} T^3 - \frac{4\alpha}{3\sqrt{\pi}} m_e^{3/2} T^{3/2} e^{-m_e/T}\).
* Speed of sound squared: \(v_s^2 = \frac{1}{3} - \frac{5\alpha}{3 \pi^{5/2}} \frac{m_e^{5/2}}{T^{5/2}} e^{-m_e/T}\).
* Total cross section for \(\gamma\gamma\to\gamma\gamma\) at low energy: \(\sigma_{\gamma\gamma\to\gamma\gamma} = \frac{973}{10125\pi} \frac{\alpha^4 \omega^6}{m_e^8}\).

## Context

This work extends the study of transport coefficients in weakly coupled relativistic gases, contrasting with the strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma studied at RHIC (Romatschke 2007, Csernai 2006, Dobado 2008) where the shear viscosity over entropy density is near a minimum. It also parallels calculations for the pion gas (Dobado 2011) and perturbative QCD (Arnold 2003). The key finding is that the Euler-Heisenberg effective theory (Heisenberg 1935) fails to capture the bulk viscosity because it cannot generate a photon thermal mass; full one-loop QED (Nieves 1983, Kapusta 2006, Andersen 2001) is required. The results enable future studies of photon gas transport at higher temperatures or with finite electron density, where Compton scattering would dominate.
