# Pairing and specific heat in hot nuclei

> The thermodynamics of pairing phase-transition in nuclei is studied in the canonical ensemble and treating the pairing correlations in a finite-temperature variation after projection BCS approach (FT-VAP). Due to the restoration of particle number conservation, the pairing gap and the specific heat calculated in the FT-VAP approach vary smoothly with the temperature, indicating a gradual transition from the superfluid to the normal phase, as expected in finite systems. We have checked that the predictions of the FT-VAP approach are very accurate when compared to the results obtained by an exact diagonalization of the pairing Hamiltonian. The influence of pairing correlations on specific heat is analysed for the isotopes $^{161,162}$Dy and $^{171,172}$Yb. It is shown that the FT-VAP approach, applied with a level density provided by mean field calculations and supplemented, at high energies, by the level density of the back-shifted Fermi gas model, can approximate reasonably well the main properties of specific heat extracted from experimental data. However, the detailed shape of the calculated specific heat is rather sensitive to the assumption made for the mean field.

## Key claim

The finite-temperature variation after projection BCS approach (FT-VAP) predicts a smooth, gradual transition from the superfluid to the normal phase in hot nuclei, in contrast to the sharp second-order transition predicted by standard FT-BCS; however, the detailed shape of the calculated specific heat is rather sensitive to the assumption made for the mean field, and the agreement with experimental data requires supplementing the mean-field level density at high energies with the back-shifted Fermi gas model.

## Method

The authors studied pairing correlations in the canonical ensemble using a Hamiltonian with single-particle energies from Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SLy4 force) or Relativistic Mean-Field (PK1 force) calculations and a seniority-type pairing interaction active in a 3 MeV window around the Fermi level. They applied the FT-VAP approach, which restores particle number conservation via projection, and compared it to exact diagonalization of the pairing Hamiltonian in the truncated space. The contribution of states outside the pairing window was included via the grand-canonical partition function of a non-interacting system, reduced to the canonical representation by the saddle point approximation, and for comparison with experiment the non-interacting partition function was replaced by the back-shifted Fermi gas model.

## Key numbers

- Pairing strength G values for the 3 MeV window: for neutrons in Yb (HF) G = 0.260 MeV, Yb (RMF) G = 0.270 MeV, Dy (RMF) G = 0.320 MeV, Dy (HF) G = 0.284 MeV; for protons: Yb (HF) G = 0.332 MeV, Yb (RMF) G = 0.345 MeV, Dy (RMF) G = 0.385 MeV, Dy (HF) G = 0.327 MeV.
- The pairing strength G was fixed to give a BCS pairing gap of approximately 0.8 MeV for both protons and neutrons.
- A small pairing re-entrance effect was observed around T = 0.5 MeV for 161Dy and around 0.2-0.3 MeV for 171Yb.
- For a schematic two-level system, the heat capacity maximum occurs at T approximately 0.417 times the level spacing Delta epsilon; for Yb nuclei with RMF-PK1, Delta epsilon_n approximately 0.26 MeV gives an expected bump around 0.1 MeV, consistent with the observed bump at 0.07-0.08 MeV.
- The specific heat is strongly suppressed by pairing correlations below T approximately 0.5 MeV.
- Three pairing strengths corresponding to BCS gaps of 0.6, 0.8, and 1.0 MeV were tested for neutrons in 162Dy.

## Context

This work extends the FT-VAP approach, previously tested on schematic Richardson model (Gambacurta 2012), to realistic descriptions of 161,162Dy and 171,172Yb isotopes, whose level densities were measured by the Oslo group (Melby 1999). It contradicts the earlier interpretation by Schiller (2001) that the S-shaped specific heat in these isotopes is generated by the superfluid-to-normal transition, showing instead that bumps can also arise from non-interacting systems with isolated single-particle states near the Fermi energy. The study enables future work incorporating continuum contributions and low-lying collective states, which are not accounted for by the pairing Hamiltonian used here.
