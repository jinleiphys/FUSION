# Quantification of Uncertainties in Nuclear Density Functional theory

> Reliable predictions of nuclear properties are needed as much to answer fundamental science questions as in applications such as reactor physics or data evaluation. Nuclear density functional theory is currently the only microscopic, global approach to nuclear structure that is applicable throughout the nuclear chart. In the past few years, a lot of effort has been devoted to setting up a general methodology to assess theoretical uncertainties in nuclear DFT calculations. In this paper, we summarize some of the recent progress in this direction. Most of the new material discussed here will be be published in separate articles.

## Key claim

Nuclear density functional theory (DFT) is inherently an imperfect model of the nucleus, and the paper summarizes recent progress in quantifying theoretical uncertainties from model errors, fitting errors, and implementation errors, with most new material to be published in separate articles.

## Method

The authors describe the standard approach of minimizing a composite chi-squared function with multiple data types (masses, radii, etc.) weighted by estimated theoretical errors per data type, as done by the UNEDF collaboration. They then present a Bayesian statistical approach that treats model parameters as random variables, using Gaussian Process techniques to construct a response function from 200 DFT calculations sampling a 12-dimensional hypercube of parameter space, from which posterior distributions are extracted.

## Key numbers

- The UNEDF1 parametrization involves 12 DFT parameters, with intervals for each parameter defined as [x*_i - 3σ_i, x*_i + 3σ_i] around the UNEDF1 value and its standard deviation
- Each DFT calculation of the UNEDF1 chi-squared function requires approximately 800 cores for 15 minutes
- The analysis of proton r.m.s. radii covers 27 spherical Ca, Ni, Sn, and Pb isotopes
- The posterior distribution was based on 200 DFT calculations sampling the 12-dimensional hypercube

## Context

This work extends the covariance analysis approach used by Reinhard (2013), Piekarewicz (2012), and Kortelainen (2013) for error propagation in DFT, and introduces Bayesian methods previously applied to semi-microscopic mass models by Goriely (2014). The methodology enables quantification of theoretical uncertainties for predictions of neutron drip lines and fission barriers in actinides, with results to be published separately by McDonnell (2014).
