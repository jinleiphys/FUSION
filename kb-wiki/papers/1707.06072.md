# Low-lying electric-dipole strengths of Ca, Ni, and Sn isotopes imprinted on total reaction cross sections

> Low-lying electric-dipole ($E1$) strength of a neutron-rich nucleus contains information on neutron-skin thickness, deformation, and shell evolution. We discuss the possibility of making use of total reaction cross sections on $^{40}$Ca, $^{120}$Sn, and $^{208}$Pb targets to probe the $E1$ strength of neutron-rich Ca, Ni, and Sn isotopes. They exhibit large enhancement of the $E1$ strength at neutron number $N > 28$, $50$, and $82$, respectively, due to a change of the single-particle orbits near the Fermi surface participating in the transitions. The density distributions and the electric-multipole strength functions of those isotopes are calculated by the Hartree-Fock+BCS and the canonical-basis-time-dependent-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov methods, respectively, using three kinds of Skyrme-type effective interaction. The nuclear and Coulomb breakup processes are respectively described with the Glauber model and the equivalent photon method in which the effect of finite-charge distribution is taken into account. The three Skyrme interactions give different results for the total reaction cross sections because of different Coulomb breakup contributions. The contribution of the low-lying $E1$ strength is amplified when the low-incident energy is chosen. With an appropriate choice of the incident energy and target nucleus, the total reaction cross section can be complementary to the Coulomb excitation for analysing the low-lying $E1$ strength of unstable nuclei.

## Key claim

Total reaction cross sections on heavy targets (⁴⁰Ca, ¹²⁰Sn, ²⁰⁸Pb) can serve as a complementary probe to Coulomb excitation for extracting low-lying E1 strength of neutron-rich Ca, Ni, and Sn isotopes, provided the incident energy is chosen low enough to amplify the low-lying E1 contribution relative to the giant resonance region.

## Method

The authors calculated ground-state densities and electric-multipole strength functions for Ca, Ni, and Sn isotopes using the Hartree-Fock+BCS and canonical-basis-time-dependent-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov methods, respectively, with three Skyrme-type effective interactions (SkM*, SLy4, SkI3). Nuclear breakup cross sections were computed with the Glauber model in the Nucleon-Target formalism, while Coulomb breakup cross sections were evaluated using the equivalent photon method with a finite-charge distribution correction for the target nucleus. The total reaction cross section was taken as the sum of nuclear and Coulomb breakup contributions.

## Key numbers

- For ¹³⁴Sn (N=84), the root-mean-square radii of the outermost 1f₇/₂ neutron orbit are 5.96 fm (SkM*), 6.13 fm (SLy4), and 6.44 fm (SkI3); corresponding single-particle energies are -3.21 MeV, -2.15 MeV, and -1.53 MeV
- For ⁸⁰Ni (N=52), the single-particle energies of the dominant outermost 1d₅/₂ orbit are -2.72 MeV (SkM*), -1.62 MeV (SLy4), and -1.31 MeV (SkI3); rms radii are 5.46 fm, 5.71 fm, and 6.05 fm
- For ⁵⁰Ca (N=30), the outermost 1p₃/₂ orbit single-particle energies are -5.76 MeV (SkM*), -6.59 MeV (SLy4), and -5.16 MeV (SkI3); rms radii are 4.60 fm, 4.47 fm, and 4.79 fm
- The finite-charge distribution correction reduces Coulomb breakup cross sections of ¹²⁰Sn by 1-3% for ⁴⁰Ca target, 3-4% for ¹²⁰Sn target, and 4-5% for ²⁰⁸Pb target at 100-1000A MeV
- For ¹¹⁸Sn+⁴⁰Ca at 77A MeV, calculated σR values are 4.20 barn (SkM*), 4.20 barn (SLy4), and 4.18 barn (SkI3); experimental value is 4.89±0.53 barn
- For ²⁰⁸Pb+⁴⁰Ca at 77A MeV, calculated σR values are 5.46 barn (SkM*), 5.43 barn (SLy4), and 5.47 barn (SkI3); experimental value is 5.33±0.50 barn

## Context

This work extends the systematic analysis of low-lying E1 strength enhancement at neutron numbers N>28, 50, and 82 for Ca, Ni, and Sn isotopes, respectively, previously predicted by Ebata (2014). It builds on the established use of total reaction cross sections for nuclear size studies (Horiuchi 2016, Takechi 2010, Kanungo 2011) by instead employing heavy targets to amplify Coulomb breakup contributions. The approach enables future experimental extraction of low-lying E1 strength for unstable nuclei where photoabsorption reactions are impractical, complementing Coulomb excitation measurements in inverse kinematics.
