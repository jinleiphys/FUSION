# Towards shell model interactions with credible uncertainties

> Background: The nuclear shell model is a powerful framework for predicting nuclear structure observables, but relies on interaction matrix elements fit to experimental data as its inputs. Extending the shell model's applicability, particularly toward dripline nuclei, requires efficient fitting methods and credible uncertainty quantification. Traditional approaches face computational challenges and may underestimate uncertainties. Purpose: We develop and test a framework combining eigenvector continuation and Markov chain Monte Carlo to efficiently fit shell model interaction matrix elements and quantify their uncertainties. Methods: Eigenvector continuation is used to emulate shell model calculations, reducing computational costs. The emulator enables Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling to optimize interaction matrix elements and rigorously assess parametric uncertainties. The framework is benchmarked using the USDB interaction in the sd shell. Results: The emulator reproduces the USDB interaction with negligible error, validating its use in shell model fitting applications. However, we find that to obtain credible predictive intervals, the model defect of the shell model itself, rather than experimental or emulator error, must be taken into account in order to obtain credible uncertainties. Conclusions: The proposed framework provides an efficient and rigorous approach for fitting shell model interactions and quantifying uncertainties. Further, the normality assumption used in the past appears sufficient to describe the distribution of interaction matrix elements. However, it is crucial to account for model correlations to avoid underestimating uncertainties.

## Key claim

The combination of eigenvector continuation and Markov chain Monte Carlo provides an efficient and rigorous framework for fitting shell model interactions and quantifying uncertainties, but credible predictive intervals require accounting for the model defect of the shell model itself rather than just experimental or emulator error.

## Method

The authors used eigenvector continuation (EC) to emulate full configuration interaction shell model calculations, constructing a reduced basis from 40 trial interactions with 10 eigenstates each, sampled uniformly around the USD interaction with 40% relative uncertainty. They then employed Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling to optimize the 66 interaction matrix elements (3 single-particle energies and 63 two-body matrix elements) and assess parametric uncertainties, benchmarking the framework against the USDB interaction in the sd-shell using 77 ground-state energies and 531 excitation energies across 77 nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The EC emulator with N=40 trial interactions and m=10 eigenstates reproduced the USDB interaction with an interaction root-mean-squared deviation (IRMS) of 12 keV relative to USDB, comparable to the full shell model accuracy of 12 keV.
- The EC emulator evaluated all 608 energy levels across 77 nuclei in approximately 5 seconds (serial) with a memory footprint of 15 GB, compared to approximately 4000 seconds for the full shell model.
- For test interactions with IRMS values up to 250 keV relative to USD, the data root-mean-squared deviation (DRMS) of the emulator was less than 30 keV, with more than half of errors below 6 keV.
- The LC chi-squared minimization fit with 56 varied linear combinations achieved a DRMS of 133 keV and IRMS of 12 keV relative to USDB, consistent with results using the full shell model.
- The theoretical uncertainty of the untruncated shell model is greater than 150 keV, an order of magnitude larger than the typical emulator error of less than 10 keV.

## Context

This work extends the uncertainty quantification efforts for the USDB interaction by Fox (2020, 2023) and Yoshida (2018), which were limited to approximate statistical methods due to computational costs. The framework enables rigorous uncertainty-quantified interaction fits beyond the sd-shell, particularly in neutron-rich model spaces essential for studying r-process nucleosynthesis, where traditional shell model calculations become computationally prohibitive.
