# Probing the possibility of hotspots on the central neutron star in HESS J1731-347

> The X-ray spectra of the neutron stars located in the centers of supernova remnants Cas A and HESS J1731-347 are well fit with carbon atmosphere models. These fits yield plausible neutron star sizes for the known or estimated distances to these supernova remnants. The evidence in favor of the presence of a pure carbon envelope at the neutron star surface is rather indirect and is based on the assumption that the emission is generated uniformly by the entire stellar surface. Although this assumption is supported by the absence of pulsations, the observational upper limit on the pulsed fraction is not very stringent. In an attempt to quantify this evidence, we investigate the possibility that the observed spectrum of the neutron star in HESS J1731-347 is a combination of the spectra produced in a hydrogen atmosphere of the hotspots and of the cooler remaining part of the neutron star surface. The lack of pulsations in this case has to be explained either by a sufficiently small angle between the neutron star spin axis and the line of sight, or by a sufficiently small angular distance between the hotspots and the neutron star rotation poles. As the observed flux from a non-uniformly emitting neutron star depends on the angular distribution of the radiation emerging from the atmosphere, we have computed two new grids of pure carbon and pure hydrogen atmosphere model spectra accounting for Compton scattering. Using new hydrogen models, we have evaluated the probability of a geometry that leads to a pulsed fraction below the observed upper limit to be about 8.2 %. Such a geometry thus seems to be rather improbable but cannot be excluded at this stage.

## Key claim

The observed X-ray spectrum of the neutron star in HESS J1731-347 can be fit by a two-component hydrogen atmosphere model with hotspots, but the probability that the geometry yields a pulsed fraction below the observed upper limit of about 7.5% is only about 8.2%, meaning such a geometry is improbable but cannot be ruled out.

## Method

The authors computed new grids of pure carbon and pure hydrogen neutron star atmosphere model spectra that account for Compton scattering, using either the Kompaneets equation or an exact angle-dependent redistribution function. They fit the observed XMM-Newton spectrum of the central compact object in HESS J1731-347 with these models, first using a single carbon atmosphere and then a two-component hydrogen atmosphere model representing hotspots and a cooler remaining surface. They then modeled light curves for a slowly rotating neutron star with two identical hotspots, varying the inclination angle and the magnetic colatitude, to determine which geometries produce a pulsed fraction below the observational upper limit.

## Key numbers

- The probability of a geometry that leads to a pulsed fraction below the observed upper limit of 7.5% is about 8.2%, assuming random observer inclination and random magnetic field inclination to the rotation axis.
- The best-fit two-component hydrogen model yields hotspot effective temperature T_sp = 4.55 MK and cold component temperature T_c = 2.04 MK, with a fractional emitting area of the hot component delta = 0.023 plus or minus 0.003.
- The best-fit interstellar hydrogen column density is N_H = 2.13 times 10 to the 22 per square centimeter.
- The reduced chi-squared for the two-component fit is 1.08 with 893 degrees of freedom, corresponding to a null-hypothesis probability of about 0.04.
- The reduced chi-squared for the single carbon atmosphere fit is about 1.07 with 895 degrees of freedom.
- The hotspot angular radius varies from 10 degrees to 16 degrees for different combinations of inclination and magnetic colatitude.
- The maximum possible pulsed fraction for the adopted neutron star parameters is about 25%.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Klochkov et al. (2015) on the central compact object in HESS J1731-347, who previously fit its X-ray spectrum with carbon atmosphere models assuming uniform emission. The authors test the alternative hypothesis that the emission comes from hydrogen atmosphere hotspots, similar to the analysis by Bogdanov (2014) for the central compact object in Kes 79, which shows strong pulsations of about 64%. The new model grids with Compton scattering and angular distributions enable future studies of non-uniformly emitting neutron stars, including those in quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries as analyzed by Elshamouty et al. (2016).
