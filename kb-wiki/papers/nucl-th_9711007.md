# Quantum-Mechanical Analysis of Single-Particle Level Density

> A quantum-mechanical calculation of the single-particle level (s.p.l.) density $g(ε)$ is carried on by using the connection with the single-particle Green's function. The relation between the imaginary part of Green's function and single-particle wave functions is used separately for the discrete and continuous states. Within the bound-states region the imaginary part of the Green's function is calculated by using the wronskian theorem. The Green's function corresponding to the continuum is written by using the regular and Jost solutions of the radial Schrodinger equation. The smooth part of the rapidly fluctuating s.p.l. density is calculated by means of the Strutinski procedure. The continuum component of the s.p.l. density has rather close values within either exact quantum-mechanical calculations with the Woods-Saxon (WS) potential, or Thomas-Fermi approximation with WS as well as finite-square potential wells, provided that the free-gas contribution is subtracted. A similar trend is obtained by means of the simple FGM formula for the s.p.l. density if the continuum effect is taken into account.

## Key claim

The continuum component of the single-particle level density obtained from exact quantum-mechanical calculations with the Woods-Saxon potential, from the Thomas-Fermi approximation with Woods-Saxon or finite square potential wells, and from the simple Fermi-gas model formula all yield rather close values, provided the free-gas contribution is subtracted and the continuum effect is taken into account. The agreement between the phenomenological Fermi-gas model formula and the quantum-mechanical calculation can be improved by using a lower average Fermi energy of approximately 20 MeV rather than the usual 38 MeV.

## Method

The single-particle level density was calculated quantum-mechanically via the imaginary part of the single-particle Green's function, using the wronskian theorem for bound states and regular and Jost solutions of the radial Schrödinger equation for continuum states. The smooth part of the rapidly fluctuating level density was extracted using the Strutinski smoothing procedure with a Gaussian averaging width of 1.2 times the harmonic-oscillator shell spacing and a Laguerre polynomial of order M=2. The free-gas contribution was subtracted for energies above zero, and the Thomas-Fermi approximation was also applied for comparison.

## Key numbers

- For the nucleus $^{56}$Fe, the smoothing parameter used was $\Gamma = 1.2\hbar\omega$, where $\hbar\omega$ is given by $\hbar\omega = 45/A^{1/3} - 25/A^{2/3}$ MeV.
- The Woods-Saxon potential depth for neutrons was $V_0 = -54 + 33(N-Z)/A$ MeV, with radius $R_V = 1.12A^{1/3} + 1.0$ fm and diffuseness $a = 0.70$ fm.
- The finite square well depth was $V_0 = -47 + 33(N-Z)/A$ MeV, with radius $R = 1.12A^{1/3} + 1.6$ fm.
- The infinite square well depth was $V_0 = -46 + 33(N-Z)/A$ MeV, with radius $R = 1.12A^{1/3} + 1.6$ fm.
- The average Fermi energy that reproduced the quantum-mechanical results was $\bar F \sim 20$ MeV, compared to the usual value of $F \sim 38$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the quantum-mechanical Green's function approach of Shlomo (1992, 1996, 1997) by calculating the imaginary part of the Green's function exactly for both bound and continuum regions separately, rather than using a smearing procedure or placing the nucleus in a finite box. The analysis also includes protons with Coulomb interaction, which Shlomo did not treat. The results enable the use of simpler Thomas-Fermi or Fermi-gas model formulas for the single-particle level density in statistical nuclear reaction calculations, provided the continuum effect and free-gas subtraction are properly handled.
