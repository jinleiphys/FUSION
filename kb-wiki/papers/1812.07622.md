# Linking nuclear reactions and nuclear structure to study exotic nuclei using the dispersive optical model

> The dispersive optical model (DOM) is employed to simultaneously describe elastic nucleon scattering data for ${}^{40}$Ca, ${}^{48}$Ca, and ${}^{208}$Pb as well as observables related to the ground state of these nuclei, with emphasis on the charge density. Such an analysis requires a fully non-local implementation of the DOM including its imaginary component. Illustrations are provided how ingredients thus generated provide critical components for the description of the $(d,p)$ and $(e,e'p)$ reaction. For the nuclei with $N > Z$ the neutron distribution is constrained by available elastic scattering and ground-state data thereby generating a prediction for the neutron skin. We identify ongoing developments including a non-local DOM analysis for ${}^{208}$Pb and point out possible extensions of the method to secure a successful extension of the DOM to rare isotopes.

## Key claim

The dispersive optical model (DOM) can simultaneously describe elastic nucleon scattering data and ground-state observables for stable nuclei, but the method's predictive power for neutron skins and reaction ingredients depends on a fully non-local implementation that includes the imaginary component of the self-energy; the author identifies ongoing developments for \({}^{208}\)Pb and notes that extension to rare isotopes requires further work on deuteron descriptions and knockout reactions.

## Method

The author employs the Green's functions method with a subtracted dispersion relation to construct a non-local nucleon self-energy that connects positive-energy elastic scattering data to negative-energy bound-state properties. This self-energy is fitted to elastic nucleon scattering cross sections, analyzing powers, total cross sections, charge densities, and level structures for \({}^{40}\)Ca, \({}^{48}\)Ca, and \({}^{208}\)Pb. The resulting DOM potentials are then used as ingredients for distorted-wave Born approximation calculations of \((d,p)\) transfer reactions and distorted-wave impulse approximation calculations of \((e,e'p)\) knockout reactions, without further adjustment to the reaction data.

## Key numbers

* Spectroscopic factors for \({}^{40}\)Ca from non-local DOM analysis of \((e,e'p)\) reaction: \(0.71\pm0.04\) for the \(0\mathrm{d}_{3/2}\) orbital and \(0.60\pm0.03\) for the \(1\mathrm{s}_{1/2}\) orbital, at outgoing proton energies of 100, 70, and 135 MeV.
* Neutron skin prediction for \({}^{48}\)Ca: \(R_n-R_p = 0.249\pm0.023\) fm from the non-local DOM analysis.
* Previous Nikhef spectroscopic factors for \({}^{40}\)Ca at 100 MeV: \(0.65\pm0.06\) for \(0\mathrm{d}_{3/2}\) and \(0.51\pm0.05\) for \(1\mathrm{s}_{1/2}\) (Kramer 1989).

## Context

This work extends the dispersive optical model beyond its original form (Mahaux 1991) by implementing a fully non-local version that constrains potentials with both positive-energy scattering data and negative-energy ground-state properties. The DOM provides an alternative to phenomenological optical potentials such as Koning and Delaroche (2003) for transfer reactions, and enables a consistent description of \((e,e'p)\) reaction ingredients without adjusting to the reaction data. The neutron skin prediction for \({}^{48}\)Ca is larger than the coupled-cluster calculation of Hagen (2016) and most mean-field calculations cited in Horowitz (2014), and will be tested by the CREX parity-violating electron scattering experiment at Jefferson Lab. Ongoing developments aim to extend the method to rare isotopes by improving the description of the deuteron and incorporating knockout reactions like \((p,pN)\).
