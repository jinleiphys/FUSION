# On Robust $β$-Spectra Shape Parameter Extraction

> Experimental extraction of $β$-shape functions, C(W), is challenging. Comparing different experimental $β$-shapes to each other and to those predicted by theory in a consistent manner is difficult. This difficulty is compounded when different parameterizations of the $β$-shape function are used. Usually some form of a power polynomial of the total electron energy is chosen for this parametrization. This choice results in extracted coefficients that are highly correlated, with their physical meaning and numerical value dependent on the order of polynomial chosen. This is true for both theoretical and experimental coefficients, and leads to challenges when comparing coefficients from polynomials of different orders. Accurately representing the highly correlated uncertainties is difficult and subtle. These issues impact the underlying physical interpretation of shape function parameters. We suggest an alternative approach based on orthogonal polynomials. Orthogonal polynomials offer more stable coefficient extraction which is less dependent on the order of the polynomial, allow for easier comparison between theory and experimental coefficients from polynomials of different orders, and offer some observations on simple physical meaning and on the statistical limits of the extracted coefficients.

## Key claim
The paper argues that using weighted orthogonal polynomials instead of standard power polynomials to parameterize β-spectrum shape functions yields more stable, less correlated coefficients that are largely independent of the chosen polynomial order, enabling more robust comparisons between experiments and theory.

## Method
The authors construct weighted orthogonal polynomials Φᵢ(W) via Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization using the allowed β-decay spectrum B(W,Z) as the weight function, with the starting basis {1, W, W², W³, ...}. They fit these orthogonal polynomials to experimental β-energy data from ³²P decay (online, preliminary dataset) over a 300–1550 keV range and compare the stability, correlations, and uncertainties of the extracted coefficients to those obtained from conventional power-polynomial fits.

## Key numbers
- For ³²P (Q_β = 1710.66(4) keV, T₁/₂ = 14.267(6) days), the orthogonal-polynomial coefficient b₁ changes from -0.0876(5) to -0.0952(11) when increasing the maximum polynomial from Φ₁ to Φ₃, while the power-polynomial coefficient a₁ changes from -0.0729(3) to -0.11(2) over the same order increase.
- The reduced χ² for the orthogonal-polynomial fit drops from 25.3 (Φ₀ only) to 1.02 (Φ₂ or Φ₃), identical to the power-polynomial reduced χ² at each corresponding order.
- The orthogonal-polynomial coefficient b₃ = 0.0015(14) for the third-order fit lies within the statistical uncertainties of the data, indicating higher-order terms are not statistically significant.

## Context
This work extends the use of orthogonal polynomials, already standard in γ-γ angular correlations (Rose 1967, Bill & Mills 2016), to β-spectroscopy. It directly addresses the challenges of coefficient instability and high correlations noted in power-polynomial extractions used in modern experiments (Kossert 2011, Paulsen 2020, Kostensalo 2023, Kostensalo 2024, Craveiro 2024). The approach enables more meaningful comparisons between experimental β-shape coefficients and theoretical predictions (Hayen 2018, Ramalho 2024, Seng 2025), and provides a clearer framework for setting limits on deviations from expected spectra, including potential beyond-standard-model physics (Gonzalez 2019, Falkowski 2021).
