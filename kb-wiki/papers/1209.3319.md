# Imaginary polarization as a way to surmount the sign problem in ab initio calculations of spin-imbalanced Fermi gases

> From ultracold atoms to quantum chromodynamics, reliable ab initio studies of strongly interacting fermions require numerical methods, typically in some form of quantum Monte Carlo calculation. Unfortunately, (non)relativistic systems at finite density (spin polarization) generally have a sign problem, such that those ab initio calculations are impractical. It is well-known, however, that in the relativistic case imaginary chemical potentials solve this problem, assuming the data can be analytically continued to the real axis. Is this feasible for nonrelativistic systems? Are the interesting features of the phase diagram accessible in this manner? By introducing complex chemical potentials, for real total particle number and imaginary polarization, the sign problem is avoided in the nonrelativistic case. To give a first answer to the above questions, we perform a mean-field study of the finite-temperature phase diagram of spin-1/2 fermions with imaginary polarization.

## Key claim

By introducing complex chemical potentials with a real average chemical potential and an imaginary polarization (asymmetry) parameter, the fermion sign problem in nonrelativistic quantum Monte Carlo calculations of spin-imbalanced Fermi gases can be avoided, and the resulting phase diagram can be analytically continued back to real polarizations; the mean-field analysis suggests that the (tri)critical point of the superfluid transition may lie within the accessible region of the phase diagram.

## Method

The authors perform a mean-field study of the finite-temperature phase diagram of spin-1/2 fermions at unitarity (infinite s-wave scattering length) by integrating out the fermion fields after introducing an auxiliary scalar field representing the superfluid order parameter. They compute the order-parameter potential as a function of temperature T, average chemical potential \(\bar{\mu}\), and imaginary asymmetry parameter \(h = i h_\text{I}\), then determine the critical temperature \(T_c\) from the gap equation where the fermion gap \(\Delta\) vanishes. The phase boundary in the \((T, h_\text{I})\) plane is analytically continued to real \(h\) using Padé approximants that respect the \(2\pi\)-periodicity in \(\beta h_\text{I}\).

## Key numbers

* The phase diagram is \(2\pi\)-periodic in \(\beta h_\text{I}\), with the domain \(\beta h_\text{I} \in [-\pi, \pi]\) shown in the study.
* For \(\bar{\mu} > 0\), the critical temperature \(T_c \to \infty\) when \(|\beta h_\text{I}| > |(\beta h_\text{I})_\infty| \simeq 2.397\) in mean-field approximation.
* The (tri)critical point in the physical \((T, h)\) plane is located at coordinates \((h_\text{cp}/\bar{\mu}, T_\text{cp}/\bar{\mu})\); the value \(|\beta_\text{cp} h_\text{cp}|\) is slightly lower than \(|(\beta h_\text{I})_\infty|\) at unitarity.
* For the pressure at \(T_0 = 1/\beta_0 \approx \bar{\mu}/2\) and \(0 \leq \beta_0 h \lesssim 1\), a fit to imaginary-\(h\) data with truncation order \(N_\text{max} = 2\) recovers the real-\(h\) result to good approximation.
* The radius of convergence for the grand canonical potential in powers of \((\beta h)^2\) is \(r \equiv |\beta h|_\text{max} = \pi\) for \(\Delta \equiv 0\) and \(\bar{\mu} > 0\), but \(r > \pi\) in the case of a finite gap \(\Delta\).

## Context

This work extends the technique of imaginary chemical potentials, previously used in relativistic quantum field theories such as lattice QCD (de Forcrand 2006) and the Gross-Neveu model (Karbstein 2006), to nonrelativistic Fermi gases at unitarity. It builds on prior mean-field studies of spin-imbalanced Fermi gases with real chemical potentials (Holland 2001, and review articles on asymmetric systems) and on lattice Monte Carlo calculations for unpolarized unitary fermions (Chen 2003, Bulgac 2005). The approach enables future ab initio quantum Monte Carlo calculations of polarized Fermi gases without a sign problem, potentially allowing access to the (tri)critical point and the zero-temperature Bertsch parameter at finite polarization, and may serve as a testbed for techniques applicable to the QCD phase diagram.
