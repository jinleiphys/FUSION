# Time scales in nuclear giant resonances

> We propose a general approach to characterise fluctuations of measured cross sections of nuclear giant resonances. Simulated cross sections are obtained from a particular, yet representative self-energy which contains all information about fragmentations. Using a wavelet analysis, we demonstrate the extraction of time scales of cascading decays into configurations of different complexity of the resonance. We argue that the spreading widths of collective excitations in nuclei are determined by the number of fragmentations as seen in the power spectrum. An analytic treatment of the wavelet analysis using a Fourier expansion of the cross section confirms this principle. A simple rule for the relative life times of states associated with hierarchies of different complexity is given.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the maxima in the power spectrum from a wavelet analysis of giant resonance cross sections directly reveal the number of fragmentations (intermediate states of increasing complexity) into which the resonance decays, and that the heights of these maxima encode the relative lifetimes of those states, provided the spreading widths follow a specific scaling.

## Method

The authors construct a simulated cross section from a self-energy with 300 randomly distributed poles (representing compound states) whose residues are modulated by four sets of Lorentzian peaks, each set representing a different number of fragmentations (6, 11, 17, and 29). They then perform a Morlet wavelet analysis on this simulated cross section and derive an analytic relation between the Fourier expansion of the cross section and the wavelet power spectrum to interpret the positions and heights of the resulting maxima.

## Key numbers

*   The simulated cross section uses 300 compound states with real parts uniformly distributed with mean distance 1/300 and imaginary parts randomly distributed in the interval [0.004, 0.007] (arbitrary energy units).
*   The four sets of fragmentations used in the simulation are \(f_1=6\), \(f_2=11\), \(f_3=17\), and \(f_4=29\).
*   For a Morlet wavelet with wave number \(k=6\) and a unit energy interval, the maxima of the power spectrum occur at \(\delta^{\rm max} \approx 0.16, 0.088, 0.057, 0.033\) for Fourier components \(m=12, 22, 34, 58\), respectively, following the relation \(\delta^{\rm max} = (k+\sqrt{2+k^2})/(2 m \pi) I\).
*   The number of fragmentations \(f_m\) is related to the Fourier component \(m\) by \(m = 2 f_m\).
*   The spreading widths are initially chosen as \(\gamma_i = 1/(2 f_i)\); a deviation to \(\gamma_4 = 1/(2.8 f_4)\) is shown to increase the height of the corresponding peak in the power spectrum.

## Context

This work extends the wavelet analysis of fine structure in the Isoscalar Giant Quadrupole Resonance (QR) observed in \((p,p')\) experiments by Shevchenko et al. (2004) and subsequent analyses by the same group. It provides a general theoretical framework, using a self-energy approach, to explain the energy scales extracted from those experiments, which had shown a schematic pattern in the widths of the fine structure. The paper enables the extraction of the number of fragmentations and relative lifetimes of intermediate states from experimental power spectra, and suggests a possible connection to self-organising structures in the nuclear many-body system, a concept discussed by Bak et al. (1987), Nilsen (1990), and Sornette (2000).
