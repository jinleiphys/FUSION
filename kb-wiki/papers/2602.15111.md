# Orbital eccentricity can make neutron star g-mode resonances observable with current gravitational-wave detectors

> Dynamical tides can provide us vital information about the properties of neutron star (NS) matter. This is particularly true for g-modes, whose frequency and tidal coupling are highly sensitive to the composition of NSs, especially in their centers, where microphysical models are the least reliable. However, due to their weak coupling to external tidal fields, their effect on the gravitational-wave (GW) signal of binary inspirals can be difficult to observe. Here we show that the detectability of these tides can be significantly enhanced by binary NSs with moderate eccentricities. This is primarily due to higher eccentric harmonics in the early phase of the binary evolution experiencing larger phase shifts, which they transport to the sensitive band of GW detectors. In addition, g-mode tides in eccentric binaries undergo several epicyclic resonances, which also amplify the total phase shift. We demonstrate that these effects increase the detectability of g-mode dynamical tides by more than an order of magnitude for eccentricities of $e_\mathrm{10Hz}\sim0.2-0.4$, making it possible to put robust constraints on g-mode properties using current GW detectors, while all relevant models could potentially be constrained with eccentric binary NSs with Einstein Telescope.

## Key claim
Orbital eccentricities of $e_\mathrm{10Hz}\sim0.2-0.4$ can enhance the detectability of neutron star g-mode dynamical tides by more than an order of magnitude, making robust constraints on g-mode properties possible with current gravitational-wave detectors, whereas circular binaries would likely require next-generation detectors like Einstein Telescope.

## Method
The authors model quadrupole tidal interactions in eccentric binary neutron stars using a Newtonian effective-one-body Lagrangian with g-mode tidal deformabilities. They derive analytic formulas for the energy exchange during epicyclic resonances, where the g-mode frequency becomes an integer multiple of the orbital frequency, and compute the total phase shift accumulated from consecutive resonances. The detectability is assessed using a Newtonian eccentric waveform model in the stationary phase approximation, summing over orbital harmonics up to a maximum order determined by the eccentricity.

## Key numbers
- The dimensionless tidal deformability for the first g-mode is taken as $\Lambda_g = 0.1$, a typical value from microphysical calculations.
- The total phase shift from g-mode resonances reaches a maximum approximately 2.5 times larger than the circular binary case, independent of g-mode frequency.
- The enhancement occurs for eccentricities $e_\mathrm{10Hz} \sim 0.2-0.4$, with the exact peak depending on the g-mode frequency.
- The number of orbits during a resonance is given by $\delta N_\mathrm{r} = \left[ \frac{5}{192\pi} \frac{(1+q)^{1/3}}{q} \frac{\ell_\mathrm{r}^{2/3}}{(m_1\omega_\alpha)^{5/3} \mathcal{F}(e_\mathrm{r})} \right]^{1/2}$, and resonances are only effective when $\delta N_\mathrm{r} \gtrsim 1$.
- The dissipation coefficient for g-modes drops by 6 orders of magnitude compared to f-modes due to scaling with $\omega_\alpha^6$, making dissipation negligible.

## Context
This work extends studies of dynamical tides in neutron star binaries, where previous work by Lai (1993) and others established that g-modes have weak tidal coupling and were expected to be observable only with next-generation detectors like Einstein Telescope for circular binaries. The authors build on studies of eccentricity-enhanced tides by Vick (2019) and Takátsy (2024) for f-modes, showing that the lower frequency of g-modes (approximately 100 Hz) allows coherent resonant excitation over multiple orbits even at moderate eccentricities. This opens the possibility of constraining nuclear matter composition and phase transitions using current LIGO-Virgo-Kagra detectors, particularly relevant given recent observations like GW200105 showing eccentricities of 0.1-0.15 at 20 Hz.
