# Triaxial relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum for exotic nuclei

> A triaxial relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (TRHBc) has been developed to incorporate triaxial deformation, pairing correlations, and continuum effects in a fully microscopic and self-consistent way, aiming for a reliable description of triaxial exotic nuclei with extreme neutron-to-proton ratios. The TRHBc formalism is presented in detail, and its numerical implementation is benchmarked against the results from the axially deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum and the TRHB theory in harmonic oscillator expansion. The TRHBc theory is applied to investigate the aluminum isotopes systematically, and the available data are well reproduced for the binding energies, one- and two-neutron separation energies, and charge radii. The nuclei near the one-neutron drip line, $^{40}$Al and $^{42}$Al, are found to be triaxially deformed with one-neutron separation energies below 1 MeV. Possible neutron halos in the triaxial nuclei $^{40}$Al and $^{42}$Al are explored by examining the single-particle levels around the Fermi surface, including their composition and contribution to the total neutron density. The existence of neutron halos in $^{40}$Al and $^{42}$Al is also supported by the halo scale, which is comparable to other halo nuclei well-established previously. More importantly, the mechanism for the halo formation in $^{40}$Al is revealed to be the triaxial deformation, which results in the decoupling of the halo orbitals from those of the core.

## Key claim
The paper develops a triaxial relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (TRHBc) that simultaneously incorporates triaxial deformation, pairing correlations, and continuum effects, and applies it to show that the halo formation in $^{40}$Al is driven specifically by triaxial deformation causing decoupling of halo orbitals from core orbitals, rather than by spherical or axially symmetric mechanisms.

## Method
The triaxial RHB equations are solved in a Dirac Woods-Saxon (DWS) basis, with nuclear densities and potentials expanded in spherical harmonics to accommodate triaxial deformation. The formalism uses a zero-range density-dependent pairing force and the PC-PK1 density functional, with blocking effects treated via the equal filling approximation for odd-mass nuclei. Numerical convergence is benchmarked against the axially deformed DRHBc theory and the triaxial RHB theory in harmonic oscillator expansion.

## Key numbers
- One-neutron separation energies for $^{40}$Al and $^{42}$Al are below 1 MeV, indicating they are near the one-neutron drip line.
- Box size $R_{\mathrm{box}} = 20$ fm and mesh size $\Delta r = 0.1$ fm yield binding energy uncertainty less than 0.01% for $^{86}$Ge.
- Energy cutoff $E_{\mathrm{cut}} = 300$ MeV gives total energy difference less than 0.01 MeV compared to $E_{\mathrm{cut}} = 350$ MeV for $^{86}$Ge.
- Angular momentum cutoff $J_{\mathrm{cut}} = 19/2$ $\hbar$ yields total energy difference less than 0.001 MeV compared to $J_{\mathrm{cut}} = 21/2$ and $23/2$ $\hbar$ for $^{86}$Ge.
- Spherical harmonic expansion truncation $\lambda_{\mathrm{max}} = 6$ gives total energy difference approximately 0.03 MeV (about 0.004% of total energy) compared to $\lambda_{\mathrm{max}} = 8$ and $10$ for $^{86}$Ge.
- Pairing strength $V_0 = -342.5$ MeV fm$^3$ and saturation density $\rho_{\mathrm{sat}} = 0.152$ fm$^{-3}$ reproduce experimental odd-even mass differences for aluminum isotopes.

## Context
This work extends the axially deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (DRHBc) developed by Zhou et al. (2010) and Li et al. (2012), which previously predicted halos in deformed nuclei like $^{42}$Mg and $^{44}$Mg but was restricted to axial symmetry. The TRHBc theory enables the study of triaxial deformation effects on halo formation, which had remained debated since the work of Tanihata (1995) and Nunes (2005) who suggested halos in deformed drip-line nuclei were unlikely. The finding that triaxial deformation drives halo formation in $^{40}$Al opens the possibility of exploring triaxial halos in other exotic nuclei near the drip line.
