# Liquid crystals in the mantles of neutron stars

> Recent calculations indicate that in the outer parts of neutron stars nuclei are rod-like or slab-like, rather than roughly spherical. We consider the elastic properties of these phases, and argue that they behave as liquid crystals, rather than rigid solids. We estimate elastic constants and discuss implications of our results for neutron star behavior.

# Liquid Crystals in the Mantles of Neutron Stars

## Key claim

The rod-like and slab-like nuclear phases in the outer parts of neutron stars behave as liquid crystals (columnar and smectic A phases, respectively) rather than as rigid crystalline solids, which reduces the effective elastic rigidity of the star's outer layers by roughly a factor of two.

## Method

The authors adopt a generalized liquid drop model with bulk, surface, and Coulomb energies, assuming uniform rods and slabs with constant total density. They estimate elastic constants by analyzing small deformations of the equilibrium configurations, using Fourier series expansions of the charge density and comparing the deformation energy to standard liquid-crystal elasticity expressions for smectic A and columnar phases.

## Key numbers

- The layered ("lasagna") phase constitutes roughly 1/2 of the mass between the core and the ordinary solid crust according to Lorenz et al., and roughly 1/3 according to Oyamatsu; the rod-like ("spaghetti") phase constitutes roughly 1/5 according to Lorenz et al. and roughly 1/2 according to Oyamatsu.
- The compression modulus for the layered phase is B = 6E_{C0}, where E_{C0} is the equilibrium Coulomb energy density.
- The splay constant for the layered phase is K_1 = 2 E_{C0} (1 + 2w - 2w^2) r_c^2 / 15, where w is the fraction of space occupied by nuclear matter and r_c is the half-thickness of a slab.
- For the columnar phase, the compression modulus is B = (3/2) E_{C0}, and the bend constant K_3 = (0.064 - 0.067) E_{C0} r_c^2 in the physically relevant range of w.
- The shear constant C for the columnar phase follows log_{10}(C/E_{C0}) ≈ 2.1(w - 0.3) in the relevant range of w.
- The columnar phase is favored for w between 0.15-0.20 and 0.30-0.35; the layered phase has an upper limit of w about 0.60-0.65.
- The ordering temperature for rods and plates is comparable to the melting temperature for matter with spherical nuclei, ∼ 10^10 K, while neutron star interior temperatures are ∼ 10^8 K.

## Context

This work extends the earlier discovery by Ravenhall et al. (1983) that nuclei at sub-nuclear densities can adopt non-spherical shapes, and the subsequent calculations by Lorenz et al. (1993) and Oyamatsu (1993) mapping the sequence of phase transitions in neutron star matter. The paper applies the liquid-crystal elasticity formalism of de Gennes (1974) to estimate elastic constants for the "spaghetti" and "lasagna" phases. The results imply that models of glitch phenomena (Alpar, 1977), magnetic field evolution (Ruderman, 1991), and starquake mechanisms must be reconsidered because the maximum elastic energy that can be stored in the crust is reduced, and the "plate tectonics" of liquid crystals differs fundamentally from that of crystalline solids.
