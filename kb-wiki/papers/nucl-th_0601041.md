# Collective excitations and energy-weighted sum rules in relativistic random phase approximation with vacuum polarization

> The isoscalar and isovector collective multipole excitations in stable nuclei are studied in the framework of relativistic random-phase approximation with the vacuum polarization arising from the nucleon-antinucleon field. A fully self-consistent calculation which guarantees the decoupling of the spurious state and the conservation of the multipole-transition current is carried out by using the derivative-expansion method for the description of the vacuum contribution. A remarkable effect of the inclusion of the vacuum polarization is the increase of the effective mass, $m^*/m_N \sim 0.8$; for all multipole modes, the energy-weighted sum rule values with the vacuum polarization are smaller than those of the relativistic model without the vacuum polarization, which typically has the effective mass $m^*/m_N \sim 0.6$. Also, the present model can give an excellent agreement with experimental data on the excitation energy, in particular, for the isoscalar quadrupole resonances in which it was previously reported that the calculated energies in the relativistic model are about 1-2 MeV above the experimental values. It is shown, further, that the change of the shell structure due to the inclusion of the vacuum polarization plays an important role in the improvement of the discrepancies seen in the dipole compression modes. On the other hand, the isoscalar monopole resonance has a similar peak whether or not the vacuum polarization is taken into account, if the compression modulus is kept the same in the analysis.

## Key claim

The inclusion of vacuum polarization from the nucleon-antinucleon field increases the effective mass to \(m^*/m_N \sim 0.8\), which reduces energy-weighted sum rule values compared to the standard relativistic model with \(m^*/m_N \sim 0.6\); however, the isoscalar monopole resonance peak remains similar with or without vacuum polarization if the compression modulus is kept fixed.

## Method

The authors performed fully self-consistent relativistic random-phase approximation (RRPA) calculations using a derivative-expansion method to incorporate vacuum polarization from the Dirac sea. They employed a relativistic Hartree approach (RHA) basis including both positive- and negative-energy nucleon states, with an extended Lagrangian that includes tensor coupling for the omega meson to restore spin-orbit splittings. The RRPA equation was solved directly by the inverse matrix method in momentum space, and the self-consistency was verified by checking the decoupling of the spurious center-of-mass state and the conservation of the multipole-transition current.

## Key numbers

- Effective mass with vacuum polarization: \(m^*/m_N \sim 0.8\); without vacuum polarization: \(m^*/m_N \sim 0.6\)
- For the isoscalar quadrupole resonance, the relativistic model without vacuum polarization gave energies about 1-2 MeV above experimental values, while the present model with vacuum polarization achieves excellent agreement
- The energy-weighted sum rule (EWSR) for the monopole states in \(^{208}\)Pb with the RHAT1 parameter set is 134% of the classical value, compared to 147% with the NL3 parameter set
- The compression moduli for the new parameter sets are \(K = 270\), 281, and 325 MeV for RHAT1, RHAT2, and RHAT3 respectively
- The lowest antinucleon dipole state is located at 1507.2 MeV for the RHAT1 parameter set

## Context

This work extends the relativistic RPA framework by including vacuum polarization effects that were previously neglected in standard RRPA calculations using the no-sea approximation, such as those by Ring and collaborators (2001) and Ma and collaborators (1997, 2001). The study addresses the long-standing problem that relativistic models with small effective mass (\(m^*/m_N \sim 0.6\)) overestimate isoscalar quadrupole resonance energies by 1-2 MeV. The inclusion of vacuum polarization, which increases the effective mass to \(\sim 0.8\), resolves this discrepancy and also addresses the need for larger effective mass suggested by beta-decay lifetime analyses of Nishimura and collaborators (2005). The work enables more reliable predictions of energy-weighted sum rules, which are important for calculating nuclear polarization corrections to muonic atoms and dispersion corrections to elastic electron scattering.
