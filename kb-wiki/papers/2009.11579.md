# Dynamical energy loss formalism: from describing suppression patterns to implications for future experiments

> Understanding properties of Quark-Gluon Plasma requires an unbiased comparison of experimental data with theoretical predictions. To that end, we developed the dynamical energy loss formalism which, in distinction to most other methods, takes into account a realistic medium composed of dynamical scattering centers. The formalism also allows making numerical predictions for a wide number of observables with the same parameter set fixed to standard literature values. In this proceedings, we overview our recently developed DREENA-C and DREENA-B frameworks, where DREENA is a computational implementation of the dynamical energy loss formalism, and where C stands for constant temperature QCD medium, while B stands for the medium modeled by 1+1D Bjorken expansion. At constant temperature our predictions overestimate $v_2$, in contrast to other models, but consistent with simple analytical estimates. With Bjorken expansion, we have a good agreement of the predictions with both $R_{AA}$ and $v_2$ measurements. We find that introducing medium evolution has a larger effect on $v_2$ predictions, but for precision predictions it has to be taken into account in $R_{AA}$ predictions as well. Based on numerical calculations and simple analytical derivations, we also propose a new observable, which we call path length sensitive suppression ratio, for which we argue that the path length dependence can be assessed in a straightforward manner. We also argue that $Pb+Pb$ vs. $Xe+Xe$ measurements make a good system to assess the path length dependence. As an outlook, we expect that introduction of more complex medium evolution (beyond Bjorken expansion) in the dynamical energy loss formalism can provide a basis for a state of the art QGP tomography tool - e.g. to jointly constrain the medium properties from the point of both high pt and low pt data.

## Key claim
The dynamical energy loss formalism, implemented in the DREENA-C (constant temperature) and DREENA-B (1+1D Bjorken expansion) frameworks, can jointly describe both $R_{AA}$ and $v_2$ data for light and heavy flavor probes in heavy-ion collisions, but only when medium evolution is included; constant-temperature predictions overestimate $v_2$, contrary to the typical "v2 puzzle" where other models underpredict it.

## Method
The authors developed the DREENA-C and DREENA-B computational frameworks, which implement the dynamical energy loss formalism based on finite-temperature field theory and generalized hard-thermal-loop (HTL) approach, including both radiative and collisional energy losses for light and heavy flavor. DREENA-C assumes a constant-temperature finite-size QCD medium, while DREENA-B incorporates 1+1D Bjorken expansion as a simple analytic medium evolution. The frameworks use a single parameter set fixed to standard literature values, with no fitting parameters, and generate predictions for $R_{AA}$ and $v_2$ across different collision systems, centralities, and energies.

## Key numbers
- For charged hadrons in 5.02 TeV Pb+Pb collisions, DREENA-B predictions show good joint agreement with ALICE and CMS $R_{AA}$ and $v_2$ data across centrality regions from 0-5% to 40-50%.
- For 5.44 TeV Xe+Xe collisions, predictions were generated before data became available, with DREENA-B showing systematically higher $R_{AA}$ and significantly lower $v_2$ compared to DREENA-C.
- The path-length sensitive suppression ratio $R_L^{XePb} \equiv \frac{1-R_{XeXe}}{1-R_{PbPb}}$ is approximately $\left(\frac{A_{Xe}}{A_{Pb}}\right)^{b/3}$, where $A_{Xe}$ and $A_{Pb}$ are the mass numbers of xenon and lead, and $b$ is the path-length exponent.

## Context
This work extends the dynamical energy loss formalism previously developed by Djordjevic and collaborators, which had explained a wide range of $R_{AA}$ data but did not include medium evolution. The DREENA-B framework addresses the "v2 puzzle" where other models (e.g., those using static scattering centers) underpredicted $v_2$ unless new phenomena were introduced. The proposed path-length sensitive suppression ratio using Pb+Pb vs. Xe+Xe collisions enables straightforward extraction of path-length dependence, which can differentiate between models with linear, quadratic, or intermediate (dynamical energy loss) path-length dependence. Future work aims to introduce more complex medium evolution beyond Bjorken expansion to create a state-of-the-art QGP tomography tool that jointly constrains medium properties from both high and low transverse momentum data.
