# Three-body nuclei and their astrophysical implications: the case of $^6$He

> The analytic THO method is generalized to study three-body nuclei of astrophysical interest, and applied to $^6$He. Results are consistent with previous publications and experimental data.

## Key claim
The analytic Transformed Harmonic Oscillator (THO) method, previously developed for two-body systems, is generalized to three-body Borromean nuclei and applied to \(^6\)He; the results are consistent with prior calculations and experimental data, but the paper presents this as a validation of the method rather than a new physical discovery.

## Method
The three-body Hamiltonian for \(^6\)He is diagonalized in a hyperspherical harmonics basis using an analytic local scaling transformation to generate the hyperradial THO functions. The basis is truncated at maximum hypermomentum \(K_{max} = 20\) with an increasing number of hyperradial excitations \(i_{max}\), and the transformation parameters are fixed at \(b = 0.7\) fm, \(\gamma = 1.4\), and \(m = 4\). The ground state energy, root-mean-square matter radius, and dipolar transition probabilities \(B(E1)\) are computed from the resulting eigenstates.

## Key numbers
- Ground state energy of \(^6\)He: \(-0.9749\) MeV.
- Root-mean-square point nucleon matter radius of \(^6\)He: \(2.554\) fm.
- Basis truncation: maximum hypermomentum \(K_{max} = 20\).
- Transformation parameters: \(b = 0.7\) fm, \(\gamma = 1.4\), \(m = 4\).

## Context
This work extends the analytic THO method, previously applied to two-body systems by Moro et al. (2009) and Lay et al. (2010), to three-body Borromean nuclei such as \(^6\)He, \(^9\)Be, and \(^{17}\)Ne. The results are compared with prior calculations by Rodríguez-Gallardo et al. (2005) and Danilin et al. (2005), and the computed \(B(E1)\) probabilities are linked to astrophysical reaction and production rates relevant to nucleosynthesis in supernovae and binary stars. The method enables future studies of three-body continuum states and their role in stellar nucleosynthesis processes.
