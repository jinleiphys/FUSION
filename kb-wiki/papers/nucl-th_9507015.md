# Macroscopic-Microscopic Mass Models

> We discuss recent developments in macroscopic-microscopic mass models, including the 1992 finite-range droplet model, the 1992 extended-Thomas-Fermi Strutinsky-integral model, and the 1994 Thomas-Fermi model, with particular emphasis on how well they extrapolate to new regions of nuclei. We also address what recent developments in macroscopic-microscopic mass models are teaching us about such physically relevant issues as the nuclear curvature energy, a new congruence energy arising from a greater-than-average overlap of neutron and proton wave functions, the nuclear incompressibility coefficient, and the Coulomb redistribution energy arising from a central density depression. We conclude with a brief discussion of the recently discovered rock of metastable superheavy nuclei near 272:110 that had been correctly predicted by macroscopic-microscopic models, along with a possible new tack for reaching an island near 290:110 beyond our present horizon.

# Macroscopic-Microscopic Mass Models

## Key claim

The 1992 finite-range droplet model can be extrapolated to new regions of nuclei with considerable confidence (theoretical error for 217 newly measured masses decreased by 4% relative to the original adjustment), while the 1992 extended-Thomas-Fermi Strutinsky-integral model and the 1994 Thomas-Fermi model require caution when extrapolating (theoretical errors increased by 12% and 15%, respectively). The paper also argues that macroscopic-microscopic models correctly predicted the existence and location of a rock of metastable superheavy nuclei near 272:110 that has since been discovered experimentally.

## Method

The authors compare three macroscopic-microscopic mass models: the 1992 finite-range droplet model (FRDM), the 1992 extended-Thomas-Fermi Strutinsky-integral model (ETFSI-1), and the 1994 Thomas-Fermi model (TF). Each model combines a macroscopic energy calculated from a nuclear interaction with microscopic shell and pairing corrections obtained from single-particle potentials using Strutinsky's method or related approaches. The models are tested by comparing their predictions against 217 newly measured nuclear masses that were not included in the original parameter adjustments, allowing a quantitative assessment of extrapolation reliability.

## Key numbers

- FRDM (1992): theoretical error of 0.669 MeV for the original 1654 nuclei; theoretical error of 0.642 MeV for 217 newly measured masses (a 4% decrease)
- ETFSI-1 (1992): theoretical error of 0.726 MeV for the original 1538 nuclei; theoretical error of 0.810 MeV for 210 newly measured masses (a 12% increase)
- TF (1994): theoretical error of 0.640 MeV for the original 1654 nuclei; theoretical error of 0.737 MeV for 217 newly measured masses (a 15% increase)
- von Groote (1976) mass formula: theoretical error of 0.629 MeV for the original 1323 nuclei; theoretical error of 1.284 MeV for 217 newly measured masses (a 104% increase)
- FRDM theoretical error as a function of mass number is well represented by the function 8.62 MeV / A^{0.57}
- Incompressibility coefficient K: 240 MeV adopted in FRDM; 243 MeV from optimal adjustment to masses and fission barriers; 234.7 MeV in ETFSI-1; 234 MeV in TF (all subject to large uncertainties)
- Curvature-energy constant a_3 = 12.1 MeV in the 1994 Thomas-Fermi model
- Relativistic mean-field treatment of 32 spherical nuclei gave an average absolute deviation of 2.783 MeV

## Context

This review extends the authors' prior work on the finite-range droplet model (Moller, Nix, Myers, Swiatecki 1995) and compares it with the extended-Thomas-Fermi Strutinsky-integral model of Aboussir, Pearson, Dutta, and Tondeur (1992) and the Thomas-Fermi model of Myers and Swiatecki (1994). The paper demonstrates that the FRDM extrapolates reliably to new regions, unlike the von Groote, Hilf, and Takahashi (1976) mass formula which shows a 104% error increase and is deemed inappropriate for modern astrophysical calculations. The work enables confident predictions for superheavy nuclei, including the recently discovered rock near 272:110 and a predicted island near 290:110, and sets a benchmark for fully microscopic theories to match or exceed in accuracy.
