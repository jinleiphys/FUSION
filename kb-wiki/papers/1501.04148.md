# Nuclear energy density functionals: what we can learn about/from their global performance?

> A short review of recent results on the global performance of covariant energy density functionals is presented. It is focused on the analysis of the accuracy of the description of physical observables of ground and excited states as well as to related theoretical uncertainties. In addition, a global analysis of pairing properties is presented and the impact of pairing on the position of two-neutron drip line is discussed.

## Key claim
This paper reviews the global performance of covariant energy density functionals (CEDFs) in describing nuclear ground and excited states, but the abstract's claim of a "short review" glosses over the fact that the analysis is specifically focused on quantifying the accuracy of these functionals and their associated theoretical uncertainties, rather than presenting a comprehensive survey of all CEDF applications.

## Method
The authors performed a systematic global analysis of covariant energy density functionals by comparing calculated physical observables of ground and excited states against experimental data across the nuclear chart. They also conducted a global analysis of pairing properties and examined how pairing correlations affect the predicted position of the two-neutron drip line.

## Key numbers
- The paper presents a global analysis of pairing properties and their impact on the two-neutron drip line position, but no specific numerical results (such as binding energies, drip line positions, or uncertainty quantifications) are provided in the available text.
- Review-type paper: no original numerics.

## Context
This work extends prior systematic studies of nuclear energy density functionals by focusing on covariant (relativistic) functionals, complementing non-relativistic approaches such as those by Bender et al. (2003) and Erler et al. (2012). The analysis of theoretical uncertainties and pairing properties enables more reliable predictions for nuclei far from stability, particularly for determining the limits of nuclear existence at the neutron drip line.
