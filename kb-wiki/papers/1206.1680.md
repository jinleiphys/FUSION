# From chi-squared to Bayesian model comparison and Levy expansions of Bose-Einstein correlations in e+e- reactions

> The usual chi-squared method of fit quality assessment is a special case of the more general method of Bayesian model comparison which involves integrals of the likelihood and prior over all possible values of all parameters. We introduce new parametrisations based on systematic expansions around the stretched exponential or Fourier-transformed Lévy source distribution, and utilise the increased discriminating power of the Bayesian approach to evaluate the relative probability of these models to be true representations of a recently measured Bose-Einstein correlation data in e+e- annihilations at LEP.

## Key claim

Bayesian model comparison, via the Bayes factor, can quantitatively distinguish between competing parametrisations of Bose-Einstein correlation data even when traditional chi-squared methods cannot, because the Bayesian approach accounts for parameter uncertainties through the determinant of the covariance matrix, not just the best-fit likelihood.

## Method

The authors applied the Laplace approximation to compute Bayes factors for seven parametrisations of the two-particle correlation function C₂(Q) fitted to 100 binned data points from L3 two-jet events (0 < Q < 4 GeV). They introduced new "Lévy polynomial" expansions (H₄, H₅) and derivative expansions (H₆, H₇) around the stretched-exponential (Lévy) source distribution, and compared these to the Gaussian (H₁), stretched exponential (H₂), and simplified τ-model (H₃) used by L3. The Bayes factor was decomposed into contributions from the log-likelihood (L), log-prior (P), and log-determinant (F) terms.

## Key numbers

- For the L3 two-jet data, the 3rd-order derivative model (H₇) had the lowest evidence value hₘ = 97.0, compared to hₘ = 100.6 for the 1st-order derivative model (H₆), yielding odds of 2^(100.6-97.0) ≈ 12:1 in favour of H₇.
- The Bayes factor gave odds of 5800:1 for the 1st-order Lévy polynomial (H₄, hₘ=98.8) versus the 3rd-order Lévy polynomial (H₅, hₘ=111.3), even though their reduced chi-squared values were nearly identical (C=0.97 for both).
- The Gaussian model (H₁) had hₘ=206.5 and reduced chi-squared C=2.57 with confidence level 3.4×10⁻¹³%; the stretched exponential (H₂) had hₘ=172.0, C=2.02, CL=1.5×10⁻⁶%.
- The simplified τ-model (H₃) had hₘ=101.8, C=1.00, CL=49.1%; the 1st-order derivative (H₆) had hₘ=100.6, C=0.98, CL=53.0%.
- The determinant term F ranged from 29.1 (H₆) to 41.6 (H₅), with the 3rd-order Lévy polynomial having the largest F value.

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian model comparison approach of Jeffreys (1961) and the Laplace approximation of Kass (1995) to femtoscopic correlation functions, directly comparing with the chi-squared methodology used by the L3 Collaboration (2011) for their two-jet Bose-Einstein correlation data at LEP. The new Lévy polynomial expansions generalise the orthogonal polynomial techniques of Csörgő (2000) to arbitrary Lévy index α, enabling systematic quantification of deviations from stretched-exponential shapes. The results enable future femtoscopic analyses to move beyond chi-squared model rejection toward quantitative model selection, though the authors caution that the Gaussian error approximation for binned count data and the dependence on prior choices remain limitations.
