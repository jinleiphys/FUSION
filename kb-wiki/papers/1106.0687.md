# On the determination of anti-neutrino spectra from nuclear reactors

> In this paper we study the effect of, well-known, higher order corrections to the allowed beta decay spectrum on the determination of anti-neutrino spectra resulting from the decays of fission fragments. In particular, we try to estimate the associated theory errors and find that induced currents like weak magnetism may ultimately limit our ability to improve the current accuracy and under certain circumstance could even largely increase the theoretical errors. We also perform a critical evaluation of the errors associated with our method to extract the anti-neutrino spectrum using synthetic beta spectra. It turns out, that a fit using only virtual beta branches with a judicious choice of the effective nuclear charge provides results with a minimal bias. We apply this method to actual data for U235, Pu239 and Pu241 and confirm, within errors, recent results, which indicate a net 3% upward shift in energy averaged anti-neutrino fluxes. However, we also find significant shape differences which can in principle be tested by high statistics anti-neutrino data samples.

## Key claim

The paper confirms a net 3% upward shift in energy-averaged antineutrino fluxes from nuclear reactors, but finds that the size of weak magnetism corrections, particularly in decays with large log ft values, may ultimately limit the ability to improve current accuracy and could under certain circumstances largely increase theoretical errors, potentially explaining the reactor antineutrino anomaly without invoking sterile neutrinos.

## Method

The author performs an independent inversion of measured total beta spectra from the Institut Laue-Langevin (ILL) for 235U, 239Pu, and 241Pu into antineutrino spectra using virtual beta branches. The method applies higher-order corrections to the allowed beta decay spectrum, including finite size effects, screening, radiative corrections, and weak magnetism, to each virtual branch individually, and uses synthetic data sets with Monte Carlo simulations to quantify bias and statistical errors associated with the inversion procedure.

## Key numbers

- Weak magnetism slope parameter dN/dE = (0.67 ± 0.26) % MeV^{-1} from 10 nuclei (excluding 3 large-log ft isotopes), compared to the universal value of 0.5% MeV^{-1} from the impulse approximation
- If large-log ft isotopes are included, dN/dE = (4.78 ± 10.5) % MeV^{-1}, giving a rate uncertainty of about 20%
- A shift of dN/dE by +0.5% MeV^{-1} causes a shift of the neutrino rate of about -1%
- The inversion procedure uses beta data with 50 keV binning and 30 virtual branches (s=5) for 235U
- The synthetic beta spectrum for 235U contains approximately 550 isotopes and about 8,000 individual beta branches
- The effective nuclear charge Z is computed using the Elton formula for nuclear radius: R = 0.0029 A^{1/3} + 0.0063 A^{-1/3} - 0.017 A^{-1} in units of m_e c^2
- A variation of 10% in A changes the total flux by 0.05%

## Context

This work extends the reevaluation of reactor neutrino spectra by Mueller (2011), which found a significant upward shift in predicted fluxes of about 3%, and addresses the reactor antineutrino anomaly reported by Mention (2011) and Kopp (2011) regarding possible sterile neutrinos with Δm² ≳ 1 eV². The paper provides an independent inversion of the ILL beta spectra from Schreckenbach (1985), Von Feilitzsch (1982), and Hahn (1989), and demonstrates that weak magnetism uncertainties, particularly from large-log ft decays, could account for the observed flux shift without new physics, while also enabling future high-statistics antineutrino experiments to test the predicted shape differences.
