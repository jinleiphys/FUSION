# How to Test the Two-Families Scenario

> We shortly summarize the two-families scenario in which both hadronic stars and strange quark stars can exist and we describe the main predictions one can obtain from it. We then concentrate on the observables that most likely will be measured in the near future, i.e. masses, radii, tidal deformabilities and moments of inertia and we present a list of objects that are candidate strange quark stars in this scheme. We show that the estimates of the radii derived up to now from observations are all compatible with the two-families scenario and in particular all the objects having large radii can easily be interpreted as strange quark stars.

# How to Test the Two-Families Scenario

## Key claim
The two-families scenario, in which both hadronic stars and strange quark stars coexist, is compatible with all current observational estimates of radii, provided that objects with large radii are interpreted as strange quark stars and objects with very small radii (around 11 km or less) are interpreted as hadronic stars. The scenario hinges on the condition that hadronic matter softens at high densities due to resonance production, limiting hadronic star maximum masses to approximately 1.5–1.6 solar masses, while strange quark stars can reach masses above 2 solar masses.

## Method
The authors construct equations of state for hadronic matter that include the production of Δ-resonances at densities of 2–3 times nuclear saturation density, leading to softening, and for quark matter using a constant speed-of-sound model with squared speed of sound fixed to 1/3 and a free parameter e₀ (energy density at vanishing pressure). They compute mass-radius and mass-tidal deformability relations for both families and compare them against observational constraints from pulsar masses, X-ray radius measurements, and the gravitational-wave event GW170817.

## Key numbers
- Maximum mass of hadronic stars in the two-families scenario: approximately (1.5–1.6) M⊙.
- Threshold mass for direct collapse to black hole in mergers of two hadronic stars: approximately 2.5 M⊙ (compared to >3 M⊙ in the one-family scenario).
- Tidal deformability for a 1.4 M⊙ star from GW170817: Λ₁.₄ = 190⁺³⁹⁰₋₁₂₀.
- Radius of RX J1856.5-3754: 12.1⁺¹.³₋₁.₆ km.
- Radius of J0437-4715: 13.1⁺⁰.⁹₋₀.₇ km.
- Radius of 4U 1702-429: 12.4⁺⁰.⁴₋₀.₄ km.
- Candidate strange quark star masses listed in Table 1 include: J0348+0432 at 2.01(4) M⊙, J0740+6620 at 2.17⁺⁰.¹¹₋₀.₁₀ M⊙, J1614-2230 at 1.908(16) M⊙.
- For a 1.4 M⊙ star: radius >13 km or I₄₅ ≥ 1.88 indicates a purely nucleonic star; 11.5 km ≤ R₁.₄ ≤ 13 km or 1.55 ≤ I₄₅ ≤ 1.88 indicates a hybrid or hyperonic star; R₁.₄ ≪ 11.5 km or I₄₅ ≪ 1.55 requires the two-family or twin-star interpretation.
- The parameter e₀ for quark matter is fixed to yield a maximum quark star mass ≳ 2.3 M⊙, based on analyses of short gamma-ray bursts with extended emission.

## Context
This paper reviews and extends the two-families scenario originally introduced by Drago et al. (2014) to resolve the tension between the existence of massive neutron stars above 2 M⊙ (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013, Cromartie 2019) and the possible existence of objects with radii of 11 km or less (Ozel 2016, Shaw 2018). The scenario contrasts with the twin-star scenario (Paschalidis 2017, Montana 2018), which also invokes quark matter but predicts that the most massive stars are hybrid stars with small radii rather than large-radius strange quark stars. Future measurements from NICER and the Square Kilometer Array, particularly of radii and moments of inertia, are expected to distinguish between these scenarios.
