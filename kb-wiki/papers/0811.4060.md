# Features of exceptional points and the continuum spectroscopy

> We discuss observable features of exceptional points and the resonance spectroscopy of $^{16}$Ne using the Shell Model Embedded in the Continuum.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that an exceptional point (EP), a square-root singularity where two eigenvalues and eigenvectors coalesce, can exist for physically relevant parameters of a realistic nuclear many-body Hamiltonian in the continuum shell model, not just in schematic models. The EP effect is robust, remaining visible even when the continuum-coupling strength is varied by 5-10% away from its critical value, and the entanglement of wave functions persists far from the exact EP energy.

## Method

The authors used the Shell Model Embedded in the Continuum (SMEC), a real-energy continuum shell model, to study the one-proton continuum of $^{16}$Ne. They constructed the energy-dependent, non-hermitian effective Hamiltonian ${\cal H}_{QQ}(E)$ in the discrete subspace, which includes coupling to scattering states and decay channels, and searched for exceptional points in the two-dimensional parameter space of total energy $E$ and continuum-coupling strength $V_0$. The method of anamneses was employed to extract quasi-bound states embedded in the continuum (QBSECs) from the scattering continuum, providing a complete single-particle basis.

## Key numbers

* The exceptional point in $^{16}$Ne is found at a total energy $E = 4.765$ MeV for the $2_3^+$ and $2_4^+$ eigenstates, with a continuum-coupling strength $V_0 = -1357.56$ MeV$\cdot$fm$^3$.
* The residual coupling strength $V_0 = -1100$ MeV$\cdot$fm$^3$ (used for realistic calculations away from the EP) was adjusted to reproduce the experimental width of the $5/2_1^+$ state in $^{15}$F.
* The EP effect remains when $V_0$ is changed by 5-10% away from its critical value of $-1357.56$ MeV$\cdot$fm$^3$.

## Context

This work extends studies of exceptional points from schematic many-body models, such as the Lipkin model (Heiss 1991), the interacting boson model (Cejnar 2006), and the 3-level pairing model (Dukelsky 2009), to a realistic nuclear continuum shell model for the first time. The results enable future experimental searches for indirect consequences of the EP effect in nuclear physics, such as the singular behavior of observables like the quadrupole moment, and motivate further work to identify the most useful reaction and structure observables for studying nuclear EP manifestations.
