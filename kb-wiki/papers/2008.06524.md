# Discovery vs. Precision in Nuclear Physics- A Tale of Three Scales

> At least three length scales are important in gaining a complete understanding of the physics of nuclei. These are the radius of the nucleus, the average inter-nucleon separation distance, and the size of the nucleon. The connections between the different scales are examined by using examples that demonstrate the direct connection between short-distance and high momentum transfer physics and also that significant high momentum content of wave functions is inevitable. The nuclear size is connected via the independent-pair approximation to the nucleon-nucleon separation distance, and this distance is connected via the concept of virtuality to the EMC effect. An explanation of the latter is presented in terms of light-front holographic wave functions of QCD. The net result is that the three scales are closely related, so that a narrow focus on any given specific range of scales may prevent an understanding of the fundamental origins of nuclear properties. It is also determined that, under certain suitable conditions, experiments are able to measure the momentum dependence of wave functions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the three relevant length scales in nuclear physics, the nuclear radius, the average inter-nucleon separation distance, and the nucleon size, are closely connected, so that a narrow focus on any single scale using effective field theory techniques may prevent understanding the fundamental origins of nuclear properties; the claim is hedged by the condition that scale separation is not very large in nuclear physics because the values of relevant distances are not widely separated.

## Method

The author examines the connections between the three length scales using simple analytical models of the nucleon-nucleon interaction (hard core plus exponential, square well, exponential, and Yukawa potentials) with parameters chosen to reproduce the measured scattering length and effective range, and uses the similarity renormalization group (SRG) framework to show how softening the nucleon-nucleon interaction hardens the probe operators. The analysis connects the nuclear radius to the inter-nucleon separation distance via the independent-pair approximation, and connects the inter-nucleon separation distance to the EMC effect via the concept of virtuality, using light-front holographic wave functions of QCD to explain the latter.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear radius for a heavy nucleus: approximately 5 fm
- Average inter-nucleon separation distance at nuclear centers: approximately 1.7 fm
- Nucleon radius: approximately 0.84 fm
- Pion Compton wavelength: 1.4 fm
- Nuclear diffuseness parameter a: 0.54 fm for nuclei with A > 20, with a derived value of 0.57 fm from average binding energy B = 16 MeV
- For the Gold nucleus, mean-square radius = 28.4 fm², with the diffuseness term contributing about 4 fm² (14% of mean-square radius, 7% of rms radius)
- For the hard core plus exponential potential model of the deuteron: hard core radius r₀ = 0.4 fm, exponential range a = 0.45 fm, binding energy 2.2 MeV
- For the hard core plus exponential potential, cutoff momentum Λ = 2.1 fm⁻¹ is insufficient for 5% accuracy in form factor when momentum transfer Q > 2.2 fm⁻¹
- For the square well potential, operator modification is important for Q > 1.5 fm⁻¹
- For the exponential potential, the form factor scales as 1/Q⁶
- For the Yukawa potential, the form factor scales as Q⁻⁴

## Context

This paper extends the line of work on effective field theory approaches in nuclear physics, specifically the V_low_k and similarity renormalization group methods developed by Bogner (2001-2009), Glazek (1993), and Szpigel (2000), which soften nucleon-nucleon interactions to improve convergence in many-body calculations. The author argues against the claim by Benhar (1993) that high-momentum components in the deuteron wave function are confirmed by data, and against the argument by Bogner (2007) and Furnstahl (2001) that wave functions are not observables, by showing that cross sections can be directly related to momentum-space wave functions under suitable kinematic conditions. The work enables a unified understanding of how the EMC effect, short-range correlations between nucleons, and mean-field effects are all connected across the three length scales, and suggests that experiments can measure the momentum dependence of wave functions under certain conditions.
