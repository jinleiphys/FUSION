# Can we decipher the composition of the core of a neutron star?

> General relativity guarantees a unique one-to-one correspondence between static observables of neutron stars (NSs) accessible by multi-messenger astronomy, such as mass-radius or tidal deformability, and the equation of state (EoS) of beta equilibrated matter. It is well known that these static properties are not enough to discern conventional NSs from hybrid stars. However, if one assumes that hadrons present in the neutron star core are only neutrons and protons, the lepton fraction is in principle determined unequivocally by the condition of chemical equilibrium. Using a simple analytical method based on a polynomial expansion of the EoS, we show that multiple solutions are possible to the beta-equilibrium equation, leading to a characteristic indetermination on the composition of the interiors of NSs, even in the purely nucleonic hypothesis. We further show that additional empirical information on symmetric matter at high densities are not very efficient to pin down the composition, if uncertainties on measurements are accounted for. We conclude that constraints on the symmetry energy at high densities only, can make meaningful impact to decipher the composition of neutron star core. Our results give a lower limit to the uncertainty on the NS core composition that can be obtained with astrophysical and terrestrial experiments.

## Key claim

Even under the purely nucleonic hypothesis (no exotic matter such as hyperons or quarks), the composition of a neutron star core cannot be uniquely determined from static astrophysical observables like mass-radius or tidal deformability, because multiple solutions to the beta-equilibrium equation exist that produce the same equation of state; additional constraints on the symmetry energy at high densities are required to resolve this degeneracy.

## Method

The authors used a meta-model polynomial expansion of the nuclear energy functional to analytically invert the beta-equilibrium equation, demonstrating multiple solutions for the proton fraction that reproduce the same beta-equilibrated equation of state. They then performed a Bayesian analysis using the relativistic mean field model FSU2 as a reference, sampling higher-order nuclear matter parameters (K_sym, Q_sat, Q_sym, Z_sat, Z_sym) with flat priors, and conditioning on pseudo-observations including the beta-equilibrium pressure at four density points, hypothetical neutron star radius measurements at three masses, and hypothetical laboratory constraints on symmetric nuclear matter and symmetry energy at suprasaturation density.

## Key numbers

- The hypothetical radius measurements used as pseudo-observations were: R_1.4Msun = 14.18 +/- 0.71 km, R_1.7Msun = 13.79 +/- 0.69 km, and R_2.01Msun = 12.93 +/- 0.65 km, all with 5% uncertainty.
- The hypothetical symmetric nuclear matter pressure constraint at 4n_sat (0.6 fm^-3) was P_SNM = 125.14 +/- 12.51 MeV (10% precision).
- The hypothetical symmetry energy constraint at 4n_sat was e_sym(0.6 fm^-3) = 131.65 +/- 13.16 MeV (10% precision).
- The prior ranges for the varied higher-order parameters were: K_sym = [-400, 200] MeV, Q_sat = [-1000, 1000] MeV, Q_sym = [-2000, 2000] MeV, Z_sat = [-5000, 5000] MeV, Z_sym = [-5000, 5000] MeV.
- The 5% error bar on radius pseudo-observations converted into approximately 20% uncertainty in the high-density equation of state.

## Context

This work extends the known "masquerade" problem (Alford 2005), where hybrid stars with quark cores can mimic purely nucleonic stars in mass-radius relations, by showing that even within the purely nucleonic hypothesis the composition is degenerate. The authors build on the meta-model approach of Margueron (2018a,b) for equation of state parametrization and use the FSU2 relativistic mean field model (Chen 2014) as a reference. The results establish a lower limit on the irreducible uncertainty in neutron star core composition that persists even with precise astrophysical measurements, and demonstrate that only direct constraints on the symmetry energy at high densities (potentially from heavy-ion collision experiments such as those by Adamczewski-Musch 2020) can meaningfully resolve this degeneracy.
