# Mononuclear caloric curve in a mean field model

> A mean field model is used to investigate if a plateau in caloric curve can be reached in mononuclear configuration. In the model the configuration will break up into many pieces as the plateau is approached.

# Mononuclear caloric curve in a mean field model

## Key claim

In a momentum-dependent mean field model of nuclear matter, the mononuclear configuration breaks up into many pieces before the caloric curve (temperature versus excitation energy per particle) flattens out, meaning a plateau in the caloric curve cannot be reached while the system remains a single nucleus. This contradicts the suggestion by Sobotka (2004) that a plateau might be achievable in mononuclear configurations.

## Method

The author performs temperature-dependent mean field calculations for infinite nuclear matter using a momentum-dependent interaction (with parameters fitted to reproduce binding energy, saturation density, compressibility, and effective mass) and a momentum-independent Skyrme-like interaction. For each fixed temperature, the free energy per particle is computed as a function of density; the equilibrium density is found by minimizing the free energy, and the corresponding excitation energy per particle and temperature define points on the caloric curve. The calculation is repeated until the temperature at which the free energy minimum disappears, signaling breakup of the mononuclear configuration.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy per particle = 16 MeV, saturation density ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³, compressibility K = 215 MeV, effective mass m*/m = 0.67 at the Fermi energy for the momentum-dependent interaction.
- With momentum-dependent interaction, the caloric curve stops (system breaks up) just below temperature 12 MeV before flattening out.
- With momentum-independent interaction, the system breaks up at approximately 12.5 MeV temperature, again before the caloric curve flattens out.
- The momentum-dependent caloric curve lies above the Fermi gas curve until they meet around temperature 11.5 MeV.
- Previous Thomas-Fermi calculations by De (1997) found a maximum in specific heat at temperature 10 MeV for ¹⁵⁰Sm and ⁸⁵Kr.

## Context

This work extends the study of nuclear caloric curves from finite nuclei to infinite nuclear matter, where the mononuclear or multi-fragment nature of the system is unambiguous. It contradicts the claim by Sobotka (2004) that a plateau in the caloric curve can be reached in mononuclear configurations, and instead reinforces the picture from the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (Bondorf, 1985), the Lattice Gas Model (Pan, 1995), and canonical thermodynamic models that the caloric curve flattens only when the nucleus breaks into many pieces. The results support the expanding emitting source model of Friedman (1990), where beyond a certain temperature the system explodes rather than remaining a single expanding nucleus.
