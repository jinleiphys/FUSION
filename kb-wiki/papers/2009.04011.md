# Empirical Consequences of Emergent Mass

> The Lagrangian that defines quantum chromodynamics (QCD), the strong interaction piece of the Standard Model, appears very simple. Nevertheless, it is responsible for an astonishing array of high-level phenomena with enormous apparent complexity, e.g., the existence, number and structure of atomic nuclei. The source of all these things can be traced to emergent mass, which might itself be QCD's self-stabilising mechanism. A background to this perspective is provided, presenting, inter alia, a discussion of the gluon mass and QCD's process-independent effective charge and highlighting an array of observable expressions of emergent mass, ranging from its manifestations in pion parton distributions to those in nucleon electromagnetic form factors.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the mass-scale of visible matter (e.g., the proton mass ~1 GeV) is overwhelmingly generated by emergent mass from quantum chromodynamics (QCD) dynamics, specifically gluon self-interactions and the trace anomaly, rather than the Higgs mechanism, which contributes only one or two percent. The author hedges that while the trace anomaly "definitely can be measured" and its magnitude in the proton is large, any understanding of the proton's mass is incomplete unless it simultaneously explains why the pion (a Nambu–Goldstone mode) has near-zero mass in the chiral limit despite being a strongly interacting composite.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework of QCD, focusing on the energy-momentum tensor trace anomaly (Equation SIQCD) and its expectation values in hadrons, using the Dyson–Schwinger equations and continuum methods alongside lattice QCD results. It compares computed hadron spectra (pseudoscalar and vector mesons, octet and decuplet baryons) from continuum and lattice approaches to experimental data, and discusses the Schwinger mechanism for gluon mass generation and the process-independent effective charge.

## Key numbers

* Proton mass: \(m_p \approx 1\ \text{GeV} \approx 2000\,m_e\) (where \(m_e\) is the electron mass); \(m_p = 1.783 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg}\).
* Higgs-generated current-quark masses for \(u\) and \(d\) quarks are more than one-hundred times smaller than \(m_p\).
* Mass-gap in pure-glue QCD from lattice studies: \(\Delta \gtrsim 1.5\ \text{GeV}\) (McNeile 2008).
* Length-scale for flux-tube disintegration in the presence of light quarks: \(r_{\not\sigma} \simeq (1/3)\ \text{fm}\).
* Pion mass in the chiral limit: \(m_\pi^2 \stackrel{\text{chiral limit}}{=} 0\) (massless Nambu–Goldstone mode).

## Context

This perspective extends the line of work on emergent mass in QCD, building on the trace anomaly concept (Tarrach) and the Schwinger mechanism for gluon mass (Cornwall 1981; Gribov 1999; Binosi 2014, 2016; Gao 2017). It contrasts with pure-glue confinement studies (Jaffe Clay Millennium Problem) and the static potential/flux-tube paradigm (Wilson 1974; Isgur 1983), arguing that such approaches are empirically irrelevant because light quarks and pions, pseudo-Nambu–Goldstone modes with \(m_\pi \ll m_p\), are essential to any realistic description of confinement. The work enables future ab initio predictions from QCD for nuclear structure parameters currently fitted in effective field theories.
