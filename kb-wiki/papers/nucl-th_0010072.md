# Ground-gamma band mixing and odd-even staggering in heavy deformed nuclei

> It is proposed that the odd-even staggering (OES) in the $γ$- bands of heavy deformed nuclei can be reasonably characterized by a discrete approximation of the fourth derivative of the odd-even energy difference as a function of angular momentum $L$. This quantity exhibits a well developed staggering pattern (zigzagging behavior with alternating signs) in rare earth nuclei and actinides with long $γ$- bands ($L\geq 10$). It is shown that the OES can be interpreted reasonably as the result of the interaction of the $γ$ band with the ground band in the framework of a Vector Boson Model with SU(3) dynamical symmetry. The model energy expression reproduces successfully the staggering pattern in all considered nuclei up to $L=12-13$. The general behavior of the OES effect in rotational regions is studied in terms of the ground--$γ$ band-mixing interaction, showing that strong OES effect occurs in regions with strong ground--$γ$ band-mixing interaction. The approach used allows a detailed comparison of the OES in $γ$ bands with the other kinds of staggering effects in nuclei and diatomic molecules.

## Key claim

The odd-even staggering (OES) in the γ-bands of heavy deformed nuclei can be interpreted as the result of the interaction between the γ-band and the ground band within the Vector Boson Model (VBM) with SU(3) dynamical symmetry, rather than the previously assumed β–γ band mixing. The model reproduces the staggering pattern successfully up to L=12–13, but the description is limited to nuclei where the ground–γ band coupling is strong and the SU(3) multiplet splitting is weak (ΔE₂ ≤ 12 for rare earths, ΔE₂ ≤ 15 for actinides).

## Method

The authors introduce a discrete approximation of the fourth derivative of the odd-even energy difference, Stg(L) = 10E(L+1)+5E(L-1)+E(L+3) − [10E(L)+5E(L+2)+E(L-2)], as a quantitative measure of the OES effect. They apply the Vector Boson Model (VBM) Hamiltonian, which includes L², L·Q·L, and A⁺A terms, to obtain analytic energy expressions for ground-band and γ-band levels (even and odd L separately) for SU(3) irreps with μ=2. The model parameters g₁, g₂, g₃, and λ are fitted to experimental ground- and γ-band levels up to L=10–12 for ten nuclei, and the theoretical Stg(L) is compared to experimental patterns.

## Key numbers

- For L=8, the smallest absolute staggering amplitude is 0.013 MeV (¹⁶⁶Er) and the largest is 0.467 MeV (¹⁵⁶Gd).
- The experimental ΔE₂ ratio (energy separation between ground and γ bands normalized to the 2⁺ ground state energy) varies within 5 ≤ ΔE₂ ≤ 20 for rare earth nuclei and 13 ≤ ΔE₂ ≤ 25 for actinides.
- For nuclei with strong SU(3) splitting (ΔE₂ > 12 for rare earths, ΔE₂ > 15 for actinides), the quantum number λ exceeds 60; examples include ¹⁷²Yb (ΔE₂ = 17.6) and ²³⁸U (ΔE₂ = 20.3).
- The rms energy deviations for the fits are σ_E = 40 keV for ¹⁵⁶Gd and σ_E = 32.7 keV for ¹⁶²Er, larger than for other nuclei.
- The staggering amplitude for Er isotopes at L=8 decreases from 0.425 MeV (¹⁶²Er) to 0.251 MeV (¹⁶⁴Er) to −0.013 MeV (¹⁶⁶Er) toward the midshell region.
- The γ-bands studied have L ≥ 10; the longest are ¹⁶⁴Er (L_max = 19) and ¹⁷⁰Yb (L_max = 17).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of OES in γ-bands that attributed the effect to β–γ band mixing in the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) SU(3) limit, as in the work of Beuschel et al. and Riedel et al. The authors resolve three known complications of the β–γ mixing approach: (i) the β-band is often unobserved or too short, (ii) the description depends on whether the γ-band lies above the β-band, and (iii) the staggering amplitude increases with β–γ separation, contrary to expectation. By using the VBM framework developed in Minkov et al. (1997, 1998), the OES is instead attributed to ground–γ band mixing, which naturally explains the decrease of staggering toward midshell nuclei where the ground–γ interaction weakens. The approach enables a unified comparison of ΔL=1 staggering in γ-bands with ΔL=2 and ΔL=4 staggering in superdeformed nuclei and diatomic molecules, and suggests a transition between the VBM ground–γ coupling scheme (near the ends of rotational regions) and the IBM β–γ coupling scheme (in midshell nuclei).
