# Shapes and Dynamics from the Time-Dependent Mean Field

> Explaining observed properties in terms of underlying shape degrees of freedom is a well--established prism with which to understand atomic nuclei. Self--consistent mean--field models provide one tool to understand nuclear shapes, and their link to other nuclear properties and observables. We present examples of how the time--dependent extension of the mean--field approach can be used in particular to shed light on nuclear shape properties, particularly looking at the giant resonances built on deformed nuclear ground states, and at dynamics in highly-deformed fission isomers. Example calculations are shown of $^{28}$Si in the first case, and $^{240}$Pu in the latter case.

## Key claim

Time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations can reveal how nuclear shape influences dynamics, but the simple model of deformation splitting in giant resonances is confounded by Landau damping from the spread of underlying single-particle energies, which can obscure the separation between modes in a model-dependent way.

## Method

The authors performed TDHF calculations using the Sky3D code with Skyrme-type effective interactions. For the giant resonance study, they applied an instantaneous monopole boost to the static ground state of \(^{28}\)Si (calculated with the SkI4 force) and extracted the time-dependent response of multipole moments. For the fission isomer study, they released constrained static Hartree-Fock configurations at different quadrupole deformations along the fission isomer valley of \(^{240}\)Pu (calculated with the SkM* force) and followed the subsequent time evolution of shape vibrations.

## Key numbers

- \(^{28}\)Si static ground state deformation: \(\beta_2 = -0.210\) using the SkI4 Skyrme force.
- Fission isomer in \(^{240}\)Pu: deformation \(\beta_{20} \simeq 0.69\) with a barrier height of approximately 5 MeV using the SkM* force.
- Initial release points for fission isomer dynamics: \(\beta_{20} = 0.71\), \(\beta_{20} = 0.77\), and \(\beta_{20} = 0.83\).

## Context

This work extends the long-standing use of self-consistent mean-field models for nuclear shapes (Stevenson 2003, 2005) by applying the TDHF method to two specific cases. The giant resonance analysis builds on the known deformation splitting predicted by Danos (1964) and shows how monopole kicks couple to quadrupole modes in deformed nuclei, as previously noted by Simenel (2003). The fission isomer dynamics in \(^{240}\)Pu extends recent studies by Goddard (2014, 2015) on non-adiabatic fission properties, demonstrating that TDHF can follow shape vibrations including octupole and hexadecupole fluctuations. The approach enables future studies of shape properties in fission fragments and more detailed analysis of multipole coupling in deformed nuclei.
