# Nuclear Astrophysics in Storage Rings

> Nuclear reaction cross sections are usually very small in typical astrophysical environments. It has been one of the major challenges of experimental nuclear astrophysics to assess the magnitude of these cross sections in the laboratory. For a successful experiment high luminosity beams are needed. Increasing the target width, one also increases the reaction yields. But, this is of limited use due to multiple scattering in the target. Storage rings are a very good way to overcome these difficulties. In principle, they can be tuned to large luminosities, and have the advantage of crossing the interaction region many times per second (typically one million/s), compensating low density internal gas targets, or low reaction rates in beam-beam collisions. Storage rings are also ideal tools for precise measurements of masses and beta-decay lifetimes of nuclei of relevance for astrophysics.

## Key claim

Storage rings can overcome the fundamental difficulty of measuring very small nuclear reaction cross sections in astrophysical environments by enabling high luminosities through repeated beam crossings (typically one million per second), compensating for low-density internal gas targets or low reaction rates in beam-beam collisions, though the paper presents this as a principle rather than a demonstrated achievement for most reactions.

## Method

The paper reviews how storage rings can be applied to nuclear astrophysics by discussing several experimental techniques: bound-beta-decay lifetime measurements using fully stripped ions stored in rings; elastic and inelastic proton scattering with internal gas targets for determining matter distributions; transfer reactions such as (d,p) for spectroscopic factors; the Trojan Horse method using breakup reactions to extract low-energy astrophysical cross sections; asymptotic normalization coefficients from peripheral transfer reactions; charge-exchange (p,n) reactions for Gamow-Teller matrix elements; and Coulomb dissociation of projectiles on heavy targets to deduce radiative capture cross sections via time reversal.

## Key numbers

- Storage rings can cross the interaction region typically one million times per second
- Bound-beta-decay half-life of bare 163Dy: 47 +5/-4 days (stable as neutral atom)
- Bound-beta-decay half-life of bare 187Re: 34 ± 7 years (neutral atom half-life is 43 Gyr)
- Plasma screening enhances reaction rates such as 3He(3He,2p)4He and 7Be(p,γ)8B by as much as 20%
- The 4He(3H,γ)7Li reaction discrepancy leads to 30%-40% uncertainty in primordial 7Li abundance and the cosmological parameter ΩB
- The 8Li(α,n)11B cross section estimated by extrapolation to Ec.m. ~ 0.5 MeV is larger than the previous value by roughly a factor of 5
- The 12C(α,γ)16O reaction cross section should be known to better than 20% for good stellar modeling
- The astrophysical S17(0) factor for 7Be(p,γ)8B derived from asymptotic normalization coefficients: 27.4 ± 4.4 eV.b
- The S17(0) value from Coulomb dissociation method: 16.7 eV.b
- The gamma width of the 5.173 MeV 1- resonance of 14O from Coulomb dissociation: Γγ = (3.1 ± 0.6) eV
- The 163Dy66+ ions were stored and accumulated in the ring for bound-beta-decay measurement
- The 187Re half-life of 34 ± 7 years is much smaller than the neutral atom half-life of 43 Gyr

## Context

This review extends the discussion of storage ring applications to nuclear astrophysics following the bound-beta-decay experiments at the ESR at GSI/Darmstadt (Jun92, Bos95), which demonstrated that fully stripped ions can have dramatically different beta-decay lifetimes than neutral atoms, with implications for cosmochronometry using the 187Re-187Os pair. The paper surveys multiple experimental techniques that could be applied at storage rings to address specific problems in nuclear astrophysics, including the solar neutrino problem (7Be(p,γ)8B), primordial nucleosynthesis (4He(3H,γ)7Li), inhomogeneous Big Bang models (8Li(α,n)11B), and the 12C(α,γ)16O reaction for stellar evolution. The work enables future measurements of reactions at astrophysical energies that are currently inaccessible with direct methods, particularly for radioactive nuclei involved in the r-process and rp-process.
