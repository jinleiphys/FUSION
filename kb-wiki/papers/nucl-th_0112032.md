# Microscopic Study of Nuclear "Pasta" by Quantum Molecular Dynamics

> Structure of cold dense stellar matter at subnuclear densities is investigated with quantum molecular dynamics (QMD). We succeeded in showing that the phases with slab-like and rod-like nuclei etc. can be formed dynamically without any assumptions on the nuclear shape. Our result suggests the existence of these kinds of phases in neutron star crusts.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that nuclear "pasta" phases (slab-like, rod-like nuclei, and cylindrical/spherical bubbles) can form dynamically in quantum molecular dynamics simulations without any pre-assumed nuclear shape, suggesting these phases likely exist in neutron star crusts that cool slowly while maintaining local thermal equilibrium.

## Method
The authors performed quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations of a (n,p,e) system with proton fractions x=0.3 and 0.5 at various nucleon densities. They started from a uniform hot nucleon gas at approximately 20 MeV, then cooled the system slowly over 10^3-10^4 fm/c to temperatures of order 0.1 MeV, using periodic boundary conditions in a cubic box with total nucleon numbers of 1372 and 2048.

## Key numbers
- Proton fractions studied: x=0.3 and 0.5
- Normal nuclear density rho_0 used as reference density
- Sphere phase observed at 0.1 rho_0
- Cylinder (rod-like) phase observed at 0.25 rho_0
- Slab phase observed at 0.4 rho_0
- Cylindrical hole phase observed at 0.5 rho_0
- Spherical hole phase observed at 0.6 rho_0
- Initial temperature: k_B T ~ 20 MeV
- Final temperature: O(0.1) MeV
- Cooling timescale: O(10^3-10^4) fm/c
- For x=0.3, pasta phases appear at densities about 0.05 rho_0 lower than for symmetric matter

## Context
This work extends previous studies by Hashimoto (1984) and Ravenhall, Pethick, and Wilson (1983) that predicted pasta phases from free energy calculations assuming specific nuclear shapes, but could not address dynamical formation. The QMD approach enables microscopic dynamical simulation without shape assumptions, and the results enable future work on whole phase diagrams of cold matter and properties of nuclear shape changes, as well as applications to neutron star crust cooling scenarios.
