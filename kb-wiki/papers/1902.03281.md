# Reactor Neutrinos: Toward Oscillations

> I shall sketch the history of reactor neutrino physics over five decades since the Reines-Cowan proof of neutrino existence in the late 50s, till the advent of the present era of precision reactor neutrino oscillation experiments. There are three chapters of this story: i) Exploration of possibilities of the reactor neutrinos in the 60s and 70s; ii) Looking for oscillations under the streetlamp in the 80s and 90s; iii) Exploring oscillations in detail with known (almost) $Δm^2_{atm}$ and $Δm^2_{sol}$.}

## Key claim

The paper argues that reactor neutrino physics has evolved from a proof-of-existence demonstration into a precision tool for oscillation measurements, but it emphasizes that the "reactor anomaly" (a ~6% deficit in measured flux relative to predictions) and the "5 MeV bump" in the observed spectrum remain unresolved, potentially indicating either new physics beyond the Standard Model or underestimated uncertainties in the predicted reactor antineutrino flux.

## Method

The paper reviews five decades of reactor neutrino experiments, beginning with the Reines-Cowan detection of the inverse beta decay (IBD) reaction (ν̄_e + p → e⁺ + n) and subsequent measurements of charged-current and neutral-current reactions on deuterium and electron scattering. It then describes the evolution from single-detector oscillation searches at baselines under 100 m (sensitive to Δm² near 1 eV²) to the KamLAND experiment at ~180 km, which used 1 kt of ultrapure liquid scintillator to observe IBD events from ~40 Japanese power reactors and measure the solar Δm²_{21} oscillation parameters. The paper also details the two methods for determining reactor neutrino spectra: summation of all fission fragment beta decays, and conversion of experimentally measured electron spectra from the ILL experiments.

## Key numbers

- Reactor neutrino production: a typical 3 GW thermal power reactor undergoes ~10²⁰ fissions/s and produces ~6×10²⁰ ν̄_e/s, with energies typical for nuclear beta decay and very few ν̄_e past 8 MeV
- IBD cross section: 63 × 10⁻⁴⁴ cm² per fission, with threshold 1.8 MeV; other reactions: charged-current on deuterium 1.1 × 10⁻⁴⁴ cm²/fission (threshold 4.0 MeV), neutral-current on deuterium 3.1 × 10⁻⁴⁴ cm²/fission (threshold 2.2 MeV), elastic scattering on electrons 0.4 × 10⁻⁴⁴ cm²/fission (energy range 1-6 MeV)
- ILL experiment (Kwon et al., 1981): original ratio of experimental to expected integral positron yield for E_{e⁺} > 1 MeV was 0.955 ± 0.035(stat) ± 0.11(syst); after 1995 correction for reactor power (operated at 1.095 times rated 57 MW thermal), the ratio became 0.832 ± 3.5%(stat) ± 8.87%(syst)
- OSIRIS experiment: R_{obs}/R_{pred} = 1.014 ± 0.108 at 7.21 m from reactor core with 19.75% ²³⁵U enrichment
- KamLAND first result (162 ton·year exposure): ratio of observed IBD events to expectations = 0.611 ± 0.085(stat) ± 0.041(syst) for ν̄_e energies above 3.4 MeV
- KamLAND final result (2881 ton·year): Δm²_{21} = 7.68^{+0.15}_{-0.14}(stat) ± 0.15(syst) × 10⁻⁵ eV², determined with ~2.5% accuracy; no-oscillation hypothesis rejected at >5σ
- KamLAND detector radiopurity: ²³⁸U reduced to (3.8 ± 0.5) × 10⁻¹⁸ g/g and ²³²Th to (5.2 ± 0.8) × 10⁻¹⁷ g/g
- Reactor anomaly: experimentally determined total flux is ~6% less than predictions from Mueller (2011) and Huber (2011), which have estimated uncertainty of 2.7%

## Context

This review extends the historical narrative from the Reines-Cowan proof of neutrino existence (Reines, 1953) through the era of oscillation searches at short baselines (e.g., Reines, 1980; Kwon, 1981) to the precision era established by KamLAND (Kam1, 2002; Kam4). The paper highlights the unresolved "reactor anomaly" first noted by Mention (2011) and the "5 MeV bump" discussed by Hayes (2016), which motivate current short-baseline experiments searching for sterile neutrinos. The work also notes the first detection of geoneutrinos from ²³⁸U and ²³²Th decay within Earth (Geo05), providing an upper limit of 60 TW for radiogenic power from these isotopes.
