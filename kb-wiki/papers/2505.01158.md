# Connecting afterglow light curves to the GRB central engine

> Gamma ray burst (GRB) afterglow light curves have the potential to inform us about presently unobserved stages in the aftermath of a neutron star merger. Using numerical simulations of short GRB afterglows we obtain an approximate quantitative connection between key aspects of the emission mechanism and the shapes of the resulting light curves. Employing simple, but efficient, parameterizations of the light curve based on a broken power law in terms of physical parameters, fitted to a large dataset of synthetic light curves, we apply basic machine learning techniques to determine the approximate connection between key input parameters of the forward shock model and the light curve parameters. Solving then the inverse problem, we find that the strength of the central engine can be reasonably accurately estimated even with very limited information. In particular, merely the position of jet-break in the on-axis, respectively the maximum in the off-axis light curve determines the kinetic energy at the tens of percent level.

## Key claim

The authors claim that the kinetic energy of a short GRB central engine can be estimated at the tens of percent level from just the jet-break time in on-axis afterglow light curves, or from the time of the maximum in off-axis light curves, but this accuracy is conditional on using a simplified top-hat jet model with fixed opening angle and other parameters, and the relation is approximate, holding at the tens of percent level only over the considered parameter ranges.

## Method

The authors generated 400 on-axis and 560 off-axis synthetic afterglow light curves using the AfterglowPy package with a top-hat jet model, varying the isotropic kinetic energy, observing angle, and circumburst density while fixing other parameters. They fit each light curve with a broken power law parametrization to extract five light curve parameters (flux and time at jet-break or maximum, and power law exponents). They then performed linear regression and trained a simple neural network without hidden layers to find the leading power-law relations between the three forward shock input parameters and the five light curve parameters.

## Key numbers

- Kinetic energy range: \(2\times10^{49}\,\mathrm{erg}\leq E_{K,\mathrm{iso}}\leq9\times10^{52}\,\mathrm{erg}\)
- Circumburst density range: \(10^{-4}\,\mathrm{cm}^{-3}\leq n\leq10^{-2}\,\mathrm{cm}^{-3}\)
- On-axis observing angle range: \(0\le\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}\leq0.18\) (radians); off-axis: \(0.25\le\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}\leq0.8\) (radians)
- Jet opening angle fixed to \(\theta_{\mathrm{jet}}=0.2\) (radians)
- For on-axis infrared light curves, the flux at jet-break follows \(F_{\mathrm{jb}}^{\left(\mathrm{IR,on}\right)} = 4.8\times10^{5}\,\mu\mathrm{Jy}\left(\frac{E_{K,\mathrm{iso}}}{10^{52}\,\mathrm{erg}}\right)^{0.94}\left(\frac{\theta_{\mathrm{obs}}}{\theta_{0}}\right)^{-0.10}\left(\frac{n}{\mathrm{cm}^{-3}}\right)^{0.93}\left(\frac{d_{L}}{40\,\mathrm{Mpc}}\right)^{-2}\)
- The relative root mean square error (RMSE) for the on-axis infrared flux at jet-break is 0.048; for the off-axis infrared peak flux it is 0.129
- The relative impact \(\chi\) of kinetic energy on on-axis jet-break flux is 0.64, on jet-break time is 0.64, and on off-axis peak flux is 0.43

## Context

This work extends the forward shock model of Sari (1997) and van Eerten (2010) by providing explicit quantitative connections between afterglow light curve morphology and central engine parameters, using the AfterglowPy simulations of Ryan (2019). It enables estimation of the kinetic energy of neutron star merger remnants from sparse afterglow observations, complementing earlier work by Zhang (2005) and Wang (2018) on light curve parametrization. The approach is limited to top-hat jets and fixed microphysical parameters, so future work could generalize to structured jets and include the jet opening angle as a free parameter.
