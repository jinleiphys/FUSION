# Origin of the narrow, single peak in the fission-fragment mass distribution for $^{258}$Fm

> We discuss the origin of the narrowness of the single peak at mass-symmetric division in the fragment mass-yield curve for spontaneous fission of $^{258}$Fm. For this purpose, we employ the macroscopic-microscopic model, and calculate a potential-energy curve at the mass-symmetric compact scission configuration, as a function of the fragment mass number, which is obtained from the single-particle wave-function densities. In the calculations, we minimize total energies by varying the deformations of the two fragments, with constraints on the mass quadrupole moment and keeping the neck radius zero, as a function of mass asymmetry. Using the obtained potential, we solve the one-dimensional Schrödinger equation with a microscopic coordinate-dependent inertial mass to calculate the fragment mass-yield curve. The calculated mass yield, expressed in terms of the microscopic mass density, is consistent with the extremely narrow experimental mass distribution.

# Origin of the narrow, single peak in the fission-fragment mass distribution for $^{258}$Fm

## Key claim
The extremely narrow full-width at half-maximum (FWHM) of the symmetric mass-yield peak in spontaneous fission of $^{258}$Fm originates from the large proton shell gap at Z=50, which restricts fluctuations in the microscopic mass-asymmetry degree of freedom at the compact scission configuration, rather than from the shape of the macroscopic potential-energy surface alone.

## Method
The authors employed the macroscopic-microscopic model with a three-quadratic-surface parametrization to calculate the five-dimensional potential-energy surface for $^{258}$Fm, constrained to shapes with zero neck radius. They computed fragment mass numbers from single-particle wave-function densities (microscopic densities) rather than from the homogeneous macroscopic volume, and solved the one-dimensional Schrödinger equation with a coordinate-dependent inertial mass (Inglis-Belyaev formula) to obtain the zero-point vibrational wave function in the mass-asymmetry coordinate at the compact scission configuration.

## Key numbers
- The saddle point energy for the compact symmetric fission path is 0.34 MeV, while that for the asymmetric fission path is 0.58 MeV.
- The scission point for compact symmetric fission occurs at dimensionless quadrupole moment $q_2 = 6.5$, with fragment deformations $\epsilon_{1,2} = 0$ and mass-asymmetry parameter $\alpha_g = 0$.
- The calculated zero-point energy is -6.05 MeV.
- The FWHM of the compact symmetric component is 3.6 u (atomic mass units).
- The proton number of the left fragment remains constant at $Z_L = 50$ in the interval $0 \leq \alpha_g \leq 0.18$ in the absence of pairing, due to the large shell gap.
- The calculated mass yield from the compact symmetric valley is normalized to 50% of the total yield, based on two-mode analysis of experimental total kinetic-energy distributions from Hulet (1989).

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of bimodal fission in Fm isotopes, where the transition from asymmetric to symmetric fission between $^{256}$Fm and $^{258}$Fm was first observed by Hoffman (1980) and Hulet (1986, 1989). Previous theoretical investigations by Pashkevich (1988) using a macroscopic-microscopic model found similar mass distribution widths in compact and elongated symmetric valleys for $^{264}$Fm but characterized fragment masses by macroscopic volume asymmetry rather than microscopic densities. The present study demonstrates that the microscopic density definition, combined with the large Z=50 shell gap, is essential to reproduce the extremely narrow experimental mass distribution. The results enable future experimental tests measuring the ratio of protons to neutrons in the mass yield curve, which would distinguish between the microscopic and macroscopic mechanisms.
