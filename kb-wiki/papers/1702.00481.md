# The $x$ and $Q^2$ dependence of $\hat{q}$, quasi-particles and the JET puzzle

> We present the first attempt to extract the "$x$" dependence of the parton distribution function (PDF) of the quark gluon plasma (QGP). In the absence of knowledge regarding the mass of a QGP constituent, we define the new variable $x_N$, the nucleonic momentum fraction, which represents the ratio of the momentum of the parton to that of a self-contained section of the plasma that has the mass of a nucleon. Calculations are compared to data for single hadron suppression in terms of the nuclear modification factor $R_{AA}$ and the azimuthal anisotropy parameter $v_{2}$, as a function of transverse momentum $p_{T}$, centrality and energy of the collision. It is demonstrated that the scale evolution of the QGP-PDF is responsible for the reduction in normalization of the jet transport coefficient $\hat{q}$, between fits to Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider (RHIC) and Large Hadron Collider (LHC) data; a puzzle, first discovered by the JET collaboration. Best fits to data are suggestive of the presence of quasi-particles with wide dispersion relations in the QGP.

# The $x$ and $Q^2$ dependence of $\hat{q}$, quasi-particles and the JET puzzle

## Key claim

The reduction in the jet transport coefficient $\hat{q}$ normalized by temperature cubed ($\hat{q}/T^3$) between RHIC and LHC energies can be explained by the scale evolution of $\hat{q}$ with jet virtuality, without requiring a non-monotonic cusp-like temperature dependence near $T_c$ as previously proposed by Xu et al. (2015). The authors demonstrate that a single set of parameters describing the parton distribution function of the quark-gluon plasma at a low scale of 1 GeV$^2$ can simultaneously fit both RHIC and LHC data on single hadron suppression.

## Method

The authors implement the higher-twist formalism of multiple scattering and multiple emission for energy loss, using a 2+1D viscous hydrodynamic simulation of the medium. They introduce a nucleonic momentum fraction variable $x_N = \hat{q}L/(2 M_N E)$ to define the parton distribution function (PDF) of the QGP, parameterized as $G(x,\mu^2=1\text{GeV}^2) = N x^\alpha (1-x)^\beta$, and evolve this PDF via DGLAP evolution to higher scales. The scale-dependent $\hat{q}$ is then obtained from the scattering of a hard parton off Glauber gluons radiated from the target PDF, and the resulting nuclear modification factor $R_{AA}$ and azimuthal anisotropy $v_2$ are compared to data at both RHIC and LHC energies across multiple centralities.

## Key numbers

- The initial temperature at LHC is only about 20% higher than at RHIC, while jet energies at LHC (hadron $p_T \sim 100$ GeV) are an order of magnitude higher than at RHIC (hadron $p_T \sim 10$ GeV).
- The fit to the $R_{AA}$ data yields a $\chi^2$/d.o.f. of 5.6 for the four centrality bins shown in Fig. Raa.
- The input PDF is defined at a scale of $\mu^2 = 1$ GeV$^2$, with the kinematic bound $k_\perp^2 < 2 x_N P^+ q^-$ setting the upper limit of exchanged transverse momentum.
- The reference entropy density used for scaling $\hat{q}$ is $s_0 = 96$/fm$^3$.
- The extracted input PDF shows a valence-like bump around $x_N \sim 0.8$ and a large sea-like contribution at small $x_N$.

## Context

This work directly addresses the "JET puzzle" identified by the JET collaboration (Burke et al., 2013), where all five energy loss formalisms tested required a lower $\hat{q}/T^3$ at LHC than at RHIC at the same temperature. The authors challenge the proposal of Xu et al. (2015) that a non-monotonic cusp in $\hat{q}$ near $T_c$ explains this difference, instead demonstrating that scale evolution of $\hat{q}$ with jet virtuality provides a natural explanation. The approach extends the methodology of Majumder (2011) by incorporating scale and energy dependence into $\hat{q}$, and is consistent with recent observations within the ASW formalism reported by Andres et al. (2016). The results enable future comparisons with experimental data beyond leading hadron suppression, including heavy-flavor observables and full jet measurements.
