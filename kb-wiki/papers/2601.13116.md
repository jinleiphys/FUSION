# Numerical study of the two-boson bound-state problem with and without partial-wave decomposition

> The validation of numerical methods is a prerequisite for reliable few-body calculations, particularly when moving beyond standard partial-wave decompositions. In this work, we present a precision benchmark for the two-boson bound-state problem, solving it using two complementary formulations: the standard one-dimensional partial-wave Lippmann--Schwinger equation and a two-dimensional formulation based directly on vector variables. While the partial-wave approach is computationally efficient for low-energy bound states, the vector-variable formulation becomes essential for scattering applications at higher energies where the partial-wave expansion converges slowly. We demonstrate the high-precision numerical equivalence of both methods using rank-one separable Yamaguchi potentials and non-separable Malfliet--Tjon interactions. Furthermore, for the Yamaguchi potential, we derive exact analytical expressions quantifying the systematic errors introduced by finite momentum- and coordinate-space cut-offs. These analytical bounds provide a rigorous tool for disentangling discretization errors from truncation effects in few-body codes. The results establish a highly controlled methodological benchmark that provides a detailed baseline for vector-variable algorithms intended for more complex three- and four-body calculations.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the two-boson bound-state problem can be solved with numerical equivalence at the $10^{-10}$ MeV level using either the standard one-dimensional partial-wave Lippmann-Schwinger equation or a two-dimensional vector-variable formulation, but only for the specific case of rank-one separable Yamaguchi potentials and non-separable Malfliet-Tjon interactions, and only when the binding energy is obtained via a unit-eigenvalue search rather than a direct symmetric eigenvalue approach.

## Method

The authors solve the homogeneous Lippmann-Schwinger equation for two-boson bound states in momentum space using two complementary discretizations: a one-dimensional partial-wave decomposition restricted to the $l=0$ channel, and a two-dimensional formulation in momentum magnitude $p$ and angular variable $x = \hat{\mathbf{z}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{p}}$ that avoids partial-wave expansion entirely. Both formulations are discretized on Gauss-Legendre quadrature grids with explicit inclusion of boundary points at $p=0$ and $x=\pm 1$, and the binding energy is found by searching for the unit eigenvalue of the non-symmetric kernel matrix $K(E)$ using the Restarted Arnoldi Method. For the Yamaguchi potential, exact analytical expressions for the systematic errors from finite momentum- and coordinate-space cut-offs are derived and used to validate the numerical results.

## Key numbers

- For the Yamaguchi potential parameter set YAMA-0 ($\beta = 1.14525$ fm$^{-1}$, $\lambda = 0.22$ fm$^{-3}$), the exact two-body binding energy is $E_2 = -2.225936306578$ MeV
- For the Yamaguchi potential parameter set YAMA-23 ($\beta = 1.3905818$ fm$^{-1}$, $\lambda = 0.3707654$ fm$^{-3}$), the exact two-body binding energy is $E_2 = -2.224567197074$ MeV
- For the Yamaguchi potential parameter set YAMA-96 ($\beta = 1.15$ fm$^{-1}$, $\lambda = 0.184$ fm$^{-3}$), the exact two-body binding energy is $E_2 = -0.471586629956$ MeV
- For the Yamaguchi potential parameter set YAMA-IV ($\beta = 1.15$ fm$^{-1}$, $\lambda = 0.179$ fm$^{-3}$), the exact two-body binding energy is $E_2 = -0.331781328055$ MeV
- The numerical equivalence between the 1D and 2D formulations is demonstrated at the $10^{-10}$ MeV consistency level for the binding energy

## Context

This work extends the earlier studies of non-partial-wave-decomposed two-body scattering calculations by Elster (1998), Schadow (1999), and others, which focused on the off-shell $T$-matrix, by instead providing a precision benchmark specifically for bound-state implementations. The analytical cutoff-error formulas derived for the Yamaguchi potential provide a rigorous tool for disentangling discretization errors from truncation effects in few-body codes, enabling future validation of vector-variable algorithms intended for three- and four-body Faddeev and Faddeev-Yakubovsky calculations.
