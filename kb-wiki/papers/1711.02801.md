# Possible origin of shoulder in the reactor antineutrino spectrum

> The re-analysis of measured $^{238}U$ integrated electron spectrum reveals a shape mismatch with respect to the so called ab initio calculation and the ratio resembles well with the recently observed reactor antineutrino shoulder or bump around 5 MeV prompt energy when plotted in a reduced scale where the first two moments (area and average) of the spectrum are made identical (KNO type scaling). However, such bumps are not seen in case of other actinides like $^{235}U$ and $^{239,241}Pu$ isotopes. Since the magnitude of the bump in $^{238}U$ is comparable with the experimental observations, $^{238}U$ can not be the only source of anomaly unless similar contributions come from other uranium and plutonium isotopes as well. Therefore, it is strongly conjectured that the integrated electron spectra of uranium and plutonium isotopes particularly in the $6$ MeV to $8$ MeV energy range may have additional contributions coming from the harder part of the reactor neutron spectrum which may not be present in the thermal measurements.

## Key claim

The paper identifies a shape mismatch (bump) in the measured $^{238}U$ integrated electron spectrum compared to the ab initio calculation, and shows that this bump resembles the reactor antineutrino shoulder around 5 MeV prompt energy when plotted on a KNO-type reduced scale. However, the author concludes that $^{238}U$ alone cannot be the sole source of the anomaly unless similar contributions come from other uranium and plutonium isotopes, and conjectures that additional contributions from the harder part of the reactor neutron spectrum may be present in the 6 MeV to 8 MeV energy range.

## Method

The author reanalyzed the measured $^{238}U$ integrated electron spectrum from fast neutron beam measurements (2014) and compared it to the Huber-Muller ab initio predictions. The experimental data points were fitted using a parametrization with six parameters, and the ratio of the fitted experimental curve to the predicted curve was examined. A KNO-type dimensionless scaling was applied where the first two moments (area and average) of the spectra were made identical, allowing shape differences to be compared more sensitively. The derived antineutrino spectrum was then obtained using the conversion procedure from the original $^{238}U$ measurement, and the resulting ratios were compared with experimental data from NEOS and RENO collaborations.

## Key numbers

- The fitted experimental $^{238}U$ electron spectrum in the range 2 MeV to 8 MeV has area $A_1 \sim 1.61$ (number of electrons per fission) with average energy $<E_1> \sim 3.03$ MeV; the corresponding predicted values are $A_2 \sim 1.54$ (number of electrons per fission) and $<E_2> \sim 3.1$ MeV.
- The fit of the $^{238}U$ experimental electron data points gave $\chi^2/ndf \sim 10.5/13$.
- $^{238}U$ contributes about $\sim 10\%$ to the total fission power generation in a nuclear reactor.
- The experimental prompt energy spectrum has a mean value of about $<E> \sim 3.0$ MeV for NEOS measurements and $<E> \sim 3.2$ MeV for RENO measurements.
- The peak in the ratio appears at around $E \sim 6.5$ MeV, which corresponds to $E/<E> \sim 2.2$ in the reduced scale.

## Context

This work extends the ongoing investigation of the reactor antineutrino spectral anomaly (the "5 MeV bump") first reported by the RENO collaboration (Seo, 2013; Choi, 2016) and subsequently confirmed by Daya Bay (An, 2016), Double Chooz (Abe, 2014), and NEOS (Ko, 2017). The paper directly addresses the debate over which fissioning isotope is responsible for the bump, with Hayes (2014) arguing for $^{238}U$ and Huber (2017) favoring $^{235}U$ based on NEOS and Daya Bay data. The author's analysis suggests that while $^{238}U$ shows a bump at the right energy, its small contribution ($\sim 10\%$) to reactor power means other isotopes must also contribute, and proposes that measurements of integrated beta spectra for all isotopes should cover energies from thermal to epithermal regions to resolve the anomaly.
