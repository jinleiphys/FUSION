# Modern nucleon-nucleon interactions and charge-symmetry breaking in nuclei

> Coulomb displacement energies, i.e., the differences between the energies of corresponding nuclear states in mirror nuclei, are evaluated using recent models for the nucleon-nucleon (NN) interaction. These modern NN potentials account for breaking of isospin symmetry and reproduce $pp$ and $pn$ phase shifts accurately. The predictions by these new potentials for the binding of $^{16}O$ are calculated. A particular focus of our study are effects due to nuclear correlations and charge-symmetry breaking (CSB). We find that the CSB terms in the modern NN interactions substantially reduce the discrepancy between theory and experiment for the Coulomb displacement energies; however, our calculations do not completely explain the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly. Potential sources for the remaining discrepancies are discussed.

## Key claim

The charge-symmetry breaking (CSB) terms in modern nucleon-nucleon potentials substantially reduce the discrepancy between theory and experiment for Coulomb displacement energies in mirror nuclei, but the calculations do not completely explain the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly; the remaining discrepancy is about 100 keV, roughly half the original 200 keV deficit.

## Method

The authors performed calculations for the nucleus 16O using three modern NN potentials (CDBonn96, CDBonn99, and AV18) that include isospin symmetry breaking and CSB. They used single-particle wave functions from Hartree-Fock calculations with effective forces that fit empirical charge distributions, then computed correlation effects in a two-step procedure: short-range correlations via a G-matrix solution of the Bethe-Goldstone equation, and long-range correlations via a Green's function approach including second-order self-energy terms from two-particle one-hole and one-particle two-hole configurations within a model space up to the 1p0f shell.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy per nucleon of 16O: -4.72 MeV (CDBonn96, ISB), -4.65 MeV (CDBonn99, ISB), -3.82 MeV (AV18, ISB) in BHF approximation; -6.70 MeV (CDBonn96, ISB), -6.70 MeV (CDBonn99, ISB), -5.76 MeV (AV18, ISB) in the full Green's function approach
- Inclusion of long-range correlations yields an additional binding energy of around 2 MeV per nucleon for all interactions considered
- The CSB contribution to Coulomb displacement energies (delta_CSB) for the p1/2 hole state: 54 keV (CDBonn96), 91 keV (CDBonn99), 103 keV (AV18)
- The CSB contribution to Coulomb displacement energies for the d5/2 particle state: 49 keV (CDBonn96), 72 keV (CDBonn99), 92 keV (AV18)
- Total Coulomb displacement energy for the p1/2 hole state: 3271 keV (CDBonn96), 3320 keV (CDBonn99), 3297 keV (AV18), compared to experimental value of 3542 keV
- Total Coulomb displacement energy for the d5/2 particle state: 3326 keV (CDBonn96), 3350 keV (CDBonn99), 3401 keV (AV18), compared to experimental value of 3542 keV
- The empirical difference between pp and nn 1S0 scattering lengths, Delta a_CSB = 1.5 +/- 0.5 fm

## Context

This work extends the long-standing effort to resolve the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly, first identified by Nolen and Schiffer (1969), where Hartree-Fock calculations with phenomenological forces like Skyrme underestimated experimental Coulomb displacement energies by about seven percent. The authors compare three modern high-precision NN potentials (AV18 by Wiringa et al. 1995, CDBonn96 by Machleidt et al. 1996, and CDBonn99) that accurately fit pp and pn phase shifts and include isospin symmetry breaking. The results show that CSB in partial waves beyond 1S0 (as included in CDBonn99 and AV18) approximately doubles the CSB contribution compared to CSB in 1S0 only (CDBonn96), but still leaves a roughly 100 keV discrepancy. The authors discuss possible explanations including nuclear structure uncertainties (comparing Sato 1976 with Suzuki et al. 1992, who found differences up to 167 keV) and alternative CSB mechanisms such as rho0-omega mixing, noting that the CSB from nucleon mass splitting in CDBonn99 already explains the full empirical Delta a_CSB, leaving no room for additional CSB contributions from other sources.
