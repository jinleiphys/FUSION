# The Nearest Millisecond Pulsar Revisited with XMM-Newton: Improved Mass-Radius Constraints for PSR J0437-4715

> I present an analysis of the deepest X-ray exposure of a radio millisecond pulsar (MSP) to date, an X-ray Multi Mirror-Newton European Photon Imaging Camera spectroscopic and timing observation of the nearest known MSP, PSR J0437--4715. The timing data clearly reveal a secondary broad X-ray pulse offset from the main pulse by $\sim$0.55 in rotational phase. In the context of a model of surface thermal emission from the hot polar caps of the neutron star, this can be plausibly explained by a magnetic dipole field that is significantly displaced from the stellar center. Such an offset, if commonplace in MSPs, has important implications for studies of the pulsar population, high energy pulsed emission, and the pulsar contribution to cosmic ray positrons. The continuum emission shows evidence for at least three thermal components, with the hottest radiation most likely originating from the hot magnetic polar caps and the cooler emission from the bulk of the surface. I present pulse phase-resolved X-ray spectroscopy of PSR J0437--4715, which for the first time, properly accounts for the system geometry of a radio pulsar. Such an approach is essential for unbiased measurements of the temperatures and emission areas of polar cap radiation from pulsars. Detailed modelling of the thermal pulses, including relativistic and atmospheric effects, provides a constraint on the redshift-corrected neutron star radius of R>11.1 km (at 3 sigma conf.) for the current radio timing mass measurement of 1.76 M_sun. This limit favors "stiff'" equations of state.

# The Nearest Millisecond Pulsar Revisited with XMM-Newton: Improved Mass-Radius Constraints for PSR J0437-4715

## Key claim
The redshift-corrected neutron star radius is constrained to R > 11.1 km at 3 sigma confidence, assuming the radio timing mass measurement of 1.76 solar masses, which favors stiff equations of state. This result depends on the assumption that the observed X-ray pulsations arise from surface thermal emission from hot polar caps with a hydrogen atmosphere, and that the magnetic dipole field is significantly displaced from the stellar center.

## Method
The author analyzed a 130-kilosecond XMM-Newton EPIC exposure of PSR J0437-4715, performing pulse profile analysis, phase-averaged spectroscopy, and phase-resolved spectroscopy of the X-ray emission in the 0.1-10 keV band. The pulse profiles were folded using the radio ephemeris from Verbiest (2008), and the thermal pulse modeling incorporated relativistic effects, atmospheric effects, and a rotating neutron star model with two hot spots, including an offset magnetic dipole configuration. The spectral fits considered multi-temperature thermal models (blackbody and non-magnetic hydrogen atmosphere) plus a power-law or Comptonized blackbody component.

## Key numbers
- Redshift-corrected neutron star radius lower limit: R > 11.1 km at 3 sigma confidence, for a mass of 1.76 solar masses
- Pulsar spin period: P = 5.76 ms; spin-down rate: dP/dt = 1.0 x 10^-20 s/s; dipole magnetic field: B = 3 x 10^8 G; characteristic age: tau ≈ 4.9 Gyr; spin-down luminosity: Edot = 3.8 x 10^33 erg/s
- Distance: 156.3 ± 1.3 pc (from Deller 2008)
- Secondary X-ray pulse offset from main pulse: ~0.55 in rotational phase
- Pulsed fractions in five energy bands: 32% ± 1% (0.275-0.35 keV), 35% ± 1% (0.35-0.55 keV), 37% ± 1% (0.55-0.75 keV), 37% ± 1% (0.75-1.1 keV), 35% ± 2% (1.1-1.7 keV)
- Usable exposure times: 104.9 ks (MOS1), 104.9 ks (MOS2), 120.4 ks (pn)
- Best-fit hydrogen atmosphere model temperatures: Teff,1 ≈ 2.14 x 10^6 K (hot polar cap), Teff,2 ≈ 0.75 x 10^6 K (cooler annular region), with effective radii of 0.15 km and 1.15 km respectively (for the two-temperature Hatm+PL model)

## Context
This work extends previous X-ray studies of PSR J0437-4715 by Pavlov (1997), Zavlin (1998, 2002, 2006), and Bogdanov (2007, 2008), using a deeper, uncontaminated XMM-Newton observation that avoids the background AGN RX J0437.4-4711 which affected earlier data. The improved mass-radius constraint (R > 11.1 km) is significantly tighter than the previous limit of R > 8.3 km from Bogdanov (2007) and favors stiff equations of state over soft ones. The detection of a secondary X-ray pulse and the inferred magnetic dipole offset have implications for studies of the millisecond pulsar population, high-energy pulsed emission mechanisms, and the pulsar contribution to cosmic ray positrons.
