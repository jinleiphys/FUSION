# X-ray bounds on cooling, composition, and magnetic field of the Cassiopeia A neutron star and young central compact objects

> We present analysis of multiple Chandra and XMM-Newton spectra, separated by 9-19 years, of four of the youngest central compact objects (CCOs) with ages < 2500 yr: CXOU J232327.9+584842 (Cassiopeia A), CXOU J160103.1-513353 (G330.2+1.0), 1WGA J1713.4-3949 (G347.3-0.5), and XMMU J172054.5-372652 (G350.1-0.3). By fitting these spectra with thermal models, we attempt to constrain each CCO's long-term cooling rate, composition, and magnetic field. For the CCO in Cassiopeia A, 14 measurements over 19 years indicate a decreasing temperature at a ten-year rate of 2.2+/-0.2 or 2.8+/-0.3 percent (1sigma error) for a constant or changing X-ray absorption, respectively. We obtain cooling rate upper limits of 17 percent for CXOU J160103.1-513353 and 6 percent for XMMU J172054.5-372652. For the oldest CCO, 1WGA J1713.4-3949, its temperature seems to have increased by 4+/-2 percent over a ten year period. Assuming each CCO's preferred distance and an emission area that is a large fraction of the total stellar surface, a non-magnetic carbon atmosphere spectrum is a good fit to spectra of all four CCOs. If distances are larger and emission areas are somewhat smaller, then equally good spectral fits are obtained using a hydrogen atmosphere with B <= 7x10^10 G or B >= 10^12 G for CXOU J160103.1-513353, B <= 10^10 G or B >= 10^12 G for XMMU J172054.5-372652, and non-magnetic hydrogen atmosphere for 1WGA J1713.4-3949. In a unified picture of CCO evolution, our results suggest most CCOs, and hence a sizable fraction of young neutron stars, have a surface magnetic field that is low early in their life but builds up over several thousand years.

## Key claim

The paper finds that the Cassiopeia A neutron star's temperature is decreasing at a ten-year rate of 2.2+/-0.2 or 2.8+/-0.3 percent depending on whether X-ray absorption is assumed constant or changing, but for the oldest CCO studied (1WGA J1713.4-3949) the temperature appears to have increased by 4+/-2 percent over a ten-year period, a result that carries the caveat that the increase may be due to systematic uncertainties rather than actual heating.

## Method

The authors analyzed multiple Chandra and XMM-Newton spectra taken 9-19 years apart for four young central compact objects (CCOs) with ages under 2500 years: CXOU J232327.9+584842 (Cassiopeia A), CXOU J160103.1-513353 (G330.2+1.0), 1WGA J1713.4-3949 (G347.3-0.5), and XMMU J172054.5-372652 (G350.1-0.3). They fitted these spectra with absorbed thermal models including blackbody, non-magnetic hydrogen (nsatmos), non-magnetic carbon (nsx), and magnetic hydrogen (nsmaxg) atmosphere models, allowing fit parameters to be linked or varied between observations to test for long-term temperature changes. For Cassiopeia A specifically, they used 14 measurements over 19 years with the latest ACIS contaminant model N0014 and fixed the distance to 3.4 kpc with emission from the entire stellar surface.

## Key numbers

- Cassiopeia A CCO: temperature decreasing at a ten-year rate of 2.2+/-0.2 percent (constant X-ray absorption) or 2.8+/-0.3 percent (changing X-ray absorption), both at 1-sigma error, from 14 measurements over 19 years
- CXOU J160103.1-513353: cooling rate upper limit of 17 percent over ten years
- XMMU J172054.5-372652: cooling rate upper limit of 6 percent over ten years
- 1WGA J1713.4-3949: temperature appears to have increased by 4+/-2 percent over a ten-year period
- For CXOU J160103.1-513353: acceptable hydrogen atmosphere fits require B less than or equal to 7x10^10 G or B greater than or equal to 10^12 G
- For XMMU J172054.5-372652: acceptable hydrogen atmosphere fits require B less than or equal to 10^10 G or B greater than or equal to 10^12 G
- Cassiopeia A SNR age: 340+/-19 years, distance 3.4^{+0.3}_{-0.1} kpc

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the Cassiopeia A neutron star cooling by Heinke and Ho (2010), Posselt and Pavlov (2018), and Wijngaarden et al. (2019), and is the first investigation of long-term variability for the other three CCOs. The results support a unified picture where most CCOs have low surface magnetic fields early in life that build up over several thousand years, consistent with the magnetic field burial model proposed by Ho (2011, 2013). The paper enables future work on neutron star cooling theory by providing observational constraints on cooling rates and surface compositions for the youngest known neutron stars.
