# Influence of initial-state momentum anisotropy on the final-state collectivity in small collision systems

> A multi-phase transport model is used to understand the origin of long-range collective azimuthal correlations in small-system collisions. To disentangle between collectivity associated with initial-state intrinsic momentum anisotropy and the collectivity arising as a final-state response to the collision geometry, we studied the development of collectivity in 5.02 TeV $p$+Pb collisions with both initial-state and final-state effects included. We find that the initial momentum anisotropy may not be fully isotropized through parton interactions, and the final-state partonic collectivity in general are correlated with both the initial momentum anisotropy and the shape of the collision geometry. The initial momentum anisotropy also influences the event by event fluctuation of collective flow. Therefore the mere evidence of geometry response of the collective flow can not rule out the presence of large contributions from the initial state.

## Key claim

The paper argues that in small collision systems like p+Pb, the final-state collective flow (elliptic flow) can receive significant contributions from initial-state momentum anisotropy that is not fully isotropized during parton interactions, meaning that observing a correlation between final flow and initial spatial geometry does not rule out large initial-state momentum contributions.

## Method

The authors used the AMPT (A Multi-Phase Transport) model with Monte Carlo Glauber initial conditions for 5.02 TeV p+Pb collisions, employing a partonic cross-section of 3 mb. They implemented a two-step procedure to precisely control the initial momentum anisotropy: first randomizing all initial parton azimuthal angles to eliminate intrinsic anisotropy, then rotating them to impose a controlled elliptic anisotropy \(V_2^{\mathrm{ini}}\) along a random event-wise direction. They then compared the elliptic flow before and after the Zhang's Parton Cascade (ZPC) stage, calculating \(v_2\) using both two-particle and four-particle cumulant methods with a pseudorapidity gap between subevents.

## Key numbers

- The study uses p+Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}} = 5.02\) TeV with a partonic cross-section of 3 mb.
- For the highest multiplicity events (\(N_{\mathrm{ch}} > 150\)), the two-particle correlation function is analyzed for partons in \(0.3 < p_T < 3\) GeV.
- When initial anisotropy \(v_2^{\mathrm{ini}} = 0.1\) is imposed, the final \(v_2\{2\}\) increases by up to about 10% at high multiplicity compared to the \(v_2^{\mathrm{ini}} = 0\) case.
- At low multiplicity, the angular correlation between the initial momentum plane and final event plane \(\langle \cos 2(\Psi_2^{\mathrm{MP}} - \Psi_2) \rangle\) is close to unity for \(v_2^{\mathrm{ini}} = 0.1\).
- The cumulant ratio \((v_2\{4\}/v_2\{2\})^4\) changes sign and magnitude depending on the value of \(v_2^{\mathrm{ini}}\) and its phase relative to the participant plane.

## Context

This work extends the debate on the origin of long-range azimuthal correlations in small collision systems, which was first observed in p+Pb collisions at the LHC by CMS (2012) and ALICE (2012), and later in p+Au, d+Au, and 3He+Au collisions at RHIC by PHENIX and STAR. The paper directly addresses the question raised by Nagle (2018) and others about whether hydrodynamics can apply in small systems, and the alternative initial-state explanation from Dusling (2013) based on gluon correlations. The findings suggest that future system scans at RHIC and LHC, as discussed by Citron (2018) and others, can help disentangle initial-state and final-state contributions by comparing the ordering of \(v_n\) across different collision systems and energies.
