# General Radial-Composition Correlations in Two-Component Many-Body Systems

> The linear correlation between RMS radius difference and composition asymmetry in two-component many-body systems is a robust feature observed across nuclear experiments, diverse nuclear structural models, molecular dynamic simulations for bimetallic clusters, and galactic modeling with self-interacting dark matter. We identify the short-range attractive central force as the key ingredient for its emergence, a mechanism underpinned by the coordinate transformation under low-energy harmonic-oscillator approximation, the virial theorem, and Pauli principle/hard core potential, in many-fermion system/classic many-body system.

# General Radial-Composition Correlations in Two-Component Many-Body Systems

## Key claim

The linear correlation between RMS radius difference and composition asymmetry in two-component many-body systems is not a trivial consequence of many-body symmetries but stems specifically from a short-range attractive central force, as identified through random-interaction ensembles that statistically filter which interaction types produce the correlation.

## Method

The study employs random-interaction ensembles within Hartree-Fock calculations for nuclear systems, randomizing two-body matrix elements for central, tensor, and spin-orbit forces separately to identify which interaction type drives the correlation. For classical systems, molecular dynamics simulations with LAMMPS are performed on CuAl nanoalloy clusters using randomized embedded-atom method potentials, and N-body simulations with REBOUND are conducted for galactic models with self-interacting dark matter.

## Key numbers

- Experimental nuclear data yield Pearson's ρ = 0.85 for the ΔR–I correlation, confirming a robust linear trend.
- The random central ensemble produces a pronounced peak at ρ ≈ 0.95, with nearly 20% of samples displaying strong linearity (ρ > 0.8), while random tensor and spin-orbit ensembles yield P(ρ > 0.9) ≈ 0.
- For attractive harmonic oscillator potentials, strong positive correlations (ρ ≈ 1) emerge only when the potential minimum is localized within r₀ < 1.5√(ħ/mω); as r₀ increases, the correlation degrades to ρ ≈ −0.8.
- The random short-range ensemble shows a single peak at (V₀ = 2, r₀ = 1) for P(ρ > 0.9), confirming short-range attraction as essential.
- For CuAl clusters, the random EAM ensemble yields P(ρ > 0.85) ≈ 50% with a peak near ρ ≈ 0.9; suppressing attraction or making it long-range reduces P(ρ > 0.85) by nearly 40%.
- In galactic simulations, when dark-matter hard-ball force range r_d is comparable to ordinary matter range r_o = 0.2 (r_d = 0.1 or 0.2), the evolution stabilizes with ρ > 0.85; for r_d = 0.02, 0.002, or 0, no correlation emerges.

## Context

This work extends the known neutron skin thickness–isospin asymmetry correlation in nuclear physics (observed experimentally and in models by Myers (1969), Myers (1974), Myers (1980), and Pethick (1996)) to classical two-component systems including bimetallic clusters and galaxies with dark matter. The random-interaction approach follows the methodology of Kota (2001), Zhao (2004), and Zelevinsky (2004) for identifying robust nuclear patterns. The results enable using the ΔR–I correlation as a potential probe of dark-matter self-interaction cross sections from galaxy surveys, and suggest experimental verification on bimetallic clusters is needed.
