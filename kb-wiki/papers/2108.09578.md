# Evidence of the triaxial structure of $\boldsymbol{^{129}}$Xe at the Large Hadron Collider

> The interpretation of the emergent collective behaviour of atomic nuclei in terms of deformed intrinsic shapes [1] is at the heart of our understanding of the rich phenomenology of their structure, ranging from nuclear energy to astrophysical applications across a vast spectrum of energy scales. A new window onto the deformation of nuclei has been recently opened with the realization that nuclear collision experiments performed at high-energy colliders, such as the CERN Large Hadron Collider (LHC), enable experimenters to identify the relative orientation of the colliding ions in a way that magnifies the manifestations of their intrinsic deformation [2]. Here we apply this technique to LHC data on collisions of $^{129}$Xe nuclei [3-5] to exhibit the first evidence of non-axiality in the ground state of ions collided at high energy. We predict that the low-energy structure of $^{129}$Xe is triaxial (a spheroid with three unequal axes), and show that such deformation can be determined from high-energy data. This result demonstrates the unique capabilities of precision collider machines such as the LHC as new means to perform imaging of the collective structure of atomic nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first evidence that the ground state of \(^{129}\)Xe is triaxial (a spheroid with three unequal axes), based on high-energy collision data from the Large Hadron Collider; the claim is hedged by noting that the low-energy structure is *predicted* to be triaxial and that the high-energy data *confirm* this prediction, with the extracted deformation parameters \(\beta\approx0.2\) and \(\gamma\approx27^\circ\) being derived from a comparison of theoretical simulations to preliminary ATLAS measurements.

## Method

The authors performed symmetry-breaking constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations to map the \((\beta_v, \gamma_v)\) plane for \(^{129}\)Xe and \(^{208}\)Pb, then constructed the lowest \(1/2^+\) state of \(^{129}\)Xe using the projected generator coordinate method (PGCM) with the SLyMR1 Skyrme interaction. They extracted average intrinsic quadrupole moments \((\bar\beta_v, \bar\gamma_v)\) from the collective wave function, fitted Woods-Saxon density profiles to the HFB one-body densities, and used these profiles in a Glauber Monte Carlo model with the T\(^{\text{R}}\)ENTo initial-condition ansatz to simulate \(^{129}\)Xe+\(^{129}\)Xe and \(^{208}\)Pb+\(^{208}\)Pb collisions, computing the Pearson correlation coefficient \(\rho(v_2^2, \langle p_t \rangle)\) as a function of centrality.

## Key numbers

- Average intrinsic quadrupole moments for \(^{129}\)Xe: \(\bar\beta_v = 0.19\), \(\bar\gamma_v = 23.6^\circ\).
- Average intrinsic quadrupole moments for \(^{208}\)Pb: \(\bar\beta_v = 0.06\), \(\bar\gamma_v = 25.3^\circ\).
- Fitted Woods-Saxon parameters for \(^{129}\)Xe: \(a = 0.492\) fm, \(R_0 = 5.601\) fm, \(\beta = 0.207\), \(\gamma = 26.93^\circ\).
- Fitted Woods-Saxon parameters for \(^{208}\)Pb: \(a = 0.537\) fm, \(R_0 = 6.647\) fm, \(\beta = 0.062\), \(\gamma = 27.04^\circ\).
- PGCM charge radii: \(r_{\text{ch}} = 4.74\) fm for \(^{129}\)Xe and \(r_{\text{ch}} = 5.46\) fm for \(^{208}\)Pb, reproducing experimental data with accuracy better than one percent.
- Simulations used \(2\times10^6\) events for Pb+Pb and \(5\times10^6\) events for Xe+Xe (for both \(\gamma=0\) and \(\gamma=26.93^\circ\)).

## Context

This work extends the technique of using elliptic flow in high-energy nuclear collisions to probe nuclear deformation, previously applied to axial deformation in \(^{238}\)U+\(^{238}\)U collisions at RHIC (STAR Collaboration, 2015) and to \(^{129}\)Xe+\(^{129}\)Xe collisions at the LHC (ALICE, CMS, ATLAS Collaborations, 2018-2019), by demonstrating sensitivity to the triaxiality parameter \(\gamma\). The method enables independent extraction of \(\gamma\) from high-energy data, complementing low-energy spectroscopy which is complicated for odd-mass nuclei by entanglement of collective and single-particle contributions (Bohr and Mottelson, 1975; Bally, 2021). The results open the possibility of determining triaxiality for other species such as \(^{197}\)Au (Scamps, 2021) and foster collaboration between low-energy and high-energy nuclear physics communities.
