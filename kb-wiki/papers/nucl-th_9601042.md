# Assessment of Triton Potential Energy

> An assessment is made of the dominant features contributing to the triton potential energy, with the objective of understanding qualitatively their origins and sensitivities. Relativistic effects, short-range repulsion, and OPEP dominance are discussed. A determination of the importance of various regions of nucleon-nucleon separation is made numerically.

# Assessment of Triton Potential Energy

## Key claim

One-pion-exchange potential (OPEP) dominates the triton binding energy, contributing 70-80% of the total potential energy, because the strong short-range repulsion creates a barrier that suppresses contributions from shorter-range forces; this dominance holds for most realistic potential models, though the paper acknowledges that non-OPEP parts still contribute 20-30% of the potential energy and that a complete understanding of short-range physics may require QCD.

## Method

The authors computed the fractional accrual of kinetic energy, potential energy, and probability as a function of interparticle separation for any pair of nucleons in the triton, using the Argonne V18 potential. They also analyzed the OPEP fraction of the total potential energy by evaluating the expectation value of the one-pion-exchange operator relative to the full potential operator. The study examined relativistic corrections, including interaction-boost corrections and the effect of changing from nonrelativistic to relativistic kinetic energy, and assessed the role of short-range repulsion in suppressing contributions from small interparticle separations.

## Key numbers

- The fraction of potential energy from OPEP, ⟨Vπ⟩/⟨V⟩, is 70-80% for most realistic potentials
- Local versions of the Nijmegen potentials produce triton binding energies of approximately 7.62(3) MeV, while nonlocal versions are more bound by approximately 100 keV
- The charged pion mass extracted from NN data is mπ+ c² = 139.4(10) MeV, and the neutral pion mass is mπ⁰ c² = 135.6(13) MeV, both consistent with free-particle masses
- Changing from nonrelativistic kinetic energy T_NR = p²/2M to relativistic T_R = √(p² + M²) − M produces 5% changes in ⟨T⟩
- Interaction-boost corrections in the triton produce approximately 250-300 keV repulsion
- Triton binding differences between the Bonn-B potential and most other potential models are approximately 300 keV, attributed to a particular relativistic treatment of the tensor operator in the Bonn-B OPEP
- The net potential energy accrual inside 0.8 fm interparticle separation is quite small, approximately 10%, with the major accrual of attractive energy occurring between 1.0 and 2.0 fm

## Context

This work extends the earlier demonstration by Friar, Gibson, and Payne (1984) that the tensor potential and the ³S₁−³D₁ partial wave dominate triton binding, and the subsequent calculations by Wiringa (1991) and Friar et al. (1993) quantifying the OPEP fraction. The paper directly addresses the Nijmegen group's phase-shift analysis (Klomp, Stoks, and de Swart, 1991; Stoks, Timmermans, and de Swart, 1993), which provided the first direct quantitative measure of OPEP's global importance. The results enable a credible separation of two-nucleon-force and three-nucleon-force mechanisms in few-nucleon systems, and support the use of chiral perturbation theory as a framework for understanding the nuclear force, as advocated by Weinberg (1990, 1991, 1992) and Ordonez and van Kolck (1992).
