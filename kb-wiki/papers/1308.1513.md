# Main restrictions in the synthesis of new superheavy elements: quasifission or/and fusion-fission

> The synthesis of superheavy elements stimulates the effort to study the peculiarities of the complete fusion with massive nuclei and to improve theoretical models in order to extract knowledge about reaction mechanism in heavy ion collisions at low energies. We compare the theoretical results of the compound nucleus (CN) formation and evaporation residue (ER) cross sections obtained for the $^{48}$Ca+$^{248}$Cm and $^{58}$Fe+$^{232}$Th reactions leading to the formation of the isotopes A=296 and A=290, respectively, of the new superheavy element Lv (Z=116). The ER cross sections, which can be measured directly, are determined by the complete fusion and survival probabilities of the heated and rotating compound nucleus. That probabilities can not be measured unambiguously but the knowledge about them is important to study the formation mechanism of the observed products. For this aim, the $^{48}$Ca+$^{249}$Cf and $^{64}$Ni+$^{232}$Th reactions have been considered too. The use of the mass values of superheavy nuclei calculated in the framework of the macroscopic-microscopic model by Warsaw group leads to smaller ER cross section for all of the reactions (excluding the $^{64}$Ni+$^{232}$Th reaction) in comparison with the case of using the masses calculated by Peter Möller {\it et al}.

# Main restrictions in the synthesis of new superheavy elements: quasifission or/and fusion-fission

## Key claim
The synthesis of superheavy elements via hot fusion reactions is primarily restricted by quasifission (the breakup of the dinuclear system before reaching the compound nucleus) rather than by fusion-fission alone, and the choice of mass model for superheavy nuclei significantly affects predicted evaporation residue cross sections, the Warsaw group masses yield smaller ER cross sections than the Möller and Nix masses for most reactions considered, except the ⁶⁴Ni+²³²Th reaction.

## Method
The authors employed the dinuclear system (DNS) model to calculate capture, quasifission, fast fission, and complete fusion cross sections for four reactions: ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁸Cm, ⁵⁸Fe+²³²Th, ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁹Cf, and ⁶⁴Ni+²³²Th. They computed the potential energy surface and driving potential for each DNS, determined the intrinsic fusion barrier B*_fus from the driving potential, and calculated evaporation residue cross sections by combining the fusion probability with survival probabilities from an advanced statistical model that accounts for damping of shell corrections with excitation energy and angular momentum. For spherical projectiles (⁴⁸Ca and ⁶⁴Ni), surface vibrations were included by averaging over quadrupole and octupole deformation parameters.

## Key numbers
- Intrinsic fusion barrier B*_fus = 8.5 MeV for the ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁸Cm reaction and 11.5 MeV for the ⁵⁸Fe+²³²Th reaction.
- The mass values of the Warsaw group are larger by 2–3 MeV for superheavy nuclei with Z > 110, and their fission barriers are smaller by 2–3 MeV, compared to the Möller and Nix mass table.
- For the ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁸Cm reaction, the fusion excitation function shows that the CN formation cross section is larger and the CN excitation energy is smaller than for the ⁵⁸Fe+²³²Th reaction.
- The quasifission barrier for the DNS formed in the ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁸Cm reaction is larger than that for the ⁵⁸Fe+²³²Th reaction across the range of fragment charge numbers.
- The damping parameters used for the microscopic fission barrier are: T₀ = 1.16 MeV, d = 0.3 MeV (for excitation energy damping), and ℓ₁/₂ = 20, Δℓ = 3 (for angular momentum damping).

## Context
This work extends the DNS model approach previously applied by Nasirov et al. (2005, 2009) and Fazio et al. (2004, 2005) to the synthesis of superheavy elements with Z=116 and Z=118, comparing two different mass models: the macroscopic-microscopic model of Möller and Nix and the Warsaw group calculations by Muntian et al. (2003). The paper builds on experimental results from Oganessian et al. (2004) and the FLNR group for the ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁸Cm reaction, and provides predictions for the ⁵⁸Fe+²³²Th and ⁶⁴Ni+²³²Th reactions that could guide future experiments. The key advance is the quantitative demonstration that quasifission dominates over fusion-fission as the main hindrance to compound nucleus formation, and that the choice of nuclear mass model substantially affects predicted cross sections.
