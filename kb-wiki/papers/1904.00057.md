# Statistical learnability of nuclear masses

> After more than 80 years from the seminal work of Weizsäcker and the liquid drop model of the atomic nucleus, deviations from experiments of mass models ($\sim$ MeV) are orders of magnitude larger than experimental errors ($\lesssim$ keV). Predicting the mass of atomic nuclei with precision is extremely challenging. This is due to the non--trivial many--body interplay of protons and neutrons in nuclei, and the complex nature of the nuclear strong force. Statistical theory of learning will be used to provide bounds to the prediction errors of model trained with a finite data set. These bounds are validated with neural network calculations, and compared with state of the art mass models. Therefore, it will be argued that the nuclear structure models investigating ground state properties explore a system on the limit of the knowledgeable, as defined by the statistical theory of learning.

# Statistical learnability of nuclear masses

## Key claim

The paper argues that the difficulty in improving nuclear mass models to reach predictive precision may not be a shortcoming of any specific model, but rather that the necessary information for a model with predictivity is "unlearnable on the basis of masses (or few ground state properties) alone" when using finite data sets. This conclusion is hedged by the caveat that the analysis applies to models trained only on ground state properties, and that including a variety of other observables could change the picture.

## Method

The author applies Vapnik-Chervonenkis (VC) dimension theory and probably approximately correct (PAC) learning bounds to nuclear mass models, treating the prediction of binding energies as a statistical learning problem. Feed-forward neural networks with varying numbers of nodes and layers are trained on the 2016 Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME16) dataset using k-fold cross-validation to validate the PAC learning bounds. The VC-dimension is then estimated for the Weizsacker semi-empirical mass formula, Skyrme density functionals (UNEDF0), and Gogny functionals (D1M), and the minimum number of data points needed to reach a given generalization error is computed from the Hoeffding inequality.

## Key numbers

- The AME16 dataset contains 3336 nuclei with measured masses (considering all nuclei with N, Z >= 8, including phenomenological estimates).
- The Weizsacker semi-empirical mass formula has a VC-dimension of at least 6 (with physical bias) or 49 (without bias), with an RMSD of 3.41 +/- 0.19 MeV.
- The Skyrme UNEDF0 functional has a VC-dimension of at least 10, with an RMSD of 1.428 MeV.
- The Gogny D1M functional has a VC-dimension of at least 14, with an RMSD of 0.798 MeV.
- The neural network NN1 (1 hidden layer of 1000 nodes) has a VC-dimension of at least 52000, with an RMSD of 4.22 +/- 1.06 MeV.
- The number of data points needed to reach a generalization error of 100 keV with 99% probability is approximately 4 x 10^4 for the Weizsacker formula (36434 for VC-dim 6, 48395 for VC-dim 49), 39343 for Skyrme UNEDF0, 41259 for Gogny D1M, and 88076 for NN1.

## Context

This work extends the application of statistical learning theory to nuclear physics, building on the observation by Aberg (2002) and Bohigas (2002) that nuclear mass distributions show chaotic behavior that is difficult to reproduce deterministically. The paper provides a quantitative framework for understanding why mass models have not improved beyond MeV-level deviations despite decades of effort, as noted by Nazarewicz (2018). The analysis suggests that next-generation functionals must include a variety of observables beyond masses, potentially involving ab-initio calculations or unification of structure and reactions, rather than relying on ground state properties alone.
