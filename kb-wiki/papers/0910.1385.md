# Detecting neutrinos from black hole neutron stars mergers

> While it is well known that neutrinos are emitted from standard core collapse protoneutron star supernovae, less attention has been focused on neutrinos from accretion disks. These disks occur in some supernovae (i.e. "collapsars") as well as in compact object mergers, and they emit neutrinos with similar properties to those from protoneutron star supernovae. These disks and their neutrinos play an important role in our understanding of gamma ray bursts as well as the nucleosynthesis they produce. We study a disk that forms in the merger of a black hole and a neutron star and examine the neutrino fluxes, luminosities and neutrino surfaces for the disk. We also estimate the number of events that would be registered in current and proposed supernova neutrino detectors if such an event were to occur in the Galaxy.

# Detecting neutrinos from black hole neutron stars mergers

## Key claim

Neutrinos from a black hole-neutron star merger accretion disk could produce a detectable signal in current and proposed supernova neutrino detectors if such an event occurred within the Galaxy, though the signal would be approximately 100 times shorter but more luminous than a protoneutron star supernova signal. The actual detectability depends on the specific disk model used, with the hydrodynamical model predicting higher neutrino energies than the steady-state disk model.

## Method

The authors used a 3D hydrodynamic simulation of a black hole-neutron star merger by Ruffert and Janka (2001) for a 1.6 solar mass neutron star and 2.5 solar mass black hole with spin parameter a=0.6, post-processing the temperature, density, and electron fraction output to calculate neutrino surfaces, fluxes, luminosities, and average energies for each neutrino flavor. General relativistic corrections were applied using the Kerr metric for energy redshift and the Schwarzschild metric for neutrino ray bending, and the resulting neutrino fluxes were convolved with detector cross sections to estimate event rates in Super-Kamiokande, AMANDA, IceCube, KamLAND, ICARUS, LANNDD, and HALO.

## Key numbers

- Total observed luminosity for the accretion disk: 1.6 × 10^54 ergs/s, approximately 100 times larger than a protoneutron star supernova luminosity of ~10^52 ergs/s
- Observed average energies at infinity: electron antineutrinos 23.4 MeV, electron neutrinos 17.3 MeV, muon/tau neutrinos 26 MeV (compared to protoneutron star values of 15, 12, and 25 MeV respectively)
- Super-Kamiokande (32 kton) would record 9100 electron antineutrino events from a black hole-neutron star merger at 10 kpc, compared to 8300 from a protoneutron star supernova
- IceCube (4800 optical modules) would record 522,500 electron antineutrino events from the merger
- Estimated signal duration at Earth: 0.07-0.15 seconds, compared to ~10 seconds for a supernova
- For the steady-state disk model (Chen and Belobodorov), electron antineutrino average energies were 11 MeV (spin a=0) and 14.4 MeV (spin a=0.95)

## Context

This work extends prior studies of neutrino emission from accretion disks, which had focused on collapsar disks from rotating massive star core collapse (Nagataki et al.) and on distinguishing accretion disk neutrino spectra from protoneutron star spectra (McLaughlin and Surman, 2007). The paper provides the first detailed estimates of neutrino event rates specifically from black hole-neutron star mergers across multiple detector types, enabling comparison with the more common core-collapse supernova signal and suggesting that the shorter, more luminous burst could provide a distinctive signature for identifying such rare events. The results also inform understanding of gamma-ray burst engines and r-process nucleosynthesis in accretion disk winds, as previously studied by Surman et al.
