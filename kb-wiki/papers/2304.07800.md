# Evidence of the Schwinger mechanism from lattice QCD

> In quantum chromodynamics (QCD), gluons acquire a mass scale through the action of the Schwinger mechanism. This mass emerges as a result of the dynamical formation of massless bound-states of gluons which manifest as longitudinally coupled poles in the vertices. In this contribution, we show how the presence of these poles can be determined from lattice QCD results for the propagators and vertices. The crucial observation that allows this determination is that the Schwinger mechanism poles induce modifications, called ``displacements'', to the Ward identities (WIs) relating two- and three-point functions. Importantly, the displacement functions correspond precisely to the Bethe-Salpeter amplitudes of the massless bound-states. We apply this idea to the case of the three-gluon vertex in pure Yang-Mills SU(3). Using lattice results in the corresponding WI, we find an unequivocal displacement and show that it is consistent with the prediction based on the Bethe-Salpeter equation.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the presence of Schwinger mechanism poles in the three-gluon vertex can be inferred from lattice QCD data by detecting a nonzero "displacement" in the Ward identity relating two- and three-point functions, even though the poles themselves are strictly longitudinal and thus invisible to direct lattice simulation of the vertex.

## Method

The authors use lattice QCD results for the gluon propagator, ghost dressing function, and the classical form factor of the three-gluon vertex in the soft gluon limit, combined with a Schwinger-Dyson equation determination of a specific derivative of the ghost-gluon kernel, to evaluate the displaced Ward identity for the three-gluon vertex in pure Yang-Mills SU(3). The displacement function extracted from this identity is then compared to the Bethe-Salpeter amplitude obtained from a direct solution of the Bethe-Salpeter equation for the massless bound-state.

## Key numbers

* The gluon propagator saturates at zero momentum to a finite value, with the inverse propagator at the origin given by \(\Delta^{-1}(0) = m^2\), where \(m\) is the dynamically generated gluon mass scale.
* The ghost dressing function \(F(q)\) becomes finite at the origin, as shown by lattice data from Boucaud (2018) and Aguilar (2021).
* The form factor \(\Ls(r)\) of the three-gluon vertex in the soft gluon limit is determined from lattice data from Aguilar (2021), with a physically motivated fit given by Eq. (C12) of Aguilar (2021).
* The Bethe-Salpeter amplitude \(\Cfat_\star(r)\) was obtained in Aguilar (2021) from the Bethe-Salpeter equation, with its scale set by matching to the result from the Ward identity displacement.

## Context

This work extends the program of Aguilar (2015, 2016, 2021, 2022) and Papavassiliou (2022) to confirm the Schwinger mechanism as the origin of gluon mass generation in QCD, building on the foundational proposal by Schwinger (1962) and later developments by Cornwall (1981), Jackiw (1973), and others. The method enables a test of the mechanism using lattice QCD data, which previously could only access transverse projections of vertices and thus could not directly detect the longitudinal Schwinger poles. This approach can be applied to other vertices and, combined with the Bethe-Salpeter equation, provides a self-consistent framework for determining the bound-state amplitudes that drive gluon mass generation.
