# Coupling between electron-spin and ferromagnetic clusters

> We analyze the adiabatic magnetization of ferromagnetic clusters in an intermediate coupling regime, where the anisotropic potential is comparable to other energy scales. We find a non-monotonic behavior of the magnetic susceptibility as a function of coupling with a peak. Coriolis coupling effects are calculated for the first time; they reduce the susceptibility somewhat.

## Key claim

The paper finds that the magnetic susceptibility of ferromagnetic clusters in an intermediate coupling regime shows a non-monotonic behavior as a function of coupling strength, with a peak; the Coriolis coupling effect, calculated for the first time, reduces the susceptibility compared to the locked-moment model, but the abstract's claim of "somewhat" reduction is qualified by the condition that this reduction depends on the ratio of spin to rotational angular momentum and becomes negligible at high temperatures.

## Method

The authors construct a quantum mechanical Hamiltonian for a ferromagnetic cluster consisting of a rotational term for the cluster, a cubic-symmetry anisotropic coupling between the super-electron spin and the cluster orientation, and a Zeeman term for the external magnetic field. They diagonalize the Hamiltonian in a basis of total angular momentum eigenstates, treating the super-electron spin S and rotational angular momentum R as large but finite quantum numbers, and compute the magnetic susceptibility via second-order perturbation theory in the Zeeman interaction under the adiabatic approximation. For numerical feasibility, they introduce a similarity transformation that scales down the angular momentum quantum numbers while preserving the relevant matrix elements.

## Key numbers

- For a typical Fe$_{100}$ cluster at 100 K, the spin value is $S \simeq 100$ and the thermal rotational angular momentum is $R \simeq 600$ in units of $\hbar$.
- The bulk cubic anisotropy constants used are $K_1 = 36$ mK/atom and $K_2 = 13$ mK/atom for iron, and $K_1 = -4$ mK/atom and $K_2 = 0$ mK/atom for nickel.
- In the strong coupling limit, the leading-order susceptibility is $\chi = \frac{2}{9}\frac{\hbar^2 S^2}{k_{\rm B} T}$, with higher-order Coriolis corrections given by $\chi = \frac{2}{9}\frac{\hbar^2 S^2}{k_{\rm B} T} \left( 1 -\frac{2}{5}\frac{\hbar^2 S^2}{2\cJ k_{\rm B} T} +\frac{4}{35}\left( \frac{\hbar^2 S^2}{2\cJ k_{\rm B} T} \right)^2 +\cdots \right)$.
- In the weak coupling and high temperature limit, the susceptibility is $\chi = \frac{2S(S+1)}{9k_{\rm B} T}$.
- The condition for neglecting the Coriolis (recoil) effect is $k_{\rm B} T \gg \frac{\hbar^2 S I_{\rm eff}(T)}{\cJ}$, where $I_{\rm eff}(T)=\sqrt{\frac{2\cJ k_{\rm B} T}{\hbar^2}}$.

## Context

This work extends the locked-spin model of Bertsch and coworkers (1996) and the superparamagnetic model to an intermediate coupling regime that bridges both limits, using a cubic rather than uniaxial anisotropy. The paper enables a more accurate extraction of the giant magnetic moment from Stern-Gerlach deflection profiles of ferromagnetic clusters, particularly for systems where the spin and rotational angular momenta are comparable, and it identifies the Coriolis (Einstein-de Haas) effect as a previously neglected correction to the magnetic susceptibility.
