# Some open questions in hydrodynamics

> When speaking of unsolved problems in physics, this is surprising at first glance to discuss the case of fluid mechanics. However, there are many deep open questions that come with the theory of fluid mechanics. In this paper, we discuss some of them that we classify in two categories, the long term behavior of solutions of equations of hydrodynamics and the definition of initial (boundary) conditions. The first set of questions come with the non-relativistic theory based on the Navier-Stokes equations. Starting from smooth initial conditions, the purpose is to understand if solutions of Navier-Stokes equations remain smooth with the time evolution. Existence for just a finite time would imply the evolution of finite time singularities, which would have a major influence on the development of turbulent phenomena. The second set of questions come with the relativistic theory of hydrodynamics. There is an accumulating evidence that this theory may be relevant for the description of the medium created in high energy heavy-ion collisions. However, this is not clear that the fundamental hypotheses of hydrodynamics are valid in this context. Also, the determination of initial conditions remains questionable. The purpose of this paper is to explore some ideas related to these questions, both in the non-relativistic and relativistic limits of fluid mechanics. We believe that these ideas do not concern only the theory side but can also be useful for interpreting results from experimental measurements.

## Key claim

The paper argues that fundamental open questions in hydrodynamics remain unresolved in both the non-relativistic and relativistic limits, specifically whether smooth solutions of the Navier-Stokes equations can develop finite-time singularities and whether the assumptions of local thermodynamic equilibrium hold for the quark-gluon plasma created in heavy-ion collisions.

## Method

The authors present a pedagogical review of the mathematical structure of non-relativistic fluid mechanics based on the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations and relativistic ideal fluid mechanics based on conservation of baryon number and energy-momentum. They derive bounds on the kinetic energy and its gradient for Navier-Stokes solutions, showing that while kinetic energy remains bounded at all times, the gradient norm can grow without bound under certain conditions. For the relativistic case, they examine whether the conditions for local thermodynamic equilibrium (mean free path much smaller than system size) can be satisfied in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and the LHC.

## Context

This paper extends the pedagogical tradition of presenting the Navier-Stokes millennium problem (Fefferman 2006, Ladyzhenskaya 2003) to a broader physics audience while connecting it to relativistic hydrodynamics used in heavy-ion physics (Heinz 2001, Gyulassy 2004). The work enables non-experts to understand the mathematical obstacles to proving global regularity of Navier-Stokes solutions and the conceptual difficulties in applying hydrodynamics to the quark-gluon plasma created at RHIC and the LHC. The authors explicitly link the two domains through the role of viscosity, noting that small relaxation times from strong interactions imply small viscosity effects in both contexts.
