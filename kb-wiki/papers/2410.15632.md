# Quark mass effects in octet baryon magnetic polarisabilities via lattice QCD

> The quark mass dependence of octet baryon magnetic polarisabilities is examined at the level of individual quark-sector contributions in the uniform background-field approach of lattice QCD. The aim is to understand the direct impact of increasing the mass of a quark flavour on the magnetic polarisability and indirect or environmental effects associated with changing the mass of spectator quarks, insensitive to the background magnetic field. Noting the need to set the electric charge of some quark flavours to zero, a fractionally charged baryon formalism is introduced. We find that increasing the mass of the charged quark flavour directly causes its contribution to the magnetic polarisability to decrease. However, increasing the mass of the spectator quark flavour indirectly acts to increase the magnetic polarisability. To gain a deeper understanding of these effects, we evaluate the predictions of the constituent quark model in this context. While the model provides a compelling explanation for the environmental effect of varying the spectator quark mass, an explanation of the direct mass dependence is more complicated as competing factors combine in the final result. The lattice results indicate the key factor is a reduction in the constituent quark magnetic moment with increasing quark mass, as it governs the strength of the magnetic transition to the nearby decuplet baryon.

## Key claim

The paper finds that increasing the mass of a charged quark directly reduces its contribution to the octet baryon magnetic polarisability, while increasing the mass of a spectator (neutral) quark indirectly increases the polarisability; the lattice QCD results indicate the key factor is a reduction in the constituent quark magnetic moment with increasing quark mass, governing the strength of the magnetic transition to the nearby decuplet baryon.

## Method

The authors use the uniform background-field approach in lattice QCD on PACS-CS ensembles, introducing a fractionally charged baryon formalism that sets the electric charge of selected quark flavours to zero to isolate individual quark-sector contributions. They construct eight fractionally charged baryons (e.g., \(ddl\), \(lld\), \(ssl\), \(lls\), \(ddh\), \(hhd\), \(ssh\), \(hhs\)) and compute magnetic polarisability differences directly at the correlator level to maximise cancellation of correlated fluctuations. The magnetic polarisability differences are extracted by fitting the effective energy shift as a function of magnetic field strength quanta \(k_d = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2\), using a weighted average of fit windows with a \(\chi^2_{\text{dof}} < 2.5\) criterion for single-state isolation.

## Key numbers

- The magnetic field quantisation condition is \(eB = \frac{2\pi}{N_x N_y a^2} \frac{1}{q_d} k_d\), where \(k_d\) is an integer specifying the field strength in multiples of the minimum uniform field strength quantum.
- The improved constituent quark model uses fit parameters \(a_1 = 0.401\) and \(a_2 = 0.532\) to scale the transition term \(\beta_1\) and the quark distribution term \(\beta_2\) respectively, best reproducing the lattice QCD data.
- The heaviest PACS-CS quark mass used for approximating strange-quark distribution radii corresponds to \(m_\pi = 701\) MeV.
- The lattice simulation uses PACS-CS ensembles with a pion mass of \(\mpi = 296\) MeV at \(\kappa = 0.13700\) for the \(ddl\) baryon example shown.

## Context

This work extends the background-field lattice QCD studies of octet baryon magnetic polarisabilities from Kabelitz et al. (2024) by isolating individual quark-sector contributions for the first time. It builds on earlier discoveries of direct and indirect environmental effects in electromagnetic form factors and magnetic moments by Leinweber (1990, 1992, 2004), and evaluates the constituent quark model predictions from Close (1979) and Kabelitz et al. (2024) against the lattice results. The findings enable a deeper understanding of how quark mass variations affect hadron electromagnetic properties, and test whether environmental effects are as relevant for magnetic polarisabilities as they are for magnetic moments.
