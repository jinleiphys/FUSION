# Evidence for dynamical chiral condensate in high-energy heavy ion collisions

> Quantum chromodynamics with light quarks features an approximate global symmetry, known as chiral symmetry, that is believed to be spontaneously broken by the vacuum expectation value of a scalar and isoscalar composite field, in addition to a small explicit breaking due to finite quark masses. For a high enough temperature, as achieved in the early universe or the fireball created by a high-energy heavy ion collision, this symmetry is expected to be restored. We show theoretically that a coherent deviation of the corresponding quantum field from its usual vacuum expectation value on the freeze-out hypersurface of a heavy-ion collision leads, after resonance decays, to a characteristic contribution to the transverse momentum spectrum of charged pions, in the very soft regime, consistent with experimental data from the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider and the Large Hadron Collider. Taken together, the experimental data with the new theoretical results provide compelling support for the existence of a chiral condensation mechanism with partial restoration of chiral symmetry at high temperature.

## Key claim

The authors show theoretically that a coherent deviation of the chiral condensate field from its vacuum expectation value on the freeze-out hypersurface of a heavy-ion collision leads, after resonance decays, to a characteristic contribution to the transverse momentum spectrum of charged pions in the very soft regime; this contribution is consistent with experimental data from RHIC and LHC, providing support for the existence of a chiral condensation mechanism with partial restoration of chiral symmetry at high temperature, but the concrete dynamics leading to the partially restored chiral condensate and its relation to the dynamics of the quark-gluon plasma are not known from first principles.

## Method

The authors model the partially restored chiral condensate as a coherent quantum state with a constant field deviation of 35 MeV on the freeze-out hypersurface (defined by T approximately 125 MeV). They compute the momentum distribution of produced sigma/f0(500) resonances using a Fourier-transform formalism on the freeze-out hypersurface, then convolve this with the Sill parametrization of the sigma spectral function (with pole parameters sqrt(s_sigma) approximately (449 - i 275) MeV) to account for isotropic decays into charged pions. The fluid-dynamic background is obtained using the FluiduM framework with T(R)ENTo initial conditions, and the final spectra are compared to experimental data from PHENIX (Au+Au at 200 GeV) and ALICE (Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV and Xe+Xe at 5.44 TeV).

## Key numbers

* Vacuum expectation value of the chiral condensate field: sigma = f_pi approximately 92.3 MeV (pion decay constant).
* Assumed constant condensate deviation on the freeze-out hypersurface: Delta_sigma_coherent = 35 MeV.
* Freeze-out temperature: T approximately 125 MeV.
* Chiral crossover temperature: approximately 155 MeV (from lattice QCD, cited from Aoki et al. 2006, 2009 and HotQCD Collaboration 2014).
* Sigma/f0(500) pole parameters: sqrt(s_sigma) approximately (449 - i 275) MeV, used in the Sill spectral function parametrization.
* Pion mass: m_pi approximately 140 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for coherent particle production previously developed for the color glass condensate (Gelis and Venugopalan 2006, Gelis 2013, 2019) and for disoriented chiral condensates (Amelino-Camelia et al. 1997). It addresses the systematic discrepancy where fluid-dynamic models underestimate the observed pion yield at low transverse momentum (Lu et al. 2024), complementing indirect experimental signals for chiral symmetry restoration explored via particle yield ratios of chiral partners (Cassing et al. 2016, Palmese et al. 2016, Moreau et al. 2017) and dilepton spectra (Rapp et al. 2010, Rapp and van Hees 2016, Sung et al. 2021, Zhou et al. 2024). The results enable future first-principles calculations of the condensate dynamics and its coupling to the quark-gluon plasma evolution.
