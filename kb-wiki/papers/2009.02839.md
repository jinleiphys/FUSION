# QCD vacuum and baryon masses

> To study a possible role of the quantum chromodynamics (QCD) vacuum in nuclear and hadron physics, we evaluate a physical quantity in a candidate of the QCD vacuum. In this study we adopt the Copenhagen (spaghetti) picture of the QCD vacuum and calculate the ground-state baryon masses in a constituent quark model. We find that the calculated baryon mass does depend on a parameter that characterizes the Copenhagen picture of the QCD vacuum and satisfies the Gell-Mann-Okubo mass relation for the baryon octet. We also observe that the effective constituent quark mass defined in this study contains a contribution attributed to the Copenhagen vacuum, that is the gluon background field. We then estimate the value of the background gluon field as a function of the up (down) constituent quark mass by using the baryon masses as inputs.

## Key claim

The baryon mass in the constituent quark model depends on a parameter characterizing the Copenhagen (spaghetti) picture of the QCD vacuum, and the Gell-Mann-Okubo mass relation for the baryon octet is satisfied; however, the masses of the baryon octet and decuplet are found to be the same because only the QCD vacuum effect is considered, omitting spin-spin interactions.

## Method

The authors placed massive constituent quarks in a constant chromomagnetic background field representing the Copenhagen vacuum, solved the Dirac equation for the quarks, and averaged over spatial angles to obtain Landau-level energies. The ground-state baryon masses were then approximated as the sum of the ground-state energies of the three constituent quarks, using the spin-flavor and color wave functions of the baryon octet. The effective constituent quark mass was defined to include a contribution from the gluon background field, and the value of the background field was estimated by using baryon masses and the gluon condensate as inputs.

## Key numbers

- For a constituent quark mass \(m = 300\) MeV, the extracted value is \(\sqrt{2\tilde{g}H} = 160\) MeV.
- Using the gluon condensate \(G_2 = 0.012\) GeV\(^4\) from QCD sum rules, the estimated values are \(\sqrt{gH} = 697\) MeV and \(m = 117\) MeV.
- Using \(G_2 = 0.028\) GeV\(^4\) from Horsley (2012), the estimated values are \(\sqrt{gH} = 862\) MeV and \(m = 39\) MeV.
- Using \(G_2 = 0.077\) GeV\(^4\) from Bali (2014), the estimated values are \(\sqrt{gH} = 1110\) MeV and \(m = -87\) MeV (the negative mass can be reinterpreted via a chiral rotation).
- The coupling constant that minimizes the vacuum energy of the Copenhagen vacuum was reported as \(\alpha_s = g^2/4\pi = 0.37\) by Ambjorn (1979).

## Context

This work extends the Copenhagen (spaghetti) picture of the QCD vacuum, originally developed by Nielsen and Ambjorn (1979), to the calculation of baryon masses within a constituent quark model. It contrasts with approaches that attribute nucleon mass primarily to the quark-antiquark condensate, such as QCD sum rules by Ioffe (1981) and the parity doublet model by DeTar and Kunihiro (1988). The results enable future studies that incorporate one-gluon exchange or meson exchanges into the Copenhagen vacuum, potentially providing a unified platform for quark matter and nuclear matter, and allowing explicit observation of QCD vacuum effects on quantities like the pion decay constant.
