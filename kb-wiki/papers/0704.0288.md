# Specific heat and bimodality in canonical and grand canonical versions of the thermodynamic model

> We address two issues in the thermodynamic model for nuclear disassembly. Surprisingly large differences in results for specific heat were seen in predictions from the canonical and grand canonical ensembles when the nuclear system passes from liquid-gas co-existence to the pure gas phase. We are able to pinpoint and understand the reasons for such and other discrepancies when they appear. There is a subtle but important difference in the physics addressed in the two models. In particular if we reformulate the parameters in the canonical model to better approximate the physics addressed in the grand canonical model, calculations for observables converge. Next we turn to the issue of bimodality in the probability distribution of the largest fragment in both canonical and grand canonical ensembles. We demonstrate that this distribution is very closely related to average multiplicities. The relationship of the bimodal distribution to phase transition is discussed.

# Specific heat and bimodality in canonical and grand canonical versions of the thermodynamic model

## Key claim

The large discrepancies between canonical and grand canonical predictions for specific heat per particle at constant volume (c_v) in nuclear disassembly models arise because the two ensembles address different physics: the grand canonical model is applicable when the number of particles N is much larger than the maximum cluster size k_max, whereas the canonical model with N = k_max represents a fundamentally different system that does not scale to the thermodynamic limit.

## Method

The authors solve a statistical equilibrium model of nuclear disassembly using both canonical and grand canonical ensembles, with one type of nucleon and no Coulomb interaction. Composites up to size k_max = 2000 are allowed, with binding energies including a volume term (W = 16 MeV) and a temperature-dependent surface tension term (σ₀ = 18 MeV, T_c = 18 MeV). The canonical ensemble is solved via a recursion relation for the partition function Q_N, while the grand canonical ensemble uses chemical equilibrium to relate chemical potentials of composites. The probability distribution of the largest fragment is computed in both ensembles using explicit formulas for P_m(k).

## Key numbers

- The surface tension parametrization uses σ₀ = 18 MeV and T_c = 18 MeV, with σ(T) = σ₀[(T_c² − T²)/(T_c² + T²)]^(5/4)
- The temperature range of focus is 3 to 8 MeV
- Canonical calculations are performed for systems with N = 2000, 100,000, and 500,000 particles, all with k_max = 2000
- For bimodality studies, the freeze-out density is ρ/ρ₀ = 0.25 and the system has N = 150 (average N = 150 in grand canonical)
- Bimodality in the largest fragment distribution appears at approximately T ≈ 5.9 MeV in the grand canonical ensemble and at T ≈ 6.2 MeV in the canonical ensemble
- The excluded volume correction uses ρ₀ ≈ 0.16 fm⁻³

## Context

This work resolves a long-standing discrepancy between canonical and grand canonical predictions for specific heat in nuclear disassembly models, extending earlier studies by Dasgupta (2001) and Bugaev (2001) that had shown conflicting results for c_v at the liquid-gas phase transition. The authors demonstrate that the grand canonical model requires N >> k_max to be valid, while the canonical model with N = k_max does not scale to the thermodynamic limit. The paper also establishes a direct connection between bimodality in the largest fragment distribution and average multiplicities, showing that for k > N/2 in the canonical ensemble, P_m(k) = n_k exactly. This enables future work with realistic two-species models including Coulomb interactions, as already begun by Chaudhuri (2006).
