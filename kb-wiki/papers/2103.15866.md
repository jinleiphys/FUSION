# Models of breakup: a final state interaction problem. In memory of Mahir Hussein

> In this paper we discuss the evolution of breakup models from fully quantum mechanical, such as the Ichimura-Austern-Vincent model to semiclassical, to eikonal approximations following the insight on the mechanism first proposed by Hussein and McVoy (HM) for the presently called stripping term. In particular we concentrate on, and stress that, the correct implementation of a quantum mechanical model of breakup requires the use of energy dependent interactions and the energy averaging procedure is a key point to understand the difference among models. On the other hand using fixed energy potentials is one of the steps towards the high energy eikonal limit first proposed by HM. However the intermediate semiclassical transfer to the continuum model (STC) of Bonaccorso and Brink does use an energy dependent nucleon-target optical potential, while fixing the core-target interaction at the incident energy. The relationship between these methods is clarified.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the correct implementation of a quantum mechanical model of breakup requires energy-dependent interactions and an energy averaging procedure, and that the semiclassical transfer to the continuum (STC) model of Bonaccorso and Brink serves as an intermediate model that treats core-target motion in the eikonal approximation while treating nucleon-target motion quantum mechanically, unlike the fully quantum mechanical Ichimura-Austern-Vincent (IAV) model or the high-energy eikonal limit of Hussein and McVoy (HM).

## Method

The authors compare three theoretical approaches to nuclear breakup reactions: the fully quantum mechanical IAV model, the eikonal HM model, and the semiclassical STC model. They derive the formal relationships between these methods, showing how the HM model emerges from IAV through eikonal approximations and how the STC model uses energy-dependent nucleon-target optical potentials with Hankel function wave functions for the initial and final states. The comparison is illustrated with numerical calculations of neutron and proton momentum distributions from the reactions $^9$Be($^{14}$O,$^{13}$O)X and $^9$Be($^{14}$O,$^{13}$N)X at 53 A.MeV incident energy.

## Key numbers

- Incident energy of 53 A.MeV for the test reactions $^9$Be($^{14}$O,$^{13}$O)X and $^9$Be($^{14}$O,$^{13}$N)X
- The STC model uses the condition $\varepsilon_f^{min} = 0$ translating into $k_1^{min}=-(\varepsilon_i+\frac{1}{2}mv^2)/(\hbar v)$ for the eikonal limit kinematics
- The paper references comparisons between IAV and STC models for neutron and proton breakup from $^{14}$O and $^{16}$C on a $^9$Be target at 53 A.MeV, citing Flavigny (2012) as the experimental source

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of nuclear breakup reactions originally developed by Hussein and McVoy (1985) and Ichimura, Austern, and Vincent (1985), and clarifies the relationship between fully quantum mechanical, semiclassical, and eikonal approaches. The paper builds on recent numerical implementations of the IAV model by Jin Lei and Moro (2015-2018) and other groups, and shows that the STC and IAV methods produce very close results when compared for the same reactions. The work enables future applications of breakup models to heavy-ion reactions at intermediate to high energies, particularly for extracting spectroscopic factors from absolute breakup cross section measurements.
