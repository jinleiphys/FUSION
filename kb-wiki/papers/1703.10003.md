# Comment on "Spreading widths of giant resonances in spherical nuclei: damped transient response" by Severyukhin et al. [arXiv:1703.05710]

> We argue whether physics of universal approach of Severyukhin et al. [arXiv:1703.05710] is approved.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the "universal approach" (UA) proposed by Severyukhin et al. does not actually describe the physical spreading width of giant resonances, because it neglects the most important coupling matrix elements (those with large values above the Gaussian tails) in favor of less important ones that follow a random Gaussian distribution.

## Method

The author compares the UA of Severyukhin et al. with prior quasiparticle phonon model (QPM) calculations from Shevchenko et al., which had divided the coupling matrix elements V^{1ph}_{2ph} between one-phonon and two-phonon states into two subspaces: a large subspace following a Gaussian distribution and a small subspace with large matrix elements above the Gaussian tails. The QPM analysis had demonstrated that fragmentation is dominated by the collective mechanism, i.e., by the large matrix elements from the second group, which the UA chooses to neglect.

## Key numbers

- The figure (taken from Shevchenko et al.) shows the distribution of coupling matrix elements V^{1ph}_{2ph} for the isoscalar giant quadrupole resonance in **²⁰⁸Pb**, with a Gaussian distribution width derived from QPM results.
- The paper references the DFG support under **Contract No. SFB 1245**.

## Context

This comment directly challenges the universal approach of Severyukhin et al. (2017), arguing that the idea of including coupling between one-phonon and two-phonon states is not original and has been employed for almost half a century. The work extends prior QPM analyses by Shevchenko et al., which had already shown that the dominant contribution to spreading widths comes from large coupling matrix elements, not from the random Gaussian component that the UA relies upon. The comment suggests that the UA's predicted width does not correspond to the physical width of giant resonances, and that the approach misses the main contribution to width formation.
