# Examining the potential synthesis of new elements with $^{294}$Og

> In the relentless pursuit of expanding the periodic table, the discovery of element 119 remains elusive, despite two decades of dedicated research efforts. The traditional fusion-evaporation approach, although fruitful in the past, now appears to be approaching its operational limits. This scenario sets the stage for considering innovative methodologies essential for further advancements in the field of superheavy elements. Here, we introduce a pioneering strategy aimed at synthesizing element 119 by adapting and extending the nuclear reaction processes previously successful in producing element $^{294}$Og. This involved the fusion of $^{48}$Ca and $^{249}$Cf. Building on this, our novel approach incorporates an additional reactive target -- specifically, hydrogen -- positioned strategically behind the $^{249}$Cf. This configuration is designed to facilitate an immediate secondary reaction of the nascent $^{294}$Og with hydrogen, potentially forging new pathways to element 119. Preliminary insights also suggest that employing isotopes like deuterium or helium-3 as targets may not only enhance the production rates of element 119 but might also pave the way for the synthesis of even heavier elements, extending up to elements 120 and 121. We delve into the technicalities and feasibility of employing a dual-target method using a $^{48}$Ca beam, exploring new horizons in the quest for the superheavy unknown.

# Examining the potential synthesis of new elements with $^{294}$Og

## Key claim
The authors propose a dual-target strategy for synthesizing element 119, where $^{294}$Og produced via $^{48}$Ca + $^{249}$Cf fusion immediately undergoes a secondary reaction with a hydrogen (or other light-nuclei) target, but the production probability for element 119 from the $^{294}$Og + p reaction is below one percent, requiring high-intensity beams and dense targets to yield approximately one isotope every 520 days under optimistic assumptions.

## Method
The authors employ the Liège Intranuclear Cascade (INCL) model (version INCL++6.33) coupled with the ABLA deexcitation model to simulate the two-stage process: first, the interaction of $^{294}$Og with light particles (protons, deuterons, helium-3, helium-4, lithium-7, lithium-8) using inverse kinematics, followed by deexcitation of the hot residual nucleus via particle evaporation, fission, and simultaneous break-up. The calculations assume a dual-target geometry where a $^{48}$Ca beam first strikes a $^{249}$Cf target to produce $^{294}$Og, which then passes through a secondary light-nuclei target; a superconducting linear accelerator is proposed to boost the kinetic energy of $^{294}$Og above the Coulomb barrier for the secondary reaction.

## Key numbers
- At a beam energy of 10 MeV, the production rate of element 119 from $^{294}$Og + p is approximately 0.45%.
- The total cross section for producing element 119 via $^{294}$Og + p peaks at approximately 16 MeV beam energy.
- Assuming a $^{48}$Ca beam intensity of 9.25 × 10$^{13}$ particles per second, a $^{249}$Cf target thickness of 0.34 mg/cm$^2$, a liquid hydrogen target of about 100 mg/cm$^2$, and a reaction cross section of 10 mb for $^{294}$Og + p, the estimated synthesis rate is 2.22 × 10$^{-8}$ isotopes of element 119 per second, corresponding to approximately one isotope every 520 days.
- Using deuterium instead of hydrogen, with a reaction cross section of about 30 mb, reduces the time to synthesize one isotope of element 119 to about 173 days.
- The kinetic energy of $^{294}$Og produced in the $^{48}$Ca + $^{249}$Cf reaction is 0.13 MeV/nucleon.

## Context
This work extends the established fusion-evaporation synthesis of $^{294}$Og (element 118) via $^{48}$Ca + $^{249}$Cf, which completed the seventh row of the periodic table, and addresses the limitations of traditional hot-fusion approaches that now appear to be approaching operational limits for producing element 119. The proposed dual-target technique builds on prior studies of two-stage reaction models (e.g., the two-step model referenced in the text) and on the INCL model's successful application to heavy-ion reactions. The authors note that RIKEN has pursued $^{51}$V + $^{248}$Cm for element 119 with a peak theoretical cross section of 0.005 pb, and that theoretical cross sections for superheavy elements 119–122 range between 10$^{-1}$ and 10$^{-6}$ pb, highlighting the need for alternative methods. This work enables future experimental efforts to synthesize elements 119, 120, and possibly 121 using dual-target setups with high-intensity beams and light secondary targets.
