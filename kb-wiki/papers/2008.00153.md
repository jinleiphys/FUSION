# Evaluation of Experimental Constraints on the $^{44}$Ti($α$,p)$^{47}$V Reaction Cross Section Relevant for Supernovae

> Due to its importance as an astronomical observable in core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe), the reactions producing and destroying $^{44}$Ti must be well constrained. Generally, statistical model calculations such as Hauser-Feshbach are employed when experimental cross sections are not available, but the variation in such adopted rates can be large. Here, data from the literature is compared with statistical model calculations of the $^{44}$Ti($α$,p)$^{47}$V reaction cross section and used to constrain the possible reaction rate variation over the temperatures relevant to CCSNe. Suggestions for targeted future measurements are given.

# Evaluation of Experimental Constraints on the $^{44}$Ti($\alpha$,p)$^{47}$V Reaction Cross Section Relevant for Supernovae

## Key claim

The available experimental data from Sonzogni (2000) and Margerin (2014) are collectively consistent with a subset of Hauser-Feshbach statistical model calculations using different alpha-nucleus optical potentials, allowing the reaction rate uncertainty to be constrained to approximately +40%/-85% at 2 GK and about +/-10% at 10 GK, but only under the assumption that the statistical models reliably reproduce the cross section shape in the astrophysically relevant energy range.

## Method

The authors performed multiple Hauser-Feshbach calculations using TALYS 1.9 with all eight available alpha-nucleus optical model potentials to compute the $^{44}$Ti($\alpha$,p)$^{47}$V cross section in 100-keV bins. They then applied standard chi-squared fitting of these theoretical curves to the combined literature data set, treating the Sonzogni (2000) cross section values as direct measurements and the Margerin (2014) point as an asymmetric 40 microbarn 1-sigma upper limit using the Feldman-Cousins approach. The recommended cross section was taken as the unweighted mean between the 1-sigma upper and lower bounds at each energy bin, and the reaction rate per particle pair was numerically integrated from this recommended cross section curve.

## Key numbers

- The full range of calculated cross sections from built-in TALYS alpha potential models spans a factor of approximately 1.5 at E_cm = 10 MeV, up to a factor of approximately 18 at E_cm = 3 MeV.
- The recommended rate uncertainty band can be approximated as about +/-10% at higher temperatures (~10 GK), increasing to +40%/-85% at 2 GK, corresponding conservatively to factors of 1.4 (up) and 0.54 (down).
- The Margerin (2014) upper limit was taken as an asymmetric 40 microbarn 1-sigma upper limit at approximately E_cm ~4 MeV.
- The Sonzogni (2000) data cover center-of-mass energies between 5.7 and 9 MeV, with a $^{44}$Ti beam intensity on target of approximately 5 x 10^5 particles per second.
- The reaction has a small negative Q value on the order of -400 keV.
- The REACLIB parameterization fit deviates from the numerically calculated rate by no more than 10% inside the critical temperature range of 2.5-5 GK.

## Context

This work extends the earlier reassessment by Hoffman (2010) and the REACLIB compilation by Cyburt (2010) by including the Margerin (2014) upper limit data and exploring a wider range of Hauser-Feshbach optical model parameter space than considered in either Sonzogni (2000) or Margerin (2014). The recommended rate provides improved constraints for core-collapse supernova models that use $^{44}$Ti as a diagnostic of explosion energy and timescale, as discussed by Fryer (2007) and Sawada (2019). The paper identifies the need for additional precision measurements at energies between those covered by the two existing experiments and for spectroscopic measurements sensitive to excited-state proton channels, which would allow direct tests of the statistical model predictions.
