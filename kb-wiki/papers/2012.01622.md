# The evolution of magnetic dipole strength in $^{100-140}$ Sn isotope chain and quenching of nucleon g factors

> The evolution of electromagnetic transitions along isotope chains is of particular importance for the nuclear structure and dynamics, as well as for the r-process nucleosynthesis. Recent measurement of inelastic proton scattering on even-even $^{112-124}$Sn isotopes provides a novel insight into the isotopic dependence of E1 and M1 strength distributions. We investigate M1 transitions in even-even $^{100-140}$Sn isotopes from a theoretical perspective, based on relativistic nuclear energy density functional. The M1 transition strength distribution is characterized by an interplay between single and double-peak structures, that can be understood from the evolution of single-particle states, their occupations governed by the pairing correlations, and two-quasiparticle transitions involved. It is shown that discrepancy between model calculations and experiment on B(M1) transition strength is considerably reduced than previously known, and the quenching of the g-factors for the free nucleons needed to reproduce the experimental data on M1 transition strength amounts $g_{eff}/g_{free}$=0.80-0.93. Since some of the B(M1) strength above the neutron threshold may be missing in the inelastic proton scattering measurement, further experimental studies are required to confirm if only small modifications of the bare g-factors are actually needed when applied in finite nuclei.

## Key claim

The discrepancy between model calculations and experimental data on M1 transition strength in even-even Sn isotopes is considerably reduced compared to previous studies, and the quenching of nucleon g factors needed to reproduce the experimental data is only \(g_{eff}/g_{free}=0.80-0.93\), which is smaller than previously reported values of 0.6-0.75. However, some M1 strength above the neutron threshold may be missing in the inelastic proton scattering measurement, so the actual quenching may be even smaller or negligible.

## Method

The authors employed the relativistic quasiparticle random-phase approximation (RQRPA) based on the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) ground state, using the DD-PC1 relativistic point-coupling interaction and the Gogny D1S force for pairing correlations. The residual RQRPA interaction included an isovector-pseudovector (IV-PV) term with strength parameter \(\alpha_{\rm IV-PV} = 0.53\ \rm MeV\,fm^{3}\), which is necessary to describe unnatural parity M1 transitions. The M1 transition strength distribution was calculated for even-even \(^{100-140}\)Sn isotopes assuming spherical symmetry.

## Key numbers

* Total M1 transition strength \(\sum B_{\rm M1}\) across the \(^{100-140}\)Sn isotope chain amounts to approximately \(22-34\ \mu^2_{\rm N}\).
* The quenching factor needed to reproduce experimental data on M1 transitions in \(^{112-124}\)Sn is \(g_{eff}/g_{free}=0.80-0.93\), higher than previously reported values of approximately 0.6-0.75.
* For \(^{114}\)Sn, the ratio of total B(M1) values between theory and experiment is 1.15; for \(^{118}\)Sn it is 1.23.
* For the Gamow-Teller transition \(^{100}\)Sn \(\to\) \(^{100}\)In, the experimental strength is \(B(GT, exp.) = 9.1^{+2.6}_{-3.0}\), and the RQRPA calculation gives \(B(GT^+)=13.67\), requiring a quenching of 0.82 in the g factor.
* The calculated M1 peak for \(^{116}\)Sn is at 7.33 MeV (theory) versus 7.92 MeV (experiment), with B(M1) of 18.99 \(\mu^2_{\rm N}\) (theory) versus 0.28 \(\mu^2_{\rm N}\) (experiment).
* For \(^{120}\)Sn, the calculated low-energy peak is at 7.17 MeV with B(M1)=18.06 \(\mu^2_{\rm N}\), while the experimental range is 7.3-9.3 MeV with total B(M1)=8.8 \(\mu^2_{\rm N}\).

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical studies of M1 transitions in Sn isotopes using relativistic energy density functionals (Kruzic 2020, Oishi 2020) and provides comparison with recent inelastic proton scattering data on \(^{112-124}\)Sn (Bassauer 2020). The results show considerably reduced discrepancy between theory and experiment compared to earlier systematic studies based on the Gogny D1M force (Goriely 2016), which required an empirical factor of 2 to reproduce experimental data. The smaller quenching factors found here (0.80-0.93 versus the previously reported 0.6-0.75 from Heyde (2010), Alarcon (1989), and others) suggest that the actual quenching of nucleon g factors in finite nuclei may be very small, but further experimental studies are needed to confirm whether M1 strength above the neutron threshold is missing in the current measurements.
