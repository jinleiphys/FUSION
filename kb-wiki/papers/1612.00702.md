# Bridging the gap between event-by-event fluctuation measurements and theory predictions in relativistic nuclear collisions

> We develop methods to deal with non-dynamical contributions to event-by-event fluctuation measurements of net-particle numbers in relativistic nuclear collisions. These contributions arise from impact parameter fluctuations and from the requirement of overall net-baryon number or net-charge conservation and may mask the dynamical fluctuations of interest, such as those due to critical endpoints in the QCD phase diagram. Within a model of independent particle sources we derive formulae for net-particle fluctuations and develop a rigorous approach to take into account contributions from participant fluctuations in realistic experimental environments and at any cumulant order. Interestingly, contributions from participant fluctuations to the second and third cumulants of net-baryon distributions are found to vanish at mid-rapidity for LHC energies while higher cumulants of even order are non-zero even when the net-baryon number at mid-rapidity is zero. At lower beam energies the effect of participant fluctuations increases and induces spurious higher moments. The necessary corrections become large and need to be carefully taken into account before comparison to theory. We also provide a procedure for selecting the optimal phase-space coverage of particles for fluctuation analyses and discuss quantitatively the necessary correction due to global charge conservation.

## Key claim

The paper develops a rigorous framework to subtract non-dynamical contributions from event-by-event fluctuation measurements of net-particle numbers, showing that participant (volume) fluctuations can mask the dynamical fluctuations of interest for QCD critical endpoint searches. The authors demonstrate that at LHC energies, contributions from participant fluctuations vanish for second and third cumulants of net-baryon distributions at mid-rapidity, but higher even cumulants remain contaminated; at lower beam energies, all cumulants are affected and corrections become large.

## Method

The authors employ a Wounded Nucleon Model (WNM) with independent particle sources, deriving analytic formulae for cumulants of net-particle distributions up to fourth order that separate dynamical contributions from participant fluctuations encoded in cumulants of the wounded nucleon distribution. They implement a Glauber Monte Carlo simulation with a two-component model (soft and hard interactions) using Negative Binomial Distributions for particle production, incorporating the actual centrality selection procedures used by the ALICE experiment at the LHC and the STAR experiment at RHIC. The model uses experimentally measured mean multiplicities of protons and antiprotons as input, and simulates 150 million minimum bias events for each collision system to compute cumulants with and without participant fluctuations.

## Key numbers

- For Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV (LHC), the mean number of antiprotons and protons are nearly identical, making the mean net-proton number zero at mid-rapidity; this causes participant fluctuation contributions to vanish for second and third cumulants of net-protons.
- For the fourth cumulant of net-protons at LHC energies, participant fluctuations modify the ratio kappa_4/kappa_2 by a factor of more than two (from unity to above two) compared to the fixed-wounded-nucleon baseline.
- For Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 7.7 GeV (lowest RHIC BES energy), even with a fine centrality bin width of 2.5%, participant fluctuations cause up to 40% deviations in the fourth cumulant of net-protons and its ratio to the second cumulant.
- The two-component model uses parameters f = 0.801 for the soft fraction, mu = 29.3 and k = 1.6 for the Negative Binomial Distribution parameters, taken from the ALICE centrality determination paper.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of volume fluctuations in fluctuation measurements (Skokov, 2013; Begun, 2014; and others) by implementing the actual centrality selection criteria used in experiments, which earlier approaches did not incorporate. The paper provides a quantitative framework for correcting experimental cumulant data before comparison to lattice QCD susceptibilities or other theoretical predictions, and also addresses corrections due to global baryon number conservation. The results enable experimental collaborations (particularly ALICE and STAR) to properly subtract non-dynamical contributions when searching for critical endpoint signatures in the QCD phase diagram, and provide guidance for selecting optimal phase-space coverage and centrality bin widths.
