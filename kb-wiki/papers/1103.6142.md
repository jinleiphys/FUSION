# Investigation of the quasifission process by theoretical analysis of experimental data of fissionlike reaction products

> The fusion excitation function is the important quantity in planning experiments for the synthesis of superheavy elements. Its values seem to be determined by the experimental study of the hindrance to complete fusion by the observation of mass, angular and energy distributions of the fissionlike fragments. There is ambiguity in establishment of the reaction mechanism leading to the observed binary fissionlike fragments. The fissionlike fragments can be produced in the quasifission, fast fission, and fusion-fission processes which have overlapping in the mass (angular, kinetic energy) distributions of fragments. The branching ratio between quasifission and complete fusion strongly depends on the characteristics of the entrance channel. In this paper we consider a wide set of reactions (with different mass asymmetry and mass symmetry parameters) with the aim to explain the role played by many quantities on the reaction mechanisms. We also present the results of study of the $^{48}$Ca+$^{249}$Bk reaction used to synthesize superheavy nuclei with Z = 117 by the determination of the evaporation residue cross sections and the effective fission barriers $<B_{\rm f}>$ of excited nuclei formed along the de-excitation cascade of the compound nucleus.

# Investigation of the quasifission process by theoretical analysis of experimental data of fissionlike reaction products

## Key claim

The branching ratio between quasifission and complete fusion strongly depends on entrance channel characteristics (mass asymmetry, shell structure, orientation angles of deformed nuclei), and the mass, angular, and energy distributions of fissionlike fragments from quasifission, fast fission, and fusion-fission processes overlap, creating ambiguity in experimental determination of the complete fusion cross section; the authors argue that the inverse-fission argument (that symmetric collisions should fuse readily because fission produces symmetric fragments) is incorrect, as demonstrated by their calculations showing fusion probabilities below 10⁻⁷ for systems like ¹³²Sn+¹²⁰Cd.

## Method

The authors employed the dinuclear system (DNS) concept to model capture, quasifission, fast fission, and complete fusion, solving transport master equations for nucleon exchange between the two DNS nuclei and calculating fusion probabilities from the branching ratio of decay rates over the quasifission barrier (potential well depth) versus the intrinsic fusion barrier (on the mass-asymmetry axis). They computed capture cross sections from the trapping of the projectile into the nucleus-nucleus potential pocket after energy dissipation, and used an advanced statistical code to calculate evaporation residue cross sections and effective fission barriers for the de-excitation cascade of the compound nucleus.

## Key numbers

- For the hypothetical ¹³²Sn+¹²⁰Cd reaction leading to ²⁵²Cf compound nucleus, the fusion probability is P_CN < 5 × 10⁻⁷
- For the ¹³⁶Xe+¹³⁶Xe reaction at the same fixed excitation energy as the ²²Ne+²⁵⁰Cf reaction (where P_CN ≈ 1), the fusion probability is P_CN < 10⁻¹⁰
- For the ¹³⁶Xe+¹³²Sn reaction, P_CN < 10⁻⁸; for the ¹³²Sn+¹⁷⁶Yb reaction, P_CN < 5 × 10⁻¹¹
- In the ⁴⁸Ca+¹⁵⁴Sm reaction at E_c.m. = 138 MeV (E*_CN = 49 MeV) and 154 MeV (E*_CN = 63 MeV), the fusion probability P_CN was calculated as a function of angular momentum ℓ
- For the ⁴⁸Ca+²⁰⁸Pb reaction, the critical angular momentum for fast fission onset is ℓ_f = 58, and the maximum of the partial fusion cross section is around ℓ = 50
- The interaction time used in the master equation solution is t_int = 5 × 10⁻²¹ s, with time step Δt = 10⁻²² s

## Context

This work extends the DNS-based theoretical framework developed by the authors and collaborators (Giardina et al., 2004; Nasirov et al., 2010) to systematically analyze a wide set of reactions with varying mass asymmetry, addressing the ambiguity in distinguishing quasifission, fast fission, and fusion-fission products. The paper contradicts the argument by authors of Ref. prc782008 and prc792009 that symmetric collisions should have high fusion probability because fission produces symmetric fragments; the authors demonstrate that this reasoning fails for systems heavier than ¹¹⁰Pd+¹¹⁰Pd. The results enable more reliable planning of superheavy element synthesis experiments by providing theoretical predictions for fusion probabilities and evaporation residue cross sections, as demonstrated for the ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁹Bk reaction used to synthesize Z=117.
