# Short-range interactions in an effective field theory approach for nucleon-nucleon scattering

> We investigate in detail the effect of making the range of the ``contact'' interaction used in effective field theory (EFT) calculations of NN scattering finite. This is done in both an effective field theory with explicit pions, and one where the pions have been integrated out. In both cases we calculate NN scattering in the ${}^1 S_0$ channel using potentials which are second-order in the EFT expansion. The contact interactions present in the EFT Lagrangian are made finite by use of a square-well regulator. We find that there is an optimal radius for this regulator, at which second-order corrections to the EFT are identically zero; for radii near optimal these second-order corrections are small. The cutoff EFTs which result from this procedure appear to be valid for momenta up to about 100 MeV/c. We also find that the radius of the square well cannot be reduced to zero if the theory is to reproduce both the experimental scattering length and effective range. Indeed, we show that, if the NN potential is the sum of a one-pion exchange piece and a short-range interaction, then the short-range piece must extend out beyond 1.1 fm, regardless of its particular form.

# Short-range interactions in an effective field theory approach for nucleon-nucleon scattering

## Key claim

The range of the short-range (contact) interaction in EFT calculations of NN scattering cannot be taken to zero if the theory is to reproduce both the experimental scattering length and effective range; there is an absolute lower bound on the range of the non-one-pion-exchange piece of the interaction, regardless of its particular form. For the EFT without explicit pions this lower bound is 1.3 fm, and when explicit one-pion exchange is included the smallest possible radius is larger than 1.1 fm.

## Method

The authors regulated the contact interactions in the EFT Lagrangian by using a square-well regulator of radius R, then solved the Schrodinger equation for NN scattering in the 1S0 channel using potentials that are second-order in the EFT expansion. This was done both for an EFT with only contact interactions (pions integrated out) and for an EFT with explicit one-pion exchange plus contact interactions; in each case the coefficients C0 and C2 were renormalized to reproduce the experimental scattering length and effective range as R was varied.

## Key numbers

- Optimal square-well radius for the pionless EFT: R = 2.6 fm, with C0 = -5.1 fm^2 and x = 1.5
- Optimal square-well radius for the EFT with explicit pions: R = 2.3 fm, with C0 = -3.3 fm^2
- Absolute lower bound on the short-range interaction radius in the pionless EFT: R = 1.3 fm
- Minimum square-well radius achievable when fitting both scattering length and effective range in the pionless EFT: approximately 1.7 fm (where C2 diverges)
- Minimum square-well radius achievable when fitting both scattering length and effective range in the EFT with explicit pions: approximately 1.4 fm (where C0 and C2 diverge)
- Experimental values used for fitting: scattering length a = -24 fm; effective range r_e = 2.7 fm
- Numerical value of d/dE (dv_E/dr)|_{E=0} for the OPE case: 10.0 (accurate to two significant figures)
- The cutoff EFTs appear valid for momenta up to about 100 MeV/c

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Phillips and Cohen (1996), who showed that zero-range interactions defined as limits of short-range ones produce constraints on the effective range, and that all non-trivial zero-range interactions must be attractive. The paper contrasts with the approach of Kaplan et al. (1996), who iterated contact interactions formally using dimensional regularization and MS-bar renormalization, showing that regulating the potential before iteration is not equivalent to that method. The results enable systematic EFT calculations of NN scattering with finite-range regulators, but also reveal a limitation: the large effective range of the 1S0 channel forces the short-range potential to extend beyond 1.1 fm, suggesting a possible breakdown of the scale separation essential for power-counting arguments. The approach is relevant to applications such as pion-deuteron scattering, the three-nucleon system, and pion photoproduction on the deuteron.
