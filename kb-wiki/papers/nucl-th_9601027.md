# Relativistic deformed mean-field calculation of binding energy differences of mirror nuclei

> Binding energy differences of mirror nuclei for A=15, 17, 27, 29, 31, 33, 39 and 41 are calculated in the framework of relativistic deformed mean-field theory. The spatial components of the vector meson fields and the photon are fully taken into account in a self-consistent manner. The calculated binding energy differences are systematically smaller than the experimental values and lend support to the existency of the Okamoto--Nolen-Schiffer anomaly found decades ago in nonrelativistic calculations. For the majority of the nuclei studied, however, the results are such that the anomaly is significantly smaller than the one obtained within state-of-the-art nonrelativistic calculations.

# Relativistic deformed mean-field calculation of binding energy differences of mirror nuclei

## Key claim

The relativistic deformed mean-field calculations produce binding energy differences for mirror nuclei that are systematically smaller than experimental values, supporting the existence of the Okamoto-Nolen-Schiffer anomaly. However, for the majority of nuclei studied, the anomaly is significantly smaller than that obtained from state-of-the-art nonrelativistic calculations, with the relativistic results tending to be closer to experiment.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent relativistic mean-field calculations for the ground-state binding energies of odd-mass mirror nuclei with A=15, 17, 27, 29, 31, 33, 39 and 41, allowing for axial deformation and fully including the spatial components of the vector meson fields and the photon field. The Dirac equation for nucleons and the Klein-Gordon equations for mesonic and Coulomb fields were solved iteratively using an expansion in eigenfunctions of an axially symmetric deformed harmonic oscillator, with the NL-SH parameter set. The Coulomb field was treated self-consistently with the Fock exchange contribution included in the Slater approximation, and the nucleon's anomalous magnetic moments were incorporated via tensor coupling to the electromagnetic field.

## Key numbers

- For A=15 (1p_{1/2}^{-1} state): experimental binding energy difference 3560 keV; relativistic result 3465 keV; deviation from experiment 95 keV (compared to 380 keV for DME and 290 keV for SkII nonrelativistic calculations)
- For A=17 (1d_{5/2} state): experimental 3500 keV; relativistic 3421 keV; deviation 79 keV (compared to 300 keV for DME and 195 keV for SkII)
- For A=29 (2s_{1/2} state): experimental 5700 keV; relativistic 5666 keV; deviation 34 keV (compared to 285 keV for DME and 235 keV for SkII)
- For A=31 (2s_{1/2}^{-1} state): experimental 6250 keV; relativistic 6211 keV; deviation 39 keV (compared to 540 keV for DME and 565 keV for SkII)
- For A=27 (1d_{5/2}^{-1} state): experimental 5610 keV; relativistic 5059 keV; deviation 551 keV (larger than both DME at 480 keV and SkII at 495 keV)
- For A=41 (1f_{7/2} state): experimental 7230 keV; relativistic 6826 keV; deviation 404 keV (between DME at 440 keV and SkII at 355 keV)
- The NL-SH parameter set uses: m_sigma = 526.059 MeV, m_omega = 783.000 MeV, m_rho = 763.000 MeV; coupling constants g_sigma = 10.444, g_omega = 12.945, g_rho = 4.383

## Context

This work extends earlier relativistic calculations by Nedjadi and Rook (1985), who used single-particle Dirac equations with Woods-Saxon potentials without self-consistency, and by Barreiro, Galeao and Krein (1995), who achieved self-consistency but assumed spherical symmetry and focused on A=16 and A=40. The calculations address the Okamoto-Nolen-Schiffer anomaly, first identified by Nolen and Schiffer (1969) and Okamoto (1964), which nonrelativistic models have struggled to resolve. The results enable future investigations of alternative explanations for the anomaly, including isovector components in the sigma-meson coupling as suggested by Saito and Thomas (1994), in-medium modifications of the neutron-proton mass difference proposed by Henley and Krein (1991), and isospin violation in meson-nucleon coupling constants as suggested by Dmitrasinovic and Pollock (1995) and Gardner, Horowitz and Piekarewicz (1995).
