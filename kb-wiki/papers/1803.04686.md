# Current status and desired accuracy of the isotopic production cross sections relevant to astrophysics of cosmic rays I. Li, Be, B, C, N

> The accuracy of the current generation of cosmic-ray (CR) experiments, such as AMS-02, PAMELA, CALET, and ISS-CREAM, is now reaching $\sim$1--3\% in a wide range in energy per nucleon from GeV/n to multi-TeV/n. Their correct interpretation could potentially lead to discoveries of new physics and subtle effects that were unthinkable just a decade ago. However, a major obstacle in doing so is the current uncertainty in the isotopic production cross sections that can be as high as 20--50\% or even larger in some cases. While there is a recently reached consensus in the astrophysics community that new measurements of cross sections are desirable, no attempt to evaluate the importance of particular reaction channels and their required accuracy has been made yet. It is, however, clear that it is a huge work that requires an incremental approach. The goal of this study is to provide the ranking of the isotopic cross sections contributing to the production of the most astrophysically important CR Li, Be, B, C, and N species. In this paper, we (i) rank the reaction channels by their importance for a production of a particular isotope, (ii) provide comparisons plots between the models and data used, and (iii) evaluate a generic beam time necessary to reach a 3\% precision in the production cross-sections pertinent to the AMS-02 experiment. This first roadmap may become a starting point in the planning of new measurement campaigns that could be carried out in several nuclear and/or particle physics facilities around the world. A comprehensive evaluation of other isotopes $Z\leq30$ will be a subject of follow-up studies.

## Key claim

The paper provides a quantitative ranking of nuclear reaction channels by their importance for producing the cosmic-ray isotopes Li, Be, B, C, and N, but the authors hedge that this ranking is based on a specific propagation model and that the required 3% precision in production cross sections is a target for the AMS-02 experiment, not a guarantee that all channels can be measured to that accuracy with current facilities.

## Method

The authors used the GALPROP propagation code to compute the contribution coefficients (C_ab) for each parent projectile a and target b reaction that produces a given secondary isotope, integrating over the cosmic-ray source spectrum and interstellar medium composition. They then compared existing cross-section data from multiple experiments (e.g., Heckman 1978, Webber 2003) against model predictions from codes such as YIELDX and DPMJET, and estimated the beam time required at typical nuclear physics facilities to achieve 3% statistical precision on the most important channels.

## Key numbers

- For boron production, the sum of contribution coefficients is 3.96; the dominant channel is 12C + H with C_ab = 0.808, followed by 16O + H (0.656) and 16O + He (0.609).
- For beryllium production, the sum of contribution coefficients is 6.48; the dominant channel is 16O + H with C_ab = 1.419, followed by 12C + H (0.986) and 16O + He (0.881).
- For carbon production, the sum of contribution coefficients is 2.40; the dominant channel is 16O + H with C_ab = 1.047, followed by 16O + He (0.184) and 24Mg + H (0.123).
- For lithium production, the sum of contribution coefficients is 5.24; the dominant channel is 16O + H with C_ab = 1.057, followed by 12C + H (0.773) and 14N + He (0.673).
- For nitrogen production, the sum of contribution coefficients is 2.32; the dominant channel is 16O + H with C_ab = 1.278, followed by 16O + He (0.219) and 24Mg + H (0.147).
- Current cross-section uncertainties are 20-50% or larger, while modern cosmic-ray experiments (AMS-02, PAMELA, CALET, ISS-CREAM) achieve 1-3% precision in flux measurements from GeV/n to multi-TeV/n.

## Context

This work extends the cross-section evaluation efforts of Webber (2003) and Moskalenko (2003) by providing a systematic ranking of channels for Li, Be, B, C, and N isotopes, which are the most astrophysically important secondary cosmic-ray species. The ranking enables experimentalists to prioritize measurements at facilities such as GSI, RIKEN, and CERN, and the authors state that a comprehensive evaluation of isotopes up to Z=30 will be the subject of follow-up studies. The paper contradicts the assumption that all production channels are equally important, showing that a small number of reactions (primarily 16O + H and 12C + H) dominate the production of each secondary isotope.
