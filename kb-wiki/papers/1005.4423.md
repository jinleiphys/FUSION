# R-matrix Methods with an application to 12C(alpha,gamma)16O

## Key claim

R-matrix theory provides a semi-empirical framework for analyzing nuclear reaction data that exactly incorporates quantum mechanical symmetries and conservation laws, but its practical application requires careful choices of truncation (number of levels and channels) and channel radius, and the extracted parameters should not be taken literally when small channel radii are used.

## Method

The author reviews the standard R-matrix formalism of Lane and Thomas (1958), describing how a parameterization is defined by levels with specified spins, parities, energies, and reduced width amplitudes for each channel. The method is applied to the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction, where angular distribution measurements are used to separate E1 and E2 ground-state capture components via the formula of Dyer and Barnes (1974), and constraints from beta-delayed alpha spectra of 16N, transfer reactions, and radiative capture measurements are combined to extrapolate the cross section to astrophysical energies near 0.3 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The 12C(alpha,gamma)16O cross section must be known for energies of approximately 0.3 MeV for helium-burning conditions, but laboratory measurements exist only for energies greater than about 1 MeV.
- Channel radii used in fitting the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction typically range between 5 and 7 fm.
- The 12C(alpha,alpha) experiment of Tischhauser et al. (2002) found a best-fit channel radius of 5.4 (+0.16/-0.27) fm.
- Very little data exist above E_c.m. = 3 MeV for the ground-state components.
- A narrow resonance exists at E_c.m. = 2.68 MeV whose angular distribution could constrain E2 interference.

## Context

This review extends the long line of R-matrix analyses motivated by the need to determine the 12C/16O ratio from helium burning, following the foundational work of Wigner (1946-1947) and the comprehensive review of Lane and Thomas (1958). Recent experimental progress discussed includes the recoil separator measurements of Schurmann et al. (2005), angular distributions by Assuncao et al. (2006), first measurements of capture into the 6.05-MeV excited state by Matei et al. (2006), a new 16N beta-delayed alpha spectrum by Tang et al. (2007), and a tight limit on the gamma-ray branching ratio of the 7.12-MeV state by Matei et al. (2008). The work enables improved extrapolations of the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O S-factor to astrophysical energies and better constraints on the reaction rate in red giant stars.
