# Nuclear radius deduced from proton diffraction by a black nucleus

> We find a new method to deduce nuclear radii from proton-nucleus elastic scattering data. In this method a nucleus is viewed as a ``black'' sphere. A diffraction pattern of protons by this sphere is equivalent to that of the Fraunhofer diffraction by a circular hole of the same radius embedded in a screen. We determine the black sphere radius in such a way as to reproduce the empirical value of the angle of the observed first diffraction peak. It is useful to identify this radius multiplied by $\sqrt{3/5}$ with the root-mean-square matter radius of the target nucleus. For most of stable isotopes of masses heavier than 50, it agrees, within the error bars, with the values that were deduced in previous elaborate analyses from the data obtained at proton incident energies higher than $\sim 800$ MeV.

# Nuclear radius deduced from proton diffraction by a black nucleus

## Key claim

The authors propose that the root-mean-square matter radius of a target nucleus can be deduced from the angle of the first diffraction peak in proton-nucleus elastic scattering by treating the nucleus as a completely absorptive "black" sphere, with the black sphere radius multiplied by sqrt(3/5) equated to the rms matter radius. This method is claimed to agree with previous elaborate analyses for most stable isotopes with mass heavier than 50 at proton incident energies above about 800 MeV, but only within the error bars and only when the experimental uncertainty in the peak angle is the dominant source of error.

## Method

The target nucleus is modeled as a black sphere of radius a that completely absorbs incident protons. Using Babinet's principle, the diffraction pattern of protons by this black sphere is equivalent to Fraunhofer diffraction by a circular hole of the same radius. The black sphere radius a is determined by matching the calculated first diffraction maximum angle to the empirically observed first peak angle theta_M from proton-nucleus elastic scattering data. The rms black sphere radius r_BS is then obtained as sqrt(3/5) times a, and this value is compared to the rms matter radius r_m deduced from conventional scattering theories such as Glauber theory and optical potential methods.

## Key numbers

- The relation between black sphere radius a and first peak angle theta_M is given by 2 p a sin(theta_M/2) = 5.1356, where p is the proton momentum in the center-of-mass frame.
- The rms black sphere radius is computed as r_BS = 3.9780 / [2p sin(theta_M/2)].
- For approximate estimates at T_p >= 800 MeV and A >= 50: r_BS ~ 4.50 (1 GeV / p_Lab) (10 deg / theta_M) fm.
- For 90Zr at T_p = 800 MeV: r_BS = 4.22 +/- 0.10 fm (from Gazzaly et al.) and r_BS = 4.21 +/- 0.11 fm (from Baker et al.).
- For 92Zr at T_p = 800 MeV: r_BS = 4.21 +/- 0.07 fm.
- For 120Sn at T_p = 800 MeV: r_BS = 4.62 +/- 0.10 fm.
- For 144Sm at T_p = 800 MeV: r_BS = 4.92 +/- 0.27 fm.
- For 154Sm at T_p = 800 MeV: r_BS = 5.24 +/- 0.09 fm.
- For 176Yb at T_p = 800 MeV: r_BS = 5.47 +/- 0.10 fm.
- For 208Pb at T_p = 800 MeV: r_BS = 5.54 +/- 0.29 fm.
- Experimental uncertainty in the measured scattering angle is typically of order or smaller than +/- 0.03 deg for existing data.
- Uncertainty in the measured proton incident energy is typically a few MeV.
- Error bars on r_BS are of order or greater than +/- 0.05 fm, determined from the half width between neighboring measured angles.

## Context

This work extends the black sphere approximation originally used by Placzek and Bethe (1940) for fast neutron elastic scattering to proton-nucleus scattering at high energies. It provides a simpler alternative to conventional methods that use Glauber theory (Alkhazov et al., 1978) or optical potential methods (Ray et al., 1992; Cooper et al., 1993) to deduce matter density distributions from proton scattering data. The method is intended to enable systematic determination of rms matter radii for neutron-rich unstable nuclei that will be studied at radioactive ion beam facilities such as GSI and RIKEN, and when combined with existing charge radius data (de Vries et al., 1987; Fricke et al., 1995), it can provide estimates of neutron skin thickness for various nuclides.
