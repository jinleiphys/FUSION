# Jet substructure in high-energy hadron collisions

> In the past years significant progress has been made toward achieving a quantitative understanding of jets and their substructure in high-energy proton-proton collisions from first principles in QCD. Precise measurements have become available from the experimental collaborations at the LHC and RHIC allowing a direct comparison of theoretical calculations and data. These developments make it possible to use jet substructure observables as precision probes in heavy-ion collisions. The radiation pattern inside jets contains valuable information about the hot and dense QCD medium which can be investigated using jet substructure techniques. By studying the soft sensitivity of the different observables it is possible to obtain important insights into the interaction of hard probes with the quark gluon plasma.

# Jet substructure in high-energy hadron collisions

## Key claim
Jet substructure observables that are groomed via the soft drop algorithm can be calculated from first principles in QCD and compared directly to data, enabling controlled probes of the quark-gluon plasma in heavy-ion collisions, provided the observables are chosen to have limited sensitivity to soft radiation.

## Method
The paper reviews three categories of jet substructure observables in proton-proton collisions: those sensitive only to collinear radiation (e.g., longitudinal momentum distributions of hadrons), those requiring resummation of large logarithms (e.g., jet mass, jet angularities), and those involving jet grooming algorithms such as soft drop declustering. For heavy-ion collisions, the paper discusses three approaches: designing observables that directly test in-medium interactions (e.g., momentum sharing fraction \(z_g\), \(N\)-subjettiness), using QCD factorization to understand how the QGP couples to hard probes, and constructing microscopic models of the QGP using effective field theory, parton showers, or holographic methods.

## Key numbers
- The ungroomed jet mass distribution from ATLAS at \(\sqrt{s}=7\) TeV for jets with \(500<p_T<600\) GeV and \(|\eta|<2\) using the anti-\(k_T\) algorithm with \(R=1\) shows a peak at \(m_J\approx 70\) GeV in data, while the purely perturbative calculation peaks at \(m_J\approx 30\) GeV.
- The soft drop groomed jet mass distribution with \(z_{\rm cut}=0.1\) and \(\beta=2\) for jets with \(p_T>600\) GeV and \(|\eta|<1.5\) at \(\sqrt{s}=13\) TeV shows good agreement between perturbative calculations and ATLAS data, requiring only small hadronization corrections.
- The transition between groomed and ungroomed behavior occurs at \(m_J^2=z_{\rm cut} R^2 p_T^2\).
- The nuclear modification factor \(R_{AA}\) is defined as \(R_{AA}=d\sigma^{AA\to{\rm jet}+X} / (\langle N_{\rm coll}\rangle d\sigma^{pp\to{\rm jet}+X})\).

## Context
This review extends the work on jet substructure factorization theorems developed by Kaufmann (2015), Kang (2016, 2017, 2018), and Procura (2009) for proton-proton collisions, and applies them to heavy-ion collisions where the QGP is produced. The paper enables future precision extractions of the QCD strong coupling constant \(\alpha_s\) from groomed jet substructure observables as proposed by Bendavid (2018), and systematic studies of color decoherence using multi-prong observables like \(N\)-subjettiness (Zardoshti, 2017) and \(D_2\) (Larkoski, 2017).
