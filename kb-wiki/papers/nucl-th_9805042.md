# Microscopic model analyses of the elastic scattering of 65 MeV protons from targets of diverse mass

> Nonlocal coordinate space optical potentials for the scattering of 65 MeV protons from nuclei ranging in mass from 6Li to 238U have been defined by folding a complex, medium dependent effective interaction with the density matrix elements of each target. The effective interaction is based upon solutions of the Lippmann--Schwinger and Brueckner--Bethe--Goldstone equations having the Paris potential as input. The nuclear structure information required in our folding model are the one body density matrix elements for the target and the single nucleon bound state wave functions that they weight. For light mass nuclei, very large basis shell model calculations have been made to obtain the one body density matrix elements. For medium and heavy mass nuclei, a very simple shell model prescription has been used. The bound state single particle wave functions that complete the nuclear density matrices are either Woods--Saxon or harmonic oscillator functions. The former are employed in most cases when large basis structure is available. For light nuclei (A < 16) Woods--Saxon potential parameters and harmonic oscillator lengths are determined from fits to electron scattering form factors. For all other nuclei, we use harmonic oscillator functions with the oscillator lengths set from an A^{1/6} mass law. Using this microscopic model, optical potentials result from which differential cross sections, analyzing powers and spin rotations are found. In general the calculated results compare very well with data when the effective interactions are determined from a mapping of nucleon--nucleon g matrices. This is not the case when effective interactions built from a mapping of (free) t matrices are used.

## Key claim
The authors claim that nonlocal coordinate-space optical potentials built by folding a complex, medium-dependent effective interaction (derived from nucleon-nucleon g matrices) with nuclear one-body density matrix elements can reproduce elastic scattering observables for 65 MeV protons across a wide mass range (from 6Li to 238U), but only when the effective interaction is based on g matrices rather than free t matrices.

## Method
The authors constructed nonlocal optical potentials by folding a complex, medium-dependent effective interaction with one-body density matrix elements (OBDME) and single-particle bound state wave functions for each target nucleus. For light nuclei (A less than or equal to 16), OBDME came from large-basis shell model calculations in complete (0+2) or (0+2+4) hbar-omega spaces, with Woods-Saxon or harmonic oscillator wave functions determined from fits to electron scattering form factors. For medium and heavy nuclei, simpler shell model prescriptions (0 hbar-omega spaces or a packing model) were used with harmonic oscillator functions whose oscillator lengths followed an A^{1/6} mass law. The effective interaction was based on solutions of the Lippmann-Schwinger and Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone equations using the Paris potential, and the resulting integro-differential Schrodinger equations were solved using the program DWBA91.

## Key numbers
- Incident proton energy: 65 MeV for all targets
- Target mass range: from 6Li to 238U (50 targets total)
- For 40Ar: differential cross section peak of 60 mb/sr at 33 degrees
- For 40Ca: differential cross section peak of 44 mb/sr at 36 degrees
- For 11Li: valence neutron binding energy set to 500 keV for halo description
- Oscillator length for medium and heavy nuclei: b = A^{1/6} fm
- Scattering angle range considered: up to 80 degrees in the center of mass
- Cross section magnitude at 80 degrees: approximately 1 mb/sr

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous microscopic folding model analyses of 200 MeV proton scattering (Dortmans and Amos, 1994; mass200) to the lower energy of 65 MeV, where medium effects and nonlocalities are expected to be stronger. The approach builds on earlier work by the Hamburg group (Rikus, 1984) and complements momentum-space microscopic analyses by Elster (1990, 1998) and Arellano (1995, 1996). The results enable predictive studies of proton scattering from exotic nuclei such as 9Li and 11Li, where electron scattering data are unavailable, and demonstrate that proton scattering can be used to study matter distributions of neutron-rich and proton-rich nuclei.
