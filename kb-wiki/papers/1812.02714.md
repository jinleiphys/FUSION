# Defining the Proton Radius: a Unified Treatment

> Background: There is significant current interest in knowing the value of the proton radius and also its proper definition. Purpose: Combine the disparate literatures of hydrogen spectroscopy and diverse modern parton distributions to show that the quantity $r_p^2\equiv -6 G_E'(0)$ is the relativistically proper definition that originates from the separate bodies of work. Methods: Use perturbation theory, light-front dynamics and elementary techniques to find relativistically correct definitions of the proton radius and charge density. Results: It is found that the very same proton radius is accessed by measurements of hydrogen spectroscopy and elastic lepton scattering. The derivation of the mean-square radius as a moment of a spherically symmetric three-dimensional density is shown to be incorrect. A relativistically-correct, two-dimensional charge density is related to the diverse modern literature of various parton distributions. Relativistically invariant moments thereof are derived in a new moment expansion, the RME.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the proton radius defined as \(r_p^2 \equiv -6 G_E'(0)\) is the relativistically proper definition that emerges consistently from both hydrogen spectroscopy and elastic lepton scattering, but that the common interpretation of this radius as the second moment of a spherically symmetric three-dimensional charge density is incorrect due to relativistic effects.

## Method

The author uses perturbation theory, light-front dynamics, and elementary techniques to derive relativistically correct definitions of the proton radius and charge density. The analysis combines the formalisms of hydrogen spectroscopy (including Breit interaction and Darwin term corrections) with lepton-proton scattering theory, and connects these to modern parton distribution frameworks including generalized parton distributions (GPDs) and Wigner distributions. A critical re-examination of Sachs' (1962) derivation of a three-dimensional charge density from the Breit frame is performed, revealing a fatal flaw in the wave-packet treatment.

## Key numbers

- The muon-hydrogen spectroscopy experiment (Pohl, 2010) measured the proton radius as \(r_p = 0.84184 (67)\) fm, while electron-hydrogen spectroscopy gave \(r_p = 0.8768 (69)\) fm, a difference of about 4% known as the proton radius puzzle.
- The energy shift in hydrogen due to proton size is given by \(\Delta E = -4\pi \alpha G_E'(0) |\psi_{n0}(0)|^2 \delta_{l0}\), which is of order \(\alpha^4\) because \(|\psi_{n0}(0)|^2\) is of order \(\alpha^3\).
- The non-relativistic moment expansion gives \(\langle r^2 \rangle_{\rm NR} \equiv \int d^3r r^2 \rho_{\rm NR}(r) = -6 G_E'(0)\) and \(\langle r^4 \rangle_{\rm NR} \equiv \int d^3r r^4 \rho_{\rm NR}(r) = 60 G_E''(0)\).

## Context

This work extends the earlier discussions of Eides (2000, 2007, 2014) on the role of the proton radius in hydrogen spectroscopy and unifies them with the lepton-scattering literature. It directly contradicts the textbook interpretation originating from Hofstadter (1956, 1958) and Sachs (1962) that \(G_E\) is the Fourier transform of a three-dimensional charge density, showing that derivation to be flawed. The paper enables a correct relativistic moment expansion (RME) for form factors and connects the two-dimensional charge density to the modern GPD framework used at facilities like a future electron-ion collider.
