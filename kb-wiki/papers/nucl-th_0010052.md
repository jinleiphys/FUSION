# Fine-tuning the basic forces of nature through the triple-alpha process in red giant stars

> We show that the synthesis of carbon and oxygen through the triple-alpha process in red giant stars is extremely sensitive to the fine details of the nucleon-nucleon (N-N) interaction. A +/-0.5% change in the strength of the N-N force would reduce either the carbon or oxygen abundance by as much as a factor of 30-1000. This result may be used to constrain some fundamental parameters of the Standard Model.

# Fine-tuning the basic forces of nature through the triple-alpha process in red giant stars

## Key claim

A ±0.5% change in the strength of the nucleon-nucleon (N-N) force would reduce either the carbon or oxygen abundance by a factor of 30–1000, but the exact magnitude of the effect depends on which effective N-N force model (MHN, MN, V1, or V2) is used, and the true behavior is expected to lie between the predictions of the MHN and MN forces.

## Method

The authors used a cluster-model description of the 0⁺₂ state of ¹²C and performed calculations with four different N-N forces (MHN, MN, V1, V2), each tuned to reproduce the experimental resonance energy of 380 keV relative to the 3α threshold. The strength of each force was multiplied by a factor p ranging from 0.996 to 1.004, and the resulting changes in the resonance energy were computed. These modified resonance energies were then used to calculate triple-alpha reaction rates, which were input into stellar models for stars of 1.3, 5, and 20 solar masses to determine the resulting carbon and oxygen abundances.

## Key numbers

- A ±0.5% change in N-N force strength reduces carbon or oxygen abundance by a factor of 30–1000.
- The resonance energy of the 0⁺₂ state of ¹²C lies at 380 keV relative to the 3α threshold.
- The strength parameter p was varied from 0.996 to 1.004 (a ±0.4% range).
- Stellar models were computed for stars with masses of 1.3, 5, and 20 solar masses (M⊙).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of fine-tuning in nuclear astrophysics by quantifying how the triple-alpha process, first identified by Hoyle (1954) as requiring a resonant state in ¹²C, depends sensitively on the N-N interaction. The authors build on their own previous cluster-model calculations and use stellar evolution models to translate changes in reaction rates into observable abundance changes. The result enables constraints on fundamental parameters of the Standard Model, such as the sum of light quark masses and the Higgs vacuum expectation value, at the 1% level, as discussed in related work on quark mass fine-tuning.
