# Hydrodynamical modeling of the deconfinement phase transition and explosive hadronization

> Dynamics of relativistic heavy-ion collisions is investigated on the basis of a simple (1+1)-dimensional hydrodynamical model in light-cone coordinates. The main emphasis is put on studying sensitivity of the dynamics and observables to the equation of state and initial conditions. Low sensitivity of pion rapidity spectra to the presence of the phase transition is demonstrated, and some inconsistencies of the equilibrium scenario are pointed out. Possible non-equilibrium effects are discussed, in particular, a possibility of an explosive disintegration of the deconfined phase into quark-gluon droplets. Simple estimates show that the characteristic droplet size should decrease with increasing the collective expansion rate. These droplets will hadronize individually by emitting hadrons from the surface. This scenario should reveal itself by strong non-statistical fluctuations of observables.

## Key claim

The paper argues that equilibrium hydrodynamics is not sensitive to the presence of a deconfinement phase transition when the initial state lies far above the transition point in energy density, and that an explosive, non-equilibrium hadronization scenario involving quark-gluon droplet formation may be necessary to resolve discrepancies with experimental emission times.

## Method

The author applies a (1+1)-dimensional hydrodynamical model in light-cone coordinates to central Au+Au collisions at RHIC energy, using a bag-like equation of state with three phases (hadronic, mixed, quark-gluon) and comparing results to a pure hadronic EOS. The model uses initial energy density profiles parameterized as flattened Gaussian distributions with total energy E ≈ 26 TeV, corresponding to Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV. In the second part, the author derives an optimal droplet size from an energy balance argument between collective kinetic energy and surface energy, and discusses the resulting multiplicity fluctuations.

## Key numbers

- Total energy of secondaries: E ≈ 26 TeV, as estimated by the BRAHMS collaboration for Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV.
- Freeze-out temperature giving best fit to BRAHMS data: T_F = 130 MeV.
- Expansion time at η=0 for freeze-out at T_F = 130 MeV: about 60 fm/c.
- Optimal droplet size R* = 3.4 fm for H⁻¹ = 20 fm/c (slow expansion) and R* = 1.5 fm for H⁻¹ = 6 fm/c (fast expansion), using γ = 10 MeV/fm² and Δℰ = 0.5 GeV/fm³.
- Optimal droplet mass M* ≈ 100 GeV for slow expansion and ≈ 10 GeV for fast expansion.
- Width of pion bump in rapidity from a single droplet: δη ≈ 0.8 at T ∼ 100 MeV.
- Characteristic angular spreading of pions from a single droplet: δφ ≈ 1.

## Context

This work extends the Landau (1953) and Bjorken (1983) hydrodynamical models by studying sensitivity to the equation of state and phase transitions, using a parameterization from Teaney (2001). The explosive hadronization scenario was first formulated in Mishustin (1999) and builds on spinodal decomposition studies by Csernai (1995), Scavenius (2001), and Randrup (2004). The paper points out inconsistencies between the long freeze-out times predicted by equilibrium hydrodynamics (∼60 fm/c) and interferometric measurements by Adler (2001) showing emission times of order 10 fm/c, motivating the non-equilibrium droplet scenario. The droplet evolution was studied numerically in Mishustin (2001) and Dumitru (2003) using Chiral Fluid Dynamics. The paper concludes that better conditions for observing the deconfinement phase transition may occur at lower energies with higher baryon density, such as at the future FAIR facility.
