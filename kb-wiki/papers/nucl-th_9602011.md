# Relativistic Hydrodynamics for Heavy--Ion Collisions: Freeze--Out and Particle Spectra

> We investigate freeze--out in hydrodynamic models for relativistic heavy--ion collisions. In particular, instantaneous freeze--out across a hypersurface of constant temperature (``isothermal'' freeze--out) is compared with that across a hypersurface at constant time in the center-of-momentum frame (``isochronous'' freeze--out). For one--dimensional (longitudinal) expansion the rapidity distributions are shown to differ significantly in the two scenarios, while the transverse momentum spectra are remarkably similar. We also investigate the rapidity distribution in greater detail and show that the Gaussian-like shape of this distribution commonly associated with the Landau expansion model in general emerges only if one neglects contributions from time-like parts of the isothermal freeze--out hypersurface.

## Key claim
The Gaussian-like rapidity distribution commonly associated with the Landau expansion model emerges only if one neglects contributions from time-like parts of the isothermal freeze-out hypersurface; a consistent calculation that includes these contributions produces double- or triple-humped distributions.

## Method
The authors performed one-dimensional (longitudinal) relativistic hydrodynamic simulations of a finite slab of matter with an ultrarelativistic ideal gas equation of state (pion gas, sound velocity c_s = 1/√3), using the SHASTA algorithm. They compared two instantaneous freeze-out scenarios: isothermal freeze-out across a hypersurface of constant temperature T_f, and isochronous freeze-out across a hypersurface of constant center-of-momentum time t_f, calculating single-inclusive rapidity and transverse momentum spectra via the Cooper-Frye formula.

## Context
This work extends the Landau expansion model (Landau, 1953) by systematically comparing isothermal and isochronous freeze-out scenarios used in relativistic heavy-ion hydrodynamics. It contradicts the common assumption that the Landau model produces Gaussian rapidity distributions (Srivastava et al., 1990s), showing that such shapes arise only when time-like hypersurface contributions are neglected, which also violates conservation laws. The results enable more realistic three-dimensional calculations using isothermal freeze-out, as already employed by Ornik et al. (1990s), and motivate future self-consistent treatments of freeze-out that account for finite space-time extent and feedback on the fluid evolution.
