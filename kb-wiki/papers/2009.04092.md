# Rodeo Algorithm for Quantum Computing

> We present a stochastic quantum computing algorithm that can prepare any eigenvector of a quantum Hamiltonian within a selected energy interval $[E-ε, E+ε]$. In order to reduce the spectral weight of all other eigenvectors by a suppression factor $δ$, the required computational effort scales as $O[|\log δ|/(p ε)]$, where $p$ is the squared overlap of the initial state with the target eigenvector. The method, which we call the rodeo algorithm, uses auxiliary qubits to control the time evolution of the Hamiltonian minus some tunable parameter $E$. With each auxiliary qubit measurement, the amplitudes of the eigenvectors are multiplied by a stochastic factor that depends on the proximity of their energy to $E$. In this manner, we converge to the target eigenvector with exponential accuracy in the number of measurements. In addition to preparing eigenvectors, the method can also compute the full spectrum of the Hamiltonian. We illustrate the performance with several examples. For energy eigenvalue determination with error $ε$, the computational scaling is $O[(\log ε)^2/(p ε)]$. For eigenstate preparation, the computational scaling is $O(\log Δ/p)$, where $Δ$ is the magnitude of the orthogonal component of the residual vector. The speed for eigenstate preparation is exponentially faster than that for phase estimation or adiabatic evolution.

# Rodeo Algorithm for Quantum Computing

## Key claim

The rodeo algorithm can prepare any eigenvector of a quantum Hamiltonian and determine its eigenvalue with exponential convergence in the number of measurement cycles, but its efficiency depends critically on the squared overlap probability *p* of the initial state with the target eigenvector; the method achieves exponential speedup over phase estimation and adiabatic evolution for eigenstate preparation only in the limit where the residual orthogonal component Δ approaches zero.

## Method

The algorithm uses auxiliary (ancilla) qubits, each initialized in the |1⟩ state, to control the time evolution of the object Hamiltonian H_obj minus a tunable parameter E. For each cycle n, the ancilla qubit undergoes a Hadamard gate, a controlled time evolution of H_obj for time t_n, a phase rotation P(E t_n), another Hadamard gate, and then measurement; the times t_n are drawn from a Gaussian random distribution with root-mean-square value t_RMS. The probability of measuring all ancilla qubits in the |1⟩ state is the product over cycles of cos²[(E_obj − E) t_n / 2], which acts as an energy filter that suppresses eigenvectors with energy E_obj ≠ E by a factor of 1/4^N for large N when t_RMS exceeds twice the inverse energy level spacing.

## Key numbers

- Computational effort to suppress spectral weight of undesired eigenvectors by factor δ scales as O[|log δ| / (p ε)], where p is the squared overlap of the initial state with the target eigenvector and ε is the energy resolution.
- For energy eigenvalue determination with error ε, computational scaling is O[(log ε)² / (p ε)].
- For eigenstate preparation with residual orthogonal component magnitude Δ, computational scaling is O(log Δ / p).
- The geometric mean of cos²θ when sampled uniformly over all θ equals 1/4, giving a spectral suppression factor of 1/4^N for large N.
- For the Heisenberg spin chain with 10 sites, J=1, and h=3, the rodeo algorithm with N=9 cycles and t_RMS=5 achieved overlap probability 0.997 with the target eigenvector at E_j = −18.1; preconditioning with adiabatic evolution for t_AE=5 increased the overlap to 0.99999.
- For the Anderson localization model on 100 sites with diagonal disorder root-mean-square value 1/2, the rodeo algorithm with N=9 cycles and t_RMS=10 reproduced the exact initial-state spectral function.

## Context

This work introduces a stochastic quantum computing algorithm that is opposite in strategy to quantum adiabatic evolution (Farhi 2000, Farhi 2001), which requires extended time evolution that becomes prohibitive for large systems. The rodeo algorithm is similar in character to the projected cooling algorithm (Lee 2019, Gustafson 2020) but can be applied to any quantum Hamiltonian and achieves exponential convergence recursively. It differs from Kitaev's iterative quantum phase estimation (Kitaev 1995) and fixed-time energy band filtering methods (Ge 2017, Lu 2020) in that it can efficiently prepare individual eigenstates of a general quantum Hamiltonian; the speed for eigenstate preparation is exponentially faster than both phase estimation and adiabatic evolution in the limit Δ → 0. The method enables preparation of energy eigenstates for measuring expectation values, transition matrix elements, and linear response functions, with potential applications to strongly-correlated electrons, nuclear structure, and lattice quantum chromodynamics.
