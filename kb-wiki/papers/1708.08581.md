# New Results on Short-Range Correlations in Nuclei

> Nuclear dynamics at short distances is one of the most fascinating topics of strong interaction physics. The physics of it is closely related to the understanding the role of the QCD in generating nuclear forces at short distances as well as understanding the dynamics of the super-dense cold nuclear matter relevant to the interior of neutron stars. With an emergence of high energy electron and proton beams there is a significant recent progress in high energy nuclear scattering experiments aimed at studies of short-range structure of nuclei. This in turn stimulated new theoretical studies resulting in the observation of several new phenomena specific to the short range structure of nuclei. In this work we review recent theoretical and experimental progress in studies of short-range correlations in nuclei and their importance for advancing our understanding of the dynamics of nuclear interactions at small distances.

# New Results on Short-Range Correlations in Nuclei

## Key claim

Short-range correlations (SRCs) in nuclei are dominated by two-nucleon correlations, with higher-order (three-nucleon) correlations parametrically suppressed, provided the NN potential behaves as V(q) ~ 1/q^n with n > 1 at high momentum. The review asserts that the dominance of proton-neutron isosinglet pairs in the momentum range of approximately 250-650 MeV/c arises from the tensor part of the NN interaction, which does not couple to isotriplet states.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical and experimental progress in short-range correlation studies using high-energy electron and proton scattering experiments. The theoretical framework employs the Lippmann-Schwinger equation for A-nucleon bound systems to demonstrate the emergence of 2N SRCs through iterative solutions, showing that the high-momentum component of nuclear wave functions is dominated by two-nucleon correlations when the NN potential has finite range with n > 1. Experimental methodology involves inclusive A(e,e')X measurements, semi-inclusive triple-coincidence measurements, and analysis of cross-section ratios to extract SRC strengths and isospin composition.

## Key numbers

- The momentum range for probing 2N SRCs is approximately 250-650 MeV/c, corresponding to average inter-nucleon separations of approximately 1.1 fm
- The ratio of nuclear to deuteron cross sections, designated by parameter a2(A,Z), was measured for a wide range of A
- The exclusive d(e,e')p n cross section was measured at Q^2 = 3.5 GeV^2, with deuteron momentum distribution measured up to 550 MeV/c
- For gold (A=197), the fraction of high-momentum protons exceeds that of neutrons by as much as 50%, with Pp = 31% and Pn = 20%
- For carbon-12, both Pp and Pn are 20%; for iron-56, Pp = 27% and Pn = 23%; for aluminum-27, Pp = 24% and Pn = 22%
- The characteristic Fermi momentum kF is approximately 250 MeV/c
- The kinematic threshold for quasi-elastic scattering requires Q^2 > m_N^2, with momentum transfer q ~ pf >= few GeV/c and spectator momentum ps ~ few hundred MeV/c

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Jastrow (1955) on short-range correlations and the asymptotic solution of the N-body Schroedinger equation by Amado (1976), which established the relation n(p) ~ |V(p)|^2/p^4 for high-momentum nucleons. It builds upon inclusive measurements at SLAC by Rock (1982), Arnold (1988), and Day (1979) that first reached the high-momentum component of nuclear wave functions through y-scaling analysis, and upon the observation by Frankfurt (1993) of scaling in cross-section ratios at 1 < x < 2. The review synthesizes recent Jefferson Lab experiments with 6 GeV continuous electron beams and Brookhaven National Laboratory experiments with 6-15 GeV/c proton beams, which together confirmed the universal nature of SRCs and established the strong dominance of proton-neutron correlations. The findings enable further studies of the EMC effect correlation with SRC strength, symmetry energy in neutron-rich matter, and the nuclear contact formalism, with upcoming 12 GeV JLab experiments expected to probe more extreme kinematics.
