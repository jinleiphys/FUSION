# Relativistic mean field study of neutron rich even-even C and Be isotopes

> Ground state properties of neutron rich even-even Be and C nuclei have been investigated using Relativistic Mean Field approach in co-ordinate space. The positions of the neutron drip line are correctly predicted for both the elements. The nucleus $^{14}$Be shows a two neutron halo but, contrary to expectation, $^{22}$C does not exhibit any halo structure. In carbon nuclei, N=16 comes out as a new magic number. The single particle level ordering observed in stable nuclei is found to be modified in neutron rich Be isotopes. Elastic partial scattering cross sections for proton scattering in inverse kinematics have been calculated using the theoretically obtained densities for some of the nuclei and compared with available experimental data. The total cross cross sections for elastic scattering have also been calculated for all the nuclei studied showing a large increase for the halo nucleus $^{14}$Be. The nuclei have also been investigated for deformation. The nuclei $^{10}$Be and $^{16,18,20}$C are observed to be deformed in their ground state.

# Relativistic mean field study of neutron rich even-even C and Be isotopes

## Key claim
Using a coordinate-space Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) plus BCS approach with the NLSH force, the authors correctly predict the neutron drip line positions for Be and C isotopes and find that $^{14}$Be exhibits a two-neutron halo while $^{22}$C does not, contrary to prior expectation. The result for $^{22}$C depends on the fact that the last filled neutron level ($2s_{1/2}$) remains bound by more than 3 MeV, preventing the extended wave function needed for a halo.

## Method
The authors performed spherical and deformed RMF+BCS calculations in coordinate space (rather than harmonic oscillator basis) using the NLSH force, with pairing treated in the constant gap approximation (two variants: RMF-I with $\Delta_n=\Delta_p=0.2$ MeV, and RMF-II with gaps adjusted to match spherical RHB pairing energy). They compared results with spherical Relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations using the Gogny D1S interaction, and calculated elastic proton scattering cross sections at 55A MeV using the JLM optical model potential in local density approximation with the theoretical densities.

## Key numbers
- $^{14}$Be shows a two-neutron halo; $^{22}$C does not exhibit any halo structure.
- In carbon nuclei, the gap between the $2s_{1/2}$ and $1d_{3/2}$ neutron levels is nearly 5 MeV, supporting N=16 as a new magic number.
- For $^{12}$Be, binding energy predictions from NLSH and NL3 forces are 4.992 MeV and 5.086 MeV, respectively; for $^{22}$C, the corresponding values are 5.568 MeV and 5.662 MeV.
- In $^{14}$Be using NL3 force, the prolate solution gives neutron deformation $\beta_{2n}=0.16$ and mass deformation $\beta_2=0.11$, in contrast to the RHB calculation of Lalazissis et al. which predicted a mass deformation near 0.4.
- The last filled neutron level in $^{22}$C is bound by more than 3 MeV.
- Elastic proton scattering cross sections were calculated at beam energy 55A MeV for $^{12,14}$Be and $^{20,22}$C; total cross sections show a large increase for $^{14}$Be over $^{12}$Be, while the rise from $^{20}$C to $^{22}$C is gradual.
- $^{10}$Be and $^{16,18,20}$C are observed to be deformed in their ground state; $^{12}$Be, $^{14}$Be, $^{14}$C, and $^{22}$C are spherical.

## Context
This work extends prior RMF studies of Be and C isotopes by Patra, Ren et al., Gmuca, Sharma et al., and Sugahara et al., most of which used harmonic oscillator basis expansions. The authors note that basis expansion methods may fail for loosely bound halo states, motivating their coordinate-space approach. The results are compared with deformed RHB calculations by Lalazissis et al. (using NL3 force) and with spherical RHB calculations. The finding that N=16 is a new magic number in carbon isotopes supports earlier suggestions in the literature. The elastic scattering calculations provide predictions that can be tested by future inverse-kinematics experiments, particularly the total cross section difference between $^{14}$Be and $^{22}$C as a halo diagnostic.
