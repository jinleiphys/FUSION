# Nucleon-nucleon resonances at intermediate energies using a complex energy formalism

> We apply our method of complex scaling, valid for a general class of potentials, in a search for nucleon-nucleon S-matrix poles up to 2 GeV laboratory kinetic energy. We find that the realistic potentials JISP16, constructed from inverse scattering, and chiral field theory potentials N$^3$LO and N$^2$LO$_{opt}$ support resonances in energy regions well above their fit regions. In some cases these resonances have widths that are narrow when compared with the real part of the S-matrix pole.

## Key claim

The complex scaling method, applied to three realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials (JISP16, N³LO, and N²LO_opt), reveals S-matrix poles in energy regions well above the potentials' fit regions (above 300–350 MeV laboratory energy), with some resonances having narrow widths relative to their real parts; however, the authors state they are "not aiming at making predictions for the existence or absence of broad dibaryonic states" because the interactions model the short-range sector differently and are fitted at lower energies.

## Method

The authors apply the complex scaling (CS) transformation to the non-relativistic Hamiltonian consisting of relative kinetic energy and a realistic NN interaction, then solve the complex symmetric eigenvalue problem by diagonalization in a harmonic oscillator basis. Resonant states are identified using the θ-trajectory method, where the resonant state is the one that remains approximately invariant under variation of the complex scaling rotation angle θ, as established by the Aguilar-Balslev-Combes theorem. The method is applied to neutron-proton scattering channels up to L=4, searching for S-matrix poles up to 2 GeV laboratory kinetic energy.

## Context

This work extends the complex scaling method, previously demonstrated for local and schematic potentials, to realistic NN interactions (JISP16, N³LO, N²LO_opt) at intermediate energies. The results are compared with the SAID data analysis group (Arndt et al.) and the WASA-at-COSY collaboration (Adlarson et al.), which found resonance-like structures in the ¹D₂, ³F₃, ³P₂-³F₂, and ³D₃-³G₃ channels; the CS calculations additionally reveal resonances in the ³P₀ and ³P₁ channels not reported in data analyses. The demonstration of stability under SRG transformations supports the adoption of these techniques for investigating resonances in finite nuclei at low energies, where the authors anticipate future interest.
