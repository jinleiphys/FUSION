# An Overview of the MUSES Calculation Engine and How It Can Be Used to Describe Neutron Stars

> For densities beyond nuclear saturation, there is still a large uncertainty in the equations of state (EoS) of dense matter that translate into uncertainties in the internal structure of neutron stars. The MUSES Calculation Engine provides a free and open-source composable workflow management system, which allows users to calculate the EoS of dense and hot matter that can be used, e.g. to describe neutron stars. For this work, we make use of two MUSES EoS modules, Crust Density Functional Theory and Chiral Mean Field model, with beta-equilibrium with leptons enforced in the Lepton module, then connected by the Synthesis module using different functions: hyperbolic tangent, Gaussian, bump, and smoothstep. We then calculate stellar structure using the QLIMR module and discuss how the different interpolating functions affect our results.

## Key claim

The MUSES Calculation Engine can produce unified neutron-star equations of state by matching a crust model (Crust-DFT) with a core model (Chiral Mean Field) using different interpolation functions, but the specific choice of interpolation function and thermodynamic variable introduces only modest uncertainty in macroscopic observables provided the matching occurs above the crust-core transition.

## Method

The authors used two MUSES equation-of-state modules, Crust Density Functional Theory for the crust and Chiral Mean Field model for the core, enforced beta-equilibrium with leptons via the Lepton module, then connected them using the Synthesis module with four different interpolation functions: hyperbolic tangent, Gaussian, bump, and smoothstep. They performed the interpolation over four different thermodynamic variable pairs: energy density as a function of baryon density, pressure as a function of density, pressure as a function of baryon chemical potential, and speed of sound squared as a function of density. Stellar structure was then calculated using the QLIMR module to solve the TOV equations for static neutron stars.

## Key numbers

- The interpolation endpoints were set at baryon densities of 0.065 fm$^{-3}$ and 0.2 fm$^{-3}$ (narrow) or 0.3 fm$^{-3}$ (broad) for the $\varepsilon(n_B)$ matching
- The canonical 1.4 $M_\odot$ star radius varied between 13 and 14 km across all stable interpolated equations of state; excluding bump functions in the $P(\mu_B)$ case, the range narrowed to 13.5–13.8 km
- The inclusion of the crust increased the radius by approximately 500–800 m compared to the original CMF curve
- The pressure difference between the two original models in the $P(\mu_B)$ interpolation was less than 5 MeV fm$^{-3}$ in magnitude

## Context

This work extends the previous analysis by ReinkePelicer (2025) that used only hyperbolic tangent matching, by systematically comparing four interpolation functions (hyperbolic tangent, Gaussian, bump, and smoothstep) and four thermodynamic variable choices. The study builds on prior work connecting hadron and quark equations of state through sigmoid functions by Kambe (2016) and Wang (2019), logistic functions by Du (2018), and bump-like functions by Albright (2015) and PhysRevC.106.014909. The results enable the MUSES framework to provide unified neutron-star equations of state with quantified systematic uncertainties from the matching procedure, allowing comparison to astrophysical constraints from gravitational-wave observations and mass-radius measurements.
