# Ab initio calculations of the isotopic dependence of nuclear clustering

> Nuclear clustering describes the appearance of structures resembling smaller nuclei such as alpha particles (4He nuclei) within the interior of a larger nucleus. While clustering is important for several well-known examples, much remains to be discovered about the general nature of clustering in nuclei. In this letter we present lattice Monte Carlo calculations based on chiral effective field theory for the ground states of helium, beryllium, carbon, and oxygen isotopes. By computing model-independent measures that probe three- and four-nucleon correlations at short distances, we determine the shape of the alpha clusters and the entanglement of nucleons comprising each alpha cluster with the outside medium. We also introduce a new computational approach called the pinhole algorithm, which solves a long-standing deficiency of auxiliary-field Monte Carlo simulations in computing density correlations relative to the center of mass. We use the pinhole algorithm to determine the proton and neutron density distributions and the geometry of cluster correlations in 12C, 14C, and 16C. The structural similarities among the carbon isotopes suggest that 14C and 16C have excitations analogous to the well-known Hoyle state resonance in 12C.

# Ab initio calculations of the isotopic dependence of nuclear clustering

## Key claim
Using lattice Monte Carlo simulations based on chiral effective field theory at leading order with only three free parameters, the authors find that alpha clusters in neutron-rich isotopes swell as the neutron excess increases, and that the structural similarities among carbon isotopes suggest 14C and 16C may have excitations analogous to the Hoyle state in 12C. The claim is hedged by the leading-order nature of the calculation and the expectation that higher-order corrections will systematically improve the results.

## Method
The authors performed auxiliary-field Monte Carlo simulations on a spatial lattice with spacing 1.97 fm and time spacing 1.97 fm/c, using a new leading-order lattice action that preserves Wigner's SU(4) spin-isospin symmetry to suppress Monte Carlo sign oscillations. They computed short-distance three-nucleon and four-nucleon correlation operators (rho_3 and rho_4) as model-independent probes of alpha clustering, and introduced the "pinhole algorithm" to compute density distributions relative to the center of mass. The ground-state energies of hydrogen, helium, beryllium, carbon, and oxygen isotopes were calculated and compared to experimental data.

## Key numbers
- Ground-state binding energies of hydrogen, helium, beryllium, carbon, and oxygen isotopes reproduced to an accuracy of 0.7 MeV per nucleon or better at leading order.
- Spatial lattice spacing: 1.97 fm; time lattice spacing: 1.97 fm/c.
- For 8Be, the rho_3-entanglement delta^{rho_3}_alpha is about 0.18; for 14Be it rises to about 0.34.
- For 12C, delta^{rho_3}_alpha is about 0.28; it rises to a maximum of about 0.50 near the neutron drip line.
- For 16O, delta^{rho_3}_alpha is about 0.50; it increases with neutron number up to 0.73.
- The Gaussian model for spin-up proton distributions used a root-mean-square radius of 2.6 fm (charge radius of 14C) and a minimum separation of 1.7 fm (charge radius of 4He).
- Proton density distributions were convolved with a Gaussian of root-mean-square radius 0.84 fm (proton charge radius).

## Context
This work extends previous lattice calculations of alpha clustering in 12C and 16O by Epelbaum et al. (2011-2013) to a broader range of isotopes, solving the sign oscillation problem that had limited such studies to nuclei with equal proton and neutron numbers. The pinhole algorithm addresses a long-standing deficiency of auxiliary-field Monte Carlo methods in computing center-of-mass density correlations. The results enable future studies of cluster states in neutron-rich carbon isotopes, particularly the bound 0+2 state in 14C at 6.59 MeV above ground state as a possible analog to the Hoyle state resonance in 12C at 7.65 MeV, and open the door to systematic higher-order chiral effective field theory calculations of clustering phenomena.
