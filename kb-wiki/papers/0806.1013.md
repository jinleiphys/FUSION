# Heavy flavor in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and RHIC II

> In the initial years of operation, experiments at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) have identified a new form of matter formed in nuclei-nuclei collisions at energy densities more than 100 times that of a cold atomic nucleus. Measurements and comparison with relativistic hydrodynamic models indicate that the matter thermalizes in an unexpectedly short time, has an energy density at least 15 times larger than needed for color deconfinement, has a temperature about twice the critical temperature predicted by lattice QCD, and appears to exhibit collective motion with ideal hydrodynamic properties - a "perfect liquid" that appears to flow with a near-zero viscosity to entropy ratio - lower than any previously observed fluid and perhaps close to a universal lower bound. However, a fundamental understanding of the medium seen in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC does not yet exist. The most important scientific challenge for the field in the next decade is the quantitative exploration of the new state of nuclear matter. That will require new data that will, in turn, require enhanced capabilities of the RHIC detectors and accelerator. In this report we discuss the scientific opportunities for an upgraded RHIC facility - RHIC II - in conjunction with improved capabilities of the two large RHIC detectors, PHENIX and STAR. We focus solely on heavy flavor probes. Their production rates are calculable using the well-established techniques of perturbative QCD and their sizable interactions with the hot QCD medium provide unique and sensitive measurements of its crucial properties making them one of the key diagnostic tools available to us.

## Key claim

Heavy flavor probes (charm and bottom quarks, quarkonia) are uniquely sensitive tools for quantitatively exploring the strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma (sQGP) formed at RHIC, but a fundamental understanding of this medium does not yet exist and will require upgraded detector and accelerator capabilities (RHIC II) to achieve the necessary precision.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical and experimental status of heavy flavor production in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC, focusing on open heavy flavor (charm and bottom hadrons) and quarkonium states (J/ψ, ψ′, χc, and the Υ family). It evaluates how measurements of nuclear modification factors (RAA), elliptic flow (v2), and transverse momentum distributions from semileptonic decays and hadronic channels can probe medium properties such as energy loss, thermalization, color screening, and coalescence. The authors project the yields achievable with the proposed RHIC II luminosity upgrade (factor of 5 increase for Au+Au) combined with detector upgrades including silicon vertex detectors for displaced vertex measurements.

## Key numbers

- Energy density of the medium formed at RHIC is at least 15 times larger than needed for color deconfinement, with temperature about twice the critical temperature predicted by lattice QCD
- Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV produce approximately 0.05 b-bbar pairs per central collision
- The charm cross section at forward rapidity (y=1.65) measured via single muons is dσ/dy = 0.145 ± 1.1% +42.7%/-49.8% μb
- The crossover point at which bottom decays become dominant over charm in the single electron spectrum at midrapidity is between 3 and 9 GeV/c; STAR and PHENIX measurements indicate bottom becomes dominant at pT ∼ 3.5 GeV/c
- The RHIC II accelerator upgrade will provide a factor of 5 increase in luminosity over present capabilities for Au+Au collisions
- The PHENIX Silicon Vertex Detector barrel will have a displaced vertex resolution of ∼ 50 μm; the endcap resolution is ∼ 90-115 μm
- The c-cbar and b-bbar cross sections at LHC increase by factors of 15 and 100 respectively compared to RHIC; J/ψ and Υ cross sections increase by factors of 13 and 55 respectively

## Context

This report extends the experimental program from the initial RHIC runs (2000-2008) by BRAHMS, PHENIX, PHOBOS, and STAR, which established the existence of a strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma with near-perfect liquid properties. It builds on theoretical work on heavy quark energy loss by Djordjevic, Gyulassy, and Vitev (2004) and Dokshitzer and Kharzeev (2001), as well as J/ψ suppression models from Matsui and Satz (1986) and coalescence models by Thews, Greco, and Rapp (2000-2003). The proposed RHIC II program enables quantitative tests of color screening, heavy quark thermalization, and coalescence mechanisms that cannot be resolved with existing data, and complements the LHC heavy-ion program where higher initial temperatures but also significant coalescence contributions to bottomonium are expected.
