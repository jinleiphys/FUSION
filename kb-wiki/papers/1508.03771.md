# Making a soft relativistic mean-field equation of state stiffer at high density

> We study relativistic mean-field (RMF) models including nucleons interacting with scalar, vector and iso-vector mean fields and self- and cross- mean-field interaction terms. Usually, in such a models the magnitude of the scalar field increases monotonically with the nucleon density, and the nucleon effective mass decreases. We demonstrate that the latter quantity stops to decrease and the equation of state stiffens, provided the mean-field self-interaction potential rises sharply in a narrow vicinity of the values of mean fields corresponding to nucleon densities $n> n_{*}>n_0$, where $n_0$ is the nuclear saturation density. As the result the limiting neutron star mass increases. This procedure offers a simple way to stiffen the equation of state at densities above $n_{*}$ without altering it at densities $n\le n_{0}$. The developed scheme allows an application to neutron stars of the RMF models, which are well fitted to finite nuclei but do not fulfill the experimental constraint on the limiting neutron star mass. The exemplary application of the method to the well-known FSUGold model allows us to increase the limiting neutron star mass from $1.72~M_\odot$ to $M \geq 2.01~M_\odot$.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that adding a sharply rising term to the scalar-field self-interaction potential in relativistic mean-field models can quench the decrease of the nucleon effective mass at high densities, thereby stiffening the equation of state and increasing the maximum neutron star mass, without altering the equation of state at and below nuclear saturation density.

## Method
The authors modify the scalar-field self-interaction potential \(U(f)\) by adding a term \(\Delta U(f)\) that rises sharply in a narrow vicinity of a chosen scalar-field value \(f_{\rm s.core}\), corresponding to a nucleon density \(n_* > n_0\). They solve the mean-field equations of motion to obtain the density-dependent scalar field \(f(n)\) and the resulting equation of state, then compute neutron star masses and radii using the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation. The method is applied to a standard nonlinear Walecka model and to the FSUGold model, with the crust matched to the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland equation of state.

## Key numbers
- For the nonlinear Walecka model with saturation parameters \(n_0=0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\), \(\mathcal{E}_0=-16\) MeV, \(K(n_0)=250\) MeV, \(E_{\rm sym}=30\) MeV, and \(f_0=0.2\), the maximum neutron star mass increases from \(1.92\,M_\odot\) (original) to \(1.96\,M_\odot\), \(2.03\,M_\odot\), and \(2.12\,M_\odot\) for cut parameters \(c_\sigma=0.4\), \(0.3\), and \(0.2\), respectively.
- For the FSUGold model, the maximum neutron star mass increases from \(1.72\,M_\odot\) (original) to \(1.89\,M_\odot\), \(1.98\,M_\odot\), and \(2.09\,M_\odot\) for \(c_\sigma=0.4\), \(0.3\), and \(0.2\), respectively, with the \(c_\sigma=0.3\) case reaching \(M \geq 2.01\,M_\odot\).
- The nucleon effective mass flattens for densities \(n>n_*\) with \(n_* \simeq 1.9\,n_0\), \(3.0\,n_0\), and \(4.0\,n_0\) for \(c_\sigma=0.2\), \(0.3\), and \(0.4\), respectively.
- For the FSUGold model with \(c_\sigma=0.2\), the neutron star radius for \(M=1.4\,M_\odot\) increases from 12.0 km to 13.3 km.

## Context
This work extends the standard relativistic mean-field approach (Walecka, 1974; Serot and Walecka, 1986) by introducing a mechanism to stiffen the equation of state at high densities, addressing the inability of models like FSUGold (Todd-Rutel and Piekarewicz, 2005) to reproduce the observed neutron star mass of \((2.01\pm 0.04)\,M_\odot\) (Demorest et al., 2010; Antoniadis et al., 2013). The method provides an alternative to density-dependent coupling constants (Typel, 2005) or field-dependent parameters (Kolomeitsev et al., 2004) and can be applied to resolve the "hyperon puzzle" by increasing the maximum mass even when hyperons are included. The scheme enables existing RMF models fitted to finite nuclei to simultaneously satisfy neutron star mass constraints without refitting their low-density properties.
