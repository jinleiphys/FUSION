# Astrophysics with Compact Objects: An Indian Perspective, Present Status and Future Vision

> Astrophysical compact objects, viz., white dwarfs, neutron stars, and black holes, are the remnants of stellar deaths at the end of their life cycles. They are ideal testbeds for various fundamental physical processes under extreme conditions that are unique in nature. Observational radio astronomy with uGMRT and OORT facilities has led to several important breakthroughs in studies of different kinds of pulsars and their emission mechanisms. On the other hand, accretion processes around compact objects are at the core of Indian astronomy research. In this context, AstroSat mission revolutionized spectro-temporal observations and measurements of accretion phenomena, quasi-periodic oscillations, and jet behaviour in binary systems hosting compact objects. Moreover, recently launched XPoSat mission is set to provide an impetus to these high-energy phenomena around compact objects by enabling us to conduct polarization measurements in the X-ray band. Further, during the past decade, numerous gravitational wave signals have been observed from coalescing black holes and neutron stars in binary systems. Recent simultaneous observation of the GW170817 event in both gravitational waves and electromagnetic channels has ushered in the era of multi-messenger astronomy. In the future, synergistic efforts among several world-class observational facilities, e.g., LIGO-India, SKA, TMT, etc., within the Indian astrophysics community will provide a significant boost to achieve several key science goals that have been delineated here. In general, this article plans to highlight scientific projects being pursued across Indian institutions in this field, the scientific challenges that this community would be focusing on, and the opportunities in the coming decade. Finally, we have also mentioned the required resources, both in the form of infrastructural and human resources.

## Key claim

This white paper outlines the current state and future vision of Indian astrophysics research on compact objects (white dwarfs, neutron stars, black holes), claiming that synergistic efforts among upcoming facilities like LIGO-India, SKA, and TMT will enable key science goals, but the paper is a community roadmap rather than presenting new experimental results.

## Method

The paper is a review and strategic planning document authored by a large consortium of Indian astrophysicists. It surveys ongoing research programs across Indian institutions in the areas of accretion and jets, white dwarf binaries, neutron star structure and equation of state, pulsar timing, and multi-messenger astronomy. The authors identify open questions in each subfield and describe the observational facilities (uGMRT, AstroSat, XPoSat, NICER) and future projects (LIGO-India, SKA, TMT, ATHENA, eXTP) that will address them, along with required infrastructure and human resources.

## Key numbers

- GW170817: the first simultaneous observation of a binary neutron star merger in both gravitational waves and electromagnetic channels, which ushered in multi-messenger astronomy and constrained stiff equations of state
- AR Scorpii: a white dwarf pulsar with orbital period 3.56 hours and spin period 117 seconds, showing pulsed emission from radio to X-rays
- Super-Chandrasekhar white dwarfs: masses as high as 2.8 solar masses inferred from over-luminous Type Ia supernovae, potentially explained by strong magnetic fields
- GW190814: a gravitational wave event suggesting a compact object of mass 2.6 solar masses, which could be either a massive neutron star or a light black hole
- NICER observations of PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620: mass-radius estimates that challenge existing equation of state predictions, suggesting neutron star matter is less compressible than previously thought
- Approximately 3400 pulsars currently known, which is less than 5% of the predicted number of detectable radio pulsars
- Only 25 accreting millisecond X-ray pulsars (AMXPs) have been discovered so far

## Context

This paper extends the tradition of Indian astrophysics community roadmaps, building on decades of work with the uGMRT and AstroSat missions. It specifically references the recycling scenario for millisecond pulsar formation (Bhattacharya 1991) and the multi-messenger breakthrough of GW170817 (LIGO/Virgo collaboration 2017). The paper enables future coordinated efforts across Indian institutions to exploit upcoming facilities like LIGO-India, SKA, and TMT, and identifies open questions such as the equation of state of dense matter, the origin of quasi-periodic oscillations, and the nature of jets from white dwarfs.
