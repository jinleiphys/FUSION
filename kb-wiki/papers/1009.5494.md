# Coulomb gauge QCD as a tool for the excited spectrum

> A distinct feature of Coulomb gauge QCD is that it can be formulated in terms of physical, transverse gluons and quarks alone. The state-counting is then transparent, and the gauge is suited for studies of the excited spectrum. Leaving aside exotic spectroscopy, which has been the subject of other publications, in this note I call attention on two recent applications. One is that the running quark mass in the mid-infrared can be probed from excited baryons thanks to parity doubling, a consequence of insensitivity to chiral symmetry breaking. Fast quarks are asymptotically free and behave as massless, so hadrons containing fast quarks decouple from the condensate. Their (power-law) rate of decoupling reflects on the rate of decreasing parity splittings, which can be measured. The second is that, in analogy with the Franck-Condon principle of molecular physics, the velocity distribution of the heavy quarks inside a heavy hadron can be mapped out by the velocity distribution of its open-flavor decay products. This is exemplified by recent data from the Belle collaboration taken at the Upsilon(10860).

# Coulomb gauge QCD as a tool for the excited spectrum

## Key claim
Coulomb gauge QCD, formulated with only physical transverse gluons and quarks, can probe the running quark mass in the mid-infrared through parity doubling in excited baryons and can map the internal velocity distribution of heavy hadrons via open-flavor decay products, as exemplified by Belle data at the Upsilon(10860).

## Method
The author expands the QCD Hamiltonian in the weak sense, restricting it to the Hilbert space of highly excited resonances where the average quark momentum is large, and separates the parity-doublet-supporting term from the smaller term proportional to m(k)/k that splits positive and negative parity partners. For heavy hadrons, the author applies an analogy to the Franck-Condon principle of molecular physics, where the velocity distribution of heavy quarks inside a heavy hadron is mapped by the velocity distribution of its open-flavor decay products, using QCD string breaking as the decay mechanism that does not significantly alter quark momenta.

## Key numbers
- The splitting between positive and negative parity partners scales as |M⁺ − M⁻| ∝ j⁻ⁱ for Δ baryons of increasing angular momentum j, where the exponent i connects to the running quark mass as m(k) ∝ k⁻²ⁱ⁺².
- In the constituent quark model with constant quark mass (i=0), the splitting falls as |M⁺ − M⁻| ∝ j⁻¹.
- The Upsilon(10860) has six two-body decay channels open (B B-bar, B_s B_s-bar*, etc.), providing six points at which the momentum distribution of the parent hadron is probed.
- The momentum distribution of the B meson in Upsilon(10860) decays shows a clear dip corresponding to the first Sturm-Liouville zero of the Upsilon(5s) wavefunction.

## Context
This work extends the parity-doubling concept of Glozman (1999) and the chiral symmetry restoration ideas of Bicudo (2009) by turning the degeneracy into a probe of the running quark mass, using lattice data from Bowman (2006) for m(k) in Landau gauge. The heavy-hadron structure analysis builds on the Cornell model of Coulomb QCD and predicts the dip in Upsilon(10860) decays later observed by the Belle collaboration, as previously predicted by Llanes-Estrada (2008). The approach enables experimental extraction of the infrared running of the quark mass from excited baryon spectra and provides a tool to study internal hadron structure without explicit knowledge of boosted wavefunctions, which are notoriously difficult to compute in Coulomb gauge QCD due to the interaction-dependent boost operators.
