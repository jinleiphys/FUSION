# Comparison of Chemical Freeze-Out Criteria in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> One of the most remarkable results to emerge from heavy-ion collisions over the past two decades is the striking regularity shown by particle yields at all energies. This has led to several very successful proposals describing particle yields over a very wide range of beam energies, reaching from 1 A GeV up to 200 A GeV, using only one or two parameters. A systematic comparison of these proposals is presented here. The conditions of fixed energy per particle, baryon+anti-baryon density, normalized entropy density as well as percolation model are investigated. The results are compared with the most recent chemical freeze-out parameters obtained in the thermal-statistical analysis of particle yields. The sensitivity and dependence of the results on parameters is analyzed and discussed. It is shown that in the energy range above the top AGS energy, within present accuracies, all chemical freeze-out criteria give a fairly good description of the particle yields. However, the low energy heavy-ion data favor the constant energy per particle as a unified condition of chemical particle freeze-out. This condition also shows the weakest sensitivity on model assumptions and parameters.

## Key claim

The paper systematically compares four proposed chemical freeze-out criteria for heavy-ion collisions and finds that, while all criteria describe the data reasonably well at energies above the top AGS energy, the low-energy data favor the condition of fixed average energy per particle (⟨E⟩/⟨N⟩ ≈ 1.08 GeV) as a unified freeze-out condition, and this criterion also shows the weakest sensitivity to model assumptions and parameter variations.

## Method

The authors used a hadron resonance gas model to calculate thermodynamic quantities (energy density, particle density, baryon density, entropy density) as functions of temperature T and baryon chemical potential μB. They then compared the freeze-out curves predicted by four criteria, fixed energy per particle, fixed baryon+anti-baryon density (nB+nB̄ ≈ 0.12/fm³), fixed entropy density over T³ (s/T³ ≈ 7), and a percolation model condition, against the chemical freeze-out parameters extracted from thermal-statistical model fits to Au+Au and Pb+Pb collision data across beam energies from 1 A GeV to 200 A GeV. The sensitivity of each criterion to excluded volume corrections, resonance mass cut-off, and strangeness suppression was quantified.

## Key numbers

- The fixed energy per particle criterion uses ⟨E⟩/⟨N⟩ ≈ 1.08 GeV as the preferred value.
- The fixed baryon+anti-baryon density criterion uses nB+nB̄ ≈ 0.12 fm⁻³.
- The fixed entropy density criterion uses s/T³ ≈ 7.
- The percolation model uses a hadronic radius of approximately rh ≈ 0.8 fm.
- The polynomial fit to the freeze-out curve T(μB) = a − bμB² − cμB⁴ gives a = 0.166 ± 0.002 GeV, b = 0.139 ± 0.016 GeV⁻¹, c = 0.053 ± 0.021 GeV⁻³.
- The energy dependence of the baryon chemical potential is parameterized as μB(√s) = d/(1 + e√s) with d = 1.308 ± 0.028 GeV and e = 0.273 ± 0.008 GeV⁻¹.
- At LHC energies, the parameterization yields μB ≈ 1 MeV.
- At SIS energies (1 A GeV), the freeze-out temperature is approximately 52 ± 1.5 MeV with μB ≈ 822 MeV.
- At RHIC (√s = 200 A GeV), temperatures range from 163 ± 4 MeV to 177 ± 7 MeV with μB from 24 ± 4 MeV to 29 ± 6 MeV.
- Strangeness degrees of freedom contribute about 25% to the total particle density at SPS and RHIC energies.

## Context

This work extends the earlier proposal by Redlich et al. (2000) that fixed energy per particle is the chemical freeze-out criterion, and systematically compares it against alternative criteria proposed by Braun-Munzinger et al. (2001) (fixed baryon+anti-baryon density), Satz (2003) (percolation model), and Stankiewicz et al. (2003) and Tawfik et al. (2004) (fixed entropy density). The analysis enables future studies to use the ⟨E⟩/⟨N⟩ ≈ 1.08 GeV condition to predict particle excitation functions and freeze-out parameters at unexplored collision energies, and provides a dynamical interpretation linking this condition to the inelasticity line in heavy-ion collisions, as supported by UrQMD model studies.
