# Effect of bulk viscosity on Elliptic Flow near QCD phase transition

> Effects of the bulk viscosity on the elliptic flow are studied. To introduce a realistic equation of state and transport coefficients, we apply the results of the lattice QCD and hadron resonance gas calculations for these quantities. We found that the bulk viscosity acts in a non trivial manner on the elliptic flow $v_{2}$. The reduction of $v_{2}$ is more effective at low $p_{T}$ compared to the case of shear viscosity, whereas at high $p_{T}$, the situation is reversed, leading to $v_{2}$ enhancement. We argue that this is caused by the competition of the critical behaviors of the equation of state and the transport coefficients. We further found that Grad's method with the 14 moments approximation is not applicable to estimate the viscous effects for the one-particle distribution function at the freeze out.

# Effect of bulk viscosity on Elliptic Flow near QCD phase transition

## Key claim
The bulk viscosity reduces elliptic flow $v_{2}$ at low transverse momentum ($p_T < 1$ GeV) by up to 15%, but enhances $v_{2}$ at high $p_T$ ($>2$ GeV) when a realistic equation of state incorporating the QCD phase transition is used, an effect not seen with an ideal gas equation of state. The authors further argue that Grad's 14 moments approximation is inapplicable for estimating viscous corrections to the one-particle distribution function at freeze-out because the required bulk viscosity magnitude for validity ($\Pi < 10^{-4}$ fm$^{-4}$) is far smaller than values reached in realistic hydrodynamic simulations.

## Method
The authors performed 2+1 dimensional relativistic dissipative hydrodynamic simulations using the memory function method and smoothed particle hydrodynamics (SPH), incorporating three different equations of state: an ideal massless quark gas (EoS I) and two lattice QCD/hadron resonance gas hybrid equations of state with different interpolation widths near $T_c \approx 200$ MeV (EoS II and EoS III). The bulk viscosity coefficient $\zeta/s$ was parameterized using a fit to lattice QCD results from Karsch and hadron resonance gas results from Noronha, with a peak near $T_c$, and the relaxation time was parameterized as $\tau_R = \zeta/(\epsilon+p) b_\Pi$ with constant $b_\Pi$ values of 3, 5, or 6.

## Key numbers
- $v_2$ decreases by a factor of 15% at low $p_T$ ($<1$ GeV) when bulk viscosity with critical behavior near the QCD phase transition is included.
- For $p_T > 2$ GeV, $v_2$ surpasses the ideal hydrodynamic value when using EoS II with the critical $\zeta/s$ profile.
- The freeze-out temperature was set to 130 MeV for pions in the Cooper-Frye method.
- The 14 moments approximation requires $\Pi < 10^{-4}$ fm$^{-4}$ at freeze-out for pions (with $m=140$ MeV, $T=130$ MeV) to keep $\delta f$ smaller than $f_0$; in simulations, $\Pi$ reaches approximately $10^{-2}$ fm$^{-4}$.
- The ratio $\Pi/p$ at freeze-out in simulations is around 10%, while the 14 moments method requires at most 2% for pions in a multi-component fluid scenario.
- The impact parameter was chosen as $b=7$ fm for Au-Au collisions, with maximum initial energy density $\varepsilon_0 = 30$ GeV for central collisions.
- The bulk viscosity coefficient fit parameters include $\lambda_1 = \lambda_3 = 0.9$, $\lambda_2 = 0.25$, $\lambda_4 = 0.22$, with various $\sigma$ parameters and $A_1 = -13.77$, $A_2 = 27.55$, $A_3 = 13.45$.

## Context
This work extends prior studies of shear viscosity effects on elliptic flow by Romatschke, Heinz, Song, and others, and specifically builds on the bulk viscosity calculation by Heinz and Song (2008) which considered phase transition effects. The authors use lattice QCD results from Karsch and Aoki for the equation of state and bulk viscosity, and hadron resonance gas results from Noronha for the hadronic phase. The finding that bulk viscosity can enhance $v_2$ at high $p_T$ through an interplay between the sound velocity minimum and viscosity accumulation near $T_c$ is a novel result, though the authors caution that hydrodynamic applicability at such high $p_T$ is questionable. The work also identifies a fundamental limitation of Grad's 14 moments method for bulk viscous corrections, suggesting that future studies need alternative approaches for freeze-out distribution function corrections.
