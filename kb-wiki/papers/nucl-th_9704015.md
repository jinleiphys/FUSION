# Microscopic Enhancement of Heavy-Element Production

> Realistic fusion barriers are calculated in a macroscopic-microscopic model for several soft-fusion heavy-ion reactions leading to heavy and superheavy elements. The results obtained in such a realistic picture are very different from those obtained in a purely macroscopic model. For reactions on 208:Pb targets, shell effects in the entrance channel result in fusion-barrier energies at the touching point that are only a few MeV higher than the ground state for compound systems near Z = 110. The entrance-channel fragment-shell effects remain far inside the touching point, almost to configurations only slightly more elongated than the ground-state configuration, where the fusion barrier has risen to about 10 MeV above the ground-state energy. Calculated single-particle level diagrams show that few level crossings occur until the peak in the fusion barrier very close to the ground-state shape is reached, which indicates that dissipation is negligible until very late in the fusion process. Whereas the fission valley in a macroscopic picture is several tens of MeV lower in energy than is the fusion valley, we find in the macroscopic-microscopic picture that the fission valley is only about 5 MeV lower than the fusion valley for soft-fusion reactions leading to compound systems near Z = 110. These results show that no significant ``extra-extra-push'' energy is needed to bring the system inside the fission saddle point and that the typical reaction energies for maximum cross section in heavy-element synthesis correspond to only a few MeV above the maximum in the fusion barrier.

## Key claim

Shell effects in the entrance channel of soft-fusion reactions on lead targets lower the fusion barrier and persist far inside the touching point, so that no significant "extra-extra-push" energy is needed to bring the system inside the fission saddle point; the typical reaction energies for maximum cross section in heavy-element synthesis correspond to only a few MeV above the maximum in the fusion barrier, contrary to macroscopic-only models that predict several tens of MeV of extra energy.

## Method

The authors calculated fusion barriers using the macroscopic-microscopic finite-range droplet model (FRDM 1992) with a Yukawa-plus-exponential nuclear energy term and a folded-Yukawa single-particle potential for the microscopic shell correction via Strutinsky's method. They modeled the fusion shape sequence as intersecting spheres for asymmetric soft-fusion reactions on 208Pb targets, computing potential-energy surfaces and single-particle level diagrams for three reactions: 50Ti+208Pb, 68Zn+208Pb, and 70Zn+208Pb. The model was formulated so that the touching-sphere configuration yields the same energy whether treated as one highly deformed nucleus or as two separate nuclei with interactions.

## Key numbers

- For reactions on 208Pb targets leading to compound systems near Z=110, fusion-barrier energies at the touching point are only a few MeV higher than the ground state.
- At configurations slightly more elongated than the ground-state shape, the fusion barrier has risen to about 10 MeV above the ground-state energy.
- In the macroscopic-microscopic picture, the fission valley is only about 5 MeV lower than the fusion valley for soft-fusion reactions leading to compound systems near Z=110, compared to several tens of MeV lower in a macroscopic-only picture.
- For the reaction 70Zn+208Pb→278112, the magic-fragment gap combination 28+82=110 for protons persists up to about r/R0=1.15, where R0 is the radius of the spherical compound system.
- For the reaction 50Ti+208Pb→258Rf, mass asymmetry effects on the fission barrier can be up to 2 MeV at distortions larger than about r/R0=1.3.
- Multi-dimensional calculations in the Fm region suggest that exploring a more general parameterization may lower the maximum fusion barrier by 2 or 3 MeV in some cases.

## Context

This work extends the macroscopic-microscopic approach previously applied to symmetric fission of nuclei near 258Fm (Moller, Nix, and Swiatecki 1987, 1989) to asymmetric soft-fusion reactions on 208Pb targets. It contradicts the predictions of purely macroscopic multi-dimensional dynamical models (Sierk and Nix 1974; Nix and Sierk 1974; Moller and Nix 1976, 1977) and the one-dimensional Bass model (Bass 1973, 1974), which cannot explain the low optimum reaction energies observed in the synthesis of elements Z=107-112 (Munzenberg et al. 1981-1996; Hofmann et al. 1995-1996). The results enable a new quantitative framework for calculating 1n evaporation-residue cross sections in heavy-element synthesis, with the next steps requiring multi-dimensional calculations of the fusion/fission landscape and dynamical studies incorporating a microscopic dissipation model.
