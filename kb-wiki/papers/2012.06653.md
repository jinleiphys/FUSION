# Statistical tools for a better optical model

> Background: Modern statistical tools provide the ability to compare the information content of observables and provide a path to explore which experiments would be most useful to give insight into and constrain theoretical models. Purpose: In this work we study three such tools in the context of nuclear reactions with the goal of constraining the optical potential. Method: The three statistical tools examined are: i) the principal component analysis; ii) the sensitivity analysis based on derivatives; and iii) the Bayesian evidence. We first apply these tools to a toy-model case, comparing the form of the imaginary part of the optical potential. Then we consider two different reaction observables, elastic angular distributions and polarization data for reactions on 48Ca at two different beam energies. Results: For the toy-model case, we find significant discrimination power in the sensitivities and the Bayesian evidence, showing clearly that the volume imaginary term is more useful to describe scattering at higher energies. When comparing between elastic cross sections and polarization data using realistic optical models, sensitivity studies indicate that both observables are roughly equally sensitive but the variability of the optical model parameters is strongly angle dependent. The Bayesian evidence shows some variability between the two observables, but the Bayes factor obtained is not sufficient to discriminate between angular distributions and polarization. Conclusions: From the cases considered, we conclude that in general elastic scattering angular distributions have similar impact in constraining the optical potential parameters compared to the polarization data. The angular ranges for the optimum experimental constraints can vary significantly with the observable considered.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that sensitivity analysis based on derivatives and Bayesian evidence can discriminate between different forms of the optical potential (volume versus surface imaginary terms), but when comparing elastic scattering angular distributions to polarization data for constraining the optical model, the Bayes factor obtained is not sufficient to discriminate between the two observable types, and both observables have roughly equal impact on constraining the optical potential parameters.

## Method

The authors applied three statistical tools, principal component analysis, derivative-based sensitivity analysis, and Bayesian evidence, first to a toy model comparing volume and surface imaginary optical potentials for proton elastic scattering on 48Ca at 9 MeV and 65 MeV, then to realistic cases of elastic angular distributions and polarization (iT11) data for nucleon scattering on 48Ca and 208Pb at various beam energies. For the realistic cases, they performed Bayesian Markov Chain Monte Carlo simulations with 1,500,000 pulls from wide Gaussian priors, using mock data generated from the global optical potential of Koning and Delaroche (2003) with 10% error, and computed the Bayesian evidence via Monte Carlo integration over the full parameter space.

## Key numbers

- For the toy model at 9 MeV, the Bayesian evidence for the surface model was 1.06 × 10⁻³ and for the volume model was 0.65 × 10⁻³, giving a Bayes factor of 0.6 favoring the surface model
- For the toy model at 65 MeV, the Bayesian evidence for the surface model was 0.02 × 10⁻³ and for the volume model was 0.13 × 10⁻³, giving a Bayes factor of 6.9 favoring the volume model
- For 48Ca(p,p) at 12 MeV, the averaged Bayesian evidence was 1.039 × 10⁻³ for cross section data and 1.208 × 10⁻³ for polarization data, with a Bayes factor of 1.16
- For 48Ca(p,p) at 21 MeV, the averaged Bayesian evidence was 1.207 × 10⁻³ for cross section data and 0.602 × 10⁻³ for polarization data, with a Bayes factor of 0.50
- For 48Ca(n,n) at 12 MeV, the averaged Bayesian evidence was 0.833 × 10⁻³ for cross section data and 0.905 × 10⁻³ for polarization data, with a Bayes factor of 1.09
- For 208Pb(p,p) at 30 MeV, the averaged Bayesian evidence was 1.437 × 10⁻³ for cross section data and 0.403 × 10⁻³ for polarization data, with a Bayes factor of 0.28
- For 208Pb(p,p) at 61 MeV, the averaged Bayesian evidence was 0.051 × 10⁻³ for cross section data and 0.073 × 10⁻³ for polarization data, with a Bayes factor of 1.44

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous Bayesian uncertainty quantification studies on nuclear reactions (Lovell 2018, King 2019, Catacora-Rios 2019) by applying formal statistical tools, principal component analysis, sensitivity analysis, and Bayesian evidence, to compare the information content of different observables for constraining the optical potential. The study builds on prior work showing that Bayesian MCMC provides more reliable uncertainty estimates than standard chi-squared minimization (King 2019), and that including polarization data through iT11 could potentially reduce optical potential uncertainties (Catacora-Rios 2019). The results enable future systematic studies across a wider range of target nuclei and beam energies to identify which observables and angular ranges provide optimal constraints on phenomenological optical potentials.
