# Dynamical control of nuclear isomer depletion via electron vortex beams

> Long-lived excited states of atomic nuclei can act as energy traps. These states, known as nuclear isomers, can store a large amount of energy over long periods of time, with a very high energy-to-mass ratio. Under natural conditions, the trapped energy is only slowly released, limited by the long isomer lifetimes. Dynamical external control of nuclear state population has proven so far very challenging, despite ground-breaking incentives for a clean and efficient energy storage solution. Here, we describe a protocol to achieve the external control of the isomeric nuclear decay by using electrons whose wavefunction has been especially designed and reshaped on demand. Recombination of these electrons into the atomic shell around the isomer can lead to the controlled release of the stored nuclear energy. On the example of $^{93m}$Mo, we show that the use of tailored electron vortex beams increases the depletion by four orders of magnitude compared to the spontaneous nuclear decay of the isomer. Furthermore, specific orbitals can sustain an enhancement of the recombination cross section for vortex electron beams by as much as six orders of magnitude, providing a handle for manipulating the capture mechanism. These findings open new prospects for controlling the interplay between atomic and nuclear degrees of freedom, with potential energy-related and high-energy radiation sources applications.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that using specially designed electron vortex beams (rather than conventional plane-wave electrons) for nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC) can increase the depletion rate of nuclear isomers by several orders of magnitude, but only when the impact parameter of the beam relative to the ion is restricted to a finite region; without this restriction, the enhancement is limited to a factor of \(p/p_z\).

## Method

The authors calculate the NEEC resonance strength for vortex electron beams by expanding the continuum electron wave function into partial waves of definite angular momentum and mapping the vortex beam parameters (topological charge \(m\), transverse momentum modulus \(\zeta\), and impact parameter \(b\)) onto the partial-wave expansion. They compare the integrated resonance strength \(S_v\) for vortex beams to the plane-wave value \(S_p\) for two nuclear isomers: \(^{93m}\)Mo (with a 4.85 keV \(E2\) transition to a gateway state) and \(^{152m}\)Eu (with a 19.70 keV \(M1\) transition). The calculations use relativistic Coulomb-Dirac wave functions for continuum electrons and GRASP92 wave functions for bound electrons, with the impact parameter restricted to \(|{\bf{b}}| \leq b = 1/\zeta\).

## Key numbers

* For \(^{93m}\)Mo, the vortex beam NEEC resonance strength for recombination into the \(2p_{3/2}\) orbital is increased by two orders of magnitude compared to the plane-wave case (topological charge \(m=5\), \(\zeta = p_z\), impact parameter range \(\zeta b = 1\)).
* For \(^{93m}\)Mo, the enhancement ratio \(S_v/S_p\) for recombination into \(f\) orbitals (e.g., \(4f_{7/2}\)) reaches up to six orders of magnitude for vortex beams with \(m=5\).
* The NEEC reaction rate per ion for \(^{93m}\)Mo depletion using a vortex beam with \(m=5\) and recombination into \(2p_{3/2}\) is approximately \(1\) s\(^{-1}\), assuming a generic vortex beam flux of \(10^{24}\) cm\(^{-2}\) s\(^{-1}\) eV\(^{-1}\); this represents an enhancement of approximately four orders of magnitude compared to the spontaneous nuclear decay of the isomer (half-life 6.8 h).
* For \(^{152m}\)Eu, the vortex beam resonance strength for recombination into \(2p_{3/2}\) reaches \(7.61 \times 10^{-3}\) b eV for \(m=5\), compared to the plane-wave value of \(1.25 \times 10^{-5}\) b eV for the same orbital.
* For \(^{152m}\)Eu, the enhancement factor for vortex versus plane-wave NEEC ranges from 1.4 (for \(2s_{1/2}\) capture) to approximately 600 (for \(2p_{3/2}\) capture with \(m=5\)).

## Context

This work extends theoretical studies of NEEC, originally predicted by Goldanskii (1976) and later investigated by Cue (1989), Harston (1999), and Palffy (2006), by introducing electron vortex beams as a new degree of freedom to control the process. It directly addresses the ongoing debate following the first claimed experimental observation of NEEC by Chiara (2018) for \(^{93}\)Mo depletion, which has been contradicted by theoretical works such as Wu (2019) and Polasik (2021). The results enable experimental designs combining storage rings (as at GSI Darmstadt) with dynamically controllable vortex beam sources (using phase masks or plasmonic near fields) to achieve controlled nuclear energy release from isomers, and suggest broader applications for vortex beams in nuclear reaction channel selection and isotope production.
