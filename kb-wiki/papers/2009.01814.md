# The importance of charged particle reactions in the r-process on supernovae and neutron stars

> We propose a $(p,xn)$ mechanism with dynamic production as a new set of nuclear reactions that could produce high density neutrons and explain the r- and rp-elements. We calculate the rate of thorium and uranium produced by our proposed mechanism and show that it is compatible with different stellar conditions found in explosive events at an initial temperature of $T \geq 3\times 10^{9} K$ with a "freeze-out" by a neutrino-driven wind. We show that charged particle reactions could explain the discrepancies in the abundances of ${}^{232}Th$ and ${}^{235,238}U$ nucleochronometers. We extend the endpoint of the rapid proton (rp) process far beyond the previous work by showing that $(p,xn)$ reactions could contribute to the nucleosynthesis of heavy stable neutron deficient nuclides, like ${}^{190}Pt$, ${}^{184}Os$, ${}^{180}W$ and ${}^{174}$Hf. This implies in a broader definition of the rp-process and has important consequences for the nucleosynthesis of heavy elements. We show that we did not need to assume an extreme condition for the drip line of super neutron-rich nuclei.

## Key claim

The authors propose that $(p,xn)$ reactions on heavy elements (specifically lead and bismuth isotopes) can serve as a dynamic neutron source for the r-process, potentially unifying the r- and rp-processes under a single mechanism they call the $r^2p$-process, without requiring extreme assumptions about the neutron drip line of super neutron-rich nuclei.

## Method

The authors constructed a nuclear reaction network of approximately 350 isotopes from lead to uranium (mass range $201 \leq A \leq 250$), incorporating $(p,1-3n)$ reactions, neutron captures, and radioactive decays. They used the TALYS code to simulate proton and neutron cross-sections, experimental half-lives from the NNDC database where available, and the semi-empirical formula of Liu (2011) for unknown half-lives. They then varied the initial lead mass, proton flux, proton energy, and time duration to find conditions that could reproduce the required neutron densities for the r-process.

## Key numbers

- Neutron flux of $\sim 10^{25} \, \text{neutrons} \cdot s^{-1} \cdot cm^{-3}$ achieved in less than 1 s with $20 \, MeV$ protons, $10^9 \, g$ or $10^{12} \, g$ of lead, and proton fluxes of $10^{24}$ or $10^{23} \, \text{protons} \cdot s^{-1} \cdot cm^{-3}$ respectively
- Neutron flux of $\sim 10^{19} \, \text{neutrons} \cdot s^{-1} \cdot cm^{-3}$ achieved with $26 \, MeV$ protons, $10^{10} \, g$ lead, and proton flux of $10^{16} \, s^{-1} \cdot cm^{-3}$
- Neutron flux of $10^{10-15} \, s^{-1} \cdot cm^{-3}$ is sufficient to produce uranium and thorium via the proposed r-path
- $(p,xn)$ cross-sections are of order barns below $50 \, MeV$, giving approximately $5 \, b$ per neutron produced
- Proton fluxes of $10^{22-23} \, p \cdot s \cdot cm^{-3}$ are required to reproduce Solar System abundances of heavy p-nuclei via $(p,3n)$ reactions in one second
- With proton flux $\phi_p = 10^{28} \, cm^{-3} \cdot s^{-1}$ and $E_p = 30 \, MeV$, secular equilibrium for ${}^{232}Th$ and ${}^{235,236,238}U$ is reached in less than one second

## Context

This work extends the $\alpha$-process proposed by Woosley (1992) and the rp-process endpoint calculations of Schatz (2001), who found the rp-process should end in the SnSbTe cycle. The authors argue that previous models based on helium-driven neutron sources (Cowan 1983, 1985) or weak interactions ($p(e^-,\nu_e)n$) have limitations, and that charged-particle reactions have been neglected in r-process models despite being proposed in jet models (Wanajo 2003, Cameron 2003). The mechanism could potentially explain discrepancies in nucleochronometer abundances of ${}^{232}Th$ and ${}^{235,238}U$ and may unify the r-, i-, and rp-processes in neutrino-driven winds from supernovae and neutron star mergers.
