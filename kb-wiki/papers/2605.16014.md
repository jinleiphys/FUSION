# Advances in laser-assisted nuclear decay and nuclear excitation

> From the synthesis and evolution of the elements to the celestial nuclear processes of stellar explosions and neutron star mergers, nuclear physics is the foundation of our understanding of the universe. After more than a century of progress, the field of nuclear physics remains vibrant. The rapid advancement of laser technology has opened unprecedented avenues in nuclear physics, driven by the interdisciplinary convergence of laser physics, nuclear structure, plasma science, and quantum dynamics. This emerging field enables studies on laser-induced nuclear excitation, laser assisted nuclear decay, and precision manipulation of nuclear isomers for optical clocks. This review comprehensively examines the research achievements over the past decade regarding the influence of lasers on radioactive charged particle emissions and nuclear excitation. Regarding theoretical developments, the review details various methods used to evaluate the interactions between lasers and nuclei, including the time-dependent Schrödinger equation for $α$ decay, proton radioactivity, and two-proton radioactivity and Fermi's golden rule for nuclear excitation as well as the application and advancement of various theoretical models and approximation methods. In experimental research, the review synthesizes significant breakthroughs in laser induced nuclear excitation experiments, particularly emphasizing the excitation of the $^{229}$Th, $^{83}$Kr, and $^{45}$Sc. These achievements provide essential groundwork for future breakthroughs in both fundamental nuclear science and its broader technological applications.

## Key claim

This review comprehensively examines theoretical and experimental advances over the past decade in laser-assisted nuclear decay (particularly alpha decay, proton radioactivity, and two-proton radioactivity) and laser-induced nuclear excitation, but the authors explicitly limit their scope to charged-particle emissions dominated by strong interactions, excluding beta decay because it involves weak interaction processes with quark-level transformations that differ inherently from electromagnetic interactions with laser fields.

## Method

The review surveys theoretical frameworks based on the time-dependent Schrodinger equation for describing the relative motion of emitted charged particles and daughter nuclei under electromagnetic fields, using the Henneberger transformation to incorporate laser field effects through an effective potential. For nuclear excitation, the review covers Fermi's golden rule and various theoretical models and approximation methods. Experimentally, the review synthesizes results from major laser facilities including ELI-NP, Omega Laser Facility, Apollon Laser Facility, NIF, and SULF, focusing on laser-induced excitation of specific nuclei.

## Key numbers

* Laser intensity has increased eight orders of magnitude over the past three decades, from 10^15 W/cm^2 to 10^23 W/cm^2
* For relativistic-intensity lasers (I > 10^18 W/cm^2) with wavelength 1 micrometer, the electric field reaches approximately 10^12 V/m with corresponding magnetic fields at kilotesla levels
* The normalized laser field parameter a_0 = 0.85 x 10^(-9) lambda_0 [micrometer] sqrt(I [W/cm^2]), with a_0 >= 1 indicating the relativistic regime
* ELI-NP has demonstrated full-energy 10.2 PW pulses after compression and transport
* NIF achieved fusion ignition on December 5, 2022, producing 3.15 MJ of energy from 2.05 MJ laser energy delivered to target; on April 7, 2025, its best trial used 2.08 MJ laser energy to generate approximately 8.6 MJ
* The 229Th isomer has excitation energy 8.36 eV (taken from PhysRevLett.132.182501)
* 212Po has alpha decay half-life 294.4 ns with decay energy 8954.19 keV
* 8B has proton radioactivity half-life 771.9 ms with decay energy 136.4 keV
* 45Fe has two-proton radioactivity half-life 2.5 ms with decay energy 1800 keV

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework for laser-nucleus interactions previously developed for alpha decay (Condon, Gurney, and Gamow 1928) to proton radioactivity and two-proton radioactivity, while also covering laser-induced nuclear excitation. The work builds on the Chirped Pulse Amplification technology (Strickland and Mourou 1985) and the development of Ti:Al2O3 gain media (Moulton 1986) that enabled the high-intensity lasers now used at facilities like ELI-NP, NIF, and SULF. The review enables future work on nuclear optical clocks (particularly using 229Th with its 8.36 eV isomer), nuclear waste management through laser-modulated decay pathways, and precision manipulation of nuclear isomers.
