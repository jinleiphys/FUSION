# Calculation of nuclear matrix elements in neutrinoless double electron capture

> We compute nuclear matrix elements for neutrinoless double electron capture on $^{152}$Gd, $^{164}$Er and $^{180}$W nuclei. Recent precise mass measurements for these nuclei have shown a large resonance enhancement factor that makes them the most promising candidates for observing this decay mode. We use an advanced energy density functional method which includes beyond mean-field effects such as symmetry restoration and shape mixing. Our calculations reproduce experimental charge radii and $B(E2)$ values predicting a large deformation for all these nuclei. This fact reduces significantly the values of the NMEs leading to half-lives larger than $10^{29}$ years for the three candidates.

## Key claim

The nuclear matrix elements (NMEs) for neutrinoless double electron capture on 152Gd, 164Er, and 180W are strongly suppressed by the large prolate deformation of these nuclei, leading to half-lives longer than 10^29 years despite the large resonance enhancement factors reported in recent precision mass measurements.

## Method

The authors use an energy density functional (EDF) method with Gogny D1S and D1M interactions, incorporating beyond-mean-field effects including particle number and angular momentum projection (symmetry restoration) and shape mixing via the generator coordinate method (GCM) with axial quadrupole deformation as the generating coordinate. Initial and final nuclear states are constructed from Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) type mean-field wave functions projected onto good particle number and angular momentum, then mixed through the Hill-Wheeler-Griffin equations. The NMEs are computed as the sum of Fermi and Gamow-Teller parts, neglecting the tensor term, with neutrino potentials including nucleon finite size corrections, short-range correlations via the unitary correlation operator method, and higher order currents.

## Key numbers

* For 152Gd: NME = 1.07 (D1S) or 0.89 (D1M); minimum half-life = 4.2 x 10^29 years (D1S) or 6.2 x 10^29 years (D1M), assuming m_beta_beta = 50 meV.
* For 164Er: NME = 0.64 (D1S) or 0.50 (D1M); minimum half-life = 1.3 x 10^34 years (D1S) or 2.1 x 10^34 years (D1M), assuming m_beta_beta = 50 meV.
* For 180W: NME = 0.58 (D1S) or 0.38 (D1M); minimum half-life = 1.6 x 10^32 years (D1S) or 3.8 x 10^32 years (D1M), assuming m_beta_beta = 50 meV.
* The collective wave function maxima occur at beta_2 ~ 0.25 for 152Gd, beta_2 ~ 0.35 for 152Sm, beta_2 ~ 0.35 for A=164 isotopes, and beta_2 ~ 0.30 for A=180 isotopes.
* The NMEs from this EDF method are a factor of approximately 3-4 smaller than those from deformed QRPA calculations (Fang et al., 2011), which gave NME ranges of 3.23-2.67 for 152Gd, 2.64-2.27 for 164Er, and 2.05-1.79 for 180W.

## Context

This work extends the EDF method previously applied to neutrinoless double beta decay (Rodriguez and Martinez-Pinedo, 2010; 2011) to the case of neutrinoless double electron capture. It directly contradicts the spherical QRPA calculations (Fang et al., 2011) that were used to estimate half-lives from the recent precision mass measurements of 152Gd (2011), 164Er (2011), and 180W (2012), finding that the inclusion of self-consistent deformation and shape mixing reduces NMEs by a factor of 3-4 compared to those QRPA results. The calculations enable more reliable half-life predictions for the most promising 0nuECEC candidates and demonstrate that the deformation dependence of NMEs is correlated with pairing energy, as previously observed in 0nu beta beta decay of 150Nd (Rodriguez and Martinez-Pinedo, 2010).
