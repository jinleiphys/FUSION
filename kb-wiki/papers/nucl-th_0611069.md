# pT-fluctuations and multiparticle clusters in heavy-ion collisions

> We investigate the centrality dependence of the pT-correlations in the event-by-event analysis of relativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC made recently by the PHENIX and STAR Collaborations. We notice that sigma^2 dynamical scales to a very good accuracy with the inverse number of the produced particles, n. This scaling can be naturally explained by formation of clusters. We discuss the nature of clusters and provide numerical estimates of correlations coming from resonance decays, which are tiny, and a model where particle are emitted from local thermalized sources moving at the same collective velocity. This "lumped cluster" model can explain the data when on the average 6-15 particles are contained in a cluster. We also point out simple relations of the popular correlation measures to the covariance, which arise under the assumptions (well holding at RHIC) that the distributions are sharply peaked in n and that the dynamical compared to statistical fluctuations are small.

# pT-fluctuations and multiparticle clusters in heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

The dynamical variance sigma^2_dyn scales with the inverse number of produced particles (1/n) to within 2% accuracy in the PHENIX data at sqrt(s_NN)=130 GeV for centralities 0-30%, which is naturally explained by a cluster picture where particles are correlated only if they belong to the same cluster, with the caveat that this scaling holds only in the fiducial centrality range where the average transverse momentum and its variance are constant.

## Method

The authors analyzed the centrality dependence of event-by-event transverse momentum fluctuations measured by PHENIX and STAR at RHIC, computing sigma^2_dyn as the difference between the measured variance of the average transverse momentum and the mixed-event variance. They derived the relation between sigma^2_dyn and the covariance of particle pairs within events, then constructed a cluster model where particles are correlated only within clusters, with the number of correlated pairs scaling as the number of clusters times the number of pairs per cluster. They also performed thermal model calculations for resonance decays using the Cooper-Frye formalism and a "lumped cluster" model where particles are emitted from local thermalized sources moving at a common collective velocity.

## Key numbers

- sigma_dyn * sqrt(bar n) is approximately constant at 60.3 +/- 1.6 (0-5% centrality), 59.2 +/- 1.5 (0-10%), 59.8 +/- 1.2 (10-20%), and 57.7 +/- 1.1 (20-30%) for PHENIX at sqrt(s_NN)=130 GeV, verifying the 1/sqrt(bar n) scaling to 2% accuracy
- The covariance per correlated pair in the cluster model is estimated as cov* = 0.035 GeV^2 / r* for PHENIX at sqrt(s_NN)=130 GeV, where r* is the effective number of particles per cluster
- For the lumped cluster model with freeze-out temperature T=165 MeV and average transverse flow velocity 0.49, the covariance per pion pair is 0.0034 GeV^2, requiring r* = 10.3 to match the data
- For T=140 MeV with average transverse flow velocity 0.58, the covariance per pion pair is 0.0063 GeV^2, requiring r* = 5.6
- The estimated number of all particles (charged and neutral) per cluster is r*_all ~ 6-15
- Resonance decays produce covariances between 0.01 GeV^2 at low masses to -0.018 GeV^2 at M ~ 1.1 GeV, which are small compared to sigma_p^2 ~ 0.08 GeV^2
- For STAR data, cov* * r* = 0.058, 0.043, 0.035, and 0.014 GeV^2 for sqrt(s_NN)=200, 130, 62, and 20 GeV respectively

## Context

This work extends the analysis of event-by-event pT fluctuations measured by PHENIX (Adcox 2002, Adler 2003) and STAR (Adams 2003, 2005) at RHIC, as well as CERES (Appelshauser 1999). The cluster scaling observation provides a constraint on models of particle production, ruling out scenarios where all particles are correlated (which would give n-independent sigma_dyn) and showing that resonance decays alone (as in the thermal model of Broniowski 2001, 2002) produce negligible correlations. The lumped cluster model, previously applied to charge balance functions by Bozek (2003) and Cheng (2004), can explain the data with 6-15 particles per cluster. The results enable future microscopic modeling of the cluster formation mechanism, whether from mini-jets (Liu 2003, PHENIX Mitchell) or hydrodynamic lumps, and the authors urge experimentalists to compute the covariance directly as a function of multiplicity n.
