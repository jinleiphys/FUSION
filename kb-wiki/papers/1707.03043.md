# Accelerator Based Fusion Reactor

> A feasibility study of fusion reactors based on accelerators is carried out. We consider a novel scheme where a beam from the accelerator hits the target plasma on the resonance of the fusion reaction and establish characteristic criteria for a workable reactor. We consider the reactions $ d + t \rightarrow n + α, d + {}^3H_e \rightarrow p + α$, and $p + {}^{11}B \rightarrow 3 α$ in this study. The critical temperature of the plasma is determined from overcoming the stopping power of the beam with the fusion energy gain. The needed plasma lifetime is determined from the width of the resonance, the beam velocity and the plasma density. We estimate the critical beam flux by balancing the energy of fusion production against the plasma thermo-energy and the loss due to stopping power for the case of an inert plasma. The product of critical flux and plasma lifetime is independent of plasma density and has a weak dependence on temperature. Even though the critical temperatures for these reactions are lower than those for the thermonuclear reactors, the critical flux is in the range of $10^{22} - 10^{24}/\rm{cm^2/s}$ for the plasma density $ρ_t = 10^{15}/{\rm cm^3}$ in the case of an inert plasma. Several approaches to control the growth of the two-stream instability are discussed. We have also considered several scenarios for practical implementation which will require further studies. Finally, we consider the case where the injected beam at the resonance energy maintains the plasma temperature and prolongs its lifetime to reach a steady state. The equations for power balance and particle number conservation are given for this case.

# Accelerator Based Fusion Reactor

## Key claim

The paper proposes that an accelerator-based fusion reactor (ABFR) could be feasible by injecting a beam at the resonance energy of the fusion reaction into a plasma target, but only if the plasma temperature is raised sufficiently high that the stopping power of the beam in the plasma drops below the fusion energy gain; the critical beam flux required is in the range of $10^{22} - 10^{24}/\rm{cm^2/s}$ for a plasma density of $10^{15}/\rm{cm^3}$, which is several orders of magnitude higher than typical linear accelerator fluxes, and the two-stream instability presents a potentially insurmountable challenge for continuous beams.

## Method

The authors establish two criteria for a workable ABFR: first, the critical plasma temperature $T_c$ at which the ratio of fusion energy production to stopping power loss reaches unity, calculated from the stopping power scaling $|dE/dx|/\rho_e = a v T^{-3/2}$; second, the critical beam flux $\phi_c$ required to overcome both stopping power losses and the energy needed to produce and sustain the plasma. The plasma lifetime $\tau_{pla}$ is determined from the resonance width $\Gamma$, beam velocity, and stopping power, and the product $\phi_c \tau_{pla}$ is shown to be independent of plasma density. The analysis considers three reactions ($d+t$, $d+{}^3He$, $p+{}^{11}B$) for both low-density ($10^{15}/\rm{cm}^3$) and high-density ($10^{21}/\rm{cm}^3$) plasma scenarios, and discusses approaches to control two-stream and Weibel instabilities.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature $T_c = 1.8$ keV for $d+t$ reaction, compared to ITER target temperature of 12.5 keV (a factor of 6.6 lower)
- Critical temperature $T_c = 7.0$ keV for $d+{}^3He$ and $T_c = 31$ keV for $p+{}^{11}B$
- Maximum temperature $T_{max} = 17$ keV for $d+t$, 66 keV for $d+{}^3He$, and 223 keV for $p+{}^{11}B$
- For $\rho_t = 10^{15}/\rm{cm}^3$: effective plasma length $l_{eff} = 1.3 \times 10^7$ cm ($d+t$), $8.5 \times 10^7$ cm ($d+{}^3He$), $1.3 \times 10^8$ cm ($p+{}^{11}B$)
- For $\rho_t = 10^{15}/\rm{cm}^3$: plasma lifetime $\tau_{pla} = 4.0 \times 10^{-2}$ s ($d+t$), $1.3 \times 10^{-1}$ s ($d+{}^3He$), $1.2 \times 10^{-1}$ s ($p+{}^{11}B$)
- For $\rho_t = 10^{21}/\rm{cm}^3$: $l_{eff} = 13$ cm ($d+t$), 85 cm ($d+{}^3He$), $1.3 \times 10^2$ cm ($p+{}^{11}B$)
- For $\rho_t = 10^{21}/\rm{cm}^3$: $\tau_{pla} = 4.0 \times 10^{-8}$ s ($d+t$), $1.3 \times 10^{-7}$ s ($d+{}^3He$), $1.2 \times 10^{-7}$ s ($p+{}^{11}B$)
- Critical flux range $10^{22} - 10^{24}/\rm{cm^2/s}$ for $\rho_t = 10^{15}/\rm{cm}^3$
- Triple product $\rho_t T_c \tau_{pla} = 7.2 \times 10^{19}$ keV s/m$^3$ for $d+t$, compared to Lawson criterion of $\sim 3 \times 10^{21}$ keV s/m$^3$
- Two-stream instability growth factor $\gamma_t \tau_{pla} = 2.4 \times 10^8$ for $d+t$ at $\rho_t = 10^{15}/\rm{cm}^3$
- Net power estimate for beam radius 0.1 cm: 29 MW ($d+t$ at $T=3$ keV), 0.35 GW ($d+He$ at $T=12$ keV), 5.1 GW ($p+B$ at $T=50$ keV)
- Bremsstrahlung loss rates: 0.95 MW ($d+t$), 95 MW ($d+He$), 4.9 GW ($p+B$)

## Context

This work extends the concept of using ion beams on plasma, previously applied for heating in Tokamak and field-reversed configuration (FRC) reactors (Guo 2015) and for fast ignition in inertial confinement (Maschke 1975, Lin 1995), to a novel scheme where the external beam itself serves as the fusion fuel. The authors contrast their approach with thermonuclear reactors like ITER (which operates at 12.5 keV, far below the $d+t$ resonance peak at 64 keV center-of-mass energy) and with earlier failed attempts at accelerator-based fusion including colliding beams in storage rings (Ruggiero 2000) and neutral beam approaches where ionization cross sections dominate. The paper identifies the critical challenge of achieving the required beam flux, which is several orders of magnitude beyond current linear accelerator capabilities such as the Spallation Neutron Source (Cousineau 2015), and proposes storage ring configurations with magnetic confinement as potential solutions, noting that recent magnetic mirror experiments (Bagryansky 2015) have achieved electron temperatures of 900 eV with lifetimes exceeding 8 ms.
