# Microscopic description of fission in Uranium isotopes with the Gogny energy density functional

> The most recent parametrizations D1S, D1N and D1M of the Gogny energy density functional are used to describe fission in the isotopes $^{232-280}$ U. Fission paths, collective masses and zero point quantum corrections, obtained within the constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov approximation, are used to compute the systematics of the spontaneous fission half-lives $t_\mathrm{SF}$, the masses and charges of the fission fragments as well as their intrinsic shapes. The Gogny-D1M parametrization has been benchmarked against available experimental data on inner and second barrier heights, excitation energies of the fission isomers and half-lives in a selected set of Pu, Cm, Cf, Fm, No, Rf, Sg, Hs and Fl nuclei. It is concluded that D1M represents a reasonable starting point to describe fission in heavy and superheavy nuclei. Special attention is also paid to understand the uncertainties in the predicted $t_\mathrm{SF}$ values arising from the different building blocks entering the standard semi-classical Wentzel-Kramers-Brillouin formula. Although the uncertainties are large, the trend with mass or neutron numbers are well reproduced and therefore the theory still has predictive power. In this respect, it is also shown that modifications of a few per cent in the pairing strength can have a significant impact on the collective masses leading to uncertainties in the $t_\mathrm{SF}$ values of several orders of magnitude.

## Key claim

The Gogny-D1M energy density functional provides a reasonable starting point for describing fission in heavy and superheavy nuclei, but the predicted spontaneous fission half-lives carry large theoretical uncertainties spanning several orders of magnitude, meaning their absolute values cannot be used with confidence while isotopic trends are reproduced with much higher accuracy.

## Method

Constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations with the Gogny D1S, D1N, and D1M parametrizations were performed for Uranium isotopes 232-280, using constraints on axially symmetric quadrupole, octupole, and necking operators. Fission paths, collective masses from the Adiabatic Time Dependent HFB (ATDHFB) and Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) with Gaussian Overlap Approximation, and zero-point rotational and vibrational corrections were computed, then fed into the Wentzel-Kramers-Brillouin (WKB) formula to obtain spontaneous fission half-lives.

## Key numbers

- The D1M parametrization achieves an rms deviation for binding energies of 0.798 MeV across all known nuclei
- For 238U, the predicted inner barrier height (axial) is 9.06 MeV versus the experimental value of 6.30 MeV; with triaxiality allowed, the inner barrier lowers to 7.46 MeV
- For 240Pu, the predicted inner barrier height (axial) is 9.45 MeV versus the experimental value of 6.05 MeV; with triaxiality, it lowers to 7.70 MeV
- The rotational correction to the potential can be as large as 6-7 MeV and is proportional to the deformation Q20
- ATDHFB collective masses are typically 1.5 to 2 times larger than GCM masses, leading to actions 20-40% larger in the ATDHFB case
- Modifications of 5 or 10 percent in the pairing strengths can modify predicted tSF values by several orders of magnitude

## Context

This work extends previous microscopic fission studies using the Gogny-D1S parametrization (which was fitted to fission properties of 240Pu and has been applied to heavy and superheavy nuclei by Warda, Egido, Robledo, and Pomorski (2002) and Delaroche (2006)) to the newer D1N and D1M parametrizations. The D1M functional had not been systematically used in fission studies before, so the authors validate it against experimental barrier heights, fission isomer excitation energies, and half-lives for a set of Pu, Cm, Cf, Fm, No, Rf, Sg, Hs, and Fl nuclei. The work enables systematic predictions of fission properties along the Uranium chain up to the neutron dripline, which is relevant for understanding the stability of superheavy elements and the r-process in stellar nucleosynthesis.
