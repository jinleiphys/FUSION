# Spherical coupled-cluster theory for open-shell nuclei

> A microscopic description of nuclei is important to understand the nuclear shell-model from fundamental principles. This is difficult to achieve for more than the lightest nuclei without an effective approximation scheme. The purpose of this paper is to define and evaluate an approximation scheme that can be used to study nuclei that are described as two particles attached to a closed (sub-)shell nucleus. The equation-of-motion coupled-cluster formalism has been used to obtain ground and excited state energies. This method is based on the diagonalization of a non-Hermitian matrix obtained from a similarity transformation of the many-body nuclear Hamiltonian. A chiral interaction at the next-to-next-to-next-to leading order using a cutoff at 500 MeV was used. The ground state energies of ${}^6$Li and ${}^6$He were in good agreement with a no-core shell-model calculation using the same interaction. Several excited states were also produced with overall good agreement. Only the $J^π=3^+$ excited state in ${}^6$Li showed a sizable deviation. The ground state energies of ${}^{18}$O, ${}^{18}$F and ${}^{18}$Ne were converged, but underbound compared to experiment. Moreover, the calculated spectra were converged and comparable to both experiment and shell-model studies in this region. Some excited states in ${}^{18}$O were high or missing in the spectrum. It was also shown that the wave function for both ground and excited states separates into an intrinsic part and a Gaussian for the center-of-mass coordinate. Spurious center-of-mass excitations are clearly identified.

## Key claim

The equation-of-motion coupled-cluster formalism, truncated at the singles and doubles level (EOM-CCSD) and reformulated in a spherical basis, can produce converged ground and excited state energies for nuclei described as two particles attached to a closed (sub-)shell core, but the method systematically underbinds ground states compared to experiment and misses or shifts some excited states, particularly the \(J^\pi=3^+\) state in \({}^6\)Li.

## Method

The intrinsic nuclear Hamiltonian with a chiral nucleon-nucleon interaction at next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order (N\(^3\)LO) with a 500 MeV cutoff was used. A single-reference coupled-cluster calculation with singles and doubles (CCSD) was first performed for a closed (sub-)shell reference nucleus, followed by a \(\Lambda\)-CCSD(T) correction to the ground state energy. The similarity-transformed Hamiltonian was then diagonalized in a truncated vector space using the two-particle-attached equation-of-motion coupled-cluster method (2PA-EOM-CCSD), where the excitation operator is truncated at the 3p-1h level, and all working equations were rederived in a spherical tensor formalism to eliminate magnetic quantum numbers.

## Key numbers

- Ground state energies of \({}^6\)Li and \({}^6\)He showed good agreement with no-core shell-model calculations using the same interaction; the \(J^\pi=3^+\) excited state in \({}^6\)Li showed a sizable deviation.
- Ground state energies of \({}^18\)O, \({}^18\)F, and \({}^18\)Ne were converged but underbound compared to experiment.
- The computational cost of CCSD scales as \(n_o^2 n_u^4\) (where \(n_o\) is the number of occupied single-particle states and \(n_u\) the number of unoccupied states), while full CCSDT scales as \(n_o^3 n_u^5\) and is computationally prohibitive.
- The largest calculations involved up to \(10^9\) basis states for the 2PA-EOM-CCSD method.

## Context

This work extends the equation-of-motion coupled-cluster framework previously applied to oxygen isotopes (Hagen 2012), calcium isotopes (Hagen 2012), and \({}^56\)Ni (Binder 2012), and builds on the general 2PA-EOM-CCSD theory presented in Jansen (2011) which was limited to small model spaces. The spherical formalism enables calculations in significantly larger model spaces than previously possible. The method has already been successfully applied to several nuclei (Hagen 2012a, Hagen 2012b, Ekstrom 2013, Lepailleur 2013), but the spherical formalism had not been presented before this paper. The author notes that to obtain closer agreement with experimental data, effects of three-nucleon forces, the scattering continuum, and additional configurations in the coupled-cluster approximations are necessary.
