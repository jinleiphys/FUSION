# Constraining the Symmetry Parameters of the Nuclear Interaction

> One of the major uncertainties in the dense matter equation of state has been the nuclear symmetry energy. The density dependence of the symmetry energy is important in nuclear astrophysics, as it controls the neutronization of matter in core-collapse supernovae, the radii of neutron stars and the thicknesses of their crusts, the rate of cooling of neutron stars, and the properties of nuclei involved in r-process nucleosynthesis. We show that fits of nuclear masses to experimental masses, combined with other experimental information from neutron skins, heavy ion collisions, giant dipole resonances and dipole polarizabilities, lead to stringent constraints on parameters that describe the symmetry energy near the nuclear saturation density. These constraints are remarkably consistent with inferences from theoretical calculations of pure neutron matter, and, furthermore, with astrophysical observations of neutron stars. The concordance of experimental, theoretical and observational analyses suggests that the symmetry parameters S_v and L are in the range 29.0 - 32.7 MeV and 40.5 - 61.9 MeV, respectively, and that the neutron star radius, for a 1.4 M_sun star, is in the narrow window 10.7 km < R < 13.1 km (90% confidence). We can also set tight limits to the size of neutron star crusts and the fractional moment of inertia they contain, as well as the overall moment of inertia and quadrupole polarizability of 1.4 M_sun stars. Our results also have implications for the disk mass and ejected mass of compact mergers involving neutron stars.

# Constraining the Symmetry Parameters of the Nuclear Interaction

## Key claim
The authors show that combining nuclear mass fits with other experimental data (neutron skins, heavy ion collisions, giant dipole resonances, dipole polarizabilities) yields tight constraints on the symmetry energy parameters S_v and L, but these constraints depend on the specific nuclear models used and the assumed nominal errors in the fitting procedures. The concordance with theoretical neutron matter calculations and astrophysical observations is noted, but the quoted ranges (S_v = 29.0-32.7 MeV, L = 40.5-61.9 MeV) represent a 90% confidence interval that depends on the particular experimental constraints included.

## Method
The authors performed least-squares fitting of a liquid droplet model to nuclear mass data from 2336 nuclei, incorporating shell, pairing, Wigner, and Coulomb exchange terms, and used the resulting correlation between S_v and L to define confidence ellipses. They then combined this mass-fit correlation with independent experimental constraints from neutron skin thicknesses of Sn isotopes (Chen 2010), giant dipole resonance centroid energies (Trippa 2008), dipole polarizabilities of 208Pb (Piekarewicz 2012), and heavy ion collision isospin diffusion (Tsang 2009), mapping each constraint onto the S_v-L plane. The overlap region of all constraints defined the allowed parameter ranges.

## Key numbers
- S_v constrained to 29.0 - 32.7 MeV (90% confidence from combined experimental constraints)
- L constrained to 40.5 - 61.9 MeV (90% confidence from combined experimental constraints)
- Neutron star radius for a 1.4 M_sun star: 10.7 km < R < 13.1 km (90% confidence)
- Best-fit values from liquid droplet mass fit: S_v0 = 29.1 MeV, L0 = 53.9 MeV
- Best-fit values from UNEDF0 microscopic fit: S_v0 = 30.5 MeV, L0 = 45.1 MeV
- Best-fit values from finite-range Thomas-Fermi fit: S_v0 = 31.2 MeV, L0 = 56.6 MeV
- Neutron skin thickness of 208Pb from Sn isotope analysis: 0.155 fm to 0.195 fm (Chen 2010)
- Dipole polarizability of 208Pb: alpha_D = 20.1 +/- 0.6 fm^3 (Tamii 2011), equivalent to r_np^208 = 0.146-0.190 fm (Piekarewicz 2012)
- Bulk symmetry energy at n=0.1 fm^-3 from GDR: 23.3 MeV < S_2(0.1) < 24.9 MeV (Trippa 2008)
- Correlation coefficient between S_v and L from UNEDF0: approximately 0.97

## Context
This work extends earlier nuclear mass fitting studies by Farine (1978) and Oyamatsu (2003), who found linear correlations between L and S_v but did not explore statistical significance, and by the UNEDF collaboration (Kortelainen 2010), who performed microscopic Hartree-Fock fits with quantified confidence intervals. The authors combine multiple experimental constraints that had previously been treated separately, including neutron skin measurements (Chen 2010), giant dipole resonances (Trippa 2008), dipole polarizabilities (Piekarewicz 2012), and heavy ion collisions (Tsang 2009), and show they are consistent with theoretical neutron matter calculations from chiral Lagrangians (Hebeler 2010) and quantum Monte Carlo (Gandolfi 2012). The results enable tighter predictions for neutron star radii, crust properties, and merger ejecta masses, and can be used to constrain supernova simulations and r-process nucleosynthesis models.
