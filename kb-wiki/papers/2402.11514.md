# High Quality Microscopic Nuclear Masses of Superheavy Nuclei

> To synthesize new superheavy elements, the accurate prediction of nuclear masses of superheavy nuclei is essential for calculations of reaction $Q$ values, neutron separation energies and $α$-decay energies, which are important for estimating beam energies, survival probabilities and also for identifications. In this work, we include existing $α$-decay energies of superheavy nuclei in the fitting procedure of extended Skyrme density functionals as corresponding nuclear masses are not available. Systematic $α$-decay energies are well reproduced with deviations smaller than 0.2 MeV. The high quality $α$-decay energies make it feasible for direct identification of new elements and new isotopes. The resulting binding energies in the heaviest region are surprisingly close to the inferences by AME2020. Our work should be useful for guiding experimental synthesis of new elements 119 and 120.

# High Quality Microscopic Nuclear Masses of Superheavy Nuclei

## Key claim
By including experimental α-decay energies of superheavy nuclei up to Z=118 in the fitting procedure of extended Skyrme density functionals, the authors obtain high-quality microscopic nuclear masses and α-decay energies for superheavy nuclei, with the caveat that deviations are slightly larger for odd-odd superheavy nuclei (up to 0.4 MeV) compared to even-even nuclei (within 0.1 MeV).

## Method
The authors performed new optimizations of extended Skyrme forces (with an additional higher-order density dependent term) using the simulated annealing method, fitting to experimental α-decay energies of even-even superheavy nuclei up to Z=118, along with binding energies and charge radii of selected nuclei. The self-consistent deformed Skyrme-Hartree-Fock+BCS (SHF-BCS) method was used with the SKYAX code in axial-symmetric coordinate-spaces, employing mixed pairing interaction with pairing strengths Vp=480 MeV and Vn=440 MeV.

## Key numbers
- Systematic α-decay energies are reproduced with deviations smaller than 0.2 MeV for all existing superheavy nuclei
- For even-N nuclei, experimental Qα is reproduced perfectly within 0.1 MeV; for odd-odd nuclei, the largest deviation is about 0.4 MeV
- The largest deviations of FRDM2012 and WS4 models from experimental Qα are around 0.5 MeV and 0.3 MeV, respectively
- The calculated Qα of 294Og is 11.642 MeV (experimental: 11.87 MeV); FRDM2012 gives 12.366 MeV, WS4 gives 12.199 MeV
- The calculated Qα of 293Ts is 11.322 MeV (experimental: 11.32 MeV); FRDM2012 gives 11.396 MeV, WS4 gives 11.622 MeV
- The calculated Qα of 292Lv is 10.881 MeV (experimental: 10.791 MeV); FRDM2012 gives 10.816 MeV, WS4 gives 11.127 MeV
- The calculated Qα of 291Mc is 10.425 MeV (experimental: 10.3 MeV); FRDM2012 gives 9.726 MeV, WS4 gives 10.192 MeV
- For Z=119 isotopes, the neutron separation energy at N=180 is 7.57 MeV, compared to 7.12 MeV from FRDM2012 and 7.27 MeV from UNEDF0
- For the 50Ti+249Bk reaction, the reaction Q value is -192.381 MeV; corrected for systematic underestimation of binding energies (0.6 MeV), it becomes approximately -191.78 MeV
- For the 54Cr+243Am reaction, the reaction Q value is -208.395 MeV; corrected, it becomes approximately -207.79 MeV
- The refitted Skyrme parameters (Sk-SHE1) yield nuclear matter incompressibility K∞=233.2128 MeV and symmetry energy as=32.3017 MeV at saturation density ρ0=0.1596 fm-3

## Context
This work extends the Skyrme density functional theory approach specifically for superheavy nuclei, where previous global mass models like FRDM2012 (Moller et al.) and WS4 (Wang et al.) show larger deviations in α-decay energies (0.5 MeV and 0.3 MeV respectively). The authors note that UNEDF0 (Kortelainen et al.) underestimates Qα of 294Og by more than 1.5 MeV. The resulting binding energies are surprisingly close to the AME2020 evaluations, while FRDM2012 binding energies are overestimated by approximately 4 MeV in the heaviest region. This work enables more reliable predictions of α-decay energies for direct identification of new elements 119 and 120, and provides reaction Q values and neutron separation energies needed for calculating production cross sections in experimental synthesis attempts at JINR, RIKEN, and GSI.
