# The Early Stages of Heavy Ion Collisions

> Heavy ion collisions pose interesting challenges to quantum chromodynamics, because they probe the parton structure of the incoming nuclei at very small longitudinal momentum fractions. Combined with the large size of nuclei, this may lead to the phenomenon of gluon saturation. The Color Glass Condensate is an effective QCD description that aims to cope with such a situation. In this talk, I show how one may study heavy ion collisions in this framework.

## Key claim

The Color Glass Condensate (CGC) effective theory can describe the early stages of heavy ion collisions, but the classical statistical approximation (CSA) used to resum unstable modes suffers from a severe ultraviolet cutoff sensitivity that breaks renormalizability, limiting its applicability.

## Method

The author reviews the CGC framework where incoming nuclei are treated as strong color currents (of order \(g^{-1}\)) flowing along the light-cone, eikonally coupled to gauge fields. Inclusive observables at leading order are obtained from classical solutions of Yang-Mills equations with null retarded boundary conditions. The next-to-leading order (NLO) correction is expressed via a quadratic operator acting on the leading-order result, and in unstable regimes this operator is exponentiated to resum the fastest-growing graphs, yielding the CSA as an average over classical solutions with Gaussian fluctuations added to the initial condition.

## Key numbers

- The initial longitudinal pressure is exactly the opposite of the energy density immediately after the collision.
- The longitudinal pressure becomes positive at a time \(Q_s \tau \sim 1\), but never becomes comparable to the transverse pressure at leading order.
- The validity of the classical approximation is bounded by \(Q_s \tau_{\rm class} \ll \alpha_s^{-3/2}\).
- In the saturated regime, each connected vacuum graph is of order \(g^{-2}\), and the sources are of order \(g^{-1}\).

## Context

This talk extends the CGC framework developed by Gribov (1984) and Gelis (2010) for describing gluon saturation in high-energy nuclear collisions. It contrasts the CSA approach, which suffers from renormalizability issues, with the more fundamental but computationally challenging Kadanoff-Baym (2PI) formalism (Hatta, 2012). The kinetic theory results reviewed (Epelbaum, 2015; Kurkela, 2015; Blaizot, 2017) show isotropization and hydrodynamization in expanding systems, but the author notes these qualitative findings need confirmation from the 2PI framework.
