# Heavy baryonic resonances, multistrange hadrons, and equilibration at energies available at the GSI Schwerionensynchrotron, SIS18

> We study the details and time dependence of particle production in nuclear collisions at a fixed target beam energy of $E_{\mathrm{lab}}= 1.76$ A GeV with the UrQMD transport model. We find that the previously proposed production mechanism for multi strange hadrons, $ϕ$ and $Ξ$, are possible due to secondary interactions of incoming nuclei of the projectile and target with already created nuclear resonances, while the Fermi momenta of the nuclei play only a minor role. We also show how the centrality dependence of these particle multiplicities can be used to confirm the proposed mechanism, as it strongly depends on the number of participants in the reaction. Furthermore we investigate the time dependence of particle production in collisions of Ca+Ca at this beam energy, in order to understand the origins of the apparent chemical equilibration of the measured particle yields. We find that indeed the light hadron yields appear to be in equilibrium already from the very early stage of the collision while in fact no local equilibration, through multi collision processes, takes place. The apparent equilibrium is reached only through the decay of the resonances according to available phase space.

## Key claim

The previously proposed production mechanism for multi-strange hadrons (φ and Ξ) at SIS18 energies is driven by secondary interactions of incoming nucleons with already created nuclear resonances, while Fermi momenta of the nuclei play only a minor role; the apparent chemical equilibrium of light hadron yields arises not from multi-collision equilibration processes but from resonance decays according to available phase space, with equilibrium appearing already from the very early stage of the collision.

## Method

The authors used the UrQMD transport model to simulate nuclear collisions at a fixed target beam energy of 1.76 A GeV, including strangeness exchange reactions and new decay channels for massive baryonic resonances. They analyzed the invariant mass distributions of N* resonances from initial nucleon-nucleon scatterings versus all decaying resonances, and extracted the time evolution of stable particle yields from Ca+Ca collisions. These yields were then fitted with two statistical hadronization model codes (THERMUS and the Florence code) to extract temperature, baryochemical potential, and strangeness suppression parameters as functions of time, and compared with a coarse-graining approach.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy: E_lab = 1.76 A GeV (fixed target)
- The peak of the maximally available invariant mass distribution for resonance creation from initial scatterings is located at the beam energy (in the CM frame) minus the nucleon mass, with a smear from Fermi momenta
- The actual invariant mass distribution of N* resonances from initial scatterings is shifted to lower masses by almost 200 MeV compared to the maximally available energy
- Heavy N* resonances decay on average about 1 fm/c later than lighter resonances, indicating they are created in secondary collisions
- The number of inelastic collisions per participant nucleon saturates at only 1-2 even after interpenetration
- The temperature and baryochemical potential from SHM fits saturate around 10 fm/c, with the temperature reaching its maximum at approximately 7 fm/c and the chemical potential at 5-6 fm/c
- The coarse-graining temperature is slightly larger than the SHM fit result by less than 5 MeV
- Good fit quality (chi-squared per degree of freedom) is achieved for times larger than 5 fm/c, with bad fit quality only at the very first time step (t ≤ 5 fm/c)
- The apparent equilibrium persists until t ≥ 13 fm/c, after which inelastic collisions cease

## Context

This work extends previous studies by the same group (Graef 2014, Steinheimer 2015) that introduced strangeness exchange reactions and heavy resonance decay channels into UrQMD to describe strange particle production at SIS18 energies. It addresses questions left open by those studies regarding the role of Fermi momenta versus secondary interactions, and the microscopic origin of apparent chemical equilibrium previously observed in experimental data from the FOPI collaboration (Agakishiev 2015, Agakishiev 2010). The results enable experimental confirmation of the proposed production mechanism through centrality dependence measurements, and challenge the interpretation of thermal fits as evidence for true equilibration, instead suggesting that resonance decays alone can produce apparent equilibrium yields. The finding that only 1-2 inelastic collisions per participant are needed to produce apparent equilibrium builds on earlier work by Cassing (1990) suggesting that 3 collisions per participant may be sufficient for thermal equilibrium.
