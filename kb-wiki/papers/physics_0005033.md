# The INT at Age Ten

> The history of the Department of Energy's Institute for Nuclear Theory, located on the campus of the University of Washington, is reviewed on the occasion of the INT's tenth anniversary.

# The INT at Age Ten

## Key claim
The Institute for Nuclear Theory (INT) has evolved from an "interesting experiment" into an institution "most responsible for the renewed optimism in our field," with its success attributed to a sound formula emphasizing interdisciplinary intersections and collaboration, though the original proposal envisioned only three annual programs attracting approximately ten visiting scientists each, whereas the INT now attracts 350 visitors per year.

## Method
The paper reviews the history, governance structure, and operational model of the INT over its first decade (1990-2000), describing its founding through the Koonin Committee's 1988 recommendation to the Nuclear Science Advisory Committee (NSAC), the competitive proposal process that selected the University of Washington site, and the subsequent development of its faculty, postdoctoral program, visitor programs, workshops, and educational outreach activities including the Research Experiences for Undergraduates (REU) program and the National Summer School in Nuclear Physics.

## Key numbers
- The INT opened in March 1990 with a three-month program on Quarks and Nuclei.
- A typical program involves 60-70 visitors, with weekly attendance of about 18, close to the INT's capacity.
- Overseas participants account for 30-40% of total program attendance.
- Approximately one-third of program proposals succeed on first submission.
- The INT receives an average of ten program proposals each year.
- Over the last five years, an average of four "stand alone" workshops have been held each year.
- The REU program generated about 230 applications for 12 positions in 2000, with an acceptance rate of nearly 90% for first offers.
- Nearly 40% of REU students are female; approximately 20% of the class later returns to graduate school at the University of Washington.
- The National Summer School in Nuclear Physics attracts 40-50 students each year.
- The INT attracts 350 visitors each year; the list of Affiliates numbers nearly 2000.
- The INT's permanent quarters opened on August 15, 1994, with 22 offices, a seminar room, and a conference room.
- The INT typically hosts five or six postdocs, about half supported by international fellowships.
- The INT hosts two Fellows appointed for five-year terms; three of four veterans of this position already hold tenured university positions.

## Context
This paper reviews the first decade of the Department of Energy's Institute for Nuclear Theory, which was created following the Koonin Committee's 1988 recommendation to establish nuclear theory centers as a complement to strengthened individual programs. The INT's development parallels that of its European counterpart, the ECT* in Trento, and the paper describes how the INT has responded to the broadening of nuclear physics frontiers driven by the relativistic heavy ion programs at CERN and Brookhaven, precision electroweak efforts at Jefferson Lab, and sophisticated solar neutrino experiments. The paper enables future evaluation of the INT's role in supporting interdisciplinary nuclear theory and provides a baseline for assessing the institute's evolution in subsequent decades.
