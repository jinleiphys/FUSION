# Dynamics of Symmetry Breaking Out of Equilibrium: From Condensed Matter to QCD and the Early Universe

> The dynamics of symmetry breaking during out of equilibrium phase transitions is a topic of great importance in many disciplines, from condensed matter to particle physics and early Universe cosmology with definite experimental impact. In these notes we provide a summary of the relevant aspects of the dynamics of symmetry breaking in many different fields with emphasis on the experimental realizations. In condensed matter we address the dynamics of phase ordering, the emergence of condensates, coarsening and dynamical scaling. In QCD the possibility of disoriented chiral condensates of pions emerging during a strongly out of equilibrium phase transition is discussed. We elaborate on the dynamics of phase ordering in phase transitions in the Early Universe, in particular the emergence of condensates and scaling in FRW cosmologies. We mention some experimental efforts in different fields that study this wide ranging phenomena and offer a quantitative theoretical description both at the phenomenological level in condensed matter, introducing the scaling hypothesis as well as at a microscopic level in quantum field theories. The emergence of semiclassical condensates and a dynamical length scale is shown in detail, in quantum field theory this length scale is constrained by causality. The large N limit provides a natural bridge to compare the solutions in different settings and to establish similarities and differences.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the dynamics of symmetry breaking during out-of-equilibrium phase transitions, characterized by the emergence of a dynamical length scale, coarsening, and dynamical scaling, is a universal phenomenon spanning condensed matter, QCD, and early Universe cosmology, but the authors emphasize that the specific realization of these features depends on whether the transition occurs in local thermodynamic equilibrium or in a quenched manner, with the latter requiring a full quantum field theoretical description for the shortest time and spatial scales.

## Method

The authors review the phenomenological Time-Dependent Ginzburg-Landau (TDGL) approach for condensed matter systems, which uses a coarse-grained free energy functional and Langevin dynamics with a stochastic noise term obeying the fluctuation-dissipation theorem. For quantum field theory settings, they describe a microscopic formulation that follows the time evolution of the quantum density matrix to capture classicalization and decoherence of fluctuations, using the large N limit as a bridge to compare solutions across different fields. The analysis covers three domains: condensed matter (phase ordering, coarsening, dynamical scaling), QCD (disoriented chiral condensates of pions), and early Universe cosmology (condensates and scaling in FRW cosmologies, defect networks via the Kibble-Zurek mechanism).

## Key numbers

- The QCD chiral phase transition critical temperature is approximately 150 MeV, with up and down quark masses of about 5 MeV and 7–10 MeV respectively, and pion mass around 135 MeV.
- In heavy ion collisions at RHIC, estimated energy density in the central region is 3–5 GeV/fm³, corresponding to temperatures of about 200 MeV, with thermalization time of about 1 fm/c.
- The early Universe QCD phase transition occurred at about 10⁻⁵ seconds after the Big Bang at a temperature of 10¹² K, while the GUT phase transition occurred at about 10⁻³⁵ seconds at 10²³ K and the EW phase transition at 10⁻¹² seconds at 10¹⁵ K.
- COBE measured temperature fluctuations in the Cosmic Microwave Background of 30 μK on a blackbody spectrum at 2.73 K.
- In superfluid ³He experiments, neutron irradiation via the reaction ³He + n → ³He⁻ + p + 764 keV heats a small portion into the normal state, followed by rapid cooling back into the superfluid phase.
- The correlation length growth in the spinodal region is described by M₀(T) = √(r₀/λ)(T_c − T)^(1/2) for T < T_c, with the spinodal region boundaries at M_s(T) = √(r₀/(3λ))(T − T_c)^(1/2).

## Context

This review extends the Kibble-Zurek mechanism (Kibble 1976, Zurek 1985) for defect formation in cosmological phase transitions to a unified framework that also covers condensed matter systems and QCD, building on the static scaling hypothesis of critical phenomena (Goldenfeld 1992) and the renormalization group approach. The work connects the phenomenological TDGL description (Bray 1994, Langer 1992, Mazenko 1991, Marco 1996) with microscopic quantum field theory treatments, enabling quantitative predictions for experimental searches of disoriented chiral condensates at RHIC, LHC, and CERN-SPS (experiment WA98), as well as for CMB anisotropy measurements that could distinguish between inflationary and topological defect scenarios for structure formation.
