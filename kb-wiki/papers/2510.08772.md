# Effect of Finite-Temperature $β$-Decay Rates on the Rapid Neutron Capture Process

> $β$-decay is known to play an essential role in the rapid neutron capture process ($r$-process) during $(n, γ) \leftrightarrow (γ, n)$ equilibrium and freeze-out when the neutron-rich nuclei decay back to stability. Recent systematic theoretical studies on $β$-decay at finite temperature indicated that under hot conditions ($T\sim10$~GK), a significant acceleration of $β$-decay rates is expected, especially for nuclei near stability. This corresponds to the early stage of the $r$-process. In this study, we investigate the effect of the $β$-decays in finite temperature using the rates calculated with the finite-temperature proton-neutron relativistic quasiparticle random-phase approximation (FT-PNRQRPA). We explore a variety of astrophysical conditions and find that the effect on the abundance pattern is significant in hot and moderately neutron-rich conditions such as are expected in magnetorotational supernovae. Accelerated $β$-decay rates also increase the heating rate in the early phase, resulting in an additional modification of the final abundance pattern.

## Key claim

The paper claims that finite-temperature beta-decay rates significantly affect r-process abundance patterns, but only under hot and moderately neutron-rich conditions such as those in magnetorotational supernovae; the effect is not universal across all astrophysical environments and depends on the complex interplay between accelerated decay rates and neutron consumption.

## Method

The authors used the finite-temperature proton-neutron relativistic quasiparticle random-phase approximation (FT-PNRQRPA) to calculate beta-decay rates for nuclei with proton numbers 20 to 60 on a grid spanning temperatures up to 10 GK and density-electron fraction products up to 10^14 g/cm^3. They implemented these rates in the PRISM nuclear reaction network code and evolved abundances along two sets of ten hydrodynamic trajectories: one from magnetorotational supernova simulations and one from hypermassive neutron star disk outflow models. The calculations included contributions from both allowed and first-forbidden transitions, with the pairing interaction improved over previous work by using the Gogny D1S interaction.

## Key numbers

- Temperature of 10 GK (approximately 0.9 MeV) is the hot condition where significant acceleration of beta-decay rates is expected, especially for nuclei near stability
- For tin isotopes near the valley of stability, half-lives are reduced by orders of magnitude with increasing temperature up to 10 GK
- The beta-decay rates were calculated on a grid spanning 0.01 to 10 GK in temperature and 10^1 to 10^14 g/cm^3 in density-electron fraction product, totaling 154 data points
- For the MHD_1 trajectory, initial density was 1.82 x 10^8 g/cm^3 and initial electron fraction was 0.233
- For the ADM_3 trajectory, initial density was 0.593 x 10^8 g/cm^3 and initial electron fraction was 0.292
- The FT-HBCS equations were solved in a harmonic oscillator basis with 20 shells for fermion states and 21 shells for boson states
- The cut-off on the sum of proton and neutron quasiparticle energies for the FT-PNRQRPA matrix diagonalization was 100 MeV

## Context

This work extends the finite-temperature beta-decay calculations of Ravlic (2021) by implementing an improved pairing interaction and applying the rates to r-process nucleosynthesis for the first time. It builds on earlier theoretical studies of beta-decay at finite temperature by Minato (2009) using Skyrme-Hartree Fock and by Litvinova (2020) using relativistic time-blocking approximation, which found temperature effects become important above 6 GK. The study enables future investigations of how temperature-dependent weak rates affect kilonova light curves and heating rates, and opens the possibility of including bound-state beta-decay effects in r-process calculations.
