# Localisation and alpha radioactivity

> Relativistic energy density functional approaches are known to well describe nuclear states which involve alpha clusters. Here, alpha emitting nuclei are analysed through the behavior of the spatial localisation of nucleonic states, calculated with an axially deformed RHB approach over the nuclear chart. The systematic occurrence of more localised valence states, having a n = 1 radial quantum number, allows to pinpoint nuclei in agreement with experimentally known alpha-emitters. The cases of 212Po and 104Te are investigated, showing the concomitant contributions of the pseudospin symmetry and the presence of n = 1 states, on the alpha preformation probability. The impact of the localisation of valence states, on alpha preformation probability, is then analysed. It allows to study shell effects on this probability, over isotopic and isotonic chains. Finally, a phenomenological law is also provided, relating this probability to the radial quantum number of the valence states.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the spatial localisation of nucleonic valence states, quantified by the radial quantum number n, is a key driver of alpha preformation probability, but the authors explicitly state that this is a qualitative approximation and that "a one-to-one quantitative correlation is not expected" due to the complexity of alpha emission.

## Method

The authors performed axially deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations using the DD-ME2 functional over the nuclear chart of even-even nuclei, computing the spatial dispersion of valence nucleonic states. They defined a localisation criterion based on the average dispersion weighted by occupation probability, requiring that the quantity <Δr>·A^{-1/6} be less than 0.7 fm to identify n=1 states. They then compared the predicted localised nuclei to experimentally known alpha-emitters and analysed the alpha preformation factor through the ratio of a phenomenological half-life formula (assuming preformation probability P=1) to experimental half-lives.

## Key numbers

- The localisation criterion threshold is <Δr>·A^{-1/6} < 0.7 fm, derived from a prefactor of 0.5 fm for n=1 states and 0.9 fm for n=2 states in the harmonic oscillator approximation.
- For 212Po, the spatial dispersion of the 1i_{11/2} neutron state is 1.17 fm, while the 2g_{9/2} state has 1.95 fm and the 3p_{1/2} state has 2.40 fm; the proton 1h_{9/2} state has 1.08 fm.
- For 104Te, the 1g_{9/2} state has a dispersion of 0.98 fm, while the 1g_{7/2} state has about 1.5 fm.
- The average dispersion of neutron valence states in 212Po is about 1.4 times larger than in 104Te.
- For even-even nuclei fulfilling the localisation criterion, the mean ratio T^{Pheno}/T^{Exp} is 2.4 ± 0.2, compared to 1.3 ± 0.2 for nuclei not fulfilling the criterion.
- The phenomenological law gives Log √P ≃ 1 - √(2n-1), yielding a reduction of the alpha preformation probability by a factor of 20-30 when switching from n=1 to n=2 valence states.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Ebran 2018) linking nucleonic localisation to cluster states, now applying the same concept to alpha radioactivity. It builds on the phenomenological analysis of Brock (1992) who showed that deviations from an enhanced Geiger-Nuttall law (assuming P=1) image the preformation probability, and on the recent experimental evidence (Auranen 2018, Xiao 2019) that 104Te has a larger alpha emission probability than 212Po. The approach enables a systematic, microscopic explanation of shell effects on alpha preformation, particularly the N=126 shell effect, and provides a simple phenomenological law relating preformation probability to the radial quantum number of valence states.
