# On the existence of Rydberg nuclear molecules

> Present nuclear detection techniques prevents us from determining if the analogue of a Rydberg molecule exists for the nuclear case. But nothing in nature disallows their existence. As in the atomic case, Rydberg nuclear molecules would be a laboratory for new aspects and applications of nuclear physics. We propose that Rydberg nuclear molecules, which represent the exotic, halo nuclei version, such as 11Be + 11Be, of the well known quasimolecules observed in stable nuclei such as 12C + 12C, might be common structures that could manifest their existence along the dripline. A study of possible candidates and the expected structure of such exotic clustering of two halo nuclei: the Rydberg nuclear molecules, is made on the basis of three diferent methods. It is shown that such cluster structures might be stable and unexpectedly common.

## Key claim

The authors propose that Rydberg nuclear molecules, exotic, loosely bound dimers of two halo nuclei such as \(^{11}\)Be + \(^{11}\)Be, might exist as stable structures along the dripline, but they hedge that present nuclear detection techniques prevent confirmation and that such critically stable dimers are "much less dramatic than their atomic counterparts."

## Method

The authors used three methods to study the \(^{10}\)Be + n + n + \(^{10}\)Be system as a candidate Rydberg nuclear molecule: (1) a pseudo-potential approach adapted from Fermi's method for neutron-nucleus scattering; (2) a Bohr-Oppenheimer approximation for adiabatic molecular states, calculating covalent bonding energies from single-particle wave functions; and (3) a variational Monte-Carlo method refined by Path Integral Monte Carlo (PIMC) to compute the ground-state energy including neutron-neutron interactions. The neutron orbitals were taken from a Woods-Saxon plus spin-orbit potential model for \(^{11}\)Be, and the \(^{10}\)Be–\(^{10}\)Be potential was a shallow Woods-Saxon well yielding zero binding at lowest s-state.

## Key numbers

- The binding energy of the \(^{10}\)Be + n + n + \(^{10}\)Be molecule at a core separation of \(R = 6\) fm is \(E_{\text{mol}} = -0.689 \pm 0.04\) MeV.
- At \(R = 8\) fm, \(E_{\text{mol}} = -0.238 \pm 0.02\) MeV.
- At \(R = 10\) fm, \(E_{\text{mol}} = -0.141 \pm 0.01\) MeV.
- At \(R = 12\) fm, \(E_{\text{mol}} = -0.0095 \pm 0.01\) MeV; the system becomes unbound for \(R \gtrsim 13\) fm.
- The neutron separation energy in \(^{11}\)Be is 0.5 MeV, and the healing distance of the halo neutron in \(^{11}\)Be is approximately 9 fm (from \(\sqrt{41.47\ \text{MeV fm}^2 / 0.5\ \text{MeV}}\)).
- The neutron-\(^{10}\)Be scattering length used is \(a_0 = 6.88\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the concept of Rydberg molecules from atomic physics (Greene et al., 2000; experimental verification by Ovel et al., 2009 and Vogt et al., 2009) to nuclear systems, proposing that halo nuclei such as \(^{11}\)Be can form covalent molecular bonds analogous to the \(H_2\) molecule. It builds on known quasimolecules in stable nuclei like \(^{12}\)C + \(^{12}\)C and on the physics of Borromean and Efimov states (Efimov, 1970; Ferlaino et al., 2009). The results suggest that such molecules might be observable in neutron-rich environments like stellar interiors or neutron star crusts, and could enable studies of clustering along the dripline, with implications for understanding dilute neutron systems such as the tetraneutron (Marqués et al., 2002; Bertulani and Zelevinsky, 2003; Kisamori et al., 2016).
