# A Variational Approach to Quantum Field Theory

> In strongly coupled field theories, perturbation theory cannot be employed to study the low-energy spectrum. Thus, non-perturbative techniques are required. We employ the variational method, a rigorous, non-perturbative approach which provides variational upper bounds on the energy eigenstates of the theory. An essential step in the variational method is the choice of trial wave function. In this work, we study the viability of employing a neural network as our variational ansatz. As a first step towards phenomenologically interesting strongly coupled theories like quantum chromodynamics, we study scalar field theories with quartic couplings.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that a neural-network-based variational ansatz can produce variational upper bounds on the ground state energy for scalar field theories with quartic couplings, but only as a first step toward phenomenologically interesting strongly coupled theories like quantum chromodynamics; the method is tested on scalar theories in d=0+1 and d=3+1 dimensions, not on gauge theories.

## Method
The authors employ the variational Monte Carlo (VMC) algorithm with a trial wave function constructed as the product of the non-interacting ground state wavefunction and a neural network with sigmoid activation functions. The neural network weights and biases serve as variational parameters, optimized by minimizing the expectation value of the Hamiltonian computed via Metropolis-Hastings sampling of field configurations distributed according to the squared trial wavefunction. The method is applied to the anharmonic oscillator (d=0+1) and to lattice-discretized ő¶‚Āī theory on a cubic lattice with L=4 sites per side (d=3+1).

## Context
This work extends the variational Monte Carlo approach, previously applied to quantum mechanical systems, to scalar field theories in 3+1 dimensions using neural network wavefunction ansatze. It builds on the universal approximation theorem for neural networks and on standard lattice Hamiltonian formulations from Gattringer (2010). The results enable future studies of excited states, optimization of the algorithm for lower computational cost, and eventual application to gauge theories such as quantum chromodynamics.
