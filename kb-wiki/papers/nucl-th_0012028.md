# Bifurcation in kinetic equation for interacting Fermi systems

> The finite duration of collisions appear as time-nonlocality in the kinetic equation. Analyzing the corresponding quantum kinetic equation for dense interacting Fermi systems a delay differential equation is obtained which combines time derivatives with finite time stepping known from the logistic mapping. The responsible delay time is explicitly calculated and discussed. As a novel feature oscillations in the time evolution of the distribution function itself appear and bifurcations up to chaotic behavior can occur. The temperature and density conditions are presented where such oscillations and bifurcations arise indicating an onset of phase transition.

# Bifurcation in kinetic equation for interacting Fermi systems

## Key claim

The nonlocal quantum kinetic equation for dense interacting Fermi systems can be reduced to a delay differential equation that exhibits oscillations, bifurcations, and chaotic behavior in the time evolution of the distribution function itself, but only within a specific temperature and density range where the delay time and relaxation time satisfy certain inequalities; this behavior is presented as a dynamical signal of an onsetting phase transition rather than a universal feature of all Fermi systems.

## Method

The author starts from a previously derived nonlocal quantum kinetic equation for quasiparticle distributions that includes finite collision duration via time-nonlocal corrections. By analyzing the zero-angle scattering channel (q=0) in the collision integral, which vanishes in local kinetic equations, and applying a relaxation time approximation, the kinetic equation is reduced to a delay differential equation of the form σ df/dt + f(t) = a f(t-τ_d)[1-f(t-τ_d)]. The parameters a, σ, and τ_d are then calculated explicitly for a separable potential model in terms of the scattering length a_0, potential range r_0, density, and temperature, and the conditions for bifurcation and chaos are derived from linear stability analysis.

## Key numbers

- The logistic map parameter a = (ε_f/T) × (12 c_2)/(π² c_1) × (β²/p_f²), where ε_f is Fermi energy, T is temperature, β is inverse potential range, p_f is Fermi momentum, and c_1, c_2 are range-dependent integrals of order unity
- The relaxation time parameter σ = (ħ ε_f/T²) × 3/(π c_1 s) × 1/a_s², where a_s = a_0 p_f/ħ is the dimensionless scattering length and s is the spin degeneracy
- The delay time τ_d = ħ/(4 ε_f) × c_3(a_s, β/p_f), where c_3 is a function given explicitly in the paper
- For nuclear matter with scattering length a_0 = 5.379 fm and β = 1.4488 ħ/fm, the temperature condition for oscillations is 0.118 ε_f > T > 0.0813 ħ ε_f/(fm × p_f), requiring Fermi momentum p_f > 2.24 ħ/fm (about four times nuclear matter density n_0 = 0.14 fm⁻³)
- The oscillation period 1/Ω ≈ 2 fm/c at temperatures around 10 MeV
- The critical condition for bifurcation onset is |a-2| >< √(1+(σΩ)²) for positive (negative) delay times respectively

## Context

This work extends the nonlocal kinetic theory developed by Spicka, Lipavsky, and Morawetz (1996, 1997) for dense Fermi systems, which covers all quantum virial corrections on the binary level and conserves density, momentum, and energy including correlated two-particle parts. The paper connects this microscopic quantum statistical approach to the phenomenology of deterministic chaos, showing that the delay differential equation interpolates between the logistic mapping of Feigenbaum (1978) and continuous time evolution, similar to models studied by Berezowski (2001) and Hopf bifurcations in mappings with memory by Hartwich and Fick (1993). The bifurcation behavior is interpreted as analogous to the relation between mean-field phase transitions and bifurcations of one-dimensional discrete maps found by Feigenbaum, Kadanoff, and Shenker (1987). This enables the prediction of specific temperature and density conditions under which the distribution function itself exhibits oscillatory and chaotic time evolution, potentially observable in heavy ion reactions.
