# The Maximal Entanglement Limit in Statistical and High Energy Physics

> These lectures advocate the idea that quantum entanglement provides a unifying foundation for both statistical physics and high-energy interactions. I argue that, at sufficiently long times or high energies, most quantum systems approach a Maximal Entanglement Limit (MEL) in which phases of quantum states become unobservable, reduced density matrices acquire a thermal form, and probabilistic descriptions emerge without invoking ergodicity or classical randomness. Within this framework, the emergence of probabilistic parton model, thermalization in the break-up of confining strings and in high-energy collisions, and the universal small $x$ behavior of structure functions arise as direct consequences of entanglement and geometry of high-dimensional Hilbert space.

## Key claim

The paper argues that at sufficiently long times or high energies, most quantum systems approach a Maximal Entanglement Limit (MEL) in which phases of quantum states become unobservable, reduced density matrices acquire a thermal form, and probabilistic descriptions emerge without invoking ergodicity or classical randomness; this claim is hedged by the qualifier that it applies to "most" rather than all quantum systems, and that integrable systems provide "sharp and physically illuminating exceptions" where integrability prevents full thermalization.

## Method

The paper develops a theoretical framework connecting quantum information theory, random matrix theory, and high-energy physics by applying Page's theorem and Wishart random matrix ensembles to describe reduced density matrices of subsystems in large Hilbert spaces. It uses the Coulomb-gas representation of random matrix eigenvalue distributions to show that typical pure states drawn from the Haar measure produce reduced density matrices close to maximally mixed states, with the joint eigenvalue distribution given by a Wishart unitary ensemble. The author then applies this framework to high-energy scattering by arguing that Lorentz time dilation and scale-invariant branching dynamics render relative phases between Fock components unobservable, leading to thermal-like reduced density matrices.

## Key numbers

* The average entanglement entropy of a subsystem of dimension m in an environment of dimension n (with n >> m) is given by ⟨S(ρ_A)⟩ = ln m - m/(2n) + O(1/n²), where ln m represents the maximal possible entropy (Page's asymptotic formula)
* The exact Page formula for average entanglement entropy is ⟨S(ρ_A)⟩ = H_{mn} - H_n - (m-1)/(2n), where H_ℓ are harmonic numbers
* In the large-dimension limit N_B >> N_A >> 1, the typical spacing between eigenvalues scales as δλ ~ 1/(N_A√N_B), causing the spectrum to cluster around the equiprobable value 1/N_A

## Context

This work extends the foundational ideas of von Neumann (1929) on quantum typicality and the Eigenstate Thermalization Hypothesis (ETH) developed by Deutsch (1991), Srednicki (1994), and Rigol (2007) by providing a unified entanglement-based framework for both statistical physics and high-energy QCD. It connects Page's theorem (1993) on typical entanglement in bipartite systems to Wigner's (1951) random matrix theory of nuclear level statistics, showing that the same mathematical structure governs both nuclear spectra and reduced density matrices. The framework enables reinterpretation of the parton model, thermalization in confining string break-up, and universal small-x behavior of structure functions as consequences of entanglement geometry rather than ergodic dynamics.
