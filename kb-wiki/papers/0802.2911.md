# Magnetization of a neutron plasma with Skyrme and Gogny forces in the presence of a strong magnetic field

> Some thermodynamical magnitudes of interest in a pure neutron plasma are studied within the framework of the non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approximation at finite density and temperature. We use Skyrme and Gogny forces to describe such a neutron plasma and study the main differences that arise in these two effective parametrizations of the nuclear interaction when a strong magnetic field induces a permanent magnetization in the gas. The existence of a non-zero permanent spin polarization in a neutron plasma is explored in the density-temperature parameter space. We find that for moderate temperatures and in the low density range up to densities $\approx 0.5ρ_0$ both parametrizations predict that as density decreases an increasingly strong magnetization is allowed. In the range $0.5 ρ_0 \lesssim ρ\lesssim 3 ρ_0$ there is an approximately constant polarization that can be as big as $\approx 12%$ for the maximum allowed interior magnetic field $B \approx 10^{18}$ G. For higher densities there is a dramatic difference in the polarization trend followed by Skyrme an Gogny forces. While the former predict a ferromagnetic phase transition, the Gogny forces prevent it keeping the magnetization below 5%.

## Key claim

In a pure neutron plasma described by non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory, a permanent spin polarization of up to approximately 12% is possible for magnetic fields up to 10^18 G, but only in the intermediate density range 0.5 ρ₀ to 3 ρ₀; at higher densities, Skyrme forces predict a ferromagnetic phase transition while Gogny forces prevent it, keeping magnetization below 5%.

## Method

The author computed the Helmholtz free energy per particle for a pure neutron plasma in the grand canonical ensemble using the non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approximation at finite density and temperature. Two Skyrme parametrizations (SLy4 and SLy7) and two Gogny parametrizations (D1S and D1P) were used to describe the nuclear interaction, with the single-particle energy including a magnetic field term μ_n σ B. The equilibrium spin polarization Δ was found by minimizing the free energy per particle with respect to Δ at fixed density, temperature, and magnetic field strength, solving self-consistently for the effective masses and chemical potentials.

## Key numbers

- Maximum interior magnetic field considered: B_max ≈ 10^18 G, derived from the scalar virial theorem for a typical neutron star with M = 1.5 M_⊙ and R = 10^−5 R_⊙.
- In the density range 0.5 ρ₀ ≲ ρ ≲ 3 ρ₀, the maximum permanent polarization is ≈ 12% for B ≈ 10^18 G at moderate temperatures.
- For densities above 3 ρ₀, Skyrme forces show a ferromagnetic transition at ≈ 4 ρ₀ (at T = 2 MeV with SLy4, even at B = 0), while Gogny forces keep magnetization below 5%.
- For magnetic field strengths below B ≈ 10^16 G, the effect on plasma polarization is almost negligible.
- At ρ = 0.5 ρ₀ and T = 5 MeV, the polarization rises from negligible values below B = 10^16 G to ≈ 12% at B_max.
- At ρ = ρ₀, T = 10 MeV, and B = 10^18 G, all four models (SLy4, SLy7, D1S, D1P) predict Δ_min ≈ 12%.
- For the Gogny D1P model at T = 5 MeV and B = 5 × 10^17 G, the high-density plateau shows a permanent residual polarization of about 2%.
- The effective neutron mass shift due to polarization is δm* ≈ 5 MeV at ρ = ρ₀ and δm* ≈ 10 MeV at ρ = 0.5 ρ₀ for the maximum B considered.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of magnetized nuclear plasmas using Skyrme forces (e.g., Prakash et al.) by performing a direct comparison with Gogny forces, which are known to avoid the spurious ferromagnetic transition that Skyrme models exhibit at high densities even without magnetic fields (Fantoni et al.). The results are relevant for understanding the microphysics of neutron star crusts and proto-neutron star cooling, particularly the possible formation of spin-polarized "pasta" phases that could affect neutrino opacities. The author notes that below 0.5 ρ₀, the gas approximation may break down and explicit treatment of clustered phases (pasta phases) is needed, as studied by previous authors.
