# On the nucleon instability of heavy oxygen isotopes

> The instability of the $^{26}$O nucleus with respect to decay through the two-neutron channel is investigated. It is shown that this isotope, unobserved in the fragmentation experiments, can exist as a narrow resonance in the system $^{24}$O+2n. A role of deformation in formation of the neutron-drip line in the region $N\sim 20$ is discussed.

# On the nucleon instability of heavy oxygen isotopes

## Key claim

The paper argues that the unobserved isotope $^{26}$O can exist as a narrow resonance in the $^{24}$O+2n system with a width on the order of $10^{-3}$ MeV, despite being unstable against two-neutron emission. The authors qualify that their calculation is an estimate of the width by order of magnitude only, not a precise prediction, because the effective pairing interaction parameters fitted for heavier nuclei near stability cannot be directly applied to this neutron-rich system.

## Method

The authors model $^{26}$O as a quasistationary state of the $^{24}$O+2n system using the Green's function method for two neutrons in the mean field of $^{24}$O. They expand the two-neutron wave function in a basis of $s_{1/2}$ and $d_{3/2}$ single-particle continuum states, using factorized wave functions with resonant enhancement factors for the $d_{3/2}$ quasistationary state and the weakly bound $2s_{1/2}$ state. The energy and width of the quasistationary state are found by solving an algebraic equation derived from the two-particle Schrödinger equation, with the effective pairing interaction treated phenomenologically.

## Key numbers

- The one-neutron separation energy for $^{24}$O is $S_n = 3.6$ MeV and the two-neutron separation energy is $S_{2n} = 5.9$ MeV, from calculations in Zverev et al. (1995).
- The estimated one-neutron separation energy for $^{26}$O is $S_n \simeq 0.7$ MeV, indicating stability against one-neutron emission.
- The quasistationary state of $^{26}$O is located at energy $E_0 \sim 0.1$ MeV with width $\Gamma \sim 10^{-3}$ MeV.
- The corresponding lifetime is $\tau \sim \hbar/\Gamma \sim 10^{-18}$ s, which is much longer than the nuclear timescale $\tau_{nucl} \sim 10^{-22}$ s.
- For the $^{24}$O nucleus, the $2s_{1/2}$ state is the shallowest bound state and the $1d_{3/2}$ state is the lowest quasistationary state.
- The pairing gap in this region is $\Delta_n \sim 2\div 3$ MeV.
- Deformation with $\beta_2 > 0.1$ could increase the binding energy of $^{26}$O by $\sim 1$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical studies of $^{26}$O by Zverev et al. (1995) using the self-consistent theory of finite Fermi systems, which found $S_n \simeq 0.7$ MeV and established the possibility of pure two-neutron instability. The paper addresses the experimental non-observation of $^{26}$O in fragmentation experiments at GANIL (Guillemaud-Mueller et al., 1990), Michigan State (Fauerbach et al., 1996), and RIKEN (Sakurai et al., 1996). The authors discuss how deformation can shift the neutron-drip line, drawing on the scenario proposed by Detraz and Viera (1989) for heavy sodium isotopes, and suggest that the observed stability of $^{31}$F may be explained by deformation effects. The narrow width predicted for the $^{26}$O resonance could enable its detection in correlation experiments measuring di-neutron emission.
