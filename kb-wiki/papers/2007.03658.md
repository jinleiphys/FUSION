# Cross sections for coherent elastic and inelastic neutrino-nucleus scattering

> The prospects of extracting new physics signals in coherent elastic neutrino--nucleus scattering (CE$ν$NS) processes are limited by the precision with which the underlying nuclear structure physics, embedded in the weak nuclear form factor, is known. We present calculations of charge and weak nuclear form factors and CE$ν$NS cross sections on $^{12}$C, $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ar, $^{56}$Fe and $^{208}$Pb nuclei. We obtain the proton and neutron densities, and charge and weak form factors by solving Hartree--Fock (HF) equations with a Skyrme (SkE2) nuclear potential. We validate our approach by comparing $^{208}$Pb and $^{40}$Ar charge form factor predictions with available elastic electron scattering data. Since CE$ν$NS experiments at stopped--pion sources are also well suited to measure inelastic charged--current and neutral--current neutrino--nucleus cross sections, we also present calculations for these processes, incorporating a continuum Random Phase Approximation (CRPA) description on top of the HF-SkE2 picture of the nucleus. Providing both coherent as well as inelastic cross sections in a consistent framework, we aim at obtaining a reliable and detailed comparison of the strength of these processes in the energy region below ~100 MeV. Furthermore, we attempt to gauge the level of theoretical uncertainty pertaining to the description of the $^{40}$Ar form factor and CE$ν$NS cross sections by comparing relative differences between recent microscopic nuclear theory and widely--used phenomenological form factor predictions. Future precision measurements of CE$ν$NS will potentially help in constraining these nuclear structure details that will in turn improve prospects of extracting new physics.

## Key claim
The precision of new physics searches using coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering (CEvNS) is limited by nuclear structure uncertainties in the weak form factor; the authors provide microscopic Hartree-Fock calculations with a Skyrme (SkE2) potential for multiple nuclei, but note that future precision measurements will be needed to constrain these nuclear structure details.

## Method
The authors solve Hartree-Fock (HF) equations with a Skyrme (SkE2) nuclear potential to obtain single-nucleon wave functions, from which proton and neutron density distributions are computed for 12C, 16O, 40Ar, 56Fe, and 208Pb. Charge and weak nuclear form factors are derived from these densities, and the approach is validated by comparing charge form factor predictions with elastic electron scattering data for 208Pb and 40Ar. For inelastic charged-current and neutral-current cross sections on 40Ar, a continuum Random Phase Approximation (CRPA) is applied on top of the HF-SkE2 ground state to incorporate long-range correlations.

## Key numbers
- CEvNS cross section on 208Pb is roughly two to three orders of magnitude larger than on 12C, reflecting the approximate N^2 scaling of the weak charge.
- For neutrino energies below about 52 MeV (pion decay-at-rest energies), the CEvNS cross section is roughly two orders of magnitude larger than inelastic quasielastic cross sections.
- The first minimum of the charge form factor occurs at q ~ 1.8 fm^-1 for 12C and at q ~ 0.65 fm^-1 for 208Pb.
- The PREX collaboration measured the weak form factor of 208Pb at a single momentum transfer of q = 0.475 fm^-1.
- The HF-SkE2 charge form factor for 40Ar agrees with elastic electron scattering data for q less than about 2 fm^-1.

## Context
This work extends microscopic nuclear structure calculations for CEvNS beyond the phenomenological Klein-Nystrand and Helm form factors used by the COHERENT collaboration (2017, 2018, 2019, 2020, 2021), and provides a consistent framework for both elastic and inelastic cross sections. The results enable improved interpretation of CEvNS measurements at stopped-pion sources like the Spallation Neutron Source and the European Spallation Source, and support supernova neutrino detection capabilities for future liquid-argon detectors such as DUNE and CCM. The authors compare their 40Ar results with coupled-cluster calculations by Payne (2019) and relativistic mean-field predictions by Yang (2019).
