# From finite nuclei to the nuclear liquid drop: leptodermous expansion based on the self-consistent mean-field theory

> The parameters of the nuclear liquid drop model, such as the volume, surface, symmetry, and curvature constants, as well as bulk radii, are extracted from the non-relativistic and relativistic energy density functionals used in microscopic calculations for finite nuclei. The microscopic liquid drop energy, obtained self-consistently for a large sample of finite, spherical nuclei, has been expanded in terms of powers of A^{-1/3} (or inverse nuclear radius) and the isospin excess (or neutron-to-proton asymmetry). In order to perform a reliable extrapolation in the inverse radius, the calculations have been carried out for nuclei with huge numbers of nucleons, of the order of 10^6. The Coulomb interaction has been ignored to be able to approach nuclei of arbitrary sizes and to avoid radial instabilities characteristic of systems with very large atomic numbers. The main contribution to the fluctuating part of the binding energy has been removed using the Green's function method to calculate the shell correction. The limitations of applying the leptodermous expansion to actual nuclei are discussed. While the leading terms in the macroscopic energy expansion can be extracted very precisely, the higher-order, isospin-dependent terms are prone to large uncertainties due to finite-size effects.

## Key claim

The macroscopic liquid-drop model parameters (volume, surface, symmetry, curvature, and surface-symmetry coefficients) can be reliably extracted from self-consistent mean-field calculations for finite nuclei, but the higher-order isospin-dependent terms (particularly the surface-symmetry coefficient) are subject to large uncertainties due to finite-size effects and residual shell fluctuations, especially in the relativistic mean-field models where uncertainties reach 10 MeV.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) and relativistic mean-field (RMF) calculations for a large sample of spherical nuclei, including systems with up to 10^6 nucleons, with the Coulomb interaction switched off to avoid radial instabilities. They subtracted shell corrections using a Green's function method to obtain smooth binding energies, then fitted the leptodermous expansion in powers of A^{-1/3} and isospin excess I^2 to extract the liquid-drop model coefficients. The surface and curvature coefficients were obtained from the effective surface-energy coefficient for isospin-symmetric nuclei, while the surface-symmetry coefficient was extracted from the reduced effective symmetry-energy coefficient averaged over I between 0.1 and 0.2.

## Key numbers

- Volume energy coefficient a_vol: ranges from -15.748 MeV (BSk6) to -16.425 MeV (NL1) across the functionals studied
- Surface energy coefficient a_surf: ranges from 17.3 MeV (SkO, BSk6) to 18.8 MeV (NL1), with theoretical uncertainty of 0.05 MeV for SHF and 0.1 MeV for RMF
- Curvature energy coefficient a_curv: ranges from 7 MeV (NL3) to 10 MeV (BSk6, SLy6, NL-Z2), with uncertainty of about 0.5 MeV
- Symmetry energy coefficient a_sym: ranges from 27.81 MeV (BSk1) to 43.48 MeV (NL1)
- Surface-symmetry coefficient a_ssym: ranges from -33 MeV (BSk6) to values below -125 MeV (NL-Z), with uncertainty of about 2 MeV for SHF and 10 MeV for RMF
- Saturation density rho_0: ranges from 0.1482 fm^{-3} (NL3) to 0.1625 fm^{-3} (SkP)
- Incompressibility K: ranges from 172.4 MeV (NL-Z2) to 271.7 MeV (NL3)

## Context

This work extends previous attempts to characterize nuclear energy-density functionals through macroscopic parameters, going beyond the standard approach of using semi-infinite nuclear matter calculations (e.g., Pearson (1994), Samyn, and Eifner (1994)) which indicated that quantum effects change surface parameters by about 5%. The authors build on the droplet model formalism of Myers (1974) and the liquid-drop model expansions reviewed by Moller (1995) and Pomorski (2002). The results enable a systematic connection between microscopic energy-density functionals and macroscopic nuclear properties, allowing future work to correlate spectroscopic features (such as effective nucleon mass) with macroscopic parameters and to assess the reliability of higher-order terms in the leptodermous expansion for actual finite nuclei.
