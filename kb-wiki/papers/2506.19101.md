# Interferometry Radii at RHIC BES Energies within the integrated HydroKinetic Model

> This study presents a correlation femtoscopy analysis of relativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC Beam Energy Scan (BES) energies using the extended integrated HydroKinetic Model (iHKMe). The model combines initial conditions from UrQMD transport simulations, a gradual pre-equilibrium relaxation stage, viscous hydrodynamic evolution, and a hadronic cascade, providing a comprehensive description of the system's full dynamical evolution. Special emphasis is placed on the impact of extended nuclear overlap times and partial thermalization, which are characteristic of intermediate collision energies (sqrt(s_NN) = 7.7-39 GeV). The corresponding correlation femtoscopy scales, or interferometry (HBT) radii, are calculated across a range of collision energies and compared with experimental data to assess the sensitivity of femtoscopic observables to the equation of state (EoS). Two EoS scenarios are explored. The model demonstrates good agreement with experimental femtoscopy measurements, particularly at lower BES energies. These results enhance our understanding of the space-time structure of the particle-emitting source and provide valuable insight into the nature of the QCD phase transition in the intermediate energy regime.

## Key claim

The extended integrated HydroKinetic Model (iHKMe) reproduces experimental femtoscopic radii across RHIC BES energies (7.7-39 GeV), with the crossover equation of state providing a slightly better overall description than the first-order phase transition EoS, though the first-order EoS performance improves at lower collision energies and may become more accurate at energies below 7.7 GeV.

## Method

The authors used the extended integrated HydroKinetic Model (iHKMe) which combines UrQMD transport for initial state generation, a gradual pre-equilibrium relaxation stage with a weight-function interpolation between UrQMD and hydrodynamic tensors, viscous hydrodynamic evolution with shear viscosity, particlization via the Cooper-Frye prescription on a constant energy density hypersurface, and a final hadronic cascade in UrQMD. For each of six collision energies (7.7, 11.5, 14.5, 19.6, 27, and 39 GeV), they performed 150 hydrodynamic simulations with 200 UrQMD afterburner events each, totaling 30,000 events per parameter set, selecting the 5% most central Au+Au collisions. They extracted three-dimensional interferometry radii (R_out, R_side, R_long) from Gaussian fits to pion correlation functions in the LCMS system as a function of transverse mass.

## Key numbers

- Collision energies studied: sqrt(s_NN) = 7.7, 11.5, 14.5, 19.6, 27, and 39 GeV
- Nuclear overlap time range: tau_overlap approximately 1-4 fm/c
- For 7.7 GeV with first-order phase transition EoS: R = 0.5 fm, tau_0 = 2.6 fm/c, tau_th = 3.2 fm/c, eta/s = 0.08, epsilon_sw = 0.35 GeV/fm^3
- For 7.7 GeV with crossover EoS: R = 0.5 fm, tau_0 = 2.6 fm/c, tau_th = 3.6 fm/c, eta/s = 0.14, epsilon_sw = 0.50 GeV/fm^3
- For 39 GeV with crossover EoS: R = 0.5 fm, tau_0 = 0.6 fm/c, tau_th = 1.4 fm/c, eta/s = 0.14, epsilon_sw = 0.50 GeV/fm^3
- The long HBT radius shows an increasing trend with collision energy, while side and out radii show minimal energy dependence
- The model overestimates the increase of long radii with energy compared to STAR experimental data

## Context

This work extends the integrated HydroKinetic Model (iHKM), previously successful at top RHIC and LHC energies (Shapoval 2020), to lower RHIC Beam Energy Scan energies using the recently extended version iHKMe (Adzhymambetov 2024). It builds on prior femtoscopy studies including EPOS-based simulations (Stefaniak 2017), THERMINATOR-based statistical studies (Brzezinski 2016), and work by Li et al. (2023) on HBT radii at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.4-7.7 GeV. The results enable further discrimination between crossover and first-order phase transition scenarios in the QCD phase diagram, particularly suggesting that measurements at even lower energies (2-4 GeV) at future FAIR experiments could reveal signatures of a first-order phase transition.
