# Uncertainty decomposition method and its application in the liquid drop model

> A method is suggested to decompose the statistical and systematic uncertainties from the residues between the calculation of a theoretical model and the observed data. The residues and the parameters of the model can be obtained through the standard statistical fitting procedures. The present work concentrates on the decomposition of the total uncertainty, of which the distribution corresponds to that of the residues. The distribution of the total uncertainty is considered as two normal distributions, statistical and systematic uncertainties. The standard deviation of the statistical part, σstat, is estimated through random parameters distributed around their best fitted values. The two normal distributions are obtained by minimizing the moments of the distribution of the residues with the fixed σstat. The method is applied to the liquid drop model (LD). The statistical and systematic uncertainties are decomposed from the residues of the nuclear binding energies with and without the consideration of the shell effect in LD. The estimated distributions of the statistical and systematic uncertainties can well describe that of the residues. The normal assumption of the distribution of the statistical and systematic uncertainties is examined through various approaches. The comparison between the distributions of the specific nuclei and those of the statistical and systematic uncertainties are consistent with the physical considerations, although the latter two can be obtained without the knowledge of these considerations. Such as, the LD are more suitable to describe the heavy nuclei. The light and heavy nuclei are indeed distributed mostly inside the distributions of the statistical and systematic uncertainties, respectively. The similar situation are also found for the nuclei close to and far from shell. The present method is also performed to nuclei around stability line.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a practical method to decompose the total uncertainty of a theoretical model into statistical and systematic components by assuming both follow normal distributions, but the method works best when the model's deficiencies are small and the sample size is large; the normal assumption for the systematic uncertainty is "not exactly" valid for models with large deficiencies like the liquid drop model without shell corrections.

## Method

The uncertainty decomposition method (UDM) fits a theoretical model to experimental data using standard statistical fitting to obtain parameter values and their uncertainties. The statistical uncertainty's standard deviation is estimated by randomly generating parameters from normal distributions centered at the best-fit values and computing the variance of the resulting model predictions. The systematic uncertainty's mean and standard deviation are then obtained by minimizing the difference between the third and fourth moments of the residue distribution and those of a mixture of two normal distributions, with the statistical variance fixed from the random parameter simulation.

## Key numbers

- Total uncertainty (standard deviation of residues) for LD6 fitted to AME2012: 2.52 MeV
- Total uncertainty (standard deviation of residues) for LD8 fitted to AME2012: 1.41 MeV
- Statistical variance for LD6 (AME2012): 1.93 MeV^2 (from m_stat=1.05 MeV, sigma_stat=1.66 MeV)
- Systematic variance for LD6 (AME2012): 4.45 MeV^2 (from m_syst=-0.88 MeV, sigma_syst=2.85 MeV)
- Statistical variance for LD8 (AME2012): 0.78 MeV^2 (from m_stat=0.91 MeV, sigma_stat=0.86 MeV)
- Systematic variance for LD8 (AME2012): 1.20 MeV^2 (from m_syst=-0.96 MeV, sigma_syst=1.22 MeV)
- Number of nuclei used from AME2012: 2302 (with experimental uncertainty smaller than 0.2 MeV)
- For unmeasured neutron-rich nuclei in LD8: estimated sigma_stat = 1.01 MeV, variance = 0.92 MeV^2

## Context

This work extends the discussion of uncertainty decomposition in nuclear models, following the framework of Dobaczewski et al. (2014) which identified statistical and systematic components of model uncertainty. The method is applied to the liquid drop model (LD), a simple macroscopic model originally developed by Myers and Swiatecki (1966), and compared with versions including an illustrative shell correction term. The UDM enables estimation of uncertainties for unmeasured nuclei, particularly neutron-rich and superheavy nuclei, and suggests that neutron separation energies are better suited for such predictions than binding energies due to smaller systematic uncertainties.
