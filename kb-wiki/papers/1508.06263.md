# Nuclear Mass Predictions for the Crustal Composition of Neutron Stars: A Bayesian Neural Network Approach

> Besides their intrinsic nuclear-structure value, nuclear mass models are essential for astrophysical applications, such as r-process nucleosynthesis and neutron-star structure. To overcome the intrinsic limitations of existing "state-of-the-art" mass models, we propose a refinement based on a Bayesian Neural Network (BNN) formalism. A novel BNN approach is implemented with the goal of optimizing mass residuals between theory and experiment. A significant improvement (of about 40%) in the mass predictions of existing models is obtained after BNN refinement. Moreover, these improved results are now accompanied by proper statistical errors. Finally, by constructing a "world average" of these predictions, a mass model is obtained that is used to predict the composition of the outer crust of a neutron star. The power of the Bayesian neural network method has been successfully demonstrated by a systematic improvement in the accuracy of the predictions of nuclear masses. Extension to other nuclear observables is a natural next step that is currently under investigation.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a Bayesian Neural Network (BNN) refinement of existing nuclear mass models yields a significant improvement of about 40% in mass predictions, but this improvement is demonstrated specifically for 290 nuclei of relevance to the outer crust of a neutron star (Z=20-50 region), not for the entire nuclear chart.

## Method

The authors train a feed-forward Bayesian Neural Network with 40 hidden nodes on the mass residuals between experimental masses from the AME2012 compilation and theoretical predictions from several mass models (liquid-drop model, Duflo-Zuker, Moller-Nix, FRDM, HFB19, and HFB21). The training set consists of 1800 randomly selected nuclei between ⁴⁰Ca and ²⁴⁰U, with 791 nuclei reserved for validation. The BNN is used to model the small and fluctuating component of nuclear masses following Strutinsky's energy theorem, and the resulting mass formula is applied to predict the composition of the outer crust of a neutron star by minimizing the chemical potential at fixed pressure.

## Key numbers

- Root-mean-square deviation for the liquid-drop model on 290 crust-relevant nuclei: 3.359 MeV before BNN refinement, reduced to 0.556 MeV after BNN refinement (an 83.5% improvement)
- Root-mean-square deviation for the Duflo-Zuker model on the same 290 nuclei: 0.526 MeV before BNN refinement, reduced to 0.303 MeV after BNN refinement (a 42.4% improvement)
- Root-mean-square deviation for FRDM on the same 290 nuclei: 0.861 MeV before BNN refinement, reduced to 0.460 MeV after BNN refinement (a 46.6% improvement)
- Root-mean-square deviation for HFB19 on the same 290 nuclei: 0.880 MeV before BNN refinement, reduced to 0.524 MeV after BNN refinement (a 40.5% improvement)
- Root-mean-square deviation for HFB21 on the same 290 nuclei: 0.816 MeV before BNN refinement, reduced to 0.555 MeV after BNN refinement (a 32.0% improvement)
- The BNN model uses 161 parameters (1+4H with H=40 hidden nodes) trained on 1800 nuclei
- The outer crust composition is computed up to a density of about 4×10¹¹ g/cm³ where neutron drip occurs

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian Neural Network approach to nuclear mass systematics, building on earlier neural network work by Athanassopoulos (2003) and the general Bayesian framework of Neal (1996). The method is inspired by Strutinsky's energy theorem (1967), which separates nuclear binding energy into a large smooth component and a small fluctuating component. The authors compare their BNN-refined results against five established mass models: the microscopic-macroscopic models of Duflo and Zuker (1995), Moller and Nix (1988), the Finite Range Droplet Model (Moller 1993), and the microscopic HFB19 and HFB21 models (Goriely 2010). The approach enables improved predictions for neutron-rich nuclei relevant to neutron star crust composition, a problem previously studied by Roca Maza (2008, 2011) and Baym (1971), where mass model uncertainties significantly affect the predicted sequence of nuclei in the outer crust.
