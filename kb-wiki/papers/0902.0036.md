# CN-Cycle Neutrinos and Solar Metalicity

> We discuss the current Standard Solar Model conflict between helioseismology and photospheric abundances, a speculation that connects this anomaly to formation of the gaseous giant planets, and a possible neutrino measurement to directly test solar core metalicity.

# CN-Cycle Neutrinos and Solar Metalicity

## Key claim
The Standard Solar Model conflict between helioseismology and photospheric abundances may be explained by metal sequestration during gaseous giant planet formation, and a future measurement of CN-cycle neutrinos could directly test the Sun’s core metalicity with sufficient precision to distinguish between the old (GS98) and new (AGS05) abundance scales.

## Method
The authors compare Standard Solar Model predictions using the older GS98 (1998) photospheric abundances (Z = 0.0169) against the newer AGS05 (2005) 3D-model abundances (Z = 0.0122), examining the resulting sound-speed discrepancies with helioseismology. They then propose a speculative mechanism where planetary formation scrubs metals from the proto-solar gas cloud, creating a two-zone Sun with a metal-poor convective envelope, and derive a correlation between the measured 8B neutrino flux and the expected CN neutrino flux that can isolate the core C+N abundance from environmental temperature effects.

## Key numbers
- Photospheric metalicity Z changed from 0.0169 (GS98) to 0.0122 (AGS05), a reduction of about 28%
- The change from GS98 to AGS05 abundances lowers the predicted 8B neutrino flux from 5.95 to 4.72 × 10^6 /cm^2 s
- The SNO NCD-phase measured 8B flux is 5.54 ± 0.32 ± 0.35 × 10^6 /cm^2 s
- The metal content difference between GS98 and AGS05 in the convective zone is 50 Earth masses (M_⊕)
- Planetary formation concentrates approximately 40–90 M_⊕ of metals in the gaseous giant planets
- The predicted 15O neutrino flux from the BPS08(GS) SSM is (2.20^{+0.73}_{-0.63}) × 10^8 /cm^2 s
- The overall theoretical uncertainty in relating a future CN neutrino flux measurement to core metalicity is about 9.6%
- Recent changes in core metalicity between abundance scales are approximately 30%

## Context
This work addresses the conflict between helioseismology and the new photospheric abundance determinations from Asplund, Grevesse, and Sauval (2005), which used 3D photosphere models and destroyed the previous agreement between Standard Solar Models and helioseismology that existed with the Grevesse and Sauval (1998) abundances. The authors extend earlier discussions of this discrepancy by speculating that planetary formation (specifically core accretion models for Jupiter and Saturn, with metal enrichments of 3–7 times solar from Guillot’s work) could have depleted metals from the solar convective zone. The paper enables a new observational test: future deep scintillator experiments such as SNO+, Borexino, or Hanohano could measure CN-cycle neutrinos to determine core metalicity independently, using the correlation with 8B neutrinos to reduce environmental uncertainties.
