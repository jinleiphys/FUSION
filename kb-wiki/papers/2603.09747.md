# Architecture as physical prior: cooperative neural network for nuclear masses

> Machine learning approaches to nuclear mass prediction have achieved remarkable accuracy, but typically rely on existing theoretical baselines or hand-crafted physics features. Here we demonstrate that these prerequisites can be supplanted by structural inductive biases embedded directly in the network architecture. We present the Cooperative Neural Network (CoNN), which predicts binding energies from raw proton and neutron numbers (Z,N) alone by additively combining four structurally constrained modules: a smooth network for bulk liquid-drop trends, discrete scalar embeddings for shell effects, a learnable two-dimensional grid for regional collective correlations, and a parity-aware network for odd--even staggering. On the AME2020 dataset, the CoNN achieves a root-mean-square deviation of 0.269 MeV across all 3558 nuclei, with 0.419 MeV on a held-out interpolation subset and 0.728 MeV on 122 nuclei newly measured since AME2016, placing it among the most accurate baseline-free approaches to direct mass prediction. Notably, the learned embeddings develop pronounced extrema at canonical magic numbers and the pairing module reproduces the expected odd--even staggering along isotopic chains, both emerging from the data without explicit supervision. These results demonstrate that physically motivated architectural constraints can effectively substitute for feature engineering, establishing architecture as physical prior as a promising paradigm for neural-network mass modeling.

## Key claim

The Cooperative Neural Network (CoNN) predicts nuclear binding energies from raw proton and neutron numbers alone, achieving accuracy comparable to models that require hand-crafted physics features, by embedding the macroscopic-microscopic decomposition directly into the network architecture rather than through feature engineering.

## Method

The CoNN additively combines four structurally constrained modules: a fully connected network with a narrow bottleneck for smooth bulk liquid-drop trends, discrete scalar embeddings indexed by Z and N for shell effects, a learnable two-dimensional grid with bilinear interpolation for regional collective correlations, and a parity-aware multilayer perceptron using modulo operations for odd-even staggering. An alternating training protocol first trains the macroscopic branch alone to establish a smooth baseline, then freezes it while training microscopic modules on the residual, with a 10:1 learning-rate ratio favoring faster microscopic adaptation. The model uses only (Z,N) as input, with approximately 7.4 × 10⁴ parameters, and is trained on AME2020 binding energies with an 80:20 temporal split separating AME2016 nuclei from 122 newly measured nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Overall RMSD of 0.269 MeV across all 3558 nuclei in AME2020
- RMSD of 0.419 MeV on the 20% held-out interpolation subset (688 nuclei)
- RMSD of 0.728 MeV on 122 nuclei newly measured since AME2016 (extrapolation set)
- RMSD of 0.237 MeV on the AME2016-overlap set (3436 nuclei)
- Extrapolation RMSD of 0.631 MeV on 12 superheavy nuclei (Z=111-115), compared to 2.444 MeV for FRDM2012 and 1.295 MeV for WS4 on the same set
- Derived quantity RMSDs: S_n = 0.317 MeV (3317 nuclei), S_2n = 0.316 MeV (3199 nuclei), S_p = 0.331 MeV (3257 nuclei), Q_alpha = 0.294 MeV (3297 nuclei), Q_beta- = 0.360 MeV (3141 nuclei)
- Parameter-matched plain MLP baseline achieves RMSD of 0.836 MeV on all 3558 nuclei and 1.232 MeV on extrapolation set, versus CoNN values of 0.269 and 0.728 MeV

## Context

This work extends the macroscopic-microscopic decomposition of Strutinsky (1967) and Brack (1972) into a neural network architecture, contrasting with residual-correction approaches like Niu (2022) that require theoretical baselines, and with feature-engineering approaches like Zeng (2024) that supplement (Z,N) with hand-crafted inputs such as parity indicators and distances to magic numbers. The CoNN achieves accuracy close to ANN7 (Zeng 2024, 0.200 MeV RMSD with 7 features) and KAN-11 (Liu 2025, 0.260 MeV with 11 features) while using only two inputs, demonstrating that architectural constraints can substitute for feature engineering. The approach enables physically interpretable decomposition of the mass surface without explicit supervision, and the modular framework could be extended to jointly predict other nuclear observables such as charge radii and beta-decay properties.
