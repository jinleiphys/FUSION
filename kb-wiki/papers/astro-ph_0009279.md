# Strange Pathways for Black Hole Formation

> Immediately after they are born, neutron stars are characterized by an entropy per baryon of order unity and by the presence of trapped neutrinos. If the only hadrons in the star are nucleons, these effects slightly reduce the maximum mass relative to cold, catalyzed matter. However, if stangeness-bearing hyperons, a kaon condensate, or quarks are also present, these effects result in an increase in the maximum mass of up to $\sim 0.3{\rm M}_{\odot}$ compared to that of a cold, neutrino-free star. This makes a sufficiently massive proto-neutron star metastable, so that after a delay of 10--100 seconds, the PNS collapses into a black hole. Such an event might be straightforward to observe as an abrupt cessation of neutrinos when the instability is triggered.

# Strange Pathways for Black Hole Formation

## Key claim

If strangeness-bearing hyperons, a kaon condensate, or quarks are present in a proto-neutron star, the maximum mass can increase by up to ~0.3 solar masses compared to a cold, neutrino-free star, making sufficiently massive proto-neutron stars metastable and causing them to collapse into a black hole after a delay of 10–100 seconds. The abstract's claim of an "abrupt cessation of neutrinos" is qualified by the paper's finding that this signal is only observable with sufficiently massive detectors (such as SNO or SuperK) for stars closer than about 10 kpc, and that the collapse timing depends strongly on which form of strangeness appears.

## Method

The paper reviews and synthesizes results from detailed numerical simulations of proto-neutron star evolution during the Kelvin-Helmholtz epoch, using equations of state that include finite temperature and neutrino trapping effects. The simulations track the evolution of central baryon density, lepton fraction, and composition (including hyperons, kaon condensates, and quark matter) for stars of varying baryonic masses, employing Gibbs' rules for phase equilibrium to describe mixed phases. Neutrino transport is treated with a diffusion scheme to compute luminosities and time scales.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass increase of up to ~0.3 solar masses for proto-neutron stars containing strangeness-bearing components compared to cold, neutrino-free stars
- Mass windows for hyperonic metastable models range from baryon masses 1.7 to 2.0 solar masses
- Lifetimes of metastable stars containing kaon condensates lie in the narrow range 40–70 seconds, decreasing mildly with increasing baryon mass
- Minimum lifetime for metastable stars with kaons is about 40 seconds, determined by the binary pulsar mass constraint limiting gravitational mass to a minimum of 1.44 solar masses
- For SN 1987A, the neutrino signal over 10–15 seconds is consistent with either a stable PNS or a metastable PNS containing hyperons with mass less than about 0.1 solar masses below the maximum mass for cold, catalyzed hyperonic matter
- In SNO, about 400 counts are expected for electron antineutrinos from a supernova at 8.5 kpc; in SuperK, up to 6000 events are expected under the same conditions
- The total neutrino energy luminosity at the end of simulations is below 10^51 erg/s for all models
- The unshocked core mass immediately following core bounce is about 0.7 solar masses

## Context

This work extends the proto-neutron star evolution calculations of Burrows (1986, 1990) and Prakash (1997) by specifically examining how the presence of strangeness in the form of hyperons, kaon condensates, or quarks affects the maximum mass and stability of proto-neutron stars. The paper builds on the simulations of Pons et al. (1999, 2000) for hyperonic and kaon-condensed matter, and of Steiner et al. (2000) for quark matter, to show that the neutrino signal from a galactic supernova could distinguish between different forms of strangeness-induced collapse. The results enable future neutrino detectors to potentially identify the composition of dense matter by observing the timing of an abrupt cessation of the neutrino signal, with collapse before 40 seconds favoring hyperon or quark instabilities over kaon condensation.
