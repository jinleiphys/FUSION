# Implementation of the CRPA model in the GENIE event generator and analysis of nuclear effects in low-energy transfer neutrino-nucleus interactions

> We present the implementation and validation of the Hartree-Fock continuum random phase approximation (HF-CRPA) model in the GENIE neutrino-nucleus interaction event generator and a comparison of the subsequent predictions to experimental measurements of lepton kinematics from interactions with no mesons in the final state. These predictions are also compared to those of other models available in GENIE. It is shown that, with respect to these models, HF-CRPA predicts a significantly different evolution of the cross section when moving between different interaction targets, when considering incoming anti-neutrinos compared to neutrinos and when changing neutrino energies. These differences are most apparent for interactions with low energy and momentum transfer. It is also clear that the impact of nucleon correlations within the HF-CRPA framework is very different than in GENIE's standard implementation of RPA corrections. Since many neutrino oscillation experiments rely on their input model to extrapolate between targets, flavours, and neutrino energies, the newly implemented HF-CRPA model provides a useful means to verify that such differences between models are appropriately covered in oscillation analysis systematic error budgets.

## Key claim

The Hartree-Fock continuum random phase approximation (HF-CRPA) model, implemented in the GENIE event generator, predicts significantly different cross-section evolution across targets, neutrino/anti-neutrino flavors, and energies compared to other GENIE models, with differences most apparent at low energy and momentum transfer; however, the implementation uses a factorisation approach that only preserves the model's inclusive lepton kinematics, and the hadron kinematics are added using approximate methods rather than the full microscopic theory.

## Method

The HF-CRPA model was implemented in GENIE using a factorisation approach where hadron tensor look-up tables (separated into vector-vector, axial-axial, and vector-axial contributions) provide the double-differential cross section as a function of outgoing lepton kinematics only, while hadron kinematics are generated using a local Fermi gas spectral function with a momentum-transfer-dependent removal energy. Separate tables were provided for carbon, oxygen, and argon targets, for both neutrinos and anti-neutrinos, and for HF with and without CRPA corrections. At momentum transfers above approximately 500 MeV, the HF-CRPA cross section is smoothly interpolated to the SuSAv2 model using a sigmoid transition function with parameters q0 = 600 MeV and Delta_q = 100 MeV.

## Key numbers

* The interpolation between HF-CRPA and SuSAv2 uses a transition region of 500 to 700 MeV in momentum transfer q, with parameters q0 = 600 MeV and Delta_q = 100 MeV.
* Hadron tensor tables are provided up to 1 GeV energy transfer and 2 GeV momentum transfer.
* The nucleon axial mass parameter in all compared models is set between 0.99 GeV and 1.03 GeV.
* The T2K muon neutrino flux peaks at 0.6 GeV, and flux-averaged cross sections are shown for this flux.
* For a value of q approximately 500 MeV, the scaling functions obtained with HF and HF-CRPA are similar to the SuSAv2 results at E_nu = 3 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the HF-CRPA model previously developed by the Ghent group (Jachowicz 2002, Pandey 2014) and its validation against electron scattering data (Nikolakopoulos 2018) into the GENIE event generator framework, enabling direct comparison with other GENIE CCQE models such as SuSAv2 (Gonzalez-Jimenez 2014, Megias 2016) and the Valencia group's Local Fermi Gas model with RPA corrections (Nieves 2011). The implementation provides neutrino oscillation experiments (T2K, NOvA, Hyper-Kamiokande, DUNE) a means to verify that model differences between targets, flavors, and energies are appropriately covered in systematic error budgets, particularly for low energy and momentum transfer interactions where nuclear effects are most relevant.
