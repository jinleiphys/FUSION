# Physics and Astrophysics of Strange Quark Matter

> 3-flavor quark matter (strange quark matter; SQM) can be stable or metastable for a wide range of strong interaction parameters. If so, SQM can play an important role in cosmology, neutron stars, cosmic ray physics, and relativistic heavy-ion collisions. As an example of the intimate connections between astrophysics and heavy-ion collision physics, this Chapter gives an overview of the physical properties of SQM in bulk and of small-baryon number strangelets; discusses the possible formation, destruction, and implications of lumps of SQM (quark nuggets) in the early Universe; and describes the structure and signature of strange stars, as well as formation and detection of strangelets in cosmic rays. It is concluded, that astrophysical and laboratory searches are complementary in many respects, and that both should be pursued to test the intriguing possibility of a strange ground state for hadronic matter, and (more generally) to improve our knowledge of the strong interactions.

## Key claim

Strange quark matter (SQM) can be stable or metastable for a wide range of strong interaction parameters, but this does not conflict with the existence of ordinary nuclei because conversion requires simultaneous weak interactions of order A (the baryon number), which has negligible probability; furthermore, finite-size effects destabilize small strangelets even if SQM is stable in bulk.

## Method

The paper reviews the MIT bag model calculations for bulk strange quark matter and for small-baryon-number strangelets, including shell model mode-filling calculations from the Dirac equation with bag boundary conditions and liquid drop model calculations using a multiple reflection expansion for the density of states. The author derives thermodynamic potentials, number densities, and energy per baryon for quark matter composed of u, d, and s quarks with finite s-quark mass, including surface and curvature energy contributions, and applies these to cosmological quark nugget formation, strange star structure, and cosmic ray strangelet searches.

## Key numbers

- Energy per baryon for bulk 3-flavor quark matter with massless quarks: 829 MeV × B_{145}^{1/4}, where B_{145}^{1/4} ≡ B^{1/4}/145 MeV
- Energy per baryon for bulk 2-flavor quark matter with massless quarks: 934 MeV × B_{145}^{1/4}
- Lower limit on bag constant from existence of ordinary nuclei: B^{1/4} > 145 MeV for α_s = 0
- Upper limit for absolute stability of bulk SQM relative to iron: B^{1/4} < 162.8 MeV
- Upper limit for metastability relative to neutron gas: B^{1/4} < 164.4 MeV
- Upper limit for metastability relative to Λ-particles: B^{1/4} < 195.2 MeV
- Bulk chemical potential for 3-flavor massless quarks: μ_i^0 = 276.2 MeV × B_{145}^{1/4}
- Bulk baryon number density for 3-flavor massless quarks: n_A^0 = 0.700 B^{3/4}
- Bulk radius per baryon for 3-flavor massless quarks: R^0 = 0.699 B^{-1/4}
- Energy per baryon for massless strangelets including curvature: [829 MeV + 351 MeV A^{-2/3}] × B_{145}^{1/4}
- Energy per baryon for strangelets with m_s = 150 MeV and B^{1/4}=145 MeV: 874 MeV + 77 MeV A^{-1/3} + 232 MeV A^{-2/3}
- Energy per baryon for strangelets with m_s = 200 MeV and B^{1/4}=145 MeV: 896 MeV + 53 MeV A^{-1/3} + 242 MeV A^{-2/3}

## Context

This review extends the original proposal by Bodmer (1971) and Witten (1984) that strange quark matter could be the ground state of hadronic matter, and summarizes calculations originally performed by Farhi and Jaffe (1984) for bulk SQM and by Gilson and Jaffe (1993) and Madsen (1994) for strangelets. The work connects astrophysical searches for strange stars and cosmological quark nuggets with laboratory searches in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, showing that both approaches are complementary for testing the strange matter hypothesis and constraining strong interaction parameters.
