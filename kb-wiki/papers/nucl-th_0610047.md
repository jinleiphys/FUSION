# Superfluid-normal phase transition in finite systems and its effect on damping of hot giant resonances

> Thermal fluctuations of quasiparticle number are included making use of the secondary Bogolyubov's transformation, which turns quasiparticles operators into modified-quasiparticle ones. This restores the unitarity relation for the generalized single-particle density operator, which is violated within the Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov (HFB) theory at finite temperature. The resulting theory is called the modified HFB (MHFB) theory, whose limit of a constant pairing interaction yields the modified BCS (MBCS) theory. Within the MBCS theory, the pairing gap never collapses at finite temperature T as it does within the BCS theory, but decreases monotonously with increasing T. It is demonstrated that this non-vanishing thermal pairing is the reason why the width of the giant dipole resonance (GDR) does not increase with T up to T around 1 MeV. At higher T, when the thermal pairing is small, the GDR width starts to increase with T. The calculations within the phonon-damping model yield the results in good agreement with the most recent experimental systematic for the GDR width as a function of T. A similar effect, which causes a small GDR width at low T, is also seen after thermal pairing is included in the thermal fluctuation model.

## Key claim
The paper claims that the pairing gap never collapses at finite temperature within the modified BCS (MBCS) theory, unlike in standard BCS theory, and that this non-vanishing thermal pairing explains why the giant dipole resonance (GDR) width does not increase with temperature up to approximately 1 MeV. The claim is conditional on the inclusion of thermal fluctuations of quasiparticle number via a secondary Bogolyubov transformation.

## Method
The authors developed the modified Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov (MHFB) theory by applying a secondary Bogolyubov transformation to include quasiparticle-number fluctuations, which restores the unitarity relation for the generalized single-particle density operator that is violated in standard finite-temperature HFB theory. In the limit of constant pairing interaction, this reduces to the modified BCS (MBCS) theory. The MBCS pairing gap was then incorporated into the phonon-damping model (PDM) to calculate the GDR width as a function of temperature.

## Key numbers
- The critical temperature for infinite systems is given as \(T_c \simeq 0.567\Delta(0)\), where \(\Delta(0)\) is the pairing gap at zero temperature.
- The GDR width remains almost constant at \(T \leq 1\) MeV, then sharply increases with \(T\) up to \(T \simeq 2-3\) MeV, and saturates at higher \(T\).
- The quasiparticle-number fluctuation on the \(i\)-th orbital is \(\delta\mathcal{N}_i^2 = n_i(1-n_i)\), and the total fluctuation is \(\delta\mathcal{N}^2 = \sum_i n_i(1-n_i)\).

## Context
This work extends the phonon-damping model (PDM) previously proposed by Dang and Arima (2002) by incorporating thermal pairing from the MBCS theory. It contradicts the standard BCS and HFB predictions of a sharp superfluid-normal phase transition at finite temperature, instead showing a smooth decrease of the pairing gap. The approach provides a microscopic alternative to the macroscopic Landau theory of phase transitions used by Moretto (1991) and Dang and Zaslavsky (1997), and enables improved predictions for GDR widths in hot nuclei formed in heavy-ion fusion reactions or light-particle scattering experiments.
