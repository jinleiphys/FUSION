# Constraining the density dependence of symmetry energy using mean field models

> The nuclear symmetry energy characterizes the variation of the binding energy as the neutron to proton ratio of nuclear systems (e.g. finite nucleus, neutron star etc.) is varied. The densities associated to these nuclear systems vary over a wide range. Studying density dependence of symmetry energy is thus a major topic of research in nuclear physics. Density dependence of symmetry energy is primarily characterized by three empirical quantities defined for infinite nuclear matter namely, symmetry energy coefficient J, slope parameter L and curvature parameter K_sym ; all of these quantities pertaining to saturation density ρ_0 of infinite nuclear matter. Since nuclear matter is not accessible in laboratories, one needs to find suitable experimental observables to constrain the values of J, L or K_sym. Though, J is quite precisely known from the experimental data on binding energies of nuclei, it is not quite the case for L or K_sym. This problem is addressed in this thesis using mean field models.

## Key claim

The thesis aims to constrain the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy, specifically the slope parameter \(L_0\) and curvature parameter \(K_{sym}^0\), using mean-field models, acknowledging that while the symmetry energy coefficient \(J\) is known to within \(\sim 0.5\) MeV, \(L_0\) and \(K_{sym}^0\) remain poorly constrained, with \(L_0\) ranging from 20–120 MeV and \(K_{sym}^0\) from \(-700\) to \(+400\) MeV across models.

## Method

The work employs both non-relativistic Skyrme Hartree-Fock and relativistic mean-field (RMF) models to calculate ground-state properties of closed-shell spherical nuclei and infinite nuclear matter. Binding energies and charge radii are computed self-consistently, and covariance analysis is used to study correlations between symmetry energy parameters and experimental observables such as neutron-skin thickness \(\Delta r_{np}\) of heavy nuclei like \(^{208}\)Pb. A simple statistical-mechanics model is also proposed to derive analytical relations between symmetry energy parameters, tested against 500 mean-field models from the literature.

## Key numbers

- The symmetry energy coefficient \(J\) (denoted \(C_2^0\) in the text) is constrained to \(\sim 32 \pm 4\) MeV from experimental data, with error reduced to \(\sim 0.5\) MeV using the finite range droplet model or double differences of nuclear masses.
- The slope parameter \(L_0\) is reported to lie in the range 20 MeV to 120 MeV across different studies.
- The curvature parameter \(K_{sym}^0\) spans a huge range from \(-700\) MeV to \(+400\) MeV across mean-field models.
- The density at the center of a nucleus is \(\sim 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\) (or \(2.7 \times 10^{14}\) g/cm\(^3\)), while the core of a neutron star reaches 5–6 times this density.
- The heaviest observed neutron star has twice the mass of the Sun and a radius of about 10 to 15 kilometers.

## Context

This thesis extends prior work using mean-field models to connect symmetry energy parameters to experimental observables, following studies such as Centelles (2009) on the correlation between \(L_0\) and neutron-skin thickness in \(^{208}\)Pb, and earlier efforts by Moller (2012) and Jiang (2012) to constrain \(J\). It enables tighter constraints on \(L_0\) and \(K_{sym}^0\) by incorporating highly asymmetric nuclei (with neutron-to-proton ratios of 2:1) into fitting protocols and by proposing a near-universal linear correlation between \(K_{sym}^0\) and other nuclear matter properties, which could guide future experiments and astrophysical models of neutron stars and r-process nucleosynthesis.
