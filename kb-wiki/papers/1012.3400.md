# Equation of State and Phase Transitions in the Nuclear and Hadronic Systems

> An investigation of strongly interacting matter equation of state remains one of the major tasks of modern high energy nuclear physics for almost a quarter of century. The present work is my doctor of science thesis which contains my contribution (42 works) to this field made between 1993 and 2008. Inhere I mainly discuss the common physical and mathematical features of several exactly solvable statistical models which describe the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition and the deconfinement phase transition. Luckily, in some cases it was possible to rigorously extend the solutions found in thermodynamic limit to finite volumes and to formulate the finite volume analogs of phases directly from the grand canonical partition. It turns out that finite volume (surface) of a system generates also the temporal constraints, i.e. the finite formation/decay time of possible states in this finite system. Among other results I would like to mention the calculation of upper and lower bounds for the surface entropy of physical clusters within the Hills and Dales model; evaluation of the second virial coefficient which accounts for the Lorentz contraction of the hard core repulsing potential between hadrons; inclusion of large width of heavy quark-gluon bags into statistical description. I believe that the suggested mathematical solution of the freeze-out problem in relativistic hydrodynamic model and in hydro-cascade model has not only an academic interest, but also has some practical value. In addition I hope that the experience gained in working out some partly successful signals of deconfinement transition can be useful for other researchers to go further in this direction.

## Key claim

This doctoral thesis claims that the spatial and temporal finiteness of strongly interacting systems created in nuclear heavy-ion collisions can be rigorously incorporated into exactly solvable statistical models of the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition and the deconfinement phase transition, with the caveat that the finite volume of the system also generates temporal constraints, i.e., a finite formation/decay time of possible states in the finite system.

## Method

The author develops and solves exactly several statistical models including a simplified Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM), the Fisher Droplet Model (FDM), the Gas of Bags Model (GBM), and the Quark Gluon Bags with Surface Tension (QGBST) model, using standard methods such as Laplace transforms, cluster and virial expansions, and the maximum term method of Lee-Yang. A novel Laplace-Fourier transform method is introduced to solve these models for finite volumes, allowing the definition of finite volume analogs of phases directly from the grand canonical partition. The freeze-out problem in relativistic hydrodynamics is addressed by extending the equations of motion with generalized functions to treat the fluid and emitted free particles as two coupled subsystems separated by a freeze-out hypersurface.

## Key numbers

- The characteristic number of particles at the beginning of a collision is about a few hundred nucleons, colliding in practically vanishing volume due to Lorentz contraction, with the system evolving for 10 to 20 fm/c and stopping in a volume of about a few thousand fm^3 filled with a few thousand secondary hadrons.
- For the SMM, the Fisher parameter tau is constrained to a narrow range 1.799 < tau < 1.846, consistent with both the ISiS Collaboration data and EOS Collaboration data, which the author argues gives direct evidence that nuclear matter has a tricritical rather than a critical point.
- For the FDM, the previously stated range of the tau index, 2 < tau < 3, must be changed to 2 < tau < 2 2/3 based on the author's rigorous analysis using the Newton-Leibnitz definition of the integral.
- The Hills and Dales Model reproduces the leading term of the famous Fisher result for the surface entropy within a few percent in the limit of small amplitude of deformations.

## Context

This work extends the statistical mechanics treatment of phase transitions in nuclear and hadronic systems developed over decades through models such as the Statistical Multifragmentation Model, Fisher Droplet Model, and Hagedorn's statistical bootstrap model. The author's exact analytical solutions for finite volumes address a long-standing problem noted by T. Hill, whose approach to defining phase transitions in finite systems the author explicitly demonstrates to be based on a wrong assumption. The results enable rigorous definitions of finite volume analogs of phases for both the nuclear liquid-gas transition and the deconfinement transition, and the solution of the freeze-out problem in relativistic hydrodynamics (unresolved for over 40 years) provides correct boundary conditions for hydrodynamic and hydrocascade models of heavy-ion collisions.
