# Two novel approaches to the hadron-quark mixed phase in compact stars

> First-order phase transitions, like the liquid-gas transition, proceed via formation of structures such as bubbles and droplets. In strongly interacting compact star matter, at the crust-core transition, but also at the hadron-quark transition in the core, these structures form different shapes dubbed "pasta phases". We describe two methods to obtain one-parameter families of hybrid equations of state (EoS) which mimic the thermodynamic behavior of pasta phases in between a low-density hadron and a high-density quark matter phase, thus generalizing the Maxwell construction. The first method replaces the behavior of pressure vs. chemical potential in a finite region around the critical %chemical potential pressure of the Maxwell construction by a polynomial interpolation. The second method uses extrapolations of the hadronic and quark matter EoS beyond the Maxwell point to define a mixing of both with weight functions bounded by finite limits around the Maxwell point. We apply both methods to the case of a hybrid EoS with a strong first order transition that entails the formation of a third family of compact stars and the corresponding mass twin phenomenon. We investigate for both models the robustness of this phenomenon against variation of the single parameter, the pressure increment at the critical chemical potential which quantifies the deviation from the Maxwell construction. We also show sets of results for other compact star observables than mass and radius, namely the moment of inertia and the baryon mass.

## Key claim

The paper presents two phenomenological interpolation methods (replacement interpolation method, RIM, and mixing interpolation method, MIM) that produce one-parameter families of hybrid equations of state mimicking the thermodynamic behavior of pasta phases at the hadron-quark transition, thereby generalizing the Maxwell construction; the key qualifier is that these methods are designed to mimic pasta-phase thermodynamics without explicitly modeling the geometrical structures, and their single free parameter is the pressure increment at the critical chemical potential.

## Method

The authors start from a four-polytrope hybrid equation of state featuring a hadronic phase, a constant-pressure Maxwell transition region, and two stiff quark-matter polytropes at high densities. The RIM replaces the pressure-versus-chemical-potential behavior in a finite interval around the Maxwell critical point with an even-order polynomial that matches the hadronic and quark-matter branches up to the k-th derivative at the interval boundaries. The MIM mixes the extrapolated hadronic and quark-matter pressure branches using polynomial switch functions and a bell-shaped pressure-increment function, with continuity conditions imposed at the boundaries and at the critical point.

## Key numbers

- The dimensionless pressure increment parameter \(\Delta_P = \Delta P / P_c\) is varied from 1% to 8% for both methods, where \(\Delta_P = 0\) reproduces the Maxwell construction.
- The hadronic polytrope (region i=1) has polytropic index \(\Gamma_1 = 4.921\) and prefactor \(\kappa_1 = 2.1680\) MeV/fm\(^3\); the constant-pressure transition polytrope (i=2) has \(\Gamma_2 = 0.0\) and \(\kappa_2 = 63.178\) MeV/fm\(^3\).
- The maximum mass on the hadronic branch is \(M_{\rm max} = 2.01 M_\odot\); the maximum mass on the hybrid branch (region i=4) is \(M_{\rm max} = 2.11 M_\odot\); the minimum mass in region i=3 is \(M_{\rm min} = 1.96 M_\odot\).
- The saturation density is \(n_0 = 0.15\) fm\(^{-3}\); the transition density boundaries are \(n_1 = 0.1650\) fm\(^{-3}\), \(n_2 = 0.3174\) fm\(^{-3}\), \(n_3 = 0.5344\) fm\(^{-3}\), and \(n_4 = 0.7500\) fm\(^{-3}\).

## Context

This work extends the interpolation approach first introduced by Masuda (2012) and followed up by Alvarez-Castillo (2014, 2017), which replaced the jump in the equation of state \(\varepsilon(P)\) with a smooth hyperbolic-tangent ansatz. The RIM was systematically formulated by Ayriyan (2017) using a parabolic interpolation, while the MIM was recently developed by Alvarez-Castillo (2018). The methods enable systematic studies of how pasta-phase-like mixed phases affect compact-star observables such as mass-radius relations, the third-family and mass-twin phenomena, and the moment of inertia and baryon mass, with relevance to interpreting the GW170817 gravitational-wave event.
