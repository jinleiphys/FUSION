# Symmetries of Neutrino Physics

> In this contribution to the proceedings of a conference honoring the career of Francesco Iachello, two applications of symmetry principles to neutrino physics are described. These applications are the connection between fermion pairing in many-body physics and the neutrino mass as well as collective neutrino oscillations

## Key claim

Symmetry principles, specifically the SU(2) quasispin algebra and the Gaudin algebra, provide a unifying mathematical framework for two distinct neutrino physics problems: the connection between fermion pairing and neutrino mass, and the exact solution of collective neutrino oscillations in the single-angle approximation.

## Method

The paper establishes an analogy between the Dirac mass term in the free fermion Hamiltonian and the SU(2) quasispin algebra used to describe fermion pairing in nuclear physics, showing that the mass term can be written as the sum of ladder operators of an SU(2) algebra. For collective neutrino oscillations, the paper presents the exact Bethe ansatz solution for the single-angle approximation Hamiltonian, where the neutrino-neutrino interaction strength is replaced by its average value over the ensemble, and derives the corresponding Bethe ansatz equations and eigenvalue expressions.

## Key numbers

- The entire gravitational binding energy of a pre-supernova star yields approximately 10^57 to 10^58 neutrinos
- A recent exact adiabatic calculation considered 108 neutrinos and antineutrinos distributed over 1200 energy bins, with an initial thermal distribution of electron neutrinos at T=10 MeV and antineutrinos of another flavor at T=12 MeV, using solar neutrino parameters and normal hierarchy

## Context

This paper extends the long-standing analogy between fermion mass and pairing gap in superconductivity, originally discussed by Nambu (1961), to the specific context of neutrino mass using SO(5) algebra. It also builds on the Gaudin algebra approach to collective neutrino oscillations, which was previously applied by Pehlivan (2011) for exact solutions, and enables testing the limits of validity of mean-field approximations used in calculations of spectral swaps and splits, as reviewed by Duan (2010). The exact solution formalism can be extended to include antineutrinos and three-flavor mixing with CP-violating phases, as shown by Pehlivan (2014).
