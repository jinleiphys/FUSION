# Open Problems in Applying Random-Matrix Theory to Nuclear Reactions

> Problems in applying random-matrix theory (RMT) to nuclear reactions arise in two domains. To justify the approach, statistical properties of isolated resonances observed experimentally must agree with RMT predictions. That agreement is less striking than would be desirable. In the implementation of the approach, the range of theoretically predicted observables is too narrow.

## Key claim

The agreement between experimental data on isolated nuclear resonances and random-matrix theory (GOE) predictions is less striking than would be desirable, with open questions remaining for the \(\Delta_3\) statistic for long level sequences and for the distribution of neutron widths; the range of theoretically predicted observables from the GOE approach to nuclear reactions is also too narrow.

## Method

The paper reviews tests of GOE spectral fluctuation predictions (level-spacing distribution via Wigner's surmise and the \(\Delta_3\) statistic, and width distribution via the Porter-Thomas distribution) against experimental data from slow-neutron scattering on medium-weight and heavy nuclei, as well as from proton scattering near the Coulomb barrier. It also examines the theoretical implementation of the GOE into the scattering matrix formalism (Eqs. 4-5) and the limitations of analytical results for \(S\)-matrix correlation functions beyond the pair-correlation function.

## Key numbers

* The Nuclear Data Ensemble (NDE) originally comprised 30 sequences of levels from 27 different nuclei, totaling 1407 resonance energies (Haq 1982).
* The reconstructed NDE contains 1245 resonances (Koehler 2011).
* For the reconstructed NDE, the \(\Delta_3\) statistic for all nuclei agrees with the GOE, but for the remaining nuclei after omitting U and Th, the deviation becomes significant for sequence lengths \(L > 20\) (Koehler 2012).
* A maximum-likelihood analysis of neutron widths for 9 nuclides gave \(\nu\) values ranging from \(0.64 \pm 0.28\) to \(1.32 \pm 0.30\), with an error-weighted mean \(\nu = 0.98 \pm 0.10\) (Cameron 1994).
* For the reconstructed NDE, the weighted average from maximum-likelihood analysis is \(\nu = 1.217 \pm 0.092\), rejecting the Porter-Thomas distribution with at least 98.17 percent statistical significance (Koehler 2011).
* For Pt nuclei, maximum-likelihood analysis gave \(\nu = 0.47\) for \({}^{192}\)Pt and \(\nu = 0.60\) for \({}^{194}\)Pt, rejecting agreement with RMT with at least 99.997 percent probability (Koehler 2010).
* Shell-model calculations for 3276 states with spin \(J = 2\) and isospin \(T = 0\) in the middle of the \(s-d\)-shell showed very good agreement with GOE predictions for both the NNS distribution and the \(\Delta_3\) statistic (Zel 1996).

## Context

This paper extends the long-standing program of testing Wigner's random-matrix theory (GOE) against nuclear resonance data, following the Bohigas-Giannoni-Schmit conjecture (Bohigas 1984) linking chaos to RMT. It directly addresses the challenges raised by Koehler (2010, 2011, 2012) who reported strong disagreement of neutron resonance data with GOE predictions, which had eroded confidence in the statistical theory of nuclear reactions. The paper enables future work by identifying the need for simulations to support maximum-likelihood analyses with cutoffs (Shriner 2014), and by calling for theoretical expressions for cross-section correlation functions beyond the Ericson regime.
