# Analysis of averaged multichannel delay times

> The physical significances and the pros and cons involved in the usage of different time delay formalisms are discussed. The delay time matrix introduced by Eisenbud, where only s-waves participate in a reaction, is in general related to the definition of an angular time delay which is shown not to be equivalent to the so-called phase time delay of Eisenbud and Wigner even for single channel scattering. Whereas the expression due to Smith which is derived from a time delayed radial wave packet is consistent with a lifetime matrix which is Hermitian, this is not true for any Eisenbud-type lifetime matrix which violates time reversal invariance. Extending the angular time delay of Nussenzveig to multiple channels, we show that if one performs an average over the directions and subtracts the forward angle contribution containing an interference of the incident and scattered waves, the multichannel angle dependent average time delay reduces to the one given by Smith. The present work also rectifies a recently misinterpreted misnomer of the relation due to Smith.

# Analysis of averaged multichannel delay times

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the multichannel angle-dependent average time delay, when averaged over directions and with the forward-angle contribution subtracted, reduces to Smith's time delay, but only after a partial-wave decomposition; the angular time delay and the radial (phase) time delay are not equivalent even for single-channel scattering, contrary to what is sometimes assumed.

## Method

The authors perform a wave-packet analysis of the scattered wave, comparing two formalisms: the radial time delay derived from following the peak of a radial wave packet (Smith's approach, which yields an \(l\)-dependent delay from the \(S\)-matrix), and the angular time delay derived from the full scattering amplitude \(f(k,\theta)\) (Nussenzveig's approach). They then extend Nussenzveig's angular time delay to multiple channels, perform a partial-wave expansion of the \(S\)-matrix, and evaluate an angle- and channel-averaged expression that includes a forward-angle subtraction term. The analysis is applied to a two-channel unitary \(S\)-matrix and to pion-nucleon elastic scattering data for the \(S_{11}\) resonances.

## Key numbers

* The single-channel phase time delay is \(\Delta t = 2\hbar\, d\delta/dE\), where \(\delta\) is the scattering phase shift.
* The multichannel radial time delay for a given partial wave \(l\) is \(\Delta t^l_{ij}(E) = \hbar\, d(\Im\mathrm{m}\,\ln S^l_{ij})/dE\).
* The angle-averaged single-channel time delay with forward subtraction yields \(\frac{\pi}{k^2} \sum_l (2l+1)\,2\hbar\, d\delta_l/dE\), recovering Wigner's phase time delay.
* The angle- and channel-averaged multichannel time delay reduces to \(\frac{\pi}{k^2} \sum_l (2l+1)\, Q^l_{ii}(E)\), where \(Q^l_{ii}\) is a diagonal element of Smith's Hermitian lifetime matrix.
* For the two-channel \(S\)-matrix example, Smith's radial time delay matrix elements are \(2\hbar\, d\delta_1/dE\) and \(2\hbar\, d\delta_2/dE\) on the diagonal, while Eisenbud's time delay elements involve both \(d\delta_i/dE\) and \(d\eta/dE\) (the inelasticity derivative).
* The Beth-Uhlenbeck formula gives the density of states difference as \(dn/dE = (2l+1)/\pi \, d\delta_l(E)/dE\).

## Context

This work extends the single-channel angular time delay formalism of Nussenzveig (1972) to multichannel scattering and clarifies the relationship between the time delay definitions of Eisenbud (1948), Wigner (1955), and Smith (1960). It corrects a misinterpretation in a recent work (Habwork, year not given) that claimed Smith's work propagated an error, showing instead that Eisenbud's and Smith's delay times differ because Eisenbud restricted his analysis to \(s\)-waves only. The results enable the use of Smith's \(l\)-dependent time delay for characterizing hadron resonances in partial-wave analyses, while also providing a connection to the directly measurable angular time delay.
