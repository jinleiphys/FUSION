# Few is different: deciphering many-body dynamics in mesoscopic quantum gases

> Emergent macroscopic descriptions of matter, such as hydrodynamics, are central to our description of complex physical systems across a wide spectrum of energy scales. The conventional understanding of these many-body phenomena has recently been shaken by a number of experimental findings. Collective behavior of matter has been observed in \emph{mesoscopic} systems, such as high-energy hadron-hadron collisions, or ultra-cold gases with only few strongly interacting fermions. In such systems, the separation of scales between macroscopic and microscopic dynamics (at the heart of any effective theory) is inapplicable. To address the conceptual challenges that arise from these observations and explore the universality of emergent descriptions of matter, the EMMI Rapid Reaction Task Force was assembled. This document summarizes the RRTF discussions on recent theoretical and experimental advances in this rapidly developing field. Leveraging technological breakthroughs in the control of quantum systems, we can now quantitatively explore what it means for a system to exhibit behavior beyond the sum of its individual parts. In particular, the report highlights how the (in)applicability of hydrodynamics and other effective theories can be probed across three principal frontiers: the size frontier, the equilibrium frontier, and the interaction frontier.

## Key claim
The paper argues that emergent collective behavior, traditionally requiring a large number of constituents and clear separation of scales, can appear in mesoscopic systems with as few as 10 fermions, challenging textbook criteria for the applicability of hydrodynamics and other effective theories.

## Method
The report summarizes discussions of the EMMI Rapid Reaction Task Force held at Heidelberg University in March 2024, which brought together theorists and experimentalists working on ultra-cold atomic gases and high-energy heavy-ion collisions. The authors compare experimental observations of elliptic flow across three frontiers: system size (from large nuclei to few-atom systems), equilibrium (from thermalized to far-from-equilibrium systems), and interaction strength (from weak to strong coupling).

## Key numbers
- Elliptic flow coefficient $v_2(p_T=2\ \mathrm{GeV}) \simeq 20\%$ observed in semi-central heavy-ion collisions at RHIC
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio $\eta/s \simeq 0.15$ near the deconfinement transition in QGP, with uncertainties of order 50%
- Shear viscosity of unitary Fermi gas at high temperature: $\eta = (0.265 \pm 0.020)(mT)^{3/2}$
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio near critical temperature for superfluidity in Fermi gas: $\eta/s \simeq 0.5 \pm 0.1$
- Knudsen number ${\rm Kn} \approx 0.2$ for large nuclei collisions at top RHIC energy with $\eta/s \approx 0.3$
- Elliptic flow observed in a system of only 10 fermions (5+5 atoms) at zero temperature in Heidelberg experiments (Brandstetter 2023)

## Context
This work extends the "small system revolution" that began with the discovery of long-range azimuthal correlations in proton-proton collisions at the LHC (CMS 2010) and proton-lead collisions (ALICE 2012), which challenged the assumption that collective flow requires large nuclear systems. The report connects this high-energy puzzle to recent ultra-cold atom experiments (Brandstetter 2023) showing elliptic flow in few-fermion systems, building on earlier EMMI efforts to bridge the two fields. It enables a new research program to quantitatively study the boundaries of effective theories like hydrodynamics, and to develop modern definitions of collective behavior that do not rely on traditional scale separation.
