# Understanding Supernova Neutrino Physics using Low-Energy Beta-Beams

> We show that fitting linear combinations of low-energy beta-beam spectra to supernova-neutrino energy-distributions reconstructs the response of a nuclear target to a supernova flux in a very accurate way. This allows one to make direct predictions about the supernova-neutrino signal in a terrestrial neutrino detector.

## Key claim

Fitting linear combinations of low-energy beta-beam spectra to supernova-neutrino energy distributions can reconstruct the response of a nuclear target to a supernova flux with very good accuracy, provided the fit uses at least three beta-beam components with gamma values restricted to integers between 5 and 15.

## Method

The authors constructed synthetic neutrino spectra as linear combinations of N=3 or N=5 low-energy beta-beam spectra, minimizing the integral of the absolute difference between the synthetic and target supernova-neutrino distributions. They then folded these synthetic spectra with nuclear cross sections for neutral-current scattering on 208Pb and compared the resulting differential cross sections to those obtained by folding the original supernova-neutrino energy distributions directly.

## Key numbers

- The synthetic spectra were constructed using N=3 and N=5 beta-beam components, with gamma values restricted to integer values between 5 and 15.
- The method can distinguish between supernova-neutrino spectra roughly 1 MeV apart in average energy.
- The comparison was performed for power-law supernova-neutrino spectra with average energies of 14 MeV, 18 MeV, and 22 MeV, and power-law indices alpha of 2, 3, and 4.

## Context

This work extends the use of beta-beams, originally proposed for neutrino oscillation studies, to low-energy nuclear astrophysics applications. The method enables direct predictions of supernova-neutrino signals in terrestrial detectors without relying on theoretical cross-section calculations, and conversely allows reconstruction of supernova-neutrino energy distribution parameters from detector signals. The approach is stable against experimentally-inferred variations in nuclear cross sections, as noted in prior work by the same authors (Jachowicz and McLaughlin, 2005).
