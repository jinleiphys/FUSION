# Baryon and meson masses in the Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model: A Bayesian approach

> We investigate the capabilities of the Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model to describe and reproduce fundamental vacuum properties of Quantum Chromodynamics, notably the hadronic spectrum. Mesons are described as quark-antiquark bound states at the level of the random phase approximation of the Bethe-Salpeter equation, while baryons are characterized as quark-diquark bound states within the static approximation of the Faddeev equation. Within a Bayesian framework, we constrain the model by phenomenologically known quantities and study the implications on its parameters and predictions in vacuum, as well as the correlations between the two. We find that within our framework, the vacuum masses of mesons and baryons can be reasonably well reproduced. Scalar diquarks need to be significantly bound in order to correctly reproduce the masses of the baryon octet, therefore enforcing values of the scalar diquark coupling larger than what is suggested by the canonical Fierz values. These findings could have important implications on the phenomenology of strongly-interacting matter at high temperature and density as well as of compact star physics.

## Key claim

The Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model can reasonably reproduce vacuum masses of mesons and baryons, but only if the scalar diquark coupling is significantly larger than the canonical Fierz value of \(3G/4\), contradicting the naive expectation from a Fierz-transformed color current-current interaction.

## Method

The authors use a three-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model with four-fermion and six-fermion contact interactions, computing meson masses as quark-antiquark bound states via the random phase approximation of the Bethe-Salpeter equation and baryon masses as quark-diquark bound states within the static approximation of the Faddeev equation. They perform a Bayesian analysis by generating \(5 \times 10^7\) random parameter sets from uniform priors on seven parameters (two bare quark masses, four coupling constants, and a momentum cutoff), then weighting each set by a Gaussian likelihood against experimental data including pseudoscalar meson masses, baryon octet and decuplet masses, and decay constants.

## Key numbers

* Pion mass: \(\overline{m_\pi} = 138.0\) MeV (barycenter of charged and neutral pions), with systematic uncertainty \(\sigma_{m_\pi} = 4.6\) MeV
* Kaon mass: \(\overline{m_K} = 495.6\) MeV (average of charged and neutral kaons), with systematic uncertainty \(\sigma_{m_K} = 3.9\) MeV
* Pion decay constant: \(f_\pi = 92.1 \pm 1.2\) MeV (with 10% inflated uncertainty for systematics)
* Kaon-to-pion decay constant ratio: \(f_K/f_\pi = 1.1928(26)\) (experimental value from Rosner (2019))
* Prior range for scalar diquark coupling ratio \(G_d/G\): 1.0 to 1.89 (minimum and maximum)
* Prior range for axial diquark coupling ratio \(G_{d,V}/G\): 0.75 to 1.3 (minimum and maximum)
* Prior range for cutoff \(\Lambda\): 550 to 625 MeV (minimum and maximum)

## Context

This work extends previous NJL studies of baryon masses at finite temperature and density by Torres-Rincon (2015) and Blanquier (2011, 2013), which found a large light quark dressed mass of approximately 480 MeV. The Bayesian approach systematically explores whether a smaller quark mass could also fit the hadron spectrum, and quantifies parameter correlations that earlier fine-tuning methods could not address. The results enable future studies of the NJL model phase diagram at finite temperature and density, and have implications for compact star physics where the diquark coupling strength affects the equation of state.
