# Progenitor Dependence of Hadron-quark Phase Transition in Failing Core-collapse Supernovae

> We study the consequences of a hadron-quark phase transition (PT) in failing core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe) which give birth to stellar-mass black holes (BH). We perform a suite of neutrino-transport general-relativistic hydrodynamic simulations in spherical symmetry with 21 progenitor models and a hybrid equation of state (EoS) including hadrons and quarks. We find that the effect of the PT on the CCSN postbounce dynamics is a function of the bounce compactness parameter $ξ_{2.2}$. For $ξ_{2.2}\gtrsim0.24$, the PT leads to a second dynamical collapse of the protocompact star (PCS). While BH formation starts immediately after this second collapse for models with $ξ_{2.2}\gtrsim0.51$, the PCS experiences a second bounce and oscillations for models with $0.24\lesssimξ_{2.2}\lesssim0.51$. These models emit potent oscillatory neutrino signals with a period of $\sim$ms for tens of ms after the second bounce, which can be a strong indicator of the PT in failing CCSNe if detected in the future. However, no shock revival occurs and BH formation inevitably takes place in our spherically-symmetric simulations. Furthermore, via a diagram of mass-specific entropy evolution of the PCS, the progenitor dependence can be understood through the appearance of third-family of compact stars emerging at large entropy induced by the PT.

## Key claim

The effect of a hadron-quark phase transition on the postbounce dynamics of failing core-collapse supernovae is a function of the bounce compactness parameter ξ₂.₂, with three distinct dynamical outcomes depending on whether ξ₂.₂ is below ~0.24, between ~0.24 and ~0.51, or above ~0.51; however, no shock revival occurs and black hole formation inevitably takes place in these spherically-symmetric simulations.

## Method

The authors performed a suite of 21 neutrino-transport general-relativistic hydrodynamic simulations in spherical symmetry using the GR1D code, with solar-metallicity progenitor models from Sukhbold et al. (2018). They employed a hybrid equation of state combining the STOS EoS for hadronic matter and the MIT bag model EoS for quark matter, with the phase transition region constructed under Gibbs conditions using a bag constant B = 145 MeV and strong coupling constant α_s = 0.7. The simulations included an M1 scheme for three-flavor neutrino transport and followed at least 2.5 M_⊙ of baryonic material from each progenitor.

## Key numbers

- The phase transition region spans from 2.5×10¹⁵ g cm⁻³ to 5.0×10¹⁵ g cm⁻³ for cold symmetric matter, and from 4.0×10¹⁴ g cm⁻³ to 4.0×10¹⁵ g cm⁻³ at T = 10 MeV and Y_e = 0.3
- The maximum gravitational mass of cold β-equilibrium compact stars for the hybrid EoS is ~2.0 M_⊙
- For models with ξ₂.₂ ≳ 0.51, black hole formation occurs within 5×10⁻⁴ s to 8×10⁻⁴ s after the second collapse (e.g., model s33: t_BH - t_2c = 5×10⁻⁴ s)
- For models with 0.24 ≲ ξ₂.₂ ≲ 0.51, the protocompact star experiences a second bounce and oscillations lasting ~50 ms, with shock expansion reaching up to ~1450 km (model s22.39)
- The oscillatory neutrino signals have a period of ~ms and are detectable in IceCube up to ~16 kpc (normal mass ordering) or ~11 kpc (inverted mass ordering) assuming conservative parameters
- The gravitational mass of the protocompact star at black hole formation ranges from ~2.02 M_⊙ to ~2.16 M_⊙ across models

## Context

This work extends the single-model study of Nakazato et al. (2013), who found that the hadron-quark phase transition shortens the time between first bounce and black hole formation in a failing supernova, by systematically exploring 21 progenitor models and demonstrating that the outcome depends on the bounce compactness parameter ξ₂.₂. The results contrast with studies using a different hybrid EoS (B165) by Sagert et al. (2009) and Zha et al. (2020), which found that the second bounce could revive the supernova shock; the present work shows that for this particular EoS, the binding energy released during the second collapse is 1-2 orders of magnitude smaller, preventing shock revival. The progenitor dependence is interpreted through the entropy-dependent mass-radius curves of compact stars and the appearance of the third-family topology described by Alford et al. (2013) and Heinimann et al. (2016). The oscillatory neutrino signals predicted here provide a potential observational signature for future galactic supernova neutrino detection by IceCube.
