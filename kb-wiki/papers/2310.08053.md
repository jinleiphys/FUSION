# Lattice real-time simulations with learned optimal kernels

> We present a simulation strategy for the real-time dynamics of quantum fields, inspired by reinforcement learning. It builds on the complex Langevin approach, which it amends with system specific prior information, a necessary prerequisite to overcome this exceptionally severe sign problem. The optimization process underlying our machine learning approach is made possible by deploying inherently stable solvers of the complex Langevin stochastic process and a novel optimality criterion derived from insight into so-called boundary terms. This conceptual and technical progress allows us to both significantly extend the range of real-time simulations in 1+1d scalar field theory beyond the state-of-the-art and to avoid discretization artifacts that plagued previous real-time field theory simulations. Limitations of and promising future directions are discussed.

## Key claim

The authors present a machine-learning-assisted complex Langevin approach that uses system-specific prior information to learn optimal kernels, enabling direct real-time simulations on the lattice that extend to at least twice the real-time extent previously accessible with contour deformation methods, while avoiding discretization artifacts that affected earlier studies.

## Method

The method builds on the complex Langevin (CL) approach, which complexifies field degrees of freedom and simulates a stochastic process in an additional Langevin time direction. The authors introduce a field-independent kernel K into the CL evolution equation and use a reinforcement-learning-inspired optimization procedure to learn optimal kernel entries. The optimization uses a low-cost functional based on the imaginary part of the fields, which provides gradients that reduce a cost functional assessing deviations of equal-time correlators from their known Euclidean values. The stochastic dynamics is solved with an implicit Euler-Maruyama integrator, and gradients are computed via automatic differentiation in Julia.

## Key numbers

* Real-time extent of t_max = 3.2 achieved in 1+1d scalar field theory with m=1, lambda=1, beta*m=4/10, which is twice the extent previously achieved using contour deformations (Alexandru 2017).
* Lattice parameters: N_t=32 points on each real-time branch, N_tau=4 steps in imaginary time, N_x=8 spatial points, with lattice spacings a_s*m=2/10 and a_t*m=1/10.
* The cost functional decreased from p[K=1] approximately 1200 to p[K_opt] approximately 7.2 after optimization.
* Langevin step size d_tau_L = 10^-3 used with adaptive step size control.
* Optimization performed over Langevin time tau_L^opt = 5, with observables obtained from three streams over tau_L^obs = 5000.
* Learning rate r_l = 10^-3 used in the Adams algorithm for iterative updates.

## Context

This work extends the complex Langevin approach to real-time simulations on the Schwinger-Keldysh contour, addressing the severe sign problem that has previously limited direct Minkowski-time simulations. It builds on prior work by Alexandru (2017) using contour deformations, which achieved real-time extents only half as large, and on earlier CL studies by Berges (2005, 2006, 2007) that suffered from divergencies and incorrect convergence. The method enables access to real-time dynamics in 1+1d scalar field theory beyond the state-of-the-art, with the potential to extend to realistic (3+1)d systems and to incorporate field-dependent kernels for more complex theories.
