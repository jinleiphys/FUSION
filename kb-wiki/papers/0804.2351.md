# Three-body structure of low-lying 12Be states

> We investigate to what extent a description of 12Be as a three-body system made of an inert 10Be-core and two neutrons is able to reproduce the experimental 12Be data. Three-body wave functions are obtained with the hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method. We study the discrete spectrum of 12Be, the structure of the different states, the predominant transition strengths, and the continuum energy spectrum after high energy fragmentation on a light target. Two 0+, one 2+, one 1- and one 0- bound states are found where the first four are known experimentally whereas the 0- is predicted as an isomeric state. An effective neutron charge, reproducing the measured B(E1) transition and the charge rms radius in 11Be, leads to a computed B(E1) transition strength for 12Be in agreement with the experimental value. For the E0 and E2 transitions the contributions from core excitations could be more significant. The experimental 10Be-neutron continuum energy spectrum is also well reproduced except in the energy region corresponding to the 3/2- resonance in 11Be where core excitations contribute.

## Key claim

The paper assesses to what extent a three-body model with an inert 10Be core and two neutrons can reproduce the experimental 12Be data, finding that the model reproduces most observables but that core excitations contribute significantly to E0 and E2 transitions and to the 3/2- resonance region in the 11Be continuum spectrum.

## Method

The authors solved the Faddeev equations in coordinate space using the hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method, treating 12Be as a 10Be core plus two neutrons. Four different neutron-10Be potentials (labeled I through IV) were constructed to reproduce the 11Be spectrum, with the s-wave potential being the most crucial; the neutron-neutron interaction used either a gaussian potential or the Argonne v8 potential. The angular part of the Faddeev equations was expanded in hyperspherical harmonics up to maximum hypermomentum values between Kmax=22 and Kmax=122 depending on the component, and the coupled radial equations were solved to obtain bound state energies and wave functions.

## Key numbers

- Ground state 0+1 binding energy: -3.60 MeV (potential I), -3.22 MeV (potential II), -3.64 MeV (potential III), -3.77 MeV (potential IV), -3.53 MeV (I+v8), compared to experimental -3.67 MeV
- 2+ state binding energy: -0.54 MeV (potential I), -0.36 MeV (potential II), -0.66 MeV (potential III), -0.96 MeV (potential IV), -0.58 MeV (I+v8), compared to experimental -1.56 ± 0.01 MeV
- 0+2 state binding energy: -0.62 MeV (potential I), -0.64 MeV (potential II), -0.64 MeV (potential III), -0.61 MeV (potential IV), -0.74 MeV (I+v8), compared to experimental -1.43 ± 0.02 MeV
- 1- state binding energy: -0.97 MeV (potentials I, II, III), -1.23 MeV (potential IV), -1.12 MeV (I+v8), compared to experimental -0.99 ± 0.03 MeV
- 0- state binding energy: -0.96 MeV (potential I), -0.89 MeV (potential II), -0.96 MeV (potential III), -0.93 MeV (potential IV), -1.17 MeV (I+v8), with no experimental value available (predicted as isomeric state)
- The 5/2+ resonance in 11Be at 1.28 MeV above threshold with width 0.4 MeV was used to fix the d-wave potential strength
- The 3/2+ resonance in 11Be at 2.90 MeV above threshold was used to fix the d3/2 potential, with width 0.4 MeV (potentials I, II, IV) or 1.1 MeV (potential III)

## Context

This work extends previous three-body calculations of 12Be using the hyperharmonic expansion method by Thompson (1994), Nunes et al. (1996b), and Nunes et al. (2002), which required extrapolation due to slow convergence and used Kmax up to 20 for the ground state. The hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method is more suitable for non-borromean systems like 12Be where two-body subsystems have bound states, and permits much larger Kmax values guaranteeing convergence. The results enable comparison with new experimental data including the 10Be+n continuum energy spectrum from Pain et al. (2006), and establish that core excitations contribute approximately 20% to the wave function, consistent with the revised estimate of Nunes et al. (2002) rather than the earlier 42% estimate of Nunes et al. (1996b).
