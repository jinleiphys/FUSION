# Structural evolution in germanium and selenium nuclei within the mapped interacting boson model based on the Gogny energy density functional

> The shape transitions and shape coexistence in the Ge and Se isotopes are studied within the interacting boson model (IBM) with the microscopic input from the self-consistent mean-field calculation based on the Gogny-D1M energy density functional. The mean-field energy surface as a function of the quadrupole shape variables $β$ and $γ$, obtained from the constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method, is mapped onto the expectation value of the IBM Hamiltonian with configuration mixing in the boson condensate state. The resultant Hamiltonian is used to compute excitation energies and electromagnetic properties of the selected nuclei $^{66-94}$Ge and $^{68-96}$Se. Our calculation suggests that many nuclei exhibit $γ$ softness. Coexistence between prolate and oblate, as well as between spherical and $γ$-soft, shapes is also observed. The method provides a reasonable description of the observed systematics of the excitation energy of the low-lying energy levels and transition strengths for nuclei below the neutron shell closure $N=50$, and provides predictions on the spectroscopy of neutron-rich Ge and Se isotopes with $52\leq N\leq 62$, where data are scarce or not available.

## Key claim

The mapped interacting boson model (IBM) based on the Gogny-D1M energy density functional provides a reasonable description of the observed systematics of low-lying excitation energies and transition strengths for Ge and Se nuclei below the neutron shell closure N=50, and offers predictions for neutron-rich isotopes with 52 ≤ N ≤ 62 where experimental data are scarce or unavailable, though the method overestimates some non-yrast state energies and shows discrepancies for E(2⁺₁) near N=40 in Ge isotopes.

## Method

Constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations based on the Gogny-D1M energy density functional were performed to obtain mean-field energy surfaces as functions of the quadrupole deformation parameters β and γ for ⁶⁶⁻⁹⁴Ge and ⁶⁸⁻⁹⁶Se. These fermionic energy surfaces were then mapped onto the expectation value of an interacting boson model (IBM) Hamiltonian with configuration mixing in the boson condensate state, using a 2×2 matrix formalism that includes normal (0p-0h) and intruder (2p-2h) configurations. The resulting IBM Hamiltonian was diagonalized to compute excitation energies, B(E2) transition probabilities, spectroscopic quadrupole moments, and ρ²(E0) monopole transition rates.

## Key numbers

- The study covers ⁶⁶⁻⁹⁴Ge and ⁶⁸⁻⁹⁶Se isotopes, with boson numbers ranging from 2 to 7 (⁶⁶⁻⁸²Ge), 3 to 8 (⁸⁴⁻⁹⁴Ge), 3 to 8 (⁶⁸⁻⁸⁴Se), and 4 to 9 (⁸⁶⁻⁹⁶Se)
- Configuration mixing was included for ⁶⁶,⁷⁰⁻⁷⁴,⁹⁰⁻⁹⁴Ge and ⁶⁸⁻⁷⁶,⁹⁰⁻⁹⁶Se based on the criterion that a second minimum in the mean-field energy surface was clear enough to constrain the intruder Hamiltonian
- The effective charges e₂ and e₀ were fitted to reproduce experimental B(E2; 2⁺₁→0⁺₁) and ρ²(E0; 0⁺₂→0⁺₁) values for N=42 and N=40 nuclei, respectively
- The energy difference between neighboring contours in the mean-field energy surface plots is 100 keV
- The parameter χ changes sign between N=38 and N=44 for the normal configuration in Ge isotopes, consistent with the oblate-to-prolate transition

## Context

This work extends the fermion-to-boson mapping procedure introduced by Nomura (2008) to the Ge and Se isotopic chains, following previous successful applications to shape coexistence (Nomura, 2016), A≈100 nuclei (Nomura, 2016), and quadrupole/octupole transitions in light actinide and rare-earth regions (Nomura, 2014, 2015). The Gogny-D1M results agree well with prior relativistic mean-field calculations by Niksic (2014) and with 5D collective Hamiltonian results based on the Gogny-D1S EDF by Gaudefroy (2009). The approach enables systematic predictions for neutron-rich isotopes beyond N=50 where experimental data are lacking, complementing phenomenological IBM studies by Duval (1983), Kaup (1983), and Padilla (2006).
