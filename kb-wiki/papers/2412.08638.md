# An Improved Precision Calculation of the $0νββ$ Contact Term within Chiral Effective Field Theory

> Neutrinoless double-beta ($0νββ$) decay is an as-yet unobserved nuclear process, which stands to provide crucial insights for model-building beyond the Standard Model of particle physics. Its detection would simultaneously confirm the hypothesis that neutrinos are Majorana fermions, thus violating lepton-number conservation, and provide the first measurement of the absolute neutrino mass scale. This work aims to improve the estimation within chiral effective field theory of the so-called ''contact term'' for $0νββ$-decay, a short-range two-nucleon effect which is unaccounted for in traditional nuclear approaches to the process. We conduct a thorough review of the justifications for this contact term and the most precise computation of its size to date ($g_ν^{NN}$ = 1.3(6) at renormalisation point $μ=m_π$), whose precision is limited by a truncation to elastic intermediate hadronic states. We then perform an extension of this analysis to a subleading class of inelastic intermediate states which we characterise, delivering an updated figure for the contact coefficient ($g_ν^{NN}$ = 1.4(3) at $μ=m_π$) with uncertainty reduced by half. Such ab initio nuclear results, especially with enhanced precision, show promise for the resolution of disagreements between estimates of $0νββ$ from different many-body methods.

## Key claim

The paper improves the precision of the contact term coefficient for neutrinoless double-beta decay within chiral effective field theory by extending the analysis to include inelastic \(NN\pi\) intermediate states, reducing the dominant uncertainty from truncation to elastic states by half. The updated coefficient \(g_\nu^{NN} = 1.4(3)\) at renormalisation point \(\mu = m_\pi\) is consistent with the previous value of \(1.3(6)\) but with significantly reduced error.

## Method

The authors first review the chiral effective field theory framework and the matching procedure of Cirigliano et al. (2021) that connects low-momentum \(\chi EFT\) to high-momentum perturbative QCD to determine the contact counterterm coefficient. They then extend this analysis by explicitly including the lowest-lying inelastic intermediate states, namely \(NN\pi\) states, in the calculation of the contact term, which previously only accounted for elastic \(NN\) intermediate states. The updated coefficient is obtained by evaluating the contributions from these \(NN\pi\) states and combining them with the existing elastic-state results, using dimensional regularisation and the same renormalisation scheme at \(\mu = m_\pi\).

## Key numbers

* Previous contact coefficient: \(g_\nu^{NN} = 1.3(6)\) at renormalisation point \(\mu = m_\pi\), with the dominant \(38\%\) relative error from truncation to elastic \(NN\) intermediate states.
* Updated contact coefficient: \(g_\nu^{NN} = 1.4(3)\) at \(\mu = m_\pi\), with uncertainty reduced by half compared to the previous result.
* The updated error budget: \(g_\nu^{NN}|_{NN + NN\pi} \simeq 1.40(20)_{V_S}(5)_{\text{parameters}}(3)_{\text{inelastic}} = 1.4(3)\), where the inelastic error is now \(3\) rather than \(50\).
* The chiral expansion parameter is \(\frac{Q}{\Lambda_\chi} \simeq \frac{m_\pi}{\Lambda_\chi} \sim \frac{1}{7.5}\), with \(\Lambda_\chi \sim 1\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the series of papers by Cirigliano et al. (2018-2021) that first identified the need for a short-range contact counterterm in neutrinoless double-beta decay calculations within chiral effective field theory. The previous most precise computation (Cirigliano et al., 2021) was limited by truncation to elastic intermediate hadronic states, which contributed a \(38\%\) relative error. By including the subleading class of inelastic \(NN\pi\) states, the present work reduces this uncertainty by half, enabling more precise ab initio nuclear matrix element calculations that may help resolve disagreements between estimates from different many-body methods such as those reviewed by Engel (2017) and Agostini et al. (2023).
