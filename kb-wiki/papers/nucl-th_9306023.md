# Microscopic multicluster description of neutron-halo nuclei with a stochastic variational method

> To test a multicluster approach for halo nuclei, we give a unified description for the ground states of $^6$He and $^8$He in a model comprising an $α$ cluster and single-neutron clusters. The intercluster wave function is taken a superposition of terms belonging to different arrangements, each defined by a set of Jacobi coordinates. Each term is then a superposition of products of gaussian functions of the individual Jacobi coordinates with different widths, projected to angular momenta $l=0$ or 1. To avoid excessively large dimensions and ``overcompleteness", stochastic methods were tested for selecting the gaussians spanning the basis. For $^6$He, we were able to calculate ground-state energies that are virtully exact within the subspace defined by the arrangements and $l$ values, and we found that preselected random sets of bases (with or without simulated annealing) yield excellent numerical convergence to this ``exact" value with thoroughly truncated bases. For $^8$He good energy convergence was achieved in a state space comprising three arrangements with all $l=0$, and there are indications showing that the contributions of other subspaces are likely to be small. The $^6$He and $^8$He energies are reproduced by the same effective force very well, and the matter radii obtained are similar to those of other sophisticated calculations.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a stochastic variational method with preselected random bases (with or without simulated annealing) can achieve excellent numerical convergence to virtually exact ground-state energies for neutron-halo nuclei within a multicluster model, while using thoroughly truncated bases. The hedge is that the "exact" energies are only exact within the subspace defined by the chosen arrangements and angular momentum values, not necessarily the full physical space.

## Method

The authors model \(^6\)He as an \(\alpha\) cluster plus two neutrons and \(^8\)He as an \(\alpha\) cluster plus four neutrons, using a linear variational method with trial wave functions that are superpositions of terms from different cluster arrangements (sets of Jacobi coordinates). Each term expands the intercluster relative motion in Gaussian functions of the Jacobi coordinates with different widths, projected to angular momenta \(l=0\) or 1. To avoid excessively large basis dimensions and overcompleteness, the authors test stochastic methods for selecting the Gaussian basis elements, including random preselection, utility testing with a minimum energy improvement threshold, and simulated annealing.

## Key numbers

- For \(^6\)He, the effective force with exchange parameter \(u=1.14\) produces a ground-state energy of \(E=-1.016\) MeV relative to the three-body breakup threshold, while the experimental value is \(-0.975\) MeV.
- The \(\alpha\) cluster size parameter is \(\nu=0.303\) fm\(^{-2}\), with rms point matter radius 1.363 fm and energy \(E_\alpha=-25.581\) MeV.
- In the T\(_{00}\) subspace of \(^6\)He, a 100-dimensional basis yields energy \(-0.395\) MeV; with stochastic preselection, dimensions of 30–50 produce energies around \(-0.367\) to \(-0.395\) MeV with 20–30 keV accuracy.
- For \(^8\)He in the \(\{[ \alpha({\rm nn})]{\rm n}\}{\rm n}\) subspace with all \(l=0\), a preselected random basis converges to \(E=-2.970\) MeV at dimension 300, while utility testing with \(\varepsilon=0.005\) MeV reaches \(-2.961\) MeV at dimension 151.
- The best single-configuration energy for \(^8\)He is from the \(\{[(\alpha{\rm n}){\rm n}]{\rm n}\}{\rm n}'\) configuration with \(l=1\) components, which improves the energy by only 0.0015 MeV when added to the best \(l=0\) multiconfiguration result.

## Context

This work extends the multicluster approach previously applied to \(^6\)Li by Csoto and Lovas (1993) and addresses shortcomings of the cluster-orbital shell model (COSM) of Suzuki, Ikeda, and Wang (1988), which struggles with convergence for systems with more valence particles. The stochastic variational method enables practical calculations for \(^8\)He as a five-cluster system, which was previously intractable, and provides a foundation for future applications of the wave functions to other observables such as \(\beta\)-decay properties.
