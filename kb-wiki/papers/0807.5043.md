# Monte Carlo approach to nuclei and nuclear matter

> We report on the most recent applications of the Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) method. The equation of state (EOS) for pure neutron matter in both normal and BCS phase and the superfluid gap in the low--density regime are computed, using a realistic Hamiltonian containing the Argonne AV8' plus Urbana IX three--nucleon interaction. Preliminary results for the EOS of isospin--asymmetric nuclear matter are also presented.

## Key claim
The Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) method can compute the equation of state and superfluid gap for neutron matter using a realistic Hamiltonian (Argonne AV8' plus Urbana IX three-nucleon interaction), but the results for isospin-asymmetric nuclear matter are only preliminary, and the three-body forces used may not describe high-density systems realistically.

## Method
The authors applied the AFDMC method to simulate large nucleonic systems (up to 114 neutrons) by sampling spin-isospin states of nucleons, using a non-relativistic Hamiltonian with Argonne AV8' two-body and Urbana IX three-body potentials. For neutron matter in the normal phase they used a Slater determinant of plane wave orbitals as the trial wave function, while for the superfluid phase they used a pfaffian (antisymmetric product of independent pairs). The simulations were guided by variational calculations using FHNC theory to compute the trial wave function.

## Key numbers
- Pure neutron matter equation of state computed for densities up to 9 times nuclear saturation density (rho_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}), relevant for neutron star interiors
- AFDMC simulations performed for neutron matter with up to 114 neutrons
- AFDMC applied to nuclear matter with up to 108 nucleons
- AFDMC used to simulate medium-sized nuclei up to A=40
- The Urbana IX three-body interaction was fitted to light nuclei and to reproduce the expected saturation energy of nuclear matter

## Context
This work extends the AFDMC method developed by Schmidt (1999) to compute properties of neutron matter and asymmetric nuclear matter, building on earlier Quantum Monte Carlo approaches by Pudliner (1997) and Pieper (2005) for light nuclei. The results show limitations of other many-body methods and of modern nuclear interactions based on two-plus three-body potentials in the high-density regime, particularly noting that Illinois three-body forces (Pieper, 2001) provide unrealistic overbinding of neutron systems at high density. The approach enables testing of nuclear interaction models without the bias of more approximate methods, and is relevant for understanding neutron star structure.
