# Manifestations of dark matter and variations of fundamental constants in atoms and astrophysical phenomena

> We present an overview of recent developments in the detection of light bosonic dark matter, including axion, pseudoscalar axion-like and scalar dark matter, which form either a coherently oscillating classical field or topological defects (solitons). We emphasise new high-precision laboratory and astrophysical measurements, in which the sought effects are linear in the underlying interaction strength between dark matter and ordinary matter, in contrast to traditional detection schemes for dark matter, where the effects are quadratic or higher order in the underlying interaction parameters and are extremely small. New terrestrial experiments include measurements with atomic clocks, spectroscopy, atomic and solid-state magnetometry, torsion pendula, ultracold neutrons, and laser interferometry. New astrophysical observations include pulsar timing, cosmic radiation lensing, Big Bang nucleosynthesis and cosmic microwave background measurements. We also discuss various recently proposed mechanisms for the induction of slow `drifts', oscillating variations and transient-in-time variations of the fundamental constants of Nature by dark matter, which offer a more natural means of producing a cosmological evolution of the fundamental constants compared with traditional dark energy-type theories, which invoke a (nearly) massless underlying field. Thus, measurements of variation of the fundamental constants gives us a new tool in dark matter searches.

## Key claim

The paper argues that measurements of variations in fundamental constants (slow drifts, oscillations, or transient changes) can serve as a new tool for detecting light bosonic dark matter, with the key advantage that the sought effects are linear in the underlying interaction strength between dark matter and ordinary matter, unlike traditional detection schemes where effects are quadratic or higher order and extremely small.

## Method

The authors present a review of theoretical mechanisms by which light bosonic dark matter (axions, pseudoscalar axion-like particles, and scalar particles) can induce variations in fundamental constants through coherently oscillating classical fields or topological defects. They survey proposed terrestrial detection methods including atomic clocks, spectroscopy, atomic and solid-state magnetometry, torsion pendula, ultracold neutron experiments, and laser interferometry, as well as astrophysical observations including pulsar timing, cosmic radiation lensing, Big Bang nucleosynthesis, and cosmic microwave background measurements.

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework connecting dark matter detection to fundamental constant variations, building on the Peccei-Quinn mechanism (Peccei and Quinn, 1977) for axions and the vacuum misalignment production mechanism (Wilczek, 1983; Abbott, 1983; Dine, 1983). The authors contrast their proposed linear-in-coupling searches with traditional WIMP scattering experiments (such as SuperCDMS, CoGeNT, CRESST, DAMA, LUX, and XENON) where effects scale as the fourth power of interaction parameters, and with traditional axion haloscope (ADMX) and helioscope (CAST) experiments. The work enables new experimental programs using atomic clocks, co-magnetometers, and other precision instruments to search for dark matter through oscillating fundamental constants, complementing existing astrophysical constraints from stellar evolution (Raffelt, 2008) and supernova energy loss arguments.
