# Parquet theory for molecular systems: Formalism and static kernel parquet approximation

> The $GW$ approximation has become a method of choice for predicting quasiparticle properties in solids and large molecular systems, owing to its favorable accuracy-cost balance. However, its accuracy is the result of a fortuitous cancellation of vertex corrections in the polarizability and self-energy. Hence, when attempting to go beyond $GW$ through inclusion of vertex corrections, the accuracy can deteriorate if this delicate balance is disrupted. In this work, we explore an alternative route that theoretically goes beyond $GW$: the parquet formalism. Unlike approaches that focus on a single correlation channel, such as the electron-hole channel in $GW$ or the particle-particle channel in $T$-matrix theory, parquet theory treats all two-body scattering channels on an equal footing. We present the formal structure of the parquet equations, which couple the one-body Green's function, the self-energy, and the two-body vertex. We discuss the approximations necessary to solve this set of equations, the advantages and limitations of this approach, outline its implementation for molecular systems, and assess its accuracy for principal ionization potentials of small molecular systems.

## Key claim

The parquet formalism provides a theoretically rigorous route beyond the $GW$ approximation by treating all two-body scattering channels (electron-hole, transversal electron-hole, and particle-particle) on an equal footing, but the authors caution that the static kernel approximation they employ to make the equations tractable for molecular systems is "quite severe" and "must therefore be carefully evaluated in practice."

## Method

The authors present the formal structure of the parquet equations, which couple the one-body Green's function, the self-energy, and the two-body vertex function through the Dyson-Schwinger equation, three Bethe-Salpeter equations (one per correlation channel), and the parquet decomposition of the vertex. To make the equations computationally feasible for molecular systems, they introduce a static kernel approximation where the electron-hole and particle-particle irreducible vertices are assumed to be instantaneous (frequency-independent) effective interactions, and they project all quantities into a spin-orbital basis. The resulting self-energy is decomposed into a second-order correlation part plus contributions from the electron-hole and particle-particle reducible vertices, which are obtained by diagonalizing effective Hamiltonian matrices derived from the Bethe-Salpeter equations.

## Key numbers

* The modern $GW$ implementations scale as $\order*{K^3}$ or $\order*{K^4}$ where $K$ is the size of the one-body basis set.
* Under the parquet approximation, the self-energy remains exact up to fourth order in the Coulomb interaction, with the next terms in the expansion of the fully irreducible vertex appearing at fourth order and corresponding self-energy contributions emerging at fifth order.
* The electron-hole Bethe-Salpeter equation yields eigenvalues $\boldsymbol{\Omega}^\eh$ corresponding to neutral excitation energies, while the particle-particle Bethe-Salpeter equation yields eigenvalues $\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{\ee}$ and $\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{\hh}$ corresponding to double electron affinities and double ionization potentials, respectively.

## Context

This work extends the parquet formalism, originally introduced by de Dominicis and Martin (1964) and later revisited by Bickers and co-workers in the context of the fluctuation-exchange (FLEX) approximation, to molecular systems for the first time. It contrasts with the $GW$ approximation (Hedin, 1965), which focuses solely on the electron-hole channel and whose accuracy relies on a fortuitous cancellation of vertex corrections, and with the particle-particle $T$-matrix approximation (Baym, 1961; Danielewicz, 1984; Marie, 2024). The approach enables a systematic, self-consistent treatment of two-particle scattering processes that respects crossing symmetry and the Pauli exclusion principle, unlike $GW$ which suffers from self-screening errors, though it sacrifices the conservation properties of Baym-Kadanoff conserving schemes (Baym, 1961, 1962).
