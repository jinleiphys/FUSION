# Tests of the nuclear equation of state and superfluid and superconducting gaps using the Cassiopeia A neutron star

> The observed rapid cooling of the Cassiopeia A neutron star can be interpreted as being caused by neutron and proton transitions from normal to superfluid and superconducting states in the stellar core. Here we present two new Chandra ACIS-S Graded observations of this neutron star and measurements of the neutron star mass M and radius R found from consistent fitting of both the X-ray spectra and cooling behavior. This comparison is only possible for individual nuclear equations of state. We test phenomenological superfluid and superconducting gap models which mimic many of the known theoretical models against the cooling behavior. Our best-fit solution to the Cassiopeia A data is one in which the (M,R) = (1.44 Msun,12.6 km) neutron star is built with the BSk21 equation of state, strong proton superconductor and moderate neutron triplet superfluid gap models, and a pure iron envelope or a thin carbon layer on top of an iron envelope, although there are still large observational and theoretical uncertainties.

## Key claim

The observed rapid cooling of the Cassiopeia A neutron star can be consistently fitted by a specific combination of nuclear equation of state and superfluid/superconducting gap models, but only when both the X-ray spectra and cooling behavior are fitted simultaneously; the best-fit solution has (M,R) = (1.44 Msun, 12.6 km) with the BSk21 equation of state, strong proton superconductor and moderate neutron triplet superfluid gap models, and a pure iron envelope or thin carbon layer on iron, although large observational and theoretical uncertainties remain.

## Method

The authors analyzed two new Chandra ACIS-S Graded observations (49-ks and 50-ks taken in 2013 and 2014) together with seven earlier observations, fitting all nine spectra simultaneously to measure surface temperatures using non-magnetic partially ionized carbon atmosphere models. They constructed neutron star models by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations with three nuclear equations of state (APR, BSk20, BSk21) and used a cooling code to simulate thermal evolution, testing phenomenological superfluid and superconducting gap models parameterized by a five-parameter formula for the gap energy as a function of Fermi momentum. For each equation of state and gap model combination, they varied the neutron star mass, calculated the temperature evolution, and performed a least squares fit to the observed temperature decline while also checking consistency with the spectral fit.

## Key numbers

- Best-fit neutron star mass and radius: (M,R) = (1.44 Msun, 12.6 km) for the BSk21 equation of state
- Best-fit radius at approximately 90% confidence: R = 11.6_{-0.2}^{+0.1} km for APR, 11.7 ± 0.1 km for BSk20, and 12.55 ± 0.05 km for BSk21
- Best-fit mass at approximately 90% confidence: M ≈ 1.4 ± 0.3 Msun for any of the three equations of state
- Observed surface temperatures (Teff) from the nine Chandra observations range from 2.145_{-0.008}^{+0.009} MK (year 2000.08) to 2.045_{-0.009}^{+0.009} MK (year 2014.36)
- Critical temperature constraints for neutron triplet superfluidity: Tcnt ≈ (5-9) × 10^8 K and for proton singlet superconductivity: Tcp ∼ (2-3) × 10^9 K
- Hydrogen column density from fits: ≈ (1.6-1.8) × 10^22 cm^{-2}, with regions near the neutron star having ≈ (1.7-2) × 10^22 cm^{-2}
- Maximum mass thresholds for direct Urca cooling: Mdu = 1.96 Msun for APR and Mdu = 1.59 Msun for BSk21; BSk20 does not produce direct Urca cooling for any mass

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Page et al. (2011) and Shternin et al. (2011) that constrained critical temperatures for superfluidity but assumed either the X-ray spectra best-fit mass or fit the temperature decline by varying mass without checking spectral consistency. The authors perform the first consistent fitting of both the Cassiopeia A neutron star spectra and temperature evolution for mass and radius, testing three nuclear equations of state (APR from Akmal et al. 1998, BSk20 and BSk21 from Fantina et al. 2013) against phenomenological gap models that mimic many theoretical calculations. The results enable future work to motivate nuclear physicists to calculate both the equation of state and superfluid/superconducting gap energies in a self-consistent manner, and to test additional gap models and envelope compositions as observational precision improves.
