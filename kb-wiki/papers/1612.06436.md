# Equilibration and freeze-out of an expanding gas in a transport approach in a Friedmann-Robertson-Walker metric

> Motivated by a recent finding of an exact solution of the relativistic Boltzmann equation in a Friedmann-Robertson-Walker spacetime, we implement this metric into the newly developed transport approach Simulating Many Accelerated Strongly-interacting Hadrons (SMASH). We study the numerical solution of the transport equation and compare it to this exact solution for massless particles. We also compare a different initial condition, for which the transport equation can be independently solved numerically. Very nice agreement is observed in both cases. Having passed these checks for the SMASH code, we study a gas of massive particles within the same spacetime, where the particle decoupling is forced by the Hubble expansion. In this simple scenario we present an analysis of the freeze-out times, as function of the masses and cross sections of the particles. The results might be of interest for their potential application to relativistic heavy-ion collisions, for the characterization of the freeze-out process in terms of hadron properties.

## Key claim
The SMASH transport code, when adapted to a Friedmann-Robertson-Walker metric, reproduces the exact analytical solution of the relativistic Boltzmann equation for massless particles with constant cross section, and can be extended to study dynamical freeze-out of massive particles where the decoupling time depends on particle mass and cross section. The freeze-out analysis relies on a toy model with an arbitrary Hubble parameter \(H = \beta t\) rather than a cosmologically motivated expansion.

## Method
The SMASH transport approach was adapted to a flat FRW metric \(ds^2 = dt^2 - a^2(t)(dx^2 + dy^2 + dz^2)\) with scale factor \(a(t) = \sqrt{1 + (b_r/l_0)t}\) for massless particles. For massive particles, a Hubble rate \(H = \beta t\) with \(\beta = 0.01\ \text{fm}^{-2}\) was used. The code simulated a spherical volume of \(N = 1.5 \times 10^5\) particles with constant elastic cross section, initial radius \(r_0 = 50\ \text{fm}\), and initial temperature \(T_0 = 0.2\ \text{GeV}\) (massless) or \(T_0 = 0.4\ \text{GeV}\) (massive). The distribution function was extracted from the simulation and compared to the analytical solution of Bazow et al. (2015, 2016) for massless particles, and to equilibrium distributions for massive particles. Freeze-out times were extracted by fitting the late-time distribution to a redshifted equilibrium form.

## Key numbers
- Particle number \(N = 1.5 \times 10^5\) in a spherical volume with initial radius \(r_0 = 50\ \text{fm}\).
- Initial temperature \(T_0 = 0.2\ \text{GeV}\) for massless simulations; \(T_0 = 0.4\ \text{GeV}\) for massive simulations.
- Fugacity \(\lambda_0 \simeq 2.7\) for the massless initial condition.
- Ratio \(b_r/l_0 = 0.1\) for the massless scale factor.
- For massive particles (\(m = 2\ \text{GeV}\), \(\sigma = 40\ \text{mb}\)), the freeze-out time extracted from fitting at \(t = 20\ \text{fm}\) is \(t_D = 5.3 \pm 0.6\ \text{fm}\).
- Decoupling temperature from the fit: \(T_D = 0.32 \pm 0.02\ \text{GeV}\).
- Ratio \((a/a_D)^2 = 41 \pm 4\) from the fit, corresponding to \(t_D = 5.2^{+0.8}_{-0.9}\ \text{fm}\).
- For the parametric study, the parameter \(\xi\) in the condition \(\xi \Gamma = H\) ranges from \(\xi \simeq 0.3\)–\(0.4\) for masses \(m > 0.5\ \text{GeV}\) and is stable around \(\xi \simeq 0.4\) for cross sections, increasing to \(\xi \simeq 0.5\) for \(\sigma = 20\ \text{mb}\).

## Context
This work validates the SMASH transport code against the exact analytical solution of the relativistic Boltzmann equation in a FRW metric found by Bazow et al. (2015, 2016), extending previous tests of transport approaches. It also introduces a toy model for dynamical freeze-out driven by Hubble expansion, analogous to the decoupling in the early universe described by Baumann, Kolb, and Bernstein. The results enable future studies of sequential freeze-out in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, where the freeze-out time depends on hadron properties such as mass and cross section, potentially testing hypotheses of collective versus sequential decoupling.
