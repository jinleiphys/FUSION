# Quantum Computing Universal Thermalization Dynamics in a (2+1)D Lattice Gauge Theory

> Simulating non-equilibrium phenomena in strongly-interacting quantum many-body systems, including thermalization, is a promising application of near-term and future quantum computation. By performing experiments on a digital quantum computer consisting of fully-connected optically-controlled trapped ions, we study the role of entanglement in the thermalization dynamics of a $Z_2$ lattice gauge theory in 2+1 spacetime dimensions. Using randomized-measurement protocols, we efficiently learn a classical approximation of non-equilibrium states that yields the gap-ratio distribution and the spectral form factor of the entanglement Hamiltonian. These observables exhibit universal early-time signals for quantum chaos, a prerequisite for thermalization. Our work, therefore, establishes quantum computers as robust tools for studying universal features of thermalization in complex many-body systems, including in gauge theories.

## Key claim

Using a digital trapped-ion quantum computer, the authors observe universal early-time signatures of quantum chaos in the entanglement spectrum of a (2+1)D Z2 lattice gauge theory, establishing that randomized-measurement-based entanglement-Hamiltonian tomography can recover these universal properties despite experimental imperfections and Trotterization errors.

## Method

The authors implement a Z2 lattice gauge theory in 2+1 dimensions on a digital quantum computer consisting of 12 qubits realized by 15 optically-controlled 171Yb+ trapped ions. They prepare initial product states that satisfy Gauss law constraints, evolve the system via Trotterized time evolution with coupling g=0.85, and perform randomized-measurement protocols with 24 random bases and 750 shots per basis to reconstruct an approximate entanglement Hamiltonian using a Bisognano-Wichmann-inspired ansatz. From the reconstructed entanglement spectrum, they compute the gap-ratio distribution and the spectral form factor to diagnose quantum chaos.

## Key numbers

- 12 qubits used to encode the (2+1)D Z2 lattice gauge theory with Lx=10 plaquettes and Ly=1 plaquette
- Coupling constant g=0.85 used for all time evolution experiments
- 4 Trotter steps implemented for time evolution, each using 12 variable-angle Molmer-Soerensen gates
- Nshots=500 experiments per measurement point for single- and two-qubit observables; Nshots=750 per basis for randomized measurements
- 24 random bases used in the randomized-measurement protocol
- 6 randomly chosen initial electric eigenstates used for thermalization studies
- Average gap ratio evolves from approximately 0.4 (Poisson value) toward approximately 0.6 (Gaussian Unitary Ensemble value)
- Power-law exponent for the entanglement spectral form factor ramp: kappa = 0.6 plus or minus 0.2

## Context

This work extends theoretical predictions by Rakovszky et al. (2019) and Chang et al. (2019) regarding entanglement-spectrum signatures of quantum chaos to an experimental setting with a lattice gauge theory, going beyond previous analog quantum simulation studies by Smith et al. (2016), Neyenhuis et al. (2017), Morong et al. (2021), and Kyprianidis et al. (2021) which had limited programmability. The results enable future quantum-computer studies of thermalization dynamics in gauge theories relevant to high-energy and nuclear physics, including models where classical simulation is intractable.
