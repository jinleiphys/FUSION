# Analysis of anisotropic flow with Lee-Yang zeroes

> We present a new method to extract anisotropic flow in heavy ion collisions from the genuine correlation among a large number of particles. Anisotropic flow is obtained from the zeroes in the complex plane of a generating function of azimuthal correlations, in close analogy with the theory of phase transitions by Lee and Yang. Flow is first estimated globally, i.e., averaged over the phase space covered by the detector, and then differentially, as a function of transverse momentum and rapidity for identified particles. The corresponding estimates are less biased by nonflow correlations than with any other method. The practical implementation of the method is rather straightforward. Furthermore, it automatically takes into account most corrections due to azimuthal anisotropies in the detector acceptance. The main limitation of the method is statistical errors, which can be significantly larger than with the ``standard'' method of flow analysis if the flow and/or the event multiplicities are too small. In practice, we expect this to be the most accurate method to analyze directed and elliptic flow in fixed-target heavy-ion collisions between 100 MeV and 10 GeV per nucleon (at the Darmstadt SIS synchrotron and the Brookhaven Alternating Gradient Synchrotron), and elliptic flow at ultrarelativistic energies (at the Brookhaven Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider, and the forthcoming Large Hadron Collider at CERN).

## Key claim

The paper presents a new method for extracting anisotropic flow from heavy-ion collisions that is less biased by nonflow correlations than any previous method, but it carries significantly larger statistical errors when flow or event multiplicities are too small, limiting its practical applicability to cases where the flow signal is sufficiently strong.

## Method

The method constructs a generating function from the projection of the event flow vector onto a fixed direction, then evaluates this generating function on the imaginary axis to find the location of its first minimum. The integrated flow is obtained from the position of this minimum using the first root of the Bessel function J0, and differential flow is extracted from a ratio of event averages involving the same generating function evaluated at that minimum. The analysis is repeated for several equally spaced values of the projection angle theta, and the results are averaged to reduce statistical uncertainties.

## Key numbers

- The first minimum of the generating function modulus |G^theta(ir)| occurs at r = r_0^theta, and the integrated flow estimate is V_n^theta{infinity} = j_01 / r_0^theta, where j_01 = 2.405 (the first root of Bessel function J0).
- For a simulated data set of 20000 events with multiplicity M = 300 and mean elliptic flow v_2 = 6%, the reconstructed integrated flow V_2{infinity}/M = 5.95%, in agreement with the input value.
- The expected statistical uncertainty on each individual V_2^theta{infinity}/M estimate is 0.092%, and the uncertainty on the average over theta is 0.051%.
- For differential flow in a bin with input v'_2 = 7% and approximately 30 protons per event, the reconstructed v'_2{infinity} = 7.00 +/- 0.26% with autocorrelations and 7.20 +/- 0.26% without autocorrelations.
- For the higher harmonic v'_4 with input 3%, the reconstructed value with autocorrelations is 3.60 +/- 0.29% and without autocorrelations is 3.03 +/- 0.29%.
- With a detector having a 60-degree blind angle (one sixth of azimuthal coverage missing), the reconstructed V_2{infinity}/<M> = 6.004 +/- 0.066% for input v_2 = 6%, requiring a correction factor of 1.0068.
- The inequality |G^theta(i r_0^theta)| < 2 / sqrt(N) should hold with 95% confidence level, where N is the number of events.

## Context

This work extends the cumulant expansion method for flow analysis developed by Borghini, Dinh, and Ollitrault (2000-2002), which isolated genuine multiparticle correlations by subtracting lower-order contributions. The new method goes beyond computing cumulants at fixed order (up to 8-particle cumulants were used at SPS) by studying the large-order behavior through the zeroes of a generating function, analogous to the Lee-Yang theory of phase transitions. It enables more reliable extraction of directed and elliptic flow at energies from 100 MeV to 10 GeV per nucleon at SIS and AGS, and elliptic flow at RHIC and LHC, where nonflow correlations from minijets and resonance decays had contaminated earlier two-particle and event-plane methods.
