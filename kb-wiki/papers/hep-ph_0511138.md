# Light Quark Condensates at Nonzero Chemical Potentials

> We show that the quark condensates for the two light up and down flavors can have significantly different values in the hadronic phase at nonzero temperature, baryon and isospin chemical potentials. We quantify this difference using a simple model.

## Key claim
The up and down quark condensates can have significantly different values in the hadronic phase at nonzero temperature, baryon and isospin chemical potentials, but only if the axial anomaly is not strong enough to enforce equality; the difference arises solely from massive hadronic modes, not from the pseudo-Goldstone bosons.

## Method
The author uses chiral perturbation theory combined with a virial expansion in the free gas approximation for massive hadrons, treating pions as the dominant low-energy modes and all heavier hadrons as non-interacting Boltzmann gases. The difference between the up and down quark condensates is computed by summing over isospin multiplets (N(939), Σ(1193), Δ(1232)) using the Feynman-Hellman theorem and SU(3) flavor symmetry to estimate the isovector coupling constants \(C_H\). The calculation is restricted to \(|\mu_I| < m_\pi\) to avoid pion superfluid phases.

## Key numbers
- \(C_N / \langle 0|\bar{q}q|0 \rangle \simeq (1.3 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{MeV}^{-1})^2\), estimated from the Gell-Mann–Oakes–Renner relation with \(m_s \simeq 25 \hat{m}\).
- The difference \(\delta = \langle \bar{u}u - \bar{d}d \rangle / \langle 0|\bar{q}q|0 \rangle\) reaches up to \(\sim 50\%\) for \(\mu_B = 750\ \text{MeV}\) and \(\mu_I = 120\ \text{MeV}\).
- The calculation is valid for temperatures below \(\sim 200\ \text{MeV}\) and chemical potentials \(|\mu_I| < m_\pi\) (pion mass \(\sim 140\ \text{MeV}\)).

## Context
This work extends earlier model studies (muBInew, muBINc) that predicted qualitative changes to the QCD phase diagram when both baryon and isospin chemical potentials are nonzero, including the possibility of two separate phase transitions. It uses the same chiral perturbation theory framework as Gerber and Leutwyler (1989) for the temperature dependence of condensates at zero chemical potential. The results enable quantitative predictions for heavy-ion-collision experiments and neutron star physics, where both \(\mu_B\) and \(\mu_I\) are nonzero, and suggest that the axial anomaly may not be strong enough to prevent flavor-dependent condensate differences.
