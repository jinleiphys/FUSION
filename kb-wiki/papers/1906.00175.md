# The structure of cold neutron star with a quark core within the MIT and NJL models

> Neutron star due to their high interior matter density are expected to be composed of a quark core, a mixed quark-hadron matter, and a layer of hadronic matter. Thus, in this paper, we compute the equation of state of these parts of neutron star to evaluate its structure properties. We use two models for describing EOS of quark matter, NJL and MIT bag models, and employ three approaches in this work. A density dependent bag constant satisfy the quark confinement in the simple MIT bag model. We also study the interaction behavior of quarks, firstly one gluon exchange within MIT bag model and the secondly dynamical mass will be held as effective interaction that roles between particles. Density dependence of quark mass is obtained from NJL self consistent model. NJL model is a effective manner for justify the chiral symmetry. Applying the Gibbs conditions the equation of state of the quarks and hadrons mixed phase is obtained. Since the hadronic matter is under the influence of strong force of nucleons, we calculate the equation of state of this phase using a powerful variational many-body technique. Finally, we calculate the mass and radius of a cold neutron star with a quark core by numerically solving the TOV equation. To check our used EOS, we compare our results with the recent observational data. Our results are in a good agreement with some observed compact objects such as $SAXJ1748.9-2021$, $4U1608-52$ and $Vela X-1$.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a cold neutron star with a quark core (hybrid star) can have its structure described by either the MIT bag model or the NJL model for quark matter, but only the simple MIT bag model (model 1) and the NJL model (model 3) satisfy both energy and stability conditions; the MIT bag model with one-gluon-exchange corrections (model 2) fails the stability condition for the coupling constants tested, and for alpha=0.5 it also fails the dominant energy condition.

## Method

The authors computed the equation of state (EOS) for three regions of a hybrid neutron star: a hadronic matter layer using the lowest order constrained variational (LOCV) many-body method, a mixed quark-hadron phase using Gibbs conditions for phase equilibrium, and a quark matter core. For the quark core, they employed three approaches: (1) a simple MIT bag model with a density-dependent bag constant, (2) an MIT bag model including one-gluon-exchange corrections with QCD coupling constants alpha=0.16 and alpha=0.5, and (3) the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model with density-dependent dynamical quark masses. The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations were then solved numerically using the resulting EOS to obtain the gravitational mass and radius of the hybrid star.

## Key numbers

* Maximum gravitational mass of hybrid star in MIT bag model (model 1): 1.8 solar masses, with radius 10 km.
* Maximum gravitational mass of hybrid star in NJL model (model 3): 1.75 solar masses, with radius 10.4 km.
* Maximum gravitational mass of neutron star without quark core: 1.98 solar masses, with radius 9.8 km.
* Central energy density for model 1: 25.8 x 10^14 g/cm^3; central pressure: 7.79 x 10^14 g/cm^3.
* Central energy density for model 3: 22.9 x 10^14 g/cm^3; central pressure: 5.56 x 10^14 g/cm^3.
* Central energy density for model 2 with alpha=0.16: 18.25 x 10^14 g/cm^3; central pressure: 17.8 x 10^14 g/cm^3.
* Central energy density for model 2 with alpha=0.5: 14.05 x 10^14 g/cm^3; central pressure: 28.9 x 10^14 g/cm^3.
* The density-dependent bag constant used a Gaussian form with B_0 = 400 MeV/fm^3 and reference density rho_0 = 0.17 fm^-3.
* NJL model parameters: cutoff Lambda = 602.3 MeV, G*Lambda^2 = 1.835 Lambda, K*Lambda^2 = 12.36.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies of hybrid stars using the MIT bag model (Yazdizadeh and Bordbar, 2017) by adding one-gluon-exchange corrections and comparing with the NJL model. The results are compared with observational data for compact objects including SAX J1748.9-2021, 4U1608-52, and Vela X-1, finding good agreement for those objects. However, the calculated maximum masses (1.75-1.8 solar masses) are lower than the mass of PSR J1614-2230 (1.97 solar masses), indicating that the EOS needs modification to match this newer observation; the authors note that other groups (referenced as works from 2013 and 2015) have parameterized their EOS to achieve masses near 2.0 solar masses for that pulsar.
