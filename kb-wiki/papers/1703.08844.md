# Estimating parameter uncertainty in binding-energy models by the frequency-domain bootstrap

> We propose using the frequency-domain bootstrap (FDB) to estimate errors of modeling parameters when the modeling error is itself a major source of uncertainty. Unlike the usual bootstrap or the simple $χ^2$ analysis, the FDB can take into account correlations between errors. It is also very fast compared to the the Gaussian process Bayesian estimate as often implemented for computer model calibration. The method is illustrated drop model of nuclear binding energies. We find that the FDB gives a more conservative estimate of the uncertainty in liquid drop parameters in better accord with more empirical estimates. For the nuclear physics application, there no apparent obstacle to apply the method to the more accurate and detailed models based on density-functional theory.

## Key claim

The frequency-domain bootstrap (FDB) provides a more conservative and realistic estimate of parameter uncertainty in nuclear binding-energy models than the standard χ² method or basic bootstrap, because it accounts for correlations between residuals that those methods ignore; however, the FDB estimate may still not be large enough to be fully realistic, as other density-functional models can give larger deviations.

## Method

The authors apply the FDB to the liquid drop model of nuclear binding energies, using experimental binding energies of 2037 nuclei from the 2003 nuclear mass table. They first perform a least-squares fit to obtain best-fit parameters and residuals, then compute the discrete Fourier transform of the residuals as a function of mass number A. The FDB ensemble is constructed by sampling the Fourier magnitudes with replacement and randomizing the phases uniformly in [0,2π], then inverse transforming to generate new residual sets; for each sample, the model parameters are re-estimated. The N-Z dependence of residuals is handled by adding a χ²-distributed scatter about the A-averaged residuals, with variance taken from the original data.

## Key numbers

- Best-fit nuclear matter binding energy: a_v = 15.58 MeV, with asymmetry term a_a = 22.18 MeV, from least-squares fit to 2037 nuclei
- Residual variance of the liquid drop fit: σ_r = 3.24 MeV
- χ² estimate of uncertainty in a_v: σ_{a_v} = 0.03 MeV
- Basic bootstrap estimate of uncertainty in a_v: σ_{a_v} = 0.03 MeV
- FDB estimate of uncertainty in a_v: σ_{a_v} = 0.17 MeV
- Gaussian process (GP) estimate of uncertainty in a_v: σ_{a_v} = 0.12 MeV
- Reference values from Skyrme energy-density functionals: a_v = 15.88 MeV with σ = 0.15 MeV (Margueron 2017) and a_v = 15.81 MeV with σ = 0.17 MeV (Roca-Maza 2016)
- Earlier fit from Myers and Swiatecki (1966): a_v = 15.68 MeV, with error estimate "say to 1 or 2 %"

## Context

This work extends the use of the frequency-domain bootstrap from time-series analysis (Kreiss and Lahiri 2012) to nuclear physics parameter estimation, addressing the known problem that χ² methods unrealistically underestimate uncertainties in nuclear models (Dobaczewski 2014). The FDB is presented as a computationally efficient alternative to Gaussian process Bayesian calibration (Kennedy and O'Hagan 2001; Higdon 2004; Higdon 2015), requiring only o(S log S) operations versus o(S³) for GP methods, and it avoids the need to specify a particular correlation function. The method enables uncertainty quantification for more sophisticated models such as density-functional theory (Roca-Maza 2016; Bertsch 2005), which achieve factor-of-two better accuracy than the liquid drop model.
