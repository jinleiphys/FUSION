# Dependence of temperatures and kinetic freeze-out volume on centrality in Au-Au and Pb-Pb collisions at high energy

> Centrality-dependent double-differential transverse momentum spectra of negatively charged particles ($π^-$, $K^-$ and $\bar p$) at mid-(pseudo)rapidity interval in nuclear collisions are analyzed by the standard distribution in terms of multi-component. The experimental data measured in gold-gold (Au-Au) collisions by the PHENIX Collaboration at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) and in lead-lead (Pb-Pb) collisions by the ALICE Collaboration at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) are studied. The effective temperature, initial temperature, kinetic freeze-out temperature, transverse flow velocity and kinetic freeze-out volume are extracted from the fitting to transverse momentum spectra. We observed, that the mentioned five quantities increase with the increase of event centrality due to the fact that the average transverse momentum increases with the increase of event centrality. This renders that larger momentum (energy) transfer and further multiple-scattering had happened in central centrality.

## Key claim

The five extracted quantities (effective temperature, initial temperature, kinetic freeze-out temperature, transverse flow velocity, and kinetic freeze-out volume) all increase with event centrality, but this result is model-dependent and contradicts some prior blast-wave model studies which find that kinetic freeze-out temperature in central collisions is less than in peripheral collisions.

## Method

The authors fit double-differential transverse momentum spectra of π⁻, K⁻, and antiprotons at mid-rapidity using a two-component standard distribution (a combination of Boltzmann, Fermi-Dirac, and Bose-Einstein distributions). The effective temperature and kinetic freeze-out volume are extracted as free parameters from the fits to PHENIX Au-Au data at 200 GeV and ALICE Pb-Pb data at 2.76 TeV. The initial temperature is obtained from the root-mean-square transverse momentum via the color string percolation model, while the kinetic freeze-out temperature and transverse flow velocity are extracted from linear fits of effective temperature versus rest mass and mean transverse momentum versus mean energy, respectively.

## Key numbers

- For Au-Au at 200 GeV, π⁻ in 0–5% centrality: effective temperature T₁ = 0.181 ± 0.014 GeV, T₂ = 0.268 ± 0.006 GeV; kinetic freeze-out volume V₁ = 745 ± 100 fm³, V₂ = 1967 ± 309 fm³
- For Au-Au at 200 GeV, π⁻ in 80–92% centrality: effective temperature T₁ = 0.068 ± 0.015 GeV, T₂ = 0.221 ± 0.006 GeV; kinetic freeze-out volume V₁ = 8.0 ± 1.0 fm³, V₂ = 213 ± 13 fm³
- For Pb-Pb at 2.76 TeV, π⁻ in 0–5% centrality: effective temperature T₁ = 0.179 ± 0.010 GeV, T₂ = 0.340 ± 0.004 GeV; kinetic freeze-out volume V₁ = 2517 ± 208 fm³, V₂ = 4099 ± 252 fm³
- For Pb-Pb at 2.76 TeV, π⁻ in 80–90% centrality: effective temperature T₁ = 0.118 ± 0.014 GeV, T₂ = 0.287 ± 0.006 GeV; kinetic freeze-out volume V₁ = 16 ± 2 fm³, V₂ = 1945 ± 102 fm³
- For Au-Au at 200 GeV, antiprotons in 0–5% centrality: effective temperature T₁ = 0.280 ± 0.017 GeV, T₂ = 0.320 ± 0.003 GeV; kinetic freeze-out volume V₁ = 36 ± 7 fm³, V₂ = 786 ± 120 fm³
- For Pb-Pb at 2.76 TeV, antiprotons in 0–5% centrality: effective temperature T₁ = 0.426 ± 0.012 GeV, T₂ = 0.421 ± 0.007 GeV; kinetic freeze-out volume V₁ = 55 ± 6 fm³, V₂ = 1965 ± 195 fm³

## Context

This work extends the systematic study of freeze-out parameters across collision systems and energies, using a two-component standard distribution rather than the blast-wave model commonly employed by other groups. The authors note that their positive correlation between kinetic freeze-out temperature and transverse flow velocity contradicts the negative correlation found in blast-wave model studies by Schnedermann et al. (1993) and others. The results enable comparison of freeze-out properties between RHIC and LHC energies and suggest that the most peripheral Pb-Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV produce similar systems to the most central Au-Au collisions at 200 GeV, supporting the effective energy hypothesis for particle production.
