# The role of three-nucleon potentials within the shell model: past and present

> We survey the impact of nuclear three-body forces on structure properties of nuclei within the shell model. It has long been acknowledged, since the seminal works of Zuker and coworkers, that three-body forces play a fundamental role in making the monopole component of shell-model Hamiltonians, derived from realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials, able to reproduce the observed evolution of the shell structure. In the vast majority of calculations, however, their effects have been taken into account by shell-model practitioners by introducing ad hoc modifications of the monopole matrix elements. During last twenty years, a new theoretical approach, framed within the chiral perturbation theory, has progressed in developing nuclear potentials, where two- and many-body components are naturally and consistently built in. This new class of nuclear forces allows to carry out nuclear structure studies that are improving our ability to understand nuclear phenomena in a microscopic approach. We provide in this work an update on the status of the nuclear shell model based on realistic Hamiltonians that are derived from two- and three-nucleon chiral potentials, focusing on the role of the three-body component to provide the observed shell evolution and closure properties, as well as the location of driplines. To this end, we present the results of shell-model calculations and their comparison with recent experimental measurements, which enlighten the relevance of the inclusion of three-nucleon forces to master our knowledge of the physics of atomic nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper argues that three-nucleon forces (3NFs) are essential for shell-model Hamiltonians derived from realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials to reproduce observed shell evolution, shell closures, and dripline locations, but the abstract glosses over the fact that most shell-model practitioners have historically accounted for 3NF effects only through ad hoc modifications of monopole matrix elements rather than through explicit inclusion of three-body terms.

## Method

The authors derive effective shell-model Hamiltonians using many-body perturbation theory starting from chiral two-nucleon potentials at N$^3$LO and three-nucleon potentials at N$^2$LO, with the three-body component treated via normal-ordering decomposition to produce effective zero-, one-, and two-body contributions. They apply these Hamiltonians in standard shell-model calculations for nuclei in the $p$-shell, $sd$-shell, and $fp$-shell regions, as well as in Gamow shell-model calculations for weakly-bound and unbound nuclei, comparing results with experimental data and with ab initio no-core shell model calculations.

## Key numbers

- The 2PE 3N-potential expression uses the pion-decay constant $f_\pi = 92.4$ MeV (natural units)
- The 1PE 3N-potential uses $\Lambda_\chi = 700$ MeV as the chiral symmetry breaking scale
- The chiral 3NF at N$^2$LO introduces two new low-energy constants $c_D$ and $c_E$ not appearing in the two-body problem
- The $V_{\rm low-k}$ renormalization procedure was employed to soften high-momentum components of the chiral N$^3$LO potential
- The 3NF contribution was shown to be crucial to obtain $^{24}$O as the last bound oxygen isotope
- 3NFs are essential to obtain the shell closure at $N=28$ and to provide single-particle energy splittings consistent with $^{56}$Ni as a doubly magic nucleus
- The Gamow shell model with 3NFs reproduces experimental resonance widths of $^{24}$O excited states
- The repulsive behavior of the 3NF is decisive to raise the energy of $^{16}$F over the proton emission threshold, leading to the Borromean nature of $^{17}$Ne

## Context

This work extends the seminal studies by Zuker and coworkers (Caurier 2005) who first identified that shell-model Hamiltonians derived from realistic two-nucleon forces require modification of their monopole component to reproduce experimental shell evolution, and by Otsuka and coworkers (Otsuka 2010) who first explicitly included 3NF effects in shell-model calculations to reproduce the oxygen isotope dripline. The paper reviews the authors' own program (Fukui 2018, Ma 2019, Coraggio 2020, Coraggio 2021) applying chiral two- and three-body potentials to shell-model and Gamow shell-model calculations across light to intermediate-mass nuclei, and enables future systematic studies of shell evolution, dripline locations, and weakly-bound nuclear structure using microscopically derived Hamiltonians consistent with quantum chromodynamics.
