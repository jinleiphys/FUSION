# Spreading widths of giant resonances in spherical nuclei: damped transient response

> We propose the universal approach to describe spreading widths of monopole, dipole and quadrupole giant resonances in heavy and superheavy spherical nuclei. Our approach is based on the ideas of the random matrix distribution of the coupling between one-phonon and two-phonon states generated in the random phase approximation. We use the Skyrme interaction SLy4 as our model Hamiltonian to create a single-particle spectrum and to analyze excited states of the doubly magic nuclei $^{132}$Sn, $^{208}$Pb and $^{310}$126. Our results demonstrate that the universal approach enables to describe gross structure of the spreading widths of the considered giant resonances.

## Key claim
The authors propose that the spreading widths of giant resonances in heavy and superheavy spherical nuclei can be described by replacing the microscopic phonon-phonon coupling matrix elements with a random Gaussian distribution whose variance is determined either from the root-mean-square of the microscopic coupling or from the onset of Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE) chaos in the nearest-neighbor spacing distribution.

## Method
The authors start from a Hartree-Fock calculation with the Skyrme interaction SLy4 to generate the single-particle spectrum, then solve the random phase approximation (RPA) in a finite-rank separable approximation to obtain one-phonon states (doorway states) and two-phonon states (background states). They diagonalize the Hamiltonian in the space of one-phonon and two-phonon configurations using either the microscopic coupling matrix elements (phonon-phonon coupling, PPC) or a random Gaussian distribution of those matrix elements with the same root-mean-square value, and compare the resulting strength distributions for the isoscalar giant monopole resonance (ISGMR), isovector giant dipole resonance (IVGDR), and isoscalar giant quadrupole resonance (ISGQR) in three doubly magic nuclei.

## Key numbers
- ISGMR in $^{208}$Pb: experimental centroid energy $13.7 \pm 0.1$ MeV and width $3.3 \pm 0.2$ MeV; PPC calculation gives centroid 14.4 MeV and width 3.9 MeV; random coupling gives centroid 14.6 MeV and width 2.7 MeV.
- IVGDR in $^{208}$Pb: experimental centroid energy 13.43 MeV and width 4.07 MeV; PPC calculation gives centroid 14.0 MeV and width 4.9 MeV; random coupling gives centroid 13.8 MeV and width 4.8 MeV.
- ISGQR in $^{208}$Pb: experimental centroid energy $10.89 \pm 0.30$ MeV and width $3.0 \pm 0.3$ MeV; PPC calculation gives centroid 13.0 MeV and width 3.1 MeV; random coupling gives centroid 13.0 MeV and width 2.1 MeV.
- ISGMR in $^{132}$Sn: PPC calculation gives centroid 16.6 MeV and width 4.7 MeV; random coupling gives centroid 16.7 MeV and width 3.4 MeV; empirical systematics gives centroid 15.71 MeV and width 2.7 MeV.
- IVGDR in $^{132}$Sn: experimental centroid $16.1 \pm 0.7$ MeV and width $4.7 \pm 2.1$ MeV; PPC calculation gives centroid 15.4 MeV and width 5.0 MeV; random coupling gives centroid 15.3 MeV and width 5.2 MeV.
- ISGMR in $^{310}$126: PPC calculation gives centroid 12.7 MeV and width 3.0 MeV; random coupling gives centroid 12.7 MeV and width 1.8 MeV; empirical systematics gives centroid 11.82 MeV.
- For the IVGDR of $^{208}$Pb, the spectral rigidity $\Delta_3$ reproduces the GOE distribution up to $L_{\text{max}} = 7$, corresponding to an energy range of about 0.2 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the quasiparticle-phonon model (QPM) and its finite-rank separable approximation (FRSA) developed by Severyukhin et al. (1998) to describe giant resonance spreading widths in heavy and superheavy nuclei. It builds on earlier particle-vibration coupling (PVC) calculations for the monopole resonance in $^{208}$Pb by Bortignon et al. and for the quadrupole resonance by Bortignon et al., and on random matrix theory (RMT) approaches to nuclear spectra. The method enables calculation of spreading widths for nuclei where full shell-model calculations are impractical, such as $^{310}$126, and suggests that the coupling strength can be determined from the onset of GOE statistics rather than from full microscopic calculation.
