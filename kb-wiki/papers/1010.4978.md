# Polarization observables in the longitudinal basis for pseudo-scalar meson photoproduction using a density matrix approach

> The complete expression for the intensity in pseudo-scalar meson photoproduction with a polarized beam, target, and recoil baryon is derived using a density matrix approach that offers great economy of notation. A Cartesian basis with spins for all particles quantized along a single direction, the longitudinal beam direction, is used for consistency and clarity in interpretation. A single spin-quantization axis for all particles enables the amplitudes to be written in a manifestly covariant fashion with simple relations to those of the well-known CGLN formalism. Possible sign discrepancies between theoretical amplitude-level expressions and experimentally measurable intensity profiles are dealt with carefully. Our motivation is to provide a coherent framework for coupled-channel partial-wave analysis of several meson photoproduction reactions, incorporating recently published and forthcoming polarization data from Jefferson Lab.

## Key claim

The paper derives a complete, self-consistent formalism for polarization observables in pseudo-scalar meson photoproduction using a longitudinal spin-quantization basis, with careful attention to sign conventions that can cause discrepancies between theoretical amplitude-level expressions and experimentally measured intensity profiles. The authors aim to provide a coherent framework for coupled-channel partial-wave analysis that can incorporate recent and forthcoming polarization data from Jefferson Lab, acknowledging that current measurements do not provide enough constraint to distinguish between production models containing different resonance contributions.

## Method

The authors use a density matrix approach following Fasano, Tabakin and Saghai (FTS) to derive the full intensity expression for pseudo-scalar meson photoproduction with polarized beam, target, and recoil baryon. They construct amplitudes in a longitudinal basis where spins for all particles are quantized along a single direction (the beam direction), and relate these to the standard CGLN amplitudes. Mirror symmetry transformations are applied to reduce the 64 terms in the full intensity expansion to 32 physically allowed terms, and the 16 polarization observables are defined as specific T_{lmn} correlation elements. The authors then provide explicit intensity profiles for beam-target, beam-recoil, and target-recoil type experiments, connecting theoretical definitions to experimentally measurable asymmetries.

## Key numbers

* The full intensity expansion contains 4 × 4 × 4 = 64 terms, of which 32 vanish under mirror symmetry invariance
* There are 16 physically measurable polarization observables: 3 single-polarization (P, Sigma, T) and 12 double-polarization (E, F, G, H, C_x, C_z, O_x, O_z, T_x, T_z, L_x, L_z), plus the unpolarized cross-section sigma_0
* The four independent longitudinal basis amplitudes L_1, L_2, L_3, L_4 are related to the CGLN amplitudes F_1, F_2, F_3, F_4 via expressions involving sin(theta^m_cm) and cos(theta^m_cm), where theta^m_cm is the polar meson production angle in the center-of-mass frame
* The beam asymmetry Sigma is defined as (sigma^(perp,0,0) - sigma^(parallel,0,0))/(sigma^(perp,0,0) + sigma^(parallel,0,0)) for linear polarization at phi = 0 and phi = pi/2
* The target asymmetry T is defined as (sigma^(0,+y,0) - sigma^(0,-y,0))/(sigma^(0,+y,0) + sigma^(0,-y,0))

## Context

This work extends the density matrix formalism of Fasano, Tabakin and Saghai (FTS) and the earlier work of Barker, Donnachie, and Storrow (BDS) on polarization observables in pseudo-scalar meson photoproduction. It addresses sign ambiguities that have persisted in the literature regarding the connection between theoretical amplitudes and experimental measurements. The authors build on the work of Chiang and Tabakin (CT), who showed that only seven carefully chosen polarization observables are needed in principle to characterize the full production amplitude, and Sandorfi et al., who demonstrated that current data do not provide enough constraint to distinguish between models with different resonance contributions. The formalism is designed to enable consistent partial-wave analysis of recent and future data from CLAS at Jefferson Lab, as well as data from GRAAL and LEPS experiments.
