# Introduction - Strong interaction in the nuclear medium: new trends

> Recent achievements in nuclear forces theory open new perspectives for the next decade of low energy nuclear physics, bringing together people from very different communities. Although many developments remain to be done, the possibility to directly use QCD to describe nuclear system is a major challenge that is within reach. In this introduction to the 2009 International Joliot-Curie School (EJC2009), new trends in the strong nuclear interaction are summarized starting from quarks and ending with finite or infinite nuclear systems. At different energy scales, selected new concepts and ideas have been discussed in a rather simple way. Recent advances in the theory of nuclear forces, thanks to chiral perturbation and effective field theories, have led to a new generation of strong nuclear interaction particularly suited to low energy nuclear physics. The interesting aspects of new interactions compared to conventional forces are underlined. Recent achievements in ab initio theories that directly start from the bare nucleon-nucleon interaction and their key role to understand the three-body force are illustrated. Finally, future perspectives for standard nuclear physics theories, namely Shell Model and Energy Density Functional, are discussed.

# Strong interaction in the nuclear medium: new trends

## Key claim

Recent advances in chiral perturbation theory and effective field theories have produced a new generation of nuclear forces that are systematically improvable and directly connected to QCD, though many developments remain to be done before QCD can be used directly to describe nuclear systems. The introduction of "soft" interactions via renormalization group techniques reveals that all conventional forces contain the same low-energy information, resolving the non-uniqueness puzzle of conventional parametrizations.

## Method

The paper reviews the progression from conventional semi-phenomenological nucleon-nucleon forces (with approximately 30 parameters adjusted to scattering data) to modern forces derived from the QCD Lagrangian via Effective Field Theory (EFT) combined with Chiral Perturbation Theory. The EFT approach expands the short-range interaction in powers of momentum transfer relative to a cutoff parameter around 1 GeV, providing a systematic framework where phase-shift calculations converge to experimental values as the expansion order increases from Leading Order to N3LO. Renormalization group techniques are then applied to produce "soft" low-momentum interactions that remove the hard core while preserving low-energy phase shifts.

## Key numbers

- Quark masses: m_u ~ 2.4 ± 0.9 MeV, m_d ~ 4.75 ± 1.25 MeV, m_s ~ 100 ± 30 MeV, with an energy gap of approximately 1 GeV separating light quarks from heavy quarks (m_c ~ 1270 MeV, m_b ~ 4200 MeV, m_t ~ 171200 ± 2100 MeV)
- The typical energy scale of interest for low-energy nuclear physics is |q| ~ M_pi = 140 MeV, while the EFT cutoff parameter is of order 1 GeV
- In the triton, 2-body forces contribute approximately 92% of the binding energy and 3-body forces contribute approximately 7%; in the alpha particle, 2-body forces contribute approximately 89% and 3-body forces contribute approximately 10%, with 4-body forces contributing less than 1%
- Only 30-40 data sets exist for hyperon-nucleon scattering compared to 2000-3000 for nucleon-nucleon scattering

## Context

This introduction to the 2009 International Joliot-Curie School (EJC2009) summarizes new trends bridging high-energy QCD physics with low-energy nuclear structure, extending prior work on conventional forces such as AV18 (Wiringa 1995), CDBonn (Machleidt 2001), and Reid93 (Stoks 1993). The paper highlights how EFT-based forces (Epelbaum 2008) and soft interactions like V_lowk (Bogner 2005) enable ab initio calculations using methods such as No-Core Shell Model (Navratil 2009), Green-Function Monte-Carlo (Pieper 2001, 2007), and Coupled-Cluster (Kowalski 2004, Hagen 2009), while also opening the path to systematic studies of hypernuclei (Polinder 2006) and nuclear matter.
