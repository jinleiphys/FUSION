# Probing dense matter in compact star cores with radio pulsar data

> Astrophysical observations of compact stars provide, in addition to collider experiments, the other big source of information on matter under extreme conditions. The largest and most precise data set about neutron stars is the timing data of radio pulsars. We show how this unique data can be used to learn about the ultra-dense matter in the compact star interior. The method relies on astro-seismology based on special global oscillation modes (r-modes) that emit gravitational waves. They would prevent pulsars from spinning with their observed high frequencies, unless the damping of these modes, determined by the microscopic properties of matter, can prevent this. We show that for each form of matter there is a distinct region in a frequency/spindown-rate diagram where r-modes can be present. We find that stars containing ungapped quark matter are consistent with both the observed radio and x-ray data, whereas, even when taking into account the considerable uncertainties, neutron star models with standard viscous damping are inconsistent with both data sets and additional damping mechanisms would be required.

## Key claim

Radio pulsar timing data can be used to probe the interior composition of compact stars via r-mode astroseismology, but the method requires that the observed millisecond pulsars are inconsistent with standard neutron star models having only viscous damping, even when accounting for considerable uncertainties, whereas stars containing ungapped quark matter are consistent with both radio and x-ray data.

## Method

The authors perform r-mode astroseismology by computing static instability regions in a temperature-frequency diagram and dynamic instability regions in a spindown-rate-frequency diagram for different star compositions. They compare these regions to observed x-ray data from low-mass x-ray binaries and radio timing data from millisecond pulsars, using an effective theory approach that encodes the interior properties of the star in a few low-energy constants. The evolution of the pulsar spin frequency, r-mode amplitude, and temperature is solved analytically by exploiting the separation of timescales between thermal and spin evolution.

## Key numbers

- For the standard neutron star model with viscous damping, the r-mode saturation amplitude required to explain the data would need to be as low as \(\alpha_{\rm sat} \lesssim 10^{-8}-10^{-7}\), but known saturation mechanisms can at most provide \(\alpha_{\rm sat} \gtrsim 10^{-6}\).
- For millisecond pulsars to cool out of the static instability region, the r-mode saturation amplitude would need to be \(\alpha_{\rm sat} \gtrsim 10^{-10}\), which is many orders of magnitude smaller than known saturation mechanisms can provide.
- The dynamic instability boundaries are compared to radio pulsar data from the Manchester (2004) catalog, where all data points are upper limits for the r-mode component of the spindown.
- The quark matter model uses a strong coupling constant \(\alpha_s = 1\) and is evaluated for a \(1.4\, M_{\odot}\) strange star with long-ranged non-Fermi-liquid interactions.

## Context

This work extends the r-mode astroseismology approach of Andersson (1998) and Owen (1998) by applying it to the comprehensive radio pulsar timing data set of Manchester (2004), which had previously been difficult to connect to interior composition. The method contradicts the standard neutron star model with viscous damping from Shternin (2008) and Sawyer (1989), showing it is inconsistent with observations, and instead supports the ungapped quark matter model of Schwenzer (2012). The results enable future discrimination between different forms of dense matter by combining thermal x-ray observations, radio timing data, and future gravitational wave data from interferometers such as those described by Harry (2010).
