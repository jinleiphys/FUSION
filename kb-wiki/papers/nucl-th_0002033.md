# Phenomenological local potentials for π^- + ^{12}C scattering from 120 to 766 MeV

> Pion-nucleus scattering cross sections are calculated by solving a Schrödinger equation reduced from the Klein-Gordon equation. Local potentials are assumed, and phenomenological potential parameters are searched energy-dependently for $π^{-} + ^{12}$C system so as to reproduce not only elastic differential cross sections but also total elastic, reaction and total cross sections at 13 pion incident energies from 120 to 766 MeV. The real and imaginary parts of the local potentials thus obtained are shown to satisfy the dispersion relation. The imaginary part of the potentials as a function of the pion energy is found to peak near the $Δ$(1232)-resonance energy. The strong absorption radius of the pion projectile with incident energies near the $Δ$-resonance region is found to be about $1.6 A^{1/3}$ fm, which is consistent with previous studies of the region where the decay of the $Δ$'s takes place in nuclei. The phenomenological local potentials are then compared with the local potentials exactly phase-shift equivalent to Kisslinger potentials for pion energies near the $Δ$-resonance.

# Phenomenological local potentials for π⁻ + ¹²C scattering from 120 to 766 MeV

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that phenomenological local Woods-Saxon potentials, obtained by fitting elastic differential cross sections together with total elastic, reaction, and total cross sections for π⁻ + ¹²C at 13 energies from 120 to 766 MeV, satisfy the dispersion relation in the outer nuclear surface region (1.3 A¹/³ to 2.0 A¹/³ fm), but the extracted potentials are not unique and the inner part of the potentials can be quite different from the Woods-Saxon form, particularly at lower energies.

## Method

The authors reduced the Klein-Gordon equation to a Schrödinger equation following Satchler's method, then searched for parameters of a Woods-Saxon local potential (six adjustable parameters: real and imaginary well depths, radii, and diffusenesses) by χ²-fitting to experimental elastic differential cross sections plus total elastic, reaction, and total cross sections at 13 pion incident energies from 120 to 766 MeV. They also constructed local potentials exactly phase-shift equivalent to Kisslinger potentials using the Krell-Ericson transformation with parameters from Sternheim and Auerbach (1970) and compared these to the phenomenological Woods-Saxon potentials.

## Key numbers

- 13 pion incident energies from 120 to 766 MeV were studied for the π⁻ + ¹²C system.
- The strong absorption radius parameter r_S near the Δ-resonance region was about 1.6 A¹/³ fm, and about 1.1 fm at E ≈ 400–500 MeV.
- The imaginary potential at r = 1.5 A¹/³ fm was fitted by a Gaussian plus constant: W₀ = -13.9 MeV, E₀ = 220 MeV, ΔE = 110 MeV, and W₁ = -3.34 MeV.
- The peak of the imaginary potential was located at E₀ ≈ 205 MeV on average for the radial region 1.3 A¹/³ fm < r < 2.0 A¹/³ fm.
- The total cross section was approximately given by σ_T ≈ 1.59 π R_S², where R_S = r_S A¹/³.
- Repulsive real potentials were obtained only at 230, 260, and 280 MeV; attractive potentials were found at all 13 energies.

## Context

This work extends the method of Satchler (1992), who reduced the Klein-Gordon equation to a Schrödinger equation and used Woods-Saxon local potentials for pion scattering on heavier nuclei (⁴⁰Ca to ²⁰⁸Pb) near the Δ-resonance. The authors apply this approach to ¹²C across a wider energy range (120–766 MeV) and include total cross sections in the fitting. The extracted local potentials are compared with local potentials phase-shift equivalent to Kisslinger potentials via the Krell-Ericson transformation, as used by Parija (1989) and Johnson and Satchler (1996). The strong absorption radius results are consistent with studies of Δ decay in nuclei by Udagawa (1980s), showing quasi-free decay occurs at r ≥ 1.6 A¹/³ fm. The method enables a simple treatment of distortion effects in DWBA or DWIA calculations for reactions such as (π, K) on ¹²C, as recently applied to hypernuclear production.
