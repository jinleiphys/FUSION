# Current in the light-front Bethe-Salpeter formalism II: Applications

> We pursue applications of the light-front reduction of current matrix elements in the Bethe-Salpeter formalism. The normalization of the reduced wave function is derived from the covariant framework and related to non-valence probabilities using familiar Fock space projection operators. Using a simple model, we obtain expressions for generalized parton distributions that are continuous. The non-vanishing of these distributions at the crossover between kinematic regimes (where the plus component of the struck quark's momentum is equal to the plus component of the momentum transfer) is tied to higher Fock components. Moreover continuity holds due to relations between Fock components at vanishing plus momentum. Lastly we apply the light-front reduction to time-like form factors and derive expressions for the generalized distribution amplitudes in this model.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that generalized parton distributions (GPDs) computed from a simple scalar model using the light-front reduction of the Bethe-Salpeter formalism are continuous at the crossover point \(x = \zeta\), and this continuity is explicitly tied to relations between Fock components at vanishing plus momentum, contrary to what a purely valence model would produce.

## Method

The authors apply the light-front reduction of current matrix elements in the Bethe-Salpeter formalism to a scalar model in (3+1) dimensions with a one-boson-exchange ladder kernel. They derive the normalization condition for the reduced light-front wave function from the covariant Bethe-Salpeter normalization, then compute generalized parton distributions from the integrand of the electromagnetic form factor in an arbitrary frame where \(\Delta^+ \neq 0\). They also apply the same reduction scheme to time-like form factors and derive expressions for generalized distribution amplitudes.

## Key numbers

- The leading-order normalization condition for the reduced wave function is \(N^{\text{LO}} \equiv \int \frac{dx d\mathbf{k}^\perp}{2 (2\pi)^3 x (1-x)} \psi^*(x,\mathbf{k}^\perp) \psi(x,\mathbf{k}^\perp) = 1\).
- The first-order correction to the normalization from higher Fock components is \(\delta N = \int \frac{dx d\mathbf{k}^\perp}{2(2\pi)^3 x (1-x)} \frac{dy d\mathbf{p}^\perp}{2 (2\pi)^3 y (1-y)} \psi^*(x,\mathbf{k}^\perp) \left( -\frac{\partial}{\partial M^2} V(x, \mathbf{k}^\perp; y, \mathbf{p}^\perp |M^2) \right) \psi(y, \mathbf{p}^\perp)\).
- The valence contribution to the GPD vanishes linearly at the crossover \(x = \zeta\) because the two-body wave function vanishes quadratically at the endpoints, while the overall weight contains a factor \(x^{\prime -1}\).
- The Born term at the crossover yields \(F(\zeta, \zeta, t)^{\text{Born}} = \int \frac{d\mathbf{k}^\perp dy d\mathbf{p}^\perp}{(16\pi^3)^2 y (1-y) y^\prime} \psi^*(y^\prime, \mathbf{p}^{\prime\perp}) \frac{g^2 \theta(y - \zeta)/(y - \zeta)}{(\mathbf{k}^\perp + \mathbf{\Delta}^\perp)^2 + m^2} D(y,\mathbf{p}^\perp;\zeta,\mathbf{k}^\perp|M^2) \psi(y, \mathbf{p}^\perp)\).
- The final-state iteration term at the crossover yields \(F(\zeta, \zeta, t)^{\text{final}} = \int \frac{d\mathbf{k}^\perp dy^\prime d\mathbf{p}^{\prime\perp}}{(16\pi^3)^2 (1-\zeta) y^\prime (1-y^\prime)} \psi^*(y^\prime, \mathbf{p}^{\prime\perp}) \frac{g^2/y}{(\mathbf{k}^\perp + \mathbf{\Delta}^\perp)^2 + m^2} D(y,\mathbf{p}^\perp;\zeta,\mathbf{k}^\perp|M^2) \psi(\zeta, \mathbf{k}^\perp)\).

## Context

This work extends the light-front reduction of current matrix elements in the Bethe-Salpeter formalism previously developed by the same authors (Tiburzi and Miller, 2002) and by Sales (1999). It directly addresses the problem of non-wave-function vertices identified by Bakker (2000) by showing how higher Fock components replace those vertices. The results enable calculations of generalized parton distributions and generalized distribution amplitudes in a framework that maintains continuity at kinematic boundaries, which is essential for interpreting beam-spin asymmetry measurements in deeply virtual Compton scattering as discussed by Diehl (1997).
