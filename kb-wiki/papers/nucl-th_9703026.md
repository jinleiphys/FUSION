# Caloric Curve for Finite Nuclei in Thomas-Fermi Theory

> In a finite temperature Thomas-Fermi theory with realistic nuclear interactions, we construct caloric curves for finite nuclei enclosed in a sphere of about $4 - 8$ times the normal nuclear volume. The specific heat capacity $C_v$ shows a peaked structure that is possibly indicative of a liquid-gas phase transition in finite nuclear systems.

## Key claim

Using a finite-temperature Thomas-Fermi theory with a realistic Seyler-Blanchard effective interaction, the specific heat capacity \(C_v\) of finite nuclei confined in a freeze-out volume shows a peaked structure that is *possibly* indicative of a liquid-gas phase transition, with the transition temperature being weakly dependent on the confinement volume beyond \(V = 8V_0\).

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent Thomas-Fermi calculations at finite temperature for two finite nuclei (\(^{150}\)Sm and \(^{85}\)Kr) using a momentum- and density-dependent Seyler-Blanchard effective interaction, with parameters fitted to bulk nuclear properties and Giant Monopole Resonance energies. The nuclei were enclosed in spherical confinement volumes of \(4V_0\) and \(8V_0\) (where \(V_0\) is the normal nuclear volume at zero temperature), and the excitation energy per particle \(E^* = E(T) - E(T=0)\) was computed as a function of temperature to construct caloric curves and the specific heat at constant volume \(C_v = (dE^*/dT)_v\).

## Key numbers

- For \(^{150}\)Sm in \(V = 8.0V_0\), the central proton density at \(T = 5\) MeV is depleted by \(\approx 4\%\) compared to zero temperature; at \(T = 10\) MeV the density becomes nearly uniform.
- For \(^{150}\)Sm in \(V = 4.0V_0\), the central density at \(T = 10\) MeV is depleted by only about \(20\%\).
- The specific heat \(C_v\) for \(^{150}\)Sm peaks at \(T \approx 10.5\) MeV for \(V = 4.0V_0\) and at \(T \approx 9.5\) MeV for \(V = 8.0V_0\) (shifted down by \(\approx 1\) MeV).
- For \(^{150}\)Sm, the classical value \(C_v = 3/2\) is reached at \(T \approx 11\) MeV for \(V = 8.0V_0\) and at \(T \approx 13\) MeV for \(V = 4.0V_0\).
- For \(^{85}\)Kr in \(V = 4V_0\), a broad bump in \(C_v\) appears at \(T \approx 11\) MeV; in \(V = 8V_0\), a sharp peak occurs at \(T \approx 10.5\) MeV.
- Switching off the Coulomb force shifts the transition temperature up by \(\approx 1\) MeV for both confinement volumes \(4V_0\) and \(8V_0\).

## Context

This work extends earlier finite-temperature Thomas-Fermi calculations by Das Gupta et al. (1995) and provides the first such calculation with a realistic effective interaction for finite nuclei, complementing fragmentation calculations in the microcanonical algorithm of Gross (1990) and the Copenhagen canonical description (Bondorf et al., 1995). The results connect to experimental observations of a near-constant temperature in the caloric curve for Au + Au collisions (Pochodzalla et al., 1995) and to the concept of a limiting temperature in finite nuclei (Levit and Bonche, 1985; Suraud et al., 1989); the authors note that the transition temperature found here (\(\approx 10\) MeV) is lower than the critical temperature for infinite nuclear matter but higher than the calculated limiting temperature for finite real nuclei, and suggest that including fluctuations with two-body correlations might further lower this temperature.
