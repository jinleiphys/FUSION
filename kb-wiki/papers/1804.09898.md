# Surface tension of quark droplets in compact stars and in the early universe

> We review the role of the surface tension of quark matter droplets in astrophysical conditions, focusing specifically on the thermodynamic conditions prevailing in cold neutron stars (NSs), in hot lepton rich proto NSs, and in early universe conditions. We analyze quark matter in chemical equilibrium under weak interactions, which is relevant for understanding the internal composition of hybrid stars, as well as just deconfined quark matter out of chemical equilibrium, which is the relevant thermodynamic state for describing the nucleation process of quark matter in NSs. We explore the role of temperature, density, trapped neutrinos, droplet size and magnetic fields within the multiple reflection expansion formalism (MRE). Quark matter is described within the frame of different effective models: the MIT bag model and the $SU(3)_f$ Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model (NJL), including color superconductivity, neutrino trapping and magnetic fields. We also analyze the deconfinement transition at vanishing chemical potential and finite temperature including the Polyakov loop. We explore some astrophysical consequences of our results.

# Surface tension of quark droplets in compact stars and in the early universe

## Key claim
The surface tension of quark matter droplets depends strongly on the effective model used (NJL vs. MIT bag model), on droplet size, and on thermodynamic conditions (temperature, density, neutrino trapping, magnetic fields), with values ranging from a few MeV/fm² to hundreds of MeV/fm²; these variations determine whether the hadron-quark interface in hybrid stars is a sharp discontinuity or a mixed phase, but the authors note that within the NJL model the surface tension is large enough to preclude mixed phases in cold hybrid stars.

## Method
The authors employ the multiple reflection expansion (MRE) formalism to compute finite-size effects on the surface tension of quark matter droplets, using two effective models: the SU(3)_f Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model and the MIT bag model, including color superconductivity (2SC phase), neutrino trapping, and magnetic fields. For proto-neutron star conditions, they couple the quark phase to a hadronic phase described by the nonlinear Walecka model under Gibbs conditions with flavor conservation, while for cold hybrid stars they consider chemical equilibrium under weak interactions. For the early universe and heavy-ion collision regime, they use the Polyakov loop extended NJL (PNJL) model at vanishing chemical potential and finite temperature.

## Key numbers
- Surface tension in cold hybrid star conditions within the NJL model is ~150 MeV/fm², which exceeds the critical value of ~65 MeV/fm² for mechanical stability of mixed phases (Voskresensky 2003, Tatsumi 2003, Maruyama 2007, Endo 2011).
- Within the MIT bag model, the surface tension of highly magnetized quark matter is in the range 2–20 MeV/fm² for a wide range of baryon number densities.
- For magnetic fields eB = 5 × 10⁻⁴ GeV², 5 × 10⁻³ GeV², and 5 × 10⁻² GeV² (1 GeV² = 1.690 × 10²⁰ G), the parallel and perpendicular surface tensions differ significantly, with the parallel component increasing and the perpendicular component decreasing with field strength.
- In the PNJL model at vanishing chemical potential, the strange quark contribution to surface tension is more than 10 times larger than the up or down quark contributions.
- For proto-neutron star conditions, the NJL model gives surface tension values at least ten times larger than the MIT bag model.

## Context
This work extends earlier calculations of quark matter surface tension by Berger (1987) (values below 5 MeV/fm²), Heiselberg (1993) and Iida (1998) (10–50 MeV/fm²), and Bombaci (2007, 2009) (~30 MeV/fm²), as well as the much larger values (~300 MeV/fm²) suggested by Alford (2001) for color-flavor locked interfaces. The results enable predictions for the nature of the hadron-quark interface in hybrid stars (sharp discontinuity vs. mixed phase), for the nucleation rate of quark droplets in proto-neutron stars, and for the geometrical structure of magnetized quark matter in "pasta-phase" configurations. The authors note that lattice QCD calculations at finite chemical potential are not available, making effective model studies essential for these regimes.
