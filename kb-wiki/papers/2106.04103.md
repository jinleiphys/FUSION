# Revisiting the variational two-particle reduced density matrix for nuclear systems

> In most nuclear many-body methods, observables are calculated using many-body wave functions explicitly. The variational two-particle reduced density matrix method is one of the few exceptions to the rule. Ground-state energies of both closed-shell and open-shell nuclear systems can indeed be evaluated by minimizing a constrained linear functional of the two-particle reduced density matrix. However, it has virtually never been used in nuclear theory, because nuclear ground states were found to be well overbound, contrary to those of atoms and molecules. Consequently, we introduced new constraints in the nuclear variational two-particle reduced density matrix method, developed recently for atomic and molecular systems. Our calculations then show that this approach can provide a proper description of nuclear systems where only valence neutrons are included. For the nuclear systems where both neutrons and protons are active, however, the energies obtained with the variational two-particle reduced density matrix method are still overbound. The possible reasons for the noticed discrepancies and solutions to this problem will be discussed.

## Key claim

The variational two-particle reduced density matrix (2RDM) method, when augmented with new constraints (partial 3-particle T1 and T2' conditions) developed for atomic and molecular systems, can provide a proper description of nuclear systems where only valence neutrons are included; however, for nuclear systems where both neutrons and protons are active, the obtained ground-state energies remain overbound.

## Method

The authors implemented the variational 2RDM method using semidefinite programming with an augmented Lagrangian boundary point algorithm, applying 2-positivity (P, Q, G) conditions and partial 3-positivity (T1 and T2') conditions as N-representability constraints. They performed calculations for nuclear systems using cluster-orbital shell-model (COSM) and standard shell-model (SM) frameworks, comparing the resulting ground-state energies with exact full configuration interaction results. The method was applied separately to systems with only valence neutrons and to systems with both active protons and neutrons.

## Key numbers

* For nuclear systems with only valence neutrons, the variational 2RDM method with PQG T1 T2' conditions produced ground-state energies that properly matched exact results, unlike earlier 2RDM calculations from the 1970s which were overbound by several MeV and sometimes tens of MeV.
* The computational cost for PQG conditions scales as O(s^4) in memory and O(s^6) in time, while adding T1 and T2' conditions increases scaling to O(s^6) in memory and O(s^9) in time, where s is the number of one-body basis states.
* When angular momentum coupling is applied, the memory and time scaling reduces to O(r^4) and O(r^6) for PQG, and O(r^6) and O(r^9) for PQG T1 T2', where r is the number of orbitals, with the ratio s/r typically around 5, yielding computational gains of 10,000 to 100,000.

## Context

This work extends the variational 2RDM method, which has been systematically developed over 50 years for atomic and molecular electronic systems (Coleman 1963, Zhao 2004, Mazziotti 2006), to nuclear systems where earlier attempts in the 1970s (Mihailovic 1975) produced severely overbound ground states. The paper introduces partial 3-positivity conditions (T1 and T2') that were previously unnecessary for weakly correlated atomic and molecular systems but prove essential for nuclear systems. The method enables future calculations for heavy nuclei where shell-model approaches face exponential scaling limitations and only mean-field methods can currently be applied systematically (Erler 2012, Bender 2003).
