# Quantum Monte Carlo calculations of the thermal conductivity of neutron star crusts

> We use the quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) techniques to calculate the static structure function $S(q)$ of a one-component ion lattice and use it to calculate the thermal conductivity $κ$ of high-density solid matter expected in the neutron star crust. By making detailed comparisons with the results for the thermal conductivity obtained using standard techniques based on the one-phonon approximation (OPA) valid at low temperature, and the multi-phonon harmonic approximation expected to be valid over a wide range of temperatures, we asses the temperature regime where $S(q)$ from QMC can be used directly to calculate $κ$. We also compare the QMC results to those obtained using classical Monte Carlo to quantitatively asses the magnitude of the quantum corrections. We find that quantum effects became relevant for the calculation of $κ$ at temperature $T \lesssim 0.3 ~Ω_\mathrm{P}$, where $Ω_\mathrm{P}$ is the ion plasma frequency. At $T \simeq 0.1 ~Ω_\mathrm{P}$ the quantum effects suppress $κ$ by about $30\%$. The comparison with the results of the OPA indicates that dynamical information beyond the static structure is needed when $T \lesssim 0.1~ Ω_\mathrm{P}$. These quantitative comparisons help to establish QMC as a viable technique to calculate $κ$ at moderate temperatures in the range $T=0.1-1~Ω_\mathrm{P}$ of relevance to the study of accreting neutron stars. This finding is especially important because QMC is the only viable technique so far for calculating $κ$ in multi-component systems at low-temperatures.

## Key claim

Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) calculations of the static structure function can be used directly to calculate the thermal conductivity of neutron star crusts at temperatures in the range T = 0.1-1 Ω_P, but dynamical information beyond the static structure is needed when T ≲ 0.1 Ω_P, and quantum effects suppress κ by about 30% at T ≃ 0.1 Ω_P.

## Method

The authors used path integral Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) and classical Monte Carlo (CMC) simulations to compute the static structure function S(q) of a one-component ion lattice with body-centered cubic (BCC) structure at fiducial densities of 10^10 and 10^11 g cm^-3. They then inserted these S(q) results into the standard expression for the electron thermal conductivity κ, which is limited by electron-ion scattering, and compared the results to those obtained using the one-phonon approximation (OPA) and the multi-phonon harmonic approximation from Potekhin (1999).

## Key numbers

- Quantum effects become relevant for κ at temperature T ≲ 0.3 Ω_P, where Ω_P is the ion plasma frequency
- At T ≃ 0.1 Ω_P, quantum effects suppress κ by about 30%
- The static approximation S'_κ(q) ≈ S'(q) is valid for calculating κ at temperatures as low as T ≈ 0.1 Ω_P
- The melting temperature T_melt ≈ 2.0 Ω_P (Z/30)(A/80)^(2/3) ρ_10^(-1/6) for the LD case (ρ = 10^10 g cm^-3) is 0.75 × 10^9 K
- The ion plasma frequency for LD matter (ρ = 10^10 g cm^-3, ^84_34Se) is 0.32 × 10^9 K
- The ion plasma frequency for HD matter (ρ = 10^11 g cm^-3, ^80_28Ni) is 0.87 × 10^9 K
- The inter-ion distance a is 149 fm for LD matter and 68 fm for HD matter
- The Debye wave number k_D = (6π^2 n_I)^(1/3) ≈ (0.41 a)^(-1)

## Context

This work extends the pioneering calculations of Flowers and Itoh (1976) and Yakovlev and Urpin (1980), who used the one-phonon approximation, and the more recent multi-phonon harmonic approximation calculations by Baiko et al. (1998) and Potekhin (1999) for one-component plasmas. The key advance is establishing QMC as a viable technique for calculating thermal conductivity at moderate temperatures (T = 0.1-1 Ω_P), which is especially important because QMC is the only viable technique so far for calculating κ in multi-component systems at low temperatures, where neither classical molecular dynamics (used by Horowitz et al., 2009 for high temperatures) nor the harmonic approximation are suitable.
