# Statistical Properties of Nuclei by the Shell Model Monte Carlo Method

> We use quantum Monte Carlo methods in the framework of the interacting nuclear shell model to calculate the statistical properties of nuclei at finite temperature and/or excitation energies. With this approach we can carry out realistic calculations in much larger configuration spaces than are possible by conventional methods. A major application of the methods has been the microscopic calculation of nuclear partition functions and level densities, taking into account both correlations and shell effects. Our results for nuclei in the mass region A ~ 50 - 70 are in remarkably good agreement with experimental level densities without any adjustable parameters and are an improvement over empirical formulas. We have recently extended the shell model theory of level statistics to higher temperatures, including continuum effects. We have also constructed simple statistical models to explain the dependence of the microscopically calculated level densities on good quantum numbers such as parity. Thermal signatures of pairing correlations are identified through odd-even effects in the heat capacity.

## Key claim

The Shell Model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method yields nuclear level densities for nuclei in the mass region A ~ 50–70 that agree remarkably well with experimental data without any adjustable parameters, but this agreement is contingent on using a specially constructed "good-sign" interaction that avoids the Monte Carlo sign problem at low temperatures.

## Method

The method applies the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to express the Gibbs ensemble of the interacting nuclear shell model as a coherent superposition of one-body propagators of non-interacting nucleons in time-dependent auxiliary fields. The resulting high-dimensional integral over these fields is evaluated by Monte Carlo sampling, with canonical traces enforced by exact particle-number projection. The thermal energy E(β) is computed from Monte Carlo samples, and the partition function Z(β) is obtained by integrating the thermodynamic relation −∂ ln Z/∂β = E(β); the level density is then extracted via the saddle-point approximation ρ(E) ≈ (1/√(2πT²C)) e^{S(E)}.

## Key numbers

- Mass region A ~ 50–70: the nuclei for which SMMC level densities were calculated and compared with experiment.
- The Monte Carlo method scales as N_s^4 (where N_s is the number of single-particle orbitals), compared with exponential scaling for direct diagonalization.
- Multipole interaction strengths were renormalized by factors of 2 (quadrupole), 1.5 (octupole), and 1 (hexadecupole) relative to a self-consistently determined surface-peaked interaction.

## Context

This work extends the interacting shell model to finite temperature in configuration spaces far larger than those accessible to conventional diagonalization, which is limited to sd-shell and low fp-shell nuclei. The SMMC results improve upon the backshifted Bethe formula (BBF) of Gilbert and Cameron (1965), which requires two adjustable parameters per nucleus and can only predict level densities to within an order of magnitude. The method enables microscopic calculation of partition functions and level densities including both correlations and shell effects, and has been extended to higher temperatures with continuum effects and to parity-dependent level densities.
