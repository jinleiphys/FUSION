# Metastable Nuclear Isomers as Dark Matter Accelerators

> Inelastic dark matter and strongly interacting dark matter are poorly constrained by direct detection experiments since they both require the scattering event to deliver energy from the nucleus into the dark matter in order to have observable effects. We propose to test these scenarios by searching for the collisional de-excitation of meta-stable nuclear isomers by the dark matter particles. The longevity of these isomers is related to a strong suppression of $γ$- and $β$-transitions, typically inhibited by a large difference in the angular momentum for the nuclear transition. The collisional de-excitation by dark matter is possible since heavy dark matter particles can have a momentum exchange with the nucleus comparable to the inverse nuclear size, hence lifting tremendous angular momentum suppression of the nuclear transition. This de-excitation can be observed either by searching for the direct effects of the decaying isomer, or through the re-scattering or decay of excited dark matter states in a nearby conventional dark matter detector setup. Existing nuclear isomer sources such as naturally occurring $^{180m}$Ta, $^{137m}$Ba produced in decaying Cesium in nuclear waste, $^{177m}$Lu from medical waste, and $^{178m}$Hf from the Department of Energy storage can be combined with current dark matter detector technology to search for this class of dark matter.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that metastable nuclear isomers can serve as targets for detecting inelastic or strongly-interacting dark matter, because heavy dark matter particles (masses above approximately 100 GeV) can transfer momentum comparable to the inverse nuclear size, thereby lifting the angular-momentum suppression that normally makes these isomers long-lived against gamma decay.

## Method

The authors calculate the rate for dark-matter-induced collisional de-excitation of four specific nuclear isomers (180mTa, 137mBa, 177mLu, 178mHf) using a nuclear inelastic form factor based on Weisskopf estimates with K-quantum-number hindrance factors. For strongly interacting dark matter, they model the dark matter traffic jam in the Earth's crust by computing the terminal sinking velocity and resulting local density enhancement from flux conservation, then derive the scattering rate using a Yukawa-type differential cross section with a hadronic mediator mass near the QCD scale.

## Key numbers

- 180mTa: lower limit on isomeric half-life τ > 10^16 years; natural abundance 0.011% in naturally occurring tantalum; 0.3 gram-year exposure assumed for projections
- 137mBa: 661 keV isomer with 2.55 minute half-life; 0.5 gram-year exposure assumed (from 100 kg equivalent of 137Cs)
- 177mLu: 970 keV above ground state with 160 day half-life; 0.1% contaminant in 177Lu; 1 mg-year exposure assumed
- 178mHf: 2.46 MeV energy excess over ground state with 31 year half-life; 1 μg-year exposure assumed
- For strongly interacting DM, density enhancement η at 300 m depth reaches a flat regime when σ_n ~ 10^-30 cm^2, corresponding to the saturated geometric cross-section 4πR_A^2
- Terminal velocity formula: v_term = (3 M_χ g T) / (m_gas^2 n ⟨σ_t v_th^3⟩)

## Context

This work extends the search for inelastic dark matter (Tucker-Smith 2001, Feldstein 2010, Pospelov 2013, Bramante 2016) and strongly interacting dark matter (Hooper 2018, DeLuca 2018, Neufeld 2018) beyond conventional direct detection limits, which are weakened because these DM models require energy transfer from the nucleus to the DM. The proposal enables new experimental strategies using existing isomer sources (naturally occurring 180mTa, nuclear waste 137mBa, medical waste 177mLu, and DOE-stored 178mHf) combined with current dark matter detector technology, either through direct observation of isomer decay or through re-scattering of excited dark matter in a nearby detector.
