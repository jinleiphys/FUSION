# Three-body forces and the limit of oxygen isotopes

> The limit of neutron-rich nuclei, the neutron drip-line, evolves regularly from light to medium-mass nuclei except for a striking anomaly in the oxygen isotopes. This anomaly is not reproduced in shell-model calculations derived from microscopic two-nucleon forces. Here, we present the first microscopic explanation of the oxygen anomaly based on three-nucleon forces that have been established in few-body systems. This leads to repulsive contributions to the interactions among excess neutrons that change the location of the neutron drip-line from $^{28}$O to the experimentally observed $^{24}$O. Since the mechanism is robust and general, our findings impact the prediction of the most neutron-rich nuclei and the synthesis of heavy elements in neutron-rich environments.

## Key claim
Three-nucleon forces provide repulsive monopole interactions between valence neutrons that are necessary to explain why the neutron drip-line in oxygen isotopes occurs at \(^{24}\)O rather than at \(^{28}\)O as predicted by calculations based only on two-nucleon forces. The mechanism is robust and general, but the paper shows that the repulsive effect is specifically visible in oxygen because the valence proton-neutron tensor attraction present in fluorine isotopes is absent.

## Method
The authors performed shell-model calculations using single-particle energies derived from microscopic two-nucleon forces (both G-matrix and low-momentum interactions \(V_{\text{low}\,k}\) from chiral N\(^3\)LO interactions) and then added contributions from three-nucleon forces. The three-nucleon forces were included via the Fujita-Miyazawa mechanism (delta excitation) and via leading-order chiral effective field theory three-nucleon interactions at N\(^2\)LO. The resulting single-particle energies and ground-state energies of oxygen isotopes were computed by diagonalization in the valence space above a \(^{16}\)O core.

## Key numbers
- The neutron drip-line in oxygen is experimentally observed at \(^{24}\)O, not at \(^{28}\)O as predicted by two-nucleon force calculations.
- The \(d_{3/2}\) single-particle energy at \(N=20\) calculated from two-nucleon forces is well-bound (negative energy), but with three-nucleon forces it remains high and unbound, matching the phenomenological SDPF-M and USD-B forces.
- Ground-state energies from \(^{16}\)O to \(^{24}\)O show a kink at \(N=16\) when three-nucleon forces are included, whereas two-nucleon-only calculations show a monotonic decrease to \(N=20\).
- The magic number \(N=16\) gap between \(s_{1/2}\) and \(d_{3/2}\) orbitals is enlarged by three-nucleon forces.

## Context
This work extends earlier shell-model explorations with three-nucleon forces up to \(^{21}\)O by Polls and Rath (no year given) and provides the first microscopic explanation of the oxygen anomaly that had been noted experimentally. The results contradict predictions from two-nucleon-only calculations (G-matrix and \(V_{\text{low}\,k}\) approaches) and are consistent with phenomenological forces SDPF-M and USD-B that were fit to data. The mechanism is related to the repulsive three-nucleon force contributions in neutron matter and enables future coupled-cluster calculations by Hagen (no year given) and density-functional studies across the nuclear chart.
