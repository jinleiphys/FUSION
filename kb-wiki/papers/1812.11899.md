# The path from finite to infinite volume: Hadronic observables from lattice QCD

> Standard Model determinations of properties of strongly interacting systems of hadrons have become possible with the powerful method of lattice quantum chromodynamics (LQCD), a method with growing applicability and reliability. While growth in computational power and innovations in algorithmic and computational approaches have been essential in advancing the state of the field, conceptual and formal developments have played a crucial role in turning the output of LQCD computations to phenomenologically valuable results. From the invention of finite-volume technology to access physical observables by Martin Lüscher over three decades ago to date, this field has grown in scope and complexity, enabling studies of scattering amplitudes and reaction rates, as well as spectroscopy of excited states of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) and resonances. Further, LQCD studies are augmented with the inclusion of quantum electrodynamics (QED), and subtleties related to the finite volume of systems in presence of QED have been understood and largely controlled. In this talk, I focus on selected developments to give a taste of the status of the field concerning the mapping between the finite and infinite-volume physics and its state-of-the-art applications.

## Key claim

The talk surveys selected formal developments that map finite-volume lattice QCD observables to infinite-volume physical quantities, emphasizing that these mappings are essential for extracting scattering amplitudes, reaction rates, and resonance properties from numerical lattice data, but that many results remain at unphysically large quark masses and require further generalization to reach the physical point.

## Method

The author reviews the diagrammatic derivation of Lüscher's finite-volume quantization condition for two-body elastic scattering, showing how the infinite sum over s-channel two-hadron loops in a finite volume can be re-expressed in terms of the on-shell scattering amplitude plus power-law finite-volume corrections. This formalism is then extended to coupled two-body channels, to two-nucleon systems with spin and isospin, to three-body elastic scattering via a dimer-particle framework, and to hadronic observables in background electromagnetic fields, where matching between finite-volume correlation functions and effective field theory descriptions yields constraints on electromagnetic structure quantities.

## Key numbers

* The finite-volume quantization condition for two-body scattering involves corrections that scale as \(e^{-RL}\), where \(L\) is the spatial extent of the cubic volume and \(R\) is the finite range of interactions (the pion Compton wavelength in QCD).
* The deuteron quadrupole moment is \(Q_d = 0.2859(3)~\mathrm{fm}^2\) and the asymptotic d/s ratio of the deuteron wavefunction is \(\eta = 0.02713(6)\).
* The ground-state energy of two nucleons in the deuteron channel in a volume of \(\sim (9~\mathrm{fm})^3\) is twice the physical value of the deuteron binding energy.
* A LQCD calculation at a flavor-symmetric point with \(m_{\pi} \approx 800~\mathrm{MeV}\) was used to constrain s-wave scattering phase shifts in two octet-baryon channels and the leading SU(3) low-energy constants of the effective field theory.

## Context

This talk extends the foundational finite-volume technology pioneered by Lüscher (1990, 1986) for two-body elastic scattering to more complex systems including coupled channels, three-body interactions, and background electromagnetic fields. It builds on generalizations by Briceno, Hansen, and collaborators for coupled-channel and three-body quantization conditions, and on the Lellouch-Lüscher formula (2000) for decay amplitudes. The work enables future lattice QCD calculations of nuclear reaction rates such as proton-proton fusion, resonance spectroscopy in mesonic systems, and electromagnetic structure of hadrons and nuclei, while noting that many current results are obtained at unphysically large quark masses and require further formal development to reach the physical point.
