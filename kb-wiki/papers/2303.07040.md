# Sr isotopes: the interplay between shape coexistence and quantum phase transitions

## Key claim
The rapid onset of deformation in Sr isotopes at A = 98 and onwards can be explained by the crossing of regular and intruder configurations, but this crossing does not always produce a quantum phase transition, in Pt, strong mixing between configurations hides the abrupt changes that would otherwise be expected from such a crossing.

## Method
The authors used the interacting boson model with configuration mixing (IBM-CM) to calculate deformation parameters β, unperturbed energies, and mean-field energy surfaces for even-even Sr isotopes, comparing them with Zr, Pt, and Hg isotopes. They analyzed the systematics of two-neutron separation energies (S₂ₙ) and the ratio E(4₁⁺)/E(2₁⁺) as functions of neutron number to identify signatures of quantum phase transitions and shape coexistence.

## Key numbers
- The crossing of regular and intruder configurations occurs at A = 98 for Sr and A = 100 for Zr, both corresponding to N = 60
- The ratio E(4₁⁺)/E(2₁⁺) for Sr and Zr changes from values close to 2 (even 1.5 for ⁹⁶⁻⁹⁸Zr) to values close to 3.3, indicating a transition from vibrational to rotational character
- The mid-shell for Pt and Hg is at N = 104, where the parabolic-like energy systematics of intruder states is centered

## Context
This work extends the Federman-Pittel mechanism (Federman and Pittel, 1977, 1979) for the onset of deformation in the Z ≈ 40 region, which emphasizes the simultaneous occupation of neutron and proton spin-orbit partners (1g₉/₂ proton and 1g₇/₂ neutron orbitals). The authors compare Sr and Zr with the well-studied shape coexistence regions of Pt and Hg (García-Ramos et al., 2009, 2011, 2014b), showing that Sr and Zr exhibit type-II quantum phase transitions (Gavrielov et al., 2019) where two crossing configurations produce more abrupt changes than the type-I QPT that occurs within a single configuration. The work enables a unified understanding of how shape coexistence can induce quantum phase transitions, while clarifying that configuration crossing alone is insufficient, the degree of mixing between configurations determines whether observable discontinuities appear.
