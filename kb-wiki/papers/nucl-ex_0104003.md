# Electromagnetic Dissociation of 8B and the Rate of the 7Be(p,gamma)8B Reaction in the Sun

> In an effort to better determine the 7Be(p,gamma)8B reaction rate, we have performed inclusive and exclusive measurements of the Coulomb dissociation of 8B. The former was a study of longitudinal momentum distributions of 7Be fragments emitted in the Coulomb breakup of intermediate energy 8B beams on Pb and Ag targets. Analysis of these data yielded the E2 contribution to the breakup cross section. In the exclusive measurement, we determined the cross section for the Coulomb breakup of 8B on Pb at low relative energies in order to infer the astrophysical S factor for the 7Be(p,gamma)8B reaction. Interpreting the measurements with 1st-order perturbation theory, we obtained SE2/SE1 = 4.7 (+ 2.0,- 1.3) times 10^-4 at Erel = 0.6 MeV, and S17(0) = 17.8 (+ 1.4,- 1.2) eV b. Semiclassical 1st-order perturbation theory and fully quantum mechanical continuum-discretized coupled channels analyses yield nearly identical results for the E1 strength relevant to solar neutrino flux calculations, suggesting that theoretical reaction mechanism uncertainties need not limit the precision of Coulomb breakup determinations of the 7Be(p,gamma)8B S factor. A recommended value of S17(0) based on a weighted average of this and other measurements is presented.

## Key claim

The Coulomb dissociation of 8B can be used to infer the astrophysical S17(0) factor for the 7Be(p,gamma)8B reaction with precision comparable to direct measurements, provided that the E2 contribution is properly accounted for and that experimental conditions are chosen to minimize nuclear and higher-order electromagnetic effects. The authors find that semiclassical first-order perturbation theory and fully quantum mechanical continuum-discretized coupled channels analyses yield nearly identical results for the E1 strength, suggesting that theoretical reaction mechanism uncertainties need not limit the precision of such determinations.

## Method

Inclusive measurements of longitudinal momentum distributions of 7Be fragments from Coulomb breakup of 44 and 81 MeV/nucleon 8B beams on Pb and Ag targets were performed at the NSCL A1200 fragment separator and S800 spectrometer to determine the E2 contribution to the breakup cross section. Exclusive measurements of the Coulomb breakup of 83 MeV/nucleon 8B on a Pb target were then carried out using multiwire drift chambers and a dipole magnet to reconstruct the 4-momenta of coincident 7Be and proton fragments, from which the relative energy spectrum was obtained. The data were analyzed using both first-order perturbation theory and continuum-discretized coupled channels (CDCC) calculations assuming a single-particle potential model for 8B structure.

## Key numbers

- SE2/SE1 = 4.7 (+2.0, -1.3) x 10^-4 at Erel = 0.6 MeV, determined from inclusive measurements on Pb targets
- S17(0) = 17.8 (+1.4, -1.2) eV b, inferred from the exclusive Coulomb breakup measurement
- Relative energy resolution of 55 keV (1 sigma) at Erel = 100 keV in the exclusive measurement
- Impact parameter cutoff of 30 fm applied, corresponding classically to Theta_8B = 1.77 degrees for 83 MeV/nucleon 8B on Pb
- Intrinsic detection efficiency of 0.414 +/- 0.008 for the exclusive measurement apparatus
- E1 contribution exceeds 90% of the breakup cross section from 130 keV to 2 MeV relative energy (excluding the 0.64 MeV 1+ resonance region)

## Context

This work extends the Coulomb dissociation technique previously applied to 8B by Motobayashi (1994), Kikuchi (1998), and Iwasa (1999), addressing the complications of multiple electromagnetic multipole contributions, nuclear absorption, and higher-order electromagnetic effects. The authors demonstrate that by carefully selecting experimental conditions (impact parameter cutoff, relative energy range) and using both perturbative and CDCC theoretical frameworks, the Coulomb dissociation method can achieve precision comparable to direct radiative capture measurements of the 7Be(p,gamma)8B reaction, which had shown large spread in absolute normalizations among experiments by Kavanagh (1960, 1969), Parker (1966), Vaughn (1970), Wiezorek (1977), Filippone (1983), Hass (1991), and Hammache (1998). The results enable more precise calculations of the solar 8B neutrino flux for interpretation of measurements at SNO and other neutrino detectors.
