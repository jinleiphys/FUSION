# A short course on Relativistic Heavy Ion Collisions

> Some ideas/concepts in relativistic heavy ion collisions are discussed. To a large extent, the discussions are non-comprehensive and non-rigorous. It is intended for fresh graduate students of Homi Bhabha National Institute, Kolkata Centre, who are intending to pursue career in theoretical /experimental high energy nuclear physics. Comments and criticisms will be appreciated.

## Key claim

This paper is a pedagogical lecture course intended for graduate students, not a research paper presenting new results; it reviews established concepts in relativistic heavy ion collisions without claiming any original findings or novel interpretations.

## Method

The course covers conceptual foundations for quark-gluon plasma (QGP) formation, kinematics of heavy ion collisions (including rapidity, pseudorapidity, Mandelstam variables, and luminosity), the ideal gas limit for QGP and hadronic resonance gas, quantum chromodynamics, color glass condensate, relativistic kinetic theory, hydrodynamic models, and experimental signals of QGP. The presentation is explicitly non-comprehensive and non-rigorous, using lecture-style exposition with illustrative figures and example calculations.

## Key numbers

* Critical energy density for QGP formation: \(\epsilon_{cr} \sim 1\) GeV/fm\(^3\); critical temperature: \(T_{cr} \sim 200\) MeV
* Normal nuclear matter density: \(\rho \sim 0.17\) fm\(^{-3}\); energy density \(\epsilon \sim 0.16\) GeV/fm\(^3\)
* Inelastic nucleon-nucleon cross sections used in Glauber model: \(\sigma_{NN} \approx 30\) mb at SPS (\(\sqrt{s}_{NN} \approx 20\) GeV), 40 mb at RHIC (\(\sqrt{s}_{NN} \approx 200\) GeV), 70 mb at LHC (\(\sqrt{s}_{NN} \geq 1000\) GeV)
* Relative strengths of fundamental forces: strong \(10^{38}\), electromagnetic \(10^{36}\), weak \(10^{25}\), gravitational 1
* Neutron star radius \(\sim 10\) km, central density \(\sim 10\) times normal nuclear matter density

## Context

This lecture course is intended for fresh graduate students at Homi Bhabha National Institute, Kolkata Centre, and provides a broad introduction to the field of relativistic heavy ion collisions. It references the four RHIC experiment white papers (BRAHMS, PHOBOS, PHENIX, STAR) as general introductions, and draws on standard textbooks by Wong, Csernai, and the Quark Matter conference series. The course enables students to enter the field by covering the essential kinematic tools, the Glauber model framework (citing Miller (2007) and Alver (2008) for Monte-Carlo implementations), and the statistical mechanics of ideal gases (citing Groot).
