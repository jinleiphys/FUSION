# Anisotropic emission of direct photons from Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt {s_{\rm NN}}=200~{\rm GeV}$ with EPOS3 and IP-Glasma

> The observed large elliptic flow ($v_2$) of direct photons in relativistic heavy ion collisions challenges us to explain. In this paper we consider only two sources of direct photons, prompt photons and thermal photons. Prompt photons are calculated with QCD to next leading order, which fits well the spectra at high transverse momentum ($p_{\rm t}$) region. Thermal photons are calculated with AMY rate in a 3+1D viscous hydrodynamical evolution of the collision systems. Two types of initial conditions, EPOS3 and IP-Glasma, are employed. Our results with IP-Glasma initial condition agree with results in published work in most cases, and the difference tells the regions effected by the absence of viscous correction in photon emission rate. Because both models are well constrained with hadron data, the hydrodynamic evolution of collision systems in the two models have many common. However, the transverse velocity of the collective motion are quite different. This makes a big difference in the anisotropic emission of direct photon emission. The observed large elliptic flow of direct photons is reproduced with EPOS3 hydrodynamic evolution.

## Key claim

The observed large elliptic flow ($v_2$) of direct photons in Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=200$ GeV is reproduced with the EPOS3 initial condition in a 3+1D viscous hydrodynamic evolution, but not with the IP-Glasma initial condition, because the two models produce quite different transverse flow velocities despite being similarly constrained by hadron data.

## Method

The authors calculated direct photon production from two sources: prompt photons using next-to-leading-order QCD with MRST2001 parton distribution functions and a K-factor of 2, and thermal photons using the AMY emission rate in a 3+1D viscous hydrodynamic evolution. Two different initial conditions were employed for the same hydrodynamic code (EPOS3102): the EPOS3 event generator (with zero initial flow at $\tau_0=0.35$ fm/c) and the IP-Glasma model (with non-zero initial flow at $\tau_0=0.4$ fm/c). Viscous corrections to the photon emission rate were ignored in both cases.

## Key numbers

* Prompt photons from QCD at next-to-leading order fit well the high-$p_{\rm t}$ spectra for Au+Au at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=200$ GeV across centralities 0-20%, 20-40%, and 40-60%.
* Thermal photons dominate at low $p_{\rm t}$ and agree with PHENIX data, but the spectra at $p_{\rm t} < 0.5$ GeV/c are evidently lower than data for all three centralities.
* The elliptic flow $v_2$ of direct photons calculated with EPOS initial condition coincides with experimental data for all three centralities (0-20%, 20-40%, 40-60%).
* The triangular flow $v_3$ of direct photons from EPOS coincides with data for centrality 0-20%, is slightly higher than data for 20-40%, and meets the large error bars for 40-60%.
* With IP-Glasma initial condition, $v_2$ and $v_3$ are lower than PHENIX data for centralities 0-20% and 20-40%, but coincide with data for centrality 40-60%.
* An upward leg in the $p_{\rm t}$ spectrum appears for $p_{\rm t} > 5$ GeV/c in 40-60% centrality Au+Au collisions with IP-Glasma, and in 0-20% centrality with EPOS.
* The hydrodynamic evolution is kept until energy density $e^{\rm th}=0.08$ GeV/fm$^3$.
* The initial time for EPOS is $\tau_0=0.35$ fm/c; for IP-Glasma it is $\tau_0=0.4$ fm/c with an earlier switch time $\tau_{\rm switch}=1/Q_s = 0.2$ fm/c.

## Context

This work extends the effort to explain the unexpectedly large direct photon $v_2$ observed by PHENIX (2011, 2015), which has challenged hydrodynamic models such as Gale et al. (2014) and transport models such as van Hees (2011) and Linnyk (2013). The authors compare two well-constrained initial conditions (EPOS3 and IP-Glasma) within the same 3+1D viscous hydrodynamic framework, building on prior work by Gale et al. (2021) and Paquet et al. (2015) who used IP-Glasma but included viscous corrections to the photon emission rate. The results show that the balance between eccentricity and flow velocity magnitude, constrained by both hadron and photon data, is critical for reproducing the observed anisotropic photon emission.
