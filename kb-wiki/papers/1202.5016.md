# Neutron Stars with Hyperons subject to Strong Magnetic Field

> Neutron stars are one of the most exotic objects in the universe and a unique laboratory to study the nuclear matter above the nuclear saturation density. In this work, we study the equation of state of the nuclear matter within a relativistic model subjected to a strong magnetic field. We then apply this EoS to study and describe some of the physical characteristics of neutron star, especially the mass-radius relation and chemical compositions. To study the influence of a the magnetic field and the hyperons in the stellar interior, we consider altogether four solutions: two different values of magnetic field to obtain a weak and a strong influence, and two configurations: a family of neutron stars formed only by protons, electrons and neutrons and a family formed by protons, electrons, neutrons, muons and hyperons. The limit and the validity of the results found are discussed with some care. In all cases the particles that constitute the neutron star are in $β$ equilibrium and zero total net charge. Our work indicates that the effect of a strong magnetic field has to be taken into account in the description of magnetars, mainly if we believe that there are hyperons in their interior, in which case, the influence of the magnetic field can increase the mass by more than 10%. We have also seen that although a magnetar can reach 2.48$M_{\odot}$, a natural explanation of why we do not know pulsars with masses above 2.0$M_{\odot}$ arises. We also discuss how the magnetic field affects the strangeness fraction in some standard neutron star masses and, to conclude our paper, we revisit the direct URCA process related to the cooling of the neutron stars and show how it is affected by the hyperons and the magnetic field.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a strong, density-dependent magnetic field can increase the maximum mass of a neutron star containing hyperons by more than 10%, but that even with this increase, a hyperonic star with a strong magnetic field reaches a maximum mass of 2.22 solar masses, which is still below the 2.48 solar masses possible for an atomic (hyperon-free) star; the authors therefore argue that the absence of observed pulsars above 2.0 solar masses is naturally explained if hyperons exist in their interiors.

## Method

The authors used the non-linear Walecka model (NLWM) in the mean-field approximation with a GM1 parametrization to describe baryonic matter, including the full baryon octet (nucleons, Lambda, Sigma, Xi) and leptons (electrons and muons) under beta equilibrium and charge neutrality. A density-dependent magnetic field was introduced, parametrized to be weak below nuclear saturation density and to reach a constant value B0 at high density, with two values chosen: 1.0 x 10^17 G (weak influence) and 3.1 x 10^18 G (strong influence). The resulting equations of state were used as input to the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to compute mass-radius relations, with checks that the magnetic field energy density and pressure did not dominate over matter contributions.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass for an atomic star (protons, neutrons, electrons) with B0 = 3.1 x 10^18 G: 2.48 solar masses, radius 12.21 km, central density 0.741 fm^-3
- Maximum mass for an atomic star with B0 = 1.0 x 10^17 G: 2.39 solar masses, radius 12.10 km, central density 0.840 fm^-3
- Maximum mass for a hyperonic star (including muons and hyperons) with B0 = 3.1 x 10^18 G: 2.22 solar masses, radius 11.80 km, central density 0.824 fm^-3
- Maximum mass for a hyperonic star with B0 = 1.0 x 10^17 G: 2.01 solar masses, radius 11.86 km, central density 0.952 fm^-3
- For a hyperonic star of 1.8 solar masses, the strangeness fraction at the center is 0.05 with B0 = 3.1 x 10^18 G (central field 7.2 x 10^17 G) and 0.08 with B0 = 1.0 x 10^17 G (central field 0.40 x 10^17 G)
- The lower mass at which the proton fraction reaches 13% (enabling direct URCA cooling) for hyperonic stars is 1.12 solar masses with B0 = 3.1 x 10^18 G and 1.08 solar masses with B0 = 1.0 x 10^17 G

## Context

This work extends prior studies of neutron stars with hyperons (Glen, 2000; Deb, 2010) and of magnetars with strong magnetic fields (Pal, 2000; Rabhi, 2008) by combining both effects with a density-dependent magnetic field. The authors compare their results to the observational constraint of a 1.97 solar mass pulsar (Demorest, 2010) and to redshift constraints from EXO0748-676 (Cottam, 2002) and 1E 1207.4-5209 (Sanwal, 2002). The paper enables future work on the cooling of magnetars via the direct URCA process and on the maximum mass limits for neutron stars with exotic matter.
