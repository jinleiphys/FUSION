# Model discrimination in pseudoscalar-meson photoproduction

> To learn about a physical system of interest, experimental results must be able to discriminate among models. We introduce a geometrical measure to quantify the distance between models for pseudoscalar-meson photoproduction in amplitude space. Experimental observables, with finite accuracy, map to probability distributions in amplitude space, and the characteristic width scale of such distributions needs to be smaller than the distance between models if the observable data are going to be useful. We therefore also introduce a method for evaluating probability distributions in amplitude space that arise as a result of one or more measurements, and show how one can use this to determine what further measurements are going to be necessary to be able to discriminate among models.

# Model discrimination in pseudoscalar-meson photoproduction

## Key claim
The paper introduces a geometrical distance measure in amplitude space to quantify how well experimental data can discriminate between different models of pseudoscalar-meson photoproduction, but the key condition is that the characteristic width of probability distributions in amplitude space arising from measurements must be smaller than the distance between models for discrimination to be possible. The authors show that even mathematically "complete sets" of polarization observables leave continuous ambiguities when finite experimental error bars are considered, and that approximately 12 different polarization observables are needed to achieve a unimodal posterior distribution.

## Method
The authors define normalized transversity amplitudes (NTA) on a 7-sphere in R^8 and introduce a distance measure D[A,B] as the minimal opening angle between two model amplitude vectors, minimized over the unobservable global phase. They then use both frequentist bootstrapping with chi-squared minimization and Bayesian nested sampling (MultiNest) to map experimental observables with finite uncertainties onto probability distributions in amplitude space, computing the standard deviation of the distance distribution and information entropy to quantify resolving power.

## Key numbers
- The distance measure D ranges from 0 to pi/2, with the prior distribution (no data) giving an amplitude uncertainty of approximately 1.08
- For the BoGa and RPR-2011 models of gamma p -> K+ Lambda, the cross-section asymmetry A clusters around 0.1-0.2 in realistic kinematics (W >= 1.65 GeV, -0.75 <= cos(theta_cm) <= 0.85), while experimental relative errors on cross sections are systematically about 0.06
- Confirmation or rejection of a single s-channel resonance requires experimental resolution of order D << pi/20 at backward angles and W >= M_R
- The information gain required to form a well-defined unimodal distribution is approximately 21 bits
- For W < 1.9 GeV with available CLAS and GRAAL data, the amplitude uncertainty is approximately 0.5, which is sufficient to locally distinguish RPR-2011 from Kaon-MAID models
- The grid for kinematic dependence studies uses Delta W = 20 MeV and Delta cos(theta_cm) = 0.1

## Context
This work extends the theoretical framework of Chiang and Tabakin (1997) on complete sets of polarization observables for pseudoscalar-meson photoproduction, and addresses the finding by Vrancx (2013) and Ireland that no true complete sets exist when dealing with finite experimental error bars. The method is demonstrated using the Bonn-Gatchina coupled-channel model (Anisovich et al., 2010-2014) and the RPR-2011 hybrid Regge-isobar model (De Cruz et al., 2012) for gamma p -> K+ Lambda, comparing against the Kaon-MAID isobar model. The framework enables experimental planning by quantifying which combinations of polarization measurements are necessary to discriminate between competing theoretical models, and can be applied to partial-wave analyses as an intermediate between local and global model comparison.
