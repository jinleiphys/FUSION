# The lowest excited states of 14C and 14O nuclei within a five-cluster model

> We study the ground and the first excited $0^{+}$ states of two mirror nuclei $^{14}$C and $^{14}$O within a five-cluster model (three alpha-particles and two extra nucleons) with the use of high accuracy variational approach with Gaussian bases. The first excited $0^{+}$ state of these nuclei is shown to be connected with a change of the structure of the two-nucleon subsystem moving in the field of the $^{12}$C cluster. The density distributions, electric form factors (both the elastic and transition ones), pair correlation functions, and the momentum distributions of the constituent particles are found. The most probable shapes of five-cluster systems are revealed.

## Key claim

The first excited $0^{+}$ state of $^{14}$C and $^{14}$O is shown to be connected with a change in the structure of the two-nucleon subsystem moving in the field of the $^{12}$C cluster, while the $^{12}$C cluster itself is practically not excited. The authors note that their set of potentials for $^{14}$O appeared to be a "more happy choice" to treat the excited $0^{+}$ state than that used for $^{14}$C, where the calculated energy is in only qualitative agreement with experiment.

## Method

The authors employed a five-cluster model treating $^{14}$C and $^{14}$O as three alpha-particles plus two extra nucleons (neutrons or protons), using a variational approach with Gaussian bases. They used effective cluster-cluster interactions including a nucleon-nucleon potential in the singlet state, generalized alpha-nucleon potentials with local attraction and non-local separable repulsion, and an alpha-alpha potential, all adjusted to reproduce ground-state binding energies and phase shifts. The wave functions for the ground and first excited $0^{+}$ states were obtained with high accuracy using stochastic Gaussian bases (K=500 for $^{14}$O, K=600 for $^{14}$C), and the Helm approximation was applied to account for finite particle sizes when calculating charge densities and form factors.

## Key numbers

- Ground state energy of $^{14}$C: calculated -20.398 MeV, experimental -20.3977 MeV
- Ground state energy of $^{14}$O: calculated -13.845 MeV, experimental -13.8445 MeV
- First excited $0^{+}$ state energy of $^{14}$C: calculated -13.223 MeV, experimental -13.808 MeV
- First excited $0^{+}$ state energy of $^{14}$O: calculated -7.932 MeV, experimental -7.925 MeV
- Charge radius of $^{14}$C ground state: calculated 2.500 fm, experimental 2.496 fm (from 1982) and 2.503 fm (from 2013)
- Charge radius of $^{14}$O ground state: calculated 2.415 fm (prediction, no experimental value)
- Charge radius of $^{14}$C first excited $0^{+}$ state: calculated 2.582 fm
- Charge radius of $^{14}$O first excited $0^{+}$ state: calculated 2.589 fm
- Relative r.m.s. distance between alpha-particles in $^{14}$C ground state: 3.189 fm; in first excited state: 3.367 fm
- Relative r.m.s. distance between extra nucleons in $^{14}$C ground state: 2.621 fm; in first excited state: 3.211 fm
- Relative r.m.s. distance between extra nucleons in $^{14}$O ground state: 2.732 fm; in first excited state: 3.305 fm

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous five-cluster calculations of the ground states of $^{14}$C and $^{14}$O (Grinyuk et al., 2016) to the first excited $0^{+}$ states. The finding that the excitation is primarily a rearrangement of the two-nucleon subsystem while the $^{12}$C core remains unexcited confirms a result first demonstrated by Suhara and Kanada-En'yo (2010) using antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) with full nucleonic degrees of freedom. The calculated transition electric form factors and charge radii for the excited states provide predictions that can be tested by future electron scattering experiments on these nuclei.
