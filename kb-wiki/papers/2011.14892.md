# Production of Heavy Elements During the Explosion of a Low-Mass Neutron Star in a Close Binary

> The nucleosynthesis of heavy elements in the scenario for the evolution of a close binary of neutron stars differing greatly in mass is considered. In contrast to the scenario for the merger of two neutron stars of comparable masses considered repeatedly in the literature, the evolution of such a binary at the final stage consists in a rapid mass transfer to the more massive star and an explosive disruption of the low-mass component. We provide the details of the explosion and calculate the abundances of the heavy elements produced in this process for various initial conditions.

# Production of Heavy Elements During the Explosion of a Low-Mass Neutron Star in a Close Binary

## Key claim
The stripping scenario in a highly asymmetric neutron star binary can produce heavy elements via the r-process during the explosive disruption of a low-mass neutron star, with abundance patterns that agree well with both solar system observations and classical neutron star merger calculations, but only for the most neutron-rich ejected layers (electron fraction Ye ≤ 0.4). The nucleosynthesis was calculated offline on fixed trajectories without integrating the contribution for all possible trajectories, making this a first step toward a self-consistent model.

## Method
The authors performed hydrodynamic simulations of the explosive disruption of a minimum-mass neutron star (0.1 solar masses) in a close binary with a 1.4 solar mass companion, assuming spherical symmetry. They calculated nucleosynthesis along four representative Lagrangian trajectories using the SYNTHeR nuclear reaction network, which includes neutron capture, beta decay, fission cycling, charged-particle reactions, and weak interactions, with the composition at temperatures above T9 = 7 set by nuclear statistical equilibrium.

## Key numbers
- Low-mass neutron star mass at explosion: 0.1 solar masses (the minimum NS mass limit)
- Roche lobe radius of low-mass NS: approximately 270 km
- Binary separation at explosion: approximately 1441 km (for component masses 1.4 and 0.1 solar masses)
- Escape speed from low-mass NS surface: 10^9 cm/s; escape speed from massive component field: 1.6 × 10^9 cm/s
- Maximum temperature along trajectory 1: T9 = 0.93 (initial composition Se-116, Ye = 0.25)
- Maximum temperature along trajectory 4: T9 = 10 (initial composition Ni-64, Ye = 0.44)
- Maximum density along trajectory 1: 4 × 10^11 g/cm^3
- Maximum density along trajectory 4: 10^9 g/cm^3
- Free neutron number density maintained above 10^22 cm^-3 for several hundred milliseconds on trajectories 1 and 2
- Expansion follows ρ ∼ t^-3.6 and T ∼ ρ^2/3 ∼ t^-2.4 after peak
- Rarefaction wave reaches center by t = 0.371 seconds after loss of hydrodynamic stability
- Shock wave crosses mantle-crust boundary at mass coordinate m ≈ 0.08 solar masses between t = 0.373 and 0.375 seconds

## Context
This work extends the stripping scenario originally proposed by Clark and Eardley (1977) and connected to gamma-ray bursts by Blinnikov et al. (1984, 1990). It provides the first nucleosynthesis calculation for this scenario, contrasting with the extensively studied neutron star merger scenario (Freiburghaus et al. 1999; Korobkin et al. 2012; Rosswog et al. 2014). The results show that the abundance pattern from the most neutron-rich ejecta (trajectories 1 and 2) produces well-structured r-process peaks at A ∼ 130 and A ∼ 196 consistent with solar system data, while also forming a rare-earth peak at A ∼ 160–170. The work enables future self-consistent calculations that couple nucleosynthesis with hydrodynamics, including heating from beta decay and fission.
