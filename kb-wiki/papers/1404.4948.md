# Oklo reactors and implications for nuclear science

> We summarize the nuclear physics interests in the Oklo natural nuclear reactors, focusing particularly on developments over the past two decades. Modeling of the reactors has become increasingly sophisticated, employing Monte Carlo simulations with realistic geometries and materials that can generate both the thermal and epithermal fractions. The water content and the temperatures of the reactors have been uncertain parameters. We discuss recent work pointing to lower temperatures than earlier assumed. Nuclear cross sections are input to all Oklo modeling and we discuss a parameter, the $^{175}$Lu ground state cross section for thermal neutron capture leading to the isomer $^{176\mathrm{m}}$ Lu, that warrants further investigation. Studies of the time dependence of dimensionless fundamental constants have been a driver for much of the recent work on Oklo. We critically review neutron resonance energy shifts and their dependence on the fine structure constant $α$ and the ratio $X_q=m_q/Λ$ (where $m_q$ is the average of the $u$ and $d$ current quark masses and $Λ$ is the mass scale of quantum chromodynamics). We suggest a formula for the combined sensitivity to $α$ and $X_q$ that exhibits the dependence on proton number $Z$ and mass number $A$, potentially allowing quantum electrodynamic and quantum chromodynamic effects to be disentangled if a broader range of isotopic abundance data becomes available.

## Key claim

The paper reviews nuclear physics interests in the Oklo natural reactors, focusing on developments over the past two decades, and suggests that recent work points to lower reactor temperatures than earlier assumed, while also proposing a formula for combined sensitivity of neutron resonance energy shifts to the fine structure constant alpha and the quark-mass parameter X_q that could disentangle quantum electrodynamic and quantum chromodynamic effects if broader isotopic abundance data become available.

## Method

The authors summarize Monte Carlo simulations using the MCNP code with realistic geometries and material compositions for reactor zones RZ2 and RZ10, modeling neutron fluxes and gamma-ray fluxes at temperatures from 20 degrees Celsius to 500 degrees Celsius. They review lutetium thermometry applied to Oklo samples, which depends on the branching ratio B^g for thermal neutron capture by 175Lu leading to the isomer 176mLu, and they critically examine neutron resonance energy shifts and their dependence on alpha and X_q using the Feynman-Hellmann theorem and classical Coulomb energy approximations.

## Key numbers

- The 235U isotopic abundance in Oklo uraninite deposits was 0.600 percent, instead of the normal 0.7202 percent
- The age of the Oklo phenomenon is reported as 1950 plus or minus 40 million years
- Reactor operation duration for zones 10 and 13 and the Bangombe site ranges from 100 to 300 thousand years
- The average neutron flux density in steady-state operation is about 10^8 cm^-2 s^-1, about five orders of magnitude less than present day power reactors
- For pulsed operation, the reactor-off time Delta t_off is 2.5 hours and the reactor-on time Delta t_on is 0.5 hours
- The multiplication coefficient k_eff is 1.033 for RZ2 and 1.036 for RZ10, with spectral indices r_O of 0.22 and 0.15 respectively
- The total prompt gamma-ray flux in RZ10 is about 3 times 10^9 gamma cm^-2 s^-1
- Lutetium thermometry for RZ10 using improved astrophysical branching ratio values yields T_O = (100 plus or minus 30) degrees Celsius
- The 97.3 meV resonance in neutron capture by 149Sm has received the most attention for studies of time variation of fundamental constants

## Context

This review extends earlier work by Naudet (1991), Cowan (1976), Petrov (1977), Kuroda (1982), Meshik (2005), and Barre (2005), and focuses on developments since 2000. The paper critically examines the lutetium thermometry results of Holliger and Devillers (1981) and Hidaka and Holliger (1998), and the resonance shift analyses of Damour and Dyson (1996) and Shlyakhter (1976). The work enables refined bounds on time variation of the fine structure constant and light quark masses, and suggests that a precision measurement of the 175Lu branching ratio at thermal energies would help resolve temperature uncertainties.
