# Core-collapse supernova simulations with reduced nucleosynthesis networks

> We present core-collapse supernova simulations including nuclear reaction networks that impact explosion dynamics and nucleosynthesis. The different composition treatment can lead to changes in the neutrino heating in the vicinity of the shock by modifying the number of nucleons and thus the neutrino-opacity of the region. This reduces the ram pressure outside the shock and allows an easier expansion. The energy released by the nuclear reactions during collapse also slows down the accretion and aids the shock expansion. In addition, nuclear energy generation in the postshocked matter produces up to $20\%$ more energetic explosions. Nucleosynthesis is affected due to the different dynamic evolution of the explosion. Our results indicate that the energy generation from nuclear reactions helps to sustain late outflows from the vicinity of the proto-neutron star, synthesizing more neutron-rich species. Furthermore, we show that there are systematic discrepancies between the ejecta calculated with in-situ and ex-situ reaction networks. These differences stem from the intrinsic characteristics of evolving the composition in hydrodynamic simulations or calculating it with Lagrangian tracer particles. The mass fractions of some Ca, Ti, Cr, and Fe isotopes are consistently underproduced in postprocessing calculations, leading to different nucleosynthesis paths. Our results suggest that large in-situ nuclear reaction networks are important for a realistic feedback of the energy generation, the neutrino heating, and a more accurate ejecta composition.

## Key claim

Including reduced nuclear reaction networks directly in core-collapse supernova simulations (rather than using simple composition treatments or postprocessing) modifies neutrino heating near the shock by changing the number of nucleons and thus the neutrino opacity, which reduces ram pressure outside the shock and aids shock expansion; the authors show that this effect, combined with nuclear energy generation, can produce up to 20% more energetic explosions, but note that the results depend on the specific network size and that systematic discrepancies exist between in-situ and ex-situ (postprocessing) nucleosynthesis calculations.

## Method

The authors performed radiation-hydrodynamic core-collapse supernova simulations that include reduced nuclear reaction networks coupled directly to the hydrodynamics, evolving the composition of a set of nuclear species during the simulation rather than using a simple equation of state or postprocessing with Lagrangian tracer particles. They compared simulations with different network sizes and treatments to assess the impact on explosion dynamics, neutrino heating, and nucleosynthesis yields, and they contrasted the in-situ network results with ex-situ postprocessing calculations using tracer particles to identify systematic discrepancies in the ejecta composition.

## Context

This work extends multidimensional core-collapse supernova simulations (e.g., Lentz 2015, Janka 2016, Roberts 2016, Burrows 2020) by incorporating reduced nuclear reaction networks directly into the hydrodynamic evolution, rather than relying solely on postprocessing with Lagrangian tracer particles as done in studies such as Winteler (2012) and Lippuner (2017). The results enable more accurate predictions of both explosion dynamics and nucleosynthesis yields, particularly for neutron-rich species in late outflows from the proto-neutron star vicinity, and highlight the need for large in-situ networks to capture realistic energy generation and neutrino heating feedback.
