# Accuracy and precision of the estimation of the number of missing levels in chaotic spectra using long-range correlations

> We study the accuracy and precision for estimating the fraction of observed levels $φ$ in quantum chaotic spectra through long-range correlations. We focus on the main statistics where theoretical formulas for the fraction of missing levels have been derived, the $Δ_3$ of Dyson and Mehta and the power spectrum of the $δ_n$ statistic. We use Monte Carlo simulations of the spectra from the diagonalization of Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble matrices with a definite number of levels randomly taken out to fit the formulas and calculate the distribution of the estimators for different sizes of the spectrum and values of $φ$. A proper averaging of the power spectrum of the $δ_n$ statistic needs to be performed for avoiding systematic errors in the estimation. Once the proper averaging is made the estimation of the fraction of observed levels has quite good accuracy for the two methods even for the lowest dimensions we consider $d=100$. However, the precision is generally better for the estimation using the power spectrum of the $δ_n$ as compared to the estimation using the $Δ_3$ statistic. This difference is clearly bigger for larger dimensions. Our results show that a careful analysis of the value of the fit in view of the ensemble distribution of the estimations is mandatory for understanding its actual significance and give a realistic error interval.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that both the $\Delta_3$ statistic and the power spectrum of the $\delta_n$ statistic provide accurate estimates of the fraction of observed levels in chaotic spectra, but the power spectrum method yields significantly better precision (smaller ensemble standard deviation) for most spectrum sizes, especially for larger dimensions and lower fractions of observed levels.

## Method

The authors generated ensembles of 1500 Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) matrices for dimensions ranging from d=100 to d=2500, then randomly removed levels to create spectra with known fractions of observed levels φ from 0.3 to 1.0. For each individual spectrum, they calculated the Δ₃(L) statistic and the power spectrum P_k^δ of the δ_n statistic, then performed nonlinear fits to the theoretical formulas that relate these statistics to φ, and finally computed the ensemble mean and standard deviation of the estimated φ values to assess accuracy and precision.

## Key numbers

- For d=2500 with φ=0.95: the power spectrum method gave μ=0.935 with σ=0.010 (1.0% precision), while Δ₃ gave μ=0.952 with σ=0.013 (1.4% precision)
- For d=2500 with φ=0.3: the power spectrum method gave μ=0.311 with σ=0.037 (12% precision), while Δ₃ gave μ=0.33 with σ=0.17 (52% precision)
- For d=200 with φ=0.95: the power spectrum method gave μ=0.931 with σ=0.035 (3.8% precision), while Δ₃ gave μ=0.951 with σ=0.039 (4.1% precision)
- For d=200 with φ=0.3: the power spectrum method gave μ=0.34 with σ=0.13 (38% precision), while Δ₃ gave μ=0.32 with σ=0.20 (63% precision)
- The optimal number of averaging intervals for the power spectrum is given by n_int = 0.5 * d^0.65

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical derivations of formulas for the Δ₃ statistic by Bohigas (2004) and Mulhall (2011) and for the P_k^δ statistic by Molina (2007), which gave ensemble-average predictions for spectra with missing levels but did not provide the ensemble standard deviation needed for error estimation. The results enable experimentalists studying chaotic quantum systems such as microwave billiards (Lawniczak 2018), microwave networks (Dietz 2017), and molecules (Mur-Petit 2015) to properly quantify the uncertainty in their estimates of missing levels and to choose the optimal statistic for their particular spectrum size and expected fraction of observed levels.
