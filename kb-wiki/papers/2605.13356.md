# Anomalous large-angle $α$-scattering in a single-folding model with microscopic densities

> We investigate anomalous large-angle scattering (ALAS) of $α$-particles from $N=Z$ nuclei within the framework of the single-folding model. Differential cross sections are calculated by folding the $α$-nucleon interaction with nuclear density distributions obtained from both relativistic and non-relativistic mean-field models. The folding procedure employs a Gaussian-form $α$-nucleon interaction, with its energy dependence and range constrained by previous theoretical studies. Our results show that ALAS in $sd$-shell nuclei is reasonably well reproduced using the microscopic densities together with an $α$-nucleon interaction characterized by a unified parameter set, in which only two parameters vary with the mass number.

# Anomalous large-angle α-scattering in a single-folding model with microscopic densities

## Key claim

The single-folding model using microscopic nuclear densities from relativistic and non-relativistic mean-field calculations, together with a unified α-nucleon interaction parameter set where only two parameters vary with mass number, can reasonably reproduce anomalous large-angle scattering (ALAS) in sd-shell nuclei, but only when the imaginary part of the optical potential is also derived within the folding framework, not assumed to have a standard Woods-Saxon form as in prior studies.

## Method

The authors calculate differential cross sections for elastic α-particle scattering by folding a Gaussian-form α-nucleon interaction with nuclear density distributions obtained from two self-consistent approaches: relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov with parity and angular-momentum projection (RHB+PGCM) and non-relativistic quark-meson coupling with quartet condensation model (QMC+QCM). Both the real and imaginary parts of the optical potential are derived within the folding framework, with the energy dependence and range of the real part fixed from theoretical estimates by Lassaut (1987). The remaining parameters, the strengths g_R and g_I of the real and imaginary components, are fitted to experimental differential cross sections for each target nucleus and α-particle energy, while the imaginary range a_I and density-dependence parameters γ_R and γ_I are kept fixed across all systems.

## Key numbers

- α-nucleon interaction real part range fixed at a_R = 2 fm, close to the value 1.97 fm from Lassaut (1987)
- Imaginary part range fixed at a_I = 2.25 fm from fitting
- Density-dependence parameters fixed at γ_R = 1.8 fm² and γ_I = 1.7 fm²
- For ²⁰Ne at 33 MeV: g_R = 2.7 ± 0.26, g_I = 0.30 ± 0.21, with correlation coefficient -0.34
- For ²⁴Mg at 22.1 MeV: g_R = 2.0 ± 0.068, g_I = 0.15 ± 0.004, with correlation coefficient 0.34
- For ²⁸Si at 40 MeV: g_R = 1.8 ± 0.064, g_I = 0.25 ± 0.023, with correlation coefficient -0.20
- For ³⁶Ar at 17.5 MeV: g_R = 2.2 ± 0.020, g_I = 0.09 ± 0.002, with correlation coefficient 0.061
- For ⁴⁰Ca at 22 MeV: g_R = 2.1 ± 0.648, g_I = 0.10 ± 0.0029, with correlation coefficient 0.90
- ²⁰Ne ground state deformation β₂ = 0.72 (experimental), compared to RHB+PGCM average β₂ = 0.27
- At θ = 120° for ²⁰Ne at 33 MeV, the differential cross section increases by nearly an order of magnitude as deformation changes from β₂ = 0 to β₂ = 0.72
- ³⁶Ar has only 4 excited states below 4.5 MeV, while ⁴⁰Ar has 24 states in the same energy region

## Context

This work extends the single-folding model approach previously applied to ALAS by Gubler (1980) and Kassem (1985), who used phenomenological density distributions and assumed a Woods-Saxon form for the imaginary potential. Here, both real and imaginary parts are derived from folding with microscopic densities from RHB+PGCM and QMC+QCM calculations, which naturally incorporate deformation and ground-state correlations. The results show that ALAS in N=Z nuclei cannot be explained solely by ground-state density effects but requires weak absorption that reflects differences in the low-energy excitation spectra, consistent with the interpretation by Michel (1983, 1986) that ALAS arises from α particles penetrating the nucleus and being scattered back by the wall of the effective potential. The approach enables future studies of α-clustering effects in N=Z nuclei using fully microscopic densities.
