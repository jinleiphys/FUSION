# Evidence for a New State of Matter: An Assessment of the Results from the CERN Lead Beam Programme

> This "position paper" collects the scientific arguments behind the CERN Press Release of February 10, 2000, which announced evidence for the creation of a new state of matter in Pb-Pb collisions at the CERN SPS. The data on which this paper is based were presented in a special seminar at CERN on the same day, a recording of which can be accessed at http://www.cern.ch/CERN/Announcements/2000/NewStateMatter/ .

# Evidence for a New State of Matter: An Assessment of the Results from the CERN Lead Beam Programme

## Key claim

The authors conclude that the collected data from the CERN SPS lead beam programme provide compelling but indirect evidence that a new state of matter, featuring many characteristics of the theoretically predicted quark-gluon plasma, has been created in Pb-Pb collisions at energy densities exceeding by more than a factor 20 that of normal nuclear matter. The evidence is circumstantial, based on final-state hadronic observables that retain memory of the initial state, and the authors explicitly state that a full characterization of the initial collision stage is not yet possible.

## Method

Seven experiments (NA44, NA45/CERES, NA49, NA50, NA52/NEWMASS, WA97/NA57, and WA98) measured multiple observables in Pb-Pb and Pb-Au collisions at 158–160 GeV per nucleon (and 40 GeV per nucleon in 1999) at the CERN SPS. The analysis combined hadron abundances and momentum distributions for more than 20 hadron species (including anti-deuterons and anti-helium), two-particle Bose-Einstein correlations for freeze-out volume extraction, dilepton (electron-positron and dimuon) spectra, direct photon searches, and charmonium (J/ψ, χc, ψ′) suppression patterns as a function of collision centrality. The data were compared to hadronic rescattering models and to predictions from lattice QCD and quark-gluon plasma signatures.

## Key numbers

- Energy density of normal nuclear matter: approximately 1 GeV/fm³ (seven times that of nuclear matter) is the threshold for the transition to the new state, as given by lattice QCD calculations with transition temperatures between 140 and 180 MeV.
- In a head-on Pb-Pb collision at the SPS, about 2500 particles are created (NA49), of which more than 99.9% are hadrons.
- The fireball volume expands by a factor of 30–50 between hadronization and freeze-out, extracted from Bose-Einstein interferometry (NA44, NA49, WA98).
- Freeze-out temperature: 100–120 MeV, with expansion velocities exceeding half the speed of light.
- Chemical freeze-out (hadronization) temperature: about 170 MeV, corresponding to an energy density of about 1 GeV/fm³.
- Initial energy density in central Pb-Pb collisions: about 3–4 GeV/fm³, with mean particle momenta corresponding to T ≈ 240 MeV.
- Strangeness enhancement: globally a factor of 2 relative to proton-induced collisions (NA49); the Omega hyperon and its antiparticle are enhanced by up to a factor of 15 (WA97).
- Dilepton excess in Pb-Au collisions in the mass region 250–700 MeV: by about a factor of 3 above expectations from hadron decays scaled from p-nucleon to Pb-Au collisions (NA45).
- J/ψ suppression requires temperatures about 30% above the color deconfinement temperature, or energy densities of about 3 GeV/fm³ (NA50).
- For S-Au collisions, not more than 5% of observed photons are emitted directly (WA80, NA45).
- NA50 observed an excess by about a factor of 2 in the dimuon spectrum in the mass region between the φ and J/ψ vector mesons.

## Context

This paper synthesizes the results of the CERN SPS lead beam programme (1994–1999) and presents the scientific basis for the CERN Press Release of February 10, 2000, which announced evidence for a new state of matter. The work extends earlier studies with lighter ions (sulphur beams) and smaller targets, where strangeness enhancement and charmonium suppression were first observed by NA35, WA85, WA94, and NA38. The findings contradict predictions from hadronic rescattering models, which fail to simultaneously explain the full set of observables. The paper sets the stage for the RHIC era at BNL, where higher collision energies (10 times higher than SPS) and dedicated detectors are expected to provide more direct measurements of the quark-gluon plasma via electromagnetic signals, jet quenching, and heavy quark production, enabling quantitative characterization of the plasma and its dynamical evolution.
