# Converged ab initio calculations of heavy nuclei

> We propose a novel storage scheme for three-nucleon (3N) interaction matrix elements relevant for the normal-ordered two-body approximation used extensively in ab initio calculations of atomic nuclei. This scheme reduces the required memory by approximately two orders of magnitude, which allows the generation of 3N interaction matrix elements with the standard truncation of $E_{\rm 3max}=28$, well beyond the previous limit of 18. We demonstrate that this is sufficient to obtain the ground-state energy of $^{132}$Sn converged to within a few MeV with respect to the $E_{\rm 3max}$ truncation.In addition, we study the asymptotic convergence behavior and perform extrapolations to the un-truncated limit. Finally, we investigate the impact of truncations made when evolving free-space 3N interactions with the similarity renormalization group. We find that the contribution of blocks with angular momentum $J_{\rm rel}>9/2$ to the ground-state energy is dominated by a basis-truncation artifact which vanishes in the large-space limit, so these computationally expensive components can be neglected. For the two sets of nuclear interactions employed in this work, the resulting binding energy of $^{132}$Sn agrees with the experimental value within theoretical uncertainties. This work enables converged ab initio calculations of heavy nuclei.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that by storing only the specific combinations of three-nucleon (3N) matrix elements required for the normal-ordered two-body (NO2B) approximation, rather than the full set, they can reach a truncation of \(E_{\rm 3max}=28\) (previously limited to 18), which is sufficient to converge the ground-state energy of \(^{132}\)Sn to within a few MeV; however, the convergence is demonstrated for soft interactions where many-body perturbation theory is applicable, and the extrapolation formula relies on the assumption that the correlation energy is dominated by second-order perturbation theory.

## Method

The authors developed a novel storage scheme that computes and stores only the 3N matrix element combinations entering the NO2B approximation for a spherical reference state, reducing memory requirements by approximately two orders of magnitude. They implemented an efficient transformation from the three-body Jacobi basis to the single-particle lab frame using a new expression for the T-coefficient that involves four nested summations instead of the previous six. The resulting matrix elements up to \(E_{\rm 3max}=28\) were generated once per interaction and then used in Hartree-Fock many-body perturbation theory (HF-MBPT) and in-medium similarity renormalization group (IMSRG) calculations for nuclei around \(^{132}\)Sn.

## Key numbers

- Memory reduction by approximately two orders of magnitude for storing 3N matrix elements in the NO2B approximation compared to the full set.
- Generation of 3N matrix elements up to \(E_{\rm 3max}=28\) achieved using approximately \(10^{5}\) CPU hours with 187 GB RAM per node (half-precision).
- Ground-state energy of \(^{132}\)Sn at \(E_{\rm 3max}=28\) with third-order MBPT: \(-1118.16\) MeV for \(e_{\rm max}=18\).
- Half-precision storage at \(e_{\rm max}=14\), \(E_{\rm 3max}=24\) introduced a total energy shift of \(-1.35\) MeV (HF shift \(-2.14\) MeV, second-order correction \(+0.68\) MeV, third-order correction \(+0.11\) MeV).
- Excitation energies of \(^{127}\)Cd converged to within 5 keV by \(E_{\rm 3max}=28\).
- Extrapolated ground-state energy of \(^{132}\)Sn to \(E_{\rm 3max}=42\) using the \(n=2\) formula: \(-1110.57(2)\) MeV (IMSRG) and \(-1097.13(2)\) MeV (HF-MBPT(2)).

## Context

This work extends the reach of ab initio calculations beyond the previous limit of \(A \sim 100\) by overcoming the \(E_{\rm 3max} \lesssim 18\) bottleneck that prevented converged calculations for heavier systems, as noted in prior studies by Binder (2014), Lascar (2017), and Arthuis (2020). The approach builds on the NO2B approximation validated by Roth (2012) and Binder (2013), and uses a similar idea to Binder (2014) but with a non-iterative transformation. This enables future controlled calculations of heavy nuclei such as \(^{208}\)Pb and isotopes relevant for searches for physics beyond the standard model (e.g., xenon, tellurium, cesium, mercury), as well as astrophysically relevant nuclei in the \(N=126\) region.
