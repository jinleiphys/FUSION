# Heavy quark energy loss far from equilibrium in a strongly coupled collision

> We compute and study the drag force acting on a heavy quark propagating through the matter produced in the collision of two sheets of energy in a strongly coupled gauge theory that can be analyzed holographically. Although this matter is initially far from equilibrium, we find that the equilibrium expression for heavy quark energy loss in a homogeneous strongly coupled plasma with the same instantaneous energy density or pressure as that at the location of the quark describes many qualitative features of our results. One interesting exception is that there is a time delay after the initial collision before the heavy quark energy loss becomes significant. At later times, once a liquid plasma described by viscous hydrodynamics has formed, expressions based upon assuming instantaneous homogeneity and equilibrium provide a semi-quantitative description of our results - as long as the rapidity of the heavy quark is not too large. For a heavy quark with large rapidity, the gradients in the velocity of the hydrodynamic fluid result in qualitative consequences for the 'drag' force acting on the quark. In certain circumstances, the force required to drag the quark through the plasma can point opposite to the velocity of the quark, meaning that the force that the plasma exerts on a quark moving through it acts in the same direction as its velocity. And, generically, the force includes a component perpendicular to the direction of motion of the quark. Our results support a straightforward approach to modeling the drag on, and energy loss of, heavy quarks with modest rapidity in heavy ion collisions, both before and after the quark-gluon plasma hydrodynamizes, and provide cautionary lessons at higher rapidity.

## Key claim

The equilibrium expression for heavy quark energy loss in a homogeneous strongly coupled plasma with the same instantaneous energy density or pressure describes many qualitative features of the drag force even when the matter is far from equilibrium, but there is a time delay after the initial collision before energy loss becomes significant, and at large rapidity the gradients in the hydrodynamic fluid velocity produce qualitative effects including a force component perpendicular to the quark's motion and, in some circumstances, a drag force that points opposite to the quark's velocity.

## Method

The authors study the collision of two planar sheets of energy density with Gaussian profiles in strongly coupled N=4 supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory using holography, where the sheets map to colliding gravitational shockwaves in asymptotically AdS5 spacetime. They add a heavy quark moving at constant velocity by including a classical string attached to the boundary and dragging its endpoint at constant velocity through the colliding gravitational wave geometry, solving Einstein's equations numerically for the background metric and then computing the string profile to extract the force needed to maintain the quark's velocity. The drag force is compared to what it would be in a homogeneous plasma in thermal equilibrium with the same instantaneous energy density or pressure at the quark's location.

## Key numbers

- The width of each Gaussian energy-density sheet is chosen as w = 0.5/μ, where μ is the energy scale defining the sheets' energy per unit area of N_c^2 μ^3/(2π^2)
- The background temperature is T_background = 0.085 μ, corresponding to an initial background energy density 213 times smaller than at the center of the sheets and 450 times smaller than at t=z=0 during the collision
- The hydrodynamization time is t_hydro = 2.8/μ using the strict criterion that actual pressures are within 15% of values from hydrodynamic constitutive relations
- At low β, the time delay in the drag force rise is of order 1/(π T_hydro)

## Context

This work extends the holographic study of colliding sheets of energy introduced by Chesler (2010) and analyzed further by Casalderrey-Solana (2013) and others, applying it to compute heavy quark energy loss for the first time in a far-from-equilibrium strongly coupled system. The results support modeling heavy quark drag with modest rapidity using equilibrium expressions both before and after hydrodynamization, but provide cautionary lessons at higher rapidity where velocity gradients produce qualitative effects absent in homogeneous equilibrium calculations. The findings contrast with weak-coupling expectations where instabilities in far-from-equilibrium matter can substantially enhance heavy-quark energy loss, as discussed by Carrington (2013).
