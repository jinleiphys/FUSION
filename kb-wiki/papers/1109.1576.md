# Accurate calibration of relativistic mean-field models: correlating observables and providing meaningful theoretical uncertainties

> Theoretical uncertainties in the predictions of relativistic mean-field models are estimated using a chi-square minimization procedure that is implemented by studying the small oscillations around the chi-square minimum. By diagonalizing the matrix of second derivatives, one gains access to a wealth of information---in the form of powerful correlations---that would normally remain hidden. We illustrate the power of the {\sl covariance analysis} by using two relativistic mean-field models: (a) the original linear Walecka model and (b) the accurately calibrated FSUGold parametrization. In addition to providing meaningful theoretical uncertainties for both model parameters and predicted observables, the covariance analysis establishes robust correlations between physical observables. In particular, we show that whereas the correlation coefficient between the slope of the symmetry energy and the neutron-skin thickness of Lead is indeed very large, a 1% measurement of the neutron radius of Lead may only be able to constrain the slope of the symmetry energy to about 30%.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that covariance analysis of relativistic mean-field models, implemented by studying small oscillations around the χ² minimum, can provide meaningful theoretical uncertainties and reveal correlations between observables, but the authors explicitly note that a 1% measurement of the neutron radius of Lead may only constrain the slope of the symmetry energy to about 30%, not to the same precision.

## Method

The authors perform a χ² minimization procedure for relativistic mean-field models, then diagonalize the matrix of second derivatives of the χ² measure to obtain eigenvalues and eigenvectors that characterize the stiffness or softness of parameter-space directions. Using the inverse of this matrix as the covariance matrix, they compute theoretical uncertainties for model parameters and predicted observables, as well as Pearson correlation coefficients between observables, for two example models: the linear Walecka model and the FSUGold parametrization.

## Key numbers

- For the linear Walecka model calibrated to three observables (saturation density, energy per nucleon, and effective Dirac mass), the two eigenvalues of the curvature matrix are λ₁ = 7.4399×10⁴ and λ₂ = 8.3195×10¹.
- The resulting uncertainties in the coupling constants are: g_s² = 93.62647 ± 6.83008 and g_v² = 180.48347 ± 14.78596, corresponding to fractional uncertainties of about 5-10%.
- The correlation coefficient between the slope of the symmetry energy and the neutron-skin thickness of Lead is stated to be "indeed very large" (no exact numerical value given in the text).
- A 1% measurement of the neutron radius of Lead may constrain the slope of the symmetry energy to about 30%.

## Context

This work extends the covariance analysis approach previously applied by Reinhard (2010) to non-relativistic density functional theory, now applied to relativistic mean-field models including the linear Walecka model and the accurately calibrated FSUGold parametrization. The analysis enables identification of which observables (such as neutron-skin thickness or electric dipole polarizability) are most sensitive to poorly-constrained parameter combinations, guiding future experimental measurements and model refinements.
