# The Love-Hate Relationship Between the Shell Model and Cluster Models

> We adopt a personal approach here reviewing several calculations over the years in which we have experienced confrontations between cluster models and the shell model. In previous cluster conferences we have noted that cluster models go hand in hand with Skyrme Hartee-Fock calculations in describing states which cannot easily, if at all, be handled by the shell model. These are the highly deformed (many particle - many hole) intruder states, linear chain states e.t.c. In the present work we will consider several topics; the quadrupole moment of $^{6}$Li, the non-existence of low lying intruders in $^{8}$Be, and then jumping to the $f_{7/2}$ shell, we discuss the two-faceted nature of the nuclei - sometimes displaying shell model properties, other times cluster properties.

# The Love-Hate Relationship Between the Shell Model and Cluster Models

## Key claim

The shell model and cluster models often give conflicting results for the same nuclear observables, yet both approaches are necessary because each succeeds in regimes where the other fails; the paper argues that the two models should be viewed as complementary rather than competing, with the shell model working better in the upper half of the f7/2 shell and cluster models being essential for highly deformed intruder states that the shell model cannot handle.

## Method

The authors review several confrontations between shell model and cluster model calculations across multiple nuclear systems, including: shell model calculations of the quadrupole moment of the J=1+ state in 6Li using 0, 2, and 4 hbar-omega spaces; shell model calculations with a quadrupole-quadrupole force to search for low-lying intruder states in 8Be; and single-j shell model calculations for 44Ti and 52Fe using five different interaction models (Models I through V) based on experimental two-body matrix elements from 42Sc and 54Co. They also perform Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations for many particle-many hole states in 40Ca and compare single-j shell model predictions for analog state excitation energies with experimental data.

## Key numbers

* Quadrupole moment of the J=1+ state in 6Li: experimental Q = -0.818(17) mb, while cluster model calculations give Q = +2.56 mb and shell model calculations in the 0 hbar-omega space give Q = -3.60 mb
* Shell model quadrupole moment of 6Li converges toward experiment with increasing model space: -3.60 mb (0 hbar-omega), -2.51 mb (2 hbar-omega), -0.085 mb (4 hbar-omega), compared to experimental -0.82 mb
* Magnetic moment of 6Li: experimental mu = 0.822 nm, shell model gives 0.866 nm (0 hbar-omega), 0.848 nm (2 hbar-omega), 0.846 nm (4 hbar-omega)
* In 40Ca, Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations give intrinsic state energies of 12.1 MeV for 4p-4h and 11.4 MeV for 8p-8h; after projection, the 4p-4h state is at 6.85 MeV and 8p-8h at 8.02 MeV
* Single analog excitation energies in the f7/2 shell: 44Ti at 9.340 MeV, 48Cr at 8.75 MeV, 52Fe at 8.559 MeV; double analog excitation energies: 46Ti at 14.153 MeV, 50Cr at 13.222 MeV
* The 2-to-1 ratio between double and single analog excitations holds experimentally for 51Cr/50Cr: 6.511 MeV and 13.022 MeV respectively
* Cross-conjugate nuclei 46Ti and 50Cr have nearly identical spectra: 2+ states at 0.889 MeV (46Ti) and 0.787 MeV (50Cr), 4+ at 2.010 MeV and 1.884 MeV, 6+ at 3.297 MeV and 3.164 MeV, 8+ at 4.896 MeV and 4.740 MeV

## Context

This review extends the long-standing debate between cluster model and shell model approaches, building on prior work by Csoto and Lovas (1996), Zheng, Vory and Barrett (1996), and the authors' own calculations with Zheng and Fayache (1995). The paper directly compares with the alpha cluster model calculations for 44Ti presented at the Santorini conference (1993) and with the work of Hasegawa and Kaneko (2000) who emphasize alpha clustering in the f7/2 shell. The results enable future work on resolving the sign discrepancy for the 6Li quadrupole moment, understanding the role of T=0 pairing in nuclei, and determining whether apparent clustering signatures in the f7/2 region are actually symmetry effects rather than genuine cluster formation.
