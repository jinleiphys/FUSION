# Dispersion theory of nucleon Compton scattering and polarizabilities

> A status report on the topic Compton scattering and polarizabilities is presented with emphasis on the scalar t-channel as entering into dispersion theory. Precise values for the polarizabilities are obtained leading to $α_p = 12.0\pm 0.6$ $(12.0)$, $β_p=1.9\mp 0.6$ $(1.9)$, $α_n= 12.5\pm 1.7$ $(13.4)$, $β_n= 2.7 \mp 1.8$ $(1.8)$ in units of $10^{-4}$ fm$^3$ and $γ^{(p)}_π= -36.4 \pm 1.5$ $(-36.6)$, $γ^{(n)}_π= 58.6 \pm 4.0$ $(58.3)$, $(γ^{(p)}_0= -0.58\pm 0.20)$, $(γ^{(n)}_0 = +0.38\pm 0.22)$ in units of $10^{-4}$ fm$^4$, for the proton (p) and neutron (n), respectively. The data given with an error are {\it recommended} experimental values with updates compared to [1] where necessary, the data in parentheses are predicted values. These predicted values are not contained in [1], but are the result of a newly developed analysis which is the main topic of the present paper. The most important recent discovery is that the largest part of the electric polarizability and the total diamagnetic polarizability of the nucleon are properties of the $σ$ meson as part of the constituent-quark structure, as expected from the mechanism of chiral symmetry breaking. This view is supported by an experiment on Compton scattering by the proton carried out in the second resonance region, where a large contribution from the $σ$ meson enters into the scattering amplitudes. This experiment led to a determination of the mass of the $σ$ meson of $m_σ= 600 \pm 70$ MeV. From the experimental $α_p$ and predicted differences $(α_n - α_p)$ neutron polarizabilities in the range $α_n= 12.0 - 13.4$ are predicted, where the uncertainties are related to the $f_0(980)$ and $a_0(980)$ scalar mesons.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the largest part of the electric polarizability and the total diamagnetic polarizability of the nucleon originate from the sigma meson as part of the constituent-quark structure, consistent with chiral symmetry breaking, but this conclusion depends on the specific interpretation of the scalar t-channel contribution and the acceptance of the sigma meson as a well-defined particle.

## Method

The authors apply dispersion theory to nucleon Compton scattering, separating the scattering amplitude into s-channel contributions from nucleon excited states (derived from high-precision analyses of photomeson data) and t-channel contributions from scalar mesons (sigma, f0(980), a0(980)). They use the quark-level linear sigma model (QLLsigmaM) to quantitatively predict the t-channel part of the electromagnetic polarizabilities, and compare these predictions with experimental data from Compton scattering experiments on the proton and neutron, as well as from electromagnetic scattering of slow neutrons.

## Key numbers

* Proton electric polarizability: alpha_p = 12.0 +/- 0.6 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
* Proton magnetic polarizability: beta_p = 1.9 -/+ 0.6 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
* Neutron electric polarizability: alpha_n = 12.5 +/- 1.7 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
* Neutron magnetic polarizability: beta_n = 2.7 -/+ 1.8 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
* Proton backward spin-polarizability: gamma_pi^(p) = -36.4 +/- 1.5 (in units of 10^-4 fm^4)
* Neutron backward spin-polarizability: gamma_pi^(n) = 58.6 +/- 4.0 (in units of 10^-4 fm^4)
* Proton forward spin-polarizability: gamma_0^(p) = -0.58 +/- 0.20 (in units of 10^-4 fm^4)
* Neutron forward spin-polarizability: gamma_0^(n) = +0.38 +/- 0.22 (in units of 10^-4 fm^4)
* Bare sigma meson mass determined from Compton scattering experiment: m_sigma = 600 +/- 70 MeV
* Baldin-Lapidus sum rule constraints: alpha_p + beta_p = 13.9 +/- 0.3, alpha_n + beta_n = 15.2 +/- 0.5 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
* Neutron electric polarizability from Coulomb scattering of neutrons: alpha_n = 12.6 +/- 2.5 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
* Neutron electric polarizability from quasifree Compton scattering: alpha_n = 12.5 +/- 2.3 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)

## Context

This work extends the dispersion theory approach to nucleon polarizabilities previously reviewed by Schumacher (2005), incorporating the quark-level linear sigma model developed by Delbourgo, Scadron and coworkers to explain the scalar t-channel contribution. The analysis builds on earlier calculations by Bernabeu, Ericson and collaborators (1974, 1977) who first identified the missing piece in the electric polarizability and the diamagnetic polarizability as arising from the scalar t-channel. The results enable precise predictions of neutron polarizabilities from proton data using isovector components, and provide a framework for interpreting Compton scattering experiments in the second resonance region, such as those carried out at MAMI (Mainz) by Galler and Wolf (2001).
