# Imaging Three-Dimensional Relative Sources from Nuclear Reactions

> One can access the space-time development of a heavy-ion reaction directly by imaging the source function from two particle correlation functions. In the case of like-charged pions, this imaging can be recast as a Fourier inversion problem. We will demonstrate how this inversion can be performed on full three-dimensional (i.e. in long, side and out coordinates) experimentally determined correlation functions. We will discuss the resulting three dimensional images of the relative sources. Finally, we will discuss how to perform the full three dimensional inversion for particles whose final state interactions are more complicated than those of the pions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that full three-dimensional (3D) source functions can be imaged from experimentally determined two-particle correlation functions, but the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) method works only for like-charged pion pairs and does not reliably report uncertainty, whereas a general inversion method works for any particle pair and provides realistic error estimates.

## Method

The authors compare two inversion methods applied to a simulated Gaussian test correlation function with Coulomb-corrected pion data. The first method uses a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm to invert the Pratt-Koonin equation, treating the kernel as a cosine transform for pions where final-state interactions are ignored. The second method implements a general 3D imaging procedure that minimizes chi-squared between the experimental and test correlation functions, solving a matrix equation for the source and its covariance matrix.

## Key numbers

- The test correlation function used Gaussian radius parameters: \(R_O = R_S = 4\) fm and \(R_L = 6\) fm.
- The FFT inversion alone produced tails that overestimate the true source by "several orders of magnitude" (no exact factor given).
- After applying a Weiner optimal filter, the FFT tails fell below the input source, and the inverted source was "not consistent with the input source" in both cases.
- The general method returned errors that were "much more realistic" than the FFT, which claimed it could image "even below noise level."

## Context

This work extends the imaging technique previously developed by Brown (1997) for angle-averaged (one-dimensional) correlation functions to full three-dimensional (long, side, out) correlation data. It directly addresses the limitations of the standard approach of extracting only correlation radii or comparing to semi-classical transport models. The paper enables future studies of non-Gaussian source shapes from resonances, three-dimensional proton sources, flow effects on correlations, and unlike-pair correlations using the general inversion method.
