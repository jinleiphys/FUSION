# Spectroscopic amplitudes and microscopic substructure effects in nucleon capture reactions

> Spectroscopic amplitudes play an important role in nuclear capture reactions. These amplitudes are shown to include both single-particle and polarization effects: the former through their spatial dependence and the latter through their normalization (the spectroscopic factors). Coupled-channels equations are developed for the spectroscopic amplitudes. These equations serve as a convenient starting point for the derivation of several approximations: Hartree, Hartree-Fock and two different single-particle models. The single-particle models include antisymmetry in different ways, but both miss many-body effects. Therefore, cross sections calculated with either of these models need to be multiplied by the spectroscopic factor.

## Key claim

Spectroscopic amplitudes contain both single-particle effects (through their spatial dependence) and polarization effects (through their normalization, the spectroscopic factors); consequently, cross sections calculated with either of the two single-particle models derived in the paper must be multiplied by the spectroscopic factor to account for many-body correlations, contradicting claims that spectroscopic factors should be omitted in peripheral capture reactions.

## Method

The authors derive exact coupled-channels equations for the spectroscopic amplitudes of an A-body system expanded in terms of (A-1)-body eigenstates, then obtain the Hartree, Hartree-Fock, and two distinct single-particle models as approximations. They separate each spectroscopic amplitude into a normalized spatial part and a spectroscopic factor, and express the transition matrix element for a one-body operator as a sum over channels involving these factors.

## Key numbers

- The sum rule for spectroscopic factors: \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty S_n = A\) (exact, model-independent).
- For a harmonic oscillator potential, center-of-mass corrections cause valence-shell spectroscopic amplitudes to increase by a factor \([A/(A-1)]^{m/2}\), where \(m\) is the major oscillator shell number.
- The asymptotic normalization coefficient is given by \(A_{nc} = \lim_{k\rightarrow -i\kappa} 4m\pi^2 \sum_{n'=1}^\infty \int d\bm{r} \; e^{i\bm{k}\bm{r}} \langle\Psi^1_{A-1}| \sum_{i=1}^{A-1} V(|\bm{r}_i - \bm{r}|)| \Psi^{n'}_{A-1}\rangle \phi_{n'}(\bm{r})\), where \(\kappa = \sqrt{2m(E_A - E_{A-1}^1)}\).
- The long-range behavior of the A-body wave function is dominated by \(\phi_1(\bm{r}_A) \propto \exp[-\sqrt{2m(E^1_{A-1} - E_A)} \, r_A]/r\).

## Context

This work extends the coupled-channels formalism for spectroscopic amplitudes originally developed for stripping reactions (Pinkston, 1960s) and clarifies the relationship between the one-body potential model, Hartree-Fock theory, and the cluster-model approach of Varga and Lovas (1990s). It directly addresses the debate between Csoto (2000), who argued that spectroscopic factors should not be used in potential-model calculations of the \(^7\)Be\((p,\gamma)^8\)B reaction, and Mukhamedzhanov et al. (2001), who advocated asymptotic normalization coefficients instead. The paper shows that the asymptotic normalization coefficient implicitly includes the spectroscopic factor, and that both single-particle and polarization effects are necessary for accurate reaction rates in astrophysical contexts such as the solar neutrino problem and primordial nucleosynthesis.
