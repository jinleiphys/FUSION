# Onset of color transparency in holographic light-front QCD

> The color transparency (CT) of a hadron, propagating with reduced absorption in a nucleus, is a fundamental property of QCD (quantum chromodynamics) reflecting its internal structure and effective size when it is produced at high transverse momentum, $Q$. CT has been confirmed in many experiments, such as semi-exclusive hard electroproduction, $e A \to e' πX$ for mesons produced at $Q^2 > 3 ~ {\rm GeV}^2$. However, a recent JLab (Jefferson Laboratory) measurement for a proton electroproduced in carbon $e\, {\rm C}\to e' p X$, where $X$ stands for the inclusive sum of all produced final states, fails to observe CT at $Q^2$ up to 14.2 GeV$^2$. In this paper, the onset of CT is determined by comparing the $Q^2$-dependence of the hadronic cross sections for the initial formation of a small color-singlet configuration using the generalized parton distributions from holographic light-front QCD. A critical dependence on the hadron's twist, $τ$, the number of hadron constituents, is found for the onset of CT, with no significant effects from the nuclear medium. This effect can explain the absence of proton CT in the present kinematic range of the JLab experiment. The proton is predicted to have a "two-stage" color transparency with the onset of CT differing for the spin-conserving (twist-3, $τ=3$) Dirac form factor with a higher onset in $Q^2$ for the spin-flip Pauli (twist-4) form factor. In contrast, the neutron is predicted to have a "one-stage" color transparency with the onset at higher $Q^2$ because of the dominance of its Pauli form factor. The model also predicts a strong dependence at low energies on the flavor of the quark current coupling to the hadron.

## Key claim

The onset of color transparency (CT) depends critically on the hadron's twist (number of constituents), with no significant effects from the nuclear medium; this twist dependence can explain the absence of observed proton CT in the JLab experiment at momentum transfers up to 14.2 GeV², because the proton requires higher Q² than mesons to form a small color-singlet configuration.

## Method

The authors computed the effective transverse impact surface of hadrons as a function of momentum transfer Q² using generalized parton distributions (GPDs) from holographic light-front QCD (HLFQCD). They derived analytic expressions for the Q²-dependent transverse size from the slope of hadronic form factors, expressed in terms of the hadron's twist τ and Regge trajectory α(t), and compared the resulting transverse sizes for different hadron species to determine the Q² threshold where color transparency should become observable.

## Key numbers

- The mass scale parameter κ = √λ = 0.534 GeV, determined from Regge trajectories for ρ and φ vector mesons
- The proton transverse-impact distance squared at Q²=0 is ⟨a²⊥⟩_p = 0.36 fm² from the model, compared to the measured value 0.43 ± 0.01 fm² from electron-proton scattering
- For pions (τ=2), CT onset occurs at Q² > 4 GeV², where the transverse surface contracts to approximately 0.22 fm²
- For the proton twist-3 component (τ=3), the same transverse size of 0.22 fm² is reached at Q² ≈ 14 GeV²
- For the proton twist-4 component (τ=4), the same transverse size requires Q² ≈ 22 GeV²
- The predicted transparency increase for e¹²C → e p X at Q² = 14 GeV² is only about 5%, from T = 0.56 to T = 0.59
- For deuteron production, CT onset is predicted at Q² ≥ 40 GeV²

## Context

This work extends the holographic light-front QCD framework developed by Brodsky and de Teramond (2014, 2020) to predict color transparency thresholds, building on earlier theoretical studies of the connection between GPDs and CT by Burkardt (2003) and Liuti (2004). The predictions explain the discrepancy between observed pion CT in experiments such as Clasie (2007) and Qian (2009) and the absence of proton CT in the recent JLab Hall C measurement by Bhetuwal (2020). The results enable quantitative predictions for future experiments at higher Q², particularly for proton and neutron electroproduction, and suggest that the φ meson could serve as a calibration tool for transparency measurements due to its small transverse size even at low Q².
