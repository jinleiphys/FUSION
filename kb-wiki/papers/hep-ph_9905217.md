# Calculation of the expansion rate of the three-volume measure in high-energy heavy-ion collisions

> In ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions the local three-volume measure is expanding in the longitudinal and transverse directions. This is similar to the Hubble-expansion of the universe, except that the former is not locally isotropic. As an example the expansion rate is calculated assuming that the energy-momentum tensor in the central region is that of an ideal fluid, undergoing Bjorken flow in longitudinal direction, and with initial conditions as expected for BNL-RHIC energy. While the longitudinal expansion of three-volume is independent of the energy density of the fluid, in case of 3+1 dimensional expansion the form of the hydrodynamical solution (rarefaction wave or deflagration shock) affects the three-volume expansion rate on the hadronization hypersurface. As a consequence the average expansion rate on that surface depends on the transverse size of the system. This may reflect in an impact-parameter dependence of the formation probability of light nuclei and of the freeze-out temperature of the strong interactions in the system.

# Calculation of the expansion rate of the three-volume measure in high-energy heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

The expansion rate of the local three-volume measure in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions depends on the transverse size of the system when transverse expansion is present, and the form of the hydrodynamical solution (rarefaction wave or deflagration shock) affects the three-volume expansion rate on the hadronization hypersurface; this may reflect in an impact-parameter dependence of light nucleus formation probability and freeze-out temperature, but only if the energy-momentum tensor is that of an ideal fluid undergoing Bjorken flow with initial conditions expected for BNL-RHIC energy.

## Method

The author solved the relativistic ideal fluid dynamics equations (energy-momentum and net baryon number conservation) numerically using the RHLLE finite-difference scheme, assuming longitudinal boost-invariance (Bjorken flow) and cylindrical symmetry. Initial conditions were chosen for BNL-RHIC energy (sqrt(s)=200 GeV per nucleon pair) with an entropy per net baryon of s/n_B=200, homogeneous on the tau=tau_0 hypersurface, and an MIT bag-model equation of state with a first-order phase transition between quark-gluon plasma and hadronic matter at T_C=160 MeV. The expansion scalar PD.u (the rate of expansion of the local three-volume measure) was computed on the hadronization hypersurface for different ratios of initial proper time tau_0 to transverse radius R_perp.

## Key numbers

- The longitudinal Hubble constant is H(tau)=1/tau for tau >= tau_0 and r_perp <= R_perp; at tau_0 ~ 0.5-1 fm, H is roughly 10^40 times larger than the present expansion rate of the universe and about 10^18 times the Hubble constant at the cosmological hadronization phase transition.
- The initial proper time employed is tau_0=0.6 fm, the average of the estimated range 0.2-1 fm for RHIC energy.
- The initial temperature for the given initial conditions and MIT bag-model EoS is T(tau_0) approximately 300 MeV.
- The critical temperature for the first-order phase transition is T_C=160 MeV, corresponding to a bag constant B=380 MeV/fm^3.
- For tau_0/R_perp=1/10, the expansion factor xi=tau*PD.u is nearly time-independent in the mixed phase until hadronization is completed, with xi=1 over a large part of the hadronization hypersurface for large R_perp (tau_0/R_perp=1/25).
- The uncertainty relation sets a time-scale of ~1/<p_perp>=0.2 fm below which the classical description breaks down.

## Context

This work extends the study of relativistic hydrodynamics in heavy-ion collisions, building on the Bjorken (1983) model of longitudinal boost-invariant expansion and on earlier work on transverse expansion by von Gersdorff, McLerran, Kataja, and Ruuskanen (1986) and by Blaizot and Ollitrault (1987). It also builds on the numerical RHLLE scheme developed by Rischke, Bernard, and Maruhn (1995) and on studies of deflagration shocks in heavy-ion collisions by Van Hove (1983) and by Gyulassy, Kajantie, Kurki-Suonio, and McLerran (1984). The paper enables future studies of how the three-volume expansion rate affects freeze-out properties, light nucleus coalescence, and the decoupling of different hadron species, and suggests that varying the initial transverse size in experiments could probe these effects.
