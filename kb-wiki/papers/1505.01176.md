# Calculating TMDs of a Large Nucleus: Quasi-Classical Approximation and Quantum Evolution

> We set up a formalism for calculating transverse-momentum-dependent parton distribution functions (TMDs) using the tools of saturation physics. By generalizing the quasi-classical Glauber-Gribov-Mueller/McLerran-Venugopalan approximation to allow for the possibility of spin-orbit coupling, we show how any TMD can be calculated in the saturation framework. This can also be applied to the TMDs of a proton by modeling it as a large "nucleus." To illustrate our technique, we calculate the quark TMDs of an unpolarized nucleus at large-x: the unpolarized quark distribution and the quark Boer-Mulders distribution. We observe that spin-orbit coupling leads to mixing between different TMDs of the nucleus and of the nucleons. We then consider the evolution of TMDs: at large-x, in the double-logarithmic approximation, we obtain the Sudakov form factor. At small-x the evolution of unpolarized-target quark TMDs is governed by BK/JIMWLK evolution, while the small-x evolution of polarized-target quark TMDs appears to be dominated by the QCD Reggeon.

## Key claim

The authors set up a formalism for calculating transverse-momentum-dependent parton distribution functions (TMDs) of a large nucleus using saturation physics, showing that any TMD can be calculated in the quasi-classical Glauber-Gribov-Mueller/McLerran-Venugopalan approximation generalized to include spin-orbit coupling, with the caveat that the results apply specifically to the regime where the atomic number A is large (A ≫ 1) and the resummation parameter α_s² A^{1/3} is of order 1.

## Method

The authors generalize the quasi-classical Glauber-Gribov-Mueller/McLerran-Venugopalan approximation to include spin-orbit coupling, factorizing the nuclear TMD quark correlator into a convolution of three factors: the nuclear Wigner distribution describing nucleon positions and momenta in the nucleus, the TMD correlator of an individual nucleon, and a semi-infinite dipole scattering amplitude describing multiple rescattering of the gauge link on spectator nucleons. They evaluate the symmetric part of the dipole scattering amplitude in the quasi-classical GGM/MV multiple scattering approximation, obtaining an exponential form involving the saturation scale Q_s. For TMD evolution at large-x, they work in the double-logarithmic approximation resumming powers of α_s ln²(Q²/k_T²), while at small-x they employ the Balitsky-Kovchegov/JIMWLK evolution for unpolarized targets and QCD Reggeon evolution for polarized targets.

## Key numbers

- The resummation parameter for the heavy nucleus is α_s² A^{1/3} ∼ O(1), where A is the atomic number
- The symmetric dipole scattering amplitude S_{xy}[∞⁻, b⁻] = exp[-(1/4)|x-y|_T² Q_s²(|(x+y)/2|_T) ((R⁻(|(x+y)/2|_T) - b⁻)/(2R⁻(|(x+y)/2|_T))) ln(1/(|x-y|_T Λ))], where Q_s is the saturation scale, R⁻ is the longitudinal radius, and Λ is an infrared regulator
- The Sivers function from the orbital angular momentum channel dominates for k_T ≲ Q_s/√(α_s), where Q_s is the saturation scale of the nucleus
- The large-x evolution in the double-logarithmic approximation resums powers of α_s ln²(Q²/k_T²) to obtain the Sudakov form factor
- The small-x evolution for unpolarized-target quark TMDs resums powers of α_s ln(1/x) via BK/JIMWLK evolution

## Context

This work extends the approach of Kovchegov (2013) for calculating the Sivers function of a large nucleus to the calculation of any TMD in the quasi-classical approximation, and it connects the saturation physics framework (Gribov, 1984; Iancu, 2003; Weigert, 2005; Jalilian-Marian, 2005; Gelis, 2010; Albacete, 2014) to the TMD formalism of Collins, Soper, and Sterman (1989). The results enable the construction of initial conditions for Collins-Soper-Sterman evolution of TMDs at large-x and provide evolution equations at small-x, which can be used for constructing a global TMD fit. The formalism reveals mixing between different TMDs of the nucleus and nucleons, for example, the nuclear Sivers function is a linear superposition of the nucleon Sivers function and the unpolarized quark distribution, while the nuclear Boer-Mulders function receives contributions from the nucleon Boer-Mulders function, transversity, and pretzelosity.
