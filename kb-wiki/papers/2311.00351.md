# Relativistic second-order dissipative and anisotropic fluid dynamics in the relaxation-time approximation for an ideal gas of massive particles

> In this paper, we study all transport coefficients of second-order dissipative fluid dynamics derived by V. E. Ambrus et al. [Phys. Rev. D 106, 076005 (2022)] from the relativistic Boltzmann equation in the relaxation-time approximation for the collision integral. These transport coefficients are computed for a classical ideal gas of massive particles, with and without taking into account the conservation of intrinsic quantum numbers. Through rigorous comparison between kinetic theory, second-order dissipative fluid dynamics, and leading-order anisotropic fluid dynamics for a (0+1)--dimensional boost-invariant flow scenario, we show that both fluid-dynamical theories describe the early far-from-equilibrium stage of the expansion reasonably well.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that both second-order dissipative fluid dynamics and leading-order anisotropic fluid dynamics describe the early far-from-equilibrium stage of a (0+1)-dimensional boost-invariant expansion reasonably well when compared to the exact solution of the Boltzmann equation in the relaxation-time approximation, but only when the transport coefficients are computed with the basis-free approximation and include the effects of particle-number conservation.

## Method

The authors compute all first- and second-order transport coefficients for relativistic second-order dissipative fluid dynamics from the relativistic Boltzmann equation using the Anderson-Witting relaxation-time approximation for the collision integral, applying both the standard DNMR moment method and the basis-free approximation of Ambrus et al. (2022). These coefficients are evaluated for a classical ideal gas of massive particles, both with and without explicit conservation of particle number. The resulting fluid-dynamical equations are then solved numerically for a (0+1)-dimensional boost-invariant flow and compared to an exact solution of the Boltzmann equation under the same conditions.

## Key numbers

* Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio: $\eta/s = \tau_R T/5$ for the massless limit, with the relaxation time $\tau_R$ as the single free parameter.
* Bulk viscosity coefficient: $\zeta = \tau_R (m_0^2/3) \alpha^{(0)}_0$, where $\alpha^{(0)}_0$ is given by Eq. (alpha0) and depends on thermodynamic integrals $J_{nq}$ and $G_{nm}$.
* Particle diffusion coefficient: $\kappa = \tau_V \alpha^{(1)}_0$, with $\alpha^{(1)}_0 = J_{1,1} - J_{2,1}/h$ where $h = (e+P)/n$ is the enthalpy per particle.
* Relaxation times: $\tau_\Pi = \tau_V = \tau_\pi = \tau_R$ in the relaxation-time approximation, all equal to the model parameter.

## Context

This work extends the transport coefficient calculations of Ambrus et al. (2022) by computing all second-order coefficients for a massive ideal gas, including bulk-shear coupling terms that were omitted in earlier studies such as Denicol et al. (2014) which only computed second-order coefficients of bulk viscous pressure without particle-number conservation effects. The results enable more accurate fluid-dynamical simulations of heavy-ion collisions and neutron star mergers by providing the complete set of transport coefficients for a massive gas in both the basis-free and DNMR approximations, and by demonstrating that anisotropic fluid dynamics can describe far-from-equilibrium evolution comparably to second-order dissipative fluid dynamics.
