# Solar neutrino physics on the beginning of 2017

> This writeup is a review of current hot topics on solar neutrinos. It is based on a talk at the conference "Neutrinos: the quest for a new physics scale", held at the CERN on March 2017, where the Organizers entrusted me with a discussion of the provocative question "whether solar neutrino physics is over". Rather than providing a straight (negative) answer, in view of an audience consisting mostly of colleagues working in theoretical particle physics, I deemed it more useful providing a description of what is the current activity of the physicists working in solar neutrinos, leaving the listener free of forming his/her own opinion apropos.

# Solar neutrino physics on the beginning of 2017

## Key claim

Solar neutrino physics is not over; rather, the field is in a healthy state with recent experimental confirmation of the basic understanding of how the Sun functions (via Borexino's measurement of PP neutrinos in 2014), but several open questions remain, including the unresolved metallicity problem in Standard Solar Models and the yet-unobserved CNO neutrino signal.

## Method

This is a review paper based on a talk at the CERN conference "Neutrinos: the quest for a new physics scale" (March 2017). The author surveys the current status of solar neutrino physics by discussing the Standard Solar Model (SSM), the nuclear reaction chains (PP chain and CNO cycle), the experimental capabilities of past and present detectors (Homestake, GALLEX/GNO, SAGE, Kamiokande, Super-Kamiokande, SNO, and Borexino), and the particle physics implications including the Mikheyev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein (MSW) matter effect. The paper also examines tensions between solar neutrino data and KamLAND reactor antineutrino data regarding the mass-squared difference Δm², and discusses speculative topics such as sterile neutrinos, neutrino decay, and non-standard interactions.

## Key numbers

- PP neutrino flux measurement by Borexino: obtained in 2014, providing experimental basis for understanding solar energy generation
- Borexino energy threshold: approximately 150 keV for electron kinetic energy
- Borexino detector mass: 278 tonnes of pseudocumene (C₉H₁₂)
- Solar neutrino mixing angle: θ₁₂ ≈ 33°
- Best-fit Δm² from KamLAND: 7.37 × 10⁻⁵ eV²
- Solar neutrino data alone indicate Δm² ≈ 4.9 × 10⁻⁵ eV² (if KamLAND results are dropped from global fit)
- Preliminary CNO neutrino flux from Borexino PhD thesis (December 2016): (5.2 ± 1.8_stat) × 10⁸ cm⁻²s⁻¹ (with a misprint noted: 10⁹ should be 10⁸)
- pep neutrino flux from same analysis: (1.31 ± 0.35_stat) × 10⁸ cm⁻²s⁻¹
- CNO cycle contributes approximately 1% of solar luminosity
- PP, pep, and Beryllium neutrinos constitute 99.98% of solar neutrino flux; Boron neutrinos are only 0.02%
- Super-Kamiokande measures a day-night regeneration effect with central value twice the expected size (weak significance)

## Context

This review extends the tradition of Bahcall (1989) and Raffelt (1996) as foundational texts in solar neutrino physics, while updating the discussion to include the post-Nobel prize era (Super-Kamiokande and SNO awarded in 2015). The paper addresses the unresolved metallicity problem first highlighted by Bahcall (2004), where helioseismology measurements contradict SSM predictions based on heavy element abundances. The most recent SSM by Vinyoles (2016) is cited as assessing the model's shortcomings. The paper enables future work on CNO neutrino detection by Borexino (which began thermal insulation in September 2015 to control radioactive backgrounds), and calls for updated sterile neutrino analyses following the framework of Cirelli (2005). The tension between solar neutrino data and KamLAND results on Δm² is presented as an open question that could motivate further investigation of CPT violation or simply reflect statistical fluctuations.
