# Event-by-event mean p_T fluctuations in pp and Pb-Pb collisions at the LHC

> The ALICE detector at the LHC is used to study the properties of the Quark-Gluon Plasma produced in heavy-ion collisions. As a reference measurement, also the analysis of proton-proton (pp) collisions is very important. In the study presented here, event-by-event fluctuations of the mean transverse momentum are analysed in pp collisions at sqrt(s) = 0.9, 2.76 and 7 TeV, and Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV as a function of the charged-particle multiplicity. In both systems, dynamical fluctuations beyond the statistical expectation are observed. In pp collisions, no significant dependence on collision energy is found, even in comparison to inclusive results at much lower collision energies. Likewise, central A-A collisions show only little dependence on collision energy. The multiplicity dependence observed in peripheral Pb-Pb data is in agreement with that in pp collisions. Going to more central Pb-Pb collisions, a clear deviation from this trend is found, reaching a significant reduction of the fluctuations in most central collisions. Comparisons to Monte Carlo event generators show good agreement in pp, but rather large differences in Pb-Pb collisions.

## Key claim

Event-by-event mean transverse momentum fluctuations in both pp and Pb-Pb collisions show significant dynamical contributions beyond statistical expectations, but the paper's central finding is that the multiplicity-dependent trend in peripheral Pb-Pb collisions agrees with an extrapolation from pp data, while central Pb-Pb collisions exhibit a clear additional reduction of fluctuations that cannot be explained by simple superposition of independent nucleon-nucleon collisions.

## Method

The analysis uses charged-particle tracks reconstructed with the ALICE Time Projection Chamber (TPC) in the pseudorapidity range |η|<0.8 and transverse momentum range 0.15 < pT < 2 GeV/c. The dynamical component of event-by-event mean pT fluctuations is isolated using the two-particle correlator Cm, defined as the mean covariance of all particle pairs within the same event relative to the inclusive mean pT in each multiplicity class. The results are presented as the dimensionless ratio √Cm / ⟨pT⟩m, which quantifies fluctuation strength relative to the average transverse momentum.

## Key numbers

- In pp collisions, inclusive relative dynamical fluctuations are about 12% of ⟨pT⟩, independent of collision energy at √s = 0.9, 2.76, and 7 TeV
- In pp collisions, the relative fluctuation decreases from 12-14% at low multiplicities to about 5% at the highest multiplicities
- A power-law fit to pp data at √s = 2.76 TeV in the interval 5 < dNch/dη < 25 yields exponent b = -0.405 ± 0.002 (stat.) ± 0.036 (syst.)
- A power-law fit to pp data at √s = 7 TeV in the interval 5 < dNch/dη < 30 yields exponent b = -0.431 ± 0.001 (stat.) ± 0.021 (syst.)
- In central Pb-Pb collisions at √sNN = 2.76 TeV, the fluctuation scale is about 1% of ⟨pT⟩, roughly one order of magnitude smaller than in inclusive pp collisions
- A power-law fit to HIJING simulation in Pb-Pb for 30 < dNch/dη < 1500 yields exponent b = -0.499 ± 0.003 (stat.) ± 0.005 (syst.), consistent with the simple superposition expectation of b = -0.5
- A power-law fit to ALICE Pb-Pb data as a function of Npart in the interval 10 < Npart < 40 yields exponent b = -0.472 ± 0.007 (stat.) ± 0.037 (syst.)
- The analysis uses 19×10^6 Pb-Pb events at √sNN = 2.76 TeV, and 6.9×10^6, 66×10^6, and 290×10^6 pp events at √s = 0.9, 2.76, and 7 TeV respectively

## Context

This work extends previous measurements of mean pT fluctuations by the CERES experiment at SPS and STAR at RHIC to LHC energies, finding that central heavy-ion collision results show little collision energy dependence when compared to those earlier experiments. The study establishes pp collisions as a model-independent baseline for heavy-ion investigations, showing that peripheral Pb-Pb data follow the same multiplicity-dependent trend as pp collisions, while central collisions deviate significantly. The comparison to Monte Carlo generators reveals that HIJING fails to describe Pb-Pb data, while AMPT simulations including collective effects show qualitative but not quantitative agreement, pointing to the importance of including collective phenomena such as radial flow and initial-state fluctuations in future models.
