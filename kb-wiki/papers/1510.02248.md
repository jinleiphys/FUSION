# Is the isoscalar monopole resonance of the $α$-particle a collective mode?

> In this contribution we review and clarify the arguments which might allow the interpretation of the isoscalar monopole resonance of $^4$He as a collective breathing mode.

## Key claim

The isoscalar monopole resonance of $^4$He fulfills two criteria generally accepted as indications of collective behavior (a breathing mode), but the authors note that observing the system with a large wavelength likely reveals the dynamics of all nucleons together, and they hedge that the degree of collectivity is measured by the ratio of resonance area to the $m_0$ moment, which reaches 0.53 (just above the 50% threshold considered indicative of strong collectivity).

## Method

The authors used the Lorentz Integral Transform (LIT) method with the Effective Interaction Hyperspherical Harmonics (EIHH) expansion to calculate the isoscalar monopole strength distributions $S_M(q,\omega)$ for $^4$He at several fixed momentum transfers. They employed the chiral effective potential at N3LO for two-body and N2LO for three-body contributions, and separated the resonance contribution from the background to compute sum-rule ratios and transition densities.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of the resonance area to the $m_0$ moment is 0.53, exceeding the 50% threshold generally considered an indication of strong collectivity.
- The ratio to the $m_1$ moment is 34%, much smaller than the $m_0$ ratio.
- The root mean square radius of $^4$He from the potential used is 1.46 fm, which corresponds almost exactly to the point where the transition density crosses zero.
- The spectrum was calculated at momentum transfer $q = 0.25$ fm$^{-1}$, where the isoscalar monopole spectrum is dominated by a single resonant bump close to threshold.

## Context

This work extends the question of collective behavior from heavier nuclei, where isoscalar giant monopole resonances (GMRs) are interpreted as breathing modes and used to extract nuclear matter compressibility, to the few-body system $^4$He. It builds on prior calculations by Bacca et al. (2013) of the transition form factor to the $0^+$ excitation and on the observation by Bacca and Marchisio (2002) of two prominent peaks in the dipole photonuclear cross section of $^6$He suggesting collective interpretations. The results enable future work evaluating $m_{-1}$ in terms of $m_3$ to further test the breathing-mode interpretation, as was done by Lipparini and Orlandini (1977) for other systems.
