# An Amended Formula for the Decay of Radioactive Material for Cosmic Times

> An amended formula for the decay of radioactive material is presented. It is a modification of the standard exponential formula. The new formula applies for long cosmic times that are comparable to the Hubble time. It reduces to the standard formula for short times. It is shown that the material decays faster than expected. The application of the new formula to direct measurements of the age of the Universe and its implications is briefly discussed.

# An Amended Formula for the Decay of Radioactive Material for Cosmic Times

## Key claim
The standard exponential decay law is modified for times comparable to the Hubble time by introducing a cosmic-time addition formula, resulting in faster decay than the standard exponential predicts. The amendment applies only when the half-life of the material is comparable to the Hubble time and measurements are made over astronomical timescales.

## Method
The authors replace the standard differential decay law dN/dt = -N/T with a modified form dN/dt' = -N/T', where the cosmic time interval dt' is related to the standard time interval dt by dt' = dt (1 - t²/τ²), with τ being the Hubble time in the limit of zero gravity. This transformation follows from a relativistic addition formula for cosmic times. The resulting differential equation is solved to obtain an amended exponential decay law, and the constant T' is determined in terms of the standard half-life T by requiring consistency at t = T.

## Key numbers
- Thorium has a half-life of 14.1 billion years, compared to the estimated 13 billion years age of the Universe.
- The amended decay formula is N(t) = N₀ exp[ -t α(t) / T ], where α(t) = (1 - t²/3τ²) / (1 - T²/3τ²) and α(t) ≥ 1 for t ≤ T.
- The modified half-life constant T' is given by T' = T (1 - T²/3τ²), so T' < T.

## Context
This work extends Carmeli's cosmological special relativity framework (Carmeli, 1995, 1996, 1997) to radioactive decay, proposing a test for the age of the Universe. The authors note that redshift measurements and stellar evolution calculations yield incompatible ages for the Universe, citing Freedman et al. (1994), Sandage (1993), and others. The amended decay formula could provide a direct measurement of cosmic time by comparing decay rates of long-lived isotopes like Thorium on Earth and in stars, potentially resolving the discrepancy between the two age-determination methods.
