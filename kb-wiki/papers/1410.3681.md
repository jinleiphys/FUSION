# Nuclear breathing mode in neutron-rich Nickel isotopes: sensitivity to the symmetry energy and the role of the continuum

> In this new era of radioactive beam facilities, the discovery of novel modes of excitation in nuclei far away from stability represents an area of intense research activity. In addition, these modes of excitation appear to be sensitive to the uncertain density dependence of the symmetry energy. We study the emergence, evolution, and nature of both the soft and giant isoscalar monopole modes as a function of neutron excess in three unstable Nickel isotopes: 56Ni, 68Ni, and 78Ni. The distribution of isoscalar monopole strength is computed in a relativistic random-phase approximation using several accurately calibrated effective interactions. In particular, a non-spectral Green's function approach is adopted that allows for an exact treatment of the continuum without any reliance on discretization. The discretization of the continuum is neither required nor admitted. In the case of 56Ni, the lack of low-energy strength results in a direct correlation between the centroid energy of the giant monopole resonance and the incompressibility coefficient of symmetric nuclear matter. In contrast, the large neutron excess in both 68Ni and 78Ni generates a significant, yet relatively featureless, amount of low-energy strength that is driven by transitions into the continuum. Moreover, the evolution of monopole strength with neutron excess displays sensitivity to the density dependence of the symmetry energy. Our results suggest that future measurements of the distribution of isoscalar monopole strength at radioactive beam facilities using a very long chain of both stable and unstable isotopes could place important constraints on the equation of state of neutron-rich matter and ultimately on the properties of neutron stars. However, given the nature of the low-energy monopole excitations, a proper treatment of the continuum is essential.

## Key claim

The low-energy isoscalar monopole strength observed in neutron-rich Nickel isotopes (68Ni and 78Ni) is not a novel soft monopole mode with discrete peaks, but rather a relatively featureless continuum driven by transitions into the continuum; the appearance of discrete low-energy peaks in earlier calculations was likely an artifact of discretizing the continuum.

## Method

The distribution of isoscalar monopole strength was computed using a relativistic random-phase approximation (RPA) with a non-spectral Green's function approach that treats the continuum exactly, without any discretization. Three accurately calibrated relativistic mean-field effective interactions were used: NL3, FSUGold, and IUFSU. The calculations were performed for three unstable Nickel isotopes: 56Ni, 68Ni, and 78Ni.

## Key numbers

- For 56Ni (neutron-proton asymmetry α=0), the incompressibility coefficients predicted by the three models are: NL3 = 271.5 MeV, FSUGold = 230.0 MeV, IUFSU = 231.3 MeV
- For 78Ni (α=0.28), the incompressibility coefficients converge to: NL3 = 215.9 MeV, FSUGold = 208.0 MeV, IUFSU = 215.8 MeV
- The slope of the symmetry energy L varies significantly among models: NL3 = 118.2 MeV, FSUGold = 60.5 MeV, IUFSU = 47.2 MeV
- The neutron-skin thickness of 78Ni ranges from 0.292 fm (IUFSU) to 0.416 fm (NL3), a difference of about 0.13 fm
- The neutron-proton asymmetries are α=0.18 for 68Ni and α=0.28 for 78Ni

## Context

This work extends the study of isoscalar monopole strength to unstable neutron-rich nuclei, following the measurement of the monopole response of 68Ni by Vandebrouck (2014). It directly contradicts earlier RPA predictions by Capelli (2009) and Khan (2011) that suggested discrete low-energy monopole peaks in neutron-rich Ni isotopes, supporting instead the conclusions of Hamamoto and Sagawa (2014) that such peaks are artifacts of continuum discretization. The results suggest that future measurements of isoscalar monopole strength across a long chain of Nickel isotopes at radioactive beam facilities could constrain the density dependence of the symmetry energy and ultimately the equation of state of neutron-rich matter relevant to neutron stars.
