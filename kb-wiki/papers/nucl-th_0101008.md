# Relativistic and non-relativistic studies of nuclear matter

> Recently we showed that while the tensor force plays an important role in nuclear matter saturation in non-relativistic studies, it does not do so in relativistic studies. The reason behind this is the role of $M^*$, the sum of nucleon mass and its attractive self-energy in nuclear matter. Yet nonrelativistic calculations at a certain level of approximation are far less difficult than comparative relativistic calculation. Naturally the question arises if one can modify a nonrelativistic method, say, the lowest order Brueckner theory (LOBT), to reproduce approximately the results of a relativistic calculation. While a many body effect, the role of $M^*$ is intrinsically relativistic. It cannot be simulated by adding multi-body forces in a nonrelativistic calculation. Instead, we examine if adding a set of recipes to LOBT can be useful for the purpose. We point out that the differences in the results arise principally from two reasons - first, the role of $M^*$ and second, the disappearance in a relativistic treatment of the gap in the hole and particle energy spectra, present in LOBT. In this paper we show that LOBT, modified by {\it recipes} to remove these two reasons, generates results quite close to those of Dirac-Brueckner theory.

## Key claim

The authors show that a non-relativistic lowest-order Brueckner theory (LOBT) can be modified with two ad-hoc "recipes", removal of the gap in the single-particle spectrum at the Fermi surface and introduction of an effective nucleon mass \(M^*\), to reproduce results close to those of relativistic Dirac-Brueckner theory, but they stress that these are recipes, not a fundamental theory, and that the \(M^*\) effect cannot be simulated by adding multi-body forces in a non-relativistic calculation.

## Method

The authors start from the non-relativistic LOBT Bethe-Goldstone equation and modify it by (1) introducing a parametrized effective mass \(M^* = M(1 - \alpha \rho/\rho_0)\) by hand, and (2) removing the gap in the single-particle spectrum at the Fermi surface by setting the constant \(A\) in the hole energy to enforce continuity. They compare two variants: a non-selfconsistent calculation (where the hole spectrum is computed once with \(m^*=M\) and then stopped) and a self-consistent calculation (where the hole spectrum is iterated to convergence), both with no gap. The Bonn-C potential is used, with the \(\sigma_1\) coupling constant varied between 6.5 and 9.0.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear matter density: \(\rho_0 = 0.17\ \text{fm}^{-3}\).
- The parametrization used for the effective mass: \(M^*/M = 1 - \alpha\,\rho/\rho_0\), with \(\alpha\) varied; the authors find that their recipes prefer \(\alpha \sim 0.15\), while the relativistic Dirac-Brueckner calculation of Amorim and Tjon finds \(\alpha \simeq 0.3\).
- The Bonn-C potential's original \(\sigma_1\) coupling constant is \(g_{\sigma_1} = 8.63\); the authors vary it between 6.5 and 9.0 to generate the Coester-line curves.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier finding (Banerjee and Tjon, 2001) that the tensor force plays a reduced role in relativistic nuclear matter saturation compared to non-relativistic treatments, due to the many-body effect of \(M^*\). It directly compares with the relativistic Dirac-Brueckner calculations of Amorim and Tjon (1998) and with the non-relativistic LOBT results of Machleidt (1989). The paper enables a practical bridge between non-relativistic and relativistic many-body approaches, but concludes that a truly satisfactory resolution requires a relativistic treatment because the \(M^*\) effect is intrinsically relativistic.
