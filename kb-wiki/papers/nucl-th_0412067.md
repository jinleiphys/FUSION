# Dynamics and thermodynamics of fragment emission from excited sources

> In this paper we study the process of fragmentation of highly excited Lennard-Jones drops by introducing the concept of emitted fragments (clusters recognizable in configuration space which live more than a minimum lifetime). We focus on the dynamics and thermodynamics of the emitted sources, and show, among other things, that this kind of process can not be cast into a sequential or simultaneous one, and how a local equilibrium scenario comes up, allowing us to define and explore a local temperature, which turns out to be a strongly time-dependent quantity.

## Key claim

The fragmentation of highly excited Lennard-Jones drops cannot be classified as purely sequential or purely simultaneous; instead, the process is a mixture of both, and the local temperature of the emitting source is strongly time-dependent, meaning the emission process cannot be treated as isothermal.

## Method

The authors performed classical molecular dynamics simulations of 147 particles interacting via a truncated and shifted Lennard-Jones potential, with a cutoff radius of \(3\sigma\). They identified emitted fragments in configuration space using the Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) algorithm, requiring a fragment to live longer than \(5t_0\) (the inverse of the natural frequency) to be considered stable. The local temperature of the biggest source was defined as the velocity fluctuations around its mean radial expansion, and the local equilibrium hypothesis was tested by comparing radial and transversal temperatures and by fitting the velocity distribution to a Maxwellian.

## Key numbers

- System: 147 Lennard-Jones particles; energies per particle studied: \(-2.0\epsilon\), \(-1.0\epsilon\), \(-0.5\epsilon\), \(-0.2\epsilon\), \(0.0\epsilon\), \(0.2\epsilon\), \(0.5\epsilon\), \(1.0\epsilon\), and \(2.0\epsilon\).
- For each energy, 1000 events were calculated; integration time step \(0.01 t_0\), total simulation time \(250 t_0\).
- At energy \(E=2.0\epsilon\), the local temperature of the biggest source at time of fragment formation was \(T_{local}=0.587\pm0.062\epsilon\).
- At energy \(E=0.0\epsilon\), the local temperature was \(T_{local}=0.619\pm0.077\epsilon\); at \(E=-2.0\epsilon\), \(T_{local}=0.627\pm0.043\epsilon\).
- The Pearson \(\chi^2\) significance \(S\) for the Maxwellian fit of the velocity distribution was \(0.86\) at \(E=-2.0\epsilon\), \(0.82\) at \(E=0.0\epsilon\), and \(0.78\) at \(E=2.0\epsilon\).
- Massive emission events (fragment mass at least 30% of the biggest source mass) occurred at any time up to \(t\sim100 t_0\); using the more restrictive criterion (fragment of at least 40 particles), no massive events occurred after \(t\sim100 t_0\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies of fragment formation in Lennard-Jones drops (Ison and Dorso, 2004) by focusing on the dynamics and thermodynamics of the emitting source rather than just fragment recognition. It contrasts with statistical models that assume a fixed freeze-out volume and thermal equilibrium (Randrup and Koonin, 1981; Fai and Randrup, 1982) and with the binomial emission model of Moretto and collaborators (1995, 1996), which the authors find fits their multiplicity distributions remarkably well despite the non-equilibrium nature of the process. The results enable the development of more realistic models of nuclear multifragmentation that incorporate time-dependent temperatures and mixed sequential/simultaneous emission.
