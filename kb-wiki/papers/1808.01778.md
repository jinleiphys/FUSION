# Magnetic field distribution in magnetars

> Using an axisymmetric numerical code, we perform an extensive study of the magnetic field configurations in non-rotating neutron stars, varying the mass, magnetic field strength and the equation of state. We find that the monopolar (spherically symmetric) part of the norm of the magnetic field can be described by a single profile, that we fit by a simple eighth-order polynomial, as a function of the star's radius. This new generic profile applies remarkably well to all magnetized neutron star configurations built on hadronic equations of state. We then apply this profile to build magnetized neutron stars in spherical symmetry, using a modified Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) system of equations. This new formalism produces slightly better results in terms of mass-radius diagrams than previous attempts to add magnetic terms to these equations. However, we show that such approaches are less accurate than usual, non-magnetized TOV models, and that consistent models must depart from spherical symmetry. Thus, our `universal' magnetic field profile is intended to serve as a tool for nuclear physicists to obtain estimates of magnetic field inside neutron stars, as a function of radial depth, in order to deduce its influence on composition and related properties. It possesses the advantage of being based on magnetic field distributions from realistic self-consistent computations, which are solutions of Maxwell's equations.

# Magnetic field distribution in magnetars

## Key claim
The monopolar (spherically symmetric) part of the norm of the magnetic field in non-rotating neutron stars follows a universal radial profile that can be described by a simple eighth-order polynomial, but this profile is intended only as an approximate tool for nuclear physicists to estimate interior field strengths, because consistent magnetized neutron star models must depart from spherical symmetry and the spherically symmetric TOV approach is less accurate than standard non-magnetized TOV models.

## Method
The authors used an axisymmetric numerical code (based on the LORENE library) to solve coupled Einstein-Maxwell equations for non-rotating neutron stars with purely poloidal magnetic fields, varying the gravitational mass, central magnetic field strength, and six different hadronic equations of state (including Skyrme, relativistic mean-field, Brueckner-Hartree-Fock, and hyperonic models). They extracted the monopolar component of the magnetic field norm via a multipolar expansion in spherical harmonics and fitted it with a polynomial function of the Schwarzschild coordinate radius normalized to the star's mean radius. They then applied this profile to a modified Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) system that includes magnetic energy density and a fitted Lorentz force term, comparing the resulting mass-radius relations against full axisymmetric solutions.

## Key numbers
- The universal monopolar profile is given by: b_0(x) = b_c × (1 - 1.6x^2 - x^4 + 4.2x^6 - 2.4x^8), where x = r̄/r_mean and b_c is the central magnetic field norm.
- The Lorentz force term in the modified TOV system is parameterized as: L(r̄) = 10^(-41) × b_c^2 × (-3.8x + 8.1x^3 - 1.6x^5 - 2.3x^7), with b_c in Gauss.
- The maximum gravitational masses for the EoS models range from 1.99 M⊙ (SFHoY) to 2.42 M⊙ (HS(DD2)), with corresponding radii at 1.4 M⊙ ranging from 11.8 km (SLy230a) to 14.5 km (STOS).
- The numerical accuracy of axisymmetric solutions was verified via the relativistic virial theorem, with relative errors always below 10^(-4).
- The study considered central magnetic fields from 10^15 G to 10^18 G, and gravitational masses of 1.6 M⊙ and 2.0 M⊙.

## Context
This work extends the self-consistent axisymmetric magnetized neutron star models of Chatterjee (2015) and Bocquet (1995) by extracting a universal magnetic field profile applicable to hadronic equations of state. It directly addresses the long-standing problem that ad hoc magnetic field profiles used in spherically symmetric calculations, such as those by Bandyopadhyay (1997) (density-dependent exponential form), Lopes and Menezes (2015) (energy-density-dependent form), and Dexheimer (2012) (chemical-potential-dependent form), do not satisfy Maxwell's equations and are physically inconsistent, as pointed out by Menezes and Alloy (2016). The universal profile enables nuclear physicists to obtain order-of-magnitude estimates of interior magnetic field strengths for studying effects on dense matter composition and transport properties, but the authors emphasize that fully consistent models require axisymmetric calculations rather than spherical approximations.
