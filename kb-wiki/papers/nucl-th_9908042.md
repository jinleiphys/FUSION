# The surface diffuseness and the spin-orbital splitting in relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov theory

> The Relativistic Continuum Hartree Bogoliubov theory (RCHB), which is the extension of the Relativistic Mean Field and the Bogoliubov transformation in the coordinate representation, has been used to study tin isotopes. The pairing correlation is taken into account by a density-dependent force of zero range. RCHB is used to describe the even-even tin isotopes all the way from the proton drip line to the neutron drip line. The contribution of the continuum which is important for nuclei near the drip-line has been taken into account. The theoretical $S_{2n}$ as well as the neutron, proton, and matter $rms$ radii are presented and compared with the experimental values where they exist. The change of the potential surface with the neutron number has been investigated. The diffuseness of the potentials in tin isotopes is analyzed through the spin-orbital splitting in order to provide new way to understand the halo phenomena in exotic nuclei. The systematic of the isospin and energy dependence of these results are extracted and analyzed.

# The surface diffuseness and the spin-orbital splitting in relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov theory

## Key claim

The spin-orbital splitting in tin isotopes decreases monotonically from the proton drip line to the neutron drip line for all orbital partners, and this reduction is driven by the increasing surface diffuseness of the nuclear potential rather than by isospin effects in the spin-orbital potential itself. The authors propose that analyzing the surface diffuseness through spin-orbital splitting provides a new way to understand halo phenomena in exotic nuclei, but note that in Sn isotopes the orbital $1i_{13/2}$ near the drip line does not contribute to binding despite the highly diffuse potential, due to its large centrifugal barrier.

## Method

The authors applied the Relativistic Continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory, which extends the Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) approach by incorporating Bogoliubov transformation in coordinate representation to treat pairing correlations and continuum states self-consistently. The pairing interaction was modeled with a density-dependent zero-range force, with its strength fixed by comparison to the Gogny force D1S for $^{150}$Sn. The RCHB equations were solved in a box of size $R = 25$ fm with step size 0.1 fm using the NLSH parameter set, including all levels within 120 MeV of the Fermi level, for even-even tin isotopes from the proton drip line to the neutron drip line.

## Key numbers

- The neutron drip line for tin isotopes is predicted at $^{176}$Sn in the RCHB calculation.
- The box size used for solving the RCHB equations was $R = 25$ fm with a step size of 0.1 fm.
- The cutoff energy for including continuum levels was 120 MeV from the Fermi level.
- The nuclear matter density $\rho_0$ used in the pairing force was 0.152 fm$^{-3}$.
- The depth of the neutron potential in $^{170}$Sn is about -60 MeV, with single particle levels beginning from -55 MeV for $1s_{1/2}$.
- The spin-orbital splitting in ($2p_{3/2}, 2p_{1/2}$) partners in $^{170}$Sn is twice as large as that in ($1p_{3/2}, 1p_{1/2}$) and three times as large as that in ($3p_{3/2}, 3p_{1/2}$).
- The potential $V_V - V_S$ is approximately 700 MeV, making its isospin dependence in the spin-orbital potential negligible.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous RCHB studies of lithium isotopes (Meng 1996) and sodium isotopes (Meng 1997), where halo phenomena and the "Giant Halo" prediction for Zr nuclei were reported. The paper systematically examines the isospin dependence of potential diffuseness and its relation to spin-orbital splitting across the entire tin isotopic chain, contrasting with light nuclei like Na where orbitals with small centrifugal barrier ($2p_{1/2}$, $2p_{3/2}$) gain binding energy from potential diffuseness near the drip line. The results enable future experimental measurements of spin-orbital splitting as an indirect probe of surface diffuseness in exotic nuclei, and provide a microscopic foundation for understanding how shell structure and centrifugal barriers affect halo formation in medium-mass nuclei.
