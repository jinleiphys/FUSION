# One-neutron halo structure of $^{29}$Ne

> We have applied the Gamow shell model to calculate nuclear observables of $^{26-31}$Ne isotopes pertaining to one-neutron halo structure, these nuclei being situated close to neutron drip-line. As both many-body correlations and continuum coupling are taken into account in that approach, halo structure can be analyzed properly. Our calculations provide good descriptions of $^{26-31}$Ne, where asymptotic behavior is crucial for that matter. One-body density, neutron root-mean-square radii of $^{26-31}$Ne, and one-neutron overlap functions of $^{29,31}$Ne have been calculated as well. Our results support the presence of a one-neutron \textit{p}-wave halo in $^{31}$Ne, already pointed out experimentally. A similar situation also occurs in the ground state of $^{29}$Ne, which is mainly a \textit{p}-wave valence neutron coupled to the inner $^{28}$Ne \textit{core}. The $3/2^+$ excited state of $^{29}$Ne, which is dominated by a \textit{d}-wave valence neutron, has also been considered. A larger radius and more extended wave function occur for the ground state of $^{29}$Ne when compared to its $3/2^+$ first excited state. The present results suggest that $^{29}$Ne is a good candidate for one-neutron \textit{p}-wave halo in the medium-mass region.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the ground state of $^{29}$Ne is a good candidate for a one-neutron *p*-wave halo in the medium-mass region, based on Gamow shell model calculations that reproduce the one-neutron separation energy to about 840 keV (consistent with the experimental value of 900 keV) and show extended density distributions and larger neutron root-mean-square radii compared to neighboring isotopes.

## Method

The authors applied the Gamow shell model (GSM) using a Berggren basis that includes bound, resonance, and scattering single-particle states, with $^{24}$O as the inert core. The Hamiltonian employed a Woods-Saxon one-body potential and a pionless effective field theory (EFT) two-body interaction up to next-to-leading order, with parameters optimized to reproduce low-lying states of $^{25,26}$O, $^{25,26}$F, and $^{26-29}$Ne. One-body densities, neutron root-mean-square radii, and one-neutron overlap functions were calculated for $^{26-31}$Ne isotopes, with spectroscopic factors extracted from the overlap functions.

## Key numbers

- One-neutron separation energy ($S_n$) of $^{29}$Ne calculated as about 840 keV, consistent with the experimental value of 900 keV.
- One-neutron separation energy ($S_n$) of $^{31}$Ne calculated as about 350 keV, close to the experimental value of 200 keV.
- Neutron root-mean-square radii of $^{29}$Ne and $^{31}$Ne lie 0.05 to 0.1 fm above the trend line formed by other neon isotopes ($^{26-30}$Ne).
- $p_{3/2}$ spectroscopic factor for $^{29}$Ne obtained as 0.540, matching the experimental value of 0.54(9).
- $p_{3/2}$ spectroscopic factor for $^{31}$Ne obtained as 0.392, consistent with the experimental value of $0.32^{+21}_{-17}$.

## Context

This work extends the study of one-neutron halo nuclei beyond the well-known cases of $^{11}$Be and $^{15,19}$C in the light mass region and the previously established one-neutron *p*-wave halo in $^{31}$Ne (Nakamura et al., 2013). The calculations support the experimental findings of Nakamura et al. (2014) for $^{31}$Ne and provide theoretical evidence that $^{29}$Ne, with its $3/2^-$ ground state dominated by *p*-wave intruder configurations, is a one-neutron halo candidate in the medium-mass region. The results enable future experimental searches for halo structure in $^{29}$Ne and suggest that similar phenomena may occur in other neutron-rich medium-mass nuclei near the drip line.
