# Low-energy limit of the radiative dipole strength in nuclei

> We explain the low-energy anomaly reported in several experimental studies of the radiative dipole strength functions in medium-mass nuclei. These strength functions at very low gamma-energies correspond to the gamma-transitions between very close nuclear excited states in the quasicontinuum. In terms of the thermal mean-field, the low-energy enhancement of the strength functions in highly-excited compound nuclei is explained by nucleonic transitions from the thermally unblocked single-quasiparticle states to the single-(quasi)particle continuum. This result is obtained within the finite-temperature quasiparticle random phase approximation in the coordinate space with exact treatment of the single-particle continuum and exactly eliminated spurious translational mode. The case of radiative dipole strength functions at the nuclear excitation energies typical for the thermal neutron capture is illustrated for 94-Mo and 144-Nd in comparison to available data.

## Key claim

The low-energy enhancement of radiative dipole strength functions in highly-excited compound nuclei is explained by nucleonic transitions from thermally unblocked single-quasiparticle states to the single-(quasi)particle continuum, within a finite-temperature mean-field framework that requires exact treatment of the single-particle continuum and exact elimination of the spurious translational mode.

## Method

The authors employ the finite-temperature quasiparticle random phase approximation in coordinate space (TCQRPA), using Matsubara temperature Green functions with a Woods-Saxon mean-field potential and Landau-Migdal effective nucleon-nucleon interaction. The spurious state from broken translation invariance is eliminated via a "forced consistency" technique generalized to finite temperature. The dipole radiative strength function is computed from the response of the density matrix to an external electromagnetic dipole field, with the temperature parameter determined from the phenomenological relation T = sqrt((E* - delta)/a) using the neutron separation energy as the excitation energy.

## Key numbers

- Temperature parameter intervals for Mo isotopes: 1.26 ≤ T ≤ 1.59 MeV for 94-Mo, 1.15 ≤ T ≤ 1.55 MeV for 96-Mo, 1.02 ≤ T ≤ 1.52 MeV for 98-Mo
- Temperature parameter intervals for Sn isotopes: 1.03 ≤ T ≤ 1.3 MeV for 116-Sn, 1.02 ≤ T ≤ 1.17 MeV for 122-Sn
- The low-energy enhancement is observed at gamma-energies E_gamma ≤ 3-4 MeV
- The finite-temperature strength at low energies shows steps at energies equal to the single-particle state energies closest to the continuum (epsilon_i = epsilon_F + E_i)
- The smearing parameter Delta = 100 keV is used for comparison with experimental data; at Delta = 10 keV the finite-temperature strength is practically saturated

## Context

This work explains the low-energy anomaly (upbend at E_gamma ≤ 3 MeV) observed in experimental studies of radiative dipole strength functions in medium-mass nuclei by Guttormsen (2005), Voinov (2004), and others, which violates the Brink-Axel hypothesis. The results extend the finite-temperature random phase approximation approaches of Ring et al. (1983) and Sommermann (1983) by including exact continuum treatment, and enable microscopic calculations of neutron capture rates for r-process nucleosynthesis that may require revision of Hauser-Feshbach models using phenomenological parameterizations like those of Kadmenskij et al. (1983) and Kopecky and Uhl (1989).
