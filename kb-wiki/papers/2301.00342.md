# Many-body collective neutrino oscillations: recent developments

> Neutrino flavor transformations in core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers represent a complex and unsolved problem that is integral to our understanding of the dynamics and nucleosynthesis in these environments. The high number densities of neutrinos present in these environments can engender various collective effects in neutrino flavor transformations, driven either by neutrino-neutrino coherent scattering, or in some cases, through collisional (incoherent) interactions. An ensemble of neutrinos undergoing coherent scattering among themselves is an interacting quantum many-body system -- as such, there is a tantalising prospect of quantum entanglement developing between the neutrinos, which can leave imprints on their flavor evolution histories. Here, we seek to summarize recent progress that has been made towards understanding this phenomenon.

## Key claim

This review summarizes recent progress on whether quantum entanglement and many-body correlations beyond the mean-field approximation can significantly affect neutrino flavor evolution in dense astrophysical environments, noting that past work gave conflicting predictions about whether such effects vanish in the large-system limit or produce substantial flavor conversion on timescales \(\tau_F \sim \mu^{-1}\log(N)\).

## Method

The authors survey theoretical approaches to the many-body neutrino problem including the Hamiltonian formulation in terms of SU(2) isospin operators, the path integral formulation with coherent states, the BBGKY hierarchy for density matrices, tensor network methods (specifically matrix product states and the time-dependent variational principle), and quantum computing implementations using Trotter-Suzuki decomposition. They also review the single-angle approximation that reduces the geometric complexity to a Gaudin-magnet-like integrable system solvable via Bethe ansatz.

## Key numbers

* Neutrino decoupling energies are of order \(\mathcal{O}(1\text{--}10)\,\mathrm{MeV}\) in core-collapse supernovae and mergers.
* In the neutrino-driven wind phase, the neutrino-sphere radius \(R_\nu \simeq 20\,\mathrm{km}\) and the neutrino-neutrino coupling \(\mu_0 \sim 10^5\,\omega_0\), where \(\omega_0 \sim 10^{-16}\,\mathrm{MeV}\) is the vacuum oscillation frequency scale.
* During the neutronization burst phase, \(R_\nu \gtrsim 50\text{--}60\,\mathrm{km}\) and \(\mu_0 \sim 10^6\,\omega_0\).
* Fast flavor oscillations occur on timescale \(\tau_F \sim \mu^{-1}\log(N)\), while slow oscillations occur on \(\tau_L \sim \mu^{-1}\sqrt{N}\).
* Classical computers can exactly simulate systems of no more than \(\simeq 20\) neutrinos; tensor network methods extend this to hundreds of neutrinos; generalized angular momentum methods for very dense gases reach thousands of neutrinos.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on neutrino-neutrino interactions by Pantaleone (1992) and the mean-field treatments by Sigl and Raffelt (1993) and Qian and Fuller (1994), which proposed the Random Phase Approximation. It addresses conflicting predictions from Bell (2003) and Friedland and Lunardini (2003, 2006) about entanglement buildup, and connects to the integrability results of Pehlivan and Balantekin (2011) and the Bethe-ansatz solutions of Birol and Pehlivan (2018). The work enables future studies using quantum computers and tensor networks to determine whether quantum corrections to mean-field dynamics are significant for supernova and merger nucleosynthesis calculations.
