# A microscopic estimate of the nuclear matter compressibility and symmetry energy in relativistic mean-field models

> The relativistic mean-field plus RPA calculations, based on effective Lagrangians with density-dependent meson-nucleon vertex functions, are employed in a microscopic analysis of the nuclear matter compressibility and symmetry energy. We compute the isoscalar monopole and the isovector dipole response of $^{208}$Pb, as well as the differences between the neutron and proton radii for $^{208}$Pb and several Sn isotopes. The comparison of the calculated excitation energies with the experimental data on the giant monopole resonance in $^{208}$Pb, restricts the nuclear matter compression modulus of structure models based on the relativistic mean-field approximation to $K_{\rm nm}\approx 250 - 270$ MeV. The isovector giant dipole resonance in $^{208}$Pb, and the available data on differences between neutron and proton radii, limit the range of the nuclear matter symmetry energy at saturation (volume asymmetry) to 32 MeV $\leq a_4 \leq$ 36 MeV.

## Key claim

The nuclear matter compression modulus in relativistic mean-field models is restricted to \(K_{\rm nm}\approx 250-270\) MeV, with 250 MeV as the lower bound, contradicting claims that values well above 200 MeV are inconsistent with experiment; this result holds only when effective interactions with density-dependent meson-nucleon couplings are used and when the symmetry energy is constrained by data on neutron radii and the isovector giant dipole resonance.

## Method

The authors performed fully self-consistent relativistic random phase approximation (RRPA) calculations of the isoscalar monopole and isovector dipole response in \({}^{208}\)Pb, using effective Lagrangians with density-dependent meson-nucleon vertex functions. They constructed three families of interactions with compression moduli \(K_{\rm nm}=230\), 250, and 270 MeV, each with five values of the volume asymmetry \(a_4=30\), 32, 34, 36, and 38 MeV, fitted to nuclear matter saturation properties and ground-state properties of ten spherical nuclei. The calculated excitation energies of the giant monopole resonance (GMR) and isovector giant dipole resonance (GDR), as well as differences between neutron and proton radii for \({}^{208}\)Pb and Sn isotopes, were compared with experimental data.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear matter compression modulus restricted to \(K_{\rm nm}\approx 250-270\) MeV, with 250 MeV as the lower limit, from comparison of calculated GMR excitation energies in \({}^{208}\)Pb with the experimental value \(E=14.17\pm 0.28\) MeV.
- Volume asymmetry (symmetry energy at saturation) limited to \(32\ \mathrm{MeV}\leq a_4\leq 36\ \mathrm{MeV}\) from neutron-proton radius differences in \({}^{208}\)Pb (experimental value \(0.20\pm 0.04\) fm from proton scattering data).
- Isovector GDR in \({}^{208}\)Pb constrains volume asymmetry to \(34\ \mathrm{MeV}\leq a_4\leq 36\ \mathrm{MeV}\), compared with experimental GDR centroid \(13.3\pm 0.1\) MeV.
- For \(K_{\rm nm}=230\) MeV, calculated GMR energies are too low for any value of \(a_4\); only \(K_{\rm nm}=270\) MeV reproduces the GMR energy for all \(a_4\) values.
- Binding energies of fitted interactions deviate from experiment by \(\leq 0.2\%\); charge radii deviate by \(\leq 0.3\%\) except for \({}^{40}\)Ca.

## Context

This work extends earlier relativistic mean-field plus RPA studies by Vretenar et al. (1997, 1999, 2000, 2001) and Niksic et al. (2002) that found \(K_{\rm nm}\approx 250-270\) MeV, and directly contradicts the conclusions of Piekarewicz (2001, 2002, 2003) who argued that relativistic models with \(K_{\rm nm}\) well above 200 MeV are inconsistent with experiment and that the discrepancy with non-relativistic models (which yield \(K_{\rm nm}\approx 210-230\) MeV, as in Blaizot et al. (1995) and Farine et al. (1997)) could be resolved by softening the symmetry energy. The authors show that the symmetry energy cannot be lowered sufficiently to allow \(K_{\rm nm}\leq 230\) MeV while still reproducing ground-state properties of finite nuclei and neutron radius data. The results enable future work to use the constrained range of \(K_{\rm nm}\) and \(a_4\) in relativistic models for studies of neutron stars, heavy-ion collisions, and supernovae.
