# Response of strongly coupled fermions on classical and quantum computers

> Studying the response of quantum systems is essential for gaining deeper insights into the fundamental nature of matter and its behavior in diverse physical contexts. Computation of nuclear response is critical for many applications, but its spectroscopically accurate description in medium-heavy nuclei in wide energy ranges remains particularly challenging because of the complex nature of nuclear quantum states in the high-level-density regime. Herein, we push the limits of configuration complexity in the classical computation of the nuclear response and present an algorithm with a quantum benefit for treating complex configurations. The classical computational method of approaching spectroscopic accuracy is implemented for medium-heavy nuclei and pioneered for the dipole response of 120Sn, while the quantum algorithm reaching the exact solution is realized for the Lipkin Hamiltonian to unravel the emergence of collectivity at strong coupling.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a variable configuration complexity approach, implemented both classically and on quantum computers, can achieve spectroscopically accurate nuclear response by systematically incorporating emergent collectivity, but the classical method is computationally heavy for large systems while the quantum algorithm shows a "quantum benefit" because the number of required quantum measurements does not significantly increase with configuration complexity.

## Method

The classical method iteratively solves the Bethe-Salpeter-Dyson equation for the response function, adding two quasiparticles (2q) per iteration: REOM^1 (2q configurations, equivalent to relativistic quasiparticle random phase approximation), REOM^2 (4q or 2q⊗phonon), and REOM^3 (6q or 2q⊗2phonon). The quantum method extends the quantum equation-of-motion (qEOM) algorithm to compute the response function for the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick (LMG) Hamiltonian, using a variational quantum eigensolver to find the ground state and then solving a generalized eigenvalue equation on a classical computer using matrix elements measured on quantum hardware.

## Key numbers

* For 120Sn, the imaginary part of the energy variable was taken as Δ = 200 keV to match experimental resolution.
* For the LMG model with N=8 fermions, the maximal configuration complexity α_m=3 is constrained by mapping the even part of the Hamiltonian onto three qubits.
* For the LMG model at α_m=3, the median error in the strength function is 1.4×10^{-9} and the 90th percentile error is 3.5×10^{-7}.
* The LMG strength function trend holds up to at least interaction strength ṽ ∼ 100.
* IBM Quantum hardware enables reliable computation up to effective coupling strength of 1.0-1.5.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic nuclear field theory (RNFT) approach of Litvinova (2022a, 2023a) by advancing the 6q configuration complexity to heavier nuclei like 120Sn, and it extends the qEOM algorithm of Ollitrault (2020) from energy eigenstates to response functions. The classical results for 120Sn are compared to experimental data from Bassauer (2020) for the giant dipole resonance, while the quantum results use the LMG Hamiltonian of Lipkin (1965) as a testbed. The work enables future studies of nuclear and other strongly coupled many-body systems on quantum computers, with the classical method providing uncertainty quantification and the quantum method offering a path to higher configuration complexity.
