# Shadow-based quantum subspace algorithm for the nuclear shell model

> In recent years, researchers have been exploring the applications of noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) computation in various fields. One important area in which quantum computation can outperform classical computers is the ground state problem of a many-body system, e.g., the nucleus. However, using a quantum computer in the NISQ era to solve a meaningful-scale system remains a challenge. To calculate the ground energy of nuclear systems, we propose a new algorithm that combines classical shadow and subspace diagonalization techniques. Our subspace is composed of matrices, with the basis of the subspace being the classical shadow of the quantum state. We test our algorithm on nuclei described by Cohen-Kurath shell model and USD shell model. We find that the accuracy of the results improves as the number of shots increases, following the Heisenberg scaling.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a quantum subspace algorithm that uses classical shadows of real-time-evolved quantum states to construct a matrix subspace, claiming that the accuracy of the ground-state energy estimate improves with the number of measurement shots following Heisenberg scaling, and that the method avoids the need for Hadamard tests and the truncation of overlap matrices required in standard quantum subspace diagonalization.

## Method

The algorithm first prepares an initial state with finite overlap with the true ground state, then evolves it under the system Hamiltonian for a set of different real times to generate a collection of quantum states. For each evolved state, a classical shadow is constructed via random Clifford measurements and post-processing on a classical computer. These shadows are used to build a subspace of matrices (tensor products of the shadow density matrices), and the Hamiltonian is diagonalized within this subspace by solving a generalized eigenvalue problem \(\tilde{H}^{s}\vec{c} = E\tilde{S}\vec{c}\), where the matrices \(\tilde{H}^{s}\) and \(\tilde{S}\) are computed classically from the shadow representations. The method is tested on nuclei described by the Cohen-Kurath shell model (p-shell, with \(^4\)He core) and the USD shell model (sd-shell, with \(^{16}\)O core).

## Key numbers

* For the nuclei \(^6\)He, \(^6\)Li, \(^7\)Be, \(^8\)Li, \(^{18}\)O, and \(^{18}\)F, the algorithm was tested using the Cohen-Kurath and USD shell model parametrizations.
* The expected deviation of the matrix elements of \(\tilde{H}^{s}\) and \(\tilde{S}\) due to finite shots scales as \(\mathcal{O}(d^3 / M^2)\), where \(d\) is the Hilbert space dimension and \(M\) is the number of measurements per quantum state (Theorem 3).
* The expected variance of the matrix elements scales such that the ratio of exponents \(a/b \leq 1\) in the expression \(\sum_{a,b} c_{a,b} d^a / M^b\) (Theorem 4), indicating that the required number of measurements per shadow grows linearly with the Hilbert space dimension.

## Context

This work extends the line of quantum subspace diagonalization methods (Klymko 2022, Yoshioka 2022) by replacing wavefunction-based subspaces with matrix subspaces built from classical shadows, thereby avoiding the Hadamard test circuits needed for measuring overlap and Hamiltonian matrix elements in standard QSD. It also contrasts with variational quantum eigensolver approaches (Dumitrescu 2018, Romero 2022) that can suffer from local minima and barren plateaus, and with imaginary-time evolution methods (Yeter 2020, Lv 2022) that require deeper circuits. The algorithm enables ground-state calculations for nuclear shell-model systems on NISQ devices with reduced circuit depth and without the need for overlap matrix truncation.
