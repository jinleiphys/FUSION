# The Central Temperature of the Sun can be Measured via the $^7$Be Solar Neutrino Line

> A precise test of the theory of stellar evolution can be performed by measuring the difference in average energy between the neutrino line produced by ${\rm ^7Be}$ electron capture in the solar interior and the corresponding neutrino line produced in a terrestrial laboratory. The high temperatures in the center of the sun broaden the line asymmetrically, FWHM = 1.6~keV, and cause an average energy shift of 1.3~keV. The width of the $^7$Be neutrino line should be taken into account in calculations of vacuum neutrino oscillations.

# The Central Temperature of the Sun can be Measured via the $^7$Be Solar Neutrino Line

## Key claim
The average energy shift of 1.3 keV between solar and laboratory $^7$Be neutrino lines can serve as a direct measurement of the first moment of the central temperature distribution of the sun, provided that a detector with sufficient energy resolution (0.1% to 1.0%) can be developed to measure this shift.

## Method
The author calculated the energy profile of the neutrino line produced by electron capture on $^7$Be in the solar interior by incorporating thermal kinetic energies of continuum electrons and $^7$Be nuclei, Doppler shifts of the $^7$Be ions, the fraction of captures from bound versus continuum orbits, and differences in atomic binding energies between solar and laboratory conditions. The line profile was averaged over the temperature distribution of the Bahcall-Pinsonneault standard solar model with helium diffusion. The absorption cross section for solar $^7$Be neutrinos on a $^7$Li detector was computed including the full energy profile and the 89.7% branching ratio for ground-state to ground-state transitions.

## Key numbers
- Average energy shift between solar and laboratory $^7$Be neutrinos: $\Delta = (1.29 \pm 0.01)$ keV
- Full width at half maximum of the solar $^7$Be neutrino line: 1.6 keV
- Peak neutrino energy in the solar profile: $q_{\rm peak} = 862.27$ keV; laboratory peak: $q_{\rm peak} = 861.84$ keV
- Half-width at half-maximum on low-energy side: 0.56 keV; on high-energy side: 1.07 keV
- Effective temperature of the high-energy exponential tail: $15 \times 10^6$ K
- Central temperature of standard solar models: $T_c = (15.58 \pm 0.08) \times 10^6$ K (range $\pm 0.5\%$)
- Central temperature from heterogeneous set of nine solar models: $T_c = (15.55 \pm 0.15) \times 10^6$ K (range $\pm 1\%$)
- Absorption cross section for solar $^7$Be neutrinos on $^7$Li: $\langle \sigma_{\rm abs} \rangle \simeq 19 \times 10^{-46}$ cm$^2$
- 75% of $^7$Be neutrinos are produced in the region $(0.04 \pm 0.03) M_\odot$
- A cryogenic $^{81}$Br detector with 1% energy resolution and $10^3$ events could measure the central temperature to about 10% accuracy

## Context
This work extends the standard solar model calculations of Bahcall and Ulrich (1988) and Bahcall and Pinsonneault (1992) by predicting a measurable energy shift in the $^7$Be neutrino line that directly probes the central solar temperature. The predicted absorption cross section on $^7$Li is nearly a factor of two larger than earlier estimates by Domogatsky (1969), which neglected differences in electron binding energies and Doppler shifts. The paper enables future experiments such as BOREXINO (Raghavan, 1990) and cryogenic detectors (Alessandrello, 1992) to measure the central temperature of the sun, and it notes that the line width should be included in precise calculations of vacuum neutrino oscillations, where the energy-width phase change ($\approx 10^{-3}$) is an order of magnitude larger than the phase change from averaging over the production region.
