# Quartetting Wave Function Approach to $^{20}$Ne: Shell Model and Local Density Approximation

> We investigate $α$-like correlations in $^{20}$Ne. A quartet of nucleons (different spin/isospin) is moving in a mean field produced by the $^{16}$O core nucleus. Improving the Thomas-Fermi model (local density approach), a shell model is considered for the core nucleus. The effective potential of the $α$-like quartet and the wave function for the center-of-mass (c.o.m.) motion are calculated and compared with other approaches.

# Quartetting Wave Function Approach to $^{20}$Ne: Shell Model and Local Density Approximation

## Key claim

The effective potential for the center-of-mass motion of an $\alpha$-like quartet in $^{20}$Ne remains nearly constant inside the $^{16}$O core nucleus, in contrast to other approaches that predict an increase with decreasing radius; this result holds both in the Thomas-Fermi (local density) approximation and in a more realistic shell-model treatment using Woods-Saxon single-particle states, though the local density approximation produces a sharp kink at the critical radius that would be smeared out in a more complete treatment.

## Method

The quartetting wave function approach decomposes the four-nucleon wave function into a center-of-mass (c.o.m.) part and an intrinsic part, then solves coupled equations for each. For the $^{16}$O core, the mean-field potential is constructed via double folding of the $\alpha$-particle and $^{16}$O density distributions with Coulomb and M3Y-type nucleon-nucleon interactions, while the intrinsic contribution includes the $\alpha$ binding energy shifted by a density-dependent Pauli blocking term. The local density approximation (Thomas-Fermi model) is improved by replacing the harmonic oscillator with Woods-Saxon single-particle states for the core, and the effective c.o.m. potential is reconstructed from the $2s^4$ quartet wave function using the inverse Schrödinger equation.

## Key numbers

- Critical density for dissolution of the $\alpha$ bound state (Mott density): $n_{\rm crit} = 0.02917$ fm$^{-3}$
- Critical radius where $^{16}$O density equals $n_{\rm crit}$: $r_{\rm crit} = 3.302$ fm
- Free $\alpha$ binding energy: $B_\alpha = 28.3$ MeV
- Chemical potential $\mu_4$ from binding energy difference $B_{^{16}{\rm O}} - B_{^{20}{\rm Ne}}$: $-33.0$ MeV
- Energy eigenvalue from Thomas-Fermi c.o.m. Schrödinger equation: $-33.0$ MeV
- Calculated rms point radius of $^{20}$Ne: 2.8644 fm (experimental: 2.87 fm)
- Woods-Saxon $2s$ state eigenvalue: $E_{2s} = -9.162$ MeV
- Quartet energy from shell-model $2s^4$ wave function: $E_{2s^4} = -36.65$ MeV
- Pauli blocking fit coefficients: $4515.9$ MeV fm$^3$, $-100935$ MeV fm$^6$, $1202538$ MeV fm$^9$
- Double-folding nucleon-nucleon interaction parameters: $c = 4650$ MeV, $d = 1900$ MeV
- $\alpha$ charge density parameters: $0.21144$ fm$^{-3}$ with $0.7024$ fm$^{-2}$ exponent; rms point radius 1.45 fm
- $^{16}$O density Woods-Saxon parameters: $0.168$ fm$^{-3}$, radius 2.6 fm, diffuseness 0.45 fm; rms point radius 2.6201 fm

## Context

This work extends the quartetting wave function approach previously applied to $\alpha$ decay of heavy nuclei like $^{208}$Po (Röpke et al., 2014; Xu et al., 2016) by replacing the local density approximation with a shell-model description of the $^{16}$O core using Woods-Saxon potentials. The result that the effective c.o.m. potential is nearly constant inside the core contradicts the increasing potentials assumed in standard optical models (McFadden, 1966; Michel, 1983; Ohkubo, 2013) and is consistent with earlier harmonic oscillator model studies (Röpke et al., 2014). The approach is closely related to the THSR (Tohsaki-Horiuchi-Schuck-Röpke) method, which treats quartetting self-consistently with full antisymmetrization and has been applied to $^{20}$Ne (Bo Zhou et al., 2013, 2014), but the present work provides a simpler framework for extracting the effective c.o.m. potential and wave function. Future work should improve the local density approximation for Pauli blocking to remove the sharp peak at the critical radius and include gradient terms neglected in the present treatment.
