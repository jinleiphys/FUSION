# A new approach to two-level model calculation of isospin mixing in nuclei

> A new method has been proposed for isolated two-level model to calculate isospin mixing probability in nuclei overcoming common limitations of usual shell model results with isoscalar nuclear Hamiltonian. The method is based on locating the unperturbed levels of the mixed doublet before mixing. Experimental and shell model level energies, electromagnetic/Gamow-Teller transition matrix elements associated with a doublet or two doublet pairs in a nucleus are used to calculate isospin mixing probability in seven self-conjugate nuclei. The four self-conjugate nuclei (P, S, Cl, Ar) considered here show large isospin mixing matrix elements and large unperturbed energy-gaps of the observed isospin-mixed doublet pairs. Large isospin mixing (31.40-48.76 %) is found in the observed doublet ($1^{+},1^{+}$) at 9828.11 and 9967.19 keV, respectively, in $^{24}Mg$. This is probably the largest isospin mixing ever found in a nucleus. This is much larger than that $({20}_{-9.904}^{+9.142}$%) has been found recently in $^{26}$Si. The method is general enough to be applicable to other two-level/multi-level mixing problems and in particular, might be useful for consideration of isospin mixing in the context of Fermi beta decay also.

# A new approach to two-level model calculation of isospin mixing in nuclei

## Key claim
The paper proposes a semi-empirical method for calculating isospin mixing probabilities in isolated two-level systems that overcomes the limitations of standard shell model calculations using isoscalar Hamiltonians, which cannot reproduce the experimental trace invariance or energy gap conditions. The method claims to find isospin mixing as large as 31.40-48.76% in the observed doublet (1+,1+) at 9828.11 and 9967.19 keV in 24Mg, described as "probably the largest isospin mixing ever found in a nucleus."

## Method
The method locates the unperturbed energy levels of a mixed doublet before isospin mixing occurs, using experimental level energies and shell model calculations of energies and electromagnetic or Gamow-Teller transition matrix elements. For nuclei with two doublet pairs, the isospin mixing probabilities for initial and final states are obtained by fitting the theoretical transition matrix element to the experimental value, using a parameter n that adjusts the shell model energy gap via the trace difference between experimental and shell model energies. For nuclei with a single doublet, the mixing probability is determined directly from the measured forbidden transition strength.

## Key numbers
- Isospin mixing probability in 24Mg: 39.57% (effective) and 27.55% (free) for the 1+ doublet at 9828.11 and 9967.19 keV, compared to 20% (+9.142%/-9.904%) found recently in 26Si
- For 30P: initial doublet mixing bi² = 4.723% (+2.150%/-2.243%), final doublet mixing bf² = 2.462% (+0.110%/-0.092%)
- For 32S: initial doublet mixing bi² = 1.237% (+0.210%/-0.213%), final doublet mixing bf² = 2.688% (+0.226%/-0.221%)
- For 34Cl: initial doublet mixing bi² = 0.237% (+0.053%/-0.055%), final doublet mixing bf² = 0.192% (+0.094%/-0.092%)
- For 36Ar: initial doublet mixing bi² = 0.440% (+0.067%/-0.059%), final doublet mixing bf² = 2.460% (+0.065%/-0.098%)
- In 54Co: measured bi² = 0.0023 (+0.0029/-0.0010), with unperturbed gap = 198.40 keV and mixing matrix element H12 = 9.55 keV
- In 64Ge: measured bf² values of 0.012 and 0.025, with a predicted value around 0.0026
- In 24Mg: mixing matrix element H12 = 68.01 keV (+1.50/-3.52) for effective charges

## Context
This work extends the two-level mixing model approach previously applied to isospin mixing in nuclei, addressing the common failure of shell model calculations with isoscalar Hamiltonians to satisfy trace invariance and energy gap conditions. The method is applied to seven self-conjugate nuclei (30P, 32S, 34Cl, 36Ar, 24Mg, 54Co, 64Ge) and builds on prior work by Brown (for GT transitions in 24Mg), Lise (for 54Co), and Fer (for 64Ge). The approach enables calculation of isospin mixing probabilities without requiring a full charge-dependent Hamiltonian, and the author suggests it may be applicable to Fermi beta decay and other two-level or multi-level mixing problems.
