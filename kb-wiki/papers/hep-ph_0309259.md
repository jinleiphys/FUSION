# Centrality-dependence of Particle Production at RHIC and the Combinational Approach

> The newly proposed combinational approach, called the grand combinational model (GCM), as would be described in detail in the text, is still under our careful scrutiny. By applying it, we have attempted to analyze here the characteristics of both the transverse momentum($p_{\rm{T}}$)-, and centrality-dependence of production of the main varieties of the secondaries measured in $AuAu$ collisions at BNL-RHIC at both $\sqrt{s_{\rm{NN}}}=130$ GeV and $\sqrt{s_{\rm{NN}}}=200$ GeV by PHENIX Collaboration. Besides, with the help of it, we have also investigated the nature of centrality-dependence of the average transverse momenta of the various major categories of particles in $AuAu$ collisions at RHIC. The model seems to survive quite smoothly the acid tests of the latest PHENIX data, as it accommodates data modestly well on these twin aspects. The study reveals a kind of universality of nature of the hadronic secondaries and also of the basic particle and nuclear interactions at high energies. However, in the end, we precisely point out both the strengths and limitations of the specific model under consideration here.

## Key claim

The Grand Combinational Model (GCM), which combines Hagedorn's parametrization for proton-proton collisions with Peitzmann's approach for nuclear scaling and a new quadratic parametrization for the exponent (De-Bhattacharyya parametrization), can describe the transverse momentum spectra and average transverse momenta of hadrons produced in AuAu collisions at RHIC across various centralities, but the model relies on two adjustable parameters (alpha and beta) that must be tuned on a case-by-case basis and does not incorporate nuclear geometry or space-time evolution.

## Method

The authors constructed the GCM by combining three elements: Hagedorn's empirical formula for the inclusive cross-section in proton-proton collisions (a power-law in transverse momentum), Peitzmann's scaling relation that connects nucleus-nucleus to nucleon-nucleon cross-sections via a factor (AB)^g(p_T), and a new quadratic parametrization g(p_T) = epsilon + alpha*p_T - beta*p_T^2 (the De-Bhattacharyya parametrization). The parameters alpha and beta were extracted by fitting the model to PHENIX data on identified charged hadron spectra vs. p_T and average transverse momentum vs. centrality for AuAu collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 130 GeV and 200 GeV, with the epsilon and normalization constant absorbed into a single constant C_3. The energy dependence of the proton-proton parameters p_0 and n was handled via empirical logarithmic formulas.

## Key numbers

- The analysis covers AuAu collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 130 GeV and sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV, using data from the PHENIX Collaboration.
- The p_T range for charged hadrons extends from low p_T up to a larger domain beyond 3 GeV/c (previous work was limited mostly to 0.8-3 GeV/c).
- The parameters alpha and beta for each secondary particle and centrality bin are tabulated (Tables 2-11) with chi^2/ndf values; the systematic trends of alpha and beta vs. number of participants (N_part) follow a logarithmic form: alpha(N_part), beta(N_part) = R + S * ln(N_part), with R and S values given in Tables 12 and 13.
- The model reproduces average transverse momentum values for pions, kaons, protons, and antiprotons at various centralities, with the exception of kaons at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV where the agreement is poorer.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous applications of the GCM (De et al., earlier publications) from minimum-bias events to centrality-specific data at RHIC, and from a limited p_T range (0.8-3 GeV/c) to a broader domain including low p_T. The approach contrasts with standard model predictions that attribute high-p_T suppression to jet energy loss in a quark-gluon plasma, as discussed by the PHENIX Collaboration and others (Jia, 2003). The observed logarithmic dependence of alpha and beta on N_part hints at parton saturation beyond a certain centrality, and the model's success enables a unified phenomenological description of both soft and hard interactions without an artificial divide.
