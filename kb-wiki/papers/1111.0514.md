# Neutron starquakes and the dynamic crust

> The most strongly magnetized neutron stars, the magnetars, have spectacular outbursts of gamma-ray flares powered by decay of the magnetic field. The rapidly changing field is strong enough that it should be able to stress and rupture the star's crust, with potentially interesting seismic consequences. This was confirmed in dramatic fashion a few years ago with the discovery of long-lived seismic vibrations excited by rare giant flares. The starquakes associated with the giant flares are, it seems, so catastrophic that they leave the whole star ringing. These discoveries have opened up the possibility of using asteroseismology to study the extreme conditions of the neutron star interior - the dense matter equation of state in crust and core, and the core magnetic field. This chapter reviews the main observational properties of the oscillations seen in the giant flares, and the state of the art in terms of theoretical models of global seismic vibrations in magnetars. I discuss efforts to extend oscillation searches to smaller magnetar bursts (which are more frequent but less energetic), and the role that crust rupturing might play in triggering magnetar flares and exciting global seismic oscillations.

## Key claim

The discovery of quasi-periodic oscillations (QPOs) in the tails of magnetar giant flares has opened the possibility of using asteroseismology to constrain the dense matter equation of state and interior magnetic field strength, but no theoretical model can yet fit all of the observed frequencies, and the interpretation remains complicated by magnetic field coupling between crust and core, superfluid effects, and uncertainties in crust composition.

## Method

The author reviews observational properties of QPOs detected in the 2004 giant flare from SGR 1806-20 and the 1998 giant flare from SGR 1900+14, using data from the Rossi X-ray Timing Explorer (RXTE) and the Ramaty High Energy Solar Spectroscopic Imager (RHESSI). The chapter then surveys theoretical models of global seismic oscillations in magnetars, covering early torsional shear mode models, magneto-elastic coupling calculations in general relativity and Newtonian gravity, superfluid effects, and crust composition uncertainties. Searches for QPOs in smaller, more frequent magnetar bursts are also discussed, along with the role of crust rupturing as a possible trigger for flares.

## Context

This chapter reviews the observational and theoretical state of the art following the discovery of QPOs in giant flares from SGR 1806-20 and SGR 1900+14, first reported by Israel (2005), Strohmayer (2005), Watts (2006), and Strohmayer (2006). It extends earlier theoretical work by Duncan (1998) on starquake-excited global seismic oscillations and by Thompson (1995) on magnetic field decay powering magnetar activity. The review identifies key open questions: the need for improved models of magneto-elastic coupling between crust and core, the role of superfluid neutrons in modifying mode frequencies, and the uncertainty in crust composition due to the poorly constrained density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy. Future work requires resolving whether QPOs arise from discrete crust modes, Alfven continuum endpoints, or gap modes in the coupled crust-core system.
