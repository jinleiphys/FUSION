# Quantum effect in the diffusion along a potential barrier: Comments on the synthesis of superheavy elements

> We discuss a quantum effect in the diffusion process by developing a theory, which takes the finite curvature of the potential field into account. The transport coefficients of our theory satisfy the well-known fluctuation-dissipation theorem in the limit of Markovian approximation in the cases of diffusion in a flat potential and in a potential well. For the diffusion along a potential barrier, the diffusion coefficient can be related to the friction coefficient by an analytic continuation of the fluctuation-dissipation theorem for the case of diffusion along a potential well in the asymptotic time, but contains strong non-Markovian effects at short times. By applying our theory to the case of realistic values of the temperature, the barrier curvature, and the friction coefficient, we show that the quantum effects will play significant roles in describing the synthesis of superheavy elements, i.e., the evolution from the fusion barrier to the conditional saddle, in terms of a diffusion process. We especially point out the importance of the memory effect, which increases at lower temperatures. It makes the net quantum effects enhance the probability of crossing the conditional saddle.

# Quantum effect in the diffusion along a potential barrier: Comments on the synthesis of superheavy elements

## Key claim

The paper shows that quantum effects, particularly non-Markovian (memory) effects, significantly enhance the probability of crossing the conditional saddle in superheavy element synthesis, contrary to the expectation that quantum effects would reduce the diffusion coefficient. This enhancement occurs at low temperatures (around 1 MeV or below) relevant to superheavy element production, where the classical fluctuation-dissipation theorem fails.

## Method

The authors develop a quantum diffusion theory by extending quasilinear response theory to include the finite curvature of the potential barrier, deriving a Fokker-Planck equation with non-Markovian transport coefficients. They apply the Caldeira-Leggett model with Ohmic dissipation and Drude regularization to compute the time-dependent ratio of diffusion to friction coefficients. The probability to cross the conditional saddle is then calculated by solving coupled equations for the fluctuations (mean square deviations) and using a Gaussian Wigner distribution function for the macroscopic motion.

## Key numbers

- Barrier curvature: \(\hbar \Omega = 1.0\) MeV, representing the conditional saddle curvature relevant to heavy nuclei.
- Cutoff frequency in Drude regularization: \(\hbar \omega_c = 20.0\) MeV.
- Reduced friction parameter: \(\beta = 5 \times 10^{21} \, \text{s}^{-1}\), taken from previous fluctuation-dissipation dynamics studies using the Langevin equation.
- Height of the conditional saddle: 4.0 MeV (used to set the initial position \(q_0\)).
- Mass parameter: corresponds to the reduced mass in the collision of mass number 48 and 238 nuclei.
- Temperatures studied: three values, with the paper emphasizing that quantum effects are important at low temperatures (around 1 MeV or below), where \(T \ll \hbar \omega_c\).

## Context

This work extends the fluctuation-dissipation dynamics approach to superheavy element synthesis developed by Frobrich et al. (1990s) and Ohta et al. (1990s), which had assumed the classical fluctuation-dissipation theorem valid at high temperatures. The paper addresses the need to examine quantum effects because superheavy elements are stabilized by shell correction energies and must be synthesized at low energies (around 1 MeV or below). It builds on prior discussions of diffusion along a potential barrier by Hanggi et al. (1990) and Rips and Pollak (2003), and contrasts with the approach of Bar and Bar (2002), whose diffusion coefficient expression does not match the asymptotic formula presented here. The results enable more realistic modeling of the fusion hindrance in superheavy element production and raise the question of whether the non-Markovian enhancement is specific to the Ohmic dissipation model or holds generally.
