# Dark Matter Effects on the Curvature of Neutron Stars within the new Quarkyonic Model Coupled with Relativistic Mean Field Theory

> For the first time, we analyze the impact of dark matter (DM) on the curvature properties of quarkyonic neutron stars (NS) using a hybrid model based on quarkyonic-effective field theory within the relativistic mean-field (E-RMF) framework. This study examines the radial variation of curvature components, including the Ricci scalar ($\cal{R}$), Ricci tensor ($\cal{J}$), Kretschmann scalar ($\cal{K}$), and Weyl tensor ($\cal{W}$), under different DM admixtures. These components offer critical insights into the spacetime geometry and gravitational field strength within the star. The analysis spans canonical mass (1.4 $M_{\odot}$) and maximum mass configurations, varying key parameters such as the transition density ($n_t$) and QCD confinement scale ($Λ_{\rm cs}$), which influence matter transitions and quark confinement. Our results reveal that DM and quarkyonic matter (QM) significantly affect the star's curvature. Central curvature values, particularly $\cal{R}$, $\cal{J}$, and $\cal{K}$, increase with DM due to higher central densities but decrease with stronger QM effects. Stiffer EOSs yield smoother curvature profiles, while softer EOSs influenced by DM redistribute curvature more dynamically. DM softens the EOS, reducing central pressure and compactness, whereas higher $n_t$ values enhance compactness and central pressures. These findings show that dark matter plays a key role in shaping the curvature of quarkyonic neutron stars, offering new insights into compact objects with exotic matter.

## Key claim
For the first time, the authors analyze how dark matter (DM) affects the curvature properties of quarkyonic neutron stars using a hybrid model, finding that central curvature values (Ricci scalar, Ricci tensor, Kretschmann scalar) increase with DM due to higher central densities but decrease with stronger quarkyonic matter effects, with the caveat that these results depend on the specific choice of transition density and QCD confinement scale parameters.

## Method
The authors combine an effective relativistic mean-field (E-RMF) nuclear model with the quarkyonic matter framework of McLerran and Reddy (2019) and Zhao and Lattimer (2020), adding non-annihilating WIMP dark matter particles (200 GeV neutralinos) that interact via Higgs exchange. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations using the G3 and IOPB-I parameter sets to compute mass, radius, and radial profiles of four curvature quantities (Ricci scalar, Ricci tensor, Kretschmann scalar, Weyl tensor) for baryonic, quarkyonic, and DM-admixed quarkyonic neutron stars at both canonical (1.4 solar masses) and maximum mass configurations.

## Key numbers
- Dark matter particle mass: 200 GeV (neutralino assumed)
- Higgs boson mass: 125 GeV; vacuum expectation value: 246 GeV; coupling constants y = 0.07 and f = 0.35
- Transition densities considered: n_t = 0.3, 0.4, 0.5 fm^{-3}; QCD confinement scale Lambda_cs = 800 MeV
- Dark matter Fermi momenta considered: k_f^{DM} = 0.00, 0.03, 0.04 GeV
- Sun's surface Kretschmann scalar: K_sun = 3.06 x 10^{-27} cm^{-2}
- Canonical neutron star mass: 1.4 solar masses; maximum observed pulsar masses cited: PSR J0952-0607 at 2.35 +/- 0.17 solar masses, PSR J0740+6620 at 2.08 +/- 0.07 solar masses
- Canonical 1.4 solar mass neutron star radius constrained to less than 13.5 km (from observational references)
- Compactness ratio of neutron star to Sun is approximately 10^5 times higher

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous study (Pattnaik et al., 2024) on DM and quark matter effects on neutron star equation of state and macroscopic properties, and builds on curvature analyses by Eksi et al. (2014) and Xiao et al. (2015) who studied GR constraints and symmetry energy dependence on curvature. It also follows Das et al. (2021) who showed that small amounts of DM may exist inside neutron stars but larger percentages cause instability, and Das et al. (2023) who analyzed anisotropy effects on surface curvature. The results enable future constraints on dense matter equations of state through gravitational wave observations and potential direct curvature measurements via atom interferometry techniques.
