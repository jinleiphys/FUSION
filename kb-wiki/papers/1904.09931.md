# Lattice QCD and Neutrino-Nucleus Scattering

> This document is one of a series of whitepapers from the USQCD collaboration. Here, we discuss opportunities for lattice QCD in neutrino-oscillation physics, which inevitably entails nucleon and nuclear structure. In addition to discussing pertinent lattice-QCD calculations of nucleon and nuclear matrix elements, the interplay with models of nuclei is discussed. This program of lattice- QCD calculations is relevant to current and upcoming neutrino experiments, becoming increasingly important on the timescale of LBNF/DUNE and HyperK.

# Lattice QCD and Neutrino-Nucleus Scattering

## Key claim

Lattice QCD calculations of nucleon form factors, particularly the axial form factor, can provide first-principles input for neutrino-nucleus scattering that is essential for interpreting oscillation experiments, but full control of systematic uncertainties requires significantly more computational resources than currently available, with estimates of 9M GPU-hours for a calculation on eleven ensembles at physical quark masses and lattice spacings as small as 0.03 fm.

## Method

The authors review the status of lattice-QCD calculations of nucleon matrix elements relevant to neutrino-nucleus scattering, focusing on electroweak form factors obtained from correlation functions computed on ensembles of gauge field configurations with dynamical sea quarks. They discuss the decomposition of charged-current matrix elements into Dirac, Pauli, axial, and induced pseudoscalar form factors, and describe the use of the z-expansion as a model-independent parametrization for extrapolating form factors to the full q^2 range needed for neutrino experiments. The paper also covers calculations of parton distribution moments via local operators, the hadron tensor from four-point correlation functions, and estimates of computing requirements based on ongoing USQCD efforts using Wilson-clover and staggered fermion formulations.

## Key numbers

- NuTeV measurement of the on-shell weak mixing angle: sin^2 theta_W = 0.2277 +/- 0.0013_stat +/- 0.0009_syst, 2.7 sigma discrepant from the world average of 0.22343 +/- 0.00007
- Strange-antistrange parton asymmetry from global analysis: <x>_{s-} approx 0.0018, with 90% confidence range -0.001 to 0.005
- Axial coupling g_A = -1.2723(23) from neutron beta decay
- Axial mass m_A from deuterium quasielastic scattering: 1.02(3) GeV; from pion electroproduction: 1.08(4) GeV; from K2K: 1.20(12) GeV; from MINOS: 1.27(15) GeV; from MiniBooNE: 1.35(17) GeV; from NOMAD with 2p-2h corrections: 1.05(6) GeV
- Reanalysis of deuterium bubble-chamber data using z-expansion: sqrt(12/r_A^2) = 1.01(24) GeV
- Estimated computing cost for nucleon form factor calculation on eleven ensembles: 9M GPU-hours (equivalent to 300M conventional CPU core-hours)
- Estimated cost to generate eight Wilson-clover ensembles: 8M GPU-hours; computation of nucleon correlation functions: 15M GPU-hours
- Lattice spacings in sample calculations range from 0.05 fm to 0.15 fm; pion masses from 130 MeV to 540 MeV
- Most lattice-QCD calculations obtain mean-squared charge radii (r_A, r_E, r_M) smaller than phenomenological extractions by about 30%

## Context

This whitepaper from the USQCD collaboration (2019) extends the program of lattice-QCD calculations for neutrino physics discussed in companion whitepapers on hadrons and nuclei (Detmold 2018) and exascale computing (Joo 2018). It addresses the NuTeV anomaly (Zeller 2001) and the longstanding discrepancy between axial form factor determinations from different experiments, particularly the tension between dipole-form extractions and model-independent z-expansion analyses (Meyer 2016, Hill 2017). The work enables future incorporation of lattice-QCD results into nuclear many-body theory and event generators such as GENIE (Andreopoulos 2009), and provides computing cost estimates that inform the resource planning for exascale calculations needed for DUNE and Hyper-Kamiokande.
