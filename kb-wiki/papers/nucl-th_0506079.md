# Semiclassical description of shell effects in finite fermion systems

> A short survey of the semiclassical periodic orbit theory, initiated by M. Gutzwiller and generalized by many other authors, is given. Via so-called semiclassical trace formmulae, gross-shell effects in bound fermion systems can be interpreted in terms of a few periodic orbits of the corresponding classical systems. In integrable systems, these are usually the shortest members of the most degenerate families or orbits, but in some systems also less degenerate orbits can determine the gross-shell structure. Applications to nuclei, metal clusters, semiconductor nanostructures, and trapped dilute atom gases are discussed.

# Semiclassical description of shell effects in finite fermion systems

## Key claim

Gross-shell effects in bound fermion systems can be interpreted semiclassically via a few periodic orbits of the corresponding classical system, but the claim is hedged: in integrable systems the shortest members of the most degenerate families usually dominate, yet in some systems less degenerate orbits can determine the gross-shell structure, and in mixed-dynamical systems a competition between periods and degeneracies of the shortest orbits governs the result.

## Method

The paper reviews the semiclassical periodic orbit theory (POT) initiated by Gutzwiller (1971), in which the quantum level density is decomposed into a smooth part (obtained via the extended Thomas-Fermi model) and an oscillating part expressed by a trace formula summing over classical periodic orbits. Coarse-graining the quantum spectrum with a Gaussian of width gamma or introducing finite temperature suppresses contributions from longer orbits, so that gross-shell effects are dominated by the shortest periodic orbits. The formalism is applied to four classes of finite fermion systems: nuclei, metal clusters, semiconductor nanostructures, and trapped dilute atom gases.

## Key numbers

* Spherical cavity: the coarse-grained level density exhibits a beating pattern with rapid shell oscillations modulated by a slow "super-shell" oscillation; the first super-shell node appears around N ~ 800-1000 fermions.
* Sodium clusters: experimental magic numbers N_i = 92, 138, 192, 264, ... correspond almost exactly to those of a spherical cavity; the slope s = N_{i+1}^{1/3} - N_i^{1/3} was measured as s_exp = 0.61 +/- 0.01.
* The POT prediction for the slope is s_pot = 0.603; self-consistent quantum-mechanical mean-field calculations give s_mf = 0.61, in quantitative agreement with experiment.
* For the super-shell beat in sodium clusters, the first node appears around N ~ 900.
* In the actinide fission barrier problem, the shortest periodic orbits are families with f=1 (axial U(1) symmetry): diagonal, triangular, and square-shaped orbits in circular planes perpendicular to the nuclear symmetry axis.

## Context

This work extends the periodic orbit theory of Gutzwiller (1971) and the trace formula generalizations by Balian and Bloch (1970) for billiards, and by Strutinsky et al. (1982) for systems with continuous symmetries. It builds on the Strutinsky shell-correction method (1967) and the Nilsson model (1955) for deformed nuclei. The semiclassical interpretation of shell effects enables the prediction of ground-state deformations in nuclei and metal clusters, the explanation of the left-right asymmetry of fission barriers (previously studied by Moller and Nilsson (1970) and Gustafsson, Moller, and Nilsson (1971)), and the understanding of super-shell oscillations in metal clusters (predicted by Nishioka et al. (1990) and observed by Pedersen et al. (1991)). For semiconductor nanostructures, it explains Aharonov-Bohm oscillations in antidot superlattices and quantum dots, and for trapped dilute atomic gases it accounts for beating shell structures in harmonically trapped fermions.
