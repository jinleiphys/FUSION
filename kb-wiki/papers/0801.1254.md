# Partial conservation of seniority and nuclear isomerism

> We point out the possibility of the {\em partial} conservation of the seniority quantum number when most eigenstates are mixed in seniority but some remain pure. This situation occurs in nuclei for the $g_{9/2}$ and $h_{9/2}$ shells where it is at the origin of the existence of seniority isomers in the ruthenium and palladium isotopes. It also occurs for $f$ bosons.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that certain four-particle states in the $g_{9/2}$ and $h_{9/2}$ shells retain exact seniority even when the nuclear interaction does not conserve seniority overall, contradicting the usual assumption that seniority mixing is universal for non-conserving interactions.

## Method

The authors derived algebraic conditions for partial seniority conservation by analyzing four-particle states in a single-$j$ shell, using coefficients of fractional parentage and Racah algebra. They solved an overcomplete set of equations for the $j=9/2$ fermion system to identify specific $J=4$ and $J=6$ states that remain pure seniority $v=4$ eigenstates for any two-body interaction. The analysis was extended to $f$ bosons ($j=3$) where analogous solutions were found numerically.

## Key numbers

- For $j=9/2$ fermions, the $J=4$ seniority $v=4$ state has energy $E = \frac{68}{33}\nu_2 + \nu_4 + \frac{13}{15}\nu_6 + \frac{114}{55}\nu_8$ for arbitrary interaction matrix elements $\nu_\lambda$
- For $j=9/2$ fermions, the $J=6$ seniority $v=4$ state has energy $E = \frac{19}{11}\nu_2 + \frac{12}{13}\nu_4 + \nu_6 + \frac{336}{143}\nu_8$
- The $B(E2)$ value between the $J=6$ and $J=4$ solvable states is $\frac{209475}{176468}$ times the $B(E2; 2^+ \rightarrow 0^+)$ of the two-particle system
- The difference in excitation energies between the $J=6$ and $J=4$ solvable states equals $-\frac{1}{3}E_x(2^+) - \frac{1}{13}E_x(4^+) + \frac{2}{15}E_x(6^+) + \frac{18}{65}E_x(8^+)$ from the two-particle system

## Context

This work extends the findings of Escuderos and Zamick (2006) and Zamick (2007) who first noted the existence of pure seniority states in the $j=9/2$ shell, placing them in the broader framework of partial dynamical symmetries as defined by Alhassid and Leviatan (1992) and Leviatan (1996). The results explain the existence of seniority isomers in ruthenium and palladium isotopes, particularly $^{94}$Ru and $^{96}$Pd, and provide parameter-independent predictions for excitation energies and transition rates that can guide experimental searches for the predicted $J^\pi=4_2^+,6_2^+$ states.
