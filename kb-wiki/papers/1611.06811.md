# Laser-Matter Interaction: Classical Regime versus Quantum Regime

> Doppler backscattering of optical laser photons on a "flying mirror" of relativistic electrons promises to yield coherent photons with MeV-range energies. We compare the nuclear interaction of such a laser pulse with the standard atom-laser interaction. The mean-field description of atoms must be replaced by a rate equation and the classical field strength, far too faint in nuclei, by the dipole transition rate. Significant nuclear excitation occurs for photon numbers much smaller than typical for atoms. That drastically reduces the requirements on the experimental realization of a "flying mirror".

## Key claim

Significant nuclear excitation from a coherent MeV laser pulse occurs via dipole transition rates (a quantum concept) rather than via classical electric field strength, and requires only about \(10^4\) photons per pulse, many orders of magnitude fewer than needed for atomic multiphoton processes, provided the nucleus relaxes between successive photon absorptions via nucleon-nucleon collisions.

## Method

The authors compare laser-atom and laser-nucleus interactions within the dipole approximation, using a quantized electromagnetic field and coherent-state description of the laser pulse. For nuclei, they derive the single-photon dipole absorption rate \(\mathcal{R}_{\rm dip}\) from first-order time-dependent perturbation theory, then argue that multiphoton absorption in medium-weight and heavy nuclei must be described by rate equations because the strong residual nucleon-nucleon interaction drives the system toward statistical equilibrium on a collision timescale \(\tau_{\rm coll} \approx 5 \times 10^{-23}\) s. They support the inadequacy of the classical field-strength approach for nuclei with a scaling argument and a three-dimensional time-dependent Hartree-Fock calculation for \(^{16}\)O.

## Key numbers

- Photon energy of the Doppler-backscattered pulse: \(\hbar \omega_0 \approx 5\) MeV, with energy spread \(\sigma \approx 50\) keV.
- Nuclear dipole width without photon-number factor: order \(100\) eV – \(1\) keV; for \(N = 10^3 - 10^4\) photons, the dipole width is boosted into the MeV domain.
- In the time-dependent Hartree-Fock calculation for \(^{16}\)O at \(\hbar \omega = 10\) MeV, the average number of absorbed photons is \(5 \times 10^{-6}\) (using an enhanced field strength of \(0.033\) MeV/fm and pulse half-width \(T_{\rm pulse} = 2000\) fm/c).
- Nucleon-nucleon collision time: \(\tau_{\rm coll} \approx 5 \times 10^{-23}\) s, corresponding to \(\hbar / \tau_{\rm coll} \approx 10\) MeV.
- Number of photons required for significant multiple nuclear excitation via rate processes: \(N \approx 10^4\); for comparison, a medium-intensity optical laser carries \(10^{10}\) photons per pulse.
- Scaling factor for the square of the dipole matrix element (classical regime): \(10^6\); scaling factor for the dipole transition rate: \(10^{13}\).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for laser-nucleus reactions developed by Pálffy et al. (2014, 2015) and Weidenmüller (2011), which used rate equations for multiphoton absorption in the perturbative and quasiadiabatic regimes. It contrasts the nuclear case with the well-established mean-field plus classical-field approach for atoms (Keldysh 1965; Faisal 1987; Popruzhenko 2014). The paper enables a concrete experimental target: coherent Doppler backscattering of a small fraction of an optical laser pulse (rather than the full pulse) can produce sufficient MeV photons to excite nuclei, substantially relaxing the requirements on the "flying mirror" technique proposed by Einstein (1905) and pursued experimentally by Kiefer et al. (2013) and others.
