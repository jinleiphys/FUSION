# Ab initio calculations for non-strange and strange few-baryon systems

> Concerning the non-strange particle systems the low-energy excitation spectra of the three- and four-body helium isotopes are studied. Objects of the study are the astrophysical $S$-factor $S_{12}$ of the radiative proton deuteron capture $d(p,γ)^3$He and the width of the $^4$He isoscalar monopole resonance.Both observables are calculated using the Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method. The LIT equations are solved via expansions of the LIT states on a specifically modified hyperspherical harmonics (HH) basis. It is illustrated that at low energies such a modification allows to work with much higher LIT resolutions than with an unmodified HH basis. It is discussed that this opens up the possibility to determine astrophysical $S$-factors as well as the width of low-lying resonances with the LIT method. In the sector of strange baryon systems binding energies of the hypernucleus $^3_Λ$H are calculated using a nonsymmetrized HH basis. The results are compared with those calculated by various other groups with different methods. For all the considered non-strange and strange baryon systems it is shown that high-precision results are obtained.

## Key claim

The Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method, when combined with a specifically modified hyperspherical harmonics basis (an (A-1)-body HH basis plus a single-particle basis for the A-th particle), can achieve sufficiently high LIT-state densities in the low-energy two-body breakup region to precisely determine astrophysical S-factors and narrow resonance widths, which is not possible with a standard A-body HH basis.

## Method

The LIT equations for the 3He dipole response and the 4He isoscalar monopole response were solved via expansions of the LIT states on a modified hyperspherical harmonics basis, consisting of an (A-1)-body HH basis times a basis set for the relative motion of the A-th particle. For the strange baryon sector, binding energies of the hypernucleus 3ΛH were calculated using a nonsymmetrized HH basis, with the correct permutational symmetry enforced via the Casimir operator and a fictitious Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

- Astrophysical S-factor S12 for d(p,γ)3He: excellent agreement between LIT method and explicit pd continuum wave function calculation (no numerical value given for S12 itself, but the agreement is demonstrated in a figure)
- 4He isoscalar monopole resonance width: 180(70) keV from LIT method, compared to experimental value of 270(50) keV
- 3ΛH binding energy with AV4'+Bodmer-Usmani potential: 2.530(3) MeV (NSHH), 2.42(6) MeV (AFDMC), 2.537(1) MeV (Faddeev-Yakubovsky)
- 3ΛH Λ separation energy with AV4'+Bodmer-Usmani potential: 0.290(3) MeV (NSHH), 0.18(6) MeV (AFDMC), 0.292(1) MeV (Faddeev-Yakubovsky)
- 3ΛH binding energy with AV8'+NSC97f potential: 2.41(2) MeV (NSHH), 2.415(1) MeV (Faddeev-Yakubovsky)
- 3ΛH Λ separation energy with AV8'+NSC97f potential: 0.17(2) MeV (NSHH), 0.189(1) MeV (Faddeev-Yakubovsky), 0.19(1) MeV (Gaussian expansion method)
- 4He binding energy with TN potential: 31.4 MeV
- LIT calculations used σI values ranging from 20 MeV down to 0.01 MeV for resolution control
- Momentum transfer q = 300 MeV/c used for the 4He monopole response calculation

## Context

This work extends the LIT method of Efros (2007) by addressing a limitation identified in Bacca (2013), where the 4He isoscalar monopole resonance width could not be determined due to insufficient LIT-state density with a standard HH basis. The modified basis approach was introduced in Leidemann (2015) and is here applied to both 3He photodisintegration and 4He electrodisintegration. For the strange sector, the NSHH benchmark calculations for 3ΛH are part of the collaborative benchmark study of Fey (2017), comparing with AFDMC (Lovato 2013), Faddeev-Yakubovsky, and Gaussian expansion method (Hiyama 2014) approaches. The results enable future LIT calculations of astrophysical S-factors for reactions involving more than three nucleons and of narrow resonance widths with realistic nuclear forces, as well as more ambitious hypernuclear calculations with realistic interaction models.
