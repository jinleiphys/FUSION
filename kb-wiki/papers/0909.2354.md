# Flow, spectra and HBT radii in heavy-ion collisions

> The expansion of the fireball created in relativistic heavy ion collisions is described using the 3+1D hydrodynamical model. Experimentally observed transverse momentum spectra at different rapdities, elliptic flow and HBT correlations of produced particles can be reproduced. We give estimates of shear viscosity corrections at freeze-out, which we find important only for the elliptic flow coefficient.

# Flow, spectra and HBT radii in heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim
Perfect fluid 3+1D hydrodynamics can reproduce measured transverse momentum spectra, HBT radii, and elliptic flow in Au-Au collisions at RHIC, but the agreement for elliptic flow is accidental because shear viscosity corrections at freeze-out significantly reduce this observable, especially at large pseudorapidities.

## Method
The authors performed 3+1D relativistic hydrodynamic simulations of Au-Au collisions at √s = 200 GeV using a short initial time of 0.25 fm/c and a realistic equation of state without a soft point. The hydrodynamic output was fed into the THERMINATOR statistical emission and resonance decay code to generate complete events. Shear viscosity corrections were then estimated by applying nonequilibrium corrections to the Cooper-Frye freeze-out formula using the velocity flow from the perfect fluid calculation.

## Key numbers
- Initial time for hydrodynamic evolution: 0.25 fm/c
- Freeze-out temperature: T_F = 150 MeV
- HBT correlation radii calculated from the model are within 10% of measured values
- For η/s = 0.16, shear viscosity corrections produce no appreciable modification of transverse momentum spectra for p⊥ < 1.5 GeV at central rapidities, but up to a 30% increase at rapidity y = 3.5 and p⊥ = 3 GeV
- Elliptic flow reduction from shear viscosity corrections: 20% at central rapidity and up to 60% at pseudorapidity 4 for η/s = 0.16

## Context
This work extends earlier 3+1D hydrodynamic calculations by Hirano (2002) by using a shorter initial time and a different equation of state from Chojnacki (2007). The finding that shear viscosity corrections significantly reduce elliptic flow, especially at large rapidities, agrees with results from Hirano (2005) where dissipation from hadronic rescattering was found important at large rapidities. The results enable future studies that must include both shear and bulk viscosity in the fluid expansion model to obtain reliable elliptic flow predictions.
