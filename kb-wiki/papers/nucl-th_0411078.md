# Anatomy of three-body decay I. Schematic models

> Sequential three-body decay proceeds via spatially confined quasi-stationary two-body configurations. Direct three-body decay populates the three-body continuum without intermediate steps. The relative importance of these decay modes is discussed in a schematic model employing only Coulomb or centrifugal barrier potentials. Decisive dimensionless charge, mass and energy ratios are derived. Sequential decay is usually favored for charged particles. Small charge and small mass of high energy is preferably emitted first. Without Coulomb potential the sequential decay is favored except when both resonance energy and intermediate two-body energy are large.

# Anatomy of three-body decay I. Schematic models

## Key claim

Sequential three-body decay is generally favored over direct decay for charged particles, but this preference is conditional: direct decay becomes preferred when the intermediate two-body resonance energy approaches the total decay energy, regardless of charges and masses. Without Coulomb potentials, sequential decay is favored except when both the resonance energy and the intermediate two-body energy are large.

## Method

The authors model three-body decay using only Coulomb and centrifugal barrier potentials in a WKB tunneling framework, treating the hyperradius as the adiabatic coordinate. They compare the WKB transmission coefficients for direct decay (simultaneous increase of all pairwise distances) and sequential decay (two-step process via an intermediate two-body configuration) by deriving analytic expressions for the action integrals. The decay paths are parametrized by scaling constants that define how inter-particle distances increase as functions of the hyperradius, with the least-action path obtained by minimizing the WKB exponent with respect to these scaling parameters.

## Key numbers

- For three identical charged particles (e.g., alpha particles in the first excited 0+ state of 12C with E ≈ 3 E12): b12/b = 0.24, b12,3/b = (2/3)^(3/2), εa ≈ sqrt(3/2), εb ≈ sqrt(3), giving Ra ≈ 2/3 (sequential decay preferred)
- For the higher-lying 0+ excitation with E ≈ 40 E12: εa ≈ 1, εb ≈ 6.5, giving Ra ≈ 0.5 (sequential decay even more favored)
- For the 2+ state in 8Be with E ≈ 2 E12: εa ≈ εb ≈ sqrt(2), giving Ra ≈ 0.7 (sequential decay still favored)
- For two-proton decay from proton dripline nuclei: sequential proton emission is preferred over sequential core emission; direct decay favored only when 7E/8 < E12 < E
- For two neutrons around a core with s-waves: T/T12,3 ≈ (E/7 MeV)^(3/2) * sqrt(E/(E - E12))
- The two-body decay width Γ12 must be smaller than about 1 MeV for the sequential decay condition d12,3/R0 > 1 to be satisfied

## Context

This work extends the classical treatment of alpha-decay (Sie87) to three-body decay by generalizing the WKB tunneling approach to three-particle final states. It builds on earlier studies of three-body decay characterization (Dan87, Boc93) and recent scaling properties for direct multi-cluster Coulomb decay (Kar04). The paper provides analytic criteria for distinguishing sequential versus direct decay mechanisms, which is essential for interpreting kinematically complete experimental data from modern detection techniques. The companion paper (Gar05) extends these results to realistic computations including short-range interactions.
