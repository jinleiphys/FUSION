# Exactly Solvable Models: The Road towards a Rigorous Treatment of Phase Transitions in Finite Nuclear Systems

> We discuss exact analytical solutions of a variety of statistical models recently obtained for finite systems by a novel powerful mathematical method, the Laplace-Fourier transform. Among them are a constrained version of the statistical multifragmentation model, the Gas of Bags Model and the Hills and Dales Model of surface partition. Thus, the Laplace-Fourier transform allows one to study the nuclear matter equation of state, the equation of state of hadronic and quark gluon matter and surface partitions on the same footing. A complete analysis of the isobaric partition singularities of these models is done for finite systems. The developed formalism allows us, for the first time, to exactly define the finite volume analogs of gaseous, liquid and mixed phases of these models from the first principles of statistical mechanics and demonstrate the pitfalls of earlier works. The found solutions may be used for building up a new theoretical apparatus to rigorously study phase transitions in finite systems. The strategic directions of future research opened by these exact results are also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the Laplace-Fourier transform method provides, for the first time, exact analytical solutions for finite-volume statistical models of nuclear matter, allowing one to rigorously define finite-volume analogs of gaseous, liquid, and mixed phases from first principles of statistical mechanics, a task the authors state is unclear whether it can be done for a general case, but which they demonstrate is possible for a class of realistic nonclassical models used in nuclear multifragmentation and relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

## Method

The authors apply the Laplace-Fourier transform to the grand canonical partition function of a simplified version of the statistical multifragmentation model (SMM), converting it into an isobaric partition function whose singularities (simple poles and branch points) are analyzed to identify gaseous and liquid phases. They then extend this method to the Gas of Bags Model (GBM) for hadron-quark-gluon plasma transitions and to the Hills and Dales Model for surface partitions, analyzing the singularities of the isobaric partition for finite systems without taking the thermodynamic limit.

## Key numbers

- The bulk binding energy per nucleon is \(W_{\rm o}=16\) MeV, with the excited-state contribution parameterized by \(\epsilon_{\rm o}=16\) MeV.
- The surface tension parameterization uses \(\sigma_{\rm o}=18\) MeV and \(T_c=18\) MeV, with \(\sigma(T)=0\) at \(T \ge T_c\).
- The normal nuclear density is \(\rho_{\rm o}=0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\), giving the excluded volume parameter \(b=1/\rho_{\rm o}\).
- The Fisher exponent \(\tau\) is constrained to the range \(1.799 < \tau < 1.846\) by comparison with ISiS and EOS Collaboration data, in contrast to the Fisher droplet model value \(\tau_{\rm FDM} \approx 2.16\).
- The critical exponents of the simplified SMM are calculated as functions of \(\tau\): \(\alpha = \alpha^\prime = 0\) at the critical point, and the scaling relations for the SMM differ from those of the Fisher droplet model.

## Context

This work extends the exact analytical solution of a simplified statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) first obtained by Bugaev (2000, 2001) and the subsequent calculation of its critical indices by Reuter (2001), which showed that the SMM scaling relations differ from those of the Fisher droplet model (Fisher, 1967). The Laplace-Fourier transform method, introduced by Bugaev (2004a), is here applied to the Gas of Bags Model (Gorenstein, 1981) for hadron-quark-gluon plasma transitions and to the Hills and Dales Model for surface partitions, providing a unified framework. The paper explicitly critiques earlier numerical approaches (Bondorf, 1995; Gross, 1997; Moretto, 1997) and the Chomaz and Gulminelli (2003) approach to bimodality, and it enables future work on a rigorous microscopic kinetics of phase transitions in finite nuclear systems.
