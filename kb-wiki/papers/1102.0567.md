# Solving the Coulomb scattering problem using the complex scaling method

> Based on the work of Nuttall and Cohen [Phys. Rev. {\bf 188} (1969) 1542] and Resigno et al{} [Phys. Rev. A {\bf 55} (1997) 4253] we present a rigorous formalism for solving the scattering problem for long-range interactions without using exact asymptotic boundary conditions. The long-range interaction may contain both Coulomb and short-range potentials. The exterior complex scaling method, applied to a specially constructed inhomogeneous Schrödinger equation, transforms the scattering problem into a boundary problem with zero boundary conditions. The local and integral representations for the scattering amplitudes have been derived. The formalism is illustrated with numerical examples.

## Key claim

The paper presents a mathematically rigorous formalism for solving the two-body Coulomb scattering problem using exterior complex scaling (ECS) without requiring exact asymptotic boundary conditions, by reformulating the problem as an inhomogeneous Schrödinger equation with a finite-range driving term; the key condition is that the splitting radius \(R\) must be chosen large enough that the asymptotic approximations for the Jost solutions and Coulomb functions become valid.

## Method

The authors split the total potential into an interior part \(V_R\) (finite range, zero for \(r > R\)) and an exterior part \(V^R\), and split the wave function accordingly into \(\Psi^R\) (containing the incident wave) and \(\Psi_R\) (the remainder). They construct \(\Psi^R\) explicitly using Jost solutions and matching conditions at \(r=R\), then derive a driven Schrödinger equation for the scaled function \(\Phi_R = (a^R)^{-1}\Psi_R\) with a finite-range source term \(-V_R \hat{j}_\ell\). Exterior complex scaling is applied to this equation, transforming the outgoing boundary condition into a zero boundary condition at infinity, and the scattering amplitude is recovered either from a local formula at \(r=R\) or from an integral over the finite interval \([0,R]\).

## Key numbers

- For the pure Coulomb potential \(V(r)=2/r\) with momentum \(k=1\) and radius \(R=40\), the numerical solution for the s-wave (\(\ell=0\)) practically coincides with the exact Coulomb function \(F_0(1/k,kr)\) in the internal region \([0,R]\).
- For the potential \(V(r) = 2/r + 15 r^2 e^{-r}\) with momentum \(k=3\) and radius \(R=20\), the accuracy of the phase shift is already as small as 1%.
- For \(\ell=0\) and \(k=3\), both the local and integral representations give comparable accuracy; for \(\ell=4\) the local representation accuracy degrades unless the condition \(kR \gg \ell(\ell+1)+\eta^2\) is satisfied.
- The s-wave scattering cross section for the potential \(V(r) = 2/r + 15 r^2 e^{-r}\) agrees with a highly optimized logarithmic derivative code (Johnson algorithm) to within a relative accuracy smaller than 0.001.

## Context

This work extends the exterior complex scaling approach of Nuttall and Cohen (1969) and Rescigno et al. (1997) to rigorously handle long-range Coulomb interactions, which previously required either exponentially decreasing potentials or the inclusion of the Coulomb potential in the unperturbed Hamiltonian (limiting applicability to two-body problems). The formalism provides a mathematically sound foundation for the approximate ECS method used by McCurdy, Rescigno and coworkers (2004) and enables future extension to three-body scattering problems with charged particles, where analytic Coulomb solutions are unavailable.
