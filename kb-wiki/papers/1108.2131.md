# Thermal Photons and Collective Flow at the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider

> We update our calculations of thermal-photon production in nuclear collisions at the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider (RHIC). Specifically, we address the recent experimental observation of an elliptic flow of direct photons comparable in magnitude to that of pions, which is at variance with expectations based on quark-gluon plasma (QGP) dominated photon radiation. Our thermal emission rate is based on previous work, i.e., resummed leading-order QGP emission and in-medium hadronic rates in the confined phase. These rates are nearly degenerate at temperatures close to the expected QCD-phase change. The rates are convoluted over an improved elliptic-fireball expansion with transverse- and elliptic-flow fields quantitatively constrained by empirical light- and strange-hadron spectra. The resulting direct-photon spectra in central Au-Au collisions are characterized by hadron-dominated emission up to transverse momenta of ~2-3 GeV. The associated large elliptic flow in the hadronic phase mitigates the discrepancy with the measured photon-v2 compared to scenarios with QGP-dominated emission.

# Thermal Photons and Collective Flow at the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider

## Key claim

The measured elliptic flow of direct photons at RHIC, which is comparable to that of pions and at variance with expectations of quark-gluon plasma (QGP) dominated emission, can be substantially mitigated by a hadron-dominated photon emission scenario where the hadronic phase carries most of the elliptic flow, provided the fireball evolution is constrained by empirical light- and strange-hadron spectra at chemical and kinetic freezeout.

## Method

The authors convolve resummed leading-order QGP emission rates and in-medium hadronic rates (from Turbide (2003)) over an improved elliptic-fireball expansion whose transverse- and elliptic-flow fields are quantitatively constrained by blast-wave fits to light-hadron (pion, kaon, proton) and multi-strange hadron (phi meson) spectra and elliptic flow at chemical freezeout (T_ch ~ 170 MeV) and kinetic freezeout (T_fo ~ 100 MeV). Chemical off-equilibrium effects are included via effective meson chemical potentials that enhance hadronic photon production channels such as pi+rho -> pi+gamma by a pion fugacity factor.

## Key numbers

- The hadronic emission dominates over QGP emission up to transverse momenta of ~2-3 GeV in central Au-Au collisions at sqrt(s)=200 A GeV.
- The fireball acceleration is set to a_T = 0.12 c^2/fm with a lifetime of ~14 fm/c, yielding a freezeout temperature of T_fo ~ 90-95 MeV and average transverse flow <beta> ~ 0.59 for central collisions.
- The calculated direct-photon v2 reaches a maximum of ~7.5% for 0-20% centrality and ~12% for 20-40% centrality, which is a factor of 3-4 larger than existing hydrodynamic calculations and reaches into the lower end of the experimental systematic uncertainties.
- For a reduced thermalization time of tau_0 ~ 0.17 fm/c (initial temperature T_0 ~ 445 MeV), the maximal v2 drops by ~15% to ~6.3% for 0-20% centrality.
- The effective slope parameter of the total thermal spectrum in the range q_t ~ 1-3 GeV is T_eff ~ 240-250 MeV, compared to the PHENIX extracted value of 221 +/- 19 (stat) +/- 19 (syst) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous thermal-photon calculations (Turbide (2003), van Hees (2007)) by incorporating an improved fireball evolution that reproduces the sequential freezeout picture from He (2010), where multi-strange hadrons decouple at chemical freezeout while light hadrons decouple later at kinetic freezeout. The results contradict earlier hydrodynamic calculations (Chatterjee (2006), Liu (2009), Holopainen (2011)) that underpredict the measured photon v2 by a factor of ~5, and suggest that the hadronic phase plays a dominant role in direct-photon production at RHIC. The work enables refined interpretations of the PHENIX direct-photon data and motivates further experimental study of omega meson contributions to the direct-photon signal at low transverse momentum.
