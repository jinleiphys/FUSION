# Wigner function approach to polarization-vorticity coupling and hydrodynamics with spin

> Newly introduced equilibrium Wigner functions for particles with spin one-half are used in the semi-classical kinetic equations to study a possible relation between thermal vorticity and spin polarization. It is shown that in global equilibrium both the thermal-vorticity and spin polarization tensors are constant but not necessarily equal. In the case of local equilibrium, we define a procedure leading to hydrodynamic equations with spin. We introduce such equations for the de~Groot, van~Leeuwen, and van~Weert (GLW) formalism as well as for the canonical scheme (these two frameworks differ by the definitions of the energy-momentum and spin tensors). It is found that the GLW and canonical versions are connected by a pseudo-gauge transformation.

## Key claim

In global equilibrium, the thermal-vorticity tensor and the spin polarization tensor are both constant but not necessarily equal; the paper shows that no direct relation between them can be established beyond this constancy, contrary to what the abstract's phrasing "possible relation" might suggest.

## Method

The author constructs equilibrium Wigner functions for spin-one-half particles using the spin density matrices introduced by Becattini (2013) and de Groot, van Leeuwen, and van Weert (1980). These functions are inserted into the semi-classical kinetic equation (the Dirac equation for the Wigner function) and expanded to first order in \(\hbar\). The resulting equations for the scalar coefficient \(\mathcal{F}_{\rm eq}\) and the axial-vector coefficient \(\mathcal{A}^\nu_{\rm eq}\) are then used to derive constraints on the hydrodynamic fields (temperature, chemical potential, fluid velocity, and spin polarization tensor) in global and local equilibrium. Finally, the charge current, energy-momentum tensor, and spin tensor are expressed in terms of the Wigner function for both the de Groot–van Leeuwen–van Weert (GLW) formalism and the canonical (Noether) formalism.

## Key numbers

* The spin polarization tensor \(\omega_{\mu\nu}\) and the thermal vorticity \(\varpi_{\mu\nu}\) are both constant in global equilibrium, but the paper explicitly states that no conclusion can be drawn about whether \(\omega_{\mu\nu} = \varpi_{\mu\nu}\).
* The parameter \(\zeta\) is defined as \(\zeta = \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{ \frac{1}{2} \omega_{\mu\nu} \omega^{\mu\nu} }\) and is interpreted as the ratio of the spin chemical potential \(\Omega\) to the temperature \(T\).
* The canonical spin tensor is related to the GLW spin tensor by \(S^{\lambda , \mu \nu }_{\rm can} = S^{\lambda , \mu \nu }_{\rm GLW} + S^{\mu , \nu \lambda }_{\rm GLW}+ S^{\nu , \lambda \mu }_{\rm GLW}\).
* The divergence of the canonical spin tensor satisfies \(\partial_\lambda S^{\lambda , \mu \nu }_{\rm can}(x) = T^{\nu\mu}_{\rm can} - T^{\mu\nu}_{\rm can}\).

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamics-with-spin framework introduced by Florkowski (2017, 2018) and the equilibrium spin-density-matrix approach of Becattini (2013). It provides a Wigner-function derivation that connects the GLW formalism (de Groot, van Leeuwen, van Weert, 1980) and the canonical Noether formalism (Itzykson, 1980) via a pseudo-gauge transformation, showing that the two formulations are equivalent at the level of conservation laws. The results enable future development of dissipative spin hydrodynamics and clarify that spin polarization and thermal vorticity are not forced to be equal even in global equilibrium.
