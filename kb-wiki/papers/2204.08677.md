# Finite amplitude method on the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum: The isoscalar giant monopole resonance in exotic nuclei

> Finite amplitude method based on the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (DRHBc-FAM) is developed and applied to study isoscalar giant monopole resonance in exotic nuclei. Validation of the numerical implementation is examined for $^{208}\textrm{Pb}$. The isoscalar giant monopole resonances for even-even calcium isotopes from $^{40}\textrm{Ca}$ to the last bound neutron-rich nucleus $^{80}\textrm{Ca}$ are calculated, and a good agreement with the available experimental centroid energies is obtained for $^{40-48}\textrm{Ca}$. For the exotic calcium isotopes, e.g., $^{68}\textrm{Ca}$ and $^{80}\textrm{Ca}$, the DRHBc-FAM calculated results are closer to the energy weighted sum rule than the calculations on the harmonic oscillator basis, which highlights the advantages of DRHBc-FAM in describing giant resonances for exotic nuclei. In order to explore the soft monopole mode in the exotic nuclei, the giant monopole resonance for the deformed exotic nucleus $^{200}\textrm{Nd}$ is investigated, where the prolate shape and the oblate shape coexist. A soft monopole mode near 6.0 MeV is found in the prolate case, and another one near 4.5 MeV is found in the oblate case. The transition density of the soft monopole mode shows in phase or out-of-phase vibrations near the surface region, which is generated by quadrupole vibrations.

## Key claim

The finite amplitude method implemented on the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (DRHBc-FAM) provides a numerically feasible approach to describe isoscalar giant monopole resonances in exotic nuclei, with the caveat that the method's advantage over harmonic oscillator basis calculations becomes significant primarily for loosely bound nuclei where continuum effects are important.

## Method

The authors implement the finite amplitude method on the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum, solving the linear response equation iteratively without truncating the two-quasiparticle configuration space. The relativistic density functional PC-PK1 is used for the particle-hole channel with a density-dependent zero-range pairing force of strength V0 = -325 MeV, and the Dirac Woods-Saxon basis is employed with a box size of 16 fm (20 fm for heavy nuclei) and energy cutoff of 120 MeV (150 MeV for heavy nuclei). The induced Hamiltonian and density variations are computed numerically using a finite difference parameter eta = 10^-6, with iteration accelerated by the modified Broyden mixing method to a tolerance of 10^-8.

## Key numbers

- For 208Pb, the DRHBc-FAM calculation shows perfect agreement with the harmonic oscillator basis calculation in the ISGMR strength function (validation check)
- For 40Ca, calculated centroid energy is 20.79 MeV (DWS basis) compared to experimental values of 20.2 MeV (RCNP) and 19.2 MeV (TAMU)
- For 48Ca, calculated centroid energy is 19.66 MeV (DWS basis) compared to experimental values of 19.5 MeV (RCNP) and 19.9 MeV (TAMU)
- For 72Ca, 98.2% of the energy weighted sum rule is exhausted below 45 MeV with DWS basis, versus 96.8% with harmonic oscillator basis
- For 200Nd, the prolate minimum has deformation beta = 0.22 with energy -1380.43 MeV, and the oblate minimum has beta = -0.25 with energy -1380.52 MeV
- Soft monopole mode found at 6.0 MeV for the prolate case and at 4.5 MeV for the oblate case in 200Nd
- Root-mean-square radii of 200Nd are 5.90 fm for the prolate case and 5.93 fm for the oblate case

## Context

This work extends the finite amplitude method, previously implemented on Skyrme density functionals (Inakura 2009, Hinohara 2013, Kortelainen 2015) and relativistic density functionals (Liang 2013, Niksic 2013, Sun 2017), to the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum developed by Zhou (2010), Li (2012), and Zhang (2020). The method enables investigation of giant resonances in exotic nuclei where deformation, pairing, and continuum effects must be treated simultaneously, and can be applied to study soft monopole modes previously observed experimentally in 11Li (Fayans 1992) and 68Ni (Vandebrouck 2014) and predicted in neutron-rich magnesium (Pei 2014), calcium (Afannsjev 2015), nickel (Sun 2021), tin (Khan 2013), and lead (Khan 2013) isotopes.
