# Neutralization of excited antiprotonic helium ion in collisions with helium atoms

> We consider a neutralization of excited antiprotonic helium ion accompanied by antiproton transitions to lower states in collisions with helium atoms $(\bar{p}\mathrm{He}^{2+})_{nl} + \mathrm{He} \rightarrow \left[ (\bar{p}\mathrm{He}^{2+})_{n_f l_f} e\right]_{1s} + \mathrm{He}^+$ in low-temperature medium. Interactions in the input and output channels are taken similar to the potentials of $\mathrm{H}^+ - \mathrm{He}$ ($X^1Σ$) and $\mathrm{H} - \mathrm{He}^+$ ($A^1Σ$) systems, respectively. The final term is shifted down by the antiproton transition energy. Therefore initial and final terms can intersect for the suitable antiproton quantum numbers leading to an appreciable cross sections of the transitions. We have found that this process can provide an additional increase of effective annihilation rates for highly excited antiprotonic helium ion even in the low-density and temperature target corresponding to known experimental conditions.

## Key claim

The neutralization of excited antiprotonic helium ions via electron transfer from neutral helium atoms, accompanied by antiproton transitions to lower states, can provide an additional increase of effective annihilation rates for highly excited antiprotonic helium ions even in the low-density and temperature target corresponding to known experimental conditions. The process is energy-allowed for antiproton transitions with Δn ≥ 2 (minimum multipolarity Δl = 2 for circular orbits and Δl = 1 for nearly-circular orbits), unlike external Auger transitions which require Δn ≥ 4 and are suppressed by Coulomb repulsion.

## Method

The authors model the neutralization reaction (p̄He²⁺)ₙₗ + He → [(p̄He²⁺)ₙfₗf e]₁ₛ + He⁺ using potential energy curves analogous to H⁺–He (X¹Σ) for the input channel and H–He⁺ (A¹Σ) for the output channel, with the final term shifted down by the antiproton transition energy and a correction to the electron binding energy. Cross sections are estimated using the Landau-Zener approximation for transitions at the crossing points of the initial and final potential curves, with the transition matrix element approximated from the electronic dipole transition moment D(R) between the X¹Σ and A¹Σ states and hydrogen-like wave functions for the antiproton.

## Key numbers

- For initial antiprotonic states with n = 28–32 and l ≤ n−1 at target temperature T = 10 K and density ρ = 5 × 10¹⁷ cm⁻³, neutralization rates reach up to 50% of radiative transition rates.
- The minimum multipolarity for neutralization transitions from circular orbits is Δl = 2 (for Δn = 2), compared to Δl = 4 for external Auger transitions from the (30, 29) state.
- For the (30, 28 → 28, 27) dipole transition at ρ = 5 × 10¹⁷ cm⁻³, the neutralization rate shows very weak energy dependence, allowing thermal averaging to be replaced by the rate at E = T.
- The isotope effect for (p̄³He⁺) versus (p̄⁴He⁺) at n = 30, T = 10 K, ρ = 5 × 10¹⁷ cm⁻³ shows rates for ³He greater than for ⁴He, approaching 10% at l = 20.
- The observed effective annihilation cross sections for initial states are σq ∼ (4–10) × 10⁻¹⁵ cm², an order of magnitude larger than theoretical cross sections for collisional Stark transitions from previous calculations at T ∼ 10⁴ K.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of collisional quenching of antiprotonic helium ions, specifically addressing the discrepancy between experimental annihilation rates observed by the ASACUSA collaboration (Korenman and Yudin cite reviews and experimental results from the collaboration) and theoretical cross sections for Stark transitions from earlier calculations (Refs. 5–7 in the paper) which were too small by an order of magnitude. The neutralization mechanism provides an additional pathway beyond the Stark transitions considered in the close-coupling approach of Refs. 8–9 and the potential energy surface calculations of Ref. 10, and must be included together with radiative transitions in cascade calculations of effective annihilation rates for antiprotonic helium ions.
