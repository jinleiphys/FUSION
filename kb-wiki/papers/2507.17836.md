# Collapsar Disk Outflows III: Detectable Neutrino and Gravitational Wave Signatures

> We investigate the neutrino and gravitational wave (GW) signals from accretion disks formed during the failed collapse of a rotating massive star (a collapsar). Following black hole formation, a neutrino-cooled, shocked accretion disk forms, which displays non-spherical oscillations for a period of seconds before becoming advective and exploding the star. We compute the neutrino and GW signals (matter quadrupole, frequencies $\lesssim 100$ Hz) from collapsar disks using global axisymmetric, viscous hydrodynamic simulations. The neutrino signal with typical energies of O$(10)$ MeV is maximal during the neutrino-cooled (NDAF) phase that follows shock formation. This phase lasts for a few seconds and is easily detectable within O$(10-100)$ kpc by the IceCube Neutrino Telescope. Additional neutrino signatures from a precursor equatorial shock and by stochastic accretion plumes during the advective phase are detectable within the galaxy. The GW signal during the NDAF phase is detectable in the galaxy by current and next-generation ground-based observatories. The explosion (memory) GW signal is similar to that of standard core-collapse supernovae and can be probed with a deci-Hertz space-based detector. Shock oscillations during the NDAF phase impart time variations with frequency O$(10-100)$ Hz to the neutrino and GW signals, encoding information about the shock dynamics and inner disk. These time variations can be detectable in neutrinos by IceCube within O$(1-10)$ kpc depending on progenitor model, flavor transformation scenario, and detailed properties of the angular momentum transport mechanism.

## Key claim

The neutrino and gravitational wave signals from collapsar accretion disks are detectable within the galaxy and nearby satellite galaxies, but the detectability depends strongly on the progenitor model, flavor transformation scenario, and the detailed properties of the angular momentum transport mechanism, with the neutrino-cooled (NDAF) phase being the most promising for multi-messenger detection.

## Method

The authors post-processed global axisymmetric, viscous hydrodynamic simulations (from Paper I) that include gray, 3-species neutrino transport through a leakage scheme with lightbulb-type absorption, a 19-isotope nuclear reaction network, and Newtonian self-gravity with a pseudo-Newtonian black hole potential. They extracted neutrino luminosities and mean energies for all flavors and computed the gravitational wave emission from the matter quadrupole moment, then used SNOwGLoBES through the SNEWPY software package to calculate IceCube event rates for different flavor transformation scenarios (unoscillated, adiabatic MSW, and full swap).

## Key numbers

- Neutrino signal during the NDAF phase has typical energies of O(10) MeV and is easily detectable within O(10-100) kpc by IceCube
- Time variations in the neutrino and GW signals have frequencies O(10-100) Hz and can be detectable by IceCube within O(1-10) kpc depending on progenitor model, flavor transformation scenario, and angular momentum transport properties
- The GW displacement during the NDAF phase is Dh_+ ~ 5 cm, comparable to the GW signal induced by SASI motions in the PNS accretion phase of CCSNe
- The GW memory signal during the ADAF phase reaches Dh_+ ~ 800 cm over a timescale of ~200 s
- The baseline model 16TI-SFHo has BH formation at 2.72 s post-bounce
- The NDAF phase takes place over the interval 11-13 s post-bounce for the baseline model
- The background rate for IceCube is 286 s^-1 for the 4,800 standard DOMs and 1.25 x 286 s^-1 for the 360 DeepCore DOMs, with 250 microsecond dead time after every count
- The high-viscosity model (alpha=0.10) does not experience an NDAF phase
- The low-viscosity model (alpha=0.01) has an NDAF phase longer than the baseline model

## Context

This work extends previous semi-analytic studies of MeV neutrino detectability from collapsar disks by Liu (2016) and GW emission modeling by Ott (2011), Kotake (2012), Cerda-Duran (2013), and Gottlieb (2024), but for the first time uses global time-dependent simulations with relevant microphysics to assess detectability. The shock oscillations during the NDAF phase are analogous to the Standing Accretion Shock Instability (SASI) in core-collapse supernovae studied by Blondin (2003) and Foglizzo (2007), but with a different underlying instability mechanism. This work enables future searches for correlated neutrino and gravitational wave signals from collapsars and provides predictions for IceCube and current/next-generation ground-based GW observatories.
