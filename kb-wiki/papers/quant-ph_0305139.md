# Comment on ``Polynomial-Time Simulation of Pairing Models on a Quantum Computer''

> Comment on the Letter ``Polynomial-Time Simulation of Pairing Models on a Quantum Computer'', L. A. Wu, M. S. Byrd and D. A. Lidar, Phys. Rev. Lett. 89, 057904 (2002).

# Comment on “Polynomial-Time Simulation of Pairing Models on a Quantum Computer”

## Key claim
The polynomial-time quantum algorithm proposed by Wu, Byrd, and Lidar (2002) is not necessary for solving pairing Hamiltonians, because classical analytical (Bethe ansatz) and numerical (density matrix renormalization group) methods already achieve any required precision with comparable or lower computational cost, and the quantum algorithm’s advantage only emerges for Hilbert spaces far larger than those accessible to near-term quantum computers.

## Method
The authors compare the computational scaling of the proposed quantum polynomial-time algorithm (PTA) against two existing classical approaches: the exact Bethe ansatz solution for integrable pairing models, and the density matrix renormalization group (DMRG) method for both integrable and nonintegrable models. They analyze the DMRG infinite algorithm applied to the reduced BCS pairing Hamiltonian, where the number of iterations scales as N/2 (with N single-particle levels) and memory usage scales as O(3 m² N) for m retained states, and contrast this with the ~N⁴ steps of the quantum PTA. They also present the general form of exactly solvable pairing Hamiltonians via Richardson’s method, parameterized by 6N+3 free parameters out of the 2N²−N possible.

## Key numbers
- DMRG achieved ground state energies for systems with N ~ 400 single-particle levels and Hilbert space dimensions ~ 10¹³⁰ with 5 significant figures of accuracy (Dukelsky et al., 2001).
- For N = 100, the exact ground state energy was obtained to 7 significant figures with moderate computational effort (Table I of Dukelsky et al., 2001).
- The DMRG infinite algorithm requires N/2 iterations, compared to ~N⁴ computational steps for the quantum PTA of Wu et al. (2002).
- With m ~ 10² retained states, DMRG memory usage of O(3 m² N) allows treatment of systems with N ~ 10³ single-particle levels.
- The most general pairing Hamiltonian has 2N²−N free parameters; the integrable subset has 6N+3 free parameters.

## Context
This comment extends the critique of quantum advantage claims for pairing models by pointing to the existing classical DMRG work of Dukelsky et al. (2001) on ultrasmall superconducting grains, which already solved systems with N ~ 400 to high precision. It also recalls the exact Richardson solution from the 1960s (Richardson, 1963) and its recent generalization to a wide class of pairing Hamiltonians (Dukelsky et al., 2001). The comment enables a more realistic assessment of when quantum computers might offer a genuine advantage: only for nonintegrable pairing Hamiltonians that cannot be solved by Bethe ansatz and whose Hilbert space dimensions exceed the reach of DMRG, which the authors argue is not yet the case.
