# Measuring the speed of sound using cumulants of baryon number

> We show that the values of the first three cumulants of the baryon number distribution can be used to calculate the isothermal speed of sound and its logarithmic derivative with respect to the baryon number density. We discuss applications of this result to heavy-ion collision experiments and address possible challenges, including effects due to baryon number conservation, differences between proton and baryon cumulants, and the influence of finite number statistics on fluctuation observables in both experiment and hadronic transport simulations. In particular, we investigate the relation between quantities calculated in infinite, continuous matter and observables obtained in simulations using a finite number of particles.

# Measuring the speed of sound using cumulants of baryon number

## Key claim
The first three cumulants of the baryon number distribution can be used to calculate the isothermal speed of sound and its logarithmic derivative with respect to baryon number density, but only in the regime where the baryon chemical potential is large compared to temperature (mu_B/T >> 1). The approximations are valid for small to moderate temperatures (T less than or approximately 100 MeV) and moderate to high baryon chemical potentials (mu_B greater than or approximately 600 MeV) for the speed of sound formula, and for mu_B greater than or approximately 200 MeV for the logarithmic derivative formula.

## Method
The authors derive thermodynamic relations connecting the isothermal speed of sound squared c_T^2 to cumulants of the baryon number distribution using Maxwell's relations and the definition of cumulants as kappa_j = V T^(j-1) (d^j P / d mu_B^j)_T. They then apply these relations to experimental data from the STAR and HADES collaborations on net baryon number cumulants and chemical freeze-out parameters from heavy-ion collisions at various collision energies, comparing the results to ideal gas predictions and the vector density functional (VDF) model.

## Key numbers
- The speed of sound squared c_T^2 extracted from experimental data at large mu_B (approximately 800 MeV) is approximately 0.04, about half of the corresponding ideal gas value.
- The quantity 1 - kappa_3 kappa_1 / kappa_2^2, which relates to the logarithmic derivative of c_T^2, reaches approximately 2.3 at mu_B approximately 800 MeV (from collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.4 GeV), indicating a sharp increase in c_T^2 with baryon number density.
- A more recent analysis by Vovchenko (2022) of proton number cumulants at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.4 GeV obtains c_T^2 approximately 0.0086 plus or minus 0.0004 and d ln c_T^2 / d ln n_B approximately 5.2 plus or minus 0.8.
- The region of validity for the speed of sound approximation is T less than or approximately 100 MeV and mu_B greater than or approximately 600 MeV; for the logarithmic derivative approximation, mu_B greater than or approximately 200 MeV.
- The saturation density of nuclear matter n_0 is approximately 0.16 fm^(-3).

## Context
This work extends the study of the speed of sound in dense nuclear matter, which has been investigated through neutron star data analyses by Bedaque (2014), Tews (2018), Annala (2019), and others suggesting c_s^2 may exceed the conformal limit of 1/3 at moderate and high baryon densities. It complements previous heavy-ion collision constraints on c_s^2: Gardim (2019) estimated c_s^2 = 0.24 plus or minus 0.04 at near-zero chemical potential using entropy density and temperature scaling; Steinheimer (2012) predicted a minimum of c_s^2 in the collision energy range sqrt(s_NN) = 4-9 GeV using Landau model and hybrid simulations; and Oliinychenko (2022) constrained c_s^2 within 0.47 plus or minus 0.12 for n_B in (2,3)n_0 and within -0.08 plus or minus 0.14 for n_B in (3,4)n_0 using Bayesian analysis of flow observables. The method enables future extraction of the speed of sound from cumulant measurements in heavy-ion collisions, with planned hadronic transport simulations to evaluate applicability to dynamical systems and to develop appropriate corrections for finite volume, baryon number conservation, and differences between proton and baryon cumulants.
