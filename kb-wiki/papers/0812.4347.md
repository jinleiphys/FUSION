# Role of unphysical solution in nucleon QCD sum rules

> We show that at certain values of QCD condensates the nucleon QCD sum rules with "pole+continuum" model for the hadron spectrum obtain an unphysical solution. This provides constrains for the values of condensates to be consistent with existence of a physical solutions. The constrains become much weaker if the radiative corrections are included perturbatively. We demonstrate that the most important dependence of nucleon mass on the quark scalar condensate becomes much weaker under factorization assumption for the four-quark and six-quark condensates.

## Key claim

The nucleon QCD sum rules with the "pole+continuum" model can yield an unphysical solution (where the continuum contribution exceeds that of the pole) for certain values of QCD condensates, and this unphysical solution can even become the absolute minimum of the chi-squared function, potentially masking the physical solution; the constraints on condensate values that allow a physical solution become much weaker when radiative corrections of order alpha_s are included perturbatively.

## Method

The authors solve the Borel-transformed QCD sum rules for the nucleon (proton) by minimizing a chi-squared function (Eq. 6) over the three unknown parameters: nucleon mass m, residue lambda^2, and continuum threshold W^2, within the Borel window 0.8 GeV^2 < M^2 < 1.4 GeV^2. They use the Ioffe current (t = -1) and vary the dimensionless parameters f_i that scale the quark condensate (f_{2q}), gluon condensate (f_b), four-quark condensate (f_{4q}), and six-quark condensate (f_{6q}) relative to their standard values, both with and without perturbative alpha_s corrections.

## Key numbers

*   With standard condensate values and including leading radiative corrections (anomalous dimensions), the physical solution gives m = 0.931 GeV, lambda^2 = 1.86 GeV^6, W^2 = 2.09 GeV^2 (Eq. 20).
*   Without any radiative corrections (L=1), the physical solution gives m = 0.930 GeV, lambda^2 = 1.79 GeV^6, W^2 = 2.00 GeV^2 (Eq. 21).
*   An unphysical solution (continuum dominates pole) appears at m = 0.6 GeV, lambda^2 = 0.79 GeV^6, W^2 = 1.0 GeV^2 when only condensates up to dimension 8 are included (Eq. 22); at M^2 = 1 GeV^2 the continuum-to-pole ratio is 2 for Pi^q and 1.75 for Pi^I.
*   With perturbative alpha_s corrections included, the physical solution becomes m = 0.94 GeV, lambda^2 = 2.00 GeV^6, W^2 = 1.90 GeV^2 (Eq. 26), while the unphysical solution becomes m = 0.60 GeV, lambda^2 = 0.56 GeV^6, W^2 = 0.70 GeV^2, with the pole contribution three times smaller than the continuum.
*   The physical solution (with anomalous dimensions only) disappears for gluon condensate scaling factor f_b > 1.04, for four-quark condensate scaling factor f_{4q} > 1.01, and for quark condensate scaling factor f_{2q} < 0.99.
*   Under the factorization assumption (f_{4q}=f_{2q}^2, f_{6q}=f_{2q}^3), the physical solution vanishes for f_{2q} > 1.35.
*   With perturbative alpha_s corrections, the physical solution exists for a broader range: f_b < 1.8, f_{4q} < 1.35, and f_{6q} > 0.2.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier studies (Drukarev et al., 2007, 2008) on unphysical solutions in nucleon QCD sum rules, and builds upon the foundational nucleon sum rules framework of Ioffe (1981) and the improved analysis of Ioffe and Smilga (1984). The results show that the existence of a physical solution (pole-dominated) imposes constraints on the allowed values of QCD condensates, which are relevant for ongoing QCD sum rules analyses of multiquark systems such as pentaquarks (reviewed by Burkert and Riska, 2005) and light tetraquarks (Britton et al., 2005), where pole dominance has been difficult to achieve. The finding that perturbative radiative corrections stabilize the physical solution and weaken the condensate constraints provides guidance for future sum rules calculations.
