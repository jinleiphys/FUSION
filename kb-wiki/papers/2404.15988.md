# Symmetries in particle physics: from nuclear isospin to the quark model

> We present a concise pedagogic introduction to group representation theory motivated by the historical developments surrounding the advent of the Eightfold Way. Abstract definitions of groups and representations are avoided in favour of the physical intuition of symmetries of the nuclear interaction. The concept of nuclear isospin is used as a physical motivation to introduce SU(2) and discuss the main techniques of representation theory. The discovery of strange particles motivates extending the symmetry group to SU(3), at first in the context of the Sakata model. We highlight the successes in fitting mesons in the SU(3) octet, discuss the drawbacks of the Sakata model for baryonic classifications, and how the Eightfold Way finally led to the quark model. This approach has two major advantages: (i) the main concepts of the theory of Lie groups are introduced and discussed without ever losing touch with its applications in particle physics; (ii) it allows the beginner to study group theory while also becoming acquainted with the historical developments of particle physics that led to the concept of quarks. In particular, in this pedagogical path the quarks appear as yet another class of particles predicted from symmetry principles, rather than being introduced ad hoc for postulating an SU(3) symmetry, as usually done in the literature.

## Key claim

This pedagogical paper argues that group representation theory for particle physics can be taught more effectively by following the historical development from nuclear isospin through the Eightfold Way to the quark model, rather than by introducing abstract group definitions or using the Standard Model gauge groups as motivation. The authors claim that in this approach quarks appear as particles predicted from symmetry principles rather than being introduced ad hoc for postulating an SU(3) symmetry.

## Method

The paper develops SU(2) representation theory starting from Heisenberg's nuclear isospin concept, using the approximate charge independence of the nuclear force and the near-degenerate masses of protons and neutrons as physical motivation. It then extends the symmetry group to SU(3) by introducing the Sakata model for strange particles, discusses its failure for baryon classification, and shows how the Eightfold Way resolved these issues and led to the quark model. The authors avoid formal group definitions, instead building representations through infinitesimal transformations and raising/lowering operators.

## Context

This work extends the pedagogical approach found in textbooks such as Halzen and Martin (1984) and Griffiths (2008), which discuss the physics without detailed group theory, and Aitchison and Hey (2004), which emphasize group theory for Standard Model applications. It also builds on the historical treatment of Gasiorowicz (1966), written before the quark model was fully confirmed. The paper enables beginners to transition smoothly to standard texts on group theory for particle physicists, including Georgi (1999), Zee (2016), and Thyssen and Ceulemans (2017).
