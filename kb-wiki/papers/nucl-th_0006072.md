# Correlations and the Dirac Structure of the Nucleon Self-Energy

> The Dirac structure of the nucleon self-energy in symmetric nuclear matter as well as neutron matter is derived from a realistic meson exchange model for the nucleon-nucleon (NN) interaction. It is demonstrated that the effects of correlations on the effective NN interaction in the nuclear medium can be parameterized by means of an effective meson exchange. This analysis leads to a very intuitive interpretation of correlation effects and also provides an efficient parametrization of an effective interaction to be used in relativistic structure calculations for finite nuclei.

# Correlations and the Dirac Structure of the Nucleon Self-Energy

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the ambiguities inherent in the standard projection method for extracting the Dirac structure of the nucleon self-energy from the Brueckner G-matrix can be minimised by splitting the G-matrix into the bare interaction V (whose Dirac structure is known) and a correction term ΔG representing correlation effects, which can then be parameterised as an effective meson exchange with zero range. This approach yields a nearly momentum-independent correction to the self-energy and provides a parametrisation that may be useful for Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock studies of finite nuclei, including those with unequal neutron and proton numbers.

## Method

The authors solve the relativistic Bethe-Goldstone equation for symmetric nuclear matter and neutron matter using three versions of the Bonn one-boson-exchange potential (Bonn A, B, and C). They decompose the resulting G-matrix into the bare interaction V and the correlation correction ΔG, then apply the projection method using Fermi covariants only to ΔG, while the Dirac structure of V is evaluated directly. The antisymmetrised scalar and vector amplitudes extracted from ΔG are used to compute the scalar (Σ^s), time-like vector (Σ^0), and space-like vector (Σ^v) components of the nucleon self-energy, from which the effective Dirac mass M* and single-particle energies are obtained.

## Key numbers

- At Fermi momentum k_F = 1.36 fm^{-1} (empirical saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter), the bare π-exchange contribution to the scalar self-energy Σ^s from the projection method reaches approximately 600 MeV in absolute value, implying an effective Dirac mass M* as small as 340 MeV; direct evaluation of the Bonn B potential yields Σ^s of order 15 MeV with opposite sign.
- The bare NN interaction V (Bonn B) yields scalar and vector self-energy components ranging from -420 MeV to -400 MeV for nucleon momenta k_1/k_F between 0 and 1 at k_F = 1.36 fm^{-1}, producing no binding energy.
- For neutron matter at density 0.4 fm^{-3} (approximately 2.5 times saturation density), the simple analysis of the single-particle energy underestimates the binding energy by 10 percent compared to the full projection method using Bonn A.
- The effective coupling constants for the zero-range parametrisation of ΔG are presented as dimensionless quantities multiplied by m_σ^2/(4π) with m_σ = 550 MeV, and are largest for the vector-isoscalar channel (g_{v,s}), reflecting stronger suppression of ω exchange by correlations.

## Context

This work extends the Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) approach of Brockmann and Machleidt (1990) and the projection method of Horowitz and Serot (1987) and Boersma and Malfliet (1994). It addresses the known ambiguity that the projection method yields different Dirac structures depending on whether pseudo-scalar or pseudo-vector coupling is assumed for pion exchange, as previously discussed by Serot and Walecka (1986) and Furnstahl et al. (1992). The authors show that the simpler method of Brockmann and Machleidt, which extracts the effective mass from the momentum dependence of the single-particle energy, works accidentally well for symmetric nuclear matter but fails for neutron matter, confirming the observation of Ulrych et al. (1997). The resulting parametrisation of correlation effects as effective meson exchange with zero range enables future DBHF calculations for finite nuclei with N=Z and N≠Z.
