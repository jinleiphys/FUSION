# How many sigmas is the solar neutrino effect?

> The minimal standard electroweak model can be tested by allowing all the solar neutrino fluxes, with undistorted energy spectra, to be free parameters in fitting the measured solar neutrino event rates, subject only to the condition that the total observed luminosity of the sun is produced by nuclear fusion. The rates of the five experiments prior to SNO (chlorine, Kamiokande, SAGE, GALLEX, Super-Kamiokande) cannot be fit by an arbitrary choice of undistorted neutrino fluxes at the level of 2.5 sigma (formally 99% C.L.). Considering just SNO and Super-Kamiokande, the discrepancy is at the 3.3 sigma level(10^{-3} C.L.). If all six experiments are fit simultaneously, the formal discrepancy increases to 4 sigma (7*10^{-5} C.L.). If the relative scaling in temperature of the nuclear reactions that produce 7Be and 8B neutrinos is taken into account, the formal discrepancy is at the 7.4 sigma level.

## Key claim

The paper quantifies the statistical significance with which the no-oscillation hypothesis (minimal standard electroweak model with undistorted neutrino energy spectra) fails to fit the combined solar neutrino event rates, showing that including all six experiments (not just SNO and Super-Kamiokande) increases the formal rejection level from 3.3 sigma to 4.0 sigma, and that imposing temperature scaling of nuclear reactions raises the discrepancy to 7.4 sigma.

## Method

The author performed chi-squared minimization fits to the measured solar neutrino event rates from chlorine, Kamiokande, SAGE, GALLEX/GNO, Super-Kamiokande, and SNO experiments, treating the seven important neutrino fluxes (pp, pep, hep, 7Be, 8B, 13N, 15O) as free parameters with undistorted energy spectra, subject only to the solar luminosity constraint. The pep flux was fixed relative to the pp flux at the ratio (2.25 ± 0.1) × 10^{-3} based on weak interaction theory, and supplementary conditions on hep and CNO neutrino fluxes were varied to test robustness. The effective number of standard deviations was computed from the chi-squared probability distribution using an asymptotic formula that is exact for one degree of freedom.

## Key numbers

- Pre-SNO era (five experiments: chlorine, Kamiokande, SAGE, GALLEX, Super-Kamiokande): no-oscillation hypothesis rejected at 2.5 sigma (99% C.L.)
- Water Cherenkov experiments only (SNO and Super-Kamiokande): discrepancy at 3.35 sigma (P = 8 × 10^{-4})
- All six experiments fit simultaneously: formal discrepancy at 4.0 sigma (P = 7 × 10^{-5})
- With temperature scaling of 7Be and 8B neutrino production imposed: rejection at 7.4 sigma (P = 1 × 10^{-13})
- Best-fit 8B neutrino flux for all six experiments: 0.4314 times the BP2000 standard solar model flux
- Best-fit pp neutrino flux for all six experiments: 1.0917 times the BP2000 flux
- Best-fit 7Be neutrino flux required to be identically zero in all free-flux fits
- SNO experiment contributed 56% of total chi-squared in the six-experiment fit

## Context

This work extends the pre-SNO free-flux analyses by Hata, Bludman, and Langacker (1994), Parke (1995), and Heeger and Robinson (1996), which had already demonstrated the inadequacy of no-oscillation fits to solar neutrino data. The paper shows that including the SNO measurement increases the statistical significance of the discrepancy by an order of magnitude compared to the pre-SNO era, and that imposing the physically motivated temperature scaling of nuclear reactions (derived from Ulmer's one-zone model and consistent with detailed Monte Carlo studies by Bahcall and Ulrich) raises the rejection to 7.4 sigma. The results enable the community to move beyond the question of whether neutrino oscillations occur to detailed studies of oscillation parameters, and the author notes that the formal statistical estimates should not be taken literally at extremely small probabilities due to likely effects of unknown systematic errors.
