# Proton electron elastic scattering and the proton charge radius

> It is suggested that proton elastic scattering on atomic electrons allows a precise measurement of the proton charge radius. Very small values of transferred momenta (up to four order of magnitude smaller than the ones presently available) can be reached with high probability.

## Key claim

Proton elastic scattering on atomic electrons can reach values of four-momentum transfer squared up to four orders of magnitude smaller than those accessible in conventional electron-proton scattering, enabling a precise determination of the proton charge radius by reducing the extrapolation uncertainty to \(Q^2=0\).

## Method

The authors derive the differential cross section for unpolarized proton-electron elastic scattering in the laboratory frame where the electron is at rest, using the one-photon-exchange approximation and including the full dependence on the electron mass. They analyze the kinematics of the inverse reaction, showing that the maximum four-momentum transfer squared is proportional to the electron mass squared, and compute the angular and energy distributions of the scattered proton and recoil electron for a 100 MeV proton beam.

## Key numbers

- For a proton beam with kinetic energy \(E_p = 100\) MeV, the maximum four-momentum transfer squared is \((Q^2)_{\text{max}} = 0.2 \times 10^{-6}\) GeV\(^2\).
- The electric contribution to the cross section dominates the magnetic one by a factor of approximately \(10^7\) over the entire allowed \(Q^2\) range.
- The maximum scattering angle for the proton is \(\sin\theta_{h,\text{max}} = m/M = 0.544 \times 10^{-3}\).
- The differential cross section integrated over the electron angular range \(10^\circ \le \theta_e \le 80^\circ\) is \(25 \times 10^4\) mb.
- At a luminosity of \(\mathcal{L} = 10^{32}\) cm\(^{-2}\) s\(^{-1}\) with 100% detector efficiency, approximately \(25 \times 10^9\) events could be collected per second.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of proton-electron scattering kinematics and observables from Gakh et al. (2011) and applies it to the proton radius puzzle, where the muonic hydrogen result of \(r_c = 0.84184(67)\) fm from Pohl et al. (2010) disagrees by five standard deviations with the CODATA value \(r_c = 0.8768(69)\) fm. The proposed method could improve upon the smallest \(Q^2\) value of 0.004 GeV\(^2\) reached in the electron-proton scattering experiment of Bernauer et al. (2010) by four orders of magnitude, potentially resolving the discrepancy through a direct measurement that avoids the large extrapolation uncertainties inherent in conventional Rosenbluth separation.
