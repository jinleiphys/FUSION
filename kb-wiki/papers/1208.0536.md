# Inverse magnetic catalysis in field theory and gauge-gravity duality

> We investigate the surface of the chiral phase transition in the three-dimensional parameter space of temperature, baryon chemical potential and magnetic field in two different approaches, the field-theoretical Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model and the holographic Sakai-Sugimoto model. The latter is a top-down approach to a gravity dual of QCD with an asymptotically large number of colors and becomes, in a certain limit, dual to an NJL-like model. Our main observation is that, at nonzero chemical potential, a magnetic field can restore chiral symmetry, in apparent contrast to the phenomenon of magnetic catalysis. This "inverse magnetic catalysis" occurs in the Sakai-Sugimoto model and, for sufficiently large coupling, in the NJL model and is related to the physics of the lowest Landau level. While in most parts our discussion is a pedagogical review of previously published results, we include new analytical results for the NJL approach and a thorough comparison of inverse magnetic catalysis in the two approaches.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that, at nonzero baryon chemical potential, a magnetic field can *restore* chiral symmetry (inverse magnetic catalysis) in both the Sakai-Sugimoto holographic model and, for sufficiently large coupling, the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, in apparent contrast to the well-known phenomenon of magnetic catalysis where a magnetic field enhances chiral symmetry breaking.

## Method

The authors compute the chiral phase transition surface in the three-dimensional parameter space of temperature, baryon chemical potential, and magnetic field using two approaches: the field-theoretical NJL model with proper time regularization and the holographic Sakai-Sugimoto model, which is a top-down gravity dual of QCD with an asymptotically large number of colors. In the NJL model, they solve the gap equation for the constituent quark mass derived from the mean-field thermodynamic potential, including Landau level quantization of fermions in the magnetic field. In the Sakai-Sugimoto model, they analyze the geometric realization of chiral symmetry breaking via the connection of flavor D-branes and anti-D-branes in the bulk, mapping out the critical surface analytically in the limit of small asymptotic separation of the flavor branes.

## Key numbers

- The critical coupling for chiral symmetry breaking at zero magnetic field and zero chemical potential is \(g > 1\) (dimensionless coupling \(g \equiv G\Lambda^2/(2\pi^2)\)), with multi-valued solutions appearing above \(g_0 \simeq 1.390\) and first-order transitions above \(g \simeq 2.106\)
- At zero temperature and zero chemical potential, the dynamical mass in the weak-coupling limit with magnetic field is \(M \simeq \sqrt{|q|B/\pi} \, e^{-\pi^2/(|q|B G)}\)
- The critical temperature for a second-order chiral phase transition at nonzero chemical potential is \(T_c(\mu)/\Lambda = \sqrt{3/(2\pi^2)} \sqrt{1 - 1/g - 2\mu^2/\Lambda^2}\)
- The axial current along the magnetic field for massless quarks at any temperature is \(\langle j^3_5\rangle = qB\mu/(2\pi^2)\)
- The critical chemical potential at zero temperature and zero magnetic field is \(\mu_0(g)/\Lambda = (1/\sqrt{2})\sqrt{1 - 1/g}\)

## Context

This work extends and compares previous studies of magnetic catalysis in the NJL model (Gusynin 1994, 1995) and holographic approaches (Filev 2007, Erdmenger 2007) to the regime of nonzero chemical potential, where inverse magnetic catalysis is observed. The results are contrasted with lattice QCD findings by the Budapest-Wuppertal collaboration (Bali 2011) that showed a drop in quark susceptibility at temperatures around 140 MeV under magnetic fields, suggesting that magnetic catalysis may not persist at higher temperatures in real QCD. The paper provides new analytical results for the NJL approach and a thorough comparison between the field-theoretical and holographic descriptions, enabling future work on understanding what prevents magnetic catalysis at larger temperatures in QCD.
