# Event-by-event fluctuations in collective quantities

> We discuss an event-by-event fluctuation analysis of particle production in heavy ion collisions. We compare different approaches to the evaluation of the event-by-event dynamical fluctuations in quantities defined on groups of particles, such quantities as mean transverse momentum, transverse momentum spectra slope, strength of anisotropic flow, etc.. The direct computation of the dynamical fluctuations and the sub-event method are discussed in more detail. We also show how the fluctuation in different variables can be related to each other.

# Event-by-event fluctuations in collective quantities

## Key claim

Event-by-event fluctuations in collective quantities such as mean transverse momentum or temperature can be extracted using either a direct method or a sub-event method, but the heat capacity of the system cannot be reliably measured from temperature fluctuations derived from transverse momentum spectra alone, contrary to earlier proposals by Stodolsky (1995) and Shuryak (1998). The sub-event method offers a practical way to eliminate certain dynamical correlations (e.g., HBT correlations) that contaminate the direct approach.

## Method

The authors compare two approaches for extracting dynamical event-by-event fluctuations from heavy-ion collision data. In the direct method, the total variance of a collective quantity X (such as mean transverse momentum or temperature) is decomposed into statistical and dynamical components, where the statistical part is calculated assuming independent particle production. The sub-event method divides each event into two independent subsystems (e.g., separated in rapidity) and computes the correlation between the same observable measured in each sub-event; this correlation directly yields the dynamical fluctuation component while automatically removing contributions from short-range correlations like Bose-Einstein correlations. The relationship between these methods and the Phi_x variable used in earlier work by Gazdzicki and Mrowczynski (1992) is also derived.

## Key numbers

* For a two-dimensional nonrelativistic ideal gas, the statistical fluctuation in temperature T satisfies (Delta T)^2_stat / T^2 = 1/M, where M is the event multiplicity.
* In a toy Monte Carlo with 4000 events of multiplicity M=1000, temperature fluctuations of delta=0.03 (giving sigma^2_dynam/T^2 = 0.75 x 10^-4) and delta=0.1 (giving sigma^2_dynam/T^2 = 0.833 x 10^-3) were successfully reconstructed.
* Using the sub-event method on the toy model with delta=0.1, the reconstructed dynamical fluctuation was sigma^2_T,dynam = (0.205 +/- 0.011) x 10^-5 GeV^2, compared to the input value of 0.208 x 10^-5 GeV^2.
* From the sub-event method using RMS values of distributions, sigma^2_T,dynam was estimated as ((4.33)^2 - (3.21)^2) x 10^-6 / 4 = 0.211 x 10^-5 GeV^2.
* For a two-dimensional ideal gas of M particles, C_V = M, while for a three-dimensional ideal gas using only two momentum components, C_V = 3M/2, demonstrating that the same measured fluctuations correspond to different heat capacities.
* The NA49 Collaboration (1998) established a limit on event-by-event fluctuations of mean transverse momentum as sigma_mpt / <mpt> < 0.01, which the authors translate to sigma_T / <T> < 0.02, compared to statistical fluctuations of about 0.07 for <M> approx 250.

## Context

This paper extends the discussion of event-by-event fluctuation analysis in heavy-ion collisions, building on proposals by Stodolsky (1995) and Shuryak (1998) to extract heat capacity from temperature fluctuations, and on the Phi_x variable introduced by Gazdzicki and Mrowczynski (1992). The authors critically examine the feasibility of measuring C_V from transverse momentum fluctuations, concluding that the heat capacity cannot be determined this way because the measured fluctuations depend only on the degrees of freedom actually observed (e.g., two momentum components) rather than the full system. The sub-event method is presented as a practical improvement over the direct approach used by the NA49 Collaboration (1998), allowing cleaner separation of event-by-event fluctuations from other dynamical correlations. The work enables future experimental searches for phase transitions by measuring excitation functions of mean transverse momentum fluctuations, even if absolute heat capacity extraction is not possible.
