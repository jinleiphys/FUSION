# New Way to Resum the Lattice QCD Taylor Series Equation of State at Finite Chemical Potential

> Taylor expansion of the thermodynamic potential in powers of the (baryo)chemical potential $μ_B$ is a well-known method to bypass the Sign Problem of Lattice QCD. Due to the difficulty in calculating the higher order Taylor coefficients, various alternative expansion schemes as well as resummation techniques have been suggested to extend the Taylor series to larger values of $μ_B$. Recently, a way to resum the contribution of the first $N$ charge density correlation functions $D_1,\dots,D_N$ to the Taylor series to all orders in $μ_B$ was proposed in Phys. Rev. Lett. 128, 2, 022001 (2022). The resummation takes the form of an exponential factor. Since the correlation functions are calculated stochastically, the exponential factor contains a bias which can be significant for large $N$ and $μ_B$. In this paper, we present a new method to calculate the QCD equation of state based on the well-known cumulant expansion from statistics. By truncating the expansion at a maximum order $M$, we end up with only finite products of the correlation functions which can be evaluated in an unbiased manner. Although our formalism is also applicable for $μ_B\ne0$, here we present it for the simpler case of a finite isospin chemical potential $μ_I$ for which there is no Sign Problem. We present and compare results for the pressure and the isospin density obtained using Taylor expansion, exponential resummation and cumulant expansion, and provide evidence that the absence of bias in the latter actually improves the convergence.

## Key claim

The paper presents a cumulant expansion method that truncates the all-orders exponential resummation of the first \(N\) charge density correlation functions at a finite order \(M\), thereby allowing unbiased stochastic estimation of the QCD equation of state; the authors provide evidence that the bias present in the exponential resummation (due to stochastic estimation of the correlation functions) can be significant for large chemical potential and large \(N\), and that the unbiased cumulant expansion actually improves convergence.

## Method

The authors apply the cumulant expansion from statistics to the logarithm of the partition function ratio, truncating at order \(M\) to obtain an expression involving only finite products of the charge density correlation functions \(D_{2n}^I\). These finite products are evaluated using unbiased estimators based on Girard-Newton identities, which avoid the bias inherent in exponentiating stochastically estimated quantities. The method is demonstrated for the case of finite isospin chemical potential \(\mu_I\) (where there is no sign problem) using 2+1-flavor HISQ gauge configurations from the HotQCD collaboration with \(\Nt=8\) at \(T=135\) MeV, comparing results for pressure and isospin density from Taylor expansion, exponential resummation, and cumulant expansion.

## Key numbers

* The calculations use approximately 20,000 gauge configurations with \(\Nt=8\) at \(T=135\) MeV.
* On each configuration, the first eight derivatives \(D_1^f,\dots,D_8^f\) were estimated stochastically using around 2000 Gaussian random volume sources for \(D_1^f\) and around 500 sources for the rest.
* Results are presented for the range \(0 \leqslant |\mu_I/T| \leqslant 2\).
* The cumulant expansion with \((N,M)=(2,4)\) and \((N,M)=(4,4)\) is compared to Taylor expansions up to order 6 and exponential resummations with \(N=2\) and \(N=4\).
* Resummed results were obtained using \(\Nrv=500\) and \(\Nrv=250\) random vectors; the \(\Nrv=250\) results lie further from Taylor and unbiased cumulant results for \(|\mu_I/T| \gtrsim 1\).

## Context

This work extends the exponential resummation approach proposed by Mondal et al. (2022) in Phys. Rev. Lett. 128, 022001, which resums the contribution of the first \(N\) charge density correlation functions to all orders in \(\mu_B\) but suffers from bias when the correlation functions are estimated stochastically. The cumulant expansion provides a finite-order truncation that can be evaluated without bias, and the authors demonstrate that bias (rather than genuine higher-order contributions) is responsible for discrepancies between the resummed and Taylor series results. The method is applicable to baryon chemical potential \(\mu_B\) as well, and the authors note connections to reweighting-based approaches and the alternative expansion scheme of Borsanyi et al. (2021).
