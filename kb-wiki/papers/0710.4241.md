# BCS-BEC crossover of neutron pairs in symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter

> We propose new types of density dependent contact pairing interaction which reproduce the pairing gaps in symmetric and neutron matter obtained by a microscopic treatment based on the nucleon-nucleon interaction. These interactions are able to simulate the pairing gaps of either the bare interaction or the interaction screened by the medium polarization effects. It is shown that the medium polarization effects cannot be cast into the density power law function usually introduced together with the contact interaction and require the introduction of another isoscalar term. The BCS-BEC crossover of neutrons pairs in symmetric and symmetric nuclear matter is studied by using these contact interactions. It is shown that the bare and screened pairing interactions lead to different features of the BCS-BEC crossover in symmetric nuclear matter. For the screened pairing interaction, a two-neutron BEC state is formed in symmetric matter at $k_{Fn}\sim 0.2$ fm$^{-1}$ (neutron density $ρ_n/ρ_0\sim 10^{-3}$). Contrary the bare interaction does not form the BEC state at any neutron density.

## Key claim

The paper proposes new density-dependent contact pairing interactions that can reproduce pairing gaps from microscopic nucleon-nucleon calculations, but the key hedge is that the medium polarization effects (screening) cannot be captured by a simple density power law and require an additional isoscalar term; furthermore, the bare and screened interactions lead to qualitatively different BCS-BEC crossover behavior, with only the screened interaction producing a two-neutron BEC state in symmetric nuclear matter at very low density.

## Method

The authors construct density-dependent contact pairing interactions of the form \(v_{nn} = v_0 \, g[\rho_n,\rho_p]\) acting in the \(^1\)S channel, where the free strength \(v_0\) is fixed by fitting the nn scattering length to reproduce low-energy phase shifts, and the density-dependent function \(g\) is adjusted to reproduce the pairing gaps in symmetric and neutron matter from the Brueckner-based calculations of Cao et al. (2006). For the screened interaction, a simple power-law form \(g_1\) fails to fit the shifted maximum of the pairing gap, so an additional isoscalar function \(g_2\) is introduced to mimic the medium polarization effects. The BCS-BEC crossover is then studied by solving the BCS gap equation and computing the Cooper pair wave function, rms radius, and probability for the partner neutrons to be correlated within the average inter-neutron distance.

## Key numbers

* For the bare pairing gap, the maximum is located at neutron Fermi momentum \(k_{Fn} = 0.87\) fm\(^{-1}\) with \(\Delta_n = 3.1\) MeV for both symmetric and neutron matter.
* For the screened pairing gap, the maximum is at \(k_{Fn} = 0.60\) fm\(^{-1}\) with \(\Delta_n = 2.70\) MeV for symmetric matter and at \(k_{Fn} = 0.83\) fm\(^{-1}\) with \(\Delta_n = 1.76\) MeV for neutron matter.
* With the screened pairing interaction, a two-neutron BEC state is formed in symmetric matter at \(k_{Fn} \sim 0.2\) fm\(^{-1}\), corresponding to a neutron density \(\rho_n/\rho_0 \sim 10^{-3}\) (where \(\rho_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\)).
* The effective neutron chemical potential \(\nu_n\) becomes negative with the screened-II interaction in symmetric nuclear matter for \(k_{Fn} = 0.05\)–\(0.35\) fm\(^{-1}\), but not at all in neutron matter.
* Reference values for the BCS-BEC crossover from the regularized model: at the BCS boundary \((k_{Fn} a_{nn})^{-1} = -1\), the probability \(P(d_n) = 0.81\) and \(\xi_{rms}/d_n = 1.10\); at the unitarity limit \((k_{Fn} a_{nn})^{-1} = 0\), \(P(d_n) = 0.99\) and \(\xi_{rms}/d_n = 0.36\); at the BEC boundary \((k_{Fn} a_{nn})^{-1} = 1\), \(P(d_n) = 1.00\) and \(\xi_{rms}/d_n = 0.19\).

## Context

This work extends the microscopic Brueckner G-matrix calculations of Cao et al. (2006), which showed that medium polarization effects reduce the pairing gap in neutron matter but enhance it in symmetric matter at low density, by constructing effective contact interactions that can be applied to finite nuclei and to the BCS-BEC crossover problem. The study builds on earlier work by Matsuo (2006) on the spatial structure of neutron Cooper pairs using finite-range and contact interactions, and on the regularized gap equation approach of Engelbrecht et al. (1997) for defining BCS-BEC boundaries. The results enable future applications to neutron-rich exotic nuclei near the drip line, expanding nuclear matter in heavy-ion collisions, and neutron star physics (cooling and glitches), where the density dependence of the pairing gap is critical.
