# Magnetic response of energy levels of superconducting nanoparticles with spin-orbit scattering

> Discrete energy levels of ultrasmall metallic grains are extracted in single-electron-tunneling-spectroscopy experiments. We study the response of these energy levels to an external magnetic field in the presence of both spin-orbit scattering and pairing correlations. In particular, we investigate $g$-factors and level curvatures that parametrize, respectively, the linear and quadratic terms in the magnetic-field dependence of the many-particle energy levels of the grain. Both of these quantities exhibit level-to-level fluctuations in the presence of spin-orbit scattering. We show that the distribution of $g$-factors is not affected by the pairing interaction and that the distribution of level curvatures is sensitive to pairing correlations even in the smallest grains in which the pairing gap is smaller than the mean single-particle level spacing. We propose the level curvature in a magnetic field as a tool to probe pairing correlations in tunneling spectroscopy experiments.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the distribution of many-body $g$-factors in superconducting nanoparticles with strong spin-orbit scattering is unaffected by pairing correlations, while the distribution of level curvatures is sensitive to pairing even in the smallest grains where the pairing gap is smaller than the mean single-particle level spacing.

## Method

The authors model the nanoparticle using the universal Hamiltonian with strong spin-orbit scattering, including a pairing interaction term but no exchange interaction. They prove analytically that the many-body $g$-factor for a tunneling transition reduces exactly to the single-particle $g$-factor of the blocked orbital, independent of pairing strength. For level curvatures, they perform exact diagonalization for model spaces up to 13 single-particle orbitals and develop a generalized BCS approach for larger spaces up to 200 orbitals, using GSE random matrices to model the single-particle spectrum.

## Key numbers

- Exact calculations performed for model spaces with $N_{\text{sp}} \le 13$ single-particle Kramers-degenerate orbitals
- BCS calculations performed for model spaces up to $N_{\text{sp}} \sim 200$ orbitals
- Ensemble of 1000 GSE random matrices of size $2N_{\text{rmt}} \times 2N_{\text{rmt}}$ with $N_{\text{rmt}} = 201$ used for numerical simulations
- The right tail of the single-particle curvature distribution in the GSE limit scales as $P(\kappa_{k_0}) \sim 1/\kappa_{k_0}^6$

## Context

This work extends studies of magnetic response in nanoparticles from the noninteracting regime (Matveev 2000, Brouwer 2000, Adam 2002) to include pairing correlations, which are relevant for superconducting grains. It builds on the universal Hamiltonian framework (Kurland 2000, Aleiner 2002) and prior work on thermodynamic observables in grains (Nesterov 2013). The paper proposes level curvature as a practical observable to detect pairing correlations in tunneling spectroscopy experiments, complementing thermodynamic probes like heat capacity and spin susceptibility (DiLorenzo 2000, Falci 2000) that are difficult to measure experimentally.
