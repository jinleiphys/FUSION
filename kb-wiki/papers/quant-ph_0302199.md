# The singular inverse square potential, limit cycles and self-adjoint extensions

> We study the radial Schroedinger equation for a particle in the field of a singular inverse square attractive potential. This potential is relevant to the fabrication of nanoscale atom optical devices, is said to be the potential describing the dipole-bound anions of polar molecules, and is the effective potential underlying the universal behavior of three-body systems in nuclear physics and atomic physics, including aspects of Bose-Einstein condensates, first described by Efimov. New results in three-body physical systems motivate the present investigation. Using the regularization method of Beane et al., we show that the corresponding ``renormalization group flow'' equation can be solved analytically. We find that it exhibits a limit cycle behavior and has infinitely many branches. We show that a physical meaning for self-adjoint extensions of the Hamiltonian arises naturally in this framework.

## Key claim

Using the regularization method of Beane et al., the renormalization group flow equation for the singular attractive inverse square potential can be solved analytically, revealing a limit cycle behavior with infinitely many branches; the authors argue that the physical meaning of self-adjoint extensions of the Hamiltonian arises naturally in this framework, but note that the ground state of the potential remains at negative infinity and no renormalization of the bound state spectrum has been achieved, contrasting with the three-body problem.

## Method

The authors study the s-wave reduced radial Schroedinger equation for a particle of mass m in an external potential consisting of an attractive square well of radius R (regularizing the short-distance behavior) matched to an attractive α/r² long-range potential. They solve the zero-energy solution analytically and derive a transcendental equation for the short-range coupling constant β = (2mα_s)^(1/2) as a function of ln(R/r_o). Using a method based on the Riemann problem in complex variable analysis, they obtain closed-form analytic solutions for β, which exhibit infinitely many branches and limit cycle behavior for all ν > 0, where ν = (2mα - 1/4)^(1/2).

## Key numbers

- The parameter ν = (2mα - 1/4)^(1/2) characterizes the strength of the attractive inverse square potential; values studied include ν = 0.5, ν = 2.0, and ν = 3.0.
- The running coupling constant β exhibits a discontinuity that is always a multiple of π; the smallest positive zero reached is β = π/2.
- For the bound state spectrum, the binding energy E_B = k²/(2m) is given by k = (1/(2r_o)) exp[(φ_o + arg Γ(1+iν) - (n+1/2)π)/ν] for n = 0, ±1, ±2, ..., where φ_o is the zero energy phase and r_o is an arbitrary scale.
- The bound state spectrum no longer depends on the cut-off radius R (for kR << 1) but instead on the arbitrary scale r_o.

## Context

This work extends the regularization method of Beane et al. (2001) for the inverse square potential, which is the effective potential underlying the universal behavior of three-body systems first described by Efimov (1970). The authors find that their analytic solutions exhibit limit cycle behavior similar to that found in the three-body counterterm of pion-less three-nucleon effective field theories (Bedaque et al., 1999; Hammer et al., 2000), but note discrepancies: no poles appear in their analytic solutions, and no evidence of multiple branches was found in the renormalized pion-less three-nucleon problem. The results enable a direct comparison between the regularization method and the mathematically rigorous theory of self-adjoint extensions (Meetz, 1964; Case, 1950), showing that the phase characterizing the self-adjoint extension can be interpreted through renormalization of the short-range coupling constant.
