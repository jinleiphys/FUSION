# Matrix Model for the Transition Expansion of Dispersive Corrections to Nucleon-Nucleus Total Cross Sections

## Key claim

The leading (second-order) term in the transition expansion for the dispersive correction to nucleon-nucleus total cross sections is accurate to better than 10% but always overestimates the exact result, while the third- and fourth-order terms nearly cancel each other, making the series less useful than the leading term alone. This conclusion is demonstrated using a finite-dimensional matrix model for the nucleon-nucleon cross section operator in the high-energy limit where longitudinal momentum transfers are ignored.

## Method

A finite-dimensional matrix model for the dimensionless cross section operator \(\hat{x}\) was constructed with two parameters: \(f\) controlling off-diagonal elements (decreasing geometrically with state separation) and \(d\) controlling diagonal elements (increasing linearly with state index). The parameters were fixed by matching the second and third moments of the operator, \(<x^2> = 1.25\) and \(<x^3> = 1.9\), for matrix dimensions \(N=3\) and \(N=6\). The dispersive correction \(G^D(t)\) and its expansion in powers of the number of inelastic transitions (orders \(n=2\) through \(n=6\)) were then evaluated for nucleon-\(^{208}\text{Pb}\) scattering using a Woods-Saxon nuclear density and integrated over impact parameter.

## Key numbers

- The nucleon-nucleon total cross section used was \(\sigma = 39.8\) mb, giving an uncorrected Glauber cross section of 3022 mb for \(^{208}\text{Pb}\).
- The maximum value of the dimensionless thickness function \(t\) at zero impact parameter was about 4.3 for \(A=208\) and \(\sigma = 39.8\) mb.
- The second moment of the cross section operator was fixed at \(<x^2> = 1.25\) and the third moment at \(<x^3> = 1.9\).
- The \(n=2\) term in the expansion was accurate to better than 10% of the exact result for both \(N=3\) and \(N=6\).
- The third- and fourth-order terms each ranged from about 20% to 40% of the exact result but cancelled to better than 4%.
- The sum of terms \(n=2\) through \(n=6\) gave results accurate to better than 1% for \(N=3\) and to about 5% for \(N=6\).
- Continuous distribution models from Ref. 1 gave dispersive corrections ranging from 199 to 232 mb for nucleon-\(^{208}\text{Pb}\) total cross sections.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Bla (year not given) and Har (year not given) for calculating dispersive corrections to nucleon-nucleus total cross sections using an operator formalism. It directly addresses the convergence of the transition expansion, for which only the leading term had been evaluated by Jen (year not given) and then only approximately. The finite matrix model provides a tractable test bed for studying higher-order terms; the author suggests that future work should include longitudinal momentum transfers (which vanish in the high-energy limit used here) and test other matrix models to see if the near-cancellation of third- and fourth-order terms is a general feature.
