# Role of the Surface Energy in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The surface energy is one of the fundamental properties nuclei, appearing in the simplest form of the semi-empirical mass formula. The surface enery has an influence on e.g. the shape of a nucleus and its ability to deform. This in turn could be expected to have an effect in fusion reactions around the Coulomb barrier where dynamical effects such as the formation of a neck is part of the fusion process. Frozen Hartree-Fock and Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock calculations are made for a series of effective interactions in which the surface energy is systematically varied, using $^{40}$Ca + $^{48}$Ca as a test case. The dynamical lowering of the barrier is greatest for the largest surface energy, contrary to naive expectations, and we speculate that this may be due to the variation in other nuclear matter properties for these effective interactions

## Key claim

The dynamical lowering of the fusion barrier between Frozen Hartree-Fock and Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock calculations is greatest for the largest surface energy, contrary to the naive expectation that a higher surface energy would make it harder to form a neck and thus lower the barrier less; the author speculates this may be due to variation in other nuclear matter properties (such as symmetry energy) across the effective interaction set.

## Method

Frozen Hartree-Fock (FHF) calculations were performed to map out the ion-ion potential for \(^{40}\)Ca + \(^{48}\)Ca collisions by placing ground-state wave functions at fixed separations and computing the interaction energy. Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations were then performed for the same system to find the lowest energy at which fusion proceeds, allowing dynamical deformation and neck formation. Both methods used eight Skyrme effective interactions (SLy5s1 through SLy5s8) in which the surface energy was systematically varied between about 18.0 MeV and 19.4 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Surface energies of the SLy5s\(n\) forces vary between about 18.0 MeV and 19.4 MeV, with \(n=1\) having the smallest surface energy.
- FHF barrier heights for \(^{40}\)Ca + \(^{48}\)Ca range from 53.457 MeV (SLy5s1, smallest surface energy) down to 53.326 MeV (SLy5s8, largest surface energy).
- TDHF barrier heights for \(^{40}\)Ca + \(^{48}\)Ca range from 51.885 \(\pm\) 0.005 MeV (SLy5s1) down to 51.665 \(\pm\) 0.005 MeV (SLy5s8).
- The dynamical lowering of the barrier (FHF minus TDHF) increases from about 1.572 MeV for SLy5s1 to about 1.661 MeV for SLy5s8, meaning the largest surface energy gives the greatest dynamical reduction.
- The asymmetry energy increases by about 1 MeV from SLy5s1 to SLy5s8.

## Context

This work extends the study of Jodon et al. (2016), who fitted Skyrme interactions with systematically varied surface energy, by applying those forces to heavy-ion fusion reactions around the Coulomb barrier. It follows the approach of Reinhard (2016), who studied how variation in symmetry energy leads to changes in neutron radii and corresponding effects on fusion cross sections. The results enable further studies of surface energy effects in heavier nuclei, giant resonances, and fission lifetimes, as planned by the author.
