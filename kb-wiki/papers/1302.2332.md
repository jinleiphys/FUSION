# Volume Fluctuation and Autocorrelation Effects in the Moment Analysis of Net-proton Multiplicity Distributions in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> Moments (Variance ($σ^2$), Skewness($S$), Kurtosis($κ$)) of multiplicity distributions of conserved quantities, such as net-baryon,net-charge and net-strangeness, are predicted to be sensitive to the correlation length of the system and connected to the thermodynamic susceptibilities computed in Lattice QCD and Hadron Resonance Gas (HRG) model. In this paper, we present several measurement artifacts that could lead to volume fluctuation and auto-correlation effects in the moment analysis of net-proton multiplicity distributions in heavy-ion collisions using the UrQMD model. We discuss methods to overcome these artifacts so that the extracted moments could be used to obtain physical conclusions. In addition we present methods to properly estimate the statistical errors in moment analysis.

# Volume Fluctuation and Autocorrelation Effects in the Moment Analysis of Net-proton Multiplicity Distributions in Heavy-Ion Collisions

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that centrality bin width effect, centrality resolution effect, and auto-correlation effect are significant measurement artifacts in moment analysis of net-proton distributions, and that these artifacts can be corrected using centrality bin width correction (CBWC) and by choosing appropriate centrality definitions, but only if the analysis is performed within the specific kinematic acceptance of mid-rapidity (|y|<0.5) and transverse momentum (0.4<pT<0.8 GeV/c) used in the UrQMD model simulations.

## Method
The authors used the UrQMD transport model to simulate Au+Au collisions at seven beam energies from 7.7 to 200 GeV, generating between 28 and 108 million events per energy. They calculated event-by-event net-proton (proton minus antiproton) multiplicity distributions within |y|<0.5 and 0.4<pT<0.8 GeV/c, then computed cumulants and moment products (Sσ and κσ²) for various centrality bins. They compared results with and without CBWC, using different η ranges for centrality definition (|η|<0.5, 1, 1.5, 2) to isolate volume fluctuation and auto-correlation effects, and validated error estimation methods (Delta theorem, Bootstrap, Sub-group) against Skellam-distributed Monte Carlo simulations.

## Key numbers
- UrQMD simulations performed at √sNN = 7.7, 11.5, 19.6, 27, 39, 62.4, and 200 GeV with event statistics of 107, 108, 50, 65, 93, 29, and 28 million, respectively
- Net-proton acceptance: |y|<0.5 and 0.4<pT<0.8 GeV/c, matching STAR experiment kinematic range
- Skellam distribution test used μ1=4.11 and μ2=2.99, corresponding to mean proton and antiproton numbers in central Au+Au at 200 GeV from STAR
- For normal distributions, error(Sσ) ∝ σ/√n and error(κσ²) ∝ σ²/√n, where n is number of events
- CBWC uses weight ωr = nr/Σnr for each r-th multiplicity bin, where nr is number of events in that bin
- 32 narrow centrality bins (0-2.5%, 2.5-5%, ..., 77.5-80%) used as reference for CBWC validation
- Nine wide centrality bins (0-5%, 5-10%, 10-20%, ..., 70-80%) used for CBWC demonstration
- κσ² shows larger sensitivity to centrality resolution effect than Sσ, with larger effect in peripheral collisions and at low energies
- Auto-correlation effect suppresses Sσ and κσ² values, more strongly at low energies than high energies

## Context
This work extends the theoretical framework connecting higher-order cumulants of conserved quantities to QCD susceptibilities and correlation length, as developed by Stephanov (2009) and the Lattice QCD community (Cheng et al., 2008; Wuppertal-Budapest collaboration). It directly addresses measurement artifacts that could affect experimental searches for the QCD critical point at RHIC, particularly the STAR experiment's net-proton fluctuation measurements (STAR Collaboration, 2010; Luo, 2012). The paper provides practical correction methods (CBWC, centrality definition with larger η coverage, exclusion of protons from centrality definition) that enable reliable comparison between experimental data and theoretical calculations from transport models or lattice QCD. These techniques are applicable to net-charge and net-strangeness moment analyses being conducted at RHIC.
