# Systematic study of complete fusion suppression for 6,7 Li nuclei using artificial neural network

> In recent decades, there has been a significant increase in the measurement of complete fusion cross-sections for various reactions, with particular emphasis on the reactions involving weakly bound projectile. It has been well established that the complete fusion cross-section involving weakly bound nuclei is suppressed at above barrier energies due to the breakup effect. Accurate determination of suppression factor is essential to understand the effect of breakup on complete fusion suppression. In this study, Feedforward Artificial Neural Network (ANN) methods based on Multilayer Perceptron is utilized to estimate the complete fusion suppression factor for reactions involving 6,7 Li projectile from the comparison of ANN predicted reduced fusion functions (F (x)) with the Universal Fusion Function(F0 (x)). Average suppression factor has been estimated as 0.68 and 0.74 for 6 Li and 7 Li induced reactions respectively. Normalized Mean Squared Error(NMSE) has been calculated as 1.85% for training and 1.92% for testing data of ANN for 6 Li case, while for 7 Li cases, the same is for 3.73% and 6.48% respectively. Present results have been compared with three other alternative methods Support Vector Regression, Random Forest Regression and Gaussian Process Regression. Relevant performance matrices has been estimated. It has been observed that, ANN algorithm is giving the better accuracy as compared with other. This results indicate that ANN might be an alternative tool for estimation of complete fusion suppression factor for weakly bound projectile.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a Feedforward Artificial Neural Network (ANN) can estimate the complete fusion suppression factor for reactions involving weakly bound \(^{6,7}\)Li projectiles with better accuracy than Support Vector Regression, Random Forest Regression, and Gaussian Process Regression, but the method is presented as a "might be" alternative tool rather than a definitive replacement for physical models.

## Method

The author trained a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) regressor with two hidden layers (64 and 32 nodes) on experimental fusion cross-section data for \(^{6,7}\)Li projectiles on targets with mass numbers from 90 to 209. The input was the dimensionless variable \(x = (E_{c.m.} - V_B)/\hbar\omega\) and the output was the reduced fusion function \(F(x)\). After training on 70% of the data and testing on 30%, the ANN-predicted \(F(x)\) was compared to the Universal Fusion Function \(F_0(x) = \ln[1 + \exp(2\pi x)]\) to extract the average suppression factor. The results were compared with Support Vector Regression, Random Forest Regression, and Gaussian Process Regression using performance metrics including Normalized Mean Squared Error (NMSE) and the coefficient of determination \(R^2\).

## Key numbers

* Average suppression factor for \(^{6}\)Li induced reactions: 0.68
* Average suppression factor for \(^{7}\)Li induced reactions: 0.74
* NMSE for \(^{6}\)Li ANN training data: 1.85%; for testing data: 1.92%
* NMSE for \(^{7}\)Li ANN training data: 3.73%; for testing data: 6.48%
* \(R^2\) for \(^{6}\)Li ANN training data: 0.98; for testing data: 0.98
* \(R^2\) for \(^{7}\)Li ANN training data: 0.97; for testing data: 0.95
* Number of data points for \(^{6}\)Li: 114; for \(^{7}\)Li: 80
* Total network parameters: 2241 (2144 weights, 97 biases)
* Maximum training iterations: 5000; initial learning rate: 0.0001

## Context

This work extends the method of Canto et al. (2009) for quantifying breakup effects on complete fusion by comparing experimental fusion functions to the Universal Fusion Function, which previously relied on coupled-channels calculations. The study applies machine learning to a problem where earlier approaches (Tripathi et al., 2002; Dasgupta et al., 2004; Rath et al., 2009) found suppression factors that depended on modeling choices, as noted by Kundu et al. (2016). The ANN approach enables systematic estimation across many target nuclei without requiring explicit coupled-channels calculations, though the physical interpretation of the suppression mechanism remains separate from the prediction.
