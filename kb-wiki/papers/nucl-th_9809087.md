# The current status of Vud

> The value of the $V_{ud}$ matrix element of the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa (CKM) matrix can be derived from nuclear superallowed beta decays, neutron decay, and pion beta decay. We survey current world data for all three. Today, the most precise value of $V_{ud}$ comes from the nuclear decays; however, the precision is limited not by experimental error but by the estimated uncertainty in theoretical corrections. The neutron data are approximately a factor of four poorer in precision but this could change dramatically in the near future as planned experiments come to fruition. The nuclear result (and the most recent of the neutron decay results) differ at the 98% confidence level from the unitarity condition for the CKM matrix. We examine the reliability of the small calculated corrections that have been applied to the data, and assess the likelihood of even higher quality nuclear data becoming available to confirm or deny the discrepancy. Some of the required experiments depend upon the availability of intense radioactive beams. Others are possible today.

## Key claim

The most precise value of $V_{ud}$ comes from nuclear superallowed beta decays, but its precision is limited not by experimental error but by the estimated uncertainty in theoretical corrections; the nuclear result (and the most recent neutron decay result) differ at the 98% confidence level from the unitarity condition for the CKM matrix.

## Method

The authors surveyed world data for three sources of $V_{ud}$: nuclear superallowed $0^+ \rightarrow 0^+$ Fermi beta decays (nine nuclei from $^{10}$C to $^{54}$Co), free neutron decay, and pion beta decay. For nuclear decays, they compiled $Q$-values, half-lives, and branching ratios from over 100 independent measurements, applied calculated radiative corrections ($\delta_R$ and $\Delta_R^V$) and isospin-symmetry-breaking corrections ($\delta_C$), and extracted the corrected $\mathcal{F}t$-values to determine $V_{ud}$. For neutron decay, they combined lifetime measurements and beta-asymmetry measurements of $\lambda = g_A/g_V$; for pion decay, they used the branching ratio and lifetime.

## Key numbers

- Average corrected $\mathcal{F}t$-value for nine superallowed transitions: $3072.3 \pm 2.0$ s (statistical error $\pm 0.9$ s, systematic error $\pm 1.1$ s added linearly)
- $V_{ud}$ from nuclear decays: $0.9740 \pm 0.0005$
- Unitarity sum from nuclear decays: $\sum_i V_{ui}^2 = 0.9968 \pm 0.0014$ (fails unity by 2.2 standard deviations)
- $V_{ud}$ from neutron decay (world average): $0.9759 \pm 0.0021$; unitarity sum $1.0007 \pm 0.0042$
- $V_{ud}$ from neutron decay using PERKEO II beta-asymmetry result ($\lambda = -1.2735 \pm 0.0021$): $0.9714 \pm 0.0015$; unitarity sum $0.9919 \pm 0.0030$
- $V_{ud}$ from pion beta decay: $0.9670 \pm 0.0161$; unitarity sum $0.9833 \pm 0.0311$
- Nucleus-independent radiative correction: $\Delta_R^V = (2.40 \pm 0.08)\%$
- If experiment were the sole contributor to the nuclear $V_{ud}$ uncertainty, it would be only $\pm 0.0001$; the largest contributions come from $\Delta_R^V$ ($\pm 0.0004$) and $\delta_C$ ($\pm 0.0003$)
- To restore unitarity, $\delta_R$ would need to shift downward by 0.3% (one-quarter of its value), or $\delta_C$ upward by 0.3% (over half its value), or some combination

## Context

This work surveys the status of $V_{ud}$ determinations as of 1998, updating the authors' earlier survey (Towner and Hardy, 1990) and their contribution to the previous WEIN conference (1995). The nuclear result's deviation from CKM unitarity at the 2.2-sigma level had also been noted by the Particle Data Group (1998). The paper examines whether adjustments to the theoretical corrections calculated by Sirlin (1987), Jaus and Rasche (1987), Towner, Hardy and Harvey (1977), and Ormand and Brown (1995) could resolve the discrepancy, concluding this is unlikely. The Saito-Thomas (1995) quark-meson coupling model correction is discussed as a speculative alternative that could increase $V_{ud}^2$ by 0.075% to 0.20% depending on nuclear density, but the authors note it is premature to apply. The work enables future experiments with intense radioactive beams to improve nuclear data, and notes that planned neutron decay experiments (such as PERKEO II) could dramatically improve precision, though the theoretical uncertainty on $\Delta_R^V$ will ultimately limit both approaches.
