# Production of hypernuclei in peripheral relativistic ion collisions

> Within a dynamical and statistical approach we study the main regularities in production of hypernuclei coming from projectile and target residues in relativistic ion collisions. We demonstrate that yields of hypernuclei increase considerably above the energy threshold for Lambda hyperons, and there is a saturation for yields of single hypernuclei with increasing the beam energy up to few TeV. Production of specific hypernuclei depend very much on the isotopic composition of the projectile, and this gives a chance to obtain exotic hypernuclei that may be difficult to reach in traditional hypernuclear experiments. Possibilities for the detection of such hypernuclei with planned and available relativistic ion facilities are discussed.

## Key claim

The yields of single hypernuclei from projectile and target residues in peripheral relativistic ion collisions saturate at beam energies above about 10 GeV per nucleon, contrary to the abstract's suggestion of a monotonic increase up to a few TeV; the saturation means that very high energies are not needed for single hypernucleus production, though double and multi-strange hypernuclei may still increase with energy.

## Method

The authors used a hybrid dynamical and statistical approach: the Dubna Cascade Model (DCM) transport code, which incorporates the Quark-Gluon String Model (QGSM) for elementary hadron collisions at high energies, to simulate the dynamical stage of peripheral heavy-ion collisions and the absorption of Lambda hyperons by spectator residues. The excited hyper-residues were then de-excited using the Fermi-break-up model (FBM) to predict the final yields of specific hypernuclei. Calculations were performed for symmetric collisions of carbon, nickel, and lead nuclei over a beam energy range from near threshold to 3 TeV per nucleon.

## Key numbers

- For carbon-carbon collisions at 20 A GeV, the probability of producing a $^{3}_{\Lambda}$H hypernucleus is about 2.5 x 10^{-5} per inelastic event.
- The background of $\pi^{-}$ + $^{3}$He pairs in a 10 MeV invariant mass interval around the $^{3}_{\Lambda}$H mass is about 5 x 10^{-3} per event at 20 A GeV, giving a signal-to-background ratio of about 1%.
- The cross-section for formation of excited hyper-residues in the saturation region (above about 10 A GeV) is about 1 to 10^2 millibarn, depending on the size of the colliding nuclei.
- Using a neutron-rich $^{16}$C projectile instead of $^{12}$C at 20 A GeV can increase the yield of the exotic hypernucleus $^{6}_{\Lambda}$H by up to two orders of magnitude.
- At beam energies of 2 A GeV, 20 A GeV, 200 A GeV, and 3 A TeV, projectile hypernuclei can propagate about 20 cm, 130 cm, 13 meters, and nearly 200 meters, respectively, before their weak decay.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Botvina 2011, Botvina 2012) on hypernucleus production in peripheral relativistic collisions by systematically investigating the energy dependence and the role of projectile isotopic composition. It builds on the established understanding of nuclear multifragmentation (SMM, ALADIN, EOS) and applies it to the strangeness sector, offering a method to produce exotic hypernuclei that are difficult to reach with traditional kaon or meson beams (Bando, Japan). The results enable future experiments at FAIR, NICA, RHIC, and LHC to design setups for detecting hypernuclei from spectator decay, particularly by exploiting the large gamma factors for spatial separation of decay vertices.
