# Tackling the Fermionic Sign Problem in the Auxiliary-Field Monte Carlo Method

> We explore a novel and straightforward solution to the sign problem that has plagued the Auxiliary-field Monte Carlo (AFMC) method applied to many-body systems for more than a decade. We present a solution to the sign problem that has plagued the Auxiliary-field Monte Carlo (AFMC) method for more than a decade and report a breakthrough where excellent agreement between AFMC and exact CI calculations for fully realistic nuclear applications is achieved. This result offers the capability, unmatched by other methods, to achieve exact solutions for large-scale quantum many-body systems.

# Tackling the Fermionic Sign Problem in the Auxiliary-Field Monte Carlo Method

## Key claim
The authors report that shifting the contour of the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to the Hartree mean-field solution mitigates the sign problem in Auxiliary-Field Monte Carlo (AFMC) calculations, achieving agreement with exact configuration-interaction (CI) results for fully realistic nuclear Hamiltonians. The method is demonstrated to work for both even-even and odd-mass nuclei, and for thermal as well as ground-state observables, with deviations at the level of 150-200 keV for ground-state energies.

## Method
The authors apply the Auxiliary-Field Monte Carlo (AFMC) method based on the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to nuclear many-body systems. They rewrite the two-body Hamiltonian by shifting each quadratic operator term by a constant field, then apply the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to the shifted quadratic form. The optimal shift is chosen as the auxiliary-field value corresponding to the Hartree mean-field solution at each imaginary-time step. The auxiliary fields are then sampled using the Gaussian factor, with the shift introducing phases that suppress negative signs in the weight function for bad-sign components of the Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers
- For $^{28}$Mg in the $sd$-shell with the Wildenthal Hamiltonian, the AFMC ground-state energy at $\beta = 3.0$ MeV$^{-1}$ is -120.370(25) MeV, compared to the exact CI result of -120.532 MeV.
- For $^{27}$Na in the $sd$-shell with the Wildenthal interaction, the AFMC ground-state energy is -99.106(55) MeV, compared to the exact CI result of -99.230 MeV.
- For $^{56}$Fe in the $fp$-shell with the GXPF1A interaction, the AFMC ground-state energy is -195.687(107) MeV, compared to the CI result of -195.901 MeV.
- The zero-temperature AFMC calculation for $^{56}$Fe took 12 CPU hours, compared to 1000 CPU hours for the CI calculation.
- The time step used in all calculations is $\Delta\beta = 1/32$ MeV$^{-1}$.
- Thermal energies are typically reproduced at the level of 300 keV or better; ground-state energies are reproduced to within 150-200 keV.

## Context
This work extends the shifted-contour method initially proposed by Rom et al. to fully realistic nuclear Hamiltonians, overcoming the sign problem that had limited previous AFMC applications to semi-realistic interactions with good sign or to extrapolation methods based on splitting the Hamiltonian into good-sign and bad-sign parts (as in the work of Y. Alhassid and collaborators). The method enables large-scale many-body calculations for mid-mass nuclei where CI methods face matrix dimensions of order $10^{20}$, and opens the possibility of combining AFMC with Skyrme-like mean-field approaches to develop a universal picture for nuclei. The ability to compute the nuclear level density microscopically is particularly relevant for astrophysical nucleosynthesis calculations.
