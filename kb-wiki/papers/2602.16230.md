# Electromagnetic Production of Kaons on the Nucleon

> Studies of the electromagnetic production of strange quarks began in the 1950s as something of a curiosity that puzzled experimentalists and theorists alike. As the datasets increased, concomitant advances in theoretical models were realized. A paradigm shift occurred in the 1990s with the development of second-generation facilities at ELSA, MAMI, SPring-8, and JLab, which brought nuclear physics experiments forward by orders of magnitude in counting statistics compared to the first-generation efforts. This was an utter boon to strangeness physics investigations, and to date, more than 50 dedicated experiments in kaon photo- and electroproduction have been completed at facilities around the world, leading to a host of experimental observables that have enabled significant advances in the exploration of strongly interacting systems that decay via $s\bar{s}$ quark pair creation. This review was designed to provide the first-ever in-depth overview of both the experimental and theoretical progress in the field of the electromagnetic production of strangeness. This work looks back over 70 years of past developments, discusses ongoing work and near-term plans, and details future possibilities being considered for third-generation facilities. Throughout this work, the primary impacts of these explorations are highlighted, along with connections to a wide range of related phenomenological applications. An important goal of this review is to provide a complete, self-contained guide into this field prepared at a level that is relevant for both new and seasoned scientists, whether experimentalists, phenomenologists, or theorists, to better understand what has been accomplished by so many dedicated folks-each building on what has come before-and to appreciate the exciting future potential for continued studies in this area. A more complete abstract is provided in the paper.

## Key claim

This review provides the first-ever comprehensive overview of both experimental and theoretical progress in the electromagnetic production of strangeness, covering over 70 years of developments from the 1950s through ongoing work and future plans for third-generation facilities, with the goal of serving as a self-contained guide for both new and seasoned scientists.

## Method

The authors systematically summarize the theoretical formalism of kaon electroproduction and photoproduction, including kinematics, production amplitudes, helicity amplitudes, transversity amplitudes, and multipole decomposition. They provide a comprehensive overview of experimental facilities and datasets from the U.S., Europe, and Asia, including the current 12-GeV CEBAF machine at JLab and plans for a possible future 22-GeV upgrade, alongside a complete layout of existing theoretical models and their status.

## Key numbers

* More than 50 dedicated experiments in kaon photo- and electroproduction have been completed at facilities worldwide
* The electromagnetic coupling constant alpha = e^2/4pi approximately 1/137, making the one-photon exchange approximation valid to within about 1% accuracy
* Threshold photon energies for the six isospin channels range from 911.1 MeV (gamma p to K+ Lambda) to 1052.1 MeV (gamma n to K+ Sigma-), with corresponding total center-of-mass energies from 1609.4 MeV to 1691.1 MeV
* The review includes a compilation of nearly 650 citations from the key literature

## Context

This review extends the most recent prior review on kaon electromagnetic production, which was published over 20 years ago as a conference proceedings by Bennhold (1999), prepared before the release of the 2005 experimental wave of data. The work builds upon insights from second-generation facilities at ELSA, MAMI, SPring-8, and JLab that brought nuclear physics experiments forward by orders of magnitude in counting statistics compared to first-generation efforts from the 1950s-1980s. This review enables future studies of missing and exotic baryon configurations, hypernuclear production as a probe of the YN interaction, and electromagnetic form factors of strange mesons.
