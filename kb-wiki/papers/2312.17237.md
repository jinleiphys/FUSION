# QGP Physics from Attractor Perturbations

> The strong longitudinal expansion characteristic of heavy-ion collisions leads to universal attractor behavior of the resulting drop of quark-gluon plasma (QGP) already at very early times. Assuming approximate boost invariance and neglecting transverse expansion at the initial time, we incorporate subsequent transverse dynamics of this system by linearizing the Mueller-Israel-Stewart theory around the transversely homogeneous attractor. The result is a system of coupled ordinary differential equations which describes the proper-time evolution of Fourier modes encoding the transverse structure of the initial energy deposition. The late-time asymptotic behavior of these solutions is described by transseries which make manifest the stability of the attractor against transverse perturbations. In this framework, information about the structure of the plasma in the transverse plane resides mostly in exponentially suppressed corrections to evolution along the attractor, which are not yet negligible at freeze-out. These findings also suggest a simple numerical approach to QGP dynamics using a finite number of Fourier modes. Physical observables can be expressed in terms of the asymptotic data evaluated at freeze-out. We demonstrate the efficacy of this approach by calculating the final multiplicity distributions and collective flow coefficients.

## Key claim

The paper shows that in a boost-invariant, transversely homogeneous attractor description of quark-gluon plasma, essentially all information about the initial transverse structure of the fireball is carried by exponentially suppressed corrections to the attractor evolution, which are not yet negligible at freeze-out; this means the attractor framework can capture collective flow observables despite the exponential damping of transverse perturbations at asymptotically late times.

## Method

The authors linearize the Mueller-Israel-Stewart (MIS) theory around a transversely homogeneous, boost-invariant attractor background, reducing the full partial differential equations to a system of coupled ordinary differential equations for Fourier modes of the transverse perturbations. They analyze the late-time asymptotic behavior of these modes using transseries methods and compare the analytic asymptotic solutions to numerical evolution of the mode equations for various wave vectors \(k\). The approach is demonstrated by reconstructing final multiplicity distributions and collective flow coefficients from the evolved Fourier modes.

## Key numbers

* The asymptotic speed of sound in MIS theory is \(c_\infty = \sqrt{\frac{1}{3}(1+4\alpha^2)}\), where \(\alpha \equiv \sqrt{C_\eta/C_\tau}\); this remains subluminal for \(\alpha^2 < 1/2\) (i.e., \(C_\tau > 2 C_\eta\)).
* For \(\mathcal{N}=4\) supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory, the transport coefficients are \(C_e = 8\pi^2/15\), \(C_\eta = 1/(4\pi)\), and \(C_\tau = (2-\ln 2)/(2\pi)\).
* The leading exponential damping coefficients for transverse perturbations are \(A_1 = A_2 = \alpha^2/(C_\tau c_\infty^2)\), \(A_3 = 3/(2C_\tau)\), \(A_4 = 1/(2C_\tau c_\infty^2)\), and \(A_5 = A_6 = 3/(4C_\tau)\); these are real, positive, and independent of wave vector \(k\).
* The late-time asymptotic solutions for perturbations include oscillatory frequencies \(\omega_1 = -\omega_2 = c_\infty k [1 + \mathcal{O}((\Lambda\tau)^{-2/3})]\) and \(\omega_5 = -\omega_6 = \alpha k [1 + \mathcal{O}((\Lambda\tau)^{-2/3})]\).
* The integration constant \(\Lambda\) from the background solution is typically \(\Lambda \approx \mathcal{O}(1)~\text{fm}^{-1}\) for initial conditions relevant to heavy-ion physics.

## Context

This work extends the Bjorken model (Bjorken, 1982) by incorporating transverse dynamics through linearization around the attractor, building on the concept of far-from-equilibrium attractors introduced by Heller and Spalinski (2015) and developed further by Romatschke (2017), Kurkela and collaborators (2020), and Blaizot and Yan (2017). The approach contrasts with earlier Navier-Stokes treatments of linearized perturbations (Floerchinger and Wiedemann, 2011) by including nonhydrodynamic sectors essential at early times, and enables a simple numerical scheme using a finite number of Fourier modes to capture collective flow effects such as elliptic flow, reminiscent of the close-limit approximation in black hole collisions (Price and Pullin, 1994).
