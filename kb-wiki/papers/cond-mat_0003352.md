# Ground-State Magnetization in Disordered Systems : Exchange vs. Off-Diagonal Interaction Fluctuations

> In a Hartree-Fock picture, itinerant ferromagnetism results from a competition between kinetic and exchange energy, with the magnetized state being favored at large interaction strength. In a recent paper \cite{spings} we showed that in contrast to this average effect, fluctuations of off-diagonal interaction matrix elements systematically reduce the ground-state magnetization. When the interaction dominates, the occurence of a non-zero ground-state magnetization depends on the ratio $λ$ between average exchange energy and the fluctuation amplitude of the off-diagonal matrix elements, and a nonzero critical value $λ_c$ is necessary to magnetize the ground-state. We extend these results by numerical studies of $λ$ for standard tight-binding models which indicate a regime of intermediate disorder where off-diagonal fluctuations should play an important role for ground-state magnetization. We also emphasize the presence of strong correlations between minimal eigenvalues of different magnetization blocks which further reduce the probability of a nonzero ground-state spin.

# Ground-State Magnetization in Disordered Systems: Exchange vs. Off-Diagonal Interaction Fluctuations

## Key claim

While the average effect of interactions favors ferromagnetism via exchange energy, fluctuations of off-diagonal interaction matrix elements systematically suppress ground-state magnetization; a nonzero ground-state magnetization occurs only when the ratio λ between average exchange energy and the fluctuation amplitude of off-diagonal matrix elements exceeds a critical value λ_c, and strong correlations between minimal eigenvalues of different magnetization blocks further reduce the probability of a nonzero ground-state spin.

## Method

The authors study a generic Hamiltonian of interacting fermions expressed in the basis of Slater determinants constructed from eigenstates of a free-fermion model, with Gaussian-distributed off-diagonal interaction matrix elements. They compute the cross-correlation function between the lowest energy levels in the two lowest-magnetization blocks for n=4, 5, and 6 electrons on m=10 orbitals, and numerically evaluate the magnetization parameter λ for a two-dimensional Anderson lattice with Hubbard and long-range interactions across varying disorder strengths and system sizes.

## Key numbers

- Critical value λ_c ≈ 2 is necessary to have a non-negligible probability of magnetizing the ground-state; a larger value λ ≈ 4 is necessary to have ⟨Δ_s⟩ = 0, resulting in a 50% probability of nonvanishing magnetization.
- For the Hamiltonian with only the two-body interaction H_U, the spin gap Δ_s^U ≈ 4U for even number n of electrons and Δ_s^U ≈ 8U for odd n.
- The cross-correlation function R(E_0,E_1) remains above 0.8 up to very large interaction strength U=10 for n=4, 5, and 6 electrons on m=10 orbitals.
- The probability to find a zero or negative spin gap from H_U alone is zero for n=5 and less than 0.002 for n=4 and 6.
- For the two-dimensional Anderson lattice with Hubbard interaction, λ ≈ 4 in the intermediate disorder regime, independent of system size for L=10, 20, 50, and 80.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier results (Jacquod and Stone, 1999) showing that off-diagonal interaction fluctuations suppress magnetization, in contrast to predictions by Andreev and Kamenev (1998) and Brouwer, Oreg, and Halperin (1999) that disorder enhances the effective interaction and promotes ferromagnetism below the Stoner threshold. The study uses methods from nuclear physics (Brody et al., 1981; Mon and French, 1975) for analyzing the many-body density of states and its variance. The results enable quantitative predictions for when ground-state magnetization can occur in disordered systems, showing that in the intermediate disorder regime the magnetization probability does not exceed 50% even for very strong interactions, and that longer-range interactions further suppress magnetization relative to the Hubbard case.
