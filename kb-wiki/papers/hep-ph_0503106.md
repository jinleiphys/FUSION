# Chiral Symmetry Restoration through Hawking-Unruh Thermalization Effect

> Comments on the chiral symmetry restoration through the Hawking-Unruh effect are given.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the thermalization effect of uniform acceleration (the Hawking-Unruh effect) can restore dynamically broken chiral symmetry in a fermion system, with the critical acceleration (and corresponding Unruh temperature) coinciding with the case of usual temperature restoration in Minkowski spacetime.

## Method

The author considers a fermion system in Minkowski spacetime where chiral symmetry is dynamically broken via the Nambu–Jona-Lasinio model, then examines what a uniformly accelerated observer would observe. The field theory is reformulated in Rindler coordinates (appropriate for an accelerated observer), Green's functions are obtained, and the effective potential method is applied to derive a gap equation for the self-consistent mass. The critical coupling and critical acceleration (critical Hawking-Unruh temperature) for symmetry breaking and restoration are then extracted.

## Key numbers

- The Unruh temperature is given by \(T_U = a/2\pi\), where \(a\) is the acceleration constant.
- In Rindler coordinates, Euclidean two-point functions are periodic in the time direction with period \(2\pi\), satisfying the Kubo-Martin-Schwinger condition.
- The critical exponent for the phase transition is that of the Landau theory of second-order phase transitions.

## Context

This work extends the author's own previous paper (Ohsaku, 2005) on dynamical chiral symmetry breaking and its restoration for a uniformly accelerated observer. It also relates to a proposal by Karzeev and Tuchin (2005) on thermalization from the Hawking-Unruh effect in relativistic heavy ion collisions, where the transition temperature could become experimentally obtainable. The paper speculates on extending the analysis to the supersymmetric Nambu–Jona-Lasinio model with soft SUSY mass, where the order of the phase transition under Hawking-Unruh thermalization changes from first-order to second-order when the soft mass becomes large enough.
