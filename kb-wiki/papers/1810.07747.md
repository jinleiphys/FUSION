# Partial conservation of seniority and its unexpected influence on E2 transitions in $g_{9/2}$ nuclei

> There exist two uniquely defined $v=4$ states in systems within a $j=9/2$ subshell, which automatically conserve seniority and do not mix with other states. Here I show that the partial conservation of seniority plays an essential role in our understanding of the electric quadrupole transitions of the semimagic nuclei involving $j=9/2$ subshells, including the long-lived $8^{+}$ isomer in $^{94}$Ru. The effects of configuration mixing from neighboring subshells on the structure of those unique states are analysed. It is shown that a sharp transition from pure seniority coupling to a significant mixture between the $v=2$ and $v=4$ states may be induced by the cross-orbital non-diagonal interaction matrix elements. Such strong mixture is essential to explain the observed E2 transition properties of $N=50$ isotones $^{96}$Pd and $^{94}$Ru.

# Partial conservation of seniority and its unexpected influence on E2 transitions in $g_{9/2}$ nuclei

## Key claim
The existence of two uniquely defined seniority $v=4$ states in $j=9/2$ subshells that automatically conserve seniority and do not mix with other states plays an essential role in understanding electric quadrupole transitions in semimagic nuclei, including the long-lived $8^{+}$ isomer in $^{94}$Ru. However, a sharp transition from pure seniority coupling to significant mixture between $v=2$ and $v=4$ states can be induced by cross-orbital non-diagonal interaction matrix elements, and such strong mixture is essential to explain the observed E2 transition properties of $N=50$ isotones $^{96}$Pd and $^{94}$Ru.

## Method
Shell model full configuration interaction calculations with exact diagonalization were performed for $(g_{9/2})^{4}$ systems within the $g_{9/2}$ orbital, and extended to model spaces including neighboring $1p_{1/2}$, $1p_{3/2}$, and $0f_{5/2}$ orbitals. The influence of seniority-non-conserving interaction matrix elements on wave functions and E2 transition strengths was studied by varying the strength of the matrix element $V_{SNC}=65V_{2}-315V_{4}+403V_{6}-153V_{8}$ and by comparing calculations with different effective interactions (jun45, jj44Ni, jj44b) in progressively enlarged model spaces.

## Key numbers
- The $8^{+}$ isomer in $^{94}$Ru lies at 2.644 MeV with a half-life of 71 $\mu$s.
- The measured $B(E2;8_{1}^{+}\rightarrow6_{1}^{+})$ for $^{96}$Pd is 8.9 (13) $e^{2}$fm$^{4}$, nearly 100 times larger than that for $^{94}$Ru which is 0.090 (5) $e^{2}$fm$^{4}$.
- The measured $B(E2;6_{1}^{+}\rightarrow4_{1}^{+})$ for $^{96}$Pd is more than eight times larger than that of $^{94}$Ru.
- The $B(E2;4_{1}^{+}\rightarrow2_{1}^{+})$ for $^{96}$Pd was measured to be 3.8 $e^{2}$fm$^{4}$, suppressed by roughly a factor of seven compared to predictions from a seniority-conserving interaction.
- The lower limit for $B(E2;4_{1}^{+}\rightarrow2_{1}^{+})$ in $^{94}$Ru is suggested to be as large as 46 $e^{2}$fm$^{4}$.
- The $B(E2;4_{1}^{+}\rightarrow2_{1}^{+})$ value for $^{72}$Ni was measured to be 50(9) $e^{2}$fm$^{4}$.
- The $6_{2}^{+}$ state in $^{94}$Ru is calculated to be 35 keV above the $8_{1}^{+}$ state with the jun45 interaction in the full $fpg$ space.
- The overlaps between the special $I=4$ and $6$, $\alpha$ states and states constructed from coupled $J=2$ pairs are 0.9982 and 0.9977, respectively.

## Context
This work extends the concept of partial conservation of seniority in $j=9/2$ subshells, previously identified by Escuderos (2006) and Van Isacker (2014), to explain E2 transition properties in semimagic nuclei. The analysis builds on earlier studies of seniority coupling in $N=50$ isotones and neutron-rich Ni isotopes, including work by Honma (2009) on the jun45 interaction and by various authors on $^{94}$Ru and $^{96}$Pd. The results enable future exploration of other $j=9/2$ nuclei, including $N=82$ isotones and neutron-rich Pb isotopes, to better understand the sharp phase transition between seniority coupling regimes induced by cross-orbital interactions.
