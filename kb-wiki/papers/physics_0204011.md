# Generalization of Boltzmann Equilibration Dynamics

> We propose a novel approach in the study of transport phenomena in dense systems or systems with long range interactions where multiple particle interactions must be taken into consideration. Within Boltzmann's kinetic formalism, we study the influence of other interacting particles in terms of a random distortion of energy and momentum conservation occurring when multi-particle interactions are considered as binary collisions. Energy and momentum conservation still holds exactly but not in each model binary collision. We show how this new system differs from the Boltzmann system and we note that our approach naturally explains the emergence of Tsallis-like equilibrium statistics in physically relevant systems in terms of the long since neglected physics of interacting and dense systems.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a generalization of the Boltzmann equation for dense systems or systems with long-range interactions by replacing the exact energy-momentum delta functions in binary collision terms with distributions over random variables that represent the influence of other interacting particles; this approach naturally yields Tsallis-like equilibrium statistics as a consequence of fluctuations in the conservation constraints, rather than by introducing Tsallis statistics as an a priori assumption.

## Method

The authors modify the standard Boltzmann transition rate by replacing the delta functions enforcing exact energy and momentum conservation in each binary collision with ensemble-averaged delta functions containing a random momentum vector epsilon and a random energy scaling factor gamma, which absorb the effects of multi-particle interactions. They then derive a detailed balance condition for the generalized transition rates and show that the equilibrium distribution of a foreground particle is given by the ensemble average of a Boltzmann factor over the distribution of gamma. For the specific case where gamma follows a Gamma distribution peaked around unity, the equilibrium distribution takes the analytic form of a Tsallis distribution.

## Key numbers

* The variance of gamma, Var(gamma) = alpha / lambda^2, and the average of gamma, Avg(gamma) = alpha / lambda, are defined in terms of the Gamma distribution parameters alpha and lambda.
* The Tsallis nonextensitivity parameter q_T is expressed as q_T = 1 + 1/alpha = <gamma^2> / <gamma>^2.
* The inverse foreground temperature beta_F is given by beta_F = (alpha/lambda) * beta = <gamma> * beta, where beta = 1/T is the inverse background temperature.
* The equilibrium distribution of the foreground particle is f^eq(p) = C [1 - beta_F (1 - q_T) E_p]^{1/(1 - q_T)}.

## Context

This work extends the Boltzmann kinetic formalism (Boltzmann 1964) to dense systems where multi-particle interactions are significant, a problem that had been largely abandoned due to divergences in successive correction terms (Cohen 1973). The approach differs from Lim (2001) by not introducing exact Tsallis equilibration upfront, and it complements the work of Beck (2001) on fluctuations in Langevin parameters and Wilk and Wlodarczyk (2000) on temperature fluctuations in the Boltzmann factor. The formalism enables the study of transport phenomena in systems with long-range interactions, inelastic collisions, and high number densities, and it provides a microscopic foundation for the widely observed Tsallis-like equilibrium distributions in such systems.
