# Quark Models and Radial Oscillations: Decoding the HESS J1731-347 Compact Object's Equation of State

> We investigate the peculiar nature of strange stars through an analysis of different quark models, i.e. vBag model and CFL model equation of states at different parameter sets, and focus on understanding the equation of state governing the intriguing central compact object (CCO) within the supernova remnant HESS J1731-347, with a mass and radius of $M = 0.77^{+0.20}_{-0.17} M_{\odot}$ and $R = 10.4^{+0.86}_{-0.78}$ km, respectively. Additionally, we compare the radial oscillations of two models to determine the frequency of the HESS J1731-347 compact object at its maximum mass. The frequencies of radial oscillations are computed for each of the four EoSs considered. In total, the 10 lowest radial frequencies for each of those EoSs have been computed. By delving into these aspects, we aim at deepening our understanding of strange stars and their connection to the observed HESS J1731-347 mass-radius relationship.

## Key claim

The paper investigates whether the central compact object in HESS J1731-347, with its unusually low mass of 0.77 solar masses, can be modeled as a strange star using vBag and color-flavor-locked (CFL) quark matter equations of state, rather than as a conventional neutron star, and computes the radial oscillation frequencies that such an object would emit if it is indeed a strange star.

## Method

The authors solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for four different quark matter equations of state: two vBag model parameter sets (with vector coupling constant K_v = 6 and 9 GeV^-2 at fixed effective bag constant B_eff = 55 MeV/fm^3) and two CFL model parameter sets (with different values of the superconducting gap Δ, bag constant B, and strange quark mass m_s). They then solved the Sturm-Liouville eigenvalue problem for radial oscillations using the general relativistic pulsation equations to compute the 10 lowest radial oscillation frequencies for each equation of state at the mass M = 0.77 M_⊙ corresponding to the HESS J1731-347 compact object.

## Key numbers

- HESS J1731-347 compact object mass: M = 0.77^{+0.20}_{-0.17} M_⊙ and radius: R = 10.4^{+0.86}_{-0.78} km
- vBag1 (B = 55 MeV/fm^3, K_v = 9 GeV^-2): maximum mass 2.15 M_⊙ at radius 11.76 km; radius at 1.4 M_⊙ is 12.15 km; radius at 0.77 M_⊙ is 10.43 km; dimensionless tidal deformability at 1.4 M_⊙ is 158
- vBag2 (B = 55 MeV/fm^3, K_v = 6 GeV^-2): maximum mass 2.06 M_⊙ at radius 11.23 km; radius at 1.4 M_⊙ is 11.55 km; radius at 0.77 M_⊙ is 9.90 km; dimensionless tidal deformability at 1.4 M_⊙ is 119
- CFL1 (B = 60 MeV/fm^3, Δ = 150 MeV, m_s = 150 MeV): maximum mass 2.62 M_⊙ at radius 13.50 km; radius at 1.4 M_⊙ is 12.47 km; radius at 0.77 M_⊙ is 10.47 km; dimensionless tidal deformability at 1.4 M_⊙ is 484
- CFL2 (B = 60 MeV/fm^3, Δ = 100 MeV, m_s = 0 MeV): maximum mass 2.36 M_⊙ at radius 12.39 km; radius at 1.4 M_⊙ is 11.86 km; radius at 0.77 M_⊙ is 10.05 km; dimensionless tidal deformability at 1.4 M_⊙ is 212
- Fundamental radial oscillation frequency (n=0) at M = 0.77 M_⊙: vBag1 = 6.83 kHz, vBag2 = 7.15 kHz, CFL1 = 6.47 kHz, CFL2 = 6.74 kHz
- First excited radial oscillation frequency (n=1) at M = 0.77 M_⊙: vBag1 = 18.08 kHz, vBag2 = 18.98 kHz, CFL1 = 14.18 kHz, CFL2 = 14.63 kHz

## Context

This work extends previous studies of strange stars using the vBag model (Klaehn et al. 2015, Rather et al. 2021) and CFL model (Lugones and Horvath 2003) to specifically address the unusually low-mass compact object in HESS J1731-347, which was recently characterized by Doroshenko et al. (2022). The minimum possible neutron star mass of 1.17 M_⊙ from Suwa et al. (2018) suggests this object may be an exotic strange star rather than a neutron star. While several prior studies (Li et al. 2023, Sagun et al. 2023, Oikonomou et al. 2023, Huang et al. 2023, Das et al. 2023, Chu 2023) have examined whether HESS J1731-347 could be a neutron star or strange star, none have calculated the radial oscillation frequencies, which this work provides as potential asteroseismology signatures for future gravitational wave detectors.
