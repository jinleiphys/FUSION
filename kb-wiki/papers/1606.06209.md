# The Muon Experimental Anomalies Are Explained by a New Interaction Proportional to Charge

> The "proton size puzzle" and the "muon anomalous moment problem" are incomplete descriptions of significant discrepancies of Standard Model calculations with experiments. What is particularly new is that the experiments and theory confront a new regime of ultra-precise physics where traditional piece-meal analysis methods fail to be self-consistent. At current levels of precision the proton size $r_{p}$, the Rydberg constant $R_{\infty}$, the fine structure constant $α$ and the electron mass (Compton wavelength $λ_{c}$) are inextricably coupled, so that the actual discrepancies might be almost anywhere, while merely {\it appearing} to be muon-derived through a historical order of assumptions. We have conducted a new global fit to all of the relevant data using the entire body of Standard Model theory. A conventional $χ^{2}$ statistic is used to fit all relevant fundamental constants with and without a generic "no-name" boson of undetermined spin that interacts universally with leptons and hadrons proportional to electric charge. The analysis discovers a new local minimum region of $χ^{2}$ where all of $r_{p}, \, R_{\infty}, \, α, \, λ_{e}$ have new values compared to previous work, while accommodating all of the data, unlike previous determinations. A new particle $X$, possibly related to the "dark photon" but more generally defined, is predicted to be observed in electron- and muon-based experiments.

## Key claim

The muon experimental anomalies (the "proton size puzzle" and the "muon anomalous moment problem") can be explained by a new universal interaction proportional to electric charge, rather than by muon-specific new physics, provided one recognizes that the fundamental constants (proton radius, Rydberg constant, fine structure constant, and electron mass) are inextricably coupled and cannot be treated independently in ultra-precise fits.

## Method

The authors performed a global chi-squared fit to all relevant data (electron and muon anomalous magnetic moments, muonic hydrogen Lamb shift, electronic hydrogen and deuterium spectroscopy, and the electron mass-to-Planck constant ratio) using the full Standard Model theory plus a generic "no-name" boson of undetermined spin that couples universally to leptons and hadrons proportional to electric charge. The fit varied the fine structure constant alpha, the Rydberg constant R_infinity, the proton charge radius r_p, the electron Compton wavelength lambda_c, and the new boson parameters (coupling alpha_X and mass m_X), comparing the null model (alpha_X = 0) against the model with the new interaction.

## Key numbers

* The null model (Standard Model with no new interaction) is ruled out by more than 15 units of chi-squared for m_X > 50 MeV.
* The best-fit proton charge radius is r_p = 0.84113(27) fm, close to the muonic hydrogen value.
* The best-fit fine structure constant shift is delta_alpha/alpha_bullet = -3.1(2.1) x 10^(-10) relative to the CODATA2014 value.
* The best-fit Rydberg constant shift is delta_R/R_bullet = 610(430) x 10^(-12) relative to the CODATA2014 value.
* The best-fit coupling parameter xi = alpha_X / m_X^2 = 1.40(38) x 10^(-11) for m_X = 50 MeV.
* The muon anomalous moment discrepancy is 2.9 x 10^(-9) (reported as 4.6 sigma or larger in the literature).
* The muonic hydrogen Lamb shift discrepancy is 75 GHz from atomic QED calculations (reported as exceeding 7 sigma).

## Context

This work extends the approach of Miller et al. (2011), who emphasized that the proton size puzzle signals something deeply wrong with current physics, by enlarging the scope to a global fit that includes all relevant fundamental constants. The analysis contradicts the widespread conclusion that any new universal interaction would produce unobserved effects in electrons, showing instead that the electron data are circularly dependent on the assumed values of fundamental constants. The paper enables future tests by predicting the muonic deuterium Lamb shift (to be measured by the CREMA collaboration) and by providing a framework for interpreting upcoming Fermilab measurements of a_mu and new muon-proton scattering experiments.
