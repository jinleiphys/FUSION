# Constraining the density dependence of symmetry energy from nuclear masses

> Empirically determined values of the nuclear volume and surface symmetry energy coefficients from nuclear masses are expressed in terms of density distributions of nucleons in heavy nuclei in the local density approximation. This is then used to extract the value of the symmetry energy slope parameter $L$. The density distributions in both spherical and well deformed nuclei calculated within microscopic framework with different energy density functionals give $L = 59.0 \pm 13.0$ MeV. Application of the method also helps in a precision determination of the neutron skin thickness of nuclei that are difficult to measure accurately.

# Constraining the density dependence of symmetry energy from nuclear masses

## Key claim
The symmetry energy slope parameter L is constrained to 59.0 ± 13.0 MeV by combining empirically determined volume and surface symmetry energy coefficients from nuclear masses with microscopic density distributions of heavy nuclei, but this value depends on the choice of functional form for the density dependence of the symmetry energy and is nearly independent of whether spherical (208Pb) or deformed (238U) nuclei are used.

## Method
The authors express the nuclear symmetry energy coefficient a_sym(A) in terms of volume and surface symmetry energy coefficients C_v(ρ0) and C_s, then use the local density approximation to relate these to the density-dependent symmetry energy C_v(ρ) in heavy nuclei. They employ three different functional forms for C_v(ρ) (a simple power law, a form separating kinetic and interaction contributions, and a DDM3Y-inspired form) and apply them to microscopic density distributions from relativistic mean-field (RMF) models for both spherical 208Pb and deformed 238U nuclei. The empirical constraints C_v^0 = 32.1 ± 0.31 MeV, C_s^0 = 58.91 ± 1.08 MeV, and ρ0 = 0.155 ± 0.008 fm^{-3} are used to extract L by requiring consistency with Eq. (13) relating C_s to the density derivative of C_v(ρ).

## Key numbers
- L = 59.0 ± 13.0 MeV, the combined range from all three functional forms and both nuclear systems
- For 208Pb: Case I gives L = 64.8 ± 7.2 MeV, R_skin = 0.195 ± 0.022 fm; Case II gives L = 64.1 ± 6.8 MeV, R_skin = 0.196 ± 0.021 fm; Case III gives L = 58.2 ± 11.7 MeV, R_skin = 0.193 ± 0.018 fm
- For 238U: Case I gives L = 62.5 ± 6.2 MeV, R_skin = 0.192 ± 0.022 fm; Case II gives L = 62.2 ± 6.8 MeV, R_skin = 0.191 ± 0.021 fm; Case III gives L = 56.0 ± 10.0 MeV, R_skin = 0.184 ± 0.014 fm
- Empirical volume symmetry coefficient C_v^0 = 32.1 ± 0.31 MeV and surface symmetry coefficient C_s^0 = 58.91 ± 1.08 MeV, taken from the double-difference analysis of Jia et al.
- Saturation density ρ0 = 0.155 ± 0.008 fm^{-3}
- Correlation coefficient between L and R_skin is 0.93 for both 208Pb and 238U, with rms error on L values of approximately 7.2 MeV

## Context
This work extends the approach of Agrawal et al. (2013) by testing the robustness of the extracted L value against different functional forms for the density dependence of symmetry energy and against the choice of heavy nucleus (spherical 208Pb versus deformed 238U). It builds on the precise determination of C_v and C_s from nuclear mass double differences by Jia et al., and on earlier estimates of L from analyses of the pygmy dipole resonance (L = 64.8 ± 15.7 MeV), giant dipole resonance (L ≈ 52 ± 7 MeV), and isospin diffusion (L = 88 ± 25 MeV). The method enables a precision determination of neutron skin thickness for nuclei that are difficult to measure experimentally, and provides tighter constraints on the isovector part of the nuclear equation of state around saturation density.
