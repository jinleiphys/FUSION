# Production of heavy and superheavy nuclei in massive fusion reactions

> Within the framework of a dinuclear system (DNS) model, the evaporation-residue excitation functions and the quasi-fission mass yields in the $^{48}$Ca induced fusion reactions are investigated systematically and compared with available experimental data. Maximal production cross sections of superheavy nuclei based on stable actinide targets are obtained. Isotopic trends in the production of the superheavy elements Z=110, 112-118 based on the actinide isotopic targets are analyzed systematically. Optimal evaporation channels and combinations as well as the corresponding excitation energies are proposed. The possible factors that influencing the isotopic dependence of the production cross sections are analyzed. The formation of the superheavy nuclei based on the isotopes U with different projectiles are also investigated and calculated.

# Production of heavy and superheavy nuclei in massive fusion reactions

## Key claim
Within the dinuclear system (DNS) model, the authors systematically reproduce available experimental evaporation-residue cross sections and quasi-fission mass yields for ⁴⁸Ca-induced fusion reactions, and predict that ⁴⁸Ca-induced reactions with actinide targets yield larger production cross sections for superheavy nuclei with Z ≥ 113 than cold fusion reactions, *provided* the optimal isotopic target, evaporation channel, and excitation energy are selected.

## Method
The DNS model treats compound-nucleus formation as a diffusion process in mass asymmetry, solved numerically via microscopically derived master equations for nucleon transfer between two touching nuclei, coupled to the relative motion. The evaporation-residue cross section is computed as a product of three factors summed over partial waves up to J_max = 30: the transmission probability through the Coulomb barrier (using an empirical coupled-channel model with a barrier distribution), the fusion probability (from the master-equation solution including quasi-fission and heavy-fragment fission via Kramers formulas), and the survival probability against fission during neutron evaporation (using a statistical model with back-shifted Bethe level densities and shell corrections). Quasi-fission mass yields are obtained by integrating the time-dependent fragment distribution weighted by the quasi-fission rate.

## Key numbers
- Maximal production cross section for the reaction ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁵Cm in the 3n channel: 3.7⁺³·⁶₋₁·₈ pb at an excitation energy of 37.9 MeV.
- Maximal production cross section for the reaction ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁸Cm in the 3n channel: 1.2 pb.
- For the reaction ⁴⁰Ar+²³⁸U, the 4n channel gives a cross section of 2.1 pb at an excitation energy of 42 MeV.
- In cold fusion reactions, the production cross section drops from 0.2 μb for ⁴⁸Ca+²⁰⁸Pb to 1 pb for ⁷⁰Zn+²⁰⁸Pb, and below 0.1 pb for Z ≥ 113.
- The quasi-fission barrier is calculated with constant parameters: Γ = 2.8 MeV, ħω^B_qf = 2.0 MeV, ħω = 3.0 MeV; fission of the heavy fragment uses ħω_g.s. = ħω_f = 1.0 MeV, Γ₀ = 2 MeV.
- Maximal angular momentum cutoff: J_max = 30.

## Context
This work extends the DNS model of Adamian et al. (2004) and the authors' own earlier studies (Feng et al., 2006, 2007) to a systematic survey of ⁴⁸Ca-induced reactions on actinide targets, covering elements Z = 110–118. It directly compares with experimental data from Dubna (Oganessian et al., 2004, 2006, 2007) and GSI (Hofmann et al., 2007), and with calculations by Adamian et al. (2004) for Pu isotopes. The results enable experimental planning by identifying optimal isotopic combinations (e.g., ²³⁵,²³⁸U, ²⁴⁵,²⁴⁷,²⁵⁰Cm, ²⁴⁸,²⁴⁹Bk) and evaporation channels (predominantly 3n and 4n) for synthesizing new superheavy elements, and highlight the need for radioactive-beam facilities to access neutron-rich actinide targets that approach the predicted island of stability.
