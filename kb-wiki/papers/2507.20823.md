# Rotating Proto-Neutron Stars Admixed with Mirror Dark Matter: A two fluid approach

> This work investigates the impact of mirror dark matter (DM) on the global properties of rotating neutron stars (NSs) across evolutionary stages, from hot, lepton-rich protoneutron stars (PNSs) to cold, catalyzed NSs along the Kelvin-Helmholtz timescale. The baryonic matter (BM) is modeled using a relativistic mean-field (RMF) approach with density-dependent couplings, while the dark sector mirrors the visible sector with analogous thermodynamic conditions. Using a two-fluid formalism with purely gravitational DM-BM interaction, we find that rotation enlarges the star, whereas DM admixture increases compactness and enhances gravitational stability. However, increased compactness due to DM lowers the threshold for rotational instabilities, making DM-admixed stars more susceptible. Rotation decreases {central temperature behavior} by redistributing thermal energy over a larger volume and reducing central density, while DM raises temperatures by deepening the gravitational potential and increasing thermal energy. Stars become more prone to collapse and rotational instabilities as frequency ($ν$) rises and the polar-to-equatorial radius ratio ($r_p/r_e$) decreases, especially near the Keplerian limit ($ν_K$). DM-admixed stars also show higher surface gravitational redshifts due to their compactness. Our results qualitatively agree with universal relations primarily derived for rotating cold stars. These findings highlight competing effects of rotation and DM on NS thermal evolution, structure, and observables, potentially offering indirect probes of DM within NSs.

# Rotating Proto-Neutron Stars Admixed with Mirror Dark Matter: A Two Fluid Approach

## Key claim

Mirror dark matter admixture increases the compactness of rotating proto-neutron stars and enhances gravitational stability, but this increased compactness simultaneously lowers the threshold for rotational instabilities, making dark-matter-admixed stars more susceptible to collapse and rotational disruption, particularly near the Keplerian limit. The authors assume the dark matter fluid is non-rotating while the baryonic matter rotates uniformly, which isolates gravitational effects but represents a simplification that may not capture torque-free differential rotation induced by frame-dragging.

## Method

The authors model baryonic matter using a relativistic mean-field approach with density-dependent DDME2 couplings, while the dark sector mirrors the visible sector with analogous thermodynamic conditions and meson fields. A two-fluid formalism with purely gravitational dark matter-baryonic matter interaction is implemented by modifying the standard rns code to treat two interpenetrating fluids, with the dark matter component assumed static and non-rotating within the stellar core. The evolution from hot, lepton-rich proto-neutron stars to cold, catalyzed neutron stars is tracked along the Kelvin-Helmholtz timescale by fixing lepton fractions and entropy per baryon at different stages: neutrino-trapped phase (Y_l=0.4, s_B=1 and Y_l=0.2, s_B=2), neutrino-transparent phase, and the cold T=0 configuration.

## Context

This work extends the two-fluid formalism for rotating neutron stars admixed with mirror dark matter developed by Konstantinou (2024) and Issifu (2024) to the evolutionary stages of proto-neutron stars, from the hot neutrino-trapped phase through deleptonization to cold catalyzed configurations. The approach follows the thermodynamic evolution scheme of Pons (1998) and Prakash (1996) for proto-neutron star cooling, and tests universal relations for rotating neutron star shapes from Morsink (2007) and Silva (2021) and mass-radius relations from Konstantinou (2022). The results enable future comparisons with NICER observations of pulsars such as PSR J0030+0451, PSR J0740+6620, and PSR J0437-4715, as well as gravitational-wave constraints from GW170817, potentially offering indirect probes of dark matter within neutron stars.
