# Hypermassive Neutron Star Disk Outflows and Blue Kilonovae

> We study mass ejection from accretion disks around newly-formed hypermassive neutron stars (HMNS). Standard kilonova model fits to GW170817 require at least a lanthanide-poor ('blue') and lanthanide-rich ('red') component. The existence of a blue component has been used as evidence for a HMNS remnant of finite lifetime, but average disk outflow velocities from existing long-term HMNS simulations fall short of the inferred value ($\sim 0.25c$) by a factor of $\sim 2$. Here we use time-dependent, axisymmetric hydrodynamic simulations of HMNS disks to explore the limits of the model and its ability to account for observations. For physically plausible parameter choices compatible with GW170817, we find that hydrodynamic models that use shear viscosity to transport angular momentum cannot eject matter with mass-averaged velocities larger than $\sim 0.15c$. While outflow velocities in our simulations can exceed the asymptotic value for a steady-state neutrino-driven wind, the increase in the average velocity due to viscosity is not sufficient. Therefore, viscous HMNS disk winds cannot reproduce by themselves the ejecta properties inferred from multi-component fits to kilonova light curves from GW170817. Three possible resolutions remain feasible within standard merger ejecta channels: more sophisticated radiative transfer models that allow for photon reprocessing between ejecta components, inclusion of magnetic stresses, or enhancement of the dynamical ejecta. We provide fits to our disk outflow models once they reach homologous expansion.

## Key claim

Viscous hydrodynamic models of accretion disks around hypermassive neutron stars (HMNS) cannot, by themselves, produce ejecta with mass-averaged velocities matching the blue kilonova component inferred from GW170817 (approximately 0.25c), even though the ejected mass can be brought closer to observed values with suitable parameter choices. The authors find that three alternative resolutions remain feasible: more sophisticated radiative transfer models allowing photon reprocessing between ejecta components, inclusion of magnetic stresses, or enhancement of the dynamical ejecta.

## Method

The authors performed time-dependent, axisymmetric (2D) hydrodynamic simulations of HMNS accretion disks using the FLASH3 code, with azimuthal rotation, a pseudo-Newtonian potential, shear viscosity via an alpha-prescription, and neutrino emission/absorption modeled with a leakage scheme and annular light bulb approximation. The HMNS was treated as a reflecting inner boundary with prescribed neutrino luminosities that decay as t^{-1/2} after 10 ms, and upon collapse to a black hole the boundary became absorbing and moved inward. A baseline model with HMNS mass 2.65 solar masses, disk mass 0.1 solar masses, HMNS radius 20 km, HMNS lifetime 10 ms, viscosity parameter alpha=0.05, and initial electron fraction Ye=0.1 was varied by changing one parameter at a time to explore sensitivity.

## Key numbers

- Baseline model ejected 0.023 solar masses of lanthanide-poor (Ye > 0.25, "blue") material with mass-averaged velocity 0.038c, and 0.010 solar masses of lanthanide-rich (Ye < 0.25, "red") material with velocity 0.091c
- The maximum mass-averaged velocity for blue ejecta across all models was 0.099c (model L53, with HMNS luminosity 2x10^53 erg/s), still below the inferred 0.2-0.3c from observations
- Model with longest HMNS lifetime (30 ms, model t30) produced 0.058 solar masses blue ejecta at 0.093c and 0.002 solar masses red ejecta at 0.159c
- Model with highest viscosity (alpha=0.10, model alpha10) produced 0.035 solar masses blue ejecta at 0.070c and 0.008 solar masses red ejecta at 0.135c
- In the most extreme case (model L53), ejected mass with speeds above 0.2c and Ye > 0.25 was less than 3x10^{-3} solar masses
- The asymptotic velocity for a steady-state neutrino-driven wind is approximately 0.12c for a luminosity of 2x10^52 erg/s
- The maximum velocity from alpha particle recombination energy alone is approximately 0.12c

## Context

This work extends the long-term HMNS disk simulations of MF14 (2014) and Lippuner (2017) by systematically exploring parameter space compatible with GW170817, and contradicts the inference that blue kilonova emission necessarily implies a HMNS remnant with disk outflows matching observed velocities. The results are compatible with the radiative transfer work of Kawaguchi (2018), who found that photon reprocessing by dynamical ejecta can eliminate the need for disk winds faster than 0.1c. The authors suggest that magnetic stresses, as seen in GRMHD simulations by Siegel (2018) and Fernandez (2018), or enhanced dynamical ejecta via viscous effects as in Shibata (2017c) and Radice (2018), may resolve the velocity discrepancy.
