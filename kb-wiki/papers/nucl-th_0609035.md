# Temperature effects on the nuclear symmetry energy and symmetry free energy with an isospin and momentum dependent interaction

> Within a self-consistent thermal model using an isospin and momentum dependent interaction (MDI) constrained by the isospin diffusion data in heavy-ion collisions, we investigate the temperature dependence of the symmetry energy $E_{sym}(ρ, T)$ and symmetry free energy $F_{sym}(ρ, T) $ for hot, isospin asymmetric nuclear matter. It is shown that the symmetry energy $E_{sym}(ρ, T)$ generally decreases with increasing temperature while the symmetry free energy $F_{sym}(ρ, T)$ exhibits opposite temperature dependence. The decrement of the symmetry energy with temperature is essentially due to the decrement of the potential energy part of the symmetry energy with temperature. The difference between the symmetry energy and symmetry free energy is found to be quite small around the saturation density of nuclear matter. While at very low densities, they differ significantly from each other. In comparison with the experimental data of temperature dependent symmetry energy extracted from the isotopic scaling analysis of intermediate mass fragments (IMF's) in heavy-ion collisions, the resulting density and temperature dependent symmetry energy $E_{sym}(ρ, T) $ is then used to estimate the average freeze-out density of the IMF's.used to estimate the average freeze-out density of the IMF's.

## Key claim

The symmetry energy \(E_{sym}(\rho, T)\) generally decreases with increasing temperature, while the symmetry free energy \(F_{sym}(\rho, T)\) exhibits the opposite temperature dependence; this behavior is essentially due to the decrement of the potential energy part of the symmetry energy with temperature, and the difference between the two quantities is quite small around saturation density but significant at very low densities.

## Method

The authors used a self-consistent thermal model based on an isospin- and momentum-dependent interaction (MDI) constrained by isospin diffusion data from heavy-ion collisions. They solved the coupled equations for the single-particle energy, chemical potentials, and phase-space distribution functions via an iterative self-consistency scheme at finite temperatures, then extracted the symmetry energy and symmetry free energy from the parabolic approximation to the energy per nucleon and free energy per nucleon of asymmetric nuclear matter.

## Key numbers

- The symmetry energy at zero temperature and normal nuclear matter density \(\rho_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\) is set to 31.6 MeV.
- The incompressibility \(K_0\) of cold symmetric nuclear matter at saturation density is 211 MeV.
- For the MDI interaction with \(x=0\), the average freeze-out density \(\rho_f\) from TAMU data is between about \(0.41\rho_0\) and \(0.52\rho_0\); for INDRA-ALADIN data it is between about \(0.42\rho_0\) and \(0.75\rho_0\).
- For the MDI interaction with \(x=-1\), the average freeze-out density \(\rho_f\) from TAMU data is between about \(0.57\rho_0\) and \(0.68\rho_0\); for INDRA-ALADIN data it is between about \(0.58\rho_0\) and \(0.84\rho_0\).
- The zero-temperature symmetry energy for MDI with \(x=0\) is parameterized as \(31.6(\rho/\rho_0)^{0.69}\) MeV, and with \(x=-1\) as \(31.6(\rho/\rho_0)^{1.05}\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of the temperature dependence of the symmetry energy by Chen et al. (2006) using a simplified degenerate Fermi gas model, and by Zuo et al. (2003) and Chen et al. (2001) using other approaches. The present self-consistent treatment with a momentum-dependent interaction constrained by isospin diffusion data (Chen et al., 2005; Li et al., 2005) provides a more realistic description of hot asymmetric nuclear matter. The results enable estimation of the average freeze-out density of intermediate mass fragments in heavy-ion collisions by comparing the calculated temperature-dependent symmetry energy with experimental data from Texas A&M University (Shetty et al., 2006) and the INDRA-ALADIN collaboration, and they suggest that isotopic scaling measurements can probe the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy.
