# Constraints on Neutron Star Crusts From Oscillations in Giant Flares

> We show that the fundamental seismic shear mode, observed as a quasi-periodic oscillation in giant flares emitted by highly-magnetized neutron stars, is particularly sensitive to the nuclear physics of the crust. The identification of an oscillation at ~ 30 Hz as the fundamental crustal shear mode requires a nuclear symmetry energy that depends very weakly on density near saturation. If the nuclear symmetry energy varies more strongly with density, then lower frequency oscillations, previously identified as torsional Alfven modes of the fluid core, could instead be associated with the crust. If this is the case, then future observations of giant flares should detect oscillations at around 18 Hz. An accurate measurement of the neutron skin thickness of lead will also constrain the frequencies predicted by the model.

# Constraints on Neutron Star Crusts From Oscillations in Giant Flares

## Key claim
The identification of a ~30 Hz quasi-periodic oscillation as the fundamental crustal shear mode requires a nuclear symmetry energy that depends very weakly on density near saturation; if the symmetry energy varies more strongly with density, then lower-frequency oscillations previously attributed to torsional Alfvén modes of the fluid core could instead be crustal modes, and future giant flares should then show oscillations at around 18 Hz.

## Method
The authors computed torsional shear oscillation frequencies of neutron star crusts using a Newtonian perturbation model in plane-parallel geometry with constant gravitational acceleration, correcting for spherical geometry by mimicking the angular Laplacian. They constructed crust models from a liquid droplet model combined with Skyrme equations of state for homogeneous nucleonic matter, fixing the crust-core transition density at 0.07 fm⁻³ and using the shear modulus from Monte Carlo simulations of Coulomb interactions. The fundamental (n=0, l=2) crust shear mode frequency was then calculated as a function of neutron star mass for nine different Skyrme interactions and one APR model, and compared to observed QPO frequencies from the 2004 hyperflare of SGR 1806-20 and the SGR 1900+14 hyperflare.

## Key numbers
- The fundamental crust shear mode frequency is near 18 Hz for models with a symmetry energy that varies more strongly with density; the 18 Hz QPO detected in the SGR 1806-20 hyperflare falls into this predicted range.
- For the SGR 1806-20 hyperflare, if 18 Hz is the fundamental (l=2), a reasonable fit to higher frequencies gives: 29 Hz (l=3), 92 Hz (l=10), 150 Hz (l=16).
- For the SGR 1900+14 hyperflare, a fundamental at ~18 Hz gives mode identifications: 28 Hz (l=3), 53.5 Hz (l=6), 84 Hz (l=9), 155 Hz (l=17).
- The 3 sigma upper limit on a QPO amplitude at 18 Hz in the SGR 1900+14 hyperflare is 7% rms; the 18 Hz QPO in SGR 1806-20 had an amplitude of 4%.
- The neutron skin thickness of lead (δR) for the APR model is estimated at 0.20 fm.
- Increasing the crust-core transition density from 0.07 fm⁻³ to 0.1 fm⁻³ changes the fundamental crust mode frequency by only about 1%.
- Recent calculations indicate the shear modulus may be lower by ~10%, which would reduce mode frequencies by a further 3-4%.

## Context
This work extends previous calculations of crust shear modes by Piro (2005) and Samuelsson (2007), which used the higher shear speed crust model of Douchin and Haensel (2001) based on the SLy4 Skyrme interaction, yielding higher fundamental frequencies near 28-29 Hz. The authors show that once the correct angular scaling is accounted for, even that model requires a rather low stellar mass. The paper makes a novel connection to the PREX experiment at Jefferson Lab, which will measure the neutron skin thickness of lead and thereby constrain the symmetry energy dependence. This work enables future giant flare observations to distinguish between crust-dominated and core-dominated mode interpretations, and suggests that a sequence of modes including overtones will be necessary for correct mode identification.
