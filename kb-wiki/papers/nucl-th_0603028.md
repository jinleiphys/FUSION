# Challenges about entropy

> The physical meaning of entropy is analyzed in the context of statistical, nuclear, atomic physics and cosmology. Only the microcanonical Boltzmann entropy leads to no contradictions in several simple, elementary and for thermodynamics important situations. The conventional canonical statistics implies several serious errors and misinterpretations. This has far reaching consequences for phase-separations as well for the usual formulations of the second law. Applications in cosmology suffer under the ubiquitous use of canonical statistics. New reformulations in terms of microcanonical statistics are highly demanded but certainly difficult.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the microcanonical Boltzmann entropy is the only formulation that avoids contradictions in thermodynamics, while the conventional canonical (Boltzmann-Gibbs) statistics leads to serious errors and misinterpretations, particularly for phase separations and in cosmology; this claim is hedged by the recognition that microcanonical reformulations are "highly demanded but certainly difficult."

## Method

The author analyzes the physical meaning of entropy across statistical, nuclear, atomic physics and cosmology by contrasting the microcanonical Boltzmann-Planck entropy \(S = k \ln W\) with the canonical ensemble approach. The analysis uses the example of an ideal gas freely expanding into a larger volume to show that entropy can increase without heat exchange or temperature change, and examines the liquid-gas phase transition in a mesoscopic cluster of 3000 sodium atoms to demonstrate that the microcanonical caloric curve exhibits back-bending (a convex region of \(S(E)\)) that is absent in the canonical ensemble. The paper also discusses implications for black hole thermodynamics and the generalized second law.

## Key numbers

- For an ideal gas freely expanding from volume \(V_1\) to \(V_2 > V_1\), the entropy change is \(\Delta S = N \ln(V_2/V_1)\).
- The liquid-gas transition in a 3000-atom sodium cluster at 1 atm pressure extends over a latent heat range from approximately 0.3 eV/atom to approximately 1.3 eV/atom.
- At 0.442 eV/atom in the sodium cluster, the fragment distribution includes 329 monomers, 7.876 dimers, and 1.295 quadrimers on average.
- For a Schwarzschild black hole, the horizon radius is \(R = 2M = 2E\), the area is \(A = 16\pi M^2\), the Hawking temperature is \(T = 1/(8\pi M)\), and the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy is \(S_{BH} = A/4 = \pi R^2 = 4\pi M^2\).

## Context

This work extends the author's prior critiques of canonical statistics (Gross 2001, 2004, 2005) and challenges the conventional wisdom that canonical and microcanonical ensembles are equivalent, especially for first-order phase transitions where the thermodynamic limit is avoided. The paper contradicts the interpretation of nuclear fragmentation by Bowman et al. (1987) and engages with debates on the arrow of time, siding with Uffink (2001) against Allahverdian and Gurzadyan (2002) on whether the second law relates to cosmology. It enables future work on microcanonical formulations for self-gravitating systems and black hole thermodynamics, referencing Bousso (2002) and Padmanabhan (2006) as directions for further development.
