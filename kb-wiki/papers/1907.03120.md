# Hot neutron stars with microscopic equations of state

> We study the properties of hot beta-stable nuclear matter using equations of state derived within the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach at finite temperature including consistent three-body forces. Simple and accurate parametrizations of the finite-temperature equations of state are provided. The properties of hot neutron stars are then investigated within this framework, in particular the temperature dependence of the maximum mass. We find very small temperature effects and analyze the interplay of the different contributions.

## Key claim

The temperature dependence of the maximum mass of a hot neutron star is very small (about one percent decrease at 50 MeV for the softest equation of state), contrary to some relativistic-mean-field models that predict increasing stability with temperature; this small effect is due to competition between increasing thermal pressures and increasing isospin symmetry of the stellar nuclear matter.

## Method

The authors extended the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) approach to finite temperature using realistic two-nucleon potentials (Argonne V18, Bonn B, Nijmegen 93) with compatible three-body forces, employing the frozen-correlations approximation where single-particle potentials at finite temperature are approximated by those at zero temperature. They calculated the free energy density for symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter, provided analytical parametrizations, and used a parabolic law to interpolate to arbitrary proton fractions. The beta-stable, neutrino-free stellar matter composition was determined from chemical equilibrium and charge neutrality, and the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations were solved assuming a constant temperature inside the star with a cold crust attached.

## Key numbers

- Maximum gravitational masses at zero temperature for the four equations of state: 2.50 solar masses (BOB), 2.36 solar masses (V18), 2.25 solar masses (N93), 1.96 solar masses (UIX).
- Relative decrease of the maximum gravitational mass at T=50 MeV: about one percent for the UIX equation of state, and less for the stiffer equations of state.
- Maximum baryonic mass shows a stronger decrease with temperature, up to about 6 percent at T=50 MeV.
- The adiabatic index at T=50 MeV remains below 1.5 at high density for all microscopic equations of state, with strong density dependence.
- The thermal pressure and thermal energy density at T=50 MeV are of the order of a few percent of the cold values at high density.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous zero-temperature BHF calculations with three-body forces (Li et al., 2018) to finite temperature, providing microscopic equations of state for use in neutron star merger simulations. The results contradict predictions from relativistic-mean-field models (e.g., LS220, Shen, SFHo) that often show increasing maximum mass with temperature, and instead agree with earlier BHF results (Burgio et al., 2011; Baldo et al., 2012) showing a slight reduction. The parametrizations enable future detailed merger simulations to test whether the temperature dependence of the nuclear equation of state can be neglected for stellar stability studies.
