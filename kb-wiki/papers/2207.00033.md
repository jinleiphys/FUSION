# Equation of state in neutron stars and supernovae

> Neutron stars and supernovae provide cosmic laboratories of highly compressed matter at supra nuclear saturation density which is beyond the reach of terrestrial experiments. The properties of dense matter is extracted by combining the knowledge of nuclear experiments and astrophysical observations via theoretical frameworks. A matter in neutron stars is neutron rich, and may further accommodate non-nucleonic degrees of freedom such as hyperons and quarks. The structure and composition of neutron stars are determined by equations of state of matter, which are the primary subject in this chapter. In case of supernovae, the time evolution includes several dynamical stages whose descriptions require equations of state at finite temperature and various lepton fractions. Equations of state also play essential roles in neutron star mergers which allow us to explore new conditions of matter not achievable in static neutron stars and supernovae. Several types of hadron-to-quark transitions, from first order transitions to crossover, are reviewed, and their characteristics are summarized.

## Key claim
This chapter reviews how equations of state for dense matter in neutron stars and supernovae are constructed by combining nuclear experiments, astrophysical observations, and theoretical frameworks, with the caveat that the precise determination of nuclear matter properties near saturation density still carries significant uncertainties that affect neutron star radius predictions by approximately 0.5-2 km.

## Method
The authors survey the conditions of density, temperature, and proton fraction realized in neutron stars, supernovae, and neutron star mergers, then examine how these conditions map to equations of state. They review nuclear matter theories including microscopic approaches (variational, quantum-Monte Carlo, many-body perturbation theory based on two- and three-nucleon forces) and phenomenological models (Skyrme-type interactions and relativistic mean-field theory), and discuss how hadron-to-quark transitions from first order to crossover are classified.

## Key numbers
- Nuclear saturation density: n0 = 0.16 fm^-3, corresponding to mass density rho0 = 3 x 10^14 g/cm^3
- Average mass density of a canonical neutron star (~1.4 solar masses): ~7 x 10^14 g/cm^3, about twice the nuclear density
- Heaviest neutron star discovered (PSR J0740+6620): mass 2.08 +/- 0.07 solar masses
- Radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star from NICER: 12.45 +/- 0.65 km
- Radius of a 2.08 solar mass neutron star from NICER: 12.35 +/- 0.75 km
- Upper bound on radius for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star from GW170817: R < 13.4 km
- LIGO estimate for neutron star radii in GW170817: R = 11.9 (+1.4, -1.4) km
- Nuclear incompressibility K(Yp=0.5): 220-260 MeV
- Symmetry energy S(n=n0): 24-36 MeV
- Slope parameter L: 30-90 MeV
- Supernova explosion energy: ~10^51 erg
- Total neutrino energy from SN 1987A: ~3 x 10^53 erg
- Average neutrino energy detected from SN 1987A: ~10 MeV over ~10 seconds

## Context
This chapter extends the long-standing effort to connect nuclear physics to astrophysical objects, building on the foundational work of Shapiro (1983) on neutron star structure and Haensel (2007) on dense matter. It synthesizes recent observational constraints from gravitational wave detection (GW170817, LIGO collaboration 2018) and NICER X-ray measurements (Miller 2021, Riley 2021) with nuclear many-body calculations by Akmal (1998), Togashi (2017), and Drischler (2017). The review enables future work on interpreting next-generation observations of supernova neutrinos and gravitational waves from neutron star mergers, and prepares for expected discoveries from the diffuse supernova neutrino background.
