# Nuclear level statistics: extending the shell model theory to higher temperatures

> The Shell Model Monte Carlo (SMMC) approach has been applied to calculate level densities and partition functions to temperatures up to ~ 1.5 - 2 MeV, with the maximal temperature limited by the size of the configuration space. Here we develop an extension of the theory that can be used to higher temperatures, taking into account the large configuration space that is needed. We first examine the configuration space limitation using an independent-particle model that includes both bound states and the continuum. The larger configuration space is then combined with the SMMC under the assumption that the effects on the partition function are factorizable. The method is demonstrated for nuclei in the iron region, extending the calculated partition functions and level densities up to T ~ 4 MeV. We find that the back-shifted Bethe formula has a much larger range of validity than was suspected from previous theory. The present theory also shows more clearly the effects of the pairing phase transition on the heat capacity.

## Key claim

The authors extend Shell Model Monte Carlo (SMMC) calculations of nuclear partition functions and level densities from the previously limiting temperature of ~1.5-2 MeV up to ~4 MeV by combining a small-space SMMC calculation with a large-space independent-particle calculation under the assumption that the effects on the partition function are factorizable, finding that the back-shifted Bethe formula remains valid to much higher temperatures than previously suspected.

## Method

The authors first examine configuration space limitations using an independent-particle model that includes both bound states and the continuum, calculating single-particle spectra and scattering phase shifts in a Woods-Saxon potential well. They then combine the larger configuration space with SMMC results from a truncated fpg9/2 major shell using the formula ln Z'_v = ln Z'_v,tr + ln Z'_sp - ln Z'_sp,tr, where the interaction corrections approach zero at high temperature as 1/T. The method is demonstrated for nuclei in the iron region (56Fe and 57Fe), with the extended partition functions converted to level densities via the saddle-point formula.

## Key numbers

- SMMC calculations were previously limited to temperatures below ~1.5-2 MeV for medium mass nuclei
- The extended method reaches temperatures up to T ~ 4 MeV
- For 56Fe, the back-shifted Bethe formula fit gives a = 5.87 MeV^-1 and Δ = 1.03 MeV (from partition function fit), with rms deviation of 0.05
- For 56Fe, the entropy fit gives a = 5.82 MeV^-1 (rms deviation ≈ 0.3)
- For 56Fe, the level density fit gives a = 5.9 MeV^-1 and Δ = 1.35 MeV (χ^2 per degree of freedom below 1), valid up to excitation energies ~90 MeV
- For 57Fe, the partition function fit gives a = 6.04 MeV^-1 and Δ = -1.29 MeV (rms deviation ≈ 0.08)
- For 57Fe, the level density fit gives a = 6.05 MeV^-1 and Δ = -1.3 MeV
- The independent-particle model gives a ≈ 5.25 MeV^-1 (K ≈ 10.7) for 56Fe
- The Woods-Saxon potential parameters: V0 = -51 + 33(N-Z)/A MeV, a = 0.67 fm, r0 = 1.32 fm, λ_ls = -0.44

## Context

This work extends the SMMC approach previously applied by Alhassid (1999, 2000) and Nakada (1997) to higher temperatures by addressing the configuration space limitation that restricted earlier calculations to a single major shell. The continuum treatment improves upon the approximate method of Agostino (1998), which enclosed the nucleus in a sphere, by using an exact phase-shift approach corresponding to the limit R → ∞. The finding that the back-shifted Bethe formula remains valid up to T ~ 4 MeV enables more reliable microscopic calculations of nuclear partition functions needed for pre-supernova collapse models and stellar thermal reaction rates, particularly for nuclei where experimental data are unavailable.
