# Adiabatic versus Faddeev for (d,p) and (p,d) reactions

> The finite range adiabatic wave approximation provides a practical method to analyze (d,p) or (p,d) reactions, however until now the level of accuracy obtained in the description of the reaction dynamics has not been determined. In this work, we perform a systematic comparison between the finite range adiabatic wave approximation and the exact Faddeev method. We include studies of $^{11}$Be(p,d)$^{10}$Be(g.s.) at $E_p=$5, 10 and 35 MeV; $^{12}$C(d,p)$^{13}$C(g.s.) at $E_d=$7, 12 and 56 MeV and $^{48}$Ca(d,p)$^{49}$Ca(g.s.) at $E_d=$19, 56 and 100 MeV. Results show that the two methods agree within $\approx 5%$ for a range of beam energies ($E_d \approx 20-40$ MeV) but differences increase significantly for very low energies and for the highest energies. Our tests show that ADWA agrees best with Faddeev when the angular momentum transfer is small $Δl=0$ and when the neutron-nucleus system is loosely bound.

## Key claim

The finite range adiabatic wave approximation (ADWA) agrees with the exact Faddeev method within approximately 5% for a range of beam energies around 10-20 MeV/u, but differences increase significantly at very low and very high beam energies; the agreement is best when the angular momentum transfer is small (Δl=0) and when the neutron-nucleus system is loosely bound.

## Method

The authors performed a systematic comparison between finite range ADWA and the exact Faddeev Alt-Grassberger-Sandhas (AGS) method for three reactions: ¹¹Be(p,d)¹⁰Be(g.s.) at proton energies of 5, 10, and 35 MeV; ¹²C(d,p)¹³C(g.s.) at deuteron energies of 7, 12, and 56 MeV; and ⁴⁸Ca(d,p)⁴⁹Ca(g.s.) at deuteron energies of 19, 56, and 100 MeV. Both calculations used the same three-body Hamiltonian with the CH89 global optical potential for nucleon-target interactions and realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions (Reid for ADWA, CDBonn for Faddeev), with the neutron bound state generated by a Woods-Saxon potential adjusted to reproduce experimental binding energies.

## Key numbers

- For ¹¹Be(p,d)¹⁰Be at Ep=10 MeV, the difference between Faddeev and ADWA was -1.12% at the first peak (θ=1°)
- For ¹¹Be(p,d)¹⁰Be at Ep=5 MeV, the difference was -22.90% at the first peak (θ=1°)
- For ¹¹Be(p,d)¹⁰Be at Ep=35 MeV, the difference was 18.50% at the first peak (θ=1°)
- For ¹²C(d,p)¹³C at Ed=12 MeV, the difference was -2.74% at the first peak (θ=15°)
- For ¹²C(d,p)¹³C at Ed=56 MeV, the difference was -14.26% at the first peak (θ=1°)
- For ⁴⁸Ca(d,p)⁴⁹Ca at Ed=19 MeV, the difference was -6.12% at the first peak (θ=8°)
- For ⁴⁸Ca(d,p)⁴⁹Ca at Ed=100 MeV, the difference was 51.0% at the first peak (θ=0°)
- For ¹¹Be(p,d)¹⁰Be at 10 MeV with l=0 and Sn=0.5 MeV, the difference was 1%; with l=3 and Sn=0.5 MeV, the difference increased to 30%
- For ¹¹Be(p,d)¹⁰Be at 10 MeV with l=0 and Sn=6.0 MeV, the difference was 23%

## Context

This work extends the validation of the finite range ADWA method, which was developed by Johnson and Tandy (1974) as an improvement over the zero-range ADWA of Johnson and Soper (1970) and the Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA). The study directly addresses the need identified in Nunes et al. (2011) where discrepancies between FR-ADWA and Faddeev for Ar isotopes varied from 6-19%. The results enable experimentalists to use the computationally inexpensive ADWA method with quantified uncertainties for (d,p) and (p,d) reactions at intermediate beam energies, particularly for the science program at the future Facility for Rare Isotope Beams.
