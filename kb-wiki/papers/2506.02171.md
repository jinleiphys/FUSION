# Kolmogorov-Arnold Wavefunctions

> This work investigates Kolmogorov-Arnold network-based wavefunction ansatz as viable representations for quantum Monte Carlo simulations. Through systematic analysis of one-dimensional model systems, we evaluate their computational efficiency and representational power against established methods. Our numerical experiments suggest some efficient training methods and we explore how the computational cost scales with desired precision, particle number, and system parameters. Roughly speaking, KANs seem to be 10 times cheaper computationally than other neural network based ansatz. We also introduce a novel approach for handling strong short-range potentials-a persistent challenge for many numerical techniques-which generalizes efficiently to higher-dimensional, physically relevant systems with short-ranged strong potentials common in atomic and nuclear physics.

## Key claim

Kolmogorov-Arnold network-based wavefunction ansatze are approximately 10 times cheaper computationally than other neural network-based ansatze for variational quantum Monte Carlo simulations, though this factor depends on details of software and hardware implementation and was only demonstrated for the specific one-dimensional models and parameters explored.

## Method

The authors implemented variational Monte Carlo using a KAN-based wavefunction ansatz where the wavefunction is written as a Gaussian factor multiplied by the exponential of a bosonic KAN, with the KAN's line-functions parametrized by quadratic splines. They trained the ansatz using the ADAM optimizer with learning rates from 10^-3 to 10^-7, estimating energies and gradients via Metropolis sampling from the probability distribution |psi|^2, and employed a staged training protocol that gradually increases the coupling strength g, the number of spline knots (up to 300), and the Monte Carlo sample size. For handling strong short-range potentials, they introduced an additional term to the ansatz consisting of a sum over particle pairs of a single-variable spline function of the coordinate difference, designed to capture the cusp behavior required by Kato's condition.

## Key numbers

- KAN-based ansatze were found to be roughly 10 times less costly in floating point operations per training step (with 10,000 samples) compared to MLP-based ansatze providing similar accuracy.
- For the solvable model with Hamiltonian H = sum_i (-1/(2m) d^2/dx_i^2 + 1/2 m omega^2 x_i^2) + sum_{i<j} (g delta(x_i - x_j) + sigma |x_i - x_j|), the exact ground state energy is E_0 = N omega/2 - m g^2 N(N^2 - 1)/24.
- The number of spline knots in all presented examples remained below 300.
- Training used learning rates ranging from 10^-3 to 10^-7.
- Monte Carlo sample sizes ranged from 2048 to 32768 for KAN training and from 64,000 to 1,000,000 for MLP training in the typical comparison shown.

## Context

This work extends the use of Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KAN-MIT, 2024) to variational quantum Monte Carlo for continuous-variable many-body systems, building on prior neural network wavefunction approaches such as Restricted Boltzmann Machines for spin systems (Carleo, 2017) and feed-forward networks for systems with Coulomb forces (Paulinet, 2020; FermiNet). The paper addresses the persistent challenge of strong short-range potentials common in nuclear physics, where prior work (Lovato, 2020-2024; Wen, 2025; Wang, 2024) has focused on short-range forces, and introduces a method for handling cusp behavior that generalizes to higher-dimensional systems relevant to atomic and nuclear physics.
