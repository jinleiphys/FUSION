# Testing the LMA solution with solar neutrinos independently of solar models

> We perform a comparative study of two methods of determining the survival probabilities of low, intermediate, and high energy solar neutrinos that emphasizes the general agreement between the Large Mixing Angle (LMA) solution and extant solar neutrino data. The first analysis is oscillation parameter-independent and the second analysis involves an approximate calculation of the survival probabilities in the three energy ranges that depends only on oscillation parameters. We show that future experiments like BOREXino, CLEAN, Heron, LENS and MOON, that measure $pp$ and $^7$Be neutrinos, will facilitate a stringent test of the LMA solution independently of the Standard Solar Model (SSM), without recourse to earth-matter effects. Throughout, we describe the role of SSM assumptions on our results. If the LMA solution passes the test without needing to be modified, it may be possible to establish that $θ_x$ is nonzero at more than $2σ$ assuming the SSM prediction for the $pp$ flux is correct.

# Testing the LMA solution with solar neutrinos independently of solar models

## Key claim

The Large Mixing Angle (LMA) solution provides a consistent explanation of all solar neutrino data when Standard Solar Model (SSM) flux constraints are imposed, but minor discrepancies remain at the 1σ level, specifically, the implied flux of low-energy neutrinos appears slightly high and intermediate-energy neutrinos slightly low. If the LMA solution survives future tests without modification, it may be possible to establish that the mixing angle θ_x is nonzero at more than 2σ, assuming the SSM prediction for the pp flux is correct.

## Method

The authors performed a comparative study of two analysis methods: a model-independent (oscillation parameter-independent) analysis that treats flux normalizations β and survival probabilities P_L, P_I, P_H as independent parameters, and an LMA analysis using approximate three-neutrino MSW survival probabilities that depend only on oscillation parameters δm²_s, θ_s, and θ_x. Both analyses were applied to current solar neutrino data from Gallium experiments, Chlorine (Homestake), and SNO (CC and NC measurements), first without SSM constraints and then with SSM flux normalizations imposed, and the same framework was projected onto future measurements from BOREXino, CLEAN, HERON, LENS, and MOON.

## Key numbers

- Current Gallium rate R_Ga = 0.54 ± 0.03 (SAGE/GALLEX/GNO)
- Current Chlorine rate R_Cl = 0.31 ± 0.03 (Homestake)
- Current SNO CC rate R_SNO^CC = 0.30 ± 0.01 (SNO D₂O and salt phases)
- Current SNO NC rate R_SNO^NC = 0.88 ± 0.06 (SNO D₂O and salt phases)
- High-energy survival probability P_H = 0.34 ± 0.03 (from SNO CC/NC ratio)
- Low-energy survival probability with SSM constraint: P_L = 0.69 ± 0.11
- Intermediate-energy survival probability with SSM constraint: P_I = 0.37 ± 0.16
- Best-fit tan²θ_s = 0.45 ± 0.06 (using δm²_s = (7.9 ± 0.55)×10⁻⁵ eV² from KamLAND)
- SSM fractional uncertainties: δ(β_L^SSM) = 0.010, δ(β_I^SSM) = 0.122, δ(β_H^SSM) = 0.163
- Future projected uncertainties: pp CC 2.6%, ⁷Be CC 2.5%, ⁸B CC 3.5%; ⁸B NC 4.8%; pp ES 1.1%, ⁷Be ES 2.6%
- With future data and SSM constraints: δ(P_L) = 3%, δ(P_I) = 11%, δ(P_H) = 6%
- With future data and SSM constraints: projected 1σ uncertainties on tan²θ_s of order 0.03 and sin²θ_x of order 0.015

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Barger et al. (2002) and responds to the tension between the LMA solution and solar neutrino data noted in earlier studies, particularly the low Chlorine rate relative to SuperKamiokande and SNO measurements. The authors build on the method of Hata (early model-independent test of SSM) and use the updated SSM of Bahcall (2004). The paper demonstrates that future measurements of pp and ⁷Be neutrinos by BOREXino, CLEAN, HERON, LENS, and MOON will enable a test of the LMA solution that is independent of solar model assumptions, without requiring earth-matter effects. If the LMA solution passes this test, it opens the possibility of establishing a nonzero θ_x (θ₁₃) at more than 2σ, which would have implications for CP violation in the lepton sector and for future long-baseline experiments.
