# Pressure and chemical potentials in the inner crust of a cold neutron star within Hartree-Fock and extended Thomas-Fermi methods

> Self-consistent mean-field methods with Skyrme-type effective interactions and semiclassical approximations, such as the Thomas-Fermi approach and its extensions are particularly well-suited for describing in a thermodynamically consistent way the various phases of the dense matter present in the interior of neutron stars. These methods have been applied to predict the composition of the different regions, including the inner crust constituted by nuclear clusters coexisting with free neutrons and electrons. Because of the computational cost, the energy is typically calculated for a few selected average baryon number densities, and the results are interpolated to obtain the pressure numerically. However, this may introduce systematic errors in the calculations of the global structure of a neutron star and its dynamical evolution. In this paper, we show how the full equation of state can be consistently calculated within the same framework by deriving exact formulas for the chemical potentials and for the pressure that can be easily implemented in existing computer codes. These formulas are applicable to both catalyzed and accreted crusts. We discuss in each case the suitable conditions to impose to determine the composition. Numerical examples are also presented and discussed. Results from refined calculations of the BSk24 equation of state for the inner crust of nonaccreted neutron stars and the corresponding adiabatic index are provided.

## Key claim

The paper derives exact formulas for the pressure and chemical potentials in the inner crust of a cold neutron star that can be directly implemented in existing Hartree-Fock and extended Thomas-Fermi computer codes, thereby avoiding the systematic errors introduced by numerical interpolation of the energy at a few selected densities. The formulas are applicable to both catalyzed and accreted crusts, but the authors note that thermodynamic inconsistencies may arise if the nucleon density distributions are parametrized rather than obtained from exact solutions of the Euler-Lagrange equations.

## Method

The authors start from the thermodynamic definitions of pressure and chemical potentials for a Wigner-Seitz cell in the inner crust, then derive explicit expressions within both the Hartree-Fock (HF) theory with Skyrme-type effective interactions and the extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) approximation up to fourth order. For the HF method, they use the Kohn-Sham approximation for Coulomb exchange and write the energy as an integral of a semilocal energy density functional; for the ETF method, they express kinetic-energy densities and spin-current densities in terms of nucleon densities and their gradients. The composition is determined by minimizing the energy under appropriate equilibrium conditions: for catalyzed matter the cluster chemical potential vanishes and beta equilibrium holds, while for accreted crusts the number of clusters is fixed. Numerical examples are provided using the BSk24 equation of state for nonaccreted neutron stars.

## Key numbers

- The neutron-drip transition occurs at mean densities above approximately 4 × 10^11 g cm^-3, where free neutrons appear in the inner crust.
- Typical neutron numbers per Wigner-Seitz cell are N_n^(c) ~ 10^2-10^3, while proton numbers are N_p^(c) ~ 40, based on results from the BSk24 equation of state (Pearson et al., 2018).
- The paper provides refined calculations of the BSk24 equation of state for the inner crust of nonaccreted neutron stars and the corresponding adiabatic index, though specific numerical values for pressure or chemical potentials at given densities are not listed in the truncated text.

## Context

This work extends the analytical pressure formula derived in Pearson et al. (2012) for the ETF framework, which was previously applied in unified equations of state (Pearson et al., 2018, BSk22-26). It addresses the issue that numerical differentiation of the total energy per nucleon, as used in the Negele and Vautherin (1973) equation of state, can introduce systematic errors that alter the adiabatic index and oscillation mode spectrum. The new formulas enable thermodynamically consistent calculations of the full equation of state without fitting errors, and can be adapted to nuclear pasta phases and to include pairing effects (discussed in Chamel et al., 2024).
