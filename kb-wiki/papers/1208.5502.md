# Anisotropy of photon production: Initial eccentricity or magnetic field

> Recent measurements of the azimuthal anisotropy of direct photons in heavy-ion collisions at the energies of RHIC show that it is of the same order as the hadronic one. This finding appears to contradict the expected dominance of photon production from a quark-gluon plasma at an early stage of a heavy-ion collision. A possible explanation of the strong azimuthal anisotropy of the photons, given recently, is based on the presence of a large magnetic field in the early phase of a collision. In this letter, we propose a method to experimentally measure the degree to which a magnetic field in heavy-ion collisions is responsible for the observed anisotropy of photon production. The experimental test proposed in this letter may potentially change our understanding of the non-equilibrium stage and possible thermalization in heavy-ion collisions.

# Anisotropy of photon production: Initial eccentricity or magnetic field

## Key claim

The authors propose that by measuring photon elliptic flow \(v_2^\gamma\) as a function of pion elliptic flow \(v_2^\pi\) within a narrow centrality class (e.g., 30–40%), one can experimentally distinguish whether the observed photon azimuthal anisotropy arises from the initial-state eccentricity of the fireball or from a strong magnetic field present at early times. The test relies on the fact that the magnetic field is approximately independent of the fluctuating eccentricity at fixed centrality, whereas hadronic flow is proportional to that eccentricity.

## Method

The authors analyze the probability distribution of the participant eccentricity \(\epsilon_2\) for Au-Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=200\) GeV using a standard Glauber model, and compute the magnetic field asymmetry \(\langle B_y^2 - B_x^2 \rangle^{1/2}\) as a function of initial eccentricity, accounting for fluctuating proton positions. They then propose selecting events within a narrow centrality class (e.g., 30–40%), measuring \(v_2^\pi\) for pions with \(p_t < 1\) GeV to reflect the initial eccentricity, and correlating this with the measured \(v_2^\gamma(p_t)\) for photons. If \(v_2^\gamma\) is proportional to \(v_2^\pi\), the anisotropy is driven by initial eccentricity; if \(v_2^\gamma\) is independent of \(v_2^\pi\), the magnetic field is the dominant source.

## Key numbers

- The average temperature of photon radiation at \(\sqrt{s}=200\) GeV in heavy-ion collisions is \(T_{\rm ave} \approx 220\) MeV, measured by PHENIX.
- The amplitude of the magnetic field in non-central heavy-ion collisions can reach up to a few \(m_\pi^2 \approx 10^{18}\) Gauss.
- The proposed measurement is best suited for mid-central and peripheral collisions, with a specific example centrality class of 30–40%.
- The condition for reliable measurement of \(v_2^\pi\) is \(\frac{1}{N_\pi} < v_2^\pi\), where \(N_\pi\) is the number of hadrons used to determine \(v_2^\pi\).

## Context

This work addresses the puzzle raised by PHENIX measurements (Adare 2011) showing that direct photon azimuthal anisotropy \(v_2^\gamma\) is comparable to hadronic \(v_2\), contradicting expectations from hydrodynamic calculations (Dion 2011) which predicted \(v_2^\gamma\) almost an order of magnitude smaller. The authors build on the proposal by Basar (2012) that a strong magnetic field, previously studied in the context of the chiral magnetic effect (Kharzeev 2007, Skokov 2009, Bzdak 2011), could generate photon anisotropy via mechanisms such as the scale anomaly of QCD\(\times\)QED (Basar 2012), the axial anomaly (Fukushima 2012), or synchrotron radiation (Tuchin 2012). The proposed measurement would enable experimental discrimination between the hadronic-flow mechanism (van Hees 2011) and the magnetic-field mechanism, potentially resolving whether early thermalization occurs in heavy-ion collisions.
