# Are Standard Solar Models Reliable?

> The sound speeds of solar models that include element diffusion agree with helioseismological measurements to a rms discrepancy of better than 0.2% throughout almost the entire sun. Models that do not include diffusion, or in which the interior of the sun is assumed to be significantly mixed, are effectively ruled out by helioseismology. Standard solar models predict the measured properties of the sun more accurately than is required for applications involving solar neutrinos.

## Key claim

Standard solar models that include element diffusion agree with helioseismological measurements to a root-mean-square discrepancy of better than 0.2% throughout almost the entire sun, with no adjustable parameters; this agreement rules out models with significant interior mixing or without diffusion, but the authors acknowledge they cannot rigorously exclude the possibility of finely-tuned nonstandard models that reproduce the same sound speeds.

## Method

The authors compared sound speeds inferred from the first year of helioseismological data from the instrument developed by Tomczyk et al. (1995) with sound speeds computed from several standard solar models, including the 1995 Bahcall-Pinsonneault model with and without diffusion, and a model incorporating the improved OPAL equation of state and opacities. They also computed sound speeds for non-standard models, including the mixed model of Cumming and Haxton (1996) and a model with the $^3{\rm He}(\alpha,\gamma)^7{\rm Be}$ reaction rate set to zero, and compared the fractional differences $\delta c/c$ as a function of radial position in the sun.

## Key numbers

- The rms discrepancy between predicted and measured sound speeds for the OPAL EOS model is 0.1%.
- The rms discrepancy for standard models including diffusion is better than 0.2% throughout almost the entire sun.
- The No Diffusion model shows discrepancies as large as 1%, with a mean squared discrepancy 22 times larger than the best model with diffusion.
- The mixed model of Cumming and Haxton shows discrepancies ranging from +8% to -5%.
- The $S_{34}=0$ model shows maximum discrepancies of about 1%, with a mean squared discrepancy 19 times larger than the standard OPAL EOS model.
- The predicted event rate for the chlorine experiment is $9.5^{+1.2}_{-1.4}$ SNU.
- The predicted event rate for the gallium experiments is $137^{+8}_{-7}$ SNU.
- The mean molecular weight in the standard solar model varies from 0.86 in the deep interior to 0.62 at the outer region of nuclear fusion ($R = 0.25 R_\odot$) to 0.60 near the solar surface.
- The temperature in the standard model varies by a factor of 53 over the range in which sound speed has been measured, and mean molecular weight varies by 43%.

## Context

This work extends earlier comparisons with less complete helioseismological data by Basu et al. (1996), Christensen-Dalsgaard et al. (1993), and Elsworth et al. (1990), and provides direct observational evidence against the mixed solar model proposed by Cumming and Haxton (1996) and against models without diffusion such as that of Turck-Chieze and Lopez (1993). The results enable stronger constraints on non-standard solar models proposed to solve the solar neutrino problem, showing that any changes to temperature or mean molecular weight must be smaller than about 0.4% to remain consistent with helioseismology, which is far too small to significantly alter the predicted neutrino fluxes.
