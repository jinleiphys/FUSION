# Bottomonium suppression from the three-loop QCD potential

> We compute the suppression of bottomonium in the quark-gluon plasma using the three-loop QCD static potential. The potential describes the spin-averaged bottomonium spectrum below threshold with a less than 1% error. Within potential nonrelativistic quantum chromodynamics and an open quantum systems framework, we compute the evolution of the bottomonium density matrix. The values of the quarkonium transport coefficients are obtained from lattice QCD measurements of the bottomonium in-medium width and thermal mass shift; we additionally include for the first time a vacuum contribution to the dispersive coefficient $γ$. Using the three-loop potential and the values of the heavy quarkonium transport coefficients, we find that the resulting bottomonium nuclear modification factor is consistent with experimental observations, while at the same time reproducing the lattice measurements of the in-medium width.

## Key claim

Using the three-loop QCD static potential (renormalon-subtracted and supplemented with a nonperturbative correction) in the Lindblad equation for bottomonium evolution yields a nuclear modification factor consistent with LHC experimental data, while simultaneously reproducing lattice QCD measurements of the in-medium width. The improvement over previous Coulomb-potential analyses is quantified by a weighted relative mean square error of Δ² = 2.1×10⁻³ for the three-loop potential versus Δ² = 0.22 for the Coulomb potential.

## Method

The authors solve the Lindblad equation for the bottomonium density matrix in the strict quantum Brownian limit using the open-source code QTraj, with the singlet and octet static potentials taken at three-loop order in renormalon-subtracted pNRQCD. The transport coefficients κ and γ are extracted by fitting the computed thermal widths and mass shifts to lattice QCD data from Larsen et al. (2019), with the vacuum contribution γ(T=0) estimated from the chromoelectric condensate and gluelump mass. The evolution is coupled to 3+1D quasiparticle anisotropic hydrodynamics simulations, sampling approximately 140,000 physical trajectories and 30 quantum trajectories per physical trajectory, with projection onto bottomonium eigenstates obtained from the improved potential.

## Key numbers

- The bottom quark mass fixed by fitting the 1S state to the PDG value M(1S) = 9.445 GeV yields M_b = 4.921 GeV for the three-loop potential (compared to M_b = 4.850 GeV for the Coulomb potential)
- The three-loop potential reproduces the spin-averaged bottomonium spectrum with less than 1% error: M(2S) = 10.042 GeV (PDG: 10.017 GeV), M(3S) = 10.395 GeV (PDG: 10.355 GeV), M(1P) = 9.887 GeV (PDG: 9.888 GeV), M(2P) = 10.279 GeV (PDG: 10.251 GeV)
- The vacuum dispersive coefficient γ(T=0) = 0.017 GeV³, obtained from the chromoelectric condensate ⟨0|g²E²(0)|0⟩ = -0.2 GeV⁴ and gluelump mass Λ_E = 1.25 GeV
- The thermal momentum diffusion coefficient extracted from lattice data is κ̂ = 1.88 ± 0.16 (weighted average from Υ and χ_b fits: κ̂_Υ = 2.08 ± 0.20, κ̂_χ = 1.52 ± 0.26)
- The weighted relative mean square error for the three-loop potential is Δ² = 2.1×10⁻³, compared to Δ² = 0.22 for the Coulomb potential with κ̂ = 0.33
- The evolution is calculated from τ_med = 0.6 fm until a final temperature T_f = 250 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous analyses by Brambilla et al. (2020, 2022, 2023) that used the leading-order Coulomb potential for bottomonium suppression, by incorporating the three-loop QCD static potential computed by Anzai, Smirnov, and collaborators (2009, 2013) with renormalon subtraction from Pineda (2001). The improved potential provides a realistic description of the bottomonium spectrum below threshold, in contrast to the poorly-performing Coulomb potential noted by Brambilla (1999). The approach enables more accurate predictions for LHC bottomonium nuclear modification factor data from ALICE (2020), ATLAS (2022), and CMS (2018, 2023), and could be extended beyond the Brownian limit by including E/(πT) corrections as done for the Coulomb potential in Brambilla (2022).
