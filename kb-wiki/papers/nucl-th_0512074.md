# Analyzing $η'$ Photoproduction Data on the Proton at Energies of 1.5--2.3 GeV

> A combined analysis of the existing data on the reactions $γp \to p η^\prime $ and $pp \to ppη^\prime $, based on a relativistic meson exchange model of hadronic interactions, is presented.

## Key claim
A combined analysis of γp → pη′ and pp → ppη′ data within a relativistic meson-exchange model shows that differential cross sections alone cannot uniquely determine nucleon resonance parameters or the NNη′ coupling constant, despite the higher precision of CLAS data compared to earlier SAPHIR data.

## Method
The photoproduction reaction was described in a tree-level approximation using a relativistic meson-exchange model, with a phenomenological contact term introduced to guarantee gauge invariance of the full amplitude. The model included nucleonic, mesonic, and nucleon resonance currents (S11, P11, and for CLAS data also spin-3/2 resonances P13 and D13). The hadro-production reaction part was obtained by replacing the photon with a relevant meson and attaching the nucleon to it, with the only free parameter being the coupling constant at the nucleon-resonance transition vertex.

## Key numbers
- The NNη′ coupling constant gNNη′ cannot be much larger than 3 based on SAPHIR data analysis; with CLAS data a more stringent upper limit of gNNη′ < 2 was obtained.
- S11 resonance mass values differing by about 100 MeV produce hardly distinguishable differential cross sections.
- A bump structure around W = 2.09 GeV in the total cross section is predicted, likely due to the D13(2080) resonance (a two-star resonance in PDG) and possibly the P11(2100) resonance.
- The incident photon energy range analyzed is 1.5–2.3 GeV, corresponding to s-channel energies W = √s in GeV as shown in the figures.

## Context
This work extends earlier conflicting model calculations for SAPHIR data by Zhao (who attributed angular distribution shape to resonance interference), Chiang et al. (who emphasized t-channel meson exchange versus Regge trajectory), and Sibirtsev et al. (who claimed mesonic current dominance). The analysis enables future work requiring more exclusive observables such as beam and target asymmetries, measurements on neutron/deuteron targets, and improved theoretical models including coupled-channel effects and realistic Nη′ final-state interactions.
