# Centrality dependence of proton and light nuclei yields as a consequence of baryon annihilation in the hadronic phase

> The centrality dependence of the $p/π$ ratio measured by the ALICE Collaboration in 5.02 TeV Pb-Pb collisions indicates a statistically significant suppression with the increase of the charged particle multiplicity once the centrality-correlated part of the systematic uncertainty is eliminated from the data. We argue that this behavior can be attributed to baryon annihilation in the hadronic phase. By implementing the $B\bar{B} \leftrightarrow 5π$ reaction within a generalized partial chemical equilibrium framework, we estimate the annihilation freeze-out temperature at different centralities, which decreases with increasing charged multiplicity and yields $T_{\rm ann} = 132 \pm 5$ MeV in 0-5% most central collisions. This value is considerably below the hadronization temperature of $T_{\rm had} \sim 160$ MeV but above the thermal (kinetic) freeze-out temperature of $T_{\rm kin} \sim 100$ MeV. Baryon annihilation reactions thus remain relevant in the initial stage of the hadronic phase but freeze out before (pseudo-)elastic hadronic scatterings. One experimentally testable consequence of this picture is a suppression of various light nuclei to proton ratios in central collisions of heavy ions.

## Key claim

The centrality-dependent suppression of the $p/\pi$ ratio measured by ALICE in 5.02 TeV Pb-Pb collisions can be attributed to baryon annihilation in the hadronic phase, with the annihilation freeze-out temperature decreasing from about 160 MeV in peripheral collisions to $132 \pm 5$ MeV in 0-5% most central collisions; this value is below the hadronization temperature of about 160 MeV but above the kinetic freeze-out temperature of about 100 MeV.

## Method

The authors extended the partial chemical equilibrium (PCE) framework to incorporate the $B\bar{B} \leftrightarrow 5\pi$ reaction, treating annihilation and regeneration reactions in relative chemical equilibrium. They used the generalized PCE framework implemented in the Thermal-FIST package to calculate the temperature dependence of the $p/\pi$ ratio, then matched the experimentally measured centrality-dependent suppression of the $p/\pi$ ratio (scaled to peripheral 80-90% collisions) to extract the annihilation freeze-out temperature $T_{\rm ann}$ at each centrality. The analysis assumed hadronization at $T_{\rm had} = 160$ MeV and used $\langle n_\pi^{N\bar{N}} \rangle = 5$ based on experimental data on $p\bar{p}$ reactions.

## Key numbers

- Suppression factor of the $p/\pi$ ratio in 0-5% central collisions relative to peripheral (80-90%) collisions: $0.78 \pm 0.05$, with a significance of more than $4\sigma$
- Annihilation freeze-out temperature in 0-5% most central collisions: $T_{\rm ann} = 132 \pm 5$ MeV
- Hadronization temperature: $T_{\rm had} \sim 160$ MeV
- Kinetic freeze-out temperature: $T_{\rm kin} \sim 100$ MeV
- Annihilation freeze-out temperature in 80-90% peripheral collisions: 160 MeV (set equal to hadronization temperature)
- Thermal-averaged cross section of the $p\bar{p}$ reaction: $\langle \sigma_{N\bar{N}} v_{\rm rel} \rangle \sim 30-70$ mb
- Estimated equilibration time for $N\bar{N}$ annihilations: $\tau_{\rm eq}^{N\bar{N}} \sim 5-11$ fm/$c$ from cross-section estimates, or $\sim 2-3$ fm/$c$ from Monte Carlo transport simulations
- Hadronic phase lifetime in central Pb-Pb collisions: 4-8 fm/$c$

## Context

This work extends the partial chemical equilibrium framework previously used to model resonance suppression (Motornenko 2019) by incorporating baryon annihilation reactions. It addresses the long-standing discrepancy between statistical hadronization model predictions and measured proton yields in central heavy-ion collisions, which had been discussed by Becattini (2012) and Steinheimer (2012) as possible evidence for baryon annihilation. The analysis uses the recently published ALICE data from 5.02 TeV Pb-Pb collisions (ALICE 2019) where systematic uncertainties were split into centrality-correlated and uncorrelated components, enabling statistically significant observation of the $p/\pi$ suppression. The results enable future work to correct proton yields for annihilation effects before performing statistical hadronization model fits, and predict a testable suppression of light nuclei to proton ratios (d/p, $^3$He/p, $^4$He/p) in central collisions that can be verified with upcoming LHC data.
