# Mean Field Methods for Atomic and Nuclear Reactions: The Link between Time--Dependent and Time--Independent Approaches

> Three variants of mean field methods for atomic and nuclear reactions are compared with respect to both conception and applicability: The time--dependent Hartree--Fock method solves the equation of motion for a Hermitian density operator as initial value problem, with the colliding fragments in a continuum state of relative motion. With no specification of the final state, the method is restricted to inclusive reactions. The time--dependent mean field method, as developed by Kerman, Levit and Negele as well as by Reinhardt, calculates the density for specific transitions and thus applies to exclusive reactions. It uses the Hubbard--Stratonovich transformation to express the full time--development operator with two--body interactions as functional integral over one--body densities. In stationary phase approximation and with Slater determinants as initial and final states, it defines non--Hermitian, time--dependent mean field equations to be solved self--consistently as boundary value problem in time. The time--independent mean field method of Giraud and Nagarajan is based on a Schwinger--type variational principle for the resolvent. It leads to a set of inhomogeneous, non--Hermitian equations of Hartree--Fock type to be solved for given total energy. All information about initial and final channels is contained in the inhomogeneities, hence the method is designed for exclusive reactions. A direct link is established between the time--dependent and time--independent versions. Their relation is non--trivial due to the non--linear nature of mean field methods.

## Key claim

The paper establishes a direct but non-trivial link between time-dependent mean field (TDMF) and time-independent mean field (TIMF) methods for atomic and nuclear reactions, showing that TIMF emerges as a static approximation of TDMF in semi-classical approximation, under the condition that the auxiliary fields vary slowly in time.

## Method

The authors compare three mean field approaches: time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) as an initial value problem for a Hermitian density operator; the TDMF method of Kerman, Levit, Negele, and Reinhardt, which uses the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to express the time-development operator as a functional integral over one-body densities and solves a boundary value problem in time with two sets of biorthogonal single-particle functions; and the TIMF method of Giraud and Nagarajan, based on a Schwinger-type variational principle for the resolvent, yielding inhomogeneous non-Hermitian Hartree-Fock-type equations at fixed total energy. The link between TDMF and TIMF is derived by applying a static approximation to TDMF, introducing single-particle propagators via Fourier transformation, and solving the resulting integrals in stationary phase approximation.

## Key numbers

- TDHF was developed approximately 20 years before the 1998 publication date, placing its origin around 1978.
- The time interval is divided into M slices of equal length epsilon = (t_f - t_i)/M, with the limit epsilon -> 0, M -> infinity such that epsilon * M = (t_f - t_i) remains finite.
- The stationary phase approximation is applied with hbar playing the role of the small parameter l, implying a semi-classical expansion.
- The norm N(T) in the functional integral depends on T = t_f - t_i.

## Context

This work extends the mean field concept from bound state problems to scattering states, comparing the original TDHF approach with the later TDMF formalism developed by Kerman, Levit, and Negele as well as by Reinhardt, and the TIMF method of Giraud and Nagarajan. The paper establishes that TIMF is a static, semi-classical approximation of TDMF, which enables practitioners to choose between time-dependent and time-independent formulations depending on computational feasibility, noting that TDMF faces practical difficulties for (3+1)-dimensional systems while TIMF is comparable in complexity to TDHF but applicable to exclusive reactions.
