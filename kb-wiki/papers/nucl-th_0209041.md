# An effective formulation on quantum hadrodynamics at finite temperatures and densities

> According to Wick's theorem, the second order self-energy corrections of hadrons in the hot and dense nuclear matter are calculated. Furthermore, the Feynman rules are summarized, and an effective formulation on quantum hadrodynamics at finite temperatures and densities is evaluated. As the strong couplings between nucleons are considered, the self-consistency of this method is discussed in the framework of relativistic mean-field approximation. Debye screening masses of the scalar and vector mesons in the hot and dense nuclear matter are calculated with this method in the relativistic mean-field approximation. The results are different from those of thermofield dynamics and Brown-Rho conjecture. Moreover, the effective masses of the photon and the nucleon in the hot and dense nuclear matter are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper presents an effective formulation of quantum hadrodynamics at finite temperatures and densities based on Wick's theorem, using vacuum Feynman propagators while treating medium effects as condensations of nucleons and mesons; the authors claim their results for Debye screening masses differ from those obtained via thermofield dynamics and the Brown-Rho conjecture, but note that the perturbation calculation does not converge due to strong couplings and self-consistency requires using interacting propagators in the relativistic mean-field approximation.

## Method

The authors calculate second-order self-energy corrections for nucleons, antinucleons, scalar mesons, and vector mesons in hot and dense nuclear matter using Wick's theorem within the Walecka-1 model (quantum hadrodynamics 1). They employ vacuum Feynman propagators and incorporate finite-temperature effects through Fermi-Dirac and Bose-Einstein distribution functions for nucleons, antinucleons, and mesons, with the chemical potential related to baryon number density. The self-energies are then used to compute Debye screening masses of scalar and vector mesons and the effective mass of the photon in the relativistic mean-field approximation, where meson field operators are replaced by their expectation values.

## Key numbers

- Scalar meson mass fixed at \(m_\sigma = 520.0\ \mathrm{MeV}\) and vector meson mass at \(m_\omega = 783.0\ \mathrm{MeV}\) for calculations.
- Saturation density of nuclear matter at zero temperature given as \(\rho_0 = 0.148\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3}\).
- Screening masses of \(\sigma\) and \(\omega\) mesons, effective nucleon mass \(M^\ast_N\), and energy per nucleon \(E/A\) calculated as functions of nucleon number density at temperature \(T = 10.0\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (presented in Table 1).
- Properties of particles calculated as functions of temperature at \(\rho_N = \rho_0 = 0.148\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3}\) (presented in Table 2).
- Effective mass of the photon given by \(m^\ast_\gamma = \sqrt{e^2 \rho_S^p / (2 M^\ast_N)}\), where \(e^2 = 4\pi\alpha\) and \(\alpha\) is the fine structure constant.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous formulation of quantum hadrodynamics (Sun 2002, Sun 2002a, Sun 2002b) from zero temperature to finite temperatures and densities, using Wick's theorem to calculate second-order self-energies. The results for screening masses of scalar and vector mesons are compared with those from thermofield dynamics (SRK 1995) and the Brown-Rho conjecture (Brown 1991), finding differences. The method enables calculation of effective masses of hadrons and photons in hot and dense nuclear matter, and the authors note that at zero temperature their meson self-energies agree with those of Furnstahl (1988).
