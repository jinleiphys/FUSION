# Chiral Symmetry Restoration at Nonzero Temperature in the SU(3) \times SU(3) Linear Sigma Model

> We study patterns of chiral symmetry breaking at zero temperature and its subsequent restoration at nonzero temperature within the $SU(3)_{r} \times SU(3)_{\ell}$ linear sigma model. Gap equations for the masses of the scalar and pseudoscalar mesons and the non-strange and strange quark condensates are systematically derived in the Hartree approximation via the Cornwall-Jackiw-Tomboulis formalism. In the chiral limit, the chiral symmetry restoring transition is found to be first order, as predicted by universality arguments. Taking the experimental values for the meson masses, however, the transition is crossover. The absence of the $U(1)_A$ anomaly is found to drive this transition closer to being first order. At large temperatures, the mixing angles between octet and singlet states approach ideal flavor mixing.

## Key claim

The chiral symmetry restoring transition in the SU(3) × SU(3) linear sigma model is first order in the chiral limit (massless quarks), but becomes a smooth crossover when experimental meson masses are used; the presence or absence of the U(1)_A anomaly shifts the transition closer to or further from first order, respectively.

## Method

The authors study the SU(3)_r × SU(3)_ℓ linear sigma model at nonzero temperature using the Hartree approximation, systematically derived from the Cornwall-Jackiw-Tomboulis (CJT) formalism. Gap equations for the scalar and pseudoscalar meson masses and for the non-strange and strange quark condensates are solved self-consistently. The analysis covers several symmetry-breaking scenarios: the chiral limit (with and without the U(1)_A anomaly) and the physically realistic case with explicit symmetry breaking via nonzero quark masses.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the O(N) model for N_f = 2 (Lenaghan and Rischke, 2000) to the N_f = 3 case using the Hartree approximation, improving upon earlier mean-field treatments of the SU(3) linear sigma model (Schaffner-Bielich, 2000). The results are relevant for understanding the nature of the chiral phase transition in QCD, complementing lattice QCD calculations that have not unambiguously settled whether the transition is first order or crossover for physical quark masses. The study also addresses the role of the U(1)_A anomaly, which lattice data suggest becomes small near the chiral transition (lattice QCD, 1999), enabling predictions for how the transition order changes if the anomaly is suppressed at high temperature.
