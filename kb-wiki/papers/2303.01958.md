# 2022 Update of the discoveries of nuclides

> The 2022 update of the discovery of nuclide project is presented. It is the first update in four years, and 36 new nuclides were observed for the first time during 2019-2022. Isotopes that have so far only been published in conference proceedings or internal reports are also listed.

## Key claim

The 2022 update reports 36 new nuclides observed for the first time during 2019-2022, but this is the first update in four years because the number of new isotopes discovered per year was the smallest since 1944, with only six in 2019 and four in 2020.

## Method

The author compiled newly discovered nuclides from refereed journal publications between 2019 and 2022, categorizing them by year, production method (fusion evaporation, projectile fragmentation, secondary beams, spallation), and laboratory. The paper also lists nuclides that have only been reported in conference proceedings or internal reports, updating a previous compilation from 2018.

## Key numbers

- 36 new nuclides were observed for the first time during 2019-2022
- Total number of observed isotopes reached 3338 by the end of 2022
- Six new nuclides were reported in 2019: $^{11}$O, $^{220}$Np, $^{165}$Pt, $^{170}$Hg, $^{68}$Br, and $^{31}$K
- Four new nuclides were reported in 2020: $^{244}$Md, $^{235}$Cm, $^{222}$Np, and $^{211}$Pa
- 15 new nuclides were reported in 2021, including nine neutron-rich isotopes ($^{101}$Br, $^{102}$Kr, $^{105}$Rb, $^{106}$Rb, $^{108}$Sr, $^{110}$Y, $^{111}$Y, $^{114}$Zr, $^{117}$Nb) plus $^{280}$Ds, $^{13}$F, $^{249}$No, $^{214}$U, $^{150}$Yb, and $^{18}$Mg
- 11 new nuclides were reported in 2022: $^{149}$Lu, $^{207}$Th, $^{264}$Lr, $^{166}$Pm, $^{168}$Sm, $^{170}$Eu, $^{172}$Gd, $^{204}$Ac, $^{251}$Lr, $^{39}$Na, and $^{286}$Mc

## Context

This work is the seventh update of the isotope discovery project originally published in a series of papers in Atomic Data and Nuclear Data Tables from 2009 through 2013 (see for example the first paper by Ginepro (2009) and the last by Fry (2013)), with summary papers in Nuclear Physics News (Thoennessen, 2012) and Reports on Progress in Physics (Thoennessen, 2013), followed by annual updates through 2019. The update enables tracking of discovery trends, showing that the largest discovery potential remains in the neutron-rich region of the chart of nuclides, where recent discoveries used projectile fragmentation or projectile fission reactions, and that new facilities like the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams at Michigan State University will likely discover many more nuclides as they reach full beam intensity.
