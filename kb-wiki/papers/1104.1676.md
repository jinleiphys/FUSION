# The Latent Heat of Single Flavor Color Superconductivity in a Magnetic Field

> We calculate the energy release associated with first-order phase transition between different types of single flavor color superconductivity in a magnetic field.

## Key claim
The paper calculates the latent heat released during first-order phase transitions between different types of single-flavor color superconductivity in a magnetic field, but the numerical estimates rely on the approximation that quark masses are ignored and that the entire quark matter core of a compact star undergoes the transition simultaneously.

## Method
The authors use a Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL)-like effective action with a mean-field approximation to compute the pressure and entropy density for each single-flavor pairing phase (CSL, planar, polar, and A phases) as functions of temperature and magnetic field. The Gibbs free energy density is minimized with respect to the gap parameter, magnetic field B, and color magnetic fields B^l, subject to constraints from the incomplete Meissner effect in nonspherical phases. The latent heat density is then obtained from the entropy difference between phases at the phase boundaries determined by equal Gibbs free energy.

## Key numbers
- For two flavors: CSL gap at zero temperature Δ₀ ≈ 0.238 MeV, reference magnetic field H₀ ≈ 5.44 × 10¹⁴ G
- For three flavors: CSL gap at zero temperature Δ₀ ≈ 0.0864 MeV, reference magnetic field H₀ ≈ 1.97 × 10¹⁴ G
- The largest latent heat density calculated for the three-flavor phase transition from phase IV to III is about 6.04 × 10⁴⁴ erg/km³
- For a compact star with quark matter radius R (km), the energy release is about 2.53 × 10⁴⁵ R³ erg, which is smaller than typical gamma-ray burst energies of 10⁵¹ to 10⁵⁴ erg
- The latent heat peaks at approximately 0.67 T_c to 0.72 T_c for all curves

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous phase diagram study (Wu et al., 2010) of single-flavor color superconductivity in a magnetic field by computing the latent heat across phase boundaries. The results are relevant for understanding possible energy bursts from compact stars, though the calculated latent heats are smaller than typical gamma-ray burst energies and may contribute to weaker X-ray radiation at later stages of a compact star's evolution, as discussed in the context of neutron star observations. The approximations used are marginal for realistic s-quark masses around 150 MeV, and the authors note that the two-flavor case is unrealistic as it assumes m_s >> μ.
