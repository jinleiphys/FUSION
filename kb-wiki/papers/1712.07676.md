# Spin-dependent distribution functions for relativistic hydrodynamics of spin-1/2 particles

> Recently advocated expressions for the phase-space dependent spin-1/2 density matrices of particles and antiparticles are analyzed in detail and reduced to the forms linear in the Dirac spin operator. This allows for a natural determination of the spin polarization vectors of particles and antiparticles by the trace of products of the spin density matrices and the Pauli matrices. We demonstrate that the total spin polarization vector obtained in this way agrees with the Pauli-Lubanski four-vector, constructed from an appropriately chosen spin tensor and boosted to the particle rest frame. We further show that several forms of the spin tensor used in the literature give the same Pauli-Lubanski four-vector.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the exponential form of the spin-dependent phase-space distribution functions for spin-1/2 particles can be reduced to an expression linear in the Dirac spin operator, and that the resulting spin polarization vector agrees with the Pauli-Lubanski four-vector constructed from the spin tensor, provided the condition \(k \cdot \omega = 0\) and \(k \cdot k - \omega \cdot \omega \geq 0\) holds (i.e., for real \(z\) interpreted as the spin chemical potential divided by temperature).

## Method

The authors start from the exponential form of the spin-dependent distribution functions \(f^\pm(x,p)\) for particles and antiparticles in local equilibrium, defined as two-by-two matrices in spin space. They resum the exponential of the polarization tensor contracted with the Dirac spin operator \(\Sigma^{\mu\nu}\) to obtain a linear form in \(\Sigma^{\mu\nu}\), then evaluate the matrix elements using Dirac bispinors and identities for the spin operator. The resulting distribution functions are decomposed in terms of Pauli matrices to extract the spin polarization three-vector, which is then compared to the Pauli-Lubanski four-vector obtained by boosting the spin tensor to the particle rest frame.

## Key numbers

* The spin chemical potential \(\Omega\) is related to the polarization tensor by \(\zeta = \Omega/T = \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{k \cdot k - \omega \cdot \omega} = \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{e} \cdot \mathbf{e}}\), where \(k\) and \(\omega\) are space-like four-vectors derived from the polarization tensor.
* The average polarization vector is \(\mathcal{P} = -\frac{1}{2} \tanh(\zeta) \bar{\mathbf{P}}\), where \(\bar{\mathbf{P}} = \mathbf{P}/(2\zeta)\) and \(\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{b}_*\) (the magnetic-like field in the particle rest frame).
* The Pauli-Lubanski four-vector per particle is \(\pi_\mu(x,p) = -\frac{\tanh(\zeta)}{4 m \zeta} \tilde{\omega}_{\mu\beta} p^\beta\), with \(\tilde{\omega}_{\mu\beta}\) the dual polarization tensor.
* For the stationary vortex example, the flow four-velocity is \(u^0 = \gamma\), \(u^1 = -\gamma \tilde{\Omega} y\), \(u^2 = \gamma \tilde{\Omega} x\), \(u^3 = 0\) with \(\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1 - \tilde{\Omega}^2 r^2}\), and the polarization fields are \(\mathbf{e} = (0,0,0)\), \(\mathbf{b} = (0,0,\tilde{\Omega}/T_0)\).

## Context

This work extends the spin-dependent distribution functions introduced by Becattini et al. (2013) and the perfect-fluid hydrodynamic framework with spin degrees of freedom developed by Florkowski et al. (2017). It provides the formal basis for calculating thermodynamic and hydrodynamic quantities from these distribution functions, showing that the spin polarization vector derived from the phase-space densities matches the Pauli-Lubanski four-vector obtained from the spin tensor used in the earlier hydrodynamic framework. The analysis demonstrates that several forms of the spin tensor from the literature (canonical form from Noether's theorem and the Groot-Leeuven-Weert form) yield the same Pauli-Lubanski four-vector, except for the Belinfante construction which sets the spin tensor to zero. This enables consistent inclusion of spin dynamics in relativistic hydrodynamics for heavy-ion collisions, going beyond the freeze-out hypersurface approach used previously.
