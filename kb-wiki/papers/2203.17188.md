# Effects of a strong phase transition on supernova explosions, compact stars and their mergers

> We outline a theoretical approach supporting strong phase transitions from normal nuclear matter to the deconfined quark-gluon plasma, in the equation of state (EOS) for compact star matter. Implications of this hypothesis are discussed for astrophysical applications. Special emphasis is devoted to potentially detectable signatures, which can be directly related with the occurrence of a sufficiently strong phase transition. Therefore, simulations of core-collapse supernovae and binary compact star mergers are considered, including the subsequent emission of gravitational waves and, in the case of supernova, in addition the neutrinos play the role of messengers.

# Effects of a strong phase transition on supernova explosions, compact stars and their mergers

## Key claim
A sufficiently strong first-order phase transition from hadronic matter to deconfined quark-gluon plasma in the equation of state for compact star matter can produce detectable signatures in gravitational waves and neutrinos from core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers, but this mechanism likely affects only the high-mass end of massive progenitor stars (above approximately 30 solar masses) and cannot account for the bulk of supernova explosions.

## Method
The authors review and synthesize results from multiple numerical simulations of core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers that employ hybrid equations of state incorporating a first-order hadron-to-quark phase transition, specifically the DD2F-RDF 1.2 model which includes repulsive vector interactions to satisfy the 2-solar-mass pulsar constraint. For supernovae, spherically symmetric and axially-symmetric simulations with approximate neutrino transport are used to track the post-bounce evolution, the formation of a second shock wave from the phase transition, and the resulting neutrino and gravitational wave emission. For mergers, simulations follow the inspiral, coalescence, and postmerger phases to identify signatures of quark deconfinement in the gravitational wave signal.

## Key numbers
- The pseudo-critical QCD transition temperature at zero baryon chemical potential is T_c(0) = 156.5 ± 1.5 MeV (HotQCD collaboration), and the chiral phase transition temperature in the chiral limit is T_c^(0) = 132^{+3}_{-6} MeV.
- The present maximum neutron star mass constraint from pulsar PSR J0740+6620 is 2.08 ± 0.07 solar masses.
- In supernova simulations with the hybrid EOS, the phase transition occurs at about 1.2 seconds post-bounce for a 50 solar mass progenitor, and the central density saturates at about five times nuclear saturation density.
- The gravitational wave amplitude at kHz frequencies is enhanced by nearly one order of magnitude for the 50 solar mass supernova simulation with a hadron-to-quark phase transition compared to canonical neutrino-driven models.
- The threshold mass for black hole formation in neutron star mergers is likely in the range between 2.8 and 3.4 solar masses.
- The electron fraction in the dynamically ejected component of phase-transition-driven supernovae is Y_e ≈ 0.2–0.25, enabling a strong r-process component up to transactinides such as plutonium.

## Context
This work extends the earlier proposal by Sagert (2008) that a hadron-quark phase transition can trigger supernova explosions, but overcomes the problem that those earlier bag-model hybrid equations of state could not satisfy the 2-solar-mass pulsar constraint from Antoniadis (2013) and Fonseca (2016, 2021). The review synthesizes recent results from Fischer (2018, 2020, 2021) and Kuroda (2021) on supernova signatures, and from Bauswein (2019) on merger simulations. It enables future observational searches for quark matter signatures in galactic supernova neutrino bursts detectable by Super-Kamiokande, and in high-frequency gravitational wave components that could be distinguished from canonical neutrino-driven explosions. The nucleosynthesis results suggest that phase-transition-driven supernovae from very massive progenitors could be a rare r-process site, complementing magneto-rotational supernovae as proposed by Winteler (2012) and Moesta (2014, 2015).
