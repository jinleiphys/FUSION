# Gluon Shadowing in Heavy-Flavor Production at the LHC

> We study the relevance of experimental data on heavy-flavor [$D^0$, $J/ψ$, $B\rightarrow J/ψ$ and $Υ(1S)$ mesons] production in proton-lead collisions at the LHC to improve our knowledge of the gluon-momentum distribution inside heavy nuclei. We observe that the nuclear effects encoded in both most recent global fits of nuclear parton densities at next-to-leading order (nCTEQ15 and EPPS16) provide a good overall description of the LHC data. We interpret this as a hint that these are the dominant ones. In turn, we perform a Bayesian-reweighting analysis for each particle data sample which shows that each of the existing heavy-quark(onium) data set clearly points --with a minimal statistical significance of 7 $σ$-- to a shadowed gluon distribution at small $x$ in the lead. Moreover, our analysis corroborates the existence of gluon antishadowing. Overall, the inclusion of such heavy-flavor data in a global fit would significantly reduce the uncertainty on the gluon density down to $x\simeq 7\times 10^{-6}$ --where no other data exist-- while keeping an agreement with the other data of the global fits. Our study accounts for the factorization-scale uncertainties which dominate for the charm(onium) sector.

## Key claim

The LHC heavy-flavor data on $D^0$, $J/\psi$, $B\rightarrow J/\psi$, and $\Upsilon(1S)$ production in proton-lead collisions are consistent with the nuclear effects encoded in the nCTEQ15 and EPPS16 nuclear parton densities at next-to-leading order, and a Bayesian-reweighting analysis shows that each data set points to a shadowed gluon distribution at small $x$ with a minimal statistical significance of 7 sigma, but the results depend on the factorization-scale uncertainties which dominate for the charm(onium) sector.

## Method

The authors used a data-driven approach where parton-scattering matrix elements squared were parametrized into empirical functions determined from proton-proton data using CT14NLO parton densities, then applied to compute the nuclear modification factor $R_{p\mathrm{Pb}}$ for each heavy-flavor hadron. They performed a Bayesian-reweighting analysis of the nCTEQ15 and EPPS16 nuclear parton densities by converting the Hessian error sets into 10,000 Monte Carlo replicas, computing the chi-squared of each heavy-flavor data set, and reweighting the replicas accordingly. The factorization scale was varied by factors of 0.5, 1.0, and 2.0 around a default scale to quantify theoretical uncertainties.

## Key numbers

- The LHC heavy-flavor data constrain the gluon density down to $x \simeq 7 \times 10^{-6}$, where no other data exist.
- For nCTEQ15, the $D^0$ data show gluon shadowing with a statistical significance of 11.7 sigma at $x = 10^{-5}$ and $\mu_F = 2$ GeV; for EPPS16, the significance is 10.9 sigma under the same conditions.
- For $J/\psi$ data, the significance of gluon shadowing is 7.3 sigma for nCTEQ15 and 7.1 sigma for EPPS16 at $x = 10^{-5}$ and $\mu_F = 2$ GeV.
- The prompt $D^0$ and $J/\psi$ data would reduce the gluon density uncertainties by a factor of 3 for EPPS16 and a factor of 2 for nCTEQ15.
- The original chi-squared per data point for the DIS NMC data with nCTEQ15 was 0.58; after reweighting with $D^0$ data, it becomes between 0.57 and 0.81 depending on the scale choice.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies that used heavy-flavor production at the LHC to constrain gluon parton densities in the proton (Zenaiev 2015, Gauld 2015, Cacciari 2015) and applies the same logic to nuclear parton densities for the first time. It builds on the nCTEQ15 analysis (Kovarik 2015) and EPPS16 analysis (Eskola 2016), which previously used RHIC pion and LHC jet data to constrain gluon densities only down to $x \sim 10^{-3}$. The results quantitatively confirm qualitative observations of gluon shadowing from $J/\psi$ photoproduction on lead (Abelev 2012, Guzey 2013, Khachatryan 2016) and corroborate the existence of gluon antishadowing predicted by Frankfurt (1990). The analysis enables the inclusion of LHC heavy-flavor data in future global fits of nuclear parton densities to significantly reduce uncertainties at very small $x$.
