# Nuclei embedded in an electron gas

> The properties of nuclei embedded in an electron gas are studied within the relativistic mean-field approach. These studies are relevant for nuclear properties in astrophysical environments such as neutron-star crusts and supernova explosions. The electron gas is treated as a constant background in the Wigner-Seitz cell approximation. We investigate the stability of nuclei with respect to alpha and beta decay. Furthermore, the influence of the electronic background on spontaneous fission of heavy and superheavy nuclei is analyzed. We find that the presence of the electrons leads to stabilizing effects for both $α$ decay and spontaneous fission for high electron densities. Furthermore, the screening effect shifts the proton dripline to more proton-rich nuclei, and the stability line with respect to beta decay is shifted to more neutron-rich nuclei. Implications for the creation and survival of very heavy nuclear systems are discussed.

## Key claim

The presence of a dense electron gas stabilizes nuclei against both alpha decay and spontaneous fission at high electron densities, and shifts the proton dripline to more proton-rich nuclei and the beta-stability line to more neutron-rich nuclei, but these effects are conditional on the electron Fermi momentum being sufficiently high (e.g., k_F = 0.5 fm^{-1} produces much stronger shifts than k_F = 0.1 fm^{-1}).

## Method

The authors employed the relativistic mean-field (RMF) model with the NL3 parameter set to compute nuclear ground-state properties, treating the electron gas as a constant background within the Wigner-Seitz cell approximation. For spherical nuclei, the cell radius was determined by charge neutrality; for deformed nuclei, the cell shape was adjusted to yield zero total quadrupole moment. The Poisson equation for the electrostatic potential was modified to include the electron density, and BCS pairing with a density-independent delta-force was used for pairing correlations.

## Key numbers

- For the heavy nucleus 240Pu, the proton rms radius decreases by approximately 0.5% when adding electrons with k_F = 0.5 fm^{-1}.
- For 208Pb at k_F = 0.5 fm^{-1}, the proton single-particle levels shift downward by approximately 10 MeV.
- At an electron density corresponding to x = 10^{-3} (k_F ≈ 25 MeV), the screening effect on the Coulomb energy is about 15%.
- For a tin nucleus at k_F = 0.1 fm^{-1}, the Wigner-Seitz cell radius is R_C ≈ 70 fm; at k_F = 0.01 fm^{-1}, R_C ≈ 700 fm.
- For 208Pb at k_F = 0.5 fm^{-1}, the Wigner-Seitz cell radius is R_C = 16.7 fm.
- For 240Pu at k_F = 0.5 fm^{-1}, the equivalent spherical Wigner-Seitz cell radius is 17.5 fm.

## Context

This work extends the seminal microscopic studies of nuclei in stellar environments by Negele and Vautherin (1973) and subsequent work by Botvina (2004, 2005), Magierski (2001), and Maruyama (2005, 2006). The results enable improved modeling of nuclear properties in neutron-star crusts and supernova environments, particularly for understanding the creation and survival of very heavy nuclear systems. The authors note that future work should include the neutron gas and full Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov calculations for nuclei near the neutron drip line.
