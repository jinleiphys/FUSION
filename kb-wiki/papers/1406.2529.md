# A high-entropy wind r-process study based on nuclear-structure quantities from the new finite-range droplet model FRDM(2012)

> Theoretical studies of the nucleosynthesis origin of the heavy elements in our Solar System (S.S.) by the rapid neutron-capture process (r-process) still face the entwined uncertainties in the possible astrophysical scenarios and the nuclear-physics properties far from stability. In this paper we present results from the investigation of an r-process in the high-entropy wind (HEW) of core-collapse supernovae (here chosen as one of the possible scenarios for this nucleosynthesis process), using new nuclear-data input calculated in a consistent approach, for masses and $β$-decay properties from the new finite-range droplet model FRDM(2012). The accuracy of the new mass model is 0.56 MeV with respect to {\sc AME2003}, to which it was adjusted. We compare the new HEW r-process abundance pattern to the latest S.S. r-process residuals and to our earlier calculations with the nuclear-structure quantities based on FRDM(1992). Substantial overall and specific local improvements in the calculated pattern of the r-process between $A\simeq 110$ and $^{209}$Bi, as well as remaining deficiencies are discussed in terms of the underlying spherical and deformed shell structure far from stability.

## Key claim

The new FRDM(2012) mass model, combined with updated QRPA beta-decay calculations, yields substantial improvements in the calculated solar-system r-process abundance pattern between A ≈ 110 and 209Bi compared to the earlier FRDM(1992) input, but the correct shape and position of the A ≈ 130 r-abundance peak can only be reproduced by a weighted superposition of different electron-fraction (Ye) components, not by a single Ye component alone.

## Method

The authors performed dynamical r-process nucleosynthesis calculations within the high-entropy wind (HEW) scenario of core-collapse supernovae, using a parameterized adiabatic expansion model with homogeneous mass zones of varying radiation entropy S (in kB/baryon). They used nuclear masses from the new FRDM(2012) model and beta-decay half-lives and delayed-neutron emission probabilities from the FRDM-QRPA formalism based on FRDM(2012), obtaining all nuclear data from a single consistent global model framework. The calculated abundance patterns were compared to solar-system r-process residuals from Lodders (2009) and Bisterzo (2011), and to earlier calculations using FRDM(1992) and QRPA(2003) input.

## Key numbers

- The FRDM(2012) mass model accuracy is 0.56 MeV with respect to AME2003, and 0.42 MeV above N ≈ 50 (the region relevant for the r-process)
- The overall model error improved from σ_th = 0.6314 MeV (FRDM(1992)) to σ_th = 0.5595 MeV (FRDM(2012)), an improvement of about 13%
- For N > 50 and S_1n < 5 MeV, the S_1n rms deviation is 0.320 MeV for FRDM(2012), compared to 0.341 MeV for FRDM(1992)
- Calculated half-lives agree with 272 experimental values from Nubase2012 with T_1/2 ≤ 1 s to within a factor of 2.68
- Calculated P_n values agree with Nubase2012 values to within a factor of 3.39 for 188 nuclei
- The total r-process duration for the whole S-range (20 ≤ S ≤ 280) is τ_r,tot ≈ 535 ms with FRDM(2012), compared to τ_r,tot ≈ 680 ms with FRDM(1992)
- The REE region (140 ≤ A ≤ 180) was underestimated by a mean factor of 1.67 with FRDM(1992), but with FRDM(2012) the overall agreement is excellent with a mean factor of 0.97

## Context

This work extends the authors' long-standing program of r-process studies using the FRDM mass models, building on the foundational HEW model of Freiburghaus (1999) and the parameter studies of Farouqi (2005, 2009, 2010). It directly compares the new FRDM(2012) results to earlier calculations with FRDM(1992) from Möller (1995) and QRPA(2003) from Möller (2003). The improved reproduction of the rare-earth-element (REE) pygmy-peak region without invoking fission recycling contradicts earlier speculations that fission cycling from unrealistically high entropy components (S ≥ 400) would be necessary. The remaining deficiencies, particularly the A ≈ 130 peak position and the trough in the 199 ≤ A ≤ 205 region, point to needed improvements in the description of shape-transition regions and shell structure far from stability, enabling future studies with multi-component Ye superpositions and comparisons to quenched mass formulas such as ETFSI-Q from Pearson (1996).
