# Predictions for sqrt{s} = 200 A*GeV Au+Au Collisions from Relativistic Hydrodynamics

> The relativistic hydrodynamical model HYLANDER-C is used to give estimates for single inclusive particle momentum spectra in $\sqrt{s}$ = 200 $GeV/nucleon$ Au+Au collisions that will be investigated experimentally in the near future. The predictions are based on initial conditions that the initial fireball has a longitudinal extension of 1.6 $fm$ and an initial energy density of 30.8 $GeV/fm^3$ as obtained from a cascade model. For the collision energy considered here, different stopping scenarios are explored for the first time. Our calculations give particle yields of the order of 10,000 to 20,000 charged particles per event.

## Key claim

The paper provides predictions for single inclusive particle momentum spectra in sqrt(s) = 200 A*GeV Au+Au collisions using the relativistic hydrodynamical model HYLANDER-C, but these predictions are explicitly conditional on the assumed initial conditions (taken from a parton cascade model), the chosen equation of state with a QGP phase transition at T_C = 200 MeV, and a fixed freeze-out temperature of T_f = 139 MeV; the authors note that the shapes of rapidity spectra are sensitive to the assumed stopping power and that information about the initial conditions, EOS, and freeze-out conditions must be gained with additional theoretical work and data when they become available.

## Method

The authors used the predictor-corrector relativistic hydrodynamical model HYLANDER-C to evolve an initial fireball with longitudinal extension Delta = 1.6 fm and initial energy density epsilon_Delta = 30.8 GeV/fm^3 (obtained from Geiger's parton cascade calculations) according to an equation of state with a phase transition to a quark-gluon plasma at critical temperature T_C = 200 MeV. They explored six different stopping scenarios by varying the initial fluid rapidity at the fireball edge (y_Delta) and the rapidity at the maximum of the initial baryonic rapidity distribution (y_m), while keeping the relative fraction of thermal energy in the central fireball (K_L = 0.115) and the width of the initial baryonic rapidity distribution (sigma = 0.4) fixed. Freeze-out was imposed at a fixed energy density corresponding to T_f = 139 MeV for all particle species, and single inclusive momentum spectra of pions, kaons, and protons were calculated including resonance decay contributions up to the third generation.

## Key numbers

- Initial fireball longitudinal extension: Delta = 1.6 fm
- Initial energy density in the Landau volume: epsilon_Delta = 30.8 GeV/fm^3 (representing 11.5% of the total available energy)
- Equilibration proper time from cascade model: tau_eq = 1.8 fm/c
- Freeze-out temperature: T_f = 139 MeV
- Critical temperature for QGP phase transition: T_C = 200 MeV
- Total fireball lifetimes across scenarios: t_max from 37.8 fm/c to 49.7 fm/c
- QGP lifetimes across scenarios: t_QGP from 16.9 fm/c to 23.7 fm/c
- Charged particle yields per event: 10,000 to 20,000
- Pion yields per scenario: N_pi+ from 4654 to 6816; N_pi- from 4748 to 6932
- Kaon yields per scenario: N_K+ from 674 to 972; N_K- from 616 to 915
- Proton yields per scenario: N_p from 305 to 364
- Under pure Landau (complete stopping) initial conditions, particle yields would be approximately 10 times larger

## Context

This work extends the relativistic hydrodynamical approach previously applied successfully to CERN/SPS fixed-target heavy-ion collisions (by the NA35, NA44, and NA49 collaborations) to the higher-energy RHIC regime, using initial conditions from Geiger's parton cascade model (Geiger, 1992-1993) rather than parametrized initial conditions. The paper explores for the first time different stopping scenarios at this collision energy, ranging between the extremes of Landau (complete stopping) and Bjorken (minimum stopping) initial conditions. The predictions are intended to guide forthcoming RHIC experiments at Brookhaven National Laboratory, and the authors emphasize that the sensitivity of observables to stopping assumptions must be resolved by future data and additional theoretical work.
