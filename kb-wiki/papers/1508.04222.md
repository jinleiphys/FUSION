# Present status of coupled-channels calculations for heavy-ion subbarrier fusion reactions

> The coupled-channels method has been a standard tool in analyzing heavy-ion fusion reactions at energies around the Coulomb barrier. We investigate three simplifications usually adopted in the coupled-channels calculations. These are i) the exclusion of non-collective excitations, ii) the assumption of coordinate independent coupling strengths, and iii) the harmonic oscillator approximation for multi-phonon excitations. In connection to the last point, we propose a novel microscopic method based on the beyond-mean-field approach in order to take into account the anharmonic effects of collective vibrations.

# Present status of coupled-channels calculations for heavy-ion subbarrier fusion reactions

## Key claim
The three standard simplifications in coupled-channels calculations, excluding non-collective excitations, assuming coordinate-independent coupling strengths, and using the harmonic oscillator approximation for multi-phonon states, are each valid only under specific conditions; the harmonic approximation in particular can overestimate subbarrier fusion cross sections, and a beyond-mean-field approach that incorporates anharmonic effects yields a minor improvement in reproducing experimental fusion barrier distributions.

## Method
The authors test the three simplifications by comparing standard coupled-channels calculations against experimental data for several heavy-ion systems. For non-collective excitations, they use a random matrix model to include many weakly-coupled states in the $^{20}$Ne+$^{90,92}$Zr systems. For coordinate-dependent coupling strengths, they cite RPA calculations with a two-center shell model. For anharmonic effects, they combine multi-reference covariant density functional theory (MR-CDFT) with coupled-channels calculations for the $^{58}$Ni+$^{58}$Ni and $^{40}$Ca+$^{58}$Ni systems, using the MR-CDFT to compute transition strengths among collective states.

## Key numbers
- For the $^{20}$Ne+$^{92}$Zr system, the experimental quasi-elastic barrier distribution is considerably more smeared than for $^{20}$Ne+$^{90}$Zr; the standard coupled-channels calculation (dashed lines) fails to reproduce the $^{92}$Zr data, while including non-collective excitations (solid lines) reproduces the smearing.
- For $^{58}$Ni+$^{58}$Ni fusion cross sections, the harmonic oscillator limit overestimates cross sections at the two lowest energies, while the MR-CDFT calculation underpredicts cross sections around 95 MeV.
- For the $^{58}$Ni+$^{58}$Ni fusion barrier distribution, the MR-CDFT calculation leads to a minor improvement by considerably smearing each peak compared to the harmonic oscillator limit.
- The MR-CDFT calculation for $^{58}$Ni shows that the $B(E2)$ value from the $0^+_2$ to the $2^+_1$ state is much smaller than that from the $4^+_1$ and $2^+_2$ states to the $2^+_1$ state, deviating from the harmonic oscillator limit where all two-phonon triplet states have exactly twice the $B(E2)$ value from the $2^+_1$ to the ground state.

## Context
This work extends the standard coupled-channels framework for heavy-ion subbarrier fusion, which was established in the late 1970s after the discovery of large subbarrier enhancement relative to simple potential model predictions (Hagino and Takigawa, 2012). The paper directly tests assumptions used in prior coupled-channels calculations (Hagino et al., 1999) and builds on recent RPA calculations by Ichikawa and Matsuyanagi (2013) showing that coupling strengths decrease near the touching point, which provides a microscopic justification for the damping factor used in adiabatic models of deep subbarrier fusion hindrance (Ichikawa et al., 2009). The proposed semi-microscopic approach combining MR-DFT with coupled-channels calculations enables future application to transitional nuclei that exhibit neither pure vibrational nor pure rotational character, and represents a step toward a fully microscopic theory of heavy-ion subbarrier fusion starting from the nucleon degree of freedom.
