# Non-boost-invariant dissipative hydrodynamics

> The one-dimensional non-boost-invariant evolution of the quark-gluon plasma, presumably produced during the early stages of heavy-ion collisions, is analyzed within the frameworks of viscous and anisotropic hydrodynamics. We neglect transverse dynamics and assume homogeneous conditions in the transverse plane but, differently from Bjorken expansion, we relax longitudinal boost invariance in order to study the rapidity dependence of various hydrodynamical observables. We compare the results obtained using several formulations of second-order viscous hydrodynamics with a recent approach to anisotropic hydrodynamics, which treats the large initial pressure anisotropy in a non-perturbative fashion. The results obtained with second-order viscous hydrodynamics depend on the particular choice of the second-order terms included, which suggests that the latter should be included in the most complete way. The results of anisotropic hydrodynamics and viscous hydrodynamics agree for the central hot part of the system, however, they differ at the edges where the approach of anisotropic hydrodynamics helps to control the undesirable growth of viscous corrections observed in standard frameworks.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that for non-boost-invariant (1+1)D expansion of quark-gluon plasma, second-order viscous hydrodynamics formulations (MIS, BRSSS, DNMR) produce results that depend significantly on the choice of second-order terms included, and all generate unphysical negative longitudinal pressure at large rapidities, whereas anisotropic hydrodynamics (AHYDRO) eliminates negative pressures by construction and substantially delays shock formation to times much larger than typical heavy-ion collision timescales.

## Method

The authors solved the (1+1)D non-boost-invariant relativistic hydrodynamic equations for a conformal fluid with zero bulk viscosity, comparing four frameworks: Müller-Israel-Stewart (MIS), Denicol-Niemi-Molnar-Rischke (DNMR), Baier-Romatschke-Son-Starinets-Stephanov (BRSSS), and anisotropic hydrodynamics (AHYDRO). All transport coefficients were obtained from kinetic theory in the relaxation time approximation (RTA) with classical statistics, and the system was initialized with a Gaussian energy density profile in spacetime rapidity with width parameter a=0.9 and initial energy density 100 GeV/fm³ at proper time τ₀=0.3 fm.

## Key numbers

- Initial energy density: ℰ₀ = 100 GeV/fm³ at τ₀ = 0.3 fm
- Gaussian rapidity profile width: a = 0.9
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratios used: 4π\bar{η} = 1 and 4π\bar{η} = 3
- Relaxation time: τ_π = τ_eq = 5\bar{η}/T
- Freeze-out temperature: T = 0.15 GeV
- Shock formation time ordering: τ_shock^BRSSS < τ_shock^MIS < τ_shock^DNMR ≪ τ_shock^AHYDRO
- DNMR second-order coefficients from RTA: δ_{ππ} = 4/3, τ_{ππ} = 10/7, φ₇ = 0
- BRSSS second-order coefficient: λ₁ = 5ητ_π/7

## Context

This work extends previous studies of non-boost-invariant expansion in perfect-fluid hydrodynamics (Florkowski 1992), viscous hydrodynamics (Bozek 2007), and early anisotropic hydrodynamics formulations (Ryblewski 2010, Martinez 2010) by using the more recent AHYDRO framework based on the anisotropic matching principle (Tinti 2015). The results show that AHYDRO agrees with DNMR and BRSSS for systems approaching local thermal equilibrium, as previously demonstrated for boost-invariant systems (Florkowski 2016), but provides a continuous description of the system as a hot central part with a free-streaming halo at large rapidities. This enables more reliable modeling of particle spectra at large rapidities and avoids numerical instabilities from shock formation that plague standard viscous frameworks.
