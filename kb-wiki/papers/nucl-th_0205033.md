# Effective interaction for pf-shell nuclei

> An effective interaction is derived for use in the full pf basis. Starting from a realistic G-matrix interaction, 195 two-body matrix elements and 4 single-particle energies are determined by fitting to 699 energy data in the mass range 47 to 66. The derived interaction successfully describes various structures of pf-shell nuclei. As examples, systematics of the energies of the first 2+ states in the Ca, Ti, Cr, Fe, and Ni isotope chains and energy levels of 56,57,58Ni are presented. The appearance of a new magic number 34 is seen.

# Effective interaction for pf-shell nuclei

## Key claim

The authors derive a new unified effective interaction (GXPF1) for the full *pf*-shell by fitting 195 two-body matrix elements and 4 single-particle energies to 699 experimental energy data points, achieving a root-mean-square error of 168 keV within the few-dimensional approximation with empirical corrections (FDA*). The interaction successfully describes cross-shell excitations across the N=Z=28 gap, but the abstract's claim of "successfully describes various structures" glosses over the fact that the fit excludes data from nuclei with A<47 and that the FDA* approximation introduces deviations up to 0.2 MeV compared to exact calculations.

## Method

Starting from a realistic G-matrix interaction based on the Bonn-C potential, the authors performed an iterative least-squares fit to 699 binding and excitation energies from 87 nuclei in the mass range A=47-66, varying 70 well-determined linear combinations of the 195 two-body matrix elements and 4 single-particle energies while keeping the remaining linear combinations fixed at their G-matrix values. Shell-model energies were computed using a few-dimensional approximation (FDA) with empirical corrections (FDA*), employing the Monte Carlo Shell Model (MCSM) to handle basis dimensions up to 2 billion, with conventional shell-model calculations by the code MSHELL used for verification. A second interaction (GXPF2) was also constructed by fixing 20 matrix elements involving the f7/2 orbit to their G-matrix values and fitting 60 linear combinations to 623 data points, yielding a rms error of 188 keV.

## Key numbers

- 195 two-body matrix elements and 4 single-particle energies were determined by fitting to 699 energy data points from 87 nuclei in the mass range A=47 to 66
- The GXPF1 interaction achieves a root-mean-square error of 168 keV within FDA*
- The GXPF2 interaction achieves a root-mean-square error of 188 keV for 623 data points within FDA*
- The effective single-particle energy gap between p1/2 and f5/2 in Ca and Ti isotopes is approximately 4.1 MeV, indicating a new N=34 magic number
- The subshell gap at N=32 between p3/2 and p1/2 is approximately 1.6 MeV
- For 56Ni, B(E2; 0+1 → 2+1) = 5.5 × 10^2 e^2fm^4 and B(E2; 0+3 → 2+2) = 1.8 × 10^3 e^2fm^4, using effective charges e_p=1.23 and e_n=0.54
- The probability of the (f7/2)^16 configuration in the 56Ni ground state is 69% for GXPF1 and 49% for GXPF2
- Total Gamow-Teller strength B(GT+) from the 56Ni ground state is 11.3 for GXPF1 and 9.5 for GXPF2, compared to 13.7 for the closed shell and 10.1 from KB3
- Single-particle energies relative to p3/2 for GXPF1: f7/2 = -2.9447 MeV, p1/2 = 1.5423 MeV, f5/2 = 4.2964 MeV

## Context

This work extends the approach of the USD interaction for the *sd*-shell (Wildenthal, 1984) to the full *pf*-shell, addressing the lack of a unified effective interaction for this region. It improves upon earlier microscopic interactions such as the G-matrix interaction (Hjorth-Jensen et al., 1995) and the monopole-modified KB3 and KB3G interactions (Poves et al., 1981; Poves and Zuker, 1981), which failed for heavier *pf*-shell nuclei like 56Ni, and the FPD6 interaction (Richter et al., 1991), which had defects in single-particle aspects of 57Ni. The interaction enables full-space shell-model calculations for the entire *pf*-shell, including predictions of a new N=34 magic number in neutron-rich Ca and Ti isotopes, and provides a foundation for studying electron capture in supernovae explosions and the stability of the N=28 magic number.
