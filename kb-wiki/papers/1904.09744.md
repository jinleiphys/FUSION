# Do the current astronomical observations exclude the existence of non-strange quark stars?

> As is pointed out in a recent work (Phys. Rev. Lett. 120, 222001), quark matter may not be strange. Inspired by this argument, we use a new self-consistent mean field approximation method to study the equation of state of cold dense matter within the framework of the two-flavor NJL model. Then the mass-radius relationship of two-flavor pure quark stars is studied. In the new self-consistent mean field approximation method we introduced, there is a free parameter $α$, which reflects the weight of different interaction channels. In principal, $α$ should be determined by experiments rather than the self-consistent mean field approximation itself. In this paper, thus, the influence of the variation of $α$ on the critical chemical potential of chiral phase transition and the stiffness of the EOS are thoroughly explored. It is found that the stiffness of EOS can be increased greatly to support a two-solar-mass pure quark star when $α$ is greater than 0.95. This means that current theoretical calculations and astronomical observations cannot rule out the possibility of a two-flavor pure quark star.

# Do the current astronomical observations exclude the existence of non-strange quark stars?

## Key claim
The authors argue that current theoretical calculations and astronomical observations cannot rule out the possibility of two-flavor (non-strange) pure quark stars, provided the free parameter α in their self-consistent mean field NJL model is greater than 0.9. This conclusion is conditional on the specific choice of the bag constant B and the parameter α, which reflects the weight of different interaction channels and must ultimately be determined by experiment rather than theory alone.

## Method
The authors use a new self-consistent mean field approximation method within the two-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, where a linear combination of the original Lagrangian and its Fierz-transformed form introduces a free parameter α. They solve the gap equations and number density relations for u and d quarks under beta-equilibrium and charge neutrality conditions, then compute the equation of state (EOS) using a redefined bag constant based on the quasi-Wigner vacuum as reference ground state. The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation is integrated to obtain mass-radius relations for pure quark stars.

## Key numbers
- The maximum mass of a two-flavor pure quark star reaches 2.05 solar masses (M_sol) when α = 0.9 and B = (90 MeV)^4.
- For this configuration, the radius of a 1.4-solar-mass quark star is 10.8 km, and the radius of a 1.6-solar-mass quark star is 10.9 km.
- The central baryonic density corresponding to the maximum mass is 0.802 fm^{-3}, with the cutoff density at 0.808 fm^{-3}.
- The minimal baryonic density on the star surface is 0.157 fm^{-3}.
- The bag constant calculated with the adopted parameter set (Λ = 653 MeV, GΛ^2 = 2.10, m = 5.0 MeV) is (158 MeV)^4.
- The critical chemical potential μ_c of the chiral phase transition is roughly 400 MeV for α = 0.92 and 600 MeV for α = 1.04.
- The chiral phase transition becomes a crossover when α is greater than 0.71.

## Context
This work extends the argument of Holdom, Ren, and Zhang (2018) that stable quark matter may not be strange, by providing a specific EOS model for two-flavor quark stars. It addresses the contradiction between quark-model predictions of the chiral phase transition chemical potential (330-380 MeV in most NJL-type models) and hadron-model estimates (around 400-600 MeV corresponding to 2-4 times nuclear saturation density). The results are consistent with constraints from the GW170817 neutron star merger, including the upper limit on the radius of a 1.4-solar-mass neutron star (≤ 13.76 km) and the lower limit on the radius of a 1.6-solar-mass neutron star (≥ 10.7 km). The work enables future studies of two-flavor versus three-flavor quark star models and suggests that further gravitational-wave data from post-merger spectra could identify phase transitions in neutron star mergers.
