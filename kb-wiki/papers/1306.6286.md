# Photo-Disintegration of the Iron Nucleus in Fractured Magnetite Rocks with Magnetostriction

> There has been considerable interest in recent experiments on iron nuclear disintegrations observed when rocks containing such nuclei are crushed and fractured. The resulting nuclear transmutations are particularly strong for the case of magnetite rocks, i.e. loadstones. We argue that the fission of the iron nucleus is a consequence of photo-disintegration. The electro-strong coupling between electromagnetic fields and nuclear giant dipole resonances are central for producing observed nuclear reactions. The large electron energies produced during the fracture of piezomagnetic rocks are closely analogous to the previously discussed case of the fracture of piezoelectric rocks. In both cases electro-weak interactions can produce neutrons and neutrinos from energetic protons and electrons thus inducing nuclear transmutations. The electro-strong condensed matter coupling discussed herein represents new many body collective nuclear photo-disintegration effects.

# Photo-Disintegration of the Iron Nucleus in Fractured Magnetite Rocks with Magnetostriction

## Key claim

The authors argue that nuclear disintegrations observed when magnetite rocks are crushed are caused by photo-disintegration of iron nuclei via giant dipole resonances, not by direct mechanical effects. The mechanism requires that the electro-strong coupling between electromagnetic fields and nuclear giant dipole resonances is central to producing the observed nuclear reactions, and the efficiency of energy transfer from fast electrons to fission products is approximately 1%.

## Method

The authors develop a theoretical model in which mechanical energy from rock fracture is converted by magnetostriction into electromagnetic field energy, producing microwave radiation that accelerates electrons to relativistic energies. These energetic electrons then collide with iron-56 nuclei, exciting them via giant dipole resonances into compound nuclear states that decay into fission products. The model employs a single damped harmonic oscillator approximation for the giant dipole resonance, uses dispersion relations and sum rules to compute cross sections, and calculates the retarding force on electrons passing through magnetite to estimate the energy transfer efficiency to nuclear excitations.

## Key numbers

- Peak cross section for photon absorption by iron-56: σ₀ = 4πZα(ħ/MΓ), where Z=26 and α≈1/137.036
- Predicted giant dipole resonance energy for ⁵⁶Fe: ħω₀ ≈ 20 MeV, from the relation ħω₀ ≈ 70 MeV / A^(1/3)
- Ratio of resonance width to frequency for ⁵⁶Fe: Γ/ω₀ ≈ 0.5 / A^(1/3), giving Γ ≈ 0.12 ω₀
- Energy transfer efficiency from fast electrons to fission products: η ≈ 1%
- Fracture stress of loadstone: σ_F ∼ 10¹⁰ erg/cm³, compared to bond stress σ_bond ∼ 10¹² erg/cm³
- Mean squared electric field at microcrack walls: |E|² ∼ 10¹¹ Gauss²
- Electron Lorentz factor from microwave fields: γ ∼ 300, corresponding to electron energy ∼ 150 MeV
- Surface density of excess electronic charges on microcrack walls: n_s ∼ 3×10¹³ /cm²
- Estimated density of fission events per unit area on microcrack walls: ϖ_s ∼ 10¹² fission events/cm²

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous theory of neutron production during fracture of piezoelectric rocks (Widom, 2013) to the case of piezomagnetic rocks such as magnetite. The model builds on experimental observations of nuclear reactions during rock crushing reported by Carpinteri (2010, 2011) and Manuello (2011), and on studies of fracture-induced radiation in magnetite by Koshevaya (2008). The theoretical framework employs the Migdal theory of nuclear Landau-Fermi liquids (Migdal, 1967, 1990) for the giant dipole resonance description. The work enables predictions of specific fission product channels (silicon and aluminum from iron; various light nuclei from oxygen) that could be searched for in future crushing experiments, and suggests that visible monolayer films of fission products should form on microcrack walls.
