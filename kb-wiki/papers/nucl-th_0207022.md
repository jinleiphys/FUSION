# Description of 8Be as Deformed Gas-like Two-Alpha-Paricle States

> In order to study non-zero spin excitations of the recently proposed alpha-cluster condensation in the self-conjugate 4n nuclei, spatial deformation is introduced into the model wave function of the alpha-cluster condensate. The rotational band states of 8Be are investigated as a first step of a test case for the study of the deformation of the alpha-cluster condensate. Calculations reproduce well the binding energy of the 0+ ground state and also the excitation energy of the 2+ state. Our 0+ wave function is found to be almost exactly equal to the 0+ wave function obtained by the generator coordinate method using Brink's 2 alpha wave function. The study shows that both the 0+ ground and 2+ excited states can be considered as having a gas-like (i.e. weakly bound) 2 alpha-cluster structure. As for the 0+ ground state, the change of the wave function, due to the introduction of the deformation, is found to be very small.

# Description of ⁸Be as Deformed Gas-like Two-Alpha-Particle States

## Key claim

The ⁸Be ground state and first excited 2⁺ state can both be described as gas-like (weakly bound) two-alpha-particle structures, with the 2⁺ state arising from spatial deformation of the alpha-cluster condensate rather than from breaking the condensate. The introduction of deformation into the alpha-condensate wave function plays essentially no role for the 0⁺ ground state but is essential for describing the 2⁺ excited state.

## Method

The authors introduced spatial deformation into the alpha-cluster condensate wave function by allowing the center-of-mass orbit of each alpha particle to have different widths in the x, y, and z directions (β_x = β_y ≠ β_z for axial symmetry). They projected states of good angular momentum from this deformed intrinsic state and calculated energy surfaces for J^π = 0⁺, 2⁺, and 4⁺ in the two-parameter space (β_x, β_z) using the Volkov No.1 effective nuclear force with Majorana parameter M = 0.56. The wave functions were compared with solutions of the 2α Hill-Wheeler equation using Brink's wave functions.

## Key numbers

- The binding energy of the 0⁺ ground state is -54.33 MeV at the energy minimum with b = 1.36 fm and β_x = β_y = β_z ≈ 3 fm, which is about 0.17 MeV below the theoretical two-alpha threshold energy of 2E_α = -54.16 MeV
- The 0⁺ energy minimum after angular momentum projection lies at β_x = β_y ≈ 1.8 fm and β_z ≈ 7.8 fm, with energy -54.45 MeV, giving an energy gain from projection of only about 0.12 MeV
- The squared overlap between the 0⁺ wave function at the energy minimum and at β_x = β_z = 3 fm is about 0.98
- For M = 0.54, the 2⁺ state has a local minimum at β_x = β_y ≈ 3.9 fm and β_z ≈ 0.0 fm with binding energy -52.28 MeV, giving an excitation energy of 2.91 MeV (experimental value: 2.9 MeV)
- For M = 0.56, the 2⁺ plateau region lies between -51.0 MeV and -51.8 MeV, corresponding to excitation energies between 2.65 MeV and 3.45 MeV
- The 4⁺ binding energy in the flat region lies between -42 MeV and -45 MeV, corresponding to excitation energies between 9.45 MeV and 12.45 MeV (experimental 4⁺ excitation energy: 11.4 MeV)
- The lowest eigen energy from the Hill-Wheeler equation is -54.444 MeV, with squared overlap of 0.9973 with the authors' 0⁺ wave function
- The single alpha particle binding energy is E_α = -27.08 MeV for b = 1.37 fm

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study of alpha-cluster condensation in ¹²C and ¹⁶O (Funaki et al., 2002) to non-zero spin excitations by introducing spatial deformation. The spherical alpha-condensate wave function used previously could only describe zero angular momentum states. The study tests the deformation approach on ⁸Be as a first step before applying it to heavier self-conjugate 4n nuclei like ¹²C. The results are compared with the generator coordinate method using Brink's 2α wave function and with the resonating group method (RGM) solutions. The work enables future study of non-zero spin excitations of alpha-condensate states in ¹²C and heavier nuclei, where solving the full RGM equation for n ≥ 4 alpha particles is very difficult.
