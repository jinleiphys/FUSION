# Parton distribution functions in nuclei

> Optimum nuclear parton distributions are determined by an analysis of muon and electron deep inelastic scattering data. Assuming simple A dependence and polynomial functions of x and 1-x for nuclear modification of parton distributions, we determine the initial distributions by a chi^2 analysis. Although valence-quark distributions are relatively well determined except for the small-x region, antiquark distributions cannot be fixed at medium and large x. It is also difficult to fix gluon distributions.

## Key claim
Optimum nuclear parton distributions are determined by a chi-squared analysis of muon and electron deep inelastic scattering data, but the results are limited: valence-quark distributions are relatively well determined except at small x, while antiquark and gluon distributions cannot be fixed at medium and large x.

## Method
The authors parametrize nuclear parton distributions as a product of free-nucleon distributions (from the MRST parametrization) and a weight function w_i(x,A,Z) that encodes nuclear modification. The weight function assumes an A dependence of 1/A^(1/3) (following Sick and Day) and a polynomial in x and (1-x) with parameters determined by a chi-squared fit to 309 data points from EMC, BCDMS, NMC, SLAC-E49, -E87, -E139, -E140, and Fermilab-E665 collaborations. The initial distributions at Q0^2 are evolved to the experimental Q^2 via leading-order DGLAP evolution, and the fit uses the ratio R_F2^A = F2^A / F2^D.

## Key numbers
- The total number of data points used in the analysis is 309.
- The chi-squared per degree of freedom is 1.93 for the quadratic fit and 1.82 for the cubic fit.
- Nuclear modification of parton distributions is typically less than 20% for medium-size nuclei.
- The analysis assumes a mass-number dependence of 1/A^(1/3) for the weight function.

## Context
This work extends earlier attempts to obtain nuclear parton distributions, such as those by Eskola, Kolhinen, and Ruuskanen, by performing a formal chi-squared optimization rather than relying on simpler assumptions. The authors note that their valence-quark distributions do not show strong shadowing, possibly due to the lack of Drell-Yan data, and that the gluon distributions remain poorly constrained because of the leading-order analysis and insufficient sensitive data. The results enable future improvements by including additional data (e.g., Drell-Yan) and refining the method, with the distributions made available via computer codes from the authors' website.
