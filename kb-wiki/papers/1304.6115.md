# The fundamental role of symmetry in nuclear models

> The purpose of these lectures is to illustrate how symmetry and pattern recognition play essential roles in the progression from experimental observation to an understanding of nuclear phenomena in terms of interacting neutrons and protons. We do not discuss weak interactions nor relativistic and sub-nucleon degrees of freedom. The explicit use of symmetry and the power of algebraic methods, in combination with analytical and geometrical methods are illustrated by their use in deriving a shell-model description of nuclear rotational dynamics and the structure of deformed nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper argues that symmetry and algebraic methods, particularly the symplectic Sp(3,R) model, provide a natural microscopic foundation for understanding nuclear rotational dynamics and deformed nuclei, but the author notes that the symplectic model contains no low-energy beta or gamma vibrational bands, and that excited K=0 bands arise from mixing of Sp(3,R) irreps rather than from the symmetry itself.

## Method

The author develops the symplectic model by starting from the Bohr collective model's quadrupole shape coordinates and promoting them to microscopic Cartesian monopole/quadrupole moments expressed in terms of nucleon coordinates and momenta. The Lie algebra is then augmented to include angular momentum operators and bilinear momentum operators, yielding the Sp(3,R) spectrum-generating algebra whose representations are constructed in a U(3) basis using harmonic oscillator raising and lowering operators. A two-parameter Hamiltonian is diagonalized within a single Sp(3,R) irreducible representation to calculate energy levels and E2 transition rates for a deformed nucleus.

## Key numbers

- The Sp(3,R) calculation for 166Er uses an irrep ⟨826.5(78,0)⟩ with a two-parameter Hamiltonian; the irrep label 826.5 corresponds to the harmonic oscillator energy N0 in units of ℏω, and (78,0) is the SU(3) quantum numbers (λ,μ)
- The Hamiltonian parameters are adjusted so that the potential minimum matches the deformation determined from experimental E2 transition rates, and χ is adjusted to give the observed energy of the 21 state
- The SU(3) → SO(3) branching rule gives angular momentum states L = λ, λ-2, ..., 1 or 0 when K=0, and L = K, K+1, ..., K+λ when K≠0, with K = μ, μ-2, ..., 1 or 0
- The giant-resonance raising operators transform as an SU(3) (2,0) tensor and create states at energy (N0+2)ℏω above the lowest-grade U(3) irrep

## Context

This work extends the Elliott SU(3) model (Elliott, 1958) for light nuclei and the Bohr-Mottelson collective model (Bohr and Mottelson, 1975) by providing a fully microscopic symplectic model that contracts to a coupled rotor-vibrator model in the large-quantum-number limit. The paper builds on the CM(3) model of Weaver, Biedenharn, and Cusson (1973) and the representation theory of Rosensteel and Rowe (1977, 1980). It enables future shell-model calculations with realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions by identifying Sp(3,R) as the appropriate coupling scheme for deformed nuclei, and it predicts that studies of K=0 bands and E2 transitions between them will provide information on the mixing of Sp(3,R) irreps in even-even nuclei.
