# Effects of Bulk Viscosity at Freezeout

> We investigate particle spectra and elliptic flow coefficients in relativistic heavy ion collisions by taking into account the distortion of phase space distributions due to bulk viscosity at freezeout. We first calculate the distortion of phase space distributions in a multi-component system within the Grad's fourteen moment method. We find some subtle issues when one matches macroscopic variables with microscopic momentum distributions in a multi-component system and develop a consistent procedure to uniquely determine the corrections in the phase space distributions. Next, we calculate particle spectra by using the Cooper-Frye formula to see the effect of the bulk viscosity. In spite of the relative smallness of the bulk viscosity, we find that it is likely to have a visible effect in particle spectra and elliptic flow coefficients. This indicates the importance of bulk viscosity together with shear viscosity if one wants to constrain the transport coefficients with better accuracy from experimental data.

## Key claim

The authors argue that, despite bulk viscosity being numerically smaller than shear viscosity in a hadron resonance gas, its effect on particle spectra and elliptic flow at freezeout can be visibly large, meaning both transport coefficients must be constrained simultaneously from experimental data rather than focusing on shear viscosity alone.

## Method

The authors used Grad's fourteen-moment method to derive the distortion of the phase-space distribution function due to bulk viscosity in a multi-component hadron resonance gas, resolving subtle issues in matching macroscopic variables to microscopic distributions. They then applied the Cooper-Frye formula to compute pion transverse momentum spectra and elliptic flow coefficients, using flow profiles from three models: Bjorken flow, a blast wave model, and a full (3+1)-dimensional ideal hydrodynamic simulation of Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV, while treating shear stress and bulk pressure in the Navier-Stokes limit.

## Key numbers

* At freezeout temperature \(T_f = 0.160\) GeV, the shear viscosity \(\eta = 1.31 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{GeV}^3\) and the bulk viscosity \(\zeta = 4.37 \times 10^{-4}\ \mathrm{GeV}^3\) for \(\alpha = 15\) in the hadron resonance gas model.
* The bulk viscosity model uses \(\zeta = \alpha (1/3 - c_s^2)^2 \eta\) with \(\alpha\) set to 15 unless otherwise noted.
* In the Bjorken flow model, the freezeout time is \(\tau = 7.5\) fm and the nuclear radius is \(R_0 = 10.0\) fm.
* In the blast wave model, the freezeout time is \(\tau = 5.25\) fm, the nuclear radius is \(R_0 = 7.5\) fm, and the flow parameters are \(u_0 = 0.55\) and \(u_2 = 0.2\).
* For the (3+1)-dimensional hydrodynamic simulation, the impact parameter is 7.2 fm in Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of shear viscosity in relativistic heavy ion collisions, such as those by Song (2007, 2008) and others, by adding a systematic treatment of bulk viscosity at freezeout using the Grad method for multi-component systems. It addresses the need to constrain transport coefficients beyond shear viscosity alone, as highlighted by discrepancies between ideal hydrodynamic models and data when using color glass condensate initial conditions (Hirano, 2006). The results enable future full three-dimensional viscous hydrodynamic simulations to include both shear and bulk viscosity effects when comparing with experimental data from RHIC.
