# X-ray Burst Oscillations: From Flame Spreading to the Cooling Wake

> Type I X-ray bursts are thermonuclear flashes observed from the surfaces of accreting neutron stars (NSs) in Low Mass X-ray Binaries. Oscillations have been observed during the rise and/or decay of some of these X-ray bursts. Those seen during the rise can be well explained by a spreading hot spot model, but large amplitude oscillations in the decay phase remain mysterious because of the absence of a clear-cut source of asymmetry. To date there have not been any quantitative studies that consistently track the oscillation amplitude both during the rise and decay (cooling tail) of bursts. Here we compute the light curves and amplitudes of oscillations in X-ray burst models that realistically account for both flame spreading and subsequent cooling. We present results for several such "cooling wake" models, a "canonical" cooling model where each patch on the NS surface heats and cools identically, or with a latitude-dependent cooling timescale set by the local effective gravity, and an "asymmetric" model where parts of the star cool at significantly different rates. We show that while the canonical cooling models can generate oscillations in the tails of bursts, they cannot easily produce the highest observed modulation amplitudes. Alternatively, a simple phenomenological model with asymmetric cooling can achieve higher amplitudes consistent with the observations.

## Key claim

The authors show that "canonical" cooling wake models (where each patch on the neutron star surface heats and cools identically, or with latitude-dependent cooling timescales) can generate oscillations in the tails of X-ray bursts but cannot easily produce the highest observed modulation amplitudes; however, a simple phenomenological model with asymmetric cooling can achieve higher amplitudes consistent with observations, though the authors note this particular model may tend to overestimate actual cooling asymmetries.

## Method

The authors computed bolometric light curves and fractional oscillation amplitudes for Type I X-ray burst models that account for flame spreading across the neutron star surface (using flame speeds proportional to 1/cos(theta) or 1/sqrt(cos(theta)) with Coriolis effects) followed by subsequent cooling. They considered three classes of models: a "symmetric" canonical model where all surface elements follow the same exponential temperature rise and decay; a "latitude-dependent" canonical model where the cooling timescale varies with latitude according to the effective gravitational acceleration; and an "asymmetric" phenomenological model where the cooling spreads in the same manner as the burning but over a longer timescale, with regions that ignited first cooling faster than those that burned last. Light curves included special- and general-relativistic effects (Doppler shifts, gravitational redshifts, light-bending in the Schwarzschild geometry) and angle-dependent emissivity.

## Key numbers

- Maximum tail oscillation amplitudes in the "symmetric" canonical cooling model range from approximately 1.5% to 8% (half-amplitude definition), depending on ignition latitude, neutron star compactness, and flame spreading geometry/speed
- The "asymmetric" cooling model produces tail oscillation amplitudes ranging from less than 10% to about 35%, depending on compactness, temperature gradient, and flame spreading speed
- Temperature parameters used in the canonical models: T_0 = 1.5 keV, Delta T = 1.5 keV, Delta t_r = 0.46 s, tau_r = 0.1 s, tau_d = 6 s, resulting in a burst rise time of about 1 s and decay time of about 11 s
- The asymmetric model uses a total cooling duration of 10 s, with cooling spreading speed taken as 0.1 times the flame speed
- Example observed burst from 4U 1728-34 shows tail oscillation amplitude reaching a maximum of approximately 15% (half-amplitude)
- Models used neutron star parameters: M = 1.4 M_sun or 1.8 M_sun, R = 10 km or 12 km, spin frequency nu = 400 Hz or 600 Hz, observer inclination i = 70 degrees (fiducial), distance D = 10 kpc

## Context

This work provides the first quantitative study that consistently tracks oscillation amplitude both during the rise and decay (cooling tail) of X-ray bursts, extending earlier work on flame spreading by Spitkovsky et al. (2002), Bhattacharya et al. (2007), and Maurer & Watts (2008), and the "cooling wake" concept proposed by Cumming & Bildsten (2000). The results show that canonical cooling models cannot explain the largest observed tail amplitudes (e.g., from 4U 1728-34), suggesting that additional physical effects such as asymmetric cooling, transverse heat flow, or variations in fuel depth or composition across the star may be needed. The work enables future observational tests, such as searching for a positive correlation between burst rise time and tail oscillation amplitude predicted by canonical models.
