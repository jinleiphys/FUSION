# Understanding of the freeze-out in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> I discuss the quantities and effects important for the freeze-out and outline a formalism for the description of continuous decoupling of particles from the fireball. Then I present a calculation of the scattering rates of pions at various temperatures and argue that it is important to take continuous particle decoupling into account when modelling the collision dynamics.

## Key claim
The sharp freeze-out hypersurface assumption common in hydrodynamic simulations is an unreliable approximation; instead, freeze-out should be treated as a continuous gradual decoupling of particles, with the sharp freeze-out being only a limiting case of this more general formalism.

## Method
The author calculates scattering rates of negative pions in a thermal hadronic gas by integrating over momenta of scatterers (π, N, N̄, K, ρ, Δ, Δ̄) using a resonance-based parametrization of hadron cross-sections. The escape probability formalism is applied, where the opacity integral along a particle's trajectory determines whether it decouples, with the fireball expansion modeled via a power-law time dependence of the scattering rate. Chemical potentials are estimated to reproduce SPS and RHIC data on pion phase-space density and particle ratios at midrapidity.

## Key numbers
- Pion scattering rates are calculated for temperatures of 90, 100, and 120 MeV.
- The "universal" pion mean-free path at freeze-out is estimated by the CERES collaboration to be of the order of 1 fm (possibly 2–3 fm).
- Reasonable escape probabilities (∼30–50%) are obtained for temperatures T ≲ 100 MeV, inferred for a particle with pt = 0 at the fireball center.
- About 10% relative contribution to the scattering rate is shifted from baryons to mesons when going from SPS to RHIC.

## Context
This work extends the escape probability formalism developed by Grassi (1994) and Sinyukov (2002) and applies it to the freeze-out problem in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions. It contradicts the sharp freeze-out hypersurface approach of Cooper and Frye (1974) and the concept of a universal freeze-out criterion proposed by Ferenc (1999) and the CERES collaboration (2002). The results suggest that sequential freeze-out (high momentum particles decoupling earlier) could provide an alternative explanation for the Mt dependence of HBT radii, which the blast-wave model has difficulty reproducing.
