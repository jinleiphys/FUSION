# Incorporating Memory Effects in Phase Separation Processes

> We consider the modification of the Cahn-Hilliard equation when a time delay process through a memory function is taken into account. We then study the process of spinodal decomposition in fast phase transitions associated with a conserved order parameter. Finite-time memory effects are seen to affect the dynamics of phase transition at short times and have the effect of delaying, in a significant way, the process of rapid growth of the order parameter that follows a quench into the spinodal region. These effects are important in several systems characterized by fast processes, like nonequilibrium dynamics in the early universe and in relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

Incorporating a finite memory relaxation time into the Cahn-Hilliard equation, via a Maxwell-Cattaneo-type modification, delays the rapid growth of the order parameter (the "spinodal explosion") following a quench into the spinodal region; this delay can be substantial enough that, in systems like relativistic heavy-ion collisions, the system might not have enough time to thermalize before expansion breaks it up.

## Method

The authors modify the Cahn-Hilliard equation by replacing the instantaneous irreversible current with a memory-integral form, using an exponential memory function with relaxation time γ. This yields a hyperbolic partial differential equation containing a second-order time derivative term (Eq. 6). They solve this "causal CH equation" analytically in the linear regime for short and late times, and numerically on a discrete spatial square lattice using a semi-implicit scheme with fast Fourier transforms, for several values of the dimensionless memory parameter γ̅.

## Key numbers

- For a quark plasma with γ = 3D and D ∼ 3.7 fm, the time scale of the fastest-growing spinodal mode is τ_c ∼ 6.1 fm, compared to τ_nc ∼ 2.2 fm for the ordinary (noncausal) Cahn-Hilliard equation, a difference of almost 200%.
- The typical reaction time in the most central Au-Au collisions at 130 A GeV is around 10–20 fm/c, while the characteristic memory time scale near the chiral phase transition is about 1 fm/c.
- The critical momentum for the late-time fluctuation modes is given by k'_c = √[(-b + √(b² + a/(4γΓ)))/a]; for k < k'_c modes are overdamped, for k > k'_c they oscillate with relaxation time 2γ.
- The maximum wave-number velocity at the critical momentum is v²(k'_c) = (2D/γ) · (1 + ξ²/(2γD)) / (1 + √(1 + ξ²/(2γD))), where D = 2bΓ and ξ = √(a/b); in the limit γ → 0 this velocity diverges, while for γ > D the equation becomes causal.

## Context

This work extends the Cahn-Hilliard equation (CH, 1958), a standard model for conserved-order-parameter phase separation, by incorporating memory effects analogous to those used in causal diffusion equations (Maxwell-Cattaneo, Jou 1990s) and in recent treatments of conserved charge evolution in relativistic heavy-ion collisions (Gavin 2004). The results show that memory delays spinodal decomposition significantly, which has implications for fast phase transitions in the early universe (GUT transitions, inflation) and for the QCD phase transition in heavy-ion collisions (Greiner 2004, Koide 2005). The paper provides the first numerical analysis of a memory-modified Cahn-Hilliard equation and suggests that experimental signals of phase transitions in RHIC (e.g., charge fluctuations, domain formation) could be delayed enough to be unobservable in current experiments.
