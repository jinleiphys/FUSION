# Evolution of chirality from transverse wobbling in $^{135}$Pr

> Chirality is a distinct signature that characterizes triaxial shapes in nuclei. We report the first observation of chirality in the nucleus $^{135}$Pr using a high-statistics Gammasphere experiment with the $^{123}$Sb($^{16}$O,4n)$^{135}$Pr reaction. Two chiral-partner bands with the configuration $π(1h_{11/2})^1\otimesν(1h_{11/2})^{-2}$ have been identified in this nucleus. Angular distribution analyses of the $ΔI = 1$ transitions connecting the two bands reveal a dominant dipole character, and quasiparticle triaxial rotor model calculations show good agreement with the data. Since the simultaneous observation of chirality and transverse wobbling in $^{135}$Pr relies critically on these angular distribution results, we also address and refute the experimental and theoretical criticisms raised in a recent work by Lv et al., presenting additional evidence that further strengthens our interpretation. This marks the first observation of both hallmarks of triaxiality-chirality and wobbling-in the same nucleus.

## Key claim

This paper reports the first observation of chirality in the nucleus $^{135}$Pr, claiming that the simultaneous observation of both chirality and transverse wobbling in the same nucleus proves their mutual relation as hallmarks of triaxiality. The claim is hedged by the fact that parity assignments for all levels within the two dipole bands are marked as tentative because polarization measurements were not possible with the present experimental setup.

## Method

The authors performed a high-statistics Gammasphere experiment using the $^{123}$Sb($^{16}$O,4n)$^{135}$Pr reaction at 80 MeV beam energy, collecting 2.5 $\times$ 10$^{10}$ three- and higher-fold gamma-ray coincidence events. They identified two chiral-partner bands (DB1 and DB2) with the configuration $\pi(1h_{11/2})^1\otimes\nu(1h_{11/2})^{-2}$ and five new $\Delta I = 1$ transitions connecting them, then carried out angular distribution analyses using a Markov Chain Monte Carlo technique to extract mixing ratios and transition-probability ratios. Quasiparticle triaxial rotor (QTR) model calculations with deformation parameters $\beta=0.19$ and $\gamma=22^\circ$ were performed and compared with experimental data.

## Key numbers

- Total combined three- and higher-fold gamma-ray coincidence events: 2.5 $\times$ 10$^{10}$
- Beam energy: 80 MeV $^{16}$O on enriched $^{123}$Sb target of thickness 697 $\mu$g/cm$^2$
- Deformation parameters used in QTR calculations: $\beta=0.19$, $\gamma=22^\circ$
- Rotor moment of inertia parameter: $\mathcal{J}_0=25.0$ $\hbar^2$/MeV
- Pairing gaps: $\Delta_\pi=\Delta_\nu=12/\sqrt{A} \approx 1.0$ MeV
- Proton chemical potential: $\lambda_\pi=-2.09$ MeV; neutron chemical potential: $\lambda_\nu=0.96$ MeV
- Minimum energy difference between DB1 and DB2 bands: 17 keV at spin $I = 41/2$
- Mixing ratios for the three lowest connecting transitions: $\delta = -0.10(6)$ for 642.2 keV, $\delta = -0.15(3)$ for 572.9 keV, $\delta = -0.11(2)$ for 476.9 keV, all indicating dominant dipole character
- Gyromagnetic ratios adopted: $g_R= Z/A=0.44$, $g_\pi(h_{11/2}) = 1.21$, $g_\nu(h_{11/2}) =-0.21$

## Context

This work extends the previous identification of transverse wobbling in $^{135}$Pr by Sensharma et al. (2020) and the two-phonon wobbling band by Sensharma et al. (2022), now establishing chirality in the same nucleus for the first time. The authors address and refute experimental and theoretical criticisms raised in a recent work by Lv et al., presenting additional angular distribution evidence that strengthens their interpretation. This simultaneous observation of both chirality and wobbling in one nucleus enables future studies of the transition between transverse chiral vibration and static chiral rotation as a function of angular momentum, and demonstrates the mutual relation between these two signatures of triaxiality.
