# Superheavy Nuclei in a Chiral Hadronic Model

> Superheavy nuclei are investigated in a nonlinear chiral SU(3)-model. The proton number Z=120 and neutron numbers of N=172, 184 and 198 are predicted to be magic. The charge distributions and alpha-decay chains hint towards a hollow structure.

## Key claim

The paper predicts that proton number Z=120 and neutron numbers N=172, 184, and 198 are magic in superheavy nuclei, but this result is conditional on the parameter set used: only the parameter sets fitted to finite nuclei (C1fit and C1hs) yield a bound doubly magic nucleus at Z=120, N=172, while the nuclear-matter-only fit (C1) gives a negative two-proton separation energy for that combination.

## Method

The authors use a nonlinear chiral SU(3) model with relativistic baryonic and mesonic degrees of freedom, adopting the mean-field approximation in spherical symmetry. Three parameter sets are employed: Set C1 fixed to vacuum hadron properties and infinite nuclear matter; Set C1fit obtained via a chi-squared fit to binding energy, surface thickness, and charge diffraction radius of eight spherical nuclei from 16O to 208Pb; and Set C1hs, which replaces 16O with 264Hs in the fit for better extrapolation to heavy nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Proton number Z=120 is predicted as a shell closure for N=172, with a pronounced peak in the two-proton gap across all three parameter sets.
- Neutron numbers N=172, 184, and 198 are predicted as closed neutron shells.
- For N=172, the nucleus 292120 is bound (S2p > 0) for parameter sets C1fit and C1hs, but unbound (S2p < 0) for set C1.
- For N=184, Z=120 yields S2p > 0 for all three parameter sets.
- The predicted alpha decay chain for Z=120, N=172 shows alpha energies dropping to about 4 MeV for Z=106.
- The charge distribution of 292120 shows a strong depletion in the center, with a radius of 8.0 fm and a link length between alpha-particles of about 3.5 fm, close to twice the diffraction radius of 4He (R_diff = 1.68 fm).

## Context

This work extends earlier Walecka-type model calculations (Rutz et al.) that predicted Z=114 and Z=120 as next shell closures, but the chiral model suggests that Z=114 is not a magic number when a known heavy nucleus (264Hs) is included in the fit. The results enable experimental searches for superheavy nuclei around Z=120, particularly at GSI where element Z=112 was found (Hofmann, 1996) and at other laboratories reporting 293118 (Ninov, 1999) and Z=114 (Oganessian, 1999). The predicted hollow charge distribution, also seen in Walecka model calculations (Bender et al.), suggests a possible Fullerene-like structure of 60 alpha-particles and approximately 60 neutrons.
