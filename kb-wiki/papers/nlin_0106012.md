# Second moment of the Husimi distribution as a measure of complexity of quantum states

> We propose the second moment of the Husimi distribution as a measure of complexity of quantum states. The inverse of this quantity represents the effective volume in phase space occupied by the Husimi distribution, and has a good correspondence with chaoticity of classical system. Its properties are similar to the classical entropy proposed by Wehrl, but it is much easier to calculate numerically. We calculate this quantity in the quartic oscillator model, and show that it works well as a measure of chaoticity of quantum states.

# Second moment of the Husimi distribution as a measure of complexity of quantum states

## Key claim
The inverse of the second moment of the Husimi distribution (denoted W₂) serves as a measure of complexity of pure quantum states that corresponds to classical chaoticity, but the authors acknowledge that at low energies (E < 50) the semiclassical approximation fails and some eigenstates reach the ergodic limit regardless of the value of the chaos parameter k, making the measure unreliable in that regime.

## Method
The authors diagonalize a two-dimensional quartic oscillator Hamiltonian H = ½(p_x² + p_y²) + ½(x⁴ + y⁴) − kx²y² in a truncated harmonic oscillator basis (maximum n_x + n_y ≤ 270, dimension 4692) for several values of the coupling parameter k. They compute the second moment M₂ of the Husimi distribution directly from the expansion coefficients using a derived algebraic formula that avoids numerical integration over phase space, then take its inverse W₂ = 1/M₂. Semiclassical calculations based on torus quantization (for integrable case k=0) and the Berry-Voros hypothesis (for ergodic limit) are performed for comparison.

## Key numbers
- The truncated Hilbert space is spanned by basis vectors with 0 ≤ n_x + n_y ≤ 270, giving dimension 4692.
- The harmonic oscillator frequency ω is set to 7.0 for all k values, chosen to optimize diagonalization for k=0.6.
- The maximum reliable energy from diagonalization is E = 800–1000, based on comparison of obtained level density with semiclassical results.
- At k=0.6, more than 90% of classical orbits are unstable according to Meyer.
- The calculation of second moments for all 4692 eigenstates takes about 30 minutes on the NEC SX-5 at Osaka University.
- For each k value (0.0, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6), 1000–1500 eigenstates with E ≤ 600 are plotted.
- At low energies (E < 50), W₂ of some eigenstates reaches the ergodic limit irrespective of k.

## Context
This work extends the concept of Wehrl's classical entropy (Wehrl, 1978) as a measure of quantum state complexity, but replaces the logarithmically-defined entropy with the second moment of the Husimi distribution, which is much easier to compute numerically. The authors note that no calculation of Wehrl's entropy existed for more than one-dimensional systems prior to this work, except for a one-dimensional time-driven system (Takahashi, 1986). The quartic oscillator model was previously used for studies of level statistics and wave functions (Bohigas, 1984), and the classical phase space structure was characterized by Meyer (1986). The results enable efficient computation of a basis-independent complexity measure for quantum states in systems up to at least two dimensions, with potential generalization to many-body systems using generalized coherent states.
