# Damped collective motion of isolated many body systems within a variational approach to functional integrals

> Two improvements with respect to previous formulations are presented for the calculation of the partition function $\mathcal{Z}$ of small, isolated and interacting many body systems. By including anharmonicities and employing a variational approach quantum effects can be treated even at very low temperatures. A method is proposed of how to include collisional damping. Finally, our approach is applied to the calculation of the decay rate of metastable systems.

## Key claim

The paper presents two improvements to the calculation of the partition function for small, isolated many-body systems: including anharmonicities and employing a variational approach to treat quantum effects even at very low temperatures, while also proposing a method to include collisional damping. The authors claim the variational approach is free of discontinuities and achieves a relative error of only about 1% even at very large inverse temperature beta, but note that for very large beta (low temperature) the method may still be insufficient as it starts from a static approximation and only includes small quantum corrections.

## Method

The authors compute the partition function Z using a functional integral approach with a Hubbard-Stratonovich transform to introduce collective degrees of freedom. They extend the Static Path Approximation (SPA) by expanding the Euclidean action to fourth order in the Fourier components of the collective variable (the Extended PSPA or ePSPA), and then apply a variational procedure where a reference action with variational parameters Omega replaces the RPA frequencies to ensure all Gaussian integrals converge. To include collisional damping, the one-body Green functions are dressed with self-energies using a parameterized form for the width Gamma that depends on frequency and temperature.

## Key numbers

- The Extended PSPA (ePSPA) breaks down at beta = 2*beta_0, where beta_0 = 1/T_0 is the inverse crossover temperature where the PSPA breaks down
- The variational approach achieves a relative error of only about 1% for the free energy, even at very large beta
- For the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model (LMGM) with effective coupling constant kappa = 1.313, the free energy is compared across approximations (SPA, PSPA, ePSPA, variational)
- For the decay rate of a particle in a one-dimensional cubic potential, the variational approach (reducing to the Feynman-Kleinert method) smoothly matches the classical Arrhenius law to the low-temperature result from the bounce solution

## Context

This work extends previous formulations of the Static Path Approximation (SPA) and Perturbed SPA (PSPA, also called SPA+RPA or Correlated SPA) as applied to small many-body systems like atomic nuclei and metal clusters, following earlier work by Alhassid and collaborators (1997) and the SPA and PSPA approaches. The variational method is generalized from the Feynman-Kleinert variational approach developed for a particle in a one-dimensional potential (Feynman and Kleinert, 1986) and from earlier applications to many-body systems using coherent states (Yoshida et al., 2000). The approach enables calculation of the decay rate of metastable systems via Langer's method relating the rate to the imaginary part of the free energy, and allows determination of all transport coefficients (inertia, friction, stiffness) in a self-consistent fashion, unlike the Caldeira-Leggett model which only provides a microscopic interpretation of dissipation.
