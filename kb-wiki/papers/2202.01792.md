# A Smörgåsbord of Skyrmions

> We study static solutions of the standard Skyrme model with a pion mass. Using approximately $10^5$ pseudo-random initial configurations made of single Skyrmions in the non-symmetrized product Ansatz and an automatic detection of repeated solutions, we find 409 local energy minimizers (Skyrmions) of the model with baryon numbers 1 through 16, of which 383 are new. In particular, we find new solutions for baryon numbers 5, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, and 16. Our results for the number of solutions per baryon number suggest that this number could grow either polynomially or exponentially. We identify new families of solutions: sheets of Skyrmions in synchronized and antisynchronized hexagonal layers (which we call graphene); chains of 2- and 3-tori; chain-like solutions containing a hinge and many clustered Skyrmions. Contrary to common lore, only the $B=12$ global energy minimizer is made of alpha particles or some chunk of a cubic crystal, whereas the $B=9,11,14,15$ minimizers contain the $B=7$ icosahedrally symmetric Skyrmion as a component. The $B=10,13,16$ are symmetric graphene-like solutions. We find $B=5$ and $B=8$ minimizers with numerically indistinguishable energies. The $B=8$ candidates are the chain of two cubes, which is a chunk of the cubic Skyrme crystal and the fullerene-type ball found originally by the rational map approximation. The $B=5$ global minimizer is either the well-known $D_{2d}$ symmetric fullerene or a new $C_{2v}$ symmetric solution. Finally, our findings show a large number of solutions have no discrete symmetries or just one symmetry, contrary to the common lore that Skyrmions are highly symmetric configurations.

## Key claim

The authors find 409 local energy minimizers (Skyrmions) for baryon numbers 1 through 16 in the standard Skyrme model with pion mass m=1, of which 383 are new, but the abstract's claim of "finding" these solutions is hedged by the fact that the results depend on the specific choice of pion mass (m=1) and would change if m were varied, with some solutions appearing or disappearing as m changes.

## Method

The authors used arrested Newton flow implemented in CUDA C on NVIDIA GPUs, starting from approximately 10^5 pseudo-random initial configurations made of single Skyrmions in the non-symmetrized product Ansatz. They evolved configurations on a 121^3 lattice with spacing 0.1333, then refined on a 361^3 lattice with spacing 0.0444 for accuracy. An automatic detection algorithm using eight characteristic parameters (normalized energy, radius, three spin moment of inertia eigenvalues, three isospin moment of inertia eigenvalues) with a threshold distance of 0.004 eliminated repeated solutions, with a gray zone between 0.004 and 0.008 checked manually.

## Key numbers

- 409 local energy minimizers found for baryon numbers 1 through 16, of which 383 are new
- Approximately 10^5 pseudo-random initial configurations were used
- Each solution took about 3-8 minutes to compute on average
- The pion mass was set to m=1 (dimensionless Skyrme units)
- The topological energy bound for m=1 is E ≥ 12π² × 1.16347
- The estimated systematic error for normalized energy comparisons is 0.00011
- The threshold distance for automatic solution matching was set to 0.004, with a gray zone of 0.004-0.008
- Lattice sizes: 121³ with spacing 0.1333 for initial evolution, 361³ with spacing 0.0444 for refinement
- The stopping threshold for evolution was ∑|δE/δn^A|² < 0.01

## Context

This work extends the systematic search for Skyrmion solutions beyond previous studies such as Battye and Sutcliffe (2006) and Battye, Houghton, and Sutcliffe (2006), which found solutions for lower baryon numbers and proposed alpha-particle cluster structures. The results contradict the common lore that large Skyrmions are composed primarily of B=4 cubes, instead finding that B=7 icosahedra and graphene-like structures appear as components in many minimizers. This enables future studies of nuclear spectra through semiclassical quantization, though the large number of near-degenerate solutions suggests that rigid body quantization approximations may break down for higher baryon numbers.
