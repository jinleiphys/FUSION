# Hierarchical Neural Filtering of Nuclear Mass Residuals and Spectral Signatures of Quantum Chaos

> In complex quantum many-body systems such as atomic nuclei, the interplay between regular collective motion and irregular intrinsic dynamics gives rise to fluctuations that cannot be fully captured by existing global theoretical models. Nuclear mass, which exhibits smooth trends across the nuclear chart together with localized deviations, provides a sensitive observable for investigating such irregular dynamics. In this work, we employ a variety of neural network architectures, which serve as controlled nonlinear filters within a Hierarchical Residual Decomposition framework to progressively extract and suppress the chaotic many-body signature (characterized by $1/f$ spectral correlations) in nuclear mass residuals. The resulting Physics-Informed Neural Ensemble (PINE) model combines multiple mass models and neural network architectures, enabling a systematic suppression of coherent and chaotic components, after which the remaining fluctuations are analyzed using Fourier-based spectral diagnostics across different mass regions. Our results show that hierarchical neural residual learning efficiently removes the dominant low-frequency correlations and suppresses the quantum-chaotic spectral rigidity, driving the residuals toward the uncorrelated white-noise limit. This systematic suppression provides a quantitative diagnostic of the underlying scale-dependent complexity and many-body correlation structure of nuclear mass deviations.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that hierarchical neural network filtering can systematically suppress quantum-chaotic signatures (characterized by $1/f$ spectral correlations) in nuclear mass residuals, driving the remaining fluctuations toward the uncorrelated white-noise limit, but only when the network capacity is progressively increased through a controlled regularization schedule.

## Method

The authors construct a Hierarchical Residual Decomposition (HRD) framework using fully connected feedforward neural networks (FFNNs) and mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures trained sequentially on nuclear mass residuals $\Delta M = M_{\mathrm{exp}} - M_{\mathrm{model}}$. Starting with strong $L_2$ regularization (approximately $10^{-1}$) and progressively relaxing it to $10^{-7}$, each network stage learns and removes the remaining correlated structure from the previous residual field. The resulting Physics-Informed Neural Ensemble (PINE) model is evaluated using one-dimensional boustrophedon Fourier analysis, two-dimensional Fourier analysis on the $(Z,N)$ lattice, and Dyson-Mehta $\Delta_3$ spectral rigidity statistics across different mass regions and shell-distance sectors.

## Key numbers

- The hierarchical training sequence ranges from approximately 100 to several hundred neural network stages per training run
- Regularization strength decreases from $L_2 \sim 10^{-1}$ to $L_2 \sim 10^{-7}$ across successive stages
- The experimental nuclear mass dataset contains approximately 2000-2500 nuclei, partitioned into two mass regions defined by $A \leq 120$ and $A > 120$
- Within each mass region, nuclei are randomly partitioned into 22 independent groups for cross-validation testing
- The shell-distance sectors are defined by $d < 5$ (with $d_Z \leq 2$ and $d_N \leq 2$) for group $g_1$, $d \geq 6$ for group $g_3$, and the remaining nuclei with $d < 6$ for the intermediate group $g_2$

## Context

This work extends the statistical analysis of nuclear mass residuals pioneered by PhysRevLett.94.102501 (2005), which examined spectral properties across different global mass models, and builds on the quantum chaos framework established by Bohigas (1984) and Guhr (1998). The hierarchical neural filtering approach differs from purely data-driven machine learning mass models (PhysRevC.93.014311, PhysRevC.96.044308) by using neural networks as controlled nonlinear filters rather than direct predictors, enabling a quantitative diagnostic of the scale-dependent complexity in nuclear mass deviations. The results enable future studies to distinguish between residual fluctuations that can be eliminated through model refinement and those that reflect intrinsic quantum-chaotic many-body dynamics.
