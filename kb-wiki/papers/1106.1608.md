# Review of Baryon Spectroscopy in Lattice QCD

> The complex patterns of the hadronic spectrum have puzzled physicists since the early discovery of the "particle zoo" in the 1960s. Today, the properties of these myriad particles are understood to be the result of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) with some modification by the electroweak interactions. Despite the discovery of this fundamental theory, the description of the hadronic spectrum has long been dominated by phenomenological models, due to the difficulties of addressing QCD in the strong-coupling regime, where nonperturbative effects are essential. By making numerical calculations in discretized spacetime, lattice gauge theory enables the ab initio study of many low-energy properties of QCD. Significant efforts are underway internationally to use lattice QCD to directly compute properties of ground and excited-state baryons. Detailed knowledge of the hadronic spectrum will provide insight into the character of these states beyond what can be extracted from models. In this review, I will focus on the latest progress in lattice calculations of the $P_{11}(1440)$, the poorly known hyperon spectrum and the energies of highly-excited states of the nucleon, Delta and other light-flavor baryons. In the heavy-flavor sector, I will concentrate on recent lattice-QCD calculations of baryon masses, particularly those that make predictions concerning yet-to-be-discovered baryons, such as $Ω_{cc}$, $Ξ^\prime_b$ or triply-heavy baryons.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD has shown great potential in reproducing experimentally measured quantities and providing higher-precision numbers than experiments in some cases, but the latest dynamical calculations of the Roper resonance have still not observed a Roper mass smaller than its negative-parity partner, and systematics due to finite-volume effects at the lightest pion mass used (156 MeV) could be significant.

## Method

The paper reviews lattice QCD calculations of baryon spectroscopy using variational methods with large operator bases to extract excited states, anisotropic lattices with finer temporal resolution to improve signal extraction, and specialized heavy-quark formulations including NRQCD, relativistic heavy-quark actions, and static-quark approximations. For light and strange baryons, the Hadron Spectroscopy Collaboration (HSC) uses dynamical anisotropic clover lattices with spatial lattice spacing 0.1227(8) fm and renormalized anisotropy 3.5, while heavy-baryon calculations employ 2+1-flavor dynamical gauge ensembles with multiple lattice spacings and chiral extrapolations using heavy-hadron chiral perturbation theory.

## Key numbers

- Lightest pion mass used in Roper studies: 156 MeV, but finite-volume systematics could be significant
- Lightest pion mass for nucleon and Delta spectra from HSC: approximately 390 MeV
- Doubly charmed baryon Xi_cc mass from lattice QCD: 3665 +/- 17 +/- 14 (+0/-35) MeV
- Doubly charmed baryon Omega_cc mass prediction: 3763 +/- 9 +/- 44 (+0/-35) MeV
- Omega_bbb mass prediction from Meinel et al. (2010): 14.371(4)_stat(11)_sys GeV
- Bottom baryon mass splittings: Lambda_b - B_d = 340(11)(8.1)(24) MeV, Sigma_b - B_d = 615(15)(12)(40) MeV, Omega_b - B_d = 749.2(9.8)(9.0)(49) MeV
- HSC spatial lattice spacing: 0.1227(8) fm determined using m_Omega
- HSC renormalized anisotropy: 3.5

## Context

This review extends the line of lattice QCD baryon spectroscopy work that began with quenched approximation calculations by Bowler et al. (1996), Flynn et al. (2003), and Chiu et al. (2005), and now incorporates dynamical 2+1-flavor ensembles from collaborations including HSC, CSSM, and MILC. The paper enables future work on identifying unstable resonances through multiparticle operators and Luscher's method for scattering phase shifts, and provides predictions for yet-to-be-discovered baryons such as Omega_cc, Xi_prime_b, and triply-heavy baryons that can guide experimental searches at facilities like CLAS at Jefferson Lab and the GlueX experiment.
