# Physics-Driven Learning for Inverse Problems in Quantum Chromodynamics

> The integration of deep learning techniques and physics-driven designs is reforming the way we address inverse problems, in which accurate physical properties are extracted from complex data sets. This is particularly relevant for quantum chromodynamics (QCD), the theory of strong interactions, with its inherent limitations in observational data and demanding computational approaches. This perspective highlights advances and potential of physics-driven learning methods, focusing on predictions of physical quantities towards QCD physics, and drawing connections to machine learning(ML). It is shown that the fusion of ML and physics can lead to more efficient and reliable problem-solving strategies. Key ideas of ML, methodology of embedding physics priors, and generative models as inverse modelling of physical probability distributions are introduced. Specific applications cover first-principle lattice calculations, and QCD physics of hadrons, neutron stars, and heavy-ion collisions. These examples provide a structured and concise overview of how incorporating prior knowledge such as symmetry, continuity and equations into deep learning designs can address diverse inverse problems across different physical sciences.

# Physics-Driven Learning for Inverse Problems in Quantum Chromodynamics

## Key claim
Physics-driven learning, embedding symmetries, principles, equations, and physical data directly into deep neural network architectures, can make inverse problems in QCD more tractable and reliable than purely data-driven approaches, but the paper presents this as a promising perspective rather than a proven universal solution, noting that many applications remain exploratory and are developed in theories simpler than full QCD.

## Method
The authors review how statistical inference (Bayesian methods) and deep learning (convolutional, graph, and equivariant neural networks) are combined to solve inverse problems in four QCD domains: lattice QCD (generating field configurations, learning actions, reconstructing spectral functions), hadron physics (extracting spectral functions via automatic differentiation, identifying exotic hadron poles from scattering amplitudes), neutron star physics (inferring equations of state from mass-radius observations), and heavy-ion collisions (classifying QCD phase transitions from final-state spectra, extracting transport properties). They describe specific physics-driven techniques including lattice-gauge-equivariant CNNs (L-CNNs) that preserve exact gauge symmetry, neural network representations with positivity and continuity constraints for spectral functions, and automatic differentiation frameworks that encode physical integral equations directly into the loss function.

## Key numbers
- Nuclear saturation density: approximately 2.7 × 10^14 g/cm^3, used as reference for compressed matter density
- Typical neutron star mass range: 1.4–2 solar masses (M⊙), with radius approximately 10 km, compared to solar radius approximately 7 × 10^5 km
- The narrow pentaquark P_c(4312) is cited as an example exotic state studied with ML methods
- The paper mentions "thousands of hadrons, photons, and leptons" produced per heavy-ion collision event

## Context
This perspective extends the line of work on applying machine learning to nuclear and particle physics problems, building on earlier reviews such as Boehnlein et al. (2021) and Zhou et al. (2023). It connects physics-driven learning to specific prior techniques: the Maximum Entropy Method of Asakawa et al. (2000) for spectral function extraction, the HAL QCD method of Ishii et al. (2006) for hadron interactions, and the Bayesian inference framework of Steiner et al. (2012) and Ozel et al. (2015) for neutron star equation of state inference. The paper enables future work by identifying open challenges: the sign problem at nonzero baryon chemical density in lattice QCD, the need for ML algorithms that deliver high-precision results with controllable systematic uncertainties, and the extension of these methods to holographic QCD, jet physics, and Electron-Ion Collider physics.
