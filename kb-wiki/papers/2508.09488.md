# Role of two-body dissipation on the mean-field dynamics validity

> The role of two-body dissipation in nuclear reactions at energies of several times Coulomb barrier remains unclear but is crucial for understanding the mechanisms of deep-inelastic reactions. In this letter, we report a systematic analysis of two-body dissipation effects on the validity of mean-field dynamics, enabled by the TDHF-QRx approach, which incorporates the collision term via the relaxation-time approximation rather than full collision calculations. For deep-inelastic reactions, the contact time between nuclei is found to increase, resulting in changes to the reaction process and fragment properties such as scattering angles and total kinetic energy. These changes become important with the increase of reaction energy and decrease of impact parameter. We identify the range of reaction condition where two-body dissipation becomes significant, providing valuable insights for the applicability of mean-field dynamics approaches. The limitations of model, particularly those arising from the incomplete conservation of the locality of two-body dissipation within the quantum framework, are also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper systematically analyzes two-body dissipation effects on mean-field dynamics using the TDHF-QRx approach, which incorporates the collision term via the relaxation-time approximation rather than full collision calculations. The authors identify the range of reaction conditions where two-body dissipation becomes significant, but note that the model may underestimate two-body dissipation in strongly localized systems due to the incomplete conservation of locality within the quantum framework.

## Method

The authors developed the TDHF-QRx approach by incorporating the quantum relaxation-time approximation (QRTA) into the Skyrme-TDHF framework, using the Sky3D code as a base. The method computes equilibrium states with density and current constraints, adjusts occupations to a Fermi distribution to conserve total energy, and mixes the equilibrium density operator into the mean-field state using a relaxation time evaluated from homogeneous nuclear matter with an effective in-medium NN collision cross-section of 40 mb. Binary heavy-ion collisions for 60Ni+60Ni and 78Kr+40Ca were computed at energies above the fusion barrier, comparing TDHF and TDHF-QRx results for fragment properties such as total kinetic energy and scattering angles.

## Key numbers

- For 60Ni+60Ni at Ec.m.=300 MeV (~3.1 VB), the relaxation time τrelax and global temperature T were calculated as functions of time for various impact parameters.
- For 78Kr+40Ca at Ec.m.=263 MeV (~2.9 VB), the experimental critical fusion angular momentum Lfus is 117.0 ℏ; TDHF gives 106.2 ℏ, while TDHF-QRx with σNN=40 mb gives 117.2 ℏ, with 80 mb gives 124.5 ℏ, and with 100 mb gives 126.4 ℏ.
- The maximum duration for a single-event calculation was set to 1600 fm/c, with a cartesian mesh of (60×60×24) Δd3 where Δd=0.8 fm and time step Δt=0.2 fm/c.
- The QRTA calculation time step ΔtRTA was set to 100Δt, determined by checking sensitivity to the evolution of collective energies.
- For 60Ni+60Ni at Ec.m.=400 MeV (~4.1 VB) with impact parameter b=7.5 fm, the one-dimensional Wigner distributions of equilibrium and mean-field states were compared at t=80 fm/c.
- The relaxation time formula uses ℏ/τrelax = 7.9 (ℏ2/m) σNN kF ρ0 T2/εF2, with σNN set to 40 mb unless explicitly stated otherwise.

## Context

This work extends the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) approach, which has achieved success in describing low-energy nuclear dynamics through one-body dissipation (Simenel 2018, PhysRevLett.120.022501), by incorporating two-body dissipation effects that become significant at intermediate energies of several times the Coulomb barrier. The approach builds on the quantum RTA framework proposed for fermion systems (Reinhard 2015) and previously applied in atomic and molecular systems (Vincendon 2017, Hughes 2023), with limited application to nuclear systems (PhysRevC.111.044604). The results enable identification of the boundary where mean-field approximation remains valid, and the authors note that further refinement is needed to address limitations from the simplified treatment of locality in two-body dissipation, particularly for systems with strong localized characters.
