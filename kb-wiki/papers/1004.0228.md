# Equation of State of Dense Matter from a density dependent relativistic mean field model

> We calculate the equation of state (EoS) of dense matter, using a relativistic mean field (RMF) model with a density dependent coupling that is a slightly modified form of the original NL3 interaction. For nonuniform nuclear matter we approximate the unit lattice as a spherical Wigner-Seitz cell, wherein the meson mean fields and nucleon Dirac wave functions are solved fully self-consistently. We also calculate uniform nuclear matter for a wide range of temperatures, densities, and proton fractions, and match them to non-uniform matter as the density decreases. The calculations took over 6,000 CPU days in Indiana University's supercomputer clusters. We tabulate the resulting EoS at over 107,000 grid points in the proton fraction range $Y_P$ = 0 to 0.56. For the temperature range $T$ = 0.16 to 15.8 MeV we cover the density range $n_B$ = 10$^{-4}$ to 1.6 fm$^{-3}$; and for the higher temperature range $T$ = 15.8 to 80 MeV we cover the larger density range $n_B$ = 10$^{-8}$ to 1.6 fm$^{-3}$. In the future we plan to study low density, low temperature (T$<$15.8 MeV), nuclear matter using a Virial expansion, and we will match the low density and high density results to generate a complete EoS table for use in astrophysical simulations of supernova and neutron star mergers.

## Key claim

The authors calculate a new equation of state (EOS) for dense matter using a relativistic mean field (RMF) model with a density-dependent sigma-nucleon coupling that is a slightly modified form of the NL3 interaction, but they note that this is only the first in a planned series of EOSs with different high-density symmetry energies, and that hyperons, pion condensates, or quark phases could increase uncertainties at high densities.

## Method

The authors used a relativistic mean field model with a density-dependent scalar meson-nucleon coupling, where at high density the model reduces to the standard NL3 parameter set. For nonuniform nuclear matter, they approximated the unit lattice as a spherical Wigner-Seitz cell and solved the meson mean fields and nucleon Dirac wave functions fully self-consistently, unlike the Thomas-Fermi approximation used by Shen et al. (1998) or the liquid drop model used by Lattimer and Swesty. They calculated uniform nuclear matter for a wide range of temperatures, densities, and proton fractions, and matched these to nonuniform matter as the density decreased, with the transition density found where uniform matter gives a lower free energy.

## Key numbers

- The EOS table covers proton fraction Y_P from 0 to 0.56, with 53 points in Y_P.
- For temperature T = 0.16 to 15.8 MeV, the density range covered is n_B = 10^{-4} to 1.6 fm^{-3} (43 density points).
- For temperature T = 15.8 to 80 MeV, the density range covered is n_B = 10^{-8} to 1.6 fm^{-3} (83 density points).
- The calculations took over 6,000 CPU days on Indiana University's supercomputer clusters.
- The EOS is tabulated at over 107,000 grid points total (17,021 points for nonuniform matter and 90,478 points for uniform matter).
- The density-dependent coupling parameters were fitted to match the unitary gas energy at zero temperature: E_U = 0.44 * (3/5) * (k_F^2 / (2m)), with best-fit values n_0 = 5 x 10^{-3} fm^{-3} and alpha = 1.2.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Shen et al. (1998), who used the TM1 interaction with Thomas-Fermi and variational approximations, and Lattimer and Swesty, who used a compressible liquid drop model with a Skyrme force, by providing a fully self-consistent Hartree calculation that includes shell effects and a density-dependent coupling to better describe low-density neutron-rich matter. The authors plan to match these results to a Virial expansion for low-density, low-temperature matter to generate a complete EOS table for use in astrophysical simulations of supernovae and neutron star mergers, and will generate additional EOSs with different high-density symmetry energies to allow correlation of simulation features with symmetry energy properties.
