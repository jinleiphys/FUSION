# Comparative effect of an addition of a surface term to the Woods-Saxon potential on the thermodynamics of a nucleon

> In this study, we reveal the difference between Woods-Saxon (WS) and Generalized Symmetric Woods-Saxon (GSWS) potentials in order to describe the physical properties of a nucleon, by means of solving Schrödinger eq. for the two potentials. The additional term squeezes the WS potential well, which leads an upward shift in the spectrum, resulting in a more realistic picture. The resulting GSWS potential does not merely accommodate extra quasi bound states, but also has modified bound state spectrum. As an application, we apply the formalism to a real problem, an $α$ particle confined in Bohrium-$270$ nucleus. The thermodynamic functions Helmholtz energy, entropy, internal energy, specific heat of the system are calculated and compared for both wells. The internal energy and the specific heat capacity increase as a result of upward shift in the spectrum. The shift of the Helmholtz free energy is a direct consequence of the shift of the spectrum. The entropy decreases because of a decrement in the number of available states.

# Comparative effect of an addition of a surface term to the Woods-Saxon potential on the thermodynamics of a nucleon

## Key claim
The Generalized Symmetric Woods-Saxon (GSWS) potential, which adds a surface term to the standard Woods-Saxon (WS) potential, produces an upward shift in the energy spectrum and reduces the number of available bound states from 22 to 21 for an alpha particle in Bohrium-270, contrary to the expectation that the additional term would accommodate extra quasi-bound states. The GSWS potential is claimed to be more realistic for describing the physical properties of a nucleon, but this conclusion is specific to the single nucleus studied (Bh-270) and the particular parameter values chosen.

## Method
The Schrödinger equation was solved analytically for both the Woods-Saxon (WS) and Generalized Symmetric Woods-Saxon (GSWS) potentials in one dimension, using a formalism based on Gamma functions and complex coefficients. Energy eigenvalues were computed for an alpha particle (mass 3727.379 MeV/c²) confined in a Bohrium-270 nucleus, using nuclear parameters of inverse diffusion parameter a=1.538 fm⁻¹, radius L=8.068 fm, potential depth V₀=75.617 MeV, and surface term strength W₀=215.523 MeV. The partition function was then constructed from the discrete energy spectra, and thermodynamic functions (Helmholtz free energy, entropy, internal energy, and isochoric specific heat) were calculated as functions of reduced temperature.

## Key numbers
- For Bh-270 (A=270), the potential depth is V₀ = 75.617 MeV, and the surface term strength is W₀ = 215.523 MeV.
- The WS potential yields 22 bound states (n=0 to n=21), with the ground state at E₀ = -75.283 MeV and the highest bound state at E₂₁ = -0.171 MeV.
- The GSWS potential yields 17 bound states (n=0 to n=16) plus 4 quasi-bound states (n=17 to n=20), with the ground state at E₀ = -75.166 MeV and the highest bound state at E₁₆ = -4.402 MeV.
- The quasi-bound states in the GSWS spectrum have complex energies: E₁₇ = 2.263 - 0.537×10⁻³ i MeV, E₁₈ = 8.929 - 0.146×10⁻¹ i MeV, E₁₉ = 15.439 - 0.133 i MeV, E₂₀ = 21.688 - 0.650 i MeV.
- The saturation entropy values are 2.66×10⁻¹⁰ MeV/K for WS and 2.62×10⁻¹⁰ MeV/K for GSWS.
- The limiting internal energy values at infinite reduced temperature are -42.586 MeV for WS and -35.535 MeV for GSWS.

## Context
This work extends the analytical treatment of the Woods-Saxon potential and its generalized forms, building on the formalism developed by Lütfüoğlu and Akdeniz (2016) for solving the Schrödinger equation with these potentials. The study applies statistical thermodynamics to nuclear physics, following approaches used by Hassanabadi et al. (2015), Pacheco et al. (2003, 2014), and Boumali (2015) for other potentials. The results enable future calculations of decay probabilities via the quasi-bound states (following Gamow 1928 and Siegert 1939) and could be extended to other nuclei or to include spin-orbit coupling effects as studied by Candemir and Bayrak (2014) and Bayrak et al. (2015).
