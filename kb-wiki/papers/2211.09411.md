# Isotopic dependence of $(n,α)$ reaction cross sections for Fe and Sn nuclei

> The $(n,α)$ reactions play an important role for the energy generation and the synthesis of chemical elements in the stars, as well as for nuclear engineering and medical applications. The aim of this study is to explore the evolution of $(n,α)$ reactions in Fe and Sn isotope chains in order to assess their properties with the increase of neutrons in target nucleus, and compare with other relevant neutron induced reactions. Model calculations of the cross sections are based on the statistical Hauser-Feshbach model in TALYS implementation, using global optical model potential that is additionally adjusted by the $(n,α)$ cross section data for $^{54}$Fe and $^{118}$Sn. The calculations of $(n,α)$ reactions in Fe and Sn isotopes provide the insight into their isospin dependence and properties over the complete relevant range of neutron energies. The results show the evolution of the cross sections with pronounced maxima at low-mass isotopes, and rather strong decrease for neutron-rich nuclei consistent with the reduction of the reaction $Q$-value and increased contributions from other exit channels from compound nucleus. The analysis of the Maxwellian averaged cross sections at temperatures in stellar environment shows that while the $(n,α)$ reactions contribute for the low-mass isotopes, in neutron induced reactions with nuclei with neutron excess, $γ$ and neutron emission dominate.

## Key claim

The isotopic dependence of (n,α) reaction cross sections for Fe and Sn nuclei is systematically explored using the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model with a global optical model potential that is additionally adjusted by fitting to experimental (n,α) cross section data for 54Fe and 118Sn, showing that the cross sections exhibit pronounced maxima at low-mass isotopes and a strong decrease for neutron-rich nuclei.

## Method

The authors employed the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model and the exciton model as implemented in the TALYS nuclear reaction code to calculate (n,α) reaction cross sections for Fe and Sn isotope chains over neutron energies up to approximately 20 MeV. The global optical model potential of Koning and Delaroche (2003) and the alpha optical model of Avrigeanu et al. (2014) were used, with rescaling factors for the geometrical parameters (radius and diffuseness parameters for volume and surface central potentials) determined by chi-squared minimization against experimental data for 54Fe and 118Sn using the MINUIT package. Maxwellian averaged cross sections (MACS) were also calculated at stellar temperatures of kT = 30 keV and kT = 90 keV.

## Key numbers

* For 54Fe, the maximal (n,α) cross section is obtained at neutron energy E = 15.62 MeV, with the chi-squared value improving from 3.14 (default TALYS-1.96) to 2.61 (optimized TALYS-1.96(a)).
* For 118Sn, the (n,α) cross section peaks around 22.27 MeV, with chi-squared improving from 27.606 (default TALYS-1.96) to 11.65 (optimized TALYS-1.96(a)).
* For 106Sn (even-even), the maximal (n,α) cross section is 194.17 mb at E = 7.8 MeV; for 116Sn, the maximal cross section is only 16.01 mb at E = 20.68 MeV.
* For 53Fe, the maximal cross section is the largest along the Fe chain, decreasing by almost two orders of magnitude for 59Fe.
* The energy of the reaction cross section peak for Fe isotopes with N > Z converges around approximately 15 MeV.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of (n,α) reactions that were often limited to stable isotopes or narrow energy ranges, such as the phenomenological formula approach of Zelenetskij (1995) and the NON-SMOKER calculations of Rauscher (2000). The systematic calculations across complete Fe and Sn isotope chains enable assessment of (n,α) reaction relevance in stellar nucleosynthesis, showing that for low-mass Fe isotopes the (n,α) channel can dominate over gamma, neutron, and proton emission, while for neutron-rich isotopes gamma and neutron emission dominate. The results also confirm the isotope effect originally postulated by Gardner (1964, 1967) and confirmed by Molla (1977).
