# Complex Geometry of Nuclei and Atoms

> We propose a new geometrical model of matter, in which neutral atoms are modelled by compact, complex algebraic surfaces. Proton and neutron numbers are determined by a surface's Chern numbers. Equivalently, they are determined by combinations of the Hodge numbers, or the Betti numbers. Geometrical constraints on algebraic surfaces allow just a finite range of neutron numbers for a given proton number. This range encompasses the known isotopes.

### Key claim
The paper proposes that neutral atoms can be modeled by compact, complex algebraic surfaces, with proton number \(P\) identified as the holomorphic Euler number \(\chi\) and baryon number \(B\) as the quantity \(\theta\) (defined from Hodge numbers), so that neutron number is \(N = \theta - \chi\). The authors claim that geometrical constraints on such surfaces allow only a finite range of neutron numbers for a given proton number, and that this range qualitatively matches the known isotopes, but they do not derive a dynamical energy function or prove that the most stable surfaces correspond to the most stable nuclei.

### Method
The authors identify topological invariants of complex algebraic surfaces, specifically the Chern numbers \(c_1^2\) and \(c_2\), the Hodge numbers, and the Betti numbers, with nuclear properties. They set the holomorphic Euler number \(\chi\) equal to the proton number \(P\) and the quantity \(\theta\) (defined as \(-h^{1,0} + h^{1,1} - h^{1,2}\)) equal to the baryon number \(B\), so that neutron number \(N = \theta - \chi\). They then apply known inequalities for minimal surfaces of general type (Bogomolov–Miyaoka–Yau and Noether inequalities) to derive allowed ranges of \(P\) and \(N\), and compare these ranges to the chart of known isotopes. They also discuss the intersection form of the surface and its relation to nuclear stability, particularly for isotopes where the neutron excess \(N - P\) is a multiple of 8.

### Key numbers
- For the projective plane \(\mathrm{CP}^2\) (model for hydrogen): \(P = 1\), \(N = 0\), Euler number \(e = 3\), signature \(\tau = 1\).
- For a K3 surface: \(P = 2\), \(N = 18\), Euler number \(e = 24\), signature \(\tau = -16\).
- For the quadric surface \(\mathrm{Q} = \mathrm{CP}^1 \times \mathrm{CP}^1\) (model for deuterium): \(e = 4\), \(\tau = 0\), corresponding to \(P = 1\), \(N = 1\).
- Allowed range for hydrogen isotopes (\(P = 1\)): \(N\) from 0 to 9 (geometrically allowed), corresponding to isotopes from \(^1\mathrm{H}\) to \(^{10}\mathrm{H}\).
- Allowed range for helium isotopes (\(P = 2\)): \(N\) from 0 to 18, corresponding to isotopes from \(^2\mathrm{He}\) to \(^{20}\mathrm{He}\).
- Inequalities from algebraic geometry: \(P > 0\), \(0 \le N < 9P\), and \(N \le 7P + 6\).
- For simply connected surfaces: \(b_2^+ = 2P - 1\) and \(b_2^- = P + N - 1\).
- Signature \(\tau = P - N\).
- For surfaces on the BMY line \(c_1^2 = 3c_2\): \(P > 1\) and \(N = 0\).
- For elliptic surfaces (including K3 and Enriques): \(c_1^2 = 0\), \(c_2 = 12P\), so \(N = 9P\) and \(\tau = -8P\).

### Context
This work extends earlier attempts to model particles geometrically, starting with Kelvin’s knot model of atoms and continuing with Skyrme’s soliton model (Skyrme, 1961) where baryon number is topological but proton and neutron numbers are not separately topological. It also builds on a prior proposal by Atiyah, Manton, and Schroers (AMS) to identify smooth 4-manifolds with fundamental particles, and on the use of Taub-NUT space as a model for the electron (J. Sparks, 2004). The new proposal enables a purely topological distinction between proton and neutron numbers via Chern numbers of algebraic surfaces, and it suggests that the valley of nuclear stability (where \(N = P\)) corresponds to surfaces with zero signature \(\tau = 0\). The paper opens the possibility of using the “botany” of algebraic surfaces, the detailed construction of surfaces with given invariants, to model specific isotopes, and of incorporating energy functionals such as the \(L^2\) norm of scalar curvature (LeBrun, 1999, 2005) to select the most stable configurations.
