# Compressed Baryonic Matter of Astrophysics

> Baryonic matter in the core of a massive and evolved star is compressed significantly to form a supra-nuclear object, and compressed baryonic matter (CBM) is then produced after supernova. The state of cold matter at a few nuclear density is pedagogically reviewed, with significant attention paid to a possible quark-cluster state conjectured from an astrophysical point of view.

## Key claim

The paper pedagogically reviews the state of cold baryonic matter at a few times nuclear density, with a focus on the conjecture that compressed baryonic matter (CBM) in pulsar-like compact stars may exist as a quark-cluster state rather than as conventional neutron star matter or free quark matter, though this hypothesis remains unconfirmed due to lack of theoretical and experimental evidence.

## Method

The authors present a pedagogical review combining order-of-magnitude estimates from Fermi gas and Heisenberg uncertainty relations with phenomenological modeling of quark-cluster matter. They discuss two specific modeling approaches: one using H-clusters (based on lattice QCD simulations of H-dibaryon) with interactions mediated by sigma-omega mesons, and another using a corresponding-state approach that analogizes quark-cluster matter to inert gases described by Lennard-Jones potentials. Observational constraints from pulsar mass measurements, thermal X-ray spectra, and timing phenomena are then compared against the predictions of different CBM models.

## Key numbers

- Baryon number inside a pulsar-like compact star: approximately 10^57 (using M_sun / m_p)
- Nuclear density rho_nucl is the threshold density for CBM
- Fermi energy for free quarks at n = 0.48 fm^{-3}: 380 MeV (non-relativistic) or 480 MeV (extremely relativistic), with dressed quark mass m_q ~ 300 MeV
- Energy scale of quarks in CBM: approximately 400 MeV from either Heisenberg relation or Fermi energy
- Mass difference between strange and up/down quarks: approximately 100 MeV
- Binding energy of H-dibaryon from lattice QCD: 10 to 40 MeV
- Maximum mass of H-cluster stars can be well above 2 M_sun
- Mass of PSR J1614-2230: 1.97 +/- 0.04 M_sun (Demorest 2010)
- Low end of black hole mass distribution: approximately 5 M_sun
- Stored gravitational energy for a pulsar with M ~ M_sun and R ~ 10 km: approximately 10^53 erg
- Expected linear polarization of neutron star thermal radiation: approximately 10%

## Context

This review extends the line of work on dense matter in compact stars, starting from Landau's (1932) concept of a gigantic nucleus through the discovery of pulsars (Hewish 1968) and the development of neutron star models. It specifically contrasts the quark-cluster star model (Xu 2003) with conventional hadron star models, hybrid/mixed star models (Ivanenko and Kurdgelaidze 1969), and quark star models (Itoh 1970; Witten 1984; Alcock 1986; Haensel 1986). The paper enables future observational tests, particularly through linear X-ray polarization measurements (to distinguish self-bound quark-cluster surfaces from neutron star atmospheres) and through mass measurements of pulsars with instruments like the Chinese FAST telescope and the LAMP X-ray polarimetry probe.
