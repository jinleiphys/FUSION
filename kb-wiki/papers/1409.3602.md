# The Role of the Electron Mass in Damping Chiral Magnetic Instability in Supernova and Neutron Stars

> We show that the nonzero electron mass plays a critical role in determining the magnetic properties of neutron stars, making it impossible to generate the chiral charge density needed to trigger a strong chiral magnetic instability during the core collapse of supernovae. This instability has been proposed as a plausible mechanism for generating extremely large helical magnetic fields in neutron stars at their birth; the mechanism relies on the generation of a large non-equilibrium chiral charge density via electron capture reactions that selectively deplete left-handed electrons during core-collapse and the early evolution of the protoneutron star. Our calculation shows that the electron chirality violation rate induced by Rutherford scattering, despite being suppressed by the smallness of the electron mass relative to the electron chemical potential, is still fast compared to the weak interaction electron capture rate. The resulting asymmetry between right and left-handed electron densities is therefore never able to attain an astrophysically relevant magnitude.

## Key claim

The nonzero electron mass makes it impossible to generate the chiral charge density needed to trigger a strong chiral magnetic instability during core-collapse supernovae, because the electron chirality violation rate induced by Rutherford scattering, despite being suppressed by the small electron mass relative to the electron chemical potential, is still fast compared to the weak interaction electron capture rate.

## Method

The authors computed the rate of electron helicity flipping via Rutherford scattering off ambient protons in the supernova plasma, using the Boltzmann equation in the linear response approximation. They compared this rate to the weak interaction electron capture rate during core collapse and to the growth rate of the chiral magnetic instability, deriving a steady-state solution for the chiral charge density where production from weak interactions balances against rapid equilibration from the electron mass. They then evaluated whether the resulting chiral chemical potential could drive exponential growth of helical magnetic fields on astrophysically relevant timescales.

## Key numbers

- Electron mass m_e = 0.51 MeV, electron Fermi momentum p_Fe ~ 100 MeV in supernova and neutron stars
- Helicity flipping rate from Rutherford scattering Gamma_m ~ 6 x 10^{-8} MeV ~ 10^{14} / s for mu_e = 100 MeV and T = 30 MeV
- Weak interaction electron capture rate Gamma_w ~ 1 / s (upper bound < 10 / s from free-fall timescale ~ 100 ms)
- Steady-state chiral charge density n_5 ~ 10^{-14} n_e, corresponding to effective chemical potential delta-bar-mu_5 ~ (1/3) x 10^{-14} mu_e
- Wavenumber of maximally unstable mode k_star^{-1} ~ 250 m for mu_e ~ 100 MeV
- Growth rate of chiral magnetic instability Gamma_CMI ~ 6 x 10^{-34} MeV ~ 10^{-12} / s
- Initial magnetic field needed for Gamma_B to compete with Gamma_m: B_star(0) ~ 5 x 10^{14} G

## Context

This work contradicts the proposal of Ohnishi (2014) that chiral magnetic instability could generate large helical magnetic fields in neutron stars at birth, by showing that the electron mass cannot be neglected as assumed in that work. The calculation extends earlier work by Vilenkin (1980) on the chiral magnetic instability and by Boyarsky (2012) on related effects in the early universe. The result shows that the electron mass plays a critical role in neutron star magnetic properties, and that alternative mechanisms are needed to explain magnetar fields of 10^{14}-10^{15} G.
