# Towards Precise and Accurate Calculations of Neutrinoless Double-Beta Decay: Project Scoping Workshop Report

> We present the results of a National Science Foundation (NSF) Project Scoping Workshop, the purpose of which was to assess the current status of calculations for the nuclear matrix elements governing neutrinoless double-beta decay and determine if more work on them is required. After reviewing important recent progress in the application of effective field theory, lattice quantum chromodynamics, and ab initio nuclear-structure theory to double-beta decay, we discuss the state of the art in nuclear-physics uncertainty quantification and then construct a road map for work in all these areas to fully complement the increasingly sensitive experiments in operation and under development. The road map contains specific projects in theoretical and computational physics as well as an uncertainty-quantification plan that employs Bayesian Model Mixing and an analysis of correlations between double-beta-decay rates and other observables. The goal of this program is a set of accurate and precise matrix elements, in all nuclei of interest to experimentalists, delivered together with carefully assessed uncertainties. Such calculations will allow crisp conclusions from the observation or non-observation of neutrinoless double-beta decay, no matter what new physics is at play.

## Key claim

The paper reports on a National Science Foundation Project Scoping Workshop that assessed the current status of neutrinoless double-beta decay nuclear matrix element calculations and concluded that, despite significant recent progress in effective field theory, lattice quantum chromodynamics, and ab initio nuclear-structure theory, the remaining O(1) uncertainties in these matrix elements will prevent crisp conclusions about lepton-number-violating physics unless a coordinated program of theoretical improvements and rigorous uncertainty quantification is carried out.

## Method

The workshop brought together experts in effective field theory, lattice quantum chromodynamics, ab initio nuclear many-body methods, and uncertainty quantification to review the current state of the art and construct a road map for future work. The report synthesizes presentations and discussions from the virtual workshop held on January 31 and February 1, 2022, covering the tower of effective field theories connecting lepton-number-violating physics at high energies to nuclear matrix elements, the status of lattice QCD calculations of few-body transition amplitudes, and the application of ab initio many-body methods such as the In-Medium Similarity Renormalization Group and Coupled Cluster theory to compute matrix elements in candidate nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The current experimental limit on the neutrinoless double-beta decay half-life of 136Xe is T^{0nu}_{1/2} > 2.3 x 10^26 years, from the KamLAND-Zen collaboration (2022).
- The anticipated investment in ton-scale neutrinoless double-beta decay experiments is in the range of 250-400 million dollars.
- The chiral effective field theory breakdown scale Lambda is approximately 500 MeV, with the expansion organized in powers of p/Lambda or m_pi/Lambda.
- The lightest isotope that can be used in an experiment is 48Ca, for which multiple ab initio methods have computed the light-neutrino-exchange nuclear matrix element using the same chiral-EFT interaction.

## Context

This workshop report extends the work of the Department of Energy Topical Collaboration on neutrinoless double-beta decay (concluded in 2021) and provides a nuclear-theory-focused complement to the Snowmass white paper (2022) and the review by Agostini and collaborators (published in Reviews of Modern Physics). The report builds on the chiral effective field theory framework developed by Cirigliano and collaborators (2017-2021), which revealed previously unrecognized contact interactions at leading order in the neutrinoless double-beta decay amplitude, and on the Bayesian uncertainty quantification methods developed by the BAND collaboration (2020-present). The road map enables future work to deliver nuclear matrix elements with quantified uncertainties for all nuclei of experimental interest, allowing interpretation of results from ton-scale experiments such as LEGEND, nEXO, and CUPID.
