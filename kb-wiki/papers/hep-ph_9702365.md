# Dynamics and Phenomenology of Charmonium Production off Nuclei

> Nuclear suppression of charmonium production in proton-nucleus interactions is poorly understood, what restrains our attempts to single out unusual effects in heavy ion collisions. We develop a phenomenological approach, based on the light-cone dynamics of charmonium production, which has much in common with deep-inelastic scattering and Drell-Yan lepton pair production. The key observation is the existence of a soft mechanism of heavy flavour production, which scales in the quark mass and dominates shadowing corrections and diffraction. It naturally explains the surprisingly strong nuclear suppression of J/Psi at large Feynman-x_F. The low-x_F region is subject to a complicated interplay of hard and soft mechanisms. With evaluated parameters we nicely describe available data on charmonium production in proton-nucleus collisions. Using these results we predict a new process, diffractive production of charmonium on a nucleon target, which fraction in the total production rate of charmonium is evaluated at 12%.

# Dynamics and Phenomenology of Charmonium Production off Nuclei

## Key claim

The paper argues that a soft mechanism of heavy flavor production, which scales in the quark mass and dominates shadowing corrections and diffraction, naturally explains the surprisingly strong nuclear suppression of J/Ψ at large Feynman-xF, but the low-xF region involves a complicated interplay of hard and soft mechanisms that the abstract does not fully detail.

## Method

The author develops a light-cone phenomenological approach to charmonium production in proton-nucleus collisions, treating the production as a fluctuation of the projectile hadron containing a c-cbar pair. The nuclear suppression factor is interpolated between low-energy and high-energy limits using the nuclear longitudinal formfactor squared, with separate treatment of soft and hard contributions to the freeing cross section. The model incorporates the evolution of the c-cbar wave packet via a coupled-channel approach with J/Ψ and Ψ' states, and includes induced energy loss effects at lower energies.

## Key numbers

- The soft freeing cross section σ_S is fixed at 12 mb, based on analysis of deep-inelastic scattering data at Q² = (1-x₁)M_Ψ² assuming x₁ ≈ 0.5
- The hard freeing cross section σ_H is estimated perturbatively at 2 mb
- The inelastic J/Ψ-nucleon cross section σ_in^{ΨN} ranges from 2 to 7 mb from experiment, with the lowest-order two-gluon graph providing 5-6 mb
- The ratio of off-diagonal to diagonal diffractive Ψ-N amplitudes ε = -√(2/3)
- The ratio of Ψ' to J/Ψ production rates R² ≈ 0.25
- The ratio r = σ_in^{Ψ'N}/σ_in^{ΨN} ≈ 7/3
- The parameter γ (ratio of weight factors of soft to hard components) is fitted as γ = 0.0037 ± 0.0015
- The fitted parameters are σ_Ψ⁰ = 1.95 ± 0.018 mb and σ_H⁰ = 0.64 ± 0.05 mb
- The predicted fraction of diffractive production of charmonium on a nucleon target is 12%
- The slope parameter B for diffractive cross section is approximately 5 GeV⁻²
- The energy dependence of σ_tot^{ΨN} is ∝ s^0.25, with expected values of 10 mb at RHIC and 26 mb at LHC for J/Ψ at rest in the center-of-mass
- The effective absorption cross section for J/Ψ is about 0.6 of the inelastic cross section when the formation length is much larger than the nuclear radius

## Context

This work extends the author's previous studies with collaborators (Kopeliovich and collaborators, 1991; Kopeliovich and Hüfner, 1995) on space-time development of charmonium production and the soft spectator mechanism of heavy flavor production presented at Hirschegg in 1995. The approach has much in common with deep-inelastic scattering and Drell-Yan lepton pair production, particularly regarding nuclear shadowing as studied by Nikolaev and Zakharov (1991) and Kopeliovich and collaborators (1997). The paper enables predictions for heavy ion collisions, where nuclear suppression in AB collisions is a product of that in pA and pB interactions, and provides a baseline for distinguishing unusual effects in heavy ion collisions from conventional nuclear effects.
