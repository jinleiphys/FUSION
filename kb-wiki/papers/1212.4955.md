# The Role of Flavor in Multiquark Spectroscopy

> In several mechanisms proposed for multiquark binding, the flavor content of the quark configuration plays a very important role. We shall review some past or recent results on multiquarks dealing with the chromomagnetic interaction and with the spin-independent potential among quarks.

## Key claim

The flavor content of multiquark configurations plays a crucial role in determining whether chromomagnetic or chromoelectric binding mechanisms can produce stable multiquark states, but the original predictions of deeply bound states such as the H dibaryon and heavy pentaquark are likely too optimistic once SU(3) breaking and reduced short-range orbital factors in dilute multiquarks are taken into account.

## Method

The paper reviews two main binding mechanisms for multiquarks: the chromomagnetic interaction, modeled as a spin-color operator with a short-range potential and constituent quark masses, and the chromoelectric (spin-independent) potential, which is flavor-independent and analogous to the Coulomb potential in atomic physics. For tetraquarks, the confining interaction is described either by a color-additive two-body potential or by a string-inspired potential with connected Steiner trees and flip-flop terms, and stability is assessed by comparing the multiquark ground state energy to the threshold of separated hadrons.

## Key numbers

- The chromomagnetic model gives a ratio of 3/4 for (Delta - N)/(rho - pi) assuming the same spatial matrix element.
- The original H dibaryon prediction gave M(H) - 2 M(Lambda) = (M(N) - M(Delta))/2 approximately 150 MeV.
- In the atomic physics analog, the positronium molecule (m+, m+, m-, m-) is stable against dissociation into two positronium atoms.
- For the asymmetric tetraquark (M+, m+, M-, m-), stability is lost for M/m greater than about 2.2.

## Context

This review extends the early work on the H dibaryon by Jaffe (1977) and the heavy pentaquark by Lipkin and the Grenoble group (1987), which used chromomagnetic interactions with SU(3) flavor symmetry and assumed equal spatial matrix elements. Later six-body calculations by Oka, Shimizu and Yazaki (1983, 2000) and analyses by Rosner (1985), Karl (1987), and Fleck (1989) found these states to be much less stable or unstable. The chromoelectric binding mechanism for tetraquarks (Q,Q,anti-q,anti-q) draws on the atomic physics of exotic molecules reviewed in ARV, and has been studied in constituent models, lattice QCD, and sum rules, with recent work by Vijande (2012) and others on string-inspired confinement. The review enables future experimental searches for double-charm tetraquarks and baryons, noting the puzzling non-confirmation of double-charm baryons seen by Selex at Fermilab.
