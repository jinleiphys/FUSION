# Bayesian model mixing with multi-reference energy density functional

> Reliably predicting nuclear properties across the entire chart of isotopes is important for applications ranging from nuclear astrophysics to superheavy science to nuclear technology. To this day, however, all the theoretical models that can scale at the level of the chart of isotopes remain semi phenomenological. Because they are fitted locally, their predictive power can vary significantly; different versions of the same theory provide different predictions. Bayesian model mixing takes advantage of such imperfect models to build a local mixture of a set of models to make improved predictions. Earlier attempts to use Bayesian model mixing for mass table calculations relied on models treated at single-reference energy density functional level, which fail to capture some of the correlations caused by configuration mixing or the restoration of broken symmetries. In this study we have applied Bayesian model mixing techniques within a multi-reference energy density functional (MR-EDF) framework. We considered predictions of two-particle separation energies from particle number projection or angular momentum projection with four different energy density functionals - a total of eight different MR-EDF models. We used a hierarchical Bayesian stacking framework with a Dirichlet prior distribution over weights together with an inverse log-ratio transform to enable positive correlations between different models. We found that Bayesian model mixing provide significantly improved predictions over results from single MR-EDF calculations.

## Key claim

Bayesian model mixing applied to multi-reference energy density functional calculations of two-particle separation energies yields significantly improved predictions over any single MR-EDF model, but only when the model weights are parametrized as nonlinear functions of input features using Gaussian processes rather than generalized linear methods.

## Method

The authors computed eight mass tables for even-even nuclei from Z=2 to Z=120 using particle number projection (PNP) and angular momentum projection (AMP) with four Skyrme energy density functionals (HFB1, SkM*, SkI3, SLy4). They applied a hierarchical Bayesian stacking framework where model weights were parametrized as functions of four input features (proton number, neutron number, total pairing energy, and axial quadrupole deformation) using either Dirichlet distributions or inverse log-ratio transformations, with weights modeled by either generalized linear methods or Gaussian processes. The models were trained on two-neutron separation energies from the 2020 Atomic Mass Evaluation for 624 even-even nuclei using an 80:20 training-validation split.

## Key numbers

- Root mean square deviation of the best single MR-EDF model (SLy4+PNP): 0.985 MeV on the total set
- Average r.m.s. error across all eight MR-EDF models: 1.20 MeV
- Standard deviation of r.m.s. errors across MR-EDF models: 139 keV
- R.m.s. error of Gaussian process Dirichlet model (GPD): 0.478 MeV on the total set (approximately 60% improvement over initial models)
- R.m.s. error of generalized linear Dirichlet model (GLD): 0.725 MeV on the total set (approximately 40% improvement)
- Number of even-even nuclei included in training and validation: 624
- Training-validation split ratio: 80:20

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian model mixing approach of Kejzlar (2023) from single-reference energy density functional calculations to multi-reference EDF calculations that include particle number and angular momentum projection. Previous attempts by Neufcourt (2019, 2020) and Giuliani (2024) used Bayesian model averaging or mixing with SR-EDF models that cannot capture correlations from configuration mixing or symmetry restoration. The study demonstrates that BMM can compensate for the systematic bias introduced when symmetry restoration is applied without refitting the underlying functional parameters, and that Gaussian process parametrization of weights provides more reliable extrapolations than generalized linear methods. This enables exploring the effects of restoring different symmetries individually and combining them statistically rather than performing computationally prohibitive simultaneous symmetry restoration and configuration mixing.
