# Ionization potential depression and Pauli blocking in degenerate plasmas at extreme densities

> New facilities explore warm dense matter (WDM) at extreme conditions where the densities are very high (e.g., carbon up to density of 50 g cm$^{-3}$) so that electrons are degenerate even at 100 eV temperature. Whereas in the non-degenerate region correlation effects such as Debye screening and its improvements are relevant for the ionization potential depression (IPD), new effects have to be considered in degenerate plasmas. In addition to the Fock shift of the self-energies, the bound-state Pauli blocking becomes important with increasing density. Taking these degeneracy effects into account leads to a reduction of the ionization potential and to a higher degree of ionization. Standard approaches to IPD such as Stewart-Pyatt and widely used opacity tables (e.g., OPAL) do not contain Pauli blocking effects for bound states so that they fail to explain experiments with WDM in the high density region. As example, results for the ionization degree of carbon plasmas are presented.

## Key claim

The paper argues that at extreme densities where electrons become degenerate (e.g., carbon at 50 g cm$^{-3}$ and 100 eV), standard ionization potential depression (IPD) models such as Stewart-Pyatt and OPAL opacity tables fail because they omit bound-state Pauli blocking and Fock shifts, and that including these degeneracy effects leads to a reduction of the ionization potential and a higher degree of ionization than those models predict.

## Method

The authors use a quantum statistical Green function approach to derive an in-medium Schrodinger equation for the two-particle problem of a C$^{5+}\) state (one bound electron on a C$^{6+}\) core). They incorporate self-energy shifts (Fock term and correlation contributions), dynamical screening via the dielectric function in the random phase approximation, and Pauli blocking via the factor [1 - f_e(p)] in the wave equation. The ionization degree is then obtained from a generalized Beth-Uhlenbeck formula that includes medium-modified bound state energies and scattering phase shifts, with the intrinsic partition function truncated at the Mott density where bound states merge with the continuum.

## Key numbers

* Carbon plasma at T = 100 eV: electrons become degenerate at electron density n_e ≈ 4 × 10^24 cm^{-3}, corresponding to a carbon mass density of 20 g cm^{-3}.
* The electron degeneracy parameter is defined as Θ = T / T_Fermi; the condition for degeneracy is n_e Λ_e^3 ≥ 1, where n_e Λ_e^3 = 8/(3 π^{1/2}) Θ^{-3/2}.
* Example density explored: carbon up to 50 g cm^{-3} at 100 eV temperature.
* The Debye screening parameter for degenerate electrons is given by κ_e^2 = 12 π^{5/2} (e^2/(4π ε_0)) n_e β F_{-1/2}(β μ_e) / (β E_F)^{3/2}.

## Context

This work extends the quantum statistical treatment of IPD beyond the classical Debye screening and Stewart-Pyatt (1966) models, and beyond the dynamical screening approach of Lin et al. (2017), by incorporating degeneracy effects that become relevant when Θ ≤ 1. It addresses the failure of standard opacity tables (OPAL) and the Stewart-Pyatt model to explain experiments at extreme densities (Hoarty 2013, Vinko 2012, Ciricosta 2012, 2016). The approach is analogous to treatments of Pauli blocking for light clusters in nuclear matter (Ropke 1986) and for hydrogen plasmas (Ebeling et al. 2009). The results enable improved modeling of warm dense matter at multi-megabar pressures, relevant to experiments at facilities such as the National Ignition Facility.
