# Comment on "Neutron Skin of $^{208}$Pb from Coherent Pion Photoproduction"

> We argue that the reaction mechanism for the coherent pion production is not known with sufficient accuracy to determine the neutron radius of 208Pb to the claimed precision of 0.03 fm.

# Comment on "Neutron Skin of $^{208}$Pb from Coherent Pion Photoproduction"

## Key claim
The claimed extraction of the neutron skin of $^{208}$Pb to 0.03 fm precision from coherent pion photoproduction is not justified, because the reaction mechanism is not known with sufficient accuracy. Higher-order corrections to the single-nucleon amplitude must be known to within about 3%, which is beyond the convergence of any chiral expansion or the accuracy of any present phenomenological model.

## Method
The authors perform a simple analytic calculation showing that the effective nuclear radius extracted from coherent $(\gamma,\pi^0)$ photoproduction is related to the true radius by $R_A^{\rm eff} = R_A + 3a'/m_\pi^2 R_A$, where $a'$ parametrizes the leading correction to the single-nucleon production operator. They then estimate the required precision in $a'$ needed to achieve the claimed 0.03 fm uncertainty in the neutron skin. They identify four sources of uncertainty in $a'$: the fitted single-nucleon amplitude data, the optical potential for pion rescattering, neglected pion charge-exchange scattering, and pion production on multiple nucleons.

## Key numbers
- The Crystal Ball at MAMI and A2 collaboration claimed $\Delta r_{np}$ of $^{208}$Pb to 0.15 fm $\pm$0.03 fm (stat.) $+0.01$ or $-0.03$ fm (syst.).
- To achieve the claimed 0.03 fm error, the parameter $a'$ must be known to $a' \approx 0.03$, corresponding to a 3% level of accuracy in higher-order corrections.
- Neglected pion charge-exchange effects are 30–40% corrections for light nuclei, far larger than the required 3% precision.

## Context
This comment challenges the analysis of the Crystal Ball at MAMI and A2 collaboration (2015), which used a single-nucleon $(\gamma,\pi^0)$ amplitude from Drechsel (2000) and a pion rescattering wave function from an optical potential. The comment extends concerns from earlier work by Miller (1974) and Keister (1978) about uncertainties in pion wave functions in the nuclear interior, and by Eramzhyan and Wright (1980) about charge-exchange effects in light targets. The authors state that a detailed calculation of the neglected amplitudes is in progress.
