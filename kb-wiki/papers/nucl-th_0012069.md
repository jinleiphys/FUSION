# Astrophysical factors: Zero energy vs. Most effective energy

## Key claim

The zero-energy astrophysical factor, which is invariably used in non-resonant thermonuclear reaction rate formulas, is shown to be very disadvantageous compared to the more natural effective energy limit (the Gamow peak energy). The paper argues that a single term of the Gamow-peak expansion yields a more accurate effective astrophysical factor than a great number of terms of the zero-energy expansion, and that the Gamow-peak approach allows the reaction rate formula to take into account both plasma and laboratory screening effects.

## Method

The author compares two expansions of the astrophysical factor S(E) used to compute the effective astrophysical factor (EAF) in non-resonant thermonuclear reaction rates: one expanded around zero energy and one expanded around the Gamow peak energy E0. The analytic forms of both expansions are presented, and the zero-energy expansion is shown to require an infinite number of temperature-dependent terms to match the accuracy of a single Gamow-peak term. The author then derives a modified reaction rate formula that incorporates the laboratory screening enhancement factor at the Gamow peak energy, and combines it with plasma screening to produce a combined screening factor.

## Key numbers

- For the reaction He3(He3,2p)He4, the screening enhancement factor according to a recent model is fL(E0) = 1.23.
- For the He3(He3,2p)He4 reaction, the screening correction term (15/12τ) ln fL(E0) is roughly 4 × 10−3, while the first significant term (5/12τ) is 7.7 × 10−3.
- For the N14(p,γ)O15 reaction, the two previously mentioned corrections are both roughly 6 × 10−3.
- The bare-nucleus value S(0) = 5.32 is given for the He3(He3,2p)He4 reaction from the LUNA collaboration (Bonetti et al., 1999).
- The laboratory screening energy for nuclei in the CNO bi-cycle or more advanced burning stages is given by UTF SL = −32.9 Z1^(4/3) Z2 eV.

## Context

This work extends the formalism of Ueda et al. (2000) on expansions of the astrophysical factor, and addresses the long-standing practice in nuclear astrophysics experiments (e.g., Krauss et al., 1987; Bonetti et al., 1999) of fitting zero-energy Taylor polynomials to low-energy data. The paper enables a simpler and more accurate calculation of thermonuclear reaction rates that automatically incorporates laboratory electron screening effects, which have been a known complication in low-energy measurements (Liolios, 2000). The combined screening factor derived here allows theoreticians to have full control over the theoretical models used for electron screening, building on the plasma screening work of Salpeter (1954) and Shaviv and Shaviv (1996).
