# Spin dependence of the antinucleon-nucleon interaction

> The status of our present knowledge on the antinucleon-nucleon interaction at low and medium energies is discussed. Special emphasis is put on aspects related to its spin dependence which are relevant for experiments planned by the PAX collaboration. Predictions for the spin-dependent antiproton-proton cross sections sigma_1 and sigma_2 are presented, utilizing antinucleon-nucleon potential models developed by the Juelich group, and compared to results based on the amplitudes of the Nijmegen partial-wave analysis.

# Spin dependence of the antinucleon-nucleon interaction

## Key claim
The spin-dependent antiproton-proton cross sections sigma_1 and sigma_2 predicted by the Juelich group's potential models A and D show significant differences from each other and from results based on the Nijmegen partial-wave analysis, reflecting the poorly known spin dependence of the antinucleon-nucleon interaction. The predictions are relevant for assessing whether spin-filtering can produce a polarized antiproton beam for the PAX experiment, but the actual achievable polarization buildup depends on which model (if any) correctly describes the spin dependence.

## Method
The Juelich group's antinucleon-nucleon potential models A(BOX) and D were constructed by applying G-parity to the Bonn nucleon-nucleon potential to obtain the elastic part, then adding annihilation contributions: model A uses a phenomenological spin-, isospin-, and energy-independent complex Gaussian potential with three free parameters fitted to total and integrated cross sections, while model D describes annihilation into two-meson channels microscopically (including pi, rho, omega, a0, f0, a1, f1, a2, f2, K, K+) with only multi-meson decay simulated by a phenomenological optical potential. Predictions for the spin-dependent total cross sections sigma_1 and sigma_2 were computed from the helicity amplitudes and compared to results from the Nijmegen partial-wave analysis and the Paris potential, including Coulomb-nuclear interference contributions integrated over scattering angles down to an acceptance angle of 8.8 mrad.

## Key numbers
- Model A predicts a maximum of sigma_1 = 12 mb at beam energy T_lab approximately 20 MeV; model D predicts a maximum of practically the same magnitude at T_lab approximately 10 MeV.
- The Paris potential calculation gave the largest value for sigma_1 as -15 mb at T_lab = 45 MeV.
- For sigma_2, models A and D predict values around 10 mb for antiproton-proton scattering at higher energies.
- The ratio sigma_2/sigma_0 is around 10% for beam energies above 50 MeV for all considered interactions.
- For sigma_1/sigma_0, only the Nijmegen partial-wave analysis yields a ratio of comparable magnitude (around 10%), while the potential models predict significantly smaller values.
- The acceptance angle used in the Coulomb-nuclear interference calculations was theta_acc = 8.8 mrad, following the FILTEX experiment setup.
- Model A reproduces measured antiproton-proton polarizations quantitatively up to p_lab approximately 800 MeV/c (T_lab approximately 300 MeV).
- Partial cross sections for the S- and P-waves are tabulated at beam momenta of 200, 400, 600, and 800 MeV/c for both elastic and charge-exchange channels, with the Nijmegen analysis differing from the Juelich models by factors of 2 or more in some P-wave partial cross sections.

## Context
This work extends the Juelich group's antinucleon-nucleon potential models (Hippchen 1990, Mull 1999) to predict spin-dependent cross sections needed for the PAX collaboration's proposed measurement of proton transversity using polarized antiprotons at FAIR. It compares these predictions with results from the Nijmegen partial-wave analysis (Timmermans 1995) and the Paris potential (Dmitriev 2008), finding significant discrepancies that highlight the poorly constrained spin dependence of the antinucleon-nucleon interaction. The results enable planning for a new experiment at CERN to test spin-filtering for antiprotons, which would provide the first data on sigma_1 and sigma_2 to constrain future theoretical models.
