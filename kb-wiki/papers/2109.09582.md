# Chiral Effective Field Theory after Thirty Years: Nuclear Lattice Simulations

> The introduction of chiral effective field theory by Steven Weinberg three decades ago has had a profound and lasting impact on nuclear physics. This brief review explores the impact of Weinberg's work on the field of nuclear lattice simulations. Rather than a summary of technical details, an effort is made to present the conceptual advances that made much of the recent progress possible.

## Key claim

Weinberg's proposed hierarchy of nuclear forces from chiral effective field theory may still face unresolved issues from combinatorial enhancement of higher-body forces in larger nuclei and many-body systems, and this fundamental question remains an active area of research today.

## Method

The paper reviews nuclear lattice simulations that combine chiral effective field theory with a spatial lattice regulator, using auxiliary fields to generate short-range nuclear interactions and treating pion fields with spatial but not temporal correlations. The Euclidean-time transfer matrix is applied to initial and final states to project out the lowest energy states, and higher-order terms in the chiral expansion are computed using perturbation theory to avoid sign and phase oscillations in the Monte Carlo sampling.

## Key numbers

- Momentum cutoff scale of about 500 MeV where spin-isospin symmetry is manifest, providing insight into why chiral effective field theory performs well near this scale
- The radius of the alpha particle sets a characteristic length scale where Pauli repulsion between identical nucleons in symmetric nuclear matter becomes strong
- The nucleon-nucleon interaction has an interaction range comparable to the radius of the alpha particle, placing nuclear physics near a quantum phase transition between a nuclear liquid and a Bose-Einstein condensate of alpha clusters

## Context

This review extends the line of work initiated by Weinberg (1990-1992) on chiral effective field theory and its application to nuclear lattice simulations, which began with early momentum-space lattice calculations by Brockmann (1992) and spatial lattice studies of nuclear matter by Muller (1999). The paper enables future work on body-centered cubic lattices, hypernuclei calculations using impurity lattice Monte Carlo, and the combination of lattice simulations with eigenvector continuation for higher-order calculations without perturbation theory.
