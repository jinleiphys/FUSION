# Relativistic approach to one nucleon knockout reactions

> We develop a fully relativistic distorted wave impulse approximation model for electron- and photon-induced one proton knockout reactions. The relativistic mean field for the bound state and the Pauli reduction for the scattering state are used, including a fully relativistic electromagnetic current operator. Results for 16O(e,e'p) cross section and structure functions are shown in various kinematic conditions and compared with nonrelativistic calculations. Nuclear transparency calculations in a Q^2 range between 0.3 and 1.8 (GeV/c)^2 are presented. Results for 16O(gamma,p) differential cross sections are displayed in an energy range between 60 and 150 MeV including two-body seagull contribution in the nuclear current.

# Relativistic approach to one nucleon knockout reactions

## Key claim

The fully relativistic distorted wave impulse approximation (RDWIA) model, using Dirac-Hartree bound states and Pauli-reduced scattering states with a fully relativistic electromagnetic current operator, provides a systematically better description of electron- and photon-induced one-proton knockout data than nonrelativistic calculations, but only when the spectroscopic factors are adjusted to values closer to unity; the improvement is most pronounced at higher proton energies (above 100 MeV) and at higher momentum transfers (Q² ≥ 0.8 (GeV/c)²), while at low energies (Tp ≈ 90 MeV) relativistic and nonrelativistic calculations become almost equivalent after rescaling.

## Method

The authors compute the nuclear current matrix elements Jμ = ⟨Ψf|jμ|Ψi⟩ using relativistic bound-state wave functions from the Dirac-Hartree solution of a relativistic Lagrangian with scalar and vector potentials, and an ejectile wave function obtained via the direct Pauli reduction scheme where the upper component is related to a Schrödinger-equivalent wave function by a Darwin factor D(r) = 1 + (S−V)/(M+E′). The one-body electromagnetic current operator is evaluated with three different off-shell prescriptions (cc1, cc2, cc3), and for photon-induced reactions a two-body seagull contribution is also included.

## Key numbers

- For ¹⁶O(e,e′p)¹⁵N at Tp = 90 MeV in parallel kinematics, the relativistic calculations require spectroscopic factors Z(p1/2) = 0.71 and Z(p3/2) = 0.60, compared to nonrelativistic values of 0.64 and 0.54 respectively.
- At Q² = 0.8 (GeV/c)² in constant (q,ω) kinematics, the same spectroscopic factors reproduce JLab data well, with sensitivity to the off-shell ambiguity in the current operator within about 10%.
- Nuclear transparency calculations are presented for Q² ranging from 0.3 to 1.8 (GeV/c)² across selected closed-shell nuclei; at Q² = 0.3 (GeV/c)² the RDWIA results lie below the data, while at Q² = 0.6, 1.3, and 1.8 (GeV/c)² they are closer to the data and fall only for higher mass numbers.
- For ¹⁶O(γ,p)¹⁵N at photon energies from 60 to 150 MeV, the relativistic one-body results reproduce the magnitude and shape at low energies, but fall below the data at higher energies with discrepancies increasing with proton angle; the seagull contribution enhances the cross section above the data, with the pure two-body term one order of magnitude lower than the one-body term but their interference being large.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic distorted wave impulse approximation framework previously applied to (e,e′p) reactions (Kelly, 1996; Udias et al., 1993) by incorporating a fully relativistic electromagnetic current operator and comparing systematically with nonrelativistic calculations from the standard DWIA approach (Boffi et al., 1996). The nuclear transparency results are compared with experimental data from Garino (1992) at Q² = 0.3 (GeV/c)² and Abbott (1998) at higher Q², showing no evidence for the onset of color transparency up to 1.8 (GeV/c)². The photoreaction calculations, including the seagull current as a first step toward meson exchange currents, indicate that a relativistic treatment of the pion-in-flight and Δ-current contributions would be needed to bring the calculated cross sections closer to the data at higher photon energies.
