# Pasta fluctuations in symmetric matter at finite temperature

> The equilibrium distributions of the different pasta geometries and their linear sizes are calculated from the mean field Gibbs energy functional in symmetric nuclear matter at finite temperature. The average sizes and shapes coincide approximately with the ones predicted by a standard pasta calculation in the coexisting phase approximation, but fluctuations are additionally calculated and seen to increase with temperature and baryonic density. The different pasta shapes are shown to coexist in a wide domain of density and temperature, in qualitative agreement with the findings of large scale molecular dynamics simulations, but with a much less expensive computational cost.

## Key claim

The equilibrium distributions of pasta geometries and their linear sizes can be calculated from a mean-field Gibbs energy functional at finite temperature, showing that different pasta shapes coexist with comparable probabilities over a wide density and temperature range, in qualitative agreement with expensive molecular dynamics simulations but at much lower computational cost.

## Method

The authors use a relativistic mean-field (RMF) model with the NL3 parameter set to compute the free energy of homogeneous nuclear matter, then apply the Coexisting Phase Approximation (CPA) to determine equilibrium densities and proton fractions of the dense and dilute phases for each pasta geometry (droplets, rods, slabs, bubbles). They extend this by computing the probability distribution of different cluster sizes and geometries using a Gibbs energy functional that includes a rearrangement term from the Coulomb self-consistency, allowing multiple geometries to coexist at a given thermodynamic condition rather than assuming a single Wigner-Seitz cell type.

## Key numbers

- Droplet geometry dominates at lower density (around 0.0119 fm^{-3}) and low temperature, but rod geometry contribution is "far from being negligible" at the same conditions
- At higher density (around 0.0278 fm^{-3}) and T=5 MeV, all three geometries (droplets, rods, slabs) coexist
- The transition from spherical to rod shape is predicted by the NL3 model around ρ_B = 0.03 fm^{-3} at zero temperature
- At the lower density (0.0119 fm^{-3}), the droplet geometry remains dominant at all temperatures considered; at the higher density (0.0278 fm^{-3}), the slab configuration replaces the spherical one as the dominant geometry
- Temperature effects on average linear sizes are "negligible" compared to density effects

## Context

This work extends the perturbative method introduced by Grams (2018) for calculating nuclear distributions from a single-nucleus Wigner-Seitz approximation, applying it for the first time to the pasta regime and using a relativistic mean-field functional instead of a non-relativistic equation of state. The results qualitatively agree with large-scale molecular dynamics simulations by Schneider (2016), Horowitz (2018), and others that show shape coexistence, but the present formalism has extremely low computational cost compared to those microscopic approaches. The authors note that more complicated geometries such as "waffle" and "parking garage" structures observed by Horowitz (2018) and Schuetrumpf (2017) are not included and may require extra corrections for supernova applications.
