# Structural Properties and $α$-Decay Chains of Transfermium Nuclei (101$\leq$Z$\leq$110)

> Transfermium nuclei (101$\leq$Z$\leq$110) are investigated thoroughly to describe structural properties viz. deformation, radii, shapes, magicity, etc. as well as their probable decay chains. These properties are explored using relativistic mean-field (RMF) approach and compared with other theories along with available experimental data. Neutron numbers N$=$152 and 162 have come forth with a deformed shell gap whereas N$=$184 is ensured as a spherical magic number. The region with N$>$168 bears witness of the phenomenon of shape transition and shape coexistence for all the considered isotopic chains. Experimental $α$-decay half-lives are compared with our theoretical half-lives obtained by using various empirical/semi-empirical formulas. The recent formula proposed by Manjunatha \textit{et al.}, which results best among the considered 10 formulas, is further modified by adding asymmetry dependent terms ($I$ and $I^2$). This modified Manjunatha formula is utilized to predict probable $α$-decay chains that are found in excellent agreement with available experimental data.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the relativistic mean-field (RMF) approach with NL3* parametrization provides an excellent description of ground-state properties and alpha-decay chains for transfermium nuclei (Z=101-110), but the agreement is qualified by the fact that the RMF model systematically predicts lower fission barriers in this region and does not show a very steep change at the deformed magic numbers N=152 and N=162 compared to other models, despite overall best agreement in two-neutron separation energies.

## Method

The authors performed axially deformed relativistic mean-field (RMF) calculations using the NL3* parameter set with a delta pairing force (V0 = 350 MeV fm^3) to compute ground-state properties of even and odd isotopes across ten elements (Md through Ds) with neutron numbers N approximately 135-186. They compared their results with experimental data and three other theoretical approaches (FRDM, WS4, HFB-24), then evaluated ten empirical/semi-empirical alpha-decay formulas against experimental half-lives for about 60 nuclei. The best-performing formula (Manjunatha et al., 2019) was modified by adding asymmetry-dependent terms (I and I^2) and refitted using experimental half-lives of 366 nuclei in the range Z=82-117, with separate coefficients for even-even, even-odd, odd-even, and odd-odd cases.

## Key numbers

- Root mean square error (RMSE) for two-neutron separation energy from RMF: 0.3650 MeV for even-N nuclei and 0.2930 MeV for odd-N nuclei, compared to 0.3989/0.3995 (FRDM), 0.3291/0.3259 (WS4), and 0.4238/0.4241 (HFB-24)
- The gap between neutron single-particle states in 288Rf (N=184) is 2.85 MeV, indicating spherical shell closure
- The gap between Nilsson orbits 7/2+[613] and 5/2+[613] in 270Hs (N=162) is 1.58 MeV, indicating a deformed sub-shell gap
- The gap between Nilsson orbits 9/2-[734] and 9/2+[615] in 254No (N=152) is less than 1 MeV, indicating weaker magicity
- RMSE for alpha-decay half-lives using the original Manjunatha formula: 2.14; using the modified Manjunatha formula (MMF-2020): 1.79
- RMSE for Q_alpha values from RMF: 0.4788, compared to 0.4792 (FRDM), 0.2671 (WS4), and 0.4843 (HFB)
- Average rate of change (ROC) of half-life with 0.05 MeV step in Q_alpha: 184.74 for Manjunatha formula, 97.44 for modified Manjunatha formula

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Saxena, 2020) on superheavy nuclei and builds upon theoretical predictions of deformed shell gaps at N=152 and N=162 by Patyk (1991), Moller (1994), and Cwiok (1994), as well as experimental confirmations by Lazarev (1994), Hofmann (2001), and Dvorak (2006). The modified Manjunatha formula enables more accurate prediction of alpha-decay chains in the transfermium region, which can guide future experimental searches for new isotopes and help resolve the competition between alpha-decay and spontaneous fission, though the authors note that the spontaneous fission formula used (Silisteanu, 2015) may need refitting with newer experimental data.
