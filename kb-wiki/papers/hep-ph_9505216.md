# Studying medium effects with the optimized $\bdelta$ expansion

> The possibility of using the optimized $δ$ expansion for studying medium effects on hadronic properties in quark or nuclear matter is investigated. The $δ$ expansion is employed to study density effects with two commonly used models in hadron and nuclear physics, the Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model for the dynamical chiral symmetry breaking and the Walecka model for the equation of state of nuclear matter. The results obtained with the $δ$ expansion are compared to those obtained with the traditional Hartree-Fock approximation. Perspectives for using the $δ$ expansion in other field theoretic models in hadron and nuclear physics are discussed.

## Key claim

The optimized δ expansion, when applied to the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model and the Walecka model while neglecting vertex corrections and vacuum effects, reproduces the same results as the traditional Hartree-Fock approximation, but the specific density dependence of observables (e.g., smooth vs. first-order phase transition) depends on which physical quantity is chosen for the principle of minimal sensitivity (PMS) criterion.

## Method

The authors introduce a linear δ expansion by constructing an interpolated Lagrangian \(S(\delta) = (1-\delta)S_0 + \delta S\) between a soluble theory \(S_0\) (containing an arbitrary mass parameter \(\mu\)) and the full theory \(S\). Physical quantities are computed as perturbation series in \(\delta\), which is then set to 1, and the arbitrary parameter \(\mu\) is fixed by requiring stationarity (PMS) of either the pion decay constant \(f_\pi\) or the energy density. In the NJL model, the quark propagator is obtained via Dyson's equation with the self-energy calculated to first order in \(\delta\); in the Walecka model, the nucleon propagator is obtained similarly to second order in \(\delta\), with the Feynman part of the nucleon propagator neglected and meson fields eliminated in favor of nucleon fields.

## Key numbers

- For the NJL model in vacuum, using the PMS on \(f_\pi\) with input \(f_\pi = 93\) MeV gives \(\Lambda = 571\) MeV, \(\mu = 553\) MeV, \(<\bar q q> = -(250\ \text{MeV})^3\), and constituent quark mass \(M_q = 574\) MeV (with \(G\Lambda^2 = 2.89\)).
- For the NJL model with PMS on the energy density, renormalizing to vacuum \(f_\pi\) and \(<\bar q q>\) and requiring \(M_q\) roughly one-third of the nucleon mass gives \(\Lambda = 653\) MeV and \(G\Lambda^2 = 1.98\); at \(P_F=0\), \(M_q = 314\) MeV, and the quark mass and condensate go to zero at \(P_F = 1.6\ \text{fm}^{-1}\).
- For the Walecka model, using only direct terms with coupling constants \(g_s^2 = 91.64\) and \(g_v^2 = 136.2\) (with \(M=939\) MeV, \(m_v=783\) MeV, \(m_\sigma=550\) MeV) yields a binding energy curve; renormalizing to bulk saturation properties gives \(g_s^2 = 83.11\) and \(g_v^2 = 108.05\).

## Context

This work extends the optimized δ expansion, previously applied to quantum mechanics and particle theory (e.g., by the authors' earlier work in Ref. [mar]), to medium effects in hadron and nuclear physics using the NJL model and the Walecka model. The results are compared directly to the traditional Hartree-Fock approximation (as used in Refs. [hs] and [klev]), showing equivalence when vertex corrections and vacuum effects are neglected. The paper enables future applications of the δ expansion to other field-theoretic models in hadron and nuclear physics, such as those involving vector mesons or more complex interactions, and highlights the sensitivity of results to the choice of PMS criterion.
