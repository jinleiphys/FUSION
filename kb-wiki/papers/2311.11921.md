# Far-from-equilibrium attractors with Full Relativistic Boltzmann approach in boost-invariant and non-boost-invariant systems

> We study the universal behavior associated with a Relativistic Boltzmann Transport (RBT) approach with the full collision integral in 0+1D conformal systems. We show that all momentum moments of the distribution function exhibit universal behavior. Furthermore, the RBT approach allows to calculate the full distribution function, showing that an attractor behavior is present in both the longitudinal and transverse momentum dependence. We compare our results to the far-from-equilibrium attractors determined with other approaches, such as kinetic theory in Relaxation Time Approximation (RTA) and relativistic hydrodynamic theories, both in their viscous (DNMR) an anisotropic (aHydro) formulations, finding a very similar evolution, but an even faster thermalization in RBT for higher order moments. For the first time, we extended this analysis also to study the attractor behavior under a temperature-dependent viscosity $η/s(T)$, accounting also for the rapid increase toward the hadronic phase. We find that a partial breaking of the scaling behavior with respect to $τ/τ_{eq}$ emerges only at $T \approx T_c$ generating a transient deviation from attractors; interestingly this in realistic finite systems may occur around the freeze-out dynamics. Finally, we investigate for the first time results beyond the boost-invariant picture, finding that also in such a case the system evolves toward the universal attractor. In particular, we present the forward and pull-back attractors at different space-time rapidities including rapidity regions where initially the distribution function is even vanishing.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a full Relativistic Boltzmann Transport (RBT) approach with the complete collision integral exhibits universal attractor behavior for all momentum moments of the distribution function in 0+1D conformal systems, but this universal scaling is only partially broken at temperatures near the critical temperature \(T_c\) when a temperature-dependent viscosity \(\eta/s(T)\) is used, and the attractor persists even when boost invariance is dynamically broken.

## Method

The authors solved the Relativistic Boltzmann Transport equation with the full collision integral using a test-particle method with a stochastic algorithm for collisions, discretizing space-time in cells with fixed transverse size and rapidity width. They initialized the system with a Romatschke-Strickland distribution at proper time \(\tau_0 = 0.1\) fm and initial temperature \(T_0 = 0.5\) GeV, varying the anisotropy parameter \(\xi_0\) and the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio \(\eta/s\). They computed normalized momentum moments \(\overline{M}^{mn}\) of the distribution function and compared results to Relaxation Time Approximation (RTA) kinetic theory, second-order viscous hydrodynamics (DNMR), and anisotropic hydrodynamics (aHydro), and extended the analysis to temperature-dependent \(\eta/s(T)\) and non-boost-invariant systems.

## Key numbers

* For \(\eta/s = 1/(4\pi)\), all normalized moments \(\overline{M}^{mn}\) reach the isotropic and thermal limit (\(\overline{M}^{mn} \to 1\)) at \(\tau \sim 2\) fm; for \(\eta/s = 10/(4\pi)\), this limit is reached at \(\tau > 20\) fm.
* The initial proper time was set to \(\tau_0 = 0.1\) fm and the initial temperature to \(T_0 = 0.5\) GeV for the main results.
* The transverse plane was a square of \(5.2 \times 5.2\) fm\(^2\) with periodic boundary conditions.
* The space-time grid used \(\Delta x = 0.4\) fm, \(\Delta y = 0.4\) fm, and \(\Delta \eta = 0.04\).
* Simulations employed about 1500 to 3000 time-steps and up to \(3 \times 10^8\) total particles, requiring 100 core-hours for 2000 time-steps and \(10^8\) particles.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of far-from-equilibrium attractors in kinetic theory, which were largely limited to the Relaxation Time Approximation (RTA) as in Strickland (2018) and Heller (2018), and to anisotropic hydrodynamics (aHydro) as in Almaalol (2018). By solving the full Boltzmann collision integral, the authors show that RBT thermalizes even faster than RTA for higher-order moments. The inclusion of temperature-dependent \(\eta/s(T)\) and the first investigation of attractors beyond boost-invariant systems enable more realistic modeling of heavy-ion collisions, particularly for small systems and at large rapidities where the distribution function may initially vanish.
