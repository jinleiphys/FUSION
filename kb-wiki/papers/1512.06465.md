# Elliptic Anisotropy $v_2$ May Be Dominated by Particle Escape instead of Hydrodynamic Flow

> It is commonly believed that azimuthal anisotropies in relativistic heavy ion collisions are generated by hydrodynamic evolution of the strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma. Here we use transport models to study how azimuthal anisotropies depend on the number of collisions that each parton suffers. We find that the majority of $v_2$ comes from the anisotropic escape of partons, not from the parton collective flow, for semi-central Au+Au collisions at 200A GeV. As expected, the fraction of $v_2$ from the anisotropic particle escape is even higher for smaller systems such as d+Au. Our transport model results also confirm that azimuthal anisotropies would be dominated by hydrodynamic flow at unrealistically-high parton cross sections. Our finding thus naturally explains the similarity of azimuthal anisotropies in small and large systems; however, it presents a challenge to the paradigm of anisotropic flow.

## Key claim

The majority of elliptic anisotropy \(v_2\) in semi-central Au+Au collisions at 200A GeV arises from the anisotropic escape of partons rather than from collective hydrodynamic flow, based on transport model simulations at the parton cross section of 3 mb that reasonably reproduces bulk data.

## Method

The authors used the string melting version of the AMPT transport model with the ZPC elastic parton cascade, tracking the complete history of parton interactions from initial nuclear encounter to final-state partons. To isolate the escape contribution from collective flow, they performed an azimuth-randomized test where the azimuthal angle of each final-state parton after each scattering was randomized while preserving transverse momentum magnitude, thereby destroying space-momentum correlations while preserving the anisotropic escape probability. They also used the MPC/Cascade transport model to check model dependence.

## Key numbers

- Average number of collisions per parton \(\langle N_{\rm coll}\rangle\) = 4.6 for semi-central Au+Au collisions at impact parameter \(b=7.3\) fm, and 1.2 for central d+Au collisions at \(b=0\) fm
- About 14% of partons in Au+Au collisions and 48% in d+Au collisions do not scatter at all before freezeout
- Final average elliptic flow \(\langle v_2 \rangle\) for Au+Au collisions: 3.9% in normal events, 2.7% in azimuth-randomized events (escape-only contribution)
- Final average elliptic flow \(\langle v_2 \rangle\) for central d+Au collisions: 2.7% in normal events, 2.5% in azimuth-randomized events
- The escape mechanism still contributes approximately 30% of the final parton \(\langle v_2 \rangle\) even at the unrealistically high parton cross section of 60 mb

## Context

This work challenges the prevailing hydrodynamic paradigm established by Heinz (2013) and others, which attributes azimuthal anisotropies primarily to collective flow of the strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma. The finding naturally explains the similarity of anisotropy signals observed in small systems such as p+Pb at the LHC (Khachatryan 2010) and d+Au at RHIC (Adare 2014), which have been described by both hydrodynamics (Bozek 2010) and the AMPT transport model (Bzdak 2014). The results call for further studies to identify unique signatures of the escape mechanism and to examine its possible role within the hydrodynamics framework.
