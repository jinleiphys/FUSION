# $α$-decay calculations of heavy nuclei using an effective Skyrme interaction

> Background: For nuclei heavier than $^{208}$Pb $α$ decay is a dominating decay mode, and in the search of new superheavy elements one often observes chains of $α$ decays. Purpose: Explore and test microscopic descriptions of $α$ decay based on theories with effective nuclear interactions. Methods: The nuclear ground states are calculated with the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) method using the Skyrme interaction. Microscopic $α$-decay formation amplitudes are calculated from the HFB wave functions, and the $R$-matrix formalism is utilized to obtain decay probabilities. Results: Using a large harmonic-oscillator basis we obtain converged $α$-decay widths. A comparison with experiment including all spherical even-even $α$ emitting nuclei shows that the model consistently predicts too small formation amplitudes while relative values are in good agreement with experiment. Conclusions: The method was found to be numerically practical even with a large basis size. The comparison of formation amplitudes suggests that the pairing type correlations included in the HFB approach cannot produce sufficient $α$-particle clustering.

# α-decay calculations of heavy nuclei using an effective Skyrme interaction

## Key claim

The Skyrme-HFB model with pairing correlations systematically predicts α-decay formation amplitudes that are too small compared to experiment for all spherical even-even α-emitting nuclei, although relative values between nuclei agree well with experiment. The authors conclude that the pairing-type correlations included in the HFB approach cannot produce sufficient α-particle clustering, even when extreme pairing strengths or surface pairing are used.

## Method

The nuclear ground states of mother and daughter nuclei were calculated using the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) method with the SLy4 Skyrme interaction and a density-dependent zero-range pairing interaction, solved in a large spherical harmonic oscillator basis up to Nmax=30. Microscopic α-decay formation amplitudes were obtained from the HFB wave functions by projecting the overlap between the mother nucleus and a product of the daughter nucleus and an intrinsic α-particle wave function with oscillator length bα=1.42 fm. The R-matrix formalism was then used to match the formation amplitude to an outgoing Coulomb wave at a matching radius beyond the range of nuclear forces, from which the decay width was obtained.

## Key numbers

- For 212Po, the calculated decay width using volume pairing (β=0) is a factor 2.4×10−4 smaller than the experimental value Γexp=1.53×10−15 MeV at the stationary point around r=8 fm.
- Using surface pairing (β=1) instead of volume pairing increases the decay width by almost one order of magnitude, but still remains well below the experimental value.
- The Lipkin-Nogami approximate particle-number restoration increases the decay width at r=9.0 fm by factors of 17 for 212Po, 36 for 210Po, and 24 for 210Pb compared to standard HFB calculations.
- For 212Po, pairing correlations increase the formation amplitude at r=9.0 fm by a factor of 16.8 (corresponding to a factor 281 larger decay width) compared to Hartree-Fock calculations with negligible pairing.
- Even with pairing strengths tuned to produce gaps twice the experimental odd-even gaps (Δth=2Δexp), the decay width remains insufficient to reproduce experiment.
- The oscillator basis size Nmax=30 was required to obtain converged formation amplitudes for separations beyond 10 fm, where nuclear forces become negligible.

## Context

This work extends microscopic α-decay calculations beyond previous approaches that used Woods-Saxon potentials with BCS pairing or shell-model approaches for specific nuclei like 212Po (Lovas 1998, Delion 2010). The use of Skyrme-HFB allows a self-consistent description applicable throughout the nuclear chart, including superheavy elements near predicted shell closures at N=184, Z=114 and 126. The method enables future inclusion of additional correlations beyond pairing, such as those proposed by Carlsson (2013), and can be extended to odd nuclei where large variations in half-lives for decays to different excited states require reliable microscopic predictions.
