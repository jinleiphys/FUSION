# Single pion-production and pion propagation in Achilles

> We extend the applicability of Achilles (A CHIcagoLand Lepton Event Simulator) by incorporating the single-pion production mechanism in a fully exclusive fashion. The electroweak interaction vertex is modeled by combining the state-of-the-art Dynamical Coupled-Channels approach with realistic hole spectral functions, which account for correlations in both the initial target state and the residual spectator system. Final-state interactions are treated using a semi-classical intranuclear cascade that leverages nuclear configurations to determine the correlated spatial distribution of protons and neutrons. The meson-baryon scattering amplitudes used in the cascade are computed within the Dynamical Coupled-Channels framework, consistent with the electroweak vertex. To model pion absorption, we employ the optical potential approach of Oset and Salcedo. As an alternative approach, we explicitly model the production and propagation of resonances which mediate pion-nucleon scattering and pion absorption. We validate out approach against pion-nucleon and pion-nucleus scattering data, and present comparisons with electron- and neutrino-nucleus measurements from e4$ν$, T2K, MINER$ν$A, and MicroBooNE.

## Key claim

The paper extends the Achilles event generator to model single-pion production in a fully exclusive manner by combining the Dynamical Coupled-Channels (DCC) approach with realistic hole spectral functions, while the abstract does not emphasize that the DCC implementation retains full dynamical information of the amplitudes (unlike the inclusive implementation in NEUT) and that two alternative cascade modes for pion propagation are tested to gauge sensitivity to in-medium effects.

## Method

The electroweak interaction vertex is modeled using the ANL-Osaka Dynamical Coupled-Channels (DCC) framework, which provides a unitary description of meson-baryon scattering and electroweak pion production by solving coupled integral equations. The DCC amplitudes are combined with realistic hole spectral functions for 12C and 40Ar nuclei to account for initial-state correlations. Final-state interactions are treated via a semi-classical intranuclear cascade that uses nuclear configurations from Green's function Monte Carlo (for 12C) or single-particle density distributions (for 40Ar), with two alternative approaches for pion propagation: one using DCC meson-baryon cross sections with Oset-Salcedo optical potential absorption, and another treating the Delta resonance as a propagating degree of freedom with explicit resonance production, propagation, and decay.

## Key numbers

- The Delta resonance vacuum width is taken as Gamma = 112 MeV for the GiBUU-style propagating Delta model.
- The nuclear saturation density used in the Oset model is rho_0 = 0.16/fm^3.
- The Oset model absorption parameterization is evaluated for pion kinetic energies up to 350 MeV, with the paper noting it extends beyond this value.
- The Delta production Lagrangian uses couplings f_NNpi = 1.008 and f_NDelta_pi = 2.202, with a form factor cutoff Lambda = 0.63 GeV.
- The DCC model parameters are determined by fitting pi N -> pi N data from the SAID database, with the model covering resonance masses including the Delta(1232) and higher N* and Delta states.

## Context

This work extends the Achilles event generator (Isaacson 2022) by adding exclusive single-pion production, building on prior implementations of the DCC model in NEUT (Yamauchi 2024) that were limited to inclusive structure functions. The approach contrasts with the Rein-Sehgal model used in NEUT and GENIE, and with the HNV model (Hernandez 2007) which is limited at intermediate energies. The two cascade modes follow approaches from GiBUU (Buss 2011) and INCL (Cugnon 1996) for propagating Delta resonances, and the Oset-Salcedo optical potential (Salcedo 1987) for absorption. The validation enables comparisons with electron and neutrino data from e4nu, T2K, MINERvA, and MicroBooNE, and is relevant for DUNE and Hyper-Kamiokande where pion production and reabsorption affect event classification.
