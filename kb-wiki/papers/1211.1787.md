# Nuclear proton and neutron distributions in the detection of weak interacting massive particles

> In the evaluation of weak interacting massive particles (WIMPs) detection rates, the WIMP-nucleus cross section is commonly described by using form factors extracted from charge distributions. In this work, we use different proton and neutron distributions taken from Hartree-Fock calculations. We study the effects of this choice on the total detection rates for six nuclei with different neutron excess, and taken from different regions of the nuclear chart. The use of different distributions for protons and neutrons becomes more important if isospin-dependent WIMP-nucleon interactions are considered. The need of distinct descriptions of proton and neutron densities reduces with the lowering of the detection energy thresholds.

## Key claim

Using distinct proton and neutron distributions from Hartree-Fock calculations, rather than the common approximation of using charge distributions for both, modifies WIMP-nucleus cross sections and detection rates; the effect becomes more important for isospin-dependent WIMP-nucleon interactions and for heavier nuclei, but the need for separate descriptions reduces as detection energy thresholds are lowered.

## Method

The authors performed non-relativistic Hartree-Fock (HF) calculations using the Gogny D1M effective nucleon-nucleon interaction to generate proton and neutron density distributions for six spherical nuclei: 16O, 40Ca, 40Ar, 72Ge, 136Xe, and 208Pb. For the three open-shell nuclei (40Ar, 72Ge, 136Xe), pairing correlations were included by solving the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) equations. These densities were then used to compute form factors and, via standard WIMP detection rate formulas, differential and total event rates, which were compared to results obtained using the commonly adopted Helm form factor and empirical charge distributions.

## Key numbers

- Binding energies per nucleon from HF calculations: 7.98 MeV (16O), 8.51 MeV (40Ca), 7.83 MeV (208Pb); experimental values are 7.98, 8.55, and 7.87 MeV respectively.
- Charge rms radii from HF calculations: 2.76 fm (16O), 3.47 fm (40Ca), 5.48 fm (208Pb); experimental values are 2.74, 3.48, and 5.50 fm respectively.
- For 40Ar, the HF+BCS calculation gives charge rms radius 3.40 fm (experimental 3.42 fm); for 72Ge, 4.02 fm (experimental 4.05 fm); for 136Xe, 4.81 fm (no experimental value given).
- Relative differences in total rates between matter-distribution and Helm-form-factor calculations reach a maximum of 3% for 16O and 10-20% for 208Pb.
- The differential rate differences between the authors' charge distributions and the Helm form factor are at most 5% in the peaks.
- Upper limits on the WIMP-proton cross section for a single event per day in a 100-ton detector were calculated for threshold energies of 1, 10, and 100 keV, for WIMP masses up to 100 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Dudek (2007), who proposed using measured charge distributions directly, by instead using microscopic Hartree-Fock densities that separately describe protons and neutrons. It also builds on the relativistic-Hartree framework of Chen (2011). The results enable more accurate predictions for WIMP detection rates, particularly for experiments using heavy targets like 136Xe and 208Pb, and show that the common Helm form factor approximation introduces systematic errors that grow with neutron excess and detection threshold energy.
