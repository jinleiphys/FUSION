# Refining mass formulas for astrophysical applications: a Bayesian neural network approach

> Exotic nuclei, particularly those near the driplines, are at the core of one of the fundamental questions driving nuclear structure and astrophysics today: what are the limits of nuclear binding? Exotic nuclei play a critical role in both informing theoretical models as well as in our understanding of the origin of the heavy elements. Our purpose is to refine existing mass models through the training of an artificial neural network that will mitigate the large model discrepancies far away from stability. The basic paradigm of our two-pronged approach is an existing mass model that captures as much as possible of the underlying physics followed by the implementation of a Bayesian Neural Network (BNN) refinement to account for the missing physics. Bayesian inference is employed to determine the parameters of the neural network so that model predictions may be accompanied by theoretical uncertainties. Despite the undeniable quality of the mass models adopted in this work, we observe a significant improvement (of about 40%) after the BNN refinement is implemented. Indeed, in the specific case of the Duflo-Zuker mass formula, we find that the rms deviation relative to experiment is reduced from rms =0.503MeV to rms=0.286 MeV. These newly refined mass tables are used to map the neutron drip lines (or rather "drip bands") and to study a few critical r-process nuclei. The BNN approach is highly successful in refining the predictions of existing mass models. In particular, the large discrepancy displayed by the original "bare" models in regions where experimental data is unavailable is considerably quenched after the BNN refinement. This lends credence to our approach and has motivated us to publish refined mass tables that we trust will be helpful for future astrophysical applications.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a Bayesian Neural Network (BNN) refinement applied to existing nuclear mass models reduces the root-mean-square deviation between theoretical predictions and experimental masses by about 40%, but this improvement is conditional on the availability of a sufficiently large training set of known masses and is demonstrated only for nuclei between ⁴⁰Ca and ²⁴⁰U.

## Method

The authors take three existing mass models (the 10-parameter Duflo-Zuker model DZ10, the 28-parameter Duflo-Zuker model DZ, and the microscopic Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov model HF19) and train a Bayesian neural network on the residuals between each model's predictions and experimental masses from the AME2012 compilation. The neural network uses a single hidden layer with tanh activation functions, and its parameters are sampled via Markov Chain Monte Carlo to produce posterior distributions that yield both refined mass predictions and associated theoretical uncertainties.

## Key numbers

- For the DZ10 model, the rms deviation relative to experiment is reduced from 0.503 MeV to 0.286 MeV after BNN refinement.
- The overall improvement across the mass models is about 40% after BNN refinement.
- The DZ10 model recalibrated in this work yields an rms deviation of 0.552 MeV relative to the AME2012 compilation for nuclei between ⁴⁰Ca and ²⁴⁰U.
- The experimental data set used for training and validation comprises over 2,000 well-measured nuclei between ⁴⁰Ca and ²⁴⁰U.
- One million Monte Carlo configurations were generated for the DZ10 recalibration, with the first 10,000 steps used for thermalization and an autocorrelation time of about 20 configurations, yielding nearly 50,000 configurations for analysis.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier BNN approach (Utama 2015, Piekarewicz 2016) from applications to neutron-star crust masses to the entire nuclear chart, and it builds on the success of the Duflo-Zuker mass models (Duflo 1995) and the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov model (Goriely 2010). The refined mass tables are intended for use in r-process nucleosynthesis calculations and neutron-star crust composition studies, where mass uncertainties of less than 100 keV are required (Mumpower 2015). The paper also provides tables of one- and two-nucleon separation energies with associated uncertainties, enabling future astrophysical simulations to incorporate statistically meaningful error estimates.
