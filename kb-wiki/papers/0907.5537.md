# Electrically Charged Strange Quark Stars

> The possible existence of compact stars made of absolutely stable strange quark matter--referred to as strange stars--was pointed out by E. Witten almost a quarter of a century ago. One of the most amazing features of such objects concerns the possible existence of ultra-strong electric fields on their surfaces, which, for ordinary strange matter, is around $10^{18}$ V/cm. If strange matter forms a color superconductor, as expected for such matter, the strength of the electric field may increase to values that exceed $10^{19}$ V/cm. The energy density associated with such huge electric fields is on the same order of magnitude as the energy density of strange matter itself, which, as shown in this paper, alters the masses and radii of strange quark stars at the 15% and 5% level, respectively. Such mass increases facilitate the interpretation of massive compact stars, with masses of around $2 M_\odot$, as strange quark stars.

## Key claim

The energy density of the ultra-strong electric fields on the surfaces of strange quark stars (around 10^19 to 10^20 V/cm) is comparable to the energy density of strange matter itself, and when included in the energy-momentum tensor, it alters the masses and radii of such stars at the 15% and 5% level respectively, which facilitates interpreting massive compact stars of around 2 solar masses as strange quark stars.

## Method

The authors model the electric charge distribution on the surface of a strange quark star as a Gaussian centered at the stellar surface, with a width parameter b and a normalization constant kappa related to a charge parameter sigma. They then solve the general relativistic stellar structure equations (the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation modified to include electric charge and field contributions) together with the MIT bag model equation of state (P = (epsilon - 4B)/3 with B^1/4 = 150 MeV) to compute mass-radius sequences for different values of sigma.

## Key numbers

- For electrically uncharged strange stars (sigma = 0): maximum mass = 2.02 solar masses, radius = 10.99 km
- For sigma = 500: maximum mass = 2.07 solar masses, radius = 11.1 km, total charge Q = 989 x 10^17 C, surface electric field E = 7.1 x 10^19 V/cm
- For sigma = 750: maximum mass = 2.15 solar masses, radius = 11.2 km, Q = 1486 x 10^17 C, E = 10.5 x 10^19 V/cm
- For sigma = 1000: maximum mass = 2.25 solar masses, radius = 11.4 km, Q = 1982 x 10^17 C, E = 13.5 x 10^19 V/cm
- The electric field strengths required to affect stellar structure are estimated as E ~ 10^19 to 10^20 V/cm, corresponding to E^2/8pi being a few percent of the typical interior pressure P ~ 100 MeV/fm^3
- The width b of the charge distribution is taken as 0.001 km for numerical reasons, but the true width is only around 10^3 fm; the results are independent of this width

## Context

This work extends the original strange quark matter hypothesis of Witten (1984) and the early strange star models of Alcock, Farhi, and Olinto (1986, 1988) by incorporating the energy density of the predicted ultra-strong surface electric fields (around 10^18 V/cm for ordinary strange matter, and exceeding 10^19 V/cm for color superconducting matter) into the general relativistic stellar structure equations. The paper shows that these fields can increase stellar masses by up to 15%, enabling the interpretation of observed massive compact stars (around 2 solar masses) as strange quark stars rather than neutron stars. The authors also note that the electric charge gradient term in the modified TOV equation is independent of the width of a narrowly concentrated charge distribution, a result applicable to any general relativistic charged star.
