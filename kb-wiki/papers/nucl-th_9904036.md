# Compressibility of Nuclear Matter from Shell Effects in Nuclei

> The compressibility of nuclear matter has received significant attention in the last decade and a variety of approaches have been employed to extract this fundamental property of matter. Recently, significant differences have emerged between the results of relativistic and non-relativistic calculations of breathing mode giant monopole resonance (GMR). This is due to a lack of understanding of the dynamics of GMR and of its exact relationship to the compression modulus of the infinite nuclear matter. Here, I present an alternative approach based upon nuclear shell effects. The shell effects are known to manifest experimentally in terms of particle-separation energies with an exceedingly high precision. Within the framework of the non-relativistic density-dependent Skyrme theory, it is shown that the compressibility of nuclear matter has a significant influence on shell effects in nuclei. It is shown that 2-neutron separation energies and hence the empirical shell effects can be used to constrain the compressibility of nuclear matter.

## Key claim

The compressibility of nuclear matter has a significant influence on shell effects in nuclei, and empirical two-neutron separation energies can be used to constrain the compression modulus K, with the data pointing to K in the range 270-300 MeV, contrary to the lower values obtained from some non-relativistic giant monopole resonance analyses.

## Method

The author constructed a series of zero-range Skyrme forces with incompressibility values ranging from 200 to 393 MeV, fitting ground-state binding energies and charge radii of key nuclei (16O, 40Ca, 90Zr, 116Sn, 124Sn, 208Pb) using Hartree-Fock calculations with BCS pairing. Two-neutron separation energies for even-mass Ni isotopes from A=52 to A=70 were calculated and compared with experimental data, particularly the difference in S2n between 56Ni (N=28 magic number) and 58Ni (N=30). The saturation density was varied over 0.140-0.170 fm^-3, the effective mass was fixed at 0.79, and the saturation binding energy was fixed at -16.0 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The experimental difference in S2n between 56Ni and 58Ni is 8.37 MeV; the Skyrme force with K=200 MeV gives 6.59 MeV, while the force with K=393 MeV gives 9.46 MeV, with the K=270 MeV curve closest to the experimental value.
- The Skyrme forces constructed have incompressibility values of 200, 220, 249, 270, 305, 327, 360, and 393 MeV, with saturation densities ranging from 0.140 to 0.170 fm^-3.
- Experimental charge radii for 58Ni, 60Ni, 62Ni, and 64Ni are 3.776, 3.815, 3.846, and 3.868 fm respectively (from muonic atom measurements), with theoretical curves falling between K=270-327 MeV.
- The force SkP (with K about 200 MeV) produces weaker shell effects than observed experimentally, consistent with the finding that low K values underestimate the S2n difference.

## Context

This work addresses the discrepancy between relativistic and non-relativistic calculations of the giant monopole resonance (GMR) and its relationship to nuclear matter compressibility, following Blaizot (1980) and others who studied the correlation between K and saturation density. The approach using shell effects from two-neutron separation energies provides an alternative to GMR-based methods, which have shown lesser sensitivity to compressibility as noted by Pearson (1991) and Shlomo and Youngblood (1993). The conclusion that K is around 270-300 MeV supports the strong shell effects near the neutron drip line predicted by the relativistic mean-field theory of Sharma, Lalazissis, Hillebrandt, and Ring (1994), in contrast to the quenched shell effects found in some non-relativistic Skyrme calculations by Dobaczewski et al. (1994).
