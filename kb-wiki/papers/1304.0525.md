# The quark-jet contribution to the fragmentation functions for the pion and kaon with the nonlocal interactions

> We investigate the unpolarized pion and kaon fragmentation functions using the nonlocal chiral-quark model. In this model the interactions between the quarks and pseudoscalar mesons is manifested nonlocally. In addition, the explicit flavor SU(3) symmetry breaking effect is taken into account in terms of the current quark masses. The results of our model are evaluated to higher $Q^2$ value $Q^2=4\,\mathrm{GeV}^2$ by the DGLAP evolution. Then we compare them with the empirical parametrizations. We find that our results are in relatively good agreement with the empirical parametrizations and the other theoretical estimations.

## Key claim

The authors claim that including the quark-jet contribution (multi-step fragmentation cascade) in the nonlocal chiral-quark model (NLChQM) produces pion and kaon fragmentation functions that are in "relatively good agreement" with empirical parametrizations, but this agreement is conditional on the specific channel and kinematic region, with notable discrepancies for kaon channels and at low momentum fraction \(z\).

## Method

The authors compute elementary (one-step) fragmentation functions for light quarks (\(u,d,s\)) fragmenting into pions and kaons using the nonlocal chiral-quark model (NLChQM), which incorporates momentum-dependent effective quark masses and explicit flavor SU(3) symmetry breaking via current quark masses \((m_u,m_d,m_s)=(5,5,150)\) MeV. They then include the quark-jet contribution by solving coupled integral equations for the fragmentation cascade via a Monte Carlo simulation, following the method developed for the Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model by Ito (2009) and Matevosyan (2010, 2011). The resulting fragmentation functions at the model scale \(\mu^2=0.36\,\mathrm{GeV}^2\) are evolved to \(Q^2=4\,\mathrm{GeV}^2\) using DGLAP evolution via the QCDNUM17 program.

## Key numbers

- The model scale is set at \(\mu^2 = 0.36\,\mathrm{GeV}^2\) and results are evolved to \(Q^2 = 4\,\mathrm{GeV}^2\).
- Current quark masses are chosen as \((m_u, m_d, m_s) = (5, 5, 150)\) MeV.
- The momentum-dependent effective quark mass uses \(M_0 \approx 350\) MeV and \(\Lambda \approx 600\) MeV, with a dipole form factor (\(n=2\)).
- Pion and kaon masses are taken as \(m_\pi = 140\) MeV and \(m_K = 495\) MeV.
- The Monte Carlo simulation for the quark-jet cascade requires \(N_\mathrm{links} \ge 15\) for convergence in NLChQM, compared to \(N_\mathrm{links} = 8\) for the NJL model.
- For the direct fragmentation function \(zD^{\pi^+}_u(z)\) at \(Q^2=4\,\mathrm{GeV}^2\), the NLChQM result lies within the HKNS uncertainty band; the peak of the elementary fragmentation function occurs at \(z=0.5\) in NLChQM versus \(z=0.8\) in the NJL model.
- For the indirect fragmentation function \(zD^{\pi^-}_u(z)\) at \(Q^2=4\,\mathrm{GeV}^2\), the NLChQM curve agrees well with both HKNS and DSS parametrizations between \(z=0.2\) and \(z=1.0\).
- The NLChQM result for \(zD^{K^-}_s(z)\) at \(Q^2=4\,\mathrm{GeV}^2\) is too large compared to empirical curves in the high \(z\) region but lies between the HKNS and DSS curves for \(z \le 0.4\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous calculations of elementary fragmentation functions in the nonlocal chiral-quark model (Nam 2011, Nam 2012) by incorporating the quark-jet contribution via the cascade method developed for the NJL model by Ito (2009) and Matevosyan (2010, 2011). The approach addresses the limitations of the earlier NLChQM results, which did not satisfy momentum sum rules and gave zero fragmentation functions for certain channels (e.g., \(u \rightarrow \pi^-\) and \(s \rightarrow K^+\)). The results are compared with the NJL-jet model and with two empirical parametrizations: HKNS (Hirai 2007) and DSS (de Florian 2007). The work enables further refinement of nonperturbative models for fragmentation functions and highlights channels where the NLChQM description remains inadequate, particularly for kaon fragmentation at high \(z\).
