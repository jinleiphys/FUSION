# Fusion-fission probabilities, cross sections and structure notes of super-heavy nuclei

> Fusion - fission probabilities in the synthesis of heaviest elements are discussed in the context of the latest experimental reports. Cross sections for superheavy nuclei are evaluated using "Fusion by Diffusion" (FBD) model. Predictive power of this approach is shown for experimentally known Lv, Og isotopes and predictions given for Z=119,120. Ground state and saddle point properties as: masses, shell corrections, pairing energies and deformations necessary for cross section estimations are calculated systematically within the multidimensional microscopic - macroscopic method based on the deformed Woods-Saxon single particle potential. In the frame of FBD approach predictions for production of elements heavier than Z = 118 are not too optimistic. For this reason, and because of high instability of superheavy nuclei, we comment on some structure effects, connected with the K-isomerism phenomenon which could lead to a significant increase in the stability of these systems.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while the "Fusion by Diffusion" (FBD) model can reproduce known cross sections for superheavy elements up to Z=118, predictions for elements Z=119 and 120 are "not too optimistic," with cross sections at the femtobarn level; however, the authors note that K-isomerism could significantly increase the stability of some superheavy nuclei, potentially enabling their study.

## Method

The authors used the "Fusion by Diffusion" (FBD) model, extended to include angular momentum dependence, to calculate evaporation residue cross sections for superheavy nuclei synthesis. Ground state and saddle point properties (masses, shell corrections, pairing energies, deformations) were calculated systematically using a multidimensional microscopic-macroscopic method based on the deformed Woods-Saxon single-particle potential with Strutinski shell and pairing corrections and the Yukawa-plus-exponential macroscopic model. The injection-point distance parameter \(s_{inj}\) was fitted to experimental cross sections at the maxima of excitation functions, and the resulting systematics were compared with those obtained using the fission-barrier heights and ground-state masses of P. Möller et al.

## Key numbers

- The largest ground state shell effect of approximately 9 MeV is observed for the semi-magic nucleus \(^{270}\)Hs (Z=108, N=162), with a second minimum of approximately 7 MeV around Z=114 and N=184.
- The root mean square deviation between calculated and experimental/empirical fission barriers is 0.7 MeV for the second fission barriers in actinides and 0.5 MeV for the first fission barriers.
- For the synthesis of Z=120 via the reaction \(^{249}\)Cf(\(^{50}\)Ti,xn)\(^{299-x}\)120, the largest cross section is about 6 fb, expected for the 3n and 4n evaporation channels.
- For the synthesis of Z=119 via the reaction \(^{252}\)Es(\(^{48}\)Ca,xn)\(^{300-x}\)119, the cross section is about 0.2 pb in the 4n channel at \(E_{c.m.} \simeq 220\) MeV.
- For the nucleus \(^{272}\)Mt, the hindrance from K-isomerism could reach six orders of magnitude, extending the lifetime from milliseconds to hours.
- Fission barriers become lower than 5.5 MeV for Z=126 according to the authors' calculations.
- Two unsuccessful attempts to produce \(^{302}\)120 at GSI provided cross-section limits of 560 fb and 90 fb, and a Dubna experiment provided a limit of 400 fb.

## Context

This work extends the FBD model of Swiatecki (1982) and the earlier work of Cap et al. to include angular momentum dependence, and it applies the microscopic-macroscopic Woods-Saxon model of Kowal et al. (2010) to calculate the necessary nuclear structure inputs. The paper contrasts its results with those from the self-consistent mean-field studies of Skalski (2015), which predict fission barriers of order 10 MeV for Z=120 and thus more optimistic production prospects, and with the fission-barrier calculations of Möller et al. (2009, 2015), which the authors find inconsistent with the expected energy dependence of the injection-point distance. The predictions for Z=119 and 120 enable experimental planning for the next generation of superheavy element searches, while the discussion of K-isomerism opens a possible pathway to studying long-lived superheavy configurations that could be analyzed chemically.
