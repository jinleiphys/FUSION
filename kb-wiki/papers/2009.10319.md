# Fully-heavy tetraquarks in strongly interacting medium

> We study the properties of fully-heavy tetraquarks at finite temperature and their production in high-energy nuclear collisions. We obtain the masses and wave functions of the exotic hadron states $cc\bar c\bar c$ and $bb\bar b\bar b$ by solving the four-body Schrödinger equation in vacuum and strongly interacting matter. In vacuum, the tetraquarks are above the corresponding meson-meson mass threshold, and the newly observed exotic state $X(6900)$ might be a $cc\bar c\bar c$ state with quantum number $J^{PC}=0^{++}$ or $1^{+-}$. In hot medium, the temperature dependence of the tetraquark masses and the dissociation temperatures are calculated. Taking the wave function at finite temperature, we construct the Wigner function for the tetraquark states and calculate, with coalescence mechanism, the production yield and transverse momentum distribution of $cc\bar c\bar c$ in heavy-ion collisions at LHC energy. In comparison with nucleon-nucleon collisions, the yield per binary collision is significantly enhanced.

## Key claim

The newly observed exotic state X(6900) might be a fully-charmed tetraquark state with quantum numbers J^PC = 0^{++} or 1^{+-}, but the authors hedge that the current experiment measures only the mass and width and cannot determine the spin and parities; the theoretical result indicates X(6900) is likely the first radial excited state 2S of cc\bar c\bar c with mass around 6908 MeV (0^{++}) or 6896 MeV (1^{+-}).

## Method

The authors solved the four-body Schrodinger equation for cc\bar c\bar c and bb\bar b\bar b systems using a potential model with Cornell-type central potential and a lattice-derived spin-spin interaction, expanding the relative wave function in hyperspherical harmonic functions with hyperangular quantum number K up to 3 (seven coupled differential equations). For finite temperature, they replaced the central potential with the lattice QCD free energy F(r,T) and solved the same coupled radial equations to obtain temperature-dependent binding energies and wave functions. The production yield in heavy-ion collisions was calculated via the coalescence mechanism using the Wigner function constructed from the finite-temperature wave function.

## Key numbers

- The cc\bar c\bar c 1S ground state masses are 6.346 GeV and 6.476 GeV for the two 0^{++} states, 6.441 GeV for 1^{+-}, and 6.475 GeV for 2^{++}
- The bb\bar b\bar b 1S ground state masses are 19.154 GeV and 19.226 GeV for the two 0^{++} states, 19.214 GeV for 1^{+-}, and 19.232 GeV for 2^{++}
- The root-mean-squared radius of cc\bar c\bar c 1S states ranges from 0.323 fm to 0.351 fm depending on the state
- The root-mean-squared radius of bb\bar b\bar b 1S states ranges from 0.180 fm to 0.186 fm
- The X(6900) candidate mass of approximately 6.9 GeV matches the calculated 2S state of cc\bar c\bar c at 6.908 GeV (0^{++}) or 6.896 GeV (1^{+-})
- The critical temperature of the deconfinement transition is taken as T_c = 165 MeV from lattice QCD
- The charm quark mass is 1.29 GeV and the bottom quark mass is 4.7 GeV in the potential model

## Context

This work extends previous potential model studies of fully-heavy tetraquarks by groups such as Debastiani (2017), Wang (2019), Yang (2020), Chen (2020), and Liu (2019), who used different potentials and numerical methods like variational Gaussian expansion. The authors note that their inverse power method can handle both ground and excited states with high precision. The finite-temperature treatment builds on earlier work by Satz (2005), Zhao (2017), and Shi (2019) who applied potential models to heavy quarkonia and heavy-flavor baryons in hot medium. The coalescence production mechanism follows the framework of Fries (2003), Molnar (2003), Lin (2002), Hwa (2002), and Oh (2009), but unlike those works which assumed Gaussian Wigner functions with adjustable parameters, this work derives the coalescence probability directly from the Schrodinger equation wave function. The study enables predictions for fully-heavy tetraquark production in heavy-ion collisions at LHC energy, where the yield per binary collision is expected to be significantly enhanced compared to nucleon-nucleon collisions.
