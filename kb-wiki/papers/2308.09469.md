# An updated mass-radius analysis of the 2017-2018 NICER data set of PSR J0030+0451

> In 2019 the NICER collaboration published the first mass and radius inferred for PSR J0030+0451, thanks to NICER observations, and consequent constraints on the equation of state characterising dense matter. Two independent analyses found a mass of $\sim 1.3-1.4\,\mathrm{M_\odot}$ and a radius of $\sim 13\,$km. They also both found that the hot spots were all located on the same hemisphere, opposite to the observer, and that at least one of them had a significantly elongated shape. Here we reanalyse, in greater detail, the same NICER data set, incorporating the effects of an updated NICER response matrix and using an upgraded analysis framework. We expand the adopted models and jointly analyse also XMM-Newton data, which enables us to better constrain the fraction of observed counts coming from PSR J0030+0451. Adopting the same models used in previous publications, we find consistent results, although with more stringent inference requirements. We also find a multi-modal structure in the posterior surface. This becomes crucial when XMM-Newton data is accounted for. Including the corresponding constraints disfavors the main solutions found previously, in favor of the new and more complex models. These have inferred masses and radii of $\sim [1.4 \mathrm{M_\odot}, 11.5$ km] and $\sim [1.7 \mathrm{M_\odot}, 14.5$ km], depending on the assumed model. They display configurations that do not require the two hot spots generating the observed X-rays to be on the same hemisphere, nor to show very elongated features, and point instead to the presence of temperature gradients and the need to account for them.

## Key claim

The reanalysis of the 2017-2018 NICER data set for PSR J0030+0451, incorporating an updated NICER response matrix and joint analysis with XMM-Newton data, finds that the previously preferred hot spot configurations (all spots on the same hemisphere with elongated shapes) are disfavored; instead, the new results point to temperature gradients and yield mass-radius solutions of approximately [1.4 solar masses, 11.5 km] or [1.7 solar masses, 14.5 km] depending on the assumed model, though the authors caution that the joint inference posteriors are not yet proven robust due to computational limitations.

## Method

The authors used the X-PSI package to perform pulse profile modeling of NICER data (folded into 32 phase bins across 300 PI channels) and XMM-Newton data, employing relativistic ray-tracing under the Oblate Schwarzschild plus Doppler approximation with a non-magnetic hydrogen atmosphere model. They tested four increasingly complex hot spot models (ST-U, ST+PST, ST+PDT, PDT-U) using nested sampling via MultiNest, and for the first time in NICER analyses included emission from the remaining neutron star surface and jointly fitted both instruments to better constrain the source count fraction.

## Key numbers

* PSR J0030+0451 rotates at approximately 205 Hz and is located at a distance of approximately 325 pc.
* The original 2019 analysis (Riley 2019) found mass = 1.34 (+0.15, -0.16) solar masses and radius = 12.71 (+1.14, -1.19) km for the preferred ST+PST model.
* The independent 2019 analysis (Miller 2019) found mass = 1.44 (+0.15, -0.14) solar masses and radius = 13.02 (+1.24, -1.06) km.
* The new joint NICER+XMM analyses yield mass-radius solutions of approximately [1.4 solar masses, 11.5 km] and [1.7 solar masses, 14.5 km], depending on the assumed model.
* The NICER data were analyzed using instrumental PI channels in the range [30, 300) and binned into 32 rotational phase bins.
* The mass prior was flat in the range (1.0, 3.0) solar masses, with radius constrained to be less than 16 km.

## Context

This work extends the original NICER analysis of PSR J0030+0451 by Riley (2019) and Miller (2019), which first inferred mass and radius from this pulsar and found hot spots all on the same hemisphere with elongated shapes. The updated analysis incorporates lessons from companion simulation studies (Vinciguerra 2023a) that revealed multi-modal posterior structures and sensitivity to noise and analysis settings. The new results, which favor temperature gradients over extreme spot shapes, will serve as a baseline for interpreting upcoming NICER observations of PSR J0030+0451 and for further constraining the dense matter equation of state, particularly in combination with results from the high-mass pulsar PSR J0740+6620 (Riley 2021, Miller 2021, Salmi 2022).
