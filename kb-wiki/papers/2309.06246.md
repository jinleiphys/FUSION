# Assumption Breakdown in Radiative Energy Loss

> We show that an integral assumption in DGLV radiative energy loss - the large formation time assumption - is violated at high-$p_T$ for phenomenologically relevant parameters. We further investigate the phenomenological impact of placing a new kinematic bound on the radiated gluon transverse momentum, which ensures that there are no contributions to the energy loss from regions of parameter space that violate the large formation time assumption. We find that this places a large sensitivity on the exact kinematic cutoff used, similar to the known collinear cutoff sensitivity, indicating the theoretical need for a rederivation of DGLV radiative energy with the large formation time assumption relaxed in order to make rigorous predictions. We additionally find that this large formation time cutoff dramatically reduces the size of a short pathlength correction to the DGLV radiative energy loss, which is of phenomenological interest in predicting suppression in small $p +A$ systems. We compute the phenomenological predictions utilizing this large formation time cutoff in both $p+A$ and $A+A$ collisions at the LHC, in a convolved radiative and elastic energy loss model.

# Assumption Breakdown in Radiative Energy Loss

## Key claim
The large formation time assumption, an integral approximation in the DGLV radiative energy loss model, is violated at high transverse momentum for phenomenologically relevant parameters; imposing a kinematic cutoff that enforces this assumption dramatically reduces the short pathlength correction and introduces a large sensitivity to the exact cutoff value, comparable to the known collinear cutoff sensitivity.

## Method
The authors evaluate the DGLV radiative energy loss kernel (first order in opacity L/λ) with and without the short pathlength correction derived by Kolbe (2015), imposing a new upper bound on the radiated gluon transverse momentum k-integral: |k|_max = Min(√(2xEμ₁), 2x(1-x)E). This bound enforces the first large formation time condition (k²/2xE ≪ μ₁) alongside the standard collinear condition. They compute fractional energy loss ΔE/E and the nuclear modification factors R_AA and R_pA for pions in central Pb+Pb and p+Pb collisions at LHC energies, using a convolved radiative and elastic energy loss model based on the Wicks-Horowitz-Djordjevic-Gyulassy (WHDG) framework.

## Key numbers
- The large formation time cutoff scales as ~E^(1/2) while the collinear cutoff scales as ~E^(1), causing the formation time cutoff to become the limiting bound at high energies.
- For the uncorrected DGLV radiative energy loss, imposing the large formation time cutoff produces a small effect (~10% change in ΔE/E).
- The short pathlength correction to DGLV, which previously gave a ~100% negative correction at p_T ~ O(100) GeV, is dramatically reduced when the large formation time cutoff is enforced.
- Sensitivity bands are computed by varying the kinematic cutoff by factors of two, with calculations using μ = 0.5 GeV, λ = 1 fm, and L = 5 fm.
- Under a naive approximation, sensitivity at the ΔE/E level is enhanced by a factor of (n-1) ~ 5 at the R_AA level, where n ~ 6 is the power-law index for gluon production spectra at the LHC.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous studies (Faraday 2023) on removing the large pathlength assumption (L ≫ μ⁻¹) in DGLV, which introduced O(e^{-μL}) corrections. It identifies a separate assumption, the large formation time assumption used in GLV (Gyulassy 2000), DGLV (Djordjevic 2003), and the short pathlength correction (Kolbe 2015), that also breaks down at high p_T. The collinear cutoff sensitivity was previously studied by Horowitz (2009). The results indicate that both the original DGLV and its short pathlength correction require rederivation with the large formation time assumption relaxed before rigorous quantitative predictions can be made for either A+A or p+A collisions. Future work could address small-system corrections to thermodynamics (Mogliacci 2018), the equation of state (Horowitz 2021), and effective coupling (Horowitz 2022).
