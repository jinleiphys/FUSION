# Foundation of Hydrodynamics for Systems with Strong Interactions

> For a dense and strongly interacting system, such as a nucleus or a strongly-coupled quark-gluon plasma, the foundation of hydrodynamics can be better found in the quantum description of constituents moving in the strong mean fields generated by all other particles. Using the result that the Schroedinger equation and the Klein-Gordon equation can be written in hydrodynamical forms, we find that the probability currents of the many-body system in the mean-field description obey a hydrodynamical equation with stress tensors arising from many contributions: quantum effects, mean-field interactions, and thermal fluctuations. The influence of various contributions to the hydrodynamical motion is expected to vary with the temperature, as the quantum and mean-field stress tensors playing more important roles at low and moderate temperatures.

## Key claim

For dense, strongly interacting systems such as nuclei or strongly-coupled quark-gluon plasmas, hydrodynamics can be founded on a quantum mean-field description rather than kinetic theory, with the total stress tensor receiving contributions from quantum effects, mean-field interactions, and thermal fluctuations; the quantum and mean-field stress tensors dominate at low and moderate temperatures, potentially allowing quasi-hydrodynamical behavior even before thermal equilibrium is reached.

## Method

The author starts from the single-particle Schroedinger equation and Klein-Gordon equation, writing each in hydrodynamical form by decomposing the wave function into amplitude and phase. For the non-relativistic case, the many-body system in the time-dependent mean-field description yields an Euler equation for the probability current with separate quantum, thermal, and mean-field stress tensors. For the relativistic case, the Klein-Gordon equation is rewritten as coupled Schroedinger-type equations for particle and antiparticle components following the Feshbach-Villars formalism, then cast into a hydrodynamical equation for the probability currents under the assumption of suppressed pair production; summing over occupied single-particle states in the mean-field description gives the final relativistic hydrodynamical equation.

## Key numbers

- For the Skyrme I nucleon-nucleon interaction: two-body interaction strength \(t_0 = -1057\) MeV/fm\(^3\) and three-body interaction strength \(t_3 = +14463\) MeV/fm\(^6\)
- For a nucleus with Skyrme interaction, the quantum stress tensor is given as \(p_{ij}^{(q)} = \frac{\hbar^2}{5 m} \left( \frac{6 \pi^2}{4} \right)^{2/3} n^{5/3} \delta_{ij}\)
- For the same system, the mean-field stress tensor is \(p_{ij}^{(v)} = \frac{3}{8} ( t_0 + \frac{1}{3} t_3 n ) n^2 \delta_{ij}\)
- For a thermally equilibrated system, the thermal stress tensor is \(p_{ij}^{(t)} = \frac{\hbar^2}{5 m} \left( \frac{6 \pi^2}{4} \right)^{2/3} n^{5/3} \left[ \frac{2mkT}{\hbar^2 m (6 \pi^2 n/4)^{2/3}} \right]^2 \delta_{ij}\)

## Context

This work extends the author's own earlier non-relativistic mean-field hydrodynamics for nuclei (Wong 1976, 1977) to the relativistic domain using the Klein-Gordon equation, building on the Feshbach-Villars (1958) separation of particle and antiparticle degrees of freedom. It provides a theoretical foundation for the liquid-drop model of Bohr and Wheeler (1938) and the dynamical fission model of Hill and Wheeler (1953), and connects to the relativistic hydrodynamics pioneered by Landau (1953) and applied to quark-gluon plasma by Bjorken (1983), Baym et al. (1983), and Ollitrault (1992, 2008). The framework enables investigation of whether the fast onset of hydrodynamical motion suggested by elliptic flow measurements (Ollitrault 2008) may arise from the dominance of mean-field and quantum stress tensors in a strongly-coupled quark-gluon plasma just above the transition temperature \(T_c\).
