# Real time description of fission

> Using the time-dependent superfluid local density approximation, the dynamics of fission is investigated in real time from just beyond the saddle to fully separated fragments. Simulations produced in this fully microscopic framework can help to assess the validity of the current approaches to fission, and to obtain estimate of fission observables. In this contribution, we concentrate on general aspects of fission dynamics.

## Key claim

The time-dependent superfluid local density approximation (TDSLDA) can describe fission dynamics in real time from just beyond the saddle point to fully separated fragments, but the results are sensitive to the choice of nuclear energy density functional (NEDF) and the initial state must be placed beyond the fission barrier because starting from the ground state would require prohibitively long simulation times.

## Method

The authors solved the TDSLDA equations, which are formally equivalent to time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (TDHFB) theory, on large 3D spatial lattices using Skyrme-type functionals (SLy4, SKM*) as input. They initialized the system at various points on the potential energy surface beyond the fission barrier and evolved the quasiparticle wavefunctions in real time using GPU-accelerated codes on supercomputers, tracking the density profile to observe fragment separation and computing observables after dividing the simulation box at the separation line.

## Key numbers

- For the SKM* NEDF, the average excitation energy of initial states was 8.3 MeV with a standard deviation of 3 MeV, not including the symmetric trajectory.
- Asymmetric fission trajectories produced fragments with average neutron number ⟨N_l⟩ = 61.8(9), average proton number ⟨Z_l⟩ = 40.9(5), average total kinetic energy ⟨TKE⟩ = 174.5(2.5) MeV, and average total excitation energy ⟨TXE⟩ = 31.5(3.8) MeV.
- The symmetric trajectory produced a much lower TKE of 149 MeV.
- A single trajectory from saddle to scission (10^3 to 10^4 fm/c) can be finished within 12 hours using 1,000 GPUs with a time step Δt = 0.03 fm/c.
- The collective flow energy remained small (around 1 MeV) and almost constant until scission, in contrast to the 15 to 20 MeV expected from adiabatic approaches.
- The average number of neutrons emitted from scission to full acceleration can reach more than 0.4, almost independent of the trajectory.

## Context

This work extends the TDSLDA approach previously applied to fission of 240Pu by Bulgac et al. (2015, 2018) and contrasts with time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations by Negele (1978) and others (Tanimura 2015, Hashimoto 2016, Goddard 2015, 2016) which required unrealistic pairing gaps or high energies to induce fission. The finding of overdamped collective motion supports the hypothesis of Randrup et al. (2011) and contradicts the TDHF+BCS results of Tanimura et al. (2017) which found distributions comparable to experiment from a narrow ensemble of initial conditions. The framework enables future studies of excitation energy sharing between fragments, fragment spin distributions, and inclusion of fluctuations and dissipation as formulated by Bulgac et al. (2018).
