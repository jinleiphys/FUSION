# Uncertainty quantification in nuclear shell model

> The uncertainty quantifications of theoretical results are of great importance to make meaningful comparisons of those results with experimental data and to make predictions in experimentally unknown regions. By quantifying uncertainties, one can make more solid statements about, e.g., origins of discrepancy in some quantities between theory and experiment. We propose a novel method for uncertainty quantification for the effective interactions of nuclear shell-model calculations as an example. The effective interaction is specified by a set of parameters, and its probability distribution in the multi-dimensional parameter space is considered. This enables us to quantify the agreement with experimental data in a statistical manner and the resulting confidence intervals show unexpectedly large variations. Moreover, we point out that a large deviation of the confidence interval for the energy in shell-model calculations from the corresponding experimental data can be used as an indicator of some exotic property, e.g. alpha clustering, etc. Other possible applications and impacts are also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a novel method for uncertainty quantification in nuclear shell-model calculations by treating the effective interaction parameters as probability distributions rather than fixed point estimates, and claims that large deviations between the resulting confidence intervals and experimental data can serve as an indicator of exotic nuclear structures such as alpha clustering, intruder configurations, or core excitations.

## Method

The authors apply Bayesian inference with a uniform prior and Laplace approximation to the 17 parameters (2 single-particle energies and 15 two-body matrix elements) of the 0p-shell effective interaction, using 33 experimental energy values from p-shell nuclei as the data set. They generate 50,000 samples from the resulting multivariate Gaussian posterior distribution, perform exact diagonalization for each sample, and construct violin plots showing the distribution of predicted energy eigenvalues for 82 low-lying states.

## Key numbers

- The root mean square error for the 33 data points with the optimal interaction (MAP estimate) is 0.35 MeV, compared to 0.57 MeV (CKpot), 0.47 MeV (CKtb1), and 0.54 MeV (CKtb2) for the Cohen-Kurath interactions.
- The typical error in the mean values of energy eigenvalues from 50,000 Laplace approximation samples is less than 0.1%.
- Adding the Hoyle state to the fitting data set worsens the total root mean square error by approximately 39% for the 74 states that were reproduced within 2-sigma error by the original LA samples.
- The typical discrepancy between the MAP estimate and the mean value of LA samples across all 82 states is about 0.1%.

## Context

This work extends Bayesian uncertainty quantification methods previously applied to mean-field calculations (Dobaczewski 2014) and nuclear potentials to the nuclear shell model for the first time. The approach enables statistical comparison with experimental data and identification of states requiring model spaces beyond the 0p-shell, such as the Hoyle state in carbon-12 and certain states in carbon-14, beryllium-10, beryllium-12, boron-10, lithium-7, and lithium-6. The methodology can be extended to valence-space Hamiltonians based on chiral effective field theory potentials and to include electromagnetic observables or Gamow-Teller transition strengths for astrophysical applications.
