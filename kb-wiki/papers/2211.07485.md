# Is the compact object associated with HESS J1731-347 a strange quark star?

> The analysis of the central compact object within the supernova remnant HESS J1731-347 suggests that it has a small radius and, even more interestingly, a mass of the order or smaller than one solar mass. This raises the question of which astrophysical process could lead to such a small mass, since the analysis of various types of SN explosions indicate that is it not possible to produce a neutron star with a mass smaller than about $1.17 M_\odot$. Here we show that masses of the order or smaller than one solar mass can be obtained in the case of strange quark stars and that it is possible to build a coherent model explaining not only the mass and the radius of that object, but also its slow cooling suggested in various analyses. We also show that an astrophysical path exists which leads to the formation of such an object, and we discuss the role played in that scenario by strangelets assumed to constitute the dark matter.

# Is the compact object associated with HESS J1731-347 a strange quark star?

## Key claim

The central compact object in HESS J1731-347, with a mass of order or smaller than one solar mass, can be explained as a strange quark star rather than a neutron star, provided that strangelets (assumed to constitute dark matter) are captured by the progenitor's dense core and trigger the conversion of the proto-neutron star into a quark star. The authors do not claim that all compact objects are quark stars; rather, they argue for a "two-families scenario" in which neutron stars and quark stars coexist, with the conversion occurring preferentially in the galactic center region where dark matter is more abundant.

## Method

The authors compare the binding energies of neutron stars and strange quark stars for a given baryonic mass, using the empirical relation BE/M_sun = 0.084 (M_g/M_sun)^2 for neutron stars and three different equations of state for quark stars (from Bombaci 2020, Ferrer 2015, and a Bayesian analysis by Traversi 2021). They then compute the capture rate of strangelets (hypothetical nuggets of strange quark matter) onto the dense silicon core of progenitor stars in the mass range (8-10) M_sun, using a modified version of the capture rate formula from Jacobs (2014) and a Navarro-Frank-White dark matter density profile. The mass distribution of cosmological strangelets is estimated from a coalescence and evaporation model following Witten (1984) and Madsen (1988).

## Key numbers

- The minimum gravitational mass for a neutron star is 1.17-1.20 M_sun (corresponding to baryonic masses 1.28-1.32 M_sun), while for a strange quark star with the same baryonic mass the gravitational mass can be 0.95-1.08 M_sun depending on the equation of state.
- The surface temperature of the central object in HESS J1731-347 is T_s = 153 (+4, -2) eV, with an age in the range (2-6) kyr.
- The capture rate of strangelets onto the dense core of a progenitor star is approximately F_c ~ 6 x 10^35 yr^(-1) A^(-1) ~ 10^12 yr^(-1) m_s^(-1), where m_s is the strangelet mass in grams, corresponding to about one capture per year for strangelets of ~10^12 g.
- The dark matter density in the galactic halo is taken as rho_DM ~ 10^(-24) g/cm^3, with a velocity of 250 km/s.
- The allowed mass range for strangelets constituting most of dark matter is 10^10 g less than or equal to m less than or equal to 10^18 g, based on observational limits from Singh Sidhu (2019).
- The distance from the Sun to HESS J1731-347 is approximately 2.5 kpc, located in the direction of the galactic center where the dark matter density can be up to a factor of ~5 higher than at the Sun's position.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Doroshenko (2022), who reported the small mass and radius of the central compact object in HESS J1731-347, and addresses the claim by Suwa (2018) that neutron stars cannot form with masses below about 1.17 M_sun. The authors build on their previous work on the two-families scenario (Berezhiani 2002, Drago 2014) and on the mechanism for producing subsolar-mass compact objects via accretion-induced collapse of white dwarfs containing strange quark matter (Di Clemente 2022). The paper also connects to the broader search for strangelets as dark matter candidates (Witten 1984, Madsen 1988, Burdin 2014) and to observations of superluminous supernovae, which the authors suggest may be associated with quark star formation in dark-matter-rich dwarf galaxies.
