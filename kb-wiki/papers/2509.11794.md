# Nuclear transitions on demand

> I show a way to tune photo-nuclear cross section effectively and therefore achieve nuclear transitions "on demand". The method is based on combinatorial enhancement of multiphoton processes under intense conditions. Taking advantage of recent advances in high-power laser systems (HPLS) and nuclear structure calculations, efficient control of nuclear transitions up to E4 in multipolarity can be reached today. The same idea can be extended to the search for rare transitions and hidden states, which applies to the $γ$-beams generated from conventional sources as well.

## Key claim

The paper claims that multiphoton absorption (nPA) processes, exploiting combinatorial enhancement under intense photon fluxes from modern high-power laser systems (HPLS), can tune effective photo-nuclear cross sections to achieve nuclear transitions "on demand" up to E4 multipolarity, but this is conditional on the availability of sufficiently intense laser-driven gamma flashes and optical photon sources.

## Method

The author derives the yield for n-photon absorption (nPA) processes, where the yield scales as the nth power of photon intensity due to combinatorial enhancement when the number of photons per mode is large. For inhomogeneous two-photon absorption, the effective cross section felt by gamma-ray photons is expressed as sigma_eff^(2PA) = I_2 * sigma_if^(2PA), where I_2 is the intensity of low-energy (eV-level) optical photons from a high-power laser. The method is demonstrated by comparing the yield of two-photon absorption against conventional stepwise pumping (1PA+1PA) through an intermediate nuclear state, using parameters from laser-driven gamma flashes.

## Key numbers

- The typical electromagnetic cross section for atoms/molecules is of order Mb, while nuclear cross sections are suppressed by a factor of 10^9 to 10^12 relative to photonics at nanoscales.
- For an originally E2 transition, the effective two-photon absorption cross section sigma_eff^(2PA) was estimated in 1979 to reach 10^-26 cm^2 (10 mb) when the optical photon energy flux P_2 = 10^10 W/cm^2.
- Modern HPLS can reach optical photon energy flux P_2 up to 10^23 W/cm^2, with pulse compression to approximately 5 micrometers and 20 femtoseconds.
- Laser-driven gamma flashes provide up to P_2 approximately 10^12 W/cm^2 per eV interval for gamma energies between 1 keV and 1 MeV.
- With state-of-the-art HPLS, effective cross sections sigma_eff approximately 10^-25 cm^2 are feasible for nuclear transitions covering multipolarity gaps up to E4.
- In the demonstration comparing 2PA to stepwise pumping, assuming 10^6 gamma photons per eV interval within a 10x10 micrometer area per shot (duration approximately 50 fs), and a typical intermediate state half-life of 1 ps (width approximately 10^-4 eV), the ratio Y_(1PA+1PA)/Y_(2PA) is approximately 10^-3, leading to a net enhancement of 2PA over stepwise pumping by approximately 10^5 when all photon energy combinations are considered.

## Context

This work extends the concept of laser-assisted nuclear excitation originally introduced by Refs. PhysRevLett.42.1397 and PhysRevC.20.1942 (1979), which proposed enhancing effective cross sections by supplying gamma photons with optical photons but was limited to E2 multipolarity and required precise energy matching. The paper shows that modern HPLS, such as those described by Yoon (2019, 2021), Li (2018), Yu (2018), ur (2015), Tanaka (2020), and Cernaianu (2025), overcome these limitations by providing much higher photon intensities and continuous gamma spectra, enabling transitions up to E4 and opening possibilities for discovering hidden nuclear states and realizing nuclear gamma-ray lasers (graser).
