# Non-Equilibrium Aspects of Relic Neutrinos: From Freeze-out to the Present Day

> In this dissertation, we study the evolution and properties of the relic (or cosmic) neutrino distribution from neutrino freeze-out at $T=O(1)$ MeV through the free-streaming era up to today, focusing on the deviation of the neutrino spectrum from equilibrium. In particular, we demonstrate the presence of chemical non-equilibrium that continues to the present day. The work naturally separates into two parts. The first focuses on aspects of the relic neutrinos that can be explored using conservation laws. The second part studies the neutrino distribution using the full general relativistic Boltzmann equation. Part one begins with an overview of the history of the Universe, from just prior to neutrino freeze-out up through the present day, placing the history of cosmic neutrino evolution in its proper context. Motivated by the Planck CMB measurements of the effective number of neutrinos, we derive those properties of neutrino freeze-out that depend only on conservation laws and are independent of the details of the scattering processes. Part one ends with a characterization of the present day neutrino spectrum as seen from Earth. The second part of this dissertation focuses on the properties of cosmic neutrinos that depend on the details of the neutrino reactions, as is necessary for modeling the non-thermal distortions from equilibrium and computing freeze-out temperatures. We detail a new spectral method for solving the Boltzmann equation, based on a dynamical basis of orthogonal polynomials, as well as an improved procedure for analytically simplifying the corresponding scattering integrals for subsequent numerical computation. We apply these novel solution methods to solve the Boltzmann equation through the neutrino freeze-out period and perform parametric studies of the dependence of neutrino freeze-out on standard model parameters.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that relic neutrinos remain in chemical non-equilibrium (characterized by a fugacity \(\Upsilon < 1\)) from freeze-out through to the present day, and that this deviation from equilibrium can explain the Planck CMB measurements of \(N_\nu > 3\) without invoking undiscovered particle species, provided the kinetic freeze-out temperature \(T_k\) is treated as a free parameter that allows additional reheating from \(e^\pm\) annihilation into neutrinos.

## Method

The work uses conservation laws (energy, entropy, and particle number) in an FRW Universe under the assumption of instantaneous chemical and kinetic equilibrium with sharp freeze-out transitions, deriving the neutrino fugacity \(\Upsilon\), photon-to-neutrino temperature ratio \(T_\gamma/T_\nu\), and effective number of neutrinos \(N_\nu\) as functions of the kinetic freeze-out temperature \(T_k\). The free-streaming neutrino distribution after kinetic freeze-out is then transformed to the rest frame of an observer moving relative to the neutrino background (e.g., Earth at \(v_\oplus = 300\) km/s relative to the CMB) to compute velocity, energy, and de Broglie wavelength distributions, as well as the \(\mathcal{O}(G_F^2)\) mechanical drag force on a moving target via potential scattering in the Born approximation and hard-sphere scattering limits.

## Key numbers

- The decoupled-limit reheating ratio \(R_\nu^{-1} = (11/4)^{1/3} \approx 1.4010\).
- The approximate power-law exponent relating \(T_\gamma/T_\nu\) to \(\Upsilon\) is \(b \approx 0.367\), with a quadratic correction coefficient \(c \approx -0.0209\).
- Planck CMB results give \(N_\nu\) in the range \(3.30 \pm 0.27\) to \(3.62 \pm 0.25\) (68% confidence level), compared to the standard theoretical value \(N_\nu^{\rm th} = 3.046\) from Mangano (2005).
- The present-day neutrino number density per flavor is \(n_\nu = [-0.3517\,\delta N_\nu^2 + 6.717\,\delta N_\nu + 56.06]\,{\rm cm}^{-3}\) where \(\delta N_\nu \equiv N_\nu - 3\).
- For normal matter density, the long-wavelength drag force is \(F_L = 2 \times 10^{-31}\,{\rm N}\,(m_\nu/0.1\,{\rm eV})^2 (V_0/1\,{\rm peV})^2 (R/1\,{\rm mm})^6 (v_{\rm rel}/v_\oplus)\).
- For nuclear matter density hard-sphere scattering, the long-wavelength drag force is \(F_L \simeq 4 \times 10^{-20}\,{\rm N}\,(R/1\,{\rm mm})^2 (v_{\rm rel}/v_\oplus)\), independent of neutrino mass.
- The Newtonian drag time constant for nuclear-density objects is \(\tau = 10^{26}\,{\rm yr}\,(R/1\,{\rm mm})\,(\rho/\rho_N)\).

## Context

This work extends the model-independent approach of Birrell, Yang, Chen, and Rafelski (2013, 2014) that used conservation laws to characterize neutrino freeze-out, and contrasts with the standard Boltzmann-equation computation by Mangano (2005) that yields \(N_\nu = 3.046\). The results provide a physically consistent parameterization of the neutrino equation of state including both \(N_\nu > 3\) and neutrino mass, which prior studies had difficulty incorporating simultaneously. The characterization of the present-day neutrino spectrum and drag force is directly relevant to the proposed PTOLEMY experiment for relic neutrino detection and to studies of compact objects such as strangelets (CUDOS).
