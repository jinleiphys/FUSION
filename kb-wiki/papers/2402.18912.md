# Hadron-quark phase transition in the neutron star with vector MIT bag model and Korea-IBS-Daegu-SKKU functional

> Employing the Korea-IBS-Daegu-SKKU (KIDS) density functional for the hadron phase and the MIT bag model with vector (vBag) model for the quark phase, we obtain hadron-quark phase transition in neutron stars considering Maxwell construction. The structural properties of the resultant hybrid stars are computed for three different values of bag constant ($B$) in the range $B^{1/4}=$(145$-$160 MeV). We study the effects of symmetry energy ($J$) on the hybrid star properties with the different KIDS model and found that $J$ has important influence not only on the transition properties like the transition mass, transition radius and jump in density due to phase transition, but also on the stability of the hybrid stars. The vector repulsion of the quark phase via the parameter $G_V$ has profound influence in obtaining reasonable hybrid star configurations, consistent with the recent astrophysical constraints on the structural properties of compact stars. Within the aforesaid range of $B$, the value of $G_V$ is constrained to be 0.3 $\lesssim G_V \lesssim$ 0.4 in order to obtain reasonable hybrid star configurations.

# Hadron-quark phase transition in the neutron star with vector MIT bag model and Korea-IBS-Daegu-SKKU functional

## Key claim

The vector repulsion parameter G_V in the vBag quark model is constrained to 0.3 ≲ G_V ≲ 0.4 for hybrid star configurations to satisfy current astrophysical constraints, but this range depends on the bag constant (B^{1/4} = 145–160 MeV) and the symmetry energy (J = 30–33 MeV); G_V = 0.25 is only allowed when B^{1/4} > 155 MeV, and G_V = 0.4 can produce unstable hybrid stars for high symmetry energy values.

## Method

The authors use the KIDS density functional for the hadronic phase and the MIT bag model with vector repulsion (vBag) for the quark phase, implementing a Maxwell construction for the hadron-quark phase transition. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain mass, radius, and tidal deformability of hybrid stars, considering three values of the bag constant (B^{1/4} = 145, 155, 160 MeV) and four KIDS models (A, B, C, D) with different symmetry energy parameters. The vector coupling G_V is varied between 0.25 and 0.4 to test consistency with observational constraints from PSR J0740+6620, GW170817, NICER, and HESS J1731-347.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass for pure hadronic stars: 2.10 M⊙ for KIDS-A (J=33 MeV, L=66 MeV, K0=230 MeV) and 2.26 M⊙ for KIDS-D (J=30 MeV, L=47 MeV, K0=260 MeV)
- Radius of canonical neutron star (R1.4): 12.28 km for KIDS-A and 12.10 km for KIDS-D
- Transition mass for KIDS-A with B^{1/4}=155 MeV and G_V=0.35: Mt=0.422 M⊙; with B^{1/4}=145 MeV and same G_V: Mt=0.389 M⊙
- Transition radius for KIDS-A with B^{1/4}=155 MeV and G_V=0.35: Rt=13.081 km; with B^{1/4}=145 MeV: Rt=13.255 km
- Tidal deformability Λ1.4: approximately 260 with G_V=0.25 and approximately 380 with G_V=0.35 (for B^{1/4}=155 MeV)
- Transition densities for KIDS-A with B^{1/4}=155 MeV and G_V=0.3: ρt^H/ρ0=1.093, ρt^Q/ρ0=2.218; maximum mass Mmax=2.011 M⊙
- Transition densities for KIDS-D with B^{1/4}=160 MeV and G_V=0.35: ρt^H/ρ0=1.803, ρt^Q/ρ0=2.179; maximum mass Mmax=2.087 M⊙
- Bag constant range: B^{1/4}=145–160 MeV
- Symmetry energy J range across KIDS models: 30–33 MeV
- Slope parameter L range: 47–66 MeV

## Context

This work extends the study of hybrid stars using the KIDS density functional (Gil 2020, 2021) combined with the vBag quark model (Lopes 2021, Kumar 2022, Laskos-Patkos 2023). It builds on prior work showing that vector repulsion is needed to satisfy the 2 M⊙ mass constraint (Lopes 2021) and that symmetry energy significantly affects neutron star properties (Gil 2020). The results constrain the vector coupling G_V more precisely than previous studies by simultaneously testing against multiple observational datasets (GW170817, NICER, HESS J1731-347). The authors note that crossover transitions (Qin 2023, Huang 2022, Constantinou 2021) may be more likely than first-order transitions in neutron star cores, suggesting future work on such scenarios.
