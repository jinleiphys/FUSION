# Photons from Quark Gluon Plasma and Hot Hadronic Matter

> The productions of real photons from quark gluon plasma and hot hadronic matter formed after the nucleus - nucleus collisions at ultra-relativistic energies are discussed. The effects of the spectral shift of the hadrons at finite temperature on the production of photons are investigated. On the basis of the present analysis it is shown that the photon spectra measured by WA98 collaboration in Pb + Pb collisions at CERN SPS energies can be explained by both QGP as well as hadronic initial states if the spectral shift of hadrons at finite temperature is taken into account. Several other works on the analysis of WA98 photon data have also been briefly discussed.

# Photons from Quark Gluon Plasma and Hot Hadronic Matter

## Key claim

The photon spectra measured by the WA98 collaboration in Pb+Pb collisions at CERN SPS energies can be explained by either a QGP initial state or a hadronic initial state, provided that the spectral shift (mass reduction) of hadrons at finite temperature is taken into account; a simple hadronic model without medium modifications is ruled out by the data. The analysis explicitly notes that the hard thermal loop approximation used for the QGP photon emission rate is not strictly valid at SPS energies where the QCD coupling g is approximately 2, making firm conclusions about QGP formation difficult.

## Method

The author computes real photon production from three sources: hard QCD processes (using perturbative QCD with CTEQ5M partons, intrinsic kT broadening with <kT^2>=0.9 GeV^2, and a K-factor of 2), thermal photons from QGP (using the two-loop hard thermal loop rate), and thermal photons from hot hadronic matter (including reactions pi rho -> pi gamma, pi pi -> rho gamma, pi pi -> eta gamma, pi eta -> pi gamma, and decays rho -> pi pi gamma, omega -> pi gamma, plus a1 exchange). The space-time evolution is modeled with (3+1)-dimensional relativistic hydrodynamics assuming boost invariance and cylindrical symmetry, using a bag model equation of state for QGP and a temperature-dependent hadronic degeneracy; the initial temperature is obtained from the measured dN/dy=700 via isentropic expansion. The effects of finite temperature on hadronic properties are implemented either through universal scaling of masses (with exponent lambda=1/2) or through the Quantum Hadrodynamics (QHD) model parametrizations, and results are compared with a scenario where masses and widths remain at vacuum values.

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature for QGP scenario: T_i = 196 MeV (from dN/dy=700, tau_i=1 fm/c, g_QGP=37, two-flavour QGP)
- Initial temperature for hadronic scenario with mass scaling: T_i = 205 MeV (assuming T_i = T_c = 160 MeV, tau_i=1 fm/c)
- Critical temperature for phase transition: T_c = 160 MeV
- Freeze-out temperature: T_f = 120 MeV
- Nuclear thickness at b=3.2 fm for WA98 central events: T_AA = 220/fm^2
- Intrinsic kT broadening parameter: <kT^2> = 0.9 GeV^2
- Centre-of-mass energy for Pb+Pb at SPS: sqrt(s) = 17.3 GeV
- Initial thermalization time: tau_i = 1 fm/c
- QGP phase lifetime: approximately 1 fm/c
- Transport model parameters: tau_0 = 8 fm/c, T_infinity = 120 MeV
- QCD coupling at T~200 MeV: g ~ 2
- Initial temperature values from other analyses of WA98 data: 335 MeV (Srivastava and Sinha), 210 MeV (Gupta and Khandai), 220 MeV (Steffen and Thoma), 200-230 MeV (Prakash et al.), 213-255 MeV (Huovinen et al.)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of thermal photon production from heavy-ion collisions by incorporating finite-temperature modifications of hadronic masses, building on the universal scaling hypothesis of Brown and Rho (1991) and the QHD model of vector meson masses. The paper directly addresses the interpretation of the WA98 collaboration data (published 2000) and compares its results with prior analyses by Srivastava and Sinha, Gupta and Khandai, Steffen and Thoma, Prakash et al., and Huovinen et al., showing that the inferred initial temperature depends sensitively on the hadronic equation of state and the lifetime of the mixed phase. The work enables future discrimination between QGP and hadronic scenarios through simultaneous measurements of photons and dileptons, and it identifies the need for lattice QCD evaluations of the photon spectral function to replace the hard thermal loop approximation, which is invalid at SPS energies and may also fail at LHC energies.
