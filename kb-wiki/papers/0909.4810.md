# Search for three-nucleon force effects on the longitudinal response function of 4He

> A detailed study of the 4He longitudinal response function R_L(ω,q) is performed at different kinematics, with particular emphasis on the role of three-nucleon forces. The effects shown are the results of an ab initio calculation where the full four-body continuum dynamics is considered via the Lorentz integral transform method. The contributions of the various multipoles to the longitudinal response function are analyzed and integral properties of the response are discussed in addition. The Argonne V18 nucleon-nucleon interaction and two different three-nucleon force models (Urbana IX, Tucson-Melbourne') are used. At lower momentum transfer (q<= 200$ MeV/c) three-nucleon forces play an important role. One even finds a dependence of R_L on the three-nucleon force model itself with differences up to 10%. Thus a Rosenbluth separation of the inclusive electron scattering cross section of 4He at low momentum transfer would be of high value in view of a discrimination between different three-nucleon force models.

# Search for three-nucleon force effects on the longitudinal response function of 4He

## Key claim

At momentum transfers q ≤ 200 MeV/c, three-nucleon forces produce a large quenching effect on the longitudinal response function R_L of 4He, with differences up to 10% between the Urbana IX and Tucson-Melbourne' three-nucleon force models, suggesting that Rosenbluth separation of inclusive electron scattering data at low momentum transfer could discriminate between different three-nucleon force models.

## Method

The authors performed ab initio calculations of the 4He longitudinal response function R_L(ω,q) using the Lorentz integral transform method, which avoids explicit calculation of continuum wave functions by solving an inhomogeneous equation with bound-state boundary conditions. They employed the Argonne V18 nucleon-nucleon interaction alone and combined with two different three-nucleon force models (Urbana IX and Tucson-Melbourne'), using the effective interaction hyperspherical harmonics method to solve the many-body problem. The response functions were obtained by inverting the Lorentz integral transform, and the charge density operator was decomposed into isoscalar and isovector Coulomb multipoles for analysis.

## Key numbers

- At q = 200 MeV/c and below, three-nucleon forces produce a quenching effect on R_L that increases with decreasing momentum transfer, with differences between the UIX and TM' three-nucleon force models reaching up to 10% on the left-hand sides of the peaks
- The AV18 potential alone gives 4He binding energy E_B = 24.35 MeV (with higher precision 24.27 MeV), while AV18+UIX gives E_B = 28.40 MeV and the Malfliet-Tjon potential gives E_B = 30.56 MeV
- The TM' force with cutoff Λ = 4.77 m_π yields binding energies of 8.47 MeV for 3H and 28.46 MeV for 4He
- The effect of three-nucleon forces on the proton-proton correlation function f_pp(q) is up to 15% in the region below q = 300 MeV/c
- For q ≤ 200 MeV/c, the contribution of the time-like region to the Coulomb sum rule remains at most 1.5%
- At q = 300 and 350 MeV/c, the high-energy tail contribution to the Coulomb sum rule is about 7% of the CSR

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous Letter (Bacca et al., 2009) with more detailed analysis and additional low-q kinematics, building on earlier studies of three-nucleon force effects in few-body systems. The calculations complement bound-state studies by Pieper (2002) and Navratil (2007) by focusing on electromagnetic reactions in the continuum, where the Lorentz integral transform method (Efros, 1994) enables ab initio calculations beyond three- and four-body breakup thresholds. The results show that at low momentum transfer, the longitudinal response is sensitive to three-nucleon force models in a way that could discriminate between phenomenological potentials like Urbana IX and Tucson-Melbourne' and future effective field theory potentials, while also enabling extraction of long-range proton-proton correlation functions without complications from relativistic effects or two-body operators that affect higher-q measurements.
