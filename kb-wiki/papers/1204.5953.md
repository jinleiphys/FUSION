# Is Radioactive Decay Really Exponential?

> Radioactive decay of an unstable isotope is widely believed to be exponential. This view is supported by experiments on rapidly decaying isotopes but is more difficult to verify for slowly decaying isotopes. The decay of 14C can be calibrated over a period of 12,550 years by comparing radiocarbon dates with dates obtained from dendrochronology. It is well known that this approach shows that radiocarbon dates of over 3,000 years are in error, which is generally attributed to past variation in atmospheric levels of 14C. We note that predicted atmospheric variation (assuming exponential decay) does not agree with results from modelling, and that theoretical quantum mechanics does not predict exact exponential decay. We give mathematical arguments that non-exponential decay should be expected for slowly decaying isotopes and explore the consequences of non-exponential decay. We propose an experimental test of this prediction of non-exponential decay for 14C. If confirmed, a foundation stone of current dating methods will have been removed, requiring a radical reappraisal both of radioisotope dating methods and of currently predicted dates obtained using these methods.

## Key claim
Radioactive decay of slowly decaying isotopes such as 14C may not be exactly exponential; the paper argues that non-exponential decay could be a contributing factor to known discrepancies in radiocarbon dates over 3,000 years, alongside atmospheric variation, rather than the sole cause being atmospheric changes.

## Method
The author derives a non-exponential decay function y(t) from the INTCAL09 calibration curve by assuming the discrepancy is entirely due to non-exponential decay (case ii), then uses this function to predict radiocarbon ages from Accelerator Mass Spectrometry (AMS) measurements. These predicted AMS ages are compared with ages from radiometric (beta-counting) methods using data from the Fourth International Radiocarbon Intercomparison (FIRI). The author also presents mathematical arguments based on a nonlinear differential equation derived from quantum mechanics, where the decay rate depends on both a linear term and a nonlinear history-dependent term.

## Key numbers
- The half-life of 14C is initially given as 5,568 ± 30 yr (Libby half-life) and later as 5,730 ± 40 yr (Cambridge half-life); the Libby half-life is still used by convention in calculating radiocarbon ages.
- The longest continuous tree-ring chronology is 12,593 years, based on German oak and pine trees supplemented by pines from Switzerland.
- Radiocarbon dates of over 3,000 years show a discrepancy of up to 15% compared with dendrochronology dates.
- A half-life of 6,030 years would make modelled Δ14C values consistent with the Beck et al. (2001) model prediction, corresponding to an increase in half-life of over 5%.
- Recent experimental results propose the half-life should be decreased by 2 ± 1%.
- The decay of 56Mn (half-life 2.5785 h) was measured experimentally up to 45 half lives with no non-exponential effects found.
- For the FIRI sample E (humic acid, dated around 11.8 kBP), the AMS mean age was 54 ± 53 14C yr higher than the age from Gas Proportional Counting, far less than the approximately 3,000 14C yr difference predicted by the author's case (ii) model.

## Context
This paper extends the theoretical work of Fonda (1978), Peres (1980), and Merzbacher (1998) on quantum mechanical deviations from exponential decay, and challenges the standard assumption used in radiocarbon dating that decay is exactly exponential. The author notes that Newton (1966) observed the exponential law is experimentally verified more than the simple statistical derivation would justify. The paper directly addresses the INTCAL09 calibration curve (Reimer 2009) and the unresolved discrepancies between measured and modelled Δ14C values noted by Chiu et al. (2007). If confirmed, the work would require reappraisal of all radioisotope dating methods that assume exponential decay of slowly decaying isotopes, as described by Aitken (1990).
