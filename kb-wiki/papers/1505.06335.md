# Cavitation in a quark gluon plasma with finite chemical potential and several transport coefficients

> We study the effects of a finite chemical potential on the occurrence of cavitation in a quark gluon plasma (QGP). We solve the evolution equations of second order viscous relativistic hydrodynamics using three different equations of state. The first one was derived in lattice QCD and represents QGP at zero chemical potential. It was previously used in the study of cavitation. The second equation of state also comes from lattice QCD and is a recent parametrization of the QGP at finite chemical potential. The third one is similar to the MIT equation of state with chemical potential and includes nonperturbative effects through the gluon condensates. We conclude that at finite chemical potential cavitation in the QGP occurs earlier than at zero chemical potential. We also consider transport coefficients from a holographic model of a non-conformal QGP at zero chemical potential. In this case cavitation does not occur.

# Cavitation in a quark gluon plasma with finite chemical potential and several transport coefficients

## Key claim

At finite chemical potential, cavitation in the quark gluon plasma occurs earlier (at smaller proper times) than at zero chemical potential, but this conclusion depends on the choice of equation of state and transport coefficients; when using a holographic non-conformal model of QGP at zero chemical potential, cavitation does not occur at all.

## Method

The authors solved the coupled evolution equations of second-order viscous relativistic hydrodynamics under Bjorken flow (boost-invariant longitudinal expansion) for a quark gluon plasma. They used three different equations of state: a lattice QCD parametrization at zero chemical potential (Model 1), a lattice QCD parametrization at finite chemical potential (Model 2), and a holographic non-conformal model (Model 2 with holographic transport coefficients). The evolution equations for energy density, shear stress, and bulk stress were integrated numerically from an initial proper time of 0.5 fm, with two sets of initial conditions (fixed initial temperature of 305 MeV or fixed initial energy density of 16 GeV/fm³).

## Key numbers

- For Model 1 at zero chemical potential with fixed initial temperature (305 MeV), cavitation starts at τ_cav = 2.3 fm.
- For Model 2(i) at zero chemical potential with fixed initial temperature (305 MeV), cavitation starts at τ_cav = 2.9 fm.
- For Model 2(i) at μ = 500 MeV with fixed initial temperature (305 MeV), cavitation starts at τ_cav = 2.63 fm.
- For Model 2(i) at μ = 50 MeV with fixed initial energy density (16 GeV/fm³), cavitation starts at τ_cav = 4.04 fm.
- For Model 2(i) at μ = 500 MeV with fixed initial energy density (16 GeV/fm³), cavitation starts at τ_cav = 3.15 fm.
- For the holographic non-conformal model at zero chemical potential, cavitation does not occur for either initial condition.

## Context

This work extends the study of cavitation in QGP pioneered by Torrieri et al. (2008) and further developed by Rajagopal and Tripuraneni (2010) to include finite baryon chemical potential, motivated by recent lattice QCD equations of state at finite chemical potential from Ratti et al. (2014) and Fodor et al. (2014). The results show that increasing chemical potential accelerates cavitation when using lattice-based equations of state, but the holographic non-conformal model from Marrochio et al. (2015) predicts no cavitation due to smaller bulk viscosity near the transition. This enables future comparisons between different microscopic approaches to transport coefficients and may help determine whether cavitation occurs in heavy ion collisions at RHIC (μ_B ~ 20-100 MeV) and LHC (μ_B ~ 1-10 MeV).
