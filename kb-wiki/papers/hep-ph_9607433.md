# Tests of electron flavor conservation with the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory

> We analyze tests of electron flavor conservation that can be performed at the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO). These tests, which utilize $^8$B solar neutrinos interacting with deuterium, measure: 1) the shape of the recoil electron spectrum in charged-current (CC) interactions (the CC spectrum shape); and 2) the ratio of the number of charged current to neutral current (NC) events (the CC/NC ratio). We determine standard model predictions for the CC spectral shape and for the CC/NC ratio, together with realistic estimates of their errors and the correlations between errors. We consider systematic uncertainties in the standard neutrino spectrum and in the charged-current and neutral current cross-sections, the SNO energy resolution and absolute energy scale, and the SNO detection efficiencies. Assuming that either matter-enhanced or vacuum neutrino oscillations solve the solar neutrino problems, we calculate the confidence levels with which electron flavor non-conservation can be detected using either the CC spectrum shape or the CC/NC ratio, or both. If the SNO detector works as expected, the neutrino oscillation solutions that best-fit the results of the four operating solar neutrino experiments can be distinguished unambiguously from the standard predictions of electron flavor conservation.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that if the SNO detector performs as expected, the neutrino oscillation solutions that best-fit the results of the four operating solar neutrino experiments can be distinguished unambiguously from the standard predictions of electron flavor conservation, but only if systematic uncertainties (theoretical and experimental) are properly accounted for and do not exceed the estimated levels.

## Method

The authors calculated standard model predictions for the charged-current (CC) recoil electron energy spectrum shape and the CC/neutral current (NC) event ratio at SNO, incorporating systematic uncertainties from the laboratory $^8$B neutrino spectrum, charged-current and neutral-current cross sections on deuterium, SNO energy resolution, absolute energy scale, detection efficiencies, and CC detection threshold. They then computed confidence levels for detecting electron flavor non-conservation under representative matter-enhanced and vacuum neutrino oscillation scenarios that best-fit existing solar neutrino data.

## Key numbers

- The best estimate for the neutral current cross section averaged over the $^8$B neutrino spectrum is $\langle \sigma(^8{\rm B}) \rangle = 0.478 (1 \pm 0.06) \times 10^{-42}~{\rm cm^{2}}$ (1$\sigma$ error of 6%).
- The standard solar model prediction for the neutral current event rate due to $^8$B neutrinos is $3.2_{-0.5}^{+0.6}~{\rm SNU}$ (1$\sigma$ error combining solar model, $^8$B spectrum, and NC cross section uncertainties).
- The SNO energy resolution width at 10 MeV is estimated as $\sigma_{10} = 1.1 \pm 0.11$ MeV (1$\sigma$ errors).
- The absolute energy scale systematic error is estimated as $\delta = \pm 100~{\rm keV} (T_e^\prime / 10~{\rm MeV})^\alpha$ with $0 \leq \alpha \leq 1$, corresponding to $\pm 1\%$ error at 10 MeV.
- The neutral current detection efficiency uncertainty is $\sigma(\epsilon_{\rm NC}) \simeq 2\%$ (1$\sigma$).
- The $^8$B neutrino spectrum uncertainty is parametrized by an effective alpha-energy offset $b = 0.025 \pm 0.104$ MeV ($\pm 3\sigma$ uncertainties).
- The CC/NC ratio is calculated using a CC detection threshold of $T_{\rm min} = 5$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Fi94 and Kw95, which neglected systematic uncertainties, by providing realistic estimates of theoretical and experimental errors and their correlations. The analysis is independent of solar models and uses the $^8$B neutrino spectrum determined from laboratory data on the $^8$B($\beta^+$)$^8$Be($2\alpha$) decay chain (BaLi). The results enable quantitative assessment of SNO's ability to detect electron flavor non-conservation and provide a framework for discriminating among competing models of new physics, though the authors note that definitive simulations must be performed by the SNO collaboration.
