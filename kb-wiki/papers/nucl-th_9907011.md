# General response function for interacting quantum liquids

> Linearizing the appropriate kinetic equation we derive general response functions including selfconsistent mean fields or density functionals and collisional dissipative contributions. The latter ones are considered in relaxation time approximation conserving successively different balance equations. The effect of collisions is represented by correlation functions which are possible to calculate with the help of the finite temperature Lindhard RPA expression. The presented results are applicable to finite temperature response of interacting quantum systems if the quasiparticle or mean field energy is parameterized within Skyrme - type of functionals including density, current and energy dependencies which can be considered alternatively as density functionals. By this way we allow to share correlations between density functional and collisional dissipative contributions appropriate for the special treatment. We present results for collective modes like the plasmon in plasma systems and the giant resonance in nuclei. The collisions lead in general to an enhanced damping of collective modes. If the collision frequency is close to the frequency of the collective mode, resonance occurs and the collective mode is enhanced showing a collisional narrowing.

## Key claim

The paper derives a general response function for interacting quantum liquids that unifies self-consistent mean-field (Skyrme-type) descriptions with collisional dissipative contributions in a relaxation-time approximation, while conserving density, momentum, and energy successively. The key hedge is that the collisional correlations are represented by correlation functions calculable from the finite-temperature Lindhard RPA expression, and the approach allows one to share correlations between density-functional and collisional contributions, provided double counting is avoided when parameterizing microscopically.

## Method

The authors linearize a quantum kinetic equation for the density operator in relaxation-time approximation, including a Skyrme-type energy functional (mean field) and a residual interaction condensed into a collisional integral. They perform a Fourier transform to frequency-momentum space and solve the resulting linearized equation together with balance equations for density, momentum, and energy, obtaining a matrix equation for the density, current, and energy response functions. The solution is expressed in terms of correlation functions (moments of the finite-temperature Lindhard polarization function) and the mean-field coefficients derived from the Skyrme parameterization.

## Key numbers

* The inverse Debye length is given by \(\kappa^2 = 4 \pi n e^2 / T\) and the plasma frequency by \(\omega_p^2 = \kappa^2 T / m\) (used for scaling in figures).
* The relaxation time is chosen as \(\tau = 5 / \omega_p\) and the wave vector as \(q = 0.384 \kappa\) for the plasma example.
* For nuclear giant dipole resonances, the wave vector \(q = 0.23 \, \text{fm}^{-1}\) (approximately \(0.17 \, k_F\)) corresponds to the inverse diameter of the nucleus \(^{208}\text{Pb}\).
* The energy-weighted sum rule (EWSR) without mean field is \(-\frac{1}{\pi} \int_0^\infty d\omega \, \omega \, \text{Im} \chi = \frac{q^2}{2m} n_0\).
* The EWSR with Skyrme mean field includes an enhancement factor \(\kappa = \frac{m}{4} [t_1 (2+x_1) + t_2 (2+x_2)] n_0\).
* The approximation including only density conservation conserves the sum rule only to about \(75\%\), while the full density-momentum-energy conserving result conserves it completely.

## Context

This work extends two lines of theoretical improvement: one starting from TDHF equations with time-reversal-broken Skyrme interactions (BVA95, HNP96) and another including collisional correlations (Mer70, HPR93, KH94, RW98) and multicomponent systems (MWF97, Br99). The derived general response function enables the treatment of dissipative effects within density-functional approaches and is applicable to collective modes such as plasmas in plasma systems and giant resonances in nuclei. The results show that collisions generally enhance damping, but when the collision frequency approaches the collective mode frequency, a resonance occurs that narrows the collective peak (collisional narrowing).
