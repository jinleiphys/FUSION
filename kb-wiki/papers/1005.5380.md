# On the Charge Separation Effect in Relativistic Heavy Ion Collisions

> In this paper, we discuss alternative means of measuring the possible presence of local parity violation in relativistic heavy ion collisions. We focus on the phenomenon of charge separation and introduce the charged dipole vector $\hat{Q}^c_1$, which will measure the charge separation on an event-by-event basis. Using Monte Carlo events, we demonstrate the method and its discriminating power. In particular we show that such an analysis will reveal the strength of charge separation effect and its azimuthal correlation with the reaction plane. We further show that our proposed method may be able to distinguish between the actual charge separation effect and effects due to certain two particle correlations. The connection to present measurements based on particle correlations is discussed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the charged dipole vector $\hat{Q}^c_1$ can serve as an alternative observable to measure charge separation effects in relativistic heavy ion collisions, potentially distinguishing between genuine parity-violating charge separation from the Chiral Magnetic Effect and background correlations, but the authors hedge that the method requires simultaneous measurement of multiple correlation observables to disentangle these contributions, and that the interpretation of existing STAR data remains ambiguous.

## Method

The authors introduce an intrinsic charge-dependent single-particle azimuthal distribution parameterized by elliptic flow $v_2$, charge separation magnitude $d_1$, and orientation angle $\Psi_{C.S.}$ relative to the reaction plane, with a random sign factor $\chi=\pm 1$ accounting for equal probability of sphaleron and anti-sphaleron transitions. They define the charged dipole vector $\hat{Q}^c_1$ via event-by-event sums of $q_i \cos\phi_i$ and $q_i \sin\phi_i$ over all charged particles, and the elliptic flow vector $\hat{Q}_2$ via sums of $\cos 2\phi_i$ and $\sin 2\phi_i$. Using Monte Carlo sampling of 100 million events with 200 $\pi^+$ and 200 $\pi^-$ per event, they generate distributions of the magnitude $Q^c_1$ and the relative angle $\Delta\Psi = \Psi^c_1 - \Psi_2$ for various combinations of $d_1$, $v_2$, and correlation scenarios, including cases with same-charge back-to-back correlations and opposite-charge same-side correlations.

## Key numbers

- The magnitude distribution curves for all cases with $d_1=0$ (pure statistical fluctuation) fall into one group, while all cases with $d_1=0.05$ fall into another group, regardless of $v_2$ values of 0.1 or 0.05
- For the Chiral Magnetic Effect scenario ($\Delta\Psi_{C.S.} = \pi/2$), the two-particle correlation $\langle\cos(\phi_i-\phi_j)\rangle_{++/--} = +d_1^2$ and the reaction-plane-dependent correlation $\langle\cos(\phi_i+\phi_j-2\Psi_{R.P.})\rangle_{++/--} = -d_1^2$, showing equal magnitude with opposite sign
- In Test-I with $d_1=0.05$ and $v_2=0.1$, the squared magnitude $\langle(Q^c_1)^2\rangle$ decreases from 799.8 (Case-IIIc, no correlations) to 253.1 (Case-IIIc-$\gamma$, both SCBB and OCSS correlations at 0.4% fraction)
- The observable $\langle\cos(2\Delta\Psi)\rangle$ remains negative for all Test-I cases with out-of-plane dipole, with absolute value reduced by correlations
- The same-charge pair correlation $\langle\cos(\phi_i-\phi_j)\rangle_{++/--}$ changes sign from +0.0025 (Case-IIIc) to -0.00131 (Case-IIIc-$\gamma$)

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of the Chiral Magnetic Effect proposed by Kharzeev and collaborators (2004-2009) and the correlation measurements by the STAR collaboration (2009) at RHIC, which showed charged-pair correlation patterns potentially consistent with local parity violation but with puzzling sign discrepancies noted by Bzdak (2009). The proposed $\hat{Q}^c_1$ analysis provides a complementary method to the Voloshin (2004) correlation approach, and the authors demonstrate that combining both methods may help distinguish genuine charge separation from background correlations arising from clusters, momentum conservation, or local charge conservation as discussed by Wang (2009) and Pratt (2010). The method enables future experimental measurements to determine whether charge separation is oriented in-plane or out-of-plane relative to the reaction plane, which is essential for evaluating the Chiral Magnetic Effect interpretation.
