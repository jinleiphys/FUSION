# Nucleosynthesis and observation of the heaviest elements

> The rapid neutron capture or 'r process' of nucleosynthesis is believed to be responsible for the production of approximately half the natural abundance of heavy elements found on the periodic table above iron (with proton number $Z=26$) and all of the heavy elements above bismuth ($Z=83$). In the course of creating the actinides and potentially superheavies, the r process must necessarily synthesize superheavy nuclei (those with extreme proton numbers, neutron numbers or both) far from isotopes accessible in the laboratory. Many questions about this process remain unanswered, such as 'where in nature may this process occur?' and 'what are the heaviest species created by this process?' In this review, we survey at a high level the nuclear properties relevant for the heaviest elements thought to be created in the r process. We provide a synopsis of the production and destruction mechanisms of these heavy species, in particular the actinides and superheavies, and discuss these heavy elements in relation to the astrophysical r process. We review the observational evidence of actinides found in the Solar system and in metal-poor stars and comment on the prospective of observing heavy-element production in explosive astrophysical events. Finally, we discuss the possibility that future observations and laboratory experiments will provide new information in understanding the production of the heaviest elements.

## Key claim

This review argues that the rapid neutron capture (r-process) is responsible for producing approximately half the natural abundance of heavy elements above iron and all heavy elements above bismuth, but emphasizes that many fundamental questions remain unanswered, including where the r-process occurs in nature and what the heaviest species created by this process actually are.

## Method

The authors survey nuclear properties relevant for the heaviest elements created in the r-process, including nuclear binding energies, mass models, neutron capture, and fission. They review production and destruction mechanisms of actinides and superheavy elements, examine observational evidence of actinides found in the Solar system and in metal-poor stars, and discuss the prospects for observing heavy-element production in explosive astrophysical events.

## Key numbers

- The r-process is believed responsible for approximately half the natural abundance of heavy elements above iron (proton number Z=26) and all heavy elements above bismuth (Z=83)
- The actinide series spans Z=89 to Z=103, with transactinide (superheavy) elements having Z greater than 103
- The heaviest known element is oganesson (Og) with Z=118
- Only five actinide elements live sufficiently long to be found naturally in significant amounts: thorium (Z=90), protactinium (Z=91), uranium (Z=92), neptunium (Z=93), and plutonium (Z=94)
- Canonical closed shells occur at nucleon magic numbers 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, and 126; the next suggested closed shell for protons is at Z=114 and for neutrons at N=184
- The latest (2020) release of the Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME) dataset contained atomic masses of roughly 2,500 species, with average reported uncertainty of 25 keV for the most recent release
- Theoretical mass models match known data on the order of a few hundred keV uncertainty
- Elements beyond iron account for only about 2% of Solar system material

## Context

This review extends the landmark 1957 work of Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, and Hoyle (Burbidge 1957) and contemporaneous work by Cameron (Cameron 1957) that established nuclear astrophysics and interpreted Solar system abundance patterns as signatures of nuclear physics structure and stellar evolution. The paper surveys current understanding of the r-process and its role in producing the heaviest elements, including the actinides and potential superheavy elements, while identifying open questions about production sites and the maximum mass of elements created. It enables future work connecting laboratory measurements of neutron-rich nuclei, astrophysical observations of metal-poor stars and explosive events, and theoretical modeling of the r-process.
