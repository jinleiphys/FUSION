# Thermodynamic versus kinetic approach to polarization-vorticity coupling

> We critically compare thermodynamic and kinetic approaches, that have been recently used to study relations between the spin polarization and fluid vorticity in systems consisting of spin-one-half particles. The thermodynamic approach refers to general properties of global thermal equilibrium with a rigid-like rotation and demonstrates that the spin-polarization and thermal-vorticity tensors are equal. On the other hand, the kinetic approach uses the concept of the Wigner function and its semi-classical expansion. In most of the works done so far, the Wigner functions satisfy kinetic equations with a vanishing collision term. We show that this assumption restricts significantly applicability of such frameworks and, in contrast to many claims found in the literature, does not allow for drawing any conclusions regarding the relation between the thermal-vorticity and spin-polarization tensors, except for the fact that the two should be constant in global equilibrium. We further show how the kinetic-theory equations including spin degrees of freedom can be used to formulate a hydrodynamic framework for particles with spin. We define hydrodynamic equations starting separately from the formulation by de~Groot, van~Leeuwen, and van~Weert and from the canonical formalism. In the former case the energy-momentum tensor is symmetric and the spin tensor is conserved, while in the later case the energy-momentum tensor is not symmetric and the spin tensor is not conserved. Nevertheless, in the two cases the total angular momentum is always conserved. Interestingly, the two approaches are connected by the pseudo-gauge transformation, which we explicitly define.

## Key claim

The paper argues that recent kinetic approaches using collisionless Wigner functions cannot, contrary to many claims in the literature, establish a general relation between spin polarization and fluid vorticity; the equality of the spin-polarization and thermal-vorticity tensors holds only in global thermodynamic equilibrium, not in the more general out-of-equilibrium situations that collisionless kinetic equations are often used to describe.

## Method

The authors perform a semiclassical expansion of the Wigner function for spin-one-half particles, using the equilibrium distribution functions of Becattini (2013) as input. They compare the thermodynamic approach, based on the density operator of Zubarev (1974) and Becattini (2012), with the kinetic approach of Gao (2012), Chen (2012), and Fang (2016), which uses collisionless kinetic equations. They then construct hydrodynamic equations from the kinetic theory using both the formulation of de Groot, van Leeuwen, and van Weert (1980) and the canonical formalism, connecting the two via a pseudo-gauge transformation.

## Key numbers

* The spin polarization tensor omega_{mu nu} and thermal vorticity tensor varpi_{mu nu} are equal in global equilibrium, where varpi_{mu nu} = -1/2 (partial_mu beta_nu - partial_nu beta_mu) is constant.
* In global equilibrium, the beta_mu field satisfies the Killing equation partial_mu beta_nu + partial_nu beta_mu = 0, with solution beta_mu(x) = beta^0_mu + varpi^0_{mu nu} x^nu, where varpi^0_{mu nu} is constant.
* The spin polarization tensor omega_{mu nu} satisfies the conditions omega_{mu nu} omega^{mu nu} >= 0 and omega_{mu nu} tilde{omega}^{mu nu} = 0, leading to the definition zeta = Omega/T = 1/2 sqrt(1/2 omega_{mu nu} omega^{mu nu}).
* The equilibrium Wigner function yields the axial vector coefficient A^pm_{eq, mu}(x,k) = -sinh(zeta)/zeta * integral dP exp(-beta dot p +/- xi) delta^{(4)}(k -/+ p) tilde{omega}_{mu nu} p^nu.

## Context

This work extends the thermodynamic framework of Becattini (2009, 2012, 2013, 2015) and the kinetic frameworks of Gao (2012), Chen (2012), and Fang (2016), which were motivated by the first measurements of Lambda-hyperon spin polarization at STAR (2017). The paper clarifies that collisionless kinetic equations cannot imply polarization induction by vorticity, contrary to earlier claims, and it enables the construction of a consistent relativistic hydrodynamic framework for spin-one-half particles by defining four distinct types of equilibrium (global, extended global, local, and extended local). The hydrodynamic equations derived from the GLW formulation and the canonical formalism are connected by a pseudo-gauge transformation, providing a foundation for future studies of spin polarization in heavy-ion collisions.
