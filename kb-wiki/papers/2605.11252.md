# Quantum tunneling, global phases and the limits of classical action reconstructions

> It was proposed recently that the Schrödinger wave function can be reconstructed exactly from a discrete superposition of classical action branches weighted by associated classical densities, without semiclassical approximations. We examine this construction for quantum tunneling through finite potential barriers and for quantum phase phenomena. Although formally consistent when the Hamilton-Jacobi equation admits globally defined real branches, the construction breaks down in classically forbidden regions where no real classical action exists. Using rectangular and Coulomb barrier tunneling in alpha decay and nuclear fusion, we show that the wave function requires either a non-vanishing quantum potential or complex-valued action. The growing barrier component fixed by global boundary conditions is essential for transmission and cannot arise from local real classical trajectories alone. Berry phase, flux quantization, Josephson tunneling, and dc SQUID interference likewise impose global phase constraints absent from local classical action transport.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the classical-action reconstruction of the Schrödinger wave function proposed by Lohmiller and Slotine (2026) breaks down for quantum tunneling through finite potential barriers and for phase-coherent quantum phenomena, because the construction requires either a non-vanishing quantum potential or complex-valued action in classically forbidden regions, and cannot generate the exponentially growing barrier component fixed by global boundary conditions from local real classical trajectories alone.

## Method

The authors examine the Lohmiller-Slotine (LS) multi-valued classical-action framework by applying it to three concrete tunneling problems: a rectangular potential barrier, Coulomb barrier tunneling in alpha decay, and Coulomb barrier penetration in nuclear fusion. They compare the LS decomposition with exact stationary solutions of the Schrödinger equation, analyzing the Madelung decomposition (quantum potential, Bohmian velocity) and the classical-action branch structure in each case. For the Coulomb problems, they evaluate whether the Kustaanheimo-Stiefel transformation mapping to a harmonic oscillator can rescue the classical-action reconstruction for both bound and unbound states.

## Key numbers

- Rectangular barrier transmission coefficient: \(\mathcal{T} = |t|^2 = \left[1 + \frac{(k^2+\kap^2)^2}{4k^2\kap^2}\sinh^2(\kap a)\right]^{-1}\), with thick-barrier approximation \(\mathcal{T} \approx \frac{16k^2\kap^2}{(k^2+\kap^2)^2}\,e^{-2\kap a}\)
- Inside the rectangular barrier, the growing exponential coefficient magnitude is \(|B| = \frac{|t|}{2}\sqrt{1+\frac{k^2}{\kap^2}}\,e^{-\kap a}\), and the decaying coefficient magnitude is \(|A| = \frac{|t|}{2}\sqrt{1+\frac{k^2}{\kap^2}}\,e^{+\kap a}\)
- For the potential step (\(a \to \infty\) limit), the quantum potential inside the barrier is \(Q_{\mathrm{step}} = -(V_0 - E)\), giving effective potential \(V_{\mathrm{eff}} = E\)
- For the Coulomb barrier in alpha decay, the radial wave function is described by Coulomb-Hankel functions \(H_L^+(\eta, \varrho) = G_L(\eta, \varrho) + i F_L(\eta, \varrho)\) with Sommerfeld parameter \(\eta = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{\hbar} \sqrt{\frac{m}{2E}}\)
- For nuclear fusion, the exterior wave function is \(u_L(r)=H_L^-(\eta,\varrho)-S_LH_L^+(\eta,\varrho)\) with fusion probability \(T_L=1-|S_L|^2\)

## Context

This work extends the critique of the classical-action reconstruction proposed by Lohmiller and Slotine (2026), who claimed the Schrödinger equation can be solved exactly using only classical action branches and classical densities. The paper shows that this construction works only for systems with globally defined real Hamilton-Jacobi branches, such as the potential step treated in LS2026, but fails for finite-barrier tunneling where the growing exponential component carries the transmission amplitude. The analysis connects to the Madelung (1926) decomposition and Bohmian mechanics (Bohm 1952), showing that the quantum potential is essential in forbidden regions. The results clarify that tunneling through Coulomb barriers in alpha decay (Gamow 1928, Gurney and Condon 1928) and nuclear fusion cannot be reproduced from local classical action transport alone, and that global phase phenomena such as Berry phase, flux quantization, Josephson tunneling, and dc SQUID interference impose constraints absent from local classical action frameworks.
