# Interpreting Single Jet Measurements in Pb+Pb Collisions at the LHC

> Results are presented from a phenomenological analysis of recent measurements of jet suppression and modifications of jet fragmentation functions in Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC. Particular emphasis is placed on the impact of the differences between quark and gluon jet quenching on the transverse momentum ($p_{T}^{jet}$) dependence of the jet $R_{AA}$ and on the fragmentation functions, $D(z)$. Primordial quark and gluon parton distributions were obtained from PYTHIA8 and were parameterized using simple power-law functions and extensions to the power-law function which were found to better describe the PYTHIA8 parton spectra. A simple model for the quark energy loss based on the shift formalism is used to model $R_{AA}$ and $D(z)$ using both analytic results and using direct Monte-Carlo sampling of the PYTHIA parton spectra. The model is capable of describing the full $p_{T}^{jet}$ , rapidity, and centrality dependence of the measured jet $R_{AA}$ using three effective parameters. A key result from the analysis is that the $D(z)$ modifications observed in the data, excluding the enhancement at low-$z$, may result primarily from the different quenching of the quarks and gluons. The model is also capable of reproducing the charged hadron $R_{AA}$ at high transverse momentum. Predictions are made for the jet $R_{AA}$ at large rapidities where it has not yet been measured and for the rapidity dependence of $D(z)$.

# Interpreting Single Jet Measurements in Pb+Pb Collisions at the LHC

## Key claim
The observed modifications to jet fragmentation functions in Pb+Pb collisions, excluding the enhancement at low z, may result primarily from the different quenching of quark and gluon jets rather than from medium-induced changes to the fragmentation process itself. The analysis rules out a constant fractional energy loss parameterization, finding instead that the extracted shift varies approximately as the square root of the jet transverse momentum.

## Method
Quark and gluon parton distributions were obtained from PYTHIA8 and parameterized using power-law functions with extensions to better describe the PYTHIA8 parton spectra. A simple model for quark energy loss based on the shift formalism was applied to model the jet nuclear modification factor R_AA and fragmentation functions D(z) using both analytic results and direct Monte-Carlo sampling of the PYTHIA parton spectra, assuming quenched jets fragment as they do in vacuum.

## Key numbers
- The extracted shift parameter alpha was found to be approximately 0.55, compatible with the prediction of Baier et al. (2001) for a shift varying as sqrt(pT_jet)
- In the forward rapidity region 2.8 < |y| < 3.5, the jet R_AA in 0-10% central collisions reaches a maximum of approximately 0.4 at around 50 GeV and decreases to approximately 0.15 at 170 GeV
- The quark fraction f_q in the forward rapidity region 2.8 < |y| < 3.5 was found to be 0.76, compared to 0.60 in the region 2.1 < |y| < 2.8
- The ratio of fragmentation functions R_D(z) evaluated in different rapidity regions relative to the inclusive rapidity interval |y| < 2.1 was predicted to reach differences at the level of 6-7%
- The jet pT spectra in the rapidity region 2.8 < |y| < 3.5 decrease by approximately four orders of magnitude between 40 GeV and 100 GeV

## Context
This work extends the phenomenological analysis of jet suppression in Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC, building on the shift formulation described by Baier et al. (2001) and the observation by Renk (2014) that the quark fraction plays a role in the evolution of jet R_AA with pT_jet. The analysis tests the picture from Casalderrey-Solana et al. (2012) where the medium cannot resolve the internal structure of many jets, and from Blaizot et al. (2013) where jets interact with the medium as a single color charge. The results enable predictions for jet R_AA at large rapidities not yet measured and for the rapidity dependence of fragmentation functions, which could test the color-charge dependence of jet quenching. The low-z enhancement in fragmentation functions that cannot be explained by this model points to the need for additional physics such as medium response or resolved jet structure.
