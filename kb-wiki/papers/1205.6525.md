# Testing Parity with Atomic Radiative Capture of $μ^-$

> The next generation of "intensity frontier" facilities will bring a significant increase in the intensity of sub-relativistic beams of $μ^-$. We show that the use of these beams in combination with thin targets of $Z\sim 30$ elements opens up the possibility of testing parity-violating interactions of muons with nuclei via direct radiative capture of muons into atomic 2S orbitals. Since atomic capture preserves longitudinal muon polarization, the measurement of the gamma ray angular asymmetry in the single photon $2S_{1/2}$-$1S_{1/2}$ transition will offer a direct test of parity. We calculate the probability of atomic radiative capture taking into account the finite size of the nucleus to show that this process can dominate over the usual muonic atom cascade, and that the as yet unobserved single photon $2S_{1/2}$-$1S_{1/2}$ transition in muonic atoms can be detected in this way using current muon facilities.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that atomic radiative capture (ARC) of muons directly into the 2S orbital of muonic atoms with Z around 30, using thin targets that do not stop the muons, can enable a test of parity-violating muon-nucleus interactions; the key hedge is that testing the Standard Model prediction requires future high-intensity muon beams, while testing some new physics models is feasible at existing facilities.

## Method

The authors calculate the cross section for muonic ARC into the 2S state by adapting the standard hydrogen-like photoelectric ionization formula, replacing the electron mass with the muon mass and introducing a correction factor that accounts for the finite nuclear charge radius and departure from the dipole approximation, solved numerically using a Schroedinger equation with a uniform nuclear charge distribution. They then estimate the probability of ARC in a thin gaseous krypton target that reduces the muon momentum from 30 MeV to 25 MeV, using Bethe-Bloch theory for energy loss, and combine this with the branching ratio for the single-photon 2S-1S transition to predict signal rates and signal-to-background ratios. The parity-violating asymmetry is computed from the 2S-2P mixing induced by the parity-violating Hamiltonian, using both Standard Model and new physics effective Lagrangians.

## Key numbers

* For krypton (Z=36, R_c=4.2 fm), the 2S-2P energy splitting is approximately 210 keV.
* The branching ratio for the single-photon 2S-1S transition for Z around 30 is approximately 2 x 10^{-3}.
* The probability of atomic radiative capture into the 2S state for a krypton target reducing muon momentum from 30 MeV to 25 MeV is approximately 2 x 10^{-7}.
* The predicted emission rate of 2S-1S photons is about 1 per 250 seconds for a muon flux of 10^7 s^{-1}.
* The Standard Model forward-backward asymmetry for Z=36 is approximately 0.5 x 10^{-4}.
* The new physics forward-backward asymmetry for Z=36 ranges from 0.5% to 11%, depending on the mediator mass.
* The estimated time to test the Standard Model asymmetry is about 10^8 seconds for a muon flux of 10^11 s^{-1}.
* The estimated time to test new physics asymmetries is about 3 x 10^5 seconds for a muon flux of 10^7 s^{-1} and an asymmetry of 0.1.

## Context

This work extends earlier proposals for parity violation tests in muonic atoms, particularly the scheme reviewed by Missimer and Simons (1991) which relied on muon cascades and was deemed impractical for Z around 30 due to background from nP-1S transitions and the dominance of nuclear muon capture over muon decay. The authors specifically build on the model of Batell, McKeen, and Pospelov (2011) which suggested that a new light vector boson mediating muon-proton interactions could explain the muonic hydrogen Lamb shift discrepancy and produce parity-violating effects stronger than the Standard Model. The proposed ARC method enables detection of the previously unobserved single-photon 2S-1S transition and allows parity tests without requiring observation of muon decay, making it feasible with current muon facilities for new physics scenarios.
