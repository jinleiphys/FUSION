# Nuclear liquid-gas phase transition and supernovae evolution

> It is shown that the large density fluctuations appearing at the onset of the first order nuclear liquid-gas phase transition can play an important role in the supernovae evolution. Due to these fluctuations, the neutrino gas may be trapped inside a thin layer of matter near the proto-neutron star surface. The resulting increase of pressure may induce strong particle ejection a few hundred milliseconds after the bounce of the collapse, contributing to the revival of the shock wave. The Hartree-Fock+RPA scheme, with a finite-range nucleon-nucleon effective interaction, is employed to estimate the effects of the neutrino trapping due to the strong density fluctuations, and to discuss qualitatively the consequences of the suggested new scenario.

# Nuclear liquid-gas phase transition and supernovae evolution

## Key claim
Large density fluctuations at the onset of the first-order nuclear liquid-gas phase transition can trap neutrinos in a thin layer near the proto-neutron star surface, increasing local pressure and potentially reviving the shock wave in core-collapse supernovae. The authors present this as a qualitative scenario based on Hartree-Fock plus RPA calculations, noting that the RPA scheme may be questionable in such a large-fluctuation regime.

## Method
The authors employed the Hartree-Fock (HF) plus Random Phase Approximation (RPA) scheme with the Gogny D1P finite-range nucleon-nucleon effective interaction to calculate the nuclear response functions and neutrino mean-free path in asymmetric nuclear matter. The RPA response functions were computed in the Landau approximation, ignoring Landau coefficients beyond the dipole one in both density and spin channels. The density-density response function and neutrino mean-free path were evaluated as functions of density for asymmetric nuclear matter at T=10 MeV with leptonic fraction Y_l=0.285.

## Key numbers
- The density range where neutrinos may be trapped is between about 0.1ρ₀ and 0.6ρ₀, where ρ₀ is nuclear matter saturation density.
- The spinodal density is close to 0.6ρ₀ in symmetric nuclear matter.
- For a typical proto-neutron star radius of 10 km and a thin layer width of 100 m, the neutrino pressure increases by a factor greater than 200 (before Pauli blocking corrections).
- The critical temperature of the liquid-gas phase transition is located between 15 and 20 MeV, depending on the nuclear model.
- At low density (ρ<ρ₀), the neutrino contribution to total pressure is about 20% or less; an increase of neutrino pressure by a factor of 5 would increase total pressure by a factor of 2.
- Neutrino energy represents about 98% of the collapse energy while the shock wave transports only 2% of it.

## Context
This work extends the earlier investigation by Sawyer (1975), who studied neutrino opacity and the equation of state of pure neutron matter and suggested that classical density fluctuations could produce an opaque layer near the star surface. The authors substantiate Sawyer's crude estimate by consistently calculating both density fluctuations and the nuclear equation of state using the Gogny D1P interaction. The paper also notes that recent work by Horowitz (2004) found high neutrino opacity inside the pasta phase due to coherent scattering off clusters, indicating that inhomogeneous nuclear matter should also trap neutrinos. The authors propose that this neutrino trapping mechanism should be included in hydrodynamical simulations of supernovae.
