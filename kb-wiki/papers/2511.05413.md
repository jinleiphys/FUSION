# Neutrino-Nucleus Scattering Cross Sections at Medium Energies

> The weak interactions of neutrinos with other Standard Model particles are well described within the Standard Model of particle physics. However, modern accelerator-based neutrino experiments employ nuclei as targets, where neutrinos interact with bound nucleons, turning a seemingly simple electroweak process into a complex many-body problem in nuclear physics. At the time of writing this Encyclopedia of Particle Physics chapter, neutrino-nucleus interactions remain one of the leading sources of systematic uncertainty in accelerator-based neutrino oscillation measurements. This chapter provides a pedagogical overview of neutrino interactions with nuclei in the medium-energy regime, spanning a few hundred MeV to several GeV. It introduces the fundamental electroweak formalism, outlines the dominant interaction mechanisms - including quasielastic scattering, resonance production, and deep inelastic scattering - and discusses how nuclear effects such as Fermi motion, nucleon-nucleon correlations, meson-exchange currents, and final-state interactions modify observable cross sections. The chapter also presents a brief survey of the foundational and most widely used theoretical models for neutrino-nucleus cross sections, together with an overview of current and upcoming accelerator-based neutrino oscillation experiments that are shaping the field. Rather than targeting experts, this chapter serves as a primer for advanced undergraduates, graduate students, and early-career researchers entering the field. It provides a concise foundation for understanding neutrino-nucleus scattering, its relevance to oscillation experiments, and its broader connections to both particle and nuclear physics.

## Key claim

Neutrino-nucleus interactions remain one of the leading sources of systematic uncertainty in accelerator-based neutrino oscillation measurements, despite the weak interactions of neutrinos being well described within the Standard Model. The chapter serves as a pedagogical primer for advanced undergraduates, graduate students, and early-career researchers rather than targeting experts, providing a concise foundation rather than a comprehensive treatment.

## Method

The chapter develops the general formalism for neutrino-nucleus scattering starting from the electroweak Lagrangian, deriving the charged-current and neutral-current interaction Lagrangians and the effective four-fermion Fermi interaction. It then presents the decomposition of the differential cross section into leptonic kinematic coefficients and nuclear response functions, expressed in terms of Coulomb, longitudinal, transverse electric, and transverse magnetic operators evaluated between nuclear many-body states. The dominant interaction channels, quasielastic scattering, resonance production, and deep inelastic scattering, are described along with nuclear effects including Fermi motion, nucleon-nucleon correlations, meson-exchange currents, and final-state interactions.

## Key numbers

- The medium-energy regime spans from a few hundred MeV to several GeV
- The W boson mass is approximately 80 GeV, justifying the low-energy propagator approximation for medium-energy neutrino scattering
- Quasielastic scattering dominates the total cross section below about 1 GeV
- The long-range region of the nucleon-nucleon potential is characterized by distances greater than approximately 2 fm, the intermediate-range region from about 1 fm to 2 fm, and the short-range region below about 1 fm

## Context

This chapter extends the pedagogical tradition of neutrino-nucleus scattering reviews such as Walecka (2004) and Donnelly (1985) by providing an updated primer connecting electroweak formalism to modern accelerator-based experiments. It surveys foundational theoretical models including the impulse approximation, spectral functions, local density approximation, random phase approximation, and chiral effective field theory (Weinberg 1990, Epelbaum 2008, Machleidt 2011). The work enables next-generation long-baseline oscillation experiments to address percent-level systematic precision requirements, with current and upcoming programs led by Fermilab in the United States and J-PARC in Japan.
