# Nucleon-nucleon correlation functions from different interactions in comparison

> Correlation functions as they can be observed in heavy-ion collisions using the femtoscopy technique are a powerful tool to study the interaction among different baryons or mesons. Specifically, the multi-nucleon correlation functions have been under intense experimental and theoretical investigation in the recent years. Due to the interest of using this observable as an input in the construction of potentials between hadrons we revisit the nucleon-nucleon correlation function and calculate it using different nuclear interactions at high precision. Since the nucleon-nucleon potential is determined to reproduce the two-nucleon scattering data, we would like to critically evaluate the amount of this information captured by the correlation function. We study the dependence of the correlations on the nuclear force giving detailed insights into the calculations, in particular the convergence behavior in the partial waves. The coupling between the different partial-wave channels is taken into account and the relevance of this effect is quantified. To make contact with precedent studies the results based on the Argonne V18 interaction are presented. Then we consider also the Norfolk NV2-IIa and NV2-IIb chiral EFT interactions. The analysis of the differences between the correlations of the various interactions shows that for momenta between 0 and 500 MeV there are variations of up to 5.9 % for the $nn$ system, of up to 1.8 % for the $np$ system, and of 1.4 % for the $pp$ system.

## Key claim

The nucleon-nucleon correlation function is largely constrained by the phase shifts and may serve as a good approximation to a strict observable under certain limitations, but the paper shows that for momenta between 0 and 500 MeV the correlation functions from different interactions vary by up to 5.9% for the nn system, indicating that the observable does capture some residual interaction-dependent information beyond what is encoded in phase shifts alone.

## Method

The authors solved the two-body scattering Schrodinger equation using a Runge-Kutta-Fehlberg method to obtain scattering wave functions, then computed correlation functions via the Koonin-Pratt formula with a Gaussian source parametrization. They included interactions in partial waves up to j=8 and non-interacting contributions up to j=35, accounting for tensor-force-induced coupling between channels. The correlation functions were calculated for the Argonne V18 interaction and the Norfolk NV2-IIa and NV2-IIb chiral EFT interactions, with electromagnetic interactions taken from Wiringa (1994).

## Key numbers

- Maximum deviation between NV2-IIa/NV2-IIb and AV18 correlation functions: 5.9% for the nn system at zero momentum, 1.8% for the np system at zero momentum, and 1.4% for the pp system around k=430 MeV
- The dip in the correlation function is located at approximately 97 MeV for nn, 99 MeV for pp, and 107 MeV for np
- Minimum values of the dip: approximately 0.96 for nn, 0.94 for pp, and 0.87 for np
- Changing the source radius from 1.0 fm to 2.0 fm moves the dip position from approximately 65 MeV to 133 MeV for the np system
- The s-wave contribution constitutes more than 98% of the overall correlation function up to k approximately 25 MeV
- Influence of partial-wave coupling: approximately 1% for nn and pp systems, reaching almost 30% for the np system
- Relative numerical uncertainties estimated below 0.2%

## Context

This work extends precedent studies using the Argonne V18 interaction by adding comparisons with the Norfolk chiral EFT interactions (NV2-IIa and NV2-IIb), which were fitted to scattering data up to T_lab=200 MeV. The study is motivated by the growing use of femtoscopy correlation functions to constrain hadronic interactions, particularly in the hypernuclear sector where scattering data are scarce, as in the nucleon-Lambda system studied by Mihaylov (2023). The analysis demonstrates that the Koonin-Pratt correlation function is strongly constrained by phase shifts, supporting its use as a supplement to scattering data for determining interaction low-energy constants, with further studies along these lines in progress.
