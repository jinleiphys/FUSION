# Test of the notch technique for determining the radial sensitivity of the optical model potential

> Detailed investigations on the notch technique are performed on the ideal data generated by the optical model potential parameters extracted from the 16O+208Pb system at the laboratory energy of 129.5 MeV, to study the sensitivities of this technique on the model parameters as well as the experimental data. It is found that, for the perturbation parameters, a sufficient large reduced fraction and an appropriate small perturbation width are necessary to determine the accurate radial sensitivity; while for the potential parameters, almost no dependence was observed. For the experimental measurements, the number of data points has little influence for the heavy target system, and the relative inner information of the nuclear potential can be derived when the measurement extended to a lower cross section.

## Key claim

The notch technique can reliably determine the radial sensitivity region of the optical model potential, but only if the perturbation parameters are chosen appropriately: a sufficiently large reduced fraction (d ≥ 0.5) and an appropriately small perturbation width (a' about 5 times the integration step) are necessary to avoid spurious sensitivity regions; the technique is nearly independent of the optical model potential parameters themselves.

## Method

The authors generated an ideal elastic scattering angular distribution for the 16O+208Pb system at Elab = 129.5 MeV by fitting experimental data with Woods-Saxon optical model parameters (r0i = 1.25 fm, ai = 0.65 fm). They then applied the notch-perturbation method using the FRESCO code, introducing localized perturbations of varying depth (d) and width (a') into the real and imaginary potentials at different radial positions, and monitoring the resulting change in χ² relative to the unperturbed calculation. The sensitivity functions were examined for dependence on perturbation parameters, optical model parameters (r0i and ai), and experimental data quality (angle interval and angular range).

## Key numbers

- The 16O+208Pb system was studied at laboratory energy Elab(16O) = 129.5 MeV
- The ideal data set was generated with a fixed angle step of 0.1° and a statistical error of 1%
- The theoretical data was cut off at θc.m. = 80°, where dσel/dσRu ≈ 6.0×10⁻⁵
- For the real part, the main sensitivity peak lies at approximately 11.92 fm with a tiny inner peak around 11.0 fm
- For the imaginary part, a major peak lies at approximately 12.20 fm and a minor peak at approximately 11.20 fm
- With data down to dσel/dσRu = 6.0×10⁻⁵, the main sensitivity region centers at 11.91 fm (real) and 12.10 fm (imaginary), with sigma widths of 0.25 fm and 0.31 fm respectively
- The interaction radius Rint is 10.56 fm, demonstrating that sensitivity is limited to surface regions
- The relative χ² value of the fifth data set (down to 6.0×10⁻⁵) is 10⁷ times larger than that of the first set (down to 0.25)

## Context

This work extends the notch-perturbation method originally developed by Cramer (1980) and addresses the lack of systematic investigation into the technique's dependence on perturbation parameters, optical model parameters, and experimental data quality. The study provides practical guidance for applying the notch technique to both tightly-bound and weakly-bound nuclear systems, particularly for experiments with radioactive ion beams (RIBs) where data points are limited. The results enable more reliable determination of radial sensitivity regions in future optical model potential studies, including investigations of the break-up threshold anomaly (BTA) observed in weakly-bound systems by Keeley (2009, 2007).
