# Simulating pasta phases by molecular dynamics and cold atoms --- Formation in supernovae and superfluid neutrons in neutron stars

> In dense stars such as collapsing cores of supernovae and neutron stars, nuclear "pasta" such as rod-like and slab-like nuclei are speculated to exist. However, whether or not they are actually formed in supernova cores is still unclear. Here we solve this problem by demonstrating that a lattice of rod-like nuclei is formed from a bcc lattice by compression. We also find that the formation process is triggered by an attractive force between nearest neighbor nuclei, which starts to act when their density profile overlaps, rather than the fission instability. We also discuss the connection between pasta phases in neutron star crusts and ultracold Fermi gases.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that a triangular lattice of rod-like nuclear pasta is formed from a body-centered cubic (bcc) lattice of spherical nuclei by compression, and that the formation is triggered by an attractive force between nearest neighbor nuclei when their density profiles overlap, rather than by the fission instability that was previously assumed.

## Method
The authors performed quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations of a system with protons, neutrons, and charge-neutralizing background electrons in a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions, using a proton fraction of x = 0.39 and 3328 total nucleons. They simulated adiabatic and isothermal compression starting from a bcc lattice of spherical nuclei at density 0.15 rho_0, increasing density by changing the box size at an average compression rate of less than or equal to 10^(-6) rho_0 per (fm/c).

## Key numbers
- Initial density: 0.15 rho_0, where rho_0 = 0.165 fm^(-3) (normal nuclear density)
- Total nucleons in simulation: N = 3328, with 1312 protons and 2016 neutrons (proton fraction x = 0.39)
- First pair of nearest-neighbor nuclei touch and fuse at density approximately 0.243 rho_0
- Compression rate: less than or equal to 10^(-6) rho_0/(fm/c), yielding time scale greater than or equal to 10^5 fm/c
- Initial temperature in adiabatic compression example: T = 0.25 MeV at t = 0 fm/c

## Context
This work solves the long-standing problem of whether nuclear pasta phases are actually formed in supernova cores, which had been questioned by Brandt (2005) and Burvenich (2005) who showed that background electrons suppress the fission barrier. The results contradict the generally accepted scenario based on the Bohr-Wheeler fission instability and instead show formation through internuclear attraction. The paper also connects pasta phases in neutron star crusts to ultracold atomic Fermi gases, showing that dripped neutron gas in pasta phases can be simulated by cold atomic Fermi gases with a narrow Feshbach resonance, building on work by Schwenk and Pethick (2005).
