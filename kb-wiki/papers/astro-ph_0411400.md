# Propagation of cosmic rays: nuclear physics in cosmic-ray studies

> The nuclei fraction in cosmic rays (CR) far exceeds the fraction of other CR species, such as antiprotons, electrons, and positrons. Thus the majority of information obtained from CR studies is based on interpretation of isotopic abundances using CR propagation models where the nuclear data and isotopic production cross sections in p- and alpha-induced reactions are the key elements. This paper presents an introduction to the astrophysics of CR and diffuse gamma rays and discusses some of the puzzles that have emerged recently due to more precise data and improved propagation models. Merging with cosmology and particle physics, astrophysics of CR has become a very dynamic field with a large potential of breakthrough and discoveries in the near future. Exploiting the data collected by the CR experiments to the fullest requires accurate nuclear cross sections.

## Key claim
The paper argues that interpreting cosmic-ray (CR) data requires accurate nuclear cross sections for isotopic production in proton- and alpha-induced reactions, and that recent puzzles (such as the EGRET gamma-ray excess above 1 GeV and the HEAT positron excess above ~8 GeV) may indicate either new physics or flaws in current propagation models, but the authors hedge that these excesses "may be telling us about processes in the ISM, in the Local Bubble, or signaling exotic physics... but also may indicate a flaw in the current models."

## Method
The authors review CR propagation modeling, focusing on the transport equation that includes diffusion, energy losses, convection, reacceleration, and nuclear spallation. They describe the GALPROP numerical model, which solves the transport equation for all CR species using a three-dimensional grid, incorporating maps of Galactic gas, interstellar radiation fields, and magnetic fields. The paper compares model predictions to data on secondary-to-primary ratios (e.g., B/C), antiproton fluxes, positron fractions, and diffuse gamma-ray emission from EGRET, OSSE, and COMPTEL.

## Key numbers
- The energy density of relativistic particles (CR) is ~1 eV cm^-3, comparable to that of interstellar radiation and magnetic fields.
- The total power of Galactic CR sources needed to sustain the observed CR density is estimated at 5 x 10^40 erg s^-1.
- The energy release per supernova in the form of CR is ~5 x 10^49 erg, assuming a supernova rate of ~3 per century.
- The EGRET data show a puzzling excess in diffuse gamma-ray emission above 1 GeV relative to model predictions.
- The HEAT experiment shows a positron fraction excess above ~8 GeV compared to diffusion model predictions for secondary production.
- Diffusive reacceleration models underproduce antiprotons by a factor of ~2 at 2 GeV.
- Solar modulation affects CR below 20 GeV/nucleon.
- Voyager 1 was at 13.3 x 10^9 km (88 AU) from the sun at the time of writing.
- GLAST will improve sensitivity for diffuse gamma-ray emission by a factor of 30.

## Context
This paper extends the work of Hunter et al. (1997) on the EGRET gamma-ray excess and Moskalenko & Strong (1998) on diffusion model predictions for positrons. It also builds on the GALPROP model development and discusses contradictions with reacceleration models that match B/C ratios but underproduce antiprotons, as shown in Moskalenko et al. (2002). The work enables future studies using GLAST data and improved nuclear cross-section databases to resolve the puzzles in CR propagation.
