# Superdeformed and Hyperdeformed States in Z=122 Isotopes

> We calculate the binding energy, root-mean-square radius and quadrupole deformation parameter for the recent, possibly discovered superheavey element Z=122, using the axially deformed relativistic mean field (RMF) and non-relativistic Skyrme Hartree-Fock (SHF) formalisms. The calculation is extended to include various isotopes of Z=122 element, strarting from A=282 to A=320. We predict highly deformed structures in the ground state for all the isotopes. A shape transition appears at about A=290 from a highly oblate to a large prolate shape, which may be considered as the superdeformed and hyperdeformed structures of Z=122 nucleus in the mean field approaches. The most stable isotope (largest binding energy per nucleon) is found to be $^{302}$122, instead of the experimentally observed $^{292}$122.

# Superdeformed and Hyperdeformed States in Z=122 Isotopes

## Key claim
The authors predict that all Z=122 isotopes from A=282 to A=320 have highly deformed ground states, with a shape transition from highly oblate to highly prolate occurring around A=290, and that the most stable isotope in the series is $^{302}$122 rather than the experimentally observed $^{292}$122. However, the claim is conditional on the mean-field approaches used (RMF with NL3 and SHF with SkI4 parameter sets), and the authors note that a constant scaling factor of about 1.0075 would make the two formalisms' binding energy curves coincide, indicating that the absolute binding energies are force-dependent.

## Method
The authors performed axially deformed relativistic mean-field (RMF) calculations using the NL3 parameter set and non-relativistic Skyrme Hartree-Fock (SHF) calculations using the SkI4 parameter set, both with constant-gap BCS pairing. They computed potential energy surfaces via constrained quadrupole moment calculations, then extracted binding energies, quadrupole deformation parameters $\beta_2$, root-mean-square radii, two-neutron separation energies, and $\alpha$-decay $Q$-values and half-lives for the isotopic chain $^{282-320}$122, comparing results with the Finite Range Droplet Model (FRDM) of Moller et al. (1997).

## Key numbers
- The most stable isotope (largest binding energy per nucleon) is $^{302}$122, not the experimentally observed $^{292}$122.
- For $^{292}$122, the RMF(NL3) potential energy surface shows minima at $\beta_2 = -0.436$, $-0.032$, and $0.523$, with energy differences of 0.48 MeV and 1.84 MeV between consecutive minima.
- For $^{292}$122, the SHF(SkI4) potential energy surface shows minima at $\beta_2 = -0.459$, $-0.159$, and $0.511$, with energy differences of 1.30 MeV and 0.48 MeV.
- The shape transition from highly oblate to highly prolate occurs at about A=290.
- The $\alpha$-decay half-life for $^{292}$122 is $T_{\alpha} = 10^{-7.23}$ s in SHF and $10^{-6.35}$ s in RMF.
- The two-neutron separation energy $S_{2n}$ for $^{294}$122 is 16.29 MeV (SHF) and 16.71 MeV (RMF); for $^{320}$122 it is 10.21 MeV (SHF) and 10.67 MeV (RMF).
- The binding energy for $^{302}$122 is 2123.01 MeV (SHF) and 2107.30 MeV (RMF); for $^{292}$122 it is 2028.81 MeV (SHF) and 2046.19 MeV (RMF).

## Context
This work extends theoretical studies of superheavy element stability beyond the predicted island of stability at Z=114, N=184 (Myers and Swiatecki, 1966) and later predictions for Z=120, N=172 or 184 (Rutz et al., 1997; Gupta et al., 1997; Patra et al.) and Z=124 or 126, N=184 (Cwiok et al., 1996; Kruppa et al., 2000). It was motivated by the possible experimental evidence for a long-lived superheavy nucleus $^{292}$122 in natural Thorium (Marinov et al., 2009), with estimated half-life $T_{1/2} \ge 10^8$ y and abundance (1-10)$\times 10^{-12}$ relative to $^{232}$Th. The results suggest that $^{302}$122 may be a more promising candidate for experimental synthesis than the observed $^{292}$122, and that the highly deformed ground states in this region could be related to the superdeformed and hyperdeformed states previously identified in neutron-deficient Th isotopes (Marinov et al., 2007).
