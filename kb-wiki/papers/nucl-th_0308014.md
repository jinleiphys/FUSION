# What did we learn and what will we learn from hydrodynamics at RHIC?

> A brief overview of the current status of hydrodynamic concepts applied to ultra relativistic heavy-ion collisions is presented. Special emphasis is placed on future prospects for extracting the thermodynamic properties and the bulk features of the created medium from experimental observations.

## Key claim

Hydrodynamic models successfully describe the strong collective flow observed at RHIC, but this success depends on the assumption of extremely rapid thermalization (within the first 1 fm/c) and an equation of state with a phase transition from QCD plasma to hadronic matter that has a soft transition region of width around 1 GeV/fm^3.

## Method

The paper reviews comparisons between experimental elliptic flow data from Au+Au collisions at RHIC and hydrodynamic calculations that solve the equations of relativistic fluid dynamics for the expanding fireball. The calculations use different equations of state (with and without a phase transition, and with varying latent heats) and treat the late hadronic stage either within a purely hydrodynamic framework or coupled to a microscopic transport model.

## Key numbers

- Elliptic flow data from Au+Au collisions at 130 AGeV (center-of-mass energy per nucleon pair) are compared to hydrodynamic calculations
- The latent heat of the transition region was varied among 0.4, 0.8, and 1.6 GeV/fm^3 in hydrodynamic calculations to study the centrality dependence of elliptic flow
- A transition width of approximately 1 GeV/fm^3 best describes the data
- Hydrodynamic calculations predict Fourier coefficients v_n up to order 8 for Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV with impact parameter b = 7 fm
- Deviations from ideal hydrodynamic behavior occur at pseudorapidities |eta| > 1

## Context

This review extends the work of Kolb and Heinz (2003) on hydrodynamic descriptions of heavy-ion collisions and builds on the initial observation of strong elliptic flow by the STAR collaboration (2001). The paper discusses how future measurements of higher-order flow harmonics, strange particle flow, and flow at forward rapidities can constrain the nuclear equation of state and probe the expected tri-critical point in the (T,mu) plane, as proposed by Fodor and Katz (2002). It also identifies the need to incorporate viscosity effects to resolve the HBT puzzle and to understand the background medium for hard probe studies.
