# NS 1987A in SN 1987A

> The possible detection of a compact object in the remnant of SN 1987A presents an unprecedented opportunity to follow its early evolution. The suspected detection stems from an excess of infrared emission from a dust blob near the compact object's predicted position. The infrared excess could be due to the decay of isotopes like 44Ti, accretion luminosity from a neutron star or black hole, magnetospheric emission or a wind originating from the spindown of a pulsar, or thermal emission from an embedded, cooling neutron star (NS 1987A). It is shown that the last possibility is the most plausible as the other explanations are disfavored by other observations and/or require fine-tuning of parameters. Not only are there indications the dust blob overlaps the predicted location of a kicked compact remnant, but its excess luminosity also matches the expected thermal power of a 30 year old neutron star. Furthermore, models of cooling neutron stars within the Minimal Cooling paradigm readily fit both NS 1987A and Cas A, the next-youngest known neutron star. If correct, a long heat transport timescale in the crust and a large effective stellar temperature are favored, implying relatively limited crustal n-1S0 superfluidity and an envelope with a thick layer of light elements, respectively. If the locations don't overlap, then pulsar spindown or accretion might be more likely, but the pulsar's period and magnetic field or the accretion rate must be rather finely tuned. In this case, NS 1987A may have enhanced cooling and/or a heavy-element envelope.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the most plausible explanation for the infrared excess from a dust blob in the SN 1987A remnant is thermal emission from an embedded, cooling neutron star (NS 1987A), but this conclusion depends on the spatial overlap between the blob and the compact object; if they do not overlap, pulsar spindown or accretion become more likely, though these require fine-tuning of parameters.

## Method

The authors modeled the cooling of a neutron star within the "Minimal Cooling" paradigm, which assumes no direct Urca neutrino emission, and compared the predicted thermal luminosity at an age of approximately 30 years to the observed blob luminosity of (40-90) solar luminosities. They also evaluated alternative explanations: heating by radioactive decay of 44Ti, accretion luminosity, magnetospheric emission or pulsar spindown, and thermal emission from a cooling neutron star, using constraints from the blob's optical depth to gamma rays, the observed upper limits on compact source luminosity in the remnant, and the distribution of known pulsars in the period-derivative diagram.

## Key numbers

* The dust blob luminosity is L_cs = (40-90) L_sun, equivalent to (1.5-3.5) x 10^35 erg/s.
* The blob has a temperature of approximately 33 K, compared to surrounding dust at 17-22 K.
* The required initial 44Ti mass in the blob to explain the luminosity via radioactive decay is at least (4.5-10.5) x 10^(-6) M_sun, which is 3%-7% of the total 44Ti ejected by SN 1987A (approximately 1.5 x 10^(-4) M_sun).
* The gamma-ray optical depth of the blob is tau_gamma less than about 1.3 x 10^(-2) for a blob nickel mass of 0.01 M_sun, implying efficient thermalization requires an implausible blob mass greater than about 0.77 M_sun.
* The inferred gravitational mass of the neutron star from progenitor models is M approximately (1.22-1.62) M_sun, and from neutrino observations is (1.38 +/- 0.43) M_sun.
* The upper limit on any compact source luminosity in the remnant is 138 L_sun (5.2 x 10^35 erg/s) in the presence of dust, and 22 L_sun without dust.
* The transverse kick velocity of the compact object is between 160 km/s and nearly 700 km/s.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Cigan (2019), who first reported the infrared excess from a dust blob in SN 1987A and suggested a compact object as the most likely explanation. The paper builds on neutron star cooling models developed by Page (2004, 2009, 2011) within the Minimal Cooling paradigm, and compares NS 1987A to the next-youngest known neutron star in Cassiopeia A. The analysis of the supernova kick and ejecta asymmetry draws on 3D explosion simulations by Janka (2017) and Wongwathanarat (2013). If confirmed, NS 1987A would provide an unprecedented opportunity to study the early thermal evolution of a neutron star and constrain crustal superfluidity and envelope composition.
