# Superheavy Elements and Ultradense Matter

> In order to characterize the mass density of superheavy elements, we solve numerically the relativistic Thomas-Fermi model of an atom. To obtain a range of mass densities for superheavy matter, this model is supplemented with an estimation of the number of electrons shared between individual atoms. Based on our computation, we expect that elements in the island of nuclear stability around $Z = 164$ will populate a mass density range of $36.0 - 68.4$ g/cm$^3$. We then extend our method to the study of macroscopic alpha particle nuclear matter condensate drops.

## Key claim

The authors predict that superheavy elements near the island of nuclear stability at \(Z = 164\) will have mass densities in the range \(36.0 - 68.4\) g/cm\(^3\), but this range depends on a linear extrapolation of the number of conduction-band electrons (\(\gamma\)) from known metals, and the actual density for any specific element depends on whether it behaves as a "typical" or "heavy" metal.

## Method

The authors solved the relativistic Thomas-Fermi model (RTFM) numerically for a spherically symmetric atom with a Fermi-distribution nuclear charge and a relativistic Fermi electron cloud. To obtain mass densities for superheavy elements, they first determined the effective net ionic charge \(\gamma\) (the number of electrons shared in the conduction band) for six known metals by matching RTFM-computed densities to experimental values, then linearly extrapolated \(\gamma\) to higher atomic numbers \(Z\). The mass density was computed from the nuclear mass (assuming \(A = 2.5Z\)) divided by the volume of the spherical atomic core defined by the radius where the electron density vanishes.

## Key numbers

- For \(Z = 164\), the predicted mass density range is \(36.0 - 68.4\) g/cm\(^3\), with typical-metal core radius \(R_{\mathrm{core}} = 1.656\) Å and heavy-metal core radius \(R_{\mathrm{core}} = 1.338\) Å.
- For \(Z = 114\), the predicted mass density range is \(19.3 - 39.9\) g/cm\(^3\).
- The asteroid 33 Polyhymnia has a measured mass density of \(\rho = 75.3 \pm 9.6\) g/cm\(^3\).
- For alpha matter with \(Z = 10^5\) and \(\gamma = 250\), mass densities range from 5350 g/cm\(^3\) (for nuclear radius \(R_N = 50000\) fm) to 2,240,000 g/cm\(^3\) (for \(R_N = 50\) fm).
- The extrapolated \(\gamma\) values for \(Z = 164\) are \(\gamma_{\mathrm{typical}} = 5.6\) and \(\gamma_{\mathrm{heavy}} = 8.5\).

## Context

This work extends prior RTFM studies by Muller and Rafelski (1974) and earlier Hartree-Fock calculations by Fricke (1971), who predicted a density of 46 g/cm\(^3\) for \(Z = 164\). The authors' density range for \(Z = 114\) (19.3 – 39.9 g/cm\(^3\)) is near but slightly higher than prior predictions by Fricke (1971) of 13.6 – 15.1 g/cm\(^3\) and by Gyanchandani (2011) of 14.4 – 27.3 g/cm\(^3\) for nearby elements. The work enables future study of whether superheavy elements or alpha-particle condensates could explain the composition of Compact Ultradense Objects (CUDOs) such as asteroid 33 Polyhymnia, and suggests that the RTFM can be extended to macroscopic alpha matter systems with very high \(Z\) where Hartree-Fock methods become computationally prohibitive.
