# Multifragmentation - what the data tell us about the different models

> We discuss what the presently collected data tell us about the mechanism of multifragmentation by comparing the results of two different models, which assume or show an opposite reaction scenario, with the recent high statistics $4π$ experiments performed by the INDRA collaboration. We find that the statistical multifragmentation model and the dynamical Quantum Molecular Dynamics approach produce almost the same results and agree both quite well with experiment. We discuss which observables may serve to overcome this deadlock on the quest for the reaction mechanism. Finally we proof that even if the system is in equilibrium, the fluctuation of the temperature due to the smallness of the system renders the caloric curve useless for the proof of a first order phase transition.

# Multifragmentation - what the data tell us about the different models

## Key claim
The statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) and the dynamical Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) approach produce almost the same results for key observables and both agree quite well with experiment, despite assuming opposite reaction scenarios. Even if the system were in equilibrium, temperature fluctuations due to the smallness of the system render the caloric curve useless for proving a first order phase transition.

## Method
The authors compare SMM (which assumes instantaneous disintegration from a statistical equilibrium at freeze-out) and QMD (which simulates the full time evolution of nucleon wave functions via a variational principle, never reaching equilibrium) against high-statistics 4π data from the INDRA collaboration for central 50 AMeV Xe + Sn collisions. Both model calculations are filtered with experimental acceptance and centrality cuts; SMM calculations are adjusted by adding a collective energy parameter E_coll = c*A, and QMD uses a minimum spanning tree for cluster identification. The comparison focuses on fragment kinetic energy spectra and charge yield distributions in the midrapidity region (60° ≤ θ_CM ≤ 120°), where a flat angular distribution suggests possible equilibrium.

## Key numbers
- For the SMM calculation best reproducing the INDRA data subset (θ_flow > 60°), the input parameters are: freeze-out density = 1/3 ρ₀, source size Z_S = 78, A_S = 186, thermal excitation energy E_thermal = 7A MeV, collective energy E_coll = 2.2A MeV.
- The temperature difference between the subsystem of fragments (Z ≥ 3) and light charged particles in SMM calculations fluctuates by about 1.7 MeV around the mean value of 0.
- The chemical potential difference between the two subsystems fluctuates by about 40% around the mean value of 0, in units of the temperature (assumed 7 MeV).
- For the FOPI experiment at 400 AMeV with equal-mass projectile/target combinations (Ru and Zr), the proton rapidity distribution shows no sign of equilibrium for y/y_beam > 0.25.

## Context
This work extends the long-standing debate between statistical and dynamical descriptions of multifragmentation, following earlier comparisons by the ALADIN collaboration (Begemann-Blaich et al., 1993) and the INDRA collaboration (Salou et al., 1997). The authors build on the observation that finite systems near phase transitions show power-law fragment distributions σ(A) ∝ A^τ (Fischer, 1967) that also appear in non-thermal processes (Jullien et al., 1992; Hu et al., 1995; Bak et al., 1987). The isospin tracing method proposed by the Texas group (Yen et al., 1995) and recently applied by the FOPI collaboration (Rami et al., 2000) is identified as a promising way to resolve the deadlock by testing equilibration via rapidity distributions of fragments from different projectile/target isospin combinations. The work also challenges the interpretation of the caloric curve by the ALADIN collaboration (Pochodzalla et al., 1995) as evidence for a first-order phase transition, arguing that temperature fluctuations in small systems (≈400 particles) make such conclusions unreliable.
