# Single particle spectrum and binding energy of nuclear matter

> In non-relativistic Brueckner calculations of nuclear matter, the self-consistent single particle potential is strongly momentum dependent. To simplify the calculations, a parabolic approximation is often used in the literature. The variation in the binding energy value introduced by the parabolic approximation is quantitatively analyzed in detail. It is found that the approximation can introduce an uncertainty of 1-2 MeV near the saturation density.

## Key claim

The parabolic (effective mass) approximation for the single particle potential in self-consistent Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) calculations introduces an uncertainty of 1-2 MeV in the binding energy near saturation density, and the approximation distorts the saturation curves, meaning that any apparent improvement in the saturation point is a spurious effect.

## Method

The authors performed fully self-consistent BHF calculations of nuclear matter using the Argonne v18 potential, including all two-body channels up to total angular momentum J = 11 hbar, with the single particle potential calculated up to a momentum cut-off of 7.5 fm^-1. They then repeated the calculations by forcing the single particle potential to a parabolic shape through a fitting procedure at each iteration step, using two different fitting ranges (k_FIT = 2 k_F and k_FIT = k_max), and compared the resulting binding energies and saturation curves to the fully self-consistent results.

## Key numbers

- The parabolic approximation introduces an uncertainty of 1-2 MeV in the binding energy near the saturation density (Fermi momentum k_F = 1.4 fm^-1).
- The fully self-consistent single particle potential was calculated up to a momentum cut-off of k_max = 7.5 fm^-1 with a grid step of 0.1 fm^-1.
- The binding energy stability under iteration was systematically reached within a few keV.
- The calculations used the Argonne v18 potential and included all two-body channels up to total angular momentum J = 11 hbar.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing Bethe-Brueckner-Goldstone (BBG) many-body approach to nuclear matter, specifically the BHF approximation with the continuous choice advocated by the Liege group (Jeukenne, Lejeune, and Mahaux, 1976). The paper quantitatively tests the common parabolic (effective mass) approximation used in many BHF calculations, such as those by Schiller, Muther, and Czerski (1999), and demonstrates that while the approximation is useful for rough estimates, it is not reliable for accurate calculations that aim to test nuclear Hamiltonians or resolve the Coester band discrepancy with empirical saturation (binding energy per nucleon of -16 MeV at density 0.17 fm^-3). The results enable future precise BHF calculations to retain the full momentum dependence of the single particle potential.
