# Unitary Fermi gas at finite temperature in the epsilon expansion

> Thermodynamics of the unitary Fermi gas at finite temperature is investigated from the perspective of the expansion over epsilon=4-d with d being the dimensionality of space. We show that the thermodynamics is dominated by bosonic excitations in the low temperature region T<<Tc. Analytic formulas for the thermodynamic functions as functions of the temperature are derived to the lowest order in epsilon in this region. In the high temperature region where T Tc, bosonic and fermionic quasiparticles are excited. We determine the critical temperature Tc of the superfluid phase transition and the thermodynamic functions around Tc to the leading and next-to-leading orders in epsilon.

## Key claim
The thermodynamics of the unitary Fermi gas at finite temperature can be studied analytically using an expansion in $\epsilon = 4-d$, where $d$ is the spatial dimension, treating $\epsilon$ as a small parameter. The results are derived to leading and next-to-leading order in $\epsilon$, but the physical case of three spatial dimensions ($\epsilon=1$) requires extrapolation or matching with an expansion around two dimensions.

## Method
The paper extends the $\epsilon$ expansion formalism, previously applied at zero temperature, to finite temperature using the imaginary time formalism. The system is described by a Lagrangian for weakly interacting fermionic and bosonic quasiparticles, with a coupling constant $g \sim \epsilon^{1/2}$. Thermodynamic functions are calculated via Feynman diagrams, with a power-counting rule for $\epsilon$ that breaks down near the critical temperature $T_c$, requiring a minor modification. The critical temperature is determined by matching the $\epsilon$ expansion with an expansion around two spatial dimensions.

## Key numbers
*   The critical temperature for the superfluid phase transition, to leading order in $\epsilon$, is given by $T_c = \mu_B / (\epsilon \, 2 \ln 2) + O(\epsilon)$, where $\mu_B$ is the boson chemical potential.
*   In the low temperature region ($T \ll \epsilon \phi_0$), the phonon contribution to the pressure is $P_\mathrm{ph} \simeq \frac{12\zeta(5)}{\pi^2} \frac{m^2 T^5}{(\epsilon \phi_0)^2}$.
*   In the intermediate temperature region ($\epsilon \phi_0 \ll T \ll \phi_0$), the phonon contribution to the pressure is $P_\mathrm{ph} \simeq \frac{\zeta(3)}{\pi^2} m^2 T^3 - \frac{m^2 T^2}{12} \epsilon \phi_0$.
*   The sound velocity of the phonon is $c_\mathrm{s} = \sqrt{\frac{\epsilon \phi_0}{4m}} + O(\epsilon^{3/2}) \simeq v_\mathrm{F} \sqrt{\epsilon^{3/2}/8}$.
*   The pressure at the critical temperature, up to next-to-leading order in $\epsilon$, is $P = T \left[ \frac{11}{2} \zeta(3) - \frac{9\ln 2 \, \zeta(3) + 11\zeta'(3)}{4} \epsilon - \epsilon C_P + \frac{\pi^2}{6} \frac{\mu}{T} + \frac{2\pi^2}{3} \frac{\mu_B}{T} \right] \left( \frac{mT}{2\pi} \right)^{d/2}$, where $C_P \approx 8.4144$.

## Context
This work extends the author's previous $\epsilon$ expansion approach for the unitary Fermi gas at zero temperature (Nishida and Son, 2006) to finite temperatures. It provides an analytic, systematic alternative to prior numerical and mean-field studies, such as Monte Carlo simulations (Wingate, 2005; Bulgac, 2005; Lee and Schafer, 2005; Burovski, 2006) and the virial expansion (Ho, 2004; Horowitz and Rupak, 2005). The results enable analytic calculations of thermodynamic functions below and above $T_c$, and the determination of $T_c$ by matching the $\epsilon$ expansion with an expansion around two spatial dimensions.
