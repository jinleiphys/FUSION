# The diffuse supernova neutrino background: an update with modern population synthesis and core-collapse simulations

> We present a new, state-of-the-art computation of the Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background (DSNB), where we use neutrino spectra from multi-dimensional, multi-second core collapse supernova simulations - including both neutron-star and black-hole forming collapses - and binary evolution effects from modern population synthesis codes. Large sets of numerical results are processed and connected in a consistent manner, using two key quantities: the mass of the star's Carbon-Oxygen (CO) core at an advanced pre-collapse stage - which depends on binary evolution effects - and the compactness parameter, which is the main descriptor of the post-collapse neutrino emission. The method enables us to model the neutrino emission of a very diverse, binary-affected population of stars, which cannot unambiguously be mapped in detail by existing core collapse simulations. We find that including black hole-forming collapses enhances the DSNB by up to 50% at energies greater than 30-40 MeV. Binary evolution effects can change the total rate of collapses and generate a sub-population of high core mass stars that are stronger neutrino emitters. However, the net effect on the DSNB is moderate - up to a 15% increase in flux - due to the rarity of these super-massive cores and to the relatively modest dependence of the neutrino emission on the CO core mass. The methodology presented here is suitable for extensions and generalizations, and therefore it lays the foundation for modern treatments of the DSNB.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new computation of the Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background (DSNB) that uses neutrino spectra from multi-dimensional, multi-second core-collapse simulations and binary evolution effects from modern population synthesis codes, finding that black-hole-forming collapses enhance the DSNB by up to 50% at energies above 30-40 MeV, while binary evolution effects produce only a moderate net increase of up to 15% in flux due to the rarity of super-massive cores and the modest dependence of neutrino emission on CO core mass.

## Method

The authors connect two sets of numerical results using the mass of the star's Carbon-Oxygen (CO) core at an advanced pre-collapse stage and the compactness parameter (ξ₂.₅ = (M_e/M_⊙)/(R/1000 km) with M_e=2.5 M_⊙) as the main descriptor of post-collapse neutrino emission. They use neutrino spectra from 100 multi-second 2D core-collapse simulations by Vartanyan et al. (2023), including both neutron-star-forming and black-hole-forming collapses, and binary population synthesis results from two codes (BPASS v2.2 and the Kinugawa et al. model) to model the neutrino emission of a binary-affected population of stars.

## Key numbers

- Black-hole-forming collapses enhance the DSNB by up to 50% at energies greater than 30-40 MeV
- Binary evolution effects produce a net increase in DSNB flux of up to 15%
- The core-collapse simulations extend up to 4 seconds post-bounce
- Black hole formation appears for ZAMS mass M ≃ 12-15 M_⊙ in the simulations of Vartanyan et al. (2023)
- The correction to total neutrino energy from extrapolating beyond 3.5 seconds is 0-35%, with ~20% being the most common outcome
- The average and rms energies of ν̄_e are slightly higher (by ~0.5 MeV) than those of ν_x, in contrast with most previous literature
- The spectral shape parameter α_w varies between 1.0 and 2.1 across the compactness range

## Context

This work extends the exploratory studies of Horiuchi et al. (2018) and Horiuchi et al. (2021), which first applied the CO-core mass to compactness mapping to DSNB calculations but used fewer progenitors (20) and shorter simulation times (t < 1 s). The methodology enables consistent interfacing of stellar evolution simulations (including binary effects from BPASS and the Kinugawa et al. code) with core-collapse neutrino emission models, and is designed to be extensible as new numerical simulations become available.
