# Production mechanism of hot nuclei in violent collisions in the Fermi energy domain

> A production mechanism of highly excited nuclei formed in violent collisions in the Fermi energy domain is investigated. The collision of two nuclei is decomposed into several stages which are treated separately. Simplified exciton concept is used for the description of pre-equilibrium emission. A modified spectator-participant scenario is used where motion along classical Coulomb trajectories is assumed. The participant and one of the spectator zones undergo incomplete fusion. Excitation energies of both cold and hot fragment are determined. Results of the calculation are compared to recent experimental data in the Fermi energy domain. Data on hot projectile-like, mid-velocity and fusion-like sources are described consistently. Geometric aspects of pre-equilibrium emission are revealed. Explanations to previously unexplained experimental phenomena are given. Energy deposited into non-thermal degrees of freedom is estimated.

## Key claim

The paper presents a phenomenological model that decomposes violent heavy-ion collisions in the Fermi energy domain (20–100 A MeV) into separate stages (pre-equilibrium emission, spectator-participant formation along classical Coulomb trajectories, and incomplete fusion) and claims that this approach can consistently describe experimental data on hot projectile-like, mid-velocity, and fusion-like sources, including previously unexplained phenomena such as large mass variances and asymmetric excitation energy sharing. The hedge is that the model uses simplified assumptions (e.g., a sharp cutoff between deep-inelastic and violent collisions at 3 fm overlap) and free parameters (σ = 0.22, κ = 0.3) that are tuned to match specific data sets, and the author notes discrepancies in transition regions between deep-inelastic and incomplete fusion scenarios.

## Method

The collision is decomposed into three stages treated separately in a Monte Carlo event-by-event fashion: (1) pre-equilibrium emission using a simplified exciton model where the emission probability depends on the ratio of current to equilibrium exciton number, with particles emitted isotropically from a fireball moving at half the projectile velocity; (2) reconstruction of the post-pre-equilibrium projectile-target system and determination of participant and spectator zones using a classical Coulomb trajectory with the minimum distance as the principal geometric parameter, where the participant zone volume is chosen randomly between the abrasion-ablation and two-sphere overlap limits; (3) incomplete fusion where the participant zone is captured by the spectator with more neighboring nucleons (within 1 fm of the separation plane), forming a hot fragment, with excitation energy determined from collisions of spectator and participant nucleons along the separation plane and kinetic energy/angle from a Serber-approximation double differential cross section formula. For events with nuclear overlap ≤ 3 fm, the deep-inelastic transfer (DIT) code of Tassan-Got is used instead of the violent-collision scenario.

## Key numbers

* Pre-equilibrium neutron multiplicities in 35 A MeV ⁴⁰,⁴⁸Ca+¹¹²Sn reactions: experimental values significantly higher than predictions by the nucleon exchange model (NEM); calculated values using σ = 0.22 and κ = 0.3 show good agreement for more central excitation energy bins.
* Pre-equilibrium neutron multiplicities in 11–30 A MeV ²⁰Ne+¹⁶⁵Ho: calculated values using σ = 0.22 and κ = 0.3 show reasonable overall agreement with experimental data from Holub and Hilscher.
* Probability of emitting four pre-equilibrium nucleons in coincidence with evaporation residues (Z ≥ 87) in 15 A MeV ²⁰Ne+²⁰⁸Pb: calculated value 0.25 versus experimental estimate 0.3; for six pre-equilibrium particles: calculated 0.07 versus experimental 0.1.
* Pre-equilibrium proton multiplicities in ³⁰ and ⁵⁰ A MeV ²⁸Si+¹¹²Sn peripheral collisions: experimental estimates 0.2 (30 A MeV) and 0.3 (50 A MeV) correspond to calculated values 0.25 and 0.34, respectively.
* Mean free path λ adopted as 6 fm for excitation energy calculation of the cold fragment.
* Threshold radial velocity for pre-equilibrium emission stopping: 4 A MeV.
* For the reaction ¹⁹⁷Au(20 A MeV)+ⁿᵃᵗTi, calculated charge centroids and velocity centroids of heavy evaporation residues follow experimental trends; typical contributing scenario involves emission of up to three pre-equilibrium neutrons followed by deep-inelastic transfer and de-excitation with excitation energies not exceeding 3 A MeV.
* For the reaction ⁹³Nb+¹¹⁶Sn at 25 A MeV, calculated mass variances σ_A² are comparable to experimental values from Casini (2000); the incomplete fusion mechanism explains both large mass variances and asymmetric excitation energy sharing.
* For four reactions at 47 A MeV (¹²C+¹¹⁶Sn, ²⁰Ne+¹⁰⁸Ag, ⁴⁰Ar+¹⁰⁰Mo, ⁶⁴Zn+⁸⁹Y), calculated distributions of excitation energy versus mass of the heavy fusion-like source are compared to data from Hagel; only the most central events (10 % of reaction cross section) are included.

## Context

This work extends the deep-inelastic transfer (DIT) code of Tassan-Got (1993) by adding a pre-equilibrium emission stage and a spectator-participant incomplete fusion mechanism for violent collisions, addressing limitations of the nucleon exchange model (NEM) of Randrup and Vandenbosch (1975) which systematically underestimated pre-equilibrium particle multiplicities. The model provides a phenomenological explanation for anomalies reported by Casini (2000) in ⁹³Nb+¹¹⁶Sn reactions (large mass variances and asymmetric excitation energy sharing that could not be explained by nucleon exchange through a window) and offers a natural extension of the DIT code for reactions in the Fermi energy domain. The approach enables consistent description of hot projectile-like, mid-velocity, and fusion-like sources across multiple experimental data sets, and reveals that pre-equilibrium emission can be understood as two-body dissipation along classical Coulomb trajectories where nucleons scattered in the radial direction are emitted, with the pre-equilibrium stage stopping when radial motion disappears.
