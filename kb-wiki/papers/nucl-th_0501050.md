# Metastable and stable equilibrium states of stellar electron-nuclear plasmas

> By minimizing free energy density, we show that the stellar core of a hydrogen burning star is not in a global thermodynamical equilibrium unless density, temperature, mass and composition assume given values. The core (as the solar interior) may be viewed more appropriately as a metastable state with very long lifetime. Slightly non-extensive distribution function could be the natural distribution for a weakly non-ideal plasma like a stellar core and represents a more appropriate approximation to this system than a Maxwellian distribution, without affecting bulk properties of stars.

## Key claim
The stellar core of a hydrogen-burning star like the Sun is not in a globally thermodynamically stable equilibrium state; it is better described as a metastable state with a very long lifetime, and a slightly non-extensive (Tsallis) distribution function may be a more appropriate approximation than a Maxwellian for this weakly non-ideal plasma, without altering the bulk properties of the star.

## Method
The authors minimized the free energy density of a non-relativistic, hot, dense electron-nuclear plasma, including corrections for quantum exclusion (fermion identity) and Coulomb correlation effects, following the approach of Landau and Lifshitz and Vasiliev. They derived two coupled equations linking electron density, temperature, and average chemical composition: one from the free energy density minimum condition and one from the total energy density minimum condition (including the virial theorem and black-body radiation). Solving these equations for a solar-like composition (average Z = 1.25) yielded the specific density, temperature, and core radius required for global thermodynamic equilibrium.

## Key numbers
- Global thermodynamic equilibrium for a Sun-like star requires an electron density of \( n_* \simeq 2.74 \times 10^{-14} \, \mathrm{fm}^{-3} \) and a thermal energy of \( \mathrm{k_B} T_* \simeq 5 \times 10^{-3} \, \mathrm{MeV} \) (about 5 keV).
- The equilibrium core radius for a star of solar mass would be \( R_* \simeq 0.21 \, R_{\odot} \), much smaller than the actual solar core.
- The number of particles inside a Debye sphere in the solar core is \( N_{\mathrm{D}} \approx 4 \), which is very small for applying Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics without criticism.
- The non-extensive parameter \( q \) required to achieve equilibrium in the Debye sphere is \( q \approx 1.001 \), a deviation of about 0.1% from unity.

## Context
This work extends the approach of Landau and Lifshitz and Vasiliev (2004) by minimizing free energy density to identify global equilibrium conditions for stellar plasmas, rather than assuming local thermodynamic equilibrium. It contradicts the common assumption in standard solar models (Bahcall, 2001; Castellani, 2003) that the solar core is in full thermodynamic equilibrium, instead supporting the view of Sakagami and Taruya (2004) that such systems are better described by non-extensive thermostatistics. The results enable future studies of how small deviations from Maxwellian distributions (e.g., from temperature fluctuations or self-generated magnetic fields as discussed by Li and Zhang, 2003) can affect nuclear fusion rates and neutrino fluxes without changing bulk stellar properties.
