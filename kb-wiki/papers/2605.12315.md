# Natural and Dyson orbitals in small helium drops

> The natural and Dyson orbitals are studied for small helium drops comprising 5 to 20 helium atoms interacting via a soft two-body gaussian potential. The wave functions of these drops have been obtained in the hyperspherical cluster model (HCM) which provides a correct description of the single-particle behaviour at large separations from the system. The natural orbitals are obtained from diagonalization of the nonlocal one-body density matrix, while Dyson orbitals are constructed by direct overlap of the wave functions of two drops differing by one boson. This overlap converges with increasing basis of the HCM. The shapes and occupancies of the natural orbitals as well as their link to Dyson overlaps and evolution with increasing number of atoms are discussed. Both natural and Dyson orbitals can be used to represent the density of the system. However, the natural orbitals representation is demonstrated to be superior. With increasing boson numbers the difference between Dyson and natural orbitals becomes less prominent and it is expected to disappear in infinitely large systems of identical bosons.

# Natural and Dyson orbitals in small helium drops

## Key claim
The natural orbitals and Dyson orbitals for small helium drops (5 to 20 atoms) share the same asymptotic decay rate determined by the separation energy, but the natural orbitals representation of the system density is superior to the Dyson orbital representation; the difference between the two orbital types becomes less prominent with increasing boson number and is expected to disappear in infinitely large systems of identical bosons.

## Method
The wave functions of helium drops with 5 to 20 atoms were obtained using the hyperspherical cluster model (HCM) with a soft two-body Gaussian potential. Natural orbitals were computed by diagonalizing the translation-invariant nonlocal one-body density matrix, while Dyson orbitals were constructed by direct overlap of wave functions of two drops differing by one boson. The HCM basis expansion was used to calculate the nonlocal density matrix elements, and the resulting eigenvalue problem was solved using a finite difference approach.

## Key numbers
- Helium drops studied: A = 5, 6, 8, 10, and 20 atoms
- Two-body Gaussian potential parameters: V0 = -1.227 K, a0 = 10.03 a.u.
- hbar^2/m = 41.281307 (a.u.)^2 K
- Ground state 0s orbital occupancy for A=5: 4.97; for A=20: 19.997
- Ground state 1s orbital occupancy for A=5: 1.39e-2; for A=20: 1.48e-3
- First excited state 0s orbital occupancy for A=5: 3.50; for A=20: 18.94
- First excited state 1s orbital occupancy for A=5: 1.00; for A=20: 1.05
- Dyson orbital expansion coefficient c0 for <5(g.s.)|4(g.s.)>: 1.546
- Dyson orbital expansion coefficient c0 for <20(g.s.)|19(g.s.)>: 4.116
- Asymptotic convergence of Dyson orbitals for ground-state final states achieved after r ~ 25 a.u.
- For <5(1ex)|4(1ex)> overlap, asymptotic form not reached even at r ~ 80 a.u.

## Context
This work extends the HCM approach previously developed for fermions (Timofeyuk 2007) and reformulated for bosons (Timofeyuk 2023) to enable calculations of the translation-invariant nonlocal one-body density matrix for the first time in systems with more than four particles. It builds on earlier studies of Dyson orbitals in small helium drops (Timofeyuk 2023) and natural orbitals in three-body systems (Suzuki 2002). The finding that all natural orbitals with the same angular momentum share identical asymptotic behavior confirms earlier observations for fermionic systems (Van Neck 1996). The pre-asymptotic abnormalities in Dyson orbitals for excited final states were first predicted for weakly-bound nuclei (Timofeyuk 2003) and confirmed by three-body calculations (Timofeyuk 2008a, 2008b). This work enables future studies of single-particle structure in bosonic systems and clarifies the relationship between natural and Dyson orbitals as system size increases toward the thermodynamic limit.
