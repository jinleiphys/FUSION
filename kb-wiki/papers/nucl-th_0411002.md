# Neutrino accompanied beta^-beta^-, beta^+beta^+, beta^+/EC and EC/EC processes within single state dominance hypothesis

> The systematic study of (anti-)neutrino accompanied beta^-beta^-, beta^+beta^+ decays and beta^+/EC, EC/EC electron captures is performed under the assumption of single intermediate nuclear state dominance. The corresponding half-lives are evaluated both for transitions to the ground state as well as to the 0^+ and 2^+ excited states of final nucleus. It is stressed that the hypothesis of single state dominance can be confirmed or ruled out by the precision measurements of the differential characteristics of the 2nbb-decays of 100Mo and 116Cd as well as beta^+/EC electron capture in 106Cd, 130Ba and 136Ce.

## Key claim

The paper systematically evaluates half-lives for all neutrino-accompanied double beta decay modes (beta⁻beta⁻, beta⁺beta⁺, beta⁺/EC, EC/EC) under the single state dominance (SSD) hypothesis, which assumes the decay proceeds solely through the 1⁺ ground state of the intermediate nucleus. The authors stress that the SSD hypothesis can be confirmed or ruled out by precision measurements of differential energy spectra, particularly for ¹⁰⁰Mo, ¹¹⁶Cd, ¹⁰⁶Cd, ¹³⁰Ba, and ¹³⁶Ce.

## Method

The authors derived half-life formulas for all four neutrino-accompanied double beta decay modes within the SSD hypothesis, using experimental log ft values from single beta decay and electron capture to determine the relevant Gamow-Teller matrix elements in a model-independent way. They treated the energy denominators exactly (without the common approximation of replacing lepton energies with their average values) and evaluated transitions to both ground state and 0⁺ and 2⁺ excited states of the final nucleus. The SSD predictions were contrasted with the alternative higher state dominance (HSD) hypothesis, where contributions from excited intermediate states dominate and the lepton-energy dependence of denominators is averaged out.

## Context

This work extends the SSD hypothesis originally proposed by Abad et al. (1984) for 2νββ-decay, and builds on earlier SSD studies by Griffiths and Vogel (1993) and Civitarese and Suhonen (1998) that used the approximation of replacing lepton energies with their average values. The authors follow the approach of Semenov et al. (2000) who showed this approximation can be inadequate for systems like A=100 and A=116 where Δ is small. The paper enables experimental tests of the SSD hypothesis through differential rate measurements in the NEMO 3 experiment and other facilities, and provides a systematic framework for evaluating all neutrino-accompanied double beta decay modes within a single, model-independent formalism.
