# Impact of Nucleon-Nucleon Bremsstrahlung Rates Beyond One-Pion Exchange

> Neutrino-pair production and annihilation through nucleon-nucleon bremsstrahlung is included in current supernova simulations by rates that are based on the one-pion-exchange approximation. Here we explore the consequences of bremsstrahlung rates based on a modern nuclear interactions for proto-neutron star cooling and the corresponding neutrino emission. We find that despite a reduction of the bremsstrahlung emission by a factor of 2-5 in the neutrinospheric region, models with the improved treatment exhibit only $\lesssim$5% changes of the neutrino luminosities and an increase of $\lesssim$0.7 MeV of the average energies of the radiated neutrino spectra, with the largest effects for the antineutrinos of all flavors and at late times. Overall, the proto-neutron star cooling evolution is slowed down modestly by $\lesssim$0.5-1 s.

# Impact of Nucleon-Nucleon Bremsstrahlung Rates Beyond One-Pion Exchange

## Key claim

Despite a reduction of the nucleon-nucleon bremsstrahlung emission by a factor of 2–5 in the neutrinospheric region when using T-matrix rates based on modern nuclear interactions instead of the standard one-pion-exchange approximation, the resulting changes to neutrino luminosities are only ≲5% and average neutrino energies increase by ≲0.7 MeV, with the proto-neutron star cooling evolution slowed by ≲0.5–1 s. The correction factor used in this study is constructed to produce upper bounds on the possible changes compared to the standard Hannestad and Raffelt (1998) rate.

## Method

The authors calculated energy-averaged inverse mean-free paths for neutrino-pair annihilation via nucleon-nucleon bremsstrahlung using a T-matrix-based formalism with phase shifts from the Nijmegen partial-wave analysis (Stoks et al., 1993), relative to one-pion-exchange neutron-only results. They derived a one-dimensional analytical correction factor as a function of density, parametrized with a temperature-density relation T(ρ) = 3 MeV (ρ/10¹¹ g cm⁻³)^{1/3} and fixed electron fraction values (0.01, 0.05, 0.1), then implemented this factor by multiplying the standard Hannestad and Raffelt (1998) rates in one-dimensional simulations of a 9.6 M⊙ and a 27 M⊙ progenitor using the Prometheus-Vertex code with proto-neutron star convection treated via a mixing-length approach.

## Key numbers

- Bremsstrahlung emission reduction factor of 2–5 in the neutrinospheric region (density regime between ~10¹² g cm⁻³ and ~10¹⁴ g cm⁻³) when comparing T-matrix rates to the one-pion-exchange Hannestad and Raffelt (1998) rate.
- Neutrino luminosity changes of ≲5% for all flavors with the improved treatment.
- Increase of average radiated neutrino energies by ≲0.7 MeV, with largest effects for antineutrinos of all flavors at late times.
- Proto-neutron star cooling evolution slowed by ≲0.5–1 s.
- For the 27 M⊙ progenitor, radiated mean energies of ν_{μ,τ} and \barν_{μ,τ} increased by up to ~0.5 MeV at late times.
- For the 27 M⊙ progenitor, ν_e and \barν_e luminosities increased by up to ~4% at t ≳ 1 s after bounce.
- For the 27 M⊙ progenitor, mean energies of ν_e and \barν_e increased by up to ~0.7 MeV with bigger differences for \barν_e.
- Correction factor extrapolated to a constant value of 0.14 for densities beyond saturation density ρ₀ = 2.8×10¹⁴ g cm⁻³.
- The 9.6 M⊙ progenitor produced a neutron star with baryonic mass ~1.363 M⊙ (gravitational ~1.252 M⊙); the 27 M⊙ progenitor produced a neutron star with baryonic mass ~1.776 M⊙ (final gravitational ~1.592 M⊙).

## Context

This work extends the T-matrix-based nucleon-nucleon bremsstrahlung calculations for neutron-proton mixtures by Bartl et al. (2014) and the earlier chiral effective field theory studies by Bacca et al. (2009, 2012) into full supernova simulations, comparing against the standard one-pion-exchange rates of Hannestad and Raffelt (1998) that are widely used in supernova codes. The results are consistent with a parallel independent study by Fischer (2016) for an 18 M⊙ progenitor, though Fischer’s models omitted proto-neutron star convection. The findings enable future work to investigate whether conclusions about the negligible impact of inelastic scattering (νNN → νNN) hold when using more sophisticated calculations with consistent recoil effects.
