# Medium Modified Cross Sections, Temperature and Finite Momentum Effects for Antikaon Production in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The medium modifications of antikaons in dense matter are studied in a coupled channel calculation for scenarios more closely related to the environment encountered in heavy-ion collisions. We find that the optical potential of the antikaons turns repulsive or is drastically reduced for finite momenta or finite temperature. Hence, the antikaon mass does not decrease substantially in heavy-ion collisions to provide an explanation for the observed antikaon production rates at threshold. We demonstrate that the in-medium production cross section of antikaons via pions and Sigma hyperons is remarkably enhanced up to an order of magnitude. The effect of in medium correction on the resulting kaon spectra is studied within a transport model. We find that both in medium correction lead to about the same enhancement of the spectrum. However, once the temperature dependence is taken into account no enhancement is found.

# Medium Modified Cross Sections, Temperature and Finite Momentum Effects for Antikaon Production in Heavy-Ion Collisions

## Key claim

The antikaon optical potential in dense matter is repulsive or drastically reduced at finite momenta (above ~250 MeV) and finite temperatures (above ~30 MeV), so that the antikaon mass does not decrease substantially in heavy-ion collisions; instead, the in-medium production cross section of antikaons via pions and Sigma hyperons is enhanced up to an order of magnitude, but this enhancement is washed out once temperature dependence is taken into account.

## Method

The authors performed a coupled channel calculation using a nonperturbative separable potential model with SU(3) flavor symmetric coupling matrices for the Kbar-N and pi-Sigma/pi-Lambda channels, including Pauli blocking effects in the nuclear medium. They computed the antikaon optical potential and in-medium cross sections at finite momentum and finite temperature, then implemented these medium modifications into a BUU transport model to simulate Ni+Ni collisions at 1.8 AGeV and compare with experimental K- spectra from the Kaos collaboration.

## Key numbers

- At normal nuclear density rho_0 and zero momentum, the selfconsistently calculated optical potential is U_K(rho_0) = -32 MeV, in contrast to standard values from kaonic atoms
- The critical temperature at which the optical potential changes sign at normal nuclear density is around T = 30 MeV
- At T = 90 MeV, the optical potential is repulsive for all densities up to 2 rho_0
- The elastic cross section K-p -> K-p at rho_0 is enhanced by a factor of 4 at threshold and up to a factor of 27 around antikaon momentum near the Fermi momentum
- The pi-Sigma -> K-p production cross sections are enhanced by a factor of 20-30 at antikaon momentum around 250 MeV
- At finite relative momentum p = 450 MeV, the cross section enhancement factor is still about 3 at threshold
- At T = 90 MeV, an enhancement factor of 2-3 remains at threshold for pi-Sigma -> K-p
- The selfconsistent calculation reduces the maximum enhancement factors for pi-Sigma channels to 2.5-3 for the pi-Sigma+ channel at threshold

## Context

This work extends the coupled channel calculations of Koch (1994) and Ohnishi (1997) for antikaons in dense matter, and directly addresses the interpretation of enhanced K- production observed at GSI by the Kaos collaboration (1997) and FOPI collaboration (1996). Previous explanations by Li (1994, 1996, 1997) and Cassing (1997) attributed the enhancement to a reduction of the antikaon effective mass, but those calculations assumed the attractive potential was independent of momentum. The authors show that finite momentum and temperature effects, which are relevant for heavy-ion collisions where antikaons have average momenta above 300 MeV and temperatures around 80-90 MeV, eliminate the mass reduction mechanism. Instead, they identify enhanced in-medium production cross sections via Sigma hyperons as a possible alternative, though temperature effects ultimately wash out even this enhancement. The work enables future transport model studies to incorporate momentum and temperature dependent medium modifications rather than relying on zero-temperature, zero-momentum approximations.
