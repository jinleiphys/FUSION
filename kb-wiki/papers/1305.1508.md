# New equations of state based on the liquid drop model of heavy nuclei and quantum approach to light nuclei for core-collapse supernova simulations

> We construct new equations of state for baryons at sub-nuclear densities for the use in core-collapse simulations of massive stars. The abundance of various nuclei is obtained together with thermodynamic quantities. A model free energy is constructed, based on the relativistic mean field theory for nucleons and the mass formula for nuclei with the proton number up to ~ 1000. The formulation is an extension of the previous model, in which we adopted the liquid drop model to all nuclei under the nuclear statistical equilibrium. We reformulate the new liquid drop model so that the temperature dependences of bulk energies could be taken into account. Furthermore, we extend the region in the nuclear chart, in which shell affects are included, by using theoretical mass data in addition to experimental ones. We also adopt a quantum theoretical mass evaluation of light nuclei, which incorporates the Pauli- and self-energy shifts that are not included in the ordinary liquid drop model. The pasta phases for heavy nuclei are taken into account in the same way as in the previous model. We find that the abundances of heavy nuclei are modified by the shell effects of nuclei and temperature dependence of bulk energies. These changes may have an important effect on the rates of electron captures and coherent neutrino scatterings on nuclei in supernova cores. The abundances of light nuclei are also modified by the new mass evaluation, which may affect the heating and cooling rates of supernova cores and shocked envelopes.

## Key claim

The new equations of state improve on the authors' previous model by incorporating temperature-dependent bulk energies for all heavy nuclei and using theoretical mass data to extend shell-effect coverage, but the critical density at which shell effects decay (10^12 g/cm^3) is chosen arbitrarily and the interpolation for shell-energy suppression makes the free energy not smooth, with pressure discontinuities that are assumed negligible.

## Method

The authors construct a model free energy for baryons at sub-nuclear densities under nuclear statistical equilibrium, combining relativistic mean field theory (TM1 parameter set) for free nucleons, a liquid drop model for heavy nuclei (proton number Z up to 1000) with separate treatment of shell, bulk, Coulomb, and surface energies, and a quantum statistical approach for light nuclei (Z ≤ 5) that includes Pauli and self-energy shifts. The free energy is minimized with respect to number densities of nuclei and nucleons under baryon number and charge conservation, solving for chemical potentials and local nucleon densities simultaneously. Pasta phases are included via interpolation between droplet and bubble states when the nuclear volume fraction reaches 0.3 and 0.7.

## Key numbers

- Heavy nuclei are considered with proton number up to ~1000 and neutron number up to 1000.
- Theoretical mass data from Koura (2005) covers 15134 nuclei without experimental mass data.
- The critical density for onset of shell-effect suppression is set at 10^12 g/cm^3, with linear interpolation to zero at the saturation density of symmetric nuclei.
- Surface tension parameters: σ₀ = 1.15 MeV/fm³ and S_s = 45.8 MeV, adopted from Lattimer (1991).
- Saturation densities of light nuclei d, t, h, and α are set to a constant 0.15 fm⁻³.
- The Pauli energy shift formula for light nuclei is valid for T ≲ 20 MeV and n_B ≲ 0.16 fm⁻³.
- The criterion for pasta phase transitions uses volume fraction thresholds of 0.3 (droplet to intermediate) and 0.7 (intermediate to bubble).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous equation of state (Furusawa 2011) by adding temperature-dependent bulk energies for all heavy nuclei and incorporating theoretical mass data from Koura (2005) alongside experimental data from Audi (2003), addressing unphysical jumps in isotope distributions seen in the earlier model. It contrasts with the single-nucleus approximation used in the Lattimer-Swesty (1991) and Shen (1998) equations of state, and with the Hempel (2010) model that excludes nuclei with Z ≳ 100. The new treatment of light nuclei as quasi-particles following Typel (2010) improves on the liquid-drop description used previously, and the results may affect electron capture rates and neutrino scattering in supernova simulations.
