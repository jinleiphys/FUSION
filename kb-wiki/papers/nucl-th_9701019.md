# Coherent eta-photoproduction from nuclei in a relativistic impulse approximation approach

> We study coherent eta-photoproduction from nuclei in a relativistic impulse approximation approach. For the elementary production amplitude we use a standard relativistic parameterization based on a set of four Lorentz- and gauge-invariant amplitudes. The photonuclear amplitude is evaluated without recourse to a nonrelativistic reduction; the full relativistic structure of the amplitude is maintained. On general arguments we show that the coherent process is sensitive to only one of the elementary amplitudes. Moreover, we show that the nuclear structure information is fully contained in the ground-state tensor density. The tensor density is evaluated in a mean-field approximation to the Walecka model and it is shown to be sensitive to relativistic effects. Distortion effects are incorporated through an eta-nucleus optical potential that is computed in a simple t-rho approximation.

# Coherent eta-photoproduction from nuclei in a relativistic impulse approximation approach

## Key claim

The coherent photoproduction of eta mesons from closed-shell nuclei is sensitive to only one component (the A1 amplitude) of the elementary production amplitude, and all nuclear structure information is contained in the ground-state tensor density rather than the vector density used in nonrelativistic approaches. The relativistic cross sections are substantially larger than those obtained from assuming the free lower-to-upper component ratio, but this conclusion depends on using the Walecka model mean-field approximation and may be modified by violations of the impulse approximation or off-shell ambiguities in the elementary amplitude.

## Method

The authors compute coherent eta-photoproduction cross sections from 40Ca using a relativistic impulse approximation that maintains the full Dirac structure of the elementary amplitude, avoiding nonrelativistic reduction. The elementary gamma N -> eta N amplitude is parameterized by four Lorentz- and gauge-invariant amplitudes, with parameters fixed by fitting to p(gamma,eta)p and d(gamma,eta)pn data from Krusche et al. (1995), including S11(1535) and D13(1520) resonance contributions plus Born terms and t-channel vector-meson exchange. The nuclear ground-state tensor density is evaluated in a mean-field approximation to the Walecka model, and final-state interactions are incorporated via an eta-nucleus optical potential of the t-rho form.

## Key numbers

- At a photon laboratory energy of 625 MeV for 40Ca, the relativistic plane-wave impulse approximation (RPWIA) cross section is at least twice as large as its nonrelativistic counterpart.
- At a photon laboratory energy of 700 MeV for 40Ca, the distorted-wave cross sections show large quenching relative to plane-wave values due to a repulsive real part and large imaginary component of the optical potential, but the relativistic calculation still shows substantial enhancement over the nonrelativistic result.
- The optical potential parameter b is given by b(p_lab) = (alpha + beta p_lab + gamma p_lab^2)^{-1} with alpha = (+0.136, -0.052) fm^{-1}, beta = (+0.035, -0.072), gamma = (-0.061, +0.009) fm.
- The elementary model reproduces data for both proton and extracted neutron amplitudes at all measured energies and angles.

## Context

This work extends the nonrelativistic plane-wave impulse approximation analyses of Boffi and Mirando (1986), Chumbalov, Eramzhyan, and Kamalov (1987), and Bennhold and Tanabe (1990) by maintaining the full relativistic structure of the amplitude and using the Walecka model (Serot and Walecka, 1986) for nuclear structure. The coherent reaction acts as a spin-isospin filter that suppresses the dominant S11(1535) contribution, allowing examination of background mechanisms such as D13(1520) excitation and vector-meson exchange. The results enable experimental tests at facilities like TJNAF and MAMI to discriminate between relativistic and nonrelativistic descriptions, and to probe medium modifications of nucleon spinors and the validity of the impulse approximation.
