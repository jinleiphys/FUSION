# Following nuclei through nucleosynthesis: a novel tracing technique

> Astrophysical nucleosynthesis is a family of diverse processes by which atomic nuclei undergo nuclear reactions and decays to form new nuclei. The complex nature of nucleosynthesis, which can involve as many as tens of thousands of interactions between thousands of nuclei, makes it difficult to study any one of these interactions in isolation using standard approaches. In this work, we present a new technique, nucleosynthesis tracing, that we use to quantify the specific role of individual nuclear reaction, decay, and fission processes in relationship to nucleosynthesis as a whole. We apply this technique to study fission and $β^{-}$-decay as they occur in the rapid neutron capture ($r$) process of nucleosynthesis.

## Key claim

The paper introduces "nucleosynthesis tracing," a new technique that quantifies the specific role of individual nuclear reactions, decays, and fission processes in nucleosynthesis without modifying the underlying simulation, unlike previous approaches that required systematic variation of nuclear properties or only provided limited insight into which aspects of abundance patterns are affected.

## Method

The authors extend traditional nuclear reaction network equations by constructing a parallel set of "traced abundances" that evolve alongside total abundances, where nuclei are added to the traced population when they participate in specified "trace-in" processes and removed via "trace-out" processes. They implement this framework as PRISMtr, a modified version of the Portable Routines for Integrated nucleoSynthesis Modeling (PRISM) code, and apply it to study fission channels and beta-minus decay in rapid neutron capture (r-process) nucleosynthesis under various neutron star merger ejecta conditions.

## Key numbers

- For dynamical ejecta conditions with incomplete fission recycling, approximately 60% to 80% of heavy nuclei across the entire pattern have participated in fission, with neutron-induced fission accounting for roughly 60% of abundances across the full mass range
- For cold tidal-tail conditions with complete fission recycling, 100% of the final abundance pattern has a traced history involving neutron-induced fission, beta-delayed fission, or spontaneous fission
- Nine fission processes (distributed across very neutron-rich neptunium with Z=93 and plutonium with Z=94 isotopes) collectively drive the effects of early-time fission events, each contributing at least 10% of the final pattern for at least one mass number A
- For the neutron star merger accretion disk wind trajectory with specific entropy s/kB=40, timescale tau=20 ms, and electron fraction Ye=0.20, fission contributions lie in the mass region 80 less than A less than 180, with roughly equal contributions from neutron-induced and beta-delayed fission channels

## Context

This work extends previous approaches that either systematically varied nuclear properties (Schatz 1998, Mumpower 2016) or analyzed overall rates of processes during nucleosynthesis (Wanajo 2007, Vassh 2019), but could not precisely quantify which nuclei are affected by individual nuclear properties. The nucleosynthesis tracing technique enables future work to identify the most crucial nuclear properties for each nucleosynthesis process, allowing experimental and theoretical campaigns to focus on properties that will most significantly constrain nucleosynthesis simulation predictions, particularly for understanding the r-process in neutron star mergers following the GW170817 event.
