# Modeling Hybrid Stars in Quark-Hadron Approaches

> The study of neutron stars, or more general compact stars, is a topic of central interest in nuclear astrophysics. Furthermore, neutron stars serve as the only physical systems whose properties can be used to infer information on cold and dense matter at several times nuclear saturation density. Therefore, neutron star physics is ideally suited to complement the studies of ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions that sample strongly interacting matter at high temperature and relatively small net baryon density. In general, in order to pin down or at least constrain the properties of dense matter, accurate measurements of neutron star properties like masses, radii, rotational frequency, and cooling behavior are needed. Here, in relatively recent times the reliable mass determination of the pulsar PSR J1614-2230 of $M = 1.97 \pm 0.04 M_\odot$ has introduced an important benchmark for modeling stars and strongly interacting matter. It puts constraints on the structure of compact stars and possible exotic phases in the core of the stars as will be discussed in this article. In order to investigate this point we will consider a model for star matter that includes hyperonic and quark degrees of freedom, and present results for compact star properties in the following.

## Key claim

The paper argues that hybrid stars (containing both hadronic and quark matter) can reach masses above 2 solar masses only if repulsive vector interactions are included in both the hyperon and quark sectors, but that introducing a strong vector coupling for quarks creates a conflict with lattice QCD results at low chemical potentials.

## Method

The authors combine a hadronic flavor-SU(3) model (including baryons from the lowest SU(3) octet and decuplet) with quark degrees of freedom coupled to the same mesonic mean fields, using a Polyakov-loop extended Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (PNJL) type approach. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations for static, spherically symmetric stars to compute mass-radius relations, and compare the Taylor coefficient \(c_2\) of the pressure expansion to lattice QCD data to test the quark vector coupling.

## Key numbers

- Pulsar PSR J1614-2230 mass: \(M = 1.97 \pm 0.04 M_\odot\) (benchmark for modeling)
- Maximum mass of purely nucleonic star with SU(6) couplings: 2.12 \(M_\odot\)
- Maximum mass including hyperons with SU(6) couplings: 2.06 \(M_\odot\)
- Maximum mass of hyper star with vanishing \(\phi\) coupling: about 1.65 \(M_\odot\)
- In the parity-doublet model, a first-order phase transition occurs for a parity-partner mass above approximately 1470 MeV
- Maximum masses in the parity-doublet quark-hadron approach: 1.75 \(M_\odot\) (compressibility \(\kappa = 330\) MeV) and 1.96 \(M_\odot\) (\(\kappa = 525\) MeV)
- Maximum mass of hybrid star with MIT bag model (non-interacting quarks): 1.8 \(M_\odot\) for purely baryonic matter
- Strangeness contribution to nucleon vector form factor: below 3 percent and consistent with 0

## Context

This work extends earlier hadronic SU(3) models (Papazoglou 1997, 1998) and previous hybrid star calculations that often produced masses below 2 \(M_\odot\) when hyperons were included (e.g., Schulze 2010, Agrawal 2012). The paper shows that SU(6) symmetry for hyperon couplings can maintain high masses, but that breaking SU(6) while keeping SU(3) to raise masses further conflicts with experimental constraints on nucleon strangeness from parity-violating electron scattering (Armstrong 2012). The key open question is how to incorporate repulsive quark interactions to reach high hybrid star masses without violating lattice QCD results at low chemical potential, as demonstrated by the disagreement in the \(c_2\) coefficient (Cheng 2008).
