# Algorithm to Obtain Inverse Potentials for $α-α$ Scattering using Variable Phase Approach

> An algorithm$^{\ref{Fig1}}$ has been developed with the purpose of obtaining inverse potentials, where the Riccati-type non-linear differential equation, also called phase equation, has been kept in tandem with the Variational Monte Carlo method. The optimization of Gaussian function parameters is achieved such that the experimental phase shifts are reproduced. The obtained SPS for various $\ ell$ channels has been compared with experimental ones with mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) as a measure. The model parameters have been optimised by suitable optimisation technique by looking for minimum value of MAPE. The results for $\ell$=0$^+$, 2$^+$ and 4$^+$ partial waves have been obtained, to match with experimental SPS, with MAPE values of $2.9$, $4.6$ and $6.2$ respectively for data up to $23$ MeV, while for higher states 6$^+$, 8$^+$ and 10$^+$ has MAPE of $3.2, 4.5$ and $5.9$ respectively for data from $53-120$ MeV. On extrapolation for data in range E$_{\ell ab.}$ = $23-120$ MeV, using the optimised parameters, the SPS are found to be in close agreement with experimental ones for the first three channels.

## Key claim

The authors present an algorithm that combines the Variable Phase Approach (Phase Function Method) with Variational Monte Carlo optimization to obtain inverse potentials for alpha-alpha scattering, claiming that the resulting double Gaussian potentials reproduce experimental phase shifts for partial waves up to l=10, but only for the elastic region up to 23 MeV; the extrapolation to higher energies (23-120 MeV) shows close agreement only for the first three channels (l=0, 2, 4), while the potentials for l=6, 8, and 10 are derived from data above 53 MeV and should be treated cautiously.

## Method

The authors use the Phase Function Method (PFM), a Riccati-type nonlinear differential equation, to compute scattering phase shifts directly from a double Gaussian nuclear potential plus a Coulomb term with an error function form factor, without solving the Schrodinger equation for wave functions. Model parameters (attraction strength, repulsion strength, and their inverse ranges) are optimized by a Variational Monte Carlo algorithm that iteratively perturbs parameters and accepts changes that reduce the mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) between calculated and experimental phase shifts. The PFM equations are integrated numerically using a Runge-Kutta 5 method from the origin to the asymptotic region for each partial wave separately.

## Key numbers

- For l=0, 2, and 4 partial waves up to 23 MeV: MAPE values of 2.9%, 4.6%, and 6.2% respectively
- For l=6, 8, and 10 partial waves from 53-120 MeV: MAPE values of 3.2%, 4.5%, and 5.9% respectively
- At E_lab = 0.6 MeV, the calculated phase shift for l=0 is 182.2 degrees, compared to the experimental value of 178 +/- 1 degree
- At E_lab = 11.8 MeV, the maximum phase shift for l=2 is 115.56 degrees, compared to the experimental value of 114.9 +/- 2 degrees
- At E_lab = 77.5 MeV, the maximum phase shift for l=4 (G-wave) is 140.52 degrees, compared to the experimental value of 137 +/- 1.8 degrees
- The alpha particle root mean square radius used is 1.44 fm
- The reduced mass factor hbar^2/(2mu) = 10.44217 MeV fm^2

## Context

This work extends the Phase Function Method applied by Laha and group to alpha-alpha scattering for higher partial waves (l=6, 8, 10) that were not previously investigated with this method, building on earlier phenomenological studies by Ali and Bodmer (who used a two-term potential for l=0, 2, 4) and Darriulat, Igo, and Pugh (who used a complex Woods-Saxon potential for l=0-10 at energies 53-120 MeV). The results confirm the conclusion of Darriulat and Laha that a single static potential cannot describe all partial waves, and the computed cross sections are compared with those of Odsuren and found to be in good agreement. The approach enables future inclusion of inelastic processes to potentially reduce MAPE and obtain more realistic potentials across the full energy range.
