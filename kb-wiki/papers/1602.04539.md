# Nuclear binding near a quantum phase transition

> How do protons and neutrons bind to form nuclei? This is the central question of ab initio nuclear structure theory. While the answer may seem as simple as the fact that nuclear forces are attractive, the full story is more complex and interesting. In this work we present numerical evidence from ab initio lattice simulations showing that nature is near a quantum phase transition, a zero-temperature transition driven by quantum fluctuations. Using lattice effective field theory, we perform Monte Carlo simulations for systems with up to twenty nucleons. For even and equal numbers of protons and neutrons, we discover a first-order transition at zero temperature from a Bose-condensed gas of alpha particles (4He nuclei) to a nuclear liquid. Whether one has an alpha-particle gas or nuclear liquid is determined by the strength of the alpha-alpha interactions, and we show that the alpha-alpha interactions depend on the strength and locality of the nucleon-nucleon interactions. This insight should be useful in improving calculations of nuclear structure and important astrophysical reactions involving alpha capture on nuclei. Our findings also provide a tool to probe the structure of alpha cluster states such as the Hoyle state responsible for the production of carbon in red giant stars and point to a connection between nuclear states and the universal physics of bosons at large scattering length.

## Key claim

Using ab initio lattice simulations, the authors show that nuclear binding is near a first-order quantum phase transition at zero temperature between a Bose-condensed gas of alpha particles and a nuclear liquid, but this conclusion depends on the degree of locality of the nucleon-nucleon interactions used in the leading-order chiral effective field theory.

## Method

The authors performed auxiliary-field Monte Carlo simulations on a periodic lattice with spatial spacing 1.97 fm and time spacing 1.32 fm, using two different leading-order chiral effective field theory interactions (A and B) that reproduce nucleon-nucleon scattering phase shifts but differ in the locality of their short-range components. They computed ground state energies for nuclei up to 20 nucleons and alpha-alpha S-wave phase shifts using the adiabatic projection method, then interpolated between interactions A and B with a mixing parameter lambda to map the zero-temperature phase diagram.

## Key numbers

- Ground state energy of 4He: -28.62(4) MeV for interaction A (LO + Coulomb), -28.45(6) MeV for interaction B (LO + Coulomb), compared to experimental -28.296 MeV
- Ground state energy of 16O: -110.5(6) MeV for interaction A (LO + Coulomb), -126.0(7) MeV for interaction B (LO + Coulomb), compared to experimental -127.619 MeV
- Ground state energy of 20Ne: -137(1) MeV for interaction A (LO + Coulomb), -164(1) MeV for interaction B (LO + Coulomb), compared to experimental -160.645 MeV
- For interaction A, the ratio of ground state energy to alpha particle energy is 1.997(6) for 8Be, 3.00(1) for 12C, 4.00(2) for 16O, and 5.03(3) for 20Ne
- The quantum phase transition occurs at lambda_infinity = 0.0(1), where the alpha-alpha scattering length crosses zero
- The critical lambda for binding 20Ne is lambda_20 = 0.2(1), for 16O is lambda_16 = 0.2(1), for 12C is lambda_12 = 0.3(1), and for 8Be is lambda_8 = 0.7(1)

## Context

This work extends ab initio lattice effective field theory calculations by Lahde et al. (2013) and Elhatisari et al. (2015) to reveal that alpha-alpha scattering is highly sensitive to the locality of nucleon-nucleon interactions, a feature not constrained by two-nucleon scattering data. The finding that nature sits near a quantum phase transition provides a new tool for studying alpha cluster states such as the Hoyle state in 12C and connects nuclear states to universal Efimov physics for bosons at large scattering length, as discussed by Efimov (1971) and Braaten (2004).
