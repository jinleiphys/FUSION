# Jet quenching as a probe of the initial stages in heavy-ion collisions

> Jet quenching provides a very flexible variety of observables which are sensitive to different energy- and time-scales of the strongly interacting matter created in heavy-ion collisions. Exploiting this versatility would make jet quenching an excellent chronometer of the yoctosecond structure of the evolution process. Here we show, for the first time, that a combination of jet quenching observables is sensitive to the initial stages of heavy-ion collisions, when the approach to local thermal equilibrium is expected to happen. Specifically, we find that in order to reproduce at the same time the inclusive particle production suppression, $R_{AA}$, and the high-$p_T$ azimuthal asymmetries, $v_2$, energy loss must be strongly suppressed for the first $\sim 0.6$ fm. This exploratory analysis shows the potential of jet observables, possibly more sophisticated than the ones studied here, to constrain the dynamics of the initial stages of the evolution.

## Key claim
The paper shows that a combination of jet quenching observables (inclusive particle suppression R_AA and high-pT azimuthal asymmetries v_2) is sensitive to the initial stages of heavy-ion collisions, and that to simultaneously reproduce both observables, energy loss must be strongly suppressed for the first approximately 0.6 fm after the collision. This is an exploratory analysis, and the authors note that the result comes from a smaller transport coefficient at early times but lacks a conclusive physical explanation.

## Method
The authors compute the nuclear modification factor R_AA and high-pT harmonic coefficients v_2 and v_3 for charged hadrons in 20-30% centrality Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV using the quenching weights formalism for radiative energy loss, embedded in event-by-event EKRT hydrodynamic simulations of the medium. The jet transport coefficient is defined as q-hat = K * 2 * epsilon^(3/4), where epsilon is the local energy density from hydrodynamics, and the single free parameter K is fitted to R_AA data. The authors vary the starting time of energy loss (tau_q = 0, 0.197 fm, or 0.572 fm) and the temperature at which energy loss stops (T_q = 175 MeV or 100 MeV) to study the sensitivity of observables to the early stages.

## Key numbers
- Energy loss must be suppressed for the first approximately 0.6 fm after the collision to simultaneously describe R_AA and v_2 data.
- The formation time of the EKRT initial condition is tau_f = 0.197 fm (from p_min = 1 GeV).
- The K-factor values from fits to R_AA data for the three early-time treatments are: 2.120 (+0.091/-0.074) for tau_q = 0 fm, 2.90 (+0.13/-0.11) for tau_q = 0.197 fm, and 4.56 +/- 0.20 for tau_q = 0.572 fm.
- The chi^2/d.o.f. for v_2 (CMS data, pT > 10 GeV) improves from 26.2 (tau_q = 0 fm) to 12.9 (tau_q = 0.197 fm) to 3.5 (tau_q = 0.572 fm).
- The chemical freeze-out temperature used to stop energy loss is T_chem = 175 MeV; the kinetic freeze-out temperature is T_dec = 100 MeV.
- For the temperature-cut scenario, K-factor values are 7.00 (T_cut = 350 MeV) and 5.77 (T_cut = 380 MeV).

## Context
This work extends previous studies that attempted to describe high-pT elliptic flow in heavy-ion collisions, which had long underestimated experimental data (e.g., Xu 2014, Liao 2008, Jia 2011, Betz 2011-2014, Armesto 2011, Horowitz 2015). The authors note that all studies that successfully describe jet harmonic coefficients (Noronha-Hostler 2016, Betz 2016, Zigic 2018, Shi 2018) implicitly start energy loss at the hydrodynamic initial time (typically 0.6 fm), effectively implementing the same time delay. The EKRT framework with its earlier formation time (0.197 fm) provides the first controlled way to vary the quenching onset. The result enables future studies to use jet observables as a chronometer of the yoctosecond-scale evolution of the initial stages, and may be extendable from large to small collision systems.
