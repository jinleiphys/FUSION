# Variational Estimates using a Discrete Variable Representation

> The advantage of using a Discrete Variable Representation (DVR) is that the Hamiltonian of two interacting particles can be constructed in a very simple form. However the DVR Hamiltonian is approximate and, as a consequence, the results cannot be considered as variational ones. We will show that the variational character of the results can be restored by performing a reduced number of integrals. In practice, for a variational description of the lowest n bound states only n(n+1)/2 integrals are necessary whereas D(D+1)/2 integrals are enough for the scattering states (D is the dimension of the S matrix). Applications of the method to the study of dimers of He, Ne and Ar, for both bound and scattering states, are presented.

## Key claim

The paper shows that variational upper bounds for bound-state energies and variational estimates for scattering matrices can be obtained from a Discrete Variable Representation (DVR) by computing a reduced number of accurate numerical integrals, even though the DVR Hamiltonian itself is approximate and its eigenvalues are not variational. For the lowest \(n\) bound states only \(n(n+1)/2\) integrals are needed, and for scattering states with a \(D\)-dimensional \(S\) matrix only \(D(D+1)/2\) integrals are required.

## Method

The authors first solve the DVR eigenvalue problem (for bound states) or the DVR linear system derived from the Kohn variational principle (for scattering states) using a basis of orthogonal polynomials with Gaussian quadrature points. They then transform the resulting DVR wave functions from the DVR point representation to \(r\)-space and compute the Hamiltonian matrix elements \(\langle \psi_i^{\mathrm{DVR}} | H | \psi_j^{\mathrm{DVR}} \rangle\) by accurate numerical integration. For bound states, diagonalizing the resulting \(n \times n\) matrix yields variational upper bounds; for scattering states, the second-order Kohn functional is evaluated using the accurately computed integral \(\langle \psi^{\mathrm{DVR}} | H - E | \psi^{\mathrm{DVR}} \rangle\). The method is applied to the dimers He\(_2\), Ne\(_2\), and Ar\(_2\) using the LM2M2, HFD-B, and HFD-C potentials, respectively.

## Key numbers

- He\(_2\) ground state: the converged VBR and \(\langle \mathrm{DVR} \rangle\) energies coincide to five digits at \(N=200\) DVR points; the DVR energy oscillates around the VBR value even above \(N=500\).
- He-He scattering length (second order, converged): \(a = 189.518\) a.u.
- Reduced mass factors used: \(\hbar^2 / M = 43.281307\) K a.u.\(^2\) for He, \(8.584089\) K a.u.\(^2\) for Ne, and \(4.336093\) K a.u.\(^2\) for Ar.
- Number of vibrational bound states predicted by the chosen potentials: one for He\(_2\), three for Ne\(_2\), and nine for Ar\(_2\).
- Nonlinear parameter \(\beta\) for the Laguerre basis: 1 a.u.\(^{-1}\) for He\(_2\), 5 a.u.\(^{-1}\) for Ne\(_2\), and 10 a.u.\(^{-1}\) for Ar\(_2\).

## Context

This work extends the Discrete Variable Representation method, widely used in molecular physics (reviewed by Light and Carrington, 2000), by restoring the variational property that is lost when Gaussian quadrature replaces exact integrals. It contrasts with the standard Variational Basis Representation (VBR), which requires \(N^2\) numerical integrals for the potential matrix and is computationally expensive for systems like rare-gas dimers that need large bases. The method enables efficient variational calculations for weakly bound van der Waals dimers (He\(_2\), Ne\(_2\), Ar\(_2\)) studied previously with potentials by Aziz and Slaman (1991) and Aziz (1993), and can be extended to other systems where the number of bound states or open channels is much smaller than the basis dimension.
