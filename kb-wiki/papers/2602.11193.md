# Addressing the ground state of the deuteron by physics-informed neural networks

> Machine learning techniques have proven to be effective in addressing the structure of atomic nuclei. Physics$-$Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) are a promising machine learning technique suitable for solving integro-differential problems such as the many-body Schrödinger problem. So far, there has been no demonstration of extracting nuclear eigenstates using such method. Here, we tackle realistic nucleon-nucleon interaction in momentum space, including models with strong high-momentum correlations, and demonstrate highly accurate results for the deuteron. We further provide additional benchmarks in coordinate space. We introduce an expression for the variational energy that enters the loss function, which can be evaluated efficiently within the PINNs framework. Results are in excellent agreement with proven numerical methods, with a relative error between the value of the predicted binding energy by the PINN and the numerical benchmark of the order of $10^{-6}$. Our approach paves the way for the exploitation of PINNs to solve more complex atomic nuclei.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) can solve the deuteron ground state with high accuracy for realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions, including models with strong high-momentum correlations, achieving a relative error in binding energy of order 10^-6 compared to numerical benchmarks, but only after careful tuning of loss function weights and training strategies, and with the caveat that the normalization condition was not fully minimized for the N^4LO case.

## Method

The authors solve the radial Schrödinger equation for the deuteron using a feed-forward neural network with 6-7 hidden layers of 256 neurons each, where the loss function combines terms enforcing the Schrödinger equation, boundary conditions, wavefunction normalization, and the variational principle. For coordinate space calculations with the Minnesota potential, they employ an auxiliary network output to compute normalization integrals; for momentum space calculations with N^4LO and CD-Bonn interactions, they use finite-difference integration and a two-component network output for the coupled ^3S_1 and ^3D_1 partial waves. The CD-Bonn solution is obtained by transfer learning from the N^4LO network, gradually extending the momentum grid from 1389 MeV/c to 8573 MeV/c.

## Key numbers

- Relative error in binding energy for the Minnesota potential in coordinate space: 1.1%
- Relative error for the N^4LO interaction in momentum space: err_E,Num = -5.21 × 10^-5
- Fidelity for the N^4LO wavefunction: F_ψ = 0.9999933
- Relative error for the CD-Bonn interaction: err_E,Num = -2.76 × 10^-7
- Fidelity for the CD-Bonn wavefunction: 1 - F_ψ ≈ 10^-9
- Momentum grid maximum: 1389.02 MeV/c for N^4LO (100 collocation points); approximately 8573 MeV/c for CD-Bonn (200 collocation points)
- Normalization integral at training stop for N^4LO: I[ψ] = 0.8219

## Context

This work extends the neural network quantum states approach previously applied to the deuteron by Keeble (2023) and few-nucleon systems by Adams (2021) and Yang (2023), but is the first demonstration of PINNs for an ab initio nuclear structure problem. The method enables solving integro-differential Schrödinger equations with realistic nuclear forces without labeled training data, and the authors suggest it can be extended to more complex nuclei. The CD-Bonn result is particularly notable as this interaction induces strong short-range correlations that make traditional diagonalization more challenging.
