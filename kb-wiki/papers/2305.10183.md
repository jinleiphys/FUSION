# Effect of the Coriolis force on the electrical conductivity of quark matter: A nonrelativistic description

> Rotating quarks and hadronic systems, produced in peripheral heavy ion collisions, can experience Coriolis force and other forces due to rotational motion. Considering only the effect of Coriolis force, we have calculated the electrical conductivity for non-relativistic rotating matter using the Relaxation Time Approximation based Boltzmann transport equation. A similarity in mathematical calculations of electrical conductivity at finite rotation and finite magnetic fields is exposed, where an equivalence role between Coriolis force on massive particle's motion and Lorentz force on charged particle's motion is noticed. As the beginning level step, we consider only the Coriolis force in the non-relativistic formalism, which will be extended in the future towards the relativistic case, and to adopt other forces for a more realistic description of the rotating quark and hadronic system.

## Key claim

The authors calculate the electrical conductivity tensor for non-relativistic rotating matter under the Coriolis force only, using the relaxation time approximation of the Boltzmann transport equation, as a first step toward a relativistic description that will include other pseudo-forces for a more realistic model of rotating quark and hadronic systems.

## Method

The authors start from the Boltzmann transport equation in the relaxation time approximation, adding only the Coriolis force term \(2m(\vec{v} \times \vec{\Omega})\) to the left-hand side while ignoring centrifugal and other pseudo-forces. They assume a constant global angular velocity \(\vec{\Omega}\) for all fluid particles and solve for the deviation \(\delta f\) from the equilibrium Fermi-Dirac distribution using an ansatz with three unknown constants. The resulting \(\delta f\) is substituted into the kinetic theory expression for electrical current density, and the conductivity tensor \(\sigma_{ij}\) is extracted by comparison with Ohm's law, yielding three independent scalar components: parallel (\(\sigma_{||}\)), perpendicular (\(\sigma_{\perp}\)), and Hall-like (\(\sigma_{\times}\)).

## Key numbers

- The parallel conductivity component is \(\sigma_{||} = \frac{g\sqrt{m}q^2}{(2\pi)^{3/2}} \tau_c T^{3/2} f_{3/2}(A)\), where \(A = e^{\mu/T}\) and \(f_{3/2}\) is the Fermi-Dirac integral.
- The perpendicular component is \(\sigma_{\perp} = \frac{g\sqrt{m}q^2}{(2\pi)^{3/2}} \frac{\tau_c}{1+(\tau_c/\tau_{\Omega})^2} T^{3/2} f_{3/2}(A)\), with \(\tau_{\Omega} \equiv 1/(2\Omega)\).
- The Hall-like component is \(\sigma_{\times} = \frac{g\sqrt{m}q^2}{(2\pi)^{3/2}} \frac{\tau_c (\tau_c/\tau_{\Omega})}{1+(\tau_c/\tau_{\Omega})^2} T^{3/2} f_{3/2}(A)\).
- For the degenerate limit \(T \to 0\), \(\sigma_{||} = \frac{4g}{3\sqrt{\pi}} \frac{\sqrt{m}q^2}{(2\pi)^{3/2}} \tau_c \mu^{3/2}\), with analogous expressions for \(\sigma_{\perp}\) and \(\sigma_{\times}\).
- For the zero-chemical-potential limit \(\mu \to 0\), \(\sigma_{||} = 0.29g \frac{\sqrt{m}q^2}{(2\pi)^{3/2}} \tau_c T^{3/2} \zeta(3/2)\), with \(\zeta(3/2)\) the Riemann zeta function.
- The crossover where \(\sigma_{\times}\) becomes more dominant than \(\sigma_{\perp}\) occurs around \(\Omega = 0.02\) GeV, using \(\tau_c = 5\) fm and \(T = 200\) MeV.
- The typical angular velocity range from heavy ion collision models is 0.003 GeV to 0.027 GeV, corresponding to \(\tau_{\Omega}\) from 3.57 fm to 25 fm.

## Context

This work extends the formalism used for electrical conductivity in a finite magnetic field (Dey 2019, Ghosh 2019, Dey 2020, Kalikotay 2020, Dey 2021, Satapathy 2021) to the case of finite rotation, exploiting the mathematical equivalence between the Lorentz force on a charged particle and the Coriolis force on a massive particle. The authors note that the same structure of the conductivity tensor appears for rotation as for a magnetic field, with the angular velocity unit vector replacing the magnetic field unit vector. This non-relativistic calculation is intended as a starting point; the authors state their immediate future objective is to develop a relativistic framework applicable to quark-gluon plasma and compact stars, where temperature and chemical potential are of order MeV rather than eV.
