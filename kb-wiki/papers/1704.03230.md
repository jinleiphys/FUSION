# Elastic Scattering Phenomenology

> We argue that, in many situations, fits to elastic scattering data that were historically, and frequently still are, considered `good', are not justifiably so describable. Information about the dynamics of nucleon-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus scattering is lost when elastic scattering phenomenology is insufficiently ambitious. It is argued that in many situations, an alternative approach is appropriate for the phenomenology of nuclear elastic scattering of nucleons and other light nuclei. The approach affords an appropriate means of evaluating folding models, one that fully exploits available empirical data. It is particularly applicable for nucleons and other light ions.

# Elastic Scattering Phenomenology

## Key claim
The paper argues that many historically accepted "good" fits to elastic scattering data are not justifiably so, and that information about nucleon-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus scattering dynamics is lost when phenomenology is insufficiently ambitious. The author advocates for model-independent fitting procedures that achieve χ² per degree of freedom near unity, particularly for nucleons and other light ions, as a necessary standard for properly evaluating folding models.

## Method
The author critiques standard Woods-Saxon and Woods-Saxon-squared parameterizations as inadequate for extracting full physical information from high-quality elastic scattering data, and proposes a two-step approach: first, fit a reasonable parameterized form to define a global potential over a wide energy range; second, fit data for a specific nucleus by adding a model-independent additive correction (e.g., sum of Bessel functions, spline functions, or Gaussians) without restricting to smooth potentials, seeking the lowest χ². The resulting phenomenological potential for a specific target nucleus should then be compared against folding model theoretical potentials, rather than directly fitting experimental data by renormalizing or adding corrections.

## Key numbers
- χ² per degree of freedom (χ²/DF) values for wide angular range alpha scattering data from ⁴⁰Ca, ⁴⁴Ca, and ⁴⁸Ca tended to be "huge," and for cases with small angular range χ²/DF was "often not much lower" (no specific numerical values given for these fits)
- The paper advocates for achieving χ²/DF ∼ 1 as a criterion for satisfactory fits, contrasting this with historical standards
- The unitarity limit |S_L| ≤ 1 must be respected in phenomenology, though local emissive regions are permitted as long as this condition holds

## Context
This work extends the author's long-standing argument for rigorous elastic scattering phenomenology, referencing prior studies of alpha particle scattering from Ca isotopes (Ref. edf1) and Ni isotopes (Ref. edf2) that used energy density functional (EDF) models alongside standard deep potentials, finding contradictory potentials that both fit "reasonably well." The paper contrasts nuclear optical model phenomenology unfavorably with electron scattering analyses (Sick, reference SickT), which have achieved much greater sophistication. The approach enables evaluation of modern folding models (DTK15, egashira, kuprikov) and dynamic polarization potential calculations, and could reveal l-dependence of the optical model potential (ldep, KR90, RK90) and connections between collectivity and OMP parameters (ghj).
