# Nuclear Structure - "ab initio"

> An ab-initio description of atomic nuclei that solves the nuclear many-body problem for realistic nuclear forces is expected to possess a high degree of predictive power. In this contribution we treat the main obstacle, namely the short-ranged repulsive and tensor correlations induced by the realistic nucleon-nucleon interaction, by means of a unitary correlation operator. This correlator applied to uncorrelated many-body states imprints short-ranged correlations that cannot be described by product states. When applied to an observable it induces the correlations into the operator, creating for example a correlated Hamiltonian suited for Slater determinants. Adding to the correlated realistic interaction a correction for three-body effects, consisting of a momentum-dependent central and spin-orbit two-body potential we obtain an effective interaction that is successfully used for all nuclei up to mass 60. Various results are shown.

## Key claim

The Unitary Correlation Operator Method (UCOM) can produce an effective interaction suitable for Hartree-Fock and configuration-mixing calculations of all nuclei up to mass 60, but only when a phenomenological momentum-dependent central and spin-orbit correction is added to account for missing three-body and higher-order contributions from the correlated Hamiltonian.

## Method

The authors apply a unitary correlation operator, consisting of a radial correlator and a tensor correlator, to uncorrelated Slater determinant states to imprint short-range correlations induced by realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions. They then perform a cluster expansion of the correlated Hamiltonian, keeping only up to two-body operators, and add a four-parameter momentum-dependent central and spin-orbit correction to account for missing three-body effects and genuine three-body forces. The resulting effective interaction is used in Hartree-Fock calculations with Fermionic Molecular Dynamics single-particle states, followed by projection after variation (PAV) and variation after projection (VAP) calculations for selected nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The radially correlated potential energy gain is about -25 MeV per particle for 4He, 16O, and 40Ca using the Bonn-A potential.
- After including both radial and tensor correlations, the total energy per particle is about -8 MeV for these nuclei.
- The correction Hamiltonian H_corr contributes typically 15% of the correlated interaction energy.
- For 12C, the VAP calculation yields 91.9 MeV binding energy compared to 84.7 MeV for V/PAV, gaining 7.2 MeV of additional binding.
- The VAP calculation for 12C gives a charge radius of 2.38 fm and B(E2) value of 24.7 e^2 fm^4, compared to experimental values of 92.2 MeV, 2.47 fm, and 39.7 ± 3.3 e^2 fm^4 respectively.
- For 16O, the VAP calculation gains about 5 MeV in binding by forming alpha-clusters compared to the spherical closed-shell configuration.

## Context

This work extends the Unitary Correlation Operator Method previously developed by the authors (Feldmeier, Neff, and Roth, 1998, 2003) to treat short-range repulsive and tensor correlations in nuclear many-body systems. It contrasts with exact ab initio methods like Green's Function Monte Carlo calculations and the No-Core Shell Model, which are limited to light nuclei, by providing an effective interaction that can be applied to medium-mass nuclei up to mass 60. The correlated interaction is similar to the V_low-k approach (Bogner et al., 2003) in producing a low-momentum interaction, and the method enables future multiconfiguration calculations for describing excited states and cluster structures such as the 3-alpha configuration in 12C and the alpha-cluster structure in 16O.
