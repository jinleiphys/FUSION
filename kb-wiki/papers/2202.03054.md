# Heavy quarks at finite temperature

> New incarnations of heavy-ion collision experiments are turning our attention to hard processes and a more fine-grained resolution of the QGP. In this endeavor quarkonia or open heavy flavors turn out to be versatile probes, which are usually described through models based on perturbative QCD, AdS, and effective field theories. The lattice provides nonperturbative input and constraints to such models.\newline In-medium bottomonia, the complex static quark-antiquark potential, and the heavy-quark momentum diffusion coefficient are key quantities where lattice gauge theory has recently achieved significant progress with impact for heavy-ion phenomenology. We review these lattice results, relate them to phenomenological applications, and close with an outlook.

## Key claim

Lattice gauge theory has recently achieved significant progress in calculating in-medium bottomonia properties, the complex static quark-antiquark potential, and the heavy-quark momentum diffusion coefficient, but these calculations still require modeling of spectral functions to solve the inverse problem relating Euclidean correlators to real-time dynamics, and the need for such modeling is not significantly affected by improvements in the lattice setup.

## Method

The paper reviews lattice calculations of heavy-quark observables at finite temperature using two main approaches: (1) gradient flow for noise reduction and renormalization in computing the chromoelectric field-strength correlator related to the heavy-quark momentum diffusion coefficient, and (2) screening correlators (spatial correlation functions) computed with the highly improved staggered quark (HISQ) action combined with tree-level improved Symanzik gauge action on (2+1)-flavor QCD lattices, as well as NRQCD-based calculations for bottomonia. The inverse problem relating Euclidean-time correlators to spectral functions is solved using models that enforce known limiting behaviors in the IR and UV.

## Key numbers

- The pseudocritical temperature of the QCD crossover is T_pc ≈ 157 MeV with an uncertainty of about 1%
- The chiral transition temperature for N_f=2 massless flavors and a physical strange quark is T_χ ≈ 132 MeV
- The deconfinement transition temperature in SU(3) pure gauge theory is T_d ≈ 270 MeV
- The most compact ground state bottomonia (Υ(1S) and η_b) are mostly unmodified until at least T ≳ 400 MeV
- The lowest, larger bottomonia (χ_{b0}, h_b) begin melting already after T ≳ 350 MeV
- The most compact ground state charmonia (J/Ψ and η_c) melt at temperatures higher than T ≳ 200 MeV
- Screening correlator calculations for bottomonia covered a temperature window of T ∼ [350, 1000] MeV using lattices with N_τ = 12, 10, and 8
- For N_f=3 with O(a)-improved Wilson fermions, deviations from leading order were only a few percent down to T ∼ 1 GeV but could not be captured quantitatively by next-to-leading order even at T ∼ 80 GeV

## Context

This review extends the line of work on in-medium heavy-quark observables that began with Matsui and Satz (1986) proposing quarkonia melting as a QGP fingerprint, and later the sequential melting picture by Karsch (2005). The paper relates lattice results to phenomenological applications, contrasting weak-coupling approaches (such as the HTL calculation by Laine (2006) and the pNRQCD calculation by Brambilla (2008)) with non-perturbative lattice input. The screening correlator approach extends earlier studies by Bazavov (2014) on open and hidden charm to bottomonia, using the HotQCD setup. The gradient flow method for heavy-quark transport opens the possibility for first calculations in full QCD, which were previously limited to the quenched approximation using multi-level algorithms by Meyer (2010), Banerjee (2011), Francis (2015), and Brambilla (2020).
