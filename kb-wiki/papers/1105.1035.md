# Chromomagnetism in nuclear matter

> Quarks are color charged particles. Due to their motion there is a strong possibility of generation of color magnetic field. It is shown that however hadrons are color singlet particles they may have non-zero color magnetic moment. Due to this color magnetic moment hadrons can show color interaction. In this paper we have studied the chromomagnetic properties of nuclear matter.

# Chromomagnetism in nuclear matter

## Key claim
Hadrons, despite being color-singlet particles, may possess a non-zero color magnetic moment due to the motion of their constituent quarks, which allows them to exhibit color interactions. The paper shows that this chromomagnetic moment has both abelian and non-abelian components, and that the resulting chromomagnetism of hadronic matter does not follow the Curie law.

## Method
The authors calculate the color magnetic moment of mesons and baryons using a string model of hadrons, where quarks are attached at the ends of a rotating string. They derive separate expressions for the abelian component (from orbital motion) and the non-abelian component (from temporal variation of color charge described by the Wong equation). They then compute the partition function for a non-interacting hadronic system and obtain the mean color magnetization as a function of temperature and external color magnetic field.

## Key numbers
- The color charge of a quark as a function of temperature is given by \( Q^2(T) = 4\pi / [9 \ln (T / 0.1254 T_c)^2] \) for three quark flavors, where \( T_c \) is the QCD phase transition temperature.
- At very large temperature, the mean color magnetization is \( \vec{M}^a = C (\mu^a \mu^b B^b) / (k_B T) \), where \( C \) is the number density of hadrons.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of chromomagnetism by Rujula, Georgi, and Glashow (who used it to explain hadron mass splittings) and by Sapirstein (who examined quark color magnetic moments in a constant external color magnetic field). It also builds on studies of phase transitions in QCD by Ninomiya and Sakai and by Kapusta. The results enable further investigation of different chromomagnetic phases of nuclear matter and the behavior of quark-gluon plasma, though the authors note that gluonic contributions are left for future work.
