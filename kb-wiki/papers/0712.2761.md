# Chiral phase transition in the presence of spinodal decomposition

> The thermodynamics of a first order chiral phase transition is considered in the presence of spinodal phase separation within the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model. The properties of the basic thermodynamic observables in the coexistence phase are discussed for zero and non-zero quark masses. We focus on observables that probe the chiral phase transition. In particular, the behavior of the specific heat and entropy as well as charge fluctuations are calculated and analyzed. We show that the specific heat and charge susceptibilities diverge at the isothermal spinodal lines. We determine the scaling behavior and compute the critical exponent $γ$ of the net quark number susceptibility at the isothermal spinodal lines within the NJL model and the Ginsburg-Landau theory. We show that in the chiral limit the critical exponent $γ=1/2$ at the tricritical point as well as along the isothermal spinodal lines. On the other hand, for finite quark masses the critical exponent at the spinodal lines, $γ=1/2$, differs from that at the critical end point, $γ=2/3$, indicating a change in the universality class. These results are independent of the particular choice of the chiral Lagrangian and should be common for all mean field approaches.

## Key claim

The paper shows that in the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, the net quark number susceptibility diverges not only at the critical end point (CEP) but also along the isothermal spinodal lines of a first-order chiral phase transition; the critical exponent at the spinodal lines is \(\gamma=1/2\) for both zero and finite quark masses, while at the CEP it is \(\gamma=2/3\) for finite masses, indicating a change in universality class that is independent of the specific chiral Lagrangian and common to all mean-field approaches.

## Method

The authors used the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model in the mean-field approximation for two quark flavors and three colors, with a three-momentum cutoff \(\Lambda=587.9\) MeV and coupling \(G_S \Lambda^2 = 2.44\), to compute the thermodynamic potential and derived susceptibilities. They analyzed the behavior of the net quark number susceptibility \(\chi_{\mu\mu}\), specific heats \(C_V\) and \(C_P\), and other thermodynamic observables across the first-order chiral phase transition, focusing on the isothermal and isentropic spinodal lines. The critical exponents were extracted numerically from the NJL model and confirmed analytically using a Ginzburg-Landau effective theory expansion of the thermodynamic potential around the spinodal points.

## Key numbers

- The three-momentum cutoff used in the NJL model is \(\Lambda = 587.9\) MeV and the coupling strength is \(G_S \Lambda^2 = 2.44\).
- For finite quark mass, the current quark mass is set to \(m_u = m_d = 5.6\) MeV, chosen to reproduce the pion mass and decay constant in vacuum.
- At the isothermal spinodal lines, the critical exponent of the net quark number susceptibility is \(\gamma = 1/2\) for both the chiral limit and finite quark masses.
- At the critical end point (CEP) for finite quark masses, the critical exponent is \(\gamma = 2/3\), while at the tricritical point (TCP) in the chiral limit it is \(\gamma = 1/2\).
- The temperature of the CEP is approximately \(T_{\rm CEP} \simeq 80\) MeV in the NJL model with finite quark mass.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of spinodal instabilities in the first-order chiral phase transition previously studied in the context of the liquid-gas transition and in heavy-ion collisions (Heiselberg, Gavin, and others). The authors build on their earlier finding (Sasaki, Friman, Redlich, 2007) that the quark number susceptibility diverges at the isothermal spinodal lines, and here they compute the critical exponents explicitly within the NJL model and Ginzburg-Landau theory. The results enable future studies of non-equilibrium dynamics in heavy-ion collisions, where spinodal decomposition may enhance fluctuations of conserved charges, and provide a mean-field baseline for comparison with beyond-mean-field calculations that would renormalize the critical exponents.
