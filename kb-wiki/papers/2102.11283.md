# Supernova neutrino signals based on long-term axisymmetric simulations

> We study theoretical neutrino signals from core-collapse supernova (CCSN) computed using axisymmetric CCSN simulations that cover the post-bounce phase up to $\sim 4$~s. We provide basic quantities of the neutrino signals such as event rates, energy spectra, and cumulative number of events at some terrestrial neutrino detectors, and then discuss some new features in the late phase that emerge in our models. Contrary to popular belief, neutrino emissions in the late phase are not always steady, but rather have temporal fluctuations, the vigor of which hinges on the CCSN model and neutrino flavor. We find that such temporal variations are not primarily driven by proto-neutron star (PNS) convection, but by fallback accretion in exploding models. We assess the detectability of these temporal variations, and find that IceCube is the most promising detector with which to resolve them. We also update fitting formulae first proposed in our previous paper for which the total neutrino energy (TONE) emitted at the CCSN source is estimated from the cumulative number of events in each detector. This will be a powerful technique with which to analyze real observations, particularly for low-statistics data.

## Key claim

Contrary to the common assumption that neutrino emissions in the late post-bounce phase (beyond ~1 second) are steady, this paper shows they exhibit temporal fluctuations; these variations are not primarily driven by proto-neutron star (PNS) convection, as previously thought, but by fallback accretion in exploding models, with the strength of the fluctuations depending on the CCSN model and neutrino flavor.

## Method

The authors performed axisymmetric (2D) neutrino-radiation hydrodynamic simulations using the F{\sc{ornax}} code with multi-energy, multi-species two-moment (M1) neutrino transport, covering the post-bounce phase up to ~4 seconds for 15 progenitor models from 12 to 26.99 solar masses. They then used the SNOwGLoBES detector software to estimate event rates, energy spectra, and cumulative event numbers at Super-Kamiokande (SK), Hyper-Kamiokande (HK), DUNE, JUNO, and IceCube, assuming a distance of 10 kpc and adiabatic MSW flavor conversions for both normal and inverted mass hierarchies.

## Key numbers

- Simulations cover the post-bounce phase up to ~4 seconds for 15 progenitor models over a mass range of 12 to 26.99 solar masses.
- The explosion energy of the 26 solar mass model is ~2.3 x 10^51 erg.
- Detector volumes assumed: SK at 32.5 ktons, HK at 220 ktons, DUNE at 40 ktons, JUNO at 20 ktons, and IceCube at 3.5 Mtons.
- The quasi-steady component of neutrino luminosity and mass accretion rate was computed using a time window (Delta t) of 300 ms.
- Neutrino mixing parameters adopted: sin^2 theta_12 = 2.97 x 10^-1 and sin^2 theta_13 = 2.15 x 10^-2.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous 3D CCSN neutrino signal study (Nagakura et al. 2021) which was limited to the early post-bounce phase (~1 second), by using longer-term 2D simulations that cover the late phase when most neutrinos are emitted. It contradicts the assumption from earlier 1D models and toy models (e.g., Hudepohl et al. 2010, Roberts et al. 2012) that late-phase neutrino emissions are steady, and it enables more realistic predictions for next-generation detectors like Hyper-Kamiokande and DUNE, as well as improved fitting formulae to estimate total neutrino energy (TONE) from observed event counts, applicable to low-statistics data such as from SN 1987A.
