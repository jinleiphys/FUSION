# The quest for light multineutron systems

> The long history of the research concerning the possible existence of bound or resonant states in light multineutron systems, essentially $^3$n and $^4$n, is reviewed. Both the experimental and the theoretical points of view have been considered, with the aim of showing a clear picture of all the different detection and calculation techniques that have been used, with particular emphasis in the issues that have been found. Finally, some aspects of the present and future research in this field are discussed.

## Key claim

This review paper surveys the long-standing search for bound or resonant states in light multineutron systems (primarily trineutron and tetraneutron), concluding that despite several experimental claims and extensive theoretical work, no definitive evidence for such states has been established, and the field remains open with contradictory theoretical predictions even when using the same nucleon-nucleon interaction.

## Method

The authors systematically review experimental approaches across three main categories: missing-mass techniques (including pion double charge exchange reactions and multinucleon transfer reactions), two-step activation probes, and recoil detection methods. On the theoretical side, they examine ab initio calculations of few-nucleon systems in the continuum that have been developed over the last twenty years, focusing on exact solutions for three- and four-neutron systems.

## Key numbers

- The upper limit for tetraneutron binding energy from experimental separation energies decreased from S_4n(^8He) = 3.1 MeV to S_4n(^19B) = 1.5(4) MeV based on recent mass measurements (AME2016)
- If the tetraneutron were bound by more than 1 MeV, alpha+4n would be the first particle threshold in ^8He, but the breakup of ^8He is dominated by the ^6He channel, so any bound tetraneutron must be bound by less than 1 MeV
- The GANIL 2002 experiment observed 6 events with proton recoils 1.4-2.2 times higher than expected for individual neutrons, consistent with a bound tetraneutron but later reinterpreted as possibly arising from a low-energy resonance (E less than or equal to 2 MeV) or pileup effects
- The RIKEN 2016 experiment used a ^8He beam at 186 MeV/nucleon on a liquid ^4He target via the ^4He(^8He,^8Be)4n reaction

## Context

This review extends the earlier work of Ogloblin (1989) and covers the renewed interest triggered by two experimental claims: the GANIL 2002 result from Marques et al. suggesting a bound tetraneutron, and the RIKEN 2016 result from Kisamori et al. using a double charge exchange reaction with exotic nuclei. The paper enables future work by cataloging the experimental challenges (neutron detection efficiencies decreasing roughly as epsilon_xn approximately (epsilon_1n)^x, with typical single-neutron efficiencies of a few percent) and the theoretical contradictions that remain unresolved, particularly regarding whether modern ab initio methods can accommodate any bound or resonant multineutron states.
