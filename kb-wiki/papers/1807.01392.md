# An improved discretization of Schrodinger-like radial equations

> A new discretization of the radial equations that appear in the solution of separable second order partial differential equations with some rotational symmetry (as the Schrodinger equation in a central potential) is presented. It cures a pathology, related to the singular behaviour of the radial function at the origin, that suffers in some cases the discretization of the second derivative with respect to the radial coordinate. This pathology causes an enormous slowing down of the convergence to the continuum limit when the two point boundary value problem posed by the radial equation is solved as a discrete matrix eigenvalue problem. The proposed discretization is a simple solution to that problem. Some illustrative examples are discussed.

# An improved discretization of Schrödinger-like radial equations

## Key claim

The paper presents a modified finite-difference discretization of the radial second derivative that cures a pathological slowing of convergence to the continuum limit when solving radial equations as matrix eigenvalue problems; the pathology occurs specifically when the radial function behaves as \(r^\nu\) with non-integer \(\nu\) near the origin, and the cure replaces the constant centrifugal coefficient \(a_i\) with a position-dependent function \(W_n(\nu_i)\) that exactly reproduces the continuum asymptotic behavior at the origin.

## Method

The authors identify that the standard central-difference discretization of the second derivative, when applied to radial functions that behave as \(r^\nu\) near the origin, produces a relative discretization error of order \((\Delta/r)^2\) that diverges as \(r \to 0\), regardless of the grid spacing \(\Delta\). They propose a "centrifugal improved discretization" in which the coefficient \(a_i\) of the \(1/r^2\) term in the radial equation is replaced by \(W_n(\nu_i) = n^2[(1+1/n)^\nu + (1-1/n)^\nu - 2]\), where \(n = r_n/\Delta\) is the grid index; this replacement ensures that the discrete equation exactly reproduces the continuum solution \(r^\nu\) near the origin. The method is tested on the two-dimensional hydrogen atom and on fluctuations around a magnetic skyrmion, with eigenvalues computed via numerical diagonalization using the ARPACK package.

## Key numbers

- For the 2D hydrogen atom ground state (\(m=0\), \(n_r=0\)): convergence exponent \(p = 1/10\) for the simple discretization versus \(p = 5/6\) for the improved discretization, from fits to \(E/E_0 = E_{\text{exact}}/E_0 + A(\xi\Delta)^p\) over \(\xi\Delta \in [0.001, 0.02]\) (simple) and \([0.001, 0.2]\) (improved).
- For the 2D hydrogen atom first excited state (\(m=0\), \(n_r=1\)): convergence exponent \(p = 1/8\) for simple versus \(p = 7/8\) for improved discretization, from fits over \(\xi\Delta \in [0.01, 0.1]\) (simple) and \([0.01, 0.2]\) (improved).
- For the 2D hydrogen atom \(m=1\) ground state: convergence exponent \(p = 1.8\) for simple versus \(p = 2\) for improved discretization, from fits over \(\xi\Delta \in [0.01, 0.1]\).
- For the skyrmion fluctuation zero mode (\(m=1\)): convergence exponent \(p = 1/8\) for simple versus \(p = 2\) for improved discretization, from fits to \(\lambda/q_0^2 = \lambda^{(c)} + A(q_0\Delta)^p\) over \(q_0\Delta \in [0.0025, 0.02]\).
- For the skyrmion fluctuation bound states \(m=0\) and \(m=2\): both discretizations yield \(p = 2\); extrapolated continuum eigenvalues are \(\lambda^{(c)}/q_0^2 = 0.3561\) for \(m=0\) and \(0.5987\) for \(m=2\) (both discretizations agree).

## Context

This work extends the numerical treatment of radial Schrödinger-like equations arising in problems with rotational symmetry, such as central potentials and soliton fluctuations. The pathology identified, slow convergence due to singular behavior at the origin, had not been previously discussed in the literature, though related difficulties in polar coordinate discretizations are noted in Trefethen (2000). The authors encountered this problem when computing fluctuations around magnetic skyrmions in Laliena (2017), where no details of the radial solution were given. The improved discretization enables accurate numerical study of transition potentials (Frank 1971), such as the inverse-square potential relevant to electron capture by polar molecules (Levy 1967) and other experimental realizations (Desfracois 1994, Denschlag 1998).
