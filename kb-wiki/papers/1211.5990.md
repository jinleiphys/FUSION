# A comparative study of statistical models for nuclear equation of state of stellar matter

> We compare three different statistical models for the equation of state (EOS) of stellar matter at subnuclear densities and temperatures (0.5-10 MeV) expected to occur during the collapse of massive stars and supernova explosions. The models introduce the distributions of various nuclear species in nuclear statistical equilibrium, but use somewhat different nuclear physics inputs. It is demonstrated that the basic thermodynamical quantities of stellar matter under these conditions are similar, except in the region of high densities and low temperatures. We demonstrate that mass and isotopic distributions have considerable differences related to the different assumptions of the models on properties of nuclei at these stellar conditions. Overall, the three models give similar trends, but the details reflect the uncertainties related to the modeling of medium effects, such as the temperature and density dependence of surface and bulk energies of heavy nuclei, and the nuclear shell structure effects. We discuss importance of new physics inputs for astrophysical calculations from experimental data obtained in intermediate energy heavy-ion collisions, in particular, the similarities of the conditions reached during supernova explosions and multifragmentation reactions.

## Key claim

The three statistical models (SMSM, HS, and FYSS) produce similar basic thermodynamic quantities for stellar matter at subnuclear densities and temperatures of 0.5-10 MeV, except in the region of high densities and low temperatures, but the mass and isotopic distributions show considerable differences that reflect uncertainties in modeling medium effects such as temperature and density dependence of surface and bulk energies of heavy nuclei and nuclear shell structure effects.

## Method

The authors compared three statistical models (SMSM, HS, and FYSS) that all assume nuclear statistical equilibrium but use different nuclear physics inputs: SMSM uses a liquid-drop model with temperature-dependent bulk and surface terms; HS uses experimental and theoretical nuclear masses with a temperature-dependent internal partition function and a relativistic mean-field (RMF) description for unbound nucleons; FYSS uses an extended liquid-drop model with experimental binding energies and RMF for unbound nucleons. The models were evaluated over wide intervals of temperature (0.5-10 MeV), baryon density, and electron fraction, and the resulting thermodynamic quantities, mass distributions, and isotopic distributions were compared.

## Key numbers

- Temperature range considered: 0.5-10 MeV, expected during collapse of massive stars and supernova explosions
- Baryon density range considered: approximately 10^{-10} to 3 times normal nuclear density (rho_0 approximately 0.15 fm^{-3})
- SMSM model parameters: w_0 = 16 MeV (bulk), epsilon_0 = 16 MeV, beta_0 = 18 MeV (surface), gamma = 25 MeV (symmetry), T_c = 18 MeV (critical temperature)
- HS model uses level density parameter a(A) = A/8 (1 - 0.8 A^{-1/3}) MeV^{-1}
- SMSM uses fragments with 1 <= A <= 1000 and 0 <= Z <= A
- FYSS considers nuclei up to atomic number 1000 and neutron number 1000
- Critical temperature extracted from experiments on finite nuclei: T_c approximately 17-20 MeV
- Normal nuclear density: rho_0 approximately 0.15 fm^{-3} (i.e., m_N rho_0 approximately 2.5 x 10^{14} g/cm^3)

## Context

This work extends previous equation of state studies for supernova matter, including the single-average-nucleus approach of Lamb and Lattimer (1970s) and the relativistic mean-field EOS of Shen (1998), by comparing three modern statistical models that include full ensembles of nuclear species. The comparison identifies which differences in nuclear composition arise from model assumptions versus genuine physical uncertainties, enabling future construction of a unified EOS that incorporates the best-verified components from each model, and motivates using experimental data from intermediate-energy heavy-ion multifragmentation reactions to constrain the properties of hot nuclei in stellar environments.
