# Evolution of the N=28 shell closure: a test bench for nuclear forces

> The evolution of the N=28 shell closure is investigated far from stability. Using the latest results obtained from various experimental techniques, we discuss the main properties of the N=28 isotones, as well as those of the N=27 and N=29 isotones. Experimental results are confronted to various theoretical predictions. These studies pinpoint the effects of several terms of the nucleon-nucleon interaction, such as the central, the spin-orbit, the tensor and the three-body force components, to account for the modification of the N=28 shell gap and spin-orbit splittings. Analogies between the evolution of the N=28 shell closure and other magic numbers originating from the spin-orbit interaction are proposed (N=14,50, 82 and 90). More generally, questions related to the evolution of nuclear forces towards the drip-line, in bubble nuclei, and for nuclei involved in the r-process nucleosynthesis are proposed and discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the evolution of the N=28 shell closure far from stability is governed by a combination of the spin-orbit, tensor, and three-body force components of the nucleon-nucleon interaction, not solely by the spin-orbit force as in the original mean-field picture. The authors hedge that while the simplified spin-orbit picture is elegant, the observed shell-gap modifications arise from complex, combined effects that are best disentangled in the intermediate-mass N=28 isotones.

## Method

The authors compiled and reviewed experimental results from beta-decay studies, atomic mass measurements, Coulomb excitation, transfer reactions, and in-beam gamma-ray spectroscopy for N=28 isotones and neighboring N=27 and N=29 isotones. They compared these experimental data to theoretical predictions from shell-model calculations (using the sdpf-U interaction) and mean-field models (relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov with DD-PC1 functional and Gogny D1S force). The analysis focused on two-neutron separation energies, excitation energies of 2+ and 4+ states, B(E2) transition probabilities, and single-particle energy evolutions derived from transfer reactions.

## Key numbers

* The N=28 shell gap grows by about 2.7 MeV between 41Ca and 49Ca, reaching a value of 4.8 MeV at 49Ca.
* The spacing between the proton 2s1/2 and 1d3/2 orbits in the K isotopic chain drops from 2.52 MeV at N=20 to -0.29 MeV at N=28, indicating the orbits have crossed.
* The 2+1 state in 42Si is at 770(19) keV, and the 4+1 state is at 2173(14) keV, giving an energy ratio R4/2 of about 2.9, corresponding to a well-deformed rotor.
* The B(E2) value in 46Ar was measured as 218(31) e2fm4 by one technique and 570 (+335/-160) e2fm4 by another, showing a significant discrepancy.
* The 0+2 isomeric state in 44S is at 1365(1) keV, with the 2+1 state at 1329(1) keV.
* The monopole interaction difference Vpn(1d3/2,1f7/2) - Vpn(2s1/2,1f7/2) is approximately -350 keV.
* The proton d5/2 - d3/2 splitting in the K isotopic chain is reduced by about 1.7 MeV between N=20 and N=28.

## Context

This review updates the authors' previous work on the N=28 shell closure (Sorlin and Porquet, 2008) and extends the discussion to include the roles of tensor and three-body forces. The paper draws analogies between the evolution of N=28 and other spin-orbit magic numbers (N=14, 50, 82, and 90), and it sets the stage for future experimental challenges including measurements of B(E2) values in 42Si and 46Ar, studies of 40Mg, and investigations of bubble nuclei and r-process nucleosynthesis. The work confronts experimental results with shell-model calculations by Nowacki and Poves (2009) and mean-field models by Li et al. (2011) and Rodriguez-Guzman et al. (2002).
