# Perturbations of self-gravitating, ellipsoidal superfluid-normal fluid mixtures

> We study the perturbation modes of rotating superfluid ellipsoidal figures of equilibrium in the framework of the two-fluid superfluid hydrodynamics and Newtonian gravity. Our calculations focus on linear perturbations of background equilibria in which the two fluids move together, the total density is uniform, and the densities of the two components are proportional to one another, with ratios that are independent of position. The motions of the two fluids are coupled by mutual friction, as formulated by Khalatnikov. We show that there are two general classes of modes for small perturbations: one class in which the two fluids move together and the other in which there is relative motion between them. The former are identical to the modes found for a single fluid, except that the rate of viscous dissipation, when computed in the secular (or ``low Reynolds number'') approximation under the assumption of a constant kinematic viscosity, is diminished by a factor $f_N$, the fraction of the total mass in the normal fluid. The relative modes are completely new, and are studied in detail for a range of values for the phenomenological mutual friction coefficients, relative densities of the superfluid and normal components, and, for Roche ellipsoids, binary mass ratios. We find that there are no new secular instabilities connected with the relative motions of the two fluid components. Moreover, although the new modes are subject to viscous dissipation (a consequence of viscosity of the normal matter), they do not emit gravitational radiation at all.

## Key claim

The paper shows that for rotating superfluid-normal fluid mixtures in ellipsoidal figures of equilibrium, there are two distinct classes of perturbation modes: one where both fluids move together (identical to single-fluid modes, but with viscous dissipation reduced by the normal fluid fraction \(f_N\)) and one where the fluids move relative to each other. Crucially, these new relative modes do not emit gravitational radiation and introduce no new secular instabilities, though they are subject to viscous dissipation from the normal component.

## Method

The authors extend Chandrasekhar's tensor virial method to two-fluid superfluid hydrodynamics with Newtonian gravity, using Khalatnikov's formulation of mutual friction forces between the superfluid and normal components. They take moments of the linearized perturbation equations for uniform-density ellipsoids (Maclaurin, Jacobi, and Roche types) where both fluids initially co-rotate with proportional densities. The relative displacement modes are derived from first-moment equations, yielding characteristic equations that include mutual friction coefficients \(\beta\), \(\beta'\), and \(\beta''\) (or equivalently drag parameters \(\eta\) and \(\eta'\)).

## Key numbers

- Viscous dissipation in the co-moving modes is diminished by a factor \(f_N\), the fraction of total mass in the normal fluid.
- For Maclaurin spheroids, the relative transverse shear modes yield a sixth-order characteristic equation; in the frictionless limit, the three modes are real and given by \(\sigma_1 = 2\Omega/3 + (s_+ + s_-)\) and \(\sigma_{2,3} = 2\Omega/3 - (1/2)(s_+ + s_-) \pm (i\sqrt{3}/2)(s_+ - s_-)\), where \(s_\pm\) are defined via cubic expressions involving \(\Omega\) and the index coefficients \(A_1, A_3\).
- The damping of relative transverse shear modes is maximal when \(\eta = 1\) (in units of \(\rho_S \omega_S\)) and decreases to zero as \(\eta \to 0\) or \(\eta \to \infty\).
- The dynamical relaxation time scale for vortex motion is \(\tau_D = (1/\langle \omega_S \rangle)(\eta/(\rho_S \omega_S) + \rho_S \omega_S / \eta)\), with minimum value when \(\eta / (\rho_S \omega_S) = 1\).

## Context

This work extends Chandrasekhar's classical theory of ellipsoidal figures of equilibrium (Chandrasekhar, 1969) to include superfluid-normal fluid mixtures, relevant for neutron star interiors where baryon fluids form superfluid condensates. It builds on prior studies of superfluid neutron star oscillations by Epstein (1988), Lindblom and Mendell (1994), and Comer et al. (1999), but allows for arbitrarily fast rotation within the Newtonian ellipsoidal approximation. The results enable future studies of how superfluidity affects oscillation modes and stability in rapidly rotating neutron stars, with implications for gravitational wave emission and pulsar spin limits.
