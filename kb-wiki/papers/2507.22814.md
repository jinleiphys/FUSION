# Quantum Simulation of Nuclear Dynamics in First Quantization

> The study of real time dynamics of nuclear systems is of great importance to provide theoretical predictions of cross sections relevant for both terrestrial experiments as well as applications in astrophysics. First principles simulations of these dynamical processes is however hindered by an exponential cost in classical resources and the possibility of performing scalable simulations using quantum computers is currently an active field of research. In this work we provide the first complete characterization of the resource requirements for studying nuclear dynamics with the full Leading Order (LO) pionless EFT Hamiltonian in first quantization employing simulation strategies using both product formulas as well as Quantum Signal Processing. In particular, we show that time evolution of such an Hamiltonian can be performed with polynomial resources in the number of particles, and logarithmic resources in the number of single-particle basis states. This result provides an exponential improvement compared with previous work on the same Hamiltonian model in second quantization. We find that interesting simulations for low energy nuclear scattering could be achievable with tens of millions of T gates and few hundred logical qubits suggesting that the study of simple nuclear reactions could be amenable for early fault tolerant quantum platforms.

## Key claim

This work provides the first complete characterization of quantum resource requirements for simulating nuclear dynamics with the full leading-order pionless effective field theory Hamiltonian in first quantization, showing that time evolution can be performed with polynomial resources in the number of particles and logarithmic resources in the number of single-particle basis states, which represents an exponential improvement in volume scaling compared to previous second-quantization approaches, though at the cost of polynomially worse scaling in particle number.

## Method

The authors encode each nucleon's position on a d-dimensional spatial lattice with M sites per direction using d*log2(M) qubits plus 2 qubits for spin and isospin, for a total of (d*log2(M)+2)*eta qubits for eta particles. They implement time evolution using both product formulas (specifically second-order and higher-order Trotter-Suzuki decompositions) and quantum signal processing (QSP) / generalized quantum signal processing (GQSP), with the kinetic energy implemented via quantum Fourier transforms and diagonal phase gates, and the potential energy implemented via multi-controlled rotation gates that check for particles occupying the same lattice site.

## Key numbers

- For second-order product formulas in first quantization, the T-gate cost scales as O-tilde(t^(3/2) * eta^(3/2) / epsilon^(1/2) * log(t*Omega*eta/epsilon) * (eta^2 + log(Omega))), where t is total time, epsilon is target error, eta is number of particles, and Omega is the size of the single-particle space
- For QSP/GQSP in first quantization, the T-gate cost scales as O((eta*t + log(eta*t/epsilon)) * log(Omega) * (eta + log(eta*t*log(Omega)/epsilon)))
- The number of qubits required for first-quantization QSP/GQSP is O(eta*log(Omega) + log(t/epsilon))
- The kinetic energy norm bound is lambda_T = (hbar^2/(2*mu*a^2)) * d * eta * pi^2, where a is the lattice spacing
- The kinetic energy T-gate cost scales as O-tilde[eta * log(Omega) * log(t*Omega*eta/epsilon)]
- The potential energy T-gate cost scales as O[eta^3 * (m + log(eta/epsilon))] where m = log2(M)

## Context

This work extends the first-quantization approach to nuclear dynamics simulation introduced by Weiss et al. (2025) for pionless EFT without three-body interactions, and provides an exponential improvement in volume scaling compared to the second-quantization approaches of Roggero (2020) and Watson et al. (2023), which required O(Omega) qubits and O(Omega) gate cost. The results suggest that low-energy nuclear scattering simulations could be achievable on early fault-tolerant quantum platforms with tens of millions of T gates and few hundred logical qubits, enabling future work on ab-initio nuclear reaction calculations that are classically intractable.
