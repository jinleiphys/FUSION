# IMSRG-Net: A machine learning-based solver for In-Medium Similarity Renormalization Group

> We present a novel method, IMSRG-Net, which utilizes machine learning techniques as a solver for the in-medium Similarity Renormalization Group (IMSRG). The primary objective of IMSRG-Net is to approximate the Magnus operators $Ω(s)$ in the IMSRG flow equation, thereby offering an alternative to the computationally intensive part of IMSRG calculations. The key idea of IMSRG-Net is its design of the loss function inspired by physics-informed neural networks to encode the underlying {\it physics}, i.e., IMSRG flow equation, into the model. Through training on a dataset comprising ten data points with flow parameters up to $s = 20$, capturing approximately one-eighth to one-quarter of the entire flow, IMSRG-Net exhibits remarkable accuracy in extrapolating the ground state energies and charge radii of ${}^{16}$O and ${}^{40}$Ca. Furthermore, this model demonstrates effectiveness in deriving effective interactions for a valence space.

## Key claim
IMSRG-Net uses a physics-informed neural network trained on only ten data points (flow parameter s up to 20, covering roughly one-eighth to one-quarter of the full IMSRG flow) to extrapolate Magnus operators and thereby reproduce ground-state energies and charge radii of ¹⁶O and ⁴⁰Ca, as well as effective valence-space interactions, with the caveat that the method is a proof-of-concept and does not yet accelerate cutting-edge IMSRG calculations on supercomputers.

## Method
The Magnus operator Ω(s) from the IMSRG flow equation is approximated by a fully connected neural network with three hidden layers (48, 48, and 16 nodes) using Tanh and Softplus activations. The loss function combines mean-squared error on Ω(s) and on its derivative η(s) = dΩ/ds (computed via numerical differentiation), with a weighting factor λ_η = 10². Training uses AdamW optimizer with online learning (batch size equal to the 10 training points at s = 17.75, 18.0, …, 20.0); validation uses s = 16.5–17.5, and test data is the converged IMSRG(2) result at s = ∞.

## Key numbers
- Ground-state energy of ¹⁶O with EM500 interaction at e_max = 10: IMSRG(2) = –165.5359 MeV, IMSRG-Net = –165.5357 MeV (difference < 1 keV).
- Ground-state energy of ⁴⁰Ca with EM500 interaction at e_max = 10: IMSRG(2) = –594.0242 MeV, IMSRG-Net = –594.0246 MeV.
- Charge radius of ¹⁶O with EM500 at e_max = 10: IMSRG(2) = 2.2485 fm, IMSRG-Net = 2.2484 fm (difference ~0.0001 fm).
- Charge radius of ⁴⁰Ca with EM500 at e_max = 10: IMSRG(2) = 2.5894 fm, IMSRG-Net = 2.5895 fm.
- Training data: 10 points with flow parameter s from 17.75 to 20.0; validation: 5 points from s = 16.5 to 17.5.
- Computational time for e_max = 10: full IMSRG(2) ~6 hours (i9-10940X) or ~4 hours (Xeon Platinum 8360Y); IMSRG-Net ~2.3 hours (RTX A4000) or ~1.2 hours (A100); reduction factor ~0.4–0.6.
- Operator vector size at e_max = 10: 18,345,624 elements (70 MB in Float32).

## Context
This work extends the IMSRG method (Tsukiyama 2011, Hergert 2016, Stroberg 2019) by replacing the computationally intensive numerical integration of the flow equation with a machine-learning surrogate. It contrasts with eigenvector continuation (Frame 2018, Avik 2021), which operates on many-body wave functions rather than operators. The approach enables rapid extrapolation of IMSRG results for ground-state properties and valence-space effective interactions, but current GPU memory limits it to e_max ≤ 10; future work may use latent-space or tensor-decomposition techniques to handle larger model spaces.
