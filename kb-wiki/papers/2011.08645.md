# The origin of the elements and other implications of gravitational wave detection for nuclear physics

> The neutron-star collision revealed by the event GW170817 gave us a first glimpse of a possible birthplace of most of our heavy elements. The multi-messenger nature of this historical event combined gravitational waves, a gamma-ray burst and optical astronomy of a ``kilonova'', bringing the first observations of rapid neutron capture (r process) nucleosynthesis after 60 years of speculation. Modeling the r process requires a prodigious amount of nuclear-physics ingredients: practically all the quantum state and interaction properties of virtually all neutron-rich nuclides, many of which may never be produced in the laboratory! Another essential contribution of nuclear physics to neutron stars (and their eventual coalescence) is the equation of state (EoS) that defines their structure and composition. The EoS, combined with the knowledge of nuclear binding energies, determines the elemental profile of the outer crust of a neutron star and the relationship between its radius and mass. In addition, the EoS determines the form of the gravitational wave signal. This article combines a tutorial presentation and bibliography with recent results that link nuclear mass spectrometry to gravitational waves via neutron stars.

## Key claim

The neutron-star merger GW170817 provides the first observational evidence that such events are a possible birthplace for most heavy elements via rapid neutron capture (r-process) nucleosynthesis, but modeling the r-process requires nuclear-physics input for neutron-rich nuclides that may never be produced in the laboratory, so theoretical models remain essential.

## Method

The paper combines a tutorial review with recent results linking nuclear mass spectrometry to neutron-star physics and gravitational waves. It discusses how Penning-trap mass measurements of exotic nuclides (such as 79Cu and 82Zn at CERN's ISOLDE facility) constrain the composition of neutron-star crusts, and how the nuclear equation of state (EoS) derived from Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov mass models (the Brussels-Montreal HFB series) determines neutron-star mass-radius relations and gravitational-wave signals.

## Key numbers

* GW170817 occurred 130 million light-years away in the Hydra constellation
* The canonical neutron star has about 1.4 solar masses; two-solar-mass neutron stars have been detected (J1614-2230 at 1.928 solar masses, J0348+0432 at 2.1 solar masses, J0740+6620 at 2.14 solar masses)
* The outer crust of a neutron star extends to a depth of about 240 meters based on experimentally constrained binding energies
* Neutron drip-line density is 4 x 10^11 g/cm^3 (2.5 x 10^-4 nucleons/fm^3)
* The outer crust reaches nuclear-matter density of about 2 x 10^8 g/cm^3
* Between 6000 and 8000 nuclides are predicted to exist as bound systems, depending on the mass model used
* Over 3000 isotopes have had their masses measured

## Context

This paper extends the line of work from B2FH (Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, and Hoyle, 1957) and Cameron (1957) on stellar nucleosynthesis, and from Lattimer and Schramm (1974) on neutron-star mergers as r-process sites. It connects the Brussels-Montreal HFB mass models (HFB-1 through HFB-32, developed from 2002 onward) to neutron-star observations, showing how mass measurements of exotic nuclides like 79Cu and 82Zn by the ISOLTRAP collaboration constrain crust composition. The work enables future gravitational-wave detections to further constrain the nuclear equation of state, as demonstrated by Bauswein et al. (recent study) and discussed by Tews, Margueron and Reddy (recent study) regarding phase transitions in neutron-star cores.
