# What is ab initio?

> Microscopic nuclear theory is based on the tenet that atomic nuclei can be accurately described as collections of point-like nucleons interacting via two- and many-body forces obeying nonrelativistic quantum mechanics -- and the concept of the ab initio approach is to calculate nuclei accordingly. The forces are fixed in free-space scattering and must be accurate. We will critically review the history of this approach from the early beginnings until today. An analysis of current ab initio calculations reveals that some mistakes of history are being repeated today. The ultimate goal of nuclear theory are high-precision ab initio calculations which, as it turns out, may be possible only at the fifths order of the chiral expansion. Thus, for its fulfillment, nuclear theory is still facing an enormous task.

## Key claim

The paper argues that many calculations published under the label of "ab initio" are not truly ab initio because they fail to use forces that are both accurate within the theoretical uncertainty of chiral effective field theory (EFT) at a given order and consistent in their theoretical foundation. The ultimate goal of high-precision ab initio calculations may be possible only at the fifth order (N4LO) of the chiral expansion, meaning nuclear theory still faces an enormous task.

## Method

The author critically reviews the history of microscopic nuclear theory from the early Brueckner theory (1954) through the phenomenological effective interactions of the 1970s and 1980s to the modern chiral EFT era after 2000. Four representative contemporary approaches to medium-mass nuclei are examined against the criteria of whether the free-space forces are accurate (predicting experiment within the EFT truncation error) and whether the forces are constructed consistently from theory. The analysis includes comparisons of ground-state energies, charge radii, and nuclear matter saturation properties.

## Key numbers

- Early BHF calculations reproduced about one half of the binding energies of closed-shell nuclei (no specific numerical value given for the half).
- Negele adjusted the Reid soft-core potential binding of nuclear matter from 11 MeV per nucleon to the presumed empirical value of 15.68 MeV.
- The "GO" group's NNLO potentials produce predictions up to 40 times outside the theoretical error of chiral EFT at NNLO.
- In the "Hoppe" case, NNLO and N3LO calculations of finite nuclei up to 68Ni predict underbinding and slightly too large radii.
- In the "Huther" case, nuclei up to 78Ni are reproduced accurately, but nuclear matter shows overbinding and no saturation at realistic densities.
- At N4LO, 13 new three-nucleon contact terms occur, and the 3NF includes all 20 operators of the most general 3NF.

## Context

This work extends the author's long-standing critique of phenomenological approaches in nuclear theory, echoing the abandonment of microscopic methods around 1973 as reflected in Baranger's lead talk at the 1973 International Conference on Nuclear Physics in Munich. The paper directly contrasts the "Magic" force (Hebeler 2011, 2021), the "GO" potentials (Ekstrom 2018, Jiang 2020), the "Hoppe" interactions (Hoppe 2019), and the "Huther" interactions (Huther 2020), finding that only the latter two qualify as truly ab initio. The analysis enables a roadmap toward N4LO calculations as the necessary next step for precision tests of the fundamental tenet of nuclear theory.
