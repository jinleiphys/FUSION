# Nuclear ground states in a consistent implementation of the time-dependent density matrix approach

> Background: Time-dependent techniques in nuclear theory often rely on mean-field or Hartree-Fock descriptions. Beyond mean-field dynamical calculations within the time-dependent density matrix (TDDM) theory have often invoked symmetry restrictions and ignored the connection between the mean-field and the induced interaction. Purpose: We study the ground states obtained in a TDDM approach for nuclei from $A=12$ to $A=24$, including examples of even and odd-even nuclei with and without intrinsic deformation. We overcome previous limitations using three-dimensional simulations and employ density-independent Skyrme interactions self-consistently. Methods: The correlated ground states are found starting from the Hartree-Fock solution, by adiabatically including the beyond-mean-field terms in real time. Results: We find that, within this approach, correlations are responsible for $\approx 4-5 \%$ of the total energy. Radii are generally unaffected by the introduction of beyond mean-field correlations. Large nuclear correlation entropies are associated to large correlation energies. By all measures, $^{12}$C is the most correlated isotope in the mass region considered. Conclusions: Our work is the starting point of a consistent implementation of the TDDM technique for applications into nuclear reactions. Our results indicate that correlation effects in structure are small, but beyond-mean-field dynamical simulations could provide new insight into several issues of interest.

# Nuclear ground states in a consistent implementation of the time-dependent density matrix approach

## Key claim

Correlation effects in nuclear ground states from A=12 to A=24 account for approximately 4-5% of the total energy within a TDDM approach that uses the same Skyrme interaction for both mean-field and residual channels, but the authors note these interactions were fitted at the mean-field level and therefore do not expect the calculations to provide a good match to experimental data.

## Method

The authors implement a fully unrestricted three-dimensional TDDM approach using density-independent Skyrme interactions (SV and SHZ2 parametrizations) for both the mean-field and residual interaction channels, overcoming previous symmetry restrictions. Correlated ground states are obtained by starting from the Hartree-Fock solution and adiabatically switching on the beyond-mean-field terms in real time using a Gaussian factor, with the BBGKY hierarchy truncated at the two-body correlation level.

## Key numbers

- Correlation energy for $^{12}$C with SV interaction: -5.5 MeV for $N_\text{max}=14$ and -8.1 MeV for $N_\text{max}=20$
- Correlation energy as percentage of total energy for $^{12}$C: 7.72% for $N_\text{max}=14$ and 11.23% for $N_\text{max}=20$
- HF binding energy for $^{16}$O with SV: 113.536 MeV compared to experimental 127.616 MeV
- HF charge radius for $^{12}$C with SV: 2.724 fm compared to experimental 2.4702 fm
- HF binding energies underestimate experimental values by about 12% on average
- HF charge radii overestimate experimental values by about 3% on average
- Adiabatic switching time parameter $\tau_2 = 32000$ fm$^2 c^{-2}$, corresponding to physical changes on a timescale of approximately 180 fm c$^{-1}$
- Time step used in all calculations: $dt = 0.2$ fm c$^{-1}$
- Grid spacing: $\Delta x = \Delta y = \Delta z = 1$ fm from -9.5 to 9.5 fm ($N_x = 20$)

## Context

This work extends the TDDM approach beyond previous implementations that imposed spherical or axial symmetry restrictions, as in the work of Tohyama (various years, 1987-2020) and others, and that used simplified residual interactions decoupled from the mean-field interaction. The authors use the same Skyrme interaction for both channels, unlike prior approaches that employed a simplified delta-function residual interaction. This consistent implementation enables future application of TDDM to nuclear reactions, particularly fusion and breakup processes, where beyond-mean-field dynamical simulations could provide new insight.
