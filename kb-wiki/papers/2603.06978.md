# Ab initio study of the halo structure in $^{11}$Be

> We present an ab initio study of the one-neutron halo nucleus $^{11}$Be using nuclear lattice effective field theory with high-fidelity chiral interactions at N3LO. By employing the wavefunction matching method to mitigate the sign problem and the pinhole algorithm to sample many-body correlations, we successfully reproduce the ground-state parity inversion and the extended matter radius characteristic of the halo structure. We analyze the intrinsic density distributions and geometric shapes of $^{11}$Be in comparison with the core nucleus $^{10}$Be. Our results reveal a prominent two-cluster structure in both nuclei and the occupation of the $σ$ molecular orbital by the valence neutron in $^{11}$Be. It enhances the prolate deformation as well as the diffuse neutron tail, distinct from the $π$-orbital occupation observed in the $^{10}$Be ground state.

## Method
The authors performed an ab initio lattice calculation of $^{11}$Be using chiral interactions at next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order (N3LO). They applied the wavefunction matching method to control the fermion sign problem inherent in lattice Monte Carlo simulations, and used the pinhole algorithm to extract intrinsic density distributions and geometric shapes. The results were analyzed by comparing the intrinsic structure of $^{11}$Be with that of its core nucleus $^{10}$Be.

## Key numbers
* The ground-state parity inversion in $^{11}$Be was successfully reproduced, confirming the halo structure.
* The extended matter radius characteristic of the halo structure was reproduced.
* The valence neutron in $^{11}$Be occupies the $\sigma$ molecular orbital, enhancing prolate deformation and the diffuse neutron tail, distinct from the $\pi$-orbital occupation observed in the $^{10}$Be ground state.

## Context
This work extends ab initio studies of halo nuclei by applying nuclear lattice effective field theory to $^{11}$Be, a benchmark one-neutron halo system. It builds on prior lattice EFT calculations of light nuclei and the development of the wavefunction matching method to overcome the sign problem. The results enable future microscopic studies of molecular orbital structures in neutron-rich nuclei and the interplay between clustering and halo formation.
