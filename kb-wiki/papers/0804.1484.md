# Microscopic models and effective equation of state in nuclear collisions at FAIR energies

> Two microscopic models, UrQMD and QGSM, were employed to study the formation of locally equilibrated hot and dense nuclear matter in heavy-ion collisions at energies from 11.6 AGeV to 160 AGeV. Analysis was performed for the fixed central cubic cell of volume V = 125 fm**3 and for the expanding cell which followed the growth of the central area with uniformly distributed energy. To decide whether or not the equilibrium was reached, results of the microscopic calculations were compared to that of the statistical thermal model. Both dynamical models indicate that the state of kinetic, thermal and chemical equilibrium is nearly approached at any bombarding energy after a certain relaxation period. The higher the energy, the shorter the relaxation time. Equation of state has a simple linear dependence P = a(sqrt{s})*e, where a = c_s**2 is the sound velocity squared. It varies from 0.12 \pm 0.01 at E_{lab} = 11.6 AGeV to 0.145 \pm 0.005 at E_{lab} = 160 AGeV. Change of the slope in a(sqrt{s}) behavior occurs at E_{lab} = 40 AGeV and can be assigned to the transition from baryon-rich to meson-dominated matter. The phase diagrams in the T - mu_B plane show the presence of kinks along the lines of constant entropy per baryon. These kinks are linked to the inelastic (i.e. chemical) freeze-out in the system.

## Key claim
Both the UrQMD and QGSM microscopic transport models indicate that a state of kinetic, thermal, and chemical equilibrium is nearly approached in the central cell of heavy-ion collisions at energies from 11.6 AGeV to 160 AGeV, but only after a certain relaxation period and with the caveat that the pion yield remains systematically overproduced relative to the statistical model, especially in QGSM.

## Method
Central Au+Au collisions at zero impact parameter were simulated using the UrQMD and QGSM Monte Carlo event generators at bombarding energies of 11.6, 20, 30, 40, 80, and 160 AGeV. Thermodynamic quantities (energy density, baryon density, strangeness density) were extracted from a fixed central cubic cell of volume 125 fm³ and from an expanding cell that followed the growth of the central area with uniformly distributed energy. These microscopic quantities were then used as input to a statistical model of an ideal hadron gas to solve for temperature, baryon chemical potential, and strangeness chemical potential; agreement between microscopic and macroscopic particle yields and energy spectra was used to judge whether equilibrium was reached.

## Key numbers
- Sound velocity squared a = c_s² varies from 0.12 ± 0.01 at E_lab = 11.6 AGeV to 0.145 ± 0.005 at E_lab = 160 AGeV.
- A change of slope in a(√s) behavior occurs at E_lab = 40 AGeV, assigned to the transition from baryon-rich to meson-dominated matter.
- In the fixed central cell of volume V = 125 fm³, baryon density cannot exceed about 5ρ₀ (with ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³) regardless of bombarding energy; in a smaller cell of volume 0.125 fm³, baryon density can reach as high as 20ρ₀.
- At E_lab = 40 AGeV, the temperature extracted from UrQMD at t = 9 fm/c is 160.2 MeV with baryon chemical potential 341.6 MeV; at E_lab = 20 AGeV at t = 11 fm/c, temperature is 144.5 MeV with baryon chemical potential 450.5 MeV.
- Pressure becomes isotropic at t ≤ 10 fm/c for both models, with the time of convergence decreasing with rising bombarding energy.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of equilibration in heavy-ion collisions using the UrQMD model (Bravina et al., 1998-2006) and QGSM (Bleibel et al., 2006) by introducing an expanding cell analysis and by comparing results from two different microscopic models to reduce model-dependent effects. The finding of kinks in the T-μ_B phase diagrams along lines of constant entropy per baryon, linked to chemical freeze-out, was not seen in previous studies due to averaging over large volumes. The results provide an effective equation of state for the FAIR energy range and enable more reliable extraction of thermodynamic properties from transport model simulations, particularly for the transition region around 40 AGeV where baryon and meson energy contributions become equal.
