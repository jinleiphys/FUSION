# The Importance of Correlations and Fluctuations on the Initial Source Eccentricity in High-Energy Nucleus-Nucleus Collisions

> In this paper, we investigate various ways of defining the initial source eccentricity using the Monte Carlo Glauber (MCG) approach. In particular, we examine the participant eccentricity, which quantifies the eccentricity of the initial source shape by the major axes of the ellipse formed by the interaction points of the participating nucleons. We show that reasonable variation of the density parameters in the Glauber calculation, as well as variations in how matter production is modeled, do not significantly modify the already established behavior of the participant eccentricity as a function of collision centrality. Focusing on event-by-event fluctuations and correlations of the distributions of participating nucleons we demonstrate that, depending on the achieved event-plane resolution, fluctuations in the elliptic flow magnitude $v_2$ lead to most measurements being sensitive to the root-mean-square, rather than the mean of the $v_2$ distribution. Neglecting correlations among participants, we derive analytical expressions for the participant eccentricity cumulants as a function of the number of participating nucleons, $\Npart$,keeping non-negligible contributions up to $\ordof{1/\Npart^3}$. We find that the derived expressions yield the same results as obtained from mixed-event MCG calculations which remove the correlations stemming from the nuclear collision process. Most importantly, we conclude from the comparison with MCG calculations that the fourth order participant eccentricity cumulant does not approach the spatial anisotropy obtained assuming a smooth nuclear matter distribution. In particular, for the Cu+Cu system, these quantities deviate from each other by almost a factor of two over a wide range in centrality.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the fourth-order participant eccentricity cumulant does not approach the spatial anisotropy obtained from a smooth nuclear matter distribution, and for Cu+Cu collisions these quantities deviate by almost a factor of two over a wide centrality range, reflecting the essential role of participant spatial correlations in the interaction of two nuclei.

## Method

The authors used Monte Carlo Glauber (MCG) calculations to simulate Au+Au and Cu+Cu collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV, distributing nucleons according to a Fermi distribution and determining collisions based on the nucleon-nucleon inelastic cross section. They computed the participant eccentricity event-by-event from the transverse positions of participating nucleons in their individual major axis frames, and derived analytical expressions for eccentricity cumulants neglecting correlations among participants, comparing these to full MCG results.

## Key numbers

- For Cu+Cu collisions, the fourth-order participant eccentricity cumulant and the spatial anisotropy from a smooth nuclear matter distribution deviate from each other by almost a factor of two over a wide range in centrality
- The nucleon-nucleon inelastic cross section used was sigma_NN = 42 mb at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV
- For Au+Au: nuclear radius R=6.38 fm, skin depth a=0.535 fm, atomic mass number 197
- For Cu+Cu: nuclear radius R=4.20 fm, skin depth a=0.596 fm, atomic mass number 63
- The minimum inter-nucleon separation distance was set to d_min=0 fm for default calculations

## Context

This work extends the participant eccentricity concept introduced by Manly (2005) and Alver (2006) for understanding elliptic flow scaling in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC. The paper contradicts the assumption from Bhalerao (2006) that analytical cumulant expressions neglecting spatial correlations would adequately describe the initial eccentricity, showing instead that correlations among participating nucleons produce significant deviations, particularly for smaller systems like Cu+Cu. These findings enable more accurate interpretation of elliptic flow measurements and their scaling with initial geometry across different collision systems.
