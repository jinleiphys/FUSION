# Superluminal pions in a hadronic fluid

> We study the propagation of pions at finite temperature and finite chemical potential in the framework of the linear sigma model with 2 quark flavors and $N_c$ colors. The velocity of massless pions in general differs from that of light. One-loop calculations show that in the chiral symmetry broken phase pions, under certain conditions, propagate faster than light.

## Key claim

One-loop calculations in the linear sigma model with two quark flavors show that massless pions in the chiral symmetry broken phase propagate faster than light in a certain range of temperature and baryon chemical potential, but only when the negative fermionic contribution to the pion self-energy exceeds the positive bosonic contribution.

## Method

The authors compute the pion self-energy at one-loop order in the linear sigma model with constituent quarks (SU(2) flavor, N_c colors) at finite temperature T and chemical potential μ. From the second derivatives of the self-energy at zero external momentum, they extract the coefficients a and b in the effective pion kinetic term, which determine the pion velocity v² = (1 + b/a)⁻¹. The chiral condensate σ(T,μ) is obtained by minimizing the one-loop thermodynamic potential, and the velocity is evaluated numerically across the broken-symmetry phase.

## Key numbers

- Pion velocity becomes superluminal (v > 1) at temperatures above approximately T_SL ≈ 50 MeV for the chosen parameters (m_F = 340 MeV, f_π = 92.4 MeV, m_σ = 796.5 MeV).
- The critical chemical potential for chiral symmetry restoration at T=0 is given by μ_c = (λπ²/2)^{1/4} f_π, with the bound μ_c ≤ m_F = 340 MeV.
- The sigma mass upper bound from the condition μ_c ≤ m_F is m_σ ≤ 2 m_F²/(π f_π), which yields m_σ = 796.5 MeV when saturated.
- For periodic fermions in a compact spatial dimension of size L, the pion velocity v_|| is always superluminal and approaches 1 as L → ∞.
- For antiperiodic fermions in a compact spatial dimension, the velocity plot is the inverse of the finite-temperature case, with superluminal or subluminal behavior depending on L.

## Context

This work extends prior one-loop calculations of pion velocity in the linear sigma model without fermions, which found subluminal propagation (Pisarski and Tytgat 1996; Son and Stephanov 1997, 1998). The superluminal effect arises specifically from the fermion loop contribution, analogous to superluminal photon propagation found in QED with compactified dimensions (Scharnhorst 1990; Barton 1990; Latorre et al. 1995) and in QED with fermions obeying periodic boundary conditions (Fosco et al. 1997). The results enable further investigation using nonperturbative methods such as lattice Monte Carlo or 1/N expansion to determine whether the effect is genuine or an artifact of the one-loop approximation.
