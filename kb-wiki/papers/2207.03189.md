# Trapped-Ion Quantum Simulation of Collective Neutrino Oscillations

> It is well known that the neutrino flavor in extreme astrophysical environments changes under the effect of three contributions: the vacuum oscillation, the interaction with the surrounding matter, and the collective oscillations due to interactions between different neutrinos. The latter adds a non-linear contribution to the equations of motion, making the description of their dynamics complex. In this work we study various strategies to simulate the coherent collective oscillations of a system of N neutrinos in the two-flavor approximation using quantum computation. This was achieved by using a pair-neutrino decomposition designed to account for the fact that the flavor Hamiltonian, in the presence of the neutrino-neutrino term, presents an all-to-all interaction that makes the implementation of the evolution dependent on the qubit topology. We analyze the Trotter error caused by the decomposition demonstrating that the complexity of the implementation of time evolution scales polynomially with the number of neutrinos and that the noisy from near-term quantum device simulation can be reduced by optimizing the quantum circuit decomposition and exploiting a full-qubit connectivity. We find that the gate complexity using second order Trotter-Suzuki formulae scales better with system size than with other decomposition methods such as Quantum Signal Processing. We finally present the application and the results of our algorithm on a real quantum device based on trapped-ions qubits.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a quantum algorithm using a pair-neutrino decomposition with separate treatment of one-body and two-body terms can simulate collective neutrino oscillations on a trapped-ion quantum device, with the Trotter error scaling polynomially (linearly) with the number of neutrinos \(N\), but this favorable scaling depends on the specific choice of qubit connectivity and pair ordering used to minimize the decomposition error.

## Method

The authors encode the two-flavor state of each neutrino into a qubit and decompose the total time evolution operator into separate one-body and two-body propagators, exploiting the fact that these commute. The two-body all-to-all interaction Hamiltonian is implemented via a product of pair propagators \(u_{ij}(t) = e^{-i h_{ij} t}\), where \(h_{ij} = J_{ij} \boldsymbol{\sigma}_i \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma}_j\), and the authors analyze the Trotter error for both first-order and second-order Trotter-Suzuki decompositions. The quantum circuit is optimized for a trapped-ion device with all-to-all qubit connectivity, using a three-layer scheme for \(N=4\) neutrinos that achieves the optimal pair ordering to minimize the decomposition error, and the algorithm is tested on a Quantinuum System Model H1-2 trapped-ion device.

## Key numbers

* For a system of \(N=4\) neutrinos with initial state \(\ket{0011}\), the exact evolution of the flavor inversion probability is computed for time steps \(dt \in [0,40] \, \mu^{-1}\), where \(\mu = \sqrt{2} G_F n_\nu\) is the energy scale.
* The first-order Trotter error bound for a single time step \(dt\) scales as \(\varepsilon_1(dt) \leq 12 \, dt^2 \mu^2 \Theta^2 / N^2 \binom{N}{3} = \mathcal{O}(dt^2 \mu^2 N)\), where \(\Theta = \max_{i,j}[1-\cos(\theta_{ij})]\).
* The number of Trotter steps \(r_1\) needed to keep total error under \(\epsilon\) for evolution time \(T\) scales as \(r_1 = \mathcal{O}(T^2 \mu^2 N / \epsilon)\).
* The second-order Trotter error bound for a single time step scales as \(\varepsilon_2(dt) \leq dt^3 \mu^3 \Theta^3 / N^3 [20 \binom{N}{3} + 56 \binom{N}{4}] = \mathcal{O}(dt^3 \mu^3 N)\).
* The number of Trotter steps \(r_2\) for second-order decomposition scales as \(r_2 = \mathcal{O}(T^{3/2} \mu^{3/2} \sqrt{N} / \sqrt{\epsilon})\).
* The mixing angle is taken as \(\theta_\nu = 0.195\), and the square mass difference \(\delta m^2\) is of order \(10^{-4} \, \text{eV}^2\).

## Context

This work extends prior quantum simulations of collective neutrino oscillations by Hall et al. (2021) and Yeter-Aydeniz et al. (2022), who studied systems with up to four neutrinos on digital quantum computers, and by Illa and Savage (2022) who used quantum annealers. The paper improves upon these approaches by providing a rigorous analysis of Trotter error scaling with system size, demonstrating that the gate complexity using second-order Trotter-Suzuki formulas scales better than Quantum Signal Processing, and by implementing the algorithm on a trapped-ion device with all-to-all qubit connectivity. This enables future simulations of larger neutrino systems that are not accessible by classical mean-field or tensor-network methods, potentially providing crucial information about the conditions required for collective modes to appear in core-collapse supernovae and neutron star mergers.
