# Resonance Production on Nuclei at High Energies: Nuclear-Medium Effects and Space-Time Picture

> The influence of nuclear matter on the properties of coherently produced resonances is discussed. It is shown that, in general, the mass distribution of resonance decay products has a two-component structure corresponding to decay outside and inside the nucleus. The first (narrow) component of the amplitude has a Breit-Wigner form determined by the vacuum values of mass and width of the resonance. The second (broad) component corresponds to interactions of the resonance with the nuclear medium. It can be also described by a Breit-Wigner shape with parameters depending e.g. on the nuclear density and on the cross section of the resonance-nucleon interaction. The resonance production is examined both at intermediate energies, where interactions with the nucleus can be considered as a series of successive local rescatterings, and at high energies, $E>E_{crit}$, where a change of interaction picture occurs. This change of mechanisms of the interactions with the nucleus is typical for the description within the Regge theory approach and is connected with the nonlocal nature of the reggeon interaction.

## Key claim

The paper argues that for coherent resonance production on finite nuclei, the invariant mass distribution of decay products necessarily exhibits a two-component structure (a narrow vacuum Breit-Wigner peak and a broader medium-modified peak), contrary to the common assumption of a single "in-medium" resonance; this structure arises because the resonance can decay either outside or inside the nucleus, and the relative weights of the two components depend on nuclear size, density, and resonance lifetime.

## Method

The authors derive the production amplitude for coherent diffractive resonance production (e.g., photoproduction of vector mesons) on finite nuclei using the eikonal approximation at intermediate energies (below a critical energy E_crit) and Gribov's reggeon exchange formalism at high energies (above E_crit). They construct the dressed Green function for resonance propagation in nuclear matter via multiple scattering from target nucleons, represented by an optical potential, and then Fourier transform to obtain the amplitude as a function of the resonance invariant mass. At high energies, they apply analytic continuation in the complex plane of intermediate invariant masses to show that non-planar reggeon diagrams dominate but yield the same formal structure as the eikonal result when only one resonance channel is relevant.

## Key numbers

- The critical energy separating the two interaction regimes is given by E_crit ~ m_a (μ R_A), where m_a is the projectile mass and μ is a hadronic scale parameter of order a few hundred MeV.
- The free resonance propagator denominator is expressed as 2|p|(q_z - Q_R) with Q_R = q_R - iγ_R/2, where q_R = (M_R² - m_a²)/(2|p|) and γ_R = Γ_R M_R/|p|.
- The medium-modified resonance parameters are M_R*² = M_R² - 2|p| Re t_R(0) ρ_0 and M_R* Γ_R* = M_R Γ_R + 2|p| Im t_R(0) ρ_0, where ρ_0 is the nuclear density and t_R(0) is the forward resonance-nucleon scattering amplitude.
- The nuclear form factor is defined as F_A(q) = ∫ d³r e^{i q·r} ρ_A(r), with typical momentum transfer |q²| ≈ q² ~ R_A^{-2} and q_z ≈ (s̃ - m_a²)/(2|p|).

## Context

This work extends the earlier analysis of Gribov (1970) and Koplik and Mueller (1975) on the nonlocal nature of high-energy hadron-nucleus scattering to the specific case of resonance production, showing that the two-component structure survives even when the space-time picture changes radically above E_crit. The results are relevant to interpreting experiments such as the SPS di-lepton measurements of ρ meson production (SPS1-SPS3, 1995-1996) and nuclear photoabsorption studies at Frascati and Mainz (MAMI, 1990s), where medium-modified resonance parameters are extracted from observed mass distributions. The formalism enables proper separation of vacuum and in-medium contributions in finite nuclei, which is necessary for extracting genuine medium effects from experimental data.
