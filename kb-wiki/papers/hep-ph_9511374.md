# Light-Cone Quantization and QCD Phenomenology

> In principle, quantum chromodynamics provides a fundamental description of hadronic and nuclear structure and dynamics in terms of their elementary quark and gluon degrees of freedom. In practice, the direct application of QCD to reactions involving the structure of hadrons is complex because of the interplay of nonperturbative effects such as color confinement and multi-quark coherence. A crucial tool in analyzing such phenomena is the use of relativistic light-cone quantum mechanics and Fock state methods to provide tractable and consistent treatments of relativistic many-body systems. We begin with a brief introduction to light-cone field theory, stressing how it may allow the derivation of a constituent picture, analogous to the constituent quark model. The Fock state representation includes all quantum fluctuations of the hadron wavefunction, including far off-shell configurations such as intrinsic charm and, in the case of nuclei, hidden color. The Fock state components of the hadron with small transverse size, which dominate hard exclusive reactions, have small color dipole moments. Thus QCD predicts minimal absorptive corrections, i.e., color transparency for quasi-elastic exclusive reactions in nuclear targets at large momentum transfer. In other applications, such as the calculation of the axial, magnetic, and quadrupole moments of light nuclei, the QCD Fock state description provides new insights which go well beyond traditional hadronic and nuclear physics.

## Key claim

Light-cone quantization provides a tractable framework for deriving a constituent quark model picture from QCD, but the approach requires truncations and effective Hamiltonians that must be constructed perturbatively, and the full extension to 3+1 dimensions remains a formidable computational challenge.

## Method

The authors review the formalism of light-cone quantization for QCD, in which hadronic eigenstates are expanded on a Fock basis of free quark and gluon states with boost-invariant momentum fractions and transverse momenta. They discuss how the light-cone Hamiltonian can be diagonalized using discretized light-cone quantization (DLCQ) in 1+1 dimensions, and describe the similarity renormalization group approach for constructing effective Hamiltonians that remove high-energy states while preserving a constituent picture. The formalism is applied to exclusive processes at large momentum transfer via factorization theorems, to inclusive reactions for structure functions, and to calculations of electromagnetic and weak form factors from wavefunction overlaps.

## Key numbers

- The invariant mass of an n-particle Fock state is given by M^2 = sum_i (k_perp_i^2 + m_i^2)/x_i, where x_i are the light-cone momentum fractions satisfying sum_i x_i = 1.
- In QCD_1+1 with fundamental matter, the mass of the lowest meson for N_c=4 agrees with the 't Hooft large-N_c limit and with lattice Hamiltonian results to within numerical accuracy.
- The ratio of baryon to meson mass as a function of N_c in QCD_1+1 agrees with strong-coupling results of Frishman and Sonnenschein (1980s).

## Context

This review extends the program of Dirac (1949) on light-cone quantization and builds on the DLCQ method developed by Pauli and Brodsky (1985) and applied to QCD_1+1 by Burkardt (1989) and Hornbostel et al. (1990). The paper enables future nonperturbative calculations of hadronic wavefunctions and structure functions for experiments at ELFE, HERMES, HERA, SLAC, and CEBAF, and provides a framework for computing color transparency effects and intrinsic heavy flavor contributions that go beyond traditional nuclear physics approaches.
