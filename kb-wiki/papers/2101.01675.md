# Bose-Einstein condensation of pions in proton-proton collisions at the Large Hadron Collider using non-extensive Tsallis statistics

> The possibility of formation of Bose-Einstein Condensation (BEC) is studied in $pp$ collisions at $\sqrt s$ = 7 TeV at the Large Hadron Collider. A thermodynamically consistent non-extensive formulation of the identified hadron transverse momentum distributions is used to estimate the critical temperature required to form BEC of charged pions, which are the most abundant species in a multi-particle production process in hadronic and nuclear collisions. The obtained results have been contrasted with the systems produced in Pb-Pb collisions to have a better understanding. We observe an explicit dependency of BEC critical temperature and number of particles in the pion condensates on the non-extensive parameter $q$, which is a measure of degree of non-equilibrium -- as $q$ decreases, the critical temperature increases and approaches to the critical temperature obtained from Bose-Einstein statistics without non-extensivity. Studies are performed on the final state multiplicity dependence of number of particles in the pion condensates in a wide range of multiplicity covering hadronic and heavy-ion collisions, using the inputs from experimental transverse momentum spectra.

# Bose-Einstein condensation of pions in proton-proton collisions at the Large Hadron Collider using non-extensive Tsallis statistics

## Key claim

Bose-Einstein condensation of charged pions may be possible in proton-proton collisions at the Large Hadron Collider, but only in low-multiplicity events where the system is far from thermal equilibrium (q > 1); in contrast, systems near equilibrium (q close to 1) have higher critical temperatures that may not be reachable at freeze-out, making BEC less likely.

## Method

The authors used a thermodynamically consistent form of Tsallis non-extensive statistics to describe the transverse momentum spectra of identified hadrons in pp collisions at sqrt(s) = 7 TeV. They extracted the temperature T and non-extensive parameter q from experimental spectra fits, then applied these parameters to the standard BEC formalism modified for non-extensivity, calculating the number of pions in the condensate versus excited states as a function of temperature and charged-particle multiplicity. The system volume was estimated from chemical freeze-out radius (1.2 fm) plus hadronic phase lifetime, consistent with HBT radius measurements from ALICE.

## Key numbers

* For q = 1 (equilibrium Bose-Einstein statistics), the critical temperature for pion BEC is approximately 105 MeV.
* For q = 1.13 (far from equilibrium), the critical temperature decreases to approximately 75 MeV.
* At the highest charged-particle multiplicity in pp collisions at sqrt(s) = 7 TeV, about 95% of pions are in excited states and about 5% occupy the ground state.
* A threshold charged-particle multiplicity of approximately ⟨dNch/dη⟩ ≈ 6 is identified, corresponding to a freeze-out temperature of about 78 MeV, below which the number of particles in the condensate exceeds those in excited states.
* The system volume is taken with a radius of 1.2 fm, consistent with the ALICE HBT radius range of 0.8–1.6 fm for pp collisions.

## Context

This work extends the study of pion BEC from heavy-ion collisions (Begun 2015) to small systems formed in pp collisions at LHC energies, motivated by observations of QGP-like features in high-multiplicity pp events (Nature 2010, Khachatryan 2010). The use of Tsallis non-extensive statistics follows Cleymans (2011) and addresses the known deviation of pp transverse momentum spectra from Boltzmann-Gibbs distributions observed at RHIC (Abelev 2006, Adare 2011) and LHC (Aamodt 2011, Abelev 2012). The results are contrasted with Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV using parameters from Azmi (2019), showing a smooth transition between collision systems as a function of final-state multiplicity. The work enables future experimental searches for pion BEC at low transverse momentum in upgraded LHC detectors.
