# Non-Gaussianity from Schwinger-Keldysh Effective Field Theory

> We present a systematic treatment of non-Gaussianity in stochastic systems using the Schwinger-Keldysh effective field theory framework, in which the non-Gaussianity is realized as nonlinear terms in the fluctuation field. We establish two stochastic formulations of the Schwinger-Keldysh effective field theory, with those nonlinear terms manifested as multiple non-Gaussian noises in the Langevin equation and as higher order diffusive terms in the Fokker-Planck equation. The equivalence of the stochastic formulations with the original Schwinger-Keldysh effective field theory is demonstrated with non-trivial examples for arbitrary non-Gaussian parameters. The stochastic formulations will be more flexible and effective in studying non-equilibrium dynamics. We also reveal an ambiguity when coarse-graining time scale and non-Gaussian parameters vanish simultaneously, which may be responsible for the unphysical divergence found in perturbative analysis.

## Key claim

The paper establishes that non-Gaussianity in stochastic systems, arising from nonlinear terms in the fluctuation field of the Schwinger-Keldysh effective field theory, can be exactly reformulated as multiple non-Gaussian noises in a Langevin equation and as higher-order diffusive terms in a Fokker-Planck equation, without assuming small non-Gaussian parameters; the authors also reveal an ambiguity when the coarse-graining time scale and non-Gaussian parameters vanish simultaneously, which may explain unphysical divergences found in perturbative analyses.

## Method

The authors start from a Schwinger-Keldysh effective field theory Lagrangian for a Brownian particle, expanded to quartic order in the fields and leading order in temporal gradient, containing Gaussian terms (quadratic in the fluctuation field) and non-Gaussian terms (cubic and quartic in the fluctuation field). They convert the non-Gaussian terms into multiple non-Gaussian noises via inverse Fourier transforms, yielding a Langevin equation with distinct noise scalings, and derive the corresponding Fokker-Planck equation with higher-order derivative terms. The equivalence among the three formulations (Schwinger-Keldysh effective field theory, Langevin equation, and Fokker-Planck equation) is demonstrated by calculating equal-time correlation functions \(\langle \Delta^2(t) \rangle\) and \(\langle \Delta^4(t) \rangle_c\) for arbitrary non-Gaussian parameters, with stability conditions imposed.

## Key numbers

- The two-point correlation function in the steady state is \(\langle \Delta_r(t) \Delta_r(t) \rangle = T/m\), where \(T\) is the temperature and \(m\) is the damping coefficient.
- The connected four-point correlation function in the steady state, for vanishing \(\epsilon_3\) and \(\epsilon\), is \(\langle \Delta_r(t)^4 \rangle_c = -6\epsilon_1/m + 6T\epsilon_2/m^2\), where \(\epsilon_1\) and \(\epsilon_2\) are non-Gaussian parameters.
- The non-Gaussian noises scale with the timestep \(\delta_t\) as \(\eta \sim \delta_t^{-3/4}\) and \(\chi \sim \delta_t^{-2/3}\), distinct from the Gaussian noise scaling \(\xi \sim \delta_t^{-1/2}\).
- The fourth moment of the \(\eta\) noise is \(\langle \eta_i^4 \rangle = -24\epsilon_1/\delta_t^3\), and the third moment of the \(\chi\) noise is \(\langle \chi_i^3 \rangle = 6\epsilon_2 \Delta_i / \delta_t^2\).

## Context

This work extends the Schwinger-Keldysh effective field theory framework for stochastic systems, which was developed by Kamenev (2011), Crossley et al. (2015), and Glorioso and Liu (2017), by systematically incorporating non-Gaussianity beyond the Gaussian noise approximation. It contrasts with earlier perturbative studies by Chakrabarty et al. (2019) that established equivalence only at lowest order in non-Gaussian parameters, and it addresses unphysical divergences found in those perturbative analyses. The stochastic formulations enable more flexible and efficient study of non-equilibrium dynamics and can be generalized to more complex systems such as hydrodynamics, building on recent work by Chen-Lin et al. (2018), Jain (2020), and others on nonlinear effects in hydrodynamics.
