# Monte Carlo approach to the excluded-volume hadron resonance gas in grand canonical and canonical ensembles

> The Monte Carlo (MC) procedure for sampling the hadron yields within hadron resonance gas (HRG) model is presented. The effects of excluded-volume due to the finite hadron eigenvolumes and of exact charge conservation within the canonical ensemble (CE) formulation are simultaneously taken into account with the help of the importance sampling technique combined with the rejection sampling. The MC procedure allows one to calculate arbitrary moments of the event-by-event hadron yields. Note that the CE formulation for the excluded-volume HRG has not been considered before. The MC simulations coincide with the known analytic results in the thermodynamic limit for the excluded-volume HRG in the grand canonical ensemble and for the CE of non-interacting particles. The MC procedure is applied to study the simultaneous excluded-volume and CE effects. These effects are considered within the full HRG to calculate the particle number fluctuations and to estimate the finite size effects.

## Key claim

The paper presents a Monte Carlo procedure that simultaneously accounts for excluded-volume effects due to finite hadron eigenvolumes and exact charge conservation within the canonical ensemble, a combination that had not been previously formulated for the hadron resonance gas model.

## Method

The authors develop a Monte Carlo sampling procedure using importance sampling combined with rejection sampling, where an auxiliary multi-Poisson distribution is used as a proposal distribution and events are accepted or rejected based on a weight function and the excluded-volume constraint. For the canonical ensemble, an additional rejection step enforces exact conservation of charges (baryon number, electric charge, strangeness) by first generating total baryon and antibaryon numbers from Poisson distributions, then sampling individual hadron yields from multinomial distributions, and rejecting configurations that violate charge conservation at each step. The procedure is applied to four different excluded-volume models: the van der Waals excluded-volume model, the Carnahan-Starling model, the diagonal eigenvolume model, and the non-diagonal eigenvolume model.

## Key numbers

- For a single-component gas with particle mass 1 GeV, degeneracy factor g=150, temperature T=150 MeV, and vanishing chemical potential, the particle number density approaches the thermodynamic limit with increasing system radius R; for hard-core radius r=1 fm, the minimum system radius below which particle density is zero is R_min ≈ 1.59 fm in the van der Waals model
- In the Carnahan-Starling model with r=1 fm, the minimum system radius is R_min = 1 fm, with strong suppression of particle number density for volumes slightly above this threshold
- For a two-component system of particles and antiparticles (mass 1 GeV, degeneracy g±=75, T=150 MeV, zero net charge), the scaled variance ω[N±] approaches 1/2 in the canonical ensemble thermodynamic limit for r=0, while it approaches 1 in the grand canonical ensemble
- At T=160 MeV in the full hadron resonance gas with zero baryon number, electric charge, and strangeness, the scaled variance ω[N±] and ω[Np] (for protons) show suppression from both excluded-volume and canonical ensemble effects as a function of system radius R

## Context

This work extends the hadron resonance gas model literature by providing the first formulation of the canonical ensemble for excluded-volume HRG, which had previously only been treated in the grand canonical ensemble (Rischke 1991, Yen 1997) or for non-interacting particles (Begun 2004). The Monte Carlo procedure enables calculation of arbitrary moments of event-by-event hadron yields, which is relevant for comparing to experimental data from small collision systems such as proton-proton and e+e- collisions, where exact charge conservation becomes important (Becattini 1995, 1997, 2000, 2010). The approach can be extended to include van der Waals interactions with attractive mean-field terms (Vovchenko 2016, 2017).
