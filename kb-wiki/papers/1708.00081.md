# Relating centrality to impact parameter in nucleus-nucleus collisions

> In ultrarelativistic heavy-ion experiments, one estimates the centrality of a collision by using a single observable, say $n$, typically given by the transverse energy or the number of tracks observed in a dedicated detector. The correlation between $n$ and the impact parameter, $b$, of the collision is then inferred by fitting a specific model of the collision dynamics, such as the Glauber model, to experimental data. The goal of this paper is to assess precisely which information about $b$ can be extracted from data without any specific model of the collision. Under the sole assumption that the probability distribution of $n$ for a fixed $b$ is Gaussian, we show that the probability distribution of the impact parameter in a narrow centrality bin can be accurately reconstructed up to $5\%$ centrality. We apply our methodology to data from the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider and the Large Hadron Collider. We propose a simple measure of the precision of the centrality determination, which can be used to compare different experiments.

# Relating centrality to impact parameter in nucleus-nucleus collisions

## Key claim

Under the sole assumption that the probability distribution of the experimental centrality observable (multiplicity or transverse energy) for a fixed impact parameter is Gaussian, the probability distribution of impact parameter in a narrow centrality bin can be accurately reconstructed up to 5% centrality without invoking any specific microscopic model of the collision dynamics such as the Glauber model or HIJING.

## Method

The authors assume the distribution of the experimental observable n (multiplicity or transverse energy) at fixed impact parameter b is Gaussian with mean n̄(c_b) and width σ(c_b), where c_b is the b-centrality (cumulative probability of impact parameter). They fit the measured probability distribution P(n) using a five-parameter function for n̄(c_b) (an exponential of a cubic polynomial in c_b) and a constant σ(0), testing two scenarios for the centrality dependence of σ: (A) σ proportional to √n̄, and (B) σ constant. The fit parameters are then used with Bayes' theorem to reconstruct P(c_b|c), the distribution of b-centrality at a given experimental centrality c.

## Key numbers

- The knee of the distribution (mean n at zero impact parameter) is reconstructed with 0.3% error in the Trento model validation, while the width σ(0) is reconstructed with about 3% error.
- The fraction of events above the knee (c_knee) is 0.349% ± 0.023% for ALICE Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV, 0.313% ± 0.011% for ATLAS, 0.314% ± 0.040% for CMS, and 0.81% ± 0.10% for STAR Au+Au at 130 GeV.
- The mean n̄(c_b) is reconstructed accurately up to 70% b-centrality in the Trento model validation.
- The reconstructed P(c_b|c) agrees with direct calculation up to 4% centrality for both scenarios (A) and (B); discrepancies appear around c = 8%.

## Context

This work extends the standard approach of using Glauber model fits (Miller 2007) or HIJING (Wang 1991) to relate centrality to impact parameter, which the authors note is disfavored by analyses of U+U collisions (Adamczyk 2015, Moreland 2014). The method provides a model-independent way to extract impact parameter information from experimental data, enabling direct comparison of the mean n at fixed b between different experiments and models without relying on the concept of participant nucleons. The authors note that the Gaussian assumption fails for proton-nucleus collisions (Adam 2014), where the tail of P(n) is exponential rather than Gaussian, and suggest this as an open question for future work.
