# Using the Th III Ion for a Nuclear Clock and Searches for New Physics

> The 229Th nucleus possesses a unique low-frequency transition at 8.4 eV, which is being considered for the development of an extremely accurate nuclear clock. We investigate an electronic bridge process in the Th III ion, where nuclear excitation occurs via electronic transitions, and demonstrate that a proper choice of laser frequencies can lead to 10,000 enhancement of this effect. Electrons also reduce 1.7 times the lifetime of the nuclear excited state. Additionally, the electronic structure of the Th III ion exhibits features that make it particularly useful for probing new physics. Notably, it contains a metastable state connected to the ground state via a weak M2 transition, which can be utilized for quantum information processing, as well as searches for oscillating axion field, violation of local Lorentz invariance, test of the Einstein's equivalence principle, and measurement of nuclear weak quadrupole moment. The electronic states of the ion present a unique case of level crossing involving the 5f, 6d, and 7s single-electron states. This crossing renders the transition frequencies highly sensitive to potential time-variation of the fine-structure constant.

# Using the Th III Ion for a Nuclear Clock and Searches for New Physics

## Key claim

The electronic bridge process in Th III can enhance nuclear excitation probability by up to 10,000 times, but this enhancement is only realized when the final electronic state is not the ground state but a low-lying excited state, and the enhancement factor does not translate linearly into excitation rate when the radiative width dominates the resonance cross section.

## Method

The authors calculated electronic bridge (EB) rates for the 8.4 eV nuclear transition in 229Th III using a two-step laser excitation scheme, where the ion is first excited from the ground state to an intermediate state t, then further excited to satisfy energy conservation for simultaneous nuclear excitation and excitation of a final electronic state s. They performed summation over intermediate electronic states n, finding that one or two terms dominate due to small energy denominators and large matrix elements, and expressed results as dimensionless enhancement factors β relative to the bare nuclear transition rate.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear transition energy: ω_N = 8.355733(2)_stat(10)_sys eV (67393 cm⁻¹) in Th atoms inside solids
- Maximum EB enhancement factor: β̃ = 10,014 for the T2 → N4 → S3 pathway (ω₁ = 38580 cm⁻¹, ω₂ = 44266 cm⁻¹, Δ_n = -19 cm⁻¹)
- Second largest enhancement: β̃ = 859 for the T3 → N4 → S3 pathway
- EB enhancement for nuclear decay: β̃ ≈ 0.7, giving predicted half-life in Th III of T_{1/2} = 820^{+350}_{-180} s (compared to 1400^{+600}_{-300} s in Th IV)
- Metastable state energy: 63 cm⁻¹ above ground state, connected via M2 transition with lifetime ∼10¹⁰ years
- Relativistic factor for EEP violation: R ≈ -1700 for the M2 transition
- Enhancement factor for fine-structure constant variation: K = -1060 for the M2 transition (W1 state at 63 cm⁻¹)
- Combined sensitivity for nuclear vs. M2 frequency comparison: δω_N/ω_N - δω_{M2}/ω_{M2} ≈ 7000 δα/α
- Bare nuclear decay width: Γ_N ≈ 5 × 10⁻⁴ Hz
- Final state S1 lifetime: 332 μs (width 3.0 kHz); S4 lifetime: 57 ms (width 17 Hz)

## Context

This work extends previous electronic bridge calculations for Th II (Dzuba and Flambaum, 2020) and Th IV to the Th III ion, which offers a richer electronic spectrum than Th IV (where no EB enhancement occurs) while being simpler to calculate reliably than Th II (where the dense spectrum prevents reliable matching of calculated and experimental levels). The Th III ion uniquely contains both a nuclear clock transition at 8.4 eV and an electronic clock transition (the M2 transition at 63 cm⁻¹) within the same system, enabling differential measurements for searches of new physics including oscillating axion fields, local Lorentz invariance violation, Einstein equivalence principle tests, and time variation of the fine-structure constant. The exceptionally large relativistic factor R ≈ -1700 for EEP violation surpasses previously studied systems where R ∼ 1 (e.g., Yb⁺), and the enhancement factor K ∼ 10³ for α-variation is several orders of magnitude larger than in other atomic clock systems, comparable only to the Dy atom transition (K ∼ 10⁶-10⁸) which is not a clock transition.
