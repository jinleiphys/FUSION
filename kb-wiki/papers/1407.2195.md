# Nuclear spin-orbit interaction and T-odd angular correlations in ternary fission

## Key claim
T-odd angular correlations observed in ternary fission of 233U and 235U by slow polarized neutrons are not evidence of time reversal invariance violation, but arise from an effective spin-orbit interaction in the final state, specifically from Coulomb repulsion between the alpha particle and the fission fragments.

## Method
The author develops a quantum model of ternary fission using hyperspherical harmonics in a three-body approach (two fragments plus alpha particle) to derive the differential cross section. The model incorporates an effective spin-orbit interaction proportional to the product of the alpha particle's orbital momentum and the total spin of the two-fragment system. The angular distribution of alpha particles is expanded in Legendre polynomials, separating contributions from odd and even multipoles (W1 and W2 terms) that correspond to two distinct T-odd asymmetries.

## Key numbers
- The maximum of the alpha-particle angular distribution is shifted to an angle of approximately 83 degrees due to Coulomb repulsion from the heavy fragment.
- The T-effect (transverse asymmetry) corresponds to dominance of the W1 term (odd Q = 1, 3, 5, ...) in the angular distribution, giving D13 ≈ D24 for 233U targets.
- The R-effect corresponds to dominance of the W2 term (even Q = 2, 4, 6, ...), giving D13 ≈ -D24 for 235U targets.
- The hypermoment N is given by N = 2n + L + l, where n = 0, 1, 2, ... is the radial quantum number, L is the relative orbital momentum of the two fragments, and l is the orbital momentum of the alpha particle.

## Context
This work extends the author's earlier model presented at a 2001 conference in Dubna (Barabanov, 2001) and addresses criticisms from Bunakov and Kadmensky (2007) who proposed a similar "Coriolis interaction" model. The paper reconciles experimental observations from measurements on 233U (Goennenwein et al., 1999; Gagarski et al., 2000) and 235U (Gagarski et al., 2000; Guseva et al., 2000) that showed opposite sign patterns for the asymmetry. The formalism predicts both T-effect and R-effect within a single framework, enabling future calculations of asymmetry coefficients for different target nuclei.
