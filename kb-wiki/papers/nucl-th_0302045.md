# The Importance of Parity-Dependence of the Nuclear Level Density in the Prediction of Astrophysical Reaction Rates

> A simple description for obtaining the parity distribution of nuclear levels in the pf + g9/2 shell as a function of excitation energy was recently derived. We implement this in a global nuclear level density model. In the framework of the statistical model, cross sections and astrophysical reaction rates are calculated in the Fe region and compared to rates obtained with the common assumption of an equal distribution of parities. We find considerable differences, especially for reactions involving particles in the exit channel.

## Key claim

Implementing a parity-dependent nuclear level density in global Hauser-Feshbach calculations can change astrophysical reaction rates by up to 20% or more for nuclei far from stability, but the effect is negligible for nuclei where experimental excited states are known.

## Method

The authors combined a Poisson-based formula for the energy-dependent parity distribution (derived from Monte Carlo shell model calculations by Alhassid et al. (2000)) with the back-shifted Fermi-gas level density model of Rauscher et al. (1997). They then used the parity-projected level densities in the global Hauser-Feshbach code NON-SMOKER to compute astrophysical reaction rates for nuclei in the iron region, comparing results to the standard assumption of equal parity distribution.

## Key numbers

- For reactions involving $^{64}$Fe, the $(n,\gamma)$ rate shows 20% difference compared to the standard equal-parity assumption.
- Up to 20 experimental states are included in the standard calculation for Ni and Zn nuclei before the theoretical level density takes over.
- The parity distribution formula was tested against Monte Carlo shell model calculations for $^{56}$Fe, $^{60}$Ni, and $^{68}$Zn.

## Context

This work extends the parity-distribution formula derived by Alhassid et al. (2000) and Nakada and Alhassid (1998) from Monte Carlo shell model calculations, implementing it into the global statistical model code NON-SMOKER developed by Rauscher and Thielemann (2000, 2001). The results show that parity dependence matters most for nuclei far from stability where no experimental level information exists, motivating extension of the approach to a wider range of nuclei on both the proton-rich and neutron-rich sides.
