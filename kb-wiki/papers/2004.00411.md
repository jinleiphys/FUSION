# A Consistency Test of EFT Power Countings from Residual Cutoff Dependence

> A method to quantitatively assess the consistency of power-counting proposals in Effective Field Theories (EFT) which are non-perturbative at leading order is presented. The Renormalisation Group evolution of an observable predicts the functional form of its residual cutoff dependence on the breakdown scale of an EFT, on the low-momentum scales, and on the order of the calculation. Passing this test is a necessary but not sufficient consistency criterion for a suggested power counting whose exact nature is disputed. In Chiral Effective Field Theory (ChiEFT) with more than one nucleon, a lack of universally accepted analytic solutions obfuscates the convergence pattern in results. This led to proposals which predict different sets of Low Energy Coefficients (LECs) at the same chiral order, and at times even predict a different ordering long-range contributions. The method may independently check whether an observable is renormalised at a given order, and proves estimates of both the breakdown scale and the momentum-dependent order-by-order convergence pattern. Conversely, it helps identify those LECs (and long-range pieces) which ensure renormalised observables at a given order. I also discuss assumptions and the relation to Wilson's Renormalisation Group; useful observable and cutoff choices; the momentum window with likely best signals; its dependence on the values and forms of cutoffs as well as on the EFT parameters; the impact of fitting LECs to data; and caveats as well as limitations. Since the test is designed to minimise the use of data, it quantitatively falsifies if the EFT has been renormalised consistently. This complements other tests which quantify how an EFT compares to experiment. Its application in particular to the 3P0 and P2-3F2 partial waves of NN scattering in ChiEFT may elucidate persistent power-counting issues.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method to quantitatively test whether a proposed power counting in an Effective Field Theory (EFT) is internally consistent by examining the residual cutoff dependence of observables, noting that passing this test is a necessary but not sufficient condition for consistency. The method is designed to minimise the use of experimental data, allowing one to quantitatively falsify whether the EFT has been renormalised consistently, rather than to compare with experiment.

## Method

The method uses the Renormalisation Group evolution of an observable to predict the functional form of its residual cutoff dependence on the breakdown scale of the EFT, on the low-momentum scales, and on the order of the calculation. By varying the cutoff and examining the relative difference of an observable at two different cutoffs, one can extract both the order to which the calculation is complete and the breakdown scale, provided the cutoff behaviour cannot be eliminated entirely and the residuals vary more slowly with momentum than with cutoff.

## Key numbers

- The breakdown scale of Chiral Effective Field Theory (ChiEFT) is estimated as Lambda_Chi approximately 700 to 1000 MeV, consistent with the masses of the omega and rho mesons.
- In a theory without the Delta(1232) resonance, the breakdown scale shrinks to approximately 300 MeV.
- The inverse scattering lengths of the NN system are gamma_t approximately 45 MeV in the 3S1 channel and gamma_s approximately -8 MeV in the 1S0 channel.
- An intrinsic scale Lambda_NN = 16 pi f_pi^2 / (g_A^2 M) approximately 300 MeV sets the strength of the one-pion exchange potential, where f_pi approximately 92 MeV and g_A approximately 1.27.
- The KSW version of ChiEFT has a radius of convergence Lambda_KSW less than or approximately 200 MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier discussions from 2003 and 2005 by Bedaque and Griesshammer, and from lectures at the 2008 US National Nuclear Physics Summer School. It addresses the long-standing dispute between power-counting proposals in NN ChiEFT, including the Weinberg pragmatic proposal, the Long/Yang 2012 proposal, the Pavon Valderrama et al. 2006 proposal, and the Birse 2005 proposal, which disagree on the number of Low Energy Coefficients at the same chiral order and on the ordering of long-range contributions. The test is designed to complement other methods that quantify how an EFT compares to experiment, and its application to the 3P0 and 3P2-3F2 partial waves of NN scattering may help resolve persistent power-counting issues.
