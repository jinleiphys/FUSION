# Nuclear deformation and neutron excess as competing effects for pygmy dipole strength

> The electromagnetic dipole strength below the neutron-separation energy has been studied for the xenon isotopes with mass numbers A = 124, 128, 132, and 134 in nuclear resonance fluorescence experiments using the ELBE bremsstrahlung facility at Helmholtz-Zentrum Dresden-Rossendorf and the HIgS facility at Triangle Universities Nuclear Laboratory Durham. The systematic study gained new information about the influence of the neutron excess as well as of nuclear deformation on the strength in the region of the pygmy dipole resonance. The results are compared with those obtained for the chain of molybdenum isotopes and with predictions of a random-phase approximation in a deformed basis. It turned out that the effect of nuclear deformation plays a minor role compared with the one caused by neutron excess. A global parametrization of the strength in terms of neutron and proton numbers allowed us to derive a formula capable of predicting the summed E1 strengths in the pygmy region for a wide mass range of nuclides.

## Key claim

The effect of nuclear deformation on the summed E1 strength in the pygmy dipole resonance region is minor compared to the effect of neutron excess, based on a study of xenon isotopes where deformation decreases with increasing neutron number, allowing the two effects to be disentangled for the first time.

## Method

Nuclear resonance fluorescence experiments were performed at the gammaELBE bremsstrahlung facility (Helmholtz-Zentrum Dresden-Rossendorf) using 12 MeV electrons on a niobium radiator, and at the HIgS facility (Triangle Universities Nuclear Laboratory) using polarized quasi-monoenergetic gamma beams. Targets were highly-enriched xenon gas (99.9%) at about 80 bar in steel containers, with boron-11 disks for flux calibration. The analysis included unfolding of detector response with GEANT4, simulation of non-nuclear background, and correction for inelastic scattering using the code gammaDEX for statistical gamma-ray cascades. Results were compared with quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA) calculations in a deformed Wood-Saxon basis and with the triple Lorentzian model (TLO).

## Key numbers

- Summed B(E1) strength in the 6-8 MeV region for xenon isotopes: values decrease from heavier isotopes toward the lightest isotope (124Xe), with the most deformed isotope showing the smallest strength.
- Summed magnetic dipole strength between 6 and 8 MeV: B(M1) = 0.14(3) e^2 fm^2 for 128Xe and B(M1) = 0.07(2) e^2 fm^2 for 134Xe, less than 10% of the E1 strength in the same region.
- The ratio r_E1 = sum B(E1) / [(N/Z - 1) * (N Z / A)] is approximately constant at 0.080(5) for the Mo, Xe, and Ba isotopes studied.
- Deformation parameters beta_2 range from about 0.21 for 124Xe to about 0.1 for the heavier xenon isotopes.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of pygmy dipole strength in molybdenum isotopes (Rusev et al., 2009) and in N=50 and N=82 isotones (Schwengner et al., 2013; Savran et al., 2008), where the effects of neutron excess and deformation could not be separated because both increased together. By studying xenon isotopes where deformation decreases with increasing neutron number, the authors demonstrate that neutron excess dominates over deformation. The resulting global parametrization in terms of N and Z can predict summed E1 strengths in the pygmy region for a wide mass range of nuclides, with applications in nuclear astrophysics and nuclear technology, though it fails for double magic nuclei like 208Pb and for N=Z nuclides.
