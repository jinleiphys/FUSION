# Application of relativistic mean field and effective field theory density to calculate scattering observables for Ca isotopes

> In the frame work of relativistic mean field (RMF) theory, we calculate the density distribution of protons and neutrons for $^{40,42, 44,48}Ca$ with NL3 and G2 parameter sets. The microscopic proton-nucleus optical potential for $p+^{40,42,44,48}Ca$ system is evaluated from Dirac NN-scattering amplitude and the density of the target nucleus using Relativistic-Love-Franey and McNeil-Ray-Wallace parametrizations. Then we estimate the scattering observables, such as elastic differential scattering cross-section, analyzing power and the spin observables with relativistic impulse approximation. We compare the results with the experimental data for some selective cases and found that the use of density as well as the scattering matrix parametrization is crucial for the theoretical prediction.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the choice of both the target nuclear density (from RMF with NL3 or E-RMF with G2 parameter sets) and the NN-scattering amplitude parametrization (Relativistic-Love-Franey or McNeil-Ray-Wallace) is crucial for accurately predicting elastic scattering observables for proton scattering on Ca isotopes, but the agreement with experimental data depends on the specific combination, with no single combination universally best across all observables and energies.

## Method

The authors first calculated proton and neutron density distributions for 40,42,44,48Ca using the relativistic mean field (RMF) formalism with the NL3 parameter set and the effective field theory motivated relativistic mean field (E-RMF) formalism with the G2 parameter set. They then constructed microscopic proton-nucleus optical potentials by folding these densities with Dirac NN-scattering amplitudes using either the Relativistic-Love-Franey (RLF) or McNeil-Ray-Wallace (MRW) parametrizations. Finally, they solved the Dirac equation for the scattering state using the Numerov algorithm and computed the elastic differential cross-section, analyzing power, and spin rotation parameter within the relativistic impulse approximation (RIA).

## Key numbers

- The extreme values of the real and imaginary parts of the scalar optical potential for p+40Ca at Elab=1000 MeV are -382.9 MeV and 110.6 MeV for RLF with NL3, and -372.4 MeV and 177.8 MeV for RLF with G2.
- For the MRW parametrization at Elab=1000 MeV, the extreme scalar potential values are -217.7 MeV (real) and 40.2 MeV (imaginary) with NL3, and -333.8 MeV (real) and 61.7 MeV (imaginary) with G2.
- The extreme vector potential values at Elab=1000 MeV are 293.0 MeV (real) and -136.0 MeV (imaginary) for RLF with NL3, and 319.7 MeV (real) and -157.5 MeV (imaginary) for RLF with G2.
- For MRW at Elab=1000 MeV, the extreme vector potential values are 124.1 MeV (real) and -82.3 MeV (imaginary) with NL3, and 115.5 MeV (real) and -77.1 MeV (imaginary) with G2.
- The RLF optical potential extends to r ~ 5 fm, while the MRW potential persists until r ~ 6 fm.
- The RLF parameters are computed for energies up to 400 MeV, making them suitable for lower Elab, while MRW is better for higher energies.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic impulse approximation (RIA) framework, previously applied to systems such as p+12C, p+9B, and p+16O at 200 MeV by Meyer (1981) and Bacher (1980), to Ca isotopes using modern RMF and E-RMF densities. It builds on the global Dirac optical potential approach of Hama (1990) and the modified Glauber model studies of Khan (2007, 2009). The results enable future applications of RIA to unstable, neutron-rich nuclei using microscopic densities from relativistic mean field models, and highlight the need for improved NN-amplitude parametrizations at intermediate energies.
