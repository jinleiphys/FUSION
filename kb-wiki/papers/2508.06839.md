# Concentrated valence nucleons transfer in heavy-ion collisions: implications for questing the stable superheavy elements

> The multinucleon transfer process is regarded as a promising pathway for producing the stable superheavy elements. However, the underlying mechanism, especially the possible transfer channels for sailing to the ``island of stability'' are poorly known. In this work, the time-dependent Hartree-Fock theory is used to investigate the collision dynamics of $^{136}$Xe, $^{198}$Pt, $^{238}$U + $^{238}$U reactions. A novel reaction channel of the concentrated valence nucleons (CVN) transferring is found in the collisions heading on the tips of $^{238}$U. These nucleons are transferred with relatively short relaxation time and break the symmetry of nucleon exchange in the early reaction stage. In consequence, the mass equilibrium with relaxation time is deviated from the systematic behavior based on the macroscopic-microscopic potential energy surface. The CVN transfer channel shows promising prospect for producing neutron-rich superheavy nuclei. In this case, we also investigated the angular distributions of products from the CVN transfer channel in the reaction $^{238}$U + $^{238}$U with Tip-Side configuration, and the optimal detection angles are predicted.

## Key claim

The authors claim that a novel "concentrated valence nucleons (CVN) transfer" channel exists in tip-on collisions of deformed \(^{238}\)U nuclei, where approximately 10 valence nucleons from one tip are transferred as a group in the early reaction stage, breaking the symmetry of nucleon exchange and deviating from the mass equilibration expected from macroscopic-microscopic potential energy surfaces. This claim is conditional on the specific orientation of the deformed nuclei and is observed only in collisions along the long axis of \(^{238}\)U.

## Method

The authors used the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory with the Skyrme SLy5 density functional, implemented in the SKY3D code, to simulate collisions of \(^{136}\)Xe, \(^{198}\)Pt, and \(^{238}\)U projectiles with a \(^{238}\)U target at incident energies from the Coulomb barrier to 1.5 times the barrier. Static Hartree-Fock calculations were first performed to obtain the ground-state density distributions of \(^{238}\)U, identifying 10 neutrons and 10 protons closest to the Fermi surface as valence nucleons. Dynamical TDHF simulations were then run for central and peripheral collisions at various orientations of the deformed \(^{238}\)U target, tracking the time evolution of nucleon densities, net transfer numbers, and angular distributions of fragments.

## Key numbers

- The deformation parameter of \(^{238}\)U from static Hartree-Fock is \(\beta_2 \approx 0.29\).
- In central collisions of \(^{136}\)Xe + \(^{238}\)U at \(E_{\text{c.m.}} = 636\) MeV along the long axis (\(x\)-direction), a net transfer of 16 nucleons from \(^{238}\)U to \(^{136}\)Xe is observed, while collisions along the short axis (\(y\)-direction) show almost no mass drift despite having about twice the contact time.
- In the early stage of the \(x\)-direction collision, the valence nucleon transfer from \(^{238}\)U to \(^{136}\)Xe reaches a peak value of 10 nucleons within approximately 200 fm/\(c\).
- For the \(^{238}\)U + \(^{238}\)U reaction, the angular distributions of primary fragments were calculated at \(E_{\text{c.m.}} = 800\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends microscopic TDHF studies of multinucleon transfer reactions, such as those by Sekizawa (2017) and Wakhle (2014), which examined orientation effects in deformed nuclei but did not identify the concentrated valence nucleon transfer mechanism. The finding of CVN transfer challenges the assumption of sequential nucleon transfer that underlies macroscopic models like those of Zagrebaev (2013) and the macroscopic-microscopic potential energy surface approach. The results suggest a new pathway for producing neutron-rich superheavy nuclei near the "island of stability" by exploiting tip-on collisions of actinide nuclei, and the predicted optimal detection angles for the \(^{238}\)U + \(^{238}\)U reaction provide guidance for future experimental searches.
