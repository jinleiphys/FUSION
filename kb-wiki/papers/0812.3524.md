# Nuclear multifragmentation and phase transition for hot nuclei

> This review article is focused on the tremendous progress realized during the last fifteen years in the understanding of multifragmentation and its relationship to the liquid-gas phase diagram of nuclei and nuclear matter. The explosion of the whole nucleus, early predicted by Bohr [N. Bohr, Nature 137 (1936) 351], is a very complex and rich subject which continues to fascinate nuclear physicists as well as theoreticians who extend the thermodynamics of phase transitions to finite systems.

# Nuclear multifragmentation and phase transition for hot nuclei

## Key claim
Multifragmentation of hot nuclei is associated with the liquid-gas phase transition predicted for infinite nuclear matter, but finite-size effects and Coulomb forces reduce the critical temperature by about 7 MeV for nuclei in the mass range A=50-400, leading to a "critical" temperature of about 10 MeV for hot pieces of nuclear matter produced in collisions between very heavy nuclei. The order and nature of the transition remain subject to debate, largely due to finite-size effects.

## Method
The authors review experimental data from heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies and hadron-nucleus collisions, comparing fragmentation properties of quasi-projectiles formed in semi-peripheral collisions with "fused" systems from more violent collisions. They examine event sorting methods (impact parameter selectors, topology selectors, multivariate analysis), fragment properties (multiplicities, charge distributions, emission times), calorimetric and thermometric measurements, and comparisons with both dynamical transport codes and statistical models based on phase space occupation.

## Key numbers
- Critical point of nuclear matter equation of state: ρ_c ≈ 0.3-0.4 ρ_0 and T_c ≈ 16-18 MeV, depending on the effective interaction and model
- Total reduction of critical temperature for finite nuclei (A=50-400): about 7 MeV, composed of 2-6 MeV from finite-size effects and 1-3 MeV from Coulomb force, leading to a "critical" temperature of about 10 MeV for very heavy nuclei
- Onset of multifragmentation: excitation energies around 3 AMeV
- Maximum fragment production: excitation energies around 9 AMeV, close to the binding energy of nuclei
- Saturation of average excitation energy of primary fragments: around 3 AMeV
- Average fragment emission times: decrease with increasing excitation energy up to about 5 AMeV, then saturate around 50-100 fm/c
- Radial expansion energies deduced from experiments: around 4-5 AMeV at the maximum of fragment production
- Vaporized quasi-projectiles studied in 95 AMeV 36Ar+58Ni collisions: excitation energy range 9.5 to 27.5 MeV, covered by varying temperature from 10 to 25 MeV in the model, with free volume fixed at 3V_0
- Largest fragment charge distribution becomes independent of total system mass when the latter exceeds A ≈ 180-190; this behavior is also observed in statistical models down to A ≈ 150

## Context
This review extends the long-standing connection between nuclear multifragmentation and the liquid-gas phase transition predicted for nuclear matter, first proposed in the early 1980s (Siemens 1983). It builds on experimental advances from 4π detectors (Souliotis 2006) and transport codes developed over two decades (Fuchs 2006, Ono 2006). The work enables further investigation of phase transition signatures in finite systems, including negative microcanonical heat capacity and bimodalities, which have also been reported in atomic cluster physics and self-gravitating systems. Open questions include the mechanism of fragment formation (spinodal decomposition vs. molecular dynamics), the order and nature of the transition, and the need for new experiments with charge and mass identification to study isospin distillation effects.
