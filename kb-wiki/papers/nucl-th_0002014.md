# Magnetic instability of quark matter

> Spontaneous magnetization of quark liquid is examined. It is pointed out that quark liquid has potential to be ferromagnetic at rather low densities.

## Key claim

Quark liquid interacting via one-gluon-exchange has the potential to become ferromagnetic at densities around or below nuclear density, but this conclusion depends on the specific values of quark mass and the quark-gluon coupling constant, and the phase transition is of first order rather than second order.

## Method

The author calculates the exchange energy of quark liquid using the one-gluon-exchange interaction, treating quarks relativistically with a polarization density matrix that includes both spin-nonflip and spin-flip contributions. The total energy (kinetic plus exchange) is computed as a function of the polarization parameter \(p\) (the fractional difference between spin-up and spin-down quark numbers) for various quark densities, using a fiducial set of parameters from the MIT bag model (\(m_q = 300\) MeV, \(\alpha_c = 2.2\)).

## Key numbers

- Critical density for the ferromagnetic phase transition: \(n_q^c \simeq 0.16\ \text{fm}^{-3}\), which corresponds to nuclear density for flavor-symmetric quark matter (using \(m_q = 300\) MeV and \(\alpha_c = 2.2\)).
- Estimated surface magnetic field for a quark star: \(B_{max} \sim O(10^{15-17})\ \text{G}\) for a quark core radius \(r_q \sim O(R)\) and quark density \(n_q = O(0.1)\ \text{fm}^{-3}\), using quark magnetic moment \(\mu_q \sim \mu_N\) (nuclear magneton \(\sim 5\times 10^{-24}\ \text{erg}\cdot\text{gauss}^{-1}\)) for massive quarks and \(10^2\mu_N\) for light quarks.
- Phase diagram presented at \(n_q = 0.3\ \text{fm}^{-3}\) (about twice nuclear density) in the \(m_q\)–\(\alpha_c\) plane, showing ferromagnetic and metastable regions.

## Context

This work extends the concept of itinerant ferromagnetism, originally proposed by Bloch (1929) for electron gas with Coulomb interaction, to quark liquid with one-gluon-exchange interaction. It provides a hadronic origin for the ultra-strong magnetic fields observed in magnetars (anomalous X-ray pulsars and soft-gamma-ray repeaters), which cannot be explained by simple flux conservation from main sequence stars. The results suggest that strange quark stars or strange quark nuggets could be macroscopically magnetized, and call for future Hartree-Fock self-consistent calculations and inclusion of ring diagrams to examine long-range correlations.
