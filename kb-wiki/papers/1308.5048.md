# Probing quantum many-body dynamics in nuclear systems

> Quantum many-body nuclear dynamics is treated at the mean-field level with the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory. Low-lying and high-lying nuclear vibrations are studied using the linear response theory. The fusion mechanism is also described for light and heavy systems. The latter exhibit fusion hindrance due to quasi-fission. Typical characteristics of quasi-fission, such as contact time and partial symmetrisation of the fragments mass in the exit channel, are reproduced by TDHF calculations. The (multi-)nucleon transfer at sub-barrier energies is also discussed.

# Probing quantum many-body dynamics in nuclear systems

## Key claim

The time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) approach reproduces typical characteristics of quasi-fission, such as contact time and partial symmetrisation of fragment masses, and describes low-lying and high-lying nuclear vibrations, but only for light systems does the calculated width of the giant monopole resonance agree with experiment, while for heavy systems the spreading width (neglected in TDHF) becomes dominant.

## Method

The TDHF equation is solved iteratively in time on a Cartesian grid with hard-boundary conditions using the tdhf3d code with the SLy4d parametrisation of the Skyrme energy density functional. For vibrational studies, a boost operator is applied to the ground-state wave-function and the time response is computed over 10–20 zeptoseconds; for collisions, the HF ground states of two nuclei are placed in a box with initial momenta determined from a Rutherford trajectory and evolved over several zeptoseconds. The linear response theory is used to extract strength functions from the time evolution of multipole moments.

## Key numbers

- Octupole response in ⁴⁸Ca: two collective modes at 5.7 MeV (8% of EWSR) and 9.8 MeV (18% of EWSR); the first peak overestimates the experimental 3⁻₁ state energy of 4.507 MeV by about 27%.
- Giant monopole resonance in ⁴⁰Ca: calculated energy 22.1 MeV (experimental 19.2 ± 0.4 MeV); calculated total width from FWHM of strength function is 4.5 MeV (experimental 4.9 ± 0.6 MeV); width from exponential fit of monopole oscillations is Γ ≃ 3.5 MeV.
- Lattice spacing Δx = 0.8 fm and time step Δt = 1.5 × 10⁻²⁴ s.
- ⁴⁰Ca+²³⁸U central collision at Ec.m. = 205.2 MeV (3% above frozen-HF barrier for equatorial configuration): contact time about 20 zs, average exit channel ¹⁰³Mo+¹⁷⁵Yb.
- ⁵⁸Ni+¹²⁴Sn central collision at Ec.m. = 144.7 MeV: neutron number distribution of light fragment compared to experimental data at Ec.m. = 153 MeV (≈0.96 VB) and θc.m. = 127.5° with same distance of closest approach R₀ = 13.93 fm.

## Context

This work extends the TDHF framework developed by Dirac (1930) and implemented in nuclear physics since the mid-1970s (Bonche 1976, Negele 1982) to treat vibrations, fusion, quasi-fission, and transfer reactions within a single mean-field model. The overestimation of low-lying octupole energies with SLy4 confirms a trend seen in ²⁰⁸Pb (Simenel 2012). The quasi-fission results connect to experimental signatures studied by Bock (1982), Toke (1985), and Hinde (1996), while the transfer calculations benchmark sequential transfer against data from Jiang (1998) and show that pairing correlations (Scamps and Lacroix 2013) and beyond-mean-field fluctuations (Simenel 2011) are needed for multi-nucleon transfer. The approach enables systematic microscopic studies of fusion hindrance and superheavy element formation.
