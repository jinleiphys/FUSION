# Short-Range Correlations and the One-Body Density Matrix in Finite Nuclei

> The effects of short-range correlations derived from a realistic meson-exchange potential on the single-particle density matrix in finite nuclei are investigated by analyzing the one-body density in terms of the natural orbits. Basic features of these natural orbits and their spectral distributions are discussed. For many observables it seems to be sufficient to approximate the one-body density matrix in terms of those natural orbits, which exhibit the largest occupation probabilities. For the investigation of the high-momentum components in the single-particle density, however, it is important to take into account natural orbits with small occupation probabilities, originating from the single-particle Green function at large negative energies.

## Key claim

Short-range correlations derived from a realistic meson-exchange potential cause the one-body density matrix in finite nuclei to be dominated by natural orbits with large occupation probabilities for most observables, but the high-momentum components of the single-particle density require including natural orbits with small occupation probabilities that originate from the single-particle Green function at large negative energies.

## Method

The authors solved the Dyson equation for the single-particle Green function in a spherical box basis for ¹⁶O, using a G-matrix derived from the One-Boson-Exchange potential B of Machleidt solved in nuclear matter with Fermi momentum 1.4 fm⁻¹ and starting energy -10 MeV. The self-energy included Hartree-Fock terms from the nuclear matter G-matrix plus 2-particle-1-hole and 2-hole-1-particle contributions calculated directly for the finite system, with the density matrix obtained by integrating the imaginary part of the Green function up to the Fermi energy. Natural orbits and their occupation numbers were then obtained by diagonalizing the one-body density matrix for each partial wave channel.

## Key numbers

- Occupation probability of the first natural orbit for s₁/₂: 0.921 (total occupation for that channel)
- Occupation probability of the first natural orbit for p₃/₂: 0.947 (total occupation for that channel)
- Occupation probability of the first natural orbit for p₁/₂: 0.93 (total occupation for that channel)
- Quasihole contributions to the α=1 natural orbits: 0.78 (s₁/₂), 0.914 (p₃/₂), and 0.898 (p₁/₂)
- Total radius of ¹⁶O from correlated calculation: 2.55 fm, compared to Hartree-Fock result of 2.59 fm
- Box radius used in calculation: 20 fm; oscillator length α = 1.72 fm⁻¹ corresponding to ℏω_osc = 14 MeV
- Experimental single-particle energies used for hole states: -47 MeV (s₁/₂), -21.8 MeV (p₃/₂), -15.7 MeV (p₁/₂)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous Green function calculations for ¹⁶O (Polls 1994, Müther 1994) by analyzing the one-body density matrix in terms of natural orbits, following the approach of natural orbit analysis used for ³He drops by Lewart (1988). The results show that for most one-body observables the density matrix can be approximated by natural orbits with the largest occupation probabilities, but studying high-momentum components requires including natural orbits from the continuum contribution at large negative energies. This enables a connection between the natural orbit decomposition and experimental observables from (e,e'p) knock-out experiments at facilities like MAMI and NIKHEF, particularly for understanding the origin of high-momentum components in the spectral function.
