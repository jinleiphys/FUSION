# Symmetry energy at subnuclear densities deduced from nuclear masses

> We examine how nuclear masses are related to the density dependence of the symmetry energy. Using a macroscopic nuclear model we calculate nuclear masses in a way dependent on the equation of state of asymmetric nuclear matter. We find by comparison with empirical two-proton separation energies that a smaller symmetry energy at subnuclear densities, corresponding to a larger density symmetry coefficient L, is favored. This tendency, which is clearly seen for nuclei that are neutron-rich, nondeformed, and light, can be understood from the property of the surface symmetry energy in a compressible liquid-drop picture.

## Key claim

The paper finds that empirical two-proton separation energies favor a larger density symmetry coefficient L (corresponding to a smaller symmetry energy at subnuclear densities), but this conclusion is conditional: it is most clearly seen for nuclei that are neutron-rich, nondeformed, and light, and the estimate of L depends on uncertainties in the parameters χ and K₀ that characterize the density dependence of the surface and bulk energy.

## Method

The authors used a macroscopic nuclear model based on an extended Thomas-Fermi approximation, with the bulk energy per nucleon expressed as a function of neutron and proton densities using potential energy densities that reproduce microscopic calculations of symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter by Friedman and Pandharipande (1981). They calculated nuclear masses for various equation-of-state parameter sets (L and K₀) consistent with mass and radius data for stable nuclei, then compared the calculated two-proton separation energies with empirical values from Audi, Wapstra, and Thibault (2003).

## Key numbers

- The density symmetry coefficient L was varied in the range 0 < L < 160 MeV, with incompressibility K₀ in the range 180 MeV < K₀ < 360 MeV
- The saturation density n₀ was constrained to 0.14–0.17 fm⁻³, the saturation energy w₀ to −16 ± 1 MeV, the symmetry energy coefficient S₀ to 25–40 MeV, and the gradient coefficient F₀ to 66 ± 6 MeV fm⁵
- The linear relation between S₀ and L was found to be S₀ ≈ 28 MeV + 0.075 L
- The rms deviations of calculated masses from measured values were ∼3–5 MeV, comparable to the Weizsäcker-Bethe formula
- The rms deviations of calculated charge radii from measured values were about 0.06 fm, comparable to the A¹/³ law
- Two specific EOS models were compared: model G with L = 5.7 MeV and model C with L = 146 MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier investigation (Oyamatsu and Iida, 2003) which explored extracting L and S₀ from masses and radii of nuclei but had concentrated on radii of unstable nuclei rather than masses. The present analysis shows that masses, particularly two-proton separation energies for light, neutron-rich, nondeformed nuclei, can also constrain L. The results are consistent with constraints from neutron skin thickness studies (Warda et al., 2009) and can be understood within a compressible liquid-drop picture where the surface symmetry energy depends on L. The findings enable future determination of L when combined with improved understanding of the parameters χ and K₀ and of the discrete behavior of separation energies.
