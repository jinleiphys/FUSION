# Emergence of rotational modes in nuclear fission

> Dinuclear systems that occur in the post-saddle to scission stage in nuclear fission process are special transient formations. The diabatic evolution at this stage is studied using the methods of non-equilibrium thermodynamics. A novel explanation for the emergence of intrinsic rotational modes that occur in such a dinucleus is developed by identifying these modes together as an open subsystem that exchanges matter and energy with its environment. The environment consists of all remaining degrees of freedom of the dinucleus, and a weak coupling of the subsystem to this environment facilitates a diabatic energy exchange. Under appropriate non-equilibrium conditions, the available energy is coupled to the work needed for the emergence of self organizing rotational modes. This comes at the expense of increasing microscopic disorder in the form of intrinsic excitations in the prefragments. A simple formalism is presented such that the magnitudes of relevant energy flow through the subsystem are obtained in terms of entropy production rate, entropy expulsion rate and net rate of change of entropy in sub-units of $MeV zs^{-1} K^{-1}$.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that intrinsic rotational modes in fissioning dinuclei emerge as self-organizing dissipative structures during the diabatic (weak damping) phase, driven by nucleon pair transfers from the neck region, but only under appropriate non-equilibrium conditions where the entropy production rate and entropy expulsion rate combine to yield a negative net rate of change of entropy in the rotational subsystem.

## Method

The dinucleus at pre-scission stage is treated as a closed thermodynamic system containing an open subsystem of rotational degrees of freedom that exchanges matter and energy with a surrounding heat bath of all remaining degrees of freedom. The authors model the initiation of rotational modes via nucleon pair transfers from the neck region, using a macroscopic dynamical model with frictional coefficients analogous to that developed by Tsang (1974) for heavy-ion collisions. The time-dependent free energy difference (relative entropy) and entropy balance equation are applied to calculate entropy production rate, entropy expulsion rate, and net entropy change for the rotational subsystem in units of MeV zs⁻¹ K⁻¹, using a one-dimensional model of saddle-to-scission motion for ²³⁶U fission.

## Key numbers

* For a single nucleon entering a prefragment from the neck with Fermi momentum, the angular momentum imparted is approximately ½ R' P_F ≈ 4 ħ.
* The empirical constants used for the saddle-to-scission motion model are c = 0.007 fm and λ = 1.0 × 10⁻²¹ s⁻¹.
* The saturation temperature T_s at scission is taken as 1 MeV for nth+²³⁵U fission.
* The root-mean-square angular momentum j_rms calculated using T = 1 MeV in the spin-cutoff formula is 8.6 ħ for symmetric split of ²³⁶U.
* The level density parameter is taken as a = A_F/8 where A_F is the mass of the symmetric prefragment.

## Context

This work extends the macroscopic dynamical model of dinuclear friction developed by Tsang (1974) and the diabatic description of fission by Swiatecki (1972) and Wali (1988), applying non-equilibrium thermodynamics concepts from Qian (2001) and Prigogine (1967) to nuclear fission for the first time. The paper provides a physical mechanism for the initiation and growth of intrinsic rotational modes, particularly the wriggling mode noted in damped nuclear reactions by Dossing (1985) and Randrup (1982), and offers a thermodynamic framework that could be tested experimentally by tracking radiated power with advanced detector setups.
