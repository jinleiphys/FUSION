# On the emission of the fast $δ-$electrons during nuclear $β^{-}$-decay in few-electron atoms

> We discuss a possibility to observe fast secondary electrons ($δ-$electrons) during nuclear $β^{-}$-decay in few-electron atoms and ions. Formulas for the corresponding probabilities and explicit expression for the energy spectrum of $δ-$electrons are derived in the closed analytical forms.

## Key claim
The paper argues that fast secondary electrons (δ-electrons) can be observed during nuclear β⁻-decay in few-electron atoms and ions, with an overall probability on the order of α³ or even α², which is larger than the lowest-order estimate of α⁴ P_e (where P_e is the probability of free-electron emission and α is the fine-structure constant).

## Method
The author applies the sudden approximation to the QED process of non-elastic electron-electron scattering, where a fast β⁻-electron from nuclear decay interacts with a bound atomic electron initially at rest. Starting from the known QED cross-section for (e⁻,e⁻)-scattering, the differential cross-section is re-expressed in terms of the dimensionless parameter Δ (the ratio of δ-electron to β-electron kinetic energies). A finite-distance correction factor ⟨a₀²/r²_eN⟩ is introduced to account for the actual electron-nucleus distance in the atom, and the final formula is integrated over the β⁻-electron spectrum using the Fermi spectral function.

## Key numbers
- The fine-structure constant α = e²/(ħc) ≈ 7.2973525664 × 10⁻³.
- The Bohr radius a₀ = 5.2917721092(17) × 10⁻⁹ cm.
- For the two-electron Na⁹⁺ ion in its ground 1¹S-state: N_e = 2, ⟨1/r²_eN⟩ ≈ 230.239 a.u., and the coefficient 16 N_e π α⁴ a₀² · ⟨1/r²_eN⟩ = 1.83799 × 10⁻²¹ cm² (at ζ = 1).
- For the Co²⁵⁺ ion in its ground 1¹S-state: ⟨1/r²_eN⟩ ≈ 1428.873 a.u., and the coefficient = 1.140662 × 10⁻²⁰ cm².
- For the four-electron Be atom in its ground 2¹S-state: the coefficient = 2.299034 × 10⁻²² cm².
- The overall probability to observe fast δ-electrons is ≈ α³ or even ≈ α².

## Context
This work extends the author's prior studies on final-state probabilities in β⁻-decay (Frolov 2005, Frolov 2015) by applying the sudden approximation to the specific case of δ-electron emission. The derived formulas enable calculation of the energy spectrum of fast secondary electrons from β⁻-decay in few-electron atoms and ions, which can be compared with experimental β⁻-spectra such as those from ⁶⁴Cu and ²¹⁰Bi studied by Cook and Neary. The analysis also notes that similar processes can occur during β⁺-decay, to be treated in a future study.
