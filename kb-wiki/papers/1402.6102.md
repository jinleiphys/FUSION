# Microscopic description of octupole shape-phase transitions in light actinides and rare-earth nuclei

> A systematic analysis of low-lying quadrupole and octupole collective states is presented, based on the microscopic energy density functional framework. By mapping the deformation constrained self-consistent axially symmetric mean-field energy surfaces onto the equivalent Hamiltonian of the $sdf$ interacting boson model (IBM), that is, onto the energy expectation value in the boson condensate state, the Hamiltonian parameters are determined. The study is based on the global relativistic energy density functional DD-PC1. The resulting IBM Hamiltonian is used to calculate excitation spectra and transition rates for the positive- and negative-parity collective states in four isotopic chains characteristic for two regions of octupole deformation and collectivity: Th, Ra, Sm and Ba. Consistent with the empirical trend, the microscopic calculation based on the systematics of $β_{2}$-$β_{3}$ energy maps, the resulting low-lying negative-parity bands and transition rates show evidence of a shape transition between stable octupole deformation and octupole vibrations characteristic for $β_{3}$-soft potentials.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a systematic microscopic analysis of low-lying quadrupole and octupole collective states, based on mapping relativistic mean-field energy surfaces onto the $sdf$ interacting boson model (IBM), shows evidence of a shape transition between stable octupole deformation and octupole vibrations in four isotopic chains (Th, Ra, Sm, Ba), consistent with empirical trends. The hedge is that this evidence is found "consistent with the empirical trend" and is characteristic for $\beta_{3}$-soft potentials, rather than claiming definitive proof of static octupole deformation in all cases.

## Method

The authors performed constrained self-consistent axially symmetric relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations using the DD-PC1 energy density functional to generate deformation energy surfaces in the ($\beta_{2}$, $\beta_{3}$) plane for each nucleus. They then mapped these microscopic energy surfaces onto the expectation value of an $sdf$ IBM Hamiltonian in the boson condensate state, determining the Hamiltonian parameters without phenomenological adjustment to experiment. The resulting IBM Hamiltonian was diagonalized using the code OCTUPOLE to calculate excitation spectra and electromagnetic transition rates for positive- and negative-parity collective states.

## Key numbers

* The RHB calculation predicts the deepest octupole minimum in $^{226}$Th, with the minimum located at ($\beta_{2}$, $\beta_{3}$) $\approx$ (0.15, 0.1) for $^{224}$Th.
* For $^{224}$Ra, the absolute minimum is found at ($\beta_{2}$, $\beta_{3}$) $\approx$ (0.2, 0.15), consistent with experimentally extracted values $\beta_{2}=0.154$ and $\beta_{3}=0.097$ from Gaffney et al. (2013).
* The effective charges used are $e_{1}=0.01$ $e$b$^{1/2}$ for all nuclei, $e_{2}=0.19$ $e$b for Th-Ra and 0.13 $e$b for Sm-Ba isotopes, and $e_{3}=0.15$ $e$b$^{3/2}$ for Th-Ra and 0.09 $e$b$^{3/2}$ for Sm-Ba isotopes.
* The total boson number $N_{B}$ ranges from 6 to 12 for $^{220-232}$Th, 5 to 10 for $^{218-228}$Ra, 7 to 12 for $^{146-156}$Sm, and 4 to 9 for $^{140-150}$Ba.
* The average octupole deformation $\langle\beta_{3}\rangle$ shows a sudden increase from $^{222}$Th to $^{224}$Th, from $^{220}$Ra to $^{222}$Ra, from $^{148}$Sm to $^{150}$Sm, and from $^{142}$Ba to $^{144}$Ba, stabilizing at $\beta_{3}\approx 0.15$ in heavier nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the method of Nomura et al. (2008) for determining IBM Hamiltonians from microscopic energy surfaces, which was previously applied to study moments of inertia (Nomura et al., 2011), gamma-softness (Nomura et al., 2012), shape coexistence (Nomura et al., 2012), and octupole shape-phase transitions in the Th isotopic chain (Nomura et al., 2013). The present study systematically analyzes four isotopic chains (Th, Ra, Sm, Ba) across two regions of octupole deformation, enabling comparison with recent Coulomb excitation experiments on $^{220}$Rn and $^{224}$Ra by Gaffney et al. (2013) and with previous mean-field calculations using the Nilsson-Strutinsky method (Nazarewicz et al., 1984, 1985) and Gogny D1S effective interaction (Robledo et al., 2010).
