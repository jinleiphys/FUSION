# Variations of Hadron Masses and Matter Properties in Dense Nuclear Matter

> Using a self-consistent quark model for nuclear matter we investigate variations of the masses of the non-strange vector mesons, the hyperons and the nucleon in dense nuclear matter (up to four times the normal nuclear density). We find that the changes in the hadron masses can be described in terms of the value of the scalar mean-field in matter. The model is then used to calculate the density dependence of the quark condensate in-medium, which turns out to be well approximated by a linear function of the nuclear density. Some relations among the hadron properties and the in-medium quark condensate are discussed.

## Key claim

The changes in hadron masses in dense nuclear matter can be described in terms of the scalar mean-field value and the number of non-strange quarks in each hadron, with the quark condensate in medium well approximated by a linear function of nuclear density, though the authors caution that meson exchange currents and pionic cloud effects are omitted.

## Method

The authors used the quark-meson coupling (QMC) model, treating nuclear matter as a collection of MIT bags for nucleons, vector mesons (omega and rho), and hyperons (Lambda, Sigma, Xi), self-consistently bound by exchange of scalar (sigma) and vector (omega, rho) mesons in mean-field approximation. The Dirac equation for quarks in a static spherical bag included interactions with the scalar and vector mean fields, with the effective quark mass defined as the bare quark mass minus the scalar potential. The coupling constants were determined by fitting the binding energy (-16 MeV) at saturation density (0.17 fm^-3) for symmetric nuclear matter and the bulk symmetry energy (33.2 MeV), and the strange quark mass was fixed to reproduce the average mass of Lambda and Sigma hyperons (1154 MeV) in free space.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear density: rho_0 = 0.17 fm^-3
- Effective nucleon mass at saturation density: M_N^* = 838, 850, 855 MeV for bag radii R_0 = 0.6, 0.8, 1.0 fm respectively
- Nuclear compressibility: K = 223, 205, 196 MeV for R_0 = 0.6, 0.8, 1.0 fm respectively
- Scalar mean-field approximation at small density: V_sigma approximately 140 MeV times (rho_B / rho_0)
- Effective nucleon mass at small density: M_N^* / M_N approximately 1 - 0.14 (rho_B / rho_0)
- Vector meson mass reduction at small density: m_v^* / m_v approximately 1 - 0.09 (rho_B / rho_0)
- Average mass of Lambda and Sigma at small density: M_H_av^* / M_H_av approximately 1 - 0.08 (rho_B / rho_0)
- Xi hyperon mass reduction at small density: M_Xi^* / M_Xi approximately 1 - 0.04 (rho_B / rho_0)
- Quark condensate ratio in symmetric nuclear matter: Q(rho_B) / Q(0) approximately 1 - 0.36 (rho_B / rho_0)
- Quark condensate ratio in neutron matter (f_p = 0): Q(rho_B) / Q(0) approximately 1 - 0.34 (rho_B / rho_0)

## Context

This work extends the quark-meson coupling model previously developed by Guichon (1988) and refined by Fleck et al. (1990) and the present authors, which had been applied to nuclear structure functions, neutron matter, and isospin symmetry breaking. The results contrast with approaches based on QCD sum rules (Hatsuda and Lee, 1992) and the Walecka model (QHD) (Saito, 1989; Williams, 1990; Hatsuda, 1994), where vector meson mass reduction arises from vacuum polarization due to nucleon-antinucleon pair excitation rather than scalar mean-field effects. The finding that the quark condensate ratio is well approximated by a linear function of density agrees with the model-independent prediction of Cohen et al. (1992) for symmetric nuclear matter, and the authors suggest that hadron mass ratios do not simply scale as (Q(rho_B)/Q(0))^(1/3) as proposed by Brown and Rho (1991), but rather follow relations based on the number of non-strange quarks.
