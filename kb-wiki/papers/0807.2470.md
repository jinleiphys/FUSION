# Charmonium and bottomonium production in heavy-ion collisions

> We review the present status in the theoretical and phenomenological understanding of charmonium and bottomonium production in heavy-ion collisions. We start by recapitulating the basic notion of "anomalous quarkonium suppression" in heavy-ion collisions and its recent amendments involving regeneration reactions. We then survey in some detail concepts and ingredients needed for a comprehensive approach to utilize heavy quarkonia as a probe of hot and dense matter. The theoretical discussion encompasses recent lattice QCD computations of quarkonium properties in the Quark-Gluon Plasma, their interpretations using effective potential models, inelastic rate calculations and insights from analyses of electromagnetic plasmas. We illustrate the powerful techniques of thermodynamic Green functions (T-matrices) to provide a general framework for implementing microscopic properties of heavy quarkonia into a kinetic theory of suppression and regeneration reactions. The theoretical concepts are tested in applications to heavy-ion reactions at SPS, RHIC and LHC. We outline perspectives for future experiments on charmonium and bottomonium production in heavy-ion collisions over a large range in energy (FAIR, RHIC-II and LHC). These are expected to provide key insights into hadronic matter under extreme conditions using quarkonium observables.

## Key claim

This review argues that a comprehensive understanding of charmonium and bottomonium production in heavy-ion collisions requires combining the traditional "anomalous suppression" picture with regeneration reactions, and that the in-medium properties of quarkonia (masses, widths, dissolution temperatures) extracted from lattice QCD and effective models are essential for a quantitative description of experimental data across SPS, RHIC, and LHC energies.

## Method

The authors survey theoretical approaches including lattice QCD computations of quarkonium spectral functions and free energies, effective potential models (screened Cornell potential, internal energy potentials), thermodynamic T-matrix approaches that unify bound and scattering states, and inelastic rate calculations for both the quark-gluon plasma and hadron gas. They compare these theoretical predictions to experimental data from heavy-ion collisions at SPS, RHIC, and LHC, and outline perspectives for future experiments at FAIR, RHIC-II, and LHC.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature in unquenched lattice QCD: T_c ≈ 180 ± 30 MeV (systematic uncertainty of ca. ±10-15%)
- Critical temperature in quenched QCD: T_c ≈ 270 MeV
- J/ψ lifetime in free space: τ_J/ψ^vac ≈ 2000 fm/c, compared to typical fireball lifetime of ~10 fm/c
- Ground-state S-wave charmonia (η_c, J/ψ) survive in QGP up to temperatures of ~2 T_c
- P-wave states (χ_c) dissolve at temperatures slightly above critical, ~1.2 T_c
- Ground-state bottomonia survive to 2.5-3 T_c or more
- Critical Debye masses for dissolution: μ_D^diss(n=1) ≈ 0.7 GeV for ground state charmonium, μ_D^diss(n=2) ≈ 0.35 GeV for ψ' and χ_c
- Critical Debye masses for bottomonium: μ_D^diss(n=1) ≈ 1.6 GeV for ground state, μ_D^diss(n=2) ≈ 0.6 GeV for Υ' and χ_b
- String-breaking separation: r_sb ≈ 1.2 fm, with F_QQ^(1)(r_sb;0) ≈ 1.1 GeV
- J/ψ dissolution temperature from T-matrix approach using internal energy: ~3 T_c (or ~2.5 T_c with alternative internal energy)
- χ_c melting temperature from T-matrix approach: ~1.3 T_c

## Context

This review extends and synthesizes earlier work on quarkonium suppression, notably the pioneering studies by Matsui and Satz (1986) on J/ψ suppression as a signature of deconfinement, and subsequent work by Karsch, Mehr, and Satz (1987) on in-medium potential models. It incorporates recent lattice QCD results from the groups of Kaczmarek (2005), Aarts (2007), and Jakovac (2006), and builds on the T-matrix framework developed by Mannarelli and Rapp (2005) and Cabrera and Rapp (2006). The review enables future experimental programs at FAIR, RHIC-II, and LHC to use quarkonium observables as quantitative probes of the quark-gluon plasma, particularly by distinguishing suppression from regeneration effects.
