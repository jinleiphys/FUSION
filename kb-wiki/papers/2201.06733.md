# Taking Neutrino Pictures via Electrons

> In this paper we discuss the prospects to take a picture of an extended neutrino source, i.e., resolving its angular neutrino luminosity distribution. This is challenging since neutrino directions cannot be directly measured but only estimated from the directions of charged particles they interact with in the detector material. This leads to an intrinsic blurring effect. We first discuss the problem in general terms and then apply our insights to solar neutrinos scattering elastically with electrons. Despite the aforementioned blurring we show how with high statistics and precision the original neutrino distributions could be reconstructed.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that it is theoretically possible to distinguish an extended neutrino source (such as a ring the size of the sun) from a point source using the double differential event rate of electrons scattered by neutrinos, but only under the condition of high statistics and precision; for solar neutrinos around 10 MeV the differences between a point and ring source are at the sub-permil level, making experimental distinction "rather hard but theoretically possible."

## Method

The authors derive a theoretical framework relating the double differential event rate of scattered electrons (with respect to electron energy and recoil direction) to the angular and energy distributions of incoming neutrinos via an invertible integral transformation involving the differential cross section. They apply this framework to solar neutrinos scattering elastically with electrons, using the Radon transform formalism adapted from directional dark matter detection (Gondolo 2002). For realistic solar neutrino sources, they compute the angular luminosity distributions from radial production zones using line-of-sight integrals of solar model data from Vinyoles (2016), and compare the double differential rates for point sources versus ring sources with the angular size of the sun.

## Key numbers

- The angular size of the sun in the sky is approximately \(4.65 \times 10^{-3}\) radians (0.267 degrees), calculated using solar radius 696,340 km and average distance 149,600,000 km.
- For a monochromatic 10 MeV neutrino source, the ratio of differential electron scattering rates between a ring source (size of the sun) and a point source shows differences at the sub-permil level.
- For a monochromatic 100 MeV neutrino source with the same ring size, the relative deviation reaches the permil level for small scattering angles.
- For a ring source ten times larger than the sun at 10 MeV, deviations reach the percent level distributed over wider scattering angles.
- The solar \(^8\)B neutrino flux used is \(f_0^{\text{8B}} = 5.46 \times 10^6\) cm\(^{-2}\) s\(^{-1}\) (GS98 prediction from Vitagliano 2019).
- The electron neutrino survival probability used is \(P_{ee} = 0.37\) (from Borexino 2018).
- The hep neutrino energy can reach up to 19 MeV, while pep neutrinos have a constant energy of 1.445 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the directional detection formalism developed for dark matter by Gondolo (2002) to relativistic neutrinos, and builds upon previous studies of neutrino source reconstruction such as Davis (2016). The framework enables future experiments like Hyper-Kamiokande, JUNO, SNO+, IceCube-GEN2, PINGU, and KM3NeT to potentially resolve the angular structure of solar neutrino sources, which could help distinguish different solar models and reconstruct three-dimensional solar properties including temperature and pressure profiles. The authors note that monochromatic neutrino sources would be particularly well-suited subjects for this technique.
