# Quiescent thermal emission from neutron stars in LMXBs

> We monitored the quiescent thermal emission from neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binaries after active periods of intense activity in x-rays (outbursts). The theoretical modeling of the thermal relaxation of the neutron star crust may be used to establish constraints on the crust composition and transport properties, depending on the astrophysical scenarios assumed. We numerically simulated the thermal evolution of the neutron star crust and compared them with inferred surface temperatures for five sources: MXB 1659-29, KS 1731-260, EXO 0748-676, XTE J1701-462 and IGR J17480-2446. We find that the evolution of MXB 1659-29, KS 1731-260 and EXO 0748-676 can be well described within a deep crustal cooling scenario. Conversely, we find that the other two sources can only be explained with models beyond crustal cooling. For the peculiar emission of XTE J1701-462 we propose alternative scenarios such as residual accretion during quiescence, additional heat sources in the outer crust, and/or thermal isolation of the inner crust due to a buried magnetic field. We also explain the very recent reported temperature of IGR J17480-2446 with an additional heat deposition in the outer crust from shallow sources.

# Quiescent thermal emission from neutron stars in LMXBs

## Key claim
The thermal relaxation of neutron star crusts after outbursts can explain the quiescent emission of three sources (MXB 1659-29, KS 1731-260, EXO 0748-676) within a deep crustal cooling scenario, but two other sources (XTE J1701-462 and IGR J17480-2446) require additional mechanisms beyond crustal cooling, such as residual accretion, shallow heat sources, or magnetic field effects.

## Method
The authors performed time-dependent numerical simulations of the thermal evolution of neutron star crusts using a BBP equation of state at low densities and a Skyrme-type SLy effective interaction for the inner crust and core, incorporating accreted crust composition from Haensel and Zdunik (2008). They compared simulated surface temperatures with observational data from five quasi-persistent sources monitored by Chandra, XMM-Newton, and Swift telescopes, fitting the data with exponential and power-law decay models to constrain crust properties such as thermal conductivity and impurity content.

## Key numbers
- Total heat released from deep crustal heating: Q_tot ≈ 1.9 MeV per accreted nucleon (from Haensel and Zdunik 2008)
- MXB 1659-29: exponential decay fit with e-folding time τ = 465 ± 25 days, base temperature b = 54 ± 2 eV, accretion rate 0.07–0.18 × 10^18 g s^-1 over 2.5 years
- KS 1731-260: exponential decay fit with τ = 418 ± 70 days, base temperature b = 67.7 ± 1.3 eV, accretion rate 0.05–0.3 × 10^18 g s^-1 over 12.5 years
- EXO 0748-676: temperature decrease from ~124 eV to ~109 eV over 19 months, accretion rate ~0.03 × 10^18 g s^-1 over 24 years
- XTE J1701-462: exponential e-folding time τ = 133 (+38, -25) days, base temperature b = 123.4 ± 0.9 eV, accretion rate 1.1 × 10^18 g s^-1 over 1.6 years
- IGR J17480-2446: exponential e-folding time τ = 157 ± 62 days, base temperature b = 84.3 ± 1.4 eV, accretion rate ~0.2 × 10^18 g s^-1 over 0.17 years
- Neutron star models: M = 1.4 M_sun (radius 11.79 km, crust width 944 m) and M = 1.6 M_sun (radius 11.61 km, crust width 735 m)
- Impurity parameter Q_imp estimated to be ~1–5 from fitting KS 1731-260 and MXB 1659-29 data (consistent with Brown and Cumming 2009)

## Context
This work extends the crustal cooling modeling pioneered by Brown (1998) and Colpi (2001), and follows the analyses of Shternin (2007) and Brown and Cumming (2009) for KS 1731-260 and MXB 1659-29, which suggested high thermal conductivity in the outer crust. The study contradicts the inefficient crust conductivity required for carbon ignition in superbursts (Cooper 2005, Cumming 2006) and addresses open questions raised by Fridriksson (2011) regarding continued cooling in KS 1731-260 and XTE J1701-462. The results enable future work on alternative heating mechanisms in the outer crust, including heat convection from chemical separation (Medin and Cumming 2014) or rapid neutrino cooling shells (Schatz 2014), and motivate further observations of the five sources to distinguish between deep crustal cooling and shallow heating scenarios.
