# Systematic analysis of proton- and deuteron-induced one-proton knockout reactions

> The ratios of the one-proton knockout cross sections by a deuteron to those by a proton are about 1.5, indicating that using deuteron is more efficient than proton in yielding large knockout cross sections. However, this ratio differs from the intuitive expectation, and its underlying mechanism remains unclear. The purpose of this study is to clarify the mechanism behind the observed ratio by theoretically describing and analyzing the deuteron- and proton-induced one-proton knockout reactions. Proton-induced one-proton knockout reactions are described within the standard distorted-wave impulse approximation (DWIA) framework, while deuteron-induced one-proton knockout reactions are treated with a new approach, DWIA-BU, that incorporates deuteron breakup into the DWIA. The ratios calculated with the DWIA-BU reproduce the experimental data reasonably, whereas those with the DWIA significantly underestimate them. The ratio of the corresponding elementary cross sections remains about 3.5 regardless of the energy, and the difference in absorption between the deuteron and the proton influences the ratios of knockout cross sections, resulting in agreement between the calculated ratios and the experimental data. It is found that the deuteron breakup is essential to reproduce the experimental ratio. The ratios of the knockout cross sections are primarily determined by the difference in the elementary cross sections and that in the absorption between the deuteron and the proton.

## Key claim

The ratio of deuteron-induced to proton-induced one-proton knockout cross sections is about 1.4 when calculated with the DWIA-BU framework that includes deuteron breakup, reasonably reproducing experimental data; this ratio arises from the combination of the elementary cross-section ratio (about 3.5) and stronger absorption of the deuteron compared to the proton, contrary to the naive expectation of a factor of 4 based on nucleon number.

## Method

The authors describe proton-induced one-proton knockout reactions A(p,2p)B using the standard distorted-wave impulse approximation (DWIA) framework. For deuteron-induced one-proton knockout reactions A(d,xp)B, they develop a new approach called DWIA-BU that incorporates deuteron breakup by adding an isotropic breakup cross-section term to the elementary d-p elastic cross-section within the DWIA. The total knockout cross sections are obtained by integrating the triple differential cross sections over the outgoing particle energies and solid angles, and the ratios of deuteron-induced to proton-induced cross sections are compared with experimental data for 12 neutron-rich nuclei at around 240 MeV per nucleon.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of deuteron-induced to proton-induced one-proton knockout cross sections calculated with DWIA-BU is about 1.4, which reasonably reproduces the experimental data
- The ratio calculated with DWIA (without breakup) is approximately 0.2, significantly underestimating the experimental data
- The ratio calculated with PWIA-BU (plane wave, no absorption) is about 3.5, independent of the target nucleus
- The ratio of the elementary total cross sections sigma_tot_dp / sigma_tot_pp remains about 3.5 regardless of the energy
- The weight ratio W^d_j / W^p_j is approximately 0.4 in the region where the weights are mainly distributed, leading to the DWIA-BU ratio of 0.4 x 3.5 = 1.4
- The isotropic approximation for the d-p breakup cross section introduces only about a 7% effect (ratio of about 0.93) at 250 MeV per nucleon

## Context

This work extends the experimental measurements by Miwa et al. (2019), who found that deuteron-to-proton knockout cross-section ratios are about 1.5 for neutron-rich nuclei at around 240 MeV per nucleon, smaller than the naively expected factor of 4. The study clarifies the mechanism behind this ratio by showing that deuteron breakup is essential for reproducing experimental data, and that the ratio is determined by the combination of elementary cross-section differences and stronger absorption of deuterons compared to protons. The DWIA-BU approach can be extended to inclusive reactions involving other projectiles beyond deuterons.
