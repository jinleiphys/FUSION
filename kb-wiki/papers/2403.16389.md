# Illuminating Systematic Trends in Nuclear Data with Generative Machine Learning Models

> We introduce a novel method for studying systematic trends in nuclear reaction data using generative adversarial networks. Libraries of nuclear cross section evaluations exhibit intricate systematic trends across the nuclear landscape, and predictive models capable of reproducing and analyzing these trends are valuable for many applications. We have developed a predictive model using deep generative adversarial networks to learn trends from the inelastic neutron scattering channel of TENDL for even-even nuclei. The system predicts cross sections based on adding/subtracting particles to/from the target nucleus. It can thus help identify cross sections that break from expected trends and predict beyond the limit of current experiments. Our model can produce good predictions for cross section curves for many nuclides, and it is most robust near the line of stability. We also create an ensemble of predictions to leverage different correlations and estimate model uncertainty. This research marks an important first step in computer generation of nuclear cross-section libraries.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a cycle-consistent generative adversarial network (cycleGAN) can learn systematic trends in inelastic neutron scattering cross sections from the TENDL library for even-even nuclei, but the model is most robust near the line of stability and its accuracy depends on the complexity of local trends around where predictions are made.

## Method

The authors first encode normalized cross section curves (0-30 MeV, 256 bins, normalized to maximum of 1) using a convolutional variational auto-encoder (VAE) to produce a latent space representation. They then train four independent cycleGANs, each corresponding to a direction on the chart of nuclides (adding/subtracting neutron pairs, proton pairs, or both), where each cycleGAN contains two translator networks and one discriminator network. The translators map between encoded cross section vectors of neighboring even-even nuclei, with the encoded vectors augmented by normalized proton and neutron numbers, and the system is trained using prediction loss, cycle loss, discriminator loss, and adversarial loss with dropout and label noise regularization.

## Key numbers

- Cross section curves are standardized to an energy domain of 0-30 MeV in 256 bins and normalized individually to a maximum of 1
- The VAE latent space dimension equals the number of principal components used, with the VAE outperforming linear PCA with the same number of variables (shown qualitatively in Fig. 2)
- Translators have 4 hidden layers of size 512; discriminators have 4 hidden layers of size 68
- Dropout probability is 50% on inner hidden layers of translators and 25% for discriminators
- Label noise standard deviation σ_ε is 0.1
- The adversarial loss coefficient λ_adv is set to 5-10; prediction loss coefficient λ_pred is set to approximately 10 at the beginning of training, then scheduled to decrease to ≤1
- Each chain of linked translations is stable for approximately 5-6 steps on average

## Context

This work extends data-driven machine learning methods previously applied to nuclear masses (Gao 2021, PhysRevC 2022, Niu 2022) and error detection in engineering libraries (Grechanuk 2021) to the domain of evaluated nuclear reaction cross sections. The authors focus on the TENDL library (Koning 2019) and the inelastic neutron scattering channel for even-even nuclei, building a foundation for developing more sophisticated generative AI models that could eventually predict cross sections beyond experimental barriers and help identify cross sections that break from expected systematic trends.
