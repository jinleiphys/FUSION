# Proton and neutron density distributions at supranormal density in low- and medium-energy heavy-ion collisions

> We report results of the first systematic simulation of proton and neutron density distributions in central heavy-ion collisions within the beam energy range of $ E_{\rm beam} \leq 800 \, \text{MeV/nucl}$ using pBUU and TDHF models. The symmetric $^\text{40}$Ca +$^\text{40}$Ca, $^\text{48}$Ca +$^\text{48}$Ca, $^\text{100}$Sn +$^\text{100}$Sn and $^\text{120}$Sn + $^\text{120}$Sn and asymmetric $^\text{40}$Ca +$^\text{48}$Ca and $^\text{100}$Sn +$^\text{120}$Sn systems were chosen for the simulations. We find limits on the maximum proton and neutron densities and the related proton-neutron asymmetry $δ$ as a function of the initial state, beam energy, system size and a symmetry energy model. While the maximum densities are almost independent of these parameters, our simulation reveals, for the first time, their subtle impact on the proton-neutron asymmetry. Most importantly, we find that variations in the proton-neutron asymmetry at maximum densities are related at most at 50\% level to the details in the symmetry energy at supranormal density. The reminder is due to the details in the symmetry energy at subnormal densities and its impact on proton and neutron distributions in the initial state. This result puts to forefront the need of a proper initialization of the nuclei in the simulation, but also brings up the question of microscopy, such as shell effects, that affect initial proton and neutron densities, but cannot be consistently incorporated into semiclassical transport models.

## Key claim

The paper reports the first systematic simulation of proton and neutron density distributions in central heavy-ion collisions, finding that variations in the proton-neutron asymmetry at maximum densities are related at most at the 50% level to the details of the symmetry energy at supranormal density; the remainder is due to the symmetry energy at subnormal densities and its impact on the initial-state distributions, highlighting the need for proper nuclear initialization in transport simulations.

## Method

The authors employed the semiclassical Boltzmann-Uhlenbeck-Uehling (pBUU) transport model with four empirical models for the density dependence of the symmetry energy (labeled S, SM, SMS, and SSM) to simulate central heavy-ion collisions at beam energies up to 800 MeV/nucleon. For beam energies at or below 40 MeV/nucleon, they additionally used the Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) model with the SV-bas Skyrme interaction. Maximum proton and neutron densities were extracted from the time evolution of the density distributions for symmetric systems (40Ca+40Ca, 48Ca+48Ca, 100Sn+100Sn, 120Sn+120Sn) and asymmetric systems (40Ca+48Ca, 100Sn+120Sn).

## Key numbers

- Highest total densities predicted at E_beam = 800 MeV/nucleon were of the order of approximately 2.5 rho_0, where rho_0 = 0.16 fm^-3, for both Sn and Ca systems.
- The highest value of the proton-neutron asymmetry delta = (rho_n^max - rho_p^max)/(rho_n^max + rho_p^max) found across all systems and beam energies was approximately 0.17.
- The pBUU simulations used 3000 test particles per physical particle to minimize statistical fluctuations.
- The symmetry energy slope parameter L was 85 MeV for the S model and 31 MeV for the SM model, both yielding similar symmetry energy values of approximately 31 MeV at normal density.
- The TDHF calculations used the SV-bas interaction with nuclear matter incompressibility K = 234 MeV, effective mass m*/m = 0.9, and symmetry energy coefficient a_sym = 30 MeV.

## Context

This work extends prior transport model studies of isospin diffusion and fragmentation in heavy-ion collisions, such as those by Tsang (2004) and Ono (2004), by providing the first systematic survey of maximum proton and neutron densities and their asymmetry across multiple systems and beam energies. The results enable better constraints on the density dependence of the symmetry energy, particularly the separation of subnormal and supranormal density contributions, and motivate improved treatment of nuclear shell effects and initial-state densities that semiclassical models like pBUU cannot consistently incorporate.
