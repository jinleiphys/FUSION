# Heavy flavor puzzle at LHC: a serendipitous interplay of jet suppression and fragmentation

> Both charged hadrons and D mesons are considered to be excellent probes of QCD matter created in ultra relativistic heavy ion collisions. Surprisingly, recent experimental observations at LHC show the same jet suppression for these two probes, which - contrary to pQCD expectations - may suggest similar energy losses for light quarks and gluons in QCD medium. We here use our recently developed energy loss formalism in a finite size dynamical QCD medium to analyze this phenomenon that we denote as the "heavy flavor puzzle at LHC". We show that this puzzle is a consequence of an unusual combination of the suppression and fragmentation patterns, and in fact does not require invoking the same energy loss for light partons. Furthermore, we show that this combination leads to a simple relationship between the suppressions of light hadrons and D mesons and the corresponding bare quark suppressions. Consequently, a coincidental matching of jet suppression and fragmentation allows considerably simplifying the interpretation of the corresponding experimental data.

# Heavy flavor puzzle at LHC: a serendipitous interplay of jet suppression and fragmentation

## Key claim
The observed equality of charged hadron and D meson suppression at LHC does not require that gluons and light quarks lose the same amount of energy in the QCD medium; instead, it arises from a coincidental combination of jet suppression and fragmentation patterns within perturbative QCD, where light hadron fragmentation distorts the bare gluon and light quark suppressions such that the resultant charged hadron suppression almost identically reproduces the bare light quark suppression.

## Method
The author applied a finite-size dynamical QCD medium energy loss formalism with no free parameters, incorporating jet energy loss in a dynamical medium, finite magnetic mass effects, running coupling, multigluon fluctuations, and path-length fluctuations. Initial parton distributions were computed at next-to-leading order using the code from Cacciari et al. (2012) and FONLL for heavy quarks, and from Kang, Vitev, and Xing (2012) for light quarks and gluons; fragmentation functions were taken from de Florian, Sassot, and Stratmann (2007) for light hadrons and from Cacciari, Nason (2003) and Braaten et al. (1995) for D mesons. The QGP temperature was set to 304 MeV as extracted by ALICE, with a Debye mass of approximately 0.9 GeV obtained self-consistently.

## Key numbers
- QGP temperature: T = 304 MeV (as extracted by ALICE from Wilde (2012))
- Debye mass: μ_E ≈ 0.9 GeV (obtained by self-consistently solving Eq. (3) from Djordjevic and Djordjevic (2013))
- Magnetic mass range: 0.4 μ_E < μ_M < 0.6 μ_E
- Charm quark mass: M = 1.2 GeV
- Bottom quark mass: M = 4.75 GeV

- Effective light quark flavors: n_f = 2.5

## Context
This work addresses the "heavy flavor puzzle at LHC," where preliminary ALICE measurements (Aamodt et al., 2013; Grelli for ALICE, 2013) showed equal R_AA for charged hadrons and D mesons in central 2.76 TeV Pb+Pb collisions, contrary to pQCD expectations that gluon-dominated charged hadrons should be more suppressed than charm-dominated D mesons. The analysis extends the author's previously developed dynamical energy loss formalism (Djordjevic, 2009; Djordjevic and Heinz, 2008) and shows that the puzzle can be resolved within pQCD without invoking exotic mechanisms such as those explored by Ficnar, Noronha, and Gyulassy (2011) or Noronha, Gyulassy, and Torrieri (2009). The finding that charged hadron suppression at LHC is a genuine probe of bare light quark suppression, combined with the similarity of bare light and charm quark suppressions, enables a simplified interpretation of experimental data.
