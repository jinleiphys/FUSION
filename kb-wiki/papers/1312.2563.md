# Vortex Rings in a Trap

> We present a simple Hamiltonian description of the dynamics of a quantized vortex ring in a trapped superfluid, compare this description with dynamical simulations, and characterize the dependence of the dynamics of the shape of the trap.

# Vortex Rings in a Trap

## Key claim

The paper presents a corrected Hamiltonian description of quantized vortex ring dynamics in a trapped superfluid, showing that Pitaevskii (2013) omitted a crucial potential energy term describing buoyancy from the density depletion in the vortex core. This missing term is essential for correctly describing the dynamics, particularly for incompressible superfluids where without it the formalism would predict constant velocity and radius regardless of trap shape.

## Method

The authors derive an energy functional for a vortex ring that includes both kinetic energy (from the standard vortex ring relations) and a potential energy term representing the buoyant force from the density depletion in the vortex core, parameterized by a fraction β of the annular tube volume. They apply this to the unitary Fermi gas (UFG) using the Thomas-Fermi approximation for the density profile, and compare the resulting constant-energy contours with full dynamical simulations from Bulgac (2013d) and the earlier model of Pitaevskii (2013).

## Key numbers

- Vortex ring kinetic energy: E_R = (1/2) m n R κ² ln(8R / e a), where κ = πħ/m is the winding and a ~ l_coh is the core size
- Vortex ring linear momentum: P = m n κ π R²
- Vortex ring velocity: v = κ/(4πR) ln(8R/a)
- For the MIT experiment (Yefsah 2013): k_F R ≈ 20, giving L = ln(8R/ea) ≈ 5, and 2ξα²β = O(1)
- The fitted parameter 2ξα²β ≈ 1.3 from comparing contours to simulations
- Oscillation period T ∝ R (up to logarithmic corrections) for fixed R_z, in disagreement with Pitaevskii (2013) who found T ∝ R^{2/3}

## Context

This work corrects and extends a recent preprint by Pitaevskii (2013) that attempted to provide a simple theoretical formalism for vortex ring motion in trapped superfluids. The corrected hydrodynamic theory explains aspects of the MIT experiment (Yefsah 2013) that observed long-lived and slowly moving "heavy solitons," complementing the numerical explanation in Bulgac (2013d) which suggested the experiment sees vortex rings, an idea supported by Cetoli (2013), Reichl (2013), and Wen (2013). The Hamiltonian description augmented with image vortices acts as a vortex filament model for vortex rings in a compressible fluid, enabling quantitative predictions for oscillation periods that depend on trap aspect ratio.
