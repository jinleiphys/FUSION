# Nuclear quantum memory and time sequencing of a single $γ$ photon

> A $γ$-ray-nuclear quantum interface is suggested as a new platform for quantum information processing, motivated by remarkable progresses in $γ$-ray quantum optics. The main advantages of a $γ$ photon over an optical photon lie in its almost perfect detectability and much tighter, potentially sub-angstrom, focusability. Nuclear ensembles hold important advantages over atomic ensembles in a unique combination of high nuclear density in bulk solids with narrow, lifetime-broadening Mössbauer transitions even at room temperature. This may lead to the densest long-lived quantum memories and the smallest size photon processors. Here we propose a technique for $γ$ photon quantum memory through a Doppler frequency comb, produced by a set of resonantly absorbing nuclear targets that move with different velocities. It provides a reliable storage, an on-demand generation, and a time sequencing of a single $γ$ photon. This scheme presents the first $γ$-photon-nuclear-ensemble interface opening a new direction of research in quantum information science.

## Key claim

The paper proposes the first quantum memory and processing interface for single γ photons using a Doppler frequency comb formed by a set of moving Mössbauer nuclear targets, but the achievable storage efficiency is fundamentally limited by the specific regime chosen: the gradient frequency comb (GFC) regime has an upper bound of 54% efficiency, while the stepwise gradient echo memory (SGEM) regime can exceed this bound but remains limited by decoherence and the number of targets.

## Method

The authors model a set of M identical Mössbauer targets (e.g., ⁵⁷Fe-enriched stainless-steel foils) moving at different velocities with equal spacing Δv, creating a Doppler frequency comb in the resonant absorption spectrum. They solve one-dimensional Maxwell-Bloch equations describing the interaction of a single γ-photon wave packet with the nuclear ensemble, analyzing two operational regimes: GFC (where echoes emerge at integer multiples of the rephasing time T₀ = 2π/(βω₀) due to beating between frequency components) and SGEM (where all target velocities are reversed at a chosen switching time T_sw before the first GFC echo, causing phase rewind and producing a controllable echo at time 2T_sw).

## Key numbers

- The 14.4 keV Mössbauer nuclear transition of ⁵⁷Fe has a decay rate Γ/(2π) = 0.55 MHz, with a nuclear density of approximately 10²³ cm⁻³ in bulk solids at room temperature.
- A 100 nm-long stainless-steel film 98% enriched with ⁵⁷Fe would be optically thick for resonant γ photons at room temperature.
- For a 7 ns input photon (intensity FWHM 4.95 ns) with M=5 targets, each with optical thickness ζ⁰ = 41.3 (total ζ = 206.5), velocity spacing Δv = 3.075 mm/s, and finesse ℱ = 32.47, the GFC regime achieves approximately 45% retrieval efficiency after 28 ns storage.
- The upper bound of the first GFC echo efficiency is 54%, achievable when ζ⁰ ≈ (4/π)ℱ ≫ 1 and π/(MΔtΓ) < ℱ < π/(ΔtΓ).
- For SGEM with M=31 targets, each with ζ⁰ = 6.67, total optical thickness 206.7, and switching time T_sw = 21 ns, the storage of a 7 ns photon for 42 ns is limited by 63% efficiency.
- The 93.3 keV transition in ⁶⁷Zn has a coherence time of 13.6 μs; the 12.4 keV transition in ⁴⁵Sc has a lifetime of 0.46 s; the 88.0 keV transition in ¹⁰⁹Ag has a lifetime of 57.1 s.

## Context

This work extends the atomic frequency comb (AFC) quantum memory technique (Afzelius 2009) and gradient echo memory (GEM) technique (Hetet 2008) from the optical to the γ-ray range, where direct transfer of optical techniques such as electromagnetically induced transparency or off-resonant Raman is not possible due to the lack of strong coherent driving fields. The scheme builds on recent experimental demonstrations of single γ-photon waveform shaping via vibrating nuclear resonant absorbers (Vagizov 2014, Shakhmuratov 2015) and opens the possibility for integrated quantum information platforms combining optical-electronic and γ-photon-nuclear functionalities in solid-state systems.
