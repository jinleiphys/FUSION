# Chiral drag force

> We provide a holographic evaluation of novel contributions to the drag force acting on a heavy quark moving through strongly interacting plasma. The new contributions are chiral in that they act in opposite directions in plasmas containing an excess of left- or right-handed quarks and in that they are proportional to the coefficient of the axial anomaly. These new contributions to the drag force act either parallel to or antiparallel to an external magnetic field or to the vorticity of the fluid plasma. In all these respects, these contributions to the drag force felt by a heavy quark are analogous to the chiral magnetic effect on light quarks. However, the new contribution to the drag force is independent of the electric charge of the heavy quark and is the same for heavy quarks and antiquarks. We show that although the chiral drag force can be non-vanishing for heavy quarks that are at rest in the local fluid rest frame, it does vanish for heavy quarks that are at rest in a suitably chosen frame. In this frame, the heavy quark at rest sees counterpropagating momentum and charge currents, both proportional to the axial anomaly coefficient, but feels no drag force. This provides strong concrete evidence for the absence of dissipation in chiral transport, something that has been predicted previously via consideration of symmetries. Along the way to our principal results, we provide a general calculation of the corrections to the drag force due to the presence of gradients in the flowing fluid in the presence of a nonzero chemical potential. We close with a consequence of our result that is at least in principle observable in heavy ion collisions, namely an anticorrelation between the direction of the CME current for light quarks in a given event and the direction of the kick given to the momentum of all the heavy quarks and antiquarks in that event.

## Key claim

The authors use holography to show that a heavy quark moving through a strongly coupled chiral plasma experiences a novel "chiral drag force" that acts parallel or antiparallel to an external magnetic field or fluid vorticity, but this force vanishes when the heavy quark is at rest in a frame where the local entropy current is zero, providing evidence that chiral transport is dissipationless.

## Method

The authors work within the holographic dual of strongly coupled N=4 supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory, representing the heavy quark as a trailing string in an AdS black hole spacetime. They solve the Nambu-Goto action for the string profile order by order in the chemical potential over temperature (μ/T) and to first order in fluid gradients, incorporating the Chern-Simons term in the bulk action to capture the axial anomaly. The drag force is extracted from the string momentum flux at the AdS boundary.

## Key numbers

- The Chern-Simons coupling in the bulk is κ = -1/(4√3) for the dual of N=4 SYM theory
- The drag force at zero chemical potential and zero gradients is f^μ_(0) = (T²√λ)/(12π) (1/γ²)(γw^μ - δ^μ_0)(6π²γ), where λ is the 't Hooft coupling and γ = 1/√(1-v²)
- The chemical potential correction to the drag force in the nonrelativistic limit (γ→1) scales as (1 + 1/3 (μ/(πT))²)
- The chemical potential correction to the drag force in the ultrarelativistic limit (γ→∞) scales as (1 + 1/2 (μ/(πT))²)
- The black hole horizon radius r₊ is related to temperature and chemical potential by r₊/(R_AdS²) = (πT/2)(1 + √(1 + 2μ²/(3π²T²)))

## Context

This work extends the holographic calculation of drag force on heavy quarks from static plasmas (Herzog 2006, Gubser 2006, Casalderrey-Solana 2006) to plasmas with nonzero chemical potential and fluid gradients, building on the gradient expansion of Lekaveckas (2013). The chiral drag force is analogous to the chiral magnetic effect (CME) for light quarks (Kharzeev 2007, Fukushima 2008, Son 2009) but acts on heavy quarks regardless of their electric charge. The result provides direct evidence for the dissipationless nature of chiral transport previously predicted from symmetry considerations, and suggests an anticorrelation between the CME current direction and the momentum kick to heavy quarks in heavy ion collisions.
