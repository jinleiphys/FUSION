# Omega photoproduction

> Photoproduction of omega-meson is analyzed within meson exchange model and Regge model and compared to rho-meson photoproduction. An interplay between two models and uncertainties in data reproduction are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while the Regge model with Pomeron exchange can reproduce high-energy omega photoproduction data by adjusting its free parameters, it cannot simultaneously describe both omega and rho photoproduction with the same parameter set and the experimentally determined gamma-omega and gamma-rho couplings from dileptonic decay widths.

## Method

The authors analyze omega photoproduction using both a meson exchange model (including pi, eta, sigma, and nucleon exchanges) and a Regge model with Pomeron, pion, and f2 exchanges. They compare their calculations to existing experimental data on total and differential cross sections for gamma + p -> omega + p, and also compare the ratio of omega to rho photoproduction cross sections.

## Key numbers

- The experimental ratio of f^2(gamma-omega) to f^2(gamma-rho) coupling constants squared from V -> e+e- decay is 0.088 +/- 0.005, which differs from the SU(3) ratio of 1/9.
- Regge model calculations underestimate experimental data on the total gamma + p -> omega + p cross section by approximately 26% at photon energies E_gamma >= 20 GeV.
- The ratio R of total omega to rho photoproduction cross sections is consistent with R = 1/9 at E_gamma >= 6 GeV.
- The ratio of omega-gamma-pi to rho-gamma-pi coupling constants squared is approximately 5.8, explaining the enhancement of omega photoproduction at E_gamma <= 6 GeV.
- Pomeron exchange calculations use parameters: beta_0 = 2.0 GeV^-1 or 2.35 GeV^-1, and cut-off mu_0 = 1.1 GeV or 2.5 GeV.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of vector meson photoproduction, which had systematically analyzed rho and phi photoproduction but only selectively studied omega photoproduction. The authors build on the Regge model framework of Donnachie and Landshoff (1992) and the meson exchange model of Sibirtsev et al. (2002), and they address the discrepancy that Pomeron exchange calculations underestimate omega photoproduction data at high energies. The results enable further investigation of rho-omega mixing and rho N -> omega N transitions via final state interactions as possible explanations for the missing 26% in omega production rates.
