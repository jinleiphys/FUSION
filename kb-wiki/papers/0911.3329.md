# Ultra-low Q values for neutrino mass measurements

> We investigate weak nuclear decays with extremely small kinetic energy release (Q value) and thus extremely good sensitivity to the absolute neutrino mass scale. In particular, we consider decays into excited daughter states, and we show that partial ionization of the parent atom can help to tune Q values to << 1 keV. We discuss several candidate isotopes undergoing beta+, beta-, bound state beta, or electron capture decay, and come to the conclusion that a neutrino mass measurement using low-Q decays might only be feasible if no ionization is required, and if future improvements in isotope production technology, nuclear mass spectroscopy, and atomic structure calculations are possible. Experiments using ions, however, are extremely challenging due to the large number of ions that must be stored. New precision data on nuclear excitation levels could help to identify further isotopes with low-Q decay modes and possibly less challenging requirements.

## Key claim
Weak nuclear decays with extremely small kinetic energy release (Q values << 1 keV) could provide enhanced sensitivity to the absolute neutrino mass scale, but a competitive neutrino mass measurement using such decays might only be feasible if no ionization of the parent atoms is required, and only if future improvements in isotope production, nuclear mass spectroscopy, and atomic structure calculations are achieved.

## Method
The authors systematically surveyed candidate isotopes undergoing beta-minus, beta-plus, bound state beta, and electron capture decay, focusing on decays into excited nuclear daughter states. They computed achievable Q values as a function of electron configuration using published atomic physics data and relativistic Hartree-Fock calculations, accounting for uncertainties in atomic mass differences (typically known to O(keV)) and electron binding energies. For each candidate, they evaluated whether the required number of stored parent particles could be achieved with current or near-future ion trap and storage ring technology.

## Key numbers
- Tritium decay has a Q value of 18.6 keV, and the Mainz and Troitsk experiments set a limit of m_nu less than or approximately 2 eV, with KATRIN aiming for sensitivity to m_nu less than or approximately 0.2 eV.
- The decay Re-187 to Os-187 offers a Q value of only 2.657 keV but suffers from a small decay rate due to being a unique first forbidden decay.
- For continuum beta decay, candidate isotopes W-188, Os-193, and Ir-194 have achievable Q values between 0 and 1.3 keV depending on the true value of Q_0.
- For bound state beta decay, Dy-163 could provide Q approximately 1.5 keV.
- For electron capture decay, Dy-159 has Q = 130 (+1200, -130) eV and Ho-163 has Q approximately 540 eV, both potentially achievable without ionization.
- Even with Q approximately 100 eV, the relative effect of m_nu = 2 eV (0.2 eV) on the decay rate for bound state beta or electron capture decay is only at the level of 10^-4 (10^-6).
- To detect a nonzero neutrino mass via low-Q decays would require observing few times 10^8 (for m_nu = 2 eV) to 10^12 (for m_nu = 0.2 eV) low-Q decays, requiring approximately 10^16 to 10^20 stored parent particles.
- Current ion traps can store up to 10^6 to 10^8 ions, and storage rings up to 10^9 to 10^11 ions; future FAIR storage rings would provide capacity of 10^12 e.

## Context
This work extends the long-standing effort to measure the absolute neutrino mass via kinematical methods, which has been dominated by tritium beta decay experiments by the Mainz collaboration (2004) and Troitsk collaboration (1995), and the upcoming KATRIN experiment. The authors propose an alternative approach using ultra-low Q value decays, building on earlier proposals for bound state beta decay of tritium by Cohen (1987) and calorimetric measurements of Ho-163 by DeRujula (1982). The paper identifies specific candidate isotopes requiring improved nuclear mass spectroscopy and atomic structure calculations, and concludes that neutral-atom decays of Dy-159 and Ho-163 are the most promising, while experiments using ions face prohibitive storage requirements.
