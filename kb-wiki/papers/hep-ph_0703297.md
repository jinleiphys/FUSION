# Introduction to Chiral Perturbation Theory

> A brief introduction to chiral perturbation theory, the effective field theory of quantum chromodynamics at low energies, is given.

## Key claim

Chiral perturbation theory (ChPT) is the effective field theory of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) at low energies, valid only when the relevant degrees of freedom are hadrons (pions, kaons, eta) rather than quarks and gluons, and when momenta are small compared to the characteristic hadronic scale of roughly 1 GeV.

## Method

The author constructs the chiral effective Lagrangian by requiring it to respect the same symmetries as QCD, particularly chiral SU(3)_L × SU(3)_R symmetry and its spontaneous breakdown to SU(3)_V. The Lagrangian is expanded in powers of derivatives (chiral ordering) and in powers of the light quark mass matrix, which is treated as an external scalar source transforming under chiral rotations. The Goldstone boson fields are represented by a matrix-valued field U = exp(i φ/f) living on the coset space SU(3)_L × SU(3)_R / SU(3)_V, and the leading-order Lagrangian contains two low-energy constants: f (the decay constant in the chiral limit) and B (the order parameter of spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking).

## Key numbers

- Pion mass m_π ≈ 138 MeV, kaon mass m_K ≈ 495 MeV, eta mass m_η ≈ 547 MeV (from the Particle Data Group)
- Pion decay constant f_π = 92.4 MeV, extracted from weak decays such as π⁺ → l⁺ ν_l
- Leading-order low-energy constant f ≈ f_π ≈ 93 MeV
- Leading-order low-energy constant B ≈ 1800 MeV, extracted from the sum-rule value ⟨0|q̄q|0⟩ = −(250 MeV)³
- Characteristic gap separating Goldstone bosons from other hadrons: Δ ∼ M_proton ∼ 1 GeV
- Leading-order ππ scattering amplitude: A(s,t,u) = s / f²

## Context

This paper provides a pedagogical introduction to chiral perturbation theory, extending the foundational work of Weinberg (1979), Gasser and Leutwyler (1984, 1985) who established the systematic framework for ChPT. The author restricts the discussion to the purely mesonic sector (excluding baryons) and emphasizes basic principles such as spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking, Goldstone's theorem, and the construction of the effective Lagrangian. The work enables readers to understand how ChPT provides a model-independent approach to low-energy QCD, complementary to lattice QCD simulations, and how it can be used to extract quantities such as light quark mass ratios from phenomenology.
