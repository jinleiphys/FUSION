# On the Macroscopic Limit of Nuclear Dissipation

> The Landau-Vlasov equation is applied to a slab of width $L$. This geometry is introduced to simulate somehow the finiteness of real nuclei but to allow for analytical solutions, nevertheless. We focus on the damping of low frequency surface modes and discuss their friction coefficient. For this quantity we study the macroscopic limit as defined by $L\to \infty$. We demonstrate that the same result can be obtained for finite $L$ by applying an appropriate frequency smoothing, if only the smearing interval is sufficiently large. The apparent, but important consequences are discussed which this result will have for the understanding of the nature of dissipation in real nuclei.

## Key claim

The macroscopic limit of nuclear dissipation (the wall formula) can be obtained for a finite slab system by applying an appropriate frequency smoothing, provided the smearing interval is sufficiently large, even though the literal macroscopic limit requires letting the system size go to infinity.

## Method

The authors applied the linearized Landau-Vlasov equation to a slab of width L with mirror-reflection boundary conditions at the moving surfaces, treating the particles as a free Fermi gas (Landau parameter F0=0). They calculated the friction coefficient for low-frequency surface modes in the zero-frequency limit by evaluating the intrinsic response function of the system. The macroscopic limit was studied both by taking L→∞ and by applying a Lorentzian frequency averaging to the finite system.

## Key numbers

- The friction coefficient for a thin slab vanishes as γ ∝ (k⊥L/π)⁴ ζ(5) where ζ(5)=1.04 (Riemann zeta function)
- For large systems (L→∞), the friction coefficient recovers the wall formula: γ = γ_wf = (π/4) ρ m v_F (where ρ is the particle density, m the nucleon mass, v_F the Fermi velocity)
- The dimensionless averaging parameter η = γ_av L / (π v_F) must be of order unity to approach the wall formula
- For a spherical nucleus, the traversal frequency v_F/L corresponds to approximately 7-10 MeV when converted to energy
- For k⊥=0, the averaged friction coefficient is always less than or equal to the wall formula, with asymptotic deviations scaling as 1/(3η²) for η≫1

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical approach of Abrosimov and Kolomietz (1990) from semi-infinite Fermi liquids to slab geometry to better simulate finite nuclear systems. The authors demonstrate analytically that frequency smoothing (analogous to the Strutinsky method used for static energies) introduces irreversibility into the dynamics, resolving the puzzle of how one-body dissipation can arise in finite nuclei where the 1p-1h excitation spectrum is not dense. The results suggest that nuclear dissipation cannot be of pure one-body nature, as the required smoothing width of 7-10 MeV implies coupling to more complicated excitations, consistent with the microscopic computations of Hofmann, Yamaji, and Jensen (1992) and Yamaji, Hofmann, and Sauer (1993) which found friction saturating at about half the wall formula value at large temperatures.
