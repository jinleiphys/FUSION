# Davydov-Chaban Hamiltonian with deformation-dependent mass term for γ = 30°

> Motivation : Several theoretical comparisons with experimental data have recently pointed out that the mass tensor of the collective Bohr Hamiltonian cannot be considered as a constant and should be taken as a function of the collective coordinates. Method : The Davydov-Chaban Hamiltonian, describing the collective motion of γ-rigid atomic nuclei, is modified by allowing the mass to depend on the nuclear deformation. Moreover, the eigenvalue problem for this Hamiltonian is solved for Davidson potential and γ = 30° involving an Asymptotic Iteration Method (AIM). The present model is conventionally called Z(4)-DDM-D (Deformation Dependent Mass with Davidson potential), in respect to the so called Z(4) model. Results : Exact analytical expressions are derived for energy spectra and normalized wave functions, for the present model. The obtained results show an overall agreement with the experimental data for 108-116Pd, 128-132Xe, 136;138Ce and 190-198Pt and an important improvement in respect to other models. Prediction of a new candidate nucleus for triaxial symmetry is made. Conclusion : The dependence of the mass on the deformation reduces the increase rate of the moment of inertia with deformation, removing a main drawback of the model and leading to an improved agreement with the corresponding experimental data.

# Davydov-Chaban Hamiltonian with deformation-dependent mass term for γ = 30°

## Key claim

The Davydov-Chaban Hamiltonian for γ-rigid triaxial nuclei, when modified so that the mass parameter depends on the deformation coordinate β (rather than being constant), yields improved agreement with experimental energy spectra and B(E2) transition probabilities for several isotopic chains, provided the deformation-dependent mass parameter a remains small (a << 1) and the Davidson potential is used for the β-vibrational part.

## Method

The authors start from the Davydov-Chaban Hamiltonian for γ-rigid nuclei with γ fixed at 30°, introduce a deformation-dependent mass of the form B(β) = B₀/(1 + aβ²)² following the Von Roos position-dependent mass formalism, and separate the angular and radial variables. The angular equation is solved using the known Meyer-ter-Vehn solution for γ = 30°, while the radial equation with the Davidson potential V(β) = β² + β₀⁴/β² is solved analytically using the Asymptotic Iteration Method (AIM) to obtain closed-form expressions for energy spectra and normalized wave functions in terms of Jacobi polynomials.

## Key numbers

- The deformation-dependent mass parameter a does not exceed 0.2 for any of the studied nuclei, consistent with the small-deformation assumption of the model.
- For the nucleus ¹¹²Pd, the smallest rms deviation σ (defined as σ = √[Σ(E_i(exp)-E_i(th))²/((N-1)E(2_g⁺)²)]) is obtained among all studied isotopes.
- For ¹³⁸Ce, the potential minimum β₀ = 0, giving a pure harmonic behavior; in this case the Z(4) and E(5) symmetries coincide in the ground and β bands.
- For ¹²⁸-¹³²Xe and ¹³⁸Ce, the deformation-dependent mass parameter a = 0, meaning no mass deformation effect is needed for these isotopes.
- The staggering function S(4) for a triaxial rotor with γ = 30° is 1.67, while for the γ-unstable model S(4) lies between -2 and -1.

## Context

This work extends the Z(4) model of Bonatsos (2005), which used an infinite square well potential with constant mass, by adding a deformation-dependent mass and the Davidson potential. It also builds on earlier applications of the Davydov-Chaban Hamiltonian with various potentials (harmonic oscillator, sextic, quartic) by Budaca (2014, 2016) and Buganu (2015), and on the deformation-dependent mass formalism applied to nuclear structure by Quesne (2004) and Chabab (2016). The results improve upon the Z(4)-Sextic model of Buganu (2015) and Budaca (2016), which used a single free parameter with constant mass. The model enables systematic calculation of energy spectra and B(E2) transition probabilities for triaxial nuclei, and predicts ¹¹⁴Pd as a new candidate nucleus for triaxial symmetry, though the authors note that further investigation within the Z(5) symmetry is needed for this nucleus.
