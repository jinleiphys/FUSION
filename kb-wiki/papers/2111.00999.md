# Dissipative superfluid relativistic magnetohydrodynamics of a multicomponent fluid: the combined effect of particle diffusion and vortices

> We formulate dissipative magnetohydrodynamic equations for finite-temperature superfluid and superconducting charged relativistic mixtures, taking into account the effects of particle diffusion and possible presence of Feynman-Onsager and/or Abrikosov vortices in the system. The equations depend on a number of phenomenological transport coefficients, which describe, in particular, relative motions of different particle species and their interaction with vortices. We demonstrate how to relate these transport coefficients to the mutual friction parameters and momentum transfer rates arising in the microscopic theory. The resulting equations can be used to study, in a unified and coherent way, a very wide range of phenomena associated with dynamical processes in neutron stars, e.g., the magnetothermal evolution, stellar oscillations and damping, as well as development and suppression of various hydrodynamic instabilities in neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper formulates dissipative relativistic magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) equations for finite-temperature superfluid and superconducting charged mixtures that include both particle diffusion and the presence of Feynman-Onsager and Abrikosov vortices, extending prior work that either ignored diffusion or neglected superfluidity. The authors note that the first-order theory may have theoretical issues with acausality and stability due to unphysical high-frequency modes, but argue these modes lie outside the hydrodynamic regime and can be discarded in practical applications such as neutron star modeling.

## Method

The authors build a first-order dissipative hydrodynamics starting from conservation laws (continuity equations, energy-momentum conservation, Maxwell equations) and derive the general form of dissipative terms that are linear in thermodynamic fluxes, ensure non-negative entropy production rate, and satisfy Onsager relations. They combine the approach of GD16 (which focused on nondissipative equations with mutual friction but ignored diffusion) with the results of DGS20 (which included diffusion but neglected superfluidity and superconductivity), adding dissipative corrections to particle currents, the energy-momentum tensor, and chemical potentials. The entropy generation equation is derived and used to relate phenomenological transport coefficients to mutual friction parameters and momentum transfer rates from microscopic theory.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star core density is several times greater than nuclear saturation density ρ₀ = 2.8 × 10¹⁴ g cm⁻³
- Magnetic fields in neutron stars may reach values ≳ 10¹⁵ G
- Neutron star radius is ∼10 km, only a few times larger than the Schwarzschild radius
- Baryons in neutron star interiors become superfluid/superconducting at temperatures T ≲ 10⁸ – 10¹⁰ K

## Context

This work extends the relativistic MHD of GD16 (which ignored particle diffusion) and the nonsuperfluid dissipative MHD of DGS20 (which neglected superfluidity and superconductivity), combining both approaches into a unified framework. It also builds on the nonrelativistic smooth-averaged hydrodynamics of Hall and Vinen (1956) and Bekarevich and Khalatnikov (1961), as well as the relativistic formulations of Rau and Wasserman (2020) and Andersson et al. (2012, 2017). The resulting equations can be used to study magnetothermal evolution, stellar oscillations and damping, and hydrodynamic instabilities in neutron stars, particularly addressing the role of particle diffusion in magnetic field evolution in superconducting neutron star cores.
