# Recent progress on light scalars: from confusion to precision using dispersion theory

> In this talk I briefly review the recent developments on light scalar meson spectroscopy, paying particular attention to the causes of major revision of the $σ$ or $f_0(500)$ meson in the Review of Particle Phsycis. This resonance, despite playing a central role in the nucleon-nucleon attraction as well as the QCD chiral symmetry breaking, has suffered a longstanding controversy which has been acknowledged to be finally settled. The combination of new and precise data together with rigorous dispersive approaches has turned the old confusing situation about the properties of these mesons, and even thir existence in some cases, into a field which now aims at precision studies.

## Key claim

The longstanding controversy over the existence and properties of the light scalar mesons, particularly the σ or f₀(500) resonance, has been resolved by combining new precise data with rigorous, model-independent dispersive approaches, leading to a major revision in the 2012 Review of Particle Physics that reduced the mass uncertainty by more than a factor of five, though the author notes the RPP criteria remain conservative and he would rely only on the most rigorous dispersive extractions.

## Method

The author reviews the application of dispersion relations, specifically Roy equations and once-subtracted GKPY equations, to ππ → ππ scattering data to obtain model-independent analytic continuations of partial-wave amplitudes into the complex plane. These dispersive constraints are used to check data consistency and to extract resonance pole positions (mass and width) from the second Riemann sheet, avoiding the unreliable Breit-Wigner parametrization that is inappropriate for very wide resonances like the σ. The Madrid-Krakow collaboration's procedure involved first performing unconstrained fits to data (UFD), then modifying parameters slightly to satisfy forward and partial-wave dispersion relations, yielding constrained fits to data (CFD) that were then continued analytically to locate poles.

* σ/f₀(500) pole from GKPY equations: √s_σ = (457⁺¹⁴₋₁₃) − i(279⁺¹¹₋₇) MeV (Garcia-Martin et al., 2011)
* σ/f₀(500) pole from Roy equations: √s_σ = (445 ± 25) − i(278⁺²²₋₁₈) MeV (Garcia-Martin et al., 2011)
* RPP 2012 "more radical" average of four dispersive analyses: √s_σ = (446 ± 6) − i(276 ± 5) MeV
* RPP 2012 conservative estimate for σ: mass 400 to 550 MeV, width 400 to 700 MeV (reduced from 400–1200 MeV mass range in 2010)
* f₀(980) pole from GKPY equations: √s = (996 ± 7) − i(25⁺¹⁰₋₆) MeV (GKPY, 2011)
* f₀(980) pole from Roy equations: √s = (1003⁺⁵₋₂₇) − i(21⁺¹⁰₋₈) MeV (GKPY, 2011)
* f₀(980) RPP 2012 mass: 990 ± 20 MeV (updated from 980 ± 10 MeV)
* K₀*(800) RPP detailed table mass: 685 ± 29 MeV, width: 547 ± 24 MeV (still listed as "needs confirmation")

## Context

This review extends the dispersive analysis program for ππ scattering that began with Roy equations (Roy, 1971) and was advanced by Caprini, Colangelo, and Leutwyler (2005) using Chiral Perturbation Theory input, and by the Madrid-Krakow collaboration (GKPY, 2011; Garcia-Martin, 2011) using new NA48/2 data from K→ππℓν decays (Batley, 2010) without ChPT input. The work resolves the longstanding "dip" versus "no-dip" controversy for the f₀(980) inelasticity and provides the rigorous foundation that enabled the RPP to reduce the σ uncertainty dramatically. The next steps include applying similar dispersive methods to the K₀*(800) to move it from "needs confirmation" to well-established status, and using models with controlled analytic structure (such as N/D or unitarized approaches) to study the N_c behavior and nature of these states, particularly whether the σ is predominantly a non-qq̅ meson.
