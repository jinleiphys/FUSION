# Effective Field Theory in Nuclear Physics

> I review recent developments in the application of effective field theory to nuclear physics. Emphasis is placed on precision two-body calculations and efforts to formulate the nuclear shell model in terms of an effective field theory.

## Key claim

Effective field theory (EFT) provides a systematic, predictive framework for nuclear physics at low energies, but the abstract glosses over the fact that for momenta at or above the pion mass, no formally consistent, converging perturbative EFT currently exists, Weinberg's power-counting gives numerically small cut-off dependence but is formally inconsistent, while the KSW scheme with perturbative pions appears not to be converging.

## Method

The paper reviews the application of EFT to nuclear systems by constructing Lagrangians with nucleons, pions, and photons as dynamical degrees of freedom, organized by power-counting in small expansion parameters (momentum relative to the chiral symmetry breaking scale or pion mass). Higher-dimension operators encapsulate short-distance physics, and observables are computed order-by-order in the expansion, with counterterms fixed by scattering data or other low-energy inputs. For two-body processes, calculations are performed both in a theory with pions (Weinberg counting) and without pions (pionless EFT), using dimensional regularization or coordinate-space cut-offs.

## Context

This review extends the program of applying EFT to nuclear physics pioneered by Weinberg (1990) and later developed by Kaplan, Savage, and Wise (1998) and Bedaque, Hammer, and van Kolck (1999). It summarizes precision two-body calculations (e.g., \( np \rightarrow d\gamma \) for Big-Bang Nucleosynthesis, weak interactions of the deuteron) and three-body calculations (Nd scattering, the Phillips line). The work enables future high-precision calculations of nuclear observables with controlled theoretical uncertainties, and motivates efforts to formulate the nuclear shell model in EFT terms (Haxton and collaborators, ongoing). Open questions include resolving the convergence issues for momenta near or above the pion mass and extending the framework to many-nucleon systems.
