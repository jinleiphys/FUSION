# Co-production of light and heavy $r$-process elements via fission deposition

> We apply for the first time fission yields determined across the chart of nuclides from the macroscopic-microscopic theory of the Finite Range Liquid Drop Model to simulations of rapid neutron capture ($r$-process) nucleosynthesis. With the fission rates and yields derived within the same theoretical framework utilized for other relevant nuclear data, our results represent an important step toward self-consistent applications of macroscopic-microscopic models in $r$-process calculations. The yields from this model are wide for nuclei with extreme neutron excess. We show that these wide distributions of neutron-rich nuclei, and particularly the asymmetric yields for key species that fission at late times in the $r$ process, can contribute significantly to the abundances of the lighter heavy elements, specifically the light precious metals palladium and silver. Since these asymmetric yields correspondingly also deposit into the lanthanide region, we consider the possible evidence for co-production by comparing our nucleosynthesis results directly with the trends in the elemental ratios of metal-poor stars rich in $r$-process material. We show that for $r$-process enhanced stars palladium over europium and silver over europium display mostly flat trends suggestive of co-production and compare to the lanthanum over europium trend which is often used to justify robustness arguments in the lanthanide region. We find that such robustness arguments may be extendable down to palladium and heavier and demonstrate that fission deposition is a mechanism by which such a universality or robustness can be achieved.

## Key claim

The authors show that fission deposition from neutron-rich nuclei, particularly asymmetric fission yields at late times in the $r$-process, can co-produce light precious metals (palladium and silver) alongside lanthanide elements, potentially explaining the flat trends in [Pd/Eu] and [Ag/Eu] ratios observed in $r$-process enhanced metal-poor stars. The claim is hedged by noting that this mechanism operates specifically in conditions where fissioning nuclei participate during nucleosynthesis, and that the robustness of the pattern depends on the interplay between fission yields and fission rates.

## Method

The authors applied for the first time fission yields from the Finite Range Liquid Drop Model (FRLDM) to $r$-process nucleosynthesis simulations of neutron star merger ejecta, using the PRISM network code. They employed FRLDM fission barriers consistently for both fission rates and yields, and used FRDM2012 masses with experimental data where available, to achieve a self-consistent macroscopic-microscopic treatment of fission. The nucleosynthetic outcomes were compared to elemental ratios from metal-poor, $r$-process enhanced stars (r-I and r-II stars) to test for co-production signatures.

## Key numbers

- FRLDM yields for neutron-rich plutonium-294 show production of $A\sim110$ nuclei almost as likely as $A\sim150$ product nuclei near the symmetric peak, with yield distribution widths $W_{\rm{d}}\sim80$ for the most neutron-rich fissioning species.
- The symmetric factor $S_{\rm{f}} = |A_{max}-A_f/2|$ for cold dynamical ejecta conditions (trajectory 1) shows values near zero at early times (symmetric fission), while typical asymmetric distributions for experimentally probed actinides have $S_{\rm{f}} \sim 20$.
- Late-time fission deposition (at 4.48 seconds) from mostly asymmetric yields introduces product nuclei to the right of the $r$-process path, with distribution widths still spanning $\sim60$ mass numbers.
- The cold dynamical ejecta conditions (trajectory 1) represent very neutron-rich material with electron fraction $Y_e \lesssim 0.05$, while hot accretion disk wind conditions with $Y_e=0.15$ also populate light precious metals via fission.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of fission cycling as a mechanism for $r$-process universality (Beun 2008, Goriely 2011, Goriely 2013, Mendoza-Temis 2015) by applying for the first time fission yields from a macroscopic-microscopic model (FRLDM) that is consistent with the same theoretical framework used for other nuclear data. It directly addresses the long-standing question of whether light precious metals (Ru, Rh, Pd, Ag) are dominantly synthesized by a light element primary process (LEPP) as argued by Montes (2007) and Hansen (2012), and demonstrates that fission deposition from a main $r$-process can contribute to these elements. The results enable future work to test whether the observed flat trends in [Pd/Eu] and [Ag/Eu] ratios across $r$-process enhanced stars can be explained by a single fission-cycling $r$-process site rather than requiring multiple astrophysical sources.
