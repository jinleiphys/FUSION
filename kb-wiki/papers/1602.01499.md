# Reactor antineutrino fluxes - status and challenges

> In this contribution we describe the current understanding of reactor antineutrino fluxes and point out some recent developments. This is not intended to be a complete review of this vast topic but merely a selection of observations and remarks, which despite their incompleteness, will highlight the status and the challenges of this field.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the long-held belief that reactor antineutrino fluxes could be predicted to within 2% uncertainty was mistaken, and that significant unresolved issues remain, including a 6% deficit in the total measured rate (the Reactor Antineutrino Anomaly) and an unexpected excess of neutrinos between 4.8 and 7.3 MeV (the "bump"), whose origin cannot be traced to a particular fissile isotope with current data.

## Method

The paper reviews the two main methods for predicting reactor antineutrino fluxes: the summation method, which uses nuclear databases (ENDF/B-VII.1, JEFF-3.1.1) to sum contributions from individual fission fragments, and the conversion method, which uses integral beta spectra measured at the Institut Laue-Langevin (ILL) by Schreckenbach et al. (1980s) for 235U, 239Pu, and 241Pu, and converts them to neutrino spectra. The author then performs a simulation study using a 5-ton detector at four reactor types (Daya Bay-like PWR, 1/3 MOX PWR, BR2 research reactor, FBTR fast breeder) to assess the ability to identify which fissile isotope produces the observed 5 MeV bump, using chi-squared hypothesis testing over 16,000 simulated experiments.

## Key numbers

- The existing world average of the measured flux is ~6% below the best prediction, known as the Reactor Antineutrino Anomaly (RAA).
- An unexpected excess of neutrinos with energies between 4.8 and 7.3 MeV was observed by Daya Bay, Reno, and Double Chooz.
- The summation method using ENDF/B-VII.1 predicts a bump similar to observations, while JEFF-3.1.1 does not; the databases have uncertainties large relative to the size of the shoulder.
- For a 1 MW_th reactor in a 1 t detector at 10 m standoff for 1 year, the Huber model predicts 4252 events for 235U (mean energy 4.25 MeV), 2796 events for 239Pu (mean energy 4.04 MeV), and 3872 events for 241Pu (mean energy 4.13 MeV).
- The bump observed by RENO makes up 2% of the total yield of events.
- Treating forbidden decays correctly increases the yield in the bump region by somewhat less than 1% of the total yield.
- To account for the entire shoulder from fast fission of 238U, the isotopes dominating the shoulder region would have to be 4 times larger in JEFF-3.1.1 and 2 times larger in ENDF/B-VII.1.
- For a 5-ton detector with 40% efficiency at MOX3, the bump in 238U can be distinguished from 239Pu at 3.7 standard deviations, and from 241Pu at 0.6 standard deviations.
- For the FBTR, the bump in 238U can be distinguished from 239Pu at 3.6 standard deviations, and from 241Pu at 1.1 standard deviations.
- Neutron capture on 99Tc (cross section ~17 barns) produces 100Tc, giving non-linear corrections of up to 1% for PWRs and several percent for naval reactors in the low-energy neutrino flux.

## Context

This paper extends the discussion of the Reactor Antineutrino Anomaly first identified by Mention (2011) and the 3% upward shift in flux predictions by Mueller (2011) and Huber (2011), which required reinterpretation of previous reactor neutrino experiments. It contradicts the prior belief that fluxes could be predicted to 2% based on the ILL beta spectra measurements by Schreckenbach et al. (1980s). The work enables future experimental campaigns using multi-reactor deployments (as proposed by Buck (2015)) to identify the fissile isotope responsible for the 5 MeV bump, and highlights the need for new measurements of integral beta spectra and improved nuclear databases.
