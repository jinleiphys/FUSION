# Hybrid method to resolve the neutrino mass hierarchy by supernova (anti)neutrino induced reactions

> We introduce a hybrid method to determine the neutrino mass hierarchy by simultaneous measurements of responses of at least two detectors to antineutrino and neutrino fluxes from accretion and cooling phases of core-collapse supernovae. The (anti)neutrino-nucleus cross sections for $^{56}$Fe and $^{208}$Pb are calculated in the framework of the relativistic nuclear energy density functional and weak interaction Hamiltonian, while the cross sections for inelastic scattering on free protons $\mathrm{p}(\barν_\mathrm{e},\mathrm{e}^{+})\mathrm{n}$ are obtained using heavy-baryon chiral perturbation theory. The modelling of (anti)neutrino fluxes emitted from a protoneutron star in a core-collapse supernova include collective and Mikheyev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein effects inside the exploding star. The particle emission rates from the elementary decay modes of the daughter nuclei are calculated for normal and inverted neutrino mass hierarchy. It is shown that simultaneous use of (anti)neutrino detectors with different target material allows to determine the neutrino mass hierarchy from the ratios of $ν_\mathrm{e}$- and $\barν_\mathrm{e}$-induced particle emissions. This hybrid method favors neutrinos from the supernova cooling phase and the implementation of detectors with heavier target nuclei ($^{208}$Pb) for the neutrino sector, while for antineutrinos the use of free protons in mineral oil or water is the appropriate choice.

## Key claim

The hybrid method can determine the neutrino mass hierarchy by simultaneously measuring ratios of neutrino- and antineutrino-induced particle emissions from at least two detectors with different target materials, but the method is almost independent of the supernova-Earth distance and relies on neutrinos from the cooling phase being more favorable than from the accretion phase.

## Method

The authors calculated charged-current (anti)neutrino-nucleus cross sections for ⁵⁶Fe and ²⁰⁸Pb using the relativistic nuclear energy density functional and weak interaction Hamiltonian, while cross sections for inelastic scattering on free protons p(ν̄ₑ, e⁺)n were obtained from heavy-baryon chiral perturbation theory. Supernova (anti)neutrino fluxes were modeled including collective neutrino-neutrino oscillations and Mikheyev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein matter effects, and particle emission rates (γ, one-neutron, two-neutron, one-proton, α) from daughter nuclei were calculated for both normal and inverted mass hierarchy using the Hauser-Feshbach code SMARAGD. The hybrid method defines hierarchy-dependent ratios of event numbers from a neutrino-sensitive detector (using ²⁰⁸Pb) and an antineutrino-sensitive detector (using free protons in mineral oil or water), separately for the accretion and cooling phases.

## Key numbers

- Average energies during accretion phase: ⟨E_{νₑ}⟩ ≈ 8–12 MeV, with electron species luminosity roughly a factor of two larger than non-electron species
- Cooling phase duration: ≈ 10 s (Kelvin-Helmholtz cooling of proto-neutron star)
- Neutrino-nucleus cross sections calculated for energies E_{νₑ(ν̄ₑ)} ≲ 30 MeV
- Neutral current contributions in neutron emission channel are less than ≈ 10% in heavy nuclei
- Emission thresholds for particle channels vary from a few hundred keV to ≈ 10 MeV
- Significant contributions to total particle emissions come from excitation energies E_i^{excit} ≲ 25 MeV, with separation energies of order 10 MeV
- Supernova occurrence rate: ≈ 1–3 events per century

## Context

This work extends previous studies of neutrino-nucleus cross sections using the relativistic nuclear energy density functional (Paa 2008, Paa 2011, Paa 2013) and earlier calculations of neutrino-induced particle emissions for lead and iron detectors (Kol 2001) and lead-based supernova-neutrino detector event rates (Eng 2003). The hybrid method improves upon traditional supernova analyses based mostly on antineutrino events by simultaneously detecting complementary νₑ and ν̄ₑ signals in at least two different detectors, providing additional constraints on the neutrino mass hierarchy. The approach enables future analyses of galactic supernova signals using existing detectors like HALO (sensitive to neutrinos via ²⁰⁸Pb) combined with water or mineral oil detectors sensitive to antineutrinos.
