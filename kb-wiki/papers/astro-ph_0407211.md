# Discovering Tau and Muon Solar Neutrino Flares above backgrounds

> Solar neutrino flares astronomy is at the edge of its discover. High energy flare particles (protons, alpha) whose self scattering within the solar corona is source of a rich prompt charged pions are also source of sharp solar neutrino "burst" (at tens-hundred MeV) produced by their pion-muon primary decay in flight. This brief (minute) solar neutrino "burst" at largest peak overcome by four-five order of magnitude the steady atmospheric neutrino noise at the Earth. Later on, solar flare particles hitting the terrestrial atmosphere may marginally increase the atmospheric neutrino flux without relevant consequences. Largest prompt "burst" solar neutrino flare may be detected in present or better in future largest neutrino underground neutrino detectors. Our estimate for the recent and exceptional October - November 2003 solar flares gives a number of events above or just near unity for Super-Kamiokande. The neutrino spectra may reflect in a subtle way the neutrino flavour mixing in flight. A surprising tau appearance may even occur for a hard ({E}_{nu}_{mu}--> {E}_{nu}_{tau} > 4 GeV) flare spectra. A comparison of the solar neutrino flare (at their birth place on Sun and after oscillation on the arrival on the Earth) with other neutrino foreground is here described and it offer an independent road map to disentangle the neutrino flavour puzzles and its secret flavour mixing angles .

## Key claim

Solar neutrino flares from high-energy flare particles (protons, alpha) can produce a brief (minute-long) neutrino burst that overcomes the steady atmospheric neutrino background by four to five orders of magnitude at peak, but the estimated number of detectable events for the exceptional October-November 2003 solar flares is only "above or just near unity" for Super-Kamiokande, making detection marginal rather than guaranteed.

## Method

The authors model the production of charged pions via proton-proton and proton-alpha inelastic scattering in the solar corona, using the known cross-section (σ_pp ≈ 4×10⁻²⁶ cm² for E_p > 2 GeV) and the solar atmospheric density profile (n_⊙ = N₀ e^{-h/h₀} with N₀ = 2.26×10¹⁷ cm⁻³, h₀ = 1.16×10⁷ cm). They compute the neutrino flux from pion and muon decay chains (π⁺ → μ⁺ + ν_μ, μ⁺ → e⁺ + ν_e + ν_μ) for both upward (escaping) and downward (solar-interior-directed) flare geometries, then apply neutrino flavor mixing over the Earth-Sun distance to predict the observable event rates in Super-Kamiokande II.

## Key numbers

- Inelastic proton-proton cross section: σ_pp(E > 2 GeV) ≈ 4×10⁻²⁶ cm²
- Solar atmospheric scale height: h₀ = 1.16×10⁷ cm; photosphere number density: N₀ = 2.26×10¹⁷ cm⁻³
- Upward scattering probability for energetic protons: P_up ≈ 0.1; downward probability: P_down ≈ 1
- Ratio of pion energy to proton energy in Δ⁺⁺ resonance: R_πp = 0.276
- Total pion flare energy from upward protons: E_πFL = 2.76×10⁻² E_FL (for flare energy E_FL)
- Mono-energetic neutrino energy from pion decay at rest: E_ν_μ = 29.8 MeV; muon energy: E_μ = 109.8 MeV
- Anti-electron neutrino number flux at sea level: N_ν̄_e ≈ 5.7×10⁴ (E_FL/10³¹ erg) (⟨E_ν̄_e⟩/100 MeV)⁻¹ cm⁻²
- Expected neutrino events in Super-Kamiokande II for down-going flare: N_ev ≈ 7.5 η (E_FL/10³¹ erg) where η ≤ 1
- Atmospheric neutrino background rate at Super-Kamiokande: 5.8 events per day, corresponding to Γ ≈ 6.7×10⁻⁵ s⁻¹
- Probability of finding one background neutrino within a 100-second time window: P ≈ 6.7×10⁻³
- Tau appearance threshold: E_ν_μ ≥ 3.46 GeV, requiring hard flare spectra with E_ν_μ → E_ν_τ ≈ 4 GeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier proposals (Fargion 2002, Fargion 2004) for detecting solar neutrino flares and compares them to known neutrino backgrounds including thermal solar neutrinos, supernova relic neutrinos, and atmospheric neutrinos (ref7). The paper suggests that existing Super-Kamiokande data from the October-November 2003 solar flares (specifically the X28 flare on November 4 at 19:50 UT) should be re-examined for correlated neutrino events, and that future larger detectors such as UNO could serve as both solar neutrino flare observatories and early warning systems for coronal mass ejections.
