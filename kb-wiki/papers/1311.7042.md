# Bimodality Phenomenon in Finite and Infinite Systems Within an Exactly Solvable Statistical Model

> We present a few explicit counterexamples to the widely spread belief about an exclusive role of the bimodal nuclear fragment size distributions as the first order phase transition signal. In thermodynamic limit the bimodality may appear at the supercritical temperatures due to the negative values of the surface tension coefficient. Such a result is found within a novel exactly solvable formulation of the simplified statistical multifragmentation model based on the virial expansion for a system of the nuclear fragments of all sizes. The developed statistical model corresponds to the compressible nuclear liquid with the tricritical endpoint located at one third of the normal nuclear density. Its exact solution for finite volumes demonstrates the bimodal fragment size distribution right inside the finite volume analog of a gaseous phase. These counterexamples clearly demonstrate the pitfalls of Hill approach to phase transitions in finite systems.

## Key claim
The paper provides explicit counterexamples showing that bimodal nuclear fragment size distributions can appear without a first-order phase transition, contradicting the widely held belief that bimodality is a robust signal of such a transition. In the thermodynamic limit, bimodality can occur at supercritical temperatures due to negative surface tension, and in finite systems it can appear inside the finite-volume analog of a gaseous phase.

## Method
The authors developed an exactly solvable formulation of the constrained statistical multifragmentation model (CSMM) based on a virial expansion for nuclear fragments of all sizes. They introduced a compressible nuclear liquid equation of state with a tricritical endpoint at one third of normal nuclear density, and a temperature-dependent surface tension that becomes negative above the critical temperature. The model was solved analytically in the thermodynamic limit and for finite volumes, allowing exact calculation of fragment size distributions.

## Key numbers
- Normal nuclear density: ρ₀ ≈ 0.17 fm⁻³ (taken at T=0 and zero pressure)
- Nucleon mass: m ≈ 940 MeV
- Nucleon degeneracy factor: z₁ = 4
- Binding energy parameter: W₀ = 16 MeV
- Critical temperature (tricritical endpoint): T_cep = 18 MeV
- Surface tension at zero temperature: σ₀ = 18 MeV
- For ν=2 case: a₂ ≈ 1.233 × 10⁻² MeV⁻¹
- For ν=4 case: a₄ ≈ 4.099 × 10⁻⁷ MeV⁻³
- Fisher topological exponent used: τ = 1.9
- Surface tension exponent: ζ = const ≥ 1
- Maximal fragment sizes studied: K(V) = 20 and K(V) = 100 nucleons
- Bimodal distributions shown for infinite system at supercritical temperature T = 21 MeV
- Bimodal distributions shown for finite system analog of gaseous phase at T = 13 MeV

## Context
This work extends the constrained statistical multifragmentation model (CSMM) previously developed by Bugaev (2005) and Sagun and Ivanytskyi (2013), and directly contradicts the approach of Chomaz (2003) and Gulminelli (2007) who considered bimodal distributions as a robust signal of phase transitions in finite systems. The paper demonstrates pitfalls in the Hill approach to phase transitions in finite systems, and enables a more careful interpretation of fragment size distributions in heavy-ion collision experiments.
