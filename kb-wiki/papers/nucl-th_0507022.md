# Can realistic nuclear interactions tolerate a resonant tetraneutron ?

> The possible existence of four-neutron resonances close to the physical energy region is explored. Faddeev-Yakubovsky equations have been solved in configuration space using realistic nucleon-nucleon interaction models. Complex Scaling and Analytical Continuation in the Coupling Constant methods were used to follow the resonance pole trajectories, which emerge out of artificially bound tetraneutron states. The final pole positions for four-neutron states lie in the third energy quadrant with negative real energy parts and should thus not be physically observable.

## Key claim

The authors conclude that realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions cannot produce a physically observable tetraneutron resonance; all calculated resonance poles lie in the third energy quadrant (negative real energy, negative imaginary energy) and are therefore subthreshold and unobservable, with an estimated accuracy of 50% for the final pole positions.

## Method

The four-neutron system was solved using Faddeev-Yakubovsky equations in configuration space with the realistic Reid 93 nucleon-nucleon potential. An attractive phenomenological four-neutron force was added to artificially bind the tetraneutron, and the resonance pole trajectories were traced as this force was reduced to zero using two complementary methods: Complex Scaling (CS) and Analytical Continuation in the Coupling Constant (ACCC). The calculations were performed for states with quantum numbers \(J^{\pi}=0^{\pm},1^{\pm},2^{\pm}\).

## Key numbers

- Critical strengths \(W_0\) required to bind tetraneutron with a 4N force of range \(\rho_0=2.5\) fm: 38.70 MeV\(\cdot\)fm\(^{-1}\) for \(J^{\pi}=0^{-}\), 38.67 for \(1^{-}\), 38.68 for \(2^{-}\), 22.90 for \(0^{+}\), 22.92 for \(1^{+}\), and 40.38 for \(2^{+}\).
- Physical resonance positions at \(W=0\) for negative parity states: \(E_{res} = -1.0-9.9i\) MeV (\(0^{-}\)), \(-1.1-9.8i\) MeV (\(1^{-}\)), \(-1.4-9.7i\) MeV (\(2^{-}\)).
- Physical resonance positions at \(W=0\) for positive parity states: \(E_{res} = -1.1-6.3i\) MeV (\(0^{+}\)), \(-1.1-6.5i\) MeV (\(1^{+}\)), \(-1.4-10.9i\) MeV (\(2^{+}\)).
- For \(J^{\pi}=2^{-}\) with \(\rho_0=2.0\) fm, the final resonance position was \(E_{res} = -2.5-12.5i\) MeV, compared to \(-1.4-9.7i\) MeV for \(\rho_0=2.5\) fm.
- Enhancing nn P-waves by a factor \(\gamma=1.2\) for the \(2^{+}\) state changed the critical strength from 40.38 to 40.02 MeV\(\cdot\)fm\(^{-1}\).
- The imaginary energy \(\Gamma = 2\,\text{Im}(-E_{res})\) for all states is approximately 20 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study of three-neutron resonances (Lazauskas and Carbonell, 2005), which found no physically observable three-neutron resonances with realistic interactions. It contradicts earlier experimental claims of a bound tetraneutron from the \(^{14}\)Be break-up reaction (Marques, 2002) and later suggestions of a near-threshold resonance (Marques, 2004). The results are in qualitative agreement with the earlier rigorous study by Sofianos et al. using the simplistic MT I-III S-wave interaction, which also found only subthreshold structures. The paper enables future work to focus on the fact that any realistic multineutron force would need to be extremely strong and short-ranged to produce a physically observable tetraneutron resonance, making such a scenario highly unlikely.
