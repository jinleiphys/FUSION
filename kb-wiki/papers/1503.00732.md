# Quantum field theory in a magnetic field: From quantum chromodynamics to graphene and Dirac semimetals

> A range of quantum field theoretical phenomena driven by external magnetic fields and their applications in relativistic systems and quasirelativistic condensed matter ones, such as graphene and Dirac/Weyl semimetals, are reviewed. We start by introducing the underlying physics of the magnetic catalysis. The dimensional reduction of the low-energy dynamics of relativistic fermions in an external magnetic field is explained and its role in catalyzing spontaneous symmetry breaking is emphasized. The general theoretical consideration is supplemented by the analysis of the magnetic catalysis in quantum electrodynamics, chromodynamics and quasirelativistic models relevant for condensed matter physics. By generalizing the ideas of the magnetic catalysis to the case of nonzero density and temperature, we argue that other interesting phenomena take place. The chiral magnetic and chiral separation effects are perhaps the most interesting among them. In addition to the general discussion of the physics underlying chiral magnetic and separation effects, we also review their possible phenomenological implications in heavy-ion collisions and compact stars. We also discuss the application of the magnetic catalysis ideas for the description of the quantum Hall effect in monolayer and bilayer graphene, and conclude that the generalized magnetic catalysis, including both the magnetic catalysis condensates and the quantum Hall ferromagnetic ones, lies at the basis of this phenomenon. We also consider how an external magnetic field affects the underlying physics in a class of three-dimensional quasirelativistic condensed matter systems, Dirac semimetals. While at sufficiently low temperatures and zero density of charge carriers, such semimetals are expected to reveal the regime of the magnetic catalysis, the regime of Weyl semimetals with chiral asymmetry is realized at nonzero density...

## Key claim

This review argues that an external magnetic field acts as a universal catalyst for spontaneous symmetry breaking in relativistic and quasirelativistic systems, with the effect being model-independent and occurring even at the weakest attractive interaction between fermions and antifermions, in contrast to the symmetry-restoring role of magnetic fields in conventional superconductivity.

## Method

The authors review analytic quantum field theoretical methods applied to the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, quantum electrodynamics (QED), and quantum chromodynamics (QCD) in external magnetic fields, using the 1/N expansion and Bethe-Salpeter equations to analyze dynamical mass generation. They also examine the dimensional reduction of fermion dynamics to the lowest Landau level (LLL) as the underlying mechanism, and compare theoretical predictions with lattice simulations and experimental data from graphene and Dirac semimetals.

- The density of states per unit area in each Landau level is |eB|/(2π²) in 2+1 dimensions.
- The energy spectrum of relativistic fermions in a magnetic field in 2+1 dimensions is given by E₀ = m for the lowest Landau level and Eₙ = ±√(m² + 2|eB|n) for n = 1,2,...
- The magnetic length is l = 1/√|eB|, which sets the scale for the localization of fermion wave functions perpendicular to the field.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on magnetic catalysis by Gusynin, Miransky, and Shovkovy (1994-1995) that established the dimensional reduction mechanism (D → D-2) in the infrared dynamics of fermion pairing. It synthesizes results from earlier NJL model studies by Klevansky (1989) and Suganuma (1990), and connects the magnetic catalysis phenomenon to the quantum Hall effect in graphene (Gorbar et al., 2007-2011) and to chiral magnetic effects in heavy-ion collisions (Kharzeev, 2007-2008). The work enables future experimental searches for magnetic catalysis signatures in Dirac and Weyl semimetals, and provides a unified framework for understanding symmetry breaking across relativistic and condensed matter systems.
