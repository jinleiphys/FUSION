# Dense hadronic matter in neutron stars

> The existence of stars with masses up to $2 M_{\odot}$ and the hints of the existence of stars with radii smaller than $\sim 11$ km seem to require, at the same time, a stiff and a soft hadronic equation of state at large densities. We argue that these two apparently contradicting constraints are actually an indication of the existence of two families of compact stars: hadronic stars which could be very compact and quark stars which could be very massive. In this respect, a crucial role is played, in the hadronic equation of state, by the delta isobars whose early appearance shifts to large densities the formation of hyperons. We also discuss how recent experimental information on the symmetry energy of nuclear matter at saturation indicate, indirectly, an early appearance of delta isobars in neutron star matter.

# Dense hadronic matter in neutron stars

## Key claim
The apparent contradiction between the need for both a stiff and a soft equation of state to simultaneously explain massive neutron stars (up to 2 solar masses) and very compact stars (radii smaller than about 11 km) can be resolved by positing two distinct families of compact stars: hadronic stars that are very compact and quark stars that are very massive, provided that delta isobars appear early in the hadronic equation of state to delay hyperon formation.

## Method
The authors adopt a Walecka-type relativistic mean field model for the hadronic equation of state, using the SFHo parametrization (Steiner 2012) which includes additional non-linear terms in the vector meson sector to constrain the symmetry energy at saturation. They compute the thresholds for delta isobars and hyperons under different coupling assumptions, particularly varying the delta-sigma meson coupling ratio x_{Delta sigma} between 1 and 1.15, and compare results with the older GM3 model (Glendenning 1991). Mass-radius relations are then calculated for pure nucleonic stars, hadronic stars with delta isobars only, hadronic stars with both hyperons and delta isobars, and pure quark stars.

## Key numbers
- Maximum masses of hadronic stars reach up to about 1.5-1.6 solar masses, while quark stars can reach masses up to 2.75 solar masses from perturbative QCD calculations (Kurkela 2009).
- In the SFHo model, delta isobars appear at a baryon density slightly below 0.4 fm^{-3}, while in the GM3 model they appear slightly above 0.5 fm^{-3}.
- The symmetry energy slope parameter L is about 81 MeV in the GM3 model, compared to about 45 MeV in the SFHo model, the latter falling within the window of 40 to 60 MeV from combined laboratory and astrophysical constraints (Lattimer 2012).
- For x_{Delta rho}=0 in the GM3 model, the threshold density for delta isobars is reduced by about 0.3 fm^{-3} relative to the x_{Delta rho}=1 case; in the SFHo model the reduction is only about 0.1 fm^{-3}.

## Context
This work extends the two-family scenario proposed by Drago (2013) by incorporating new constraints on the symmetry energy from Lattimer (2012) and using the SFHo equation of state from Steiner (2012). It contrasts with the earlier GM3 model of Glendenning (1991) where delta isobars appear after hyperons and at densities too high to be reached in compact stars. The scenario enables a resolution of the tension between the 2-solar-mass neutron star discoveries (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013) and the small-radius hints from quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries (Guillot 2013), and predicts that quark stars should show anomalous cooling histories, distinct spin frequency distributions, and that neutron star merger remnants should be quark stars before collapsing to black holes.
