# Theory of QED radiative corrections to neutrino scattering at accelerator energies

> Control over quantum electrodynamics (QED) radiative corrections is critical for precise determination of neutrino oscillation probabilities from observed (anti)neutrino detection rates. It is particularly important to understand any difference between such corrections for different flavors of (anti)neutrinos in charged-current interactions. We provide theoretical foundations for calculating these corrections. Using effective field theory, the corrections are shown to factorize into soft, collinear, and hard functions. The soft and collinear functions contain large logarithms in perturbation theory but are computable from QED. The hard function parametrizes hadronic structure but is free from large logarithms. Using a simple model for the hard function, we investigate the numerical impact of QED corrections in charged-current (anti)neutrino-nucleon elastic cross sections and cross-section ratios at GeV energies. We consider the implications of mass singularity theorems that govern the lepton-mass dependence of cross sections for sufficiently inclusive observables and demonstrate the cancellation of leading hadronic and nuclear corrections in phenomenologically relevant observables.

## Key claim

The paper provides a theoretical framework for computing QED radiative corrections to charged-current (anti)neutrino-nucleon scattering at GeV energies, showing that these corrections factorize into soft, collinear, and hard functions. The key hedge is that the hard function, which parametrizes hadronic structure, is nonperturbative and cannot be computed from QED alone, though it is free from large logarithms and cancels in flavor ratios of cross sections for electron versus muon (anti)neutrinos.

## Method

Using soft-collinear effective theory (SCET), the authors decompose the full QED radiative corrections into contributions from soft, collinear, and hard momentum regions, with the hard scale set by the nucleon mass and neutrino energy (~1 GeV). They compute the soft and collinear functions perturbatively in QED, resumming large logarithms of the form ln(E_nu/m_ell), while the hard function is parametrized using phenomenological form factors. The formalism is first demonstrated in the static limit (m_ell << E_nu << M) where the entire calculation is perturbative, then generalized beyond the static limit to realistic kinematics where E_nu ~ M.

## Key numbers

- Soft-photon cutoff parameter Delta E is between 10 and tens of MeV for experiments using polystyrene scintillator, water, or liquid argon detectors, based on Compton scattering dominance ranges (20 keV to 30 MeV for polystyrene/water, 150 keV to 12 MeV for liquid argon)
- Collinear cone size Delta theta for electrons/positrons: 9 degrees for polystyrene scintillator, 10 degrees for water, and 16 degrees for liquid argon at 500 MeV e+/- energy; factor ~2 lower at 3 GeV
- Collinear cone size Delta theta for muons: approximately 2 degrees for few hundred MeV kinetic energies in T2K scintillator, dropping to approximately 1.5 degrees at few GeV energies
- In the static limit, the inclusive cross section correction delta^tot = (3/4) ln(mu^2 / (4 E_nu^2)) + 17/4 + pi^2/3, independent of lepton flavor in the limit m_ell/E_nu << 1

## Context

This work extends the theoretical treatment of QED radiative corrections for neutrino scattering beyond earlier calculations such as De Rujula (1979) and Day (2012), providing a systematic effective field theory framework that separates perturbative QED corrections from nonperturbative hadronic physics. The factorization approach enables resummation of large logarithms that appear in flavor-dependent corrections, which is critical for precise neutrino oscillation analyses at experiments like T2K, NOvA, DUNE, and Hyper-Kamiokande. The paper demonstrates that leading hadronic and nuclear corrections cancel in phenomenologically relevant cross-section ratios for different lepton flavors, enabling future work to incorporate these results into nuclear cross-section models for oscillation measurements.
