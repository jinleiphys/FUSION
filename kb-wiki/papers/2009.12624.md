# Study of Cluster Structures in Nuclei through the Ratio Method. A Tribute to Mahir Hussein

> For one-neutron halo nuclei, the cross section for elastic scattering and breakup at intermediate energy exhibit similar angular dependences. The Recoil Excitation and Breakup (REB) model of reactions elegantly explains this feature. It also leads to the idea of a new reaction observable to study the structure of loosely-bound nuclear systems: the Ratio. This observable consists of the ratio of angular distributions for different reaction channels, viz. elastic scattering and breakup, which cancels most of the dependence on the reaction mechanism; in particular it is insensitive to the choice of optical potentials that simulate the projectile-target interaction. This new observable is very sensitive to the structure of the projectile. In this article, we review the Ratio Method and its extension to low beam energies and proton-halo nuclei.

## Key claim

The Ratio Method, which takes the ratio of breakup to summed (elastic plus inelastic) angular distributions, produces a reaction observable that is nearly independent of the reaction mechanism and optical potential choices, and is therefore highly sensitive to the projectile's halo structure, provided the collision occurs under conditions where the Recoil Excitation and Breakup (REB) model's approximations (adiabatic treatment and negligible valence-nucleon–target interaction) are reasonably valid.

## Method

The authors performed Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA) calculations for collisions of the one-neutron halo nucleus \(^{11}\)Be on \(^{12}\)C and \(^{208}\)Pb at intermediate energies (67–69 MeV/nucleon), and Continuum-Discretised Coupled Channel (CDCC) calculations for \(^{11}\)Be on \(^{12}\)C at 20 MeV/nucleon. They computed the summed cross section (elastic plus inelastic plus breakup) and the breakup angular distribution, then took their ratio \(\mathcal{R}_{\rm sum}\) and compared it to the REB-predicted form factor \(|F_{E,0}(\vec{Q})|^2\). Additional DEA calculations were performed for the one-proton halo nucleus \(^{8}\)B on the same targets at 44 MeV/nucleon. In all cases, calculations were repeated with the valence-nucleon–target interaction switched off and with alternative core–target optical potentials to test the method's robustness.

## Key numbers

* For \(^{11}\)Be on \(^{12}\)C at 67 MeV/nucleon and on \(^{208}\)Pb at 69 MeV/nucleon, the ratio \(\mathcal{R}_{\rm sum}\) obtained from DEA calculations agrees well with the REB form factor, with only small remnant oscillations; the ratio on the two different targets is nearly identical when plotted as a function of momentum transfer \(Q\).
* For \(^{11}\)Be on \(^{12}\)C at 20 MeV/nucleon, the CDCC ratio agrees excellently with the REB prediction; calculations using an alternative \(^{10}\)Be–\(^{12}\)C optical potential produce individual cross sections differing by up to two orders of magnitude at large angles, yet their ratio is nearly superimposed with the original calculation.
* For \(^{11}\)Be on \(^{208}\)Pb at 20 MeV/nucleon, the DEA ratio shows poorer agreement with the REB prediction: at forward angles the REB overestimates the dynamical result, and at larger angles the REB overestimates due to the valence-neutron–target interaction.
* For \(^{8}\)B on \(^{12}\)C at 44 MeV/nucleon, the DEA ratio is in good agreement with the REB prediction; on \(^{208}\)Pb at the same energy, the agreement is less satisfactory.

## Context

This work extends the earlier finding by Capel, Hussein, and collaborators (2010) that elastic-scattering and breakup angular distributions for one-neutron halo nuclei exhibit similar angular dependences. The Ratio Method was first proposed by Capel, Johnson, and Nunes (2011) and tested in detail by the same authors (2013). The present review summarises those tests and subsequent extensions to lower beam energies (Capel, Canto, and Nunes, 2016) and to proton-halo nuclei (Yang, Capel, and Pang, 2019). The method avoids the traditional subtraction technique for removing nuclear contributions from Coulomb breakup data, which was criticised by Hussein and collaborators (2006). It enables direct extraction of projectile structure information (e.g., separation energy and orbital angular momentum of the halo nucleon) without relying on poorly constrained optical potentials, and could be applied to other loosely-bound systems beyond single-nucleon halos.
