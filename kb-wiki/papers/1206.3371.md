# Particlization in hybrid models

> In hybrid models, which combine hydrodynamical and transport approaches to describe different stages of heavy-ion collisions, conversion of fluid to individual particles, particlization, is a non-trivial technical problem. We describe in detail how to find the particlization hypersurface in a 3+1 dimensional model, and how to sample the particle distributions evaluated using the Cooper-Frye procedure to create an ensemble of particles as an initial state for the transport stage. We also discuss the role and magnitude of the negative contributions in the Cooper-Frye procedure.

## Key claim

The paper details technical aspects of particlization in hybrid models, emphasizing that the choice of switching criterion from fluid dynamics to transport is an equally free parameter as the freeze-out temperature in conventional hydrodynamical models, and that final results are somewhat sensitive to this switching criterion.

## Method

The authors describe a "disordered lines" algorithm for finding the particlization hypersurface in 3+1 dimensions by reducing the four-dimensional problem into a series of three-dimensional problems, using linear interpolation to locate isosurfaces on a discrete grid. They then sample particle distributions evaluated via the Cooper-Frye procedure using Monte Carlo methods, employing two sampling algorithms ("allcells" and "mode") to generate ensembles of particles as initial states for the hadronic transport stage (UrQMD). The negative contributions in the Cooper-Frye procedure are handled by ignoring surface elements where flow is directed inward and using the net number of particles passing outward surface elements with a thermal distribution modified by a theta-function.

## Key numbers

- In central Au+Au collisions at the highest RHIC energy (√s_NN = 200 GeV), there are roughly 10^7 hypersurface elements but only approximately 10,000 particles produced.
- For pions, the Bose distribution expansion is summed over k=1 to 10 terms, at which point it has sufficiently converged.
- The sampling procedure uses a threshold of N < 0.01 to interpret the particle number directly as a sampling probability; for larger N, a Poisson distribution with N as the mean value is sampled.
- Calculations are performed for E_lab = 160A GeV Pb+Pb (SPS) and √s_NN = 200 GeV Au+Au (RHIC) collisions.

## Context

This work extends the development of hybrid models that combine hydrodynamical and transport approaches, as previously implemented by Bass (2000), Teaney (2001), Hirano (2005), Nonaka (2006), Petersen (2008), Werner (2010), Song (2010), Karpenko (2012), and Hirano (2012). The paper addresses technical challenges in particlization that arise when moving from the simplified isochronous switching surfaces used in earlier work (Petersen 2008) to more realistic 3+1 dimensional hypersurfaces. The algorithm described enables event-by-event hydrodynamical calculations with irregular initial conditions, and the authors note that their approach differs from the projection method of Pang (2012) and the VESTA/STEVE algorithms of Schlei (2010), which are faster and produce higher quality surfaces.
