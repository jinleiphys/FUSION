# Mechanisms of direct reactions with halo nuclei

> Halo nuclei are exotic nuclei which exhibit a strongly clusterised structure: they can be seen as one or two valence nucleons loosely bound to a core. Being observed at the ridge of the valley of stability, halo nuclei are studied mostly through reactions. In this contribution the reaction models most commonly used to analyse experimental data are reviewed and compared to one another. A reaction observable built on the ratio of two angular distributions is then presented. This ratio enables removing most of the sensitivity to the reaction mechanism, which emphasises the effects of nuclear structure on the reaction.

## Key claim
The ratio of angular distributions for quasi-elastic scattering and breakup is nearly independent of the reaction mechanism and depends primarily on projectile structure, as predicted by the Recoil Excitation and Breakup (REB) model, but this claim is tested only within the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA) for one-neutron halo nuclei and has not yet been validated experimentally.

## Method
The authors compare three reaction models for halo-nucleus breakup: the Continuum Discretised Coupled Channel (CDCC) model, the Time-Dependent (TD) semiclassical approach, and the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA). They apply these models to the breakup of 15C on Pb at 68A MeV and compare the resulting energy and angular distributions. They then compute the ratio of the breakup angular distribution to the summed quasi-elastic angular distribution for 11Be on Pb at 69A MeV using DEA and compare it to the REB prediction.

## Key numbers
- Breakup of 15C on Pb at 68A MeV: all three models (CDCC, TD, DEA) agree with each other and with experimental data from RIKEN (Nak09).
- Breakup of 11Be on Pb at 69A MeV: DEA calculations show the ratio of breakup angular distribution (at E=0.1 MeV) to summed quasi-elastic cross section is smooth with scattering angle and matches the REB prediction.
- The ratio computed for a carbon target is nearly superimposed to the one obtained on lead, confirming independence from target choice.

## Context
This work extends the comparison of reaction models for halo nuclei (CDCC, TD, DEA) previously applied to systems like 11Be and 15C, and builds on the REB model of Johnson, which predicted the factorisation of cross sections into a pointlike scattering part and a structure-dependent form factor. The ratio technique, if validated experimentally, would enable extraction of halo-nucleus structure information (binding energy, orbital, radial wave function) without sensitivity to the reaction mechanism, and could be extended to Borromean systems like 11Li and possibly proton halos.
