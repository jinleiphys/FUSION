# Open issues in extracting nuclear structure information from the breakup of exotic nuclei

> The open issues in the development of models for the breakup of exotic nuclei and the link with the extraction of structure information from experimental data are reviewed. The question of the improvement of the description of exotic nuclei within reaction models is approached in the perspective of previous analyses of the sensitivity of these models to that description. Future developments of reaction models are suggested, such as the inclusion of various channels within one model. The search for new reaction observables that can emphasise more details of exotic nuclear structure is also proposed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while breakup reactions are a key tool for probing exotic nuclear structure, the extraction of reliable structure information is hindered by open issues in reaction modelling, particularly the need to improve projectile descriptions beyond simple two-body models and to develop reaction models that are both accurate and computationally tractable. The author hedges that a fully microscopic projectile description may not be necessary because breakup is a peripheral reaction sensitive mainly to the asymptotic normalization constant (ANC), but that realistic continuum descriptions (phaseshifts) are essential.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical few-body framework for describing breakup reactions of exotic nuclei, where the projectile is modelled as a core plus one or two loosely-bound valence nucleons. It surveys the main reaction models used to solve the three-body Schrodinger equation: the continuum discretised coupled channel (CDCC) method, the time-dependent (TD) semiclassical model, and eikonal-based approaches (E-CDCC, DEA, CCE). The review then examines specific open issues: improving the projectile description (microscopic inputs, core excitation, four-body breakup for two-neutron halos), extending reaction model validity (including Coulomb deflection at low energy, relativistic effects at high energy), coupling breakup to other reaction channels (elastic scattering, knockout), and developing new observables like the ratio method that are insensitive to reaction details.

## Key numbers

* The relativistic correction to the Coulomb P-T interaction in E-CDCC leads to changes in breakup cross sections at forward angles of about 10% at 100A MeV and 15% at 250A MeV (Ogata and Bertulani, 2009, 2010).
* The ratio method for one-neutron halo nuclei shows nearly identical results for Pb at 69A MeV and C at 67A MeV, confirming its independence from the reaction mechanism (Capel et al., 2011).
* CDCC calculations for the elastic scattering of the two-neutron halo nucleus He-6 on Bi at 22.5 MeV illustrate the need for both three-body projectile description and breakup channel coupling (Matsumoto et al., 2006).
* DWBA calculations including core excitation for the resonant breakup of Be-11 on C at 67A MeV show that the single-particle model (dashed line) cannot reproduce the experimental diffractive pattern, while core excitation contributions (dash-dotted line) and their interference are crucial (Morales et al., 2012; data from Fukuda et al., 2004).

## Context

This review extends the line of work on reaction models for exotic nuclei, building on prior developments such as CDCC (Kamimura, 1986; Yahiro et al., 1986), the time-dependent model (Kido et al., 1994; Esbensen et al., 1995), and eikonal approximations (Glauber, 1959; Ogata et al., 2003; Capel et al., 2005). It addresses open questions raised by comparisons between models, such as the discrepancy between XCDCC calculations (Summers et al., 2006) and DWBA calculations (Chatterjee et al., 2011; Moro et al., 2012) regarding the role of core excitation in breakup. The paper enables future work by suggesting specific developments: extending eikonal-based models (E-CDCC, DEA) to lower energies via Coulomb deflection, incorporating relativistic effects for beam energies above 100A MeV, and developing the ratio method for charged halos and three-body projectiles.
