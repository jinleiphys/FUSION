# Study of resonance states of $^{11}$Be with isospectral bound state microscopic potential

> The theoretical procedure of supersymmetric quantum mechanics (SQM) is adopted for the first time to study quasi-bound states of a weakly bound nuclear system using microscopic potential. The density dependent M3Y (DDM3Y) effective interaction was found earlier to give a satisfactory description of radioactivity, nuclear matter and scattering. In the present work, we have generated a two-body potential microscopically in a single folding model using the DDM3Y effective interaction. From this potential, SQM generated a family of isospectral potentials for $^{11}$Be ($^{10}$Be + n). We investigated the 5/2$^+$, 3/2$^-$ and 3/2$^+$ resonance states of $^{11}$Be. The experimental data and the present calculations of excitation energies of the above resonance states are found to be in good agreement.

# Study of resonance states of $^{11}$Be with isospectral bound state microscopic potential

## Key claim
The supersymmetric quantum mechanics (SQM) technique, combined with a microscopically generated DDM3Y folding potential, can reproduce the excitation energies of the 5/2$^+$, 3/2$^-$, and 3/2$^+$ resonance states of $^{11}$Be to within experimental uncertainty for the first two states, but the 3/2$^+$ state shows a significant deviation (3.40 MeV calculated vs. 3.89 ± 0.02 MeV experimental). The method works only when the ground state of the same spin-parity exists (for bound-state-based isospectral potentials) or when a bound-state-in-continuum (BIC) technique is used for resonances with no bound state of the same $J^\pi$.

## Method
A two-body model of $^{11}$Be as $^{10}$Be + n was adopted. The nucleon-nucleus potential was generated via a single folding calculation using the density-dependent M3Y (DDM3Y) effective interaction with the ground-state density of $^{10}$Be. For each resonance state, supersymmetric quantum mechanics (SQM) was used to construct a family of strictly isospectral potentials $\hat{v}(r;\lambda)$ from the original folding potential $v(r)$; for states with no bound state of the same $J^\pi$ (5/2$^+$, 3/2$^-$, 3/2$^+$), the bound-state-in-continuum (BIC) generalization was employed. The resonance energy $E_R$ was extracted from the maximum of the trapping probability $C(E)$ inside the well-barrier combination, and the width $\Gamma$ was computed semiclassically via the WKB transmission probability through the barrier.

## Key numbers
- 5/2$^+$ resonance: calculated excitation energy $E_x = 1.77$ MeV (experimental $1.77 \pm 0.02$ MeV); calculated width $\Gamma = 124$ keV (experimental $100 \pm 10$ keV).
- 3/2$^-$ resonance: calculated excitation energy $E_x = 2.67$ MeV (experimental $2.67 \pm 0.02$ MeV); calculated width $\Gamma = 188$ keV (experimental $200 \pm 10$ keV).
- 3/2$^+$ resonance: calculated excitation energy $E_x = 3.40$ MeV (experimental $3.89 \pm 0.02$ MeV); calculated width $\Gamma = 110$ keV (experimental $< 50$ keV).
- The DDM3Y potential was normalized by a factor of 1.5 to reproduce the $^{11}$Be ground state ($1/2^+$, $l=0$).
- The zero-range pseudo-potential parameter $J_{00}(E) = -276 (1 - 0.005 E/A)$ MeV·fm$^3$ was used, with density dependence constants $c = 2.07$ and $b = 1.62$ fm$^2$.

## Context
This work extends the SQM-based isospectral potential technique previously applied to weakly bound nuclei such as $^6$He, $^6$Li, and $^6$Be using phenomenological bound-state potentials (Dutta et al., 2003, 2004) to a microscopic DDM3Y folding potential for the first time. It contrasts with an earlier study of the $^{11}$Be 5/2$^+$ resonance by Majumdar et al. (2009) that used a Wood-Saxon potential with parameters adjusted to fit two bound states and one resonance; the present microscopic approach allows simultaneous study of multiple resonance states without parameter adjustment per state. The DDM3Y interaction had previously been validated for radioactivity, nuclear matter, and scattering (Gupta et al., 2006; Basu, 2004), and this work demonstrates its applicability to quasi-bound states of halo nuclei. The results enable future study of unknown resonance states in other exotic nuclei using the same technique.
