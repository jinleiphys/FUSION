# Quantifying alpha clustering in light nuclei from binding energies

> What is the origin of nuclear clustering and how does it emerge from the nuclear interaction? While there is ample experimental evidence for this phenomenon, its theoretical characterization directly from nucleons as degrees of freedom remains a challenge, making it difficult to improve nuclear forces using clustering observables. In this work, a simple ratio of energies based on effective scale arguments is proposed to assess the quality of nuclear forces on alpha clustering (${ {}^{4}\text{He} }$) in bound and narrow ground-state nuclei. The proposed clustering ratio is parameter-free and correctly identify basic alpha clustering features in light nuclei. It is found that in nuclei ranging from ${ {}^{6}\text{Li} }$ to ${ {}^{14}\text{C} }$, state-of-the-art \textit{ab initio} nuclear forces underestimate the degree of alpha clustering in key nuclei such as ${ {}^{8}\text{Be} }$ and ${ {}^{12}\text{C} }$. Stringent constraints on binding energies are then provided by back-propagating 10\% relative uncertainties on the experimental clustering ratios using a parallel Markov chain Monte Carlo algorithm. It is demonstrated that the binding energies of the nuclei $^{6,7}$Li, $^7$Be, $^{10,11}$B, and $^{11}$C need to be obtained within tens of keV precision by nuclear forces to ensure a proper reproduction of basic alpha clustering features in light nuclei. This study provides a new and practical path to guide future optimizations of nuclear forces with a potential impact for medium-mass nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a parameter-free ratio of binding energies, called the one-alpha clustering ratio, to indirectly quantify alpha clustering in light nuclei; it claims that state-of-the-art *ab initio* nuclear forces systematically underestimate alpha clustering in key nuclei such as ⁸Be and ¹²C, but this conclusion is limited to qualitative statements because the measure provides only *a posteriori* tests of cluster presence.

## Method

The author defines a one-alpha clustering ratio r₁(A,α) = |E(A) − E(α)| / Qα(A), where Qα(A) is the alpha-separation energy, and computes it using experimental binding energies for nuclei from ⁶Li to ¹⁴C. The ratio is then evaluated using binding energies from density functional theory (SLy4, UNEDF0, UNEDF1, SkP, SkM*) and *ab initio* methods (Green's function Monte Carlo with AV18+IL7 and NV2+3Ia potentials, no-core shell model with JISP16, Daejon16, and LENPIC interactions). A parallel Markov chain Monte Carlo algorithm with 5000 steps and ΔE = 10 keV back-propagates 10% relative uncertainties on the experimental clustering ratios to constrain the required precision of theoretical binding energies.

## Key numbers

- The one-alpha clustering ratio r₁ for ⁸Be is 313.3 using experimental data, the largest value in the dataset.
- The one-alpha clustering ratio for the Hoyle state (7.65 MeV excitation in ¹²C) is r₁ = 193.8.
- The one-alpha clustering ratio for the 3⁻ state at 9.64 MeV in ¹²C is r₁ = 23.78.
- The one-alpha clustering ratio for the 0₂⁺ state of ¹⁴C at 6.59 MeV is r₁ = 12.97.
- The binding energies of ⁶Li, ⁷Li, ⁷Be, ¹⁰B, ¹¹B, and ¹¹C must be reproduced within tens of keV precision (standard deviations σ(E) < 30 keV from MCMC) to ensure proper reproduction of alpha clustering features; specifically, σ(E) = 3 keV for ⁶Li, 4 keV for ⁷Li, 2 keV for ⁷Be, 8 keV for ¹⁰B, 20 keV for ¹¹B, and 15 keV for ¹¹C.
- The MCMC used Nsteps = 5000 and ΔE = 10 keV, averaging 20 runs of 50 independent MCMC runs.

## Context

This work extends the study of nuclear clustering from qualitative Ikeda diagrams (Ikeda, 1968) to a quantitative, parameter-free measure based solely on binding energies, enabling low-cost assessment of nuclear forces. It contradicts the expectation that modern *ab initio* forces fitted on few-body data should automatically reproduce clustering, showing systematic underestimation in ⁸Be and ¹²C. The results enable future optimization of nuclear forces, particularly the NNLOsat-type interactions (Ekstrom, 2015), by including binding energies of the six identified nuclei, and suggest a link between three-body forces and alpha clustering as previously indicated in studies of ¹⁰B by Navratil (2007) and Nollett (2007).
