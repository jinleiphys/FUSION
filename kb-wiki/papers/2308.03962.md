# Neutrinos and nucleosynthesis of elements

> Neutrinos are known to play important roles in many astrophysical scenarios from the early period of the big bang to current stellar evolution being a unique messenger of the fusion reactions occurring in the center of our sun. In particular, neutrinos are crucial in determining the dynamics and the composition evolution in explosive events such as core-collapse supernovae and the merger of two neutron stars. In this paper, we review the current understanding of supernovae and binary neutron star mergers by focusing on the role of neutrinos therein. Several recent improvements on the theoretical modeling of neutrino interaction rates in nuclear matter as well as their impact on the heavy element nucleosynthesis in the supernova neutrino-driven wind are discussed, including the neutrino-nucleon opacity at the mean field level taking into account the relativistic kinematics of nucleons, the effect due to the nucleon-nucleon correlation, and the nucleon-nucleon bremsstrahlung. We also review the framework used to compute the neutrino-nucleus interactions and the up-to-date yield prediction for isotopes from neutrino nucleosynthesis occurring in the outer envelope of the supernova progenitor star during the explosion. Here improved predictions of energy spectra of supernova neutrinos of all flavors have had significant impact on the nucleosynthesis yields. Rapid progresses in modeling the flavor oscillations of neutrinos in these environments, including several novel mechanisms for collective neutrino oscillations and their potential impacts on various nucleosynthesis processes are summarized.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that neutrinos play a crucial and multifaceted role in determining both the dynamics and the nucleosynthetic outcomes of core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers, but the authors emphasize that current multi-dimensional simulations still yield explosion energies (a few times 10^50 erg) that are lower than canonical observational estimates (a few times 10^51 erg), suggesting either insufficient simulation duration or missing input physics.

## Method

The authors review the current theoretical understanding of core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers, focusing on neutrino physics. ## Key numbers

- The nuclear burning timescale during silicon burning is approximately 0.52 days for a core mass of 1.5 solar masses and luminosity of 3.5 solar luminosities
- The nuclear saturation density is n_sat = 0.15 fm^-3 (rho_sat = 2.4 x 10^14 g cm^-3)
- The electron neutrino luminosity during the post-bounce phase can be approximated as L_nu_e ~ 1.5 x 10^52 erg s^-1 for a proto-neutron star mass of 1.5 solar masses, radius of 80 km, and mass accretion rate of 0.03 solar masses per second
- The muon rest mass is m_mu = 105.66 MeV, above which muonic weak processes become relevant
- Neutrino-driven explosions of low-mass progenitors (8-10 solar masses) yield explosion energies of about 10^50 erg with ejected nickel masses ranging between 10^-3 and 10^-1 solar masses
- The electron neutrino luminosity during collapse is larger than pair production spectra by about 4 orders of magnitude
- The electron fraction drops from initially about Y_e = 0.3 to Y_e = 0.15 after the launch of the nu_e burst

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on nucleosynthesis from Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, and Hoyle (1957) and Cameron (1957), and builds upon decades of supernova modeling by groups including the Garching (VERTEX code), Princeton (FORNAX code), and Oak Ridge (CHIMERA code) collaborations. The paper enables future work by identifying open questions, particularly the tension between simulated and observed explosion energies, and by summarizing the current state of neutrino interaction rates and flavor oscillation mechanisms that must be incorporated into next-generation multi-dimensional simulations with full Boltzmann neutrino transport.
