# Asymmetric nuclear matter and neutron star properties

> In this work we calculate the total mass, radius, moment of inertia, and surface gravitational redshift for neutron stars using various equations of state (EOS). Modern meson-exchange potential models are used to evaluate the $G$-matrix for asymmetric nuclear matter. We calculate both a non-relativistic and a relativistic EOS. Of importance here is the fact that relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculations for symmetric nuclear matter fit the empirical data, which are not reproduced by non-relativistic calculations. Relativistic effects are known to be important at high densities, giving an increased repulsion. This leads to a stiffer EOS compared to the EOS derived with a non-relativistic approach. Both the non-relativistic and the relativistic EOS yield moments of inertia and redshifts in agreement with the accepted values. The relativistic EOS yields, however, too large mass and radius. The implications are discussed.

## Key claim

The relativistic Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) equation of state (EOS) yields neutron star masses and radii that are too large compared to accepted values, even though both the non-relativistic and relativistic EOS produce moments of inertia and surface gravitational redshifts in agreement with observations.

## Method

The authors solved the Bethe-Goldstone equation for asymmetric nuclear matter using modern meson-exchange potential models (Bonn A, B, and C potentials from Machleidt 1989), computing both non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) and relativistic Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) equations of state. They then used these EOS as input to the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations to calculate neutron star mass, radius, moment of inertia, and surface gravitational redshift for various central densities and proton fractions.

## Key numbers

- The relativistic effects become significant at densities above about 0.15 fm$^{-3}$, independent of proton fraction.
- The non-relativistic and relativistic EOS were calculated over the density range 0.1 fm$^{-3} \leq n \leq 0.8$ fm$^{-3}$.
- The energy shift due to relativistic effects is only a few MeV larger for neutron matter than for symmetric nuclear matter.
- The relativistic contribution to the energy per particle scales approximately as $(n/n_0)^{8/3}$, where $n_0$ is the nuclear matter saturation density.
- The maximum mass from all microscopic calculations is given as $M < 2.7 M_\odot$.
- Observations of binary pulsars give a common mass region of $1.3 M_\odot < M < 1.9 M_\odot$.
- The minimum mass of a stable neutron star is $M \sim 0.1 M_\odot$ with central density $\rho \sim 10^{14}$ g/cm$^3$ and radius $R \sim 200$ km.

## Context

This work extends the Dirac-Brueckner approach of Brockmann and Machleidt (1990) to asymmetric nuclear matter and neutron star properties, building on earlier non-relativistic calculations by Wiringa et al. (1988) and others. The results enable comparison between relativistic and non-relativistic predictions for neutron star observables, though the authors note that additional mechanisms (such as hyperons, pion or kaon condensation, or quark matter) may reduce the stiffness of the EOS at high densities.
