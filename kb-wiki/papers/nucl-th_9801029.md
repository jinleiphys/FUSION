# Low Freeze-out Temperature and High Collective Velocities in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> On the basis of a nine-parameter expanding source model that includes special relativity, quantum statistics, resonance decays, and freeze-out on a realistic hypersurface in spacetime, we analyze in detail invariant pi+, pi-, K+, and K- one-particle multiplicity distributions and pi+ and K+ two-particle correlations in nearly central collisions of Si + Au at a laboratory bombarding energy per nucleon of 14.6 GeV/c. By considering separately the one-particle data and the correlation data, we find that the central baryon density, nuclear temperature, transverse collective velocity, longitudinal collective velocity, and source velocity are determined primarily by one-particle multiplicity distributions and that the transverse radius, longitudinal proper time, width in proper time, and pion incoherence fraction are determined primarily by two-particle correlations. By considering separately the pion data and the kaon data, we find that although the pion freeze-out occurs somewhat later than the kaon freeze-out, the 99% confidence-level error bars associated with the two freeze-outs overlap. These and other detailed studies confirm our earlier conclusion based on the simultaneous consideration of the pion and kaon one-particle and correlation data that the freeze-out temperature is less than 100 MeV and that both the longitudinal and transverse collective velocities--which are anti-correlated with the temperature--are substantial. We also discuss the flaws in several previous analyses that yielded a much higher freeze-out temperature of approximately 140 MeV for both this reaction and other reactions involving heavier projectiles and/or higher bombarding energies.

# Low Freeze-out Temperature and High Collective Velocities in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

## Key claim
The freeze-out temperature in central Si+Au collisions at 14.6 GeV/c per nucleon is less than 100 MeV, with substantial longitudinal and transverse collective velocities that are anti-correlated with the temperature. This conclusion holds despite the 99% confidence-level error bars for pion and kaon freeze-out overlapping, indicating that pion freeze-out occurs somewhat later than kaon freeze-out but not at a statistically significant level.

## Method
A nine-parameter expanding source model incorporating special relativity, quantum statistics, resonance decays (10 meson and baryon resonances), and freeze-out on a realistic hypersurface in spacetime was used to analyze invariant π+, π-, K+, and K- one-particle multiplicity distributions and π+ and K+ two-particle correlations from Experiment E-802 at the Brookhaven AGS. The model parameters were determined by minimizing χ² for 1416 data points, with separate analyses performed on one-particle data alone, correlation data alone, pion data alone, kaon data alone, and with constrained or generalized freeze-out hypersurfaces.

## Key numbers
- Nuclear temperature T = 92.9 ± 4.4 MeV (simultaneous fit of all data, 99% confidence)
- Transverse collective velocity v_t = 0.683 ± 0.048 c (simultaneous fit)
- Longitudinal collective velocity v_ℓ = 0.900 +0.023/-0.029 c (simultaneous fit)
- Central baryon density n/n_0 = 0.145 +0.063/-0.045 (simultaneous fit, where n_0 = 0.153 fm⁻³)
- Transverse radius R_t = 8.0 ± 1.6 fm (simultaneous fit)
- Longitudinal proper time τ_f = 8.2 ± 2.2 fm/c (simultaneous fit)
- Pion incoherence fraction λ_π = 0.65 ± 0.11 (simultaneous fit)
- χ²/ν = 1.055 for 1407 degrees of freedom (simultaneous fit)
- When pions alone are considered: T = 78 ± 20 MeV, τ_f = 11.1 ± 5.2 fm/c
- When kaons alone are considered: T = 101 ± 10 MeV, τ_f = 6.6 ± 3.8 fm/c
- With unnormalized one-particle data: T = 98 ± 27 MeV
- Including proton data (contaminated by spectators): T = 97.0 ± 4.0 MeV, χ²/ν = 1.465

## Context
This work extends the author's earlier analysis (Nix, 1997) using the same nine-parameter model to confirm a low freeze-out temperature below 100 MeV, consistent with conclusions by Csernai and collaborators (1995). It directly contradicts analyses by Xu (1993), Braun-Munzinger and collaborators (1995), and others who reported freeze-out temperatures of approximately 140 MeV for this reaction and similar systems. The paper identifies two major flaws in those prior analyses: (1) neglect of relativity in extrapolating slope parameters to zero particle mass, where the correct relativistic treatment (Siemens and Rasmussen, 1979; Schnedermann, Sollfrank, and Heinz, 1993) yields T ≈ 92 ± 10 MeV from the same data; and (2) accumulation of approximations in thermal models including neglect of resonance decays, quantum statistics, and absolute normalization information. The results enable more reliable extraction of freeze-out properties in heavy-ion collisions and provide a benchmark for searching for quark-gluon plasma signatures through discontinuities in freeze-out parameters as functions of bombarding energy and system size.
