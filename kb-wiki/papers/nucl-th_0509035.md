# Indirect techniques in nuclear astrophysics. Asymptotic Normalization Coefficient and Trojan Horse

> Owing to the presence of the Coulomb barrier at astrophysically relevant kinetic energies it is very difficult, or sometimes impossible, to measure astrophysical reaction rates in the laboratory. That is why different indirect techniques are being used along with direct measurements. Here we address two important indirect techniques, the asymptotic normalization coefficient (ANC) and the Trojan Horse (TH) methods. We discuss the application of the ANC technique for calculation of the astrophysical processes in the presence of subthreshold bound states, in particular, two different mechanisms are discussed: direct capture to the subthreshold state and capture to the low-lying bound states through the subthreshold state, which plays the role of the subthreshold resonance. The ANC technique can also be used to determine the interference sign of the resonant and nonresonant (direct) terms of the reaction amplitude. The TH method is unique indirect technique allowing one to measure astrophysical rearrangement reactions down to astrophysically relevant energies. We explain why there is no Coulomb barrier in the sub-process amplitudes extracted from the TH reaction. The expressions for the TH amplitude for direct and resonant cases are presented.

## Key claim

The paper argues that indirect techniques, specifically the Asymptotic Normalization Coefficient (ANC) method and the Trojan Horse (TH) method, can determine astrophysical reaction rates at energies where direct measurements are impossible due to the Coulomb barrier, but the ANC method's accuracy depends on the reaction being peripheral and the TH method extracts half-off-energy-shell amplitudes whose virtuality is compensated by the binding energy of the fragments in the projectile, not solely by high-momentum components.

## Method

The ANC method determines the normalization of peripheral radiative capture cross sections by measuring the asymptotic normalization coefficient from transfer reactions (e.g., \(^{14}\mathrm{N}(^{3}\mathrm{He},d)^{15}\mathrm{O}\) at 26.3 MeV) and using it to calculate the astrophysical S-factor, including contributions from subthreshold bound states and interference between resonant and nonresonant amplitudes via R-matrix formalism. The Trojan Horse method extracts the cross section of a two-body sub-reaction (e.g., \(x+A \to b+B\)) from a three-body reaction \(a+A \to y+b+B\) performed above the Coulomb barrier, selecting the quasi-free contribution where the spectator \(y\) carries away excess energy, and folding the amplitude with the Fourier component of the bound-state wave function of \(a=(xy)\).

## Key numbers

- ANC for \(^{14}\mathrm{N}+p \to ^{15}\mathrm{O}(3/2^{+},\,6.79\ \mathrm{MeV})\): \(C^{2}=27.1 \pm 6.8\ \mathrm{fm}^{-1}\).
- Astrophysical S-factor for \(^{14}\mathrm{N}(p,\gamma)^{15}\mathrm{O}\) capture to the 6.79 MeV state: \(S(0)=1.40 \pm 0.20\ \mathrm{keV\,b}\).
- Total astrophysical S-factor at zero energy for \(^{14}\mathrm{N}(p,\gamma)^{15}\mathrm{O}\): \(S(0)=1.70 \pm 0.22\ \mathrm{keV\,b}\).
- Astrophysical S-factor for capture to the ground state of \(^{15}\mathrm{O}\): \(S(0)=0.15 \pm 0.07\ \mathrm{keV\,b}\).
- Gamma width of the second resonance in \(^{12}\mathrm{N}\) measured at RIKEN: \(13.0 \pm 0.5\ \mathrm{meV}\); channel radiative width at channel radius \(r_{0}=5.0\ \mathrm{fm}\): \(\Gamma_{\gamma}(E_{R})_{\mathrm{ch}}=54\ \mathrm{meV}\).
- Channel radiative width for \(^{13}\mathrm{N}(p,\gamma)^{14}\mathrm{O}\) at resonance energy and \(r_{0}=5\ \mathrm{fm}\): \(|[\Gamma_{\gamma}]_{\mathrm{ch}}|=0.81\times 10^{-6}\ \mathrm{eV}\); internal part solutions: \(-2.73\ \mathrm{eV}^{1/2}\) (destructive) or \(0.93\ \mathrm{eV}^{1/2}\) (constructive).

## Context

This work extends the ANC method introduced by Mukhamedzhanov and Timofeyuk (1990) and Xu et al. (1994) to reactions involving subthreshold bound states and interference effects, and it advances the Trojan Horse method originally proposed by Baur (1986) by clarifying the role of binding energy in compensating virtuality. The results for \(^{14}\mathrm{N}(p,\gamma)^{15}\mathrm{O}\) agree with direct measurements from the LUNA collaboration and imply an increased age of main-sequence turnoff in globular clusters, as noted by Charbonnel et al. (2001). The techniques enable determination of reaction rates for key processes in the CNO cycle, hot CNO cycle, and alternative paths bypassing the 3\(\alpha\) reaction in low-metallicity stars.
