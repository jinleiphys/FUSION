# Peak of sound velocity, scale symmetry, and nuclear force in baryonic matter

> The sound velocity in homogeneous matter has fundamental significance as it relates to the stiffness of the equation of state of compact star matter. In this work, we investigate the density evolution of the sound velocity in homogeneous {neutron matter at zero temperature} by using an effective field theory implemented with a conformal compensator -- the nonlinear realization of scale symmetry -- regarded as the source of the lightest scalar meson. We find that the peak of sound velocity emerges naturally in the intermediate density region, $(1-2.5)n_0$, without resorting to any transitions from hadron to exotic configurations or introducing new degrees of freedom. This phenomenon is not found in the Walecka-type models where the sigma meson is included in the linear-type approach, therefore it is an intrinsic character of the dilaton compensator approach through the matching of the QCD trace anomaly; a mechanism has not been found before, and it connects to the character of the lightest scalar meson. In addition, these observations shed light on how the hidden scale symmetry manifests in the nuclear medium from the unitarity limit in dilute matter to the dilaton limit in compact star matter.

## Key claim

The peak of sound velocity in homogeneous neutron matter emerges naturally in the intermediate density region (1-2.5 times nuclear saturation density) from the nonlinear realization of scale symmetry via the dilaton compensator field, without requiring any transition from hadronic to exotic configurations or introduction of new degrees of freedom. This mechanism is not present in Walecka-type models where the sigma meson is included in a linear approach.

## Method

The authors employ a generalized effective field theory (GnEFT) that implements scale symmetry through a conformal compensator field, treating the lightest scalar meson as a dilaton associated with the trace anomaly of QCD. They include baryonic and mesonic Lagrangians with medium effects parameterized by Brown-Rho scaling, and solve the mean-field equations for two parameter sets (bsHLS-L and bsHLS-H) that reproduce nuclear matter properties at saturation density. The sound velocity is then computed from the equation of state derived from this framework.

## Key numbers

- The peak of sound velocity occurs in the density range (1-2.5) n0, where n0 ≈ 0.16 fm^-3 is the normal nuclear matter density
- For parameter set bsHLS-H: saturation density n0 = 0.159 fm^-3, binding energy e0 = -16.0 MeV, symmetry energy E_sym(n0) = 29.2 MeV, incompressibility K = 284 MeV
- For parameter set bsHLS-L: saturation density n0 = 0.159 fm^-3, binding energy e0 = -16.0 MeV, symmetry energy E_sym(n0) = 30.5 MeV, incompressibility K = 232 MeV
- The dilaton field expectation value f_chi is set equal to f_pi = 92.4 MeV for consistency with chiral expansion
- The anomalous dimension parameter beta' = 1.15 for bsHLS-H and 0.395 for bsHLS-L

## Context

This work extends the dilaton-compensated effective field theory approach previously developed by Li (2016) and Ma (2019) for describing nuclear matter from equilibrium density to compact-star densities. It contradicts the prevailing view that the sound velocity peak must arise from hadron-quark transitions (McLerran 2018, Baym 2019), topology changes (Ma 2018, 2019), or chiral symmetry restoration (Hippert 2021). The finding connects to the analysis of Fujimoto (2022) who attributed the peak to the derivative of the trace anomaly approaching the conformal limit. The results enable experimental tests at facilities such as HIAF and RAON, and provide insights into how hidden scale symmetry manifests from the unitarity limit in dilute matter to the dilaton limit in compact stars.
