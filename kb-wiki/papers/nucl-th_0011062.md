# Comparison of Transfer-to-Continuum and Eikonal Models of Projectile Fragmentation Reactions

> Spectroscopic properties of nuclei are accessible with projectile fragmentation reactions, but approximations made in the reaction theory can limit the accuracy of the determinations. We examine here two models that have rather different approximations for the nucleon wave function, the target interaction, and the treatment of the finite duration of the reaction. The nucleon-target interaction is treated differently in the eikonal and the transfer-to-continuum model, but the differences are more significant for light targets. We propose a new parameterization with that in mind. We also propose a new formula to calculate the amplitude that combines the better treatment of the wave function in the eikonal model with the better treatment of the target interaction in the transfer-to-continuum model.

## Key claim

The paper concludes that present models used to calculate projectile fragmentation breakup cross sections may overestimate the true value, but the error should be of the order of 20% or less, with the caveat that this estimate does not account for target breakup contributions which could account for another 20% of measured cross sections.

## Method

The authors compared the transfer-to-continuum (TC) model, which uses the on-shell neutron-target scattering matrix and asymptotic Hankel function wave functions, with the eikonal model, which uses an eikonal S-matrix and allows realistic wave functions. They performed calculations for neutron removal from s and p states in 12Be on a 9Be target at various incident energies, using a newly parameterized neutron-9Be optical potential fitted to experimental total cross sections in the 10-180 MeV range. The comparison examined differences in the treatment of the neutron-target S-matrix, the initial state wave function approximation, and the finite duration of the reaction.

## Key numbers

- The condition for the sudden eikonal approximation to be valid is satisfied from about 80A MeV for a typical binding energy of 10 MeV or more
- For a halo with separation energy around 0.5 MeV, the condition |k_z^min| >> gamma_i is satisfied at all initial energies
- The maximum neutron parallel momentum for a 12Be projectile is k_z^max = 0.96 fm^-1
- The integrated probabilities obtained with the three approximations (TC with Hankel function, TC with realistic wave function, and eikonal) differ by not more than about 10%
- The total cross sections from the eikonal model are larger by about 20% than the TC calculations at all energies except 20A MeV
- At 20A MeV, the eikonal and optical model calculations give very close results due to compensating effects
- The stripping cross section is almost three times larger than the diffraction cross section based on peak values

## Context

This work extends previous comparisons of projectile fragmentation models by Bonaccorso and collaborators (1999) and Bertsch et al. (1998), specifically addressing the validity of approximations in the eikonal and transfer-to-continuum methods. The authors propose a new formula (Eq. A10) that combines the better treatment of the wave function from the eikonal model with the better treatment of the target interaction from the TC model, and suggest that future experiments should detect the final excitation state of the target in coincidence with breakup events, as done by Henri et al. for normal heavy ions.
