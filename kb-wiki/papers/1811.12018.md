# Comparison of cuprates and nuclear matter pairing properties. Quartet (alpha-particle) condensation in nuclear systems

## Key claim

Nuclear pairing and cuprate superconductivity share superficial similarities, both exhibit a dome-shaped phase diagram and a pseudo-gap phase, but the underlying physical mechanisms are likely different; in nuclear matter the dome arises simply from the finite range of the nuclear force, whereas in cuprates alternative explanations remain possible.

## Method

The author compares the ratio of pairing gap to Fermi energy (Δ/EF) and the density-dependent phase diagrams of neutron matter and high-Tc superconductors, referencing Green's Function Monte Carlo calculations and effective pairing forces. For alpha-particle condensation, the THSR (Tohsaki-Horiuchi-Schuck-Röpke) wave function, a product of three identical alpha-particle wave functions, antisymmetrized and containing two variational parameters (b and B), is variationally minimized with an effective nucleon-nucleon force to describe the ground state and first excited 0⁺ state (Hoyle state) of ¹²C.

## Key numbers

- Ratio Δ/EF for neutron-neutron pairing in neutron matter can be as large as 1/5.
- The Hoyle state in ¹²C is situated at 7.56 MeV excitation energy.
- The variational parameter b (size of individual alpha particles) remains close to the free-space value of approximately 1.37 fm.
- The variational parameter B for the Hoyle state takes on about three times the value of b.
- The radius of the Hoyle state is approximately 3.8 fm, corresponding to a volume larger by a factor of 3–4 compared to the ground state radius of 2.4 fm.
- The Hoyle state shows over 70% occupancy of alpha particles in the 0S orbit, with other occupancies down by over a factor of ten.

## Context

This work extends the THSR approach to alpha-particle condensation first proposed by Tohsaki, Horiuchi, Schuck, and Röpke (2001) for self-conjugate nuclei such as ¹²C and ¹⁶O. The comparison of nuclear pairing with cuprate superconductivity builds on known similarities in phase diagrams, referencing prior work on the pseudo-gap phase in high-Tc materials (Georges) and nuclear matter (Röpke). The Hoyle state is the critical doorway for the triple-alpha reaction in stellar nucleosynthesis, and its accurate description enables predictions for Hoyle-analog states in heavier self-conjugate nuclei like ¹⁶O.
