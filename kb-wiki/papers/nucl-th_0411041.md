# Intensity interferometry of thermal photons from relativistic heavy ion collisions

> Intensity interferometry of thermal photons, having transverse momenta $k_T \approx $ 0.1 -- 2.0 GeV, produced in relativistic collision of heavy nuclei is studied. It is seen to provide an accurate information about the temporal and spatial structure of the interacting system. The source dimensions and their $k_T$ dependence revealed by the photon interferometry, display a richness not seen in pion interferometry. We attribute this to difference in the source functions, the fact that photons come out from every stage of the collision and from every point in the system, and the fact that the rate of production of photons is different for the quark-gluon plasma, which dominates the early hot stage, and the hadronic matter which populates the last phase of the collision dynamics. The usefulness of this procedure is demonstrated by an application to collision of lead nuclei at the CERN SPS. Prediction for the transverse momentum dependence of the sizes for SPS, RHIC, and LHC energies are given.

## Key claim

Photon intensity interferometry can reveal a richer transverse-momentum dependence of source dimensions than pion interferometry, because photons are emitted from every stage of the collision and from every point in the system, with different production rates for quark-gluon plasma and hadronic matter. The study demonstrates this for lead-lead collisions at CERN SPS and provides predictions for RHIC and LHC energies, but the results for the one-dimensional invariant-radius analysis at SPS are model-dependent because they rely on correlation radii taken from pion interferometry.

## Method

The author performed hydrodynamic simulations of central heavy-ion collisions assuming boost-invariant longitudinal expansion and azimuthally symmetric transverse expansion, with a quark-hadron phase transition at 180 MeV and freeze-out at 100 MeV. Photon production rates were computed using leading-order results for quark matter and state-of-the-art hadronic rates including form factors and hadronic bremsstrahlung, with the equation of state including all particles with mass below 2.5 GeV. The correlation function was evaluated from the space-time emission function using the standard formalism for outward, sideward, and longitudinal momentum differences, and the radii were obtained from the inverse of the mean-squared momentum differences weighted by the correlation function.

## Key numbers

- Photon transverse momentum range studied: \(k_T \approx 0.1\) – 2.0 GeV
- For SPS lead-lead collisions, the one-dimensional invariant radius \(R_{\text{inv}} \approx 6.5\) fm for \(0.10 \leq k_T \leq 0.20\) GeV and 5.3 fm for \(0.20 \leq k_T \leq 0.30\) GeV
- WA98 experimental results for \(R_{\text{inv}}\) (after multiplying by \(\sqrt{2}\) to match conventions): 8.34 \(\pm\) 1.7 fm and 8.63 \(\pm\) 2.0 fm for the same \(k_T\) windows
- At SPS, the sideward radii at \(k_T = 0.15\) and 0.25 GeV are 4.93 fm and 4.85 fm; longitudinal radii are 3.7 fm and 2.5 fm; outward radii are 9.97 fm and 9.39 fm
- At LHC energies, all radii decrease roughly as \(1/k_T^\alpha\) with \(\alpha \approx 0.27\) for outward, 0.14 for sideward, and 0.94 for longitudinal correlations
- Particle rapidity density at midrapidity used for RHIC: \(dN/dy = 1260\); for LHC: \(dN/dy = 5625\)

## Context

This work extends earlier exploratory studies of photon interferometry by Srivastava (DKS) and by Peressounko (ORS) by incorporating improved photon production rates from quark matter (Guy, Patrick) and hadronic matter (Turbide et al., Simon), a more complete hadronic equation of state, and hadronic form factors. The study addresses the "HBT puzzle" in pion interferometry, where hydrodynamic models predict outward radii up to a factor of 2 larger than sideward radii, contrary to experimental observations. The results enable future three-dimensional correlation measurements at RHIC and LHC to constrain the space-time evolution of the quark-gluon plasma and distinguish between quark-matter and hadronic-matter photon sources.
