# Exact representations of many body interactions with RBM neural networks

> Restricted Boltzmann Machines (RBM) are simple statistical models defined on a bipartite graph which have been successfully used in studying more complicated many-body systems, both classical and quantum. In this work, we exploit the representation power of RBMs to provide an exact decomposition of many-body contact interactions into one-body operators coupled to discrete auxiliary fields. This construction generalizes the well known Hirsch's transform used for the Hubbard model to more complicated theories such as Pionless EFT in nuclear physics, which we analyze in detail. We also discuss possible applications of our mapping for quantum annealing applications and conclude with some implications for RBM parameter optimization through machine learning.

## Key claim

The paper shows that Restricted Boltzmann Machines (RBMs) can provide an exact decomposition of many-body contact interactions into one-body operators coupled to discrete auxiliary fields, generalizing Hirsch's transform beyond the Hubbard model to theories such as pionless effective field theory in nuclear physics; the construction works for any interaction that can be expressed in terms of idempotent or involutive operators, and the mapping is exact rather than approximate.

## Method

The authors construct a hybrid quantum-classical RBM where the visible layer consists of quantum density operators (or other idempotent/involutive operators) and the hidden layer consists of classical binary (or categorical) auxiliary fields. By tracing out the hidden layer, the effective Hamiltonian for the visible layer is expressed as a cumulant generating function, and the coupling strengths of the resulting many-body interactions are determined by solving linear systems obtained from working in the eigenbasis of the density operators. The approach is applied explicitly to two-body and three-body contact interactions, with the RBM parameters chosen to produce desired coupling strengths while removing unwanted lower-order terms via bias adjustments.

## Key numbers

* For two-body interactions, the relation between RBM parameters and physical couplings is given by: \(A^{(1)}_{\mu} = -\ln \left(\frac{e^{-(C+W_{\mu})}+1}{e^{-C}+1}\right)\) and \(A^{(2)} = -\ln \left(\frac{e^{-(C+\sum_{\mu=1}^2 W_{\mu})}+1}{e^{-C}+1}\right)-\sum_{\mu=1}^2 A^{(1)}_{\mu}\) (Eq. 2bd_general).
* For the Hirsch transform special case with repulsive interactions, the coupling constant \(a\) satisfies \(\tanh(a)^2 = \tanh\left(\frac{A^{(2)}}{4}\right)\) (Eq. a2).
* For three-body interactions with symmetric couplings, the relations are: \(A^{(1)}=-\ln \left(\frac{e^{-(C+W)}+1}{e^{-C}+1}\right)\), \(A^{(2)}=-\ln \left(\frac{e^{-(C+2W)}+1}{e^{-C}+1}\right) - 2 A^{(1)}\), and \(A^{(3)}=-\ln \left(\frac{e^{-(C+3W)}+1}{e^{-C}+1}\right)-3 A^{(2)}- 3 A^{(1)}\) (Eq. 3bd_symmetric).
* For an attractive three-body interaction (\(A^{(3)}<0\)), the choice \(C=-2W\) yields \(|A^{(3)}|=\ln \left(\cosh ^4\left(\frac{W}{2}\right) \text{sech}(W)\right)\); for repulsive (\(A^{(3)}>0\)), \(C=-W\) gives the same magnitude.

## Context

This work extends the discrete auxiliary-field mapping pioneered by Hirsch (1983) for the Hubbard model to more general many-body interactions, including the three-body contact forces that appear in pionless effective field theory for nuclear physics. It overcomes limitations of prior approaches: the continuous Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation (Stratonovich 1957, Hubbard 1959) cannot handle three-body forces that are not perfect squares, and the method of Chen et al. (2004) for three-body interactions requires the condition \(V^2 < -2 \alpha_t U^3\) which fails for repulsive two-body couplings. The RBM construction also improves upon the Deep Boltzmann Machine mapping of Yoshioka (2019) by reducing network depth and removing restrictions on coupling constant values. This enables new auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo calculations for nuclear systems with three-body forces and suggests applications for quantum annealing and machine learning optimization of RBM parameters.
