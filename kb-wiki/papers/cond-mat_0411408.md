# Microcanonical Thermostatistics as Foundation of Thermodynamics. The microscopic origin of condensation and phase separations

> Conventional thermo-statistics address infinite homogeneous systems within the canonical ensemble. However, some 150 years ago the original motivation of thermodynamics was the description of steam engines, i.e. boiling water. Its essential physics is the separation of the gas phase from the liquid. Of course, boiling water is inhomogeneous and as such cannot be treated by canonical thermo-statistics. Then it is not astonishing, that a phase transition of first order is signaled canonically by a Yang-Lee singularity. Thus it is only treated correctly by microcanonical Boltzmann-Planck statistics. This is elaborated in the present article. It turns out that the Boltzmann-Planck statistics is much richer and gives fundamental insight into statistical mechanics and especially into entropy. This can even be done to some extend rigorously and analytically. The microcanonical entropy has a very simple physical meaning: It measures the microscopic uncertainty that we have about the system, i.e. the number of points in $6N$-dim phase, which are consistent with our information about the system. It can rigorously be split into an ideal-gas part and a configuration part which contains all the physics and especially is responsible for all phase transitions. The deep and essential difference between ``extensive'' and ``intensive'' control parameters, i.e. microcanonical and canonical statistics, is exemplified by rotating, self-gravitating systems.

## Key claim

The paper argues that microcanonical Boltzmann-Planck statistics, not canonical Gibbs statistics, is the correct foundation for thermodynamics because it can treat inhomogeneous systems and phase separations (such as boiling water) that canonical ensemble cannot handle; the key qualifier is that this applies to finite, non-extensive, or self-gravitating systems where the thermodynamic limit and extensivity assumptions fail.

## Method

The author derives the microcanonical partition sum (sum of states) for a classical N-body system with Hamiltonian H = Σ p_i²/2m + Φ_int, then splits the microcanonical entropy S(E,N,V) rigorously into an ideal-gas part S_id-gas and a configuration part S_conf that contains all phase transition physics. The convexity of S(E) is identified as the generic microcanonical signal of first-order phase transitions and phase separation, demonstrated analytically through the volume dependence of the configuration integral and illustrated with the ferromagnetic Potts model (q=10) on a 50×50 lattice.

## Key numbers

- For the ferromagnetic Potts model (q=10) on a 50×50 lattice with periodic boundary conditions: at energy e₁ the system is in the pure ordered phase, at e₃ in the pure disordered phase; at e_a slightly above e₁ the temperature T_a is higher than T₂ and even higher than T_b at e_b slightly below e₃
- For an ideal gas expanding from volume V₁ to V₂ (V₂ > V₁): entropy change ΔS = N ln(V₂/V₁)
- For the configuration entropy at low energies: S_conf → ln{[V₀N/V]^(N-1)} < 0 as E → E₀, where V₀N ≪ V is the eigen-volume of the condensed droplet
- For the configuration entropy at high energies: S_conf → 0 for E ≫ Φ_int
- For the solid-liquid transition: S_conf → ln{[d³/V₀N]^(N-1)} where d³ is the lattice cage volume per molecule

## Context

This work extends the microcanonical approach to thermodynamics developed by Boltzmann (1877) and Planck, and directly addresses the original motivation of thermodynamics, steam engines and the liquid-gas transition of water, which conventional canonical statistics (Boltzmann-Gibbs) cannot treat for inhomogeneous systems. The paper contradicts the standard canonical treatment where first-order phase transitions appear only as Yang-Lee singularities in the thermodynamic limit, and builds on prior work by Clausius (1850, 1854, 1865) on entropy and Prigogine (1954) on internal versus external entropy production. The analysis enables the study of phase separations in finite systems, atomic clusters, hot nuclei, and rotating self-gravitating systems where the canonical ensemble fails due to non-extensivity or long-range interactions.
