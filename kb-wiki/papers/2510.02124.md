# A low-circuit-depth quantum computing approach to the nuclear shell model

> In this work, we introduce a new qubit mapping strategy for the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) applied to nuclear shell model calculations, where each Slater determinant (SD) is mapped to a qubit, rather than assigning qubits to individual single-particle states. While this approach may increase the total number of qubits required in some cases, it enables the construction of simpler quantum circuits that are more compatible with current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices. We apply this method to seven nuclei: Four lithium isotopes $^{6-9}$Li from the \textit{p}-shell, $^{18}$F from the \textit{sd}-shell, and two heavier nuclei ($^{210}$Po, and $^{210}$Pb). We run circuits representing their ground states on a noisy simulator (IBM's \textit{FakeFez} backend) and quantum hardware ($ibm\_pittsburgh$). For heavier nuclei, we demonstrate the feasibility of simulating $^{210}$Po and $^{210}$Pb as 22- and 29-qubit systems, respectively. Additionally, we employ Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE) via two-qubit gate folding to mitigate errors in both simulated and hardware-executed results. Post-mitigation, the best results show less than 4 \% deviation from shell model predictions across all nuclei studied. This SD-based qubit mapping proves particularly effective for lighter nuclei and two-nucleon systems, offering a promising route for near-term quantum simulations in nuclear physics.

# A low-circuit-depth quantum computing approach to the nuclear shell model

## Key claim
The authors propose mapping each Slater determinant (SD) configuration to a single qubit rather than mapping individual single-particle states to qubits, which increases qubit count in some cases but enables simpler quantum circuits with lower depth. The method achieves less than 4% deviation from shell model predictions after error mitigation, but this best-case result applies only to the post-mitigation data and not to all raw hardware runs.

## Method
The authors construct a qubit Hamiltonian in the basis of Slater determinants rather than single-particle states, using the Jordan-Wigner transformation without the parity Z-gate string. They apply a Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) with a single-excitation Givens rotation ansatz (ladder of nearest-neighbor single excitations) to find ground state energies, then execute the optimized circuits on IBM's FakeFez noisy simulator and ibm_pittsburgh quantum hardware. Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE) via two-qubit gate folding is applied to mitigate errors in both simulated and hardware results.

## Key numbers
- Seven nuclei studied: four lithium isotopes (6-9Li), 18F, 210Po, and 210Pb.
- 6Li ground state: 8-qubit system with 14 two-qubit gates and depth 36 (optimized: depth 67).
- 7-9Li ground states: 15-qubit systems with 28 two-qubit gates and depth 71 (optimized: depth 118).
- 18F: 25-qubit system with 48 two-qubit gates and depth 121 (optimized: depth 213).
- 210Po: 22-qubit system with 42 two-qubit gates and depth 86 (optimized: depth 191).
- 210Pb: 29-qubit system with 56 two-qubit gates and depth 114 (optimized: depth 246).
- Noisy simulation for 6Li and 7Li showed approximately 7% underbinding relative to shell model reference energies.
- Noisy simulation for 8Li showed 2.22% underbinding; for 9Li showed 1.46% underbinding.
- Post-mitigation, best results show less than 4% deviation from shell model predictions across all nuclei studied.
- 210Po reference binding energy: -8.762 MeV; noisy simulation average: -9.438 MeV (approximately 8% more bound).
- 210Pb reference binding energy: -9.091 MeV; hardware average: -16.830 MeV (85% more bound).

## Context
This work extends the application of variational quantum algorithms to nuclear shell model calculations, building on prior approaches that map single-particle states to qubits (Bhoy et al., 2024; Kiss et al., 2022; Romero et al., 2022). The SD-based mapping trades increased qubit count for reduced circuit depth, making it more compatible with current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices. The method enables simulation of heavier nuclei such as 210Po and 210Pb as 22- and 29-qubit systems, and the simplified circuit structure may facilitate future inclusion of three-nucleon forces within the same design strategy.
