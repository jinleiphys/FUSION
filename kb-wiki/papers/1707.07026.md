# Different Realizations of Cooper-Frye Sampling with Conservation Laws

> Approaches based on viscous hydrodynamics for the hot and dense stage and hadronic transport for the final dilute rescattering stage are successfully applied to the dynamic description of heavy ion reactions at high beam energies. One crucial step in such hybrid approaches is the so called particlization, the transition between the hydrodynamic description to microscopic degrees of freedom. For this purpose, individual particles are sampled on the Cooper-Frye hypersurface. In this work, 4 different realizations of sampling algorithms are compared, where three of them incorporate global conservation laws of quantum numbers in each event. The algorithms are compared within two types of scenarios: simple "box" hypersurface consisting of only one static cell and a typical particlization hypersurface for Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}} = 200$ GeV. For all algorithms the mean multiplicities (or particle spectra) remain unaffected by global conservation laws in the case of large volumes. In contrast, the fluctuations of the particle numbers are affected considerably. The fluctuations of the newly developed SPREW algorithm based on exponential weights and the recently suggested SER algorithm based on ensemble rejection are smaller than without conservation laws and agree with the expectation from the canonical ensemble. The previously applied mode sampling algorithm produces dramatically larger fluctuations, than it is expected in the corresponding microcanonical ensemble, and therefore should be avoided in fluctuation studies. This study might be of interest for investigations of particle fluctuations and correlations, e.g. the suggested signatures for a phase transition or a critical endpoint, in hybrid approaches that are affected by global conservation laws.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that enforcing global conservation laws (baryon number, strangeness, electric charge, energy, and momentum) during Cooper-Frye particlization does not affect mean multiplicities or particle spectra in large-volume systems, but it significantly alters event-by-event fluctuations; the newly developed SPREW algorithm and the SER algorithm produce fluctuations matching the canonical ensemble, while the previously used mode sampling algorithm produces dramatically larger fluctuations than expected and should be avoided in fluctuation studies.

## Method

Four sampling algorithms were compared: conventional grand-canonical sampling (no conservation laws), mode sampling (used in UrQMD hybrid approach), SER (sequential ensemble rejection), and the newly proposed SPREW (single particle rejection with exponential weights). The algorithms were tested on a simple static "box" hypersurface at T=150 MeV with volumes corresponding to box lengths of 5 fm and 20 fm, and on a realistic Cooper-Frye hypersurface from ideal hydrodynamic simulations of central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV using the CLVisc code with a freeze-out temperature of 137 MeV. Mean multiplicities, scaled variances, and distributions of conserved quantities were compared across algorithms and against analytical expectations from canonical and microcanonical ensembles.

## Key numbers

- For the box test with length l=5 fm, the mode sampling introduced species-dependent biases in mean multiplicities, while SPREW and SER reproduced grand-canonical means within statistical errors.
- For the box test with length l=20 fm, all algorithms reproduced expected mean values within 1% accuracy.
- The scaled variance (sigma^2/<N>) for SPREW and SER (without energy conservation) matched the canonical expectation within statistical errors for both box sizes; mode sampling produced scaled variances exceeding even the grand-canonical values.
- For Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV, all three algorithms reproduced mean multiplicities for the most abundant particle species within better than 0.5% accuracy.
- The relative difference for central Au+Au collisions at the highest RHIC energy does not exceed 2% for bulk observables when comparing grand-canonical sampling to sampling with conservation laws.
- The typical rescaling factor a for momenta in SPREW sampling is |a| ~ 3% for Au-Au collisions.
- The SER algorithm uses an energy rejection criterion of |E_sampled - E_surface|/E_surface < 0.01.

## Context

This work extends the study of particlization algorithms in hybrid hydrodynamics+transport approaches, which are the standard model for describing heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC energies (reviews by Hirano (2012) and Petersen (2014)). It directly addresses the issue raised by Oliinychenko (2016) that the mode sampling algorithm (originally implemented in the UrQMD hybrid approach by Petersen (2008) and Huovinen (2012)) produces multiplicity fluctuations that depend on internal details. The new SPREW algorithm provides a fast alternative to the SER algorithm (called "unbiased Becattini-Ferroni sampling" in Oliinychenko (2016)) for generating canonical ensembles, enabling reliable studies of particle fluctuations and correlations that are signatures for a phase transition or critical endpoint in the QCD phase diagram, as investigated experimentally by Adamczyk (2013) and Aggarwal (2010).
