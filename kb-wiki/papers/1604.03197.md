# Frontiers in Nuclear Astrophysics

> The synthesis of nuclei in diverse cosmic scenarios is reviewed, with a summary of the basic concepts involved before a discussion of the current status in each case is made. We review the physics of the early universe, the proton to neutron ratio influence in the observed helium abundance, reaction networks, the formation of elements up to beryllium, the inhomogeneous Big Bang model, and the Big Bang nucleosynthesis constraints on cosmological models. Attention is paid to element production in stars, together with the details of the pp chain, the pp reaction, $^3$He formation and destruction, electron capture on $^7$Be, the importance of $^8$B formation and its relation to solar neutrinos, and neutrino oscillations. Nucleosynthesis in massive stars is also reviewed, with focus on the CNO cycle and its hot companion cycle, the rp-process, triple-$α$ capture, and red giants and AGB stars. The stellar burning of carbon, neon, oxygen, and silicon is presented in a separate section, as well as the slow and rapid nucleon capture processes and the importance of medium modifications due to electrons also for pycnonuclear reactions. The nucleosynthesis in cataclysmic events such as in novae, X-ray bursters and in core-collapse supernovae, the role of neutrinos, and the supernova radioactivity and light-curve is further discussed, as well as the structure of neutron stars and its equation of state. A brief review of the element composition found in cosmic rays is made in the end.

# Frontiers in Nuclear Astrophysics

## Key claim

This review paper surveys the current understanding of nucleosynthesis across cosmic environments, from the Big Bang to stellar and explosive scenarios, but explicitly notes that the primordial lithium abundance remains a potential exception to the otherwise successful predictions of Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN). The authors caution that the review cannot properly reference all seminal works in this vast field.

## Method

The paper systematically reviews the physics of the early Universe, including the proton-to-neutron ratio's influence on helium abundance, reaction networks, and BBN constraints on cosmological models, using Friedmann cosmology and Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics. It then examines stellar nucleosynthesis covering the pp chain, CNO cycle, rp-process, triple-alpha capture, and the burning of carbon, neon, oxygen, and silicon, followed by discussions of slow and rapid neutron capture processes, pycnonuclear reactions, and nucleosynthesis in novae, X-ray bursters, and core-collapse supernovae. The review also addresses neutron star structure and equation of state, and briefly covers cosmic ray composition.

## Key numbers

- Primordial helium mass fraction of 25% and hydrogen mass fraction of 75% are explained by a neutron-to-proton ratio of n/p = 1/7 at the start of BBN
- The baryon-to-photon ratio from WMAP is eta = (6.16 +/- 0.15) x 10^(-10); from Planck it is Omega_b h^2 = 0.02207 +/- 0.00033
- WMAP yields Universe age t_0 = 13.72 +/- 0.12 Gyr; Planck yields t_0 = 13.813 +/- 0.058 Gyr
- The deuteron binding energy is 2.24 MeV; deuterium formation occurs at T ~ 1.25 x 10^9 K, corresponding to t ~ 100 s after the Big Bang
- The neutron-proton mass difference is Delta m = 1.294 MeV
- The reaction rate for p + n -> d + gamma at BBN energies is sigma v ~ 5 x 10^(-20) cm^3/s
- The CMB temperature is 2.725 +/- 0.001 K with photon density n_gamma = 410.5 cm^(-3)
- The dimensionless Hubble constant from WMAP is h = 0.705 +/- 0.013; from Planck h = 0.674 +/- 0.014
- The critical density from WMAP is rho_crit = (9.3 +/- 0.2) x 10^(-30) g/cm^3

## Context

This review extends the foundational BBN work of Wagoner (1967), Schramm and Turner (1998), and the observational constraints from WMAP (Komatsu 2011) and Planck (Ade 2014), while also covering stellar nucleosynthesis developments from the pp chain through explosive scenarios. The paper enables future work by summarizing open questions such as the lithium abundance problem and the need for improved cross section measurements, particularly noting that the np fusion cross section at BBN energies is known to about 3% precision and that higher precision may not be necessary for baryon density constraints.
