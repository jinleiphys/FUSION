# Phase-space densities and effects of resonance decays in hydrodynamic approach to heavy ion collisions

> A method allowing analysis of the overpopulation of phase-space in heavy ion collisions in a model independent way is proposed within the hydrodynamic approach. It makes it possible to extract a chemical potential of thermal pions at freeze out irrespective of the form of freeze-out (isothermal) hypersurface in Minkowski space and transverse flows on it. The contributions of resonance (with masses up to 2 GeV) decays to spectra, interferometry volumes and phase-space densities are calculated and discussed in detail. The estimates of average phase-space densities and chemical potentials of thermal pions are obtained for SPS and RHIC energies. They demonstrate that multibosonic phenomena at those energies might be considered as a correction factor rather than as a significant physical effect. The analysis of the evolution of the pion average phase-space density in chemically frozen hadron systems shows that it is almost constant or slightly increases with time while the particle density and phase-space density at each space point drops down rapidly during the system's expansion. We found that, unlike the particle density, the average phase-space density has no direct link to the freeze-out criterion and final thermodynamic parameters, being connected rather to the initial phase-space density of hadronic matter formed in relativistic nucleus-nucleus collisions.

## Key claim

The average phase-space density (APSD) of pions in chemically frozen hadron systems is approximately conserved or even slightly increases during the three-dimensional hydrodynamic expansion, unlike the particle density which drops rapidly; this behavior means that the final APSD reflects the initial phase-space density at hadronization rather than the freeze-out conditions, and that multibosonic effects at SPS and RHIC energies may be only a correction factor rather than a significant physical effect.

## Method

The authors propose a model-independent method within the hydrodynamic approach to extract the chemical potential of thermal pions at freeze-out, independent of the freeze-out hypersurface shape and transverse flows. They calculate the contributions of resonance decays (with masses up to 2 GeV) to pion spectra, interferometry volumes, and phase-space densities using a quasi-classical approximation for the emission function, based on a chemically frozen hadron-resonance gas model with parameters extracted from Pb+Pb collisions at 158 AGeV. The evolution of the APSD is analyzed using both analytically solved non-relativistic hydrodynamic models and a relativistic Bjorken-like model with transverse expansion, and the conservation of the totally averaged phase-space density is derived from thermodynamic identities and hydrodynamic equations.

## Key numbers

- For Pb+Pb SPS collisions at 158 AGeV, the chemical freeze-out parameters are: temperature T_ch = 0.164 GeV, baryonic chemical potential mu_B,ch = 0.224 GeV, proper time tau_ch = 7.24 fm, transverse radius R_ch = 4.63 fm, transverse velocity v_R,ch = 0.304, energy density epsilon_ch = 0.420 GeV/fm^3, and particle density n_ch = 0.421 1/fm^3.
- The APSD changes less than 10 percent during the evolution from hadronization to kinetic freeze-out for the SPS parameters, and at small transverse momenta the APSD is even higher at the final stage than at the initial stage.
- The particle density in the central part of the system drops by a factor of 6 between tau = 7.24 fm/c and tau = 15 fm/c, while the APSD changes not more than 35 percent and increases at small p_T.
- The kinetic freeze-out parameters for Pb+Pb at 158 AGeV are: T_th = 0.135 GeV, R(tau_th) = 5.3 fm, v_R = 0.4, and proper freeze-out time tau_th = 8.2 fm/c for direct pions.
- The mean mass of the chemically frozen hadron-resonance Boltzmann gas is 0.662 GeV, significantly higher than the hadronization temperature.
- For the RHIC estimate, the transverse flow velocity is enhanced from v_R = 0.4 to v_R = 0.6.

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamic analysis of chemically frozen hadron-resonance gas evolution from Akkelin, Sinyukov, and collaborators (2002) and addresses the HBT puzzle observed at RHIC (Pratt, 2002; Adamova, 2002) where interferometry volumes are smaller than expected from multiplicity scaling. The method enables model-independent extraction of pion chemical potentials at freeze-out, building on the Bertsch (1980) interferometry method for measuring phase-space densities, and allows quantitative assessment of whether multibosonic effects (Lednicky and Sinyukov, 1991; Akkelin, 1995) are relevant to the HBT puzzle. The analysis of resonance contributions extends the work of the Krakow group (2002) by including finite resonance widths and analyzing relative contributions to interferometry radii.
