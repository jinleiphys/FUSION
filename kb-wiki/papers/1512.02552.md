# General spin and pseudospin symmetries of the Dirac equation

> In the 70's Smith and Tassie, and Bell and Ruegg independently found SU(2) symmetries of the Dirac equation with scalar and vector potentials. These symmetries, known as pseudospin and spin symmetries, have been extensively researched and applied to several physical systems. Twenty years after, in 1997, the pseudospin symmetry has been revealed by Ginocchio as a relativistic symmetry of the atomic nuclei when it is described by relativistic mean field hadronic models. The main feature of these symmetries is the suppression of the spin-orbit coupling either in the upper or lower components of the Dirac spinor, thereby turning the respective second-order equations into Schrödinger-like equations, i.e, without a matrix structure. In this paper we propose a generalization of these SU(2) symmetries for potentials in the Dirac equation with several Lorentz structures, which also allow for the suppression of the matrix structure of second-order equation equation of either the upper or lower components of the Dirac spinor. We derive the general properties of those potentials and list some possible candidates, which include the usual spin-pseudospin potentials, and also 2- and 1-dimensional potentials. An application for a particular physical system in two dimensions, electrons in graphene, is suggested.

## Key claim

The paper generalizes the known SU(2) spin and pseudospin symmetries of the Dirac equation, previously restricted to scalar and vector potentials, to arbitrary Lorentz structures, provided the potential matrix \(O\) satisfies \(O^2=I\), anticommutes with \(\alpha_i\), and commutes with \(\Sigma_i\). The symmetries hold exactly only when one of the projected potentials \(V_\pm\) is constant, and the generators commute with the Hamiltonian only under those conditions.

## Method

The authors start from the time-independent Dirac equation with a general Hermitian potential \(V\) and introduce projectors \(P_\pm=(I\pm O)/2\) based on a matrix \(O\) that anticommutes with \(\alpha_i\). By setting one of the projected potentials \(V_+\) or \(V_-\) to a constant, they derive Schrödinger-like second-order equations for the upper or lower spinor component, respectively, and construct the corresponding SU(2) generators \(\mathbf{S}_\pm\). They verify the algebra and commutation with the Hamiltonian, then enumerate possible \(O\) matrices satisfying the required conditions, including \(\beta=\gamma^0\), \(i\gamma^0\gamma^5\), and, under weaker constraints, \(\boldsymbol{\lambda}\cdot\boldsymbol{\alpha}\) or \(i\beta\boldsymbol{\lambda}\cdot\boldsymbol{\alpha}\) for 2- and 1-dimensional systems.

## Key numbers

- The constant \(C_-\) (or \(C_+\)) that replaces one projected potential appears in the Schrödinger-like equation as \((E-C_-)(E-V_+)\) for the spin symmetry case and \((E-C_+)(E-V_-)\) for the pseudospin symmetry case.
- For the 2-dimensional case with \(O=\alpha_3\), the condition \(\hat{p}_3\,\psi=0\) is required, and the symmetry generator reduces to \(\Sigma_3\), a U(1) generator.
- For the 1-dimensional case with \(O=i\beta\alpha_z\), the condition \(\boldsymbol{\lambda}\times\hat{\mathbf{p}}\,\psi=0\) is required, with \(\boldsymbol{\lambda}=\hat{e}_z\) giving a potential depending only on \(z\).

## Context

This work extends the original SU(2) symmetries found by Smith and Tassie (1970s) and Bell and Ruegg (1970s) for scalar and vector potentials, and later applied to nuclear pseudospin symmetry by Ginocchio (1997). It also generalizes the spin symmetry used to explain suppressed spin-orbit splittings in heavy-light mesons. The results enable the study of these symmetries in systems with pseudoscalar potentials (e.g., baryons interacting with pions and omega mesons) and, in reduced dimensions, in graphene where massless Dirac electrons can realize the 2-dimensional symmetry with an \(\alpha_z\) potential.
