# A different nature between the radio AXPs in comparison to the others SGRs/AXPs

> SGRs/AXPs are considered a subclass of pulsars powered by magnetic energy and not by rotation, as normal radio pulsars. They are understood as strongly magnetized neutron star, with large periods of rotation $P\sim(2-12)$ s, and large spin-down, with typical $\dot{P}\sim(10^{-13}-10^{-10})$ s/s in contrast to $\dot{P}\sim10^{-15}$ s for ordinary pulsars. Their persistent X-ray luminosity, as well as the bursts and flares typical of these sources, are instead believed to be powered by the decay of their ultrastrong magnetic field. SGRs/AXPs typically have a larger X-ray luminosity that can not be explained by their spin-down luminosity ($L_X>\dot{E}_{\rm rot}$), unlike rotation-powered pulsars. However, the recent discovery of radio-pulsed emission in four of this class of sources, where the spin-down rotational energy lost $\dot{E}_{\rm rot}$ is larger than the X-ray luminosity $L_X$ during the quiescent state - as in normal pulsars - opens the question of the nature of these radio sources in comparison to the others of this class. In this contribution, we show that the radio SGRs/AXPs obey a linear log-log relation between $L_X$ and $\dot{E}_{\rm rot}$, very similar to the one satisfied by X-ray and gamma-ray neutron star pulsars, suggesting their neutron star nature. Furthermore, we show that almost all the high-B pulsars are also near the line found for the radio AXPs. In contrast, for almost all the others SGRs/AXPs, $\log L_X$ does not vary too much as function of $\log \dot{E}_{\rm rot}$, a phenomenology not shared by X-ray neutron star pulsars, suggesting a different nature for these sources.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the four radio-emitting SGRs/AXPs (XTE J1810-197, 1E 1547.0-5408, PSR J1622-4950, and SGR J1745-29) are rotation-powered neutron star pulsars like ordinary pulsars, while the remaining non-radio SGRs/AXPs may instead be rotation-powered magnetized white dwarfs, suggesting a fundamentally different nature between the two groups.

## Method

The authors plotted the quiescent X-ray luminosity \(L_X\) against the spin-down rotational energy loss \(\dot{E}_{\rm rot}\) for all known SGRs/AXPs and high-B pulsars, using data from the McGill SGR/AXP online catalog and published pulsar catalogs. They performed a log-log linear fit to the radio AXP data points and compared the resulting relation to the known X-ray neutron star pulsar line from Becker and Trumper. They also examined the X-ray efficiency \(\eta_X = L_X / \dot{E}_{\rm rot}\) for both groups.

## Key numbers

- The four radio AXPs obey a linear log-log relation: \(\log L_X = \log(6.2265 \times 10^7) + 0.7239 \log \dot{E}_{\rm rot}\), very similar to the X-ray neutron star pulsar line.
- The radio AXPs have X-ray efficiency \(\eta_X \sim (10^{-2} - 10^{-1})\), compared to normal pulsars where \(\eta_X \sim (10^{-3} - 10^{-4})\).
- The non-radio source CXO J1647 has rotational period \(P = 10.61\) s, an upper limit on period derivative \(\dot{P} < 4 \times 10^{-12}\) s/s, and X-ray luminosity \(L_X = 4.5 \times 10^{32}\) erg/s.
- For the white dwarf interpretation of non-radio SGRs/AXPs, the magnetic dipole moment range is \(10^{34} \leq m \leq 10^{36}\) emu, while radio AXPs as neutron stars have magnetic dipole moment \(m \sim 10^{32}\) emu.

## Context

This work extends the "fundamental plane for magnetars" proposed by Rea et al. (2012), which predicted radio emission only when quiescent X-ray luminosity is smaller than rotational energy loss. It contrasts with the standard magnetar model of Duncan and Thompson, where SGR/AXP emission is powered by magnetic field decay. The authors build on their previous work (Coelho and Malheiro 2012; Malheiro et al. 2012) suggesting that non-radio SGRs/AXPs could be massive magnetized white dwarfs, and they note that the radio AXPs are very similar to the high-B pulsars found by Kaspi and Olausen. The results enable a testable distinction between two populations: rotation-powered neutron stars (radio AXPs and high-B pulsars) versus possibly rotation-powered white dwarfs (the other SGRs/AXPs).
