# Laboratory Density Functionals

> We compare several definitions of the density of a self-bound system, such as a nucleus, in relation with its center-of-mass zero-point motion. A trivial deconvolution relates the internal density to the density defined in the laboratory frame. This result is useful for the practical definition of density functionals.

# Laboratory Density Functionals

## Key claim
The paper argues that a density functional theory (DFT) for self-bound nuclear systems can be formulated using laboratory-frame densities rather than internal densities, because the laboratory density contains the same information as the internal density via a simple deconvolution that corrects for center-of-mass zero-point motion. This claim is conditional on the Fourier transform of the laboratory density showing the expected Gaussian decay at large momenta; otherwise, the deconvolution will fail.

## Method
The author compares the laboratory-frame density ρ(r) and the internal density σ(ξ) for an A-nucleon system, where the center-of-mass (CM) is trapped by adding a harmonic oscillator term A ω² R²/2 to the Hamiltonian. Using the factorization of the ground state into a Gaussian CM wave function Γ(R) and an internal wave function ψ_int in Jacobi coordinates, the paper derives a convolution relation between ρ and σ, showing that ρ is obtained by smearing σ with the CM Gaussian. The author then argues that this convolution can be inverted via deconvolution, making the laboratory density suitable for DFT.

## Key numbers
- The rescaling factor relating the internal coordinate ξ_{A-1} to the single-nucleon coordinate r_A relative to the CM is A/(A-1), as given by ξ_{A-1} = (A/(A-1))(r_A - R).
- The convolution relation is ρ(r) = ∫ dR [Γ(R)]² σ[(A/(A-1))(r-R)], showing the CM Gaussian Γ(R) blurs the internal density.

## Context
This work extends the observation by Engel (2007) that standard nuclear Hamiltonians forbid a proper definition of nuclear density because the CM delocalizes in a plane wave. The paper provides a practical resolution by showing that laboratory densities can be used in DFT and then deconvolved to recover the internal density. This enables the construction of density functionals for self-bound systems using the more convenient laboratory-frame representation, with the caveat that expansions in harmonic oscillator functions constrained to satisfy matter conservation are recommended to avoid the deconvolution syndrome known from generator coordinate methods.
