# Bayesian averaging for ground state masses of atomic nuclei in a Machine Learning approach

> We present global predictions of the ground state mass of atomic nuclei based on a novel Machine Learning (ML) algorithm. We combine precision nuclear experimental measurements together with theoretical predictions of unmeasured nuclei. This hybrid data set is used to train a probabilistic neural network. In addition to training on this data, a physics-based loss function is employed to help refine the solutions. The resultant Bayesian averaged predictions have excellent performance compared to the testing set and come with well-quantified uncertainties which are critical for contemporary scientific applications. We assess extrapolations of the model's predictions and estimate the growth of uncertainties in the region far from measurements.

## Key claim

The authors claim that a Mixture Density Network trained on a hybrid dataset of high-precision experimental masses (AME2020) and lower-precision theoretical predictions (FRDM2012, WS4, HFB32), combined with a physics-based loss function enforcing Garvey-Kelson relations, produces global mass predictions with well-quantified uncertainties that extrapolate reasonably into unknown regions; however, the model's performance degrades for light nuclei (A < 50) and uncertainties grow substantially roughly ten neutron numbers beyond the last measured isotope.

## Method

The authors constructed a Mixture Density Network (MDN) with 6 hidden layers and 10 nodes per layer, using a nine-component physics-based input vector (Z, N, A, isospin asymmetry, even-odd indicators for Z/N/A, and valence nucleon numbers from closed shells). Training used approximately 500 nuclei from AME2020 (with 50 stochastic realizations each) plus 50 unmeasured nuclei from each of three theoretical mass models (FRDM2012, WS4, HFB32) with 20 realizations each, employing a total loss function that combines a log-likelihood data loss with a physics loss based on Garvey-Kelson mass relations. The network outputs a Gaussian mixture (single Gaussian for masses) whose posterior distribution is sampled thousands of times to produce mean mass predictions and uncertainties for any (Z,N) combination.

## Key numbers

* Training set of 506 AME2020 nuclei achieved σ_RMS = 0.279 MeV; the full AME2020 test set gave σ_RMS = 0.395 MeV, an increase of roughly 0.116 MeV.
* The theoretical models used for hybrid training had σ_RMS values (relative to AME2020) of 0.606 MeV (FRDM2012), 0.295 MeV (WS4), and 0.608 MeV (HFB32); the WS4 uncertainty was increased to 0.500 MeV for unmeasured nuclei.
* Average reported mass uncertainty in AME2020 is roughly 25 keV.
* The MDN model used 6 hidden layers, 10 hidden nodes per layer, learning rate 0.0002, weight-decay regularization 0.01, and physics loss strength λ_physics = 1.0.
* Calculations were limited to nuclei with A ≥ 50; lighter nuclei showed residuals on the order of several MeV while heavier nuclei showed residuals around 0.3 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the direct neural network approach to nuclear masses introduced by Lovell (2022) and Mumpower (2022), who showed that physically motivated input features and a physics-based loss function improve predictions. It contrasts with Gaussian Process model averaging (Neufcourt 2019), which reverts to the mean when extrapolating, and with residual-correction approaches (Utama 2016a) that are model-specific. The hybrid data approach combining experimental and theoretical masses enables predictions for unmeasured nuclei relevant to astrophysical applications such as r-process nucleosynthesis, though the neutron dripline remains poorly constrained.
