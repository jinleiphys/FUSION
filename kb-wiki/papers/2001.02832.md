# An {\it ab-initio} Gamow shell model approach with a core

> Gamow shell model (GSM) is usually performed within the Woods-Saxon (WS) basis in which the WS parameters need to be determined by fitting experimental single-particle energies including their resonance widths. In the multi-shell case, such a fit is difficult due to the lack of experimental data of cross-shell single-particle energies and widths. In this paper, we develop an {\it ab-initio} GSM by introducing the Gamow Hartree-Fock (GHF) basis that is obtained using the same interaction as the one used in the construction of the shell-model Hamiltonian. GSM makes use of the complex-momentum Berggren representation, then including resonance and continuum components. Hence, GSM gives a good description of weakly bound and unbound nuclei. Starting from chiral effective field theory and employing many-body perturbation theory (MBPT) (called nondegenerate $\hat Q$-box folded-diagram renormalization) in the GHF basis, a multi-shell Hamiltonian ({\it sd-pf} shells in this work) can be constructed. The single-particle energies and their resonance widths can also been obtained using MBPT. We investigated $^{23-28}$O and $^{23-31}$F isotopes, for which multi-shell calculations are necessary. Calculations show that continuum effects and the inclusion of the {\it pf} shell are important elements to understand the structure of nuclei close to and beyond driplines.

## Key claim
The paper develops an *ab-initio* Gamow shell model (GSM) that replaces the phenomenological Woods-Saxon basis with a Gamow Hartree-Fock (GHF) basis derived from the same chiral interaction used for the shell-model Hamiltonian, thereby avoiding the need to fit Woods-Saxon parameters to experimental single-particle energies and widths in multi-shell calculations.

## Method
The authors start from the chiral NNLO\(_{\text{opt}}\) nucleon-nucleon interaction and perform Gamow Hartree-Fock calculations in a plane-wave basis to generate a Berggren single-particle basis containing bound, resonant, and scattering states. Using many-body perturbation theory (the nondegenerate \(\hat{Q}\)-box folded-diagram renormalization method) within this GHF basis, they construct a multi-shell valence-space Hamiltonian for the \(sd\)-\(pf\) shells, with the one-body part (\(\hat{S}\)-box) taken to third order and the two-body part (\(\hat{Q}\)-box) to second order. The resulting complex-symmetric GSM Hamiltonian is diagonalized using the Jacobi-Davidson method, with \(^{22}\)O as the inert core.

## Key numbers
- The \(\nu 0d_{3/2}\) single-particle energy is 0.97 MeV with a resonance width of 0.11 MeV (experimental value: 1.26 MeV with unknown width).
- The \(\nu 1p_{3/2}\) single-particle energy is 2.60 MeV with a width of 5.12 MeV; the \(\nu 1p_{1/2}\) is 2.64 MeV with a width of 5.21 MeV.
- The \(\pi 0d_{5/2}\) single-particle energy is \(-11.71\) MeV (experimental: \(-13.29\) MeV); the \(\pi 1s_{1/2}\) is \(-7.81\) MeV; the \(\pi 0d_{3/2}\) is \(-7.17\) MeV.
- The continuum effect pulls the ground-state energy of \(^{26}\)O down by about 0.5 MeV and of \(^{28}\)O down by about 1 MeV.
- The \(^{28}\)O ground state has a particle-emission width of about several tens of keV.
- The \(^{26}\)O ground state has a resonance width of about 30 keV.
- The \(^{26}\)O first \(2^{+}\) excited state is reproduced; superposed resonant states with \(J^{\pi}=0^{-},1^{-},2^{-},3^{-}\) are predicted at energies around 2.8 MeV with widths around 5.2 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous GSM development (Sun 2017) that used a Woods-Saxon basis and the CD-Bonn potential, now replacing it with a fully microscopic GHF basis from chiral effective field theory. It provides the first systematic *ab-initio* GSM calculations with continuum coupling for neutron-rich oxygen and fluorine isotopes, addressing the "oxygen anomaly" and the fluorine dripline. The results enable future studies of weakly bound and unbound nuclei beyond the dripline, and the method can be extended to include three-nucleon forces and larger model spaces, though the computational cost currently limits full continuum treatment for nuclei heavier than \(^{29}\)F.
