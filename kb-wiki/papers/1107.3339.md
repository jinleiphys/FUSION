# Contribution of isovector mesons to the symmetry energy in a microscopic model

> We examine the potential energy contribution to the symmetry energy (in the parabolic approximation) arising from the isovector mesons, π, ρ, and δ. The significance of a microscopic model which incorporates all important mesons is revealed. In particular, we demonstrate the importance of the pion for a realistic investigation of isospin-sensitive systems.

# Contribution of isovector mesons to the symmetry energy in a microscopic model

## Key claim
The pion provides the largest contribution of any isovector meson to the symmetry energy in a microscopic, parameter-free Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculation, contrary to mean-field approaches that omit the pion. The magnitude and sign of each meson's contribution depend critically on the partial-wave structure of neutron matter versus symmetric nuclear matter, with the pion's effect being largest in models with stronger tensor forces.

## Method
The author performed Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) calculations of isospin asymmetric nuclear matter using the Bonn A, B, and C one-boson-exchange potentials, which differ primarily in the pion-nucleon form factor and tensor force strength. The in-medium reaction matrix (G-matrix) was solved self-consistently for neutrons and protons separately, with the single-particle potential parametrized in terms of scalar and vector fields. The potential energy contributions from individual isovector mesons (π, ρ, δ) were isolated by comparing calculations with and without each meson included in the interaction.

## Key numbers
- At density 0.185 fm⁻³ (k_F = 1.4 fm⁻¹ in SNM), the difference between potential energy contributions to neutron matter and symmetric nuclear matter from the pion is 20.78 MeV (Bonn B), 15.98 MeV (Bonn A), and 24.42 MeV (Bonn C).
- The corresponding difference for the ρ meson is -5.90 MeV (Bonn B), -4.68 MeV (Bonn A), and -5.48 MeV (Bonn C).
- The corresponding difference for the δ meson is -6.78 MeV (Bonn B), -2.80 MeV (Bonn A), and -10.24 MeV (Bonn C).
- The total symmetry energy at this density, including kinetic contributions, is 33.7 MeV.
- The pion contribution to symmetric nuclear matter potential energy is -17.08 MeV (Bonn B), -11.38 MeV (Bonn A), and -21.75 MeV (Bonn C).
- The ρ meson contribution to symmetric nuclear matter potential energy is 7.21 MeV (Bonn B), 6.18 MeV (Bonn A), and 6.47 MeV (Bonn C).
- The δ meson contribution to symmetric nuclear matter potential energy is 3.56 MeV (Bonn B), 1.57 MeV (Bonn A), and 5.13 MeV (Bonn C).

## Context
This work extends microscopic DBHF studies of asymmetric nuclear matter by the Idaho group (Sammarruca and collaborators, 2003, 2010) and contrasts with relativistic mean-field approaches such as QHD-I (Walecka, 1974) and QHD-II, which typically omit the pion. The results contradict earlier findings by DiToro and collaborators and Liu and collaborators, who reported much larger ρ and δ contributions (approximately -40 MeV and 50 MeV at saturation density) in mean-field calculations without pions. The paper enables more realistic modeling of isospin-sensitive systems including neutron stars and rare isotopes, and provides a benchmark for constraining the density dependence of the symmetry energy through heavy-ion collision experiments at GSI, MSU, and other facilities.
