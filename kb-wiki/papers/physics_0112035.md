# Reevaluation of the role of nuclear uncertainties in experiments on atomic parity violation with isotopic chains

> In light of new data on neutron distributions from experiments with antiprotonic atoms [ Trzcinska {\it et al.}, Phys. Rev. Lett. 87, 082501 (2001)], we reexamine the role of nuclear-structure uncertainties in the interpretation of measurements of parity violation in atoms using chains of isotopes of the same element. With these new nuclear data, we find an improvement in the sensitivity of isotopic chain measurements to ``new physics'' beyond the standard model. We compare possible constraints on ``new physics'' with the most accurate to date single-isotope probe of parity violation in the Cs atom. We conclude that presently isotopic chain experiments employing atoms with nuclear charges Z < 50 may result in more accurate tests of the weak interaction.

# Reevaluation of the role of nuclear uncertainties in experiments on atomic parity violation with isotopic chains

## Key claim

Using new empirical data on neutron distributions from antiprotonic atom experiments (Trzcinska et al., 2001), the nuclear-structure uncertainty in isotopic chain measurements of atomic parity violation is reduced, making such measurements competitive with single-isotope Cs determinations for atoms with nuclear charge Z less than about 50, but only for certain "new physics" scenarios, and assuming the experimental error in the ratio of parity-violating amplitudes can be made sufficiently small.

## Method

The authors re-derive the expression for the uncertainty in the "new physics" coupling parameter δF from isotopic ratio measurements, incorporating the empirical linear relation ΔR_np = (-0.04 ± 0.03) + (1.01 ± 0.15)(N-Z)/(N+Z) fm obtained from antiprotonic atom data. They compute δF for representative isotope pairs of Ba, Sm, Yb, and Pb using conservative error estimates from the empirical fit, and compare these to constraints derived from the 1% precision single-isotope Cs measurement for two illustrative new physics scenarios (extra Z' boson and isoscalar couplings). They then extend the analysis to atoms with Z between 40 and 82, identifying the Z range where nuclear uncertainties become subdominant.

## Key numbers

- ΔR_np = (-0.04 ± 0.03) + (1.01 ± 0.15)(N-Z)/(N+Z) fm, the empirical linear relation from antiprotonic atom data (Trzcinska et al., 2001)
- For heavy atoms (Z > 20), δΔR_n ≈ 0.03 fm, the isotope-independent uncertainty term dominating the error budget
- δF (nuclear uncertainty contribution) for Ba (Z=56, A=130/138): 6.2 × 10^{-3}
- δF for Sm (Z=62, A=144/154): 6.5 × 10^{-3}
- δF for Yb (Z=70, A=168/176): 12 × 10^{-3}
- δF for Pb (Z=82, A=204/208): 39 × 10^{-3}
- Single-isotope Cs constraint δF = 3.7 × 10^{-3} for extra Z' scenario (λ=0, h_u=0)
- Single-isotope Cs constraint δF = 5.9 × 10^{-3} for isoscalar scenario (λ=1, h_u=h_d)
- Required experimental accuracy in ratio of PNC amplitudes: approximately 0.3% for Ba and Sm, 0.2% for Yb, 0.1% for Pb
- The nuclear-structure uncertainty δF grows as Z^{8/3}

## Context

This work extends the earlier proposal by Dzuba, Flambaum, and Khrustalev (1986) to use isotopic ratios to cancel atomic-structure uncertainties in parity violation measurements, which was criticized by Fortson, Pang, and Wilets (1990) for its sensitivity to neutron distribution uncertainties. The authors use new empirical neutron skin data from antiprotonic atoms (Trzcinska et al., 2001) to replace the previously used model-dependent nuclear structure calculations, which gave differences in neutron radii varying by factors of four between relativistic and non-relativistic models. This enables a systematic search for suitable atomic systems with Z less than about 50, where the nuclear uncertainty becomes comparable to or smaller than the current 1% precision of the Cs single-isotope measurement (Wieman and co-workers, 1997/1999). The work also corrects an earlier claim by Xia, Moulton, and Young (1990) of enhanced parity-violating amplitude in Ba, finding instead that configuration interaction reduces the predicted enhancement by a factor of 30.
