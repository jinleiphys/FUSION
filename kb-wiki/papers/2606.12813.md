# Accidental Symmetry in the Tavis-Cummings Model via the Schwinger Boson Representation

> The Jaynes-Cummings (JC) Hamiltonian is a paradigmatic model of light-matter interaction and, more generally, qubit-boson interactions, widely used across atomic, optical, and superconducting qubit platforms. In the multi-qubit setting, where n qubits are identically coupled to a single boson mode, this interaction is known as the Tavis-Cummings (TC) Hamiltonian. The structure of the TC model is usually understood in terms of two standard symmetries: permutation invariance of the qubits and a U(1) symmetry associated with conservation of the total excitation number. Here we identify an additional, independent "accidental" symmetry of the TC Hamiltonian and construct the corresponding conserved observable. We show that, for n>2 qubits, this symmetry imposes strong constraints on the realizable unitary transformations. These constraints persist in the presence of the global $J_z$ Hamiltonian, but are removed by adding $J_z^2$, even though $J_z^2$ preserves both permutation invariance and the U(1) symmetry. Finally, we explain the origin of this previously unnoticed symmetry using Schwinger's boson representation of angular momentum. These restrictions have important implications for controllability of the TC system and for its applications to quantum computing, which are investigated further in a companion paper.

## Key claim

The Tavis-Cummings (TC) Hamiltonian possesses an additional "accidental" symmetry beyond the well-known permutation invariance and U(1) excitation-number conservation, which for systems with three or more qubits imposes nontrivial constraints on realizable unitary transformations that persist even in the presence of the global $J_z$ Hamiltonian but are removed by adding $J_z^2$.

## Method

The authors analyze the matrix elements of the TC Hamiltonian in the total angular momentum basis $\ket{j,m,\alpha}\otimes\ket{k}_{\text{osc}}$ and identify a linear transformation mixing the qubit quantum numbers $j$, $m$ with the bosonic occupation number $k$ that leaves these matrix elements invariant. They formalize this accidental symmetry by showing that for each pair of angular momenta $j > j' > 0$, there exists a unique pair of invariant subspaces (one unfilled sector with angular momentum $j$ and one filled sector with angular momentum $j'$) whose restrictions of the TC Hamiltonian are unitarily equivalent, with the equivalence explained via Schwinger's boson representation of angular momentum.

## Key numbers

* For $n \ge 3$ qubits, the accidental symmetry imposes nontrivial restrictions and conservation laws beyond the familiar permutational and U(1) symmetries.
* The number of pairs of sectors related by the accidental symmetry is $\frac{1}{2} \times \lceil n/2 \rceil \times (\lceil n/2 \rceil - 1)$; for $n=3$ qubits there is exactly one such pairwise equivalence ($j=3/2$ and $j'=1/2$), and for $n=6$ qubits there are three such pairs.
* The dimension of each paired subspace is $2j'+1$, where $j'$ is the smaller angular momentum in the pair.
* The difference in charges between paired sectors is $q' - q = 3(j - j')$, i.e., thrice the difference in angular momenta.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of the Tavis-Cummings model (Tavis and Cummings, 1968) beyond its two standard symmetries of permutation invariance and U(1) excitation-number conservation. The accidental symmetry has implications for controllability of qubit-bosonic systems coupled via TC interactions and for quantum computing applications, which are investigated further in companion papers by the same authors. The symmetry is explained using Schwinger's oscillator model of angular momentum (Schwinger, 1952; Sakurai and Napolitano, 2020).
