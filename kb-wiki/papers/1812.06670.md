# Study of the effect of newly calculated phase space factor on beta-decay half-lives

> We present results for $β$-decay half-lives based on a new recipe for calculation of phase space factors recently introduced. Our study includes $fp$-shell and heavier nuclei of experimental and astrophysical interests. The investigation of the kinematics of some $β$-decay half-lives is presented, and new phase space factor values are compared with those obtained with previous theoretical approximations. Accurate calculation of nuclear matrix elements is a pre-requisite for reliable computation of $β$-decay half-lives and is not the subject of this paper. This paper explores if improvements in calculating the $β$-decay half-lives can be obtained when using a given set of nuclear matrix elements and employing the new values of the phase space factors. Although the largest uncertainty in half-lives computations come from the nuclear matrix elements, introduction of the new values of the phase space factors may improve the comparison with experiment. The new half-lives are systematically larger than previous calculations and may have interesting consequences for calculation of stellar rates.

## Key claim

The paper investigates whether using newly calculated, more rigorous phase space factors (PSFs) can improve the agreement of theoretical beta-decay half-lives with experimental data, while explicitly stating that the largest uncertainty comes from nuclear matrix elements (NMEs) which are not the focus of this work. The new half-lives are systematically larger than previous calculations, and the improvement from the new PSFs is modest, with the authors noting that further improvement would require more reliable NMEs.

## Method

The authors computed beta-decay half-lives for fp-shell and heavier nuclei using the proton-neutron quasi-particle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) model in a deformed basis with a separable schematic potential, employing Nilsson model single-particle states and BCS pairing. They calculated phase space factors by solving the Dirac equation numerically with a realistic Coulomb potential derived from a shell-model proton density distribution, including screening effects via the Thomas-Fermi equation, and compared these new PSFs with the conventional Gove and Martin (1971) approximation. The same set of NMEs was used for both PSF recipes to isolate the effect of the PSF calculation on the half-lives.

## Key numbers

* For the 28 nuclei studied, the newly calculated half-lives are systematically larger than those computed with the Gove and Martin (1971) PSFs; the mean percentage deviation is 2.35% for beta+/EC decay and 2.73% for beta- decay.
* The maximum percentage deviation for beta+/EC decay is 4.05% for the nucleus 56Ni, while the smallest difference was noted for 52Fe.
* For beta- decay, the largest percentage deviation is 3.82% for 100Sr and 2.04% for 152Nd.
* For virtual nuclei, the log10(PSF) differences between the two methods range from about 0.003 to 0.202, with the largest deviations occurring for high-Z, low-Q cases (e.g., Z=90, A=230, Q=0.05 MeV: log10(F) = -7.088 vs -7.272 for beta+; -1.857 vs -2.026 for beta-).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous calculation of phase space factors for positron decay and electron capture (Sab16) to include beta- decay reactions, and applies them to a broader set of nuclei of astrophysical interest. It compares the new PSF results with the standard approximation of Gove and Martin (1971), which used point-nucleus Coulomb potentials with empirical corrections for finite size and screening. The results suggest that the new PSFs can modestly improve half-life predictions, and the authors plan to extend this work to stellar beta-decay rates for astrophysical applications.
