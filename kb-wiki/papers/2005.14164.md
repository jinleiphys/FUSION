# Equation of state constraints from nuclear physics, neutron star masses, and future moment of inertia measurements

> We explore constraints on the equation of state (EOS) of neutron-rich matter based on microscopic calculations up to nuclear densities and observations of neutron stars. In a previous work we showed that predictions based on modern nuclear interactions derived within chiral effective field theory and the observation of two-solar-mass neutron stars result in a robust uncertainty range for neutron star radii and the EOS over a wide range of densities. In this work we extend this study, employing both the piecewise polytrope extension from Hebeler et al. as well as the speed of sound model of Greif et al., and show that moment of inertia measurements of neutron stars can significantly improve the constraints on the EOS and neutron star radii.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that future moment of inertia measurements of neutron stars, combined with chiral effective field theory calculations up to 1.1 times nuclear saturation density and the existence of two-solar-mass neutron stars, can reduce the allowed range for neutron star radii by about 50% or more, depending on the measured value of the moment of inertia.

## Method

The authors combine microscopic calculations of neutron-rich matter up to density 1.1 rho_0 (where rho_0 = 2.8 x 10^14 g/cm^3) based on chiral effective field theory interactions with two different high-density extensions: a piecewise polytrope model following Hebeler et al. (2013) and a speed-of-sound parameterization following Greif et al. (2019). They impose a minimum neutron star mass constraint of 2.05 solar masses (the 68% lower limit from PSR J0740+6620), causality constraints (speed of sound less than speed of light), and then explore how hypothetical moment of inertia measurements with 10% and 20% relative uncertainty would further constrain the equation of state and neutron star radii for masses of 1.338, 2.0, and 2.4 solar masses.

## Key numbers

* For a 1.338 solar mass neutron star (PSR J0737-3039A), the moment of inertia is predicted to be in the range I = 53.2 to 85.7 solar masses km^2 from the piecewise polytrope extension.
* With a 10% moment of inertia measurement at I_c = 70 solar masses km^2 (intermediate case) for a 1.338 solar mass star, the radius range narrows to R = 11.3-12.9 km, compared to R = 10.2-13.6 km without moment of inertia information.
* For a 1.338 solar mass star with I_c = 55 solar masses km^2 (low case) and 10% uncertainty, the radius range is R = 10.2-11.4 km (piecewise polytrope) or R = 10.4-11.5 km (speed of sound model).
* For a 1.338 solar mass star with I_c = 85 solar masses km^2 (high case) and 10% uncertainty, the radius range is R = 12.5-13.6 km (piecewise polytrope) or R = 12.6-13.2 km (speed of sound model).
* The speed of sound must reach values around 0.65 times the speed of light to be compatible with two-solar-mass neutron stars; no equation of state with c_s/c less than or equal to 1/sqrt(3) (approximately 0.577) for all densities can support such heavy stars.
* The moment of inertia can reach values up to about 290 solar masses km^2 for very heavy neutron stars.

## Context

This work extends the previous framework of Hebeler et al. (2013) and Greif et al. (2019) by incorporating the updated mass measurement of PSR J0740+6620 at 2.14 solar masses from Cromartie et al. (2019) and systematically exploring how future moment of inertia measurements (particularly for the double pulsar system PSR J0737-3039) can improve equation of state constraints. The predicted moment of inertia range for a 1.338 solar mass star (53.2-85.7 solar masses km^2) is significantly narrower than the range of 21.1-113.2 solar masses km^2 found by Raithel et al. (2016), and similar to the 60.3-90.5 solar masses km^2 range from Gordon et al. (2016). The results are consistent with gravitational-wave constraints from GW170817 from LIGO/Virgo (2019) and enable future measurements from NICER and LIGO/Virgo to further narrow the equation of state in a model-independent way.
