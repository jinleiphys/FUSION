# Disoriented Chiral Condensate Formation in Heavy Ion Collisions?

> One of the main aims of present and upcoming high energy heavy ion collision experiments is to study new phases of matter at extreme temperature and density. It is expected that a nontrivial classical pion field configuration can occasionally form during the out-of-equilibrium chiral phase transition. We have recently shown that, contrarily to what has been assumed so far, this configuration is not identical to the so-called disoriented chiral condensate (DCC), proposed in the early 1990's. A detailed analysis reveals that a more realistic picture is that of an ``unpolarized'' DCC, where the Fourier modes of the field have completely independent orientations in isospin space instead of being aligned with each other as in the original DCC. This has important implications concerning the possible detection of the phenomenon. In particular, the main expected signature of the original DCC, which is used in most experimental searches, is absent in the unpolarized case. We point out that the fact that no evidence of DCC formation has been reported so far in nuclear collisions actually agrees with our present theoretical understanding. New experimental strategies should be designed to look for the unpolarized DCC in existing data from SPS as well as in future searches at RHIC and LHC.

# Disoriented Chiral Condensate Formation in Heavy Ion Collisions?

## Key claim
Contrary to the long-standing assumption that a disoriented chiral condensate (DCC) formed during the out-of-equilibrium chiral phase transition consists of Fourier modes aligned in a common isospin direction, the actual configuration produced in the quench scenario is an "unpolarized" DCC where each mode has a completely independent orientation in isospin space. This means the primary experimental signature used in all searches to date, the inverse square root law for neutral pion fraction fluctuations, is absent, and the failure to observe DCC signals is consistent with current theory.

## Method
The author performed a detailed statistical analysis of classical pion field configurations produced by numerically solving the equations of motion of the O(4) linear sigma model on a lattice, using the quench scenario of Rajagopal and Wilczek (1993). Initial field configurations were sampled from Gaussian distributions with variances smaller than those of the high-temperature phase, and the exact time evolution of each configuration was followed to study the isospin structure of amplified Fourier modes on an event-by-event basis.

## Key numbers
- The probability of forming an "observable" DCC in central Pb-Pb collisions at CERN SPS energies is bounded by Proba ≤ 10^{-3}, where "observable" means the number of DCC pions exceeds typical multiplicity fluctuations (Krzywicki, 1998).
- The linear sigma model parameters were chosen so that m_π = 135 MeV, m_σ = 600 MeV, and f_π = 92.5 MeV.
- The neutral fraction distribution for a single amplified mode at final time t_f = 10 fm is given by P(f) = 1/2 [F_Ω(f) + F_{-Ω}(f)], where Ω = (4 + ω_k²)/(4 − ω_k²) and ω_k is the free pion dispersion relation.
- The neutral fraction distribution in a bin containing only 10 amplified modes already shows considerable reduction of the expected DCC signal compared to the single-mode case.

## Context
This work extends the quench scenario for DCC formation originally proposed by Rajagopal and Wilczek (1993), which was based on the earlier idealized DCC picture of Blaizot and Krzywicki (1992) and Bjorken (1991). The author resolves a long-standing paradox noted by Gavin (1993) that simulations of the out-of-equilibrium phase transition never produced the large neutral-fraction fluctuations predicted by the original DCC model. The results imply that experimental searches at SPS (WA98, NA49), the Tevatron (MiniMax), and future searches at RHIC and LHC must design new strategies sensitive to the unpolarized nature of the DCC, rather than relying on the inverse square root law for total neutral pion fraction.
