# Internal Heating in Magnetars: Role of Electron Captures

> The role of electron captures by nuclei in the shallow heating of magnetars is further investigated using both nuclear measurements and the theoretical atomic mass table HFB-27. Starting from the composition of the outer crust in full equilibrium, we have calculated the onset of electron captures and the heat released due to the slow decay of the magnetic field. Numerical results are found to be similar to those previously obtained with the HFB-24 atomic mass model and are consistent with neutron-star cooling data.

## Key claim

Electron captures by nuclei in the outer crust of magnetars, driven by slow magnetic-field decay, can provide a robust source of internal heating even if parts of the crust are melted, and the maximum heat released is essentially determined by nuclear data alone, independent of the magnetic field strength.

## Method

The authors calculated the onset pressure for electron captures and the associated heat release using nuclear masses from the 2016 Atomic Mass Evaluation, excitation energies from the IAEA Nuclear Data section, and theoretical masses from the HFB-27 atomic mass model, assuming an initial outer-crust composition in full thermodynamic equilibrium and an extremely quantizing magnetic field with dimensionless strength B_star = 2000. They compared these results with earlier calculations using the HFB-24 mass model and considered both ground-state-to-ground-state and ground-state-to-excited-state transitions, as well as pycnonuclear fusion of light elements (carbon and oxygen).

## Key numbers

- Heat released per nucleon for the reaction 12C → 12Be: ~0.14 MeV (ground-state-to-ground-state transition, determined by experimental data).
- Heat released per nucleon for the reaction 16O → 16C: ~0.15 MeV (ground-state-to-ground-state transition, determined by experimental data).
- Heat released per nucleon for the reaction 62Cr → 62Ti: ~0.09 MeV (ground-state-to-ground-state transition).
- Heat released per nucleon for the reaction 82Ge → 82Zn: ~0.1 MeV (ground-state-to-excited-state transition, determined by experimental data).
- Heat released per nucleon for the reaction 88Sr → 88Kr: ~0.08 MeV (ground-state-to-excited-state transition, determined by experimental data).
- Heat released per nucleon for the reaction 88Kr → 88Se: ~0.07 MeV (ground-state-to-excited-state transition, determined by experimental data).
- Heat released per nucleon for the reaction 86Kr → 86Se: ~0.09 MeV (ground-state-to-excited-state transition, determined by experimental data).
- Maximum heat released per nucleon from electron captures: ~0.1 MeV.
- Maximum heat released per nucleon from pycnonuclear fusions: ~1–2 MeV.
- Density range for heat deposition: ρ_beta ~ 10^10–10^11 g cm^-3 (pressure P_beta ~ 10^29–10^30 dyn cm^-2) for the magnetic field considered.
- Estimated heat power: W^∞ ~ 10^35–10^36 erg/s.
- Characteristic time for electron captures: τ ~ a few thousand years (same order as kinematic age of magnetars).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Chamel 2021) by using the HFB-27 atomic mass model instead of HFB-24, finding similar results for the main heat sources but significant differences for minor reactions such as 56Ti → 56Ca and 80Zn → 80Ni. The heating mechanism is analogous to crustal heating in accreting neutron stars (Haensel 1990), with compression induced by magnetic-field decay rather than accretion. The density range where most heat is released matches that inferred from magnetar cooling simulations (Kaminker 2006, Kaminker 2009), and the estimated heat power is consistent with those simulations.
