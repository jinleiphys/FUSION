# Neutron-induced astrophysical reaction rates for translead nuclei

> Neutron-induced reaction rates, including fission, are calculated in the temperature range 1.d8 <T (K) < 1.d10 within the framework of the statistical model for targets with atomic number 83 < Z < 119 (from Po to Uuo) from the neutron to the proton drip-line. Four sets of rates have been calculated, utilizing - where possible - consistent nuclear data for neutron separation energies and fission barriers from Thomas-Fermi (TF), Extended Thomas-Fermi plus Strutinsky Integral (ETFSI), Finite-Range Droplet Model (FRDM) and Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov (HFB) predictions. Tables of calculated values as well as analytic seven parameter fits in the standard REACLIB format are supplied. We also discuss the sensitivity of the rates to the input, aiming at a better understanding of the uncertainties introduced by the nuclear input.

## Key claim

Neutron-induced reaction rates (including fission and neutron capture) for translead nuclei (Z=84 to 118) from the neutron to proton drip-line are calculated using the statistical Hauser-Feshbach model, but the authors caution that the rates are purely based on the statistical model and that at shell closures and low neutron separation energies the level densities become too small for the model to be applicable, requiring future separate study of direct reaction and resonant contributions.

## Method

The authors applied the Wolfenstein-Hauser-Feshbach statistical formalism for compound nuclear reactions to compute neutron-induced cross sections and reaction rates, including both (n,gamma) and fission channels, for targets with atomic numbers 84 to 118. Four sets of rates were calculated using consistent nuclear data for neutron separation energies and fission barriers from four different global models: Thomas-Fermi (TF), Extended Thomas-Fermi plus Strutinsky Integral (ETFSI), Finite-Range Droplet Model (FRDM), and Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov (HFB-14). Fission transmission coefficients were evaluated using a double-humped fission barrier approach with Hill-Wheeler inverted parabola shapes, and level densities were obtained from a back-shifted Fermi-gas model with parameters derived from the corresponding mass model.

## Key numbers

- Temperature range for rate calculations: 10^8 K to 10^10 K (1.d8 < T(K) < 1.d10)
- Atomic number range of targets: Z=84 to Z=118 (from Po to Uuo)
- For experimentally known nuclei near stability, agreement between calculated and measured (n,gamma) cross sections is within a factor of 1.5
- For neutron-induced fission cross sections using experimental fission barriers, agreement with evaluated data is within factors of two to three for most cases, with outliers up to a factor of ten
- When using purely theoretical fission barrier predictions (no experimental input), calculated fission cross sections can differ from measured values by up to a factor of 10^4
- For the nucleus 262U, the ETFSI model gives B_f1 = 3.9 MeV, B_f2 = 5.30 MeV, S_n = 4.46 MeV; the TF model gives B_f1 = 1.20 MeV, B_f2 = 4.56 MeV, S_n = 4.05 MeV
- Rate sets based on ETFSI versus TF or FRDM+TF predictions can differ by up to eight orders of magnitude for nuclei with neutron numbers near N=184
- For 58 isotopes from Th to Fm, Maxwellian-averaged fission cross sections at 30 keV were compared to JENDL-3.3 evaluated data

## Context

This work extends the earlier rate sets of Rauscher and Thielemann (2000) for (n,gamma) reactions to elements with Z>83 and completes the earlier work of Panov et al. (2005) by providing neutron-induced fission rates for the full translead region up to Z=118. The paper provides four complete sets of rates using different mass and fission barrier predictions (TF, ETFSI, FRDM, HFB-14) to allow nucleosynthesis studies to explore the variation range, which is essential because the astrophysical site of the r-process remains unknown. The rates are intended for studying the endpoint of the r-process and the possible production of superheavy elements, and the authors note that neutron-induced fission has been shown recently to be more important than beta-delayed fission in r-process nucleosynthesis, contrary to earlier assumptions.
