# Monte Carlo simulations on the Lefschetz thimble: taming the sign problem

> We present the first practical Monte Carlo calculations of the recently proposed Lefschetz thimble formulation of quantum field theories. Our results provide strong evidence that the numerical sign problem that afflicts Monte Carlo calculations of models with complex actions can be softened significantly by changing the domain of integration to the Lefschetz thimble or approximations thereof. We study the interacting complex scalar field theory (relativistic Bose gas) in lattices of size up to 8^4 using a computationally inexpensive approximation of the Lefschetz thimble. Our results are in excellent agreement with known results. We show that - at least in the case of the relativistic Bose gas - the thimble can be systematically approached and the remaining residual phase leads to a much more tractable sign problem (if at all) than the original formulation. This is especially encouraging in view of the wide applicability - in principle - of our method to quantum field theories with a sign problem. We believe that this opens up new possibilities for accurate Monte Carlo calculations in strongly interacting systems of sizes much larger that previously possible.

## Key claim
The paper presents the first practical Monte Carlo calculations using the Lefschetz thimble formulation, showing that the sign problem for the relativistic Bose gas can be significantly softened by integrating on the thimble or its approximations, but the results are restricted to small lattices (up to 8^4) and the method relies on a computationally inexpensive approximation of the true thimble.

## Method
The authors study the interacting complex scalar field theory (relativistic Bose gas) in 3+1 dimensions on lattices of size 4^4, 6^4, and 8^4. They implement an Aurora Monte Carlo algorithm that generates configurations on the tangent space G_0 (the quadratic approximation to the Lefschetz thimble) using Langevin dynamics with Gaussian noise projected onto the tangent space, regularized by discarding the rare diverging trajectories. They also explore moving closer to the true thimble by integrating the steepest descent equations using a 4th order Runge-Kutta method.

## Key numbers
- Lattice volumes studied: 4^4, 6^4, and 8^4
- Mass and coupling fixed at m = 1 = lambda; chemical potential mu varied from 0 to 1.3
- Divergence probability on G_0: approximately 1.8% on 4^4, 0.8% on 6^4, and less than 0.7% on 8^4 (with h = 5 x 10^{-3})
- Langevin time step Delta t = 10^{-4} used for main simulations, with tests at 10^{-3} and 10^{-5} showing no significant difference
- For tau = 4 x 10^{-2}, fluctuations of the imaginary part of the action S_I were suppressed by a factor of approximately 0.5 (4^4), 0.6 (6^4), and 0.7 (8^4) at mu = 1.2
- Clear signal of transition observed around mu approximately 1.1

## Context
This work extends the Lefschetz thimble formulation proposed by Cristoforetti et al. (2012) from a theoretical framework to a practical Monte Carlo method. It directly addresses the sign problem that plagues lattice QCD at high baryonic density, real-time quantum field theories, and the repulsive Hubbard model. The results are validated against alternative methods for the relativistic Bose gas including the Worm Algorithm of Gattringer and Kloiber (2012) and the complex Langevin approach of Aarts (2008). The method enables future applications to larger systems and other models with sign problems, though the authors note that more efficient integration algorithms will be needed for larger volumes.
