# Half lives of spherical proton emitters using density dependent M3Y interaction

> The proton radioactivity lifetimes of spherical proton emitters from the ground and the isomeric states are calculated using the microscopic nucleon-nucleus interaction potentials. The daughter nuclei density distributions are folded with a realistic density dependent M3Y effective interaction supplemented by a zero-range pseudo-potential. The density dependence parameters of the interaction are extracted from the nuclear matter calculations. The saturation energy per nucleon used for nuclear matter calculations is determined from the co-efficient of the volume term of Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula which is evaluated by fitting the recent experimental and estimated atomic mass excesses from Audi-Wapstra-Thibault atomic mass table by minimizing the mean square deviation. The quantum mechanical tunneling probability is calculated within the WKB approximation. Spherical charge distributions are used for calculating the Coulomb interaction potentials. These calculations provide good estimates for the observed proton radioactivity lifetimes.

# Half lives of spherical proton emitters using density dependent M3Y interaction

## Key claim
The authors calculate proton radioactivity lifetimes for spherical proton emitters using microscopic nucleon-nucleus interaction potentials obtained by single folding daughter density distributions with a density-dependent M3Y effective interaction, and find good agreement with experimental data across a wide range of nuclei, though the agreement is not exact, with some calculated half-lives differing from experimental values by up to about one order of magnitude.

## Method
The authors first determine the volume energy coefficient of the Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula by fitting theoretical mass excesses to 3179 experimental and extrapolated atomic mass excesses from the Audi-Wapstra-Thibault atomic mass table, minimizing the root mean square deviation. They then use this volume coefficient as the saturation energy per nucleon for nuclear matter calculations, from which they extract the density dependence parameters (C and β) of the DDM3Y effective interaction. The nuclear potential for each proton-daughter system is obtained by single folding the daughter nucleus density distribution (described by a spherically symmetric Fermi function) with the DDM3Y interaction. The total interaction energy includes nuclear, Coulomb (from a spherical charge distribution), and centrifugal terms, and the proton decay half-life is computed using the WKB approximation for barrier penetration, with zero-point vibration energies taken from an empirical law extended to protons.

## Key numbers
- Volume energy coefficient: a_v = 15.2602 ± 0.0196 MeV, obtained from fitting mass excesses of 3179 nuclei
- Surface energy coefficient: a_s = 16.2673 ± 0.0621 MeV
- Coulomb energy coefficient: a_c = 0.688974 ± 0.001357 MeV
- Symmetry energy coefficient: a_sym = 22.2090 ± 0.0481 MeV
- Pairing energy coefficient: a_p = 10.0757 ± 0.8536 MeV
- Nuclear matter saturation density: ρ_0 = 0.1533 fm^{-3}
- Density dependence parameters: C = 2.07, β = 1.668 fm^2
- Nuclear matter incompressibility: K_0 = 293.4 MeV
- For 185Bi (ground state, l=0, Q=1.624 MeV): experimental log10(T) = -4.229 s, calculated log10(T) = -5.43 s
- For 109I (l=2, Q=0.829 MeV): experimental log10(T) = -4.000 s, calculated log10(T) = -4.25 s
- For 147Tm (isomeric state, l=2, Q=1.139 MeV): experimental log10(T) = -3.444 s, calculated log10(T) = -3.38 s

## Context
This work extends the application of density-dependent M3Y effective interactions to proton radioactivity, following earlier studies of cluster radioactivity using similar microscopic potentials. The calculated half-lives are compared with experimental data from Audi and collaborators (2005) and with results from the unified fission model (UFM) and distorted wave Born approximation (DWBA) estimates from the same reference. The approach provides a theoretically grounded alternative to phenomenological models for calculating proton emission lifetimes, and the good agreement with data suggests the method can be used to predict half-lives for yet-unobserved proton emitters near the proton drip line.
