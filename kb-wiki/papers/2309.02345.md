# Nontrivial features in the speed of sound inside neutron stars

> Measurements of neutron star masses, radii, and tidal deformability have direct connections to nuclear physics via the equation of state (EoS), which for the cold, catalyzed matter in neutron star cores is commonly represented as the pressure as a function of energy density. Microscopic models with exotic degrees of freedom display nontrivial structure in the speed of sound ($c_s$) in the form of first-order phase transitions and bumps, oscillations, and plateaus in the case of crossovers and higher-order phase transitions. We present a procedure based on Gaussian processes to generate an ensemble of EoSs that include nontrivial features. Using a Bayesian analysis incorporating measurements from X-ray sources, gravitational wave observations, and perturbative QCD results, we show that these features are compatible with current constraints. We investigate the possibility of a global maximum in $c_s$ that occurs within the densities realized in neutron stars -- implying a softening of the EoS and possibly an exotic phase in the core of massive stars -- and find that such a global maximum is consistent with, but not required by, current constraints.

## Key claim

The authors find that nontrivial features in the speed of sound (such as bumps, plateaus, and spikes) are compatible with current observational constraints from X-ray sources, gravitational waves, and perturbative QCD, but such features are not required by the data; a global maximum in the speed of sound occurring within neutron star densities is consistent with but not demanded by current constraints.

## Method

The authors generate ensembles of equations of state using Gaussian processes (GPs) with a squared-exponential kernel and a linear mean function for the auxiliary variable phi as a function of log10 pressure. They then introduce modified Gaussian processes (mGPs) that add specific nontrivial features (bumps, plateaus, spikes) to a baseline GP to mimic the behavior of nuclear physics models with exotic degrees of freedom. Both families of EoS are subjected to Bayesian parameter estimation incorporating measurements from NICER X-ray observations of PSR J0030-0451 and PSR J0740+6620, gravitational wave tidal deformability constraints from GW170817 and GW190425, the existence of heavy pulsars, and perturbative QCD consistency constraints.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: n_sat = 0.16 fm^{-3}
- The GP mean function uses slope m = 2 for the "hard GP" and m = 1.6 for the "soft GP" over the domain 32.7 <= log10 p (erg cm^{-3}) <= 37
- The squared-exponential kernel uses hyperparameters l = sigma = 1 for both benchmark GP models
- White-noise variance sigma^2_wn = 0.0003 is added for numerical stability
- The GP domain extends to log10 p = 37 (instead of 36 in prior work) with 100 sampling points (instead of 50)
- Perturbative QCD constraints apply at densities n_B >= 40 n_sat where c_s^2 approaches 1/3 from below
- Chiral effective field theory breaks down at densities around 2 n_sat
- The core-crust transition is set at 0.5 n_sat

## Context

This work extends the Gaussian process approach to neutron star EoS inference developed by Miller et al. (2021) and others (Landry 2018, Essick 2019, Legred 2021) by introducing modified GPs that can capture multiscale correlations and sharp features seen in microscopic models such as chiral mean field theory (CMF) models with delta resonances, hyperons, and quarks (Dexheimer 2008-2022), the QHC19 EoS (Baym 2019), and relativistic density-functional approaches (Ivanytskyi 2022). The paper addresses concerns raised by Tan et al. (2020) and Legred et al. (2022) that standard parametric representations and GPs with fixed correlation lengths may be unable to capture behavior consistent with nuclear physics models containing exotic degrees of freedom. The results enable future work to determine whether additional observational data can discriminate between EoS with and without nontrivial features in the speed of sound.
