# Neutrino mass and Extreme Value Distributions in $β$-decay

> We propose a new application of the Extreme Value Theory for distributions with compact support. The novelty of our proposal is the use of these tools to estimate the neutrino mass from the energy spectrum of electrons in $β$-decay. In this way the dependence of the result on the mass of the neutrino is considerably enhanced increasing the sensitivity of the experiment.

## Key claim

The authors propose that applying Extreme Value Theory (EVT) to the highest-energy electron from a set of \(n\) beta decays enhances the sensitivity to the neutrino mass by a factor \(n^{2/3}\), making it possible to probe neutrino masses one order of magnitude lower than current bounds, provided the experimental conditions (such as those of the KATRIN experiment) allow for \(n\) around \(10^{12}\).

## Method

The authors apply EVT to the electron energy spectrum from tritium beta decay. They consider the distribution of the maximum electron energy among \(n\) independent decays, rescale the variable as \(x' = x^{n^{-1/3}}\) to obtain a nontrivial limit, and derive the limiting cumulative distribution \(M(x) = e^{-\lambda (-\log x)^\alpha}\). They then expand the cumulative distribution for a small neutrino mass \(y = m_\nu c^2/(E_0 - m_e c^2)\) to first order in \(y^2\), obtaining an expression for \(M_n(x)\) and the probability density \(P_n(x)\) that depends on the combination \(n^{2/3} y^2\).

## Key numbers

* For tritium beta decay, the end point kinetic energy is \(\epsilon_0 = 18.560\) keV (from Otten).
* The parameter \(\lambda\) for the massless neutrino case is \(\lambda = a(1)/3 \approx 2.04208\).
* For \(n = 10^{12}\) and a neutrino mass of \(m_\nu/m_e = 10^{-6}\) (corresponding to \(y = 2.8 \times 10^{-5}\)), the expansion parameter is \(n^{2/3} y^2 = 0.078\).
* The expectation value of the rescaled variable for zero neutrino mass is \(\langle x^{n^{1/3}} \rangle_{y=0} \approx 0.510864\); the correction for nonzero mass is \(-0.496771\, n^{2/3} y^2\).
* For a neutrino mass of \(m_\nu \sim 0.5\) eV, a relative precision of 50% in a single measurement requires an energy resolution \(\Delta E \sim 0.88\) eV and \(n = 2 \times 10^{13}\) (one extreme event every 200 seconds for a \(10^{11}\) Bq source).
* The authors state that with \(n \approx 10^{12}\), neutrino mass upper bounds of the order of 0.25 eV could be obtained.

## Context

This work extends the application of Extreme Value Theory for compact-support distributions, previously developed by the same authors (Esteve and Falceto), to the specific problem of neutrino mass determination from beta decay. It directly addresses the challenge that the electron spectrum near the endpoint depends only quadratically on the neutrino mass, making direct measurements difficult. The method is proposed as a new analysis strategy for experiments like KATRIN (KATRIN1, KATRIN2) and could enable probing neutrino masses one order of magnitude below current limits, though going further would require increasing \(n\) by a factor of \(10^3\).
