# Paths to Superheavy Nuclei

> This document summarizes the discussions and outcomes of the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams Theory Alliance (FRIB-TA) topical program "The path to Superheavy Isotopes" held in June 2024 at FRIB. Its content is non-exhaustive, reflecting topics chosen and discussed by the participants. The program aimed to assess the current status of theory in superheavy nuclei (SHN) research and identify necessary theoretical developments to guide experimental programs and determine fruitful production mechanisms. This report details the intersection of SHN research with other fields, provides an overview of production mechanisms and theoretical models, discusses future needs in theory and experiment, explores other potential avenues for SHN synthesis, and highlights the importance of building a strong theory community in this area.

## Key claim

This white paper from the FRIB-TA topical program (June 2024) assesses that while superheavy nuclei (SHN) with Z >= 104 are expected to have a region of enhanced stability near Z ~ 114, N ~ 184, the exact degree of stability and the full extent of this "peninsula" remain unknown, and experimental advances guided by theory are required to understand it.

## Method

The document summarizes discussions from a topical program held at FRIB in June 2024, providing a non-exhaustive overview of production mechanisms (hot fusion, cold fusion, multinucleon transfer) and theoretical models (Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock, stochastic dynamics, Fusion by Diffusion, Dinuclear System model, Coupled Channels) used in superheavy nuclei research. The report identifies necessary theoretical developments to guide experimental programs, including improvements to capture, compound nucleus formation, and survival probability calculations.

## Key numbers

* The heaviest element produced, Oganesson (Z=118), was synthesized via the hot fusion reaction 48Ca + 249Cf (Z=98 target).
* The hypothetical superheavy nucleus 780254526 is used to illustrate competing configurations (normal, bubble, toroid) predicted by nuclear DFT.
* Oganesson is predicted to be a solid at ambient conditions with a melting point of about 325 K.
* Elements 113-118 were synthesized via hot fusion reactions of 48Ca impinging on increasingly heavier actinide targets.
* Projectiles heavier than 48Ca being considered include 50Ti, 51V, and 54Cr, although evaporation residue yields rapidly decrease with increasing projectile charge.

## Context

This white paper extends discussions from recent reviews on superheavy nuclei (Nazarewicz 2018, Giuliani 2019, Smits 2023, smits 2024) and the Long Range Plan for Nuclear Science (2023). It addresses the limitations of current production methods, noting that stable-beam fusion with long-lived targets produces neutron-deficient SHN and that the short half-life of Es (Z=99) precludes its use as a target. The report enables future work by identifying needed theoretical developments in fusion dynamics, multinucleon transfer, and survival probability calculations to guide experimental searches for elements 119 and 120 and more neutron-rich superheavy isotopes.
