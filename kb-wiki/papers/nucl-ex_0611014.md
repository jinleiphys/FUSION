# Bimodalities : a survey of experimental data and models

> Bimodal distributions of some chosen variables measured in nuclear collisions were recently proposed as a non ambiguous signature of a first order phase transition in nuclei. This section presents a compilation of both theoretical and experimental studies on bimodalities performed so far, in relation with the liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear matter.

## Key claim

Bimodality in the probability distribution of certain variables measured in nuclear collisions is a necessary and sufficient condition for a first-order phase transition in finite systems, but the experimental observation of bimodality does not uniquely identify the type of phase transition (liquid-gas versus shape-related or other transitions) and may be driven by spin rather than excitation energy in some models.

## Method

The authors compiled experimental data from central and peripheral nuclear collisions across multiple facilities (INDRA, ALADIN, MULTICS-MINIBALL, NIMROD) for systems with masses from approximately 40 to 250, analyzing distributions of charge asymmetry variables built from the two or three largest fragments, the largest fragment charge, and excitation energy. They compared these experimental results with predictions from several models: the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM), Classical Molecular Dynamics with a Lennard-Jones potential, the Heavy Ion Phase Space Exploration model (HIPSE), Lattice-Gas simulations, and percolation calculations.

## Key numbers

* For the Ni+Au system at 32A and 52A MeV, the asymmetry variable Z1 - 3(Z2 + Z3) showed a bimodal distribution; at 90A MeV the bimodality disappeared (Bellaize et al., 2002).
* For the Xe+Sn system between 32A and 50A MeV, bimodality in the charge asymmetry between heavy (Z >= 13) and light (Z = 3-12) fragments was present at all energies, with roughly equal numbers of liquid-type and gas-type events at 39A MeV (Borderie et al., 2002).
* For central Ni+Ni collisions, a bimodal distribution of the largest fragment was observed at 52A MeV, with a minimum at about 80% of the peak value (Lau et al., 2006).
* For Au+Au at 1A GeV, the charge asymmetry Z1 - Z2 - Z3 showed two components in the Zbound = 53-55 selection: one centered near 0 (multifragmentation) and one near 40 (evaporation residue) (Trautmann et al., 2005).
* For Ar+Ni at 47A MeV, equipartition between residue-evaporation and multifragmentation topologies occurred at E*/A = 5.5 MeV (Ma et al., 2004).
* In SMM simulations, the caloric curve plateau appeared between 4 and 7 MeV/nucleon for nuclei from Kr to Au (Buyukcizmeci, Ogul, and Botvina, 2005).
* In HIPSE simulations of Xe+Sn at 50A MeV, the bimodality in charge asymmetry was attributed to spin rather than excitation energy, with a sharp transition around 60-70 hbar (Lopez et al., 2005).

## Context

This survey extends the theoretical framework of Chomaz and Gulminelli (2003, 2004) linking bimodality to first-order phase transitions via the Yang-Lee theorem and convex intruder of thermodynamic potentials, and builds on earlier Ising model studies by Binder and Landau (1984). The compilation of experimental results from INDRA, ALADIN, MULTICS-MINIBALL, and NIMROD collaborations shows bimodality across a wide mass range (A=40-200) and collision geometries, but the authors note that the order parameter remains ambiguous: Lattice-Gas simulations show that Amax is an unambiguous signature of first-order transitions, while asymmetry variables can also signal second-order transitions as in percolation. The work enables future studies to distinguish between liquid-gas phase transitions and shape-driven transitions by examining whether bimodality appears in energy or volume distributions rather than solely in fragment charge asymmetries.
