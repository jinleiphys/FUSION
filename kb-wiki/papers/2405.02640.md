# Compactification of Determinant Expansions via Transcorrelation

> Although selected configuration interaction (SCI) algorithms can tackle much larger Hilbert spaces than the conventional full CI (FCI) method, the scaling of their computational cost with respect to the system size remains inherently exponential. Additionally, inaccuracies in describing the correlation hole at small interelectronic distances lead to the slow convergence of the electronic energy relative to the size of the one-electron basis set. To alleviate these effects, we show that the non-Hermitian, transcorrelated (TC) version of SCI significantly compactifies the determinant space, allowing to reach a given accuracy with a much smaller number of determinants. Furthermore, we note a significant acceleration in the convergence of the TC-SCI energy as the basis set size increases. The extent of this compression and the energy convergence rate are closely linked to the accuracy of the correlation factor used for the similarity transformation of the Coulombic Hamiltonian. Our systematic investigation of small molecular systems in increasingly large basis sets illustrates the magnitude of these effects.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that applying a non-Hermitian transcorrelated (TC) similarity transformation to the Hamiltonian within selected configuration interaction (SCI) algorithms significantly compresses the determinant expansion needed to reach a given accuracy, but the extent of this compression and the acceleration of basis-set convergence depend critically on the accuracy of the correlation factor used in the transformation.

## Method

The authors implemented a transcorrelated version of the CIPSI (Configuration Interaction using a Perturbative Selection made Iteratively) algorithm, where the bare Coulomb Hamiltonian is similarity-transformed using a Jastrow correlation factor, producing a non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian with up to three-body terms. The three-body operator was approximated via normal-ordering with respect to biorthogonal TC-SCF orbitals, reducing it to effective one- and two-body contributions. The TC-CIPSI algorithm iteratively selects determinants based on a second-order perturbative criterion adapted for the non-Hermitian case, and the TC-FCI energy is estimated by linear extrapolation of the zeroth-order energy as a function of the second-order correction.

## Key numbers

- For the water molecule, the TC-SCI approach achieves a given accuracy with a determinant space that is "significantly" more compact than standard SCI, though the paper does not provide a single numerical compression ratio in the abstract or introduction.
- The second-order perturbative correction in TC-SCI, \(E_{\text{TC}}^{(2)}\), contains both positive and negative contributions, unlike the standard Hermitian case where it is strictly negative.

## Context

This work extends the transcorrelated approach to selected CI methods, building on prior developments of TC theory by Boys and Handy (1969) and more recent TC implementations in coupled-cluster, FCIQMC, and MPS frameworks by groups including Luo, Alavi, and others (2010-2023). The paper specifically addresses the exponential scaling bottleneck of SCI algorithms noted by Holmes, Umrigar, and Sharma (2017) and the slow basis-set convergence arising from the electron-electron cusp problem identified by Kato (1951, 1957) and Pack and Byers Brown (1966). The results enable more efficient treatment of larger molecular systems with reduced basis-set errors, though the non-Hermitian nature introduces challenges for energy extrapolation that are absent in standard Hermitian SCI.
