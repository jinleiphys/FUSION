# Gravitational waves from phase transition of NS to QS

> In this article, we perform a 2-d simulation of combustion of neutron star (NS) to hybrid star (HS). We assume that a sudden density fluctuation at the center of the NS initiates a shock discontinuity near the center of the star. This shock discontinuity deconfines NM to 2-f QM, initiating combustion of the star. This combustion front propagates from the center to the surface converting NM to 2-f QM. This combustion stops at a radius of $6 km$ inside the star, as at this density the NM is much stable than QM. Beyond $6 km$ although the combustion stops but the shock wave propagates to the surface. We study the gravitational wave signal for such a PT of NS to HS. We find that such PT has unique GW strain of amplitude $10^{-22}$. These signals last for few tens of $μs$ and shows small oscillating behaviour. The power spectrum consists of peaks and at fairly high frequency range. The conversion to NS to HS has a unique signature which would help in defining the PT and the fate of the NS.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a phase transition from neutron star (NS) to hybrid star (HS) produces a gravitational wave strain of amplitude $10^{-22}$ with a unique oscillatory signature, but this result depends on the assumption that the combustion front stops at a radius of 6 km inside the star, converting only the inner core to 2-flavor quark matter while the outer layers remain nuclear matter.

## Method

The authors performed a 2-dimensional hydrodynamic simulation of shock-induced combustion in an axisymmetric neutron star, using the PLZ equation of state for nuclear matter and the MIT bag model (bag constant $B^{1/4}=140$ MeV, quark coupling $a_4=0.5$) for 2-flavor quark matter. They used the rotating neutron star (RNS) code to generate initial stellar profiles for different angles $\theta$, then evolved the shock front using the GR1D general relativistic hydrodynamics code along 65 angular directions, with a density fluctuation at 0.5 km from the center initiating the combustion. The gravitational wave strain was computed from the second time derivative of the quadrupole moment using the Savitzky-Golay filter for differentiation.

## Key numbers

- Gravitational wave strain amplitude: $10^{-22}$ for all four stellar models (PLZ-M1 through PLZ-M4)
- Combustion front propagates from 0.5 km to 6 km radius, taking approximately 30 $\mu$s
- The star model PLZ-M1 has mass $1.50 M_{\odot}$, central density $5.0 \times 10^{14}$ g/cc, equatorial radius 15 km, and rotational frequency $\omega = 0.3 \times 10^{-4}$ Hz
- Power spectrum peaks at frequencies: 23.80 kHz, 95.23 kHz, 166.66 kHz, and 261.90 kHz for model PLZ-M1
- For model PLZ-M4 (mass $2.2 M_{\odot}$, central density $6.6 \times 10^{14}$ g/cc), power spectrum peaks at: 23.80 kHz, 71.42 kHz, 119.04 kHz, 190.47 kHz, and 238.09 kHz
- The phase transition from nuclear matter to 2-flavor quark matter occurs at pressure $p=110$ MeV/fm$^3$, corresponding to a radial distance of 6 km
- The 2-flavor quark matter becomes stable relative to nuclear matter at pressure $p=340$ MeV/fm$^3$

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous 1-dimensional study (Prasad, 2019) of dynamical phase transition in neutron stars to a 2-dimensional axisymmetric calculation, enabling computation of gravitational wave emission from the quadrupole moment change. It differs from earlier approaches by Lin et al. (2006) and Abdikamalov et al. (2009), who treated the phase transition as instantaneous and calculated gravitational waves from the subsequent stellar pulsation and micro-collapse, finding strain amplitudes of order $10^{-23}$. The present work focuses on gravitational waves generated during the dynamical combustion process itself, which occurs on a timescale of tens of microseconds and produces signals at much higher frequencies (tens to hundreds of kHz) than the millisecond-scale signals from the post-transition collapse. This enables future high-frequency gravitational wave detectors to potentially distinguish between neutron stars that convert to hybrid stars versus those that convert to strange stars.
