# Isospin effects in the disappearance of flow as a function of colliding geometry

> We study the effect of isospin degree of freedom on the balance energy (E$_{bal}$) as well as its mass dependence throughout the mass range 48-270 for two sets of isobaric systems with N/Z = 1 and 1.4 at different colliding geometries ranging from central to peripheral ones. Our findings reveal the dominance of Coulomb repulsion in isospin effects on E$_{bal}$ as well as its mass dependence throughout the range of the colliding geometry. Our results also indicate that the effect of symmetry energy and nucleon-nucleon cross section on E$_{bal}$ is uniform throughout the mass range and throughout the colliding geometry. We also present the counter balancing of nucleon-nucleon collisions and mean field by reducing the Coulomb and the counter balancing of Coulomb and mean filed by removing the nucleon-nucleon collisions.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that Coulomb repulsion, rather than symmetry energy or the isospin dependence of the nucleon-nucleon cross section, is the dominant mechanism driving isospin effects on the balance energy (E$_{bal}$) and its mass dependence across all colliding geometries from central to peripheral, for isobaric systems with N/Z = 1.0 and 1.4. This conclusion is drawn from calculations where the Coulomb potential is reduced by a factor of 100, which nearly eliminates the difference in E$_{bal}$ between the two isobaric sets, whereas the effects of symmetry energy and cross section are found to be uniform throughout the mass range and colliding geometry.

## Method

The study uses the isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics (IQMD) model to simulate heavy-ion collisions for two sets of isobaric systems (N/Z = 1.0 and 1.4) with combined masses from 48 to 270. Reactions are simulated at incident energies around E$_{bal}$ in steps of 10 MeV/nucleon, for four impact parameter bins covering reduced impact parameters from 0.15 to 0.85. The balance energy is extracted from the directed transverse momentum using a straight-line interpolation, and the mass dependence is fitted with a power law E$_{bal}$ ∝ A$^{\tau}$; the Coulomb potential is then reduced by a factor of 100 to isolate its contribution from symmetry energy and cross-section effects.

## Key numbers

- The mass dependence exponent τ for systems with N/Z = 1.0 (τ₁) ranges from -0.36 ± 0.01 (BIN 1) to -0.93 ± 0.08 (BIN 4) with full Coulomb and including A=48; for N/Z = 1.4 (τ₁.₄) it ranges from -0.33 ± 0.01 to -0.70 ± 0.01 over the same bins.
- With Coulomb reduced by a factor of 100, τ₁ ranges from -0.25 ± 0.02 (BIN 1) to -0.41 ± 0.02 (BIN 4) and τ₁.₄ ranges from -0.26 ± 0.03 to -0.41 ± 0.02, showing nearly identical values for both N/Z ratios.
- Excluding the lightest system (A=48) from the fit, τ₁ with full Coulomb becomes -0.38 ± 0.03 (BIN 1) to -1.14 ± 0.25 (BIN 4); τ₁.₄ becomes -0.31 ± 0.02 to -0.70 ± 0.03.
- The average density at E$_{bal}$ is nearly independent of mass, with exponent τ = -0.05, while E$_{bal}$ itself decreases more sharply with τ = -0.42 (for the full Coulomb case, specific bin not stated).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Gautam and Sood, 2010) on isospin effects at semi-central geometry to the full range of colliding geometries, and builds on earlier findings by Li (1996), Pak (1997), and Daffin (1998) that neutron-rich systems have higher E$_{bal}$ than neutron-deficient ones. The results contradict the notion that symmetry energy or isospin-dependent cross section dominate the isospin dependence of flow disappearance, instead establishing Coulomb repulsion as the primary driver; this enables future experimental studies with radioactive ion beams at facilities such as FAIR and RIKEN to focus on isolating Coulomb effects from symmetry energy contributions in collective flow observables.
