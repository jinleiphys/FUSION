# Statistical treatment of nuclear clusters in the continuum

> The evaluation of the sub-saturation nuclear equation of state at finite temperature requires a proper state counting of the internal partition sum of nuclei which are immersed in the background of their continuum states. This classical statistical problem is addressed within the self-consistent mean-field approximation, which naturally accounts for isospin and effective mass effects in the nuclear density of states. The nuclear free energy is decomposed into bulk and surface terms, allowing a simple analytical prescription for the subtraction of gas states from the nuclear partition sum, that avoids double counting of unbound single particle states. We show that this correction leads to a sizeable effect in the composition of matter at high temperature and low proton fractions, such as it is formed in supernova collapse, early proto-neutron star evolution, as well as laboratory experiments. Specifically, the energy stored in the internal nuclear degrees of freedom is reduced, as well as the mass fraction of heavy clusters in the statistical equilibrium. The gas subtraction prescription is compared to different phenomenological methods proposed in the literature, based on a high energy truncation of the partition sum. We show that none of these methods satisfactorily reproduces the gas subtracted level density, if the temperature overcomes ~4 MeV.

## Key claim

The paper shows that a thermodynamically consistent subtraction of continuum (unbound) states from the internal partition sum of heavy nuclei leads to a sizeable reduction in the energy stored in internal nuclear degrees of freedom and in the mass fraction of heavy clusters at high temperature (above approximately 4 MeV) and low proton fractions, conditions relevant for supernova collapse and proto-neutron star evolution. The authors demonstrate that none of the simpler phenomenological truncation methods (cutting the partition sum at the nucleon separation energy or at the total binding energy) satisfactorily reproduces the gas-subtracted level density when the temperature exceeds about 4 MeV.

## Method

The authors compute the internal partition sum of heavy and medium-heavy nuclei within the self-consistent mean-field approximation using a compressible liquid drop model with a Skyrme energy functional (SLy5 parameters). The nuclear free energy is decomposed into bulk, surface, Coulomb, and translational terms, and the bulk part of the cluster partition sum is obtained by subtracting the grand canonical thermodynamic potential of a uniform nucleon gas from that of the nucleus-plus-gas system, following the method of Tubbs and Koonin (1979) but extended to include density-dependent effective masses and mean-field potentials. The level density is then calculated via inverse Laplace transform in the saddle-point approximation, and the average excitation energy and cluster mass fractions are evaluated for different thermodynamic conditions.

## Key numbers

- The gas subtraction leads to a sizeable effect when the temperature overcomes approximately 4 MeV; above this temperature, none of the phenomenological truncation methods satisfactorily reproduce the gas-subtracted level density.
- The level density parameter in the Bethe approximation is given by \(a = \frac{\pi^2}{4} \frac{A}{e_F}\), where \(e_F\) is the Fermi energy.
- The deviation of the full Laplace-transform level density from the Bethe approximation is safely negligible for moderate excitations of the order \(e^* \lessapprox 5\) MeV/nucleon.
- The average excitation energy per baryon is computed for external gas densities of \(n_g = 0.02 n_0\), \(0.05 n_0\), and \(0.1 n_0\), where \(n_0\) is the saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter.
- The bulk part of the level density for symmetric nuclei is shown as a function of excitation energy per baryon up to approximately 10 MeV/nucleon in the figures.

## Context

This work extends the seminal continuum subtraction technique introduced by Tubbs and Koonin (1979) and later developed by Bonche, Levit, and Vautherin (1984) using self-consistent Hartree-Fock methods. It addresses the double-counting problem of continuum states in the internal nuclear partition sum, which was recently identified by Raduta (2019) as a major source of model dependence in sub-saturation equations of state. The paper provides analytical expressions for the bulk free energy that can be implemented in realistic equations of state for supernova and neutron-star merger simulations, and it shows that the commonly used phenomenological prescriptions (cutting the partition sum at the nucleon separation energy or at the total binding energy, as used by Hempel (2010) and others) are not equivalent to the consistent gas subtraction at high temperatures.
