# Continued fraction representation of the Coulomb Green's operator and unified description of bound, resonant and scattering states

> If a quantum mechanical Hamiltonian has an infinite symmetric tridiagonal (Jacobi) matrix form in some discrete Hilbert-space basis representation, then its Green's operator can be constructed in terms of a continued fraction. As an illustrative example we discuss the Coulomb Green's operator in Coulomb-Sturmian basis representation. Based on this representation, a quantum mechanical approximation method for solving Lippmann-Schwinger integral equations can be established, which is equally applicable for bound-, resonant- and scattering-state problems with free and Coulombic asymptotics as well. The performance of this technique is illustrated with a detailed investigation of a nuclear potential describing the interaction of two $α$ particles.

# Continued fraction representation of the Coulomb Green's operator and unified description of bound, resonant and scattering states

## Key claim

The Coulomb Green's operator can be constructed in Coulomb-Sturmian basis via a continued fraction that converges only for negative energies, but can be analytically continued to the whole complex energy plane by replacing the tail of the continued fraction with its explicit analytical form, enabling a unified treatment of bound, resonant, and scattering states.

## Method

The authors observe that the Coulomb Hamiltonian has an infinite symmetric tridiagonal (Jacobi) matrix form in the Coulomb-Sturmian basis. They construct the Green's matrix elements from the Jacobi matrix using a continued fraction, where the tail of the continued fraction is replaced by an analytical expression to achieve convergence on the whole complex energy plane. The Lippmann-Schwinger equation is then solved by approximating only the short-range potential term via a separable expansion on a finite subset of the Coulomb-Sturmian basis, while keeping the Coulomb Green's operator exact.

## Key numbers

- For the uncharged α-α potential (Z=0), the three l=0 bound states converge to energies of approximately -76.9036145 MeV, -29.00048 MeV, and -1.6087408214 MeV (the latter two from the text, the first from comparison with Schmid (year unspecified))
- For the charged α-α potential (Z=2), the l=4 resonance is located at E = 11.791038 - i 1.788957 MeV
- Experimental resonance energies cited for comparison: l=0 at 0.09189 MeV, l=2 at 3.132 ± 0.030 MeV, l=4 at 11.5 ± 0.3 MeV; widths Γ/2: l=0 = (3.4 ± 0.9)×10⁻⁶ MeV, l=2 = 0.750 ± 0.010 MeV, l=4 ≈ 1.75 MeV
- Computational performance: bound-state energy determination (40×40 matrices, 6 zero-search steps) took 0.06 seconds; resonance energy (12 zero-search steps) took 0.8 seconds; three phase shift values took 0.19, 0.11, and 0.08 seconds respectively on a 266 MHz Linux PC

## Context

This work extends the discrete Hilbert-space basis expansion method previously developed by Papp et al. (1990s) using Coulomb-Sturmian bases for solving Lippmann-Schwinger equations, where the Coulomb Green's matrix had been calculated via hypergeometric functions. The continued fraction representation simplifies the calculation considerably and provides analytic continuation to the whole complex energy plane, enabling unified treatment of bound, resonant, and scattering states. This enables solving Faddeev equations for three-body Coulombic systems as demonstrated in prior work by Papp et al. (1990s), and can be adapted to other Hamiltonians with Jacobi matrix structure such as the harmonic oscillator and relativistic Coulomb problems.
