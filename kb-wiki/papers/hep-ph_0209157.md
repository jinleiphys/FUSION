# Quark-Hadron Duality

> Quark-hadron duality and its potential applications are discussed. We focus on theoretical efforts to model duality.

## Key claim

Quark-hadron duality, in its practical form, holds that properly averaged hadronic observables in certain kinematic regimes can be described by perturbative QCD (pQCD), but the paper emphasizes that this is a more restrictive statement than the general "degrees of freedom" duality, and that a firm quantitative understanding of where and how precisely duality holds is still lacking.

## Method

The authors review theoretical efforts to model duality by constructing solvable models of hadrons, typically treating quarks as scalars, using a scalar linear confining potential, and assuming a spectator system of infinite mass, then comparing bound-bound transitions (resonance excitation) to bound-free transitions (free quark final state). They solve the Klein-Gordon equation for the relativistic case and compare scaling functions and moments for both the all-scalar case and the case with spin-1/2 electrons and spin-1 photons.

## Key numbers

- Constituent quark mass set to \( m = 0.33 \) GeV and string tension to \( 0.16 \) GeV\(^2\) in the model; results do not depend crucially on these precise values.
- Duality in inclusive electron scattering holds even at very low \( Q^2 \approx 0.5 \) GeV\(^2\), as shown by Jefferson Lab data.
- Moments of the data flatten out at rather low \( Q^2 \approx 2 \) GeV\(^2\).
- The duality scale for active \( u, d, s \) flavors in dilepton production is 1.5 GeV; this scale is reduced in-medium.
- In the model, the scaling function for the bound-bound transition with electromagnetic current is \( {\cal{S}}_{2,cq} = \frac{m^2 u_{Bj}^2}{\pi^\frac{1}{2}\beta E_0} \exp{\left( -\frac{(E_0-mu_{Bj})^2}{\beta^2} \right)} \), and the bound-bound and bound-free transitions yield the same scaling function.
- In a similar model by other authors (using a semi-relativistic Hamiltonian with massless quarks), the bound-bound and bound-free scaling curves differ by about 30%.

## Context

This paper reviews the status of quark-hadron duality as of 2002, extending the original observation by Bloom and Gilman (1970) in inclusive electron scattering and the high-precision confirmation at Jefferson Lab. It discusses duality in semileptonic heavy quark decays (Isgur and Wise; others), \( e^+ e^- \rightarrow hadrons \) (Weinberg; Halzen and Martin), and dilepton production in heavy ion reactions (Rapp and others). The theoretical modeling efforts described, using scalar quarks and confining potentials, build on work by the authors themselves (Jeschonnek and Van Orden) and by others (Close and others; Paris and others). The paper enables future experiments at Jefferson Lab's 12 GeV upgrade and polarized measurements of the neutron asymmetry \( A_1^n \) at high \( x_{Bj} \), but notes that a firm quantitative understanding of duality is still needed before such applications can be fully trusted.
