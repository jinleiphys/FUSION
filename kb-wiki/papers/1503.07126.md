# Bose enhancement and the ridge

> We point out that Bose enhancement in a hadronic wave function generically leads to correlations between produced particles. We show explicitly, by calculating the projectile density matrix in the Color Glass Condensate approach to high-energy hadronic collisions, that the Bose enhancement of gluons in the projectile leads to azimuthal collimation of long range rapidity correlations of the produced particles, the so-called ridge correlations.

## Key claim

Bose enhancement of gluons in the projectile wave function generically produces azimuthally collimated long-range rapidity correlations (the ridge) in hadronic collisions, but this effect is subleading in the number of colors (suppressed by 1/N_c^2) and is only operational when the two gluons share identical quantum numbers.

## Method

The authors calculate the projectile density matrix for soft gluons in the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) approach, using the McLerran-Venugopalan model with a Gaussian weight for the valence color charge density. They then compute the two-gluon correlator D(k1,k2) in the projectile wave function and show how the Bose enhancement term, which appears as a delta-function contribution when the two gluon momenta are equal, is transmitted to final-state correlations through the scattering on the target via the glasma graph formalism of Dumitru, Dusling, and collaborators.

## Key numbers

- The Bose enhancement term in the two-gluon correlator is suppressed relative to the classical term by a factor of 1/[S(N_c^2-1)], where S is the transverse area of the projectile and N_c is the number of colors.
- The delta-function contributions in the correlator appear as δ^(2)(k1-k2) and δ^(2)(k1+k2), with the latter arising from the reality of the gluon field scattering amplitude.
- The suppression of the Bose enhancement term relative to the classical term scales as 1/N_c^2, since enhancement only operates when both gluons are in the same color state.

## Context

This work provides the physical mechanism underlying the glasma graph calculations of Dumitru, Dusling, and collaborators (2011) that successfully reproduced ridge correlation systematics, showing that those numerical results arise from Bose statistics in the initial state rather than from final-state flow effects. The mechanism is distinct from Hanbury-Brown-Twiss (HBT) correlations between emitted particles and is more general than the specific CGC calculation, having been widely used for identical mesons in heavy-ion collisions as reviewed by Weiner (1999). The authors suggest that analogous Pauli blocking for fermions in the initial state should produce anticollimation (a valley rather than a ridge at Δφ=0), which could be observable in correlations between D-mesons or B-mesons where quark fragmentation dominates over gluon fragmentation.
