# QCD Sum Rules for Skeptics

> A new Monte-Carlo based uncertainty analysis is introduced to quantitatively determine the predictive ability of QCD sum rules. A comprehensive analysis of ground state rho-meson and nucleon spectral properties is performed. Many of the findings contradict the conventional wisdom of both practitioners and skeptics alike. Associations between the phenomenological fit parameters are particularly interesting as they reveal how the sum rules resolve the spectral properties. The use of derivative sum rules for the determination of rho-meson spectral properties is shown to be a very unfavorable approach. Most prior nucleon sum rule analyses are based on a sum rule which is found to be invalid; the results are suspect, and should be reevaluated. The ``Ioffe formula'', argued by many to qualitatively encapsulate a description of the nucleon mass in terms of the chiral symmetry breaking order parameter is misleading at best. QCD Sum Rules are found to be self-consistent without contributions from direct instantons. This implies that instanton effects are adequately accounted for in the nonperturbative vacuum condensates. This in-depth examination of QCD sum rule self consistency paints a favorable picture for further quantitative refinements of the QCD sum rule approach.

## Key claim

QCD sum rules are predictive and self-consistent when a rigorous Monte-Carlo based uncertainty analysis is performed, but most prior nucleon sum rule analyses are based on an invalid sum rule and their results should be reevaluated; the widely-trusted "Ioffe formula" relating the nucleon mass to the chiral condensate is misleading at best.

## Method

A Monte-Carlo uncertainty analysis was introduced to generate Gaussian distributions for the vacuum condensate values appearing in the operator product expansion (OPE), reflecting the spread of values assumed in previously published QCD sum rule analyses. These distributions provided uncertainty estimates for the OPE, which were then used in a chi-squared fit to determine phenomenological fit parameters (masses, coupling strengths, and continuum thresholds) for the rho-meson and nucleon ground states. The analysis examined the Borel regime selection criteria, OPE convergence, and ground-state dominance conditions, and tested the self-consistency of the sum rules without including direct instanton contributions.

## Key numbers

- For the rho-meson, derivative sum rules were shown to be a very unfavorable approach; the analysis found that the standard Borel sum rules give a rho mass consistent with experiment when the Borel regime is properly selected.
- The Ioffe formula gives M_N = 0.90 GeV for Borel mass M = 1 GeV, and M_N = 0.97 GeV when setting M = M_N, using the quark condensate value of (0.225 GeV)^3 from the PCAC relation.
- The continuum model contributions to the sum rules are typically 10 to 50% of the ground state contribution.
- QCD sum rules were found to be self-consistent without contributions from direct instantons, implying instanton effects are adequately accounted for in the nonperturbative vacuum condensates.

## Context

This work extends the foundational QCD sum rule approach of Shifman, Vainshtein, and Zakharov (1979) by introducing a rigorous Monte-Carlo uncertainty analysis that had been absent from the field. It contradicts the conventional wisdom of both practitioners and skeptics by showing that most prior nucleon sum rule analyses (based on the interpolating field advocated by Ioffe in 1983) are invalid, and that the Ioffe formula's qualitative relationship between the nucleon mass and the chiral condensate is misleading. The analysis enables future quantitative refinements of the QCD sum rule approach for hadronic observables in vacuum and nuclear matter, including topics such as rho-omega mixing where lattice QCD faces formidable obstacles.
