# Isospin Physics in Heavy-Ion Collisions at Intermediate Energies

> In nuclear collisions induced by stable or radioactive neutron-rich nuclei a transient state of nuclear matter with an appreciable isospin asymmetry as well as thermal and compressional excitation can be created. This offers the possibility to study the properties of nuclear matter in the region between symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter. In this review, we discuss recent theoretical studies of the equation of state of isospin-asymmetric nuclear matter and its relations to the properties of neutron stars and radioactive nuclei. Chemical and mechanical instabilities as well as the liquid-gas phase transition in asymmetric nuclear matter are investigated. The in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections at different isospin states are reviewed as they affect significantly the dynamics of heavy ion collisions induced by radioactive beams. We then discuss an isospin-dependent transport model, which includes different mean-field potentials and cross sections for the proton and neutron, and its application to these reactions. Furthermore, we review the comparisons between theoretical predictions and available experimental data. In particular, we discuss the study of nuclear stopping in terms of isospin equilibration, the dependence of nuclear collective flow and balance energy on the isospin-dependent nuclear equation of state and cross sections, the isospin dependence of total nuclear reaction cross sections, and the role of isospin in preequilibrium nucleon emissions and subthreshold pion production.

## Key claim

This review argues that the isospin-dependent part of the nuclear equation of state, particularly the symmetry energy at high densities, remains highly uncertain despite extensive theoretical work, and that collisions of neutron-rich or radioactive nuclei at intermediate energies provide the most promising experimental tool to constrain these properties.

## Method

The authors review theoretical predictions for the equation of state of isospin-asymmetric nuclear matter from multiple approaches including non-relativistic Skyrme-Hartree-Fock, relativistic mean-field theory, relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock, and variational many-body calculations. They then discuss an isospin-dependent transport model that incorporates different mean-field potentials and cross sections for protons and neutrons, and compare its predictions with experimental data on nuclear stopping, collective flow, balance energy, preequilibrium nucleon emissions, and subthreshold pion production.

## Key numbers

- The compressibility at saturation for symmetric nuclear matter K0(0) ranges from 185 MeV (Paris interaction) to 370.3 MeV (SI' interaction) across different models
- The parameter a in K0(delta)=K0(0)(1-a*delta^2) ranges from 1.01 (FP interaction) to 2.196 (AV14+UVII interaction)
- At delta=1/3, the compressibility K0(1/3) is 20-30% below K0(0), with values from 143.3 MeV (Paris) to 318.0 MeV (SI')
- The saturation density rho0(0) ranges from 0.1453 fm^-3 (SIII) to 0.289 fm^-3 (Paris)
- The symmetry energy at normal nuclear matter density e_sym(rho0) is in the range 27-36 MeV from the mass formula, with non-relativistic Hartree-Fock giving 27-38 MeV and relativistic mean-field theory giving 35-42 MeV
- The slope L of the symmetry energy at normal density ranges from 9.91 MeV (SIII) to 138 MeV (DHF (e))
- The curvature K_sym ranges from -393.7 MeV (SIII) to 466 MeV (DHF (b))
- The critical proton fraction for the direct URCA process in neutron stars is about 1/9 (approximately 11%)

## Context

This review extends earlier work on asymmetric nuclear matter dating back to Brueckner et al. (late 1960s) and Siemens (1970), and synthesizes results from multiple theoretical approaches including the non-relativistic Brueckner approach (Sjoberg 1974, Cugnon 1987, Bombaci and Lombardo 1991), relativistic mean-field theory (Serot, Chin 1977, Horowitz 1987), and variational many-body calculations (Wiringa 1988). The work connects laboratory heavy-ion collision experiments to astrophysical applications, particularly the prompt supernova explosion model (Baron, Cooperstein, and Kahana) which requires a 30% reduction in compressibility at delta=1/3, and neutron star cooling mechanisms where the direct URCA process becomes possible above a critical proton fraction (Lattimer 1991). The review enables future experimental programs using radioactive beams to constrain the density dependence of the symmetry energy, which remains the most uncertain property of dense nuclear matter.
