# Soft QCD Modeling of Meson Electromagnetic Form Factors

> We summarize recent progress in soft QCD modeling based on the set of Dyson--Schwinger equations truncated to ladder-rainbow level. We pay particular attention to electromagnetic elastic and transition form factors of the pion. This covariant approach accommodates quark confinement and implements the QCD one-loop renormalization group behavior. The dressed quark propagators are compared to the most recent lattice-QCD data.

# Soft QCD Modeling of Meson Electromagnetic Form Factors

## Key claim
The Dyson-Schwinger equation approach truncated to ladder-rainbow level, combined with an effective gluon interaction that incorporates both infrared enhancement and perturbative QCD running, can reproduce a wide range of meson observables including electromagnetic form factors, but the asymptotic behavior of the pion form factor is not yet reached at momentum transfers up to 3 GeV², and numerical limitations prevent accurate determination of the asymptotic constants.

## Method
The authors solve the Dyson-Schwinger equation for the dressed quark propagator in rainbow truncation and the Bethe-Salpeter equation for meson bound states in ladder truncation, using a model effective coupling that combines a Gaussian infrared term with the perturbative QCD running coupling. Electromagnetic form factors are computed in impulse approximation using the dressed quark propagators, dressed photon-quark vertex constrained by the Ward-Takahashi identity, and the meson Bethe-Salpeter amplitudes expanded in a complete set of covariants. The model parameters are fitted to the chiral condensate, pion mass, and pion decay constant, with all other predictions then parameter-free.

## Context
This work extends the Dyson-Schwinger equation approach of Maris and Roberts (1997, 1999) for light mesons to electromagnetic form factors, building on earlier studies of the pion charge form factor by Maris (1998). The model reproduces lattice QCD data for the quark propagator from Skullerud (2000) and compares favorably with Jefferson Lab data for the pion form factor from Volmer (2001). The results enable future calculations of transition form factors for two-virtual-photon processes, which can test the pion distribution amplitude through the light-cone operator product expansion framework of Lepage and Brodsky (1980).
