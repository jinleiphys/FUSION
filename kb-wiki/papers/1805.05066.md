# Nucleon Excited States from Lattice QCD and Hamiltonian Effective Field Theory

> An approach for relating the nucleon excited states extracted from lattice QCD and the nucleon resonances of experimental data has been developed using the Hamiltonian effective field theory (HEFT) method. By formulating HEFT in the finite volume of the lattice, the eigenstates of the Hamiltonian model can be related to the energy eigenstates observed in Lattice simulations. By taking the infinite-volume limit of HEFT, information from the lattice is linked to experiment. The approach opens a new window for the study of experimentally-observed resonances from the first principles of lattice QCD calculations. With the Hamiltonian approach, one not only describes the spectra of lattice-QCD eigenstates through the eigenvalues of the finite-volume Hamiltonian matrix, but one also learns the composition of the lattice-QCD eigenstates via the eigenvectors of the Hamiltonian matrix. One learns the composition of the states in terms of the meson-baryon basis states considered in formulating the effective field theory. One also learns the composition of the resonances observed in Nature. In this paper, we will focus on recent breakthroughs in our understanding of the structure of the $N^*(1535)$, $N^*(1440)$ and $Λ^*(1405)$ resonances using this method.

## Key claim

The Hamiltonian effective field theory (HEFT) approach, when combined with lattice QCD results from three-quark local operators, reveals that the $N^*(1440)$ and $\Lambda^*(1405)$ resonances are predominantly meson-baryon molecular states rather than three-quark states, while the $N^*(1535)$ is dominated by a three-quark core; this conclusion depends on the assumption that three-quark local operators couple most strongly to the bare baryon component of the Hamiltonian.

## Method

The authors construct a Hamiltonian effective field theory (HEFT) model in the infinite volume, fitting its parameters to experimental scattering data (phase shifts, inelasticities, and cross sections) for the relevant coupled channels. They then discretize the Hamiltonian in a finite cubic volume of length $L$ to generate energy eigenvalues and eigenvectors, which are compared to lattice QCD results at various pion masses. The eigenvectors reveal the composition of each finite-volume eigenstate in terms of bare baryon (three-quark) and meson-baryon basis states, allowing identification of which states are most likely excited by three-quark lattice operators.

## Key numbers

- For $N^*(1535)$: the bare mass slope is fixed by fitting lattice QCD data at $L \simeq 1.98$ fm and $L \simeq 2.90$ fm; the Hamiltonian eigenstates around 1535 MeV at physical pion mass are dominated by bare-state contributions.
- For $\Lambda^*(1405)$: at physical pion mass, the second eigenstate around 1405 MeV takes approximately 5% bare state component; the fourth eigenstate with the largest bare state component lies above 1600 MeV.
- For $N^*(1440)$: Scenario I (consistent with lattice data) has a bare mass of 2.0 GeV, while Scenario II (inconsistent with lattice data) has a bare mass of 1.7 GeV; the first radial excited nucleon of the quark model is around 1.9 GeV.
- The form factor cutoff parameters are $\Lambda = 0.8$ GeV for $N^*(1535)$ and $\Lambda = 1.0$ GeV for $\Lambda^*(1405)$; the pion decay constant is $f = 92.4$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the HEFT approach previously developed by Hall (2013), Wu (2014), and Liu (2015-2016) to simultaneously describe lattice QCD spectra and experimental scattering data for three low-lying resonances. It contradicts the simple quark-model picture that all low-lying baryon resonances are three-quark states, showing instead that the $\Lambda^*(1405)$ and $N^*(1440)$ are predominantly meson-baryon molecular states, while the $N^*(1535)$ remains a three-quark-dominated state. The approach enables future studies of other resonances using combined lattice QCD and experimental data, and requires new lattice simulations on multiple volumes to further resolve the nature of these states.
