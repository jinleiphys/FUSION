# Dense Quark Matter Conductivity in Ultra-Intense Magnetic Field

> Heavy-ion collisions generate a huge magnetic field of the order of $10^{18} G $ for the duration of about 0.2 fm/c. This time may become an order of magnitude longer if the electrical conductivity of quark matter is large. We calculate the conductivity in the regime of high density and show that contrary to naive expectations it only weakly depends on the MF.

## Key claim

The electrical conductivity of dense quark matter at low or moderate temperature depends only weakly on the magnetic field strength, contrary to naive expectations that it would scale with the field; this rigidity holds up to magnetic fields where \((eB/\mu)\tau \sim 1\).

## Method

The authors calculate the electrical conductivity tensor for dense quark matter using the Matsubara Green's function formalism, decomposing the conductivity into a semi-classical Drude (Boltzmann) contribution and a quantum correction. For the Drude part, they evaluate the one-loop diagram with disorder averaging, performing momentum integration near the Fermi surface and summation over Matsubara frequencies, then generalize to include a constant magnetic field along the \(z\)-axis. The quantum contribution is estimated via order-of-magnitude analysis of fluctuation Cooper pair diagrams.

## Key numbers

- For \(\mu = 400\) MeV, \(T = 100\) MeV, \(v_F = 1\), and \(\tau = 0.8\) fm, the zero-frequency Drude conductivity is \(\sigma \simeq 0.04\) fm\(^{-1}\).
- The transverse conductivity in a magnetic field is \(\sigma_\bot(\omega = 0, \Omega) = \sigma_0 / (1 + \Omega^2 \tau^2)\), where \(\Omega = eB / \mu\) and \(\sigma_0\) is the zero-field conductivity.
- For \(\mu = 400\) MeV and \(\tau = 0.8\) fm, the condition \(\Omega^2 \tau^2 < 1\) holds up to \(eB < 5 m_\pi^2\) (where \(m_\pi^2 \sim 10^{18}\) G).
- The quantum conductivity estimate is \(|\sigma'| \sim e^2 / \pi^3 l\) in zero field and \(|\sigma'| \sim e^2 / \pi^3 l_B\) in a magnetic field, where \(l_B = (eB)^{-1/2}\).

## Context

This work extends calculations of electrical conductivity in quark matter from the high-temperature, zero-chemical-potential regime (previously studied by lattice Monte Carlo methods, yielding results differing by an order of magnitude) to the high-density, low-temperature regime relevant for neutron stars and future experiments at NICA and FAIR. The result that conductivity is rigid against magnetic fields was later confirmed by a calculation in the instanton liquid model (reference [13] in the paper). The approach adapts methods from condensed matter physics for disordered systems near the superconducting phase transition, and the quantum correction estimate connects to the Ioffe-Regel criterion for Anderson localization.
