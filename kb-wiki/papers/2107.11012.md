# Finding signatures of the nuclear symmetry energy in heavy-ion collisions with deep learning

> A deep convolutional neural network (CNN) is developed to study symmetry energy $E_{\rm sym}(ρ)$ effects by learning the mapping between the symmetry energy and the two-dimensional (transverse momentum and rapidity) distributions of protons and neutrons in heavy-ion collisions. Supervised training is performed with labelled data-set from the ultrarelativistic quantum molecular dynamics (UrQMD) model simulation. It is found that, by using proton spectra on event-by-event basis as input, the accuracy for classifying the soft and stiff $E_{\rm sym}(ρ)$ is about 60% due to large event-by-event fluctuations, while by setting event-summed proton spectra as input, the classification accuracy increases to 98%. The accuracy for 5-label (5 different $E_{\rm sym}(ρ)$) classification task are about 58% and 72% by using proton and neutron spectra, respectively. For the regression task, the mean absolute error (MAE) which measures the average magnitude of the absolute differences between the predicted and actual $L$ (the slope parameter of $E_{\rm sym}(ρ)$) are about 20.4 and 14.8 MeV by using proton and neutron spectra, respectively. Fingerprints of the density-dependent nuclear symmetry energy on the transverse momentum and rapidity distributions of protons and neutrons can be identified by convolutional neural network algorithm.

## Key claim

A convolutional neural network can identify fingerprints of the density-dependent nuclear symmetry energy in the transverse momentum and rapidity distributions of protons and neutrons from heavy-ion collisions, but only when event-by-event fluctuations are suppressed by summing over 100 events; on an event-by-event basis, the classification accuracy for soft versus stiff symmetry energy is only about 60%.

## Method

The authors simulated 800,000 Au+Au collisions at 0.4 GeV per nucleon and impact parameter b=5 fm using the ultrarelativistic quantum molecular dynamics (UrQMD) model with five different Skyrme interactions that produce symmetry energies with slope parameters L ranging from 5.8 to 159.0 MeV. They constructed two-dimensional histograms (20 bins in transverse momentum from 0 to 1 GeV/c and 40 bins in rapidity from -2 to 2) of protons and neutrons, either from single events or summed over 100 events, and trained a convolutional neural network with two convolution layers and one fully-connected layer to perform classification (soft vs stiff, or five-label) and regression (predicting L).

## Key numbers

- Two-class classification accuracy for soft (Skz4, L=5.8 MeV) vs stiff (SkI1, L=159.0 MeV) symmetry energy: about 60% using event-by-event proton spectra; increases to 98% using event-summed proton spectra (100 events per sample).
- Five-class classification accuracy using event-summed spectra: about 58% with proton spectra, about 72% with neutron spectra.
- Mean absolute error (MAE) for regression of the slope parameter L: about 20.4 MeV using event-summed proton spectra, about 14.8 MeV using event-summed neutron spectra.
- For classifying SV-sym34 (L=81.2 MeV) vs SkI2 (L=106.4 MeV) using event-summed proton spectra, the accuracy is about 63%, the lowest among all pairs because their L difference is the smallest (about 25 MeV).

## Context

This work extends the use of deep learning in heavy-ion physics, previously applied to impact parameter determination (Li 2020) and QCD property extraction (Du 2019, Zhao 2021), to the problem of constraining the nuclear symmetry energy at supra-saturation densities. The authors note that conventional observables such as rapidity distributions show differences too faint to distinguish between different symmetry energies by standard analysis. The results demonstrate that deep learning can extract symmetry energy signals from the transverse momentum and rapidity distributions, enabling future application to experimental data to obtain tighter constraints on the high-density behavior of the symmetry energy.
