# Rod-shaped Nuclei at Extreme Spin and Isospin

> The anomalous rod shape in carbon isotopes has been investigated in the framework of the cranking covariant density functional theory, and two mechanisms to stabilize such a novel shape with respect to the bending motion, extreme spin, and isospin, are simultaneously discussed for the first time in a self-consistent and microscopic way. By adding valence neutrons and rotating the system, we have found the mechanism stabilizing the rod shape; i.e., the $σ$ orbitals (parallel to the symmetry axis) of the valence neutrons, important for the rod shape, are lowered by the rotation due to the Coriolis term. The spin and isospin effects enhance the stability of the rod-shaped configuration. This provides a strong hint that a rod shape could be realized in nuclei towards extreme spin and isospin.

## Key claim

The rod-shaped (linear-chain) configuration in carbon isotopes can be stabilized by simultaneously increasing isospin (adding valence neutrons) and spin (rotating the nucleus), with the Coriolis term lowering the energy of the crucial σ orbitals of valence neutrons; however, the stabilization is conditional on the specific neutron occupation and rotational frequency, as seen in the case of ¹⁵C where the rod shape is only stabilized above a rotational frequency of about 2.0 MeV.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent cranking covariant density functional theory (CDFT) calculations using the DD-ME2 energy density functional, solving the Dirac equation in a rotating intrinsic frame with a 3D Cartesian harmonic oscillator basis of N=12 major shells. They started from an initial field with very large prolate deformation for ¹²C to obtain a 3α linear-chain solution, then used that as the initial potential for self-consistent calculations of carbon isotopes from A=12 to A=20 at various rotational frequencies, filling orbitals according to their energy from the bottom of the well. Pairing correlations were neglected as the level density for the rod-shaped states was low.

## Key numbers

- Moments of inertia for ¹²,¹³,¹⁴C are about 2.5 (MeV)⁻¹ℏ², close to the classical rigid rotor value of around 3.0 (MeV)⁻¹ℏ².
- Moments of inertia for ¹⁹C and ²⁰C are around 4.6 (MeV)⁻¹ℏ², much larger than for ¹²,¹³,¹⁴C.
- A backbending phenomenon (abrupt increase in moment of inertia) occurs around ℏω = 2.0 MeV for ¹⁵,¹⁶,¹⁷,¹⁸C.
- The calculated ground-state energies for C isotopes from A=12 to A=20 agree with experimental data with a root-mean-square deviation of around 2.7 MeV.
- Excitation energies of the predicted rod-shaped states at ℏω = 0.0 MeV are estimated to be between around 13 and 18 MeV, much lower than the previous suggested value of around 25 MeV from Itagaki (2001).
- A moment of inertia of ℏ²/2ℐ ∼ 120 keV was reported by Freer et al. (2014) for ¹⁴C, corresponding to the present results when a neutron(s) occupies the σ orbit around the 3α chain.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of linear-chain states in light nuclei, particularly the 3α linear-chain configuration first suggested by Morinaga (1956) for the Hoyle state in ¹²C, which was later reinterpreted as an α-condensate-like state by Tohsaki (2001) and Suhara (2014). It builds on the cluster model calculations of Itagaki (2001, 2004) that identified the role of σ orbitals in stabilizing elongated shapes in neutron-rich carbon isotopes, and on density functional theory investigations of linear-chain configurations in ¹⁶,²⁰C by Maruhn (2010), ¹⁶O by Flocard (1984), Bender (2003), Ichikawa (2011), and Yao (2014), and other light N=Z nuclei by Ebran (2014). The present work is the first to simultaneously treat both extreme isospin and spin mechanisms for rod-shape stabilization in a self-consistent microscopic framework without assuming clusters a priori, and it enables future experimental validation of the predicted energy spectra for carbon isotopes.
