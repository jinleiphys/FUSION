# The Density Matrix Renormalization Group for finite Fermi systems

> The Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG) was introduced by Steven White in 1992 as a method for accurately describing the properties of one-dimensional quantum lattices. The method, as originally introduced, was based on the iterative inclusion of sites on a real-space lattice. Based on its enormous success in that domain, it was subsequently proposed that the DMRG could be modified for use on finite Fermi systems, through the replacement of real-space lattice sites by an appropriately ordered set of single-particle levels. Since then, there has been an enormous amount of work on the subject, ranging from efforts to clarify the optimal means of implementing the algorithm to extensive applications in a variety of fields. In this article, we review these recent developments. Following a description of the real-space DMRG method, we discuss the key steps that were undertaken to modify it for use on finite Fermi systems and then describe its applications to Quantum Chemistry, ultrasmall superconducting grains, finite nuclei and two-dimensional electron systems. We also describe a recent development which permits symmetries to be taken into account consistently throughout the DMRG algorithm. We close with an outlook for future applications of the method.

## Key claim

The Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG), originally developed for one-dimensional quantum lattices, can be adapted to finite Fermi systems by replacing real-space lattice sites with an appropriately ordered set of single-particle levels, but the accuracy of the method depends critically on the ordering of these levels and the nature of correlations in the system.

## Method

The authors review how the real-space DMRG method was modified for finite Fermi systems through two main approaches: the momentum-space DMRG (kDMRG), which replaces real-space sites with momentum states, and the particle-hole DMRG (phDMRG), which optimally accounts for correlations near the Fermi energy. The method uses an iterative truncation procedure based on the reduced density matrix, retaining only the states with the largest density matrix eigenvalues to maximize the overlap between the approximate and exact wave functions. A sweeping algorithm is employed, where the system is grown from left to right and then reversed, updating the information from the previous sweep until convergence is achieved.

## Key numbers

- The real-space DMRG produced results for the ground state energy of the S=1 Heisenberg chain accurate to twelve significant figures, well beyond the precision of large-scale diagonalization methods or Monte Carlo techniques.
- In the Hubbard model, each site admits precisely four states (empty, spin-up, spin-down, or fully occupied), so the Fock space dimension grows as 4^N, where N is the number of sites.
- The largest reported exact diagonalization of the Hubbard model was for a 4x4 2D lattice.
- The Wilson RG applied to 1D Hubbard models begins to deviate significantly from exact results in the fourth iteration.

## Context

This review extends the DMRG methodology introduced by White (1992) for one-dimensional quantum lattices to finite Fermi systems, including quantum chemistry, ultrasmall superconducting grains, finite nuclei, and two-dimensional electron systems. The work builds on earlier applications of DMRG to spin chains, Hubbard models, and t-J models, and describes recent developments that permit symmetries to be taken into account consistently throughout the algorithm, enabling future applications to medium-mass and heavy nuclei beyond the reach of exact shell-model diagonalization.
