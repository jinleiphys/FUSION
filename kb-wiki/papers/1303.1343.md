# Nuclear Masses and Neutron Stars

> Precision mass spectrometry of neutron-rich nuclei is of great relevance for astrophysics. Masses of exotic nuclides impose constraints on models for the nuclear interaction and thus affect the description of the equation of state of nuclear matter, which can be extended to describe neutron-star matter. With knowledge of the masses of nuclides near shell closures, one can also derive the neutron-star crustal composition. The Penning-trap mass spectrometer ISOLTRAP at CERN-ISOLDE has recently achieved a breakthrough measuring the mass of 82Zn, which allowed constraining neutron-star crust composition to deeper layers (Wolf et al., PRL 110, 2013). We perform a more detailed study on the sequence of nuclei in the outer crust of neutron stars with input from different nuclear models to illustrate the sensitivity to masses and the robustness of neutron-star models. The dominant role of the N=50 and N=82 closed neutron shells for the crustal composition is confirmed.

## Key claim

Precision mass measurements of neutron-rich nuclei, specifically the new mass of 82Zn measured by ISOLTRAP, directly constrain the equilibrium composition of the outer crust of neutron stars, but the robustness of the resulting crustal sequence depends on which nuclear mass model is used to supplement experimentally unavailable data.

## Method

The authors calculated the equilibrium composition of the outer crust of a cold, nonaccreting neutron star by minimizing the Gibbs free energy per baryon for each pressure, using the Wigner-Seitz cell approximation with contributions from nuclear binding energies, Coulomb lattice energy, and electron energy. They tested 25 different nuclear mass tables (including HFB-19, HFB-20, HFB-21, SkP, MSk7, and the finite-range-droplet model) both with and without the new experimental mass of 82Zn, and integrated the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to determine the depth profile.

## Key numbers

* The new mass measurement of 82Zn excludes this nucleus from the outer crust; the experimentally determined equilibrium composition is now constrained to a new depth of 223 m for a canonical neutron star of 1.4 solar mass and 10 km radius.
* The neutron-drip density marking the transition to the inner crust is about 4 x 10^11 g/cm^3.
* The saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter is rho_0 = 0.16 fm^-3 = 2 x 10^14 g/cm^3.
* The pulsar J1614-2230 has a mass of 1.97(4) solar masses.
* For the HFB-19 mass table, 126Mo is predicted to occur only in the narrow density interval (2.09322 - 2.09399) x 10^11 g/cm^3.

## Context

This work extends the landmark study of Baym, Pethick, and Sutherland (1971) on the outer crust composition, and the authors' own previous study from 2006 (Ruster et al.). It uses the recent ISOLTRAP measurement of 82Zn (Wolf et al., PRL 110, 2013) to test the sensitivity of crustal models to a single mass value. The results confirm the dominant role of the N=50 and N=82 closed neutron shells for crustal composition and show that the transition to the inner crust occurs at about the same pressure for all models, while more recent microscopic models tend to predict a richer variety of species with thinner layers.
