# A Precise Determination of (Anti)neutrino Fluxes with (Anti)neutrino-Hydrogen Interactions

> We present a novel method to accurately determine the flux of neutrinos and antineutrinos, one of the dominant systematic uncertainty affecting current and future long-baseline neutrino experiments, as well as precision neutrino scattering experiment. Using exclusive topologies in $ν(\bar ν)$-hydrogen interactions, $ν_μp \to μ^- p π^+$, $\bar ν_μp \to μ^+ p π^-$, and $\bar ν_μp \to μ^+ n$ with small hadronic energy, we achieve an overall accuracy on the relative fluxes better than 1\% in the energy range covering most of the available flux. Since we cannot rely on simulations nor model corrections at this level of precision, we present techniques to constrain all relevant systematic uncertainties using data themselves. The method can be implemented using the approach we recently proposed to collect high statistics samples of $ν(\bar ν)$-hydrogen interactions in a low-density and high-resolution detector, which could serve as part of the near detector complex in a long-baseline neutrino experiment, as well as a dedicated beam monitoring detector.

# A Precise Determination of (Anti)neutrino Fluxes with (Anti)neutrino-Hydrogen Interactions

## Key claim

The authors claim that relative neutrino and antineutrino fluxes can be determined with an overall accuracy better than 1% using exclusive topologies in neutrino-hydrogen interactions, but only when restricting to events with small hadronic energy (nu < 0.5 GeV) and using data-driven techniques to constrain systematic uncertainties rather than relying on simulations or model corrections at this level of precision.

## Method

The method uses exclusive charged-current processes on hydrogen targets: nu_mu p -> mu- p pi+, antinu_mu p -> mu+ p pi-, and antinu_mu p -> mu+ n (quasi-elastic). Hydrogen interactions are isolated by subtracting events from dedicated CH2 plastic and graphite (pure carbon) targets embedded within a low-density straw tube tracker (density ~0.16 g/cm3). A kinematic selection based on transverse momentum balance identifies neutrino-hydrogen interactions with efficiencies exceeding 90% and purities of 80-92%, while the residual carbon background is subtracted using data from the graphite target. The analysis uses three event generators (NuWro, GiBUU, GENIE) with input neutrino spectra similar to the Long-Baseline Neutrino Facility (LBNF) and DUNE experiment, and detector effects are evaluated using GEANT4 with reconstruction smearing consistent with NOMAD data.

## Key numbers

- Overall accuracy on relative fluxes better than 1% in the energy range covering most of the available flux
- Efficiency of the nu < 0.5 GeV cut for nu_mu p -> mu- p pi+ on hydrogen: 25.2% (low energy beam), 45.6% (high energy beam)
- Efficiency of the nu < 0.25 GeV cut for antinu_mu p -> mu+ n on hydrogen: 76.0% (low energy beam), 68.0% (high energy beam)
- Total of 560,000 events expected in the nu_mu flux sample with nu < 0.5 GeV
- Total of 2.24 x 10^6 selected mu- p pi+ events on hydrogen without nu cut
- Total of 812,000 reconstructed quasi-elastic events on hydrogen, with 617,000 having nu < 0.25 GeV
- Expected 135,000 reconstructed antinu_mu p -> mu+ n quasi-elastic events with Q2 < 0.05 GeV2 (corresponding to nu < 27 MeV)
- Momentum resolution delta p/p ~ 3%
- Muon energy scale uncertainty of 0.2% (achieved by NOMAD experiment)
- Expected 264,000 K0 -> pi+ pi- decays and 293,000 Lambda -> p pi- decays in the CP-optimized forward horn current configuration
- Expected 1,981,000 K0 decays and 1,998,000 Lambda decays in the nu_tau-optimized forward horn current configuration
- Absolute nu_mu flux uncertainty of 1.9% (low energy beam) and 1.4% (high energy beam) from nu e- -> nu e- elastic scattering
- Model-independent constraint from Q2 distribution yields relative flux uncertainty below 0.2% at all energies

## Context

This work extends the approach proposed by Duyang et al. (2018) for detecting neutrino-hydrogen interactions via subtraction between CH2 and graphite targets, and applies it to flux determination. It addresses the dominant systematic uncertainty in current and future long-baseline neutrino oscillation experiments (such as DUNE at LBNF) and precision neutrino scattering experiments. The method overcomes limitations of the traditional low-nu technique used by experiments like those of Mishra (1990) and Bodek (2012), which rely on nuclear targets (C to Pb) and suffer from systematic uncertainties due to nuclear smearing effects including Fermi motion, binding, off-shell corrections, meson exchange currents, nuclear shadowing (Kulagin, 2004-2014), and final state interactions (Alvarez-Ruso, 2017). The technique enables precision tests of fundamental interactions and can be implemented as part of a near detector complex in a long-baseline neutrino experiment or as a dedicated beam monitoring detector.
