# Gradient Terms in the Microscopic Description of K-Atoms

> We analyze the spectra of kaonic atoms using optical potentials withnon-local (gradient) terms. The magnitude of the non-local termsfollows from a self consistent many-body calculation of the kaon selfenergy in nuclear matter, which is based on s-wave kaon nucleoninteractions. The optical potentials exhibit strong non-linearitiesin the nucleon density and sizeable non-local terms. We find that thenon-local terms are quantitatively important in the analysis of thespectra and that a phenomenologically successful description can be obtained for p-wave like optical potentials. It is suggested that themicroscopic form of the non-local interaction terms is obtainedsystematically by means of a semi-classical expansion of the nucleusstructure. The resulting optical potential leads to less pronounced non-local effects.

## Key claim

The paper argues that non-local (gradient) terms in the optical potential for kaonic atoms are quantitatively important for describing the spectra, but the precise form of the gradient ordering must be derived systematically; a semi-classical expansion of the nucleus structure yields a microscopic optical potential that produces less pronounced non-local effects than phenomenological gradient orderings.

## Method

The authors compute the kaon self energy in nuclear matter using a self-consistent many-body calculation based on s-wave kaon-nucleon interactions, then extract density-dependent effective scattering lengths and slope parameters. They construct three phenomenological non-local optical potentials with different gradient orderings and solve the Klein-Gordon equation for kaonic atoms of ¹⁰B, ¹²C, ²⁷Al, and ³²S, comparing the resulting level shifts and widths to empirical data. A semi-classical expansion of the nucleus structure is then performed to derive a systematic form of the non-local optical potential, which is applied to ¹²C and ⁵⁸Ni.

## Key numbers

- The low-density s-wave K⁻N scattering length is a_KN = (-0.18 + 0.67 i) fm.
- The phenomenological non-local parameter from Mizoguchi, Hirenzaki, and Toki is b ≈ (0.47 + i 0.30) fm³.
- At nuclear saturation density (ρ = 0.17 fm⁻³), the real part of the optical potential shows moderate attraction of less than 40 MeV.
- The quasi-particle energy for a K⁻ at rest at saturation density is 380 MeV.
- For ¹²C (3d→2p transition), the empirical nuclear level shift is -573 ± 80 keV and width is 1.73 ± 0.15 keV; the low-density theorem gives -ΔE = 475 eV and Γ = 761 eV.
- For ⁵⁸Ni (5g→4f transition), the empirical nuclear level shift is -213 ± 52 keV and width is 1.230 ± 0.140 keV; the low-density theorem gives -ΔE = 199 eV and Γ = 476 eV.
- The effective slope parameters at zero density are b_eff(0) ≈ (-2.45 + i 0.95) fm GeV⁻¹ and c_eff(0) ≈ (-1.35 + i 0.53) fm.
- The wave function and mass renormalization functions Z(r) and Z̄(r) for ⁵⁸Ni show deviations of about 50% from the asymptotic value 1.

## Context

This work extends the self-consistent many-body approach of Lutz (1998) to kaonic atoms, addressing the long-standing puzzle that the low-density optical potential using the K⁻N scattering length contradicts kaonic atom data, as shown by Friedman, Gal, and Batty (1994). The authors demonstrate that non-local effects, previously emphasized by Thies (1976) and phenomenologically modeled by Mizoguchi, Hirenzaki, and Toki (1997), are quantitatively important. The semi-classical expansion provides a systematic method to derive the non-local optical potential, enabling future microscopic calculations that properly account for the Λ(1405) resonance structure in nuclear matter.
