# Chiral thermodynamics of dense hadronic matter

> We discuss phases of hot and dense hadronic matter using chiral Lagrangians. A two-flavored parity doublet model constrained by the nuclear matter ground state predicts chiral symmetry restoration. The model thermodynamics is shown within the mean field approximation. A field-theoretical constraint on possible phases from the anomaly matching is also discussed.

# Chiral thermodynamics of dense hadronic matter

## Key claim
A two-flavored parity doublet model constrained by the nuclear matter ground state predicts that chiral symmetry restoration can occur as either a weak first-order phase transition or a crossover, depending on the mass of the negative-parity nucleon partner; the model also suggests that the anomaly matching condition does not exclude a chirally restored phase with confinement for systems composed only of up and down quarks.

## Method
The author employs a parity doublet model within the mean field approximation, using chiral Lagrangians with two quark flavors and mirror assignment of chirality to nucleons. The model is constrained by the nuclear matter saturation point at baryon density ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³, and the thermodynamics is computed for hot and dense hadronic matter to map the phase structure including liquid-gas and chiral transitions.

## Key numbers
- Nuclear matter saturation density: ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³.
- At zero temperature, a first-order liquid-gas transition occurs at baryon chemical potential μ_B = 923 MeV.
- With negative-parity nucleon mass m_{N-} = 1500 MeV, a weak first-order chiral transition appears at ρ ~ 10 ρ₀.
- With lower negative-parity mass m_{N-} = 1200 MeV, no true chiral phase transition occurs, only a crossover at ρ ~ 3 ρ₀.
- The liquid-gas transition survives up to temperature T = 27 MeV.
- The chiral crossover and meson-baryon transition lines intersect at (T, μ_B) ~ (150, 450) MeV.

## Context
This work extends earlier parity doublet model studies of hot and dense hadronic matter (Sasaki and collaborators, prior work) and builds on Walecka-type models that incorporate nuclear matter saturation. The results connect to the concept of quarkyonic matter and the estimated triple point where hadronic matter, quarkyonic matter, and quark-gluon plasma may coexist (McLerran and Pisarski, 2007). The anomaly matching discussion follows the approach of 't Hooft (1980) and Shifman (1984) and constrains possible phases in modeling QCD matter, leaving open the possibility of a chirally restored but confined phase for two-flavor systems.
