# Comparison of Yields of neutron rich nuclei in Proton and Photon induced $^{238}$U fission

> A comparative study of fission of actinides specially $^{238}$U, by proton and bremsstrahlung photon is performed. Relative mass distribution of $^{238}$U fission fragments have been explored theoretically for both proton and photon induced fission. The integrated yield along with charge distribution of the products are calculated to find out the neutron richness in comparison to the nuclei produced by r-process in nucleosynthesis. Some r-process nuclei in intermediate mass range for symmetric fission mode are found to be produced almost two order of magnitude more for proton induced fission than photofission, although rest of the neutron rich nuclei in the asymmetric mode are produced in comparable proportion for both the processes.

# Comparison of Yields of neutron rich nuclei in Proton and Photon induced $^{238}$U fission

## Key claim

Proton-induced fission of $^{238}$U at 35 MeV produces r-process nuclei in the symmetric fission mode (mass range 110–125) with cross sections almost two orders of magnitude larger than photofission at 13.7 MeV mean photon energy, although for the asymmetric fission mode the two processes produce neutron-rich nuclei in comparable proportions. The advantage of proton-induced fission is most pronounced specifically for nuclei with 8–16 neutron excess, while photofission remains preferred for overall target thermal management.

## Method

The authors used an empirical three-Gaussian parametrization of the mass yield distribution for proton-induced fission of $^{238}$U, with symmetric mode centered at A = 118 and asymmetric modes at A = 99 and A = 137, fitted to experimental data from Ohtsuki (1991) and Rubchenya (2005) for proton energies from 12 to 50 MeV. Total fission cross sections were calculated using the Prokofiev (2001) systematics with parameters fitted to experimental data from Baba (1997) and Isaev (2008) in the 13–63 MeV range, and compared with TALYS and PACE4 code predictions. Charge distributions were modeled with a single Gaussian using liquid-drop-model most-stable charges, and production cross sections for individual fragments were obtained by multiplying the total fission cross section by the fractional yield per 100 fission events.

## Key numbers

- For $^{122}$Cd (A/Z = 2.54), the proton-induced fission cross section at 35 MeV is 21.52 mb, while the photofission cross section at 13.7 MeV is 0.29 mb, giving a ratio of 74.04.
- For $^{117}$Pd (A/Z = 2.54), the proton-induced cross section is 19.86 mb versus 0.33 mb for photofission, ratio 60.56.
- For $^{96}$Kr (A/Z = 2.66), the proton-induced cross section is 0.19 mb versus 0.0021 mb for photofission, ratio 91.43.
- For $^{80}$Zn (A/Z = 2.66), the proton-induced cross section is 0.11 mb versus 0.0026 mb for photofission, ratio 40.00.
- For $^{133}$Sn (A/Z = 2.66), the proton-induced cross section is 0.039 mb versus 0.0022 mb for photofission, ratio 17.59.
- The ratio of cross sections ($\sigma_p^{35}/\sigma_\gamma^{13.7}$) reaches approximately 70–90 in the symmetric fission mass region (A = 110–125) and approximately 10 for the asymmetric mass regions.
- The total fission cross section for proton-induced fission saturates at approximately 30 MeV incident energy.
- The photofission cross section maximizes at a mean photon energy of 13.7 MeV (Giant Dipole Resonance region).
- The Gaussian width parameter $C_p$ for charge distribution was fitted as 0.95.
- The fitting parameters for the total fission cross section systematics are $R_{11} = 2730 \pm 82.962$, $R_{12} = 34.99 \pm 0.034$, $R_{13} = 2.07 \pm 0.120$, with $P_2 = 0.111$ and $P_3 = 12.1$ held constant.

## Context

This work extends the systematic comparison of proton-induced and photon-induced fission of actinides, building on prior studies of photofission at the Giant Dipole Resonance by Bhattacharya (2015, 2016) and the Prokofiev (2001) systematics for proton-induced fission cross sections. The results are directly relevant to the design of radioactive ion beam facilities such as ARIEL at TRIUMF, the ALTO facility at IPN Orsay, JINR Dubna, and the planned ANURIB facility at the Variable Energy Cyclotron Centre in Kolkata, which use e-LINACs for photofission production. The paper suggests that while photofission offers better thermal management in target design, a complementary proton-induced fission capability could be advantageous for producing specific very neutron-rich nuclei in the symmetric mass region, particularly those relevant to r-process nucleosynthesis studies.
