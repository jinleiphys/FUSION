# Time Dependence of Nucleon Correlation Functions in Chiral Perturbation Theory

> We consider corrections to nucleon correlation functions arising from times that are far from the asymptotic limit. For such times, the single nucleon state is contaminated by the pion-nucleon and pion-delta continuum. We use heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory to derive the spectral representation of the nucleon two-point function. Finite time corrections to the axial current correlation function are also derived. Pion-nucleon excited state contributions drive the axial correlator upward, while contributions from the interference of pion-delta and pion-nucleon states drive the axial correlator downward. Our results can be compared qualitatively to optimized nucleon correlators calculated in lattice QCD, because the chiral corrections characterize only low-energy excitations above the ground state. We show that improved nucleon operators can lead to an underestimation of the nucleon axial charge.

## Key claim

Corrections to nucleon correlation functions from times far from the asymptotic limit, arising from the pion-nucleon and pion-delta continuum, can lead to overestimation of the nucleon mass and either overestimation or underestimation of the nucleon axial charge depending on the interplay of competing excited-state contributions; improved nucleon operators that reduce pion-nucleon contamination in two-point functions can actually lead to an underestimation of the axial charge in three-point functions because the negative interference term between pion-delta and pion-nucleon states becomes relatively more important.

## Method

The author used heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory at one-loop order to compute the spectral representation of the nucleon two-point function and the finite-time corrections to the axial current three-point function. The computation was performed in coordinate space, with mass and wavefunction renormalization fixing the single-nucleon contribution at asymptotically large times. The resulting spectral weights for pion-nucleon and pion-delta fluctuations were derived, and the ratio of the axial three-point to two-point functions was constructed to study the time dependence of the axial charge extraction.

## Key numbers

- The pion-nucleon spectral weight near threshold vanishes as (E - m_pi)^(3/2), proportional to [E^2 - m_pi^2]^(3/2) / E^2, with coefficient 6 g_A^2 / (4 pi f)^2
- The pion-delta spectral weight near threshold vanishes as [(E - Delta)^2 - m_pi^2]^(3/2) / E^2, with coefficient 16 g_DeltaN^2 / (3 (4 pi f)^2)
- For m_pi = 300 MeV, fitting the nucleon two-point function with a single exponential over the range 6 <= tau/a <= 12 (with a = 0.125 fm) overestimates the nucleon mass by approximately 3%
- The asymptotic expansion of the nucleon two-point function for large times contains a leading correction proportional to (3 g_A m_pi / (4 pi f))^2 * exp(-m_pi tau) / (m_pi tau)^(5/2)
- The times over which the asymptotic expansion in 1/(m_pi tau) is controlled are quite large, tau >= 50 a for a = 0.125 fm
- Low-energy constants used: g_A = 1.27, g_DeltaN = 1.5, Delta = 290 MeV, f = 130 MeV

## Context

This work extends the understanding of excited-state contamination in lattice QCD correlation functions, specifically addressing the signal-to-noise problem for nucleons identified by Lepage (1989) where noise is dominated by coupling to three-pions. The results provide a field-theoretic example of difficulties encountered in extracting hadronic observables, complementing variational method approaches used by Michael (1985) and Luscher (1990) and recent lattice studies of the baryon spectrum by Burch (2006), Basak (2007), Lin (2008), and Bulava (2009). The finding that improved nucleon operators can lead to underestimation of the axial charge offers a possible explanation for the falling values of G_A observed in dynamical three-flavor simulations by Yamazaki (2008), which had been attributed to finite volume effects inconsistent with expectations from chiral perturbation theory by Beane (2004) and Detmold (2004).
