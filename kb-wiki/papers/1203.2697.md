# Berry Curvature, Triangle Anomalies, and the Chiral Magnetic Effect in Fermi Liquids

> In a three-dimensional Fermi liquid, quasiparticles near the Fermi surface may possess a Berry curvature. We show that if the Berry curvature has a nonvanishing flux through the Fermi surface, the particle number associated with this Fermi surface has a triangle anomaly in external electromagnetic fields. We show how Landau's Fermi liquid theory should be modified to take into account the Berry curvature. We show that the "chiral magnetic effect" also emerges from the Berry curvature flux.

## Key claim

The paper shows that if the Berry curvature has a nonvanishing flux through a Fermi surface, the particle number associated with that Fermi surface exhibits a triangle anomaly in external electromagnetic fields, with the anomaly coefficient determined solely by the monopole charge of the Berry curvature on the Fermi surface, independent of interaction strength.

## Method

The authors reformulate Landau's Fermi liquid theory as a Hamiltonian system with a commutation relation for the occupation number operator, then modify this commutation relation to incorporate Berry curvature by altering the classical Poisson bracket. They derive the density-density commutator and show that a nonzero Berry curvature flux through the Fermi surface produces an anomalous term proportional to the external magnetic field, which leads to the anomalous nonconservation of particle number and the chiral magnetic effect.

## Key numbers

- The anomalous nonconservation equation is: ∂n/∂t + ∇·j = (k/4π²) E·B, where k is the monopole charge of Berry curvature flux through the Fermi surface.
- The chiral magnetic effect current is: j = (k/4π²) μ B, where μ is the chemical potential.
- For two Fermi surfaces with opposite monopole charges (k=+1 and k=-1) and unequal chemical potentials, the current equals (μ₊-μ₋)B/4π².
- The monopole charge k is defined as k = (1/2π) ∫ dS·Ω, where Ω is the Berry curvature.

## Context

This work extends the connection between Berry curvature and anomalous transport from noninteracting systems to interacting Fermi liquids, building on Landau's Fermi liquid theory (Landau) and prior work on Berry phase effects in semiclassical electron dynamics (Sundaram and Niu, 1999) and the anomalous Hall effect (Haldane, 2004). The paper shows that triangle anomalies, previously known in relativistic quantum field theory (Adler, 1969; Bell and Jackiw, 1969), are properties of any Fermi liquid with Berry curvature flux, enabling the study of anomaly-related phenomena such as the chiral magnetic effect (Nielsen and Ninomiya, 1983; Fukushima, Kharzeev, and Warringa, 2008) in strongly interacting systems and condensed matter contexts like doped Weyl semimetals (Vishwanath, 2011; Burkov and Balents, 2011).
