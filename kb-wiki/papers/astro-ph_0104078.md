# Quantum Field Theoretic Description of Matter in the Universe

> Quantum field theory at finite temperature and density can be used for describing the physics of relativistic plasmas. Such systems are frequently encountered in astrophysical situations, such as the early Universe, Supernova explosions, and the interior of neutron stars. After a brief introduction to thermal field theory the usefulness of this approach in astrophysics will be exemplified in three different cases. First the interaction of neutrinos within a Supernova plasma will be discussed. Then the possible presence of quark matter in a neutron star core and finally the interaction of light with the Cosmic Microwave Background will be considered.

## Key claim

Quantum field theory at finite temperature and density can be used to describe relativistic plasmas in astrophysical settings, but the paper presents this as an illustrative review of three specific applications rather than a single unified result; the abstract's claim of "usefulness" is hedged by the paper's focus on selected examples (neutrino interactions in supernovae, quark matter in neutron stars, and light interacting with the cosmic microwave background) rather than a comprehensive demonstration.

## Method

The paper uses thermal field theory, specifically the imaginary-time formalism (ITF) and real-time formalism (RTF), to compute self-energies, effective propagators, and dispersion relations for particles in a medium. For neutrinos in a supernova plasma, the differential rate for νν̅-annihilation is derived from the polarization tensor including effects of a strong magnetic field via Landau levels. For strange quark matter, the one-loop quark self-energy in the high-density limit yields an effective density-dependent quark mass, which is used to construct a quasiparticle gas equation of state. For light interacting with the cosmic microwave background, the photon self-energy is computed from an effective Lagrangian for photon-photon interaction at finite temperature.

## Key numbers

- Thermal photon "mass" in QED: m_γ² = e²T²/9, derived from the hard-thermal-loop (HTL) limit of the polarization tensor.
- Effective quark mass at zero momentum: m_q* = m_q/2 + (m_q²/4 + g²μ²/(6π²))^(1/2), where μ ≈ 300 MeV and g ≈ 2–4.
- Presence of a quark matter core reduces the radius of a neutron star by typically 20–30%, and reduces the maximum mass to about 1.5 M_⊙.
- Phase velocity of light in the cosmic microwave background at decoupling (T ≈ 3000 K): v_p = 1 − 5.6 × 10⁻³¹; at present (T = 2.7 K): v_p = 1 − 4.7 × 10⁻⁴³.
- Upper limits on photon mass: laboratory experiments give m_γ < 2 × 10⁻¹⁶ eV; galactic magnetic field gives m_γ < 10⁻²⁷ eV.

## Context

This paper reviews and extends prior work applying thermal field theory to astrophysical plasmas, including the MSW effect from neutrino self-energies (Noetzold 1988), the plasmon effect for neutrino pair emission in stars (Adams 1963; Braaten 1991), and the sphaleron decay rate during the electroweak phase transition (Boedeker 1998). The discussion of strange quark matter builds on the ideal Fermi gas model of Witten (1984) and Farhi (1984), while the photon-photon interaction in the CMB uses the effective Lagrangian of Heisenberg (1936). The paper enables future work on neutrino processes in strong magnetic fields relevant to magnetars and gamma-ray bursts, and on the observable consequences of quark matter cores in neutron stars.
