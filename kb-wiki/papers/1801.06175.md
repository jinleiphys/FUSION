# Nuclear deformation in the configuration-interaction shell model

> We review a method that we recently introduced to calculate the finite-temperature distribution of the axial quadrupole operator in the laboratory frame using the auxiliary-field Monte Carlo technique in the framework of the configuration-interaction shell model. We also discuss recent work to determine the probability distribution of the quadrupole shape tensor as a function of intrinsic deformation $β,γ$ by expanding its logarithm in quadrupole invariants. We demonstrate our method for an isotope chain of samarium nuclei whose ground states describe a crossover from spherical to deformed shapes.

# Nuclear deformation in the configuration-interaction shell model

## Key claim
The finite-temperature distribution of the axial quadrupole operator in the laboratory frame, calculated using the auxiliary-field Monte Carlo method within the configuration-interaction shell model, provides a model-independent signature of nuclear deformation, and the intrinsic shape distribution can be extracted via a Landau-like expansion in quadrupole invariants up to fourth order. The method is demonstrated for samarium isotopes whose ground states describe a crossover from spherical to deformed shapes, but the intrinsic shape distributions are validated only by comparing the resulting lab-frame distributions with the directly calculated AFMC distributions.

## Method
The authors used the auxiliary-field Monte Carlo (AFMC) method, also known as the shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method, to represent the Gibbs operator as a superposition of non-interacting propagators via the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation. They computed the laboratory-frame distribution P(q) of the axial quadrupole operator Q20 by representing the delta function as a Fourier integral and using a discrete Fourier transform, with angle averaging over 21 rotation angles to improve thermalization. The intrinsic-frame distribution P(T,β,γ) was obtained by expanding its logarithm in quadrupole invariants up to fourth order (β², β³cos3γ, β⁴) and matching the expectation values of these invariants to those computed from the lab-frame moments.

## Key numbers
- A time slice of Δβ = 1/64 MeV⁻¹ was used in the discretized Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation.
- Approximately 5000 auxiliary-field configurations were used for each temperature.
- The quadrupole deformation parameters are defined as q_{2μ} = (3/√(5π)) 3 r₀² A^{5/3} α_{2μ} with r₀ = 1.2 fm.
- The metric for transformation to intrinsic-frame variables is (1/2) β⁴ |sin(3γ)| dβ dγ dΩ.
- The quadrupole invariants up to fourth order are: ⟨Q·Q⟩ = 5⟨Q₂₀²⟩, ⟨(Q×Q)⁽²⁾·Q⟩ = -5√(7/2)⟨Q₂₀³⟩, and ⟨(Q·Q)²⟩ = (35/3)⟨Q₂₀⁴⟩.
- The intrinsic invariants in terms of β,γ are: α·α = β², [α×α]₂·α = -√(2/7) β³ cos(3γ), and (α·α)² = β⁴.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous development of the AFMC method for calculating axial quadrupole distributions in the laboratory frame (Alhassid 2014, Gilbreth 2017) and their recent work on quadrupole shape distributions in the intrinsic frame (Mustonen 2017). It builds on the use of quadrupole invariants introduced by Kumar (1972) and Cline (1986) to extract intrinsic deformation without invoking a mean-field approximation, as previously applied in lighter nuclei (Henderson 2016, Schmitt 2017). The method enables the calculation of deformation-dependent level densities for modeling nuclear shape dynamics such as fission, using a saddle-point approximation to convert the finite-temperature intrinsic shape distribution to level densities as a function of excitation energy and intrinsic deformation.
