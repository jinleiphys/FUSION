# The Prediction of the Maximum Modes of Decay of Mesons

> In an earlier work, several properties of fundamental particles were brought together by a simple equation based on continuity and discreteness. It is shown here, that the maximum modes of decay of all fundamental particles can also be predicted without any arbitrary parameters. The method used is to break up the mean lifetimes of particles to obtain the maximum modes of decay. This is done by using a binary expansion of $\hbar/MT$ where M is the mass of the particle and T is the mean lifetime. The agreements between that obtained from theory and experiment are remarkable. The ordering of the flavours plays an important part in understanding the reasons for this agreement. It is shown that the Zeno effect in Quantum mechanics is connected with use of the binary series.

# The Prediction of the Maximum Modes of Decay of Mesons

## Key claim
The maximum decay modes (MDM) of all fundamental particles can be predicted without any arbitrary parameters by expanding the dimensionless quantity ħ/MT (where M is mass and T is mean lifetime) into a binary series, with the claim that the resulting agreements between theory and experiment are "remarkable", though the paper acknowledges that for many particles no measured MDM values exist, and the predicted values for those are "comparatively small, showing that it is difficult to measure their MDM's."

## Method
The author computes the integer n from the relation ħ/MT = n/2^n for each particle using published mass and lifetime data, then expands ħ/MT as a binary decimal series. The number of leading zeros (H) in the binary expansion and the binary decimal value (BD) are used to construct empirical expressions, specifically, differences of (H_m - H_{m-1}) log 2 + log(BD_m/BD_{m-1}) and differences of (log M)/BD ordered by flavour, which are compared to the measured maximum decay modes. The particles are ordered by serial number within flavour groups, and the consecutive absolute differences of these quantities are plotted against experimental MDM values.

## Key numbers
- For π± meson: n = 58, number of leading zeros = 53, binary decimal = 1.813, MDM = 100%
- For π0 meson: n = 29, number of leading zeros = 25, binary decimal = 1.813, MDM = 99%
- For η meson: n = 23, number of leading zeros = 19, binary decimal = 1.348, MDM = 71%
- For ρ(770) meson: n = 5, number of leading zeros = 3, binary decimal = 1.25, MDM = 100%
- For K± meson: n = 59, number of leading zeros = 54, binary decimal = 1.844, MDM = 64%
- For J/ψ(1S): n = 19, number of leading zeros = 15, binary decimal = 1.0, MDM = 86%
- For B± meson: n = 49, number of leading zeros = 44, binary decimal = 1.531, MDM = 6%
- For τ lepton: n = 45, number of leading zeros = 40, binary decimal = 1.406, MDM = 65%
- The half-life of the proton is predicted from the half-life of the neutron as 5.33 × 10^33 years

## Context
This work extends the author's earlier systematic approach (Ramanna and Sharma, Current Science, 1997) that used the dimensionless equation ħ/MT = n/2^n to systematize mean half-lives of fundamental particles, beta and alpha emitters, and to determine parity in beta decay and energy levels of light nuclei. The present paper attempts to connect this framework to the quantum Zeno effect by relating the ratio N/2^N (from survival probability ratios) to the width-to-lifetime ratio Γ/T from equation (1). The binary expansion method is presented as a way to predict maximum decay modes for mesons where no measurements exist, though the paper notes these predicted values are small and difficult to measure experimentally.
