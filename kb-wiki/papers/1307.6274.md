# Three-nucleon forces in the 1/Nc expansion

> The operator structures that can contribute to three-nucleon forces are classified in the 1/Nc expansion. At leading order in 1/Nc a spin-flavor independent term is present, as are the spin-flavor structures associated with the Fujita-Miyazawa three-nucleon force. Modern phenomenological three-nucleon forces are thus consistent with this O(Nc) leading force, corrections to which are suppressed by a power series in 1/Nc^2. A complete basis of operators for the three-nucleon force, including all independent momentum structures, is given explicitly up to next-to-leading order in the 1/Nc expansion.

## Key claim

The operator structures of three-nucleon forces are classified in the 1/Nc expansion, with the leading-order force containing a spin-flavor independent term and the spin-flavor structures of the Fujita-Miyazawa three-nucleon force; corrections are suppressed by powers of 1/Nc^2, but the analysis only identifies operator structures in the three-nucleon energy that do not arise from the large-Nc analysis of the NN potential, without providing information on the momentum dependence of the force.

## Method

The authors extended the 1/Nc analysis of the NN potential by Kaplan and Manohar (1997) to the three-nucleon system, starting from the Hartree expansion for the nuclear Hamiltonian in the large-Nc limit. They classified spin-flavor structures using the SU(4) algebra generated by one-body quark operators S, I, and G, then reduced products of operators acting on the same nucleon using Casimir relations and reduction rules. The resulting operator structures were combined with momentum tensors formed from Jacobi momenta p and q, respecting parity, time-reversal, and permutation symmetry, to construct a complete basis of three-nucleon force operators up to next-to-leading order in 1/Nc.

## Key numbers

* The leading-order three-nucleon force is O(Nc), with corrections suppressed by a power series in 1/Nc^2, where 1/Nc^2 ≈ 1/10 in our world.
* The triton binding energy is underestimated by about 800 keV when using two-nucleon potentials alone.
* The quadratic SU(4) Casimir operator yields the identity G^{ia}G^{ia} = (3/16) Nc(Nc+4) 1 - (1/4) I^a I^a - (1/4) S^i S^i.
* Matrix elements of G between nucleon states are O(Nc), while matrix elements of S and I are O(1).
* The leading-order NN potential contains three structures: one S=0, L=0 unit operator; one S=0, L=0 operator σ1·σ2 τ1·τ2; and one S=2, L=2 tensor operator [p- p-]2 · [σ1 σ2]2 τ1·τ2, all multiplied by Nc.
* At subleading order O(1/Nc) in the NN potential, there are four L=0 structures, two L=1 structures, and two L=2 structures.

## Context

This work extends the 1/Nc expansion analysis of nuclear forces, previously applied to the NN potential by Kaplan and Manohar (1997) and by Savage and Kaplan (1996), to the three-nucleon system. The leading-order three-nucleon force is consistent with the Fujita-Miyazawa (1957) force and with the leading-order chiral perturbation theory three-nucleon force derived by van Kolck (1994), which includes the same spin-flavor structures. The classification provides a systematic framework for identifying the most relevant operator structures in three-nucleon forces, which could help resolve puzzles such as the Ay discrepancy in neutron-deuteron scattering that neither model three-nucleon forces nor leading-order chiral three-nucleon forces can explain.
