# Topological Currents in Neutron Stars: Kicks, Precession, Toroidal Fields, and Magnetic Helicity

> The effects of anomalies in high density QCD are striking. We consider a direct application of one of these effects, namely topological currents, on the physics of neutron stars. All the elements required for topological currents are present in neutron stars: degenerate matter, large magnetic fields, and P-parity violating processes. These conditions lead to the creation of vector currents capable of carrying momentum and inducing magnetic fields. We estimate the size of these currents for many representative states of dense matter in the neutron star and argue that they could be responsible for the large proper motion of neutron stars (kicks), the toroidal magnetic field and finite magnetic helicity needed for stability of the poloidal field, and the resolution of the conflict between type-II superconductivity and precession. Though these observational effects appear unrelated, they likely originate from the same physics -- they are all P-odd phenomena that stem from a topological current generated by parity violation.

## Key claim

Topological vector currents, induced by parity violation in dense QCD matter, can simultaneously explain several seemingly unrelated neutron star phenomena (kicks, toroidal fields, magnetic helicity, and precession) provided that the neutron star is a finite system where the weak interaction mean free path exceeds the stellar radius, preventing the washout of the left-right asymmetry.

## Method

The authors estimate the magnitude of topological vector currents induced by an imbalance between left- and right-handed electron chemical potentials in the presence of a magnetic field, using the relation ⟨j⟩ = (μ_l − μ_r)eΦ/(2π²) at zero temperature. They evaluate this current for multiple representative states of dense matter in neutron stars, normal nuclear matter (direct and modified Urca processes), matter with kaon condensates, and quark matter, by calculating the asymmetry produced by parity-violating weak interactions and the magnetic flux structure (type-II vortices or intermediate state domains). The current is confined to regions where magnetic flux is present, and the total current leaving the star depends only on the total magnetic flux, not its detailed spatial distribution.

## Key numbers

- Electron Fermi momentum in normal nuclear matter: k_e ≈ 100 (n/n₀)^(2/3) MeV, where n₀ = 0.17 fm⁻³ is nuclear density.
- Neutron Fermi momentum at nuclear density: k_N ≈ 340 (n/n₀)^(1/3) MeV.
- Quark Fermi momentum in quark matter: k_q ≈ 235 (n_b/n₀)^(1/3) MeV, with typical core value k_q ∼ 400 MeV.
- Electron fraction in quark matter: Y_e = n_e/n_b ≈ 0.01.
- Total number of quantum flux units in a neutron star: N_v ∼ 10³¹ B₁₂, where B₁₂ ≡ B/(10¹² G).
- Ratio of area occupied by flux tubes to total stellar area: A_vortices/A_star ≈ 10⁻³ · B₁₂.
- Quantum flux unit: Φ₀ = 2π/q = 2 × 10⁻⁷ G·cm².
- Typical domain size in intermediate state: a ∼ 10√(Rλ) ∼ 10⁻¹ cm, where R ∼ 10 km and λ ∼ 100 fm.
- The kick mechanism can explain proper motion of hyperfast pulsars with v > 800 km/s, specifically B1508+55 moving at v = 1083⁺¹⁰³₋₉₀ km/s.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analysis (Charbonneau and Zhitnitsky, 2007) which showed that topological currents could change superconductivity from type-II to type-I in neutron stars, by providing quantitative estimates of the induced currents across multiple dense matter scenarios. It directly addresses the conflict between type-II superconductivity and observed neutron star precession raised by Link (2003, 2006), and proposes a unified mechanism for kicks, toroidal field generation, and magnetic helicity production that does not rely on rotation-induced instabilities (which produce P-even correlations ⟨B·Ω⟩ incapable of generating helicity). The mechanism is similar to neutrino emission-based kick models but differs crucially in that it operates at low temperatures (T ≪ MeV) and produces sustained kicks over hundreds of years rather than seconds, consistent with observational analysis by Spruit (1998) and spin-kick correlations observed by Wang (2006) and Rankin (2007).
