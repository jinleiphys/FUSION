# High-precision calculation of the strange nucleon electromagnetic form factors

> We report a direct lattice QCD calculation of the strange nucleon electromagnetic form factors $G_E^s$ and $G_M^s$ in the kinematic range $0 \leq Q^2 \lesssim 1.2\: {\rm GeV}^2$. For the first time, both $G_E^s$ and $G_M^s$ are shown to be nonzero with high significance. This work uses closer-to-physical lattice parameters than previous calculations, and achieves an unprecedented statistical precision by implementing a recently proposed variance reduction technique called hierarchical probing. We perform model-independent fits of the form factor shapes using the $z$-expansion and determine the strange electric and magnetic radii and magnetic moment. We compare our results to parity-violating electron-proton scattering data and to other theoretical studies.

# High-precision calculation of the strange nucleon electromagnetic form factors

## Key claim

Both strange nucleon electromagnetic form factors $G_E^s$ and $G_M^s$ are shown to be nonzero with high significance for the first time, but this result is obtained at a pion mass of 317 MeV rather than at the physical pion mass, and the physical-point estimates rely on a linear interpolation in $m_\text{loop}^2$ rather than a controlled chiral extrapolation.

## Method

The authors perform a direct lattice QCD calculation of the strange-quark disconnected contributions to the nucleon electromagnetic form factors on a $32^3 \times 96$ lattice with dimensions $(3.6\ \text{fm})^3 \times (10.9\ \text{fm})$ using 2+1 flavors of dynamical clover-improved Wilson fermions at a pion mass of 317 MeV. They evaluate the disconnected quark loops using hierarchical probing, a variance reduction technique based on Hadamard vectors, with 128 vectors eliminating variance from neighboring lattice sites up to distance 4, and combine this with 1028 gauge configurations and 96 source locations per configuration. The $Q^2$ dependence is fit using the $z$-expansion with $k_\text{max}=5$, and the strange electric and magnetic radii and magnetic moment are extracted from the fit coefficients.

## Key numbers

- Strange electric radius at the physical point: $(r_E^2)^s = -0.0067(10)(17)(15)\ \text{fm}^2$
- Strange magnetic radius at the physical point: $(r_M^2)^s = -0.018(6)(5)(5)\ \text{fm}^2$
- Strange magnetic moment at the physical point: $\mu^s = -0.022(4)(4)(6)\ \mu_N$
- At pion mass 317 MeV: $(r_E^2)^s = -0.0054(9)(6)(11)(2)\ \text{fm}^2$, $(r_M^2)^s = -0.0147(61)(28)(34)(5)\ \text{fm}^2$, $\mu^s = -0.0184(45)(12)(32)(1)\ \mu_N^\text{lat}$ where $\mu_N^\text{lat}$ uses the lattice nucleon mass of 1067(8) MeV
- Lattice dimensions: $(3.6\ \text{fm})^3 \times (10.9\ \text{fm})$ with $32^3 \times 96$ sites
- Pion mass: 317 MeV; $m_\pi L = 5.9$
- Kinematic range: $0 \leq Q^2 \lesssim 1.2\ \text{GeV}^2$ with 59 different $Q^2$ values
- Variance reduction factor: approximately 10 compared to traditional noise method with equal computational cost
- Source-sink separations: 1.14 fm (main results) and 1.37 fm (excited-state estimate)

## Context

This work extends direct lattice QCD calculations of strange nucleon form factors beyond earlier studies by Doi et al. (2009), which used a $16^3 \times 32$ lattice with pion masses 600-840 MeV and found $G_M^s$ negative at 2-3 sigma significance while $G_E^s$ was consistent with zero, and by Babich et al. (2010), which used an anisotropic $24^3 \times 64$ lattice at 416 MeV pion mass with large uncertainties. The results enable comparison with parity-violating electron-proton scattering experiments (Spayde 2003, Beise 2004, Aniol 2004, Maas 2004, Aniol 2005, Armstrong 2005, Acha 2006, Androic 2009, Baunack 2009, Ahmed 2011) and with phenomenological determinations of the strange magnetic moment (Shanahan 2014, Leinweber 2004, Wang 2014, Hobbs 2014, Lyubovitskij 2002, Hammer 1999). The techniques demonstrated here for reducing noise in disconnected quark loop calculations are applicable to precision studies of other nucleon matrix elements and to the proton charge radius puzzle (Pohl 2010, Antognini 2013).
