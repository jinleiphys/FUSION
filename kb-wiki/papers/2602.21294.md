# Shock-induced chiral magnetic effect

> Weak-interaction-mediated chiral imbalance generation in idealized massless electrons during core-collapse supernovae was once proposed to be the source of strong magnetic fields found in neutron stars. The effect goes by the name of chiral plasma instability (CPI). However, it was found that a finite electron mass damps out this process, inactivating the instability and preventing magnetic field growth. In this work we show that the instability can survive in the presence of abrupt density and temperature perturbation that drives the system sufficiently far out of weak equilibrium. As an example, we work with such perturbations generated by shockwaves which are common during both core collapse as well as neutron star mergers. We find that the chiral imbalance resulting from shock waves, under the right conditions of density and temperature, can sustain the chiral plasma instability despite the damping from the electron mass. Additionally, in an already magnetized medium, the chiral magnetic effect resulting from shock wave density and temperature perturbation can generate substantial ohmic heating. Our results imply that shockwaves during core-collapse supernovae and merging neutron stars can act as a source of strong heating in a magnetized medium as well as CPI.

## Key claim

The authors show that the chiral plasma instability (CPI), previously thought to be inactivated by finite electron mass in core-collapse supernovae, can survive when abrupt density and temperature perturbations (such as those generated by shockwaves) drive the system sufficiently far out of weak equilibrium, provided the right conditions of density and temperature are met.

## Method

The authors model shockwave propagation through dense neutron-proton-electron (npe) matter using Rankine-Hugoniot relations, treating the upstream region as cold and in beta equilibrium. They calculate the density and temperature jumps across the shock front for both non-interacting and interacting equations of state, then estimate the chiral imbalance generated downstream from weak interaction processes. They evaluate whether the resulting background chiral chemical potential can sustain CPI growth or generate ohmic heating by comparing the CPI growth rate and Joule heating energy to the weak equilibration timescale.

## Key numbers

* For case I (upstream electron chemical potential μ_e = 100 MeV), the downstream density jump ratio n_B,2/n_B,1 ranges from approximately 1.5 to 4 depending on the shock strength, with downstream temperatures reaching tens of MeV.
* For case II (upstream electron chemical potential μ_e = 200 MeV), similar density jumps occur with downstream temperatures also reaching tens of MeV.
* The CPI growth rate is given by Γ_CPI = (α_EM^3 μ_5^2 log(α_EM^{-1}))/(π^2 μ_e), where α_EM is the fine structure constant and μ_5 is the chiral chemical potential.
* The Joule heating energy density dissipated in time δt is ε_J = (2/π^2) α_EM^3 log(α_EM^{-1}) (μ_5^2 B_0^2/μ_e) (δt), where B_0 is the background magnetic field strength.
* The width of the non-equilibrium region behind the shock is L_ne = v_s t_weak, where v_s is the shock velocity and t_weak is the weak interaction equilibration timescale.
* The conductivity of dense npe matter is σ ≈ μ_e/(4α_EM) × 1/log(α_EM^{-1}).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Grabowska (2014), who showed that finite electron mass damps CPI in core-collapse supernovae under constant weak interaction rates, and follows the suggestion by Kaplan (2016) that rapid density fluctuations could sustain CPI. It also builds on the work of Sigl and Leite (2015), who considered turbulent density fluctuations on scales smaller than the neutrino mean free path. The results enable future detailed simulations of shockwave environments in core-collapse supernovae and neutron star mergers to determine whether CPI and CME-induced Joule heating can significantly impact magnetic field evolution and thermal energy budgets.
