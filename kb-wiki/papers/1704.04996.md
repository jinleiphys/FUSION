# Optimized fluid dynamics for heavy ion collisions

> The (viscous) anisotropic hydrodynamic approach, especially after perturbative inclusion of all residual viscous terms, has been shown to dramatically outperform viscous hydrodynamics in several simplified situations for which exact solutions exist but which share with realistic expansion scenarios the problem of large dissipative currents. We will report on the present status of applying viscous anisotropic hydrodynamics in a highly efficient simulation of the full three-dimensional quark-gluon plasma. Results from accelerated $3{+}1$-dimensional viscous hydrodynamic simulations using graphics processing units will be compared to the anisotropic frameworks.

## Key claim

The viscous anisotropic hydrodynamic (VAH) approach, which includes residual dissipative currents via Israel-Stewart-like perturbative transport equations, is shown to outperform standard viscous hydrodynamics (VH) in simulations of the quark-gluon plasma, particularly at early times when momentum anisotropies are large; the advantage is demonstrated for a specific shear viscosity of η/s = 0.2 and an initial anisotropy parameter ξ₀ = 10.

## Method

The authors implemented a 3+1-dimensional second-order viscous hydrodynamic code on graphics processing units (GPU-VH) and compared its performance to a CPU version. They then developed a second-order viscous anisotropic hydrodynamic (VAH) framework that evolves the energy-momentum tensor decomposed with respect to the fluid four-velocity and a space-like four-vector along the beam direction, including evolution equations for the longitudinal pressure, residual bulk viscous pressure, energy-momentum diffusion current, and transverse shear stress tensor. The equations were solved numerically using a two-step Runge-Kutta scheme for time integration and the Kurganov-Tadmor algorithm for spatial derivatives, with initial conditions from a Monte Carlo Glauber model at τ₀ = 0.25 fm/c.

## Key numbers

- GPU-VH achieved speed-up factors of approximately 100–138 relative to CPU-VH on a single core, depending on grid resolution; for a 256 × 256 × 128 grid, the CPU took 129239.959 ms/step while the GPU took 939.340 ms/step, giving a speedup of 137.586.
- At τ = 0.5 fm/c, VAH showed about 15% more pressure anisotropy (PL/P⊥) in the center of the fireball and about 30% larger anisotropies outside the freeze-out surface near |x| = 5 fm compared to VH.
- The evolution was started at τ₀ = 0.25 fm/c with an initial central temperature T₀ = 0.6 GeV and initial anisotropy ξ₀ = 10.
- The freeze-out temperature used was T_f = 155 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the leading-order anisotropic hydrodynamics framework reviewed by Strickland (2014) by including residual shear stress components through perturbative transport equations derived from kinetic theory, following the approach of Denicol et al. (2014) for standard viscous hydrodynamics. The GPU acceleration builds on the earlier GPU-VH code described by Bazow et al. (2016). The VAH framework enables more reliable dynamical simulations of heavy ion collisions with fewer parameters, reducing uncertainty from the early evolution stage when the quark-gluon plasma is highly anisotropic, and can be coupled with Bayesian statistical methods for parameter estimation.
