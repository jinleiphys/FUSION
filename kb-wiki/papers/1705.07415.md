# An Empirical Scaling formula for fragments production in projectile fragmentation reactions

> A scaling phenomenon in the cross section for fragments has been found in the projectile fragmentation reaction, and an empirical scaling formula is proposed by considering the dependence of cross section on the size and asymmetry of the reaction system and the fragment itself. Furthermore, the empirical scaling formula is used to predict the production of fragment in the $^{68}$Ni/$^{69}$Cu/$^{72}$Zn + $^9$Be reactions around 90$A$ MeV. Compared to the results calculated by the statistical abrasion ablation model and the {\sc epax3} parameterizations, the empirical scaling formula can better reproduce the measured fragments. The empirical scaling formula can be used to predict the yield for fragment in projectile fragmentation reaction.

## Key claim

The paper proposes an empirical scaling formula that can reproduce measured fragment cross sections in projectile fragmentation reactions better than the statistical abrasion ablation model and the EPAX3 parameterizations, but only for fragments with mass ratio \(A_f/A_P \leq 0.8\) to avoid the influence of neutron skin structure in peripheral collisions.

## Method

The authors analyzed measured cross sections for fragments from 140\(A\) MeV \(^{40,48}\)Ca + \(^{9}\)Be and \(^{58,64}\)Ni + \(^{9}\)Be reactions taken from Mocko (2006) at NSCL. They constructed an empirical scaling formula that multiplies the measured cross section by terms accounting for the size of the projectile relative to a reference system, the isospin difference between systems, the asymmetry difference between projectile and fragment, and a size effect between fragments, with parameters \(a\), \(b\), and \(K\) adjusted piecewise based on the neutron excess \(I = N - Z\) of the fragment. The formula was then used to predict cross sections for fragments in the 94.3\(A\) MeV \(^{68}\)Ni + \(^{9}\)Be, 98.1\(A\) MeV \(^{69}\)Cu + \(^{9}\)Be, and 95.4\(A\) MeV \(^{72}\)Zn + \(^{9}\)Be reactions, and compared to predictions from a modified statistical abrasion ablation model and the EPAX3 parameterizations.

## Key numbers

- The scaling formula was developed using data from 140\(A\) MeV \(^{40,48}\)Ca + \(^{9}\)Be and \(^{58,64}\)Ni + \(^{9}\)Be reactions, with fragments restricted to \(A_f/A_P \leq 0.8\).
- The reference system was the \(^{58}\)Ni + \(^{9}\)Be reaction, which has \(N/Z = 1.071\).
- The \(N/Z\) values of the projectile nuclei used span from 1.0 (\(^{40}\)Ca) to 1.4 (\(^{48}\)Ca).
- Predictions were made for reactions at 93.7\(A\) MeV \(^{64}\)Ni + \(^{9}\)Be, 94.3\(A\) MeV \(^{68}\)Ni + \(^{9}\)Be, 98.1\(A\) MeV \(^{69}\)Cu + \(^{9}\)Be, and 95.4\(A\) MeV \(^{72}\)Zn + \(^{9}\)Be, with the \(^{69}\)Cu, \(^{72}\)Zn, and \(^{68}\)Ni projectiles having \(N/Z\) of 1.379, 1.40, and 1.429 respectively.
- For fragments with \(I \leq 1\), the parameter \(a = 1\); for \(1 < I \leq 5\), \(a = -1\); for \(I > 5\), \(a = 1\).
- For fragments with \(I \leq 1\) and \((N/Z)_P < (N/Z)_R\), \(b = |I|\); for \((N/Z)_P = (N/Z)_R\), \(b = 0\); for \((N/Z)_P > (N/Z)_R\), \(b = I - 1\).
- For fragments with \(1 < I \leq 5\), \(b = -1\); for \(I > 5\), \(b = 1\).
- For the \(^{69}\)Cu, \(^{72}\)Zn, and \(^{68}\)Ni predictions, \(K = 4\) for \(I \leq 1\) and \(K = 1/2^{(I - 2)}\) for \(I \geq 5\).

## Context

This work extends the scaling phenomena found in projectile fragmentation, such as the isoscaling phenomenon of Tsang (2001), the \(m\)-scaling phenomenon (\(m = (N-Z)/A\)) of earlier work, and the isobaric ratio difference scaling phenomenon of the authors' previous studies (2015, 2016). It improves upon the EPAX2 and EPAX3 parameterizations by providing a simpler empirical formula with fewer complex parameters, and it enables prediction of fragment yields for reactions around 100\(A\) MeV, such as the \(^{68}\)Ni/\(^{69}\)Cu/\(^{72}\)Zn + \(^{9}\)Be reactions measured by Lukyanov (2009) at NSCL. The formula is limited to fragments with \(A_f/A_P \leq 0.8\) to avoid the influence of neutron skin structure in peripheral collisions.
