# Hot spots and gluon field fluctuations as causes of eccentricity in small systems

> We calculate eccentricities in high energy proton-nucleus collisions, by calculating correlation functions of the energy density field of the Glasma immediately after the collision event at proper time tau = 0. We separately consider the effects of color charge and geometrical hot spot fluctuations, analytically performing the averages over both in a dilute-dense limit. We show that geometric fluctuations of hot spots inside the proton are the dominant source of eccentricity whereas color charge fluctuations only give a negligible correction. The size and number of hot spots are the most important parameters characterizing the eccentricities.

## Key claim

Geometric fluctuations of hot spots inside the proton are the dominant source of eccentricity in high-energy proton-nucleus collisions, while color charge fluctuations give only a negligible correction; this result is obtained in a dilute-dense limit where the proton is treated as dilute and the nucleus as a homogeneous infinite sheet.

## Method

The authors calculate correlation functions of the energy density field of the Glasma immediately after the collision at proper time tau = 0, using the Color-Glass Condensate (CGC) framework. The nucleus is treated as a homogeneous infinite sheet with Gaussian color charge fluctuations included to all orders in the color field, using the Golec-Biernat Wusthoff (GBW) parametrization for the Wilson line two-point function. The proton is modeled as a collection of N_q Gaussian hot spots of size r, with positions fluctuating within a proton of size R; the proton color charges are treated as dilute, allowing linearization of the energy density in its color charge, and averages over both hot spot positions and color charge fluctuations are performed analytically.

## Key numbers

- The nuclear saturation scale Q_s is the only parameter needed to characterize the infinite, homogeneous target nucleus in the GBW parametrization.
- The proton radius parameter R, hot spot size r, number of hot spots N_q, color charge density mu_0, infrared regulator mass m (confinement scale regulator m ~ Lambda_QCD), and short-distance cutoff C_0 are the phenomenological parameters characterizing the hot spot model.
- The energy density expectation value is given by <epsilon(x)> = (N Q_s^2)/(2 C_F) * <beta^{j,b}_x beta^{j,b}_x>, where N is the number of colors and C_F is the Casimir invariant.
- The two-point function of color charge densities within a hot spot is <rho^a(x) rho^b(y)> = sum_{i=1}^{N_q} mu^2((x+y)/2 - b_i) delta^{(2)}(x-y) delta^{ab}, where mu^2 is a Gaussian profile with width r.

## Context

This work extends the IP-Glasma model (Schenke 2012, Mantysaari 2017) by providing an analytically tractable simplification in the dilute-dense limit, enabling clean separation of geometric hot spot fluctuations from color charge fluctuations. Unlike IP-Glasma, the model neglects nucleon-level structure on the nucleus side (corresponding to the large nuclear mass number A -> infinity limit) and does not include additional "Q_s fluctuations" that make color charge fluctuations non-Gaussian (Lappi 2015). The results enable future extensions incorporating nucleon position fluctuations in the target and further constraints from experimental data on eccentricities in small collision systems.
