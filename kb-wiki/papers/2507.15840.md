# TASI/CERN/KITP Lecture Notes on "Toward Quantum Computing Gauge Theories of Nature"

> A hallmark of the computational campaign in nuclear and particle physics is the lattice-gauge-theory program. It continues to enable theoretical predictions for a range of phenomena in nature from the underlying Standard Model. The emergence of a new computational paradigm based on quantum computing, therefore, can introduce further advances in this program. In particular, it is believed that quantum computing will make possible first-principles studies of matter at extreme densities, and in and out of equilibrium, hence improving our theoretical description of early universe, astrophysical environments, and high-energy particle collisions. Developing and advancing a quantum-computing based lattice-gauge-theory program, therefore, is a vibrant and fast-moving area of research in theoretical nuclear and particle physics. These lecture notes introduce the topic of quantum computing lattice gauge theories in a pedagogical manner, with an emphasis on theoretical and algorithmic aspects of the program, and on the most common approaches and practices, to keep the presentation focused and useful. Hamiltonian formulation of lattice gauge theories is introduced within the Kogut-Susskind framework, the notion of Hilbert space and physical states is discussed, and some elementary numerical methods for performing Hamiltonian simulations are discussed. Quantum-simulation preliminaries and digital quantum-computing basics are presented, which set the stage for concrete examples of gauge-theory quantum-circuit design and resource analysis. A step-by-step analysis is provided for a simpler Abelian gauge theory, and an overview of our current understanding of the quantum-computing cost of quantum chromodynamics is presented in the end. Examples and exercises augment the material, and reinforce the concepts and methods introduced throughout.

## Key claim

These lecture notes argue that quantum computing can enable first-principles studies of gauge theories in regimes inaccessible to classical Monte Carlo methods, particularly at finite baryon density and for real-time dynamics, but the author emphasizes that this requires careful choices of Hamiltonian formulation, basis truncation, and quantum-circuit design whose optimal forms remain open research questions.

## Method

The notes present the Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian formulation of lattice gauge theories on a spatial lattice with continuous time, starting from U(1) Abelian theory and extending to SU(2) and SU(3) non-Abelian theories coupled to staggered fermions. The author then introduces basis choices for the fermionic and gauge degrees of freedom, including the electric-field (irreducible representation) basis for gauge links, and discusses the Gauss-law constraint that must be imposed to select physical states. Finally, the notes outline how these Hamiltonian formulations can be translated into digital quantum-computing algorithms through quantum-circuit design and resource analysis.

## Key numbers

* For a U(1) lattice gauge theory in (1+1)D with electric-field cutoff Λ=1 and N=2 staggered-lattice sites with periodic boundary conditions, the naive Hilbert space contains 2² × 3² = 36 states, but only 5 states satisfy the Gauss-law constraints.
* The electric-field basis for a U(1) gauge link is truncated to |E_x| ≤ Λ, giving a truncated on-link Hilbert space of size 2Λ+1.
* For a system with N_f fermion sites and N_ℓ links, the full Hilbert space dimension before imposing Gauss law is 2^{N_f} × (2Λ+1)^{N_ℓ}.

## Context

These lecture notes extend the lattice-gauge-theory program initiated by Wilson (1974) and the Hamiltonian formulation developed by Kogut and Susskind (1975), which have enabled classical Monte Carlo computations for phenomena in single-hadron and multi-hadron physics as reviewed by Aoki (2024) and others. The notes address the fundamental limitation identified by Troyer and Wiese (2005) that Monte Carlo methods suffer from the sign problem at finite baryon density and cannot access real-time dynamics, motivating the quantum-computing approach. This work enables future first-principles quantum simulations of matter at extreme densities relevant to the early universe, astrophysical environments, and high-energy particle collisions, building on the broader program reviewed by Bauer (2023) and others.
