# Effective Field Theory Factorization for Diffraction

> We derive a factorization formula for coherent and incoherent $ep$ diffraction using the soft collinear effective theory, utilizing multiple power expansion parameters to handle different kinematic regions. This goes beyond the known hard-collinear diffractive factorization to address the small-$x$ Regge dynamics and Pomeron exchange from first principles. The effective field theory analysis also uncovers and factorizes an important irreducible incoherent background generated by color-nonsinglet exchange, dubbed "quasi-diffraction", for which we calculate the associated Sudakov suppression. For unpolarized scattering we show that there are four diffractive structure functions at leading power, and point out the importance of studying $F_{3,4}^D$ through asymmetries, in addition to $F_{2,L}^D$. For the quasi-diffractive background, we make model independent predictions for ratios of the corresponding structure functions in a perturbative kinematic region. Our analysis also makes predictions for six leading-power spin-dependent structure functions. Finally, we provide connections to diffractive parton distributions, and assess the Ingelman-Schlein model. Our work lays a path for further QCD-based studies of diffraction.

## Key claim

The paper derives a first-principles Regge factorization formula for coherent and incoherent $ep$ diffraction using soft collinear effective theory (SCET), going beyond the known hard-collinear diffractive factorization to address small-$x$ Regge dynamics and Pomeron exchange; the factorization reveals that diffractive structure functions must be written as a sum of a color-singlet signal and an irreducible incoherent background from color-nonsinglet exchange ("quasi-diffraction"), with the latter subject to calculable Sudakov suppression.

## Method

The authors apply SCET with Glauber interactions to derive a factorization formula for inclusive $ep$ diffraction in the DIS region ($Q^2 \gg \Lambda_{\rm QCD}^2$), using multiple power expansion parameters to separate hard-collinear, Regge, and soft dynamics. The factorization decomposes diffractive structure functions into universal hadronic matrix elements: $F_i^{D\,{\rm diff}} = B\otimes_{\perp} S_i$ for the diffractive signal and $F_i^{D\,{\rm quasi}}=B \otimes_{\perp} U\otimes_{\pm} S_i$ for the quasi-diffractive background, where $B$ describes the proton-to-forward proton/jet transition, $S_i$ contains the photon interaction and central hadronic radiation, and $U$ encodes radiation in the rapidity gap that passes experimental cuts. The analysis identifies five distinct power counting parameters and computes the soft function describing the central rapidity region at leading order in $\alpha_s$.

## Key numbers

- Diffractive processes comprise approximately 10% of the $ep$ cross section at HERA and up to 40% of the $pp$ cross section at the LHC, and may account for up to 20% of the $ep$ cross section at the planned Electron-Ion Collider (EIC)
- At the LHC, the typical lab-frame rapidity gap between nearby particles is approximately 0.15, so a diffractive event must display a significantly larger gap, typically $\gtrsim 3$
- The diffractive cross section can be expressed in terms of 18 independent structure functions: four unpolarized ($F_{2,L,3,4}^D$) and 14 polarized
- At leading order in the forward-scattering power counting, the spin-independent diffractive structure functions $F_{2,L}^D$ are the same size as the often-ignored structure functions $F_{3,4}^D$
- The quasi-diffractive background requires only one perturbative Glauber exchange, while the diffractive signal requires at least two, implying the Sudakov suppression of the quasi-diffractive background is partly compensated by an $\alpha_s(\mu)$ suppression of the diffractive signal

## Context

This work extends the hard-collinear diffractive factorization of Berera (1994, 1995) and Collins (1997), which expressed diffractive $ep$ cross sections as a convolution of a hard coefficient and a diffractive parton distribution function obeying DGLAP evolution, but lacked a rigorous field-theoretic QCD factorization for the Regge limit. The analysis provides a first-principles derivation of Regge factorization for diffraction, connecting to the Ingelman-Schlein model (1984) and enabling systematic studies of small-$x$ dynamics including gluon saturation and nuclear shadowing at the EIC and beyond. The framework also quantifies the irreducible background from color-nonsinglet exchange ("quasi-diffraction"), which has an identical experimental signature to desired diffractive events and depends on experimental detection thresholds.
