# The phase diagram of ultra quantum liquids

> We discuss the dependence of the phase diagram of a hypothetical isotope of helium with nuclear mass less than 4 atomic mass units. We argue that with decreasing nucleus mass, the temperature of the superfluid phase transition (about 2.2 K in real He-4) increases, while that of the liquid-gas critical point (about 5.2 K in real He-4) decreases. We discuss various scenarios that may occur when the two temperatures approach each other and the order parameters of the superfluid and the liquid-gas phase transitions interact with each other. The simplest scenario, in which both order parameters become critical at particular values of the nuclear mass, temperature, and pressure, can be ruled out through on an analysis of the Landau theory. We argue that in the most likely scenario, as the nuclear mass decreases, first, a tricritical point appears on the line separating the superfluid and the normal fluid phase, then the critical point disappears under the first-order part of superfluid phase transition line, and in the end the tricritical point disappears. The last change in the phase diagram occurs when the two-body scattering length crosses zero, which corresponds to the nuclear mass of about 1.55 u. We develop a quantitative theory that allows one to determine the phase diagram in the vicinity of this point. Finally, we discuss several ways to physically realize such liquids.

## Key claim

The authors argue that as the nuclear mass of a hypothetical helium isotope is reduced below 4 atomic mass units, the superfluid transition temperature increases while the liquid-gas critical temperature decreases, leading to a specific sequence of topological changes in the phase diagram. The simplest scenario where both order parameters become critical at a single multicritical point is ruled out because it would require fine-tuning four parameters while only three (temperature, pressure, and nuclear mass) are available.

## Method

The authors analyze the phase diagram using Landau mean-field theory with two coupled order parameters: a complex superfluid condensate and a real liquid-gas order parameter (density). They numerically minimize the resulting thermodynamic potential as a function of the nuclear mass parameter, and supplement this with a finite-temperature field-theoretic calculation for the regime where the two-body scattering length approaches zero. The field theory integrates out nonzero Matsubara frequency modes to obtain a three-dimensional effective theory for the zero-frequency condensate mode, with tadpole diagrams dominating the loop expansion in the dilute limit.

## Key numbers

* The superfluid phase transition in real He-4 occurs at approximately 2.2 K, and the liquid-gas critical point at approximately 5.2 K.
* The last change in the phase diagram occurs when the two-body scattering length crosses zero, corresponding to a nuclear mass of about 1.55 atomic mass units (using the Aziz potential) or about 1.58 u (using the Lennard-Jones potential).
* The de Boer parameter for physical He-4 (mass 4 u) is approximately 0.426; the largest zero of the scattering length occurs at approximately 0.679.
* For the Lennard-Jones potential at the critical de Boer parameter, the three-body scattering hypervolume D is approximately 47 sigma^4, where sigma = 2.556 angstroms.
* The product a^2 r_0 (scattering length squared times effective range) at the critical de Boer parameter is approximately 3.23 sigma^3.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of how nuclear mass affects the zero-temperature ground state of helium (Nosanow 1975, Miller 1977, Egger 2011) and the isotopic dependence of the critical point (Young 1980). The analysis of the tricritical point and the disappearance of the liquid-gas critical point builds on the Landau theory framework and on prior work on dilute quantum droplets (Bulgac 2002, Zwerger 2019). The results enable predictions for the phase diagram of ultra-quantum liquids that could potentially be realized in systems such as spin-polarized hydrogen or other light bosonic fluids.
