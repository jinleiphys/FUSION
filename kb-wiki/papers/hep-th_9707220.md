# Spin-Statistics, Spin-Locality, and TCP: Three Distinct Theorems

> I show that the spin-statistics theorem has been confused with another theorem that I call the spin-locality theorem. I also argue that the spin-statistics theorem properly depends on the properties of asymptotic fields which are free fields. In addition, I discuss how ghosts evade both theorems, give the basis of the spin-statistics theorem for fields without asymptotic limits such as quark and gluon fields, and emphasise the weakness of the requirements for the $TCP$ theorem.

## Key claim

The spin-statistics theorem (about particles) has been confused with a distinct "spin-locality theorem" (about fields), and the TCP theorem requires much weaker conditions than either, as illustrated by a neutral scalar field quantized with Fermi statistics that obeys TCP but violates both spin-statistics and spin-locality.

## Method

Greenberg separates three theorems by their assumptions and conclusions: the spin-statistics theorem uses asymptotic free fields and local commutativity of observables to prove integer-spin particles are bosons and odd-half-integer-spin particles are fermions; the spin-locality theorem uses spacelike (anti)commutativity of fields to prove that wrong associations cause the field to vanish; the TCP theorem requires only weak local commutativity at Jost points. He amplifies Jost's example of a free neutral scalar field quantized with Fermi statistics to show it obeys TCP but produces nonlocal observables, and discusses how ghosts in gauge theories evade both theorems via indefinite metric.

## Key numbers

* For a neutral spin-0 scalar field with Fermi statistics, the anticommutator of the field is \([\phi(x),\phi(y)]_+ = \Delta^{(1)}(x-y)\), which is not local.
* For a spin-zero field, the commutator of currents contains the local distribution \(i\Delta(x-y)\) for Bose statistics and the nonlocal distribution \(\Delta^{(1)}(x-y)\) for Fermi statistics.
* For a spin-one-half field, the commutator of currents contains the local distribution \(iS(x-y)\) for Fermi statistics and the nonlocal distribution \(S^{(1)}(x-y)\) for Bose statistics.
* Ghost and antighost asymptotic fields satisfy \([C^{as}_i(x),\bar{C}^{as}_j(y)]_+ = -\delta_{ij}D(x-y)\) with anticommutators of \(C^{as}\) with itself and \(\bar{C}^{as}\) with itself vanishing.
* The ghost annihilation and creation operators obey \([C^{(as)}(k),\bar{C}^{(as)\dagger}(l)]_+ = i 2E_k \delta(\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{l})\) and \([\bar{C}^{(as)}(k),C^{(as)\dagger}(l)]_+ = -i 2E_k \delta(\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{l})\).

## Context

This paper clarifies a long-standing confusion between the spin-statistics theorem (Fierz 1939, Pauli 1940) and the spin-locality theorem (Luders and Zumino 1958, Burgoyne 1958), which are often conflated in the literature. It extends Jost's (1963) example of a wrong-statistics field that obeys TCP to show the TCP theorem's weakness, and discusses how Nakanishi and Ojima's (1982) treatment of ghosts in gauge theories evades both theorems via indefinite metric. The work enables clearer separation of foundational theorems in quantum field theory and provides a basis for understanding spin-statistics for confined fields like quarks and gluons that lack asymptotic limits.
