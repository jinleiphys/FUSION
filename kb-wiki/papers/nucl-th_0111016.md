# Future galactic supernova neutrino signal: What can we learn?

> The next supernova in our galaxy will be detected by a variety of neutrino detectors. In this lecture I discuss the set of observables needed to constrain the models of supernova neutrino emission. They are the flux normalizations, and average energies, of each of the three expected components of the neutrino flux: $ν_e$, $\barν_e$, and $ν_x$ (all the other four flavors combined). I show how the existing, or soon to be operational, neutrino detectors will be able to determine the magnitude of these observables, and estimate the corresponding rates.

# Future galactic supernova neutrino signal: What can we learn?

## Key claim
By combining data from existing or soon-to-be-operational neutrino detectors, it will be possible to determine simultaneously and independently the luminosities and average energies of all three expected components of the supernova neutrino flux (νₑ, ν̄ₑ, and νₓ), provided the next galactic supernova occurs at a typical distance of 10 kpc and follows the "standard" emission model; substantial deviations from this template would indicate either unexpected supernova behavior or the effects of neutrino oscillations.

## Method
The author defines a "template" for extracting supernova properties by calculating expected event rates in SuperKamiokande, SNO, KamLAND, and Borexino for each neutrino flavor component. For ν̄ₑ, the charged-current reaction on free protons (ν̄ₑ + p → e⁺ + n) provides high statistics and spectral information. For νₑ, the charged-current reaction on deuterons in SNO (νₑ + d → e⁻ + p + p) and on ¹⁶O in SuperKamiokande are evaluated, with the latter becoming significant only if oscillations raise the effective νₑ temperature. For νₓ (ν_μ, ν_τ and their antiparticles), neutral-current signals are identified: ¹⁶O excitation in water Cherenkov detectors, deuteron disintegration in SNO, ¹²C excitation to the 15.11 MeV state in scintillation detectors, and elastic scattering on protons in low-threshold detectors.

## Key numbers
- For a "standard" supernova at 10 kpc with binding energy 3×10⁵³ erg and emission time ~10 s: expected ν̄ₑ events in SuperKamiokande ~8300 e⁺ events; in SNO light water ~360 events; in SNO deuteron channel ~80 events; in KamLAND ~330 events.
- Expected νₑ events: in SNO deuteron channel ~80 events; in SuperKamiokande via ¹⁶O only ~20 events at T(νₑ)=3.5 MeV, rising to ~860 events if oscillations raise effective temperature to 8 MeV.
- Expected νₓ neutral-current events: in SuperKamiokande via ¹⁶O excitation ~700 events; in SNO deuteron disintegration ~400 νₓ events plus ~85 from νₑ and ν̄ₑ; in KamLAND via ¹²C 15.11 MeV γ ~60 events.
- Hierarchy of decoupling temperatures: T(νₓ) ~8 MeV > T(ν̄ₑ) ~5 MeV > T(νₑ) ~3.5 MeV, corresponding to average energies ⟨E(νₓ)⟩ ~25 MeV > ⟨E(ν̄ₑ)⟩ ~16 MeV > ⟨E(νₑ)⟩ ~11 MeV.
- Elastic scattering on protons in scintillation detectors could yield ~10% resolution on both νₓ temperature and total energy carried by νₓ neutrinos.

## Context
This lecture extends the analysis of the SN1987A observations by Kamiokande (1987) and IMB (1987), which detected only ν̄ₑ with 11 and 8 events respectively, and builds on the supernova neutrino emission models of Woosley and Janka. The work provides a quantitative framework for the next galactic supernova, enabling tests of the equal-luminosity assumption across all six neutrino flavors and the predicted hierarchy of decoupling temperatures. The proposed template also allows discrimination between standard supernova behavior and scenarios involving neutrino oscillations, as previously explored by Haxton and Kolbe for ¹⁶O reactions and by Beacom for elastic scattering on protons.
