# From nuclear multifragmentation reactions to supernova explosions

> In this talk I discuss properties of hot stellar matter at sub-nuclear densities which is formed in supernova explosions. I emphasize that thermodynamic conditions there are rather similar to those created in the laboratory by intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions. Theoretical methods developed for the description of multi-fragment final states in such reactions can be used also for description of the stellar matter. I present main steps of the statistical approach to the equation of state and nuclear composition, dealing with an ensemble of nuclear species instead of one "average" nucleus.

## Key claim

The thermodynamic conditions in supernova explosions at sub-nuclear densities are similar to those created in intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions, so theoretical methods developed for multifragmentation reactions can be applied to describe stellar matter. The paper presents a statistical approach that considers an ensemble of nuclear species rather than a single "average" nucleus, which is a more realistic description than previous calculations.

## Method

The author generalizes the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) for supernova conditions by including electrons for charge neutrality, photons, and a neutrino wind. The nuclear composition is calculated within the Grand Canonical Ensemble using chemical potentials determined from baryon number conservation and electro-neutrality, with the internal free energy of nuclear species parameterized in a liquid-drop model including bulk, surface, symmetry, and Coulomb terms with electron screening. Numerical calculations fix temperature T, baryon density ρ_B, and electron fraction Y_e, then iteratively find chemical potentials to compute yields of all nuclei with 1 ≤ A ≤ 1000 and 0 ≤ Z ≤ A.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear density: ρ_0 = 0.15 fm⁻³
- Temperatures reached in supernova: T ≈ (0.5 to 10) MeV
- Baryon densities reached: ρ_B ≈ (10⁻⁵ to 2) ρ_0
- Model parameters: w_0 = 16 MeV, ε_0 = 16 MeV, β_0 = 18 MeV, T_c = 18 MeV, γ = 25 MeV
- r_0 = 1.17 fm for Coulomb term
- For Y_e = 0.4 at T = 1 MeV, most probable nuclei have Z/A ratios of 0.400, 0.406, and 0.439 at densities 0.1ρ_0, 10⁻³ρ_0, and 10⁻⁵ρ_0 respectively
- For Y_e = 0.2 at T = 1 MeV, corresponding Z/A ratios are 0.280, 0.359, and 0.420
- At T = 3 MeV and Y_L = 0.2, heavy nuclei with 200 < A < 400 appear at ρ_B = 0.1ρ_0 with Z/A ≈ 0.27
- Along isentrope S/B = 1.0, widest mass distribution at T = 3.39 MeV, ρ_B = 10⁻¹ρ_0 extends to A ≈ 230
- Along isentrope S/B = 4.0, nuclei with 20 < A < 80 survive at ρ_B = 10⁻³ρ_0, T = 1.03 MeV
- Internal excitation energies at T ≤ 3 MeV are less than 1.0 MeV/nucleon

## Context

This work extends the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM), reviewed in SMM (year not given), which successfully describes multifragmentation reactions, to supernova conditions. It builds on initial steps by Botvina (2004) and Botvina (2005), and contrasts with earlier calculations by Lamb (year not given) and Lattimer (year not given) that considered only one "average" nucleus. The approach enables more realistic calculations of nuclear composition and equation of state for supernova simulations, which is important for understanding electron capture rates (Hix, year not given), neutrino-induced reactions (Hor, year not given), and the r-process (Qui, year not given).
