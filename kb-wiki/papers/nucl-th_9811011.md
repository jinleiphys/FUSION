# Thermal analysis of hadron multiplicities from relativistic quantum molecular dynamics

> Some questions arising in the application of the thermal model to hadron production in heavy ion collisions are studied. We do so by applying the thermal model of hadron production to particle yields calculated by the microscopic transport model RQMD(v2.3). We study the bias of incomplete information about the final hadronic state on the extraction of thermal parameters.It is found that the subset of particles measured typically in the experiments looks more thermal than the complete set of stable particles. The hadrons which show the largest deviations from thermal behaviour in RQMD(v2.3) are the multistrange baryons and antibaryons. We also looked at the influence of rapidity cuts on the extraction of thermal parameters and found that they lead to different thermal parameters and larger disagreement between the RQMD yields and the thermal model.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the subset of hadrons typically measured in experiments appears more thermal than the complete set of stable particles from the RQMD(v2.3) transport model, and that the multistrange baryons and antibaryons (especially Omega and anti-Omega) show the largest deviations from thermal behavior, but the thermal parameters extracted from the reduced set are still close to those from the full set.

## Method

The authors applied a grand-canonical thermal model with temperature T, chemical potentials for u, d, s quarks, and a strangeness saturation factor gamma_s to fit the 4pi multiplicities of stable hadrons generated by the RQMD(v2.3) transport model for S+S and Pb+Pb collisions at SPS energies. They compared fits using only the subset of particles typically measured in experiments to fits using all stable hadrons, and also studied the effect of applying rapidity cuts of varying width around midrapidity on the extracted thermal parameters.

## Key numbers

- For Pb+Pb collisions, the extracted temperature from the thermal fit to the reduced set of RQMD particles differed by about 30 MeV from the experimental fit in Becattini (1998)
- The chi-squared per degree of freedom increased by a factor of about 25 when fitting all stable particles compared to the reduced set (e.g., from chi2/DOF = 2.37/4 to 28.00/3 for Pb+Pb using only statistical errors)
- The largest deviations from the thermal fit in standard deviations were: Omega (S+S, 29 s.d.; Pb+Pb, 15 s.d.), anti-Omega (S+S, 9 s.d.; Pb+Pb, 8 s.d.), phi (S+S, 6.7 s.d.; Pb+Pb, 4.7 s.d.), and K+ (S+S, 9 s.d.)
- Excluding Omega, anti-Omega, and all Xi states from the fit reduced chi2/DOF from 224/21 to 8.5/15 for S+S and from 191/21 to 6.9/15 for Pb+Pb
- The average deviation of the best thermal fit from RQMD mean multiplicity was about 20% for the full fit, about 8% excluding Omega and anti-Omega, and about 4% excluding Omega, all Xi states and their antiparticles
- For Pb+Pb at 158A GeV, the extracted temperature was 154 MeV with mu_B/T = 1.57, compared to 140 MeV and mu_B/T = 1.50 from a UrQMD study by Bass (1998)

## Context

This work extends the debate on whether hadron production in heavy ion collisions is thermal, following earlier thermal model studies by Becattini (1998), Rafelski (1991), and Letessier (1998). The authors use RQMD(v2.3) as a controlled microscopic model to test the bias from incomplete experimental information, finding that the multistrange baryons (especially Omega) are the main source of deviation from thermal behavior, consistent with the early decoupling of Omega seen in Hecke (1998). The results enable future studies to focus on whether the extracted chemical freeze-out parameters are meaningful given violations of global equilibrium, and caution that rapidity cuts introduce a nonnegligible bias on thermal parameters.
