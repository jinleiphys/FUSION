# Emerging collectivity from the nuclear structure of $^{132}$Xe: Inelastic neutron scattering studies and shell-model calculations

> Inelastic neutron scattering was used to study the low-lying nuclear structure of $^{132}$Xe. A comprehensive level scheme is presented, as well as new level lifetimes, multipole mixing ratios, branching ratios, and transition probabilities. Comparisons of these data as well as previously measured $E2$ strengths and $g$ factors are made with new shell-model calculations for $^{132,134,136}$Xe to explore the emergence of collectivity in the Xe isotopes with $N$ < 82 near the closed shell.

## Key claim

The paper presents a comprehensive experimental level scheme for $^{132}$Xe from inelastic neutron scattering, and through comparisons with new shell-model calculations for $^{132,134,136}$Xe, it tracks the emergence of collectivity as the number of neutron holes increases from the closed-shell $^{136}$Xe, but the data show that neither $^{130}$Xe nor $^{132}$Xe emerges as a clear-cut representation of the E(5) critical-point symmetry.

## Method

Inelastic neutron scattering (INS) was performed on a solid, highly enriched $^{132}$XeF$_2$ sample, with an additional angular distribution measurement at an incident neutron energy of 3.4 MeV. Level lifetimes were extracted using the Doppler-shift attenuation method (DSAM), and multipole mixing ratios, branching ratios, and transition probabilities were determined from $\gamma$-ray angular distributions and excitation functions. Large-basis shell-model calculations including all orbitals in the $50 \le N,Z \leq 82$ major shell ($0g_{7/2}$, $1d_{5/2}$, $1d_{3/2}$, $2s_{1/2}$, and $0h_{11/2}$) were performed for $^{132,134,136}$Xe to compare with the experimental data.

## Context

This work extends previous INS studies on $^{130,132}$Xe by Peters et al. (2016) that sought a comparison with the E(5) critical-point symmetry, for which $^{130}$Xe had been proposed as a candidate by Coquard et al. (2009); the present full analysis up to 3.3 MeV shows that neither nucleus is a clear-cut representation of that symmetry. The new data and shell-model calculations, similar to those of Vogt et al. (2016, 2017) but focused on low-lying states, enable tracking the emergence of collectivity from the seniority structure of $^{136}$Xe (Van Isacker, 2018) as neutron holes increase, by examining patterns in level energies, $E2$ transition strengths, excited-state $g$ factors, and wavefunction complexity across the isotopic chain. The comprehensive level scheme and transition probabilities provide benchmarks for future microscopic calculations of nuclear structure in the $N<82$ xenon isotopes.
