# Probing Nuclear Symmetry Energy and its Imprints on Properties of Nuclei, Nuclear Reactions, Neutron Stars and Gravitational Waves

> Significant progress has been made in recent years in constraining nuclear symmetry energy at and below the saturation density of nuclear matter using data from both terrestrial nuclear experiments and astrophysical observations. However, many interesting questions remain to be studied especially at supra-saturation densities. In this lecture note, after a brief summary of the currently available constraints on nuclear symmetry energy near the saturation density we first discuss the relationship between the symmetry energy and the isopin and momentum dependence of the single-nucleon potential in isospin-asymmetric nuclear medium. We then discuss several open issues regarding effects of the tensor force induced neutron-proton short-range correlation (SRC) on nuclear symmetry energy. Finally, as an example of the impacts of nuclear symmetry energy on properties of neutron stars and gravitational waves, we illustrate effects of the high-density symmetry energy on the tidal polarizability of neutron stars in coalescing binaries.

# Probing Nuclear Symmetry Energy and its Imprints on Properties of Nuclei, Nuclear Reactions, Neutron Stars and Gravitational Waves

## Key claim

The authors argue that while significant progress has been made in constraining the nuclear symmetry energy near and below saturation density, the high-density behavior remains the most uncertain property of dense neutron-rich matter; they present their own best estimates of \(E_{\text{sym}}(\rho_0) = 31 \pm 2\) MeV and \(L(\rho_0) = 50 \pm 20\) MeV but caution that given the widely scattered constraints, it is difficult to calculate a community-average with physically meaningful error bars.

## Method

The paper is a lecture note that reviews constraints on the density-dependent nuclear symmetry energy from terrestrial experiments (isospin diffusion, isoscaling, neutron-skin measurements, pygmy dipole resonances, nucleon optical potentials, atomic mass fits) and astrophysical observations (neutron star mass-radius correlations, gravitational binding energy, crust torsional oscillations, r-mode instability). It derives analytic relations between the symmetry energy and the isoscalar and isovector single-nucleon potentials using the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem, and illustrates the impact of tensor-force-induced short-range correlations on the kinetic part of the symmetry energy. As a case study, it computes the tidal polarizability of neutron stars using two models (IU-FSU RMF and SkIU-FSU SHF) that share the same symmetry energy at and below saturation but differ at supra-saturation densities.

## Key numbers

- Best empirical values for symmetry energy at saturation density: \(E_{\text{sym}}(\rho_0) = 31 \pm 2\) MeV and slope \(L(\rho_0) = 50 \pm 20\) MeV, quoted with "optimistic error bars."
- From global nucleon optical potentials, Xu et al. obtained \(E_{\text{sym}}(\rho_0) = 31.3 \pm 4.5\) MeV and \(L(\rho_0) = 52.7 \pm 22.5\) MeV.
- From the same optical potential analysis, the neutron-proton effective mass splitting is \((m_n^* - m_p^*)/m = (0.32 \pm 0.15)\delta\).
- For symmetric nuclear matter at saturation density (\(k_F = 1.33\) fm\(^{-1}\)), the average kinetic energy with 20% high-momentum nucleons is about 40 MeV, roughly twice the free Fermi gas value of about 22 MeV.
- For a canonical 1.4 \(M_\odot\) neutron star, the tidal polarizability changes by 41.41% between the IU-FSU model (\(\lambda = 2.828 \times 10^{36}\)) and the SkIU-FSU model (\(\lambda = 1.657 \times 10^{36}\)).

## Context

This lecture note extends the body of work on constraining the nuclear symmetry energy, summarizing constraints from multiple approaches including isospin diffusion experiments (IBUU04-2005, ImQMD-2009, ImQMD-2010), isoscaling (isoscaling-2007), neutron-skin analyses (DM+n-skin 2009, SHF+n-skin), pygmy dipole resonances (PDR 2007 and 2010), and astrophysical analyses (Steiner 2010, Steiner 2012). It directly builds on the authors' prior work (Xu et al. 2010, Chen et al.) relating symmetry energy to single-nucleon potentials via the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem, and on Xu et al.'s phenomenological study of tensor-force effects on short-range correlations. The tidal polarizability analysis follows the framework of Flanagan and Hinderer (2008) and Damour and Nagar (2009, 2010, 2012). The paper identifies open questions regarding the tensor-force-induced isospin dependence of short-range correlations and the short-range behavior of the tensor force, and points to the Einstein Telescope as a future probe of high-density symmetry energy.
