# $\textit{Ab initio}$ Exact Calculation of Strongly-Correlated Nucleonic Matter

> Dense nucleonic matter is of vital importance for understanding compact stars and inferring the transition into deconfined quark phase. We present $\textit{ab initio}$ exact calculations of infinite nucleonic matter with the state-of-the-art full configuration-interaction quantum Monte Carlo (FCIQMC) method, enabling us to rigorously benchmark many-body methods and assess the degree to which the nucleonic matter is correlated. This method has been numerically validated by exact diagonalization within a small model space. Calculations of nucleonic matter using chiral nuclear forces reveal that symmetric nuclear matter is strikingly strongly correlated, raising questions on previous $\textit{ab initio}$ calculations of nuclear matter with many-body expansion truncations and offering insights into simultaneous descriptions of finite nuclei and infinite nucleonic matter from first principles.

# Ab Initio Exact Calculation of Strongly-Correlated Nucleonic Matter

## Key claim
Symmetric nuclear matter is strikingly more strongly correlated than previously expected, with high-order many-body correlations contributing up to approximately 40 MeV per particle at twice saturation density for hard chiral interactions. This finding raises questions about previous ab initio calculations that relied on many-body expansion truncations, though the authors note that the FCIQMC method itself uses the initiator approximation which introduces a systematic bias that must be extrapolated away by increasing the total number of walkers.

## Method
The authors apply the full configuration-interaction quantum Monte Carlo (FCIQMC) method to infinite nucleonic matter, representing the wavefunction as a dynamic population of signed walkers distributed across Slater determinants in the full Hilbert space. The method solves the imaginary-time Schrodinger equation via a stochastic algorithm with spawning, death/cloning, and annihilation steps, using the initiator approximation to enhance efficiency for large systems. Calculations are performed in a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions using chiral nuclear forces at N2LO, including both Delta-less (N2LO from Huther et al.) and Delta-full (Delta N2LO_GO) interactions, with the three-nucleon force included at the normal-ordered two-body level for comparison with other methods.

## Key numbers
- For the Richardson pairing model benchmark (4 nucleons, 4 levels, delta=1.0 MeV): FCIQMC with approximately 10^4 walkers reproduces exact energies across all pairing strengths with negligible statistical errors, while truncated methods show substantial deviations for |g| greater than approximately 1.0 MeV
- For symmetric nuclear matter with the Delta-less N2LO(Huther) interaction at 450 MeV cutoff: the energy contribution from missing high-order correlations is about 10 MeV around saturation density and about 40 MeV at 2.0 times saturation density (2.0 rho_0)
- For symmetric nuclear matter with the Delta-full Delta N2LO_GO(450) interaction: differences between FCIQMC and other truncated methods reach up to 2 MeV per particle
- For pure neutron matter with both interactions: all methods yield similar energies per particle with differences below 0.5 MeV
- The empirical saturation window is given as rho_0 = 0.164 +/- 0.007 fm^-3 and E_0/A = -15.86 +/- 0.57 MeV

## Context
This work extends the FCIQMC method, originally developed in quantum chemistry (Booth 2009, Cleland 2010), to infinite nucleonic matter, providing exact benchmarks for methods that rely on many-body expansion truncations. The authors compare against many-body perturbation theory up to 5th order (Drischler 2020, 2026), coupled-cluster truncated at doubles with perturbative triples (Hagen 2014), self-consistent Green's function with ADC approximation (Barbieri 2017, Marino 2026), and in-medium similarity renormalization group at the normal-ordered two-body level (Udiani 2024, Zhen 2025). The results contradict the assumption that truncated many-body methods are adequate for symmetric nuclear matter, particularly with harder interactions, and establish FCIQMC as a benchmarking tool that can help disentangle Hamiltonian deficiencies from many-body approximations in the long-standing nuclear matter saturation problem.
