# Viscosity and dissipative hydrodynamics from effective field theory

> With the goal of deriving dissipative hydrodynamics from an action, we study classical actions for open systems, which follow from the generic structure of effective actions in the Schwinger-Keldysh Closed-Time-Path formalism with two time axes and a doubling of degrees of freedom. The central structural feature of such effective actions is the coupling between degrees of freedom on the two time axes. This reflects the fact that from an effective field theory point of view, dissipation is the loss of energy of the low-energy hydrodynamical degrees of freedom to the integrated-out, UV degrees of freedom of the environment. The dynamics of only the hydrodynamical modes may therefore not posses a conserved stress-energy tensor. After a general discussion of the CTP effective actions, we use the variational principle to derive the energy-momentum balance equation for a dissipative fluid from an effective Goldstone action of the long-range hydrodynamical modes. Despite the absence of conserved energy and momentum, we show that we can construct the first-order dissipative stress-energy tensor and derive the Navier-Stokes equations near hydrodynamical equilibrium. The shear viscosity is shown to vanish in the classical theory under consideration, while the bulk viscosity is determined by the form of the effective action. We also discuss the thermodynamics of the system and analyse the entropy production.

## Key claim

The paper claims that dissipative hydrodynamics can be derived from an action principle by using the Schwinger-Keldysh Closed-Time-Path (CTP) formalism with doubled degrees of freedom, but only near hydrodynamical equilibrium; the shear viscosity vanishes in the classical theory under consideration, while the bulk viscosity is determined by the form of the effective action.

## Method

The authors construct a classical effective field theory for dissipative hydrodynamics by doubling the Goldstone scalar fields (phi^+ and phi^-) that describe long-range hydrodynamical modes, following the CTP formalism with two time axes. They write a CTP action with zeroth-order and first-order gradient-expanded terms, including couplings between the two time axes (the influence functional), and derive the energy-momentum balance equation by varying only the phi^+ fields. The resulting equations of motion take the form of the Navier-Stokes equations near equilibrium, with the stress-energy tensor constructed from the effective action coefficients.

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity eta_1 = (b^3 / 3) * sum_{i,j,k} (j - k) * bar_f_{ijk} (vanishes in the classical theory under consideration)
- Bulk viscosity is determined by coefficients chi_1 and chi_2, with chi_1 = chi_2 + b * beta, where chi_2 = (b^3 / 3) * sum_{i,j,k} sum_{l <= m} [(j - k) * bar_f_{ijk} - C_{ijk,lm}] and beta = (b^2 / 3) * sum_{i,j,k} i * bar_f_{ijk}
- Energy density epsilon = -F, pressure p = F - b * partial_b F + (b^2 / 3) * sum_{i <= j} bar_G'_ij * (i + j)
- Temperature T = -partial_b F
- Entropy density s = b (from identification of K^mu as entropy current)

## Context

This work extends the non-dissipative hydrodynamics from effective field theory developed by Dubovsky et al. (2005, 2011) and Bhattacharya et al. (2012), which used Goldstone modes and volume-preserving diffeomorphisms to derive ideal and second-order hydrodynamics from an action. The paper introduces dissipation via the CTP formalism, following the influence functional approach of Feynman and Vernon (1963), and enables a variational derivation of dissipative hydrodynamics, though the vanishing shear viscosity suggests the volume-preserving diffeomorphism symmetry may be too restrictive for full viscous fluid equations.
