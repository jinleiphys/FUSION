# International Workshop on Next Generation Gamma-Ray Source

> A workshop on The Next Generation Gamma-Ray Sources sponsored by the Office of Nuclear Physics at the Department of Energy, was held November 17--19, 2016 in Bethesda, Maryland. The goals of the workshop were to identify basic and applied research opportunities at the frontiers of nuclear physics that would be made possible by the beam capabilities of an advanced laser Compton beam facility. To anchor the scientific vision to realistically achievable beam specifications using proven technologies, the workshop brought together experts in the fields of electron accelerators, lasers, and optics to examine the technical options for achieving the beam specifications required by the most compelling parts of the proposed research programs. An international assembly of participants included current and prospective $γ$-ray beam users, accelerator and light-source physicists, and federal agency program managers. Sessions were organized to foster interactions between the beam users and facility developers, allowing for information sharing and mutual feedback between the two groups. The workshop findings and recommendations are summarized in this whitepaper.

## Key claim

A next-generation Compton gamma-ray beam facility can enable significant advances in nuclear security applications, particularly for detecting shielded special nuclear materials (SNM) in cargo, but the specific performance improvements depend on achieving beam specifications that are realistically achievable using proven technologies such as storage rings or superconducting linacs combined with high-finesse optical cavities.

## Method

The workshop assembled experts in electron accelerators, lasers, and optics alongside current and prospective gamma-ray beam users to identify research opportunities and technical options for an advanced laser Compton beam facility. Sessions were organized to foster interactions between beam users and facility developers, allowing information sharing and mutual feedback. The workshop findings and recommendations are summarized in this whitepaper, which reviews the nuclear resonance fluorescence (NRF) notch technique for isotope identification, surveys existing Compton gamma-ray source facilities worldwide, and evaluates technology choices for next-generation sources.

## Key numbers

- HIGS at Duke University has demonstrated a maximum total flux of about 3×10^10 gamma/s around 10 MeV, two to three orders of magnitude more flux than other Compton gamma-ray source facilities ever built and operated
- The HIGS facility covers an energy range from about 1 to 100 MeV, with envisioned extensions first to 130 MeV and then to 150 MeV
- Ground state radiative widths (Gamma_gamma) for useful NRF states below particle separation energies of around 6 to 8 MeV are typically 10 to 100 meV
- The notch technique relies on resonance absorption widths typically less than 1 eV, requiring indirect detection methods
- The ELI-NP VEGA system under construction will deliver gamma-ray beams with energy continuously variable from 1 to 19.5 MeV and on-target flux of approximately 10^8 gamma/s
- The Thomson cross section at zero-recoil limit is 6.652×10^-29 m^2
- For the notch technique, the standard collimator diameter will be around 25 mm (1 inch), with lead collimators below 7 MeV and aluminium collimators at higher energies

## Context

This whitepaper summarizes the findings of a 2016 workshop sponsored by the Office of Nuclear Physics at the Department of Energy, extending the work of earlier Compton gamma-ray facilities such as HIGS (operational since 1996), LEGS, and Graal, as well as the Ladon project at Frascati (1978). The notch technique for cargo inspection was originally proposed by Bertozzi and Ledoux (2005), with subsequent measurements at HIGS by Hagmann et al. (2009) demonstrating that notch refilling due to small-angle scattering is likely negligible for most cargo inspection scenarios. The paper enables future experimental studies comparing the notch technique to scattered-gamma detection for high-Z nuclei, measurements of effective NRF cross sections for key isotopes including 238U, 239Pu, 235U, 208Pb, 12C, 14N, 16O, and 24Mg, and benchmarking of computer models for cargo interrogation.
