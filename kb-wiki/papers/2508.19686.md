# Microscopic nuclear structure study of $^{229}$Th by Projected Shell Model

> $^{229}$Th, a crucial candidate for nuclear clocks and many other applications, is a typical heavy nucleus with an extremely low-energy isomeric state $^{229m}$Th. A detailed study of the nuclear structure of $^{229}$Th is performed here by the microscopic model of state-of-the-art projected shell model. Our calculation describes well low-energy levels of $^{229}$Th, and provides a reduced transition probability $B(M1)$ of $0.0240$ W.u. for the isomeric transition which agrees well with the radiative lifetime of $^{229m}$Th measured recently. Our result supports a small multipole mixing for the cross-band transition of the second-excited state of $^{229}$Th, suggesting that further investigations on the inconsistencies in the decay of the second-excited state should be necessary. The physics behind these properties is revealed by the analysis of the nuclear wave functions. Our findings provide a deep insight into $^{229}$Th from the microscopic nuclear structure point of view, and offer the chance for further studies for nuclear clocks and relevant topics by microscopic nuclear structure theory.

## Key claim

The projected shell model (PSM) calculation reproduces the low-energy level structure of $^{229}$Th and yields a reduced $M1$ transition probability $B(M1) = 0.0240$ W.u. for the isomeric transition, which agrees with recent measurements of the radiative lifetime of $^{229m}$Th, but the authors note that the isomer energy is sensitive to the neutron $N=6$ single-particle levels and the pairing strengths, and that the model cannot precisely describe the near-degeneracy of the $5/2^{-}$ and $7/2^{-}$ levels in the negative-parity bands.

## Method

The authors apply the projected shell model (PSM) to $^{229}$Th, starting from a deformed Nilsson mean field with BCS pairing correlations. They construct a multi-quasiparticle basis including up to 3-quasiparticle configurations (1-quasiparticle and 3-quasiparticle states) for neutrons and protons, with three major shells for each species, and restore rotational and reflection symmetries via angular-momentum and parity projection. The Hamiltonian includes quadrupole-quadrupole, octupole-octupole, hexadecapole-hexadecapole, monopole-pairing, and quadrupole-pairing interactions, and the Hill-Wheeler equation is solved to obtain the laboratory-frame wave functions and electromagnetic transition strengths.

## Key numbers

- $B(M1) = 0.0240$ W.u. for the isomeric transition ($3/2^+$ isomer to $5/2^+$ ground state), consistent with recent measurements (e.g., $0.0213^{+0.0013}_{-0.0012}$ W.u. from Hiraki (2024), $0.0214^{+0.0002}_{-0.0001}$ W.u. from Zhang (2024)).
- $B(E2) = 8.74$ W.u. (standard effective charges) or $10.95$ W.u. (modified effective charges) for the same isomeric transition.
- $B(M1) = 0.00339$ W.u. for the $5/2^+$ (isomer band) to $5/2^+$ (ground band) transition, compared to the experimental value $0.00326 \pm 0.00076$ W.u. from Nikolay (2022).
- $B(E2) = 191.5$ W.u. (standard) or $239.8$ W.u. (modified) for the in-band $9/2^+$ to $7/2^+$ transition in the yrast band, compared to the experimental $170 \pm 30$ W.u.
- Intrinsic deformation parameters: quadrupole $\varepsilon_2 = 0.19$, octupole $\varepsilon_3 = 0.066$, hexadecapole $\varepsilon_4 = -0.089$.
- The $5/2^+$ ground state has $77.4\%$ occupation of the $n 5/2 [633]$ configuration; the $3/2^+$ isomer has $82.8\%$ occupation of the $n 3/2 [631]$ configuration.

## Context

This work extends prior nuclear structure studies of $^{229}$Th that used collective models, such as the rotational model (Barci 2003), the quasiparticle-plus-phonon model (Ruchowska 2006), and the collective quadrupole-octupole vibration-rotation model (Nikolay 2017, 2019, 2022), by applying a fully microscopic projected shell model that accounts for single-particle and collective degrees of freedom. The calculated $B(M1)$ for the isomeric transition resolves a discrepancy where earlier collective-model predictions (Nikolay 2017, 2019) gave smaller values than recent experimental results. The PSM wave functions provide a microscopic basis for further studies of nuclear clocks and related applications, and the approach can be extended to other heavy nuclei with low-lying isomers.
