# Precise determination of the f0(500) and f0(980) parameters in dispersive analysis of the pipi data

> We review the use of new and precise dispersive equations, which also implement crossing symmetry, in order to shed further light on the long-standing puzzle in the parameters of the f0(500), as well as the f0(980). This puzzle is finally being settled thanks to several analyses carried out during the last years. In this talk we show how our very recent dispersive data analysis allowed for a precise and model independent determination of the amplitudes for the S,P,D and F waves. In particular, we show how the analytic continuation of once subtracted dispersion relations for the S0 wave to the complex energy plane leads to very precise results for the f0(500) pole: sqrt(s)_pole = 457^(+14)_(-13) - i 279^(+11)_(-7) MeV and for the f0(980) pole: sqrt(s)_pole = 996+/-7 - i 25^(+10)_(-6) MeV. We also comment on how these results have been already used for other practical applications, including a refit of a previous model to the pipi S-wave amplitudes below 1000 MeV, which improves its consistency with the poles found with the dispersive approach.

## Key claim

The dispersive analysis of pion-pion scattering data, constrained by once-subtracted dispersion relations that implement crossing symmetry (GKPY equations), yields precise and model-independent pole parameters for the f0(500) and f0(980) resonances, with uncertainties that are typically smaller than those obtained from twice-subtracted Roy equations.

## Method

The authors performed a constrained fit to experimental pi-pi scattering data for S, P, D, and F partial waves from threshold to about 1400 MeV, including recent K_{l4} results. They imposed consistency with Roy-type dispersion relations, forward dispersion relations, sum rules, and the once-subtracted GKPY equations by minimizing a total chi-squared that combines data agreement with averaged distances between input and output real parts of the amplitudes. The analytic continuation of the resulting S0-wave amplitudes to the complex energy plane was then performed using the same dispersive integrals to extract resonance pole positions and couplings.

## Key numbers

* f0(500) pole from GKPY equations: sqrt(s)_pole = 457^(+14)_(-13) - i 279^(+11)_(-7) MeV
* f0(980) pole from GKPY equations: sqrt(s)_pole = 996 +/- 7 - i 25^(+10)_(-6) MeV
* f0(500) coupling from GKPY equations: |g| = 3.59^(+0.11)_(-0.13) GeV
* f0(980) coupling from GKPY equations: |g| = 2.3 +/- 0.2 GeV
* rho(770) pole from GKPY equations: sqrt(s)_pole = (763.7^(+1.7)_(-1.5)) - i (73.2^(+1.0)_(-1.1)) MeV
* Averaged differences between input and output for the constrained fit: d^2_Roy = 0.14, d^2_GKPY = 0.32, d^2_FDR = 0.4 (compared to 0.87, 1.9, and 1.98 respectively for the unconstrained fit to data only)

## Context

This work extends the dispersive analysis program of Garcia-Martin, Kaminski, Pelaez, and Ruiz de Elvira (2011) by applying once-subtracted GKPY dispersion relations to achieve higher precision than the twice-subtracted Roy equations used by Caprini, Colangelo, and Leutwyler (2006), whose results agree well despite not using Chiral Perturbation Theory input. The results directly influenced the Particle Data Group's 2012 reevaluation, which changed the lightest scalar-isoscalar meson name from f0(600) to f0(500) and dramatically reduced its uncertainty range from M = 400-1200 MeV to M = 400-550 MeV. The parameterizations have already been used in precision analyses with pions in final states, and a refit of the older model by Surovtsev and collaborators using GKPY constraints moved their sigma pole from 616.5 - i 554.0 MeV to 473.7 - i 297.8 MeV, improving total chi-squared from 2.4 to 1.3.
