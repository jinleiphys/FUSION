# Low energy neutrino scattering : from fundamental interaction studies to astrophysics

> Neutrino scattering at low energies is essential for a variety of timely applications potentially having fundamental implications, e.g. unraveling unknown neutrino properties, such as the third neutrino mixing angle, the detection of the diffuse supernova neutrino background, or of cosmological neutrinos and furnishing a new constraint to double-beta decay calculations. Here we discuss some applications, the present status and the perspectives.

# Low energy neutrino scattering: from fundamental interaction studies to astrophysics

## Key claim

Low-energy neutrino scattering on nucleons and nuclei is essential for multiple applications with fundamental implications, but the practical utility of supernova neutrinos for unraveling neutrino properties or supernova physics depends on significant improvements in both the modeling of core-collapse explosions and neutrino propagation in dense media, as well as in the precision of neutrino-nucleus cross sections.

## Method

The paper reviews the current status and future perspectives of low-energy neutrino scattering, discussing its applications to core-collapse supernova physics, detection of the diffuse supernova neutrino background (DSNB), and cosmological neutrinos. It surveys existing experimental constraints, theoretical approaches to computing neutrino-nucleus cross sections (including non-relativistic and relativistic microscopic models), and proposed future facilities such as low-energy beta-beams and spallation sources.

## Key numbers

- Super-Kamiokande upper limit on DSNB: 1.2 $\bar{\nu}_e$ cm$^{-2}$s$^{-1}$ at 90% C.L.
- LSD upper limit on DSNB: 6.8 $\times$ 10$^3$ $\nu_e$ cm$^{-2}$s$^{-1}$ at 90% C.L.
- A measurement of the Weinberg angle at 10% precision level at a low-energy beta-beam facility could improve the LNSD measurement by about a factor of 2.
- Only three nuclei have been studied experimentally for low-energy neutrino scattering: deuteron, carbon, and iron.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on low-energy neutrino scattering for astrophysical applications, building on earlier proposals such as Haxton and Johnson (1987) for detecting galactic neutrinos via $^{97}$Tc production from $^{98}$Mo ore, and Engel and collaborators (2002) on using lead detectors to distinguish between large and small values of $\sin^2\theta_{13}$. The paper discusses how recent developments in collective neutrino oscillations (including neutrino-neutrino interactions) and shock wave effects in supernovae have created a new paradigm for interpreting supernova neutrino signals, while also noting that significant discrepancies remain among theoretical calculations of neutrino-nucleus cross sections, particularly for forbidden transitions and for nuclei of interest such as $^{12}$C, $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ar, $^{56}$Fe, $^{98}$Mo, $^{97}$Tc, and $^{208}$Pb.
