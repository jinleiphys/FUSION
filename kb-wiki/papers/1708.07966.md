# Computation of the Binding Energies in the Inverse Problem Framework

> We formalized the nuclear mass problem in the inverse problem framework. This approach allows us to infer the underlying model parameters from experimental observation, rather than to predict the observations from the model parameters. The inverse problem was formulated for the numericaly generalized the semi-empirical mass formula of Bethe and von Weizsäcker. It was solved in step by step way based on the AME2012 nuclear database. The solution of the overdetermined system of nonlinear equations has been obtained with the help of the Aleksandrov's auto-regularization method of Gauss-Newton type for ill-posed problems. In the obtained generalized model the corrections to the binding energy depend on nine proton (2, 8, 14, 20, 28, 50, 82, 108, 124) and ten neutron (2, 8, 14, 20, 28, 50, 82, 124, 152, 202) magic numbers as well on the asymptotic boundaries of their influence. These results help us to evaluate the borders of the nuclear landscape and show their limit. The efficiency of the applied approach was checked by comparing relevant results with the results obtained independently.

## Key claim

The authors formalize the nuclear mass problem as an inverse problem, inferring model parameters directly from experimental data rather than predicting observations from a model, and claim that this approach improves agreement with experimental masses and predicts drip-lines more accurately than earlier calculations with the modified Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula alone.

## Method

The inverse problem was formulated by numerically generalizing the semi-empirical Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula and solving an overdetermined system of 2564 nonlinear equations (one per experimental nucleus from the AME2012 database) using Aleksandrov's auto-regularization method of Gauss-Newton type for ill-posed problems. The solution was obtained iteratively: first solving for binding energy for given proton number Z and mass number A, then using that as a seed to find atomic masses, then nuclear masses, and repeating until convergence. The generalized model includes corrections depending on nine proton magic numbers (2, 8, 14, 20, 28, 50, 82, 108, 124) and ten neutron magic numbers (2, 8, 14, 20, 28, 50, 82, 124, 152, 202), with 17 linearly independent variables and 241 free parameters total.

## Context

This work extends the semi-empirical Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula by treating it as an inverse problem, building on prior regularization methods by Tikhonov (1963, 1983) and Aleksandrov (1971, 1973). The results improve upon earlier drip-line predictions made with the modified Bethe-Weizsäcker formula alone, as in PhysRevC.65.037301, ADHIKARI (2004), and BASU (2004), and enable predictions for binding energies, masses, and mass excess of recently discovered superheavy nuclei at the DGFRS separator (Oganessian 2015). The approach also allows evaluation of the boundaries of the nuclear landscape and the location of proton and neutron drip lines.
