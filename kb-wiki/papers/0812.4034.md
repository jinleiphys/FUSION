# Symmetries at and Near Critical Points of Quantum Phase Transitions in Nuclei

> We examine several types of symmetries which are relevant to quantum phase transitions in nuclei. These include: critical-point, quasidynamical, and partial dynamical symmetries.

## Key claim
The paper examines whether symmetries can persist at and near critical points of quantum phase transitions in nuclei, finding that several types of intermediate symmetries (critical-point, quasidynamical, and partial dynamical symmetries) can survive despite the strong mixing expected at these points.

## Method
The authors study quantum phase transitions in the interacting boson model (IBM), which describes quadrupole collective states in nuclei using a system of N monopole (s) and quadrupole (d) bosons. They construct Hamiltonians that interpolate between different dynamical symmetry chains (U(5)-SU(3) and U(5)-O(6)) and analyze the symmetry content of eigenstates as a function of the control parameter alpha, particularly at the critical point alpha_c where the energy surface becomes flat or shows degenerate minima.

## Key numbers
- For the U(5)-SU(3) first-order transition: critical control parameter alpha_c = 16N/(34N-27)
- For the U(5)-O(6) second-order transition: critical control parameter alpha_c = N/(2N-1)
- The first-order critical surface has a barrier height h approximately 10^{-3} and deformed minimum at beta_0 = sqrt(2)/4
- The X(5) benchmark applies to first-order transitions between spherical and axially-deformed nuclei; the E(5) benchmark applies to second-order transitions between spherical and gamma-soft deformed nuclei

## Context
This work extends the critical-point symmetries E(5) and X(5) introduced by Iachello (2000, 2001) in the geometric Bohr Hamiltonian framework, and connects them to the algebraic IBM description of shape-phase transitions studied by Iachello and Zamfir (2004). The concept of quasidynamical symmetry, where selected low-lying states continue to exhibit properties of the closest dynamical symmetry limit despite strong mixing, was previously developed by Rowe (2004, 2005). The paper enables further study of how symmetries can persist in regions of rapid structural change in nuclei, with empirical examples shown for Sm-152 and Nd-150 exhibiting X(5)-like structure.
