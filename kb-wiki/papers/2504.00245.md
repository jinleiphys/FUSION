# The Short-Baseline Near Detector at Fermilab

> SBND is a 112 ton liquid argon time projection chamber (LArTPC) neutrino detector located 110 meters from the Booster Neutrino Beam (BNB) target at Fermilab. Its main goals include searches for eV-scale sterile neutrinos as part of the Short-Baseline Neutrino (SBN) program, other searches for physics beyond the Standard Model, and precision studies of neutrino-argon interactions. In addition, SBND is providing a platform for LArTPC neutrino detector technology development and is an excellent training ground for the international group of scientists and engineers working towards the upcoming flagship Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment (DUNE). SBND began operation in July 2024, and started collecting stable neutrino beam data in December 2024 with an unprecedented rate of ~7,000 neutrino events per day. During its currently approved operation plans (2024-2027), SBND is expected to accumulate nearly 10 million neutrino interactions. The near detector dataset will be instrumental in testing the sterile neutrino hypothesis with unprecedented sensitivity in SBN and in probing signals of beyond the Standard Model physics. It will also be used to significantly advance our understanding of the physics of neutrino-argon interactions ahead of DUNE. After the planned accelerator restart at Fermilab (2029+), opportunities are being explored to operate SBND in antineutrino mode in order to address the scarcity of antineutrino-argon scattering data, or in a dedicated beam-dump mode to significantly enhance sensitivity to searches for new physics. SBND is an international effort, with approximately 40% of institutions from Europe, contributing to detector construction, commissioning, software development, and data analysis. Continued European involvement and leadership are essential during SBND's operations and analysis phase for both the success of SBND, SBN and its role leading up to DUNE.

## Key claim

SBND is a 112-ton liquid argon time projection chamber (LArTPC) located 110 meters from the Booster Neutrino Beam target at Fermilab that began stable data-taking in December 2024; its primary goal is to test the eV-scale sterile neutrino hypothesis as part of the Short-Baseline Neutrino program, but the paper emphasizes that the SBN program can cover the parameter regions allowed by past anomalies at approximately 5 sigma significance only if both the near (SBND) and far (ICARUS) detectors operate together and the multi-channel oscillation search (appearance and disappearance) is performed.

## Method

The detector is a 112-ton liquid argon time projection chamber positioned 110 meters from the BNB target, collecting neutrino interactions from an 8 GeV proton beam on a beryllium target. SBND exploits the "PRISM" feature by dividing the detector face into off-axis angle slices from 0.2 degrees to 1.6 degrees, each exposed to a different neutrino flux with mean energy shifts up to approximately 200 MeV, enabling systematic uncertainty constraints. The collaboration uses the same target and detector technology (LArTPC) as the far detector ICARUS to achieve high correlation between near and far detectors, reducing systematic uncertainties in the sterile neutrino oscillation search.

## Key numbers

- Detector mass: 112 tons of liquid argon
- Distance from beam target: 110 meters
- Neutrino event rate: approximately 7,000 neutrino events per day
- Expected total exposure: 10 x 10^20 protons on target over the approved 2024-2027 operation period
- Expected total neutrino interactions: nearly 10 million neutrino interactions on argon
- Expected annual event rates: approximately 2,000,000 nu_mu CC events per year and approximately 15,000 nu_e CC events per year
- Beam flux composition: approximately 92.5% nu_mu, approximately 6.9% nu_mu-bar, approximately 0.6% nu_e/nu_e-bar
- Total flux uncertainty at the peak of the muon neutrino flux: approximately 8%
- BNB neutrino flux spectrum peaks near 0.8 GeV (second oscillation maximum for DUNE) with substantial sample up to approximately 2.6 GeV (first DUNE oscillation maximum)
- European institutions: approximately 40% of SBND-affiliated universities and research institutions

## Context

This paper describes the status and physics program of SBND, the near detector of the Short-Baseline Neutrino program at Fermilab, which builds on the legacy of MicroBooNE (MicroBooNE, 2015) and addresses short-baseline neutrino anomalies reviewed by Machado (2019). The SBN program aligns with recommendations from the 2014 and 2023 US Particle Physics Project Prioritization Panel reports and the 2020 Update of the European Strategy for Particle Physics. SBND's neutrino-argon cross-section measurements will directly inform the Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment (DUNE), and its BSM search capabilities complement energy-frontier experiments by probing lower-mass new particles with smaller couplings. The paper also notes that new hadron production data from the NA61/SHINE experiment at CERN could further reduce flux uncertainties.
