# A new scheme for heavy nuclei: proxy-SU(3)

> The SU(3) symmetry realized by J. P. Elliott in the sd nuclear shell is destroyed in heavier shells by the strong spin-orbit interaction. However, the SU(3) symmetry has been used for the description of heavy nuclei in terms of bosons in the framework of the Interacting Boson Approximation, as well as in terms of fermions using the pseudo-SU(3) approximation. We introduce a new fermionic approximation, called the proxy-SU(3), and we discuss how some of its novel predictions come out as a consequence of the short range of the nucleon-nucleon interaction and the Pauli principle.

# proxy-SU(3): A new scheme for heavy nuclei

## Key claim
The proxy-SU(3) scheme provides a fermionic approximation that restores SU(3) symmetry in heavy nuclei where the spin-orbit interaction destroys Elliott's original SU(3) symmetry; the scheme's novel predictions for prolate-over-oblate dominance arise specifically from the short-range nature of the nucleon-nucleon interaction combined with the Pauli principle, without requiring a specific Hamiltonian.

## Method
The authors analyze the highest-weight irreducible representations (irreps) of SU(3) for even numbers of particles in the U(6), U(10), U(15), U(21), U(28), and U(36) shells, comparing them to irreps with the highest eigenvalue of the second-order Casimir operator C₂(λ,μ). They identify which irreps are single (appearing only once in the relevant decomposition) and restrict to irreps with even λ and μ, consistent with even-even ground states having zero angular momentum. The particle-hole symmetry breaking is then interpreted through Young diagrams to assess spatial symmetry versus antisymmetry.

## Key numbers
- For 10 particles in the U(15) (sdg) shell, the highest-weight irrep is (20,4); for 10 holes (20 particles), the highest-weight irrep is (20,0), not the particle-hole conjugate (4,20).
- Oblate irreps appear only for specific particle counts: 24 particles in U(15), 36 and 34 particles in U(21), 50 and 48 particles in U(28), and 66, 64, and 62 particles in U(36).
- The exceptions where oblate irreps become winners occur for 6 particles in U(10) through U(36), for 8 particles in U(21) through U(36), and for 10 particles only in U(36).

## Context
This work extends the proxy-SU(3) scheme introduced by Bonatsos et al. (2015, 2016), which was previously justified through Nilsson model calculations and provided parameter-free predictions for deformation parameters β and γ in rare earth nuclei. The scheme contrasts with the pseudo-SU(3) fermionic approximation and the Interacting Boson Approximation (IBA) bosonic approach, both of which also restore SU(3) symmetry in heavy nuclei. The results enable a microscopic explanation for the prolate-over-oblate dominance in deformed nuclei and identify the border of the prolate-to-oblate transition, building on the earlier work of Casten (1990) and Ring and Schuck (1980) on the short-range nature of the nucleon-nucleon interaction.
