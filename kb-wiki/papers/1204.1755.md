# Benchmarks of the full configuration interaction, Monte Carlo shell model, and no-core full configuration methods

> We report no-core solutions for properties of light nuclei with three different approaches in order to assess the accuracy and convergence rates of each method. Full configuration interaction (FCI), Monte Carlo shell model (MCSM) and no core full configuration (NCFC) approaches are solved separately for the ground state energy and other properties of seven light nuclei using the realistic JISP16 nucleon-nucleon interaction. The results are consistent among the different approaches. The methods differ significantly in how the required computational resources scale with increasing particle number for a given accuracy.

## Key claim
The three methods (FCI, MCSM, NCFC) produce consistent results for ground state energies and other properties of light nuclei using the JISP16 nucleon-nucleon interaction, but the MCSM approach scales as A^{3-5} with nucleon number for fixed accuracy, which is significantly slower than the A^{12-14} scaling of NCFC and the A^{18-20} scaling of FCI, making MCSM the superior approach for heavier nuclei.

## Method
The authors performed no-core calculations for seven light nuclei (^4He, ^6He, ^6Li, ^7Li, ^8Be, ^10B, ^12C) using three distinct many-body methods: Full Configuration Interaction (FCI) with an Nshell cutoff in a harmonic oscillator single-particle basis, Monte Carlo Shell Model (MCSM) with stochastic sampling of angular-momentum and parity projected deformed Slater determinants, and No-Core Full Configuration (NCFC) with an Nmax truncation and extrapolation to the infinite basis limit. All calculations used the JISP16 NN interaction without renormalization and without three-nucleon forces, and results were compared at fixed finite cutoffs with the optimal oscillator energy that minimizes the ground state energy.

## Context
This work extends the no-core Monte Carlo Shell Model approach introduced by Otsuka (1998) to treat systems without a core, and compares it with the established Full Configuration Interaction method and the No-Core Full Configuration method of Maris (2009). The benchmark validates the MCSM as an accurate approximation to FCI in smaller basis spaces while demonstrating its superior scaling properties for heavier nuclei. The results enable future applications of the no-core MCSM to medium-mass nuclei where FCI and NCFC become computationally prohibitive, and complement other ongoing efforts such as the Importance-Truncated NCSM of Roth and the symmetry-adapted NCSM of Dytrych.
