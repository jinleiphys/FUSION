# Analytical determination of the structure and nuclear abundances of the outer crust of a cold nonaccreted neutron star

> A very fast iterative method is presented to calculate the internal constitution of the outer crust of a cold nonaccreted neutron star, making use of very accurate analytical formulas for the transition pressures between adjacent crustal layers and their density. In addition to the composition of the different crustal layers, their depth and their baryonic mass content can be simultaneously estimated using an approximate solution of Einstein's general relativistic equations. The overall computing time is drastically reduced compared to the traditional approach, thus opening the door to large-scale statistical studies and sensitivity analyses.

# Analytical determination of the structure and nuclear abundances of the outer crust of a cold nonaccreted neutron star

## Key claim

The paper presents a very fast iterative method that uses analytical formulas for transition pressures and densities to determine the composition, depth, and baryonic mass content of the outer crust of a cold nonaccreted neutron star, achieving a speedup of approximately 4 x 10^4 to 3.6 x 10^6 times compared to the traditional numerical minimization approach, with relative errors in transition pressures and densities of at most 0.25% and typically much smaller.

## Method

The method determines the sequence of equilibrium nuclides iteratively starting from 56Fe at the stellar surface, finding the nuclide that yields the lowest transition pressure to the next layer using an analytical solution of a quadratic polynomial equation derived from the Gibbs free energy equilibrium condition expanded to first order in the fine structure constant. The transition pressures, densities, and baryon chemical potentials are calculated using closed-form expressions that account for electron exchange and charge polarization corrections, while the nuclear abundances and layer depths are obtained from approximate analytical solutions of the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations under the thin-crust approximation. The method was applied using experimental nuclear masses from the 2016 Atomic Mass Evaluation supplemented with the HFB-27 nuclear mass table.

## Key numbers

- Computing time of 0.06 seconds using an Intel Core i7-975 processor, compared to 37 minutes (approximately 4 x 10^4 times longer) for the traditional approach using 18000 pressure values with step delta P = 10^{-3} P
- Relative errors on transition pressures and densities reach at most 0.25%, but are typically of order 10^{-3}% or less
- Relative errors on baryon chemical potentials do not exceed 6.4 x 10^{-5}%
- Relative errors on depths are at most 5.8 x 10^{-2}%
- Relative errors on abundances exhibit larger deviations up to 2%
- The most abundant element is 120Sr representing about 32% of the crustal mass
- Transition from 56Fe to 62Ni occurs at gamma_e^{1->2} approximately 1.9 MeV
- Neutron-drip baryon chemical potential is m_n c^2 approximately 939.565 MeV
- Surface baryon chemical potential from 56Fe is approximately 930.412 MeV

## Context

This work extends the analytical approach developed by Chamel (2016) and Chamel (2016b) for calculating transition pressures between crustal layers, and provides a computationally efficient alternative to the traditional numerical minimization method used since the pioneering studies of Tondeur (1971) and Baym, Pethick, and Sutherland (1971). The method enables large-scale statistical studies and sensitivity analyses across different nuclear mass tables, as recently undertaken by Pastore (2019), and is relevant for understanding the composition of neutron-star crust material ejected in binary neutron-star mergers like GW170817, which influences r-process nucleosynthesis yields as studied by Goriely (2011).
