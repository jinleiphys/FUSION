# TASI Lectures on Astrophysical Aspects of Neutrinos

> Neutrino astronomy is on the verge of discovering new sources, and this will lead to important advances in astrophysics, cosmology, particle physics, and nuclear physics. This paper is meant for non-experts, so that they might better understand the basic issues in this field.

## Key claim

Neutrino astronomy is on the verge of discovering new sources, but to date only two extraterrestrial sources have been observed (the Sun and Supernova 1987A), and the paper focuses on introducing non-experts to the basic framework of supernova neutrinos and high-energy neutrinos rather than presenting new experimental results.

## Method

The paper reviews the physics of core-collapse supernova neutrino emission, including the gravitational binding energy release of approximately 3 × 10^53 erg, the diffusion of neutrinos through the proto-neutron star, and the detection mechanisms in water Cherenkov detectors like Super-Kamiokande. It then discusses the Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background (DSNB) and the proposed GADZOOKS! technique of adding 0.2% dissolved gadolinium trichloride to Super-Kamiokande to tag neutrons from inverse beta decay. For high-energy neutrinos, the paper describes the hadronic production mechanism via pion decay chains and the detection of muon neutrinos through charged-current deep inelastic scattering in detectors like AMANDA and IceCube.

## Key numbers

- Gravitational binding energy release from core collapse: approximately 3 × 10^53 erg (or 2 × 10^59 MeV), based on a neutron star mass of m = 1.4 solar masses and radius of about 10 km
- Supernova explosion energy: about 10^51 erg (1 "f.o.e." or 1 "Bethe")
- Expected detected events for a Milky Way supernova in Super-Kamiokande: about 10^4 events at a typical distance of D ≃ 10 kpc
- DSNB flux limit from Super-Kamiokande (2003): 1.2 cm^−2 s^−1 for electron antineutrinos above 19.3 MeV
- Largest plausible DSNB flux calculated by Kaplinghat, Steigman, and Walker (2000): 2.2 cm^−2 s^−1 for electron antineutrinos above 19.3 MeV
- Neutrino burst duration from SN 1987A: about 10 seconds, with typical energies in the low tens of MeV
- Initial neutrino flavor ratios for hadronic sources: φ_e : φ_μ : φ_τ = 1 : 2 : 0, becoming 1 : 1 : 1 after vacuum mixing
- Cosmic rays observed at energies as high as 10^20 eV

## Context

This paper extends the pedagogical tradition of TASI lectures by providing an accessible overview of neutrino astrophysics for non-experts, covering both supernova neutrinos (building on the SN 1987A observations by Kamiokande, IMB, and Baksan) and high-energy neutrinos (connecting to AMANDA and IceCube). It enables future work by explaining the GADZOOKS! technique for DSNB detection (Beacom and Vagins, 2004) and the synthesis of star formation rate data (Hopkins and Beacom) to constrain neutrino emission per supernova, while also highlighting open questions such as whether observed high-energy gamma-ray sources are powered by hadronic or leptonic mechanisms.
