# Shell structure from nuclear observables

> The appearance and disappearance of shells and subshells are determined using a previously introduced method of structural analysis. This work extends the approach and applies it to protons, in addition to neutrons, in an attempt to provide a more complete understanding of shell structure in nuclei. Experimental observables including the mean-square charge radius, as well as other spectroscopic and mass related quantities are analyzed for extrema. This analysis also uses differential observables among adjacent even-even nuclei to serve as the derivatives for these quantities of interest. Local extrema in these quantities indicate shell structure and the lack of local extrema indicate missing shell closures. The shell structure of low-mass nuclei is inconsistent likely as a consequence of the single-particle structure. Additionally, multiple shell features occurring in midshell regions are determined by combining information from two or more observables. Our results near stability complement previous observations further out.

## Key claim

The authors extend a previously introduced method of structural analysis using derivatives of experimental observables to identify both proton and neutron shell closures across the chart of nuclides, but note that local extrema in these quantities indicate shell structure while the lack of local extrema indicates missing shell closures, with the caveat that some unrealistic shell features may appear when only a single observable supports the claim.

## Method

The authors analyzed experimental data for even-even nuclei along isotopic and isotonic chains, searching for extrema in observables (E(2₁⁺), E(4₁⁺), S₂ₙ, S₂ₚ, binding energy minus liquid drop, B(E2), and mean-square charge radius) and in their first differences (δₙO and δₚO) defined as differences between adjacent nuclei. For each observable, a primary shell feature was identified from extrema in the observable itself, while secondary features came from the derivatives before and after the point of interest; the procedure required data for five consecutive nuclei so that extrema in both the observable and its derivatives could be determined.

## Key numbers

- The liquid drop binding energy fit used coefficients a_v = 15.79 MeV, a_s = -18.12 MeV, κ = -7.18, a_c = -0.7147 MeV, and Δ = +5.49 MeV (for even-even nuclei), with a root mean squared standard deviation of σ = 2.65 MeV for 2353 nuclides with N, Z > 8 in the 2012 Atomic Mass Evaluation
- The N=28 shell closure in ⁴²Si has a particularly low first excited state at 770 keV
- The average 2₁⁺ energy of five zirconium isotopes (⁹⁰,⁹²,⁹⁴,⁹⁶,⁹⁸Zr) at the Z=40 sub-shell closure is more than three and a half times larger than the average energy of all other zirconium isotopes
- The N=82 shell closure feature in E(6₁⁺) has in most cases moved to N=78 or N=80
- Only 11 shell closure features were identified using B(E2) values, with three shell closure features determined to be missing
- Four nuclides contain neutron shell closure features using the minimum of ⟨r²⟩: ²⁴Ne and ²⁶Mg at N=14, and ⁸⁶Kr and ⁸⁸Sr at N=50

## Context

This work extends the derivatives technique introduced by Cakirli, Casten, and Blaum (2010) to a larger scale, applying it to both protons and neutrons across the entire chart of nuclides using experimental observables near stability. The results complement previous observations further from stability, such as new neutron shell closures at N=14 and N=16 in oxygen isotopes (observed by others) and the proposed closure at N=34 in ⁵⁴Ca. The analysis enables improved predictive power for shell structure far from stability by establishing which shell features near stability are robust, and identifies two critical criteria for sub-shell creation: a shell closure in the companion particle and a change in spin and parity.
