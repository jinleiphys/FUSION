# Density Matrix Renormalization Group study of $^{48}$Cr and $^{56}$Ni

> We discuss the development of an angular-momentum-conserving variant of the Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG) method for use in large-scale shell-model calculations of atomic nuclei and report a first application of the method to the ground state of $^{56}$Ni and improved results for $^{48}$Cr. In both cases, we see a high level of agreement with the exact results. A comparison of the two shows a dramatic reduction in the fraction of the space required to achieve accuracy as the size of the problem grows.

## Key claim

The angular-momentum-conserving variant of the Density Matrix Renormalization Group (JDMRG) achieves high agreement with exact shell-model results for the ground states of ⁴⁸Cr and ⁵⁶Ni, but the fraction of the full Hilbert space required to reach that accuracy drops dramatically as the problem size increases, for ⁵⁶Ni only about 1% of the space is needed to come within roughly 60 keV of the exact energy.

## Method

The authors implement a three-block JDMRG algorithm that works in a coupled (J-scheme) basis to preserve angular momentum, with neutron and proton orbitals placed at opposite ends of a lattice chain. A warmup phase initializes the truncated block states by iteratively adding orbits from both ends, followed by a sweep phase that updates each block by sweeping through the orbits in both directions until convergence. The KB3 interaction with single-particle energies from Poves (2001) is used, assuming a doubly magic ⁴⁰Ca core and distributing valence nucleons over the 2p-1f shell.

## Key numbers

- ⁴⁸Cr ground-state energy: JDMRG with m=200 gives -32.947 MeV, compared to the exact value of -32.953 MeV; the full 0⁺ space has 41,355 states, and JDMRG uses about 25% of that space (max dimension 12,076) to reach within a few keV.
- ⁵⁶Ni ground-state energy: JDMRG with m=200 gives -78.399 MeV, compared to the exact value of -78.46 MeV; the full J-scheme space has 15,443,684 states, and JDMRG uses about 1% of that space (max dimension 192,878) to come within about 60 keV.
- ⁴⁸Cr low-lying excited states at m=200: E(2⁺₁) = -32.13 MeV (exact -32.15 MeV), E(4⁺₁) = -31.10 MeV (exact -31.13 MeV), E(6⁺₁) = -29.47 MeV (exact -29.55 MeV), E(0⁺₂) = -28.48 MeV (exact -28.56 MeV), with dimensions ranging from 54,891 to 85,168 compared to exact dimensions of 182,421 to 246,979.

## Context

This work extends the JDMRG method previously applied to the nuclear shell model by Pittel and Sandulescu (2006) and to the Gamow Shell Model by Nazarewicz (2004), and it improves upon earlier m-scheme DMRG calculations for ⁵⁶Ni by Papenbrock and Dean (2003), which could not achieve better than 400 keV accuracy. The results demonstrate that the JDMRG becomes more efficient relative to the full space as the nuclear system grows, suggesting the method is promising for heavier nuclei; the authors plan to test the GXPF1A interaction for ⁵⁶Ni and to explore splitting large orbits (e.g., 1g₉/₂) into smaller lattice sites to handle even heavier systems.
