# Conserved Charge Fluctuations and Susceptibilities in Strongly Interacting Matter

> We study the conserved charge fluctuations, as quantified by the corresponding susceptibilities, in strongly interacting matter as motived by the quark-gluon plasma. Using the gauge-gravity correspondence approach, we study the patterns of conserved charge fluctuations in two types of holographic models for QCD, the D4/D8 and the D3/D7 models. We compute and compare the quark number susceptibilities in both models and find an interesting common feature of the two: at very strong coupling higher order susceptibilities are suppressed and the conserved charge fluctuations become purely Guassian. In light of the state-of-the-art lattice QCD results we also discuss what we can learn from these susceptibilities about the underlying degrees of freedom in the $1\sim 2T_c$ quark-gluon plasma and examine the viability of different ideas such as holography, quasi-particles, as well as bound states. From analyzes of second order susceptibilities we conclude that the bound states exist and are important in the $1\sim 2T_c$ region. We further construct and make predictions for several ratios of fourth-order susceptibilities that can sensitively reveal such bound states.

## Key claim

The authors use gauge-gravity duality to compute conserved charge susceptibilities in two holographic QCD models and find that, at very strong coupling, higher-order susceptibilities are suppressed and fluctuations become purely Gaussian; they further argue from second-order susceptibilities that bound states exist and are important in the 1–2 T_c region of the quark-gluon plasma, but this conclusion is conditional on the specific holographic models and the interpretation of lattice QCD data.

## Method

The authors compute quark number susceptibilities (up to sixth order) in two holographic models: the D4/D8 (Sakai-Sugimoto) model and the D3/D7 model. In the D4/D8 model, susceptibilities are derived analytically from the pressure as a function of temperature and density, using chain-rule differentiation of the hypergeometric functions that relate chemical potential to density. In the D3/D7 model, the susceptibilities are obtained by numerically solving the D7-brane embedding equation of motion and then differentiating the chemical potential–density relation via integrals of the embedding profile. Both models are studied in their finite-density (QGP and cold dense) phases, and results are compared with lattice QCD data from the Wuppertal-Budapest group (Borsanyi, 2011) and the BNL-Bielefeld group (Bazavov, 2012).

## Key numbers

- In the D4/D8 model at zero chemical potential, the second-order susceptibility per flavor is χ₂^q = ξ₂ λ^(3-2) (T/T_c)^(3-2) with ξ₂ ≈ 0.071 for N_c = N_f = 3.
- In the D4/D8 model at zero chemical potential, the fourth-order susceptibility per flavor has ξ₄ ≈ 2.2 and the sixth-order has ξ₆ ≈ -158 for N_c = N_f = 3.
- In the D4/D8 model, the ratio X₄^q / X₂^q at the phase boundary point (T_c, μ_c) is 0.133 in the QGP phase and 0.123 in the cold dense phase.
- In the D3/D7 model at very high temperature and vanishing density, the second-order susceptibility per flavor approaches χ₂^q → 3/2, the fourth-order approaches χ₄^q → 9/λ, and the sixth-order approaches χ₆^q → -540/(7λ²).
- In the D3/D7 model, the critical temperature is T_c = 0.764 × (2M_q/√λ) and the critical chemical potential is μ_c = M_q.

## Context

This work extends prior holographic studies of susceptibilities by Kim (2009) and Kim (2010) in the D4/D8 and D3/D7 models, respectively, and provides a systematic comparison of fluctuation patterns across both models. The results are discussed in light of lattice QCD data from the Wuppertal-Budapest group (Borsanyi, 2011) and the BNL-Bielefeld group (Bazavov, 2012), which show distinctive patterns in the 1–2 T_c region that cannot be explained by free hadronic resonance gas or Stefan-Boltzmann gas benchmarks. The paper enables future work on using ratios of fourth-order susceptibilities as sensitive probes of bound states in the quark-gluon plasma, and it provides holographic predictions that can be tested against experimental measurements from the RHIC Beam Energy Scan.
