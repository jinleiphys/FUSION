# Detection of very small neutrino masses in double-beta decay using laser tagging

> We describe an approach to the study of neutrino masses that combines quantum optics techniques with radiation detectors to obtain unprecedented sensitivity. With it the search for Majorana neutrino masses down to $\sim$10 meV will become accessible. The experimental technique uses the possibility of individually detecting $\rm Ba^+$-ions in the final state of $\rm ^{136}Xe$ double-beta decay via resonant excitation with a set of lasers aimed at a specific location in a large Time Projection Chamber. The specificity of the atomic levels provides tagging and, together with more traditional event recognition parameters, greatly suppresses radioactive backgrounds.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that combining laser-induced fluorescence tagging of individual Ba⁺ ions with a large xenon gas Time Projection Chamber can achieve a Majorana neutrino mass sensitivity down to approximately 10 meV, but this sensitivity depends on the assumption that the Ba⁺ tagging system reduces all radioactive backgrounds by at least three orders of magnitude relative to the Gotthard experiment, making a 10-ton detector essentially background-free.

## Method

The authors describe a hybrid detector consisting of a large (40 m³) gas-phase Time Projection Chamber (TPC) filled with isotopically enriched ¹³⁶Xe at 5–10 atm, containing 1–10 tons of xenon. After a double-beta decay event, the resulting Ba⁺ ion is neutralized by a quencher gas and then individually detected by steering two lasers (493 nm and 650 nm) to the event location, resonantly exciting the ion and observing its fluorescence at 493 nm. The TPC provides event topology, energy measurement, and spatial localization, while the laser tagging provides an independent background rejection variable.

## Key numbers

- Projected neutrino mass sensitivity for a 1-ton ¹³⁶Xe detector after 5 years: ⟨m_ν⟩ = 0.051 eV (QRPA) or 0.14 eV (Shell Model), assuming σ_E/E = 2.8%
- Projected neutrino mass sensitivity for a 10-ton ¹³⁶Xe detector after 10 years: ⟨m_ν⟩ = 0.013 eV (QRPA) or 0.037 eV (Shell Model), assuming σ_E/E = 2.0%
- At 5 atm pressure, the Ba⁺ ion diffuses only ≈ 0.7 mm in 1 second, and can emit more than 10⁷ photons at 493 nm during that time
- The pressure-broadened linewidth at 5 atm is ≈ 20 GHz, with a saturation intensity of ≈ 5 W/cm²
- The 493 nm transition has a spontaneous lifetime of 8 ns and when saturated radiates 6 × 10⁷ photons/s
- The TPC volume is 40 m³, with a drift distance of 250 cm and an electric field of ≈ 1 kV/cm
- Expected position resolution (σ) better than 5 mm in both transverse and longitudinal coordinates
- Energy resolution no worse than σ_E/E = 2.5% at 1.592 MeV (extrapolated from Gotthard TPC)
- For a 1-ton detector with σ_E/E = 2.8%, the 2νββ background in the asymmetric interval [Q_ββ, Q_ββ+2σ_E] is 0.36 events yr⁻¹ ton⁻¹
- The projected half-life limit for 1 ton after 5 years: T_{1/2}^{0νββ} = 8.3 × 10²⁶ yr
- The projected half-life limit for 10 tons after 10 years: T_{1/2}^{0νββ} = 1.3 × 10²⁸ yr

## Context

This work extends the concept of Ba⁺ tagging first proposed by Moe (1991) and builds on the Gotthard ¹³⁶Xe TPC experiment (which achieved ⟨m_ν⟩ < 2.2 eV with 4.87 kg·yr exposure) and the Heidelberg-Moscow ⁷⁶Ge experiment (which achieved ⟨m_ν⟩ < 0.2 eV with 24.16 kg·yr exposure). The proposed technique is compared with other third-generation projects: NEMO3 (expected sensitivity 0.1 eV using 7–20 kg of source foils), CUORE (expected sensitivity ≈ 0.1 eV using 600 kg TeO₂ crystals), and GENIUS (goal of ≈ 0.01 eV using up to 1 ton of ⁷⁶Ge in liquid nitrogen). The authors claim this is the only third-generation project that plans to use a novel background suppression technique (Ba tagging) rather than purely calorimetric approaches.
