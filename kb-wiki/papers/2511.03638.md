# Probing the structure of $f_{0}(980)$ from the elliptic flow in p-Pb collisions at the LHC

> The $f_{0}(980)$ is a light scalar meson whose internal structure remains under debate and investigation. Assuming that the $f_0(980)$ is a $K\bar K$ molecule that can only survive at the kinetic freeze-out of the evolving bulk matter, we implement the coalescence model to study its transverse momentum ($p_T$) spectra and elliptic flow ($v_2$) in high-multiplicity p-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=5.02$ TeV. Using the well-tuned kaon phase-space distributions from the Hydro-Coal-Frag model, our $K\bar{K}$ coalescence calculations with reasonable values for the $f_0(980)$ radius successfully reproduce the elliptic flow measured by CMS over the range $0 < p_{T} < 12$ GeV and also agree with the $p_T$-spectra from ALICE. These results in heavy ion collisions are consistent with the $K\bar K$ molecular picture of the $f_0(980)$. We also find that the number-of-constituent scaling of $v_2$ for the $f_0(980)$ is violated in p-Pb collisions at the LHC because most $f_0(980)$ are produced from the coalescence of kaons that have different momenta. Our study demonstrates the necessity of realistic coalescence model calculations and also explains why the CMS interpretation of the $f_0(980)$ as an ordinary $q\bar q$ meson is no longer valid by interpreting the measured $v_2$ with a simple scaling formula based on the assumption of equal momentum coalescence. The investigation also provides a novel way to explore the internal structure of light exotic hadrons that can be abundantly produced in relativistic heavy and/or light ion collisions.

## Key claim

Assuming the $f_0(980)$ is a $K\bar K$ molecule that survives only at kinetic freeze-out, the authors' coalescence model calculations reproduce the CMS elliptic flow data and are consistent with ALICE spectra, contradicting the CMS conclusion that the $f_0(980)$ is an ordinary $q\bar q$ meson. The CMS interpretation is shown to be invalid because it relied on a simple scaling formula based on equal-momentum coalescence, which is violated for realistic $f_0(980)$ radii.

## Method

The authors implemented a coalescence model where the $f_0(980)$ is formed via $K + \bar{K} \to f_0(980)$ at kinetic freeze-out, using a Gaussian Wigner function for the $f_0(980)$ internal wave function with width related to its root-mean-square (RMS) radius. The kaon phase-space distributions at freeze-out were obtained from the well-tuned Hydro-Coal-Frag hybrid model, which combines hydrodynamics, quark coalescence, and string fragmentation with subsequent hadronic evolution via UrQMD. The coalescence probability was calculated from the overlap of the $f_0(980)$ Wigner function with the kaon and antikaon phase-space distributions on the switching hypersurface.

## Key numbers

- The $f_0(980)$ RMS radius was estimated to be in the range 1.0--1.5 fm, based on a binding energy $E_B \approx 10\text{--}20$ MeV and the relation $R \sim 1/\gamma$ for a near-threshold molecular state.
- The coalescence model calculations reproduced the CMS elliptic flow $v_2(p_T)$ over the transverse momentum range $0 < p_T < 12$ GeV in 0--20% p-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5.02$ TeV.
- The contribution from $\pi^+ \pi^-$ coalescence to $f_0(980)$ production was only about $1.0 \times 10^{-6}$ of the measured yield for an RMS radius of 1 fm, and $2.0 \times 10^{-13}$ for 1.5 fm.
- For a large RMS radius around 10 fm, the NC scaling of $v_2$ is approximately restored, but such a radius is deemed unrealistic given the $f_0(980)$ decay width.

## Context

This work extends coalescence model calculations, previously applied to light nuclei like the deuteron (Zhao 2018, Zhao 2020), to the exotic hadron $f_0(980)$ in p-Pb collisions. It directly contradicts the CMS Collaboration (2023) interpretation that the $f_0(980)$ is an ordinary $q\bar q$ meson based on simple number-of-constituent-quark scaling of $v_2$, and instead supports the $K\bar K$ molecular picture favored by hadron physics studies (Baru 2003). The approach provides a novel method to probe the internal structure of light exotic hadrons produced abundantly in relativistic heavy-ion and light-ion collisions.
