# Kinetic theory for classical and quantum many-body chaos

> For perturbative scalar field theories, the late-time-limit of the out-of-time-ordered correlation function that measures (quantum) chaos is shown to be equal to a Boltzmann-type kinetic equation that measures the total gross (instead of net) particle exchange between phase space cells, weighted by a function of energy. This derivation gives a concrete form to numerous attempts to derive chaotic many-body dynamics from ad hoc kinetic equations. A period of exponential growth in the total gross exchange determines the Lyapunov exponent of the chaotic system. Physically, the exponential growth is a front propagating into an unstable state in phase space. As in conventional Boltzmann transport, which follows from the dynamics of the net particle number density exchange, the kernel of this kinetic integral equation is also set by the 2-to-2 scattering rate. This provides a mathematically precise statement of the known fact that in dilute weakly coupled gases transport and scrambling (or ergodicity) are controlled by the same physics.

## Key claim

For perturbative scalar field theories, the late-time limit of the out-of-time-ordered correlator (OTOC) that measures quantum chaos is shown to be equal to a Boltzmann-type kinetic equation that tracks the total gross particle exchange between phase space cells weighted by an odd function of energy, rather than the net particle number density; this exponential growth in the gross exchange determines the Lyapunov exponent of the chaotic system, but only before the Ehrenfest time cutoff.

## Method

The authors derive the OTOC from a Bethe-Salpeter equation (BSE) for the four-point Schwinger-Keldysh correlator in the limit of small frequency and momentum, using a pinching pole approximation. They show that the resulting integral equation for the OTOC maps onto a kinetic equation whose kernel is the 2-to-2 scattering rate, identical in structure to the linearized Boltzmann equation for transport but with the sign of the outflow term flipped and weighted by an odd energy function. The spectrum of this kinetic operator is computed numerically by diagonalizing the discretized integral kernel on a non-uniform momentum grid.

## Key numbers

- Leading Lyapunov exponent in the high-temperature limit (β → 0) for the N×N Hermitian scalar Φ⁴ theory: λ_L ≈ (0.025 T²)/(48 m) × (1/2) |T₁₂→₃₄|² ≈ (0.025/4) × g⁴(N²+5)T²/(144 m)
- The 2-to-2 transition probability squared for the Φ⁴ theory, traced over external states: |T₁₂→₃₄|² = (1/6) g⁴ (N² + 5)
- The scrambling (Ehrenfest) time: t_scr = 1/(g⁴ N² L) × ln(1/(g⁴ L c₀)), where L is a constant from the kinetic kernel and c₀ is an integration constant
- Numerical diagonalization used N=1000 grid points on the domain p ∈ [m/N, N×m] for the spectrum plots

## Context

This work provides a mathematically precise connection between chaos and transport in dilute weakly coupled theories, extending prior attempts to derive chaotic dynamics from ad hoc kinetic equations (e.g., van Zon et al. (1999) and Dorfman et al. (1998)). It builds on the OTOC formalism developed for strongly coupled systems by Shenker and Stanford (2013), Maldacena et al. (2015), and Polchinski et al. (2015), and on weakly coupled OTOC studies by Stanford (2015) and Aleiner et al. (2016). The result enables computation of Lyapunov exponents directly from scattering amplitudes, and suggests that information about scrambling may be extractable from standard Schwinger-Keldysh correlators rather than requiring extended contours, consistent with the "pole-skipping" phenomenon discovered in holographic theories by Grozdanov et al. (2017).
