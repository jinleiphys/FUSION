# Initial state fluctuations and final state correlations: Status and open questions

> The recent appreciation of the importance of event-by-event fluctuations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions has lead to a large amount of diverse theoretical and experimental activity. In particular, there is significant interest in understanding the fluctuations in the initial stage of a collision, how exactly these fluctuations are propagated through the system evolution, and how they are manifested in correlations between measured particles. In order to address these questions a workshop was organized on "Initial State Fluctuations and Final State Correlations", held at ECT* in Trento, Italy during the week of 2--6 July, 2012. The goal was to collect recent work in order to provide a coherent picture of the current status of our understanding, to identify important questions that remain open, and to set a course for future research. Here we report the outcome of the presentations and discussions, focusing on the most important conclusions.

# Initial state fluctuations and final state correlations: Status and open questions

## Key claim
Event-by-event fluctuations in the initial geometry of relativistic heavy-ion collisions are crucial for understanding all long-range pair correlation data, but there is no single well-defined choice between competing models (e.g., Glauber versus CGC); instead a large space of possible physical pictures, parameters, and implementations exists, and the field has not yet reached consensus on the best model or the precise scale of fluctuations.

## Method
The paper summarizes the outcomes of a workshop held at ECT* in Trento, Italy (2-6 July 2012) that collected recent theoretical and experimental work on initial-state fluctuations and final-state correlations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. Presentations and discussions covered initial condition modeling (Monte Carlo Glauber, MC-KLN, dynamical transport approaches, SU(3) Yang-Mills field evolution), hydrodynamic response characterization, comparison of theory to experimental data, and proposals for future measurements.

## Context
This workshop summary extends the understanding that elliptic flow (the second Fourier component of azimuthal particle distributions) provided strong evidence for a strongly-coupled, low-viscosity QGP at RHIC, and that odd harmonics such as triangular flow were previously considered negligible. It builds on Monte Carlo Glauber and MC-KLN models (Roland, Dumitru, Broniowski, Alvioli) and dynamical transport approaches (Ollitrault, Werner, Petersen, Pang). The paper identifies open questions including: constraining fluctuation scales (flux-tube radius, correlation length), understanding longitudinal fluctuations (Gavin, Poskanzer), determining whether all mixed harmonic event-plane correlations can be reproduced without brute-force event-by-event calculations, and standardizing definitions of initial anisotropies epsilon_n and Glauber Monte Carlo model variations.
