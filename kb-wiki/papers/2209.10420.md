# Unified Balance Functions

> The use of charge balance functions in heavy-ion collision studies was initially proposed as a probe of delayed hadronization and two-stage quark production in these collisions. It later emerged that general balance functions can also serve as a probe of the diffusivity of light quarks as well as the evolution of the systems formed in heavy-ion collisions. In this work, we reexamine the formulation of general balance functions and consider how to best define and measure these correlation functions in terms of differences of conditional densities of unlike-sign and like-sign particle pairs. We define general balance functions in terms of associated particle functions and show these obey a simple sum rule. We additionally proceed to distinguish between balance functions expressed as differences of conditional densities valid irrespective of experimental acceptance boundaries and bound balance functions that explicitly account for the limited acceptance of experiments. General balance functions are additionally extended to accommodate strange, baryon, as well as charm and bottom quantum numbers based on the densities of these quantum numbers.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that general balance functions should be defined as differences of correlation functions (cumulants) rather than raw conditional densities, and that these definitions must include a subtraction term to account for non-vanishing net charge Q, ensuring the integral converges to unity in full acceptance even for systems like pp or Pb–Pb with substantial initial net charge.

## Method

The authors rederive balance functions starting from integral quantities (factorial moments and cumulants) and then extend to differential forms based on associated particle functions A₂^{α|β} defined as C₂^{αβ}/ρ₁^{β}, where C₂^{αβ} = ρ₂^{αβ} − ρ₁^{α}ρ₁^{β}. They distinguish between “unbound” balance functions valid irrespective of acceptance boundaries and “bound” balance functions that average over the trigger particle’s position within a finite acceptance Ω. The formalism is generalized to multiply charged species by replacing particle densities with electric charge densities summed over charge states.

## Key numbers

- For pp collisions with net charge Q=2, the integral balance function I^{+-}(4π) equals 1+Q = 3 and I^{-+}(4π) equals 1−Q = −1 in the naive definition without net-charge subtraction.
- For Pb–Pb collisions, the naive integral would yield 1+(82+82) = 165 or 1−(82+82) = −163 depending on trigger species, motivating the net-charge subtraction.
- In the ideal 4π limit with vanishing net charge, the integral balance function converges to unity: I^{+-}(4π) → 1 and I^{-+}(4π) → 1.
- For a system with N_s pair sources and net charge Q, the event-wise yields are N₁⁺ = N_s + Q, N₁⁻ = N_s, N₂⁺⁻ = (N_s+Q)N_s, N₂⁻⁺ = N_s(N_s+Q), N₂⁺⁺ = (N_s+Q)(N_s+Q−1), N₂⁻⁻ = N_s(N_s−1).

## Context

This work extends the original charge balance function formalism introduced by Bass (2000) and Jeon (2001) for probing delayed hadronization and two-stage quark production, and later developed by Pratt (2019, 2021) for light quark diffusivity measurements. The paper provides a unified mathematical framework that for the first time explicitly extends balance functions to strange, baryon, charm, and bottom quantum numbers, and clarifies the distinction between acceptance-independent and bound (acceptance-averaged) definitions. This enables consistent comparisons between experimental measurements at RHIC and LHC and theoretical calculations of QGP transport properties.
