# Many-body approximations to the superfluid gap and critical temperature in pure neutron matter

> We compute singlet pairing gaps and critical temperatures in pure neutron matter with different many-body approximations. Medium effects tend to reduce gaps and critical temperatures compared to the standard BCS ansatz. In the mean-field approximation, the ratio of these two quantities remains constant across a wide range of densities. This constant ratio is close to the universal prediction of BCS theory, whether three-neutron interactions are included or not. Using a more sophisticated many-body approach that incorporates the effect of short-range correlations in pairing properties, we find that the gap to critical temperature ratio in the low-density regime is substantially larger than the BCS prediction, independently of the interaction. In this region, our results are relatively close to experiments and theoretical calculations from the unitary Fermi gas. We also find evidence for a different density dependence of zero-temperature gaps and critical temperatures in neutron matter.

## Key claim

The ratio of the zero-temperature superfluid gap to the critical temperature in pure neutron matter remains constant and close to the BCS universal value of 1.764 within the mean-field (BCS+HF) approximation, but becomes substantially larger (approximately 2.4 ± 0.3) in the low-density regime when short-range correlations are included via a more sophisticated many-body approach, independently of the interaction used.

## Method

The authors computed singlet pairing gaps and critical temperatures in pure neutron matter using two different many-body approximations: a Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (BCS+HF) approach with a mean-field single-particle spectrum, and a beyond-BCS approach incorporating short-range correlations (SRC) via convolutions of normal propagators from finite-temperature self-consistent Green's function (SCGF) calculations. Both methods used chiral N3LO Entem-Machleidt (EM) two-nucleon interactions with and without N2LO three-nucleon forces (3NF), solving the gap equation iteratively at multiple temperatures to determine the critical temperature where the gap vanishes.

## Key numbers

- For the BCS+HF approximation with the EM interaction, the maximum zero-temperature gap is approximately 3 MeV at a Fermi momentum of 0.9 fm⁻¹; with 3NF included, the maximum gap is reduced by about 0.2 MeV.
- For the BCS+HF approximation at density ρ = 0.04 fm⁻³, the critical temperature is T_c = 1.15 ± 0.05 MeV with the EM interaction and T_c = 0.85 ± 0.05 MeV with EM+3NF.
- The BCS+HF ratio Δ₀/T_c remains constant at approximately 1.764 across a wide density range, within uncertainties of about 0.1.
- For the SRC approximation, the maximum zero-temperature gap is approximately 2.4 MeV, and the maximum critical temperature is below 1 MeV.
- In the SRC approach at low Fermi momenta (k_F < 1 fm⁻¹), the ratio Δ₀/T_c is 2.4 ± 0.3, substantially larger than the BCS prediction of 1.764.
- The SRC results for the EM interaction show a gap closure Fermi momentum of k₂^Δ = 1.46 fm⁻¹ and a critical temperature closure Fermi momentum of k₂^{T_c} = 1.40 fm⁻¹.

## Context

This work extends previous BCS calculations of pairing gaps in neutron matter (Dean 2003, Drischler 2017) by systematically computing both zero-temperature gaps and critical temperatures within the same many-body frameworks, and by examining their ratio. The results contradict the common astrophysical practice of assuming the BCS universal ratio Δ₀/T_c = 1.764 (Abrikosov 1965) for neutron star applications (Yakovlev 2004, Ho 2015). The SRC results are consistent with experimental and theoretical values from the unitary Fermi gas (Boettcher 2014, Randeria 2014) and with quantum Monte Carlo results (Gandolfi 2022) at low densities, suggesting that neutron star cooling and glitch simulations should use density-dependent ratios rather than the BCS constant. The authors note that a fully consistent treatment will require the development of the Nambu-covariant Green's function framework (Drissi 2021a, Drissi 2021b).
