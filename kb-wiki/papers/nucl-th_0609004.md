# Doorway states in nuclear reactions as a manifestation of the "super-radiant" mechanism

> A mechanism is considered for generating doorway states and intermediate structure in low-energy nuclear reactions as a result of collectivization of widths of unstable intrinsic states coupled to common decay channels. At the limit of strong continuum coupling, the segregation of broad (''super-radiating") and narrow (''trapped") states occurs revealing the separation of direct and compound processes. We discuss the conditions for the appearance of intermediate structure in this process and doorways related to certain decay channels.

# Doorway states in nuclear reactions as a manifestation of the "super-radiant" mechanism

## Key claim

Doorway states and intermediate structure in low-energy nuclear reactions can arise from the collectivization of widths of unstable intrinsic states coupled to common decay channels, where at strong continuum coupling the segregation of broad ("super-radiating") and narrow ("trapped") states occurs. The authors discuss conditions for intermediate structure to appear and doorways related to specific decay channels, but present this as a theoretical mechanism rather than a demonstrated experimental observation.

## Method

The authors develop a theoretical framework based on the "super-radiant" mechanism, where intrinsic nuclear states coupled to common decay channels undergo width collectivization. At the limit of strong continuum coupling, the system segregates into broad states that dominate direct reactions and narrow states that correspond to compound processes, with doorway states emerging as intermediate structures in this framework.

## Context

This work extends the concept of super-radiance, previously studied in other areas of physics, to nuclear reactions as an explanation for doorway states and intermediate structure. The mechanism provides a unified description of the separation between direct and compound nuclear processes. The paper enables future theoretical and experimental investigations into how specific decay channels can generate doorway states through width collectivization, and opens questions about the quantitative conditions required for intermediate structure to appear in different nuclear systems.
