# BCS-to-BEC crossover from the exact BCS solution

> The BCS-to-BEC crossover, as well as the nature of Cooper pairs, in a superconducting and Fermi superfluid medium is studied from the exact ground state wavefunction of the reduced BCS Hamiltonian. As the strength of the interaction increases, the ground state continuously evolves from a mixed-system of quasifree fermions and pair resonances (BCS), to pair resonances and quasibound molecules (pseudogap), and finally to a system of quasibound molecules (BEC). A single unified scenario arises where the Cooper-pair wavefunction has a unique functional form. Several exact analytic expressions, such as the binding energy and condensate fraction, are derived. We compare our results with recent experiments in ultracold atomic Fermi gases.

## Key claim

The BCS-to-BEC crossover is a continuous evolution of the ground state, not a sharp phase transition, and the Cooper-pair wavefunction retains a single exponential functional form throughout, contrary to the oscillatory forms obtained from standard BCS mean-field or pair-correlation definitions. The authors emphasize that boundaries between BCS, pseudogap, and BEC regimes are arbitrary because there is no symmetry order parameter or non-analyticity differentiating them.

## Method

The authors used the exact Richardson solution to the reduced BCS Hamiltonian for spin-1/2 fermions in a three-dimensional box with an attractive s-wave contact interaction. They analytically took the thermodynamic limit (N, V → ∞ at fixed density ρ = k_F³/3π²) by solving the Gaudin integral equations, which reduce to a self-consistent gap equation and number equation regularized via the scattering length a_s. The ground state is characterized by a continuous arc Γ of complex pair energies E_ℓ in the complex plane, whose geometry defines the crossover regimes.

## Key numbers

* At the Feshbach resonance (η = 1/k_F a_s = 0), the fraction of complex pair energies f = 0.87 and the condensate fraction λ ≈ 0.7.
* The BCS-pseudogap boundary occurs at η = 0.37, where all pair energies become complex (f = 1) and the effective Fermi sea disappears.
* The pseudogap-BEC boundary occurs at η = 0.55, where the chemical potential μ = 0.
* In the extreme BCS limit (η → -∞), the gap Δ = (8/e²) exp(π η/2) in units of ε_F, and the condensate fraction λ = (3π/e²) exp(π η/2).
* In the extreme BEC limit (η → +∞), the chemical potential μ = -η², the gap Δ = √(16η/3π), and the condensate fraction λ = 1.
* The Cooper-pair size at the arc extremes in the BCS limit is ξ_E = π ξ₀/√2, where ξ₀ = 2/(π Δ) is Pippard's coherence length.

## Context

This work extends the exact solution of the reduced BCS Hamiltonian, originally developed by Richardson (1963) and Gaudin, to the full BCS-to-BEC crossover with a quadratic single-particle dispersion. It contrasts with the mean-field ansatz of Leggett (1980) and the pair-correlation function approach, showing that those definitions produce oscillatory wavefunctions that only coincide with the exact Cooper-pair wavefunction in the extreme BEC limit. The results provide a qualitative explanation for experiments on ultracold atomic Fermi gases near a Feshbach resonance, such as those by Regal et al. (2004), without requiring a projection method. The work enables future studies using the exact ansatz as a variational state for more realistic interactions or for two-channel atom-molecule models.
