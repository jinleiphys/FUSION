# Universal Relation for the Neutron Star Maximum Mass within Relativistic Mean-Field Theories

> We obtain a universal relation for the neutron star maximum mass arising from a particular combination of the saturation density ($n_0$), the effective mass ($m^*$), and (when present) the vector meson self-coupling constant ($ζ$) within the relativistic mean-field model framework. Observations of massive neutron stars heavier than $\sim 2M_{\odot}$ have eliminated the softest equation of state from consideration and impose strong constraints on nuclear interactions used to model dense nuclear matter. To date there have been numerous attempts to refine relativistic mean-field models by including the presence of additional mesons, such as the delta meson, and couplings. We show that current RMF models, including our own constructions, exhibit a maximum neutron star mass that is primarily determined by the combination of the saturation density, the effective mass at saturation, and the vector meson self-coupling constant. When constraining the pure neutron matter equation of state using chiral effective field theory (ChEFT) at low densities, 250 parameter sets were generated to derive an empirical formula for the maximum mass of neutron stars and apply the formula with the present relativistic mean field models.

## Key claim

The paper claims that within the relativistic mean-field (RMF) framework, the maximum mass of a neutron star is primarily determined by only three nuclear matter properties: the saturation density ($n_0$), the effective mass at saturation ($m^*$), and the vector meson self-coupling constant ($\zeta$), with other properties such as incompressibility ($K$) and binding energy per nucleon ($B/A$) having negligible impact. This claim is hedged by the condition that the relation holds when the pure neutron matter equation of state is constrained by chiral effective field theory (ChEFT) at low densities and when hyperons and delta mesons are excluded from the minimal model.

## Method

The authors generated 250 parameter sets within the RMF framework by varying the saturation density ($n_0$) from 0.145 to 0.165 fm$^{-3}$, the effective mass ($m^*$) from 0.54 to 0.72, and the vector meson self-coupling ($\zeta$) over values 0, 0.001, 0.002, and 0.005, while fixing the incompressibility at $K=240$ MeV and binding energy per nucleon at $B/A=-16.3$ MeV. The isospin-related parameters ($g_\rho$, $\Lambda_v$) were constrained by fitting the pure neutron matter equation of state to ChEFT results from Holt and Kaiser (2017) at densities up to $0.21$ fm$^{-3}$ using a chi-square minimization. The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations were then solved for each equation of state to obtain the maximum neutron star mass, and a least-squares fitting procedure was used to derive an empirical formula for $M_{\rm max}$ as a function of $n_0$, $m^*$, and $\zeta$.

## Key numbers

* Saturation density $n_0$ was varied over the range $0.145$ to $0.165$ fm$^{-3}$ in steps of $0.005$ fm$^{-3}$.
* Effective mass $m^*$ was varied over the range $0.54$ to $0.72$ in steps of $0.02$.
* Vector meson self-coupling $\zeta$ was varied over the values $0$, $0.001$, $0.002$, and $0.005$; cases with $\zeta=0.01$ were excluded because none predicted a $\sim 2.0 M_\odot$ neutron star.
* Incompressibility $K$ was fixed at $240$ MeV and binding energy per nucleon $B/A$ at $-16.3$ MeV.
* The empirical formula coefficients were determined as: $f(\zeta) = -123900\ \zeta^2 + 1084.4\ \zeta - 3.6812$, $g(\zeta) = -312652\ \zeta^2 + 2681.2\ \zeta - 8.4824$, $h(\zeta) = +148575\ \zeta^2 - 1335.3\ \zeta + 6.1959$, where $M_{\rm max} = f(\zeta)\, n_0 + g(\zeta)\, m^* + h(\zeta)$.

## Context

This work extends previous efforts to refine RMF models by including additional mesons and couplings, such as the delta meson studied by Virender Thakur (2022), Fan Li (2022), and Miyatsu (2023), which were found to enhance the maximum mass but also introduced ambiguities in the effective mass splitting. The paper builds on the observation that existing RMF parameter sets like NL3 (Lalazissis, 1997), FSU (Todd-Rutel, 2005), and IU-FSU (Fattoyev, 2010) either fail to predict sufficiently high maximum masses or have other deficiencies, and it enables future work to quickly estimate the maximum mass of a neutron star from a given RMF Lagrangian without solving the TOV equations. The empirical formula also provides a systematic understanding of how RMF theory relates to the maximum mass, which can guide the development of new parameter sets that simultaneously describe finite nuclei and neutron star observations.
