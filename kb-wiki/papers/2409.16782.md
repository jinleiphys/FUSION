# Magnetic dipole contribution to the $γ$-spectrum from $d$-$t$ collisions

> One in about a few hundred thousand sub-Coulomb $d$-$t$ collisions is accompanied by the emission of a $γ$-photon. The $γ$-spectrum from these collisions is dominated by a 16.7 MeV peak, corresponding to the population of the $p$-wave $3/2^-$ $α$-$n$ ground-state resonance in the E1 transition from an intermediate $d$-wave $α$-$n$ state, corresponding to the $^5$He excited state around 16.7 MeV. The strength of this spectrum at the large $γ$-energy endpoint decreases fast both due to kinematic factors and due to centrifugal repulsion between neutron and $α$-particle at near-zero relative energies. However, no centrifugal repulsion would occur if $s$-wave $α$-$n$ states were populated in magnetic dipole transition. Therefore, one can expect that close to the $γ$-spectrum endpoint around 17.5 MeV the M1 transition could dominate, leading to additional counts in experimental spectra which could be easily misinterpreted as a background. This work presents calculations of the M1 contribution to the $d+t\rightarrow α+n+γ$ cross section caused both by convection and magnetization currents. While the first contribution was found to be negligible, the one from magnetization depends strongly on the choice of the model for the $d$-$t$ channel scattering wave function. Using $d$-$t$ interaction models from the literature, represented either by a gaussian of by a square well, resulted in unrealistically strong M1 contribution. With constructed in the present work optical folding potential, correctly representing the sizes of deuteron and triton, the M1 contribution near the endpoint is reduced to about 2$\%$ of the 16.7-MeV E1 peak. Arguments in favor of more detailed calculations, that could potentially change this number, are put forward.

## Key claim

The M1 contribution to the $d+t \rightarrow \alpha+n+\gamma$ cross section near the 17.5 MeV gamma-energy endpoint is reduced to about 2% of the 16.7-MeV E1 peak when using an optical folding potential that correctly represents deuteron and triton sizes, but this number could change with more detailed calculations.

## Method

The M1 transition amplitude was calculated using the exact M1 operator, separated into convection-current and magnetization-current contributions, for the $d+t \rightarrow \alpha+n+\gamma$ reaction at 50 keV center-of-mass energy. The final $\alpha$-$n$ wave function was obtained from a potential model with Perey-correction for nonlocality, while the initial $d$-$t$ channel wave function was generated using three different interaction models: a gaussian potential from Dubovichenko et al, a square well optical model from Wu et al, and a newly constructed optical folding potential. The differential cross section was computed and expressed as a branching ratio $\sigma_{dt\gamma}(E_{\gamma})/\sigma_{dt}$.

## Key numbers

- Convection-induced M1 branching ratio integrated above $E_{\gamma} = 0.85$ MeV: $5.15 \times 10^{-8}$, about three orders of magnitude smaller than the E1 value of $5 \times 10^{-5}$
- Gaussian potential (Dubovichenko et al) M1 integrated branching ratio: $4.1 \times 10^{-5}$, comparable to the E1 transition
- Square well potential (Wu et al) M1 integrated branching ratio: $1.3 \times 10^{-6}$, about 3% of the E1 value
- Optical folding model M1 integrated branching ratio above $E_{\gamma}=10$ MeV: $7.7 \times 10^{-5}$, about 1% of the E1 value
- At the main 16.7 MeV peak, the optical folding model M1 contribution is about 2% of the E1 peak
- The $d$-$t$ reaction cross section used for folding model normalization: 4.3 b, corresponding to astrophysical S-factor of 27 MeV b

## Context

This work extends the first theoretical prediction of the $d+t \rightarrow \alpha+n+\gamma$ reaction reported by Timofeyuk (2024), which calculated the E1 contribution and found the main 16.7 MeV peak. The M1 contribution had been neglected previously because magnetic dipole transitions are typically suppressed compared to E1, but the absence of centrifugal repulsion for $s$-wave $\alpha$-$n$ states near the endpoint motivated this study. The results suggest that experimental spectra from inertial confinement fusion facilities (Kim 2012, Jee 2021, Mea 2021, Moh 2022, Moh 2023, Mea 2024) and JET (Dal 2024, Reb 2024) may contain M1-originated counts near the endpoint that could be misidentified as background, and the present calculations provide a basis for correcting this systematic error.
