# Systematic of isovector and isoscalar giant quadrupole resonances in normal and superfluid deformed nuclei

> The systematic study of isoscalar (IS) and isovector (IV) giant quadrupole responses (GQR) in normal and superfluid nuclei presented in [G. Scamps and D. Lacroix, Phys. Rev. 88, 044310 (2013)] is extended to the case of axially deformed and triaxial nuclei. The static and dynamical energy density functional based on Skyrme effective interaction are used to study static properties and dynamical response functions over the whole nuclear chart. Among the 749 nuclei that are considered, 301 and 65 are respectively found to be prolate and oblate while 54 do not present any symmetry axis. For these nuclei, the IS- and IV-GQR response functions are systematically obtained. In these nuclei, different aspects related to the interplay between deformation and collective motion are studied. We show that some aspects like the fragmentation of the response induced by deformation effects in axially symmetric and triaxial nuclei can be rather well understood using simple arguments. Besides this simplicity, more complex effects show up like the appearance of non-trivial deformation effects on the collective motion damping or the influence of hexadecapole or higher-orders effects. A specific study is made on the triaxial nuclei where the absence of symmetry axis adds further complexity to the nuclear response. The relative importance of geometric deformation effects and coupling to other vibrational modes are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper extends a previous systematic study of isoscalar (IS) and isovector (IV) giant quadrupole resonances (GQR) from spherical nuclei to axially deformed and triaxial nuclei across the nuclear chart, showing that while deformation-induced fragmentation of the response can be understood with simple geometric and fluid-dynamical models, more complex effects such as hexadecapole deformation and coupling to other vibrational modes also contribute significantly, especially to the damping width.

## Method

The authors used the static and dynamical Skyrme energy density functional with the SkM* interaction in the mean-field channel, employing the EV8 code for ground-state initialization and a time-dependent Hartree-Fock plus BCS (TDHF+BCS) approach to compute the dynamical response. For 749 nuclei spanning the nuclear chart, they applied quadrupole boost operators for each K-projection (K=0, ±1, ±2) to the ground state, followed by time evolution to extract the IS-GQR and IV-GQR strength functions. The response functions were analyzed by fitting Lorentzian shapes to extract mean energies and widths for each K-component and for the total strength.

## Key numbers

- 749 nuclei were considered in total; among these, 301 were found to be prolate, 65 oblate, and 54 triaxial (no symmetry axis)
- For 7 nuclei, convergence of the static solution could not be achieved; these were removed from the analysis
- The sum rule was satisfied within a maximum error of 0.5% in all considered nuclei
- The total IS-GQR width as a function of deformation parameter δ was fitted as Γ_tot = 0.35 + 23.7 δ^2 [MeV]
- The fitted coefficients for the IS-GQR energy splitting (E_{|K|} = E̅_{2+} (1 + a₁ δ + a₂ δ²)): for |K|=0, a₁ = -0.240, a₂ = -0.672; for |K|=1, a₁ = -0.167, a₂ = 0.308; for |K|=2, a₁ = 0.305, a₂ = -0.036
- For the IV-GQR: |K|=0, a₁ = -0.277, a₂ = -0.052; |K|=1, a₁ = -0.111, a₂ = 0.032; |K|=2, a₁ = 0.250, a₂ = -0.006

## Context

This work extends the systematic study of GQR in spherical nuclei by Scamps and Lacroix (2013) to deformed systems, using the same TDHF+BCS methodology. The results are compared with state-of-the-art quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) calculations for axially deformed nuclei by Yoshida (2013), showing excellent agreement for peak positions and reasonable agreement for widths. The paper demonstrates that TDHF+BCS can serve as a powerful tool for systematic studies of giant resonances across the nuclear chart, including triaxial nuclei where QRPA approaches remain challenging, and provides a benchmark for understanding the interplay between deformation, pairing, and collective motion in nuclear response functions.
