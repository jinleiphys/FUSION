# Examination of the $^{22}$C radius determination with interaction cross sections

> A nuclear radius of $^{22}$C is investigated with the total reaction cross sections at medium- to high-incident energies in order to resolve the radius puzzle in which two recent interaction cross section measurements using $^1$H and $^{12}$C targets show the quite different radii. The cross sections of $^{22}$C are calculated consistently for these target nuclei within a reliable microscopic framework, the Glauber theory. To describe appropriately such a reaction involving a spatially extended nucleus, the multiple scattering processes within the Glauber theory are fully taken into account, that is, the multi-dimensional integration in the Glauber amplitude is evaluated using a Monte Carlo technique without recourse to the optical-limit approximation. We discuss the sensitivity of the spatially extended halo tail to the total reaction cross sections. The root-mean-square matter radius obtained in this study is consistent with that extracted from the recent cross section measurement on $^{12}$C target. We show that the simultaneous reproduction of the two recent measured cross sections is not feasible within this framework.

## Key claim

The authors show that the root-mean-square matter radius of $^{22}$C extracted from the interaction cross section on a $^{12}$C target is consistent with the recent high-precision measurement, but that simultaneous reproduction of both the $^{12}$C-target and $^{1}$H-target cross section measurements is not feasible within the Glauber framework, even when full multiple-scattering effects are included via Monte Carlo integration.

## Method

The authors calculate total reaction cross sections for $^{22}$C on $^{1}$H and $^{12}$C targets using the Glauber multiple-scattering theory with the multi-dimensional Glauber amplitude evaluated by Monte Carlo integration, avoiding the optical-limit approximation. The $^{22}$C wave function is constructed from Woods-Saxon single-particle states with a $^{20}$C core plus two halo neutrons in the $1s_{1/2}$ orbital, where the $l=0$ potential depth is varied to control the matter radius. The Monte Carlo integration uses $10^8$ configurations to achieve convergence within 1% for the extended halo wave function.

## Key numbers

- The two-neutron separation energy of $^{22}$C is 0.42$\pm$0.94 MeV (Audi 2003) and 0.14$\pm$0.46 MeV (Gaudefroy 2012).
- The first interaction cross section measurement on $^{1}$H target at 40 MeV/nucleon gave 1338$\pm$274 mb, yielding a matter radius of 5.4$\pm$0.9 fm (Tanaka 2010).
- The high-precision measurement on $^{12}$C target at $\sim$240 MeV/nucleon gave a radius of 3.44$\pm$0.08 fm (Togano 2016).
- The authors extract a root-mean-square matter radius of 3.38$\pm$0.10 fm from the $^{12}$C-target data, consistent with the Togano (2016) result.
- The $^{20}$C root-mean-square matter radius used in the calculations is 3.03 fm, determined from the interaction cross section at 905 MeV (Ozawa 2001).
- The point-proton radius of $^{12}$C used to fix the harmonic-oscillator length is 2.33 fm (Angeli 2013).

## Context

This work addresses the "radius puzzle" of $^{22}$C, where the interaction cross section measurement on $^{1}$H target by Tanaka (2010) gave a much larger radius (5.4$\pm$0.9 fm) than the later measurement on $^{12}$C target by Togano (2016) (3.44$\pm$0.08 fm). The authors extend prior Glauber analyses that used the optical-limit approximation by employing a full Monte Carlo evaluation of the Glauber amplitude, following the approach of Varga (2002) and Gibbs (2012). The results confirm the smaller radius from the $^{12}$C-target measurement and demonstrate that the $^{1}$H-target data cannot be simultaneously reproduced, suggesting systematic issues with the earlier experiment. This work enables more reliable radius extractions for halo nuclei and quantifies the limitations of using inclusive reaction cross sections with different targets.
