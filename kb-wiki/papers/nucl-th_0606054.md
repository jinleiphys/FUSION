# Relativistic QRPA description of isoscalar compression modes in open-shell nuclei in the A$\approx$60 mass region

> Very recent inelastic $α$-scattering data on the isoscalar monopole and dipole strength distributions in $^{56}$Fe, $^{58}$Ni, and $^{60}$Ni are analyzed in the relativistic quasiparticle random-phase approximation (RQRPA) with the DD-ME2 effective nuclear interaction (nuclear matter compression modulus K$_{nm}= 251$ MeV). In all three nuclei the calculation nicely reproduces the observed asymmetric shapes of the monopole strength, and the bimodal structure of the dipole strength distributions. The calculated centroid and mean energies are in very good qualitative agreement with the experimental values both for the monopole, and for the low- and high-energy components of the dipole transition strengths. It is noted, however, that while DD-ME2 reproduces in detail the excitation energies of the giant monopole resonances (GMR) in nuclei with $A \ge 90$, the theoretical centroids are systematically above the experimental values in lighter nuclei with $A \leq 60$. The latter can be reproduced with an effective interaction with a lower value of K$_{nm} \approx $ 230 MeV but, because of the asymmetric shapes and pronounced fragmentation of the monopole strength distributions, isoscalar GMR data in light nuclei cannot provide accurate estimates of the nuclear matter compression modulus.

## Key claim

The relativistic QRPA with the DD-ME2 interaction (nuclear matter compression modulus K_nm = 251 MeV) reproduces the asymmetric monopole and bimodal dipole strength distributions in ⁵⁶Fe, ⁵⁸Ni, and ⁶⁰Ni, but the theoretical centroid energies for the giant monopole resonance are systematically above experimental values in these lighter nuclei (A ≤ 60), whereas the same interaction reproduces data well for A ≥ 90. The data in light nuclei can be matched with a lower K_nm ≈ 230 MeV, but the asymmetric shapes and fragmentation prevent accurate extraction of the nuclear matter compression modulus from these light systems.

## Method

Fully self-consistent relativistic quasiparticle random-phase approximation (RQRPA) calculations were performed in the canonical single-nucleon basis of the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) model. The DD-ME2 density-dependent meson-nucleon interaction was used in the particle-hole channel, and the finite-range Gogny force in the particle-particle channel, with the same interaction parameters in both the RHB and RQRPA equations to ensure self-consistency and eliminate spurious components. Isoscalar E0 and E1 strength distributions were calculated for ⁵⁶Fe, ⁵⁸Ni, and ⁶⁰Ni and compared with recent inelastic alpha-scattering data from Lui et al. (2006).

## Key numbers

*   Nuclear matter compression modulus of DD-ME2: K_nm = 251 MeV.
*   For nuclei with A ≥ 90, DD-ME2 reproduces ISGMR excitation energies in detail; for A ≤ 60, theoretical centroids are systematically above experimental values.
*   The ISGMR centroids in A ≤ 60 nuclei are better described by an effective interaction with K_nm ≈ 230 MeV.
*   In the experiment, between 56% and 72% of the isoscalar E1 strength was located below 40 MeV excitation energy in these nuclei.
*   The calculated low-energy E1 component centroid energies are systematically below experimental values by approximately 1–4 MeV, depending on the choice of separation energy E_c.

## Context

This work extends previous relativistic RPA analyses of isoscalar compression modes in heavier nuclei (Vretenar et al., 2003) to the A ≈ 60 mass region, where experimental data were recently provided by Lui et al. (2006). The study addresses the open question of whether light-nucleus ISGMR data can constrain the nuclear matter compression modulus, concluding that surface incompressibility and strong fragmentation limit such extraction. The results contrast with earlier non-relativistic Skyrme RPA studies (Colo et al., 2004; Shlomo et al., 2002) that found K_nm = 230–240 MeV for heavy nuclei, and support the relativistic framework's narrower allowed range of K_nm = 250–270 MeV for heavier systems while showing that lighter nuclei require a softer equation of state.
