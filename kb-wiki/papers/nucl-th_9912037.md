# An investigation of standard thermodynamic quantities as determined via models of nuclear multifragmentation

> Both simple and sophisticated models are frequently used in an attempt to understand how real nuclei breakup when subjected to large excitation energies, a process known as nuclear multifragmentation. Many of these models assume equilibriumthermodynamics and produce results often interpreted as evidence of a phase transition. This work examines one class of models and employs standard thermodynamical procedure to explore the possible existence and nature of a phase transition. The role of various terms, e.g. Coulomb and surface energy, is discussed.

## Key claim

This work examines whether standard thermodynamic analysis of statistical multifragmentation models can reliably identify a phase transition, finding that the critical temperature and density depend sensitively on model choices such as the inclusion of Coulomb and surface energy terms, and that the critical densities obtained are too high to be physically realizable with non-overlapping spherical nuclei.

## Method

The authors compute the canonical partition function via a recursion relation for a system of 162 nucleons, using the free energy of nuclear fragments parameterized with bulk, symmetry, surface, and Coulomb terms. They vary the free volume of the system over a wide range (Vf/V0 from 2e-8 to 2e8) and temperature from 1 to 14 MeV, then derive thermodynamic quantities including pressure, isothermal compressibility, and specific heat by numerical differentiation. Three model versions are compared: V1 (full model with Coulomb), V2 (no Coulomb), and V3 (no Coulomb, temperature-independent surface term), with a van der Waals fluid used as a benchmark.

## Key numbers

* Critical temperature for V1: 7.575 ± 0.01 MeV
* Critical temperature for V2: 6.875 ± 0.01 MeV
* Critical temperature for V3: 10.975 ± 0.01 MeV
* Critical density for V1: 0.94 ± 0.01 × ρ0 (where ρ0 is normal nuclear density)
* Critical density for V2: 0.74 ± 0.01 × ρ0
* Critical density for V3: 0.85 ± 0.01 × ρ0
* Critical pressure for V1: (3.2 ± 0.1) × 10^(-3) MeV/fm^3
* Critical pressure for V2: (7.3 ± 0.1) × 10^(-4) MeV/fm^3
* Critical pressure for V3: (2.4 ± 0.1) × 10^(-3) MeV/fm^3
* Compressibility factor Cf for V1: 0.49 ± 0.02
* Compressibility factor Cf for V2: 0.16 ± 0.02
* Compressibility factor Cf for V3: 0.28 ± 0.02
* System size used in calculations: 162 nucleons with A/Z = 2.5
* Parameters used: W0 = 16 MeV, ε0 = 16 MeV, γ = 25 MeV, β0 = 18 MeV, Tc = 16 MeV, r0 = 1.17 fm

## Context

This work extends the statistical multifragmentation model approach of Bondorf et al. (1995) and the canonical partition function method of Mekjian (1991) by allowing the system volume to vary and examining the effects of Coulomb and surface energy parameterizations on the thermodynamics. The authors find that the critical temperature increases when Coulomb is added (V1 vs V2), which is counterintuitive compared to models by Jaqaman et al. (1984) and De et al. (1995) that show the opposite behavior. The results raise questions about whether such phenomenological models can self-consistently describe a phase transition in finite nuclear systems, and suggest that if physical constraints on fragment packing are imposed, only first-order phase transitions are possible.
