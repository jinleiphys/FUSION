# Accelerating Eigenvalue Computation for Nuclear Structure Calculations via Perturbative Corrections

> We present a new method for computing the lowest few eigenvalues and the corresponding eigenvectors of a nuclear many-body Hamiltonian represented in a truncated configuration interaction subspace, i.e., the no-core shell model (NCSM). The method uses the hierarchical structure of the NCSM Hamiltonian to partition the Hamiltonian as the sum of two matrices. The first matrix corresponds to the Hamiltonian represented in a small configuration space, whereas the second is viewed as the perturbation to the first matrix. Eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the first matrix can be computed efficiently. Perturbative corrections to the eigenvectors of the first matrix can be obtained from the solutions of a sequence of linear systems of equations defined in the small configuration space. These correction vectors can be combined with the approximate eigenvectors of the first matrix to construct a subspace from which more accurate approximations of the desired eigenpairs can be obtained. We call this method a Subspace Projection with Perturbative Corrections (SPPC) method. We show by numerical examples that the SPPC method can be more efficient than conventional iterative methods for solving large-scale eigenvalue problems such as the Lanczos, block Lanczos and the locally optimal block preconditioned conjugate gradient (LOBPCG) method. The method can also be combined with other methods to avoid convergence stagnation.

## Key claim

The Subspace Projection with Perturbative Corrections (SPPC) method can compute the lowest few eigenvalues and eigenvectors of a no-core shell model Hamiltonian more efficiently than conventional iterative methods such as Lanczos, block Lanczos, and LOBPCG, but only when the perturbation order is kept low (typically up to 15) because the method can stagnate at higher orders and must then be combined with other algorithms to avoid convergence stagnation.

## Method

The Hamiltonian matrix H is partitioned as H = H0 + V, where H0 contains the leading submatrix from a smaller configuration space (lower Nmax) padded with zeros, and V is treated as a perturbation. The eigenvectors of H0 are computed efficiently from the small submatrix, and perturbative correction vectors of successively higher order are obtained by solving a sequence of linear systems that reduce to much smaller systems in the small configuration space. These correction vectors are combined with the initial eigenvectors to form a low-dimensional subspace, from which improved approximations to the desired eigenpairs are extracted via the Rayleigh-Ritz procedure.

## Key numbers

- The leading submatrix of H for 12C at Nmax=4 is approximately 63 times smaller than the full matrix (the submatrix corresponds to Nmax=2)
- The SPPC method requires only kev sparse matrix-vector multiplications (SpMVs) per iteration, where kev is the number of desired eigenpairs
- The highest order of perturbation is typically limited to 15, beyond which no significant improvement in the approximate eigenpair can be observed

## Context

This work extends the eigenvector continuation (EC) approach of Frame (2018) and the perturbative correction subspace ideas of Franzke (2022) and Demol (2020) by providing a detailed computational cost analysis and comparison with state-of-the-art eigensolvers. Unlike prior EC methods that require computing eigenvectors of full-sized Hamiltonians at multiple parameter values, SPPC constructs its subspace from perturbative corrections obtained by solving much smaller linear systems. The method enables faster computation of nuclear structure observables in the no-core shell model and can be combined with algorithms such as Lanczos, block Lanczos, LOBPCG, or RMM-DIIS to overcome convergence stagnation at higher perturbation orders.
