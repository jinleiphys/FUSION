# Toward hybrid quantum simulations with qubits and qumodes on trapped-ion platforms

> We explore the feasibility of gate-based hybrid quantum computing using both discrete (qubit) and continuous (qumode) variables on trapped-ion platforms. Trapped-ion systems have demonstrated record one- and two-qubit gate fidelities and long qubit coherence times, while qumodes, which can be represented by the collective vibrational modes of the ion chain, have remained relatively unexplored for their use in computing. Using numerical simulations, we show that high-fidelity hybrid gates and measurement operations can be achieved for existing trapped-ion quantum platforms. As an exemplary application, we consider quantum simulations of the Jaynes-Cummings-Hubbard model, which is given by a one-dimensional chain of interacting spin and boson degrees of freedom. Using classical simulations, we study its real-time evolution and develop a suitable variational quantum algorithm for ground state preparation. Our results motivate further studies of hybrid quantum computing in this context, which may lead to direct applications in condensed matter and fundamental particle and nuclear physics.

## Key claim

Using numerical simulations, the authors demonstrate that high-fidelity hybrid qubit-qumode gates and measurement operations are feasible on existing trapped-ion platforms, provided that gate times remain within the coherence times of the qumodes (approximately 10-100 milliseconds in linear Paul traps). The work focuses on gate-based hybrid quantum computing, as opposed to analog-digital hybrid simulations considered in prior work.

## Method

The authors model a linear Paul trap with eight \(^{171}\text{Yb}^+\) ions, where qubits are encoded in hyperfine states and qumodes are realized as collective vibrational (phonon) modes of the ion chain. They calculate the mode spectrum and participation vectors by minimizing the total potential (trap plus Coulomb repulsion) and linearizing the net force on each ion. Gate times and fidelities for qubit, qumode, and hybrid gates are estimated from experimental demonstrations and theoretical predictions in the literature, with a focus on the Jaynes-Cummings-Hubbard model as an exemplary application.

## Key numbers

- Trap frequencies for the pseudopotential: \(\{\omega_x, \omega_y, \omega_z\} = 2\pi \times \{2.0, 2.5, 0.12\}\) MHz.
- Qubit coherence times of several seconds (for \(^{171}\text{Yb}^+\) hyperfine levels); motional mode coherence times of 10-100 milliseconds in linear Paul traps, reaching 1 second in Penning traps.
- Estimated gate time for a controlled-NOT (CNOT) gate: 30 microseconds with 99.9% fidelity.
- Estimated gate time for a red sideband (RSB) or blue sideband (BSB) hybrid gate: 200 microseconds with 99.9% fidelity.
- Estimated gate time for a Kerr gate: 10 milliseconds with 95% fidelity (the slowest gate considered).
- Estimated gate time for a beam splitter gate: 250 microseconds with 99% fidelity.
- For a chain of eight ions with modes from a single radial band, using two control qubits is sufficient to keep all beam splitter gate times well below the typical qumode coherence time (assumed 5 ms) when coupling seven modes.

## Context

This work extends the hybrid qubit-qumode framework recently developed by the Co-design Center for Quantum Advantage (C2QA) (Stavenger 2022, Liu 2024, Crane 2024), which focused primarily on superconducting circuit implementations. The authors highlight key differences between trapped-ion and superconducting platforms, such as the higher connectivity but shorter qumode coherence times in trapped ions. The study enables future hybrid quantum simulations of the Jaynes-Cummings-Hubbard model and related lattice field theories, building on prior work on the JCH model by Angelakis (2007), Schmidt (2009), and Nunnenkamp (2011).
