# Particle production in Ultra-relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions : A Statistical-Thermal Model Review

> The current status of various thermal and statistical descriptions of particle production in the ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions experiments is presented in detail. We discuss the formulation of various types of thermal models of a hot and dense hadron gas (HG) and the methods incorporated in implementing the interactions between hadrons. We first obtain the parameterization of center-of-mass energy ($\sqrt{s_{NN}}$) in terms of temperature ($T$) and baryon chemical potential ($μ_B$) obtained by analyzing the particle ratios at the freeze-out over a broad energy range from the lowest Alternating Gradient Synchrotron (AGS) energy to the highest Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider (RHIC) energies. The results of various thermal models together with the experimental results for the various ratios of yields of produced hadrons are then compared. We have derived some new universal conditions emerging at the chemical freeze-out of HG fireball which demonstrate the independence with respect to the energy as well as the structure of the nuclei used in the collision. Further, we perform the calculation of various transport properties of HG such as shear viscosity-to-entropy density ratio ($η/s$) etc. using thermal model and compare with the results of other models. We also present the calculation of the rapidity as well as transverse mass spectra of various hadrons in the thermal HG model. The purpose of this review article is to organize and summarize the experimental data obtained in various experiments with heavy-ion collisions and then to examine and analyze them using thermal models so that a firm conclusion regarding the formation of quark-gluon plasma (QGP) can be obtained.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that thermal and statistical models, particularly those incorporating excluded-volume effects for baryons, provide a satisfactory description of particle production in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions across a wide energy range, but the authors note that a clean, unambiguous signal for quark-gluon plasma (QGP) formation has not yet been established in the laboratory, and understanding the background hadron gas is essential regardless of whether QGP is formed.

## Method

The authors review and compare multiple thermal model formulations for a hot, dense hadron gas, including the Hagedorn model, Cleymans-Suhonen model, Rischke-Gorenstein-Stocken-Greiner (RGSG) model, and their own new excluded-volume model. They extract chemical freeze-out parameters (temperature T and baryon chemical potential μ_B) by analyzing particle yield ratios over a broad energy range from AGS to RHIC, and calculate transport properties such as shear viscosity-to-entropy density ratio (η/s). The authors also compute rapidity and transverse mass spectra of hadrons using their thermal model, incorporating quantum statistics and imposing strangeness neutrality.

## Key numbers

- The energy density of the QGP phase is expected to reach above 3–5 GeV/fm³, equivalent to a temperature of 200–300 MeV.
- The pre-equilibrium state time is predicted to be about 1 fm/c or less.
- The mixed phase in a first-order phase transition is expected to last for a relatively long time (τ > 10 fm/c).
- The new excluded-volume model uses a hard-core radius r = 0.8 fm for each baryon, with all baryons and mesons having masses up to a cut-off value of 2 GeV/c².
- The new excluded-volume model cannot perform calculations for T > 185 MeV and μ_B > 450 MeV when using Boltzmann statistics, though the quantum statistics extension removes this limitation.

## Context

This review extends and systematizes the work of numerous prior thermal model studies, including those by Cleymans (1987), Rischke (1991), Hagedorn (1980), and Singh (1996), among many others cited in the text. The paper compares various excluded-volume and mean-field approaches for the hadron gas equation of state, addressing issues of thermodynamic consistency that plagued earlier models. The work enables future comparisons between thermal model predictions and experimental data from AGS through LHC energies, and provides a framework for testing whether observed particle yields indicate thermal equilibration and QGP formation.
