# Many-Body Theory of the Electroweak Nuclear Response

> After a brief review of the theoretical description of nuclei based on nonrelativistic many-body theory and realistic hamiltonians, these lectures focus on its application to the analysis of the electroweak response. Special emphasis is given to electron-nucleus scattering, whose experimental study has provided a wealth of information on nuclear structure and dynamics, exposing the limitations of the shell model. The extension of the formalism to the case of neutrino-nucleus interactions, whose quantitative understanding is required to reduce the systematic uncertainty of neutrino oscillation experiments, is also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that nonrelativistic nuclear many-body theory (NMBT), using realistic Hamiltonians fitted to deuteron properties and nucleon-nucleon scattering data, can provide a quantitatively accurate description of electroweak nuclear responses, but only when dynamical correlations beyond the shell model are explicitly included; the abstract's claim of applicability to neutrino-nucleus interactions is conditional on the extension of the formalism being "also discussed" rather than demonstrated with results.

## Method

The author reviews the formalism of NMBT, starting from a Hamiltonian with two- and three-nucleon potentials fitted to scattering phase shifts and few-body binding energies, then introduces correlated basis function (CBF) perturbation theory to handle short-range repulsion. The nuclear response to a scalar probe is expressed via the polarization propagator and spectral functions, with the impulse approximation (IA) derived by factorizing the cross section into a product of the hole spectral function and a free-nucleon tensor. For electron-nucleus scattering, the cross section is written in Born approximation in terms of structure functions \(W_1\) and \(W_2\), and the IA is extended to include final-state interactions (FSI) through a folding function obtained from the NN scattering amplitude in the eikonal approximation.

## Context

This lecture paper extends the nonrelativistic many-body framework developed by Fetter and Walecka (1971) and Benhar, Fabrocini, and Fantoni (1989, 1992) for electron-nucleus scattering, and applies it to the problem of neutrino-nucleus interactions needed for oscillation experiments. It contrasts the shell model (which fails to account for correlation-induced depletion of spectroscopic strength) with correlated basis function methods and G-matrix perturbation theory (Day, 1967). The work enables future calculations of neutrino-nucleus cross sections with quantified systematic uncertainties, though the neutrino-specific results are not presented here.
