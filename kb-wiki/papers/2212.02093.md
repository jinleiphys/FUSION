# A Kohn-Sham Scheme Based Neural Network for Nuclear Systems

> A Kohn-Sham scheme based multi-task neural network is elaborated for the supervised learning of nuclear shell evolution. The training set is composed of the single-particle wave functions and occupation probabilities of 320 nuclei, calculated by the Skyrme density functional theory. It is found that the deduced density distributions, momentum distributions, and charge radii are in good agreements with the benchmarking results for the untrained nuclei. In particular, accomplishing shell evolution leads to a remarkable improvement in the extrapolation of nuclear density. After a further charge-radius-based calibration, the network evolves a stronger predictive capability. This opens the possibility to infer correlations among observables by combining experimental data for nuclear complex systems.

## Key claim

A Kohn-Sham scheme based multi-task neural network (KSN) can generate nuclear single-particle wave functions and occupation probabilities that reproduce Skyrme-Hartree-Fock results for untrained nuclei, but the extrapolation performance depends critically on the network learning shell evolution rather than just densities; the network's predictive capability improves after calibration with experimental charge radii, though it still partly relies on theoretical calculations.

## Method

The authors constructed a multi-task fully connected neural network with 30 branch cells, each corresponding to a single-particle state, taking proton number Z and neutron number N as input and outputting \(\sqrt{w_i}\varphi_i(r)\) on a 1D radial grid (0.1 fm spacing, 150 points). The network was trained in two stages: first, a pre-training step using a loss function comparing predicted \(\sqrt{w_i'}\varphi_i(r)\) to Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) with SkM* interaction plus BCS pairing targets for 320 nuclei; second, a fine-tuning step that enforced particle number conservation via a normalization operator and added a penalty term for mass number deviation. A subsequent calibration step incorporated experimental charge radii for over 600 nuclei with Z>40 into the loss function.

## Key numbers

- Training set: 320 nuclei (approximately 10% of discovered nuclei)
- Radial grid: 150 points with spacing 0.1 fm, from r=0 to r=14.9 fm
- Pre-training: 1500 epochs (about 20 GPU minutes); fine-tuning: 500 epochs (about 15 GPU minutes)
- For extrapolated Pb isotopes, the root-mean-square orthogonality deviation \(\mathcal{O}\) values: \(\mathcal{O}(^{212}\mathrm{Pb}) = 0.0021\), \(\mathcal{O}(^{222}\mathrm{Pb}) = 0.0022\), \(\mathcal{O}(^{232}\mathrm{Pb}) = 0.0036\)
- For the extrapolated nucleus \(^{232}\)Pb, the excess nucleon number \(\Delta = 0.64\) was superimposed on the \(2g_{7/2}\) state
- Charge radii calculated by SHF+BCS deviate from experiment by less than 0.01 fm for Pb and Sn isotopes
- The penalty factor for mass number deviation in Loss\(_2\) was \(\mu=0.05\)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous neural network approach (Yang 2022) that studied density and binding energy correlations using charge radius residuals, and also builds on earlier machine learning studies of nuclear masses (Niu 2018, Ma 2020), charge radii (Utama 2016, Wu 2020, Dong 2022), and the reconstruction of the Hohenberg-Kohn map in electronic systems (Moreno 2020). The KSN approach enables direct calibration of single-particle states with experimental data, opening the possibility to construct non-parametric density functionals and improve nuclear effective interactions through back-propagation of experimental binding energies.
