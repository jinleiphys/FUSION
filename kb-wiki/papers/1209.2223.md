# Conditions for equivalence of Statistical Ensembles in Nuclear Multifragmentation

> Statistical models based on canonical and grand canonical ensembles are extensively used to study intermediate energy heavy ion collisions. The underlying physical assumption behind canonical and grand canonical models is fundamentally different, and in principle agree only in the thermodynamical limit when the number of particles become infinite. Nevertheless, we show that these models are equivalent in the sense that they predict similar results if certain conditions are met even for finite nuclei. In particular, the results converge when nuclear multifragmentation leads to the formation of predominantly nucleons and low mass clusters. The conditions under which the equivalence holds are amenable to present day experiments.

# Conditions for equivalence of Statistical Ensembles in Nuclear Multifragmentation

## Key claim
Canonical and grand canonical statistical models of nuclear multifragmentation can yield equivalent results for finite nuclei, not only in the thermodynamic limit, provided the nucleus fragments predominantly into nucleons and low-mass clusters. This equivalence holds when the fraction of fragments larger than 80% of the source size is negligible, a condition achievable at experimentally accessible temperatures, freeze-out volumes, source sizes, and asymmetries.

## Method
The authors compared total charge distributions from canonical and grand canonical multifragmentation models for a range of source sizes (A0 = 60, 96, 144), temperatures (3.8, 5, 8 MeV), freeze-out volumes (3V0, 4V0, 5V0), and isospin asymmetries (y = 0, 0.17, 0.33). They computed the ratio η of high-mass fragments (A > 0.8 A0) to total multiplicity in the grand canonical model to quantify the conditions under which the two ensembles converge.

## Key numbers
- At T = 3.8 MeV for a source with A0 = 60, Z0 = 25 at Vf = 3V0, the difference between canonical and grand canonical charge distributions is maximum; at T = 8 MeV the results are "very close to each other."
- For the same source at T = 5 MeV, increasing freeze-out volume from Vf = 3V0 to Vf = 5V0 brings the two ensembles into agreement.
- For sources with A0 = 60 at T = 5 MeV and Vf = 3V0, the symmetric nucleus (y = 0) shows the least difference between ensembles, while the most asymmetric (y = 0.33) shows the maximum difference.
- For sources with fixed asymmetry y = 0.17 at T = 5 MeV and Vf = 3V0, increasing source size from A0 = 60 to A0 = 144 reduces the difference between the two ensembles.

## Context
This work extends the known result that canonical and grand canonical ensembles agree only in the thermodynamic limit (Reif, 1965) by demonstrating equivalence for finite nuclei under specific fragmentation conditions. It builds on prior statistical multifragmentation models by Das et al. (2005) and Chaudhuri and Das Gupta (2009), and shows that the convergence condition is the same as that required for microcanonical-canonical equivalence. The findings enable experimentalists to use the computationally simpler grand canonical model when analyzing data from intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions that produce predominantly light fragments, and identify the regimes where the two models diverge and must be treated separately.
