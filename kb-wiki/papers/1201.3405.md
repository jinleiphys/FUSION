# Relativistic Noise

> The relativistic theory of hydrodynamic fluctuations, or noise, is derived and applied to high energy heavy ion collisions. These fluctuations are inherent in any space-time varying system and are in addition to initial state fluctuations. We illustrate the effects with the boost-invariant Bjorken solution to the hydrodynamic equations. Long range correlations in rapidity are induced by propagation of sound modes. The magnitude of these correlations are directly proportional to the viscosities. These fluctuations should be enhanced near a phase transition or rapid crossover.

# Relativistic Noise

## Key claim

Hydrodynamic fluctuations in high-energy heavy ion collisions produce long-range correlations in rapidity whose magnitude is directly proportional to the shear and bulk viscosities; these fluctuations are inherent in any space-time varying system and are additional to initial state fluctuations, and should be enhanced near a phase transition or rapid crossover.

## Method

The authors derive a relativistic theory of hydrodynamic fluctuations by adding stochastic sources to the energy-momentum tensor and baryon current, with correlation functions determined by the fluctuation-dissipation theorem. They apply this theory to the boost-invariant Bjorken model, treating fluctuations in temperature and flow velocity as perturbations, and compute two-particle correlations in momentum-space rapidity after freeze-out.

## Key numbers

- The correlation function for dN/deta fluctuations is proportional to (eta/s)_0, the ratio of shear viscosity to entropy density, assumed temperature independent.
- Example calculation uses T_0 = 600 MeV, T_f = 150 MeV, tau_f = 10 fm, and N_eff = 47.5 effective bosonic degrees of freedom.
- The sound horizon appears at xi = 2 v_s ln(tau_f/tau), with v_s^2 = 1/3 for the equation of state P = epsilon/3.
- The correlation function contains a factor (T_0/T_f)^2 and a factor 1/(T_f tau_f) in equation (eq:dNdN/N).

## Context

This work extends the theory of hydrodynamic fluctuations from non-relativistic statistical physics (Landau and Lifshitz, Statistical Physics Part 2, Section 88) to the relativistic regime for application to heavy ion collisions. It builds on the boost-invariant Bjorken model and connects to experimental observations of the near-side ridge seen by STAR, PHOBOS, and CMS collaborations. The results enable extraction of transport coefficients from correlation measurements and motivate full 3+1 dimensional dissipative hydrodynamic simulations (Gale et al.) as well as studies of fluctuations near a possible QCD critical point.
