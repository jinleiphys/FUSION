# QCD in heavy ion collisions

> These lectures provide a modern introduction to selected topics in the physics of ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions which shed light on the fundamental theory of strong interactions, the Quantum Chromodynamics. The emphasis is on the partonic forms of QCD matter which exist in the early and intermediate stages of a collision -- the colour glass condensate, the glasma, and the quark-gluon plasma -- and on the effective theories that are used for their description. These theories provide qualitative and even quantitative insight into a wealth of remarkable phenomena observed in nucleus-nucleus or deuteron-nucleus collisions at RHIC and/or the LHC, like the suppression of particle production and of azimuthal correlations at forward rapidities, the energy and centrality dependence of the multiplicities, the ridge effect, the limiting fragmentation, the jet quenching, or the dijet asymmetry.

## Key claim

These lectures argue that the partonic stages of ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions, the colour glass condensate, glasma, and quark-gluon plasma, can be described by effective theories rooted in QCD, but the coupling strength varies across stages: the early stages are weakly coupled (α_s ∼ 0.3) and amenable to perturbative resummations, while the later stages may require strong-coupling descriptions (e.g., AdS/CFT) to explain phenomena like rapid thermalization and small viscosity, even though thermodynamic properties appear consistent with weak coupling.

## Method

The paper reviews the space-time picture of a heavy ion collision, identifying distinct stages (CGC, glasma, QGP, hadron gas, freeze-out) and the effective theories appropriate for each: the CGC effective theory for high-density gluon fields prior to and immediately after collision, kinetic theory for the transition to particles, hard thermal loop theory for weakly coupled QGP thermodynamics, and hydrodynamics with viscosity parameters for the expanding fluid. It also discusses hard probes (jets, photons) as tools to infer medium properties via medium-induced gluon radiation at weak coupling or AdS/CFT at strong coupling.

## Key numbers

- Lorentz boost factor γ ∼ 100 for nuclei at RHIC/LHC, giving longitudinal extent smaller by this factor than transverse extent.
- Typical gluon transverse momentum k⊥ ≃ 2 GeV for x = 10⁻⁴ in a Pb or Au nucleus.
- Average energy density in Pb+Pb collisions at LHC: ε ≳ 15 GeV/fm³, about 10 times nuclear matter density and 3 times that in Au+Au at RHIC.
- Critical temperature for deconfinement from lattice QCD: T_c ≃ 150–180 MeV.
- Freeze-out temperature from particle abundance ratios at RHIC: T_f ≃ 170 MeV in both Au+Au and p+p collisions.
- QCD coupling α_s(Q²) ≃ 0.1 at Q = 100 GeV; α_s(Q²) ≃ 0.4 at Q = 2 GeV.
- Thermalization time suggested by data: τ ∼ 1 fm/c; perturbative QCD predicts τ ≳ 10 fm/c.
- Maximal QGP temperature estimated from average energy density: T ∼ 500–600 MeV, with α_s ∼ 0.3–0.4 (g ∼ 1.5–2).
- Hard probe threshold: E⊥ ≳ 20 GeV at LHC for hard probes; very hard jets with E⊥ ≳ 100 GeV are strongly influenced by the medium.

## Context

These lectures extend the theoretical framework developed over the last decade for QCD matter under extreme conditions, building on the CGC formalism for high-energy nuclear wavefunctions and the AdS/CFT correspondence for strongly coupled plasmas. The work synthesizes results from RHIC and LHC experiments, such as elliptic flow, jet quenching, and dijet asymmetry, to argue that different stages of a heavy ion collision require different effective theories, and that the small viscosity-to-entropy ratio η/s inferred from elliptic flow data is inconsistent with weak-coupling kinetic theory but naturally emerges from strong-coupling calculations via AdS/CFT. This enables a unified but stage-dependent understanding of phenomena from the initial CGC through the QGP to freeze-out, while leaving open questions about the precise mechanism of rapid thermalization and the interplay between weak and strong coupling in the same system.
