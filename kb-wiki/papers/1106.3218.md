# Nuclear effects in atomic transitions

> Atomic electrons are sensitive to the properties of the nucleus they are bound to, such as nuclear mass, charge distribution, spin, magnetization distribution, or even excited level scheme. These nuclear parameters are reflected in the atomic transition energies. A very precise determination of atomic spectra may thus reveal information about the nucleus, otherwise hardly accessible via nuclear physics experiments. This work reviews theoretical and experimental aspects of the nuclear effects that can be identified in atomic structure data. An introduction to the theory of isotope shifts and hyperfine splitting of atomic spectra is given, together with an overview of the typical experimental techniques used in high-precision atomic spectroscopy. More exotic effects at the borderline between atomic and nuclear physics, such as parity violation in atomic transitions due to the weak interaction, or nuclear polarization and nuclear excitation by electron capture, are also addressed.

# Nuclear effects in atomic transitions

## Key claim

This review argues that high-precision atomic spectroscopy can extract nuclear properties (such as charge radii, spins, and multipole moments) that are otherwise difficult to access via nuclear physics experiments, but the reliability of this extraction depends critically on accurate theoretical calculations of mass shifts, field shifts, QED corrections, and nuclear polarization contributions, which often carry the largest theoretical error bars.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical formalism for isotope shifts (mass shift and volume/field shift) and hyperfine splitting in atomic spectra, covering both nonrelativistic and relativistic treatments including the nuclear recoil operator and QED framework. It then surveys five experimental methods used to determine nuclear charge radii: elastic electron scattering, muonic atom x-ray spectroscopy, optical laser spectroscopy, x-ray spectroscopy of highly charged ions, and dielectronic recombination measurements, with emphasis on how each method's precision and model dependencies affect the extraction of nuclear parameters such as the root-mean-square (RMS) charge radius.

## Key numbers

- The finite nuclear size leads to a shift of resonance energies on the order of **200 eV** for K-shell electrons in uranium (Z=92).
- The field shift between ²³⁵U and ²³⁸U for K-shell electrons is **less than 1 eV**.
- The finite-nuclear-size correction for H-like uranium ²³⁸U⁹¹⁺ is **198.39 eV** when including nuclear deformation and **198.61 eV** for a spherical nucleus with the same RMS radius, giving a **220 meV** difference between the two models.
- The nuclear size effects scale with nuclear charge as **Z⁵ up to Z⁶**.
- The empirical formula for RMS radius is ⟨r²⟩¹/² = (0.836 A¹/³ + 0.570) fm, with accuracy better than **0.05 fm** for nuclei with A > 9.
- Elastic electron scattering achieves typical accuracies of **0.2%** for RMS radii.
- The isotope shifts of ³⁶Ar versus ⁴⁰Ar were measured with **sub-ppm accuracy** for transitions in Ar¹³⁺ and Ar¹⁴⁺.
- The 1s–2s transition frequency in hydrogen has been measured with **10⁻¹⁴ accuracy** for determining the proton radius.

## Context

This review extends the long tradition of using atomic spectra to probe nuclear structure, dating back to the discovery of isotope shifts by Schüler and Keyston (1931) in thallium and mercury. It connects the established methods of electron scattering (pioneered by Hofstadter in the 1950s) and muonic atom spectroscopy (first providing accurate nuclear size measurements by Fitch and Rainwater in 1953) with newer techniques such as dielectronic recombination on highly charged ions. The work enables future extraction of nuclear charge radii for exotic, short-lived nuclei at facilities like RIKEN and GSI, and addresses open questions about the role of nuclear deformation in field shift calculations and the need for improved theoretical treatment of nuclear polarization contributions.
