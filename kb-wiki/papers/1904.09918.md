# Observability of sharp phase transitions in neutron stars

> With central densities as high as 5-10 times the nuclear saturation density, neutron stars exhibit extreme conditions that cannot be observed elsewhere. They are ideal astrophysical laboratories for probing the composition and properties of cold, ultra-dense matter. We shall discuss taking into account currently available data from observation, how to reveal possible sharp phase transitions in dense neutron star cores.

## Key claim

The paper argues that strong first-order phase transitions in neutron star cores can produce observable signatures in global static properties (mass, radius, tidal deformability, moment of inertia) and in r-mode damping, but that detecting exotic matter from such measurements is probably very difficult or even unfeasible unless the phase transitions are sufficiently strong to dramatically affect these quantities.

## Method

The author applies the constant-speed-of-sound (CSS) parameterization of a first-order phase transition to a high-density phase, specifying the critical pressure, the energy density discontinuity, and the stiffness of the high-density phase. For hadronic matter, the Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) and SFHo equations of state are used as illustrative examples. The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equations are solved numerically to obtain mass-radius relations and tidal deformabilities, and the dissipated power from phase-conversion dissipation (PCD) at sharp hadron-quark interfaces is computed analytically in the subthermal regime to estimate r-mode saturation amplitudes.

## Key numbers

- Central densities in neutron stars reach 5-10 times the nuclear saturation density.
- The maximum mass constraint from pulsar observations is M_max >= 2 M_sun; a recent measurement of PSR J0740+6620 gives M = 2.17_{-0.10}^{+0.11} M_sun at 1-sigma.
- If confirmed that R_{M=2.1 M_sun} <= 12 km or R_{M=2.0 M_sun} <= 11.5 km, then soft quark matter with c^2_QM <= 1/3 becomes incompatible with first-order transitions.
- For the CSS parameterization, the allowed parameter space requires c^2_QM >= 0.5 when the hadronic EoS is soft and c^2_QM >= 0.4 when the hadronic EoS is stiff.
- For a specific hybrid star example (hadronic EoS from Hebeler (2010), CSS parameters n_trans = 4.0 n_0, Delta_epsilon/epsilon_trans = 0.2, c^2_QM = 1), the r-mode saturation amplitude from PCD is of order 10^{-12} at rotation frequency f = 600 Hz and temperature T = 10^8 K.
- For the SFHo hadronic EoS, the minimum quark core size for effective PCD damping depends on transition density: for n_trans = 2.5 n_0, hybrid stars >= 1 M_sun with R_b/R >= 0.3 can have effective damping; for n_trans = 4.0 n_0, only massive stars ~ 2 M_sun with R_b/R >= 0.5 qualify.
- The maximum-mass neutron star based on SFHo alone has central density n_max ~ 7.25 n_0.
- The deviation in the universal relation between dimensionless moment of inertia and tidal deformability can be as large as ~9% with sequential first-order phase transitions.

## Context

This work extends the CSS parameterization approach of Alford (2013) and Alford (2015) for first-order phase transitions in neutron stars, and builds on the phase-conversion dissipation mechanism introduced by Alford (2014). It addresses the tension between the existence of two-solar-mass pulsars (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013) and the small tidal deformability inferred from GW170817 (LIGO 2017), showing that strong sharp phase transitions can reconcile these constraints. The paper enables future work on distinguishing between hadronic, crossover, and sharp phase-transition scenarios using multi-messenger observations including gravitational-wave detections, pulsar timing measurements of moment of inertia, and X-ray radius measurements.
