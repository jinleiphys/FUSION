# Orbital M1 versus E2 strength in deformed nuclei: A new energy weighted sum rule

> Within the unified model of Bohr and Mottelson we derive the following linear energy weighted sum rule for low energy orbital 1$^+$ excitations in even-even deformed nuclei $$S_{\rm LE}^{\rm lew} (M_1^{\rm orb}) \cong (6/5) ε(B(E2; 0^+_1 \rightarrow 2_1^+ K=0)/Z e^2<r^2>^2) μ^2_N$$ with B(E2) the E2 strength for the transition from the ground state to the first excited state in the ground state rotational band, $<r^2>$ the charge r.m.s. radius squared and $ε$ the binding energy per nucleon in the nuclear ground state. It is shown that this energy weighted sum rule is in good agreement with available experimental data. The sum rule is derived using a simple ansatz for the intrinsic ground state wave function that predicts also high energy 1$^+$ strength at 2$\hbar ω$ carrying 50\% of the total $m_1$ moment of the orbital M1 operator.

# Orbital M1 versus E2 strength in deformed nuclei: A new energy weighted sum rule

## Key claim

Within the unified model of Bohr and Mottelson, the authors derive a linear energy weighted sum rule for low-energy orbital 1⁺ excitations in even-even deformed nuclei that relates the M1 strength to the B(E2) strength of the ground-state rotational band, the charge r.m.s. radius, and the binding energy per nucleon. The derivation uses a simple ansatz for the intrinsic ground state wave function that also predicts high-energy 1⁺ strength at 2ħω carrying 50% of the total m₁ moment of the orbital M1 operator, meaning the sum rule applies only to the low-energy half of the total strength.

## Method

The authors evaluate the double commutator of the orbital magnetic dipole operator with the Hamiltonian using two complementary approaches: first with a quadrupole-quadrupole two-body interaction, and second with a deformed one-body field (anisotropic harmonic oscillator) within the Bohr-Mottelson unified model. The self-consistency condition between the oscillator frequencies and the nuclear shape is used to replace harmonic oscillator parameters with experimentally known quantities: B(E2) values, charge r.m.s. radii, binding energies per nucleon, and the collective gyromagnetic ratio g_R. The final expression for the low-energy part of the sum rule is obtained by taking half of the total sum rule value, as the model predicts equal contributions from low-energy (ΔN=0) and high-energy (ΔN=2) particle-hole excitations.

## Key numbers

- The derived low-energy linear energy weighted sum rule is: S^{lew}_{LE}(M1^{orb}) ≅ (6/5) ε (B(E2; 0⁺₁ → 2⁺₁ K=0) / (Z e² ⟨r²⟩²)) μ²_N, where ε is the binding energy per nucleon.
- For ¹⁵⁰Nd: experimental sum ∑_x E_x B(0⁺→1⁺_x) = 5.95 μ²_N MeV (E_x ≤ 4 MeV), theoretical S^{lew}_{LE} = 6.439 μ²_N MeV.
- For ¹⁵²Sm: experimental sum = 7.003 μ²_N MeV, theoretical = 7.310 μ²_N MeV.
- For ¹⁵⁴Sm: experimental sum = 8.189 μ²_N MeV, theoretical = 8.968 μ²_N MeV.
- For ¹⁶⁴Dy: experimental sum = 9.678 μ²_N MeV, theoretical = 10.462 μ²_N MeV.
- The high-energy 1⁺ strength at 2ħω is predicted to carry 50% of the total m₁ moment of the orbital M1 operator.
- The factor G_{πν} = g_R(1-g_R)2A/Z is approximately 1 for most deformed rare-earth nuclei, oscillating between 0.9 and 1.2.

## Context

This work extends earlier sum rule approaches by Heyde and De Coester (1991) using the interacting boson model (IBM-2) and by Zamick and Zheng (1991) using a quadrupole-quadrupole interaction, but introduces the mean field and separates low-energy (ΔN=0) from high-energy (ΔN=2) contributions for the first time. The results are consistent with microscopic deformed HF+BCS calculations by Garrido, Moya de Guerra, Sarriguren and Udias (1991) which found that summed orbital 1⁺ strength in Sm and Nd isotopes is proportional to β² and accounts for about 50% of the total strength. The paper enables experimental tests of whether the high-energy orbital M1 strength is damped or concentrated by residual interactions, a question raised by the differing predictions of QRPA calculations by Hamamoto and Magnusson (1991) versus Zawischa and Speth (1991).
