# Nuclear History, Politics, and Futures from (A)toms-to(Z)oom: Design and Deployment of a Remote-Learning Special-Topics Course For Nuclear Engineering Education

> To address the lack of familiarity with nuclear history common among nuclear engineers and physicists, we outline the design and deployment of a special-topics course entitled "NE290: Nuclear History, Politics, and Futures" across which we contextualize the importance of the field at its inception, in current affairs, and in future endeavors. We begin by outlining the gaps in secondary educational offerings for nuclear history and their importance in consideration with nontechnical engineering guidelines. We then outline a number ABET specifications as pedagogical goals for NE290 from which we derive a list of target student learning objectives. Next, we outline the NE290 syllabus in terms of assignments and an overview of course content in the form of a class timeline. We provide an extensive description of the materials and teaching methodologies for the four units of NE290: 20th-Century Physics, Physics in WWII, the Early Cold War, and the Late Cold War and Modern Era. We detail the sequence of lectures across the course and historical timelines -- leading up to a showcasing of NE290 final projects which mirror in creativity the novelty of course offering. Because NE290 was first offered during Spring 2021 during the COVID-19 pandemic, additional measures in the form of new tools were used to augment the mandate of remote learning such as videoconferencing technology to recruit geographically diverse guest lecturers, and used the MIRO tool for virtual whiteboarding. Lastly, we provide an accounting of course outcomes drawn from student feedback which -- in tandem with the complete distribution of course material -- facilitates the integration of nuclear history into the curriculum for the wider nuclear engineering and physics communities.

# Nuclear History, Politics, and Futures from (A)toms-to(Z)oom: Design and Deployment of a Remote-Learning Special-Topics Course For Nuclear Engineering Education

## Key claim

The paper argues that a dedicated special-topics course on nuclear history can address the lack of familiarity with nuclear history common among nuclear engineers and physicists, and that such a course can meet and exceed nontechnical ABET student outcomes, but only if the course is designed with specific learning objectives based on Bloom's Taxonomy and delivered with interactive, remote-friendly tools.

## Method

The authors designed and deployed a graduate-level special-topics course (NE290) at UC Berkeley during Spring 2021, organized into four historical units: 20th-Century Physics, Physics in WWII, the Early Cold War, and the Late Cold War and Modern Era. The course used weekly reading responses (30% of grade), class participation (20%), and a term paper (50%) as assessments, and leveraged videoconferencing to recruit geographically diverse guest lecturers and the MIRO tool for virtual collaborative timeline whiteboarding. The authors provide a complete accounting of course materials, syllabus, lecture slides, and student outcomes to facilitate replication by other institutions.

## Key numbers

- Weekly reading responses accounted for 30% of the course grade, class participation 20%, and the term paper 50%.
- The course was first offered during Spring 2021, during the COVID-19 pandemic.
- Guest lectures were delivered by Dr. Tim Koeth (University of Maryland), Dr. Carl Willis (University of New Mexico), Dr. Alex Wellerstein, Dr. Miriam Hiebert, and Marty Pfeiffer.
- Students were assigned weekly responses of approximately 1 page to reading materials and class lectures.
- The class divided itself in approximately equal measure between the two final project options (Historical Answers to Modern Problems and Nuclear Bedtime Story).

## Context

This work extends the pedagogical approach of using historical narrative in nuclear engineering education, addressing a gap in ABET accreditation criteria (2020-2021) which require technical competencies in mathematics, nuclear physics, radiation transport, nuclear systems, fuel cycles, radiation detection, and system design, but include no requirement for understanding nuclear history. The course design builds on prior educational frameworks including Bloom's Taxonomy (Bloom, 1984) and ABET's Code of Ethics, and enables future integration of nuclear history into standard nuclear engineering curricula by providing complete, openly distributed course materials. The paper enables other institutions to replicate the course structure and adapt the remote-learning tools (MIRO, videoconferencing) for in-person or hybrid offerings.
