# Projection Operator Formalisms and the Nuclear Shell Model

> The shell model solve the nuclear many-body problem in a restricted model space and takes into account the restricted nature of the space by using effective interactions and operators. In this paper two different methods for generating the effective interactions are considered. One is based on a partial solution of the Schrodinger equation (Bloch-Horowitz or the Feshbach projection formalism) and other on linear algebra (Lee-Suzuki). The two methods are derived in a parallel manner so that the difference and similarities become apparent. The connections with the renormalization group are also pointed out.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the essential difference between the Bloch-Horowitz (Feshbach) and Lee-Suzuki methods for generating effective interactions in the nuclear shell model is how the operator \(\omega\) is determined: the Bloch-Horowitz method uses a partial solution of the Schrödinger equation (making it energy-dependent and state-specific), while the Lee-Suzuki method uses a matrix inversion (making it energy-independent but limited to a set of states with the same dimension as the model space).

## Method

The paper derives both the Bloch-Horowitz and Lee-Suzuki methods in parallel, starting from a common definition of an operator \(\omega\) that maps model-space (\(P\)-space) components of a wave function onto the complementary (\(Q\)-space) components. For Bloch-Horowitz, \(\omega\) is obtained by solving the Schrödinger equation for a single eigenstate, yielding an energy-dependent expression \(\omega = \frac{1}{E_\psi - QHQ - i\epsilon} QHP\). For Lee-Suzuki, \(\omega\) is determined by requiring that a chosen set of orthonormal states \(|\alpha_P\rangle\) spanning the \(P\) space can be expanded in terms of the \(P\)-space projections of full-space eigenstates \(|k\rangle\), which requires inverting the matrix \(\langle \alpha'_P | k \rangle\). The paper then shows how both methods lead to effective interactions and operators, and connects them to renormalization group approaches.

## Key numbers

- The Bloch-Horowitz effective interaction is given by \(PHP + PHQ \frac{1}{E_\psi - QHQ - i\epsilon} QHP\), which reproduces the fully off-shell t-matrix.
- The Lee-Suzuki method requires the inverse of \(\langle \alpha'_P | k \rangle\) to exist, implying the number of states in the \(P\) space equals the number of states in the chosen set \(|k\rangle\).
- The Lee-Suzuki method can work for no more states than the number of states in the \(P\) space, in contrast to the Bloch-Horowitz method which can describe any state with non-zero overlap with the model space.
- The unitary transformation for Lee-Suzuki is given by \(U = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+P\omega^\dagger Q \omega P}} (P+ P\omega^\dagger Q)\), which maps states from the \(|k\rangle\) space into the \(P\) space.

## Context

This paper extends the formal comparison of projection operator methods in nuclear shell-model theory, building on the Bloch-Horowitz formalism (Bloch and Horowitz, 1958) and the Feshbach projection operator formalism (Feshbach, 1958), as well as the Lee-Suzuki method (Lee and Suzuki, 1980) used in no-core shell model calculations. The work clarifies the relationship between these methods and renormalization group approaches, specifically connecting the Bloch-Horowitz method to the work of Birse et al. (2001) on the renormalization group treatment of two-body scattering, and the Lee-Suzuki method to the derivation of the \(V_{\text{low } k}\) effective interaction (Bogner et al., 2003; Schwenk et al., 2005). The paper enables practitioners to choose between energy-dependent or energy-independent effective interactions depending on the application, and clarifies the mathematical conditions under which each method is valid.
