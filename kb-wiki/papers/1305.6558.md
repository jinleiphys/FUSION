# Dilepton creation based on an analytic hydrodynamic solution

> High-energy collisions of various nuclei, so called ``Little Bangs'' are observed at various experiments of heavy ion colliders. The time evolution of the strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma created in heavy ion collisions can be described by hydrodynamical models. After expansion and cooling, the hadrons are created in a freeze-out. Their distribution describes the final state of this medium. To investigate the time evolution one needs to analyze penetrating probes, such as direct photon or dilepton observables, as these particles are created throughout the evolution of the medium. In this paper we analyze an 1+3 dimensional analytic solution of relativistic hydrodynamics, and we calculate dilepton transverse momentum and invariant mass distributions. We investigate the dependence of dilepton production on time evolution parameters, such as emission duration and equation of state. Using parameters from earlier fits of this model to photon and hadron spectra, we compare our calculations to measurements as well. The most important feature of this work is that dilepton observables are calculated from an exact, analytic, 1+3D solution of relativistic hydrodynamics that is also compatible with hadronic and direct photon observables.

## Key claim

Dilepton observables are calculated from an exact, analytic, 1+3D solution of relativistic hydrodynamics that is also compatible with hadronic and direct photon observables, but the calculation assumes a constant speed of sound (constant equation of state parameter κ) and uses vacuum spectral functions for vector mesons rather than in-medium values.

## Method

The authors start from the 1+3D ellipsoidally symmetric analytic hydrodynamic solution of Csörgő et al. (2003) and compute the dilepton production rate by integrating the Jüttner-distributed phase-space density of incoming particles (quarks or pions) over space-time, using a second-order saddlepoint approximation. The dilepton source is evaluated separately for quark-antiquark annihilation in the quark-gluon plasma phase and for pion annihilation through vector mesons (ρ, ρ', ρ'', ω, φ) in the hadron gas phase, with the cross-sections taken from Kajantie et al. (1986) and Asakawa et al. (1993). The resulting analytic formula for the dilepton invariant mass and transverse momentum distribution depends on the equation of state parameter κ = 1/c_s² and the time integration limits (initial and final temperatures).

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out proper time τ₀ = 7.7 fm/c from fits to RHIC Au+Au data at √s_NN = 200 GeV
- Central freeze-out temperature T_fo = 204 MeV for RHIC Au+Au (from hadron fits)
- Central freeze-out temperature T_fo = 140 MeV for SPS In+In at 158 AGeV
- Equation of state parameter κ = 7.7 (corresponding to c_s = 0.36 ± 0.02) from direct photon fits
- Initial QGP temperature T_ini = 270 MeV and final hadron gas temperature T_final = 170 MeV for RHIC (corresponding to ξ = 0.49-1.0 for QGP and ξ = 1.0-1.6 for hadron phase)
- Initial QGP temperature T_ini = 200 MeV and final hadron gas temperature T_final = 130 MeV for SPS (corresponding to ξ = 0.4-1.0 for QGP and ξ = 1.0-1.2 for hadron phase)
- Normalization factors for ρ' and ρ'' mesons: 8.02 × 10⁻³ and 5.93 × 10⁻³ respectively

## Context

This work extends the analytic hydrodynamic approach of Csörgő et al. (2003) by calculating dilepton observables, completing the set of penetrating probes (hadrons, direct photons, and now dileptons) from the same exact 1+3D solution. It builds on previous calculations of hadronic observables by Csanád et al. (2009) and direct photon observables by Csanád et al. (2011) from the same model, which were successfully compared to RHIC data. The results enable extraction of the equation of state and emission duration from dilepton data, complementing numerical hydrodynamic simulations by Kajantie et al. (1986), Asakawa et al. (1993), and others, while maintaining analytic understanding of parameter dependencies. The comparison to PHENIX and NA60 data shows that at RHIC quark annihilation dominates, while at SPS pion annihilation through ρ mesons is also important, though the simple vacuum spectral functions used here cannot fully describe the data above 1 GeV/c².
