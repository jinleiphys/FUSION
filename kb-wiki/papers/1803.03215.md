# Deep Learning: A Tool for Computational Nuclear Physics

> In recent years, several successful applications of the Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) have emerged in nuclear physics and high-energy physics, as well as in biology, chemistry, meteorology, and other fields of science. A major goal of nuclear theory is to predict nuclear structure and nuclear reactions from the underlying theory of the strong interactions, Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD). With access to powerful High Performance Computing (HPC) systems, several ab initio approaches, such as the No-Core Shell Model (NCSM), have been developed to calculate the properties of atomic nuclei. However, to accurately solve for the properties of atomic nuclei, one faces immense theoretical and computational challenges. The present study proposes a feed-forward ANN method for predicting the properties of atomic nuclei like ground state energy and ground state point proton root-mean-square (rms) radius based on NCSM results in computationally accessible basis spaces. The designed ANNs are sufficient to produce results for these two very different observables in 6Li from the ab initio NCSM results in small basis spaces that satisfy the theoretical physics condition: independence of basis space parameters in the limit of extremely large matrices. We also provide comparisons of the results from ANNs with established methods of estimating the results in the infinite matrix limit.

## Key claim

The authors propose a feed-forward artificial neural network (ANN) method to predict ground state energy and ground state point proton root-mean-square (rms) radius for the nucleus 6Li, using only No-Core Shell Model (NCSM) results from computationally accessible small basis spaces (up to Nmax = 10), under the condition that the ANN outputs satisfy independence of basis space parameters in the limit of extremely large matrices.

## Method

The authors trained feed-forward ANNs on ab initio NCSM results for 6Li obtained with the Daejeon16 nucleon-nucleon interaction using the MFDn code, using data from basis spaces up through Nmax = 10 (Hamiltonian matrix dimension about 9.7 million). The ANNs took the two basis space parameters, harmonic oscillator energy hbar-Omega and basis truncation parameter Nmax, as inputs and were trained to predict ground state energy and ground state point proton rms radius. The trained ANNs were then used to predict these observables at larger basis spaces up to Nmax = 18 (matrix dimension about 2.8 billion), and the results were compared with established extrapolation methods for estimating the infinite matrix limit.

## Key numbers

* Ground state energy and ground state point proton rms radius were predicted for 6Li using NCSM data up through Nmax = 10, where the Hamiltonian matrix dimension is about 9.7 million.
* The ANN predictions were compared with NCSM results available up through Nmax = 18, where the Hamiltonian matrix dimension reaches about 2.8 billion.
* The ANN method was applied to two different observables: ground state energy and ground state point proton rms radius.

## Context

This work extends prior ab initio NCSM calculations for light nuclei using the Daejeon16 interaction (Shirokov 2016), which showed improved convergence over the JISP16 interaction (Shirokov 2007). The ANN approach addresses the computational challenge that NCSM calculations become prohibitively expensive as basis space increases, building on prior applications of ANNs in nuclear physics for tasks such as constructing nuclear charge radii models (Akkoyun 2013), determining proton separation energies (Athanassopoulos 2004), and developing nuclear mass systematics (Athanassopoulos 2004). The method enables predictions for heavier nuclei where direct NCSM calculations at large basis spaces are computationally infeasible.
