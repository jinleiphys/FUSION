# From moments of the distribution function to hydrodynamics: The non-conformal case

> We study the one-dimensional boost-invariant Boltzmann equation in the relaxation-time approximation using special moments of the distribution function for a system with a finite particle mass. The infinite hierarchy of moments can be truncated by keeping only the three lowest moments that correspond to the three independent components of the energy-momentum tensor. We show that such a three-moment truncation reproduces accurately the exact solution of the kinetic equation after a simple renormalization that takes into account the effects of the neglected higher moments. We derive second-order Israel-Stewart hydrodynamic equations from the three-moment equations, and show that, for most physically relevant initial conditions, these equations yield results comparable to those of the three-moment truncation, albeit less accurate. We attribute this feature to the fact that the structure of Israel-Stewart equations is similar to that of the three-moment truncation. In particular, the presence of the relaxation term in the Israel-Stewart equations, yields an early-time regime that mimics approximately the collisionless regime. A detailed comparison of the three-moment truncation with second-order non-conformal hydrodynamics reveals ambiguities in the definition of second-order transport coefficients. These ambiguities affect the ability of Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics to reproduce results of kinetic theory.

## Key claim

The three-moment truncation of the Boltzmann equation (keeping only the moments corresponding to the three independent components of the energy-momentum tensor) reproduces the exact kinetic-theory attractor for massive particles after a simple renormalization that accounts for neglected higher moments, but the resulting Israel-Stewart hydrodynamic equations contain ambiguities in second-order transport coefficients that limit their ability to match kinetic theory.

## Method

The authors studied the one-dimensional boost-invariant (Bjorken) Boltzmann equation in the relaxation-time approximation for a gas of massive particles. They introduced two infinite hierarchies of special moments of the distribution function: the L-moments (which capture the energy density and longitudinal pressure) and the M-moments (which capture the trace of the energy-momentum tensor). They truncated these hierarchies to the three lowest moments (L0, L1, M0) that correspond to the independent components of the energy-momentum tensor, and compared the resulting evolution equations to both the exact kinetic solution and to second-order Israel-Stewart hydrodynamic equations derived via a Chapman-Enskog expansion.

## Key numbers

- The coefficients for the L-moment equations are a0 = 4/3, c0 = 2/3, a1 = 38/21, b1 = 8/15, c1 = -12/35; for the M-moment equations, a0' = 2/3 and c0' = -2/3.
- In the collisionless regime, the energy density scales as epsilon(tau) proportional to 1/tau at late times (tau >> tau_in) and as 1/tau^2 at early times (tau << tau_in).
- The eigenvalues of the two-moment truncation for the L-moments are lambda0 approximately 0.93 and lambda1 approximately 2.21, converging to exact values lambda0 = 1 and lambda1 = 2 as truncation level increases.
- The real eigenvalue for the M-moment equations converges to mu0 = 1, but at truncation n=10 the value is only approximately 0.7.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Blaizot and collaborators (2017-2021) from massless to massive particles, where the non-vanishing trace of the energy-momentum tensor introduces an additional degree of freedom (the bulk pressure). The paper confirms conclusions of Chattopadhyay (2021) and Jaiswal (2021) regarding an early-time attractor in the longitudinal pressure, but differs from Chen (2021) and Kamata (2022) in that the essential fixed points are associated with distinct physical regimes (collisionless and hydrodynamic) rather than being sought within second-order hydrodynamic flows. The results enable systematic comparison between kinetic theory and hydrodynamic descriptions of heavy-ion collisions with finite particle mass.
