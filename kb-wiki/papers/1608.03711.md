# Coordinate-Space Solver for Superfluid Many-Fermion Systems with Shifted Conjugate Orthogonal Conjugate Gradient Method

> Self-consistent approaches to superfluid many-fermion systems in 3-dimensions (and subsequent time-dependent approaches) require a large number of diagonalizations of very large dimension hermitian matrices, which results in enormous computational costs. We present an approach based on the shifted conjugate-orthogonal conjugate-gradient (COCG) method for the evaluation of the Green's function, from which we subsequently extract various densities (particle number, spin, current, kinetic energy, etc.) of a nuclear system needed in self-consistent approaches. The approach eschews the construction of the quasiparticle wavefunctions and their corresponding quasiparticle energies, which are never explicitly needed in any density functional approaches. As benchmarks we present calculations for nuclei with axial symmetry, including the ground state of spherical (magic or semi-magic) and axially deformed nuclei, the saddle-point in the $^{240}$Pu constrained fission path, and a vortex in the neutron star crust.

## Key claim

The shifted conjugate-orthogonal conjugate-gradient (COCG) method can extract all local densities needed for self-consistent superfluid nuclear density functional theory calculations directly from the Green's function, avoiding explicit diagonalization of the large HFB matrix and the construction of quasiparticle wavefunctions and energies, provided the contour integral encloses only the eigenvalues in the interval [0, E_cut] on the real axis.

## Method

The authors solve the HFB equation in three-dimensional coordinate space by computing the Green's function via the shifted COCG Krylov method for a set of complex energy shifts on a contour integral path. From the Green's function, they extract normal density, anomalous density, kinetic energy density, and spin-current density using contour integrals and integration by parts to avoid direct computation of derivatives. The method requires only matrix-vector multiplications with the sparse HFB matrix, implemented efficiently using fast Fourier transforms, and treats each spatial point independently, enabling near-perfect parallel scaling.

## Key numbers

- HFB matrix dimension for a medium-size volume of 32×32×48 fm³ with lattice resolution dx=1 fm (p_cut ≈ 600 MeV/c) is N² = (4×32×32×48)² ≈ 200,000² matrix elements, requiring more than 0.5 TB memory to store
- A typical direct diagonalization of such a matrix takes about 40 minutes using ScaLAPACK on 36,864 processor cores at NERSC Edison, costing 49,152 CPU hours per diagonalization
- Self-consistent convergence requires approximately 100 diagonalizations, generating about 10 million CPU hours per converged calculation
- The saturation density used in the pairing strength parametrization is ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³

## Context

This work extends the shifted COCG method previously applied to electronic systems (Yamamoto 2008, Takayama 2006) and nuclear shell-model calculations (Mizusawa 2010, Shimizu 2016) to the full three-dimensional coordinate-space HFB problem with pairing and spin-orbit coupling. It replaces the direct parallel diagonalization procedure used in earlier work by Stetcu (2011, 2015) and Bulgac (2016), offering a method that exhibits near-perfect strong scaling and avoids the O(N³) complexity and memory requirements of traditional diagonalization approaches. The method enables self-consistent calculations for large deformed systems, fission paths, and neutron star crust vortices that were previously computationally prohibitive.
