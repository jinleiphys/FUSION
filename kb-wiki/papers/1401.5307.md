# Nuclear forces and ab initio calculations of atomic nuclei

> Nuclear forces and the nuclear many-body problem have been some of Gerry Brown's main topics in his so productive life as a theoretical physicist. In this talk, I outline how Gerry's work laid the foundations of the modern theory of nuclear forces and ab initio calculations of atomic nuclei. I also present some recent developments obtained in the framework of nuclear lattice simulations.

## Key claim

The talk outlines how Gerald E. Brown's work laid the foundations for the modern theory of nuclear forces and ab initio calculations of atomic nuclei, and presents recent developments in nuclear lattice simulations; the abstract glosses over the fact that the talk is a personal recollection that cannot be exhaustive and leaves topics like bag models, Skyrmions, and the V_low-k approach to other speakers.

## Method

The paper reviews the historical development of nuclear force models based on chiral symmetry, three- and four-body forces, and two-pion exchange from pion-nucleon scattering, as pioneered by Brown and collaborators. It then describes the chiral effective field theory (EFT) approach to nuclear forces, including the quark mass dependence of nuclear forces using K-factors. Finally, it presents results from nuclear lattice simulations (NLEFT) on a discretized Euclidean space-time torus, using auxiliary field Monte Carlo methods to compute ground-state properties and excited states of nuclei up to A=28.

- Limit on quark mass variations from Big Bang nucleosynthesis: delta m_q / m_q = (2 +/- 4)% (in the isospin limit)
- Limit on Higgs VEV variation: |delta v/v| = |delta m_q/m_q| <= 0.9%
- Ground-state energy of 16O at NNLO+3N+4N_eff: -131.3(5) MeV (experimental: -127.62 MeV)
- First excited 0+ state of 16O at NNLO+3N+4N_eff: -123(2) MeV (experimental: -121.57 MeV)
- First 2+ state of 16O at NNLO+3N+4N_eff: -123(2) MeV (experimental: -120.70 MeV)
- Shifts in fundamental parameters at the (2-3)% level are unlikely to be detrimental to the development of life (based on triple-alpha process calculations)

## Context

This talk extends the foundational work of Gerald E. Brown on nuclear forces, including his chiral symmetry studies (Brown 1985), three-nucleon forces, and the Stony Brook potential (Jackson 1975). It builds on Weinberg's (1990) chiral EFT power counting for multi-nucleon systems and presents nuclear lattice simulations that enable ab initio calculations of medium-mass nuclei, including the first such calculation of the Hoyle state in 12C (Epelbaum 2011). The work enables future studies of nuclear reactions on the lattice, the equation of state of neutron matter, and further exploration of the quark mass dependence of nuclear binding.
