# Lattice effective field theory for nuclei from A = 4 to A = 28

> We present an overview of the extension of Nuclear Lattice Effective Field Theory simulations to the regime of medium-mass nuclei. We focus on the determination of the ground-state energies of the alpha nuclei $^{16}$O, $^{20}$Ne, $^{24}$Mg and $^{28}$Si by means of Euclidean time projection.

## Key claim

Nuclear Lattice Effective Field Theory (NLEFT) simulations can be extended to medium-mass alpha nuclei up to A = 28, but the NNLO results show increasing over-binding for A >= 16 which is rectified by introducing a non-local 4N contact interaction tuned to the empirical binding energy of 24Mg, rather than by including higher-order chiral EFT corrections.

## Method

The authors performed Auxiliary-Field Quantum Monte Carlo lattice simulations with a spatial lattice spacing of 1.97 fm in a periodic cube of length 11.8 fm. They used Euclidean time projection starting from Slater-determinant trial states composed of delocalized standing waves, first projecting with an SU(4)-symmetric Hamiltonian and then evolving with the full leading-order Hamiltonian. NLO and NNLO contributions, including two-nucleon forces, three-nucleon forces, and electromagnetic/isospin-breaking corrections, were treated perturbatively using correlated fits of spectral representations with up to 6 trial states.

## Key numbers

- 16O: LO energy = -147.3(5) MeV; NNLO+4N energy = -131.3(5) MeV; empirical binding energy = -127.62 MeV
- 20Ne: LO energy = -199.7(9) MeV; NNLO+4N energy = -165.9(9) MeV; empirical binding energy = -160.64 MeV
- 24Mg: LO energy = -253(2) MeV; NNLO+4N energy = -198(2) MeV; empirical binding energy = -198.26 MeV
- 28Si: LO energy = -330(3) MeV; NNLO+4N energy = -233(3) MeV; empirical binding energy = -236.54 MeV
- Spatial lattice spacing a = 1.97 fm; periodic cube length L = 11.8 fm

## Context

This work extends NLEFT simulations from light nuclei (A <= 12, previously studied by Epelbaum (2009, 2011, 2012) including the Hoyle state) into the medium-mass regime up to A = 28. The approach differs from other ab initio methods such as those of Hagen (2012), Jurgenson (2013), Roth (2011), Hergert (2012), Lovato (2013), and Duguet in that it is an unconstrained Monte Carlo calculation without truncated basis expansions or many-body perturbation theory. The results enable future NLEFT simulations with smaller lattice spacing and inclusion of N3LO corrections to address the remaining over-binding issue for A >= 16.
