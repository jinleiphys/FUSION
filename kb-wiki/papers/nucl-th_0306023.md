# "Modified" BCS theory at finite temperature and its flaws

> Drawbacks of a recently suggested modified BCS theory at finite temperature in [Phys. Rev. C {\bf 67}, 014304 (2003)] are discussed. We conclude that the thermal behavior of the pairing correlations is not under control within this model.

## Key claim

The modified BCS (MBCS) theory at finite temperature proposed in Phys. Rev. C 67, 014304 (2003) is not a valid extension of BCS theory, because its gap equation lacks a trivial solution corresponding to the breaking of Cooper pairs, and the thermal behavior of pairing correlations is not under control within this model.

## Method

The authors analyze the MBCS equations, which apply a temperature-dependent unitary transformation to Bogoliubov quasiparticles using thermal Fermi-Dirac occupation numbers \(n_j\), producing new bar quasiparticles with coefficients \(\bar{u}_j\) and \(\bar{v}_j\). They analytically examine the MBCS gap equation \(\bar{\Delta}(T) = G \sum_j \Omega_j \bar{u}_j \bar{v}_j\) and numerically compute the pairing gap, \(\bar{v}_j\) coefficients, and particle-number fluctuations for the nucleus \(^{76}\)Ni, comparing results to conventional BCS theory.

## Key numbers

- The conventional BCS critical temperature is \(T_c \approx 0.57 \cdot \Delta_{T=0}\) for particle levels.
- The MBCS pairing gap reaches zero at \(\bar{T}_c \approx 2.1\) MeV in \(^{76}\)Ni, but then continues to decrease and becomes negative.
- Above \(\bar{T}_c\), the \(\bar{v}_j\) coefficients for particle levels (2d\(_{5/2}\), 3s\(_{1/2}\)) near the Fermi surface become negative.

## Context

This work critiques the MBCS approach by Dang and collaborators (2003), which claimed to wash out the sharp superfluid-normal phase transition and suppress particle-number fluctuations at high temperature. The authors show that the MBCS gap equation cannot produce a zero gap that remains zero at higher temperatures, making the claimed superfluid-normal phase transition meaningless. The analysis enables future work to avoid these inconsistencies when extending BCS theory to finite temperature.
