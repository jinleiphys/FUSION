# Is the Equation of State of strongly interacting matter observable ?

> I review the available empirical information on the equation of state of cold strongly interacting matter, as well as the prospects for obtaining new insights from the experimental study of gravitational waves emitted by neutron stars.

## Key claim
The equation of state (EOS) of cold strongly interacting matter is not directly observable, but can be constrained by combining nuclear data, neutron star mass measurements, and future gravitational wave observations; the abstract's claim that empirical information is "available" glosses over the fact that current constraints are weak, with all reasonable models supporting a 1.4 solar mass star, and only a confirmed mass above 1.8 solar masses would rule out soft EOS models.

## Method
The paper reviews constraints on the zero-temperature EOS from nuclear binding energies and the semiempirical mass formula, which yield the equilibrium density and compressibility of symmetric nuclear matter. It then describes how neutron star masses, obtained from radio pulsar timing and X-ray binary analysis, are used with the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equations to test EOS models. Finally, it discusses how the frequencies of nonradial oscillation modes (w-modes and g-modes) of neutron stars, which depend on the EOS, could be extracted from gravitational wave signals to provide additional constraints.

## Key numbers
- Equilibrium density of symmetric nuclear matter: n0 ~ 0.16 fm^-3
- Binding energy per nucleon at equilibrium: e0 = -16 MeV/A
- Compressibility modulus K ranges from ~200 MeV (soft EOS) to ~300 MeV (stiff EOS)
- Canonical neutron star mass from radio pulsar timing: M = 1.35 ± 0.04 solar masses
- Mass of Vela X-1 pulsar: M = 1.86 ± 0.33 solar masses
- For a bag constant B = 200 MeV/fm^3, the nucleon-to-quark matter transition region is 5.4 < n/n0 < 9.8, with a maximum mass reduction ΔM_max ~ 10%
- For B = 122 MeV/fm^3, the transition region is 4.9 < n/n0 < 7.5, with ΔM_max ~ 20%
- Typical neutron star mass ~1.4 solar masses, radius ~10 km
- Typical neutron star temperature <~ 10^9 K (~100 keV)

## Context
This review extends earlier work on nuclear matter EOS constraints from nuclear systematics and neutron star observations, particularly referencing the compressibility measurements of nuclear vibrational states and the mass determinations from radio pulsars and X-ray binaries. It builds on theoretical models of neutron star matter using nonrelativistic many-body theory (Pandharipande and Ravenhall) and relativistic mean-field approaches (Glendenning), as well as the MIT bag model for quark matter. The paper enables future work by identifying that gravitational wave detection of w-modes and g-modes from neutron stars could discriminate between EOS models, and that Quantum Monte Carlo calculations (Carlson et al.) and studies of color superconducting phases (Alford et al.) will be needed for improved theoretical descriptions.
