# Isospin dynamics in nuclear structure

> We discuss some special aspects of the nuclear many-body problem related to isospin transfer. The major quantity of interest is the in-medium propagator of a particle-hole configuration of the proton-neutron character, which determines the nuclear response to isospin transferring external fields. One of the most studied excitation modes is the Gamow-Teller resonance (GTR), which can, therefore, be used as a sensitive test for the theoretical approaches. Its low-energy part, which is responsible for the beta decay half-lives, is especially convenient for this. Models benchmarked against the GTR can be used to predict other, more exotic, excitations studied at nuclear rare isotope beam facilities and in astrophysics. As far as the precision is concerned, the major problem in such an analysis is to disentangle the effects related to the underlying interaction and those caused by the many-body correlations. Therefore, approaches (i) based on fundamental concepts for the nucleon-nucleon interaction which (ii) include complex many-body dynamics are the preferred ones. We discuss progress and obstacles on the way to such approaches.

## Key claim

The paper argues that disentangling the effects of the underlying nucleon-nucleon interaction from those caused by many-body correlations is the major problem for precision in describing isospin-transfer excitations; therefore, approaches that are both based on fundamental concepts for the interaction and include complex many-body dynamics are preferred, but the authors discuss progress and obstacles rather than claiming a fully solved framework.

## Method

The authors employ the equation-of-motion method for the two-fermion propagator (response function) within the Green function formalism, truncating the infinite hierarchy at the two-fermion level to obtain a closed system of equations for the response functions in particle-hole, hole-hole, and particle-particle channels. The static part of the integral kernel is approximated by effective meson exchange from covariant density functional theory, while the dynamical part is modeled by quasiparticle-vibration coupling (QVC) in the relativistic quasiparticle time blocking approximation (RQTBA). The charge-exchange version (pn-RQTBA) is applied to Gamow-Teller resonances and isovector giant resonances in medium-mass and neutron-rich nuclei, with results compared to experimental charge-exchange reaction data.

## Key numbers

- Gamow-Teller strength distributions in nickel isotopes \(^{68-78}\)Ni were calculated with a smearing parameter of 200 keV for the overall strength and 20 keV for low-energy resolution to resolve individual states.
- Beta decay half-lives for \(^{68-78}\)Ni decrease systematically by one or two orders of magnitude when QVC effects are included compared to the pn-RQRPA calculation.
- For the \(^{100}\)Mo(t,\(^3\)He) reaction, the upper limit for the direct-decay branching ratio from excited \(^{100}\)Nb was determined to be 20\(\pm\)6%, indicating predominantly statistical decay.
- The pn-R(Q)TBA calculations for the isovector giant monopole and dipole resonances in \(^{28}\)Si describe the shapes of experimental cross sections reasonably, but give overall smaller values compared to measured cross sections, attributed to deficiencies in the reaction calculations.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic time blocking approximation (RTBA) and its generalized versions (RQTBA), previously successful for neutral-channel excitations such as giant multipole resonances and pygmy dipole modes, into the charge-exchange (proton-neutron) channel. The approach builds on earlier developments by Tselyaev (1989) and Litvinova, Ring, and Tselyaev (2007-2013) for particle-vibration coupling, and is benchmarked against the Gamow-Teller resonance as a sensitive test. The pn-RQTBA enables predictions for exotic isospin-flip excitations relevant to rare isotope beam facilities and astrophysical processes such as r-process nucleosynthesis, and provides a framework for comparing with experimental data from charge-exchange reactions like those at NSCL using the S800 spectrometer and GRETINA.
