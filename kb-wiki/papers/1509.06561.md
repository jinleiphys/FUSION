# Equation of state constraints for the cold dense matter inside neutron stars using the cooling tail method

> The cooling phase of thermonuclear (type-I) X-ray bursts can be used to constrain the neutron star (NS) compactness by comparing the observed cooling tracks of bursts to accurate theoretical atmosphere model calculations. By applying the so-called cooling tail method, where the information from the whole cooling track is used, we constrain the mass, radius, and distance for three different NSs in low-mass X-ray binaries 4U 1702-429, 4U 1724-307, and SAX J1810.8-260. Care is taken to only use the hard state bursts where it is thought that only the NS surface alone is emitting. We then utilize a Markov chain Monte Carlo algorithm within a Bayesian framework to obtain a parameterized equation of state (EoS) of cold dense matter from our initial mass and radius constraints. This allows us to set limits on various nuclear parameters and to constrain an empirical pressure-density relation for the dense matter. Our predicted EoS results in NS radius between 10.5-12.8 km (95% confidence limits) for a mass of 1.4 $M_{\odot}$. Due to systematic errors and uncertainty in the composition these results should be interpreted as lower limits for the radius.

## Key claim

The cooling tail method applied to hard-state photospheric radius expansion bursts from three neutron stars yields a predicted radius between 10.5-12.8 km (95% confidence) for a 1.4 solar mass star, but due to systematic errors and composition uncertainty these results should be interpreted as lower limits for the radius rather than absolute measurements.

## Method

The authors used time-resolved spectroscopy of type-I X-ray bursts from three neutron stars (4U 1702-429, 4U 1724-307, and SAX J1810.8-2609) observed with the Rossi X-ray Timing Explorer/PCA instrument, selecting only hard-state bursts where the neutron star surface alone is thought to emit. They applied the cooling tail method, comparing the observed evolution of blackbody normalization versus flux to theoretical atmosphere model color-correction curves, using a Bayesian framework with Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling to infer mass, radius, and distance for each source. These individual constraints were then combined to fit a parameterized equation of state for cold dense matter, yielding an empirical pressure-density relation.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star radius between 10.5-12.8 km (95% confidence limits) for a mass of 1.4 solar masses
- Eddington flux for 4U 1702-429: 0.80 (+0.01/-0.02 at 68% confidence, +0.02/-0.02 at 95% confidence) in units of 10^-7 erg cm^-2 s^-1, using a pure helium atmosphere model
- Eddington flux for 4U 1724-307: 0.58 (+0.02/-0.02 at 68% confidence, +0.04/-0.03 at 95% confidence) in units of 10^-7 erg cm^-2 s^-1, using a solar composition model (SolA001)
- Eddington flux for SAX J1810.8-2609: 0.79 (+0.02/-0.02 at 68% confidence, +0.03/-0.03 at 95% confidence) in units of 10^-7 erg cm^-2 s^-1, using a solar composition model (SolA001)
- Angular size parameter A for 4U 1702-429: 0.192 (+0.001/-0.002 at 68% confidence, +0.002/-0.004 at 95% confidence) in units of (km/10 kpc)^(-1/2)
- Angular size parameter A for 4U 1724-307: 0.184 (+0.002/-0.003 at 68% confidence, +0.003/-0.004 at 95% confidence) in units of (km/10 kpc)^(-1/2)
- Angular size parameter A for SAX J1810.8-2609: 0.169 (+0.002/-0.002 at 68% confidence, +0.004/-0.003 at 95% confidence) in units of (km/10 kpc)^(-1/2)
- Distance to 4U 1702-429: 5.6 (+0.3/-0.4 at 68% confidence, +0.6/-0.9 at 95% confidence) kpc
- Distance to 4U 1724-307: 4.9 (+0.5/-0.6 at 68% confidence, +0.7/-1.1 at 95% confidence) kpc
- Distance to SAX J1810.8-2609: 4.3 (+0.4/-0.5 at 68% confidence, +0.6/-1.0 at 95% confidence) kpc

## Context

This work extends the cooling tail method developed by Suleimanov et al. (2011, 2012) and Poutanen et al. (2014) by applying strict hard-state burst selection criteria simultaneously to multiple sources, building on the finding by Kajava et al. (2014) that burst cooling properties depend on accretion rate and spectral state. The results enable constraints on nuclear physics parameters such as the symmetry energy and the pressure of neutron-rich matter at saturation density, and provide an empirical pressure-density relation that can be compared with theoretical equation of state models from nuclear many-body calculations and heavy-ion collision experiments.
