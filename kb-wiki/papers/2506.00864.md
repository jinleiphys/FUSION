# Perspectives for hyperon and hypernuclei physics

> Hypernuclei, nuclei containing one or more hyperons, serve as unique laboratories for probing the non-perturbative quantum chromodynamics (QCD). Recent progress in hypernuclear physics, driven by advanced experimental techniques and theoretical innovations, is briefly reviewed with a focus on key findings and unresolved challenges, such as the precise determination of the hypertriton binding energy, investigations of charge symmetry breaking in mirror hypernuclei, and the search for exotic systems, including the neutral nn$Λ$ state. Experimental breakthroughs, including invariant-mass analyses and femtoscopy studies in heavy-ion collisions, as well as high-resolution $γ$-spectroscopy, have enabled precise studies of light hypernuclei and offered critical insights into the hyperon-nucleon interaction. Theoretical progress, including ab initio calculations based on chiral effective field theory and lattice QCD, has further enhanced our understanding of hyperon-nucleon and hyperon-hyperon interactions.

# Perspectives for hyperon and hypernuclei physics

## Key claim

The paper reviews recent progress in hypernuclear physics while emphasizing that despite experimental breakthroughs, the field remains limited to a few hypernuclei with often low event counts and limited resolution; the authors argue that upcoming multi-facility experiments combined with state-of-the-art theory will enable a comprehensive understanding of strange hadrons in cold hadronic matter, but note that unresolved challenges such as the precise determination of the hypertriton binding energy and the charge symmetry breaking puzzle persist.

## Method

The paper surveys experimental techniques including invariant-mass analyses and femtoscopy in heavy-ion collisions, high-resolution gamma-spectroscopy with germanium detectors, missing-mass spectroscopy using electron and kaon beams, and nuclear emulsion studies. On the theoretical side, it reviews meson-exchange models (Nijmegen and Bonn-Juelich families), lattice QCD simulations (NPLQCD and HAL QCD collaborations), and chiral effective field theory approaches at leading order through next-to-next-to-leading order, including recent relativistic chiral EFT developments from the Beihang group.

- Hypertriton binding energy from STAR: 406 ± 120 (stat.) ± 110 (syst.) keV
- Hypertriton binding energy from ALICE: 102 ± 63 (stat.) ± 67 (syst.) keV
- Global average of hypertriton binding energy: 0.17 ± 0.06 MeV (Chen 2023)
- Charge symmetry breaking in A=4 hypernuclei ground states from emulsion: ΔB⁴_Λ(0⁺) = 350 ± 40 keV
- Charge symmetry breaking in A=4 hypernuclei excited states from J-PARC E13: ΔB⁴_Λ(1⁺) = -30 ± 50 keV
- Updated CSB from Mainz A1 Collaboration: ΔB⁴_Λ(0⁺) = 233 ± 92 keV, ΔB⁴_Λ(1⁺) = -83 ± 93 keV
- STAR CSB measurement in Au+Au at √s_NN = 3 GeV: ΔB⁴_Λ(0⁺) = 160 ± 140 (stat.) ± 100 (syst.) keV, ΔB⁴_Λ(1⁺) = -160 ± 140 (stat.) ± 100 (syst.) keV
- Charge symmetry breaking in A=7 hypernuclei: ΔB⁷_Λn = 420 ± 280 keV, ΔB⁷_Λp = 100 ± 90 keV
- nnΛ system candidate binding energy from GSI: approximately 1 MeV
- Beam energy for FRS/WASA experiment: 1.96 GeV/nucleon
- Threshold energy for strangeness production in heavy-ion collisions: 1.6 GeV/nucleon
- HAL QCD lattice simulation pion mass near physical point: m_π = 146 MeV

## Context

This review extends the line of hypernuclear physics surveys by Davis (2005), Gal (2016), Chen (2018), and Tolos (2020), covering developments through 2025. It highlights tensions between the STAR and ALICE hypertriton binding energy measurements and between modern results and the classic emulsion compilation by Juric (1973). The paper enables future work by cataloging planned experiments at GSI/FAIR (WASA-FRS, R3B), J-PARC (E07, E13, P73/P77), Mainz Microtron, Jefferson Lab, ALICE at LHC Runs 3 and 4, and the ELENA antiproton facility at CERN, while identifying open questions in charge symmetry breaking, the existence of the nnΛ state, and the role of three-body forces in neutron star equations of state.
