# Cluster and toroidal aspects of isoscalar dipole excitations in $^{12}$C

> We investigated cluster and toroidal aspects of isoscalar dipole excitations in $^{12}$C based on the shifted basis antisymmetrized molecular dynamics combined with the generator coordinate method, which can describes 1p-1h excitations and $3α$ dynamics. In the $E=10-15$ MeV region, we found two low-energy dipole modes separating from the giant dipole resonance. One is the developed $3α$-cluster state and the other is the toroidal dipole mode. The cluster state is characterized by the large amplitude cluster motion beyond the 1p-1h model space, whereas the toroidal dipole mode is predominantly described by 1p-1h excitations on the ground state. The low-energy dipole states are remarkably excited by the toroidal dipole operator, which can measure the nuclear vorticity. For compressive dipole transition strengths, a major part is distributed in the $30-50$ MeV region for the giant dipole resonance, and 5\% of the total energy weighted sum exist in the $E<20$ MeV region.

## Key claim

The paper claims that in $^{12}$C, two distinct low-energy isoscalar dipole modes exist in the $E=10-15$ MeV region below the giant dipole resonance: one is a developed $3\alpha$-cluster state and the other is a toroidal dipole mode, with the cluster state characterized by large amplitude cluster motion beyond the 1p-1h model space while the toroidal mode is predominantly described by 1p-1h excitations on the ground state.

## Method

The authors applied the shifted basis antisymmetrized molecular dynamics combined with the generator coordinate method (sAMD+GCM) to $^{12}$C, using the MV1 central force with two-range Gaussian two-body terms and a zero-range three-body term, plus the G3RS spin-orbit force. They superposed 192 shifted AMD wave functions for 1p-1h excitations together with various $3\alpha$-cluster configurations from the Brink-Bloch cluster model, with the width parameter $\nu=0.19$ fm$^{-2}$ chosen to minimize the ground state energy. The final wave functions for $0^+_k$ and $1^-_k$ states were obtained by diagonalizing the norm and Hamiltonian matrices after angular-momentum and parity projection.

## Key numbers

- Two low-energy dipole states were found in the $E=10-15$ MeV region: the $1^-_1$ state at 12.6 MeV (assigned to the experimental $1^-$ at 10.844 MeV) and the $1^-_2$ state at 14.8 MeV
- 5% of the total energy-weighted sum for compressive dipole transition strengths exists in the $E<20$ MeV region
- The energy-weighted sum of low-energy toroidal dipole strengths is 40-50% of the total energy-weighted sum from the full sAMD+GCM calculation
- The low-energy compressive dipole transition strength ratio is 2.5% in the sAMD alone, rising to 4-5% when $3\alpha$ configurations are included
- The $1^-_2$ state contains 40% $1\hbar\omega$ component indicating dominant 1p-1h excitations
- The $0^+_1$ ground state has 85% overlap with the E-$3\alpha$ wave function with $r=1$ fm, $D=2$ fm, $\theta=\pi/2$, and $\Lambda=0.5$
- The $1^-_1$ state has 60% overlap with the Brink-Bloch $3\alpha$ wave function with $r=5.8$ fm, $D=5$ fm, and $\theta=\pi/4$
- The $1^-_2$ state has 75% overlap with the 1p-1h dominant toroidal dipole mode of the sAMD, reduced to 50% in the full sAMD+GCM due to coupling with the $3\alpha$-cluster mode

## Context

This work extends the sAMD+GCM method previously applied by Kanada-En'yo (2015) to calculate isoscalar monopole and dipole transition strengths in $^{12}$C, now specifically analyzing the toroidal and cluster nature of the low-energy dipole excitations. The results are consistent with $3\alpha$-cluster model calculations by Kamimura (1981) and Uegaki (1977) for the $1^-_1$ state, and the toroidal dipole mode analysis follows the framework of Kvasil (2011) for separating toroidal, compressive, and vortical modes. The work enables future studies of how the large-amplitude cluster mode and the 1p-1h toroidal mode couple in light nuclei, and provides a microscopic understanding of low-energy dipole strengths observed in $\alpha$ inelastic scattering experiments by John (2003).
