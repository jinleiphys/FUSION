# Spectroscopy and excited-state $g$~factors in weakly collective ${^{111}}$Cd: confronting collective and microscopic models

> The even cadmium isotopes near the neutron midshell have long been considered good examples of vibrational nuclei. However, the vibrational nature of these nuclei has been questioned based on E2 transition rates that are not consistent with vibrational excitations. In the neighbouring odd-mass nuclei, the g factors of the low-excitation collective states have been shown to be more consistent with a deformed rotational core than a vibrational core. Beyond the comparison of vibrational versus rotational models, recent advances in computational power have made shell-model calculations feasible for Cd isotopes, which may give insights into the emergence and nature of collectivity in the Cd isotopes. Collective excitations in the A ~ 100 region were studied through magnetic moments and electromagnetic transitions in 111Cd. The spectroscopy of 111Cd has been studied following Coulomb excitation. Angular correlation measurements, transient-field g-factor measurements and lifetime measurements by the Doppler-broadened line shape method were performed. The structure of the nucleus was explored in relation to particle-vibration versus particle-rotor interpretations. Large-scale shell-model calculations were performed with the SR88MHJM Hamiltonian. Excited-state g factors have been measured, spin assignments examined and lifetimes determined. Attention was given to the reported $5/2^{+}$ 753-keV and $3/2^{+}$ 755-keV states. The $3/2^{+}$ 755-keV level was not observed; evidence is presented that the reported $3/2^+$ state was a misidentification of the $5/2^{+}$ 753-keV state. It is shown that the g factors and level structure of 111Cd are not readily explained by the particle-vibration model. A particle-rotor approach has both successes and limitations. The shell-model approach successfully reproduces much of the known low-excitation structure in 111Cd.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the low-excitation structure and g factors of 111Cd are not readily explained by the particle-vibration model, while a particle-rotor approach has both successes and limitations; the large-scale shell-model approach successfully reproduces much of the known low-excitation structure, though the abstract's claim of success is qualified by the particle-rotor model's mixed performance.

## Method

The spectroscopy of 111Cd was studied following Coulomb excitation with a 90-MeV 32S beam on an enriched 111Cd target. Angular correlation measurements, transient-field g-factor measurements, and lifetime measurements by the Doppler-broadened line shape (DBLS) method were performed. Large-scale shell-model calculations were performed with the SR88MHJM Hamiltonian, and the results were compared with particle-vibration and particle-rotor model calculations.

## Key numbers

*   g factor of the 342.1-keV 3/2+ state: adopted value +0.9(3), from weighted average of present measurement (+1.2(4)) and previous results.
*   g factor of the 620.2-keV 5/2+ state: adopted value +0.22(4), from weighted average of present measurement (+0.27(7)) and previous results.
*   g factor of the 752.8-keV 5/2+ state: +0.5(2), measured for the first time in this work.
*   Lifetime of the 752.8-keV 5/2+ state: 4.0(16) ps, determined by DBLS analysis, corresponding to a B(E2) of 11.2(+3.7/-2.2 statistical +5.8/-2.6 systematic) W.u.
*   Lifetime of the 681-keV 3/2+ state in 113Cd: lower limit of tau >= 7 ps, consistent with previous B(E2) measurements.
*   The 3/2+ 755-keV state reported in Nuclear Data Sheets was not observed; only a single peak at 752.8 keV was found, confirming the 5/2+ assignment for the 753-keV state.

## Context

This work extends the debate over whether Cd isotopes near the neutron midshell are vibrational or rotational in nature, following earlier studies by Stuchbery (2016) that showed g factors in 111,113Cd were more consistent with a particle-rotor than a particle-vibration description. The paper contradicts the existence of a previously reported 3/2+ state at 755 keV (Blachot 2009) and resolves a discrepancy in the lifetime of the 681-keV state in 113Cd. The large-scale shell-model calculations, similar to those by Schmidt (2017) for 98-108Cd and Yordanov (2018) for 101-109Cd, enable a microscopic perspective on the emergence of collectivity in this mass region.
