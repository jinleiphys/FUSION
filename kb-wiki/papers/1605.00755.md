# Beyond mean-field boson-fermion model for odd-mass nuclei

> A novel method for calculating spectroscopic properties of medium-mass and heavy atomic nuclei with an odd number of nucleons is introduced, based on the framework of nuclear energy density functional theory and the particle-core coupling scheme. The deformation energy surface of the even-even core, as well as the spherical single-particle energies and occupation probabilities of the odd particle(s), are obtained in a self-consistent mean-field calculation determined by the choice of the energy density functional and pairing interaction. This method uniquely determines the parameters of the Hamiltonian of the boson core, and only the strength of the particle-core coupling is specifically adjusted to selected data for a particular nucleus. The approach is illustrated in a systematic study of low-energy excitation spectra and transition rates of axially deformed odd-mass Eu isotopes.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a method for calculating spectroscopic properties of odd-mass nuclei that combines nuclear energy density functional theory with the interacting boson-fermion model, where only the strength of the particle-core coupling is adjusted to data while all other Hamiltonian parameters are determined microscopically. The method is illustrated for axially deformed odd-mass Eu isotopes, but the authors note that the boson-fermion coupling strength must be fitted separately for positive- and negative-parity states in each nucleus.

## Method

The deformation energy surface of the even-even core is obtained from a constrained self-consistent relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov calculation using the DD-PC1 energy density functional and a separable pairing force, then mapped onto the expectation value of the interacting boson model Hamiltonian in the intrinsic (coherent) state to determine the boson Hamiltonian parameters. Spherical single-particle energies and occupation probabilities for the odd particle are obtained from the same self-consistent mean-field calculation constrained at zero deformation. The boson-fermion Hamiltonian is diagonalized numerically in the spherical basis of coupled boson and fermion states, with the three strength parameters (quadrupole, exchange, and monopole) adjusted to reproduce the energies of the first excited state and the band-head of the second-lowest band.

## Key numbers

- The even-even core $^{154}$Sm has a prolate equilibrium minimum at $\beta_{\rm min} \approx 0.34$ from the RHB calculation
- Boson Hamiltonian parameters for $^{154}$Sm (neutron number N=90): $\epsilon_d = 0.29$ MeV, $\kappa = -0.079$ MeV, $\kappa^{\prime} = -0.021$ MeV, $\chi = -0.55$ (dimensionless)
- Single-particle energies relative to $1g_{7/2}$ orbit: $\epsilon_{d5/2} = 3.24$ MeV, $\epsilon_{d3/2} = 5.11$ MeV, $\epsilon_{s1/2} = 5.88$ MeV for N=90
- Occupation probabilities from RHB: $v^2_{g7/2} \approx 0.96$, $v^2_{d5/2} \approx 0.55$, $v^2_{h11/2} \approx 0.13$
- The energy spacing $|\epsilon_{g7/2} - \epsilon_{d5/2}| \approx 3$ MeV, in contrast to less than 0.5 MeV in the phenomenological calculation of Scholten (1982)
- Calculated spectroscopic quadrupole moment $Q({5/2}^+_1) = +1.79$ b for $^{153}$Eu compared to experimental $+2.28(9)$ b
- Calculated magnetic moment $\mu({5/2}^+_1) = +1.54$ $\mu_N$ for $^{153}$Eu compared to experimental $+1.53$ $\mu_N$

## Context

This work extends the method of Nomura et al. (2008) for determining interacting boson model parameters from energy density functional calculations to odd-mass nuclei, making it equivalent to the phenomenological interacting boson-fermion model of Iachello and Scholten. The approach contrasts with the fully self-consistent generator coordinate method extension to odd-mass systems reported by Bally et al. (2014), which faces significant computational challenges for heavy nuclei due to time-reversal symmetry breaking. The method enables systematic, computationally feasible studies of odd-mass nuclei across isotopic chains, as demonstrated for $^{151,153,155}$Eu, and can be compared directly with the phenomenological calculation of Scholten (1982).
