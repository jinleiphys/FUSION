# Constraining a possible time variation of the gravitational constant G with terrestrial nuclear laboratory data

> Testing the constancy of the gravitational constant G has been a longstanding fundamental question in natural science. As first suggested by Jofré, Reisenegger and Fernández [1], Dirac's hypothesis of a decreasing gravitational constant $G$ with time due to the expansion of the Universe would induce changes in the composition of neutron stars, causing dissipation and internal heating. Eventually, neutron stars reach their quasi-stationary states where cooling due to neutrino and photon emissions balances the internal heating. The correlation of surface temperatures and radii of some old neutron stars may thus carry useful information about the changing rate of G. Using the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy constrained by recent terrestrial laboratory data on isospin diffusion in heavy-ion reactions at intermediate energies and the size of neutron skin in $^{208}Pb$ within the gravitochemical heating formalism, we obtain an upper limit of the relative changing rate of $|\dot{G}/G|\le4\times 10^{-12}yr^{-1}$ consistent with the best available estimates in the literature.

## Key claim

Using the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy constrained by terrestrial nuclear laboratory data (isospin diffusion in heavy-ion reactions and neutron skin in 208Pb), the authors obtain an upper limit for the relative time variation of the gravitational constant G in the range (4.5–21) × 10⁻¹² yr⁻¹, which is consistent with the best available estimates in the literature but depends on which equation of state (EOS) is used and whether fast or slow cooling processes operate in the neutron star interior.

## Method

The authors applied the gravitochemical heating formalism of Jofré, Reisenegger and Fernández (2006) to neutron star models constructed from several equations of state, including the MDI interaction with symmetry energy constrained by isospin diffusion data and neutron skin measurements. They calculated the stationary surface temperature of old neutron stars (specifically PSR J0437-4715) by solving the thermal balance and chemical imbalance evolution equations, then compared predicted temperatures with observational constraints from Kargaltsev et al. (2003) to derive upper limits on |Ġ/G| for both slow (modified Urca only) and fast (direct Urca allowed) cooling scenarios.

## Key numbers

- Upper limit on |Ġ/G| from the x=0 EOS (slow cooling): 4.5 × 10⁻¹² yr⁻¹, chosen so predictions lie just above the 90% confidence contour of Kargaltsev et al. (2003)
- Upper limit on |Ġ/G| from the x=-1 EOS (fast cooling via direct Urca): 2.1 × 10⁻¹¹ yr⁻¹, chosen so predictions lie just above the same observational constraints
- Mass range of PSR J0437-4715: (1.1–1.5) M⊙ from Hotan et al. (2006); alternative mass constraint 1.58 ± 0.18 M⊙ from van Straten et al. (2001)
- Maximum masses of neutron star models: MDI(x=0) 1.91 M⊙ (radius 9.89 km), MDI(x=-1) 1.97 M⊙ (radius 10.85 km), APR 2.19 M⊙ (radius 9.98 km), Bonn B 2.24 M⊙ (radius 10.88 km), Hyp 1.25 M⊙ (radius 9.96 km), Hyb 1.51 M⊙ (radius 9.14 km)
- Symmetry energy at saturation density: e_sym(ρ₀) ≈ 32 MeV
- Compression modulus of saturated nuclear matter: κ = 211 MeV

## Context

This work extends the gravitochemical heating method introduced by Jofré, Reisenegger and Fernández (2006) by incorporating equations of state whose symmetry energy is constrained by recent terrestrial nuclear laboratory data, specifically isospin diffusion in heavy-ion reactions and neutron skin measurements in 208Pb. The resulting upper limits are compared with previous constraints from big bang nucleosynthesis (Copi et al., 2004) which gave |Ġ/G| ≤ 4 × 10⁻¹³ yr⁻¹ over the age of the Universe, and with other methods summarized in Table 1 including lunar laser ranging, binary pulsar orbits, and helioseismology. The work enables more reliable constraints on time variation of G by reducing the uncertainty from the nuclear equation of state, and suggests that combined roto- and gravitochemical heating could explain the observed surface temperature of PSR J0437-4715 with a smaller upper limit on |Ġ/G|.
