# The Long Journey from Ab Initio Calculations to Density Functional Theory for Nuclear Large Amplitude Collective Motion

> At present there are two vastly different ab initio approaches to the description of the the many-body dynamics: the Density Functional Theory (DFT) and the functional integral (path integral) approaches. On one hand, if implemented exactly, the DFT approach can allow in principle the exact evaluation of arbitrary one-body observable. However, when applied to Large Amplitude Collective Motion (LACM) this approach needs to be extended in order to accommodate the phenomenon of surface-hoping, when adiabaticity is strongly violated and the description of a system using a single (generalized) Slater determinant is not valid anymore. The functional integral approach on the other hand does not appear to have such restrictions, but its implementation does not appear to be straightforward endeavor. However, within a functional integral approach one seems to be able to evaluate in principle any kind of observables, such as the fragment mass and energy distributions in nuclear fission. These two radically approaches can likely be brought brought together by formulating a stochastic time-dependent DFT approach to many-body dynamics.

# The Long Journey from Ab Initio Calculations to Density Functional Theory for Nuclear Large Amplitude Collective Motion

## Key claim

The paper argues that the standard theoretical framework for nuclear Large Amplitude Collective Motion (LACM), based on the Hill-Wheeler equation and the Generator Coordinate Method, is not a controlled approximation because the size of the theoretical error is unknown and cannot be quantified. The author proposes that a stochastic time-dependent Density Functional Theory (TDDFT) approach, combining functional integral methods with DFT, could resolve these difficulties, but acknowledges that agreement with experiment would initially be worse.

## Method

The paper reviews and critiques the current approach to LACM, which represents the nuclear many-body wave function as an integral over generalized Slater determinants parameterized by collective variables, leading to the Hill-Wheeler equation. The author then outlines two alternative ab initio approaches: the real-time functional integral approach, where the stationary phase approximation leads to a quantization condition involving the Berry phase, and the Time-Dependent Density Functional Theory approach, which can in principle evaluate one-body observables exactly. The paper proposes merging these approaches into a stochastic TDDFT framework, drawing on the surface-hopping formalism developed in chemistry by Tully to handle non-adiabatic transitions when adiabaticity is violated.

## Key numbers

- The Green Function Monte-Carlo (GFMC) approach cannot currently study nuclei heavier than oxygen, setting an upper limit on particle number for that method.
- The binding energy of $^{208}$Pb may have theoretical corrections from relativistic effects, medium modifications, and Delta-isobar excitations that could be larger or smaller than 1 MeV, with no reliable estimate available.
- In one cited calculation by Moller and collaborators, at least five different collective variables and approximately five million different nuclear shape configurations were needed, requiring roughly a thousand times more computing time than generating the entire nuclear mass table in a selfconsistent meanfield approach.
- The paper discusses a study by Negele and collaborators on the "spontaneous fission" of $^{32}$S, where artificially increasing the proton electric charge forced the nucleus to split into two $^{16}$O fragments.

## Context

This paper extends the long-standing critique of the Hill-Wheeler and Generator Coordinate Method approaches to nuclear fission and LACM, which were originally formulated by Hill and Wheeler (1953) and developed further by Klein and collaborators. The author contrasts these methods with the Green Function Monte-Carlo approach of Pandharipande, Carlson, Pieper, Schiavilla, Schmidt, and Wiringa, as well as the Coupled-Cluster method and the No-Core-Shell Model, noting that heavy nuclei remain out of reach for direct ab initio attack. The paper draws on the surface-hopping formalism of Tully from chemistry and the work of Berry on geometric phases to argue that a stochastic TDDFT approach could handle the non-adiabatic transitions and level crossings that occur in nuclear LACM, particularly in fission. The work enables future development of a controlled approximation for nuclear fission dynamics with quantifiable theoretical errors, rather than relying on parameter fine-tuning to match experimental data.
