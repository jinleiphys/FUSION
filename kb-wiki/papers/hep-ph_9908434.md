# Flavour symmetry as a Spontaneously Broken Discrete Permutation Symmetry Embedded in Colour

## Key claim
Flavour symmetry can be reinterpreted as a discrete permutation symmetry of valence quarks with respect to the quark-antiquark sea, which can be spontaneously broken without generating massless Goldstone bosons, a mechanism intermediate between the Nambu-Goldstone and Wigner modes of symmetry breaking, where the sea replaces the vacuum as the symmetry-breaking agent.

## Method
The author constructs a model in which the quark-antiquark sea is represented as a diagonal matrix in colour space, with entries corresponding to the flavour labels (u, d, s). Flavour symmetry is defined as the freedom to permute these diagonal entries while maintaining the diagonal form; a global colour transformation rotates both valence and sea components together, leaving the hadron unchanged, while a permutation applied only to the valence part changes the flavour state. The asymmetric sea is generated dynamically through quantum loops (e.g., K* → K*π, π → πσ), where virtual strange-antistrange pairs are less frequent than non-strange pairs, and this asymmetry can become self-enhancing.

## Key numbers
- The model takes N_f = N_c = 3 for the discussion, though the arguments are claimed to hold for any number of flavours and colours.
- The sea matrix is written as S(qq̅) = ε₀λ₀ + ε₃λ₃ + ε₈λ₈ = diag(x, y, z), with the diagonal terms labelled by flavour: S(uū), S(dd̅), S(ss̅).
- The charge operator Q = diag(2/3, -1/3, -1/3) and strangeness operator S = diag(0, 0, 1) are given explicitly.
- The valence quarks of a meson form a degenerate nonet in colour (9 nonets in total before gluon dressing); for baryons the valence quarks form a 3⊗3⊗3 = 1⊕8⊕8⊕10 multiplet.

## Context
This work extends the author's own previous dynamical calculations (Tornqvist, 1990s) involving scalars, pseudoscalars, and vectors in simplified models, which demonstrated that the spontaneous symmetry breaking goes in the right direction compared to experiment and naturally explains the approximate Okubo-Zweig-Iizuka rule, near-ideal mixing, and the equal spacing rule of meson multiplets. The mechanism differs from the colour-flavour locking of Schafer and Wilczek (late 1990s), which is applied to high-density QCD where the sea and vacuum merge. The paper also connects to the historical discussion of superselection rules from the era when isospin invariance was introduced.
