# Phase separation in low-density neutron matter

> Low-density neutron matter has been studied extensively for many decades, with a view to better understanding the properties of neutron-star crusts and neutron-rich nuclei. Neutron matter is beyond experimental control, but in the past decade it has become possible to create systems of fermionic ultracold atomic gases in a regime close to low-density neutron matter. In both these contexts pairing is significant, making simple perturbative approaches impossible to apply and necessitating ab initio microscopic simulations. Atomic experiments have also probed polarized matter. In this work, we study population-imbalanced neutron matter (possibly relevant to magnetars and to density functionals of nuclei) arriving at the lowest-energy configuration to date. For small to intermediate relative fractions the system turns out to be fully normal, while beyond a critical polarization we find phase coexistence between a partially polarized normal neutron gas and a balanced superfluid gas. As in cold atoms, a homogeneous polarized superfluid is close to stability but not stable with respect to phase separation. We also study the dependence of the critical polarization on the density.

## Key claim
For low-density neutron matter at zero temperature, a homogeneous polarized superfluid is close to stability but is not stable with respect to phase separation; instead, beyond a critical polarization the system forms a mixed phase of a partially polarized normal neutron gas and a balanced superfluid gas, while for small to intermediate relative fractions the system is fully normal.

## Method
The authors performed fixed-node Green's Function Monte Carlo (GFMC) simulations of neutron matter using the AV4' interaction, which includes both s-wave and p-wave components. They calculated ground-state energies for normal (non-superfluid) neutron gas at three densities and five polarization values, using trial wave functions composed of two Slater determinants (one for each spin species) multiplied by Jastrow correlation functions. They then used these energies, together with previously calculated superfluid energies, to construct the mixed phase by equating chemical potentials and pressures between the normal polarized phase and the unpolarized superfluid phase.

## Key numbers
- Densities studied: n1 = 6.65 × 10⁻⁴ fm⁻³, n2 = 2.16 × 10⁻³ fm⁻³, n3 = 5.32 × 10⁻³ fm⁻³ (0.41%, 1.35%, and 3.32% of nuclear saturation density n0 = 0.16 fm⁻³)
- Critical relative fractions (x_c) at which phase separation begins: x_c1 = 0.74 at n1, x_c2 = 0.78 at n2, x_c3 = 0.88 at n3
- Critical chemical potential splitting at n2: δμ_c2/E_F = 0.16(2), compared to the superfluid gap Δ_SF2/E_F ∼ 0.23
- Critical chemical potential splitting at n1: δμ_c1/E_F = 0.2; at n3: δμ_c3/E_F = 0.07
- Neutron-neutron scattering length a ∼ −18.6 fm and effective range r_e ∼ 2.7 fm
- For fixed μ = 1.94 MeV, the critical δμ_c = 0.53 MeV at density n2

## Context
This work extends Quantum Monte Carlo studies of low-density neutron matter (Carlson and Morales 2003, Gezerlis 2008, 2010, 2011) to include polarization effects, and is directly motivated by the behavior of ultracold atomic Fermi gases at unitarity (Lobo 2006, Pilati 2008, Giorgini 2008), where phase separation between a balanced superfluid and a polarized normal gas was observed. The results provide microscopic constraints for Skyrme density functional theories used to study neutron-rich nuclei (Bender 2003, Chamel 2008) and are relevant for understanding the thermal properties of neutron star crusts, particularly the specific heat and its effect on crustal transient phenomena (Brown 2009).
