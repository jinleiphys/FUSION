# Quasi-Particle Degrees of Freedom versus the Perfect Fluid as Descriptors of the Quark-Gluon Plasma

> The hot nuclear matter created at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) has been characterized by near-perfect fluid behavior. We demonstrate that this stands in contradiction to the identification of QCD quasi-particles with the thermodynamic degrees of freedom in the early (fluid) stage of heavy ion collisions. The empirical observation of constituent quark ``$n_q$'' scaling of elliptic flow is juxtaposed with the lack of such scaling behavior in hydrodynamic fluid calculations followed by Cooper-Frye freeze-out to hadrons. A ``quasi-particle transport'' time stage after viscous effects break down the hydrodynamic fluid stage, but prior to hadronization, is proposed to reconcile these apparent contradictions. However, without a detailed understanding of the transitions between these stages, the ``$n_q$'' scaling is not a necessary consequence of this prescription. Also, if the duration of this stage is too short, it may not support well defined quasi-particles. By comparing and contrasting the coalescence of quarks into hadrons with the similar process of producing light nuclei from nucleons, it is shown that the observation of ``$n_{q}$'' scaling in the final state does not necessarily imply that the constituent degrees of freedom were the relevant ones in the initial state.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the observation of constituent quark number ($n_q$) scaling of elliptic flow in RHIC data does not necessarily imply that quasi-particles with quark quantum numbers were the relevant degrees of freedom in the early (fluid) stage of the collision, and that the near-perfect fluid behavior of the quark-gluon plasma stands in contradiction to identifying QCD quasi-particles with the thermodynamic degrees of freedom in that early stage.

## Method

The authors compare experimental elliptic flow ($v_2$) data for identified hadrons from PHENIX and STAR collaborations in minimum bias Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200$ GeV with hydrodynamic calculations using Cooper-Frye freeze-out to hadrons. They quantify the degree of $n_q$ scaling violation in the hydrodynamic calculations versus the data, and perform Monte Carlo simulations of resonance decays ($\rho$ and $\Delta$) to assess their effect on the scaling. They also derive order-of-magnitude estimates for the ratio of quasi-particle width $\Gamma$ to mass $m$ from viscosity relations to assess whether well-defined quasi-particles can coexist with near-perfect fluid behavior.

## Key numbers

- The $n_q$ scaling behavior holds at the $\pm 5\%$ level for all hadron species measured for $(m_T-m)/n_q > 0.2$ GeV; below this value deviations are larger ($\pm 15-25\%$)
- The hydrodynamic calculation results show $\pm 15-25\%$ level deviations of various hadrons from the reference line when plotted as $v_2/n_q$ vs. $(m_T-m)/n_q$, whereas experimental data on the identical scale do not show such deviations
- The hydrodynamic calculation uses initial time $\tau = 0.6$ fm/$c$, freeze-out temperature $T_{fo} = 130$ MeV, and a first order phase transition at $T_c = 165$ MeV
- The viscosity to entropy density ratio lower bound from AdS/CFT duality is $\eta/s \geq 1/4\pi$
- From the derived estimates, if the thermal mass of quasi-particles is $1-3 \cdot T$, then the ratio $\Gamma/m$ is of order one

## Context

This work extends the debate over whether the quark-gluon plasma created at RHIC is best described as a near-perfect fluid or as a gas of quasi-particles, following earlier work by Blaizot (1999) and Peshier (2004, 2005) on quasi-particle models matching lattice QCD, and hydrodynamic calculations by Huovinen (2005) and Teaney (2003). The paper contradicts claims in experimental papers by Afanasiev (2007) and Abelev (2007) that $n_q$ scaling implies partonic collectivity dominates the expansion dynamics. It enables future work to develop a multi-stage freeze-out approach with a quasi-particle transport stage after viscous effects break down the hydrodynamic fluid stage but prior to hadronization, though the authors note that without detailed understanding of the transitions between stages, the $n_q$ scaling is not a necessary consequence of this prescription.
