# The effects of medium on nuclear properties in multifragmentation

> In multifragmentation of hot nuclear matter, properties of fragments embedded in a soup of nucleonic gas and other fragments should be modified as compared with isolated nuclei. Such modifications are studied within a simple model where only nucleons and one kind of heavy nuclei are considered. The interaction between different species is described with a momentum-dependent two-body potential whose parameters are fitted to reproduce properties of cold isolated nuclei. The internal energy of heavy fragments is parametrized according to a liquid-drop model with density and temperature dependent parameters. Calculations are carried out for several subnuclear densities and moderate temperatures, for isospin-symmetric and asymmetric systems. We find that the fragments get stretched due to interactions with the medium and their binding energies decrease with increasing temperature and density of nuclear matter.

## Key claim

The authors find that fragments embedded in a hot nucleonic gas and surrounded by other fragments become stretched and their binding energies decrease with increasing temperature and density of the surrounding nuclear matter, but the effect is dominated by nucleon-fragment interactions rather than fragment-fragment interactions, and the magnitude of the modification depends on the baryon density of the disassembling system.

## Method

The authors model multifragmentation using a simplified system containing only free neutrons, free protons, and one species of heavy nucleus (A, Z) in thermodynamic equilibrium. The interaction between all species is described by the momentum- and density-dependent Seyler-Blanchard (SBM) effective interaction, with parameters fitted to reproduce properties of cold isolated nuclei. The internal energy and free energy of the heavy fragments are parametrized using a liquid-drop model with density- and temperature-dependent volume, surface, and symmetry coefficients, and the Coulomb energy is treated in the Wigner-Seitz approximation. The equilibrium configuration is found by minimizing the total free energy of the system with respect to the internal nucleon density of the fragments, subject to conservation of baryon number and isospin and chemical equilibrium between fragments and the nucleon gas.

## Key numbers

- The critical temperature for symmetric nuclear matter with the SBM interaction is T_c = 14.9 MeV, where the surface energy vanishes.
- The symmetry coefficient a_sym for cold symmetric nuclear matter at saturation density is taken as 31.0 MeV, with the ratio alpha/beta = 2.4 for the A-dependence.
- The isoscalar volume incompressibility K_infinity = 240 MeV, symmetry incompressibility K_sym = -101 MeV, and L = 59.8 MeV for the SBM interaction.
- The effective mass of the nucleon from the momentum dependence is 0.62 m for symmetric nuclear matter.
- At baryon density rho_b = 0.005 fm^{-3} and temperatures around 7-8 MeV, nuclei like 40Ca and 56Fe expand in volume by approximately 25% in the full (1,1,1) calculation, compared to approximately 17-19% for isolated hot nuclei in phase equilibrium with their own vapor.
- Increasing the baryon density to rho_b = 0.02 fm^{-3} causes a further expansion of fragments by approximately 4% beyond the lower-density case.

## Context

This work extends statistical models of multifragmentation (Bonche 1985, Gross 1990, Pal 1997, De 2000) and studies of the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition (Meyer 1983, Horowitz 2004, De 2006) by explicitly calculating how the properties of fragments are modified by the surrounding medium. The authors note that recent experiments (Fevre 2005, Iglio 2006) and analyses (Shetty 2007) suggest the symmetry energy is progressively reduced with excitation energy, and that quantum statistical approaches (Typel 2010, Voskresenskaya 2010) show cluster properties are modified in medium. The results enable more realistic modeling of multifragmentation in heavy-ion collisions and astrophysical contexts such as supernova explosions and explosive nucleosynthesis (Shen 1998, Ishizuka 2008, Botvina 2001, Hempel 2011), where the in-medium modification of nuclear binding energies and densities must be accounted for.
