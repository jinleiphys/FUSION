# A Second Relativistic Mean Field and Virial Equation of State for Astrophysical Simulations

> We generate a second equation of state (EOS) of nuclear matter for a wide range of temperatures, densities, and proton fractions for use in supernovae, neutron star mergers, and black hole formation simulations. We employ full relativistic mean field (RMF) calculations for matter at intermediate density and high density, and the Virial expansion of a non-ideal gas for matter at low density. For this EOS we use the RMF effective interaction FSUGold, whereas our earlier EOS was based on the RMF effective interaction NL3. The FSUGold interaction has a lower pressure at high densities compared to the NL3 interaction. We calculate the resulting EOS at over 100,000 grid points in the temperature range $T$ = 0 to 80 MeV, the density range $n_B$ = 10$^{-8}$ to 1.6 fm$^{-3}$, and the proton fraction range $Y_p$ = 0 to 0.56. We then interpolate these data points using a suitable scheme to generate a thermodynamically consistent equation of state table on a finer grid. We discuss differences between this EOS, our NL3 based EOS, and previous EOSs by Lattimer-Swesty and H. Shen et al for the thermodynamic properties, composition, and neutron star structure. The original FSUGold interaction produces an EOS, that we call FSU1.7, that has a maximum neutron star mass of 1.7 solar masses. A modification in the high density EOS is introduced to increase the maximum neutron star mass to 2.1 solar masses and results in a slightly different EOS that we call FSU2.1. The EOS tables for FSU1.7 and FSU2.1 are available for download.

## Key claim
The paper generates two new equation of state (EOS) tables for astrophysical simulations using the FSUGold relativistic mean field interaction, which produces a softer EOS at high density than the authors' earlier NL3-based EOS. The original FSUGold interaction yields a maximum neutron star mass of only 1.7 solar masses, so a modification is introduced to raise this to 2.1 solar masses, resulting in two distinct EOS tables (FSU1.7 and FSU2.1).

## Method
The authors performed full relativistic mean field (RMF) calculations for nuclear matter at intermediate and high densities, combined with a Virial expansion of a non-ideal gas for low-density matter. They calculated the EOS at over 100,000 grid points covering temperature T = 0 to 80 MeV, baryon density n_B = 10^{-8} to 1.6 fm^{-3}, and proton fraction Y_p = 0 to 0.56, then interpolated these data using a bicubic scheme to produce thermodynamically consistent tables on a finer grid. For the FSU2.1 variant, an additional pressure term dP = a (rho^2 - rho_0^2) with a = 2 x 10^{-5} MeV^{-2} and rho_0 = 0.2 fm^{-3} was added at high densities to increase the maximum neutron star mass.

## Key numbers
- Maximum neutron star mass for FSU1.7: 1.7 solar masses
- Maximum neutron star mass for FSU2.1: 2.1 solar masses
- Maximum neutron star mass for NL3: 2.8 solar masses
- Radii for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: approximately 13 km (FSU1.7), 14 km (FSU2.1), and 15 km (NL3)
- Temperature range of the EOS table: T = 0 to 80 MeV
- Baryon density range: n_B = 10^{-8} to 1.6 fm^{-3}
- Proton fraction range: Y_p = 0 to 0.56
- Total of 107,000 grid points in the 3-dimensional parameter space
- Saturation density for both FSUGold and NL3: 0.148 fm^{-3}
- Incompressibility K_0: 230.0 MeV (FSUGold) vs 271.5 MeV (NL3)
- Symmetry energy J: 32.59 MeV (FSUGold) vs 37.29 MeV (NL3)
- Slope of symmetry energy L: 60.5 MeV (FSUGold) vs 118.2 MeV (NL3)
- Neutron skin thickness in ^{208}Pb: 0.21 fm (FSUGold) vs 0.28 fm (NL3)

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous EOS based on the NL3 interaction (Shen, Horowitz, O'Connor, 2011) by replacing it with the FSUGold interaction, which better satisfies constraints from dilute Fermi gases and heavy-ion experiments. The new EOS tables are compared with the Lattimer-Swesty EOS (based on a compressible liquid drop model with Skyrme force) and the H. Shen et al. EOS (based on RMF with Thomas-Fermi approximation). The FSU2.1 variant addresses the recent observation of a 1.97 solar mass neutron star, which the original FSUGold interaction could not support. These tables enable more realistic simulations of supernovae, neutron star mergers, and black hole formation, and the differences between the FSU and NL3 EOS may help constrain nuclear matter properties through comparison with astrophysical observables.
