# Causal hydrodynamics from kinetic theory by doublet scheme in renormalization-group method

> We develop a general framework in the renormalization-group (RG) method for extracting a mesoscopic dynamics from an evolution equation by incorporating some excited (fast) modes as additional components to the invariant manifold spanned by zero modes. We call this framework the doublet scheme. The validity of the doublet scheme is first tested and demonstrated by taking the Lorenz model as a simple three-dimensional dynamical system; it is shown that the two-dimensional reduced dynamics on the attractive manifold composed of the would-be zero and a fast modes are successfully obtained in a natural way. We then apply the doublet scheme to construct causal hydrodynamics as a mesoscopic dynamics of kinetic theory, i.e., the Boltzmann equation, in a systematic manner with no ad-hoc assumption. It is found that our equation has the same form as thirteen-moment causal hydrodynamic equation, but the microscopic formulae of the transport coefficients and relaxation times are different. In fact, in contrast to the Grad equation, our equation leads to the same expressions for the transport coefficients as given by the Chapman-Enskog expansion method and suggests novel formulae of the relaxation times expressed in terms of relaxation functions which allow a natural physical interpretation of the relaxation times. Furthermore, our theory nicely gives the explicit forms of the distribution function and the thirteen hydrodynamic variables in terms of the linearized collision operator, which in turn clearly suggest the proper ansatz forms of them to be adopted in the method of moments.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the doublet scheme in the renormalization-group (RG) method can systematically derive causal hydrodynamics from the Boltzmann equation without ad-hoc assumptions, producing equations with the same form as Grad's thirteen-moment equation but with transport coefficients matching the Chapman-Enskog expansion and novel relaxation-time formulae expressed in terms of relaxation functions.

## Method

The authors first develop the doublet scheme as a general framework within the RG method for extracting mesoscopic dynamics from evolution equations by incorporating excited (fast) modes as additional components of the invariant manifold alongside zero modes. They test the scheme on the Lorenz model as a three-dimensional dynamical system, then apply it to the Boltzmann equation to construct causal hydrodynamic equations, deriving microscopic expressions for transport coefficients and relaxation times from the linearized collision operator without assuming specific functional forms for the distribution function.

## Key numbers

- The Lorenz model is reduced from three dimensions to a two-dimensional reduced dynamics on the attractive manifold composed of would-be zero and fast modes.
- The resulting causal hydrodynamic equation contains thirteen dynamical variables, matching the thirteen-moment approximation.
- The transport coefficients obtained from the doublet scheme are identical to those given by the Chapman-Enskog expansion method, in contrast to the Grad equation which gives different expressions.
- The relaxation times are expressed in terms of relaxation functions, providing a natural physical interpretation not present in the Grad equation.

## Context

This work extends the RG method previously applied by Hatta and the authors (2010) to derive the Navier-Stokes equation from the Boltzmann equation, now incorporating excited modes to capture mesoscopic dynamics beyond the slow hydrodynamic regime. The paper addresses the known inconsistency between Grad's thirteen-moment equation (Grad, 1949) and numerical solutions of the Boltzmann equation in the mesoscopic regime (Torrilhon, 2000), and provides a systematic alternative to the method of moments that avoids ad-hoc ansatz for the distribution function. The results enable construction of causal hydrodynamic equations that are consistent with the Boltzmann equation while preserving causality, which is relevant for applications in plasma physics and photon transport where the Navier-Stokes equation fails.
