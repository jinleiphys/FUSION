# Probing thermonuclear supernova explosions with neutrinos

> Aims: We present neutrino light curves and energy spectra for two representative type Ia supernova explosion models: a pure deflagration and a delayed detonation. Methods: We calculate the neutrino flux from $β$ processes using nuclear statistical equilibrium abundances convoluted with approximate neutrino spectra of the individual nuclei and the thermal neutrino spectrum (pair+plasma). Results: Although the two considered thermonuclear supernova explosion scenarios are expected to produce almost identical electromagnetic output, their neutrino signatures appear vastly different, which allow an unambiguous identification of the explosion mechanism: a pure deflagration produces a single peak in the neutrino light curve, while the addition of the second maximum characterizes a delayed-detonation. We identified the following main contributors to the neutrino signal: (1) weak electron neutrino emission from electron captures (in particular on the protons Co55 and Ni56) and numerous beta-active nuclei produced by the thermonuclear flame and/or detonation front, (2) electron antineutrinos from positron captures on neutrons, and (3) the thermal emission from pair annihilation. We estimate that a pure deflagration supernova explosion at a distance of 1 kpc would trigger about 14 events in the future 50 kt liquid scintillator detector and some 19 events in a 0.5 Mt water Cherenkov-type detector. Conclusions: While in contrast to core-collapse supernovae neutrinos carry only a very small fraction of the energy produced in the thermonuclear supernova explosion, the SN Ia neutrino signal provides information that allows us to unambiguously distinguish between different possible explosion scenarios. These studies will become feasible with the next generation of proposed neutrino observatories.

# Probing thermonuclear supernova explosions with neutrinos

## Key claim

Although pure deflagration and delayed-detonation type Ia supernova explosion models produce nearly identical electromagnetic output, their neutrino signatures are vastly different, allowing unambiguous identification of the explosion mechanism; however, the neutrino signal carries only a very small fraction of the total explosion energy, and detection will only become feasible with the next generation of proposed neutrino observatories.

## Method

The authors post-processed two representative multidimensional hydrodynamical explosion models (a pure deflagration and a delayed detonation) by computing neutrino emission from thermal processes (pair annihilation and plasmon decay) and weak nuclear processes (electron/positron captures and beta decays). For regions in nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE), isotopic abundances were calculated using an 800-isotope network, and neutrino spectra were computed using parameterized effective Q-values and rates from the Fuller-Fowler-Newman tabulations, with the electron fraction assumed to be 0.5.

## Key numbers

- Pure deflagration model (n7d1r10t15c): total neutrino energy radiated = 3.85 × 10^49 erg, which is 0.03 (3%) of the nuclear energy released; explosion energy ≈ 0.97 Bethe (1 Bethe = 10^51 erg)
- Delayed-detonation model (Y12): total neutrino energy radiated = 8.2 × 10^48 erg, which is 0.004 (0.4%) of the nuclear energy; explosion energy ≈ 1.36 Bethe
- Peak neutrino luminosity for pure deflagration: 1.92 × 10^50 erg/s, nearly one order of magnitude larger than the first-peak luminosity of the delayed-detonation model (1.1 × 10^49 erg/s)
- At peak emission in the pure deflagration, only 3% of the total white dwarf mass is emitting neutrinos; in the delayed-detonation detonation phase, approximately 30% of the white dwarf mass participates in neutrino emission
- A pure deflagration at 1 kpc would trigger about 14 events in a future 50 kt liquid scintillator detector and about 19 events in a 0.5 Mt water Cherenkov-type detector
- Average electron neutrino energy for pure deflagration: 3.8 MeV; for delayed-detonation total: 3.5 MeV
- Average electron antineutrino energy for pure deflagration: 2.9 MeV; for delayed-detonation total: 2.0 MeV

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Kunugise and Iwamoto (2006, 2007) on neutrino emission from the spherically symmetric W7 explosion model, and the original suggestion by Odrzywolek and collaborators (2004) that thermonuclear deflagrations produce a detectable neutrino signal. The authors show that pure deflagrations produce a single-peaked neutrino light curve with a generic form similar to the W7 model, while delayed detonations produce a characteristic double-peaked structure from the initial deflagration stage and the subsequent detonation. These results enable future neutrino observatories to distinguish between explosion mechanisms that are otherwise electromagnetically indistinguishable, though the low event rates require detectors of 50 kt (liquid scintillator) or 0.5 Mt (water Cherenkov) scale.
