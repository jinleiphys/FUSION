# On local and global equilibrium in heavy ion collisions

> The thermal model is commonly used in two different ways for the description of hadron production in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collision. One is the application of the thermal model to 4pi integrated data and the other is the thermal description of central dN/dy ratios. While the first method implicitly assumes global equilibrium the other scenario assumes Bjorken scaling within the investigated rapidity range. Both assumptions are only approximations for real physical collision systems. We study the impact of both approximations for the extraction of thermal parameters on the exemplary case of S+S collisions at SPS energies. The particle distributions are modeled by a hydrodynamical description of the relevant collision system.

## Key claim

The thermal model's two common approaches, fitting to 4pi integrated data (implicitly assuming global equilibrium) or to central dN/dy ratios (assuming Bjorken scaling), are only approximations for real collisions; for S+S at SPS energies, both methods yield reasonable thermal parameters despite the system being only in local equilibrium, but the choice between them depends on the amount of longitudinal flow.

## Method

The author performed a hydrodynamic simulation of S+S collisions at 200 A GeV with a freeze-out temperature of 140 MeV, using an equation of state with few hadronic resonances but then calculating particle spectra with a resonance spectrum up to 1.7 GeV. The resulting 4pi-integrated particle yields (which are in local but not global equilibrium) were fitted with a global thermal model (constant T, mu_B, mu_S) via chi-squared minimization. Separately, the same yields were integrated over finite rapidity intervals and fitted assuming Bjorken scaling, and the results were compared to two hypothetical cases: a globally equilibrated system with the same longitudinal flow, and a static fireball.

## Key numbers

- The average baryon chemical potential from the hydrodynamic simulation is ⟨μ_B⟩ = 193 MeV, with a spread of order 100 MeV around this average.
- The global fit to 4pi yields recovers a temperature nearly equal to the input 140 MeV and a fitted μ_B^fit = 205.7 MeV, which lies between ⟨μ_B⟩ = 193.2 MeV and the value from the average baryon fugacity (218.4 MeV).
- The average deviation of individual yields from the global fit is about 4%, with the largest deviations (about 10%) for anti-nucleons and the Omega baryon.
- For the static fireball with no flow, the extracted temperature and μ_B change considerably when going to smaller rapidity windows, with chi-squared increasing drastically.
- For the globally equilibrated case with longitudinal flow, the extracted thermal parameters remain rather constant even for the smallest rapidity window, with only a small increase in temperature and μ_B.

## Context

This work extends the ongoing debate on whether hadron production in heavy ion collisions is dominated by statistical phase-space filling (Fermi 1950, Becattini 1997) or by dynamical constraints. It directly addresses the validity of two common thermal-model analysis methods used by groups such as Cleymans, Braun-Munzinger, and Becattini: the global fit to 4pi data (Sollfrank 1994, Tiwari 1996, Becattini 1998) and the fit to central rapidity ratios (Davidson, Letessier, Cleymans, Andersen 1994, Braun-Munzinger, Panagiotou, Gorenstein, Spieles 1997, Sollfrank 1997a). The study uses a hydrodynamic simulation (Rischke 1998) to generate a realistic freeze-out surface, and compares its results to earlier work with the RQMD event generator (Sorge 1995, Sollfrank 1998b). The conclusion that at SPS energies enough longitudinal flow exists to justify the Bjorken scaling assumption enables future thermal analyses at RHIC and LHC to use central dN/dy ratios, while recommending 4pi analysis for lower energies at GSI.
