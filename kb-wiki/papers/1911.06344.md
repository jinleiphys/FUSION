# Primary fission fragment mass yields across the chart of nuclides

> We have calculated a complete set of primary fission fragment mass yields, $Y(A)$, for heavy nuclei across the chart of nuclides, including those of particular relevance to the rapid neutron capture process ($r$ process) of nucleosynthesis. We assume that the nuclear shape dynamics are strongly damped which allows for a description of the fission process via Brownian shape motion across nuclear potential-energy surfaces. The macroscopic energy of the potential was obtained with the Finite-Range Liquid-Drop Model (FRLDM), while the microscopic terms were extracted from the single-particle level spectra in the fissioning system by the Strutinsky procedure for the shell energies and the BCS treatment for the pairing energies. For each nucleus considered, the fission fragment mass yield, $Y(A)$, is obtained from 50,000 -- 500,000 random walks on the appropriate potential-energy surface. The full mass and charge yield, $Y(Z,A)$, is then calculated by invoking the Wahl systematics. With this method, we have calculated a comprehensive set of fission-fragment yields from over 3,800 nuclides bounded by $80\leq Z \leq 130$ and $A\leq330$; these yields are provided as an ASCII formatted database in the supplemental material. We compare our yields to known data and discuss general trends that emerge in low-energy fission yields across the chart of nuclides.

## Key claim

The authors calculate a complete set of primary fission fragment mass yields for over 3,800 nuclides by assuming the nuclear shape dynamics are strongly damped, which allows them to treat the fission process as Brownian shape motion on potential-energy surfaces; the key hedge is that this strong-damping assumption avoids the need for the collective inertial-mass tensor, which is otherwise poorly known and computationally prohibitive to calculate microscopically.

## Method

The authors employed the macroscopic-microscopic method using the Finite-Range Liquid-Drop Model (FRLDM) for the macroscopic energy and the Strutinsky procedure with BCS pairing for the microscopic shell and pairing corrections, all evaluated on a five-dimensional three-quadratic-surface (3QS) shape parameterization. For each of the 3,800+ nuclides, they performed 50,000 to 500,000 random walks on the corresponding potential-energy surface to obtain the primary fission fragment mass yield Y(A), then used the Wahl systematics to obtain the full mass and charge yield Y(Z,A). The shape evolution was treated in the strongly damped limit, where the Smoluchowski equation replaces the full Langevin equation, eliminating the need for the collective inertial-mass tensor.

## Key numbers

* Fission fragment yields were calculated for over 3,800 nuclides bounded by 80 ≤ Z ≤ 130 and A ≤ 330.
* For each nucleus, the mass yield Y(A) was obtained from 50,000 to 500,000 random walks on the potential-energy surface.
* The fragment mass resolution from the asymmetry step size is ΔA = 2.4 for ²⁴⁰Pu and ΔA ≈ 3 for the heaviest nuclei considered (A = 330).
* The five-dimensional shape lattice contains over ten million sites, with indices I=1,…,45 for quadrupole moment Q₂, J=1,…,15 for neck radius c, K,L=1,…,15 for endcap deformations ε from -0.2 to 0.5, and M=-33,…,33 for asymmetry.
* The macroscopic energy parameters include a_v = 16.02500 MeV, a_s = 21.33000 MeV, r_0 = 1.16 fm, and a = 0.68 fm, among others listed in the FRLDM parameter set.

## Context

This work extends the Brownian shape-motion approach previously developed by Randrup and Möller (2011, 2013) and Möller et al. (2015) from individual nuclei to a comprehensive survey across the chart of nuclides, covering the entire region relevant to the rapid neutron capture process (r-process) of nucleosynthesis. The macroscopic-microscopic method contrasts with fully microscopic approaches such as Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations, which are too computationally expensive for large-scale global studies (Schunck et al., 2016). The calculated yields enable improved modeling of r-process nucleosynthesis in astrophysical explosions and provide a systematic database for comparison with experimental data and phenomenological models such as those of Wahl (1988) and Brosa et al. (1990).
