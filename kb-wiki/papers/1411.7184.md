# Transport coefficients in second-order non-conformal viscous hydrodynamics

> Based on the exact solution of Boltzmann kinetic equation in the relaxation-time approximation, the precision of the two most recent formulations of relativistic second-order non-conformal viscous hydrodynamics (14-moment approximation and causal Chapman-Enskog method), standard Israel-Stewart theory, and anisotropic hydrodynamics framework, in the simple case of one-dimensional Bjorken expansion, is tested. It is demonstrated that the failure of Israel-Stewart theory in reproducing exact solutions of the Boltzmann kinetic equation occurs due to neglecting and/or choosing wrong forms of some of the second-order transport coefficients. In particular, the importance of shear--bulk couplings in the evolution equations for dissipative quantities is shown. One finds that, in the case of the bulk viscous pressure correction, such coupling terms are as important as the corresponding first-order Navier-Stokes term and must be included in order to obtain, at least qualitative, overall agreement with the kinetic theory.

## Key claim

The Israel-Stewart theory fails to reproduce exact solutions of the Boltzmann kinetic equation because it neglects or uses incorrect forms of certain second-order transport coefficients; in particular, shear–bulk coupling terms in the evolution equations for dissipative quantities are as important as the first-order Navier-Stokes term for the bulk viscous pressure correction and must be included to obtain at least qualitative agreement with kinetic theory.

## Method

The author compared four formulations of relativistic second-order viscous hydrodynamics (Israel-Stewart theory, 14-moment approximation, causal Chapman-Enskog method, and anisotropic hydrodynamics) against the exact solution of the Boltzmann kinetic equation in the relaxation-time approximation, restricted to the case of one-dimensional boost-invariant (Bjorken) expansion. The evolution equations for the bulk pressure correction and the shear-stress tensor were solved numerically for each formulation, and the resulting pressure anisotropy and bulk pressure correction were compared to the exact kinetic-theory solution.

## Key numbers

- The pressure anisotropy \( \mathcal{P}_L/\mathcal{P}_T \) and bulk pressure correction scaled by proper time were computed for all formulations and compared to the exact solution (Fig. 1 and Fig. 2); the exact solution is shown as red solid lines in both figures.
- Israel-Stewart theory (purple dashed lines) provided the poorest description of both shear and bulk viscous corrections relative to the exact solution.
- The 14-moment approximation without shear–bulk couplings (green dashed-dotted lines) failed to capture the bulk pressure correction, while inclusion of shear–bulk couplings (brown dotted lines) yielded at least qualitative agreement.
- The Chapman-Enskog method (blue dashed lines) provided the best overall description for the massive, non-conformal case.
- Anisotropic hydrodynamics (black dashed-dotted-dotted lines) described the bulk pressure correction at the same level as the 14-moment approximation but gave a better description of the pressure anisotropy.

## Context

This work extends prior exact solutions of the Boltzmann kinetic equation for Bjorken flow (Florkowski 2013, Florkowski 2014) and tests the accuracy of several dissipative hydrodynamics formulations against those exact solutions. It contradicts the common use of Israel-Stewart theory (Israel 1979) by showing that its neglect of shear–bulk couplings leads to significant deviations from kinetic theory. The results enable improved modeling of the quark-gluon plasma in heavy-ion collisions by identifying the Chapman-Enskog method (Jaiswal 2013) as the most accurate second-order non-conformal formulation, while also noting that anisotropic hydrodynamics (Nopoush 2014) performs best in the conformal limit.
