# What does really mean the "barrier distribution function" derived from backward angle quasi-elastic scattering

> The so called "barrier distribution" derived from quasi-elastic backscattering of heavy ions gives us, in fact, an information about the "reaction threshold distribution". For light nuclear systems these distributions are quite close, but for heavy ones (when interaction of nuclei becomes more and more complicated and the potential barrier can disappear at all) they may significantly differ. The same should hold also for weakly bound neutron rich projectiles, for which the neutron transfer and break-up reaction channels play an important role at near-barrier energies. This is not a terminology problem but important finding, because experiments on measurement of the backward angle quasi-elastic scattering for deriving the "barrier distributions" for heavy nuclear systems (which may be used for the production of superheavy elements) are planned to be performed in several laboratories.

# What does really mean the "barrier distribution function" derived from backward angle quasi-elastic scattering

## Key claim
The "barrier distribution" extracted from quasi-elastic backscattering actually reflects the "reaction threshold distribution" (the derivative of the total reaction probability), not the fusion barrier distribution. For heavy nuclear systems, these two distributions can differ significantly because deep inelastic scattering and other non-fusion reaction channels contribute to the total reaction probability, and for very heavy systems the potential barrier may disappear entirely.

## Method
The author re-examines the unitarity relation that connects quasi-elastic scattering to the total reaction probability, showing that for heavy systems the quasi-elastic reflection coefficient equals one minus the probability for all reaction channels (including deep inelastic scattering), not just fusion. The derivative of the quasi-elastic cross section therefore gives the distribution of reaction thresholds rather than fusion barrier heights. The analysis compares the formalism for light systems (where fusion dominates the reaction cross section) with heavy systems (where other channels become important).

## Key numbers
- For reactions of heavy projectiles (48Ti, 54Cr, 56Fe, 64Ni) on 208Pb, the centroids of derived barrier distributions are shifted to lower energy by 3–10 MeV compared to predicted barrier heights (Mitsuoka 2007).
- The same low-energy shift was observed for the reaction 86Kr + 208Pb (Ntshangase 2007).

## Context
This work extends the barrier distribution method introduced by Timmers (1995), which successfully derived fusion barrier distributions from quasi-elastic scattering for medium-mass systems like 16O + 154Sm. The paper corrects the interpretation for heavy systems, where the method was applied by Mitsuoka (2007) and Ntshangase (2007) to reactions relevant for superheavy element production. The finding warns that planned experiments using quasi-elastic scattering to extract barrier distributions for heavy nuclear combinations will instead measure reaction threshold distributions, which are systematically shifted to lower energies.
