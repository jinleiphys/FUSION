# Second-order hydrodynamics for fermionic cold atoms: Detailed analysis of transport coefficients and relaxation times

> We give a detailed derivation of the second-order (local) hydrodynamics for Boltzmann equation with an external force by using the renormalization group method. In this method, we solve the Boltzmann equation faithfully to extract the hydrodynamics without recourse to any ansatz. Our method leads to microscopic expressions of not only all the transport coefficients that are of the same form as those in Chapman-Enskog method but also those of the viscous relaxation times $τ_i$ that admit physically natural interpretations. As an example, we apply our microscopic expressions to calculate the transport coefficients and the relaxation times of the cold fermionic atoms in a quantitative way, where the transition probability in the collision term is given explicitly in terms of the $s$-wave scattering length $a_s$. We thereby discuss the quantum statistical effects, temperature dependence, and scattering-length dependence of the first-order transport coefficients and the viscous relaxation times: It is shown that as the temperature is lowered, the transport coefficients and the relaxation times increase rapidly because Pauli principle acts effectively. On the other hand, as $a_s$ is increased, these quantities decrease and become vanishingly small at unitarity because of the strong coupling. The numerical calculation shows that the relation $τ_π=η/P$, which is derived in the relaxation-time approximation and used in most of literature without almost any foundation, turns out to be satisfied quite well, while the similar relation for the relaxation time $τ_J$ of the heat conductivity is satisfied only approximately with a considerable error.

## Key claim

The paper derives second-order hydrodynamics from the Boltzmann equation using the renormalization group method without recourse to any ansatz, and shows that the relation τ_π = η/P (derived in the relaxation-time approximation) holds quite well numerically, while the analogous relation for the heat conductivity relaxation time τ_J is satisfied only approximately with considerable error.

## Method

The authors apply the renormalization group method to solve the Boltzmann equation for non-relativistic fermionic cold atoms with an external force, expanding the distribution function perturbatively in the Knudsen number. They identify slow variables (temperature, fluid velocity, chemical potential) and excited modes (stress tensor and heat flow) using a doublet scheme, then project the RG equation onto the zero-mode space and the excited-mode space to obtain the continuity equations and relaxation equations respectively. The microscopic expressions for transport coefficients and viscous relaxation times are converted to linear integral equations and solved numerically without approximation, using the s-wave scattering length a_s to specify the collision term.

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity η and heat conductivity λ are given by the Green-Kubo-type formulas: η = (1/10T) ∫₀^∞ ds ⟨π̂^{ij}(0), π̂^{ij}(s)⟩ and λ = (1/3T²) ∫₀^∞ ds ⟨Ĵ^{i}(0), Ĵ^{i}(s)⟩
- Viscous relaxation times are given by τ_π = [∫₀^∞ ds s ⟨π̂^{ij}(0), π̂^{ij}(s)⟩] / [∫₀^∞ ds ⟨π̂^{ij}(0), π̂^{ij}(s)⟩] and τ_J = [∫₀^∞ ds s ⟨Ĵ^{i}(0), Ĵ^{i}(s)⟩] / [∫₀^∞ ds ⟨Ĵ^{i}(0), Ĵ^{i}(s)⟩]
- The relation τ_π = η/P is satisfied quite well numerically, while the analogous relation for τ_J is satisfied only approximately with considerable error
- As temperature is lowered, transport coefficients and relaxation times increase rapidly due to Pauli blocking; as a_s increases toward unitarity, these quantities decrease and become vanishingly small

## Context

This work extends the renormalization group method for deriving hydrodynamics from kinetic theory, previously applied by Tsumura, Kunihiro, and collaborators (2013, 2015) to both relativistic and non-relativistic systems, by incorporating quantum statistics and external forces. The microscopic expressions for first-order transport coefficients coincide with those from the Chapman-Enskog method, while the viscous relaxation times are given novel microscopic expressions in terms of correlation functions. The numerical results enable quantitative comparison with the relaxation-time approximation widely used in the literature (Bruun 2007, Braby 2011, Chao 2012) and provide a foundation for applying second-order hydrodynamics to ultracold Fermi gases, where the shear viscosity approaches a quantum bound at unitarity as observed experimentally (Ohara 2002, Kinast 2004, Bartenstein 2004).
