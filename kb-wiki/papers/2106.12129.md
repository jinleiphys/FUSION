# Investigation of the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition in the static AMD

> Nuclear liquid-gas phase transitions are investigated in the framework of static antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (static AMD) model under either a constant volume or a constant pressure. A deuteron quadrupole momentum fluctuation thermometer is applied to extract the temperature of fragmenting systems of $^{36}$Ar and $^{100}$Sn. A plateau structure of caloric curves is observed under a constant volume for those system with a density $ρ\leq$ 0.03 fm$^{-3}$. A clear backbending in the caloric curves, which indicates a first order phase transition, is observed under a constant pressure with all pressures studied. The similar behavior of caloric curves of $^{36}$Ar and $^{100}$Sn systems indicates that there is no strong system size effect under a constant volume or a constant pressure. Both the mass distributions and the light particle multiplicities show a strong $α$ clusterization at low excitation energies in the static AMD simulations. The liquid-gas phase transition measures of the multiplicity derivative (dM/dT) and the normalized variance of $Z_{max}$ (NVZ) are applied. The experimental caloric curves are also compared with those of $^{100}$Sn of the static AMD simulations under both the constant volume and the constant pressure conditions. Discussions are presented with the available experimental results and those from the static AMD simulations. Large errors in the experimental temperature measurements and those in the reconstruction technique for the primary fragmenting source hinder to draw a conclusion whether the phase transition occurs under either a constant volume or a constant pressure. This study suggests that different measures for the liquid-gas phase transitions should be examined besides the caloric curves in order to draw a conclusion.

## Key claim

The paper investigates nuclear liquid-gas phase transitions using the static antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (static AMD) model, but concludes that large errors in experimental temperature measurements and in the reconstruction technique for the primary fragmenting source hinder a definitive conclusion on whether the phase transition occurs under constant volume or constant pressure conditions.

## Method

Static AMD simulations were performed for 36Ar and 100Sn systems at excitation energies from 2 to 25 MeV per nucleon, using container wall radii corresponding to densities from approximately 0.003 to 0.07 fm^-3. The microcanonical temperature was extracted from the average kinetic energy of nucleons in a gaseous subsystem, and a deuteron quadrupole momentum fluctuation thermometer was applied for comparison with experimental data. Caloric curves were constructed under both constant volume and constant pressure conditions, and the multiplicity derivative (dM/dT) and normalized variance of Z_max (NVZ) were examined as phase transition measures.

## Key numbers

- Excitation energy range: 2 MeV to 25 MeV per nucleon, with step of 0.5 MeV for both 36Ar and 100Sn
- Container wall radii: 5 fm to 14.5 fm for 36Ar (density from 0.00282 to 0.0688 fm^-3); 7 fm to 20 fm for 100Sn (density from 0.00296 to 0.0696 fm^-3)
- Decoherence parameter tau_0 = 5 fm/c used throughout
- Time evolution calculated up to 55000 fm/c for 36Ar and 25000 fm/c for 100Sn, with first 5000 fm/c discarded for thermal equilibrium
- Plateau structure in caloric curves observed under constant volume for densities rho <= 0.03 fm^-3
- Phase transition temperature approximately 6 MeV from the static AMD simulations for 100Sn
- Backbending in caloric curves observed under constant pressure for all pressures studied (0.01 to 0.20 MeV/fm^3)

## Context

This work extends previous studies using the statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) by Lin et al. (2018, 2019), where a constant volume was assumed, to the static AMD framework where fragments can be deformed in phase space. The results are compared with experimental caloric curves from Natowitz et al. (2002) for source mass ranges A = 60-100 and A = 100-140. The study suggests that multiple measures for liquid-gas phase transitions should be examined alongside caloric curves, and that the static AMD results may help distinguish whether the phase transition occurs under constant volume or constant pressure conditions in heavy-ion collisions.
