# Chaos in fermionic many-body systems and the metal-insulator transition

> We show that finite Fermi systems governed by a mean field and a few-body interaction generically possess spectral fluctuations of the Wigner-Dyson type and are, thus, chaotic. Our argument is based on an analogy to the metal-insulator transition. We construct a sparse random-matrix ensemble ScE that mimics that transition. Our claim then follows from the fact that the generic random-matrix ensemble modeling a fermionic interacting many-body system is much less sparse than ScE.

## Key claim

Finite Fermi systems governed by a mean field and a few-body interaction generically possess spectral fluctuations of the Wigner-Dyson type and are thus chaotic, provided the interaction is at least two-body (k >= 2); the argument relies on an analogy to the metal-insulator transition and the construction of a sparse random-matrix ensemble (ScE) that is even sparser than the generic embedded ensemble (EGOE(k)), showing that EGOE(k) lies on the metallic side of the transition.

## Method

The authors construct a sparse random-matrix ensemble called the scaffolding ensemble (ScE), defined by a recursively built matrix A^(n) of dimension N = 2^n with exactly n = ln N / ln 2 nonzero off-diagonal elements per row, and compare its spectral properties to those of the Embedded Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (EGOE(k)) for k-body interactions. They apply Levitov's criterion (based on the sum S of off-diagonal variances relative to diagonal variances) to both ScE and EGOE(k), and perform numerical diagonalizations of ScE for n = 10 to 13 with varying diagonal strength alpha to extract nearest-neighbor spacing distributions, the Sigma^(2) statistic, and inverse participation ratio (IPR) distributions.

## Key numbers

- For ScE with n = 13 and alpha = 17, the fraction of nonzero matrix elements per row is less than 2 x 10^(-3).
- For EGOE(2) with matrices of similar dimension (N ~ 2^13), the fraction of nonzero matrix elements per row is about two orders of magnitude larger than for ScE (i.e., roughly 0.2).
- The fractal dimension D2 extracted from the IPR distribution of ScE at alpha = 17 is approximately 0.85.
- The IPR distribution for ScE at alpha = 17 exhibits a power-law tail with exponent x2 approximately 1.5.
- For ScE, the average spectrum has a semicircular shape with radius lambda^(sc) = sqrt(n + alpha), approximately sqrt(n) for n >> alpha.
- For EGOE(k) under neglect of correlations, the semicircle radius squared is (lambda^(EGOE(k)))^2 = 2 * C(m,k) + sum_{p=1}^k C(m-p, k-p) * C(m,p) * C(l-m, p), where C denotes binomial coefficients.

## Context

This work extends the line of inquiry into whether two-body interactions generically produce chaos in fermionic many-body systems, a question previously addressed by Jacquod and Shepelyansky (1997) using perturbative and numerical methods for random one- and two-body interactions. The paper introduces a novel analogy to the metal-insulator transition (MIT) and the power-law random band matrix (PLRBM) ensemble of Mirlin et al. (1996), and applies Levitov's criterion (1990) to sparse random matrices for the first time. The results enable a general argument that EGOE(k) for k >= 2 lies on the delocalized side of the MIT, implying Wigner-Dyson statistics for finite Fermi systems with few-body interactions, and they clarify the separate roles of integrability and correlations between matrix elements.
