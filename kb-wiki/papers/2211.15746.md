# Precision Studies of QCD in the Low Energy Domain of the EIC

> The manuscript focuses on the high impact science of the EIC with objective to identify a portion of the science program for QCD precision studies that requires or greatly benefits from high luminosity and low center-of-mass energies. The science topics include (1) Generalized Parton Distributions, 3D imagining and mechanical properties of the nucleon (2) mass and spin of the nucleon (3) Momentum dependence of the nucleon in semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering (4) Exotic meson spectroscopy (5) Science highlights of nuclei (6) Precision studies of Lattice QCD in the EIC era (7) Science of far-forward particle detection (8) Radiative effects and corrections (9) Artificial Intelligence (10) EIC interaction regions for high impact science program with discovery potential. This paper documents the scientific basis for supporting such a program and helps to define the path toward the realization of the second EIC interaction region.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a second interaction region at the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC), optimized for high luminosity at low to medium center-of-mass energies (20 to 60 GeV), is essential for precision QCD studies including 3D imaging of nucleons, mechanical properties, and exotic meson spectroscopy, but this program specifically requires instantaneous luminosity from 0.5×10^34 to 1.0×10^34 cm^-2 s^-1 in the 45 to 100 GeV range, with significantly lower luminosity at 28 and 140 GeV.

## Method

The authors organized two workshops (December 2020 and March-July 2021) with over 400 international participants to identify science topics that benefit from high luminosity at low to medium center-of-mass energies, then formed technical working groups to develop this white paper documenting the scientific basis for a second EIC interaction region at IP8. The document reviews ten science topics including generalized parton distributions (GPDs), gravitational form factors, transverse momentum dependent parton distributions (TMDs), exotic meson spectroscopy, nuclear structure, lattice QCD, far-forward detection, radiative corrections, artificial intelligence applications, and interaction region design.

## Key numbers

- Instantaneous luminosity from 0.5×10^34 cm^-2 s^-1 up to 1.0×10^34 cm^-2 s^-1 achievable in the center-of-mass range of 45 to 100 GeV
- Maximal integrated luminosity of 100 fb^-1 annually based on 10^7 seconds of operation
- Center-of-mass energy range of 20 to 140 GeV for the full EIC, with low-to-medium range of 20 to 60 GeV emphasized for precision studies
- Electron beam energy range from 2.5 GeV to 18 GeV, proton beam energies between 41 GeV and 275 GeV
- Both electron and proton beam polarizations greater than 70%
- Crossing angle of 25 mrad for the primary interaction region
- Peak luminosity of L=10^34 cm^-2 s^-1 at a center-of-mass energy of 105 GeV for the primary IR design
- Maximum accelerator optical β-functions in final focus quadrupole magnets of β_max ≤ 1800 m for protons in the vertical direction
- Intra-beam scattering growth times of τ_IBS > 2 hours
- 1160 colliding bunches in each beam for the primary IR design
- Second focus approximately 50 m downstream of the collision point at IP8

## Context

This white paper extends the science case established in prior EIC documents including the EIC Conceptual Design Report and the work of Accardi (2012) and Abdul Khalek (2021), specifically focusing on the need for a second interaction region optimized for precision QCD studies at low to medium center-of-mass energies. The document builds on results from Jefferson Laboratory's 12 GeV CEBAF facility in the valence quark region and aims to connect that kinematic range to sea quark and gluon dominated regions at medium and small x_B values from 0.01 to 0.1. The paper enables future planning for the second EIC detector at IP8, including a high-resolution forward ion spectrometer and specialized instrumentation for far-forward particle detection, and identifies artificial intelligence methods as a cross-cutting tool for detector design, simulation acceleration, and extraction of Compton form factors from experimental data.
