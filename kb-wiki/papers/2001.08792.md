# Characterizing $r$-Process Sites through Actinide Production

> Of the variations in the elemental abundance patterns of stars enhanced with $r$-process elements, the variation in the relative actinide-to-lanthanide ratio is among the most significant. We investigate the source of these actinide differences in order to determine whether these variations are due to natural differences in astrophysical sites, or due to the uncertain nuclear properties that are accessed in $r$-process sites. We find that variations between relative stellar actinide abundances is most likely astrophysical in nature, owing to how neutron-rich the ejecta from an $r$-process event may be. Furthermore, if an $r$-process site is capable of generating variations in the neutron-richness of its ejected material, then only one type of $r$-process site is needed to explain all levels of observed relative actinide enhancements.

## Key claim

The paper finds that variations in the relative actinide-to-lanthanide abundances observed in r-process enhanced stars are most likely astrophysical in nature, specifically due to differences in how neutron-rich the ejecta from an r-process event may be, rather than due to uncertain nuclear properties. The authors further claim that if a single r-process site can generate variations in the neutron-richness of its ejected material, then only one type of r-process site is needed to explain all observed levels of relative actinide enhancements.

## Method

The authors ran nucleosynthesis simulations using the PRISM network code with a neutron-star merger (NSM) trajectory at an initial electron fraction of Ye = 0.035, testing four variations on nuclear input (FRDM2012 masses, Duflo-Zuker masses, Kodama and Takahashi fission fragment distributions, and Marketin beta-decay rates). They then varied the initial electron fraction Ye from 0.005 to 0.250 in steps of 0.005 across 50 simulations per nuclear input case, and developed an "Actinide-Dilution" (AD) model using a double-Gaussian distribution of Ye representing dynamical ejecta and disk wind components, followed by an "Actinide-Dilution with Matching" (ADM) model that randomly selects 15 Ye values and tests whether the resulting Zr/Dy, Th/Dy, and U/Th ratios match observational constraints from the actinide-boost star J0954+5246.

## Key numbers

* The initial electron fraction Ye = 0.035 from the Rosswog NSM simulation was used as the baseline trajectory for nucleosynthesis simulations.
* Only one Ye value, approximately 0.17, produced consistent ages for the actinide-boost star J0954+5246, yielding an age of 11 Gyr (10 Gyr plus the 1 Gyr end-time of the simulations).
* The mass ratio between wind and dynamical ejecta components was set to m_w/m_dyn = 3, based on estimates from GW170817.
* The ADM model required all three abundance ratio constraints (Zr/Dy, Th/Dy, U/Th) to be within about 0.2 dex of the input observational values.
* The difference between an actinide-poor case (log(Th/Dy) < -1.20) and an actinide-boost case (log(Th/Dy) > -0.90) requires only about 15% more ejecta material at very low Ye values.
* The U/Th age from the Marketin beta-decay case gave an age of about 17 Gyr, much greater than the age of the Universe.

## Context

This work extends studies of r-process enhanced stars and the actinide-boost phenomenon, which was previously observed in about 30% of r-process enhanced stars. The authors build on prior work by Wanajo (2002) and Farouqi (2010) who provided initial production ratios from r-process waiting point calculations and supernova simulations, and on the GW170817 event which confirmed neutron-star mergers as an r-process site (Abbott 2017). The paper enables future studies to use the ADM model to characterize the ejecta distribution of individual r-process events from stellar abundance patterns, potentially distinguishing between different merger scenarios without requiring multiple distinct r-process site types.
