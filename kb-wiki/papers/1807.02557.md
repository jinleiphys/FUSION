# The Elasticity of Nuclear Pasta

> The elastic properties of neutron star crusts are relevant for a variety of currently observable or near-future electromagnetic and gravitational wave phenomena. These phenomena may depend on the elastic properties of nuclear pasta found in the inner crust. We present large scale classical molecular dynamics simulations where we deform nuclear pasta. We simulate idealized samples of nuclear pasta and describe their breaking mechanism. We also deform nuclear pasta that is arranged into many domains, similar to what is known for the ions in neutron star crusts. Our results show that nuclear pasta may be the strongest known material, perhaps with a shear modulus of $10^{30}\,\text{ergs/cm}^3$ and breaking strain greater than 0.1.

# The Elasticity of Nuclear Pasta

## Key claim

Nuclear pasta may be the strongest known material, with a shear modulus possibly as large as $10^{31}\,\text{ergs/cm}^3$ and breaking strain greater than 0.1, but these results are model-dependent and use proton fractions much larger than those expected in the inner crust of neutron stars; a lower proton fraction likely reduces the shear modulus, perhaps to $10^{30}\,\text{ergs/cm}^3$.

## Method

The authors performed large-scale classical molecular dynamics simulations using the Indiana University Molecular Dynamics code (IUMD) to deform idealized samples of nuclear pasta in the lasagna and waffle phases, applying volume-preserving tensile strains at constant strain rates. They simulated both single-domain perfect plates and a large multi-domain system of 3,276,800 nucleons at proton fraction $Y_P = 0.3$, which formed seven differently oriented domains connected by filamentary defects. Stresses were calculated from the nucleon positions, velocities, and interparticle forces during deformation.

## Key numbers

- Shear modulus from helicoidal defects connecting inclined plates: $5 \times 10^{30}\,\text{erg/cm}^3$, with a maximum shear stress of $3 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{MeV/fm}^3$ at an estimated shear strain of 0.1
- Breaking strain of idealized lasagna plates in the lasagna and waffle phases: approximately $\epsilon \approx 0.3$
- Plates pulled apart along the $z$ direction deformed continuously without breaking up to a strain $\epsilon_z = 1$
- Plates strained along the $x$ axis broke at $\epsilon_x \approx 0.3$ (first event) and $\epsilon_x \approx 0.8$ (second event)
- Inclined plates with helicoidal defects broke at $\epsilon_y \approx 0.2$
- Inclined plates without defects broke near $\epsilon_z \approx 0.68$
- Multi-domain simulation strained to $\epsilon_x = 0.25$ showed no breaking, with stresses growing linearly until $\epsilon_x \sim 0.12$
- Comparison shear modulus for ion crust at pasta densities using the effective shear modulus of Horowitz and Hughto: $\mu = 1.1 \times 10^{30}\,\text{erg/cm}^3$
- Simulations performed at temperature $T = 1$ MeV and nucleon number density $n = 0.05\,\text{fm}^{-3}$

## Context

This work extends the analytic model of Pethick and Potekhin (1998), who first studied the elasticity of nuclear pasta and predicted that disconnected plates have near zero shear modulus between plates. The simulations confirm this qualitative behavior but also show that helicoidal defects (filaments connecting plates) can support shear stresses, producing a nonzero shear modulus. The multi-domain simulation, the largest of nuclear pasta to date, suggests that domain boundaries allow nucleons to flow freely between adjacent domains, preventing catastrophic failure for realistic astrophysical strains. This motivates further work on pasta elasticity, particularly the role of superfluidity, and suggests that neutron stars may support large buried mountains in the inner crust that could be sources of continuous gravitational waves for LIGO and Virgo.
