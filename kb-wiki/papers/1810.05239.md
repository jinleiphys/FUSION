# Ernest Henley's Isospin and the Ensuing Progress

> Ernest Henley's contributions to understanding isospin symmetry and the experimental and theoretical progress that followed are reviewed. Many experimentalists and theorists worked to bring Ernest's early vision of the subject to fruition. This progress and wide-ranging impact is described.

## Key claim

Ernest Henley established isospin as an approximate symmetry of the strong interaction, coining the term "approximate symmetry" to denote a symmetry slightly broken by small effects, and organized the nuclear force into distinct classes of charge independence and charge symmetry breaking that remain the standard classification.

## Method

The paper reviews Henley's 1970 foundational work on isospin symmetry and the subsequent experimental and theoretical program that followed, including measurements of charge symmetry breaking in nucleon-nucleon scattering (analyzing power differences in np elastic scattering), the forbidden reaction dd → απ⁰, and the forward-backward asymmetry in np → dπ⁰. The review also discusses the QCD basis of isospin breaking through the up-down quark mass difference and electromagnetic effects, and the application of effective field theory to understand these phenomena.

## Key numbers

- The nn interaction is more attractive than the np one in the ¹S₀ state, established through measurements of π⁻+D → nn+γ
- Total cross section for dd → απ⁰: 12.7 ± 2.2 pb at 228.5 MeV and 15.1 ± 3.1 pb at 231.8 MeV (IUCF measurement)
- The ratio ε ≡ (m_u−m_d)/(m_u+m_d) ∼ 1/3 from meson masses
- Charge symmetry breaking corrections to nucleon electromagnetic form factors are of order 2×10⁻³ (Miller 1997) or between 0.01 and 0.04 (Kubis and Lewis 2006) for the magnetic form factor
- The influence of up-down quark mass difference on the n-p mass difference is about 2.5 times larger in magnitude than electromagnetic effects (lattice QCD calculation by Borsanyi et al. 2014)
- NuTeV anomaly: the charge symmetry breaking correction to the Paschos-Wolfenstein relation is about 50% of the discrepancy with other determinations of the Weinberg angle

## Context

This review extends Henley's (1970) foundational work on isospin symmetry and the subsequent Henley and Miller (1977) classification of nucleon-nucleon forces into Classes I-IV. It describes how experimental programs at TRIUMF and IUCF tested charge symmetry breaking predictions, and how modern effective field theory approaches (Weinberg 1978, van Kolck 1999) and lattice QCD (Borsanyi et al. 2014) now provide a framework for understanding isospin breaking from the up-down quark mass difference. The work enables future studies of charge symmetry breaking in parity-violating electron scattering and improved tests of the CKM matrix unitarity through superallowed Fermi beta decay.
