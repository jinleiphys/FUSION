# Froissart Bounds for Amplitudes and Cross Sections at High Energies

## Key claim
Violation of the familiar log-squared bound on total cross sections does not necessarily violate unitarity (contrary to common opinion), but instead corresponds to a rapid high-energy increase of the amplitude in nonphysical regions. The canonical Froissart bound of \(\ln^2 s\) requires an additional, unproven hypothesis that the amplitude in every nonphysical configuration cannot grow faster than some finite power of energy.

## Method
The paper revisits the derivation of the Froissart theorem by examining the analytic properties of elastic scattering amplitudes combined with unitarity in both the scattering channel and the cross channel. It uses the asymptotic behavior of Legendre functions \(P_l(z)\) at large \(l\) to show that the rate of high-energy increase is much more moderate for physical angles than for nonphysical ones, and that the familiar bound emerges only when one imposes an extra power-boundedness assumption on nonphysical configurations.

## Key numbers
- Existing data for different hadrons agree with the hypothesis that total cross sections asymptotically grow as \(\ln^2 s\) (where \(s\) is the c.m.s. energy squared).
- A "heretic" description of total cross sections corresponds to a power increase as \(s^{\delta}\) with a small exponent \(\delta \approx 0.08\).
- Analysis of accelerator data for \(pp\) and \(p\bar{p}\) scattering assuming asymptotic behavior \((\ln s)^\alpha\) with free parameter \(\alpha\): earlier data agree with \(\alpha = 2\), but addition of first LHC data provides a statistically meaningful excess \(\alpha > 2\).

## Context
This work extends the original Froissart theorem (Froissart, 1961) by clarifying that the log-squared bound is not a direct consequence of unitarity alone, but requires an additional hypothesis about power boundedness in nonphysical regions. It also builds on the earlier demonstration by Gribov (1961) that classical diffraction is incompatible with analyticity and cross-channel unitarity. The analysis enables future LHC data to test whether the present increase of total cross sections is saturated, and suggests that if the total cross section grows with the maximal possible rate, its ratio to the diffraction slope should stay constant or decrease.
