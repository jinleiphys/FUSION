# Evidence of Nuclear Urca Process in the Ocean of Neutron-Star Superburst MAXI J1752$-$457

> We propose that the rapid cooling of the neutron star following its X-ray superburst in MAXI J1752$-$457 over a period of 4 days, observed by two Japanese satellites, MAXI and NinjaSat, is due to enhanced neutrino emission from cycles of electron capture and $β^{-}$ decay involving odd-$A$ nuclei (or Urca pairs) in the ocean. Hence, this work provides the first indication of the possible existence of such a ``nuclear Urca process". The observation of MAXI J1752$-$457 implies a hot ignition layer with a maximum temperature of $\sim4~{\rm GK}$, located near the Urca shell in the ocean, such that the nuclear Urca process becomes dominant for up to $\sim2$ days after the superburst. This behavior is distinct from that of normal Type-I X-ray bursts, which are triggered by hydrogen or helium burning in much shallower layers than those of superbursts. Our findings enable probing of superburst ashes through Urca pairs via long-term monitoring of crust cooling on day-long timescales.

## Key claim

The rapid early-phase cooling of the neutron star following the X-ray superburst in MAXI J1752$-$457, observed over 4 days, is proposed to be caused by enhanced neutrino emission from nuclear Urca cycles (electron capture and beta-minus decay involving odd-A nuclei) in the ocean; this provides the first indication of the possible existence of such a "nuclear Urca process," but the claim is conditional on the hot ignition layer reaching a maximum temperature of approximately 4 GK located near the Urca shell.

## Method

The authors constructed thermal evolution models by solving the general relativistic heat-diffusion equation using the dSTAR code, following the methodology of a companion paper. They adopted a phenomenological treatment where superburst ignition occurs at pressures below the carbon ignition pressure with uniformly distributed energy release, modeled as a 0.1-day accretion episode before cooling onset. A Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) approach with the emcee sampler was used to explore the parameter space of neutron-star core mass and radius, while fixing the heating strength and ignition pressure from observational constraints, and comparing the resulting cooling curves to the MAXI and NinjaSat observations.

## Key numbers

- The observed power-law indices for the cooling flux are alpha_f = 0.9 +/- 0.2 (first epoch) and alpha_s = 1.7 +/- 0.7 (second epoch), indicating faster early-time cooling than the standard values of 0.2 and 4/3.
- The diffusion timescale for ocean Urca cooling is estimated as approximately 2.0 days for a depth of 100 m, density of 10^8 g cm^-3, electron fraction of 0.5, and atomic number Z=26.
- Urca cooling can dominate when the neutrino luminosity exceeds roughly 10^37 erg s^-1, corresponding to a temperature T_9 greater than approximately 3.47 for a total Urca cooling strength sum_i X_i L_{34,i} = 2 and surface gravity g_14 = 2.
- The ocean Urca cooling was represented by two sources at log10(P/[dyn cm^-2]) = 26 and 26.5, corresponding to depths of 72 m and 96 m, with cooling strengths X_1 L_{34,1} = 2.4 and X_2 L_{34,2} = 0.8, giving a total of 3.2 (compared to 1.6 estimated by Deibel (2016) for superburst ashes, with a factor-of-two uncertainty from nuclear physics).
- The best-fit model used a heating strength E = 3.39 x 10^17 erg and ignition pressure P_ign = 5.0 x 10^26 dyn cm^-2, with a neutron-star core mass of 1.18 solar masses and core radius of 7.96 km.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of nuclear Urca cooling in accreting neutron stars, first proposed by Schatz (2014) and systematically studied by Deibel (2015, 2016) who identified 85 possible Urca pairs and their cooling effects on sources like MAXI J0556-332. The finding provides the first observational evidence for the operation of the nuclear Urca process in the ocean, distinct from normal Type-I X-ray bursts triggered by hydrogen or helium burning in shallower layers, and enables probing of superburst ashes through Urca pairs via long-term monitoring of crust cooling on day-long timescales.
