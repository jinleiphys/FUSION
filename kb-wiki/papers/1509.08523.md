# Nucleon Resonance Transition Formfactors

> We discuss recent results from CLAS on electromagnetic resonance transition amplitudes and their dependence on the distance scale ($Q^2$). From the comparison of these results with most advanced theoretical calculations within QCD-based approaches there is clear evidence that meson-baryon contributions are present and important at large distances, i.e. small $Q^2$, and that quark core contributions dominate the short distance behavior.

## Key claim

The paper argues that electromagnetic resonance transition amplitudes measured by CLAS provide clear evidence that both meson-baryon contributions (dominant at large distances, i.e., small $Q^2$) and quark core contributions (dominant at short distances, i.e., high $Q^2$) are present and important, with the caveat that the relative strength of these components varies significantly across different resonances and that the quark core alone often cannot explain the full measured strength at low $Q^2$.

## Method

The authors analyzed exclusive meson electroproduction data from CLAS at Jefferson Lab, extracting electromagnetic transition amplitudes (electrocouplings) for nucleon resonances over a wide range of $Q^2$ (from the photon point to about 7.0 GeV$^2$). These experimental amplitudes were compared with theoretical calculations from light-front relativistic quark models (LF RQM), dynamical coupled-channel models (DCCM), QCD-based Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSE/QCD), and light-cone sum rules (LC SR) to disentangle the contributions from three-quark (qqq) components and meson-baryon (higher Fock state) components.

## Key numbers

- For the $N\Delta(1232)$ transition, at the photon point ($Q^2 = 0$) more than 50% of the magnetic transition form factor strength may be due to non-quark contributions, while at $Q^2 \ge 3$ GeV$^2$ the quark content becomes the biggest contributor.
- The Roper $N(1440){1\over 2}^+$ resonance: the dressed pole mass from coupled-channel calculations moved from a bare value of 1.736 GeV to 1.365 GeV (a shift of over 350 MeV), close to the experimentally observed mass.
- For the $N(1535){1\over 2}^-$ state, the LF RQM and LC SR (NLO) calculations describe the transverse amplitude $A_{1/2}$ well for $Q^2 \ge 1.5$ GeV$^2$, and the scalar amplitude $S_{1/2}$ is well described by LC SR (NLO) at $Q^2 \ge 1.5$ GeV$^2$.
- For the $N(1675){5\over 2}^-$ state, the 3-quark transition amplitude from the proton is suppressed ($A^q_{1/2} = A^q_{3/2} = 0$), allowing direct access to non-quark components; the extracted amplitudes show considerable coupling through $A_{1/2}$ and much smaller $A_{3/2}$ at $Q^2 \ge 1.8$ GeV$^2$.
- For the $N(1680){5\over 2}^+$ state, the $A_{3/2}$ amplitude dominates at $Q^2=0$ but drops rapidly with $Q^2$, while $A_{1/2}$ becomes the leading amplitude at larger $Q^2$, with the change less rapid than predicted by quark models.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing effort to understand nucleon resonance structure using electromagnetic probes, building on the quark model predictions of Gell-Mann (1964) and Zweig (1964) and addressing the puzzle of "missing" states. The results contradict earlier non-relativistic constituent quark model (nrCQM) predictions for the Roper resonance (which had the wrong sign and magnitude of the transverse amplitude) and support interpretations of the Roper as the first radial excitation of the nucleon, resolving decades-old discrepancies. The findings enable future extraction of transition charge densities in the light front (following Tiator, 2008) and provide a pathway to relate resonance electrocouplings to first-principles QCD calculations via DSE/QCD (Segovia, 2015) and NLO LCSR (Anikin, 2015).
