# Reconstructing parton distribution functions from Ioffe time data: from Bayesian methods to Neural Networks

> The computation of the parton distribution functions (PDF) or distribution amplitudes (DA) of hadrons from first principles lattice QCD constitutes a central open problem. In this study, we present and evaluate the efficiency of a selection of methods for inverse problems to reconstruct the full $x$-dependence of PDFs. Our starting point are the so called Ioffe time PDFs, which are accessible from Euclidean time calculations in conjunction with a matching procedure. Using realistic mock data tests, we find that the ill-posed incomplete Fourier transform underlying the reconstruction requires careful regularization, for which both the Bayesian approach as well as neural networks are efficient and flexible choices.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that reconstructing the full x-dependence of parton distribution functions (PDFs) from Ioffe time data requires careful regularization because the underlying incomplete Fourier transform is ill-posed, and both Bayesian methods and neural networks provide efficient and flexible regularization choices, but only when the Ioffe time range is realistically limited (e.g., ν ∈ [0,20]) and data precision is at the level of 10⁻² to 10⁻³ relative error.

## Method

The authors generate realistic mock data for the Ioffe time PDF Q_R(ν, μ²) using two scenarios: scenario A uses experimentally motivated PDFs with a divergence at x=0 (a<0), and scenario B uses a deformed PDF that goes to zero at the origin (a>0). They then test three advanced reconstruction methods, the Backus-Gilbert method, neural network parametrization, and Bayesian inference, to invert the cosine transform Q_R(ν) = ∫₀¹ dx cos(νx) q_v(x) and recover the valence quark PDF q_v(x). Preconditioning is applied by rescaling the kernel with a phenomenological fit function p(x) to make the remainder h(x) = q_v(x)/p(x) slowly varying.

## Key numbers

- For the direct inversion method, when ν ∈ [0,20] (a realistic lattice range), even with relative errors as small as ΔQ_R/Q_R = 10⁻⁶, the reconstruction shows significant unphysical fluctuations.
- For ν ∈ [0,100], the direct inversion requires ΔQ_R/Q_R = 10⁻⁵ for a reasonably stable reconstruction.
- For ν ∈ [0,40π] (the full Brillouin zone), the direct inversion works with ΔQ_R/Q_R = 10⁻² and produces accurate results except at x=0.
- The trapezoid integration rule for the cosine transform reaches 100% relative error at ν=15 with 10 interpolating points, while the improved linear interpolation integration achieves 65% relative error at ν=15 that remains almost constant up to ν=100.
- The mock data tests use N_ν = 40 data points for Q_R over the Ioffe time intervals I₀=[0,10], I₁=[0,20], I₂=[0,100], and I₃=[0,40π].

## Context

This work extends the pseudo-PDF formalism of Radyushkin (2017) and the quasi-PDF formalism of Ji (2013) by addressing the practical numerical challenge of inverting the Fourier transform that connects lattice-calculated matrix elements to PDFs. It directly responds to concerns raised by Rossi and Testa (2017, 2018) about the inversion problem, which were refuted theoretically by Ji (2017), Radyushkin (2018), and Karpie (2018), but here the authors show that the numerical inversion is genuinely ill-posed and requires regularization. The study enables future lattice QCD calculations to reliably extract PDFs from Ioffe time data with controlled systematic uncertainties, and its conclusions also apply to quasi-PDF reconstruction from lattice matrix elements.
