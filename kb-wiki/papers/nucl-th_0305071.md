# A comparison of statistical hadronization models

> We investigate the sensitivity of fits of hadron spectra produced in heavy ion collisions to the choice of statistical hadronization model. We start by giving an overview of statistical model ambiguities, and what they tell us about freeze-out dynamics. We then use Montecarlo generated data to determine sensitivity to model choice. We fit the statistical hadronization models under consideration to RHIC data, and find that a comparison $χ^2$ fits can shed light on some presently contentious questions.

# A comparison of statistical hadronization models

## Key claim

The choice of statistical hadronization model (including resonance treatment, normalization scheme, freeze-out geometry, and flow profile) can shift fitted freeze-out temperatures by as much as 20 MeV, and while RHIC data slightly favors a chemical non-equilibrium explosive freeze-out scenario, there is not enough evidence to make a definitive conclusion about this issue.

## Method

The authors generated Monte Carlo data samples of hadron spectra (pions, kaons, protons, antiprotons, hyperons, and their resonances) using three different freeze-out geometries and flow profiles, all with a fixed input temperature of 140 MeV, maximum transverse flow of 0.55, and out-of-equilibrium chemistry parameters. They then fitted these samples with various statistical hadronization models (differing in resonance inclusion, normalization via particle-specific parameters versus chemical potentials, inclusion of non-equilibrium phase space occupancy factors, and freeze-out geometry) and compared the resulting chi-squared profiles. Finally, they applied the same fitting procedure to RHIC data at sqrt(s_NN) = 130 GeV.

## Key numbers

- Fitted freeze-out temperatures in the literature range from as low as 110 MeV to as high as 170 MeV, depending on model choice.
- Inclusion or exclusion of resonance feed-down can shift the fitted temperature by as much as 20 MeV.
- Monte Carlo data were generated with temperature = 140 MeV, maximum transverse flow = 0.55, gamma_q = 1.4, and gamma_s/gamma_q = 0.8.
- A power-law component contributing roughly 6% of the total pion yield was needed to fully account for the lowest momentum pions in RHIC data.
- The fit to RHIC data used two different system volumes, one for STAR particles and another for PHENIX particles, due to different trigger requirements and acceptance regions.

## Context

This work extends the ongoing debate over statistical hadronization model fits to heavy ion collision data, where fitted temperatures have varied considerably (110-170 MeV) across different groups such as Rafelski (2002), the PBM group (1999, 2001), and others. The paper systematically compares the Cooper-Frye freeze-out prescription with different treatments of resonance decays, normalization schemes (particle-specific versus chemical potentials with flavor phase space occupancy factors), and freeze-out geometries (constant lab time, fitted hypersurface slope, and proper-time freeze-out). The results enable future studies to use chi-squared comparisons between models to discriminate among freeze-out scenarios, and the authors suggest that a greater sample of heavy resonance spectra taken within the same centrality bin as light particles would help establish whether chemical potentials are a good normalization method.
