# Understanding Charge Radii with Machine Learning: Discovering Physics Expressions

> We introduce a robust, interpretable machine learning (ML) framework that combines numerical regression for high-accuracy predictions with symbolic regression to uncover the underlying physics. This hybrid approach effciently derives analytical expressions by leveraging the smoothed predictions of optimized ML models, a significant acceleration over direct symbolic regression on raw experimental data. We apply this framework, as an example, to nuclear charge radii across the nuclear chart, notably including light nuclei that are often excluded from such studies. We employ Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LGBM) and Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) models to map correlations between charge radii and key physical features: mass $A^{1/3}$ and proton number $Z^{1/3}$ dependencies, total binding energy, and for the first time, the pairing gap. Our models are rigorously trained using four-fold cross-validation with automated hyperparameter optimization, ensuring robustness and generalizability, which is critical for the typically small and skewed datasets in nuclear physics. Finally, we distill the knowledge from the initial LGBM and GPR models into simplified, interpretable mathematical expressions via symbolic regression, white-boxing these ML models. The derived formulas provide physical insights comparable to traditional many-body models and demonstrate a powerful pathway for physics expression discovery guided by ML.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a hybrid machine learning framework combining numerical regression (LGBM and GPR) with symbolic regression can derive interpretable analytical expressions for nuclear charge radii across the full nuclear chart, including light nuclei often excluded from such studies, but the derived expressions at low complexity (level 8) achieve only moderate accuracy (RMSE ~0.07-0.08 fm) and fail to capture localized effects like shell-gap kinks, requiring higher complexity (level 15) for better fidelity.

## Method

The authors trained Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LGBM) and Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) models on a dataset of 956 measured charge radii using four-fold cross-validation with automated hyperparameter optimization via Optuna. Input features included mass number dependence A^(1/3), proton number Z^(1/3), total binding energy, neutron number, isospin asymmetry, Casten factor, and the pairing gap (introduced for the first time in ML studies of charge radii). After training, the optimized models were used to predict charge radii for 2601 additional nuclei with known binding energies, and symbolic regression via PySRRegressor was applied to the full set of 3557 predicted-plus-extrapolated values to distill simplified mathematical expressions.

## Key numbers

- RMSE for LGBM out-of-fold predictions: 0.0561 fm
- RMSE for GPR out-of-fold predictions: 0.0315 fm
- Dataset of 956 measured charge radii spanning from proton (0.87 fm) to curium (Z=96, 5.90 fm)
- GPR prediction for ^1H: 0.8293 (1.316) fm versus experimental 0.8783 (0.0086) fm
- GPR prediction for deuteron: 2.2624 (0.0888) fm versus experimental 2.1421 (0.0088) fm
- GPR prediction for triton: 1.5741 (0.1629) fm versus experimental 1.7591 (0.0363) fm
- GPR extrapolation for ^134Sn: 4.7253 (0.0329) fm versus experimental 4.7317 (0.0014) fm
- Symbolic regression expression from GPR at complexity level 8: R = 0.386 A^(1/3) + 0.536 Z^(1/3) + 0.185 BEA + 0.617, with RMSE of 0.079 fm
- Symbolic regression expression from LGBM at complexity level 8: R = 0.540 A^(1/3) - 0.013 N + 1.655 BEA + 1.265, with RMSE of 0.070 fm

## Context

This work extends prior ML studies of nuclear charge radii by Utama (2016), Ma (2020), Dong (2023), Shang (2024), and Li (2025), which typically excluded light nuclei and relied on single train-test splits. It also builds on the symbolic regression approach of Munoz (2025) but accelerates the process by applying symbolic regression to smoothed ML model outputs rather than raw experimental data. The derived expressions reproduce the volume, Coulomb, and asymmetry terms of the liquid-drop model (Weizsacker 1935, Duflo 1994) and enable future extension to other observables such as magnetic moments, quadrupole moments, and decay Q-values.
