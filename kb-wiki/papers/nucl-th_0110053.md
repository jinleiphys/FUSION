# Selected Topics in High Energy Semi-Exclusive Electro-Nuclear Reactions

> We review the present status of the theory of high energy reactions with semi-exclusive nucleon electro-production from nuclear targets. We demonstrate how the increase of transferred energies in these reactions opens a complete new window in studying the microscopic nuclear structure at small distances. The simplifications in theoretical descriptions associated with the increase of the energies are discussed. The theoretical framework for calculation of high energy nuclear reactions based on the effective Feynman diagram rules is described in details. The result of this approach is the generalized eikonal approximation (GEA), which is reduced to Glauber approximation when nucleon recoil is neglected. The method of GEA is demonstrated in the calculation of high energy electro-disintegration of the deuteron and A=3 targets. Subsequently we generalize the obtained formulae for A>3 nuclei. The relation of GEA to the Glauber theory is analyzed. Then based on the GEA framework we discuss some of the phenomena which can be studied in exclusive reactions, these are: nuclear transparency and short-range correlations in nuclei. We illustrate how light-cone dynamics of high-energy scattering emerge naturally in high energy electro-nuclear reactions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the Perturbed Static Path Approximation (PSPA) provides a self-consistent microscopic framework for calculating decay rates of metastable Fermi systems, extending the Caldeira-Leggett model by deriving all transport coefficients (inertia, stiffness, damping) from a separable two-body interaction rather than assuming them phenomenologically. The approach is valid only above a crossover temperature \(T_0\), below which genuine quantum tunneling dominates and the PSPA breaks down.

## Method

The authors start from a Hamiltonian with a separable two-body interaction \(\hat{H} = \hat{H}_0 + \frac{k}{2} \hat{F}\hat{F}\) and introduce the collective variable \(q(\tau)\) via a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation. The partition function is evaluated within the PSPA by expanding the action to second order in the fluctuations around the static path, yielding a Gaussian correction factor \(\mathcal{C}^{\text{PSPA}}\) expressed in terms of the retarded response function \(\chi(q_0, i\nu_r)\). To incorporate dissipation, the independent-particle response function is replaced by a continuous Lorentzian model for \(\chi''(q_0, \omega)\), from which local transport coefficients (inertia \(M\), stiffness \(M\Omega^2\), damping \(\Gamma\)) are derived. The decay rate is then obtained via the imaginary part of the free energy at the barrier, using saddle-point integration of the PSPA partition function.

## Key numbers

- The crossover temperature for damped motion is given by \(T_0(q_0) = \frac{\hbar |\varpi(q_0)|}{2\pi} \left( \sqrt{1 + \eta^2(q_0)} - \eta(q_0) \right)\), where \(\eta(q_0) = \Gamma(q_0) / (2|\varpi(q_0)|)\) is the dimensionless damping parameter.
- For strong damping \(\eta(q_0) \gg 1\), the local crossover temperature scales as \(T_0(q_0) \sim 1/(2\eta(q_0))\).
- The decay rate formula for \(T > T_0\) is \(R = \frac{2T_0}{\hbar} \frac{\text{Im} Z(\beta)}{\text{Re} Z(\beta)}\), with the partition functions evaluated at the minimum and barrier via saddle-point approximation.
- The PSPA correction factor for damped systems is \(\mathcal{C}^{\text{PSPA}}(\beta, q_0) = \prod_{r>0} \frac{\nu_r^2 + \Gamma(q_0) \nu_r + \Omega^2(q_0)}{\nu_r^2 + \Gamma(q_0) \nu_r + \varpi^2(q_0)}\), where \(\nu_r = 2\pi r T / \hbar\) are Matsubara frequencies.

## Context

This work extends the Caldeira-Leggett model (Caldeira and Leggett, 1983) for dissipative tunneling by providing a microscopic derivation of transport coefficients from a separable two-body interaction, rather than assuming a phenomenological heat bath. It builds on the Static Path Approximation (SPA) and its perturbed version (PSPA) developed by Alhassid et al. (1984), Arve et al. (1988), and Attias et al. (1997), and connects to the linear response formulation of nuclear transport theory reviewed by Hofmann (1997). The formalism enables first-principles calculations of fission decay rates in heavy nuclei at finite temperature, with the crossover temperature \(T_0\) marking the transition from thermally activated decay to quantum tunneling.
