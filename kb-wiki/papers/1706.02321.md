# Analytic predictions for nuclear shapes, the prolate dominance and the prolate-oblate shape transition in the proxy-SU(3) model

## Key claim

Using a new approximate analytic parameter-free proxy-SU(3) scheme, the authors predict nuclear shape observables (gamma and beta deformation variables), the global feature of prolate dominance, and the locus of the prolate-oblate shape transition, with the caveat that the predictions rely on a proxy-SU(3) symmetry that omits the highest-lying unique parity orbital from each major shell, and the results are compared with empirical data rather than derived from first-principles calculations.

## Method

The authors apply the proxy-SU(3) symmetry scheme, which replaces all but one of the intruder unique parity orbitals in medium and heavy mass nuclei by the highest j-orbital from the next lower shell that has identical angular momentum projection properties, producing a full oscillator shell with U(X) symmetry. For a given nucleus, the ground state SU(3) irreducible representation (lambda, mu) is obtained through the outer product of the relevant proton and neutron SU(3) irreps, and these values are mapped to the collective model deformation variables gamma and beta using linear mappings between the invariants of SU(3) and the collective model. The predictions are compared with empirical results for nuclei with protons in the 50-82 shell and neutrons in the 82-126 shell, as well as for nuclei with both protons and neutrons in the 50-82 shell.

## Key numbers

* The proxy-SU(3) scheme predicts the prolate-oblate transition occurs at neutron number N approximately 116 for W and Os isotopes, in agreement with data suggesting the first oblate nucleus in W is at 190W (N=116) and in Os at 192-194Os (N=116-118).
* For the rare earths with protons in the 50-82 shell and neutrons also in the 50-82 shell, the first oblate nuclei appear at N=72 for W, Os, and Pt (146W, 148Os, 150Pt), and at N=74 for Hf (146Hf).
* The proxy-SU(3) scheme uses a value of r0=0.87 for the dimensionless mean square radius parameter, determined from a fit over a wide range of nuclei.
* The proxy sdg shell (for protons in the 50-82 shell) has size 30 nucleons instead of the normal 32, and the proxy pfh shell (for neutrons in the 82-126 shell) has size 42 nucleons instead of the normal 44.

## Context

This work extends the proxy-SU(3) symmetry introduced by the same group (Bonatsos et al., 2017) and is similar in spirit to the pseudo-SU(3) approach (Arima et al., 1969; Hecht and Adler, 1969; Ginocchio, 1978) but uses a different ansatz to obtain a valence space symmetry. The predictions are compared with empirical data and with results from self-consistent Skyrme Hartree-Fock plus BCS calculations (Sarriguren, 2007), Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations (Robledo et al., 2009; Nomura et al., 2010, 2011), and the interacting boson model (Jolie et al., 2001, 2002; Zhang et al., 2012). The work enables parameter-free predictions of nuclear shapes for deformed nuclei and identifies regions where oblate shapes are expected, particularly in neutron-deficient rare earths around N approximately 72 that are not yet experimentally accessible.
