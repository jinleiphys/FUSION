# Restoring symmetries in quantum computing using Classical Shadows

> We introduce a method to enforce some symmetries starting from a trial wave-function prepared on quantum computers that might not respect these symmetries. The technique eliminates the necessity for performing the projection on the quantum computer itself. Instead, this task is conducted as a post-processing step on the system's "Classical Shadow". Illustrations of the approach are given for the parity, particle number, and spin projectors that are of particular interest in interacting many-body systems. We compare the method with another classical post-processing technique based on direct measurements of the quantum register. We show that the present scheme can be competitive to predict observables on symmetry-restored states once optimization through derandomization is employed. The technique is illustrated through its application to compute the projected energy for the pairing model Hamiltonian.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a method to enforce symmetries (parity, particle number, spin) on trial wavefunctions prepared on quantum computers that may break those symmetries, performing the symmetry restoration entirely as a classical post-processing step on the system's "Classical Shadow" rather than on the quantum device itself, and shows that this approach can be competitive with direct measurement techniques when derandomization optimization is employed.

## Method

The method combines the Classical Shadows tomography framework with symmetry projection operators. A symmetry-breaking state is prepared on a quantum register, randomized circuits are applied, and measurements produce classical bitstrings that are stored as snapshots. The symmetry projector, expressed as a linear combination of unitary operators that are tensor products of single-qubit gates, is applied classically to each snapshot to obtain expectation values of observables on the symmetry-restored state. The technique is demonstrated for parity, particle number, and spin projectors, and applied to compute the projected energy for the pairing model Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

- For the pairing Hamiltonian with equidistant doubly degenerate single-particle levels (ε_i = i Δε, i = 0 to q-1), the number of independent Pauli strings to evaluate with the Classical Shadow for parity projection is 5, 33, 73, and 129 for q = 2, 4, 6, and 8 qubits respectively.
- The parity projection demonstration used q = 4 qubits with an initial state having p_+ = 0.3 and p_- = 0.7 probabilities.
- The parity projector can be implemented with linear complexity O(q), using up to 2q CNOT gates and a single Z-rotation gate for the form e^{i(π/2)Π}.
- Results for projected energy on the even parity subspace are shown for q = 2, 4, and 6 qubits using 1000 shots for density matrix reconstruction.

## Context

This work extends the symmetry-breaking/symmetry-restoring (SB/SR) technique previously incorporated into VQE algorithms by Ruiz Guzman (2022) and reviewed by Lacroix (2023). It contrasts with prior approaches that perform symmetry restoration on the quantum device itself using Quantum Phase Estimation (Lacroix 2020, Siwach 2021, Ruiz Guzman 2022) or quantum Oracle and Linear Unitary Combination (Ruiz Guzman 2023). The method enables the delegation of symmetry restoration to classical post-processing, reducing quantum resource requirements for NISQ devices, and provides simultaneous access to expectation values across all symmetry eigenspaces from a single classical shadow.
