# Study of the hottest droplet of fluid through correlations and fluctuations of collective variables

> In this thesis, we focus on the fluctuations and correlations of the collective observables such as the mean transverse momentum per particle ($[p_T]$) and harmonic flow coefficients ($v_n$) of particles produced in the ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and the LHC. Specifically, we show that the fluctuations of harmonic flow can be probed by the factorization-breaking coefficients between flow vectors in different $p_T$-bins. Experimental difficulty can be reduced by taking one of the flow vectors momentum averaged. Fluctuations cause a decorrelation between the flow vectors, which can be attributed to equal contributions from the flow magnitude and flow angle decorrelation. We study fluctuations of mean transverse momentum per particle ($[p_T]$) in ultra-central collisions and show that our model can explain the steep fall of its variance observed by the ATLAS collaboration. We also present robust predictions for the skewness and kurtosis, and highlight the role of impact parameter fluctuations in ultracentral collisions. We study the Pearson correlation coefficients between $[p_T]$ and $v_n^2$, which can map the initial state correlations between the shape and size of the fireball. We show that higher order normalized and symmetric cumulants between these observables can be constructed, which put useful additional constraints on the initial state properties. Furthermore, we study the momentum dependent Pearson correlation between $[p_T]$ and the transverse momentum dependent flow. It shows sensitivity to the Gaussian width of the nucleon at the initial state. Finally, we show that such correlations and fluctuations of collective observables can be used to study nuclear deformation and put robust constraints on their deformation parameters through high energy nuclear collisions.

# Study of the hottest droplet of fluid through correlations and fluctuations of collective variables

## Key claim

The thesis demonstrates that event-by-event fluctuations and correlations of collective observables (mean transverse momentum per particle $[p_T]$ and harmonic flow coefficients $v_n$) can be used to probe initial-state properties of the quark-gluon plasma and to constrain nuclear deformation parameters, but the model's success in explaining the steep fall of $[p_T]$ variance observed by ATLAS relies on fitting the magnitude of fluctuations to data rather than obtaining it from first-principles hydrodynamic calculations, which overestimate the fluctuations.

## Method

The author employs relativistic viscous hydrodynamic simulations using the MUSIC code with boost-invariant geometry, constant shear viscosity to entropy ratio $\eta/s=0.12$, and freeze-out temperature $T_f=135$ MeV, with initial entropy distributions from the TRENTO model (parameter $p=0$, $k=2.5$, nucleon-nucleon cross section $\sigma_{NN}=7.0$ fm$^2$). A toy model is developed where the transverse momentum dependent flow vector $V_n(p)$ is expressed as a small deviation from the integrated flow, $V_n(p) = C(p) V_n + \delta(p)$, with the assumption that the correlation between the integrated flow and the random small fluctuations vanishes, $\langle V_n \cdot \delta(p)^\star \rangle = 0$. The analysis includes fitting the variance of $[p_T]$ fluctuations to ATLAS data using three parameters: $\sigma_{p_T}(0)$ (standard deviation at $b=0$), $\alpha$ (impact parameter dependence), and the Pearson correlation coefficient $r$ between $[p_T]$ and the centrality estimator.

## Key numbers

- The QGP effective temperature is 212 MeV ($\equiv 10^{12}$ K), approximately $10^5$ times the temperature of the Sun's core.
- The QGP medium survives for approximately $10^{-22}$ seconds (few fm/c).
- In Pb+Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV, a single head-on collision creates around 35000 hadrons, mostly pions ($\sim 80\%$), with kaons ($\sim 17\%$) and protons ($\sim 3\%$).
- The hydrodynamic calculation yields average multiplicity $\overline{N_{ch}} = 6660 \pm 30$, much larger than ATLAS data due to detection efficiency and acceptance effects.
- The width of $[p_T]$ fluctuations from the hydrodynamic calculation is $\sigma_{p_T}(0) = 13 \pm 1$ MeV/$c$, compared to the ATLAS-inferred value of 9.357 MeV/$c$.
- The Pearson correlation coefficient between $N_{ch}$ and $[p_T]$ from hydrodynamics is $r_{N_{ch}} \sim 0.674$, in excellent agreement with the fit value $r_{N_{ch}} = 0.676$ from ATLAS data.
- HIJING simulations give average multiplicity $\overline{N_{ch}} = 5149$ and average $p_T$ of 941 MeV/$c$, lower than the hydrodynamic simulation ($\overline{p_T} = 1074$ MeV/$c$).
- The fit parameter $\sigma_{p_T}(0)$ is close to 10 MeV/$c$, corresponding to relative dynamical fluctuations of order 1% in central collisions.
- The fit parameter $\alpha$ is close to 1.2, indicating faster variation of dynamical fluctuations with impact parameter than statistical fluctuations ($\alpha=1$).
- The Pearson correlation coefficient $r$ between $[p_T]$ and centrality estimator is between 0.6 and 0.7 for both $N_{ch}$ and $E_T$ based data sets.
- The Duke and JETSCAPE parametrizations yield $\gamma = 2.83$ and $\gamma = 1.90$ respectively for the variance-mean relation of $N_{ch}$.

## Context

This thesis extends the line of work on event-by-event fluctuations and correlations of collective observables in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and the LHC, building on hydrodynamic frameworks such as MUSIC (Schenke et al., 2010) and initial-state models like TRENTO (Moreland et al., 2014). The study addresses the long-standing problem that hydrodynamics overestimates $[p_T]$ fluctuations (Bozek, 2012), which can be remedied by tuning initial density fluctuations (Bozek, 2017; Bernhard, 2019; JETSCAPE, 2020). The work enables future constraints on nuclear deformation parameters through high-energy nuclear collisions of deformed nuclei (e.g., U+U, Xe+Xe, Ru+Ru, Zr+Zr), following prior studies by Giacalone (2019, 2020, 2021), Jia (2021, 2022), and experimental measurements by STAR (2015), ALICE (2018), CMS (2019), and ATLAS (2019, 2020).
