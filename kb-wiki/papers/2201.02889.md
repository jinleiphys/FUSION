# Beta-delayed fission in the coupled Quasi-particle Random Phase Approximation plus Hauser-Feshbach approach

> Beta-delayed neutron emission and $β$-delayed fission ($β$df) probabilities were calculated for heavy, neutron-rich nuclei using the Los Alamos coupled Quasi-Particle Random Phase Approximation plus Hauser-Feshbach (QRPA+HF) approach. In this model, the compound nucleus is initially populated by $β$-decay and is followed through subsequent statistical decays taking into account competition between neutrons, $γ$-rays and fission. The primary output of these calculations includes branching ratios along with neutron and $γ$-ray spectra. We find a relatively large region of heavy nuclides where the probability of $β$df is near 100%. For a subset of nuclei near the neutron dripline, delayed neutron emission and the probability to fission are both large which leads to the possibility of multi-chance $β$df (mc-$β$df). We comment on prospective neutron-rich nuclei that could be probed by future experimental campaigns and provide a full table of branching ratios in ASCII format in the supplemental material for use in various applications.

## Key claim

The authors find a relatively large region of heavy neutron-rich nuclides where the probability of beta-delayed fission is near 100%, and for a subset of nuclei near the neutron dripline, both delayed neutron emission and fission probability are large, leading to multi-chance beta-delayed fission; however, these predictions depend on the specific choices of fission barrier heights, mass models, and beta-strength functions, with the authors noting that closer to stability the calculations are more susceptible to deviations.

## Method

The authors used the Los Alamos coupled Quasi-Particle Random Phase Approximation plus Hauser-Feshbach (QRPA+HF) approach, where the compound nucleus is initially populated by beta-decay using QRPA calculations of the beta-strength function (including both Gamow-Teller and First-Forbidden contributions from Moller et al. 2018), and then the subsequent statistical decay is followed through the Hauser-Feshbach framework with competition between neutron emission, gamma de-excitation, and fission. The fission transmission coefficients were calculated using the Hill-Wheeler formula with fission barrier heights from the Finite-Range Liquid-Drop Model (FRLDM) of Moller et al. 2015, and the calculations used ground state properties from the 2012 Finite Range Droplet Model (FRDM2012) with Gilbert-Cameron level densities and the Koning-Delaroche global optical potential for neutron transmission.

## Key numbers

- For the example nucleus 282Bk (Z=97, A=282), the Q-value is approximately 11 MeV, the first daughter 282Cf has a neutron separation energy Sn=3.33 MeV and fission barrier height Bf=3.82 MeV, the second daughter has Sn=2.36 MeV and Bf=4.47 MeV, and the third daughter has Sn=3.65 MeV and Bf=4.54 MeV; the average neutron multiplicity is ⟨n⟩=1.07.
- For 295Fm, the first daughter 295Md has Sn=3.15 MeV while Bf=2.69 MeV, so fission operates at low excitation energy and neutron emission reaches a maximum ratio of 25% of the total transmission near threshold.
- The Gaussian width used for smoothing the beta-strength function scales as A^(-0.57) for excitations above 2 MeV, resulting in a spreading of roughly 200 keV for A=200 nuclei.
- The calculations cover all neutron-rich nuclei from stability to extreme neutron excess with an upper mass limit of A=330.
- The authors identified 35 nuclei close to stability (with 90 ≤ Z ≤ 120, Pf ≥ 1%, and no more than 5 neutrons away from the last neutron-rich value in NuBase 2020) as prospective candidates for experimental measurement.
- The cumulative beta-delayed fission probability reaches near 100% near N=180 for plutonium (Z=94) and near N=190 for berkelium (Z=97).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical description of beta-delayed fission using the QRPA+HF framework previously introduced by Kawano et al. (2008) and Mumpower et al. (2016), building on the foundational work of Thielemann et al. (1983) who laid the groundwork for the theoretical description of beta-delayed fission and its application to the r-process of nucleosynthesis. The results show that beyond the N=184 shell closure, many nuclei have a cumulative beta-delayed fission probability of 100%, which prevents the production of superheavy elements in nature, extending further than previous calculations by Thielemann et al. (1983) and suggesting that a neutron-rich pathway to populate superheavies via alpha-decay is unlikely. The findings enable future experimental campaigns by providing a list of 35 candidate nuclei near stability that may have measurable beta-delayed fission branching ratios, and the tabulated branching ratios in the supplemental material can be used in astrophysical r-process network simulations.
