# Upper bounds on r-mode amplitudes from observations of low-mass X-ray binary neutron stars

> We present upper limits on the amplitude of r-mode oscillations, and gravitational-radiation-induced spin-down rates, in low mass X-ray binary neutron stars, under the assumption that the quiescent neutron star luminosity is powered by dissipation from a steady-state r-mode. For masses $< 2 M_{\odot}$ we find dimensionless r-mode amplitudes in the range from about $1\times 10^{-8}$ to $1.5\times 10^{-6}$. For the accreting millisecond X-ray pulsar sources with known quiescent spin-down rates these limits suggest that $\lesssim 1%$ of the observed rate can be due to an unstable r-mode. Interestingly, the source with the highest amplitude limit, NGC 6440, could have an r-mode spin-down rate comparable to the observed, quiescent rate for SAX J1808-3658. Thus, quiescent spin-down measurements for this source would be particularly interesting. For all sources considered here our amplitude limits suggest that gravitational wave signals are likely too weak for detection with Advanced LIGO. Our highest mass model ($2.21\, M_{\odot}$) can support enhanced, direct Urca neutrino emission in the core and thus can have higher r-mode amplitudes. Indeed, the inferred r-mode spin-down rates at these higher amplitudes are inconsistent with the observed spin-down rates for some of the sources, such as IGR J00291+5934 and XTE J1751-305. In the absence of other significant sources of internal heat, these results could be used to place an upper limit on the masses of these sources if they were made of hadronic matter, or alternatively it could be used to probe the existence of exotic matter in them if their masses were known.

## Key claim

Under the assumption that quiescent neutron star luminosity is entirely powered by dissipation from a steady-state r-mode, the authors derive upper limits on r-mode amplitudes that are significantly lower than those obtained from the "spin equilibrium" assumption, implying that r-mode torque does not balance accretion-driven spin-up in these systems.

## Method

The authors used observed quiescent luminosities and surface temperatures of low-mass X-ray binary neutron stars to constrain r-mode amplitudes via a thermal equilibrium argument, where gravitational radiation pumps energy into the r-mode and all dissipated energy must be radiated from the star. They computed core temperatures from observed effective surface temperatures using the Potekhin (1997) relation for fully accreted envelopes, then determined neutrino luminosities to estimate total heat deposited by gravitational radiation. Three neutron star models were constructed using the APR equation of state with masses of 1.4, 2.0, and 2.21 solar masses, where the highest mass model allows direct Urca neutrino emission in a core of radius 5.9 km.

## Key numbers

- Dimensionless r-mode amplitudes for masses below 2 solar masses range from about 1 × 10⁻⁸ to 1.5 × 10⁻⁶
- For accreting millisecond X-ray pulsars with known quiescent spin-down rates, less than 1% of the observed spin-down rate can be due to an unstable r-mode
- Spin-equilibrium amplitude estimates for three sources (IGR J00291, SAX J1808-3658, XTE J1814-338) are all in the range of approximately 1-3 × 10⁻⁷ across all three neutron star models
- The 2.21 solar mass model has a direct Urca core radius of 5.9 km
- Observed quiescent spin-down rates for SAX J1808-3658 and IGR J00291+5934 are approximately 1-3 × 10⁻¹⁵ Hz s⁻¹
- The peak spin-up rate during outburst for IGR J00291 is about 3 × 10⁻¹³ Hz s⁻¹, roughly 100 times larger than the spin-down torque

## Context

This work extends the thermal equilibrium argument of Brown and Ushomirsky (2000) by using observed quiescent luminosities to directly constrain r-mode amplitudes, rather than estimating luminosities from spin-equilibrium amplitudes. The results contradict the assumption that long-term accretion torque is balanced by r-mode torque, as assumed in earlier work by Brown and Ushomirsky (2000), Ho et al. (2011), and others. The findings enable future quiescent spin-down measurements for sources like NGC 6440 to test whether r-mode spin-down could be comparable to observed rates, and could be used to place upper limits on neutron star masses or probe exotic matter composition.
