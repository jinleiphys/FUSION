# Properties of the nuclear medium

> We review our knowledge on the properties of the nuclear medium that have been studied, along many years, on the basis of many-body theory, laboratory experiments and astrophysical observations. First we consider the realm of phenomenological laboratory data and astrophysical observations, and the hints they can give on the characteristics that the nuclear medium should possess. The analysis is based on phenomenological models, that however have a strong basis on physical intuition and an impressive success. More microscopic models are also considered, and it is shown that they are able to give invaluable information on the nuclear medium, in particular on its Equation of State. The interplay between laboratory experiments and astrophysical observations are particularly stressed, and it is shown how their complementarity enriches enormously our insights into the structure of the nuclear medium. We then introduce the nucleon-nucleon interaction and the microscopic many-body theory of nuclear matter, with a critical discussion about the different approaches and their results. The Landau Fermi Liquid theory is introduced and briefly discussed. As illustrative example, we discuss neutron matter at very low density, and it is shown how it can be treated within the many-body theory. A section is dedicated to the pairing problem. The connection with nuclear structure is then discussed, on the basis of the Energy Density Functional method. The possibility to link the physics of exotic nuclei and the astrophysics of neutron stars is particularly stressed. Finally we discuss the thermal properties of the nuclear medium, in particular the liquid-gas phase transition and its connection with the phenomenology on heavy ion reactions and the cooling evolution of neutron stars. The presentation has been taken for non-specialists and possibly for non-nuclear physicists.

## Key claim

This review argues that the properties of the nuclear medium, particularly its Equation of State, can serve as a unifying framework linking nuclear structure theory (including exotic nuclei) with neutron star astrophysics, but the authors acknowledge that the connection between macroscopic nuclear matter properties and finite nuclei remains limited and that parameters like the symmetry energy and incompressibility extracted from phenomenological data still carry significant uncertainties.

## Method

The authors survey the field by first reviewing phenomenological constraints from the semi-empirical mass formula, giant resonances in nuclei, and heavy ion collision data, then introducing microscopic many-body theory based on the nucleon-nucleon interaction and Brueckner-type calculations. They discuss the Landau Fermi Liquid theory, the pairing problem, the Energy Density Functional method, and thermal properties including the liquid-gas phase transition, with the presentation aimed at non-specialists.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter: approximately 0.16 fm^{-3}, extracted from electron scattering data on medium-heavy nuclei
- Free Fermi gas energy per particle at saturation: e = 75.03 ρ^{2/3} MeV, with ρ in fm^{-3}
- Free Fermi gas incompressibility K at saturation: approximately 221 MeV, using the definition K = k_F^2 (d^2e/dk_F^2)
- Symmetry energy coefficient a_{sy} from free Fermi gas at ρ_0: approximately 12 MeV, while phenomenological fits from medium-heavy nuclei give more than twice that value
- Bulk energy coefficient a_V from the semi-empirical mass formula: very close to -16 MeV
- Nuclear matter incompressibility constrained from monopole giant resonance data: between 210 and 250 MeV
- Single particle level density at Fermi energy for free gas at ρ_0: approximately N/23 MeV^{-1}

## Context

This review extends the long tradition of connecting nuclear matter properties to finite nuclei via the Liquid Drop Model (Weizsäcker 1935, Bethe and Bacher 1936) and the Droplet Model (Myers and Swiatecki 1969, 1974). It discusses how the Skyrme functional method (Chamel et al. 2008, 2009) and relativistic mean field approaches have been used to extract the incompressibility from monopole giant resonances, but notes that the relationship is not unique and depends on other force details (Colo et al. 2004, Sharma et al. 2006). The review enables future work linking exotic nuclei physics with neutron star astrophysics, while identifying open questions about the density dependence of the symmetry energy and the precise value of the incompressibility modulus.
