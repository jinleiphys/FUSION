# Special Relativity and Reactions with Unstable Nuclei

> Dynamical relativistic effects are often neglected in the description of reactions with unstable nuclear beams at intermediate energies (E ~ 100 MeV/nucleon). Evidently, this introduces sizable errors in experimental analysis and theoretical descriptions of these reactions. This is particularly important for the experiments held in GANIL/France, MSU/USA, RIKEN/Japan and GSI/Germany. I review a few examples where relativistic effects have been studied in nucleus-nucleus scattering at intermediate energies.

# Special Relativity and Reactions with Unstable Nuclei

## Key claim
Dynamical relativistic effects in nucleus-nucleus reactions at intermediate energies (around 100 MeV/nucleon) are often neglected in experimental analysis and theoretical descriptions, but this introduces sizable errors that can reach up to 50% in Coulomb excitation cross sections at energies common to radioactive beam facilities (50-100 MeV/nucleon), even though non-relativistic treatments are safe below about 10 MeV/nucleon and straight-line relativistic treatments are adequate above about 500 MeV/nucleon.

## Method
The paper reviews several theoretical frameworks for incorporating special relativity into reaction calculations: semiclassical methods with Darwin Lagrangians for elastic scattering, Coulomb excitation theory with orbital integrals containing relativistic corrections to both the trajectory and the interaction, eikonal (Glauber) methods for strong interactions, and a relativistic continuum discretized coupled-channels (RCDCC) formalism derived from the Klein-Gordon equation. The author compares non-relativistic, straight-line relativistic, and "full" relativistic calculations for specific nuclear systems to quantify the corrections.

## Key numbers
- In 208Pb+208Pb collisions at 100 MeV/nucleon, the scattering angle increases by up to 6% when relativistic corrections are included, and the elastic scattering cross section changes by approximately 13% for center-of-mass scattering angles around 0-4 degrees.
- For light projectiles on heavy targets (e.g., 11Li+208Pb), about 80% of the relativistic correction to elastic scattering comes from changes in masses, with only about 20% due to magnetic interactions and retardation.
- At 10 MeV/nucleon, relativistic corrections to Coulomb excitation cross sections are important only at the level of about 1%; at 500 MeV/nucleon, the correct treatment of recoil corrections is relevant at the level of about 1%.
- At energies around 50 to 100 MeV/nucleon, relativistic corrections to Coulomb excitation cross sections can add up to 50%.
- For the dissociation reaction 8B+Pb at 83 MeV/nucleon, the difference between full relativistic RCDCC calculations and calculations with gamma set to unity is of the order of 4-9%.

## Context
This review extends earlier work on relativistic effects in nuclear reactions, including the Darwin Lagrangian treatment of elastic scattering by AAB90, the straight-line relativistic Coulomb excitation formalism of Winther and Alder (1979), and the non-relativistic Coulomb excitation theory of Alder and Winther (1975). The paper specifically addresses the energy regime of major radioactive beam facilities (GANIL, MSU, RIKEN, GSI) where standard DWBA codes (FRESCO, ECIS, DWUCK) include relativity only in kinematic relations but not in the reaction dynamics. The work enables more reliable extraction of nuclear properties such as B(E2) values and gamma-ray angular distributions from Coulomb excitation experiments at intermediate energies, and suggests that previous experimental results analyzed with non-relativistic methods may need to be revisited.
