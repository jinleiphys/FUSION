# Effects of short-range correlation reduced kinetic symmetry energy in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies

> Besides earlier predictions based on both phenomenological models and modern microscopic many-body theories, circumstantial evidence was recently found for a reduced kinetic symmetry energy of isospin-asymmetric nucleonic matter compared to the free Fermi gas model prediction due to the short-range correlation of high-momentum neutron-proton pairs. While keeping the total symmetry energy near the saturation density of nuclear matter consistent with existing experimental constraints, we examine the correspondingly enhanced role of the isospin degree of freedom in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies due to the reduced (enhanced) kinetic (potential) symmetry energy. Important observable consequences are investigated.

# Effects of short-range correlation reduced kinetic symmetry energy in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies

## Key claim
While keeping the total symmetry energy at saturation density fixed at values consistent with existing experimental constraints (global average of 31.6 ± 2.66 MeV), the short-range correlation (SRC) reduced kinetic symmetry energy (which can become negative) forces a correspondingly enhanced potential symmetry energy, which significantly strengthens the role of the isospin degree of freedom in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies. The authors emphasize this is an exploratory study to gain qualitative understanding, and that a lot more work is needed to draw strong conclusions from quantitative comparison with data.

## Method
The authors use the IBUU transport model with a momentum-independent potential to simulate heavy-ion collisions, parameterizing the symmetry energy as E_sym(ρ) = η·E_sym^kin(FG)(ρ) + [S0 − η·E_sym^kin(FG)(ρ0)](ρ/ρ0)^γ, where η controls the kinetic part and γ controls the density dependence of the potential part. They fix S0 at 30 MeV and compare results with η=1 (free Fermi gas kinetic symmetry energy) versus η=0 (no kinetic symmetry energy) for two values of γ (0.5 and 1.0), examining the time evolution of neutron/proton ratios in gas and liquid phases, free neutron/proton ratios as functions of kinetic energy and transverse momentum, and the π−/π+ ratio evolution.

## Key numbers
- The global average of the total symmetry energy at saturation density is S0 = 31.6 ± 2.66 MeV (LiBA13).
- The free Fermi gas kinetic symmetry energy at ρ0 is approximately 12.5 MeV from the formula E_sym^kin(FG)(ρ) ≈ 12.5(ρ/ρ0)^(2/3).
- The potential symmetry energy at ρ0 when using the free Fermi gas kinetic part is approximately 19.1 MeV (from S0 − E_sym^kin(FG)(ρ0)).
- The kinetic symmetry energy at ρ0 when SRC is considered is reduced to about −(9 ± 7) MeV (OrHen14).
- The best fit to NSCL/MSU data gives η = −0.30(1 ± 18.53%) and γ = 0.80(1 ± 5.98%), corresponding to a kinetic symmetry energy of E_sym^kin(ρ0) = −(3.8 ± 0.7) MeV at ρ0 (OrHen14).
- The maximum density reached at 50 MeV/nucleon beam energy is about 1.2ρ0; at 120 MeV/nucleon it is about 1.7−2ρ0.
- The global mean values from 28 analyses give S0 = 31.6 ± 2.6 MeV and L = 58.9 ± 16.0 MeV (LiBA13).

## Context
This work extends earlier predictions from both phenomenological models (XuLi) and microscopic many-body theories (Vid11, Lov11, Car12, Rio14) that short-range correlations from the tensor force reduce the kinetic symmetry energy. It builds on circumstantial evidence from (e,e′) scattering experiments (Hen14) and heavy-ion collision experiments (OrHen14). The paper shows that existing constraints on the S0−L correlation from analyses of atomic masses (lat12) and dipole polarizability of 208Pb (Tamii) do not limit the partition between kinetic and potential symmetry energy. The results enable future work to incorporate SRC effects consistently in transport model initialization, off-shell propagation of high-momentum nucleons, and momentum-dependent symmetry potentials, and explain why IBUU calculations without SRC-reduced kinetic symmetry energy (Kon15) fall below NSCL/MSU data (Cou14).
