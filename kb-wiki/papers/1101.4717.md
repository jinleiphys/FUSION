# Nuclear Radii of Unstable Nuclei

> In the presented review we discuss the problem of extraction the parameters of nuclear matter and charge distribution in stable and unstable isotopes. A substantial amount of information on the nuclear radii and other distribution parameters in light exotic nuclei has been obtained from experiments on intermediate-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions. The analyses of these experiments is usually performed with the help of the Glauber Theory. We review the main assumptions the Glauber Theory and show how this theoretical approach is used to calculate reaction and interaction cross sections. We also show how radii of nuclear matter can be obtained from the analysis of the experimental data on reaction cross sections. In the provided analysis reaction cross sections were calculated in optical and rigid approximations of the Glauber Theory as well as using explicit expressions. Numerical calculations of Glauber's explicit expressions for reaction cross sections were done using Monte Carlo technique. Recent results of the precise measurements of charge radii of light exotic nuclei which were done using the laser-spectroscopy technique as well as the method of the investigation of the nuclear matter distributions in proton elastic scattering experiments in inverse kinematics are also discussed.

## Key claim

This review argues that while the Glauber Theory is the standard framework for extracting nuclear matter radii from reaction cross sections in intermediate-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions, the extracted radii values depend significantly on which approximation of the theory (optical, rigid target, or explicit Monte Carlo) is used, and the optical approximation can be inaccurate by 10-15% for integrated cross sections.

## Method

The authors review the formal derivation of the Glauber Theory for nucleon-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus elastic scattering, presenting the optical approximation, the rigid target approximation, and the explicit Monte Carlo method (using the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm) for calculating the scattering matrix S_AB(b) and reaction cross sections. They then discuss how nuclear matter radii are extracted by comparing calculated reaction cross sections to experimental data, and separately review charge radii measurements from laser-spectroscopy experiments and nuclear matter distributions from proton elastic scattering in inverse kinematics.

## Key numbers

- The Bradt-Peters formula parameterization for stable nuclei at high energy gives R_0 = 1.48 ± 0.03 fm and c = 1.32 ± 0.05
- The optical approximation differs from data by approximately 10-15% in σ_AB^(tot)
- Coulomb contributions to light ion inelastic collisions are about 3 ± 1% for ¹²C-Pb interaction cross sections
- Coulomb contributions are estimated at 10 ± 3% for ¹¹Li-Al and 30 ± 12% for ¹¹Li-Pb collisions
- For ³⁴Cl on ¹²C target, the difference between reaction and interaction cross sections was approximately 10 mb (about 1% of σ_AB^(r))
- For projectiles with A < 80 on carbon targets, the average difference between interaction and reaction cross sections was approximately 60 mb (about 4-6% of σ_AB^(r))
- At 800 MeV per nucleon: β² = 0.2 fm², σ_pp^(tot) = 47 mb, σ_pn^(tot) = 38 mb, σ_pp^(el) = 25.4 mb, σ_pn^(el) = 26 mb
- At 1 GeV per nucleon: β² = 0.22 fm²

## Context

This review extends the line of work on nuclear radii measurements from Hofstadter's electron scattering experiments on stable nuclei and the early Glauber Theory applications, to the study of unstable (radioactive) nuclei using radioactive beams. It builds on the seminal finding by Tanihata (1985) that ¹¹Li has a much larger radius than neighboring nuclei, suggesting a halo structure, and discusses subsequent analyses using the Glauber Theory by authors such as Alkhazov, Ozawa, and others. The review enables future work by clarifying the accuracy of different Glauber approximations and by discussing how proton elastic scattering in inverse kinematics can provide separate information on core and halo radii.
