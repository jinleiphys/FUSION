# The Stability of Nuclear Matter in the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio Model

> Using the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model to describe the nucleon as a quark-diquark state, we discuss the stability of nuclear matter in a hybrid model for the ground state at finite nucleon density. It is shown that a simple extension of the model to simulate the effects of confinement leads to a scalar polarizability of the nucleon. This, in turn, leads to a less attractive effective interaction between the nucleons, helping to achieve saturation of the nuclear matter ground state. It is also pointed out that that the same effect naturally leads to a suppression of ``Z-graph'' contributions with increasing scalar potential.

## Key claim

Using the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model to describe the nucleon as a quark-diquark bound state, the authors show that incorporating a simple simulation of confinement effects via an infrared cut-off produces a repulsive scalar polarizability of the nucleon, which reduces the attractive effective nucleon-nucleon interaction and can lead to saturation of nuclear matter; however, the calculation is limited to the "static approximation" to the full Faddeev equation, which becomes worse for a nucleon in the medium, leaving an ambiguity that should be resolved in a future numerical analysis.

## Method

The authors construct a hybrid model for the nuclear matter ground state based on the NJL model, where the nucleon is described as a quark-diquark state using the static approximation to the relativistic Faddeev equation. The energy density is expressed as the sum of a vacuum quark loop contribution, a vector meson exchange term, and a Fermi sea term for nucleons moving in self-consistent scalar and vector mean fields. The scalar polarizability of the nucleon is introduced by incorporating an infrared cut-off to remove unphysical thresholds for the decay of the quark-diquark bound state, and the resulting effective two-nucleon interaction (Landau-Migdal interaction) is analyzed via low-density expansions.

## Key numbers

* The vacuum value of the constituent quark mass is denoted \(M_0\), and the vacuum nucleon mass is \(M_{N0}\); the effective sigma meson mass in the vacuum is \(M_{\sigma 0} = 2M_0\) for the chiral limit \(m=0\).
* The low-density expansion of the energy density is given by \({\cal E}^* = \rho \left(M_{N0} + \frac{3}{10} \frac{p_F^2}{M_{N0}}\right) + \frac{\rho^2}{2} f_{0,L=0} + {\cal O}(p_F^7)\), where \(f_{0,L=0}\) is the zero-density Landau-Migdal parameter.
* The low-density expansion of the quark condensate is \(\frac{\langle {\overline \psi}\psi \rangle^*}{\langle {\overline \psi}\psi \rangle_0} = 1 - \rho\,\frac{\Sigma_{\pi N,0}}{(F_{\pi}M_{\pi 0})^2 C}\left(1-\frac{3}{10} \frac{p_F^2}{M_{N0}^2}\right) - \frac{\rho^2}{2}\frac{\Sigma_{\pi NN,0}}{(F_{\pi}M_{\pi 0})^2 C} + {\cal O}(p_F^7)\), where \(\Sigma_{\pi N,0}\) is the one-body pion-nucleon sigma term in vacuum and \(C = 1 - m/M_0\).
* The vector coupling constant is defined via \(G_{\omega} = \frac{1}{18} \frac{g_{\omega}^2}{M_{\omega}^2}\), and the sigma-nucleon coupling constant at zero density is \(g_{\sigma 0} = g \left(\partial M_N / \partial M\right)_{M=M_0}\).
* The one-body sigma term at finite density is \(\Sigma^*_{\pi N} = \frac{m}{2 G_{\pi}} \frac{M_N^*}{E_F^*}\frac{g g^*_{\sigma}}{M^{*2}_{\sigma}}\).

## Context

This work extends the Faddeev approach to the nucleon in the NJL model (FAD1, FAD2, GROUND) to the problem of nuclear matter saturation, a long-standing issue in chiral models where vacuum fluctuation effects (tadpole-type diagrams) cause an instability in the mean-field (Hartree) approximation. The authors contrast their approach with the Walecka model (WAL) and the quark meson coupling model of Guichon (GUI), and they note that earlier attempts to include higher-order interactions (BOG, KOCH) or Dirac sea fluctuations (WAL, LBA) did not resolve the problem. The results suggest that a full Faddeev calculation, rather than the static approximation used here, will very probably lead to a saturating nuclear matter ground state, enabling future studies of medium modifications of nucleon properties such as form factors and structure functions.
