# Machine learning approach to QCD kinetic theory

> The effective kinetic theory (EKT) of QCD provides a possible picture of various non-equilibrium processes in heavy- and light-ion collisions. While there have been substantial advances in simulating the EKT in simple systems with enhanced symmetry, eventually, event-by-event simulations will be required for a comprehensive phenomenological modeling. As of now, these simulations are prohibitively expensive due to the numerical complexity of the Monte Carlo evaluation of the collision kernels. In this talk, we show how the evaluation of the collision kernels can be performed using neural networks paving the way to full event-by-event simulations.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that neural networks can replace the Monte Carlo evaluation of QCD kinetic theory collision kernels, achieving a systematic speed-up of roughly three orders of magnitude in full evolution calculations, though the networks show growing error bars and reduced stability when the system approaches thermal equilibrium.

## Method

The authors generate training data (approximately 100,000 pairs) from the Monte Carlo solver of the effective kinetic theory (EKT), using initial conditions inspired by the Color Glass Condensate framework and perturbations around equilibrium. They preprocess the data by transforming to energy distributions \(p^3 f\) and \(p^3 C\), subtracting the thermal equilibrium distribution, and standardizing features. Separate neural networks with ReLU-activated linear hidden layers are trained for the \(1\leftrightarrow2\) and \(2\leftrightarrow2\) collision kernels, with architecture optimization performed using RayTune. Time evolution is computed with a fourth-order Runge-Kutta algorithm using an ensemble of the ten best-performing networks, with error estimation via the Jackknife method.

## Key numbers

- Speed-up of roughly three orders of magnitude in the calculation of the full evolution compared to the Monte Carlo approach.
- Training dataset size: of the order of 100,000 training pairs for both the 1D system (approximately 100 MB) and the 3D system (approximately 50 GB).
- The neural network predictions conserve energy density and reproduce the Monte Carlo evolution trend for number density and distribution moments, though error bars grow near equilibrium.

## Context

This work extends prior simulations of QCD effective kinetic theory that were limited to simple systems with enhanced symmetry, such as longitudinal boost-invariant systems with infinite transverse size (Kurkela 2015), the diffusion approximation of the \(2\leftrightarrow2\) kernel (Mueller 1999), and the Relaxation Time Approximation (Kurkela 2020). The neural network approach enables event-by-event simulations for comprehensive phenomenological modeling of heavy- and light-ion collisions, which were previously prohibitively expensive due to the numerical complexity of Monte Carlo collision kernel evaluation. The method exploits the spatial locality of the Boltzmann equation to apply the same trained network to each spatial cell.
