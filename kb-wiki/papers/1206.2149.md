# Sequential suppression of quarkonia and high-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions

> According to the usual application of the sequential-suppression picture to the dynamics of heavy quarkonia in the hot medium formed in ultrarelativistic nuclear collisions, quark-antiquark pairs created in a given bound or unbound state remain in that same state as the medium evolves. We argue that this scenario implicitly assumes an adiabatic evolution of the quarkonia, and we show that the validity of the adiabaticity assumption is questionable.

## Key claim

The sequential-suppression picture of heavy quarkonia in ultrarelativistic nuclear collisions implicitly assumes an adiabatic evolution of quark-antiquark pairs, but the validity of this adiabaticity assumption is questionable because the in-medium potential evolves too quickly for the pairs to remain in instantaneous eigenstates.

## Method

The authors test the adiabaticity assumption by evaluating the standard quantum-mechanical adiabatic criterion, which compares the matrix elements of the time derivative of the Hamiltonian (here, the effective quark-antiquark potential) to the squared energy differences between eigenstates. They combine the temperature evolution from dissipative hydrodynamics simulations of central Pb-Pb collisions at the LHC with a lattice-QCD parameterization of the in-medium heavy-quark potential to estimate the relevant timescales and transition probabilities.

## Key numbers

- The temperature at the center of the fireball in central Pb-Pb collisions at the LHC drops at a rate of 30–50 MeV per fm/\(c\) during the first 7 fm/\(c\) of evolution (while \(T > 200\) MeV).
- The matrix elements of the derivative of the potential with respect to temperature between eigenstates are approximately 200–500 MeV·fm.
- The numerator in the adiabatic criterion is of order (80–160 MeV)\(^2\); the denominator for excited \(b\bar b\) states is of order (100–350 MeV)\(^2\), yielding ratios that can be smaller than 0.1 in some channels but larger than 1 in others.
- The typical size of a bound bottomonium is \(r_{\rm rms} \approx 0.3\)–0.75 fm, and the characteristic quark velocity is \(v \sim 0.3c\), giving an orbital timescale \(\tau \approx 1\)–2.5 fm/\(c\); over this time the QGP cools by 30–75 MeV.
- For a dipolar interaction model, the time for a transition to occur with probability 1 (half the Rabi period \(\pi/\omega_R\)) is of order 2–20 fm/\(c\), depending on the transition Bohr frequency, medium size, and coupling strength.

## Context

This work critically examines the standard sequential-suppression picture of quarkonia as a QGP thermometer, which was built on lattice-QCD studies of screening (Matsui and Satz, 1986) and subsequent work on state-dependent dissociation temperatures (Karsch et al., 1987; Digal et al., 2001). The authors argue that the usual assumption that a quarkonium state remains in the same eigenstate as the medium evolves is not justified, and they call for dynamical microscopic descriptions of quarkonium evolution in rapidly changing media, such as those attempted in Borghini and Dutta (2011) and Dutta (in preparation), rather than relying on equilibrium-based formalisms like lattice QCD or finite-temperature field theory.
