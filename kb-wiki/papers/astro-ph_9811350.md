# Can a supernova be located by its neutrinos?

> A future core-collapse supernova in our Galaxy will be detected by several neutrino detectors around the world. The neutrinos escape from the supernova core over several seconds from the time of collapse, unlike the electromagnetic radiation, emitted from the envelope, which is delayed by a time of order hours. In addition, the electromagnetic radiation can be obscured by dust in the intervening interstellar space. The question therefore arises whether a supernova can be located by its neutrinos alone. The early warning of a supernova and its location might allow greatly improved astronomical observations. The theme of the present work is a careful and realistic assessment of this question, taking into account the statistical significance of the various neutrino signals. Not surprisingly, neutrino-electron forward scattering leads to a good determination of the supernova direction, even in the presence of the large and nearly isotropic background from other reactions. Even with the most pessimistic background assumptions, SuperKamiokande (SK) and the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO) can restrict the supernova direction to be within circles of radius $5^\circ$ and $20^\circ$, respectively. Other reactions with more events but weaker angular dependence are much less useful for locating the supernova. Finally, there is the oft-discussed possibility of triangulation, i.e., determination of the supernova direction based on an arrival time delay between different detectors. Given the expected statistics we show that, contrary to previous estimates, this technique does not allow a good determination of the supernova direction.

## Key claim

The paper provides a careful and realistic assessment of whether a supernova can be located by its neutrinos alone, concluding that neutrino-electron forward scattering can determine the direction to within circles of radius 5° for SuperKamiokande and 20° for the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory even under the most pessimistic background assumptions, but that triangulation using arrival time delays between detectors does not allow a good determination of the supernova direction, contrary to previous optimistic estimates.

## Method

The authors calculated the centroiding precision for neutrino-electron scattering events by modeling the signal as a two-dimensional Gaussian peak on a flat isotropic background, using the DuMond test to determine the minimum achievable error. For neutrino-nucleus charged-current reactions, they used forward-backward asymmetry analysis with Legendre expansion to estimate pointing errors. For triangulation, they applied the Rao-Cramer theorem to a schematic model of the neutrino event rate consisting of an exponential rise (time constant τ₁ ≈ 30 ms) followed by exponential decay (τ₂ ≈ 3 s), calculating the minimum possible statistical error on the time delay between detectors.

## Key numbers

- SuperKamiokande can restrict the supernova direction to within a circle of radius 5° under the most pessimistic background assumptions (signal events N_S = 320, background ratio R ≈ 3.0, correction factor C(R) ≈ 3.7)
- The Sudbury Neutrino Observatory can restrict the supernova direction to within a circle of radius 20° under the most pessimistic background assumptions (N_S = 25, R ≈ 3.8, C(R) ≈ 4.1)
- For neutrino-electron scattering, the combined average scattering angle ⟨cos α⟩ = 0.98, corresponding to about 11°, with a one-sigma Gaussian smearing width of 25° from multiple scattering
- For triangulation with nonzero risetime (τ₁ = 30 ms, τ₂ = 3 s), the minimum error on the time delay for SNO is δ(Δt) ≈ 15 ms, giving δ(cos θ) ≈ 0.50 at one sigma
- For the zero risetime case, the first-event technique gives δ(Δt) ≈ 8 ms for SNO and δ(cos θ) ≈ 0.25 at one sigma
- The supernova distance D can be determined with relative precision of order 25% using the event rate, given the binding energy uncertainty of 50% (E_B = (3.0 ± 1.5) × 10⁵³ ergs)

## Context

This work extends earlier discussions of supernova neutrino location by Burrows (1988), Zatsepin (1968), and LoSecco (1989), and directly contradicts previous optimistic estimates for triangulation by Habig (1998), Vagins (1998), and Virtue (1998). The paper provides the quantitative basis for the design of the international supernova early alert network, showing that neutrino-electron scattering in SuperKamiokande and SNO can provide useful directional information, but that triangulation between detectors is too crude to be relied upon for locating the supernova.
