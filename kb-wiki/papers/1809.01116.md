# Identifying a first-order phase transition in neutron star mergers through gravitational waves

> We identify an observable imprint of a first-order hadron-quark phase transition at supranuclear densities on the gravitational-wave (GW) emission of neutron star mergers. Specifically, we show that the dominant postmerger GW frequency f_peak may exhibit a significant deviation from an empirical relation between f_peak and the tidal deformability if a strong first-order phase transition leads to the formation of a gravitationally stable extended quark matter core in the postmerger remnant. A comparison of the GW signatures from a large, representative sample of microphysical, purely hadronic equations of state indicates that this imprint is only observed in those systems which undergo a strong first-order phase transition. Such a shift of the dominant postmerger GW frequency can be revealed by future GW observations, which would provide evidence for the existence of a strong first-order phase transition in the interior of neutron stars.

## Key claim

The dominant postmerger gravitational-wave frequency \(f_\mathrm{peak}\) can exhibit a significant deviation (up to ~0.5 kHz) from the empirical \(f_\mathrm{peak}\)-tidal-deformability relation if a strong first-order hadron-quark phase transition produces a gravitationally stable extended quark matter core in the remnant; this signature is claimed to be unique to such a transition because a large representative sample of purely hadronic equations of state (including those with hyperons or continuous quark transitions) does not produce the same shift.

## Method

The authors performed neutron star merger simulations using a relativistic smooth particle hydrodynamics code with the conformal flatness approximation for equal-mass 1.35-1.35 \(M_\odot\) binaries (total mass 2.7 \(M_\odot\)). They employed the temperature-dependent microscopic hybrid equation of state DD2F-SF (seven variants with different onset densities and density jumps) and compared the resulting gravitational-wave spectra and postmerger peak frequencies against a representative sample of 15 purely hadronic equations of state, including three models with a second-order hyperon transition and two models (ALF2, ALF4) with a continuous quark transition.

## Key numbers

- For the purely hadronic reference EOS DD2F, the dominant postmerger frequency is 3.098 kHz; for the hybrid DD2F-SF-1 model (with a strong first-order phase transition) the frequency rises to 3.3–3.7 kHz, a shift of 448 Hz above the value predicted by the \(f_\mathrm{peak}(\Lambda)\) fit for the same tidal deformability.
- The least-square fit to the purely hadronic EOSs gives \(f_\mathrm{peak} = (6.486\times10^{-7}\,\Lambda^2 - 2.231\times10^{-3}\,\Lambda + 4.1)\) kHz, with a maximum deviation of 113 Hz and an average scatter of 44 Hz.
- The maximum rest-mass density reached in the first milliseconds after merger for DD2F-SF-1 is higher than for DD2F, and the correlation \(\rho_\mathrm{max}^\mathrm{max} = (a f_\mathrm{peak}^2 + b f_\mathrm{peak} + c)\) g cm\(^{-3}\) (with \(a=1.89\times10^{14}\), \(b=-4.13\times10^{14}\), \(c=4.66\times10^{14}\)) is provided for the purely hadronic sample.
- The onset density of the phase transition for DD2F-SF-1 is given at \(T=0\) and 20 MeV; the quark matter core comprises about 20–30% of the total mass of the remnant.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of phase-transition signatures in neutron star mergers (Oechslin 2004, Bauswein 2010, Sekiguchi 2011, Radice 2017, Most 2018) by providing the first demonstration that a large, representative set of purely hadronic equations of state cannot mimic the frequency shift, thus making the signature a potential unambiguous indicator. The result contrasts with the CMF model of Dexheimer (2010), which produces only a small quark core and a much weaker frequency shift. The paper enables future gravitational-wave observations to test for a strong first-order phase transition at supranuclear densities, complementing heavy-ion collision experiments at FAIR and NICA.
