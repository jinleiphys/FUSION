# A percent-level determination of the nucleon axial coupling from Quantum Chromodynamics

> The $\textit{axial coupling of the nucleon}$, $g_A$, is the strength of its coupling to the $\textit{weak}$ axial current of the Standard Model of particle physics, in much the same way as the electric charge is the strength of the coupling to the electromagnetic current. This axial coupling dictates the rate at which neutrons decay to protons, the strength of the attractive long-range force between nucleons and other features of nuclear physics. Precision tests of the Standard Model in nuclear environments require a quantitative understanding of nuclear physics rooted in Quantum Chromodynamics, a pillar of the Standard Model. The prominence of $g_A$ makes it a benchmark quantity to determine theoretically - a difficult task because quantum chromodynamics is non-perturbative, precluding known analytical methods. Lattice Quantum Chromodynamics provides a rigorous, non-perturbative definition of quantum chromodynamics that can be implemented numerically. It has been estimated that a precision of two percent would be possible by 2020 if two challenges are overcome: contamination of $g_A$ from excited states must be controlled in the calculations and statistical precision must be improved markedly. Here we report a calculation of $g_A^{QCD} = 1.271\pm0.013$, using an unconventional method inspired by the Feynman-Hellmann theorem that overcomes these challenges.

## Key claim

The authors report a lattice QCD calculation of the nucleon axial coupling \(g_A = 1.271 \pm 0.013\) using a Feynman-Hellmann inspired method that overcomes the two major challenges of excited-state contamination and statistical precision, achieving a total uncertainty of approximately 1% and yielding a value commensurate with the experimentally determined value \(g_A^{\text{PDG}} = 1.2723(23)\).

## Method

The calculation uses lattice QCD on three lattice spacings (approximately 0.15, 0.12, and 0.09 fm), five values of the pion mass ranging from 130 to 400 MeV, and multiple volumes, employing the HISQ discretization for sea quarks and Möbius domain-wall valence quarks. Instead of the standard three-point function method, the authors apply a Feynman-Hellmann inspired technique that sums over all current insertion times, allowing access to more data at reduced computational cost and enabling simultaneous nonlinear regression of the spectrum and matrix elements to control excited-state contamination. The results are extrapolated to the physical pion mass, continuum limit, and infinite volume using chiral perturbation theory and Symanzik effective field theory, with a model average over several extrapolation functions.

## Key numbers

*   \(g_A^{\text{QCD}} = 1.271 \pm 0.013\) (total uncertainty), with the breakdown \(g_A = 1.2711(103)^{\text{s}}(39)^{\chi}(15)^{\text{a}}(19)^{\text{v}}(04)^{\text{I}}(55)^{\text{M}}\) where s=statistical, \(\chi\)=chiral, a=continuum, v=infinite volume, I=isospin breaking, M=model-selection.
*   The experimentally determined value is \(g_A^{\text{PDG}} = 1.2723(23)\).
*   The calculation uses pion masses \(m_\pi\) ranging between 130 and 400 MeV, with the physical pion mass \(m_\pi^{\text{nature}} = 139.6\) MeV.
*   Lattice spacings are approximately 0.15, 0.12, and 0.09 fm, with spatial extents such that \(3 \lesssim L/r_p \lesssim 8\) where the proton radius \(r_p \sim 0.8\) fm.
*   The pion decay constant is \(F_\pi^{\text{nature}} = 92.1(1.2)\) MeV, giving the chiral expansion parameter \(\epsilon_\pi^{\text{nature}} \sim 0.12\).

## Context

This work extends previous lattice QCD calculations of \(g_A\) by the LHPC (2005), CLS (2012, 2017), QCDSF (2013), RQCD (2014), ETMC (2015, 2017), and PNDME (2016) collaborations, which were limited by excited-state contamination and statistical noise. The Feynman-Hellmann method, proposed by Bouchard et al. (2016), enables the first percent-level determination from lattice QCD with all systematic uncertainties controlled, providing a benchmark for the broader nuclear physics program rooted in QCD and enabling stringent bounds on right-handed beyond-Standard-Model currents when combined with collider and low-energy experimental results.
