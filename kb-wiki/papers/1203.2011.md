# The contrasting fission potential-energy structure of actinides and mercury isotopes

> Fission-fragment mass distributions are asymmetric in fission of typical actinide nuclei for nucleon number $A$ in the range $228 \lnsim A \lnsim 258$ and proton number $Z$ in the range $90\lnsim Z \lnsim 100$. For somewhat lighter systems it has been observed that fission mass distributions are usually symmetric. However, a recent experiment showed that fission of $^{180}$Hg following electron capture on $^{180}$Tl is asymmetric. We calculate potential-energy surfaces for a typical actinide nucleus and for 12 even isotopes in the range $^{178}$Hg--$^{200}$Hg, to investigate the similarities and differences of actinide compared to mercury potential surfaces and to what extent fission-fragment properties, in particular shell structure, relate to the structure of the static potential-energy surfaces. Potential-energy surfaces are calculated in the macroscopic-microscopic approach as functions of fiveshape coordinates for more than five million shapes. The structure of the surfaces are investigated by use of an immersion technique. We determine properties of minima, saddle points, valleys, and ridges between valleys in the 5D shape-coordinate space. Along the mercury isotope chain the barrier heights and the ridge heights and persistence with elongation vary significantly and show no obvious connection to possible fragment shell structure, in contrast to the actinide region, where there is a deep asymmetric valley extending from the saddle point to scission. The mechanism of asymmetric fission must be very different in the lighter proton-rich mercury isotopes compared to the actinide region and is apparently unrelated to fragment shell structure. Isotopes lighter than $^{192}$Hg have the saddle point blocked from a deep symmetric valley by a significant ridge. The ridge vanishes for the heavier Hg isotopes, for which we would expect a qualitatively different asymmetry of the fragments.

## Key claim

The mechanism of asymmetric fission in neutron-deficient mercury isotopes (around A=180) is fundamentally different from that in actinides, and is apparently unrelated to fragment shell structure; in mercury, the asymmetry originates from local potential-energy structure near the saddle point, whereas in actinides a deep asymmetric valley extends from the saddle to scission.

## Method

The authors calculated five-dimensional potential-energy surfaces for 12 even mercury isotopes (178-200Hg) and for 236U using the macroscopic-microscopic Finite-Range Liquid-Drop Model (FRLDM) with the 2002 parameter set, evaluating energies at over five million grid points in a shape space defined by quadrupole moment, neck parameter, fragment deformations, and mass asymmetry. They applied an immersion technique to identify minima, saddle points, valleys, and ridges in the 5D deformation space, and compared the structure of the potential-energy surfaces between the mercury isotopes and the actinide nucleus 236U.

## Key numbers

- For 180Hg, the fission saddle point energy is 11.35 MeV, with shape parameters q2=7.84, epsilon_H=0.275, epsilon_L=0.30, and alpha_g=0.14 (corresponding to fragment masses 102.6/77.4)
- For 198Hg, the fission saddle point energy is 15.47 MeV, with shape parameters q2=10.08, epsilon_H=0.35, epsilon_L=0.10, and alpha_g=0.12 (corresponding to fragment masses 110.9/87.1)
- The ridge separating the optimal fission path from the symmetric fusion valley for 180Hg is initially 8 MeV above the saddle region; for 198Hg the corresponding ridge is only 2 MeV high
- For 180Hg, the separating ridge vanishes at q2=10.31, with fragment masses 108.0/72.0; for 198Hg, the ridge vanishes at q2=13.47, with fragment masses 115.8/81.8
- For 236U, the asymmetric valley is shielded from the symmetric valley by a ridge about 5 MeV high along its entire path
- The microscopic energy at scission for 236U is -12 MeV at q2=9, remaining substantial at -6 MeV at the saddle-point deformation q2=5; for 180Hg the microscopic energy fluctuates between +/-2 MeV along the trajectory
- The Brownian shape-motion model predicted fragment masses 104.4/75.6 for 180Hg, while the experimental result was 100/80, with the calculated width about twice the experimental result of 9 mass units

## Context

This work extends the analysis of high-dimensional potential-energy surfaces previously applied to actinides by Moller (2001, 2009) to the newly discovered asymmetric fission of 180Hg reported by Andreyev (2010). The results contradict simple fragment-shell arguments that would predict symmetric fission into two 90Zr fragments due to the N=50 magic number, and instead show that the potential-energy structure in mercury isotopes lacks the persistent asymmetric valley characteristic of actinides. The findings challenge fission theories to reproduce data across both regions without region-specific parameter adjustments, and the authors encourage further experiments in the mass range A=180-200 to provide additional constraints.
