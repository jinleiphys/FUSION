# Inelastic neutrino scattering off hot nuclei in supernova environments

> We study inelastic neutrino scattering off hot nuclei for temperatures relevant under supernova conditions. The method we use is based on the quasiparticle random phase approximation extended to finite temperatures within the thermo field dynamics (TQRPA). The method allows a transparent treatment of upward and downward transitions in hot nuclei, avoiding the application of Brink's hypothesis. For the sample nuclei $^{56}$Fe and $^{82}$Ge we perform a detailed analysis of thermal effects on the strength distributions of allowed Gamow-Teller (GT) transitions which dominate the scattering process at low neutrino energies. For $^{56}$Fe and $^{82}$Ge the finite temperature cross-sections are calculated by taking into account the contribution of allowed and forbidden transitions. The observed enhancement of the cross-section at low neutrino energies is explained by considering thermal effects on the GT strength. For $^{56}$Fe we compare the calculated cross-sections to those obtained earlier from a hybrid approach that combines large-scale shell-model and RPA calculations.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the TQRPA method, which avoids Brink's hypothesis, predicts temperature-dependent strength distributions for both upward and downward Gamow-Teller transitions in hot nuclei, leading to a significant enhancement of the inelastic neutrino-nucleus cross-section at low neutrino energies (below approximately 15 MeV) that cannot be fully captured by approaches relying on Brink's hypothesis.

## Method

The authors employ the thermal quasiparticle random phase approximation (TQRPA) within the thermo field dynamics (TFD) formalism to calculate inelastic neutrino-nucleus scattering cross-sections for the sample nuclei ⁵⁶Fe and ⁸²Ge at finite temperatures relevant for supernova conditions. The method diagonalizes a thermal Hamiltonian containing mean fields, BCS pairing, and separable particle-hole residual interactions (multipole and spin-multipole components up to J^π = 3^±), generating thermal phonon states whose energies and transition strengths are temperature-dependent. The cross-sections are computed using the Walecka-Donnelly formalism, summing over all allowed and forbidden transitions from the thermal vacuum to both non-tilde (upward) and tilde (downward) one-phonon states.

## Key numbers

* For ⁵⁶Fe at zero temperature, the lowest 1⁺ state has energy 4.06 MeV; for ⁸²Ge, the lowest 1⁺ state has energy 2.67 MeV.
* The proton and neutron pairing gaps at zero temperature are Δ_p = 1.57 MeV and Δ_n = 1.36 MeV for ⁵⁶Fe, and Δ_p = 1.22 MeV and Δ_n = 0.0 MeV for ⁸²Ge.
* The critical temperature for pairing collapse is T_cr ≈ 0.8 MeV for ⁵⁶Fe and T_cr ≈ 0.6 MeV for ⁸²Ge.
* At zero temperature for ⁵⁶Fe, the total cross-section at E_ν = 10 MeV is 1.69 × 10⁻⁴² cm²; at E_ν = 20 MeV it is 5.64 × 10⁻⁴² cm²; at E_ν = 60 MeV it is 2.87 × 10⁻⁴² cm².
* For ⁵⁶Fe at zero temperature, the GT₀ resonance centroid is located around 10 MeV excitation energy.
* For neutrino energies between 30 and 60 MeV, the GT₀ operator overestimates the cross-section by about 25% compared to the full q-dependent 1⁺ operator.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous TQRPA study on ⁵⁴Fe (Dzhioev et al., 2011) to the neutron-rich nucleus ⁸²Ge and provides a detailed comparison with the hybrid approach of Juodagalvis et al. (2005) and Kolbe et al. (2003), which combines large-scale shell-model calculations for Gamow-Teller transitions with RPA for forbidden transitions. The TQRPA method directly addresses the limitations of Brink's hypothesis used in the hybrid approach, following experimental evidence from studies of giant dipole resonances in hot nuclei (Bortignon, 1998; Santonocito, 2006) and shell-model Monte Carlo calculations (Radha et al., 1997) showing temperature-dependent strength distributions. The results enable more accurate modeling of neutrino transport in core-collapse supernova simulations, particularly for neutron-rich nuclei beyond the pf-shell that are relevant at later stages of collapse.
