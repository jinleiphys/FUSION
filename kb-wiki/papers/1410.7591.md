# The Scission-Point Configuration within the Two-Center Shell Model Shape Parameterization

> Within the two-center shell model parameterization we have defined the optimal shape which fissioning nuclei attain just before the scission and calculated the total deformation energy (liquid drop part plus the shell correction) as function of the mass asymmetry and elongation at the scission point. The three minima corresponding to mass symmetric and two mass asymmetric peaks in the mass distribution of fission fragments are found in the deformation energy at the scission point. The calculated deformation energy is used in quasi-static approximation for the estimation of the total kinetic and excitation energy of fission fragments and the total number of emitted prompt neutrons. The calculated results reproduce rather well the experimental data on the position of the peaks in the mass distribution of fission fragments, the total kinetic and excitation energy of fission fragments. The calculated value of neutron multiplicity is somewhat larger than experimental results.

## Key claim

The scission-point configuration can be formally defined as the maximal elongation at which a mononuclear shape exists, and the total deformation energy (liquid-drop plus shell correction) at this critical elongation exhibits three minima corresponding to mass-symmetric and two mass-asymmetric fission fragment peaks; however, the calculated neutron multiplicity is somewhat larger than experimental results, indicating that the quasi-static approximation may overestimate the excitation energy available for neutron emission.

## Method

The authors used the two-center shell model parameterization (TCSMP) to describe nuclear shapes, with five deformation parameters: distance between oscillator centers, mass asymmetry, deformations of left and right fragments, and a neck parameter. They minimized the liquid-drop energy at fixed elongation and mass asymmetry to find optimal shapes, then added the shell correction (calculated via Strutinsky averaging with a Woods-Saxon potential expanded in Cassini ovaloids) to obtain the total deformation energy at the scission point. The resulting energy surface was used in a canonical distribution (with a collective temperature parameter) to estimate mass yields, total kinetic energy, excitation energy, and prompt neutron multiplicity for thermal-neutron-induced fission of several actinides.

## Key numbers

- The maximal elongation for mononuclear shapes lies in the range \(2.32 \leq R_{12}^{\rm max}/R_0 \leq 2.35\) for fissility parameters \(0.4 \leq x_{\rm LD} \leq 0.9\).
- For symmetric splitting (\(\alpha=0\)), the difference between the deformation energy calculated with optimal shapes and TCSMP does not exceed 0.4 MeV.
- For mass asymmetry \(\alpha=0.2\), the difference in the barrier region is of order 0.8 MeV; with the \(\delta_1=\delta_2\) restriction, the difference reaches about 1.6 MeV at the barrier.
- At the scission point, the neck radius at which the shape splits into two fragments is approximately \(R_{\rm neck} \approx 0.3 R_0\).
- The collective temperature parameter used in the canonical distribution is \(T_{\rm coll} = 1.5\) MeV, somewhat larger than the value of about 1 MeV found in prior scission-point models.
- The three minima in the scission-point deformation energy for \(^{236}{\rm U}\) occur at heavy fragment masses \(A_H \approx 141\), \(A_H \approx 134\), and at symmetric mass split.

## Context

This work extends the optimal-shape method of Strutinsky (1968) by incorporating mass asymmetry and neck degrees of freedom within the two-center shell model parameterization, following earlier developments by Ivanyuk et al. (2008, 2012, 2014). The approach is similar to the scission-point model of Wilkins, Steinberg, and Chasman (1976) and its later refinements, but uses a formally defined scission point rather than a fitted neck-rupture criterion. The calculated results reproduce the peak positions in mass distributions, total kinetic energy, and excitation energy for thermal-neutron fission of \(^{232}{\rm Th}\), \(^{235}{\rm U}\), \(^{239}{\rm Pu}\), and \(^{245}{\rm Cm}\), enabling future dynamical calculations to use the scission-point energy surface as a starting condition for fragment de-excitation.
