# Shell model for heavy nuclei and its application in nuclear astrophysics

> Performing shell model calculations for heavy nuclei is a long-standing problem in nuclear physics. The shell model truncation in the configuration space is an unavoidable step. The Projected Shell Model (PSM) truncates the space under the guidance of the deformed mean-field solutions. This implies that the PSM uses a novel and efficient way to bridge the two conventional methods: the deformed mean-field approximations, which are widely applied to heavy nuclei but able to describe the physics only in the intrinsic frame, and the spherical shell model diagonalization method, which is most fundamental but feasible only for small systems. We discuss the basic philosophy in construction of the PSM (or generally PSM-like) approach. Several examples from the PSM calculations are presented. Astrophysical applications are emphasized.

## Key claim

The Projected Shell Model (PSM) provides an efficient truncation scheme for shell-model calculations in heavy nuclei by using a deformed mean-field basis, but the quality of results depends on the choice of effective interactions and single-particle states, which remain open questions for unexplored regions away from beta-stability.

## Method

The PSM starts with deformed Nilsson model plus BCS calculations to generate quasiparticle (qp) states, then restores broken rotational symmetry via angular-momentum-projection techniques to form a shell-model basis in the laboratory frame. A pairing plus quadrupole-quadrupole Hamiltonian with quadrupole-pairing term is diagonalized in this projected multi-qp basis, with the strength of the quadrupole-quadrupole force determined self-consistently from the quadrupole deformation parameter epsilon_2.

## Key numbers

* For $^{48}$Cr: PSM uses three major shells (N=1,2,3) for both neutrons and protons, truncated at deformation epsilon_2=0.25, with basis dimension less than 100; results agree with full pf-shell model and experimental data for gamma-ray energies and B(E2) values across the entire spin region.
* For $^{178}$Hf: calculation uses deformation parameters epsilon_2=0.251 and epsilon_4=0.056, with valence space including three major shells (N=4,5,6 for neutrons; N=3,4,5 for protons); the 31-year isomer lies at approximately 2.5 MeV excitation energy.
* For $^{254}$No: deformed basis constructed with epsilon_2=0.25, pairing strengths G_1=21.24, G_2=13.13, and G_Q=0.14; predicted bandhead energies for the two 8^- bands are 1.266 MeV and 1.516 MeV, close to the experimental 8^- isomer at 1.293 MeV; predicted bandhead energy for the 16^+ band is 2.750 MeV, corresponding to the experimental 184-microsecond isomer.
* For shape isomers in rp-process path: $^{68}$Se has an oblate ground state at epsilon_2 approximately -0.25 with a prolate minimum at epsilon_2 approximately 0.4 lying 1.1 MeV higher; $^{72}$Kr has a similar oblate ground state with prolate minimum at 0.7 MeV excitation, and the observed shape-isomer in $^{72}$Kr has energy 671 keV with half-life tau = 38 +/- 3 ns.

## Context

This work extends the spherical shell-model approach of Caurier et al. (2005) and the mean-field methods by bridging them through the PSM, which was originally developed by Hara and Iwasaki (1980). The paper demonstrates that PSM can reproduce results comparable to large-scale pf-shell model calculations for light nuclei like $^{48}$Cr (as benchmarked by Caurier et al.) while also handling heavy nuclei like $^{178}$Hf and superheavy nuclei like $^{254}$No. The astrophysical applications build on the work of Langanke and Martinez-Pinedo (2003) who showed that shell-model calculations could significantly modify nuclear astrophysics results, and the shape-isomer calculations extend the rp-process studies of Schatz et al. (1998) by showing that isomer branches could alter nucleosynthesis flow in x-ray bursts.
