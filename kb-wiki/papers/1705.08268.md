# Inhomogeneity growth in two-component fermionic systems

> The dynamics of fermionic many-body systems is investigated in the framework of Boltzmann-Langevin (BL) stochastic one-body approaches. Within the recently introduced BLOB model, we examine the interplay between mean-field effects and two-body correlations, of stochastic nature, for nuclear matter at moderate temperature and in several density conditions, corresponding to stable or mechanically unstable situations. Numerical results are compared to analytic expectations for the fluctuation amplitude of isoscalar and isovector densities, probing the link to the properties of the employed effective interaction, namely symmetry energy (for isovector modes) and incompressibility (for isoscalar modes). For unstable systems, clusterization is observed. The associated features are compared to analytical results for the typical length and time scales characterizing the growth of unstable modes in nuclear matter and for the isotopic variance of the emerging fragments. We show that the BLOB model is generally better suited than simplified approaches previously introduced to solve the BL equation, and it is therefore more advantageous in applications to open systems, like heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim
The BLOB model, which solves the Boltzmann-Langevin equation in full phase space, reproduces analytic expectations for fluctuation amplitudes in nuclear matter more accurately than simplified approaches like the Stochastic Mean Field (SMF) model, particularly because BLOB preserves the Poisson statistics for collision fluctuations (variance equals mean) regardless of the number of test particles, while SMF reduces variance by a factor equal to the number of test particles.

## Method
The authors performed Boltzmann-Langevin simulations of nuclear matter at moderate temperature (3 MeV) using the BLOB model, where fluctuations are introduced through stochastic nucleon-nucleon collisions involving extended phase-space portions (wave packets) with proper Pauli blocking. They compared the fluctuation amplitude of isoscalar and isovector densities to analytic expectations for Fermi liquids, examining both stable density conditions and mechanically unstable conditions where clusterization occurs. The study also compared BLOB results to the SMF approach, where fluctuations are projected onto coordinate space only.

## Key numbers
- At saturation density (rho_0 = 0.16 fm^-3), Pauli rejection rates were 98% in BLOB and 95% in SMF, resulting in different effective collision rates
- The variance of the number of effective collisions per nucleon in BLOB equals the mean value, with no dependence on the number of test particles (N_test), while in SMF the variance is reduced by a factor N_test relative to the mean
- Calculations were performed at temperature 3 MeV across several density conditions corresponding to stable or mechanically unstable situations

## Context
This work extends the Boltzmann-Langevin approach to nuclear matter dynamics, building on the BLOB model introduced by Napolitani (2013) and Rizzo (2008). It provides a quantitative benchmark for stochastic one-body approaches before applying them to open systems like heavy-ion collisions at Fermi energies, where fragment formation and isotopic distributions depend on fluctuation dynamics. The study contrasts with the SMF approach of Colonna (1998), which projects fluctuations only onto coordinate space, and with earlier Bauer and Bertsch (1987) approaches that applied Pauli blocking in average rather than to specific phase-space portions.
