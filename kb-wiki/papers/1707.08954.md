# Structure and decays of nuclear three-body systems: the Gamow coupled-channel method in Jacobi coordinates

> ${\bf Background:}$ Weakly bound and unbound nuclear states appearing around particle thresholds are prototypical open quantum systems. Theories of such states must take into account configuration mixing effects in the presence of strong coupling to the particle continuum space. ${\bf Purpose:}$ To describe structure and decays of three-body systems, we developed a Gamow coupled-channel (GCC) approach in Jacobi coordinates by employing the complex-momentum formalism. We benchmarked the new framework against the complex-energy Gamow Shell Model (GSM). ${\bf Methods:}$ The GCC formalism is expressed in Jacobi coordinates, so that the center-of-mass motion is automatically eliminated. To solve the coupled-channel equations, we use hyperspherical harmonics to describe the angular wave functions while the radial wave functions are expanded in the Berggren ensemble, which includes bound, scattering and Gamow states. ${\bf Results:}$ We show that the GCC method is both accurate and robust. Its results for energies, decay widths, and nucleon-nucleon angular correlations are in good agreement with the GSM results. ${\bf Conclusions:}$ We have demonstrated that a three-body GSM formalism explicitly constructed in cluster-orbital shell model coordinates provides similar results to a GCC framework expressed in Jacobi coordinates, provided that a large configuration space is employed. Our calculations for $A=6$ systems and $^{26}$O show that nucleon-nucleon angular correlations are sensitive to the valence-neutron interaction. The new GCC technique has many attractive features when applied to bound and unbound states of three-body systems: it is precise, efficient, and can be extended by introducing a microscopic model of the core.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Gamow coupled-channel (GCC) method in Jacobi coordinates and the Gamow Shell Model (GSM) in cluster-orbital shell model coordinates produce similar results for three-body nuclear systems, provided that a sufficiently large configuration space is employed; the key hedge is that the equivalence holds only when the model space is large enough, as the two frameworks differ in their treatment of center-of-mass motion and asymptotic behavior.

## Method

The authors developed a Gamow coupled-channel (GCC) approach using Jacobi coordinates to eliminate center-of-mass motion exactly, with hyperspherical harmonics describing angular wave functions and the Berggren ensemble (including bound, scattering, and Gamow states) expanding radial wave functions. They benchmarked GCC against the Gamow Shell Model (GSM), which uses cluster-orbital shell model coordinates and treats the center-of-mass motion via a recoil term expanded in a harmonic oscillator basis. Both methods employed the same Woods-Saxon core-valence potentials and Minnesota nucleon-nucleon interactions, with calculations performed for A=6 systems (He, Li, Be) and O-26.

## Key numbers

- For A=6 systems with ℓ_max=10, the energy difference between GCC and GSM predictions is around 20 keV for most states, with a maximum deviation of ~70 keV for the 3+ state of Li-6
- The ground state of He-5 is predicted at E=0.732 MeV with width Γ=0.622 MeV; the first excited 1/2- state at E=2.126 MeV with Γ=5.838 MeV
- For O-26 ground state: GCC gives 69 keV energy, GSM gives 101 keV; dominant GCC configurations are 46% (p,p) and 44% (s,s)
- For O-26 2+ state: GCC gives 1150 keV with width 14 keV, GSM gives 1137 keV with width 33 keV
- GCC calculations with K_max=40 show energy differences as little as 2 keV when varying K_max from 30 to 40
- The decay width for the 2+ state of He-6 is 42 keV (GCC) and 98 keV (GSM); for the 0+ state of Be-6 it is 54 keV (GCC) and 25 keV (GSM)

## Context

This work extends the Gamow Shell Model framework developed by Michel (2002) and the complex scaling method studies by Kruppa (2014) and Descouvemont (2003) for three-body nuclear systems. The new GCC method enables precise description of weakly bound and unbound three-body systems near particle thresholds, particularly relevant for understanding two-neutron emission from nuclei like O-26, which exhibits dineutron-type correlations as studied by Hagino (2016) and Fossez (2017). The approach can be extended by introducing a microscopic model of the core, and the demonstrated sensitivity of nucleon-nucleon angular correlations to the valence-neutron interaction provides a tool for constraining effective interactions in future experimental studies.
