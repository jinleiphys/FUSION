# About Entropy and Thermalization - a Miniworkshop Perspective

> We present a summary and perspective view of the Miniworkshop on ``{\it Entropy and Thermalization}'' in strong interactions (convener J. Rafelski), which was part of the NATO Advanced Research Workshop on ``{\it Hot Hadronic Matter}'' that took place in Divonne, 27 June to 1 July 1994. To appear in the Proceedings (Plenum Press).

## Key claim

This paper summarizes the Miniworkshop on "Entropy and Thermalization" in strong interactions, concluding that there exists a rich diversity (if not confusion) of concepts related to measuring entropy to characterize high-multiplicity events, and that the fundamental problem of characterizing these reactions is far from being solved.

## Method

The authors report on presentations and round-table discussions from a miniworkshop held as part of the NATO Advanced Research Workshop on "Hot Hadronic Matter" in Divonne, 27 June to 1 July 1994. They summarize contributions on decoherence theory, irreversibility in quantum physics, Bose-Einstein correlations, fireball models, parton cascade simulations, the Schwinger mechanism, environment-induced quantum decoherence, and chaos in classical Yang-Mills fields.

## Key numbers

- The parton cascade simulation shows rapid saturation of entropy production on a time scale of 0.5 fm/c or less, with specific entropy per particle S/N ≈ 4, approximately the ideal parton gas value.
- The maximal Lyapunov exponent of classical Yang-Mills fields yields a damping rate of coloured collective excitations proportional to g²T, with a thermalization time τ_S⁰ ≈ 0.5 fm/c at T ≈ 200 MeV.
- In the fireball model, approximately 70% of the measured entropy must be produced already during the pre-thermal phase.
- The primary contribution to entropy in the parton cascade amounts to about 40% of the total, stemming from the decoherence process when incoming hadronic wave functions are perturbed by initial-state QCD interactions.

## Context

This paper extends discussions from earlier work on entropy in strong interactions, referencing the work of Carruthers (Pete1) on correlation entropies and cumulants, and the work of Elze (Thomas, I, II) on environment-induced decoherence in QCD. It enables further study of how decoherence theory, classical Yang-Mills chaos, and parton cascade simulations can be connected to understand thermalization in heavy-ion collisions, and highlights the need to reconcile the fireball model approach of Letessier with two-particle correlation studies.
