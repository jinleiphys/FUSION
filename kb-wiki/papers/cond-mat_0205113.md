# Searches for violation of fundamental time reversal and space reflection symmetries in solid state experiments

> The electric dipole moment (EDM) of a particle violates both time reversal (T) and space reflection (P) symmetries. There have been recent suggestions for searches of the electron EDM using solid state experiments [1,2]. These experiments could improve the sensitivity compared to present atomic and molecular experiments by several orders of magnitude. In the present paper we calculate the expected effect. We also suggest that this kind of experiment is sensitive to T,P-violation in nuclear forces and calculate effects caused by the nuclear Schiff moment. The compounds under consideration contain magnetic Gd$^{3+}$ ions and oxygen O$^{2-}$ ions. We demonstrate that the main mechanism for the T,P-odd effects is related to the penetration of the Oxygen 2p-electrons to the Gd core. All the effects are related to the deformation of the crystal lattice.

## Key claim

The paper calculates that solid-state experiments with Gadolinium Gallium Garnet (GGG) and Gadolinium Iron Garnet (GdIG) could detect T,P-violation from both the electron electric dipole moment (EDM) and the nuclear Schiff moment, but the naive estimate of the induced voltage is canceled by the Schiff theorem and the actual effect depends on penetration of Oxygen 2p-electrons into the Gd core combined with lattice deformation.

## Method

The authors calculated the T,P-odd energy shift for Gd³⁺ ions in garnet crystals by matching Oxygen 2pσ-orbital wave functions to Gd 6s and 6p wave functions at a matching sphere of radius Γ around each Gd ion, using relativistic Hartree-Fock methods. They then computed the asymmetric charge density arising from lattice deformation (Gd ion displacement X relative to surrounding Oxygen ions) and evaluated the resulting energy correction via integrals over the Gd core region, accounting for both direct and compensating terms from the Schiff theorem.

## Key numbers

- Current limit on electron EDM from Thallium beam experiment: d_e < 1.6 × 10⁻²⁷ e·cm (Berkeley experiment)
- Current limit on nuclear Schiff moment from ¹⁹⁹Hg: S_N(¹⁹⁹Hg) < 0.7 × 10⁻⁴⁹ e·cm³ (Seattle experiment)
- Gd³⁺ EDM enhancement coefficient: K = -4.9 ± 1.6 (from prior calculation by Stefan)
- Number density of Gd in garnet: n_Gd = 1.235 × 10²² cm⁻³
- Naive induced voltage across L=10 cm sample (ignoring Schiff theorem): V = 2 × 10⁻⁹ V
- Dielectric constant: ε ≈ 15 for GdIG, ε ≈ 30 for GGG
- Naive T,P-odd energy shift per Gd ion (ignoring Schiff theorem): δε = 0.9 × 10⁻²² eV at E = 10 kV/cm
- Gd-O distance: 4.53 a_B (Bohr radii)
- Matching sphere radius used for calculations: Γ = 2.5 a_B
- Calculated integrals: I₁ = -4.1 × 10⁻³ a_B⁻¹, I₂ = -5.3 × 10⁻³ a_B⁻¹, I₃ = -7.5 × 10⁻³ a_B⁻¹, ⟨r_fd⟩ = 0.55 a_B
- Final coefficient for T,P-odd energy correction: A = 0.11, giving Δε(X) = -A (e/a_B²) (X/a_B) d_e

## Context

This work extends the solid-state EDM search proposals of Lamoreaux (1999) and Hunter (1999) for GGG and GdIG crystals by providing the first detailed calculation of the expected T,P-odd effects, including the crucial cancellation from the Schiff theorem that reduces the naive estimate. The method of matching wave functions at a sphere boundary follows the approach previously used by Sushkov and collaborators for calculating nuclear quadrupole resonance frequencies in La₂CuO₄, where it achieved ~10% accuracy. The results enable quantitative predictions for next-generation solid-state EDM experiments that could improve sensitivity by several orders of magnitude over current atomic beam experiments, and also establish sensitivity to nuclear Schiff moments from Gd isotopes.
