# Nonperturbative study of quantum many-body correlation effects in neutron stars: Equation of state

> Although neutron stars have been studied for decades, their internal structure remains enigmatic, mainly due to large uncertainties in the equation of state. In neutron stars, the nucleons are strongly interacting by exchanging mesons, which can lead to significant quantum many-body correlation effects. Mean-field calculations failed to capture these effects. Here, we develop a nonperturbative quantum field-theoretic approach to handle strongly correlated dense nuclear matter within the framework of quantum hadrodynamics. We show that the many-body effects can be incorporated in the Dyson-Schwinger equation of the nucleon propagator. Based on a linear $σ$-$ω$-$ρ$ model, we successfully reproduce six empirical observable quantities of saturation nuclear matter by tuning six parameters. After including the many-body effects into the equation of state of realistic neutron star matter, we obtain a mass-radius relation that is comparable with recent astrophysical observations of neutron stars.

## Key claim

The authors develop a nonperturbative quantum field-theoretic approach based on the Dyson-Schwinger equation for the nucleon propagator to incorporate many-body correlation effects into the neutron star equation of state, but the resulting mass-radius relation depends on tuning the symmetry energy slope \(L_{\mathrm{s}}\) to roughly 30-50 MeV to match observations, and the maximal mass of about \(2.67 M_{\odot}\) might be modified if additional degrees of freedom are considered.

## Method

The authors start from a linear \(\sigma\)-\(\omega\)-\(\rho\)-\(\delta\) quantum hadrodynamics model without meson self-couplings or cross-couplings, avoiding the instability problem of relativistic mean-field theory. They derive and numerically solve the self-consistent Dyson-Schwinger equation for the renormalized nucleon propagator \(G(k)\), decomposing it into three coupled nonlinear integral equations for the renormalization functions \(A_0(\varepsilon)\), \(A_1(\varepsilon)\), and \(A_2(\varepsilon)\) that encode many-body effects on wave function, velocity, and mass. After solving these equations iteratively until convergence (error factors below \(10^{-6}\)), they construct a renormalized Lagrangian density with averaged constants \(\bar{A}_{0,1,2}\) and apply standard relativistic mean-field theory to obtain analytical expressions for the equation of state and observable nuclear matter quantities.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density \(\rho_{\mathrm{B}0}\), binding energy per nucleon \(E_{\mathrm{b}}\), effective nucleon mass \(m^{\ast}_{\mathrm{N}}\), compressible modulus \(K\), symmetry energy \(E_{\mathrm{s}}\), and symmetry energy slope \(L_{\mathrm{s}}\) are reproduced by tuning six parameters (no explicit numerical values for these six quantities are given in the truncated text).
- The energy cutoff for the Dyson-Schwinger equation integration is \(\Omega_{\mathrm{c}} = 1000\) MeV, chosen because the Fermi energy at \(6\rho_{\mathrm{B}0}\) is roughly 1000 MeV.
- The maximal neutron star mass obtained is about \(2.67 M_{\odot}\).
- The symmetry energy slope \(L_{\mathrm{s}}\) is tuned to roughly 30-50 MeV to make radii of low-mass neutron stars comparable with astrophysical observations.
- Bare nucleon mass \(m_{\mathrm{N}} = 939\) MeV; meson masses are \(m_{\sigma} = 550\) MeV, \(m_{\omega} = 783\) MeV, \(m_{\rho} = 763\) MeV, and \(m_{\delta} = 980\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends quantum hadrodynamics beyond relativistic mean-field theory (Walecka, 1974; Boguta and Bodmer, 1977; Serot, 1979) by incorporating quantum many-body correlation effects that mean-field calculations fail to capture. The approach is inspired by the success of Eliashberg theory over BCS mean-field theory in superconductivity. The method enables computation of the neutron star equation of state and mass-radius relation at high densities where chiral effective field theory (Meissner; Machleidt) becomes unreliable (above \(2\rho_{\mathrm{B}0}\)) and where quantum Monte Carlo methods (Carlson, 2015) face fermion sign problems and finite-size effects.
