# Thermodynamics of magnetized matter in hot and dense QCD

> This chapter, to appear in the section on QCD under extreme conditions within the Encyclopedia of Nuclear Physics, aims to provide a pedagogical introduction to the physics of quarks and gluons in the presence of high temperature, nonzero (isospin) density and strong background electromagnetic fields. Extreme conditions of these types are relevant for the description of high-energy heavy-ion collisions, neutron stars and their mergers, as well as the evolution of the early Universe in its first microsecond. Most of the existing results on this topic have been obtained by means of first-principles simulations of the discretized theory of the strong interactions, lattice Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD). This lays the focus of this review chapter, although various calculations within effective theories of QCD -- most notably chiral perturbation theory -- are also discussed. Furthermore, we provide an outlook concerning open questions and yet uncharted parameter regions within this fascinating system.

## Key claim

This review chapter provides a pedagogical introduction to the thermodynamics of quarks and gluons under extreme conditions, high temperature, nonzero isospin density, and strong background electromagnetic fields, with the caveat that most first-principles lattice QCD results are limited to parameter regions where direct simulations are possible (vanishing baryon chemical potential, vanishing strangeness chemical potential, and real electric fields), while other regions require indirect methods such as Taylor expansions or analytic continuations.

## Method

The authors review results from first-principles lattice QCD simulations on a Euclidean space-time grid, where the partition function is evaluated via importance sampling of gluon configurations, and from chiral perturbation theory (χPT) at leading and next-to-leading order, which describes low-energy QCD dynamics in terms of pion fields. For the isospin-asymmetric case, lattice simulations include a pion source parameter λ to handle near-zero singular values of the Dirac operator in the pion condensed phase, which must be extrapolated to zero. For nonzero electromagnetic fields, the flux of magnetic and electric fields is quantized on finite periodic volumes as \(a^2 N_s^2 q_d B = 2\pi N_b\) and \(a^2 N_s N_t i q_d E = 2\pi N_e\) with integers \(N_b, N_e\).

## Key numbers

- The QCD crossover at zero density and zero electromagnetic field occurs at a temperature of about \(T_c \approx 150\) MeV (Aarts 2023).
- At magnetic fields above \(B_c \approx (4-9)\) GeV², the transition turns into a first-order phase transition through a second-order Z(2) critical endpoint (Endrodi 2015, D'Elia 2021).
- The transition to the pion condensed (BEC) phase at nonzero isospin chemical potential occurs at zero temperature at \(\mu_I = m_\pi \approx 135\) MeV via a second-order phase transition in the O(2) universality class (Brandt 2017).
- Within functional approaches, indications for a critical endpoint at large baryon chemical potential are observed at around \(\mu_B \approx 600\) MeV (Fischer 2026).
- The critical isospin chemical potential \(\mu_I^c\) equals the vacuum pion mass \(m_\pi^0\) at leading order in χPT (Son 2000).

## Context

This review extends the body of work on QCD thermodynamics under extreme conditions, building on lattice QCD results for the equation of state at nonzero isospin chemical potential (Brandt 2018, Abbott 2023, Abbott 2024, Vovchenko 2020, Brandt 2022) and for the effect of magnetic fields on thermodynamic observables (Endrodi 2024). It also discusses χPT predictions for the BEC phase boundary (Adhikari 2020) and the zero-temperature phase diagram in the \(\mu_I\)-\(\mu_S\) plane (Adhikari 2019). The chapter enables future work on uncharted parameter regions, such as the combined effects of nonzero baryon density, isospin density, and electromagnetic fields, and highlights open questions regarding the existence of a critical endpoint at large baryon chemical potential, color superconductivity, and inhomogeneous phases.
