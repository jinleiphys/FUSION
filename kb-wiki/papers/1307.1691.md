# The influence of Strong Magnetic Field in Hyperonic Neutron Stars

> The physics of neutron stars leads historically towards Landau's speculation. Even before the discovery of the neutron, he postulated the possible existence of stars more compact than white dwarfs, containing matter of the order of nuclear density. From a modern point of view neutron stars are compact objects maintained by the equilibrium between gravity and the degeneracy pressure of the fermions together with a strong nuclear repulsion force due to the high density reached in their interior. While the physics in the vicinity of nuclear saturation density is well know from phenomenology, the physics of ultra-dense nuclear matter is still an open puzzle. In this work we study dense nuclear matter within a relativistic model, allowing hyperons to be present through beta equilibrium. The presence of hyperons is justifiable since the constituents of neutron stars are fermions. So, according to the Pauli principle, as the baryon density increases, so do the Fermi momentum and the Fermi energy. On the other hand, this hyperonic matter softens the equation of state (EoS) and a recent measurement of pulsar PSR J1614-2230 implies that the EoS has to be stiff enough to produce a 2.0 $M_{\odot}$ pulsar. We also consider Duncan's magnetar ideas and study the influence of strong magnetic fields on the EoS. We see that a strong magnetic field produces very massive neutron stars, in agreement with the astronomical observations.

# The influence of Strong Magnetic Field in Hyperonic Neutron Stars

## Key claim
Strong magnetic fields (up to ~10^18 G) can stiffen the equation of state of hyperonic neutron star matter enough to produce stars with masses up to 2.22 solar masses, consistent with the observed 2.0 solar mass pulsar PSR J1614-2230, but only if the hyperon-meson coupling parametrization (GC or SU(6)) and magnetic field strength are both chosen appropriately; the SU(6) parametrization fails to reach 2.0 solar masses without a strong magnetic field.

## Method
The authors model dense nuclear matter in beta equilibrium using the non-linear Walecka model (NLWM) with the GM1 parametrization, including the full baryon octet (nucleons and hyperons) and leptons (electrons and muons) in a mean-field approximation. They incorporate a density-dependent magnetic field via Landau level quantization for charged particles, and solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to obtain mass-radius relations. Two hyperon-meson coupling schemes are compared: the Glendenning Conjecture (GC) and SU(6) symmetry, with magnetic field strengths of 1.0 × 10^17 G (weak) and 3.1 × 10^18 G (strong).

## Key numbers
- Maximum neutron star mass with GC coupling and weak magnetic field (0.1 × 10^18 G): 2.01 solar masses, radius 11.86 km, central density 0.952 fm^-3, strangeness fraction 0.21
- Maximum neutron star mass with GC coupling and strong magnetic field (3.1 × 10^18 G): 2.22 solar masses, radius 11.80 km, central density 0.842 fm^-3, strangeness fraction 0.18
- Maximum neutron star mass with SU(6) coupling and weak magnetic field (0.1 × 10^18 G): 1.78 solar masses, radius 12.75 km, central density 0.787 fm^-3, strangeness fraction 0.24
- Maximum neutron star mass with SU(6) coupling and strong magnetic field (3.1 × 10^18 G): 2.09 solar masses, radius 11.95 km, central density 0.590 fm^-3, strangeness fraction 0.16
- Lowest mass with strangeness fraction of 5%: ranges from 1.65 solar masses (SU(6), weak field) to 1.81 solar masses (GC, strong field)
- Strong magnetic field can increase the maximum mass by almost 18%

## Context
This work extends the study of hyperonic neutron stars by Lopes and collaborators, using the GM1 parametrization of the NLWM, and builds on the magnetar concept introduced by Duncan. It addresses the tension between the softening of the equation of state caused by hyperons and the requirement from the Demorest measurement of PSR J1614-2230 (2010) that the equation of state must support a 2.0 solar mass pulsar. The results show that strong magnetic fields can reconcile hyperonic matter with observed massive pulsars, and that the choice of hyperon coupling scheme (GC vs. SU(6)) significantly affects the predicted maximum mass and composition. The work enables further studies of magnetar structure and the role of magnetic fields in dense matter, and notes that the GM3 parametrization (studied in a companion paper by Lopes) gives different density-mass relations.
