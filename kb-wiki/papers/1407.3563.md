# Collective Hamiltonian for wobbling modes

> The simple, longitudinal, and transverse wobblers are systematically studied within the framework of collective Hamiltonian, where the collective potential and mass parameter included are obtained based on the tilted axis cranking approach. Solving the collective Hamiltonian by diagonalization, the energies and the wave functions of the wobbling states are obtained. The obtained results are compared with those by harmonic approximation formula and particle rotor model. The wobbling energies calculated by the collective Hamiltonian are closer to the exact solutions by particle rotor model than harmonic approximation formula. It is confirmed that the wobbling frequency increases with the rotational frequency in simple and longitudinal wobbling motions while decreases in transverse wobbling motion. These variation trends are related to the stiffness of the collective potential in the collective Hamiltonian.

## Key claim

The collective Hamiltonian approach, using a collective potential from tilted axis cranking (TAC) and a mass parameter derived from the harmonic frozen alignment (HFA) approximation, reproduces wobbling energies closer to the exact particle rotor model (PRM) solutions than the simple harmonic approximation (HA) formula, particularly for higher wobbling phonon numbers. The paper confirms that the wobbling frequency increases with rotational frequency for simple and longitudinal wobblers but decreases for transverse wobblers, with these trends linked to the stiffness of the collective potential.

## Method

The authors constructed a one-dimensional collective Hamiltonian using the azimuthal angle φ as the collective coordinate, with the collective potential obtained by minimizing the TAC total Routhian with respect to the polar angle θ for each φ. The mass parameter was derived analytically: for the simple wobbler it is a constant based on the three principal moments of inertia, while for longitudinal and transverse wobblers it depends on rotational frequency via an effective moment of inertia that includes the odd particle's angular momentum. The collective Hamiltonian was then solved by diagonalization to obtain wobbling energy levels and wave functions, and the results were compared with the harmonic approximation formula and exact particle rotor model solutions.

## Key numbers

- For the simple wobbler at ℏω = 0.1 MeV, the energy difference between φ = ±90° and φ = 0° is ~30 keV; at ℏω = 0.4 MeV, this difference reaches ~600 keV.
- For the longitudinal wobbler at ℏω = 0.1 MeV, the energy difference between φ = ±90° and φ = 0° is ~540 keV; at ℏω = 0.4 MeV, it reaches ~2080 keV.
- The proton contribution to the longitudinal wobbler potential increases from ~510 keV at ℏω = 0.1 MeV to 1480 keV at ℏω = 0.4 MeV.
- The mass parameter for the simple wobbler is constant, given by B = (J₂ J₃)/(J₁ - J₃), where J₁, J₂, J₃ are the moments of inertia of the three principal axes.
- For longitudinal and transverse wobblers, the mass parameter is B(ω) = (J₂ J₃)/((J₁ - J₃) + j/ω), where j is the odd particle angular momentum and ω is the rotational frequency.
- The critical rotational frequency for the transverse wobbler, where the wobbling frequency reaches zero, is ℏω_c = j/(J₂ - J₁).

## Context

This work extends the collective Hamiltonian formalism previously applied to chiral vibrations (Chen et al., 2013) to systematically study three types of wobbling motion: simple, longitudinal, and transverse. It builds on the classification of wobbling modes introduced by Frauendorf and Dönau (2014), who derived analytic HFA formulas for longitudinal and transverse wobblers, and on the particle rotor model calculations of Hamamoto (2002, 2003) and Tanabe (2006, 2008). The approach enables a microscopic interpretation of wobbling frequency trends in terms of the stiffness of the collective potential, and it provides a framework that can be applied to realistic nuclear systems where experimental wobbling bands have been observed in Lu isotopes (Odegard, 2001; Jensen, 2002; Schonwasser, 2003) and in ¹³⁵Pr (Frauendorf, 2014).
