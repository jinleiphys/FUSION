# Inspection of the detection cross section dependence of the Gallium Anomaly

> We discuss in detail the dependence of the Gallium Anomaly on the detection cross section. We provide updated values of the size of the Gallium Anomaly and find that its significance is larger than about $5σ$ for all the detection cross section models. We discuss the dependence of the Gallium Anomaly on the assumed value of the half life of ${}^{71}\text{Ge}$, which determines the cross sections of the transitions from the ground state of ${}^{71}\text{Ga}$ to the ground state of ${}^{71}\text{Ge}$. We show that a value of the ${}^{71}\text{Ge}$ half life which is larger than the standard one can reduce or even solve the Gallium Anomaly. Considering the short-baseline neutrino oscillation interpretation of the Gallium Anomaly, we show that a value of the ${}^{71}\text{Ge}$ half life which is larger than the standard one can reduce the tension with the results of other experiments. Since the standard value of the ${}^{71}\text{Ge}$ half life was measured in 1985, we advocate the importance of new measurements with modern technique and apparatus for a better assessment of the Gallium Anomaly.

## Key claim

The Gallium Anomaly's statistical significance exceeds about 5σ for all detection cross section models considered, but a value of the ⁷¹Ge half life larger than the standard one (11.43 ± 0.03 d) can reduce or even eliminate the anomaly, and can also reduce the tension between the Gallium data and other short-baseline neutrino oscillation experiments.

## Method

The authors analyzed data from the GALLEX, SAGE, and BEST radioactive source experiments, which measure the production rate of ⁷¹Ge from electron neutrino capture on ⁷¹Ga. They constructed an improved χ² function that incorporates a lower bound on the detection cross section given by the Ground State model (which assumes negligible transitions to excited states of ⁷¹Ge), and applied this to six different cross section models (Ground State, Bahcall, Haxton, Frekers, Kostensalo, and Semenov). They then examined how the average ratio of measured to predicted ⁷¹Ge production rates and the anomaly significance change when varying the assumed ⁷¹Ge half life across the range of published measurements.

## Key numbers

- The average ratio of measured to predicted ⁷¹Ge production rates using the Hampel and Remsberg (HR) half life is 0.845 ± 0.031 for the Ground State model, 0.804 ± 0.037 for Bahcall, 0.731 ± 0.088 for Haxton, 0.789 ± 0.033 for Frekers, 0.825 ± 0.031 for Kostensalo, and 0.787 ± 0.033 for Semenov.
- The significance of the Gallium Anomaly (in σ) for each model with the HR half life is: Ground State 5.0, Bahcall 5.2, Haxton 5.1, Frekers 6.1, Kostensalo 5.5, Semenov 6.1.
- Published measurements of the ⁷¹Ge half life differ by about 17%, ranging from 10.5 ± 0.4 d (Rudstam, 1956) to 12.5 ± 0.1 d (Bisi et al., 1955), with the standard HR value being 11.43 ± 0.03 d (Hampel and Remsberg, 1985).
- In the Ground State model, using the largest half life measurement (12.5 ± 0.1 d) reduces the anomaly to 0.924 ± 0.035, corresponding to about 2.2σ.
- To cancel the Gallium Anomaly in the Ground State model requires T₁/₂(⁷¹Ge) = 13.5 ± 0.5 d.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the Gallium Anomaly first identified in GALLEX and SAGE data (Abdurashitov, 2005; Giunti, 2006) and recently confirmed by the BEST experiment (Barinov, 2021, 2022). The authors improve upon their own previous χ² treatment (Giunti, 2022) by implementing a physically motivated lower bound on the cross section, which avoids the "Peelle's Pertinent Puzzle" problem. The paper highlights that the standard ⁷¹Ge half life measurement dates from 1985 (Hampel and Remsberg) and advocates for new measurements with modern techniques, as a larger half life could resolve the anomaly without invoking sterile neutrino oscillations.
