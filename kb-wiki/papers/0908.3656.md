# Causal Viscous Hydrodynamics for Relativistic Heavy Ion Collisions

> The viscosity of the QGP is a presently hotly debated subject. Since its computation from first principles is difficult, it is desirable to try to extract it from experimental data. Viscous hydrodynamics provides a tool that can attack this problem and which may work in regions where ideal hydrodynamics begins to fail. This thesis focuses on viscous hydrodynamics for relativistic heavy ion collisions. We first review the 2nd order viscous equations obtained from different approaches, and then report on the work of the Ohio State University group on setting up the equations for causal viscous hydrodynamics in 2+1 dimensions and solving them numerically for central and noncentral Cu+Cu and Au+Au collisions at RHIC energies and above. We discuss shear and bulk viscous effects on the hydrodynamic evolution of entropy density, temperature, collective flow, and flow anisotropies, and on the hadron multiplicity, single particle spectra and elliptic flow. Viscous entropy production and its influence on the centrality dependence of hadron multiplicities and the multiplicity scaling of eccentricity-scaled elliptic flow are studied in viscous hydrodynamics and compared with experimental data. The dynamical effects of using different versions of the Israel-Stewart second order formalism for causal viscous fluid dynamics are discussed, resolving some of the apparent discrepancies between early results reported by different groups. Finally, we assess the present status of constraining the shear viscosity to entropy ratio of the hot and dense matter created at RHIC.

## Key claim

This thesis demonstrates that causal viscous hydrodynamics (specifically the Israel-Stewart second-order formalism implemented in the VISH2+1 code) can successfully describe relativistic heavy ion collisions at RHIC energies, but the sign and magnitude of viscous corrections to azimuthally averaged particle spectra are fragile and depend on details of the dynamical evolution and hydrodynamic properties on the freeze-out surface, unlike the more robust viscous corrections to elliptic flow.

## Method

The author developed and tested VISH2+1 (Viscous Israel-Stewart Hydrodynamics in 2+1 dimensions), a numerical code that solves the second-order Israel-Stewart equations for causal viscous hydrodynamics with shear and bulk viscosity. The code was validated by comparing its ideal fluid limit against the existing AZHYDRO code, by reproducing analytical (0+1)-dimensional Navier-Stokes solutions for boost-invariant longitudinal expansion, and by demonstrating that the Israel-Stewart algorithm reduces to the Navier-Stokes limit for small relaxation times. The code was then applied to central and noncentral Cu+Cu and Au+Au collisions at RHIC energies using various equations of state.

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio of η/s = 0.08 used for viscous calculations of freeze-out times in Au+Au and Cu+Au collisions
- Initial temperature T₀ = 360 MeV at initial time τ₀ = 0.6 fm/c used for the (0+1)-dimensional analytical test
- Relaxation time τ_π = 3η/sT = 0.03 fm/c used for the Navier-Stokes limit test with η/s = T/(2 GeV)
- Freeze-out energy density e_dec = 0.085 GeV/fm³ used for both the blast wave model and hydrodynamic model comparisons
- Blast wave model parameters for Cu+Cu: R₀ = 6 fm, a₀ = 0.5, τ_dec = 4.1 fm/c
- Critical temperature range from lattice QCD: 185 MeV < T_c < 195 MeV (Bazavov et al.) with alternative range 155 MeV < T_c < 174 MeV (Aoki et al.)

## Context

This work extends the ideal hydrodynamic framework that successfully described strong collective flow at RHIC (Kolb 2003, Huovinen 2003) by incorporating viscous effects, addressing the need to extract QGP viscosity from experimental data. The thesis resolves apparent discrepancies between early viscous hydrodynamics results reported by different groups by comparing different versions of the Israel-Stewart formalism. The VISH2+1 code enables systematic studies of shear and bulk viscosity effects on observables, and the comparison with the blast wave model (Teaney 2003) reveals that the blast wave approach has serious limitations for estimating viscous corrections to spectra.
