# Possible bound nuclei beyond the two-neutron drip line in the $50\leqslant Z \leqslant 70$ region

> Possible bound nuclei beyond the two-neutron drip line in the $50\leqslant Z \leqslant 70$ region are investigated by using the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum with density functional PC-PK1. Bound nuclei beyond the drip lines of $_{56}$Ba, $_{58}$Ce, $_{62}$Sm, $_{64}$Gd and $_{66}$Dy are predicted, forming peninsulas of stability in nuclear landscape. Near these peninsulas, several multi-neutron emitters are predicted. The underlying mechanism of the peninsulas of stability is investigated by studying the total energy, Fermi surface, quadrupole deformation and the single-neutron spectrum in the canonical basis. It is found that the deformation effect is crucial for forming the peninsulas of stability, and pairing correlations are also essential in specific cases. The dependence on the deformation evolution is also discussed. The decay rates of multi-neutron radioactivity in Ba and Sm isotopic chains are estimated by using the direct decay model.

# Possible bound nuclei beyond the two-neutron drip line in the $50\leqslant Z \leqslant 70$ region

## Key claim
Using the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (DRHBc) with density functional PC-PK1, bound nuclei are predicted beyond the two-neutron drip line for Ba, Ce, Sm, Gd, and Dy isotopes, forming peninsulas of stability. The deformation effect is crucial for forming these peninsulas, and pairing correlations are also essential in specific cases, particularly for Sm, Gd, and Dy where deformation alone is insufficient.

## Method
The deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov equations were solved in a spherical Dirac Woods-Saxon basis with box size 20 fm and mesh size 0.1 fm, using the point-coupling density functional PC-PK1. A density-dependent zero-range pairing force with strength V0 = -325 MeV fm^3 and a pairing window of 100 MeV was employed. The total energy, neutron Fermi energy, quadrupole deformation, and single-neutron spectrum in the canonical basis were analyzed for even-even nuclei near and beyond the neutron drip line in the region Z = 50 to 70. Multi-neutron emission decay rates were estimated using the direct decay model.

## Key numbers
- The two-neutron drip line for Sn through Ba (Z=50-56) lies at the magic number N=126; for Ce (Z=58) the drip line extends to N=128; for Nd (Z=60) the drip line extends dramatically to N=154.
- Three Ba isotopes with N=136 to 140 and nine Ce isotopes with N=134 to 150 are predicted as bound nuclei beyond the drip line.
- Four isolated bound nuclei beyond the drip line are predicted: 232Sm, 238Gd, 240Gd, and 250Dy.
- For Ba isotopes, the quadrupole deformation β2 increases from 0.135 at N=132 to 0.217 at N=140, corresponding to the region of bound nuclei beyond the drip line.
- The neutron Fermi energy for 188Ba (N=132) becomes negative again after being positive at N=128 and 130, with a value of -0.01 MeV at N=142 where S2n = -0.05 MeV.
- For 232Sm (N=170), the neutron Fermi energy is negative and the total energy is lower than that of the drip-line nucleus 224Sm (N=162), making it a bound nucleus beyond the drip line.
- The estimated lower limit of the half-life for 188Ba via 4n emission is approximately 10^-14 s for the (s^2 d^2) configuration, and via 6n emission approximately 10^-10 s for the [s^2 d^4] configuration.
- The decay energies for 188Ba are Q4n = 0.181 MeV and Q6n = 0.463 MeV.

## Context
This work extends earlier predictions of reentrant stability beyond the neutron drip line found by Stoitsov (2003) using nonrelativistic Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations in a transformed harmonic oscillator basis, and by Afanasjev (2013) using relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov calculations in a harmonic oscillator basis. Unlike those studies, the present work uses the DRHBc theory which properly treats continuum effects and deformation simultaneously, and also examines stability against multi-neutron emission rather than only two-neutron separation energy. The results enable future experimental searches for multi-neutron radioactivity in heavy nuclei, with estimated half-lives that may be accessible to current experimental techniques.
