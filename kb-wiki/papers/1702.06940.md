# The Core-Collapse Supernova Explosion Mechanism

> The explosion mechanism of core-collapse supernovae is a long-standing problem in stellar astrophysics. We briefly outline the main contenders for a solution and review recent efforts to model core-collapse supernova explosions by means of multi-dimensional simulations. We discuss several suggestions for solving the problem of missing or delayed neutrino-driven explosions in three-dimensional supernova models, including -- among others -- variations in the microphysics and large seed perturbations in convective burning shells. Focusing on the neutrino-driven mechanism, we summarise currents efforts to predict supernova explosion and remnant properties based on first-principle models and on more phenomenological approaches.

# The Core-Collapse Supernova Explosion Mechanism

## Key claim

The neutrino-driven mechanism can explain core-collapse supernova explosions, but three-dimensional simulations have shown a trend toward missing or delayed explosions compared to two-dimensional models, suggesting that missing physical ingredients, such as variations in microphysics or large seed perturbations from convective burning shells, may be needed to achieve robust shock revival across a wide range of progenitor masses.

## Method

The paper reviews multi-dimensional neutrino hydrodynamics simulations of core-collapse supernovae, focusing on the neutrino-driven mechanism. It examines how three-dimensional simulations with multi-group neutrino transport have been compared to axisymmetric (2D) models, and analyzes the role of turbulent convection, the standing accretion shock instability (SASI), progenitor asphericities, rotation, and magnetic fields in facilitating shock revival. The review also discusses parameterised one-dimensional models used to predict explosion and remnant properties.

## Key numbers

- Explosion energies in neutrino-driven models have an upper limit of approximately \(2 \times 10^{51} \, \mathrm{erg}\) (from parameterised models).
- Hypernova explosion energies reach up to approximately \(10^{52} \, \mathrm{erg}\), which are likely out of reach for the neutrino-driven mechanism.
- A reduction of neutral-current scattering opacities of approximately 20% in the neutrinospheric region was sufficient to tip the scale in favour of an explosion in a \(20 M_\odot\) progenitor (Melson 2015b).
- Initial convective Mach numbers greater than approximately 0.05 and dominant wavenumbers \(\ell \lesssim 4\) are required for a significant reduction of the critical luminosity in the perturbation-aided mechanism (Mueller 2015a).
- In a simulation of an \(18 M_\odot\) progenitor with 3D initial conditions, a neutrino-driven explosion developed 250 ms after bounce, whereas shock revival did not occur in a control run with spherically symmetric initial conditions for at least another 300 ms (Mueller 2016b).
- Pre-collapse spin periods of approximately 1 s are required for rotation to have a dramatic effect on shock revival (Takiwaki 2016).
- Strong initial magnetic fields of approximately \(10^{12} \, \mathrm{G}\) are required for magnetic fields to play a subsidiary role in neutrino-driven explosions (Obergaulinger 2014).
- Current stellar evolution models predict pre-collapse spin periods of greater than approximately 30 s and magnetic fields of less than approximately \(10^{10} \, \mathrm{G}\) for solar-metallicity supernova progenitors (Heger 2005).
- Typical observed explosion energies are \(5 \times 10^{50} \, \mathrm{erg}\) to \(9 \times 10^{50} \, \mathrm{erg}\) (Kasen 2009).
- Parameterised models show explosions up to approximately \(15 M_\odot\) and several islands of explodability alternating with black hole formation up to approximately \(30 M_\odot\).

## Context

This review extends the long-standing effort to understand the core-collapse supernova explosion mechanism, building on earlier reviews such as Janka (2012) and on decades of multi-dimensional simulations. It addresses the recent challenge that three-dimensional models produce missing or delayed explosions compared to two-dimensional models, a discrepancy first highlighted by Hanke (2012) and Tamborra (2014a). The paper discusses potential solutions including microphysics uncertainties explored by Melson (2015b) and the perturbation-aided mechanism proposed by Couch (2013) and Mueller (2015a). It enables future work by identifying the need for self-consistent 3D progenitor models and by advocating a two-pronged approach combining first-principle simulations with parameterised models to predict supernova explosion and remnant properties.
