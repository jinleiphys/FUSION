# Pion Interferometry for Hydrodynamical Expanding Source with a Finite Baryon Density

> We calculate the two-pion correlation function for an expanding hadron source with a finite baryon density. The space-time evolution of the source is described by relativistic hydrodynamics and the Hanbury-Brown-Twiss (HBT) radius is extracted after effects of collective expansion and multiple scattering on the HBT interferometry have been taken into account, using quantum probability amplitudes in a path-integral formalism. We find that this radius is substantially smaller than the HBT radius extracted from the freeze-out configuration.

# Pion Interferometry for Hydrodynamical Expanding Source with a Finite Baryon Density

## Key claim
The HBT radius extracted from the two-pion correlation function, when calculated using quantum probability amplitudes in a path-integral formalism that accounts for collective expansion and multiple scattering, is substantially smaller than the HBT radius extracted from the conventional freeze-out configuration. This challenges the conventional assumption that HBT interferometry measures the freeze-out source distribution.

## Method
The authors model the space-time evolution of a hadron source (nucleons, deltas, and pions) with finite baryon density using relativistic hydrodynamics with spherical geometry, solving the conservation equations for energy, momentum, and baryon number via the HLLE scheme and Sod's operator splitting method. They then construct the two-pion correlation function using quantum probability amplitudes in a path-integral formalism, incorporating Glauber's multiple scattering model for pion absorption by nucleons (via delta resonance formation) and the trajectories of test pions from the hydrodynamical solution. The HBT radius is extracted by fitting the correlation function to a Gaussian parameterization.

## Key numbers
- Initial energy density: $\epsilon_0 = 0.5$ GeV/fm$^3$, with Gaussian radius $R_0 = 4.0$ fm.
- Initial temperature at $r=0$: $T_0 = 137$ MeV; initial baryon density at $r=0$: $n_{b0} = 0.36$ fm$^{-3}$.
- Freeze-out temperature: $T_f = 0.5 T_0$ (approximately 68.5 MeV).
- HBT radius from freeze-out configuration [case (a)]: 6.87 fm.
- HBT radius from the present formalism including absorption [case (b)]: 5.31 fm.
- HBT radius from hydrodynamical expansion without absorption [case (c)]: 4.32 fm.
- Delta resonance width: $\Gamma = 120$ MeV.
- Estimated critical temperature for quark-gluon plasma to hadronic gas transition: $T_c = 160$ MeV.

## Context
This work extends the quantum-mechanical treatment of HBT interferometry proposed by Wong (2003), applying it to a hydrodynamically expanding source with finite baryon density, in contrast to earlier conventional treatments that assumed HBT measures the freeze-out configuration. The results contradict the conventional viewpoint (as noted by Molínar et al., 2004; Pratt, 2003; and Heinz, 2002) that could not explain the insensitivity of HBT radii to collision energy and the $R_{\rm out}/R_{\rm side} \approx 0.9-1.1$ values observed at RHIC by PHENIX (2002) and STAR (2001). The approach enables more realistic extraction of source sizes from HBT data by properly accounting for rescattering and collective dynamics within a quantum-mechanical framework.
