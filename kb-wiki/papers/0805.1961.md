# The effect of atomic electrons on nuclear fission

> We calculate correction to the nuclear fission barrier produced by the atomic electrons. The result presented in analytical form is convenient to use in future nuclear calculations. The atomic electrons have a small stabilizing effect on nuclei, increasing lifetime in nuclear fission channel. This effect gives a new instrument to study the fission process.

## Key claim

Atomic electrons produce a small stabilizing effect on nuclear fission, increasing the fission barrier and thus the nuclear lifetime; the effect is small for all known nuclei but may be detectable by comparing fission probabilities of bare nuclei (stripped of all electrons) with those in neutral atoms, and is expected to be more significant for superheavy nuclei near the hypothetical "stability island" (Z=114 to 126).

## Method

The authors performed relativistic Hartree-Fock calculations for neutral atoms with nuclear charges Z from 80 to 160, solving the Dirac equation self-consistently for single-electron orbitals using a Fermi distribution for nuclear charge density. They computed the total electron energy for two different nuclear radii (representing the minimum and maximum of the fission barrier, assuming a 10% change in radius) and used a simple parabolic barrier model to estimate the resulting change in fission tunneling probability. The numerical results were fitted to an analytical formula for the total electron energy as a function of nuclear charge Z and nuclear radius r_N.

## Key numbers

- For Z=120 (near the stability island), the change in total electron energy between the two nuclear radii is 0.00118 MeV, corresponding to a 1.5% decrease in fission probability (assuming ℏω=0.5 MeV).
- For Z=130, the change in total electron energy is 0.00327 MeV, corresponding to a 4.0% decrease in fission probability.
- For Z=160, the change in total electron energy is 0.06669 MeV, corresponding to a 57% decrease in fission probability (roughly doubling the nuclear lifetime).
- The 1s electrons contribute about 30% of the total electron energy and more than half of its change due to the change of nuclear radius.
- At ℏω=1 MeV, the change in fission probability is about 2 times smaller than at ℏω=0.5 MeV.
- The analytical fit formula for total electron energy (Eq. etotal) is accurate to a few percent from Z=1 up to Z=100.

## Context

This work extends the study of electron effects on nuclear processes, contrasting with the muon case considered by Leander (where the muon is inside the nucleus and a rotating-frame calculation is appropriate). The authors provide an analytical formula that can be used in nuclear calculations of fission probabilities, enabling a new experimental approach: comparing fission rates of bare nuclei versus neutral atoms to study the fission process. The effect is much smaller for alpha and proton emission barriers, but the same formulas can be used to estimate those cases.
