# Bootstrapping Two-Nucleon Effective Field Theories

> Chiral EFT yields singular potentials that require regularization and renormalization when implemented in a dynamical equation such as the Lippmann--Schwinger equation. We employ two different approaches, renormalization with contact terms -- as is most commonly done in chiral EFT -- and the exact N/D method with multiple subtractions. We start with a toy model in which we can control the finite-range expansion of the potential, treating the full potential as the `exact' theory. To assess the statistical consistency of the approaches with the full theory, we use the bootstrap technique. We apply the same framework to study the consistency of chiral EFT at LO and NLO with the Granada phase-shift analysis in the $^1S_0$ two-nucleon partial wave. Our results show that the NLO potential significantly extends the energy range over which the theory remains valid.

# Bootstrapping Two-Nucleon Effective Field Theories

## Key claim
The exact N/D method with multiple subtractions can reproduce the phase shifts of a singular two-nucleon potential without any regulator, and the bootstrap technique shows that including the next-to-leading-order (NLO) finite-range interaction significantly extends the energy range over which the effective theory remains statistically consistent with the exact theory, provided enough renormalization conditions are imposed.

## Method
The authors construct a toy model potential consisting of a regular one-pion-exchange-like term plus three singular terms that together yield a regular short-range behavior. They solve the scattering problem using two approaches: (i) the standard Lippmann-Schwinger equation with a super-Gaussian regulator (cutoff 600 MeV) and contact-term renormalization, and (ii) the exact N/D method with multiple subtractions, which requires no regulator. They generate pseudo-experimental phase-shift data from the full potential with Gaussian noise, then perform bootstrap analyses (2000 experiments) to test whether the truncated potentials (LO: only the longest-range term; NLO: adding the next-longest-range term) are statistically consistent with the full theory at various precision levels and energy ranges.

## Key numbers
- Cutoff used for the regularized approach: Λ = 600 MeV.
- Toy model parameters: m_π = 138.5 MeV, m_1 = 1000 MeV, m_2 = 1200 MeV, m_N = 938.919 MeV, α = 0.1, α_1 = +5.0 GeV^{-2} (repulsive case) or −3.0 GeV^{-2} (attractive case).
- Effective-range expansion parameters of the full potential: scattering length a = −0.61518 fm, effective range r = 28.148 fm, shape parameter v_2 = 19.007 fm^3.
- For the repulsive case with three renormalization conditions (N/D_22), LO theory is accurate to 0.01° up to ≈150 MeV, while NLO theory extends this to ≈300 MeV.
- Bootstrap χ²/dof values for N/D_22 with k_max = 400 MeV and Δδ = 0.01°: 0.999 ± 0.071 (bootstrap) vs. 0.998^{+0.073}_{-0.069} (theoretical distribution).

## Context
This work extends the debate on renormalization of chiral effective field theory for nucleon-nucleon scattering, following the Weinberg power-counting proposal (Weinberg, 1990, 1991) and subsequent discussions by Kaplan (1996), Birse (2005), Valderrama (2009), Epelbaum (2006, 2017), and van Kolck (2020). The exact N/D method was previously developed by Entem, Oller, and collaborators (2016, 2018, 2021) as an alternative to the infinite-cutoff limit of the Lippmann-Schwinger equation. The bootstrap technique applied here follows the approach of Navarro Perez (2014), who used bootstrap with delta-shell potentials. The toy model itself is based on a proposal by Epelbaum (2018) to illustrate limitations of infinite-cutoff renormalization. The results enable a statistically rigorous method to determine the energy range where a given order of chiral EFT is consistent with scattering data, and can be applied to real nucleon-nucleon phase-shift analyses.
