# The Challenge of the EMC Effect: existing data and future directions

> Since the discovery that the ratio of inclusive charged lepton (per-nucleon) cross sections from a nucleus A to the deuteron is not unity - even in deep inelastic scattering kinematics - a great deal of experimental and theoretical effort has gone into understanding the phenomenon. The EMC effect, as it is now known, shows that even in the most extreme kinematic conditions the effects of the nucleon being bound in a nucleus can not be ignored. In this paper we collect the most precise data available for various nuclear to deuteron ratios, as well as provide a commentary on the current status of the theoretical understanding of this thirty year old effect.

## Key claim

The paper collects and reviews the most precise existing data on nuclear-to-deuteron deep inelastic scattering cross section ratios, concluding that while certain targets like carbon and iron have been precisely measured over the full range of Bjorken x, many other targets lack complete coverage, and the origin of the EMC effect remains unresolved thirty years after its discovery.

## Method

The authors compiled world measurements of inclusive charged lepton (electron and muon) scattering cross section ratios for various nuclear targets relative to deuterium, covering the full range of Bjorken x from the shadowing region through the EMC effect region to the Fermi motion region. They assessed the precision, x-coverage, and systematic uncertainties of existing data for each target, and reviewed theoretical approaches alongside planned future experiments at Jefferson Lab, COMPASS, and Fermilab.

## Key numbers

- The isoscalar correction for non-isoscalar nuclei can reach 10–15% at x of 0.8 for a gold target, depending on the parametrization of F2^n/F2^p.
- The isoscalar correction for ^3He and ^197Au varies by at most 3.5% when different parametrizations for n/p are used.
- The predicted size of the flavor-dependent effect in Parity Violating Deep Inelastic Scattering (PVDIS) could be as large as 5%.
- The expected size of the flavor-dependent observable in Semi-inclusive Deep Inelastic Scattering (SIDIS) could be as large as 10%.
- The E906 experiment at Fermilab will extend the x coverage of Drell-Yan measurements up to x approximately 0.45.
- The point-to-point uncertainties for JLab ^3He data range from 1.1% to 2.4%, with a normalization uncertainty of 1.84%.

## Context

This review extends the experimental and theoretical work on the EMC effect first observed by the European Muon Collaboration in 1983, and compiles data from experiments including SLAC, NMC, BCDMS, HERMES, and Jefferson Lab. The paper highlights the recent correlation observed between the EMC effect and the Short Range Correlation plateau (Weinstein 2010, Hen 2012, Arrington 2012), and discusses how future measurements at Jefferson Lab after the 12 GeV Upgrade, COMPASS-II at CERN, and Fermilab E906 will address open questions about flavor dependence, sea quark modification, and the polarized EMC effect.
