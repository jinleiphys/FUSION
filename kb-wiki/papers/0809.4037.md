# Nuclear Halos and Drip Lines in Symmetry-Conserving Continuum HFB Theory

> We review the properties of nuclear halos and nuclear skins in drip line nuclei in the framework of the spherical Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov theory with continuum effects and projection on good particle number with the Gogny force. We first establish the position of the un-projected HFB drip lines for the two most employed parametrizations of the Gogny force and show that the use of finite-range interactions leads almost always to small-sized halos, even in the least bound nuclei, which is in agreement with most mean-field predictions. We also discuss the size of the neutron skin at the drip line and its relation to neutron asymmetry. The impact of particle-number projection and its conceptual consequences near the drip line are analyzed in detail. In particular, we discuss the role of the chemical potential in a projected theory and the criteria required to define the drip line. We show that including particle number projection can shift the latter, in particular near closed shells. We notice that, as a result, the size of the halo can be increased due to larger pairing correlations. However, combining the most realistic pairing interaction, a proper treatment of the continuum and particle number projection does not permit to reproduce the very large halos observed in very light nuclei.

# Nuclear Halos and Drip Lines in Symmetry-Conserving Continuum HFB Theory

## Key claim

The authors show that combining finite-range Gogny interactions with proper treatment of the continuum and particle-number projection does not permit reproduction of the very large halos observed in very light nuclei, even though including particle-number projection can shift the drip line position and increase halo size due to larger pairing correlations.

## Method

The authors performed spherical Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using a Woods-Saxon basis with vanishing boundary conditions in a box of 20 fm, employing the finite-range Gogny D1 and D1S interactions in both the particle-hole and particle-particle channels. The neutron drip line was determined by the criterion that the neutron Fermi energy becomes positive (λ_n > 0), or by Koopmans' theorem at shell closures where pairing correlations vanish. The halo size was quantified using the Helm method, computing δR_halo = R_geom − R_Helm from the form factor of the one-body density.

## Key numbers

- The neutron drip line for Zirconium isotopes is at N = 82 for the D1S interaction and N = 84 for the D1 interaction, compared to N = 84 for Skyrme SLy4 and N = 92–94 for Skyrme SKP and SKM*.
- For Tin isotopes near the drip line, the halo parameter δR_halo is approximately 0.15 fm with Gogny interactions, while Skyrme SLy4 results reported in prior work indicate a size of about 0.8 fm.
- For Zirconium isotopes at the drip line, δR_halo ≈ 0.06 fm for D1S in ¹²²Zr and δR_halo ≈ 0.11 fm for D1 in ¹²⁴Zr.
- The neutron skin in ¹⁷⁰Sn is ΔR_Helm ≈ 0.57 fm for D1S, compared to ΔR_Helm ≈ 0.70 fm for Skyrme SLy4.
- For Neon isotopes, the drip line is at N = 20 for both D1 and D1S interactions, while the last known bound isotope is at N = 24; the neutron rms radius at N = 20 is r_n = 3.39 fm with D1S, compared to RHB results of r_n ≈ 3.42 fm.
- The variation after projection method can shift the position of the drip lines by 2 neutrons near closed shells.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous method for treating the continuum in configuration space with finite-range Gogny interactions (Schunck and Egido, 2006) to the specific problems of nuclear halos and skins near the neutron drip line. It directly compares with prior mean-field predictions from Skyrme HFB calculations using SLy4, SKP, and SKM* interactions (HelmDoba, 2006; Stoitsov et al., 2003) and relativistic mean-field calculations (PRL_RMF_1, PRL_RMF_2) that predicted giant halos in Neon and Zirconium isotopes. The results enable systematic benchmarking of finite-range versus zero-range interactions for halo phenomena and establish that particle-number projection alone cannot resolve the discrepancy between mean-field predictions and experimentally observed large halos in very light nuclei.
