# Similar final states from different initial states using new exact solutions of relativistic hydrodynamics

> We present exact, analytic and simple solutions of relativistic perfect fluid hydrodynamics. The solutions allow us to calculate the rapidity distribution of the particles produced at the freeze-out, and fit them to the measured rapidity distribution data. We also give an advanced estimation of the energy density reached in heavy ion collisions, and an improved estimation of the life-time of the reaction.

## Key claim
Different initial conditions in relativistic hydrodynamics can lead to the same final freeze-out distributions at mid-rapidity for a fixed equation of state, but the width of the rapidity distribution can be used to distinguish between these different time evolution scenarios.

## Method
The authors derived a new family of exact, analytic solutions to relativistic perfect fluid hydrodynamics in 1+1 and 1+d dimensions, parameterized by an acceleration parameter λ. They calculated the rapidity distribution of particles at freeze-out using a saddle-point integration, then fitted this analytic approximation to experimental data from the BRAHMS collaboration to extract the acceleration parameter and correct the Bjorken estimates of initial energy density and reaction lifetime.

## Key numbers
- λ = 1.18 ± 0.01 from fits to BRAHMS dn/dy data for central (0-5%) Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV, with fit range -3 < y < 3 and CL = 0.6%
- Corrected initial energy density ε_c = (2.0 ± 0.1) × ε_Bj = 10.0 ± 0.5 GeV/fm³, using ε_Bj = 5 GeV/fm³ and τ_f/τ_0 = 8 ± 2 fm/c
- Corrected lifetime τ_c = λ × τ_Bj, implying an 18 ± 1% increase in the estimated lifetime of the reaction
- For the analytic approximation of the rapidity distribution, valid for λ > 1/2 and m/T_f >> 1, the error is within about 10% for λ > 1 and not too large T_f

## Context
This work extends the Landau-Khalatnikov solution (Landau 1953, Khalatnikov 1954), which is accelerating but mathematically complicated, and the Hwa-Bjorken solution (Hwa 1974, Bjorken 1982), which is simple but accelerationless and boost-invariant. The new solutions combine the advantages of both: they are accelerating, explicit, and analytic. The results enable improved estimates of initial energy density and reaction lifetime in heavy-ion collisions, correcting the Bjorken formula (Bjorken 1982) by accounting for acceleration effects, and can be tested against future experimental data from RHIC and LHC.
