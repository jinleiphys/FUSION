# Covariant Density Functional Theory in Nuclear Physics and Astrophysics

> How does subatomic matter organize itself? Neutron stars are cosmic laboratories uniquely poised to answer this fundamental question that lies at the heart of nuclear science. Newly commissioned rare isotope facilities, telescopes operating across the entire electromagnetic spectrum, and ever more sensitive gravitational wave detectors will probe the properties of neutron-rich matter with unprecedented precision over an enormous range of densities. Yet, a coordinated effort between observation, experiment, and theoretical research is of paramount importance for realizing the full potential of these investments. Theoretical nuclear physics provides valuable insights into the properties of neutron-rich matter in regimes that are not presently accessible to experiment or observation. In particular, nuclear density functional theory is likely the only tractable framework that can bridge the entire nuclear landscape by connecting finite nuclei to neutron stars. This compelling connection is the main scope of the present review.

## Key claim

Covariant density functional theory (DFT) is presented as the only tractable framework that can simultaneously describe finite nuclei and neutron stars across 18 orders of magnitude in length scale, but the authors acknowledge that present-day nuclear energy density functionals remain largely empirical, with parameters fitted to selected properties of atomic nuclei rather than derived from the underlying nucleon-nucleon interaction.

## Method

The authors develop covariant DFT using a Lagrangian density with nucleon fields interacting via exchange of isoscalar-scalar sigma mesons, isoscalar-vector omega mesons, isovector-vector rho mesons, and photons, supplemented by nonlinear meson self-interactions (kappa, lambda, zeta, and Lambda_v). They solve the resulting Dirac equation for nucleons and Klein-Gordon equations for meson fields self-consistently in the mean-field limit to obtain ground-state densities and binding energies of finite nuclei. The same energy density functional is then used to compute the equation of state, which serves as the sole input for the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to determine neutron star mass-radius relations and tidal deformabilities.

## Key numbers

- The most massive neutron star observed to date has mass M = 2.1^{+0.10}_{-0.09} solar masses, reported by Cromartie and collaborators (2019).
- Oppenheimer and Volkoff (1939) predicted that a neutron star supported exclusively by degenerate neutron pressure would collapse above 0.7 solar masses.
- The Chandrasekhar limit for white dwarf collapse is M_Ch = 1.4 solar masses.
- The dimensionless tidal deformability Lambda depends on the compactness parameter xi = R_s/R raised to the fifth power, where R_s is the Schwarzschild radius (typically 3-6 km for a neutron star of radius 12-14 km).
- The Schwarzschild radius of a neutron star is comparable to its radius, giving v_esc^2/c^2 = 2GM/(c^2 R) approximately 0.5.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on nuclear density functional theory from Skyrme (1956, 1959) and Vautherin and Brink (1971) in the non-relativistic domain to the covariant framework pioneered by Walecka (1974) and Boguta and Bodmer (1977). It connects to the recent gravitational wave detection GW170817 by Abbott and collaborators (2017), which provided constraints on the equation of state from tidal deformability measurements, and to the PREX-II measurement of the neutron skin thickness of Pb-208 at Jefferson Lab. The work enables future coordinated efforts between rare isotope facilities, electromagnetic telescopes, and gravitational wave detectors to probe neutron-rich matter properties across an enormous range of densities.
