# Heaven and Earth: Nuclear Astrophysics after GW170817

> The historical detection of gravitational waves from the binary neutron star merger GW170817 is providing fundamental new insights into the astrophysical site for the creation of the heaviest elements in the cosmos and on the equation of state of neutron-rich matter. Shortly after this historical detection, electromagnetic observations of neutron stars together with measurements of the properties of neutron-rich nuclei at terrestrial facilities have placed additional constraints on the dynamics of neutron-rich matter. It is this unique synergy between heaven and earth that is the focus of this article.

# Heaven and Earth: Nuclear Astrophysics after GW170817

## Key claim
The synergy between gravitational-wave observations of binary neutron star mergers, electromagnetic measurements of neutron star masses and radii, and terrestrial experiments on neutron-rich nuclei is providing a unified picture of the equation of state of neutron-rich matter, though no single method can determine the EOS over the entire density range and each rung on the "density ladder" only informs the EOS in a suitable overlapping domain.

## Method
The paper reviews the constraints on the equation of state from three complementary approaches: tidal deformability measurements from the gravitational-wave signal GW170817, pulsar timing measurements of the Shapiro delay to determine the mass of PSR J0740, and pulse profile modeling by the NICER mission to extract simultaneous mass-radius information for millisecond pulsars. The author then connects these astrophysical constraints to terrestrial measurements of the neutron skin thickness of 208Pb from the PREX experiment, using the slope of the nuclear symmetry energy L as a bridge between nuclear and astrophysical scales.

## Key numbers
- Tidal deformability from GW170817: Λ₁.₄ = 190⁺³⁹⁰₋₁₂₀, suggesting neutron stars are fairly compact and favoring a soft equation of state
- Common radius of the two coalescing neutron stars in GW170817: R₁ = R₂ = (11.9 ± 1.4) km at 90% confidence level
- Mass of PSR J0740 from Shapiro delay: M = (2.08 ± 0.07) M⊙, with a Shapiro delay of about 20 μs
- Average stellar radius of PSR J0740 from NICER: about 12.4 km, suggesting a relatively stiff EOS at densities two-to-three times nuclear saturation
- Neutron skin thickness of 208Pb from PREX: R_skin²⁰⁸ = 0.283 fm
- Correlation coefficient between neutron skin thickness of 208Pb and slope of symmetry energy L: ρ = 0.98
- Gravitational wave strain amplitude for GW170817 at distance 40 Mpc: h ∼ 10⁻²³

## Context
This review extends the work of the LIGO-Virgo collaboration (Abbott 2017, 2018) on GW170817, the NICER collaboration (Riley 2019, 2021; Miller 2019, 2021) on mass-radius measurements, and the PREX collaboration (Adhikari 2021, 2022) on neutron skin measurements. The paper synthesizes these results into the "equation of state density ladder" concept from the 2023 Long Range Plan for Nuclear Science, showing how each method constrains a different density regime. The unique EOS profile that emerges, stiff near saturation, soft at intermediate densities, and stiff again at the highest densities, is noted to resemble the shape predicted by Wiringa, Fiks, and Fabrocini (1988) using the AV14+UVII Hamiltonian, and may indicate an exotic phase transition in the stellar interior. Future experiments such as MREX, the Athena X-ray mission, and third-generation gravitational wave observatories (Cosmic Explorer and Einstein Telescope) promise to measure stellar radii with precision of 100 meters.
