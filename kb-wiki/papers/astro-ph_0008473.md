# Neutrino-electron processes in a strongly magnetized thermal plasma

> We present a new method of calculating the rate of neutrino-electron interactions in a strong magnetic field based on finite temperature field theory. Using this method, in which the effect of the magnetic field on the electron states is taken into account exactly, we calculate the rates of all of the lowest order neutrino-electron interactions in a plasma. As an example of the use of this technique, we explicitly calculate the rate at which neutrinos and antineutrinos annihilate in a highly magnetized plasma, and compare that to the rate in an unmagnetized plasma. The most important channel for energy deposition is the gyromagnetic absorption of a neutrino-antineutrino pair on an electron or positron in the plasma ($ν\barν e^\pm\leftrightarrow e^\pm$). Our results show that the rate of annihilation increases with the magnetic field strength once it reaches a certain critical value, which is dependent on the incident neutrino energies and the ambient temperature of the plasma. It is also shown that the annihilation rates are strongly dependent on the angle between the incident particles and the direction of the magnetic field. If sufficiently strong fields exist in the regions surrounding the core of a type II supernovae or in the central engines of gamma ray bursts, these processes will lead to more efficient plasma heating mechanism than in an unmagnetized medium, and moreover, one which is intrinsically anisotropic.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new finite-temperature field theory method for calculating neutrino-electron interaction rates in a strong magnetic field, showing that the neutrino-antineutrino annihilation rate increases significantly once the magnetic field exceeds a critical value that depends on neutrino energies and plasma temperature, and that the rate is strongly anisotropic with respect to the magnetic field direction.

## Method

The authors use finite-temperature field theory to relate neutrino-electron interaction rates to the imaginary part of the polarization tensor in a magnetized plasma, following the approach of Gale and Kapusta (1991) originally developed for dilepton production. The polarization tensor components (vector-vector, vector-axial, and axial-axial) are computed using exact electron wave functions in a magnetic field, with the electron states described by Landau levels. The imaginary part of the polarization tensor is extracted via the Plemelj formula, and the differential annihilation rate is obtained by contracting with the neutrino leptonic tensor, then integrated over neutrino distribution functions to obtain total rates.

## Key numbers

- The critical magnetic field for enhancement is \(B_{cr} = 4.4 \times 10^{13} \,\mbox{G}\), the field strength at which the cyclotron energy equals the electron rest mass.
- Magnetar field strengths up to \(4 \times 10^{15} \,\mbox{G}\) have been inferred from observations, providing astrophysical motivation for the study.
- In the unmagnetized, zero-temperature, massless electron limit, the differential annihilation rate is \(\frac{dR}{d^3q_1d^3q_2} = \frac{2 G_F^2 (c_V^2 + c_A^2)}{3 \pi} q_1^2 q_2^2 (1 - \cos\Theta)^2\), where \(\Theta\) is the angle between neutrino and antineutrino.
- The energy conservation condition for pair creation requires \(\delta > [\sqrt{1+2n'B} + \sqrt{1+2nB}]^2\), while for gyromagnetic absorption on electrons or positrons the condition is \(0 < \delta < [\sqrt{1+2n'B} - \sqrt{1+2nB}]^2\), where \(\delta = \omega^2 - k_\parallel^2\).

## Context

This work extends prior calculations by Kuznetsov and Mikheev (1990s) on neutrino-electron scattering in strong fields (which restricted electrons to the lowest Landau level) and by Benesh and Horowitz (1990s) on neutrino-antineutrino annihilation (which examined only nearly parallel and head-on collisions). The authors find that the new channel of gyromagnetic absorption (\(\nu\bar{\nu} e^\pm \leftrightarrow e^\pm\)) is more important than direct pair creation over an astrophysically interesting range of phase space. The results enable more accurate modeling of neutrino heating in core-collapse supernovae and gamma-ray burst central engines, where supercritical magnetic fields may exist, and predict intrinsically anisotropic energy deposition.
