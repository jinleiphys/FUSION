# Antiprotonic Helium Atoms

> Metastable antiprotonic helium atoms $^{3,4}\! H\! e\bar pe$ have been discovered recently in experiments of the delayed annihilation of antiprotons in helium media. These exotic atoms survive for an enormous time (about tens of microseconds) and carry the extremely large total angular momentum $L\sim 30-40$. The theoretical treatment of the intrinsic properties of antiprotonic helium atoms, their formation and collisions with atoms and molecules is discussed.

## Key claim

Metastable antiprotonic helium atoms ($^{3,4}\! H\! e\bar pe$) survive for tens of microseconds due to a combination of inhibited annihilation from extremely large total angular momentum ($L\sim 30-40$), inhibited Auger decay from high electron angular momentum ($l_e\ge 4$), and suppressed collisional de-excitation; the paper reviews theoretical calculations of their intrinsic properties, formation, and collisions, noting that the agreement between experimental and theoretical transition wavelengths unambiguously confirms their existence, though a possible ambiguity remains in the assignment of the 470.724 nm transition.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical calculations of energy levels using variational wave functions (e.g., two-parameter and correlated trial functions), the Born-Oppenheimer approximation, and configuration interaction methods. Radiative dipole transition rates and Auger decay rates are computed for states of the $^{4}\! H\! e\bar pe$ system, and the energy-level splitting due to spin-dependent relativistic interactions (Breit interaction) is calculated in first-order perturbation theory. Formation probabilities and initial populations are estimated via semiclassical models and quasi-classical conceptions, and collisional quenching by helium atoms and impurities is analyzed using a multi-channel approach.

## Key numbers

- Antiprotonic helium atoms survive for about tens of microseconds, compared to the mean lifetime of antiprotons in media of $\sim 10^{-12}$ s.
- Total angular momentum $L\sim 30-40$ for metastable states.
- Measured transition wavelengths: $597.259\pm 0.002$ nm (assigned to $(36,4\rightarrow 35,4)$) and $470.724\pm 0.002$ nm (assigned to $(35,3\rightarrow 34,3)$) in $^{4}\! H\! e\bar pe$.
- Theoretical transition wavelengths from Korobov (1995): 597.229 nm and 470.705 nm for the same transitions.
- Experimental decay rates: $0.72 \times 10^6$ s$^{-1}$ for state $(35,4)$ and $1.18 \times 10^6$ s$^{-1}$ for state $(34,3)$; theoretical radiative rates range from $0.597$ to $0.619 \times 10^6$ s$^{-1}$ and $0.713$ to $0.754 \times 10^6$ s$^{-1}$, respectively.
- Auger decay rates: $10^5$ s$^{-1}$ for multipolarity $l_e=4$, $10^8$ s$^{-1}$ for $l_e=3$.
- Energy-level splitting $\Delta E_L$ for states with $32\leq L\leq 37$ ranges from $-0.223$ to $-0.313 \times 10^{-4}$ eV.
- Formation probability per stopped antiproton: calculated as 0.22 (semiclassical model) and about 0.3 (quasi-classical), versus experimental value $0.03-0.04$.
- Quenching cross-section for molecular hydrogen: $\sigma_q v = 6 \times 10^{-11}$ cm$^3$/s.
- States with $L+N \leq 40$ are stable against collisional quenching at all densities up to liquid helium; states with $L+N=41$ are intermediate; states with $L+N \geq 42$ are fully destroyed at $p \geq 1$ bar.

## Context

This paper reviews the theoretical understanding of antiprotonic helium atoms following their experimental discovery in delayed annihilation experiments (Yamazaki et al., 1993; Iwasaki et al., 1993; Morita et al., 1994) and subsequent laser-induced resonant annihilation measurements (Morita et al., 1994; Hayano et al., 1994). It extends earlier predictions by Russell (1970) and calculations by Ahlrichs et al. (1982) and others, and it highlights the need for precise few-body calculations to resolve discrepancies between experimental and theoretical decay rates, as well as to understand formation probabilities, collisional quenching, and the influence of impurities. The work enables further study of antiproton properties (e.g., magnetic moment) via precise spectroscopy and opens the possibility of producing antihydrogen via reactions with positronium.
