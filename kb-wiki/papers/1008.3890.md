# Dynamical r-process studies within the neutrino-driven wind scenario and its sensitivity to the nuclear physics input

> We use results from long-time core-collapse supernovae simulations to investigate the impact of the late time evolution of the ejecta and of the nuclear physics input on the calculated r-process abundances. Based on the latest hydrodynamical simulations, heavy r-process elements cannot be synthesized in the neutrino-driven winds that follow the supernova explosion. However, by artificially increasing the wind entropy, elements up to A=195 can be made. In this way one can reproduce the typical behavior of high-entropy ejecta where the r-process is expected to occur. We identify which nuclear physics input is more important depending on the dynamical evolution of the ejecta. When the evolution proceeds at high temperatures (hot r-process), an (n,g)-(g,n) equilibrium is reached. While at low temperature (cold r-process) there is a competition between neutron captures and beta decays. In the first phase of the r-process, while enough neutrons are available, the most relevant nuclear physics input are the nuclear masses for the hot r-process and the neutron capture and beta-decay rates for the cold r-process. At the end of this phase, the abundances follow a steady beta flow for the hot r-process and a steady flow of neutron captures and beta decays for the cold r-process. After neutrons are almost exhausted, matter decays to stability and our results show that in both cases neutron captures are key for determining the final abundances, the position of the r-process peaks, and the formation of the rare-earth peak. In all the cases studied, we find that the freeze out occurs in a timescale of several seconds.

## Key claim

Based on the latest hydrodynamical simulations, heavy r-process elements cannot be synthesized in the neutrino-driven winds that follow the supernova explosion under realistic conditions; however, by artificially increasing the wind entropy by a factor of two, elements up to A=195 can be made, allowing the study of how nuclear physics input affects r-process abundances under different dynamical evolutions.

## Method

The authors used a hydrodynamical trajectory from a 15 solar mass progenitor supernova simulation (model M15-l2-r1 of Arcones, Janka, and Scheck 2007), ejected at 8 seconds after bounce, and artificially increased the entropy by a factor of two (by reducing the density by a factor of two) to achieve a neutron-to-seed ratio sufficient for heavy r-process nucleosynthesis. They employed two reaction networks: an extended network for seed formation including charged-particle reactions, and a faster r-process network of 5300 nuclei from Z=14 to Z=110 using a fully implicit Newton-Raphson scheme. Four different mass models were used (FRDM, ETFSI-Q, HFB-17, and Duflo-Zuker) with their consistently calculated neutron capture rates, and the evolution was studied for both hot r-process (with neutron capture-photodissociation equilibrium) and cold r-process (with competition between neutron captures and beta decays) scenarios.

## Key numbers

- The original trajectory from simulations had a neutron-to-seed ratio of approximately 10^{-2} after freeze-out of charged-particle reactions, insufficient for heavy r-process elements
- By reducing the density by a factor of two, the entropy increased to approximately 200 k_B per nucleon, yielding a neutron-to-seed ratio of approximately 70
- The hot r-process evolution with reverse shock at T=1 GK showed neutron capture and photodissociation timescales much shorter than beta-decay timescales (tau_{(n,gamma)} = tau_{(gamma,n)} << tau_beta)
- The cold r-process evolution showed neutron capture timescales approximately equal to beta-decay timescales (tau_{(n,gamma)} approximately tau_beta << tau_{(gamma,n)}) once temperature dropped below approximately 0.5 GK
- The hot r-process reached the N=82 shell closure at approximately 30 ms and the N=126 shell closure at approximately 500 ms
- The freeze-out occurs in a timescale of several seconds in all cases studied
- The hot r-process r-process path at freeze-out was located at a neutron separation energy of S_n = 2.8 MeV

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamical simulations of Arcones, Janka, and Scheck (2007) by using their trajectories to study r-process nucleosynthesis, which those simulations could not produce under realistic conditions. It provides a link between the behavior of nuclear masses far from stability and features in the final abundances, going beyond classical r-process calculations (Kratz, Bitouzet et al. 1993; Kratz, Pfeiffer, and Thielemann 1998) and parametric studies (Freiburghaus, Rembges et al. 1999; Farouqi et al. 2010) by using trajectories from actual hydrodynamical simulations. The results enable future studies of how beta-decay rates (to be explored in later work) and neutron-capture cross sections determine r-process abundances, and show that neutron captures after freeze-out are key for determining final abundances, the position of r-process peaks, and the formation of the rare-earth peak.
