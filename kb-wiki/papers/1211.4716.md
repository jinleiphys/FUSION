# Exact Solutions of Bethe-Salpeter Equations with Instantaneous Interactions

> The Bethe-Salpeter approach allows for quantum-field-theoretic descriptions of relativistic bound states; its inherent complexity, however, usually prevents to find its exact solutions. Under suitable simplifying assumptions about the systems discussed, we derive analytically some examples of rigorous solutions to the instantaneous homogeneous Bethe-Salpeter equation by relating tentative solutions to the interactions responsible for the formation of bound states.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that exact analytic solutions of the instantaneous homogeneous Bethe–Salpeter equation can be constructed by inverting the problem: rather than solving for bound-state amplitudes from a given potential, one postulates a convenient form for the amplitude and then derives the corresponding interaction potential, provided the bound-state mass is set to zero and the constituents are massless or have specific masses.

## Method

The authors start from the reduced Salpeter equation (a three-dimensional instantaneous approximation to the Bethe–Salpeter equation) for spin-singlet fermion–antifermion bound states. They assume spherical symmetry and a convolution-type interaction kernel, then apply Fourier–Bessel transforms to convert the momentum-space integral equations into configuration-space relations. By postulating analytic forms for the momentum-space Salpeter amplitude (exponential, Gaussian, or rational functions) and setting the bound-state mass to zero, they algebraically solve for the radial configuration-space potential V(r) that would produce that amplitude.

## Key numbers

* For massless constituents with an exponential amplitude φ(p) = 2 λ^(3/2) exp(−λ p) (λ > 0), the resulting non-confining potential for Lorentz-vector kernels (η = 2) is V(r) = (2/(η λ)) (1 − 4 λ^2/(r^2 + λ^2)), with V(0) = −6/(η λ) and V(r) → 2/(η λ) as r → ∞.
* For the same exponential amplitude with a time-component Lorentz-vector kernel, the potential is V(r) = (1/λ) (1 − 8 λ^2/(r^2 + λ^2)), with V(0) = −7/λ and V(r) → 1/λ as r → ∞.
* For massless constituents with a Gaussian amplitude φ(p) = 2 (8 λ^3/π)^(1/4) exp(−λ p^2) (λ > 0), the confining potential for Lorentz-vector kernels (η = 2) is V(r) = (1/(η λ)) [(r − 2λ/r) erfi(r/(2√λ)) − 2 √(λ/π) exp(r^2/(4λ))], with V(0) = −4/(η √(π λ)).
* For massive constituents (m > 0) with a rational amplitude φ(p) = √(2/π) 4 μ^(5/2)/(p^2 + μ^2)^2 (μ > 0) and the specific linear combination of Lorentz structures, the configuration-space potential is derived for the case μ = m, but the explicit numerical form is not given in the truncated text.

## Context

This work extends the Neumann–Wigner inversion technique (von Neumann and Wigner, 1929) from nonrelativistic quantum mechanics to the relativistic Bethe–Salpeter formalism. It provides exact analytic examples that can serve as benchmarks for testing numerical solution methods and for judging the validity of further approximations, such as the Salpeter equation (Salpeter, 1952) and the reduced Salpeter equation (Lucha, 1992). The results also connect to stability analyses of Salpeter equations with confining interactions (Lucha, 2007; Lucha, 2008; Lucha, 2010).
