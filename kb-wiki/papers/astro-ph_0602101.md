# The Cosmic Stellar Birth and Death Rates

> The cosmic stellar birth rate can be measured by standard astronomical techniques. It can also be probed via the cosmic stellar death rate, though until recently, this was much less precise. However, recent results based on measured supernova rates, and importantly, also on the attendant diffuse fluxes of neutrinos and gamma rays, have become competitive, and a concordant history of stellar birth and death is emerging. The neutrino flux from all past core-collapse supernovae, while faint, is realistically within reach of detection in Super-Kamiokande, and a useful limit has already been set. I will discuss predictions for this flux, the prospects for neutrino detection, the implications for understanding core-collapse supernovae, and a new limit on the contribution of type-Ia supernovae to the diffuse gamma-ray background.

## Key claim

The cosmic stellar birth rate and death rate can be linked through measurements of the diffuse neutrino background from type-II supernovae and the diffuse gamma-ray background from type-Ia supernovae, with the neutrino background now providing a competitive constraint on the star formation rate history, though the actual flux is likely close to the current upper limit rather than firmly detected.

## Method

The author reviews the measured supernova rates and the diffuse neutrino flux limit from Super-Kamiokande, then uses these to constrain the normalization of the cosmic star formation rate history under reasonable assumptions about neutrino emission per supernova. The type-Ia supernova gamma-ray background is predicted from the star formation rate and type-Ia supernova rate data, and compared to the measured gamma-ray background. The analysis follows the "Concordance Model" approach of Beacom (2006), which balances the upper limit from neutrino data against the lower limit from astronomical data.

## Key numbers

- The Super-Kamiokande collaboration published a flux limit in 2003 that was about 200 times more restrictive than the earlier limit from Kamiokande, placing it in the range of realistic models.
- The gravitational energy release per type-II supernova is approximately 10^53 erg.
- The thermonuclear energy release per type-Ia supernova is approximately 10^51 erg.
- The detectable neutrinos from type-II supernovae have energies of a few tens of MeV, emitted on a timescale of several seconds.
- The detectable gamma rays from type-Ia supernovae have energies of a few MeV, emitted on a timescale of up to several months.
- Adding about 0.2% dissolved gadolinium trichloride to Super-Kamiokande would allow coincidence detection of the positron and neutron in antineutrino-proton interactions.

## Context

This work extends the earlier proposal by Clayton and Silk (1977) for detecting the gamma-ray background from type-Ia supernovae, and the later work by Seidov (1978), Krauss, Glashow, and Schramm (1984), and Woosley (1984) on the neutrino background from type-II supernovae. The Super-Kamiokande limit from Malek (2003) is taken as a starting point, and the analysis follows the Concordance Model of Beacom (2006). The results enable future detection of the supernova neutrino background with improved sensitivity using the GADZOOKS! proposal, which would allow a new direct measurement of supernova neutrino emission parameters and help resolve lingering mysteries from the SN 1987A data.
