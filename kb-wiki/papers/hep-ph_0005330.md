# Influence of the U(1)_A Anomaly on the QCD Phase Transition

> The SU(3)_{r} \times SU(3)_{\ell} linear sigma model is used to study the chiral symmetry restoring phase transition of QCD at nonzero temperature. The line of second order phase transitions separating the first order and smooth crossover regions is located in the plane of the strange and nonstrange quark masses. It is found that if the U(1)_{A} symmetry is explicitly broken by the U(1)_{A} anomaly then there is a smooth crossover to the chirally symmetric phase for physical values of the quark masses. If the U(1)_{A} anomaly is absent, then there is a phase transition provided that the σmeson mass is at least 600 MeV. In both cases, the region of first order phase transitions in the quark mass plane is enlarged as the mass of the σmeson is increased.

# Influence of the U(1)_A Anomaly on the QCD Phase Transition

## Key claim

Using the SU(3)_r × SU(3)_ℓ linear sigma model with the Cornwall-Jackiw-Tomboulis (CJT) formalism in the Hartree approximation, the paper finds that if the U(1)_A symmetry is explicitly broken by the anomaly, there is a smooth crossover to the chirally symmetric phase for physical quark masses; however, if the U(1)_A anomaly is absent, a first-order phase transition occurs for physical quark masses provided the σ meson mass is at least 600 MeV.

## Method

The SU(3)_r × SU(3)_ℓ linear sigma model Lagrangian was used with a cubic coupling c that breaks U(1)_A symmetry (c ≠ 0) or leaves it exact (c = 0). The CJT formalism in the tadpole-resummed (Hartree) approximation was employed to derive gap equations for the condensates and the scalar and pseudoscalar nonet meson masses at nonzero temperature, which were solved numerically as functions of temperature for varying strange and nonstrange current quark masses. The order of the phase transition was determined by examining the continuity of the condensates as functions of temperature.

## Key numbers

- For c ≠ 0 (U(1)_A broken) and m_σ = 1000 MeV, the ratio of the critical up-down quark mass to the physical up-down quark mass for m_strange/m_up,down = 32 was found to be ∼ 0.20 (compared to ∼ 0.01 in prior work by Gavin, Gocksch, and Pisarski (1994)).
- For c ≠ 0 with m_up = m_down = 0 and m_σ = 800 MeV, the critical strange quark mass is about 16 MeV; for m_σ = 900 MeV, it is 260 MeV.
- For c = 0 (U(1)_A exact) and m_σ = 600 MeV, the physical point (m_up,down ≈ 10 MeV, m_strange ≈ 150 MeV) lies just outside the first-order region.
- The physical masses used for fitting were m_π = 138 MeV, m_K = 496 MeV, m_up = m_down = 10 MeV, and m_strange = 150 MeV.
- The proportionality constants relating background fields to current quark masses were a = 4.64 × 10⁻⁶ MeV⁻² and b = 2.27 × 10⁻⁶ MeV⁻².

## Context

This work extends earlier studies using the SU(3)_r × SU(3)_ℓ linear sigma model by Meyer-Ortmanns and Schaefer (1996), Metzger, Meyer-Ortmanns, and Pirner (1994), Gavin, Gocksch, and Pisarski (1994), and Meyer-Ortmanns, Pirner, and Patkós (1992), but without fixing the ratio of strange to nonstrange quark masses. The results agree qualitatively with lattice simulations by the Columbia group (Brown et al., 1990) and JLQCD collaboration (Aoki et al., 1999). The finding that for c = 0 the second-order line departs from the strange quark mass axis was also predicted using large-N_c chiral perturbation theory by Escribano, Ling, and Tytgat (2000). The work enables further investigation of whether the U(1)_A symmetry is restored near the chiral transition temperature, a question with implications for the nature of the QCD phase transition in heavy-ion collisions.
