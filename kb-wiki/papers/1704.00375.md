# Effect of strong magnetic fields on the crust-core transition and inner crust of neutron stars

> The Vlasov equation is used to determine the dispersion relation for the eigenmodes of magnetized nuclear and neutral stellar matter, taking into account the anomalous magnetic moment of nucleons. The formalism is applied to the determination of the dynamical spinodal section, a quantity that gives a good estimation of the crust-core transition in neutron stars. We study the effect of strong magnetic fields, of the order of $10^{15}-10^{17}$ G, on the extension of the crust of magnetized neutron stars. The dynamical instability region of neutron-proton-electron ($npe$) matter at subsaturation densities is determined within a relativistic mean field model. It is shown that a strong magnetic field has a large effect on the instability region, defining the crust-core transition as a succession of stable and unstable regions due to the opening of new Landau levels. The effect of the anomalous magnetic moment is non-negligible for fields larger than 10$^{15}$ G. The complexity of the crust at the transition to the core and the increase of the crust thickness may have direct impact on the properties of neutrons stars related with the crust.

# Effect of strong magnetic fields on the crust-core transition and inner crust of neutron stars

## Key claim

Strong magnetic fields of order $10^{15}-10^{17}$ G significantly alter the crust-core transition region of neutron stars, producing a succession of stable and unstable regions rather than a single transition density, due to the opening of new Landau levels; the anomalous magnetic moment of nucleons has a non-negligible effect for fields larger than $10^{15}$ G, reducing the instability sections.

## Method

The authors used the relativistic Vlasov equation to derive the dispersion relation for longitudinal eigenmodes propagating along the magnetic field direction in neutron-proton-electron ($npe$) matter at subsaturation densities, within a relativistic mean-field (RMF) model (NL3$\omega\rho$). The dynamical spinodal surface was obtained by solving the linearized equations of motion for small oscillations around equilibrium, imposing $\omega=0$ to identify the boundary of unstable regions, and the growth rates of unstable modes were calculated to estimate cluster sizes in the inner crust.

## Key numbers

- Magnetic field values studied: $B = 4.41 \times 10^{15}$ G ($B^*=10^2$), $4.41 \times 10^{16}$ G ($B^*=10^3$), and $4.41 \times 10^{17}$ G ($B^*=10^4$), where $B_{ce}=4.41\times10^{13}$ G is the electron critical magnetic field.
- For $B^*=10^4$, the onset of homogeneous matter in $\beta$-equilibrium shifts to a baryonic density of $0.100$ fm$^{-3}$ (pressure $0.818$ MeV/fm$^3$); for $B^*=10^3$, $0.103$ fm$^{-3}$ ($0.833$ MeV/fm$^3$); for $B^*=10^2$, $0.105$ fm$^{-3}$ ($0.863$ MeV/fm$^3$).
- The complex transition region has a thickness of approximately $0.02$ fm$^{-3}$ even for the weaker fields.
- For neutron-rich matter, instability regions extend to densities almost 40% larger than the crust-core transition density for $B=0$.
- The momentum transfer used for spinodal calculations was $k=75$ MeV, which gives a spinodal section close to the envelope of all sections.
- For $B^*=10^4$ at $\rho=0.04$ fm$^{-3}$ with proton fraction $y_p=0.035$, the instability persists up to $k>300$ MeV, well above the maximum $k$ for $B=0$.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the thermodynamical spinodal under magnetic fields by Aziz (2008) and pasta phases by Lima (2013), which considered fields more intense than those expected in magnetar crusts. It also complements the authors' earlier introduction of this formalism in Fang (2016) by presenting the full Vlasov formalism and analyzing the role of the anomalous magnetic moment. The results enable improved modeling of magnetar crust structure, including the fractional moment of inertia relevant for glitch interpretation (Link, 1999; Chamel, 2013), and connect to studies of the outer crust under magnetic fields by Chamel (2012, 2015) and to the resistive amorphous layer proposed by Pons (2013) at the crust-core interface.
