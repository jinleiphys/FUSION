# Complex-energy approach to sum rules within nuclear density functional theory

> The linear response of the nucleus to an external field contains unique information about the effective interaction, correlations, and properties of its excited states. To characterize the response, it is useful to use its energy-weighted moments, or sum rules. By comparing computed sum rules with experimental values, the information content of the response can be utilized in the optimization process of the nuclear Hamiltonian or EDF. But the additional information comes at a price: compared to the ground state, computation of excited states is more demanding. To establish an efficient framework to compute sum rules of the response that is adaptable to the optimization of the nuclear EDF and large-scale surveys of collective strength, we have developed a new technique within the complex-energy FAM based on the QRPA. To compute sum rules, we carry out contour integration of the response function in the complex-energy plane. We benchmark our results against the conventional matrix formulation of the QRPA theory, the Thouless theorem for the energy-weighted sum rule, and the dielectric theorem for the inverse energy-weighted sum rule. We demonstrate that calculated sum-rule values agree with those obtained from the matrix formulation of the QRPA. We also discuss the applicability of both the Thouless theorem about the energy-weighted sum rule and the dielectric theorem for the inverse energy-weighted sum rule to nuclear density functional theory in cases when the EDF is not based on a Hamiltonian. The proposed sum-rule technique based on the complex-energy FAM is a tool of choice when optimizing effective interactions or energy functionals. The method is very efficient and well-adaptable to parallel computing. The FAM formulation is especially useful when standard theorems based on commutation relations involving the nuclear Hamiltonian and external field cannot be used.

## Key claim

The authors propose that sum rules of the nuclear response can be efficiently computed via contour integration of the response function in the complex-energy plane using the finite-amplitude method (FAM) based on the quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA), but the applicability of standard theorems (Thouless theorem for the energy-weighted sum rule and dielectric theorem for the inverse energy-weighted sum rule) to nuclear density functional theory is not guaranteed when the energy density functional (EDF) is not based on a Hamiltonian.

## Method

The authors compute sum rules by carrying out contour integration of the response function in the complex-energy plane using the complex-energy FAM within the QRPA framework. They benchmark their results against the conventional matrix formulation of QRPA (MQRPA), the Thouless theorem for the energy-weighted sum rule, and the dielectric theorem for the inverse energy-weighted sum rule. The method is tested on the oblate configuration of 24Mg computed with the SLy4 Skyrme EDF using the HFB solver HFBTHO in a model space of five harmonic oscillator shells with volume pairing.

## Key numbers

- The smallest and largest QRPA poles in the test case appear at 1.3 MeV and 128.7 MeV, respectively; contour radii were set to R_A2 = 1 MeV and R_A1 = 200 MeV.
- For the isoscalar monopole operator, the energy-weighted sum rule m_1 converges to 4199.44 MeV e^2 fm^4 with N_A1 = 12 integration points along semicircle A_1, achieving 6-digit accuracy already with N_A1 = 5.
- The inverse energy-weighted sum rule m_{-1} for the isoscalar monopole operator converges to 5.00385 MeV^{-1} e^2 fm^4 with N_A2 = 16 integration points along semicircle A_2.
- For the isoscalar monopole operator, the FAM m_1 value is 4199.44 MeV e^2 fm^4 compared to the MQRPA value of 4200.82 MeV e^2 fm^4; for the isovector monopole operator, FAM gives 4734.30 MeV e^2 fm^4 versus MQRPA 4735.03 MeV e^2 fm^4.
- In the larger model space of N_sh = 20, the FAM energy-weighted sum rule for the isoscalar monopole is 4530.01 MeV e^2 fm^4, compared to the HFB value of 4529.46 MeV e^2 fm^4.

## Context

This work extends the contour integration technique for discrete QRPA modes (PhysRevC.87.064309) to sum-rule calculations, providing an efficient alternative to the direct matrix formulation of QRPA (PhysRevC.79.054329) which requires knowledge of the large QRPA matrix. The method is designed for optimizing next-generation nuclear EDFs informed by experimental data on multipole and charge-exchange strength, and is particularly useful when standard theorems based on commutation relations (Thouless 1961; Brink 1976; Bohigas 1979) cannot be applied due to the EDF not being derived from a Hamiltonian. The approach also enables large-scale surveys of collective strength and is well-adaptable to parallel computing.
