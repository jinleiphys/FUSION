# Small-x Physics: From HERA to LHC and beyond

> We summarize the lessons learned from studies of hard scattering processes in high-energy electron-proton collisions at HERA and antiproton-proton collisions at the Tevatron, with the aim of predicting new strong interaction phenomena observable in next-generation experiments at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). Processes reviewed include inclusive deep-inelastic scattering (DIS) at small x, exclusive and diffractive processes in DIS and hadron-hadron scattering, as well as color transparency and nuclear shadowing effects. A unified treatment of these processes is outlined, based on factorization theorems of quantum chromodynamics, and using the correspondence between the "parton" picture in the infinite-momentum frame and the "dipole" picture of high-energy processes in the target rest frame. The crucial role of the three-dimensional quark and gluon structure of the nucleon is emphasized. A new dynamical effect predicted at high energies is the unitarity, or black disk, limit (BDL) in the interaction of small dipoles with hadronic matter, due to the increase of the gluon density at small x. This effect is marginally visible in diffractive DIS at HERA and will lead to the complete disappearance of Bjorken scaling at higher energies. In hadron-hadron scattering at LHC energies and beyond (cosmic ray physics), the BDL will be a standard feature of the dynamics, with implications for (a) hadron production at forward and central rapidities in central proton-proton and proton-nucleus collisions, in particular events with heavy particle production (Higgs), (b) proton-proton elastic scattering, (c) heavy-ion collisions. We also outline the possibilities for studies of diffractive processes and photon-induced reactions (ultraperipheral collisions) at LHC, as well as possible measurements with a future electron-ion collider.

## Key claim

The black disk limit (BDL) in dipole-hadron interactions, driven by the rising gluon density at small x, is marginally visible in diffractive DIS at HERA but will become a standard dynamical feature at LHC energies, with the caveat that in hadron-hadron collisions one deals mostly with gluon-gluon dipoles whose cross section is 9/4 times larger than the quark-antiquark dipoles dominating ep scattering.

## Method

The authors review hard scattering processes in high-energy electron-proton collisions at HERA and antiproton-proton collisions at the Tevatron, applying QCD factorization theorems and the correspondence between the parton picture in the infinite-momentum frame and the dipole picture in the target rest frame. They analyze inclusive deep-inelastic scattering (DIS) at small x, exclusive and diffractive processes, color transparency, and nuclear shadowing effects, using DGLAP evolution equations and the optical model of dipole-hadron scattering to quantify the approach to the unitarity limit.

## Key numbers

- The gluon density in the proton at HERA has been measured down to values x ~ 10^{-4}
- At LHC, hard processes can probe parton distributions down to values of x ~ 10^{-7}
- The dipole-hadron cross section scales as sigma^{qq-hadron} proportional to d^2, up to logarithmic corrections in d
- The cross section for gluon-gluon dipoles scattering from hadronic matter is 9/4 times larger than that of quark-antiquark dipoles
- Leading partons in central pp collisions at LHC are predicted to acquire transverse momenta p_perp^2 ~ several 10 GeV^2
- Partons in the nucleon with x > 10^{-2} and significant transverse momenta are concentrated in a transverse area substantially smaller than 1 fm^2

## Context

This review extends the lessons from HERA and Tevatron experiments to predict new QCD phenomena at LHC, building on the foundational work of Gribov on the increase of interaction radius with energy, the Froissart bound for total cross sections, and the DGLAP evolution equations for parton densities. The work enables predictions for central pp/pA/AA collisions at LHC energies, diffractive processes, photon-induced reactions in ultraperipheral collisions, and outlines measurement possibilities for a future electron-ion collider.
