# Initial state geometry and the role of hydrodynamics in proton-proton, proton-nucleus and deuteron-nucleus collisions

> We apply the successful Monte Carlo Glauber and IP-Glasma initial state models of heavy ion collisions to the much smaller size systems produced in proton-proton, proton-nucleus and deuteron- nucleus collisions. We observe a significantly greater sensitivity of the initial state geometry to details of multi-particle production in these models compared to nucleus-nucleus collisions. In particular, we find that the size of the system produced in p+A collisions is very similar to the one produced in p+p collisions, and predict comparable Hanbury-Brown-Twiss radii in the absence of flow in both systems. Differences in the eccentricities computed in the models are large, while differences amongst the generated flow coefficients v_2 and v_3 are smaller. For a large number of participants in proton-lead collisions, the v_2 generated in the IP-Glasma model is comparable to the value obtained in proton-proton collisions. Viscous corrections to flow are large over characteristic lifetimes in the smaller size systems. In contrast, viscous contributions are significantly diminished over the longer space-time evolution of a heavy ion collision.

## Key claim

The applicability of hydrodynamics to small systems (proton-proton, proton-nucleus, and deuteron-nucleus collisions) is strongly dependent on assumptions about the nature of initial multi-particle dynamics, much more so than in collisions of heavy nuclei; the authors argue that initial state geometry in these small systems is far more sensitive to details of how energy is deposited than in nucleus-nucleus collisions.

## Method

The authors applied the Monte Carlo Glauber model (with several variants for energy density deposition) and the IP-Glasma initial state model (which computes gluon fields from fluctuating color charges via classical Yang-Mills equations, with saturation scales determined from the IP-Sat dipole model fit to HERA data) to proton-proton, proton-nucleus, and deuteron-nucleus collisions. They computed spatial eccentricities and then evolved the initial conditions using event-by-event viscous hydrodynamics with constant eta/s=0.08, initial time tau_0=0.2 fm, and freeze-out temperature T_fo=120 MeV to generate flow coefficients v_2 and v_3.

## Key numbers

* System size r_max in p+p and p+Pb collisions grows approximately linearly with (dN/dy)^(1/3); for a given (dN/dy)^(1/3), p+p and p+Pb system sizes are comparable (Fig. 3).
* In the IP-Glasma model, the ellipticity epsilon_2 in p+Pb collisions is two to five times smaller than in the MC-Glauber model, depending on N_part.
* For p+Pb collisions at N_part=14, v_2 in the IP-Glasma model is approximately a factor of two smaller than in the MC-Glauber 1 model; at N_part=20, it is about 60% lower.
* In p+p collisions at sqrt(s)=7 TeV, the IP-Glasma model gives integrated v_2 (p_T>0.5 GeV) of approximately 0.02 at b=0 fm and 0.035 at b=1 fm; v_3 is approximately 0.01 for both impact parameters.
* In d+Au collisions, the mean distance in the transverse plane between the two nucleons in a deuteron is 2.52 fm.
* The MC-Glauber 1 model yields epsilon_2 exactly unity and epsilon_3=0 for N_part=2 in p+Pb collisions.

## Context

This work extends the comparison of initial state models (MC-Glauber and IP-Glasma) from heavy-ion collisions to small systems, where the authors find much greater sensitivity to model details. It directly contrasts with hydrodynamic approaches such as those of Bozek (2011, 2012, 2013) that use MC-Glauber initial conditions to describe p+Pb and d+Au ridge data, and with the Glasma graph approach of Dusling (2012, 2013) that attributes azimuthal collimation to initial-state correlations. The results suggest that HBT radii measurements can discriminate between models, and that viscous corrections to flow are large over characteristic lifetimes in small systems, unlike in heavy-ion collisions where they are diminished over longer space-time evolution.
