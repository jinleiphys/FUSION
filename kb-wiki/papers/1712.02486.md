# The effect of the fluctuating proton size on the study of chiral magnetic effect in proton-nucleus collisions

> High energy proton-nucleus (pA) collisions provide an important constraint on the study of the chiral magnetic effect in QCD matter. Naively, in pA collisions one expects no correlation between the orientation of event plane as reconstructed from the azimuthal distribution of produced hadrons and the orientation of magnetic field. If this is the case, any charge-dependent hadron correlations can only result from the background. Nevertheless, in this paper we point out that in high multiplicity pA collisions a correlation between the magnetic field and the event plane can appear. This is because triggering on the high hadron multiplicity amounts to selecting Fock components of the incident proton with a large number of partons that are expected to have a transverse size much larger than the average proton size. We introduce the effect of the fluctuating proton size in the Monte Carlo Glauber model and evaluate the resulting correlation between the magnetic field and the second-order event plane in both pA and nucleus-nucleus (AA) collisions. The fluctuating proton size is found to result in a significant correlation between magnetic field and the event plane in pA collisions, even though the magnitude of the correlation is still much smaller than in AA collisions. This result opens a possibility of studying the chiral magnetic effect in small systems.

## Key claim
In high-multiplicity proton-nucleus collisions, selecting events with a large number of produced hadrons corresponds to selecting proton Fock states with many partons and thus a larger-than-average transverse size; this fluctuating proton size can generate a significant correlation between the magnetic field direction and the second-order event plane, even though the correlation remains much smaller than in nucleus-nucleus collisions.

## Method
The authors implemented a Monte Carlo Glauber model in which the projectile proton's transverse radius fluctuates event-by-event according to the charged-particle multiplicity distribution from PYTHIA 8 at \(\sqrt{s}=13\) TeV, using the relation \(R_p^2(N) \approx (N/\bar{N}) R_0^2\) with \(R_0 = 0.75\) fm. For each simulated pPb and PbPb event at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5.02\) TeV, they computed the impact parameter, number of participants, participant plane, reaction plane, and the angular correlation \(\langle \cos(2\Psi_B - 2\Psi_{\mathrm{EP}}) \rangle\) between the magnetic field direction (taken perpendicular to the impact parameter) and the event plane (approximated by the participant plane).

## Key numbers
- Average proton radius \(R_0 \approx 0.75\) fm, derived from the measured inelastic pp cross section \(\sigma^{pp}_{in} \approx 70\) mb at \(\sqrt{s}=13\) TeV.
- For events with \(N/\bar{N} \simeq 10\), the selected proton configuration size exceeds 3 fm, corresponding to a light nucleus with mass number \(A \simeq 25\).
- In pPb collisions with fluctuating proton size, the angular correlation \(\langle \cos(2\Psi_B - 2\Psi_{\mathrm{EP}}) \rangle\) becomes significantly nonzero for \(N_{\mathrm{part}} \gtrsim 10\), whereas with fixed proton size the correlation quickly drops to zero.
- The overall \(\Delta\gamma\) correlator in pPb collisions is enhanced by about one order of magnitude in the fluctuating proton scenario compared to the fixed-proton scenario, but remains several times smaller than in PbPb collisions (PbPb data points are scaled by \(10^{-1}\) for visibility in the comparison figure).

## Context
This work extends the CMS Collaboration studies (Khachatryan 2016, Sirunyan 2017) that measured charge-dependent correlations in high-multiplicity pPb collisions and argued that the event plane and magnetic field are uncorrelated for a fixed small proton size. The authors introduce the fluctuating proton size concept based on the Gribov diffusion picture (Gribov 2009) and the relation between parton multiplicity and hadron multiplicity (Kharzeev 2017). The results open the possibility of searching for the chiral magnetic effect in small colliding systems, which could provide direct evidence of deconfinement and chiral symmetry restoration in proton-nucleus collisions.
