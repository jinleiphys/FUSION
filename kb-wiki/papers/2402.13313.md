# Spin-Triplet Pairing in Heavy Nuclei is Stable Against Deformation

> Any experimental evidence of nucleons paired in spin-triplet states will confirm the existence of an exotic phase of nuclear matter. This type of nuclear superfluidity has been hypothesized in heavy nuclei, where the antagonizing spin-orbit effects are damped, and there it oftentimes coexists with traditional spin-singlet pairing, leading to the possibility of mixed-spin pairing. Realistic nuclear deformation, not considered in such studies, could make-or-break these proposals, since its effect on triplet-pairing, and the competition (and coexistence) of the two superfluid phases, was expected to be crucial. We report on a thorough study on the effect of deformation on triplet-, singlet-, and mixed-spin pairing in the relevant region of the nuclear chart. We find that, at low isospin asymmetries, spin-triplet pairing is enhanced by deformation, while below the proton-drip line, the novel superfluid phase survives alongside the usual spin-singlet pairing. These results suggest that spin-triplet superfluidity exists in realistic nuclei and can be probed in the lab.

## Key claim
The paper claims that spin-triplet pairing in heavy nuclei near the $N=Z$ line (mass region $A\sim 130$) is not destroyed by realistic nuclear deformation; instead, at low isospin asymmetries, deformation enhances spin-triplet pairing, and the exotic superfluid phase survives alongside conventional spin-singlet pairing below the proton-drip line. This conclusion is conditional on the specific deformation parameters predicted by Möller (2016) and on the ratio of triplet to singlet interaction strengths being set to $v_t/v_s = 1.38$, though the authors state their conclusions are not sensitive to this ratio.

## Method
The authors performed Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov (HFB) calculations using a Hamiltonian with a deformed Woods-Saxon potential and a deformed spin-orbit term, plus a contact pairing interaction acting in six spin-isospin channels (three spin-singlet, three spin-triplet). They used the gradient method to minimize the HFB energy, constraining neutron and proton numbers and six pairing amplitudes to explore different pairing phases. Calculations were done for all nuclei in the mass region $A\sim 130$ (neutron numbers 50 to 75) at spherical shapes, at intermediate quadrupole deformations, and at the realistic axial deformations prescribed by Möller (2016), which include quadrupole and higher-order multipole components.

## Key numbers
- The ratio of triplet to singlet interaction strengths used is $v_t/v_s = 1.38$, tuned to reproduce earlier works (Bertsch 2011, Gezerlis 2011, Bulthuis 2016, Rrapaj 2019).
- Nuclei are classified as spin-singlet if $A_{\rm singlet} > 0.8$, spin-triplet if $A_{\rm singlet} < 0.2$, and mixed-spin otherwise, where $A_{\rm singlet}$ is the squared fraction of singlet pairing amplitudes.
- Correlation energies for realistically deformed nuclei with spin-triplet or mixed-spin pairing are approximately $2\ \mathrm{MeV}$.
- Pairing gaps (odd-even staggering) are distinguished as strong (above $0.4\ \mathrm{MeV}$) or weak (below $0.4\ \mathrm{MeV}$); nuclei with correlation energy below $0.5\ \mathrm{MeV}$ are excluded from the spin-character classification.
- Specific nuclei identified with sizable spin-triplet or mixed-spin condensates within the proton-drip line include $^{125}\mathrm{Pm}$, $^{126}\mathrm{Pm}$, and $^{127}\mathrm{Pm}$ (Z=61).

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of spin-triplet pairing in heavy nuclei by Bertsch (2011), Gezerlis (2011), Bulthuis (2016), and Rrapaj (2019), which neglected nuclear deformation. It directly addresses the concern raised by Frauendorf (2014) that deformation could make-or-break proposals for spin-triplet superfluidity. The results confirm that spin-triplet pairing survives realistic deformation, enabling future *ab initio* calculations (e.g., Tichai 2018, Hergert 2018, Soma 2021, Hu 2024) that can use these HFB ground states as reference states, and suggesting experimental probes of mixed-spin condensates in nuclei accessible below the proton-drip line.
