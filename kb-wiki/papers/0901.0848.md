# Probing neutron correlations through nuclear break-up

> The effect of initial correlations between nucleons on the nuclear break-up mechanism is studied. A quantum transport theory which extends standard mean-field approach is developed to incorporate short range pairing correlation as well as direct nucleon-nucleon collisions. A time evolution of the nuclear break-up from a correlated system leading to the emission of two particles to the continuum is performed. We show that initial correlations have strong influence on relative angles between particles emitted in coincidence. The present qualitative study indicates that nuclear break-up might be a tool to infer the residual interaction between nucleons in the nuclear medium.

## Key claim

Initial correlations between nucleons have a strong influence on the relative angles between particles emitted in coincidence during nuclear break-up, and this mechanism might serve as a tool to infer the residual interaction between nucleons in the nuclear medium. The study is qualitative and indicates that proper transport models are necessary to interpret experimental coincidence measurements.

## Method

The authors developed a quantum transport theory called TDDM$^{\rm P}$, an approximation to Time-Dependent Density Matrix (TDDM) theory that groups single-particles into pairs and assumes only components of the residual interaction and correlation between such pairs are non-zero. They initialized correlated oxygen isotopes using an adiabatic switching-on of the residual interaction, then simulated the nuclear break-up of an oxygen nucleus impinging on a $^{208}$Pb target by treating the collision partner as a one-body time-dependent external perturbation (a moving Woods-Saxon potential) with a straight-line trajectory at grazing impact parameter.

## Key numbers

- The three-dimensional Cartesian mesh used for calculations had size (80 fm)$^3$ with a step of 0.8 fm and a time step of 0.45 fm/c.
- The adiabatic switching time constant $\tau$ was set to 300 fm/c, achieving very good convergence.
- Effective pairing gaps for $^{22}$O: TDDM$^{\rm P}$ gave -3.5 MeV, compared to -3.1 MeV from full TDDM and -3.3 MeV from HFB.
- Effective pairing gaps for $^{24}$O: TDDM$^{\rm P}$ gave -3.1 MeV, compared to -2.7 MeV from full TDDM and -3.4 MeV from HFB.
- The impact parameter for the collision was 11 fm (grazing condition), with the $^{208}$Pb equivalent sharp radius set to 7.11 fm, Woods-Saxon depth $V_0$ = -50 MeV, and diffuseness $a$ = 0.6 fm.
- The residual interaction parameters included an Attractive force ($v_0$ = -300, $\alpha$ = 1/2, $\beta$ = 1), a Repulsive force ($v_0$ = +300, $\alpha$ = 1/2, $\beta$ = 1), a Volume force ($v_0$ = -159.6, $\alpha$ = 0), a Surface force ($v_0$ = -483.2, $\alpha$ = 1, $\beta$ = 1), and a Mixed force ($v_0$ = -248.5, $\alpha$ = 1/2, $\beta$ = 1), all with $\rho_0$ = 0.16 fm$^{-3}$.

## Context

This work extends mean-field approaches like Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) by incorporating short-range pairing correlations and direct nucleon-nucleon collisions, going beyond the independent particle hypothesis used in earlier break-up models (Lac 1999). It builds on the Time-Dependent Density Matrix (TDDM) theory (Wan 1985) and addresses difficulties in numerical convergence encountered in prior TDDM applications (Pfi 1994). The results suggest that coincidence measurements of two-nucleon emission, combined with dedicated transport models, could provide a method to study internal correlations in nuclei and to constrain the residual interaction used in Energy Density Functional (EDF) theories, complementing experimental investigations of "cigar-like" configurations in lighter nuclei (Mar 2000, Ass 2008).
