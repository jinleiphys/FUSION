# The paradox of coherent photoproduction in incoherent interactions

> The Good-Walker (GW) paradigm relates coherent and incoherent exclusive reactions to the average target configuration and its nucleonic and partonic fluctuations. In it, coherent photoproduction occurs when the target remains in the ground state, while, in incoherent photoproduction, the target breaks up. However, the GW paradigm fails to explain the observation of coherent vector meson photoproduction accompanied by nuclear breakup in ultra-peripheral collisions (UPCs) , and in peripheral relativistic heavy-ion collisions. In the latter, hundreds of particles can be created. This writeup will explore this paradox, and also present an alternate, semi-classical approach toward coherent production: adding the amplitudes with an appropriate propagator. The semi-classical approach explains the transverse momentum dependence of exclusive vector meson production in UPCs, but does not address the target final state. I will address these two approaches, and suggest possible future work to resolve the paradox \cite{Klein:2023zlf}.

# The paradox of coherent photoproduction in incoherent interactions

## Key claim
The Good-Walker (GW) paradigm, which relates coherent photoproduction to the average target configuration and incoherent production to fluctuations, fails to explain observations of coherent vector meson photoproduction accompanied by nuclear breakup in ultra-peripheral collisions (UPCs) and peripheral relativistic heavy-ion collisions; a semi-classical approach based on adding amplitudes with an appropriate propagator can explain the transverse momentum dependence but does not address the target final state.

## Method
The paper compares the Good-Walker formalism, where coherent cross-sections are computed from squared averaged amplitudes and incoherent cross-sections from the variance of amplitudes over target configurations, against a semi-classical approach where amplitudes from individual nucleons are summed with phase factors depending on nucleon positions and momentum transfer. The semi-classical approach is applied to vector meson photoproduction data from STAR and ALICE, including cases with nuclear dissociation from Coulomb excitation, bremsstrahlung, and pair production, as well as peripheral heavy-ion collisions producing hundreds of particles.

## Key numbers
- For a grazing lead-lead collision at the LHC (impact parameter approximately 2 nuclear radii), the average number of electron-positron pairs produced is about 2.5, independent of other interactions during the encounter.
- For energy transfers above 5-8 MeV, heavy nuclei typically dissociate via proton or neutron emission; the momentum transfer required to cause nucleon emission is at least 100 to 125 MeV/c.
- Lead-208 has its lowest lying excited state at 2.6 MeV, while gold-197 has its lowest lying state at 77 keV with a 1.9 nsec lifetime; for a 110 GeV/n gold nucleus, the characteristic decay distance is about 70 m.
- In STAR rho photoproduction data, the cross-section in the range 0.2 < |t| < 0.45 GeV^2 was well fit by a dipole form factor modeling a single proton.
- At pT approximately 5 times hbar divided by nuclear radius, the amplitudes for one-photon and two-photon processes to produce a vector meson and excite the target are similar.

## Context
This work extends the Good-Walker formalism (Good and Walker, 1960) and Miettinen-Pumplin approach (Miettinen and Pumplin, 1978) for diffractive scattering, which have been the standard framework for relating coherent and incoherent photoproduction to nuclear fluctuations. The paper challenges this paradigm using data from STAR (2002, 2007, 2017, 2019) and ALICE (2020, 2021, 2022) showing coherent photoproduction with nuclear breakup, and proposes a semi-classical approach that has been previously applied in Glauber calculations (Gribov, 1968) and to light vector meson photoproduction (Klein, 1999; Frankfurt, 2002). The resolution of this paradox is critical for interpreting how incoherent production is sensitive to nucleonic and partonic fluctuations, with implications for nuclear structure studies at the future Electron-Ion Collider.
