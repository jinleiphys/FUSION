# Methods on compositeness and related aspects

> In many physical applications, bound states and/or resonances are observed, which raises the question whether these states are elementary or composite. Here we elaborate on several methods for calculating the compositeness $X$ of bound states and resonances in Quantum Mechanics, and in Quantum Field Theory by introducing particle number operators. For resonances $X$ is typically complex and we discuss how to get meaningful results by using certain phase transformations in the $S$ matrix.

# Methods on Compositeness and Related Aspects

## Key claim

The paper argues that compositeness \(X\) of bound states and resonances can be defined and calculated using number operators in quantum field theory, making it an observable quantity expressed in terms of S-matrix elements; for resonances, where \(X\) is typically complex, meaningful real results can be obtained by taking the absolute value \(|X_i|\) after applying phase transformations to the S-matrix.

## Method

The author derives compositeness from the expectation value of the number operator \(N_D\) in the Dirac picture, expressed as \(X = \frac{1}{2}\langle \psi_B | N_D | \psi_B \rangle\) for bound states, and extends this to resonances via analytic continuation to the second Riemann sheet. Explicit formulas are given in terms of partial-wave amplitudes and coupling functions \(g_{\ell S}(k)\), with the compositeness expressed as an integral over momentum. For resonances, the author uses phase transformations of the S-matrix to render the complex compositeness real by taking its absolute value, and also presents a sum rule derived from two-body unitarity and the derivative of the unitary loop function.

## Key numbers

- For the deuteron studied within chiral perturbation theory up to N\(^4\)LO with an estimated error of around 4%, the compositeness is \(X = 0.96\)–\(1.0\).
- The binding momentum of the deuteron is approximately 45 MeV.
- The virtual state in the \(^1S_0\) nucleon-nucleon partial wave has \(|k_R| \approx 10\) MeV, much smaller than 45 MeV.
- For a narrow resonance, the partial decay width is given by \(\Gamma_i = |g_i|^2 / (8\pi M_R^2)\).
- In the effective range expansion, the compositeness for a resonance is \(|X| = (2r/a - 1)^{-1}\), where \(a\) is the scattering length and \(r\) the effective range.
- For a resonance described by the effective range expansion, \(|X| \leq 1\) corresponds to the condition \(M_R \geq 0\), with \(|X| = 1\) for \(M_R = 0\) and \(\Gamma > 0\).

## Context

This work extends the foundational approach of Weinberg (1962, 1963, 1965) on compositeness and elementariness, and builds on the number-operator method introduced by Oller (2017). It provides explicit formulas for calculating compositeness in quantum field theory and for resonances, complementing earlier work by Guo (2015) on sum rules and by Kang (2016) on the effective range expansion. The methods enable systematic classification of hadronic states as elementary or composite, with applications to states such as the deuteron, the \(f_0(980)\), and the \(\rho(770)\) resonance, and can be connected to lattice QCD calculations.
