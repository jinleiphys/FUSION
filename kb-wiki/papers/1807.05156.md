# Impact of alternative transmission coefficient parameterizations on Hauser-Feshbach theory

> We investigate different formulations of the transmission coefficient $T_c$, including the form implied by Moldauer's ``sum rule for resonance reactions'' [P.A. Moldauer, Phys. Rev. Lett. 19, 1047 (1967)], the SPRT method [G. Noguere, et al. EPJ Web Conf. 146, 02036 (2017)] and the Moldauer-Simonius form [M. Simonius, Phys. Lett. 52B, 279 (1974); P.A. Moldauer, Phys. Rev. 157, 907 (1967)]. Within these different formulations, we compute the neutron transmission coefficients in the resolved and unresolved resonance regions, allowing a direct comparison with the transmission coefficients computed using an optical model potential. For nuclei for which there are no measured resonances, these approaches allow one to predict the average neutron resonance parameters directly from the optical model and level densities. Some of the approaches are valid in both the strong and weak coupling limits (i.e., any value of the average width and mean level spacing). Finally, both the Moldauer-Simonius and Moldauer's Sum Rule forms approaches suggest that superradiance, that is, the quantum chaotic enhancement of certain channels, may be a common phenomena in nuclear collisions. Our results suggest why superradiance has been previously overlooked. We apply our approach to neutron reactions on the closed shell $^{90}$Zr nucleus and the mid-shell $^{197}$Au nucleus.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that alternative formulations of the transmission coefficient, specifically the Moldauer-Simonius form and Moldauer's Sum Rule form, predict superradiance (quantum chaotic enhancement of certain channels) in compound nuclear reactions, an effect that has been previously overlooked because standard Hauser-Feshbach calculations implicitly assume the weak coupling limit where this behavior is absent.

## Method

The authors compared three parameterizations of the transmission coefficient \(T_c\): the SPRT method, Moldauer's Sum Rule form, and the Moldauer-Simonius form, examining their behavior across the weak and strong coupling limits. They then modified the standard Hauser-Feshbach equation with Width Fluctuation Correction by replacing the ratio \(\overline{\Gamma}_b/\sum_c\overline{\Gamma}_c\) with expressions derived from each transmission coefficient formulation, and applied these modified formulas to compute neutron transmission coefficients and compound reaction cross sections for \(^{90}\)Zr and \(^{197}\)Au.

## Key numbers

- The weak coupling limit (\(x_c \ll 1\)) gives \(T_c^\text{weak} \approx 2x_c(1-x_c)\) as the first two terms, matching all three forms; the linear approximation only matches below \(x_c \approx 0.25\), while the quadratic expansion works up to \(x_c \approx 0.5\)
- The three transmission coefficient forms agree within roughly 5% up to \(x_c \approx 2\), where the SPRT method diverges from the other two forms
- The SPRT transmission coefficient peaks at \(x_c = 2\) (corresponding to \(\overline{\Gamma}_c \sim \frac{2}{3}D\)) and then decreases to zero as \(x_c \rightarrow \infty\)
- The Moldauer-Simonius and Sum Rule forms both approach \(T_c \rightarrow 1\) as \(x_c \rightarrow \infty\), with differences between them at most 5% in the overlapping resonance region (\(\overline{\Gamma}_c \sim D\))

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework connecting resonance parameters (average widths \(\overline{\Gamma}_c\) and level spacings \(D\)) to transmission coefficients used in Hauser-Feshbach theory, building on Moldauer's sum rule (Moldauer, 1967), the SPRT method developed by Noguere et al. (2017), and the Moldauer-Simonius form (Simonius, 1974; Moldauer, 1967). The results enable a unified description of the resolved resonance region, unresolved resonance region, and fast neutron region, and suggest that superradiance, previously discussed in other mesoscopic systems by Auerbach (2011), may be a common phenomenon in nuclear collisions that has been overlooked due to the routine use of the weak coupling approximation.
