# Pulsars: Macro-nuclei with 3-flavour symmetry

> A pulsar-like compact star is the rump left behind after a supernova where normal baryonic matter is intensely compressed by gravity, but the real state of such compressed baryonic matter is still not well understood because of the non-perturbative nature of the fundamental color interaction. We argue that pulsars could be of condensed matter of quark clusters, i.e., "quark-cluster stars" which distinguish from conventional neutron and quark stars. In comparison with 2-flavour symmetric micro-nuclei, a quark-cluster star could simply be considered as a macro-nucleus with 3-flavour symmetry. New research achievements both theoretical and observational are briefly presented.

# Pulsars: Macro-nuclei with 3-flavour symmetry

## Key claim

Pulsars could be "quark-cluster stars" composed of condensed matter of quark clusters with 3-flavour (up, down, strange) symmetry, rather than conventional neutron stars or quark stars. The author argues that while micro-nuclei in everyday matter are 2-flavour symmetric (protons and neutrons), macro-nuclei with baryon numbers much greater than 10^9 could be 3-flavour symmetric because strangeness is easily excited at nuclear or supra-nuclear densities and electrons contribute negligible energy in such systems.

## Method

The paper compares micro-nuclei (ordinary atomic nuclei) with macro-nuclei (pulsar-scale objects) by examining their flavour symmetry, quark-cluster composition, and interaction properties. It derives a minimum mass threshold for accretion onto strange matter by balancing thermal energy against the Coulomb barrier at the strange matter surface, using the condition kT > ηZV_q for significant barrier penetration. Observational consequences are drawn from five listed similarities and differences between micro- and macro-nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The energy scale of dense matter at nuclear or supra-nuclear densities is estimated to be ~500 MeV, larger than the mass difference (~100 MeV) between s-quark and u/d-quarks
- The Compton wavelength of quarks is λ_c = 0.024 Å, below which electrons are non-relativistic
- Electron kinetic energy in atomic systems is estimated at ~10^-5 MeV from Heisenberg's uncertainty relation
- The rest mass of the electron is m_e c^2 = 0.511 MeV
- Constituent quark mass is m_q ~ 300 MeV
- The kinematic energy of a cluster in both micro and macro nuclei is ~0.5 GeV, much smaller than the rest mass (>2 GeV) of a strange quark-cluster
- Massive pulsar-like compact stars could have masses as high as 3 M_⊙ and even higher
- Strange quark-cluster stars could have masses as low as ~10^-2 M_⊙ from accretion-induced collapse of white dwarfs
- The minimum mass for accretion onto strange matter is M_min ~ 1.8 × 10^-4 (Z/A)^(3/2) η_(-4)^(3/2) V_(q10)^(3/2) ρ_(av2)^(-1/2) M_⊙, with typical value ~60 M_⊕ (Earth masses) and radius ~500 meters
- Nuclear density ρ_0 = 2.8 × 10^14 g/cm^3
- Strange quark-nuggets could have masses as low as ~10^20 GeV ~ 10^-4 g

## Context

This work extends the quark-cluster star model proposed by Xu (2003) and builds on the idea that strange matter could be 3-flavour symmetric, contrasting with the conventional neutron star model and the quark star model. The paper connects to prior work on strange quark-nuggets from Witten (1984), accretion-induced collapse of white dwarfs (Xu 2005), and strange planets around strange stars (Xu 2006). It also references lattice QCD calculations of Λ-Λ interactions by Beane (2011) and Inoue (2011) to support the possibility of heavy quark clusters. The observational consequences enable tests through mass-radius relations, thermal X-ray spectra, super-bursts, and glitch mechanisms in anomalous X-ray pulsars and soft gamma-ray repeaters.
