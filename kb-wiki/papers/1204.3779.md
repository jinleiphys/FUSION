# New relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics from kinetic theory

> Starting with the relativistic Boltzmann equation where the collision term is generalized to include nonlocal effects via gradients of the phase-space distribution function, and using Grad's 14-moment approximation for the distribution function, we derive equations for the relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics. We compare them with the corresponding equations obtained in the standard Israel-Stewart and related approaches. Our method generates all the second-order terms that are allowed by symmetry, some of which have been missed by the traditional approaches based on the 14-moment approximation, and the coefficients of other terms are altered. The first-order or Navier-Stokes equations too get modified. Significance of these findings is demonstrated in the framework of one-dimensional scaling expansion of the matter formed in relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper derives relativistic dissipative fluid-dynamic equations from a Boltzmann equation with a nonlocal collision term that includes gradients of the phase-space distribution function, producing all second-order terms allowed by symmetry, some of which were missed by the standard Israel-Stewart approach and by the Denicol-Koide-Rischke (DKR) derivation, and also modifying the first-order Navier-Stokes equations, with the magnitude of these modifications controlled by parameters \(a, b_1, b_2\) that are constrained by the second law of thermodynamics (\(|a| < 1\)) and by the requirement that corrections remain small.

## Method

Starting from the relativistic Boltzmann equation, the authors generalize the collision term to include nonlocal effects by Taylor-expanding the distribution functions at the collision points up to second order in the space-time separation \(\xi^\mu\), leading to a modified Boltzmann equation with gradient terms. Using Grad's 14-moment approximation for the distribution function and following the DKR approach (which uses the definitions of the dissipative currents rather than the second moment of the Boltzmann equation), they derive evolution equations for the bulk viscous pressure \(\Pi\), particle diffusion current \(n^\mu\), and shear stress tensor \(\pi^{\mu\nu}\). The resulting equations contain all symmetry-allowed second-order terms, with coefficients expressed as functions of the hydrodynamic variables and the nonlocality parameters \(a, b_1, b_2\).

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio \(\eta/s \gtrsim 1.5/(4\pi)\) is the threshold above which the Israel-Stewart approach becomes marginal, based on comparison to transport theory (Huovinen 2008, El 2008).
- For the Bjorken flow numerical test: initial temperature \(T_0 = 360\) MeV (typical for RHIC) or 500 MeV (typical for LHC), initial proper time \(\tau_0 = 0.9\) fm/c, \(\eta/s = 0.16\), and initial shear pressure \(\pi_0 = 4\eta/(3\tau_0)\) for the anisotropic case or \(\pi_0 = 0\) for the isotropic case.
- In the modified first-order equations, the shear viscosity is rescaled to \(\tilde a \eta\) where \(\tilde a = 1 - a\), with \(|a| < 1\) from the second law of thermodynamics.
- For the numerical demonstration, initial corrections due to \(a, b_1, b_2\) are set to about 10% for first-order terms and about 20% for second-order terms.

## Context

This work extends the kinetic-theory derivation of relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics beyond the standard Israel-Stewart theory (Israel 1979) and the DKR approach (Denicol 2010), which both assumed a local collision term. It shows that the nonlocality of collisions within a fluid element generates additional second-order terms (e.g., those involving acceleration \(\dot u\) and vorticity \(\omega\)) that were missed in those earlier formulations and in numerical heavy-ion collision studies (Romatschke 2007, Song 2007, Luzum 2010). The results are consistent with a recent generalization of the 14-moment method (Denicol 2012) that also generates all symmetry-allowed terms. The new equations enable more accurate extraction of \(\eta/s\) from experimental data at RHIC and LHC, and may help resolve discrepancies in baryon spectra and constituent quark number scaling violations observed in Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 2.76\) TeV (Floris 2011, Krzewicki 2011).
