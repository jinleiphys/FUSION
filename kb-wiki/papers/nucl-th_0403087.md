# Prompt muon-induced fission: a sensitive probe for nuclear energy dissipation and fission dynamics

> Following the formation of an excited muonic atom, inner shell transitions may proceed without photon emission by inverse internal conversion, i.e. the muonic excitation energy is transferred to the nucleus. In actinides, the 2p -> 1s and the 3d -> 1s muonic transitions result in excitation of the nuclear giant dipole and giant quadrupole resonances, respectively, which act as doorway states for fission. The nuclear excitation energy is typically 6.5 - 10 MeV. Because the muon lifetime is long compared to the timescale of prompt nuclear fission, the motion of the muon in the Coulomb field of the fissioning nucleus may be utilized to learn about the dynamics of fission.

## Key claim

The muon attachment probability to the light fission fragment in prompt muon-induced fission can serve as a sensitive probe of nuclear energy dissipation and fission dynamics, but the sensitivity depends on the mass asymmetry of the fragments and the position of the avoided crossing point relative to the scission point, which may limit the ability to distinguish between different dissipation mechanisms.

## Method

The authors solve the non-relativistic time-dependent Schrodinger equation using the Born-Oppenheimer expansion method in terms of quasimolecular wavefunctions, and also solve the relativistic time-dependent Dirac equation on a 3-D Cartesian lattice utilizing the B-Spline collocation method. The fission dynamics is treated classically with one collective coordinate R, using a coordinate-dependent mass parameter and an empirical double-humped fission potential, with a linear friction force introduced to account for nuclear energy dissipation between the outer fission barrier and the scission point.

## Key numbers

- Muonic reduced Compton wavelength: 1.87 fm
- Reduced Compton time: 6.23 x 10^{-24} s
- Nuclear excitation energy from muonic transitions: 6.5 - 10 MeV
- For 2p to 1s transition in actinides: 6.6 MeV (E1, giant dipole resonance)
- For 3d to 1s transition in actinides: 9.5 MeV (E2, giant quadrupole resonance)
- For 238U, giant dipole resonance located at 12.8 MeV with width 6 MeV; giant quadrupole resonance at 9.9 MeV with width 6.8 MeV
- For 237Np, dissipated energy of order 0-10 MeV and fission time delay due to friction of up to 2 x 10^{-21} s
- Muon attachment probability to light fragment PL = 0.042 at relative fragment velocity v = 0.08c, and PL = 0.015 at v = 0.06c, for fragment charge asymmetry Z_H/Z_L = 55/39 = 1.41
- Muonic K-shell binding energies: I_H = 5.93 MeV (heavy fragment), I_L = 3.45 MeV (light fragment)
- Free muonic leptonic decay rate: (2.2 x 10^{-6} s)^{-1}
- Nuclear muon capture rate for actinides: approximately (7.5 x 10^{-8} s)^{-1}
- Delayed fission via nuclear muon capture occurs with mean lifetime tau_capt = (7-8) x 10^{-8} s
- Experimental finding: (74 +/- 15)% of prompt events attributed to 3d to 1s transition, (26 +/- 15)% to 2p to 1s transition (Johansson et al.)

## Context

This work extends earlier nonrelativistic calculations for muon-induced fission by groups starting in 1980 (Mamyrbayev et al., Mueller and Rafelski, Oberacker et al., Karpeshin) and presents the first relativistic calculations using the Dirac equation for the muon, which became feasible in 1992 (Oberacker and Umar). The paper builds on the pioneering work of Davies, Sierk and Nix (1976) who calculated the effect of viscosity on fission dynamics, and aims to distinguish between two-body collision dissipation and one-body friction caused by the mean field. The results enable experimental tests using muon beams at facilities such as LAMPF, TRIUMF, CERN, and PSI to determine the dominant dissipation mechanism in nuclear fission.
