# Primordial Nucleosynthesis with CMB Inputs: Probing the Early Universe and Light Element Astrophysics

> Cosmic microwave background (CMB) determinations of the baryon-to-photon ratio $η\propto Ω_{\rm baryon} h^2$ will remove the last free parameter from (standard) big bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) calculations. This will make BBN a much sharper probe of early universe physics, for example, greatly refining the BBN measurement of the effective number of light neutrino species, $N_{ν,eff}$. We show how the CMB can improve this limit, given current light element data. Moreover, it will become possible to constrain $N_{ν,eff}$ independent of \he4, by using other elements, notably deuterium; this will allow for sharper limits and tests of systematics. For example, a 3% measurement of $η$, together with a 10% (3%) measurement of primordial D/H, can measure $N_{ν,eff}$ to a 95% confidence level of $σ_{95%}(N_ν) = 1.8$ (1.0) if $η\sim 6.0\times 10^{-10}$. If instead, one adopts the standard model value $N_{ν,eff}=3$, then one can use $η$ (and its uncertainty) from the CMB to make accurate predictions for the primordial abundances. These determinations can in turn become key inputs in the nucleosynthesis history (chemical evolution) of galaxies thereby placing constraints on such models.

## Key claim

The paper shows that once the cosmic microwave background (CMB) provides an independent measurement of the baryon-to-photon ratio η, big bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) will shift from being a probe of baryon density to a sharper probe of early-universe particle physics (e.g., the effective number of light neutrino species Nν,eff) and of astrophysical processes, but the precision of these new constraints depends critically on the accuracy of both the CMB η measurement and the observed primordial light-element abundances.

## Method

The authors perform a likelihood analysis that convolves BBN theoretical predictions (from Monte Carlo simulations) for the abundances of 4He, D, 3He, and 7Li as functions of η and Nν,eff with a Gaussian likelihood for a CMB-determined η. They then compute projected constraints on Nν,eff for various assumed CMB precisions (30%, 10%, 3% in η) and for different observational abundance uncertainties, considering both high-η (η10 = 5.8) and low-η (η10 = 2.4) scenarios.

## Key numbers

- A 3% measurement of η together with a 10% (3%) measurement of primordial D/H can measure Nν,eff to a 95% confidence level of σ95%(Nν) = 1.8 (1.0) if η ~ 6.0 × 10−10.
- Using 4He alone with a CMB η measurement at 10% precision gives σ95%(Nν) = 0.8 (95% CL) for the nominal 4He uncertainty; if the 4He uncertainty is a factor of 2 larger, this degrades to σ95%(Nν) = 1.7.
- Using deuterium alone (D/H = (3.0 ± 0.4) × 10−5) with η10 = 5.8 × (1 ± 0.03) yields σ95%(Nν) = 2.2.
- Current 20% CMB η determinations give 95% CL upper limits: Nν < 3.6 using 4He with η10 = 5.8, and Nν < 3.9 using 4He with η10 = 2.4.
- With a 10% measurement of η and D/H as in eq. (lowd), the 95% CL limit is Nν < 5.9; with 3% uncertainty in both η and D/H, the limit reduces to Nν < 4.0.

## Context

This work extends the standard BBN framework (e.g., Olive & Thomas 1995, Copi, Fields & Olive 2000) by showing how upcoming CMB missions (MAP with ~10% η precision, Planck with ~3%) will transform BBN from a baryometer into a tool for early-universe particle physics and astrophysics. It enables future cross-checks of Nν,eff using deuterium independently of 4He, and allows accurate predictions of primordial abundances that can constrain galactic chemical evolution models and stellar depletion processes.
