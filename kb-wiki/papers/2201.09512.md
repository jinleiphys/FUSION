# Coupled-channels calculations for nuclear reactions: from exotic nuclei to superheavy elements

> Atomic nuclei are composite systems, and they may be dynamically excited during nuclear reactions. Such excitations are not only relevant to inelastic scattering but they also affect other reaction processes such as elastic scattering and fusion. The coupled-channels approach is a framework which can describe these reaction processes in a unified manner. It expands the total wave function of the system in terms of the ground and excited states of the colliding nuclei, and solves the coupled Shrödinger equations to obtain the $S$-matrix, from which several cross sections can be constructed. This approach has been a standard tool to analyze experimental data for nuclear reactions. In this paper, we review the present status and the recent developments of the coupled-channels approach. This includes the microscopic coupled-channels method and its application to cluster physics, the continuum discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) method for breakup reactions, the semi-microscopic approach to heavy-ion subbarrier fusion reactions, the channel coupling effects on nuclear astrophysics and syntheses of superheavy elements, and inclusive breakup and incomplete fusion reactions of weakly-bound nuclei.

## Key claim

The coupled-channels approach remains a standard tool for analyzing low-energy nuclear reactions, but its predictive power depends on approximations such as the isocentrifugal approximation or the use of phenomenological collective models; the paper reviews recent extensions including microscopic coupled-channels methods, continuum discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) for breakup, and applications to superheavy element synthesis, where channel coupling effects are essential but must be treated with care.

## Method

The authors review the coupled-channels formalism, which expands the total wave function in terms of ground and excited states of the colliding nuclei and solves coupled Schrödinger equations to obtain the S-matrix and cross sections. They describe three numerical solution methods: direct integration (e.g., Numerov or modified Numerov), iterative methods (suitable for non-local coupling potentials as in transfer reactions), and variational methods based on the Kohn-Hulthén-Kato principle. The review covers extensions including the isocentrifugal approximation for heavy-ion fusion, the CDCC method for breakup reactions of weakly-bound nuclei, and the microscopic coupled-channels approach using double-folding procedures with complex G-matrix interactions.

## Key numbers

- The example one-dimensional two-channel calculation uses a Gaussian barrier with V0 = 100 MeV and σ = 3 fm, coupling strength F0 = 3 MeV, particle mass m = 29 mN with mNc² = 938 MeV, and excited channel energy ε1 = 2 MeV.
- The deformation parameter βλ is related to the experimental transition probability by βλ = (4π)/(3 ZT RT^λ) √(B(Eλ)↑/e²).
- The rotational energy for ground-state rotational bands of even-even nuclei is given by εI = I(I+1)ħ²/(2J), where J is the moment of inertia.
- The absorption cross section is expressed as σabs = (π/K²) ΣJT (2JT+1) (1 - Σc |S^{JT}_{cc0}|²).

## Context

This review extends earlier summaries of the coupled-channels approach by Tamura (1965) and textbooks by Satchler (1983), Frobrich and Lipperheide (1996), Broglia and Winther (2004), Bertulani and Danielewicz (2004), Thompson and Nunes (2009), and Canto and Hussein (2013). It covers recent developments including the CDCC method (Kamimura 1986, Austern et al. 1987, Yahiro et al. 2012) for neutron-rich nuclei, the microscopic coupled-channels approach (Takashina et al. 2010, Furumoto et al. 2013, Minomo et al. 2016, Kim et al. 2008) applied to cluster structure of light nuclei (Kanada-En'yo 2019-2021), and the Ichimura-Austern-Vincent (IAV) method for inclusive breakup reactions. The work enables more reliable predictions for reactions in unknown nuclear regions and for superheavy element synthesis.
