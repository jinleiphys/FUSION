# Comparison of potential models of nucleus-nucleus bremsstrahlung

> At low photon energies, the potential models of nucleus-nucleus bremsstrahlung are based on electric transition multipole operators, which are derived either only from the nuclear current or only from the charge density by making the long-wavelength approximation and using the Siegert theorem. In the latter case, the bremsstrahlung matrix elements are divergent and some regularization techniques are used to obtain finite values for the bremsstrahlung cross sections. From an extension of the Siegert theorem, which is not based on the long-wavelength approximation, a new potential model of nucleus-nucleus bremsstrahlung is developed. Only convergent integrals are included in this approach. Formal links between bremsstrahlung cross sections obtained in these different models are made. Furthermore, three different ways to calculate the regularized matrix elements are discussed and criticized. Some prescriptions for a proper implementation of the regularization are deduced. A numerical comparison between the different models is done by applying them to the $α+α$ bremsstrahlung.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the long-wavelength-approximation (LWA) Siegert approach to nucleus-nucleus bremsstrahlung leads to divergent matrix elements that require regularization, but an extended Siegert theorem not relying on the LWA yields convergent integrals and can formally justify the regularized results without introducing divergent expressions.

## Method

The authors develop a potential model where colliding nuclei are treated as point-like particles interacting via a real central potential, and derive electric transition multipole operators from both the nuclear current (non-Siegert) and the charge density (Siegert) using an extension of the Siegert theorem that avoids the long-wavelength approximation. They compare three regularization techniques for the divergent LWA Siegert matrix elements: the fixed epsilon_0 method of Garrido, Fedorov, and Jensen (2012), an integration by parts (IP) method inspired by Tanimura and Mosel (1985), and a contour integration (CI) method based on Vincent and Fortune (1970). The different models are applied numerically to alpha+alpha bremsstrahlung.

## Key numbers

- The differential bremsstrahlung cross section formula is given by d sigma/d E_gamma = (E_gamma / (pi^2 hbar^5 c)) * (p_f^2 / (1+delta_12)) * sum over sigma, lambda, mu of (1/(2 lambda+1)) times the integral over theta_f of |u^sigma_{lambda mu}(theta_f,0)|^2 sin theta_f d theta_f, where delta_12 equals 1 for identical nuclei and 0 otherwise.
- The effective charge for electric multipole transitions is Z^(lambda)_eff = Z_1 (m_2/m)^lambda + Z_2 (-m_1/m)^lambda, where m = m_1 + m_2.
- The non-Siegert electric transition multipole operator contains terms proportional to (2 lambda+1)!! / (k_gamma^{lambda+1}) and involves spherical Bessel functions j_lambda(k rho) which behave asymptotically as oscillating functions divided by rho.
- The LWA Siegert operator leads to integrals of the form integral_0^infinity rho^lambda u^f_{l_f} u^i_{l_i} d rho, which diverge for continuum-to-continuum transitions because the integrand behaves as an oscillating function times rho^lambda with lambda > 0.

## Context

This work extends the potential model approach to nucleus-nucleus bremsstrahlung previously used by authors such as P. H. (1982), B. D. (1985), L. R. (1986), and B. S. D. (1991), which avoided the Siegert theorem and used only convergent matrix elements from the nuclear current. It also directly addresses the regularization techniques introduced by Tanimura and Mosel (1985), L. R. (1986c), and Garrido, Fedorov, and Jensen (2012) for the divergent LWA Siegert approach. The extended Siegert method was previously applied to microscopic descriptions of alpha+alpha and alpha+N systems by Dohet-Eraly and Baye (2013, 2014). The paper enables reliable calculation of differential bremsstrahlung cross sections without the arbitrariness of regularization parameters, and clarifies the formal relationships between different potential models.
