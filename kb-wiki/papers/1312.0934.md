# Feynman Diagrams and Rooted Maps

> The Rooted Maps Theory, a branch of the Theory of Homology, is shown to be a powerful tool for investigating the topological properties of Feynman diagrams, related to the single particle propagator in the quantum many-body systems. The numerical correspondence between the number of this class of Feynman diagrams as a function of perturbative order and the number of rooted maps as a function of the number of edges is studied. A graphical procedure to associate Feynman diagrams and rooted maps is then stated. Finally, starting from rooted maps principles, an original definition of the genus of a Feynman diagram, which totally differs from the usual one, is given.

# Feynman Diagrams and Rooted Maps

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates a numerical correspondence between the number of connected Feynman diagrams for the single-particle propagator in non-relativistic many-body fermion theory (without Furry's theorem cancellations) and the number of rooted maps on orientable surfaces regardless of genus, as a function of perturbative order and number of edges respectively. The authors propose a graphical "quotient procedure" to associate individual Feynman diagrams with specific rooted maps, and introduce a new definition of the genus of a Feynman diagram based on rooted maps principles, which differs from the usual definition.

## Method

The authors develop a step-by-step graphical "quotient procedure" that transforms a Feynman diagram of the single-particle Green's function into a rooted map: first connecting the initial and final points to form a closed graph with a rooted arc, then converting interaction lines to solid lines and propagation lines to dotted lines to obtain a quotient graph, collapsing all dotted lines to vertices, and finally embedding the quotient graph on an orientable surface with the minimum number of holes needed to remove all crossings. They apply this procedure to Feynman diagrams up to third perturbative order, using the enumeration of rooted maps from Walsh and Lehman (1972) and Arquès and Béraud (1999) to verify the correspondence.

## Key numbers

- The number of Feynman diagrams for the exact electron propagator (without Furry's theorem) as a function of perturbative order follows the sequence: 1, 2, 10, 74, 706, ... which matches the Arquès-Walsh sequence for rooted maps regardless of genus as a function of number of edges.
- The number of rooted maps M(n) on orientable surfaces regardless of genus as a function of edges n: M(0)=1, M(1)=2, M(2)=10, M(3)=74, M(4)=706.
- For rooted maps with 2 edges: 9 maps on the sphere (genus 0) and 1 map on the torus (genus 1), summing to 10 total.
- For rooted maps with 3 edges: 5 maps on the sphere (genus 0) and 10 maps on the torus (genus 1), summing to 74 total.
- For rooted maps with 4 edges: 14 maps on the sphere (genus 0), 70 on the torus (genus 1), and 21 on genus 2 surfaces, summing to 706 total.
- The Euler-Poincaré invariant: V - E + F = 2 - 2g, where V is vertices, E is edges, F is faces, and g is genus.

## Context

This work extends the combinatorial enumeration of Feynman diagrams previously studied by Cvitanović, Lautrup and Pearson (1978) for QED and by Kucinskii and Sadovskii for many-body theory, connecting it to the mathematical theory of rooted maps developed by Tutte (1960s), Walsh and Lehman (1972), and Arquès and Béraud (1999). The paper provides a graphical procedure to establish a one-to-one topological correspondence between Feynman diagrams and rooted maps, which enables the application of map enumeration techniques to classify Feynman diagrams by their topological genus. This connection may suggest links between general relativity and quantum mechanics through the topological properties of Feynman diagrams, and provides a systematic method for enumerating higher-order perturbative contributions in many-body theory where Furry's theorem does not apply.
