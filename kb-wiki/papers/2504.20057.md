# Can 1957 cold-fusion explain 1989 cold-fusion?

> The possibility that muon-catalyzed nuclear fusion at ambient temperature takes place in deuterated metals is analyzed theoretically. It is suggested that the muon-catalyzed deuterium-deuterium (dd) or deuterium-tritium (dt) fusion rate, experimentally observed in liquid deuterium, increases in a PdD crystal. The main reason is that, in the palladium crystal matrix, deuterium diffuses in ionic (d+), rather than molecular (D2), form, thereby favoring the rate of (d$μ$d)+ formation. The slightly enhanced deuterium density and the possibly reduced muon sticking probability (compared to liquid hydrogen) point to an increase of the fusion-rate, unfortunately counterbalanced by the probability of $μ^-$ capture by the Pd nucleus, whose value should be evaluated by a dedicated experiment. If this mechanism were possible, we advance the hypothesis that 1989 cold fusion might have been randomly triggered by cosmic muons: an order-of-magnitude analysis shows that, under special conditions, a mW power per $μ^-$ might be produced. Moreover, recent experimental results showing that neutrons can sustain deuterium fusion in out-of-equilibrium conditions might explain the remaining power generation. However, reliable quantitative figures are difficult to obtain theoretically, and an experiment is suggested, to be conducted at muon-beam sources, to verify or invalidate this idea.

## Key claim

The paper suggests that the 1989 "cold fusion" excess heat observed in deuterated palladium electrolytic cells might have been randomly triggered by cosmic-ray muons, which could catalyze deuterium-deuterium (dd) or deuterium-tritium (dt) fusion at ambient temperature within the PdD crystal matrix; however, the author emphasizes that reliable quantitative figures are difficult to obtain theoretically and that a dedicated experiment at muon-beam sources is required to verify or invalidate the idea.

## Method

The author theoretically analyzes muon-catalyzed fusion in a PdD crystal by adapting Jackson's (1957) formalism for muonic molecule formation and fusion rates, incorporating condensed-matter properties of palladium hydride. The analysis evaluates three critical parameters: the formation rate of muonic (dμd)+ molecules in the PdD lattice (using diffusion data and the fact that deuterium enters as d+ rather than D2), the effective muon sticking probability after fusion (estimated via stopping-power simulations using the Astar code with silver as a proxy for palladium), and the probability of muon capture by palladium nuclei. The author also considers the role of "hot" neutrons from muon-catalyzed fusion as a potential trigger for further deuterium fusion reactions in the crystal, based on recent experiments with photon-induced fusion in TiD2 and ErD.

## Key numbers

- The muon lifetime is τμ ≃ 2.2 × 10⁻⁶ s.
- The dd fusion rate in muonic molecules is estimated at ≃ 10¹⁰ s⁻¹, and the dt fusion rate up to ≃ 10¹² s⁻¹.
- For dd fusion in pure deuterium gas, the maximum power per muon is 5 mW (corresponding to 11 nJ of energy), but after accounting for molecule formation time and sticking probability, the realistic power is ≃ 25 μW per muon, yielding about 5 pJ of energy per muon.
- For dt fusion, the ideal power is 3 W per muon (6 μJ), but realistic estimates give 150 μW per muon and about 300 pJ of energy per muon.
- The number of fusion reactions per muon is N_R ≃ 8 for dd fusion (with sticking probability p_st ≃ 0.12) and N_R ≃ 150 for dt fusion (p_st ≃ 0.007).
- The diffusion time for deuterium ions between octahedral sites in PdD is t_d ≃ 10⁻¹⁰ s, giving a molecule formation rate f_c up to 10¹⁰ s⁻¹.
- The estimated effective sticking probability in PdD for dd fusion is reduced by about 80% compared to liquid hydrogen, from p_st ≃ 0.12 to p_st ≃ 0.025.
- The cosmic muon flux at sea level is roughly one muon per minute on a cm² surface, with about 45% being μ⁻.
- The lattice parameter of PdH is a ≃ 4.08 Å.
- The energy released in dd fusion channels is Q₁ ≃ 4.0 MeV (p + t channel) and Q₂ ≃ 3.3 MeV (³He + n channel); dt fusion releases Q₃ ≃ 17.6 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of muon-catalyzed fusion developed by Alvarez (1957) and Jackson (1957), and later refined by Vesman (1967) and Ponomarev (1970s), who discovered resonant muonic molecule formation. The author applies this known physics to the controversial 1989 cold fusion claims of Pons and Fleischmann, proposing that cosmic muons could act as random triggers. The paper also connects to recent experiments by the author's group (2023-2024) showing that 2 MeV photons can induce deuterium fusion in TiD₂ and ErD crystals via "hot" neutrons, suggesting that neutrons from muon-catalyzed fusion might sustain the reaction. The work enables a testable hypothesis: if correct, experiments at muon-beam facilities should show enhanced fusion rates in PdD compared to liquid deuterium, and the randomness of 1989 cold fusion results could be explained by the stochastic nature of cosmic muon arrival.
