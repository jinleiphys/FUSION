# Core-collapse supernovae and neutrino properties

> We highlight developments in the domain of supernova neutrinos. We discuss the importance of the future observation, by running and upcoming experiments, of the neutrino signals from the next supernova as well as of the diffuse supernova neutrino background.

# Core-collapse supernovae and neutrino properties

## Key claim
The observation of neutrino signals from the next galactic supernova and the diffuse supernova neutrino background (DSNB) will provide crucial information on the supernova explosion mechanism and on neutrino properties, but the interpretation of these signals is complicated by the unresolved non-linear many-body problem of neutrino flavor evolution in dense environments, particularly the impact of neutrino-neutrino interactions.

## Method
This is a review paper that surveys the current theoretical understanding and experimental prospects for supernova neutrino physics. It discusses the flavor evolution equations (Liouville-Von-Neumann equations for single particle density matrices) including vacuum, matter, and neutrino-neutrino interaction terms, and reviews the status of slow and fast collective flavor modes, the MSW effect, shock wave effects, and non-standard neutrino interactions. The paper also summarizes the capabilities of current and upcoming detectors (Super-Kamiokande, Hyper-Kamiokande, DUNE, JUNO, IceCube) for measuring supernova neutrino signals and the DSNB.

## Key numbers
- Core-collapse supernovae emit about 10^58 neutrinos, taking away 99% of the gravitational binding energy (about 3 x 10^53 erg)
- SN1987A at 50 kpc produced 24 detected antineutrino events across Kamiokande, IMB, and Baksan detectors
- The mean time between galactic supernovae is 50 +/- 20 years
- A supernova at 10 kpc would produce 110 electron neutrino events in DUNE (up to 40 s), about 10 muon/tau neutrinos in JUNO, and 250 antineutrinos in Super-Kamiokande
- The neutrino speed limit from SN1987A gives |c - c_nu|/c < 2 x 10^-9
- The neutrino magnetic moment limit from SN1987A is mu_nu < 1.5 - 5 x 10^-11 mu_B
- The total gravitational binding energy could be determined with about 11% precision with Super-Kamiokande and 3% with Hyper-Kamiokande
- The DSNB detection window is typically between 10 MeV and 30 MeV
- The effective number of degrees of freedom from cosmological Boltzmann equation calculations is N_eff = 3.0440

## Context
This review extends the line of work following the SN1987A observations by Kamiokande (Hirata 1987), IMB (Bionta 1987), and Baksan (Alekseev 1988), which provided the first and only direct neutrino detection from a supernova. The paper discusses how future observations will build on the delayed neutrino-heating explosion mechanism suggested by Bethe and Wilson (1985) and the multidimensional simulations by Janka (2017), Radice (2017), and Bruenn (2018). The review covers the unresolved challenges in neutrino flavor evolution identified by Pantaleone (1992) and the bulb model of Duan (2006), and connects to the DSNB searches by Super-Kamiokande (2021) and the upcoming measurements by JUNO and Hyper-Kamiokande. The paper enables a comprehensive view of how the next supernova observation and DSNB detection will test current explosion models and constrain neutrino properties including mass ordering, magnetic moment, and non-standard interactions.
