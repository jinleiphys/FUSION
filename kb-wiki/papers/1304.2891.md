# Short-distance properties of Coulomb systems

> We use the operator product expansion to derive exact results for the momentum distribution and the static structure factor at high momentum for a jellium model of electrons in both two and three dimensions. It is shown that independent of the precise state of the Coulomb system and for arbitrary temperatures, the asymptotic behavior is a power law in the momentum, whose strength is determined by the contact value of the pair distribution function $g(0)$. The power-law tails are quantum effects which vanish in the classical limit $\hbar \to 0$. A leading order virial expansion shows that the classical and the high-temperature limit do not agree.

## Key claim

The paper uses the operator product expansion (OPE) to prove that, for any state of the jellium model (including inhomogeneous or anisotropic phases, at zero or finite temperature), the high-momentum tails of the momentum distribution and static structure factor are universal power laws whose strength is determined solely by the contact value of the pair distribution function \(g(0)\); the abstract's claim of "exact results" is qualified by the fact that the OPE derivation is justified a posteriori by agreement with mathematically precise results from other methods, rather than by a rigorous proof of convergence for the OPE in non-relativistic Coulomb systems.

## Method

The authors apply the operator product expansion (OPE) to equal-time operator products in the jellium model, treating the Coulomb interaction as instantaneous. They determine the state-independent Wilson coefficients by matching matrix elements of the OPE between few-particle states, solving the zero-energy two-particle s-wave Schrodinger equation for the leading coefficient. The OPE is then applied to the density-density correlator and the one-particle density matrix, extracting the non-analytic terms that dominate the Fourier transform at high momentum.

## Key numbers

* In 3D, the momentum distribution tail is \(n(\mathbf{q}) = (4\pi/a_0)^2 \, n^2 g(0) / q^8\) (Eq. 5.1 in the text).
* In 2D, the momentum distribution tail is \(n(\mathbf{q}) = (2\pi/a_0)^2 \, n^2 g(0) / q^6\) (Eq. 5.1).
* In 3D, the static structure factor tail is \(S(\mathbf{q}) - 1 = - (8\pi/a_0) \, n g(0) / q^4\) (Eq. 5.2).
* In 2D, the static structure factor tail is \(S(\mathbf{q}) - 1 = - (4\pi/a_0) \, n g(0) / q^3\) (Eq. 5.2).
* The dimensionless tail strength \(s(r_s)\) for the momentum distribution is \(s(r_s) = (9/2) \alpha^8 g(0) r_s^2\) in 3D and \(s(r_s) = 2 \alpha^6 g(0) r_s^2\) in 2D, where \(\alpha = (4/9\pi)^{1/3} \simeq 0.521\) in 3D and \(\alpha = 1/\sqrt{2} \simeq 0.707\) in 2D (Eq. 5.3).
* Quantum Monte Carlo results give \(s(10) \simeq 0.006\) in 2D and \(s(10) \simeq 0.009\) in 3D, almost two orders of magnitude smaller than the 1D short-range repulsion value \(s(\infty) = 32\ln 2/(3\pi^2) \simeq 0.749\).

## Context

This work extends the short-distance analysis of Coulomb systems pioneered by Kimball (1973, 1975) and Rajagopal et al. (1977), who derived the cusp condition and high-momentum tails from wavefunction arguments, by providing a derivation at the operator level via the OPE that applies to any state (including Wigner crystals and finite-temperature states). The results are analogous to the Tan relations for zero-range interactions (Tan, 2005; Braaten, 2012; Castin, 2012) and enable improved interpolation schemes for the pair distribution function, such as those by Gori-Giorgi, Sacchetti and Bachelet (2000), which can be used to develop better exchange-correlation functionals in density functional theory. The OPE derivation also reveals that standard approximations like RPA violate the cusp condition, while extensions including local field corrections (Singwi, Tosi, Land and Sjolander, 1968) obey it but violate the compressibility sum rule.
