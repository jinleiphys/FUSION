# Chiral propulsion: the method of effective boundary conditions

> We propose to apply an "effective boundary condition" method to the problem of chiral propulsion. For the case of a rotating helix moving through a fluid at a low Reynolds number, the method amounts to replacing the original helix (in the limit of small pitch) by a cylinder, but with a special kind of partial slip boundary conditions replacing the non-slip boundary conditions on the original helix. These boundary conditions are constructed to reproduce far-field velocities of the original problem, and are defined by a few parameters (slipping lengths) that can be extracted from a problem in planar rather than cylindrical geometry. We derive the chiral propulsion coefficients for spirals, helicoids, helically modulated cylinders, and some of their generalizations using the introduced method. In the case of spirals, we compare our results with the ones derived by Lighthill and find a very good agreement. The proposed method is general and can be applied to any helical shape in the limit of a small pitch. We have established that for a broad class of helical surfaces the dependence of the chiral propulsion on the helical angle $θ$ is universal, $χ\sim \cosθ\sin 2θ$ with the maximal propulsion achieved at the universal angle $θ_m = \tan^{-1}(1/\sqrt{2})\approx 35.26^\circ$.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that for any helical shape with a small pitch (pitch much smaller than the cylinder radius), the chiral propulsion coefficient follows a universal angular dependence \(\chi \sim \cos\theta \sin 2\theta\), with maximal propulsion at \(\theta_m = \tan^{-1}(1/\sqrt{2}) \approx 35.26^\circ\), but this result is conditional on the applicability of the effective boundary condition method (surface details must be smaller than the object's radius and both slip lengths must be of the same order determined by the scale \(L\)).

## Method

The authors replace the original helical surface (in the limit of small pitch) with a cylinder of radius \(R\) that has tensorial Navier partial slip boundary conditions (PSBC) on its surface, characterized by slip lengths \(h_{\parallel}\), \(h_{\perp}\), and \(h\). These effective boundary condition parameters are extracted from solving a planar geometry problem (an array of parallel wires or protruding plates) rather than the full cylindrical problem. The Stokes equations are then solved analytically for the cylinder with these effective PSBC to obtain the chiral propulsion coefficient \(\chi = U_z / \Omega_z\).

## Key numbers

*   The universal maximal propulsion angle is \(\theta_m = \tan^{-1}(1/\sqrt{2}) \approx 35.26^\circ\) for a broad class of helical surfaces.
*   For an \(N\)-spiral with wire radius \(a\) and inter-wire distance \(2L\), the chiral propulsion coefficient is given by \(N\frac{\chi_N}{R} = \frac{\left(\frac{1}{2}+\ln{\frac{L}{\pi a}}\right)\cos\theta \sin 2\theta}{2-\frac{L}{2\pi R}(1+\cos 2\theta) +\frac{L}{\pi R}\ln{\frac{L}{\pi a}}(3-\cos 2\theta)}\).
*   For an \(N\)-helicoid in the limit of large \(N\) (neglecting \(1/N\) corrections), the chiral propulsion coefficient is \(N\frac{\chi_{N-helicoid}}{R}\approx 0.85 \cos{\theta}\sin 2\theta\).
*   For a single helicoid (N=1) with a small pitch, the propulsion coefficient is \(\frac{\chi_{helicoid}}{R}\approx 0.02 \frac{d^2}{R^2}\) for \(d\ll R\).
*   The planar geometry slip lengths for an array of protruding plates are \(h_{\perp} \approx 0.17 L\) and \(h_{\parallel} \approx 0.44 L\).

## Context

This work extends the slender-body theory of Lighthill (1975) for spiral propulsion to a broader class of helical shapes (helicoids, helically modulated cylinders, N-spirals) in the small-pitch limit. The method of effective boundary conditions provides an alternative to perturbative approaches that fail for small pitch (e.g., the work of Li and colleagues). This enables the calculation of chiral propulsion for complex helical surfaces where exact solutions to the Stokes equations are not available, and it predicts a universal optimal helix angle for propulsion.
