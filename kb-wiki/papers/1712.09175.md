# Effects of causality on the fluidity and viscous horizon of quark-gluon plasma

> The second order Israel-Stewart-M$\ddot{u}$ller relativistic hydrodynamics has been applied to study the effects of causality on the acoustic oscillation in relativistic fluid. Causal dispersion relations have been derived with non-vanishing shear viscosity, bulk viscosity and thermal conductivity at non-zero temperature and chemical potential. These relations have been used to investigate the fluidity of Quark Gluon Plasma (QGP) at finite temperature ($T$). Results of the first order dissipative hydrodynamics have been obtained as limiting case of the second order theory. The effects of the causality on the fluidity near the transition point and on viscous horizon are found to be significant. We observe that the inclusion of causality reduces the fluidity of QGP. It has also been shown that the inclusion of large magnetic field in the causal hydrodynamics alters the fluidity of QGP

## Key claim

The inclusion of causality via second-order Israel-Stewart-Muller hydrodynamics increases the fluidity measure of QGP near the transition temperature \(T_c\), making flow more difficult than predicted by first-order (acausal) theory; the effect is significant near the transition point and is also altered by the presence of a large magnetic field.

## Method

The authors derive causal dispersion relations for sound waves in a relativistic fluid by applying second-order Israel-Stewart-Muller hydrodynamics with non-zero shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, and thermal conductivity at finite temperature and baryonic chemical potential. They then use these dispersion relations to compute the viscous horizon (the threshold wave vector above which sound cannot propagate) and a dimensionless fluidity measure \(F = L_\eta / L_\rho\), where \(L_\eta\) is a dissipative length scale and \(L_\rho\) is the inter-particle distance. The analysis is extended to include a constant external magnetic field, and results are compared with the acausal (first-order) limit obtained by setting second-order coefficients to zero.

## Key numbers

* The transition temperature from QGP to hadrons is around \(T_c \sim 155\) MeV, from lattice QCD simulations at zero chemical potential.
* The causal viscous horizon for longitudinal modes is given by \(k^{causal}_{v, long} = \sqrt{ (P+\epsilon) / [ (t/2)(\zeta/3 + 4\eta/3) + (1/9)\zeta^2\beta_0 + (8/3)\eta^2\beta_2 ] }\), where \(t\) is time, \(P\) is pressure, \(\epsilon\) is energy density, \(\zeta\) is bulk viscosity, \(\eta\) is shear viscosity, and \(\beta_0, \beta_2\) are second-order relaxation coefficients.
* The acausal limit of the longitudinal viscous horizon is \(k^{acausal}_{v, long} = \sqrt{ (P+\epsilon) / [ (t/2)(\zeta/3 + 4\eta/3) ] }\).
* The fluidity measure \(F\) for a system with vanishing net charge density is \(F = [\rho^{1/3}/4] \{ \zeta^2 + 8\zeta\eta + 16\eta^2 + 2(P+\epsilon)\beta_0\zeta^2 (dP/d\epsilon) + 48\eta^2\beta_2(P+\epsilon)(dP/d\epsilon) \}^{1/2} / \{ (P+\epsilon)^2 (dP/d\epsilon) \}^{1/2}\), where \(\rho\) is particle number density.

## Context

This work extends the study of QGP fluidity by Koch et al. (2008), which used first-order (acausal) hydrodynamics and found a fluidity measure based on shear viscosity alone. The present paper incorporates second-order causal hydrodynamics following Israel and Stewart (1979) and includes bulk viscosity, thermal conductivity, and relaxation coefficients, showing that causality reduces fluidity (makes flow more difficult) near the transition temperature. The results enable more accurate modeling of sound propagation and viscous damping in heavy-ion collisions, particularly for estimating the viscous horizon which affects flow harmonics \(v_n/v_2\) as discussed by Lacey et al. (2006) and Staig and Shuryak (2011). The inclusion of magnetic field effects follows the work of Skokov et al. (2009) on transient magnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions.
