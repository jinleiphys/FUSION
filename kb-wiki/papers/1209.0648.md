# Fukushima plutonium effect and blow-up regimes in neutron-multiplying media

> It is shown that the capture and fission cross-sections of 238U and 239Pu increase with temperature within 1000-3000 K range, in contrast to those of 235U, that under certain conditions may lead to the so-called blow-up modes, stimulating the anomalous neutron flux and nuclear fuel temperature growth. Some features of the blow-up regimes in neutron-multiplying media are discussed.

## Key claim
The capture and fission cross-sections of 238U and 239Pu increase monotonically with temperature in the 1000-3000 K range, unlike those of 235U, which may under certain conditions create a positive feedback loop leading to "blow-up modes" where temperature and neutron flux grow to infinity in finite time. The authors emphasize these are preliminary qualitative results based on constant neutron flux assumptions and without solving the full coupled neutron kinetics and heat transfer equations.

## Method
The authors calculated resonance neutron cross-sections for 235U, 238U, 239U, and 239Pu as functions of neutron energy and medium temperature (300-3000 K) using the Doppler effect formalism with data from the ENDF/B-VII database. They then averaged these cross-sections over a combined neutron spectrum consisting of a Maxwell distribution for thermal neutrons, a Fermi spectrum for epithermal neutrons, and a 239Pu fission spectrum for fast neutrons, with the joining energy between Maxwell and Fermi spectra treated as a free parameter. Using these temperature-dependent averaged cross-sections, they computed the heat source density for various uranium-plutonium compositions at constant neutron flux and solved the heat transfer equation with nonlinear heat sources to demonstrate blow-up regimes.

## Key numbers
- The capture and fission cross-sections of 238U and 239Pu increase with temperature within the 1000-3000 K range, while those of 235U decrease following the ~1/sqrt(En) dependence
- The heat source density for uranium-plutonium fissile medium was approximated by a power function of temperature with exponent 4 (q_T ~ T^(1+delta) where delta > 1)
- For blow-up mode calculations, the heat capacity of 238U was approximated by a 6th-order polynomial: cp(T) ≈ -7.206 + 0.64T - 0.0047T^2 + 0.0000126T^3 + 2.004×10^(-8)T^4 - 1.60×10^(-10)T^5 - 2.15×10^(-13)T^6
- The thermal conductivity of 238U was approximated as ℵ(T) ≈ 21.575 + 0.0152661 T
- For the model reactor with 6000 K boundary conditions, the heat conductivity coefficient was set to ℵ = 0.18×10^(-4) × T using the Wiedemann-Franz law
- The specific heat capacity at constant pressure was set to cp ≈ 6 cal/(mol·deg) according to the Dulong-Petit law
- The model reactor fissile medium was a cube of size 10.0×10.0×10.0 m
- The established temperature field in the model reactor reached 10^8 K and persisted for at least 50 seconds
- The melting temperature of uranium-oxide fuel is approximately 3000 degrees Celsius (3273 K)
- The melting temperature of 238U is 1400 K

## Context
This work extends qualitative estimates by Ukraintsev (Obninsk Institute of Atomic Energetics, Russia) who suggested the possibility of 239Pu cross-section growth in the 300-1500 degrees Celsius range. The paper addresses a gap in the literature where no experimental or theoretical data existed on the temperature dependence of 238U and 239Pu capture and fission cross-sections in the 1000-3000 degrees Celsius range. The findings have implications for fast traveling wave reactors (TWR) of the Feoktistov (uranium-plutonium) and Teller (thorium-uranium) types, and suggest that blow-up modes could explain the fuel melting observed during the Fukushima nuclear accident where MOX fuel containing plutonium was used in the third reactor. The work enables further investigation of controlled blow-up regimes for potential thermonuclear fusion conditions, though the authors note that modern literature lacks data on heat capacity and thermal conductivity of fissile materials above 1500 K.
