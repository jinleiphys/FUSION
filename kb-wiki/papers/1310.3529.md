# On the origin of long-range azimuthal correlations in hadronic collisions

> We review the models suggested, to date, as an explanation for the so called "ridge" phenomenon, an elongation in rapidity of 2-particle correlations seen at RHIC and LHC energies. We argue that these models can be divided into two phenomenologically distinct classes: "Hotspot+flow" driven correlations, where initial state correlations created by structures local in configuration space are collimated by transverse flow, and models where the azimuthal correlation is created through local partonic interactions in a high gluon density initial state. We further argue that the key to distinguish these two scenarios is to experimentally examine the role flow plays in the ridge. The measurement of a strong double ridge in $pA$ and $dA$ collisions allows a good opportunity to understand the ridge's origin. In this sense, particle-identified correlations are particularly promising way of testing the assumptions at the base of either of the two models above. The successful fit of the pA ridge by hydrodynamic models \cite{us}, therefore, suggests that the ridge is primarily a "final state effect" generated by radial expansion, although its scaling across system sizes is non-trivial to reconcile with hydrodynamic expectations. We close by discussing experimental observables capable of clarifying the situation.

# On the origin of long-range azimuthal correlations in hadronic collisions

## Key claim

The ridge phenomenon in hadronic collisions can be divided into two phenomenologically distinct classes: "Hotspot+flow" driven correlations where initial state correlations are collimated by transverse flow, and models where azimuthal correlation is created through local partonic interactions in a high gluon density initial state. The author argues that while hydrodynamic models successfully fit the pA ridge, suggesting it is primarily a final state effect generated by radial expansion, the scaling of the ridge across system sizes is non-trivial to reconcile with hydrodynamic expectations, and particle-identified correlations offer a promising way to distinguish between the two scenarios.

## Method

The author reviews existing models for the ridge phenomenon and classifies them into two categories: hydrodynamic flow models where transverse flow collimates initial state hotspots, and initial state models where partonic interactions in a high gluon density environment generate angular correlations. The paper analyzes Knudsen number scaling arguments to compare ridge behavior across pp, pA/dA, and AA collision systems, and derives predictions for particle-identified two-particle correlation functions from both hydrodynamic (Cooper-Frye freezeout) and initial state (fragmentation-based) frameworks. The author then proposes specific experimental tests using identified particle correlations, including ratios of harmonic coefficients across particle species and system sizes.

## Key numbers

- Knudsen number estimate for pp collisions: Kn_pp approximately 0.6, assuming eta/s = 1/(4 pi) and tau_0 = 1 fm
- Knudsen number scaling relations: Kn_pA ~ N_part^(-1/3) K ~ (dN/dy)^(-1/3); Kn_AA ~ N_part^(-1/9-1/3) Kn_pp s^(-0.02) ~ (dN/dy)^(-4/9)
- Temperature scaling estimate: T_pA ~ N_part^(1/3) T_pp, with Knudsen number difference between pp and pA of no more than 30-50%
- Knudsen number increase from AA to pA/dA: approximately order 2-4 when transverse size is counted as R
- For pA collisions, R_pA(p_T) approximately 1 from p_T approximately 3-4 GeV onward
- Threshold for hydrodynamic v_n(p_T) to vanish: p_T >> (T^3 epsilon_n R/Kn)^(1/2-2/3)

## Context

This review extends the ongoing debate about the origin of the ridge phenomenon first observed at RHIC and later at LHC energies, following experimental observations from STAR, PHENIX, PHOBOS, ALICE, ATLAS, and CMS collaborations. The paper contrasts hydrodynamic approaches (Voloshin, Moschelli, Gavin, Hama) with initial state models based on partonic interactions (Kovner, Dusling, Venu, Kovchegov) and CGC-based calculations. The work builds on the observation of a strong double ridge in pA and dA collisions (paridge1-5) and the empirical v_2 scaling formula found by gtscaling and gtscaling2. The author identifies particle-identified correlations as a key discriminator, noting that hydrodynamic calculations (Torrieri, Bozek) successfully fit the PID ridge while in-vacuum fragmentation ansatze fail to reproduce the experimental pattern, though the scaling difficulties across system sizes remain unresolved.
