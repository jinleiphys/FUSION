# Scattering wave packets of hadrons in gauge theories: Preparation on a quantum computer

> Quantum simulation holds promise of enabling a complete description of high-energy scattering processes rooted in gauge theories of the Standard Model. A first step in such simulations is preparation of interacting hadronic wave packets. To create the wave packets, one typically resorts to adiabatic evolution to bridge between wave packets in the free theory and those in the interacting theory, rendering the simulation resource intensive. In this work, we construct a wave-packet creation operator directly in the interacting theory to circumvent adiabatic evolution, taking advantage of resource-efficient schemes for ground-state preparation, such as variational quantum eigensolvers. By means of an ansatz for bound mesonic excitations in confining gauge theories, which is subsequently optimized using classical or quantum methods, we show that interacting mesonic wave packets can be created efficiently and accurately using digital quantum algorithms that we develop. Specifically, we obtain high-fidelity mesonic wave packets in the $Z_2$ and $U(1)$ lattice gauge theories coupled to fermionic matter in 1+1 dimensions. Our method is applicable to both perturbative and non-perturbative regimes of couplings. The wave-packet creation circuit for the case of the $Z_2$ lattice gauge theory is built and implemented on the Quantinuum H1-1 trapped-ion quantum computer using 13 qubits and up to 308 entangling gates. The fidelities agree well with classical benchmark calculations after employing a simple symmetry-based noise-mitigation technique. This work serves as a step toward quantum computing scattering processes in quantum chromodynamics.

## Key claim

The authors construct a wave-packet creation operator directly in the interacting theory of confining gauge theories, circumventing the need for adiabatic evolution that is typically required in the Jordan-Lee-Preskill protocol, but the method is demonstrated only for 1+1-dimensional Abelian lattice gauge theories (Z₂ and U(1)) and relies on a variational ansatz that must be optimized classically or quantumly for each momentum sector.

## Method

The authors propose a hybrid classical-quantum algorithm that first prepares the interacting ground state of a lattice gauge theory using a variational quantum eigensolver, then applies an optimized mesonic creation operator ansatz built from fermion-antifermion pairs connected by gauge-link strings, weighted by a Gaussian distribution in relative momentum. The ansatz parameters (width and average separation) are optimized by energy minimization in each momentum sector, and the resulting momentum-eigenstate operators are assembled into a wave-packet creation operator using a Gaussian momentum-space profile. The circuit for the Z₂ lattice gauge theory is implemented on a trapped-ion quantum computer using 13 qubits and up to 308 entangling gates.

## Key numbers

- The wave-packet creation circuit for the Z₂ lattice gauge theory was implemented on the Quantinuum H1-1 trapped-ion quantum computer using **13 qubits** and up to **308 entangling gates**.
- The system studied had **6 fermionic sites** (12+1 qubits) for the Z₂ lattice gauge theory implementation.
- The method was demonstrated for Z₂ and U(1) lattice gauge theories in **1+1 dimensions** with one flavor of staggered fermions.

## Context

This work extends the tensor-network approach of Rigobello (2021) for constructing mesonic wave packets in the lattice Schwinger model, which worked well only in the weak-coupling regime, by adding a layer of classical or quantum optimization that makes the ansatz suitable for both perturbative and non-perturbative regimes. The method circumvents the adiabatic evolution required in the Jordan-Lee-Preskill protocol (Jordan, 2012) for scattering simulations on quantum computers, and builds upon variational quantum eigensolver techniques for ground-state preparation (Peruzzo, 2014; McClean, 2016). This work enables quantum simulation of hadron-hadron collisions in lower-dimensional gauge theories on current-generation quantum hardware, serving as a step toward quantum computing scattering processes in quantum chromodynamics.
