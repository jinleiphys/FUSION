# The Primordial Lithium Problem

> Big-bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) theory, together with the precise WMAP cosmic baryon density, makes tight predictions for the abundances of the lightest elements. Deuterium and 4He measurements agree well with expectations, but 7Li observations lie a factor 3-4 below the BBN+WMAP prediction. This 4-5σ mismatch constitutes the cosmic "lithium problem," with disparate solutions possible. (1) Astrophysical systematics in the observations could exist but are increasingly constrained. (2) Nuclear physics experiments provide a wealth of well-measured cross-section data, but 7Be destruction could be enhanced by unknown or poorly-measured resonances, such as 7Be + 3He -> 10C^* -> p + 9B. (3) Physics beyond the Standard Model can alter the 7Li abundance, though D and 4He must remain unperturbed; we discuss such scenarios, highlighting decaying Supersymmetric particles and time-varying fundamental constants. Present and planned experiments could reveal which (if any) of these is the solution to the problem.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the "lithium problem" is a genuine 4-5σ discrepancy between BBN+WMAP predictions and observed 7Li abundances, but it qualifies that the problem could be resolved by one of three distinct classes of solution: astrophysical systematics in the observations, new or revised nuclear physics (such as poorly-measured resonances), or physics beyond the Standard Model.

## Method
The author reviews the standard BBN framework, including the nuclear reaction network and the role of the baryon-to-photon ratio η, and then compares BBN predictions (using the WMAP-derived η as an input) to observed primordial abundances of D, 4He, and 7Li. The analysis uses likelihood distributions to quantify the agreement between theory and observations for each light element, highlighting the mismatch for 7Li. The paper then systematically evaluates three categories of potential solutions: astrophysical (e.g., stellar temperature systematics, lithium depletion), nuclear physics (e.g., new reaction pathways like 7Be + 3He → 10C* → p + 9B), and beyond-Standard-Model physics (e.g., decaying supersymmetric particles, time-varying constants).

## Key numbers
- The observed 7Li/H abundance is (1.23^{+0.68}_{-0.32}) × 10^{-10} at 95% CL, based on a careful error budget dominated by systematics.
- The WMAP 7-year data gives η = (6.19 ± 0.15) × 10^{-10}, a 2.4% measurement.
- The discrepancy between BBN+WMAP predictions and observed 7Li is a factor of 2.4-4.3, representing a 4.2-5.3σ mismatch.
- The observed D/H abundance is (2.82 ± 0.21) × 10^{-5} from seven high-redshift systems.
- The observed primordial 4He mass fraction is Y_p = 0.249 ± 0.009.
- The standard BBN prediction for 6Li/H is about 10^{-14}, or 6Li/7Li ≤ 10^{-4}, far below the claimed 6Li plateau of 6Li/7Li ≈ 0.05.

## Context
This review extends the work of Cyburt (2004), Coc (2004), and others who first identified the lithium problem using WMAP data, and it contrasts the severe 7Li discrepancy with the spectacular agreement for deuterium and good agreement for 4He. The paper enables future experimental searches for new resonances (e.g., 7Be + d and 7Be + 3He reactions) and theoretical work on decaying supersymmetric particles or time-varying constants, while noting that astrophysical solutions (e.g., stellar depletion models by Korn (2006)) remain possible but are increasingly constrained by the small scatter in the Spite plateau and the presence of 6Li.
