# Nuclear Physics Neutrino PreTown Meeting: Summary and Recommendations

> In preparation for the nuclear physics Long Range Plan exercise, a group of 104 neutrino physicists met in Seattle September 21-23 to discuss both the present state of the field and the new opportunities of the next decade. This report summarizes the conclusions of that meeting and presents its recommendations. Further information is available at the workshop's web site. This report will be further reviewed at the upcoming Oakland Town Meeting.

## Key claim

The paper argues that nuclear physics has a unique opportunity to lead major discoveries in neutrino physics and astrophysics over the next decade, but this requires overcoming historical obstacles in US funding and infrastructure for non-accelerator neutrino experiments, particularly the lack of a deep underground laboratory.

## Method

The meeting was organized around five working groups: solar neutrinos; supernova neutrinos and the supernova mechanism; reactor and accelerator neutrinos; underground laboratories; and neutrino mass (double beta decay and tritium beta decay). Plenary speakers summarized the field's status, working groups discussed future directions, and conclusions were presented in a final plenary session with group discussion of priorities.

## Key numbers

- Atmospheric neutrino flux measurements suggest a seesaw mass of about 10^15 GeV, close to the grand unification scale
- Neutrinoless double beta decay lifetime limits have improved by a factor of two every two years over the past two decades, now reaching approximately 10^25 years
- Current detector masses for double beta decay are about 10 kg, with next-generation experiments requiring about 1000 kg
- The current limit on the Majorana mass is in the range (0.4-1.0) eV, with the spread reflecting nuclear matrix element uncertainties
- Tritium beta decay mass limits have reached 2.2 eV (95% confidence level)
- The Heidelberg-Moscow experiment has set a limit of 2 x 10^25 years on the 76Ge lifetime after more than 35 kg-years of data
- Next-generation double beta decay experiments aim for Majorana mass limits in the range 0.03-0.10 eV
- The delta m^2 deduced by SuperKamiokande is centered around about (0.05 eV)^2
- Typical scale of major solar neutrino experiments is now in the $25-50M range
- Anticipated cost of a typical 1000 kg double beta decay experiment is about $10M, exclusive of isotope enrichment costs
- The Karlsruhe-Mainz-Troitsk tritium beta decay project aims to push mass limits to about 0.5 eV with an estimated cost of $10-15M

## Context

This white paper summarizes the conclusions of a 2000 meeting of 104 neutrino physicists in preparation for the nuclear physics Long Range Plan exercise. It extends the community's response to the atmospheric neutrino results from SuperKamiokande and the construction of SNO, Borexino, and KamLAND, while noting that Europe (Gran Sasso) and Japan (Kamioka/SuperKamiokande) have moved ahead of the US in underground laboratory infrastructure. The recommendations aim to enable next-generation solar neutrino detectors for low-energy pp and 7Be neutrinos, ton-scale double beta decay experiments, a neutrino bunker at the Spallation Neutron Source, and a theory initiative for multi-dimensional supernova modeling with realistic neutrino transport.
