# Quantum Chromodynamics at small Bjorken-x

> With the advent of very powerful particle accelerators, such as RHIC and the LHC, it becomes possible to study QCD in high energy collisions, in which the gluon content of the proton or nucleus is probed and its density becomes often large enough for nonlinear effects to play a role. This small-x regime of QCD is well described by an effective theory known as the Color Glass Condensate (CGC). In this thesis, we introduce the CGC and apply it to two different problems. First, we use the CGC to study forward heavy-quark production in pA collisions. When the quarks are nearly back-to-back, the CGC result coincides with the one in the TMD factorization approach. This allows us to extract the small-x limit of the Weizsäcker-Williams gluon distribution, as well as the dipole distribution and one extra gluon TMD. Each of these gluon TMDs is accompanied by a partner, which couples via the quark mass and which describes the linearly polarized gluon content of the unpolarized nucleus. We calculate the six resulting gluon TMDs analytically in the MV model, and evolve them in rapidity using a numerical implementation of JIMWLK. The second problem is situated within heavy-ion physics. Jets, produced in the scattering of two nuclei, travel through the Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP) before reaching the detector, and are attenuated as a result of their interaction with this medium. This phenomenon, known as jet quenching, is one of the main probes to investigate the QGP. We focus on the transverse momentum broadening of a hard particle traveling through a nuclear medium, and employ small-x techniques to attempt to resum the leading logarithmic corrections due to soft gluon radiation. Although, ultimately, we can only solve the resulting in-medium evolution equation to DLA accuracy, we do present a concise framework for the problem, and draw a detailed comparison with the CGC and with the literature.

## Key claim

The thesis demonstrates that the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) effective theory reproduces both High-Energy Factorization (HEF) and Transverse Momentum Dependent (TMD) factorization results for forward heavy-quark production in proton-nucleus collisions in the appropriate kinematic limits, but only when the produced quarks are nearly back-to-back in the transverse plane and have large transverse momenta.

## Method

The author calculated the CGC cross section for forward heavy diquark production in pA collisions analytically, then extracted six gluon TMD distribution functions (three of which couple only through the heavy quark mass and correspond to linearly polarized gluons) by matching the CGC result to the TMD factorization framework. These gluon TMDs were computed analytically in the McLerran-Venugopalan (MV) model at finite Nc, and their rapidity evolution was studied numerically using a lattice QCD implementation of the JIMWLK evolution equation. Separately, the author generalized the JIMWLK equation to apply to an extended nuclear medium to resum leading logarithmic corrections to transverse momentum broadening of a hard particle traveling through a Quark-Gluon Plasma.

## Key numbers

- Six gluon TMD distribution functions were identified as relevant for forward heavy diquark production, with four of them being new results (Eqs. F1, H1, F2, H2 at finite Nc)
- The analysis covers Bjorken-x values down to approximately 5×10⁻⁵, as shown in the HERA structure function data reproduced in the thesis
- The virtuality Q² of the probing photon in DIS ranges from low values up to the scales accessible at RHIC and the LHC
- The evolution equations were solved to double leading logarithmic approximation (DLA) accuracy, though the original goal of single leading logarithmic (LLA) resummation was not achieved

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations by Marquet and collaborators on forward dijet production by including the heavy quark mass, and builds upon the Color Glass Condensate framework developed by McLerran, Venugopalan, and others. The thesis connects the CGC approach to TMD factorization, which was previously studied by various groups including those working on the small-x limit of gluon TMDs. For the jet quenching problem, the author draws on the BDMPS formalism and earlier work by Iancu, as well as calculations by Blaizot and collaborators on the renormalization group approach to transverse momentum broadening. The results enable future phenomenological studies of forward heavy-quark production at RHIC and the LHC, and provide a framework for understanding the relationship between different factorization schemes in small-x QCD.
