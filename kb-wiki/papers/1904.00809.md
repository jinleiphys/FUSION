# $^{93m}$Mo isomer depletion via beam-based nuclear excitation by electron capture

> A recent nuclear physics experiment [C. J. Chiara {\it et al.}, Nature (London) {\bf 554}, 216 (2018)] reports the first direct observation of nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC) in the depletion of the $^{93m}$Mo isomer. The experiment used a beam-based setup in which Mo highly charged ions with nuclei in the isomeric state $^{93m}$Mo at 2.4 MeV excitation energy were slowed down in a solid-state target. In this process, nuclear excitation to a higher triggering level led to isomer depletion. The reported excitation probability $P_{\rm{exc}} = 0.01$ was solely attributed to the so-far unobserved process of NEEC in lack of a different known channel of comparable efficiency. In this work, we investigate theoretically the beam-based setup and calculate excitation rates via NEEC using state-of-the-art atomic structure and ion stopping power models. For all scenarios, our results disagree with the experimental data by approximately nine orders of magnitude. This stands in conflict with the conclusion that NEEC was the excitation mechanism behind the observed depletion rate.

# $^{93m}$Mo isomer depletion via beam-based nuclear excitation by electron capture

## Key claim

The authors theoretically model the beam-based NEEC scenario reported by Chiara et al. (2018) and find that the calculated NEEC excitation probability disagrees with the experimentally reported value by approximately nine orders of magnitude, contradicting the conclusion that NEEC was the excitation mechanism behind the observed isomer depletion.

## Method

The authors calculate NEEC cross sections using a Multi-Configurational-Dirac-Fock method for relativistic bound electronic wavefunctions and numerical solutions to the Dirac equation for free electrons under the single-active electron approximation. They model the ion charge state distribution using two different fitting formulas (Nikolaev and Dmitriev 1968; Schiwietz and Grande 2001) and the stopping power using the CasP code and the semi-empirical Javanainen formula, then integrate over all possible recombination channels (648 channels for charge states Mo$^{14+}$ to Mo$^{42+}$ into $L$, $M$, $N$, and $O$ shells) and the charge state distribution to obtain the total NEEC probability per $^{93m}$Mo ion.

## Key numbers

- Theoretical NEEC probability per $^{93m}$Mo ion: $2.58 \times 10^{-11}$ (using CasP-q stopping power and Schiwietz-Grande charge state distribution), compared to the experimental excitation probability $P_{\rm{exc}} = 0.01$ reported by Chiara et al. (2018), a discrepancy of approximately nine orders of magnitude.
- The largest partial NEEC probability for a single channel is $P_{q}^{\alpha} \sim 10^{-9}$ for recombination into the $2p_{3/2}$ orbital of the $L$ shell.
- NEEC probabilities for recombination into $M$, $N$, and $O$ shells are approximately $10^{-11}$, $10^{-12}$, and $10^{-12}$, respectively.
- The nuclear transition energy from the isomeric state to the triggering level is 4.85 keV (electric quadrupole, $E2$), with a nuclear reduced transition probability $B = 3.5$ W.u. (Weisskopf units).
- The natural width of the triggering level $T$ at 2429.80 keV is $10^{-7}$ eV for NEEC into the electronic ground state; for capture into excited electronic configurations, the electronic width is typically on the order of 1 eV.
- For a Pb target, the calculated NEEC probability is approximately $5 \times 10^{-11}$ (using the Javanainen stopping power model and charge state distribution model (ii)).

## Context

This work provides the first detailed theoretical calculation of NEEC probabilities for the beam-based isomer depletion scenario reported by Chiara et al. (2018), which claimed the first direct observation of NEEC. The authors find that their calculated NEEC probabilities are approximately $10^{-11}$, nine orders of magnitude smaller than the experimental value of 0.01, and note that estimates of Coulomb excitation and inelastic scattering probabilities from Chiara et al. (2018) yield approximately $10^{-6}$ for Pb and C targets respectively, still too small to explain the observed excitation but much larger than the NEEC probability. The results suggest that non-resonant nuclear excitation processes may be more efficient than NEEC in this setup, and that the observed isomer depletion may be due to a novel channel so far disregarded in state-of-the-art theory.
