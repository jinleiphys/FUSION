# A semiclassical description of the Autocorrelations in Nuclear Masses

> Nuclear mass autocorrelations are investigated as a function of the number of nucleons. The fluctuating part of these autocorrelations is modeled by a parameter free model in which the nucleons are confined in a rigid sphere. Explicit results are obtained by using periodic orbit theory. Despite the simplicity of the model we have found a remarkable quantitative agreement of the mass autocorrelations for all nuclei in the nuclear data chart. In order to achieve a similar degree of agreement for the nuclear masses themselves it is necessary to consider additional variables such as multipolar corrections to the spherical shape and an effective number of nucleons. Our findings suggest that higher order effects like nuclear deformations or residual interactions have little relevance in the description of the fluctuations of the nuclear autocorrelations.

## Key claim
The authors claim that a parameter-free spherical cavity model, using periodic orbit theory, achieves remarkable quantitative agreement with experimental nuclear mass autocorrelations across the entire nuclear data chart, but only for the autocorrelations themselves, not for the nuclear masses, which require additional multipolar corrections and an effective number of nucleons.

## Method
The fluctuating part of the nuclear mass (difference between experimental mass and liquid drop model prediction) was modeled by treating protons and neutrons as free fermions confined in a rigid sphere of radius R = r0 * N_nuc^(1/3) with r0 ~ 1.1 fm. The oscillatory part of the spectral density was computed analytically using periodic orbit theory (trace formula) for a spherical cavity, summing over classical periodic orbits (diameters and regular polygons). The autocorrelation function C(q) was then evaluated for the 2140 combinations of neutron and proton numbers in the nuclear data chart, averaging over nuclei with the same neutron number, same mass number, or using a boustrophedon ordering scheme.

## Key numbers
- The spherical cavity radius is set to R = r0 * N_nuc^(1/3) with r0 ~ 1.1 fm.
- The coherence length cutoff for periodic orbits is set to xi ~ 5 R, corresponding to an effective temperature close to 1 MeV.
- The autocorrelation C(q) was computed for neutron numbers N = 30 to 154 and for N = 60 to 154, and for mass numbers A = 50 to 254 and A = 116 to 254.
- The power spectrum of the mass fluctuations decays as S(k) ~ k^(-alpha) with alpha ~ 4.2, close to the theoretical prediction alpha = 4 for integrable classical dynamics.
- The analysis used 2140 combinations of N and Z from the nuclear data chart.

## Context
This work extends the spherical cavity model previously applied to nuclear mass fluctuations (Patricio et al.) and to metal clusters (Pav98), showing that the model works better for autocorrelations than for the masses themselves. It contrasts with more sophisticated models like the finite range droplet model (Moll95) and the Duflo-Zuker model (Duf94), which include explicit deformation and residual interactions. The results suggest that higher-order effects like nuclear deformations and residual interactions have little relevance for mass autocorrelations, and the power spectrum analysis provides a test for integrability in many-body systems. The boustrophedon ordering scheme follows Hir04b and Bar05.
