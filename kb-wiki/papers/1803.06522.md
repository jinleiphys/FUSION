# Fragment Distribution in Reactions of $^{78,86}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta

> Within the framework of the isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics model along with the GEMINI model, the reaction of $^{86}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta at 80,120 and 160 MeV/nucleon and the reaction of $^{78}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta at 160 MeV/nucleon are studied, and the production cross sections of the generated fragments are calculated. More intermediate and large mass fragments can be produced in the reaction with a large range of impact parameter. The production cross sections of nuclei such as the isotopes of Si and P generally decrease with the increasing incident energy. The isotopes near the neutron drip line are produced more in the neutron-rich system $^{86}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta.

## Key claim

The production cross sections of isotopes near the neutron drip line (such as neutron-rich Si and P isotopes) are larger in the neutron-rich system $^{86}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta than in the less neutron-rich system $^{78}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta, while isotopes near the proton drip line are produced more in the $^{78}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta reaction; this isospin dependence holds at a fixed incident energy of 160 MeV/nucleon.

## Method

The authors used the isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics (IQMD) model to simulate the dynamical evolution of the heavy-ion collisions, followed by the statistical decay model GEMINI to describe the sequential decay of excited fragments via a Monte Carlo method. They calculated fragment production cross sections for $^{86}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta at 80, 120, and 160 MeV/nucleon and for $^{78}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta at 160 MeV/nucleon, with impact parameters ranging from 0 to 10 fm. The IQMD model uses Gaussian wave packets for nucleons and evolves the system via the Euler-Lagrange equations derived from a generalized variational principle.

## Key numbers

- For $^{86}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta at 160 MeV/nucleon, the impact parameter range used was $b = 0-10$ fm; intermediate and large mass fragments were produced more with a larger range of impact parameter, while light mass fragments showed weak dependence.
- The production cross sections of Si isotopes ($^{22-42}$Si) in $^{86}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta decreased as incident energy increased from 80 MeV/nucleon to 160 MeV/nucleon; the difference for $^{24}$Si between 80 and 160 MeV/nucleon was "more obvious."
- The production cross sections of P isotopes ($^{24-46}$P) in $^{86}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta decreased as incident energy increased from 80 MeV/nucleon to 160 MeV/nucleon; the gap for $^{27}$P and $^{45}$P between 80 and 160 MeV/nucleon was "obvious."
- In $^{86}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta at 160 MeV/nucleon, the peak production cross sections for Si isotopes were at $^{28-30}$Si; for P isotopes, the peaks were at $^{31-33}$P.
- $^{42}$Si and $^{46}$P are noted as unknown nuclei not yet synthesized experimentally.
- For $^{78}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta at 160 MeV/nucleon, $^{41}$Si was not produced, and $^{44-46}$P were not found.

## Context

This work extends the application of the IQMD+GEMINI framework to study fragment distributions in asymmetric heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies (80-160 MeV/nucleon), building on prior studies of multifragmentation and rare isotope production. The results are compared with experimental data and EPAX calculations for Fe isotopes in the $^{86}$Kr+$^{181}$Ta reaction at 64 MeV/nucleon, where the IQMD+GEMINI simulation showed good agreement while EPAX underestimated the data. The findings provide guidance for selecting reaction systems and incident energies to produce unknown nuclides near the drip lines, particularly for neutron-rich isotopes relevant to nucleosynthesis studies.
