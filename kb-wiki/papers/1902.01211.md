# A next-generation LHC heavy-ion experiment

> The present document discusses plans for a compact, next-generation multi-purpose detector at the LHC as a follow-up to the present ALICE experiment. The aim is to build a nearly massless barrel detector consisting of truly cylindrical layers based on curved wafer-scale ultra-thin silicon sensors with MAPS technology, featuring an unprecedented low material budget of 0.05% X$_0$ per layer, with the innermost layers possibly positioned inside the beam pipe. In addition to superior tracking and vertexing capabilities over a wide momentum range down to a few tens of MeV/$c$, the detector will provide particle identification via time-of-flight determination with about 20~ps resolution. In addition, electron and photon identification will be performed in a separate shower detector. The proposed detector is conceived for studies of pp, pA and AA collisions at luminosities a factor of 20 to 50 times higher than possible with the upgraded ALICE detector, enabling a rich physics program ranging from measurements with electromagnetic probes at ultra-low transverse momenta to precision physics in the charm and beauty sector.

# A next-generation LHC heavy-ion experiment

## Key claim
The document proposes a compact, next-generation multi-purpose detector for the LHC as a follow-up to ALICE, based on curved wafer-scale ultra-thin silicon MAPS sensors with a material budget of 0.05% X0 per layer. The authors express interest in such an experiment but present it as a conceptual design and physics case, not a finalized project, noting that the detector could be installed during LHC Long Shutdown 4 at Interaction Point 2 where ALICE is currently installed.

## Method
The authors describe a detector concept consisting of a central barrel and two end-caps covering |eta| < 4, embedded in a 0.5 T solenoid magnet (with the option of 1 T or larger). The barrel contains an Inner Tracker with 3 layers inside the beam pipe, an Outer Tracker with 7 layers, a Time-Of-Flight detector using CMOS MAPS or LGAD technology with approximately 20 ps resolution, and an electromagnetic Shower Pixel Detector. Tracking performance was studied using a fast Monte Carlo tool accounting for multiple scattering, detector occupancy, and deterministic energy loss.

## Key numbers
- Material budget of 0.05% X0 per layer for the inner tracker layers, with innermost layer at 18 mm radial distance from the beam line
- Time-of-flight resolution of about 20 ps for particle identification
- Luminosity capability a factor of 20 to 50 times higher than possible with the upgraded ALICE detector
- Impact parameter resolution below 10 um for pT approximately 1 GeV/c and below 100 um down to pT approximately 0.1 GeV/c
- Relative momentum resolution of approximately 2% at pT approximately 30 GeV/c
- Detector radial and longitudinal dimensions of 1.2 m and 4 m respectively
- Pixel sensor with approximately 10^6 pixels per square centimeter, each measuring about 10x10 um, with position resolution better than 3 um
- Outer tracker pixel size of about 30x30 um with spatial resolution of about 5 um and material budget of about 0.5% X0 per layer
- Outer tracker material budget of about 0.5% X0 per layer
- Radiation level at the time-of-flight system location of 10^12 1 MeV n_eq/cm^2

## Context
This document extends the ALICE collaboration's work on the Inner Tracking System upgrade (ITS2) and the proposed ITS3 upgrade with three truly cylindrical layers, as described in prior ALICE upgrade documents. The proposed detector would enable measurements of electromagnetic probes at ultra-low transverse momenta and precision heavy flavor physics that are currently beyond reach, including the detection of Multiply Heavy Flavoured baryons such as Xi_cc and Omega_ccc, for which enhancements of factors 10^2 to 10^3 are predicted in AA relative to pp collisions by calculations from Becattini (2005), Cho (2017), Zhao (2016), He (2014), and Andronic. The experiment would also allow tests of chiral symmetry restoration via precision measurement of the thermal dilepton continuum in the rho-a1 sector, and measurements of soft photon production down to 50-100 MeV/c using the conversion method pioneered by ALICE.
