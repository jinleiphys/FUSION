# Nuclear Giant Resonances and Linear Response

> We search for nonlinear effects in nuclear giant resonances (GRs), in particular the isovector dipole and the isoscalar quadrupole modes. To that end, we employ a spectral analysis of time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) dynamics using Skyrme forces. Based on TDHF calculations over a wide range of excitation amplitudes, we explore the collectivity and degree of harmonic motion in these modes. Both GR modes turn out to be highly harmonic in heavy nuclei from A=100 on. There is no trace of a transition to irregular motion and multiple resonances are predicted. Slight anharmonicities are seen for light nuclei, particularly for $^{16}$O. These are mainly caused by the spin-orbit splitting.

## Key claim

The authors find that isovector dipole and isoscalar quadrupole giant resonances are highly harmonic in heavy nuclei from A=100 onward, with no transition to irregular motion, but slight anharmonicities appear in light nuclei (particularly 16O) mainly due to spin-orbit splitting.

## Method

The authors performed time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations using the Skyrme parametrization SLy6 on a three-dimensional Cartesian grid with no symmetry restrictions. They applied instantaneous boost excitations of varying amplitude to the ground state wavefunctions of doubly magic nuclei from 16O to 208Pb, recorded the multipole moment expectation values over time, and Fourier-transformed the signals into frequency domain spectra using a folding width of 1.6 MeV to suppress fine structure.

## Key numbers

- For 16O, the actual resonance peak is 0.6 MeV below the RPA frequency; for 40Ca, the shift is 0.25 MeV below.
- The spectral fragmentation width varies from 4 MeV in 16O down to 2 MeV for 208Pb.
- The folding width used for spectral filtering was 1.6 MeV for all nuclei.
- The effective time window for filtering was about 250 fm/c.
- The numerical grid was 24x24x24 with spacing of 1 fm.
- The harmonic regime for dipole resonances holds for nuclei with A >= 100.

## Context

This work extends prior TDHF spectral analysis techniques (Yabana 1996, Calvayrac 1997, Maruhn 2005) into the nonlinear regime to test the small-amplitude assumption underlying Random-Phase-Approximation (RPA) descriptions of giant resonances. It contrasts with earlier findings that the isovector monopole mode is closer to the chaotic regime (Vretenar 1999) and that low-energy quadrupole modes quickly turn chaotic (Umezawa 1985). The results confirm the existence of double resonances in heavy nuclei as observed experimentally (Bortignon 1996, Aumann 1998) and support the fluid-dynamical findings of Bernard and collaborators (1997).
