# Path Integral Quantum Monte Carlo Method for Light Nuclei

> I describe the first continuous space nuclear path integral quantum Monte Carlo method, and calculate the ground state properties of light nuclei including Deuteron, Triton, Helium-3 and Helium-4, using both local chiral interaction up to next-to-next-to-leading-order and the Argonne $v_6'$ interaction. Compared with diffusion based quantum Monte Carlo methods such as Green's function Monte Carlo and auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo, path integral quantum Monte Carlo has the advantage that it can directly calculate the expectation value of operators without tradeoff, whether they commute with the Hamiltonian or not. For operators that commute with the Hamiltonian, e.g., the Hamiltonian itself, the path integral quantum Monte Carlo light-nuclei results agree with Green's function Monte Carlo and auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo results. For other operator expectations which are important to understand nuclear measurements but do not commute with the Hamiltonian and therefore cannot be accurately calculated by diffusion based quantum Monte Carlo methods without tradeoff, the path integral quantum Monte Carlo method gives reliable results. I show root-mean-square radii, one-particle number density distributions, and Euclidean response functions for single-nucleon couplings. I also systematically describe all the sampling algorithms used in this work, the strategies to make the computation efficient, the error estimations, and the details of the implementation of the code to perform calculations. This work can serve as a benchmark test for future calculations of larger nuclei or finite temperature nuclear matter using path integral quantum Monte Carlo.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first continuous-space nuclear path integral quantum Monte Carlo (PIMC) method capable of calculating ground-state properties of light nuclei (deuteron, triton, helium-3, helium-4) using realistic interactions, with the key advantage over diffusion-based methods (GFMC, AFDMC) that it can directly compute expectation values of operators that do not commute with the Hamiltonian without tradeoff, provided the total imaginary time is sufficiently large to project out the ground state.

## Method

The method breaks the total imaginary time \(\tau\) into \(N\) slices of step \(\Delta\tau\), inserting complete sets of spatial configurations (beads) at each slice, and approximates the short-time propagator \(e^{-H\Delta\tau}\) using a Trotter breakup \(e^{-V\Delta\tau/2} e^{-T\Delta\tau} e^{-V\Delta\tau/2}\). The ground-state expectation value of an operator \(\hat{O}\) is computed as \(\langle \hat{O} \rangle = \text{Re}\langle \Psi_T| e^{-H\tau_1} \hat{O} e^{-H\tau_2} |\Psi_T\rangle / \text{Re}\langle \Psi_T| e^{-H\tau} |\Psi_T\rangle\), where \(\Psi_T\) is a trial wave function and \(\tau = \tau_1 + \tau_2\). The calculation uses both local chiral interactions up to next-to-next-to-leading order (N\(^2\)LO) and the Argonne \(v_6'\) interaction, with multi-level Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling to efficiently update the path configurations.

## Key numbers

- Ground-state energies for deuteron, triton, helium-3, and helium-4 calculated with both chiral N\(^2\)LO and Argonne \(v_6'\) interactions; PIMC results for operators commuting with the Hamiltonian (e.g., energy) agree with GFMC and AFDMC results.
- Root-mean-square radii, one-particle number density distributions, and Euclidean response functions for single-nucleon couplings are reported for these light nuclei.
- The computational cost of a PIMC walker is estimated to be about \(N\) times more than a GFMC walker, where \(N\) is the number of imaginary-time slices, and overall PIMC requires more than twice the computation time of GFMC for equally uncorrelated walkers.
- GFMC for \(^{12}\)C costs about 130 million core hours on the MIRA supercomputer (Argonne National Laboratory), providing context for the computational expense of PIMC.

## Context

This work extends the path integral Monte Carlo method, previously applied to condensed helium atoms (Ceperley, 1995) and lattice nuclei (Lee, 2009, 2012), to continuous-space nuclear calculations with realistic potentials such as Argonne and chiral interactions. It contrasts with diffusion-based methods like Green's function Monte Carlo (Carlson, 1987) and auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo (Schmidt and Fantoni, 1999), which cannot accurately calculate operators that do not commute with the Hamiltonian without tradeoff. The method enables future benchmark calculations of larger nuclei or finite-temperature nuclear matter using PIMC.
