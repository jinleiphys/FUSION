# A Model for Phase Transition based on Statistical Disassembly of Nuclei at Intermediate Energies

> Consider a model of particles (nucleons) which has a two-body interaction which leads to bound composites with saturation properties. These properties are : all composites have the same density and the ground state energies of composites with k nucleons are given by -kW+σk^{2/3} where W and σare positive constants. W represents a volume term and σa surface tension term. These values are taken from nuclear physics. We show that in the large N limit where N is the number of particles such an assembly in a large enclosure at finite temperature shows properties of liquid-gas phase transition. We do not use the two-body interaction but the gross properties of the composites only. We show that (a) the p-ρisotherms show a region where pressure does not change as $ρ$ changes just as in Maxwell construction of a Van der Waals gas, (b) in this region the chemical potential does not change and (c) the model obeys the celebrated Clausius-Clapeyron relations. A scaling law for the yields of composites emerges. For a finite number of particles N (upto some thousands) the problem can be easily solved on a computer. This allows us to study finite particle number effects which modify phase transition effects. The model is calculationally simple. Monte-Carlo simulations are not needed.

# A Model for Phase Transition based on Statistical Disassembly of Nuclei at Intermediate Energies

## Key claim

The authors show that a model of nucleons forming bound composites with saturation properties (constant density, volume energy -kW and surface tension σk²/³) exhibits a first-order liquid-gas phase transition in the thermodynamic limit (large N), but only when the maximum allowed cluster size k_max is sufficiently large (at least 2000); for small systems with k_max around 200, the liquid-gas coexistence behavior disappears.

## Method

The model treats an assembly of N identical nucleons in a volume V at temperature T, where nucleons can form bound composites (monomers, dimers, trimers, etc.) with ground state energies given by -kW + σk²/³, using nuclear physics values W=16 MeV and σ=18 MeV. The grand canonical partition function is constructed from the translational and intrinsic partition functions of each composite species, with chemical equilibrium enforced so that a k-nucleon composite has chemical potential kμ. For a given temperature and density, the chemical potential μ is solved from the density sum rule, and pressure is computed as p = T Σ n_k / V. The model is also solved canonically for finite N using a recursion relation for the partition function Q_N.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy parameters: W = 16 MeV (volume term), σ₀ = 18 MeV (surface tension at T=0)
- Surface tension temperature dependence: σ(T) = σ₀[(T_c² - T²)/(T_c² + T²)]^(5/4) with T_c = 18 MeV
- Excitation energy parameter: ε₀ ≈ 16 MeV (Fermi-gas formula)
- Normal nuclear density: ρ₀ ≈ 0.16 fm⁻³
- In the coexistence region at T=7 MeV and ρ/ρ₀=0.3, the extrapolated infinite-k_max limit gives μ = -18.504 MeV and p = 0.0294 MeV fm⁻³
- Latent heat per particle: 12.66 MeV at T=6 MeV, 11.55 MeV at T=7.5 MeV
- Clausius-Clapeyron relation verified: at T=7 MeV, dp/dT = 0.0535 fm⁻³ from Fig. 1 vs. 0.0530 fm⁻³ from formula (12); at T=6 MeV, 0.0129 fm⁻³ vs. 0.0108 fm⁻³
- Canonical model solved for N up to 3000 in seconds via recursion relation

## Context

This work extends the statistical multifragmentation approach used in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies (50-100 MeV per nucleon), where a thermalized system expands and disassembles into composites at a freeze-out volume. The model simplifies earlier realistic treatments by considering only one particle type and neglecting Coulomb interactions, allowing clear demonstration of phase transition properties. It builds on the same underlying physics as the Copenhagen Statistical Multifragmentation Model (Bondorf 1995), the microcanonical models of Gross (1997) and Randrup and Koonin (1987), and earlier work by Das Gupta and Mekjian (1998) on finite nuclei. The results are similar to but distinct from the grand canonical approach of Bugaev, Gorenstein, Mishustin, and Greiner (2000), who identified a critical point at T_c=18 MeV and ρ/ρ₀=1. The model enables study of finite particle number effects on phase transition observables, which is relevant for interpreting experimental data from small nuclear systems.
