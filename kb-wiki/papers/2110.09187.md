# Strange quark matter and proto-strange stars in an equivaparticle model

> The properties of strange quark matter and the structures of (proto-)strange stars are studied within the framework of an equivparticle model, where a new quark mass scaling and self-consistent thermodynamic treatment are adopted. Our results show that the perturbative interaction has a strong impact on the properties of strange quark matter. It is found that the energy per baryon increases with temperature, while the free energy decreases and eventually becomes negative. At fixed temperatures, the pressure at the minimum free energy per baryon is zero, suggesting that the thermodynamic self-consistency is preserved. Additionally, the sound velocity v in quark matter approaches to the extreme relativistic limit (c/sqrt(3)) as the density increases. By increasing the strengths of confinement parameter D and perturbation parameter C, the tendency for v to approach the extreme relativistic limit at high density is slightly weakened. For (proto-)strange stars, in contrast to the quark mass scalings adopted in previous publications, the new quark mass scaling can accommodate massive proto-strange stars with their maximum mass surpassing twice the solar mass at T = 50 MeV.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a new quark mass scaling, which includes both confinement and first-order perturbative interactions, can accommodate proto-strange stars with maximum masses surpassing twice the solar mass at T = 50 MeV, but only for specific parameter sets where the perturbative strength C is sufficiently large and the confinement parameter D is sufficiently small; the surface density of such massive stars can become very low, potentially signaling a phase transition to nuclear matter.

## Method

The authors adopt an equivparticle model where quark masses depend on baryon number density and temperature through a scaling that includes a confinement term (D/n_b^(1/3)) and a perturbative term (C n_b^(1/3)), with a temperature-dependent factor that ensures masses vanish above the critical temperature. They enforce thermodynamic self-consistency by deriving thermodynamic quantities from the free energy density, imposing charge neutrality and weak equilibrium conditions for strange quark matter, and solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equation to obtain stellar mass-radius relations for proto-strange stars at finite temperature.

## Key numbers

- Current quark masses used: m_u0 = 5 MeV, m_d0 = 10 MeV, m_s0 = 100 MeV.
- Parameter range: 120 MeV ≤ √D ≤ 270 MeV and C ≤ 1.1676; specific sets used include (C, √D in MeV) = (0.7, 129), (0.6, 160), (0.4, 160), (0, 160), (0.4, 140), and (0.4, 129).
- For parameter set (C, √D) = (0.4, 140 MeV) at T = 50 MeV: maximum star mass ≈ 2.10 M⊙, central baryon density at maximum mass = 0.53 fm⁻³, surface density = 0.045 fm⁻³, maximum radius ≈ 15.56 km at central density 0.23 fm⁻³.
- For parameter set (C, √D) = (0, 160 MeV) at T = 50 MeV: maximum star mass ≈ 1.63 M⊙, central baryon density at maximum mass = 1.03 fm⁻³, surface density = 0.126 fm⁻³, maximum radius ≈ 11.5 km at central density 0.5 fm⁻³.
- For parameter set (C, √D) = (0.4, 160 MeV) at T = 50 MeV: maximum star mass ≈ 1.51 M⊙, central baryon density at maximum mass = 0.975 fm⁻³, surface density = 0.103 fm⁻³, maximum radius ≈ 10.98 km at central density 0.41 fm⁻³.
- For parameter set (C, √D) = (0.6, 160 MeV) at T = 50 MeV: maximum star mass ≈ 1.50 M⊙, central baryon density at maximum mass = 0.95 fm⁻³, surface density = 0.093 fm⁻³, maximum radius ≈ 11.12 km at central density 0.38 fm⁻³.
- Critical temperature for hadron-to-QGP transition: T_c = 175 MeV; parameter Λ = 280 MeV in the temperature-dependent mass scaling.

## Context

This work extends the equivparticle model with density-dependent quark masses (Peng 2000, Xia 2014) to finite temperature by incorporating a mass scaling that includes both confinement and first-order perturbative interactions, building on earlier temperature-dependent scalings by Wen (2005) and Lu (2016). The results enable the study of proto-strange stars at high temperatures (up to 50 MeV) and show that the new scaling can produce stars exceeding 2 M⊙, which was not possible with earlier mass scalings that omitted the perturbative term; the low surface densities found for massive stars suggest a need for further investigation of the QCD phase diagram and hybrid star structures.
