# An attractive nucleon-nucleon spin-orbit force from skyrmions with dilatons

> Within the skyrmion approach for the nucleon-nucleon force, difficulties have been experienced in obtaining an isoscalar attractive spin-orbit potential, in parallel to the problems of finding attraction in the isoscalar central potential. We here study the spin-orbit force using a skyrmion with four- and six-derivative stabilizing terms in the lagrangian as well as with the crucial addition of a dilaton. With these features present the spin-orbit force proves to be attractive as does the central potential. In the absence of the dilaton, attraction can also be found for the spin-orbit potential but only at the expense of a greatly over-emphasized term with six derivatives and a continuing absence of attraction in the central potential.

## Key claim

Within the skyrmion approach, an attractive isoscalar nucleon-nucleon spin-orbit potential can be obtained when the lagrangian includes both four- and six-derivative stabilizing terms together with a dilaton field; without the dilaton, attraction in the spin-orbit potential requires an artificially large six-derivative term and still fails to produce attraction in the central potential.

## Method

The authors start from a skyrmion lagrangian containing the usual two-derivative term, a four-derivative Skyrme term (coefficient \(e\)), a six-derivative repulsive term (coefficient \(\epsilon\)), and a dilaton field coupled to enforce scale invariance. They use the product ansatz for the \(B=2\) system and an additive dilaton ansatz, then compute the isoscalar spin-orbit potential by projecting the \(B=2\) energy onto nucleon states and identifying the \(\mathbf{S}\cdot\mathbf{L}\) component. The hedgehog profiles are obtained by solving the equations of motion for the \(B=1\) system, with parameters constrained to give reasonable nucleon and \(\Delta\) masses.

## Key numbers

- With \(F_\pi = 143\) MeV and \(e = 20.0\) (yielding \(M_N = 942\) MeV, \(M_\Delta = 1174\) MeV), both the spin-orbit and central potentials are attractive when the dilaton, \({\cal L}_4\), and \({\cal L}_6\) are all present (Fig. 1).
- With \(F_\pi = 140\) MeV and \(e = 9.0\) (yielding \(M_N = 976\) MeV, \(M_\Delta = 1169\) MeV), both potentials are again attractive with the same ingredients (Fig. 2).
- Without the dilaton, using \(F_\pi = 112\) MeV and \(e = 4.84\) (yielding \(M_N = 978\) MeV, \(M_\Delta = 1106\) MeV), neither the spin-orbit nor the central potential is attractive (Fig. 3).
- Without the dilaton and using only \({\cal L}_6\) (setting \({\cal L}_4 \equiv 0\)) with \(F_\pi = 140\) MeV (yielding \(M_N = 942\) MeV, \(M_\Delta = 1251\) MeV), the spin-orbit potential becomes attractive but the central potential does not (Fig. 4).
- For values of the Skyrme parameter \(e\) less than about 9, the spin-orbit potential does not become attractive in the range \(1 < R < 2\) fm even with the dilaton and \({\cal L}_6\) present.

## Context

This work extends earlier skyrmion studies of the nucleon-nucleon force, which had struggled to obtain an attractive isoscalar spin-orbit potential using the simple product ansatz (Riska and Dannbom, 1988; Otofuji et al., 1988). It builds on the suggestion by Riska and Schwesinger (1989) that a six-derivative term might produce spin-orbit attraction, and on prior work showing that a dilaton field can generate central attraction (Yabu, Schwesinger, and Holzwarth, 1989; Tsushima and Riska, 1993). The present results show that combining the dilaton with both four- and six-derivative terms can simultaneously yield attractive central and spin-orbit potentials, though the required Skyrme parameter \(e \ge 9\) is somewhat larger than the range \(e \approx 5 \pm 2\) suggested by \(\pi\pi\) scattering (Donoghue, Golowich, and Holstein, 1984).
