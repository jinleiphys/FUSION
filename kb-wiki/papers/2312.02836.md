# A Framework for Exploring Nuclear Physics Sensitivity in Numerical Simulations

> We describe the AMReX-Astrophysics framework for exploring the sensitivity of astrophysical simulations to the details of a nuclear reaction network, including the number of nuclei, choice of reaction rates, and approximations used. This is explored by modeling a simple detonation with the Castro simulation code. The entire simulation methodology is open-source and GPU-enabled.

## Key claim

The paper presents the AMReX-Astrophysics framework for systematically testing how the choice of nuclear reaction network (number of nuclei, specific reaction rates, and approximations) affects astrophysical simulation outcomes, demonstrated through a 1D helium detonation model rather than a full multidimensional astrophysical scenario.

## Method

The authors used the pynucastro library to generate custom reaction networks of varying complexity, then coupled these networks through the Microphysics library with the Castro compressible hydrodynamics code to simulate a 1D detonation wave. They compared three networks: a 19-isotope aprox19 network, a 22-isotope subch_simple network, and a 36-isotope He-C-Fe network that included tabulated weak rates for iron-peak nuclei. The simulations used a uniform density of 1.1e9 g/cm^3 with a perturbed temperature region and 99% He + 1% N composition, run on a domain of 4.0e7 cm with adaptive mesh refinement reaching 1024 zones.

## Key numbers

- Peak temperature reached approximately 3e9 K in the He-C-Fe network simulation
- Peak specific nuclear energy generation rate reached approximately 1e21 erg/g/s in the He-C-Fe network
- All three networks reached a shock speed of approximately 1e4 km/s after about 15 ms
- He-C-Fe and subch_simple showed roughly double the shock speed compared to aprox19 for times less than 15 ms
- He-C-Fe required nearly 4 times the CPU-hours compared to subch_simple, which has about 1/3 of the total number of rates
- The detonation simulation domain extended to 4.0e7 cm with 256 coarse grid zones and two refinement levels reaching 1024 zones (resolution approximately 0.4 km)
- Initial density was 1.1e9 g/cm^3 with perturbed temperature of 1.1e9 K over 0 to 1.2e7 cm and background temperature of 1.75e8 K
- Composition was 99% He-4 and 1% N-14

## Context

This work extends the sensitivity study methodology previously applied to X-ray bursts by Chen (2023) and builds on the sub-Chandrasekhar double-detonation model simulations by Zingale (2023). The framework enables systematic identification of which reaction rates and network sizes are necessary for accurate nucleosynthesis studies in thermonuclear explosions, particularly for determining the correct mass abundance of Ni-56 (whose decay chain produces the observed Type Ia supernova light curve) versus the Ni-58 predominantly produced by the larger He-C-Fe network.
