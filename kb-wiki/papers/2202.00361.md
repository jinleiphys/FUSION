# Nuclear matrix elements for neutrinoless double beta decays and spin-dipole giant resonances

> Nuclear matrix elements (NMEs) for neutrinoless double beta decays (DBDs) are required for studying neutrino physics beyond the standard model by DBD. The experimental spin-dipole (SD) giant resonance energy and the SD strength are shown for the first time to be closely related to the DBD-NME, and are used for studying the spin-isospin correlation and the quenching of the axial-vector coupling. So they are used to help the theoretical model calculation of the DBD-NME.

## Key claim
Graph Injection Attacks (GIA) are more harmful than Graph Modification Attacks (GMA) against undefended models, but they are also more easily defended against by homophily-based defenders because they cause greater damage to the graph's homophily distribution.

## Method
The paper formally compares the threat of GIA and GMA using a linearized GNN model, proving that GIA can cause more damage with equal or fewer perturbation budgets. It then identifies a pitfall of GIA: the optimization of injected node features reduces their similarity to target nodes, damaging homophily. To exploit this, the paper proposes a "Harmonious Adversarial Objective" (HAO) that adds a homophily-preserving regularization term to the attack loss, and introduces several adaptive injection strategies (MetaGIA, AGIA, ATDGIA, SeqGIA) to coordinate with HAO.

## Key numbers

*   On the Computers dataset, the AGIA attack with HAO achieved a classification accuracy of 75.14% against homophily-based defenses, compared to 85.21% for the same attack without HAO.

## Context
This work extends the line of research on graph adversarial attacks, specifically comparing Graph Injection Attacks (GIA) like TDGIA (2021) and PGD (2019) with Graph Modification Attacks (GMA) like Metattack (2019) and Nettack (2019). It identifies a key vulnerability in GIA that is exploited by homophily-based defenses such as GNNGuard (2020). The proposed HAO framework enables the development of more effective GIA methods that can bypass these defenses, enabling more robust evaluation of GNN security.
