# Deeply inelastic scattering structure functions on a hybrid quantum computer

> We outline a strategy to compute deeply inelastic scattering structure functions using a hybrid quantum computer. Our approach takes advantage of the representation of the fermion determinant in the QCD path integral as a quantum mechanical path integral over 0+1-dimensional fermionic and bosonic worldlines. The proper time evolution of these worldlines can be determined on a quantum computer. While extremely challenging in general, the problem simplifies in the Regge limit of QCD, where the interaction of the worldlines with gauge fields is strongly localized in proper time and the corresponding quantum circuits can be written down. As a first application, we employ the Color Glass Condensate effective theory to construct the quantum algorithm for a simple dipole model of the $F_2$ structure function. We outline further how this computation scales up in complexity and extends in scope to other real-time correlation functions.

## Key claim

The authors propose a strategy to compute deeply inelastic scattering (DIS) structure functions on a hybrid quantum computer, but only for the Regge limit of QCD (small Bjorken x), where the interaction of fermionic worldlines with gauge fields is strongly localized in proper time and the corresponding quantum circuits can be written down; the general problem remains extremely challenging.

## Method

The authors express the fermion determinant in the QCD path integral as a quantum mechanical path integral over 0+1-dimensional fermionic and bosonic worldlines, then digitize the proper time evolution of these worldlines on a quantum computer. They apply the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) effective field theory to construct a quantum algorithm for a simple dipole model of the F2 structure function, using a shock wave background field to represent the target hadron in the Regge limit. The spinor trace in the worldline effective action is evaluated on a quantum computer via a circuit that uses an auxiliary control qubit and controlled unitary operations to measure the trace of the relevant operators.

## Key numbers

* The structure function F2 is computed for the Regge limit where Bjorken x satisfies x_Bj ≈ Q^2/s → 0, with Q^2 fixed and s the squared center-of-mass energy.
* The dipole-hadron cross-section is given by D_ρ(x_⊥) = (1/N_c) tr_c [1 - U_ρ(x_⊥) U_ρ^†(0_⊥)], where N_c = 3 for SU(3) color.
* The photon wave functions involve MacDonald functions K_0 and K_1 with argument Q̄_f x_⊥, where Q̄_f^2 = z(1-z)Q^2 + m_f^2.
* The quantum circuit requires 2 qubits for the fermionic degrees of freedom, with the initial density matrix for the n-qubit system being ρ_n = I_n / 2^n.

## Context

This work extends the worldline formalism of Strassler (1992) and subsequent authors to quantum computing, and applies it to the Color Glass Condensate effective theory of McLerran, Venugopalan, and collaborators (1993-1994) for DIS in the Regge limit. The approach aims to overcome limitations of classical lattice QCD computations, which are restricted to Euclidean spacetime and low moments of structure functions via the operator product expansion, as reviewed by Lin (2017) and Detmold (2019). The paper enables future work on quantum computation of real-time correlation functions and scattering amplitudes that are classically challenging, with a follow-up paper planned for detailed circuit implementation on quantum hardware.
