# Principal Component Analysis of Azimuthal Flow in Intermediate-Energy Heavy-Ion Reactions

> Principal Component Analysis (PCA) via Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of large datasets is an adaptive exploratory method to uncover natural patterns underlying the data. Several recent applications of the PCA-SVD to event-by-event single-particle azimuthal angle distribution matrices in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC-LHC energies indicate that the sine and cosine functions chosen {\it a priori} in the traditional Fourier analysis are naturally the most optimal basis for azimuthal flow studies according to the data itself. We perform PCA-SVD analyses of mid-central Au+Au collisions at $E_{\rm beam}/A$=1.23 GeV simulated using an isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (IBUU) transport model to address the following two questions: (1) if the principal components of the covariance matrix of nucleon azimuthal angle distributions in heavy-ion reactions around 1 GeV/nucleon are naturally sine and/or cosine functions and (2) what if any advantages the PCA-SVD may have over the traditional flow analysis using the Fourier expansion for studying the EOS of dense nuclear matter. We find that (1) in none of our analyses the principal components come out naturally as sine and/or cosine functions, (2) while both the eigenvectors and eigenvalues of the covariance matrix are appreciably EOS dependent, the PCA-SVD has no apparent advantage over the traditional Fourier analysis for studying the EOS of dense nuclear matter using the azimuthal collective flow in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The authors find that in intermediate-energy Au+Au collisions at 1.23 GeV/nucleon simulated with the IBUU transport model, the principal components of the covariance matrix of nucleon azimuthal angle distributions do not naturally emerge as sine and/or cosine functions, and the PCA-SVD approach has no apparent advantage over the traditional Fourier analysis for studying the equation of state of dense nuclear matter using azimuthal collective flow.

## Method

The authors performed PCA via Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) on event-by-event matrices of normalized free nucleon azimuthal angle distributions from 0.5 million to 1.5 million simulated mid-central Au+Au collisions at E_beam/A = 1.23 GeV using an isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (IBUU) transport model with a soft (K=230 MeV) and a stiff (K=380 MeV) equation of state. They applied three different data pre-processing schemes, non-centered (normalized raw data), column-centered, and standardized data matrices, and compared the resulting PC loadings, singular values, and event-averaged PC coefficients with the traditional Fourier flow coefficients.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy: E_beam/A = 1.23 GeV for Au+Au collisions
- Impact parameters used: b = 6 fm, b = 9 fm, and mixed events with b between 6 fm and 9 fm
- Number of events: 0.5 million events for fixed impact parameter (b=6 fm or b=9 fm) and 1.5 million mixed events
- Number of azimuthal angle bins: 10 bins on each side of a central bin at pi/2, with bin size pi/21
- Incompressibility values: K = 230 MeV (soft EOS) and K = 380 MeV (stiff EOS)
- Maximum central density reached: about 2.3 rho_0 around t = 13 fm/c
- Standard deviations increased by about 5% when reducing test particles from 500 to 50 per nucleon

## Context

This work extends the PCA-SVD methodology previously applied to ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC-LHC energies by Liu (2019) and Alt (2019), where principal components were found to naturally resemble sine and cosine functions, to the intermediate-energy regime around 1 GeV/nucleon. The study uses the IBUU transport model, which has been extensively used for extracting equation of state information from collective flow data in the traditional Fourier analysis approach. The results indicate that the PCA-SVD method, while useful at ultra-relativistic energies, does not provide advantages over standard Fourier analysis for studying the nuclear equation of state at intermediate energies, and the authors emphasize that their conclusions are specific to the BUU model framework which may not contain sufficient dynamical fluctuations.
