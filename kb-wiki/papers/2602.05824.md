# Mesoscopic chemical potentials across the (hyper)nuclear landscape

> Finite nuclei constrain the dense-matter equation of state (EOS), yet they are self-bound quantum droplets far from the thermodynamic limit. Motivated by an analogy to quantum dots, we show that the nuclear chart nevertheless defines a mesoscopic regime in which mesoscopic chemical-potential analogs $\{μ_B,μ_Q,μ_S\}$ can be extracted directly from nuclear and hypernuclear binding energies after consistent Coulomb subtraction. These are discrete finite-difference response functions -- local slopes of the strong-interaction energy landscape -- not equilibrium grand-canonical chemical potentials. The nuclear chart itself supplies an "ensemble of nearby droplets": finite differences across neighboring nuclei suppress shell- and pairing-scale oscillations while retaining the smooth bulk trend, producing robust slopes without a macroscopic limit. Thus, the data provide empirical local derivatives that any strangeness-enabled EOS must reproduce near saturation. Mapping the measured (hyper)nuclear landscape at $T\simeq 0$, we find smooth, numerically stable responses, including a large, negative strangeness chemical-potential analog, and we identify specific hypernuclear measurements that can directly test and sharpen these EOS constraints.

# Mesoscopic chemical potentials across the (hyper)nuclear landscape

## Key claim

The nuclear chart defines a mesoscopic regime from which chemical-potential analogs {μ_B, μ_Q, μ_S} can be extracted as discrete finite-difference response functions of the strong-interaction energy landscape, not as equilibrium grand-canonical chemical potentials; these provide empirical local derivatives that any strangeness-enabled equation of state must reproduce near saturation, but the extraction relies on the assumption that finite-difference averaging across neighboring nuclei suppresses shell- and pairing-scale oscillations while retaining the smooth bulk trend.

## Method

The author extracts mesoscopic chemical-potential analogs from experimental nuclear and hypernuclear masses (AME2020 tables and hypernuclear databases) and charge radii (Angeli database supplemented by phenomenological fits), after subtracting Coulomb contributions to isolate the strong-force energy. Finite-difference midpoint formulas of order O(h), O(h^2), and O(h^4) are applied across neighboring nuclei on the discrete {A, Z, N_Λ} chart, varying one conserved charge (baryon number B, electric charge Q, strangeness S) while holding the others fixed, with step size h=1. The resulting chemical potentials are averaged over bins of isospin asymmetry δ_I using A^{1/3}-weighted means, with statistical errors from jackknife resampling and systematic errors from experimental uncertainties propagated in the uncorrelated approximation.

## Key numbers

- μ_B spans approximately 920 to 940 MeV across isospin asymmetries up to |δ_I| ≲ 0.4
- μ_Q spans approximately +10 to -15 MeV across the same isospin range
- μ_S spans approximately -185 to -165 MeV, remaining large and negative even at isospin symmetry
- For isospin-symmetric matter, ⟨μ_B⟩(0) ≈ 927 MeV, close to infinite nuclear matter predictions of μ_B^{V=∞} ≈ 922 MeV
- At δ_I = 0, μ_Q ≈ -3 MeV, consistent with the systematic residual of (p - sT)/n_B ≈ -3 MeV from the self-consistency diagnostic
- The self-consistency check (p/n_B - sT/n_B) yields values of approximately -3 MeV, with error bars often consistent with zero, 1-2 orders of magnitude smaller than the extracted chemical potentials

## Context

This work extends the analogy between finite nuclei and quantum dots (Tarucha, 2001) to define effective chemical potentials through energy differences across sequences of finite systems rather than through a thermodynamic limit. It provides a direct mapping between nuclear/astrophysical variables (isospin asymmetry δ_I, charge fraction Y_Q, strangeness fraction Y_S) and mesoscopic chemical-potential analogs, enabling model-agnostic calibration targets for hyperonic and strange-matter equations of state. The results enable testing of strangeness-enabled EOS models against empirical mesoscopic bands, and identify specific hypernuclear measurements (e.g., improved binding energies of ⁹_ΛBe or ⁸_ΛLi, kaon-bound nuclei, proton-rich double-hypernuclei) that could sharpen these constraints and connect nuclear structure experiments to neutron-star EOS modeling.
