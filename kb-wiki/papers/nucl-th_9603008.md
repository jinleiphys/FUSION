# Relativistic bound-state equations in three dimensions

> Firstly, a systematic procedure is derived for obtaining three-dimensional bound-state equations from four-dimensional ones. Unlike ``quasi-potential approaches'' this procedure does not involve the use of delta-function constraints on the relative four-momentum. In the absence of negative-energy states, the kernels of the three-dimensional equations derived by this technique may be represented as sums of time-ordered perturbation theory diagrams. Consequently, such equations have two major advantages over quasi-potential equations: they may easily be written down in any Lorentz frame, and they include the meson-retardation effects present in the original four-dimensional equation. Secondly, a simple four-dimensional equation with the correct one-body limit is obtained by a reorganization of the generalized ladder Bethe-Salpeter kernel. Thirdly, our approach to deriving three-dimensional equations is applied to this four-dimensional equation, thus yielding a retarded interaction for use in the three-dimensional bound-state equation of Wallace and Mandelzweig. The resulting three-dimensional equation has the correct one-body limit and may be systematically improved upon. The quality of the three-dimensional equation, and our general technique for deriving such equations, is then tested by calculating bound-state properties in a scalar field theory using six different bound-state equations. It is found that equations obtained using the method espoused here approximate the wave functions obtained from their parent four-dimensional equations significantly better than the corresponding quasi-potential equations do.

## Key claim

The paper presents a systematic procedure for deriving three-dimensional bound-state equations from four-dimensional Bethe-Salpeter equations that, unlike quasi-potential approaches, does not use delta-function constraints on the relative four-momentum, thereby avoiding unphysical singularities in boost corrections and allowing systematic improvement; the resulting three-dimensional equations approximate the wave functions from their parent four-dimensional equations "significantly better" than corresponding quasi-potential equations do, but this superiority is demonstrated only in a scalar field theory, not in realistic nuclear physics.

## Method

The authors split the Bethe-Salpeter kernel K into a three-dimensional piece K₁ (independent of the zeroth component of relative four-momentum in the center-of-mass frame) and a remainder K₂, then formally eliminate K₂ to obtain a three-dimensional equation Γ₁ = K₁⟨𝒢⟩Γ₁ where ⟨𝒢⟩ is the integrated equal-time Green's function. They then derive a four-dimensional equation Γ = K⁽²⁾(G₀ + G_C)Γ by approximating the crossed-box graph as an iterate of the ladder kernel, which has the correct one-body limit, and apply their three-dimensional reduction technique to this equation to produce a retarded interaction for the Wallace-Mandelzweig bound-state equation. Numerical tests compare six different bound-state equations in a scalar φ²σ field theory.

## Key numbers

- The time-ordered perturbation theory interaction for second-order exchange is given by K₁⁽²⁾(p',p;E,P) = (g₁g₂/2ω) 𝒩 (1/(E⁺ - ε₁ - ε'₂ - ω) + 1/(E⁺ - ε₂ - ε'₁ - ω)), where 𝒩 = 4m₁m₂ for spin-zero particles and 𝒩 = 1 for spin-half particles.
- The leading-order boost correction to the interaction in the equal-mass case, to second order in P, is K̃₁⁽²⁾(q;P) = K̃₁⁽²⁾(q;0) - (P²/4m²)K̃₁⁽²⁾(q;0) - (1/8m²)P·q P·∇_q K̃₁⁽²⁾(q;0).
- The coupling between ++ and -- states in the Klein potential is suppressed by a factor ω/m in the equal-mass case, which is zero in the static limit.

## Context

This work extends the three-dimensional reduction methods of Klein (1953, 1954) and the quasi-potential approach of Wallace and Mandelzweig (1987, 1988, 1989), providing a delta-function-free procedure that avoids the unphysical singularities that arise in boosted quasi-potential interactions as noted by Devine and Wallace (1994, 1995). The technique enables systematic inclusion of retardation effects and dynamical boost corrections in three-dimensional bound-state equations, with the leading-order boost correction matching that derived by Forest, Pandharipande, and Friar (1995). The resulting formalism can be applied to deuteron calculations and electromagnetic reactions where boosted vertex functions are required, and connects to the two-pion exchange work of Rijken and Stoks (1991, 1995) and Lahiff and Afnan (1996).
