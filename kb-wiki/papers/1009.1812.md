# Virial Expansion of the Nuclear Equation of State

> We study the equation of state (EOS) of nuclear matter as function of density. We expand the energy per particle (E/A) of symmetric infinite nuclear matter in powers of the density to take into account 2,3,. . .,N-body forces. New EOS are proposed by fitting ground state properties of nuclear matter (binding energy, compressibility and pressure) and assuming that at high densities a second order phase transition to the Quark Gluon Plasma (QGP) occurs. The latter phase transition is due to symmetry breaking at high density from nuclear matter (locally color white) to the QGP (globally color white). In the simplest implementation of a second order phase transition we calculate the critical exponent ? by using Landau's theory of phase transition. We find ? = 3. Refining the properties of the EOS near the critical point gives ? = 5 in agreement with experimental results. We also discuss some scenarios for the EOS at finite temperatures.

# Virial Expansion of the Nuclear Equation of State

## Key claim

The authors propose new equations of state for symmetric infinite nuclear matter by expanding the energy per particle in powers of density up to sixth order, assuming a second-order phase transition to the Quark Gluon Plasma (QGP) at high density. The critical exponent delta is found to be 3 in the simplest mean-field implementation, but refining the EOS near the critical point gives delta = 5, which the authors claim agrees with experimental results.

## Method

The energy per particle of symmetric infinite nuclear matter is expanded in a virial series in powers of the reduced density rho/rho_0, with coefficients determined by imposing ground-state conditions (binding energy -15 MeV, zero pressure, compressibility K=225 MeV at normal density rho_0=0.165 fm^-3) plus conditions that the first and second derivatives of pressure vanish at a critical density for a second-order phase transition to QGP at T=0. For the delta=5 case, the fourth derivative of pressure is also set to zero, requiring terms up to O(rho^6) in the expansion. Finite-temperature extensions are made using both classical ideal gas and Fermi gas approximations with a level density parameter.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear density taken as rho_0 = 0.165 fm^-3 (from a range of 0.145-0.17 fm^-3)
- Compressibility K = 225 MeV used for the main EOS fits (range 180-240 MeV cited from literature)
- Binding energy at ground state set to -15 MeV per nucleon
- For the CCSdelta3 EOS (fourth-order expansion): critical reduced density rho_c/rho_0 = 2.9354 for the QGP phase transition
- For the CCSdelta5 EOS (sixth-order expansion): critical reduced density rho_c/rho_0 = 5.2 for the QGP phase transition
- Liquid-gas critical temperature T_c = 18.05 MeV and critical density rho_c = 0.3724 rho_0 for the fourth-order EOS in the Fermi gas approximation
- Liquid-gas critical temperature T_c = 17.932 MeV and critical density rho_c = 0.3717 rho_0 for the sixth-order EOS in the Fermi gas approximation
- Classical approximation gives liquid-gas T_c = 9 MeV and rho_c = 0.3 rho_0
- Coefficients for CCSdelta5: A1 = -137.96 MeV, A2 = 126.25 MeV, A3 = -49.46 MeV, A4 = 9.55 MeV, A5 = -0.92 MeV, A6 = 0.0035 MeV
- Coefficients for CCSdelta3: A1 = -136.89 MeV, A2 = 120.99 MeV, A3 = -41.32 MeV, A4 = 4.72 MeV

## Context

This work extends conventional Skyrme-type parameterizations of the nuclear equation of state (such as the CK200 and CK225 forms with sigma = 7/6 and 4/3 respectively) by adding higher-order virial terms to accommodate a second-order phase transition to QGP at high density. The approach contrasts with first-order phase transition scenarios discussed in the literature (Csernai and others) and with MIT bag model estimates giving a critical density of about five times normal density for a bag constant B^(1/4) = 206 MeV. The authors note that their liquid-gas critical temperature of about 18 MeV differs by nearly a factor of two between classical and quantum treatments, and they argue that experimental analyses using classical approximations (Albergo, Pochodzalla, and others) should be revisited with proper quantum statistics. The work enables microscopic calculations in heavy-ion collisions and comparisons with collective flow data to distinguish between first-order and second-order QGP transitions.
