# Recent Advances in the Theory of Nuclear Forces and its Impact on Microscopic Nuclear Structure

> The theory of nuclear forces has made great progress since the turn of the millenium using the framework of chiral effective field theory (ChEFT). The advantage of this approach, which was originally proposed by Weinberg, is that it has a firm basis in quantum-chromodynamics and allows for quantitative calculations. Moreover, this theory generates two-nucleon forces (2NF) and many-body forces on an equal footing and provides an explanation for the empirically known fact that 2NF >> 3NF >> 4NF. I will present the recent advances in more detail and put them into historical context. In addition, I will also provide a critical evaluation of the progress made including a discussion of the limitations of the ChEFT approach.

# Recent Advances in the Theory of Nuclear Forces and its Impact on Microscopic Nuclear Structure

## Key claim

Chiral effective field theory (ChEFT) provides a firm basis in quantum chromodynamics for nuclear forces and generates two-nucleon forces (2NF) and many-body forces on an equal footing, but the approach is limited to center-of-mass momenta below approximately 2.2 fm^-1 (equivalent to nuclear matter densities up to about 4 times saturation density), beyond which chiral potentials do not make reasonable predictions.

## Method

The author reviews the historical development of nuclear force theory from Yukawa's meson theory through one-boson-exchange models and quark models to chiral effective field theory (ChEFT) as proposed by Weinberg. The method involves constructing nucleon-nucleon (NN) potentials using chiral perturbation theory (chiPT) at increasing orders (LO, NLO, NNLO, N^3LO), where the NN amplitude is determined by contact terms and pion-exchange diagrams, and applying these potentials in a scattering equation (Lippmann-Schwinger or Schrodinger equation) to obtain the NN amplitude. The potentials are multiplied by a regulator function exp[-(p/Lambda)^(2n) - (p'/Lambda)^(2n)] with cutoff Lambda much less than the chiral symmetry breaking scale of approximately 1 GeV.

## Key numbers

- At NLO, chi^2/datum for fitting the world np data below 290 MeV ranges from 67 to 105 (2402 data points)
- At NNLO, chi^2/datum for the same np data improves to between 12 and 27
- At N^3LO, the Idaho potential achieves chi^2/datum = 1.1 for np data below 290 MeV, comparable to the Argonne V18 potential at chi^2/datum = 1.04
- For pp data below 290 MeV (2057 data points), the best Idaho N^3LO potential achieves chi^2/datum = 1.5, while the Argonne V18 achieves 1.4
- The Bochum/Juelich N^3LO potentials produce chi^2/datum ranging from 1.7 to 7.9 for np data and 2.9 to 22.3 for pp data
- The chiral symmetry breaking scale Lambda_chi is approximately 1 GeV, and typical cutoff parameters Lambda are approximately 500 MeV
- Chiral potentials are reliable only for center-of-mass momenta p, p' less than or approximately equal to 2.2 fm^-1, corresponding to nuclear matter density approximately 4 times saturation density (rho_0)

## Context

This review extends the line of work initiated by Weinberg (1990) on applying chiral effective field theory to nuclear forces, following earlier meson-theoretic approaches such as the Paris potential (Lacombe et al., 1980) and Bonn potentials (Machleidt, 1989), as well as QCD-inspired quark models (Myhrer and Wroldsen, 1988). The N^3LO chiral NN potentials developed by Entem and Machleidt (2003) and by Epelbaum et al. (2005) now achieve precision comparable to high-precision phenomenological potentials like Argonne V18 (Wiringa et al., 1995) and CD-Bonn (Machleidt, 2001), enabling applications in no-core shell model, coupled cluster formalism, and unitary-model-operator approach for microscopic nuclear structure. The most important outstanding issue is the three-nucleon force at N^3LO, which is under construction and may help resolve the Ay puzzle in nucleon-deuteron scattering that is not resolved by the three-nucleon force at NNLO.
