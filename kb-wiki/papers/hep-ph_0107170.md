# Inconsistencies in Models for RHIC and LHC

> The interpretation of experimental results at RHIC and in the future also at LHC requires very reliable and realistic models. Considerable effort has been devoted to the development of such models during the past decade, many of them being heavily used in order to analyze data. It is the purpose of this paper to point out serious inconsistencies in the above-mentioned approaches. We will demonstrate that requiring theoretical self-consistency reduces the freedom in modeling high energy nuclear scattering enormously. We will introduce a fully self-consistent formulation of the multiple-scattering scheme in the framework of a Gribov-Regge type effective theory. In addition, we develop new computational techniques which allow for the first time a satisfactory solution of the problem in the sense that calculations of observable quantities can be done strictly within a self-consistent formalism.

## Key claim

The paper argues that existing Gribov-Regge and eikonalized parton models for high-energy nuclear collisions contain serious internal inconsistencies, primarily because they treat energy conservation differently in cross-section calculations versus particle production and treat multiple Pomerons as non-identical. The authors claim that requiring full theoretical self-consistency drastically reduces the freedom in modeling and that their new "Parton-based Gribov-Regge Theory" provides the first fully self-consistent formulation.

## Method

The authors develop a multiple-scattering formalism within a Gribov-Regge type effective theory where the elastic nucleus-nucleus scattering amplitude is built from purely parallel elementary interactions between partonic constituents of nucleons, with explicit integration over momentum fractions to enforce energy conservation. They define the inelastic cross section as a sum over classes of interference terms (cut diagrams) characterized by the number of inelastic interactions and the momentum fractions of all elementary interactions, and then use Markov chain Monte Carlo techniques with a Metropolis acceptance algorithm to generate configurations according to the resulting high-dimensional probability distribution.

## Key numbers

- The Markov chain requires \( 66.7 \, k_{\mathrm{max}} \) iterations for convergence, where \( k_{\mathrm{max}} \) is an upper limit estimate of the number of nucleon-nucleon interactions.
- Using the approximation \( \Omega_0 \) alone (without the full Markov chain) introduces errors reaching about 100 percent already at the nucleon-nucleon level.
- The soft scale \( Q_0^2 \) is introduced as the boundary below which perturbative calculations are no longer valid, with the squared mass of the unknown soft object estimated as \( Q_0^2 / x \), where \( x \) is the momentum fraction of the first parton relative to the nucleon.

## Context

This work extends the Gribov-Regge approach (Gribov 1968, 1969) and the eikonalized parton model (Sjostrand 1987) by pointing out that these models use inconsistent treatments of energy conservation between cross-section calculations and particle production, and that they treat the first Pomeron differently from subsequent ones despite the cross-section formulas treating all Pomerons as identical. The new formalism enables practical calculations of observable quantities within a self-consistent framework for the first time, using the Glauber-Gribov approach (Glauber 1959, Gribov 1969) for nucleus-nucleus scattering and Markov chain techniques (Hlatchuk 1998) for numerical implementation.
