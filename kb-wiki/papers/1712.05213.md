# Monte-Carlo statistical hadronization in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> A brief introduction to the statistical hadronization approach to particle production in relativistic heavy-ion collisions is given. In the context of fluid dynamics modeling various aspects of hadron emission at the freeze-out are discussed. Practical applications of the presented concepts are presented within the THERMINATOR Monte-Carlo hadron generator.

## Key claim

The paper provides a pedagogical introduction to the statistical hadronization approach in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, emphasizing that while the single-freeze-out scenario with isothermal decoupling is a crude simplification, it yields a surprisingly successful description of experimental data when implemented in the THERMINATOR Monte-Carlo hadron generator.

## Method

The author reviews relativistic perfect fluid dynamics as the framework for describing the evolution of strongly interacting matter, deriving the equations from local conservation laws of energy-momentum and charge currents. The freeze-out process is modeled using the Cooper-Frye formalism, where particles decouple from the fluid on a three-dimensional hypersurface defined by a constant freeze-out temperature, and the resulting hadron spectra are computed via Monte-Carlo sampling within the THERMINATOR generator. The initial conditions for the fluid dynamical evolution are provided by the tilted source model based on the Glauber model, with parameters fitted to reproduce charged hadron multiplicity and rapidity spectra at RHIC.

## Key numbers

- The admixture of binary collisions in the initial energy density profile is controlled by the parameter κ = 0.14, fitted to reproduce the centrality dependence of charged hadron multiplicity.
- For RHIC, the rapidity density profile parameters are Δς = 2.3 and σς = 1.6, deduced from fits to the final rapidity spectrum of charged hadrons.
- The nucleon rapidity is given by y_N = log(2√s_NN/m_N) − ς_shift, with ς_shift = 2 treated as a phenomenological parameter.
- The isothermal freeze-out surfaces shown in figures correspond to temperatures T ∈ {0.4, 0.3, 0.2, 0.1} GeV.

## Context

This work extends the long tradition of statistical hadronization models dating back to Fermi, Landau, and Hagedorn, and specifically builds upon the single-freeze-out model developed by Broniowski et al. (2001-2006) and the tilted source initial state model by Bozek (2010). The paper enables the practical application of these concepts through the THERMINATOR Monte-Carlo generator, allowing for detailed comparison with experimental observables such as directed flow v_1 at RHIC, and opens the possibility of more sophisticated switching procedures at the particlization temperature as discussed by Huovinen (2012).
