# Chiral magnetic effect in a cylindrical domain

> We compute the chiral magnetic effect (CME) in a cylindrical region coaxial with the external magnetic field. As the boundary condition we require vanishing of the radial component of the electric current on the cylinder side wall. We find that when the magnetic length is comparable to or larger than the cylinder radius, the CME is suppressed compared to the corresponding result in an infinite medium. As a result, for a given cylinder radius, the suppression is stronger in weak fields. We argue that the electric current generated by the CME vanishes at the cylinder wall and monotonically increases toward the symmetry axis.

## Key claim

The chiral magnetic effect (CME) in a cylindrical domain coaxial with an external magnetic field is suppressed relative to the infinite-medium result when the magnetic length is comparable to or larger than the cylinder radius; the suppression is stronger for weaker fields at a fixed cylinder radius, and the electric current vanishes at the cylinder wall and increases monotonically toward the symmetry axis.

## Method

The authors solved the Dirac equation for a massless fermion in a constant homogeneous magnetic field inside a cylinder of radius \(R\) with MIT bag boundary conditions requiring the vanishing of the radial component of the electric current on the side wall. They used the transverse polarization basis and computed the thermal expectation value of the electric current along the magnetic field direction via Vilenkin’s statistical-mechanics method, assuming thermal equilibrium at finite axial chemical potential \(\mu_A\). The energy spectrum was obtained by numerically solving the boundary-condition constraint for the principal quantum number \(\lambda\) as a function of the dimensionless parameter \(\rho_R = R^2/(2 l_B^2)\), where \(l_B = \hbar c/\sqrt{|qB|}\) is the magnetic length.

## Key numbers

* The dimensionless parameter controlling finite-size effects is \(\rho_R = R^2/(2 l_B^2)\); when \(\rho_R \sim 1\) the magnetic length is comparable to the cylinder radius and Landau levels are strongly deformed.
* For the lowest-Landau-level-like mode, the principal quantum number \(\lambda_{\rm LLL}\) scales as \(\lambda_{\rm LLL} \approx e^{-2\rho_R} \rho_R^{2m} / \Gamma(m+1/2)^2\) in the small-\(\lambda\) expansion, showing exponential suppression with increasing \(\rho_R\).
* The energy levels are given by \(\beta E_{n,i} = \sqrt{2\lambda_i \beta^2 |qB|}\) (with \(p_z=0\)), computed numerically for \(\beta^2|qB|=0.1\) and various \(\rho_R\) values (100, 50, 10, 1, 0.5, 0.2) as shown in Fig. 1.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of the CME in infinite media (Kharzeev 2004, Fukushima 2008) and in slab geometries where the CME current was found to vanish when the magnetic field is normal to the boundary (Gorbar 2015, Sitenko 2016) or to be locally finite near the boundary when the field is parallel (Valgushev 2015). By introducing a cylindrical geometry with MIT bag boundary conditions, the authors provide an analytically tractable model for finite-volume effects in systems such as quark-gluon plasma domains, neutron stars, and Dirac/Weyl semimetals. The results enable future studies of CME suppression in realistic finite systems and can be compared with lattice calculations and with the spectral boundary conditions used by Chen (2017) and Sadooghi (2021).
