# Shell-model calculation of $^{100}$Mo double-$β$ decay

> For the first time, the calculation of the nuclear matrix element of the double-$β$ decay of $^{100}$Mo, with and without the emission of two neutrinos, is performed in the framework of the nuclear shell model. This task is accomplished starting from a realistic nucleon-nucleon potential, then the effective shell-model Hamiltonian and decay operators are derived within the many-body perturbation theory. The exotic features which characterize the structure of Mo isotopes -- such as shape coexistence and triaxiality softness -- push the shell-model computational problem beyond its present limits, making it necessary to truncate the model space. This has been done with the goal to preserve as much as possible the role of the rejected degrees of freedom in an effective approach that has been introduced and tested in previous studies. This procedure is grounded on the analysis of the effective single-particle energies of a large-scale shell-model Hamiltonian, that leads to a truncation of the number of the orbitals belonging to the model space. Then, the original Hamiltonian generates a new one by way of a unitary transformation onto the reduced model space, to retain effectively the role of the excluded single-particle orbitals. The predictivity of our calculation of the nuclear matrix element for the neutrinoless double-$β$ decay of $^{100}$Mo is supported by the comparison with experiment of the calculated spectra, electromagnetic transition strengths, Gamow-Teller transition strengths and the two-neutrino double-beta nuclear matrix elements.

## Key claim

For the first time, the nuclear matrix element for both the two-neutrino and neutrinoless double-beta decay of \(^{100}\)Mo is calculated within the nuclear shell model, but this required a truncation of the model space from four proton and five neutron orbitals to four proton and four neutron orbitals, with the excluded degrees of freedom reincorporated via a unitary transformation.

## Method

The authors start from the high-precision CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon potential, renormalize its high-momentum components using the \(V_{\text{low-}k}\) procedure, and then derive an effective shell-model Hamiltonian and decay operators via many-body perturbation theory (Kuo-Lee-Ratcliff folded-diagram expansion, with the \(\hat{Q}\)-box expanded to third order). Because the full model space of four proton and five neutron orbitals outside a \(^{78}\)Ni core is computationally prohibitive for the double-beta decay calculation, they analyze the effective single-particle energies to justify truncating the neutron space to four orbitals, then perform a unitary transformation of the original Hamiltonian onto this reduced space to retain the effects of the excluded orbitals. The nuclear matrix elements are computed using the shell-model code ANTOINE, with the two-neutrino matrix element obtained via the Lanczos strength-function method and the neutrinoless matrix element within the closure approximation.

## Key numbers

- The \(Q\)-value for the double-beta decay of \(^{100}\)Mo is \(Q_{\beta\beta} = 3034.36 \pm 0.17\) keV.
- The CUPID-Mo experiment has set a limit on the neutrinoless double-beta decay half-life of \(^{100}\)Mo of \(T^{0\nu}_{1/2} > 1.5 \times 10^{24}\) yr.
- The natural abundance of \(^{100}\)Mo is \(9.7\%\).
- The proton single-particle energy spacings (in MeV) from the effective Hamiltonian in the [45] model space are: \(0f_{5/2}\) at 0.0, \(1p_{3/2}\) at 1.6, \(1p_{1/2}\) at 2.1, \(0g_{9/2}\) at 4.3.
- The neutron single-particle energy spacings (in MeV) from the same Hamiltonian are: \(2s_{1/2}\) at 0.0, \(1d_{5/2}\) at 0.4, \(1d_{3/2}\) at 1.1, \(0g_{7/2}\) at 2.8, \(0h_{11/2}\) at 3.2.

## Context

This work extends the realistic shell-model approach previously applied by the same group to lighter nuclei such as \(^{48}\)Ca, \(^{76}\)Ge, \(^{82}\)Se, \(^{130}\)Te, and \(^{136}\)Xe (Coraggio 2017, 2019, 2020) to the challenging case of \(^{100}\)Mo, whose shape coexistence and triaxiality have made it accessible mainly to collective models like the interacting boson model (Barea 2013, 2015), energy density functional methods (Vaquero 2013, Yao 2015), and quasiparticle random-phase approximation (Simkovic 2013, Hyvarinen 2015). The calculation enables a shell-model-based prediction of the neutrinoless double-beta decay nuclear matrix element for \(^{100}\)Mo, which is needed to interpret ongoing experiments such as AMoRE, NEMO 3, CUPID-Mo, and the future ton-scale CUPID.
