# A theoretical analysis of mid-mass neutron halos. What changes going from few- to many-body systems regarding neutron halos?

> The present contribution summarizes the content and slightly updates the discussion of a recently proposed theoretical analysis of the halo phenomenon in many-fermion systems. We focus here on applications to potential neutron halos in mid-mass nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper argues that neutron halos can exist in mid-mass nuclei (beyond the light p-shell and sd-shell region), but their formation requires a specific set of energy-scale conditions: a very small one-neutron separation energy (a few hundred keV), a narrow bunch of low-lying states in the (N-1)-body system with an energy spread not exceeding about 1 MeV, and a large gap (at least four or five times the separation energy) separating those low-lying states from higher excitations. The abstract's claim of a "theoretical analysis" glosses over the strong dependence of the results on the specific energy density functional parameterization used, with the text noting that up to 100 percent variations of the halo parameters can occur based on acceptable variations of the functional parameters.

## Method

The author applies a previously developed method (Rotival and Duguet, 2007) that analyzes the one-nucleon density distribution to identify spatially decorrelated halo regions without requiring an a priori separation into core and halo parts. The method identifies a radius r_0 from the second derivative of the log-density profile, then computes quantitative halo parameters including the average number of neutrons in the halo region (N_halo) and the halo contribution to the root-mean-square radius (delta R_halo). Single-reference energy density functional (SR-EDF) calculations using the Skyrme SLy4 functional with a density-dependent delta interaction for pairing are performed for chromium and tin isotopes, solving Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov equations in spherical symmetry.

## Key numbers

- One-neutron separation energy S_n for ^80Cr: approximately 430 keV, with four excited states within an energy spread Delta E of approximately 470 keV, separated from higher-excited states by E' of approximately 3.2 MeV
- The phenomenological estimate for halo formation in light nuclei, 2 MeV/A^(2/3), gives approximately 137 keV for A=80
- Average number of nucleons in the halo region (N_halo) for ^80Cr: approximately 0.45 neutrons
- Halo contribution to the total neutron r.m.s. radius (delta R_halo) for Cr isotopes: up to approximately 0.13 fm
- For ^174Sn: one-neutron separation energy S_n = E approximately 1.5 MeV, with 10 low-lying states over an energy spread Delta E approximately 3.4 MeV, separated from higher excitations by E' approximately 5.6 MeV
- Maximum N_halo for Sn isotopes: approximately 0.18 neutrons
- Maximum delta R_halo for Sn isotopes: approximately 0.02 fm
- The phenomenological estimate for halo formation in ^174Sn would require S_n approximately 64 keV

## Context

This work extends the theoretical analysis of halo phenomena from light halo nuclei (such as ^11Li, ^6He, ^11Be, ^14Be, ^17B, and ^19C, first identified experimentally by Tanihata (1985) and others) to mid-mass systems, using a method previously developed by Rotival and Duguet (2007). The analysis demonstrates that chromium isotopes near the neutron drip-line (^78-80Cr) are predicted to exhibit halos with a collective character involving both J=1/2 and J=5/2 states, contrasting with the simpler single-orbital picture in light halo nuclei. The work also shows that tin isotopes near the drip-line (^174Sn) do not develop halos due to their larger mass, higher angular-momentum ground states, and unfavorable energy scales, instead displaying only neutron skins. The results enable future experimental searches with next-generation radioactive ion beam facilities and highlight the need for improved understanding of the energy density functional parameterization, as the predicted halo parameters show significant sensitivity to the functional choice.
