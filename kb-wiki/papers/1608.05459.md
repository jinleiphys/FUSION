# Quantum Chromodynamics: Computational Aspects

> We present a brief introduction to QCD, the QCD phase diagram, and non-equilibrium phenomena in QCD. We emphasize aspects of the theory that can be addressed using computational methods, in particular euclidean path integral Monte Carlo, fluid dynamics, kinetic theory, classical field theory and holographic duality.

## Key claim

This chapter provides a selective overview of computational methods for QCD, emphasizing that the richness of the QCD phase diagram is reflected in the large number of computational approaches, but the author explicitly limits the discussion to "a number of very simple yet powerful ideas" rather than attempting a comprehensive survey, due to space constraints and the author's stated limited expertise outside computational physics.

## Method

The paper reviews Euclidean path integral Monte Carlo methods using the Metropolis algorithm, illustrated with a one-dimensional double-well potential as a pedagogical example. It then discusses the QCD Lagrangian, asymptotic freedom, chiral symmetry breaking, and the QCD phase diagram as a function of temperature and baryon chemical potential, connecting these to computational approaches including lattice QCD with the Wilson action, fluid dynamics, kinetic theory, classical field theory, and holographic duality.

## Key numbers

- The QCD fine structure constant at the Z boson pole: α_s(m_Z) = 0.1184 ± 0.0007 (Nakamura 2010)
- The QCD scale parameter in the MSbar scheme: Λ_QCD ≈ 200 MeV (Nakamura 2010)
- Proton mass: m_p = 935 MeV
- Charged pion mass: m_π = 139 MeV
- Rho meson mass: m_ρ = 770 MeV
- Pion decay constant: f_π = 93 MeV
- Chiral transition temperature (from chiral susceptibility): T_c ≈ 151 ± 3 ± 3 MeV (Aoki 2006, 2009), consistent with 154 ± 9 MeV (Bazavov 2011, 2014)
- Nuclear matter saturation density: ρ_0 ≈ 0.15 fm^{-3}
- Nuclear liquid-gas critical point: T ≈ 18 MeV and ρ ≈ ρ_0/3 (Sauer 1976, Pochodzalla 1995, Elliott 2013)
- Up and down quark masses: m_u ≈ m_d ≈ 10 MeV; strange quark mass: m_s ≈ 120 MeV

## Context

This review extends the pedagogical tradition of introducing computational QCD methods, building on foundational work in lattice gauge theory (Creutz 1980, Shuryak 1984, 1987) and the author's own prior work (Schaefer 2004). It provides a bridge between textbook quantum mechanics and modern computational approaches to the QCD phase diagram, enabling readers to understand how Euclidean path integral Monte Carlo connects to lattice QCD simulations of the quark-gluon plasma transition, color superconductivity at high density (Alford 1998, Schaefer 1999), and the search for the QCD critical endpoint (Stephanov 1998, 2004; Fodor 2001, 2004; Allton 2002; Karsch 2003; Gavai 2008; Datta 2012).
