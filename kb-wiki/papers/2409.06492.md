# Beam energy dependence of net-hyperon yield and its implication on baryon transport mechanism

> In the constituent quark model, each quark inside a baryon carries 1/3 unit of the baryon number. An alternative picture exists where the center of a Y-shaped topology of gluon fields, called the baryon junction, carries a unit baryon number. Studying baryon transport over a large rapidity gap ($δy$) in nuclear collisions provides a possible tool to distinguish these two pictures. A recent analysis of global data on net-proton yield at mid-rapidity in Au+Au collisions showed an exponential dependence on $δy$ and the exponential slope does not vary with event centrality, favoring the baryon junction picture. Since junctions are flavor blind, hyperons -- baryons containing valence strange quarks -- are expected to exhibit a similar behavior as the proton. This study aims to test this prediction by analyzing hyperon yields in Au+Au collisions at various energies. We observe that net-hyperon yields, after correcting for the strangeness production suppression, adhere to the expected exponential form. The extracted slope parameters for net-$Λ$, net-$Ξ$ and net-$Ω$ are consistent with each other and with those of net-proton within uncertainties, and exhibit no centrality dependence, further substantiating the baryon junction picture. Various implementations of the \texttt{PYTHIA} event generator, primarily based on valence quarks for baryon transport, are unable to simultaneously describe the slope parameters for all baryons.

## Key claim

The authors claim that after correcting for strangeness production suppression using the $K^-/\pi^-$ ratio, net-hyperon yields (net-$\Lambda$, net-$\Xi$, net-$\Omega$) in Au+Au collisions exhibit an exponential dependence on the rapidity gap $\delta y$, with slope parameters consistent with each other and with net-proton results, supporting the flavor-blind baryon junction picture. The hedge is that this agreement only holds after applying the strangeness suppression correction; without it, the raw net-hyperon yields do not follow the expected exponential form.

## Method

The authors compiled published net-hyperon yields ($\Lambda$, $\Xi$, $\Omega$) at mid-rapidity ($|y| < 0.5$) from the RHIC Beam Energy Scan Phase-I program for Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 7.7, 11.5, 19.6, 27, 39, 62.4, and 200 GeV. They corrected these yields for strangeness production suppression by dividing by $(R_{K^-/\pi^-})^n$, where $n$ is the number of valence strange quarks in the hyperon and $R_{K^-/\pi^-}$ is the $K^-/\pi^-$ yield ratio measured in 0-10% central Au+Au collisions. The corrected net-hyperon yields per participant nucleon were then fitted with an exponential function $(dN/dy)_\text{net-hyperon} \propto e^{-\alpha_B \delta y}$ to extract the slope parameter $\alpha_B$ for each hyperon species across different centrality classes.

## Key numbers

- Net-$\Lambda$ slope parameters $\alpha_B$ range from 0.75 to 0.97 across different centrality classes in Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 7.7 - 200 GeV.
- Net-$\Xi$ slope parameters $\alpha_B$ range from 0.82 to 1.15 across different centrality classes.
- Net-$\Omega$ slope parameter $\alpha_B$ = 1.01 $\pm$ 0.12 for the 0-60% centrality interval.
- The net-proton slope parameter from earlier analysis is $\alpha_B$ = 0.64 $\pm$ 0.05 for 0-80% centrality Au+Au collisions.
- The $K^-/\pi^-$ ratio in 0-10% central Au+Au collisions increases monotonically with beam energy from $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 7.7 GeV to 200 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the baryon junction analysis of net-proton yields by Lewis et al. (2023) to hyperons, testing the flavor-blind prediction of the baryon junction mechanism proposed by Kharzeev (1996). The results contradict the valence quark transport picture implemented in various tunes of the PYTHIA event generator (Sjostrand 2006, Skands 2010), which cannot simultaneously describe the slope parameters for all baryon species. The findings enable future tests of baryon transport mechanisms in other collision systems and at higher energies from LHC experiments.
