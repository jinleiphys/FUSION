# Nuclear Fragmentation and Its Parallels

> A model for the fragmentation of a nucleus is developed. Parallels of the description of this process with other areas are shown which include Feynman's theory of the $λ$ transition in liquid Helium, Bose condensation, and Markov process models used in stochastic networks and polymer physics. These parallels are used to generalize and further develop a previous exactly solvable model of nuclear fragmentation. An analysis of some experimental data is given.

## Key claim

The paper extends a previously exactly solvable canonical ensemble model of nuclear fragmentation by allowing more general forms for the cluster-size-dependent weights, and shows that this generalized model has formal parallels with Feynman's theory of the lambda transition in liquid Helium, Bose condensation, and Markov process models from polymer physics; however, the experimental data for gold fragmentation at 0.99 GeV/amu are insufficient to distinguish which specific model variant is correct.

## Method

The authors develop a canonical ensemble model where each fragmentation of A nucleons into clusters of size k is assigned a weight proportional to (x/beta_k)^(n_k), with x containing the physical parameters (temperature, volume, binding energy) and beta_k encoding the cluster-size dependence. They derive a recurrence relation for the partition function Z_A(x;beta) and use it to compute ensemble averages of cluster multiplicities. The model is then compared to Feynman's path-integral treatment of the lambda transition in liquid Helium, to Markov process models of polymerization, and to percolation and partitioning-only models of nuclear fragmentation.

## Key numbers

- For the fragmentation of 197Au at 0.99 GeV/amu, several model variants give excellent fits to experimental data, but the statistics are insufficient to distinguish among them.
- The partition function for the case beta_k = k yields Z_A(x) = (1/A!) sum_k |S_A^(k)| x^k, where S_A^(k) are Stirling numbers of the first kind.
- For the case beta_k = 1, the partition function is Z_A(x) = (1/A) x L_(A-1)^1(-x), with L_A^1 a Laguerre polynomial.
- For the case beta_k = k!, Z_A(x) = (1/A!) sum_k S_A^(k) x^k, where S_A^(k) are Stirling numbers of the second kind.
- For an ideal Bose gas in d dimensions, the model uses beta_k = k^tau with tau = 1 + d/2.
- In the large-A limit for beta_k = k^tau, the coefficient z_A^(2) approaches zeta(tau), the Riemann zeta function.
- The percolation-like cluster distribution from the partitioning-only model gives <n_k> approx 1/(exp{(pi^2/(6A))^(1/2) k} - 1).
- For the x model with beta_k = k and x = 1+epsilon, the small-k distribution is <n_k> approx (x/k) exp(-epsilon k/A), giving tau=1, sigma=1.

## Context

This work extends the exactly solvable canonical ensemble models developed by Mekjian (1991) and collaborators, which previously treated only the special cases x_k = x (the "x model") and x_1 = xy, x_k = x for k>1 (the "xy model"). The paper draws explicit parallels to Feynman's (1953) theory of the lambda transition in liquid Helium, showing that the cycle-class decomposition of permutations in Feynman's path integral has the same mathematical structure as the cluster-weighting scheme. It also connects to Markov process models of polymerization studied by Kelly (1979), showing that the canonical ensemble models can be derived from detailed-balance conditions on joining and splitting rates. The comparison with experimental data for 197Au fragmentation at 0.99 GeV/amu shows that multiple model variants fit equally well, leaving model discrimination as an open problem for future experiments with better statistics.
