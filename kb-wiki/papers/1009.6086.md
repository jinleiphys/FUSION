# A=3 Clustering in Nuclei

> Alpha clustering in nuclei, at present is a well studied and reasonably well accepted property of the nucleus. Less well appreciated and more ambiguous is the role of A=3 clustering, i.e. helion and triton, in nuclei. Here we try to place A=3 clustering in nuclei into its proper perspective, first by pointing out strong experimental evidences which indicate its clear presence in nuclei and secondly showing as to how to include these A=3 clusters in a proper and consistent theoretical understanding of the nuclear phenomenon.

## Key claim

The paper argues that A=3 clusters (helion and triton) exist as pre-formed entities inside nuclei with experimental evidence as strong as that for alpha clustering, despite the prevailing theoretical view that the Pauli exclusion principle should preclude such clustering in the nuclear interior.

## Method

The authors review experimental knockout and transfer reactions (proton-induced, electron-induced, and heavy-ion-induced) that show direct removal of helion and triton clusters from various target nuclei, leaving residual nuclei in low-excitation states. They then critique the theoretical assumption of equivalence between different multi-cluster configurations (e.g., alpha+d versus triton+helion in 6Li), arguing that this equivalence relies on neglecting three-body forces, which are now known to be essential for reproducing binding energies of A=3 and A=4 nuclei. They propose a model where the fundamental building blocks of nuclei are only n, p, triton, and helion, with the alpha particle treated as a composite of an A=3 cluster plus a nucleon.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy of alpha particle: 28 MeV; binding energy of triton and helion: approximately 8 MeV (used to argue that high binding energy alone cannot explain clustering).
- Central density of 3He and 4He is about twice as large as the density of all other nuclei (from density distributions shown in figures).
- Poskanzer et al. (1974) found cross sections for high-energy 3He and 4He emission from Ag and U targets bombarded with relativistic heavy ions were two to three orders of magnitude higher than for proton-induced reactions at comparable energy.
- Artun et al. bombarded protons at 150, 230, 600, and 960 MeV on targets of N, O, P, S, V, Al, Si, Ca, and Fe, finding clean evidence for knockout of alpha, helion, and triton as single entities.
- Cunsolo et al. used the reaction 14C(6Li,t)17O at E(6Li)=34 MeV, confirming dominant direct 3He transfer.
- Harwood and Kemper found that states in 15N at 9.155, 9.83, and 10.69 MeV were populated strongly in both three-particle and four-particle transfer reactions, suggesting large overlap between triton and alpha cluster states.

## Context

This work extends the line of alpha-clustering studies (Ikeda et al., Mac Gregor) by arguing that A=3 clustering is equally fundamental and has been overlooked due to the prevailing "multi-cluster equivalence" concept (e.g., in the Oscillator Model for 6Li). The authors contradict the view that only alpha clusters are significant because of high binding energy, citing experiments by Artun et al., Poskanzer et al., and Ohkubo and Hinabayashi that show direct evidence for helion and triton clusters. The paper enables a new model where n, p, triton, and helion are the basic building blocks, with the alpha particle treated as a composite (3+1) structure, and introduces a new SU(2) symmetry group (nusospin) for the (triton, helion) pair.
