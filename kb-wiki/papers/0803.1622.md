# Crucial inputs to nucleosynthesis calculations

> The first part of the paper discusses nuclear properties relevant to predict compound reactions. The second part addresses direct reactions with special emphasis on direct neutron capture.

# Crucial inputs to nucleosynthesis calculations

## Key claim
The paper argues that while the statistical Hauser-Feshbach model is applicable for most astrophysical reactions, direct neutron capture becomes non-negligible and potentially dominant for nuclei near the driplines and in closed-shell nuclei, but this conclusion is conditional on the nuclear level density being low at the formation energy and on the absence of strong resonances.

## Method
The author reviews nuclear properties required for compound reaction calculations in the Hauser-Feshbach model, including nuclear level densities, averaged radiative widths with E1 and M1 transitions, optical potentials for neutrons, protons, and alpha particles, and the treatment of isospin suppression. For direct reactions, the paper describes a statistical direct capture model that replaces sums over discrete final states with integrations over level densities and uses energy-dependent spectroscopic factors parameterized by a Fermi function.

## Key numbers
- Astrophysically relevant energies are E ≤ 10 MeV for charged projectiles and E ≤ 1 MeV for neutrons
- The JLM optical potential for neutrons and protons required a 70% increase in imaginary depth to reproduce measured S-factors for 70Ge(p,γ) and 76Ge(p,n) reactions
- The 144Sm(α,γ) reaction shows the strongest discrepancy between experiment and theory at the lowest measured energy of 10.2 MeV
- Alpha capture cross sections at low energies can be described within a factor of two by the Satchler potential derived from alpha scattering at tens of MeV
- The statistical model is applicable for nuclei with A ≥ 20, except near closed shells or driplines where level densities are low

## Context
This paper extends the discussion of reaction mechanism interplay described in Descouvemont (2007) and Rauscher et al. (1997), and addresses the longstanding problem of predicting cross sections for unstable nuclei not accessible in the laboratory. The work enables improved nucleosynthesis calculations for the r-process and rp-process by identifying where direct capture must supplement the Hauser-Feshbach model, and highlights the need for future measurements of sub-Coulomb alpha capture reactions and further experimental tests of pygmy resonance predictions away from stability.
