# Implication of the proton-deuteron radiative capture for Big Bang Nucleosynthesis

> The astrophysical $S$-factor for the radiative capture $d(p,γ)^3$He in the energy-range of interest for Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) is calculated using an {\it ab-initio} approach. The nuclear Hamiltonian retains both two- and three-nucleon interactions - the Argonne $v_{18}$ and the Urbana IX, respectively. Both one- and many-body contributions to the nuclear current operator are included. The former retain for the first time, besides the $1/m$ leading order contribution ($m$ is the nucleon mass), also the next-to-leading order term, proportional to $1/m^3$. The many-body currents are constructed in order to satisfy the current conservation relation with the adopted Hamiltonian model. The hyperspherical harmonics technique is applied to solve the $A=3$ bound and scattering states. A particular attention is used in this second case in order to obtain, in the energy range of BBN, an uncertainty on the astrophysical $S$-factor of the order or below $\sim$1 %. Then, in this energy range, the $S$-factor is found to be $\sim$10 % larger than the currently adopted values.Part of this increase (1-3 %) is due to the $1/m^3$ one-body operator, while the remaining is due to the new more accurate scattering wave functions. We have studied the implication of this new determination for the $d(p,γ)^3$He $S$-factor on deuterium primordial abundance. We find that the predicted theoretical value for $^2$H/H is in excellent agreement with its experimental determination, using the most recent determination of baryon density of Planck experiment, and with a standard number of relativistic degrees of freedom $N_{\rm eff}=3.046$ during primordial nucleosynthesis.

## Key claim
The new *ab-initio* calculation of the \(d(p,\gamma)^3\)He astrophysical \(S\)-factor in the Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) energy range (30–300 keV) yields values systematically \(\sim\)10% larger than previously adopted values, with a theoretical uncertainty below \(\sim\)1%. This increase brings the predicted primordial deuterium abundance into excellent agreement with the experimental determination of Cooke (2014) and the Planck 2015 baryon density, without requiring a non-standard number of relativistic degrees of freedom.

## Method
The authors solved the \(A=3\) bound and scattering states using the hyperspherical harmonics (HH) technique with the Argonne \(v_{18}\) two-nucleon and Urbana IX three-nucleon interactions. The nuclear current operator included both one-body terms (retaining for the first time the next-to-leading order \(1/m^3\) relativistic correction) and many-body currents constructed to satisfy current conservation with the adopted Hamiltonian. The numerical uncertainty from the \(p-d\) scattering wave functions was reduced to below 1% by verifying \(\langle H \rangle = E - B_d\) within 0.1% for each partial wave.

## Key numbers
- The astrophysical \(S\)-factor at \(E = 10\) keV is 0.286 keV b, with a wave-function uncertainty (\(\Delta S_{WF}\)) of 0.1% and a relativistic correction (\(\Delta\mathbf{j}^{(RC)}\)) of +0.8%.
- At \(E = 300\) keV, the \(S\)-factor is 2.716 keV b, with \(\Delta S_{WF} = 0.5\%\) and \(\Delta\mathbf{j}^{(RC)} = +2.7\%\).
- The \(1/m^3\) one-body contribution increases the \(S\)-factor by 1–3% over the entire energy range; the remaining 5–8% increase comes from more accurate scattering wave functions.
- Using the new \(S\)-factor, the predicted primordial deuterium-to-hydrogen ratio for Planck 2015 \(\Omega_b h^2 = 0.02225 \pm 0.00016\) and standard \(N_{\rm eff} = 3.046\) is \(^2\)H/H = \((2.49 \pm 0.03 \pm 0.03) \times 10^{-5}\) (nuclear rate and \(\Omega_b h^2\) errors, respectively).
- Marginalizing over baryon density yields \(N_{\rm eff} = 3.18 \pm 0.16\) (68% C.L.), consistent with the standard value of 3.046.

## Context
This work extends the *ab-initio* calculations of Marcucci et al. (2005) by including the \(1/m^3\) one-body current operator, previously found essential by Girlanda et al. (2010) for the related \(d(n,\gamma)^3\)H capture, and by reducing the numerical uncertainty from the HH scattering solution to below 1%. The new \(S\)-factor resolves the tension between the Planck 2015 baryon density, the experimental deuterium abundance of Cooke (2014), and standard BBN predictions, which earlier studies (Nollett, 2011; Di Valentino, 2014) had suggested could be alleviated by a \(\sim\)10% increase in the \(d(p,\gamma)^3\)He rate. The results motivate a direct experimental measurement of the \(S\)-factor in the BBN energy range, planned by the LUNA Collaboration.
