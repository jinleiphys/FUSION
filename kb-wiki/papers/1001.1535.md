# Toward the Application of Three-Dimensional Approach to Few-body Atomic Bound States

> The first step toward the application of an effective non partial wave (PW) numerical approach to few-body atomic bound states has been taken. The two-body transition amplitude which appears in the kernel of three-dimensional Faddeev-Yakubovsky integral equations is calculated as function of two-body Jacobi momentum vectors, i.e. as a function of the magnitude of initial and final momentum vectors and the angle between them. For numerical calculation the realistic interatomic interactions HFDHE2, HFD-B, LM2M2 and TTY are used. The angular and momentum dependence of the fully off-shell transition amplitude is studied at negative energies. It has been numerically shown that, similar to the nuclear case, the transition amplitude exhibits a characteristic angular behavior in the vicinity of 4He dimer pole.

# Toward the Application of Three-Dimensional Approach to Few-body Atomic Bound States

## Key claim
The authors have taken the first step toward applying a non-partial-wave (non-PW) numerical method, previously successful for nuclear systems, to few-body atomic bound states by calculating the two-body transition amplitude as a function of Jacobi momentum vectors; they numerically show that, similar to the nuclear case, the transition amplitude exhibits a characteristic angular behavior in the vicinity of the 4He dimer pole, but only for negative energies close to the dimer binding energy.

## Method
The two-body fully off-shell transition amplitude was calculated by solving the inhomogeneous Lippmann-Schwinger integral equation in a three-dimensional momentum-space representation without partial wave decomposition, using the realistic interatomic potentials HFDHE2, HFD-B, LM2M2, and TTY. The integral equation was discretized with 200 mesh points for Jacobi momenta and 150 for the angle variable for the dimer calculation, and 40 momentum and 41 angle mesh grids for the transition amplitude calculation, with the azimuthal integration performed using 10 mesh points on the interval [0, pi/2]. The resulting system of linear equations was solved using the Lapack Fortran library.

## Key numbers
- Dimer binding energy for HFDHE2 potential: -0.83011 mK (compared to -0.83012 mK from partial wave calculation)
- Dimer binding energy for HFD-B potential: -1.68540 mK (compared to -1.68541 mK from partial wave calculation)
- Dimer binding energy for LM2M2 potential: -1.30347 mK (compared to -1.30348 mK from partial wave calculation)
- Dimer binding energy for TTY potential: -1.30962 mK (compared to -1.30962 mK from partial wave calculation)
- Experimental dimer binding energy: -1.1 mK with uncertainty +0.3/-0.2 mK (Grisenti 2000)
- Transition amplitude calculated at energy E = -100 mK, close to the 4He trimer binding energy
- Angular dependence of on-shell transition amplitude T(p0,p0,x;E) studied over energy range -400 mK to -1 mK
- For the potential calculation, cutoff values of r_max = 30 Angstrom and 200 mesh points were used
- The 4He atom mass defined by hbar^2/m = 12.12 K Angstrom^2
- Maximum Jacobi momentum cutoff: p_max = 20 Angstrom^-1

## Context
This work extends the non-partial-wave approach developed by Elster et al. (1997) for nuclear systems using Malfliet-Tjon type potentials to atomic systems with realistic interatomic interactions. It addresses the limitation that previous few-body atomic calculations using Faddeev-Yakubovsky equations required a large number of partial waves due to the strong short-range repulsion in interatomic potentials, as noted in studies by Nielsen, Roudnev (2000), and Kolganova. The calculated transition amplitudes are intended to be used as input for three-dimensional Faddeev-Yakubovsky integral equations to study 4He trimer and tetramer ground and excited states, with those calculations reported as underway.
