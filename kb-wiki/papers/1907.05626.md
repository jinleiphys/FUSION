# Origin of energy shift in kaonic atom and kaon-nucleus interaction

> The $K^-$-nucleus optical potential is revisited to investigate its global feature phenomenologically. It is a puzzle that the energy shift is found to be repulsive in all of the observed kaonic atom, although the $K^-N$ interaction is known to be so attractive as to form the $Λ(1405)$ resonance. To solve this puzzle, we examine the $K^-$ optical potential in the linear density approximation and determine the potential parameters of each kaonic atom so as to reproduce the observed energy shift and absorption width. We find two types of the potentials. One potential has a so large real part as to provide nuclear states with the same quantum number to the atomic state in the last orbit. The level repulsion between the atomic state and the nuclear states takes place due to their mixing, and it makes the atomic state shifted repulsively. The other type of the potential has a large imaginary part and the imaginary part works repulsively for atomic states. We find that only the latter solution reproduce a wide of the observed data, and thus is realized as a $K^-$-nucleus potential for kaonic atom. In the linear nuclear density optical potential, the picture that the repulsive shifts in the atomic states stem from the existence of the nuclear states does not globally stand up. This implies that the $K^-$-nucleus optical potential should have a large imaginary part. We examine some nonlinear density effects and find that the conclusion does not change. We also confirm that the conventionally known optical potentials are categorized into the latter type of the potential.

## Key claim

The repulsive energy shift observed in all kaonic atoms does not globally originate from level repulsion between atomic and nuclear states, as previously hypothesized; instead, the shift is consistently explained by a large imaginary part of the K- nucleus optical potential, which acts repulsively on the atomic states.

## Method

The authors solved the Klein-Gordon equation for K- atoms using a linear nuclear density optical potential of the form V_opt(r) = -(V_0 + iW_0) rho_N(r)/rho_0, with Woods-Saxon nuclear densities. For each kaonic atom, they determined the potential parameters V_0 and W_0 by fitting to the experimental energy shift and absorption width. They then tested the universality of each set of parameters by calculating shifts and widths for other kaonic atoms and comparing with experimental data.

## Key numbers

- For the Cu-1 kaonic atom, three potential parameter sets reproduced the observed shift (-0.240 keV) and width (1.650 keV): Potential 1 (V_0 = 79.5 MeV, W_0 = 114.5 MeV), Potential 2 (V_0 = 78.0 MeV, W_0 = 20.0 MeV), and Potential 3 (V_0 = 199.5 MeV, W_0 = 28.0 MeV).
- Potential 1 parameters across all atoms showed imaginary parts larger than real parts, with values ranging from V_0 = 23.5 MeV (Cu-2) to 79.5 MeV (Cu-1) and W_0 = 79.0 MeV (Mg) to 164.0 MeV (Ni).
- Potential 2 parameters showed real parts larger than imaginary parts, with V_0 ranging from 64.5 MeV (Cd) to 128.0 MeV (Mg) and W_0 from 13.0 MeV (In) to 31.0 MeV (Si).
- For the Cu-1 atom with Potential 2, reducing the potential strength by a factor lambda_2 = 0.8 caused the nuclear state binding energy to become negative (-2.0 MeV) and the atomic shift to become attractive (-0.216 keV).

## Context

This work addresses the long-standing puzzle that K- N interactions are attractive enough to form the Lambda(1405) resonance, yet all kaonic atoms show repulsive energy shifts. The authors extend prior global fitting work by Friedman (1994) and theoretical calculations using chiral unitary approaches by Hirenzaki (2000) and others. The finding that a large imaginary part, rather than level repulsion from nuclear states, is the universal origin of the repulsive shift resolves this puzzle and supports the shallower optical potential picture found in earlier global fits. The results imply that future experiments should focus on observing nuclear states of kaonic nuclei to precisely determine the real part of the optical potential.
