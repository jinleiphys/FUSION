# Equation of State of Quantum Gases Beyond the Van der Waals Approximation

> A recently suggested equation of state with the induced surface tension is generalized to the case of quantum gases with mean-field interaction. The self-consistency conditions of such a model and the necessary one to obey the Third Law of thermodynamics are found. The quantum virial expansion of the Van der Waals models of such a type is analyzed and its virial coefficients are given. In contrast to traditional beliefs, it is shown that an inclusion of the third and higher virial coefficients of the gas of hard spheres into the interaction pressure of the Van der Waals models either breaks down the Third Law of thermodynamics or does not allow one to go beyond the Van der Waals approximation at low temperatures. It is demonstrated that the generalized equation of state with the induced surface tension allows one to avoid such problems and to safely go beyond the Van der Waals approximation. Besides, the effective virial expansion for quantum version of the induced surface tension equation of state is established and all corresponding virial coefficients are found exactly.The explicit expressions for the true quantum virial coefficients of an arbitrary order of this equation of state are given in the low density approximation. A few basic constraints on such models which are necessary to describe the nuclear and hadronic matter properties are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that including third and higher virial coefficients of the hard-sphere gas into the interaction pressure of quantum Van der Waals models either violates the Third Law of thermodynamics or prevents going beyond the Van der Waals approximation at low temperatures; the generalized equation of state with induced surface tension avoids these problems and allows a consistent extension beyond the Van der Waals approximation for quantum gases.

## Method

The authors generalize the classical induced surface tension (IST) equation of state to quantum gases with mean-field interactions, formulating a system of equations for pressure and induced surface tension with two effective chemical potentials. They analyze the self-consistency conditions required for thermodynamic identities and the Third Law, derive the quantum virial expansion for both the Van der Waals and IST models, and compute all virial coefficients explicitly in the low-density approximation using cluster integrals for ideal quantum gases.

## Key numbers

- The Van der Waals excluded volume parameter is \(b \equiv 4 V_0 = \frac{16 \pi}{3} R_p^3\), where \(R_p\) is the hard-core radius.
- For the quantum Van der Waals model of nuclear matter with parameters from Vovch15 (\(b=3.42\) fm\(^3\), \(a_{attr}=329\) MeV\(\cdot\)fm\(^3\)), the full second virial coefficient \(a_2^{Q, tot}(T)\) is zero at \(T\simeq 0.32\) MeV and \(T\simeq 90.5\) MeV, and equals approximately \(1.33\) fm\(^3\) at \(T=150\) MeV, corresponding to an equivalent hard-core radius \(R_{eq} \simeq 0.46\) fm.
- For the IST equation of state with parameter \(\alpha = \alpha_B \equiv 1.245\), the fourth virial coefficient of the classical hard-sphere gas is reproduced exactly, while the third virial coefficient is recovered with a relative error of about 16%.
- The hard-core radius of nucleons in the most advanced hadron resonance gas model is 0.365 fm (Bugaev:ALICE, VetaNEW, Bugaev17b), and in the IST equation of state for nuclear matter this radius is below 0.4 fm (Aleksei17).

## Context

This work extends the induced surface tension equation of state previously developed for Boltzmann statistics (Bugaev:13NPA, Bugaev:ALICE, VetaNEW) to quantum statistics, and critically analyzes the quantum Van der Waals models of Vovch14, Vovch15, and Redlich16. The authors show that the approach of Typel16 for including excluded volume corrections via a linear temperature-dependent effective potential also violates the Third Law. The results enable consistent modeling of dense hadronic, nuclear, and neutron matter, as well as quantum liquids, beyond the second virial coefficient while maintaining thermodynamic consistency at all temperatures.
