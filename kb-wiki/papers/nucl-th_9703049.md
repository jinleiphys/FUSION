# The effects of varying the strengths of tensor and spin-orbit interactions on M1 and E2 rates in ^{12}C: Comparison of results in $ΔN = 0$ and $ΔN = 0 + 2\hbarω$ spaces}

> The energies and transition rates to $J=1^{+} T=1$ and $J=2^{+} T=0,1$ states in ^{12}C with matrix elements fitted to realistic $G$ matrix elements obtained in non-relativistic approaches are studied. Then the effects of varying the strengths of the two-body tensor and spin-orbit interactions are also considered. The calculations are done in both a small space (0p) and a large space (0p + $2\hbarω$). In the small space the B(M1) from ground to the $J=1^{+} T=1$ is enhanced and gets closer to experiment if the strength of the spin-orbit interaction is increased and/or if that of the tensor interaction is made weaker. In a large space the spin B(M1) gets reduced by almost a factor of two. A `self-weakening' mechanism for the tensor interaction which succeeded in explaining anomalies in other nuclei does not seem to work for this case.

## Key claim

In a small (0p) shell-model space, the B(M1) transition from the ground state to the \(J=1^{+} T=1\) state in \(^{12}\)C can be enhanced toward the experimental value by increasing the two-body spin-orbit strength and/or decreasing the tensor strength; however, in a large (0p + \(2\hbar\omega\)) space, the B(M1) is reduced by almost a factor of two relative to the small space, and the "self-weakening mechanism" for the tensor interaction that succeeded in explaining anomalies in \(^{6}\)Li, \(A=14\), and \(^{16}\)O does not work for this case.

## Method

Shell-model calculations were performed in both a small space (\(\Delta N = 0\), the 0p shell) and a large space (\(\Delta N = 0 + 2\hbar\omega\)) for \(^{12}\)C, using matrix elements fitted to realistic \(G\) matrix elements obtained from non-relativistic approaches. The interaction was parameterized as \(V = V_c + x V_{\text{s.o.}} + y V_{\text{t}}\), where \(x\) and \(y\) scale the strengths of the two-body spin-orbit and tensor interactions, respectively; four combinations \((x,y) = (1,1), (1.5,1), (1,0.5), (1.5,0.5)\) were studied. B(M1) and B(E2) transition rates to \(J=1^{+} T=1\) and \(J=2^{+} T=0,1\) states were computed and compared with experimental values.

## Key numbers

- Experimental B(M1) for \(0^{+} T=0 \rightarrow 1^{+} T=1\) in \(^{12}\)C: 2.63(8) \(\mu_N^2\) (Ajzenberg-Selove, 1990)
- Small space (\(x=1, y=1\)) B(M1): 0.89 \(\mu_N^2\) (much smaller than experiment); B(M1)\(_\sigma\) = 0.58 \(\mu_N^2\), B(M1)\(_l\) = 0.03 \(\mu_N^2\)
- Large space (\(x=1, y=1\)) B(M1): 0.54 \(\mu_N^2\); B(M1)\(_\sigma\) = 0.31 \(\mu_N^2\) (quenching factor 0.53 relative to small space)
- Small space (\(x=1.5, y=0.5\)) B(M1): 2.54 \(\mu_N^2\) (close to experimental 2.63 \(\mu_N^2\)); B(M1)\(_\sigma\) = 2.57 \(\mu_N^2\)
- Large space (\(x=1.5, y=1\)) B(M1): 1.29 \(\mu_N^2\) (decrease from small space value of 1.89 \(\mu_N^2\))
- Experimental B(E2) \(0_1 \rightarrow 2_1\) in \(^{12}\)C: \(\sim 40\) \(e^2\) fm\(^4\) (Raman, 1987)
- Small space (\(x=1, y=1\)) isoscalar B(E2) sum: 82.7 \(e^2\) fm\(^4\) (with effective charges \(e_p=1.5, e_n=0.5\)); 95% in lowest \(2^+ T=0\) state
- Large space (\(x=1, y=1\)) isoscalar B(E2) sum over 500 states: 42.5 \(e^2\) fm\(^4\) (with bare charges \(e_p=1, e_n=0\))
- Large space isovector B(E2) sum: 21.28 \(e^2\) fm\(^4\) (over 500 states), nearly independent of \((x,y)\) (variation < 1%)

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Zheng and Zamick (1990) and Zheng, Zamick, and Muther (1995), who found that realistic \(G\) matrix calculations in the 0p shell gave B(M1) values much too small for the \(J=1^{+} T=1\) state in \(^{12}\)C. The paper tests whether the "self-weakening mechanism" proposed by Fayache, Zheng, and Zamick (1995), where higher-shell admixtures effectively weaken the tensor interaction in the valence space, applies to \(^{12}\)C, as it did for the quadrupole moment of \(^{6}\)Li, the Gamow-Teller matrix element in the \(A=14\) system, and the energy splitting in \(^{16}\)O. The results show that for \(^{12}\)C, large-space calculations with the full tensor strength (\(y=1\)) yield smaller B(M1) values than small-space calculations, contradicting the self-weakening expectation; instead, the only way to approach experiment appears to require enhanced spin-orbit strength (as in Dirac phenomenology, Serot and Walecka, 1986) and/or a genuinely weaker tensor interaction (as in the universal scaling ideas of Brown and Rho, 1991). The work also demonstrates that isoscalar B(E2) strengths in the large space are about half those in the small space with standard effective charges, suggesting that the sum \(e_p+e_n\) should be \(\approx 1.4\) rather than 2 for \(^{12}\)C.
