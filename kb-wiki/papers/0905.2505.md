# Avoid the Tsunami of the Dirac sea in the Imaginary Time Step method

> The discrete single-particle spectra in both the Fermi and Dirac sea have been calculated by the imaginary time step (ITS) method for the Schrödinger-like equation after avoiding the "tsunami" of the Dirac sea, i.e., the diving behavior of the single-particle level into the Dirac sea in the direct application of the ITS method for the Dirac equation. It is found that by the transform from the Dirac equation to the Schrödinger-like equation, the single-particle spectra, which extend from the positive to the negative infinity, can be separately obtained by the ITS evolution in either the Fermi sea or the Dirac sea. Identical results with those in the conventional shooting method have been obtained via the ITS evolution for the equivalent Schrödinger-like equation, which demonstrates the feasibility, practicality and reliability of the present algorithm and dispels the doubts on the ITS method in the relativistic system.

## Key claim

The imaginary time step (ITS) method cannot be applied directly to the Dirac equation because all single-particle levels inevitably dive into the Dirac sea (the "tsunami" disaster); however, by transforming the Dirac equation into an equivalent Schrodinger-like equation, the ITS method can separately and reliably obtain discrete single-particle spectra in either the Fermi sea or the Dirac sea.

## Method

The authors applied the ITS evolution to the Schrodinger-like equation derived from the radial Dirac equation, using the effective Hamiltonian that depends on the single-particle energy and contains an effective mass term. For states in the Fermi sea, they evolved the Schrodinger-like equation directly; for states in the Dirac sea, they applied the same technique to the charge-conjugated Schrodinger-like equation. The upper component of the Dirac spinor was obtained by ITS evolution, the lower component was reconstructed from the upper component via the relation G_a = (1/(2M_+)) (dF_a/dr + (kappa_a/r) F_a), and the full Dirac spinors were orthonormalized at each iteration.

## Key numbers

* For the neutron 2s_{1/2} orbital in the Dirac sea, the ITS evolution for the Dirac equation with a box of R=10 fm and dr=0.5 fm produced single-particle energies and radial wave functions in good agreement with "exact" results from the shooting method with R=20 fm and dr=0.1 fm.
* The ITS evolution for the Schrodinger-like equation used a spherical box of R=20 fm and mesh size dr=0.1 fm, with a typical time step of Delta t = 10^{-26} s.
* The mesh sizes tested for the direct Dirac equation ITS evolution were dr=0.5 fm, 0.2 fm, and 0.1 fm, showing that smaller mesh sizes caused the single-particle energy to dive deeper into the Dirac sea.

## Context

This work extends the ITS method, previously successful in non-relativistic mean-field calculations (ev8-80; ev8-cpc05), to relativistic systems described by the Relativistic Mean Field approach (Serot 1986; Ring 1996; Vretenar 2005). It addresses a specific challenge noted in earlier attempts to apply ITS to relativistic problems (Gogelein 2007), where the Dirac sea caused numerical instability. The method enables straightforward extension from spherical to deformed relativistic calculations, which is important for describing exotic nuclei and deformed halo phenomena, and provides an alternative to the Woods-Saxon basis expansion method (Zhou 2003) that becomes time-consuming for heavy systems.
