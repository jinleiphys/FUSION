# Neutrinos from dense environments : Flavor mechanisms, theoretical approaches, observations, and new directions

> Neutrino masses and mixings produce vacuum oscillations, an established quantum mechanical phenomenon. In matter, the Mikheev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein effect, due to neutrino interactions with the background particles, triggers resonant flavor modification. In dense environments, such as core-collapse supernovae or compact mergers, sizable neutrino-neutrino interactions, shock waves and turbulence impact the neutrino flavor content under a variety of phenomena. Theoretical approaches of neutrino propagation range from the mean-field approximation to the full quantum kinetic equations. Intriguing connections have been uncovered between weakly interacting dense neutrino gases and other many-body systems and domains, from condensed matter and nuclear physics to quantum computing. Besides the intrinsic theoretical interest, establishing how neutrinos change flavor contributes to answer the longstanding open questions of how massive stars explode and of the r-process sites. It is also important for future observations of core-collapse supernova neutrinos and of the diffuse supernova neutrino background that should be discovered in the foreseeable future.

## Key claim

This review argues that neutrino flavor evolution in dense astrophysical environments (core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers) involves novel conversion phenomena beyond the established Mikheev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein (MSW) effect, but the precise impact on supernova explosion mechanisms and r-process nucleosynthesis remains uncertain and depends on the specific assumptions made about neutrino properties and interactions.

## Method

The paper provides a comprehensive review of theoretical approaches to neutrino flavor evolution in dense environments, ranging from the mean-field approximation to full quantum kinetic equations. It surveys the historical development of neutrino physics from Pauli's 1930 hypothesis through the discovery of vacuum oscillations, the MSW effect, and the SN1987A observation, then examines current theoretical frameworks for understanding neutrino-neutrino interactions, shock waves, and turbulence in supernovae and compact mergers. The review also connects these theoretical developments to observational prospects for supernova neutrinos and the diffuse supernova neutrino background (DSNB).

## Key numbers

* Solar neutrino mixing parameters: $\theta_{12} \approx 35^{\circ}$, $\Delta m^2_{21} = 7.5 \times 10^{-5}$ eV$^2$ (solar mass-squared difference)
* Atmospheric neutrino mixing parameters: $\theta_{23} \approx 45^{\circ}$, $\Delta m^2_{32} = 2.4 \times 10^{-3}$ eV$^2$ (atmospheric mass-squared difference)
* Reactor mixing angle: $\theta_{13} \approx 8.2^{\circ}$
* KATRIN upper limit on effective $\nu_e$ mass: $m < 0.8$ eV at 90% confidence level
* Supernova neutrino event predictions for a galactic supernova at 10 kpc: up to $3 \times 10^3$ events in DUNE, up to about $8 \times 10^3$ in JUNO, almost $10^4$ in Super-Kamiokande, $10^5$ in Hyper-Kamiokande, and $10^6$ in IceCube
* DSNB upper limits: 2.7 $\bar{\nu}_e$ cm$^{-2}$s$^{-1}$ ($E_{\nu} > 17.3$ MeV, 90% C.L.) from Super-Kamiokande SK-I to SK-IV combined data
* Core-collapse supernova rate in the Milky Way: 1 to 3 per century, with mean time of occurrence $61^{+24}_{-14}$ years (Rozwadowska 2020)
* GW170817 distance: 40 Mpc (lenticular galaxy NGC 4993)

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on neutrino oscillations by Pontecorvo (1957), Gribov and Pontecorvo (1968), and the MSW effect discovered by Mikheev and Smirnov (1986), building on Wolfenstein's (1977, 1979) matter effects. It connects to the delayed neutrino-heating mechanism for supernova explosions proposed by Bethe and Wilson (1984) and the r-process nucleosynthesis framework of Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, and Hoyle (1957) and Cameron (1958). The review enables future work on understanding how flavor evolution affects supernova explosion mechanisms and r-process sites, particularly in light of the GW170817 kilonova observation that provided the first evidence for r-process elements in binary neutron star mergers.
