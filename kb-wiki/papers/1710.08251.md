# Fermi liquid, clustering, and structure factor in dilute warm nuclear matter

> Properties of nuclear systems at subsaturation densities can be obtained from different approaches. We demonstrate the use of the density autocorrelation function which is related to the isothermal compressibility and, after integration, to the equation of state. This way we connect the Landau Fermi liquid theory well elaborated in nuclear physics with the approaches to dilute nuclear matter describing cluster formation. A quantum statistical approach is presented, based on the cluster decomposition of the polarization function. The fundamental quantity to be calculated is the dynamic structure factor. Comparing with the Landau Fermi liquid theory which is reproduced in lowest approximation, the account of bound state formation and continuum correlations gives the correct low-density result as described by the second virial coefficient and by the mass action law (nuclear statistical equilibrium). Going to higher densities, the inclusion of medium effects is more involved compared with other quantum statistical approaches, but the relation to the Landau Fermi liquid theory gives a promising approach to describe not only thermodynamic but also collective excitations and non-equilibrium properties of nuclear systems in a wide region of the phase diagram.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the Landau Fermi-liquid theory, which is well-established for dense nuclear matter near saturation, can be systematically extended to include cluster formation and bound states at subsaturation densities by using the dynamic structure factor and the density autocorrelation function as the central quantities, thereby connecting the Fermi-liquid approach with quantum statistical approaches that correctly reproduce the low-density virial expansion and nuclear statistical equilibrium.

## Method

The authors employ a quantum statistical approach based on the cluster decomposition of the polarization function (the Fourier transform of the van Hove time-dependent pair correlation function) within both the Matsubara thermodynamic Green function technique and the real-time non-equilibrium Green function technique. They relate the dynamic structure factor to the isothermal compressibility via the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, and then integrate the compressibility to obtain the equation of state. The approach is validated by comparing with the Landau Fermi-liquid theory in the degenerate limit and with the Beth-Uhlenbeck formula and mass action law in the low-density limit, using the quasiparticle approximation and the relativistic mean-field approximation as intermediate benchmarks.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density: \( n_{\rm sat} \approx 0.15 \) fm\(^{-3}\)
- Temperature range considered: \( T \lesssim 20 \) MeV
- The degeneracy factor \( g = 2 \) for pure neutron matter and \( g = 4 \) for isospin-symmetric matter (including spin and isospin)
- The non-relativistic effective mass \( m^* \) is defined as \( m^* = (d^2 \epsilon_p / dp^2) \) at \( p = k_{\rm F} \), where \( k_{\rm F} = (6 \pi^2 n / g)^{1/3} \)
- The incompressibility in Hartree-Fock approximation is given by \( K^{\rm HF} = \hbar^2 k_{\rm F}^2 / (3m) \left[ 1 + (m^* k_{\rm F} / (2 \pi^2)) \tilde{V}(k_{\rm F}) \right] \), where \( \tilde{V}(k_{\rm F}) \) is the angular-averaged interaction at the Fermi surface

## Context

This work extends the Landau Fermi-liquid theory developed by Landau (1956) and applied to nuclear systems by Migdal, which describes low-lying excitations via phenomenological Landau-Migdal parameters but cannot naturally incorporate cluster formation. It also extends the relativistic mean-field approach of Walecka and the generalized RMF approach of Typel (1999) and Ferreira (2012) that treat light clusters as new degrees of freedom. The quantum statistical approach of Roepke, Massen, and Schuck (RMS) and the Beth-Uhlenbeck formula for the second virial coefficient are reproduced as limiting cases. The work enables a unified description of thermodynamic properties, collective excitations, and non-equilibrium properties of warm nuclear matter across a wide region of the phase diagram, including applications to core-collapse supernovae and heavy ion collisions.
