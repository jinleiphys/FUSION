# Role of Fragment Higher Static Deformations in the Cold Binary Fission of $^{252}$Cf

> We study the binary cold fission of $^{252}$Cf in the frame of a cluster model where the fragments are born to their respective ground states and interact via a double-folded potential with deformation effects taken into account up to multipolarity $λ=4$. The preformation factors were neglected. In the case when the fragments are assumed to be spherical or with ground state quadrupole deformation, the $Q$-value principle dictates the occurence of a narrow region around the double magic $^{132}$Sn, like in the case of cluster radioactivity. When the hexadecupole deformation is turned on, an entire mass-region of cold fission in the range 138 - 156 for the heavy fragment arise, in agreement with the experimental observations. This fact suggests that in the above mentioned mass-region, contrary to the usual cluster radioactivity where the daughter nucleus is always a neutron/proton (or both) closed shell or nearly closed shell spherical nucleus, the clusterization mechanism seems to be strongly influenced by the hexadecupole deformations rather than the $Q$-value.

## Key claim

When hexadecupole (λ=4) deformations of the fragments are included in the cluster model, an entire mass region of cold fission for the heavy fragment in the range 138–156 emerges in agreement with experimental observations, whereas with only spherical or quadrupole-deformed fragments the Q-value principle restricts cold fission to a narrow region around the double-magic ¹³²Sn. The authors suggest that in this mass region, the clusterization mechanism is strongly influenced by hexadecupole deformations rather than the Q-value.

## Method

The authors used a deformation-dependent cluster model for the spontaneous cold (neutronless) binary fission of ²⁵²Cf, assuming the fragments are already separated and born in their ground states, with preformation factors neglected. The interaction barrier between the two fragments was calculated using a double-folded potential with the M3Y nucleon-nucleon effective interaction, incorporating ground-state deformations up to multipolarity λ=4 (quadrupole, octupole, and hexadecupole) taken from the macroscopic-microscopic model of Möller et al. (1995). Penetrabilities through the barrier were computed using the WKB approximation, and relative isotopic yields were obtained as the ratio of each channel's penetrability to the sum over all fragmentations.

## Key numbers

- The probability for neutronless fission of ²⁵²Cf is 0.0021 ± 0.0008.
- The potential barriers between fragments have a width of about 2 to 3 fm.
- The exit point from the potential barrier for the two fragments is at a center-of-mass distance between 16 and 17 fm.
- The M3Y pseudopotential strength used was Ĵ₀₀ = -276 MeV·fm³.
- The liquid-drop model parameters used were aₚ = aₙ = 0.5 fm and r₀ₚ = r₀ₙ = (R − 1/R)A⁻¹/³ fm with R = 1.28A¹/³ + 0.8A⁻¹/³ − 0.76.
- The hexadecupole-deformed mass region for the heavy fragment extends from A₂ = 138 to 156, with the most pronounced peaks at A₂ = 138, 140, 146, 150, and 154.
- When hexadecupole deformation is included, yields for A₂ = 143 are shifted uniformly by a factor of four (in log scale) compared to the case with only quadrupole deformation.

## Context

This work extends the cluster model approach previously applied to cluster radioactivity (Sandulescu and Greiner, 1992) to the cold binary fission of ²⁵²Cf. It directly addresses the experimental observations of two distinct cold fission mass regions reported by the Tübingen group (Gönnenwein et al., 1997; Mutterer et al., 1996): a narrow region around mass split 120/132 and a broader region from 96/156 to 114/138. The paper shows that the broader region arises from hexadecupole deformations rather than Q-value dominance, contrasting with cluster radioactivity where the daughter nucleus is always a closed-shell or nearly closed-shell spherical nucleus. The results enable future investigations of cold fission yields at finite excitation energy and suggest that preformation factors must be included to fully reproduce the spherical region around ¹³²Sn.
