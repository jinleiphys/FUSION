# Finite temperature and chemical potential in lattice QCD and its critical point

> We propose a method to study lattice QCD at finite temperature (T) and chemical potential (μ). We compare the method with direct results and with the Glasgow method by using n_f=4 QCD at Im(μ)\neq0. We locate the critical endpoint (E) of QCD on the Re(μ)-T plane. We use n_f=2+1 dynamical staggered quarks with semi-realistic masses on L_t=4 lattices. Our results are based on {\cal{O}}(10^3-10^4) configurations.

## Key claim

The paper claims to locate the critical endpoint (E) of QCD on the Re(μ)-T plane using a multi-parameter reweighting method, but the results are exploratory and based on semi-realistic quark masses on L_t=4 lattices, with the endpoint likely moving closer to the μ=0 axis when quark masses are reduced.

## Method

The authors propose a multi-parameter reweighting technique that generates an ensemble of QCD configurations at μ=0 and T_c, then reweights these configurations to finite μ and lower T using Ferrenberg-Swendsen reweighting in both the gauge coupling β and the chemical potential μ. They locate the critical endpoint by studying the complex Lee-Yang zeros of the partition function as a function of volume on L_t=4 lattices with n_f=2+1 dynamical staggered quarks (m_u,d=0.025, m_s=0.2), extrapolating the imaginary part of the zero β_0 to the infinite volume limit.

## Key numbers

- Critical endpoint baryonic chemical potential: μ_E = 725 ± 35 MeV
- Critical endpoint temperature: T_E = 160 ± 3.5 MeV
- Transition temperature at μ_B=0: T_c = 172 ± 3 MeV
- Quark chemical potential at endpoint: μ_end = 0.375(20) (in lattice units)
- Bare quark masses used: m_u,d = 0.025, m_s = 0.2 (lattice units)
- Lattice sizes: L_t=4, L_s=4,6,8; T=0 runs on 10^3·16 lattices
- Number of configurations: 14000 (L_s=4), 3600 (L_s=6), 840 (L_s=8)
- (R_0 · m_π) = 0.73(6), indicating quark masses at least four times physical values

## Context

This work extends earlier model-based predictions of a QCD critical point (e.g., crude models with m_s=∞ suggesting μ_E ≈ 700 MeV) by providing the first ab initio lattice QCD calculation of the endpoint location. The method improves upon the Glasgow method (which suffered from overlap problems even with 20 million configurations) by reweighting transition configurations to transition ones rather than hadronic to transition ones. The results enable future lattice studies to extrapolate to the thermodynamic, chiral, and continuum limits, and suggest that the endpoint may be accessible at lower μ if quark masses are reduced toward physical values.
