# Nuclear surface diffuseness revealed in nucleon-nucleus diffraction

> Nuclear surface provides useful information on nuclear radius, nuclear structure as well as properties of nuclear matter. We discuss the relationship between the nuclear surface diffuseness and elastic scattering differential cross section at the first diffraction peak of high-energy nucleon-nucleus scattering as an efficient tool in order to extract the nuclear surface information from limited experimental data involving short-lived unstable nuclei. The high-energy reaction is described by a reliable microscopic reaction theory, the Glauber model. Extending the idea of the black sphere model, we find one-to-one correspondence between the nuclear bulk structure information and proton elastic scattering diffraction peak. This implies that we can extract both the nuclear radius and diffuseness simultaneously, using the position of the first diffraction peak and its magnitude of the elastic scattering differential cross section. We confirm the reliability of this approach by using realistic density distributions obtained by a mean-field model.

# Nuclear surface diffuseness revealed in nucleon-nucleon diffraction

## Key claim
The position of the first diffraction peak in high-energy proton-nucleus elastic scattering determines the nuclear radius, while the magnitude of the differential cross section at that peak determines the nuclear surface diffuseness, but this one-to-one correspondence holds only for medium-to-heavy nuclei whose density profiles can be approximated by a two-parameter Fermi distribution, and the method may fail for very weakly bound systems such as halo nuclei.

## Method
The authors performed Glauber model calculations in the optical-limit approximation (OLA) for proton-nucleus elastic scattering at incident energies of 325, 550, and 800 MeV, using two-parameter Fermi (2pF) density distributions with fixed root-mean-square radii but varying diffuseness parameters. They then tested the extraction procedure by treating Skyrme-Hartree-Fock + BCS density distributions of Ca, Ni, Zr, Sn, Yb, and Pb isotopes as "experimental data" and determining the 2pF radius and diffuseness parameters that reproduce the first diffraction peak position and magnitude.

## Key numbers
- The first peak position θ_M is independent of diffuseness parameter a for medium-to-heavy nuclei (A ≳ 70), with deviations appearing only for small A with large a = 0.7 fm.
- The extracted rms radius from the 2pF fit agrees with the HF+BCS rms radius to within less than 1% for all incident energies (325, 550, 800 MeV).
- The incident-energy dependence of the extracted diffuseness parameter a is small, at most ~0.005 fm between 325 and 800 MeV.
- The black sphere model estimate of the total reaction cross section deviates from the Glauber result by typically ~5% and at most ~10% for Pb isotopes with standard diffuseness a = 0.5–0.6 fm.
- The scattering angle estimated from the total reaction cross section via the black sphere relation differs from the true first peak position by only a few degrees.
- For neutron-rich 132Sn, the extracted proton and neutron diffuseness parameters are consistent across different energy pairs, while for stable 120Sn and 208Pb the extracted values scatter although rms radii converge within ~0.5%.

## Context
This work extends the black sphere model of Kohama et al. (2004, 2005, 2016) by incorporating nuclear surface diffuseness explicitly through the Glauber model, and builds on the observation by Amado et al. (1980) that the cross section at the first diffraction peak is enhanced with smaller diffuseness. The method enables extraction of both nuclear radius and diffuseness from limited forward-angle elastic scattering data, which is particularly relevant for short-lived unstable nuclei studied at radioactive ion beam facilities. The approach complements the work of Terashima et al. (2008) and Zenihiro et al. (2010) on neutron density extraction from proton scattering, and the systematic analyses of total reaction cross sections by Takechi et al. (2010, 2014), Minomo et al. (2011, 2012), Sumi et al. (2012), Horiuchi et al. (2012), and Watanabe et al. (2014). The separation of proton and neutron diffuseness using energy-dependent pp and pn cross sections follows the idea of Horiuchi et al. (2014, 2016).
