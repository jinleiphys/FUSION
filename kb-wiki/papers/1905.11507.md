# Superheavy nuclei in microscopic collective Hamiltonian approach: the impact of beyond mean field correlations on the ground state and fission properties

> The impact of beyond mean field effects on the ground state and fission properties of superheavy nuclei has been investigated in a five-dimensional collective Hamiltonian based on covariant density functional theory. The inclusion of dynamical correlations reduces the impact of the $Z=120$ shell closure and induces substantial collectivity for the majority of the $Z=120$ nuclei which otherwise are spherical at the mean field level (as seen in the calculations with the PC-PK1 functional). Thus, they lead to a substantial convergence of the predictions of the functionals DD-PC1 and PC-PK1 which are different at the mean field level. On the contrary, the predictions of these two functionals remain distinctly different for the $N=184$ nuclei even when dynamical correlations are included. These nuclei are mostly spherical (oblate) in the calculations with PC-PK1 (DD-PC1). Our calculations for the first time reveal significant impact of dynamical correlations on the heights of inner fission barriers of superheavy nuclei with soft potential energy surfaces, the minimum of which at the mean field level is located at spherical shape. These correlations affect the fission barriers of the nuclei, which are deformed in the ground state at the mean field level, to a lesser degree.

## Key claim

The inclusion of dynamical correlations via a five-dimensional collective Hamiltonian reduces the impact of the Z=120 shell closure and induces substantial collectivity in most Z=120 nuclei that are spherical at the mean field level, but the predictions of the PC-PK1 and DD-PC1 functionals remain distinctly different for N=184 nuclei even after including these correlations.

## Method

The authors performed triaxial relativistic mean field plus BCS (RMF+BCS) calculations constrained on axial Q20 and triaxial Q22 mass quadrupole moments, covering a grid of beta2 from 0 to 0.6 in steps of 0.05 and gamma from 0 to 60 degrees in steps of 6 degrees. They then constructed a five-dimensional collective Hamiltonian (5DCH) from these constrained calculations, with mass parameters from the cranking approximation, moments of inertia from the Inglis-Belyaev formula, and collective potentials including zero-point-energy (ZPE) corrections for vibrational and rotational motion. The fission barriers were computed by comparing the saddle and minimum of the collective energy surfaces, with a correction procedure using N_F=20 fermionic basis calculations at the mean field level to improve numerical accuracy.

## Key numbers

- The dynamical contribution to fission barrier height (E^{FB}_{dyn}) calculated with N_F=18 and N_F=20 differs by less than 20 keV
- For the DD-PC1 functional, modifications of fission barrier height by dynamical correlations are typically in the range of plus or minus 0.5 MeV, with exceptions for (Z=116, N=174), (Z=120, N=186), and (Z=116, N=184) where modifications approach or exceed 1 MeV
- Dynamical correlation energies at the saddles of inner fission barriers are around 5 MeV in all nuclei under study
- The Z=112-118, N=184 nuclei show E(2_1^+) approximately 1.0 MeV with low B(E2; 2_1^+ to 0_1^+) values in PC-PK1 calculations, indicating truly spherical character

## Context

This work extends the mean field studies of superheavy nuclei by Afanasjev et al. (2015) and the fission barrier calculations of Afanasjev et al. (2017) by incorporating beyond mean field correlations for the first time in a relativistic framework for these nuclei. The results show that dynamical correlations substantially modify the ground state deformations and fission barriers of nuclei with soft potential energy surfaces, particularly those spherical at the mean field level, while having limited impact on already deformed nuclei. This enables more reliable predictions for the island of stability and fission properties of superheavy elements, though the persistent differences between the PC-PK1 and DD-PC1 functionals for N=184 nuclei highlight the need for experimental data to discriminate between these predictions.
