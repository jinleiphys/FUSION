# Product units in gated recurrent units improve nuclear-mass prediction

> The prediction of masses of atomic nuclei using machine learning can complement theoretical models and advance the exploration of poorly known domains of the nuclear chart. We propose a machine learning technique based on gated recurrent units (GRU), which have demonstrated competitive performance in nuclear-mass prediction by exploiting long-term dependencies. By integrating multiplicative interactions and product-unit transformations within recurrent units, we report significant improvements in nuclear-mass prediction. Computations are performed in the complex domain to jointly capture amplitude and phase dynamics. For interpolation and temporal-extrapolation tasks based on the atomic mass evaluation (AME2016 and AME2020), the complex additive-multiplicative product-unit gated recurrent unit (AM-PU-GRU) model consistently achieves the lowest prediction errors, with an interpolation RMSE of 0.227 $\pm$ 0.004 MeV and an extrapolation RMSE of 0.179 $\pm$ 0.015 MeV. These results surpass other state-of-the-art machine learning models and also outperform the real-valued GRU baseline and product-unit ablation variants, while remaining robust to different theoretical priors, including WS4 and SEMF. Our findings establish complex-valued product-unit recurrent networks as a new benchmark for sequence-based nuclear-mass prediction.

## Key claim

The complex additive-multiplicative product-unit gated recurrent unit (AM-PU-GRU) model achieves the lowest prediction errors for nuclear-mass interpolation and temporal extrapolation tasks, but this performance is conditional on using WS4 or SEMF theoretical priors for target-nucleus initialization and on the specific sequence construction of five neighboring nuclides.

## Method

The authors constructed sequences of five neighboring nuclides for each target nucleus, using four experimentally measured neighbors and initializing the target with a theoretical mass model (WS4 or SEMF). They developed complex-valued extensions of gated recurrent units that incorporate multiplicative interactions and product-unit transformations, specifically the MI-PU-GRU and AM-PU-GRU architectures, where all weights, hidden states, and activations are complex-valued while gates remain real-valued. Models were trained on AME2016 and AME2020 datasets using MSE loss and the RAdam optimizer for 3000 epochs, with interpolation evaluated via random 70-15-15 splits and temporal extrapolation tested by training on AME2016 and evaluating on nuclei newly added in AME2020.

## Key numbers

* Interpolation RMSE of 0.227 ± 0.004 MeV for the complex AM-PU-GRU model on AME2020 data with WS4 prior (Experiment I)
* Extrapolation RMSE of 0.179 ± 0.015 MeV for the complex AM-PU-GRU model when trained on AME2016 and tested on AME2020-added nuclei with WS4 prior (Experiment II)
* Interpolation RMSE of 0.253 ± 0.003 MeV for the complex AM-PU-GRU model on the 5% hold-out from AME2016 in Experiment II
* Real-valued GRU baseline achieved interpolation RMSE of 0.242 ± 0.004 MeV (Experiment I) and extrapolation RMSE of 0.205 ± 0.018 MeV (Experiment II)
* Under SEMF initialization, the complex AM-PU-GRU achieved interpolation RMSE of 0.401 MeV (Experiment I) and extrapolation RMSE of 0.477 MeV (Experiment II)

## Context

This work extends the product-unit neural network approach of Durbin and Rumelhart (1989) and the complex-valued product-unit networks of Dellen et al. (2024) and Li et al. (2024) by integrating these multiplicative transformations into gated recurrent units for nuclear-mass prediction. The results improve upon prior recurrent architectures reported by Jalili et al. (2025), who achieved GRU interpolation RMSE of 0.459 MeV, and outperform the complex product-unit network (CPUN) of Dellen et al. (2024) which achieved 0.438 MeV. The approach enables more accurate predictions in poorly known regions of the nuclear chart and establishes complex-valued product-unit recurrent networks as a new benchmark for sequence-based nuclear-mass prediction, while the ablation study reveals that product-unit transformations are the key driver of improvement rather than multiplicative interactions alone.
