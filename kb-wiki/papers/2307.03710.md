# Thermal behavior as indicator for hyperons in binary neutron star merger remnants

> We provide the first comprehensive study of hyperons in neutron star mergers and quantify their specific impact. We discuss the thermal behavior of hyperonic equations of state~(EoSs) as a distinguishing feature from purely nucleonic models in the remnants of binary mergers using a large set of numerical simulations. Finite temperature enhances the production of hyperons, which leads to a reduced pressure as highly degenerate nucleons are depopulated. This results in a characteristic increase of the dominant postmerger gravitational-wave frequency by up to $\sim150$~Hz compared to purely nucleonic EoS models. By our comparative approach we can directly link this effect to the occurrence of hyperons. Although this feature is generally weak, it is in principle measurable if the EoS and stellar parameters of cold neutron stars are sufficiently well determined. Considering that the mass-radius relations of purely nucleonic and hyperonic EoSs may be indistinguishable and the overall challenge to infer the presence of hyperons in neutron stars, these findings are important as a new route to answer the outstanding question about hyperonic degrees of freedom in high-density matter.

## Key claim

Finite temperature enhances hyperon production in binary neutron star merger remnants, which reduces thermal pressure and increases the dominant postmerger gravitational-wave frequency by up to ~150 Hz compared to purely nucleonic equations of state, but this feature is generally weak and only measurable if the equation of state and stellar parameters of cold neutron stars are sufficiently well determined.

## Method

The authors performed general-relativistic smoothed particle hydrodynamics simulations of binary neutron star mergers using a large set of temperature- and composition-dependent equations of state, including nine hyperonic models and twenty nucleonic models. They compared simulations using the full temperature-dependent tables against simulations using the same equations of state restricted to zero temperature and supplemented with an ideal-gas treatment of thermal pressure with a thermal index of 1.75, which mimics the thermal behavior of purely nucleonic matter. The difference in the dominant postmerger gravitational-wave frequency between these two simulation sets isolates the thermal signature of hyperons.

## Key numbers

- Dominant postmerger gravitational-wave frequency increases by up to ~150 Hz for hyperonic equations of state compared to purely nucleonic models
- Maximum deviation of purely nucleonic models from a quadratic fit of postmerger frequency versus tidal deformability at 1.75 solar masses is 55 Hz, with an average deviation of 28 Hz
- A frequency shift of 100 Hz corresponds to a change in neutron star radius of about 250 m based on empirical relations
- For the hyperonic model R(DD2YDelta)1.2-1.1, the postmerger frequency from the full temperature-dependent simulation is 3.26 kHz versus 3.14 kHz from the ideal-gas treatment
- For the hyperonic model SFHOY, the postmerger frequency from the full temperature-dependent simulation is 3.60 kHz versus 3.46 kHz from the ideal-gas treatment
- The mass- and time-averaged thermal ideal-gas index for hyperonic models ranges from 0.99 (R(DD2YDelta)1.2-1.3) to 1.69 (DNS), while for nucleonic models it ranges from 1.52 (DSH Large SL) to 1.82 (TM1)
- Hyperon fraction in the remnant ranges from 0.003 (DNS, QMC-A) to 0.185 (R(DD2YDelta)1.2-1.1)

## Context

This work provides the first comprehensive study of hyperons in neutron star mergers, extending earlier comparisons of individual models by Sekiguchi (2011) and Radice (2016) that could not definitively attribute observational differences to hyperons. The study addresses the "hyperon puzzle", the tension between expected softening of the equation of state from hyperons and the observation of two-solar-mass neutron stars, by showing that even when mass-radius relations of cold neutron stars are indistinguishable between hyperonic and nucleonic models, thermal effects in merger remnants can reveal hyperonic degrees of freedom. This enables a new observational route to determine whether hyperons exist in high-density matter, complementing efforts from hypernuclear structure data (Feliciello 2015, Tamura 2013, Gal 2016) and scattering experiments (HADES 2016, STAR 2014, ALICE 2018-2023).
