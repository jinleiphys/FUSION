# Noncommuting conserved charges in quantum thermodynamics and beyond

> Thermodynamic systems typically conserve quantities ("charges") such as energy and particle number. The charges are often assumed implicitly to commute with each other. Yet quantum phenomena such as uncertainty relations rely on observables' failure to commute. How do noncommuting charges affect thermodynamic phenomena? This question, upon arising at the intersection of quantum information theory and thermodynamics, spread recently across many-body physics. Charges' noncommutation has been found to invalidate derivations of the thermal state's form, decrease entropy production, conflict with the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis, and more. This Perspective surveys key results in, opportunities for, and work adjacent to the quantum thermodynamics of noncommuting charges. Open problems include a conceptual puzzle: Evidence suggests that noncommuting charges may hinder thermalization in some ways while enhancing thermalization in others.

## Key claim

Noncommuting conserved charges challenge fundamental thermodynamic expectations, including the form of thermal states, entropy production, and the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis, but evidence suggests they may both hinder and enhance thermalization in different ways, leaving a conceptual puzzle about their net effect.

## Method

The authors survey theoretical results from quantum information theory and many-body physics, analyzing how noncommuting charges affect thermodynamic phenomena such as thermal-state derivations, microcanonical ensembles, resource theories, and the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis. They examine the non-Abelian thermal state (NATS) form ρ_NATS ∝ e^{-β(H - Σ_a μ_a Q_a)}, approximate microcanonical (AMC) subspaces, and complete passivity arguments, while also discussing experimental realizations with trapped-ion chains.

## Key numbers

- The average relative entropy between a subsystem state from an AMC subspace and the NATS is bounded as ⟨D(ρ^(ℓ) || ρ_NATS)⟩_ℓ ≤ θ/√N + θ', where N is the number of degrees of freedom and θ, θ' are N-independent constants (Yunger Halpern et al., 2016)
- The Heisenberg model Hamiltonian H_Heis^tot = Σ_j Σ_{α=x,y,z} (σ_{+α}^(j) σ_{-α}^{(j+1)} + h.c.) = Σ_j σ⃗^(j) · σ⃗^(j+1) transports three noncommuting spin-component charges locally while conserving them globally
- In the infinite-N limit, charge densities commute: lim_{N→∞} (1/N²)[Q_a^tot, Q_{a'}^tot] = 0 for all a, a' (Balian and Balazs, 1987)
- Experimental demonstration used 6–21 trapped ions in a linear Paul chain, with two qubits forming the system and the rest forming the environment (Kranzl et al., 2023)

## Context

This Perspective extends early work by Jaynes (1957) on the maximum-entropy principle with noncommuting observables and by Balian and Balazs (1987) on microcanonical ensembles for noncommuting charges. It builds on quantum-information-theoretic thermodynamics results from Lostaglio (2014), Yunger Halpern (2016, 2018), and Guryanova et al. (2016), and connects to many-body physics via the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis (Deutsch, 1991; Srednicki, 1994; Rigol et al., 2008). The work enables re-examination of every thermodynamic and chaotic phenomenon under noncommuting charges, with open problems including whether noncommuting charges ultimately hinder or enhance thermalization.
