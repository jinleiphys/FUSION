# Resonances in 28Si+28Si. I

> A molecular model developed for resonances observed in medium light heavy-ion collisions is described. At high spins in 28Si+28Si (oblate-oblate system), a stable dinuclear configuration is found to be equator-equator touching one. The normal modes around the equilibrium are investigated. These modes are expected to be the origin of a large number of resonances observed. Furthermore, due to the axially asymmetric shape of the stable configuration of 28Si+28Si, the system rotates preferentially around the axis with the largest moment of inertia, which gives rise to wobbling motion ($K$-mixing). Energy spectra for the normal modes and for the extended model including the wobbling motion are given.

## Key claim
The paper claims that the sharp high-spin resonances observed in $^{28}\mathrm{Si}+{}^{28}\mathrm{Si}$ scattering originate from a stable dinuclear molecular configuration (equator-equator touching) whose triaxial shape induces wobbling motion ($K$-mixing), but this is presented as a theoretical model whose detailed comparison with experimental data is deferred to a subsequent paper.

## Method
The authors construct a dinuclear molecular model for two oblate $^{28}\mathrm{Si}$ nuclei, defining seven degrees of freedom: the relative vector and the Euler angles of each nucleus in a rotating molecular frame. They derive the classical kinetic energy, quantize it, and separate the Hamiltonian into rotational, internal, and Coriolis terms. The equilibrium configuration is found by inspecting the multi-dimensional potential energy surface, and normal modes around this minimum are solved using a harmonic approximation. For the triaxial equilibrium, the rotational spectrum is obtained by diagonalizing the asymmetric rotator Hamiltonian, effectively incorporating $K$-mixing without explicit Coriolis terms.

## Key numbers
* The stable dinuclear configuration for the $^{28}\mathrm{Si}+{}^{28}\mathrm{Si}$ system is the equator-equator touching one, due to the oblate deformation of $^{28}\mathrm{Si}$.
* The experimental resonances in $^{28}\mathrm{Si}+{}^{28}\mathrm{Si}$ were observed at $\theta_{\mathrm{cm}}=90^\circ$ in the laboratory energy range $E_{\mathrm{lab}}=101\ \mathrm{MeV}$ to $128\ \mathrm{MeV}$, with broad bumps of about $2\ \mathrm{MeV}$ width.
* Spin assignments for these bumps were $J=34 \sim 42$, corresponding to grazing partial waves.
* Within each bump, several sharp peaks were found with total widths of about $150\ \mathrm{keV}$.
* The level density of sharp resonances in these systems is over one per MeV.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous molecular model for the $^{24}\mathrm{Mg}+{}^{24}\mathrm{Mg}$ system (Uegaki, 1989, 1993, 1994), where the stable configuration is pole-to-pole due to prolate deformations, to the oblate-oblate $^{28}\mathrm{Si}+{}^{28}\mathrm{Si}$ system. It contrasts with the Band Crossing Model (BCM) used for lighter systems like $^{12}\mathrm{C}+{}^{12}\mathrm{C}$ and $^{16}\mathrm{O}+{}^{16}\mathrm{O}$, and with earlier theoretical works by Maass and Broglia that could not reproduce the observed level density. The model enables future theoretical analyses of decay properties and detailed comparisons with experimental data from Beck (2000) and Nouicer (1999), which are promised in a succeeding paper.
