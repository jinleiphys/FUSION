# Statistical analysis of a dynamical multifragmentation path

> A microcanonical multifragmentation model (MMM) is used for investigating whether equilibration really occurs in the dynamical evolution of two heavy ion collisions simulated via a stochastic mean field approach (SMF). The standard deviation function between the dynamically obtained freeze-out fragment distributions corresponding to the reaction $^{129}$Xe+$^{119}$Sn at 32 MeV/u and the MMM ones corresponding to a wide range of mass, excitation energy, freeze-out volume and nuclear level density cut-off parameter shows a unique minimum. A distinct statistically equilibrated stage is identified in the dynamical evolution of the system.

# Statistical analysis of a dynamical multifragmentation path

## Key claim

The dynamically obtained freeze-out fragment distributions from a stochastic mean field simulation of the reaction 129Xe+119Sn at 32 MeV/u can be reproduced by a microcanonical multifragmentation model (MMM) with a unique set of parameters, indicating that a statistically equilibrated stage exists in the dynamical evolution; however, this equilibration occurs at an earlier time (around 140-150 fm/c) and at a smaller freeze-out volume (V/V0 = 3.4) than the dynamical freeze-out time (240 fm/c) where fragments are fully formed.

## Method

The authors used a stochastic mean field (SMF) approach to simulate central collisions of 129Xe+119Sn at 32 MeV/u, extracting fragment distributions at the dynamical freeze-out time (240 fm/c). They then employed a microcanonical multifragmentation model (MMM) with a Metropolis-type simulation to generate statistical fragment distributions, varying the source parameters (mass A, excitation energy E, freeze-out volume V/V0, and level density cut-off tau) over a four-dimensional grid. The best fit between dynamical and statistical results was found by minimizing an error function that compares bound mass, bound charge, number of intermediate mass fragments (IMF), fragment excitation energy, and the three largest charges per event.

## Key numbers

- The reaction studied is 129Xe+119Sn at 32 MeV/u, with only very central collisions considered.
- The dynamical freeze-out time is 240 fm/c; the equilibrium freeze-out time is estimated at 140-150 fm/c.
- The global minimum of the error function occurs at A = 210, Z = 87, V/V0 = 3.4, E = 5.7 MeV/u, tau = infinity.
- The minimum error function values are: 0.15 for tau = 12 MeV, 0.11 for tau = 16 MeV, and 0.06 for tau = infinity.
- Dynamical results: <A_bound> = 199, <Z_bound> = 84, <epsilon_IMF> = 4.3 MeV/u, <Z_max1> = 41.95, <Z_max2> = 22.5, <Z_max3> = 13.3.
- Statistical results at the minimum: <A_bound> = 199.03, <Z_bound> = 83.74, <epsilon_IMF> = 4.21 MeV/u, <Z_max1> = 42.34, <Z_max2> = 24.35, <Z_max3> = 11.4.
- The dynamical events have <(V_tilde/V0)_IMF> = 9.08; the statistical events have <(V_tilde/V0)_IMF> = 4.93.
- The Coulomb propagation time from the equilibrium freeze-out volume to the dynamical freeze-out volume is around 100 fm/c.

## Context

This work extends the line of research comparing statistical multifragmentation models to dynamical simulations and experimental data, specifically building on the stochastic mean field approach of Colonna et al. (2004) and the microcanonical multifragmentation model of Raduta and Raduta (1997, 2000, 2002). The study addresses the long-standing question of whether the fragment distributions observed in heavy ion collisions truly reflect a statistically equilibrated source, or whether dynamical effects dominate. The key advance is the identification of a specific time window (140-150 fm/c) where statistical equilibrium is reached, intermediate between the onset of spinodal decomposition and the final fragment configuration. This enables future studies to use the extracted freeze-out volume (V/V0 = 3.4) and excitation energy (5.7 MeV/u) as constraints for locating the system in the nuclear matter phase diagram, an effort noted as being in progress by Piantelli et al. (2005) and Parlog et al. (2005).
