# Pygmy Resonances and Neutron Skins

> Motivated by a recent experiment, the distribution of electric dipole strength in the neutron-rich 68Ni isotope was computed using a relativistic random phase approximation with a set of effective interactions that - although well calibrated - predict significantly different values for the neutron-skin thickness in 208Pb. The emergence of low-energy "Pygmy" strength that exhausts about 5-8% of the energy weighted sum rule (EWSR) is clearly identified. In addition to the EWSR, special emphasis is placed on the dipole polarizability. In particular, our results suggest a strong correlation between the dipole polarizability of 68Ni and the neutron-skin thickness of 208Pb. Yet we find a correlation just as strong and an even larger sensitivity between the neutron-skin thickness of 208Pb and the fraction of the dipole polarizability exhausted by the Pygmy resonance. These findings suggest that the dipole polarizability may be used as a proxy for the neutron skin.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the dipole polarizability of \({}^{68}\)Ni is strongly correlated with the neutron-skin thickness of \({}^{208}\)Pb, and that the fraction of the dipole polarizability exhausted by the Pygmy resonance shows an even larger sensitivity to that neutron-skin thickness, suggesting the dipole polarizability may serve as a proxy for the neutron skin. However, the correlation is demonstrated only within a specific set of relativistic mean-field models, and the authors note that the fraction of the energy-weighted sum rule (EWSR) exhausted by the Pygmy resonance is a meager 5-8%, while its contribution to the dipole polarizability can reach 20-25%.

## Method

The electric dipole (E1) response of \({}^{68}\)Ni was computed using a relativistic random phase approximation (RPA) built on self-consistent mean-field ground states derived from a Lagrangian density with nonlinear meson interactions. Two families of effective interactions (NL3 and FSUGold) were tuned by varying the isovector parameters \(\Lambda_{\rm v}\) and \(g_{\rho}\) while keeping the symmetry energy fixed at about 26 MeV at a baryon density of about 0.1 fm\(^{-3}\), generating a range of predictions for the neutron-skin thickness of \({}^{208}\)Pb. The dipole strength distribution was then separated into low-energy (Pygmy) and high-energy (Giant) regions using a transition energy of \(\omega_t = 11.25\) MeV, and various moments of the distribution (including the dipole polarizability \(\alpha_D\)) were computed.

## Key numbers

* The Pygmy resonance in \({}^{68}\)Ni exhausts about 5-8% of the energy-weighted sum rule (EWSR) across the models studied.
* The dipole polarizability \(\alpha_D\) of \({}^{68}\)Ni ranges from 3.887 fm\(^3\) (FSU with \(\Lambda_{\rm v}=0.04\)) to 4.716 fm\(^3\) (NL3 with \(\Lambda_{\rm v}=0.00\)).
* The neutron-skin thickness of \({}^{208}\)Pb ranges from 0.166 fm (NL3 with \(\Lambda_{\rm v}=0.04\)) to 0.286 fm (FSU with \(\Lambda_{\rm v}=0.00\)).
* The slope of the symmetry energy at saturation density \(L\) ranges from 46.607 MeV (NL3 with \(\Lambda_{\rm v}=0.04\)) to 118.189 MeV (NL3 with \(\Lambda_{\rm v}=0.00\)).
* The total \(m_1\) moment (EWSR) for \({}^{68}\)Ni varies only slightly, from 276.857 fm\(^2\)MeV (FSU with \(\Lambda_{\rm v}=0.04\)) to 284.548 fm\(^2\)MeV (NL3 with \(\Lambda_{\rm v}=0.00\)), consistent with the model-independent TRK sum rule of about 14.8 \((NZ/A)\) fm\(^2\)MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Piekarewicz (2006) and Carbone et al. (2010) that linked the Pygmy dipole resonance to the neutron-skin thickness and the slope of the symmetry energy, and it directly addresses a claim by Reinhard and Nazarewicz (2010) that the neutron skin is strongly correlated with the dipole polarizability but weakly correlated with low-energy dipole strength. The results suggest that measurements of the dipole polarizability in neutron-rich nuclei like \({}^{68}\)Ni could provide a complementary constraint on the neutron-skin thickness of \({}^{208}\)Pb, which is the target of the Lead Radius Experiment (PREx) at Jefferson Laboratory.
