# The E2/M1 Ratio, a Status Report

> Recent determinations of the E2/M1 ratio are reviewed. Many aspects of these studies have been questioned. Here we focus on the selection of a database, methods used in the extraction of this ratio, and interpretation(s) of the results.

# The E2/M1 Ratio, a Status Report

## Key claim
The E2/M1 ratio extracted from pion photoproduction data is fairly model-independent when a consistent database is used, but the numerical value depends strongly on which experimental datasets are included in the fit; the apparent disagreement between groups (values ranging from about -1.5% to -3%) is primarily database-dependent rather than model-dependent, though unresolved discrepancies between cross section measurements from different laboratories remain.

## Method
The paper reviews multipole analyses of pion photoproduction data from multiple groups (Mainz, LEGS, RPI, VPI, BNL), comparing fits to various subsets of the existing database. The E2/M1 ratio is defined as Im(E1+^{3/2})/Im(M1+^{3/2}) at the energy where Re(M1+^{3/2}) goes to zero (the K-matrix pole), and is also examined at the T-matrix pole via residue ratios. The authors test the validity of approximating the ratio from a polynomial fit to polarized π⁰p cross sections and compare results across different multipole solutions.

## Key numbers
- The VPI group initially reported an E2/M1 ratio of -1.5 ± 0.5% (no units, dimensionless ratio)
- A forced fit to preliminary LEGS Σ data gave -2.9%
- The joint LEGS photoproduction and Compton scattering analysis gave -2.85 ± 0.34 ± 0.21%
- Mainz group(s) find values of about -2.5%
- LEGS and RPI groups find values closer to and slightly above -3%
- The difference between the polynomial approximation R(π⁰p) and the exact E2/M1 ratio is 0.2% or less for Mainz or SM95 solutions, about 0.3% for BNL solutions, and about 0.5% for RPI solutions
- The RPI fit (searching only s- and p-waves) predicts LEGS π⁰p Σ data at 322.6 and 333.55 MeV with χ²/data significantly less than unity
- For fits excluding older datasets, the T-matrix pole ratio has a modulus of about 0.06 and phase of about -125°

## Context
This status report reviews work following the Baryons '92 conference, where the VPI group (Workman, 1992) and RPI group (DMW, 1991) found values near -1.5%, in contrast to later Mainz (Beck, 1997; HDT, 1997), LEGS (Sandorfi, 1997), and RPI (1997) analyses yielding larger magnitudes (-2.5% to -3%). The paper extends discussions from earlier multipole analyses and addresses the database dependence highlighted by comparisons between Bonn, Mainz, and BNL cross section measurements. It enables future work by clarifying that the K-matrix pole ratio is stable given consistent data, while the T-matrix pole ratio (about 0.06, -125°) is even more stable across models, and by identifying the need to resolve cross section discrepancies between laboratories and to better connect quark-model predictions with phenomenological resonance properties.
