# Fourier coefficients of noninterdependent collective motions in heavy-ion collisions

> We present a scenario in heavy-ion collisions where different modes of collective motions are noninterdependent, driven by factorized actions in the created nuclear medium. Such physics mechanisms could each dominate at a distinct evolution stage, or coexist simultaneously. If the probability of particle emission is modulated by each nondependent collective motion with a single-harmonic Fourier expansion, the particle azimuthal distribution should be the product of all these expansions. Consequently, nonleading cross terms between collectivity modes appear, and their contributions to experimental observables could be significant. In particular, we argue that the chiral magnetic effect (CME) and elliptic flow can develop separately, with their convolution affecting the observable that is sensitive to the shear-induced CME. We will use the event-by-event anomalous-viscous fluid dynamics model to illustrate the effects of this scenario. Besides giving insights into searches for the CME, we also propose feasible experimental tests based on conventional flow harmonics, and demonstrate the emergence of nonleading cross terms with a multiphase transport model.

# Fourier coefficients of noninterdependent collective motions in heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

If different collective motions in heavy-ion collisions (such as the chiral magnetic effect and elliptic flow) develop independently via factorized actions, the particle azimuthal distribution should be described by the product of single-harmonic Fourier expansions rather than their sum, producing nonleading cross terms that can significantly affect experimental observables. The authors argue that this scenario is more self-consistent than the standard linear Fourier expansion, but note that the difference between the true and observed coefficients is negligible for many harmonics and depends on the relative magnitudes of the contributing modes.

## Method

The authors derive analytic relations connecting the Fourier coefficients from a standard linear expansion ($a_n$, $v_n$) to the "true" coefficients from a factorized product expansion ($\tilde{a}_n$, $\tilde{v}_n$), focusing on the coupling between $\tilde{a}_1$ (CME-induced charge separation), $\tilde{a}_3$ (shear-induced CME), and $v_2$ (elliptic flow). They test these relations using the Event-by-Event Anomalous-Viscous Fluid Dynamics (EBE-AVFD) model for 30-40% Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200$ GeV with $5.8 \times 10^7$ events, and the multi-phase transport (AMPT) model for 20-50% Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 14.6$ GeV.

## Key numbers

- $5.8 \times 10^7$ events of Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200$ GeV in the 30-40% centrality range were analyzed with EBE-AVFD
- The chirality charge density parameter was set to $n_5/s = 0.1$, the same as used in the siCME study by the same group
- The fit function $a_1(v_2) = a_1(0) \times (1 + C v_2)$ returned parameter $C$ close to $-1$ for all particle species ($\pi^+$, $K^+$, $p$, $\pi^-$, $K^-$, $\bar{p}$) in EBE-AVFD simulations
- Particle selection used rapidity range $|y| < 1$ and transverse momentum range $0.2 < p_T < 2$ GeV/$c$
- AMPT simulations were performed for Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 14.6$ GeV in the 20-50% centrality range

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for understanding collective flow and the chiral magnetic effect (CME) in heavy-ion collisions, building on the hydrodynamic description of elliptic flow (hydro, 2000s) and the CME mechanism (CME, 2008). It directly addresses the shear-induced CME (siCME) proposal (siCME, 2022) and provides an alternative interpretation of the $a_3$ observable that was previously attributed solely to siCME. The authors note that concerns about factorization have been raised from the perspective of nonflow (Ollitrault, 2013; Lim, 2019; Kikola, 2012) or decorrelation (Bozek, 2018; Barbosa, 2021), but none involved noninterdependent collective motions. The work enables experimental tests using conventional flow harmonics at RHIC and the LHC to distinguish between the factorized and linear Fourier descriptions, and provides demodulation formulas to extract the true physics coefficients from experimental observables.
