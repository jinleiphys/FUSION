# Constraining heavy quark energy loss using $B$ and $D$ meson measurements in heavy ion collision at RHIC and LHC energies

> In this work, we calculate energy loss of heavy quark (charm and bottom) due to elastic collisions and gluon radiation in hot/dense medium. The collisional energy loss has been obtained using QCD calculations. The radiative energy loss is calculated using reaction operator formalism and generalized dead cone approach. We rederive the energy loss expression using same assumptions as generalized dead cone approach but obtain slightly different results. We also improve the model employed to calculate path length and the system evolution. The nuclear modification factors $R_{AA}$ including shadowing and energy loss are evaluated for $B$ and $D$ mesons and are compared with the measurements in PbPb collision at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 2.76 TeV and with the D meson and Heavy flavour (HF) electrons measurements in AuAu collision at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 200 GeV. The radiative energy loss calculated by reaction operator formalism added with collisional energy loss describes the RHIC HF electron suppression in high $p_{T}$ range. It also describes the LHC measurement of $B$ meson suppression but overestimates the suppression of $D$ meson. The radiative energy loss from generalized dead cone approach describes the charm suppression at both RHIC as well as LHC energies and requires energy loss due to collisions to be added in order to describe the bottom suppression at LHC.

## Key claim

The paper shows that no single combination of radiative and collisional energy loss models simultaneously describes all measured nuclear modification factors for both charm and bottom hadrons at RHIC and LHC energies; the generalized dead cone approach (AJMS) describes charm suppression without collisional energy loss, while bottom suppression at LHC requires both radiative and collisional energy loss added together, and the DGLV reaction operator formalism plus collisional loss describes RHIC heavy flavor electrons and LHC B mesons but overestimates D meson suppression at LHC.

## Method

The authors calculated heavy quark (charm and bottom) production cross sections to NLO in pQCD using CT10 parton densities with Peterson fragmentation, then computed collisional energy loss using the Peigne and Peshier (PP) formalism and radiative energy loss using both the DGLV reaction operator formalism and the generalized dead cone approach (AJMS), including a rederivation that yields a corrected AJMS expression. They incorporated nuclear shadowing via EPS09 NLO parameter sets and modeled QGP evolution with an isentropic cylindrical expansion using lattice QCD equation of state, calculating path lengths from nuclear geometry and averaging energy loss over the temperature evolution to obtain nuclear modification factors R_AA for B and D mesons.

## Key numbers

- Charm quark mass m_c = 1.27 GeV, bottom quark mass m_b = 4.65 GeV
- Peterson fragmentation parameters: epsilon_c = 0.016, epsilon_b = 0.0012
- Strong coupling constant alpha_s = 0.3 used in collisional energy loss calculations
- Initial temperature T_0 = 0.303 GeV for 0-10% centrality AuAu at RHIC sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV
- Initial temperature T_0 = 0.481 GeV for 0-20% centrality PbPb at LHC sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV
- QGP lifetime tau_f = 3.0 fm/c for RHIC 0-10% centrality, tau_f = 6.0 fm/c for LHC 0-20% centrality
- Average path length L = 5.63 fm for RHIC 0-10% centrality, L = 5.62 fm for LHC 0-20% centrality
- Radiative energy loss of bottom quark by AJMS starts dominating collisional energy loss at quark energy above 11 GeV at RHIC and above 10 GeV at LHC
- DGLV radiative energy loss of bottom quark remains below collisional energy loss up to 25 GeV at RHIC and up to 22 GeV at LHC

## Context

This work extends prior heavy quark energy loss calculations using the DGLV reaction operator formalism (Gyulassy, Levai, Vitev, 2000-2001; Djordjevic, Gyulassy, Levai, Vitev, 2005; Wicks, 2007) and the generalized dead cone approach AJMS (Abir, Jamil, Mustafa, Srivastava, 2012), while improving the path length and QGP evolution model compared to those earlier works. The results enable future constraints from upcoming high luminosity PbPb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 5 TeV, which will measure heavy quarks in wider kinematic ranges to better distinguish between energy loss models.
