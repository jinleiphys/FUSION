# Dispersion-theoretical analysis of the electromagnetic form factors of the nucleon: Past, present and future

> We review the dispersion-theoretical analysis of the electromagnetic form factors of the nucleon. We emphasize in particular the role of unitarity and analyticity in the construction of the isoscalar and isovector spectral functions. We present new results on the extraction of the nucleon radii, the electric and magnetic form factors and the extraction of $ω$-meson couplings. All this is supplemented by a detailed calculation of the theoretical uncertainties, using bootstrap and Bayesian methods to pin down the statistical errors, while systematic errors are determined from variations of the spectral functions. We also discuss the physics of the time-like form factors and point out further issues to be addressed in this framework.

## Key claim

The paper argues that dispersion-theoretical analyses of nucleon electromagnetic form factors consistently yield a small proton charge radius (around 0.84 fm), which was historically at odds with the then-accepted "large" radius from electron scattering and electronic Lamb shift measurements, and that this discrepancy (the "proton radius puzzle") has now been resolved in favor of the small radius from dispersion theory.

## Method

The authors review and extend the dispersion-theoretical framework where nucleon form factors are expressed as unsubtracted dispersion integrals of spectral functions built from unitarity and analyticity. The isoscalar spectral function includes contributions from the two-pion continuum (generating the rho meson), the omega and phi mesons, the K-bar-K and pi-rho continua, and additional effective poles; the isovector spectral function similarly includes the two-pion continuum plus effective poles. The fits use electron-proton scattering data (including the recent PRad data at extremely low momentum transfer), neutron form factor data from light nuclei, and time-like form factor data, with statistical uncertainties determined via bootstrap and Bayesian methods and systematic uncertainties from varying the number of effective poles and the spectral function inputs.

## Key numbers

* Proton charge radius: \(r_E^p = 0.838^{+0.005}_{-0.004}{}^{+0.004}_{-0.003}\) fm (from the most recent analysis Lin:2021umk, with statistical and systematic errors)
* Proton magnetic radius: \(r_M^p = 0.847 \pm 0.004 \pm 0.004\) fm (from the same analysis)
* Neutron charge radius squared: \((r_E^n)^2 = -0.117^{+0.007}_{-0.011}\) fm\(^2\) (from Belushkin:2006qa)
* Neutron magnetic radius: \(r_M^n = 0.862^{+0.009}_{-0.008}\) fm (from Belushkin:2006qa)
* The dipole form factor parameter: \(G_{\rm dip}(Q^2) = (1 + Q^2 / 0.71\;{\rm GeV}^2)^{-2}\)
* The muonic hydrogen measurement gave \(r_E^p = 0.84184(67)\) fm (Pohl:2010zza), differing by 5 sigma from the then-CODATA value
* The updated CODATA value is \(r_E^p = 0.8414(19)\) fm

## Context

This review extends the dispersion-theoretical analyses of the Karlsruhe group (Hohler:1976ax), the Bonn-Mainz group (Mergell:1995bf, Hammer:2003ai), and the Belushkin-Hammer-Meissner work (Belushkin:2006qa), incorporating the high-precision MAMI-C data (Bernauer:2010wm) and the recent PRad data (Xiong:2019umf). The improved isovector spectral functions from Hoferichter:2016duk, based on Roy-Steiner equations for pion-nucleon scattering, are used as input. The work enables precise extraction of nucleon radii and form factors, resolves the proton radius puzzle in favor of the small radius, and provides a framework for analyzing future low-momentum-transfer electron scattering experiments and time-like form factor measurements.
