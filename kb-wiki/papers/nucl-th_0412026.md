# Model for Restoration of Heavy-Ion Potentials at Intermediate Energies

> Three types of microscopic nucleus-nucleus optical potentials are constructed using three patterns for their real and imaginary parts. Two of these patterns are the real $V^H$ and imaginary $W^H$ parts of the potential which reproduces the high-energy amplitude of scattering in the microscopic Glauber-Sitenko theory. Another template $V^{DF}$ is calculated within the standard double-folding model with the exchange term included. For either of the three tested potentials, the contribution of real and imaginary patterns is adjusted by introducing two fitted factors. An acceptable agreement with the experimental data on elastic differential cross-sections was obtained for scattering the $^{16, 17}$O heavy-ions at about hundred Mev/nucleon on different target-nuclei. The relativization effect is also studied and found that, to somewhat, it improves the agreement with experimental data.

# Model for Restoration of Heavy-Ion Potentials at Intermediate Energies

## Key claim
The authors propose that microscopic nucleus-nucleus optical potentials constructed from three combinations of high-energy approximation (HEA) and double-folding (DF) pattern potentials, with only two fitted normalization factors each, can reproduce elastic scattering data for oxygen isotopes at around 100 MeV/nucleon, though the agreement is described as "acceptable" rather than perfect and the method is presented as a proof of concept rather than a definitive solution.

## Method
Three types of microscopic optical potentials were constructed: type A using the HEA real part V^H and imaginary part W^H; type B using the double-folding real part V^DF with the HEA imaginary part W^H; and type C using V^DF for both real and imaginary parts. The real HEA potential V^H was obtained via inverse Fourier transform of the Glauber-Sitenko phase, while V^DF was calculated using the standard double-folding model with M3Y-type effective interactions including exchange terms and density dependence. For each potential type, only two free normalization factors N_r and N_im were fitted to experimental elastic differential cross-section data for 16O and 17O projectiles on various target nuclei at laboratory energies of 1503 MeV and 1435 MeV respectively.

## Key numbers
- 16O + 40Ca at E_lab = 1503 MeV: U^B_opt = V^DF + i1.32W^H; U^C_opt = V^DF + i0.88V^DF
- 16O + 208Pb at E_lab = 1503 MeV: U^B_opt = V^DF + iW^H; U^C_opt = V^DF + i0.6V^DF
- 17O + 60Ni at E_lab = 1435 MeV: U^C_opt = 0.6V^DF + i0.6V^DF
- 17O + 90Zr at E_lab = 1435 MeV: U^B_opt = 0.6V^DF + i0.9W^H; U^C_opt = 0.6V^DF + i0.5V^DF
- 17O + 120Sn at E_lab = 1435 MeV: U^B_opt = 0.5V^DF + i0.9W^H; U^C_opt = 0.5V^DF + i0.5V^DF
- 17O + 208Pb at E_lab = 1435 MeV: U^B_opt = 0.5V^DF + i1.3W^H; U^C_opt = 0.5V^DF + i0.8V^DF
- 16O + 90Zr at E_lab = 1503 MeV: U^A_opt = 1.13V^H + iW^H
- The HEA calculations are valid for scattering angles theta < 5.5 degrees

## Context
This work extends the microscopic approach to heavy-ion optical potentials developed in earlier studies by Lukyanov and collaborators (2003) that used the Glauber-Sitenko high-energy approximation to generate surface-equivalent Woods-Saxon potentials. It contrasts with the standard semi-microscopic approach (Satchler and Love 1979; Knyazkov and Shevchenko 1990) where only the real part is calculated microscopically via double-folding while the imaginary part is taken in a phenomenological Woods-Saxon form with three or more adjustable parameters. The method enables future studies to use microscopic pattern potentials with only two fitted parameters instead of the six or more required for phenomenological Woods-Saxon potentials, and the authors note that the relativization effect somewhat improves agreement with experimental data for 16O + 40Ca at 1503 MeV.
