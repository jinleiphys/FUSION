# Constructing Inverse Potentials from Scattering Phase Shifts using Physics-Informed Neural Networks: Application to Neutron-Alpha Scattering

> We develop a physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) framework for the inverse scattering problem in nuclear physics and apply it to the $P_{3/2}$ partial wave of neutron-alpha elastic scattering. The radial potential is represented by a feed-forward network whose output is multiplied by a Gaussian envelope, embedding the finite-range condition directly into the architecture rather than through a soft penalty term. This distinction proves essential: without the envelope, the optimizer produces potentials with non-vanishing tails and the resulting phase shifts remain inconsistent with the data regardless of training duration, demonstrating that hard structural constraints are indispensable for physically meaningful solutions to nuclear inverse problems. Phase shifts are generated at each scattering energy by numerically integrating the variable-phase equation with a fourth-order Runge-Kutta scheme, making the entire pipeline end-to-end differentiable.Training converges stably to a loss near $3\times10^{-4}$ and recovers a smooth, purely attractive central potential with a well depth of $-60.47$~MeV. Adding the centrifugal barrier to the learned potential reveals a well-defined barrier-well structure that naturally accounts for the $P_{3/2}$ resonance. The extracted resonance parameters, $E_{r} = 0.95$~MeV and $Γ_{r} = 0.78$~MeV, together with the P-wave effective-range parameters, are in good agreement with expected values. A leave-one-out analysis confirms that the reconstruction is stable against the removal of any single data point. These results establish physics-guided machine learning as a reliable route to potential reconstruction from nuclear scattering data.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that embedding the finite-range condition as a hard architectural constraint (a Gaussian envelope) rather than a soft penalty term is essential for obtaining physically meaningful inverse potentials from scattering phase shifts using physics-informed neural networks; without this constraint, the optimizer produces potentials with non-vanishing tails and phase shifts that remain inconsistent with the data regardless of training duration.

## Method

The radial potential for the $P_{3/2}$ partial wave of neutron-alpha elastic scattering is represented by a feed-forward neural network whose output is multiplied by a Gaussian envelope with scale $R = 3.0$ fm, enforcing the finite-range condition architecturally. Phase shifts are computed at each scattering energy by numerically integrating the variable-phase equation using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta scheme, making the entire pipeline end-to-end differentiable via automatic differentiation. The network is trained on 22 phase-shift values from Satchler et al. (1968) using the Adam optimizer with a composite loss function that includes data fidelity, smoothness regularization, finite-range reinforcement, and a low-energy threshold constraint.

## Key numbers

- Training loss converges to approximately $3\times10^{-4}$ after 6000 epochs
- Recovered central potential has a well depth of $-60.47$ MeV
- Effective potential (including centrifugal barrier) has minimum $[r_{\rm min}, V_{\rm min}] = [1.71\ \text{fm}, -13.60\ \text{MeV}]$ and barrier peak $[r_{\rm CB}, V_{\rm CB}] = [4.98\ \text{fm}, +2.03\ \text{MeV}]$
- Extracted resonance parameters: $E_{r} = 0.95$ MeV and $\Gamma_{r} = 0.78$ MeV
- All 22 residuals between model and experimental phase shifts lie within $\pm 1.4^{\circ}$; mean-square residual is $0.5^{\circ}$
- Integration grid: $r \in [0.01, 10.0]$ fm with step size $\Delta r = 0.01$ fm; halving $\Delta r$ to 0.005 fm changed phase shifts by less than $0.01^{\circ}$

## Context

This work extends prior inverse scattering efforts by the same group using genetic algorithms with a reference potential approach for systems including alpha-carbon-12, nucleon-deuteron, neutron-proton, proton-proton, and alpha-deuteron scattering (Awasthi et al., 2024, 2025; Sharma et al., 2025, 2026). The PINN framework provides a gradient-based alternative that overcomes the computational intensity and lack of analytical gradients in metaheuristic methods, while also improving upon standard phenomenological optical models (Hodgson, 1994) and R-matrix theory (Wigner, 1947; Descouvemont, 2010) by avoiding bias from assumed functional forms. The approach enables reliable potential reconstruction from nuclear scattering data and can be extended to other partial waves, spin-orbit coupling, and more complex nuclear systems.
