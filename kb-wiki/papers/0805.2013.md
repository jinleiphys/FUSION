# CN-Cycle Solar Neutrinos and Sun's Primordial Core Metalicity

> We argue that it may be possible to exploit neutrinos from the CN cycle and pp chain to determine the primordial solar core abundances of C and N at an interesting level of precision. Such a measurement would allow a comparison of the Sun's deep interior composition with it surface, testing a key assumption of the standard solar model (SSM), a homogeneous zero-age Sun. It would also provide a cross-check on recent photospheric abundance determinations that have altered the once excellent agreement between the SSM and helioseismology. As further motivation, we discuss a speculative possibility in which photospheric abundance/helioseismology puzzle is connected with the solar-system metal differentiation that accompanied formation of the gaseous giant planets. The theoretical relationship between core C and N and the 13N and 15O solar neutrino fluxes can be made more precise (and more general) by making use of the Super-Kamiokande and SNO 8B neutrino capture rates, which calibrate the temperature of the solar core. The primordial C and N abundances can then be obtained from these neutrino fluxes and from a product of nuclear rates, with little residual solar model dependence. We describe some of the recent experimental advances that could allow this comparison to be made (theoretically) at about the 9% level, and note that this uncertainty may be reduced further due to ongoing work on the S-factor for 14N(p,gamma). The envisioned measurement might be possible in deep, large-volume detectors using organic scintillator, e.g., Borexino or SNO+

## Key claim

The authors argue that measuring CN-cycle solar neutrinos (from 13N and 15O beta decay) could determine the primordial solar core abundances of carbon and nitrogen at about the 9% theoretical precision level, provided the 8B neutrino flux measured by SNO and Super-Kamiokande is used to calibrate the solar core temperature and thus reduce environmental uncertainties from the standard solar model.

## Method

The authors use logarithmic partial derivatives of neutrino fluxes with respect to standard solar model input parameters (solar properties, metal abundances, and nuclear S-factors) from the BPS08(AGS) solar model to decompose the 13N neutrino flux into environmental, nuclear, and primordial C+N abundance contributions. They then derive a scaling relation that expresses the 13N flux in terms of the measured 8B flux (which acts as a core thermometer) and a product of nuclear rates, with the constant K(13,8)=0.608 chosen to minimize residual environmental uncertainties from the 11 varied solar and abundance parameters.

## Key numbers

- Predicted 13N neutrino flux: (2.93 +0.91 -0.82) x 10^8 /cm^2 s from the BPS08(GS) standard solar model
- Predicted 15O neutrino flux: (2.20 +0.73 -0.63) x 10^8 /cm^2 s from the BPS08(GS) standard solar model
- Predicted 17F neutrino flux: (5.82 +/- 3.04) x 10^6 /cm^2 s from the BPS08(GS) standard solar model
- BPS08(GS) 8B flux prediction: 5.95 x 10^6 /cm^2 s; BPS08(AGS) prediction: 4.72 x 10^6 /cm^2 s
- SNO salt-phase 8B flux measurement: 4.94 +/- 0.21 (stat) +0.38 -0.34 x 10^6 /cm^2 s
- Super-Kamiokande combined best-fit 8B flux: 4.91 (unbinned) and 4.86 (binned) x 10^6 /cm^2 s
- The constant K(13,8) relating 13N and 8B fluxes is 0.608
- The 13N flux dependence on C and N abundances is (X(12C)/X(12C)_SSM)^0.874 x (X(14N)/X(14N)_SSM)^0.142, giving a 1.016 power overall (2% steeper than linear)
- Estimated net environmental uncertainty in phi(13N) from varying 11 SSM parameters: 7.5%
- Theoretical precision for the C and N abundance determination: approximately 9%

## Context

This work extends the standard solar model program of Bahcall and collaborators (Bahcall 2000, 2004, 2005) and Turck-Chieze and collaborators, which previously used solar neutrino fluxes primarily to test neutrino oscillations. The paper directly addresses the conflict between helioseismology and the revised photospheric abundances from Asplund (2005), which lowered the solar metallicity from Z=0.0169 (Grevesse and Sauval 1998) to Z=0.0122 and worsened agreement with sound-speed profiles. The authors also discuss a speculative connection to planetary metal differentiation, building on earlier two-zone models by Castro (2007) and accretion scenarios by Winnick (2005). A measurement of CN-cycle neutrinos would enable the first experimental test of the CN cycle in massive main-sequence stars and provide a cross-check on photospheric abundance determinations independent of atmospheric modeling.
