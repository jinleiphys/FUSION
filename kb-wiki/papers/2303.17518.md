# Constraining a relativistic mean field model using neutron star mass-radius measurements I: Nucleonic models

> Measurements of neutron star mass and radius or tidal deformability deliver unique insight into the equation of state (EOS) of cold dense matter. EOS inference is very often done using generalized parametric or non-parametric models which deliver no information on composition. In this paper we consider a microscopic nuclear EOS model based on a field theoretical approach. We show that current measurements from NICER and gravitational wave observations constrain primarily the symmetric nuclear matter EOS. We then explore what could be delivered by measurements of mass and radius at the level anticipated for future large-area X-ray timing telescopes. These should be able to place very strong limits on the symmetric nuclear matter EOS, in addition to constraining the nuclear symmetry energy that determines the proton fraction inside the neutron star.

## Key claim
Current NICER and gravitational wave observations primarily constrain the symmetric nuclear matter equation of state, while future large-area X-ray timing telescopes should be able to place very strong limits on both the symmetric nuclear matter EOS and the nuclear symmetry energy that determines the proton fraction inside neutron stars.

## Method
The authors used a relativistic mean field (RMF) model based on the FSU2R parametrization for nucleonic matter, where nucleons interact through exchange of sigma, omega and rho mesons. They performed Bayesian inference using nested sampling (MLFriends algorithm via UltraNest) to constrain the seven-dimensional EOS parameter space, applying priors informed by nuclear saturation properties and then updating with astrophysical data. For current constraints they used the mass of PSR J0740+6620 from radio timing, tidal deformabilities from GW170817 and GW190425, and NICER mass-radius measurements of PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620; for future constraints they simulated mass-radius measurements at the quality anticipated for STROBE-X and eXTP.

## Key numbers
- PSR J0740+6620 mass: 2.08 +/- 0.07 solar masses from radio timing (Fonseca 2021)
- Prior ranges for nuclear saturation properties: saturation density n0 from 0.15 to 0.17 fm^-3, binding energy E/A from 15.8 to 16.2 MeV, incompressibility K from 175 to 315 MeV, effective mass M*/m from 0.55 to 0.65, symmetry energy J between 25 and 38 MeV, slope of symmetry energy L between 30 and 86 MeV
- The K constraint was implemented as a super-Gaussian centered at 250 MeV with a width of 150 MeV, effectively restricting K to approximately 100 to 400 MeV
- After applying the K constraint, the maximum radius from the prior was under 15 km (compared to 16.18 km without the K constraint)
- The FSU2R model parameters used as central values: g_sigma^2 = 107.58, g_omega^2 = 182.39, g_rho^2 = 206.43, kappa = 3.0911, lambda = -0.001680, zeta = 0.024, Lambda_omega = 0.045
- Posterior median values from current observations: g_sigma^2 = 108.22 (+7.49/-7.32), g_omega^2 = 175.67 (+21.42/-17.64), g_rho^2 = 140.05 (+47.15/-44.72), kappa = 2.47 (+1.34/-1.37), lambda = 0.0035 (+0.0095/-0.0092), zeta = 0.0228 (+0.0047/-0.0048), Lambda_omega = 0.0225 (+0.0153/-0.0154)

## Context
This work extends the approach of Traversi (2020) and Malik (2022) who used RMF models for Bayesian inference of neutron star EOS, but uses the FSU2R parametrization developed by Tolos (2016, 2017) which was optimized to describe finite nuclei properties and two-solar-mass neutron stars with radii below 13 km. The methodology follows the Bayesian framework of Raaijmakers (2019, 2020, 2021) and Greif (2019). Unlike meta-model approaches using polytropes (Read 2009, Annala 2018), speed of sound parametrizations (Tews 2018, Annala 2020), or Gaussian processes (Landry 2020, Essick 2021), this microscopic approach preserves information about composition and automatically incorporates causality. The results enable future work on constraining the nuclear symmetry energy and proton fraction, and inform observational strategy for next-generation X-ray telescopes like eXTP and STROBE-X.
