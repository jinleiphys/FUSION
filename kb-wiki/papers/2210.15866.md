# $β^{+}$ Gamow-Teller strengths from unstable $^{14}$O via the $(d,{}^2\text{He})$ reaction in inverse kinematics

> For the first time, the $(d,{}^2\text{He})$ reaction was successfully used in inverse kinematics to extract the Gamow-Teller transition strength in the $β^{+}$ direction from an unstable nucleus. The nucleus studied was $^{14}$O, and the Gamow-Teller transition strength to $^{14}$N was extracted up to an excitation energy of 22 MeV. The measurement of the $(d,{}^2\text{He})$ reaction in inverse kinematics was made possible by the combination of an active target time projection chamber and a magnetic spectrometer. The data were used to test shell-model and state-of-the-art coupled cluster calculations. Shell-model calculations reproduce the measured Gamow-Teller strength distribution up to about 15 MeV reasonably well, after the application of a phenomenological quenching factor. Coupled-cluster calculation reproduces the full strength distribution well without such quenching, owing to the large model space, the inclusion of strong correlations, and the coupling of the weak interaction to two nucleons through two-body currents. This indicates that such calculations provide a very promising path for answering long-standing questions about the observed quenching of Gamow-Teller strengths in nuclei.

## Key claim

The (d,2He) reaction was successfully used in inverse kinematics for the first time to extract Gamow-Teller transition strengths in the beta+ direction from an unstable nucleus (14O), but the proportionality between cross section and B(GT) fails for the extremely weak ground-state transition (B(GT) = 2x10^-4), where the measured cross section was more than 100 times larger than expected.

## Method

A 150-MeV/nucleon 14O beam produced at the NSCL Coupled Cyclotron Facility impinged on a deuterium-filled active-target time-projection chamber (AT-TPC), which served as both target and tracking medium for the two outgoing protons from the (d,2He) reaction. The beam-like fragment (14N or its decay products) was detected in the S800 magnetic spectrograph for event triggering and identification. The Gamow-Teller strength distribution was extracted via a multipole decomposition analysis (MDA) of the differential cross sections, using angular distributions calculated with the Adiabatic Coupled-Channels Born Approximation code ACCBA and the ATTPCROOT simulation package.

## Key numbers

- Unit cross section: sigma_GT = 2.74 +/- 0.29 mb/sr, consistent with the value of 2.58 +/- 0.14 mb/sr from the 12C(d,2He) reaction in forward kinematics at 85 MeV/nucleon
- B(GT) for the 1+ state at 3.95 MeV in 14N: 2.73, known from beta decay
- Excitation energy range covered: up to 22 MeV in 14N
- Summed experimental GT strength up to 22 MeV: Sigma B(GT) = 3.69 +/- 0.75
- Coupled-cluster calculation summed GT strength: Sigma B(GT) = 3.71
- Shell-model calculation summed GT strength after quenching factor of 0.67: Sigma B(GT) = 4.02
- Excitation energy resolution: approximately 2.1 MeV FWHM near Q=0, approximately 1.2 MeV at other Q values
- 14O beam purity: 70%, with intensities between 0.2 to 0.7 million particles per second
- Deuterium gas pressure in AT-TPC: 530 Torr (+/- 0.5%), corresponding to 11.7 mg/cm^2 thickness

## Context

This work extends charge-exchange reaction capabilities to the beta+/electron-capture direction for unstable nuclei, complementing the earlier development of (p,n) reactions in inverse kinematics by Sasano et al. (2011) for the beta- direction. The results test shell-model calculations (which require a phenomenological quenching factor of 0.67) and state-of-the-art coupled-cluster calculations using the chiral potential 1.8/2.0 (EM) with three-nucleon forces and two-body currents, following the approach of Gysbers et al. (2019) that explained GT quenching in the A=14 multiplet. The method enables future tests of ab-initio theories and shell-model calculations for nuclei far from stability, which is important for constraining electron-capture rates in astrophysical scenarios such as core-collapse supernovae and neutron star crusts.
