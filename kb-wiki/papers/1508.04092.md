# Superconductivity and Superfluidity as Universal Emergent Phenomena

> Superconductivity (SC) or superfluidity (SF) is observed across a remarkably broad range of fermionic systems: in BCS, cuprate, iron-based, organic, and heavy-fermion superconductors, and superfluid helium-3 in condensed matter; in a variety of SC/SF phenomena in low-energy nuclear physics; in ultracold, trapped atomic gases; and in various exotic possibilities in neutron stars. The range of physical conditions and differences in microscopic physics defy all attempts to unify this behavior in any conventional picture. Here we propose a unification through the shared symmetry properties of the emergent condensed states, with microscopic differences absorbed into parameters. This, in turn, forces a rethinking of specific occurrences of SC/SF such as cuprate high-temperature superconductivity, which becomes far less mysterious when seen as part of a continuum of behavior shared by a variety of other systems.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that all fermionic superconductivity and superfluidity can be unified through shared dynamical symmetries of the emergent condensed states, with microscopic differences absorbed into parameters, rather than through any common microscopic mechanism. The authors hedge that this unification is a conjecture based on the observation that all cases examined thus far in condensed matter and nuclear physics derive from only two highest symmetries, SO(8) or Sp(6).

## Method

The authors survey phase diagrams across diverse systems (cuprates, iron-based, heavy-fermion, organic superconductors, superfluid helium-3, nuclear structure, ultracold atomic gases, and neutron stars) and identify common features such as proximity of superconducting and antiferromagnetic phases. They then argue that the collective Hilbert space for SC/SF states must have the same dynamical symmetries across fields, and identify candidate Lie algebras (SO(8), Sp(6), SO(7), SU(4)) that satisfy constraints from counting physical operators (pair creation/annihilation, spin, charge, and collective mode generators). They demonstrate quantitative agreement with data using SU(4) model calculations for cuprate phase diagrams and Fermion Dynamical Symmetry Model (FDSM) calculations for rare earth nuclear transition rates.

## Key numbers

- Minimum of 8 generators required for condensed matter SC/SF (creation and annihilation operators with spin degeneracy) and 12 generators for nuclear physics (counting total angular momentum J=0 and J=2 pairs)
- Minimum of 15 generators for condensed matter and 21 for nuclear physics when including antiferromagnetic, quadrupole, charge, and spin/angular momentum generators
- Upper limit of no more than 28 generators needed for previous applications to high-Tc SC and heavy nuclei
- Only four candidate algebras meet conditions with more than 10 and less than 35 generators: SO(8), Sp(6), SO(7), and SU(4)
- SU(4) model predicts that the derivative of the SC order parameter with respect to doping x diverges at x=0 (infinitesimal doping causes instability of undoped AF Mott state toward SC)
- SU(4) model predicts that the derivative of the AF order parameter with respect to doping x diverges at critical doping x_q (small change in doping causes divergence in AF correlations near x_q)

## Context

This work extends the dynamical symmetry approach previously applied in nuclear physics (FDSM, Guidry and Wu, 2001), condensed matter (Guidry, 2004), and high-Tc cuprates (Sun, 2006) to propose a universal classification of all fermionic SC/SF. It contradicts the conventional view that BCS theory, even generalized for unconventional pairing, can describe the diversity of SC/SF behavior, arguing instead that conventional BCS represents only a special limit where competing collective modes are neglected. The paper enables a rethinking of specific occurrences such as cuprate high-temperature superconductivity as part of a continuum rather than an anomaly, and suggests that future work can apply the SO(8) or Sp(6) dynamical symmetry framework to calculate observables across different fields.
