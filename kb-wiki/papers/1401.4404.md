# Comparison of Expanded Preequilibrium CEM Model with CEM03.03 and Experimental Data, FY2013

> Emission of light fragments (LF) from nuclear reactions is an open question. Different reaction mechanisms contribute to their production; the relative roles of each, and how they change with incident energy, mass number of the target, and the type and emission energy of the fragments is not completely understood. None of the available models are able to accurately predict emission of LF from arbitrary reactions. However, the ability to describe production of LF (especially at energies $\gtrsim 30$ MeV) from many reactions is important for different applications, such as cosmic-ray-induced Single Event Upsets (SEUs), radiation protection, and cancer therapy with proton and heavy-ion beams, to name just a few. The Cascade-Exciton Model (CEM) version 03.03 and the Los Alamos version of the Quark-Gluon String Model (LAQGSM) version 03.03 event generators in Monte Carlo N-Particle Transport Code version 6 (MCNP6) describe quite well the spectra of fragments with sizes up to $^{4}$He across a broad range of target masses and incident energies (up to $\sim 5$ GeV for CEM and up to $\sim 1$ TeV/A for LAQGSM). However, they do not predict the high-energy tails of LF spectra heavier than $^4$He well. Most LF with energies above several tens of MeV are emitted during the precompound stage of a reaction. The current versions of the CEM and LAQGSM event generators do not account for precompound emission of LF larger than $^{4}$He. The aim of our work is to extend the precompound model in them to include such processes, leading to an increase of predictive power of LF-production in MCNP6. Extending our models to include emission of fragments heavier than $^4$He at the precompound stage has already provided preliminary results that have much better agreement with experimental data.

## Key claim

Extending the Modified Exciton Model (MEM) in CEM03.03 to allow precompound emission of light fragments heavier than ⁴He (up to ²⁸Mg) provides dramatically improved agreement with experimental double-differential cross sections for intermediate-to-high-energy fragment spectra, though the work of fully parameterizing the γⱼ factor over a mesh of residual nuclei energy and mass number remains incomplete.

## Method

The authors expanded the precompound stage of the Cascade-Exciton Model (CEM) by modifying the MEM to emit 66 isotopes up to ²⁸Mg, adding code-wide crash protection via if-statements for divide-by-zero and square-root errors, and recalibrating the γⱼ emission probability factor for each fragment by fitting to experimental data for specific reactions. They compared the modified CEM03.03 results against unmodified CEM03.03 and experimental data for proton-induced reactions on ²⁷Al and natAg at several incident energies.

## Key numbers

- For 200 MeV p + ²⁷Al → ⁶Li, the modified MEM produced dramatically improved agreement with Machner et al. data at intermediate to high energies, where the unmodified CEM03.03 fell off sharply above ~25 MeV.
- For 190 MeV p + natAg, the modified MEM used γⱼ = 0.08 for ⁶Li and γⱼ = 0.2 for ⁷Li, with residual nucleus parameters E* = 68.6 ± 49.1 MeV, Z = 47.1 ± 0.7, A = 106.2 ± 0.5.
- For 300 MeV p + natAg, the modified MEM used γⱼ = 0.012 for ⁶Li and γⱼ = 0.01 for ⁷Li, with residual nucleus parameters E* = 83.4 ± 63.4 MeV, Z = 46.8 ± 0.8, A = 105.3 ± 1.2.
- For 480 MeV p + natAg, the modified MEM used γⱼ = 0.01 for ⁶Li and γⱼ = 0.01 for ⁷Li, with residual nucleus parameters E* = 83.4 ± 63.4 MeV, Z = 46.8 ± 0.8, A = 105.3 ± 1.2 (same table as 300 MeV case).
- The unmodified CEM03.03 predicts the high-energy tails of fragments heavier than ⁴He poorly; the evaporative peak matches well at energies below ~25 MeV, but the precompound contribution (MEM and coalescence) does not account for fragments heavier than ⁴He.

## Context

This work extends the CEM and LAQGSM event generators used in MCNP6, which previously described spectra of fragments up to ⁴He well but failed for heavier fragments at high energies. It builds on prior efforts including the 2002 CEM study of preequilibrium light fragment emission (CEM2k2f, never completed), the INCL+ABLA model modifications by Cugnon et al. (2013) for clusters up to A=12, and the Kalbach systematics (1988) for angular distributions. The expanded model enables better predictions for applications such as cosmic-ray-induced single event upsets, radiation protection, and proton/heavy-ion cancer therapy, and addresses the 2008-2010 IAEA Benchmark of Spallation Models recommendation.
