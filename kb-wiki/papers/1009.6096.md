# Can a supernova bang twice?

> The implications of a QCD phase transition at high temperatures and densities for core-collapse supernovae are discussed. For a strong first order phase transition to quark matter, various scenarios have been put forward in the literature. Here, detailed numerical simulations including neutrino transport are presented, where it is found that a second shock wave due to the QCD phase transition emerges shortly after bounce. It is demonstrated that such a supernova banging twice results in a second peak in the antineutrino spectrum. This second peak is clearly detectable in present neutrino detectors for a galactic supernova.

## Key claim

For a strong first-order QCD phase transition with a sufficiently low bag constant, a second shock wave emerges from the formation of quark matter in core-collapse supernova simulations, producing a second peak in the antineutrino spectrum that is clearly detectable in present neutrino detectors for a galactic supernova; the effect depends on the progenitor mass and the chosen bag constant, with the quark core appearing between 200 and 500 ms after bounce.

## Method

The authors performed full hydrodynamical simulations of core-collapse supernovae including neutrino transport for progenitor masses of 10 and 15 solar masses, using the Shen et al. equation of state for nuclear matter and adding the QCD phase transition via the MIT bag model. The phase transition to quark matter was implemented as a strong first-order transition, and the resulting dynamical evolution was tracked from bounce through the formation of a second shock wave and the subsequent antineutrino emission.

## Key numbers

- Maximum cold hybrid star masses: 1.56 solar masses for bag constant B^(1/4)=162 MeV, and 1.50 solar masses for B^(1/4)=165 MeV.
- With one-gluon exchange corrections (alpha_s=0.3) and bag constant B^(1/4)=155 MeV, the maximum mass increases to 1.67 solar masses.
- The quark core appears between 200 and 500 ms after bounce, depending on progenitor mass and bag constant.
- For a galactic supernova at 10 kpc distance, the second antineutrino peak is clearly visible in Super-Kamiokande and IceCube detectors, appearing at around 260 ms in the simulated data.
- The phase transition to the mixed phase can occur just slightly above normal nuclear matter density at temperature T=20 MeV for proton fraction Y_p=0.3.
- Nucleation timescales for strange quark matter are below the supernova timescale for surface tensions sigma < 20 MeV fm^(-2).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of QCD phase transitions in core-collapse supernovae by Takahara and Sato (1988) and Gentile et al. (1993) by including full neutrino transport, and contrasts with the results of Nakazato, Sumiyoshi, and Yamada (2008, 2010) who found no second shock wave for high initial masses of 100 solar masses. The simulations build on the earlier work of Sagert et al. (2008) and Fischer et al. (2010), and the detectability analysis follows Dasgupta et al. (2009). The results enable future observational searches for a second neutrino burst from galactic supernovae using Super-Kamiokande and IceCube, and motivate further studies of the QCD phase diagram at high densities relevant for astrophysical systems.
