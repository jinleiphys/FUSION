# Entanglement and Many-Body effects in Collective Neutrino Oscillations

> Collective neutrino oscillations play a crucial role in transporting lepton flavor in astrophysical settings, such as supernovae, where the neutrino density is large. In this regime, neutrino-neutrino interactions are important and simulations in the mean-field approximation show evidence for collective oscillations occurring at time scales much larger than those associated with vacuum oscillations. In this work, we study the out-of-equilibrium dynamics of a corresponding spin model using Matrix Product States and show how collective bipolar oscillations can be triggered by many-body correlations if appropriate initial conditions are present. We find entanglement entropies scaling at most logarithmically in the system size suggesting that classical tensor network methods could be efficient in describing collective neutrino dynamics more generally. These observation provide a clear path forward, not only to increase the accuracy of current simulations, but also to elucidate the mechanism behind collective flavor oscillations without resorting to the mean field approximation.

## Key claim

Collective bipolar neutrino oscillations can be triggered by many-body correlations even without vacuum mixing, but only in an intermediate regime of the energy asymmetry parameter \(0 < \delta_\omega/\mu \lesssim 1/4\); for negative asymmetries or large positive asymmetries the mean-field prediction of no flavor evolution is essentially recovered.

## Method

The author maps the two-flavor neutrino system with forward-scattering interactions onto an SU(2) spin model (a variant of the Lipkin-Meshov-Glick model) with two equally sized beams initialized in a fully polarized product state in the mass basis. Real-time evolution is simulated using a Matrix Product State (MPS) ansatz with a swap-network Trotter decomposition to handle all-to-all interactions, truncating singular values below \(10^{-10}\). The simulations are performed for systems up to \(N=128\) neutrinos without invoking the mean-field approximation.

## Key numbers

* For \(N=96\) neutrinos, strong flavor oscillations appear for \(0 < \delta_\omega \lesssim \mu\); for \(\delta_\omega < \mu/2\) the oscillations can lead to net flavor inversion in a beam.
* The time to reach the first minimum of the survival probability scales as \(\tau_F \propto \log(N)\) in the intermediate regime, compared to \(\tau_L \propto \sqrt{N}\) in the high-density limit \(\delta_\omega = 0\).
* The entanglement entropy scales at most logarithmically with system size, allowing MPS simulations with maximum bond dimension approximately \(N/2\) for systems with \(N \gtrsim 100\).
* A fixed time step \(\delta = 0.05\,\mu^{-1}\) was used for all simulations.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of collective neutrino oscillations that relied on mean-field approximations (e.g., Duan 2010, Izaguirre 2017) and addresses conflicting results about the role of entanglement: Friedland (2003, 2006) predicted vanishingly small many-body effects in the large-N limit, while Bell (2003) and Sawyer (2004) found substantial flavor evolution on fast time scales. The MPS approach enables first-principle simulations beyond mean field for systems up to \(N=128\), and the observed logarithmic entanglement scaling suggests that classical tensor network methods can efficiently describe collective neutrino dynamics more generally, providing a path toward more realistic simulations including spatial inhomogeneity.
