# The power of two: Assessing the impact of a second measurement of the weak-charge form factor of 208Pb

> [Background] Besides its intrinsic value as a fundamental nuclear-structure observable, the weak-charge density of 208Pb - a quantity that is closely related to its neutron distribution - is of fundamental importance in constraining the equation of state of neutron-rich matter. [Purpose] To assess the impact that a second electroweak measurement of the weak-charge form factor of 208Pb may have on the determination of its overall weak-charge density. [Methods] Using the two putative experimental values of the form factor, together with a simple implementation of Bayes' theorem, we calibrate a theoretically sound - yet surprisingly little known - symmetrized Fermi function, that is characterized by a density and form factor that are both known exactly in closed form. [Results] Using the charge form factor of 208Pb as a proxy for its weak-charge form factor, we demonstrate that using only two experimental points to calibrate the symmetrized Fermi function is sufficient to accurately reproduce the experimental charge form factor over a significant range of momentum transfers. [Conclusions] It is demonstrated that a second measurement of the weak-charge form factor of 208Pb supplemented by a robust theoretical input in the form of the symmetrized Fermi function, would place significant constraints on the neutron distribution of 208Pb and, ultimately, on the equation of state of neutron-rich matter.

# The power of two: Assessing the impact of a second measurement of the weak-charge form factor of 208Pb

## Key claim

Using only two experimental measurements of the weak-charge form factor of 208Pb at suitably chosen momentum transfers, combined with a symmetrized Fermi function parametrization, is sufficient to accurately reproduce the full form factor over a significant range of momentum transfers and to determine the charge radius with good precision, provided the second measurement is placed near the first diffraction maximum near q = 0.8 fm^{-1}.

## Method

The authors used the well-known charge form factor of 208Pb as a proxy for the weak-charge form factor, simulating a single PREX-like measurement at q1 = 0.5 fm^{-1} and a hypothetical second measurement at q2 = 0.8 fm^{-1}. They calibrated the two parameters (half-density radius c and surface diffuseness a) of the symmetrized Fermi function using Bayes' theorem with a Gaussian prior derived from mean-field model predictions, implementing a Metropolis Monte-Carlo algorithm to generate the posterior distribution of parameters.

## Key numbers

- The first simulated measurement point was at q1 = 0.5 fm^{-1} with F_ch(q1) = 0.210 and an assumed experimental uncertainty of Delta F_exp = 0.005
- The second simulated measurement point was at q2 = 0.8 fm^{-1} with F_ch(q2) = -0.0614 and the same assumed uncertainty of Delta F_exp = 0.005
- From two measurements, the extracted charge radius of 208Pb was R_ch = 5.504 +/- 0.045 fm, compared to the experimental value of R_ch = 5.5012 +/- 0.0013 fm (Angeli 2013)
- The extracted SFermi parameters were c = 6.655 +/- 0.081 fm and a = 0.514 +/- 0.066 fm
- The PREX measurement of the weak form factor at q = 0.475 fm^{-1} gave F_wk = 0.204 +/- 0.028 (exp) +/- 0.001 (model)
- The PREX determination of the neutron skin thickness of 208Pb was R_skin = 0.33^{+0.16}_{-0.18} fm

## Context

This work extends the PREX experiment (Abrahamyan 2012, Horowitz 2012), which measured the weak-charge form factor of 208Pb at a single momentum transfer and provided the first model-independent determination of the neutron skin thickness, albeit with large statistical uncertainty. The paper addresses the fundamental limitation that a single measurement can only constrain a linear combination of the radius and surface thickness parameters, as understood from the Bethe-Weizsacker liquid drop model. By demonstrating that two measurements with the symmetrized Fermi function can accurately reproduce the form factor, the work provides a statistical framework for optimizing the second measurement point for the approved PREX-II experiment and for future measurements at the planned MESA facility in Mainz. The symmetrized Fermi function itself, introduced by Sprung (1997), offers an analytic form factor that avoids the cusp at the origin present in the conventional Woods-Saxon parametrization.
