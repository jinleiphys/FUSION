# Modern Ab Initio Approaches and Applications in Few-Nucleon Physics with A \ge 4

> We present an overview of the evolution of ab initio methods for few-nucleon systems with A \ge 4, tracing the progress made that today allows precision calculations for these systems. First a succinct description of the diverse approaches is given. In order to identify analogies and differences the methods are grouped according to different formulations of the quantum mechanical many-body problem. Various significant applications from the past and present are described. We discuss the results with emphasis on the developments following the original implementations of the approaches. In particular we highlight benchmark results which represent important milestones towards setting an ever growing standard for theoretical calculations. This is relevant for meaningful comparisons with experimental data. Such comparisons may reveal whether a specific force model is appropriate for the description of nuclear dynamics.

## Key claim

This review traces the evolution of ab initio methods for few-nucleon systems with A ≥ 4, arguing that precise ab initio results, obtained with controlled, converged approximations, allow unambiguous tests of whether a given Hamiltonian (including two- and three-nucleon forces) correctly describes nuclear dynamics, but the authors note that an infinite number of phase-shift-equivalent potentials exist and only comparison with experiment for A > 3 observables can discriminate among them.

## Method

The paper categorizes ab initio methods by their formulation of the quantum mechanical many-body problem: Faddeev and Faddeev-Yakubovsky (FY) formalisms in both coordinate and momentum space, variational methods (including the no-core shell model), similarity transformation approaches (such as SRG and UCOM), and Monte Carlo techniques (including Green's function Monte Carlo). For each category, the authors describe the mathematical structure, the treatment of boundary conditions for bound and scattering states, and the channel truncation schemes required for practical calculations, with particular emphasis on the FY equations for the four-nucleon system where the wave function is decomposed into 18 components (12 K-class and 6 H-class) leading to two coupled equations after exploiting permutation symmetry.

## Key numbers

- 4He binding energy from FY calculations with the Nijmegen NN potential (no Coulomb): 25.03 MeV with full potential (98K+90H channels), compared to 24.55 MeV with interaction restricted to 3s1-3d1 and 1s0 partial waves (15K+9H channels)
- 4He binding energy from FY calculations with the Paris NN potential: 24.26 MeV (full potential) versus 23.63 MeV (restricted partial waves)
- 4He binding energy from FY calculations with the AV14 NN potential: 24.62 MeV (full potential) versus 23.77 MeV (restricted partial waves)
- For n-3H and p-3He scattering below three-body break-up threshold, achieving ~1% precision requires including all channels with j_x ≤ 4, j_y ≤ 4, and j_z ≤ 3 in a j-j coupling scheme
- Most sophisticated FY bound-state calculation included couplings to T=1 and T=2 isospin channels requiring 4200 K and 2000 H channels, with truncation at j_u ≤ 6, l_u ≤ 8, and sum of l_u ≤ 14

## Context

This review extends the earlier work of Faddeev (1961) on three-body equations and Yakubovsky (1967) on four-body generalizations, and documents the progression from early A=4 calculations in the late 1980s to modern precision benchmarks. The paper situates the FY momentum-space calculations of Kamada and Glöckle (1993) as the first rigorous four-nucleon bound-state results, and discusses how the Lorentz integral transform method of Efros and Leideman (1994) and the no-core shell model of Navratil and Vary (2000) enabled new classes of calculations. The work enables systematic benchmarking of different ab initio methods against each other and against experimental data to validate nuclear force models, including modern chiral potentials from Epelbaum and Machleidt (2011).
