# Octupole response and stability of spherical shape in heavy nuclei

> The isoscalar octupole response of a heavy spherical nucleus is analyzed in a semiclassical model based on the linearized Vlasov equation. The octupole strength function is evaluated with different degrees of approximation. The zero-order fixed-surface response displays a remarkable concentration of strength in the $1\hbarω$ and $3\hbarω$ regions, in excellent agreement with the quantum single-particle response. The collective fixed-surface response reproduces both the high- and low-energy octupole rsonances, but not the low-lying $3^{-}$ collective states, while the moving-surface response function gives a good qualitative description of all the main features of the octupole response in heavy nuclei. The role of triangular nucleon orbits, that have been related to a possible instability of the spherical shape with respect to octupole-type deformations, is discussed within this model. It is found that, rather than creating instability, the triangular trajectories are the only classical orbits contributing to the damping of low-energy octupole excitations.

# Octupole response and stability of spherical shape in heavy nuclei

## Key claim

The vanishing of eigenfrequencies associated with closed triangular nucleon orbits does not cause an instability of the spherical shape against octupole-type deformations in heavy nuclei; instead, these triangular trajectories are the only classical orbits that contribute to the Landau damping of low-energy octupole excitations. This conclusion holds for any mass number A, as the product κ_BM * R_3^0(0) is A-independent.

## Method

The isoscalar octupole response of a spherical nucleus with A=208 nucleons in a square-well potential (radius R = r_0 A^{1/3}, r_0 = 1.2 fm) was analyzed using a semiclassical model based on the linearized Vlasov equation. The octupole strength function was evaluated at three levels of approximation: zero-order fixed-surface (single-particle) response, collective fixed-surface response with a separable octupole-octupole residual interaction, and moving-surface response that allows the nuclear surface to vibrate according to the liquid-drop model. The coupling constant κ_3 was determined phenomenologically by requiring the high-energy octupole resonance peak to agree with experiment, yielding κ_3 ≈ 2κ_BM where κ_BM = -4π m ω_0^2 / (3 A R^4).

## Key numbers

- The zero-order single-particle octupole strength is concentrated in two regions around 8 MeV and 24 MeV, corresponding to the 1ℏω and 3ℏω quantum regions.
- The critical coupling strength for instability is κ_3cr = -1 / R_3^0(0) ≈ -3.14 × 10^{-5} MeV/fm^6.
- The self-consistent estimate of the octupole coupling is κ_BM ≈ -1 × 10^{-5} MeV/fm^6, and the phenomenological value used is κ_3 ≈ 2κ_BM.
- The surface tension parameter is σ ≈ 1 MeV fm^{-2}.
- The limit of the Fourier coefficient for triangular orbits is lim_{x→√3/2} Q^{(3)}_{∓1 ±3}(x) = -R^3/4.
- The zero-frequency real part of the single-particle response is Re R_3^0(0) = -r_0^6 A^3 / (8π ε_F).

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical Vlasov approach previously applied to lower multipolarities (Abrosimov, Dellafiore, Matera, 2000-2002) to the octupole channel, and directly addresses the speculation by Bohr and Mottelson (1975) that closed triangular orbits might cause octupole instability in nuclei beyond ²⁰⁸Pb. The moving-surface response reproduces the high-energy octupole resonance (HEOR), low-energy octupole resonance (LEOR), and a low-energy double hump in qualitative agreement with experiment (van der Woude, 1987) and RPA calculations (Liu and Brown, 1987; Lanza and Lombardo, 1999). The model enables a classical interpretation of Landau damping in the octupole channel and clarifies that the liquid-drop model instability condition for odd multipolarities remains the relevant criterion for shape instability.
