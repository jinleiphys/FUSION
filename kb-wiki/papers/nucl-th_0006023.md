# SU(6) breaking effects in the nucleon elastic electromagnetic form factors

> The effects of SU(6) breaking on the nucleon elastic form factors are investigated within the constituent quark model formulated on the light-front. It is shown that the kinematical SU(6) breaking caused by the Melosh rotations of the quark spins as well as the dynamical SU(6) breaking due to the mixed-symmetry component generated in the nucleon wave function by the spin-dependent terms of the quark-quark interaction, can affect both GEn(Q**2) and GMp(Q**2)/GMn(Q**2). The calculated GEn(Q**2) is found to be qualitatively consistent with existing data. while the calculations of GM(Q**2) based on the plus component of the current are found to be plagued by spurious effects related to the loss of the rotational covariance in the light-front formalism. These unwanted effects can be avoided by considering the transverse y-component of the current. In this way our light-front predictions are found to be consistent with the data on both GEn(Q**2) and GMp(Q**2)/GMn(Q**2). Finally, it is shown that a suppression of the ratio GEp(Q**2)/GMp(Q**2) with respect to the dipole-fit prediction can be expected in the constituent quark model provided the relativistic effects generated by the Melosh rotations of the constituent spins are taken into account.

## Key claim

The paper shows that both kinematical SU(6) breaking from Melosh rotations of quark spins and dynamical SU(6) breaking from mixed-symmetry components in the nucleon wave function are needed to reproduce nucleon elastic form factor data, but only about 65% of the experimental neutron charge radius can be explained without invoking non-vanishing constituent quark sizes or many-body currents.

## Method

The authors calculated the nucleon Sachs form factors G_E^n(Q^2) and G_M^p(Q^2)/G_M^n(Q^2) within the light-front constituent quark model, using nucleon wave functions from the one-gluon-exchange (OGE) quark potential model of Capstick and Isgur (1986) and the chiral quark potential model of Glozman et al. The form factors were computed from matrix elements of the one-body electromagnetic current, comparing results from the plus component and the transverse y-component of the current, and contrasting the full light-front calculation with the non-relativistic limit and the zitterbewegung approximation.

## Key numbers

- The experimental neutron charge radius is ⟨r⟩_n,exp^2 = -0.113 ± 0.005 fm^2
- The OGE model predicts a neutron charge radius of ⟨r⟩_n^2 = -0.0083 fm^2 when the mixed-symmetry S'-wave is included, which is about 7% of the experimental value
- The chiral model predicts ⟨r⟩_n^2 = -0.0090 fm^2 with the S'-wave included
- The average constituent quark transverse momentum ⟨p_⊥⟩ in the OGE model is 0.58 GeV with the S'-wave included, and 0.61 GeV in the chiral model
- The proton charge radius predicted by both models is approximately 0.32 fm, compared to the experimental value of about 0.8 fm
- The symmetric S-wave probability in the OGE model is P_S = 98.04%, the mixed-symmetry S'-wave probability is P_S' = 1.70%, and P- and D-waves are 0.05% and 0.21% respectively
- The SU(6) symmetry prediction for the magnetic form factor ratio is G_M^p/G_M^n = -3/2, while the experimental value is approximately μ_p/μ_n ≈ -1.46

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Simula (1999) by including the effects of mixed-symmetry wave functions from the OGE model of Capstick and Isgur (1986) and the chiral model of Glozman et al., going beyond the pure SU(6)-symmetric wave function assumption. The paper addresses the interpretation of the neutron charge radius raised by Isgur (1999) regarding zitterbewegung contributions, and shows that once spurious effects from the loss of rotational covariance in the light-front formalism are avoided by using the transverse y-component of the current, the constituent quark model predictions become consistent with data on both G_E^n(Q^2) and G_M^p(Q^2)/G_M^n(Q^2). The results enable further investigation of the observed deviation of the ratio μ_p G_E^p/G_M^p from unity at Jefferson Lab.
