# GW190814: Impact of a 2.6 solar mass neutron star on nucleonic equations of state

> Is the secondary component of GW190814 the lightest black hole or the heaviest neutron star ever discovered in a double compact-object system [R. Abbott et al., ApJ Lett., 896, L44 (2020)]? This is the central question animating this letter. Covariant density functional theory provides a unique framework to investigate both the properties of finite nuclei and neutron stars, while enforcing causality at all densities. By tuning existing energy density functionals we were able to: (a) account for a 2.6 Msun neutron star, (b) satisfy the original constraint on the tidal deformability of a 1.4 Msun neutron star, and (c) reproduce ground-state properties of finite nuclei. Yet, for the class of models explored in this work, we find that the stiffening of the equation of state required to support super-massive neutron stars is inconsistent with either constraints obtained from energetic heavy-ion collisions or from the low deformability of medium-mass stars. Thus, we speculate that the maximum neutron star mass can not be significantly higher than the existing observational limit and that the 2.6 Msun compact object is likely to be the lightest black hole ever discovered.

## Key claim

The authors show that a nucleonic equation of state can be tuned to produce a 2.6 solar mass neutron star while satisfying constraints from finite nuclei, NICER, and the original GW170817 tidal deformability limit; however, they find that such a stiff equation of state is inconsistent with constraints from heavy-ion collision flow data and the revised, tighter tidal deformability limit from GW170817, leading them to speculate that the 2.6 solar mass object is likely a black hole rather than a neutron star.

## Method

The authors employ covariant density functional theory, tuning the quartic isoscalar-vector self-interaction parameter ζ in the Lagrangian density to stiffen the symmetric-matter equation of state and produce a maximum neutron star mass of 2.6 solar masses. They construct a new energy density functional called "BigApple" by starting from the IUFSU functional, reducing the slope of the symmetry energy to obtain a neutron skin thickness of 0.15 fm for 208Pb, adjusting the incompressibility coefficient to 227 MeV, and re-tuning the scalar meson mass, saturation density, and binding energy at saturation to preserve agreement with binding energies and charge radii of 40Ca and 208Pb. Neutron star structure is computed by enforcing charge neutrality and chemical equilibrium with nucleons and leptons only, excluding exotic degrees of freedom.

## Key numbers

- Maximum neutron star mass of 2.600 solar masses predicted by the BigApple functional
- Tidal deformability of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: Λ₁.₄ = 717.3 (BigApple), compared to the revised GW170817 upper limit of Λ₁.₄ = 190⁺³⁹⁰₋₁₂₀ at 90% confidence
- Radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: R₁.₄ = 12.960 km (BigApple)
- Pressure at four times saturation density predicted by BigApple is nearly twice as large as the upper limit extracted from heavy-ion flow data
- Incompressibility coefficient K = 227.001 MeV for BigApple
- Symmetry energy slope L = 39.800 MeV for BigApple
- Neutron skin thickness of 208Pb: 0.151 fm (BigApple prediction)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Fattoyev et al. (2017) on covariant density functionals and neutron star properties, directly responding to the GW190814 discovery by Abbott et al. (2020) of a 2.6 solar mass compact object. The authors compare their BigApple functional against the IUFSU and FSUGarnet functionals from Fattoyev et al. (2017) and Chen et al. (2014), and find that the stiff equation of state required for a 2.6 solar mass neutron star conflicts with heavy-ion flow constraints from Danielewicz et al. (2002) and the revised tidal deformability limit from Abbott et al. (2018). The analysis supports the upper limit on maximum neutron star mass of about 2.17 solar masses suggested by Margalit and Metzger (2017) and is consistent with the 2.14⁺⁰.¹⁰₋₀.₀₉ solar mass neutron star observed by Cromartie et al. (2019).
