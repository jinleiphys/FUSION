# Temperatures and chemical potentials at kinetic freeze-out in relativistic heavy ion collisions from coarse grained transport simulations

> Using the UrQMD/coarse graining approach we explore the kinetic freeze-out stage in central Au + Au collisions at various energies. These studies allow us to obtain detailed information on the thermodynamic properties (e.g. temperature and chemical potential) of the system during the kinetic decoupling stage. We explore five relevant collision energies in detail, ranging from $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.4\,\mathrm{GeV}$ (GSI-SIS) to $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200\,\mathrm{GeV}$ (RHIC). By adopting a standard Hadron Resonance Gas equation of state, we determine the average temperature $\langle T \rangle$ and the average baryon chemical potential $\langleμ_{\mathrm{B}}\rangle$ on the space-time hyper-surface of last interaction. The results highlight the nature of the kinetic freeze-out as a continuous process. This differential decoupling is an important aspect often missed when summarizing data as single points in the phase diagram as e.g. done in Blast-Wave fits. We compare the key properties of the system derived by using our approach with other models and we briefly review similarities and differences.

## Key claim

The kinetic freeze-out in relativistic heavy ion collisions is a continuous process occurring over extended ranges of time, temperature, and baryon chemical potential, rather than a single point in the phase diagram as commonly assumed in Blast-Wave fits; the authors emphasize that representing freeze-out as a single point is an oversimplification that misses the differential decoupling of different parts of the system.

## Method

The authors used the UrQMD transport model in cascade mode to simulate central Au+Au collisions (0-5% centrality) at five center-of-mass energies from 2.4 GeV to 200 GeV. They employed a coarse-graining procedure to compute the average net-baryon current, energy density, and net-baryon density on a spatial grid with 0.8 fm resolution (1.0 fm at 200 GeV), then used a Hadron Resonance Gas equation of state to extract temperature and baryon chemical potential at each space-time point. The kinetic freeze-out coordinates were defined microscopically as the time and position of each hadron's last interaction (including both scatterings and strong decays), and these coordinates were matched to the corresponding coarse-grained cell values.

## Key numbers

* Five collision energies were explored: sqrt(s_NN) = 2.4, 4.5, 7.7, 19.6, and 200 GeV, covering GSI-SIS to RHIC energies.
* The spatial grid resolution was 0.8 fm for all energies except sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV, where 1.0 fm resolution was used.
* The decoupling stage duration was typically 15-20 fm/c (FWHM), corresponding to a damping rate Gamma(t_max)=30-40 MeV.
* The peak kinetic emission temperature did not rise above approximately 150 MeV even at the highest energies.
* At low collision energies (sqrt(s_NN) below 7 GeV), kinetic and chemical freeze-out temperatures differed by only 5-10 MeV; at energies above 7 GeV, the difference exceeded 40-50 MeV.
* At sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV, approximately 85% of kinetically frozen-out hadrons could be assigned coarse-grained cell values; at lower energies, this fraction exceeded 95%.

## Context

This work extends earlier coarse-graining studies by Endres et al. (2014-2015) and Inghirami et al. (2018), which were used for dilepton and photon production, by applying the method specifically to the kinetic freeze-out stage. The results contrast with Blast-Wave model fits by Abelev et al. (2008), Adamczyk et al. (2017), and Rode et al. (2018), which treat freeze-out as a single point in the phase diagram; the authors find systematically higher kinetic freeze-out temperatures than those Blast-Wave fits, particularly at high collision energy. The study enables future work incorporating a QGP/hydrodynamic stage and improved equations of state to better understand the differences between microscopic transport and phenomenological Blast-Wave approaches.
