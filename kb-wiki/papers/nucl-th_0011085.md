# Size-shrinking of deuterons in very dilute superfluid nuclear matter

## Key claim
Using the strong-coupling BCS approach, deuterons in superfluid nuclear matter first shrink by approximately 35% as density increases from zero before they begin expanding, with the minimum coherence length occurring at a density of about 0.036 fm⁻³.

## Method
The authors solved the BCS gap equation for symmetric nuclear matter using the Paris potential projected into the ³S₁ channel, with single-particle energies calculated from the same potential in the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) approximation. The coherence length (deuteron size) was computed from the pairing function in momentum space, defined as ξ² = ∫ d𝐫 r² |ψ(𝐫)|² / ∫ d𝐫 |ψ(𝐫)|², and evaluated both with free and BHF single-particle spectra.

## Key numbers
- Minimum coherence length occurs at density ρ ≈ 0.036 fm⁻³, corresponding to a deuteron radius of less than 2 fm and an interparticle distance of about 3 fm
- At vanishing density, the coherence length approaches the free deuteron radius
- The deuterons shrink by approximately 35% before expanding again
- The zero-density deuteron binding energy is 2μ₀ = -2.2 MeV
- The density at the minimum is approximately ρ/ρ₀ ≈ 1/20 relative to nuclear saturation density

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous strong-coupling BCS study of the BEC-BCS crossover in nuclear matter (Lombardo and Schuck, 2000) and confirms a general prediction by Pieri and Strinati (2000) that bound fermion pairs shrink with increasing density before expanding. The results suggest that deuteron Bose-Einstein condensation could occur in the far tails of heavy nuclei produced at exotic nuclear beam facilities, and that peripheral nuclear reactions or expanding nuclear matter in low-energy heavy-ion collisions might exhibit signatures of such condensation phenomena.
