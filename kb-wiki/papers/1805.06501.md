# Evolution of shell structure in exotic nuclei

> The atomic nucleus is a quantum many-body system whose constituent nucleons (protons and neutrons) are subject to complex nucleon-nucleon interactions that include spin- and isospin-dependent components. For stable nuclei, already several decades ago, emerging seemingly regular patterns in some observables could be described successfully within a shell-model picture that results in particularly stable nuclei at certain magic fillings of the shells with protons and/or neutrons: N,Z = 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126. However, in short-lived, so-called exotic nuclei or rare isotopes, characterized by a large N/Z asymmetry and located far away from the valley of beta stability on the nuclear chart, these magic numbers, viewed through observables, were shown to change. These changes in the regime of exotic nuclei offer an unprecedented view at the roles of the various components of the nuclear force when theoretical descriptions are confronted with experimental data on exotic nuclei where certain effects are enhanced. This article reviews the driving forces behind shell evolution from a theoretical point of view and connects this to experimental signatures.

## Key claim

The paper argues that shell evolution in exotic nuclei is driven primarily by the monopole component of the effective nucleon-nucleon interaction, with the tensor force playing a uniquely important role, and that this mechanism explains both the disappearance of traditional magic numbers and the emergence of new ones far from stability, though the authors note that the hypothetical "shell quenching" effect from diffuse density distributions remains a challenge for future experiments and is distinct from the monopole-driven evolution they describe.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical framework of shell evolution by decomposing the effective nucleon-nucleon interaction into monopole, pairing, and higher-multipole (primarily quadrupole) components, then analyzing how the monopole interaction shifts effective single-particle energies as a function of proton or neutron occupation numbers. They connect this formalism to experimental signatures such as the systematics of first 2⁺ excitation energies across isotopic chains, and illustrate the decomposition using shell-model calculations for Mg isotopes with the USDA and SDPF-M interactions.

## Key numbers

- The nucleon density in the nuclear interior is approximately 0.17 nucleons/fm³.
- The established magic numbers for stable nuclei are N,Z = 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126.
- Elevated first 2⁺ excitation energies are observed at N=16 (²⁴O), N=32 (⁵²Ca), N=34 (⁵⁴Ca), and N=40, indicating new or enhanced shell closures in exotic nuclei.
- For Mg isotopes, the monopole contribution to the ground-state energy increases in magnitude up to N=20, while the quadrupole-dominated "other terms" contribution peaks at N=12 (²⁴Mg) and is reduced by N=18.

## Context

This review extends the earlier work on monopole interactions by Bansal and French (1970s) and by Poves and Zuker (1981), and builds on the shell-evolution concept introduced by Otsuka (2005) to explain tensor-force monopole effects. The paper connects theoretical mechanisms to experimental signatures such as the 2⁺ systematics compiled by Pritychenko (2016), and enables predictions for shell structure in unexplored regions of the nuclear chart relevant to the r-process in neutron star mergers and supernovae.
