# High-precision evaluation of Wigner's d-matrix by exact diagonalization

> The precise calculations of the Wigner's d-matrix are important in various research fields. Due to the presence of large numbers, direct calculations of the matrix using the Wigner's formula suffer from loss of precision. We present a simple method to avoid this problem by expanding the d-matrix into a complex Fourier series and calculate the Fourier coefficients by exactly diagonalizing the angular-momentum operator $J_{y}$ in the eigenbasis of $J_{z}$. This method allows us to compute the d-matrix and its various derivatives for spins up to a few thousand. The precision of the d-matrix from our method is about $10^{-14}$ for spins up to $100$.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method to compute Wigner's d-matrix by expanding it into a complex Fourier series and calculating the Fourier coefficients via exact diagonalization of the angular-momentum operator \(J_y\) in the eigenbasis of \(J_z\), achieving an absolute error of about \(10^{-14}\) for spins up to 100; the method avoids the large-number problem that plagues direct evaluation of Wigner's formula, but the maximum absolute error scales as \(O(j^2 \times 10^{-14})\) for the d-matrix itself and \(O(j^k \times 10^{-14})\) for its \(k\)th-order derivative.

## Method

The authors express the Fourier coefficients of the d-matrix as inner products \(\langle j,m|j,\mu\rangle_y\) between eigenstates of \(J_z\) and eigenstates of \(J_y\). They construct the tridiagonal Hermitian matrix representing \(J_y\) in the \(J_z\) basis and diagonalize it using standard numerical routines (e.g., the ZHBEV subroutine of LAPACK) to obtain all eigenvectors and eigenvalues. Once the eigenvectors are known, all elements of the d-matrix for any angle \(\theta\) and all their derivatives are obtained from the Fourier series using the same set of coefficients, requiring only a single diagonalization per spin \(j\).

## Key numbers

* Absolute error of the d-matrix is about \(10^{-14}\) for spin \(j=100\) (maximum absolute error \(3.275 \times 10^{-14}\) at \(j=100\)).
* Maximum absolute error of the d-matrix scales as \(\Delta_{\mathrm{abs,max}} \approx (a j^2 + b) \times 10^{-14}\) with \(a = 2.568 \times 10^{-4}\) and \(b = 0.5758\) for \(j\) up to 100.
* Maximum absolute error of the first-order derivative is approximately \(j \times \Delta_{\mathrm{abs,max}}\).
* Relative error is \(\lesssim 10^{-10}\) within the central region defined by \(m^2 + n^2 - 2mn\cos\theta \leq j(j+1)\sin^2\theta\).
* For \(j=100\) and \(\theta=\pi/6\), the exact value of \(d_{\pm j,\mp j}^{j}(\pi/6)\) is \(3.974 \times 10^{-118}\).

## Context

This work extends the Fourier-series approach of Tajima (2015) by replacing symbolic computation of Fourier coefficients with direct numerical diagonalization, eliminating the need for formula-manipulation software. It improves upon recurrence-relation methods (Edmonds, 1957; Gumerov and Duraiswami, 2005) that suffer from numerical instability at high spin, and provides a clear error scaling that is competitive with or better than prior approaches for spins above 405. The method enables high-precision calculation of the d-matrix and its derivatives for spins up to a few thousand, which is directly applicable to quantum metrology (e.g., Fisher information calculations in Ramsey interferometry) and other fields requiring accurate rotation matrices for large angular momenta.
