# Quasielastic Lepton-Nucleus Scattering and the Correlated Fermi Gas Model

> The future neutrino research program will require improved precision. A major source of uncertainty is the interaction of neutrinos with nuclei that serve as targets for such experiments. Broadly speaking, this interaction often depends, e.g., for Charge-Current Quasi-Elastic (CCQE) scattering, on the combination of "nucleon physics" expressed by form factors and "nuclear physics" expressed by a nuclear model. It is important to get a good handle on both. This talk presents a fully analytic implementation of the Correlated Fermi Gas (CFG) Model for CCQE electron-nuclei and neutrino-nuclei scattering. The implementation is used to compare separately form factors and nuclear model effects for both electron-carbon and neutrino-carbon scattering data.

# Quasielastic Lepton-Nucleus Scattering and the Correlated Fermi Gas Model

## Key claim
The Correlated Fermi Gas (CFG) model can be implemented fully analytically for charge-current quasielastic lepton-nucleus scattering, enabling separate comparison of nucleon form factor effects and nuclear model effects. For electron-carbon scattering, nuclear model differences between RFG and CFG are large while form factor differences are small; for flux-averaged neutrino-carbon scattering, the opposite holds, form factor differences are large but nuclear model differences become indistinguishable after flux averaging.

## Method
The authors implemented the CFG model of Hen, Li, Guo, Weinstein, and Piasetzky (2014) analytically for CCQE electron-nucleus and neutrino-nucleus scattering, extending the standard Relativistic Fermi Gas (RFG) model by adding a high-momentum tail above the Fermi momentum to account for short-range correlated nucleon pairs. They computed nuclear cross sections by convolving initial and final nucleon momentum distributions with the single-nucleon cross section, including six possible transitions between momentum regions. For electron scattering they compared RFG and CFG predictions using fixed vector form factors from Borah et al. (2020) and also compared two different vector form factor parameterizations. For neutrino scattering they compared RFG and CFG predictions using multiple axial form factor extractions (dipole, MBGH, Mainz 22, NME 22, MINERvA, PNDME 23, ETMC 23) both for mono-energetic 1 GeV neutrinos and for flux-averaged predictions compared to published MiniBooNE data.

## Key numbers
- The CFG model parameter lambda is approximately 2.75 plus or minus 0.25; in the formal limit lambda to 1 the RFG model is recovered, but this limit is never obtained in practice.
- Approximately 20 percent of nucleons have momentum greater than the Fermi momentum, appearing in short-range correlated neutron-proton pairs.
- Electron-carbon scattering comparisons were performed at incident electron energy 480 MeV with scattering angle 60 degrees, and at 680 MeV with scattering angles 36 degrees and 60 degrees.
- Neutrino-carbon scattering comparisons used an incoming neutrino energy of 1 GeV with scattering angle cos theta_mu = 0.85.
- Flux-averaged comparisons used MiniBooNE muon-neutrino data with scattering angle bin 0.8 less than cos theta_mu less than 0.9.
- The dipole axial form factor used M_A = 1.014 plus or minus 0.014 GeV.

## Context
This work extends the CFG model originally proposed by Hen, Li, Guo, Weinstein, and Piasetzky (2014) for nuclear structure to the domain of lepton-nucleus scattering, providing a fully analytic implementation that enables systematic comparison with the standard RFG model. The results show that for electron scattering, nuclear model uncertainties dominate over form factor uncertainties, while for flux-averaged neutrino scattering the situation reverses, a finding that informs systematic error budgets for experiments like MiniBooNE (2010). The analytic implementation enables future studies of semi-inclusive neutrino-nucleus scattering to potentially distinguish CFG from RFG after flux averaging, and can be combined with other effects such as final state interactions.
