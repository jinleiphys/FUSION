# A Comprehensive Monte Carlo Framework for Jet-Quenching

> This article presents the motivation for developing a comprehensive modeling framework in which different models and parameter inputs can be compared and evaluated for a large range of jet-quenching observables measured in relativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and the LHC. The concept of a framework us discussed within the context of recent efforts by the JET Collaboration, the authors of JEWEL, and the JETSCAPE collaborations. The framework ingredients for each of these approaches is presented with a sample of important results from each. The role of advanced statistical tools in comparing models to data is also discussed, along with the need for a more detailed accounting of correlated errors in experimental results.

# A Comprehensive Monte Carlo Framework for Jet-Quenching

## Key claim

A multi-stage, modular Monte Carlo framework for jet-quenching is needed to systematically compare different energy-loss models against a large range of observables from RHIC and the LHC, but the full potential of such a framework requires experimental collaborations to release full covariance matrices for correlated systematic errors, which is not yet standard practice.

## Method

The paper reviews and compares three approaches to jet-quenching modeling: the JET Collaboration's multi-model comparison of $\hat{q}$ values using leading hadron $R_{AA}$, the JEWEL Monte Carlo framework with PYTHIA6 and 1+1D Bjorken hydrodynamics, and the JETSCAPE framework which implements multi-stage modularity with virtuality-ordered evolution using MATTER, LBT, or MARTINI modules. The author discusses how Bayesian statistical methods are preferable to frequentist chi-squared approaches for comparing models with many parameters to data, and illustrates the impact of correlated versus uncorrelated systematic errors on model fits using hypothetical examples.

## Key numbers

- The JET Collaboration used five different approaches to constrain $\hat{q}$ using the leading hadron nuclear modification factor $R_{AA}$, with 2D+1 viscous hydrodynamic profiles (except HT-BW which used 3+1D ideal hydro) and central Glauber initial conditions (except HT-M which used MC-KLN).
- JEWEL results for Pb+Pb at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 2.76 TeV show reasonable agreement with ATLAS and CMS measurements for most observables, except for the $R_{CP}$ nuclear modification factor in the peripheral region.
- JETSCAPE results shown at Quark Matter 2019 used MATTER+LBT with a virtuality switching parameter $Q_0$ = 2 GeV.
- A chi-squared analysis of soft physics signatures by the author (Soltz 2012) took longer and produced fewer insights than a concurrent Bayesian analysis by the MADAI Collaboration (Novak 2013).

## Context

This work extends the multi-model comparison approach pioneered by the JET Collaboration (Burke 2014) and the single-framework approach of JEWEL (Zapp 2011-2013) into the JETSCAPE framework, which applies multi-stage modularity to jet evolution. The paper builds on the success of Bayesian statistical methods in soft physics demonstrated by the MADAI Collaboration (Novak 2013) and the Duke QCD theory group (Bernhard 2016), and argues that similar approaches should be applied to hard probes. The framework enables systematic model-to-data comparisons for jet observables, but the author identifies the need for experimental collaborations to release full covariance matrices for correlated systematic errors to fully realize this potential.
