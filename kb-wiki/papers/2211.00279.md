# Microscopic formulation of the interacting boson model for reflection asymmetric nuclei

> Reflection asymmetric, octupole shapes in nuclei are a prominent aspect of nuclear structure, and have been recurrently studied over the decades. Recent experiments using radioactive-ion beams have provided evidence for stable octupole shapes. A variety of nuclear models have been employed for the related theoretical analyses. We review recent studies on the nuclear octupole shapes and collective excitations within the interacting boson model. A special focus is placed on the microscopic formulation of this model by using the mean-field method that is based on the framework of nuclear density functional theory. As an illustrative example, a stable octupole deformation, and a shape phase transition as a function of nucleon number that involves both quadrupole and octupole degrees of freedom are shown to occur in light actinides. Systematic spectroscopic studies indicate enhancement of the octupole collectivity in a wide mass region. Couplings between the octupole and additional degrees of freedom are incorporated in a microscopic manner in the boson system, and shown to play a crucial role in the description of the related intriguing nuclear structure phenomena such as the shape coexistence.

## Key claim

The paper reviews how the interacting boson model (IBM) can be derived microscopically from nuclear density functional theory through a mapping of mean-field potential energy surfaces onto bosonic energy surfaces, showing that this approach reproduces octupole collectivity and shape phase transitions without phenomenological adjustment of the Hamiltonian to octupole data.

## Method

The author performs constrained self-consistent mean-field calculations using the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov method with the DD-PC1 functional and separable pairing force to obtain potential energy surfaces in the quadrupole ($\beta_2$) and octupole ($\beta_3$) deformation planes. These fermionic energy surfaces are then mapped onto the bosonic energy surface of an $sdf$-IBM Hamiltonian by equating the two surfaces in the vicinity of the global minimum and determining the Hamiltonian parameters and proportionality constants between fermionic and bosonic deformation variables. The resulting IBM Hamiltonian is numerically diagonalized to obtain excitation energies and transition probabilities, and the method is extended to odd-mass nuclei via the interacting boson-fermion model and to shape coexistence via configuration mixing.

## Key numbers

* For $^{226}$Th, the octupole deformation is most pronounced with a stable octupole minimum; the signature splitting $S(I)$ is nearly zero for $^{220-226}$Th, indicating an alternating parity band
* The $3^-_1$ excitation energy in light actinides near $N=134$ falls below $E_x < 0.5$ MeV
* For $^{144}$Ba, the calculated $B(E3; 3^-_1 \to 0^+_1)$ transition probability is 23 W.u., compared to the experimental value of $48^{+25}_{-34}$ W.u.
* For $^{145}$Ba, predicted $B(E3)$ values for octupole transitions include 25 W.u. for ${15/2}^-_4 \to {9/2}^+_1$, 31 W.u. for ${19/2}^-_2 \to {13/2}^+_1$, 4.7 W.u. for ${11/2}^+_1 \to {5/2}^-_1$, and 15 W.u. for ${15/2}^+_1 \to {9/2}^-_1$
* For $^{76}$Kr, the $\beta_2$-$\beta_3$ energy surface has a global minimum at ($\beta_2$, $\beta_3$) $\approx$ (0.0, 0.05) with non-zero octupole deformation

## Context

This work extends the microscopic formulation of the IBM for quadrupole collective states developed by Nomura (2008, 2010, 2011) to include octupole degrees of freedom, building on earlier phenomenological $sdf$- and $spdf$-boson model studies by Engel (1985, 1987) and Barfield (1988). The method enables systematic spectroscopic studies of octupole collectivity across wide mass regions and allows incorporation of couplings between octupole and additional degrees of freedom, including particle-boson coupling in odd-mass nuclei and configuration mixing for shape coexistence, which are relevant for interpreting recent radioactive-ion beam experiments on octupole-deformed nuclei such as those by Gaffney (2013), Bucher (2016, 2017), and Butler (2020).
