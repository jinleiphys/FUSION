# Viscosity of High Energy Nuclear Fluids

> Relativistic high energy heavy ion collision cross sections have been interpreted in terms of almost ideal liquid droplets of nuclear matter. The experimental low viscosity of these nuclear fluids have been of considerable recent quantum chromodynamic interest. The viscosity is here discussed in terms of the string fragmentation models wherein the temperature dependence of the nuclear fluid viscosity obeys the Vogel-Fulcher-Tammann law.

# Viscosity of High Energy Nuclear Fluids

## Key claim

The viscosity of high-energy nuclear fluids produced in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions follows a Vogel-Fulcher-Tammann temperature dependence derived from string fragmentation models, becoming quantum-limited and extremely small only when the temperature is well above the Hagedorn temperature (T ≫ T₀ ≈ 207 MeV), but rising dramatically as the temperature approaches T₀ from above.

## Method

The authors derive Kubo formula sum rules relating the zero-frequency shear viscosity to the relaxation time of off-diagonal pressure tensor elements. They then apply the Lund string fragmentation model, treating quarks connected by gluon flux tubes as polymer-like strings, and compute the temperature-dependent cooling time for hot strings using microcanonical statistical thermodynamics. The viscosity is obtained by combining the relaxation time expression with the Kubo relation η = ρc²∞τ.

## Key numbers

- Hagedorn temperature: T₀ ≈ 207 MeV, derived from string tension σ via k_B T₀ = √(3ħcσ/4π)
- String energy parameter: Φ = 7k_B T₀/2 ≈ 725 MeV
- Fermi velocity in liquid drop model: v_F/c ≈ 0.27
- Nuclear radius parameter: a ≈ 1.2 × 10⁻¹³ cm
- Quantum viscosity lower bound: η > 2πħn, where n is the number density of constituent particles

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous string fragmentation model studies (Parihar 2006a, Parihar 2006b) to compute viscosity, and addresses the widely discussed observation of nearly ideal fluid behavior in relativistic heavy ion collisions reported by Heinz (2005), Shuryak (2004a, 2004b, 2004c), and others. The paper contrasts the stringy liquid picture with the deconfined quark-gluon plasma interpretation and with lattice QCD computations (Karsch 2004), and derives a quantum viscosity bound previously conjectured by Shuryak (2005). The approach enables a quantitative connection between string fragmentation dynamics and the low viscosity observed in ultra-relativistic nuclear collisions.
