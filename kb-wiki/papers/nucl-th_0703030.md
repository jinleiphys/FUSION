# Microscopic Calculation of Fusion Cross-Sections

> A microscopic calculation of cross sections for fusion of oxygen isotopes $^{16}$O, $^{22}$O and $^{24}$O is presented. Fermionic Molecular Dynamics wave functions are used to describe the fully antisymmetrized and angular momentum projected nucleus-nucleus system. The same effective nucleon-nucleon interaction is used to determine the ground state properties of the nuclei as well as the nucleus-nucleus interaction. From the microscopic many-body wave function the corresponding wave function for the relative motion of two point-like nuclei is derived by a method proposed by Friedrich which leads to a local effective nucleus-nucleus potential. Finally the Schr{ö}dinger equation with incoming wave boundary conditions is solved to obtain the penetration factors for the different partial waves. With these the S-factor for the fusion process is calculated. A good agreement with experimental data is obtained for the $^{16}$O-$^{16}$O cross section. Much enhanced cross sections are found for the neutron-rich oxygen isotopes.

# Microscopic Calculation of Fusion Cross-Sections

## Key claim

The authors present a fully microscopic calculation of fusion cross sections for oxygen isotopes using Fermionic Molecular Dynamics, finding that fusion cross sections for neutron-rich oxygen isotopes (²²O and ²⁴O) are enhanced by 6–8 orders of magnitude compared to ¹⁶O, but this result depends on the frozen ground-state approximation; adiabatic effects modify the low-energy behavior, particularly for ²⁴O where the frozen-configuration potential does not allow fusion below 2 MeV.

## Method

The authors construct fully antisymmetrized and angular-momentum-projected many-body wave functions for the two-nucleus system using Fermionic Molecular Dynamics (FMD) with Gaussian wave packets. They derive a local effective nucleus-nucleus potential from the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) energy surfaces via a two-step transformation through Resonating Group Method (RGM) basis states, following Friedrich's method. The Schrödinger equation is then solved with incoming wave boundary conditions (IWBC) to obtain penetration factors and fusion cross sections, using the same effective nucleon-nucleon interaction (derived from Argonne V18 via the Unitary Correlation Operator method) for both ground states and the interacting system.

## Key numbers

- Ground state binding energies from FMD: ¹⁶O at -120.07 MeV (experiment: -127.62 MeV), ²²O at -153.88 MeV (experiment: -162.03 MeV), ²⁴O at -158.75 MeV (experiment: -168.48 MeV)
- Matter radii from FMD: ¹⁶O at 2.50 fm (experiment: 2.54±0.02 fm), ²²O at 2.80 fm (experiment: 2.88±0.06 fm), ²⁴O at 2.95 fm (experiment: 3.19±0.13 fm)
- Fusion cross sections for neutron-rich oxygen isotopes (²²O, ²⁴O) are enhanced by 6–8 orders of magnitude compared to ¹⁶O
- For ²⁴O, no fusion occurs below E_cm = 2 MeV in the frozen-configuration calculation
- The effective potential fit to GCM matrix elements achieves deviations less than 50 keV between collective and GCM matrix elements
- Variations of the adiabatic potential depth from -0.5 MeV to -10 MeV change the S-factor by less than 50%

## Context

This work extends the Fermionic Molecular Dynamics approach (FMD, developed by the same authors in 2000 and 2004) to low-energy fusion reactions, providing a consistent microscopic description of both nuclear structure and reaction dynamics. It contrasts with phenomenological barrier penetration models (Caughlan and Fowler, 1988) and parametrized potentials (Gasques et al., 2004) that cannot systematically describe neutron-rich nuclei with extended neutron densities. The calculation reproduces experimental ¹⁶O+¹⁶O fusion data (Spinka et al., 1972; Hulke et al., 1980; Wu and Barnes, 1984; Thomas et al., 1986) without adjustable parameters, and does not find the fusion hindrance predicted by Jiang et al. (2007) for ¹⁶O+¹⁶O below 7 MeV. The approach enables future coupled-channel calculations including excited states and adiabatic distortions, which are particularly important for reactions involving neutron-rich nuclei far from stability.
