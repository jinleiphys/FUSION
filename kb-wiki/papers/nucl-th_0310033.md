# Nuclear Tetrahedral Symmetry

> We recall the main features of the Td (tetrahedral) symmetry in atomic nuclei and present realistic mean-field calculations supporting the existence such a symmetry all over the nuclear chart. A few potential candidate-nuclei are investigated and the possible experimental signatures of the tetrahedral symmetry are also briefly discussed.

## Key claim

Realistic mean-field calculations support the existence of tetrahedral (Td) symmetry in atomic nuclei across the nuclear chart, but the best experimental candidates may not be the nuclei with tetrahedral magic numbers themselves; instead, nuclei in their vicinity, where pairing effects or quasiparticle excitations can favour tetrahedral minima, may be more promising.

## Method

The authors performed two types of mean-field calculations: a macroscopic-microscopic approach combining a liquid-drop term with shell and pairing corrections, and self-consistent Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-Bogoljubov (HFB) calculations using the SLy4 parametrization. The nuclear shape was parametrized by a spherical-harmonics expansion, with tetrahedral symmetry realized by setting only the α₃₂ deformation parameter nonzero. Total energies were minimized over β₂, γ, and all octupole and hexadecapole degrees of freedom using a gradient method.

## Key numbers

- Tetrahedral magic numbers predicted for neutrons: N = 16, 20, 32, 40, 56, 70, 90, 112, 136
- Tetrahedral magic numbers predicted for protons: Z = 16, 20, 32, 40, 56, 70, 90, 112, 126
- In ⁸⁰Zr and ¹¹⁰Zr, a tetrahedral minimum appears at β₂ = 0 with α₃₂ ~ 0.15, separated from the prolate ground-state by a barrier up to a couple of MeV
- In HFB calculations with SLy4 parametrization, the tetrahedral minimum is the ground state in ¹¹⁰Zr (0.0 MeV) and ¹¹²Zr (0.0 MeV), while the prolate minima lie at +0.072 MeV and +0.299 MeV respectively
- In ¹⁰⁸Zr with SLy4, the tetrahedral minimum lies at 0.0 MeV, with the prolate minimum lower at -1.099 MeV
- Tetrahedral minima are predicted at about 1–2 MeV above ground states in most cases

## Context

This work extends earlier group-theory predictions by the same authors (2002) that tetrahedral symmetry could be encountered across the nuclear chart, and is consistent with independent self-consistent Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations published earlier by other groups. The results suggest that tetrahedral shape isomers in zirconium isotopes are within reach of current experimental facilities, and that experimental signatures such as parity-doublet rotational bands without E1 inter-band transitions (due to vanishing dipole moment) and a unique 16-fold quasiparticle degeneracy could be used to identify such states.
