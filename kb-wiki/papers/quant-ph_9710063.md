# Open Quantum Systems, Entropy and Chaos

> Entropy generation in quantum sytems is tied to the existence of a nonclassical environment (heat bath or other) with which the system interacts. The continuous `measuring' of the open system by its environment induces decoherence of its wave function and entropy increase. Examples of nonrelativistic quantum Brownian motion and of interacting scalar fields illustrate these general concepts. It is shown that the Hartree-Fock approximation around the bare classical limit can lead to spurious semiquantum chaos, which may affect the determination of entropy production and thermalization also in other cases.

## Key claim

Entropy generation in quantum systems requires an open system interacting with a nonclassical environment; the von Neumann entropy of a closed quantum system is strictly constant under unitary time evolution, so the transition from a pure initial state to a mixed final state with nonzero entropy must arise from tracing over environmental degrees of freedom, and the Hartree-Fock approximation around the bare classical limit can produce spurious semiquantum chaos that may affect entropy production and thermalization calculations.

## Method

The paper develops a theoretical framework using the functional Schrödinger equation and Dirac's time-dependent variational principle with Gaussian trial wave functionals to implement the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) approximation for coupled scalar fields representing system and environment. For the nonrelativistic case, a Caldeira-Leggett-type model of quantum Brownian motion with bilinear coordinate-coordinate coupling between a particle and an infinite set of harmonic oscillators is analyzed in the strong-coupling limit at short times. For the field theory case, the effective action is derived for two interacting scalar fields with a bilinear interaction, and the von Neumann entropy of the system is expressed in terms of the two-point correlation functions of the system and environment fields.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of effective photon to QGP degrees of freedom in thermal equilibrium is given by \(N_\gamma / N_{QGP} = 2\tilde\sigma VT^3 / 37\tilde\sigma VT^3\), indicating about 5% or less of entropy can be generated by bremsstrahlung photons, though a nonequilibrium distribution with higher effective photon temperature may lead to a sizeable correction.
- The decoherence time \(\tau_D\) is found to be several ten orders of magnitude smaller than the thermalization time \(\tau_{th}\) for models of macroscopic bodies interacting with their environment (cosmic background radiation, atmospheric gas particles).
- For the nonrelativistic quantum Brownian motion model, the dimensionless time variable is defined as \(t_+ \equiv \Omega t (2g_0 \Omega/M)^{1/2}\), where \(\Omega\) is a high-frequency cutoff (such as \(\Lambda_{QCD} \approx 200\) MeV), \(g_0\) is a dimensionless coupling constant, and \(M\) is the particle mass.
- The parameter \(\alpha \equiv (1/2g_0)(\Omega/M)(w_0\Omega)^{-4}\) determines wave packet behavior: \(\alpha > 1\) gives faster spreading than free quantum mechanical wave packets, \(\alpha = 1\) gives constant width, and \(\alpha < 1\) gives squeezing.
- Sizeable entropy can be reached in very short times, e.g., 0.1 fm/c, though numbers depend on model parameters; the ultrarelativistic/high-T limit gives 4 units of entropy per particle.

## Context

This work extends the environment-induced decoherence approach of Zurek (1991), Omnes (1994), and Gell-Mann and Hartle (1993) to strongly interacting systems relevant for relativistic heavy-ion collisions and quark-gluon plasma formation. The paper addresses the unresolved problem of how an initially pure quantum state (e.g., two colliding nuclear wave packets with zero entropy) could evolve into the mixed high-entropy-density QGP state, which is a conceptual cornerstone of the search for quark-gluon plasma as reflected in the "Quark Matter" conference series. The results enable future dynamical calculations of entropy production in high-energy collisions, including the role of bremsstrahlung photons as an environment, and provide a framework for comparing decoherence and thermalization time scales in strongly interacting matter.
