# Nuclear fusion as a probe for octupole deformation in $^{224}$Ra

> $\textit{Background}$: Nuclear fusion has been shown to be a perfect probe to study the different nuclear shapes. However, the possibility of testing octupole deformation of a nucleus with this tool has not been fully explored yet. The presence of a stactic octupole deformation in nuclei will enhanced a possible permanent electric dipole moment, leading to a possible demonstration of parity violation. $\textit{Purpose}$: To check whether static octupole deformation or octupole vibration in fusion give qualitatively different results so that both situations can be experimentally disentangled. $\textit{Method}$: Fusion cross sections are computed in the Coupled-Channels formalism making use of the Ingoing-Wave Boundary Conditions (IWBC) for the systems $^{16}$O+$^{144}$Ba and $^{16}$O+$^{224}$Ra. $\textit{Results}$: Barrier distributions of the two considered schemes show different patterns. For the $^{224}$Ra case, the octupole deformation parameter is large enough to create a sizeable difference. $\textit{Conclusions}$: The measurement of barrier distributions can be an excellent probe to clarify the presence of octupole deformation.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that measuring barrier distributions in subbarrier fusion reactions can serve as a complementary experimental probe to distinguish between static octupole deformation and octupole vibration in nuclei, but the final possibility of disentangling the two situations will depend on the available experimental accuracy and the strength of the octupole deformation.

## Method

Fusion cross sections were computed using the Coupled-Channels formalism with Ingoing-Wave Boundary Conditions (IWBC) for the systems 16O+144Ba and 16O+224Ra. The authors modeled the targets either as quadrupole-plus-octupole deformed rotors (static deformation) or as quadrupole deformed nuclei with an octupole vibration, using theoretical deformation parameters from Nazarewicz (1984) and experimental excitation energies from the National Nuclear Data Center. The coupling potentials were derived from a Woods-Saxon potential with Akyuz-Winther parametrization, and the resulting barrier distributions (derivatives of transmission probabilities) were compared between the two coupling schemes.

## Key numbers

- Quadrupole deformation parameter beta2 = 0.149 for 144Ba and beta2 = 0.138 for 224Ra, from Nazarewicz (1984)
- Octupole deformation parameter beta3 = 0.068 for 144Ba and beta3 = 0.099 for 224Ra, from Nazarewicz (1984)
- Experimental excitation energies for 144Ba: 2+ state at 0.199 MeV, 4+ at 0.530 MeV, 6+ at 0.962 MeV; 1- at 0.759 MeV, 3- at 0.838 MeV, 5- at 1.038 MeV
- Experimental excitation energies for 224Ra: 2+ at 0.084 MeV, 4+ at 0.250 MeV, 6+ at 0.479 MeV; 1- at 0.216 MeV, 3- at 0.290 MeV, 5- at 0.433 MeV
- The paper notes that 232Th could have an even larger beta3 = 0.14, as investigated by Hagino and Sannohe

## Context

This work extends the use of subbarrier fusion as a probe for nuclear shapes, previously established for quadrupole deformation by Balantekin (1998) and Hagino (2012), into the domain of octupole deformation. The study is motivated by the search for static octupole deformation in nuclei like 144Ba and 224Ra, which would enhance nuclear Schiff moments and contribute to atomic electric dipole moments, as discussed by Dobaczewski (2005) and Gribakin (2009). The authors note that Coulomb excitation measurements by Woltersheim (1993) and Butler (1996) can provide transition probabilities but cannot easily distinguish static deformation from dynamic vibrations, making fusion barrier distributions a potentially complementary tool. The work enables future experimental tests at radioactive beam facilities to clarify the nature of octupole correlations in heavy nuclei.
