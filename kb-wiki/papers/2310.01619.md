# Indication of Sharp and Strong Phase-Transitions from NICER Observations

> In this letter, we present a new, weakly model-dependent, test for ``standard" equations of state (EoS) models that disfavor sharp and strong phase-transitions, by using neutron star mass and radius observations. We show the radii of two neutron stars observed by NICER (PSR J0740+6620 and PSR 0030+0451) are correlated if these two neutron stars are built upon standard EoS models. The radii of neutron stars with different masses are sensitive to the pressures at different densities, and the pressures at different densities are strongly correlated in standard EoS models. We further show that the correlation of the neutron star radii can be significantly weakened, when additional degrees of freedom concerning the first-order phase transitions are added into the EoSs. We propose a new quantity, ${D}_{\mathrm{L}}$, which measures the extent to which the linear correlation of the radii of two neutron stars is weakened. Our method gives a $48\%$ identification probability (with a $5\%$ false alarm rate) of finding beyond standard EoS models in NICER observations. Future observations with higher measurement accuracy can confirm or rule out this identification. Our method is generalizable to any pair of neutron star masses and can be employed with other sets of observations in the future.

## Key claim

The paper presents a weakly model-dependent test suggesting that current NICER observations of two neutron stars (PSR J0740+6620 and PSR J0030+0451) may be inconsistent with "standard" equations of state that disfavor sharp and strong first-order phase transitions, but the claim is hedged by a 48% identification probability at a 5% false alarm rate, meaning the result is not definitive.

## Method

The authors constructed a linear correlation line for the radii of two neutron stars with masses 1.34 and 2.0 solar masses using minimum and maximum radius constraints from chiral effective field theory and the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff maximum mass. They defined a quantity D_L that measures the perpendicular distance of a given (R_1.34, R_2.0) point from this correlation line. They compared the D_L distribution from NICER observations against distributions from three standard EoS parameterizations (3P, 4L, and CEFT15unif) and from the Constant Speed of Sound (CSS) parameterization that includes sharp first-order phase transitions.

## Key numbers

- Identification probability P_I = 48% for detecting beyond-standard EoS models from NICER data, with a false alarm rate P_FA = 4.7% when using the 3P model as the standard scenario
- Threshold D_L^Th = 0.9 km, above which a deviation from standard EoS models is claimed
- Minimum radius for a 1.34 solar mass neutron star: R_min_1.34 = 9.7 +/- 0.4 km
- Minimum radius for a 2.0 solar mass neutron star: R_min_2.0 = 9.0 +/- 0.0 km
- Maximum radius for a 1.34 solar mass neutron star: R_max_1.34 = 12.6 +/- 0.3 km
- Maximum radius for a 2.0 solar mass neutron star: R_max_2.0 = 13.1 +/- 0.2 km
- For mock data B (R_1.34 = 13.9 +/- 0.5 km, R_2.0 = 11.2 +/- 0.5 km), the identification probability rises to 99%

## Context

This work extends the study of neutron star radius correlations inspired by the strong correlation between neutron skin thicknesses of Pb-208 and Ca-48 observed in theoretical nuclear models (Reinhard 2022). It contrasts with previous approaches that reconstruct full mass-radius curves or use Bayesian factors to identify phase transitions (Legred 2021, Essick 2023), instead using a two-point correlation method that does not require constructing EoS parameterizations with phase transitions. The method enables future application to additional neutron star pairs from next-generation gravitational wave detectors and X-ray observations, and generalizes the "twin star" scenario (Benic 2014, Mishustin 2002, Alford 2017) to neutron stars with unequal masses.
