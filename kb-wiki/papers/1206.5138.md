# Hadron Mass Spectrum and the Shear Viscosity to Entropy Density Ratio of Hot Hadronic Matter

> Lattice calculations of the QCD trace anomaly at temperatures $T<160$ MeV have been shown to match hadron resonance gas model calculations, which include an exponentially rising hadron mass spectrum. In this paper we perform a more detailed comparison of the model calculations to lattice data that confirms the need for an exponentially increasing density of hadronic states. Also, we find that the lattice data is compatible with a hadron density of states that goes as $ρ(m) \sim m^{-a}\exp(m/T_H) $ at large $m$ with $a> 5/2$ (where $T_H \sim 167$ MeV). With this specific subleading contribution to the density of states, heavy resonances are most likely undergo 2-body decay (instead of multi-particle decay), which facilitates their inclusion into hadron transport codes. Moreover, estimates for the shear viscosity and the shear relaxation time coefficient of the hadron resonance model computed within the excluded volume approximation suggest that these transport coefficients are sensitive to the parameters that define the hadron mass spectrum.

## Key claim

The paper confirms that lattice QCD trace anomaly data at temperatures below 160 MeV require an exponentially increasing hadron mass spectrum, and finds that the data are compatible with a density of states of the form ρ(m) ∼ m^{-a} exp(m/T_H) at large m with a > 5/2 and T_H ∼ 167 MeV, which implies heavy resonances predominantly undergo 2-body decay rather than multi-particle decay.

## Method

The authors compared four different functional forms for the hadron density of states (ρ_1 through ρ_4) within the hadron resonance gas model to N_t = 10 lattice data from Borsanyi et al. (2010) for the trace anomaly, pressure, and speed of sound. They then computed the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio using the excluded volume approximation with a constant hard-core radius, and evaluated the shear relaxation time coefficient from the causality and stability inequality.

## Key numbers

- The Hagedorn temperature extracted from the fit is T_H ∼ 167 MeV for the density of states with a > 5/2.
- The maximum temperature at which the hadron resonance gas model is applicable is ∼ 155 MeV.
- For the exponentially rising spectra ρ_1, ρ_2, and ρ_3, the excluded volume radius cannot be larger than 0.2 fm to maintain agreement with lattice data.
- At T = 155 MeV, η/s computed with ρ_3 and r = 0.2 fm remains an order of magnitude above the viscosity lower bound 1/(4π).
- When refitting to only high-temperature lattice data with r = 0.5 fm, η/s drops to ∼ 1/(4π) near T = 160 MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Noronha-Hostler et al. (2007-2009) and Majumder et al. (2010) that used an exponentially rising hadron mass spectrum to match lattice QCD thermodynamics, but now uses the lower pseudo-critical temperature T_c ∼ 155 MeV from Borsanyi et al. (2010) and Bazavov et al. (2011) instead of the earlier T_c ∼ 196 MeV from Cheng et al. (2007). The results show that hot hadronic matter below 160 MeV is far from being a nearly perfect fluid, contrary to earlier estimates at T ∼ 190 MeV, and highlight the sensitivity of transport coefficients to the precise temperature dependence of thermodynamic quantities. The findings enable improved modeling of the transition from viscous hydrodynamics to hadronic transport in heavy ion collisions.
