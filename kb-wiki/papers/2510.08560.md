# Gamma-Ray Spectra of $R$-Process Nuclei

> The radioactive decay of unstable nuclei created in the rapid neutron capture process releases a large amount of $γ$-rays. When the ejecta are optically thick, these $γ$-rays may contribute to an associated kilonova. Once transparent, prominent spectral features will be directly observable in current and future $γ$-ray detectors. In this work, we study and compare the $γ$-ray spectra of different representative $r$-process trajectories across a broad range of timescales, from hours to 50,000 years, identifying the nuclei which significantly contribute. We discuss these findings in the context of observability, noting that there are several practical challenges to connecting observed signatures to specific nuclei. However, if these challenges can be overcome, direct observation of $γ$-rays from $r$-process sites can provide insight into the fundamental physics underpinning the $r$-process.

## Key claim
The paper characterizes which nuclei dominate the gamma-ray emission from r-process nucleosynthesis across different trajectory strengths and timescales, but explicitly notes that observability depends on overcoming practical challenges such as spectral broadening, background sources, and nuclear data uncertainties, meaning the identified spectral features are not guaranteed to be detectable.

## Method
The authors combined detailed beta-decay gamma-ray spectra from ENDF/B-VIII.0 and theoretical calculations with nuclear reaction network simulations using PRISM version 1.6.0, adopting a single hydrodynamic trajectory from a gamma-ray burst cocoon model and varying the initial electron fraction (Ye = 0.034, 0.175, 0.25, 0.4) to produce four representative r-process abundance patterns. They calculated the total gamma-ray emission spectrum as a function of energy and time by summing the product of each nucleus's decay flow and its individual gamma-ray spectrum, then performed spectral decomposition to identify which nuclei contribute significantly at eight timescales from 6 hours to 50,000 years.

## Key numbers
* Four simulations with initial electron fractions Ye = 0.4 (Sim A, limited r-process), 0.25 (Sim B, weak r-process), 0.175 (Sim C, strong r-process), and 0.034 (Sim D, extended r-process with actinides)
* Timescales analyzed: 6 hours, 1 day, 1 week, 1 month, 1 year, 50 years, 1000 years, and 50,000 years
* At 50,000 years, the flux estimate for a remnant at 8 kpc with 0.1 solar mass ejecta and S(E) = 10^6 s^-1 MeV^-1 g^-1 at 1 MeV gives F = 6.0 x 10^-10 MeV s^-1 cm^-2, approximately three orders of magnitude below current detector sensitivity thresholds
* The nucleus 250Cm has a lifetime of approximately 8300 years with decay branching estimated at 8% beta-minus, 18% alpha, and 74% fission
* The nucleus 128Sb has a ground state beta-decay lifetime of 9 hours and an isomer at 43.9 keV excitation energy with a beta-decay lifetime of 10.8 minutes

## Context
This work extends prior studies of gamma-ray signatures from r-process sites, including calculations by Hotokezaka (2016) on kilonova gamma-ray signals, Korobkin (2020) on gamma-ray transport and spectral lines in kilonovae, and Qian (1998) on gamma-rays from the r-process in supernovae. It differs from these by focusing on characterizing the contributing nuclei across a broad range of timescales and r-process strengths rather than modeling observability for a specific site. The results enable future work to match observed gamma-ray lines to specific nuclei, potentially constraining the astrophysical site and conditions of the r-process, though the authors caution that spectral broadening, background sources, and nuclear data uncertainties (particularly for heavy actinides and nuclear isomers) present significant challenges.
