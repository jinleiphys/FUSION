# Parton Distribution Functions and their Generalizations

> This article is an introduction to parton distribution functions and their generalizations which describe the quark and gluon structure of hadrons, and can be measured in various high-energy scattering processes. We provide the theoretical background, highlight both historical and recent developments, explain the connections between the different functions, and expose in which processes these functions can be accessed and what we can learn from them about hadron structure.

## Key claim

This article provides an introductory overview of parton distribution functions (PDFs) and their generalizations (TMDs, GPDs, GTMDs) that describe the quark and gluon structure of hadrons, emphasizing that these functions are non-perturbative objects which cannot be computed in perturbative QCD and must instead be extracted from experimental data or computed using non-perturbative methods such as lattice QCD or phenomenological models.

## Method

The authors present the formal QCD definitions of the correlators from which PDFs, transverse momentum-dependent distributions (TMDs), generalized parton distributions (GPDs), and generalized transverse momentum-dependent distributions (GTMDs) are derived through specific limits and projections, as shown in Eqs. (FF-correlator) through (GTMD-correlator). They explain how these functions are accessed experimentally through inclusive deep-inelastic scattering (DIS), semi-inclusive DIS (SIDIS), deeply virtual Compton scattering (DVCS), and the Drell-Yan process, and discuss the theoretical properties including evolution, universality, and interpretation in impact-parameter and phase-space representations.

## Context

This chapter extends the pedagogical and reference literature on hadron structure, building on the foundational work of Feynman (1972) on the parton model, the development of QCD following the DIS experiments at SLAC (Bloom 1969, Breidenbach 1969) and Gargamelle (Eichten 1973), and the subsequent theoretical developments of TMDs by Ralston (1979) and Collins (1981), GPDs by Muller (1994), Ji (1996), and Radyushkin (1996), and GTMDs by Ji (2003) and Belitsky (2003). The article enables newcomers to understand the connections between the different distribution functions and identifies open questions such as the theoretical explanation of confinement and the experimental access to GTMDs through newly proposed processes.
