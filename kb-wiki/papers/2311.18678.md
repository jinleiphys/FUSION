# Scaling behaviour of $dN/dy$ in high-energy collisions

> From a recently found family of analytic, finite and accelerating 1+1-dimensional solutions to perfect fluid relativistic hydrodynamics, we derive simple and powerful formulae to describe the rapidity and pseudorapidity density distributions. By introducing a new scaling function, we notice that the rapidity distribution data of the different experiments all collapse into a single curve. This data-collapsing (or scaling) behaviour in the rapidity distributions suggests that high-energy $p+p$ collisions may be described as collective systems.

## Key claim
The authors derive simple formulae for rapidity and pseudorapidity density distributions from a family of analytic, finite, accelerating 1+1-dimensional perfect-fluid hydrodynamic solutions, and show that rapidity distribution data from different experiments collapse onto a single scaling curve. This data-collapsing behavior suggests that high-energy p+p collisions may be describable as collective systems, though the claim is hedged as a suggestion rather than a definitive conclusion.

## Method
The authors start from a recently found family of analytic, finite, accelerating 1+1-dimensional solutions to perfect-fluid relativistic hydrodynamics. From these solutions they derive closed-form expressions for the rapidity density (dN/dy) and pseudorapidity density (dN/dη) distributions. They then introduce a new scaling function and test it against experimental rapidity distribution data from multiple experiments to see whether the data collapse onto a single universal curve.

## Key numbers
- The rapidity distribution data from different experiments all collapse into a single curve when plotted using the newly introduced scaling function, indicating a universal scaling behavior.
- The paper reports no specific numerical values for the scaling function parameters or for the rapidity density values themselves in the provided text.

## Context
This work extends a line of research on analytic solutions to relativistic hydrodynamics, specifically building on a recently found family of accelerating 1+1-dimensional perfect-fluid solutions. The observed scaling behavior in p+p collisions suggests that these small systems may exhibit collective behavior similar to that seen in heavy-ion collisions, which would contradict the traditional view that p+p collisions are too small to form a collectively expanding medium. The results enable future studies to use the scaling function as a tool for comparing rapidity distributions across different collision energies and systems, and may motivate further theoretical work on the onset of collectivity in small systems.
