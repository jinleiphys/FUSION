# Strong Interaction Dynamics and Fermi $β$ decay in the nucleon and the nucleus

> Nuclear super-allowed $β$ decay has been used to obtain tight limits on the value of the CKM matrix element $V_{ud}$ that is important for unitarity tests, and therefore important for tests of the standard model. Current requirements on precision are so intense that effects formerly thought too small to matter are now relevant. This article is a brief review of personal efforts to include the effects of strong interactions on Fermi $β$ decay. First I examine the role of isospin violation in the decay of the neutron. The size of the necessary correction depends upon detailed strong-interaction dynamics. The isospin violating parts of the nucleon wave function, important at the low energy of $β$ decay, can be constrained by data taken at much higher energies, via measurements, for example, of $ed\to e'π^\pm +\rm X $ reactions at Jefferson Laboratory. The next focus is on the role of nuclear short-ranged correlations, which affect the value of the correction needed to account for isospin violation in extracting the value of $V_{ud}$. The net result is that effects previously considered as irrelevant have become relevant for both neutron and nuclear $β$ decay.

## Key claim

Strong interaction dynamics, specifically charge-symmetry breaking in the nucleon wave function and nuclear short-ranged correlations, produce corrections to Fermi beta decay matrix elements that are about the same size as current experimental uncertainties, making effects previously considered irrelevant now relevant for extracting the CKM matrix element \(V_{ud}\).

## Method

The author reviews calculations using the non-relativistic quark model with harmonic oscillator confinement, including the up-down quark mass difference, electromagnetic interaction, and one-gluon exchange, to compute second-order charge-symmetry breaking corrections to the neutron beta decay matrix element. For nuclear superallowed beta decay, the author extends the formalism of Miller and Schwenk (2008, 2009) by developing an effective one-body Coulomb-correction operator and evaluating its matrix element in a system including short-ranged correlations via the reaction matrix (G-matrix) approach, using Wigner-Brillouin perturbation theory.

## Key numbers

- The current most precise value of \(V_{ud}\) from superallowed nuclear decays is \(0.97373 \pm 0.00031\) (Hardy and Towner, 2020); the Particle Data Group gives the same central value with uncertainty \(\pm 0.00014\).
- The charge-symmetry breaking correction to the neutron beta decay matrix element is on the order of \(10^{-4}\), about 100 times larger than the earlier estimate of \(10^{-6}\) by Behrends and Sirlin (1960).
- The isospin-breaking correction \(\delta_C\) for the \(0f_{7/2}\) orbital of \(^{42}\text{Ti}\) is \(0.960(63)\%\) (Hardy and Towner, 2020); a 20% change in that number would alter \(V_{ud}\) by about \(10^{-3}\), larger than the current uncertainty.
- The single-particle Coulomb correction \(\delta_{C0}\) for the \(^{46}\text{V}\) \(f_{7/2}\) state is \(0.267\%\), agreeing with the result of Towner and Hardy (2007).
- Spectroscopic factors from electron scattering experiments typically observe only about 60-70% of the expected number of protons, implying a depletion of 30-40% due to short-range and long-range correlations.

## Context

This work extends the formalism of Miller and Schwenk (2008, 2009), which questioned the Towner-Hardy approach to isospin corrections in superallowed beta decay, by incorporating the effects of nucleon-nucleon short-ranged correlations that were unaccounted for in the original Towner-Hardy formalism. The author connects low-energy beta decay to high-energy measurements at Jefferson Laboratory (experiment E12-09-002) of semi-inclusive pion production from deuterium, which can constrain charge-symmetry breaking in parton distributions. The paper enables future detailed state-of-the-art nuclear calculations of superallowed beta decay, which are a high priority for testing CKM matrix unitarity and the Standard Model.
