# The unexpected uses of a bowling pin: exploiting $^{20}$Ne isotopes for precision characterizations of collectivity in small systems

> Whether or not femto-scale droplets of quark-gluon plasma (QGP) are formed in so-called small systems at high-energy colliders is a pressing question in the phenomenology of the strong interaction. For proton-proton or proton-nucleus collisions the answer is inconclusive due to the large theoretical uncertainties plaguing the description of these processes. While upcoming data on collisions of $^{16}$O nuclei may mitigate these uncertainties in the near future, here we demonstrate the unique possibilities offered by complementing $^{16}$O$^{16}$O data with collisions of $^{20}$Ne ions. We couple both NLEFT and PGCM ab initio descriptions of the structure of $^{20}$Ne and $^{16}$O to hydrodynamic simulations of $^{16}$O$^{16}$O and $^{20}$Ne$^{20}$Ne collisions at high energy. We isolate the imprints of the bowling-pin shape of $^{20}$Ne on the collective flow of hadrons, which can be used to perform quantitative tests of the hydrodynamic QGP paradigm. In particular, we predict that the elliptic flow of $^{20}$Ne$^{20}$Ne collisions is enhanced by as much as 1.170(8)$_{\rm stat.}$(30)$_{\rm syst.}$ for NLEFT and 1.139(6)$_{\rm stat.}$(39)$_{\rm syst.}$ for PGCM relative to $^{16}$O$^{16}$O collisions for the 1% most central events. At the same time, theoretical uncertainties largely cancel when studying relative variations of observables between two systems. This demonstrates a method based on experiments with two light-ion species for precision characterizations of the collective dynamics and its emergence in a small system.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that comparing collisions of deformed $^{20}$Ne nuclei with spherical $^{16}$O nuclei can isolate signatures of initial-state nuclear geometry in the collective flow of hadrons, enabling quantitative tests of the hydrodynamic quark-gluon plasma paradigm in small systems. The key hedge is that this method works only if theoretical uncertainties largely cancel when studying relative variations of observables between two systems of similar mass.

## Method

The authors coupled two independent *ab initio* nuclear structure frameworks, Nuclear Lattice Effective Field Theory (NLEFT) and the Projected Generator Coordinate Method (PGCM), to provide nucleon configurations for $^{16}$O and $^{20}$Ne. These configurations were used as input to the *Trajectum* event-by-event relativistic viscous hydrodynamic framework, which simulated $^{16}$O$^{16}$O and $^{20}$Ne$^{20}$Ne collisions at high energy. The hydrodynamic parameters were sampled from a posterior distribution inferred from a Bayesian analysis of $^{208}$Pb$^{208}$Pb collisions, and final-state hadronic rescattering was computed using the SMASH code.

## Key numbers

- The elliptic flow ratio $v_2\{2\}_{\text{NeNe}} / v_2\{2\}_{\text{OO}}$ for the 0-1% most central events is 1.170(8)$_{\text{stat.}}$(30)$_{\text{syst.}}$ for NLEFT and 1.139(6)$_{\text{stat.}}$(39)$_{\text{syst.}}$ for PGCM.
- The NLEFT charge RMS radii are 2.76 fm for $^{16}$O and 3.17 fm for $^{20}$Ne (ratio 1.14); the clustered PGCM radii are 2.87 fm and 3.09 fm (ratio 1.08), compared to experimental values of 2.6955 fm and 3.0055 fm (ratio 1.11).
- The PGCM $^{16}$O density shows tetrahedral clustering with two short edges of 2.30 fm and two long edges of 2.55 fm.
- The hydrodynamic simulations used a critical temperature of approximately 154 MeV for hadronization.

## Context

This work extends the system-geometry scan approach previously pursued at RHIC comparing $p$$^{197}$Au and $d$$^{197}$Au collisions (PHENIX 2018, STAR 2022-2023) and at the LHC comparing $^{208}$Pb$^{208}$Pb with $p$$^{208}$Pb collisions (CMS 2013, ATLAS 2017, ALICE 2019). It improves upon the planned $^{16}$O$^{16}$O runs (STAR 2023 preliminary data, LHC run expected 2025) by adding $^{20}$Ne as a second light-ion species with an extreme bowling-pin shape. The method enables precision characterizations of collective dynamics in small systems and opens avenues for studying path-length-dependent hard-probe modifications, fixed-target experiments at LHCb, and ultra-peripheral collision imaging of gluon distributions.
