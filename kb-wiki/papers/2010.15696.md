# Independent Normalization for $γ$-ray Strength Functions: The Shape Method

> The Shape method, a novel approach to obtain the functional form of the $γ$-ray strength function ($γ$SF) in the absence of neutron resonance spacing data, is introduced. When used in connection with the Oslo method the slope of the Nuclear Level Density (NLD) is obtained simultaneously. The foundation of the Shape method lies in the primary $γ$-ray transitions which preserve information on the functional form of the $γ$SF. The Shape method has been applied to $^{56}$Fe, $^{92}$Zr, $^{164}$Dy, and $^{240}$Pu, which are representative cases for the variety of situations encountered in typical NLD and $γ$SF studies. The comparisons of results from the Shape method to those from the Oslo method demonstrate that the functional form of the $γ$SF is retained regardless of nuclear structure details or $J^π$ values of the states fed by the primary transitions.

## Key claim
The Shape method provides a mostly model-independent approach to determine the functional form of the gamma-ray strength function (gammaSF) and the slope of the nuclear level density (NLD) when neutron resonance spacing data (D0) are unavailable, by exploiting primary gamma-ray transitions to discrete final states of known spin and parity.

## Method
The method uses the first-generation matrix P(E_gamma, E_i) from the Oslo method and selects two diagonals corresponding to decays to specific final states (e.g., ground state and first excited state). For each initial excitation energy E_i, the counts in the two diagonals are corrected by E_gamma^3 and, if the final states have different spins, by the spin distribution g(E,J). The resulting pairs of gammaSF data points are then connected via a logarithmic interpolation and scaling (sewing technique) to reconstruct the full functional form of the gammaSF.

## Key numbers
- For 56Fe, the NLD at the neutron separation energy S_n = 11.197 MeV was estimated from systematics as rho(S_n) = 2870(680) MeV^-1, with the average total radiative width estimated as 1900(600) meV.
- For 92Zr, the s-wave neutron resonance spacing D0 = 514(15) eV, with S_n = 8.635 MeV and rho(S_n) = 16640(490) MeV^-1.
- For 164Dy, D0 = 6.8(6) eV, S_n = 7.658 MeV, and rho(S_n) = 2.59(52) x 10^6 MeV^-1.
- For 240Pu, D0 = 2.20(9) eV, S_n = 6.534 MeV, and rho(S_n) = 32.7(66) x 10^6 MeV^-1.
- The first-generation matrices used approximately 30-40 keV/channel on both axes, further compressed into bins of approximately 120 keV/channel.

## Context
This work extends the Oslo method (Schiller 2000) by providing a normalization prescription for nuclei where D0 and average total radiative width data are unavailable, which is the case for many nuclei away from stability. The Shape method builds on concepts from the Ratio method (Wiedeking 2012) and the chi^2 method (Krticka 2016, Jones 2018), which also extract gammaSF shapes from primary transitions to discrete states. The method enables systematic application to nuclei studied via the beta-Oslo method (Spyrou 2014, Liddick 2016) and inverse-Oslo method (Ingeberg 2020), addressing the need for reliable gammaSF and NLD inputs for Hauser-Feshbach reaction models used in astrophysical nucleosynthesis calculations.
