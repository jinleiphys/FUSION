# Calibration of nuclear charge density distribution by back-propagation neural networks

> Based on the back-propagation neural networks and density functional theory, a supervised learning is performed firstly to generate the nuclear charge density distributions. The charge density is further calibrated to the experimental charge radii by a composite loss function. It is found that, when the parity, pairing, and shell effects are taken into account, about $96\%$ of the nuclei in the validation set fall within two standard deviations of the predicted charge radii. The calibrated charge density is then mapped to the matter density, and further mapped to the binding energies according to the Hohenberg-Kohn theorem. It provides an improved description of some nuclei in both binding energies and charge radii. Moreover, the anomalous overbinding in $^{48}$Ca implies the existence of an indispensable beyond-mean-field effect.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a back-propagation neural network can calibrate nuclear charge density distributions to experimental charge radii using a composite loss function, achieving about 96% of nuclei in the validation set falling within two standard deviations of predicted charge radii, but only when parity, pairing, and shell effects are explicitly included as input features.

## Method

The authors first train a neural network (charge density generator, CDG) to reproduce charge density distributions from Skyrme Hartree-Fock plus Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (SHF+BCS) theory with the SkM* interaction. They then fine-tune the network using a composite loss function that combines a normalized mean-square-error for the density distribution and a Pearson chi-squared divergence for the charge radius, with a weighting factor of 0.7 favoring experimental data. Three network architectures are tested: a deep fully connected network (CDG-1), a convolutional network (CDG-2), and a network with an explicit feature layer encoding parity, pairing, and shell effects (CDG-3). The calibrated charge density is then mapped to matter density and further to binding energy via a separate feedforward network (CDTBE map) trained on multiple Skyrme interactions.

## Key numbers

- About 96% of nuclei in the validation set fall within two standard deviations of the predicted charge radii for CDG-3 (the network with parity, pairing, and shell effects).
- The accuracy of CDG-3 at one standard deviation is close to 80%.
- For CDG-1 and CDG-2, the number of experimental data falling within two standard deviations does not exceed 80%.
- The training set consists of 640 nuclei (10 batches of 64) from about 900 measured nuclei; the remaining nuclei form the validation set.
- The charge density is computed on a mesh with r_i = 0.1 x i fm for i = 0, 1, ..., 149 (150 grid points).
- The training costs only 10 GPU minutes.
- For Tb isotopes, the predicted charge radii are systematically larger than experimental central values by about 0.1 fm.

## Context

This work extends the use of back-propagation neural networks for nuclear density distributions (Yang 2021) by incorporating experimental charge radii as constraints, going beyond earlier residual-learning approaches for nuclear masses (Niu 2018) and charge radii (Utama 2016, Wu 2020). The method enables consistent prediction of multiple observables (charge radii and binding energies) through the Hohenberg-Kohn map, and the anomalous overbinding in 48Ca suggests the need for beyond-mean-field effects as discussed in Perera (2021). The approach can be migrated to any other theoretical model and provides predictions for unmeasured nuclei such as Dy and Tb isotopes.
