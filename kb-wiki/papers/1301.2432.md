# How "free" are free neutrons in neutron-star crusts and what does it imply for pulsar glitches ?

> The neutron superfluid permeating the inner crust of mature neutron stars is expected to play a key role in various astrophysical phenomena like pulsar glitches. Despite the absence of viscous drag, the neutron superfluid can still be coupled to the solid crust due to non-dissipative entrainment effects. Entrainment challenges the interpretation of pulsar glitches and suggests that a revision of the interpretation of other observed neutron-star phenomena might be necessary.

# How "free" are free neutrons in neutron-star crusts and what does it imply for pulsar glitches?

## Key claim
Due to non-dissipative entrainment effects (Bragg diffraction of unbound neutrons by the crustal lattice), the neutron superfluid in the inner crust is much more strongly coupled to the solid crust than previously assumed, which challenges the standard interpretation of large pulsar glitches as angular momentum transfer from the crustal superfluid to the star. The analysis implies that very active glitching pulsars should have unusually low masses (significantly below 1.4 solar masses), a prediction contradicted by the Vela pulsar's association with a supernova remnant.

## Method
The author applied band theory of solids to systematically compute the density of conduction neutrons (neutrons effectively free to move independently of nuclei) across all regions of the inner crust, characterizing entrainment via an effective neutron mass. The resulting entrainment was then incorporated into the angular momentum balance for glitching pulsars, using the constraint that the product of fractional moments of inertia must exceed a statistical threshold derived from observed glitch activity.

## Key numbers
- The amplitude of pulsar glitches ranges from ΔΩ/Ω ∼ 10⁻⁹ up to ∼ 10⁻⁵.
- The statistical parameter G, defined as (1/t) Σ (ΔΩᵢ / |Ω̇|), is approximately 1.7% from a survey of glitching pulsars.
- The neutron superfluid is most strongly entrained at average baryon densities n̄ ∼ 0.02–0.03 fm⁻³.
- Taking entrainment into account, the quantity (Iₛ)²/(I Iₛₛ) ≈ 0.17 I_crust/I, requiring the fractional moment of inertia of the crust to exceed ∼ 10% to satisfy the glitch constraint.
- The estimated ratio of superfluid moment of inertia to total moment of inertia Iₛ/I is about 1–2% at most when entrainment is neglected.

## Context
This work extends the entrainment formalism first introduced by Carter (2005) and the band-theory calculations of Chamel (2012) to the specific problem of pulsar glitches. It directly contradicts the earlier conclusion by Link, Epstein, and Lattimer (1999) that the crustal superfluid carries sufficient angular momentum to explain glitches, because those authors neglected entrainment. The paper enables a re-examination of crust-core coupling mechanisms, including the possible role of nuclear pastas, crystal defects, and the type of proton superconductivity, as discussed by Alpar (1984), Sedrakian (1995), and others.
