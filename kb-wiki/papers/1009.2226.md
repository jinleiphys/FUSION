# Statistical description of complex nuclear phases in supernovae and proto-neutron stars

> We develop a phenomenological statistical model for dilute star matter at finite temperature, in which free nucleons are treated within a mean-field approximation and nuclei are considered to form a loosely interacting cluster gas. Its domain of applicability, that is baryonic densities ranging from about $ρ>10^8$ g $\cdot$ cm$^{-3}$ to normal nuclear density, temperatures between 1 and 20 MeV and proton fractions between 0.5 and 0, make it suitable for the description of baryonic matter produced in supernovae explosions and proto-neutron stars. The first finding is that, contrary to the common belief, the crust-core transition is not first order, and for all subsaturation densities matter can be viewed as a continuous fluid mixture between free nucleons and massive nuclei. As a consequence, the equations of state and the associated observables do not present any discontinuity over the whole thermodynamic range. We further investigate the nuclear matter composition over a wide range of densities and temperatures. At high density and temperature our model accounts for a much larger mass fraction bound in medium nuclei with respect to traditional approaches as Lattimer-Swesty, with sizeable consequences on the thermodynamic quantities. The equations of state agree well with the presently used EOS only at low temperatures and in the homogeneous matter phase, while important differences are present in the crust-core transition region. The correlation among the composition of baryonic matter and neutrino opacity is finally discussed, and we show that the two problems can be effectively decoupled.

## Key claim

Contrary to the common belief that the crust-core transition in supernovae and proto-neutron star matter is first order, the authors find that for all subsaturation densities matter can be viewed as a continuous fluid mixture between free nucleons and massive nuclei, meaning the equations of state and associated observables present no discontinuity over the whole thermodynamic range.

## Method

The authors develop a phenomenological statistical model where free nucleons are treated within a finite-temperature Hartree-Fock mean-field approximation using the SKM* Skyrme parameterization, while nuclei are considered as a loosely interacting cluster gas described by a Nuclear Statistical Equilibrium approach with excluded volume corrections. The clusterized component is estimated numerically using a Metropolis-Monte-Carlo technique, with cluster binding energies given by a liquid-drop parameterization and Coulomb interaction energy evaluated in the Wigner-Seitz approximation. The model is applied to baryonic densities from about 10^8 g·cm^-3 up to normal nuclear density, temperatures between 1 and 20 MeV, and proton fractions between 0.5 and 0.

## Key numbers

- Density range: from about ρ > 10^8 g·cm^-3 to normal nuclear density (ρ_0 = 0.1603 fm^-3)
- Temperature range: 1 to 20 MeV
- Proton fraction range: 0.5 to 0
- SKM* force parameters: saturation density ρ_0 = 0.1603 fm^-3, binding energy E/A = -15.78 MeV, incompressibility K_∞ = 216.7 MeV, symmetry energy C_sym = 30.03 MeV, effective mass m*/m = 0.79
- Liquid-drop parameters: a_v = 15.4941 MeV, a_s = 17.9439 MeV, a_I = 1.7826
- Critical temperature for surface term vanishing: T_C = 12 MeV
- Level density parameter: a = 0.114 A + 0.098 A^(2/3) MeV^-1 with τ = 9 MeV
- Simulation volume: V = 2.9 × 10^4 fm^3
- Lowest accessible density from finite volume: ρ_min ≈ 1.1 × 10^-4 fm^-3 (with N_min = 1, A_min = 3)

## Context

This work extends the Nuclear Statistical Equilibrium (NSE) approach of Mishustin, Blinnikov, and Souza by incorporating in-medium effects through a mean-field treatment of free nucleons and excluded volume corrections, overcoming the limitation that NSE models cannot describe the crust-core transition. It contrasts with the Lattimer-Swesty (1991) and Shen equation of state approaches, which treat the transition as first order via Maxwell construction and use a single heavy nucleus approximation. The model enables improved calculations of neutrino opacity in supernovae and proto-neutron stars, showing that the composition and opacity problems can be effectively decoupled.
