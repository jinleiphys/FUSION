# The Flow Constraint Influence on the Properties of Nuclear Matter Critical Endpoint

> We propose a novel family of equations of state for symmetric nuclear matter based on the induced surface tension concept for the hard-core repulsion. It is shown that having only four adjustable parameters the suggested equations of state can, simultaneously, reproduce not only the main properties of the nuclear matter ground state, but the proton flow constraint up its maximal particle number densities. Varying the model parameters we carefully examine the range of values of incompressibility constant of normal nuclear matter and its critical temperature which are consistent with the proton flow constraint. This analysis allows us to show that the physically most justified value of nuclear matter critical temperature is 15.5-18 MeV, the incompressibility constant is 270-315 MeV and the hard-core radius of nucleons is less than 0.4 fm.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a novel four-parameter equation of state (EoS) based on the induced surface tension (IST) concept can simultaneously reproduce the ground-state properties of symmetric nuclear matter and obey the proton flow constraint, and that this analysis yields a physically most justified range for the critical temperature of 15.5–18 MeV and the incompressibility constant of 270–315 MeV, but only if the nucleon hard-core radius is less than 0.4 fm.

## Method

The authors construct a family of equations of state for symmetric nuclear matter using the induced surface tension (IST) formalism for hard-core repulsion, with a power-law mean-field attractive potential \(U(n) = C_d^2 n^\kappa\) and a constant surface potential \(U_0\). The four adjustable parameters (hard-core radius \(R\), \(C_d^2\), \(\kappa\), and \(U_0\)) are fixed by requiring that the EoS reproduces the ground state properties (zero pressure at normal density \(n_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\), binding energy per nucleon \(W_0 = -16\) MeV) and simultaneously obeys the proton flow constraint of Danielewicz (2002) up to a particle number density of about 0.75 fm\(^{-3}\). The model is solved for several values of \(\kappa\) (0.1, 0.15, 0.2, 0.25, 0.3) and the resulting incompressibility constant \(K_0\) and critical endpoint parameters are mapped against the flow constraint.

## Key numbers

*   Normal nuclear matter density: \(n_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\).
*   Binding energy per nucleon: \(W_0 = -16\) MeV.
*   Critical temperature range consistent with the proton flow constraint and a hard-core radius \(R \le 0.4\) fm: \(T_c = 15.5\)–\(18\) MeV.
*   Incompressibility constant range consistent with the same constraints: \(K_0 = 270\)–\(315\) MeV.
*   Upper limit on nucleon hard-core radius from the maximal packing fraction \(\eta_{max} = 0.2\): \(R \le 0.4\) fm.
*   For the weak radius constraint \(R \le 0.45\) fm, the allowed lower range of \(K_0\) is \(K_0 \in [230; 250]\) MeV and \(T_c \in [13.2; 14.3]\) MeV.
*   Critical compressibility factor \(Z_c = p_c/(T_c n_c)\) ranges from 0.117 to 0.249 across the parameter sets.
*   For \(\kappa = 0.3\), the hard-core radius is \(R = 0.53\)–\(0.54\) fm, with corresponding \(T_c = 11.16\)–\(11.3\) MeV and \(K_0 = 192\)–\(199\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the induced surface tension (IST) approach for hard-core repulsion, previously applied to hadron resonance gas models by Bugaev et al. (2016, 2017), to symmetric nuclear matter. It directly addresses the proton flow constraint of Danielewicz (2002), which only 104 out of 263 relativistic mean-field (RMF) models analyzed by Dutra et al. (2014) could satisfy. The results are compared to RMF studies by Delfino et al. (2016) and Menezes et al. (2017), and the paper finds that the IST EoS yields critical compressibility factors (\(Z_c \approx 0.117\)–\(0.249\)) much lower than those of RMF models (\(Z_c^{RMF} \approx 0.284\)–\(0.331\)), bridging nuclear matter EoS to those of ordinary non-organic liquids. The analysis enables future systematic studies of correlations between \(K_0\) and \(T_c\) and suggests that the non-analytic density dependence of the interaction pressure may be an inherent property of nuclear Fermi liquids.
