# Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations of nuclear properties in the drip-point region of neutron star crust

> In the present paper we explore the neutron-drip region of cold non-rotating isolated neutron stars. We have performed extended nuclear-structure calculations for nuclei embedded in the electron gas. For modeling the outer crust we use a set of Wigner-Seitz cells, where every cell contains one nucleus surrounded by a cloud of relativistic electrons. Above the drip point a non-relativistic neutron gas occurs in the cell. These calculations are carried out within the Hartree-Fock approach in combination with Skyrme effective interactions. For every baryon density we have determined the configuration with a minimal total energy. The drip elements and corresponding drip densities have been determined for about 240 different parametrizations of Skyrme forces used in the literature. We demonstrate that the calculated drip-point densities depend essentially on the Skyrme parametrization used. Even the drip elements and the occupied shells in the drip region differ for different parametrizations. We have found that the number of neutrons building the neutron gas at the drip point also depends essentially on the Skyrme force chosen. Nevertheless, the number density of the neutron gas in the drip-region is more or less the same ($\sim 10^{-5} \frac{neutrons}{{fm}^{3}}$). The drip densities obtained within our approach are generally lower than predicted earlier.

## Key claim

The neutron drip point in cold non-rotating isolated neutron stars is more likely between 3.0 × 10¹¹ g/cm³ and 4.0 × 10¹¹ g/cm³ than between 4.0 × 10¹¹ g/cm³ and 5.0 × 10¹¹ g/cm³ as found previously, but this result depends essentially on the Skyrme parametrization used, with about 240 different parametrizations yielding different drip elements and occupied shells.

## Method

The authors performed Hartree-Fock calculations with BCS pairing using Skyrme effective interactions for nuclei embedded in a relativistic electron gas within spherical Wigner-Seitz cells. For each baryon density, they determined the configuration with minimal total energy by running through all possible combinations of protons (Z=2 to 130) and neutrons (N=Z-2 to 15×Z) for even-even nuclei. The electron background was treated as uniform, with the cell radius determined from charge neutrality, and the neutron drip condition was defined as the configuration where the neutron chemical potential equals the neutron rest mass.

## Key numbers

- Drip density for SkM* parametrization: 4.6 × 10¹¹ g/cm³ with ⁸²Ti as the drip nucleus
- Drip density range across all parametrizations: between 3.0 × 10¹¹ g/cm³ and 4.0 × 10¹¹ g/cm³ (authors' preferred range), compared to the previously accepted range of 4.0 × 10¹¹ g/cm³ to 5.0 × 10¹¹ g/cm³
- Number density of the neutron gas in the drip region: approximately 10⁻⁵ neutrons/fm³, consistent across different Skyrme forces
- For the liquid-drop model using parameters from Bethge (1996): drip density of 6.658 × 10¹¹ g/cm³ with drip nucleus ¹⁴⁴Nb (Z=41)
- For the liquid-drop model using parameters from Pethick and Ravenhall (1991): drip density of 3.5 × 10¹¹ g/cm³ with drip nucleus ¹²²Y (Z=39)
- For the symmetry-energy-based estimate using SkM* parameters: drip density of 4.274 × 10¹¹ g/cm³
- Electron chemical potential from liquid-drop model with Bethge (1996) parameters: 29.544 MeV
- Electron Fermi momentum from liquid-drop model with Bethge (1996) parameters: 1.133 × 10⁻⁴ fm⁻³, corresponding to electron radius 12.81 fm
- Number of dripped neutrons per cell varies from 2 to 12 depending on the Skyrme force used

## Context

This work extends the earlier thermodynamic approach of Baym, Pethick, and Sutherland (1971), who estimated the drip density at 4.3 × 10¹¹ g/cm³ with ¹¹⁸Kr, and the energy-density functional method of Negele and Vautherin (1973). It also builds on the liquid-drop model estimates of Pethick and Ravenhall (1991) and the symmetry-energy-based estimates of Haensel, Potekhin, and Yakovlev (2007). The authors use the large collection of Skyrme parametrizations compiled by Dutra et al. (2012). The results enable more precise modeling of the inner crust thickness, which affects predictions for tidal deformability in neutron-star mergers and cooling rates of magnetars.
