# Gauge theory of Riemann ellipsoids

> The classical theory of Riemann ellipsoids is formulated naturally as a gauge theory based on a principal G-bundle ${\cal P}$. The structure group G=SO(3) is the vorticity group, and the bundle ${\cal P}=GL_+(3, R})$ is the connected component of the general linear group. The base manifold is the space of positive-definite real $3\times 3$ symmetric matrices, identified geometrically with the space of inertia ellipsoids. The bundle ${\cal P}$ is also a Riemannian manifold whose metric is determined by the kinetic energy. Nonholonomic constraints determine connections on the bundle. In particular, the trivial connection corresponds to rigid body motion, the natural Riemannian connection - to irrotational flow, and the invariant connection - to the falling cat. The curvature form determines the fluid's field tensor which is an analogue of the familiar Faraday tensor.

## Key claim

The classical theory of Riemann ellipsoids can be formulated as a non-Abelian gauge theory where the structure group G=SO(3) is the vorticity group, the bundle is the connected component of the general linear group GL+(3,R), and the base manifold is the space of positive-definite real 3x3 symmetric matrices identified with inertia ellipsoids; the paper demonstrates this correspondence but does not claim that this gauge formulation replaces existing dynamical descriptions, only that it provides a natural mathematical framework.

## Method

The authors construct a principal G-bundle P = GL+(3,R) over the base manifold Q of positive-definite symmetric 3x3 matrices, with structure group G=SO(3) acting by right multiplication. They equip P with a Riemannian metric determined by the kinetic energy and show that nonholonomic constraints correspond to connections on the bundle: the trivial connection corresponds to rigid body motion, the natural Riemannian connection to irrotational flow, and the invariant connection to the falling cat problem. The equations of motion are derived using Lagrange's equations with constraints, and the curvature form is identified as an analogue of the Faraday tensor.

## Key numbers

- The configuration space P = GL+(3,R) has dimension 9 (the connected component of the general linear group of 3x3 real matrices with positive determinant)
- The base manifold Q is six-dimensional (the space of positive-definite real 3x3 symmetric matrices)
- The structure group G = SO(3) is three-dimensional
- For a triaxial rigid ellipsoid with principal half-lengths a1, a2, a3, the moments of inertia are I1 = (MR0^2/5)(a2^2 + a3^2), etc., where M is total mass and R0 is a unit of length
- The kinetic energy of a rotating and vibrating ellipsoid is T = (MR0^2/10)(-tr(A^2 Omega^2) + tr(dot(A)^2)), where A = diag(a1,a2,a3) and Omega is the angular velocity in the rotating frame

## Context

This work extends the gauge theory approach to rotating systems previously developed by Littlejohn and Reinsch and by Shapere and Wilczek (1990s), where the gauge group was the rotation group and the conserved quantity was angular momentum. The present paper presents a fundamentally different model where the gauge group is the vorticity group and the conserved quantity is the Kelvin circulation, while angular momentum is also conserved. The mathematical framework parallels the Weinberg-Salam theory of electroweak interactions (Weinberg, 1967) but with the important difference that the base manifold is the space of ellipsoidal surfaces rather than Minkowski spacetime, making the differential geometry more complicated mathematically.
