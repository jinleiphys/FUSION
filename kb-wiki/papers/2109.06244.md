# Neutrino interactions in liquid scintillators including active-sterile neutrino mixing

> Neutrinos play an important role in core-collapse supernova events since they are a key piece to understand the explosion mechanisms. The analysis of the neutrino fluxes can bring answers to neutrino's related problems e.g.: mass hierarchy, spectral splitting, sterile neutrinos, etc. In this work we study the impact of neutrino oscillations and the possible existence of eV sterile neutrinos upon the supernova neutrino flux $F_ν(E)$. We have calculated the energy distribution of the neutrino flux from a supernova and the total number of events which would be detected in a liquid scintillator. We also present an analysis for the conversion probabilities as a function of the active-sterile neutrino mixing parameters. Finally, we have carried out a statistical analysis to extract values for the mixing parameters of the model.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the inclusion of a light sterile neutrino in the 3+1 mixing scheme can significantly alter the supernova neutrino flux and the number of events detected in a liquid scintillator, but the effect depends strongly on the mixing angle theta_14 and on whether an initial sterile neutrino flux is present in the supernova; the authors hedge that the adiabatic approximation for the sterile resonance is only suitable for certain parameter ranges, specifically for Delta m^2_41 greater than about 10^-2 eV^2 or mixing angles larger than about pi/50.

## Method

The authors computed supernova neutrino fluxes using a power-law or Fermi-Dirac distribution for the initial neutrino spectra, with mean energies of 12, 15, and 18 MeV for nu_e, antinu_e, and nu_x respectively, and a total energy release of 3 x 10^53 erg distributed over 20 seconds. They solved the time-dependent Schroedinger equation for neutrino flavor evolution in matter, including the MSW effect for three active neutrinos and an additional sterile neutrino in the 3+1 scheme, using the crossing probabilities P_H, P_L, and P_S derived from the Landau-Zener formula. The number of inverse beta decay events in a 3 kT liquid scintillator detector (based on alkyl benzene, with 2.2 x 10^32 free protons) was calculated by integrating the antinu_e flux multiplied by the cross section from Strumia and Vissani (2003), for a supernova at 10 kpc distance.

## Key numbers

- Total energy released by the supernova: E_nu^tot = 3 x 10^53 erg, distributed equally among flavors
- Mean energies used for active neutrinos: <E_nu_e> = 12 MeV, <E_antinu_e> = 15 MeV, <E_nu_x> = 18 MeV
- For active-only oscillations with power-law distribution, total events: 803 (normal hierarchy) and 901 (inverse hierarchy)
- For active-only oscillations with FD0 distribution, total events: 830 (NH) and 930 (IH)
- For active-sterile mixing with no initial sterile flux and theta_14 = 0.6, total events: 527 (NH) and 587 (IH)
- For active-sterile mixing with initial sterile flux, theta_14 = 0.6, and <E_nu_s> = 10 MeV, total events: 699 (NH) and 760 (IH)
- Active-sterile square mass difference fixed to Delta m^2_14 = 1.3 eV^2
- Threshold energy for inverse beta decay: E_min = 1.806 MeV
- Number of free protons in the reference 3 kT detector: N_p = 2.2 x 10^32

## Context

This work extends the formalism of McLaughlin, Fetter, Balantekin, and Fuller (1999) for active-sterile neutrino mixing in supernovae, applying it specifically to inverse beta decay detection in liquid scintillators rather than r-process nucleosynthesis. It builds on the SNO+ collaboration (2015) estimates of event rates for different reaction channels and considers the 3+1 mixing scheme with parameters motivated by short-baseline anomalies reported by Aguilar-Arevalo (2018) and Dentler (2018). The results enable future statistical analyses to extract mixing parameters from supernova neutrino signals, and the authors note that the number of events does not depend on the square mass difference but strongly depends on the mixing angle, with inverse hierarchy consistently producing more events than normal hierarchy.
