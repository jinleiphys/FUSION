# Propagation of gauge fields in hot and dense plasmas at higher orders

> Thermal field theory is indispensable for describing hot and dense systems. Yet perturbative calculations are often stymied by a host of energy scales, and tend to converge slowly. This means that precise results require the apt use of effective field theories. In this paper we refine the effective description of slowly varying gauge field known as hard thermal loops. We match this effective theory to the full theory to two-loops. Our results apply for any renormalizable model and fermion chemical potential. We also discuss how to consistently define asymptotic masses at higher orders; and how to treat spectral functions close to the lightcone. In particular, we demonstrate that the gluon mass is well-defined to next-to-leading order.

# Propagation of gauge fields in hot and dense plasmas at higher orders

## Key claim
The effective description of slowly varying gauge fields known as hard thermal loops is refined by matching to the full theory at two-loop order, with the result applying for any renormalizable model and fermion chemical potential. The gluon mass is demonstrated to be well-defined to next-to-leading order, but only after resumming ill-behaved terms that arise from hard particles traveling long distances near the lightcone.

## Method
The author integrates out hard modes with energy of order T using transport equations, calculating the current generated by hard particles propagating in a soft field background. The matching is performed in Feynman gauge by expanding the one-loop self-energy with thermal masses inserted into the hard particle propagators, yielding two-loop corrections to the hard thermal loop effective theory. The resulting Lorentz structures are classified into three conserved forms, with the third structure appearing only when a chemical potential is present.

## Key numbers
- Leading-order photon mass: m_gamma^2 = e^2 T^2/6, with next-to-leading order correction: m_gamma^2 = e^2 T^2/6 - e^4 T^2/(36 pi^2) [log(mu e^gamma/(4 pi T)) - 1/2 + 2 log 2] - (1/(4 pi^2)) e^2 m_F^2 + O(e^6), where m_F^2 = e^2 T^2/4
- Leading-order gluon mass: (m_g^2)_LO = (1/6)(C_A + N_q T_F) g_s^2 T^2 + (1/2) N_q T_F (mu_F^2/pi^2) g_s^2
- For N_q = 6 quarks at zero chemical potential, the next-to-leading order asymptotic gluon mass is roughly 30% smaller than the leading-order one
- Gluon damping rate at next-to-leading order is boosted by roughly a factor of 2 compared to leading order

## Context
This work extends the hard thermal loop effective theory originally developed by Braaten and Pisarski (1989-1991) and Blaizot and Iancu (1993) to two-loop precision, complementing an independent calculation for quantum chromodynamics at finite chemical potential by Gorda et al. (2022). The results enable more accurate predictions for dense astrophysical objects like neutron stars and for particle production in hot plasmas, where next-to-leading order corrections to asymptotic masses and damping rates are essential. The author also provides three-loop predictions based on the pattern of thermal mass resummation, which can guide future calculations.
