# Testing and improving shear viscous phase space correction models

> Comparison of hydrodynamic calculations with experimental data inevitably requires a model for converting the fluid to particles. In this work, nonlinear $2\to 2$ kinetic theory is used to assess the overall accuracy of various shear viscous fluid-to-particle conversion models, such as the quadratic Grad corrections, the Strickland-Romatschke (SR) ansatz, self-consistent shear corrections from linearized kinetic theory, and the correction from the relaxation time approach. We test how well the conversion models can reconstruct, using solely the hydrodynamic fields computed from the transport, the phase space density for a massless one-component gas undergoing a 0+1D longitudinal boost-invariant expansion with approximately constant specific shear viscosity in the range $\sim 0.03 \le η/s \le \sim 0.2$. In general we find that at early times the SR form is the most accurate, whereas at late times or for small $η/s\sim 0.05$ the self-consistent corrections from kinetic theory perform the best. In addition, we show that the reconstruction accuracy of additive shear viscous $f = f_{\rm eq} + δf$ models dramatically improves if one ensures, through "exponentiation", that $f$ is always positive. We also illustrate how even more accurate viscous $δf$ models can be constructed if one includes information about the past evolution of the system via the first time derivative of hydrodynamic fields. Such time derivatives are readily available in hydrodynamic simulations, though usually not included in the output.

## Key claim

The paper tests how well various shear viscous fluid-to-particle conversion models reconstruct the actual phase space density from hydrodynamic fields in a massless one-component gas undergoing 0+1D boost-invariant expansion, finding that the Strickland-Romatschke form is most accurate at early times while self-consistent corrections from linearized kinetic theory perform best at late times or for small specific shear viscosity η/s ∼ 0.05, with the caveat that these conclusions apply to the specific system and expansion geometry studied.

## Method

The authors used nonlinear 2→2 kinetic theory solved numerically with the MPC/Grid transport solver to simulate a massless one-component gas in a 0+1D longitudinal boost-invariant Bjorken expansion with approximately constant specific shear viscosity. They extracted the exact hydrodynamic fields (energy density, shear stress, particle density) from the transport solution and then inverted these fields to determine parameters for four shear viscous δf models: quadratic Grad corrections, Strickland-Romatschke (SR) ansatz, self-consistent corrections from linearized kinetic theory (approximated by a power-law Grad form with exponent α = 3/2), and the relaxation time approximation (RTA, α = 1). The reconstruction accuracy was quantified by the root-mean-squared relative error in the momentum distribution across pT and rapidity-rapidity difference ξ bins.

## Key numbers

* Specific shear viscosity range tested: 0.03 ≤ η/s ≤ 0.2, corresponding to initial inverse Knudsen numbers K0 = 1 to 6.47
* For η/s ≈ 0.2 (K0 = 1), shear stress reached |πL| ≈ 0.4p in magnitude
* Transport simulations used 270 million test particles total, with about 90 million test particles analyzed in the midrapidity window |η| < 2
* RMS errors were evaluated across bins of size ΔpT = 0.16 T0 and Δξ ≈ 0.1, covering pT ≤ 12 Teff and |ξ| ≤ 4, with bins requiring at least 200 counts
* The power-law Grad form with exponent α = 3/2 approximates the self-consistent linearized kinetic theory result where χ(x) ≈ const/√x, giving δf/feq ∼ p^(3/2) momentum dependence

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Huovinen and Molnar (2008) that used transport solutions from the MPC cascade to test formulations of viscous hydrodynamics, but here the focus is on testing particlization models rather than hydrodynamic evolution equations. The paper enables improved fluid-to-particle conversion in hybrid hydrodynamic+transport approaches by demonstrating that "exponentiation" of additive δf models to ensure positivity dramatically improves accuracy, and that including the first time derivative of hydrodynamic fields (readily available in simulations but usually not output) allows intelligent switching between models in their respective regions of validity.
