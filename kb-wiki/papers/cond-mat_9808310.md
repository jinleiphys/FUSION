# Mean-Field Theory for Spin Ladders Using Angular-Momentum Coupled Bases

> We study properties of two-leg Heisenberg spin ladders in a mean-field approximation using a variety of angular-momentum coupled bases. The mean-field theory proposed by Gopalan, Rice, and Sigrist, which uses a rung basis, assumes that the mean-field ground state consists of a condensate of spin-singlets along the rungs of the ladder. We generalize this approach to larger angular-momentum coupled bases which incorporate---by their mere definition---a substantial fraction of the important short-range structure of these materials. In these bases the mean-field ground-state remains a condensate of spin singlet---but now with each involving a larger fraction of the spins in the ladder. As expected, the ``purity'' of the ground-state, as judged by the condensate fraction, increases with the size of the elementary block defining the basis. Moreover, the coupling to quasiparticle excitations becomes weaker as the size of the elementary block increases. Thus, the weak-coupling limit of the theory becomes an accurate representation of the underlying mean-field dynamics. We illustrate the method by computing static and dynamic properties of two-leg ladders in the various angular-momentum coupled bases.

# Mean-Field Theory for Spin Ladders Using Angular-Momentum Coupled Bases

## Key claim

The mean-field theory of Gopalan, Rice, and Sigrist for two-leg Heisenberg spin ladders can be generalized to larger angular-momentum coupled bases (plaquette and 2×4 bases), where the ground state remains a condensate of spin singlets but now each singlet involves a larger fraction of the spins; the weak-coupling limit becomes an accurate representation of the underlying mean-field dynamics as the elementary block size increases, though truncation errors become increasingly severe for the largest bases.

## Method

The authors start from the Heisenberg Hamiltonian for a two-leg spin ladder with antiferromagnetic nearest-neighbor couplings J⊥ (rungs) and J∥ (chains), expressed in second-quantized form using bosonic creation operators for rung singlets and triplets. They implement a mean-field approximation where zero-momentum spin-singlet bosons are replaced by c-numbers (a condensate), then diagonalize the resulting bilinear Hamiltonian via a Bogoliubov transformation to obtain quasiparticle energies and the ground state. The method is generalized from the original rung basis (2×1 blocks) to plaquette (2×2 blocks) and 2×4 blocks by computing the relevant singlet energy ε₀, triplet energy ε₁, and singlet-triplet matrix element α for each basis, then solving the same mean-field equations with these parameters.

## Key numbers

- Ground-state energy per site in the rung basis at λ=1 (isotropic point): the corrected mean-field result is within 6% of the exact answer, compared to 18% error in the original Gopalan, Rice, and Sigrist (1994) calculation
- Singlet energy ε₀ = -2 and triplet energy ε₁ = -1 for the plaquette basis (2×2 block), with singlet-triplet matrix element α = -1/3
- Ratio |α/Δ₀| improves from 1/2 in the rung basis to 1/3 in the plaquette basis
- For the 2×4 basis: ε₀ = -4.29307, ε₁ = -3.52286, α = -0.20805 (≈ -1/5), giving |α/Δ₀| ≈ 1/4
- The spin-spin correlation length ξ is given analytically by ξ = 1/ln[d/(1-√(1-d²))], where d is a parameter determined from the condensate fraction

## Context

This work extends the mean-field theory of Gopalan, Rice, and Sigrist (1994) for two-leg Heisenberg spin ladders, which originally used a rung basis where each rung forms a spin-singlet condensate. The authors correct a factor-of-three error in the zero-point motion term from that earlier work, finding that the corrected rung-basis results agree much better with known properties from density matrix renormalization group calculations (White 1994) and other methods. The plaquette basis, introduced previously by the same authors (Piekarewicz and Shepard 1997), offers the best compromise between incorporating short-range physics and avoiding severe truncations, enabling reliable calculations of static properties (energy, spin gap, correlation length) and dynamic spin response functions that could be compared with future inelastic neutron scattering experiments on single crystals.
