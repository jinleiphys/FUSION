# Can very compact and very massive neutron stars both exist?

> The existence of neutron stars with masses of $\sim 2\,M_{\odot}$ requires a stiff equation of state at high densities. On the other hand, the necessary appearance also at high densities of new degrees of freedom, such as hyperons and $Δ$ resonances, can lead to a strong softening of the equation of state with resulting maximum masses of $\sim 1.5\, M_{\odot}$ and radii smaller than $\sim 10$ km. Hints for the existence of compact stellar objects with very small radii have been found in recent statistical analysis of quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries in globular clusters. We propose an interpretation of these two apparently contradicting measurements, large masses and small radii, in terms of two separate families of compact stars: hadronic stars, whose equation of state is soft, can be very compact, while quark stars, whose equation of state is stiff, can be very massive. In this respect an early appearance of $Δ$ resonances is crucial to guarantee the stability of the branch of hadronic stars. Our proposal could be tested by measurements of radii with an error of $\sim 1$ km, which is within reach of the planned LOFT satellite, and it would be further strengthened by the discovery of compact stars heavier than $\sim 2\,M_{\odot}$.

# Can very compact and very massive neutron stars both exist?

## Key claim

The apparent contradiction between observations of very massive neutron stars (~2 solar masses) and very compact neutron stars (radii <10 km) can be resolved if these objects belong to two separate families: hadronic stars (soft equation of state, very compact, lower maximum mass) and quark stars (stiff equation of state, less compact, higher maximum mass). The early appearance of Δ resonances in hadronic matter is crucial to stabilize the hadronic branch and allow very small radii.

## Method

The authors construct hadronic equations of state using a relativistic mean field model (GM3 parametrization) including nucleons, hyperons, and Δ resonances, with two choices for the Δ-meson coupling constants (x_σΔ=1.25, x_ωΔ=1 and x_σΔ=1.15, x_ωΔ=0.9). For quark stars, they use the MIT bag model with effective bag constant B_eff and perturbative QCD parameter a_4, adopting two parameter sets from Weissenborn (2011). They compute mass-radius relations for both families and compare with observational constraints from quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of hadronic stars containing both Δ resonances and hyperons is close to 1.5 solar masses for the parameter sets considered
- Hadronic star radii can reach down to 8 km (red dashed line in their Fig. 2)
- Quark star set1 (B_eff^(1/4)=142 MeV, a_4=0.9) allows a maximum mass of 2 solar masses
- Quark star set2 (B_eff^(1/4)=127 MeV, a_4=0.6) reaches a maximum mass of 2.4 solar masses
- Quark stars with masses above ~1.5-1.6 solar masses have radii greater than ~11 km
- The appearance of hyperons reduces the maximum mass of hadronic stars by ~0.1-0.2 solar masses
- The symmetry energy at saturation in the GM3 model is S_v=32.5 MeV
- Hyperon potential depths used: U_Λ^N=-28 MeV, U_Σ^N=+30 MeV, U_Ξ^N=-18 MeV

## Context

This work addresses the tension between the 2 solar mass neutron star discoveries (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013) requiring stiff equations of state and statistical analyses of quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries (Guillot 2013) suggesting very small radii. Previous two-family models (Berezhiani 2002, Bombaci 2004) did not simultaneously satisfy both constraints. The proposal can be tested by radius measurements with ~1 km precision, within reach of the planned LOFT satellite, and would be further strengthened by discovery of compact stars heavier than 2 solar masses, such as the candidate PSR B1957+20 with estimated mass 2.4 solar masses (van Kerkwijk 2010).
