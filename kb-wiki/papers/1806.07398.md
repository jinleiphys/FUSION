# Manifestly Soft Gauge Invariant Formulation of vNRQCD

> Homogeneous power counting in Non-Relativistic QCD (NRQCD) implies the simultaneous existence of both soft and ultrasoft gluons. In the velocity renormalization group (vNRQCD) formalism we show that operators involving soft fields interacting with heavy potential quarks can be put in a manifestly gauge invariant form by utilizing gluon and quark building blocks which contain Wilson lines, and are analogous to those used in the soft collinear effective theory. This leads to several simplifications, in particular significantly reducing the size of the operator basis, which stream-lines matching and anomalous dimension calculations at subleading order in the velocity expansion. Also, soft ghosts no longer couple via potential like interactions to the heavy quark fields, and hence do not appear until two loops. Furthermore the the color structures that appear at each order in $α_s$ in the static potential are only those which should arise according to non-Abelian exponentiation. We also discuss how zero-bin subtractions clarify the role of $i\varepsilon$ terms in the heavy quark propagator poles carrying soft momenta. Even though the choice of the direction of the soft Wilson lines in our building blocks does not matter, there is still a limited set of consistent choices, and the predicted form of the eikonal poles does influence results at two-loops and beyond.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that operators involving soft fields interacting with heavy potential quarks in the velocity renormalization group (vNRQCD) formalism can be reformulated in a manifestly soft gauge invariant form using Wilson line building blocks analogous to those in soft collinear effective theory, though the choice of the direction of the soft Wilson lines does not matter only if proper zero-bin subtractions are performed, and the predicted form of eikonal poles influences results at two-loops and beyond.

## Method

The authors construct soft gauge invariant building blocks for gluons and quarks by attaching soft Wilson lines in the time-like direction to the soft covariant derivative and soft quark fields, respectively. They then match the full QCD Compton scattering amplitudes onto a manifestly gauge invariant vNRQCD Lagrangian containing operators built from these building blocks, using on-shell conditions and equations of motion to determine the Wilson coefficients at tree level and discussing the structure of one-loop matching. The formalism is applied to simplify the calculation of anomalous dimensions of the heavy quark potential to order O(v^2) and to clarify the role of iε terms in heavy quark propagator poles carrying soft momenta via zero-bin subtractions.

## Key numbers

- The leading power coefficient for the spatial gluon operator is U_{ij}^{(0)} = -2 q^0 δ_{ij} / (p' - p)^2, matching the previous result from Luke et al. (1999)
- The soft building block B^μ scales as v and the soft quark building block Ξ scales as v^{3/2} in the velocity power counting
- Soft ghosts no longer couple via potential-like interactions to heavy quark fields and do not appear until two loops
- The color structures appearing at each order in α_s in the static potential are only those which should arise according to non-Abelian exponentiation

## Context

This work extends the vNRQCD formalism of Luke et al. (1999) by introducing manifest soft gauge invariance, following ideas from Rothstein et al. (2016) in the context of forward scattering in soft collinear effective theory. The reformulation significantly reduces the size of the operator basis compared to the original vNRQCD approach of Manohar et al. (1999-2000), streamlining matching and anomalous dimension calculations at subleading order in the velocity expansion. The formalism enables simplified computation of the running of subleading potentials and clarifies the role of zero-bin subtractions from Manohar et al. (2006) in resolving iε prescription issues relevant to the two-loop Coulomb potential calculations of Peter (1996) and Schroder (1998).
