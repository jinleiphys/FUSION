# On Quantum Scattering of a System with Internal Degrees of Freedom

> We study the quantum tunnelling of a very complex object of which only part is coupled to an external potential ( the potential barrier ). We treat this problem as the tunnelling of a particle (part of the system affected by the potential) coupled to a ``heat bath" ( all of the remaining degrees of freedom ) from the point of view of the wavefunction of the whole system. We obtain an effective scattering equation for this wavefunction which resembles the integral equation for the wavefunction of the particle in the absence of the bath. We show that the effect of the interaction is to renormalize the mass and the potential of the problem and that these new parameters can be solely described in terms of the spectral function of the excitations of the system.

## Key claim

The paper shows that a complex system (e.g., a heavy nucleus or large molecule) tunneling through a potential barrier behaves as a single particle with a renormalized mass, subject to a temperature-dependent effective potential, provided the internal degrees of freedom remain in thermal equilibrium during the scattering process.

## Method

The authors model the complex system as a single particle (the degree of freedom that feels the external potential) linearly coupled to a finite set of harmonic oscillators (the internal degrees of freedom). They diagonalize the Hamiltonian in the absence of the external potential using a unitary transformation, then reintroduce the potential as a perturbation. By averaging over the oscillator states in thermal equilibrium, they derive an effective scattering equation for the center-of-mass wavefunction, which takes the form of a standard scattering equation but with a renormalized mass and a Gaussian-smeared effective potential.

## Key numbers

* The renormalized mass of the system is \( M = m + \sum_j \mu_j \), where \( \mu_j = C_j^2 / (m_j \omega_j^4) \).
* For a Gaussian bare potential \( V(x) = V_0 \exp(-x^2/a^2) \), the effective potential height is reduced by a factor \( \sqrt{1 + 4\Gamma^2/a^2} \), and the effective width is increased by the same factor.
* The transmission coefficient for high and thin barriers ( \( \kappa a \gg 1 \) ) is approximately \( |t|^2 \simeq 16 (k\kappa/(k^2+\kappa^2))^2 e^{-4\kappa \langle x \rangle_{\text{eff}}} \), where \( \langle x \rangle_{\text{eff}} = a \sqrt{1 + 4\Gamma^2/a^2} \).
* The exponent in the transmission coefficient is \( 4\kappa \langle x \rangle_{\text{eff}} = 4\sqrt{2 M V_0 a^2} \sqrt{\sqrt{1+4r^2} - (1+4r^2)f} \), where \( r = \Gamma/a \) and \( f = E_K/V_0 \).

## Context

This work extends the formalism of dissipative quantum tunneling developed by Caldeira and Leggett (1983) and Leggett et al. (1987), but applies it to a finite system where the total energy is conserved and the internal degrees of freedom move together with the particle toward the barrier. The approach differs from the path-integral methods used in those prior works by employing a time-independent scattering formalism. The results enable the study of tunneling of complex nuclei or molecules through electromagnetic or other barriers, with the spectral function of the internal excitations determining the renormalized parameters.
