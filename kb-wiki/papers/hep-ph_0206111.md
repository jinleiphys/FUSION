# Physics of Event Generators

> An event generator for nuclear collisions is a microscopic model, obtained from extrapolating elementary interactions -- as electron-positron annihilation, deep inelastic scattering, and proton-proton interactions -- towards proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus scattering, by using Monte Carlo techniques. In this paper, we will discuss the physical concepts behind such event generators. We first present some qualitative discussion of nuclear scattering, before discussing particle production and strings. We then discuss the parton model, and finally multiple scattering theory.

## Key claim

Event generators for nuclear collisions must divide the reaction into distinct stages (initial, pre-equilibrium, QGP, phase transition, hadron gas, freeze-out) because no single formalism can account for a complete nucleus-nucleus collision; the initial stage in particular requires a fully quantum mechanical treatment, and semi-classical hadronic cascades cannot describe the very first interactions since longitudinal string excitation is kinematically impossible.

## Method

The paper reviews the physical concepts behind event generators by first discussing nuclear scattering with Lorentz contraction and the space-time picture of heavy-ion collisions, then presenting the string model for particle production (using the Nambu-Goto Lagrangian and gauge-fixed wave equations), followed by the parton model for deep inelastic scattering and proton-proton interactions, and finally multiple scattering theory based on Gribov-Regge theory with cut diagrams representing unitarity. The author compares several existing approaches: Pythia and HIJING (parton model based), QGS, DPM, and VENUS (Gribov-Regge based), and the newer NEXUS event generator which implements a "Parton-based Gribov-Regge Theory" that treats energy conservation consistently for both cross sections and particle production.

## Key numbers

* At RHIC, the Lorentz gamma factor is gamma = 100; at LHC it is about gamma = 3000, making the longitudinal size of nuclei approximately 0.1 fm at RHIC and 0.01 fm at LHC.
* Electron-positron annihilation data are available up to 200 GeV in the center-of-mass system.
* The string tension kappa appears in the Nambu-Goto Lagrangian L = -kappa sqrt((X' dot X)^2 - X'^2 X_dot^2).
* The soft virtuality scale Q0 is of the order 1 GeV, used to define the mass of the soft object as m^2 = Q0^2 / x.
* Rapidity distributions from flat strings are shown at 14, 22, and 34 GeV, with constant plateau height as energy increases.
* Hadron production data in e+e- annihilation are shown at 29 GeV and 91 GeV.

## Context

This invited lecture (Pan-American Advanced Study Institute, 2002) reviews the theoretical foundations of event generators for nuclear collisions, extending earlier work on string models (e.g., the VENUS model by Werner (1993)) and Gribov-Regge theory (Kaidalov (1982), Capella (1994), Aurenche (1994)). The paper contrasts the parton model approach used in Pythia (Sjostrand (1987)) and HIJING (Wang (1996)) with the newer NEXUS model (Drescher (2000)) that solves the inconsistency between cross section and particle production calculations. It enables future work on the pre-equilibrium stage via parton cascades (Geiger (1992)), hydrodynamical evolution (Baym (1984), Rischke (1998), Aguiar (2000)), and hadronic transport codes like UrQMD (Bass (1998), Bleicher (1999)), ART (Li (1995)), and JAM (Nara (1997)).
