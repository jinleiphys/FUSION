# Relativistic energy-density functional approach to magnetic-dipole excitation and its sum rule

> Magnetic-dipole (M1) excitations of $^{18}$O and $^{42}$Ca nuclei are investigated within a relativistic nuclear energy density functional framework. In our last work \cite{2019OP}, these nuclei are found to have unique M1 excitation and its sum rule, because of their characteristic structure: the system consists of the shell-closure core plus two neutrons. For a more systematic investigation of the M1 mode, we have implemented a framework based on the relativistic nuclear energy density functional (RNEDF). For benchmark, we have performed the RNEDF calculations combined with the random-phase approximation (RPA). We evaluate the M1 excitation of $^{18}$O and $^{42}$Ca, whose sum-rule value (SRV) of the M1 transitions can be useful to test the computational implementation \cite{2019OP}. We also apply this RNEDF method to $^{208}$Pb, whose M1 property has been precisely measured \cite{1979Holt,1987Koehler,1988Laszewski,2016Birkhan}. Up to the level of the M1 sum rule, our result is in agreement with the experiments, except the discrepancy related with the quenching factors for $g$ coefficients.

## Key claim

The relativistic nuclear energy density functional (RNEDF) framework combined with random-phase approximation (RPA) reproduces the no-pairing sum-rule value (SRV) for M1 excitations in \(^{18}\)O and \(^{42}\)Ca consistently with a three-body model, and for \(^{208}\)Pb the calculated SRV agrees with experiment only after applying a quenching factor \(\zeta \simeq 0.6-0.7\) to the \(g\) coefficients, which the authors fix to \(\zeta=1\) in their baseline calculation.

## Method

The authors employed a relativistic mean-field model at the Hartree level for ground-state properties, using the point-coupling interaction DD-PC1, and described nuclear excitations via the relativistic random-phase approximation (RPA). They computed the M1 excitation strength \(B_{\rm M1}(E_{\gamma})\) for transitions from the \(0^{+}\) ground state to \(1^{+}\) excited states, assuming spherical symmetry and neglecting pairing correlations. The M1 operator was constructed from single-particle spin and orbital angular-momentum operators with free-nucleon \(g\) coefficients, and the sum-rule value \(S_{\rm M1}\) was obtained by integrating \(B_{\rm M1}(E_{\gamma})\) up to 60 MeV.

## Key numbers

* For \(^{18}\)O, the M1 sum-rule value \(S_{\rm M1} = 2.73\ \mu_{\rm N}^2\) from RNEDF (DD-PC1), compared to 2.79 \(\mu_{\rm N}^2\) from the three-body model (3BM) of Oishi (2019).
* For \(^{42}\)Ca, the M1 sum-rule value \(S_{\rm M1} = 2.91\ \mu_{\rm N}^2\) from RNEDF, compared to 2.99 \(\mu_{\rm N}^2\) from the three-body model.
* For \(^{208}\)Pb, the M1 sum-rule value \(S_{\rm M1} = 52.86\ \mu_{\rm N}^2\) from RNEDF, while the experimental SRV is 15–20 \(\mu_{\rm N}^2\) from Laszewski (1988) and Birkhan (2016); applying a quenching factor \(\zeta \simeq 0.6-0.7\) reduces the calculated value to approximately 20 \(\mu_{\rm N}^2\).
* The mean-excitation energy for \(^{208}\)Pb is measured as 7.3 MeV from Holt (1979), Koehler (1987), Laszewski (1988), and Birkhan (2016), and the RNEDF calculation yields a mean-excitation energy in good agreement with this value.

## Context

This work extends the three-body model study of Oishi (2019) for M1 excitations in \(^{18}\)O and \(^{42}\)Ca to a fully relativistic mean-field framework, enabling systematic calculations across the nuclear chart. The RNEDF approach is benchmarked against the no-pairing sum rule from that prior work and then applied to \(^{208}\)Pb, where experimental data from Holt (1979), Koehler (1987), Laszewski (1988), and Birkhan (2016) are available. The results enable future studies of the pseudo-vector interaction terms in the residual RPA interaction and the inclusion of pairing effects via the relativistic quasiparticle random-phase approximation, which are needed to improve agreement with experiment and to predict M1 and Gamow-Teller excitations for applications such as neutrino-nucleus reactions in supernova physics.
