# Density dependencies of interaction strengths and their influence on nuclear matter and neutron star in relativistic mean field theory

> The density dependencies of various effective interaction strengths in the relativistic mean field are studied and carefully compared for nuclear matter and neutron stars. The influences of different density dependencies are presented and discussed on mean field potentials, saturation properties for nuclear matter, equations of state, maximum masses and corresponding radii for neutron stars. Though the interaction strengths and the potentials given by various interactions are quite different in nuclear matter, the differences of saturation properties are subtle, except for NL2 and TM2, which are mainly used for light nuclei, while the properties by various interactions for pure neutron matter are quite different. To get an equation of state for neutron matter without any ambiguity, it is necessary to constrain the effective interactions either by microscopic many-body calculations for the neutron matter data or the data of nuclei with extreme isospin. For neutron stars, the interaction with large interaction strengths give strong potentials and large Oppenheimer-Volkoff (OV) mass limits. The density-dependent interactions DD-ME1 and TW-99 favor a large neutron population due to their weak $ρ$-meson field at high densities. The OV mass limits calculated from different equations of state are 2.02 $\sim$ 2.81$ M_\odot$, and the corresponding radii are 10.78 $\sim$ 13.27 km. After the inclusion of the hyperons, the corresponding values become 1.52 $\sim$ 2.06 $M_\odot$ and 10.24 $\sim$ 11.38 km.

## Key claim

The paper systematically compares the density dependencies of effective interaction strengths in relativistic mean field (RMF) theory for both nonlinear and density-dependent couplings, finding that while the interaction strengths and potentials differ substantially across models, the saturation properties of symmetric nuclear matter are nearly identical for most interactions (except NL2 and TM2), whereas pure neutron matter properties show large model dependence.

## Method

The authors solved the RMF equations for symmetric nuclear matter, pure neutron matter, and neutron star matter using nine different effective interactions: the nonlinear models NL1, NL2, NL3, NLSH, TM1, TM2, and GL-97, and the density-dependent models TW-99 and DD-ME1. For each interaction, they computed the density-dependent effective coupling strengths for the sigma, omega, and rho mesons, the resulting scalar and vector potentials, and the equations of state, then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (OV) equations to obtain neutron star masses and radii, both with and without hyperon degrees of freedom.

## Key numbers

- Saturation densities for symmetric nuclear matter are similar across interactions: rho_0 = 0.166 +/- 0.018 fm^{-3}, with binding energies E_B/A = -16.33 +/- 0.10 MeV, except for TM2 (rho_0 = 0.132 fm^{-3}) and NL2 (larger E_B/A)
- The difference in scalar potentials between NL2 and TM2 at rho = 0.15 fm^{-3} is about 120 MeV, and the difference in vector potentials is about 130 MeV
- For pure neutron matter at rho = 0.15 fm^{-3}, the largest difference in total potentials between NL2 and TW-99 is 40 MeV
- The OV mass limits from different equations of state range from 2.02 to 2.81 solar masses, with corresponding radii from 10.78 to 13.27 km
- After including hyperons, the OV mass limits become 1.52 to 2.06 solar masses, with radii from 10.24 to 11.38 km
- The incompressibility K for GL-97 is 240 MeV, with effective mass m*/m = 0.78

## Context

This work extends the systematic comparison of RMF interactions by examining both nonlinear models (developed by Joon et al. (1986), Lalazissis et al. (1997), Sharma et al. (1993), Sugahara and Toki (1994), and Glendenning (1997)) and density-dependent models (TW-99 by Typel and Wolter (1999), and DD-ME1 by Niksic et al. (2002)). The study enables a clearer understanding of which interaction features are robust for neutron star modeling and which require further constraint from microscopic many-body calculations or data from nuclei with extreme isospin.
