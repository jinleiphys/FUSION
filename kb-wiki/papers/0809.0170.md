# Bimodality and Coulomb effects with a canonical thermodynamic model

> The effect of the Coulomb interaction on the phase diagram of finite nuclei is studied within the Canonical Thermodynamic Model. If Coulomb effects are artificially switched off, this model shows a phenomenology consistent with the liquid-gas phase transition. The inclusion of Coulomb does not significantly affect the phase diagram but it drastically modifies the nature and order parameter of the transition. A clear understanding of the phenomenon can be achieved looking at the distribution of the largest fragment produced in each fragmentation event. Possible connections with experimental observations are outlined.

# Bimodality and Coulomb effects with a canonical thermodynamic model

## Key claim

The Canonical Thermodynamic Model (CTM) shows a phenomenology consistent with a liquid-gas phase transition only when Coulomb interactions are artificially switched off; with full Coulomb included, the transition temperature decreases and the fragmentation pattern changes from liquid-gas to a transition from evaporation to asymmetric fission, but the phase diagram itself is not significantly altered.

## Method

The authors use the Canonical Thermodynamic Model (CTM) to calculate the partition function for a fragmenting nuclear source with A=150 nucleons and Z=75 protons, allowing break-up into all possible clusters within drip-lines. They compute the equation of state (pressure versus density), the distribution of the largest fragment charge (z₁) and second largest fragment charge (z₂), the specific heat cᵥ, and the bimodality signal, varying the Coulomb strength parameter x_c from 0 (no Coulomb) to 1 (full Coulomb). The freeze-out density is treated as a free parameter, and the Wigner-Seitz approximation is used to incorporate Coulomb interactions between composites.

## Key numbers

- For the symmetric system A=150, Z=75 with no Coulomb (x_c=0), the backbending in the P-ρ isotherms indicates a first-order transition; the bimodality line and the maximum of cᵥ coincide and fall in the backbending region.
- For the fully charged system (x_c=1, A=150, Z=75), the isotherms become monotonically increasing and the phase transition disappears; the system is beyond the proton drip-line.
- For the intermediate Coulomb strength x_c=0.64, the system A=150, Z=75 has the same Coulomb energy as the physical system A=150, Z=60.
- At the bimodality point without Coulomb (x_c=0), the liquid-like peak corresponds to a dominant cluster of about 75% of the total mass, while the gas-like peak has the largest fragment at about 25% of the total size.
- With full Coulomb (x_c=1), the high-density solution corresponds to an evaporation residue, and the low-density solution has the largest fragment peaked at z₁ = Z/2 and the second largest fragment ranging from z₂ = Z/12 to z₂ = Z/2.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of phase transitions in finite nuclear systems using the CTM (Das Gupta 2001) and addresses the debate on bimodality as a signature of first-order transitions raised by experiments on quasi-projectile fragmentation of Au+Au (Pichon 2006, Bruno 2007, Bonnet 2007). The results contradict the expectation from mean-field models of infinite nuclear matter (Ducoin 2007, Serot 1992) that isospin effects strongly affect the equation of state; in CTM, isospin has little influence on the P-ρ correlation. The finding that energy fluctuations peak at a density lower than the spinodal region in charged systems is consistent with the MMM fragmentation model study of Raduta (2007). The authors note that CTM produces results close to the Copenhagen Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) (Botvina 2005), suggesting the results are characteristic of statistical equilibrium models. The work enables future comparison of CTM with experimental bimodality data from Au+Au collisions to resolve the ongoing debate on the interpretation of the observed transition (Lacroix 2007, Trautmann 2007, Aichelin 2007).
