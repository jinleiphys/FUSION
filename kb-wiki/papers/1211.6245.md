# "Strongly interacting matter in magnetic fields": an overview

> This is an introduction to the volume of Lecture Notes in Physics on "Strongly interacting matter in magnetic fields". The volume combines contributions written by a number of experts on different aspects of the problem. The response of QCD matter to intense magnetic fields has attracted a lot of interest recently. On the theoretical side, this interest stems from the possibility to explore the plethora of novel phenomena arising from the interplay of magnetic field with QCD dynamics. On the experimental side, the interest is motivated by the recent results on the behavior of quark-gluon plasma in a strong magnetic field created in relativistic heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC. The purpose of this introduction is to provide a brief overview and a guide to the individual contributions where these topics are covered in detail.

## Key claim

This introductory chapter to a Lecture Notes volume claims that the response of QCD matter to intense magnetic fields has attracted recent interest both theoretically (to explore novel phenomena from the interplay of magnetic fields with QCD dynamics) and experimentally (motivated by results on quark-gluon plasma behavior at RHIC and LHC), but the authors explicitly state that the volume does not express a "final word" and instead serves as a snapshot of ongoing work with many open questions.

## Method

The paper is an editorial overview of a multi-author Lecture Notes volume, summarizing contributions on two main themes: equilibrium properties of QCD matter in magnetic fields (phase structure, magnetic catalysis, inverse magnetic catalysis) and anomaly-induced transport phenomena (Chiral Magnetic Effect, Chiral Vortical Effect). The authors describe the theoretical tools employed across the volume's chapters, including lattice QCD, Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) and Polyakov-loop extended NJL (PNJL) models, the MIT bag model, quark-meson models, and holographic approaches via the AdS/CFT correspondence (Sakai-Sugimoto model, D3/D7-brane setups).

## Key numbers

- Magnetic field strength in relativistic heavy ion collisions: \( eB \sim m_{\pi}^2 \) (approximately \( 10^{18} \) G)
- Magnetic field on magnetar surfaces: \( \sim 10^{15} \) G, possibly much larger in stellar interiors
- QCD scale: \( \Lambda_{\rm QCD} \sim 200 \) MeV, corresponding to magnetic field strengths \( B \sim (200\ {\rm MeV})^2 \simeq 2 \times 10^{18} \) G
- For hot medium at zero chemical potential, lattice studies with physical quark masses show the (pseudo-)critical temperature \( T_c \) for the chiral crossover decreases with magnetic field, while model calculations show a monotonically increasing \( T_c \)
- For dense medium at zero temperature, the critical chemical potential can decrease with magnetic field (inverse magnetic catalysis), in contrast to the naive expectation from magnetic catalysis

## Context

This overview introduces a Lecture Notes volume that extends and synthesizes recent work on QCD matter in magnetic fields, covering both equilibrium phase structure and non-equilibrium transport phenomena. The volume addresses magnetic catalysis (reviewed in Shovkovy, 2012) and inverse magnetic catalysis (Preis et al., 2012), the Chiral Magnetic Effect and Chiral Vortical Effect (reviewed in Zhitnitsky, 2012; Fukushima, 2012; Basar, 2012; Zakharov, 2012), and holographic approaches to condensed matter systems (Albash, 2012; D'Hoker and Kraus, 2012). The work enables future experimental tests at RHIC and LHC, particularly the proposed Uranium-Uranium collision test to separate CME from backgrounds, with the STAR Collaboration having presented results at Quark Matter 2012 indicating the signal vanishes in the absence of a magnetic field.
