# Nuclear and dark matter heating in massive white dwarf stars

> Recently, Cheng et al. identified a number of massive white dwarfs (WD) that appear to have an additional heat source providing a luminosity near $\approx 10^{-3}L_\odot$ for multiple Gyr. In this paper we explore heating from electron capture and pycnonuclear reactions. We also explore heating from dark matter annihilation. WD stars appear to be too small to capture enough dark matter for this to be important. Finally, if dark matter condenses to very high densities inside a WD this could ignite nuclear reactions. We calculate the enhanced central density of a WD in the gravitational potential of a very dense dark matter core. While this might start a supernova, it seems unlikely to provide modest heating for a long time. We conclude that electron capture, pycnonuclear, and dark matter reactions are unlikely to provide significant heating in the massive WD that Cheng considers.

# Nuclear and dark matter heating in massive white dwarf stars

## Key claim
Electron capture, pycnonuclear fusion, and dark matter annihilation are all unlikely to provide the additional heat source (luminosity near ≈10⁻³ L⊙ for multiple Gyr) observed in massive white dwarfs with masses between 1.08 and 1.23 M⊙, because the required central densities are too low in these stars and dark matter capture rates are insufficient.

## Method
The author calculated threshold densities for electron capture reactions on ¹²C, ¹⁶O, ²⁰Ne, ⁵⁶Fe, and ⁵⁴Fe using atomic masses and compared these to the central densities of white dwarfs of various masses from a relativistic free Fermi gas equation of state. For pycnonuclear fusion, the required reaction rate for ¹²C+¹²C to produce 10⁻³ L⊙ was estimated and compared to published rates. Dark matter heating was bounded by assuming every particle contacting the star is captured, using conservation of angular momentum to derive an upper limit on the capture cross section. The effect of a dense dark matter core on the central density of conventional matter was calculated from hydrostatic equilibrium.

## Key numbers
- The observed additional luminosity is ≈10⁻³ L⊙ for multiple Gyr (from Cheng et al. 2019)
- Central density of a 1.23 M⊙ C/O white dwarf is only 1.9×10⁸ g/cm³
- Threshold density for ²⁰Ne electron capture is 6.2×10⁹ g/cm³, reached at 1.40 M⊙
- Threshold density for ¹²C electron capture is 3.9×10¹⁰ g/cm³, reached at 1.42 M⊙
- Threshold density for ⁵⁶Fe electron capture is 1.1×10⁹ g/cm³, reached at 1.16 M⊙
- Threshold density for ⁵⁴Fe electron capture is 2.1×10⁷ g/cm³, reached at 0.80 M⊙
- Threshold density for ¹²C+¹²C pycnonuclear fusion is ≈3×10⁹ g/cm³, reached at 1.38 M⊙
- Upper limit on dark matter heating luminosity for a 1.2 M⊙ C/O white dwarf: L_D ≤ 3×10⁻⁹ L⊙
- Dark matter capture cross section uses v_d ≈ 220 km/s and ρ_d ≈ 0.4 GeV c⁻² cm⁻³
- Pycnonuclear fusion requires a reaction rate of roughly R ≈ 5×10¹¹ cm⁻³ s⁻¹ to produce 10⁻³ L⊙
- Central density of a 1.25 M⊙ white dwarf is ρ_c⁰ = 2×10⁸ g/cm³

## Context
This paper addresses the puzzle raised by Cheng et al. (2019), who identified massive white dwarfs with an unexplained heat source that cannot be explained by latent heat from crystallization or gravitational energy from ²²Ne sedimentation (Bildsten 2001). The work extends prior studies of electron capture (1990) and pycnonuclear fusion (1969, 2006) to the specific mass range of these observed stars, and also considers dark matter scenarios explored by others (2014, 2015, 2018). The conclusion that none of these mechanisms provide sufficient heating leaves the origin of the extra luminosity as an open question, motivating further searches for alternative heating sources or revised white dwarf cooling models.
