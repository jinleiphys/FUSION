# Standard Model Physics and the Digital Quantum Revolution: Thoughts about the Interface

> Advances in isolating, controlling and entangling quantum systems are transforming what was once a curious feature of quantum mechanics into a vehicle for disruptive scientific and technological progress. Pursuing the vision articulated by Feynman, a concerted effort across many areas of research and development is introducing prototypical digital quantum devices into the computing ecosystem available to domain scientists. Through interactions with these early quantum devices, the abstract vision of exploring classically-intractable quantum systems is evolving toward becoming a tangible reality. Beyond catalyzing these technological advances, entanglement is enabling parallel progress as a diagnostic for quantum correlations and as an organizational tool, both guiding improved understanding of quantum many-body systems and quantum field theories defining and emerging from the Standard Model. From the perspective of three domain science theorists, this article compiles thoughts about the interface on entanglement, complexity, and quantum simulation in an effort to contextualize recent NISQ-era progress with the scientific objectives of nuclear and high-energy physics.

## Key claim

The authors argue that while quantum simulation of Standard Model physics is not expected to achieve high-precision calculations at scale during the NISQ era, entanglement can serve as an organizational principle and diagnostic tool to guide the development of quantumly tractable approximations, potentially extending the scientific reach of bounded-error quantum simulation beyond naive asymptotic complexity classifications.

## Method

The paper presents a perspective from three domain science theorists, reviewing the interface between quantum information science and nuclear/high-energy physics. It examines entanglement measures (Schmidt decomposition, entanglement entropy, negativity, mutual information) and their application to quantum many-body systems and quantum field theories, surveys quantum simulation algorithms and techniques relevant to Standard Model problems, and discusses three specific application areas being pursued with available NISQ-era quantum devices.

## Key numbers

- For an ideal quantum computer with an n-qubit register, the Hilbert space dimensionality is d = 2^n; for n = 299, d ~ 10^90, larger than the estimated number of atoms in the universe (~10^86)
- Current universal gate-set quantum devices operate in the tens of qubits range and are expected to scale to hundreds in the next few years
- Quantum annealing devices now operate with thousands of qubits (citing D-Wave systems)
- The truncated tensor network description requires memory scaling ~ χ^2 n, linear in the number of qubits but exponential in the entanglement measure χ

## Context

This review extends the vision articulated by Feynman (1982) and others regarding quantum simulation of complex quantum systems, contextualizing recent NISQ-era progress described by Preskill (2018) with the scientific objectives of nuclear and high-energy physics. It builds upon early quantum simulations of the deuteron binding energy by Dumitrescu (2018) and similar computations of light nuclei with EFT interactions by Lu (2018) and others, while also connecting to tensor network methods reviewed by Banuls (2018) and entanglement studies in conformal field theories by Calabrese (2004). The paper enables future work on quantum advantage thresholds for lattice gauge theory, nuclear structure and reactions, and coherent neutrino evolution.
