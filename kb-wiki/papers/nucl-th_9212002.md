# Exact 1-Fermion Loop Contributions in 1+1 Dimensional Solitons

> We find solutions to the 1+1 dimensional scalar-only linear sigma model. A new method is used to compute 1-fermion loop contributions exactly and agreement with published results employing other methods is excellent. A renormalization scheme which differs from that commonly used in such calculations but similar to that required in 1+3 dimensions is also presented. We compare ``kink'' {\it versus} ``shallow bag'' solutions paying careful attention to the implications of the 1-fermion loop contributions for the stability of the former. We find that, for small fermion multiplicities, self-consistent shallow bag solutions are always more bound than their metastable kink counterparts. However, as the fermion multiplicity increases, shallow bags evolve into kinks which eventually are the only self-consistent configurations. This situation is qualitatively the same for the two renormalization schemes considered. When we construct ``baryons'', each containing three fermions, the kink configuration is typically more bound than the shallow bag when 1-fermion loop contributions are included.

## Key claim

The paper finds that when 1-fermion loop contributions are included exactly, shallow bag solutions are always more bound than metastable kink solutions for small fermion multiplicities, but as fermion multiplicity increases, shallow bags evolve into kinks which eventually become the only self-consistent configurations; this qualitative behavior holds for both renormalization schemes considered.

## Method

The authors solve the coupled Dirac and Klein-Gordon equations for the 1+1 dimensional scalar-only linear sigma model by expanding fermion spinors and the scalar field in a truncated Fourier basis with antiperiodic boundary conditions, then diagonalizing the Dirac Hamiltonian matrix to obtain the full spectrum. The 1-fermion loop contributions from the negative energy sea are computed exactly by subtracting counterterms identified from a power-series expansion of the unrenormalized energy density in the scalar potential, using either a "minimal" chiral renormalization scheme (matching earlier work) or a "full" chiral renormalization scheme that includes derivative and higher-order counterterms analogous to those required in 1+3 dimensions. Self-consistent solutions are obtained by iterating between the fermion eigenvalue problem and the scalar field equation until convergence.

## Key numbers

- For the special case λ/g² = 2 with minimal renormalization, the 1-fermion loop energy for the kink solution is E_loop = g/π ≈ 0.318310 g, agreeing with the analytic result of Campbell and Liao to within 0.004% for a basis of N_order = 100.
- For λ/g² = 0.5 with minimal renormalization, the non-iterated (perturbative) 1-loop energy is 0.348 g and the self-consistent value is 0.338 g, reproducing Wasson's published results exactly.
- With full renormalization, the 1-loop energies are roughly two-thirds of those found with the minimal scheme; for λ/g² = 2, less than 1/2% of the 1-loop energy comes from sea levels with momentum greater than approximately 2g (about twice the asymptotic fermion mass).
- For λ/g² = 0.5 with full renormalization, the classical kink solution is self-consistent (unlike the minimal scheme where self-consistency occurs at λ/g² = 2).

## Context

This work extends exact calculations of 1-fermion loop contributions to soliton solutions in the linear sigma model, previously performed by Campbell and Liao (1990) for a single special coupling and by Wasson (1992) for multiple couplings with self-consistent treatment. The authors introduce a new Fourier-space matrix diagonalization method that automatically satisfies local completeness relations and is computationally efficient, and they present a "full" chiral renormalization scheme that includes counterterms required in 1+3 dimensions but not previously applied in 1+1 dimensional studies. The results enable systematic comparison of kink versus shallow bag stability across coupling parameters and fermion multiplicities, with implications for models of hadronic structure and abnormal nuclear matter phases.
