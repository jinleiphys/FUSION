# Atmospheric Antideuteron Flux Within a Dynamical Coalescence Approach

> Cosmic antideuterons are considered as one of the most promising tools for the indirect detection of dark matter due to their ultra-low astrophysical backgrounds. Currently only upper limits on the antideuteron flux exist, but advancements in experimental detection technology may soon lead to positive signals. A major source of background is the production of secondary antideuterons through collisions of cosmic rays with the surrounding medium. In this study, antideuteron production is modeled using a multiphase transport model (AMPT) coupled with a dynamical coalescence model. By applying a widely used leaky box model and incorporating specific processes, we present a new theoretical baseline for atmospheric secondary antideuteron flux, including a tertiary contribution, from primary cosmic rays interacting with Earth's atmosphere. Our results indicate that the atmospheric antideuteron flux are within the range of various existing calculations and remain well below the upper limits set by the Balloon-borne Experiment with a Superconducting Spectrometer (BESS). The atmospheric antideuteron is found to dominate the antideuteron background at kinetic energies below $0.26 $ GeV/n.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new theoretical baseline for atmospheric secondary antideuteron flux, including a tertiary contribution, using a multiphase transport model (AMPT) coupled with a dynamical coalescence model rather than the traditional coalescence factor approach. The authors find that atmospheric antideuteron flux dominates the antideuteron background at kinetic energies below 0.26 GeV/n, but the results remain within the range of existing calculations and well below the upper limits set by BESS experiments.

## Method

The authors used the string melting version of the AMPT model to simulate proton collisions with atmospheric nuclei (nitrogen, oxygen, carbon) and generate full phase-space information of antiprotons and antineutrons. Antideuteron formation was modeled via a dynamical coalescence approach using a Wigner function of the deuteron internal wave function with a root-mean-square radius of 1.96 fm. The propagation of antideuterons through Earth's atmosphere was described using a leaky box model with an escape length of 11.8 g/cm² and average density of 2.28×10⁻²⁴ g/cm³, incorporating both secondary and tertiary source terms. The primary cosmic ray proton flux was parameterized based on experimental measurements and divided into 9 energy bins from 10 GeV/n to 5×10⁴ GeV/n, with 0.5 million events simulated per channel.

## Key numbers

- Atmospheric antideuteron flux dominates the antideuteron background at kinetic energies below 0.26 GeV/n
- Secondary antideuteron flux reaches approximately 10⁻⁹ to 10⁻⁸ m⁻² s⁻¹ sr⁻¹ (GeV/n)⁻¹ from 0.5 GeV/n to 1 GeV/n
- BESS upper limit on antideuteron differential flux: 1.9×10⁻⁴ (m² s sr GeV/n)⁻¹ at 95% confidence level in the range 0.17–1.15 GeV/n
- BESS-Polar II upper limit: 6.7×10⁻⁵ (m² s sr GeV/n)⁻¹ at 95% confidence level in the energy range 0.163–1.100 GeV/n
- GAPS sensitivity estimate: 2.0×10⁻⁶ (m² s sr GeV/n)⁻¹
- Antideuteron production cross sections for p+N collisions range from 2.130×10⁻⁵ mb at 20 GeV/n to 5.084×10⁻¹ mb at 34095 GeV/n
- The escape length used: λ_esc = 11.8 g/cm²
- Average atmospheric density: ρ = 2.28×10⁻²⁴ g/cm³
- Atmosphere composition number density: N:O:C = 1 : 0.27 : 5.1×10⁻⁴ cm⁻³
- Mean mass of target air: ⟨m_air⟩ = 14.58 amu

## Context

This work extends previous calculations of secondary antideuteron production by Duperray (2005) and Donato (2001, 2008) by replacing the traditional coalescence factor approach with a dynamical coalescence model coupled to the AMPT event generator. The calculation provides a new theoretical baseline for atmospheric antideuteron background that is crucial for interpreting data from AMS-02, GAPS, and BESS experiments searching for dark matter signals. The inclusion of the tertiary component, which redistributes antideuterons from higher to lower energies through non-annihilating inelastic interactions, follows the treatment of Simon (1998) and Donato (2001) and is particularly important for low-energy antideuteron predictions relevant to near-future experiments like GAPS.
