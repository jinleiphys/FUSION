# Ratios of Fluctuation Observables in the Search for the QCD Critical Point

> The QCD critical point can be found in heavy ion collision experiments via the non-monotonic behavior of many fluctuation observables as a function of the collision energy. The event-by-event fluctuations of various particle multiplicities are enhanced in those collisions that freeze out near the critical point. Higher, non-Gaussian, moments of the event-by-event distributions of such observables are particularly sensitive to critical fluctuations, since their magnitude depends on the critical correlation length to a high power. We present quantitative estimates of the contribution of critical fluctuations to the third and fourth moments of the pion and proton, as well as estimates of various measures of pion-proton correlations, all as a function of the same five non-universal parameters. We show how to use nontrivial but parameter independent ratios among these more than a dozen fluctuation observables to discover the critical point. We also construct ratios that, if the critical point is found, can be used to overconstrain the values of the non-universal parameters.

# Ratios of Fluctuation Observables in the Search for the QCD Critical Point

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that ratios of higher cumulants of pion and proton multiplicity fluctuations, after subtracting Poisson contributions, can serve as parameter-independent signatures of the QCD critical point. Specifically, five such ratios are predicted to equal exactly 1 regardless of the values of the five non-universal parameters (couplings and correlation length), providing a robust test for critical fluctuations that does not rely on uncertain theoretical inputs.

## Method
The authors compute the critical contribution to two-, three-, and four-particle correlators for pions and protons arising from exchanges of the critical sigma mode, using effective couplings from the linear sigma model (G = 300 MeV for pions, g_p = 7 for protons) and universal Ising-class couplings (tilde-lambda_3 = 4, tilde-lambda_4 = 12). They integrate these correlators over momentum to obtain normalized cumulants kappa_{ipjpi} for various combinations of i protons and j pions, then construct ratios that cancel the dependence on the correlation length xi and all four non-universal coupling parameters. The analysis assumes a Boltzmann gas baseline with Poisson statistics and uses an empirical parametrization of the freeze-out temperature as a function of baryon chemical potential.

## Key numbers
- The maximal correlation length xi_max that can be reached near the critical point is estimated to be 1.5-3 fm, compared to the natural value of approximately 0.5 fm away from the critical point.
- The benchmark values used for the universal dimensionless couplings are tilde-lambda_3 = 4 and tilde-lambda_4 = 12, taken as midpoints of known ranges from Tsypin (1994).
- The pion-sigma coupling is g_pi = G/m_pi = 2.1, derived from G = 300 MeV; the proton-sigma coupling is g_p = 7.
- The five parameter-independent ratios listed in Table I (e.g., kappa_{3p} kappa_{2pi}^{3/2} / kappa_{3pi} kappa_{2p}^{3/2}, kappa_{4p} kappa_{2pi}^2 / kappa_{4pi} kappa_{2p}^2, and three others) are all predicted to equal exactly 1.
- The width Delta in baryon chemical potential within which xi exceeds 1 fm is varied in the numerical examples, with a representative value of Delta = 100 MeV shown in the right panel of Figure 1.

## Context
This work extends the theoretical framework of Stephanov (1998, 1999) on critical fluctuations in heavy-ion collisions by providing quantitative estimates of non-Gaussian moments (third and fourth cumulants) for pions and protons, and by constructing parameter-independent ratios. It builds on the universal scaling relations of the Ising universality class from Tsypin (1994) and on estimates of the maximal correlation length achievable in heavy-ion collisions from Berdnikov (1999), Son (2004), and Nonaka (2004). The paper enables experimental searches at RHIC and SPS to test for the QCD critical point using ratios that are free from theoretical uncertainties in the coupling constants, complementing the approach of measuring individual cumulants whose magnitudes depend on poorly known parameters.
