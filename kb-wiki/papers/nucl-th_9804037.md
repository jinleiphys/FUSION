# Photon-induced two-nucleon knockout reactions to discrete final states

> Cross sections and photon asymmetries of the exclusive 16O(γ,pn)14N and 16O(γ,pp)14C knockout reactions are calculated for transitions to the low-lying discrete final states of the residual nucleus in the photon-energy range between 100 and 400 MeV. Exclusive reactions may represent a test of reaction mechanisms and a promising tool for investigating the dynamics of nucleon pairs in different states. Cross sections and asymmetries for both (γ,pn) and (γ,pp) turn out to be only slightly affected by short-range correlations and dominated by two-body currents. Therefore, two-nucleon knockout reactions induced by real photons appear well suited to investigate the nuclear current and the selectivity of individual transitions to its different components.

## Key claim
Cross sections and photon asymmetries for exclusive \(^{16}\mathrm{O}(\gamma,\mathrm{pn})^{14}\mathrm{N}\) and \(^{16}\mathrm{O}(\gamma,\mathrm{pp})^{14}\mathrm{C}\) reactions to discrete final states are dominated by two-body currents and only slightly affected by short-range correlations, but this conclusion is explicitly qualified by the use of a simple central and state-independent Jastrow correlation function for the pn case and by the absence of a spectral-function calculation for pn pairs.

## Method
The authors calculate exclusive two-nucleon knockout cross sections and photon asymmetries for transitions to low-lying discrete final states of \(^{14}\mathrm{N}\) and \(^{14}\mathrm{C}\) in the photon-energy range 100–400 MeV. For the pp reaction, two-nucleon overlap integrals are taken from a calculation of the two-proton spectral function of \(^{16}\mathrm{O}\) that treats long-range and short-range correlations consistently; for the pn reaction, a simpler prescription is used where the overlap is the product of a coupled shell-model pair function and a Jastrow-type correlation function. The nuclear current includes one-body (convective and spin) and two-body (seagull, pion-in-flight, and \(\Delta\)-isobar) currents, and final-state interactions are treated via distorted waves from a phenomenological optical potential. Calculations are performed in coplanar symmetrical kinematics where the two nucleons are emitted at equal energies and equal but opposite angles.

## Key numbers
- Photon-energy range considered: 100 to 400 MeV.
- For the \(^{16}\mathrm{O}(\gamma,\mathrm{pn})^{14}\mathrm{N}\) reaction, transitions are calculated to the \(1^+_1\) (ground state, \(T=0\)), \(0^+\) (2.31 MeV, \(T=1\)), \(1^+_2\) (3.95 MeV, \(T=0\)), \(2^+\) (7.03 MeV, \(T=0\)), and \(3^+\) (11.05 MeV, \(T=0\)) states of \(^{14}\mathrm{N}\).
- The harmonic oscillator parameter for the center-of-mass radial wave function is \(b = 1.77\) fm.
- The coupling constant for the seagull and pion-in-flight currents is \(f^2/(4\pi) = 0.079\).
- The \(\Delta\) resonance mass is taken as \(M_\Delta = 1232\) MeV.
- The missing-momentum range explored extends up to about 250–300 MeV/\(c\).
- At \(E_\gamma = 100\) MeV, the seagull current is dominant for all transitions; its pure contribution slightly decreases as photon energy increases.

## Context
This work extends the theoretical model previously applied to the \(^{16}\mathrm{O}(\mathrm{e,e'pp})^{14}\mathrm{C}\) reaction (Giusti and Pacati, 1997; Geurts et al., 1996) to photon-induced reactions, and improves upon earlier calculations for \((\gamma,\mathrm{pn})\) reactions (Ryckebusch et al., 1993; Giusti and Pacati, 1993) by including pion-in-flight diagrams and a full treatment of spin and isospin couplings for transitions to discrete final states. The results are intended to guide upcoming high-resolution experiments at Mainz (MAMI) that aim to separate individual final states and measure momentum distributions, and they complement earlier measurements at LEGS (245–315 MeV) that lacked sufficient energy resolution to resolve final states. The finding that two-body currents dominate over short-range correlations in \((\gamma,\mathrm{NN})\) reactions is consistent with the conclusions of Ryckebusch et al. (1993) and the LEGS collaboration (1997).
