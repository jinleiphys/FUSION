# Zero-pairing and zero-temperature limits of finite temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov theory

> Recently, the zero-pairing limit of Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) mean-field theory was studied in detail in arXiv:2006.02871. It was shown that such a limit is always well-defined for any particle number A, but the resulting many-body description differs qualitatively depending on whether the system is of closed-(sub)shell or open-(sub)shell nature. Here, we extend the discussion to the more general framework of Finite-Temperature HFB (FTHFB) which deals with statistical density operators, instead of pure many-body states. We scrutinize in detail the zero-temperature and zero-pairing limits of such a description, and in particular the combination of both limits. For closed-shell systems, we find that the FTHFB formulism reduces to the (zero-temperature) Hartree-Fock formulism, i.e. we recover the textbook solution. For open-shell systems, however, the resulting description depends on the order in which both limits are taken: if the zero-temperature limit is performed first, the FTHFB density operator demotes to a pure state which is a linear combination of a finite number of Slater determinants, i.e. the case of arXiv:2006.02871. If the zero-pairing limit is performed first, the FTHFB density operator remains a mixture of a finite number of Slater determinants with non-zero entropy, even as the temperature vanishes. These analytical findings are illustrated numerically for a series of Oxygen isotopes.

## Key claim

For open-shell systems, the finite-temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (FTHFB) density operator does not reduce to the Hartree-Fock (HF) formalism in the combined zero-temperature and zero-pairing limits; instead, the resulting description depends on the order in which the two limits are taken, meaning the limits do not commute for these systems.

## Method

The authors analytically studied the zero-temperature limit (T→0) and zero-pairing limit (Δ→0) of the FTHFB statistical density operator, both separately and jointly, while constraining the average particle number to an integer value A. They implemented the zero-pairing limit by scaling the Bogoliubov field Δ down to zero in the HFB Hamiltonian via a constraining term added to the grand potential, and the zero-temperature limit by taking β→∞ in the density operator. The analysis was performed under time-reversal invariance and illustrated numerically for a series of Oxygen isotopes using a two-nucleon interaction.

## Key numbers

- For closed-shell systems in the combined limits, the FTHFB formalism reduces to the (zero-temperature) Hartree-Fock formalism, recovering the textbook solution.
- For open-shell systems when the zero-temperature limit is performed first, the FTHFB density operator demotes to a pure state which is a linear combination of a finite number of Slater determinants with different (even) numbers of particles.
- For open-shell systems when the zero-pairing limit is performed first, the FTHFB density operator remains a statistical mixture of a finite number of Slater determinants with both even and odd particle numbers, with non-zero entropy even as temperature vanishes.
- The particle-number variance does not vanish in either case and has limiting values that differ by a factor of two between the two orders of taking limits.
- In the zero-pairing limit at finite temperature, the mean occupation of each valence state (where |ε_v − λ| = 0) is f_v = 1/2, with statistical weight ζ_v = 1.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the zero-pairing limit of straight HFB theory studied in Duguet (2020) to the more general finite-temperature framework. The results demonstrate that while FTHFB trivially reduces to HF for closed-shell systems, open-shell systems (which arise only when symmetry restrictions are imposed) yield a non-trivial description that depends on the order of limits. This sheds new light on the behavior of this well-studied many-body formalism and has consequences for thermal expectation values of observables in practical calculations where symmetry restrictions are common.
