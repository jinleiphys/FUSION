# Proton-Neutron Pairs in Heavy Deformed Nuclei

> The microscopic justification of the emergence of SU(3) symmetry in heavy nuclei remains an interesting problem. In the past, the pseudo-SU(3) approach has been used, with considerable success. Recent results seem to suggest that the key for understanding the emergence of SU(3) symmetry lies in the properties of the proton-neutron interaction, namely in the formation of (S=1, T=0) p-n pairs in heavy nuclei, especially when the numbers of valence protons and valence neutrons are nearly equal. Although this idea has been around for many years, since the introduction of the Federman-Pittel mechanism, it is only recently that information about the p-n interaction could be obtained from nuclear masses, which become available from modern facilities. Based on this information, a new coupling scheme for heavy deformed nuclei has been suggested and is under development.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the emergence of SU(3) symmetry in heavy deformed nuclei can be understood through the formation of proton-neutron pairs of Nilsson orbitals of the type ΔK[ΔN Δn_z ΔΛ] = 0[110], where these pairs link orbitals across adjacent major shells and treat orbitals of both parities on equal footing, in contrast to the pseudo-SU(3) approach which only maps normal parity levels.

## Method

The authors analyze the filling order of Nilsson orbitals from standard Nilsson diagrams for protons and neutrons in the 28-50, 50-82, and 82-126 major shells, identifying which orbitals form 0[110] partner pairs (where the neutron orbital differs from its proton partner by ΔN=1, Δn_z=1, ΔΛ=0). They then compare the resulting orbital structure to the predictions of the Federman-Pittel mechanism and the pseudo-SU(3) scheme, showing that the 0[110] pairing scheme can restore a complete SU(3) symmetry by mapping opposite-parity orbitals onto the missing normal-parity orbitals, leaving only a single high-lying Nilsson orbital isolated.

## Key numbers

- In ¹⁶⁸Er₆₈ (18 valence protons, 18 valence neutrons), the lowest 9 proton orbitals and lowest 9 neutron orbitals are occupied, all forming 0[110] pairs; the last pair is (p:7/2[523], n:7/2[633]).
- In ¹⁷²Yb₇₀ (20 valence protons, 20 valence neutrons), the lowest 10 proton orbitals and lowest 10 neutron orbitals are occupied, all forming 0[110] pairs; the last pair is (p:1/2[411], n:1/2[521]).
- In ¹⁷⁶Hf₇₂ (22 valence protons, 22 valence neutrons), the 11th pair (p:7/2[404], n:5/2[512]) is NOT of the 0[110] type, so no maximum δV_pn appears; the maximum appears for ¹⁷⁸Hf₇₂ where the neutron orbital 7/2[514] meets its 0[110] partner 7/2[404].
- In ¹⁸⁰W₇₄ (24 valence protons, 24 valence neutrons), the lowest 12 proton orbitals and lowest 12 neutron orbitals are occupied, all forming 0[110] pairs, giving maximum δV_pn.
- Well-deformed nuclei exhibiting IBA SU(3) symmetry are expected in the rare earth region up to 24 valence protons and 24 valence neutrons (up to ¹⁸⁰W₁₀₆).
- In the 50-82 proton shell, the 10 positive parity orbitals are kept intact; out of the 6 negative parity orbitals (1h11/2), the first 5 are mapped onto 1g9/2, while the 11/2[505] orbital remains isolated.
- In the 82-126 neutron shell, the 15 negative parity orbitals are kept intact; out of the 7 positive parity orbitals (1i13/2), the first 6 are mapped onto 1h11/2, while the 13/2[606] orbital remains isolated.

## Context

This work extends the Federman-Pittel mechanism (Federman and Pittel, late 1970s), which identified spin-orbit partner proton-neutron pairs as drivers of deformation, by specifying the exact Nilsson orbital pairs (0[110] type) that produce maximum δV_pn. It contrasts with the pseudo-SU(3) scheme (developed in the early 1970s), which maps only normal parity levels onto a lower-N shell while keeping opposite-parity orbitals separate; the new scheme instead keeps normal parity levels intact and maps opposite-parity orbitals (except one) onto the missing normal-parity levels, restoring the original SU(3) symmetry. The approach enables microscopic calculations for heavy deformed nuclei that treat both parities on equal footing, with preliminary results reported in Bonatsos (2015) and further work in progress.
