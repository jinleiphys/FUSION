# Form factor ratio from unpolarized elastic electron proton scattering

> A reanalysis of unpolarized electron-proton elastic scattering data is done in terms of the electric to magnetic form factor squared ratio. This observable is in principle more robust against experimental correlations and global normalizations. The present analysis shows indeed that it is a useful quantity that contains reliable and coherent information. The comparison with the ratio extracted from the measurement of the longitudinal to transverse polarization of the recoil proton in polarized electron-proton scattering shows that the results are compatible within the experimental errors. Limits are set on the kinematics where the physical information on the form factors can be safely extracted. The results presented in this work bring a decisive piece of information to the controversy on the deviation of the proton form factors from the dipole dependence.

## Key claim

The reanalysis of unpolarized electron-proton elastic scattering data in terms of the squared electric-to-magnetic form factor ratio shows that the results are compatible with polarization data within experimental errors, provided that the normalization procedure applied to the data from Andivahis (1994) is revised; the authors argue that the discrepancy between unpolarized and polarized experiments is not evident for older experiments when proper radiative corrections and independent treatment of spectrometer settings are applied.

## Method

The authors reanalyze existing unpolarized elastic electron-proton scattering cross section data by fitting the reduced cross section at fixed four-momentum transfer squared Q² using the expression σ_red = G_M²(R²ε + τ), where R² = (G_E/G_M)² and G_M² are treated as independent parameters, thereby avoiding correlations inherent in the Rosenbluth separation. For the data from Andivahis (1994), four different analyses are performed: using the published data with the original renormalization (Analysis I), without renormalization of the two spectrometer settings (Analysis II), using only the high-energy points (Analysis III), and applying a Q²-dependent normalization factor derived from aligning the low-energy point to the high-energy fit (Analysis IV). The extracted squared ratio μ²R² is compared with the ratio from polarization experiments by the JLab GEp collaboration.

## Key numbers

- For the Andivahis (1994) data at Q² = 1.75 GeV², the original published value of μ²R² is 0.828 ± 0.109, while the reanalysis without renormalization (Analysis II) gives 0.648 ± 0.089.
- At Q² = 2.50 GeV², the original value is 0.679 ± 0.115, while Analysis II gives 0.414 ± 0.108.
- At Q² = 3.25 GeV², the original value is 0.716 ± 0.200, while Analysis II gives 0.260 ± 0.183.
- At Q² = 5.00 GeV², the original value is 0.867 ± 0.330, while Analysis II gives 0.131 ± 0.295.
- The normalization factor for the low-energy point from Andivahis (1994) at Q² = 1.75 GeV² is 0.951144 ± 0.0156952, and at Q² = 5.00 GeV² it is 0.982138 ± 0.0142443, showing a systematic increase with Q².
- For the Bartel (1973) data at Q² = 1.75 GeV², the extracted μ²R² is 0.608 ± 0.207, consistent with the decreasing trend seen in polarization data.
- For the Christy (2004) data at Q² = 2.20 GeV², the extracted μ²R² is 0.766 ± 0.107.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Pacetti (2015) and addresses the long-standing controversy between form factor ratios extracted from unpolarized Rosenbluth separation and polarized Akhiezer-Rekalo method measurements. The authors show that most older unpolarized data sets (Bartel 1973, Janssen 1966, Berger 1971, Christy 2004) are consistent with the decreasing ratio observed by the JLab GEp collaboration (Puckett 2011), contradicting the claim of a systematic discrepancy that motivated two-photon exchange explanations. The analysis enables a critical reassessment of normalization procedures in Rosenbluth extractions and suggests that proper treatment of radiative corrections and spectrometer acceptance may resolve the apparent discrepancy without invoking large two-photon contributions.
