# Viability of nuclear $α$-particle condensates A reply to N.T. Zinner and A.S. Jensen, arXiv:nucl/th0712.1191

> In a recent paper, arXiv0712.1191 [nucl-th], Zinner and Jensen (ZJ) expressed strong doubts about the concept of alpha-particle condensation in finite nuclei. In this article we give a reply which, essentially, is point by point (but not in the order).

## Key claim

The authors defend the concept of alpha-particle condensation in finite nuclei against criticisms by Zinner and Jensen (2007), arguing that the Hoyle state in 12C and the 0_6+ state in 16O are genuine alpha-particle condensates, but only in a finite-system sense analogous to nuclear pairing or deformation, not as macroscopic Bose-Einstein condensation.

## Method

The authors analyze the alpha-particle density matrix using Jacobi coordinates (orthogonal internal coordinates) to remove the center-of-mass motion, showing that the Hoyle state's three alpha particles occupy the identical 0S orbit to nearly 75%. They compare the THSR condensate wave function, which interpolates between a pure harmonic oscillator Slater determinant (B=b) and a pure product state of alpha particles (B>>b), and examine the radial parts of single-alpha S orbits for 12C and 16O.

## Key numbers

- In the Hoyle state of 12C, the three alpha particles form a bosonic product state with all bosons in the identical 0S state to nearly 75%.
- The 0_6+ state in 16O at 15.1 MeV excitation energy shows over 60% of the alpha particles condensed.
- The alpha-disintegration threshold in 12C is at 7.24 MeV; in 16O it is at 14.4 MeV.
- The 0_6+ state in 16O at 15.1 MeV has a decay width of 160 keV, indicating a startlingly long lifetime for such a high excitation energy.

## Context

This paper is a direct reply to Zinner and Jensen (2007), who questioned the uniqueness of the alpha-particle density matrix definition and the viability of alpha condensates in heavier nuclei. The authors extend their previous work on the Hoyle state (Matsumura 2004, Yamada 2005) and the 0_6+ state in 16O (Funaki 2008), and connect to theoretical predictions of alpha condensation in infinite nuclear matter at low densities (Roepke 1998, Beyer 2000). The work enables further study of alpha-cluster states in self-conjugate nuclei and their identification via lifetime and decay properties.
