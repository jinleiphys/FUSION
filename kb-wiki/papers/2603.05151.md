# Simulating Lattice Gauge Theories with Virtual Rishons

> Classical tensor network and hybrid quantum-classical algorithms are promising candidates for the investigation of real-time properties of lattice gauge theories. We develop here a novel framework which enforces gauge symmetry via a quantum-link virtual rishon representation applied at intermediate steps. Crucially, the gauge and matter degrees of freedom are dynamical variables encoded in terms of qubits, enabling analysis of gauge theories in $d+1$ spacetime dimensions. We benchmark this framework in a U(1) gauge theory with and without matter fields. For $d = 1$, the multi-flavor Schwinger model with $1\leq N_f\leq3$ flavors is analyzed for arbitrary boundary conditions and nonzero topological angle, capturing signatures of the underlying Wess-Zumino-Witten conformal field theory. For $d = 2$, we extract the confining string tension in close agreement with continuum expectations. These results establish the virtual rishon framework as a scalable and robust approach for the simulation of lattice gauge theories using both classical tensor networks as well as near-term quantum hardware.

## Key claim

The virtual rishon (VR) framework enforces gauge symmetry exactly via an intermediate quantum-link representation that separates gauge and matter degrees of freedom, enabling resource-efficient qubit encodings for both classical tensor network and quantum hardware simulations of lattice gauge theories; the key qualifier is that the rishons serve as an auxiliary analytical tool and do not enlarge the physical Hilbert space.

## Method

The authors represent U(1) gauge link operators as pairs of bosonic rishons (site-centric bosonic operators) using the quantum-link model, which transforms the overlapping Gauss law constraints into strictly local conserved charges. They then enforce the link constraint via a projector that fuses each rishon pair back into a single rotor degree of freedom, recovering the original Hamiltonian while preserving the block-diagonal gauge-invariant structure. For qubit encoding, each rishon number operator is expanded in binary using Pauli Z operators, and the projector factorizes into two-qubit site projectors on the odd parity sector of equal binary power rishon-qubit pairs.

## Key numbers

- Central charge extracted for N_f=1 Schwinger model at θ=π: c_ext = 0.509 ± 0.001 (expected c_Ising = 1/2), from a single wavefunction using periodic boundary conditions with system sizes L ∈ {16, 24, 32, 64, 96} unit cells and N_r=4 qubits per rotor
- Central charge for N_f=2 massless Schwinger model: c_ext = 0.999 ± 0.005 (expected c_SU(2) = 1)
- Central charge for N_f=3 massless Schwinger model: c_ext = 2.071 ± 0.009 (expected c_SU(3) = 2)
- Central charge for N_f=2 extracted from N_f=3 with m_3 ≫ m_{1,2}: c_ext = 1.085 ± 0.008
- Central charge for Ising critical point extracted from N_f=3 with m_{3,2} massive and m_1 at critical value: c_ext = 0.489 ± 0.003
- String tension in d=2 pure U(1) gauge theory: at large couplings matches expected g²/2; best extracted decay constant ν_0 = 0.223 for g ∈ [0.8, 1.0] (expected ν_0 = 0.321)
- Simulations used up to 12×7 grid sizes with rotor dimension 16 (4 qubits), total ~300 qubits, bond dimensions up to χ=8192, using up to 32 GB RAM and 24 cores

## Context

This work extends the quantum-link rishon representation originally developed by Chandrasekharan (1996) and Brower (1999) by applying it as an intermediate analytical tool rather than as a physical enlargement of the Hilbert space. It differs qualitatively from approaches using energy penalties (Banerjee 2012, Zohar 2015), parametrization in irreducible representations (Silvi 2014, Tagliacozzo 2012), or gauge redundancy for quantum error correction. The framework enables systematic extraction of central charges for the multi-flavor Schwinger model, building on prior tensor network studies by Byrnes (2002), Banuls (2016), and Dempsey (2024), and computes string tensions in 2+1D U(1) gauge theory that can be compared to continuum expectations from Polyakov (1975, 1976) and Monte Carlo results by Loan (2003). The approach is designed to be extended to non-abelian gauge theories in future work.
