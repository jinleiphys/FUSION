# How to model Bose-Einstein correlations

> We propose new algorithm for numerical modelling of Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC). It is based on the fact that identical particles subjected to BEC do, by definition, bunch themselves in a maximal possible way, restricted only by conservation laws, in the same cells in phase-space.

## Key claim

The authors propose a new algorithm for modeling Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) that works by reassigning charges to particles within each event, rather than by altering momenta or applying event weights, and they claim this preserves both energy-momentum distributions and total multiplicity while producing BEC of all orders.

## Method

The algorithm takes the energy-momenta and spacio-temporal positions of particles from an event generator, then reassigns charges (positive, negative, or neutral) by randomly selecting a sign and a seed particle, then assigning the same sign to neighboring particles in phase-space according to a weight function P(i,j) that depends on momentum separation (and optionally spatial separation or temperature). The procedure repeats until all particles have been assigned charges, forming phase-space cells of like-charged particles that naturally produce Bose-Einstein correlations.

## Key numbers

- For constant weights P=0.5, the extracted "size" R from exponential fits to C2(Q) varies between 0.66 fm and 0.87 fm for CAS sources with mass M from 10 GeV to 100 GeV, and between 0.83 fm and 1.32 fm for MaxEnt sources over the same mass range.
- For the "most natural" Gaussian weights, R varies from 0.60 fm to 0.57 fm for CAS and from 0.94 fm to 1.67 fm for MaxEnt over the same mass range.
- The real sizes of CAS sources change from 0.29 fm for M=10 GeV to 1.61 fm for M=100 GeV.
- For a single CAS source of M=100 GeV divided into 2^k subsources treated together with P=0.5, the extracted R values are 0.87 fm (k=0), 1.29 fm (k=1), 1.99 fm (k=2), and 3.35 fm (k=3).
- For the same divided source with Gaussian P, R values are 0.57 fm (k=0), 3.26 fm (k=1), 4.01 fm (k=2), and 5.59 fm (k=3).

## Context

This work extends prior approaches to modeling BEC that either shift momenta of like-charge particles (as in the method of Lonnblad and Sjostrand, 1998) or apply event weights (as in the methods of Andersen and Reider, 1997; Fialkowski and Wit, 1998; Wibig, 1999). The algorithm builds on the concept of elementary emitting cells (EEC) introduced by Bialas, Srednicki, and Wosiek (1998) and on a statistical model based on information theory by Oleszczuk, Mrowczynski, and Turski (1991). The method enables modeling of BEC without altering single-particle distributions or energy-momentum balance, and the authors note it can be generalized to three dimensions and should account for resonances and final-state interactions in future work.
