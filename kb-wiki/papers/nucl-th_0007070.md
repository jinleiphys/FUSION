# Prediction of nuclear reaction rates for astrophysics

> This is an overview over the prediction of astrophysical reaction rates in the statistical model, with special emphasis on the parametrization of optical alpha+nucleus potentials.

## Key claim

The paper argues that existing global optical alpha+nucleus potentials, mostly parametrized above 70 MeV, require improved low-energy extrapolations for astrophysical reaction-rate calculations; the proposed potential with an energy-dependent imaginary part (volume plus surface Woods-Saxon terms) can reproduce the low-energy excitation curve of 144Sm(alpha,gamma)148Gd, but more experimental data are needed for consistent analysis.

## Method

The author develops a new optical alpha+nucleus potential whose imaginary part is the sum of a volume Woods-Saxon term and a surface term, with depths that depend exponentially on energy (volume depth increasing, surface depth decreasing at higher energies). The real part is given by a folding potential, and the total volume integral follows a previously derived relation (Rauscher 1998). This potential is incorporated into an improved version of the SMOKER statistical-model code (Rauscher and Thielemann, in press), which also includes updated level densities (Rauscher 1997) and treatment of isospin effects.

## Key numbers

* The widely used global alpha+nucleus potentials are parametrized at energies above about 70 MeV (e.g., Nolte 1987).
* The low-energy excitation curve of 144Sm(alpha,gamma)148Gd (Somorjai 1998) is reproduced by the new potential description.
* The volume depth W_v(E) = C - alpha e^{-beta E} and surface depth W_s(E) = D + gamma e^{-delta E} are exponentially dependent on energy.

## Context

This work extends earlier statistical-model calculations that used simplified square-well potentials (Arnould 1972, Woosley 1978) or a phenomenological Woods-Saxon potential (Thielemann 1987, based on Mann 1978 and McFadden 1966). It builds on recent folding-potential studies (Mohr 1994, Atzrott 1996) that found systematic mass- and energy-dependence of optical potentials, and on the global parametrization of volume integrals (Rauscher 1998). The new potential and code enable more reliable predictions of astrophysical reaction rates for unstable nuclei, particularly for alpha capture on self-conjugate targets and proton capture above the neutron separation energy, but further experimental data are needed to validate the approach across different energies and target masses.
