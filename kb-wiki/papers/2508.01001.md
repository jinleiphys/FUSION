# Criticality analysis of nuclear binding energy neural networks

> Machine learning methods, in particular deep learning methods such as artificial neural networks (ANNs) with many layers, have become widespread and useful tools in nuclear physics. However, these ANNs are typically treated as ``black boxes'', with their architecture (width, depth, and weight/bias initialization) and the training algorithm and parameters chosen empirically by optimizing learning based on limited exploration. We test a non-empirical approach to understanding and optimizing nuclear physics ANNs by adapting a criticality analysis based on renormalization group flows in terms of the hyperparameters for weight/bias initialization, training rates, and the ratio of depth to width. This treatment utilizes the statistical properties of neural network initialization to find a generating functional for network outputs at any layer, allowing for a path integral formulation of the ANN outputs as a Euclidean statistical field theory. We use a prototypical example to test the applicability of this approach: a simple ANN for nuclear binding energies. We find that with training using a stochastic gradient descent optimizer, the predicted criticality behavior is realized, and optimal performance is found with critical tuning. However, the use of an adaptive learning algorithm leads to somewhat superior results without concern for tuning and thus obscures the analysis. Nevertheless, the criticality analysis offers a way to look within the black box of ANNs, which is a first step towards potential improvements in network performance beyond using adaptive optimizers.

## Key claim

The authors test whether a non-empirical criticality analysis based on renormalization group flows can predict optimal hyperparameter choices for nuclear physics neural networks, finding that the predicted critical tuning yields optimal performance when using stochastic gradient descent, but that adaptive learning algorithms achieve somewhat superior results without requiring such tuning, thereby obscuring the criticality analysis.

## Method

The authors construct a feed-forward artificial neural network with two inputs and one output, using mean-zero Gaussian initialization for weights and biases, and train it on nuclear binding energy data from AME2020. They analyze the network using artificial neural network field theory (ANNFT), which formulates the output distribution as a Euclidean statistical field theory with a path integral generating functional, using the ratio of depth to width as an expansion parameter to treat non-Gaussian correlations perturbatively. The criticality analysis identifies initialization hyperparameters (variances C_W and C_b) that keep the variance constant under renormalization group flow with depth, preventing exploding or vanishing gradients.

## Key numbers

* The weight initialization variance is scaled by 1/n (where n is the hidden layer width) to maintain a useful large-n limit, with the unscaled variance C_W and bias variance C_b being the relevant hyperparameters
* The ratio r = ℓ_out/n (depth divided by width) serves as the expansion parameter that suppresses higher-order non-Gaussian correlations in the limit of small r
* Fluctuations in the covariance matrix of the conditional distribution for the second hidden layer scale as 1/n_1, where n_1 is the width of the prior layer, making them perturbatively suppressed at large width
* The variance of covariance fluctuations is given by (1/n_1)(C_W^(2))^2 times the connected fourth-order moment, establishing the four-point vertex for neuron interactions

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of Roberts (2021), Halverson (2020), and Halverson (2021), who developed the field theory approach to neural networks using renormalization group methods, by testing it on a practical nuclear physics problem. The paper applies ANNFT to binding energy prediction, a task previously addressed by empirical neural network approaches such as Zeng (2022), and demonstrates that while critical tuning works for stochastic gradient descent, adaptive optimizers like those discussed by Liu (2021) can bypass the need for such analysis. The results enable future work on improving network performance beyond what adaptive optimizers provide, and on applying the criticality framework to more complex nuclear physics networks.
