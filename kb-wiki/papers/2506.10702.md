# Extraction of Effective Parameters from Transverse Momentum Spectra of Heavy Quarkonia in Proton-Proton Collisions at the LHC

> The effective string tension ($κ$) in the Schwinger mechanism and the effective temperature ($T$) in Bose-Einstein statistics are extracted from the transverse momentum ($p_T$) spectra of heavy quarkonia produced in proton-proton (p+p) collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). Here, $T$ derived from the heavy quarkonium $p_T$ spectra also serves as the initial effective temperature (effective temperature at the initial stage) of small collision systems. This is because, despite the absence of quark-gluon plasma (QGP) formation during the collisions, which leaves $T$ largely unaffected by QGP-related effects, the initial geometric asymmetry and local partonic thermalization still induce radial and transverse flows, thereby contributing to an increase in $T$. The effective parameters ($κ$ and $T$) are obtained by fitting the experimental $p_T$ spectra of $J/ψ$ and $Υ(nS)$ ($n=1$, 2, and 3) within various rapidity intervals, produced in p+p collisions at center-of-mass energies of $\sqrt{s}=13$ and 8 TeV, as measured by the LHCb Collaboration. It is found that the multi-component distribution structured within the framework of the Schwinger mechanism or Bose-Einstein statistics can effectively describe the heavy quarkonium $p_T$ spectra in small collision systems. With decreasing rapidity in the forward region, both $κ$ and $T$ increase, indicating a directly proportional relationship between them. Based on $κ$, the average minimum strong force radius of participant quarks is determined.

## Key claim

The effective string tension (κ) and effective temperature (T) extracted from heavy quarkonium pT spectra in p+p collisions are not direct measures of the QCD string tension or thermal temperature, but rather phenomenological parameters that incorporate flow effects from initial geometric asymmetry and local partonic thermalization, even in the absence of quark-gluon plasma formation.

## Method

The authors fitted three-component Schwinger mechanism distributions and three-component Bose-Einstein distributions to the double differential cross sections d²σ/(dydpT) of prompt J/ψ, J/ψ from b-quarks, and Υ(nS) (n=1,2,3) measured by the LHCb Collaboration in p+p collisions at √s=13 and 8 TeV across various forward rapidity intervals. The effective string tension κ was extracted from the Schwinger mechanism framework, while the effective temperature T was obtained from Bose-Einstein statistics, with both parameters averaged over the three components weighted by their fractional contributions.

## Key numbers

- κ values range from ~20–165 GeV/fm at √s=13 TeV and ~15–140 GeV/fm at √s=8 TeV, much larger than the classical QCD string tension (~1 GeV/fm)
- T values range from ~0.75–2.15 GeV at √s=13 TeV and ~0.69–1.98 GeV at √s=8 TeV, significantly larger than typical QGP freeze-out temperature (~0.16 GeV)
- The average minimum strong force radius Rmin of participant quarks ranges from 0.03–0.09 fm, approximately one tenth of nucleon radii (0.3–1.0 fm)
- Both κ and T increase from the rapidity interval 4.0<y<4.5 to 2.0<y<2.5 in the forward region
- The ratio of parameters at 13 and 8 TeV aligns more closely with ln(13000)/ln(8000) than with 13000/8000 or √(13000/8000), where √s is expressed in GeV

## Context

This work extends the application of Schwinger mechanism and Bose-Einstein statistics, previously used for large collision systems, to small p+p collision systems where QGP formation is debated. The extracted initial effective temperatures are comparable to those obtained via the color string percolation method in previous work by the same group (Zhang et al., prior studies), but higher than values from the relative fugacity method (lower energies, ~0.2–0.6 GeV) and perfect hydrodynamic solutions (~0.4–0.5 GeV). The results enable future investigation of the relationship between initial energy density and parton collective behavior in small systems, and suggest a potential logarithmic dependence of κ and T on collision energy that requires further data fitting across multiple energies.
