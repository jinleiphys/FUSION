# The ratio method: a new tool to study one-neutron halo nuclei

> Recently a new observable to study halo nuclei was introduced, based on the ratio between breakup and elastic angular cross sections. This new observable is shown by the analysis of specific reactions to be independent of the reaction mechanism and to provide nuclear-structure information of the projectile. Here we explore the details of this ratio method, including the sensitivity to binding energy and angular momentum of the projectile. We also study the reliability of the method with breakup energy. Finally, we provide guidelines and specific examples for experimentalists who wish to apply this method.

## Key claim

The ratio of breakup to elastic (or summed) angular cross sections for one-neutron halo nuclei is nearly independent of the reaction mechanism and strongly reduces sensitivity to the poorly known core-target interaction, thereby providing a clean probe of projectile nuclear structure; however, small residual discrepancies arise from neglecting the neutron-target interaction and the adiabatic approximation, particularly at very forward angles in Coulomb-dominated reactions.

## Method

The authors model one-neutron halo projectiles as a two-body system (core plus neutron) and solve the three-body scattering problem using the Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (DEA), which treats Coulomb and nuclear interactions on equal footing without the adiabatic approximation. They compute elastic, inelastic, and breakup angular distributions for reactions of 11Be on 12C at 67 MeV/nucleon and on 208Pb at 69 MeV/nucleon, and for 19C on Pb at 67 MeV/nucleon, then take the ratio of breakup to summed (elastic plus inelastic plus breakup) cross sections. The results are compared to predictions from the Recoil Excitation and Breakup (REB) model, which factorizes the cross sections into a point-like scattering term and a form factor containing the projectile structure.

## Key numbers

* For 11Be on 12C at 67 MeV/nucleon, the ratio R_sum at a continuum energy E = 0.1 MeV agrees well with the REB form factor |F_{E,0}|^2, with only slight residual oscillations at forward angles.
* For 11Be on 208Pb at 69 MeV/nucleon, the ratio R_sum at E = 0.1 MeV also matches the REB prediction, except at very forward angles where the REB overestimates the ratio due to the adiabatic approximation failing for Coulomb-dominated reactions.
* Changing the one-neutron separation energy of a 11Be-like system from 0.5 MeV to 50 keV or 5.0 MeV changes the magnitude of the ratio by two orders of magnitude, with the shape also differing significantly.
* Folding the DEA angular distributions with a Gaussian of standard deviation 0.41 degrees (the angular resolution of the RIKEN experiment) removes the residual oscillations in the ratio to the point where they would no longer be detectable.

## Context

This work extends the ratio method introduced in Capel et al. (2010) and Capel et al. (2013, PLB), which was inspired by the Recoil Excitation and Breakup model of Johnson et al. (1997). The method addresses the problem that breakup and elastic angular distributions of halo nuclei are strongly model-dependent due to uncertainties in the core-target interaction, as noted by Capel et al. (2004). By demonstrating the ratio's independence of reaction mechanism across light (C) and heavy (Pb) targets and across beam energies from 40 to 100 MeV/nucleon, the paper provides experimentalists with guidelines for extracting nuclear-structure information (binding energy, orbital angular momentum) from halo nuclei such as 11Be and 19C, and enables future studies of heavier halo candidates like 31Ne and excited-state halos.
