# Lattice Gauge Theory and the Origin of Mass

> Most of the mass of everyday objects resides in atomic nuclei; the total of the electrons' mass adds up to less than one part in a thousand. The nuclei are composed of nucleons---protons and neutrons---whose nuclear binding energy, though tremendous on a human scale, is small compared to their rest energy. The nucleons are, in turn, composites of massless gluons and nearly massless quarks. It is the energy of these confined objects, via $M=E/c^2$, that is responsible for everyday mass. This article discusses the physics of this mechanism and the role of lattice gauge theory in establishing its connection to quantum chromodynamics.

## Key claim

The mass of everyday objects originates primarily from the energy of confined quarks and gluons inside nucleons, via \(M=E/c^2\), and lattice gauge theory provides the nonperturbative framework to establish this connection to quantum chromodynamics (QCD). The article reviews this mechanism but notes that the connection relies on numerical Monte Carlo calculations and strong-coupling expansions, not on a fully rigorous analytic proof that confinement persists from the strong-coupling limit to the weak-coupling regime of continuum QCD.

## Method

The paper reviews the development of lattice gauge theory, starting from Wilson's 1974 formulation that replaces continuous spacetime with a discrete lattice to define the functional integral nonperturbatively. It describes how the expectation value of Wilson loops yields an area law for the quark-antiquark potential in the strong-coupling limit, signaling confinement, and how numerical Monte Carlo simulations with importance sampling are used to compute hadron masses from two-point correlation functions. The text also discusses fermion doubling and various lattice fermion formulations (staggered, Wilson, twisted-mass, domain-wall, overlap) that address this problem while preserving or breaking chiral symmetry as needed.

## Key numbers

- The density of nuclear material ranges from \(20\times10^{13}\) g cm\(^{-3}\) for a gold nucleus to \(60\times10^{13}\) g cm\(^{-3}\) for an isolated proton.
- Nuclear fusion producing \({}^4\)He releases less than 1% of the total nuclear rest energy.
- In QCD with \(n_f\le16\), the one-loop beta function coefficient is \(\beta_0 = -\frac{2}{3}\frac{n_f}{16\pi^2} + \frac{11}{3}\frac{N}{16\pi^2}\), yielding asymptotic freedom for \(n_f\le16\).
- At strongest coupling, the string tension satisfies \(\sigma a^2 = \ln[g_0^2(a)/2N]\); at weakest coupling, \(\sigma a^2 \propto e^{-1/\beta_0 g_0^2(a)}\).
- Strong-coupling expansions for glueball masses yield ratios \(M_{2^{++}}/M_{0^{++}}\approx1\) and \(M_{1^{+-}}/M_{0^{++}}=1.8\pm0.3\).
- The pion mass satisfies \(M_\pi^2 \propto m_0\langle\bar{\psi}\psi\rangle\) in the strong-coupling regime with chiral symmetry breaking.

## Context

This review extends the line of work initiated by Wilson (1974) on lattice gauge theory and confinement, building on earlier lattice field theories by Wentzel (1940) and Schiff (1953) and on the discovery of asymptotic freedom by Politzer (1973) and Gross and Wilczek (1973). It also references Creutz's pioneering numerical calculations (1980) that demonstrated a smooth connection between strong- and weak-coupling regimes for the string tension. The article enables a deeper qualitative and quantitative understanding of how QCD generates the mass of nucleons and, hence, atomic nuclei, and it sets the stage for future lattice calculations of hadron structure and nuclear properties with controlled systematic uncertainties.
