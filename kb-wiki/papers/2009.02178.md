# Bose-Einstein Condensation of Quantum Hard-Spheres as a Deposition Phase Transition and New Relations Between Bosonic and Fermionic Pressures

> We investigate the phase transition of Bose-Einstein particles with the hard-core repulsion in the grand canonical ensemble within the Van der Waals approximation. It is shown that the pressure of non-relativistic Bose-Einstein particles is mathematically equivalent to the pressure of simplified version of the statistical multifragmentation model of nuclei with the vanishing surface tension coefficient and the Fisher exponent $τ_F = \frac{5}{2}$, which for such parameters has the 1-st order phase transition. The found similarity of these equations of state allows us to show that within the present approach the high density phase of Bose-Einstein particles is a classical macro-cluster with vanishing entropy at any temperature which, similarly to the classical hard spheres, is a kind of solid state. To show this we establish new relations which allow us to identically represent the pressure of Fermi-Dirac particles in terms of pressures of Bose-Einstein particles of two sorts.

## Key claim

The paper argues that Bose-Einstein condensation of quantum hard-spheres, treated in the Van der Waals approximation, is a first-order deposition phase transition from gas to solid (not a gas-liquid transition as previously argued by Huang), because the high-density phase has vanishing entropy at any temperature and a density equal to the close-packing limit of classical hard spheres.

## Method

The authors analyze the grand canonical partition function for non-relativistic Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac particles with hard-core repulsion using the Van der Waals approximation, where the effective chemical potential is shifted by the excluded volume term \( \nu = \mu - 4 V_0 p \). They expand the logarithmic pressure integral into a series over cluster sizes and compare it term-by-term with the exact solution of the simplified statistical multifragmentation model (sSMM) of nuclei. They also derive a new mathematical identity (the BE-FD decomposition identity) that expresses the pressure of an ideal Bose gas as a sum of fermionic pressures plus a residual bosonic term, and apply it to analyze the nature of the condensed phase.

## Key numbers

- The Fisher exponent of the equivalent sSMM is \( \tau_F = \frac{5}{2} \) (or index \( \tau = 4 \) in the notation of Refs. KABsmm1, KABsmm2).
- The critical pressure at the phase transition curve is \( p_{c-} = T g \left[ \frac{m T}{2 \pi \hbar^2} \right]^{3/2} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^{5/2}} \), which reduces to the standard integral representation involving the Riemann zeta function \( \zeta(5/2) \).
- The particle number density of the dense (solid) phase is \( n_s = 1/(4 V_0) \), independent of temperature.
- The energy density of the dense phase is \( \varepsilon_s = m/(4 V_0) \), corresponding to particles at rest.
- The Bose-Einstein condensation temperature is given by \( T_c^{\text{BE}} = \frac{2 \pi \hbar^2}{m} \left[ \frac{1}{g \zeta(3/2)} \cdot \frac{n_-}{1 - 4 V_0 n_-} \right]^{2/3} \).
- For Fermi-Dirac particles at \( \nu = m \), the sum \( \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} (-1)^{(k+1)} / k^{1/2} \approx 0.6049 \), showing the derivative does not vanish and thus no first-order phase transition occurs.
- For massless, chargeless particles in \( D=3 \), the identity yields \( \int_0^\infty x^3/(e^x-1) dx = (8/7) \int_0^\infty x^3/(e^x+1) dx = \pi^4/15 \).

## Context

This work extends the exact solution of the simplified statistical multifragmentation model (sSMM) developed by Bondorf (1985) and solved exactly by Bugaev (2000, 2001) and Reuter (2008) to the problem of Bose-Einstein condensation with hard-core repulsion. It contradicts the textbook interpretation by Huang (1963) that Bose-Einstein condensation is a gas-liquid phase transition, arguing instead that it is a deposition (gas-solid) transition based on the vanishing entropy of the dense phase. The BE-FD decomposition identity provides a new tool to relate bosonic and fermionic equations of state, and the analysis of the Fisher exponent's role in determining phase transition order builds on the finite-size scaling results of Reuter (2008). The work enables future studies of whether more realistic equations of state for quantum particles at high densities (near \( 0.45/V_0 \) to \( 0.55/V_0 \)) and high temperatures will match the deposition phase transition of classical hard spheres.
