# A new scheme for isomer pumping and depletion with high-power lasers

> We propose a novel scheme for the population and depletion of nuclear isomers. The scheme combines the $γ$-photons with energies $\gtrsim 10$ keV emitted during the interaction of a contemporary high-intensity laser pulse with a plasma and one or multiple photon beams supplied by intense lasers. Due to nonlinear effects, two- or multi-photon absorption dominates over the conventional multi-step one-photon process for an optimized gamma flash. Moreover, this nonlinear effect can be greatly enhanced with the help of externally supplied low-energy photons coming from another laser. These low-energy photons act such that the effective cross-section experienced by the $γ$-photons becomes tunable, growing with the intensity $I_0$ of the beam. Assuming $I_{0}\sim 10^{18}$ Wcm$^{-2}$ for the photon beam, an effective cross-section as large as $10^{-21}$ cm$^2$ to $10^{-28}$ cm$^2$ for the $γ-$photon can be achieved. Thus, within state-of-the-art 10 PW laser facilities, the yields from two-photon absorption can reach $10^6$ to $10^9$ isomers per shot for selected states that are separated from their ground state by E2 transitions. Similar yields for transitions with higher multipolarities can be accommodated by multi-photon absorption with additional photons provided.

## Key claim
The authors propose that combining gamma photons (energies above 10 keV) from high-intensity laser-plasma interactions with externally supplied intense optical/infrared laser beams enables nonlinear two- or multi-photon absorption to dominate over conventional sequential single-photon processes for isomer pumping and depletion, but only when the supplied laser intensity reaches sufficient thresholds and when favorable intermediate nuclear states exist within narrow energy gaps.

## Method
The authors derive theoretical expressions for the effective cross-sections of two-photon absorption (2PA) and multi-photon absorption (nPA) in nuclear systems, using Weisskopf estimates for nuclear matrix elements and considering two cases: one where the supplied photons undergo M1 transitions (case a) and one where they undergo E1 transitions (case b). They then estimate yields by combining the calculated effective cross-sections with gamma-flux spectra from laser-plasma interactions (converted from published simulation data) and with supplied laser intensities up to 10^18 W/cm^2, integrating over the continuous gamma energy distribution from 1 keV to 50 MeV.

## Key numbers
- Effective cross-section for gamma photons reaches 10^-21 cm^2 to 10^-28 cm^2 when the supplied photon beam has intensity I_0 ~ 10^18 W/cm^2
- Yields from two-photon absorption can reach 10^6 to 10^9 isomers per shot for states separated from ground state by E2 transitions, using state-of-the-art 10 PW laser facilities
- For a single gamma-flash alone (without external laser), 2PA yields are estimated at 10^-4 isomers per shot within 1 cm target thickness, compared to 10^-9 for sequential 1PA+1PA under the same conditions
- The effective cross-section for 2PA with E1+E1 transitions scales as approximately 3 x 10^-51 * (E_gamma / w_>) * P_2 * (A^(4/3) / (Delta E)^2) * G, where P_2 is supplied laser intensity in W/cm^2 and Delta E is the energy gap in eV
- For 4PA in A~200 nuclei, the effective cross-section reaches approximately 10^-25 cm^2 using current laser technology

## Context
This work extends earlier proposals for nuclear isomer pumping using multi-photon absorption (Goppert-Mayer 1931; Collins 1982; PhysRevC.23.50, PhysRevC.20.1942) by incorporating modern high-power laser systems that can deliver intensities up to 10^23 W/cm^2. It addresses the long-standing "graser dilemma" (RevModPhys.53.687, RevModPhys.69.1085) where natural isomer half-life/width combinations require impractical pumping powers. The scheme enables experimental investigation of isomer pumping and depletion at facilities like ELI-NP, potentially allowing access to isomers with half-lives exceeding one year and transitions with higher multipolarities through multi-photon absorption.
