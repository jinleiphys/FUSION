# Theory of Single Charge Exchange Heavy Ion Reactions

> The theory of heavy ion single charge exchange reactions is reformulated. In momentum space the reaction amplitude factorizes into a product of projectile and target transition form factors, folded with the nucleon-nucleon isovector interaction and a distortion coefficient which accounts for initial and final state ion-ion elastic interactions. The multipole structure of the transition form factors is studied in detail for Fermi-type non-spin flip and Gamow-Teller-type spin flip transitions, also serving to establish the connection to nuclear beta decay. The reaction kernel is evaluated for central and rank-2 tensor interactions. Initial and final state elastic ion-ion interaction are shown to be dominated by the imaginary part of the optical potential allowing to evaluate the reaction coefficients in the strong absorption limit, realized by the black disk approximation. In that limit the distortion coefficient is evaluated in closed form, revealing the relation to the total reaction cross section and the geometry of the transition form factors. It is shown that at small momentum transfer distortion effects reduce to a simple scaling factor, allowing to define reduced forward-angle cross section which is given by nuclear matrix elements of beta decay-type. The response function formalism is used to describe nuclear charge changing transitions. Spectral distributions obtained by a self-consistent HFB and QRPA approach are discussed for $τ_\pm$ excitations of $^{18}O$ and $^{40}Ca$, respectively, and compared to spectroscopic data. The interplay of nuclear structure and reaction dynamics is illustrated for the single charge exchange reaction $^{18}O+^{40}Ca \to ^{18}F+^{40}K$ at $T_{lab}=270$ MeV.

# Theory of Single Charge Exchange Heavy Ion Reactions

## Key claim

The paper reformulates heavy ion single charge exchange (SCE) reaction theory to show that, under the strong absorption (black disk) approximation and at small momentum transfer, distortion effects reduce to a simple scaling factor, allowing forward-angle cross sections to be expressed in terms of nuclear matrix elements of beta-decay type, but this factorization holds only when the distortion coefficient is evaluated in the black disk limit and for Gaussian form factors.

## Method

The reaction amplitude is expressed in momentum space, factorizing into projectile and target transition form factors folded with the nucleon-nucleon isovector interaction and a distortion coefficient accounting for initial and final state elastic ion-ion interactions. The multipole structure of transition form factors is analyzed for Fermi-type (non-spin flip) and Gamow-Teller-type (spin flip) transitions, with the reaction kernel evaluated for central and rank-2 tensor interactions. The distortion coefficient is evaluated in the strong absorption limit using the black disk approximation, and spectral distributions are obtained via self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogolubov (HFB) and Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) approaches.

## Key numbers

- The reaction $^{18}O+^{40}Ca \to ^{18}F+^{40}K$ is studied at $T_{lab}=270$ MeV as the illustrative case.
- Nuclear transition form factors typically decrease rapidly beyond $p \sim 300$ MeV/c, facilitating numerical evaluations.
- The black disk approximation allows the distortion coefficient to be evaluated in closed form, revealing its relation to the total reaction cross section and the geometry of the transition form factors.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of Taddeucci et al. (1987) for light ion charge exchange reactions to heavy ion systems, building on earlier microscopic approaches by Brendel (1988), Lenske (1989), and Cappuzzello et al. (2004) that used distorted wave methods. The reformulation is motivated by the NUMEN project at LNS Catania, which aims to determine nuclear matrix elements for $\Delta Z = \pm 1$ and $\Delta Z = \pm 2$ excitations from heavy ion charge exchange data. The paper establishes a connection between heavy ion SCE cross sections and beta-decay matrix elements, enabling extraction of spectroscopic information from heavy ion reactions comparable to what has been achieved with light ion reactions.
