# Derivation of the M$_n$/M$_p$ ratio in exotic nuclei

> A generalized formula is provided, to calculate the M$_n$/M$_p$ ratio of the multipole transition matrix elements, in the framework of the so-called phenomenological analysis. It takes into account the possible difference between the neutron and proton radii and diffuseness, which can occur, especially in exotic nuclei. The validity domain of the original Bernstein formula is discussed, in the case of the proton scattering probe at few tens of MeV. The largest discrepancies are obtained for very neutron-rich nuclei (N/Z$\gtrsim$1.6) or when the electromagnetic deformation parameter is larger than the proton scattering one. The reduction of the statistical error bars, and the study of very neutron-rich nuclei at facilities of exotic beams of new generation, should favor the use of the generalized Bernstein formula.

# Derivation of the M$_n$/M$_p$ ratio in exotic nuclei

## Key claim

The paper provides a generalized Bernstein formula for the M$_n$/M$_p$ ratio that accounts for differences between neutron and proton radii and diffuseness, which the original Bernstein formula assumes to be identical. The largest discrepancies between the generalized and original formulas occur for very neutron-rich nuclei with N/Z $\gtrsim$ 1.6, or when the electromagnetic deformation parameter is larger than the proton scattering one, but current experimental error bars (10-30%) often make the two approaches quantitatively compatible for not-too-exotic nuclei.

## Method

The author derives a generalized Bernstein formula (Eq. 13) starting from a Woods-Saxon optical potential decomposition into neutron and proton contributions, incorporating separate radii R$_{n,p}$ and diffuseness a$_{n,p}$ for neutrons and protons. Phenomenological parameterizations for neutron and proton radii (from Warda 1998 and Wang 2001) and separation energies (from Vogel 2001) are used to compute the required inputs. The generalized formula is then applied to proton scattering at approximately 50 MeV/nucleon on Oxygen, Sulfur, and Argon isotopic chains, and the results are compared with both the original Bernstein formula and microscopic BCS+QRPA calculations using the JLM optical potential.

## Key numbers

- For N/Z $\gtrsim$ 1.6, the largest discrepancies between original and generalized Bernstein formulas are obtained, with relative differences exceeding 10% for very neutron-rich nuclei such as $^{48}$S and beyond.
- For proton-rich nuclei with N/Z $\approx$ 0.8 (e.g., $^{14}$O, $^{28}$S, $^{32}$Ar), the generalized formula yields variations of 10% to 20% in the M$_n$/M$_p$ ratio compared to the original formula.
- For Sulfur isotopes, the generalized formula gives (M$_n$/M$_p$)/(N/Z) = 0.85 $\pm$ 0.18 for $^{30}$S and 1.26 $\pm$ 0.31 for $^{40}$S, compared to microscopic values of 0.88 $\pm$ 0.21 and 1.01 $\pm$ 0.27 respectively.
- For Argon isotopes, the generalized formula gives (M$_n$/M$_p$)/(N/Z) = 1.06 $\pm$ 0.21 for $^{34}$Ar and 1.21 $\pm$ 0.19 for $^{44}$Ar, compared to microscopic values of 0.99 $\pm$ 0.17 and 1.13 $\pm$ 0.27 respectively.
- For Oxygen isotopes, the generalized formula gives (M$_n$/M$_p$)/(N/Z) = 1.05 $\pm$ 0.14 for $^{18}$O and 1.48 $\pm$ 0.63 for $^{22}$O, compared to microscopic values of 0.88 $\pm$ 0.19 and 1.4 $\pm$ 0.5 respectively.
- The optical potential diffuseness a is taken as 0.7 fm, based on Becchetti-Greenlees (1969) and Koning-Delaroche (2003) parameterizations.
- For proton scattering at 10-50 MeV, the b$_n$/b$_p$ ratio is 3 (from Bernstein 1981, 1983).

## Context

This work extends the original Bernstein formula (Bernstein 1981, 1983) which assumed identical neutron and proton radii and diffuseness, and which had been known to provide questionable results in Ar, S, and O isotopic chains (Khan 2000, 2001). The generalized formula enables phenomenological extraction of M$_n$/M$_p$ ratios for exotic nuclei where neutron and proton density distributions differ significantly, and is expected to become more useful as new-generation exotic beam facilities reduce statistical error bars. The approach is complementary to microscopic analyses using BCS+QRPA transition densities with the JLM optical potential (Khan 2000, 2001; Becheva 2006), and could be extended to other probes such as neutrons, alpha particles, or pions in future work.
