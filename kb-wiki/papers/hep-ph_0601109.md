# Reply to Comment on "Surprises in threshold antikaon-nucleon physics"

> In their Comment, Borasoy et al. [arXiv:hep-ph/0512279], criticize our results [PRL 95 (2005) 172502] that accommodate both scattering data and the new accurate measurement by DEAR of the shift and width of kaonic hydrogen. In our calculations we have employed unitary chiral perturbation theory (UCHPT). We discuss why their arguments are irrelevant or do not hold.

## Key claim

The authors defend their earlier result that a narrow I=1 pole just below the K⁻p threshold at (1431.21 − i 1.28) MeV is physically meaningful, arguing that it does not violate the Wigner condition when the phase of the scattering amplitude is considered rather than the elastic phase shift, and that its narrow width arises from small couplings to open channels.

## Method

The authors employ unitary chiral perturbation theory (UCHPT), which resums the right-hand (unitarity) cut while calculating other cuts in the interacting kernel at a given order in chiral perturbation theory (CHPT), matching the chiral series order by order. They argue that the interacting kernel used in their original Letter (Oller et al., PRL 95, 172502) induces residual u-channel cuts only in the π⁰Λ → π⁰Λ channel below 1.4 GeV, where no data exist, and that these deviations are negligible for πΣ and absent in other channels.

## Key numbers

- Pole position at (1431.21 − i 1.28) MeV, just below the K⁻p threshold at 1431.95 MeV
- Couplings of the narrow resonance to I=1 states: (1.8, 4.5, 1.0, 0.7, 0.5) GeV to (KΞ, ηΣ, K̅N, πΣ, πΛ), respectively
- The ηΣ threshold is approximately 200 MeV above the resonance mass
- The derivative of the phase of the scattering amplitude is −150 fm, compared to −300 fm for the elastic phase shift claimed by Borasoy et al.

## Context

This reply responds to a Comment by Borasoy et al. (2005) criticizing the original results of Oller, Prades, and Verbeni (PRL 95, 2005), which simultaneously accommodated scattering data and the DEAR measurement of kaonic hydrogen shift and width. The authors distinguish their UCHPT approach from treatments that eliminate u-channel cuts by taking them as constants above some energy, which they argue violates chiral symmetry and crossing even at leading order. The work enables further discussion of whether the narrow I=1 state near threshold is a genuine physical resonance or an artifact of the theoretical framework, and highlights the need for higher-precision data on πΣ event distributions to resolve the issue.
