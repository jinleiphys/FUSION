# Achieving high baryon densities in the fragmentation regions in heavy ion collisions at top RHIC energy

> Heavy ion collisions at extremely high energy, such as the top energy at RHIC, exhibit the property of transparency where there is a clear separation between the almost net-baryon-free central rapidity region and the net-baryon-rich fragmentation region. We calculate the net-baryon rapidity loss and the nuclear excitation energy using the energy-momentum tensor obtained from the McLerran-Venugopalan model. Nuclear compression during the collision is further estimated using a simple space-time picture. The results show that extremely high baryon densities, about twenty times larger than the normal nuclear density, can be achieved in the fragmentation regions.

# Achieving high baryon densities in the fragmentation regions in heavy ion collisions at top RHIC energy

## Key claim
Extremely high baryon densities, about twenty times larger than normal nuclear density, can be achieved in the fragmentation regions of Au-Au collisions at top RHIC energy, but this result depends on using the glasma energy-momentum tensor from the McLerran-Venugopalan model combined with a simple space-time picture of nuclear compression, and the energy density obtained may be overestimated due to the monotonic increase of nuclear excitation energy.

## Method
The authors model central Au-Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV using 1+1 dimensional motion with transverse motion ignored, treating each nucleus as a collection of slabs. They use the glasma energy-momentum tensor from the McLerran-Venugopalan model, computed via small-tau power series expansion, to solve energy-momentum conservation equations that yield time evolution of the slab rapidity and excitation energy. Nuclear compression in the local rest frame is then estimated using the same space-time collision model as Anishetty (1980), with the Woods-Saxon distribution for nuclear density and the calculated rapidity loss.

## Key numbers
- Maximum baryon density achieved is about 3 baryons/fm^3, which is 20 times larger than normal atomic nuclear density of 0.155 nucleons/fm^3
- For the central core region (r_perp = 0): net baryon density n_B = 3.0 baryons/fm^3, energy density epsilon_B = 20 GeV/fm^3, temperature T = 299 MeV, baryon chemical potential mu_B = 1061 MeV, entropy per baryon s/n_B = 26.2
- For peripheral region (r_perp = 5.25 fm): baryon density n_B = 1.5 baryons/fm^3, energy density epsilon_B = 5.5 GeV/fm^3, temperature T = 205 MeV, baryon chemical potential mu_B = 1007 MeV, entropy per baryon s/n_B = 18.9
- Rapidity for central core decreases from initial value 5.36 to about 2.5 within 0.1 to 0.2 fm/c; average rapidity loss at tau = 0.6 fm/c is about 2.4
- Momentum space cut-off scale Q treated as varying parameter from 3 GeV to 5 GeV; initial energy density at central region matched to epsilon(r_perp=0, tau=0.6 fm/c) = 30 GeV/fm^3

## Context
This work extends the original proposal by Anishetty, Koehler and McLerran (1980) who studied fragmentation regions and argued for possible quark-gluon plasma formation with energy density about 2 GeV/fm^3 and compression factor 3.5. It contrasts with the dominant focus on the central rapidity region following Bjorken (1983), and differs from conventional string models such as Mishustin (2002) by using the glasma energy-momentum tensor which includes nonvanishing off-diagonal terms from transverse color fields. The results provide initial conditions for subsequent hydrodynamic evolution in the fragmentation regions and could enable studies of the QCD phase diagram at high baryon chemical potential, with the authors suggesting that scanning large rapidity regions at high energy is equivalent to low energy beam energy scanning.
