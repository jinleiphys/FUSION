# Renormalization of the leading-order chiral nucleon-nucleon interaction and bulk properties of nuclear matter

> We renormalize the two-nucleon interaction at leading order (LO) in chiral perturbation theory using the scheme proposed by Nogga, Timmermans, and van Kolck--also known as modified Weinberg counting. With this interaction, we calculate the energy per nucleon of symmetric nuclear matter in the Brueckner pair approximation and obtain a converged, cutoff-independent result that shows saturation, but also substantial underbinding. We find that the renormalized LO interaction is characterized by an extraordinarily strong tensor force (from one-pion exchange), which is the major cause for the lack of binding. The huge tensor force also leads to the unusually large wound integral of 40% in nuclear matter, which implies a very slow convergence of the hole-line or coupled-cluster expansion, rendering this interaction impractical for many-body calculations. In view of the unusual properties of the renormalized LO interaction and in view of the poor convergence of the nuclear many-body problem with this interaction, there is doubt if this interaction and its predictions can serve as a reasonable and efficient starting point that is improved by perturbative corrections.

## Key claim

The renormalized leading-order (LO) chiral nucleon-nucleon interaction, using modified Weinberg counting, produces a converged, cutoff-independent energy per nucleon in symmetric nuclear matter that shows saturation but substantial underbinding; the authors express doubt that this interaction can serve as a reasonable starting point improved by perturbative corrections due to the unusually strong tensor force and poor convergence of the many-body expansion.

## Method

The authors renormalize the LO chiral NN potential by solving the Lippmann-Schwinger equation with a regulator function and adjusting contact parameters to reproduce empirical scattering lengths (S-waves) and phase shifts at 50 MeV (P and D waves with promoted contacts), following the modified Weinberg counting scheme of Nogga, Timmermans, and van Kolck (2005). They then calculate the energy per nucleon of symmetric nuclear matter using the Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone method (Brueckner pair approximation) with the gap choice for the single-particle potential, solving the Bethe-Goldstone integral equation by matrix inversion in partial-wave decomposition.

## Key numbers

- Saturation occurs at Fermi momentum k_F ≈ 1.0 fm^{-1} with energy per nucleon E/A = -2.6 MeV, compared to empirical values of k_F = 1.35 ± 0.05 fm^{-1} and E/A = -16 ± 1 MeV
- The deuteron D-state probability P_D for the converged LO interaction is 7.2%, versus 4.51% for the N3LO potential (Entem and Machleidt, 2003) and 4.85% for CD-Bonn (Machleidt, 2001)
- The total wound integral κ is 40.5% for the LO interaction, compared to 10.1% for AV18 (Wiringa et al., 1995), 5.8% for CD-Bonn, and 5.0% for N3LO
- The ^3S_1-^3D_1 transition contributes 11.2% to the LO wound integral, versus 5.3% for AV18, 3.4% for CD-Bonn, and 1.7% for N3LO
- The ^3P_0 state contributes 10.9% to the LO wound integral, whereas conventional potentials have at most 0.2%
- Cutoff independence is demonstrated by comparing results at Λ = 5 GeV and Λ = 10 GeV, which are indistinguishable on the scale of the figures

## Context

This work extends the renormalization study of Nogga, Timmermans, and van Kolck (2005), who achieved cutoff independence for NN phase shifts and the triton binding energy at LO, to the nuclear many-body problem of infinite nuclear matter. The results contrast with perturbative chiral approaches such as Kaiser, Fritsch, and Weise (2002), which used low cutoffs (0.4-0.5 GeV) and obtained stronger attraction. The finding that the renormalized LO interaction produces an extraordinarily strong tensor force and a wound integral of 40% implies very slow convergence of the hole-line or coupled-cluster expansion, making this interaction impractical for many-body calculations; the authors suggest that higher-order contributions (NLO or beyond) including tensor contact terms may resolve the problem by simulating the short-range ρ-meson exchange that reduces the pion's tensor force.
