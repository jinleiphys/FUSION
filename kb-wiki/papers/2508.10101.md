# Spectral function approach in NuWro: modeling of multinucleon final states in quasielastic scattering

> Neutrino-oscillation experiments performed in the few-GeV energy region create an urgent demand for a significant improvement in the accuracy of modeling of neutrino interactions with atomic nuclei. Here, we report an updated implementation of the spectral function approach in the \nuwro{} Monte Carlo generator, which consistently treats multinucleon final-states in quasielastic scattering at the inclusive and exclusive level. After validating its accuracy against inclusive electron-scattering data, we compare its predictions to various neutrino cross sections from MicroBooNE. We find that with the multinucleon contribution, these data are reproduced with $χ^2$ per degree of freedom of 1.3--1.8, compared to 2.7--7.2 without it.

## Key claim

The updated spectral function implementation in the NuWro Monte Carlo generator, which includes final-state interactions and correlated nucleons for multinucleon final states, reproduces MicroBooNE neutrino cross section data with a χ² per degree of freedom of 1.3–1.8, compared to 2.7–7.2 without these contributions, but the agreement depends on the specific differential cross section and the accuracy of the intranuclear cascade model.

## Method

The authors implemented a convolution scheme for final-state interactions in the spectral function approach within the NuWro generator, using a folding function that incorporates nuclear transparency and a finite-width distribution to account for energy exchanges between the struck nucleon and the spectator system. For argon targets, they separated the spectral function into mean-field (∼80% of strength) and correlated (∼20% of strength) parts, modeling correlated nucleons using a quasi-deuteron model with Gaussian center-of-mass and relative momentum distributions. They validated the model against inclusive electron-scattering data on carbon before comparing predictions to MicroBooNE charged-current cross sections for events with exactly two protons (CC2p0π) or exactly one proton (CC1p0π) without pions.

## Key numbers

- χ² per degree of freedom for CC2p0π cross section as a function of cosθ_pp: 1.31 (10.5/8) with final-state interactions and correlated protons, versus 2.68 (21.5/8) without them
- χ² per degree of freedom for CC2p0π cross section as a function of cosθ_μ2p: 1.82 (14.6/8) with final-state interactions and correlated protons, versus 6.22 (49.8/8) without them
- χ² per degree of freedom for CC2p0π cross section as a function of δp_T: 0.82 (5.7/7) with final-state interactions and correlated protons, versus 2.33 (16.3/7) without them
- χ² per degree of freedom for CC1p0π cross section as a function of δα_T: 1.69 (11.9/7) with final-state interactions and correlated protons, versus 7.16 (50.2/7) without them
- χ² per degree of freedom for CC1p0π cross section as a function of δp_T: 1.81 (23.6/13) with final-state interactions and correlated protons, versus 4.99 (64.8/13) without them
- Nuclear transparency for carbon at kinetic energy 69 MeV: 0.76, with χ² = 1.23 (25.90/21) compared to experimental data
- Nuclear transparency uncertainty for argon estimated at 3%
- Threshold energy for correlated spectral function in argon: 25.20 MeV
- Correlated part constitutes ∼20% of the total spectral function strength

## Context

This work extends the spectral function approach previously implemented in NuWro by Banerjee et al. (2023) and Prasad et al. (2024), adding consistent treatment of final-state interactions and correlated nucleons for multinucleon final states. It directly addresses the underestimation of MicroBooNE CC1p0π cross sections reported by Nikolakopoulos et al. (2024), who concluded that two-nucleon knockout contributions and interference between one- and two-body currents were needed. The improved model enables more accurate simulations for the Fermilab short-baseline program (SBND, MicroBooNE, and ICARUS) and future neutrino-oscillation experiments such as DUNE and Hyper-Kamiokande, though the authors note that the intranuclear cascade may still underestimate absorptive processes and that optical potentials from elastic scattering data may not be fully accurate at kinematics where quasielastic scattering and resonance production overlap.
