# Application of the Renormalization-group Method to the Reduction of Transport Equations

> We first give a comprehensive review of the renormalization group method for global and asymptotic analysis, putting an emphasis on the relevance to the classical theory of envelopes and on the importance of the existence of invariant manifolds of the dynamics under consideration. We clarify that an essential point of the method is to convert the problem from solving differential equations to obtaining suitable initial (or boundary) conditions:The RG equation determines the slow motion of the would-be integral constants in the unperturbative solution on the invariant manifold. The RG method is applied to derive the Navier-Stokes equation from the Boltzmann equation, as an example of the reduction of dynamics. We work out to obtain the transport coefficients in terms of the one-body distribution function.

## Key claim

The renormalization-group (RG) method provides a systematic perturbative framework for reducing the Boltzmann equation to the Navier-Stokes equation, with the transport coefficients expressed in terms of the one-body distribution function; the derivation is performed by introducing a small parameter \(\epsilon\) in front of the spatial derivative term and constructing the invariant manifold order by order, with the caveat that the second-order analysis neglects the second-order term of \(\tilde{\mathbf{f}}^{(1)}\) which would produce the so-called Burnett terms absent in the usual Navier-Stokes equations.

## Method

The authors apply the RG method to the Boltzmann equation by first introducing a small parameter \(\epsilon\) multiplying the spatial derivative term \(\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla f\) to enable perturbation theory. They expand the distribution function around an arbitrary initial time \(t_0\) and solve the resulting hierarchy of equations order by order, using the condition that fast modes orthogonal to the zero modes of the linearized collision operator must vanish to determine the initial conditions and thereby the deformation of the invariant manifold. The RG equation, derived from the envelope condition \(\partial \tilde{f}/\partial t_0|_{t_0=t}=0\), yields the slow dynamics of the would-be integration constants (the hydrodynamic variables) on the invariant manifold, producing the Navier-Stokes equation and explicit expressions for the transport coefficients.

## Key numbers

* The perturbative expansion parameter \(\epsilon\) is introduced in front of the spatial derivative term \(\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla f\) in the Boltzmann equation, with \(|\epsilon| < 1\) assumed.
* The linear operator \(A\) in the generic formulation has \(m\) zero eigenvalues (the dimension of \(\ker A\)) and other eigenvalues with negative real parts; the projection operators \(P\) and \(Q\) project onto the kernel and its orthogonal complement, respectively.
* The first-order RG equation for the slow variables \(C_i(t)\) is \(\dot{C}_i(t) = \epsilon \langle \tilde{\mathbf{U}}_i, \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{W}_0[\mathbf{C}]) \rangle\) for \(i=1,2,\dots,m\), where \(\tilde{\mathbf{U}}_i\) are the adjoint zero eigenvectors.
* The second-order solution for the damped harmonic oscillator example gives the frequency correction \(\theta(t_0) = -\frac{\epsilon^2}{8}t_0 + \bar{\theta}\), matching \(\sqrt{1-\epsilon^2/4} = 1 - \epsilon^2/8 + O(\epsilon^4)\).

## Context

This work extends the RG method for global and asymptotic analysis, previously developed by the same authors and collaborators (Kunihiro 1995, 1997; Ei, Fujii, and Kunihiro 2000), to the reduction of the Boltzmann equation to fluid dynamics. The approach builds on the concept of invariant manifolds introduced by Bogoliubov (1945) for the fluid dynamical limit of the Boltzmann equation and by Kuramoto (1984) for the reduction of dynamics in general systems. The derivation of the Navier-Stokes equation from the Boltzmann equation via the RG method provides a systematic alternative to the Chapman-Enskog expansion and enables the explicit calculation of transport coefficients from the microscopic distribution function, with the potential to extend the method to higher-order hydrodynamics or to other kinetic equations.
