# Strange Quark Matter and Compact Stars

> Astrophysicists distinguish between three different types of compact stars. These are white dwarfs, neutron stars, and black holes. The former contain matter in one of the densest forms found in the Universe which, together with the unprecedented progress in observational astronomy, make such stars superb astrophysical laboratories for a broad range of most striking physical phenomena. These range from nuclear processes on the stellar surface to processes in electron degenerate matter at subnuclear densities to boson condensates and the existence of new states of baryonic matter--like color superconducting quark matter--at supernuclear densities. More than that, according to the strange matter hypothesis strange quark matter could be more stable than nuclear matter, in which case neutron stars should be largely composed of pure quark matter possibly enveloped in thin nuclear crusts. Another remarkable implication of the hypothesis is the possible existence of a new class of white dwarfs. This article aims at giving an overview of all these striking physical possibilities, with an emphasis on the astrophysical phenomenology of strange quark matter. Possible observational signatures associated with the theoretically proposed states of matter inside compact stars are discussed as well. They will provide most valuable information about the phase diagram of superdense nuclear matter at high baryon number density but low temperature, which is not accessible to relativistic heavy ion collision experiments.

## Key claim

This review article surveys the theoretical possibility that strange quark matter could be more stable than nuclear matter (the strange matter hypothesis), in which case neutron stars would be largely composed of pure quark matter possibly enveloped in thin nuclear crusts, and a new class of white dwarfs might exist. The author emphasizes that these possibilities remain theoretical, as the density at which the expected phase transition to quark matter occurs is not known from experiment and lattice QCD simulations do not yet provide a conclusive guide.

## Method

The paper reviews relativistic mean-field models (Hartree, Hartree-Fock, and Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approximations) and non-relativistic treatments (hole-line expansion, variational Monte Carlo, Thomas-Fermi, and Skyrme density functional approaches) for the equation of state of neutron star matter. These models are solved self-consistently under conditions of electric charge neutrality and chemical equilibrium, incorporating baryons (p, n, Σ, Λ, Ξ), leptons (e⁻, μ⁻), meson condensates (π⁻, K⁻), and quark deconfinement to u, d, s quark matter, including color superconducting phases.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear matter saturation density: ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³ (energy density ε₀ = 140 MeV/fm³)
- Characteristic nucleon radius r_N ∼ 1 fm gives touching density ∼ 0.24 fm⁻³, less than twice ρ₀
- Binding energy of nuclear matter at saturation: E/A = −16.0 MeV
- Effective nucleon mass at saturation: m*_N/m_N = 0.79
- Nuclear matter incompressibility: K ≃ 225 MeV (believed between 220 and 250 MeV)
- Symmetry energy at saturation: a_s = 32.5 MeV
- Quark deconfinement onset in one model: 230 MeV/fm³ (less than 2 ε₀); pure quark matter above 950 MeV/fm³ (∼7 ε₀)
- Color superconducting gap energy: between ∼50 and 100 MeV
- Neutron drip density: ∼4 × 10¹¹ g/cm³
- Strange quark mass: probably less than 100 MeV from lattice results

## Context

This review extends the line of work on compact star composition initiated by Bodmer (1971) and Witten (1984) regarding absolutely stable strange quark matter, and by earlier authors (Ivanenko 1965, Itoh 1970, Baym 1976) on quark matter in neutron star cores. It builds on the relativistic many-body framework of Walecka (1974) and Serot (1986), and on non-relativistic variational calculations by Wiringa (1988) and Akmal (1997). The paper enables future observational tests of color superconductivity via neutron star cooling, pulsar glitches, and cosmic ray experiments (AMS, ECCO), as well as discrimination between strange stars and neutron stars through mass-radius measurements.
