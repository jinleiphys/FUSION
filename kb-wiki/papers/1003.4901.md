# Interaction of strangelets with ordinary nuclei

> Strangelets (hypothetical stable lumps of strange quarkmatter) of astrophysical origin may be ultimately detected in specific cosmic ray experiments. The initial mass distribution resulting from the possible astrophysical production sites would be subject to reprocessing in the interstellar medium and in the earth's atmosphere. In order to get a better understanding of the claims for the detection of this still hypothetic state of hadronic matter, we present a study of strangelet-nucleus interactions including several physical processes of interest (abrasion, fusion, fission, excitation and de-excitation of the strangelets), to address the fate of the baryon number along the strangelet path. It is shown that, although fusion may be important for low-energy strangelets in the interstellar medium (thus increasing the initial baryon number A), in the earth's atmosphere the loss of the baryon number should be the dominant process. The consequences of these findings are briefly addressed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while fusion may increase the baryon number of low-energy strangelets in the interstellar medium, baryon number loss (primarily through abrasion) should be the dominant process for strangelets traversing the Earth's atmosphere, contrary to earlier suggestions that fusion could allow strangelets to penetrate deeply.

## Method

The authors constructed a Monte Carlo code that tracks individual strangelet-nucleus collisions, randomly sampling impact parameters and excitation energies. For each collision, the code checks conditions for fusion (based on energy deposition relative to the Coulomb barrier and binding energy), abrasion (using a geometric model from Wilson et al. that calculates the sheared-off overlap region), and fission (using a liquid drop model with activation energies). After each interaction, the code evaluates de-excitation through photon, neutrino, and pion emission as well as neutron evaporation based on the calculated temperature of the strangelet.

## Key numbers

- The mean free path for nucleon-nucleon interaction used in the abrasion model is taken from a parametrisation of the nucleon-nucleon cross section, with the mean energy transferred to a nucleon per unit intersected path of order 13 MeV/fm
- For strangelets interacting with protons in the interstellar medium, the highest excitation energies acquired were not enough by a factor of at least 10 to cause fission of relatively low-mass strangelets
- The mean excitation energy after collisions in the atmosphere is up to approximately 800 MeV for abrasion, up to approximately 1.3 GeV for unpaired strangelets after fusion with nitrogen, and up to approximately 3 GeV for CFL strangelets after fusion with nitrogen
- Temperatures after fusion in the atmosphere are lower than approximately 6 MeV for unpaired strangelets and up to approximately 18 MeV for CFL strangelets when fusion is followed by fission
- For strangelets without pairing and CFL with baryon number greater than 1000, temperatures from fusion (not followed by fission) are always lower than approximately 3 MeV and approximately 14 MeV, respectively
- The column density travelled between collisions of strangelets with nuclei in the atmosphere is taken as approximately 30 g/cm^2, with a typical energy loss per collision of Delta E/E approximately 3 percent
- For high-mass strangelets in the atmosphere, the total decrease in baryon number from abrasion can reach values of order 10^2

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Menos (proposing mass loss) and Mais (proposing mass gain through fusion) by simultaneously considering abrasion, fusion, fission, and de-excitation processes within a single consistent framework. The authors adapt the abrasion model of Wilson et al. (originally developed for ordinary nuclear spallation) to strangelet-nucleus interactions, and compare their results to the fission model of Wu (which included rotational energy but did not model spallation in detail). The analysis enables better predictions for the reprocessing of strangelet mass distributions in the interstellar medium and atmosphere, and provides a framework for evaluating claims of strangelet detection in cosmic ray experiments such as those by Price, the SLIM collaboration, and others studying Centauro-type events.
