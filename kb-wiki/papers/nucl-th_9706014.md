# Matrix Elements between Nuclear Compound States and Dynamical Enhancement of the Weak Interaction

> A method to calculate the mean squared matrix element of weak interaction between compound states is developed. The result is expressed in terms of matrix elements of the nucleon-nucleon strong and weak interactions times the Fermi distribution functions at finite temperature. Numerical calculations for $^{233}$Th are in excellent agreement with recent measurements of parity nonconservation effects in neutron capture. In fact, our calculations prove that the factor of dynamical enhancement (ratio of compound-nucleus effect to single-particle one) really exceeds 100, thus making it unnesessary to assume a value of the weak constant bigger than standard one ($g\simeq 10^{8} ε\sim 1÷4$).

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the dynamical enhancement factor (ratio of compound-nucleus parity-violating effect to single-particle one) exceeds 100, making it unnecessary to assume a weak interaction constant larger than the standard value (g ≃ 10⁸ ε ∼ 1–4), contrary to the alternative "valence mechanism" which would require the neutron weak constant to be two orders of magnitude larger than theory predicts.

## Method

The authors develop a statistical model to calculate the mean squared weak matrix element between compound nuclear states of opposite parity, expressing it in terms of nucleon-nucleon strong and weak interaction matrix elements multiplied by Fermi distribution functions at finite temperature. They use the equivalence of canonical and microcanonical ensembles for systems with many degrees of freedom, introducing an effective nuclear temperature T = 0.6 MeV determined from consistency conditions for excitation energy. Numerical calculations for ²³³Th employ a Woods-Saxon single-particle basis and the Landau-Migdal particle-hole interaction with standard parameter values, computing the mean squared weak matrix element via Eq. ## Key numbers

- For ²³³Th, the calculated mean squared weak matrix element √(W²) = 2.08 meV (case I, standard procedure) and 3.57 meV (case II, exchange terms included explicitly), compared to the experimental value 1.39⁺⁰·⁵⁵₋₀·₃₈ meV
- The single-particle (valence) estimate w_val ≃ (1/N̄) × g_n × 0.740 eV = 1.72 × 10⁻³ meV, making the statistical contribution 10³ times larger
- The number of principal components N̄ = πΓ_spr/(2d) with d = 17 eV and Γ_spr ≃ 2 MeV, giving N̄⁻¹/² ≃ 2.3 × 10⁻³
- The temperature used in the canonical ensemble is T = 0.6 MeV, determined from the consistency condition for excitation energy equal to the neutron separation energy

## Context

This work extends the statistical model of dynamical enhancement developed by Haas (1962) and Sushkov and Flambaum (1980-1981), which predicted large parity-violating effects in neutron capture due to mixing of closely lying compound nuclear levels of opposite parity. It contradicts the "valence mechanism" of Zaretsky and Sirotkin (1983) and Nghiem Dinh (1985), which assumes the effect is of single-particle nature and would require the neutron weak constant to be two orders of magnitude larger than standard theory. The method avoids hypotheses used in earlier works by Johnson, Bowman, and Yoo (1990) and by Urin and Vyazankin (1991), and enables calculation of weak matrix elements between compound states without assuming proportionality to the square of the frequency of a time-dependent field or to T².
