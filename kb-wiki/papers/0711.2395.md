# The Casimir effect as scattering problem

> We show that Casimir-force calculations for a finite number of non-overlapping obstacles can be mapped onto quantum-mechanical billiard-type problems which are characterized by the scattering of a fictitious point particle off the very same obstacles. With the help of a modified Krein trace formula the genuine/finite part of the Casimir energy is determined as the energy-weighted integral over the log-determinant of the multi-scattering matrix of the analog billiard problem. The formalism is self-regulating and inherently shows that the Casimir energy is governed by the infrared end of the multi-scattering phase shifts or spectrum of the fluctuating field. The calculation is exact and in principle applicable for any separation(s) between the obstacles. In practice, it is more suited for large- to medium-range separations. We report especially about the Casimir energy of a fluctuating massless scalar field between two spheres or a sphere and a plate under Dirichlet and Neumann boundary conditions. But the formalism can easily be extended to any number of spheres and/or planes in three or arbitrary dimensions, with a variety of boundary conditions or non-overlapping potentials/non-ideal reflectors.

## Key claim

The Casimir energy for a finite number of non-overlapping obstacles can be mapped exactly onto a quantum-mechanical billiard-type scattering problem, but the formalism is in practice more suited for large- to medium-range separations because the angular-momentum matrices become cumbersome at very close distances (below about 0.1 times the sphere radius).

## Method

The geometry-dependent part of the density of states is extracted via a modified Krein trace formula that uses the inverse multi-scattering matrix instead of the full S-matrix, thereby subtracting out the bulk and single-obstacle contributions. The Casimir energy is then obtained as an energy-weighted integral over the log-determinant of this multi-scattering matrix, which after a Wick rotation becomes an integral over imaginary wave numbers. For the sphere-plate case under Dirichlet boundary conditions, the determinant is extracted from the antisymmetric (Dirichlet) sector of the two-sphere system by exploiting the reflection symmetry across the vertical symmetry plane.

## Key numbers

* For the fermionic Casimir effect of two spherical cavities of common radius a at center-to-center separation r, the integrated density of states in the s-wave approximation is approximately (a^2 / (pi r^2)) sin[2(r - a)k] plus terms of order (ka)^3.
* The fermionic Casimir energy for two spherical cavities scales as a^2 / L^3 with L = r - 2a, while the sphere-plate fermionic Casimir energy scales as a / L^2 with L = r - a.
* For the scalar Dirichlet sphere-plate system at large separations L >> a, the Casimir energy asymptotically approaches - (pi^3 hbar c a) / (1440 L^2) times a factor of 1.847, which is twice the semiclassical result.
* The p-wave contribution to the scalar Dirichlet sphere-plate Casimir energy at large separations scales as - (5 pi^3 hbar c a^3) / (1440 L^4) times (90 / pi^4).
* The semiclassical approximation for the scalar Dirichlet sphere-plate Casimir energy is given by - (hbar c / (16 pi)) (a / L^2) (pi^4 / 90) times [1 - (5/pi^2 - 1/3)(L/a) + O((L/a)^2)].

## Context

This work extends the Krein trace formula approach previously applied to the non-relativistic fermionic Casimir effect (BW01, BMW05) to the scalar Casimir effect with massless fields, building on earlier multi-scattering matrix formulations for n-disk and n-sphere systems (Gaspard, Wirzba 1998; HWG97). The exact results for the sphere-plate scalar Casimir energy agree with worldline numerical data by Gies and coworkers (2005, 2006) and with semiclassical calculations by Schaden and Spruch, while also providing a systematic asymptotic expansion to sixth subleading order in collaboration with Bordag. The formalism enables exact calculations for any number of spheres and planes with various boundary conditions, and clarifies the relationship between the s-wave dominated scalar Casimir effect and the p-wave dominated electromagnetic Casimir-Polder interaction.
