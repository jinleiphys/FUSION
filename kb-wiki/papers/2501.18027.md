# Spectral-statistics properties of the experimental and theoretical light baryon and meson spectra

> We compare the statistical fluctuation properties of the baryon and meson experimental mass spectra with those obtained from theoretical models (quark models and lattice QCD). We find that for the experimental spectra the statistical properties are close to those predicted by Random Matrix Theory for chaotic systems, while for the theoretical ones they are in general closer to those predicted for integrable systems and safely incompatible with those of chaotic systems. We stress the importance of the agreement of the fluctuation properties between experiment and theoretical models, as they determine the dynamical regime and the complexity of the real interactions. We emphasize the new statistical method we use, adapted for properly analyzing the fluctuation properties for very short spectral sequences.

## Key claim

The experimental baryon and meson mass spectra exhibit spectral fluctuation properties close to those predicted by Random Matrix Theory for chaotic systems (Wigner-like), while theoretical spectra from quark models and lattice QCD are generally closer to integrable (Poisson-like) predictions and safely incompatible with chaos; the authors stress that this discrepancy cannot be explained by the "missing resonances" problem, as incomplete spectra would be pushed toward Poisson rather than away from it.

## Method

The authors split the experimental and theoretical hadron spectra into sequences with fixed good quantum numbers (spin J, isospin I, parity P, and C-parity for mesons), then performed local unfolding on each short sequence to remove the smooth level density. They constructed distorted reference distributions (Wigner, Poisson, and Berry-Robnik) by applying the same unfolding procedure to ensembles of 1000 GOE and Poisson spectra with identical sequence lengths, and compared the experimental and theoretical nearest-neighbor spacing distributions (NNSD) using the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test and moment analysis.

## Key numbers

- Experimental baryon spectrum: 53 levels distributed in 14 sequences; K-S test p-values: p_DW = 0.82 (vs distorted Wigner), p_DP = 0.26 (vs distorted Poisson)
- Experimental meson spectrum: 129 levels distributed in 23 sequences; best-fit Berry-Robnik parameter f = 0.78 ± 0.03; K-S test p-values: p_DW = 0.38, p_DP = 0.13, p_DBR = 0.65
- Theoretical baryon spectra: CI model (Capstick-Isgur) d=145 levels, p_DW = 5×10⁻⁴; L1 model d=164 levels, p_DW = 10⁻⁴; L2 model d=124 levels, p_DW = 2×10⁻⁴
- Error-bar robustness test for baryons: mean p_DW = 0.81 ± 0.08 over 1000 realizations; for mesons: mean p_DBR = 0.72 ± 0.06, mean p_DP = 0.27 ± 0.09

## Context

This work extends the earlier statistical analysis of Fernandez et al. (2007) on baryons and Munoz et al. (2012) on mesons, using updated data from the 2014 Review of Particle Physics and a refined method that constructs distorted reference distributions from ensembles of 1000 realizations to properly handle very short spectral sequences. The results confirm that quark models (Capstick-Isgur 1986, Loring et al. 2001) and the lattice QCD calculation by the Hadron Spectrum Collaboration (Dudek et al.) fail to reproduce the chaotic fluctuation properties of the experimental spectra, suggesting these theoretical approaches may not be suitable for predicting missing resonances or describing the dynamical regime of the real strong interaction.
