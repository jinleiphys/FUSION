# Structure and Cooling of Neutron and Hybrid Stars

> The study of neutron stars is a topic of central interest in the investigation of the properties of strongly compressed hadronic matter. Whereas in heavy-ion collisions the fireball, created in the collision zone, contains very hot matter, with varying density depending on the beam energy, neutron stars largely sample the region of cold and dense matter with the exception of the very short time period of the existence of the proto-neutron star. Therefore, neutron star physics, in addition to its general importance in astrophysics, is a crucial complement to heavy-ion physics in the study of strongly interacting matter. In the following, model approaches will be introduced to calculate properties of neutron stars that incorporate baryons and quarks. These approaches are also able to describe the state of matter over a wide range of temperatures and densities, which is essential if one wants to connect and correlate star observables and results from heavy-ion collisions. The effect of exotic particles and quark cores on neutron star properties will be considered. In addition to the gross properties of the stars like their masses and radii their expected inner composition is quite sensitive to the models used. The effect of the composition can be studied through the analysis of the cooling curve of the star. In addition, we consider the effect of rotation, as in this case the particle composition of the star can be modified quite drastically.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while neutron star masses alone cannot strongly constrain the interior composition (as different degrees of freedom can produce similar masses), the combination of rotation and cooling curves, specifically the delayed onset of direct Urca cooling due to spin-down, can provide a sensitive probe of the star's internal structure, potentially explaining the observed rapid temperature drop of the neutron star in Cassiopeia A.

## Method

The authors use a unified chiral SU(3) hadronic model extended to include quark degrees of freedom via a Polyakov-loop-inspired field, solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for static and rotating stars. They compute mass-radius relations and particle populations for stars containing nucleons, hyperons, Delta resonances, and quark cores, then model the cooling evolution by combining neutrino emission from direct Urca processes with the effects of rotation and spin-down on central density.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of purely hadronic stars including nucleons, hyperons, and spin-3/2 decuplet: 1.93 solar masses (down from 2.12 solar masses for nucleons only)
- Maximum mass of hybrid stars with quark core in the quark-hadron model: 1.93 solar masses
- Strangeness fraction in the core of the heaviest purely hadronic star: about 0.1 strange quarks per baryon
- Reducing hyperon vector coupling by 50 percent increases strangeness fraction ten-fold to 1.0 and reduces maximum mass by half a solar mass
- Measured mass of pulsar PSR J1614-2230: 1.97 ± 0.04 solar masses
- Fastest known rotator PSR J1748-2446ad: rotational frequency of 716 Hz
- Central energy density increases by typically 50 percent during spin-down from Kepler frequency to zero rotation
- Rapid temperature drop in cooling curves occurs after about 100 years for static stars; for rotating stars the drop can be delayed, with the Cassiopeia A neutron star showing a steep drop 300 years after supernova

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous chiral SU(3) hadronic model (Papazoglou 1997, 1998; Dexheimer 2008) by incorporating quarks and a Polyakov-loop deconfinement mechanism, and by adding rotation and cooling calculations. It directly addresses the tension between the 2-solar-mass pulsar PSR J1614-2230 (Demorest 2010) and the tendency of hyperonic and simple MIT bag quark models to produce lower maximum masses (as discussed by Vidana 2011, Weissenborn 2012, and others). The cooling analysis builds on the first real-time observation of neutron star cooling in Cassiopeia A (Heinke 2010) and proposes rotation-delayed Urca cooling as an explanation, enabling future two-dimensional cooling simulations (Negreiros 2012) to test against observational data.
