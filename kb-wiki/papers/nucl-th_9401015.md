# Simple Formula for Nuclear Charge Radius

> A new formula for the nuclear charge radius is proposed, dependent on the mass number (A) and neutron excess (N-Z) in the nucleus. It is simple and it reproduces all the experimentally available mean square radii and their isotopic shifts of even--even nuclei much better than other frequently used relations.

# Simple Formula for Nuclear Charge Radius

## Key claim

The authors propose a three-parameter phenomenological formula for the nuclear charge radius that reproduces both absolute mean square charge radii and isotopic shifts for even–even nuclei with proton number Z ≥ 8, achieving significantly better agreement than the liquid drop or liquid droplet models. The formula includes a term proportional to 1/A which is necessary to obtain reasonable results for lighter nuclei (Z < 38), whereas for heavier nuclei (Z ≥ 38) a simpler two-parameter form without the 1/A term suffices.

## Method

The authors fit the parameters of a spherical charge radius formula R₀₀ = r₀ A^(1/3) (1 + κ/A − α (N−Z)/A) by minimizing the sum of squared errors simultaneously against experimental mean square charge radii (86 data points) and isotopic shifts (220 data points) for even–even nuclei with Z ≥ 8. The mean square radii are calculated macroscopically assuming a uniform charge distribution and using equilibrium nuclear deformations (βλ up to λ=6) taken from Möller et al. (1993) to account for deformation effects.

## Key numbers

- Best-fit parameters for the full three-parameter formula: r₀ = 1.240 fm, κ = 1.646, α = 0.191
- For nuclei with Z ≥ 38, the two-parameter formula (κ = 0) gives: r₀ = 1.256 fm, α = 0.202
- The sum of square errors for mean square radii using the three-parameter formula is more than six times lower than in the liquid droplet model of Myers and Schmidt (1983)
- The α parameter (isospin dependence) is stable around α ∼ 0.2 regardless of the number of fitted data or inclusion of the κ/A term
- For the two-parameter formula without the 1/A term, the earlier parameter set from Nerlo-Pomorska and Pomorski (1993) was r₀ = 1.25 fm, α = 0.2

## Context

This work extends earlier efforts by the same authors (Nerlo-Pomorska and Pomorski, 1993) that reproduced isotopic shifts but failed to give correct absolute mean square radii for light nuclei. It improves upon the traditional liquid drop formula (R₀₀ = r₀ A^(1/3) with r₀ = 1.2 fm) and the liquid droplet model of Myers and Schmidt (1983), both of which systematically underestimate mean square charge radii, especially for lighter elements. The new formula enables more accurate macroscopic calculations of nuclear charge distributions and can serve as a reference for microscopic models or for predicting radii of nuclei not yet measured experimentally.
