# Structure and decay at rapid proton capture waiting points

> We investigate the region of the nuclear chart around $A \simeq 70$ from a three-body perspective, where we compute reaction rates for the radiative capture of two protons. One key quantity is here the photon dissociation cross section for the inverse process where two protons are liberated from the borromean nucleus by photon bombardment. We find a number of peaks at low photon energy in this cross section where each peak is located at the energy corresponding to population of a three-body resonance. Thus, for these energies the decay or capture processes proceed through these resonances. However, the next step in the dissociation process still has the option of following several paths, that is either sequential decay by emission of one proton at a time with an intermediate two-body resonance as stepping stone, or direct decay into the continuum of both protons simultaneously. The astrophysical reaction rate is obtained by folding of the cross section as function of energy with the occupation probability for a Maxwell-Boltzmann temperature distribution. The reaction rate is then a function of temperature, and of course depending on the underlying three-body bound state and resonance structures. We show that a very simple formula at low temperature reproduces the elaborate numerically computed reaction rate.

## Key claim

The paper shows that for the borromean nucleus \(^{68}\)Se\(+p+p\), the astrophysical reaction rate for two-proton radiative capture can be accurately reproduced at low temperatures by a simple analytic formula based on a single Breit-Wigner resonance, provided the three-body resonances are well-separated and narrow. This simplification glosses over the fact that the full calculation must include both sequential and direct decay paths, and that the dipole transition rate can drop by many orders of magnitude if the \(d_{5/2}\) resonance energy is increased sufficiently, making the quadrupole transition dominant.

## Method

The authors use a hyperspherical adiabatic expansion of the Faddeev equations in coordinate space to solve the three-body problem for \(^{68}\)Se\(+p+p\). They employ a phenomenological proton-proton interaction and a Woods-Saxon core-proton potential with central and spin-orbit parts, tuning the strengths to exclude Pauli-forbidden states and isolate specific orbitals (\(f_{5/2}\), \(p_{3/2}\), \(d_{5/2}\)). The photon dissociation cross section is computed from the overlap between the bound three-body state and discretized continuum states via electric multipole operators, and the astrophysical reaction rate is obtained by folding this cross section with a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution. A simplified rate formula is derived by assuming a Breit-Wigner shape for isolated narrow resonances.

## Key numbers

- The two-body energy of the \(p_{3/2}\) and \(f_{5/2}\) orbitals is set to 0.64 MeV, matching the measured proton separation energy of \(^{69}\)Br from San (2014).
- The quadrupole transition (\(2^+ \rightarrow 0^+\)) reaction rate is computed; the dipole transition (\(1^- \rightarrow 0^+\)) rate is scaled down by a factor of \(10^4\) in the figure.
- When the \(d_{5/2}\) resonance energy is increased to 1.5 MeV, the dipole rate changes; when increased to 3.0 MeV, the potential cannot sustain a \(1^-\) resonance and the dipole rate drops by many orders of magnitude.
- The alternative limit uses both \(p_{3/2}\) and \(f_{5/2}\) orbitals at 0.74 MeV, corresponding to the Borromean region boundary, to produce a second quadrupole rate curve.

## Context

This work extends three-body calculations, previously limited to nuclei lighter than \(^{38}\)Ca (Gor 1995), to the heavier critical rp-process waiting point \(^{68}\)Se, identified as the most important such nucleus by Tu (2011). The study builds on earlier few-body treatments of Borromean systems (Zhu 1993, Hov 2014) and provides a flexible method to adjust reaction rates as new two-body energy data become available, which is particularly relevant for the \(A \simeq 70\) region where experiments and shell-model calculations (Nic 2014, Nes 2014) indicate dominant \(f_{5/2}\) and \(p_{3/2}\) orbitals. The simplified rate formula enables rapid recalculation of astrophysical rates without repeating the full three-body computation.
