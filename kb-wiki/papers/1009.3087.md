# Number-conserving theory of nuclear pairing gaps: a global assessment

> We study odd-even mass staggering of nuclei, also called pairing gaps, using a Skyrme self-consistent mean-field theory and a numerically exact treatment of the pairing Hamiltonian. We find that the configuration-space Monte Carlo method proposed by Cerf and Martin offers a practical computational procedure to carry out the numerical solutions in large-dimensional model spaces. Refitting the global strength of the pairing interaction for 443 neutron pairing gaps in our number-conserving treatment, we find the correction to the pairing correlation energies and pairing gaps to have rms values of 0.6 MeV and 0.12 MeV, respectively. The exact treatment provides a significant improvement in the fit to experimental gaps, although it is partially masked by a larger rms error due to deficiencies in other aspects of the theory such as the mean-field energy functional.

# Number-conserving theory of nuclear pairing gaps: a global assessment

## Key claim

The configuration-space Monte Carlo (CSMC) method provides a practical numerically exact treatment of the pairing Hamiltonian for a global survey of 443 neutron pairing gaps, but the improvement over BCS theory is partially masked by larger rms errors from deficiencies in the mean-field energy functional itself.

## Method

The authors constructed pairing Hamiltonians from self-consistent mean-field (SCMF) calculations using the SLy4 Skyrme functional and a density-dependent contact pairing interaction, extracting single-particle energies and pairing matrix elements from converged SCMF solutions. They solved these Hamiltonians exactly using the configuration-space Monte Carlo (CSMC) method of Cerf and Martin (1993), which avoids the sign problem when all pairing matrix elements are attractive, and replaced the BCS ground-state energy with the exact CSMC ground-state energy while refitting the global pairing strength V0 to minimize rms residuals against experimental data.

## Key numbers

- The rms difference between CSMC and BCS pairing correlation energies is 0.6 MeV for the data set of over 900 nuclei.
- The rms difference between CSMC and BCS pairing gaps is 0.12 MeV.
- The rms residual of CSMC pairing gaps with respect to experiment is 0.24 MeV, compared to 0.28 MeV for BCS and 0.30 MeV for the original SCMF treatment from Bertsch et al. (2009).
- The fitted pairing strength V0 for CSMC is 627 MeV fm^3, compared to 667 MeV fm^3 for BCS and 700 MeV fm^3 for the original SCMF treatment.
- For nuclei whose SCMF residual exceeds 0.5 MeV, the BCS rms increases to 0.68 MeV while the CSMC rms is 0.52 MeV, an improvement of 0.14 MeV.
- The largest model space used was Omega=64 for the nucleus N=156, Z=100, with each CSMC calculation taking about 30 minutes on a single processor.

## Context

This work extends the global survey of nuclear pairing gaps by Bertsch et al. (2009), which found that approximately 25% of nuclei lacked a BCS pairing condensate and that the Lipkin-Nogami (LN) extension of BCS provided only modest improvement. The CSMC method, previously applied to individual isotope chains by Cerf and Martin (1993) and by Caurier et al. (1998), is here used for the first time in a global survey, enabling exact treatment of pairing in model spaces up to Omega=64 that would be prohibitively large for direct matrix diagonalization. The results enable future work to isolate the remaining deficiencies in the mean-field energy functional, which currently dominate the overall error budget.
