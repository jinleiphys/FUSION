# Mass ejection in failed supernovae: equation of state and neutrino loss dependence

> A failed core-collapse supernova from a non-rotating progenitor can eject mass due to a weakening of gravity associated to neutrino emission by the protoneutron star. This mechanism yields observable transients and sets an upper limit to the mass of the black hole (BH) remnant. Previous global simulations of this mechanism have included neutrino losses parametrically, however, with direct implications for the ejecta mass and energy. Here we evolve the inner supernova core with a spherically-symmetric, general-relativistic neutrino radiation-hydrodynamic code until BH formation. We then use the result in a Newtonian code that follows the response of the outer layers of the star to the change in gravity and resolves the surface pressure scale height. We find that the dense-matter equation of state (EOS) can introduce a factor $\sim 2$ variation in gravitational mass lost to neutrinos, with a stiff EOS matching previous parametric results, and a soft EOS yielding lower ejecta masses and energies by a factor of several. This difference is caused primarily by the longer time to BH formation in stiffer EOSs. With a soft EOS, our red and yellow supergiant progenitors fail to unbind mass if hydrogen recombination energy is not included. Using a linear ramp in time for mass-energy lost to neutrinos (with suitable parameters) yields a stellar response within $\sim 10\%$ of that obtained using the detailed history of neutrino losses. Our results imply quantitative but not qualitative modifications to previous predictions for shock breakout, plateau emission, and final BH masses from these events.

# Mass ejection in failed supernovae: equation of state and neutrino loss dependence

## Key claim
The dense-matter equation of state introduces a factor ~2 variation in gravitational mass lost to neutrinos during failed supernovae, with stiff EOSs matching previous parametric results while soft EOSs yield lower ejecta masses and energies by a factor of several; however, these modifications are quantitative rather than qualitative for predictions of shock breakout, plateau emission, and final black hole masses.

## Method
The authors evolved the inner supernova core from collapse until black hole formation using the spherically-symmetric, general-relativistic neutrino radiation-hydrodynamic code GR1D with three different dense-matter equations of state (SFHo, DD2, LS220). They then used the resulting gravitational mass-loss history as input to a Newtonian hydrodynamic code (FLASH) that followed the response of the outer stellar layers to the change in gravity, resolving the surface pressure scale height. For comparison, they also tested a linear ramp parameterization of the neutrino mass-loss history and a direct remapping of initial conditions from GR1D to FLASH.

## Key numbers
- Gravitational mass lost to neutrinos at black hole formation: 0.196 Msun (R15 with SFHo), 0.222 Msun (R15 with LS220), >0.262 Msun (R15 with DD2)
- Time to black hole formation: 2.836 s (R15 with SFHo), 2.947 s (R15 with LS220), >4.359 s (R15 with DD2)
- Ejecta mass for R15 progenitor: 2.19 Msun (SFHo), 2.42 Msun (LS220), 3.37 Msun (DD2)
- Ejecta mass for B25 progenitor: 2.80e-2 Msun (SFHo), 3.18e-2 Msun (LS220), 5.45e-2 Msun (DD2)
- Ejecta mass for W40 progenitor: 1.44e-4 Msun (SFHo), 1.63e-4 Msun (LS220), 6.30e-4 Msun (DD2)
- Maximum kinetic energy of acoustic pulse for R15: 2.15e47 erg (SFHo), 2.42e47 erg (LS220), 3.59e47 erg (DD2)
- Linear ramp approximation yields stellar response within ~10% of detailed neutrino loss history
- With soft EOS (SFHo), red and yellow supergiant progenitors fail to unbind mass if hydrogen recombination energy is not included

## Context
This work extends the parametric approach of Lovegrove and Woosley (2013) and Fernandez (2018) by replacing analytic prescriptions for neutrino-driven gravitational mass loss with self-consistent radiation-hydrodynamic calculations. The results enable more reliable predictions for electromagnetic transients from failed supernovae, including shock breakout and plateau emission, and improve upper limits on black hole remnant masses. The study connects to prior core-collapse simulations by O'Connor (2015) and Pan (2018) by using the same progenitor models (s20, s40) and equations of state for calibration.
