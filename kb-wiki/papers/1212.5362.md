# Many-body correlations in Semiclassical Molecular Dynamics and Skyrme interaction

> Constraint Molecular dynamics CoMD calculations have been performed for asymmetric nuclear matter (NM) by using a simple effective interactions of the Skyrme type. The set of parameter values reproducing common accepted saturation properties of nuclear matter have been obtained for different degree of stiffness characterizing the iso-vectorial potential density dependence. A comparison with results obtained in the limit of the Semi-Classical Mean Field approximation using the same kind of interaction put in evidence the role played by the many-body correlations in to explain the noticeable differences obtained in the parameter values in the two cases. Even if from a numerical point of view the obtained results are strictly valid for the CoMD model, some rather general feature of the discussed correlations can give a wider meaning to the obtained differences being strongly related to the spacial correlations generated in the semiclassical wave packets dynamics.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the energy-density functional produced by Constraint Molecular Dynamics (CoMD) calculations differs significantly from that obtained in the Semi-Classical Mean Field Approximation (Se-MFA) when using the same Skyrme-type effective interaction, with the differences arising from many-body spatial correlations that are naturally present in the wave-packet dynamics but absent in the mean-field treatment. The author notes that while the numerical results are strictly valid for the CoMD model, the general features of the correlations may have wider meaning due to their connection to spatial correlations generated in semiclassical wave-packet dynamics.

## Method

The author performed Constraint Molecular Dynamics (CoMD) calculations for asymmetric nuclear matter using a Skyrme-type effective interaction with isoscalar two-body, three-body, and isovector terms, employing four different form factors for the isovector density dependence. The total energy per nucleon was evaluated by solving the many-body problem using the equation of motion regulating the wave-packet dynamics, with the kinetic energy contribution obtained self-consistently through a cooling-warming procedure coupled with Pauli-principle constraints on occupation numbers. The results were compared with those from the Semi-Classical Mean Field Approximation using the same interaction, where the energy functional depends only on the local density rather than on overlap integrals and spatial correlation functions.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density: ρ₀ = 0.165 fm⁻³
- Binding energy at saturation: E(ρ₀) = -16 MeV
- Compressibility modulus for symmetric nuclear matter: K_NM(ρ₀) = 220 MeV
- Symmetry energy at saturation density: e_sym ≈ 28 MeV (specifically 28.6 MeV for T₄ = 32 MeV)
- Skyrme parameter values obtained from Se-MFA: T₀ = -263 MeV, T₃ = 208 MeV, σ = 1.25
- Density range explored: 0.7ρ₀ to 1.2ρ₀ in steps of 0.1ρ₀
- Asymmetry parameter range investigated: β = 0 to 0.2
- System sizes: 2000 nucleons for probability distribution calculations, 3500 particles for kinetic energy studies
- Wave-packet width in coordinate space: σ_r of order 1-2 fm, with typical spreading volume of 2-10 fm³

## Context

This work extends the comparison between mean-field based approaches (such as the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck transport models of BNV, VUU, and BUU types) and molecular dynamics models (QMD, CoMD, AMD, FMD) by examining how the same Skyrme effective interaction produces different energy-density functionals in the two frameworks. The study is motivated by the work of Reinhard et al. who obtained liquid drop parameters from Skyrme-Hartree-Fock and relativistic mean-field calculations, and it builds on the CoMD model developed by Papa et al. The results enable a better understanding of how spatial correlations affect the relationship between the elementary nucleon-nucleon interaction and the effective energy functional used in nuclear matter calculations, particularly for the symmetry energy and its density dependence.
