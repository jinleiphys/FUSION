# Monte Carlo Methods for Nuclear Structure

> Quantum Monte Carlo methods find fruitful application in large shell model problems. These methods reduce the imaginary-time many-body evolution operator to a coherent superposition of one-body evolutions in a fluctuating one-body field; the resultant path integral is evaluated stochastically. After a brief review of the capabilities and general strategy of Shell Model Monte Carlo methods, I discuss results and insights obtained from a number of applications. These include the ground state and thermal properties of {\it pf}-shell nuclei, the thermal and rotational behavior of rare-earth and $γ$-soft nuclei, and the calculation of double beta-decay matrix elements. Prospects for further progress in such calculations are also discussed.

## Key claim

Shell Model Monte Carlo (SMMC) methods can calculate thermal averages of observables and ground-state properties for nuclear shell-model problems with Hilbert-space dimensions in the billions, but they cannot provide detailed spectroscopy of individual excited states except for the ground state obtained at very low temperature.

## Method

The many-body imaginary-time evolution operator is transformed into a coherent superposition of one-body evolutions in a fluctuating one-body field via a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation; the resulting path integral over auxiliary fields is evaluated stochastically using Monte Carlo sampling on massively parallel computers. The numerical effort scales as the cube of the number of single-particle states and is independent of the number of valence nucleons, in contrast to conventional diagonalization methods.

## Context

This work extends the Shell Model Monte Carlo methods developed at Caltech over the previous five years, building on foundational papers by Johnson et al. (1992), Lang et al. (1993), Alhassid et al. (1994), and Ormand et al. (1994). The results provide the first complete pf-shell calculations of ground-state and thermal properties for Fe-peak nuclei, resolve the long-standing discrepancy between independent-particle estimates and measured Gamow-Teller strengths, and offer the first microscopic many-body description of gamma-soft nuclei near A=124. The methods enable future calculations of presupernova electron-capture rates, two-neutrino double-beta decay matrix elements for 76Ge, 128Te, and 130Te, and systematic studies of rare-earth nuclei at finite temperature and spin.
