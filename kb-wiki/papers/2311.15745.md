# Prospects of identifying the presence of Strange Stars using Gravitational Waves from binary systems

> The existence of self-bound strange stars is a long-standing mystery in astrophysics. Future astrophysical data, even with improved precision, may not allow us to discriminate them from neutron stars, given the uncertainties in observational and theoretical modeling. In this work, we propose a unique strategy to distinguish strange stars from neutron stars using gravitational waves from binary compact star systems. We demonstrate that empirical relations connecting f-mode frequencies with tidal deformation are distinct for the two classes of compact objects, irrespective of their equations of state. Therefore simultaneous measurement of f-mode frequency and tidal deformability from the inspiral phase of compact binary mergers with the next-generation detectors can provide smoking gun evidence for the presence of strange stars. This would have crucial implications not only in gravitational wave physics but multidisciplinary fields such as nuclear and high energy physics.

## Key claim
The paper proposes that simultaneous measurement of the f-mode frequency and tidal deformability from gravitational wave signals of binary compact star mergers can distinguish strange stars from neutron stars, because the empirical f-Love relations connecting these quantities are distinct for the two classes of objects regardless of their equations of state. The hedge is that this method requires next-generation gravitational wave detectors and that the distinguishability is strongest for lower-mass strange stars (still above 1 solar mass), where the separation between the two f-Love relations is largest.

## Method
The authors computed the f-Love relation for strange stars by solving for the quadrupolar f-mode frequency in approximately 10^4 strange star configurations, using the quartic parametrization of the MIT bag model with parameters a4, B, and fixed strange quark mass ms = 100 MeV. They then simulated binary gravitational wave signals with a TaylorF2 waveform including 3.5 PN point particle phase, adiabatic tidal effects to 7.5 PN order, and quadrupolar f-mode dynamical tidal corrections, assuming a GW170817-like mass configuration (M1 = 1.475 solar masses, M2 = 1.26 solar masses) at 50 Mpc distance. Bayesian parameter estimation was performed using nested sampling (dynesty) in bilby_pipe, and the Bayes factor comparing the neutron star f-Love relation (from Pradhan 2023) against the strange star f-Love relation was computed to determine which class of object the data favored.

## Key numbers
- The f-Love relation for strange stars is given by a 6th-order polynomial fit: m*omega_2 = sum_{k=0}^{6} a_k [ln(Lambda_2)]^k, with coefficients a0 = 1.61e-1, a1 = -1.385e-2, a2 = -2.235e-2, a3 = 5.881e-3, a4 = -8.007e-4, a5 = -5.929e-5, a6 = -1.88e-6.
- For the simulated strange star binary with SQM3 EOS at 50 Mpc, the Bayes factor log10[mathcal{B}^{NS}_{SS}] indicated substantial evidence in favor of strange stars (between -1 and -1/2 on the Kass 1995 scale).
- For the stiffer SQM5 strange star EOS model, the Bayes factor showed strong evidence in favor of strange stars (log10[mathcal{B}^{NS}_{SS}] between -2 and -1).
- For neutron star equations of state tested as a control, log10[mathcal{B}^{NS}_{SS}] was always approximately 0.5, favoring the neutron star f-Love relation.
- The simulated binary had component masses M1 = 1.475 solar masses and M2 = 1.26 solar masses.

## Context
This work extends the f-Love universal relations first introduced by Chan (2014) and the neutron star f-Love relation developed by Pradhan (2023). It contradicts the assumption that strange stars and neutron stars remain degenerate in the mass range above 1 solar mass, as seen in mass-radius measurements of candidates like HESS J1731-347 (Doroshenko 2022). The methodology enables a single gravitational wave detection with next-generation detectors (Cosmic Explorer) to provide smoking gun evidence for strange stars, without requiring a series of precise measurements across multiple objects, and can be applied to each companion individually to identify hybrid neutron star-strange star binary systems.
