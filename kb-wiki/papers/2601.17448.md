# Basis Representation for Nuclear Densities from Principal Component Analysis

> We develop an efficient method to represent nuclear densities using basis functions extracted via Principal Component Analysis (PCA). Applying PCA to densities of 75 nuclei calculated with the relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory yields an orthogonal set of components that efficiently capture the dominant features of nuclear density distributions, which can be used as basis functions for nuclear density representation. The first five basis functions account for more than 99.999\% of the total variance, demonstrating the efficiency of these PCA basis functions. The PCA basis achieves significantly higher accuracy and faster convergence than the Fourier-Bessel and Sum-of-Gaussians methods for reconstructing both theoretical and experimental densities. This approach provides an efficient and robust representation of nuclear densities, offering a practical tool for experimental density representation and for theories where densities play a central role, such as the orbital-free density functional theory, or the double folding model for nuclear reactions.

## Key claim

The paper claims that PCA-derived basis functions, extracted from a training set of 75 RCHB proton densities, can represent nuclear densities with significantly higher accuracy and faster convergence than Fourier-Bessel or Sum-of-Gaussians methods, but this performance is conditional on the training set being representative and on applying a scaling normalization that maps each nucleus to the same root-mean-square radius and proton number as Ca-40.

## Method

The authors computed proton density distributions for 75 nuclei using relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory with the PC-PK1 functional under a spherical assumption. They applied a scaling procedure that normalizes each density to the root-mean-square radius (4.104 fm) and proton number (20) of Ca-40, then performed principal component analysis on the covariance matrix of these scaled densities to extract orthogonal basis functions (principal components). The PCA basis was compared to Fourier-Bessel (FB) expansion and Sum-of-Gaussians (SOG) fitting by reconstructing both the original RCHB densities and experimental proton densities from Wu (2025), evaluating accuracy via the relative integrated density difference and root-mean-square radius deviations.

## Key numbers

- The first five PCA basis functions account for more than 99.999% of the total variance in the training set of 75 nuclear densities.
- PC1 alone explains 98.60% of the total variance.
- With 5 fitting parameters, the PCA method achieved relative integrated density differences of 0.004 for Sr-88 and Pb-208, 0.009 for Zr-94, and 0.011 for Sm-154, compared to values of 0.084-0.265 for SOG and 0.129-0.207 for FB under the same parameter count.
- The density distributions were defined over 101 radial grid points covering r = 0 to 10 fm.
- The training set consisted of 75 nuclei, with the covariance matrix having dimensions 101 x 101.

## Context

This work extends the model-independent density representation methods of Dreher (1974) for Fourier-Bessel and Sick (1974) for Sum-of-Gaussians, which are standard in experimental charge density extraction from elastic electron scattering. The PCA approach provides a more compact and accurate basis for representing nuclear densities, enabling practical applications in orbital-free density functional theory as pursued by Wu (2022) and Colo (2023), and in double folding models for nuclear reactions. The method also offers a systematic alternative to the empirical parameterizations (modified harmonic-oscillator, two-parameter Fermi, three-parameter Fermi, three-parameter Gaussian) that have been used since the work of Hofstadter (1956).
