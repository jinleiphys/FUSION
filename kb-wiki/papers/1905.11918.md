# Time-dependent relaxation of observables in complex quantum systems

> We consider time-dependent relaxation of observables in quantum systems of chaotic and regular type. We show that the spread of the wave function in the Hilbert space is determined by the survival probability which is known to have pre-exponential, exponential, and long-term power-law limiting behaviors. This result relies on complexity of the wave functions and thus is generic to many systems. In the chaotic limit modeled by the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble we show that the survival probability obtained analytically also fully defines the relaxation timescale of observables. This is not the case in general, using realistic nuclear shell model and the quadrupole moment as an observable we demonstrate that the relaxation time is significantly longer than defined by the survival probability of the initial state. An example of the non-chaotic limit of coherent and squeezed states provides an additional illustration.

## Key claim

The paper shows that in the fully chaotic limit (Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble), the relaxation timescale of observables is fully determined by the survival probability of the initial state, but this is **not** generally true: using the realistic nuclear shell model for ²⁴Mg and the quadrupole moment as an observable, the relaxation time is significantly longer than that defined by the survival probability.

## Method

The authors analytically derive the time evolution of observables in the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) limit using the survival probability and the assumption of orthogonal invariance, obtaining closed-form expressions in terms of Bessel functions. They then perform numerical shell-model calculations for ²⁴Mg (twelve valence particles above ¹⁶O core) using the quadrupole moment as the observable, and compare the relaxation dynamics to the GOE predictions. Additionally, they study a bosonic model with random two-body interactions and the regular limit of coherent and squeezed harmonic oscillator states.

## Key numbers

- GOE matrix dimension used in numerical examples: 10⁴
- For the GOE, the survival probability is given by \( f(\tau) = \frac{1}{\tau} J_1(2\tau) \), where \(\tau = \lambda t\) and \(\lambda\) is the spectral width
- The number of principal components in the GOE limit approaches \( \mathcal{N}_{\text{pc}}/\mathcal{N} = 0.5 \) at large times for complex basis states, and \( \mathcal{N}/3 \) for real basis states
- The asymptotic power-law decay of the survival probability for large times in the GOE: \( |f(\tau)|^2 \simeq 1/(\pi \tau^3) \)
- For the bosonic model: six spinless bosons occupying eleven single-particle levels, with \( \mathcal{N} = 8008 \) many-body states
- The short-time survival probability for the bosonic model follows \( |F(\tau)|^2 = \exp(-\tau^2) \)
- The roots of the Bessel function where \( f(\tau) = 0 \) occur at \( \tau = 1.91585,\ 3.50779,\ 5.08673, \dots \)

## Context

This work extends the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis (ETH) framework to non-stationary dynamics, building on prior studies of quantum chaos by Porter and Brody, and on the concept of fidelity and Loschmidt echo reviewed by Gorin et al. (2006). The paper connects to the broader literature on quantum thermalization in Hilbert space (Mori et al., 2018) and to the universal bound on chaos proposed by Maldacena et al. (2015). The results enable a quantitative understanding of how the survival probability alone governs relaxation in fully chaotic systems, while highlighting the need for more detailed modeling in realistic nuclear systems where the mean-field basis breaks orthogonal invariance.
