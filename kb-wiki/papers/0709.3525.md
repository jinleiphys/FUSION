# Parameterization of the Woods-Saxon Potential for Shell-Model Calculations

> The drastically expanded use of the Woods-Saxon potential in modern day nuclear physics and the availability of new nuclear data motivated us to review and optimize the parameters of this potential to the experimental single-nucleon spectra around the doubly-magic nuclei between $^{16}$O and $^{208}$Pb. We obtain a parameterization which is applicable over the whole nuclear chart for nuclides between $^{16}$O and the heaviest elements. Apart from Coulomb components the obtained parameter set is isospin symmetric. We demonstrate that the potential provides a good description of the nuclear mean field leading to quality single-particle spectra, nuclear radii, prediction of drip-lines, shell closures and other properties. Thus presented Woods-Saxon fit provides adequate single-particle basis for shell model calculations bridging over into the continuum.

## Key claim

The authors obtain a parameterization of the Woods-Saxon potential that is applicable over the whole nuclear chart for nuclides between $^{16}$O and the heaviest elements, but the fit is based on experimental single-nucleon spectra only around doubly-magic nuclei between $^{16}$O and $^{208}$Pb, and the parameter set is isospin symmetric apart from Coulomb components.

## Method

The authors extracted experimental single-particle and single-hole binding energies from the NNDC database for nuclei around the doubly-magic cores $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca, $^{48}$Ca, $^{56}$Ni, $^{100}$Sn, $^{132}$Sn and $^{208}$Pb, using spectroscopic factors to compute weighted centroid energies where fragmentation was observed. They then performed a least-squares fit of the Woods-Saxon Hamiltonian parameters, central depth, radius, diffuseness, spin-orbit strength, radius, and diffuseness, to these experimental energies, using a reduced mass in the kinetic energy operator and an isospin-dependent central potential following the Lane prescription.

## Key numbers

- The central potential depth is parameterized as $V = V_0 \left(1 - \frac{4\kappa}{A} \langle \mathbf{t} \cdot \mathbf{T}' \rangle \right)$, with the isospin term evaluated explicitly for each $N$ and $Z$ combination.
- The radius parameters are defined as $R = R_C = R_0 A^{1/3}$ and $R_{SO} = R_{0,SO} A^{1/3}$, with $R_0$ and $R_{0,SO}$ constant across the nuclear chart.
- The surface diffuseness for both central and spin-orbit potentials is assumed constant and size independent: $a = a_{SO} = \text{const}$.
- The spin-orbit strength is given by $\tilde{V} = \lambda V_0$, with $\lambda$ a constant.
- For $^{17}$O, the $1d_{3/2}$ neutron resonance at 5.085 MeV excitation energy has a neutron width of 96 keV and spectroscopic factor 0.689; the weighted single-particle energy adopted is 1.710 MeV (unbound).
- For $^{17}$F, the $1d_{3/2}$ proton resonance at 5.000 MeV excitation energy has a proton width of 1530 keV and spectroscopic factor 0.845; the weighted single-particle energy adopted is 4.688 MeV (unbound).
- The $1d_{3/2}$ neutron hole in $^{40}$Ca has binding energy -15.64 MeV; the $1f_{7/2}$ neutron particle in $^{41}$Ca has binding energy -8.36 MeV.
- The $1g_{9/2}$ neutron orbital in $^{100}$Sn has binding energy -17.93(30) MeV; the $1g_{9/2}$ proton orbital in $^{100}$Sn has binding energy -2.92(20) MeV.
- The $2f_{7/2}$ neutron particle in $^{133}$Sn has binding energy -2.47 MeV; the $1g_{7/2}$ proton particle in $^{133}$Sb has binding energy -9.68 MeV.
- The $3p_{1/2}$ neutron hole in $^{207}$Pb has binding energy -7.37 MeV; the $1h_{9/2}$ proton particle in $^{209}$Bi has binding energy -3.80 MeV.

## Context

This work extends and updates previous Woods-Saxon parameterizations, most notably the "Universal" parameterization of Dudek (1982), which was adjusted to $^{208}$Pb but had shortcomings for lighter nuclei and predicted charge radii inconsistent with experiment (Dudek, 1995). The new "Seminole" parameterization is isospin-symmetric, uses a reduced mass for correct kinematics in light nuclei, and is intended to provide an adequate single-particle basis for shell-model calculations that bridge into the continuum, enabling studies of nuclei far from stability and nuclear reaction physics.
