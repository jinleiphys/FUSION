# Warm stellar matter with neutrino trapping

> The properties of hybrid stars formed by hadronic and quark matter in beta-equilibrium at fixed entropies are described by appropriate equations of state (EOS) in the framework of relativistic mean-field theory. In this work we include the possibility of neutrino trapped EOS and compare the star properties with the ones obtained after deleptonization, when neutrinos have already diffused out. We use the nonlinear Walecka model for the hadron matter with two different sets for the hyperon couplings and the MIT Bag and the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio models for the quark matter. The phase transition to a deconfined quark phase is investigated. Depending on the model and the parameter set used, the mixed phase may or may not exist in the EOS at high densities. The star properties are calculated for each equation of state. The maximum mass stellar configurations obtained within the NJL have larger masses than the ones obtained within the Bag model. The Bag model predicts a mixed phase in the interior of the most massive stable stars while, depending on the hyperon couplings, the NJL model predicts a mixed phase or pure quark matter. Comparing with neutrino free stars, the maximum allowed baryonic masses for protoneutron stars are $\sim 0.4 M_\odot$ larger for the Bag model and $\sim 0.1 M_\odot$ larger for the NJL model when neutrino trapping is imposed.

# Warm stellar matter with neutrino trapping

## Key claim

The maximum baryonic masses of protoneutron stars with neutrino trapping are larger than those of neutrino-free stars by approximately 0.4 solar masses for the Bag model and approximately 0.1 solar masses for the NJL model, but these differences depend on the quark model used and the hyperon coupling set chosen, and the most massive neutrino-trapped stars become unstable after cooling and deleptonization.

## Method

The authors constructed equations of state for hybrid stars containing hadronic matter, a mixed phase, and quark matter, using the nonlinear Walecka model for hadrons with two different hyperon coupling sets, and both the MIT Bag model and the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model for quark matter. They imposed beta equilibrium with and without neutrino trapping (fixed lepton fraction Y_L = 0.4) at fixed entropies S = 0, 1, and 2, and solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain stellar mass and composition profiles.

## Key numbers

- Maximum gravitational mass for NJL model with neutrino trapping: approximately 2.0 solar masses; without trapping: approximately 0.15 solar masses lower.
- Maximum gravitational mass for Bag model with neutrino trapping: approximately 1.9 solar masses; without trapping: approximately 0.4 solar masses lower.
- Maximum baryonic mass difference between neutrino-trapped and neutrino-free stars: approximately 0.4 solar masses for the Bag model, approximately 0.1 solar masses for the NJL model.
- Saturation density parameters used: rho_0 = 0.153 fm^{-3}, binding energy -16.3 MeV, symmetry coefficient 32.5 MeV, compression modulus 300 MeV, effective mass 0.7 M.
- Bag constant: B^{1/4} = 190 MeV.
- NJL model parameters: cutoff Lambda = 631.4 MeV, g_S Lambda^2 = 1.824, g_D Lambda^5 = -9.4, m_u = m_d = 5.6 MeV, m_s = 135.6 MeV.
- At 10 rho_0, strangeness fraction for NJL model: 0.25 (neutrino free), 0.18 (with trapping); for Bag model: 0.31 (neutrino free), 0.27 (with trapping).

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Menezes and Providencia (2003) on hybrid star properties at different temperatures, now incorporating neutrino trapping relevant for protoneutron stars during the first 10-20 seconds after core collapse. The results are compared with earlier work by Prakash et al. (1997) on protoneutron star evolution, and the authors find systematically larger masses for the Bag model than those reported by Prakash et al. (1997) who used a harder bag constant of 197 MeV. The findings enable predictions about the stability of protoneutron stars after deleptonization and suggest that the most massive neutrino-trapped stars may decay into low-mass black holes, as previously discussed by Prakash et al. (1997) and Vidana et al. (2002).
