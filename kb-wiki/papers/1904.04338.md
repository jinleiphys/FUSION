# Toward convergence of effective field theory simulations on digital quantum computers

> We report results for simulating an effective field theory to compute the binding energy of the deuteron nucleus using a hybrid algorithm on a trapped-ion quantum computer. Two increasingly complex unitary coupled-cluster ansaetze have been used to compute the binding energy to within a few percent for successively more complex Hamiltonians. By increasing the complexity of the Hamiltonian, allowing more terms in the effective field theory expansion and calculating their expectation values, we present a benchmark for quantum computers based on their ability to scalably calculate the effective field theory with increasing accuracy. Our result of $E_4=-2.220 \pm 0.179$MeV may be compared with the exact Deuteron ground-state energy $-2.224$MeV. We also demonstrate an error mitigation technique using Richardson extrapolation on ion traps for the first time. The error mitigation circuit represents a record for deepest quantum circuit on a trapped-ion quantum computer.

## Key claim
The paper reports that a trapped-ion quantum computer can compute the deuteron binding energy using an effective field theory (EFT) simulation with a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm, achieving results within error bars of the exact value. The key hedge is that the four-qubit result of -2.220 ± 0.179 MeV is statistically consistent with the exact deuteron ground-state energy of -2.224 MeV, but only after applying Richardson extrapolation error mitigation, and the authors note that experimental results deviate more from exact UCCS results as ansatz size increases due to error accumulation.

## Method
The authors implemented a unitary coupled-cluster singles (UCCS) ansatz on a trapped-ion quantum computer using 5 or 7 ytterbium-171 ions, with qubits encoded in hyperfine-split ground states. They applied Jordan-Wigner transformation to map the N-oscillator-basis deuteron Hamiltonian to qubit Hamiltonians for N=2, 3, and 4, then used a re-parameterization in hyperspherical coordinates to construct nearest-neighbor-compatible quantum circuits. Error mitigation was performed using Richardson extrapolation by replacing each XX(θ) gate with XX(θ) followed by M repetitions of identity operations [XX(-θ)XX(θ)]^M for M=0,1,2,3, then linearly extrapolating to the zero-noise limit.

## Key numbers
- For the three-qubit ansatz (H3), the zero-noise-limit extrapolated binding energy was -2.030 ± 0.034 MeV, compared to the theoretically expected value of -2.046 MeV.
- For the four-qubit ansatz (H4), the zero-noise-limit extrapolated binding energy was -2.220 ± 0.179 MeV, compared to the theoretically expected value of -2.143 MeV and the exact deuteron ground-state energy of -2.224 MeV.
- The largest circuit run for the four-qubit experiment involved implementing 35 two-qubit XX gates.
- Single-qubit rotation fidelities were approximately 99.5% per ion; XX gate fidelities were 99.3%, 97.7%, and 99.0% on ion pairs (1,3), (1,5), and (3,5) respectively for the three-qubit experiment.
- The average three-qubit state readout fidelity was 0.978; the average four-qubit readout fidelity was 96.3%.
- The T2 coherence time with idle qubits was measured to be 1.5(5) seconds.

## Context
This work extends the deuteron EFT simulation previously performed by Dumitrescu (2018) on superconducting quantum computers (IBM QX5 and Rigetti 19Q), improving the three-qubit result from 3% error on IBM QX5 to 0.7% error on the trapped-ion system. The paper establishes a benchmark for hybrid quantum-classical computing performance scaling as problem complexity increases, and the nearest-neighbor connectivity requirement enables implementation across any quantum computing platform. The results enable future work on scaling EFT simulations to larger basis sizes and benchmarking upcoming quantum information processors.
