# Goodness of Generalized Seniority in Semi-magic Nuclei

> Symmetry plays an important role in understanding the nuclear structure properties from the rotation of a nucleus to the spin, parity and isospin of nuclear states. This simplifies the complexity of the nuclear problems in one way or the other. Seniority is also a well known quantum number which arises due to the symmetry in the pairing interaction of nuclei. We present empirical as well as theoretical evidences based on decay rates which support the goodness of seniority at higher spins as well as in nrich or, n-deficient nuclei. We find that the generalized seniority governs the identical trends of high-spin isomers in different semi-magic chains, where different set of nucleon orbitals from different valence spaces are involved.

## Key claim

Generalized seniority remains a reasonably good quantum number for high-spin isomers in semi-magic nuclei (Z=50 isotopes, N=82 isotones, and Z=82 isotopes), even though the active valence spaces and intruder orbitals differ across these chains. The claim is hedged by noting that the goodness of seniority is demonstrated for a specific set of states (the high-spin isomers) and that configuration mixing is essential to describe the B(E2) trends in Sn isotopes, whereas the N=82 chain shows a dominance of the pure seniority scheme.

## Method

The authors applied the generalized seniority formalism, based on a quasi-spin scheme for degenerate multi-j orbitals, to calculate B(E2) transition probabilities for high-spin isomers in three semi-magic chains. They used the seniority reduction formula for Δv=0 transitions, fitting proportionality constants to measured B(E2) values at specific nuclei (e.g., ¹¹⁹Sn for odd-A Sn isomers, ¹⁴⁶Dy for N=82 even-A isomers, ¹⁹⁸Pb for Z=82 even-A isomers) and then comparing the calculated parabolic trends with experimental data across the chains. The active valence spaces were defined as h₁₁/₂, d₃/₂, s₁/₂ for the Z=50 and N=82 chains (with Z=64 as core for N=82), and i₁₃/₂, f₇/₂, p₃/₂ for the Z=82 chain.

## Key numbers

- For the Z=50 isotopes, the 10⁺ and 27/2⁻ isomers exhibit half-life maxima at neutron numbers 72 and 73 respectively, where the h₁₁/₂ neutron orbital becomes half-filled.
- For the N=82 isotones, the 10⁺ and 27/2⁻ isomers exhibit half-life maxima at Z=70 and 71 respectively, where the h₁₁/₂ proton orbital becomes half-filled.
- The ratio R(15:2) = ΔE(15/2⁻ → 11/2⁻) / ΔE(2⁺ → 0⁺) is ~1 for ¹¹⁴–¹²⁵Sn isotopes (e.g., 0.99 for ¹¹⁶Sn/¹¹⁷Sn, 0.96 for ¹²⁴Sn/¹²⁵Sn).
- The ratio R(31:12) = ΔE(31/2⁻ → 27/2⁻) / ΔE(12⁺ → 10⁺) is ~1 for ¹¹⁸–¹²⁵Sn isotopes (e.g., 0.95 for ¹¹⁸Sn/¹¹⁹Sn, 0.88 for ¹²⁴Sn/¹²⁵Sn).
- For the N=82 isotones, R(15:2) is ~1 (e.g., 0.93 for ¹⁴⁸Dy/¹⁴⁹Ho, 0.94 for ¹⁵⁰Er/¹⁵¹Tm).
- For the Z=82 isotopes, the ratio R(17:2) = ΔE(17/2⁺ → 13/2⁺) / ΔE(2⁺ → 0⁺) is ~1 for ¹⁹⁰–¹⁹⁷Pb (e.g., 1.056 for ¹⁹⁰Pb/¹⁹¹Pb, 0.959 for ¹⁹⁶Pb/¹⁹⁷Pb).
- In the generalized seniority calculations for the N=82 chain, Ω=6 gave the best fit to the B(E2) data for the 10⁺ isomers, while Ω=9 gave the best fit for the Z=50 chain.
- For the odd-A Sn 27/2⁻ isomers, Ω=9 (mixing of h₁₁/₂, d₃/₂, s₁/₂) gave the best fit to experimental B(E2) data.
- For the odd-A N=82 27/2⁻ isomers, Ω=6 gave the best fit, indicating dominance of the h₁₁/₂ orbital.
- For the odd-A Pb 33/2⁺ isomers, Ω=13 (mixing of i₁₃/₂, f₇/₂, p₃/₂) gave the best fit.

## Context

This work extends the generalized seniority formalism previously applied to even-A Sn isotopes (Maheshwari 2016) to odd-A Sn isotopes and to the N=82 and Z=82 semi-magic chains. It builds on the identification of seniority isomers in Sn isotopes by Daly (1980, 1986), Fogelberg (1981), and others, and on the characterization of 10⁺ and 27/2⁻ isomers in N=82 isotones by McNeill (1989) and Astier (2012). The results enable predictions of high-seniority v=4 isomers in the N=82 and Z=82 chains, where none are currently known, and suggest that the generalized seniority scheme can be used to estimate half-lives and predict new isomers in unknown cases.
