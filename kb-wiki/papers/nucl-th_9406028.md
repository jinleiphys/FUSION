# Asymmetric nuclear matter and neutron star properties

> We calculate properties of neutron stars such as mass and radius using a relativistic Dirac-Brueckner-hartree-Fock apprach for asymmetric nuclear matter. For pure neutron matter we find the maximum mass to be 2.4 solar masses with a radius of 12 km. For a proton fraction of 30% we find a max mass of 2.1 solar masses and a radius of 10.5 km. The implications are discussed.

## Key claim
The authors calculate neutron star masses and radii using a relativistic Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach for asymmetric nuclear matter, finding that the equation of state for pure neutron matter is too stiff to match observed maximum masses of approximately 1.4 to 1.9 solar masses, but that increased proton fractions (e.g., from kaon or pion condensation) soften the equation of state and reduce maximum masses.

## Method
The authors used the Bonn A meson-exchange potential to evaluate the relativistic G-matrix for asymmetric nuclear matter, treating the Pauli operator exactly rather than extrapolating from symmetric matter and pure neutron matter. They solved the Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock equations self-consistently to obtain single-particle energies and the energy per particle for various proton fractions, then used the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equation to compute neutron star masses and radii from the resulting equation of state.

## Key numbers
- Maximum mass for pure neutron matter: 2.4 solar masses with a radius of 12 km
- Maximum mass for a proton fraction of 30%: 2.1 solar masses with a radius of 10.5 km
- Maximum mass for a softened equation of state (e.g., with condensates): approximately 2.0 solar masses with a radius of approximately 10 km
- The equation of state is valid in a density range from 0.1 fm^{-3} to 0.8 fm^{-3}

## Context
This work extends the Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach of Brockmann and Machleidt (1990) to asymmetric nuclear matter with an exact treatment of the Pauli operator, differing from the extrapolation method used by Huber et al. (1993). The results show that increased proton fractions (as might occur from kaon or pion condensation, as discussed by Brown (1993) and others) soften the equation of state, bringing maximum masses closer to the observed range of 1.4 to 1.9 solar masses cited by Thorsett (1993). The authors note that higher-order many-body contributions, as studied by Jiang et al. (1993) for symmetric matter, could further soften the equation of state.
