# Model-based quantitative methods to predict irradiation-induced swelling in alloys

> Predicting volume swelling of structural materials in nuclear reactors under high-dose neutron irradiations based on existing low-dose experiments or irradiation data with high-dose-rate energetic particles has been a long-standing challenge for safety evaluation and rapidly screening irradiation-resistant materials in nuclear energy systems. Here, we build an Additional Defect Absorption Model that describes the irradiation-induced swelling effects produced by energetic electrons, heavy-ions, and neutrons by considering additional defect sinks inherent in the irradiation process. Based on this model, we establish quantitative methods to predict high-dose swelling from low-dose behavior and obtain the equivalent irradiation dose for different energetic particles when the dose rates differ by several orders of magnitude. Furthermore, we propose a universal parameter to characterize the swelling resistance of various alloys and predict their radiation tolerances under different radiation conditions. This work provides quantitative prediction methods for evaluating irradiation-induced swelling effects of structural materials, which is critical to the safety and material development for advanced nuclear reactors.

## Key claim
The paper claims that the Additional Defect Absorption Model (ADAM) can quantitatively predict high-dose volume swelling from low-dose behavior and establish equivalent irradiation doses between different energetic particles, but only for the same material at the same elevated temperature or within a narrow temperature window, and the model parameters must be fitted from existing experimental data.

## Method
The authors developed the Additional Defect Absorption Model (ADAM) by introducing an extra sink term into classical rate theory to account for irradiation-generated defect sinks that capture mobile point defects at high dose rates. They first validated the model against electron irradiation data from HVEM experiments, then extended it to heavy-ion and neutron irradiations by treating the in-cascade displacement process as one stage of defect evolution. The model was fitted to experimental swelling data for various alloys (316L, 304L, 316SS, D9, HT9, T91, F82H, Fe-Cr-Ni alloys) under neutron, heavy-ion, and electron irradiations at different dose rates and temperatures.

## Key numbers
- For electron irradiation of 316L at 530 °C, the model fits swelling data with a threshold dose Δ₀ that increases linearly with dose rate G_dpa
- For Fe-15Cr-16Ni-0.25Ti, the model predicts equivalent swelling between neutron irradiation at 7.8×10⁻⁷ dpa/s and 1.7×10⁻⁶ dpa/s, with predicted data lying within experimental scatter
- For T91 steel, the model predicts equivalent swelling between self-ion irradiation at 1×10⁻³ dpa/s and neutron irradiation at 3×10⁻⁶ dpa/s, with predictions agreeing reasonably with available neutron data
- Heavy-ion dose rates are 10³-10⁴ times higher than neutron dose rates, while electron HVEM dose rates are 10⁻²-10⁻³ dpa/s
- Neutron irradiation-induced stable dislocation loop density saturates at around 2-3 dpa

## Context
This work extends the rate theory approach of Brailsford and Mansur (1970s) and the production bias model of Golubov and Singh (1997-2012) by providing a simpler, application-oriented model for complex alloys. It addresses the long-standing challenge of predicting neutron irradiation swelling from high-dose-rate heavy-ion experiments, which Was et al. (2002, 2014) had previously applied to low-dose proton-neutron comparisons. The model enables rapid screening of irradiation-resistant materials for advanced nuclear reactors and lifetime extension of current reactors, and provides a quantitative basis for comparing electron, heavy-ion, and neutron irradiation effects.
