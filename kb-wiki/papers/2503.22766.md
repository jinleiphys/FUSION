# Crossover between the zeptosecond and attosecond physics

> Nuclear orbiting resonances have been revealed at the sub-barrier energies as an atomic phenomenon by means of x-ray spectroscopy experiments. This interpretation is supported by several phenomenological models and theoretical estimates of the nuclear orbiting timescale and cross-section, inelastic scattering cross section including both nuclear and Coulomb excitation, and the Wigner-Smith time delay. We demonstrate that a multi-photon exchange during nuclear orbiting is responsible for an atomic excitation. Furthermore, proximity of the projectile and target nucleus during the nuclear orbiting modifies the effective charge of the projectile. Even though this orbiting induced excitation is triggered in zeptoseconds, it can still be observed in the attosecond time scale because of the Wigner-Smith time delay inherent to autoionization. Thus, we demonstrate the crossover between the zeptosecond and attosecond time scales which are native to nuclear and atomic physics, respectively. Markedly, this crossover may be the reason for x-ray production from ultra short nuclear processes ($\leq 10^{-21}$ sec). This explanation is likely to resolve the fission time scale anomaly and can stimulate cross-disciplinary research ranging from solid state to high-energy physics.

# Crossover between the zeptosecond and attosecond physics

## Key claim
Nuclear orbiting resonances at sub-barrier energies, previously interpreted as a nuclear phenomenon, are actually an atomic phenomenon observable via x-ray spectroscopy because the Wigner-Smith time delay inherent to autoionization allows a process triggered in zeptoseconds to be observed on the attosecond time scale, thereby demonstrating a crossover between nuclear and atomic time scales.

## Method
The authors performed coupled-channel scattering calculations using the CCFULL-SC code to compute inelastic scattering cross sections for three systems (12C+56Fe, 12C+58Ni, 12C+63Cu), and calculated nuclear orbiting cross sections using the prescription of Korsch (1983) with Woods-Saxon potentials. They determined the lifetime of dinuclear orbiting complexes from measured x-ray energy jumps using multi-configuration Dirac-Fock calculations (GRASP2K) to obtain characteristic atomic times, and compared these with lifetimes from the one-body dissipation HICOL model.

## Key numbers
- Resonance energies (Bres) for the three systems: 21.17 MeV (12C+56Fe), 22.97 MeV (12C+58Ni), 22.88 MeV (12C+63Cu)
- Orbital angular momenta of the dinuclear complexes: 12 hbar for 12C+56Fe and 12C+58Ni, 11 hbar for 12C+63Cu
- Orbiting radii: 9.02 fm (12C+56Fe), 9.06 fm (12C+58Ni), 9.23 fm (12C+63Cu), all approximately 0.2 fm smaller than the fusion barrier radii
- Measured x-ray energy jumps at resonance: 0.025 keV (12C+56Fe), 0.032 keV (12C+58Ni), 0.035 keV (12C+63Cu)
- Dinuclear complex lifetimes from the present model (tm): 3.0 zs (12C+56Fe), 3.1 zs (12C+58Ni), 3.0 zs (12C+63Cu)
- Dinuclear complex lifetimes from HICOL model (th): 1.3 zs (12C+56Fe), 1.2 zs (12C+58Ni), 1.2 zs (12C+63Cu)
- Characteristic atomic times (t0): 0.383 as (12C+56Fe), 0.328 as (12C+58Ni), 0.306 as (12C+63Cu)
- Interaction barrier energies (Bint): 17.43 MeV (12C+56Fe), 19.48 MeV (12C+58Ni), 20.05 MeV (12C+63Cu)
- Fusion barrier energies (Bfus): 22.68 MeV (12C+56Fe), 24.37 MeV (12C+58Ni), 24.89 MeV (12C+63Cu)

## Context
This work extends the discovery by Sharma and Nandi (2017) of resonance-like structures in x-ray spectra near the fusion barrier energy, which were previously attributed to shake-off ionization from sudden nuclear recoil. The authors resolve shortcomings of that interpretation by identifying nuclear orbiting resonances (dinuclear complexes) as the underlying mechanism, building on earlier observations of anomalous large-angle elastic scattering in light-heavy ion systems by Braun and collaborators. The crossover between zeptosecond nuclear processes and attosecond atomic observation via Wigner-Smith time delay (Wigner 1955, Smith 1960) may resolve the fission time scale anomaly and enable cross-disciplinary research connecting solid-state physics to high-energy physics.
