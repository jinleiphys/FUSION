# Coefficients and terms of the liquid drop model and mass formula

> The coefficients of different combinations of terms of the liquid drop model have been determined by a least square fitting procedure to the experimental atomic masses. The nuclear masses can also be reproduced using a Coulomb radius taking into account the increase of the ratio $R\_0/A^{1/3}$ with increasing mass, the fitted surface energy coefficient remaining around 18 MeV.

# Coefficients and terms of the liquid drop model and mass formula

## Key claim
The nuclear masses of 1522 nuclei can be reproduced with a root-mean-square deviation as low as 0.58 MeV using a liquid-drop-type expansion that includes volume, surface, Coulomb, proton form-factor, and Wigner energy terms, provided that theoretical shell and pairing energies are also included. The surface energy coefficient remains around 18 MeV even when a more realistic Coulomb radius formula (which accounts for the increase of R₀/A¹/³ with mass) is used without an adjustable Coulomb parameter.

## Method
The authors performed least-squares fits of various combinations of liquid-drop-model terms to the experimental atomic masses of 1522 nuclei (with N and Z > 7 and mass uncertainty < 100 keV) from Audi (2003). They used theoretical shell energies from the Thomas-Fermi model of Myers and Swiatecki (1994, 1996) and a pairing energy term with a fixed coefficient aₚ = 11 MeV. The Coulomb energy was calculated either with the standard radius formula R₀ = r₀A¹/³ (with r₀ as a free parameter) or with a fixed effective sharp radius formula R₀ = 1.28A¹/³ − 0.76 + 0.8A⁻¹/³, and the quality of fit was compared using the root-mean-square deviation σ.

## Key numbers
- Best fit using R₀ = r₀A¹/³ with volume, surface, Coulomb, proton form-factor, Wigner, pairing, and shell terms: σ = 0.58 MeV (with aᵥ = 15.5424 MeV, aₛ = 18.0625 MeV, r₀ = 1.2046 fm, fₚ = −1.40745, no curvature or constant term).
- Best fit using the fixed Coulomb radius formula R₀ = 1.28A¹/³ − 0.76 + 0.8A⁻¹/³ with the same term set: σ = 0.60 MeV (with aᵥ = 15.6089 MeV, aₛ = 17.9021 MeV, kₛ = 2.4111, fₚ = −1.69912, W = 32.1647 MeV).
- Bethe-Weizsäcker formula fit (volume, surface, Coulomb with Z(Z−1), asymmetry, pairing, shell): σ = 1.17 MeV (aᵥ = 15.7827 MeV, aₛ = 17.9042 MeV, r₀ = 1.193 fm, kᵥ = 1.505).
- The difference between theoretical and experimental masses never exceeds 2.25 MeV and is less than 1.15 MeV for A > 100.
- The effective sharp radius formula gives r₀ = 1.13 fm for ⁴⁸Ca and r₀ = 1.18 fm for ²⁴⁸Cm.
- The shell energy contribution to the binding energy of ²⁰⁸Pb is 12.84 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula (Weizsäcker 1935, Bethe 1936) and the finite-range liquid drop model of Moller (1995) by systematically testing which combinations of higher-order terms (curvature, constant, proton form-factor, Wigner) improve the fit to experimental masses. The authors show that the proton form-factor correction and Wigner term are essential for reaching σ ≈ 0.6 MeV, while curvature and constant terms do not provide stable improvements. The results support the Generalized Liquid Drop Model used by Royer and collaborators (1984, 1985, 2000, 2001, 2002, 2003) for fission, fusion, and decay barriers, and enable more reliable mass predictions for superheavy nuclei and nuclei near drip lines.
