# An Evidence of Mass Dependent Differential Kinetic Freeze-out Scenario Observed in Pb-Pb Collisions at 2.76 TeV

> Transverse momentum spectra of different particles produced in mid-rapidity interval in lead-lead (Pb-Pb) collisions with different centrality intervals, measured by the ALICE Collaboration at center-of-mass energy per nucleon pair $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76$ TeV, are conformably and approximately described by the Tsallis distribution. The dependences of parameters (effective temperature, entropy index, and normalization factor) on event centrality and particle rest mass are obtained. The source temperature at the kinetic freeze-out is obtained to be the intercept in the linear relation between effective temperature and particle rest mass, while the particle transverse flow velocity is approximately extracted to be the slope in the linear relation between mean transverse momentum and particle rest mass. It is shown that the source temperature increases with increase of particle rest mass, which exhibits an evidence of mass dependent differential kinetic freeze-out scenario or multiple kinetic freeze-out scenario.

# An Evidence of Mass Dependent Differential Kinetic Freeze-out Scenario Observed in Pb-Pb Collisions at 2.76 TeV

## Key claim

The kinetic freeze-out temperature extracted from the linear relation between effective temperature and particle rest mass increases with increasing particle rest mass, providing evidence for a mass dependent differential (multiple) kinetic freeze-out scenario rather than a single or double freeze-out scenario. The claim is hedged by the authors' acknowledgment that the intercept values have large errors and that the method is indirect, with different fitting choices (including or excluding deuterons and helium-3) yielding different freeze-out temperatures.

## Method

The authors fitted the Tsallis distribution to ALICE Collaboration data for transverse momentum spectra of π⁺, K⁺, p, d, and ³He produced in mid-rapidity (|y| < 0.5) in Pb-Pb collisions at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV for centrality interval 0–20%, as well as deuteron spectra across multiple centrality intervals and ³He spectra for two centrality intervals. The effective temperature T, entropy index q, and normalization factor N₀ were extracted via least-squares fitting. The kinetic freeze-out temperature was obtained as the intercept in a linear fit of effective temperature versus particle rest mass, while the transverse flow velocity was extracted as the slope in a linear fit of mean transverse momentum versus mean moving mass, using Monte Carlo calculations in the source rest frame.

## Key numbers

- Effective temperature T for π⁺ in 0–20% centrality: 0.134 ± 0.012 GeV (q = 1.0980 ± 0.0100)
- Effective temperature T for K⁺ in 0–20% centrality: 0.271 ± 0.020 GeV (q = 1.0210 ± 0.0060)
- Effective temperature T for p in 0–20% centrality: 0.411 ± 0.033 GeV (q = 1.0010 ± 0.0009)
- Effective temperature T for d in 0–20% centrality: 0.645 ± 0.068 GeV (q = 1.0010 ± 0.0008)
- Effective temperature T for ³He in 0–20% centrality: 0.781 ± 0.085 GeV (q = 1.0013 ± 0.0009)
- Kinetic freeze-out temperature (intercept) from linear fit including π⁺, K⁺, and p: 0.091 ± 0.009 GeV
- Kinetic freeze-out temperature (intercept) including π⁺, K⁺, p, and d: 0.115 ± 0.017 GeV
- Kinetic freeze-out temperature (intercept) including π⁺, K⁺, p, d, and ³He: 0.148 ± 0.032 GeV
- Transverse flow velocity (slope) from ⟨p_T⟩ vs. ⟨m⟩ including π⁺, K⁺, and p: 0.502 ± 0.004 (dimensionless, close to 0.5c)
- Transverse flow velocity including π⁺, K⁺, p, and d: 0.490 ± 0.003
- Transverse flow velocity including π⁺, K⁺, p, d, and ³He: 0.468 ± 0.009
- Effective temperature for d in 0–10% centrality: 0.659 ± 0.039 GeV
- Effective temperature for d in 60–80% centrality: 0.345 ± 0.021 GeV

## Context

This work extends the study of kinetic freeze-out scenarios in heavy-ion collisions, building on the single freeze-out scenario (one set of parameters for all particles) and double freeze-out scenario (separate parameters for strange and non-strange particles) discussed in prior literature. The authors specifically reference the multi-kinetic freeze-out scenario of earlier work (2015) and compare their extracted freeze-out temperatures with those from the blast-wave model applied by the ALICE Collaboration (2015) to the same data, noting that the blast-wave model gives lower freeze-out temperatures in central collisions than peripheral collisions, opposite to their finding. The results enable further investigation of whether the mass-dependent differential freeze-out scenario is universal across collision systems and energies, and suggest that the Tsallis distribution provides an alternative to the blast-wave model for extracting freeze-out parameters.
