# Influence of spin polarizability on liquid gas phase transition in the nuclear matter

> In this paper, we investigate the liquid gas phase transition for the spin polarized nuclear matter. Applying the lowest order constrained variational (LOCV) method, and using two microscopic potentials, $AV_{18}$ and $UV_{14}$+TNI, we calculate the free energy, equation of state, order parameter, entropy, heat capacity and compressibility to derive the critical properties of spin polarized nuclear matter. Our results indicate that for the spin polarized nuclear matter, the second order phase transition takes place at lower temperatures with respect to the unpolarized one. It is also shown that the critical temperature of our spin polarized nuclear matter with a specific value of spin polarization parameter is in good agreement with the experimental result.

## Key claim
For spin-polarized symmetric nuclear matter, the second-order liquid-gas phase transition occurs at lower temperatures than for unpolarized matter, and the critical temperature is a symmetric function of the spin polarization parameter; the critical temperature of polarized matter with a specific spin polarization parameter can match the experimental value of about 16.6 MeV.

## Method
The authors used the lowest order constrained variational (LOCV) method at finite temperature to compute the free energy, equation of state, and other thermodynamic properties of symmetric nuclear matter in unpolarized, ferromagnetic, and antiferromagnetic spin states. They employed two microscopic nucleon-nucleon potentials, AV18 and UV14+TNI, and applied the Maxwell construction to determine the liquid-gas coexistence curves and critical points. The single-particle energies were approximated using a quadratic form with momentum-independent effective masses treated as variational parameters.

## Key numbers
- For the UV14+TNI potential, the critical temperature for unpolarized matter is 18.0 MeV; for ferromagnetic matter (δp=±0.5) it is 15.1 MeV; for antiferromagnetic matter (δp=±0.5) it is 14.1 MeV.
- For the AV18 potential, the critical temperature for ferromagnetic matter (δp=±0.5) is 15.0 MeV; for antiferromagnetic matter (δp=±0.5) it is 14.0 MeV.
- For ferromagnetic nuclear matter with UV14+TNI, the critical temperature equals the experimental value of 16.6 MeV at δp=±0.31; with AV18, this occurs at δp=±0.44.
- For antiferromagnetic nuclear matter with UV14+TNI, the critical temperature equals 16.6 MeV at δp=±0.25; with AV18, this occurs at δp=±0.41.
- For UV14+TNI, the critical density for unpolarized matter is 0.07 fm^-3; for ferromagnetic (δp=±0.50) it is 0.06 fm^-3; for antiferromagnetic (δp=±0.50) it is 0.06 fm^-3.
- For AV18, the critical density for ferromagnetic (δp=±0.50) is 0.11 fm^-3; for antiferromagnetic (δp=±0.50) it is 0.09 fm^-3.
- For UV14+TNI, the critical pressure for unpolarized matter is 0.28 MeV fm^-3; for ferromagnetic (δp=±0.50) it is 0.13 MeV fm^-3; for antiferromagnetic (δp=±0.50) it is 0.11 MeV fm^-3.
- For AV18, the critical pressure for ferromagnetic (δp=±0.50) is 0.30 MeV fm^-3; for antiferromagnetic (δp=±0.50) it is 0.33 MeV fm^-3.
- The critical exponents for all magnetic states and both potentials are nearly identical; for example, β ranges from 0.46184 to 0.60078, δ from 2.54265 to 2.65974, α from 0.60539 to 0.81497, and γ from 0.77925 to 0.87677, with uncertainties.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous LOCV study of liquid-gas phase transitions in spin-unpolarized asymmetric nuclear matter (Bordbar, 2011) to include spin polarization effects. It contrasts with prior theoretical results: for example, the critical temperature for unpolarized matter with AV18 using the LOCV method (24.3 MeV from the authors' earlier work) is higher than values from the Self-Consistent Green's Functions approach (11.6 MeV for AV18, Rios 2009) or the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock method (18.1 MeV for AV18, Rios 2009). The results enable future studies of spin-polarized matter in astrophysical contexts such as protoneutron stars and heavy-ion collisions, and provide a benchmark for comparing critical exponents across different spin states.
