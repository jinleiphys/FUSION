# Neural-network quantum states for solving few-body problems: application to Efimov physics

> Neural-network quantum states have recently emerged as a powerful method for solving quantum many-body problems, with notable successes in lattice systems. Here, we extend this approach to strongly interacting few-body problems in continuous space, and demonstrate its capability by computing the Efimov states and associated few-body bound states. Using a fully connected feedforward neural network with Jacobi coordinates as inputs, combined with a projection method, we compute the ground and first excited states for three- to six-body systems of identical bosons at unitarity, as well as a mass-imbalanced fermionic system consisting of two identical fermions and a third particle. The obtained energies of the ground and first excited states agree well with previously reported results. Furthermore, the proposed approach also reproduces key features of Efimov states, including the discrete scale invariance, the characteristic geometric structure of the wave function, and the critical-mass behavior in mass-imbalanced fermionic systems. Our method can be readily applied to a broad class of strongly correlated few-body problems in continuous space.

## Key claim

The authors extend neural-network quantum states from lattice systems to strongly interacting few-body problems in continuous space, demonstrating that the method accurately captures Efimov states and associated few-body bound states for three- to six-body systems of identical bosons at unitarity and for mass-imbalanced fermionic systems, with the caveat that the method's accuracy depends on including explicit two-body correlations in the trial wave function and that the first excited state for N=3 shows larger fluctuations (approximately 10 percent) due to the multiscale nature of Efimov states.

## Method

The authors use a fully connected feedforward neural network with three hidden layers of 32 units each and SiLU activation functions, taking Jacobi coordinates (distances and angles between Jacobi vectors) as inputs to enforce translational and rotational symmetry. They combine the network output with an explicit two-body correlation function derived from the zero-energy solution of the Poschl-Teller potential, then minimize the variational energy using the Adam optimizer with Metropolis-Hastings sampling. For excited states, they apply an orthogonal projection method that subtracts the ground-state wave function from the trial wave function before minimization.

- Ground-state energy for N=3 bosons: -0.1347 (in units of hbar^2/(m b^2)), compared to reference value -0.1345
- First excited-state energy for N=3 bosons: -2.63 x 10^{-4} (in units of hbar^2/(m b^2))
- Ratio sqrt(E0/E1) for N=3 bosons: 22.6, compared to reference value 22.4 and universal zero-range value approximately 22.7
- Ground-state energy for N=4 bosons: -0.8261 (reference: -0.8259); first excited: -0.1550 (reference: -0.1548)
- Ground-state energy for N=5 bosons: -2.315 (reference: -2.313); first excited: -0.9399 (reference: -0.9428)
- Ground-state energy for N=6 bosons: -4.745 (reference: -4.731); first excited: -2.663 (reference: -2.667)
- Critical mass ratio for fermionic Efimov states: M/m > 13.606 (from prior work by Efimov (1973) and others)

## Context

This work extends neural-network quantum state methods, previously applied to lattice systems by Carleo and Troyer (2017) and to continuous-space bosonic ground states by Saito (2018), to compute both ground and excited states of few-body systems exhibiting Efimov physics. The results agree with and slightly improve upon prior calculations using hyperspherical harmonic expansion by von Stecher (2010) and correlated Gaussian basis methods by Blume and Daily (2009). The method enables future studies of strongly correlated few-body problems across nuclear physics, cold atomic systems, and helium clusters, and can be extended to other interaction potentials beyond the Poschl-Teller form used here.
