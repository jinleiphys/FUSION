# Non-characteristic Half-lives in Radioactive Decay

> Half-lives of radionuclides span more than 50 orders of magnitude. We characterize the probability distribution of this broad-range data set at the same time that explore a method for fitting power-laws and testing goodness-of-fit. It is found that the procedure proposed recently by Clauset et al. [SIAM Rev. 51, 661 (2009)] does not perform well as it rejects the power-law hypothesis even for power-law synthetic data. In contrast, we establish the existence of a power-law exponent with a value around 1.1 for the half-life density, which can be explained by the sharp relationship between decay rate and released energy, for different disintegration types. For the case of alpha emission, this relationship constitutes an original mechanism of power-law generation.

# Non-characteristic Half-lives in Radioactive Decay

## Key claim

The distribution of radionuclide half-lives follows a power-law tail with an exponent around 1.1, but the Clauset et al. (2009) method for fitting power-laws fails to detect this, rejecting the power-law hypothesis even for synthetic power-law data; the power-law behavior can be explained by the sharp relationship between decay rate and released energy for different disintegration types.

## Method

The authors analyzed 3002 unstable nuclides from the Lund/LBNL Nuclear Data Search, excluding 30 with only bounded half-lives and the neutron, but including 209Bi. They applied maximum-likelihood estimation of the power-law exponent and Kolmogorov-Smirnov distance minimization, both with and without upper truncation, to fit the half-life probability density. They also tested the Clauset et al. (2009) method on synthetic power-law data to evaluate its reliability, and derived the power-law exponent from the Gamow model for alpha decay and the Sargent rule for beta decay.

## Key numbers

- Power-law tail for half-lives above 8.8 × 10⁷ s (≈3 years): exponent τ = 1.09 ± 0.01, spanning more than 23 orders of magnitude, with p = 33% ± 5% (128 nuclides in range)
- Double-truncated power-law fit with a = 126 s and b = 7.9 × 10⁶ s: exponent τ = 1.18 ± 0.01, KS distance d_m = 0.012, p = 88% ± 3% (range over 4 decades)
- Double-truncated power-law fit with a = 126 s and b = 1.3 × 10¹³ s: exponent τ = 1.19 ± 0.01, d_m = 0.015, p = 39% ± 5% (1277 nuclides, range over 11 orders of magnitude)
- Short half-life power-law regime (a = 5.0 × 10⁻⁵ s, b = 0.32 s): exponent τ = 0.65 ± 0.02, d_m = 0.023, p = 40% ± 5% (460 nuclides)
- For alpha decay, the Gamow model yields an asymptotic 1/t tail (τ = 1.0), in agreement with the empirical exponent
- For beta decay, the Sargent rule (t ∝ 1/Q⁵) gives an asymptotic power-law exponent τ = 1.2, with the actual exponent constrained to the range (1, 1.2)

## Context

This work extends the study of scale invariance in physical systems to radioactive decay, finding that half-lives, despite each nuclide having a characteristic decay time, collectively show no predominant scale. The authors demonstrate that the Clauset et al. (2009) method, widely used for power-law fitting, is unreliable for data with small exponents, as it rejected the power-law hypothesis even for synthetic power-law data in 80% of simulations. The power-law generation mechanism for alpha decay, derived from the Gamow model and the Geiger-Nuttall rule, constitutes an original mechanism distinct from those collected in Sornette (2000) and Newman (2005). The results enable future work on whether a single exponent τ ≈ 1.15 ± 0.10 could describe all radioactive half-lives above a few minutes, potentially spanning 30 orders of magnitude, and suggest similar mechanisms may explain the half-life distribution of elementary particles.
