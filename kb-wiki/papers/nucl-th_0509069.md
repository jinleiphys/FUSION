# Applications of Skyrme energy-density functional to fusion reactions spanning the fusion barriers

> The Skyrme energy density functional has been applied to the study of heavy-ion fusion reactions. The barriers for fusion reactions are calculated by the Skyrme energy density functional with proton and neutron density distributions determined by using restricted density variational (RDV) method within the same energy density functional together with semi-classical approach known as the extended semi-classical Thomas-Fermi method. Based on the fusion barrier obtained, we propose a parametrization of the empirical barrier distribution to take into account the multi-dimensional character of real barrier and then apply it to calculate the fusion excitation functions in terms of barrier penetration concept. A large number of measured fusion excitation functions spanning the fusion barriers can be reproduced well. The competition between suppression and enhancement effects on sub-barrier fusion caused by neutron-shell-closure and excess neutron effects is studied.

# Applications of Skyrme energy-density functional to fusion reactions spanning the fusion barriers

## Key claim

The Skyrme energy-density functional, combined with a parametrized empirical barrier distribution composed of two Gaussian functions, can reproduce measured fusion excitation functions for a wide range of heavy-ion systems spanning the fusion barrier, provided that a single adjustable parameter (gamma) is tuned to account for neutron-shell-closure suppression or neutron-rich enhancement effects at sub-barrier energies.

## Method

The authors calculate ground-state proton and neutron density distributions for projectile and target nuclei using the restricted density variational (RDV) method within the semi-classical Extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF2) approach with the SkM* Skyrme force. Using these frozen densities, they compute the entrance-channel fusion barrier (height B0, radius R0, curvature hbar-omega0) from the same Skyrme energy-density functional. They then construct an effective barrier distribution Deff(B) as a piecewise combination of two Gaussian functions whose widths and peak positions depend only on B0 and a reducing factor f=0.926, and compute fusion excitation functions via Wong's formula integrated over this distribution.

## Key numbers

* Barrier heights B0 for more than 80 fusion systems (Z1Z2 > 150) are calculated; for example, 28Si+28Si: B0 = 29.86 MeV, R0 = 8.75 fm, hbar-omega0 = 2.37 MeV; 48Ca+208Pb: B0 = 183.17 MeV, R0 = 12.0 fm, hbar-omega0 = 4.43 MeV.
* Relative deviations of calculated barrier heights from proximity potentials are less than 2.5% for the systems studied.
* Including fourth-order ETF terms (ETF4) lowers fusion barriers by about 1 to 2 MeV compared to ETF2 results.
* The reducing factor for the effective barrier height is fixed at f = 0.926.
* For neutron-closed-shell reactions (e.g., 33S+90Zr), gamma = 3.6 is needed to suppress sub-barrier fusion; for neutron-rich reactions (e.g., 32S+154Sm), gamma = 0.5 is needed to enhance sub-barrier fusion.

## Context

This work extends the semi-classical Skyrme energy-density functional approach of Denisov (2002) by using a self-consistent RDV method for densities rather than Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations, and by proposing a simple two-Gaussian parametrization of the barrier distribution as an alternative to full coupled-channels calculations (Hagino 1999). The approach is benchmarked against the proximity potential of Myers (2000) and the analytical barrier form of Dobrowolski (2003). It enables systematic predictions of fusion excitation functions for unmeasured systems without the computational cost of coupled-channels models, and quantifies the competition between neutron-shell-closure suppression and neutron-rich enhancement effects observed in experiments by Timmers (1998) and others.
