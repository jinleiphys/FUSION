# Eta-nuclear interaction: optical vs. coupled channels

> The existence of etamesic nuclei has been speculated for a long time without firm experimental evidence. Much of the effort has taken place in final state interactions on production. One crucial factor in seeing a quasibound state is its width, which should be related to the imaginary part of the scattering length. Comparing two models for eta-N scattering giving the same elementary scattering length, a simple optical potential and an explicit coupling to the pionic channel, it is seen that the latter yields a much smaller imaginary part for eta-nucleus scattering. This decrease of absorption may also mean a possibility for narrow eta-nuclear states.

# Eta-nuclear interaction: optical vs. coupled channels

## Key claim
While the abstract states that a coupled-channels model yields a much smaller imaginary part for eta-nucleus scattering than a simple optical potential, the actual hedge is that this result holds only when both models are adjusted to give the same elementary eta-N scattering lengths, and the effect is strongly dependent on the range of the elementary interaction and the size of the nucleus. The decrease in absorption is traced primarily to the large wave number of the pionic channel (minimum 2.7 fm^-1) combined with the softer nuclear form factor, not to any fundamental difference in the elementary interaction strength.

## Method
The author compares two models for eta-nucleus scattering: a standard single-channel optical potential where the imaginary part represents absorption, and a coupled-channels model that explicitly couples the eta-nucleus system to the pion-nucleus channel via a transition potential. Both models are first calibrated to produce identical elementary eta-N scattering lengths (with the constraint a_I = 0.5 a_R), then applied to calculate eta scattering from ^3He and ^12C using Gaussian and harmonic oscillator nuclear density profiles. The coupled model solves coupled Schrodinger and Klein-Gordon equations for the eta and pion channels, with the pion channel having a large local momentum from the eta-pion mass difference.

## Key numbers
- The elementary eta-N scattering length real part is varied over the interval 0.2–1.0 fm, with the imaginary part fixed at half the real part (a_I = 0.5 a_R).
- For ^3He with elementary range b = 0.3 fm, the imaginary part of the nuclear scattering length differs by more than two orders of magnitude between the optical model and coupled channels (solid vs. dashed in Fig. 1).
- The free pion momentum in the coupled channel is at minimum 2.7 fm^-1, from the eta-pion mass difference.
- For ^3He, the nuclear density profile uses a Gaussian with range parameter b(eta^3He) = sqrt(2/3) * r_rms = 1.55 fm, with r_rms = 1.9 fm and normalization A = 3.
- For ^12C, the density profile uses a modified harmonic oscillator with parameters 1.672 fm and normalization 4π∫ρ r^2 dr = 12.
- For the most realistic model (coupled channels plus two-pion inelasticity) with Re a(eta N) ≈ 0.3 fm, the estimated bound state energy is E ≈ -(2 + i) MeV.
- The elementary range parameter b is tested at 0.3 fm and 0.6 fm; the latter gives larger nuclear scattering lengths but still much smaller imaginary parts in the coupled case.

## Context
This work extends the optical model approach of Wilkin (1993) and addresses the long-standing puzzle of why experimental extractions of the eta-^3He scattering length (Sibirtsev, 2004; COSY-11, 2007; ANKE, 2007) give much smaller imaginary parts than most theoretical predictions. The coupled-channels treatment provides a theoretical justification for these small imaginary parts, which is crucial because Haider and Liu (1986) showed that a bound state requires |a_R| > a_I, and Sibirtsev (2004) extended this condition to include the effective range. The results enable more optimistic searches for narrow eta-mesic nuclei, particularly in heavier systems like carbon where binding is expected, and suggest that the explicit treatment of the open pion channel may resolve discrepancies between theory and experiment that have persisted since the work of Bhalerao and Liu (1985).
