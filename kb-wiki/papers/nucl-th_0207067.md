# Constraining URCA cooling of neutron stars from the neutron radius of 208Pb

> Recent observations by the Chandra observatory suggest that some neutron stars may cool rapidly, perhaps by the direct URCA process which requires a high proton fraction. The proton fraction is determined by the nuclear symmetry energy whose density dependence may be constrained by measuring the neutron radius of a heavy nucleus, such as 208Pb. Such a measurement is necessary for a reliable extrapolation of the proton fraction to the higher densities present in the neutron star. A large neutron radius in 208Pb implies a stiff symmetry energy that grows rapidly with density, thereby favoring a high proton fraction and allowing direct URCA cooling. Predictions for the neutron radius in 208Pb are correlated to the proton fraction in dense matter by using a variety of relativistic effective field-theory models. Models that predict a neutron (Rn) minus proton (Rp) root-mean-square radius in 208Pb to be Rn-Rp<0.20 fm have proton fractions too small to allow the direct URCA cooling of 1.4 solar-mass neutron stars. Conversely, if Rn-Rp>0.25 fm, the direct URCA process is allowed (by all models) to cool down a 1.4 solar-mass neutron star. The Parity Radius Experiment at Jefferson Laboratory aims to measure the neutron radius in 208Pb accurately and model independently via parity-violating electron scattering. Such a measurement would greatly enhance our ability to either confirm or dismiss the direct URCA cooling of neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a measurement of the neutron skin thickness of 208Pb (Rn minus Rp) can determine whether the direct URCA process is responsible for rapid cooling of 1.4 solar-mass neutron stars, but only if the measurement is accurate enough to distinguish between the threshold values of 0.20 fm and 0.25 fm. The hedge is that this correlation holds for the specific set of relativistic effective field-theory models considered, and the authors caution that pairing gaps and other uncertainties could modify the cooling picture.

## Method

The authors used a relativistic effective field-theory framework with nonlinear couplings between isoscalar and isovector mesons to generate a range of parameter sets (NL3, S271, Z271) that reproduce known ground-state properties of symmetric nuclear matter and the binding energy of 208Pb. They varied the isoscalar-isovector coupling parameters (Lambda_s and Lambda_v) to modify the density dependence of the symmetry energy, then computed the neutron skin of 208Pb and the equilibrium proton fraction in beta-equilibrium matter (neutrons, protons, electrons, muons) as a function of baryon density. Finally, they solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to determine the minimum neutron-star mass that allows direct URCA cooling for each parameter set.

## Key numbers

- Models with Rn-Rp < 0.20 fm in 208Pb produce proton fractions too small to allow direct URCA cooling of 1.4 solar-mass neutron stars.
- Models with Rn-Rp > 0.25 fm in 208Pb allow direct URCA cooling of 1.4 solar-mass neutron stars for all parameter sets considered.
- The Parity Radius Experiment at Jefferson Laboratory aims to measure the neutron radius in 208Pb to 1% accuracy (plus or minus 0.05 fm).
- For the NL3 parameter set with Lambda_v = 0, the neutron skin is 0.280 fm, the URCA threshold density is 0.205 fm^-3, and the minimum URCA mass is 0.838 solar masses.
- For the NL3 set with Lambda_v = 0.025, the neutron skin is 0.209 fm, the URCA threshold density is 0.442 fm^-3, and the minimum URCA mass is 2.449 solar masses.
- The nonrelativistic equation of state of Friedman and Pandharipande yields a neutron skin in 208Pb of 0.16 plus or minus 0.02 fm.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Brown (2000) who showed that the neutron radius of 208Pb determines the pressure of neutron-rich matter at normal densities, and by Horowitz and Piekarewicz (2001) who correlated the neutron radius to neutron-star crust properties. The paper directly addresses the debate between Yakovlev and collaborators (who use a relativistic mean-field equation of state allowing direct URCA for stars above 1.358 solar masses) and Tsuruta and collaborators (who rely on pion condensation to explain rapid cooling). A measurement of the neutron skin in 208Pb via parity-violating electron scattering at Jefferson Laboratory could decisively determine whether the direct URCA process operates in 1.4 solar-mass neutron stars, or whether exotic states of matter (meson condensates, hyperons, or quark matter) are required to explain observed rapid cooling.
