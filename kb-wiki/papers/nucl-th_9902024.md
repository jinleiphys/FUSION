# Disoriented Chiral Condensates, Pion Probability Distributions and Parallels with Disordered System

> A general expression is discussed for pion probability distributions coming from relativistic heavy ion collisions. The general expression contains as limits: 1) The disoriented chiral condensate (DCC), 2) the negative binomial distribution and Pearson type III distribution, 3) a binomial or Gaussian result, 4) and a Poisson distribution. This general expression approximates other distributions such as a signal to noise laser distribution. Similarities and differences of the DCC distribution with these other distribution are studied. A connection with the theory of disordered systems will be discussed which include spin-glasses, randomly broken objects, random and chaotic maps.

## Key claim

The paper presents a general Pólya distribution for pion probability distributions from relativistic heavy ion collisions that contains the disoriented chiral condensate (DCC), negative binomial, binomial/Gaussian, and Poisson distributions as special limiting cases, but the DCC distribution is uniquely characterized not just by the often-quoted \(1/\sqrt{R_3}\) falloff in neutral pions but by the coupled rise in charged pion yields that other distributions cannot reproduce.

## Method

The author derives a general Pólya distribution \(P_k(N, x, \gamma) = \binom{N}{k} \frac{\mathrm{Beta}[N-k+x, k+\gamma]}{\mathrm{Beta}[x,\gamma]}\) and shows its limiting forms for specific parameter choices: \(x=1, \gamma=1/2\) gives the DCC distribution; \(N \to \infty, x \to \infty\) with fixed ratio gives the negative binomial distribution; \(x \to \infty, \gamma \to \infty\) gives a binomial distribution; and the Poisson limit emerges from the negative binomial when \(\gamma \gg \langle k \rangle\). The author compares the DCC distribution to the negative binomial and Glauber-Lach signal-to-noise laser distribution, and maps the general distribution onto probability functions from disordered systems including spin-glasses, randomly broken objects, random permutations, random maps, and chaotic maps.

## Key numbers

- For the DCC choice \(x=1, \gamma=1/2\), the mean number of neutral pions is \(\langle n_0 \rangle = 2N/3\) with variance \(\langle n_0^2 \rangle - \langle n_0 \rangle^2 = (4/5) \langle n_0 \rangle (1 + \langle n_0 \rangle)\); for charged pions, \(\langle n_+^2 \rangle - \langle n_+ \rangle^2 = (1/5) \langle n_+ \rangle (1 + \langle n_+ \rangle) = (1/4) (\langle n_0^2 \rangle - \langle n_0 \rangle^2)\).
- The DCC probability distribution for neutral pions asymptotically behaves as \(P(R_3) \sim 1/(2\sqrt{R_3})\) where \(R_3 = n_0/(n_0 + n_+ + n_-)\).
- For a single DCC domain with maximum \(N_1\), the variance is \(\sigma_1^2 = (2/15) N_1 (1 + 2N_1/3)\); for \(m\) domains with total \(N = m N_1\), the total variance is \(\sigma_m^2 = (2/15) N (1 + 2N/3m)\), reducing to \((2/9)N\) when \(m = N\) (binomial result).
- The negative binomial distribution with \(\gamma = 1/2\) and \(\langle k \rangle = 100\) is compared to the DCC distribution with \(N = 300\) and \(\langle k \rangle = 100\); the DCC distribution is also compared to a signal-to-noise laser model with \(S = 62\), \(NL = 38\), \(\alpha = 1/2\), and \(\langle k \rangle = 100\).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for pion multiplicity distributions beyond the negative binomial distribution used by Van Hove and Giovannini (clan model) and Carruthers and Shih (fractal structures), and beyond the Glauber-Lach signal-to-noise model from quantum optics. The key advance is showing that the DCC distribution from a QCD phase transition has a unique coupling between neutral and charged pion channels, the neutral pions fall as \(1/\sqrt{k}\) while charged pions rise as \(1/\sqrt{N-k}\), which cannot be reproduced by negative binomial or laser models, suggesting that event-by-event data should investigate both channels simultaneously. The connection to disordered systems (spin-glasses, randomly broken objects, random permutations, random and chaotic maps) provides a mathematical bridge between heavy-ion phenomenology and statistical physics of complex systems.
