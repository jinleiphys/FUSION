# Thermodynamics of a finite system of classical particles with short and long range interactions and nuclear fragmentation

> We describe a finite inhomogeneous three dimensional system of classical particles which interact through short and (or) long range interactions by means of a simple analytic spin model. The thermodynamic properties of the system are worked out in the framework of the grand canonical ensemble. It is shown that the system experiences a phase transition at fixed average density in the thermodynamic limit. The phase diagram and the caloric curve are constructed and compared with numerical simulations. The implications of our results concerning the caloric curve are discussed in connection with the interpretation of corresponding experimental data.

## Key claim
The spherical model approximation of a finite classical particle system with short- and long-range interactions predicts a continuous phase transition at all fixed densities, producing a smooth caloric curve with only a kink rather than a plateau, contrary to the first-order transition seen in lattice gas models.

## Method
The authors map a three-dimensional lattice of occupied or empty cells (Ising-like variables) onto the exactly solvable spherical model by replacing discrete spins with continuous real variables constrained by a mean spherical condition. They compute the grand canonical partition function analytically via Fourier transformation, obtaining the energy per particle and specific heat from the resulting dispersion relations. For the long-range case, they extend the Hamiltonian to include multiple neighbor shells with parametrized Coulomb-like interactions, and compare analytic results to Metropolis Monte Carlo simulations of a cellular model with nuclear and Coulomb forces.

## Key numbers
- Critical temperature condition: \(\beta_c = 2W_3(0)/V_0\) with \(W_3(0) = 0.2527\) (integral of modified Bessel functions) for density \(\rho/\rho_0 = 1/2\) and \(M=0\).
- Normal density \(\rho_0\) is the reference density of nuclear matter; the magnetization \(M = 2\rho/\rho_0 - 1\).
- For numerical simulations, cell linear dimension \(d = 1.8\ \mathrm{fm}\) and attractive short-range interaction strength \(V_0 = 5.5\ \mathrm{MeV}\) used to fit the caloric curve.
- Specific heat exponent \(\alpha = -1\) at the transition; specific heat remains finite but its derivative is discontinuous in the thermodynamic limit.

## Context
This work extends the authors' earlier homogeneous spherical model (Boose et al., 1995) to an inhomogeneous three-dimensional system with both short- and long-range interactions, including Coulomb repulsion. It contrasts with lattice gas models (e.g., Ising model studies from the 1990s) that show a first-order liquid-gas phase transition; the spherical model yields a continuous transition at all densities due to the fixed magnetization constraint. The results enable analytic calculation of \(n\)-body correlation functions for fragment size distributions, and the smooth caloric curve provides a benchmark for interpreting experimental nuclear fragmentation data where no clear phase transition plateau is observed.
