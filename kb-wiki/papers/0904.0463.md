# Recent developments in no-core shell-model calculations

> We present an overview of recent results and developments of the no-core shell model (NCSM), an ab initio approach to the nuclear many-body problem for light nuclei. In this approach, we start from realistic two-nucleon or two- plus three-nucleon interactions. Many-body calculations are performed using a finite harmonic-oscillator (HO) basis. To facilitate convergence for realistic inter-nucleon interactions that generate strong short-range correlations, we derive effective interactions by unitary transformations that are tailored to the HO basis truncation. For soft realistic interactions this might not be necessary. If that is the case, the NCSM calculations are variational. In either case, the ab initio NCSM preserves translational invariance of the nuclear many-body problem. In this review, we, in particular, highlight results obtained with the chiral two- plus three-nucleon interactions. We discuss efforts to extend the applicability of the NCSM to heavier nuclei and larger model spaces using importance-truncation schemes and/or use of effective interactions with a core. We outline an extension of the ab initio NCSM to the description of nuclear reactions by the resonating group method technique. A future direction of the approach, the ab initio NCSM with continuum, which will provide a complete description of nuclei as open systems with coupling of bound and continuum states, is given in the concluding part of the review.

# Recent developments in no-core shell-model calculations

## Key claim

The no-core shell model (NCSM) provides an *ab initio* approach to the nuclear many-body problem for light nuclei up to A=16 and beyond, but its convergence depends critically on whether the inter-nucleon interactions are "soft" enough to be used bare in a variational calculation or require a unitary-transformation-derived effective interaction to handle strong short-range correlations in a truncated harmonic-oscillator basis.

## Method

The NCSM treats all A nucleons as active (no inert core) using a finite harmonic-oscillator (HO) basis truncated by a maximal total HO energy. For realistic interactions that generate strong short-range correlations (e.g., AV18, CD-Bonn 2000, INOY), the authors derive effective two-body and three-body interactions via the Okubo-Lee-Suzuki unitary transformation method applied to two-nucleon and three-nucleon systems, which are then used in the A-nucleon calculation. For soft potentials (e.g., V_low_k, SRG, UCOM, JISP, or chiral N^3LO), the bare interactions can be used directly in variational calculations. The approach preserves translational invariance by using either Jacobi-coordinate HO bases (for A ≤ 4) or single-nucleon Slater determinant HO bases (for A > 4) with the center-of-mass kept in the 0ħΩ state.

## Key numbers

- The NCSM is applicable to light nuclei up to A=16 and beyond.
- For A=6 and N_max=6 (6ħΩ) space, the three-nucleon model space is defined by N_123max=8; for p-shell nuclei with A ≥ 7 and N_max=6, N_123max=9.
- For p-shell nuclei with A > 5, the two-nucleon model space truncation is N_12max = N_max + 2.
- The two-nucleon Hamiltonian is solved for all two-nucleon channels up to j=8; for higher j channels only the kinetic-energy term is used.
- Standard NN potentials (AV18, CD-Bonn 2000, INOY) fit nucleon-nucleon phase shifts with high precision up to typically 350 MeV.

## Context

This review extends the NCSM formalism first developed in Navratil and Barrett (2000) and the earlier no-core calculations using G-matrix-based interactions by Zheng, Barrett, and Vary. It contrasts with other *ab initio* methods such as the Green's function Monte Carlo method (applicable up to A=12) and the coupled cluster method (typically for closed-shell and nearby nuclei), as well as Faddeev and Yakubovsky methods for few-nucleon systems. The work enables future extensions to heavier nuclei via importance-truncation schemes and effective interactions with a core, and to nuclear reactions through coupling with the resonating group method and the proposed *ab initio* NCSM with continuum for describing open quantum systems.
