# Using Lithium and Beryllium to Study Structure and Evolution of Rotating Stars

> The chemical composition of the Sun is still a highly controversial issue. No solar model has yet been able to simultaneously reproduce the solar lithium and beryllium abundances, along with helioseismic results, including the rotation profile. Lithium and beryllium are fragile elements that are highly sensitive to the physical conditions, as well as to transport and mixing processes within and below the convective zone (CZ). Uncovering the transport mechanisms responsible for the depletion of Li and Be in the Sun is crucial for using them as tools to understand stellar interiors and the associated transport and mixing processes. We constructed rotating solar models based on Magg's abundance scale, incorporating the effects of convective overshoot and magnetic fields. The rotating model exhibits superior sound speed and density profile and successfully reproduces the observed ratios $r_{02}$ and $r_{13}$. It also matches the seismically inferred CZ depth, surface helium abundance, and rotation profile, as well as the detected Li and Be abundances and neutrino fluxes within $1σ$. The depletion of Li is dominated by convective overshoot and rotational mixing, while Be depletion is primarily driven by gravitational settling and rotational mixing. The presence of the tachocline accelerates Li depletion but slows down Be depletion. These distinct depletion mechanisms result in the surface abundances of Li and Be evolving differently over time.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a rotating solar model incorporating convective overshoot, magnetic fields, and a tachocline can simultaneously reproduce the observed lithium and beryllium abundances, helioseismic rotation profile, sound speed and density profiles, convective zone depth, surface helium abundance, and neutrino fluxes within 1 sigma, but only when the mixing efficiency is carefully tuned and the tachocline is included to differentially affect lithium and beryllium depletion.

## Method

The authors constructed rotating solar models using the Yale Rotating Stellar Evolution Code with Magg (2022) abundance mixtures, OPAL and OP opacity tables, and included convective overshoot with a parameter delta_ov = 0.09, magnetic field effects, and angular momentum loss via Kawaler's relation. They calibrated five models (MBS22, MBS22r1, MBS22r2, MBS22r3, MBS22rt) to present solar luminosity, radius, mass, and age, varying the mixing efficiency parameters f_cm and including a tachocline of 0.05 solar radii in model MBS22rt with enhanced mixing within that region.

## Key numbers

- Surface lithium abundance of model MBS22rt: 0.94 dex, consistent with the observed 0.96 +/- 0.05 dex from Wang (2021)
- Surface beryllium abundance of model MBS22rt: 1.31 dex, consistent with the observed 1.32 +/- 0.05 dex from Korotin (2022)
- Convective zone base radius of MBS22rt: 0.713 solar radii, matching the seismically inferred value of 0.713 +/- 0.001 solar radii from Basu (1997)
- Surface helium abundance of MBS22rt: 0.2517, in agreement with helioseismic inferences
- Temperature at the base of the tachocline in MBS22rt: approximately 2.6 x 10^6 K, sufficient to destroy lithium but not beryllium
- Upper limit of surface beryllium abundance predicted by rotating models: 1.34 dex
- Neutrino fluxes from MBS22rt: pp flux 5.98 x 10^10 cm^-2 s^-1, 8B flux 5.55 x 10^6 cm^-2 s^-1, both within 1 sigma of measurements from Bellini (2011, 2012) and Ahmet (2004)

## Context

This work extends previous rotating solar models by Yang (2007, 2016, 2024) and addresses the failure of the Eggenberger (2022) rotating model to simultaneously match beryllium abundance, convective zone depth, and sound speed profile. The authors show that lithium depletion is dominated by convective overshoot and rotational mixing while beryllium depletion is primarily driven by gravitational settling and rotational mixing, with the tachocline accelerating lithium depletion but slowing beryllium depletion. This enables using lithium and beryllium as complementary tracers for disentangling different transport mechanisms in solar-type stars and helps constrain the primordial lithium abundance from observations of metal-poor stars.
