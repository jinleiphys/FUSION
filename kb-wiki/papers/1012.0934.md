# The dark atoms of dark matter

> The nonbaryonic dark matter of the Universe is assumed to consist of new stable particles. A specific case is possible, when new stable particles bear ordinary electric charge and bind in heavy "atoms" by ordinary Coulomb interaction. Such possibility is severely restricted by the constraints on anomalous isotopes of light elements that form positively charged heavy species with ordinary electrons. The trouble is avoided, if stable particles $X^{--}$ with charge -2 are in excess over their antiparticles (with charge +2) and there are no stable particles with charges +1 and -1. Then primordial helium, formed in Big Bang Nucleosynthesis, captures all $X^{--}$ in neutral "atoms" of O-helium (OHe), thus creating a specific Warmer than Cold nuclear-interacting composite dark matter. Slowed down in the terrestrial matter, OHe is elusive for direct methods of underground dark. However OHe-nucleus interaction leads to their binding and in OHe-Na system the energy of such level can be in the interval of energy 2-4 keV. It explains the results of DAMA/NaI and DAMA/LIBRA experiments. The puzzles of direct dark matter searches appear in this solution as a reflection of nontrivial nuclear physics of OHe. (abridged)

## Key claim

The paper argues that the DAMA/NaI and DAMA/LIBRA dark matter detection signals can be explained by radiative capture of O-helium (OHe) atoms by sodium nuclei in the detector, forming a bound state with binding energy in the 2-4 keV range, but only under specific nuclear physics parameters for the scalar isoscalar Yukawa potential and the assumption that stable doubly-charged negative particles exist in excess over their antiparticles.

## Method

The authors solve the Schrodinger equation for the relative motion of a nucleus and an OHe atom, approximating the combined nuclear and Coulomb potentials with a rectangular well model. The OHe-nucleus potential includes Coulomb attraction from the central X−− particle, a dipole Coulomb barrier from the helium shell, and a Yukawa nuclear attraction mediated by sigma meson exchange between the helium nucleus and the target nucleus. The existence and energy of bound states are determined by varying the sigma meson mass and coupling strength within ranges compatible with nuclear matter models.

## Key numbers

* OHe ionization potential: I_o ≈ 1.6 MeV (or ≈ 1.3 MeV with charge distribution in He nucleus accounted for)
* OHe size: R_o ≈ 2 × 10^{-13} cm
* OHe-nucleus elastic cross section: σ ≈ π R_o^2 ≈ 10^{-25} cm^2
* Drift velocity of OHe in terrestrial matter: V ≈ 80 S_3 A^{1/2} cm/s, where S_3 = m_o/(1 TeV) and A ≈ 30
* Local OHe dark matter density near Earth: n_0 = 3 × 10^{-4} S_3^{-1} cm^{-3}
* Binding energy of Na-OHe system: 2-4 keV (specifically 3 keV used for signal reproduction)
* Rate of OHe radiative capture by nucleus: σv = (f π α)/(m_p^2) × (3/√2) × (Z/A)^2 × T/√(A m_p E), with f = (m_n - m_p)/m_N ≈ 1.4 × 10^{-3}
* Annual modulation amplitude formula given with numerical factor a_i = 4.3 × 10^{10} for sodium

## Context

This work extends the O-helium composite dark matter scenario previously developed by Khlopov and collaborators (2006-2009), which proposes that stable doubly-charged particles X−− bind with primordial helium to form neutral OHe atoms. The paper addresses the long-standing puzzle that DAMA/NaI and DAMA/LIBRA observe annual modulation while other direct detection experiments like CDMS (Akerib, 2005) and XENON100 find null results. The key insight is that OHe-nucleus bound states exist only for intermediate-mass nuclei (between boron and titanium), explaining why DAMA's sodium iodide detector sees a signal while experiments using germanium or xenon do not. This enables a consistent interpretation of the conflicting direct detection results without invoking different dark matter particles for different experiments.
