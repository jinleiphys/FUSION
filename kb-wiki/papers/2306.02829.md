# Dynamic Calculations of Magnetic Field and Implications on Spin Polarization and Spin Alignment in Heavy Ion Collisions

> Magnetic field plays a crucial role in various novel phenomena in heavy-ion collisions. We solve the Maxwell equations numerically in a medium with time-dependent electric conductivity by using the Finite-Difference Time-Domain (FDTD) algorithm. We investigate the time evolution of magnetic fields in two scenarios with different electric conductivities at collision energies ranging from $\sqrt{s_\text{NN}}=$ 7.7 to 200 GeV. Our results suggest that the magnetic field may not persist long enough to induce a significant splitting between the global spin polarizations of $Λ$ and $\barΛ$ at freeze-out stage. However, our results do not rule out the possibility of the magnetic field influencing the spin (anti-)alignment of vector mesons.

# Dynamic Calculations of Magnetic Field and Implications on Spin Polarization and Spin Alignment in Heavy Ion Collisions

## Key claim
The magnetic field generated in heavy-ion collisions may not persist long enough to induce a significant splitting between the global spin polarizations of Λ and anti-Λ at freeze-out, but the results do not rule out the possibility of the magnetic field influencing the spin (anti-)alignment of vector mesons because fluctuations in the magnetic field can produce correlated quark spin polarizations even when the average field is small.

## Method
The authors numerically solve the Maxwell equations using the Finite-Difference Time-Domain (FDTD) algorithm in a medium with time-dependent electric conductivity. They initialize the electromagnetic field at t=0 using the Lienard-Wiechert potential for two nuclei moving at constant velocity with Wood-Saxon charge distributions, then evolve the fields forward in time for Au+Au collisions at impact parameter b=7 fm. Two conductivity scenarios are studied: a step-function conductivity that turns on at collision time (σ = σ₀ θ(t)), and a time-decreasing conductivity that accounts for QGP expansion (σ = σ₀ θ(t)/(1 + t/t₀)^(1/3)), with σ₀ = 5.8 MeV and t₀ = 0.5 fm/c.

## Key numbers
- Magnetic field at the collision center reaches initial values of order 10^18 Gauss in Au+Au at √s_NN = 200 GeV and 10^19 Gauss in Pb+Pb at √s_NN = 5020 GeV (cited from prior work)
- At late times (t > 7 fm/c), the magnetic field magnitude is of order eB_y ~ 10^-3 to 10^-2 m_π^2 across collision energies from 7.7 to 200 GeV
- The magnetic-field-induced splitting between Λ and anti-Λ global spin polarizations is estimated as P_anti-Λ - P_Λ = 0.0826 × (eB/m_π^2), yielding a splitting no larger than 0.1%
- The step-function conductivity case (σ = σ₀ θ(t)) shows negligible difference from vacuum baseline at early times (t < 1 fm/c for 200 GeV, t < 3 fm/c for 7.7 GeV)
- The time-decreasing conductivity case (σ = σ₀ θ(t)/(1 + t/t₀)^(1/3)) produces magnetic fields intermediate between vacuum and constant-conductivity cases

## Context
This work extends prior analytical calculations of magnetic field evolution in heavy-ion collisions that assumed constant electric conductivity, such as those by Tuchin (2013) and Gursoy et al. (2014). The authors show that the analytical formula for constant conductivity significantly overestimates the magnetic field at early and intermediate times compared to the more realistic numerical treatment with time-dependent conductivity. The results are consistent with recent STAR data (2023) which places upper limits on Λ/anti-Λ polarization splitting of 0.24% at 19.6 GeV and 0.35% at 27 GeV, and with similar conclusions from Peng et al. (2022). The work enables more accurate predictions for magnetic-field-sensitive observables and clarifies that spin alignment of vector mesons (measured via ρ₀₀) can receive contributions from field fluctuations even when the average magnetic field is too small to affect global hyperon polarization.
