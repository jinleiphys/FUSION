# Local thermodynamical equilibrium and the beta frame for a quantum relativistic fluid

> We discuss the concept of local thermodynamical equilibrium in relativistic hydrodynamics in flat spacetime in a quantum statistical framework without an underlying kinetic description, suitable for strongly interacting fluids. We show that the appropriate definition of local equilibrium naturally leads to the introduction of a relativistic hydrodynamical frame in which the four-velocity vector is the one of a relativistic thermometer at equilibrium with the fluid, parallel to the inverse temperature four-vector β, which then becomes a primary quantity. We show that this frame is the most appropriate for the expansion of stress-energy tensor from local thermodynamical equilibrium and that therein the local laws of thermodynamics take on their simplest form. We discuss the difference between the βframe and Landau frame and present an instance where they differ.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the appropriate definition of local thermodynamical equilibrium (LTE) in relativistic quantum statistical mechanics, without recourse to kinetic theory, naturally leads to the inverse temperature four-vector \(\beta\) as a primary field and defines a new hydrodynamical frame (the \(\beta\) frame) where the fluid four-velocity is \(u = \beta/\sqrt{\beta^2}\). This frame is claimed to be the most natural for expanding the stress-energy tensor from LTE and for expressing local thermodynamic relations in their simplest form, but the authors acknowledge that for a vorticous \(\beta\) field the identification \(n = \hat\beta\) is not possible and must be modified through an iterative construction.

## Method

The authors define LTE via the maximization of von Neumann entropy with constraints on energy-momentum and charge densities, using a covariant density operator on a family of spacelike hypersurfaces. They show that the Lagrange multiplier \(\beta\) (the inverse temperature four-vector) becomes a primary quantity, and they construct a field \(b(x)\) with vanishing external derivative to define vorticity-free hypersurfaces for general \(\beta\) fields. The stress-energy tensor is then expanded from LTE using Zubarev's linear response method, treating gradients of \(\beta\) and \(\xi\) as perturbations.

## Key numbers

- The inverse temperature four-vector \(\beta\) satisfies \(\beta^2 = 1/T^2\) and \(\xi = \mu/T\), where \(T\) is the local temperature and \(\mu\) the chemical potential.
- For a thermometer moving with four-velocity \(v\) in a system characterized by \(\beta\), the marked temperature is \(T_T = 1/(\beta(x) \cdot v)\), with the maximum temperature \(T_0 = 1/\sqrt{\beta^2}\) attained when \(u = v\).
- The global equilibrium condition requires \(\partial_\mu \beta_\nu + \partial_\nu \beta_\mu = 0\) and \(\partial_\mu \xi = 0\), leading to \(\beta_\nu = b_\nu + \varpi_{\nu\lambda} x^\lambda\) with \(\varpi_{\nu\lambda} = -\frac{1}{2}(\partial_\nu\beta_\lambda - \partial_\lambda\beta_\nu)\).

## Context

This work extends the concept of local thermodynamical equilibrium from non-relativistic statistical mechanics (Balian) to a fully covariant quantum relativistic framework without kinetic theory, suitable for strongly interacting fluids like the Quark-Gluon Plasma near QCD critical temperature. It supports and generalizes the argument by Van and Biro (2012) that \(\beta\) defines an independent hydrodynamical frame, and it contrasts this \(\beta\) frame with the Landau frame, providing an instance where they differ. The paper enables a first-principles derivation of transport coefficients via Green-Kubo formulas (Hosoya et al., 1984; Baier et al., 2008) without assuming a kinetic description.
