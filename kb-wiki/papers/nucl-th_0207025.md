# The Density Matrix Renormalization Group Method for Realistic Large-Scale Nuclear Shell-Model Calculations

> The Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG) method is developed for application to realistic nuclear systems. Test results are reported for 24Mg.

## Key claim

The particle-hole Density Matrix Renormalization Group (p-h DMRG) method, when applied to the realistic nuclear system 24Mg, does not show the rapid exponential convergence with the number of kept states p that was observed in earlier single-j shell test problems; instead, achieving high accuracy requires retaining a large fraction of the full Hilbert space.

## Method

The authors applied the p-h DMRG method to 24Mg, treating it as a 16O core plus four valence neutrons and four valence protons in the sd-shell (1d5/2, 2s1/2, 1d3/2 orbits) using the Wildenthal USD interaction. They split the spherical shell-model levels into doubly-degenerate levels via an axially-deformed Hartree-Fock calculation, then iteratively added particle and hole levels closest to the Fermi surface, truncating the basis by diagonalizing mixed density matrices constructed from the lowest eigenstates. Calculations were performed both in a spherical basis and in the deformed Hartree-Fock single-particle basis, and results were compared to exact shell-model diagonalization in the m-scheme (Hilbert space dimension 28,503).

## Key numbers

- For 24Mg, the exact shell-model Hilbert space in the m-scheme has dimension 28,503.
- In the spherical basis, achieving 0.2% accuracy for the ground state energy required p = 50, at which point 97% of the shell-model states are taken into account.
- The single-j test system involved 40 identical nucleons in a j = 99/2 orbit, where the exact Hamiltonian matrix would have dimension 3.84007 × 10^25.
- For 24Mg, the DMRG procedure involved a total of three iterations; in the first iteration both particle and hole levels were added, and in subsequent iterations only particle levels were added.

## Context

This work extends the p-h DMRG method, previously tested on simplified single-j shell problems with pairing plus quadrupole interactions (Dukelsky et al., 2001; Pittel et al., 2001), to a realistic nuclear system with a standard shell-model interaction (Wildenthal's USD interaction). The results show that the method converges much more slowly for 24Mg than for the single-j test cases, suggesting that the small model space may be a factor. The authors plan to test the method on the larger exactly-solvable system 48Cr and to incorporate sweeps (White, 1992) to improve accuracy, with the ultimate goal of treating larger-scale nuclear structure problems beyond the reach of exact diagonalization.
