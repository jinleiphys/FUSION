# Compatibility of Exotic States with Neutron Star Observation

> We consider the effect of hard core repulsion in the baryon-baryon interaction at short distance to the properties of a neutron star. We obtain that, even with hyperons in the interior of a neutron star, the neutron star mass can be as large as $\sim 2 M_\odot$.

## Key claim

Even with hyperons in the interior of a neutron star, the neutron star mass can be as large as approximately 2 solar masses, provided the hard core radius of baryons is at least 0.3 fm.

## Method

The authors used a quantum hadrodynamics (QHD) mean-field model for octet baryons, incorporating hard-core repulsion via an excluded volume parametrization. Meson-nucleon coupling constants were fitted to five nuclear saturation properties (density 0.17 fm^-3, binding energy 16.0 MeV, symmetry energy 32.5 MeV, compression modulus 300 MeV, nucleon effective mass 0.75 m*_N) for each chosen hard core radius, while meson-hyperon couplings were set by quark counting rules. The equation of state for neutron star matter was determined self-consistently under baryon number conservation, charge neutrality, and beta-equilibrium, and the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation was solved to obtain the mass-radius relation.

## Key numbers

- Maximum neutron star mass with hyperons and hard core radius 0.3 fm: 1.70 solar masses, with radius 11.5 km and central density 5.9 times nuclear saturation density.
- Maximum neutron star mass with hyperons and hard core radius 0.4 fm: 1.97 solar masses, with radius 12.2 km and central density 5.2 times nuclear saturation density.
- Maximum neutron star mass with nucleons only and no hard core (r0 = 0 fm): 2.10 solar masses, with radius 10.9 km and central density 6.4 times nuclear saturation density.
- Maximum neutron star mass with nucleons only and hard core radius 0.5 fm: 2.34 solar masses, with radius 11.7 km and central density 5.4 times nuclear saturation density.
- Hard core radius values considered: 0, 0.2, 0.3, 0.4, and 0.5 fm.

## Context

This work extends the debate on whether exotic states (specifically hyperonic matter) are compatible with observed neutron star masses near 2 solar masses, following claims by Ozel (2006) that large masses rule out exotic states and counter-arguments by Klahn (2006). The approach builds on prior QHD models and excluded-volume treatments used in heavy-ion collision studies by Rischke (1991), Kagiyama (1992), and Cleymans (1993). The result shows that hyperonic matter, which typically softens the equation of state, can still yield large masses when hard-core repulsion is included, and the required hard core radii of 0.3-0.5 fm are consistent with values from Hamada-Johnston (1962) and Reid (1968) potential models.
