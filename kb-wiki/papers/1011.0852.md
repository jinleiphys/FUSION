# Multi-quark hadrons from Heavy Ion Collisions

> Identifying hadronic molecular states and/or hadrons with multi-quark components either with or without exotic quantum numbers is a long standing challenge in hadronic physics. We suggest that studying the production of these hadrons in relativistic heavy ion collisions offer a promising resolution to this problem as yields of exotic hadrons are expected to be strongly affected by their structures. Using the coalescence model for hadron production, we find that compared to the case of a non-exotic hadron with normal quark numbers, the yield of an exotic hadron is typically an order of magnitude smaller when it is a compact multi-quark state and a factor of two or more larger when it is a loosely bound hadronic molecule. We further find that due to the appreciable numbers of charm and bottom quarks produced in heavy ion collisions at RHIC and even larger numbers expected at LHC, some of the newly proposed heavy exotic states could be produced and realistically measured in these experiments.

## Key claim

The yield of an exotic hadron produced in relativistic heavy ion collisions is typically an order of magnitude smaller than that of a normal hadron when it is a compact multi-quark state, and a factor of two or more larger when it is a loosely bound hadronic molecule, based on calculations using the coalescence model.

## Method

The authors used the statistical model to normalize expected yields of hadrons assuming thermal equilibrium, then applied the coalescence model based on harmonic oscillator wave functions to compute yields for different internal configurations (compact multi-quark vs. molecular). Oscillator frequencies were fixed by requiring the coalescence model to reproduce reference normal hadron yields from the statistical model, with separate frequencies for light quarks (550 MeV), strange quarks (519 MeV), and charm quarks (385 MeV). For weakly bound hadronic molecules, yields were estimated using coalescence of hadrons at the kinetic freezeout point (T_F = 125 MeV).

## Key numbers

- For RHIC (sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV): V_H = 1908 fm^3, T_H = 175 MeV, mu_B = 20 MeV, mu_s = 10 MeV, gamma_c = 6.40, total charm quark number N_c = 3
- For LHC (sqrt(s_NN) = 5.5 TeV): V_H = 5152 fm^3, T_H = 175 MeV, mu_B = 0 MeV, mu_s = 0 MeV, gamma_c = 15.8, total charm quark number N_c = 20
- Coalescence factor for adding an s-wave u/d-quark: about 0.360; for a p-wave u/d-quark: about 0.093
- For f0(980) as a K-bar-K molecule: omega = 67.8 MeV using binding energy = 11.3 MeV
- For X(3872) as a D-bar-D* molecule: omega = 3.6 MeV
- For Lambda(1405) as a K-bar-N molecule: omega = 20.5 MeV (from radius) to 174 MeV (from binding energy)
- Predicted yield of f0(980) at RHIC: 0.10 (compact tetraquark), 13 (molecular), 5.6 (statistical model)
- Predicted yield of X(3872) at RHIC: 4.0e-5 (compact tetraquark), 7.8e-4 (molecular), 2.9e-4 (statistical model)
- Predicted yield of D_s(2317) at RHIC: 2.1e-3 (compact tetraquark), 1.6e-2 (molecular), 5.6e-2 (statistical model)
- STAR preliminary measurement: f0(980)/rho0 ~ 0.2, giving f0(980) ~ 8 when using statistical model prediction for rho0 = 42

## Context

This work extends the approach of Chen (2003) and Chen (2007) and Lee (2007) who first suggested using heavy ion collisions to study exotic hadron structure. It contrasts with previous methods that relied on decay or reaction rates (Close, 1992) or searches in elementary particle reactions. The results enable discrimination between compact multi-quark and molecular configurations for states like f0(980), a0(980), D_s(2317), X(3872), and Lambda(1405), and suggest that heavy exotic states containing charm quarks could be realistically measured at RHIC and LHC, potentially leading to the first observation of new multi-quark hadrons.
