# Stability of spherical nuclei in the inner crust of neutron stars

> Neutron stars are the densest objects in the Universe. In this paper we consider so-called inner crust - the layer, where neutron-excess nuclei are immersed into degenerate gas of electrons and sea of quasi-free neutrons. It was generally believed that spherical nuclei become unstable with respect to quadrupole deformations at high densities and here we consider this instability. Within perturbative approach we show that spherical nuclei with equilibrium number density are, in fact, stable with respect to infinitesimal quadrupole deformation. This is due to background of degenerate electrons and associated electrostatic potential which maintain stability of spherical nuclei. However, if the number of atomic nuclei per unit volume is much less than the equilibrium value, instability can arise. To avoid confusion we stress that our results are limited to infinitesimal deformations and do not guaranty strict thermodynamic stability of spherical nuclei. In particular, they does not exclude that substantially non-spherical nuclei (so-called pasta phase) represent thermodynamic equilibrium state of the densest layers of neutron star crust. Rather our results points that spherical nuclei can be metastable even if they are not energetically favourable and the timescale of transformation of spherical nuclei to the pasta phases should be estimated subsequently.

## Key claim

Within a perturbative compressible liquid drop model (CLDM) approach, spherical nuclei in the inner crust of neutron stars are stable with respect to infinitesimal quadrupole deformations when their number density corresponds to the equilibrium value, contradicting the earlier assumption that they become unstable at a filling factor of 0.125. However, the authors stress that this result is limited to infinitesimal deformations and does not guarantee strict thermodynamic stability, meaning spherical nuclei can be metastable even if non-spherical "pasta" phases are energetically favored.

## Method

The authors applied a CLDM, following the approach of Bohr and Wheeler (1939), to calculate the change in energy when a spherical nucleus is deformed into a spheroid with semi-axes \(R(1+\varepsilon)\) and \(R/\sqrt{1+\varepsilon}\), where \(\varepsilon\) is an infinitesimal deformation parameter. The Wigner-Seitz cell was assumed to remain spherical, and the energy change was calculated analytically up to second order in \(\varepsilon\), incorporating the effects of the degenerate electron background and the electrostatic potential it induces. The analysis was performed for both an equilibrium crust (where the virial theorem of Baym et al. (1971) applies) and a non-equilibrium crust, using the CLDM of GC20 which includes neutron skin effects.

## Key numbers

*   The inner crust of neutron stars extends from a density \(\rho_\mathrm{drip} \approx 4.3 \times 10^{11}\) g/cm\(^3\) to \(\sim 10^{14}\) g/cm\(^3\).
*   For an equilibrium crust, the energy change for a deformation is \(\delta E = \frac{3}{5}\frac{Z^2e^2}{R} \left(\frac{3}{5}-\frac{6}{5}u^{\frac{1}{3}}+\frac{9}{10}u \right)\varepsilon^2\), which remains positive for any filling factor \(u \in (0,1)\).
*   For a non-equilibrium crust with the SLy4 potential, the nuclei number density must be lower than the equilibrium value by a factor of 2.2-2.4 to ensure instability at a baryon number density \(n_b > 0.047\) fm\(^{-3}\).
*   The earlier, now-contradicted, instability criterion predicted spherical nuclei to become absolutely unstable when the filling factor \(u\) reaches a value of \(1/8 = 0.125\).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Pethick and Ravenhall (1995), who applied the Bohr-Wheeler (1939) instability criterion for isolated nuclei to the inner crust, predicting instability at a filling factor of 0.125. The authors correct the analysis of Kobyakov and Wcislak (2021), which also suggested enhanced stability but was based on what the authors argue are inaccurate boundary conditions. The results show that spherical nuclei can be metastable up to filling factors of \(\sim 0.2\), where pasta phases become energetically favorable as shown by Hashimoto et al. (1984), opening the task of estimating the timescale for the transition from spherical to non-spherical shapes.
