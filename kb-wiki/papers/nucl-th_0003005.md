# On the choice of colliding beams to study deformation effects in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> It has been suggested that collisions between deformed shapes will lead to interesting effects on various observables such as K production and elliptic flow. Simple formulae can be written down which show how to choose the colliding beams which will maximise the effects of deformation.

# On the choice of colliding beams to study deformation effects in relativistic heavy ion collisions

## Key claim

Collisions between deformed prolate nuclei can produce significantly different physical results for tip-on-tip versus side-on-side orientations, but quantum mechanics prevents perfect alignment; the paper provides quantitative criteria for choosing beams and nuclear states that maximize the observable effects of deformation, with the caveat that even with optimal choices the alignment is imperfect.

## Method

The authors use the rotational model of deformed nuclei, expressing the ground-state wavefunction of an odd-A deformed nucleus in terms of Eulerian angles and intrinsic deformed states. They compute the expectation value of the quadrupole operator in the laboratory frame, using Clebsch-Gordan coefficients to quantify the reduction in alignment caused by quantum mechanics, and derive a reduction factor R = (I2M0|IM)(I2I0|II) that determines how well the nuclear symmetry axis can be aligned with the collision axis.

## Key numbers

- The reduction factor for alignment in the direction of the symmetry axis, for M = I, is given by (I2I0|II)^2 = I(2I-1)/[(I+1)(2I+3)], which approaches 1 as I approaches infinity.
- Ground state spins of 9/2 are available in deformed regions; the most advantageous nucleus cited is 176Lu, which has ground state spin 7.
- For odd-A nuclei, the "best" body-on-body collisions require the state |I, 1/2> with the collision axis defining the z-direction.

## Context

This work extends calculations by Shuryak (2000) and Li (2000) suggesting that tip-on-tip collisions of prolate deformed nuclei produce different results from side-on-side collisions for observables such as K production and elliptic flow. The paper provides practical guidance for experimental design, showing that using polarized beams with high-spin ground-state nuclei can enhance the occurrence of interesting deformation effects that would otherwise be buried among uninteresting events when using unpolarized beams. This enables future experiments to deliberately select beam species and polarization states to maximize sensitivity to nuclear deformation in relativistic heavy ion collisions.
