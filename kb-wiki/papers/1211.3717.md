# Two-proton removal from $^{44}$S and the structure of $^{42}$Si

> Newly-published $^{42}$Si gamma-ray spectra and a final-state-inclusive $^{42}$Si production cross section value, obtained in a higher-statistics intermediate-energy two-proton removal experiment from $^{44}$S, are considered in terms of the final-state-exclusive cross sections computed using proposed shell-model effective interactions for nuclei near N=28. Specifically, we give cross section predictions when using the two nucleon amplitudes of the two-proton overlaps $<^{42}$Si$ (J^π)\,|\,^{44}$S$>$ computed using the newly-proposed {\sc sdpf-mu} shell-model Hamiltonian. We show that these partial cross sections or their longitudinal momentum distributions should enable a less-tentative interpretation of the measured gamma-ray spectra and provide a more quantitative assessment of proposed shell-model Hamiltonians in this interesting and challenging region of the chart of nuclides.

## Key claim

The paper predicts that final-state-exclusive cross sections and longitudinal momentum distributions from the $^{44}$S($-2$p) reaction, computed using the sdpf-mu shell-model Hamiltonian, should enable a less-tentative interpretation of the measured $^{42}$Si gamma-ray spectra than the level energies alone can provide, but the predictions are conditional on the ratio of experimental to theoretical inclusive cross section being consistent with the previously observed systematics of $R_s(2N) \approx 0.5$.

## Method

The authors calculated two-proton removal cross sections for individual $^{42}$Si final states using two-nucleon amplitudes (TNAs) from shell-model wave functions computed with the sdpf-mu Hamiltonian, and separately with the sdpf-u-si interaction. The reaction was modeled as a sudden, direct removal process at 210 MeV per nucleon on a carbon target, using eikonal S-matrices from double- and single-folding optical potentials with a zero-range effective nucleon-nucleon interaction. The Woods-Saxon binding potentials for the removed protons were constrained by the evaluated 2p separation energy of 40.2(5) MeV and the shell-model excitation energies, with radius parameters fitted to Hartree-Fock (SkX) proton single-particle rms radii.

## Key numbers

* Inclusive experimental $^{42}$Si production cross section: 0.15(2) mb from the Takeuchi et al. (2012) experiment at 210 MeV per nucleon.
* Calculated inclusive cross section using sdpf-mu TNAs: 0.37 mb, giving an experimental-to-theoretical ratio $R_s(2N) = 0.41(6)$.
* Calculated inclusive cross section using sdpf-u-si TNAs: 0.69 mb.
* sdpf-mu predicted $^{42}$Si $2_1^+$ state energy: 821 keV; $4_1^+$ state energy: 2271 keV; $0_2^+$ state energy: 2573 keV.
* Tentatively assigned $^{42}$Si $4_1^+$ state from experiment: 2173(14) keV, decaying via a 1431(11) keV gamma ray in coincidence with the 742(8) keV $2_1^+ \rightarrow 0^+$ transition.
* sdpf-mu predicts 50% of the inclusive cross section passes through the $2_1^+$ state, consistent with the earlier GANIL measurement reporting 44(10)% of events accompanied by a $2_1^+$ to ground-state gamma ray (Bastin et al., 2007).
* sdpf-mu predicts the ground state (0.186 mb) and first excited $0_2^+$ state (0.103 mb) have the strongest individual partial cross sections.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the Takeuchi et al. (2012) experiment at RIKEN RIBF, which measured $^{42}$Si gamma-ray spectra following two-proton removal from $^{44}$S, by providing quantitative reaction-theory predictions for the sdpf-mu shell-model Hamiltonian of Utsuno et al. (2012). The calculations enable a more detailed assessment of the sdpf-mu interaction than comparisons of level energies alone, and are consistent with the earlier lower-statistics GANIL measurement by Bastin et al. (2007). The predicted strong population of an excited $0_2^+$ state near 2.5 MeV and the distinct momentum distribution widths for $4_1^+$ versus $0_2^+$ states provide specific fingerprints for future experimental tests, while the authors note that complementary one-proton removal from $^{43}$P could also contribute to the $^{42}$Si spectroscopy discussion.
