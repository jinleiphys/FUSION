# HBT Interferometry: Historical Perspective

> I review the history of HBT interferometry, since its discovery in the mid 1950's, up to the recent developments and results from BNL/RHIC experiments. I focus the discussion on the contributions to the subject given by members of our Brazilian group.

## Key claim

This review argues that HBT interferometry, originally developed for stellar radius measurements, has been successfully adapted to high-energy nuclear collisions, but the simple Gaussian parametrization of the correlation function is insufficient for expanding sources; the interpretation requires model-dependent formalisms that account for dynamics, resonance decays, and finite statistics effects that prevent the correlation function from reaching its ideal maximum value of 2 at zero relative momentum.

## Method

The paper reviews the historical development of HBT interferometry from its 1950s origins in radio-astronomy through its application to high-energy collisions, focusing on contributions from the Brazilian group. The author describes the theoretical framework using the Covariant Current Ensemble formalism, where the two-particle correlation function is expressed as \(C(q,K)=1+\lambda |G(q,K)|^2/[G(k_1,k_1)G(k_2,k_2)]\), and discusses how the group applied one-dimensional Landau hydrodynamics with a QGP-to-hadron phase transition to model expanding sources, comparing predictions with experimental data from CERN/ISR and CERN/SPS.

## Key numbers

- The original GGLP experiment at Bevalac/LBL used \(\bar{p}p\) collisions at 1.05 GeV/c, discovering identical pion angular correlations as a consequence of Bose-Einstein statistics.
- The Gaussian parametrization \(C(Q^2)=1+e^{-Q^2 r^2}\) was introduced by Goldhaber, Goldhaber, Lee and Pais (1959), where \(Q^2=-(k_1-k_2)^2\).
- The chaoticity parameter \(\lambda\) was introduced by Deutschmann et al. (1978) to reduce systematic errors in Gaussian fits, since experimental data never reached the ideal maximum of 2 at \(q=0\).
- In the Kopylov-Podgoretski\u\i-Grishin parametrization, the correlation function takes the form \(C(k_1,k_2)=1\pm[2J_1(q_T R)/(q_T R)]^2[1+(q_0\tau)^2]^{-1}\), where \(q_T\) is transverse momentum difference and \(\tau\) is the lifetime parameter.
- The Gamow factor for Coulomb final state interaction is \(\Upsilon(q)=q_c/q/(\exp(q_c/q)-1)\) with \(q_c=2\pi\alpha m\), where \(\alpha\) is the fine structure constant.
- In the Brazilian group's hydrodynamical model, the initial temperature was related to fireball mass by \(T_0=0.0989\sqrt{M}\), with the critical temperature \(T_c\) assumed to be of order the pion mass.
- The group compared predictions with CERN/ISR data at \(\sqrt{s}=53\) GeV, successfully describing two-pion and two-kaon correlation data without introducing the \(\lambda\) parameter (fixing \(\lambda=1\)).
- For NA35 O+Au collisions at 200 GeV/nuclear, the group showed that resonance decays (from \(\omega\), \(\eta\), \(\eta'\)) could explain preliminary data without assuming QGP formation, using parameters from the ATTILA version of the Lund model.

## Context

This review extends the historical narrative of HBT interferometry from its discovery by Hanbury-Brown and Twiss (1950s) through the GGLP discovery of identical pion correlations in high-energy collisions (1959), and covers subsequent developments by Kopylov, Podgoretski\u\i, and Grishin (1970s), Cocconi (1974), and the introduction of the chaoticity parameter by Deutschmann et al. (1978). The Brazilian group's work, starting with Padula's PhD thesis suggested by Bjorken (1983), applied Landau hydrodynamics to study expansion effects in HBT, showing that the correlation function depends on the average momentum of the pair due to dynamical effects, and later demonstrated that resonance decays could mimic QGP signatures in NA35 data, contradicting the earlier interpretation by Bertsch (1988). The review enables future work by emphasizing that static Gaussian fits are inadequate for expanding sources and that more powerful formalisms (Covariant Current Ensemble, Wigner function methods) are necessary for interpreting HBT data from RHIC experiments.
