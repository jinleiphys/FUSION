# Challenges to the Good-Walker paradigm in coherent and incoherent photoproduction

> High-energy vector meson photoproduction is an important tool for studying the partonic structure of matter at low Bjorken$-x$. In the Good-Walker (GW) paradigm, the cross-section $dσ/dt$ for coherent production of vector mesons or other final states, depends the average transverse distribution of gluons, while the incoherent cross-section depends on fluctuations in the nuclear structure, due to variations in nucleon positions, and/or gluonic hot spots. However, predictions of the the GW paradigm seemingly conflict with data from multiple experiments which observe coherent production of vector mesons accompanied by nuclear excitation, or in peripheral relativistic heavy-ion collisions. These data are consistent with a simpler, semi-classical approach. We will discuss this contradiction and explore how and why GW fails. We will also contrast the significant differences in incoherent photoproduction on $^{197}$Au and $^{208}$Pb targets in the GW approach with the much smaller expected differences in their low$-x$ gluon content.

# Challenges to the Good-Walker paradigm in coherent and incoherent photoproduction

## Key claim

The Good-Walker (GW) paradigm fails to explain coherent vector meson photoproduction accompanied by nuclear excitation in ultra-peripheral collisions and coherent photoproduction in peripheral heavy-ion collisions, despite being the standard framework for interpreting such data. A semi-classical approach based on adding amplitudes from individual nucleons is more effective for these cases, though the two approaches make similar predictions for coherent production while diverging significantly on incoherent production.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental data from STAR and ALICE on coherent photoproduction of ρ, ω, ρ′, and J/ψ mesons in ultra-peripheral collisions where nuclear breakup occurs, and coherent J/ψ photoproduction in peripheral heavy-ion collisions down to about 30% centrality. It compares these observations against the Good-Walker formalism (Eqs. 1-3) and a semi-classical approach (Eq. 4) where amplitudes from individual nucleons are summed with phase factors exp(i·k·x_i). The analysis examines how multi-photon exchange, bremsstrahlung, and lepton pair production violate the GW assumption of a single incident particle, and considers the implications of nuclear excitation energy thresholds for incoherent production at low |t|.

## Key numbers

- For lead-lead collisions at the LHC, the cross-section for two-photon production of e⁺e⁻ pairs is about 200,000 barns
- For near-grazing lead-lead collisions (b ≈ 2R_A), the average number of produced e⁺e⁻ pairs is more than one
- The energies required to eject nucleons from ¹⁹⁷Au and ²⁰⁸Pb range from 5.27 to 8.07 MeV
- The minimum initial nucleon recoil momentum corresponding to these thresholds is 100 to 125 MeV/c
- ²⁰⁸Pb has its lowest lying excited state at 2.6 MeV; ¹⁹⁷Au has its lowest lying state at 77 keV with a 1.9 nsec lifetime
- For 110 GeV/n gold nuclei at the EIC, the characteristic decay distance for the 77 keV state is about 70 m
- STAR ρ photoproduction data at large |t| (0.2 < |t| < 0.45 GeV²) was fit to a dipole form factor consistent with a single proton target
- In the Sartre generator, the downturn reduces the cross-section for incoherent φ electroproduction by a factor of 2-3 as |t| → 0 compared to the exponential baseline

## Context

This work extends critiques of the Good-Walker paradigm (Good 1960, Miettinen 1978) by confronting it with experimental data from STAR (2002-2022) and ALICE (2020-2022) that show coherent photoproduction occurs even when nuclear targets break up. The semi-classical approach follows earlier work by Baltz (2002) on multi-photon exchange and has been implemented in Monte Carlo generators like STARlight (Klein 2016) and eSTARlight (Lomnitz 2018), which use nuclear and nucleon form factors to predict p_T spectra. The paper contrasts these with the GW-based Sartre generator (Toll 2012, 2013) and BeAGLE (Chang 2022). The analysis enables future work on quantifying multi-photon exchange effects, developing better models of nuclear excitation from Pomeron exchange, and informing detector designs for the Electron-Ion Collider.
