# Positron production in collision of heavy nuclei

> We consider the electromagnetic production of positron in collision of slow heavy nuclei, with the simultaneously produced electron captured by one of the nuclei. The cross-section of the discussed process exceeds essentially the cross-section of $e^+e^-$ production.

## Key claim

The cross-section for positron production (with the electron captured by a nucleus) in slow heavy-ion collisions is proportional to the nuclear velocity \(v \simeq 0.1\) and scales as \(Z^2 \alpha^2 v / m^2\), exceeding the cross-section for free \(e^+e^-\) pair production by roughly three orders of magnitude.

## Method

The author calculates the total cross-section from the Feynman diagram where a virtual photon is exchanged between the two colliding heavy nuclei, producing an electron-positron pair; the electron is immediately captured by one nucleus while the positron is treated as free. The amplitude is evaluated in the limit of infinite nuclear mass \(M \to \infty\), with the nuclear velocity taken as \(v \simeq 0.1\), and the cross-section is obtained by including factors for the two possible capturing nuclei and the two positron polarizations.

## Key numbers

- Total cross-section for positron production: \(\sigma \simeq 10^{-22} \, \text{cm}^2\) for slow heavy nuclei with \(v \simeq 0.1\).
- This exceeds the cross-section for \(e^+e^-\) production in uranium-uranium collisions, which is close to \(10^{-25} \, \text{cm}^2\).
- The cross-section formula is \(\sigma = 4 Z^2 \alpha^2 v / m^2\), with \(v \simeq 0.1\) assumed for the nuclear velocity.

## Context

This work revisits the problem of positron production in slow heavy-ion collisions, previously addressed in several papers (e.g., the references cited as [1-3] in the text). The author provides a simpler direct solution and finds a cross-section substantially larger than that for free \(e^+e^-\) pair production, which may have implications for experimental searches for positron signals in low-energy heavy-ion collisions. The result enables quantitative predictions for future experiments with uranium or other heavy nuclei.
