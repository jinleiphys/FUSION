# Semi-relativistic antisymmetrized molecular dynamics for energetic neutron production in intermediate energy heavy-ion reactions

> Relativistic corrections have been made in the non-relativistic antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) simulations to apply to the high energy neutron production in the $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C and $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C collisions at incident energies of 290 and 400 MeV/nucleon. The corrections are made in kinematics alone and no nucleon-nucleon inelastic scatterings nor meson productions are taken into account, and AMD with the relativistic corrections is called semi-relativistic AMD. The three-nucleon collision (3NC) and Fermi boost in the collision processes are taken into account in the non-relativistic AMD. Since the relativistic corrections tend to compensate in each other, the difference between the semi-relativistic and non-relativistic results become small. High energy tails of the available experimental neutron double differential cross sections, especially at larger angles, are well reproduced by AMD with the 3NC term both with non-relativistic and semi-relativistic simulations. These results indicate that the high energy neutrons are dominantly produced by the 3NC process in this incident energy range.

## Key claim

The authors show that semi-relativistic corrections to non-relativistic antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) simulations tend to compensate each other, so the difference between semi-relativistic and non-relativistic results is small for neutron production in light heavy-ion collisions at 290 and 400 MeV/nucleon; the high-energy neutron tails are dominantly produced by the three-nucleon collision (3NC) process, not by relativistic kinematics.

## Method

The authors modified the non-relativistic AMD framework by adding a Fermi boost and a three-nucleon collision (3NC) term, following the Bonasera formulation. Relativistic corrections were applied in three kinematic steps: boosting the input system from the laboratory frame to the center-of-mass frame using relativistic kinematics, performing the non-relativistic AMD simulation, then boosting back and calculating neutron kinetic energies relativistically. No nucleon-nucleon inelastic scatterings or meson productions were included. The simulations used the Gogny effective interaction and a constant 3NC cross section of 40 mb, with about one to two million events per reaction system.

## Key numbers

- Incident energies: 290 and 400 MeV/nucleon for $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C, and 290 MeV/nucleon for $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C
- Neutron energies measured up to about 900 MeV in the experiments
- Fermi momentum parameter $P_{PF} = 250$ MeV/c used in the Fermi boost
- Constant 3NC cross section of 40 mb used in the simulations
- Width parameter $\nu = 0.16$ fm$^{-2}$ for the Gaussian wave packets
- Impact parameter range b = 0-8 fm for event generation
- Relativistic calculation of neutron kinetic energy is reduced about 20% at $E_{kin} \ge 200$ MeV compared to non-relativistic calculation
- At 290 MeV/nucleon $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C, Satoh's data at 75$^{\circ}$ and 90$^{\circ}$ show about twice larger cross sections than the calculations
- At 400 MeV/nucleon $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C, experimental data at 5$^{\circ}$ and 10$^{\circ}$ show peaks with 2-3 times less yields than simulations

## Context

This work extends the AMD framework with 3NC previously applied to proton spectra up to 137 MeV/nucleon by Wada (2017) and the Fermi boost approach by Lin (2016), now applied to higher incident energies (290-400 MeV/nucleon) for neutron production. The 3NC process follows the approach of Bonasera (1994) in the extended Boltzmann-Nordheim-Vlasov model. The experimental data come from Iwata (2001) and Satoh (2011) measured at the HIMAC facility for cancer therapy applications. The results enable future studies at even higher incident energies (560-600 MeV/nucleon) where relativistic effects may become more significant, and suggest that non-relativistic AMD remains valid in this energy range for light systems.
