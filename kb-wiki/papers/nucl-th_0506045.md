# Momentum spectra, anisotropic flow, and ideal fluids

> If the matter produced in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions reaches thermal equilibrium, its subsequent evolution follows the laws of ideal fluid dynamics. We show that general predictions can be made on this basis alone, irrespective of the details of the hydrodynamical model. We derive several scaling rules for momentum spectra and anisotropic flow (in particular the elliptic flow, v2, and the hexadecupole flow, v4) of identified particles. Comparison with existing data is briefly discussed, and qualitative predictions are made for LHC.

# Momentum spectra, anisotropic flow, and ideal fluids

## Key claim

If the matter produced in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions reaches thermal equilibrium and evolves as an ideal fluid, general scaling rules for momentum spectra and anisotropic flow coefficients (v2, v4) can be derived without assuming a specific hydrodynamical model, provided one distinguishes between "slow" particles (whose velocity can be matched by the fluid) and "fast" particles (whose velocity exceeds the maximum fluid velocity). The results are expected to hold only in the limit where collective expansion dominates over thermal motion (small freeze-out temperature T), and the authors note that at RHIC this approximation is poor for pions but may be reasonable for kaons and heavier hadrons.

## Method

The authors start from the Cooper-Frye freeze-out prescription for single-particle momentum spectra from an ideal fluid and perform a saddle-point integration of the freeze-out integral in the limit of small freeze-out temperature T. They separate particles into two classes: slow particles, for which the fluid velocity somewhere on the freeze-out surface matches the particle velocity (so the minimum of p^μ u_μ equals the particle mass m), and fast particles, for which the minimum occurs at the maximum fluid velocity u_max. For each class, they evaluate the integral analytically to obtain explicit formulas for the momentum spectrum and the anisotropic flow coefficients v2 and v4.

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out temperature at RHIC: T ~ 100 MeV (from fits in Retiere (2003) and Hirano (2004))
- Inverse slope parameter for pions at RHIC: T_eff = 210 MeV (Adler, 2003)
- Best-fit value of radial flow rapidity at RHIC: ρ0 ≃ 0.9, corresponding to u_max = sinh ρ0 ~ 1 (Retiere, 2003)
- For fast particles at RHIC, the saddle-point condition (Eq. 6) is satisfied by at least a factor of 2 for pions with p_t > 0.7 GeV/c, kaons with p_t > 1.2 GeV/c, and (anti)protons with p_t > 1.8 GeV/c
- The parameter V2 (Fourier coefficient of u_max(φ)) is of order 4% for semi-central Au-Au collisions at RHIC
- For v4 at RHIC, the condition for the universal relation v4 = v2^2/2 holds for pions with p_t > 0.8 GeV/c
- The STAR Collaboration's experimental v4 value is a factor of 2 to 3 higher than the prediction from Eq. (13)
- In a numerical 3-d hydrodynamical calculation, v4/v2^2 approaches 0.63 (not 0.5) at large p_t for midcentral collisions (b = 8 fm)

## Context

This work extends the blast-wave parameterization approach (Huovinen, 2001; Retiere, 2003) by deriving general scaling rules that do not assume a specific fluid velocity profile, while also making the specific assumption that collective motion dominates over thermal motion. The results contradict the earlier claim by Kolb (2003) that v4 is a sensitive probe of initial conditions; instead, the authors find a universal result that can be used as a probe of ideal fluid behavior. The paper enables experimental tests of whether the matter created at RHIC and LHC behaves as an ideal fluid, with the prediction that the ratio v4/(v2)^2 should be lower at LHC than at RHIC.
