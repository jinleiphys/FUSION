# Charm Production in Hadronic and Heavy-Ion Collisions at RHIC and LHC Energies to $O(α_s^3)$

> We present results on rapidity and transverse momentum distributions of inclusive charm quark production in hadronic and heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC energies, including the next-to-leading order, $O(α_s^3)$, radiative corrections and the nuclear shadowing effect. We determine the hadronic and the {\it effective} (in-medium) K-factor for the differential and total inclusive charm cross sections. We find the fraction of central and inelastic events that contain at least one charm quark pair at LHC energies and obtain the effective $A$-dependence of the inclusive charm production in proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions at RHIC and in nucleus-nucleus collisions at the LHC. We discuss theoretical uncertainties inherent in our calculation. In particular, we show how different extrapolations of gluon density in a nucleon {\em and} in a nucleus to the low-$x$ region introduce large theoretical uncertainty in the calculation of charm production at LHC energies.

## Key claim

The next-to-leading order (NLO, O(α_s^3)) radiative corrections and nuclear shadowing effects together significantly modify charm production cross sections and distributions at RHIC and LHC energies, but the theoretical uncertainty from different extrapolations of the gluon density to low x introduces a factor of about 6 uncertainty in the total cross section at LHC energies, which the abstract mentions but does not quantify.

## Method

The authors computed inclusive charm quark production in hadronic (p-p) and heavy-ion (p-Au, Au-Au) collisions at RHIC (√s = 200 GeV per nucleon) and LHC (√s = 7 TeV per nucleon) energies, including O(α_s^3) radiative corrections to the partonic cross sections and nuclear shadowing effects on parton distributions. They used several parton distribution function sets (MRS D0, MRS D--, MRS A) with different low-x behavior, two choices of renormalization scale (Q = m_c and Q = 2m_c), and a charm quark mass m_c = 1.5 GeV. Nuclear geometry was treated with Woods-Saxon and Gaussian density distributions, and nuclear shadowing was implemented via a parametrization fitted to deep inelastic lepton-nucleus data.

## Key numbers

- Total cross section for charm production in p-p collisions at RHIC: 180 μb – 210 μb (for Q² = m_c²) and 112 μb – 126 μb (for Q² = 4m_c²)
- Total cross section for charm production in p-p collisions at LHC: 2.4 mb – 15.3 mb (for Q² = m_c²) and 1.4 mb – 9.2 mb (for Q² = 4m_c²)
- Hadronic K-factor (NLO/LO ratio) for total cross section: 3.7 at E_beam = 50 GeV, 2.2 at E_beam = 2 TeV; between 2 and 2.1 at RHIC; between 2.3 and 2.8 at LHC
- Effective A-dependence exponent α_eff for central (inelastic) Au-Au collisions: 1.27 (1.94) at RHIC and 1.2 (1.87) at LHC
- Fraction of central events containing at least one charm quark pair: 98% at RHIC, 99% at LHC
- Fraction of inelastic events containing at least one charm quark pair: 38% at RHIC, 54% – 72% at LHC
- Nuclear shadowing suppression of parton distribution in gold at x ≤ 10⁻⁴: about 62%
- Effective K-factor for p_T distribution at LHC: varies from 0.6 at p_T = 0.7 GeV to 4.6 at p_T = 6 GeV (hadronic K-factor varies from 1.4 to 7.6 in same range)
- Effective K-factor for rapidity distribution at LHC in central region: about 1.1 for Au-Au collisions (hadronic K-factor about 2.5)
- Contribution from initial-state gluons: about 95% at RHIC and 99% at LHC

## Context

This work extends NLO QCD calculations of heavy quark production (pioneered by Nason, Dawson, and Ellis) to heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC energies, incorporating nuclear shadowing effects. The results provide the QCD background needed to evaluate whether enhanced charm production can serve as a signal for quark-gluon plasma formation, as proposed by Shuryak and others. The large theoretical uncertainties at LHC energies, especially from low-x gluon extrapolations, motivate future measurements at RHIC and LHC to constrain parton distributions in nuclei and test perturbative QCD in the small-x, small-Q² regime.
