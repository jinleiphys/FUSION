# Hadrons and Few-Body Physics

> We present a selection of topics with an interplay of hadron and few-body physics. This includes few-nucleon systems, light hypernuclei and quark dynamics for baryons and multiquarks. It is stressed that standard quark models predict very few stable multiquarks.

## Key claim

The paper argues that standard quark models predict very few stable multiquark states, with stability restricted to very peculiar configurations, and that many experimental candidates are actually resonances rather than bound states.

## Method

The author reviews the application of few-body techniques to hadron physics, including hyperspherical expansions, Faddeev-type equations, variational methods with correlated Gaussians, and Born-Oppenheimer approximations for doubly-heavy baryons. The discussion covers the color-dependence of quark potentials, including the "1/2 rule" linking meson and baryon interactions, the Y-shaped string potential for baryons, and the double-Y string for tetraquarks, as well as the stabilization method and complex scaling for treating resonances.

## Key numbers

- The Thomas collapse analysis inferred from the ratio E3/E2 that the Yukawa meson should be lighter than 200 MeV
- For the harmonic oscillator model of baryons, the naive diquark approximation introduces an error of a factor sqrt(2/3) in the leading term
- The positronium molecule (M^+M^+m^-m^-) becomes unstable when 1/2.2 less than M/m less than 2.2
- For tetraquarks in the chromo-harmonic Hamiltonian, the potential in the TT color state is V_TT = 3/4(x^2+y^2) + 1/2 z^2, while the diquark approximation replaces 3/4 by 1/2
- The H = uuddss hexaquark was predicted bound well below the baryon-baryon threshold under SU(3)F symmetry, but lattice calculations find its mass near the threshold

## Context

This review extends the long tradition of applying few-body methods to hadron physics, from the early work of Thomas (1935) and Efimov (1970) on few-nucleon systems, through the quark model calculations of Greenberg (1964) and Dalitz (1965), to contemporary studies of multiquarks. The paper connects to specific prior work including Jaffe's (1976, 1977) chromomagnetic analysis of tetraquarks and the H dibaryon, Gavela et al. (1978) on explicit tetraquark calculations, and the LHCb pentaquark discoveries (2015, 2019). It enables future work on the continuum spectrum of multiquark resonances using techniques like complex scaling and stabilization methods, and highlights the need for careful few-body treatments rather than simplified diquark approximations.
