# Nuclear clustering and the electron screening puzzle

> Electron screening changes appreciably the magnitude of astrophysical nuclear reactions within stars. This effect is also observed in laboratory experiments on Earth, where atomic electrons are present in the nuclear targets. Theoretical models were developed over the past 30 years and experimental measurements have been carried out to study electron screening in thermonuclear reactions. None of the theoretical models were able to explain the high values of the experimentally determined screening potentials. We explore the possibility that the "electron screening puzzle" is due to nuclear clusterization and polarization effects in the fusion reactions. We will discuss the supporting arguments for this scenario.

## Key claim

The "electron screening puzzle", where experimentally measured screening potentials exceed theoretical predictions, may actually be a nuclear structure effect caused by clusterization and polarization of nuclei during fusion, rather than an electron screening phenomenon.

## Method

The authors analyze experimental data for thermonuclear reactions involving light nuclei and compare measured screening potentials to the adiabatic limit. They argue that reactions involving cluster-like nuclei (e.g., \(^6\)Li, \(^7\)Li, \(^9\)Be, \(^{10}\)B, \(^{11}\)B) show systematically larger discrepancies than reactions with non-cluster-like nuclei. They support this with a model where the nuclear wavefunction includes cluster configurations (e.g., d+\(\alpha\) for \(^6\)Li), and the Coulomb penetrability for each cluster configuration is calculated separately, with polarization and orientation effects enhancing fusion at low energies.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing electron screening puzzle, where experimental screening potentials exceed the adiabatic limit of Assenbaum (1987) and Rolfs (1988) by factors of 1.5 to 3 for cluster-like nuclei. The authors propose that nuclear clustering and polarization, analogous to the Oppenheimer-Phillips effect (Greife, 1995), can explain the discrepancy without invoking new electron screening physics. This enables future experimental studies of reactions such as \(^6\)Li + \(^6\)Li, \(^7\)Li + \(^7\)Li, and \(^9\)Be + \(^3\)He at thermonuclear energies to test the cluster hypothesis, and motivates ab-initio calculations of cluster pre-formation amplitudes as called for in NavQua (2013) and Xu (2017).
