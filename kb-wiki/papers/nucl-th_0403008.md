# Acceptance Corrections and Extreme-Independent Models in Relativistic Heavy Ion Collisions

> Kopeliovich's suggestion [nucl-th/0306044] to perform nuclear geometry (Glauber) calculations using different cross sections according to the experimental configuration is quite different from the standard practice of the last 20 years and leads to a different nuclear geometry definition for each experiment. The standard procedure for experimentalists is to perform the nuclear geometry calculation using the total inelastic N-N cross section, which results in a common nuclear geometry definition for all experiments. The incomplete acceptance of individual experiments is taken into account by correcting the detector response for the probability of measuring zero for an inelastic collision, which can often be determined experimentally. This clearly separates experimental issues such as different acceptances from theoretical issues which should apply in general to all experiments. Extreme-Independent models are used to illustrate the conditions for which the two methods give consistent or inconsistent results.

## Key claim

Kopeliovich's suggestion to perform nuclear geometry (Glauber) calculations using different cross sections according to experimental configuration leads to a different nuclear geometry definition for each experiment, whereas the standard procedure of using the total inelastic N-N cross section with acceptance corrections applied at the detector level works in general and keeps a common nuclear geometry definition for all experiments.

## Method

The paper uses extreme-independent models (Wounded Nucleon Model, Wounded Projectile Nucleon Model, Number of Collisions Model, and Additive Quark Model) to compare two approaches: (1) performing the nuclear geometry calculation with the total inelastic N-N cross section and correcting the detector response for the probability of measuring zero for an inelastic collision, versus (2) using the experimentally detected cross section directly in the nuclear geometry calculation. The author derives the mathematical formalism for the Number of Collisions Model, showing how the probability p0 that an N-N inelastic collision produces no signal on the detector enters the convolution of the E_T distribution, and then tests whether the two methods yield identical results under different model assumptions.

## Key numbers

- The total inelastic N-N cross section at RHIC for sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV is 42 mb, used by all four RHIC experiments for their nuclear geometry calculations.
- Kopeliovich suggests using the N-N non-diffractive cross section of 30 mb for the PHOBOS and PHENIX detectors instead of 42 mb.
- The ratio 30/42 = 0.714 corresponds to 1-p0, where p0 is the probability for an inelastic N-N collision to produce zero signal on the detector.
- At AGS energies, 30 mb was used for the inelastic N-N cross section, corresponding to a nucleon-nucleon mean free path of approximately 2.2 fm at nuclear density.

## Context

This paper addresses a suggestion by Kopeliovich (2003) to tailor nuclear geometry calculations to individual experimental acceptances, which contradicts the standard practice established over the previous 20 years by experiments such as E802 (1987), WA80, and NA35. The work extends the formalism of the Wounded Nucleon Model (Bialas, Bleszynski, Czyz, 1976) and the Wounded Projectile Nucleon Model (E802, 1987) to show that acceptance corrections should be applied at the detector level rather than by modifying the input cross section to the Glauber calculation. This enables straightforward comparisons among the four RHIC experiments (PHENIX, PHOBOS, STAR, BRAHMS) and preserves the possibility of directly measuring projectile participants using Zero Degree Calorimeters in future experiments.
