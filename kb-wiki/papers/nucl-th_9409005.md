# Study of 3he(e,e') Longitudinal Response Functions with the Integral-Transform Method

> The method of integral transforms is first applied for studying the $^3$He longitudinal response functions. The transforms are calculated from localized bound-state-type solutions to an inhomogenous Schrödinger-type three-body equation. Several versions of local $s$-wave spin-dependent potentials supplemented with a singlet $p$-wave potential and with the proton-proton Coulomb interaction are used as a two-nucleon input. The conventional charge density operator is utilized. The three-body equations are solved with a high acuracy. It is found that the contribution of the $T=3/2$ final states to the problem is suppressed and it amounts about 15\%. This might be ascribed to symmetry requirements. The contributions of the $p$-wave $NN$ interaction and of the Coulomb interaction are found to amount several per cent. Uncertainty due to different choices of $s$-wave $NN$ forces is of a similar magnitude provided that the low-energy $NN$ data are properly described. The results are compared with the integral transforms of the experimental response functions. For $q=300$ MeV/c experimental and theoretical results coincide within their uncertainties. For $q=500$ MeV/c a noticeable difference is detected.

## Key claim

The integral-transform method is applied for the first time to study the 3He longitudinal response functions, and the authors find that for q=300 MeV/c the experimental and theoretical results coincide within their uncertainties, while for q=500 MeV/c a noticeable difference is detected. The abstract's claim of agreement at 300 MeV/c is qualified by the need to account for 5% systematic uncertainties in the experimental data and comparable theoretical uncertainties.

## Method

The authors solve an inhomogeneous Schrodinger-type three-body equation for localized bound-state-type solutions using the hyperspherical expansion method, with complete convergence achieved up to hyperspherical momentum K_max=30 and orbital angular momentum L_max=8. The integral transforms of the response functions are calculated from these solutions using several versions of local s-wave spin-dependent NN potentials (MT(I+III), S2, S3, EH) supplemented with a realistic singlet p-wave potential and the proton-proton Coulomb interaction, employing the conventional charge density operator with free-nucleon form factors. The calculated transforms are compared directly with integral transforms obtained from experimental data at q=300 and 500 MeV/c rather than inverting the transforms to obtain the response functions themselves.

## Key numbers

- The T=3/2 final state contribution to the integral transform ranges between 12% and 17% for all sigma values and both q values considered (300 and 500 MeV/c)
- At q=300 MeV/c, the contributions of the p-wave NN interaction and the Coulomb interaction do not exceed several percent or the standard uncertainties in the experimental Phi functions
- For q=500 MeV/c, the p-wave and Coulomb contributions are even much smaller than at 300 MeV/c
- The maximum number of coupled basis states equaled 861 when solving the inhomogeneous equations
- Extrapolated values from K_max=30 to K_max=infinity differ from the K_max=30 results by less than 1%
- The best alpha values for fitting the high-energy tails of experimental spectra range between about 4 and 5 for all q values considered

## Context

This work extends the integral-transform method, previously developed by Efros (1985) and Efros, Leidemann, and Orlandini (1993), from the deuteron case to the A=3 system, and represents the first microscopic study of 3He longitudinal response functions using this approach. The only prior direct calculation of these response functions with full final-state interaction was performed by van Meijgaard and Tjon (1992), who used a local s-wave central potential without higher partial waves or Coulomb interaction. The results enable future application of the method to the alpha-particle case and demonstrate that the conventional charge density operator works at q=300 MeV/c but shows deviations at q=500 MeV/c that may be attributed to relativistic effects.
