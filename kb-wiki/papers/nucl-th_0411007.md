# Statistical hadronization with resonances

> We introduce the equilibrium and non-equilibrium statistical hadronization picture of particle production in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions. We describe the related physical reaction scenarios, and show how these can lead to quark pair yield non-equilibrium. Using the SHARE1.2 program suite we quantitatively model particle yields and ratios for RHIC-130 run. We study how experimental particle ratios can differentiate between model scenarios, and discuss in depth the importance of hadronic resonances in understanding of hadron production processes.

# Statistical hadronization with resonances

## Key claim
The paper argues that chemical equilibrium should be tested against experimental data rather than assumed, and that the statistical hadronization model with resonances can accommodate both equilibrium and non-equilibrium scenarios; however, the current RHIC-130 data cannot definitively distinguish between full non-equilibrium and strangeness-only non-equilibrium due to parameter correlations and limited precision.

## Method
The authors use the grand canonical ensemble with Fermi-Dirac and Bose-Einstein distribution functions to compute hadron yields, introducing fugacity factors that separate chemical potentials (λ) from phase-space occupancy parameters (γ) for light quarks, strange quarks, and charm quarks. They incorporate resonance decays via sequential decay chains with Clebsch-Gordan coefficients for isospin symmetry, and treat finite resonance widths using energy-dependent Breit-Wigner distributions with threshold phase-space factors. The SHARE1.2 program suite is employed to fit particle yields and ratios from the RHIC-130 run by minimizing χ², with statistical significance P used to compare models having different numbers of degrees of freedom.

## Key numbers
- Hagedorn temperature: T_H ≈ 160 MeV, where the canonical partition function diverges (Eq. \ref{hagZ})
- RHIC-130 best-fit temperature for full non-equilibrium (γ_q, γ_s vary, Γ=0): T = 133 ± 10 MeV
- RHIC-130 best-fit temperature for chemical equilibrium (γ_q = γ_s = 1, Γ=0): T = 158 ± 13 MeV
- RHIC-130 light quark occupancy in full non-equilibrium (Γ=0): γ_q = 1.66 ± 0.013
- RHIC-130 strange quark occupancy in full non-equilibrium (Γ=0): γ_s = 2.41 ± 0.61
- RHIC-130 χ²/n for full non-equilibrium (Γ=0): 0.4243 with P = 94.61% (16 data points, 5 parameters)
- RHIC-130 χ²/n for chemical equilibrium (Γ=0): 1.0255 with P = 42.25% (16 data points, 3 parameters)
- RHIC-130 χ²/n for strangeness-only non-equilibrium (γ_q=1, γ_s varies, Γ=0): 0.6067 with P = 83.85% (16 data points, 4 parameters)
- Weak decay feed-down contamination for protons at RHIC: 50-60% from hyperon decays

## Context
This work extends the Fermi-Hagedorn statistical model (Hagedorn, 1960s) by incorporating chemical non-equilibrium via quark-pair occupancy parameters γ_q and γ_s, building on earlier developments by Koch (1986) and the authors' own prior work (Letessier, Rafelski). The paper introduces the SHARE1.2 program suite as an open-source tool for standardized statistical hadronization analysis, enabling systematic comparison across different experiments and energy regimes. The results contradict the canonical strangeness suppression model (can1, can2) which predicted a rapid approach to grand canonical equilibrium with increasing participant number; SPS data at 40 and 158 GeV (spscan1, spscan2, spscan3) show this prediction disagrees decisively with experiment. The work enables future high-statistics RHIC 200 GeV data, SPS energy scans, and LHC measurements to distinguish between equilibrium and non-equilibrium hadronization scenarios, with resonance ratios such as K*/K identified as key observables for constraining freeze-out temperature independently of chemical factors.
