# Relativistic resistive dissipative magnetohydrodynamics from the relaxation time approximation

> Here we derive the relativistic resistive dissipative second-order magnetohydrodynamic evolution equations using the Boltzmann equation, thus extending our work from the previous paper \href{https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/JHEP03(2021)216}{JHEP 03 (2021) 216} where we considered the non-resistive limit. We solve the Boltzmann equation for a system of particles and antiparticles using the relaxation time approximation and the Chapman-Enskog like gradient expansion for the off-equilibrium distribution function, truncating beyond second-order. In the first order, the bulk and shear stress are independent of the electromagnetic field, however, the diffusion current, shows a dependence on the electric field. In the first order, the transport coefficients~(shear and bulk stress) are shown to be independent of the electromagnetic field. The diffusion current, however, shows a dependence on the electric field. In the second-order, the new transport coefficients that couple electromagnetic field with the dissipative quantities appear, which are different from those obtained in the 14-moment approximation~\cite{Denicol:2019iyh} in the presence of the electromagnetic field. Also we found out the various components of conductivity in this case.

## Key claim

This paper derives second-order relativistic resistive magnetohydrodynamic evolution equations from the Boltzmann equation using the relaxation time approximation, extending the authors' previous work (Panda et al., 2021) from the non-resistive (ideal MHD) limit to include finite electrical conductivity and explicit electric field dependence.

## Method

The authors solve the relativistic Boltzmann equation for a system of particles and antiparticles using the relaxation time approximation for the collision kernel and a Chapman-Enskog-like gradient expansion for the off-equilibrium distribution function, truncating beyond second order. They compute the first-order and second-order corrections to the distribution function and then evaluate the corresponding dissipative fluxes (bulk stress, shear stress, and diffusion current) by taking moments of these corrections, obtaining evolution equations for each dissipative quantity.

## Key numbers

- First-order bulk stress: Π(1) = −τ_c β_Π θ, where β_Π = (5β/3)J_42^(1)+ + X J_31^(0)+ − Y J_21^(0)−
- First-order diffusion current: V^μ_(1) = τ_c β_V (∇^μ α + β q E^μ), where β_V = (1/h)J_21^(0)− − J_21^(1)−
- First-order shear stress: π^μν_(1) = 2τ_c β_π σ^μν, where β_π = β J_42^(1)+
- The expansion parameters are Kn = τ_c ∂_μ, χ = qBτ_c/T, and ξ = qEτ_c/T

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous non-resistive MHD derivation (Panda et al., 2021) by including finite electrical conductivity, addressing the inconsistency noted by Denicol et al. (2019) that taking σ → ∞ while keeping other transport coefficients finite is problematic. The resistive MHD framework enables study of magnetic reconnection and finite electric field effects in heavy-ion collisions, with applications to chiral magnetic effect studies (Kharzeev et al., 2007; Fukushima et al., 2008) and condensed matter systems such as Dirac and Weyl semimetals. The transport coefficients obtained differ from those in the 14-moment approximation (Denicol et al., 2019) in the presence of electromagnetic fields.
