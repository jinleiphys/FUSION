# PSR J0030+0451 Mass and Radius from NICER Data and Implications for the Properties of Neutron Star Matter

> Neutron stars are not only of astrophysical interest, but are also of great interest to nuclear physicists, because their attributes can be used to determine the properties of the dense matter in their cores. One of the most informative approaches for determining the equation of state of this dense matter is to measure both a star's equatorial circumferential radius $R_e$ and its gravitational mass $M$. Here we report estimates of the mass and radius of the isolated 205.53 Hz millisecond pulsar PSR J0030+0451 obtained using a Bayesian inference approach to analyze its energy-dependent thermal X-ray waveform, which was observed using the Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer (NICER). This approach is thought to be less subject to systematic errors than other approaches for estimating neutron star radii. We explored a variety of emission patterns on the stellar surface. Our best-fit model has three oval, uniform-temperature emitting spots and provides an excellent description of the pulse waveform observed using NICER. The radius and mass estimates given by this model are $R_e = 13.02^{+1.24}_{-1.06}$ km and $M = 1.44^{+0.15}_{-0.14}\ M_\odot$ (68%). The independent analysis reported in the companion paper by Riley et al. (2019) explores different emitting spot models, but finds spot shapes and locations and estimates of $R_e$ and $M$ that are consistent with those found in this work. We show that our measurements of $R_e$ and $M$ for PSR J0030$+$0451 improve the astrophysical constraints on the equation of state of cold, catalyzed matter above nuclear saturation density.

## Key claim

The mass and radius of PSR J0030+0451 are estimated from NICER data using a Bayesian inference approach that is thought to be less subject to systematic errors than other methods, but the results depend on the assumed model of the heated surface spots; the best-fit model with three oval, uniform-temperature spots yields radius and mass estimates that are consistent with the companion analysis by Riley et al. (2019), but models with two oval spots give statistically indistinguishable results.

## Method

The authors analyzed the energy-dependent thermal X-ray pulse waveform of the 205.53 Hz millisecond pulsar PSR J0030+0451 observed by NICER over 18 months, using a Bayesian inference approach to fit models of heated surface spots. They modeled the emission assuming a fully ionized hydrogen atmosphere with negligible magnetic field effects, used the oblate-star Schwarzschild-spacetime approximation for light-ray tracing, and explored models with one to four circular or oval uniform-temperature spots, ultimately preferring a three-oval-spot model. Parameter estimation was performed using Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling, with priors on compactness, mass, spot geometries, temperatures, observer inclination, hydrogen column density, and distance.

## Key numbers

- Radius: \(R_e = 13.02^{+1.24}_{-1.06}\) km (68% credible interval) from the best-fit three-oval-spot model
- Mass: \(M = 1.44^{+0.15}_{-0.14}\ M_\odot\) (68% credible interval) from the same model
- Rotation frequency of PSR J0030+0451: 205.53 Hz
- Period derivative: \(\dot{P} = 1.02 \times 10^{-20}\)
- Inferred surface magnetic field lower bound: \(B \sim 4 \times 10^8\) G (assuming centered dipole)
- Possible surface field strength at hot spots: up to \(B \sim 4 \times 10^9\) G (if offset dipole)
- Critical magnetic field for non-perturbative effects: \(B_c = 2 \times 10^9\) G
- Hydrogen column density prior range: \(0-2.5 \times 10^{20}\) cm\(^{-2}\)
- Distance prior: Gaussian with mean 0.325 kpc and standard deviation 0.017 kpc

## Context

This work extends prior radius estimates from quiescent neutron stars, thermonuclear X-ray bursts, and accretion-powered millisecond pulsars (e.g., Steiner et al. 2018, Ozel et al. 2016, Nattila et al. 2017) which typically gave radii of 10-14 km but were susceptible to systematic errors. The NICER analysis is expected to be less biased than those methods, as verified by pre-launch synthetic waveform studies (Lo et al. 2013, Miller & Lamb 2015). The results are consistent with the independent analysis by Riley et al. (2019) and improve astrophysical constraints on the equation of state of cold catalyzed matter above nuclear saturation density, enabling tighter tests of nuclear theory predictions.
