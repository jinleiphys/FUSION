# A dynamical description of neutron star crusts

> Neutron Stars are natural laboratories where fundamental properties of matter under extreme conditions can be explored. Modern nuclear physics input as well as many-body theories are valuable tools which may allow us to improve our understanding of the physics of those compact objects. In this work the occurrence of exotic structures in the outermost layers of neutron stars is investigated within the framework of a microscopic model. In this approach the nucleonic dynamics is described by a time-dependent mean field approach at around zero temperature. Starting from an initial crystalline lattice of nuclei at subnuclear densities the system evolves toward a manifold of self-organized structures with different shapes and similar energies. These structures are studied in terms of a phase diagram in density and the corresponding sensitivity to the isospin-dependent part of the equation of state and to the isotopic composition is investigated.

# A dynamical description of neutron star crusts

## Key claim

The paper claims that exotic "pasta" structures (rods, slabs, etc.) in neutron star crusts emerge naturally from a time-dependent mean-field dynamical simulation starting from an initial crystalline lattice, without assuming any particular shape symmetry. However, the results are limited to a pure mean-field (non-dissipative) description using a simple phenomenological local potential, and the authors note that more complex non-local forces and beyond-mean-field effects could strongly modify the observed structures.

## Method

The authors solve a time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) equation (with the collision term set to zero) by projecting single-particle wave functions onto a spline wavelet basis, which provides a moving basis that tracks both centroid positions and widths. Starting from an initial simple cubic lattice of Oxygen nuclei prepared in Wigner-Seitz cells, the system evolves self-consistently under periodic boundary conditions, using a zero-range density-dependent effective interaction with a soft equation of state (incompressibility modulus K∞ = 200 MeV). The resulting structures are classified using Morphological Image Analysis techniques to produce phase diagrams in the plane of threshold density versus mean density.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of infinite nuclear matter: ρ∞ = 0.145 fm⁻³
- Mean density of initial Oxygen lattice: ⟨ρ⟩ = 0.02 fm⁻³ (for the symmetric x=0.5 case shown in the crystal figure)
- Mean density for the asymmetric evolution study: ⟨ρ⟩ = 0.072 fm⁻³ (with proton fraction xp=0.1)
- Threshold density used in the 3D evolution snapshots: ρt = 0.065 fm⁻³
- Incompressibility modulus: K∞ = 200 MeV (soft EOS)
- Asymmetry parameter Ω varied in the range: -125 MeV to -75 MeV
- Symmetry energy parameter c fixed to: 20 MeV
- Force parameters: t'₀/ρ∞ = -356 MeV·fm³, t'₃/ρ∞^(ν+1) = 303 MeV·fm^(3(ν+1)) with ν=1/6
- Mean density for perturbed lattice study: ⟨ρ⟩ = 0.06 fm⁻³ (corresponding to 0.4 ρ∞)
- Threshold densities for perturbed lattice: ρt = 0.04 fm⁻³ (xp=0.5) and 0.05 fm⁻³ (xp=0.2)
- Time evolution checked over thousands of fm/c for metastable configurations

## Context

This work extends a dynamical approach (the DYWAN model, originally developed for heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies) to the study of neutron star crusts, contrasting with previous static approaches that assumed well-defined symmetries for pasta phases (pasta0, initial prediction of non-spherical composites). The results confirm the same ordering of structures (spherical → cylindrical → planar → complex → uniform) predicted by static models and recent calculations (pasta1, Watanabe 2012), but show sensitivity to the isospin-dependent part of the equation of state. The work enables future studies of transport properties in the crust and connects to astrophysical consequences such as neutrino scattering, supernova core collapse, and gravitational wave radiation, as well as to fragment formation in heavy-ion collisions (Horowitz 2004).
