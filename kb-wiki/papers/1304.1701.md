# Propagation of nuclear data uncertainties for ELECTRA burn-up calculations

> The European Lead-Cooled Training Reactor (ELECTRA) has been proposed as a training reactor for fast systems within the Swedish nuclear program. It is a low-power fast reactor cooled by pure liquid lead. In this work, we propagate the uncertainties in Pu-239 transport data to uncertainties in the fuel inventory of ELECTRA during the reactor life using the Total Monte Carlo approach (TMC). Within the TENDL project the nuclear models input parameters were randomized within their uncertainties and 740 Pu-239 nuclear data libraries were generated. These libraries are used as inputs to reactor codes, in our case SERPENT, to perform uncertainty analysis of nuclear reactor inventory during burn-up. The uncertainty in the inventory determines uncertainties in: the long-term radio-toxicity, the decay heat, the evolution of reactivity parameters, gas pressure and volatile fission product content. In this work, a methodology called fast TMC is utilized, which reduces the overall calculation time. The uncertainty in the long-term radiotoxicity, decay heat, gas pressure and volatile fission products were found to be insignificant. However, the uncertainty of some minor actinides were observed to be rather large and therefore their impact on multiple recycling should be investigated further. It was also found that, criticality benchmarks can be used to reduce inventory uncertainties due to nuclear data. Further studies are needed to include fission yield uncertainties, more isotopes, and a larger set of benchmarks.

## Key claim

The uncertainty in the long-term radiotoxicity, decay heat, gas pressure, and volatile fission products due to Pu-239 transport data uncertainties is small for the ELECTRA reactor; however, the uncertainties on some minor actinides (e.g., 11% in Cf-249) are large enough that their impact on safety parameters in multiple recycling should be investigated further.

## Method

The Total Monte Carlo (TMC) approach was applied using 740 randomized Pu-239 nuclear data libraries from the TENDL project, processed into ACE files with NJOY. These libraries were used as input to the SERPENT 1.1.17 code for coupled neutron transport and depletion calculations on a full-core 3-D model of the ELECTRA reactor, performing burnup from 0 to 55 MWd/kg over eight steps and decay calculations up to 10^6 years after end of life. A "fast TMC" methodology was employed, running 700 simulations with constant nuclear data to estimate statistical uncertainties and 740 simulations with different random files to infer the nuclear data uncertainty component.

## Key numbers

- ELECTRA is a low-power fast reactor with a thermal power of 0.5 MW.
- The fuel is composed of 60 mol% ZrN and 40 mol% PuN, with a TRU vector containing Pu-238 (3.5 at.%), Pu-239 (51.9 at.%), Pu-240 (23.8 at.%), Pu-241 (11.7 at.%), Pu-242 (7.9 at.%), and Am-241 (1.2 at.%).
- Burnup calculations were performed from 0 to 55 MWd/kg, corresponding to 7442 full power days at end of life.
- The total reactivity loss at end of life was 9800 +/- 200 pcm, with 13.8% of initial plutonium converted into fission products or americium.
- The uncertainty in Cf-249 inventory due to Pu-239 transport data was found to be 11%.
- The full core burnup calculation took 24 hours on 60 cores.
- A 25% reduction in uncertainty was achieved for Am-241 inventory by applying a chi-squared acceptance criterion based on the Jezebel criticality benchmark.

## Context

This work extends the Total Monte Carlo methodology (Koning 2008) to burnup calculations for the ELECTRA reactor, which was previously characterized by Wallenius (2013) in terms of reactivity loss and fuel composition. The study uses randomized nuclear data libraries from the TENDL project (Rochman 2006) and applies a fast TMC approach (Rochman 2007) to reduce computational requirements. The results enable further investigation of minor actinide uncertainties in multiple recycling scenarios and demonstrate that criticality benchmarks (such as Jezebel from Briggs 2006) can be used to reduce inventory uncertainties for some isotopes, though not for Pu-239 and Pu-240.
