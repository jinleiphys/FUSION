# Progress on nuclear modifications of structure functions

> We report progress on nuclear structure functions, especially on their nuclear modifications and a new tensor structure function for the deuteron. To understand nuclear structure functions is an important step toward describing nuclei and QCD matters from low to high densities and from low to high energies in terms of fundamental quark and gluon degrees of freedom beyond conventional hadron and nuclear physics. It is also practically important for understanding new phenomena in high-energy heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC. Furthermore, since systematic errors of current neutrino-oscillation experiments are dominated by uncertainties of neutrino-nucleus interactions, such studies are valuable for finding new physics beyond current framework. Next, a new tensor-polarized structure function $b_1$ is discussed for the deuteron. There was a measurement by HERMES; however, its data are inconsistent with the conventional convolution estimate based on the standard deuteron model with D-state admixture. This fact suggests that a new hadronic phenomenon should exist in the tensor-polarized deuteron at high energies, and it will be experimentally investigated at JLab from the end of 2010's.

## Key claim

Nuclear parton distribution functions (NPDFs) cannot currently be calculated from theoretical models with better than 10% accuracy, and for applications such as future leptonic CP violation measurements, neutrino-nucleus cross sections need to be calculated within 5% accuracy, making global analysis of experimental data the preferred approach. The HERMES measurement of the tensor-polarized structure function $b_1$ for the deuteron is an order-of-magnitude larger than the conventional convolution model prediction based on the standard deuteron model with D-state admixture, suggesting a new hadronic phenomenon exists.

## Method

The author reviews the status of NPDFs determined by global $\chi^2$ analysis of world data on high-energy lepton-nucleus, proton-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus reactions, using parametrized nuclear modification functions $w_i(x,A,Z)$ at an initial scale $Q_0^2=1$ GeV$^2$ with DGLAP evolution. For the tensor structure function $b_1$, the author describes a parametrization of tensor-polarized parton distributions $\delta_{_T} q$ and $\delta_{_T} \bar q$ in the deuteron, assuming a common function $\delta_{_T} w(x)$ with a node, and performing a $\chi^2$ analysis against the HERMES data under two scenarios: with and without tensor-polarized antiquark distributions.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear medium effects are typically 10-20% depending on nuclear size, as measured from experimental data.
- The kinematical range of $x$ for nuclear DIS data extends from approximately 0.005 to 0.7.
- For future leptonic CP violation measurements, neutrino-nucleus cross sections need to be calculated within 5% accuracy.
- The HERMES $b_1$ data are an order-of-magnitude larger than the standard convolution model prediction for the deuteron.
- The JLab $b_1$ experiment was approved and measurement will start around the year 2019.
- The initial scale for the HKN NPDF parametrization is $Q_0^2=1$ GeV$^2$.
- The DIS region is defined by the cut $W^2 \ge 4$ GeV$^2$ with $Q^2 \ge 1$ GeV$^2$.

## Context

This work extends the global analysis approach for nucleon PDFs (used at LHC for beyond-standard-model searches) to nuclear PDFs, addressing two major issues: undetermined gluon shadowing at small $x$ due to the lack of a lepton-nucleus collider similar to HERA, and inconsistencies between charged-lepton DIS and neutrino DIS modifications reported by the nCTEQ group. The tensor structure function $b_1$ work builds on the HERMES measurement and the sum rule analogous to the Gottfried sum, which previously led to the discovery of flavor-asymmetric light-antiquark distributions ($\bar u \ne \bar d$). The results enable future experimental investigations at JLab starting around 2019 and potential measurements of tensor-polarized antiquark distributions via polarized Drell-Yan processes at facilities including Fermilab-MI, RHIC, CERN-COMPASS, J-PARC, GSI-FAIR, and U70.
