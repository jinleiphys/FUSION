# Dynamical effects in fusion with exotic nuclei

> [Background] Reactions with stable beams have demonstrated a strong interplay between nuclear structure and fusion. Exotic beam facilities open new perspectives to understand the impact of neutron skin, large isospin, and weak binding energies on fusion. Microscopic theories of fusion are required to guide future experiments. [Purpose] To investigate new effects of exotic structures and dynamics in near-barrier fusion with exotic nuclei. [Method] Microscopic approaches based on the Hartree-Fock (HF) mean-field theory are used for studying fusion barriers in $^{40-54}$Ca+$^{116}$Sn reactions for even isotopes. Bare potential barriers are obtained assuming frozen HF ground-state densities. Dynamical effects on the barrier are accounted for in time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations of the collisions. Vibrational couplings are studied in the coupled-channel framework and near-barrier nucleon transfer is investigated with TDHF calculations. [Results] The development of a neutron skin in exotic calcium isotopes strongly lowers the bare potential barrier. However, this static effect is not apparent when dynamical effects are included. On the contrary, a fusion hindrance is observed in TDHF calculations with the most neutron rich calcium isotopes which cannot be explained by vibrational couplings. Transfer reactions are also important in these systems due to charge equilibration processes. [Conclusions] Despite its impact on the bare potential, the neutron skin is not seen as playing an important role in the fusion dynamics. However, the charge transfer with exotic projectiles could lead to an increase of the Coulomb repulsion between the fragments, suppressing fusion. The effect of transfer and dissipative mechanisms on fusion with exotic nuclei deserve further studies.

## Key claim

The development of a neutron skin in exotic calcium isotopes strongly lowers the bare potential barrier in static calculations, but this static effect is not apparent when dynamical effects are included; instead, a fusion hindrance is observed in time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations with the most neutron-rich calcium isotopes, which cannot be explained by vibrational couplings.

## Method

The authors used Hartree-Fock (HF) mean-field theory to compute bare potential barriers for even calcium isotopes from 40 to 54 reacting with 116Sn, assuming frozen HF ground-state densities. Dynamical effects on the fusion barrier were then studied using time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations of central collisions, with fusion thresholds extracted by varying the center-of-mass energy in 0.1 MeV steps. Vibrational couplings were investigated separately using coupled-channel calculations with vibrational properties obtained from TDHF linear response theory, and near-barrier nucleon transfer was studied through TDHF calculations.

## Key numbers

- The frozen HF barrier height for 40Ca+116Sn is approximately 119.7 MeV at a radius of 11.3 fm, matching the Akyuz-Winther phenomenological potential.
- TDHF fusion thresholds were determined with a numerical uncertainty of plus or minus 0.05 MeV, using energy steps of 0.1 MeV.
- The TDHF-calculated low-lying octupole phonon energy in 40Ca is 3.44 MeV with a deformation parameter beta_3 of 0.224, compared to the experimental energy of 3.737 MeV and beta_3 range of 0.30 to 0.41.
- For 54Ca, the TDHF-calculated octupole phonon energy is 2.92 MeV with beta_3 of 0.226.
- The neutron single-particle shell gap above 48Ca is approximately 5 MeV in energy between the 1f7/2 and 2p3/2 shells.

## Context

This work extends microscopic fusion studies using the frozen HF and TDHF methods previously developed by Simenel (2013) and others, applying them specifically to exotic calcium isotopes to investigate the role of neutron skins. The results contradict the expectation from static calculations that neutron skins would enhance sub-barrier fusion, and instead suggest that charge transfer and dissipative mechanisms in exotic systems may suppress fusion. The study enables future experimental investigations with exotic beams at facilities like those studying 132Sn and 134Te reactions (Kohley 2011, 2013), and calls for further theoretical work on transfer and dissipative mechanisms in fusion with exotic nuclei.
