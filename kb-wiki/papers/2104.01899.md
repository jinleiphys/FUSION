# The "Folk Theorem" on Effective Field Theory: A Case for Nuclear Physics

> Although largely unrecognized, Gerry Brown had played a seminal and prescient role for the development of the currently heralded "first-principles approach" to nuclear dynamics known as "nuclear effective field theory" ($n$EFT for short). I give a brief account in what way he entered -- together with his Korean colleagues -- at the earliest stage of its development and what he left behind for the future in paving the road to go {\it way beyond} the standard $n$EFT. I do this in the context of Gerry's original conceptual ideas conceived after the "dilepton fiasco" that indicate a surprising new role of hidden local and scale symmetries in nuclear physics.

# The "Folk Theorem" on Effective Field Theory: A Case for Nuclear Physics

## Key claim
The paper argues that Gerry Brown's prescient contributions to nuclear effective field theory (nEFT) were largely unrecognized, and that his approach incorporating hidden local symmetry (HLS) and scale symmetry, termed "GnEFT", is necessary to go beyond the standard nEFT, particularly for dense matter relevant to compact stars. The claim is hedged by the admission that the "Folk Theorem" has no rigorous proof and that the approach remains speculative at high densities.

## Method
The author provides a historical and conceptual review of Gerry Brown's role in developing nuclear EFT, tracing ideas from the 1981 Erice School through the formulation of the Cheshire Cat Principle (1985) and the Brown-Rho (BR) scaling paper (1991). The paper contrasts the standard nEFT approach (pions and nucleons only, with Weinberg counting) against GnEFT, which explicitly includes vector mesons (ρ, ω) and the scalar dilaton σ via hidden local symmetry and scale symmetry, treated as dynamically generated composite gauge fields. The author evaluates the success and limitations of both approaches in describing nuclear phenomena from normal density (~n₀) to compact-star densities (~5-7 n₀).

## Key numbers
- The cutoff scale for standard nEFT is Λ ~ (400-500) MeV, above which the approach may break down.
- The effective axial-vector coupling constant in nuclear matter predicted by BR scaling is g_A^L ≈ 1, corresponding to a quenching factor q^L ≡ g_A^L/g_A ≈ 0.78 (with g_A = 1.276 from neutron beta decay).
- In light nuclei, the simple shell model quenching factor is q^light ≈ 0.75(2), giving g_A^light ≈ 1.
- The density regime where standard nEFT works successfully is n ≲ 2n₀, while compact-star densities reach n_star ~ (5-7)n₀.
- The conformal speed predicted for compact-star cores is v_s/c² ≈ 1/3.
- Two-body current corrections to the Gamow-Teller operator in ¹⁰⁰Sn were found to be ≳ 25%, which the author argues is unnaturally large for chiral counting.

## Context
This paper extends the historical narrative of nuclear EFT development, specifically highlighting Gerry Brown's contributions alongside Weinberg's foundational papers (Weinberg 1990, 1991) and the author's own work (Rho 1991). It addresses the "dilepton fiasco" where heavy-ion experiments misinterpreted Brown-Rho scaling (Brown and Rho 1991) as predicting dropping vector meson masses near phase transitions, when the correct prediction was a narrow peak ("needle in a haystack") due to the vector manifestation fixed point (Harada and Yamawaki). The paper connects to ongoing work on compact-star physics, the quenched g_A puzzle, and the possible emergence of pseudo-conformal symmetry at high density, as well as recent developments involving fractional quantum Hall droplets from the η′ meson (Komargodski).
