# Reaction mechanisms of pair transfer

> The mechanisms of nuclear transfer reactions are described for the transfer of two nucleons from one nucleus to another. Two-nucleon overlap functions are defined in various coordinate systems, and their transformation coefficients given between coordinate systems. Post and prior couplings are defined for sequential transfer mechanisms, and it is demonstrated that the combination of `prior-post' couplings avoids non-orthogonality terms, but does not avoid couplings that do not have good zero-range approximations. The simultaneous and sequential mechanisms are demonstrated for the $^{124}$Sn(p,t)$^{122}$Sn reaction at 25 MeV using shell-model overlap functions. The interference between the various simultaneous and sequential amplitudes is shown.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that modern calculations including both simultaneous and sequential transfer mechanisms, with proper finite-range and shell-model overlap functions, can reduce the historical "unhappiness factor" (discrepancy between theory and experiment) much closer to unity for the 124Sn(p,t)122Sn reaction at 25 MeV, contrary to earlier claims that simultaneous transfer alone gave better agreement.

## Method

The author calculates two-nucleon transfer cross sections for the 124Sn(p,t)122Sn reaction at 25 MeV using shell-model overlap functions from the NuShell code, with a model space of five neutron orbitals (0g7/2, 1d5/2, 1d3/2, 2s1/2, 0h11/2) and two-body matrix elements derived from the CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon interaction. Both simultaneous (one-step) and sequential (two-step via intermediate deuteron channel) transfer amplitudes are computed in finite-range DWBA, using the prior-post coupling form for the sequential mechanism to avoid non-orthogonality terms. The triton, deuteron, and proton optical potentials are taken from Li (2007), Daehnick (1980), and Chapel Hill 89 (Varner 1991) respectively.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for pair transfer reactions developed over the past 50 years by authors including Yoshida (1962), Glendenning (1965), and Broglia (1973), among others. It directly addresses the historical "unhappiness factor" problem noted by Mermaz (1980) and Vaagen (1979), where theory predictions fell well below experimental data. The paper demonstrates that modern calculations by Potel et al. (2011) achieve considerably better agreement with experiment, and shows that the prior-post coupling form for sequential transfer avoids non-orthogonality terms that plague other coupling combinations. This enables future calculations to properly include both simultaneous and sequential mechanisms with their coherent interference, rather than relying on zero-range approximations or treating only simultaneous transfer.
