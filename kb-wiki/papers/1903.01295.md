# Cooling of hypernuclear compact stars: Hartree-Fock models and high-density pairing

> The thermal evolution of hypernuclear compact stars is studied for stellar models constructed on the basis of covariant density functional theory in Hartree and Hartree-Fock approximation. Parametrizations of both types are consistent with the astrophysical mass constraints on compact stars and available hypernuclear data. We discuss the differences of these density functionals and highlight the effects they have on the composition and on the cooling of hypernuclear stars. It is shown that hypernuclear stars computed with density functional models that have a low symmetry energy slope, $L$, are fairly consistent with the cooling data of observed compact stars. The class of stellar models based on larger $L$ values gives rise to the direct Urca process at low densities, which leads to significantly faster cooling. We conjecture high-density pairing for protons and $Λ$'s in the $P$-wave channel and provide simple scaling arguments to obtain these gaps. As a consequence the most massive stellar models with masses $1.8 \le M/M_{\odot} \le2$ experience slower cooling by hyperonic dUrca processes which involve $Λ$'s and protons.

## Key claim

Hypernuclear compact stars computed with density functional models that have a low symmetry energy slope, L, are fairly consistent with observed cooling data, while models with larger L values give rise to the direct Urca process at low densities leading to significantly faster cooling; the authors conjecture high-density pairing for protons and Lambda hyperons in the P-wave channel, which causes the most massive stellar models (1.8 to 2 solar masses) to experience slower cooling by hyperonic direct Urca processes involving Lambdas and protons.

## Method

The authors constructed stellar models using covariant density functional theory in both Hartree and Hartree-Fock approximations, adding four Hartree-Fock models to three previously studied Hartree models. They computed the thermal evolution of hypernuclear compact stars using these equations of state, which include tensor interactions and pion exchanges in the Hartree-Fock case, and incorporated high-density P-wave pairing gaps for protons and Lambdas derived from simple symmetry arguments and scaling relations.

## Key numbers

- Maximum masses of hypernuclear stars range from 1.99 to 2.12 solar masses across the seven models (DDME2: 2.12, SWL: 2.00, GM1A: 1.99, PKA1: 2.01, PKO1: 2.02, PKO2: 2.03, PKO3: 2.02)
- Symmetry energy slope L values: DDME2 has 51.2 MeV, SWL has 55.0 MeV, GM1A has 94.4 MeV, PKA1 has 103.5 MeV, PKO1 has 97.7 MeV, PKO2 has 75.9 MeV, PKO3 has 83.0 MeV
- Lambda S-wave pairing gaps range from 0.5 to 1.2 MeV for Fermi momenta between 0.2 and 1.3 inverse femtometers
- Proton S-wave pairing gap from Chen (1993) has a maximum of approximately 1 MeV
- Nucleonic direct Urca threshold densities: 0.28 inverse femtometers for GM1A, 0.25 for PKA1, 0.25 for PKO1, 0.30 for PKO2, 0.28 for PKO3; DDME2 does not allow nucleonic dUrca in stable stars
- For a 1.4 solar mass star, the central energy density of the APR equation of state is 1.36 to 1.73 times larger than the energy densities from the relativistic DFT equations of state
- Neutron central densities for a 1.0 solar mass star: 0.26 inverse femtometers for DDME2, 0.29 for SWL, approximately 0.22 for PK models

## Context

This work extends the systematic study of hypernuclear compact star cooling initiated in Raduta (2018) (Paper I) by adding Hartree-Fock equations of state from Li (2018) that include tensor interactions and pion exchanges. It contrasts with the "nuclear medium cooling" scenario of Grigorian (2018) and the work of Tolos (2018) which used FSU class relativistic density functional models; the key difference is the inclusion of high-density P-wave pairing for protons and Lambdas, which slows cooling in the most massive stars. The results enable further constraints on dense nuclear matter composition by comparing cooling predictions with observational data from pulsars such as PSR J0348+0432 (2.01 solar masses) and PSR J1614-2230 (1.93 solar masses).
