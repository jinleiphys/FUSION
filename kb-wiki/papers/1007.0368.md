# Event-by-event hydrodynamics and elliptic flow from fluctuating initial state

> We develop a framework for event-by-event ideal hydrodynamics to study the differential elliptic flow which is measured at different centralities in Au+Au collisions at Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC). Fluctuating initial energy density profiles, which here are the event-by-event analogues of the eWN profiles, are created using a Monte Carlo Glauber model. Using the same event plane method for obtaining $v_2$ as in the data analysis, we can reproduce both the measured centrality dependence and the $p_T$ shape of charged-particle elliptic flow up to $p_T\sim2$~GeV. We also consider the relation of elliptic flow to the initial state eccentricity using different reference planes, and discuss the correlation between the physical event plane and the initial participant plane. Our results demonstrate that event-by-event hydrodynamics with initial state fluctuations must be accounted for before a meaningful lower limit for viscosity can be obtained from elliptic flow data.

## Key claim

Event-by-event ideal hydrodynamics with Monte Carlo Glauber fluctuating initial energy density profiles can simultaneously reproduce the measured centrality dependence and the $p_T$ shape of charged-particle elliptic flow up to $p_T\sim2$ GeV in Au+Au collisions at RHIC, but only when the elliptic flow is computed using the same event-plane method as in experimental data analysis. The authors demonstrate that before a meaningful lower limit for viscosity can be obtained from elliptic flow data, event-by-event hydrodynamics with initial state fluctuations must be accounted for.

## Method

The authors developed a (2+1)-dimensional ideal hydrodynamic framework with longitudinal boost-invariance, using the SHASTA algorithm for numerical evolution and the Laine-Schroder equation of state with a cross-over transition. Fluctuating initial energy density profiles were generated from a Monte Carlo Glauber model by smearing wounded nucleon positions with a 2D Gaussian of width sigma = 0.4 fm or 0.8 fm, with initial time tau_0 = 0.17 fm. Thermal hadron spectra were computed via the Cooper-Frye method at freeze-out temperature T_dec = 160 MeV, and resonance decays were handled using PYTHIA 6.4, with elliptic flow determined using the event-plane method with two-subevent resolution correction.

## Key numbers

- For Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV, the inelastic nucleon-nucleon cross section used was sigma_NN = 42 mb
- The Woods-Saxon parameters for gold were radius R_A = 6.37 fm and surface thickness d = 0.54 fm
- The initial time was fixed to tau_0 = 0.17 fm for all events
- The freeze-out temperature was T_dec = 160 MeV
- The overall normalization constant K = 37.8 GeV/fm was used for energy density profiles
- Two Gaussian smearing widths were studied: sigma = 0.4 fm and 0.8 fm
- 500 hydrodynamic runs were performed per centrality class, with 20 final state events from each run, totaling 10,000 events per centrality class
- For averaged initial states, 20,000 initial states were summed together
- The ratio v2{EP}/v2{RP} was of order 10 in central collisions and of order 1.2 in semi-peripheral (20-30%) collisions
- Doubling sigma from 0.4 fm to 0.8 fm caused only about 10% changes in v2(p_T)

## Context

This work extends previous ideal and viscous hydrodynamic studies (Hirano 2001, Huovinen 2001, Kolb 2001, Teaney 2001, Romatschke 2007, Song 2007) that tended to underestimate elliptic flow in central collisions, by implementing genuine event-by-event hydrodynamics with fluctuating initial states from a Monte Carlo Glauber model. The approach builds on earlier event-by-event frameworks (Hama 2004, Andrade 2006, Petersen 2009, Werner 2010) and addresses the specific problems of the v2 deficit in central collisions and the unsatisfactory shape and centrality dependence of v2(p_T) seen in averaged-initial-state calculations (Niemi 2008). The results demonstrate that the participant plane is a good approximation for the event plane, and that extracting a non-zero lower limit for viscosity from elliptic flow data is practically impossible without further constraints on the initial state.
