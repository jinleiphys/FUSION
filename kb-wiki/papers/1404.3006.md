# Microscopic study of the effect of intrinsic degrees of freedom on fusion

> Fusion cross-sections are computed for the $^{40}$Ca$+^{40}$Ca system over a wide energy range with two microscopic approaches where the only phenomenological input is the Skyrme energy density functional. The first method is based on the coupled-channels formalism, using the bare nucleus-nucleus potential calculated with the frozen Hartree-Fock technique and the deformation parameters of vibrational states computed with the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) approach. The second method is based on the density-constrained TDHF method to generate nucleus-nucleus potentials from TDHF evolution. Both approaches incorporate the effect of couplings to internal degrees of freedoms in different ways. The predictions are in relatively good agreement with experimental data.

# Microscopic study of the effect of intrinsic degrees of freedom on fusion

## Key claim
Fusion cross-sections for the ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca system computed from two fully microscopic approaches (coupled-channels with TDHF-derived inputs and density-constrained TDHF) agree relatively well with experimental data over a wide energy range, but the abstract glosses over that the coupled-channels calculations still underestimate cross-sections near the barrier (suggesting additional couplings are needed) and the DC-TDHF calculations overestimate them there.

## Method
The first method uses the frozen Hartree-Fock technique with the SLy4d Skyrme functional to compute the bare nucleus-nucleus potential, then extracts energies and deformation parameters of vibrational states (3⁻₁ and giant quadrupole resonance) from TDHF linear-response strength functions, and feeds these into standard coupled-channels calculations using the ccfull code. The second method employs density-constrained TDHF (DC-TDHF) to extract nucleus-nucleus potentials directly from TDHF trajectories at multiple bombarding energies, then uses each potential in a one-barrier penetration model to compute fusion cross-sections, combining results from energies between 53 and 65 MeV.

## Key numbers
- Frozen HF barrier height: V_B = 54.6 MeV at R_B = 9.9 fm for ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca.
- TDHF-computed 3⁻₁ state energy in ⁴⁰Ca: 3.44 MeV (experimental value is 3.74 MeV).
- Deformation parameter β₃ for the 3⁻₁ state of ⁴⁰Ca from TDHF: 0.24 (experimental β₃ is 0.3–0.4).
- TDHF-computed GQR energy in ⁴⁰Ca: ~18.1 MeV; coupling strength β₂ = 0.16.
- TDHF fusion threshold for ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca: 53.15 ± 0.05 MeV, which is ~0.15 MeV lower than the centroid of the CC barrier distribution with 3⁻₁ and GQR couplings.
- DC-TDHF potentials computed at bombarding energies E_c.m. = 55, 60, and 65 MeV show decreasing barrier height and increasing barrier radius with decreasing energy.

## Context
This work extends the microscopic approach of Simenel (2013) by applying it to the benchmark ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca system, and contrasts with standard coupled-channels calculations that rely on phenomenological potentials (e.g., Akyüz and Winter 1979, São Paulo 2002) and experimental deformation parameters. The DC-TDHF method follows the energy-dependent potential concept introduced by Washiyama and Lacroix (2008) and developed by Umar and Oberacker (2006). The results enable future studies of more complex systems including rotational couplings (Simenel 2004, Umar 2008), asymmetric systems with transfer channels, heavier systems with dissipative dynamics, and extensions beyond Hartree-Fock using pairing (Avez 2008, Ebata 2010, Scamps 2013).
