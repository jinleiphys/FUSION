# Supernova Neutrinos: The Accretion Disk Scenario

> Neutrinos from core collapse supernovae can be emitted from a rapidly accreting disk surrounding a black hole, instead of the canonical proto-neutron star. For Galactic events, detector count rates are considerable and in fact can be in the thousands for Super-Kamiokande. The rate of occurrence of these accreting disks in the Galaxy is predicted to be on the order of 10^-5 yr^-1, yet there is little observational evidence to provide an upper limit on their formation rate. It would therefore be useful to discriminate between neutrinos which have been produced in a proto-neutron star and those which have been produced accretion disks. In order to distinguish between the two scenarios, either the time profile of the neutrino luminosity or the relative fluxes of different neutrino flavors may be considered. There are some signals that would clearly point to one scenario or the other.

## Key claim

Neutrinos from a Galactic core-collapse supernova could be emitted from a rapidly accreting disk surrounding a black hole (AD-BH) instead of the canonical proto-neutron star (PNS), and the ratio of total energy flux to charged-current flux can distinguish the two scenarios, but this distinction depends on the unknown neutrino mass hierarchy and the value of the mixing angle θ13, with the most dramatic signature (a ratio as large as 1600:1) occurring only under specific oscillation conditions.

## Method

The authors modeled neutrino emission from steady-state accretion disks with accretion rates of 0.01, 0.1, and 1.0 solar masses per second around a 3 solar mass black hole, using previous calculations of disk structure and neutrino transport from Surman and McLaughlin (2004). They compared the resulting neutrino energy spectra and flavor-dependent fluxes to those from a canonical proto-neutron star, then propagated the emitted neutrino fluxes through the supernova envelope accounting for matter-enhanced neutrino oscillations under both normal and inverted mass hierarchies, with adiabatic or non-adiabatic transitions at the two resonance densities. Predicted event rates were computed for several detectors (Super-Kamiokande, KamLAND, SNO, OMNIS) assuming a supernova at 10 kpc.

## Key numbers

- Predicted Galactic rate of accreting-disk-black-hole formation: on the order of 10⁻⁵ yr⁻¹
- Accretion rates considered: 0.1 M⊙/s to 1.0 M⊙/s for disks producing enough energy for a gamma-ray burst
- Gravitational binding energy released for a 3 M⊙ black hole processing mass M: 9 × (M/M⊙) × 10⁵³ erg
- Fraction of binding energy emitted as neutrinos: roughly 20% for 1.0 M⊙/s disks, 5% for 0.1 M⊙/s disks
- Average electron neutrino energy at the trapping surface for a 1 M⊙/s disk: ⟨Eνe⟩ ~ 13–14 MeV; average electron antineutrino energy: ⟨Eν̄e⟩ ~ 16–18 MeV
- For a PNS at 10 kpc, Super-Kamiokande predicts 7000 events in the ν̄e + p → n + e⁺ channel; for a 1.0 M⊙/s AD-BH at 10 kpc, the same channel yields 2800 events (normal hierarchy) or as few as 10 events (inverted hierarchy with adiabatic θ13 resonance)
- Ratio of total energy flux to νe flux for a 1.0 M⊙/s AD-BH: as large as 1600:1 under specific oscillation scenarios (inverted hierarchy, adiabatic high-density resonance)

## Context

This work extends the study of supernova neutrino signals beyond the standard proto-neutron star model, which had been the canonical emission source in prior work by Keil, Raffelt, and Janka (2003) and Dighe and Smirnov (2000). The accretion disk scenario was motivated by simulations of collapsars by MacFadyen and Woosley (1999) and of neutron star mergers by Ruffert and Janka (1998) and Rosswog (2005). The paper enables future Galactic supernova observations to discriminate between PNS and AD-BH formation using neutrino detectors, provided that both charged-current and neutral-current measurements are available; it also highlights that the unknown neutrino mass hierarchy and the value of θ13 (constrained by the CHOOZ experiment to sin²θ13 < 0.1) introduce significant uncertainty into the interpretation.
