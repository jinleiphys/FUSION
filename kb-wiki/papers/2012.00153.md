# Anomalies in Radioactive Decay Rates: A Bibliography of Measurements and Theory

> Knowledge of the decay rates (or half-lives) of radioisotopes is critical in many fields, including medicine, archeology, and nuclear physics, to name just a few. Central to the many uses of radioisotopes is the belief that decay rates are fundamental constants of nature, just as the masses of the radioisotopes themselves are. Recently, the belief that decay rates are fundamental constants has been called into question following the observation of various reported anomalies in decay rates, such as apparent periodic variations. The purpose of this bibliography is to collect in one place the relevant literature on both sides of this issue in the expectation that doing so will deepen our understanding of the available data.

## Key claim

This bibliography collects literature on both sides of the debate over whether radioactive decay rates are truly fundamental constants, noting that while some experiments report periodic variations in decay rates, other equally well-done experiments have sought but not found such fluctuations, and there is currently no quantitative theory to account for the observed anomalies.

## Method

The authors compiled a bibliography of experimental and theoretical papers addressing potential time-dependent variations in radioactive decay rates, categorizing each reference by whether it reports variations (V) or no variations (NV) from the expected exponential decay law. They separately considered alpha-decays, beta-decays, electron capture-decays, and gamma-decays, and cross-referenced experiments in a table by isotope, decay type, detector type, institution, and observed periodicities.

## Key numbers

- The half-life of $^{32}$Si measured at Brookhaven National Laboratory between 1982-1986 exhibited clear annual periodicities that could not be accounted for by temperature variations or other conventional environmental influences.
- A precipitous drop in the decay rate of $^{54}$Mn was observed in association with a significant solar storm on 13 December 2006.
- During the GW170817 neutron star inspiral, $^{44}$Ti and $^{60}$Co exhibited an effect similar to that observed for $^{32}$Si and $^{36}$Cl, but $^{137}$Cs did not.
- The annual fluctuations $\Delta \Gamma_{i}/\Gamma_{i}$ in many decay rates $\Gamma_{i}$ are approximately the same, even though the magnitudes of the $\Gamma_{i}$ vary over 9 orders of magnitude.

## Context

This work extends the ongoing debate initiated by experiments such as Alburger et al. (1986) at Brookhaven National Laboratory on $^{32}$Si and Siegert et al. (1998) at the Physikalisch-Technische Bundesanstalt, which reported annual variations in decay rates, while Pomme et al. (2016) argued such fluctuations are due to experimental influences and improper uncertainty calculations. The bibliography enables researchers to systematically compare experiments reporting variations against those finding no variations, and to test proposed mechanisms such as solar neutrino flux modulation, axionic dark matter coupling, or phase-space modifications from the ambient medium.
