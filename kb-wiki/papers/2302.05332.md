# Universality Classes of Relativistic Fluid Dynamics: Applications

> Using a formalism that was recently developed in a companion paper, we rigorously prove the equivalence, in the linear regime, of a number of apparently different relativistic hydrodynamic theories proposed in the literature. In particular, we show that Hydro+ is indistinguishable from the Israel-Stewart theory for bulk viscosity, which in turn is indistinguishable from a reacting mixture. The two-fluid model for superfluidity coincides with the Israel-Stewart theory for heat conduction in the limit of infinite conductivity, and this explains why the latter has a second sound. Also, MIS$^*$ is equivalent to the Burgers model for viscoelasticity, and this implies that the former must exhibit an elastic behavior at high frequencies. Additionally, we show that if the degrees of freedom and the conservation laws of a hydrodynamic theory have the same geometric character as those of the Israel-Stewart theory, then such theory must be indistinguishable from the Israel-Stewart theory in the linear regime. This explains why all second-order theories turn out to be identical near equilibrium. Finally, we construct the first linearized model for a relativistic supersolid that is proven to be causal, stable, and strongly hyperbolic.

## Key claim

The paper rigorously proves that several apparently different relativistic hydrodynamic theories are mathematically equivalent in the linear regime near equilibrium, meaning that physically distinct systems can be described by identical equations of motion. This equivalence holds only for linear deviations about homogeneous and isotropic equilibria, and the authors stress that the theories may have been constructed with different domains of applicability or different assumptions about transport coefficients.

## Method

The authors apply a formalism developed in a companion paper (Paper I) that classifies relativistic hydrodynamic theories by the transformation properties of their degrees of freedom and conserved densities under the SO(3) rotation group. They use Theorem 1 of Paper I, which states that if an equilibrium state is thermodynamically stable and obeys the second law, the linearized equations take a specific symmetric hyperbolic form. By showing that different theories share the same classification numbers (the same "universality class"), they demonstrate that a linear change of variables maps one theory's equations into another's.

## Key numbers

- Hydro+ belongs to universality class (3,1,0)-(2,1,0), the same as Israel-Stewart theory for bulk viscosity; the mapping yields bulk viscosity coefficient zeta = (beta / Xi) * (partial P / partial lambda)^2 and relaxation time tau_Pi = (1 / Xi) * (partial phi / partial lambda), where Xi is the entropy production coefficient.
- The characteristic speed of Israel-Stewart bulk viscosity in the linear regime is c_UV^2 = (partial P / partial rho) evaluated at constant s/b plus zeta / (tau_Pi * (rho + P)), which equals the characteristic speed of the baryon-pion mixture.
- The two-fluid model for superfluidity coincides with Israel-Stewart theory for heat conduction in the limit of infinite heat conductivity, explaining why the latter has a second sound.
- The authors construct the first linearized model for a relativistic supersolid that is proven to be causal, stable, and strongly hyperbolic, as a representative of universality class (3,2,1)-(3,2,1).

## Context

This work extends the classification framework developed in the companion paper (Paper I, Gavassino, Disconzi, and Noronha 2023) to demonstrate explicit equivalences between existing theories. It shows that Hydro+ (developed by Stephanov and Yin 2018 for fluids near the critical point) is mathematically equivalent to Israel-Stewart bulk viscosity, that a baryon-pion mixture in neutron stars is equivalent to bulk-viscous Israel-Stewart theory (previously observed numerically by Camelio and collaborators 2022), and that MIS* (proposed by Ke and Yin 2022) is equivalent to the Burgers model for viscoelasticity. The paper explains why all second-order theories (including those by Hiscock and Lindblom 1983, Baier and collaborators 2008, and Denicol and collaborators 2012) are identical near equilibrium: any theory with the same degrees of freedom and conservation laws as Israel-Stewart must be indistinguishable from it in the linear regime. This enables systematic classification of relativistic fluid theories and provides a rigorous foundation for constructing causal, stable models of new systems such as supersolids.
