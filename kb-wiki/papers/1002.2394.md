# The effect of shear and bulk viscosities on elliptic flow

> In this work, we examine the effect of shear and bulk viscosities on elliptic flow by taking a realistic parameterization of the shear and bulk viscous coefficients, $η$ and $ζ$, and their respective relaxation times, $τ_π$ and $τ_Π$. We argue that the behaviors close to ideal fluid observed at RHIC energies may be related to non-trivial temperature dependence of these transport coefficients.

## Key claim

The near-ideal fluid behavior of elliptic flow observed at RHIC energies may not directly reflect a small shear viscosity coefficient η/s, but rather the combined effect of η/s and its relaxation time τπ, which can suppress the growth of the shear stress tensor even when η/s is large.

## Method

The authors solved relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics in three spatial dimensions using the memory function method and the Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) method with approximately 80000 SPH particles and a smoothing parameter h = 0.6 fm. They used a factorized initial energy density profile with impact parameter b = 7.5 fm, the equation of state from Denicol et al. (2009), and temperature-dependent parameterizations of η/s and ζ/s from lattice QCD calculations (Nakamura 2004) and Noronha (2009), with corresponding relaxation times τπ = η/P and τΠ = 9ζ/(ε − 3P).

## Key numbers

- The elliptic flow v₂(pT) calculated with temperature-dependent η/s is well reproduced by an effective constant shear viscosity ηeff/s = 0.12.
- The minimum of η/s occurs around T ∼ 180 MeV.
- The relaxation time τπ can reach values around 10 fm, comparable to the hydrodynamic evolution time scale of ∼10 fm.
- The freeze-out temperature used was Tf = 130 MeV.
- The impact parameter was set to b = 7.5 fm.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of dissipative hydrodynamics in heavy-ion collisions beyond the common assumption that η/s is constant and that shear viscosity alone determines elliptic flow, as done in earlier studies by Romatschke (2007). The authors show that the relaxation time τπ, often neglected, plays a crucial role and that the effective viscosity extracted from data may not directly measure η/s at the phase transition. This enables future studies at LHC energies, where the system spends more time in the quark-gluon plasma phase, and highlights the need for better calculations of the temperature dependence of relaxation times.
