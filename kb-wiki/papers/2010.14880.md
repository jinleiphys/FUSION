# Identified particle spectra in Pb-Pb, Xe-Xe and p-Pb collisions with Tsallis blast-wave model

> We investigate the identified hadrons transverse momentum ($p_{\rm T}$) spectra in Pb-Pb (Pb-Pb, Xe-Xe, p-Pb) collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=$ 2.76 (5.02, 5.44, 5.02) TeV in the framework of Tsallis-blast wave (TBW) model with a linear transverse velocity profile and with a constant velocity profile. In this model, the Tsallis temperature ($T$), the average radial flow velocity ($\langle β\rangle$) and the degree of non-equilibrium ($q$) of the system are common for all hadrons when a combined fit is performed to the $p_{\rm T}$ spectra of different particles at a given centrality. It is found that the model can describe the particle spectra well up to 3 GeV/c. For both profiles, the transverse flow velocity decreases from central to peripheral collisions while the non-extensive parameter $q$ exhibits the opposite behavior, indicating a more rapid expansion and less off-equilibrium of the system in more central collisions. Moreover, we observe that in central collisions $\langle β\rangle$ and $q$ ($T$) from the fit with the linear profile are smaller (is slightly larger) than those (that) with the constant profile, while in peripheral collisions $\langle β\rangle$, $T$ and $q$ from the former are compatible with those from the latter. We also derived and discussed the relation between the Tsallis temperature and the thermal temperature. In addition, to check whether a scenario of an early freeze-out of strange particles at the LHC exists, the particle spectra are investigated by grouping them into strange and non-strange hadrons. The combined fit gives an insight on the degree of non-equilibrium, the radial flow and the Tsallis temperature of the system at the kinetic decoupling. It provides a comparison between the results at different energies in the same collision system and the results in different collision systems at the same or similar energy.

## Key claim

The Tsallis blast-wave (TBW) model with either a linear or constant transverse velocity profile can describe identified hadron transverse momentum spectra up to 3 GeV/c across multiple collision systems (Pb-Pb, Xe-Xe, p-Pb) and energies, but the extracted parameters depend on the choice of velocity profile in central collisions while becoming compatible in peripheral collisions.

## Method

The authors performed combined least-chi-squared fits of the TBW model to published ALICE data for identified hadron (pi, K, p, K*0, phi, K_S0, Lambda, Xi, Omega) pT spectra at mid-rapidity, with the Tsallis temperature T, average radial flow velocity <beta>, and non-extensive parameter q as common free parameters for all particle species at a given centrality. Two transverse velocity profiles were tested: a linear profile (beta proportional to radius, n=1) and a constant profile (beta independent of radius, n=0). The fit range was restricted to pT below 3 GeV/c, with a lower bound of 0.5 GeV/c for pions to exclude resonance decay contributions.

- In Pb-Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV, 60-80% centrality with linear profile: <beta> = 0.293 +/- 0.012 +/- 0.014, T = 0.107 +/- 0.003 +/- 0.027 GeV, q = 1.094 +/- 0.003 +/- 0.021
- In Pb-Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV, 0-10% centrality with linear profile (excluding protons): <beta> = 0.567 +/- 0.004 +/- 0.010, T = 0.094 +/- 0.002 +/- 0.019 GeV, q = 1.044 +/- 0.004 +/- 0.029

## Context

This work extends the TBW model studies previously applied to Au-Au collisions at 200 GeV (TBW_1, 2012) and to strange versus non-strange hadrons (TBW_2) by applying the model to LHC energies across multiple collision systems (Pb-Pb, Xe-Xe, p-Pb). The results complement the standard Boltzmann-Gibbs blast-wave (BGBW) analyses by the ALICE collaboration (LHC_1, LHC_2, LHC_3) and the STAR collaboration (STAR_1, STAR_2, STAR_3), showing that the TBW model extends the description of spectra to higher pT than the BGBW model. The comparison of linear versus constant velocity profiles and the grouping of strange versus non-strange hadrons enables investigation of freeze-out dynamics and the possible early decoupling of strange particles at LHC energies.
