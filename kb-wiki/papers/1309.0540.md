# Statistical Global Model of beta- Half-lives and r-Process Nucleosynthesis

> Purpose: Our objective is to apply an improved statistical global model of beta^- decay half-life systematics [1] generated by machine-learning techniques to the prediction of beta half-lives relevant to r-process nuclei. The primary aim of this application is to complement existing r-process-clock and matter-flow studies, thereby providing additional theoretical support for the planning of future activities of the world's network of rare-isotope laboratories. Results: Results are presented for nuclides situated on the r-ladders at N=50, 82, and 126 where abundances peak, as well as for nuclides that affect abundances between peaks or may be relevant to r-processes under different astrophysical scenarios. The half-lives of some of the targeted neutron-rich nuclides have either been recently measured or will be accessible at rare-isotope laboratories in the relatively near future. The results of our large-scale data-driven half-life calculations (generated by a "theory-thin" global statistical model) are compared to available experimental data, including recent measurements on very neutron-rich nuclei along an r-process path far from the valley of $β$ stability. Comparison is also made with corresponding results from traditional global models derived by semi-phenomenological "theory-thick" approaches.

## Key claim
The paper applies a "theory-thin" statistical model based on an artificial neural network to predict beta-minus half-lives for r-process nuclei, claiming that such data-driven machine-learning techniques can yield reliable predictions that may serve as inputs to different astrophysical scenarios for the r-process. The hedge is that these predictions are intended to *complement* existing r-process-clock and matter-flow studies, not replace them, and that the model is limited to nuclei that decay 100% by the beta-minus mode with a cutoff at 10^6 s.

## Method
The authors used a fully connected, multilayer artificial neural network with a feed-forward perceptron architecture [3-5-5-5-5-1|116], trained on a subset of existing beta-minus decay half-life data using the Levenberg-Marquardt learning algorithm with Bayesian regularization and cross-validation. The model was then applied to predict half-lives for neutron-rich nuclides on the r-process ladders at N=50, 82, and 126, as well as for nuclides affecting abundances between peaks. The results were compared to available experimental data and to predictions from traditional "theory-thick" global models such as the pnQRPA+ffGT approach of Moller and the DF3+CQRPA approach of Borzov.

## Key numbers
- The neural network architecture used is [3-5-5-5-5-1|116], indicating 3 input nodes, 5 hidden layers of 5 nodes each, 1 output node, and 116 training data points.
- The model cutoff for half-life predictions is set at 10^6 s.
- Predictions are presented for nuclides on r-ladders at N=50, N=82, and N=126, where r-process abundances peak.
- For the N=50 ladder, half-life predictions are shown for mass numbers ranging from approximately 72 to 84, with values spanning from 10^0 ms to 10^7 ms.
- For the N=82 ladder, half-life predictions are shown for mass numbers ranging from approximately 120 to 135, with values spanning from 10^0 ms to 10^8 ms.
- For the N=126 ladder, half-life predictions are shown for mass numbers ranging from approximately 198 to 206, with values spanning from 10^2 ms to 10^8 ms.

## Context
This work extends the authors' prior neural-network model (Costiris 2009) for beta-decay half-life systematics to the specific context of r-process nucleosynthesis. It provides a "theory-thin" alternative to traditional semi-phenomenological "theory-thick" approaches such as the pnQRPA+ffGT model of Moller and the DF3+CQRPA model of Borzov. The results are intended to support planning for future experiments at rare-isotope laboratories by providing predictions for neutron-rich nuclei that are not yet experimentally accessible, complementing studies of r-process clocks and matter flow.
