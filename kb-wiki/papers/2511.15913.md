# Post-Supernova Accretion of Light Elements onto a New-Born Neutron Star and NS 1987A

> We model early accretion of light elements, He, C, and O, onto a new-born neutron star using the public stellar evolution code MESA, simulating what may happen during the first few years of its life. We find that, under the appropriate conditions, significant amounts of these elements can be accreted up to densities of 10^9 g/cc without triggering a nuclear explosion that would convert them into heavy elements. These results help to understand observations that favor light elements in the atmospheres of young cooling neutron stars, as the one found in the supernova remnant Cassiopeia A, and also add support to the recent indications for the presence of a neutron star, NS 1987A, in the remnant of SN 1987A.

## Key claim

Under specific conditions, significant amounts of light elements (He, C, O) can be accreted onto a new-born neutron star up to densities of 10^9 g cm^-3 without triggering a nuclear explosion that would convert them into heavy elements, provided the accretion stops before the accreted layer reaches the critical depth where unstable burning ignites.

## Method

The authors used the public stellar evolution code MESA (version 15140) to model accretion of He, C, and O onto a neutron star envelope initially composed of pure 56Fe, with a base density of 10^10 or 10^11 g cm^-3 and a base luminosity of 10^35 erg s^-1. They simulated accretion at rates of 10^-8 solar masses per year (roughly one third of the Eddington rate) and at the Eddington rate, using the approx21 nuclear network, and followed the evolution until an explosion occurred, mapping the boundary between stable and unstable burning as a function of composition.

## Key numbers

- Pure 16O accretion: explosion occurs at density rho_expl^O ~ 3 x 10^10 g cm^-3, after centuries of accretion.
- Pure 12C accretion: explosion occurs at column depth y_expl^C ~ 3 x 10^12 g cm^-2, density rho_expl^C ~ 10^9 g cm^-3, after 2.6 years of accretion.
- Pure 4He accretion: explosion occurs at column depth y_expl^He ~ 3 x 10^8 g cm^-2, density rho_expl^He ~ 10^6 g cm^-3, after a few hours of accretion.
- For accretion of 80% 16O and 20% 4He at 10^-8 solar masses per year, explosion was triggered after 4.55 years by unstable 12C burning.
- For accretion of 30% 12C and 70% 4He at 10^-8 solar masses per year, explosion occurred after 3.25 hours triggered by unstable 4He burning.
- The base luminosity used was L_b = 10^35 erg s^-1, with variations by factors of 1/2 and 2 considered.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of neutron star envelope models by Gudmundsson (1983) and Potekhin (1997) to the case of a new-born, hot neutron star accreting light elements shortly after formation. It addresses the puzzle of NS 1987A, where Page (2020) showed that a thick light-element envelope would raise the surface luminosity to the 1-3 x 10^35 erg s^-1 range needed to explain the ALMA-observed warm dust blob, while a heavy-element envelope would fall below 10^35 erg s^-1. The results also help explain observations favoring carbon atmospheres in young cooling neutron stars like the Central Compact Object in Cassiopeia A (Ho, 2009). The work enables future studies of hydrogen accretion and more detailed modeling of the early accretion phase in SN 1987A.
