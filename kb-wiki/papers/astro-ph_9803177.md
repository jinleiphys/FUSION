# Quantifying uncertainties in primordial nucleosynthesis without Monte Carlo simulations

> We present a simple method for determining the (correlated) uncertainties of the light element abundances expected from big bang nucleosynthesis, which avoids the need for lengthy Monte Carlo simulations. Our approach helps to clarify the role of the different nuclear reactions contributing to a particular elemental abundance and makes it easy to implement energy-independent changes in the measured reaction rates. As an application, we demonstrate how this method simplifies the statistical estimation of the nucleon-to-photon ratio through comparison of the standard BBN predictions with the observationally inferred abundances.

## Key claim

The authors present a simple analytic method based on linear error propagation for determining the correlated uncertainties of light element abundances from big bang nucleosynthesis, which avoids lengthy Monte Carlo simulations. The method works well because the input fractional uncertainties in reaction rates are relatively small and the output uncertainties are regularized by the central limit theorem, though it cannot address asymmetric or temperature-dependent uncertainties that Monte Carlo approaches can handle.

## Method

The method computes the logarithmic derivatives λ_ik(η) of each elemental abundance Y_i with respect to each nuclear reaction rate R_k, evaluated once with a BBN numerical code. These derivatives are then used in a linear propagation formula to construct the full abundance error matrix σ²_ij(η) from the input 1σ reaction rate uncertainties, enabling calculation of abundance errors and their correlations without repeated Monte Carlo sampling. The authors provide polynomial fits to Y_i(η) and λ_ik(η) for η in the range 10⁻¹⁰ to 10⁻⁹, allowing all subsequent calculations to be performed on a pocket calculator.

## Key numbers

- Neutron lifetime adopted: τ_n = 886.7 ± 1.9 s (world average), compared to 888.54 ± 3.73 s used in Smith et al. (1993)
- For data set A (high D, low ⁴He): best-fit η = 1.78⁺⁰·⁵⁴₋₀·₃₄ × 10⁻¹⁰ at 95% C.L.
- For data set B (low D, high ⁴He): best-fit η = 5.13⁺⁰·⁷²₋₀·₆₆ × 10⁻¹⁰ at 95% C.L.
- Data set A abundances: D/H = (1.9 ± 0.4) × 10⁻⁴, ⁴He mass fraction = 0.234 ± 0.0054, ⁷Li/H = (1.6 ± 0.36) × 10⁻¹⁰
- Data set B abundances: D/H = (3.40 ± 0.25) × 10⁻⁵, ⁴He mass fraction = 0.243 ± 0.003, ⁷Li/H = (1.73 ± 0.12) × 10⁻¹⁰
- Fractional uncertainties for key reaction rates: R₁ (neutron decay) ±0.21%, R₂ (p(n,γ)d) ±7%, R₇ (t(α,γ)⁷Li) ±26%, R₁₀ (³He(α,γ)⁷Be) ±16%

## Context

This work extends the Monte Carlo approach to BBN uncertainty quantification pioneered by Krauss and Romanelli (1990) and later refined by Kernan and Krauss (1994) to include error correlations. The method enables rapid statistical estimation of the nucleon-to-photon ratio η through simple χ² statistics, which previously required lengthy Monte Carlo simulations as done by Hata et al. (1995) and Olive et al. (1997). The authors note that forthcoming CMB surveys MAP and PLANCK are expected to determine η to within ~5%, providing an independent check of BBN, while precise ⁴He measurements remain crucial for probing the expansion rate during nucleosynthesis and possible new physics beyond the Standard Model.
