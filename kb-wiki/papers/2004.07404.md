# Neutrinoless Double Beta Decay from Lattice QCD: The Long-Distance $π^{-} \rightarrow π^{+} e^{-} e^{-}$ Amplitude

> Neutrinoless double beta decay (\( 0 νββ\)) is a hypothetical nuclear decay mode with important implications. In particular, observation of this decay would demonstrate that the neutrino is a Majorana particle and that lepton number conservation is violated in nature. Relating experimental constraints on \(0 νββ\) decay rates to the neutrino masses requires theoretical input in the form of non-perturbative nuclear matrix elements which remain difficult to calculate reliably. This work marks a first step toward providing a general lattice QCD framework for computing long-distance \(0 νββ\) matrix elements in the case where the decay is mediated by a light Majorana neutrino. The relevant formalism is developed and then tested by computing the simplest such matrix element describing an unphysical \( π^{-} \rightarrow π^{+} e^{-} e^{-} \) transition on a series of domain wall fermion ensembles. The resulting lattice data is then fit to next-to-leading-order chiral perturbation theory, allowing a fully-controlled extraction of the low energy constant governing the transition rate, \(g_ν^{ππ}(μ= 770 \,\, \mathrm{MeV}) = -10.78(12)_{\rm stat}(51)_{\rm sys}\). Finally, future prospects for calculations of more complicated processes, such as the phenomenologically important \(n^{0} n^{0} \rightarrow p^{+} p^{+} e^{-} e^{-}\) decay, are discussed.

## Key claim

This work provides a first lattice QCD framework for computing long-distance neutrinoless double beta decay matrix elements mediated by a light Majorana neutrino, but only tests it on the unphysical \(\pi^{-} \rightarrow \pi^{+} e^{-} e^{-}\) transition rather than a nuclear decay, with the extraction of the low-energy constant relying on chiral perturbation theory extrapolation to physical quark masses.

## Method

The authors compute the \(\pi^{-} \rightarrow \pi^{+} e^{-} e^{-}\) four-point function on five RBC/UKQCD domain wall fermion ensembles with two lattice spacings (inverse lattice spacings of 1.784(5) GeV and 2.382(8) GeV) and pion masses ranging from 302.0(1.1) MeV to 432.2(1.4) MeV. They use a fast Fourier transform algorithm to efficiently integrate over the spacetime locations of the two weak current insertions, then extract the matrix element \(M^{0\nu}\) by performing linear fits to the integrated four-point function at large source-sink separation after subtracting the vacuum intermediate state contribution. The lattice results are simultaneously extrapolated to the physical pion mass, continuum limit, and infinite volume limit using next-to-leading-order chiral perturbation theory.

## Key numbers

* Low-energy constant \(g_{\nu}^{\pi\pi}(\mu = 770\ \mathrm{MeV}) = -10.78(12)_{\rm stat}(51)_{\rm sys}\) from the preferred fit (A3) to data with pion masses 339.6(1.2) MeV to 432.2(1.4) MeV
* Physical-point dimensionless matrix element \(\mathcal{S}_{\pi\pi}^{\rm phys.} = 1.1054(14)_{\rm stat}(6)_{\rm sys}\) from fit A3
* Physical-point matrix element \(M^{0\nu}_{\rm phys.} = 0.018799(57)_{\rm stat}(17)_{\rm sys}\ \mathrm{GeV}^{2}\) from fit A3
* Pion masses on individual ensembles: 432.2(1.4) MeV (24I, \(am_l=0.01\)), 339.6(1.2) MeV (24I, \(am_l=0.005\)), 410.8(1.5) MeV (32I, \(am_l=0.008\)), 359.7(1.2) MeV (32I, \(am_l=0.006\)), 302.0(1.1) MeV (32I, \(am_l=0.004\))
* Pion decay constants on individual ensembles range from \(af_{\pi}=0.06194(27)\) (32I, \(am_l=0.004\)) to \(af_{\pi}=0.09177(25)\) (24I, \(am_l=0.01\))

## Context

This work extends the lattice QCD methodology for rare kaon decays to the neutrinoless double beta decay problem, building on the formalism introduced by Detmold et al. (2018) for computing the required four-point contractions via fast Fourier transforms. It provides the first lattice QCD determination of the low-energy constant \(g_{\nu}^{\pi\pi}\), which enters the chiral perturbation theory description of the \(\pi^{-}\rightarrow\pi^{+}e^{-}e^{-}\) amplitude, and establishes a framework that can be extended to the phenomenologically relevant \(nn\rightarrow pp\ e^{-}e^{-}\) decay in nuclei.
