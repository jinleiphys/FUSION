# Probing the high-density behavior of symmetry energy with gravitational waves

> Gravitational wave (GW) astronomy opens up an entirely new window on the Universe to probe the equations of state (EOS) of neutron-rich matter. With the advent of next generation GW detectors, measuring the gravitational radiation from coalescing binary neutron star systems, mountains on rotating neutron stars, and stellar oscillation modes may become possible in the near future. Using a set of model EOSs satisfying the latest constraints from terrestrial nuclear experiments, state of the art nuclear many-body calculations of the pure neutron matter EOS, and astrophysical observations consistently, we study various GW signatures of the high-density behavior of the nuclear symmetry energy, which is considered among the most uncertain properties of dense neutron-rich nucleonic matter. In particular, we find the tidal polarizability of neutron stars, potentially measurable in binary systems just prior to merger, is more sensitive to the high density component of the nuclear symmetry energy than the symmetry energy at nuclear saturation density. We also find that the upper limit on the GW strain amplitude from elliptically deformed stars is very sensitive to the density dependence of the symmetry energy. This suggests that future developments in modeling of the neutron star crust, and direct gravitational wave signals from accreting binaries will provide a wealth of information on the EOS of neutron-rich matter. We also review the sensitivity of the $r$-mode instability window to the density dependence of the symmetry energy. Whereas models with larger values of the density slope of the symmetry energy at saturation seem to be disfavored by the current observational data, within a simple $r$-mode model, we point out that a subsequent softer behavior of the symmetry energy at high densities (hinted at by recent observational interpretations) could rule them in.

## Key claim

The tidal polarizability of neutron stars is more sensitive to the high-density component of the nuclear symmetry energy (above about 1.5 times saturation density) than to the symmetry energy at nuclear saturation density, though this sensitivity is at the limit of detection for Advanced LIGO-Virgo for 1.4 solar mass stars and depends on the availability of nearby binary systems.

## Method

The authors constructed seventeen different relativistic mean-field (RMF) parameterizations and one Skyrme Hartree-Fock (SHF) model, all satisfying constraints from terrestrial nuclear experiments, microscopic pure neutron matter calculations, and astrophysical observations including a maximum neutron star mass of about 2 solar masses. They then calculated the tidal Love numbers and polarizabilities by integrating the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations together with the tidal perturbation equations, and computed gravitational wave strain amplitudes from elliptically deformed stars using the maximum quadrupole moment formalism.

## Key numbers

- Tidal polarizability of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star changes by 41% between the IU-FSU model (2.828 × 10^36 cm^2 g s^2) and the SkIU-FSU model (1.657 × 10^36 cm^2 g s^2), which differ only in their high-density symmetry energy behavior
- Varying the slope parameter L between 42 MeV and 58 MeV changes the tidal polarizability by less than 2% for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star
- The seventeen RMF models predict canonical neutron star radii between 12.33 km and 13.22 km
- The uncertainty in measuring the mass-weighted tidal polarizability for a single Advanced LIGO-Virgo detector is given by Δλ̃ = 1.0 × 10^42 (M/M⊙)^2.5 (M2/M1)^0.1 (f_end/Hz)^(-2.2) (D/100 Mpc) cm^2 g s^2
- For the Einstein Telescope, the corresponding uncertainty coefficient is α = 8.4 × 10^40 cm^2 g s^2
- The symmetry energy with IU-FSU is 40% to 60% higher than with SkIU-FSU in the density range of 3ρ0 to 4ρ0

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of tidal polarizability by Fattoyev et al. (2012) and gravitational wave signals from deformed pulsars by Krastev et al. (2008), using a consistent set of model equations of state that satisfy modern constraints from both nuclear experiments and astrophysical observations. The results demonstrate that future gravitational wave detectors, particularly the Einstein Telescope, could provide stringent constraints on the high-density behavior of the symmetry energy, complementing terrestrial experiments and neutrino observations from core-collapse supernovae as proposed by Roberts et al. (2011).
