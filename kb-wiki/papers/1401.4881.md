# Partial and Quasi Dynamical Symmetries in Nuclei

> One of the interesting aspects in the study of atomic nuclei is the strikingly regular behaviour many display in spite of being complex quantum-mechanical systems, prompting the universal question of how regularity emerges out of complexity. It is often conjectured that symmetries play a pivotal role in our understanding of this emerging simplicity. But most symmetries are likely to be broken, partial or both. Under such more realistic conditions, does the concept of symmetry still provide a basis for our understanding of regularity? I suggest that this requires the enlarged concepts of partial and quasi dynamical symmetry.

# Partial and Quasi Dynamical Symmetries in Nuclei

## Key claim

The author argues that the standard concept of dynamical symmetry is too restrictive to explain the regular behavior observed in complex nuclear systems, and that the enlarged concepts of partial dynamical symmetry (PDS) and quasi dynamical symmetry (QDS) are required. The claim is hedged by the observation that most symmetries in realistic nuclei are likely to be broken, partial, or both, yet large regions of parameter space in the interacting boson model remain amenable to some symmetry treatment when these extended concepts are applied.

## Method

The paper uses the extended consistent-Q formalism (ECQF) of the interacting boson model (IBM) as a laboratory to investigate symmetry properties across the Casten triangle of parameter space. Wave-function entropy (WFE) is computed for eigenstates with angular momentum L=0 and boson number N=15 in U(5), SU(3), and SO(6) bases to quantify the degree of dynamical symmetry, partial dynamical symmetry, and quasi dynamical symmetry. For QDS, a similarity measure based on the overlap of eigenstate decompositions across different angular momenta (L=0 to 10) is used. A separate shell-model analysis of seniority in the j=9/2 shell is presented for the nuclei 94Ru and 72Ni.

## Key numbers

- The 8+ isomer in 94Ru lies at 2.644 MeV with a half-life of 71 microseconds
- The 8+ level in 72Ni has a half-life of less than 20 nanoseconds
- The ECQF hamiltonian parameters range over 0 ≤ ξ ≤ 1 and -√7/2 ≤ χ ≤ 0 for the conventional Casten triangle
- Boson number N=15 is used for the WFE calculations
- Angular momentum values L=0,2,...,10 are used for the QDS similarity measure
- Seniority conservation for any two-body interaction is exact for j ≤ 7/2, while partial seniority conservation is a geometric property valid only for j=9/2

## Context

This work extends the foundational dynamical symmetry analyses of the IBM by Arima and Iachello (1975, 1976) and the ECQF formulation by Warner and Casten (1983). It builds on the formal definitions of PDS by Leviatan (2011) and QDS by Rochford and Rowe (1988). The paper demonstrates that while exact dynamical symmetries are confined to small regions of the Casten triangle (the U(5), SU(3), and SO(6) limits), PDS and QDS apply to much larger parameter regions, enabling a symmetry-based understanding of nuclear collective behavior where previously none was thought to exist. The seniority analysis connects to the work of Racah (1943) and de-Shalit and Talmi (1963), and explains the existence of seniority isomers in semi-magic nuclei such as 94Ru, 212Pb, 214Pb, and 128Pd.
