# Probing neutron-star matter in the lab: similarities and differences between binary mergers and heavy-ion collisions

> Binary neutron-star mergers and heavy-ion collisions are related through the properties of the hot and dense nuclear matter formed during these extreme events. In particular, low-energy heavy-ion collisions offer exciting prospects to recreate such {extreme} conditions in the laboratory. However, it remains unexplored to what degree those collisions can actually reproduce hot and dense matter formed in binary neutron star mergers. As a way to understand similarities and differences between these systems, we {discuss their geometry and }perform a direct numerical comparison of the thermodynamic conditions probed in both collisions. To enable a direct comparison, we employ a finite-temperature equation of state able to describe the entire high-energy phase diagram of Quantum Chromodynamics. Putting side by side the evolution of both systems, we find that laboratory heavy-ion collisions at the energy range of $E_{\mathrm{lab}}=0.4 - 0.6\ A$ MeV probe (thermodynamic) states of matter that are very similar to those created in binary neutron-star mergers. These results can inform future low-energy heavy-ion collisions probing this regime.

## Key claim

Laboratory heavy-ion collisions at beam energies of $E_{\mathrm{lab}}=0.4-0.6\ A$ MeV probe thermodynamic states of matter that are very similar to those created in binary neutron-star mergers, specifically along isentropes of entropy per baryon $S/A = [1.8, 2.2]$, but this similarity is confined mostly to the merger phase and depends on using the same microphysical equation of state for both systems.

## Method

The authors performed relativistic hydrodynamics simulations of both binary neutron-star mergers (using general-relativistic hydrodynamics with the Frankfurt/IllinoisGRMHD code) and low-energy heavy-ion collisions (using the Frankfurt SHASTA code), employing the same Chiral Mean Field (CMF) equation of state across both systems. For heavy-ion collisions, they simulated near-central Au+Au collisions at lab energies of 450 and 600 A MeV with impact parameter $b=2$ fm; for binary neutron-star mergers, they simulated equal-mass irrotational binaries with total masses of 2.6 and 2.8 solar masses. They compared the thermodynamic conditions by binning fluid cells by temperature and density across the full spacetime evolution, using entropy per baryon as a figure of merit.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of the CMF model: $n_{\mathrm{sat}} = 0.15\ \mathrm{fm^{-3}}$
- Binding energy per nucleon: $E_0/B = -15.2$ MeV
- Symmetry energy: $S_0 = 31.9$ MeV
- Symmetry energy slope: $L = 57$ MeV
- Incompressibility: $K_0 = 267$ MeV
- Beam energies studied for heavy-ion collisions: $E_{\mathrm{lab}} = 450$ and $600\ A$ MeV
- Total binary masses studied: $M_{\mathrm{tot}} = 2.6$ and $2.8\ M_\odot$
- Entropy per baryon range characteristic of hot matter in both systems: $S/A = [1.8, 2.2]$
- Maximum densities reached in binary neutron-star mergers: $n \lesssim 3.4\ n_{\mathrm{sat}}$
- Maximum temperatures reached in binary neutron-star mergers: $T \lesssim 40$ MeV
- Freeze-out density for heavy-ion collisions: $n \approx \tfrac{1}{2} n_{\mathrm{sat}} \approx 0.08\ \mathrm{fm^{-3}}$
- Grid spacing for heavy-ion collision simulations: $\Delta x = 0.2$ fm
- Highest resolution for binary neutron-star merger simulations: $\Delta x = 250$ m

## Context

This work provides the first direct numerical comparison of thermodynamic conditions between binary neutron-star mergers and heavy-ion collisions using a consistent microphysical equation of state. It extends prior work by Danielewicz (2002) on pressure constraints from low-energy heavy-ion collisions and by Huth (2021) on joint constraints from multi-messenger observations, and it directly addresses claims from the HADES collaboration (2019) about forming hot matter at several times saturation density in low-energy collisions. The results enable future low-energy heavy-ion experiments at facilities like GSI to be designed specifically to reproduce binary neutron-star merger conditions, and they inform interpretation of post-merger gravitational-wave signals for next-generation detectors.
