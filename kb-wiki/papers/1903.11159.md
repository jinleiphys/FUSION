# Electric and magnetic dipole modes in high-resolution inelastic proton scattering at $0^\circ$

> Inelastic proton scattering under extreme forward angles including $0^\circ$ and at energies of a few hundred MeV has been established as a new spectroscopic tool for the study of complete dipole strength distributions in nuclei. Such data allow an extraction of the electric dipole polarizability which provides important constraints parameters of the symmetry energy, which determine the neutron skin thickness and the equation of state (EOS) of neutron-rich matter. Also new insight into the much-debated nature of the pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) is obtained. Additionally, the isovector spin-M1 resonance can be studied in heavy nuclei, where only limited experimental information exists so far. Together with much improved results on the isoscalar spin-M1 strength distributions in $N = Z$ nuclei, these data shed new light on the phenomenon of quenching of the nuclear spin response. Using dispersion matching techniques, high energy resolution ($ΔE/E \leq 10^{-4} \,\,$ full width at half maximum, FWHM) can be achieved in the experiments. In spherical-vibrational nuclei considerable fine structure is observed in the energy region of the isovector giant dipole resonance (IVGDR). A quantitative analysis of the fine structure with wavelet methods provides information on the role of different damping mechanisms contributing to the width of the IVGDR. Furthermore, level densities can be extracted from a fluctuation analysis at excitation energies well above neutron threshold, a region hardly accessible by other means. The combination of the gamma strength function (GSF) extracted from the E1 and M1 strength distributions with the independently derived level density permits novel tests of the Brink-Axel hypothesis underlying all calculations of statistical model reaction cross sections in astrophysical applications in the energy region of the PDR.

## Key claim

Inelastic proton scattering at extreme forward angles including 0° and at energies of a few hundred MeV has been established as a spectroscopic tool that can extract complete dipole strength distributions in nuclei, but the method's utility depends on achieving high energy resolution (ΔE/E ≤ 10⁻⁴ FWHM) through dispersion matching techniques, and the extracted electric dipole polarizability provides constraints on the symmetry energy parameters that determine neutron skin thickness and the equation of state of neutron-rich matter.

## Method

The authors review the experimental technique of inelastic proton scattering at 0° using dispersion matching to achieve high energy resolution, enabling separation of electric (E1) and magnetic (M1) dipole modes. The method extracts dipole strength distributions by measuring cross sections at extreme forward angles and analyzing the fine structure of the isovector giant dipole resonance (IVGDR) using wavelet methods to study damping mechanisms. Level densities are extracted from fluctuation analysis at excitation energies above the neutron threshold, and the gamma strength function (GSF) from E1 and M1 strength distributions is combined with independently derived level densities to test the Brink-Axel hypothesis.

## Key numbers

- Energy resolution: ΔE/E ≤ 10⁻⁴ full width at half maximum (FWHM) achieved through dispersion matching techniques
- The electric dipole polarizability extracted from the data provides constraints on parameters of the symmetry energy, which determine the neutron skin thickness and the equation of state of neutron-rich matter
- Level densities can be extracted from fluctuation analysis at excitation energies well above the neutron threshold, a region hardly accessible by other means

## Context

This work extends the experimental program on dipole strength distributions pioneered by Kneissl, Pietralla, and Zilges (2006) and the theoretical framework of Love and Franey (1981) for proton-nucleus interactions. The technique enables new tests of the Brink-Axel hypothesis (Brink 1955, Axel 1962) underlying statistical model reaction cross sections in astrophysical applications, and provides constraints on the symmetry energy that complement studies by Lattimer (2012), Hebeler et al. (2015), and the neutron star merger observations of Abbott et al. (2017). The method opens the possibility of studying the pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) nature, the isovector spin-M1 resonance in heavy nuclei, and the quenching of the nuclear spin response in N=Z nuclei.
