# Jet and Leading Hadron Production in High-energy Heavy-ion Collisions

> Jet tomography has become a powerful tool for the study of properties of dense matter in high-energy heavy-ion collisions. I will discuss recent progresses in the phenomenological study of jet quenching, including momentum, colliding energy and nuclear size dependence of single hadron suppression, modification of dihadron correlations and the soft hadron distribution associated with a quenched jet.

# Jet and Leading Hadron Production in High-energy Heavy-ion Collisions

## Key claim
Jet quenching in high-energy heavy-ion collisions, as studied through single hadron suppression, dihadron correlations, and azimuthal anisotropy, provides evidence for the formation of a strongly interactive quark-gluon plasma (sQGP), but the quantitative extraction of medium properties depends on the assumed energy-loss mechanism (e.g., QCD vs. non-QCD) and the kinematic regime, with the initial gluon density estimated to be about 30 times higher than in cold nuclear matter only under specific model assumptions.

## Method
The paper uses a twist-expansion framework within the collinear factorized parton model to derive medium-modified fragmentation functions via modified DGLAP evolution equations, incorporating the Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal (LPM) interference effect through twist-four parton matrix elements. Single inclusive hadron spectra and back-to-back dihadron spectra are then calculated by convolving nuclear parton distributions, elementary parton scattering cross sections, and these modified fragmentation functions, with the medium gluon density parameterized as a one-dimensionally expanding system. The model is calibrated to reproduce the suppression of single hadron spectra in central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s)=200 GeV, and then used to predict centrality, energy, and nuclear-size dependence.

## Key numbers
- Initial gluon density in central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s)=200 GeV is estimated to be about 30 times higher than in cold Au nuclei, at an initial time tau_0=0.2 fm/c.
- The energy density in central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s)=200 GeV is estimated to be about 100 times higher than in cold nuclear matter.
- The parton energy loss for a gluon is 9/4 times larger than for a quark (Delta E_g/Delta E_q=9/4) in the QCD case.
- At sqrt(s)=200 GeV, the hadron suppression factor R_AA(p_T) is almost independent of p_T in the range p_T=5-20 GeV.
- At sqrt(s)=63 GeV, R_AA(p_T) decreases with p_T for p_T around 10 GeV.
- The difference between QCD and non-QCD energy loss cases is about 50% at sqrt(s)=200 GeV and grows to about a factor of 2 at LHC energy (sqrt(s)=5500 GeV).
- The away-side two-hadron correlation I_AA(z_T) starts to increase at small z_T<0.2.
- The K factor used in the parton model is approximately 1.5-2 to account for higher-order pQCD corrections.

## Context
This work extends the phenomenological study of jet quenching originally proposed by Bjorken (1982) and developed in the HIJING model by Wang and Gyulassy (1992), and builds on the theory of non-Abelian radiative energy loss incorporating LPM interference effects. The twist-expansion approach for medium-modified fragmentation functions was previously applied to deeply inelastic scattering (DIS) on nuclear targets, successfully describing HERMES data (Wang and Wang, 2002). The paper enables future studies of jet tomography using direct gamma-tagged jets, which would avoid trigger bias effects present in hadron-triggered measurements, and suggests that the observed angular distribution of soft hadrons relative to quenched jets could be explained by either Mach cone formation from sonic shock waves (Stoecker, 2005) or Cerenkov-like gluon bremsstrahlung (Ruppert, 2005).
