# Dynamical determination of the quadrupole mass moment of a white dwarf

> In this letter we dynamically determine the quadrupole mass moment Q of the magnetic white dwarf WD 0137-349 by analyzing the period of the recently discovered brown dwarf moving around it in a close 2-hr orbit. It turns out that a purely Newtonian model for the orbit of WD 0137-349B, assumed circular and equatorial, is adequate, given the present-day accuracy in knowing the orbital parameters of such a binary system. Our result is Q=(-1.4615 +/- 0.9004) 10^47 kg m^2 for i=35 deg. It is able to accommodate the 3-sigma significant discrepancy of (1.0 +/- 0.3) 10^-8 s^-2 between the inverse square of the phenomenologically determined orbital period and the inverse square of the calculated Keplerian one. The impact of i, for which a range Delta i of possible values close to 35 deg is considered, is investigated as well.

## Key claim
The quadrupole mass moment \(Q\) of the white dwarf WD 0137-349 is dynamically determined from the orbital period of its brown dwarf companion, under the assumption that the orbit is circular and equatorial; the result is able to accommodate a 3-sigma discrepancy between the phenomenologically measured orbital period and the purely Keplerian period, but the derived \(Q\) is highly sensitive to the assumed orbital inclination \(i\).

## Method
The author models the relative motion of the brown dwarf (WD 0137-349B) around the white dwarf using a Newtonian radial acceleration that includes a quadrupole term from the white dwarf's gravitational potential, assuming a circular equatorial orbit. The quadrupole mass moment \(Q\) is extracted from an exact expression relating \(Q\) to the orbital period \(P_{\rm b}\), the semimajor axis \(a\), and the total mass \(M\), using independently measured masses and the phenomenologically determined orbital period. Post-Newtonian corrections are shown to be negligible given the current measurement precision.

## Key numbers
- \(Q = (-1.4615 \pm 0.9004) \times 10^{47}\ \text{kg m}^2\) for an assumed inclination \(i = 35\ \text{deg}\).
- The discrepancy between the inverse square of the measured orbital period and the inverse square of the Keplerian period is \((1.0 \pm 0.3) \times 10^{-8}\ \text{s}^{-2}\) at the 3-sigma level for \(i = 35\ \text{deg}\).
- For \(i = 30\ \text{deg}\), \(Q = (-3.9541 \pm 1.8210) \times 10^{47}\ \text{kg m}^2\); for \(i = 40\ \text{deg}\), \(Q = (-4.657 \pm 4.983) \times 10^{46}\ \text{kg m}^2\).
- A variation in inclination of \(\Delta i / i = 11\%\) (i.e., \(i = 35 \pm 2\ \text{deg}\)) yields \(\Delta Q / Q \approx 83\%\); a variation of \(\Delta i / i = 28\%\) yields \(\Delta Q / Q \approx 200\%\).
- The orbital period of the brown dwarf is \(P_{\rm b} = 0.0803 \pm 0.0002\ \text{days}\); the projected semimajor axis is \(a_{\rm bc} \sin i = 0.375 \pm 0.014\ R_{\odot}\).

## Context
This work extends the author’s previous method for determining the quadrupole mass moment in the double pulsar PSR J0737-3039A/B (Iorio 2006) and for placing upper limits in millisecond-pulsar binaries (Iorio 2006). It applies the same technique to the white-dwarf–brown-dwarf binary WD 0137-349, using orbital parameters from Maxted (2006) and others. The result provides a dynamical measurement of a white dwarf’s quadrupole moment, which can inform models of the equation of state of dense matter; future work could re-analyze the raw data with a model that includes the quadrupole term directly.
