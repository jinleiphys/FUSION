# Future physics opportunities for high-density QCD at the LHC with heavy-ion and proton beams

> The future opportunities for high-density QCD studies with ion and proton beams at the LHC are presented. Four major scientific goals are identified: the characterisation of the macroscopic long wavelength Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP) properties with unprecedented precision, the investigation of the microscopic parton dynamics underlying QGP properties, the development of a unified picture of particle production and QCD dynamics from small (pp) to large (nucleus--nucleus) systems, the exploration of parton densities in nuclei in a broad ($x$, $Q^2$) kinematic range and the search for the possible onset of parton saturation. In order to address these scientific goals, high-luminosity Pb-Pb and p-Pb programmes are considered as priorities for Runs 3 and 4, complemented by high-multiplicity studies in pp collisions and a short run with oxygen ions. High-luminosity runs with intermediate-mass nuclei, for example Ar or Kr, are considered as an appealing case for extending the heavy-ion programme at the LHC beyond Run 4. The potential of the High-Energy LHC to probe QCD matter with newly-available observables, at twice larger center-of-mass energies than the LHC, is investigated.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the LHC heavy-ion programme can achieve a comprehensive understanding of high-density QCD by pursuing four major scientific goals, but this requires a prioritized high-luminosity Pb-Pb and p-Pb programme in Runs 3 and 4, complemented by high-multiplicity pp studies and a short oxygen run, with the caveat that the feasibility of reaching the full HL-LHC design luminosity depends on solving beam-physics challenges such as bound-free pair production losses and collimation efficiency.

## Method

The paper reviews the achieved heavy-ion performance of the LHC in Runs 1 and 2, including Pb-Pb and p-Pb collision parameters, and projects the expected performance for Runs 3 and 4 (HL-LHC) and the proposed HE-LHC. It presents beam parameter tables comparing design values, achieved values from 2010-2018, and upgrade goals, and discusses the technical challenges of collimation, bound-free pair production, and beam-beam effects for future runs.

## Key numbers

- Pb-Pb peak luminosity in 2018 reached 6.1 x 10^27 cm^-2 s^-1, exceeding the original design value of 1 x 10^27 cm^-2 s^-1 by a factor of more than 6
- Integrated Pb-Pb luminosity per experiment in 2018 reached 900-1800 microbarn^-1, compared to the design goal of 1000 microbarn^-1
- HL-LHC upgrade target for Pb-Pb peak luminosity is 7 x 10^27 cm^-2 s^-1, with an integrated luminosity of 10^4 microbarn^-1 per experiment over 4 runs
- p-Pb peak luminosity in 2016 reached 850 x 10^27 cm^-2 s^-1, compared to the design value of 150 x 10^27 cm^-2 s^-1
- Projected p-Pb integrated luminosity for ATLAS and CMS in a future run is approximately 0.7 pb^-1, a factor of 3.5 larger than the 2016 integrated luminosity
- Bound-free pair production cross-section at 6.37 Z TeV is approximately 276 barn, leading to secondary beam power of up to 120 W at peak luminosity
- Stored Pb beam energy in 2018 reached 13.3 MJ per beam, with the HL-LHC target of 21 MJ per beam

## Context

This paper extends the LHC heavy-ion programme beyond the original LHC Design Report (2004) and the p-Pb design study by Salgado et al. (2011), documenting how the achieved performance has already exceeded design specifications. It enables future precision studies of Quark-Gluon Plasma properties, parton dynamics, and nuclear parton densities, and opens the question of whether the proposed HE-LHC, operating at twice the centre-of-mass energy, can probe QCD matter with new observables. The paper also identifies open technical challenges, particularly the management of bound-free pair production losses and the need for additional collimators (TCLDs) in the dispersion suppressors to safely handle higher stored beam energies.
