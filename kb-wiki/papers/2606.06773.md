# Lepton interactions from GeV to EeV

> In this work, we investigate the phenomenological consequences of neutrino and muon interactions with matter. In our studies, we focused in phenomenological predictions for two experiments: FASER and IceCube. FASER is a detector located at the LHC that measures neutrinos produced in proton-proton collisions. A new version of FASER, FASER2, has been proposed to operate in the Forward Physics Facility during the high-luminosity regime of the LHC. The intense flux of tau neutrinos expected at FASER2 motivated us to study the polarization effects of the tau produced in charged current interactions. Our results show that the produced taus will not be completely polarized. Among the Standard Model particles, only neutrinos and muons produced in proton-proton collisions at the LHC can reach FASER. In our study, we show that muon-initiated events can reveal interesting nucleon properties, such as nuclear effects and the existence of an intrinsic charm. The high number of events induced by neutrinos at FASER motivated us to study rare processes in neutrino interaction, such as the neutrino trident. Our results indicate that the neutrino trident process can be observed at FASER2. We have also studied muon trident at the LHC, and we showed that tau pair production can be observed for the first time in this reaction. In contrast to neutrinos detected at the LHC, the neutrinos observed at IceCube come from natural sources, being mainly atmospheric and astrophysical neutrinos. IceCube is capable of observing neutrinos across a wide energy spectrum, ranging from a few GeV to beyond PeV. We show that the study of these events can contribute to our understanding of the structure of target hadrons, as well as the search for physics effects beyond the Standard Model in the propagation of these neutrinos in the universe until they reach the Earth.

## Key claim
The paper claims that tau leptons produced in charged-current tau neutrino interactions at FASER2 will not be completely polarized, and that the neutrino trident process can be observed at FASER2 with more than 5 sigma statistical significance for the first time, but these predictions depend on the high-luminosity operation of the Forward Physics Facility and, for the trident sensitivity to beyond-Standard-Model effects, on a future FASER2 operating at the Future Circular Collider.

## Method
The author performed phenomenological studies of neutrino and muon interactions with matter, focusing on predictions for the FASER/FASER2 detectors at the LHC and for IceCube. For FASER2, the author calculated tau polarization effects in charged-current interactions, including the role of the structure function F5, and evaluated the observability of neutrino trident (pair production of charged leptons via gauge boson fusion in neutrino-nucleus interactions) and muon trident processes. For IceCube, the author analyzed High Energy Start Events to study hadron structure and beyond-Standard-Model effects in neutrino propagation.

## Key numbers
- Event rates at FASER2 are predicted to be leveraged by a factor of approximately 200 compared to the current FASER detector.
- The neutrino trident process at FASER2 is predicted to be observable with more than 5 sigma of statistical significance.
- IceCube observes neutrinos across a wide energy spectrum ranging from a few GeV to beyond PeV.
- The most energetic neutrino ever observed by humans, of extraterrestrial origin, has an estimated energy of over 200 PeV (measured by KM3NeT).

## Context
This work extends phenomenological studies of high-energy neutrino interactions at collider and astrophysical experiments, building on the first observations of collider neutrinos by the FASER and SND@LHC Collaborations (2023-2024) and on IceCube's detection of astrophysical neutrinos (2013). The study of tau polarization and the structure function F5 in tau neutrino interactions at FASER2 connects to prior work on weak interaction structure functions, while the neutrino trident analysis enables searches for physics beyond the Standard Model. The muon-initiated event studies at FASER probe nucleon properties such as nuclear effects and intrinsic charm, complementing measurements from experiments like MINERvA and T2K.
