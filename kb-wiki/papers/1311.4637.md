# Determination of compositeness of the Lambda(1405) resonance from its radiative decay

> The radiative decay of $Λ(1405)$ is investigated from the viewpoint of compositeness, which corresponds to the amount of two-body states composing resonances as well as bound states. For a $\bar{K}N (I=0)$ bound state without couplings to other channels, we establish a relation between the radiative decay width and the compositeness. Especially the radiative decay width of the bound state is proportional to the compositeness. Applying the formulation to $Λ(1405)$, we observe that the decay to $Λγ$ is dominated by the $K^{-}p$ component inside $Λ(1405)$, because in this decay $π^{+} Σ^{-}$ and $π^{-} Σ^{+}$ strongly cancel each other and the $πΣ$ component can contribute to the $Λγ$ decay only through the slight isospin breaking. This means that the decay $Λ(1405) \to Λγ$ is suitable for the study of the $\bar{K} N$ component in $Λ(1405)$. Fixing the $Λ(1405)$-$πΣ$ coupling constant from the usual decay of $Λ(1405) \to πΣ$, we show a relation between the absolute value of the $\bar{K} N$ compositeness for $Λ(1405)$ and the radiative decay width of $Λ(1405) \to Λγ$ and $Σ^{0} γ$, and we find that large decay width to $Λγ$ implies large $\bar{K}N$ compositeness for $Λ(1405)$. By using the "experimental" data on the radiative decay widths, which is based on an isobar model fitting of the $K^{-}p$ atom data, we estimate the $\bar{K}N$ compositeness for $Λ(1405)$. We also discuss the pole position dependence of our relation on the $Λ(1405)$ radiative decay width and the effects of the two-pole structure for $Λ(1405)$.

## Key claim

The radiative decay width of Λ(1405) → Λγ is proportional to the K⁻p component inside Λ(1405) and therefore can be used to determine the magnitude of the K̅N compositeness, provided one fixes the Λ(1405)–πΣ coupling constant from the observed Λ(1405) → πΣ decay width and accounts for the two-pole structure of Λ(1405).

## Method

The authors formulate the Λ(1405) radiative decay to Λγ and Σ⁰γ using a chiral unitary approach, where the decay amplitude is computed from meson-baryon loop diagrams (K⁻p, π⁺Σ⁻, π⁻Σ⁺, K⁺Ξ⁻) with the Λ(1405) coupling constants taken from the pole residues of the scattering amplitude. The compositeness X_i for each two-body channel is defined as X_i = – g_i² dG_i/d√s evaluated at the pole position, and the radiative decay width is expressed as a function of the K̅N compositeness by relating the squared coupling constant g_{K̅N}² to X_{K̅N} through the derivative of the loop integral. The calculation uses physical hadron masses with slight isospin breaking but assumes isospin symmetry for the coupling constants except where chiral unitary results are employed.

## Key numbers

- The πΣ compositeness of Λ(1405) is |X_{πΣ}| = 0.19, evaluated using M_{Λ(1405)} = 1405 MeV and Γ_{Λ(1405)} = 50 MeV from the Particle Data Group, giving |g_{πΣ}| = 0.91.
- For a K̅N bound state without couplings to other channels, the ratio Γ_{Λγ} / Γ_{Σ⁰γ} is approximately 14, arising from the SU(3) coupling strengths: Ṽ_{K⁻pΛ} ≈ –0.63/f and Ṽ_{K⁻pΣ⁰} ≈ 0.17/f.
- Two binding energies are considered for the K̅N bound state: B_E = 10 MeV (M_B = 1425 MeV) and B_E = 30 MeV (M_B = 1405 MeV).
- The radiative decay width of the K̅N bound state is proportional to the K̅N compositeness X_{K̅N} for both Λγ and Σ⁰γ final states.

## Context

This work extends the concept of compositeness, previously applied to the ρ meson (Aceti 2012) and K* meson (Xiao 2012), to the Λ(1405) resonance by using radiative decay as a probe of the K̅N component. It builds on the chiral unitary approach of Kaiser (1995), Oset (1997), and Jido (2003), which dynamically generates Λ(1405) as a meson-baryon bound state and predicts a two-pole structure. The radiative decay widths were previously calculated in several models (Geng 2007, Doring 2010), but this work provides a direct relation between the decay width and the compositeness, enabling experimental determination of the K̅N component from measured radiative widths. The method can be applied to future data from K⁻p atom experiments or photoproduction measurements to distinguish between the two Λ(1405) poles.
