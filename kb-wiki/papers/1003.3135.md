# On the balance energy and nuclear dynamics in peripheral heavy-ion collisions

> We present here the system size dependence of balance energy for semi-central and peripheral collisions using quantum molecular dynamics model. For this study, the reactions of $Ne^{20}+Ne^{20}$, $Ca^{40}+Ca^{40}$, $Ni^{58}+Ni^{58}$, $Nb^{93}+Nb^{93}$, $Xe^{131}+Xe^{131}$ and $Au^{197}+Au^{197}$ are simulated at different incident energies and impact parameters. A hard equation of state along with nucleon-nucleon cross-sections between 40 - 55 mb explains the data nicely. Interestingly, balance energy follows a power law $\propto{A^τ}$ for the mass dependence at all colliding geometries. The power factor $τ$ is close to -1/3 in central collisions whereas it is -2/3 for peripheral collisions suggesting stronger system size dependence at peripheral geometries. This also suggests that in the absence of momentum dependent interactions, Coulomb's interaction plays an exceedingly significant role. These results are further analyzed for nuclear dynamics at the balance point.

## Key claim

The mass dependence of the balance energy follows a power law \(\propto A^{\tau}\) at all colliding geometries, but the power factor \(\tau\) is close to \(-1/3\) for central collisions and \(-2/3\) for peripheral collisions, indicating a stronger system size dependence at peripheral geometries; this deviation from the central-collision trend suggests that Coulomb repulsion plays an increasingly significant role in the absence of momentum-dependent interactions.

## Method

The authors simulated symmetric reactions of \(^{20}\text{Ne}+^{20}\text{Ne}\), \(^{40}\text{Ca}+^{40}\text{Ca}\), \(^{58}\text{Ni}+^{58}\text{Ni}\), \(^{93}\text{Nb}+^{93}\text{Nb}\), \(^{131}\text{Xe}+^{131}\text{Xe}\), and \(^{197}\text{Au}+^{197}\text{Au}\) using the quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) model with a hard equation of state and constant nucleon-nucleon cross-sections between 40 and 55 mb. They extracted the balance energy (energy of vanishing flow) at reduced impact parameters \(\hat{b} = 0.15, 0.30, 0.45, 0.60\) by analyzing the slope of the transverse momentum distribution as a function of rapidity and the time evolution of the total directed transverse momentum.

## Key numbers

- For the \(^{40}\text{Ca}+^{40}\text{Ca}\) reaction, the balance energy is 50–57 MeV/nucleon for central collisions (\(\hat{b}=0.15\)) and increases to 149.5 MeV/nucleon at \(\hat{b}=0.60\).
- For the \(^{20}\text{Ne}+^{20}\text{Ne}\) reaction, the balance energy is 76 MeV/nucleon at \(\hat{b}=0.15\) and rises to 360 MeV/nucleon at \(\hat{b}=0.60\).
- For the \(^{197}\text{Au}+^{197}\text{Au}\) reaction, the balance energy lies between 40 and 78 MeV/nucleon.
- The power-law exponent \(\tau\) for the mass dependence of balance energy is \(-0.25\) at \(\hat{b}=0.15\), \(-0.22\) at \(\hat{b}=0.30\), \(-0.34\) at \(\hat{b}=0.45\), and \(-0.53\) at \(\hat{b}=0.60\) (excluding the \(^{20}\text{Ne}+^{20}\text{Ne}\) reaction from the fit).
- At \(\hat{b}=0.45\), the exponent \(\tau\) is \(-0.44\) for \(\sigma_{NN}=40\) mb and \(-0.37\) for \(\sigma_{NN}=55\) mb; at \(\hat{b}=0.60\), \(\tau\) is \(-0.66\) for 40 mb and \(-0.57\) for 55 mb.
- The time for which density exceeds half of normal matter density (\(\rho > 0.5\rho_0\)) follows a power law \(\propto A^{\tau'}\) with \(\tau' = 0.42\) for central collisions and \(\tau' = 0.67\) at \(\hat{b}=0.9\); for \(^{197}\text{Au}+^{197}\text{Au}\) this time is 80 fm/c at \(\hat{b}=0.15\) and 20 fm/c at \(\hat{b}=0.9\).

## Context

This work extends the study of balance energy by Puri and Sood (2010) from central and semi-central collisions to semi-central and peripheral collisions across a wide mass range (A=40 to 394). It confirms the earlier finding of a \(-1/3\) power law for central collisions reported by Westfall (1993) and others, but reveals a steeper \(-2/3\) dependence for peripheral collisions, which the authors attribute to the enhanced role of Coulomb repulsion in low-density, peripheral geometries. The results provide a systematic parameterization of balance energy for non-central collisions and motivate future studies with momentum-dependent interactions to test whether they alter the observed trends.
