# COBRA - Double beta decay searches using CdTe detectors

> A new approach (called COBRA) for investigating double beta decay using CdTe (CdZnTe) semiconductor detectors is proposed. It follows the idea that source and detector are identical. This will allow simultaneous measurements of 5 $β^-β^-$ - and 4 $β^+β^+$ - emitters at once. Half-life limits for neutrinoless double beta decay of Cd-116 and Te-130 can be improved by more than one order of magnitude with respect to current limits and sensitivities on the effective Majorana neutrino mass of less than 1 eV can be obtained. Furthermore, for the first time a realistic chance of observing double electron capture processes exists. Additional searches for rare processes like the 4-fold forbidden Cd-113 $β$-decay, the electron capture of Te-123 and dark matter detection can be performed. The achievable limits are evaluated for 10 kg of such detectors and can be scaled accordingly towards higher detector masses because of the modular design of the proposed experiment.

## Key claim

The COBRA experiment proposes that using CdTe (or CdZnTe) semiconductor detectors as both source and detector for double beta decay searches can improve half-life limits for neutrinoless double beta decay of Cd-116 and Te-130 by more than one order of magnitude, but this sensitivity depends on achieving a background-free detector and assumes the experimentally obtained background levels of 0.2 counts/keV/kg/a for Te-130 and 0.03 counts/keV/kg/a for Cd-116.

## Method

The experiment uses CdTe (or CdZnTe) semiconductor detectors where the double beta decay isotopes are intrinsic to the detector material itself, allowing source and detector to be identical. The detectors can be operated either by measuring the sum energy of both electrons or as pixelised detectors for simultaneous tracking and energy measurement. The proposed setup consists of an array of CdTe detectors installed between two NaI detectors for active veto and coincidence measurements, surrounded by an OFHC copper shield, low-level lead, and a muon veto of plastic scintillators.

## Key numbers

- For Cd-116 two-neutrino double beta decay, an assumed half-life of 3 x 10^19 a produces a count rate of 94 events/day with 10 kg of detectors.
- For Te-130 two-neutrino double beta decay, an assumed half-life range of 0.7 - 2.7 x 10^21 a produces 6 - 23 events/day, corresponding to a 6.4% - 24.5% contribution to the Cd-116 spectrum.
- Current lower limits on neutrinoless double beta decay half-lives are 7 x 10^22 a for Cd-116 and 1.44 x 10^23 a for Te-130 (both at 90% confidence level).
- For the double electron capture of Cd-106, a theoretical predicted half-life of 4 x 10^20 a gives an expected count rate of about 550 events/year.
- For the double electron capture of Te-120, the expected number is 1.2 x 10^7 events/day, compared to a current limit on the beta+/EC decay of 4.2 x 10^12 a.
- The Te-123 electron capture transition energy is 51.3 +/- 0.2 keV, with a disputed half-life ranging from about 10^13 years to 2.4 +/- 0.9 x 10^19 a.
- The Cd-113 four-fold forbidden beta decay has an uncertain half-life of about 8 x 10^15 a and a Q-value of about 320 keV.

## Context

This work extends the approach of using semiconductor detectors as both source and detector for double beta decay, following earlier attempts with CdTe by Mitchel (1988). The proposed experiment can probe the DAMA dark matter evidence within reasonable time scales and can address the discrepancy in Te-123 half-life measurements between Watt (1962) and Alessandrello (1996). The modular design allows scaling to higher detector masses, and the results would complement existing Ge-76 limits from Baudis (1999) while addressing uncertainties from nuclear matrix element calculations.
