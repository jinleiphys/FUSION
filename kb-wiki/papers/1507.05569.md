# Fluid dynamic propagation of initial baryon number perturbations on a Bjorken flow background

> Baryon number density perturbations offer a possible route to experimentally measure baryon number susceptibilities and heat conductivity of the quark gluon plasma. We study the fluid dynamical evolution of local and event-by-event fluctuations of baryon number density, flow velocity and energy density on top of a (generalized) Bjorken expansion. To that end we use a background-fluctuation splitting and a Bessel-Fourier decomposition for the fluctuating part of the fluid dynamical fields with respect to the azimuthal angle, the radius in the transverse plane and rapidity. We examine how the time evolution of linear perturbations depends on the equation of state as well as on shear viscosity, bulk viscosity and heat conductivity for modes with different azimuthal, radial and rapidity wave numbers. Finally we discuss how this information is accessible to experiments in terms of the transverse and rapidity dependence of correlation functions for baryonic particles in high energy nuclear collisions.

## Key claim

The paper studies how initial baryon number density perturbations evolve hydrodynamically on top of a Bjorken expansion background, showing that the time evolution of these linear perturbations depends on the equation of state as well as on shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, and heat conductivity; the authors explicitly hedge that they concentrate on initial state fluctuations rather than thermodynamic fluctuations, and that their numerical calculations use simplified parametrizations of transport coefficients from strongly coupled theories as toy models.

## Method

The authors perform a background-fluctuation splitting of the relativistic fluid dynamic equations at finite baryon chemical potential, then apply a Bessel-Fourier decomposition for the fluctuating parts of the fluid fields with respect to azimuthal angle, transverse radius, and rapidity. They solve the linearized evolution equations for perturbations of baryon number density, flow velocity, and energy density on top of a (generalized) Bjorken boost-invariant background, using both an ideal gas equation of state and a lattice-QCD-based parametrization from the Wuppertal collaboration (Borsanyi et al., 2012), with transport coefficients taken from strongly coupled holographic theories.

## Key numbers

* Shear viscosity over entropy ratio is taken as \(\eta/s = 1/(4\pi)\) for strongly coupled theories (Policastro et al., 2001), with the authors varying this value to study "weak" and "strong" regimes.
* The lattice QCD parametrization for the trace anomaly at \(\mu=0\) uses parameters \(h_0 = 0.1396\), \(h_1 = -0.1800\), \(h_2 = 0.0350\), \(f_0 = 2.76\), \(f_1 = 6.79\), \(f_2 = -5.29\), \(g_1 = -0.47\), \(g_2 = 1.04\) (Borsanyi et al., 2012).
* The lattice QCD parametrization for the leading-order Taylor coefficient \(\chi_2(T)\) uses parameters \(h_3 = -0.5022\), \(h_4 = 0.5950\), \(f_3 = 0.0940\), \(f_4 = 6.3290\), \(f_5 = -4.8303\) (Borsanyi et al., 2012).
* The lattice-based equation of state is stated to be valid for \(\mu/T < 3\) in the temperature window \(0 < T < 400\) MeV.
* For weakly coupled QCD, the shear viscosity scales as \(\eta(T) \sim T^3/(g^4 \log(1/g))\) (Arnold et al., 2000, 2003, 2006).
* For strongly coupled theories, heat conductivity is given by \(\kappa(T,\mu) = 8\pi^2 (T/\mu^2) \eta(T,\mu)\) (Son and Starinets, 2006).

## Context

This work extends the formalism of background-fluctuation splitting and Bessel-Fourier decomposition previously developed for energy density perturbations (Floerchinger, 2011, 2013, 2014) to the case of baryon number density perturbations, which have been largely neglected in high-energy heavy ion collisions where baryon chemical potential is small. The study enables a potential experimental route to measure baryon number susceptibilities and heat conductivity of the quark-gluon plasma via correlation functions of baryonic particles, complementing existing efforts to locate the QCD critical point through cumulants of particle multiplicity distributions (Stephanov, 1998, 1999; Berdnikov and Rajagopal, 1999; Athanasiou et al., 2010).
