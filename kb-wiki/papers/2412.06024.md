# Quasiparticle second-order dissipative hydrodynamics at finite chemical potential

> We extend the derivation of second-order relativistic viscous hydrodynamics to incorporate the effects of baryon current, a non-vanishing chemical potential, and a realistic equation of state. Starting from a microscopic quantum theory, we employ a quasiparticle approximation to describe the evolution of hydrodynamic degrees of freedom and establish its connection to the Wigner formalism. Using methods from relativistic kinetic theory, we perform a second-order expansion to derive a closed set of equations for the components of the stress-energy tensor and the baryon current. The resulting transport coefficients, which depend on the equation of state, are obtained through a unified prescription that ensures thermodynamic consistency.

## Key claim

The authors extend the derivation of second-order relativistic viscous hydrodynamics to include baryon current and finite chemical potential by employing a quasiparticle approximation with temperature- and chemical-potential-dependent masses, but the quasiparticles "cannot correspond to known particles" and are expected to represent either dressed quarks and gluons or mesons and hadrons depending on the expansion stage.

## Method

Starting from a microscopic quantum theory, the authors employ a quasiparticle approximation to describe the evolution of hydrodynamic degrees of freedom and establish its connection to the Wigner formalism. Using methods from relativistic kinetic theory, they perform a second-order expansion to derive a closed set of equations for the components of the stress-energy tensor and the baryon current. The resulting transport coefficients, which depend on the equation of state, are obtained through a unified prescription that ensures thermodynamic consistency by adding an extra contribution to the energy-momentum tensor interpreted as a mean-field interaction in the Boltzmann equation.

## Context

This work extends the Israel-Stewart second-order theory (Israel 1976, Israel 1979) to incorporate finite chemical potential and baryon current, which is relevant for heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC where baryon stopping creates regions with non-zero baryon density. The quasiparticle approach builds on earlier work by Gorenstein (1995), Romatschke (2011), Sasaki (2008), and Bluhm (2010) who introduced temperature-dependent masses to achieve agreement with lattice QCD results. The derivation enables future extraction of transport coefficients such as shear viscosity (η), bulk viscosity (ζ), and charge diffusion (κ) from a thermodynamically consistent framework that can reproduce any equation of state at finite chemical potential.
