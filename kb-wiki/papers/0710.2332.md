# Application of the RMF mass model to the r-process and the influence of mass uncertainties

> A new mass table calculated by the relativistic mean field approach with the state-dependent BCS method for the pairing correlation is applied for the first time to study r-process nucleosynthesis. The solar r-process abundance is well reproduced within a waiting-point approximation approach. Using an exponential fitting procedure to find the required astrophysical conditions, the influence of mass uncertainty is investigated. R-process calculations using the FRDM, ETFSI-Q and HFB-13 mass tables have been used for that purpose. It is found that the nuclear physical uncertainty can significantly influence the deduced astrophysical conditions for the r-process site. In addition, the influence of the shell closure and shape transition have been examined in detail in the r-process simulations.

## Key claim

The solar r-process abundance pattern can be well reproduced using the new relativistic mean-field (RMF) mass table within a waiting-point approximation approach, but the deduced astrophysical conditions (neutron irradiation time and weighting factors) depend significantly on which mass model is used, with the RMF model requiring up to a factor of 4 longer timescales than the FRDM and HFB-13 models.

## Method

The authors applied the RMF mass table with state-dependent BCS pairing to a site-independent r-process model using sixteen components with neutron densities from 10^20 to 3×10^27 cm^-3 at a fixed temperature of 1.5 GK. They performed least-square fits to the solar r-process abundances using an exponential fitting procedure to determine the neutron irradiation time τ and weighting factor w as functions of neutron density. For comparison, they repeated the same procedure using the FRDM, ETFSI-Q, and HFB-13 mass tables while keeping the same β-decay properties from Möller (2003) and using experimental data when available.

## Key numbers

- The rms deviation of one-neutron separation energy S_n for the RMF model is 654 keV, compared to 399 keV (FRDM), 528 keV (ETFSI-Q), and 546 keV (HFB-13), for nuclei with Z, N ≥ 8.
- The best-fit r-process components using RMF masses give τ(n_n) = 0.454 n_n^0.040 s and w(n_n) = 2.1 n_n^0.02.
- The ratio of abundance at A=130 to abundance at A=195 using RMF masses is 2.8 when experimental data are included, increasing to 24.6 without experimental data; for FRDM masses the ratio changes from 4.0 to 1.3.
- The RMF fit requires component durations up to 6 s, while FRDM and HFB-13 simulations require only up to 1.5 s.
- The RMF shell gap at N=82 is around 2 MeV larger than other models for Z between 45 and 60.

## Context

This work extends the application of microscopic mass models to r-process nucleosynthesis by using the RMF mass table for the first time in such calculations, alongside the first use of the HFB-13 model. It builds on prior site-independent r-process studies using the FRDM model by Pfeiffer et al. (1997) and Kratz et al. (2006), and on the exponential fitting approach of Chen et al. (1995). The results show that nuclear mass uncertainty significantly affects the deduced astrophysical conditions, and that the abundance underproduction before the A~130 and A~195 peaks can be attributed to a combination of shell structure and shape transition effects, except for the HFB-13 case where β-decay rates are implicated. This enables future work to disentangle nuclear physics uncertainties from astrophysical conditions in dynamical r-process simulations.
