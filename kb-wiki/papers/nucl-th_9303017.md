# Quantum theory of large amplitude collective motion and the Born-Oppenheimer method

> We study the quantum foundations of a theory of large amplitude collective motion for a Hamiltonian expressed in terms of canonical variables. In previous work the separation into slow and fast (collective and non-collective) variables was carried out without the explicit intervention of the Born Oppenheimer approach. The addition of the Born Oppenheimer assumption not only provides support for the results found previously in leading approximation, but also facilitates an extension of the theory to include an approximate description of the fast variables and their interaction with the slow ones. Among other corrections, one encounters the Berry vector and scalar potential. The formalism is illustrated with the aid of some simple examples, where the potentials in question are actually evaluated and where the accuracy of the Born Oppenheimer approximation is tested. Variational formulations of both Hamiltonian and Lagrangian type are described for the equations of motion for the slow variables.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the Born-Oppenheimer approximation, when added to the authors' existing theory of large amplitude collective motion, provides support for earlier leading-order results and enables an extension that includes an approximate description of the fast variables and their interaction with the slow ones, including Berry vector and scalar potentials, but only under the restriction that the coordinate transformations are point transformations.

## Method

The authors start from a Hamiltonian expressed in canonical variables with a curved-space mass tensor and apply a point transformation to separate collective (slow) and non-collective (fast) coordinates. They then introduce the Born-Oppenheimer approximation to integrate out the fast variables, expanding the potential and mass tensor in powers of the non-collective coordinates and keeping terms up to second order. The fast-variable Hamiltonian is treated as a normal mode problem for each fixed value of the collective coordinates, and the effective collective Hamiltonian is constructed including correction terms from the zero-point motion, quantum potential from curved space, and Berry potentials.

## Key numbers

- The quantum potential from curved space (term Δ^(3)V) is of order N^{-2} compared to the main term in the potential energy, where N is the number of degrees of freedom.
- The zero-point motion contribution from the fast variables (Δ^(1)V + Δ^(4)V) is of order N^{-1} compared to the leading term.
- The fast-variable Hamiltonian H_NC takes the form H_NC(Q) = Σ_α (n̂_α + 1/2) Ω_α(Q), where Ω_α are normal mode frequencies dependent on the instantaneous value of the collective coordinates.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous theory of large amplitude collective motion (Klein and collaborators, 1980s) by adding the Born-Oppenheimer approximation, which was absent in earlier formulations. The approach is contrasted with molecular physics where the separation into slow and fast variables is more straightforward, and with the work of Bulgac (1980s) on simple examples and Girard, LeTourneux, and Vinet (1980s) on the dipole-quadrupole problem in spherical nuclei. The paper enables systematic inclusion of quantum corrections and Berry phase effects in nuclear collective motion calculations, with a companion paper in preparation that will compare this approach with the alternative method of Walet, Klein, and collaborators (1990s).
