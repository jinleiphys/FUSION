# Giant resonances under extreme conditions

> The theoretical description of nuclear resonances at zero and finite temperatures is presented. The following issues are addressed: 1) Giant dipole resonances (GDR) in highly excited nuclei, including both low and high regions of temperature. The results of calculations are obtained within the phonon-damping model, thermal shape-fluctuation model including thermal pairing, and compared with experimental data. 2) The electromagnetic cross sections of the double GDRs (DGDR) in Xe-136 and Pb-208. The results obtained in theoretical calculations are compared with the experimental data for the DGDR cross sections in exclusive measurements at near-relativistic energies. 3) GDR and pygmy dipole resonances (PDR) in neutron-rich nuclei, where the effect of coupling of the GDR to complicated configurations on the PDR is analyzed.

# Giant resonances under extreme conditions

## Key claim

The phonon-damping model (PDM) can simultaneously describe the temperature dependence of the giant dipole resonance (GDR) width, the electromagnetic cross sections of double giant dipole resonances (DGDR), and the pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) in neutron-rich nuclei, provided that thermal pairing fluctuations are included to explain the near-constant GDR width at low temperatures (T ≤ 1 MeV) and that anharmonicity between coupled GDRs is accounted for to reproduce the experimental DGDR cross sections.

## Method

The author employs the phonon-damping model (PDM), which couples a harmonic phonon field (representing collective vibrations such as the GDR) to a quasiparticle field via a Hamiltonian that includes superfluid pairing. The GDR width is decomposed into a quantal width (from particle-hole–phonon coupling) and a thermal width (from particle-particle and hole-hole–phonon coupling), with the polarization operator derived using double-time Green's functions. For finite temperatures, the modified BCS (MBCS) theory is used to include quasiparticle-number fluctuations, yielding a thermal pairing gap that does not vanish at a critical temperature. The DGDR strength function is calculated microscopically within the PDM, and the electromagnetic cross sections are obtained by folding photoabsorption cross sections with the virtual photon spectrum.

## Key numbers

- For ¹²⁰Sn, the MBCS pairing gap at T=0 is Δ̄(0) ≈ 1.4 MeV (with pairing parameter Gν = 0.13 MeV), and the BCS gap collapses at Tc ≈ 0.79 MeV, while the MBCS gap persists up to T ≈ 5 MeV.
- For ¹³⁶Xe GDR: theoretical energy 15.6 MeV vs. experimental 15.2 MeV; theoretical width 4.96 MeV vs. experimental 4.8 MeV; theoretical EM cross section 1676.28 mb vs. experimental 1420(42)±100 mb.
- For ²⁰⁸Pb GDR: theoretical energy 13.5 MeV vs. experimental 13.4 MeV; theoretical width 4.04 MeV vs. experimental 4.0 MeV; theoretical EM cross section 3039.67 mb vs. experimental 3280±50 mb.
- For ¹³⁶Xe DGDR: theoretical energy 29.2 MeV vs. experimental 28.3±0.7 MeV; theoretical width 7.0 MeV vs. experimental 6.3±1.6 MeV; theoretical EM cross section 159.33 mb vs. experimental 164(85)±35 mb.
- For ²⁰⁸Pb DGDR: theoretical energy 26.6 MeV vs. experimental 26.6±0.8 MeV; theoretical width 6.3 MeV vs. experimental 6.3±1.3 MeV; theoretical EM cross section 420.92 mb vs. experimental 380±40 mb.
- The experimental EM cross section enhancement over the harmonic folding model is about 178% for ¹³⁶Xe and 133% for ²⁰⁸Pb.
- The GDR width in ¹²⁰Sn remains nearly constant at T ≤ 1 MeV, increases sharply up to T ≈ 2–3 MeV, and saturates at higher T.

## Context

This work extends the phonon-damping model originally proposed in 1998 for hot GDR damping to include thermal pairing via the MBCS theory (Dang, 2006) and anharmonicity in DGDR. It addresses discrepancies between experimental DGDR cross sections and predictions from the harmonic folding model, which gave energies EDGDR = 2EGDR and widths ΓDGDR = 2ΓGDR (Lorentzian) or √2 ΓGDR (Gaussian). The results contradict predictions from relativistic RPA (which shows a clear PDR bump at ~8 MeV) and the particle-hole–phonon coupling model (which finds no collectivity below 10 MeV) for neutron-rich nuclei. The work enables future extraction of clean PDR signals in electromagnetic cross sections using low-energy (~50 MeV/n), high-intensity beams on neutron-rich oxygen and calcium isotopes.
