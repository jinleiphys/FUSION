# Skyrme-Rpa Description of Dipole Giant Resonance in Heavy and Superheavy Nuclei

> The E1(T=1) isovector dipole giant resonance (GDR) in heavy and super-heavy deformed nuclei is analyzed over a sample of 18 rare-earth nuclei, 4 actinides and three chains of super-heavy elements (Z=102, 114 and 120). Basis of the description is self-consistent separable RPA (SRPA) using the Skyrme force SLy6. The self-consistent model well reproduces the experimental data (energies and widths) in the rare-earth and actinide region. The trend of the resonance peak energies follows the estimates from collective models, showing a bias to the volume mode for the rare-earths isotopes and a mix of volume and surface modes for actinides and super-heavy elements. The widths of the GDR are mainly determined by the Landau fragmentation which in turn is found to be strongly influenced by deformation. A deformation splitting of the GDR can contribute about one third to the width and about 1 MeV further broadening can be associated to mechanism beyond the mean-field description (escape, coupling with complex configurations).

## Key claim

The self-consistent separable RPA (SRPA) with the Skyrme force SLy6 reproduces experimental GDR energies and widths in rare-earth and actinide nuclei, but the agreement is less perfect for Hf, W, and Os isotopes, where calculated strengths show slight down-shifts or up-shifts of up to about 1 MeV, partly attributable to the use of neutron yield rather than photoabsorption data.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent separable RPA (SRPA) calculations using the Skyrme functional SLy6 on a cylindrical coordinate-space grid with 0.7 fm mesh size, including contributions from time-odd current density, Coulomb, and BCS pairing. They used four input operators (rY1μ and j(qir)Y1μ with i=1,2,3) plus an r³Y3μ operator for multipole mixing, and smoothed the strength function with a Lorentzian averaging parameter Δ=2 MeV. Equilibrium quadrupole deformations were found by total energy minimization, and the configuration space extended to single-particle states up to about 65 MeV, exhausting 92-95% of the energy-weighted sum rule.

## Key numbers

- The calculated GDR energy centroids in rare-earth nuclei are closer to the Steinwedel-Jensen estimate E_SJ = 81 A^(-1/3) MeV, while in actinides and super-heavy nuclei they are closer to the Berman-Fultz estimate E_BF = (31.2 A^(-1/3) + 20.6 A^(-1/6)) MeV.
- Deformation splitting ΔE contributes about one third to the total GDR width, reaching a maximum of about 40% in the first half of the rare-earth region (A < 176) and 30-33% in actinides.
- An additional broadening of about 1 MeV from Lorentz averaging (Δ=2 MeV) suffices to reach realistic experimental patterns, simulating missing mechanisms beyond mean-field (escape widths, coupling with complex configurations).
- In deformed nuclei, the width of the μ=1 branch (Γ₁) is larger than the μ=0 branch (Γ₀); for example, in ¹⁵⁶Gd (β₂=0.347), Γ₀=3.05 MeV and Γ₁=4.74 MeV, while in spherical ³⁰⁴114 (β₂=-0.006), Γ₀=3.98 MeV and Γ₁=3.99 MeV.
- The collective shifts from unperturbed two-quasiparticle strengths to final RPA strengths are 4.0 MeV in ¹⁵⁶Gd and 3.4 MeV in ³⁰⁴120.
- The calculated quadrupole moments Q₂ agree excellently with experimental values for rare-earth and actinide nuclei and with macroscopic-microscopic model values from Baran (2005) for super-heavy nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous SRPA studies of GDR in spherical and deformed nuclei (Nesterenko 2002, 2006, 2007) to a systematic survey of 37 nuclei across rare-earth, actinide, and super-heavy regions, using the Skyrme force SLy6. It confirms that the Steinwedel-Jensen collective model works well for medium-heavy nuclei while a mix of Steinwedel-Jensen and Goldhaber-Teller scenarios (Berman-Fultz estimate) is more appropriate for heavy and super-heavy nuclei. The results enable predictions for GDR properties in super-heavy nuclei (Z=102, 114, 120) where no experimental data exist, and demonstrate that Landau fragmentation and deformation splitting dominate the GDR width (70-90%), with only about 1 MeV additional broadening needed from mechanisms beyond mean-field.
