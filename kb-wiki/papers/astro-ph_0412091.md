# Nuclear Input for Core-collapse Models

> We review the nuclear physics input necessary for the study of the collapse of massive stars as precursor to supernova explosions. Recent theoretical advances for the calculation of the relevant weak-interaction processes and their influence in the collapse and postbounce dynamics are discussed. Future improvements are expected to come with the advent of radioactive ion-beam facilities.

# Nuclear Input for Core-collapse Models

## Key claim

The paper reviews how improved shell-model calculations of weak-interaction rates (particularly electron capture and beta decay) significantly alter presupernova evolution compared to earlier standard tabulations, but notes that even with these improved nuclear inputs, no spherically symmetric simulation with accurate neutrino transport has yet produced a successful supernova explosion, suggesting that multi-dimensional effects or additional missing physics are required.

## Method

The authors review the nuclear physics input for core-collapse supernova models, focusing on weak-interaction processes (electron capture, beta decay, neutrino scattering) and their implementation in hydrodynamical simulations. They compare the standard Fuller-Fowler-Newman (FFN) rates with modern large-scale shell-model calculations for nuclei in the iron mass range (A=45-65), and discuss how these rates affect presupernova evolution, core collapse dynamics, and the postbounce phase when incorporated into stellar evolution codes and Boltzmann neutrino transport simulations.

## Key numbers

- Central electron-to-baryon ratio Y_e at onset of core collapse increased by 0.01–0.015 for shell-model rates compared to FFN rates; for a 20 M_sun star, Y_e changed from 0.43 (WW model) to 0.445 (new model), increasing the Chandrasekhar mass by about 0.075 M_sun
- Iron core masses are generally smaller in new models; for stars with M ≤ 20 M_sun the reduction is about 0.05 M_sun
- The bounce-shock must provide 1.5 × 10^51 erg per 0.1 M_sun of dissociated material between the inner core edge and iron core edge
- General relativistic effects reduce the inner core mass by about 20% compared to Newtonian simulations
- The inner core mass evolves proportionally to Y_e^2 during collapse
- Shell-model calculations use a quenching factor of (0.74)^2 for the Gamow-Teller strength (shown for 51V comparison with data)

## Context

This review extends the work of Fuller, Fowler and Newman (1980-1985) who provided the standard weak-interaction rate tabulations for astrophysical nuclei, and builds on shell-model calculations by Langanke and Martinez-Pinedo (2000, 2001) and Oda et al. (1994) for sd-shell and pf-shell nuclei. The paper discusses how these improved rates were incorporated into stellar evolution calculations by Heger, Langanke et al. (2001) and into core-collapse simulations with Boltzmann neutrino transport by Liebendoerfer, Rampp et al. (2005). The review enables future work with radioactive ion-beam facilities to measure Gamow-Teller distributions via charge-exchange reactions such as (t,3He) and (d,2He), which will further constrain the shell-model calculations for unstable nuclei that dominate electron capture during presupernova evolution.
