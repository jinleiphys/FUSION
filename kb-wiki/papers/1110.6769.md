# Potential Energy Surfaces of Actinide Nuclei from a Multi-dimensional Constraint Covariant Density Functional Theory: Barrier Heights and Saddle Point Shapes

> For the first time the potential energy surfaces of actinide nuclei in the $(β_{20}, β_{22}, β_{30})$ deformation space are obtained from a multi-dimensional constrained covariant density functional theory. With this newly developed theory we are able to explore the importance of the triaxial and octupole shapes simultaneously along the whole fission path. It is found that besides the octupole deformation, the triaxiality also plays an important role upon the second fission barriers. The outer barrier as well as the inner barrier are lowered by the triaxial deformation compared with axially symmetric results. This lowering effect for the reflection asymmetric outer barrier is 0.5 $\sim$ 1 MeV, accounting for $10 \sim 20%$ of the barrier height. With the inclusion of the triaxial deformation, a good agreement with the data for the outer barriers of actinide nuclei is achieved.

## Key claim

For the first time, the potential energy surfaces of actinide nuclei in the (β20, β22, β30) deformation space are obtained from a multi-dimensional constrained covariant density functional theory, revealing that triaxial deformation lowers the outer fission barrier by 0.5 to 1 MeV (10 to 20% of the barrier height) when both triaxial and octupole degrees of freedom are included simultaneously.

## Method

The authors performed one-, two-, and three-dimensional constrained calculations using covariant density functional theory (CDFT) with the PC-PK1 parameter set, breaking both axial and reflection symmetries to allow triaxial (β22) and octupole (β30) deformations. The nuclear shape was parametrized using mass multipole operators, and the BCS approach with a delta pairing force was implemented. For the outer barrier, a two-dimensional calculation in the (β20, β30) plane was first performed to identify the lowest fission path, then a one-dimensional β20-constraint calculation along that path allowed triaxial and octupole deformations to locate the second saddle point.

## Key numbers

- The triaxial deformation lowers the outer barrier of 240Pu by about 1 MeV compared with axially symmetric results.
- For most actinide nuclei investigated, the triaxiality lowers the outer barrier by 0.5 to 1 MeV, accounting for about 10 to 20% of the barrier height.
- The triaxial deformation reduces the inner barrier height of 240Pu by more than 2 MeV.
- For the actinide nuclei studied, the triaxiality lowers the inner barrier heights by 1 to 4 MeV.
- The binding energy of 240Pu at β20=1.3 varies by only about 130 keV when Ncut increases from 16 to 18, and by 20 keV from 18 to 20, ensuring 0.2 MeV accuracy with Ncut=16.
- Around the first saddle point of 240Pu, β22 values are about 0.06 to 0.12 (γ ~ 10°); around the outer barrier, β22 values are about 0.02 to 0.03 (γ ~ 2°).
- For 238U, the triaxiality reduces the inner barrier height by about 1.5 MeV.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of fission barriers in actinide nuclei that separately considered triaxial deformation for the inner barrier (e.g., Girod (1983), Abusara (2010)) and octupole deformation for the outer barrier (e.g., Rutz (1995)). It directly addresses the point raised by Skalski (1991) that there is no reason for a fissioning nucleus not to penetrate all symmetry-breaking shapes on its way from the first to the second saddle. The multi-dimensional constraint CDFT enables simultaneous treatment of triaxial and octupole degrees of freedom, achieving good agreement with empirical barrier heights from RIPL-3 (Capote, 2009) and providing a framework for more accurate predictions of fission barriers in superheavy nuclei.
