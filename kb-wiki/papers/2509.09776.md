# Extraction of neutron-capture cross sections on $^{92}$Zr using the charge-exchange Oslo method

> The $^{93}$Nb($t$,$^{3}$He) reaction at 115 MeV/u was studied to demonstrate that nuclear level densities and $γ$-ray strength functions can be extracted from charge-exchange reactions at intermediate energies using the Oslo technique. The matrix of excitation energy in $^{93}$Zr, reconstructed from the ($t$,$^{3}$He) reaction, versus the energy of $γ$ rays emitted by the excited $^{93}$Zr nuclei, was obtained in an experiment with the S800 Spectrograph operated in coincidence with the GRETINA $γ$-ray detector. The extracted level density and $γ$-ray strength function obtained by applying the Oslo method to this matrix were used to estimate the $^{92}$Zr($n$,$γ$)$^{93}$Zr cross section by combining the new results with other experimental data and theoretical calculations for $E$1 and $M$1 strength functions at higher energies. Good agreement with direct measurements of the $^{92}$Zr($n$,$γ$)$^{93}$Zr cross section was found. The contribution from the upbend in the extracted $γ$-ray strength function was important to achieve the consistency as the neutron-capture cross section without this contribution is significantly below the direct measurements otherwise. Since charge-exchange reactions at intermediate energies have long been used for extracting Gamow-Teller strengths, the successful demonstration of the charge-exchange Oslo method enables experiments in which ($n$,$γ$) cross sections and Gamow-Teller strengths can be measured simultaneously, which is of benefit for astrophysical studies.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that nuclear level densities and gamma-ray strength functions can be extracted from charge-exchange reactions at intermediate energies using the Oslo technique, but only for excitation energies up to 7.2 MeV in the residual nucleus, which is above the neutron separation energy of 6.734 MeV due to the hindrance of neutron emission by the angular momentum barrier for high-spin states.

## Method

The authors performed the 93Nb(t,3He) reaction at 115 MeV/u using a secondary triton beam at the NSCL Coupled Cyclotron Facility, detecting 3He particles in the S800 Spectrograph in coincidence with gamma rays from the de-excitation of 93Zr in GRETINA. The resulting excitation-energy versus gamma-energy matrix was unfolded for the detector response, reduced to a primary gamma-ray matrix using an iterative procedure, and then fitted to extract the nuclear level density and gamma-ray transmission coefficient via a chi-squared minimization procedure. The extracted level density and gamma-ray strength function were normalized using known low-lying states, neutron resonance data, and the average total radiative width at the neutron separation energy.

## Key numbers

* The neutron separation energy Sn of 93Zr is 6.734 MeV.
* The average s-wave resonance spacing D0 at Sn is (4.0 +/- 0.5) x 10^-3 MeV.
* The average total radiative width at Sn, <Gamma_gamma0>, is 95 +/- 10 meV.
* The nuclear temperature TCT from the Constant Temperature model fit is 0.74 (+0.12/-0.09) MeV.
* The level density at Sn was calculated using D0 and spin distribution models, with the fraction of states missed at Sn ranging from 5% to 25% depending on the assumed spin range and spin cut-off model.
* The extracted gamma-ray strength function was used to estimate the 92Zr(n,gamma)93Zr cross section, with the upbend contribution being important for consistency with direct measurements.

## Context

This work extends the Oslo method, previously applied to reactions such as (p,p') and (d,p), to charge-exchange reactions at intermediate energies, specifically the (t,3He) reaction. The charge-exchange Oslo method enables simultaneous extraction of neutron-capture cross sections and Gamow-Teller strengths from a single measurement, which is beneficial for astrophysical studies of nucleosynthesis and weak interaction rates. The method was validated by comparing the indirectly determined 92Zr(n,gamma)93Zr cross section with direct measurements from Macklin (1967), Ohgama (2005), and Tagliente (2010, 2022), and the results provide new information about Maxwellian averaged cross sections at temperatures above 30 keV, which were previously uncertain.
