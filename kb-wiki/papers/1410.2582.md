# Elastic nucleon-nucleus scattering as a direct probe of correlations beyond the independent-particle model

> Employing a recently-developed dispersive optical model (DOM) which allows a complete description of experimental data both above (up to 200 MeV) and below the Fermi energy in $^{40}$Ca, we demonstrate that elastic nucleon-nucleus scattering data constrain the spectral strength in the continuum of orbits that are nominally bound in the independent-particle model. In the energy domain between 0 and 200 MeV, the integrated strength or depletion number is highly sensitive to the separation of the IPM orbit to the scattering continuum. This sensitivity is determined by the influence of the surface-absorption properties of the DOM self-energy. For an ab initio calculation employing the self-energy of the charge-dependent Bonn (CDBonn) interaction which only includes the effect of short-range correlations, no such sensitivity is obtained and a depletion of 4% is predicted between 0 and 200 MeV irrespective of the orbit. The ab initio spectral strength generated with the CDBonn interaction approaches the empirical DOM spectral strength at 200 MeV. Both spectral distributions allow for an additional 3-5% of the strength at even higher energies which is associated with the influence of short-range correlations. We suggest that the non-local form of the DOM allows for an analysis of elastic-nucleon-scattering data that directly determines the depletion of bound orbits. While obviously relevant for the analysis of elastic nucleon scattering on stable targets, this conclusion holds equally well for experiments involving rare isotopes in inverse kinematics as well as experiments with electrons on atoms or molecules.

## Key claim
Elastic nucleon-nucleus scattering data, when analyzed with a nonlocal dispersive optical model (DOM), can directly constrain the spectral strength in the continuum for orbits that are nominally bound in the independent-particle model (IPM), thereby providing a quantitative measure of their depletion. This sensitivity is conditional on the surface-absorption properties of the DOM self-energy and is not reproduced by an *ab initio* calculation that includes only short-range correlations.

## Method
The authors employed a nonlocal dispersive optical model (DOM) for \(^{40}\)Ca, previously constrained by all available experimental data up to 200 MeV, to calculate the reducible self-energy in momentum space. They then computed the particle spectral function and folded it with overlap functions of bound orbits to extract the spectral strength in the continuum for each orbit. These DOM results were contrasted with an *ab initio* calculation using the charge-dependent Bonn (CDBonn) interaction, which includes only short-range correlations.

## Key numbers
- For the DOM analysis of \(^{40}\)Ca, the depletion number integrated from 0 to 200 MeV (\(d_{nlj}[0,200]\)) ranges from 0.032 for the \(0s_{1/2}\) orbit to 0.320 for the \(0f_{5/2}\) orbit.
- The CDBonn calculation predicts a uniform depletion of 4% (0.04) between 0 and 200 MeV for all orbits, with specific values such as 0.035 for \(0s_{1/2}\) and 0.036 for \(0f_{5/2}\).
- Both the DOM and CDBonn spectral distributions allow for an additional 3-5% of the strength at energies above 200 MeV, associated with short-range correlations.
- The sum rule fraction \(n_{n \ell j} + d_{n \ell j}[\varepsilon_F,200]\) for the DOM ranges from 0.947 to 0.970 across all listed orbits.

## Context
This work extends the dispersive optical model pioneered by Mahaux and Sartor (1991) and builds on a recent nonlocal DOM analysis of \(^{40}\)Ca by Hossein et al. (2014) that described all data below the Fermi energy and elastic-scattering data. It contrasts with *ab initio* calculations using the CDBonn interaction (Machleidt, 1995) that only include short-range correlations, as applied to \(^{40}\)Ca by Dussan et al. (2011). The results enable future nonlocal DOM analyses of elastic nucleon scattering on rare isotopes in inverse kinematics, as well as analogous studies in atomic and molecular physics.
