# Quantum Field Theory in Large N Wonderland: Three Lectures

> In these lecture notes, I review how to use large N techniques to solve quantum field theories in various dimensions. In particular, the case of N-dimensional quantum mechanics, non-relativistic cold and dense neutron matter, and scalar field theory in four dimensions are covered. A recurring theme is that large N solutions are fully non-perturbative, and can be used to reliably access quantum field theory for parameter regions where weak-coupling expansions simply fail.

# Quantum Field Theory in Large N Wonderland: Three Lectures

## Key claim

Large N expansion techniques can provide fully non-perturbative, first-principles quantitative solutions to quantum field theories in parameter regions where weak-coupling expansions fail, though the leading-order results typically differ from exact numerical results by about 30 percent when extrapolated to N=1, with next-to-leading order corrections reducing this discrepancy to about 13 percent.

## Method

The author introduces auxiliary fields via Hubbard-Stratonovich transformations to convert quartic interactions into quadratic forms, then integrates out the original fields to obtain an effective action. The path integral is evaluated in the large N limit by treating the zero-mode of the auxiliary field as the saddle point and expanding fluctuations in powers of 1/N, with the leading-order contribution coming from the saddle point and next-to-leading order from Gaussian fluctuations around it.

## Key numbers

- Ground state energy for N-dimensional quartic oscillator at large N: E(ζ*) = 3(2λ)^(1/3)N/8 ≈ λ^(1/3)N × 0.47247
- Exact ground state energy for N=1 quartic anharmonic oscillator: E₀ = λ^(1/3) × 0.66799
- Next-to-leading order large N ground state energy: E₀ = (2λ)^(1/3)(3N/8 + (√6-2)/2) + O(N⁻¹)
- Neutron s-wave scattering length: a₀ ≈ -18.5 fm
- Leading-order large N result for pure neutron matter energy per particle is about 30 percent higher than Monte Carlo calculations from Gezerlis et al. (2009, 2011), Lynn et al. (2015, 2018), and Lu et al. (2018)

## Context

These lecture notes extend the large N techniques developed in the 1970s by t'Hooft (1973), Abbott (1975), and Linde (1976) for scalar and fermionic theories, which had remained somewhat dormant since then. The work enables first-principles calculations of transport coefficients, finite temperature correlators, finite-density systems, and real-time evolution in quantum field theory, with particular applications to the O(N) model in 2+1 dimensions studied by Romatschke (2019, 2021) and to non-relativistic neutron matter using pionless EFT as developed by Bedaque (2002) and Kaplan (1998).
