# Cavitation and thermal photon production in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> We investigate the thermal photon production-rates using one dimensional boost-invariant second order relativistic hydrodynamics to find proper time evolution of the energy density and the temperature. The effect of bulk-viscosity and non-ideal equation of state are taken into account in a manner consistent with recent lattice QCD estimates. It is shown that the \textit{non-ideal} gas equation of state i.e $ε-3 P \neq 0$ behaviour of the expanding plasma, which is important near the phase-transition point, can significantly slow down the hydrodynamic expansion and thereby increase the photon production-rates. Inclusion of the bulk viscosity may also have similar effect on the hydrodynamic evolution. However the effect of bulk viscosity is shown to be significantly lower than the \textit{non-ideal} gas equation of state. We also analyze the interesting phenomenon of bulk viscosity induced cavitation making the hydrodynamical description invalid. It is shown that ignoring the cavitiation phenomenon can lead to a very significant over estimation of the photon flux. It is argued that this feature could be relevant in studying signature of cavitation in relativistic heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the non-ideal equation of state (where ε − 3P ≠ 0) near the phase transition temperature significantly slows hydrodynamic expansion and increases thermal photon production rates, while bulk viscosity can induce cavitation that invalidates the hydrodynamic description; ignoring cavitation leads to a very significant overestimation of the photon flux.

## Method

The authors use one-dimensional boost-invariant second-order Israel-Stewart relativistic hydrodynamics to compute the proper time evolution of energy density and temperature in the QGP fireball. They incorporate a non-ideal equation of state parameterized from lattice QCD results (Bazavov et al., 2009) and a temperature-dependent bulk viscosity to entropy density ratio ζ/s parameterized from Meyer (2008), with shear viscosity fixed at η/s = 1/4π. Thermal photon production rates from Compton scattering, qq̅ annihilation, bremsstrahlung, and annihilation-with-scattering processes are integrated over the hydrodynamic space-time history to obtain the total photon spectrum.

## Key numbers

- With non-ideal equation of state and no viscosity, the system takes almost double the time to reach Tc compared to the ideal massless equation of state case.
- For the base bulk viscosity parameters (a = 0.901, ΔT = Tc/14.5), cavitation occurs at τc ≈ 2.5 fm/c when the system temperature is still ∼210 MeV, well above Tc = 0.190 GeV.
- If cavitation is ignored, the system would take τf = 5.5 fm/c to reach Tc, leading to overestimation of photon rates by ∼200% at p⊥ = 0.5 GeV and ∼50% at p⊥ = 2 GeV.
- At photon energy E = 1 GeV, the photon flux for the non-ideal equation of state is 60% larger than for the ideal equation of state case.
- Reducing ΔT to half its base value enhances photon production by ∼75% at p⊥ = 0.5 GeV and ∼55% at p⊥ = 1 GeV; reducing to ΔT/4 enhances production by ∼110% at p⊥ = 0.5 GeV and ∼80% at p⊥ = 1 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of bulk viscosity effects in heavy-ion collisions from earlier studies by Kouno et al. (2010) on cavitation and by Monnai and Hirano (2008) on bulk viscosity in one-dimensional hydrodynamics. It also builds on thermal photon production calculations using viscous hydrodynamics by Dusling and Teaney (2009) and by the authors' previous work on shear viscosity and photons. The results enable experimental searches for cavitation signatures in RHIC data and provide conservative estimates of bulk viscosity effects on photon spectra, noting that the one-dimensional boost-invariant approximation underestimates bulk viscosity effects compared to full three-dimensional treatments.
