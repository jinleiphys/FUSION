# Thermal Evolution and Light Curves of Young Bare Strange Stars

> The cooling of a young bare strange star is studied numerically by solving the equations of energy conservation and heat transport for both normal and superconducting strange quark matter inside the star. We show that the thermal luminosity from the strange star surface, due to both photon emission and e+e- pair production, may be orders of magnitude higher than the Eddington limit, for about one day for normal quark matter but possibly for up to a hundred years for superconducting quark matter, while the maximum of the photon spectrum is in hard X-rays with a mean energy of ~ 100 keV or even more. This differs both qualitatively and quantitatively from the photon emission from young neutron stars and provides a definite observational signature for bare strange stars. It is shown that the energy gap of superconducting strange quark matter may be estimated from the light curves if it is in the range from ~ 0.5 MeV to a few MeV.

## Key claim

The thermal luminosity of a young bare strange star can exceed the Eddington limit by many orders of magnitude for up to about one day if the quark matter is normal, and possibly for up to a hundred years if the quark matter is in a superconducting phase with secondary pairing, provided the strange star is bare (i.e., has no normal-matter envelope) and the surface temperature remains above roughly 5 × 10^8 K.

## Method

The authors solved the general relativistic equations of energy conservation and heat transport for a 1.4 solar mass strange star, using a Henyey-type cooling code. They modeled the star's interior with an equation of state for strange quark matter (bag constant B = (140 MeV)^4, QCD coupling α_s = 0.3, strange quark mass m_s = 150 MeV) and considered four scenarios: normal (unpaired) quark matter, the 2SC superconducting phase, a mixture of 2SC and CFL phases, and the 2SC phase with secondary pairing of the u_3-d_3 and s quarks. The surface boundary condition used the energy flux from thermal photons and e+e- pair emission from the bare quark surface.

## Key numbers

- The thermal luminosity from the strange star surface may be many orders of magnitude higher than the Eddington limit (L_Edd ≈ 1.3 × 10^38 (M/M_sun) erg/s) for a period of several days after formation for normal quark matter.
- For superconducting quark matter with secondary pairing (cases [B] and [C]), super-Eddington luminosities may persist for up to about 10^7 seconds (~115 days) and 3 × 10^10 seconds (~950 years), respectively.
- The mean energy of photons far from the strange star is ~100 keV or more, and when the photon luminosity decreases from ~10^41 erg/s to ~10^36 erg/s, the mean photon energy increases from ~30 keV to ~500 keV.
- The energy gap of superconducting strange quark matter that may be estimated from light curves is in the range from ~0.5 MeV to a few MeV.
- The critical temperature of superconducting quark matter is related to the zero-temperature energy gap by T_c ≈ 0.57 Δ(0).

## Context

This work extends the earlier studies of bare strange stars by Alcock, Farhi, and Olinto (1986) and Usov (1998, 2001), who showed that the enormous surface electric field of a bare strange star induces intense e+e- pair production and hard X-ray emission above the Eddington limit. The present paper provides the first detailed numerical models of the thermal evolution and light curves of such stars, considering both normal and superconducting quark matter phases. The results differ qualitatively and quantitatively from the thermal emission of young neutron stars and from strange stars with a crust of normal matter, as studied by Schaab et al. (1997) and Page et al. (2000). The calculations enable observational discrimination between bare strange stars and neutron stars, and suggest that the energy gap of superconducting quark matter could be inferred from observed light curves.
