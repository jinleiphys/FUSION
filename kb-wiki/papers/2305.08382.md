# Systematic study of fusion barriers with energy dependent barrier radius

> Considering energy dependence of the barrier radius in heavy-ion fusion reactions, a modified Siwek-Wilczyński (MSW) fusion cross section formula is proposed. With the MSW formula, the fusion barrier parameters for 367 reaction systems are systematically extracted, based on 443 datasets of measured cross sections. We find that the fusion excitation functions for about $60\%$ reaction systems can be better described by introducing the energy dependence of the barrier radius which is due to the dynamical effects at energies near and below the barrier. Considering both the influence of the geometry radii and that of the reduced de Broglie wavelength of the colliding nuclei, the barrier heights are well reproduced with only one model parameter. The extracted barrier radius parameters linearly decrease with the effective fissility parameter, and the width of the barrier distribution relates to the barrier height and as well as the reduced de Broglie wavelength at energies around the Coulomb barrier.

# Systematic study of fusion barriers with energy dependent barrier radius

## Key claim
The authors propose a modified Siwek-Wilczyński (MSW) formula that introduces an energy-dependent barrier radius, finding that fusion excitation functions for about 60% of reaction systems can be better described by this modification, particularly due to dynamical effects at energies near and below the Coulomb barrier. The improvement is conditional: for 173 out of 443 datasets the relative improvement in the combined chi-squared measure is smaller than 0.1%, while for 122 datasets the improvement exceeds 10%.

## Method
The authors performed time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations for 16O+208Pb and 34S+168Er to extract the energy dependence of the barrier radius, then proposed an empirical correction term to the barrier radius involving a complementary error function. They fitted the four-parameter MSW formula (barrier height V_B, standard deviation W, traditional barrier radius R_0, and correction factor ΔR) to 443 datasets of measured fusion and fission cross sections for 367 projectile-target combinations using the Minuit minimization program, combining chi-squared per point and logarithmic mean-square deviation into a single figure of merit.

## Key numbers
- 443 datasets of measured fusion (and/or fission) cross sections for 367 different projectile-target combinations were analyzed
- For about 60% of reaction systems, the fusion excitation functions are better described with the energy-dependent barrier radius
- The barrier height V_B is reproduced with an RMS deviation of 1.52 MeV for all considered reactions using only one model parameter
- The relative RMS error for barrier height predictions is 2.83% with the proposed formula, compared to 4.29% from the three-parameter WKJ formula and 2.84% from the two-parameter MCW formula
- The extracted barrier radius parameter R_0/(A_1^{1/3}+A_2^{1/3}) linearly decreases with effective fissility parameter x_eff, from about 1.6 fm for very light systems to about 1.0 fm for heavy systems
- For 34S+168Er at tip-side orientation, the radius parameter falls from 1.4 fm at E ≈ V_B to 1.1 fm at E ≈ 1.3 V_B
- The proposed formula for R_0 is R_0 = (1.62 - 0.57 x_eff)(A_1^{1/3}+A_2^{1/3})
- The proposed formula for W is W = (0.014 + 0.135 λ̄_B) V_B, where λ̄_B is the reduced de Broglie wavelength at E = V_B

## Context
This work extends the systematic barrier extraction approach of Wen et al. (2022) and Jiang et al. (2018), who applied the standard SW formula to 29 reaction systems, by incorporating energy dependence of the barrier radius motivated by TDHF calculations and the generalized Wong formula of Rowley and Hagino (2015). The energy-dependent barrier radius accounts for dynamical effects such as nuclear density rearrangement and neutron transfer in the neck region, which are not captured by the standard SW formula. The extracted barrier systematics enable improved predictions of fusion cross sections for heavy-ion reactions relevant to super-heavy element synthesis, though the systematics of the correction factor ΔR remain unclear and require further investigation of shell effects and Q-value changes due to nucleon transfer.
