> Lectures delivered at the 17th National Nuclear Physics Summer School 2005, Berkeley, CA, June 6-17, 2005.

# Five lectures on effective field theory

## Key claim
Effective field theory (EFT) provides a systematic framework for expanding Lagrangians in local operators involving only light degrees of freedom, where the expansion is in powers of external momenta divided by the scale of heavy physics. The utility of EFT extends beyond simplifying perturbative calculations to enabling predictive power in nonperturbative theories like low-energy QCD, where power counting of operators is combined with symmetry constraints of the underlying theory.

## Method
The lectures develop EFT by first analyzing scaling dimensions of operators in relativistic scalar field theory, classifying operators as relevant, marginal, or irrelevant based on how their coefficients scale with momentum. The analysis is extended to nonrelativistic theories where time and space scale differently (t → ξ²t, x → ξx), and to Heavy Quark Effective Theory (HQET) where the heavy quark momentum is decomposed as p^μ = m v^μ + k^μ with residual momentum k^μ of order Λ_QCD. Several qualitative applications are worked through: Fermi's theory of weak interactions, Rayleigh scattering of light from neutral atoms, and the binding energy of charmonium in nuclei.

## Key numbers
- Fermi constant: G_F = 1.166 × 10⁻⁵ GeV⁻², derived from matching tree-level W/Z exchange to the effective four-fermion interaction
- W boson mass: approximately 80 GeV; Z boson mass: approximately 91 GeV
- QCD scale Λ_QCD: approximately 200 MeV, used as the typical momentum scale for gluons in a nucleus
- Neutrino cross-section scales as σ_ν ≃ G_F² s, explaining why low-energy neutrinos are difficult to detect
- Rayleigh scattering cross-section scales as σ ∝ E_γ⁴ r₀⁶, growing as the fourth power of photon energy
- Charmonium binding energy estimate: E_B ∼ r₀³ Λ_QCD⁴ ≃ Λ_QCD⁴ / (α_s m_c)³

## Context
These lectures extend the pedagogical tradition of EFT reviews by Georgi (1994), Cohen (1993), Manohar (1996), Polchinski (1992), Schafer (2003), and Lepage (1997), providing an idiosyncratic treatment focused on the author's own research applications. The work enables practitioners to construct EFTs for systems with multiple length scales, from weak interactions (Fermi theory) to nonrelativistic bound states (NRQED, NRQCD) and heavy quark systems (HQET). The scaling analysis of nonrelativistic theories shows that the φ⁴ interaction, marginal for relativistic scalars, becomes irrelevant for nonrelativistic particles, which has direct consequences for understanding δ-function potentials in the Schrödinger equation.
