# Chiral Corrections and the Axial Charge of the Delta

> Chiral corrections to the delta axial charge are determined using heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory. Knowledge of this axial coupling is necessary to assess virtual-delta contributions to nucleon and delta observables. We give isospin relations useful for a lattice determination of the axial coupling. Furthermore we detail partially quenched chiral corrections, which are relevant to address partial quenching and/or mixed action errors in lattice calculations of the delta axial charge.

## Key claim
Chiral corrections to the delta axial charge are computed using heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory, but the modulus |GΔΔ| remains relatively stable under these corrections, while the real and imaginary parts individually show significant pion-mass dependence and scale uncertainty from the unknown next-to-leading-order coupling.

## Method
The authors use heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory (HBχPT) with SU(2)L ⊗ SU(2)R symmetry to compute one-loop chiral corrections to the delta axial charge GΔΔ. They evaluate loop diagrams involving pion-nucleon and pion-delta interactions, including wavefunction renormalization, and also perform the partially quenched generalization (PQχPT) to address lattice QCD extrapolations with valence and sea quark masses.

## Key numbers
- The pion decay constant is taken as f = 132 MeV.
- The renormalization scale μ is varied from 800 to 1200 MeV to assess uncertainty from the NLO coupling A.
- The valence pion mass mπ is given by mπ² = 2λm, and the sea pion mass mjj² = 2λmj.
- The delta-nucleon mass splitting in the chiral limit is denoted Δ, and the delta is stable on the lattice when mπ > Δ.

## Context
This work extends earlier lattice QCD studies of delta electromagnetic moments and transitions (Leinweber 1992, Alexandrou 2004-2007) and the first lattice studies of the axial nucleon-to-delta transition (Alexandrou 2006-2007). The computed chiral corrections enable lattice determinations of GΔΔ to be extrapolated to the physical point, where the axial matrix element becomes complex valued, and provide the partially quenched expressions needed to address partial quenching and mixed-action errors in lattice calculations.
