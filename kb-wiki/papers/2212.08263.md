# Speed of sound and liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear matter

> We investigate the speed of sound in nuclear matter at finite temperature and density~(chemical potential) in the nonlinear Walecka model. The numerical results suggest that the behaviors of sound speed are closely related to the the nuclear liquid-gas (LG) phase transition and the associated spinodal structure. The adiabatic sound speed is nonzero at the critical endpoint (CEP) in the mean field approximation. We further derive the boundary of vanishing sound velocity in the temperature-density phase diagram, and point out the region where the sound wave equation is broken. The distinction between the speed of sound in nuclear matter and that in quark matter contains important information about the equation of state of strongly interacting matter at intermediate and high density. We also formulate the relations between differently defined speed of sound using the fundamental thermodynamic relations.

## Key claim

The adiabatic speed of sound (at constant entropy per baryon) is nonzero at the critical endpoint of the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition under the mean-field approximation, contrary to what might be expected for a second-order critical point. The authors derive the boundary of vanishing sound velocity in the temperature-density phase diagram and identify a region where the sound wave equation breaks down because the squared sound speed becomes negative.

## Method

The authors employ the nonlinear Walecka model in the mean-field approximation to describe symmetric nuclear matter, with model parameters fixed to nuclear saturation properties (compression modulus K=240 MeV, effective nucleon mass m*_N=0.75m_N, binding energy B/A=-16.0 MeV at saturation density rho_0=0.16 fm^-3). They compute the speed of sound under five different thermodynamic constraints (constant s/rho_B, constant rho_B, constant s, constant T, and constant mu_B) using derived thermodynamic formulas expressed in terms of temperature and baryon chemical potential or density. The phase structure of the liquid-gas transition, including the first-order transition line, spinodal lines, and the critical endpoint, is mapped alongside the sound speed contours across the full phase diagram.

## Key numbers

- The squared adiabatic sound speed c^2_{s/rho_B} at the critical endpoint of the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition is nonzero but small in the mean-field approximation (specific numerical value not given in text, but stated as "not zero but a small value")
- For mu_B < 1320 MeV, c^2_{s/rho_B} at higher temperature is larger than at lower temperature; for mu_B > 1320 MeV, the opposite behavior occurs
- At very large chemical potential, c^2_{s/rho_B} approaches approximately 0.8 in nuclear matter
- The squared sound speed c^2_{s/rho_B} along the chemical freeze-out line shows a small peak at sqrt(s_NN) approximately 6 GeV
- The squared sound speed c^2_T at constant temperature is zero on the spinodal line boundary and positive in both stable and metastable phases
- The squared sound speed c^2_{mu_B} at constant chemical potential is negative inside the spinodal line and positive in metastable and stable phases

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study of sound speed in QCD matter using the PNJL model (He et al., 2022) to the nuclear matter sector using the nonlinear Walecka model, enabling direct comparison between hadronic and quark matter sound speeds. The study connects to experimental efforts to extract sound speed from heavy-ion collision data (Gardim et al., 2020; Sahu et al., 2021; Biswas et al., 2020) and to neutron star research where the density-dependent sound velocity influences mass-radius relations and tidal deformability (Tews et al., 2018; Greif et al., 2019; Forbes et al., 2019). The results enable future investigation of hadron-quark phase transitions in neutron star cores, where the speed of sound is expected to show a peak at intermediate densities before approaching the conformal value of 1/3.
