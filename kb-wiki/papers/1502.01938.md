# Hawking-Unruh hadronization and strangeness production in high energy collisions

> The interpretation of quark ($q$)- antiquark ($\bar q$) pairs production and the sequential string breaking as tunneling through the event horizon of colour confinement leads to a thermal hadronic spectrum with a universal Unruh temperature, $T \simeq 165$ Mev,related to the quark acceleration, $a$, by $T=a/2π$. The resulting temperature depends on the quark mass and then on the content of the produced hadrons, causing a deviation from full equilibrium and hence a suppression of strange particle production in elementary collisions. In nucleus-nucleus collisions, where the quark density is much bigger, one has to introduce an average temperature (acceleration) which dilutes the quark mass effect and the strangeness suppression almost disappears.

# Hawking-Unruh hadronization and strangeness production in high energy collisions

## Key claim

The thermal hadron spectrum observed in high energy collisions arises from quark-antiquark pair production as tunneling through the event horizon of colour confinement, yielding a universal Unruh temperature of approximately 165 MeV; however, the hadronization temperature depends on the quark mass, so strange hadrons form at lower temperatures than non-strange ones, producing strangeness suppression in elementary collisions that is diluted in nucleus-nucleus collisions where an average temperature washes out the mass effect.

## Method

The authors model string breaking as Hawking-Unruh radiation, where the acceleration of a quark due to the string tension sigma determines the Unruh temperature T = a/(2 pi). For hadrons containing strange quarks, the effective quark mass w_q modifies the acceleration, leading to species-dependent hadronization temperatures (e.g., T(0s) for kaons, T(ss) for phi mesons). In heavy ion collisions, the large number of causally connected quarks forces an average acceleration and temperature, computed self-consistently from the ratio of strange to light quarks, which restores equilibrium and reduces strangeness suppression.

## Key numbers

- Universal hadronization temperature for massless quarks: T(00) = 178 MeV for sigma = 0.2 GeV^2.
- For strange quark mass m_s = 0.100 GeV and sigma = 0.2 GeV^2: T(0s) = 167 MeV (kaons), T(ss) = 157 MeV (phi mesons), T(00s) = 171 MeV (Lambda/Sigma), T(0ss) = 164 MeV (Xi), T(sss) = 157 MeV (Omega).
- Crude estimate of strangeness suppression factor from temperature difference: gamma_s approximately 0.73 for sigma = 0.2 GeV^2, m_s = 0.1 GeV, T_pi = 178 MeV, T_k = 167 MeV.
- Average equilibrium temperature in heavy ion collisions (toy model): T_bar = 174 MeV for sigma = 0.2 GeV^2, m_s = 0.1 GeV.
- Wroblewski factor lambda in elementary collisions: approximately 0.26; in nucleus-nucleus collisions: approximately 0.5 experimentally, with toy model giving 0.33 when using average temperature.
- Average energy per particle at central rapidity for mu_B approximately 0: <E>/<N> = 1.09 +/- 0.08 GeV for sigma = 0.19 +/- 0.03 GeV^2.

## Context

This work extends the Hawking-Unruh hadronization framework proposed by Castorina, Kharzeev, and Satz (2007) to explain the universal hadronization temperature observed in statistical hadronization models (Becattini and collaborators, 1990s-2000s). It provides a parameter-free mechanism for strangeness suppression in elementary collisions (e+e- and pp) that previously required an ad-hoc gamma_s factor (Rafelski, 1980s), and explains the disappearance of this suppression in heavy ion collisions where multiple parton interactions create larger causally connected regions (Hama and collaborators, 2000s). The approach enables a unified description of hadron production across all collision systems without free parameters beyond the string tension and strange quark mass.
