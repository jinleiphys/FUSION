# Mathematical aspects of the nuclear glory phenomenon: from backward focusing to Chebyshev polynomials

> The angular dependence of the cumulative particles production off nuclei near the kinematical boundary for multistep process is defined by characteristic polynomials in angular variables $J_N^2(z_N^θ)$, where $θ$ is the polar angle defining the momentum of the final (cumulative) particle, $z_N^θ= cos (θ/N)$, the integer $N$ being the multiplicity of the process (the number of interactions). Physical argumentation, exploring the small phase space method, leads to the appearance of equations for these polynomials $J_N^2[cos(π/N)]=0$. The recurrent relations between polynomials with different $N$ are obtained, and their connection with known in mathematics Chebyshev polynomials of 2-d kind is established. As a result of this equality, differential cross section of the cumulative particle production has characteristic behaviour $dσ\sim 1/ \sqrt {π- θ}$ at $θ\sim π$ (the backward focusing effect). Such behaviour takes place for any multiplicity of the interaction, beginning with $n=3$, elastic or inelastic (with resonances excitations in intermediate states), and can be called the nuclear glory phenomenon, or 'Buddha's light' of cumulative particles.

# Mathematical aspects of the nuclear glory phenomenon: from backward focusing to Chebyshev polynomials

## Key claim

The differential cross section for cumulative particle production via multistep processes (multiplicity N >= 3) exhibits a characteristic backward focusing effect, scaling as dσ ~ 1/√(π - θ) near θ ~ π, which the author terms the "nuclear glory phenomenon" or "Buddha's light." This behavior follows from the condition J_N²[cos(π/N)] = 0 for characteristic polynomials that are shown to be proportional to Chebyshev polynomials of the second kind, and holds for both elastic and inelastic rescatterings (including resonance excitations in intermediate states).

## Method

The author applies the small phase space method, previously developed in Kopeliovich (2002) and Long (2008), to calculate the probability of multiple interaction processes (MIP) near kinematical boundaries for cumulative particle production. The method expands the angular variables (azimuthal deviations φ_k and polar deviations ϑ_k) around the optimal kinematics where all scattering angles equal θ/N, leading to a quadratic form in these deviations whose determinant defines characteristic polynomials J_N²(z_N^θ) with z_N^θ = cos(θ/N). Recurrent relations for these polynomials are derived from the quadratic form, and their connection to Chebyshev polynomials of the second kind (Chebyshev-Korkin-Zolotarev polynomials) is established via the identity (2z_N^θ)^(N-1) J_N²(z_N^θ) = U_{N-1}(z_N^θ).

## Key numbers

- The differential cross section near θ ~ π scales as dσ ~ 1/√(π - θ) for any multiplicity N ≥ 3.
- For N=3: J_3²(z) = 1 - 1/(4z²); for N=4: J_4²(z) = 1 - 1/(2z²); for N=5: J_5²(z) = 1 - 3/(4z²) + 1/(16z⁴).
- The maximal final momentum for N-fold rescattering of light particles is ω_N^max ≈ N(2m/θ²) + m/(6N), valid for N ≥ 2.
- For nucleon-nucleon rescattering: p_N^max ≈ N(2m/θ²) - m/(3N), valid for N > 3.
- Including Fermi motion (p_F^max/m ≈ 0.27): p_N^max ≈ N(2m/θ²)[1 + (p_F^max/(2m))(θ + 1/θ)] at large θ.
- The relative increase of final momentum due to resonance excitation in intermediate states is Δk_f/k_f ≈ (1/N) Σ_{l=1}^{N-1} ΔM_l²/k_l².
- The number of different resonance-excitation processes grows as (N_r + 1)^(N-1), where N_r is the number of resonances.

## Context

This work extends the small phase space method developed by Kopeliovich (2002) and Long (2008) for calculating multistep process contributions to cumulative particle production, which had been studied experimentally since the 1970s at JINR (Dubna) and ITEP (Moscow) following the early work of Baldin (1971) and Leksin (1972). The paper establishes that the characteristic polynomials J_N² appearing in the angular dependence are identical (up to a factor) to Chebyshev polynomials of the second kind (Chebyshev-Korkin-Zolotarev polynomials from the mid-19th century), providing a mathematical foundation for the backward focusing effect. The author notes that while multistep processes provide a non-negligible contribution to cumulative production cross sections, contributions from interactions with few-nucleon (multiquark) clusters in nuclei cannot be excluded, and detecting the focusing effect for different types of produced particles (baryons and mesons) would serve as a "smoking gun" for the MIP mechanism.
