# The first-order phase transition in the neutron star from the deep neural network

> This study investigates the first-order phase transition within neutron stars, leveraging the deep neural network (DNN) framework alongside contemporary astronomical measurements. The equation of state (EOS) for neutron stars is delineated in a piecewise polytropic form, with the speed of sound ($c_s$) serving as a pivotal determinant. In the phase transition region, $c_s$ is presumed to be zero, while in other intervals, it is optimized utilizing the DNN. Various onset energy densities of phase transition ($\varepsilon_{pt}$), spanning from $2\varepsilon_0$ to $3\varepsilon_0$ (where $\varepsilon_0$ denotes the energy density at nuclear saturation density), as well as phase transition widths ($Δ\varepsilon$) ranging from $0.5\varepsilon_0$ to $\varepsilon_0$, are examined. Our findings underscore that smaller values of $\varepsilon_{pt}$ lead to a more substantial impact of $Δ\varepsilon$ on neutron star properties, encompassing maximum mass, corresponding radius, tidal deformability, phase transition mass, and trace anomaly. Conversely, when $\varepsilon_{pt}$ exceeds $2.5\varepsilon_0$, the influence of $Δ\varepsilon$ diminishes, resulting in a stiffer EOS compared to scenarios lacking a phase transition. Furthermore, the trace anomaly at high density shifts to negative values upon the commencement of the phase transition. It is noteworthy that the correlations between the average speed of sound at different energy density segments demonstrate a notably weak connection. The discernment of whether a phase transition has occurred with the present observables of neutron stars poses a challenging task.

## Key claim

The study finds that the impact of the phase transition width on neutron star properties is substantial only when the onset energy density is low (below 2.5 times nuclear saturation energy density), and that distinguishing whether a first-order phase transition has occurred using current neutron star observables remains challenging.

## Method

The authors used a deep neural network (DNN) framework to generate equations of state for neutron star matter, parameterized in a piecewise polytropic form with the average speed of sound in each density segment as the output. The first-order phase transition was implemented via the Maxwell construction, setting the speed of sound to zero in the phase transition region, with onset energy densities from 2 to 3 times nuclear saturation energy density and phase transition widths of 0.5 or 1.0 times that density. The DNN was trained on 5 million synthetic datasets generated from 17 neutron star observables, and 100 independent DNNs were trained for each phase transition scenario to quantify uncertainties.

## Key numbers

- Onset energy densities of phase transition were varied from 2.0 to 3.0 times nuclear saturation energy density (2ε₀ to 3ε₀)
- Phase transition widths of 0.5ε₀ and 1.0ε₀ were examined
- For Δε = 1.0ε₀ and ε_pt = 2.0ε₀ at 68% confidence: maximum mass = 1.936⁺⁰·⁰⁷¹₋₀.₀₇₈ M⊙, radius at 1.4 M⊙ = 11.59⁺⁰·⁵²₋₀.₄₅ km, tidal deformability Λ₁.₄ = 251.08⁺¹⁰³·⁹⁰₋₆₉.₂₆
- For Δε = 0.5ε₀ and ε_pt = 2.0ε₀ at 68% confidence: maximum mass = 1.802⁺⁰·⁰⁷³₋₀.₀₈₀ M⊙, radius at 1.4 M⊙ = 10.70⁺⁰·³⁰₋₀.₂₈ km, tidal deformability Λ₁.₄ = 151.52⁺³⁰·³⁰₋₂₃.₅₇
- For Δε = 1.0ε₀ and ε_pt = 3.0ε₀ at 68% confidence: maximum mass = 2.032⁺⁰·⁰⁶⁰₋₀.₀₆₄ M⊙, radius at 1.4 M⊙ = 12.37⁺⁰·²⁴₋₀.₂₆ km, tidal deformability Λ₁.₄ = 489.18⁺⁷³·⁵⁹₋₆₄.₉₄
- Density jump η ranged from approximately 0.145 to 0.458 across all scenarios
- Without phase transition at 68% confidence: Λ₁.₄ = 294.37⁺⁷⁷·⁹²₋₆₉.₇₅

## Context

This work extends the DNN-based equation of state generation approach pioneered by Fujimoto et al. (2020, 2021) by explicitly incorporating first-order phase transitions via the Maxwell construction, where the speed of sound is set to zero in the transition region. The study builds on prior investigations of hadron-quark phase transitions in neutron stars, including the work of Ju et al. (2021) which found that quark matter EOS becomes softer in high-density regions after a Maxwell-constructed first-order phase transition. The results enable future work to better constrain phase transition parameters as more neutron star observations become available, particularly for heavy neutron stars.
