# Theoretical tools for neutrino scattering: interplay between lattice QCD, EFTs, nuclear physics, phenomenology, and neutrino event generators

> Maximizing the discovery potential of increasingly precise neutrino experiments will require an improved theoretical understanding of neutrino-nucleus cross sections over a wide range of energies. Low-energy interactions are needed to reconstruct the energies of astrophysical neutrinos from supernovae bursts and search for new physics using increasingly precise measurement of coherent elastic neutrino scattering. Higher-energy interactions involve a variety of reaction mechanisms including quasi-elastic scattering, resonance production, and deep inelastic scattering that must all be included to reliably predict cross sections for energies relevant to DUNE and other accelerator neutrino experiments. This white paper discusses the theoretical status, challenges, required resources, and path forward for achieving precise predictions of neutrino-nucleus scattering and emphasizes the need for a coordinated theoretical effort involved lattice QCD, nuclear effective theories, phenomenological models of the transition region, and event generators.

# Theoretical tools for neutrino scattering: interplay between lattice QCD, EFTs, nuclear physics, phenomenology, and neutrino event generators

## Key claim

Achieving few-percent uncertainties on GeV-scale neutrino-nucleus cross sections for DUNE will require coordinated theoretical work across lattice QCD, nuclear effective theories, phenomenological models, and event generators, but the axial current response of the nucleon at finite $Q^2$ remains largely unknown and experimentally unconstrained, particularly for inelastic processes and resonance transitions.

## Method

The paper is a Snowmass 2021 cross-frontier white paper that surveys the theoretical status of neutrino-nucleus scattering across energy regimes from coherent elastic scattering (CEvNS) through quasi-elastic, resonance, shallow inelastic (SIS), and deep inelastic (DIS) scattering. It identifies specific high-priority theoretical topics requiring study over the next 5-10 years, including lattice QCD calculations of nucleon elastic axial form factors, few-nucleon electroweak matrix elements, and transition form factors; extension of quantum Monte Carlo calculations of inclusive electroweak response functions to $^{16}$O and $^{40}$Ar; incorporation of modern nucleon form factor evaluations into nuclear many-body approaches; and development of consistent models for the SIS-to-DIS transition region in event generators.

## Key numbers

- Around 50% of interactions in the future DUNE experiment will be in the SIS and DIS regions with $W$ above the mass of the $\Delta$ resonance
- The pion production threshold is at $W \approx 1080$ MeV
- The SIS region is defined as $W$ above the $\Delta(1232)$ and at moderate $Q^2 \lesssim 1$ GeV$^2$
- The DCC model predicts cross sections up to $W$ of about 2.2 GeV
- Lattice QCD calculations of nucleon elastic axial form factors with few-percent precision are achievable with present techniques and computing resources
- Nuclear many-body calculations of quasi-elastic scattering could achieve few-percent uncertainties over the next five to ten years
- Resonant and non-resonant pion production processes must be understood at the ten-percent level to achieve few-percent overall cross-section uncertainties for DUNE

## Context

This white paper extends and synthesizes the work of multiple prior approaches including the Rein-Sehgal model (1980) for resonance scattering, the GiBUU model (2011) which relies on the MAID analysis of electron-nucleon pion production, the dynamical coupled-channel (DCC) approach (2013-2016) for resonance and multi-pion final states, and the Bodek-Yang PDF-based approach (1998-2010) for the transition to DIS. It was informed by LOIs received in the first stage of the Snowmass community planning exercise and by dedicated workshops in 2020 and 2021. The paper enables a coordinated path forward for achieving precise theoretical predictions that can be consistently incorporated into neutrino event generators, which is essential for DUNE, Hyper-Kamiokande, and other next-generation oscillation experiments, as well as for CEvNS measurements at COHERENT and astrophysical neutrino observations.
