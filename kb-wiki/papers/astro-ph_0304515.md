# Signatures of QCD Phase Transition in a Newborn Compact Star

> We study the scenario that a new born strange quark star cools to the Quantum ChromoDynamics (QCD) phase transition temperature and converts to a neutron star, and we calculate the evolution of temperature and luminosity of the compact star. We argue that the conversion energy released can be of the order $10^{53}$ erg. We also propose that a second neutrino burst will be emitted at the completion of this phase transition.

# Signatures of QCD Phase Transition in a Newborn Compact Star

## Key claim

The authors propose that a newborn strange quark star, formed immediately after a supernova explosion, cools to the QCD phase transition temperature and converts to a neutron star, releasing up to ~10⁵³ erg of conversion energy and producing a second neutrino burst. This scenario depends on the assumption that strange quark matter is stable only at temperatures above the phase transition temperature T_p, which is taken as a constant 1 or 10 MeV for the calculation, and the authors note that lattice QCD results for T_p at high chemical potential remain highly uncertain.

## Method

The authors model a newborn strange star with initial temperature ~40 MeV, using a perturbative QCD equation of state for cold, dense quark matter up to second order in the strong coupling constant α_s, with a renormalization subtraction point parameter Λ̃ = 2.473. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations for a static, spherically symmetric star, then compute the thermal evolution via a uniform-temperature approximation for the quark phase and a heat conduction equation for the neutron star crust, including heat capacities, neutrino emissivities (quark URCA process, direct URCA processes), photon emission (e⁺e⁻ pair emission, blackbody radiation), and the effect of quark superfluidity with a gap Δ = 100 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature of the newborn strange star: T_i ~ 40 MeV
- Phase transition temperatures considered: T_p = 1 MeV and T_p = 10 MeV
- Maximum gravitational mass for Λ̃ = 2.473: 1.516 M_⊙, with baryonic mass 1.60 M_⊙ and radius 8.54 km
- Conversion energy released: of order 10⁵³ erg
- Total energy radiated by e⁺e⁻ pair emission: 4.24 × 10⁵⁰ erg for T_p = 10 MeV; 1.47 × 10⁵³ erg for T_p = 1 MeV
- Total energy radiated in neutrinos: 3.15 × 10⁵³ erg for T_p = 10 MeV; 1.27 × 10⁵³ erg for T_p = 1 MeV
- For T_p = 10 MeV, the peak neutrino intensity of about 10⁵⁶ erg s⁻¹ lasts for 10⁻³ s, with neutrino diffusion time scale ~1–10 s
- For T_p = 1 MeV, the two neutrino bursts can be separated by as long as 10⁵ s
- Surface temperature reaches 10⁷ K within the first second (T_p = 10 MeV) to first hundred thousand seconds (T_p = 1 MeV)
- Quark matter specific heat: c_q = 2.5 × 10²⁰ ρ̃²/³ T_9 erg cm⁻³ K⁻¹, where ρ̃ is baryon density in units of ρ₀ = 0.17 fm⁻³ and T_9 ≡ T/10⁹ K
- Quark URCA neutrino emissivity: ε_d ≈ 2.2 × 10²⁶ α_s ρ̃ Y_e¹/³ T_9⁶ erg cm⁻³ s⁻¹, with α_s = 0.4 and electron fraction Y_e = 0.001
- Inner crust thermal conductivity: K = 2.8 × 10²¹ / T_9 erg cm⁻¹ s⁻¹ K⁻¹

## Context

This work extends the Bodmer-Witten proposal (Bodmer, 1971; Witten, 1984) that strange quark matter may be the absolute ground state, and the subsequent studies of strange star properties by Alcock et al. (1986), Farhi and Jaffe (1984), and Glendenning (1996). It contrasts with previous proposals by Benvenuto and Horvath (1989) and Aguilera et al. (2002) for a second neutrino burst: those models considered a phase transition from neutron star to quark star, whereas this work considers the reverse transition from quark star to neutron star. The authors note that if multiple neutrino bursts are observed (as may be the case for the Kamiokande data for SN1987A by Hirata et al., 1987), the two scenarios can be distinguished by the cooling rate before versus after the phase transition and by the final compact star size. The work enables future hydrodynamic calculations of the supernova-to-compact-star evolution and more detailed neutrino transport modeling, which the authors identify as necessary next steps.
