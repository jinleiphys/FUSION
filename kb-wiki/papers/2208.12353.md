# Wave functions of $(I,J^P) = (\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{3}{2}^\mp)$ baryons

> Using a Poincaré-covariant quark+diquark Faddeev equation, we provide structural information on the four lightest $(I,J^P)=(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{3}{2}^\mp)$ baryon multiplets. These systems may contain five distinct types of diquarks; but in order to obtain reliable results, it is sufficient to retain only isoscalar-scalar and isovector-axialvector correlations, with the latter being especially important. Viewed with low resolution, the Faddeev equation description of these states bears some resemblance to the associated quark model pictures; namely, they form a set of states related via orbital angular momentum excitation: the negative parity states are primarily $\mathsf P$-wave in character, whereas the positive parity states are $\mathsf D$ wave. However, a closer look reveals far greater structural complexity than is typical of quark model descriptions, with $\mathsf P$, $\mathsf D$, $\mathsf S$, $\mathsf F$ waves and interferences between them all playing a large role in forming observables. Large momentum transfer resonance electroexcitation measurements can be used to test these predictions and may thereby provide insights into the nature of emergent hadron mass.

## Key claim

Using a Poincaré-covariant quark+diquark Faddeev equation, the authors provide structural information on the four lightest $(I,J^P)=(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{3}{2}^\mp)$ baryon multiplets, finding that while the negative parity states are primarily P-wave and positive parity states are D-wave when viewed at low resolution, the actual wave functions contain substantial S, P, D, F waves and interferences between them, far exceeding the complexity of typical quark model descriptions.

## Method

The authors solve a Poincaré-covariant quark+diquark Faddeev equation (Fig. 1) for baryons with total isospin $I=\tfrac{1}{2}$ and spin-parity $J^P=\tfrac{3}{2}^\mp$, retaining isoscalar-scalar $(0,0^+)$ and isovector-axialvector $(1,1^+)$ diquark correlations as the dominant degrees of freedom, while also including isoscalar-pseudoscalar $(0,0^-)$ and isovector-vector $(1,1^-)$ correlations. The Faddeev amplitude is expanded in a basis of Dirac and Lorentz structures (Eqs. 4-5), yielding a $28\times 28$ matrix equation reduced to $20\times 20$ via isospin symmetry, which is solved numerically to obtain masses and wave functions for the ground and first excited states in both parity channels.

## Key numbers

- Calculated dressed-quark core masses (in GeV): $N(1520)\tfrac{3}{2}^-$ at $1.68(8)$, $N(1700)\tfrac{3}{2}^-$ at $1.82(9)$, $N(1720)\tfrac{3}{2}^+$ at $1.78(8)$, $N(1900)\tfrac{3}{2}^+$ at $2.05(7)$, with uncertainties from $\pm5\%$ variation in diquark masses.
- After subtracting a meson-baryon final-state interaction correction $\delta^{N_{3/2}}_{\rm MB}=0.13$ GeV, the calculated masses agree with empirical pole positions from the Particle Data Group (Workman 2022).
- Effective diquark masses used (in GeV): $m_{[ud]_{0^+}}=0.8$, $m_{\{uu\}_{1^+}}=0.9$, $m_{[ud]_{0^-}}=1.3$, $m_{[ud]_{1^-}}=1.4$.
- Canonical normalisation constants for diquark amplitudes: $g_{0^+}=14.8$, $g_{1^+}=12.7$, $g_{0^-}=6.59$, $g_{1^-}^{I=0}=3.27$, $g_{1^-}^{I=1}=1.59$.
- In the Faddeev amplitude, axialvector diquarks contribute 70.1% to $N(1520)\tfrac{3}{2}^-$, 58.7% to $N(1700)\tfrac{3}{2}^-$, 50.0% to $N(1720)\tfrac{3}{2}^+$, and 71.7% to $N(1900)\tfrac{3}{2}^+$; scalar diquarks contribute 20.3%, 7.4%, 44.4%, and 0.0% respectively.

## Context

This work extends the Poincaré-covariant Faddeev equation approach previously applied to $(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2}^\pm)$ baryons by Chen (2017) and to $(\tfrac{3}{2},\tfrac{3}{2}^\mp)$ baryons by Liu (2022), now providing the first predictions for the structure of low-lying $(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{3}{2}^\mp)$ states. The results contradict simple quark model expectations (Crede 2013) that these states form clean parity-partner pairs related by orbital angular momentum excitation; instead, the wave functions show complex mixing of multiple partial waves. The predictions can be tested by large momentum transfer resonance electroexcitation measurements, which may provide insights into emergent hadron mass (EHM) and its connection to dynamical chiral symmetry breaking.
