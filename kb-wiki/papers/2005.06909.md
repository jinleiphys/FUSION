# Two-pole structures in QCD: Facts, not fantasy!

> The two-pole structure refers to the fact that particular single states in the spectrum as listed in the PDG tables are often two states. The story began with the $Λ(1405)$, when in 2001, using unitarized chiral perturbation theory, it was observed that there are two poles in the complex plane, one close to the $\bar{K}p$ and the other close to the $πΣ$ threshold. This was later understood combining the SU(3) limit and group-theoretical arguments. Different unitarization approaches that all lead to the two-pole structure have been considered in the mean time, showing some spread in the pole positions. This fact is now part of the PDG book, though it is not yet listed in the summary tables. Here, I will discuss the open ends and critically review approaches that can not deal with this issue. In the meson sector some excited charm mesons are good candidates for such a two-pole structure. Next, I consider in detail the $D_0^*(2300)$, that is another candidate for this scenario. Combining lattice QCD with chiral unitary approaches in the finite volume, the precise data of the Hadron Spectrum Collaboration for coupled-channel $Dπ$, $Dη$, $D_s\bar{K}$ scattering in the isospin $I=1/2$ channel indeed reveal its two-pole structure. Further states in the heavy meson sector with $I=1/2$ exhibiting this phenomenon are predicted, especially in the beauty meson sector. I also discuss the relation of these two-pole structures and the possible molecular nature of the states under consideration.

# Two-pole structures in QCD: Facts, not fantasy!

## Key claim
The two-pole structure, where single states listed in the PDG tables are actually two distinct states, is an established fact for the Λ(1405) and the D₀*(2300), though the PDG summary tables still list each as one resonance only. The claim is conditional on using unitarized chiral perturbation theory approaches that respect chiral symmetry constraints; approaches that violate these constraints (e.g., by mishandling off-shell effects) may find only one pole.

## Method
The author reviews unitarized chiral perturbation theory (CHPT) applied to coupled-channel meson-baryon and meson-meson scattering, where the Bethe-Salpeter equation is solved in the on-shell approximation to resum the fundamental bubble diagram. For the Λ(1405), next-to-leading order (NLO) chiral SU(3) Lagrangians are used with dimensional regularization of the scalar loop function, and the resulting T-matrix poles are located on the second Riemann sheet. For the D₀*(2300), lattice QCD data from the Hadron Spectrum Collaboration for coupled-channel Dπ, Dη, DₛK̅ scattering in the I=1/2 channel are combined with chiral unitary approaches formulated in a finite volume.

## Key numbers
- Λ(1405) pole 1 (lower): (1379.2 − i 27.6) MeV, close to the πΣ threshold
- Λ(1405) pole 2 (higher): (1433.7 − i 11.0) MeV, close to the K⁻p threshold
- PDG-listed Λ(1405) mass: 1405.1⁺¹·³₋₁·₀ MeV, width: 50.5 ± 2.0 MeV
- Higher pole real part across approaches: ≈ 1420–1430 MeV; imaginary part: −10 to −40 MeV
- SU(3) limit parameters: m₀ = 1151 MeV, M₀ = 368 MeV, a₀ = −2.148

## Context
This work extends the two-pole discovery for the Λ(1405) first made in Oller and Meißner (2001) using unitarized CHPT, later understood via SU(3) group theory by Jido et al. (2003). It reviews the NLO confirmation by three independent groups, Ikeda et al. (2012), Guo et al. (2012), and Mai et al. (2012), and the comparative study by Cieplý et al. (2016). The paper enables future searches for two-pole structures in the heavy meson sector, particularly in beauty mesons with I=1/2, and connects these structures to the molecular nature of the states. It critically refutes claims by Revai (2017) and Anisovich et al. (2019) that challenge the two-pole scenario, showing those approaches violate chiral symmetry constraints.
