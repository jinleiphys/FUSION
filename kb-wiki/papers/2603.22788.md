# Production probability of super-heavy nuclei in fusion

> The synthesis of super-heavy nuclei (SHN) through fusion reactions is a critical area of nuclear physics, offering insights into nuclear stability and the limits of the periodic table. However, theoretical predictions of evaporation residue cross sections $ σ_{\rm {ER} }$ remain challenging due to large uncertainties arising from complex reaction mechanisms and sensitive model parameters. In this work, a new and analytical formula is proposed for systematically describing the production probabilities of SHN with atomic number $Z\ge 110$, based on barrier tunneling concept. Together with the empirical barrier distribution method for describing capture, an improved model, EBD3, reproduces 64 measured $ σ_{\rm {ER} }$ within one order of magnitude, with a root-mean-square deviation of 0.351. The model successfully captures key quantities in fission-like process, including fission barrier height, mass asymmetry, depth of capture pocket and the effective fusion barrier height. Predictions for the synthesis of element 119 are presented, identifying promising projectile-target combinations such as $^{45}$Sc + $^{249}$Cf with a maximum cross section of $107.5^{+120}_{-56.7}$ fb. The maximum cross section falls to $3.2^{+3.6}_{-1.7}$ fb for $^{54}$Cr + $^{243}$Am at the optimal incident energy of 244 MeV.

# Production probability of super-heavy nuclei in fusion

## Key claim

The paper proposes a new analytical formula (EBD3) for systematically describing production probabilities of super-heavy nuclei (SHN) with atomic number Z ≥ 110, which reproduces 64 measured evaporation residue cross sections within one order of magnitude. The model's accuracy depends on the assumption that the macroscopic part of the survival probability follows a Fermi function of charge number and mass asymmetry, and that the fission barrier height uncertainty of approximately 0.3 MeV from the WS4 mass model is sufficient to estimate cross section uncertainties.

## Method

The author develops the EBD3 model by factorizing the evaporation residue cross section into a capture cross section (calculated with the existing EBD2.2 empirical barrier distribution method) and an average production probability P = P_CN × W_sur. The production probability is expressed as a product of three terms: a macroscopic part P_mac describing the survival probability of the di-nuclear system against fission-like processes (parameterized as a Fermi function of charge number Z and mass asymmetry η), and two energy-dependent terms P_1 and P_2 for the survival of the di-nuclear system and the compound nucleus respectively, with separate parameter sets for hot fusion (actinide targets) and cold fusion (lead/bismuth targets). The model incorporates fission barrier heights from the WS4 macroscopic-microscopic model, capture pocket depths from Skyrme energy density functional calculations, and shell gaps from WS4.

## Context

This work extends the empirical barrier distribution (EBD) approach previously developed by Wang (2026) for capture cross sections (EBD2.2) to include evaporation residue cross sections for SHN with Z ≥ 110. The model provides an alternative to more complex approaches such as the di-nuclear system (DNS) model used by Adamian (2020) and Nasirov (2024), transport models by Guo (2023) and Li (2025), and the HIVAP statistical model by Reisdorf (1985). The predictions for element 119 synthesis are compared with recent DNS model calculations by Zhang (2026) and Wang (2025), as well as earlier HIVAP predictions by Wang (2011). The model enables systematic guidance for future experiments aiming to synthesize elements 119 and 120, and provides a framework suitable for further machine-learning analysis of super-heavy fusion reactions.
