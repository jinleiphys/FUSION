# Effect of quark-jet energy loss on direct photons in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We discuss the transverse momentum distribution of thermal and prompt photons in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions. The prompt photons are mostly produced by fragmentation of quark jets (bremsstrahlung). If these quark jets suffer a large energy loss in heavy-ion reactions, prompt photons are significantly suppressed. Thermal electromagnetic radiation from the quark-gluon plasma and the hadron gas might then dominate the intermediate k_T-range. In central Au+Au collisions at \sqrt{s}=200A GeV, and in the range 2 GeV < k_T < 4 GeV, the inverse slope of the thermal radiation is 360-460 MeV, while that of the QCD photons is 500-1000 MeV. This large difference might allow to disentangle the various sources of direct photons experimentally. At \sqrt{s}=5.5A TeV, nuclear shadowing and quark-jet quenching suppresses the QCD-photons strongly for k_T<5 GeV. Thermal radiation dominates in this k_T-region.

# Effect of quark-jet energy loss on direct photons in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

If quark jets suffer a large energy loss in heavy-ion reactions (due to induced gluon radiation while propagating through the hot and dense central region), prompt photons from jet fragmentation are significantly suppressed, potentially allowing thermal radiation from the quark-gluon plasma and hadron gas to dominate the intermediate transverse momentum range of 2–4 GeV. The authors present this as a first estimate of the maximal effect, comparing the prompt photon spectrum with and without the contribution from quark-jet Bremsstrahlung, where the latter corresponds to the case of fully quenched quark jets.

## Method

The authors calculate prompt photon production in p+p reactions at leading order (with K-factors of 2 at RHIC energy and 1.5 at LHC energy) using GRV-95 parton distribution functions, then scale to heavy-ion collisions using nuclear overlap functions and shadowing parametrizations from Eskola (1993). Thermal photon spectra are computed assuming longitudinally boost-invariant and cylindrically symmetric transverse hydrodynamical expansion of the quark-gluon plasma and hadron gas, with the MIT bag-model equation of state for two massless quark flavours and a bag constant of 380 MeV/fm³, matched via Gibbs conditions to an ideal gas of massive π, η, ρ, and ω mesons at a critical temperature of 160 MeV.

## Key numbers

- For central Au+Au collisions at √s = 200A GeV, in the range 2 GeV < k_T < 4 GeV: inverse slope of thermal radiation is 360–460 MeV; inverse slope of QCD photons is 500–1000 MeV
- At RHIC energy: thermal inverse slope T*_th = 360 MeV at k_T = 2.2 GeV and T*_th = 460 MeV at k_T = 3.8 GeV; prompt inverse slope T*_prompt = 500 MeV at k_T = 2.2 GeV and T*_prompt = 750 MeV at k_T = 3.8 GeV
- Initial conditions for thermal calculation at RHIC: τ_i = 0.124 fm/c, T_i = 530 MeV, total entropy dS/dη = 5000 for Au+Au
- Initial conditions for thermal calculation at LHC: τ_i = 0.1 fm/c, T_i = 880 MeV, total entropy dS/dη = 18700 for Pb+Pb
- Nuclear overlap functions: T_AuAu(b=0) = 29 mb⁻¹, T_PbPb(b=0) = 32 mb⁻¹
- At LHC energy (√s = 5.5A TeV) and k_T = 2 GeV: prompt radiation is two orders of magnitude below the thermal contribution

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of thermal photon observability by Shuryak (1978), Ruuskanen (1992), and Srivastava (1992), and prompt photon calculations by Hammon (1998) and Hwa (1985). It proposes prompt photons as an alternative observable for quark-jet quenching, complementing the study of high-p_T hadron spectra proposed by Wang and Gyulassy (1992). The results suggest that at RHIC, strong jet quenching might suppress prompt photons below thermal radiation for k_T ≤ 3 GeV, while at LHC the combination of higher initial temperatures, nuclear shadowing, and quark-jet quenching makes conditions even more favorable for detecting thermal electromagnetic radiation from the quark-gluon plasma. The authors note that a pre-equilibrium stage would also emit photons, as studied by Kämpfer and Pavlenko (1992) and within the parton cascade approach by Srivastava and Geiger (1998).
