# Impact of the nuclear equation of state on the formation of twin stars

> Twin stars-two stable neutron stars (NSs) with the same mass but different radii have long been proposed to appear as a consequence of a possible first-order phase transition in NS matter. Within a meta-model for the EOS of hybrid stars, we revisit the viability of twin stars and its dependence on numerous parameters characterizing the EOS of nuclear matter, quark matter, and the phase transition between them. While essentially no experimental constraint exists for the last two, parameters characterizing the EOS of neutron-rich nucleonic matter have been constrained within various ranges by terrestrial experiments and astrophysical observations. Within these ranges, the impact of nuclear EOS and crust-core transition density on the formation of twin stars is studied. It is found that the symmetry energy of neutron-rich nucleonic matter notably influences the formation of twin stars, particularly through its slope $L$ and curvature $K_{\rm sym}$. Conversely, varying the EOS of symmetric nuclear matter within their currently known uncertainty ranges shows minimal influence on the formation of twin stars.

## Key claim

The paper finds that twin stars can exist within the currently allowed whole space of nuclear equation of state parameters, contrary to the authors' previous work where no evidence for twin stars was found when nuclear matter parameters were fixed at their most probable values; however, the formation of twin stars depends notably on the symmetry energy of neutron-rich nucleonic matter, particularly its slope \(L\) and curvature \(K_{\rm sym}\), while the equation of state of symmetric nuclear matter shows minimal influence.

## Method

The authors used a meta-model for the equation of state of hybrid stars, parameterizing the nuclear matter EOS via Taylor-like expansions for symmetric nuclear matter energy \(E_0(\rho)\) and symmetry energy \(E_{\rm sym}(\rho)\) with coefficients treated as free parameters, and describing quark matter and the hadron-quark phase transition using the constant speed of sound (CSS) model with three parameters: transition density \(\rho_t\), energy density discontinuity \(\Delta\varepsilon\), and squared speed of sound \(c_s^2\). They varied each nuclear EOS parameter individually within its currently known uncertainty ranges while keeping all other parameters at their most probable values, then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain mass-radius relations and extracted the twin star mass range \(\Delta M\) and maximum radius separation \(\Delta R\).

## Key numbers

- The slope of symmetry energy is constrained to \(L = 58.7 \pm 28.1\) MeV, and its curvature to \(K_{\rm sym} = -100 \pm 100\) MeV.
- The skewness of symmetric nuclear matter is constrained to \(J_0 = -190 \pm 100\) MeV; varying \(J_0\) within this range introduces a maximum uncertainty of approximately 9% to the mass at a fixed radius for \(\rho_t = 2\rho_0\) and up to about 15% for \(\rho_t = 3\rho_0\).
- The twin star mass range \(\Delta M\) is at most about 0.05 M\(_\odot\) across all mass-radius curves shown; increasing \(c_s^2\) from 0.6 to 1 decreases \(\Delta M\) by less than 0.005 M\(_\odot\).
- The maximum radius separation \(\Delta R\) observed is approximately 1.95 km, occurring for \(\rho_t = 3\rho_0\) and \(c_s^2 = 0.6\); for most parameter choices \(\Delta R\) remains below about 1.2 km.
- The energy density discontinuity \(\Delta\varepsilon\) was varied from 150 to 350 MeV\(\cdot\)fm\(^{-3}\); twin stars appear only when \(\Delta\varepsilon \geq 250\) MeV\(\cdot\)fm\(^{-3}\).
- The transition density \(\rho_t\) was varied from \(1.5\rho_0\) to \(3.5\rho_0\); the maximum mass \(M_{\rm TOV}\) decreases from 2.2 M\(_\odot\) to 2.0 M\(_\odot\) as \(\rho_t\) increases.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies using Bayesian inference (Xie 2021) and forward-modeling (Zhang 2023) where twin stars were disfavored when nuclear parameters were fixed at their most probable values, and contrasts with a study by Li et al. (2024) that concluded low-mass twin stars could satisfy current observations by varying \(L\) and \(J_0\) in much wider ranges than those constrained by terrestrial experiments and astrophysical observations. The results enable future searches for twin stars using next-generation X-ray observatories and gravitational wave detectors expected to measure neutron star radii with precision below 0.1 km.
