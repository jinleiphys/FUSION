# Cold nuclear matter

> The behavior of nuclear matter is studied at low densities and temperatures using classical molecular dynamics with three different sets of potentials with different compressibility. Nuclear matter is found to arrange in crystalline structures around the saturation density and in non-homogeneous (i.e. pasta-like) structures at lower densities. Similar results were obtained with a simple Lennard-Jones potential. Finite size effects are analysed and the existence of the non-homogeneous structures is shown to be inherent to the use of periodic boundary conditions and the finitude of the system. For large enough systems the non-homogeneous structures are limited to one sphere, one rod or one slab per simulation cell, which are shown to be minimal surface structures under cubic periodic boundary conditions at the corresponding volume fraction. The relevance of these findings to the simulations of neutron star and supernovae matter is discussed.

# Cold nuclear matter

## Key claim

Nuclear matter at subsaturation densities and low temperatures self-assembles into pasta-like structures even in the complete absence of Coulomb interaction, contradicting the long-held assumption that Coulomb repulsion is essential for pasta formation. The non-homogeneous structures arise primarily from geometric constraints imposed by periodic boundary conditions and finite system size, not from frustration between short-range attraction and long-range repulsion.

## Method

Classical molecular dynamics simulations were performed for symmetric nuclear matter (proton fraction x=0.5) using three different nuclear potentials: the Pandharipande medium (compressibility ~250 MeV) and stiff (compressibility ~535 MeV) potentials, and a simple semiclassical potential (SSP). Systems of A=1728 to A=13824 nucleons were placed in cubic cells under periodic boundary conditions at densities 0.01 fm^{-3} ≤ ρ ≤ 1.25ρ₀ and temperatures 0.001 MeV ≤ T ≤ 1.0 MeV, with temperature controlled via the Andersen thermostat. The resulting structures were characterized using mean curvature and Euler number topology analysis, and compared to simple geometric models of minimal surfaces.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density for the Pandharipande medium potential: ρ₀ = 0.16 fm^{-3} with binding energy E(ρ₀) = 16 MeV/nucleon
- Bulk modulus extracted from the Pandharipande medium potential B1 lattice: 283 MeV (compared to the quoted 250 MeV)
- Bulk modulus extracted from the SSP B2 lattice: 418 MeV
- Bulk modulus extracted from the Pandharipande stiff potential B3 lattice: 494 MeV (compared to the quoted 535 MeV using B1)
- Saturation density for the stiff Pandharipande potential: ρ = 0.15 fm^{-3} with binding energy 16.5 MeV/nucleon
- Saturation binding energy for the SSP: approximately -20.3 MeV/nucleon at ρ = 0.16 fm^{-3}
- Cutoff radius for Pandharipande potentials: r_c = 5.4 fm
- Transition from homogeneous to pasta structures occurs at ρ ≈ 0.13 fm^{-3} for the Pandharipande medium potential
- Solid-solid phase transition between B1 and B3 structures occurs at ρ ≈ 0.17 fm^{-3} for the stiff Pandharipande potential
- Total volume occupied by nucleons in non-homogeneous configurations varies by less than 10% while cell volume increases almost tenfold

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Dorso et al., 2012) which first observed that pasta structures persist without Coulomb interaction, and contradicts the long-standing picture from Koonin (1985) and Ravenhall (1983) that attributed pasta formation to competition between nuclear surface energy and Coulomb repulsion. The finding that Lennard-Jones systems produce identical pasta structures under periodic boundary conditions, consistent with observations by Binder (2012) in grand canonical Monte Carlo simulations, demonstrates the geometric origin of these structures. The results imply that simulations of neutron star crust matter must carefully account for finite-size effects and periodic boundary conditions, and that the role of Coulomb interactions in determining the actual pasta phases in astrophysical environments requires re-examination.
