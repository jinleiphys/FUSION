# Impact of the nuclear equation of state on the explodability of massive stars

> In recent years, astrophysical observations have placed tight constraints on key properties of the nuclear equation of state (EoS). Using 45 two-dimensional simulations for three different EoS compatible with the current tight constraints, we show that the EoS remains a major uncertainty for the outcome of core-collapse supernovae. Whereas explosions are obtained in most cases for the SFHo and SFHx EoS, for the CMF EoS, which includes a crossover from nucleonic matter to a quark phase, explosions occur only for 2 out of 15 progenitors. Less favourable conditions for neutrino-driven explosions arise for the CMF EoS due to lower neutrino luminosities and mean energies and slightly weaker contraction of the warm proto-neutron star. Our results suggest that the explodability of massive stars cannot yet be predicted based on first principles without better knowledge of the nuclear EoS. Conversely, observational constraints on stellar explodability may help further constrain the EoS.

## Key claim

Even with modern nuclear equations of state (EoS) that satisfy tight observational constraints on neutron star properties, the choice of EoS remains a major uncertainty for predicting which massive stars explode as core-collapse supernovae; the CMF EoS (which includes a crossover to quark matter) produces explosions in only 2 out of 15 progenitors, whereas the SFHo and SFHx EoS produce explosions in most cases.

## Method

The authors performed 45 two-dimensional axisymmetric simulations using the neutrino hydrodynamics code CoCoNuT-FMT, with three different nuclear EoS (SFHo, SFHx, and CMF) that all fall within current experimental and observational constraints. They simulated 15 solar-metallicity progenitor models with zero-age main sequence masses from 9.71 to 36.61 solar masses, running each simulation for several seconds after bounce to determine whether shock revival (explosion) occurs.

## Key numbers

- For the CMF EoS, only 2 out of 15 progenitor models underwent shock revival (s9.71 and s19.5); the remaining 13 models did not explode
- For the SFHo EoS, explosions occurred in 13 out of 15 models, with only s15 and s18 failing to explode
- For the SFHx EoS, explosions occurred in 12 out of 15 models, with s14 and s15 failing to explode (but s18 exploded)
- The maximum neutron star masses for the three EoS are: 2.10 solar masses (CMF), 2.059 solar masses (SFHo), and 2.13 solar masses (SFHx)
- Model s36.61 with the SFHo EoS reached an explosion energy of 3.37 x 10^51 erg
- The delay in shock revival for model s9.71 with the CMF EoS was 80-90 ms compared to the SFHo and SFHx models

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Janka (2012), Suwa (2013), and Yasin (2020) that demonstrated EoS effects on explosion conditions, but those used older EoS now ruled out by constraints from nuclear experiments and neutron star observations. The authors show that even with the tighter constraints from Lattimer (2013), Drischler (2017), and Raithel (2019), the EoS remains a major uncertainty. The results imply that quantitative predictions of neutron star versus black hole formation from first-principle simulations are not yet possible, and that multi-messenger observations of a Galactic supernova could help further constrain the nuclear EoS.
