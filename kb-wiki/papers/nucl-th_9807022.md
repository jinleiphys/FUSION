# Comparison of Regularization Methods for Nucleon-Nucleon Effective Field Theory

> The characteristics of a meaningful effective field theory (EFT) analysis are discussed and compared with traditional approaches to NN scattering. A key feature of an EFT treatment is a systematic expansion in powers of momentum, which is demonstrated using an error analysis introduced by Lepage. A clear graphical determination of the radius of convergence for the momentum expansion is also obtained. I use these techniques to compare cutoff regularization, two forms of dimensional regularization, and the dibaryon approach, using a simple model for illustration. The naturalness of the parameters and predictions for other observables are also shown.

# Comparison of Regularization Methods for Nucleon-Nucleon Effective Field Theory

## Key claim
All regularization methods except dimensional regularization with modified minimal subtraction (DR[MS]) are consistent with basic EFT features, systematic improvement order-by-order, radius of convergence set by the scale of new physics, and accurate predictions for other observables, but only when the scattering length is small; for large scattering lengths, DR[MS] fails because it produces unnatural constants and a small radius of convergence, while cutoff regularization (CR[G]) and dimensional regularization with power divergence subtraction (DR[PDS]) succeed.

## Method
The author uses a delta-shell potential as an exactly solvable model for the underlying short-range physics, with parameters chosen to mimic the large scattering length of nucleon-nucleon scattering in the 1S0 channel. The effective potential is expanded in powers of momentum with dimensionless coefficients, and the constants are determined order-by-order by fitting the error in p cot delta(p) to a polynomial using a weighted minimization procedure, following the Lepage error analysis. The Lippmann-Schwinger equation is solved for each regularization scheme (CR[G], DR[MS], DR[PDS], and the dibaryon approach), and the resulting phase shifts and binding energies are compared to the exact results.

## Key numbers
- For the delta-shell potential with g=0.99, the scattering length is a_s = 99/Lambda (one hundred times larger than for g=-10, where a_s = -0.909/Lambda), while the effective range is r_e = 2(g+1)/(3g) * 1/Lambda, which is of natural size for all g.
- For DR[MS] with g=1.01 (large scattering length), the radius of convergence is p/Lambda ~ 0.1, compared to p/Lambda ~ 1 for g=-10 (small scattering length).
- For DR[MS] with g=1.01, the fitted constants are c=84.2, d=-5640, e=378000 (unnatural), while for CR[G] with the same g, the constants are c=-1.42, d=0.842, e=-0.181 (natural).
- The exact binding energy for the delta-shell potential ranges from 6.48e-2 MeV (g=1.01) to 812 MeV (g=2.5).
- For CR[G] fit to Nijmegen 1S0 NN scattering data with cutoff Lambda=250 MeV, one- and two-constant results show systematic improvement and a clear radius of convergence.

## Context
This work extends the error analysis introduced by Lepage (Lepage, 1997) to compare regularization schemes for nonperturbative nucleon-nucleon effective field theory, following earlier debates about the nature and limitations of EFT expansions in this context (Maryland group, Lepage, Kaplan, Savage, and Wise). The delta-shell potential was previously used by Kaplan (Kaplan, 1997) to illustrate the dibaryon approach, and the author confirms that the inclusion of long-distance pion physics does not change the conclusions about short-range expansion properties. The results enable systematic EFT treatments of many-nucleon systems with controlled error estimates, in contrast to traditional phenomenological potentials like the Reid potential (Reid, 1968), which are well-suited for global fits but lack a clear separation scale for systematic predictions.
