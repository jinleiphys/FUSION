# Relativistic many body theory of the electric dipole moment of $^{129}$Xe and its implications for probing new physics beyond the Standard Model

> We report the results of our theoretical studies of the time-reversal and parity violating electric dipole moment (EDM) of $^{129}$Xe arising from the nuclear Schiff moment (NSM) and the electron-nucleus tensor-pseudotensor (T-PT) interaction based on the self-consistent and the normal relativistic coupled-cluster methods. The important many-body effects are highlighted and their contributions are explicitly presented. The uncertainties in the calculations of the correlation and relativistic effects are determined by estimating the contributions of the triples excitations, and the Breit interaction respectively, which together amount to about 0.7% for the NSM and 0.2% for the T-PT interactions. The results of our present work in combination with improved experimental limits for $^{129}$Xe EDM in the future would tighten the constraints on the hadronic CP violating quantities, and this could provide important insights into new physics beyond the Standard Model of elementary particles.

## Key claim

The paper presents relativistic many-body calculations of the electric dipole moment (EDM) of $^{129}$Xe arising from the nuclear Schiff moment (NSM) and the tensor-pseudotensor (T-PT) electron-nucleus interaction, claiming that the results are more accurate and reliable than those for $^{199}$Hg EDM, and that $^{129}$Xe EDM may be a more promising probe of new physics beyond the Standard Model, contingent on future experimental improvements.

## Method

The authors employed two variants of the relativistic coupled-cluster (RCC) theory: the self-consistent RCC singles and doubles method (RCCSD(SC)) and the relativistic normal coupled-cluster singles and doubles method (RNCCSD). These methods were applied to compute the atomic EDM of $^{129}$Xe arising from the NSM and T-PT interactions, using Gaussian-type orbitals to obtain the Dirac-Fock wave function. The contributions from higher-order many-body effects were evaluated by comparing results at different levels of approximation, including coupled-perturbed Dirac-Fock (CPDF) and RCCSD methods, and uncertainties were estimated by computing contributions from triples excitations and the Breit interaction.

## Key numbers

* The estimated uncertainties from triples excitations and Breit interaction together amount to about 0.7% for the NSM and 0.2% for the T-PT interactions.
* The RNCCSD results give $\eta = 0.49$ (where $\eta = d_a / (\langle \sigma_N \rangle C_T \times 10^{20} |e| \text{cm})$) and $\zeta = 0.32$ (where $\zeta = d_a / (S \times 10^{17} |e| \text{cm}/(|e|\text{fm}^3))$).
* The RCCSD(SC) results give $\eta = 0.48$ and $\zeta = 0.32$, differing from RNCCSD by 2.0% for T-PT and in complete agreement for NSM.
* Combining the latest experimental limit $|d_a| < 1.5 \times 10^{-27} |e|\text{cm}$ (95% C.L.) with RNCCSD calculations yields upper limits $|S| < 4.7 \times 10^{-10} |e|\text{fm}^3$ and $|C_T| < 6.1 \times 10^{-7}$ (for $\langle \sigma_N \rangle = 1/2$).
* The Breit interaction contributions were found to be 0.6% and 0.9% of the total Dirac-Coulomb contributions in the CPDF and RCCSD approximations respectively.
* The net estimated errors from omitted effects are $1.1 \times 10^{-3}$ for the T-PT interaction and $2.1 \times 10^{-3}$ for the NSM.

## Context

This work extends previous relativistic many-body calculations of $^{129}$Xe EDM by Singh et al. (2015) and others, overcoming limitations by using self-consistent and normal variants of RCC theory that include higher-order excitations systematically. The results are compared with earlier CPDF calculations by Dzuba and Flambaum (2012) and Latha et al. (2009). The improved atomic calculations, combined with nuclear structure calculations for $^{129}$Xe by Dmitriev (2005) and Yoshinaga (2015) that are more reliable than those for $^{199}$Hg, enable tighter constraints on hadronic CP-violating quantities such as quark chromo-EDMs and the QCD theta parameter, and could help distinguish between different models of new physics beyond the Standard Model.
