# The Yet-Unobserved Multi-Messenger Gravitational-Wave Universe

> Observations with next-generation ground-based detectors further enhanced with multi-messenger (electromagnetic and neutrino) detections will allow us to probe new extreme astrophysics. Target sources included: core-collapse supernovae, continuous emission from isolated or accreting neutron stars, and bursts from magnetars and other pulsars.

## Key claim

Observations with next-generation ground-based gravitational-wave detectors, enhanced by multi-messenger electromagnetic and neutrino detections, will be necessary to probe new extreme astrophysics from core-collapse supernovae, continuous emission from neutron stars, and bursts from magnetars and other pulsars, as signal strengths are generally too low for reliable detection with second-generation detectors even at design sensitivity.

## Method

The paper surveys predicted gravitational-wave signals from three source classes: core-collapse supernovae (using 3D simulations to characterize emission from convection, the standing accretion shock instability, and proto-neutron-star oscillations), continuous waves from isolated and accreting neutron stars (considering crustal deformations, magnetic field effects, and r-mode instabilities), and bursts from magnetars and pulsar glitches. For each class, the authors compare predicted characteristic strain amplitudes against sensitivity curves of existing and planned detectors including Advanced LIGO, the Einstein Telescope, and Cosmic Explorer.

## Key numbers

- Predicted signal-to-noise ratios for 3D core-collapse supernova simulations at 100 kpc are in the range 0.5-6 for Advanced LIGO at design sensitivity, versus 12-130 for third-generation detectors (Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer)
- Continuous wave upper limits for a canonical neutron star at 10 kpc correspond to ellipticity smaller than 10^-5 above 500 Hz and smaller than 10^-7 above 150 Hz
- The most stringent continuous wave upper limit for a known pulsar (J0711-6830) is 1.2 x 10^-8
- Maximum fiducial ellipticities for isolated neutron stars are approximately 2 x 10^-6 for the SLy equation of state
- Magnetar giant flare strain at 10 kpc with polar magnetic field 10^15 G is approximately h ~ 10^-27
- Core-collapse supernova f-mode frequencies range from approximately 200-500 Hz in the early stage (first few hundred ms after bounce) to 500-2000 Hz in the later stage (hundreds of ms to seconds after bounce)
- Standing accretion shock instability frequencies are approximately 100-250 Hz in both 2D and 3D simulations

## Context

This white paper extends the case for third-generation gravitational-wave detectors beyond the already-detected binary merger sources (Abbott 2016, LIGO Scientific 2018) to yet-unobserved source classes. It builds on prior simulation work by Murphy (2009), Yakunin (2015), Morozova, Andresen (2017), and Mueller (2012, 2013, 2017) on core-collapse supernova gravitational-wave signatures, and on continuous wave calculations by Johnson-McDaniel (2012) and Ushomirsky (2000). The paper enables future observational strategies for multi-messenger campaigns combining gravitational-wave detectors with neutrino detectors such as Super-Kamiokande/Hyper-Kamiokande, DUNE, JUNO, and IceCube, and electromagnetic facilities for source localization and host galaxy identification.
