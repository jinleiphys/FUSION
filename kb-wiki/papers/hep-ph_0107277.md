# Reactor-based Neutrino Oscillation Experiments

> The status of neutrino oscillation searches employing nuclear reactors as sources is reviewed. This technique, a direct continuation of the experiments that proved the existence of neutrinos, is today an essential tool in investigating the indications of oscillations found in studying neutrinos produced in the sun and in the earth's atmosphere. The low-energy of the reactor \nuebar makes them an ideal tool to explore oscillations with small mass differences and relatively large mixing angles. In the last several years the determination of the reactor anti-neutrino flux and spectrum has reached a high degree of accuracy. Hence measurements of these quantities at a given distance L can be readily compared with the expectation at L = 0, thus testing \nuebar disappearance. While two experiments, Chooz and Palo Verde, with baselines of about 1 km and thus sensitive to the neutrino mass differences associated with the atmospheric neutrino anomaly, have collected data and published results recently, an ambitious project with a baseline of more than 100 km, Kamland, is preparing to take data. This ultimate reactor experiment will have a sensitivity sufficient to explore part of the oscillation phase space relevant to solar neutrino scenarios. It is the only envisioned experiment with a terrestrial source of neutrinos capable of addressing the solar neutrino puzzle.

## Key claim

Reactor-based neutrino oscillation experiments are disappearance-only searches for \(\bar{\nu}_e\) that can probe very small mass-squared differences (\(\Delta m^2\)) and relatively large mixing angles, but their sensitivity is limited to mixing angles above about 10% at large \(\Delta m^2\) because the low neutrino energy (few MeV) prevents appearance searches and makes neutral-current detection of oscillated flavors impractical.

## Method

The authors review the methodology of reactor neutrino oscillation experiments, which measure the flux and energy spectrum of \(\bar{\nu}_e\) from nuclear reactors at a known distance \(L\) and compare them to the expected flux at \(L=0\) (the reactor core). The detection reaction is inverse beta decay (\(\bar{\nu}_e + p \rightarrow e^+ + n\)), which has a threshold of about 1.8 MeV; the coincidence of the positron and neutron signals provides background rejection. The \(\bar{\nu}_e\) yield is determined from the reactor thermal power, fuel composition, and burn-up state, combined with measured fission rates for the four main isotopes (\(^{235}\)U, \(^{238}\)U, \(^{239}\)Pu, \(^{241}\)Pu) and known beta-decay spectra of fission fragments.

## Key numbers

* Reactor \(\bar{\nu}_e\) yield: approximately \(6 \times 10^{20}\) \(\bar{\nu}_e\) per core per second, with about 1.5 \(\bar{\nu}_e\) per fission above the 1.8 MeV detection threshold (about 25% of the total).
* Oscillation length formula: \(L_{\text{osc}} = 2.48 \, E_\nu(\text{MeV}) / \Delta m^2(\text{eV}^2)\) meters.
* Typical commercial light-water reactor thermal power: about 3 GW\(_{th}\).
* Chooz and Palo Verde experiments: baselines of about 1 km, fiducial masses of order 10 tons, sensitive to \(\Delta m^2\) values associated with the atmospheric neutrino anomaly (\(10^{-2}\) to \(10^{-3}\) eV\(^2\)).
* KamLAND experiment: baseline larger than 100 km, designed to test the large-mixing-angle MSW solution of the solar neutrino puzzle (\(\Delta m^2\) up to \(10^{-4}\) eV\(^2\)).
* Number of neutrino flavors from LEP data: \(2.994 \pm 0.012\) from combined fit (Groom et al. 2000).
* LSND oscillation probability: about \(3 \times 10^{-3}\) for \(\bar{\nu}_\mu \rightarrow \bar{\nu}_e\).

## Context

This review summarizes the status of reactor neutrino oscillation experiments as of 2001, extending the long tradition of reactor-based neutrino studies that began with the Reines and Cowan (1953, 1959) discovery of the neutrino. The paper places the Chooz and Palo Verde experiments (baselines ~1 km) in the context of the atmospheric neutrino anomaly observed by Super-Kamiokande (Fukuda 1998, 2000), and describes the upcoming KamLAND experiment as the only terrestrial neutrino source capable of testing the large-mixing-angle MSW solution to the solar neutrino deficit (Kirsten 2000, Fukuda 2001, Ahmad 2001). The review also contrasts reactor experiments with accelerator-based projects such as MINOS (Wojcicki 2001a) and the CERN-to-Gran-Sasso program (Opera, Icarus; Wojcicki 2001b), which can probe oscillation channels not involving \(\bar{\nu}_e\) and achieve sub-1% mixing sensitivity.
