# Nuclear Incompressibility at Finite Temperature and Entropy

> Features of the nuclear isothermal incompressibility $κ$ and adiabatic incompressibility $κ_Q$ are investigated. The calculations are done at zero and finite temperatures and non zero entropy and for several equations of state. It is shown that $κ_Q$ decreases with increasing entropy while the isothermal $κ$ increases with increasing $T$. A duality is found between the adiabatic $κ_Q$ and the T=0 isothermal $κ$. Our isothermal results are compared with a recent lattice Monte Carlo calculation done at finite $T$. The necessity of including correlations is shown if $κ$ is to have a peak with increasing $T$ as seen in the Monte Carlo calculations. A peak in $κ$ is linked to attractive scattering correlations in two nucleons channel in the virial expansion in our approach which are Pauli blocked at low $T$.

# Nuclear Incompressibility at Finite Temperature and Entropy

## Key claim

The isothermal incompressibility κ increases with temperature and can exhibit a peak (as seen in lattice Monte Carlo calculations) only if attractive scattering correlations in the two-nucleon channel are included, because these correlations are Pauli blocked at low temperature but become active at higher temperature; the adiabatic incompressibility κ_Q instead decreases with increasing entropy and exhibits a duality with the T=0 isothermal incompressibility.

## Method

The authors use a Skyrme-type mean-field parametrization of symmetric nuclear matter with a repulsive term proportional to ρ^(1+α), where α controls the stiffness of the equation of state, and compute the isothermal and adiabatic incompressibilities from the second derivative of the energy per particle with respect to density at constant temperature or constant entropy. At higher temperatures, they replace the degenerate Fermi gas kinetic energy with a virial expansion in ρλ³, incorporating antisymmetrization coefficients c₁, c₂, and then extend the formalism to include attractive correlations via cluster-like terms in the grand canonical partition function, using nucleon-nucleon phase shifts and bound-state contributions to model the effect of correlated pairs.

## Key numbers

- At T=0, the incompressibility κ = 165 + 207α MeV; for α=1/3, κ = 234 MeV; for α→0, κ = 165 MeV; for α=1, κ = 372 MeV.
- At finite temperature (α=1/3), κ = 242 MeV at T=2.5 MeV, 265 MeV at T=5 MeV, and 302 MeV at T=7.5 MeV.
- Low-T expansion gives κ(α=1/3) = 234 + 1.32 T² MeV (T in MeV).
- At fixed entropy (α=1/3), κ = 234 − 38 (S/A)² MeV.
- In the high-T limit (α=1/3), κ saturates at 468 MeV with x_m = 1.53 (ρ_m = 1.53 ρ₀); for α→0, κ saturates at 380 MeV with x_m = 1.84.
- The lattice Monte Carlo calculation of Müller et al. shows a peak in κ of about 1500 MeV at T ≈ 14 MeV and saturation density 2ρ₀ = 0.3 fm⁻³, then a flat value near 250 MeV above T=20 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the study of nuclear incompressibility from zero temperature (Zamick, Blaizot, Pearson) to finite temperature and entropy, and directly compares its mean-field results with the quantum Monte Carlo lattice calculations of Müller et al. (2004), which showed a dramatic peak in κ near T=14 MeV. The authors also connect their approach to the virial expansion methods of Huang and to earlier work by Chase, Mekjian, and Lee on fragmentation models and recursive partition functions. The paper enables future studies of how correlations, particularly attractive two-nucleon scattering channels, modify the equation of state at high density and temperature, with implications for heavy-ion collisions and supernova astrophysics.
