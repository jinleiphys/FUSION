# Comparing effective temperatures in standard and Tsallis distributions from transverse momentum spectra in small collision systems

> The transverse momentum ($p_T$) spectra of identified light charged hadrons, specifically bosons ($π^{\pm}$ and $K^{\pm}$) as well as fermions [$p(\bar p)$], produced in small collision systems, namely deuteron-gold (d+Au) and proton-proton (p+p) collisions at the top energy of the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) with a center-of-mass energy of $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV, are investigated in this paper. In present study, d+Au collisions are categorized into three centrality classes: central (0--20\%), semi-central (20--40\%), and peripheral (40--100\%) collisions. Various types of distributions, including standard [Bose-Einstein (Fermi-Dirac) and Boltzmann] and Tsallis distributions, are employed to fit the same $p_T$ spectra to derive different effective temperatures denoted as $T_{eff}$. The results indicate that $T_{eff}$ values obtained from Bose-Einstein, Boltzmann, Fermi-Dirac, and Tsallis distributions exhibit systematically a decreasing trend. Meanwhile, these $T_{eff}$ values also show a decreasing trend with a decrease in collision centrality. Furthermore, based on the spectra of given particles, a perfect linear relationship is observed between different pairwise combinations of $T_{eff}$ derived from both Boltzmann and Bose-Einstein (Fermi-Dirac) distributions as well as between Tsallis and Bose-Einstein (Fermi-Dirac) distributions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that effective temperatures extracted from Bose-Einstein, Fermi-Dirac, Boltzmann, and Tsallis distributions fitted to the same transverse momentum spectra in small collision systems (d+Au and p+p at 200 GeV) follow systematic decreasing trends and exhibit approximate linear relationships between pairwise combinations, with the caveat that these relationships are model-dependent and the Tsallis distribution systematically underestimates the effective temperature compared to Bose-Einstein/Fermi-Dirac baselines.

## Method

The authors fitted three-component Bose-Einstein (for pions and kaons) and three-component Fermi-Dirac (for protons/antiprotons) distributions, three-component Boltzmann distributions, and single-component Tsallis distributions to the same experimental transverse momentum spectra of identified light charged hadrons produced in d+Au collisions at three centrality classes (0-20%, 20-40%, 40-100%) and in p+p collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV, using data from the STAR Collaboration. The effective temperatures from each distribution were extracted and compared across centrality classes and particle species, and linear relationships between pairwise combinations of effective temperatures were quantified.

## Key numbers

- Center-of-mass energy: sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV for both d+Au and p+p collisions at RHIC
- Three centrality classes for d+Au: central (0-20%), semi-central (20-40%), and peripheral (40-100%)
- Rapidity range for all measurements: |y|<0.5
- For the further investigation of linear relationships, Tsallis temperatures were varied over a range from 0.006 to 0.26 GeV in increments of 0.002, 0.01, or 0.02 GeV, with specific examples shown at T_Tsallis=0.10, 0.14, 0.18, and 0.22 GeV
- The entropy index q extracted from Tsallis distributions approaches unity, with values closer to 1 for central collisions than peripheral collisions

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Zhang et al., 2024) which examined Boltzmann and Tsallis temperatures in large heavy-ion collision systems (Au+Au and Pb+Pb), now focusing on small collision systems (d+Au and p+p) at top RHIC energy. The paper also builds on earlier findings (Zhang et al., 2024) of approximate linear relationships between Tsallis and Boltzmann temperatures from Au+Au collisions at 130 and 200 GeV and Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV. The results enable the use of Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac temperatures as standard baselines for cross-model comparisons of effective temperatures in high-energy nuclear collisions, and suggest that partial thermalization persists even in small collision systems where non-equilibrium effects are expected to be significant.
