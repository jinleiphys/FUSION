# Stability of the Zagreb Carnegie-Mellon-Berkeley model

> In ref. [1] we have used the Zagreb realization of Carnegie-Melon-Berkeley coupled-channel, unitary model as a tool for extracting pole positions from the world collection of partial wave data, with the aim of eliminating model dependence in pole-search procedures. In order that the method is sensible, we in this paper discuss the stability of the method with respect to the strong variation of different model ingredients. We show that the Zagreb CMB procedure is very stable with strong variation of the model assumptions, and that it can reliably predict the pole positions of the fitted partial wave amplitudes.

## Key claim

The Zagreb realization of the Carnegie-Mellon-Berkeley (CMB) coupled-channel unitary model is shown to be very stable under strong variation of model assumptions, so that it can reliably predict pole positions of fitted partial wave amplitudes; however, the reliability of the isobar model itself cannot be tested in this way, and the analysis involves the approximation that possible three-body states are effectively represented by a group of two-body ones.

## Method

The authors tested stability by drastically modifying one model ingredient at a time, such as the channel-resonance form factor, dispersion integral subtraction constant, effective channel mass, number of channels, and background parameterization, while fitting to a fixed input data set of pseudo-data points generated from the Zagreb model’s own partial wave amplitudes with normally distributed errors (standard deviation 0.02). Pole positions were extracted by analytic continuation of the channel propagator using a Pietarinen expansion, and the resulting shifts in pole positions were taken as a measure of sensitivity to each model assumption.

## Key numbers

- The standard deviation for pseudo-data generation was set to 0.02, similar to the average error value of GWU data.
- The Pietarinen expansion required 7 or 8 coefficients at most for a good fit.
- The input data set included three channels: πN → πN, πN → ηN, and πN → π²N.
- The S₁₁(1518) pole is identified with the N(1535) S₁₁ PDG resonance; the P₁₁(1708) pole is identified with N(1710) P₁₁, and a new P₁₁(1728) pole is not confirmed by PDG.
- Reduced chi-squared values for fits with one, two, and three channels are compared in Tables 2–4 (not fully reproduced in the truncated text), showing that fitting all three channels yields slightly better χ² than fitting only two.

## Context

This work extends the authors’ previous pole-extraction study (Osmanović et al., 2011) by quantifying the stability of the Zagreb CMB method against variations in model ingredients, addressing concerns that different analytic continuations from different partial-wave analyses (e.g., Karlsruhe-Helsinki KH80, VPI/GWU, EBAC, Jülich, Dubna-Mainz-Taipei, Giessen) produce different pole sets. The results enable a more reliable averaging of pole positions across multiple partial-wave analyses, reducing systematic uncertainty from model dependence, and provide a foundation for future work on the correct choice of analytic form.
