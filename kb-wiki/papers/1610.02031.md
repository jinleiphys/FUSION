# The Asymptotic Form of Non-Global Logarithms, Black Disc Saturation, and Gluonic Deserts

> We develop an asymptotic perturbation theory for the large logarithmic behavior of the non-linear integro-differential equation describing the soft correlations of QCD jet measurements, the Banfi-Marchesini-Smye (BMS) equation. This equation captures the late-time evolution of radiating color dipoles after a hard collision. This allows us to prove that at large values of the control variable (the non-global logarithm, a function of the infra-red energy scales associated with distinct hard jets in an event), the distribution has a gaussian tail. We compute the decay width analytically, giving a closed form expression, and find it to be jet geometry independent, up to the number of legs of the dipole in the active jet. Enabling the asymptotic expansion is the correct perturbative seed, where we perturb around an anzats encoding formally no real emissions, an intuition motivated by the buffer region found in jet dynamics. This must be supplemented with the correct application of the BFKL approximation to the BMS equation in collinear limits. Comparing to the asymptotics of the conformally related evolution equation encountered in small-x physics, the Balitisky-Kovchegov (BK) equation, we find that the asymptotic form of the non-global logarithms directly maps to the black-disc unitarity limit of the BK equation, despite the contrasting physical pictures. Indeed, we recover the equations of saturation physics in the final state dynamics of QCD.

## Key claim

The paper proves that at large values of the non-global logarithm (NGL), the distribution has a Gaussian tail with a decay width that is independent of jet geometry, depending only on whether the dipole legs are inside or outside the active jet region. This result is conditional on the existence of a "buffer region" where real emissions are dynamically suppressed at large angles, and the analysis relies on a specific asymptotic expansion that perturbs around an ansatz encoding formally no real emissions.

## Method

The authors develop an asymptotic perturbation theory for the Banfi-Marchesini-Smye (BMS) equation by first introducing a collinear regulator (the delta-regulator) to form a linear differential inequality that bounds the solution from below. They then use this bounding solution as a perturbative seed, and apply the Balitsky-Fadin-Kuraev-Lipatov (BFKL) equation to approximate the BMS equation in the collinear limit at small angles. The asymptotic condition is derived by splitting the angular integration into a small-angle region (where the BFKL solution applies) and a large-angle region (where the collinearly regulated ansatz dominates), and demanding consistency between the two regions as the NGL grows large.

## Key numbers

* The critical exponent for the slowest possible asymptotic decay is nu_min approximately 0.62755.
* The Gaussian decay coefficient for the in-out dipole configuration (one leg in the active jet, one outside) is chi(nu_min)/(4 nu_min) approximately 1.2208.
* For the in-in dipole configuration (both legs in the active jet), the decay is twice as strong: chi(nu_min)/(2 nu_min).
* The critical angle defining the buffer region boundary scales as theta_c(L) proportional to exp(-chi(nu)/(2 nu) L).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical understanding of non-global logarithms beyond fixed-order expansions and numerical resummations, which were previously limited to small L regions. The authors build on the BMS equation of Banfi, Marchesini, and Smye (2002) and the dressed gluon expansion of Larkoski, Neill, and others (2016), which revealed a finite radius of convergence due to the buffer region. The paper establishes a direct mapping between the asymptotic form of NGLs and the black-disc unitarity limit of the Balitsky-Kovchegov (BK) equation from small-x physics, despite the contrasting physical pictures of initial-state versus final-state dynamics. This enables future analytic studies of the far-tail region of jet observables and suggests that saturation physics equations appear in final-state QCD dynamics.
