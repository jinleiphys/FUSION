# How long does the hydrogen atom live?

> It is possible that the proton is stable while atomic hydrogen is not. This is the case in models with new particles carrying baryon number which are light enough to be stable themselves but heavy enough so that proton decay is kinematically blocked. Models of new physics that explain the neutron lifetime anomaly generically have this feature, allowing for atomic hydrogen to decay through electron capture on a proton. We calculate the radiative hydrogen decay rate involving the emission of a few hundred keV photon, which makes this process detectable in experiment. In particular, we show that the low energy part of the Borexino spectrum is sensitive to radiative hydrogen decay, and turn this into a limit on the hydrogen lifetime of order $10^{30}~\rm s$ or stronger. For models where the neutron mixes with a dark baryon, $χ$, this limits the mixing angle to roughly $10^{-11}$, restricting the $n\toχγ$ branching to $10^{-4}$, over a wide range of parameter space.

## Key claim
Atomic hydrogen can decay via electron capture on a proton even if the proton itself is stable, provided new particles carrying baryon number exist with masses between 937.76 MeV and 938.78 MeV. The radiative decay mode emitting a few hundred keV photon makes this process detectable, and Borexino data constrain the hydrogen lifetime to approximately 10^30 s or stronger, but only for models where the new particle masses fall in this specific window that kinematically blocks proton decay.

## Method
The authors calculate the radiative hydrogen decay rate for three scenarios: a toy model with a dark scalar φ coupling to proton and electron; a neutron-dark baryon mixing model where the neutron mixes with a Dirac fermion χ carrying baryon number; and a general effective field theory with two light neutral fermions carrying baryon and lepton number. They compute the photon energy spectrum from radiative hydrogen decay and compare it to Borexino data from Agostini (2015) by fitting the measured spectrum with background components from solar neutrinos, 14C, 210Bi, 210Po, and pileup, then determining the 90% confidence level upper limits on the signal parameters.

## Key numbers
- Hydrogen lifetime limit from Borexino: τ_H ≳ 10^30 s or stronger (order 10^30 s)
- Neutron mixing angle limit: θ ≲ 10^-11 for models where neutron mixes with dark baryon χ
- n→χγ branching ratio limit: Br_n→χγ ≲ 10^-4
- Toy model hydrogen decay rate: Γ_H→φγ = (1/10^32 s) × (λ/10^-20)^2 × (Q/m_e)
- Neutron mixing model hydrogen decay rate: Γ_H→νχ = (1/10^27 s) × (θ/10^-9)^2 × (Q/m_e)^2
- Radiative branching fraction in neutron mixing model: Br_H→νχγ ≃ 2×10^-4 × (Q/m_e)^2
- Radiative branching fraction photon spectrum peak: dBr/dω ≃ (5×10^-6/keV) × (ω/m_e) × (1 - ω/Q)^2, peaked at ω = Q/3
- EFT with m_ℓ=m_b hydrogen decay rate: Γ_H→ℓb = (3×10^27 s)^-1 × √(Q/m_e) × (100 PeV/Λ)^4
- Expected event rates at Borexino: 4×10^4/(100 t day) × (θ/10^-9)^2 × (Q/m_e)^4 for neutron mixing; 3×10^3/(100 t day) × (10^32 s/τ_H) for toy model; 2×10^4/(100 t day) × (100 PeV/Λ)^4 × (Q/m_e)^4 for EFT
- Borexino background rate above 225 keV: about 600/(100 t day)
- Lower bound on m_χ from 9Be stability: 937.993 MeV
- Upper bound on m_χ from hydrogen decay: 938.78 MeV (m_p + m_e)
- Lower bound on m_χ from proton stability: 937.76 MeV (m_p - m_e)

## Context
This work extends the study of baryon number violation in models where new particles with masses near the nucleon mass scale can explain the neutron lifetime anomaly, as proposed by Fornal and Grinstein (2018). The authors build on the observation by Berezhiani (2018) that atomic hydrogen can decay through electron capture in neutron mixing models, and use Borexino data from Agostini (2015) to set constraints that are stronger than direct searches for n→χγ by Tang (2018) over a wide range of parameter space. The results enable future searches for hydrogen decay using other organic scintillators or hydrogen-doped detectors, and provide a direct test of dark matter models involving dark baryons without the nuclear physics complications of searches in heavier nuclei like 11Be.
