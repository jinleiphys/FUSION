# Hydrodynamical study of neutrino-driven wind as an r-process site

> We study the neutrino-driven wind from the proto-neutron star by the general relativistic hydrodynamical simulations. We examine the properties of the neutrino-driven wind to explore the possibility of the r-process nucleosynthesis. The numerical simulations with the neutrino heating and cooling processes are performed with the assumption of the constant neutrino luminosity by using realistic profiles of the proto-neutron star (PNS) as well as simplified models. The dependence on the mass of PNS and the neutrino luminosity is studied systematically. Comparisons with the analytic treatment in the previous studies are also done. In the cases with the realistic PNS, we found that the entropy per baryon and the expansion time scale are neither high nor short enough for the r-process within the current assumptions. On the other hand, we found that the expansion time scale obtained by the hydrodynamical simulations is systematically shorter than that in the analytic solutions due to our proper treatment of the equation of state. This fact might lead to the increase of the neutron-to-seed ratio, which is suitable for the r-process in the neutrino-driven wind. Indeed, in the case of massive and compact proto-neutron stars with high neutrino luminosities, the expansion time scale is found short enough in the hydrodynamical simulations and the r-process elements up to A ~ 200 are produced in the r-process network calculation.

## Key claim

The paper finds that in models based on realistic proto-neutron star cooling simulations, the entropy per baryon and expansion time scale are neither high nor short enough for the r-process under the current assumptions; however, the expansion time scale obtained from hydrodynamical simulations is systematically shorter than in analytic solutions due to the proper treatment of the equation of state, and for massive and compact proto-neutron stars with high neutrino luminosities, the expansion time scale becomes short enough to produce r-process elements up to mass number A ~ 200.

## Method

The authors performed general relativistic, spherically symmetric, implicit Lagrangian hydrodynamical simulations of the neutrino-driven wind from proto-neutron stars, incorporating neutrino heating and cooling processes (including charged-current reactions on nucleons, neutrino-electron scattering, and pair processes) under the optically thin limit. Initial models were constructed either from proto-neutron star cooling simulations (with baryon masses of 1.62 and 2.00 solar masses) or by solving the Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation for given mass and radius, using the relativistic equation of state table of Shen et al. (1998) extended to low densities. Neutrino spectra were assumed monochromatic with average energies of 10 MeV for electron neutrinos, 20 MeV for electron antineutrinos, and 30 MeV for muon and tau neutrinos, with constant luminosities common to all flavors.

## Key numbers

- Entropy per baryon reached up to 87 (in units of k_B) at T=0.5 MeV for the model based on a 1.62 solar mass proto-neutron star with total neutrino luminosity 6 x 10^51 ergs/s
- Expansion time scale (e-fold time of temperature at T=0.5 MeV) was 0.15 seconds for the same model
- Mass loss rate was 1.5 x 10^-5 solar masses per second for the same model
- Electron fraction reached 0.46 at T=0.5 MeV for model p06 with average electron antineutrino energy 20 MeV, and 0.38 for a model with average electron antineutrino energy 30 MeV
- For model b09 (mass 2.0 solar masses, radius 10 km, total luminosity 6 x 10^52 ergs/s), the neutron-to-seed ratio at T_9=2.5 was 120 and the electron fraction was 0.42, producing r-process elements up to A ~ 200 including the third peak at A=195 and second peak at A=130

## Context

This work extends the general relativistic analytic studies of the neutrino-driven wind by Cardall and Fuller (1997) and Otsuki et al. (1999) by performing full hydrodynamical simulations, and contrasts with earlier non-relativistic studies by Qian and Woosley (1996) and Witti et al. (1994) and Takahashi et al. (1994) which found insufficient entropy for the r-process. The key advance is that the hydrodynamical treatment with a proper equation of state yields systematically shorter expansion time scales than analytic treatments, relaxing the constraints on neutrino luminosity required for successful r-process nucleosynthesis. This enables future work incorporating more realistic, time-dependent neutrino spectra from proto-neutron star cooling simulations to determine whether the high-entropy bubble scenario or the rapid expansion scenario is realized.
