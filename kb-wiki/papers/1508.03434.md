# Solving the Balitsky-Kovchegov equation at next to leading order accuracy

> We present the first numerical solution to the next to leading order Balitsky-Kovchegov (BK) equation in coordinate space in the large-$N_\mathrm{c}$ limit. In addition to the dipole operator we also solve the evolution of the "conformal dipole" for which the conformal invariance breaking double logarithmic term is absent from the evolution equation. The NLO corrections are shown to slow down the evolution. We show that the solution depends strongly on the details of the initial condition, and that the solution to the equation is not positive definite with all initial conditions relevant for phenomenological applications.

## Key claim

The first numerical solution to the next-to-leading-order (NLO) Balitsky-Kovchegov (BK) equation in coordinate space shows that the NLO corrections slow down the evolution, but the solution is not positive definite with initial conditions relevant for phenomenological applications, and the evolution speed can become negative at small dipole sizes due to a double logarithmic term.

## Method

The authors solved the NLO BK equation numerically in coordinate space for the dipole scattering amplitude in the large-\(N_\mathrm{c}\) limit, using a modified McLerran-Venugopalan (MV) model as the initial condition with an anomalous dimension parameter \(\gamma\). They also solved the evolution for the "conformal dipole," which removes the double logarithmic term from the evolution equation, and implemented a resummation of double logarithmic contributions to all orders following Iancu (2015).

## Key numbers

- Evolution speed \(\partial_y N(r)/N(r)\) becomes negative at all dipole sizes for initial saturation scale \(Q_\mathrm{s,0}/\Lambda_\mathrm{QCD}\) values where the strong coupling constant and NLO corrections are largest (e.g., small \(Q_\mathrm{s,0}\)).
- With initial anomalous dimension \(\gamma=1.0\), the anomalous dimension \(\gamma(r) = d \ln N(r)/d \ln r^2\) increases rapidly during evolution, approaching a step-function form; with \(\gamma=0.6\) in the initial condition, the evolution speed remains positive at small dipoles (\(r\Lambda_\mathrm{QCD} \gtrsim 10^{-6}\)) within the studied rapidity interval up to \(y=30\).
- The double logarithmic term (proportional to \(\ln X^2/r^2 \ln Y^2/r^2\) in the kernel \(K_1\)) is identified as the term driving the evolution speed negative; resummation of this term shifts the negativity to significantly smaller dipoles but does not completely solve the problem.

## Context

This work provides the first numerical solution to the NLO BK equation, extending the leading-order BK framework used in fits to HERA data by Albacete (2010) and Lappi (2013). The results show that the NLO equation, as derived by Balitsky (2008), is unstable for phenomenologically relevant initial conditions due to double logarithmic contributions. This motivates resummation schemes proposed by Iancu (2015) and further study of kinematical constraints as in Motyka (2009) and Beuf (2014), aiming toward a stable NLO framework for deep inelastic scattering and saturation phenomenology.
