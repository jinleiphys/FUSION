# Self-Consistency Requirement in High-Energy Nuclear Scattering

> Practically all serious calculations of exclusive particle production in ultra-relativistic nuclear or hadronic interactions are performed in the framework of Gribov-Regge theory or the eikonalized parton model scheme. It is the purpose of this paper to point out serious inconsistencies in the above-mentioned approaches. We will demonstrate that requiring theoretical self-consistency reduces the freedom in modeling high energy nuclear scattering enormously. We will introduce a fully self-consistent formulation of the multiple-scattering scheme in the framework of a Gribov-Regge type effective theory. In addition, we develop new computational techniques which allow for the first time a satisfactory solution of the problem in the sense that calculation s of observable quantities can be done strictly within a self-consistent formalism.

## Key claim

The paper argues that standard Gribov-Regge and eikonalized parton model approaches to ultra-relativistic nuclear scattering are internally inconsistent because they use different treatments of energy conservation for cross section calculations versus particle production, and treat successive Pomerons differently from the first one. The authors claim that requiring full self-consistency, including explicit energy conservation at all levels, drastically reduces the freedom in modeling high-energy nuclear scattering.

## Method

The authors develop a "Parton-based Gribov-Regge Theory" where elastic and inelastic scattering amplitudes are defined using cut diagram techniques from field theory, with elementary interactions classified as soft, semi-hard, or hard depending on whether valence quarks, sea quarks (emitted from soft Pomerons), or hard parton-parton scattering are involved. For nucleus-nucleus scattering, they construct the elastic amplitude from purely parallel elementary interactions between partonic constituents, with explicit integration over momentum fractions to enforce energy conservation. To make the formalism computationally tractable, they implement a Markov chain Monte Carlo method using the Metropolis algorithm, where new configurations are proposed by replacing single elementary interactions according to an approximate distribution and accepted with a probability that ensures detailed balance.

## Key numbers

- The Markov chain method achieves excellent agreement with conventional techniques using \( 66.7\, k_{\mathrm{max}} \) iterations, where \( k_{\mathrm{max}} \) is an upper limit estimate of the number of nucleon-nucleon interactions.
- Using the approximate distribution \( \Omega_0 \) alone (without the full self-consistent treatment) introduces errors reaching about 100% even at the nucleon-nucleon level.

## Context

This work extends the Gribov-Regge approach (Gribov, 1968; Gribov, 1969) and the eikonalized parton model (Sjostrand, 1987) by pointing out inconsistencies in how these frameworks handle energy conservation and Pomeron identity in multiple scattering. The authors specifically criticize earlier treatments (Braun, 1990; Abramovsky, 1992) for using different models for cross section calculations and particle production. The new formalism enables practical calculations of observable quantities within a self-consistent framework, with the Markov chain techniques (Hladik, 1998) providing the first computationally feasible solution for the high-dimensional probability distributions that arise.
