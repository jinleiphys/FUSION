# Remark on statistical model fits to particle ratios in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> In order to determine the chemical freeze-out parameters of the hadron-emitting source in relativistic heavy ion collisions some studies in literature perform fits by using as data input a subsample of ratios calculated out of experimentally measured hadron yields instead of yields themselves. We show that this is a statistically incorrect method fit, implying a bias in the extracted parameters.

## Key claim

Using a subsample of particle ratios formed *a posteriori* from experimentally measured hadron yields, instead of the yields themselves, in statistical model fits to determine chemical freeze-out parameters is a statistically incorrect method that introduces bias and inefficiency in the parameter estimators, even if correlations between ratios are taken into account.

## Method

The author demonstrates the statistical flaw through three simple analytical examples (weighted average, linear model, exponential model) comparing maximum-likelihood fits to the original measurements with fits to a chosen subsample of ratios. A realistic case study is then performed using STAR midrapidity yields at √s_NN = 130 GeV, where a fit to 11 ratios (chosen to enhance the effect) is compared to the correct fit to the 12 original yields, both with and without including correlations in the ratio covariance matrix.

## Key numbers

- In the realistic STAR example at √s_NN = 130 GeV, the fit to yields gives T = 168.4 ± 3.2 MeV and μ_B = 36.2 ± 0.6 MeV; the fit to ratios without correlations gives T = 175.6 ± 3.0 MeV and μ_B = 23.4 ± 0.4 MeV, a difference of 8.2 MeV (2.61σ) in temperature and 13 MeV (21σ) in baryon-chemical potential.
- Including correlations in the ratio fit reduces the discrepancy: T = 170.3 ± 3.0 MeV and μ_B = 33.1 ± 0.6 MeV.
- The χ²/dof values are 6.3/10 for the yield fit, 6.6/10 for the ratio fit without correlations, and 7.6/10 for the ratio fit with correlations.
- In the exponential model example (example 3), the difference between the correct estimate of parameter b and the ratio-method estimate is 2%.

## Context

This work extends the author's earlier discussion of data-input discrepancies in statistical model fits (Becattini 2006) and directly critiques the common practice, used by several groups since the original application by Braun-Munzinger et al. (1999), of replacing yields with ratios to eliminate the volume parameter. The paper shows that this practice introduces a systematic bias that can be as large as O(10) MeV in temperature and baryon-chemical potential, and recommends that future analyses should avoid this method to achieve the best accuracy.
