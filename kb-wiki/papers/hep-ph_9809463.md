# Do we observe Lévy flights in cosmic rays?

> We argue that the so called long flying component (LFC) observed in some cosmic ray experiments are yet another manifestation of Lévy distributions (with index $q=1.3$), this time of the distribution observation probability of the depths of starting points of cascades. It means that LFC is governed by the so called long-tail Lévy-like anomalous superdiffusion, a phenomenon frequently encountered in Nature. Its connection with the so called Tsallis's statistics is also briefly discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the long flying component (LFC) observed in cosmic ray experiments is a manifestation of Levy distributions (with index q=1.3) describing the distribution of depths of cascade starting points, rather than being a separate physical phenomenon requiring new dynamics. The authors propose that even without detailed dynamical knowledge of the origin of such effects, the power-law behavior can be parametrized with a single new parameter q.

## Method

The authors fit the depth distribution of cascade starting points from the Pamir experiment's extra thick lead chamber to a power-law function derived from Tsallis's generalized statistics, specifically dN(T)/dT = const * [1 - (1-q)T/lambda]^(1/(1-q)). They derive this form by replacing the constant absorption length lambda in the standard exponential decay with a depth-dependent lambda(T) = lambda - (1-q)T, and connect this to Levy distributions through the relation q = (3+gamma)/(1+gamma) for large x.

## Key numbers

- The parameter q equals 1.3 for the depth distribution of cascade starting points in the Pamir lead chamber
- The absorption length lambda = 18.85 +/- 6.62 c.u. (cascade units) for hadron interactions in the lead chamber
- The effective depth-dependent absorption length becomes lambda(T) = lambda + 0.3 T (since 1-q = -0.3)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous explanation of the LFC based on fluctuating cross sections (Wilk and Wlodarczyk, 1998) by providing a statistical mechanics framework. It connects the observed power-law behavior in cosmic ray cascades to Levy flights and Tsallis's generalized statistics (Tsallis, 1988), which have been observed in diverse systems including heartbeats, albatross travel patterns, stock market behavior, and leaky faucet dynamics. The approach enables a unified description of leading particle spectra in multiparticle production processes using a single physical parameter q, which the authors state is under investigation.
