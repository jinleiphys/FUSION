# Light exotic nuclei with extreme neutron excess and $2 \leq z \leq 8$

> Using HF+BCS method we study light nuclei with nuclear charge in the range $2 \leq Z \leq 8$ and lying near the neutron drip line. The HF method uses effective Skyrme forces and allows for axial deformations. We find that the neutron drip line forms stability peninsulas at $^{18}$He and $^{40}$C. These isotopes are found to be stable against one neutron emission and possess the highest known neutron to proton ratio in stable nuclei.

## Key claim

Using HF+BCS calculations with Skyrme forces and allowing for axial deformations, the authors find that the neutron drip line for light nuclei with \(2 \leq Z \leq 8\) forms stability peninsulas at \(^{18}\)He and \(^{40}\)C, which are stable against one-neutron emission and possess the highest known neutron-to-proton ratio in stable nuclei; however, this stability depends on the specific Skyrme force parametrization (SkI2) and the complete filling of neutron subshells with large angular momentum.

## Method

The deformed Hartree-Fock (DHF) method with effective Skyrme forces (primarily SkI2) was used, expanding single-particle wave functions in an axially deformed harmonic oscillator basis up to principal quantum number \(N_0 = 18\) (1330 basis functions). Pairing was treated in the BCS scheme with a constant pairing strength \(G = (19.5/(N+Z))[1 \pm 0.51(N-Z)/(N+Z)]\), restricted to bound one-particle states in DHF calculations. For nuclei on stability peninsulas, additional spherical Hartree-Fock (SHF) calculations were performed directly in coordinate space to analyze continuum states and localized quasibound states under centrifugal barriers.

## Key numbers

- \(^{18}\)He: one-neutron separation energy \(S_n = 0.45\) MeV (DHF) and \(S_n = 0.406\) MeV (SHF); neutron-to-proton ratio \(N/Z = 8\); centrifugal barrier height 1.35 MeV.
- \(^{40}\)C: one-neutron separation energy \(S_n = 0.597\) MeV (DHF) and \(S_n = 0.594\) MeV (SHF); neutron-to-proton ratio \(N/Z \simeq 5.67\); centrifugal barrier height 2.48 MeV.
- \(^{18}\)He: neutron-proton rms radius difference \(\Delta R_{n,p} = 4.288 - 2.150 = 2.138\) fm; ratio \(\langle r^2_n\rangle^{1/2} / \langle r^2_p\rangle^{1/2} \simeq 1.994\).
- \(^{40}\)C: neutron-proton rms radius difference \(\Delta R_{n,p} = 4.709 - 2.800 = 1.909\) fm; ratio \(\langle r^2_n\rangle^{1/2} / \langle r^2_p\rangle^{1/2} \simeq 1.682\).
- \(^{32}\)C: quadrupole deformation \(\beta = -0.32\); stability restoration at \(N=26\) from the \(1f_{7/2}\) subshell member with \(\Omega = 7/2^{-}\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Tarasov et al., earlier papers from 2007-2014) on islands and peninsulas of stability beyond the conventional neutron drip line at magic and new magic neutron numbers (N = 32, 58, 82, 126, 184, 258). The results complement earlier findings for heavier nuclei and demonstrate that the maximal neutron-to-proton ratio is attainable only in light nuclei. The existence of stability peninsulas at \(^{18}\)He and \(^{40}\)C enables future experimental searches at facilities like FRIB (planned for 2022) and provides a theoretical upper bound for the neutron-to-proton ratio in stable nuclei.
