# Evolution of a proto-neutron star with a nuclear many-body equation of state: Neutrino luminosity and gravitational wave frequencies

> In a core-collapse supernova, a huge amount of energy is released in the Kelvin-Helmholtz phase subsequent to the explosion, when the proto-neutron star cools and deleptonizes as it loses neutrinos. Most of this energy is emitted through neutrinos, but a fraction of it can be released through gravitational waves. We model the evolution of a proto-neutron star in the Kelvin-Helmholtz phase using a general relativistic numerical code, and a recently proposed finite temperature, many-body equation of state; from this we consistently compute the diffusion coefficients driving the evolution. To include the many-body equation of state, we develop a new fitting formula for the high density baryon free energy at finite temperature and intermediate proton fraction. We estimate the emitted neutrino signal, assessing its detectability by present terrestrial detectors, and we determine the frequencies and damping times of the quasi-normal modes which would characterize the gravitational wave signal emitted in this stage.

## Key claim

The paper models the Kelvin-Helmholtz phase of proto-neutron star evolution using a consistent many-body equation of state (CBF-EI) that includes finite-temperature nuclear dynamics, but the results depend on the specific choice of equation of state and the assumption that the evolution can be treated as a sequence of quasi-stationary configurations after about 200 ms from core bounce.

## Method

The authors developed a general relativistic numerical code that evolves a proto-neutron star through the Kelvin-Helmholtz phase using quasi-stationary configurations with neutrino diffusion determining the thermal and composition evolution. They implemented three finite-temperature nucleonic equations of state (GM3 mean field, CBF-EI many-body, and LS-bulk) and developed a new polynomial fitting formula for the baryon free energy at finite temperature and intermediate proton fraction to incorporate the computationally expensive many-body equation of state. Neutrino diffusion coefficients were computed consistently from each equation of state using effective masses and single-particle potentials, and the quasi-normal mode frequencies and damping times were determined from the evolving stellar configurations.

## Key numbers

- The CBF-EI equation of state has a saturation density of 0.16 fm⁻³, binding energy of approximately -11 MeV, incompressibility of 180 MeV, and symmetry energy of 30 MeV
- The GM3 equation of state has a saturation density of 0.153 fm⁻³, binding energy of -16.3 MeV, incompressibility of 240 MeV, and symmetry energy of 32.5 MeV
- The LS-bulk equation of state has a saturation density of 0.155 fm⁻³, binding energy of -16 MeV, incompressibility of 220 MeV, and symmetry energy of 29.3 MeV
- The maximum gravitational mass for GM3 and LS-bulk is approximately 2.02 solar masses, while for CBF-EI it is approximately 2.34 solar masses
- The fitting formula for the interacting baryon free energy reproduces the GM3 equation of state with differences of less than 0.02 MeV for given temperature and baryon density, and less than 0.05 MeV for CBF-EI, compared to interacting baryon free energies on the order of 10 MeV
- The CBF-EI equation of state required linear extrapolation in logarithms of pressure, baryon density, and energy density for densities above 0.48 fm⁻³ to describe stars with gravitational mass above about 1.64 solar masses

## Context

This work extends the quasi-stationary proto-neutron star evolution models of Burrows and Lattimer (1986) and Pons et al. (1999), which used mean field equations of state, by incorporating a consistent many-body nuclear equation of state (CBF-EI) from Benhar and Lovato (2017). Previous gravitational wave frequency calculations by Ferrari et al. (2003, 2004) used mean field equations of state, while Burgio et al. (2011) employed a many-body equation of state but with entropy and lepton fraction profiles included artificially. The new code enables consistent computation of neutrino diffusion coefficients from the many-body equation of state and provides gravitational wave frequencies and damping times for the evolving proto-neutron star, which can be compared with future supernova observations.
