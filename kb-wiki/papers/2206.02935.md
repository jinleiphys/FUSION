# Delta baryons in neutron stars

> By applying a relativistic mean-field description of neutron star matter with density dependent couplings, we analyse the properties of two different matter compositions: nucleonic matter with delta baryons and nucleonic matter with hyperons and delta baryons. The delta-meson couplings are allowed to vary within a wide range of values obtained by experimental data, while the hyperon-meson couplings are fitted to hypernuclear properties. Neutron star properties with no deconfinement phase transition are studied. It is verified that many models are excluded because the effective nucleon mass becomes zero before the maximum mass configuration is attained. Hyperon-free with delta-dominated composition compact stars are possible, the deltic stars. It is found that with a convenient choice of parameters the existence of deltic stars with 80% of delta baryons at the center of the star is possible. However, the presence of hyperons lowers the delta baryon fraction to values below 20% at the center and below 30% at 2-3 saturation densities. It is discussed that in the presence of delta baryons, the hyperon softening is not so drastic because deltas couple more strongly to the $ω$-meson, and the stiffness of the equation of state is determined by the $ω$-dominance at high densities. The speed of sound reflects very well this behavior. The compactness of the pulsar RX J0720.4-3125 imposes $x_{σΔ}>x_{ωΔ}>1$ and favors $x_{ρΔ}>1$.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that delta baryons can constitute up to 80% of the central composition in hyperon-free neutron stars ("deltic stars"), but when hyperons are also present, the delta fraction is limited to below 20% at the center and below 30% at 2-3 times saturation density, with the condition that many parameter combinations are excluded because the effective nucleon mass becomes zero before the maximum mass configuration is reached.

## Method

The authors employed a relativistic mean-field model with density-dependent couplings (DDME2 parameterization) to describe neutron star matter in beta-equilibrium at zero temperature, considering two compositions: nucleons with delta baryons (NΔ) and nucleons with both hyperons and delta baryons (NHΔ). The delta-meson couplings (x_σΔ, x_ωΔ, x_ρΔ) were varied over a wide range informed by experimental constraints, while hyperon-meson couplings were fixed to hypernuclear binding energies and SU(6) symmetry values, and the resulting equations of state were solved with the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density for DDME2 model: n_0 = 0.152 fm^{-3}, with binding energy B/A = 16.14 MeV, incompressibility K = 251 MeV, symmetry energy E_sym = 32.3 MeV, slope L = 51 MeV, and effective nucleon mass M*/M = 0.57
- Maximum central density for NΔ composition: approximately n_B = 0.85 fm^{-3}; for NHΔ composition: approximately n_B = 1.00 fm^{-3}
- Maximum mass for pure hyperonic (NH) composition: M = 2.07 M_Sun
- NICER constraints for PSR J0030+0451: M = 1.34^{+0.15}_{-0.16} M_Sun and R = 12.71^{+1.14}_{-1.19} km (Riley 2019), and M = 1.44^{+0.15}_{-0.14} M_Sun and R = 13.02^{+1.24}_{-1.06} km (Miller 2019)
- PSR J0740+6620 constraints: M = 2.072^{+0.067}_{-0.066} M_Sun and R = 12.39^{+1.30}_{-0.98} km (Riley 2021)
- Hyperon fraction at center of maximum-mass stars: approximately 50%; delta fraction at center: below 20%
- Delta fraction at 2-3 times saturation density in hyperonic matter: below 30%

## Context

This work extends earlier studies on delta baryons in neutron stars by Drago (2014), Kolomeitsev (2016), and Ribes (2019), and addresses the problem of vanishing nucleon effective mass that was noted but not fully explored in those works. The paper systematically maps the allowed parameter space for delta-meson couplings, showing that the compactness of pulsar RX J0720.4-3125 imposes x_σΔ > x_ωΔ > 1 and favors x_ρΔ > 1. The results enable future studies of hadron-quark deconfinement phase transitions in scenarios where the effective nucleon mass would otherwise become zero, and provide constraints for gravitational wave signatures from the speed of sound behavior.
