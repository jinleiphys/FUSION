# Radiative processes on a quantum computer

> Radiative processes, where a photon/neutrino is emitted as a result of a collision or decay of a particle, play a central role in atomic, nuclear and particle physics. Their rate is determined by certain off-diagonal matrix elements with different initial and final states. We propose a method to compute them using quantum computers. It relies on a single extra qubit that, in a certain sense, represents the photon/neutrino. The generic formula relating this matrix element to the amplitude and frequency of oscillations of the extra qubit follows simply in the near resonance case. We demonstrate the feasibility of the method by using it in actual quantum computations and simulations of simple systems.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a method to compute off-diagonal transition matrix elements (which determine radiative process rates) on a quantum computer by coupling the physical system to a single extra "photon" qubit, but the method is demonstrated only for discrete energy levels in simple systems and the authors note that extension to continuum states (as in real nuclear reactions) requires future work.

## Method

The method adds a single auxiliary qubit representing a photon mode to the quantum register encoding the nuclear system, with the total Hamiltonian including a coupling term proportional to the transition operator of interest. The system is initialized in the state with the photon qubit in the excited state and the nuclear system in the initial eigenstate, then time-evolved under the full Hamiltonian. By measuring the photon qubit at various times and fitting the resulting Rabi oscillation probability to a two-level formula, the off-diagonal matrix element between initial and final nuclear states can be extracted. The authors demonstrate the method using second-order Suzuki-Trotter time evolution on a lattice discretization of a one-dimensional simple harmonic oscillator (SHO) and on a two-level system implemented on actual IBM quantum hardware.

## Key numbers

- For the SHO simulation with mass \(m = 940\) MeV, oscillator frequency \(\omega_0 = 8\) MeV, and \(\sigma = 11\) MeV, the fitted energy difference \(\Delta \bar{E}\) and Rabi frequency \(\bar{\omega}_1\) at photon frequency \(\omega = \omega_0/2\) and at resonance \(\omega = \omega_0\) agreed with the expected values \(\omega_0\) and \(\omega_1^\text{(th)}\) respectively, with finite volume and discretization errors reported as negligible.
- For the two-level system on IBMQ-Manila hardware with \(q=10\) and the same \(\omega_0 = 8\) MeV, the measured Rabi frequency was \(\omega_1^\text{(meas.)} \approx 1.03 \pm 0.03 \, \omega_1^\text{(th)}\) after averaging over maxima and minima locations.
- Lattice spacing \(a\) was varied between 0.5 fm and 2 fm with physical volume fixed at \(a(L-1) \sim 12\) fm, and Trotter time step \(\Delta t\) was varied to keep Trotterization error within 1%.

## Context

This work extends quantum computing methods for nuclear physics beyond ground-state calculations to off-diagonal transition matrix elements needed for radiative capture and decay processes. The approach differs from the method of Roggero (2018) by introducing a tunable photon frequency that can be set near resonance to enhance the signal, and from the Luscher (1990) approach used in lattice QCD for elastic scattering. The method enables calculation of electroweak transition rates on quantum computers and can be generalized to charge-exchange reactions, though the authors note that extension to continuum states (as in real processes like \(d(\gamma,n)p\)) requires future work involving the density of states.
