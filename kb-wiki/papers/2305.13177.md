# Production of bottomonia states in proton+proton and heavy-ion collisions

> In this work, we review the experimental and theoretical developments of bottomonia production in proton+proton and heavy-ion collisions. The bottomonia production process is proving to be one of the most robust processes to investigate the fundamental aspects of Quantum Chromodynamics at both low and high temperatures. The LHC experiments in the last decade have produced large statistics of bottomonia states in wide kinematic ranges in various collision systems. The bottomonia have three $Υ$ S-states which are reconstructed in dilepton invariant mass channel with high mass resolution by LHC detectors and P-states are measured via their decay to S-states. We start with the details of measurements in proton+proton collisions and their understanding in terms of various effective theoretical models. Here we cover both the Tevatron and LHC measurements with $\sqrt{s}$ spanning from 1.8 TeV to 13 TeV. The bottomonia states have particularly been very good probes to understand strongly interacting matter produced in heavy-ion collisions. The Pb+Pb collisions have been performed at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 2.76 TeV and 5.02 TeV at LHC. This led to the detailed study of the modification of bottomonia yields as a function of various observables and collision energy. At the same time, the improved results of bottomonia production became available from RHIC experiments which have proven to be useful for a quantitative comparison. A systematic study of bottomonia production in p+p, p+Pb and Pb+Pb has been very useful to understand the medium effects in these collision systems. We review some of the (if not all the) models of bottomonia evolution due to various processes in a large dynamically evolving medium and discuss these in comparison with the measurements.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that bottomonia suppression in heavy-ion collisions follows a sequential pattern based on binding energies, with the suppression increasing weakly with collision energy, but the authors note that precise measurements at high transverse momentum are still required to ascertain whether the suppression becomes constant at high $p_T$, and that the interpretation of cold-nuclear matter effects remains uncertain due to poorly constrained nuclear gluon densities.

## Method

The authors compile and compare experimental measurements of bottomonia nuclear modification factors ($R_{AA}$) and azimuthal anisotropy ($v_2$) from RHIC (STAR, PHENIX) and LHC (CMS, ATLAS, ALICE) experiments across multiple collision systems (p+p, p+Pb, Pb+Pb) and energies ($\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 200 GeV, 2.76 TeV, 5.02 TeV). They also review theoretical models including lattice QCD calculations of quarkonium spectral functions, potential model calculations using temperature-dependent heavy quark potentials, and cold-nuclear matter effects from nuclear parton distribution functions.

- $\Upsilon$(2S) $v_2$ in Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 5.02 TeV: -0.063 $\pm$ 0.085 (stat) $\pm$ 0.037 (syst) for 10-90% centrality interval measured by CMS
- Ratio $\sigma_{pp}^{1S}/\sigma_{pp}^{2S}$ = 0.26 at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 5.02 TeV from CMS
- Scaling factor $dN/d\eta|_{5.02}/dN/d\eta|_{2.76}$ = 1.22 used to convert $N_{\rm tracks}$ between LHC energies
- Dissociation temperature upper bounds from potential model: $\Upsilon$(1S) at $2T_c$, $\chi_{bJ}(1P)$ at $1.3T_c$, $\Upsilon$(2S) at $1.2T_c$, J/$\psi$ at $1.2T_c$
- String tension $\sigma$ = 0.223 GeV used in potential model calculations
- Coupling constant $\alpha$ = 0.385 chosen to match zero-temperature binding energy data

## Context

This review extends the experimental and theoretical work on bottomonia production from the Tevatron era (1.8 TeV) through LHC Runs 1 and 2 (up to 13 TeV), building on the foundational quarkonium suppression prediction by Matsui and Satz (1986). The paper compares results across RHIC (STAR at 200 GeV) and LHC (CMS, ALICE, ATLAS at 2.76 and 5.02 TeV), and discusses potential model calculations by Strickland (2011), Krouppa (2016, 2018) using lattice QCD-inspired potentials from Petreczky (2009, 2010). The work enables future studies of the temperature dependence of the quark-gluon plasma through sequential bottomonia melting patterns, though the authors note that recombination of bottom quarks is expected to be negligible at LHC temperatures due to the bottom quark mass (~4.5 GeV) being three times larger than the charm quark mass.
