# Hydrodynamic flow from RHIC to LHC

> The hydrodynamic model for the expansion of the fireball in relativistic heavy-ion collisions is presented. Calculations using relativistic hydrodynamics of a fluid with small viscosity yield a satisfactory description of the experimental data on the particle spectra, the elliptic flow or the interferometry radii.

## Key claim

The hydrodynamic model with small viscosity provides a satisfactory description of soft observables (particle spectra, elliptic flow, interferometry radii) at both RHIC and LHC energies, but the same calculations that describe the elliptic flow of identified particles cannot reproduce the values for the triangular flow.

## Method

The author uses a boost-invariant second-order viscous hydrodynamic model in 2+1 dimensions, solving the evolution of the energy-momentum tensor with shear and bulk viscosity corrections. The initial density profile in the transverse plane is taken from the Glauber model, and particle emission at the freeze-out temperature of 135 MeV is performed using the THERMINATOR event generator including non-equilibrium corrections from shear and bulk viscosity. The parameters are adjusted to RHIC data with shear viscosity to entropy ratio η/s = 0.08 and bulk viscosity to entropy ratio ζ/s = 0.04 (bulk viscosity only in the hadronic phase).

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity to entropy ratio η/s = 0.08 and bulk viscosity to entropy ratio ζ/s = 0.04 used in the model, adjusted to RHIC data
- Freeze-out temperature of 135 MeV used for particle emission
- Upper bound on shear viscosity to entropy ratio η/s < 0.2, close to estimates from strongly coupled theories
- Pb-Pb collisions at center-of-mass energy √s = 2760 GeV at the LHC
- Au-Au collisions at √s = 200 GeV at RHIC
- The elliptic flow of charged particles as function of transverse momentum is very similar at RHIC and LHC, showing saturation when √s increases from 200 to 2760 GeV

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamic modeling approach from RHIC energies (Kolb 2003, Florkowski 2010) to LHC energies, building on previous viscous hydrodynamic calculations (Romatschke 2009, Teaney 2009). The results confirm the small shear viscosity to entropy ratio (η/s < 0.2) inferred from RHIC data and show that the stronger transverse flow at LHC explains the mass splitting in elliptic flow. The failure to simultaneously describe elliptic and triangular flow of identified particles points to the need for improved treatment of initial state fluctuations and possibly earlier decoupling of multistrange baryons, enabling future work to constrain the initial conditions and viscosity parameters more precisely.
