# Delineating effects of tensor force on the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy

> In this talk, we report results of our recent studies to delineate effects of the tensor force on the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy within phenomenological models. The tensor force active in the isosinglet neutron-proton interaction channel leads to appreciable depletion/population of nucleons below/above the Fermi surface in the single-nucleon momentum distribution in cold symmetric nuclear matter (SNM). We found that as a consequence of the high momentum tail in SNM the kinetic part of the symmetry energy $E^{kin}_{sym}(ρ)$ is significantly below the well-known Fermi gas model prediction of approximately $12.5 (ρ/ρ_0)^{2/3}$. With about 15% nucleons in the high momentum tail as indicated by the recent experiments at J-Lab by the CLAS Collaboration, the $E^{kin}_{sym}(ρ)$ is negligibly small. It even becomes negative when more nucleons are in the high momentum tail in SNM. These features have recently been confirmed by three independent studies based on the state-of-the-art microscopic nuclear many-body theories. In addition, we also estimate the second-order tensor force contribution to the potential part of the symmetry energy. Implications of these findings in extracting information about nuclear symmetry energy from nuclear reactions are discussed briefly.

# Delineating effects of tensor force on the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy

## Key claim
The tensor force induced high-momentum tail in symmetric nuclear matter (SNM) reduces the kinetic part of the symmetry energy far below the standard Fermi gas prediction, making it negligibly small when approximately 15% of nucleons occupy the high-momentum tail as indicated by CLAS experiments at J-Lab, and even negative at higher percentages; however, the second-order tensor contribution to the potential part of the symmetry energy is large and can largely compensate this reduction, especially at normal density.

## Method
The authors parameterize the single-nucleon momentum distribution in SNM with a step function below the Fermi momentum and an exponential tail above, varying the percentage of nucleons in the high-momentum tail from 0% to 20%. They compute the kinetic symmetry energy from the momentum distribution integral, and evaluate the second-order tensor contribution to the potential symmetry energy using the Kuo-Brown effective tensor interaction with several short-range tensor forces (pi+rho exchange, G-matrix, M3Y, AV18). The central force contribution is assessed using the MDI (Momentum-Dependent Interaction) potential with the same modified momentum distributions.

## Key numbers
- The Fermi gas model prediction for the kinetic symmetry energy is approximately 12.5 (ρ/ρ₀)^(2/3) MeV
- CLAS Collaboration experiments indicate per-nucleon probabilities of two-nucleon short-range correlations of 15.4±3.3% for ⁴He, 19.3±4.1% for ¹²C, and 22.7±4.7% for ⁵⁶Fe
- For SNM at saturation density (k_F=1.33 fm⁻¹), the average kinetic energy with 20% high-momentum nucleons is about 40 MeV, compared to about 22 MeV for the free Fermi gas
- The tensor force contribution to the symmetry energy at normal density ranges from about 7 to 15 MeV depending on the short-range tensor interaction used
- The effective energy denominator e_eff used in the second-order tensor calculation is approximately 200 MeV around normal density

## Context
This work extends phenomenological studies of the symmetry energy by Xu, Li, and Li (2011) and addresses the long-standing uncertainty in the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy, particularly at supra-saturation densities. The finding that the kinetic symmetry energy becomes negligible or negative was qualitatively confirmed by three independent microscopic many-body calculations: the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach (Vidana, 2012), the Self-Consistent Green's Functions approach (Carbone et al., 2012), and the Fermi-Hypernetted-Chain calculations (Lovato et al., 2012). The second-order tensor contribution was also verified by Wang et al. (2012) using 2-particle-2-hole calculations. The results challenge the common practice of writing the total symmetry energy as E_sym(ρ) = 12.5 (ρ/ρ₀)^(2/3) + E^pot_sym(ρ), which neglects tensor force effects on the kinetic part, and imply that interaction parameters in mean-field models need re-adjustment if tensor contributions are included.
