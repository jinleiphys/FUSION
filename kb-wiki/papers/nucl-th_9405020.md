# Laboratory Mesurements in Nuclear Astrophysics

> After reviewing some of the basic concepts, nomenclatures and parametrizations of Astronomy, Astrophysics and Cosmology, we introduce a few central problems in Nuclear Astrophysics, including the hot-CNO cycle, helium burning in massive stars, and solar neutrino's. We demonstarte that SECONDARY (RADIOACTIVE) NUCLEAR BEAMS allow for considerable progress on these problems.

## Key claim

Secondary (radioactive) nuclear beams allow for considerable progress on central problems in nuclear astrophysics, including the hot-CNO cycle, helium burning in massive stars, and solar neutrinos, by enabling measurements in a time-reversed fashion that can act as a "narrow band width Hi Fi amplifier" for small cross sections.

## Method

The paper reviews the basic concepts, nomenclatures, and parametrizations of astronomy, astrophysics, and cosmology, then introduces the reaction theory (including the S-factor parametrization, the Gamow window, and the Breit-Wigner formula for resonant reactions) and applies it to specific burning processes: the PP chains, the CNO and hot-CNO cycles, and helium burning in massive stars. It then proposes experimental solutions using secondary (radioactive) beams, including direct and indirect measurements of the \(^{13}N(p,\gamma)^{14}O\) reaction, helium burning studied via the beta-delayed alpha-emission of \(^{16}N\), and the Coulomb dissociation of \(^{14}O\) and \(^{8}B\).

## Key numbers

- Solar mass: \(M_\odot = 1.989 \times 10^{30}\) kg; solar radius: \(R_\odot = 6.9598 \times 10^8\) meters; average solar density: \(\rho_\odot = 1.4\ \text{g/cm}^3\); central solar density: \(\rho = 158\ \text{g/cm}^3\); central solar temperature: 15.7 MK.
- The Gamow window for the \(^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O\) reaction in helium burning at 200 MK is approximately 315 keV.
- The Hubble constant is between 50 and 100 km/sec/Mpc, with a most probable value of 65, corresponding to a universe age between 10 and 20 billion years (most probable age approximately 17 BY).
- The primordial helium fraction from big bang nucleosynthesis is \(Y_p = 24\%\).
- The Chandrasekhar limit for white dwarf mass is approximately \(1.4 M_\odot\).

## Context

This lecture series extends the foundational work on thermonuclear reaction rates from the Kellogg Radiation Laboratory at Caltech, particularly the seminal papers of Fowler (1967, 1975) and the reviews by Fowler (1984) and Rolfs and Rodney (1988). It addresses unsolved problems in helium and hydrogen burning that remained despite concentrated experimental and theoretical effort, and it proposes new solutions using radioactive nuclear beams (RNB) now available at many laboratories worldwide. The work enables future tests of the standard model of particle physics via solar neutrinos and the study of the rp process in exploding stars.
