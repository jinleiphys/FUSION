# Implementation of the finite amplitude method for the relativistic quasiparticle random-phase approximation

> A new implementation of the finite amplitude method (FAM) for the solution of the relativistic quasiparticle random-phase approximation (RQRPA) is presented, based on the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) model for deformed nuclei. The numerical accuracy and stability of the FAM -- RQRPA is tested in a calculation of the monopole response of $^{22}$O. As an illustrative example, the model is applied to a study of the evolution of monopole strength in the chain of Sm isotopes, including the splitting of the giant monopole resonance in axially deformed systems.

## Key claim

The finite amplitude method (FAM) provides an efficient iterative solution to the relativistic quasiparticle random-phase approximation (RQRPA) that avoids computing and diagonalizing the full QRPA matrix, but the method requires careful numerical differentiation with a parameter eta set to 10^-6 to balance accuracy against numerical noise, and the Broyden iteration scheme needs an increased history size (M=20 versus M=7 for ground-state calculations) to achieve convergence within 40 iterations.

## Method

The authors implement the FAM for the RQRPA by starting from a stationary relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) solution for deformed nuclei, then applying a weak harmonic external field and linearizing the time-dependent RHB equations. The induced fields (changes in the single-particle Hamiltonian and pairing field) are computed by numerical differentiation of the stationary RHB functional with respect to the generalized density, using a small parameter eta, rather than by evaluating explicit second derivatives. The resulting QRPA-like equations for the forward and backward amplitudes X and Y are solved iteratively using the Broyden method, with the Dirac spinors expanded in an axially symmetric harmonic oscillator basis.

## Key numbers

- The FAM iteration convergence threshold is set to epsilon = 10^-6 for the maximal difference between collective amplitudes in successive iterations.
- The Broyden history size is M=20 for FAM calculations, compared to M=7 for ground-state RHB calculations; FAM solutions require less than 40 iterations for all examples shown.
- The numerical differentiation parameter eta = 10^-6 is used throughout the study; reducing eta below 10^-6 introduces numerical noise that deteriorates accuracy.
- The Lorentzian smearing width is Gamma = 2*gamma = 0.5 MeV for the test calculation on 22O.
- The harmonic oscillator basis uses N_max=18 oscillator shells for the upper component and N_max=19 shells for the lower component of Dirac spinors.
- For the Sm isotope chain, the low-energy (LE) peak is analyzed in the interval 10 < E < 14.5 MeV and the high-energy (HE) peak in 14.5 < E < 20 MeV; the sum of LE and HE components amounts to about 90% of the energy-weighted sum rule (EWSR) within 20 MeV, and extending the integration to 50 MeV exhausts over 99% of the EWSR.
- For the constrained deformation study of 144Sm, the energy intervals for prolate configurations are 10-14.5 MeV (LE) and 14.5-20 MeV (HE); for oblate configurations the intervals are 10-15.5 MeV (LE) and 15.5-22.5 MeV (HE).

## Context

This work extends the finite amplitude method, previously applied to non-relativistic Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov frameworks by Avogadro and Nakatsukasa (2011) and by Stoitsov et al. (2011), to the relativistic RQRPA based on the RHB model for deformed nuclei. The standard matrix RQRPA for spherical nuclei was formulated by Paar et al. (2003) and extended to deformed axially symmetric systems by Pena Arteaga et al. (2009), but the computational cost of the matrix approach has prevented systematic studies of multipole response in heavy deformed nuclei. The present FAM implementation enables efficient calculation of the monopole response across the chain of Sm isotopes (132-160), including the splitting of the giant monopole resonance in deformed systems, and can be applied to systematic studies of collective excitations in medium-heavy and heavy deformed nuclei that were previously computationally prohibitive.
