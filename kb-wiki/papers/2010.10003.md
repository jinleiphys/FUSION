# Signatures of $α$ clustering in $^{16}$O by using a multiphase transport model

> $α$-clustered structures in light nuclei could be studied through "snapshots" taken by relativistic heavy-ion collisions. A multiphase transport (AMPT) model is employed to simulate the initial structure of collision nuclei and the proceeding collisions at center of mass energy $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 6.37 TeV. This initial structure can finally be reflected in the subsequent observations, such as elliptic flow ($v_{2}$), triangular flow ($v_{3}$) and quadrangular flow ($v_4$). Three sets of the collision systems are chosen to illustrate system scan is a good way to identify the exotic $α$-clustered nuclear structure, case I: $\mathrm{^{16}O}$ nucleus (with or without $α$-cluster) + ordinary nuclei (always in Woods-Saxon distribution) in most central collisions, case II: $\mathrm{^{16}O}$ nucleus (with or without $α$-cluster) + $\mathrm{^{197}Au}$ nucleus collisions for centrality dependence, and case III: symmetric collision systems (namely, $^{10}$B + $^{10}$B, $^{12}$C + $^{12}$C, $^{16}$O + $^{16}$O (with or without $α$-cluster), $^{20}$Ne + $^{20}$Ne, and $^{40}$Ca + $^{40}$Ca) in most central collisions. Our calculations propose that relativistic heavy-ion collision experiments at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 6.37 TeV are promised to distinguish the tetrahedron structure of $\mathrm{^{16}O}$ from the Woods-Saxon one and shed lights on the system scan projects in experiments.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the ratio of triangular flow to elliptic flow ($v_3/v_2$) as a function of participant number can distinguish the tetrahedron alpha-clustered structure of $^{16}$O from the conventional Woods-Saxon distribution in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, but only for specific collision systems (most central collisions of $^{16}$O with ordinary nuclei or symmetric systems) where the geometry of the overlap region does not dominate.

## Method

The authors used the AMPT (A Multi-Phase Transport) model to simulate collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 6.37 TeV, embedding either a tetrahedron 4-alpha cluster configuration for $^{16}$O (with side length 3.42 fm, giving RMS radius 2.699 fm) or a Woods-Saxon distribution. They calculated eccentricity coefficients ($\varepsilon_2$, $\varepsilon_3$, $\varepsilon_4$, $\varepsilon_4^L$) from initial partons and anisotropic flow coefficients ($v_2$, $v_3$, $v_4$, $v_4^L$) from final-state charged hadrons ($\pi^\pm$, $K^\pm$, p, $\bar{p}$) with rapidity cut $-0.5 < y < 0.5$ and transverse momentum cut $0.2 < p_T < 3$ GeV/$c$, using two-particle correlations with a pseudorapidity gap to suppress non-flow effects.

## Key numbers

- Tetrahedron structure for $^{16}$O uses side length of 3.42 fm, giving RMS radius 2.699 fm (compared to Woods-Saxon RMS 2.726 fm and experimental value 2.6991 fm)
- Center-of-mass energy: $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 6.37 TeV for all collisions
- Transverse momentum cut for flow analysis: $0.2 < p_T < 3$ GeV/$c$
- Rapidity cut for flow analysis: $-0.5 < y < 0.5$
- For case II (O + Au), centralities used: 40-50%, 30-40%, 20-30%, 10-20%, 0-10%

## Context

This work extends the proposal by Broniowski (2014) that nuclear structure phenomena can be studied through relativistic heavy-ion collisions, and builds on prior AMPT model studies of system scans at RHIC and LHC energies (e.g., PhysRevC.99.044904, PhysRevC.101.021901). The tetrahedron configuration parameters are inherited from an Extended Quantum Molecular Dynamics (EQMD) model study (PhysRevLett.113.032506). The results enable experimental system scan projects at RHIC or LHC to identify alpha clustering in $^{16}$O, and the insensitivity of $v_n/\varepsilon_n$ ratios to geometric configuration suggests a possible extraction of shear viscosity over entropy density ($\eta/s$) independent of initial structure.
