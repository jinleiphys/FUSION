# Scaling of transverse energies and multiplicities with atomic number and energy in ultrarelativistic nuclear collisions

> We compute how the initial energy density and produced gluon, quark and antiquark numbers scale with atomic number and beam energy in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions. The computation is based on the argument that the effect of all momentum scales can be estimated by performing the computation at one transverse momentum scale, the saturation momentum. The initial numbers are converted to final ones by assuming kinetic thermalisation and adiabatic expansion. The main emphasis of the study is at LHC and RHIC energies but it is observed that even at SPS energies this approach leads to results which are not unreasonable: what is usually described as a completely soft nonperturbative process can also be described in terms of gluons and quarks. The key element is the use of the saturation scale.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the initial energy density and multiplicities in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions can be estimated by performing perturbative QCD computations at a single transverse momentum scale, the saturation momentum \(p_{\rm sat}\), which effectively represents the contribution of all momentum scales both above and below it. The authors hedge that this approach gives "not unreasonable" results even at SPS energies where soft nonperturbative physics normally dominates, but they acknowledge that perturbation theory at the SPS saturation scale of 0.73 GeV is not expected to be very accurate.

## Method
The authors compute the average number of produced gluons, quarks, and antiquarks with \(p_T > p_0\) in central \(A+A\) collisions using leading-order pQCD with a constant \(K\)-factor of 2, the EKS98 nuclear shadowing parametrization, and parton distribution functions from the literature. The saturation scale \(p_{\rm sat}\) is determined by solving the geometric criterion \(N(p_0) = p_0^2 R_A^2\), where \(N(p_0)\) is the total number of produced quanta and \(R_A = 1.12 A^{1/3}\) fm is the nuclear radius. Initial values of transverse energy \(E_{Ti}\) and number \(N_i\) are evaluated at \(p_0 = p_{\rm sat}\), then converted to final observables by assuming kinetic thermalisation of the gluonic component and adiabatic boost-invariant expansion.

## Key numbers
- Saturation scale for Pb+Pb (\(A=208\)): \(p_{\rm sat} = 0.73\) GeV at SPS (\(\sqrt{s}=17\) GeV), 1.13 GeV at RHIC (\(\sqrt{s}=200\) GeV), and 2.13 GeV at LHC (\(\sqrt{s}=5500\) GeV).
- Initial number of produced quanta for \(A=208\): \(N_i = 598\) at SPS, 1440 at RHIC, and 5140 at LHC.
- Initial transverse energy for \(A=208\): \(E_{Ti} = 600\) GeV at SPS, 2360 GeV at RHIC, and 17000 GeV at LHC.
- Initial energy density for \(A=208\): \(\epsilon_i = 16.1\) GeV/fm\(^3\) at SPS, 98.2 GeV/fm\(^3\) at RHIC, and 1330 GeV/fm\(^3\) at LHC.
- Initial temperature for \(A=208\): \(T_i = 0.39\) GeV at SPS, 0.62 GeV at RHIC, and 1.19 GeV at LHC.
- Scaling fit for saturation momentum: \(p_{\rm sat} = 0.208\ {\rm GeV}\ A^{0.128} (\sqrt{s})^{0.191}\).
- Scaling fit for initial number: \(N_i = 1.383\ A^{0.922} (\sqrt{s})^{0.383}\).
- Scaling fit for initial transverse energy: \(E_{Ti} = 0.386\ {\rm GeV}\ A^{1.043} (\sqrt{s})^{0.595}\).
- Predicted charged multiplicity per unit rapidity for \(A=208\): \(N_{\rm ch} \approx 330\)–340 at SPS (\(\sqrt{s}=17\) GeV), 870 at RHIC (\(\sqrt{s}=200\) GeV), and 3300 at LHC (\(\sqrt{s}=5500\) GeV).
- Predicted final transverse energy per unit rapidity for \(A=208\): approximately 400 GeV at SPS (compared to observed 400 GeV), 660 GeV at RHIC, and 2900 GeV at LHC.

## Context
This work extends the saturation approach of Blaizot and Mueller (1987) and Eskola, Kajantie, Ruuskanen, and Tuominen (1998) by computing explicit scaling laws for both initial and final observables across a wide range of energies and atomic numbers. It enables predictions for RHIC and LHC experiments by providing quantitative estimates of charged multiplicities and transverse energies based on a single-scale saturation picture, while also showing that even SPS data can be roughly described by this hard perturbative method. The authors note that their LHC predictions are about 25% larger than those in Eskola et al. (1998) due to compensating effects from shadowing, a slightly larger \(p_{\rm sat}\), and a \(K\)-factor of 2 instead of 1.
