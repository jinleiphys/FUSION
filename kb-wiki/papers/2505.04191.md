# Heisenberg Uncertainty Inequality and Breaking of Isospin Symmetry in Atomic Nuclei

> The Heisenberg uncertainty inequality is used to derive a rigorous lower bound to the amount of isospin impurities in $N=Z$ atomic nuclei, caused by the violation of isospin symmetry. The bound is fixed by the difference between the neutron and proton radii and the sum of the charge exchange monopole strengths. It can be used to check the consistency of advanced many-body calculations accounting for the breaking of isospin symmetry. The uncertainty inequality is also employed to derive an upper bound to the isovector dipole moment in terms of the amount of isospin impurities, providing an insightful connection between the violation of parity and isospin symmetries.

# Heisenberg Uncertainty Inequality and Breaking of Isospin Symmetry in Atomic Nuclei

## Key claim

The Heisenberg uncertainty inequality provides a rigorous lower bound on isospin impurities in $N=Z$ nuclei, but this bound is only as strong as the choice of the auxiliary operator $B$; the monopole choice emphasizes Coulomb-driven mixing, while other choices (e.g., dipole) yield different bounds connecting isospin violation to parity violation.

## Method

The author applies a generalized Heisenberg uncertainty inequality for non-Hermitian operators (Eq. PS) to the operators $A = T_-$ (the isospin lowering operator) and $B = M_- = \sum_j r_j^2 t_{-,j}$ (the isovector monopole operator). The fluctuation $\langle \{T_+, T_-\} \rangle$ gives the total isospin content $\langle \mathbf{T}^2 - T_z^2 \rangle$, while the commutator $[A^\dagger, B]$ reduces to $N\langle r_n^2 \rangle - Z\langle r_p^2 \rangle$, the difference between neutron and proton mean-square radii. The fluctuation of $B$ is identified with the sum of charge-exchange monopole strengths $\sigma^M_+ + \sigma^M_-$. For $N=Z$ nuclei, the inequality yields a lower bound on $\langle \mathbf{T}^2 \rangle$; a second inequality using the dipole operator $D_-$ yields an upper bound on the isovector dipole moment $d_z$ in terms of isospin impurities.

## Key numbers

- The lower bound on isospin impurities in $N=Z$ nuclei: $\langle \mathbf{T}^2 \rangle \ge \frac{N^2(\langle r_n^2 \rangle - \langle r_p^2 \rangle)^2}{8\sigma^M_z}$, where $\sigma^M_z$ is the static structure factor of the isovector monopole operator $M_z = \sum_j r_j^2 t_{z,j}$.
- For $N \neq Z$ nuclei, the inequality becomes $N\langle r_n^2 \rangle - Z\langle r_p^2 \rangle \le \sqrt{(N-Z)(\sigma^M_+ + \sigma^M_-)}$.
- The dipole inequality for $N=Z$ nuclei: $d_z^2 \le 8 \langle \mathbf{T}^2 \rangle \sigma_z^D$, where $d_z = N(\langle z_n \rangle - \langle z_p \rangle)$ is the isovector dipole moment and $\sigma_z^D$ is the dipole strength.
- In a simplified Coulomb-only model with a single $T=1$ monopole state at energy $E_M - E_0$, the inequality reduces to an identity: $\langle \mathbf{T}^2 \rangle = 2\alpha^2 \sigma^M_z / (E_M - E_0)^2$ and $N(\langle r_n^2 \rangle - \langle r_p^2 \rangle) = 4\alpha \sigma^M_z / (E_M - E_0)$, with $\alpha = Ze^2/2R^3$.

## Context

This work extends the generalized Heisenberg inequality formalism previously applied by Lev Pitaevskii and Stringari (1991, 1993) to systems with spontaneously broken continuous symmetries, now applied to isospin symmetry broken explicitly by the Coulomb force and other interactions. It provides model-independent consistency checks for advanced many-body calculations, such as those by Donnelly (2008), Viviani (2005), and Loc (2019), and for mean-field approaches reviewed by Sheikh (2021). The results connect to recent studies of superallowed beta decay near the $N=Z$ drip line by Kaneko (2017), Petrovici (2018), Choudhary (2025), and Algora (2025), where proton-neutron pairing correlations contribute to isospin breaking. The dipole inequality links isospin violation to parity violation, relevant to electric dipole moment studies by Pospelov (2005), van Kolck (2011), and Navratil (2021). Future work will investigate isospin impurities near the $N=Z$ drip line with improved choices for the auxiliary operator $B$ and the role of thermal fluctuations.
