# Sensitivity studies for r-process nucleosynthesis in three astrophysical scenarios

> In rapid neutron capture, or r-process, nucleosynthesis, heavy elements are built up via a sequence of neutron captures and beta decays that involves thousands of nuclei far from stability. Though we understand the basics of how the r-process proceeds, its astrophysical site is still not conclusively known. The nuclear network simulations we use to test potential astrophysical scenarios require nuclear physics data (masses, beta decay lifetimes, neutron capture rates, fission probabilities) for all of the nuclei on the neutron-rich side of the nuclear chart, from the valley of stability to the neutron drip line. Here we discuss recent sensitivity studies that aim to determine which individual pieces of nuclear data are the most crucial for r-process calculations. We consider three types of astrophysical scenarios: a traditional hot r-process, a cold r-process in which the temperature and density drop rapidly, and a neutron star merger trajectory.

## Key claim

The sensitivity of r-process abundance patterns to individual nuclear masses, beta decay rates, and neutron capture rates depends strongly on the astrophysical scenario (hot r-process, cold r-process, or neutron star merger), but the most important nuclei across all scenarios are those near closed shells and in the rare earth region, 10-20 neutrons from stability.

## Method

The authors performed sensitivity studies using an r-process nuclear network code, taking nuclear masses from Moller et al. (1995), beta decay rates from Moller et al. (2003), and neutron capture rates from Rauscher and Thielemann (2000) where experimental data were unavailable. For each of three astrophysical trajectories (a parameterized hot wind, a cold neutrino-driven wind, and a neutron star merger), they varied individual binding energies by ±1 MeV, beta decay rates by a factor of 10, and neutron capture rates by a factor of 100, then compared final abundance patterns to the baseline using a sensitivity measure F.

## Key numbers

- Binding energies were varied by ±1 MeV in the mass sensitivity studies, as in Surman et al. (2013).
- Beta decay rates were varied by a factor of 10 in the beta decay sensitivity studies, as in Cass et al. (2012).
- Neutron capture rates were varied by a factor of 100 in the neutron capture sensitivity studies, as in Surman et al. (2009) and Mumpower et al. (2012).
- The hot r-process baseline used entropy s/k = 50, dynamical timescale τ_dyn = 80 ms, and initial electron fraction Y_e = 0.250, from a parameterized wind trajectory of Meyer (2002).
- The cold r-process baseline used a trajectory from Arcones et al. (2007) with electron fraction artificially reduced to Y_e = 0.31.
- The sensitivity measure F is defined as F = 100 × [Σ_A |A Y_baseline(A) - A Y_increase(A)| + Σ_A |A Y_baseline(A) - A Y_decrease(A)|] / 2.

## Context

This work extends prior sensitivity studies for neutron capture rates (Beun et al. 2008; Surman et al. 2009, 2011; Mumpower et al. 2012), nuclear masses (Brett et al. 2012; Surman et al. 2013), and beta decay rates (Cass et al. 2012; Surman et al. 2013) by comparing results across three distinct astrophysical scenarios: a classic hot r-process, a cold neutrino-driven wind r-process, and a neutron star merger r-process. The findings enable experimental campaigns at next-generation radioactive beam facilities to prioritize measurements of nuclei near closed shells and in the rare earth region, which are consistently the most influential across all scenarios.
