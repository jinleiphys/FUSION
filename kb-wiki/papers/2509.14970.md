# Study of Heavy Quarkonia Melting in QGP Using Deep Neural Networks

> Machine learning techniques have emerged as powerful tools for tackling non-perturbative challenges in quantum chromodynamics. In this study, we introduce a data-driven framework employing deep neural networks to systematically predict the temperature-dependent behavior of the screening mass $m_D(T)$ and the strong coupling constant $α_s(T)$ within a quark-gluon plasma medium. These medium-sensitive quantities are subsequently employed to compute the thermal widths $Γ_{\text{n}}(T)$ and binding energies $E_B(T)$ of heavy quarkonia states, specifically charmonia and bottomonia, by numerically solving the Schrödinger equation with medium-modified heavy quark potentials. To estimate the dissociation temperatures $T_d$ of various quarkonia states, we employ two complementary dissociation criteria: the conventional one, where $2E_B(T_d) = Γ_{\text{n}}(T_d)$, and an additional lower bound criterion defined by $E_B(T) = 3T$. This dual-criterion approach provides a more constrained and physically motivated estimate of the temperature range over which quarkonia states dissolve in the QGP environment. Our machine learning-enhanced predictions show excellent agreement with available lattice QCD results, especially for the ground states $Υ(1S)$ and $J/ψ$, and offer new perspectives on the sequential suppression pattern detected in relativistic heavy-ion collision experiments. Overall, this work advances the quantitative description of quarkonium suppression and demonstrates the prospect of modern machine learning methods to bridge theoretical predictions and experimental observations, thereby contributing significantly to QGP tomography.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a deep neural network trained on lattice QCD data can predict the temperature-dependent Debye screening mass and strong coupling constant, which when used in a medium-modified potential model yields dissociation temperatures for heavy quarkonia that agree with lattice QCD results and experimental suppression patterns; however, the framework combines perturbative-inspired analytic expressions with ML-optimized inputs, making it a hybrid rather than a fully non-perturbative approach.

## Method

The authors train a deep neural network (eight-layer ResNet with 32 neurons per layer and swish activation) on 50 lattice QCD data points from HotQCD covering T/Tc in [1, 3] to extract quasi-parton masses, from which the strong coupling constant αs(T) and Debye mass mD(T) are computed using leading-order hard thermal loop formulas. These quantities are then used to construct a complex in-medium heavy quark potential, whose real part is inserted into the radial Schrodinger equation (solved via finite difference method with 4000 grid points up to 30 fm) to obtain binding energies EB(T) and wavefunctions, while the thermal width Γn(T) is computed as the expectation value of the imaginary potential over the wavefunction. Dissociation temperatures are determined using two criteria: the conventional condition 2EB(Td) = Γn(Td) and a lower bound criterion EB(T) = 3T.

## Key numbers

- The training data covers temperature range T/Tc in [1, 3] with critical temperature Tc = 0.15 GeV, using 50 numerical data points from HotQCD calculations
- The radial Schrodinger equation is solved on N = 4000 grid points extending to rmax = 30 fm
- For Υ(1S), the dissociation temperature range is Td in [1.38, 1.99] Tc from the two criteria (EB = 3T gives 1.38 Tc; 2EB = Γ gives 1.99 Tc)
- For Υ(2S), the dissociation temperature range is Td in [1.10, 1.29] Tc (EB = 3T gives 1.10 Tc; 2EB = Γ gives 1.29 Tc)
- For J/ψ, the dissociation temperature range is Td in [1.13, 1.30] Tc (EB = 3T gives 1.13 Tc; 2EB = Γ gives 1.30 Tc)
- For ψ(2S), both criteria give Td ≤ 1.00 Tc
- The total training error after 50,000 epochs converges to approximately 10^{-5}

## Context

This work extends the potential model approach to quarkonium dissociation by Mocsy and Petreczky (2007) and Digal et al. (2001), who used similar dissociation criteria but without machine learning inputs for the screening mass. The ML-enhanced framework bridges lattice QCD constraints from HotQCD (2014) with continuum phenomenology, providing a data-driven determination of mD(T) and αs(T) that improves upon earlier perturbative calculations by Jamal et al. (2018) and Agotiya et al. (2016). The results enable more quantitative predictions for sequential quarkonium suppression patterns observed at RHIC and LHC, and can be used to refine QGP tomography by connecting theoretical predictions to experimental measurements of nuclear modification factors RAA and elliptic flow v2.
