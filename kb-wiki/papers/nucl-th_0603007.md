# Scaling and Interference in the Dissociation of Halo Nuclei

> The dissociation of halo nuclei through their collision with light and heavy targets is considered within the Continuum Discretized Coupled Channels theory. We study the one-proton halo nucleus $^8$B and the one-neutron halo nucleus $^{11}$Be, as well as the more normal $^7$Be. The procedure previously employed to extract the Coulomb dissociation cross section by subtracting the nuclear one is critically assessed, and the scaling law usually assumed for the target mass dependence of the nuclear breakup cross section is also tested. It is found that the nuclear breakup cross section for these very loosely bound nuclei does indeed behave as $a+bA^{1/3}$. However, it does not have the geometrically inspired form of a circular ring which seems to be the case for normal nuclei such as $^{7}$Be. We find further that we cannot ignore Coulomb-nuclear interference effects, which may be constructive or destructive in nature, and so the errors in previously extracted B(E1) using the subtraction procedure are almost certainly underestimated.

# Scaling and Interference in the Dissociation of Halo Nuclei

## Key claim
The nuclear breakup cross section for halo nuclei does scale as \(a + b A_T^{1/3}\) with target mass number, but it does not follow the geometrically inspired circular-ring form that holds for normal nuclei such as \(^7\)Be; furthermore, Coulomb-nuclear interference effects cannot be ignored, meaning that previously extracted \(B(E1)\) values using the subtraction procedure have almost certainly underestimated errors.

## Method
The authors performed full Continuum Discretized Coupled Channels (CDCC) calculations for the elastic breakup of \(^8\)B, \(^{11}\)Be, and \(^7\)Be projectiles incident on light, medium, and heavy targets at laboratory energies where data exist. Projectile states were included up to partial waves \(\ell_{\rm max}=3\) and relative energy \(\epsilon_{\rm max}=10\) MeV, with the continuum discretized into momentum bins; coupled-channel equations were solved out to \(R_{\rm max}=500\) fm. Separate "Coulomb only" and "nuclear only" calculations were also performed to isolate the interference term \(\sigma_I = \sigma_{CN} - \sigma_C - \sigma_N\).

## Key numbers
- Nuclear breakup cross sections for \(^8\)B and \(^{11}\)Be at all studied energies (44, 70, and 200 MeV/nucleon) follow a linear dependence on \(A_T^{1/3}\), but with negative intercept \(P_1\) in most cases, unlike the positive intercept expected from the geometrical ring formula.
- For \(^{11}\)Be + \(^{208}\)Pb at 200 MeV/nucleon, the scaling factor \(L\) extracted from CDCC is 5.9, close to the eikonal value of about 5.5 and consistent with the experimental value \(5.6 \pm 0.4\) from Refs. [1,2].
- For \(^{11}\)Be + \(^{208}\)Pb at 70 MeV/nucleon, the CDCC value of \(L\) is 3.82, significantly larger than the experimental value \(2.1 \pm 0.5\) from Ref. [3].
- For \(^8\)B on \(^{208}\)Pb at 44 MeV/nucleon with \(b_{\rm min}=20\) fm, the ratio \(\sigma_C / \hat{\sigma}_C\) approaches unity, but for \(^{11}\)Be on the same target the ratio deviates substantially from unity even at \(b_{\rm min} \gtrsim 30\) fm.
- For \(^7\)Be at 100 MeV/nucleon, the scaling law of Eq. (a+a) is fully satisfied with both \(P_1\) and \(P_2\) positive.

## Context
This work critically assesses the standard subtraction procedure used to extract Coulomb dissociation cross sections from inclusive breakup measurements, a method previously employed by Nakada (1999), Datta (2003), and others to obtain \(B(E1)\) distributions for halo nuclei. The authors directly test the scaling law proposed by Acquadro (1980) and challenge the claims of Dasso (1998, 1999) and Nagarajan (2001) that nuclear contributions could be much larger than Coulomb contributions. The results show that CDCC calculations are necessary to obtain credible \(B(E1)\) values, and that the interference term \(\sigma_I\) can be either constructive or destructive depending on the projectile, invalidating the simple incoherent subtraction used in prior analyses. This enables future work to properly account for Coulomb-nuclear interference when extracting electromagnetic response functions from dissociation data.
