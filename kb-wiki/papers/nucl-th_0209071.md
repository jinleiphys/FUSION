# Cumulative structure function in terms of nucleonic wave function of the nucleus

> The structure function of the nucleus in the cumulative region $x>1$ is studied in terms of nucleon degrees of freedom. At high $Q^2$ the resulting expressions are presented as a sum of contributions from few-nucleon correlations. Two-nucleon correlations are studied in some detail. Spin variables are averaged out. In the region $1<x<2$ the structure functions are calculated for the relativistic interaction proposed by F.Gross {\it et al}. They are found to fall with $x$ faster than the exponential. For Carbon at $x=1.05$, where the method is not rigorously applicable, they turn out to be rougly twice larger than the experimental data.

## Key claim

The cumulative nuclear structure function for $x>1$ can be described purely in terms of nucleonic degrees of freedom using relativistic internucleon potentials, without invoking quark degrees of freedom. However, at $x=1.05$ for Carbon, where the method is not rigorously applicable, the calculated values are roughly twice larger than the experimental data, indicating the approach may only be reliable at higher $x$ values.

## Method

The authors used the impulse approximation at high $Q^2$ to express the nuclear structure function as a convolution of the nucleon structure function with a relativistic nucleon distribution in the nucleus. They separated contributions from few-nucleon correlations, focusing on two-nucleon correlations in the region $1<x<2$, and calculated the high-momentum asymptotics using the relativistic Bethe-Salpeter potential from Gross, Van Orden, and Holinde (1992), represented by a sum of $\pi$, $\eta$, $\sigma$, $\omega$, and $\rho$ exchanges with form factors. The nuclear factor $w_2$ was computed from the standard Woods-Saxon nuclear density, and the GRV LO parametrization from Glueck, Reya, and Vogt (1995) was used for the nucleon structure function.

## Key numbers

- For Carbon at $x=1.05$ and $Q^2=61$ (GeV/c)$^2$, the calculated structure function values range from 126 to 162 (x 10$^6$) across four potential models, compared to the experimental value of 64.1 $\pm$ 7.4 (x 10$^6$)
- For Carbon at $x=1.15$ and $Q^2=61$ (GeV/c)$^2$, calculated values range from 30.9 to 40.7 (x 10$^6$), while the experimental upper limit is $<$27.0 (x 10$^6$)
- For Carbon at $x=1.30$ and $Q^2=61$ (GeV/c)$^2$, calculated values range from 2.71 to 4.15 (x 10$^6$), with experimental upper limit $<$6.9 (x 10$^6$)
- The cumulative structure function falls by a factor of about 4 as $Q^2$ rises from 3 to 1000 (GeV/c)$^2$ at moderate $x$
- The slope of $F^{(A)}(x)$ changes from 10 to 40 as $x$ rises from 1.2 to 1.7
- The nuclear factor $(A-1)w_2/m^2$ shows an $A$-dependence where the exponent $\alpha$ goes down from 0.3 to 0.15 as $A$ rises from 20 to 200

## Context

This work extends the approach advocated by Frankfurt and Strikman (1981) that cumulative phenomena could be described in terms of nucleons with appropriately relativized wave functions, and contrasts with the QCD-based approach of Braun and Vechernin (1994) which used quark degrees of freedom with an infrared cutoff parameter. The method enables testing of relativistic internucleon potentials from Gross, Van Orden, and Holinde (1992) against cumulative structure function data, which provides a more stringent test than low-energy observables. The authors note that experimental data are scarce, limited to deuteron data from Shuetz et al. (1977) and carbon data from the BCDMS Collaboration, Benvenuti et al. (1994), at relatively low $x<1.5$.
