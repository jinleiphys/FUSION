# Small radii of neutron stars as an indication of novel in-medium effects

> At present, neutron star radii from both observations and model predictions remain very uncertain. Whereas different models can predict a wide range of neutron star radii, it is not possible for most models to predict radii that are smaller than about 10 km, thus if such small radii are established in the future they will be very difficult to reconcile with model estimates. By invoking a new term in the equation of state that enhances the energy density, but leaves the pressure unchanged we simulate the current uncertainty in the neutron star radii. This new term can be possibly due to the exchange of the weakly interacting light U-boson with appropriate in-medium parameters, which does not compromise the success of the conventional nuclear models. The validity of this new scheme will be tested eventually by more precise measurements of neutron star radii.

## Key claim

If future observations firmly establish neutron star radii smaller than about 10 km, such small radii would be very difficult to reconcile with most current nuclear equation-of-state models; the authors propose that adding a term to the energy density that is linear in baryon density while leaving the pressure unchanged can reduce neutron star radii by about 3 km without violating the maximum mass constraint, and this term could be realized by a weakly interacting light U-boson with appropriate in-medium parameters.

## Method

The authors add an amending term \(C_L \rho_B\) to the energy density of several relativistic mean-field (RMF) models (SLC, SLCd, SL3, NL3040) while keeping the pressure unchanged, then solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to compute neutron star mass-radius trajectories. They interpret the amending term as arising from a density-dependent vector coupling constant, which they relate to the exchange of a light U-boson with an in-medium mass that depends on baryon density.

## Key numbers

- The amending coefficient \(C_L\) is varied up to 300 MeV, causing a reduction of about 3 km in the neutron star radius.
- With \(C_L = 300\) MeV, the mass-radius trajectories for the SLC and SLCd models fall into the regime extracted by Guillot et al. (2013) of \(R_{\rm NS} = 9.4 \pm 1.2\) km.
- Softening the symmetry energy alone (reducing the slope parameter \(L\) from 97.1 MeV to 45 MeV) reduces the canonical neutron star radius by at most 1.5 km.
- The slope parameter \(L\) for the SLC and SL3 models is 92.3 and 97.1 MeV respectively; for SLCd and NL3040 it is 61.5 and 45.0 MeV respectively.
- For a U-boson coupling constant \(g_u = 0.01\) and bare mass \(m_{u0} = 0.1\) MeV, the in-medium U-boson mass is within a few MeV; for \(C_L = 300\) MeV the mass is close to 1 MeV.

## Context

This work addresses the tension between neutron star radius extractions as low as \(9.4 \pm 1.2\) km from quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries (Guillot et al., 2014) and the inability of standard nuclear models to produce radii below about 10-11 km while still supporting a \(2 M_\odot\) maximum mass (Demorest et al., 2010; Antoniadis et al., 2013). The proposed mechanism differs from typical phase transitions that reduce maximum mass but keep radii roughly constant, and from the inversion approach of Chen and Piekarewicz (2015). The U-boson interpretation connects to earlier work on fifth-force mediators (Fayet, 1980, 1986) and previous studies of U-boson effects in neutron stars (Krivoruchenko et al., 2009; Wen et al., 2009; Zhang et al., 2011). The scheme will be tested by more precise neutron star radius measurements.
