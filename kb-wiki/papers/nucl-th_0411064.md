# The nuclear pairing problem: new perspectives

> The overview of the Exact Pairing technique based on the quasispin symmetry is presented. Extensions of this method are discussed in relation to mean field, quadrupole collectivity, electromagnetic transitions, and many-body level density. Realistic calculations compared with experimental data are used to support the methods as well as to emphasize the manifestations of pairing correlations in nuclear many-body systems.

# The nuclear pairing problem: new perspectives

## Key claim
The Exact Pairing (EP) technique based on quasispin symmetry provides a numerically simple and exact solution to the nuclear pairing problem that eliminates the conceptual drawbacks of the BCS approximation, particularly for finite mesoscopic systems where particle number violation and the sharp phase transition to a normal phase are problematic. However, the EP method alone is insufficient for describing excited states with unpaired particles, such as the first 2⁺ state, where residual interactions beyond pairing become crucial.

## Method
The authors solve the pairing problem exactly using the quasispin SU(2) algebra, where the operators for pair creation, pair annihilation, and level occupancy on each j-level form a partial quasispin vector. The partial seniority sⱼ (number of unpaired particles on each level) is preserved by the pairing interaction, defining classes of states closed under pairing regardless of the matrix elements Gⱼⱼ'. The Hamiltonian matrix within each seniority class is sparse and of modest dimension, allowing fast numerical diagonalization. For the full many-body problem, the EP solution is used as a starting point, with monopole interactions added to account for smooth mean-field changes, and iterative Hartree-Fock cycles built on the EP occupation numbers to incorporate deformation effects.

## Key numbers
- For ¹¹⁴Sn with 14 neutrons above ¹⁰⁰Sn in the space of g₇/₂, d₅/₂, d₃/₂, s₁/₂, and h₁₁/₂ neutron orbitals: typical differences between BCS and exact occupation numbers are on the level of 10%; pair transfer amplitudes differ on the level of 20%, with the BCS result around their mean value.
- For ⁴⁸Ca, where the 0f₇/₂ orbital is completely filled, the BCS approximation gives a trivial solution while the exact solution predicts a correlation energy close to 2 MeV; for ⁵²Ca, where the 1p₃/₂ orbital is filled, the exact solution again differs from BCS by almost 2 MeV.
- For tin isotopes, the EP + monopole calculation reproduces the total ground state energy within 0.4 MeV error, but the calculated E(2⁺) is almost a factor of 2 too high and B(E2;2⁺₁→0⁺₁) is grossly underestimated compared to experiment.
- For the density of states in ¹¹⁶Sn, the EP+monopole calculation is significantly lower than the experimental data; the amplitude of seniority-induced oscillations in the theoretical curve must be scaled down by a factor of 1/2.5 to match the observed peak scale.

## Context
This work extends the exact pairing solution based on quasispin symmetry, originally developed by Racah (1943) and later by Richardson (1963), to realistic nuclear structure calculations beyond the degenerate limit. It directly addresses the shortcomings of the BCS approach as applied to finite nuclei, which were first pointed out by Belyaev (1959). The EP method enables calculations for heavy nuclei where full shell model diagonalization is not feasible, and provides a foundation for incorporating quadrupole collectivity and other residual interactions. The study of level density peaks connected to seniority classes builds on shell model simulations by Dean (2002) and experimental techniques by Melby (1999), and opens the way to understanding the gradual quenching of pairing correlations in finite systems rather than a sharp phase transition.
