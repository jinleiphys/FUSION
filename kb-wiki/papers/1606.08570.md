# Pseudospin symmetry in nuclear structure and its supersymmetric representation

> The quasi-degeneracy between the single-particle states $(n,\,l,\,j=l+1/2)$ and $(n-1,\,l+2,\,j=l+3/2)$ indicates a special and hidden symmetry in atomic nuclei---the so-called pseudospin symmetry (PSS)---which is an important concept in both spherical and deformed nuclei. A number of phenomena in nuclear structure have been successfully interpreted directly or implicitly by this symmetry, including nuclear superdeformed configurations, identical bands, quantized alignment, pseudospin partner bands, and so on. Since the PSS was recognized as a relativistic symmetry in 1990s, there have been comprehensive efforts to understand its properties in various systems and potentials. In this Review, we mainly focus on the latest progress on the supersymmetric (SUSY) representation of PSS, and one of the key targets is to understand its symmetry-breaking mechanism in realistic nuclei in a quantitative and perturbative way. The SUSY quantum mechanics and its applications to the SU(2) and U(3) symmetries of the Dirac Hamiltonian are discussed in detail. It is shown that the origin of PSS and its symmetry-breaking mechanism, which are deeply hidden in the origin Hamiltonian, can be traced by its SUSY partner Hamiltonian. Essential open questions, such as the SUSY representation of PSS in the deformed system, are pointed out.

## Key claim

This review argues that the origin of pseudospin symmetry (PSS) and its symmetry-breaking mechanism in realistic nuclei can be traced quantitatively and perturbatively using the supersymmetric (SUSY) partner Hamiltonian, even though the symmetry-breaking in the original Dirac Hamiltonian is not perturbative in the standard sense.

## Method

The paper reviews the application of supersymmetric quantum mechanics to the Dirac Hamiltonian for spherical nuclei, using intertwining relations to construct SUSY partner Hamiltonians that map pseudospin doublets. It then discusses how combining SUSY quantum mechanics with the similarity renormalization group (SRG) technique yields a Hermitian effective Hamiltonian that enables perturbative calculations of PSS breaking, bridging the gap between earlier SUSY descriptions and quantitative perturbation theory.

## Key numbers

* The pseudospin symmetry limit is given by \(d\Sigma(r)/dr = 0\), where \(\Sigma(r) \equiv S(r) + V(r)\) is the sum of scalar and vector potentials; this condition is more general than \(\Sigma(r) = 0\).
* The spin-symmetry limit (for antiparticle states) is given by \(d\Delta(r)/dr = 0\), where \(\Delta(r) \equiv V(r) - S(r)\).
* The ratio \(v_{ls}/v_{ll} = 4\) between spin-orbit and orbit-orbit interaction strengths was identified by Bohr, Hamamoto, and Mottelson (1982) as the condition for pseudospin symmetry in the Nilsson Hamiltonian.
* Traditional nuclear magic numbers are 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82 for both protons and neutrons, and 126 for neutrons in stable nuclei.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on pseudospin symmetry that began with Hecht and Adler (1969) and Arima, Harvey, and Shimizu (1969), who first identified the near degeneracy between \((n, l, j=l+1/2)\) and \((n-1, l+2, j=l+3/2)\) states. It builds on Ginocchio's (1997) recognition of PSS as a relativistic symmetry of the Dirac Hamiltonian and on Leviatan's (2004) application of SUSY quantum mechanics to the Dirac equation. The review focuses on the most recent progress using SUSY representations combined with the similarity renormalization group technique (Guo and coauthors, 2012-2014) to make PSS breaking quantitatively tractable, while noting that open questions remain, particularly the SUSY representation of PSS in deformed systems.
