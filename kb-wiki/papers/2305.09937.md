# Machine learning study to identify collective flow in small and large colliding systems

> Collective flow has been found to be similar between small colliding systems ($p$ $+$ $p$ and $p$ $+$ A collisions) and large colliding systems (peripheral A $+$ A collisions) at the CERN Large Hadron Collider. In order to study the differences of collective flow between small and large colliding systems, we employ a point cloud network to identify $p$ $+$ Pb collisions and peripheral Pb $+$ Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} =$ 5.02 TeV generated from a multiphase transport model (AMPT). After removing the discrepancies in the pseudorapidity distribution and the $p_{\rm T}$ spectra, we capture the discrepancy in collective flow. Although the verification accuracy of our PCN is limited due to similar event-by-event distributions of elliptic and triangular flow, we demonstrate that collective flow between $p$ $+$ Pb collisions and peripheral Pb $+$ Pb collisions becomes more distinct with increasing final hadron multiplicity and parton scattering cross section. This study not only highlights the potential of PCN techniques in advancing the understanding of collective flow in varying colliding systems, but more importantly lays the groundwork for the future PCN-related research.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a point cloud network (PCN) can distinguish between p+Pb and peripheral Pb+Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV generated by the AMPT model, but only after removing differences in pseudorapidity distributions and pT spectra; the verification accuracy is limited because the event-by-event distributions of elliptic and triangular flow are similar between the two systems.

## Method

The authors simulated 2 million events each for peripheral Pb+Pb and p+Pb minimum bias collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.02 TeV using the string-melting version of the AMPT model. They trained a point cloud network on final-state hadron information within |eta| < 2.4, using either four-momentum (px, py, pz, E) or two-momentum (px, py) inputs, with additional preprocessing including normalization by transverse momentum and random rotation to isolate collective flow effects. The events were grouped into charged-particle multiplicity bins (90-120, 120-150, 150-185, 185-230) to compare systems at similar volumes, and the network was trained with parton scattering cross sections of 0 mb, 3 mb, and 10 mb.

## Key numbers

- Test accuracy exceeds 99% when training with four-momentum (px, py, pz, E) of final particles across all pseudorapidity ranges studied
- Test accuracy drops by more than 10% after normalizing two-momentum inputs by pT, indicating pT distribution is the main distinguishing feature
- Test accuracy with two-momentum input is over 60% and drops less than 2% after random rotation
- For 0 mb parton cross section at low Nch, the PCN cannot distinguish the two colliding systems (accuracy near 50%)
- Test accuracy increases with both charged-particle multiplicity Nch and parton scattering cross section (0 mb, 3 mb, 10 mb)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of collective flow in small colliding systems (p+p and p+A) versus large colliding systems (peripheral A+A) at the LHC, where similar flow patterns have been observed. The authors build on prior AMPT model studies by Lin (2014), Orjuela Koop (2015), and Ma (2016) that showed a 3 mb parton scattering cross section describes both large and small systems, and on the work of Wang (2022) demonstrating that collectivity from parton scatterings is stronger in large systems. The study enables future PCN-based analyses of experimental data to determine whether small colliding systems produce quark-gluon plasma, and could be applied to classify the origin of atmospheric particle showers in cosmic ray physics at the Telescope Array Project and Pierre Auger Observatory.
