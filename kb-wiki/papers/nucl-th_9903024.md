# Method of integral transforms for few-body reactions

> The method of integral transforms is reviewed. In the framework of this method reaction observables are obtained with the bound--state calculation techniques. New developments are reported.

## Key claim

The method of integral transforms allows reaction observables to be obtained using bound-state calculation techniques, avoiding explicit treatment of continuum wave functions; however, the inversion of the transform from the calculated quantities to the physical response requires regularization and is stable only when the transform kernel is chosen appropriately, with complex transform variables substantially improving accuracy over real ones.

## Method

The method expresses reaction observables in terms of "E-overlaps" r(E) which are integrals over continuum states of products of wave function overlaps with a delta function in energy. Instead of computing r(E) directly, an integral transform Φ(σ) = ⟨χ₂|K(σ, Ĥ)χ₁⟩ is evaluated by solving a Schrödinger-like equation with a source term using bound-state techniques. The physical r(E) is then obtained by numerically inverting the integral equation, typically by expanding r(E) in a basis and fitting the transform of that expansion to the computed Φ(σ) values.

## Key numbers

- For few-nucleon systems, σ_I values between several MeV and 20 MeV were found most convenient for the Lorentz kernel inversion.
- For the three-cluster ¹¹Li response, convenient σ_I values proved comparable to the width of the peak.
- The Lorentz kernel K(σ,ε) = [(ε+σ*)(ε+σ)]⁻¹ with complex σ = -σ_R + iσ_I is used; as σ_I → 0, K(σ,ε) → (π/|σ_I|)δ(σ_R-ε) and Φ(σ) → (π/|σ_I|)R(σ_R).

## Context

This work reviews and extends the integral transform method first proposed by Efros (1980) for comparing Stieltjes transforms of response functions to experiment, and later formulated generally by Efros (1985). The key improvement of using complex transform variables was introduced by Efros, Leidemann, and Orlandini (1994), which substantially improved inversion accuracy. Successful applications to three- and four-nucleon (e,e') and total photodisintegration cross sections were given by Martinelli et al. (1995), Efros, Leidemann, and Orlandini (1997a, 1997b, 1997c, 1999), and Efros, Leidemann, Orlandini, and Tommasi (1999), while the four-nucleon spectral function was obtained by Efros, Leidemann, and Orlandini (1998). The method enables calculation of inclusive and exclusive reaction observables without specifying reaction channels when solving the dynamic equations, and automatically accounts for long-range Coulomb final-state interactions in perturbation-induced reactions.
