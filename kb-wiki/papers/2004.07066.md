# Towards a reliable lower bound on the location of the critical endpoint

> We perform the first direct determination of the position of the leading singularity of the pressure in the complex chemical potential $μ_B$ plane in lattice QCD using numerical simulations with 2-stout improved rooted staggered fermions. This provides a direct determination of the radius of convergence of the Taylor expansion of the pressure that does not rely on a finite-order truncation of the expansion. The analyticity issues in the complex $μ_B$ plane of the grand canonical partition function of QCD with rooted staggered fermions are solved with a careful redefinition of the fermion determinant that makes it a polynomial in the fugacity on any finite lattice, without changing the continuum limit of the observables. By performing a finite volume scaling study at a single coarse lattice spacing, we show that the limiting singularity is not on the real line in the thermodynamic limit, thus showing that the radius of convergence of the Taylor expansion gives a lower bound on the location of a possible phase transition. In the vicinity of the crossover temperature at zero chemical potential, the radius of convergence turns out to be $μ_B/T \approx 2$ and roughly temperature independent.

## Key claim
The radius of convergence of the Taylor expansion of the pressure gives a rigorous lower bound on the location of a possible critical endpoint, but the leading singularity in the thermodynamic limit is not on the real axis, meaning the radius of convergence does not correspond to a genuine phase transition. At the crossover temperature near zero chemical potential, the radius of convergence is approximately μ_B/T ≈ 2 and roughly temperature independent.

## Method
The authors performed lattice QCD simulations with 2-stout improved rooted staggered fermions on N_t=4 lattices with spatial volumes of 6³, 8³, 10³, and 12³. They introduced a new definition of the two-flavor fermion determinant that avoids square roots by grouping closely lying eigenvalues of the reduced matrix into pairs and substituting each pair with an average eigenvalue, making the partition function a polynomial in the fugacity on any finite lattice. They then determined the position of the leading Lee-Yang zero (the singularity closest to μ=0) and performed a finite volume scaling study using a linear extrapolation in 1/V.

## Key numbers
- The radius of convergence at the crossover temperature is μ_B/T ≈ 2, roughly temperature independent.
- Simulations used N_t=4 lattices with spatial volumes of 6³, 8³, 10³, and 12³.
- The infinite volume extrapolation of the imaginary part of the leading Lee-Yang zero was performed using a linear fit in 1/V for volumes 8³, 10³, and 12³ at β=β_c=3.35.
- The crossover temperature at μ_B=0 is T_c ≈ 150-160 MeV (cited from Borsanyi (2010)).

## Context
This work extends the study of the QCD phase diagram by providing a direct method to determine the radius of convergence of the Taylor expansion of the pressure, circumventing the problems of finite-order truncation that affect previous estimates from Taylor coefficients (Bazavov (2017), D'Elia (2016), Fodor (2019)). The method solves analyticity issues with rooted staggered fermions that produce spurious branch point singularities at finite lattice spacing, as discussed by Hasenfratz (1991). The result that the leading singularity is complex rather than on the real axis shows that the radius of convergence provides only a lower bound on the critical endpoint location, not the endpoint itself. This enables future work on finer lattices to determine the continuum limit of the radius of convergence directly, rather than through individual Taylor coefficients.
