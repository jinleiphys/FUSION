# Hadrons in Dense Matter and Medium Effects in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> Theoretical and experimental studies of hot and/or dense matter, such as is created in high-energy heavy-ion collisions, and encountered in compact objects in astrophysics, constitute one of the most active frontiers in nuclear physics. In these Lectures, we discuss various approaches to the description of hot and/or dense matter, including the simple Skyrme-type parameterization and relativistic Walecka-type models, as well as microscopic Dirac-Brueckner and QCD sum rule approaches. As density and/or temperature of the hadronic system increases, chiral symmetry is gradually restored, as indicated by the decrease of quark condensate. This has profound effects on the properties of hadrons, especially their masses. We review various theoretical predictions for hadron properties in dense matter. Experimentally, possible medium modifications of hadron properties can be studied through the measurements of particle spectra, flow, and particularly, electromagnetic observables. Particle production, especially the production of rare particles such as kaons, vector mesons, and antiparticles, provides useful insight into heavy-ion collision dynamics, and hadron properties in dense matter. Collective flows of various kinds are important observables in heavy-ion collisions. They probe essentially the entire reaction process, and thus are very useful for the determination of the reaction dynamics. They also reflect the properties of hadrons in dense matter. Electromagnetic signals are considered penetrating probes that may carry undistorted information about the early stage of high-energy heavy-ion collisions. We discuss various theoretical calculations of dilepton and photon production in heavy-ion collisions at SPS energies and various medium effects that have been proposed to explain the observed low-mass dilepton enhancement.

## Key claim

This lecture series reviews theoretical predictions that as density and/or temperature of a hadronic system increases, chiral symmetry is gradually restored (indicated by a decrease of the quark condensate), which has profound effects on hadron properties, especially their masses; the author emphasizes that the question of how this decrease manifests in experimental observables is still under intense debate.

## Method

The paper reviews multiple theoretical approaches to hot and/or dense matter: simple Skyrme-type parameterization, relativistic Walecka-type models, microscopic Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) calculations, and QCD sum rule approaches. It then discusses how these frameworks predict in-medium hadron properties and surveys experimental observables, particle spectra and yields, collective flows (in-plane, out-of-plane, radial), and electromagnetic signals (dileptons and photons), that can probe medium effects in heavy-ion collisions from SIS to SPS energies (1–200 AGeV).

## Key numbers

- Quark condensate in vacuum: \(\langle\bar qq\rangle_0 \approx -(245\ \text{MeV})^3\), estimated from the Gell-Mann–Oakes–Renner relation using \(m_\pi\approx 138\ \text{MeV}\), \(f_\pi\approx 93\ \text{MeV}\), and \(m_q\approx 5.5\ \text{MeV}\).
- At normal nuclear matter density \(\rho_0\approx 0.16\ \text{fm}^{-3}\), the quark condensate decreases by about 1/3 in leading order, using \(\Sigma_{\pi N}\approx 45\ \text{MeV}\).
- The deconfinement phase transition to quark-gluon plasma is predicted by lattice QCD to occur at a critical energy density \(\varepsilon \approx 1-3\ \text{GeV/fm}^3\).
- Heavy-ion collision energies surveyed range from 1 AGeV (BEVALAC, SIS) to 10 AGeV (AGS) and 200 AGeV (SPS).

## Context

This review extends earlier work on chiral symmetry restoration and in-medium hadron properties, referencing lattice QCD simulations (Bernard 1992, Karsch 1995, Laermann 1996) and chiral perturbation theory (Gerber and Leutwyler 1989). It builds on transport models based on Walecka-type \(\sigma\)-\(\omega\) models (Ko 1987, Ko 1988) and discusses the observed low-mass dilepton enhancement by the CERES and HELIOS-3 collaborations, as well as single photon spectra from the WA80 collaboration. The review enables future work connecting theoretical predictions of in-medium hadron masses to experimental observables in heavy-ion collisions across a range of bombarding energies.
