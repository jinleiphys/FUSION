# Broyden's Method in Nuclear Structure Calculations

> Broyden's method, widely used in quantum chemistry electronic-structure calculations for the numerical solution of nonlinear equations in many variables, is applied in the context of the nuclear many-body problem. Examples include the unitary gas problem, the nuclear density functional theory with Skyrme functionals, and the nuclear coupled-cluster theory. The stability of the method, its ease of use, and its rapid convergence rates make Broyden's method a tool of choice for large-scale nuclear structure calculations.

## Key claim

Broyden's method, a quasi-Newton algorithm for solving large sets of nonlinear equations, is applied to nuclear structure calculations and achieves convergence in 3 to 10 times fewer iterations than linear-mixing schemes, provided that all parameters adjusted during the iteration (including chemical potentials and Lipkin-Nogami parameters) are included in the Broyden vector and that the system's functions are smooth (e.g., by introducing a small finite temperature when discontinuities arise).

## Method

The authors implement both the standard Broyden method (for smaller systems, storing the full approximate inverse Jacobian) and the modified Broyden method of Johnson (for large systems, storing only M previous iteration vectors and an M×M matrix). In the nuclear density functional theory examples, the Broyden vector contains the self-consistent mean-field potentials (particle-hole and pairing fields) evaluated on a Gauss-Hermite quadrature grid; in the coupled-cluster example, it contains the cluster amplitudes. The method is tested against simple linear mixing (where input and output are mixed with a fixed parameter α) for three distinct nuclear many-body problems.

## Key numbers

* For the unitary Fermi gas in a spherical trap with 60 particles (N=162 Broyden variables), Broyden's method converges in approximately 10–15 iterations while linear mixing requires about 40–50 iterations (Figure 1, top panel).
* For the superdeformed band in ¹⁵¹Tb at ħω_y=0.5 MeV using HFODD with N_osc=15, Broyden's method (α=0.7, M=7) reaches a tolerance of 10⁻⁶ after ~30 iterations, while linear mixing (α=0.5) requires ~130 iterations (Figure 2).
* For the spherical ground state of ²⁰⁸Pb with SkP functional and N_osc=12, Broyden's method converges in ~15 iterations versus ~60 for linear mixing (Figure 3).
* For a spherical HO basis with N_osc=12, the Broyden vector size is N=773,344 (≈6.2 MB); with M=8 retained vectors this requires ~100 MB memory overhead. For N_osc=20, the vector size grows to N=3,259,872 (≈416 MB additional memory).
* In the HFODD implementation, the Broyden vector contains 44 self-consistent field components on the Gauss-Hermite grid: real scalar fields (U_t, M_t), real vector fields (Σ_t, C_t, I_t), a real tensor field (B↔_t), and complex scalar pairing fields (Re[ħ_t], Im[ħ_t]).

## Context

This work extends the use of Broyden's method, already standard in quantum chemistry electronic-structure calculations (as implemented in the PWscf package under GNU General Public License), to three areas of nuclear structure: the unitary Fermi gas problem, Skyrme density functional theory (using the HFODD code of Dobaczewski et al.), and coupled-cluster theory. The paper is part of the Universal Nuclear Energy Density Functional (UNEDF) project and enables large-scale self-consistent calculations across the nuclear chart, including symmetry-unconstrained cranked HFB calculations for high-spin states and fission pathways, where linear mixing converges prohibitively slowly. The modified Broyden method of Johnson (2001) and Vanderbilt and Louie (1984) is adapted to handle up to ~10⁸ unknowns in coupled-cluster calculations.
