# Dispersion and suppression of sound near QCD critical point

> We have used second order relativistic hydrodynamics equipped with equation of state which includes the critical point to study the propagation of perturbation in a relativistic QCD fluid. Dispersion relation for the sound wave has been derived to ascertain the fate of the perturbation in the fluid near the QCD critical end point (CEP). We observe that the threshold value of the wavelength of the sound in the fluid diverges at the CEP, implying that all the modes of the perturbations are dissipated at this point. Some consequences of the suppression of sound near the critical point have been discussed.

## Key claim
The threshold wavelength for sound propagation diverges at the QCD critical end point (CEP), meaning all modes of perturbation are dissipated there. This result depends on using second-order relativistic hydrodynamics with an equation of state that includes the CEP, and the divergence follows from the speed of sound approaching zero near the critical point.

## Method
The authors constructed an equation of state by mapping the QCD phase transition onto the 3D Ising model universality class, using a linear mapping from Ising parameters (r, H) to (μ, T) with the CEP at (154 MeV, 367 MeV). They derived dispersion relations for sound waves in a relativistic viscous fluid using Israel-Stewart second-order hydrodynamics, including shear viscosity and thermal conductivity, and solved for the threshold wavevector k_th where the imaginary part of the frequency equals the real part.

## Key numbers
- CEP location: (T_c, μ_c) = (154 MeV, 367 MeV)
- Critical region parameters: ΔT_c = 0.1 GeV, Δμ_c = 0.2 GeV, B = 2
- Threshold wavevector in Navier-Stokes limit: k_th = (3/2) c_s (h/s) / (η/s)
- The threshold wavelength λ_th = 2π/k_th diverges at the CEP because c_s → 0

## Context
This work extends the study of critical phenomena in QCD matter using hydrodynamic approaches, building on the mapping of the QCD phase transition to the 3D Ising model as done by Asakawa (2009) and others. It uses the Israel-Stewart (1976) second-order hydrodynamics to avoid causality violations of Navier-Stokes theory, and applies the fluidity criterion developed by Liao (2010) to quantify sound suppression. The results enable experimental searches for the CEP through beam energy scan programs at RHIC, FAIR, and NICA by predicting that sound modes with wavelengths below a diverging threshold will be completely dissipated near the critical point.
