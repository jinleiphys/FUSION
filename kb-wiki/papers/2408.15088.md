# Why do newer degrees of freedom appear in higher-order truncated hydrodynamic theory?

> An exact derivation of relativistic hydrodynamics from an underlying microscopic theory has been shown to be an all-order theory. From the relativistic transport equation of kinetic theory, the full expressions of hydrodynamic viscous fluxes have been derived which turn out to include all orders of out-of-equilibrium derivative corrections. It has been shown, that for maintaining causality, it is imperative that the temporal derivatives must include all orders, which can be resummed in non-local, relaxation operator-like forms and finally `integrated in' introducing newer degrees of freedom. The theory can of course be truncated at any higher spatial orders, but the power over the the infinite temporal sum increases correspondingly such that the causality is respected. As a result, the theory truncated at any higher order of spatial gradient, requires newer degrees of freedom for each increasing order.

## Key claim

The paper argues that in any causal, relativistic dissipative hydrodynamic theory derived from kinetic theory, the temporal derivatives must be kept to all orders (infinite sum) while spatial gradients can be truncated at finite order; the requirement to preserve causality forces the introduction of new "non-fluid" degrees of freedom for each increasing order of spatial gradient truncation.

## Method

Starting from the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation with a linearized relaxation time approximation (RTA) collision kernel, the author derives the exact (all-order) expression for the shear viscous tensor \(\pi^{\mu\nu}\) in a conformal, chargeless system. The derivation involves expanding the denominator operator \((1+\tau_R D + \frac{\tau_R}{\tilde{E}_p}\tilde{p}^{\langle\lambda\rangle}\nabla_{\lambda})\) as an infinite derivative sum series within the Knudsen number limit \(K_n<1\), performing moment integrals using standard formulas for massless particles, and then recasting the resulting nonlocal temporal operators into a local set of equations by "integrating in" new degrees of freedom.

## Key numbers

* The shear viscosity coefficient under RTA is \(\eta = (4/5)P\tau_R\), where \(P\) is pressure and \(\tau_R\) is the relaxation time.
* The moment integral formula for a massless system gives coefficients \(a_{nl} = (-1)^l \frac{T^2}{2\pi^2}\frac{n!(n+1)!}{(n-2l)!(2l+1)!}\).
* The first-order (MIS) theory corresponds to keeping spatial gradients up to \(m=0\) (no \(n\) contribution), yielding \((1+\tau_R D)\pi^{\alpha\beta}=2\eta\sigma^{\alpha\beta}\).
* The third-order theory corresponds to keeping spatial gradients up to \(m=0,1\) and \(n=0\), requiring an additional degree of freedom beyond \(\pi^{\mu\nu}\) to maintain causality.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Mitra (2023) which argued that MIS theory includes all orders of derivative corrections and that all-order resummation is equivalent to introducing new degrees of freedom. It directly addresses the causality issues raised by Brito (2021) for third-order theories, where the shear viscous tensor alone was insufficient and additional nonequilibrium variables were required. The paper contrasts this approach with the Bemfica-Disconzi-Noronha-Kovtun (BDNK) first-order causal-stable theory (Bemfica 2017, Kovtun 2019), which uses only primary fluid variables (\(T, u^\mu\)) but with out-of-equilibrium definitions. The results enable a systematic understanding of why higher-order hydrodynamic truncations inevitably require more degrees of freedom, and provide a framework for constructing causal higher-order theories from kinetic theory.
