# Spinodal density enhancements in simulations of relativistic nuclear collisions

> We recently introduced a fluid-dynamical model for simulating relativistic nuclear collisions in the presence of a first-order phase transition and made explorative studies of head-on lead-lead collisions. We give here a more detailed account of this novel theoretical tool and carry out more exhaustive studies of the phase-separation dynamics. Extracting the density enhancement caused by the spinodal instabilities, the associated clump size distribution, and the resulting transverse flow velocity, we examine the sensitivity of these quantities to the strength of the gradient term that promotes the phase separation, to the details of the initial density fluctuations that form the seeds for the subsequent amplification, and to the equation of state.

## Key claim

The spinodal instabilities associated with a first-order phase transition can produce significant density enhancements in relativistic nuclear collisions, but the magnitude of this effect depends sensitively on the collision energy, the strength of the gradient term (which governs the interface tension), and the initial density fluctuations; the optimal energy range for observing these effects is approximately \(E_{\rm lab}\approx 2-4\,A\,{\rm GeV}\) for the specific equation of state employed.

## Method

The authors performed three-dimensional ideal fluid-dynamical simulations of head-on lead-lead collisions using the SHASTA code, with initial conditions generated by the UrQMD cascade model. They employed a two-phase equation of state constructed by splining between a hadronic gas and a quark-gluon plasma free energy density, incorporating a gradient term in the local pressure to emulate finite-range effects essential for spinodal decomposition. For comparison, they also performed simulations with a one-phase Maxwell-constructed equation of state that removes the instabilities but is otherwise identical.

## Key numbers

- The gradient term range parameter was set to \(a = 0.033\,{\rm fm}\), yielding a zero-temperature interface tension of \(\sigma_0 \approx 10\,{\rm MeV}/{\rm fm}^2\).
- The nuclear saturation density used was \(\rho_s = 0.153/{\rm fm}^3\).
- The optimal collision energy range for maximum density clumping was found to be \(E_{\rm lab} \approx 2-4\,A\,{\rm GeV}\).
- For central lead-lead collisions at \(3\,A\,{\rm GeV}\), the difference between the two-phase and one-phase size distributions peaked at clumps containing 5-8 baryons, using a density cutoff of \(\rho_{\rm min} = 7\rho_s\).
- The normalized density moment for \(N=7\) showed enhancement by well over an order of magnitude with the two-phase equation of state compared to negligible enhancement with the one-phase partner.
- The critical temperature for the phase transition was \(T_{\rm crit}\) with the critical point at \((\rho_{\rm crit}, T_{\rm crit})\), and the cross-over temperature at vanishing baryon chemical potential was \(T_\times \approx 150-160\,{\rm MeV}\) (from lattice QCD, cited from Borsanyi (2010) and Bazavov (2010)).

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier fluid-dynamical model introduced in Steinheimer (2012) for simulating relativistic nuclear collisions with a first-order phase transition, providing more detailed studies of phase-separation dynamics. The approach builds on prior theoretical work on spinodal instabilities in nuclear matter by Randrup (2003, 2009, 2010) and the Physics Reports review, and is motivated by experimental searches for the QCD critical point at RHIC (RHIC-BES), CBM (CBM-book), and NICA (NICA). The results enable future studies of whether the predicted density clumps can survive through hadronic rescattering and be observable in two-particle correlation functions, as suggested by Chen (2011).
