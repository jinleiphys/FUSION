# Hadronic structure from double parton scattering

> In the present paper we consider the so-called effective cross section, a quantity which encodes the experimental knowledge on double parton scattering in hadronic collisions that has been accomulated so far. We show that the effective cross section, under some assumptions close to those adopted in its experimental extractions, can be used to obtain a range of mean transverse distance between an interacting parton pair in double parton scattering. Therefore we have proved that the effective cross section offers a way to access information on the hadronic structure.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the experimentally extracted effective cross section in double parton scattering can be used to derive a range of mean transverse distances between the two interacting partons, under the same factorized assumptions used in experimental extractions of sigma_eff.

## Method

The authors start from the definition of the effective cross section sigma_eff in terms of the transverse form factor f(k_perp) and use general properties of this form factor derived from the hadronic wave function. They derive inequalities relating the integral defining sigma_eff to the mean squared transverse distance between partons, using the Bessel expansion of the form factor and the fact that d_2(k_perp) has a maximum at k_perp=0. The lower bound is obtained from the maximum property of d_2(k_perp), while the upper bound requires additional assumptions about the functional form of f(k_perp).

## Key numbers

- Lower bound: mean squared transverse distance ⟨b²⟩ ≥ σ_eff/(3π)
- The effective cross section sigma_eff is defined through the relation σ_eff⁻¹ = ∫ d²k_perp/(2π)² f(k_perp)²
- The form factor satisfies f(k_perp=0)=1 and f(k_perp→∞)=0

## Context

This work extends the historical approach of extracting the proton radius from electromagnetic form factors in elastic processes to the case of partonic distances in double parton scattering. The authors build on the formalism of double parton distribution functions (dPDFs) developed by Paver (1982) and others, and on model calculations of dPDFs by Mel_19, noiold, noi1, noij2, and noir. The result enables access to hadronic structure information from existing experimental sigma_eff data without requiring detailed knowledge of the transverse dependence of dPDFs.
