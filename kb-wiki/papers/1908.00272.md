# How accurately we know the standard $^{252}$Cf(sf) neutron multiplicity?

> Small uncertainties obtained for the Neutron Standards have been associated with possible missing correlations in the input data, with an incomplete uncertainty budget of the employed experimental database or with unrecognized uncertainty sources common to many measurements. While further detailed studies may improve the first two issues, the issue of potential unrecognized uncertainties and correlations between different experiments has long been neglected. We address this gap with a test-case study ons the evaluation of the total neutron multiplicity of the $^{252}$Cf(sf) source, which is included in the evaluation of the Thermal Neutron Constants within the Neutron Standards.

## Key claim

The standard evaluated uncertainty of 0.13% for the total neutron multiplicity of the $^{252}$Cf(sf) source is likely too small because it assumes all fifteen input measurements are independent, whereas the observed spread in measured values suggests a larger unknown systematic uncertainty (USU) of 0.6% that introduces inter-experiment correlations.

## Method

The authors re-examined the fifteen experimental data points used in the Axton (1986) evaluation of the $^{252}$Cf(sf) total neutron multiplicity, which employed scintillator detectors, Mn-bath measurements, and Boron-pile measurements. They compared the weighted generalized least-square (GLSQ) fit result (assuming independent measurements) to an un-weighted mean and standard deviation of the same data, and applied the unrecognized systematic uncertainty (USU) estimation method previously used in Neutron Standards evaluations to quantify the minimum achievable uncertainty for a given experimental technique.

## Key numbers

- The standard evaluated value of $\overline{\nu}_{tot}$ for $^{252}$Cf(sf) is $3.764 \pm 0.005$ (0.13%), derived from a GLSQ fit assuming all fifteen experiments are independent.
- The un-weighted mean of the fifteen measured values is $3.765 \pm 0.023$ (0.6%), which better reflects the observed spread in the data.
- The GLSQ uncertainty of 0.005 is approximately $0.023 / \sqrt{15}$, showing the strong reduction from the independence assumption.
- The USU estimated in the Neutron Standards (Carlson 2018) was 0.4% after discarding two outlier values; using all fifteen data points yields a larger USU of 0.6%.

## Context

This work addresses a long-neglected issue in the Neutron Data Standards evaluations (Carlson 2009, Carlson 2018): the possibility that unrecognized systematic uncertainties and correlations between different experiments produce evaluated uncertainties that are too small. The test case of $^{252}$Cf(sf) neutron multiplicity extends earlier work by Badikov (1992, 2003) and Gai (2007a) on unrecognized uncertainty estimation, and questions the key assumption of Axton (1986) and Divadeenam and Stehn (1984) that all multiplicity measurements are basically independent. The results suggest that an updated Neutron Standard evaluation may be required, and that new experimental methods are needed to reduce unknown systematic uncertainties.
