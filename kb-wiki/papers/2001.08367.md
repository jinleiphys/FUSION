# An approach to constrain models of accreting neutron stars with the use of an equation of state

> We investigate X-ray bursts during the thermal evolution of an accreting neutron star which corresponds to the X-ray burster GS\ 1826-24. Physical quantities of the neutron star are included using an equation of state below and above the nuclear matter density. We adopt an equation of state and construct an approximate network that saves the computational time and calculates nuclear energy generation rates accompanying the abundance evolutions. The mass and radius of the neutron star are got by solving the stellar evolution equations from the center to the surface which involve necessary information such as the nuclear energy generation in accreting layers, heating from the crust, and neutrino emissions inside the stellar core. We reproduce the light curve and recurrence time of the X-ray burst from GS 1826-24 within the standard deviation of 1$σ$ for the assumed accretion rate, metallicity, and equation of state. It is concluded that the observed recurrence time is consistent with the theoretical model having metallicity of the initial CNO elements $Z_{\rm CNO}$ = 0.01. We suggest that the nuclear reaction rates responsible for the $rp$-process should be examined in detail, because the rates may change the shape of the light curve and our conclusion.

## Key claim

The observed recurrence time and light curve of the X-ray burster GS 1826-24 can be reproduced within 1σ standard deviation by a theoretical model that includes a full general relativistic stellar evolution code with an equation of state, but only for a specific metallicity of initial CNO elements (Z_CNO = 0.01) and a specific neutron star mass (1.58 M⊙); the authors caution that uncertain nuclear reaction rates in the rp-process could change the light curve shape and alter this conclusion.

## Method

The authors solved the full general relativistic stellar structure and evolution equations from the center to the surface of an accreting neutron star, using the Lattimer-Swesty equation of state with incompressibilities K = 180 or 220 MeV for the inner layers (ρ ≥ 10^12.8 g cm^−3) and connecting to outer-layer equations of state. They constructed an approximate nuclear reaction network (APRX3) containing 88 nuclides to follow the rp-process and nuclear energy generation, and compared the resulting theoretical light curves and recurrence times to RXTE observations of GS 1826-24 using a χ² statistical method with the distance and anisotropy factor as free parameters.

## Key numbers

- Best-fit model (L2n20Z1): neutron star mass M = 1.58 M⊙, radius R = 12.6 km, accretion rate Ṁ_−9 = 2.0 (i.e., 2.0 × 10^−9 M⊙ yr^−1), metallicity Z_CNO = 0.01.
- Recurrence time for best-fit model: Δt = 3.56 ± 0.07 hr, compared to observed Δt = 3.536 ± 0.04 hr (Galloway 2017).
- Peak luminosity for best-fit model: L_pk = 1.16 ± 0.08 × 10^38 erg s^−1.
- Reduced χ² minimum for best-fit model: χ²_ν,min = 1.48 (2007 data).
- Gravitational redshift for best-fit model: z_g = 0.26.
- Ratio of burst anisotropy to persistent anisotropy for best-fit model: ξ_b/ξ_p = 1.01, implying inclination angle θ ≈ 58.6°.
- For the softer EoS (K = 180 MeV) with M = 1.57 M⊙, R = 11.9 km, and Ṁ_−9 = 3.0, the peak luminosity was lower (0.95 × 10^38 erg s^−1) and recurrence time shorter (2.08 hr) at Z_CNO = 0.01.
- The approximate network APRX3 (88 nuclides) ran 6.3 times faster than the full network of 897 nuclides.

## Context

This work extends earlier modeling of GS 1826-24 by Heger et al. (2007) and Lampe et al. (2016), who used large nuclear networks but did not include an equation of state at high density above nuclear matter or solve the full stellar structure from center to surface. The authors also build on the approximate network approach of Wallace and Woosley (1981) and Hanawa et al. (1983). The results enable future constraints on the equation of state beyond nuclear saturation density by comparing theoretical and observed light curves, and highlight the need for improved nuclear reaction rate measurements for the rp-process, as noted by Meisel et al. (2018, 2019).
