# Neutron Density Distributions of Neutron-Rich Nuclei Studied with the Isobaric Yield Ratio Difference

> The isobaric yield ratio difference (IBD) between two reactions of similar experimental setups is found to be sensitive to nuclear density differences between projectiles. In this article, the IBD probe is used to study the density variation in neutron-rich $^{48}$Ca. By adjusting diffuseness in the neutron density distribution, three different neutron density distributions of $^{48}$Ca are obtained. The yields of fragments in the 80$A$ MeV $^{40, 48}$Ca + $^{12}$C reactions are calculated by using a modified statistical abrasion-ablation model. It is found that the IBD results obtained from the prefragments are sensitive to the density distribution of the projectile, while the IBD results from the final fragments are less sensitive to the density distribution of the projectile.

## Key claim

The isobaric yield ratio difference (IBD) probe is sensitive to neutron density variations in the projectile nucleus, but this sensitivity is much stronger for prefragments (before evaporation) than for final fragments (after evaporation), making it unclear whether the probe can reliably detect density changes in neutron-rich nuclei using experimentally measurable final fragments.

## Method

The authors used a modified statistical abrasion-ablation (SAA) model to calculate fragment yields in 80\(A\) MeV \(^{40,48}\)Ca + \(^{12}\)C reactions. Three different neutron density distributions for \(^{48}\)Ca were generated by adjusting the diffuseness parameter \(f_n\) (set to 1, 2, and 3) in a Fermi-type density distribution, while keeping the proton density distribution unchanged. The IBD quantity \(\Delta\mu_{21}/T\) was computed from both prefragment yields (before evaporation) and final fragment yields (after evaporation) for each \(f_n\) value, and compared to the difference in neutron density between \(^{48}\)Ca and \(^{40}\)Ca.

## Key numbers

- The IBD results from prefragments show plateau heights that increase with \(f_n\); for \(f_n = 1\) the plateau heights are smaller than the measured value of approximately 2 from Mocko (2006), while for \(f_n = 2\) the plateau heights are similar to that value.
- For final fragments, the plateau heights of \(\Delta\mu_{21}/T\) are about 0.5, which is much smaller than the measured results from IBD13PRC but larger than those from IBD14Ca.
- The excitation energy of a prefragment is calculated as \(E^{*} = 13.3 \langle A(b) \rangle\) MeV, where \(\langle A(b) \rangle\) is the average number of abraded nucleons.
- The calculations are performed for reactions at 80\(A\) MeV beam energy.

## Context

This work extends the IBD method previously developed by Ma et al. (IBD13PRC, IBD13JPG) to study neutron density distributions in neutron-rich nuclei, specifically \(^{48}\)Ca. It builds on the isobaric yield ratio (IYR) method introduced by Huang et al. (2010) and the isoscaling method for symmetry energy studies. The results show that while prefragments are sensitive to density changes, the evaporation process in the SAA model significantly reduces this sensitivity in final fragments, suggesting that further improvement of the evaporation treatment in the SAA model is needed before the IBD probe can be reliably used to extract neutron density information from experimental data.
