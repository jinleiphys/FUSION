# Magnetized bottom-up thermalization in heavy-ion collisions

> We investigate how a strong magnetic field generated in noncentral heavy-ion collisions may modify the bottom-up equilibration scenario. In the conventional weak-coupling picture, the earliest stages of the evolution are dominated by overoccupied gluons, while quark production is parametrically delayed. In a background magnetic field, however, additional inelastic channels become kinematically allowed or enhanced, most notably gluon decay into quark-antiquark pairs, $g\to q+\bar q$. Using parametric estimates, we show that for sufficiently strong fields, with $|eB|$ approaching the saturation scale squared, $Q_s^2$, magnetic-field-induced quark production can become important during the earliest stages of bottom-up evolution. This mechanism can populate the hard quark sector, modify the chemical composition of the pre-equilibrium matter, and provide an additional pathway toward chemical equilibration. We also discuss possible back-reaction effects, including quark-antiquark annihilation, depletion of the hard-gluon sector, and the potential feedback of early quark production on the electromagnetic conductivity of the medium. This exploratory study of a magnetically assisted bottom-up scenario provides a natural extension of the standard framework, with qualitative predictions that depend sensitively on the lifetime and spacetime profile of the magnetic field.

## Key claim

The authors propose that a sufficiently strong magnetic field (with \(|eB|\) approaching the saturation scale squared \(Q_s^2\)) can make magnetic-field-induced gluon decay into quark-antiquark pairs important during the earliest stages of bottom-up thermalization, but they emphasize that the quantitative importance depends sensitively on the lifetime and spacetime profile of the magnetic field, and that for weaker fields (\(|eB|\ll Q_s^2\)) the effect is correspondingly suppressed.

## Method

The authors perform parametric estimates within the weak-coupling bottom-up thermalization framework, extending it by incorporating magnetic-field-induced inelastic channels, most notably \(g\to q+\bar q\). They derive rate equations for the coupled evolution of hard gluon and hard quark number densities that include both the magnetic-field-induced production term and a phenomenological annihilation back-reaction term, and they estimate the corresponding occupation numbers and scaling behaviors during the first and second stages of bottom-up evolution.

## Key numbers

* The magnetic field can reach values of order \(|eB|\simeq 10 m_\pi^2\) at RHIC energies and \(|eB|\simeq 70 m_\pi^2\) at LHC energies during the earliest moments after the collision.
* The gluon decay rate into quark-antiquark pairs scales parametrically as \(\Gamma_{g\to q\bar q}\sim \alpha_s |eB|/Q_s\).
* Full thermal equilibrium in the standard scenario is reached at \(\tau_{\text{th}}\sim \alpha_s^{-13/5}Q_s^{-1}\) with final temperature \(T_{\text{final}}\sim Q_s\alpha_s^{1/4}\).
* The hard-quark density produced by magnetic-field-induced gluon decay is parametrically of order \(|eB|Q_s\) after a short transient.
* For \(|eB|\sim Q_s^2\), the quark occupation number can become of order unity, suggesting substantial modification of the pre-equilibrium matter composition.
* The quark density becomes comparable to the gluon density around \(\tau \sim Q_s/(\alpha_s |eB|)\) for \(\alpha_s = 0.01\).
* The soft-sector contribution to the quark density scales as \(n_{q,\text{soft}} \sim \alpha_s^{3/4}|eB|Q_s (Q_s\tau)^{1/2}\) during the second stage.

## Context

This work extends the standard bottom-up thermalization scenario developed by Baier (2000) to include the strong magnetic fields generated in noncentral heavy-ion collisions, which were previously studied by Skokov (2009), Voronyuk (2011), Deng (2012), and Tuchin (2013). The magnetic-field-induced gluon decay mechanism builds on earlier work by Erber (1966), Wang (2020, 2021), Chen (2024), and Ghosh (2024) showing that such processes become kinematically allowed in a magnetized medium. The study provides a natural extension of the conventional weak-coupling framework and enables future work incorporating explicit spacetime dependence of the electromagnetic field, self-consistent feedback between quark production and conductivity, and kinetic equations with realistic momentum anisotropies and magnetic-field-dependent collision kernels.
