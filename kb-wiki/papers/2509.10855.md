# Onset of Bjorken Flow in Quantum Evolution of the Massive Schwinger Model

> The onset of hydrodynamics in the hot medium created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions is a crucial theoretical question. A first-principle simulation requires a real-time, non-perturbative calculation of the quantum system. In this Letter, we perform such simulations using the tensor network method, which enables large-scale quantum many-body simulations by retaining only the most essential quantum states for collective behaviors. We focus on the massive Schwinger model, a low-dimensional analog of quantum chromodynamics (QCD), as they share important properties such as confinement and chiral symmetry breaking. Starting from an initial state that puts a localized excitation atop the vacuum and mimics the energy deposition from colliding nuclei, we observe hydrodynamic behavior consistent with Bjorken flow in all relevant degrees of freedom: energy density, fluid velocity, and bulk pressure. The time scale for hydrodynamic onset aligns with the thermalization time of the quantum distribution function.

## Key claim
The authors observe that in the massive Schwinger model, a low-dimensional analog of QCD, the onset of Bjorken-like hydrodynamic flow occurs at a timescale of approximately τ ∼ 10 g⁻¹, which aligns with the thermalization time of the quantum distribution function, but only in the strong-coupling (small fermion mass) regime; the behavior is absent when the coupling is weak (large fermion mass).

## Method
The authors perform real-time quantum many-body simulations of the massive Schwinger model using tensor network methods (Matrix Product States with bond dimension D=500) on a lattice of N=100 sites with spacing a = 1/2 g⁻¹. They prepare an initial state consisting of the vacuum perturbed by a localized energy excitation mimicking energy deposition from colliding nuclei, then evolve the system via the Time-Evolving Block Decimation method with time step Δt = 0.01 g⁻¹. The full space-time evolution of the energy-momentum tensor T^{μν} is computed, and the fluid velocity is extracted in the Landau-Lifshitz frame.

## Key numbers
- Lattice spacing: a = 1/2 g⁻¹, with N = 100 lattice sites and bond dimension D = 500
- Time step size: Δt = 0.01 g⁻¹
- Hydrodynamic onset timescale: τ ∼ 10 g⁻¹ for the strong coupling case (m = g/10)
- Speed of sound for strong coupling: c_s² = 0.5 (taken from the equation of state at the corresponding energy density)
- Speed of sound for weak coupling (m = 2g): c_s² = 0.05
- Bulk pressure oscillation amplitude bounded within Navier-Stokes solution (Π ∝ τ⁻²) after τ - τ_ini ≳ 1 g⁻¹
- Rapidity plateau structure in energy density observed for proper time τ - τ_ini ≳ 3 g⁻¹
- At late time (τ - τ_ini ∼ 20 g⁻¹), energy density becomes dilute and quantum fluctuations become more pronounced

## Context
This work extends previous studies of thermalization in the Schwinger model (Florio 2024, Florio 2025) and collective motion (Janik 2025) by directly probing the emergence of hydrodynamic behavior from the energy-momentum tensor evolution in an isolated quantum system without external sources. It contrasts with classical hydrodynamic attractor studies (Heller 2015, Romatschke 2017) and semi-classical Boltzmann approaches (Kurkela 2015, Blaizot 2011) by providing a fully quantum mechanical, first-principles simulation. The results enable future investigations into whether strong coupling or small fermion mass plays the dominant role in accelerating thermalization, which the authors plan to address by studying theories where mass and coupling can be separated.
