# Dynamical response of twin stars to perturbations

> If a strong first-order phase transition takes place at sufficiently high rest-mass densities in the equation of state (EOS) modelling compact stars, a new branch will appear in the mass-radius sequence of stable equilibria. This branch will be populated by stars comprising a quark-matter core and a hadronic-matter envelope, i.e., hybrid stars, which represent ``twin-star'' solutions to equilibria having the same mass but a fully hadronic EOS. While both branches are stable to linear perturbations, it is unclear which of the twin solutions is the ``favoured'' one, that is, which of the two configurations is expected to be found in nature. We assess this point by performing a large campaign of general-relativistic simulations aimed at assessing the response of compact stars on the two branches to perturbations of various strength. In this way, we find that, independently of whether the stars populate the hadronic or the twin branch, their response is characterised by a critical-perturbation strength such that the star will oscillate on the original branch for subcritical perturbations and migrate to the neighbouring branch for supercritical perturbations while conserving rest-mass. Because the critical values are different for stars with the same rest-mass but sitting on either branch, it is possible to define as favoured the part of the branch that has the largest critical perturbation, thus correcting the common wisdom that stellar models on the twin branch are the favoured ones. Interestingly, we show that the binding energies on the two branches can be used to deduce without simulations which of the stellar configurations is more likely to be found in nature.

## Key claim

The paper corrects the common wisdom that twin-branch (hybrid) stars are the favoured configurations, showing instead that the favoured branch depends on the stellar mass and can be determined by comparing critical perturbation thresholds or binding energies, with a "neutral mass" existing where both branches are equally favoured.

## Method

The authors performed over 500 spherically symmetric general-relativistic hydrodynamic simulations using the open-source code GR1D, solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for initial data and evolving with a finite-volume scheme. They introduced constant inward radial velocity perturbations of varying strength (lambda_H,T) on static stellar models from both the hadronic branch (HB) and twin branch (TB), using a piecewise polytropic equation of state with a Maxwell-type first-order phase transition region where the speed of sound is very small but nonzero.

## Key numbers

- The two representative models with rest-mass M_b = 1.4000 M_sun have gravitational masses M = 1.3119 M_sun (TB) and M = 1.3124 M_sun (HB), with radii differing by more than 2 km (11.6540 km for TB, 13.9110 km for HB)
- Critical perturbation velocity for the HB model (lambda_H,crit) = 0.021 c, while for the TB model (lambda_T,crit) = 0.040 c
- The neutral mass where both branches are equally favoured is M_neut = 1.2988 M_sun for the primary equation of state studied
- The binding energy mapping to critical perturbation holds with a precision of 0.1 percent
- For category II twin stars, neutral masses were found at M_neut = 1.886 M_sun and 1.716 M_sun for two equations of state, while a third equation of state showed no neutral mass with TB stars above approximately 1.355 M_sun collapsing to black holes instead of migrating

## Context

This work extends the study of twin stars first systematically classified by Christian (2018) and Montana (2019) into categories I through IV based on the relative maximum masses of the hadronic and twin branches. It builds on earlier demonstrations by Font (2002) that unstable stellar models can migrate to equilibrium configurations under perturbations, and on the extensive twin-star literature reviewed by Haque (2026). The results enable future work to predict which twin configuration is more likely to be observed without performing numerical simulations, simply by comparing binding energies, and open the question of black hole collapse criticality for very compact twin stars.
