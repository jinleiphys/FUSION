# Reduction of SO in $^{34}$Si: weak binding or density-depletion effect ?

> The reduction of the neutron spin-orbit splitting $2p_{3/2} - 2p_{1/2}$ between the $^{41}$Ca and $^{35}$Si isotones is a unique feature throughout the chart of nuclides, as the spin-orbit splitting usually increases with $A$. Moreover, its way of decrease, gradual between $^{41}$Ca and $^{35}$Si or abrupt between $^{37}$S and $^{35}$Si, as well as its origin, caused by the weak binding energy of the $p$ states or by the sudden central proton density depletion in $^{35}$Si, are subject of debate. The results reported here using the self-consistent Covariant Energy Density Functional calculations with the DD-ME2 parametrization rather point to an abrupt, local decrease in $^{35}$Si, and to the large dominance of the central density depletion effect. It is concluded that weak binding, central density depletion as well as correlations must be taken into account to fully evaluate the amplitude and causes of this spin-orbit reduction.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the reduction of the neutron spin-orbit splitting between the $2p_{3/2}$ and $2p_{1/2}$ orbitals in $^{35}$Si relative to heavier $N=21$ isotones is primarily caused by the sudden depletion of central proton density due to the emptying of the $2s_{1/2}$ proton orbital, with only about 10% of the reduction attributable to weak binding effects, contrary to the claim of Kay et al. (2017) that weak binding alone can fully explain the reduction.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent Covariant Energy Density Functional (CEDF) calculations using the DD-ME2 parametrization for the $N=21$ isotones $^{41}$Ca, $^{39}$Ar, $^{37}$S, and $^{35}$Si, solving the relativistic mean-field equations in a large box to obtain correct asymptotic behavior of weakly bound orbitals. They also performed modified Woods-Saxon potential calculations that included a central density depletion derived from the $2s_{1/2}$ proton wave function, and compared the $2p_{3/2}-2p_{1/2}$ splittings obtained from one-neutron removal energies in odd-$N$ nuclei and from single-particle energies in even-even $N=20$ cores.

## Key numbers

* The $2p_{3/2}-2p_{1/2}$ spin-orbit splitting in $^{35}$Si is 1.134 MeV, compared to approximately 2 MeV in $^{41}$Ca, $^{39}$Ar, and $^{37}$S (with only 0.5% variation among these three).
* In $^{35}$Si, the $3/2^-$ state is bound by 1.560(40) MeV and the $1/2^-$ state by 0.426(40) MeV, while the $7/2^-$ ground state is bound by 2.470(40) MeV.
* The CEDF calculation gives a $2p_{3/2}-2p_{1/2}$ splitting of 0.72 MeV in $^{35}$Si (odd system) and 0.76 MeV (even system), compared to 1.66 MeV and 1.71 MeV in $^{41}$Ca.
* The Woods-Saxon calculation with central depletion yields a splitting of 1.35 MeV (with depletion but no weak binding effect) and 0.91 MeV (with both depletion and weak binding), compared to 1.8 MeV with neither effect.
* The CEDF calculation attributes about 10% of the SO splitting reduction between $^{37}$S and $^{35}$Si to the continuum effect from weak binding.
* The proton $2p$ spin-orbit splitting in $^{131}$In is 988 keV, falling on the global trend of Mairle (1993).
* The neutron $3p_{3/2}-3p_{1/2}$ splitting around $^{132}$Sn is 0.51 MeV, lying about 24% below the global trend.

## Context

This work extends the debate on the origin of spin-orbit splitting reduction in neutron-rich nuclei, directly contradicting the conclusion of Kay et al. (2017) that weak binding alone can fully explain the reduction in the $N=21$ isotones. The results support earlier work by Burgunder et al. (2014), Li et al. (2016), and Duguet et al. (2017) that identified central density depletion as the major cause, and are consistent with the shell-model calculations of Otsuka et al. (2020) that found the main reduction from two-body LS forces with an additional 8% from continuum effects. The paper enables future experimental studies of $^{57}$Ni to clarify unexplained reductions in the $A\approx60$ region, and demonstrates that self-consistent calculations including density depletion, weak binding, and correlations are necessary to fully understand spin-orbit splitting evolution.
