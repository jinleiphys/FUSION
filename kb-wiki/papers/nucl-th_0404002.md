# Neutrino-Nucleus Cross Section Measurements using Stopped Pions and Low Energy Beta Beams

> Two new facilities have recently been proposed to measure low energy neutrino-nucleus cross sections, the nu-SNS (Spallation Neutron Source) and low energy beta beams. The former produces neutrinos by pion decay at rest, while the latter produces neutrinos from the beta decays of accelerated ions. One of the uses of neutrino-nucleus cross section measurements is for supernova studies, where typical neutrino energies are 10s of MeV. In this energy range there are many different components to the nuclear response and this makes the theoretical interpretation of the results of such an experiment complex. Although even one measurement on a heavy nucleus such as lead is much anticipated, more than one data set would be still better. We suggest that this can be done by breaking the electron spectrum down into the parts produced in coincidence with one or two neutrons, running a beta beam at more than one energy, comparing the spectra produced with pions and a beta beam or any combination of these.

## Key claim

While the abstract states that measuring neutrino-nucleus cross sections at the proposed nu-SNS and beta beam facilities would be valuable for supernova studies, the paper's actual claim is that a single measurement on a heavy nucleus like lead, while anticipated, would be insufficient to disentangle the complex nuclear response; instead, multiple complementary data sets are needed, achievable by combining electron spectra tagged by neutron coincidence, operating a beta beam at more than one energy, or comparing spectra from both pion and beta beam sources.

## Method

The author calculates charged-current electron neutrino capture on a 10-ton lead-208 target, using cross sections from Engel et al. (2003), for three neutrino sources: stopped pions from the nu-SNS (with a production rate of 10^15 neutrinos per second, target at 20 meters), and a Neon-18 beta beam boosted to gamma factors of 5, 10, and 15 (with 10^13 ions per second, target at 10 meters from a 90-meter straight section). The electron energy spectra are decomposed into contributions from allowed transitions (0+, 1+) and various forbidden multipoles, and further separated by the number of spalled neutrons (zero, one, or two) produced in coincidence.

## Key numbers

* For the stopped pion source on lead-208: flux-averaged charged current cross section of 41 x 10^-40 cm^2, average electron energy of 21 MeV, and 180 total events per day (120 with one neutron, 66 with two neutrons).
* For the beta beam at gamma=5: flux-averaged cross section of 8.5 x 10^-40 cm^2, average electron energy of 11 MeV, and 1.1 total events per day (0.8 with one neutron, 0.05 with two neutrons).
* For the beta beam at gamma=10: flux-averaged cross section of 52 x 10^-40 cm^2, average electron energy of 25 MeV, and 26 total events per day (13 with one neutron, 10 with two neutrons).
* For the beta beam at gamma=15: flux-averaged cross section of 110 x 10^-40 cm^2, average electron energy of 40 MeV, and 95 total events per day (34 with one neutron, 53 with two neutrons).
* Neon-18 has a half-life of 1.67 seconds and a Q-value of 3.93 MeV, giving a maximum neutrino energy of about 3.4 MeV in its rest frame.
* For the pion source, 80% of two-neutron events come from 0-, 1-, and 2- forbidden transitions; 75% of one-neutron events come from allowed transitions.
* At gamma=15, the 2+, 3+, 3-, 4+, and 4- multipoles contribute 38% of the total signal.

## Context

This work extends the proposals for low-energy neutrino-nucleus measurements at the nu-SNS facility (nu-SNS collaboration, 2004) and beta beam facilities (Zucchelli, 2002; Volpe, 2003) by showing how multiple complementary measurements can disentangle the nuclear response. The calculations use cross sections from Engel et al. (2003) and build on prior theoretical work on lead-208 by Jachowicz and Heyde (2003), Fuller et al. (1999), Kolbe and Langanke (2001), and Volpe et al. (2002). The results enable a strategy for calibrating supernova neutrino detectors such as OMNIS and LAND, and for constraining the nuclear structure inputs needed for supernova neutrino-nucleosynthesis calculations.
