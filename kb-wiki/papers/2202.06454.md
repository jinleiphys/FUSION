# Reply to Comment on "Phase-space consideration on barrier transmission in a time-dependent variational approach with superposed wave packets"

> In the paper by Hasegawa, Hagino and Tanimura (HHT) [Phys. Lett. B 808 (2020) 135693, arXiv:2006.06944], they concluded that quantum tunneling was simulated by a time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM). In contrast, difficulties of TDGCM in describing quantum tunneling were pointed out by Ono in the paper [Phys. Lett. B 826 (2022) 136931, arXiv:2201.02966]. Recently, HHT submitted a comment [arXiv:2202.00513v1], by which they appear to give some counterarguments to Ono's paper. Here I examine their arguments, reviewing the main points of Ono's paper. The confusion in HHT's arguments may be mostly resolved by recognizing how the wave packets interfere with each other when they are coherently superposed.

## Key claim

The TDGCM simulation of barrier transmission claimed by Hasegawa, Hagino, and Tanimura (2020) to represent quantum tunneling is actually of classical nature due to high-momentum components in the prepared initial state, and the confusion in their counterarguments can be resolved by recognizing how wave packets interfere when coherently superposed.

## Method

The author examines the time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) applied to a one-particle-in-one-dimension problem equivalent to the relative motion of two colliding nuclei, where the wave function is expressed as a superposition of Gaussian wave packets with fixed width parameter. The analysis focuses on the time evolution of the phase-space distribution (Wigner and Husimi functions) to understand whether barrier passage arises from quantum tunneling or classical barrier passage due to the momentum distribution of the initial state.

## Key numbers

- The width parameter of the Gaussian wave packets is fixed at ν_r = 1 fm^{-2}, giving spatial width Δx = 1/(2√ν_r) and momentum width Δp = ħ√ν_r, with minimum uncertainty ΔxΔp = ½ħ.
- The barrier height is V_B = 0.13 MeV, and the initial energy E(t=0) = 0.11 MeV was chosen below this barrier.
- The reduced mass is μ = 1876 MeV.
- The minimum energy of the two-wave-packet superposition is E = 4.60 MeV at (x̃, p̃) = (±0.80 fm, 0), while the initial condition chosen by HHT was (x̃, p̃) = (0.05 fm, 0.61 MeV/c) with E = 10.33 MeV.
- During free propagation, the momentum centers p_a(t) change by approximately 100 MeV/c over 5 fm/c.

## Context

This reply extends the author's previous criticism (Ono, 2022) of the claim by Hasegawa, Hagino, and Tanimura (2020) that TDGCM can simulate quantum tunneling. The author contrasts TDGCM with entangled trajectory molecular dynamics (ETMD) developed in quantum chemistry by Donoso and Martens (2001, 2003), noting that in ETMD free propagation occurs naturally without entanglement, while TDGCM already exhibits non-trivial entangled trajectories for free propagation. The work enables future careful analysis of whether TDGCM can be qualified for studying quantum tunneling, requiring tests similar to those performed for ETMD.
