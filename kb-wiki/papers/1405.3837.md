# Influence of the symmetry energy on nuclear pasta in neutron star crusts

> We investigate the effects of the symmetry energy on nuclear pasta phases and the crust-core transition in neutron stars. We employ the relativistic mean-field approach and the coexisting phases method to study the properties of pasta phases presented in the inner crust of neutron stars. It is found that the slope of the nuclear symmetry energy at saturation density plays an important role in the crust-core transition and pasta phase properties. The correlation between the symmetry energy slope and the crust-core transition density obtained in this study is consistent with those obtained by other methods.

## Key claim

The slope of the nuclear symmetry energy at saturation density, \(L\), is shown to have a clear correlation with the crust-core transition density and pasta phase properties, but this correlation is nonmonotonic for the transition pressure, which depends on competing effects and becomes weaker near \(L \sim 60\) MeV.

## Method

The authors employed the relativistic mean-field (RMF) approach with the TM1 and IUFSU parametrizations, generating two sets of models in which the symmetry energy at saturation density was fixed while the slope \(L\) was varied by tuning the \(\omega\)-\(\rho\) coupling strength. They used the coexisting phases method within the Wigner-Seitz approximation to describe the inner crust matter, with the surface tension calculated self-consistently via a Thomas-Fermi approach rather than using a parametrized form. The energy densities of five pasta shapes (droplet, rod, slab, tube, bubble) were compared to determine the stable configuration, and the crust-core transition was identified as the density where the homogeneous phase has lower energy density than the pasta phase.

## Key numbers

- For the original TM1 model, \(E_{\text{sym}} = 36.9\) MeV and \(L = 110.8\) MeV; for the original IUFSU model, \(E_{\text{sym}} = 31.3\) MeV and \(L = 47.2\) MeV.
- The range of \(L\) studied was \(50 \leq L \leq 110.8\) MeV for TM1-based models and \(47.2 \leq L \leq 80\) MeV for IUFSU-based models.
- The crust-core transition density for the original TM1 model was \(n_{b,t} = 0.058\ \text{fm}^{-3}\).
- The maximum neutron-star mass for the original TM1 model (\(L=110.8\) MeV) was about \(2.18\ M_\odot\), decreasing to \(2.11\ M_\odot\) for \(L=50\) MeV; for the original IUFSU model (\(L=47.2\) MeV) it was about \(1.94\ M_\odot\), increasing to \(1.95\ M_\odot\) for \(L=80\) MeV.
- Protons began to drip at \(P_b = 0.40\ \text{MeV/fm}^3\) for the original TM1 model and at \(P_b = 0.34\ \text{MeV/fm}^3\) for the original IUFSU model.
- For larger \(L\) (\(L \geq 80\) MeV in TM1, \(L \geq 60\) MeV in IUFSU), only the droplet configuration appeared before the crust-core transition.

## Context

This work extends studies of pasta phases and the crust-core transition using the Thomas-Fermi approximation (Oyamatsu 2007) and the liquid-drop model (Watanabe 2000), and it directly compares with results from dynamical and thermodynamical methods (Ducoin 2011). The authors confirm the correlation between the symmetry energy slope \(L\) and the crust-core transition density found in those earlier works, while also showing that the transition pressure depends nonmonotonically on \(L\) due to competing effects. The results enable more precise modeling of neutron star crust structure and help constrain the density dependence of the symmetry energy from astrophysical observations.
