# Mesons and Baryons: Parity Partners

> We calculate masses of light and heavy mesons as well as baryons of negative parity containing $u,d,s,c$ and $b$ quarks. It is an extension of our previous work where we had studied the positive parity baryons. We adopt a quark-diquark picture of baryons where the diquarks are non-pointlike with a finite spatial extension. The mathematical foundation for this analysis is implemented through a symmetry-preserving Schwinger-Dyson equations treatment of a vector-vector contact interaction, which preserves key features of quantum chromodynamics, such as confinement, chiral symmetry breaking, axial vector Ward-Takahashi identity and low-energy Goldberger-Treiman relations. This treatment simultaneously describes mesons and provides attractive correlations for diquarks in the $\overline{3}$ representation. Employing this model, we compute the spectrum and masses of all spin-1/2 and spin-3/2 baryons of negative parity, supplementing our earlier evaluation of positive parity baryons, containing 1, 2 or 3 heavy quarks. In the process, we calculate masses of a multitude of mesons and corresponding diquarks. Wherever possible, we make comparisons of our results with known experimental observations as well as theoretical predictions of several models and approaches including lattice quantum chromodynamics, finding satisfactory agreement. We also make predictions for heavier states not yet observed in the experiment.

## Key claim

The paper extends a previous quark-diquark model for positive-parity baryons to compute masses of negative-parity baryons (spin-1/2 and spin-3/2) containing up to three heavy quarks, using a symmetry-preserving contact interaction that incorporates spin-orbit repulsion via a phenomenological coupling factor \(g_{SO}\) to reproduce the observed mass splitting between parity partners.

## Method

The authors solve the homogeneous Bethe-Salpeter equation for mesons and the Faddeev equation for baryons within a vector-vector contact interaction framework, using a quark-diquark picture where diquarks are non-pointlike with finite spatial extension. The same interaction that describes mesons generates diquark correlations in the color-antitriplet channel with half the attractive strength. For scalar and axial-vector channels, a spin-orbit repulsion factor \(g_{SO}\) is introduced to mimic the dressed-quark chromomagnetic moment, with values \(g_{SO}^{0^+}=0.32\) and \(g_{SO}^{1^+}=0.25\) chosen to reproduce experimental mass splittings.

## Key numbers

* Pion mass: 0.14 GeV (experimental 0.139 GeV, difference 0.7%)
* \(\rho\)-meson mass: approximately 19% above the experimental value of 770 MeV
* Mass splitting between \(\rho(\tu\bar\td)\) and \(a_1(\tu\bar\td)\): 440 MeV
* Mass splitting between \(\Upsilon(\tb\bar{\tb})\) and \(\chi_{b1}(\tb\bar{\tb})\): 100 MeV (less than 5% error)
* \(\eta_c(\tc\bar{\tc})\) mass: 2.98 GeV (experimental 2.98 GeV)
* \(\eta_b(\tb\bar{\tb})\) mass: 9.40 GeV (experimental 9.40 GeV)
* \(\chi_{c0}(\tc\bar{\tc})\) mass: 3.35 GeV (experimental 3.414 GeV)
* \(\chi_{b0}(\tb\bar{\tb})\) mass: 9.50 GeV (experimental 9.859 GeV)
* \(K_0^*(\tu\bar{\ts})\) mass: 1.33 GeV (experimental 1.430 GeV, difference 7%)
* \(D_0^*(\tc\bar{\tu})\) mass: 2.32 GeV (experimental 2.300 GeV)
* \(D_{s0}^*(\tc\bar{\ts})\) mass: 2.43 GeV (experimental 2.317 GeV)
* \(B_c^+(\tc\bar{\tb})\) mass: 6.29 GeV (experimental 6.27 GeV)
* Dressed quark masses: \(M_u=0.367\) GeV, \(M_s=0.53\) GeV, \(M_c=1.52\) GeV, \(M_b=4.68\) GeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study of positive-parity baryons (Gutierrez-Guerrero, 2019) to their negative-parity partners, employing the same contact interaction framework. The chiral-partner structure of hadrons including one heavy quark was previously studied by Nowak (1992, 2003) and Bardeen (1993, 2003), while doubly heavy baryons with negative parity were investigated by Ma (2015) as chiral partners of positive parity heavy baryons. The results enable predictions for heavier baryon states not yet observed experimentally, particularly triply heavy baryons whose detection remains challenging at current facilities like LHCb and Belle II.
