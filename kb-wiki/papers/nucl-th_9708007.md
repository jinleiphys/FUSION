# Estimating the nuclear level density with the Monte Carlo shell model

> A method for making realistic estimates of the density of levels in even-even nuclei is presented making use of the Monte Carlo shell model (MCSM). The procedure follows three basic steps: (1) computation of the thermal energy with the MCSM, (2) evaluation of the partition function by integrating the thermal energy, and (3) evaluating the level density by performing the inverse Laplace transform of the partition function using Maximum Entropy reconstruction techniques. It is found that results obtained with schematic interactions, which do not have a sign problem in the MCSM, compare well with realistic shell-model interactions provided an important isospin dependence is accounted for.

## Key claim

The Monte Carlo shell model (MCSM) can produce realistic estimates of nuclear level densities for even-even nuclei, but only if the schematic interaction used (which avoids the sign problem) is corrected for its improper isospin dependence by adding an approximate \(a\hat T^2\) term; the method is validated for \({}^{24}\)Mg up to excitation energies of about 20 MeV.

## Method

The procedure has four steps: (1) compute the thermal expectation value of the Hamiltonian \(E(\beta)\) using the MCSM with a schematic surface-delta interaction (SDI); (2) correct \(E(\beta)\) for the missing isospin dependence using a first-order perturbative expansion in \(\beta\) of the \(a\hat T^2\) term; (3) obtain the partition function \(Z(\beta)\) by integrating the corrected thermal energy; (4) perform the inverse Laplace transform of \(Z(\beta)\) using maximum-entropy reconstruction techniques to extract the level density \(\rho(E)\). The method is benchmarked against exact shell-model diagonalization results for \({}^{24}\)Mg using the USD interaction.

## Key numbers

* For \({}^{24}\)Mg, the SDI interaction with the isospin correction factor \(a = 1.507\) MeV shifts the centroid of the level density from \(\sim 36\) MeV (uncorrected SDI) to \(\sim 40\) MeV, compared to the USD centroid at \(\sim 42\) MeV.
* The MCSM calculations used inverse temperatures \(\beta\) from 0 to 1 MeV\(^{-1}\) in steps of \(\Delta\beta = 1/80\) MeV\(^{-1}\), with 2000 Monte Carlo samples per \(\beta\) value and up to 40 time slices at \(\beta = 1\) MeV\(^{-1}\).
* Typical Monte Carlo uncertainties for the energy observable ranged from 10 keV at small \(\beta\) to about 120 keV at \(\beta \sim 1\) MeV\(^{-1}\).
* The ground-state energy of \({}^{24}\)Mg was evaluated as \(-76.844(200)\) MeV by extrapolating MCSM calculations for \(\beta\) from 1.75 to 2.25 MeV\(^{-1}\).
* The exact level density for \({}^{24}\)Mg using the USD Hamiltonian was obtained from 28,503 eigenvalues (for \(J_z = 0\)) binned into 1 MeV bins including the \(2J+1\) degeneracy.

## Context

This work extends the spectral distribution methods of French and others (1970s) and the GOE-based subspace method of Pluhař and Weidenmüller (1988) by using the MCSM to compute thermal properties exactly within large model spaces, avoiding the limitations of higher-moment calculations and the GOE approximation. The method enables estimates of level densities for nuclei of astrophysical interest, such as \({}^{22}\)Mg, \({}^{26}\)Si, \({}^{30}\)S, and \({}^{34}\)Ar for \((\alpha,\gamma)\) reaction rates, and for \({}^{162}\)Dy and \({}^{172}\)Yb where experimental level-density data exist up to the neutron separation energy.
