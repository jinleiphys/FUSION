# Electromagnetic interaction models for Monte Carlo simulation of protons and alpha particles

> Electromagnetic interactions of protons and alpha particles are modeled in a form that is suitable for Monte Carlo simulation of the transport of charged particles. The differential cross section (DCS) for elastic collisions with neutral atoms is expressed as the product of the DCS for collisions with the bare nucleus and a correction factor that accounts for the screening of the nuclear charge by the atomic electrons. The screening factor is obtained as the ratio of the DCS for scattering of the projectile by an atom with a point nucleus and the parameterized Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater (DHFS) electron density, calculated from the eikonal approximation, and the Rutherford DCS for collisions with the bare point nucleus. Inelastic collisions, which cause electronic excitations of the material, are described by means of the plane-wave Born approximation, with an empirical simple model of the generalized oscillator strength (GOS) that combines several extended oscillators with resonance energies and strengths determined from the atomic configurations and from the empirical mean excitation energy of the material. The contributions from inner subshells are renormalized to agree with realistic ionization cross sections calculated numerically from the DHFS self-consistent model of atoms by means of the plane-wave Born approximation. The resulting DCS allows analytical random sampling of individual hard inelastic interactions.

## Key claim
The paper presents a practical model for elastic and inelastic electromagnetic cross sections of protons and alpha particles that is specifically designed for efficient Monte Carlo transport simulation. The key qualifier is that the model relies on an additivity approximation for molecules and on global optical-model potentials, which are acknowledged to be less reliable for light target nuclei and for very high energies, but are justified because the majority of collisions occur at small angles where the screened Coulomb potential dominates.

## Method
Elastic differential cross sections (DCS) are calculated as the product of the DCS for scattering by the bare nucleus (computed via partial-wave analysis with global optical-model potentials) and a screening factor derived from the eikonal approximation for a Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater (DHFS) electron density. Inelastic collisions are described using the plane-wave Born approximation with an empirical generalized oscillator strength (GOS) model, where inner-subshell contributions are renormalized to match DHFS ionization cross sections. The resulting DCSs are tabulated on a logarithmic energy grid from 100 keV to 1 GeV for elements from hydrogen (Z=1) to einsteinium (Z=99), and are designed to allow analytical random sampling of individual hard interactions.

## Key numbers
- The database covers laboratory kinetic energies from **100 keV to 1 GeV** for each element.
- The energy grid is logarithmic with **35 points per decade**.
- For each energy, the DCS is tabulated for a grid of **1000 polar angles**.
- The maximum energy loss in an elastic collision is given by a formula involving the projectile mass (e.g., proton rest mass is **1836.15 electron masses**; alpha rest mass is **7294.30 electron masses**).
- The atomic radius is approximated as **Z^{-1/3} a_0**, where the Bohr radius a_0 is **5.292 x 10^{-9} cm**.
- The nuclear radius is approximated as **1.2 A^{1/3} fm**.
- The Koning and Delaroche optical-model potential is used for protons with kinetic energies between **1 keV and about 200 MeV** (extrapolated to 300 MeV).
- The Su and Han optical-model potential for alpha particles is valid for kinetic energies up to **386 MeV**.

## Context
This work extends the semi-relativistic approach of Salvat and Quesada (2020) for elastic collisions by incorporating a more comprehensive treatment of nuclear effects using global optical-model potentials, such as those by Koning and Delaroche (2003) for protons and Su and Han (2015) for alphas. It also provides a complete inelastic model based on the plane-wave Born approximation with a renormalized GOS. The resulting database and sampling algorithms enable the implementation of detailed (class-II) Monte Carlo simulations of proton and alpha particle transport, which are superior to condensed (class-I) simulations because they treat hard interactions exactly.
