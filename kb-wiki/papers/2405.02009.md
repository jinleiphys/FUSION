# On the (in)consistency of perturbation theory at finite temperature

> A well-known difficulty of perturbative approaches to quantum field theory at finite temperature is the necessity to address theoretical constraints that are not present in the vacuum theory. In this work, we use lattice simulations of scalar correlation functions in massive $ϕ^{4}$ theory to analyse the extent to which these constraints affect the perturbative predictions. We find that the standard perturbative predictions deteriorate even in the absence of infrared divergences at relatively low temperatures, and that this is directly connected to the analytic structure of the propagators used in the expansion. This suggests that the incorporation of non-perturbative thermal effects in the propagators is essential for a consistent perturbative formulation of scalar quantum field theories at finite temperature. By utilising the spectral constraints imposed on finite-temperature correlation functions, we explore how these effects manifest themselves in the lattice data, and discuss why the presence of distinct thermoparticle excitations provides a potential resolution to these issues.

## Key claim

Standard perturbative predictions for scalar correlation functions at finite temperature deteriorate even in the absence of infrared divergences at relatively low temperatures (0 < T/m ≲ O(1)), and this deterioration is directly connected to the analytic structure of the propagators used in the expansion, suggesting that non-perturbative thermal effects in the propagators are essential for a consistent perturbative formulation.

## Method

The authors performed lattice simulations of two-point correlation functions in 3+1-dimensional massive φ⁴ theory in the symmetric phase, using a fixed-scale approach where the lattice spacing remains constant and temperature is changed in discrete steps by varying Nτ. They compared these numerical results with finite-temperature lattice perturbation theory predictions computed to two-loop order (including tadpole, cactus, and sunset diagrams) on identical finite volumes and lattice spacings, using the same bare parameter sets to eliminate discretisation and finite-volume systematics. The relative deviation Δn between perturbative and simulated correlators at a correlation distance zm=1 was used as a quantitative measure of perturbative accuracy.

## Key numbers

- For the vacuum-like system with bare parameters (am₀=0.315, g₀=0.5) giving gR=0.49, the two-loop relative deviation Δ₂ at zm=1 was 0.8% (0.6% uncertainty), with χ²/d.o.f.=1.7
- For the same parameter set at T/m=1.35 (Nτ=2), Δ₂ remained 0.4% (0.6% uncertainty) with χ²/d.o.f.=0.5
- For the strongest coupling studied (am₀=0.15, g₀=1.5) giving gR=1.25, the vacuum two-loop deviation Δ₂ was 2.3% (0.6% uncertainty) with χ²/d.o.f.=10.3
- For this same strong coupling at T/m=1.38 (Nτ=2), Δ₂ grew to 22.2% (0.7% uncertainty) with χ²/d.o.f.=1262.3, and the two-loop prediction was worse than the one-loop prediction (Δ₁=8.7%)
- For the 64³×4 lattice with (am₀=0.0625, g₀=1.0) giving gR=0.91 at T/m=0.90, Δ₂ was 7.6% (0.2% uncertainty) with χ²/d.o.f.=588.9, again worse than the one-loop prediction (Δ₁=4.6%)

## Context

This work extends the formal arguments of Weldon (1998, 2001) and Landsman (1988) regarding the inconsistency of finite-temperature perturbation theory due to the Narnhofer-Requardt-Thirring theorem, which rules out real dispersion relations for interacting thermal states. The authors provide explicit numerical evidence that these issues quantitatively affect perturbative predictions even at moderate temperatures where infrared divergences are absent, contradicting the expectation that resummation techniques (as used by Braaten and Nieto, 1994, or Kajantie et al., 2002) would suffice. The results support the alternative framework proposed by Bros, Buchholz, and collaborators (1992-2003) involving distinct thermoparticle excitations as a potential resolution, and demonstrate that lattice simulations can serve as a controlled testing ground for these non-perturbative thermal effects.
