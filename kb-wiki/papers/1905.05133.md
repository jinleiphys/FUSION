# High-temperature expansion of the viscosity in interacting quantum gases

> We compute the frequency-dependent shear and bulk viscosity spectral functions of an interacting Fermi gas in a quantum virial expansion up to second quadratic order in the fugacity parameter $z=e^{βμ}$, which is small at high temperatures. Calculations are carried out using a diagrammatic finite-temperature field-theoretic framework, in which the analytic continuation from Matsubara to real frequencies is carried out in closed analytic form. Besides a possible zero-frequency Drude peak, our results for the spectral functions show a broad continuous spectrum at all frequencies with an additional bound-state contribution for frequencies larger than the dimer-breaking energy. Our results are consistent with various sum rules and universal high-frequency tails. In the low-frequency limit, the shear viscosity spectral function is recast as a collision integral, which reproduces known results for the static shear viscosity from kinetic theory. Our findings for the static bulk viscosity of a Fermi gas near unitarity, however, show a nonanalytic dependence on the scattering length, at variance with kinetic theory.

## Key claim

The paper computes the frequency-dependent shear and bulk viscosity spectral functions of an interacting Fermi gas using a quantum virial expansion up to second order in the fugacity \(z = e^{\beta\mu}\), which is valid only when \(z \ll 1\) (the high-temperature, nondegenerate regime). The static bulk viscosity near unitarity shows a nonanalytic dependence on the scattering length, which is at variance with kinetic theory predictions.

## Method

The authors use a diagrammatic finite-temperature field-theoretic framework in imaginary time, expanding the single-particle Green's function in powers of the fugacity \(z\). They compute the retarded stress tensor correlation function (for shear viscosity) and the contact correlation function (for bulk viscosity) up to \(\mathcal{O}(z^2)\), performing the analytic continuation from Matsubara to real frequencies in closed analytic form using Laplace transforms and Bromwich integrals. The calculation involves evaluating self-energy, Maki-Thompson, and Aslamazov-Larkin diagrams, with the two-particle scattering matrix \(T_2\) encoding the interaction effects.

## Key numbers

- The static shear viscosity from the virial expansion agrees with kinetic theory results; the static bulk viscosity near unitarity in 3D is given by \(\lim_{a \to -\infty} \frac{\lambda_T^3}{\hbar z^2} \zeta |_{\omega\to 0} = \dfrac{\sqrt{2} }{9 \pi^2} \biggl(\frac{\lambda_T}{a}\biggr)^2 \Bigl(\ln \dfrac{2 \pi e^{-\gamma_E} a^2}{\lambda_T^2} - 1\Bigr)\), where \(\lambda_T\) is the thermal wavelength, \(a\) the scattering length, and \(\gamma_E\) the Euler-Mascheroni constant.
- The shear viscosity spectral function at low frequencies diverges as \(\lim_{\omega \to 0} \eta(\omega) \sim 1/\omega^2\) from the continuum part.
- The bulk viscosity spectral function saturates to a finite constant value at low frequencies (from the continuum part), with an additional bound-state contribution above the dimer-breaking energy when two-particle bound states exist.
- The high-frequency tails of both spectral functions decay as \(\eta(\omega), \zeta(\omega) \stackrel{\omega\to\infty}{\longrightarrow} C \omega^{-\frac{4-d}{2}}\), with magnitude set by the Tan contact parameter \(C\).

## Context

This work extends the quantum virial expansion method, previously applied to thermodynamic quantities and spectral functions by Kaplan (2011) and Leyronas (2011), to transport coefficients. It provides a first-principles microscopic calculation of viscosity spectral functions that is consistent with sum rules and universal high-frequency tails from Taylor (2010) and Hofmann (2011). The results enable direct comparison with kinetic theory calculations by Dusling (2013) and with experimental measurements of the dynamic structure factor via Bragg spectroscopy, as well as predictions for energy absorption from oscillating scattering length modulations.
