# Effects of electrons on nuclear clock transition frequency in $^{229}$Th ions

> We perform calculations of the energy shift of the nuclear clock transition frequency $^{229}$Th as a function of the number of electrons in Th ion. We demonstrate that the dependence of the nuclear frequency on electron configuration is significant. E.g., removing one electron from the atom leads to relative shift of the nuclear frequency $\sim 10^{-7}$, which is twelve orders of magnitude larger than expected relative uncertainty of the nuclear clock transition frequency ($\sim 10^{-19}$). This leads to difference of the nuclear clock frequencies in Th~IV, Th~III, Th~II and Th~I. The relative change of the nuclear frequency between neutral Th and its bare nucleus is 1\%. We also calculate the field shift constants for isotopic and isomeric shifts of atomic electron transitions in Th ions.

# Effects of electrons on nuclear clock transition frequency in $^{229}$Th ions

## Key claim

The nuclear clock transition frequency in $^{229}$Th depends significantly on the number of bound electrons, with relative shifts between different ionization states reaching $10^{-7}$ to $10^{-6}$, which is twelve orders of magnitude larger than the projected relative uncertainty of the nuclear clock ($10^{-19}$). This means the nuclear transition frequency is intrinsically different in Th IV, Th III, Th II, and Th I, rather than being a single universal value.

## Method

The authors used a combination of the single-double coupled cluster plus configuration interaction (SD+CI) method to obtain many-electron wave functions and the random-phase approximation (RPA) to compute the effective field shift operator for valence electrons in Th IV, Th III, Th II, and Th I. The field shift constants $F_a$ for each electronic state were then combined with the known change in nuclear root-mean-square radius between the ground and isomeric nuclear states, $\delta \langle r^2 \rangle^{229m,229}$, to calculate the electronic shift of the nuclear transition frequency for each ion.

## Key numbers

- Removing one electron from neutral Th leads to a relative shift of the nuclear frequency of approximately $10^{-7}$.
- The relative change of the nuclear frequency between neutral Th and its bare nucleus is 1%.
- The difference in nuclear frequency between Th II and Th III is 1.3 GHz ($5.6 \times 10^{-6}$ eV), corresponding to a relative shift of $6.4 \times 10^{-7}$.
- The difference in nuclear frequency between Th I and Th II is 0.10 GHz ($4.3 \times 10^{-7}$ eV), corresponding to a relative shift of $5.0 \times 10^{-8}$.
- The difference in nuclear frequency between Th III and Th IV is -0.15 GHz ($-6.5 \times 10^{-7}$ eV), corresponding to a relative shift of $-7.3 \times 10^{-8}$.
- The field shift constant for the ground state of Th IV ($5f$, $^2$F$^o_{5/2}$) is -55.0 GHz/fm$^2$.
- The field shift constant for the ground state of Th III ($5f6d$, $^3$H$^o_4$) is -68.0 GHz/fm$^2$.
- The field shift constant for the ground state of Th II ($6d^27s$, $^2$D$_{3/2}$) is 49.6 GHz/fm$^2$.
- The field shift constant for the ground state of Th I ($6d^27s^2$, $^3$F$_2$) is 58.6 GHz/fm$^2$.
- The total electronic shift from all core electrons between neutral Th and bare nucleus is $2.07 \times 10^7$ MHz = $8.57 \times 10^{-2}$ eV.
- The authors derive a revised value for the isotopic field shift $\delta \langle r^2 \rangle^{232,229}$ = 0.320(15) fm$^2$, and a revised isomeric shift $\delta \langle r^2 \rangle^{229m,229}$ = 0.0112(13) fm$^2$.

## Context

This work extends prior calculations of field shift constants for Th ions by Safronova et al. (2018, cited as "dr") and provides improved values that yield more consistent results for the isotopic shift $\delta \langle r^2 \rangle^{232,229}$ when compared with experimental data from Marinova (2020). The results are critical for the design of nuclear clocks based on $^{229}$Th, whether using Th IV, Th III, Th II, Th I, or solid-state systems, as each system will have a different nuclear transition frequency that must be accounted for in clock operation and comparisons between different clock realizations.
