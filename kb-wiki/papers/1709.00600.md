# Isotope shift, non-linearity of King plots and the search for new particles

> We derive a mean-field relativistic formula for the isotope shift of an electronic energy level for arbitrary angular momentum; we then use it to predict the spectra of superheavy metastable neutron-rich isotopes belonging to the hypothetical island of stability. Our results may be applied to the search for superheavy atoms in astrophysical spectra using the known values of the transition frequencies for the neutron deficient isotopes produced in the laboratory. An example of a relevant astrophysical system may be the spectra of the Przybylski's star where superheavy elements up to Z=99 have been possibly identified. In addition, it has been recently suggested to use the measurements of King plot non-linearity in a search for hypothetical new light bosons. On the other hand, one can find the non-linear corrections to the King-plot arising already in the Standard Model framework. We investigate contributions to the non-linearity arising from relativistic effects in the isotope field-shift, the nuclear polarizability and many-body effects. It is found that the nuclear polarizability contribution can lead to the significant deviation of the King plot from linearity. Therefore, the measurements of the non-linearity of King plots may be applied to obtain the nuclear polarizability change between individual isotopes. We then proceed with providing a rough analytical estimate of the non-linearity arising solely from the effect of a hypothetical scalar boson. Our predictions give theoretical limitations on the sensitivity of the search for new interactions and should help to identify the most suitable atoms for corresponding experiments.

## Key claim
The nuclear polarizability contribution can produce significant deviation of the King plot from linearity within the Standard Model, meaning that measurements of King plot non-linearity must account for this effect before attributing any observed non-linearity to hypothetical new bosons. The authors provide a rough analytical estimate of the non-linearity arising from a hypothetical scalar boson, giving theoretical limitations on the sensitivity of such searches.

## Method
The authors derive a relativistic mean-field formula for the isotope field shift in many-electron atoms for arbitrary electron angular momentum, using first-order perturbation theory with a uniformly charged spherical nucleus and matching the electron density inside the nucleus to Coulomb wave functions at the nuclear surface. They then apply this formula to estimate isotope shifts for superheavy elements near the hypothetical island of stability (neutron number N=184) and to calculate King plot non-linearity contributions from relativistic effects in the field shift, nuclear polarizability, and many-body effects. The non-linearity is quantified by comparing three isotope pairs and expanding the difference between modified frequencies to identify conditions under which non-linearity vanishes.

## Context
This work extends the Racah-Rosenthal-Breit formula for isotope field shift, which was derived only for s-wave electrons and is not valid in heavy atoms where relativistic effects are important. The authors provide a relativistic generalization valid for arbitrary angular momentum, building on earlier work by Shabaev (1993) on finite nuclear size corrections in one-electron atoms. The results enable two applications: predicting spectra of superheavy neutron-rich isotopes for astrophysical searches (such as in Przybylski's star, where elements up to Z=99 have been possibly identified by Gopka (2008)), and constraining the interpretation of King plot non-linearity measurements that have been proposed by Berengut (2017) as a probe of new light bosons. The finding that nuclear polarizability produces significant Standard Model non-linearity means that future experiments must disentangle this effect from any beyond-Standard-Model signal, and the authors suggest that King plot measurements could instead be used to extract nuclear polarizability changes between isotopes.
