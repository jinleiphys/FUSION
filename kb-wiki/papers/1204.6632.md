# Identity Method for the Determination of the Moments of Multiplicity Distributions

> Recently the identity method was proposed to calculate second moments of the multiplicity distributions from event-by-event measurements in the presence of the effects of incomplete particle identification. In this paper the method is extended for higher moments. The moments of smeared multiplicity distributions are calculated using single-particle identity variables. The problem of finding the moments of the multiplicity distributions is reduced to solving of a system of linear equations.

## Key claim

The identity method, previously limited to second moments of multiplicity distributions, can be extended to calculate third and higher moments by reducing the problem to solving a system of linear equations, provided that all lower-order moments have already been determined.

## Method

The authors define single-particle identity variables w_j(x) as the ratio of the inclusive distribution ¤ü_j(x) for particle species j to the total inclusive distribution ¤ü(x) summed over all species. Event quantities W_j are constructed by summing w_j(x) over all particles in an event. The moments of the W-quantities are expressed as linear combinations of the unknown moments of the true multiplicity distributions N_j, with coefficients that depend only on the experimentally measurable ¤ü_j(x) functions. The method is tested on simulated data with three particle species (protons, pions, kaons) using Poisson-distributed multiplicities, including a case with strong correlations where the number of kaons is set equal to the number of protons in each event.

## Context

This work extends the identity method originally proposed by Gazdzicki (2011) for two particle species and further developed by Gorenstein (2011) for second moments of multiple species. The ability to measure third and higher moments of multiplicity distributions is motivated by the search for the QCD critical point in nucleus-nucleus collisions, as emphasized by Stephanov (2009) and other authors. The method enables experimental extraction of higher-order fluctuation observables from data affected by incomplete particle identification, which is essential for ongoing measurements at CERN SPS and BNL RHIC.
