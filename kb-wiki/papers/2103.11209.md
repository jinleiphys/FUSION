# Evolution of Primordial Neutrino Helicities in Cosmic Gravitational Inhomogeneities

> Relic neutrinos from the Big Bang decoupled from the hot plasma predominantly in helicity eigenstates. Their subsequent propagation through gravitational inhomogeneities of the Universe alters the helicities of both Dirac and Majorana neutrinos, thus providing an independent probe of the evolving universe. We determine here the probability that relic neutrinos flip their helicity, in terms of the spectrum of density inhomogeneities measured in the Cosmic Microwave Background. As we find, for Dirac neutrinos the gravitational helicity modifications are intermediate between the effects of magnetic fields if the neutrino magnetic moment is of the magnitude predicted in the Standard Model and the much larger effects if the magnetic moment is of the scale consistent with the excess of low energy electron events seen by the XENON1T experiment. We give succinct derivations, within general relativity, of the semi-classical response of a spinning particle to a weak gravitational field in an expanding universe, and estimate the helicity modifications of neutrinos emitted by the Sun caused by the Sun's gravity.

## Key claim

Gravitational inhomogeneities in the Universe alter the helicities of both Dirac and Majorana relic neutrinos, with the effect for Dirac neutrinos being intermediate between the helicity modifications from a Standard Model magnetic moment and those from a magnetic moment large enough to explain the XENON1T low-energy electron excess. The probability of helicity flip is expressed in terms of the spectrum of density inhomogeneities measured in the Cosmic Microwave Background.

## Method

The authors derive semiclassical equations from general relativity for the bending of a spinning particle's momentum and spin in a weak gravitational potential in an expanding Friedmann-Robertson-Walker universe. They integrate the resulting angular deflections over the matter-dominated and radiation-dominated eras, using the Planck collaboration power spectrum of density fluctuations to compute the mean squared bending angles of neutrino momentum, spin, and spin relative to momentum as functions of neutrino mass. They also compute the average helicity rotation of solar neutrinos by integrating over the Sun's mass distribution and neutrino emissivity profile from standard solar models.

## Key numbers

- Present neutrino temperature: \(T_{\nu 0} = 1.945 \pm 0.001\) K = \((1.676 \pm 0.001) \times 10^{-4}\) eV
- Mean squared bending angle of neutrino momentum in matter-dominated era: \(\langle (\Delta\theta_p)^2\rangle \simeq 2.2 \times 10^{-6}\), corresponding to \(\sim 5.1\) arcmin
- For a presently relativistic neutrino with mass less than \(10^{-4}\) eV, the helicity flipping probability is \(\sim 6 \times 10^{-7}\)
- For the heaviest neutrino (mass at least 50 meV), the population of flipped helicities approaches one in \(10^5\)
- Average helicity rotation angle for solar neutrinos: \(\langle \theta \rangle = -1.54 \, GM/(\gamma v^2 R)\) from numerical integration using solar model distributions
- Scale of spin bending of a non-relativistic thermal neutrino of mass \(m_\nu = 10^{-2}\) eV by density fluctuations is comparable to that from a galactic magnetic field \(\sim 10\ \mu\)G if the neutrino magnetic moment is \(\mu_\nu \sim 5 \times 10^{-18}\)
- Standard Model estimate of neutrino magnetic moment: \(\sim 3 \times 10^{-21} m_{-2} \mu_B\) where \(m_{-2}\) is neutrino mass in units of \(10^{-2}\) eV
- Magnetic moment that would explain XENON1T excess: \(1.4-2.9 \times 10^{-11} \mu_B\)

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier study of magnetic helicity modification of relic neutrinos (Baym and Peng, 2020) by calculating the gravitational contribution, which acts equally on Dirac and Majorana neutrinos unlike magnetic effects. The gravitational lensing of the Cosmic Neutrino Background is analogous to CMB lensing studied by Planck and others (e.g., Lewis and Challinor, 2006), but neutrino lensing is not restricted by reionization effects. The results enable estimates of how gravitational helicity flipping could affect detection rates in experiments such as PTOLEMY, which uses inverse tritium beta decay to capture relic neutrinos, though the predicted flipping probabilities are too small for current experiments.
