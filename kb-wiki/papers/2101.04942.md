# A Demonstration of Hadron Mass Origin from QCD Trace Anomaly

> Quantum chromodynamics (QCD) claims that the major source of the nucleon invariant mass is not the Higgs mechanism but the trace anomaly in QCD energy momentum tensor. Although experimental and theoretical results support such conclusion, a direct demonstration is still absent. We present the first Lattice QCD calculation of the quark and gluon trace anomaly contributions to the hadron masses, using the overlap fermion on the 2+1 flavor dynamical Domain wall quark ensemble at $m_π=340$ MeV and lattice spacing $a=$0.1105 fm. The result shows that the gluon trace anomaly contributes to most of the nucleon mass, and the contribution in the pion state is smaller than that in others nearly by a factor $\sim$10 since the gluon trace anomaly density inside pion is different from the other hadrons and the magnitude is much smaller. The gluon trace anomaly coefficient $β/g^3=-0.056(6)$ we obtained is consistent with its regularization independent leading order value $(-11+\frac{2N_f}{3})/(4π)^2$ perfectly.

# A Demonstration of Hadron Mass Origin from QCD Trace Anomaly

## Key claim

The authors present the first direct lattice QCD verification that the gluon trace anomaly contributes most of the nucleon mass, while in the pion state the gluon trace anomaly contribution is smaller by a factor of approximately 10. This demonstration relies on a partially quenched setup at a single lattice spacing and pion mass of 340 MeV, with the anomalous dimensions determined non-perturbatively from the pseudoscalar and vector meson states at one valence quark mass.

## Method

The calculation uses a 2+1 flavor Domain-wall fermion ensemble from the RBC collaboration on a 24^3 x 64 lattice with lattice spacing a = 0.1105 fm and pion mass m_pi = 340 MeV. Overlap fermions are used for the valence quarks to avoid chiral symmetry breaking artifacts in the trace of the energy-momentum tensor. The authors perform a joint correlated fit of two-point and summed three-point correlation functions to extract the hadron mass M_H, the quark mass term <H_m>_H, and the gluon action term <F^2>_H simultaneously for nucleon, pseudoscalar, and vector meson states at five valence quark masses. The bare anomalous dimensions gamma_m and beta/g^3 are solved from the trace anomaly sum rule using the pseudoscalar and vector meson results at the heaviest valence quark mass.

## Key numbers

- Lattice spacing a = 0.1105(3) fm, pion mass m_pi = 340 MeV, kaon mass m_K = 593 MeV on a 24^3 x 64 ensemble with 203 configurations
- Bare anomalous dimension gamma_m = 0.38(3) obtained from solving the sum rule for PS and V mesons at m_v a = 0.3
- Bare gluon trace anomaly coefficient beta/g^3 = -0.056(6), consistent with the regularization independent leading order value (-11 + 2N_f/3)/(4pi)^2 = -0.057(7)
- Gluon trace anomaly contributes approximately 800 MeV to the nucleon and rho meson mass, but only about 100 MeV (approximately 30%) to the pion mass at the unitary point with 340 MeV pion mass
- Estimated gluon trace anomaly contribution in the physical pion state: 43(2) MeV, using the GMOR relation and Feynman-Hellman theorem
- Estimated gluon trace anomaly contribution in the nucleon at physical light quark mass: 816(10) MeV, considering three light quark contributions from prior work

## Context

This work provides the first direct lattice QCD demonstration of the trace anomaly mechanism for hadron mass generation, which was proposed over 40 years ago based on the work of Shifman (1978) and Ji (1994, 1995). The result confirms that the gluon trace anomaly dominates the nucleon mass, consistent with indirect extractions from SU(3) flavor breaking in baryons by Yang (2015, 2018). The finding that the gluon trace anomaly density inside the pion is much smaller and even negative at the center opens a new avenue for understanding the difference between the pion and other hadrons. The non-perturbative determination of gamma_m and beta/g^3 at a given lattice spacing enables future calculations of the QCD equation of state at finite temperature, proton gravitational form factors, and the relation between trace anomaly and dynamically generated parton masses.
