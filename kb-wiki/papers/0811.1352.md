# Femtoscopically Probing the Freeze-out Configuration in Heavy Ion Collisions

> Two-particle femtoscopy reveals the space-time substructure of the freeze-out configuration from heavy ion collisions. Detailed fingerprints of bulk collectivity are evident in space-momentum correlations, which have been systematically measured as a function of particle type, three-momentum, and collision conditions. A clear scenario, dominated by hydrodynamic-type flow emerges. Reproducing the strength and features of the femtoscopic signals in models involves important physical quantities like the Equation of State, as well as less fundamental technical details. An interesting approximate "factorization" in the measured systematics suggests that the overall physical freeze-out scale is set by final state chemistry, but the kinematic substructure is largely universal. Referring to previous results from hadron and lepton collisions, we point to the importance of determining whether these "universal" trends persist from the largest to the smallest systems. We review theoretical expectations for heavy ion femtoscopy at the LHC, and point to directions needing further theory and experimental work at RHIC and the LHC.

# Femtoscopically Probing the Freeze-out Configuration in Heavy Ion Collisions

## Key claim

Two-particle femtoscopy reveals that the space-time substructure of the freeze-out configuration in heavy ion collisions is dominated by hydrodynamic-type flow, but reproducing the full set of femtoscopic signals requires incorporating multiple physical effects, including a stiff equation of state, pre-equilibrium transverse acceleration, shear viscosity, and improved modeling of non-equilibrium hadronic chemistry, none of which alone can account for the initial ~50% discrepancy in the $R_{\rm out}/R_{\rm side}$ ratio.

## Method

The authors review systematic measurements of Gaussian femtoscopic radii ($R_{\rm out}$, $R_{\rm side}$, $R_{\rm long}$) as functions of particle type, transverse momentum, rapidity, azimuthal angle relative to the reaction plane, and collision conditions (energy, centrality, system size) from AGS, SPS, and RHIC experiments. They compare these data to predictions from hydrodynamic models, cascade models, and blast-wave parameterizations, progressively incorporating pre-thermal acceleration, realistic equations of state from lattice QCD, shear and bulk viscosity, longitudinal acceleration, and improved relative wave functions to assess which features are required to reproduce the measured source radii.

## Key numbers

- Final state entropy of ~4450 ± 10% units for central 130A GeV Au+Au collisions at RHIC, inferred from combining femtoscopy and spectra
- Energy densities at RHIC at 1 fm/c are in the neighborhood of 10 GeV/fm³, roughly a factor of 5-10 higher than where one might expect a minimum in $P/\epsilon$
- The bag model equation of state predicted $P/\epsilon$ ≈ 3/40 at energy densities near 2 GeV/fm³, with dissolution times >50 fm/c, but actual dissolution times were closer to 10 fm/c
- Incorporating a finite speed of sound near $T_c$ from lattice calculations lowers the $R_{\rm out}/R_{\rm side}$ ratio at the 10% level
- Improved modeling of non-equilibrium hadronic chemistry lowers $R_{\rm out}/R_{\rm side}$ by ~10%
- Shear viscosity lowers $R_{\rm out}/R_{\rm side}$ ratios by 10-15%
- Pre-equilibrium transverse acceleration lowers $R_{\rm out}/R_{\rm side}$ by ~10%
- More compact fireballs from collisional scaling lower $R_{\rm out}/R_{\rm side}$ by 5-10%
- Parametric blast-wave analyses may underestimate true collision lifetimes by 5-10% due to neglecting longitudinal acceleration
- STAR reported the offset between pions and kaons from non-identical particle correlations to be between 4 and 7.5 fm, consistent with blast wave expectations of 7 fm
- Freeze-out temperatures of order 120 MeV and flow velocities on order of 0.5c are indicated by the kinematic dependences of the source radii
- $R_{\rm long}$ scales as $m_T^{-\alpha}$ where $\alpha$ is slightly larger than 0.5

## Context

This review extends the femtoscopic program from earlier SPS measurements by NA35 (1988) and AGS experiments, which had expected long-lived sources from a first-order phase transition but found dissolution times closer to 10 fm/c rather than the >50 fm/c predicted by bag model equations of state. The work contradicts early RHIC-era hydrodynamic models (Soff 2000, Kolb 2003) that predicted $R_{\rm out}/R_{\rm side}$ ≥ 1.5 and emission times of 15-20 fm/c, while cascade models (Lin 2002, Humanic 2002) provided better descriptions. The review points to the need for more systematic azimuthally-sensitive femtoscopy and non-identical particle correlations at RHIC and the LHC, and questions whether the observed "universal" scaling of source sizes with charged-particle multiplicity persists from the largest to the smallest collision systems, including p+p and d+Au collisions.
