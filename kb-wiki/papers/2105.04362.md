# Incorporating the Coulomb potential into a finite, unitary perturbation theory

> We have constructed a perturbation theory to treat interactions that can include the Coulomb interaction, describing a physical problem that is often encountered in nuclear physics. The Coulomb part is not treated perturbatively; the exact solutions are employed. The method is an extension of the results presented in Hoffmann (2021 J. Math. Phys. 62 032105). It is designed to calculate phase shifts directly rather than the full form of the wavefunctions in position space. We present formulas that allow calculation of the phase shifts to second order in the perturbation. The phase shift results to second order, for a short-range potential, were compared with the exact solution, where we found an error of third order in the coupling strength. A different model, meant as a simple approximation of nuclear scattering of a proton on Helium-4 and including a Coulomb potential and a spherical well, was constructed to test the theory. The wavepacket scattering formalism of Hoffmann (2017 J. Phy. B: At. Mol. Opt. Phys 50 215302), known to give everywhere finite results, was employed. We found physically acceptable results and a cross section of the correct order of magnitude.

## Key claim

The paper constructs a finite, unitary perturbation theory that can incorporate the Coulomb potential by treating it exactly (not perturbatively) and perturbing only around the known Coulomb solutions, thereby avoiding the divergences that arise when the Coulomb potential itself is treated perturbatively. The method is designed to calculate phase shifts directly rather than full wavefunctions, and the central result is that the second-order contributions remain finite for perturbing potentials that fall off faster than \(1/r\).

## Method

The author extends a previously developed unitary perturbation theory (Hoffmann 2021, J. Math. Phys. 62 032105) from \(s\)-wave (\(l=0\)) to general angular momentum \(l\), relating free and interacting momentum eigenvectors by a unitary transformation whose generator is expanded as a power series in a dimensionless coupling constant. For the Coulomb-nuclear problem, the exact Coulomb solutions (regular Coulomb wavefunctions) are used as the unperturbed basis, and the nuclear potential is treated as the perturbation; formulas for the first- and second-order phase shift contributions are derived in terms of principal-part integrals over matrix elements of the perturbing potential between Coulomb wavefunctions. The method is tested on an exactly solvable spherical barrier/well model and then applied to a model of proton-\(^4\)He scattering using a spherical well plus Coulomb potential, with cross sections computed via the wavepacket scattering formalism of Hoffmann (2017, J. Phys. B: At. Mol. Opt. Phys. 50 215302).

## Key numbers

* For the spherical barrier/well test with \(l=0\) and \(\kappa = pR = 10\), the second-order approximation shows an error of \(\mathcal{O}(\eta^3)\) in the coupling strength \(\eta\).
* For the proton-\(^4\)He model: reduced mass \(\mu = 749\ \mathrm{MeV}\), incident proton momentum \(p = 237\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (energy \(E = 29.5\ \mathrm{MeV}\)), velocity \(\beta = v/c = 0.302\), nuclear well depth \(V_0 = -30.2\ \mathrm{MeV}\), nuclear radius \(R = 2.06\ \mathrm{fm}\), \(\kappa = pR = 2.48\), Coulomb coupling \(\eta^C = 0.0461\), and nuclear coupling \(\eta^+ = -1\).
* The first-order phase shift for \(l=0\) in the proton-\(^4\)He model is \(\delta_0^{(+,1)} = 1.230\), the second-order contribution is \(\delta_0^{(+,2)} = -0.316\), and the sum is \(0.914\) compared to the exact nuclear-only phase shift of \(0.805\).
* The differential cross section peak for the Coulomb-plus-nuclear model is approximately \(0.07\ \mathrm{barn}\); scaling by \((237/408)^2\) gives \(24\ \mathrm{mb}\), within the range of experimental measurements by Votta et al. (1974) at \(85\ \mathrm{MeV}\).

## Context

This work extends the unitary perturbation theory of Hoffmann (2021, J. Math. Phys.) from \(s\)-wave to general angular momentum and, as its central contribution, adapts the method to include the Coulomb potential exactly, solving the Coulomb-nuclear interference problem that is fundamental in nuclear scattering. The approach contrasts with numerical integration methods (Vigo-Aguiar 2005, Simos 1997) that obtain full wavefunctions and with the WKB method used by Bethe (1958) for the Coulomb-nuclear phase; the present method is complementary, offering fast direct phase-shift calculation for perturbative systems. The results enable finite, physically realistic cross-section calculations for charged-particle scattering, as demonstrated by the proton-\(^4\)He model, and the author intends to extend the method to the relativistic regime in future work.
