# Relativistic mean-field models of neutron-rich matter

> The aim of this chapter, focused on relativistic mean-field models and part of the Encyclopedia of Nuclear Physics, is to provide an introductory, self-contained discussion accessible to a broad audience, including advanced undergraduate students. The chapter surveys the fundamental ideas, assumptions, and theoretical framework underlying relativistic mean-field models, and illustrates their wide range of applications across nuclear science. Particular emphasis is placed on the central role that these models play in the construction of equations of state for strongly interacting matter, as well as on the intimate connections between nuclear experiments, astrophysical observations, and theoretical modeling. In this context, relativistic mean-field theory is shown to provide a unified description of bulk nuclear properties and dense neutron-rich matter, enabling the interpretation of the remarkable structural and observational properties of neutron stars in the emerging era of multi-messenger astronomy.

## Key claim

Relativistic mean-field (RMF) models provide a unified description of bulk nuclear properties and dense neutron-rich matter, enabling the interpretation of neutron-star structure in the era of multi-messenger astronomy, but the chapter presents this as a pedagogical survey of the framework rather than a claim of a specific new result.

## Method

The chapter develops the formalism of relativistic mean-field theory starting from a relativistic free Fermi gas, first for one-component systems and then for two-component systems with a neutron-proton asymmetry parameter. It introduces the Walecka (sigma-omega) model, where nucleons interact via exchange of a scalar sigma meson providing intermediate-range attraction and a vector omega meson providing short-range repulsion, with meson fields treated in the mean-field approximation. The framework is extended to neutron-star matter by incorporating charge neutrality and beta equilibrium conditions, which determine the equilibrium proton fraction at each density.

## Key numbers

- At high densities in chemical equilibrium, the proton-to-neutron ratio approaches 1/8, corresponding to a proton fraction Y_p = 1/9 and neutron-proton asymmetry alpha = 7/9.
- The fraction of thermally excited neutrons in a neutron-star core is approximately N_ex/N ~ T/T_F ~ 10^{-3}, with thermal contributions to energy density and pressure scaling as (T/T_F)^2 ~ 10^{-6}.
- The millisecond pulsar PSR J0740+6620 has a measured mass of M = 2.08 +/- 0.07 solar masses, currently among the most massive and precisely measured neutron stars.
- The Chandrasekhar mass for white-dwarf collapse is M_ch ~ 1.4 solar masses.
- In the nonrelativistic limit (xF << 1), the symmetry energy S(n) scales as (1/6) xF^2; in the ultra-relativistic limit (xF >> 1), it scales as (1/6) xF.

## Context

This chapter is part of the Encyclopedia of Nuclear Physics and provides an introductory survey of relativistic mean-field models for a broad audience including advanced undergraduates. It extends the foundational work of Walecka (1974) and earlier relativistic approaches by Johnson and Teller (1955), Duerr (1956), and Miller and Green (1972), while connecting to modern constraints from the GW170817 binary neutron star merger detected by LIGO-Virgo, pulsar timing observations of PSR J0740+6620 by Cromartie (2019) and Fonseca (2021), and NICER pulse-profile modeling of neutron-star masses and radii by Riley (2019, 2021), Miller (2019, 2021), and Choudhury (2024). The chapter enables students and researchers to understand how RMF models are calibrated against both laboratory data and astrophysical observations to construct equations of state for neutron-star matter.
