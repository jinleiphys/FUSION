# Twin Stars within the SU(3) Chiral Quark-Meson Model

> We present new stable solutions of the Tolman Oppenheimer Volkoff equations for quark stars using a quark matter equation of state based on the SU(3) Quark-Meson model that exhibits the onset of the chiral phase transition. These new solutions appear as two stable branches in the mass-radius relation allowing for so called twin stars, i.e. two stable quark star solutions with the same mass, but distinctly different radii. We find solutions which are compatible with causality, the stability conditions of dense matter, the astrophysical constraints of the rotation of the millisecond pulsar PSR J1748-2446ad and the 2$M_{\odot}$ pulsar mass constraint.

## Key claim

The paper claims that twin star solutions (two stable quark stars with the same mass but different radii) can arise from a single equation of state within the SU(3) Chiral Quark-Meson model, provided the vacuum pressure is kept below approximately 100 MeV and the vector coupling and sigma meson mass are tuned appropriately; the chiral phase transition in this model is a crossover rather than a first-order transition, in contrast to typical hybrid star twin star scenarios.

## Method

The authors solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations using an equation of state derived from the SU(3) Chiral Quark-Meson model in the mean-field approximation at zero temperature. They scan the parameter space of the model by varying the vacuum pressure B^(1/4), the vector coupling constant g_omega, and the sigma meson mass m_sigma, while fixing the constituent quark mass at 300 MeV. For each parameter set, they compute the mass-radius relation and identify stable branches, then check compatibility with causality, the stability conditions of dense matter, the 2 solar mass pulsar constraint, and the rotation limit from the millisecond pulsar PSR J1748-2446ad.

## Key numbers

- For m_sigma = 600 MeV, g_omega = 4, and B^(1/4) = 37.5 MeV: first maximum mass 2.99 solar masses at radius 28.46 km; second maximum mass 2.56 solar masses at radius 19.18 km
- For m_sigma = 600 MeV, g_omega = 4, and B^(1/4) = 52.5 MeV: first maximum mass 2.46 solar masses at radius 23.54 km; second maximum mass 2.46 solar masses at radius 15.94 km
- For m_sigma = 600 MeV, g_omega = 4, and B^(1/4) = 65 MeV: first maximum mass 1.89 solar masses at radius 19.44 km; second maximum mass 2.38 solar masses at radius 14.07 km
- For m_sigma = 600 MeV, g_omega = 4, and B^(1/4) = 100 MeV: only one maximum mass of 2.12 solar masses at radius 10.83 km
- Twin star solutions for m_sigma = 600 MeV require B^(1/4) between approximately 30 and 70 MeV and g_omega greater than approximately 2 to satisfy the 2 solar mass constraint
- Radii of allowed twin star configurations lie between approximately 12 km and 22 km
- For m_sigma = 800 MeV, twin stars reaching 2 solar masses are only possible for g_omega between approximately 1 and 2 and B^(1/4) below approximately 20 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous studies of twin stars that typically required two separate equations of state merged with a first-order phase transition, as in the hybrid star models of Glendenning (1992) and Benic et al. (2014). The authors show that a single quark matter equation of state with a crossover chiral phase transition can produce twin stars, supporting the robustness of the twin star phenomenon found in Benic et al. (2014) and Alvarez-Castillo (2014). The results enable future observational tests with missions such as NICER, which could identify twin stars by measuring two stars with the same mass but different radii at kilometer precision.
