# Target normal single-spin asymmetry in inclusive electron-nucleon scattering with two-photon exchange: Analysis using $1/N_c$ expansion

> We calculate the target normal single-spin asymmetry caused by two-photon exchange in inclusive electron-nucleon scattering in the resonance region. Our analysis uses the $1/N_c$ expansion of low-energy QCD and combines $N$ and $Δ$ intermediate and final states using the contracted $SU(4)$ spin-flavor symmetry. The normal spin asymmetry obtained in leading-order accuracy in $1/N_c$ has magnitude $\sim 10^{-2}$ and different sign in $ep$ and $en$ scattering. It can be measured in electron scattering at lab energies $\sim$ 0.5-1.5 GeV and provides a clean probe of two-photon exchange dynamics.

# Target normal single-spin asymmetry in inclusive electron-nucleon scattering with two-photon exchange: Analysis using $1/N_c$ expansion

## Key claim

The target normal single-spin asymmetry in inclusive electron-nucleon scattering, arising from two-photon exchange, has magnitude $\sim 10^{-2}$ and opposite sign for proton and neutron targets, but this leading-order result from the $1/N_c$ expansion is only a fair approximation at CM momenta $k \sim 0.3$–$0.6$ GeV and large scattering angles $\theta \sim \pi/2$, with an estimated accuracy of order $\sim 1/3$ pending computation of $1/N_c$ corrections.

## Method

The authors calculate the target normal single-spin asymmetry in inclusive electron-nucleon scattering in the resonance region using the $1/N_c$ expansion of low-energy QCD, which organizes hadron masses, couplings, and form factors based on scaling in the large-$N_c$ limit. The analysis employs the contracted $SU(4)$ spin-flavor symmetry to treat $N$ and $\Delta$ baryons as members of the ground-state multiplet, with the isovector magnetic current proportional to the spin-flavor generator $\hat{G}^{i3}$ providing the leading contribution. The spin-dependent cross section is obtained from the interference of one-photon exchange ($e^2$) and two-photon exchange ($e^4$) amplitudes, where the absorptive part of the $e^4$ amplitude is computed as the product of two $e^2$ amplitudes integrated over intermediate-state phase space, with sums over $N$ and $\Delta$ intermediate and final states performed using group-theoretical completeness relations.

## Key numbers

- The target normal single-spin asymmetry $A_N$ reaches values $\sim 10^{-2}$ at CM scattering angle $\theta \sim \pi/2$ and CM momentum $k \sim 0.6$ GeV.
- The spin-dependent cross section is proportional to the initial nucleon isospin $(2 I_{3\istate}) = \pm 1$, giving $A_N[ep] = -A_N[en]$.
- The asymmetry vanishes at $\theta = 0$ and $\theta = \pi$, where the normal vector to the scattering plane vanishes.
- The $1/N_c$ expansion is applied in the domain where initial and final CM momenta are $O(N_c^0)$, corresponding to $\sqrt{s} = O(N_c)$ and $\sqrt{s} - m = O(N_c^0)$, with final-state masses satisfying $m_X - m = O(N_c^{-1})$.
- The large-$N_c$ form factor $F(t)$ is matched to the physical nucleon isovector magnetic form factor $G_M^V(t) \equiv \frac{1}{2}[G_M^p(t) - G_M^n(t)]$ using the standard dipole form $(1 - t/0.71 \, \textrm{GeV}^2)^{-2}$.

## Context

This work extends the study of two-photon exchange effects in electron-nucleon scattering, which have been implicated in the proton form factor ratio discrepancy between Rosenbluth separation and polarization transfer methods at Jefferson Lab (Jefferson Lab Hall A, 1999; Guichon and Vanderhaeghen, 2003; Blunden, Melnitchouk, and Tjon, 2003). The calculation provides a theoretical framework for the target normal single-spin asymmetry in the resonance region, complementing deep-inelastic scattering measurements by HERMES (2009) on proton and by Katich et al. (2013) on $^3$He targets, and supporting planned measurements at Jefferson Lab (Grauvogel et al., 2021). The $1/N_c$ expansion method enables a consistent treatment of $N$ and $\Delta$ states and can be extended to include $N^\ast$ intermediate states and real photon emission in higher orders, as well as to explore the transition between the resonance and deep-inelastic scattering regions.
