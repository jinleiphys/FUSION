# Exploration of resonances by analytic continuation in the coupling constant

> The energy and the width of resonance states are determined by analytic continuation of bound-state energies as a function of the coupling constant (potential strength). The advantage of the method is that the existing techniques for calculation of bound states can be applied, without any modifications, to determine the position of resonances. Various numerical examples show the applicability of the method for three-body systems, including the excited states of the $^6$He and $^6$Li.

## Key claim

The analytic continuation in the coupling constant (ACCC) method can determine resonance energies and widths by solving bound-state problems for several potential strengths and using a Pade approximation, but the method requires bound-state energies accurate to typically four or more digits, especially near threshold, and the form of the singularity in the coupling constant for three-body systems is likely different from the two-body case, requiring higher-order Pade terms.

## Method

The Hamiltonian is written as H(lambda) = H1 + lambda H2, where H2 is the attractive part of the interaction. Bound-state energies are computed for several coupling constants lambda above the threshold value lambda0 (where E=0), using the stochastic variational method with a Gaussian basis. The square root of the energy k_l is parameterized as a Pade approximant in x = sqrt(lambda - lambda0), with coefficients determined by solving a system of linear equations from the bound-state energies; for lambda below lambda0, x becomes pure imaginary and k_l becomes complex, giving the resonance energy E and width Gamma via E - i Gamma/2 = k_l^2.

## Key numbers

- For the two-range Gaussian potential test case (hbar=c=m=1), ACCC results agreed with direct numerical integration (DNI) for resonances of different partial waves, including the 1S excited resonance state.
- For wide resonances with a reduced barrier (V0 = 0.25 instead of V0 = 2), ACCC and DNI agreed very well even when widths were twice the energies.
- For the three-boson system (hbar^2/m = 41.47 MeV fm^2), ACCC with M=3, 5, and 7 terms gave the same resonance position as complex scaling method (CSM) over a wide range of potential strength.
- For the 6He 2+ state (T=1): ACCC gave E = 0.73 MeV, Gamma = 0.07 MeV; CSM gave E = 0.74 MeV, Gamma = 0.06 MeV; experimental values are E = 0.82 +/- 0.025 MeV, Gamma = 0.133 +/- 0.020 MeV.
- For the 6Li 0+ state (T=1): ACCC gave E = 0.21 MeV, Gamma = 0.003 MeV; CSM gave E = 0.22 MeV, Gamma = 0.001 MeV; experimental E = -0.137 MeV.
- For the 6Li 2+ state (T=1): ACCC gave E = 1.61 MeV, Gamma = 0.27 MeV; CSM gave E = 1.59 MeV, Gamma = 0.28 MeV; experimental E = 1.696 +/- 0.015 MeV, Gamma = 0.54 +/- 0.020 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the ACCC method proposed by Kukulin et al. (1977, 1979), which had previously been applied only to simple two-body test cases such as the square-well potential and a macroscopic alpha-alpha system. The paper demonstrates ACCC for three-body systems including the Borromean nuclei 6He and 6Li, comparing results to the complex scaling method of Ho (1983) and to experimental data. The approach enables the study of resonances in microscopic cluster models without computing complex matrix elements or solving complex eigenvalue problems, and the authors suggest it could be combined with other microscopic methods like the shell model or Hartree-Fock method.
