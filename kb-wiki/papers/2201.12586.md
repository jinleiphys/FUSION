# Bayesian reconstruction of impact parameter distributions from two observables for intermediate energy heavy ion collisions

> To reconstruct the impact parameter distributions from the selected events sample or centrality, which is defined by two-observables, at intermediate energy heavy ion collisions, we extend the approach proposed by Das \textit{et al.} [Phys. Rev. C 97, 014905 (2018)], Rogly \textit{et al.} [Phys. Rev. C 98, 024902 (2018)], and Frankland \textit{et al.} [Phys. Rev. C 104, 034609 (2021)]. Based on deep investigations of the fluctuation mechanism, we found that the intrinsic fluctuations are mainly generated in the microscopic stochasticity of initialization and nucleon-nucleon collisions in the nonequilibrium process of heavy ion collisions, and this leads the observables to fluctuate with respect to impact parameter in a Gaussian form. In this work, the multiplicity of the charged particles and the total transverse momentum of the light charged particles are used simultaneously to model-independently reconstruct the impact parameter distributions for selected events or centrality based on the Bayesian method. For sorting the centrality with two observables, we propose to use the $K$-means clustering method (an unsupervised machine learning algorithm), which can automatically sort events when the class number is given. Furthermore, the reconstructed impact parameter distributions from data of the two observables can be used to learn the correlation between multiplicity and transverse momentum at different centralities, which may be useful for understanding the fragmentation mechanism.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a one-to-one correspondence between impact parameter and observables is impossible even with many observables, because intrinsic fluctuations from initialization stochasticity and nucleon-nucleon collisions destroy this correspondence; therefore the goal must be reconstruction of impact parameter distributions rather than unique determination.

## Method

The authors used the improved quantum molecular dynamics (ImQMD) model to simulate 112Sn+112Sn collisions at 50 and 120 MeV/u, generating 1,000,000 events with impact parameters randomly distributed from 0 to b_max. They compared standard initialization (large phase-space differences between events) with perturbative initialization (tiny differences, D_1k less than 10^-7) in both full mode (mean field plus nucleon-nucleon collisions) and Vlasov mode (mean field only). They then applied a Bayesian method with a two-dimensional Gaussian fluctuation kernel for the observables (charged-particle multiplicity M and total transverse momentum of light charged particles p_t^tot) to reconstruct impact parameter distributions, and proposed using K-means clustering for centrality sorting in the two-observable space.

## Key numbers

* For 112Sn+112Sn at 120 MeV/u, the maximum multiplicity of charged particles M_max is 86 and the maximum total transverse momentum of light charged particles p_t,max^tot is 30 GeV/c.
* For 112Sn+112Sn at 50 MeV/u, M_max is 61 and p_t,max^tot is 16 GeV/c.
* The widths of multiplicity distributions from standard initialization in full mode are 4.13 at both 50 and 120 MeV/u; from perturbative initialization they are 3.74 at 50 MeV/u and 3.97 at 120 MeV/u.
* In Vlasov mode with perturbative initialization, the widths of multiplicity and p_t^tot distributions are 0 (delta distributions) at both beam energies.
* The reduced chi-square values for the two-dimensional Gaussian fit to P(X|b) are below 1.07 for impact parameters up to 5 fm, rising to 2.89 at 11 fm for 120 MeV/u and 1.34 at 11 fm for 50 MeV/u.

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian reconstruction approach of Das (2018), Rogly (2018), and Frankland (2021) from single-observable to two-observable reconstruction for intermediate-energy heavy ion collisions, and provides the first physical explanation of the fluctuation kernel origin using the ImQMD model. The method enables model-independent reconstruction of impact parameter distributions from experimental data and can reveal correlations between multiplicity and transverse momentum at different centralities, which may help constrain the equation of state and understand fragmentation mechanisms. The authors also introduce K-means clustering for automatic centrality sorting in multi-observable space, addressing a practical issue not handled in prior Bayesian approaches.
