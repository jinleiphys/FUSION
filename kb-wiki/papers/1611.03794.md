# On the PBF neutrino losses in superfluid cores of neutron stars

> Axial anomalous contributions into neutrino PBF losses due to triplet pairing of neutrons are still ignored in modeling the evolution of neutron stars. In this paper, the influence of the anomalous axial contributions onto the rate of neutron stars cooling is estimated.

## Key claim

The paper estimates that the anomalous axial contributions to neutrino pair-breaking-formation (PBF) losses from triplet-paired neutrons, which are usually ignored in neutron star cooling models, can increase the cooling rate compared to the traditional approach that discards these terms.

## Method

The author uses the NSCOOL code to simulate the thermal evolution of a spherically symmetric neutron star, adopting the same neutron star model as Page (2009) but with a modified reaction constant. The neutrino emissivity from triplet pairing is computed using the formula derived in Leinson (2010), which includes the anomalous weak interactions that suppress the vector channel and reduce axial-channel losses by a factor of four compared to the earlier expression from Yakovlev (1999).

## Key numbers

- The neutrino emissivity formula (Equation Q) includes the factor \( (k_B T)^7 \) and the function \( F_t(\Delta_{\mathbf{n}}/T) \), where the anisotropic energy gap is \( \Delta_{\mathbf{n}} = \Delta_0(T) \sqrt{1 + 3\cos^2\theta} \).
- The simulation uses a neutron star mass of \( M = 1.4\,M_{\odot} \) with an iron envelope.
- The critical temperature \( T_c \) for neutron superfluidity and proton superconductivity is shown as a function of matter density for the APR equation of state (Figure 1, left panel).

## Context

This work extends the minimal cooling paradigm of Page (2009) by including anomalous axial contributions that were previously neglected in standard treatments such as Yakovlev (1999). The results enable more accurate modeling of neutron star cooling trajectories, which can help interpret observational data on surface temperatures, although the author notes that model parameters like critical temperatures are known only to order of magnitude.
