# GSI Oscillations as Laboratory for Testing of New Physics

> We analyse recent experimental data on the GSI oscillations of the hydrogen-like heavy {^{142}}{\rm Pm}^{60+} ions that is a time modulation of the K-shell electron capture (EC) decay rate. We follow the mechanism of the GSI oscillations, caused by an interference of the neutrino flavour mass-eigenstates in a content of the electron neutrino. We give arguments that these experimental data show i) an existence of sterile neutrinos that is necessary for an explanation of a phase-shift, ii) an observation of CP violation, related to a phase-shift, and iii) an influence of the Quantum Zeno Effect, explaining different values of the amplitude and phase-shift for two runs of measurements with different time resolutions and different numbers of consecutive measurements.

## Key claim

The GSI oscillations (time modulation of K-shell electron capture decay rates in hydrogen-like heavy ions) can be explained by interference of neutrino flavor mass-eigenstates, and the experimental data show evidence for sterile neutrinos, CP violation, and the Quantum Zeno Effect, but only if energy-momentum conservation is violated in individual decay channels due to interactions of ions with the measuring apparatus in the storage ring.

## Method

The authors analyze experimental data on the time-modulated EC decay rates of hydrogen-like \(^{142}\mathrm{Pm}^{60+}\) ions measured at GSI with two different time resolutions (32 ms and 64 ms) and numbers of consecutive measurements (N=1688 and N=844). They extend the standard 3-flavor neutrino mixing framework to include a fourth sterile neutrino flavor with a 4×4 mixing matrix containing a CP-violating phase δ₁₂, which appears as the phase-shift in the oscillatory term of the EC decay rate. They then apply the Quantum Zeno Effect formalism to explain the dependence of the measured amplitude and phase-shift on the number of consecutive measurements, deriving functional forms a(N) and Δφ(N) that vanish in the limit of infinite measurements.

## Key numbers

- Period of time modulation: T = 7.11(11) s (for δt = 32 ms, N = 1688) and T = 7.12(11) s (for δt = 64 ms, N = 844)
- Amplitude: a = 0.107(24) (δt = 32 ms, N = 1688) and a = 0.134(27) (δt = 64 ms, N = 844)
- Phase-shift: φ = 2.35(48) rad (δt = 32 ms, N = 1688) and φ = 1.78(44) rad (δt = 64 ms, N = 844)
- Combined value (Δm²₂₁)GSI = 2.19(3)×10⁻⁴ eV², exceeding the KamLAND value (Δm²₂₁)KL = 7.59(21)×10⁻⁵ eV² by a factor of 2.9
- Lorentz factor γ = 1.43 for the stored ions
- Zeno time for \(^{142}\mathrm{Pm}^{60+}\): τ_Z = 6.01×10⁻¹⁰ s (for EC decay constant effects); for amplitude/phase-shift effects: τ_Z^(a) = 82(10) s (N=1688) and τ_Z^(a) = 78(10) s (N=844)
- Amplitude as function of N: a = 4.17(64)/√N, giving a = 0.102(16) for N=1688 and a = 0.144(22) for N=844
- Phase-shift as function of N: Δφ = 2π × 47.5(7.7)/N^(4/5), giving Δφ = 0.78(13) rad for N=1688 and Δφ = 1.36(22) rad for N=844

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier mechanism for GSI oscillations (Ivanov 2009a, 2010a, 2010b, 2009b) based on neutrino mass-eigenstate interference, which predicts the A-scaling of periods (T ~ A) observed experimentally. It contradicts alternative explanations by Giunti (2008), Kienert et al. (2008), Gal (2010), Pavlichenkov (2008, 2010), Krainov (2012), Lambiase et al. (2013a, 2013b), and Giacosa and Pagliara (2013), which do not predict the A-scaling and incorrectly predict time modulation in β⁺ decays. The analysis enables new experiments on \(^{108}\mathrm{Ag}^{46+}\) to verify A-scaling and provide information on neutrino mass-eigenstate masses, and it proposes GSI oscillations as a unique laboratory for observing CP-violating phases not accessible in conventional neutrino oscillation experiments.
