# Results of the Hydrodynamics Approach to Heavy Ion Collisions

> Recent hydrodynamical calculations for Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s) = 130 AGeV energy are reviewed, and the initial conditions of hydrodynamical evolution necessary to reproduce experimental data are discussed.

## Key claim

Hydrodynamical models can reproduce the single-particle RHIC data (transverse momentum spectra and elliptic anisotropy) for Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 130 GeV up to p_T ~ 1.5–2 GeV, but the HBT radii remain unexplained; the initial conditions required to fit the data are not uniquely determined, though all successful calculations require rapid thermalization (tau_0 ≤ 1 fm/c) and an average initial energy density well above the critical value.

## Method

The author reviews several hydrodynamical calculations (primarily Kolb and Heinz (2002)) that solve relativistic hydrodynamics with a given equation of state (EOS Q with a first-order phase transition or EOS H without one) and initial conditions fixed to reproduce charged particle multiplicities. The calculations assume boost-invariant expansion (except Hirano (2001) which uses full 3D expansion) and use the Cooper-Frye prescription for freeze-out, with freeze-out temperatures ranging from 125 to 160 MeV. Comparisons are made to experimental data from PHENIX and STAR on p_T spectra, elliptic flow v_2 as a function of p_T, centrality, and pseudorapidity, and HBT radii.

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out temperature in Kolb and Heinz (2002): T_f = 130 MeV; chemical freeze-out temperature from thermal models: T_ch = 165–175 MeV.
- In Kolb and Heinz (2002), antiproton spectra are reproduced within experimental errors up to p_T = 3 GeV/c; for peripheral collisions with impact parameter b > 10 fm, excess of particles appears at p_T > 1.5 GeV/c.
- Elliptic anisotropy v_2 of charged hadrons reaches hydrodynamical values up to p_T < 2 GeV; above p_T = 2 GeV the data saturates while hydrodynamics increases monotonically.
- Pion anisotropy deviates from hydrodynamics around p_T = 1.5–2 GeV; proton anisotropy reaches hydrodynamical values even at p_T ~ 2.5 GeV.
- Average initial energy density scaled to tau = 1 fm/c: 5.4 GeV/fm^3 (Kolb and Heinz), 5.8 GeV/fm^3 (Hirano), ~6 GeV/fm^3 (Teaney et al.), 3.9 GeV/fm^3 (Morita et al.), 6.5 GeV/fm^3 (Eskola et al.).
- Peak initial energy densities range from 6 to 160 GeV/fm^3 across calculations; average values at 1 fm/c range from 3.9 to 6.5 GeV/fm^3, but after accounting for transverse area all are 5–6 GeV/fm^3, similar to the Bjorken estimate of 4.6 GeV/fm^3 (Adcox, 2001).
- Initial times tau_0 range from 0.19 fm/c (Eskola et al.) to 1.0 fm/c (Teaney et al., Morita et al.).
- A delay of 1 or 2 fm/c in thermalization reduces v_2 by 10% or 25%, respectively (Kolb et al., 2000).

## Context

This review extends the early RHIC hydrodynamical calculations of Kolb et al. (2000), Huovinen et al. (2001), and Teaney et al. (2001) by comparing to more recent data on p_T spectra, elliptic flow, and HBT radii. It highlights the "HBT puzzle" where all hydrodynamical calculations give R_out/R_side > 1 while data gives ~1, and notes that proton v_2 favors a first-order phase transition (soft EOS) while HBT radii favor a crossover (stiff EOS). The work enables future studies to refine freeze-out descriptions (e.g., hybrid hydro+cascade models like Teaney et al. (2001)) and to better constrain initial conditions, particularly the initial time and energy density profile.
