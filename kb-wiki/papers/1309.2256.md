# Evaluation of disconnected quark loops for hadron structure using GPUs

> A number of stochastic methods developed for the calculation of fermion loops are investigated and compared, in particular with respect to their efficiency when implemented on Graphics Processing Units (GPUs). We assess the performance of the various methods by studying the convergence and statistical accuracy obtained for observables that require a large number of stochastic noise vectors, such as the isoscalar nucleon axial charge. The various methods are also examined for the evaluation of sigma-terms where noise reduction techniques specific to the twisted mass formulation can be utilized thus reducing the required number of stochastic noise vectors.

## Key claim

The paper systematically compares stochastic methods for computing disconnected quark loops on GPUs, finding that the Truncated Solver Method (TSM) combined with the one-end trick provides the most efficient approach for observables requiring many noise vectors (like the isoscalar axial charge), while for sigma-terms the one-end trick alone suffices without TSM.

## Method

The authors evaluated disconnected quark loop contributions to nucleon matrix elements using an ensemble of \(N_f=2+1+1\) twisted mass fermion gauge configurations on a \(32^3 \times 64\) lattice with lattice spacing \(a=0.082(1)(4)\) fm and pion mass \(\sim 370\) MeV. They compared several stochastic estimation techniques: the standard stochastic method, the Truncated Solver Method (TSM) with low- and high-precision inversions, the one-end trick exploiting twisted mass identities, time-dilution, and the Hopping Parameter Expansion (HPE). All inversions and contractions were implemented on NVIDIA GPUs using a modified version of the QUDA library, with multi-GPU parallelization via MPI.

## Key numbers

- Lattice size: \(32^3 \times 64\), lattice spacing \(a = 0.082(1)(4)\) fm, pion mass \(\sim 370\) MeV (B55.32 ensemble)
- For the TSM, optimal parameters were \(N_{\rm HP} = 8\) and \(N_{\rm LP} = 200\)–\(300\) for the isoscalar axial charge, with \(N_{\rm LP} \sim 20\) times \(N_{\rm HP}\)
- For the sigma-term \(\sigma_{\pi N}\), \(N_{\rm LP} = 0\) was sufficient (no TSM needed), while for \(g_A\) at least \(N_{\rm LP} = 500\) was required with \(N_{\rm HP} = 24\)
- Strong scaling showed \(\sim 90\%\) performance increase when adding a second GPU, with TFlop sustained performance achievable with a few GPUs
- Weak scaling was nearly perfect for local volume \(V = 24^4\)

## Context

This work extends prior algorithmic developments for disconnected quark loops, including the one-end trick (Boucaud 2008, Michael 2007, Dinter 2012), dilution (Bernardson 1993, Viehoff 1997, O'Cais 2004, Foley 2005, Alexandrou 2012), the Truncated Solver Method (Alexandrou 2012, Collins 2007, Bali 2009), and the Hopping Parameter Expansion (Boucaud 2008, McNeile 2000). The study demonstrates that combining TSM with the one-end trick on GPUs enables reliable computation of flavor-singlet observables like the isoscalar axial charge and sigma-terms, which were previously neglected due to computational cost. The results enable future high-statistics calculations of disconnected contributions to hadron structure, with the authors noting that precise physical results are reserved for a follow-up publication.
