# Using Continuum Level Density in the Pairing Hamiltonian: BCS and Exact Solutions

> Pairing plays a central role in nuclear systems. The simplest model for the pairing is the constant-pairing Hamiltonian. The aim of the present paper is to include the continuum single particle level density in the constant pairing Hamiltonian and to make a comparison between the approximate BCS and the exact Richardson solutions. The continuum is introduced by using the continuum single particle level density. It is shown that the continuum makes an important contribution to the pairing parameter even in those case when the continuum is weakly populated. It is shown that while the approximate BCS solution depends on the model space the exact Richardson solution does not.

# Using Continuum Level Density in the Pairing Hamiltonian: BCS and Exact Solutions

## Key claim

The continuum single particle level density makes an important contribution to the pairing parameter even when the continuum is weakly populated (less than 4% occupation); however, while the approximate BCS solution depends on the size of the model space, the exact Richardson solution does not, and the ground state energy is almost independent of both the model space and the model solution when the pairing strength parameter is renormalized.

## Method

The constant-pairing Hamiltonian was solved in a real-energy continuum representation using the continuum single particle level density (CSPLD), defined relative to the free particle density via the phase shift derivative from a Woods-Saxon potential. The BCS gap and particle number equations were generalized to include the CSPLD integral, and the Richardson equations for exact pair energies were similarly extended with the CSPLD. Calculations were performed for Tin isotopes above the 132Sn core using two model spaces: one with only valence bound states (Mb) and one including all partial waves up to lmax=20 with a cutoff energy of 100 MeV (Mc).

## Key numbers

- The bound single particle energies for valence orbits in the Woods-Saxon potential were: 1f7/2 = -2.442 MeV, 2p3/2 = -1.395 MeV, 0h9/2 = -0.923 MeV, 2p1/2 = -0.736 MeV, and 1f5/2 = -0.148 MeV.
- The pairing energy for the reference nucleus 148Sn (N=16) was P(16) = 0.985 MeV, taken from theoretical mass tables.
- The fitted strength parameter chi for the BCS solution was chi_BCS_b = 23.66 MeV for the bound model space Mb and chi_BCS_c = 14.67 MeV for the continuum model space Mc.
- The fitted strength parameter chi for the Richardson solution was chi_Rich_b = 18.4 MeV for Mb and chi_Rich_c = 12.25 MeV for Mc.
- The continuum occupation did not exceed 4% of the total particle number for any isotope in the Mc model space.
- The BCS overestimated the pair correlation energy by an average of 278 keV across the isotope chain, with a maximum difference of 848 keV at the middle of the shell.
- Resonances in the CSPLD had widths: Gamma(i13/2) = 0.436 x 10^-4 MeV, Gamma(g9/2) = 0.887 MeV, Gamma(i11/2) = 0.382 MeV, Gamma(k15/2) = 0.251 MeV, Gamma(l17/2) = 1.735 MeV.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of the BCS solution with continuum by Sandulescu (1997), Kruppa (2001), and Dussel (2007), and the Richardson solution with resonant continuum by Hasegawa (2003), by introducing the continuum through the real-energy CSPLD rather than complex energy representations. The use of the CSPLD relative to the free particle density follows the approach of Beth (1937) and Mosel (1973) to eliminate spurious particle gas effects. The results enable future study of even Carbon isotopes in the Richardson model with CSPLD and comparison between real-energy and complex-energy continuum representations.
