# Universal chemical freeze-out as a phase transition signature

> It is shown that kinetic freeze-out in relativistic heavy-ion collisions invariably entails a non-trivial dependence of the freeze-out temperature on the collision centrality. The centrality independence of the chemical freeze-out temperature observed in Au+Au collisions at RHIC is therefore inconsistent with the hypothesis that hadron abundances decouple kinetically from inelastic hadron-hadron interactions. On the other hand, it is consistent with the hypothesis that chemical decoupling is driven by the quark-hadron phase transition, and that the observed universal chemical freeze-out reflects its critical temperature, independent of the dynamical state of the collision fireball as it passes through the phase transition.

# Universal chemical freeze-out as a phase transition signature

## Key claim

The centrality independence of the chemical freeze-out temperature observed in Au+Au collisions at RHIC is inconsistent with a kinetic decoupling process from inelastic hadronic interactions, but is consistent with chemical decoupling driven by the quark-hadron phase transition, where the universal chemical freeze-out temperature reflects the critical temperature of that phase transition.

## Method

The authors used (2+1)-dimensional longitudinally boost-invariant hydrodynamic simulations (AZHYDRO code) with standard initial conditions for 200 A GeV Au+Au collisions, defining kinetic freeze-out surfaces by equating the local expansion rate to the local scattering rate (with a proportionality constant ξ). They then applied the same kinetic freeze-out criterion to chemical decoupling, exploring different temperature dependences of inelastic scattering rates (parametrized as T^n) to test whether centrality-independent chemical freeze-out temperatures could arise from a kinetic process.

## Key numbers

- Lattice QCD critical temperature values cited: T_c = 173±15 MeV (older value), T_c(χ_ψ̄ψ) = 151±3±3 MeV, T_c(χ_s) ≈ T_c(P) = 176±3±4 MeV, T_c = 192±7±4 MeV (from different groups and observables)
- Chemical freeze-out temperature from STAR: T_chem = 163±4 MeV, μ_B = 24±4 MeV for central Au+Au collisions (with alternative fit giving T_chem = 157±6 MeV, μ_B = 22±4 MeV)
- Kinetic freeze-out temperature from STAR: T_kin = 89±12 MeV (most central) to T_kin = 127±13 MeV (most peripheral) in 200 A GeV Au+Au collisions
- Average radial flow from STAR: ⟨β⟩ = 0.59±0.05 (most central) to ⟨β⟩ = 0.24±0.08 (most peripheral)
- Pion scattering rate parametrization used: 1/τ^π_scatt = (59.5 fm⁻¹) × (T/1 GeV)^3.45
- To achieve centrality independence of chemical freeze-out temperature, inelastic scattering rates must grow with temperature as T^n with n ≳ 20

## Context

This work extends the debate between two camps in heavy-ion physics: Camp I (Becattini, Heinz, Stock) holds that hadrons are "born into chemical equilibrium" via statistical hadronization at the phase transition, while Camp II (Rapp, Shuryak, Greiner) holds that chemical freeze-out is a kinetic process within the hadronic phase. The authors show that the centrality independence of T_chem cannot be explained by kinetic decoupling, supporting the phase transition interpretation. This enables using chemical freeze-out temperatures as a direct probe of the QCD critical temperature, avoiding the failures of earlier phase transition signatures such as the van Hove plateau (van Hove 1982), directed flow collapse (Rischke 1995), and elliptic flow minima (Kolb, Sollfrank, Heinz 2000).
