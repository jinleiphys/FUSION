# Symmetry energy effect on the secondary component of GW190814 as a neutron star

> The secondary component of GW190814 with a mass of 2.50-2.67 $M_{\odot}$ may be the lightest black hole or the heaviest neutron star ever observed in a binary compact object system. To explore the possible equation of state (EOS), which can support such massive neutron star, we apply the relativistic mean-field model with a density-dependent isovector coupling constant to describe the neutron-star matter. The acceptable EOS should satisfy some constraints: the EOS model can provide a satisfactory description of the nuclei; the maximum mass $M_\textrm{TOV}$ is above 2.6 $M_{\odot}$; the tidal deformability of a canonical 1.4 $M_{\odot}$ neutron star $Λ_{1.4}$ should lie in the constrained range from GW170817. In this paper, we find that the nuclear symmetry energy and its density dependence play a crucial role in determining the EOS of neutron-star matter. The constraints from the mass of 2.6 $M_{\odot}$ and the tidal deformability $Λ_{1.4}=616_{-158}^{+273}$ (based on the assumption that GW190814 is a neutron star-black hole binary) can be satisfied as the slope of symmetry energy $L \leq 50$ MeV. Even including the constraint of $Λ_{1.4}=190_{-120}^{+390}$ from GW170817 which suppresses the EOS stiffness at low density, the possibility that the secondary component of GW190814 is a massive neutron star cannot be excluded in this study.

## Key claim
The nuclear symmetry energy slope $L$ must be $\leq 50$ MeV for the relativistic mean-field model to simultaneously satisfy the mass constraint of a 2.6 $M_\odot$ neutron star (the secondary component of GW190814) and the tidal deformability constraint $\Lambda_{1.4}=616_{-158}^{+273}$ from the neutron star-black hole binary assumption; even with the additional, more restrictive GW170817 constraint $\Lambda_{1.4}=190_{-120}^{+390}$, the possibility that the secondary is a neutron star cannot be excluded.

## Method
The authors use the relativistic mean-field (RMF) model with a density-dependent isovector coupling constant (the RMFL model) based on the NL3 parametrization, generating a series of NL3L parameter sets with different symmetry energy slope $L$ values (30–118.5 MeV) while keeping all other saturation properties fixed. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to compute neutron-star mass-radius relations, tidal deformabilities, and central densities, then compare these predictions against observational constraints from GW190814, GW170817, NICER, and known massive pulsars.

## Key numbers
- The secondary component of GW190814 has a mass of 2.50–2.67 $M_\odot$.
- The tidal deformability constraint for a 1.4 $M_\odot$ neutron star under the neutron star-black hole assumption is $\Lambda_{1.4}=616_{-158}^{+273}$; the GW170817 constraint is $\Lambda_{1.4}=190_{-120}^{+390}$.
- The symmetry energy slope must satisfy $L \leq 50$ MeV to meet the GW190814 neutron star-black hole constraints.
- For NL3L-30 ($L=30$ MeV): $M_\text{TOV}=2.75\,M_\odot$, $R_{1.4}=13.04$ km, $\Lambda_{1.4}=608.8$.
- For NL3L-50 ($L=50$ MeV): $M_\text{TOV}=2.74\,M_\odot$, $R_{1.4}=13.31$ km, $\Lambda_{1.4}=815.9$.
- For NL3L-80 ($L=80$ MeV): $M_\text{TOV}=2.73\,M_\odot$, $R_{1.4}=13.80$ km, $\Lambda_{1.4}=975.2$.
- For the original NL3 ($L=118.5$ MeV): $M_\text{TOV}=2.77\,M_\odot$, $R_{1.4}=14.55$ km, $\Lambda_{1.4}=1242.4$.
- The NICER constraints on PSR J0030+0451 give mass-radius ranges of ($1.44^{+0.15}_{-0.14}\,M_\odot$, $13.02^{+1.24}_{-1.06}$ km) and ($1.34^{+0.15}_{-0.16}\,M_\odot$, $12.71^{+1.14}_{-1.19}$ km) from different groups.

## Context
This work extends the study of the nature of GW190814’s secondary component, following phenomenological EOS approaches by Godzieba et al. (2020) and the BigApple parameter set by Fattoyev et al. (2020). It specifically investigates the role of the symmetry energy slope $L$, which is poorly constrained by current observations (Oertel 2017), and shows that smaller $L$ values (30–50 MeV) can satisfy the mass and tidal deformability constraints simultaneously. The results enable future gravitational-wave detections to further constrain the EOS by providing precise tidal deformability measurements, and they leave open the possibility that the 2.6 $M_\odot$ object is a neutron star rather than a black hole.
