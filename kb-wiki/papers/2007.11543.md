# Crystals of superconducting Baryonic tubes in the low energy limit of QCD at finite density

> The low energy limit of QCD admits (crystals of) superconducting Baryonic tubes at finite density. We begin with the Maxwell-gauged Skyrme model in (3+1)-dimensions (which is the low energy limit of QCD in the leading order of the large N expansion). We construct an ansatz able to reduce the seven coupled field equations in a sector of high Baryonic charge to just one linear Schrodinger-like equation with an effective potential (which can be computed explicitly) periodic in the two spatial directions orthogonal to the axis of the tubes. The solutions represent ordered arrays of Baryonic superconducting tubes as (most of) the Baryonic charge and total energy is concentrated in the tube-shaped regions. They carry a persistent current (which vanishes outside the tubes) even in the limit of vanishing U(1) gauge field: such a current cannot be deformed continuously to zero as it is tied to the topological charge. Then, we discuss the subleading corrections in the 't Hooft expansion to the Skyrme model (called usually \mathcal{L}_{6}$, $\mathcal{L}_{8}$ and so on). Remarkably, the very same ansatz allows to construct analytically these crystals of superconducting Baryonic tubes at any order in the 't Hooft expansion. Thus, no matter how many subleading terms are included, these ordered arrays of gauged solitons are described by the same ansatz and keep their main properties manifesting a universal character. On the other hand, the subleading terms can affect the stability properties of the configurations setting lower bounds on the allowed Baryon density.

## Key claim

The Maxwell-gauged Skyrme model (the low energy limit of QCD at leading order in the large N expansion) admits analytic solutions describing ordered arrays of superconducting Baryonic tubes at finite density, and the same ansatz works at any order in the 't Hooft expansion, though subleading terms can set lower bounds on the allowed Baryon density.

## Method

The authors begin with the U(1) gauged Skyrme model in (3+1) dimensions and construct an ansatz for the SU(2)-valued Skyrme field and the Maxwell potential that reduces the seven coupled nonlinear field equations (three from the generalized Skyrme model plus four Maxwell equations) to a single first-order integrable ordinary differential equation for the soliton profile and a single linear Schrodinger-like equation for the relevant component of the Maxwell field. The ansatz uses a flat metric with a box volume parameter L, with the Skyrme field parameterized by functions α(r), Θ = qθ, and Φ = p(t/L - φ), where p and q are integers, and the Maxwell potential takes the form Aμ = (u(r,θ), 0, 0, -L u(r,θ)). The same ansatz is shown to work when subleading corrections L6, L8, and higher are included.

## Key numbers

- The topological (Baryonic) charge of each configuration is B = -np, where n is an integer related to the boundary condition α(2π) - α(0) = nπ and p is the integer appearing in the ansatz for Φ.
- The adimensional coordinates have ranges 0 ≤ r ≤ 2π, 0 ≤ θ ≤ π, 0 ≤ φ ≤ 2π, and the box volume is 4π³L³.
- The integer q in the ansatz is defined as q = 2v + 1 (odd integer) with v ∈ ℕ, and p ∈ ℕ with p ≠ 0.
- The energy density expression includes terms proportional to the Skyrme couplings K and λ, the pion mass m, and subleading coefficients c6 and c8, with a necessary condition c6 ≥ 0 for positive definiteness.

## Context

This work extends the analytic construction of gauged multi-soliton solutions introduced in Canfora et al. (2016, 2017) and provides the first explicit (3+1)-dimensional analytic examples of superconducting strings of the type proposed by Witten (1985), built from ingredients arising naturally in the standard model. The results contrast with numerical string solutions in the Skyrme model with mass term (Jackson, Nitta 2007) which are classically unstable with zero topological density; the present configurations are topologically protected. The analytic method enables future study of electromagnetic properties of these crystals, which would otherwise require solving a fully coupled nonlinear system numerically.
