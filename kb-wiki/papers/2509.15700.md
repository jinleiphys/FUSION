# Compressibility and speed of sound in magnetized nuclear matter with broken scale invariance

> The thermodynamical properties of magnetized nuclear matter at finite temperature and baryon chemical potential are studied within an effective model incorporating the QCD trace anomaly effect. The presence of magnetic field induces anisotropic structure in the energy momentum tensor due to the broken rotational invariance. The study exhibits a phase transition through the sudden change of the effective nucleon mass in a certain range of baryon chemical potential and temperature. The addition of nucleonic vacuum contribution at finite magnetic field leads to the magnetic catalysis effect. The change in squared speed of sound with chemical potential at various temperatures is closely connected to the nature of phase transition in nuclear matter. The pressure anisotropy results in different values of sound speed and isothermal compressibility in the parallel and perpendicular directions with respect to the magnetic field. The smaller values of isothermal compressibility in the parallel direction compared to the perpendicular one indicate that the equation of state is stiffer along the magnetic field direction. The studies of these thermodynamic observables can have significant importance in analyzing the properties of some compact astrophysical objects as well as in the context of non-central heavy ion collision experiments.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that in magnetized nuclear matter with broken scale invariance, the isothermal compressibility and speed of sound become anisotropic, with the parallel component of compressibility being smaller than the perpendicular component, indicating a stiffer equation of state along the magnetic field direction; however, this result is conditional on the specific choice of fixed entropy per baryon density for the sound speed calculation and on the inclusion of the nucleonic vacuum contribution at finite magnetic field.

## Method

The authors employ a relativistic mean-field Lagrangian that incorporates the QCD trace anomaly through a logarithmic scalar gluonium (dilaton) potential, with nucleons, scalar sigma mesons, vector omega mesons, and the dilaton field as effective degrees of freedom. The thermodynamic potential is computed in the mean-field approximation for symmetric nuclear matter at finite temperature, baryon chemical potential, and background magnetic field, including Landau quantization for protons and a magnetized vacuum contribution via the Hurwitz zeta function. The coupled equations of motion for the mean meson fields are solved self-consistently, and the anisotropic pressure components (parallel and perpendicular to the magnetic field) are used to derive the isothermal compressibility and squared speed of sound along isentropic curves.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density: \(\rho_0 = 0.153\ \text{fm}^{-3}\); binding energy: \(-16.3\) MeV; compressibility: \(K \approx 300\) MeV at saturation (zero temperature, zero magnetic field)
- Model parameters: \(g_{\sigma}/m_{\sigma} = 0.0132\ \text{MeV}^{-1}\), \(g_{\omega}/m_{\omega} = 0.0105\ \text{MeV}^{-1}\), \(b = 0.01\), \(c = 0.0146\), \(B_g = (200\ \text{MeV})^4\)
- Landau mass at saturation: \(\sqrt{m^{*2}_N + k_F^2} = 0.83 m_N\)
- The paper reports that isothermal compressibility in the parallel direction is smaller than in the perpendicular direction at finite magnetic field, but no explicit numerical values for compressibility or sound speed at specific field strengths or chemical potentials are provided in the truncated text.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic mean-field model with scale-invariance breaking (Kapusta (2001)) to include finite temperature and magnetic field effects, building on prior studies of magnetized nuclear matter using the Walecka model (Glendenning (1997), Boguta (1977)) and the linear sigma model with chiral and scale symmetry breaking (Papazoglou (1999)). The anisotropic compressibility and sound speed results are relevant for understanding the equation of state of magnetars and proto-neutron stars, where interior magnetic fields can reach \(\sim 10^{18}\) G, and for non-central heavy-ion collision experiments at RHIC and LHC where transient magnetic fields are generated.
