# Cluster-decay of hot $^{56}$Ni$^*$ formed in $^{32}$S+$^{24}$Mg reaction

> The decay of $^{56}Ni^*$, formed in $^{32}S+^{24}Mg$ reaction at the incident energies $E_{cm}$=51.6 and 60.5 MeV, is calculated as a cluster decay process within the Preformed Cluster-decay Model (PCM) of Gupta et al. re-formulated for hot compound systems. The observed deformed shapes of the exit channel fragments are simulated by introducing the neck-length parameter at the scission configuration, which nearly coincides the $^{56}Ni$ saddle configuration. This is the only parameter of the model, which though is also defined in terms of the binding energy of the hot compound system and the ground-state binding energies of the various emitted fragments. The calculated s-wave cross sections for nuclear shapes with outgoing fragments separated within nuclear proximity limit (here $\sim$0.3 fm) can be compared with the experimental data, and the TKEs are found to be in reasonably good agreement with experiments for the angular momentum effects added in the sticking limit for the moment of inertia. Also, some light particle production (other than the statistical evaporation residue, not treated here) is predicted at these energies and, interestingly, $^4He$, which belongs to evaporation residue, is found missing as a dynamical cluster-decay fragment.

## Key claim

The Preformed Cluster-decay Model (PCM), reformulated for hot compound systems, can reproduce the measured fragment cross sections and total kinetic energies (TKEs) for the decay of $^{56}Ni^*$ formed in the $^{32}S+^{24}Mg$ reaction, but only when the neck-length parameter $\overline{\Delta R}$ and angular momentum effects are tuned separately for each incident energy, and the agreement is limited to s-wave cross sections for fragments separated within the nuclear proximity limit (approximately 0.3 fm).

## Method

The authors applied the Preformed Cluster-decay Model (PCM) of Gupta et al., reformulated for hot compound systems, to calculate the decay of $^{56}Ni^*$ formed in the $^{32}S+^{24}Mg$ reaction at $E_{cm}=51.6$ and 60.5 MeV. The model treats complex fragments as dynamical collective mass motion of preformed fragments through a barrier, using a neck-length parameter $\overline{\Delta R}$ at the scission configuration to simulate deformed fragment shapes and neck formation, with temperature effects included either only in shell corrections or in all terms of the potential (liquid drop energy, Coulomb, and proximity potentials). The s-wave cross sections were calculated and normalized to experimental data, while TKEs were computed with angular momentum effects added in the sticking limit for the moment of inertia.

## Key numbers

- Incident energies: $E_{cm}=51.6$ and 60.5 MeV for the $^{32}S+^{24}Mg \rightarrow ^{56}Ni^*$ reaction
- Neck-length parameter values: $\overline{\Delta R}=0.30$ fm at $E_{cm}=51.6$ MeV and $\overline{\Delta R}=0.29$ fm at $E_{cm}=60.5$ MeV (for temperature effects in shell corrections only)
- Angular momentum values for TKE calculations: $\ell = 24\hbar$ at $E_{cm}=51.6$ MeV and $\ell = 25\hbar$ at $E_{cm}=60.5$ MeV (sticking limit)
- Compound nucleus temperatures: $T=3.39$ MeV and $T=3.60$ MeV corresponding to $E_{cm}=51.6$ and 60.5 MeV, respectively
- Nuclear proximity limit for fragment separation: approximately 0.3 fm
- $Q_{in}$ for the entrance channel: +16.68 MeV for $^{32}S+^{24}Mg$
- Surface width temperature dependence: $b(T)=0.99(1+0.009T^2)$ fm
- Radius temperature dependence: $R_i(T)=1.07(1+0.01T) A_i^{1/3}$ fm
- Shell correction damping temperature: $T_0=1.5$ MeV

## Context

This work extends the Preformed Cluster-decay Model (PCM) previously applied to ground-state decays and to $^{116}Ba^*$ decay (Gupta 2002) to the light compound system $^{56}Ni^*$, building on experimental measurements by Sanders (1987, 1989) for the $^{32}S+^{24}Mg$ reaction. The model provides an alternative to statistical fission models such as the transition-state model of Sanders (1989, 1991) and the Extended Hauser-Feshbach method of Matsuse (1997), incorporating structure effects via preformation probabilities that are absent in purely statistical approaches. The results enable future inclusion of angular momentum effects in the fragmentation potential and extension to other light compound systems where resonance behavior has been observed (Beck 2001).
