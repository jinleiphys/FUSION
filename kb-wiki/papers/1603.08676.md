# Rapidity fluctuations in the initial state

> We analyze two-particle pseudorapidity correlations in a simple model, where strings of fluctuating length are attached to wounded nucleons.The obtained straightforward formulas allow us to understand the anatomy of the correlations, i.e., to identify the component due to the fluctuation of the number of wounded nucleons and the contribution from the string length fluctuations. Our results reproduce qualitatively and semiquantitatively the basic features of the recent correlation measurements at the LHC.

## Key claim
The paper shows that two-particle pseudorapidity correlations in high-energy nuclear collisions can be understood analytically in a simple model where strings of fluctuating length are attached to wounded nucleons, and that the fluctuation in the number of wounded nucleons alone is insufficient to explain the observed long-range correlations, string length fluctuations contribute a significant component.

## Method
The authors derive analytic expressions for the two-particle pseudorapidity correlation function \(C(\eta_1, \eta_2)\) in a model where each wounded nucleon pulls a string whose other endpoint is randomly distributed over the available space-time rapidity range. They separate the correlation into contributions from fluctuations in the number of wounded nucleons and from fluctuations in string length, and compare their results qualitatively and semiquantitatively to ATLAS measurements for Pb-Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV and p-Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV.

## Key numbers
- For Pb-Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV, centrality 30–40%, the correlation function \(\overline{C}_N(\eta_1,\eta_2)\) is computed and compared to ATLAS data; the experimental acceptance range for pseudorapidity is \(Y = 2.4\).
- For p-Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV, correlation functions \(\overline{C}(\eta_1,\eta_2)\) and \(\overline{C}_N(\eta_1,\eta_2)\) are shown.
- The scaled rapidities are defined as \(u_{1,2} = \eta_{1,2} / y_b\), where \(y_b\) is the beam rapidity.

## Context
This work extends the earlier observation by Bzdak (2012) that fluctuations in the number of wounded nucleons generate nontrivial longitudinal correlations, by showing that string length fluctuations add a significant long-range component. The model provides intuitive understanding for mechanisms present in more advanced string-decay models such as those by Andersson (1983), Wang (1991), Lin (2004), and Monnai (2015). The analytic formulas enable decomposition of correlation sources and can be used to interpret ongoing LHC measurements.
