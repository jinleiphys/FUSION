# IAEA Photonuclear Data Library 2019

> Photo-induced reaction cross section data are of importance for a variety of current or emerging applications, such as radiation shielding design and radiation transport analyses, calculations of absorbed dose in the human body during radiotherapy, physics and technology of fission reactors (influence of photo-reactions on neutron balance) and fusion reactors (plasma diagnostics and shielding), activation analyses, safeguards and inspection technologies, nuclear waste transmutation, medical isotope production and astrophysical applications. Since the release of the IAEA Photonuclear Data Library in 1999 however, new experimental data as well as new methods to assess the reliability of experimental cross sections have become available. Theoretical models and input parameters used to evaluate photo-induced reactions have improved significantly over the years. In addition, new measurements of partial photoneutron cross sections using mono-energetic photon beams and advanced neutron detection systems have been performed allowing for the validation of the evaluations and assessments of the experimental data. Furthermore, technological advances have led to the construction of new and more powerful gamma-beam facilities, therefore new data needs are emerging. We report our coordinated efforts to address these data needs and present the results of the new evaluations of more than 200 nuclides included in the new updated IAEA Photonuclear Data Library, where the photon energy goes up to 200 MeV. We discuss the new assessment method and make recommendations to the user community in cases where the experimental data are discrepant and the assessments disagree. In addition, in the absence of experimental data, we present model predictions for photo-induced reaction cross section on nuclides of potential interest to medical radioisotope production.

## Key claim

The updated IAEA Photonuclear Data Library 2019 provides evaluated photo-induced reaction cross sections and neutron spectra for 220 isotopes up to 200 MeV, incorporating new experimental data from laser Compton scattering facilities and a new neutron-multiplicity sorting technique, while also offering model predictions for nuclides of potential interest to medical radioisotope production where no experimental data exist.

## Method

The coordinated research project revisited all 164 isotopes from the 1999 library and added evaluations for 37 isotopes with available experimental data plus 9 isotopes identified for medical applications, using new Giant Dipole Resonance parameters from the updated Atlas of GDR parameters and improved reaction models. All available experimental data from the EXFOR database up to April 2019 were considered, including new measurements using the direct neutron-multiplicity sorting technique, and evaluations were extended to 200 MeV for accelerator-driven transmutation technologies.

## Key numbers

* 220 isotopes were evaluated in total (164 from the previous library plus 56 new evaluations)
* Photon energy range extends up to 200 MeV
* The 1999 library contained 164 isotopes across four categories: structural/shielding materials, biological materials, fissionable materials, and other materials
* 40 major isotopes of 29 elements were identified as most important for applications
* 37 isotopes with available experimental data were newly evaluated
* 9 isotopes were identified as relevant for medical applications and evaluated
* The laser Compton scattering facility at NewSUBARU operates with electron beams at 0.5, 1.0, or 1.5 GeV
* LCS gamma-ray energy spreads are a few percent in full width at half maximum: 68, 247, 325, 578, and 965 keV for 6.0, 10, 20, 30, and 39 MeV beams respectively
* The electron beam energy at NewSUBARU was calibrated with accuracy of order 10^-5

## Context

This work updates the IAEA Photonuclear Data Library from 1999, which contained evaluated data for 164 isotopes based on earlier measurements from facilities like Livermore and Saclay using positron annihilation in flight techniques. The new library addresses long-standing discrepancies between Livermore and Saclay data using improved assessment methods and new measurements from facilities such as HI gamma S and NewSUBARU, and enables applications in medical isotope production, radiation shielding, and accelerator-driven transmutation technologies. The evaluations also form the basis for the recommended photonuclear photon strength functions in Goriely (2019).
