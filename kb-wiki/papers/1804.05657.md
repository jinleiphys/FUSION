# Effective Stellar $β$-Decay Rates of Nuclei with Long-lived Isomers: $^{26}$Al and $^{34}$Cl

> Isotopes with low-lying long-lived isomers can behave very differently from other isotopes in astrophysical environments. In particular, the assumption of thermal equilibrium in computing the temperature-dependent $β$-decay rates of such isotopes can fail below certain temperatures. We focus on the $β$-decay of $^{26}$Al since it is one of the most important isotopes in observational astrophysics and has a low-lying isomeric state; we compare and contrast these results with $^{34}$Cl. We rule out recently reported $^{26}$Al effective $β$-decay rates that showed large differences from previous calculations, finding that we agree with the earlier results. We conclude that in general, effective $β$-decay rates should be defined separately for the ground and isomeric states at temperatures where thermal equilibrium cannot be achieved.

## Key claim

The paper rules out recently reported effective beta-decay rates for 26Al that showed large deviations from previous calculations at temperatures above about 30 keV, finding instead that their own calculations agree with the earlier results of Coc (2000). The authors conclude that for isotopes with long-lived isomers, effective beta-decay rates should be defined separately for the ground state and isomeric state at temperatures where thermal equilibrium cannot be achieved.

## Method

The authors computed effective beta-decay rates by time-evolving the nuclear state populations according to coupled differential equations that include internal transition rates and beta-decay rates, then finding the time at which the total isotopic abundance drops by a factor of e. They used measured internal transition and beta-decay rates from the Evaluated Nuclear Structure Data File (ENSDF) where available, and estimated unknown rates using shell model calculations with the USDB interaction, diagonalized with NUSHELLX@MSU and OXBASH codes. The effective beta-decay rates were calculated separately for the ground state and isomeric state by starting with all nuclei initially in that state.

## Key numbers

- 26Al ground state laboratory beta-decay half-life: 0.717 Myr
- 26Al isomeric state at 228 keV with beta-decay half-life: 6.35 s
- 26Al isomeric state to ground state M5 transition rate estimated from shell model: 2.73 x 10^-13 s^-1
- 34Cl ground state beta-decay half-life: 1.53 s
- 34Cl isomeric state at 146 keV with beta-decay half-life: 58 min
- For 26Al, thermal equilibrium is achieved for temperatures above approximately 40 keV
- For 34Cl, thermal equilibrium is achieved for temperatures above approximately 25 keV

## Context

This work extends the line of research on effective beta-decay rates for 26Al previously calculated by Coc (2000), Runkle (2001), and Iliadis (2011), and contradicts the recent results of Reifarth (2018) that found large deviations at high temperatures. The paper clarifies that for isotopes with low-lying long-lived isomers, the ground state and isomeric state must be treated as separate species in nucleosynthesis calculations at temperatures below thermal equilibrium, and that at intermediate temperatures even this approach can fail because the beta-decay rate becomes time-dependent. This enables more accurate treatment of 26Al and 34Cl in stellar nucleosynthesis models, particularly for core hydrogen burning (temperatures below about 10 keV) and shell carbon/neon burning (temperatures above about 90 keV).
