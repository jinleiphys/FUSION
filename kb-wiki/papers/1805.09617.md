# The cathode tube effect: heavy quarks probing the Glasma in p-Pb collisions

> We study the propagation of charm quarks in the early stage of high energy proton-lead collision, considering the interaction of these quarks with the evolving Glasma by means of the Wong equations. Neglecting quantum fluctuations at the initial time the Glasma is made of longitudinal fields, but the dynamics leads to a quick formation of transverse fields; we estimate such a formation time as $Δt\approx 0.1$ fm/c which is of the same order of the formation time of heavy quark pairs $t_\mathrm{formation}\approx 1/(2m)$. Limiting ourselves to the simple case of a static longitudinal geometry, we find that heavy quarks are accelerated by the strong transverse color fields in the early stage and this leads to a tilting of the $c-$quarks spectrum towards higher $p_T$ states. This average acceleration can be understood in terms of drag and diffusion of $c-$quarks in a hot medium and appears to be similar to the one felt by the electrons ejected by the electron cannon in a cathode tube: we dub this effect as {\it cathode tube effect}. The tilting of the spectrum affects the nuclear modification factor, $R_\mathrm{pPb}$, suppressing this below one at low $p_T$ and making it larger than one at intermediate $p_T$. We compute $R_\mathrm{pPb}(p_T)$ after the evolution of charm quarks in the gluon fields and we find that its shape is in qualitative agreement with the measurements of the same quantity for $D-$mesons in proton-lead collisions.

## Key claim

The propagation of charm quarks in the evolving Glasma fields of p-Pb collisions produces a tilting of the charm quark spectrum toward higher transverse momentum, which the authors call the "cathode tube effect." This effect suppresses the nuclear modification factor R_pPb below one at low p_T and makes it larger than one at intermediate p_T, with the qualitative shape resembling experimental measurements for D-mesons, though the authors caution that longitudinal expansion is not included and they do not aim for a precise fit to data.

## Method

The authors solve the classical Yang-Mills equations for the gluon fields of the Glasma and the Wong equations for charm quark propagation in these fields, neglecting backreaction of the quarks on the fields. The initial Glasma is set up using the McLerran-Venugopalan model with a constituent quark model for the proton and a uniform distribution for the lead nucleus, with saturation scales tuned to sqrt(s)=5.02 TeV. Charm quarks are initialized with a FONLL perturbative production spectrum and evolved for 1 fm/c, after which fragmentation to D-mesons is applied using a standard fragmentation function.

## Key numbers

- Formation time of transverse fields from the initially longitudinal Glasma: Delta t approximately 0.1 fm/c
- Charm quark formation time: t_formation approximately 1/(2m) = 0.06 fm/c for m=1.5 GeV
- Charm quark mass used in the calculation: m=1.5 GeV
- Saturation scale for the proton at LHC energy sqrt(s)=5.02 TeV: Q_s = 0.80 GeV, giving average g^2 mu_p = 1.41 GeV
- Two values used for g^2 mu of the Pb nucleus at LHC energy: 3.4 GeV (IP-Sat model) and 5.2 GeV (naive scaling)
- Lattice spacing for the transverse box: delta x = 0.04 fm
- Transverse box size: 4 fm with a 91x91 lattice
- Fragmentation function parameter: epsilon_c = 0.06

## Context

This work extends previous studies of heavy quark propagation in the Glasma by Mrowczynski (2017), which used a Fokker-Planck equation with a small transferred momentum approximation and a static gluonic medium, by instead solving the full classical Yang-Mills and Wong equations with a dynamically evolving gluon field. The results suggest that the measured R_pPb for D-mesons in p-Pb collisions may contain a signature of the Glasma itself, and the authors plan to include longitudinal expansion and couple to relativistic transport or Langevin dynamics in future work to quantify the effect of a hot medium.
