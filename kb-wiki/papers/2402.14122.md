# Automated Resonance Identification in Nuclear Data Evaluation

> Global and national efforts to deliver high-quality nuclear data to users have a broad impact across applications such as national security, reactor operation, basic science, medical fields, and more. Cross section evaluation is a large part this effort as it combines theory and experiment to produce suggested values and uncertainty for reaction probabilities. In most isotopes, the cross section exhibits resonant behavior in what is called the resonance region of incident neutron energy. Resonance region evaluation is a specialized type of nuclear data evaluation that can require significant, manual effort and months of time from expert scientists. In this article, non-convex, non-linear optimization methods are combined with concepts of inferential statistics to infer a set of optimized resonance models from experimental data in an automated manner that is not dependent on prior evaluation(s). This methodology aims to enhance the workflow of a resonance evaluator by minimizing time, effort, and prior biases while improving reproducibility and document-ability, addressing widely recognized challenges in the field.

## Key claim

The paper presents a methodology that combines non-convex, non-linear optimization (Levenberg-Marquardt) with backwards stepwise selection to infer resonance model parameters from experimental data without dependence on prior evaluations, but the final selection of model complexity (number of resonances) is deliberately left to the expert judgment of the evaluator rather than fully automated.

## Method

The authors built their automated resonance identification algorithm around the SAMMY resonance analysis code, exploiting a user-input relative prior parameter covariance scaled by a factor zeta to implement a Levenberg-Marquardt optimization algorithm. The algorithm proceeds in two steps: first, regression with an initial feature bank containing an excess of resonances to address non-convexity; second, backwards stepwise selection that iteratively eliminates resonances to find the optimal model for each integer number of resonances, starting from a many-resonance model and reducing down to zero.

## Key numbers

- The search space for exhaustive subset regression scales as 2^p, where p is the number of explanatory variables, making it infeasible for large models
- Stepwise selection reduces the search space to scale by 1 + p(p+1)/2, which is much more computationally feasible
- The AIC and BIC criteria are calculated using equations where k is the number of model parameters and n is the number of observations, with an additional term for corrected AIC (AICc) of (2k^2 + 2k)/(n-k-1)
- The methodology targets medium-to-heavy mass nuclides with A > 20, where the Reich-Moore approximation of R-Matrix theory is most commonly applied

## Context

This work extends the proof of concept from Walton (2022) to full fidelity for real evaluations, addressing the recognized need for reproducibility in nuclear data evaluation formally identified by WPEC subgroup 49. The methodology contrasts with the traditional GLLS approach used in SAMMY and REFIT, which requires manual addition of resonance parameters and is heavily dependent on prior evaluations, and complements recent developments such as MCMC sampling in SAMMY and spin group assignment tools from Brookhaven National Laboratory. The approach enables evaluators to obtain reliable and consistent model estimates without prior evaluation dependence, though data curation and final model complexity selection remain in the evaluator's domain.
