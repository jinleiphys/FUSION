# Anomalous Chiral Transport in Heavy Ion Collisions from Anomalous-Viscous Fluid Dynamics

> Chiral anomaly is a fundamental aspect of quantum theories with chiral fermions. How such microscopic anomaly manifests itself in a macroscopic many-body system with chiral fermions, is a highly nontrivial question that has recently attracted significant interest. As it turns out, unusual transport currents can be induced by chiral anomaly under suitable conditions in such systems, with the notable example of the Chiral Magnetic Effect (CME) where a vector current (e.g. electric current) is generated along an external magnetic field. A lot of efforts have been made to search for CME in heavy ion collisions, by measuring the charge separation effect induced by the CME transport. A crucial challenge in such effort, is the quantitative prediction for the CME signal. In this paper, we develop the Anomalous-Viscous Fluid Dynamics (AVFD) framework, which implements the anomalous fluid dynamics to describe the evolution of fermion currents in QGP, on top of the neutral bulk background described by the VISH2+1 hydrodynamic simulations for heavy ion collisions. With this new tool, we quantitatively and systematically investigate the dependence of the CME signal to a series of theoretical inputs and associated uncertainties. With realistic estimates of initial conditions and magnetic field lifetime, the predicted CME signal is quantitatively consistent with measured change separation data in 200GeV Au-Au collisions. Based on analysis of Au-Au collisions, we further make predictions for the CME observable to be measured in the planned isobaric (Ru-Ru v.s. Zr-Zr ) collision experiment, which could provide a most decisive test of the CME in heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper develops the Anomalous-Viscous Fluid Dynamics (AVFD) framework to provide quantitative predictions for the Chiral Magnetic Effect (CME) signal in heavy ion collisions, and claims that with realistic estimates of initial conditions and magnetic field lifetime, the predicted CME signal is quantitatively consistent with measured charge separation data in 200 GeV Au-Au collisions. The hedge is that the magnetic field lifetime remains an open question and the results depend substantially on the chosen time dependence of the magnetic field.

## Method

The authors implement anomalous fluid dynamics to evolve right-handed and left-handed fermion currents as linear perturbations on top of a neutral bulk background described by the VISH2+1 viscous hydrodynamic simulation, which is boost-invariant and uses the Israel-Stewart framework with a lattice-based equation of state. The fermion current evolution equations include both normal viscous transport (diffusion with relaxation) and the anomalous CME current term proportional to the chiral chemical potential times the magnetic field, with the magnetic field parameterized as B = B0/(1 + τ²/τ_B²) with τ_B = 0.6 fm/c. The charge separation signal is computed from the dipole term in the electric charge chemical potential on the freeze-out surface and converted to the H-correlator (the variance of the charge dipole coefficient a₁).

## Key numbers

- Peak magnetic field at √s_NN = 200 GeV Au-Au collisions reaches eB ~ 5 m_π² (or B ~ 10¹⁵ Tesla); at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV LHC collisions it reaches ~ 70 m_π².
- The hydrodynamic evolution starts at proper time τ₀,hydro = 0.6 fm/c, and the magnetic field lifetime parameter τ_B is set to 0.6 fm/c for the baseline calculation.
- The CME signal (charge separation a₁) grows rapidly with magnetic field lifetime τ_B, with visible differences among three parameterizations (B ∝ (1+τ²/τ_B²)⁻¹, (1+τ²/τ_B²)⁻³/², and exp(-τ/τ_B)) even for the same τ_B value.
- The initial axial charge condition used for comparison of magnetic field effects is n₅/s = 0.1.

## Context

This work extends prior anomalous hydrodynamic approaches by Yin (2015), Hirono (2014), Yee (2013), and Hongo (2013) by incorporating realistic bulk evolution from VISH2+1 (Shen, 2014) and normal viscous transport effects simultaneously, making it the most matured framework for simulating anomalous transport in heavy ion collisions. The AVFD framework enables quantitative calibration of theoretical uncertainties in magnetic field lifetime, initial conditions, and transport coefficients, and provides predictions for the planned isobaric Ru-Ru versus Zr-Zr collision experiment at RHIC, which could provide a decisive test of the CME.
