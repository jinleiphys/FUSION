# Cluster radioactivity from trans-tin to superheavy region using an improved empirical formula

> A simple relation $(aZ_{c} + b)(Z_{d}/Q)^{1/2} + (cZ_{c} + d)$ of estimation of the half-life of cluster emission is further improved for cluster and $α$-decays, separately, by incorporating isospin of parent nucleus as well as angular momentum taken away by the emitted particle. This improved version is not only found robust in producing experimental half-lives belonging to the trans-tin and trans-lead regions but also elucidates cluster emission in superheavy nuclei over the usual $α$-decay. Considering daughter nuclei around the doubly magic $^{100}$Sn and $^{208}$Pb nuclei for trans-tin and trans-lead (including superheavy) parents, respectively, a systematic and extensive study of 56$\leq$Z$\leq$120 isotopes is performed for the light and heavy cluster emissions. A fair competition among cluster emission, $α$-decay, spontaneous fission, and $β$-decay is observed in this broad range resulting in a substantial probability of C to Sr clusters from several nuclei, which demonstrates the adequacy of shell effects. The present article proposes a single, improved, latest-fitted, and effective formula of cluster radioactivity that can be used to estimate precise half-lives for a wide range of the periodic chart from trans-tin to superheavy nuclei.

## Key claim
The authors present an improved empirical formula (ITM) for cluster radioactivity half-lives that, by separately fitting coefficients for trans-tin and trans-lead regions and incorporating isospin and angular momentum terms, achieves lower root-mean-square error than existing formulas across a wide range of nuclei (Z=56 to 120), though the formula is fitted separately for the two regions rather than being a single universal parameter set.

## Method
The authors modified the Tavares-Medeiros (TM) formula by adding an isospin-dependent term (proportional to sqrt[I(I+1)]) and an angular-momentum-dependent term (proportional to sqrt[l(l+1)]), then fitted the six coefficients separately for cluster decay and alpha decay in both the trans-tin region (daughter near 100Sn) and the trans-lead region (daughter near 208Pb). For the trans-tin region, where only one experimental cluster decay datum exists (12C from 114Ba), they supplemented the fit with 199 GLDM theoretical data points; for the trans-lead region, they used 61 experimental cluster decay data points. They then calculated half-lives for all isotopes with Z=56-120 and compared cluster emission with alpha decay, beta decay (trans-tin), and spontaneous fission (trans-lead and superheavy) using branching ratios.

## Key numbers
- RMSE of ITM formula for 62 cluster decay data points: 0.66, compared to 1.81 for the original TM formula, 1.52 for UDL, and 1.94 for New UDL.
- For the trans-tin region, the ITM formula yields theoretical half-life uncertainties of ±0.71 (cluster) and ±0.40 (alpha); for the trans-lead region, ±0.56 (cluster) and ±0.41 (alpha).
- For 114Ba emitting 12C, the ITM formula gives log10 T1/2 = 4.68 s (cluster) with Q-value 19.00 MeV and l=0, compared to the experimental lower bound of >4.10 s.
- For 113La emitting 13N to 100Sn, the ITM formula predicts log10 T1/2 = -0.51 s (cluster) with Q-value 26.61 MeV, yielding a branching ratio of 13.13% for cluster emission versus 86.86% for alpha decay.

## Context
This work extends the empirical formula approach of Tavares and Medeiros (2013) by incorporating isospin and angular momentum effects, following similar modifications applied to other formulas such as the improved unified formula (IUF) of Ismail et al. (2022) and the improved semi-empirical formula (ISEF) of Cheng et al. (2022). The authors address the limitation that previous formulas like UDL (Qi et al., 2009) overestimate the 114Ba half-life by four orders of magnitude, while Horoi (2004) and NRDX (2008) formulas fail for superheavy clusters. The results enable systematic predictions of cluster radioactivity competition with alpha decay, beta decay, and spontaneous fission across the entire chart from trans-tin to superheavy nuclei, providing guidance for future experimental searches.
