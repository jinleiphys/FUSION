# Entrance Channel Dynamics of Hot and Cold Fusion Reactions Leading to Superheavy Elements

> We investigate the entrance channel dynamics for the reactions $\mathrm{^{70}Zn}+\mathrm{^{208}Pb}$ and $\mathrm{^{48}Ca}+\mathrm{^{238}U}$ using the fully microscopic time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory coupled with a density constraint. We calculate excitation energies and capture cross-sections relevant for the study of superheavy formations. We discuss the deformation dependence of the ion-ion potential for the $\mathrm{^{48}Ca}+\mathrm{^{238}U}$ system and perform an alignment angle averaging for the calculation of the capture cross-section. The results show that this parameter-free approach can generate results in good agreement with experiment and other theories.

# Entrance Channel Dynamics of Hot and Cold Fusion Reactions Leading to Superheavy Elements

## Key claim

The fully microscopic time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory coupled with a density constraint can generate capture cross-sections and excitation energies for superheavy element formation reactions that agree reasonably well with experiment and other models, but only for the entrance channel dynamics up to the capture point; the subsequent compound nucleus formation, quasi-fission, and survival probabilities are beyond the scope of TDHF due to the absence of quantal shell effects and fluctuations.

## Method

The authors performed 3D TDHF calculations using the Skyrme SLy4 force for the reactions 70Zn+208Pb and 48Ca+238U, representing cold and hot fusion pathways to element Z=112. They extracted ion-ion interaction potentials via the density-constrained TDHF (DC-TDHF) method, which performs a static Hartree-Fock minimization at each time step while holding the instantaneous TDHF densities fixed, and calculated excitation energies by separating the TDHF motion into collective and intrinsic parts. For the deformed 238U target, they performed calculations at three alignment angles (β=0°, 45°, 90°) and averaged the capture cross-section over alignment probabilities from Coulomb excitation.

## Key numbers

- For the 48Ca+238U system, the capture cross-section calculated using the Incoming Wave Boundary Condition method shows reasonable agreement with experimental data from Og07; the alignment probability due to Coulomb excitation is in the range 0.46–0.52 and does not vary appreciably with energy.
- For the 70Zn+208Pb system at the experimental laboratory energy of 346.1 MeV, the calculated capture cross-section is in reasonable agreement with the model calculation from GH00; at the lower laboratory energy of 343.8 MeV, the lower potential energy curve substantially underestimates the model calculation.
- The excitation energy at the potential minimum inside the barrier for the 48Ca+238U system at β=0° alignment is higher than for the 70Zn+208Pb system at the same center-of-mass energy, but the β=0° contribution is multiplied by sin(β) in the alignment averaging, making its contribution very small.
- For the 48Ca+238U system, the potential barriers for polar orientation (β=0°) are much lower and peak at larger ion-ion separation distances than for equatorial orientation (β=90°), which are much higher and peak at smaller R values.

## Context

This work extends the DC-TDHF method developed by Umar and Oberacker (2006) to superheavy element formation, specifically comparing the cold-fusion reaction 70Zn+208Pb (used by Hofmann et al., 2000, 2002) with the hot-fusion reaction 48Ca+238U (used by Oganessian et al., 2004, 2007). The study addresses the long-standing question of why hot-fusion evaporation residue cross-sections are up to three times larger than cold-fusion ones, by examining entrance channel dynamics that influence the dinuclear system at the capture point. The results enable future microscopic calculations of capture cross-sections for other superheavy systems and highlight the need for improved Skyrme parametrizations that incorporate deformation and scattering data, as well as the need to go beyond TDHF to describe the subsequent quasi-fission and fusion-fission stages.
