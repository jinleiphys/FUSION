# Low-energy nuclear physics and global neutron star properties

> We address the question of the role of low-energy nuclear physics data in constraining neutron star global properties, e.g., masses, radii, angular momentum, and tidal deformability, in the absence of a phase transition in dense matter. To do so, we assess the capacity of 415 relativistic mean field and non-relativistic Skyrme-type interactions to reproduce the ground state binding energies, the charge radii and the giant monopole resonances of a set of spherical nuclei. The interactions are classified according to their ability to describe these characteristics and we show that a tight correlation between the symmetry energy and its slope is obtained providing $N=Z$ and $N\ne Z$ nuclei are described with the same accuracy (mainly driven by the charge radius data). By additionally imposing the constraints from isobaric analog states and neutron skin radius in $^{208}$Pb, we obtain the following estimates: $E_{sym,2}=31.8\pm 0.7$ MeV and $L_{sym,2}=58.1\pm 9.0$ MeV. We then analyze predictions of neutron star properties and we find that the 1.4$M_\odot$ neutron star (NS) radius lies between 12 and 14 km for the "better" nuclear interactions. We show that i) the better reproduction of low-energy nuclear physics data by the nuclear models only weakly impacts the global properties of canonical mass neutron stars and ii) the experimental constraint on the symmetry energy is the most effective one for reducing the uncertainties in NS matter. However, since the density region where constraints are required are well above densities in finite nuclei, the largest uncertainty originates from the density dependence of the EDF, which remains largely unknown.

## Key claim

The paper shows that reproducing low-energy nuclear physics data (binding energies, charge radii, giant monopole resonances) with nuclear models only weakly constrains the global properties of canonical-mass neutron stars (such as radius), provided no phase transition occurs in dense matter; the largest uncertainty in neutron star predictions instead originates from the unknown density dependence of the energy density functional at densities well above saturation.

## Method

The authors assessed 415 relativistic mean field (RMF) and non-relativistic Skyrme-type interactions by comparing their predictions to experimental ground-state binding energies, charge radii, and isoscalar giant monopole resonance energies for a set of spherical doubly magic nuclei. They classified the interactions into groups based on their accuracy in reproducing these low-energy data, then propagated the predictions of each group to neutron star matter via the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations, and analyzed correlations between nuclear empirical parameters (symmetry energy, its slope, incompressibility) and neutron star global properties.

## Key numbers

* The symmetry energy at saturation density (quadratic contribution) is constrained to \(E_{\mathrm{sym},2} = 31.8 \pm 0.7\) MeV, and its slope to \(L_{\mathrm{sym},2} = 58.1 \pm 9.0\) MeV, after imposing constraints from isobaric analog states and the neutron skin radius in \(^{208}\)Pb.
* For the "better" nuclear interactions, the radius of a 1.4 \(M_\odot\) neutron star lies between 12 and 14 km.
* The uncertainty in the model prediction for the ISGMR centroid energy in \(^{208}\)Pb is taken as \(\delta_{\mathrm{GMR}} = 0.7\) MeV, corresponding to an uncertainty in the incompressibility modulus of about 15 MeV.
* The uncertainty in the nuclear charge radius used for model filtering is \(\delta_{R_{ch}} \approx 0.1 A^{-1/3}\) fm.

## Context

This work extends earlier analyses by Stone (2007), Dutra (2012), and Dutra (2014), which confronted large sets of Skyrme and relativistic interactions with neutron star observables without first filtering them by their ability to describe finite nuclei. The present study directly compares model predictions to experimental low-energy nuclear data before propagating to dense matter, and finds that such filtering only weakly reduces the spread in neutron star radii. The results contradict the linear correlation between tidal deformability and incompressibility reported by Souza (2020) and instead align with the weak correlation found by Wei (2020) using Brueckner-Hartree-Fock models. The paper enables future work to focus on constraining the density dependence of the equation of state at two to four times saturation density, rather than relying solely on low-energy nuclear data.
