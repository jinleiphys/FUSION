# A model for nuclear matter fragmentation: phase diagram and cluster distributions

> We develop a model in the framework of nuclear fragmentation at thermodynamic equilibrium which can be mapped onto an Ising model with constant magnetization. We work out the thermodynamic properties of the model as well as the properties of the fragment size distributions. We show that two types of phase transitions can be found for high density systems. They merge into a unique transition at low density. An analysis of the critical exponents which characterize observables for different densities in the thermodynamic limit shows that these transitions look like continuous second order transitions.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the lattice gas model (LGM) for nuclear fragmentation, when formulated in the canonical ensemble with a fixed number of particles (the Ising model with fixed magnetization, IMFM), exhibits a phase diagram with two distinct phase transitions for high densities that merge into a single transition at low density, and these transitions appear to be continuous second-order transitions in the thermodynamic limit, a result that contrasts with the first-order transition found in the grand canonical formulation of the same model.

## Method

The authors map the canonical-ensemble LGM with fixed particle number onto a three-dimensional Ising model with fixed magnetization (IMFM) and simulate it using a Metropolis spin-exchange algorithm (Kawasaki dynamics) on cubic lattices of sizes up to \(L=48\). They compute the specific heat \(C_v\) and the second moment of the droplet size distribution \(S_2\) as functions of inverse temperature \(\beta\) at fixed densities \(\rho = 0.3, 0.5, 0.7\), using finite-size scaling to extract critical exponents \(\alpha\), \(\nu\), \(\bar\gamma\), and \(\bar\nu\). Droplets are defined via the Coniglio-Klein bond probability \(p_b = 1 - e^{-2J/k_B T}\) applied to Ising clusters.

## Key numbers

* At \(\rho = 0.5\) (the symmetric point), the thermodynamic transition occurs at \(\beta_c^\mathrm{T}(\infty) \approx 0.22165\) (in units where \(J/k_B = 1\)), with critical exponent \(\alpha/\nu = 0.10(2)\) and \(\nu = 0.63(2)\), consistent with the 3D Ising universality class.
* At \(\rho = 0.3\), the thermodynamic transition has \(\alpha/\nu = 0.10(3)\) and \(\nu = 0.63(3)\), showing the same universality class as at \(\rho = 0.5\).
* The droplet transition at \(\rho = 0.5\) yields \(\bar\gamma/\bar\nu = 1.97(3)\) and \(\bar\nu = 0.88(2)\), giving \(\bar\eta = 0.03(3)\) and \(\tau = 2.21(2)\) from the hyperscaling relation.
* At \(\rho = 0.7\), the droplet transition gives \(\bar\gamma/\bar\nu = 2.00(3)\) and \(\bar\nu = 0.89(2)\), yielding \(\bar\eta = 0.00(3)\) and \(\tau = 2.20(2)\).
* The exponent \(\tau\) measured directly from the droplet size distribution at the droplet transition is \(\tau = 2.20(2)\) for \(\rho = 0.5\) and \(\tau = 2.19(2)\) for \(\rho = 0.7\).

## Context

This work extends the lattice gas model (LGM) approach to nuclear fragmentation previously studied by Campi (1994), Pan and Das Gupta (1995, 1996), and Chomaz et al. (1997), who used the grand canonical ensemble with fixed chemical potential. The authors explicitly show that the canonical and grand canonical formulations are not equivalent: the grand canonical LGM exhibits a first-order liquid-gas transition, while the canonical IMFM shows continuous second-order transitions. The droplet transition line (where fragment size distributions follow power laws) is identified with the Kertész line from percolation theory (Kertész, 1989), and the results enable future work on the microcanonical ensemble and direct comparison with experimental multifragmentation data, where the exponent \(\tau \approx 2.2\) has been observed and associated with three-dimensional bond percolation (Zheng, 1995).
