# Resonance capture by hydrogenous impurities and losses of ultracold neutrons in solid material traps

> The capture of trapped ultracold neutrons (UCNs) by closed hydrogenous impurities within a solid coating of the trap is discussed as a possible cause of observed anomalously large losses of UCNs in solid material UCN traps. Then significant losses of UCNs arise only if resonances occur in the UCN-impurity scattering amplitude. For a large size impurity, higher partial waves in the UCN-impurity interaction are important, and they are taken into account in the present paper. The method of the calculation is applicable to irregular shape impurities as well. A small distortion of an impurity shape, if it splits the resonance, can increase the UCN losses by a few times. UCN losses in the beryllium trap are calculated assuming they are due to the UCN capture by ice spherical impurities within the coating of the trap walls. Both s- and p-wave resonances contribute significantly to the UCN losses considered. As an example, observed anomalous large UCN losses are achieved if the average radius of the impurity is about 600 Angstroms and the impurity density is about 3*10^{14}/cm^3. A distortion of the spherical shape of the impurity could increase the UCN losses and therefore decrease the impurity density.

## Key claim

Significant ultracold neutron (UCN) losses in solid material traps can arise from resonance capture by hydrogenous impurities within the trap coating, but only when resonances occur in the UCN-impurity scattering amplitude; for large impurities, higher partial waves (beyond s-wave) are essential, and a small distortion of the impurity shape that splits a resonance can increase losses by a factor of a few.

## Method

The author develops a theoretical framework for UCN scattering by a single isolated impurity embedded in the solid coating of a trap, using a wave-function matching approach at the vacuum-material boundary. The UCN wave function in the material is expressed as a superposition of analytically continued plane-wave solutions for an infinite medium containing impurities, and the scattering amplitude is expanded over partial waves (s, p, d, etc.) for a spherical impurity with a constant interaction potential. Numerical computations are performed for ice impurities in a beryllium trap, solving the resulting integral equations for the scattering amplitude and integrating over impurity depths and UCN angular distributions to obtain loss coefficients.

## Key numbers

- The experimental UCN losses in beryllium traps require an effective loss parameter η = 3×10⁻⁵, which exceeds the coherent absorption value for beryllium (η = 2.47×10⁻⁷) by a factor of about 100.
- For hydrogen impurities, the imaginary part parameter w ≈ 3×10⁻⁶; achieving η ∼ 10⁻⁵ with small impurities would require an impurity volume fraction p_V ∼ 10, which is unacceptable.
- Under resonance conditions, the UCN-impurity scattering amplitude can reach magnitudes ∼ 100 km.
- Observed anomalous large UCN losses are achieved when the average impurity radius is about 600 Å and the impurity density is about 3×10¹⁴/cm³.
- The impurity radii considered range from 453.7 Å to 875.2 Å; radii below 464.2 Å produce no resonance for UCN energies below 58 cm (1 cm energy = 1.025×10⁻⁹ eV).
- The s-wave resonance occurs for radii approximately 460–740 Å; both s- and p-wave resonances contribute for radii larger than 740 Å.
- The impurities occupy 0.25 of the total volume in the example fitting experimental data.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of UCN losses in beryllium and graphite traps by Serebrov (2000s) and others, which reported anomalously large losses that could not be explained by inelastic scattering (which would give a ∼T³ temperature dependence), surface hydrogen contamination, or coherent absorption by beryllium. The paper also addresses the concept of UCN localization around lattice defects proposed by Serebrov, which the author argues is based on a mistaken solution of the scattering problem (citing Barabanov). The method generalizes earlier calculations of UCN scattering by vacuum cavities (Ignatovich) to include higher partial waves and arbitrary impurity shapes, and it enables future calculations for more realistic impurity distributions (shape, orientation, and size) that could reduce the required impurity volume fraction.
