# Color path-integral Monte-Carlo simulations of quark-gluon plasma: Thermodynamic and transport properties

> Based on the quasiparticle model of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP), a color quantum path-integral Monte-Carlo (PIMC) method for calculation of thermodynamic properties and -- closely related to the latter -- a Wigner dynamics method for calculation of transport properties of the QGP are formulated. The QGP partition function is presented in the form of a color path integral with a new relativistic measure instead of the Gaussian one traditionally used in the Feynman-Wiener path integral. It is shown that the PIMC method is able to reproduce the lattice QCD equation of state at zero baryon chemical potential at realistic model parameters (i.e. quasiparticle masses and coupling constant) and also yields valuable insight into the internal structure of the QGP. Our results indicate that the QGP reveals quantum liquid-like (rather than gas-like) properties up to the highest considered temperature of 525 MeV. The pair distribution functions clearly reflect the existence of gluon-gluon bound states, i.e. glueballs, at temperatures just above the phase transition, while meson-like $q\bar{q}$ bound states are not found. The calculated self-diffusion coefficient agrees well with some estimates of the heavy-quark diffusion constant available from recent lattice data and also with an analysis of heavy-quark quenching in experiments on ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions, however, appreciably exceeds other estimates. The lattice and heavy-quark-quenching results on the heavy-quark diffusion are still rather diverse. The obtained results for the shear viscosity are in the range of those deduced from an analysis of the experimental elliptic flow in ultrarelativistic heavy ions collisions, i.e. in terms the viscosity-to-entropy ratio, $1/4π< η/S < 2.5/4π$, in the temperature range from 170 to 440 MeV.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a color path-integral Monte-Carlo (PIMC) method, based on a quasiparticle model with relativistic kinetic energy and color Coulomb interactions, can reproduce the lattice QCD equation of state at zero baryon chemical potential and reveals that the quark-gluon plasma exhibits quantum liquid-like properties up to 525 MeV, though the authors note that the lattice and heavy-quark-quenching results on heavy-quark diffusion remain "still rather diverse."

## Method

The authors formulate the QGP partition function as a color path integral using a relativistic measure (replacing the Gaussian measure of standard Feynman-Wiener path integrals) and develop a procedure for sampling color variables according to the SU(3) group Haar measure with quadratic and cubic Casimir conditions. For thermodynamic properties, they use PIMC simulations with a Kelbg-type effective potential to handle the color Coulomb interaction, while for transport properties they combine the path integral approach with a Wigner dynamics method, using Kubo formulas to calculate kinetic coefficients from equilibrium quasiparticle configurations generated by PIMC.

## Key numbers

- The QGP exhibits quantum liquid-like properties up to the highest considered temperature of 525 MeV
- The viscosity-to-entropy ratio lies in the range \(1/4\pi < \eta/S < 2.5/4\pi\) for temperatures from 170 to 440 MeV
- The minimum of the viscosity-to-entropy ratio is reached at approximately 300 MeV, above the expected phase transition temperature
- Quasiparticle masses are of order or higher than the temperature (\(m \gsim T\)) at zero net-baryon density

## Context

This work extends previous classical nonrelativistic molecular dynamics simulations of the QGP by Shuryak and collaborators (2004-2005) by incorporating quantum effects, spin statistics, and dominant relativistic effects through a path integral Monte-Carlo framework. The approach builds on earlier PIMC methods successfully applied to strongly coupled electrodynamic plasmas by Filinov, Bonitz, and others (2000-2007), and on the use of Kelbg-type effective potentials for quantum corrections. The results enable predictions of transport properties (self-diffusion and shear viscosity) that are still poorly accessible from lattice QCD, which has only computed viscosity for pure gluonic matter (Meyer, 2007).
