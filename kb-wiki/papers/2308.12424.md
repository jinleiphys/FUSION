# Virtual states in the coupled-channel problems with an improved complex scaling method

> We improve the complex scaling method (CSM) to obtain virtual states, which were previously challenging in the conventional CSM. Our approach solves the Schrödinger equation in the momentum space as an eigenvalue problem by choosing the flexible contours. It proves to be highly effective in identifying the poles across the different Riemann sheets in the multichannel scatterings. It is more straightforward and efficient than searching for the zeros of the Fredholm determinant of the Lippmann-Schwinger equation using the root-finding algorithms. This advancement significantly extends the capabilities of the CSM in accurately characterizing the resonances and virtual states in quantum systems.

## Key claim

The improved complex scaling method (ICSM) solves the Schrödinger equation in momentum space as an eigenvalue problem by choosing flexible integration contours, enabling direct access to virtual state poles that the conventional complex scaling method (CSM) cannot reach, provided the potential remains analytic along the chosen contour.

## Method

The authors replace the standard real-axis momentum integration contour with a piecewise flexible contour in the complex momentum plane, designed to enclose the target Riemann sheet region containing virtual state poles. They discretize the resulting integral equation using Gauss-Legendre quadrature and solve it as an eigenvalue problem, applying distinct contours for different channels in multichannel problems. The method is tested on separable toy potentials, the HAL QCD \(DD^*\) potential, and the high-quality \(NN\) Reid93 potential, with results compared against the Lippmann-Schwinger equation (LSE) and conventional CSM.

## Key numbers

- For the separable potential \(V_{\mathrm{Ex.1}}\) with coupling \(g=1\): virtual state pole at \(E_{\mathrm{pole}} = -1.526\) (dimensionless units) on Riemann sheet II, obtained by both ICSM and LSE; CSM yields no pole.
- For the \(np\) (\(^1S_0\)) Reid93 potential: virtual state pole at \(E_{np,v} = -0.066\ \mathrm{MeV}\) from both ICSM and LSE; CSM fails to obtain the virtual state.
- For the HAL QCD \(DD^*\) potential: virtual state pole at \(E^{\mathrm{ICSM}}_{DD^*,v} = -69\ \mathrm{keV}\) from ICSM and LSE; CSM yields no pole.
- For the two-channel separable potential with \((g_1,g_2,g_c)=(2,8,1.5)\): poles at \(E_{\mathrm{pole}} = -0.496\) on RS-(II,I), \(1.633-0.082i\) on RS-(II,I), \(1.633+0.082i\) on RS-(II,I), and \(-0.862\) on RS-(II,II); CSM obtains only the \(1.633-0.082i\) pole.

## Context

This work extends the complex scaling method (Aguilar 1971, Balslev 1971) to directly obtain virtual states, which previously required indirect approaches such as analytical continuation in the coupling constant (Tanaka 1999), the Jost function method (Masui 2000), or extraction from level density (Odsuren 2017). The ICSM is more straightforward and efficient than searching for zeros of the Fredholm determinant of the Lippmann-Schwinger equation using root-finding algorithms. This advancement enables accurate characterization of near-threshold poles in systems such as the \(T_{cc}^+\) state studied via the HAL QCD potential (Lyu 2023) and the \(np\) virtual state in nuclear physics (Adhikari 1982, Carlson 1997).
