# On the hadron mass decomposition

> We argue that the standard decompositions of the hadron mass overlook pressure effects, and hence should be interpreted with great care. Based on the semiclassical picture, we propose a new decomposition that properly accounts for these pressure effects. Because of Lorentz covariance, we stress that the hadron mass decomposition automatically comes along with a stability constraint, which we discuss for the first time. We show also that if a hadron is seen as made of quarks and gluons, one cannot decompose its mass into more than two contributions without running into trouble with the consistency of the physical interpretation. In particular, the so-called quark mass and trace anomaly contributions appear to be purely conventional. Based on the current phenomenological values, we find that in average quarks exert a repulsive force inside nucleons, balanced exactly by the gluon attractive force.

## Key claim

The standard decompositions of the hadron mass (trace decomposition and Ji's decomposition) overlook pressure effects and should be interpreted with great care; the paper proposes a new decomposition based on a semiclassical picture that properly accounts for these pressure effects, and argues that if a hadron is seen as made of quarks and gluons, one cannot decompose its mass into more than two contributions without running into trouble with the consistency of the physical interpretation.

## Method

The author starts from the general parametrization of the forward matrix element of a rank-two tensor in a hadron state, expressed in terms of two energy-momentum form factors \(A_i(0)\) and \(\bar C_i(0)\). By interpreting the expectation value of each contribution to the energy-momentum tensor averaged over the hadron proper volume, the author identifies the partial proper energy density \(\varepsilon_i\) and isotropic pressure \(p_i\) for each constituent, leading to a decomposition of the hadron mass into partial internal energies \(U_i = \varepsilon_i V\) and a stability constraint \(\sum_i W_i = 0\) where \(W_i = p_i V\) is the partial pressure-volume work. The QCD energy-momentum tensor is then decomposed into quark and gluon contributions only, forming an effective coupled two-fluid picture.

## Key numbers

- The current light quark masses from the Brout-Englert-Higgs mechanism correspond to only about 1% of the nucleon mass.
- The QCD scale parameter is \(\Lambda_\text{QCD} \approx 0.2\) GeV.
- In Ji's decomposition, the trace of the EMT contributes to 25% of the hadron mass (\(\langle\hat T^{00}\rangle|_{\vec P=\vec 0} = \tfrac{1}{4} M\)) and the traceless part contributes 75% (\(\langle\bar T^{00}\rangle|_{\vec P=\vec 0} = \tfrac{3}{4} M\)).
- For the pressure-volume work in the rest frame, the traceless part contributes \(\langle\bar T^{33}\rangle|_{\vec P=\vec 0} = \tfrac{1}{4} M\) and the trace part contributes \(\langle\hat T^{33}\rangle|_{\vec P=\vec 0} = -\tfrac{1}{4} M\).

## Context

This work critiques the standard trace decomposition (Shifman 1978, Kharzeev 2002, Roberts 2016) and Ji's decomposition (Ji 1995) of the hadron mass, arguing that both overlook pressure effects and that the so-called quark mass and trace anomaly contributions are purely conventional. The paper extends the semiclassical picture used in the MIT bag model (Chodos 1974) and the concept of energy-momentum form factors (Ji 1996, Leader 2013) to propose a new decomposition based on an effective coupled two-fluid picture of quarks and gluons. This enables a clearer physical interpretation where quarks exert a repulsive force and gluons an attractive force inside nucleons, balanced exactly for stability, and suggests that future work should avoid decomposing the hadron mass into more than two contributions.
