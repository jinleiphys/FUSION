# Initial energy-momentum to final flow: a general framework for heavy-ion collisions

> The evolution of a relativistic heavy-ion collision is typically understood as a process that transmutes the initial geometry of the system into the final momentum distribution of observed hadrons, which can be described via a cumulant expansion of the initial distribution of energy density and is represented at leading order as the well-known eccentricity scaling of anisotropic flow. We extend this framework to include the contribution from initial momentum-space properties, as encoded in other components of the energy-momentum tensor. We confirm the validity of the framework in state-of-the-art hydrodynamic simulations of large and small systems. With this new framework, it is possible to separate the effects of early-time dynamics from those of final-state evolution, even in the case when the distribution of energy does not fully determine subsequent evolution, as for example, in small systems. Specifically, we answer the question of when and how azimuthal correlations from the initial state survive to the final state. In very small systems such as $p$-$p$, for example, initial momentum degrees of freedom dominate over energy. Thus, even if the system forms a quark-gluon plasma that is well described by hydrodynamics, the usual hydrodynamic picture of the transmutation of initial geometry to final momentum anisotropy is broken. Nevertheless, we show that the hydrodynamic response to the full energy-momentum tensor can be well understood in a similar manner as larger systems. Additionally, this framework elucidates the generic features of the system's evolution that are responsible for the impressive success of hydrodynamic simulations, but which may still hold even in cases where hydrodynamics is not applicable.

## Key claim

The paper extends the standard cumulant expansion framework that connects initial geometry to final anisotropic flow in heavy-ion collisions by including contributions from initial momentum density and stress tensor components, showing that even in small systems where the usual geometric picture breaks down, the hydrodynamic response to the full energy-momentum tensor can still be understood in a similar manner as larger systems.

## Method

The authors propose a generalized scalar field rho = T^{tau tau} - alpha partial_i T^{tau i} + beta partial_i partial_j T^{ij} from which cumulants are constructed via a 2D Fourier transform, then test the resulting estimators for elliptic and triangular flow against state-of-the-art IP-Glasma + MUSIC hydrodynamic simulations of Pb-Pb, p-Pb, and p-p collisions at LHC energies, followed by UrQMD hadronic afterburner.

## Key numbers

- The leading-order estimator for elliptic flow V_2 involves the term -2 alpha <r e^{i phi}>_U - 2 beta <1>_C added to the standard energy-density eccentricity, where U = T^{tau x} + i T^{tau y} and C = T^{xx} - T^{yy} + 2 i T^{xy}
- The leading-order estimator for triangular flow V_3 involves the term -3 alpha <r^2 e^{i2 phi}>_U - 6 beta <r e^{i phi}>_C added to the standard energy-density triangularity
- The Pearson correlation coefficient Q_n(alpha, beta) = Re <V_n V_n^{(est)*}> / sqrt(<|V_n|^2> <|V_n^{(est)}|^2>) is used to quantify the event-by-event success of the estimator, with Q_n = 1 indicating a perfect linear correlation

## Context

This work extends the cumulant expansion framework originally developed for energy-density-only initial conditions (Gardim 2011, Hippert 2020) to include momentum-space degrees of freedom, addressing a limitation noted in prior studies of small collision systems. The framework enables separation of early-time dynamics from final-state evolution effects, and the authors note that the quantity <1>_C in their estimator closely resembles the "momentum eccentricity" previously used as a proxy for elliptic flow (Luzum 2009, Giacalone 2020). The approach is motivated by conservation laws and is expected to hold even when hydrodynamics is not strictly applicable, as long as energy-momentum conservation dominates the dynamics.
