# Cosmic ray strangelets

> Searching for strangelets in cosmic rays may be the best way to test the possible stability of strange quark matter. I review calculations of the astrophysical strangelet flux in the GV--TV rigidity range, which will be investigated from the Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer (AMS-02) on the International Space Station, and discuss the merits of strangelets as ultra-high energy cosmic rays at EeV--ZeV energies, beyond the Greisen-Zatsepin-Kuzmin cutoff. I also address some ``counter-arguments'' sometimes raised against the possibility of stable strangelets. It will be argued that stability of strange quark matter remains a viable possibility, which must be tested by experiments.

# Cosmic ray strangelets

## Key claim

Strange quark matter stability remains a viable possibility that must be tested by experiments, but the actual flux predictions for cosmic ray strangelets are strongly parameter-dependent, with uncertainties hidden in a normalization parameter Λ of order unity that is proportional to the galactic production rate and inversely proportional to interstellar medium density and cosmic ray confinement volume.

## Method

The author calculates the astrophysical strangelet flux by solving a steady-state propagation equation that includes source terms from supernova acceleration, diffusion, escape from the Galaxy, energy loss, spallation, and reacceleration from passing supernova shock waves. The flux is estimated analytically under the assumption that spallation dominates at the cutoff rigidity set by solar modulation or geomagnetic cutoff, with all strangelets released from binary strange star collisions assumed to have a single baryon number A due to the lack of a reliable mass spectrum calculation.

## Key numbers

- Galactic binary neutron star (quark star) coalescence rate: 83.0_{-66.1}^{+209.1} Myr^{-1} (95% confidence), corresponding to roughly one collision every 10,000 years in the Galaxy
- Conservative galactic strangelet production rate estimate: 10^{-10} solar masses per year
- Typical fragment baryon number from tidal disruption: A ≈ 4 × 10^{38} σ_{20} a_{30}^3, where σ_{20} ≈ 1 is surface tension in units of 20 MeV/fm^3 and a_{30} is stellar separation in units of 30 km
- Total flux formula for solar modulation domination: F_total ≈ 2 × 10^5 m^{-2} yr^{-1} sterad^{-1} A^{-1.067} Z^{-0.6} Λ, reproducing numerical results within 20% for Z > 10
- For ordinary strangelets with strange quark mass 150 MeV: Z ≈ 0.1A for A << 700 and Z ≈ 8A^{1/3} for A >> 700
- For colour-flavour locked strangelets: Z ≈ 0.3A^{2/3}
- Maximum baryon number of a gravitationally unstable strange star: A_max ≈ 2 × 10^{57}
- AMS-02 detector acceptance: approximately 0.5 m^2 sterad, with a 0.86 Tesla superconducting dipole field
- Solar modulation cutoff rigidity: R_SM ≈ (A/Z)^{1/2} GV
- Geomagnetic cutoff range for AMS: 1–15 GV depending on geomagnetic latitude
- GZK-cutoff for strangelets moves to energies much higher than observed cosmic ray energies up to 3 × 10^{20} eV

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework for strangelet production from binary strange star collisions originally developed by Madsen (2001) and Madsen (2004), and provides flux predictions for the AMS-02 experiment on the International Space Station. The paper addresses counter-arguments by Balberg (2004) who claimed that ultrahigh energy strangelets are ruled out because they would transform all neutron stars into strange stars; Madsen counters that ultrahigh energy strangelets would be destroyed in collisions rather than serve as seeds for conversion. The work enables experimental tests of strange quark matter stability through cosmic ray searches, particularly with AMS-02 and lunar soil searches, which could settle the issue within a few years.
