# Probabilistic observables, conditional correlations, and quantum physics

> We discuss the classical statistics of isolated subsystems. Only a small part of the information contained in the classical probability distribution for the subsystem and its environment is available for the description of the isolated subsystem. The "coarse graining of the information" to micro-states implies probabilistic observables. For two-level probabilistic observables only a probability for finding the values one or minus one can be given for any micro-state, while such observables can be realized as classical observables with sharp values on a substate level. For a continuous family of micro-states parameterized by a sphere all the quantum mechanical laws for a two-state system follow under the assumption that the purity of the ensemble is conserved by the time evolution. The non-commutative correlation functions of quantum mechanics correspond to the use of conditional correlation functions in classical statistics. We further discuss the classical statistical realization of entanglement within a system corresponding to four-state quantum mechanics. We conclude that quantum mechanics can be derived from a classical statistical setting with infinitely many micro-states.

# Probabilistic observables, conditional correlations, and quantum physics

## Key claim

Quantum mechanics can be derived from a classical statistical setting with infinitely many micro-states, provided that the classical statistics uses conditional correlation functions rather than classical pointwise correlations, and that the purity of the ensemble is conserved by the time evolution. The paper argues that the difference between quantum and classical statistics is only apparent, but this equivalence holds specifically for isolated subsystems where information about the environment is coarse-grained away.

## Method

The author constructs a classical statistical ensemble with infinitely many micro-states, where probabilistic two-level observables (taking only values ±1) are defined on each micro-state via a probability distribution rather than a fixed value. For a continuous family of micro-states parameterized by a sphere, the author shows that all quantum mechanical laws for a two-state system follow when the purity P = Σ_k ρ_k² = 2 tr ρ² − 1 is conserved by the time evolution. The mapping to quantum mechanics is completed by identifying the density matrix ρ from effective probabilities, deriving the von Neumann and Schrödinger equations, and showing that conditional correlation functions in classical statistics correspond to non-commutative operator products in quantum mechanics.

## Key numbers

- Purity defined as P = Σ_k ρ_k² = 2 tr ρ² − 1, where conservation of this quantity under time evolution yields unitary quantum evolution.
- For two-level observables, the expectation value of the square satisfies ⟨A²⟩ = 1 for arbitrary probability distributions {p_σ}.
- The two-level observable values are restricted to the range −1 ≤ \bar A_σ ≤ 1 for each micro-state σ.
- The probabilities for finding values +1 or −1 in a micro-state are given by w_{σ+} = (1 + \bar A_σ)/2 and w_{σ-} = (1 − \bar A_σ)/2.

## Context

This work extends the author's earlier program (Wetterich, refs. [CWN, 14A, 19A]) of constructing classical statistical realizations of quantum systems, including continuous quantum mechanics for a particle in a potential. It directly addresses the Einstein-Rosen-Podolski paradox (EPR, 1935) and Bell's inequalities (Bell, 1964) by arguing that the classical statistical systems describing quantum mechanics are "incomplete" in the sense that joint probabilities for all pairs of observables are not defined, thus avoiding the assumptions underlying Bell's theorem. The approach contrasts with deterministic local hidden variable theories and with "classical extension" approaches (Busch and collaborators) that construct joint probabilities for non-commuting observables. The work enables a classical statistical understanding of entanglement, interference, and the distinction between bosons and fermions in four-state quantum systems, and can be generalized to M-state quantum mechanics in the limit M → ∞.
