# Non-perturbative flavor asymmetry in the nucleon and deuteron: The light-front Hamiltonian effective field theory approach

> We investigate non-perturbative multi-pion contributions to nucleon flavor asymmetry within the framework of Light-Front Hamiltonian Effective Field Theory (LFHEFT). Utilizing a Fock sector expansion, we systematically incorporate pionic degrees of freedom, with the nucleon-pion interactions governed by a scalar variant of chiral effective field theory. Our results demonstrate that the non-perturbatively calculated longitudinal momentum distributions exhibit significant deviations from leading-order perturbative predictions, emphasizing the importance of higher-order Fock components in describing the proton's sea quark structure. Furthermore, we demonstrate the feasibility of extending this framework to investigate nuclear effects in light nuclei, such as the deuteron. This unified approach provides a consistent basis for analyzing the interplay between intrinsic nucleon structure and nuclear modifications, potentially offering new insights into the flavor asymmetry observed in fixed-target and collider experiments.

## Key claim

Non-perturbative multi-pion contributions to nucleon flavor asymmetry, computed via Light-Front Hamiltonian Effective Field Theory (LFHEFT) with up to three-pion Fock sectors, produce longitudinal momentum distributions that deviate significantly from leading-order perturbative predictions, yet both perturbative and non-perturbative approaches can describe the SeaQuest \(\bar{d}-\bar{u}\) data; the non-perturbative treatment improves the \(\bar{d}/\bar{u}\) ratio description, and extending this framework to the deuteron suggests that multi-pion components are essential for understanding nuclear effects in flavor asymmetry.

## Method

The authors constructed physical nucleon and deuteron states via Fock sector expansions (e.g., \(|N\rangle + |N\pi\rangle + |N\pi\pi\rangle + |N\pi\pi\pi\rangle\) for the nucleon) with interactions governed by a scalar variant of chiral effective field theory, using dimensional coupling constants \(g_{N\pi}\) and \(g_\Delta\). They solved the light-front Schrödinger equation non-perturbatively for up to four-body truncations to obtain light-front wave functions, from which longitudinal momentum distributions (LMDs) and quark distributions were computed via convolution formulas. For the deuteron, they applied the Wilson-Bloch method to construct an effective two-body Hamiltonian by integrating out the \(|NN\pi\rangle\) sector, solving the resulting eigenvalue equation for binding energies between 2.2 MeV and 500 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Integrated flavor asymmetry over \(0.13 < x < 0.45\): \(\int_{0.13}^{0.45} dx [\bar{d}(x) - \bar{u}(x)] = 0.0122(7)\), consistent with the SeaQuest measurement of \(0.0159(60)\) reported by FNAL E906 (2022).
- Non-perturbative couplings set to \(g_{N\pi} = 6.0\) GeV (\(\alpha = 0.8\)) and \(g_\Delta = 6.5\) GeV to reproduce SeaQuest \(\bar{d} - \bar{u}\) data.
- Deuteron binding energy reproduced at \(\alpha \approx 0.14\) for \(E_B^{(D)} \approx 2.2\) MeV; coupling range supporting a stable bound state: \(0.12 < \alpha < 0.33\).
- Deuteron LMD computed at binding energies of 2.2 MeV, 200 MeV, and 500 MeV, with corresponding coupling constants \(\alpha \approx 0.14\), 0.24, and 0.28 respectively.

## Context

This work extends the pion cloud model approach of Alberg and Miller (2021), who used leading-order light-cone perturbation theory with chiral EFT to describe flavor asymmetry observed in SeaQuest experiments, by incorporating non-perturbative multi-pion contributions via LFHEFT. The results address the tension between fixed-target experiments (NuSea, SeaQuest) reporting a \(\bar{d}\) surplus and recent LHC measurements from Ma et al. (2025) finding no significant flavor asymmetry, by providing a framework to investigate whether nuclear effects in deuterium targets may explain the discrepancy. The deuteron analysis, building on the Bloch method from Chakrabarti (2001) and Karmanov (2016), enables future unified calculations of intrinsic nucleon structure and nuclear modifications, with full four-body equations currently in progress.
