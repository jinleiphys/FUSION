# Optimal Qubit Purification and Unitary Schur Sampling via Random SWAP Tests

> The goal of qubit purification is to combine multiple noisy copies of an unknown pure quantum state to obtain one or more copies that are closer to the pure state. We show that a simple protocol based solely on random SWAP tests achieves the same fidelity as the Schur transform, which is optimal. This protocol relies only on elementary two-qubit SWAP tests, which project a pair of qubits onto the singlet or triplet subspaces, to identify and isolate singlet pairs, and then proceeds with the remaining qubits. For a system of $n$ qubits, we show that after approximately $T \approx n \ln n$ random SWAP tests, a sharp transition occurs: the probability of detecting any new singlet decreases exponentially with $T$. Similarly, the fidelity of each remaining qubit approaches the optimal value given by the Schur transform, up to an error that is exponentially small in $T$. More broadly, this protocol achieves what is known as weak Schur sampling and unitary Schur sampling with error $ε$, after only $2n \ln(n ε^{-1})$ SWAP tests. That is, it provides a lossless method for extracting any information invariant under permutations of qubits, making it a powerful subroutine for tasks such as quantum state tomography and metrology.

## Key claim

The authors show that a protocol using only random two-qubit SWAP tests achieves the same fidelity as the optimal Schur transform for qubit purification, with the condition that after approximately \(T \approx n \ln n\) tests a sharp transition occurs where the probability of detecting additional singlets becomes exponentially small in \(T\).

## Method

The protocol performs SWAP tests on randomly chosen pairs of qubits, projecting each pair onto either the singlet subspace \((|01\rangle - |10\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\) or the orthogonal triplet subspace. When a pair is found in the singlet state, it is discarded and the protocol continues with the remaining qubits; otherwise, another random pair is selected and tested. This process is repeated for \(T\) SWAP tests, and the output consists of the remaining qubits along with a classical register tracking the number of detected singlets.

## Key numbers

* For a system of \(n\) qubits, after approximately \(T \approx n \ln n\) random SWAP tests, a sharp transition occurs in the probability of detecting new singlets.
* The protocol achieves weak Schur sampling and unitary Schur sampling with error \(\epsilon\) after \(T \ge 2n \ln(n \epsilon^{-1})\) SWAP tests.
* For qubit purification of \(\rho^{\otimes n}\) with \(\rho = (1-p)|\psi\rangle\langle\psi| + p\mathbb{I}/2\), the optimal fidelity is \(\langle\psi| \rho_{\text{opt}}(n)|\psi\rangle = 1 - \frac{1}{2n} \frac{p}{(1 - p)^2} + \mathcal{O}(1/n^2)\), and this fidelity can be achieved up to an additional error \(\epsilon\) using \(2n\ln(n \epsilon^{-1})\) random SWAP tests.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Childs et al. (2025) who used sequential SWAP tests for qubit purification but with suboptimal fidelity and gate complexity due to discarding qubits when the triplet outcome occurs. It also builds on Freedman et al. (2021) who showed that random SWAP test outcomes determine total angular momentum, and on Rudolph and Virmani (2023) who used repeated angular momentum measurements to grow a subset of qubits in the totally symmetric subspace. The protocol enables efficient extraction of permutationally invariant information for tasks such as quantum state tomography and metrology.
