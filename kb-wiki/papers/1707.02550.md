# Heavy sterile neutrino in dark matter searches

> Sterile neutrinos are possible dark matter candidates. We examine here possible detection mechanisms, assuming that the neutrino has a mass of about 50 keV and couples to the ordinary neutrino. Even though this neutrino is quite heavy, it is non relativistic with a maximum kinetic energy of 0.1 eV. Thus new experimental techniques are required for its detection. We estimate the expected event rate in the following cases: i) Measure electron recoils in the case of materials with very low electron binding. ii) Low temperature crystal bolometers. iii) Spin induced atomic excitations at very low temperatures, leading to a characteristic photon spectrum. iv) Observation of resonances in antineutrino absorption by a nucleus undergoing electron capture. v) Neutrino induced electron events beyond the end point energy of beta decaying systems, e.g. in the tritium decay studied by KATRIN.

## Key claim

The authors examine detection mechanisms for a sterile neutrino dark matter candidate with a mass of about 50 keV, but emphasize that despite its large mass the neutrino is non-relativistic with a maximum kinetic energy of only 0.1 eV, requiring entirely new experimental techniques beyond those used for standard WIMP searches.

## Method

The authors calculate expected event rates for five detection channels: (i) electron recoils in materials with very low electron binding, such as superconductors; (ii) low-temperature crystal bolometers (specifically CUORE-style TeO₂ detectors) where the sterile neutrino deposits energy by exciting phonon modes via coherent Z-exchange with neutrons; (iii) spin-induced atomic excitations at very low temperatures between Zeeman-split atomic levels, monitored by laser-induced fluorescence; (iv) resonant antineutrino absorption by a nucleus undergoing electron capture, using the example of ¹⁵⁷Tb; and (v) neutrino-induced electron events beyond the beta-decay endpoint energy, as in the KATRIN tritium experiment. All rates are expressed in terms of the unknown mixing parameter C²_ν between the sterile and ordinary electron neutrino.

## Key numbers

- Sterile neutrino mass assumed: 50 keV; maximum kinetic energy: 0.04 eV; average kinetic energy: 8.0 × 10⁻³ eV.
- For electron recoil in a superconductor with 2 × 10²⁴ electron pairs: event rate = 2.89 × 10⁻⁴ C²_ν per year.
- For a 1 kg ¹³⁰TeO₂ bolometer target (N=78 neutrons): event rate = 51 C²_ν per kg-year.
- For atomic excitation with a target of mass number A=100: event rate = 1.8 × 10⁻⁴ C²_ν (C_{ℓ,j₁,m₁,j₂,m₂})² per kg-year.
- For resonant antineutrino capture on ¹⁵⁷Tb (K-shell, binding energy 50.24 keV): velocity-averaged cross section = 8.98 × 10⁻⁴⁶ C²_ν cm²; event rate = 19 C²_ν per year for a 1 kg target.
- Dark matter density assumed: 0.3 GeV/cm³; sterile neutrino flux: 1.32 × 10¹¹ cm⁻² s⁻¹.

## Context

This work extends the sterile neutrino dark matter scenario in the 48–300 keV mass range discussed by Mavromatos (2016) and others, and addresses the detection challenge posed by the sub-eV kinetic energy of such candidates. The authors build on prior proposals for light WIMP detection using superconductors (Hochberg, Pyle, Zhao 2015) and on the resonant antineutrino capture method previously studied by Vergados and Novikov (2014) for relic neutrino detection. The paper enables future experimental searches by providing quantitative rate estimates for five distinct detection channels, though all rates depend on the unknown mixing parameter C²_ν.
