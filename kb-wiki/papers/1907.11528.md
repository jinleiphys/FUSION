# Cascade Calculations with Schematic Interactions

> In previous works we considered schematic Hamiltonians represented by simplified matrices. We defined 2 transition operators and calculated transition strengths from the ground state to all exited states. In many cases the strengths decreased nearly exponentially with excitation energy. Now we do the reverse We start with the highest energy state and calculate the cascade of transitions until the ground states is reached. On a log plot we show the average transition strength as a function of the number of energy intervals that were crossed. We give an analytic proof of exponential behavior for transition strength in the weak coupling limit for the T2 transition operator.

# Cascade Calculations with Schematic Interactions

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that for a schematic heptadiagonal matrix Hamiltonian with the T₂ transition operator, the average transition strength in cascade (de-excitation) calculations shows exponential decrease with energy interval number, and they provide an analytic proof that this exponential behavior holds in the weak coupling limit (v/E very small), though the cascade and excitation results are "remarkably similar" but "not identical."

## Method
The authors construct 11×11 tridiagonal, pentadiagonal, and heptadiagonal matrix Hamiltonians with diagonal elements nE (n=0 to 10) and off-diagonal coupling parameters v, w, x. They define the T₂ transition operator with matrix elements ⟨n|T₂|n+1⟩ = √(n+1) and compute transition strengths starting from the highest energy state (n=10) and cascading down to the ground state, presenting results as log plots of average transition strength versus number of energy intervals crossed. They also provide an analytic weak-coupling proof for the tridiagonal case showing that ln(O²) approaches (n-1) ln((v/E)²), demonstrating exponential behavior.

## Key numbers
- For the tridiagonal case with v/E=0.1, the cascade curve shows the "best linear behavior" on a log plot, implying exponential behavior.
- For v/E=1 (tridiagonal), the curve is "dominantly linear but there is a slight arch."
- For v=0.01 (tridiagonal), there is "a kink at n=9" in the cascade results.
- In the pentadiagonal case with v=1, there is "a large dip at n=2" due to destructive interference.
- In the heptadiagonal case with v=w=x=1, there is a "pronounced dip at n=3."
- For the heptadiagonal case with v=1, w=1/2!, x=1/3!, the cascade curve "looks quite linear again."
- In the weak coupling limit (v/E=0.0001) for the T₂ operator, the analytic sum for transition 0→10 is -201.1, matching the exact value of -201.1.
- For the T₁ operator at v/E=0.0001, the transition 0→10 has value -∞ (zero strength).

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous studies of schematic matrix Hamiltonians (Wolfe and Zamick, 2015-2019) where they calculated transition strengths from the ground state to excited states and observed nearly exponential decrease with excitation energy. The cascade approach is motivated by experimental gamma-ray de-excitation studies (e.g., Brown and Larsen, 2014; and references [6-12] in the paper) which show increasing average M1 strength at low gamma-ray energies. The authors note that Brown and Larsen (2014) also used log plots and found linear behavior, suggesting the exponential phenomenon is more widespread than initially thought. This work enables future comparison between schematic model predictions and realistic nuclear structure calculations of gamma-ray cascades.
