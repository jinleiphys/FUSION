# Transition-state dynamics in complex quantum systems

> A model is proposed for studying the reaction dynamics in complex quantum systems in which the complete mixing of states is hindered by an internal barrier. Such systems are often treated by the transition-state theory, also known in chemistry as RRKM theory, but the validity of the theory is questionable when there is no identifiable coordinate associated with the barrier. The model consists of two Gaussian Orthogonal Ensembles (GOE) of internal levels coupled to each other and to the wave functions in the entrance and decay channels. We find that the transition-state formula can be derived from the model under some easily justifiable approximations. In particular, the assumption in transition-state theory that the reaction rates are insensitive to the decay widths of the internal states on the far side of the barrier is fulfilled for broad range of Hamiltonian parameters. More doubtful is the common assumption that the transmission factor $T$ across the barrier is unity or can be modeled by a one-dimensional Hamiltonian giving $T$ close to unity above the barrier. This is not the case in the model; we find that the transmission factor only approaches one under special conditions that are not likely to be fulfilled without a strong collective component in the Hamiltonian.

# Transition-state dynamics in complex quantum systems

## Key claim

The transition-state (RRKM) formula for reaction rates can be derived from a two-reservoir Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) model under justifiable approximations, but the common assumption that the transmission factor T across the barrier is unity or near-unity is not generally valid; the transmission factor only approaches one under special conditions requiring a strong collective component in the Hamiltonian.

## Method

The authors construct a Hamiltonian coupling two GOE reservoirs of internal states through a two-site internal bridge channel, with the entrance channel represented in a discrete-basis formalism. They compute the branching ratio between decays from the two reservoirs by solving the effective Hamiltonian for wave function amplitudes and fluxes, then derive analytic expressions for the self-energies using continuum integrals over the GOE level density. The transition-state limit is obtained by taking ensemble averages of the fluctuating self-energy quantities and comparing the resulting branching ratio to the analytic transition-state formula.

## Key numbers

- The branching ratio from numerical sampling of the Hamiltonian ensemble with all coupling parameters v = 0.1, decay widths Gamma = 0.1, and hopping parameters t1 = t2 = -1 is Br = 0.048 ± 0.048, matching the analytic prediction of Br = 0.048.
- The level density at the center of the GOE spectrum is given by rho_0 = N_g^(1/2) / (pi v_g), where N_g is the number of states and v_g is the root-mean-square matrix element.
- The half-width of the GOE spectrum is E_m = 2 N_g^(1/2) v_g.
- For the diagonal self-energy w_kk, the ensemble average is w^e_kk ≈ -i pi v_k^2 rho_0 for Gamma_g much less than E_m.
- For the off-diagonal self-energy squared, the expectation value is ⟨|w_kk'|^2⟩ ≈ 2 pi v_k^2 v_k'^2 rho_0 / Gamma_g for Gamma_g much less than E_m.
- The expectation value of w_kk'^2 is ⟨w_kk'^2⟩ ≈ -pi v_2^2 v_3^2 rho_0 / E_m, smaller than |w_23|^2 by a factor of Gamma_g / (2 E_m).

## Context

This work extends the two-reservoir GOE model previously used to study fluctuations of decay widths in unimolecular reactions (see Poli (1990) and citations therein) to test the foundations of transition-state theory (also known as RRKM theory, developed by Marcus (1951, 1952) and others). The model enables a qualified analytic derivation of the transition-state formula and identifies conditions under which the transmission factor deviates from unity, which is relevant for applications in nuclear reaction theory and chemical dynamics where no identifiable coordinate is associated with the barrier. The authors note that future work could consider two chaotic sets of states coupled through a single state instead of a channel, using the quantum-dot formula from Alhassid (2000).
