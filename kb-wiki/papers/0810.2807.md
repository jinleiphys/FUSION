# A theory of jet shapes and cross sections: from hadrons to nuclei

> For jets, with great power comes great opportunity. The unprecedented center of mass energies available at the LHC open new windows on the QGP: we demonstrate that jet shape and jet cross section measurements become feasible as a new, differential and accurate test of the underlying QCD theory. We present a first step in understanding these shapes and cross sections in heavy ion reactions. Our approach allows for detailed simulations of the experimental acceptance/cuts that help isolate jets in such high-multiplicity environment. It is demonstrated for the first time that the pattern of stimulated gluon emission can be correlated with a variable quenching of the jet rates and provide an approximately model-independent approach to determining the characteristics of the medium-induced bremsstrahlung spectrum. Surprisingly, in realistic simulations of parton propagation through the QGP we find a minimal increase in the mean jet radius even for large jet attenuation. Jet broadening is manifest in the tails of the energy distribution away from the jet axis and its quantification requires high statistics measurements that will be possible at the LHC.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that jet shape and jet cross section measurements at the LHC can serve as a new, differential test of QCD theory in heavy ion collisions, and that the pattern of stimulated gluon emission can be correlated with variable quenching of jet rates to provide an approximately model-independent approach to determining the characteristics of the medium-induced bremsstrahlung spectrum. The hedge is that this is presented as a "first step" in understanding these observables in heavy ion reactions, and the claim of model-independence is qualified as "approximately."

## Method

The authors develop an analytic perturbative QCD framework for jet shapes in p+p collisions, incorporating leading-order parton splitting, Sudakov resummation at modified leading logarithmic accuracy (MLLA), and power corrections for non-perturbative effects, building upon the work of Seymour (1997). They then extend this to heavy ion collisions by incorporating medium-induced radiative energy loss in the GLV (Gyulassy-Levai-Vitev) formalism, which expands the radiation pattern in correlations between multiple scattering centers in the quark-gluon plasma. The approach allows for detailed simulations of experimental acceptance cuts and minimum particle energy thresholds, and the authors present numerical results for Pb+Pb collisions at LHC energies of √s = 5.5 TeV.

## Key numbers

- Jet shapes are calculated for p+p collisions at √s = 5.5 TeV at the LHC for jet transverse energies E_T = 50, 100, 250, and 500 GeV, with cone radii R = 0.7 and R = 0.4
- Minimum particle energy cuts of ω^min = 10 GeV are applied, corresponding to z_min = p_T min / E_T values of 0.2, 0.1, 0.04, and 0.02
- The non-perturbative scale parameters used are ᾱ_0'(2 GeV, 0) = 0.52 and ᾱ_0'(3 GeV, 0) = 0.42, from Dokshitzer (1995) and Seymour (1997)
- The QGP density can be determined with 20-25% "statistical" accuracy from leading particle quenching fits, but systematic discrepancies in extracted medium properties span an order of magnitude
- The ratio L/λ_g ≈ 5 for typical jets in the QGP, while the assumption L/λ_g → ∞ (≥ 10) is often violated

## Context

This work extends the theoretical understanding of jet quenching from inclusive hadron suppression measurements at RHIC, as established by Gyulassy (2003) and others, to more differential jet observables that can be measured at the LHC. The authors build upon the jet shape formalism developed by Seymour (1997) for e+e- and hadronic collisions, and incorporate the GLV formalism for medium-induced radiation. The approach enables experimental access to the QGP response to quark and gluon propagation through the evolution of the jet nuclear modification factor R_AA^jet as a function of jet radius R^max and minimum particle energy ω^min, potentially resolving the order-of-magnitude systematic discrepancies in extracted medium properties that plague single-particle suppression analyses.
