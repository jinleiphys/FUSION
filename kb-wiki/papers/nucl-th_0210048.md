# Dependence of Nuclear Level Density on Vibrational State Damping

## Key claim

The variation factor K (ratio of level densities with and without vibrational states) depends rather strongly on the relaxation times of collective state damping, with different parametrizations of the relaxation time producing significantly different results for the level density.

## Method

The authors use a response function approach within the random phase approximation to include vibrational states in the calculation of nuclear level density. The level density is computed via the standard saddle-point method, and the variation factor K is obtained from the partition function modified by a vibrational contribution expressed in terms of complex poles (energy and width) of the nuclear response function. Three different parametrizations of the collision relaxation time τ_c are adopted, based on transport theory, a doorway state model, and a modified doorway state model, with retardation effects included.

## Key numbers

- For nucleus ⁵⁶Fe, the low-lying quadrupole state energy used is E_{2⁺, exp} = 0.847 MeV.
- The relaxation time parametrization from transport theory is ℏ/τ_c(ε,T) = ((ε/2π)² + T²)/4.07 MeV.
- The doorway state model relaxation time uses ℏ/τ_c(ε,T) = 2π (K_m/A³) (g³/2) ((ε+U)²/(3+ n̄)), with n̄ = 0.843 a T and level density parameter a = A/8 MeV⁻¹.
- The modified doorway state model relaxation time uses ℏ/τ_c(ε,T) = 2π (K_B/A³) (g³/8) (ε+U), where K_B is defined from agreement with transport theory at T=0 and at GQR energy.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Ignatyuk (1983, 1993) on the effect of collective states on level density at low excitation energies, and by Plujko (2002) who found the variation factor K is practically independent of the type of statistical approach used. The response function method with different relaxation time parametrizations provides an additional possibility for investigating the dependence of relaxation time on temperature and collective state energy, and can be compared with microscopic calculations and experimental values for nuclei such as ⁵⁶Fe.
