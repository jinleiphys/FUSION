# Strongly interacting matter in extreme magnetic fields

> Magnetic fields are ubiquitous across different physical systems of current interest; from the early Universe, compact astrophysical objects and heavy-ion collisions to condensed matter systems. A proper treatment of the effects produced by magnetic fields during the dynamical evolution of these systems, can help to understand observables that otherwise show a puzzling behavior. Furthermore, when these fields are comparable to or stronger than Λ_QCD, they serve as excellent probes to help elucidate the physics of strongly interacting matter under extreme conditions of temperature and density. In this work we provide a comprehensive review of recent developments on the description of QED and QCD systems where magnetic field driven effects are important. These include the modification of meson static properties such as masses and form factors, the chiral magnetic effect, the description of anomalous transport coefficients, superconductivity in extreme magnetic fields, the properties of neutron stars, the evolution of heavy-ion collisions, as well as effects on the QCD phase diagram. We describe recent theory and phenomenological developments using effective models as well as LQCD methods. The work represents a state-of-the-art review of the field, motivated by presentations and discussions during the "Workshop on Strongly Interacting Matter in Strong Electromagnetic Fields" that took place in the European Centre for Theoretical Studies in Nuclear Physics and Related Areas (ECT*) in the city of Trento, Italy, September 25-29, 2023.

## Key claim

Strong magnetic fields (up to ~10^17 G) cause an extension of the neutron star inner crust with additional disconnected regions of non-homogeneous "pasta" matter above the zero-field crust-core boundary, but the existence and size of these extra regions depend on the symmetry energy behavior in the crustal equation of state.

## Method

The authors used relativistic mean-field models (NL3 and NL3ωρ) to describe neutron star inner crust matter under strong magnetic fields, computing pasta phase structures via both the coexistence-phase (CP) approximation and the compressible liquid drop (CLD) model. They imposed β-equilibrium and charge neutrality, included the anomalous magnetic moment of nucleons, and compared their results with dynamical spinodal calculations from the Vlasov formalism.

## Key numbers

- Magnetic field strengths considered: up to B* = 10^4, corresponding to B = 4.41 × 10^17 G (critical electron field B^c_e = 4.414 × 10^13 G)
- NL3 model: symmetry energy slope L = 118 MeV; NL3ωρ model: L = 55 MeV
- For NL3ωρ at B = 4.41 × 10^17 G with AMM, the extra non-homogeneous regions are approximately double in number and narrower compared to the case without AMM
- For NL3 model with B* = 5 × 10^3, the crust-core transition density (ρ_cc) shifts to higher values relative to the B = 0 case
- For NL3ωρ model with B* = 5 × 10^3, the crust-core transition density decreases and no extra region appears in the CLD calculation (unlike the CP calculation)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of magnetized neutron star crusts by Fang (2016, 2017) and Wang (2022) using dynamical spinodal calculations, and by Pais (2015) and Scurto (2023) for pasta phase modeling. The results show that the symmetry energy behavior at subsaturation densities determines the magnetic field effect: NL3ωρ has higher symmetry energy below ~0.1 fm^-3, giving larger proton fractions and smaller magnetic field effects compared to NL3. The authors note that a self-consistent calculation of magnetized surface tension is needed for future work, as the current surface tension parametrization comes from a fit to Thomas-Fermi calculations without magnetic fields.
