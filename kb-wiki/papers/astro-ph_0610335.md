# Determining the neutron star equation of state using the narrow-band gravitational wave detector Schenberg

> We briefly review the properties of quasi-normal modes of neutron stars and black holes. We analyse the consequences of a possible detection of such modes via the gravitational waves associated with them, especially addressing our study to the Brazilian spherical antenna, on which a possible detection would occur at 3.0-3.4 kHz. A question related to any putative gravitational wave detection concerns the source that produces it. We argue that, since the characteristic damping times for the gravitational waves of neutron stars and black holes are different, a detection can distinguish between them, and also distinguish the neutron stars oscillating modes. Moreover, since the source can be identified by its characteristic damping time, we are able to extract information about the neutron star or black hole. This information would lead, for example, to a strong constraint in the nuclear matter equation of state, namely the compression modulus should be K=220 MeV.

## Key claim

If the narrow-band spherical gravitational wave detector Schenberg (operating at 3.0-3.4 kHz) detects a quasi-normal mode from a neutron star, the damping time can distinguish it from a black hole signal, and the combined frequency and damping time can constrain the nuclear matter equation of state; specifically, a detection would imply a compression modulus of approximately K=220 MeV, but this conclusion depends on the assumption that the detected mode is the fundamental (f-) mode of a neutron star composed of nuclear matter rather than a strange quark star.

## Method

The authors apply empirical relations from Benhar et al. (2004) that connect the frequency and damping time of neutron star f- and p-modes to stellar mass and radius, inverting these relations to determine which mass-radius combinations produce gravitational waves in the 3.0-3.4 kHz Schenberg bandwidth. They then compare these mass-radius constraints to predictions from specific nuclear matter equations of state (the Taurines parameterized model and a non-linear model) and quark matter models (MIT bag model and chromo-dielectric model), and also examine the relation between the compression modulus K and the maximum neutron star mass for the Taurines model.

## Key numbers

* The Schenberg antenna operates in the frequency band 3.0-3.4 kHz; the Mini-GRAIL detector operates at 2.8-3.0 kHz.
* For a Schwarzschild black hole, the fundamental quadrupole quasi-normal mode frequency is ω = 12.07 kHz (M_sun / M), so Schenberg would only see black holes with masses about 3.5-4.0 M_sun; the damping time for such black holes is about 0.2 ms.
* A typical neutron star f-mode has frequency ω ~ 2.8 kHz and damping time τ ~ 0.1 s; the first p-mode has ω ~ 6 kHz and τ ~ 0.6 s.
* For a putative detection of a standard 1.4 M_sun neutron star f-mode in the Schenberg band, a very small radius R ~ 7.3 km would be inferred; for a 1.0 M_sun f-mode unstable neutron star, the radius would be R = 6.58 ± 0.79 km.
* For the Taurines model, the maximum neutron star mass constraint from PSR J0751+1807 (M = 2.1 ± 0.2 M_sun) requires K ≥ 220 MeV; stellar models with maximum mass radius R ≤ 10 km have compression modulus 215 < K < 225 MeV.
* For the MIT bag model, the set B = 100 MeV/fm^3, α_c = 0.35, and m_s = 200 MeV fits the optimum Schenberg detection region, while B = 60 MeV/fm^3, α_c = 0, and m_s = 150 MeV could not describe stars emitting 3.0-3.4 kHz waves.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Kokkotas et al. (2001) on using gravitational wave f-mode detections to constrain neutron star equation of state, and applies it specifically to the narrow bandwidth of the Schenberg and Mini-GRAIL spherical antennas. The authors build on empirical mode-frequency relations from Benhar et al. (2004) and use the Taurines et al. (2004) parameterized nuclear matter model to connect the compression modulus K to observable mass-radius combinations. A detection in the Schenberg band would provide stronger constraints on K than any earth-based nuclear experiment, and could also distinguish between neutron stars and strange quark stars, with implications for the bag constant B and perturbative constant α_c in QCD models.
