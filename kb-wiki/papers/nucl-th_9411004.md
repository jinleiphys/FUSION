# Color Transparency Assumptions

## Key claim

Using an exactly solvable nonrelativistic QED model of a two-quark "nucleon" bound by the Coulomb potential, the authors show that color transparency (CT) can occur in their model, but only because the soft interaction matrix elements cut off contributions from highly excited intermediate states, meaning the expansion rate of the point-like configuration (PLC) is inversely proportional to momentum transfer Q, and the eikonal approximation remains valid precisely because high-energy states are not important.

## Method

The authors construct a nonrelativistic model of a neutral two-quark system (mass ratio 2:1) bound by the Coulomb potential with variable strength α (0 to 3). A high-momentum-transfer reaction is modeled by a photon of momentum Q absorbed by the heavy quark, creating a PLC. The subsequent expansion of this wave packet through a nuclear medium is computed using the Green's function propagator, with the soft final-state interaction modeled as the forward scattering amplitude between the two-quark wave packet and target "nucleons" (identical two-quark ground states). The effective cross section σ_eff(Z) is evaluated numerically for 600 discrete states and a continuous variable k in [0,10], sufficient to reproduce the analytic result for Q r_B up to 20.

## Key numbers

- The effective cross section at formation is σ_eff(0) = 2 / (Q² r_B²/4 + 1), showing that the effective size scales as b² ~ 1/Q² for large Q².
- The expansion distance Z_exp is linear with Q, with the slope determined by α (the coupling strength).
- Calculations are performed for momentum transfer Q r_B up to 20, with α values of 1/137, 2, and 3.
- The upper limit of α = 3 corresponds to the ground state rest mass M_1 = 0; α = 0 gives the nonrelativistic limit.
- For the continuous spectrum, the maximum energy of states relevant for PLC expansion grows much slower than Q, with k_max ~ √Q sufficient to reproduce the full σ_eff(Z).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of color transparency developed by Mueller (1982) and Brodsky (1982), and directly builds on the approach of Jennings and Miller (1990, 1991) who used data for matrix elements and form factors. The authors provide a fully solvable model that tests each of the three basic CT assumptions (PLC formation, suppressed interactions, slow expansion) that previous work had to treat phenomenologically. The results support the "linear expansion" model of Farrar et al. (1988) and Frankfurt et al. (1989), and demonstrate that the eikonal approximation used in Glauber treatments is self-consistently justified because high-energy intermediate states are cut off by the soft interaction. This enables future work to use the model to study CT in (e,e'p) reactions at CEBAF and BNL.
