# Level-resolved quantum statistical theory of electron capture into many-electron compound resonances in highly charged ions

> The strong mixing of many-electron basis states in excited atoms and ions with open $f$ shells results in very large numbers of complex, chaotic eigenstates that cannot be computed to any degree of accuracy. Describing the processes which involve such states requires the use of a statistical theory. Electron capture into these 'compound resonances' leads to electron-ion recombination rates that are orders of magnitude greater than those of direct, radiative recombination, and cannot be described by standard theories of dielectronic recombination. Previous statistical theories considered this as a two-electron capture process which populates a pair of single-particle orbitals, followed by 'spreading' of the two-electron states into chaotically mixed eigenstates. This method is similar to a configuration-average approach, as it neglects potentially important effects of spectator electrons and conservation of total angular momentum. In this work we develop a statistical theory which considers electron capture into 'doorway' states with definite angular momentum obtained by the configuration interaction method. We apply this approach to electron recombination with W$^{20+}$, considering 2 million doorway states. Despite strong effects from the spectator electrons, we find that the results of the earlier theories largely hold. Finally, we extract the fluorescence yield (the probability of photoemission and hence recombination) by comparison with experiment.

## Key claim

The authors develop a level-resolved quantum statistical theory for electron capture into compound resonances in highly charged ions, explicitly including spectator electrons and total angular momentum conservation. They find that despite strong effects from spectator electrons and angular momentum, the results of earlier configuration-averaged statistical theories largely hold when integrated over all doorway states.

## Method

The authors performed configuration interaction (CI) calculations for 2,014,212 excited levels of W19+ (the compound ion), corresponding to 63 configurations with energies from -13 to 114 eV relative to the ionization threshold. They calculated autoionization widths from the W20+ target ground state into these doorway states with definite total angular momentum J and parity, using a relativistic Hartree-Fock basis with B-splines for excited states up to 8spdfg and continuum orbitals with l ≤ 6. The level-resolved capture cross section was then compared with the configuration-averaged (CA) MBQC statistical theory using the same single-particle orbitals and a spreading width of 18.5 eV.

## Key numbers

- 2,014,212 excited levels of W19+ were calculated, corresponding to 63 configurations with configuration-average energies in the range -13 to 114 eV relative to the W19+ ionization threshold
- The largest Hamiltonian matrix diagonalised was for the 4d-1 4f8 5p 5f configuration with J = 9/2, containing 25,112 CSFs
- The spreading width used was Γspr = 0.68 a.u. = 18.5 eV, taken from Dzuba (2012)
- The total (integrated) strength Iτ for individual configurations agreed between level-resolved and CA theory to within approximately 15%
- The energy spacing between compound resonances in W19+ is less than 10-6 eV
- The fluorescence yield wf was found to be approximately 1 for incident electron energies approaching 0 eV, dropping to around 0.2 for energies above 15 eV (from prior work by Dzuba (2013))

## Context

This work extends the MBQC statistical theory developed by Flambaum (1993, 2002) and Dzuba (2012, 2013) for electron-ion recombination in complex ions with open f-shells, which was previously applied to Au25+ by Hoffknecht (1998) and Flambaum (2002). The earlier configuration-averaged approach treated spectator electrons as a spherically-symmetric frozen core, which the present work tests by explicitly including the 4f7 core electrons and total angular momentum conservation. The results enable more reliable modelling of tungsten recombination rates for fusion plasma applications, particularly for ITER, and resolve the discrepancy between measured recombination cross sections and conventional dielectronic recombination calculations by Badnell (2012).
