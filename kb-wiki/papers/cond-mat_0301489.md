# Comment on ``Ramsey Fringes in a Bose-Einstein Condensate between Atoms and Molecules''

> In Phys. Rev. Lett. 89, 180401 (2002) [cond-mat/0204504], Kokkelmans and Holland interpret the results of a recent experiment at JILA that demonstrated atom-molecule coherence in a Bose-Einstein condensate. Using a mean-field approximation to a resonance field theory involving an atom condensate and a molecular condensate, they find that the molecular condensate is tiny compared to the atom condensate. We show that if the probability for the molecular field to create a diatomic molecule is correctly included, the numbers of atoms in the atom condensate and in the condensate of diatomic molecules are comparable.

## Key claim
The interpretation by Kokkelmans and Holland (2002) that the molecular condensate density is orders of magnitude smaller than the atom condensate density is incorrect; when the probability for the molecular field to create a diatomic molecule (the renormalization constant Z) is correctly included, the numbers of atoms in the atom condensate and in the dimer condensate are comparable.

## Method
Braaten, Hammer, and Kusunoki re-analyze the mean-field resonance field theory used by Kokkelmans and Holland, distinguishing between the Feshbach molecule and the dimer (the energy eigenstate including hyperfine interactions). They compute the renormalization constant Z, which gives the probability for the dimer to be a Feshbach molecule, using analytic two-body results from the resonance field theory and the experimental parameters from Kokkelmans and Holland. They then apply this correction to the molecular condensate density reported in the original work.

## Key numbers
- During the evolve time, the renormalization constant Z⁻¹ = 18.3, yielding a corrected dimer condensate atom number density 2 Z⁻¹ |φ_m|² that oscillates around 0.119 with an amplitude of about 0.011.
- The mean corrected dimer condensate density (0.119) is about 1/7 of the atom condensate density |φ_a|² (which oscillates around 0.80 with amplitude 0.02).
- The amplitude of the dimer condensate oscillation (0.011) is more than 1/2 that of the atom condensate oscillation (0.02).
- During the second pulse, Z⁻¹ increases to 1280 and then decreases to 3.7, canceling most of the dramatic changes in |φ_m|² (which decreases by a factor of 26 and increases by a factor of 118).

## Context
This Comment corrects the interpretation of Kokkelmans and Holland (2002) regarding the JILA experiment by Donley et al. (2002) that demonstrated atom-molecule coherence in a Bose-Einstein condensate. The authors show that the molecular condensate plays a major role in the observed oscillations, contrary to the original claim that the oscillations involve coherent flow between the atom condensate and noncondensate atoms. This work enables a more accurate treatment of atom-molecule coherence near Feshbach resonances, where the distinction between Feshbach molecules and dimers is essential.
