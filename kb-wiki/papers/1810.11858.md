# Particle-hole symmetry breaking due to Pauli blocking

> Particle-hole symmetry has been used on several occasions in nuclear structure over the years. We prove that particle-hole symmetry is broken in nuclear shells possessing the proxy-SU(3) symmetry. The breaking of the symmetry is rooted in the Pauli principle and the short range nature of the nucleon-nucleon interaction. The breaking of the symmetry explains the dominance of prolate over oblate shapes in deformed nuclei and determines the regions of prolate to oblate shape transitions in the nuclear chart. Furthermore, it is related to the existence of specific regions of shape coexistence across the nuclear chart, surrounded by regions in which shape coexistence does not occur.

## Key claim

Particle-hole symmetry is broken in nuclear shells that possess the proxy-SU(3) symmetry, with the breaking rooted in the Pauli principle and the short-range nature of the nucleon-nucleon interaction. The symmetry breaking is demonstrated through group-theoretical analysis of highest-weight SU(3) irreducible representations, showing that the irrep for holes is not the conjugate of the irrep for particles.

## Method

The authors compute the highest-weight SU(3) irreducible representations (irreps) for even numbers of particles in the proxy-SU(3) shells (U(6), U(10), U(15), U(21), U(28), U(36)) using existing group-theoretical codes. They compare the irreps for a given number of particles with the irreps for the corresponding number of holes (i.e., the complement to the shell capacity), demonstrating that the hole irreps are not the conjugates of the particle irreps. The physical origin is explained by noting that the short-range nucleon-nucleon force favors maximally symmetric spatial configurations, while the Pauli principle forces the spin-isospin part to be as antisymmetric as possible, leading to the most symmetric spatial irreps allowed by the Pauli principle.

## Key numbers

- The proxy-SU(3) sdg shell accommodates 30 like nucleons, while the corresponding shell-model 50-82 shell accommodates 32 like nucleons.
- For 6 particles in the proxy-SU(3) pfh shell (U(21)), the highest-weight irrep is (24,0); for 6 holes (36 particles), the irrep is (6,18), whereas the conjugate of (24,0) would be (0,24).
- Shape coexistence regions are identified at neutron numbers 60-70 (near Z=50) and 96-112 (near Z=82), with subregions 64-70 and 104-112 where 3D-HO magic numbers give oblate shapes while proxy-SU(3) gives prolate shapes.
- The regions where 3D-HO magic numbers lead to lower deformation than spin-orbit magic numbers are 34-42, 60-72, 96-116, and 146-172 (truncated to 34-40, 60-70, 96-112, 146-168 for same-oscillator-quanta comparison).

## Context

This work extends the proxy-SU(3) symmetry introduced by Bonatsos et al. (2017) and builds on the Elliott SU(3) model (1958) for the sd shell, as well as the Federman-Pittel (1977) observation that proton-neutron pairs with similar quantum numbers drive deformation. The particle-hole symmetry breaking explains the long-standing puzzle of prolate over oblate dominance in deformed nuclei (Heyde and Wood, 1991) and determines prolate-to-oblate shape transition regions (Linnemann, 2005). The competition between proxy-SU(3) magic numbers and 3D-HO magic numbers (Sorlin, 2008) provides a group-theoretical framework for predicting shape coexistence regions across the nuclear chart, complementing relativistic mean-field calculations (Lalazissis, 1998) and the Interacting Boson Model (Iachello and Arima, 1987).
