# Microscopic self-energy of ${}^{40}$Ca from the charge-dependent Bonn potential

> The effects of short-range correlations on the nucleon self-energy in $^{40}$Ca are investigated using the charge-dependent Bonn (CDBonn) interaction. Comparisons are made with recent results for the self-energy of $^{40}$Ca derived from the dispersive optical-model (DOM). Particular emphasis is placed on the non-locality of the imaginary part of the microscopic self-energy which suggests that future DOM analyses should include this feature. In particular, data below the Fermi energy appear sensitive to the implied orbital angular momentum dependence of the self-energy. Quasiparticle properties obtained for the CDBonn interaction are substantially more mean-field-like than the corresponding DOM results with spectroscopic factors larger by about 0.2 e.g. Reaction cross sections obtained from the microscopic self-energy for scattering energies up to 100 MeV indicate that an adequate description of volume absorption is obtained while a considerable fraction of surface absorption is missing. The analysis of the non-locality of the imaginary part of the microscopic self-energy suggests that a simple gaussian provides an adequate description, albeit with rather large values for $β$, the non-locality parameter.

# Microscopic self-energy of ${}^{40}$Ca from the charge-dependent Bonn potential

## Key claim

The microscopic self-energy calculated from the CDBonn interaction for ${}^{40}$Ca yields quasiparticle properties that are substantially more mean-field-like than dispersive optical-model (DOM) results, with spectroscopic factors larger by about 0.2; however, the authors note that the CDBonn interaction is "relatively soft" and that the absence of long-range correlations in the microscopic calculation means the imaginary part of the self-energy ends at a much lower energy ($-38$ MeV) than in the DOM, which includes a surface term for long-range correlations.

## Method

The self-energy was calculated in two steps: first, a $\mathcal{G}$-matrix was computed in nuclear matter using the CDBonn interaction via the Bethe-Goldstone equation, then this $\mathcal{G}$-matrix was used to construct the finite-nucleus self-energy by including second-order diagrams coupling to two-particle–one-hole (2p1h) and one-particle–two-hole (1p2h) states, with harmonic oscillator states for occupied hole states and plane waves for intermediate unbound particle states. The irreducible self-energy was obtained by using the imaginary parts of the 2p1h and 1p2h diagrams and applying dispersion relations to obtain the corresponding real parts, then the Dyson equation was solved in momentum space to obtain spectral functions, quasihole properties, and scattering observables.

## Key numbers

- Spectroscopic factors from the CDBonn interaction are larger than DOM results by about 0.2
- The imaginary part of the CDBonn self-energy ends at $\varepsilon_{T}^- = -38$ MeV (compared to the DOM which extends to $\varepsilon_F$)
- Total calculated neutron number from the microscopic self-energy is $N_{calc} = 19.3$ when including partial waves up to $\ell = 4$
- The particle-hole gap from the CDBonn self-energy is more than 10 MeV, compared to the DOM at 6.1 MeV and the experimental value of 6.8 MeV
- The $d$-state spin-orbit splitting below the Fermi energy is 4 MeV from CDBonn, compared to 6.7 MeV experimentally and 5.7 MeV from the DOM
- The $0s_{1/2}$ quasihole energy shifts from $-56.1$ MeV (BHF only) to $-43.6$ MeV (full self-energy), a shift of almost 13 MeV
- Reaction cross sections were calculated for scattering energies up to 100 MeV

## Context

This work extends the microscopic self-energy calculations previously performed for ${}^{16}$O by Muther et al. (1994, 1995) and Polls et al. (1995) to the heavier nucleus ${}^{40}$Ca, using the softer CDBonn interaction instead of the Bonn-B potential. The results are compared with DOM analyses of ${}^{40}$Ca by Charity et al. (2007) and Dickhoff et al. (2010), which incorporate long-range correlations through surface terms. The finding that the non-locality of the imaginary part of the microscopic self-energy is important for generating the correct particle number suggests that future DOM analyses should include non-local imaginary components, and the authors note that a simple Gaussian provides an adequate description of this non-locality, albeit with rather large values for the non-locality parameter $\beta$.
