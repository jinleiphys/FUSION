# Electrical conductivity of the quark-gluon plasma from the low energy limit of photon and dilepton spectra

> Fluid dynamic considerations are used to determine the electric current spectral density in the regime of small energies and momenta. The spectral density in this regime is parameterized by the electric conductivity, the charge susceptibility, and the relaxation time for the electric current, which is needed for relativistic causality. Experimentally, the spectral function can be accessed through the production rates of photons and dileptons in the expanding quark-gluon plasma. We use fluid dynamic simulations of high energy nuclear collisions, together with the transport limit of the spectral density, to obtain photon and dielectron spectra for different values of the conductivity and relaxation times. The yields of photon and dileptons produced in the plasma are compared to the background from decays of short-lived hadrons. We discuss how experiments can constrain the electrical conductivity and associated relaxation time of the quark-gluon plasma.

# Electrical conductivity of the quark-gluon plasma from the low energy limit of photon and dilepton spectra

## Key claim

The electric current spectral density in the fluid dynamic regime of small frequencies and momenta can be parameterized by the electrical conductivity, charge susceptibility, and a relaxation time needed for relativistic causality; the resulting photon and dilepton spectra encode this conductivity, but experimental extraction is challenging because the signal-to-background ratio from hadron decays varies from about 70% to 0.4% for photons at low transverse momentum depending on the conductivity value.

## Method

The authors used relativistic fluid dynamic simulations with the code FluiduM to model the space-time evolution of central (0-5% centrality) Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.02 TeV, integrating the fluid dynamic expression for the electric current spectral density over the fireball volume up to a freeze-out temperature of 140 MeV. They computed thermal photon and dielectron spectra for four values of electrical conductivity to temperature ratio (sigma/T = 0.01, 0.1, 1, 2) with fixed relaxation time tau = 5/T, and compared these thermal yields to background from decays of short-lived hadrons calculated using FastReso and PYTHIA 8.

## Key numbers

- Electrical conductivity values investigated: sigma/T = 0.01, 0.1, 1, 2
- Relaxation time fixed at tau = 5/T for all calculations
- Freeze-out temperature: T = 140 MeV
- Static charge susceptibility from lattice QCD: chi_hat approximately 0.6
- For photons at p_T = 1 MeV, signal-to-background ratio ranges from about 70% (sigma/T = 2) to about 0.4% (sigma/T = 0.01)
- For dielectrons at low p_T, signal-to-background ratio ranges from about 67% (sigma/T = 2) to about 0.3% (sigma/T = 0.01)
- For dielectrons with M > 100 MeV at sigma/T = 0.1, signal-to-background ratio improves by a factor of about 4 to about 6.7%
- Causality bound requires tau > D = sigma/chi

## Context

This work extends fluid dynamic treatments of the quark-gluon plasma (previously focused on shear and bulk viscosities as reviewed by Teaney (2009), Heinz (2013), and Busza (2018)) to the electrical conductivity, which has a similar status as transport coefficients. Prior theoretical calculations of electrical conductivity span a wide range: perturbative QCD by Arnold, Moore and Yaffe (2000, 2003) gave 0.19 < sigma/T < 2; lattice QCD by Aarts (2020) gave 0.003 < sigma/T < 0.018; and relaxation time approximation by Greif and Greiner (2016) gave 0.001 < sigma/T < 0.3. Yin (2014) extracted an effective conductivity 0.04 < sigma/T < 0.1 from PHENIX direct-photon data. The paper enables experimental extraction of electrical conductivity from soft photon and dilepton spectra at LHC energies, with the photon Hanbury Brown-Twiss method (previously applied at SPS energies by the WA98 collaboration) identified as a promising approach to overcome hadronic decay backgrounds.
