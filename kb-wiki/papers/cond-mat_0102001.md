# Chaos and Interactions in Quantum Dots

> Quantum dots are small conducting devices containing up to several thousand electrons. We focus here on closed dots whose single-electron dynamics are mostly chaotic. The mesoscopic fluctuations of the conduction properties of such dots reveal the effects of one-body chaos, quantum coherence and electron-electron interactions.

## Key claim

Closed quantum dots with chaotic single-particle dynamics exhibit mesoscopic fluctuations in conductance that are shaped by the interplay of one-body chaos, quantum coherence, and electron-electron interactions; the paper argues that while the constant-interaction model combined with random-matrix theory explains some statistical properties (e.g., peak-height distributions), interactions beyond the charging energy are necessary to account for observed peak-spacing distributions and saturation of peak-to-peak correlations.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical approaches including the constant-interaction (CI) model, random-matrix theory (RMT) for the Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE) and Gaussian unitary ensemble (GUE), Gaussian processes for parametric correlations, and a random interaction matrix model (RIMM) that combines a GOE/GUE one-body part with a random two-body interaction. Experimental conductance peak-height distributions, weak localization dips, parametric correlations, and peak-spacing statistics are compared with these theoretical predictions.

## Key numbers

- Peak-height distributions for one-channel symmetric leads: \( P_{\rm GOE}(g) = \sqrt{2 / \pi g} \, e^{-2g} \) and \( P_{\rm GUE}(g) = 4g e^{-2g} [K_0(2g) + K_1(2g)] \), where \( K_0, K_1 \) are modified Bessel functions.
- Weak localization crossover field: \( B_{\rm cr} \approx 6 \) mT in the experiment of Folk et al. (2000), with theoretical estimate \( B_{\rm cr} \mathcal{A} / \Phi_0 = \kappa (2\pi \tau_c / \tau_H)^{1/2} = \kappa g_T^{-1/2} = \kappa (4\pi^2) \mathcal{N}^{-1/4} \).
- Parametric correlation field for conductance peak heights: \( c_g(\Delta B) \approx [1 / (1 + (\Delta B / B_c)^2)]^2 \); semiclassical estimate gives correlation flux \( \Phi_c \approx 0.3 \Phi_0 \), while experiment yields \( \approx 0.8 \Phi_0 \).
- Peak-spacing distribution width ratio: \( \sigma_{\rm GOE}(\Delta_2) / \sigma_{\rm GUE}(\Delta_2) \sim 1.1-1.3 \) in experiment (Patel et al., 1998), consistent with RIMM calculations.
- Spectral scrambling: number of added electrons for complete scrambling \( m_c \sim \beta g_T / \lambda \sim \beta \mathcal{N}^{1/2} / \lambda \) or \( m_c \sim (\beta g_T)^{1/2} / \lambda \sim \beta^{1/2} \mathcal{N}^{1/4} / \lambda \) in the presence of surface charge.

## Context

This review extends the random-matrix theory approach to quantum transport pioneered by Jalabert, Stone, and Alhassid (1992) and Beenakker (1997), and addresses discrepancies between the CI-plus-RMT model and experiments by Chang et al. (1996), Folk et al. (1996), and Patel et al. (1998). The paper introduces the random interaction matrix model (RIMM) as a framework to unify the RMT-like behavior of peak-height statistics with the Gaussian-like peak-spacing distributions observed in experiments, enabling future studies of interaction effects in chaotic quantum dots.
