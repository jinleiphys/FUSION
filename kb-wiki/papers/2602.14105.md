# Non-Hermitian Quantum Mechanics of Open Quantum Systems: Revisiting The One-Body Problem

> We review analyses of open quantum systems. We show how non-Hermiticity arises in an open quantum system with an infinite environment, focusing on the one-body problem. One of the reasons for taking the present approach is that we can solve the problem completely, making it easier to see the structures of problems involving open quantum systems. We show that this results in the discovery of a new complete set, which is one of the main topics of the present article. Another reason for focusing on the one-body problem is that the theory permits the strong coupling between the system and the environment. In the current research landscape, it is valuable to revisit the one-body problem for open quantum systems, which can be solved accurately for arbitrary strengths of the system-environment couplings. A rigorous understanding of the problem structures in the present approach will be helpful when we tackle problems with many-body interactions. First, we consider potential scattering and directly define the resonant state as an eigenstate of the Schrödinger equation under the Siegert outgoing boundary condition. We show that the resonant eigenstate can have a complex energy eigenvalue, even though the Hamiltonian is seemingly Hermitian. Second, we introduce the Feshbach formalism, which eliminates the infinite degrees of freedom of the environment and represents its effect as a complex potential. The resulting effective Hamiltonian is explicitly non-Hermitian. By unifying these two ways of defining resonant states, we obtain a new complete set of bases for the scattering problem that contains all discrete eigenstates, including resonant states. We finally mention the non-Markovian dynamics of open quantum systems. We emphasize the time-reversal symmetry of the dynamics that continuously connects the past and the future. We can capture it using the new complete set that we develop here.

## Key claim

The paper argues that non-Hermiticity in open quantum systems arises only when the environment has an infinite number of degrees of freedom, and that resonant states with complex energy eigenvalues can legitimately exist because the Hamiltonian is Hermitian only within a specific functional space of square-integrable functions, not for the exponentially diverging eigenfunctions of resonant states.

## Method

The authors first define resonant states as eigenstates of the time-independent Schrödinger equation under the Siegert outgoing boundary condition, solving for complex wave numbers in a one-dimensional potential scattering problem with delta-function potentials. They then introduce the Feshbach formalism, which eliminates the infinite environmental degrees of freedom and represents their effect as a complex potential, yielding an explicitly non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian. By unifying these two approaches, they construct a new complete set of basis states for the scattering problem that includes all discrete eigenstates, including resonant states.

## Key numbers

- For the case of two positive delta potentials at \(x=\pm\ell\) with strength \(V_1\) (dimensionless parameter \(\alpha_1 = \ell v_1 = 1\)), the resonance poles are found as crossing points of the curves \(\eta = -\frac{1}{2}\ln[\pm\frac{\xi}{\alpha_1}\csc(2\xi)]\) and \(\eta = -\xi\cot(2\xi)-\alpha_1\), where \(\xi = k\ell\) and \(\eta = \kappa\ell\) for complex wave number \(K = k + i\kappa\)
- For the case with an additional delta potential at the origin of strength \(V_0\) (dimensionless parameter \(\alpha_0 = 3\)) and \(\alpha_1 = 1\), the resonance condition becomes \((\ii\xi-\eta-\alpha_0)e^{2i\xi-2\eta} = -(\ii\xi-\eta+\alpha_0)(1 - \frac{i\xi-\eta}{\alpha_1})\), solved numerically via density plots and Newton-Raphson method
- The transmission coefficient \(\mathcal{T} = 1/|T_{11}|^2\) for the three-delta potential case shows resonance peaks that do not always align with pole locations; for \(\alpha_0 = 3\) and \(\alpha_1 = 1\), only one broad resonance peak appears for a pair of odd-parity and even-parity resonance poles

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on resonant states dating back to Gamow (1928) for alpha decay and Siegert (1939) for the outgoing boundary condition, as well as Feshbach (1958, 1962) who justified non-Hermitian Hamiltonians by eliminating environmental degrees of freedom. The paper unifies these two storylines and builds on Newton (1960, 1982) who proved that bound states plus the continuum of scattering states form a complete set, and Simon (2000) who proved specific arrangements of bound and anti-bound states for semi-infinite spaces. The new complete set developed here enables rigorous treatment of non-Markovian dynamics in open quantum systems for arbitrary coupling strengths, beyond the Born-Markov approximation used in the GKSL equation (Gorini 1976, Lindblad 1976).
