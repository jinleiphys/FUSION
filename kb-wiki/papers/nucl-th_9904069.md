# On nuclear transport at small excitations

> Numerical computations of transport coefficients at low temperatures are presented for shapes typically encountered in nuclear fission. The influence of quantum effects of the nucleonic degrees of freedom is examined, with pair correlations included. Consequences for global collective motion are studied for the case of the decay rate. The range of temperatures is specified above which this motion may be described as a quantal diffusion process.

# On nuclear transport at small excitations

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that at low temperatures (below approximately 1 MeV), nuclear transport coefficients are strongly suppressed by quantum effects and pair correlations, such that the damping factor η falls below 0.1 and Kramers' high-viscosity formula for the fission decay rate no longer applies; the authors specify that the locally harmonic approximation (LHA) breaks down below a critical temperature Tc, limiting the regime where quantal diffusion descriptions are valid.

## Method

The authors performed numerical calculations of transport coefficients (friction γ, inertia M, and local stiffness C) for the nucleus ²²⁴Th using a deformed shell model with BCS-type pair correlations, computing the damping factor η = γ/(2√(M|C|)) at both the potential minimum and the fission barrier. The imaginary part of the nucleonic self-energy was approximated by a constant value at the Fermi energy, and the gap parameter Δ was determined self-consistently from the gap equation as a function of temperature. The resulting transport coefficients were then used to evaluate Kramers' decay rate and quantum corrections via a product formula for the correction factor fQ.

## Key numbers

- The pairing transition temperature Tpair is approximately 0.5 MeV, below which pair correlations are present.
- Below Tpair ≈ 0.5 MeV, the damping factor η is smaller than about 0.1 at both the minimum and the barrier.
- Up to T ≈ 1 MeV, η stays below about 0.2 at the potential minimum and below about 0.3 at the fission barrier.
- The collective frequency ω (denoted ϖ) is about 1 MeV/ħ at both the potential minimum and the saddle, with accuracy of about 20%.
- The critical temperature Tc below which the diffusion equation loses meaning satisfies Tc ≤ ħϖb/π, with ħϖb of order 1 MeV, and Tc < Tpair.
- Quantum corrections to the Kramers decay rate change the rate by about 30% or less in the temperature range studied, dropping to about 10% at T = 1 MeV.
- The macroscopic (liquid-drop) model underestimates the decay rate by about a factor of 10 compared to the microscopic results.

## Context

This work extends the linear response approach to nuclear transport developed by Hofmann (reviewed in Hofmann, 1997) to the low-temperature regime where quantum effects and pair correlations dominate, building on earlier calculations by Ivanyuk and Hofmann (1998) that examined transport at higher temperatures. The results contradict macroscopic models based on wall friction and liquid-drop inertia, which predict much larger damping, and show that at low temperatures the damping is weak enough that Kramers' low-viscosity limit may become relevant for the first time in nuclear physics. The paper identifies the breakdown of the locally harmonic approximation below Tc as a key open problem for describing cold production of super-heavy elements, and suggests that further microscopic studies of collisions under pair correlations and fluctuations in the gap parameter are needed.
