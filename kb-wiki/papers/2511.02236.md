# Magnetic-type Love number differentiating quark stars from neutron stars

> The quark star (QS) is a hypothetical and yet undiscovered stellar object, and its existence would mark a paradigm shift in research on nuclear and quark matter. Although compactness is a well-known signature for distinguishing between two branches of QSs and neutron stars (NSs), some QSs can overlap with NSs in the radius-mass plane. To manifest their evident differences, we investigate the tidal properties of QSs and NSs. We then find that the magnetic-type Love number is a robust indicator for differentiating between QSs and NSs, whereas the electric-type one is insufficient when QSs and NSs have similar masses and radii. Finally, we show that gravitational waves from binary star mergers can be sensitive to differences between QSs and NSs to the detectable level.

## Key claim

The magnetic-type quadrupolar Love number \(k_2^\mathrm{mag}\) can robustly differentiate quark stars from neutron stars even when their masses and radii are nearly degenerate, whereas the electric-type Love number \(k_2^\mathrm{el}\) is insufficient in that overlapping region. The authors hedge that this holds for masses \(M \gtrsim 1.4\,M_\odot\) where the two stellar types can masquerade as each other.

## Method

The authors constructed 28 quark star equations of state (EoSs) using a polytropic interpolation between a nonzero surface energy density \(\varepsilon_0\) and a perturbative QCD high-density regime, with parameters from Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) models and pQCD-based EoSs. For comparison, they employed 26 neutron star EoSs from a standard polytropic parametrization (Read 2008). They computed the electric-type (\(k_2^\mathrm{el}\)) and magnetic-type (\(k_2^\mathrm{mag}\)) quadrupolar Love numbers for each EoS by solving the stellar structure equations with irrotational fluid assumptions, then compared the \(M\)-\(R\), \(M\)-\(k_2^\mathrm{el}\), and \(M\)-\(k_2^\mathrm{mag}\) relations.

## Key numbers

- For neutron stars in the high-mass range \(1.4\,M_\odot \lesssim M \lesssim 2.0\,M_\odot\), the magnetic-type Love number satisfies \(|k_2^\mathrm{mag}| \lesssim 0.0033\); a compact object with \(|k_2^\mathrm{mag}| \gtrsim 0.0033\) is unlikely to be a neutron star.
- A value of \(|k_2^\mathrm{mag}| \approx 0.004\) would constitute a "smoking gun" for a quark star if confirmed with significant confidence.
- To distinguish a binary quark star merger from a binary neutron star merger via gravitational waves, the required single-detector signal-to-noise ratio is \(\rho = 63.9\) at 68% confidence and \(\rho = 82.2\) at 90% confidence in a 4-dimensional parameter space \((\Lambda_i, \Sigma_i)\); for a 6-dimensional space \((M_i, \Lambda_i, \Sigma_i)\) the required \(\rho\) is 78.0 (68%) and 96.2 (90%).
- For reference, the combined SNR of GW170817 was 32.4, about half the required threshold.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of tidal deformability in compact stars (Hinderer 2007, Damour 2009, Binnington 2009, Yagi 2013, Postnikov 2010) by systematically comparing 28 quark star EoSs against 26 neutron star EoSs. Prior work (Postnikov 2010) had noted that \(k_2^\mathrm{el}\) takes similar values for both types in the high-mass range. The authors show that the magnetic-type Love number breaks this degeneracy, enabling differentiation even when masses and radii overlap. The result enables future gravitational-wave observations to identify quark star candidates among objects that appear neutron-star-like in mass and radius, provided third-generation detectors (such as the Einstein Telescope) can achieve the required sensitivity.
