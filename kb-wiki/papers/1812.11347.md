# Particle Yields and Ratios within Equilibrium and Non-Equilibrium Statistics

> In characterizing the yields and ratios various of well identified particles in the ALICE experiment, we utilize extensive {\it additive} thermal approaches, to which various missing states of the hadron resonances are taken into consideration, as well. Despite some non-equilibrium conditions that are slightly driving this statistical approach away from equilibrium, the approaches are and remain additive and extensive. Besides van der Waals repulsive interactions (assuming that the gas constituents are no longer point-like, i.e. finite-volume corrections taken into consideration), finite pion chemical potentials as well as perturbations to the light and strange quark occupation factors are taken into account. When confronting our calculations to the ALICE measurements, we conclude that the proposed conditions for various aspects deriving the system out of equilibrium notably improve the reproduction of the experimental results, i.e. improving the statistical fits, especially the finite pion chemical potential. This points out to the great role that the non-equilibrium pion production would play, and the contributions that the hadron resonance missing states come up with, even when the principles of statistical extensivity and additivity aren't violated. These results seem to propose revising the conclusions propagated by most of the field, that the produced particles quickly reach a state of local equilibrium leading to a collective expansion often described by fluid dynamics. This situation seems not remaining restrictively valid, at very large collision energies.

## Key claim

The authors argue that particle yields and ratios in Pb+Pb collisions at LHC energies can be well reproduced by an extensive (additive) thermal model, provided that non-equilibrium conditions are included, specifically a finite pion chemical potential and quark occupation factors differing from unity, contradicting the common assumption that the produced particles quickly reach local equilibrium at very high collision energies.

## Method

The authors use an extended Hadron Resonance Gas (HRG) model with van der Waals repulsive interactions (finite-volume corrections) and include missing hadron resonance states predicted by Capstick (1986) but not yet experimentally confirmed. They introduce a finite pion chemical potential based on Bogolubov's superfluidity theory for a non-perfect Bose-Einstein gas, and allow the light and strange quark occupation factors (gamma_l and gamma_s) to deviate from unity. The model is fitted to ALICE measurements of particle yields and ratios from central Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV, with the quality of fit assessed by chi-squared per degree of freedom.

## Key numbers

- The best fit parameters at non-equilibrium (gamma_l != 1, gamma_s != 1) with missing states are: mu_B = 0.126 +/- 0.01 MeV, gamma = 1.19 +/- 0.05, gamma_s = 1.24 +/- 0.07, mu_pi = 40.12 +/- 0.65 MeV, and T = 156.45 +/- 1.75 MeV.
- The hard-core radius of hadrons is set at r <= 0.2 fm, below which excluded volume corrections become negligible.
- The study uses hadron resonances with mass <= 2 GeV from the PDG compilation (2018) plus missing baryon states from Capstick (1986).
- The pion-to-proton ratio overestimation by a factor of 1.5 in earlier blast-wave model studies (PRC2014) is addressed by the finite pion chemical potential approach.

## Context

This work extends the equilibrium HRG model approach of Tawfik (2014) and others to LHC energies, where previous attempts using Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics faced challenges such as the pion-to-proton anomaly. It builds on the proposal by Ruuskanen (1990) for non-equilibrium pion production and Bogolubov's superfluidity theory (1947) for Bose-Einstein condensation. The results contradict the standard assumption of rapid local equilibration used in hydrodynamic models, and suggest that finite pion chemical potential is essential for reproducing ALICE data. The authors compare their results with THERMUS predictions and with generic (non)extensive statistical approaches (Tawfik 2017, 2018), finding that extensive models with non-equilibrium pion production work better than Tsallis-type approaches at LHC energies.
