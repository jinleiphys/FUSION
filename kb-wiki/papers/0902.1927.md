# Relativistic shock waves in viscous gluon matter

> We solve the relativistic Riemann problem in viscous gluon matter employing a microscopic parton cascade. We demonstrate the transition from ideal to viscous shock waves by varying the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio $η/s$ from zero to infinity. We show that an $η/s$ ratio larger than 0.2 prevents the development of well-defined shock waves on timescales typical for ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. Comparisons with viscous hydrodynamic calculations confirm our findings.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that a shear viscosity to entropy density ratio η/s larger than 0.2 prevents the development of well-defined shock waves on timescales typical for ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC, meaning that observable Mach cones would require η/s smaller than this threshold.

## Method
The authors solved the relativistic Riemann problem in viscous gluon matter using the BAMPS microscopic parton cascade, which solves the Boltzmann equation for on-shell gluons with binary isotropic scattering. They varied the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio η/s from zero to infinity by locally adjusting the cross section, and compared their results with viscous hydrodynamic calculations using the Israel-Stewart equations solved via the vSHASTA numerical algorithm.

## Key numbers
- For η/s = 0.001, the ideal hydrodynamic solution was reproduced to very high precision by BAMPS
- For η/s = 0.1, the shock plateau formed at t = 3.2 fm/c
- The critical Knudsen number for shock formation was K_f = 0.053 for the given initial pressure ratio
- For η/s = 0.2, a shock will not be visible until 6-7.2 fm/c, which likely exceeds the QGP lifetime at RHIC
- The temperature used in the formation time calculation was T = 350 MeV

## Context
This work extends earlier theoretical predictions of shock waves in heavy-ion collisions from Scheid (1974) and experimental observations by Baumgardt (1975) and Gutbrod (1989). It directly addresses whether the η/s values below 0.4 deduced from elliptic flow measurements by Luzum (2008) and Xu (2007) are small enough to allow shock wave formation. The results enable future studies of Mach cone formation from jets within the BAMPS approach, as noted by Neufeld (2008).
