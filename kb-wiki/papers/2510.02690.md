# Implications of a Weakening N = 126 Shell Closure Away from Stability for r-Process Astrophysical Conditions

> The formation of the third r-process abundance peak near A = 195 is highly sensitive to both nuclear structure far from stability and the astrophysical conditions that produce the heaviest elements. In particular, the N = 126 shell closure plays a crucial role in shaping this peak. Experimental data hints that the shell weakens as proton number departs from Z = 82, a trend largely missed by global mass models. To investigate its impact on r-process nucleosynthesis, we employ both standard global models with strong closures and modified Duflo-Zuker (DZ) models that reproduce the weakening, combined with three sets of beta minus decay rates. Strong shell closures generate sharply peaked abundances, whereas weakened closures consistent with the experimental trend produce broader, flatter patterns. Accurately reproducing the solar third peak under weakened shell strength requires highly neutron-rich conditions (Ye <= 0.175) and slower decay rates. These results demonstrate that a weakening N = 126 shell closure away from stability imposes significant constraints on the astrophysical environments of the r-process and underscores the need for precise mass measurements and improved characterization of beta minus decay properties in this region.

# Implications of a Weakening N = 126 Shell Closure Away from Stability for r-Process Astrophysical Conditions

## Key claim

If the N = 126 shell closure weakens away from stability as suggested by experimental data from the Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME2020), then reproducing the solar third r-process peak requires both highly neutron-rich conditions (electron fraction Ye ≤ 0.175) and slower beta-minus decay rates (specifically the MLR or Ney models), while stronger shell closures produce acceptable peaks under a wider range of conditions.

## Method

The authors modified the Duflo-Zuker (DZ) mass model by varying its 17th coefficient, associated with a monopole-quadrupole term, to create two variants: a weaker shell closure (WK-DZ) consistent with the AME2020 trend and a stronger shell closure (ST-DZ) comparable to FRDM and HFB predictions. They combined these mass tables with three sets of beta-minus decay rates (MLR, Ney, and MKT) in r-process simulations using the PRISM reaction network, adopting hot astrophysical trajectories from simulated disk outflows spanning electron fractions Ye = 0.05–0.35 and entropies per baryon s/k = 20 and 40.

## Key numbers

- The solar third r-process peak occurs near mass number A ~ 195
- Electron fraction threshold for successful third peak reproduction under weakened shell closure: Ye ≤ 0.175
- For Ye ≥ 0.20, weakened shell closure models produce broader, flattened peaks inconsistent with solar data
- Peak-to-flank ratios for solar data: 0.023 from Arnould et al. (2007) and 0.028 from Beer et al. (1997)
- For Ye = 0.175 with MLR and Ney beta-decay rates, peak-to-flank ratios are comparable to or below the solar values
- HFB model predicts D2n values ranging from 4 to 5 MeV for the N = 126 shell gap
- The DZ model includes 33 parameters, of which 31 are nonzero
- Trajectory weighting for higher-Ye cases must be reduced to roughly 1 percent of original values to recover a well-defined peak under weakened shell closure

## Context

This work extends studies of r-process nucleosynthesis that connect nuclear structure to abundance pattern formation, particularly the role of waiting points at closed neutron shells as established by Burbidge et al. (1957) and the waiting point concept from 1983. The authors build on prior work by Mumpower et al. (2016, 2018) on hot r-process conditions and Traver et al. (2020) on trajectory weighting from disk outflows. The study directly addresses the tension between global mass models (FRDM, HFB) that predict strong constant shell closures and experimental AME2020 data suggesting weakening, enabling constraints on astrophysical environments such as neutron star mergers and accretion-disk outflows. The results motivate precision mass measurements at facilities like the Argonne N = 126 Factory and improved beta-decay rate characterization.
