# Isotopic dependence of the fragments' internal temperatures observed in multifragment emission

> The internal temperatures of fragments produced by an excited nuclear source are investigated using the microcanonical version of the Statistical Multifragmentation Model, with discrete energy. We focus on the fragments' properties at the breakup stage, before they have time to deexcite by particle emission. Since the adopted model provides the excitation energy distribution of these primordial fragments, it allows one to calculate the temperatures of different isotope families and infer on the sensitivity to their isospin composition. It is found that, due to the functional form of the nuclear density of states and the excitation energy distribution of the fragments, proton rich isotopes are hotter than neutron rich ones. This property has been taken to be an indication of earlier emission of the former from a source that cools down as it expands and emits fragments. Although this scenario is incompatible with the prompt breakup of a thermally equilibrated source, our results reveal that the latter framework also provides the same qualitative features just mentioned. Therefore they suggest that this property cannot be taken as evidence for non-equilibrium emission. We also found that this sensitivity to the isotopic composition of the fragments depends on the isospin composition of the source, and that it is weakened as the excitation energy of the source increases.

## Key claim
Using the microcanonical Statistical Multifragmentation Model with discrete energy (SMM-DE), the authors show that proton-rich primary fragments are hotter than neutron-rich ones even when all fragments are produced simultaneously from a thermally equilibrated source, contradicting the interpretation that this temperature difference necessarily indicates non-equilibrium sequential emission.

## Method
The authors employed the SMM-DE, which uses recurrence relations to enforce exact conservation of baryon number, charge, and discrete energy in each fragmentation mode. From the model's output of excitation energy distributions for each fragment species, they calculated internal temperatures via the standard thermodynamic relation involving the derivative of the logarithm of the nuclear density of states with respect to excitation energy.

## Key numbers
- Source excitation energy \(E^*/A = 4\) MeV used for the primary calculations on \(^{112}\)Sn and \(^{112}\)Ba sources.
- Source excitation energy \(E^*/A = 10\) MeV used to demonstrate weakening of isotopic temperature dependence.
- Breakup density set to \(\rho_0/3\), where \(\rho_0\) is the saturation density.

## Context
This work extends previous studies using the Expanding Evaporating Source model (Friedman and Lynch, 1983) and experimental observations (e.g., from the INDRA collaboration, 2013) which suggested that proton-rich isotopes are emitted earlier and are therefore hotter than neutron-rich ones. The authors show that the same qualitative behavior arises from the equilibrium prompt breakup described by the SMM-DE, contradicting the interpretation that this temperature difference is a signature of non-equilibrium emission. The results also predict that the isotopic sensitivity weakens at higher source excitation energies, which the authors suggest could be tested experimentally.
