# Effects of Initial Density Fluctuations on Cumulants in Au + Au Collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 7.7 GeV

> Within the ultrarelativistic quantum molecular dynamics (UrQMD) model, the effect of initial density fluctuations on cumulants of the net-proton multiplicity distribution in Au + Au Collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 7.7 GeV was investigated by varying the minimum distance $d_{\rm min}$ between two nucleons in the initialization. It was found that the initial density fluctuations increased with the decrease of $d_{\rm min}$ from 1.6 fm to 1.0 fm, and the influence of $d_{\rm min}$ on the magnitude of the net-proton number fluctuation in a narrow pseudorapidity window ($Δη\leq$ 4) was negligible even if it indeed affected the density evolution during the collision. At a broad pseudorapidity window ($Δη\geq$ 4), the cumulant ratios were enlarged when the initial density fluctuations were increased with the smaller value of $d_{\rm min}$, and this enhancement was comparable to that observed in the presence of the nuclear mean-field potential. Moreover, the enhanced cumulants were more evident in collisions with a larger impact parameter. The present work demonstrates that the fingerprint of the initial density fluctuations on the cumulants in a broad pseudorapidity window is clearly visible, while it is not obvious as the pseudorapidity window becomes narrow.

## Key claim

By varying the minimum distance \(d_{\rm min}\) between two nucleons in the UrQMD initialization, the authors show that initial density fluctuations affect net-proton cumulant ratios only in a broad pseudorapidity window (\(\Delta\eta \geq 4\)), while their influence is negligible in a narrow window (\(\Delta\eta \leq 4\)), even though the fluctuations do affect the density evolution during the collision.

## Method

The ultrarelativistic quantum molecular dynamics (UrQMD) model was used to simulate Au + Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 7.7\) GeV. The initial density fluctuations were controlled by varying the minimum distance \(d_{\rm min}\) between two nucleons in the initialization from 1.6 fm to 1.0 fm. Simulations were performed both with a mean-field potential (UrQMD/m) and in pure cascade mode (UrQMD/c), and cumulant ratios \(C_2/C_1\) and \(C_3/C_2\) of net-proton multiplicity distributions were calculated in coordinate space and in momentum space as a function of pseudorapidity window \(\Delta\eta\) and impact parameter.

## Key numbers

- The standard deviation \(\sigma_{\rho}\) of the initial density distribution obtained with \(d_{\rm min}=1.6\) fm is about one half of that obtained with \(d_{\rm min}=1.0\) fm.
- At initial time and up to t = 1 fm/c, \(C_2/C_1\) of net baryons in the central region (|x|, |y|, |z| < 1 fm) with \(d_{\rm min}=1.0\) fm was much larger than with \(d_{\rm min}=1.6\) fm.
- For \(\Delta\eta \geq 4\), both \(C_2/C_1\) and \(C_3/C_2\) obtained with \(d_{\rm min}=1.0\) fm were larger than those with \(d_{\rm min}=1.6\) fm, and this enhancement was comparable to that caused by the nuclear mean-field potential.
- The effects of both \(d_{\rm min}\) and the nuclear mean-field potential on cumulant ratios were more evident at impact parameter \(b = 5\) fm than at \(b = 0\) fm.

## Context

This work extends studies of initial-state effects on cumulants of conserved quantities in heavy-ion collisions, which are key observables in the Beam Energy Scan (BES) program at RHIC for locating the QCD critical end point. Prior theoretical work has considered detector efficiency, volume fluctuation (Skokov 2012; Xu 2016; Li 2017; Luo 2013), charge and baryon conservation (Sakaida 2014; Shuryak 2018), and nuclear structure effects such as deformation (Jia 2021) and density distribution (Xu 2017), but the influence of initial density fluctuations on cumulants had not been widely studied. The results show that initial density fluctuations can leave a measurable fingerprint on cumulant ratios in a broad pseudorapidity window, which must be accounted for when interpreting experimental data from BES and future programs at FAIR, NICA, and HIAF.
