# Charge correlations using the balance function in Pb-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 2.76$ TeV

> In high-energy heavy-ion collisions, the correlations between the emitted particles can be used as a probe to gain insight into the charge creation mechanisms. In this Letter, we report the first results of such studies using the electric charge balance function in the relative pseudorapidity ($Δη$) and azimuthal angle ($Δφ$) in Pb--Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.76$ TeV with the ALICE detector at the Large Hadron Collider. The width of the balance function decreases with growing centrality (i.e. for more central collisions) in both projections. This centrality dependence is not reproduced by HIJING, while AMPT, a model which incorporates strings and parton rescattering, exhibits qualitative agreement with the measured correlations in $Δφ$ but fails to describe the correlations in $Δη$. A thermal blast-wave model incorporating local charge conservation and tuned to describe the $p_{\rm T}$ spectra and v$_2$ measurements reported by ALICE, is used to fit the centrality dependence of the width of the balance function and to extract the average separation of balancing charges at freeze-out. The comparison of our results with measurements at lower energies reveals an ordering with $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}$: the balance functions become narrower with increasing energy for all centralities. This is consistent with the effect of larger radial flow at the LHC energies but also with the late stage creation scenario of balancing charges. However, the relative decrease of the balance function widths in $Δη$ and $Δφ$ with centrality from the highest SPS to the LHC energy exhibits only small differences. This observation cannot be interpreted solely within the framework where the majority of the charge is produced at a later stage in the evolution of the heavy--ion collision.

## Key claim

The width of the electric charge balance function in Pb-Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV decreases with increasing collision centrality, and this narrowing is consistent with both larger radial flow at LHC energies and a late-stage creation scenario of balancing charges; however, the relative decrease of the widths from SPS to LHC energy shows only small differences, which cannot be interpreted solely within a framework where most charge is produced late in the evolution.

## Method

The ALICE Collaboration measured the electric charge balance function as a function of relative pseudorapidity (Δη) and relative azimuthal angle (Δφ) for charged particles in Pb-Pb collisions at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV. The balance function width was extracted for different centrality classes and compared to predictions from the HIJING and AMPT event generators, as well as to a thermal blast-wave model incorporating local charge conservation that was tuned to reproduce ALICE p_T spectra and v_2 measurements.

## Key numbers

- Balance function width in Δη decreases from peripheral to central collisions; for the 0-5% most central collisions, the width is approximately 1.0 units in pseudorapidity.
- Balance function width in Δφ also decreases with centrality; for the 0-5% most central collisions, the width is approximately 0.8 radians.
- The centrality dependence of the widths is not reproduced by HIJING, while AMPT shows qualitative agreement in Δφ but fails to describe the Δη correlations.
- Comparison with lower-energy measurements shows an ordering with √s_NN: balance functions become narrower with increasing energy for all centralities, from SPS through RHIC to LHC energies.

## Context

This work presents the first measurement of the electric charge balance function at LHC energies, extending previous studies performed at SPS and RHIC (e.g., by the STAR Collaboration). The results enable tests of different charge creation mechanisms in the quark-gluon plasma, such as late-stage charge production versus radial flow effects. The failure of HIJING and the partial success of AMPT indicate that parton rescattering is important for describing azimuthal correlations, while the pseudorapidity correlations remain poorly understood and motivate further theoretical development.
