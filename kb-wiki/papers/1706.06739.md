# Accuracy of the lepton-nucleus scattering models in the quasielastic region

> We present a discussion of models of nuclear effects used to describe an inclusive electron-nucleus scattering in the quasielastic (QE) peak region, aiming to compare them and draw conclusion of their reliability when applied in neutrino-nucleus interactions. A basic motivation is to reduce systematic errors in neutrino oscillation experiments. We concentrate on the neutrino energy profile of the T2K experiment, which provides us a region of the greatest importance in terms of the highest contribution to the charge-current quasielastic (CCQE) cross section. We choose only electron-nucleus data that overlaps with this region. In order to clearify the analysis, we split the data sets into three groups and draw conclusion separately from each one of them. We selected six models for this comparison: Benhar's spectral function with and without final state interaction (Benhar's SF+FSI), Valencia spectral function (Valencia SF), for higher energy transfer only with the hole spectral function; GiBUU and also the local and global Fermi gas models. The latter two are included as a benchmark to quantify the effect of various nuclear effects. All the six models are often used in neutrino scattering studies. A short theoretical description of each model is given. Although in the selected data sets the QE mechanism dominates, we also briefly discuss a possible impact of $2p2h$ and $Δ$ contributions.

## Key claim

The reliability of nuclear models for lepton-nucleus scattering in the quasielastic region depends strongly on the energy transfer range, with models performing well only in specific kinematic regions; the analysis is limited to electron scattering data that overlaps with the T2K neutrino energy profile, and conclusions about neutrino interactions are indirect because the axial current is missing for electrons.

## Method

The author selected six nuclear models (Benhar's spectral function with and without final state interaction, Valencia spectral function, GiBUU, local Fermi gas, and global Fermi gas) and compared their predictions against 32 electron-carbon scattering data sets chosen to overlap with the T2K experiment's CCQE cross-section region. The data sets were divided into three groups based on energy transfer (Region I: 30-50 MeV, Region II: 50-125 MeV, Region III: 125-200 MeV), and each model's differential cross-section predictions were evaluated separately for each region.

## Key numbers

* Region I (energy transfer 30-50 MeV): local Fermi gas overestimates the QE peak by up to a factor of approximately 2 and shifts it by up to 15 MeV; Benhar's spectral function misplaces the QE peak by 20-25 MeV and overestimates it by up to 10%.
* Region II (energy transfer 50-125 MeV): this region contains 18 data sets with at least 95% of the maximal CCQE peak height; the maximum CCQE peak height is 62.5 × 10⁻³⁸ cm²/GeV² at approximately 85 MeV energy transfer.
* Region III (energy transfer 125-200 MeV): contains 9 data sets; the ratio σ_T/σ_L at the QE peak reaches values up to 1.5 (for the E=680 MeV, θ=60° data set), indicating significant non-QE contributions.
* The nonrelativistic calculation of self-energies in the Valencia spectral function induces an error of approximately 7-10% in the integrated cross section for incoming lepton energy of 500 MeV.

## Context

This work extends comparisons of nuclear models for electron scattering (Benhar 1994, Nieves 2004, Buss 2011) by focusing specifically on the kinematic region relevant to the T2K neutrino oscillation experiment (Abe 2011, Abe 2013). The analysis enables quantification of theoretical uncertainties in neutrino-nucleus interaction models used in Monte Carlo generators, which is essential for reducing systematic errors in neutrino oscillation measurements to the 1-2% level required for CP violation phase determination (Alvarez-Ruso 2017). The paper identifies that Region II (50-125 MeV energy transfer) is the most important for T2K and where models perform best, while Region I requires collective excitation treatments such as CRPA or RPA (Gil 1997, Martini 2016, Kolbe 2003) that go beyond the impulse approximation.
