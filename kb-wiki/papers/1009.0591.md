# Is there further evidence for spatial variation of fundamental constants?

> The detection of a spatial variation of the fine-structure constant, alpha, based on study of quasar absorption systems has recently been reported. The physics that causes this alpha-variation should have other observable manifestations, and this motivates us to look for complementary astrophysical effects. In this paper we propose a method to test whether spatial variation of fundamental constants existed during the epoch of big bang nucleosynthesis. Using existing measurements of primordial deuterium abundance we find very weak indications that such a signature might exist, but the paucity of measurements precludes any firm conclusion. We also examine existing quasar absorption spectra data that are sensitive to variation of the electron-to-proton mass ratio, mu, and x = (alpha^2 mu g_p) for spatial variation.

## Key claim

The paper finds weak indications that a spatial dipole in fundamental constants, previously detected for the fine-structure constant in quasar absorption systems, may also be present in measurements of the electron-to-proton mass ratio and in primordial deuterium abundances from big bang nucleosynthesis; however, the paucity of data precludes any firm conclusion.

## Method

The authors model spatial variation of fundamental constants as a dipole function of comoving distance and angle from a fixed axis, using the standard ΛCDM cosmology. They fit this dipole model to existing measurements of the electron-to-proton mass ratio from molecular hydrogen absorption lines in four quasar systems, to the combination x = α² μ g_p from 21-cm and UV line comparisons in nine systems, and to seven measurements of primordial deuterium abundance from quasar absorption systems at z ~ 2.5. Goodness-of-fit is evaluated using χ² per degree of freedom and F-tests, and the best-fit dipole directions are compared to the previously reported "Australian dipole" for α-variation.

## Key numbers

- For the electron-to-proton mass ratio μ from H₂ absorption lines: the dipole model with direction fixed to the Australian dipole (17.4 h, -58°) gives d_μ = 2.6(1.3)×10⁻⁶/Gyr, with reduced χ²/ν = 0.33; the F-test probability that this improvement over the null hypothesis occurs by chance is 4%.
- For the three-parameter dipole fit to the μ data: d_μ = 3.3(1.5)×10⁻⁶/Gyr, RA_d = 16.7(1.5) h, Dec_d = -62(5)°, consistent with the Australian dipole.
- For the Australian dipole of α-variation from quasar data: δα/α = (1.10±0.25)×10⁻⁶ r cos ψ, with dipole axis at (17.4±0.6 h, -58±6°).
- For primordial deuterium abundance: the dipole model with fixed Australian dipole direction gives d_D = 0.0045(35), with χ² = 17.50 (ν = 5) compared to the monopole χ² = 19.13 (ν = 6).
- Interpreting the deuterium dipole in terms of α-variation yields δα/α = 0.003 Ξ(r) at the time of BBN, much larger than the quasar absorption result of ~10⁻⁶ Ξ(r).
- Interpreting in terms of the quark mass ratio X_q = m_q/Λ_QCD yields δX_q/X_q = 0.0013(10) Ξ(r).

## Context

This work extends the detection of a spatial dipole in the fine-structure constant reported by Webb et al. (2010) to other fundamental constants and to the epoch of big bang nucleosynthesis. It reinterprets existing measurements of μ-variation from King et al. (2008) and Malec et al. (2010), and of x = α² μ g_p from Tzanavaris et al. (2007), in terms of spatial variation rather than time variation. The analysis of primordial deuterium abundance uses data compiled by Pettini et al. (2008) and theoretical sensitivity coefficients from Dent et al. (2007) and Flambaum et al. (2007). The results motivate future measurements of primordial abundances at high redshift across a wider sky coverage to confirm or refute the dipole signal, and suggest that measurements of lithium-7 and helium-4 could provide independent tests.
