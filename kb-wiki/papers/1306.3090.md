# The Surface Tension of Magnetized Quark Matter

> The surface tension of quark matter plays a crucial role for the possibility of quark matter nucleation during the formation of compact stellar objects and also for the existence of a mixed phase within hybrid stars. However, despite its importance, this quantity does not have a well established numerical value. Some early estimates have predicted that, at zero temperature, the value falls within the wide range $γ_0\approx10-300{\rm\ MeV/fm^2}$ but, very recently, different model applications have reduced these numerical values to fall within the range $γ_0\approx5-30{\rm\ MeV/fm^2}$ which would favor the phase conversion process as well as the appearance of a mixed phase in hybrid stars. In magnetars one should also account for the presence of very high magnetic fields which may reach up to about $ eB\approx 3-30\, m_π^2$ ($B \approx 10^{19}-10^{20} \,G$) at the core of the star so that it may also be important to analyze how the presence of a magnetic field affects the surface tension. With this aim we consider magnetized two flavor quark matter, described by the Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model. We show that although the surface tension oscillates around its B=0 value, when $0 < eB \lesssim 10 \, m_π^2$, it only reaches values which are still relatively small. For $eB \approx 5\, m_π^2$ the B=0 surface tension value drops by about 30% while for $eB \gtrsim 10\, m_π^2$ it quickly raises with the field intensity so that the phase conversion and the presence of a mixed phase should be suppressed if extremely high fields are present. We also investigate how thermal effects influence the surface tension for magnetized quark matter.

## Key claim

The surface tension of magnetized two-flavor quark matter, computed within the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model, oscillates around its zero-field value for magnetic fields up to about \(eB \approx 10\, m_\pi^2\) and remains relatively small, but for fields above \(eB \gtrsim 10\, m_\pi^2\) it rises quickly, potentially suppressing phase conversion and mixed-phase formation in magnetars.

## Method

The authors used the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model in the mean-field approximation to describe two-flavor quark matter in a uniform magnetic field. They applied the geometric approach of Randrup (1979) to compute the surface tension from the free energy density of uniform matter in the unstable density region, using the Maxwell construction to determine the coexistence densities. The effective interaction range was set to \(a = 0.33\,\mathrm{fm}\), approximately the inverse sigma meson mass, and the model parameters (\(\Lambda = 590\,\mathrm{MeV}\), \(G\Lambda^2 = 2.435\), \(m = 6\,\mathrm{MeV}\)) were fixed to reproduce vacuum pion properties.

## Key numbers

- At zero temperature and zero magnetic field, the surface tension is \(\gamma_0 = 30.38\,\mathrm{MeV/fm^2}\).
- For \(eB = 6\, m_\pi^2\) at zero temperature, the surface tension drops to \(\gamma_0 = 18.63\,\mathrm{MeV/fm^2}\), a decrease of about 30% from the \(B=0\) value.
- For \(eB = 15\, m_\pi^2\) at zero temperature, the surface tension rises to \(\gamma_0 = 73.68\,\mathrm{MeV/fm^2}\).
- The critical temperature for the chiral transition is \(T_c = 81.1\,\mathrm{MeV}\) at \(B=0\), \(T_c = 84.9\,\mathrm{MeV}\) at \(eB = 6\, m_\pi^2\), and \(T_c = 115.8\,\mathrm{MeV}\) at \(eB = 15\, m_\pi^2\).
- The effective quark mass at zero temperature and zero chemical potential increases with field: \(M \approx 400\,\mathrm{MeV}\) at \(B=0\), \(416\,\mathrm{MeV}\) at \(eB = 6\, m_\pi^2\), and \(467\,\mathrm{MeV}\) at \(eB = 15\, m_\pi^2\).

## Context

This work extends the zero-field surface tension calculations for the NJL model by Pinto et al. (2012) to include the effect of strong magnetic fields relevant for magnetars. The results show that for fields up to about \(eB \approx 10\, m_\pi^2\) the surface tension remains small enough to allow mixed-phase formation in hybrid stars, consistent with the recent low estimates of Leticia et al. (2012) and Pinto et al. (2012), but for higher fields the tension grows, favoring a sharp interface as in the earlier estimates of Heiselberg et al. (1993) and Alford et al. (2001). The study enables more realistic modeling of phase conversion in magnetars and hybrid stars with strong magnetic fields.
