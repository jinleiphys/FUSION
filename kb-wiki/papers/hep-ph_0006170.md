# QCD and the Structure of the Nucleon in Electron Scattering

> The internal structure of the nucleon is discussed within the context of QCD. Recent progress in understanding the distribution of flavor and spin in the nucleon is reviewed, and prospects for extending our knowledge of nucleon structure in electron scattering experiments at modern facilities such as Jefferson Lab are outlined.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the internal structure of the nucleon within QCD, emphasizing that while perturbative QCD successfully describes high-energy processes, the transition from quarks and gluons to physical hadrons remains unsolved, and non-perturbative effects are still important for momentum transfers up to at least approximately 10 GeV².

## Method

The paper presents a pedagogical review of QCD elements, electron-nucleon scattering kinematics, elastic form factors, and deep-inelastic structure functions, using the operator product expansion and parton model to connect measured observables to quark and gluon distributions. It discusses chiral symmetry breaking and its role in understanding nucleon structure from low-energy form factors to deep-inelastic scattering, and outlines experimental prospects at Jefferson Lab.

## Key numbers

- The dipole form factor parameter Q₀² = 0.71 GeV² gives proton electric and magnetic r.m.s. radii of 0.81 fm.
- The proton magnetic moment is μ_p = 2.79 μ₀ and the neutron magnetic moment is μ_n = -1.91 μ₀ (in Bohr magnetons).
- The Δ resonance appears at W = 1232 GeV (invariant mass of the hadronic final state).
- The approximate boundary between resonance and deep-inelastic scattering regions is at W = 2 GeV.
- Non-perturbative effects remain important for Q² at least up to ≈ 10 GeV².

## Context

This review extends the line of work on nucleon structure from early SLAC deep-inelastic scattering experiments (EarlySLAC, 1969) and the development of the parton model by Feynman (1969) and Bjorken and Paschos. It builds on the operator product expansion of Wilson and the chiral perturbation theory framework for low-energy QCD. The paper enables future experimental programs at Jefferson Lab to probe the transition between non-perturbative and perturbative regimes, and to measure flavor-separated form factors and spin-dependent structure functions.
