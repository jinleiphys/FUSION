# Atomic corrections for the unique first forbidden $β$ transition of $^{187}$Re

> In this paper, we reexamine one of the most promising candidates for determining the neutrino mass scale -- the unique first forbidden $β$ transition from $^{187}$Re($5/2^+$) to $^{187}$Os($1/2^-$). With the lowest-known ground-state to ground-state $Q$-value for a $β$ transition at $2.4709$ keV, rhenium's $β$ decay can offer insights into the neutrino mass scale puzzle. However, understanding its electron spectrum is a complex task. Besides involving a mixture of $s_{1/2}$-state and $p_{3/2}$-state electrons, the rhenium $β$ spectrum could be strongly influenced by various atomic corrections. In addition to our previous paper, we have incorporated finite nuclear size, diffuse nuclear surface, screening, and exchange corrections into the rhenium $β$ decay model. We have accounted for the last two effects within the framework of the Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater self-consistent method. We have discovered that both screening and exchange effects significantly alter the partial decay rates for the $s_{1/2}$- and $p_{3/2}$-state emission channels, while still maintaining the experimentally confirmed dominance of the $p_{3/2}$-state emission. The ratio between the respective decay rates has been found to be approximately $10^4$. When compared to the other corrections, the exchange effect stands out due to the modification it induces in the spectrum shape. We demonstrate that calculations with and without the exchange effect lead to entirely different shape factors for the decay spectrum. Finally, we illustrate that to preserve the linearity of the Kurie plot, it is essential to include the exchange correction in its definition. We conclude that atomic effects, especially the exchange effect, should be taken into account in current and future investigations of the neutrino mass scale from $β$ decays.

## Key claim

The exchange correction, calculated within a Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater self-consistent framework, not only alters the partial decay rates for the s1/2 and p3/2 emission channels in the 187Re beta decay but also fundamentally changes the shape of the spectrum, shifting the shape factor from an increasing linear behavior to a decreasing quadratic one, which is essential to preserve the linearity of the Kurie plot for neutrino mass determination.

## Method

The authors solved the Dirac equation for continuum electron wave functions using the RADIAL subroutine package, incorporating four approximation schemes: a uniformly charged spherical nucleus (scheme A), a point-like nucleus (scheme B), a Fermi-distributed nuclear charge density (scheme C), and a full potential including nuclear, electronic, and Slater exchange potentials from the Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater self-consistent method (scheme D). Bound electron orbitals were computed using the DHFS.f program for the ground-state electron configuration of 187Os+, and the exchange correction was calculated by extending the formalism of Harston (1992) and Pyper (1998) from allowed beta transitions to the unique first forbidden transition of 187Re.

## Key numbers

- The ground-state to ground-state Q-value for the 187Re beta transition is 2.4709 keV, the lowest known for a beta transition.
- The ratio between the p3/2-state and s1/2-state emission decay rates is approximately 10^4.
- The nuclear radius used was R = r0 A^(1/3) with r0 = 1.2 fm, and for the Fermi distribution the half-way radius was c_rms = 1.07 A^(1/3) = 6.118 fm with surface thickness a = 0.546 fm.
- Binding energies for the 6s1/2 orbital of neutral 187Re were calculated as -7.98 eV (true DHFS) and -7.67 eV (modified DHFS), compared to the experimental value of -7.9 +/- 1 eV.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous investigation of 187Re beta decay (Rastislav, 2011) by incorporating finite nuclear size, diffuse nuclear surface, screening, and exchange corrections that were previously missing from the theoretical description. The study addresses a key gap identified during the MARE project (Nucciotti, 2008), where lack of theoretical knowledge about the rhenium beta spectrum contributed to the project's transition to holmium-based experiments (ECHo and HOLMES). The results enable more accurate modeling of low and ultra-low Q-value beta transitions for future neutrino mass determination experiments, including potential candidates with Q-values under 1 keV (Gamage, 2019; Redshaw, 2023; Keblbeck, 2023).
