# Photoabsorption Cross Sections studied within the axially deformed Relativistic Quasiparticle Finite Amplitude Framework

> Photoabsorption cross sections for 235 stable nuclei, ranging from $^{40}$Ca to $^{209}$Bi, were investigated by the quasiparticle finite amplitude method (QFAM) based on the axially deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) approach using relativistic point-coupling interaction DD-PC1, with extensions to odd-A nuclei. GDR parameters based on the standard Lorentzian (SLO) model were extracted from QFAM results and compared with those from experimental data recommended by IAEA. Good agreement was achieved for giant dipole resonance (GDR) peak energies, while resonance widths were underestimated and hence peak cross sections were overestimated due to the lack of higher-order many-body correlations. These discrepancies were much improved in deformed nuclei. The effects of deformation on photoabsorption cross sections were examined systematically. The comparison of photoabsorption cross sections among QFAM results and discrepant experimental data revealed the potential of QFAM calculations in the evaluation of photonuclear data.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the axially deformed relativistic quasiparticle finite amplitude method (QFAM) can reproduce giant dipole resonance (GDR) peak energies in good agreement with IAEA-recommended experimental data for 235 stable nuclei, but systematically underestimates resonance widths and overestimates peak cross sections due to the omission of higher-order many-body correlations, with these discrepancies being much smaller in deformed nuclei.

## Method

The authors extended the quasiparticle finite amplitude method (QFAM) based on the axially deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) approach to include odd-A nuclei using canonical occupation probabilities from the equal filling approximation. They calculated photoabsorption cross sections for 235 stable nuclei from ⁴⁰Ca to ²⁰⁹Bi using the relativistic point-coupling interaction DD-PC1, solving the linear response equations iteratively on an axially deformed harmonic oscillator basis with principal quantum number truncation N=20 for strength functions. GDR parameters (peak energy, width, and peak cross section) were extracted by fitting the QFAM results to the standard Lorentzian (SLO) model separately for the K=0 and K=1 components, or to a single SLO fit when the energy splitting between components was 1 MeV or less.

## Key numbers

- 235 stable nuclei were studied, ranging from ⁴⁰Ca to ²⁰⁹Bi, including 146 even-even nuclei and 89 odd-A nuclei
- Ground-state quadrupole deformation parameters β₂ ranged from -0.3 to 0.4 across all considered nuclei
- The smearing parameter γ was fixed to 0.5 MeV, equivalent to a full width at half maximum of 1 MeV
- Photoabsorption cross sections were calculated for incident photon energies from 0 to 50 MeV with an energy step Δω = 0.1 MeV
- For the spherical nucleus ²³N, the total energy difference between the present RHB result (-146.025590 MeV) and a spherical RHB reference (-146.025662 MeV) was less than 1 keV
- The energy splitting threshold for treating deformation as negligible was set at |E_r,∥ - E_r,⊥| ≤ 1 MeV, corresponding generally to nuclei with |β₂| < 0.1
- For Nd and Sm isotopes, splitting of the GDR into two peaks became visible in the total photoabsorption cross section when the deformation parameter exceeded approximately 0.2

## Context

This work extends the relativistic QFAM framework, previously developed for even-even nuclei by Bjelčić et al. (2023), to odd-A nuclei and applies it systematically across the nuclear chart for the first time. It contrasts with phenomenological approaches such as the Standard Lorentzian (SLO) model of Brink (1955) and Axel (1962), and with spherical approximations used in earlier microscopic studies by Goriely et al. (2002, 2004) and Daoutidis et al. (2012). The results enable future systematic evaluations of photonuclear data for nuclei where experimental data are discrepant or unavailable, particularly for nuclei far from stability, and provide a microscopic foundation for testing the phenomenological splitting formula of Danos (1958).
