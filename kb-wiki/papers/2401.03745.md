# Can $GW$ Handle Multireference Systems?

> Due to the infinite summation of bubble diagrams, the $GW$ approximation of Green's function perturbation theory has proven particularly effective in the weak correlation regime, where this family of Feynman diagrams is important. However, the performance of $GW$ in multireference molecular systems, characterized by strong electron correlation, remains relatively unexplored. In the present study, we investigate the ability of $GW$ to handle closed-shell multireference systems in their singlet ground state by examining four paradigmatic scenarios. Firstly, we analyze a prototypical example of a chemical reaction involving strong correlation: the potential energy curve of \ce{BeH2} during the insertion of a beryllium atom into a hydrogen molecule. Secondly, we compute the electron detachment and attachment energies of a set of molecules that exhibit a variable degree of multireference character at their respective equilibrium geometries: \ce{LiF}, \ce{BeO}, \ce{BN}, \ce{C2}, \ce{B2}, and \ce{O3}. Thirdly, we consider a \ce{H6} cluster with a triangular arrangement, which features a notable degree of spin frustration. Finally, the dissociation curve of the \ce{HF} molecule is studied as an example of single bond breaking. These investigations highlight a nuanced perspective on the performance of $GW$ for strong correlation, depending on the level of self-consistency, the choice of initial guess, and the presence of spin-symmetry breaking at the Hartree-Fock level.

## Key claim

The paper finds that the GW approximation can provide a quantitative description of multireference molecular systems except in regions of very strong static correlation, where the agreement is only qualitative; the performance depends critically on the level of self-consistency, the choice of initial Hartree-Fock (HF) reference, and whether spin-symmetry breaking is present at the HF level.

## Method

The authors performed GW calculations at three levels of self-consistency (single-shot G0W0, eigenvalue-self-consistent evGW, and quasiparticle-self-consistent qsGW) starting from restricted Hartree-Fock (RHF) references, and compared the resulting principal ionization potentials (IPs) and electron affinities (EAs) to full configuration interaction (FCI) reference values. They examined four paradigmatic closed-shell multireference scenarios: the Be + H2 insertion reaction potential energy curve, a set of six molecules (LiF, BeO, BN, C2, B2, O3) with varying multireference character at equilibrium, a triangular H6 cluster exhibiting spin frustration, and the HF dissociation curve.

## Key numbers

*   For the Be + H2 reaction at the most multireference geometry (x = 2.75 bohr), the worst-case GW deviation from FCI is 0.7 eV for the IP and 0.5 eV for the EA.
*   For the boron dimer (B2), G0W0@RHF yields an IP of 9.06 eV (error +0.09 eV) and an EA of 2.05 eV (error +0.19 eV) relative to FCI values of 8.97 eV and 1.86 eV, respectively.
*   For the carbon dimer (C2), G0W0@RHF using the lower-energy RHF solution (#2) gives an IP of 12.42 eV (error -0.03 eV) and an EA of 4.40 eV (error +1.40 eV) relative to FCI values of 12.45 eV and 3.00 eV.
*   For ozone (O3), G0W0@RHF yields an IP of 13.50 eV (error +0.92 eV) and an EA of 1.96 eV (error +0.68 eV) relative to FCI values of 12.58 eV and 1.28 eV.
*   For the triangular H6 cluster, the RHF-based G0W0 IP is 6.55 eV (error -2.72 eV) and EA is -2.94 eV (error +2.29 eV) relative to FCI values of 9.27 eV and -5.23 eV; using a UHF starting point (#1) reduces the G0W0 IP error to +0.82 eV and the EA error to -0.05 eV.

## Context

This work extends the assessment of the GW approximation, previously known to be accurate for weakly correlated systems (e.g., the GW100 benchmark set by van Setten (2015)), into the domain of molecular strong (static) correlation. It systematically tests GW on closed-shell multireference systems where multiple electronic configurations are nearly degenerate, complementing prior studies of GW in strongly correlated condensed matter (e.g., Verdozzi (1995), Di Sabatino (2016)) and alternative Green's function approaches like the T-matrix approximation. The results enable a nuanced understanding of GW's limitations and guide the choice of self-consistency scheme and reference for future applications to strongly correlated molecular systems.
