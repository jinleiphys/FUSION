# Extracting (n,g) direct capture cross sections from Coulomb dissociation: application to $^{14}$C(n,$γ$)$^{15}$C

> A methodology for extracting neutron direct capture rates from Coulomb dissociation data is developed and applied to the Coulomb dissociation of 15C on 208Pb at 68 MeV/nucleon. Full Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel calculations are performed and an asymptotic normalization coefficient is determined from a fit to the breakup data. Direct neutron capture calculations using the extracted asymptotic normalization coefficient provide $(n,γ)$ cross sections consistent with direct measurements. Our results show that the Coulomb Dissociation data can be reliably used for extracting the cross section for 14C(n,g)15C if the appropriate reaction theory is used. The resulting error bars are of comparable magnitude to those from the direct measurement. This procedure can be used more generally to extract capture cross sections from breakup reactions whenever the desired capture process is fully peripheral.

## Key claim

Coulomb dissociation data can be reliably used to extract the $^{14}$C(n,$\gamma$)$^{15}$C cross section, but only when the appropriate reaction theory (full Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel calculations) is used; the extracted cross sections are consistent with direct measurements and provide error bars of comparable magnitude, but the method is only valid when the capture process is fully peripheral.

## Method

Full Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (CDCC) calculations were performed for the Coulomb dissociation of $^{15}$C on $^{208}$Pb at 68 MeV/nucleon, treating Coulomb and nuclear effects on the same footing with multipole excitations up to Q=3 and partial waves up to l=3. A set of $^{14}$C+n single-particle potentials spanning a range of asymptotic normalization coefficients (ANCs) was used to calculate breakup energy distributions, and the ANC was determined by minimizing $\chi^2$ relative to the breakup data from Nakamura (2003). Direct neutron capture cross sections were then calculated using the extracted ANC and its uncertainty from $\chi^2_{min}+1$.

## Key numbers

- ANC for the $^{15}$C ground state extracted from the RIKEN Coulomb dissociation data: $C_0 = 1.28 \pm 0.01$ fm$^{-1/2}$
- At 23 keV neutron energy, the predicted capture cross section from the RIKEN data is $7.0 \pm 0.2$ $\mu$b, compared to the direct measurement of $7.1 \pm 0.5$ $\mu$b
- The CDCC model space included $^{14}$C+n relative energies up to $E_{rel} \le 8$ MeV, with maximum projectile-target partial wave $L_{max}=9000$, and radial integration out to $R_{max}=1000$ fm
- The set of $^{14}$C+n potentials produced ANCs ranging from 1.254 to 1.376 fm$^{-1/2}$ (from Table 1)

## Context

This work extends the Coulomb dissociation method for extracting neutron capture cross sections, which was previously analyzed using the virtual photon method by Baur (1986) and Bertulani (1988), and addresses discrepancies between direct measurements by Reifarth (2005, 2006), Coulomb dissociation data from Nakamura (2003) and Horvath (2002), and mirror symmetry results from Timofeyuk (2006). The procedure enables reliable extraction of neutron capture rates for fully peripheral reactions on short-lived unstable nuclei where direct measurements are not possible, and demonstrates that the earlier analysis of the 35 MeV/nucleon data by Horvath (2002) contained invalid assumptions about nuclear-Coulomb interference, partial wave composition, and multistep effects that led to capture cross sections approximately half those from other methods.
