# Novel Source of Nonlocality in the Optical Model

> In this work we fit $neutron$ - $^{12}C$ elastic scattering angular distributions in the energy range 12 to 20 MeV, by adding a velocity dependent term to the optical potential. This term introduces a wave function gradient, whose coefficient is real and position dependent, and which represents a nonlocality. We pay special attention to the prominent backscattering minima which depend sensitively on the incident energies, and which are a tell-tale of nonlocalities. Reasonable fits to the analyzing power data are also obtained as a by-product. All our potentials have the form of conventional Woods - Saxon shapes or their derivatives. The number of our parameters (12) is smaller than the number for other local optical potentials, and they vary monotically with energy, while the strengths of the real and imaginary parts of the central potential are nearly constants. Our nonlocality is in contrast to other forms of nonlocalities introduced previously.

## Key claim

The authors introduce a novel nonlocality into the non-relativistic optical model by adding a real, position-dependent velocity-dependent term (a wave function gradient term) to the potential, which they interpret as arising from a spatially variable effective mass of the nucleon in the nuclear medium. They claim this term is distinct from the complex Darwin term in Dirac-based formulations and from other previously studied nonlocalities such as parity-dependent potentials or Perey-Buck nonlocalities.

## Method

The authors fit neutron-12C elastic scattering angular distributions at incident energies from 12 to 20 MeV using a velocity-dependent optical potential (VDOP). The potential consists of a conventional Woods-Saxon central term (real and imaginary), a spin-orbit term, and an added gradient term proportional to the derivative of a Woods-Saxon function, representing the velocity-dependent nonlocality. The 12 fit parameters were varied to reproduce the experimental angular distributions, with special attention to the pronounced backscattering minima, and the resulting potentials were then used to predict analyzing power data without further fitting.

## Key numbers

- Energy range of fits: 12 to 20 MeV for neutron-12C elastic scattering.
- Number of fit parameters: 12, compared to 9-20 for conventional local optical potentials.
- Real central potential strength V0: ranges from 39.0 to 44.7 MeV across 12-20 MeV; imaginary central strength W: ranges from 5.7 to 6.6 MeV.
- Spin-orbit strength Vso: ranges from 11.59+3.67i MeV (at 14 MeV) to 17.19+21.25i MeV (at 20 MeV); spin-orbit radius rso decreases monotonically from 1.15 fm (12 MeV) to 0.80 fm (20 MeV).
- Velocity-dependent term strength ρ0: ranges from -1.46 to -2.27 (dimensionless); its radius rρ ranges from 1.20 to 1.80 fm.
- The peak of ρ(r) lies within the diffused edge of the central potential, supporting interpretation as gradient of nuclear mass density.

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to incorporate nonlocality into the optical model, specifically contrasting with the parity-dependent nonlocality introduced by Cooper and Mackintosh (1990s) and the Perey-Buck (1962) exponential nonlocality form. The authors note that conventional local optical potentials (e.g., Dave and Gould, 1980s; Koning and Delaroche, 2003) could not reproduce the pronounced backscattering minima at 18 and 20 MeV, which the VDOP fits well. The work enables future investigation into whether this velocity-dependent term simulates exchange effects, channel coupling to excited states (Feshbach nonlocality), or represents a genuinely new effective-mass nonlocality.
