# Models, measurements, and effective field theory: proton capture on Beryllium-7 at next-to-leading order

> We employ an effective field theory (EFT) that exploits the separation of scales in the p-wave halo nucleus $^8\mathrm{B}$ to describe the process $^7\mathrm{Be}(p,γ)^8\mathrm{B}$ up to a center-of-mass energy of 500 keV. The calculation, for which we develop the lagrangian and power counting, is carried out up to next-to-leading order (NLO) in the EFT expansion. The power counting we adopt implies that Coulomb interactions must be included to all orders in $α_{\rm em}$. We do this via EFT Feynman diagrams computed in time-ordered perturbation theory, and so recover existing quantum-mechanical technology such as the two-potential formalism for the treatment of the Coulomb-nuclear interference. Meanwhile the strong interactions and the E1 operator are dealt with via EFT expansions in powers of momenta, with a breakdown scale set by the size of the ${}^7$Be core, $Λ\approx 70$ MeV. Up to NLO the relevant physics in the different channels that enter the radiative capture reaction is encoded in ten different EFT couplings. The result is a model-independent parametrization for the reaction amplitude in the energy regime of interest. To show the connection to previous results we fix the EFT couplings using results from a number of potential model and microscopic calculations in the literature. Each of these models corresponds to a particular point in the space of EFTs. The EFT structure therefore provides a very general way to quantify the model uncertainty in calculations of $^7\mathrm{Be}(p,γ)^8\mathrm{B}$. We also demonstrate that the only N$^2$LO corrections in $^7\mathrm{Be}(p,γ)^8\mathrm{B}$ come from an inelasticity that is practically of N$^3$LO size in the energy range of interest, and so the truncation error in our calculation is effectively N$^3$LO. We also discuss the relation of our extrapolated $S(0)$ to the previous standard evaluation.

## Key claim

The paper develops a model-independent effective field theory (EFT) parametrization for the \(^7\mathrm{Be}(p,\gamma)^8\mathrm{B}\) reaction up to 500 keV center-of-mass energy, with the truncation error effectively at N\(^3\)LO because the only N\(^2\)LO correction (an inelasticity) is practically of N\(^3\)LO size in the energy range of interest.

## Method

The authors constructed a halo EFT Lagrangian for the \(^8\mathrm{B}\) system, treating \(^7\mathrm{Be}\) as a core with one low-energy excitation and the proton as a valence nucleon, with power counting based on velocity scaling. They computed Feynman diagrams in time-ordered perturbation theory up to next-to-leading order (NLO), including Coulomb interactions to all orders in \(\alpha_{\mathrm{em}}\) via the two-potential formalism, and encoded the relevant physics in ten EFT couplings. The EFT parameters were fixed by fitting to results from potential model and microscopic calculations in the literature (Davids and Typel 2003, Esbensen 1996, Navratil 2006, Descouvemont 2004), projecting each model into the EFT parameter space.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy of \(^8\mathrm{B}\) in the \(^7\mathrm{Be}+p\) channel: \(B = 0.1364\) MeV, corresponding to binding momentum \(\gamma = 14.96\) MeV.
- Coulomb momentum: \(k_C = 23.9487\) MeV.
- Excitation energy of \(^7\mathrm{Be}^*\): \(E_* = 0.4291\) MeV, corresponding to momentum \(\gamma_\Delta = 26.5352\) MeV.
- Breakdown scale of the EFT: \(\Lambda \approx 70\) MeV, set by the \(^7\mathrm{Be}\) core size (from the \(^3\mathrm{He}+^4\mathrm{He}\) breakup threshold at 1.5874 MeV).
- \(s\)-wave scattering lengths in the two spin channels: approximately 25 fm and \(-7\) fm (Angulo 2003).
- The EFT expansion parameter is \(\gamma/\Lambda \approx 0.2\).

## Context

This work extends halo EFT methods previously applied to neutron-core systems (Bedaque 2003, Hammer 2011, Rupak 2011) and to systems with Coulomb interactions (Kong 1999, Higa 2008, Ryberg 2015) to the specific case of \(^7\mathrm{Be}(p,\gamma)^8\mathrm{B}\). It provides a framework to quantify model uncertainty in calculations of this reaction, which is crucial for interpreting solar neutrino data from chlorine- and water-based detectors (Adelberger 2010, Robertson 2012). The EFT parametrization enables consistent combination of experimental data and theoretical calculations, and the authors have applied Bayesian methods to constrain the low-energy \(S\)-factor in a companion paper (Zhang 2015).
