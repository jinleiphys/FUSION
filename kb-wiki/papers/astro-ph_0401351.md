# Angular Correlations of the MeV Cosmic Gamma Ray Background

> The measured cosmic gamma ray background (CGB) spectrum at MeV energies is in reasonable agreement with the predicted contribution from type Ia supernovae (SNIa). But the characteristic features in the SNIa gamma ray spectrum, weakened by integration over source redshifts, are hard to measure, and additionally the contributions from other sources in the MeV range are uncertain, so that the SNIa origin of the MeV CGB remains unproven. Since different CGB sources have different clustering properties and redshift distributions, by combining the CGB spectrum and angular correlation measurements, the contributions to the CGB could be identified and separated. The SNIa CGB large-scale structure follows that of galaxies. Its rms fluctuation at degree scales has a characteristic energy dependence, ranging from $\sim 1%$ to order of unity and can be measured to several percent precision by proposed future satellites such as the Advanced Compton Telescope. With the identification of the SNIa contribution, the SNIa rate could be measured unambiguously as a function of redshift up to $z \sim 1$, by combining both the spectrum and angular correlation measurements, yielding new constraints on the star formation rate to even higher redshifts. Finally, we show that the gamma ray and neutrino backgrounds from supernovae should be closely connected, allowing an important consistency test from the measured data. Identification of the astrophysical contributions to the CGB would allow much greater sensitivity to an isotropic high-redshift CGB contribution arising in extra dimension or dark matter models.

## Key claim

The SNIa origin of the MeV cosmic gamma ray background (CGB) remains unproven because the characteristic spectral features are weakened by redshift integration and other sources may contribute, but combining CGB spectrum and angular correlation measurements can break this degeneracy and identify the SNIa contribution.

## Method

The authors calculate the SNIa CGB mean flux spectrum, angular autocorrelation power spectrum, and cross-correlation power spectrum with galaxies, using Limber's equation and assuming SNIa follow the galaxy distribution with constant bias. They model the SNIa rate from the star formation rate with a characteristic delay time of 1 Gyr, adopt a flat Lambda-CDM cosmology with Omega_m=0.3 and h=0.7, and use the galaxy correlation function inferred from SDSS with slope alpha=-1.7. They propose inversion techniques using the sparse kernel matrices that result from the line emission features to recover the supernova rate as a function of redshift.

## Key numbers

- The SNIa CGB rms fluctuation at degree scales ranges from ~1% to order of unity, depending on the energy band
- With ACT and SDSS, several percent precision in both C_gamma and C_gammaG measurements would be possible
- The gamma flux is ~10^7 (100 cm^2)^(-1) sr^(-1) yr^(-1), requiring a field of view of ~1 sr and integration time of ~1 year
- About N ~ 10^7 SNIa can be observed in a one-year survey, giving Poisson noise less than 10^(-3)
- The supernova neutrino background flux should exceed the supernova gamma ray background flux by ~10^4, with F_nu ~ 10^2 cm^(-2) s^(-1) and F_gamma ~ 10^(-2) cm^(-2) s^(-1)
- The SNIa rate could be measured up to z ~ 1 using the proposed techniques, and the mean flux inversion could probe to z ~ 1.5

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Watanabe (1999) and Ruiz-Lapuente (2000) that showed the SNIa contribution could account for the observed CGB spectrum from COMPTEL and SMM, but could not prove the SNIa origin due to degeneracies with other sources. The proposed angular correlation techniques differ from the uncorrelated Poisson fluctuations considered by Cline (1990) and Gao (1990). The paper enables unambiguous measurement of the SNIa rate as a function of redshift, new constraints on the star formation rate, and improved sensitivity to isotropic high-redshift CGB contributions from extra dimension or dark matter models, as well as a consistency test between the gamma ray and neutrino backgrounds from supernovae.
