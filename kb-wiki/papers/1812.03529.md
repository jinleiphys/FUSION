# Properties of Neutron Stars with hyperon cores in parameterized hydrostatic conditions

> Models of neutron stars (NSs) with hyperon cores are constructed with an effective chiral model in mean-field approximation. The hyperon couplings are fixed by reproducing their experimentally determined binding energies. The impact of these couplings on population of different particles and the equation of state (EoS) are studied in this work. The global properties of NSs like gravitational mass, radius, baryonic mass and central density are calculated using parameterized Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations (PTOV) with special emphasis on two effects of pressure - one contributing to total mass density and the other to self gravity of the star. We find that with PTOV solutions in static conditions, a softer EoS (including hyperons) can also lead to massive stellar configurations of NSs, which are in well agreement with the observed maximum mass bound of $\approx 2 M_{\odot}$ (PSR J0348-0432). Estimates of $R_{1.4}$ and $R_{1.6}$, obtained with the PTOV equations are consistent with the recent findings of the same from the data analysis of gravitational waves (GW170817) observation. Keywords: Neutron Star; Hyperons; Equation of State; parameterized Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations

## Key claim

Using parameterized Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (PTOV) equations that separate two distinct roles of pressure (inertial pressure contributing to mass density and pressure contributing to self-gravity), a softer equation of state including hyperons can still produce neutron star configurations with masses up to approximately 2 solar masses, consistent with the observed maximum mass bound of PSR J0348-0432, provided the parameters β and χ are chosen appropriately.

## Method

The authors constructed models of neutron stars with hyperon cores using an effective chiral model in mean-field approximation, including the full baryon octet (n, p, Λ, Σ⁻,⁰,⁺, Ξ⁻,⁰). Hyperon coupling constants were fixed by reproducing experimentally determined binding energies of each hyperon species at saturation density: -28 MeV for Λ, +30 MeV for Σ, and -18 MeV for Ξ. The equations of state were then solved using parameterized Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations, varying only the two pressure-related parameters β (inertial pressure contribution to mass density) and χ (pressure contribution to self-gravity), while keeping other parameters at their general relativistic values.

## Key numbers

- Maximum gravitational mass for model NM-I ranges from 1.76 M⊙ (β=1, χ=1, standard GR) to 2.13 M⊙ (β=0, χ=0), with corresponding radii 11.2 km to 11.6 km
- Maximum gravitational mass for model NM-II ranges from 1.65 M⊙ (β=1, χ=1) to 2.05 M⊙ (β=0, χ=0), with corresponding radii 11.0 km to 11.4 km
- Central energy density is approximately 1.84 × 10¹⁵ g cm⁻³ for NM-I and approximately 1.48 × 10¹⁵ g cm⁻³ for NM-II
- R₁.₄ (radius of 1.4 M⊙ star) ranges from 12.5 km to 13.2 km for NM-I and from 11.6 km to 12.3 km for NM-II
- R₁.₆ ranges from 12.4 km to 13.1 km for NM-I and from 11.2 km to 12.2 km for NM-II
- Λ hyperons appear first at 1.9ρ₀ for NM-I and 2.2ρ₀ for NM-II; Ξ⁻ appears at 2.7ρ₀ (NM-I) and 3.3ρ₀ (NM-II); Σ⁻ appears at 3.9ρ₀ (NM-I) and 4.1ρ₀ (NM-II)
- Nuclear saturation density ρ₀ = 0.153 fm⁻³ for both models; symmetry energy coefficient J = 32 MeV for both models

## Context

This work extends the parameterized TOV approach of Velten (2016) and Schwab (2008) to neutron stars with hyperon cores, addressing the "hyperon puzzle" where inclusion of hyperons typically softens the equation of state and reduces maximum mass below the 2 M⊙ threshold. The results show that modifying how pressure contributes to mass density and self-gravity can reconcile hyperon-rich equations of state with observed massive neutron stars like PSR J0348-0432 and PSR J1614-2230, without invoking exotic matter or modified gravity theories. The calculated R₁.₄ and R₁.₆ values are consistent with constraints from the gravitational wave event GW170817, and the work suggests that self-gravity effects are as important as the equation of state in determining neutron star masses.
