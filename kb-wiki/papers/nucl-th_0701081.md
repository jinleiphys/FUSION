# Exotic modes of excitation in atomic nuclei far from stability

> We review recent studies of the evolution of collective excitations in atomic nuclei far from the valley of $β$-stability. Collective degrees of freedom govern essential aspects of nuclear structure, and for several decades the study of collective modes such as rotations and vibrations has played a vital role in our understanding of complex properties of nuclei. The multipole response of unstable nuclei and the possible occurrence of new exotic modes of excitation in weakly-bound nuclear systems, present a rapidly growing field of research, but only few experimental studies of these phenomena have been reported so far. Valuable data on the evolution of the low-energy dipole response in unstable neutron-rich nuclei have been gathered in recent experiments, but the available information is not sufficient to determine the nature of observed excitations. Even in stable nuclei various modes of giant collective oscillations had been predicted by theory years before they were observed, and for that reason it is very important to perform detailed theoretical studies of the evolution of collective modes of excitation in nuclei far from stability. We therefore discuss the modern theoretical tools that have been developed in recent years for the description of collective excitations in weakly-bound nuclei. The review focuses on the applications of these models to studies of the evolution of low-energy dipole modes from stable nuclei to systems near the particle emission threshold, to analyses of various isoscalar modes, those for which data are already available, as well as those that could be observed in future experiments, to a description of charge-exchange modes and their evolution in neutron-rich nuclei, and to studies of the role of exotic low-energy modes in astrophysical processes.

## Key claim

This review argues that the evolution of collective excitations in nuclei far from stability, particularly the emergence of exotic low-energy modes such as the pygmy dipole resonance, can be understood through modern theoretical frameworks (density functional theory and random-phase approximation extensions), but the available experimental data are insufficient to definitively determine the nature of the observed excitations in unstable neutron-rich nuclei.

## Method

The authors survey theoretical approaches for describing collective excitations in weakly-bound nuclei, focusing on relativistic and non-relativistic density functional theory combined with random-phase approximation (RPA) and its extensions (quasiparticle RPA, continuum RPA, and models including particle-vibration coupling). They apply these frameworks to analyze the evolution of low-energy dipole modes from stable nuclei to systems near the particle emission threshold, isoscalar modes, charge-exchange modes in neutron-rich nuclei, and the role of exotic modes in astrophysical processes.

## Key numbers

- Low-energy dipole strength (pygmy resonance) in neutron-rich nuclei: observed below 10 MeV in experiments on unstable nuclei such as those by Leistenschneider (2001) and Tryggestad (2002, 2003)
- Isoscalar giant monopole resonance (ISGMR) centroid energies: measured in stable nuclei at approximately 13-14 MeV for medium-mass nuclei (Youngblood 2004, Uchida 2004)
- Isoscalar giant dipole resonance (ISGDR) strength: observed in experiments by Clark (2001) and Itoh (2003) at energies around 20-25 MeV
- Gamow-Teller resonance (GTR) strength: measured in (p,n) reactions at energies around 15-20 MeV excitation (Doering 1975, Wakasa 1997)
- Pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) in stable nuclei: observed in (gamma,gamma') experiments at energies 5-8 MeV with integrated cross sections of order 1-10 mb MeV (Ryezayeva 2002, Hartmann 2004)

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework of collective nuclear excitations from stable nuclei (described in textbooks by Ring and Schuck (1980), Blaizot and Ripka (1986), and Bertsch and Broglia (1994)) to exotic neutron-rich systems near the drip lines. It builds on earlier work on giant resonances (Harakeh and Van Der Woude 2001, Bortignon 1998) and the development of relativistic mean-field models (Vretenar 2005). The review enables future experimental programs at radioactive beam facilities to test predictions of low-energy dipole modes, isoscalar modes, and charge-exchange excitations, and it connects these nuclear structure studies to astrophysical processes such as nucleosynthesis in explosive stellar environments (Goriely 2004, Langanke and Martinez-Pinedo 2003).
