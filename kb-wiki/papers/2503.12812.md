# Nuclear Excitation and Control Induced by Intense Vortex Laser

> The existing intense laser-based approaches for nuclear excitation offer ultrafast temporal resolution and high efficiency compared to traditional accelerator probes. However, controlling nuclear properties such as spin and magnetic moment remains an unprecedented challenge. Here, we put forward a novel method for nuclear excitation and control induced by intense vortex lasers. We develop a theory incorporating the orbital angular momentum (OAM) of vortex laser within the nuclear hyperfine mixing framework. We find that intense vortex laser can effectively excite hydrogen-like thorium-229 nucleus and induce three-dimensional rotation of the nuclear magnetic moment. This rotation arises from the localized electromagnetic field and new transition channels excited by the vortex laser, and can be reconstructed through radiation spectrum analysis. Moreover, the OAM of vortex laser enables the chaotic system to exhibit topologically protected periodic patterns in nuclear excitation and radiation, facilitating precise experimental measurements. Our findings underscore the potential of vortex laser for high-precision nuclear control and imaging, deepening our understanding of nuclear properties and hyperfine structure, and advancing quantum information and nuclear technologies.

# Nuclear Excitation and Control Induced by Intense Vortex Laser

## Key claim

Intense vortex lasers carrying orbital angular momentum can excite hydrogen-like thorium-229 nuclei and induce three-dimensional rotation of the nuclear magnetic moment, while also imposing topologically protected periodic patterns on an otherwise chaotic nuclear excitation and radiation system. The claim is conditional on the ion being positioned off the vortex laser axis (nonzero impact parameter) and on the laser intensity being sufficiently high to enter the nonlinear nonperturbative regime.

## Method

The authors developed a theory that incorporates the orbital angular momentum (OAM) of a vortex laser into the nuclear hyperfine mixing (NHM) framework for hydrogen-like thorium-229 ions (229Th89+). They solved the time-dependent Schrödinger equation for a four-level system comprising hyperfine-split states, using a Bessel-Gauss mode vector potential for the vortex laser with linear polarization, 800 nm wavelength, and a sin-squared temporal envelope. The radiation spectrum was calculated from the second time derivative of the laser-induced magnetic dipole moment, and the nuclear excitation probability was obtained from the population of the isomeric state at the end of the laser pulse.

## Key numbers

- Laser wavelength: 800 nm, corresponding to optical period T0 = 2.67 fs
- Laser peak intensities considered: 10^17 W/cm^2 and 10^22 W/cm^2
- NHM mixing coefficient: c_m ≈ -0.031
- Pulse duration: 20 cycles (τ = 20 T0) for radiation spectra calculations; 10 cycles (τ = 10 T0) for excitation probability versus intensity scans
- For non-vortex laser at 10^22 W/cm^2, total radiation spectrum ~10^-5 (arbitrary units)
- Vortex laser radiation enhancement: several orders of magnitude compared to non-vortex case
- Excitation probability periodicity: P^V_exc(φ_b) = P^V_exc(φ_b + 180°/m_l)
- Number of radiation maxima/minima across full azimuthal angle: 2m_l (e.g., 2 for m_l=1, 4 for m_l=2)
- Probability of 1s_{1/2} electron excitation to 2p_{1/2} state at 10^23 W/cm^2: approximately 10^-5
- Energy gap between 2s_{1/2} and 2p_{1/2} states in 229Th87+: approximately 245 eV
- Non-vortex laser excitation probability begins saturating after 10^21 W/cm^2; vortex laser saturation delayed until approximately 10^22 W/cm^2

## Context

This work extends prior theoretical studies on laser-nucleus interactions with hydrogen-like thorium-229 ions by Zhang (2024), which predicted over 10% isomeric excitation using non-vortex femtosecond lasers but noted chaotic and unpredictable excitation and radiation patterns. It also builds on the authors' own previous theoretical work on relativistic vortex particles (gamma rays and electrons) interacting with nuclei in the giant resonance regime (Lu 2023, Lu 2025). The method differs from traditional nuclear magnetic resonance probes (limited to μeV transitions in light nuclei) and accelerator-based probes (limited by beam luminosity). The findings enable new approaches for high-precision nuclear control and imaging on femtosecond timescales, with potential applications in quantum information science and nuclear technologies, though experimental realization requires existing ion storage rings or Penning traps combined with vortex laser capabilities currently available at PW laser facilities such as PHELIX at GSI (10^18 W/cm^2) and SULF at SIOM (10^20 W/cm^2).
