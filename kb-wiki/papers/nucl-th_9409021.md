# Binding energies and nonradiative decay rates of $H\! edμ$-molecular ions

> The method of hyperspherical "surface" functions has been applied to the calculation of eigenvalues and eigenfunctions of muonic molecular ions $^{3,4}\! H\! edμ$. Binding energies and nonradiative decay rates for the states of the total angular momentum $L=0,~1,~2$ have been obtained.

# Wiki Page: Binding Energies and Nonradiative Decay Rates of $H\!ed\mu$-Molecular Ions

## Key claim
The hyperspherical "surface" functions method yields binding energies and nonradiative decay rates for the $^{3,4}\!H\!ed\mu$ systems that are consistent with prior calculations, but the calculated decay rates show significant variation across different theoretical approaches, indicating that the one-level approximation used here gives an upper bound on eigenenergies and that the decay rate calculation is highly sensitive to fine details of the wavefunctions.

## Method
The authors solved the three-body Schrodinger equation for muonic molecular ions $^{3,4}\!H\!ed\mu$ using the hyperspherical "surface" functions method, expanding the wavefunction in a basis of channel-type functions (hydrogen-like functions for clustered configurations) and hyperspherical harmonics (for compact three-body configurations). The basis size was adjusted as a function of the hyperradius $\rho$ to maintain numerical stability. Binding energies were obtained from the eigenvalue problem for the lowest effective potential $\varepsilon_2(\rho)$ in a decoupled one-level approximation, and nonradiative decay rates were computed from the matrix element of the channel coupling operator between the bound state and the continuum wavefunction of the $H\!e\mu + H$ channel.

## Context
This work extends prior calculations of muonic molecular ions using the Born-Oppenheimer approximation by Ger (1993) and Kravtsov (1993), and variational calculations by Kino (1993) and Hara (1993). The hyperspherical method avoids ad hoc mass and threshold adjustments required in the Born-Oppenheimer approach, and the systematic basis adjustment with hyperradius improves numerical stability. The results enable more reliable comparison with experimental gamma-ray yields from $H\!ed\mu$ decay (Matsuzaki 1993, Ishida 1993) and motivate further systematic study of formation, rearrangement, and decay processes within a unified theoretical framework.
