# Collective Flow Global Collective Flow in Heavy Ion Reactions from the Beginnings to the Future

> Fluid dynamical models preceded the first heavy ion accelerator experiments, and led to the main trend of this research since then. In recent years fluid dynamical processes became a dominant direction of research in high energy heavy ion reactions. The Quark-gluon Plasma formed in these reactions has low viscosity, which leads to significant fluctuations and turbulent instabilities. One has to study and separate these two effects, but this is not done yet in a systematic way. Here we present a few selected points of the early developments, the most interesting collective flow instabilities, their origins, their possible ways of detection and separation form random fluctuations arising from different origins, among these the most studied is the randomness of the initial configuration in the transverse plane.

# Global Collective Flow in Heavy Ion Reactions from the Beginnings to the Future

## Key claim

The paper argues that collective flow in heavy ion reactions must be systematically separated into a Global Collective (background) component and a Fluctuating component, but this separation has not yet been performed in a systematic way. The authors present a reformulated harmonic expansion method that enables this separation using event-by-event determination of the reaction plane and center-of-mass rapidity, which the abstract glosses over by not specifying the mathematical framework for achieving this separation.

## Method

The authors reformulate the standard azimuthal harmonic expansion by expressing flow coefficients in terms of cosine and sine components relative to the experimentally determined Reaction Plane, rather than using separate Event Plane angles for each harmonic. They then separate Global Collective flow from Fluctuations by exploiting symmetry properties: Global Collective flow coefficients must be even functions of rapidity for even harmonics and odd functions for odd harmonics, while sine terms arise only from fluctuations. The Fluctuating component is obtained by subtracting the symmetrized Global Collective component from the total measured flow.

## Key numbers

- Reynolds number for stability: Re ≤ 1000–2000 for laminar flow
- Shear viscosity at RHIC energies: η ≈ 50–500 MeV/fm²c, giving Re ≈ 10–100 (from studies by Molnar (2008) and Heinz (2008))
- Reynolds number at 50–800 MeV/nucleon energies: Re ≈ 7–8 (from scaling analysis by Bonasera and collaborators (1988))
- Ratio η/s ≈ 1 (range 0.5–5) for both energy regimes
- Lower bound for η/s from Kovtun, Son and Starinets (2005): η/s = 1/4π for certain field theories dual to black branes
- Negative v₁(p_t) observed at low rapidities for p_t < 1.2–1.5 GeV/c (from ATLAS (2012) and ALICE (2012) measurements)

## Context

This review extends the early fluid dynamical predictions of collective flow from Scheid, Müller and Greiner (1974) and the first unambiguous experimental confirmation by Gustafsson et al. (1984) using the Plastic Ball detector at Berkeley. The paper addresses the current challenge of separating Global Collective flow from random fluctuations arising from initial state configurations in the transverse plane, which became critical after the discovery of low viscosity in the Quark-Gluon Plasma. The proposed separation method enables future extraction of QGP properties from flow measurements at RHIC and LHC, and can be applied to two-particle correlation studies and polarization measurements.
