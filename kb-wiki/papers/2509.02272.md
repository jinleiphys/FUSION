# Quantum simulations of Green's functions for small superfluid systems

> An end-to-end strategy for hybrid quantum-classical computations of Green's functions in many-body systems is presented and applied to the pairing model. The scheme makes explicit use of the spectral representation of the Green's function, which entails the calculation of the $N$-body ground state as well as eigenstates and associated energies of the $(N\pm1)$-body neighbors. While the former is accessed via variational techniques, the latter are constructed by means of the quantum subspace expansion method. Different ansatzes for the ground-state wave function, originating from either classical or quantum approaches, are tested and compared to exact calculations. The resulting one-body Green's functions prove to be accurate approximations of the exact one for a large range of parameters, including across the normal-to-superfluid transition. As a byproduct, this approach yields a good description of odd systems provided that the starting even system is well reproduced by the variational ansatz.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a hybrid quantum-classical strategy using the Lehmann representation of Green's functions can produce accurate approximations for small superfluid systems across the normal-to-superfluid transition, provided that the even-N ground state is well reproduced by the variational ansatz and that the quantum subspace expansion method is used to construct odd-neighbor eigenstates.

## Method

The authors implement a three-step hybrid quantum-classical procedure: first, the even-N ground state is approximated using variational quantum eigensolver techniques with either particle-number projected BCS states or symmetry-preserving ansatzes; second, states and energies in the (N+1)- and (N-1)-body sectors are constructed via the quantum subspace expansion method using single-particle creation and annihilation operators as the operator pool; third, the one-body Green's function is built from the Lehmann representation using the computed eigenstates, eigenenergies, and spectroscopic amplitudes. The method is benchmarked on the Richardson pairing model with equidistant doubly-degenerate single-particle levels, using noiseless quantum emulators and compared to exact full configuration interaction calculations on classical computers.

## Key numbers

* For a system of N=8 particles on D=8 doubly-degenerate levels at coupling strength g=0.6 (in units of Delta E), the exact Green's function trace F(omega) is computed via FCI and shown as reference, with a spectral width parameter eta=0.04.
* The full Fock space for 8 particles on 8 doubly-degenerate levels has 2^16 = 65536 states, which reduces to binom(8,4) = 70 states when using good particle number and seniority nu=0 constraints.
* The (N+1)- and (N-1)-body relevant subspaces reduce to sizes (2D - N + 2)*binom(D,(N-2)/2) and (2D - N)*binom(D,N/2) respectively when considering only seniority nu=1 states that contribute to the Green's function.

## Context

This work extends the quantum subspace expansion approach for Green's functions, recently highlighted by Dhadwal et al. (2024), to nuclear-physics-relevant pairing Hamiltonians. It builds upon prior quantum computing benchmarks for the Richardson model using symmetry-breaking and restoration techniques by Lacroix et al. (2020, 2023) and Ruiz Guzman et al. (2021, 2022, 2023, 2024), as well as variational symmetry-preserving ansatzes by Khamoshi et al. (2023) and Zhang et al. (2024). The method enables future applications to more realistic nuclear systems on quantum hardware, particularly for computing spectroscopic information in odd-mass nuclei and for extending self-consistent Green's function approaches to systems beyond classical computational reach.
