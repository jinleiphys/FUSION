# Temperature dependent sound velocity in hydrodynamic equations for relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We analyze the effects of different forms of the sound-velocity function cs(T) on the hydrodynamic evolution of matter formed in the central region of relativistic heavy-ion collisions. At high temperatures (above the critical temperature Tc) the sound velocity is calculated from the recent lattice simulations of QCD, while in the low temperature region it is obtained from the hadron gas model. In the intermediate region we use different interpolations characterized by the values of the sound velocity at the local maximum (at T = 0.4 Tc) and local minimum (at T = Tc). In all considered cases the temperature dependent sound velocity functions yield the entropy density, which is consistent with the lattice QCD simulations at high temperature. Our calculations show that the presence of a distinct minimum of the sound velocity leads to a very long (about 20 fm/c) evolution time of the system, which is not compatible with the recent estimates based on the HBT interferometry. Hence, we conclude that the hydrodynamic description is favored in the case where the cross-over phase transition renders the smooth sound velocity function with a possible shallow minimum at Tc.

## Key claim
The presence of a distinct minimum in the sound velocity function \(c_s(T)\) near the critical temperature leads to a very long hydrodynamic evolution time (about 20 fm/c) that is incompatible with estimates from HBT interferometry, so a smooth sound velocity function with only a possible shallow minimum at \(T_c\) is favored for the hydrodynamic description.

## Method
The author solved relativistic boost-invariant hydrodynamic equations with cylindrical symmetry for central heavy-ion collisions at RHIC, using three different interpolations of the sound velocity function \(c_s(T)\) between lattice QCD results at high temperature and a hadron gas model at low temperature. The three cases differed by the depth of the local minimum at the critical temperature \(T_c = 170\) MeV, while the entropy density at high temperatures was kept identical across cases by adjusting the sound velocity in the intermediate region. The initial temperature profile was set proportional to the nuclear thickness function with a central initial temperature of \(2 T_c\).

## Key numbers
- Critical temperature used: \(T_c = 170\) MeV.
- Sound velocity at \(T_c\) in case I: \(c_s(T_c) = 0.37\); in case II: \(c_s(T_c) = 0.28\) (25% lower than case I); in case III: \(c_s(T_c) = 0.19\) (50% lower than case I).
- Initial central entropy density: \(s_0 = 70.5\ \text{fm}^{-3}\), yielding initial central temperature \(T(t_0, r=0) = 2 T_c\).
- In case I, the center cools to \(0.8 T_c\) after about 15 fm; the time to pass the phase transition is about 10 fm.
- In case II, the central temperature does not drop below \(0.8 T_c\) before 20 fm.
- In case III, the system does not pass the phase transition within the considered evolution time of 20 fm.

## Context
This work extends the studies of Chojnacki (2007) and supplements the analysis by Huovinen (2005) on the impact of different equations of state on particle spectra and elliptic flow. The results contradict the idea that a softest point in the QCD equation of state (as discussed by Hung (1995), Rischke (1995), and Heinz (2005)) would produce long-lived states; instead, the short lifetime indicated by RHIC HBT data favors a smooth sound velocity function. This enables future hydrodynamic models to constrain the equation of state by requiring consistency with observed system lifetimes.
