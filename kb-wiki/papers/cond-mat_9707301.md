# Random Matrix Theories in Quantum Physics: Common Concepts

> We review the development of random-matrix theory (RMT) during the last decade. We emphasize both the theoretical aspects, and the application of the theory to a number of fields. These comprise chaotic and disordered systems, the localization problem, many-body quantum systems, the Calogero-Sutherland model, chiral symmetry breaking in QCD, and quantum gravity in two dimensions. The review is preceded by a brief historical survey of the developments of RMT and of localization theory since their inception. We emphasize the concepts common to the above-mentioned fields as well as the great diversity of RMT. In view of the universality of RMT, we suggest that the current development signals the emergence of a new "statistical mechanics": Stochasticity and general symmetry requirements lead to universal laws not based on dynamical principles.

## Key claim

The paper argues that random-matrix theory (RMT) has undergone an explosive development during the last decade (roughly 1987-1997), extending far beyond its original nuclear-physics domain to encompass chaotic systems, disordered solids, quantum chromodynamics, and quantum gravity, and that this signals the emergence of a new "statistical mechanics" where stochasticity and symmetry requirements produce universal laws not derived from dynamical principles.

## Method

The authors conducted a comprehensive review of theoretical developments in RMT and its applications across multiple fields, including chaotic and disordered systems, many-body quantum systems, the Calogero-Sutherland model, chiral symmetry breaking in QCD, and two-dimensional quantum gravity. They organized the review around common concepts, beginning with a historical survey of RMT and localization theory from 1951 to 1983, then covering formal developments (supersymmetry technique, spectral observables, scattering theory), applications to many-body systems, quantum chaology, disordered systems, and field theory, and concluding with a discussion of universality.

## Key numbers

- The nuclear data ensemble comprises 1726 spacings of levels of the same spin and parity from multiple nuclei, used to test the nearest-neighbor spacing distribution against the GOE prediction
- For s-wave resonances in neutron scattering on 238U near neutron threshold (about 8 MeV above ground state), the average spacing is typically 10 eV
- The three Gaussian ensembles are characterized by the symmetry index β = 1 (GOE, real symmetric matrices), β = 2 (GUE, Hermitian matrices), and β = 4 (GSE, quaternion matrices)
- In the limit N → ∞, the spectrum of the Gaussian ensembles has length 4λ and the average level density follows the semicircle law ρ(E) = (N/πλ) √(1 - (E/(2λ))²)
- The Wigner surmise for the nearest-neighbor spacing distribution has the form p_β(s) = a_β s^β exp(-b_β s²), showing β-dependent level repulsion at small spacings

## Context

This review extends and synthesizes the earlier development of RMT by Wigner (1951), Dyson (1962-1963), and Mehta (1960s), and connects it with localization theory initiated by Anderson (1958) and developed by Mott. The paper covers two key developments around 1983-1984: Efetov's supersymmetry technique (originally for disordered solids) and the Bohigas conjecture linking RMT to spectral fluctuations of classically chaotic quantum systems. The review enables future work by establishing RMT as a unified framework applicable to diverse fields including mesoscopic physics, quantum chaos, QCD, and quantum gravity, and by identifying universality as a central open question.
