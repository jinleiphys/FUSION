# Signatures of Chiral Dynamics in Low Energy Compton Scattering off the Nucleon

> We present a projector formalism which allows to define dynamical polarizabilities of the nucleon from a multipole expansion of the nucleon Compton amplitudes. We give predictions for the energy dependence of these dynamical polarizabilities both from dispersion theory and from leading-one-loop chiral effective field theory. Based on the good agreement between the two theoretical frameworks, we conclude that the energy dependence of the dynamical polarizabilities is dominated by chiral dynamics, except in those multipole channels where the first nucleon resonance Delta(1232) can be excited. Both the dispersion theory framework and a chiral effective field theory with explicit Delta(1232) degrees of freedom lead to a very good description of the available low energy proton Compton data. We discuss the sensitivity of the proton Compton cross section to dynamical polarizabilities of different multipole content and present a fit of the static electric and magnetic dipole polarizabilities from low-energy Compton data up to omega=170 MeV, finding alpha_E=(11.04+-1.36)*10^(-4) fm^3, beta_M =(2.76-+1.36)*10^(-4) fm^3.

## Key claim

The energy dependence of the nucleon's dynamical polarizabilities is dominated by chiral dynamics (pionic fluctuations) except in multipole channels where the Delta(1232) resonance can be excited, based on the good agreement between dispersion theory and leading-one-loop chiral effective field theory predictions.

## Method

The authors developed a projector formalism to define dynamical polarizabilities from a multipole expansion of the nucleon Compton amplitudes, separating structure-dependent (non-pole) contributions from pole contributions (nucleon s- and u-channel, pion t-channel). They computed the energy dependence of these dynamical polarizabilities using two independent theoretical frameworks: subtracted fixed-t dispersion relations (with input from pion-photoproduction multipoles and static polarizabilities from a global fit) and leading-one-loop chiral effective field theory (both Heavy Baryon Chiral Perturbation Theory without explicit Delta and the Small Scale Expansion including explicit Delta(1232) degrees of freedom).

## Key numbers

- Static electric dipole polarizability from fit to low-energy Compton data up to omega = 170 MeV: alpha_E = (11.04 +/- 1.36) x 10^(-4) fm^3
- Static magnetic dipole polarizability from fit to low-energy Compton data up to omega = 170 MeV: beta_M = (2.76 -/+ 1.36) x 10^(-4) fm^3
- Input sum of proton static polarizabilities from global fit: alpha_E^p + beta_M^p = 13.8 x 10^(-4) fm^3
- Input difference of proton static polarizabilities from global fit: alpha_E^p - beta_M^p = 10.5 x 10^(-4) fm^3
- Input backward spin polarizability of proton (pi0-pole subtracted): gamma_pi^p = 10.6 x 10^(-4) fm^4
- Input sum of neutron static polarizabilities: alpha_E^n + beta_M^n = 15.2 x 10^(-4) fm^3
- Input difference of neutron static polarizabilities: alpha_E^n - beta_M^n = 9.8 x 10^(-4) fm^3
- Input backward spin polarizability of neutron: gamma_pi^n = 13.6 x 10^(-4) fm^4
- pi0-pole contribution to backward spin polarizability: gamma_pi^(p, pi0-pole) = -46.7 x 10^(-4) fm^4

## Context

This work extends the theoretical concept of dynamical polarizabilities introduced in Griesshammer and Hemmert (2001) by providing a first analysis of the sensitivity of actual proton Compton cross section data to these quantities. It bridges dispersion theory calculations (following the subtracted fixed-t dispersion relation approach of Drechsel, Pasquini, and Vanderhaeghen (1999)) with chiral effective field theory calculations (from Beane, Kaplan, and McGovern (2003) for HBChiPT and from Hemmert, Holstein, Knochlein, and Scherer (1997-1998) for the Small Scale Expansion with explicit Delta). The work enables future experimental extraction of multipole-specific dynamical polarizabilities beyond the static electric and magnetic dipole polarizabilities, potentially providing a more discriminating test of the internal structure mechanisms of the nucleon.
