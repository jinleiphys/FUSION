# Hadron and light nucleus radii from electron scattering

> Conceptually, radii are amongst the simplest Poincaré-invariant properties that can be associated with hadrons and light nuclei. Accurate values of these quantities are necessary so that one may judge the character of putative solutions to the strong interaction problem within the Standard Model. However, limiting their ability to serve in this role, recent measurements and new analyses of older data have revealed uncertainties and imprecisions in the radii of the proton, pion, kaon, and deuteron. In the context of radius measurement using electron + hadron elastic scattering, the past decade has shown that reliable extraction requires complete elimination of bias associated with practitioner-dependent choices of data fitting functions. Different answers to that challenge have been offered; and this perspective describes the statistical Schlessinger point method (SPM), in unifying applications to proton, pion, kaon, and deuteron radii. Grounded in analytic function theory, independent of assumptions about underlying dynamics, free from practitioner-induced bias, and applicable in the same form to diverse systems and observables, the SPM returns an objective expression of the information contained in any data under consideration. Its robust nature and versatility make it suitable for use in many branches of experiment and theory.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the long-standing "proton radius puzzle", the discrepancy between proton charge radii extracted from electron-proton scattering and from muonic hydrogen Lamb shift measurements, is resolved when the statistical Schlessinger point method (SPM) is applied to the scattering data, provided the method is used with precise, densely-packed data at very low momentum transfers, thereby eliminating systematic bias from practitioner-dependent fitting functions.

## Method

The authors apply the statistical Schlessinger point method (SPM), a continued-fraction interpolation scheme grounded in analytic function theory, to extract charge and magnetic radii from electron+hadron elastic scattering data. For a given dataset, they generate 1000 bootstrap replicas, smooth each replica using an optimal roughness penalty, then randomly select M < N data points (with 6 ≲ M ≲ N/2) to construct thousands of distinct interpolating functions, from which the radius is obtained via the derivative at Q²=0; the final result is the average over all M-values with combined statistical and M-variation uncertainties.

## Key numbers

*   Proton charge radius from PRad 1.1 GeV beam data (SPM): 0.842 ± 0.008_stat fm.
*   Proton charge radius from PRad 2.2 GeV beam data (SPM): 0.824 ± 0.003_stat fm.
*   Combined PRad data (SPM): 0.838 ± 0.005_stat fm.
*   Proton charge radius from A1 low-Q² data (SPM, 0.0038 ≤ Q²/GeV² ≤ 0.014): 0.856 ± 0.014_stat fm.
*   Combined PRad and A1 results (SPM): 0.847 ± 0.008_stat fm.
*   Proton charge radius from muonic hydrogen Lamb shift: 0.84136(39) fm (Pohl 2010, Antognini 1900ns).
*   Proton charge radius from atomic hydrogen 2S→4P transition: 0.8335(95) fm (Beyer 2017).
*   Proton charge radius from atomic hydrogen Lamb shift: 0.833(10) fm (Bezginov 1007).
*   Proton charge radius from 1S→3S and 1S→2S transitions in atomic hydrogen: 0.8482(38) fm (Grinin 1061).
*   Proton charge radius from muonic deuterium: 0.8356(20) fm (Pohl 2016).
*   PDG assessment of proton radius: 0.8409 ± 0.0004 fm.
*   PRad experiment reached Q² = 2.1 × 10⁻⁴ GeV², the deepest reach yet achieved.
*   A1 data stretch to Q² = 3.8 × 10⁻³ GeV², roughly 20 times larger than the smallest PRad value.

## Context

This work extends the application of the statistical Schlessinger point method, previously used in other contexts (Schlessinger 1966, Tripolt 2016, Chen 2018), to unify the extraction of charge radii for the proton, pion, kaon, and deuteron from electron scattering data. The SPM analysis reconciles the conflicting results from the A1 Collaboration (Bernauer 2010) and the PRad experiment (Xiong 2019), showing both are consistent with muonic hydrogen measurements (Pohl 2010, Antognini 1900ns), thereby resolving the "proton radius puzzle" (Gao 2021). The method enables objective, function-form-independent extractions that can be applied to future experiments, such as the proposed deuteron charge radius measurement (JlabDRad).
