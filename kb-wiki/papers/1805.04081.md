# Opacity dependence of elliptic flow in kinetic theory

> The observation of large azimuthal anisotropies $v_n$ in the particle spectra of proton-proton (pp) and proton-nucleus (pA) collisions challenges fluid dynamic interpretations of $v_n$, as it remains unclear how small collision systems can hydrodynamize and to what extent hydrodynamization is needed to build up $v_n$. Here, we study in a simple kinetic theory how the same physics that leads to hydrodynamization in large systems represents itself in small systems. We observe that one third to one half of the elliptic flow signal seen in fully hydrodynamized systems can be built up in collisions that extend over only one mean free path $l_{\rm mfp}$ and that do not hydrodynamize. This is qualitatively in line with observing a sizeable $v_2$ in $pp$ collisions for which other characteristics of soft multi-particle production seem well-described in a free-streaming picture. We further expose a significant system size dependence in the accuracy of hybrid approaches that match kinetic theory to viscous fluid dynamics. The implications of these findings for a reliable extraction of shear viscosity are discussed.

# Opacity dependence of elliptic flow in kinetic theory

## Key claim

Between one third and one half of the elliptic flow signal seen in fully hydrodynamized large collision systems can be built up in collisions that extend over only one mean free path and that do not hydrodynamize, provided the system is characterized by an opacity parameter $\hat\gamma \leq 1-1.5$. This supports the idea that very small systems such as pp or pA collisions may build up a sizeable fraction of $v_2/\epsilon_2$ while still operating close to the free-streaming limit.

## Method

The authors solved a boost-invariant kinetic transport equation with an isotropization-time approximation (ITA) collision kernel, evolving the energy-momentum tensor $T^{\mu\nu}$ from azimuthally anisotropic Gaussian initial conditions. They developed a novel numerical approach solving the transport equation exactly to all orders in opacity $\hat\gamma = R^{3/4}\gamma (\varepsilon_0 \tau_0)^{1/4}$, which measures transverse system size $R$ in units of mean free path. For the matching study, they linearized Israel-Stewart viscous fluid dynamic equations and interfaced them with the pre-hydrodynamic kinetic evolution at a switching time $\tau_s$.

## Key numbers

- For small system sizes to first order in $\hat\gamma$, $v_2/\epsilon_2 = 0.212 \hat{\gamma}$ (the "single-hit" linear response).
- The ideal fluid-dynamic upper limit for $v_2/\epsilon_2$ (initialized at $\tau_s \rightarrow 0$) is 0.51.
- Systems are characterized as not hydrodynamized for $\hat\gamma \leq 1.5$, partially hydrodynamized for $1.5 \leq \hat\gamma \leq 4$, and almost completely hydrodynamized for $\hat\gamma \geq 4$, based on comparison of transport results to the first-order viscous constitutive equation at the center $r=0$.
- For a fixed value $v_2/\epsilon_2 \approx 0.32$, a simulation using matching at $\tau_s = 0.15 R$ would yield $\hat\gamma \approx 2$ while the full transport calculation gives $\hat\gamma \approx 4$, illustrating factor-of-two uncertainties in extracting $\hat\gamma$ (and thus $\eta/s$).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous first-order-in-opacity study (Kurkela 2018) which established that all linear and non-linear structures in $v_n$ arise already at small $\hat\gamma$, with $v_2/\epsilon_2 = 0.212 \hat{\gamma}$. It provides a full all-orders solution bridging the free-streaming and hydrodynamic limits, complementing studies of "hydrodynamization without thermalization" (Heller 2011, Chesler 2010, Kurkela 2015, Keegan 2015). The results enable systematic quantification of system-size-dependent uncertainties in hybrid approaches that match kinetic transport to viscous fluid dynamics, which is essential for reliable extraction of shear viscosity from small collision systems. The ITA is found to reproduce the $T^{\mu\nu}$ evolution of QCD weak coupling effective kinetic theory (Arnold 2002) within approximately 15%.
