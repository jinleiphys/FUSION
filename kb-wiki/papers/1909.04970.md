# Oscillating nuclear electric dipole moments inside atoms

> Interaction with the axion dark matter (DM) field generates an oscillating nuclear electric dipole moment (EDM) with a frequency corresponding to the axion's Compton frequency. Within an atom, an oscillating EDM can drive electric dipole transitions in the electronic shell. In the absence of radiation, and if the axion frequency matches a dipole transition, it can promote the electron into the excited state. The excitation events can be detected, for example, via subsequent uorescence or photoionization. Here we calculate the rates of such transitions. For a single light atom and an axion Compton frequency resonant with a transition energy corresponding to 1 eV, the rate is on the order of 10^(-22) per year, so a macroscopic atomic sample would be needed. A fundamental challenge is discriminating against background processes that may lead to the excitation of the same electric dipole transition. The ways to enhance the signals to potentially observable levels exceeding backgrounds and to search for axions in an extended frequency range are discussed.

## Key claim

Axion dark matter can induce an oscillating nuclear electric dipole moment (EDM) that drives electric-dipole (E1) transitions in atoms when the axion Compton frequency matches an atomic transition frequency, but the rate per atom is astronomically low (on the order of 10^(-22) per year for a single light atom at 1 eV), requiring macroscopic samples and careful background discrimination.

## Method

The authors derived the matrix element for the interaction between atomic electrons and an oscillating nuclear EDM, expressing it in terms of the electric-dipole transition amplitude and the square of the transition frequency. They then calculated the resonant transition probability using standard time-dependent perturbation theory, incorporating the width of the virialized axion frequency distribution, and scaled the result to typical atomic parameters (1 eV transition energy, allowed E1 amplitude of 3 ea, and nuclear EDM of 4×10^(-16) θ e cm).

## Key numbers

- For a single light atom with an axion Compton frequency resonant at 1 eV, the transition rate is on the order of 10^(-22) per year.
- The transition probability scales as 1/Z^2, favoring light atoms such as H, He, and Li.
- The approximate time for one transition is t = (2×10^22 / N) Z^2 (1 eV / ℏω)^3 (3 e a / |⟨2|D_z|1⟩|)^2 (4×10^(-16) θ e cm / d)^2 years, where N is the number of atoms.
- The axion field amplitude corresponds to θ_0 ≈ 4×10^(-18) for the local dark matter density.
- The neutron EDM from the QCD θ-term is d_n = -(2.7 ± 1.2)×10^(-16) θ e cm, and the proton EDM is d_p = (2.1 ± 1.2)×10^(-16) θ e cm.
- The ³He EDM is d(³He) = (-4.2 ± 1.5)×10^(-16) θ e cm.
- The transition probability scales as 1/n^4 for principal quantum number n, and for Rydberg states t ∝ Z^2 n^5.
- The width of the virialized axion frequency distribution is Γ ≈ 10^(-6) ω.

## Context

This work extends the concept of axion-induced oscillating nuclear EDMs, previously discussed by Graham (2013) and Stadnik (2014), from nucleons and nuclei to atomic transitions. It complements the CASPEr experiments (Antoine, 2019; Comagnetometer, 2019) that search for axion-induced spin precession via nuclear magnetic resonance, and the neutron-EDM (nEDM, 2019) and atomic co-magnetometer (Volansky, 2019) re-analyses. The results enable searches for axion dark matter using atomic excitation events detected via fluorescence or photoionization, with potential signal enhancement through interference with a reference electromagnetic field, and tunability of resonances via Zeeman and Stark effects.
