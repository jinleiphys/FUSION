# Temperatures of Fragment Kinetic Energy Spectra

> Multifragmentation reactions without large compression in the initial state (proton-induced reactions, reverse-kinematics, projectile fragmentation) are examined, and it is verified quantitatively that the high temperatures obtained from fragment kinetic energy spectra and lower temperatures obtained from observables such as level population or isotope ratios can be understood in a common framework.

# Temperatures of Fragment Kinetic Energy Spectra

## Key claim
The high apparent temperatures (≈15 MeV) extracted from fragment kinetic energy spectra in multifragmentation reactions without large initial compression can be understood as a consequence of adding Fermi momenta of individual nucleons in the fragment, rather than reflecting the true internal temperature of the system. This reconciles the discrepancy between these high kinetic temperatures and the lower temperatures (≈5–8 MeV) obtained from isotope ratios, level population ratios, and statistical model fits.

## Method
The author applies the single-particle model, previously used by Feshbach (1973), Goldhaber (1974), and Bertsch (1981) for projectile fragmentation, to compute fragment momentum distributions by summing the individual momentum vectors of all nucleons in a fragment. The single-particle momentum distribution is taken as a Fermi-Dirac distribution at internal temperature T_in, and the resulting fragment kinetic energy spectrum is shown via the central limit theorem to be Boltzmann-like with an apparent temperature T_f = σ²/m_N, where σ² is the variance of the single-particle momentum distribution including a recoil correction factor (A−A_f)/(A−1).

## Key numbers
- The apparent fragment kinetic temperature at zero internal temperature is T_f(T_in=0) = (A−A_f)/(A−1) × (2/5)E_F, where E_F is the Fermi energy.
- For a freeze-out density of 0.5 ρ₀ and internal temperature T_in = 6 MeV, the Fermi energy is 24 MeV and the slope constant (ignoring recoil correction) is T_f = 12 MeV.
- The Sommerfeld expansion approximation for T_f(T_in) works to better than 5% up to T_in/E_F = 0.3.
- The dispersion used in numerical calculations is σ² = (2/5)E_F m_N = 0.014 GeV².
- Final state interaction corrections are of order T_in/(A_f T_f), about 5% for carbon fragments.
- Pauli correlations can suppress the dispersion by about 30% for projectile fragmentation of ⁴⁰Ca, as noted by Bertsch (1981).

## Context
This work extends the single-particle model of Goldhaber (1974) and Bertsch (1981), originally applied to projectile fragmentation, to explain the temperature discrepancy in proton-induced and reverse-kinematics multifragmentation reactions. It contradicts the assumption that the high kinetic temperatures reflect the true thermal state of the system, and instead shows they arise from Fermi motion. The results enable a unified interpretation of different temperature observables and suggest that projectile fragmentation may involve a more dilute system at higher internal temperature than conventionally assumed, rather than a cold breakup at nuclear matter density.
