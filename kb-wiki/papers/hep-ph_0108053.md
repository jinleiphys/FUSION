# Unitary and Analytic Models in Particle Physics Phenomenology

> After two decades of a development of the unitary and analytic models of the electromagnetic structure of hadrons and nuclei their main principles are briefly formulated, then a general scheme of their applications to the electromagnetic, weak and strong interaction processes are traced out and finally, some results of their successful applications are reviewed.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the unitary and analytic modeling approach for hadron form factors and partial wave amplitudes, claiming that this phenomenological method can successfully describe electromagnetic, weak, and strong interaction processes despite the breakdown of perturbative QCD at low momentum transfer, but only to the extent that the models depend on unknown parameters evaluated by comparison with experimental data.

## Method

The authors construct form factors and partial wave amplitudes as analytic functions on a four-sheeted Riemann surface by applying a nonlinear transformation to pole expressions, which generates square-root branch points on the positive real axis and pairs of complex conjugate poles on unphysical sheets corresponding to resonances. The electromagnetic form factor of any pseudoscalar meson is represented as a sum of terms for resonances below and above an effective threshold, with an asymptotic term determined by the number of constituent quarks. For baryons and light nuclei with more than two constituent quarks, the form factors are first written as a sum of pole expressions, then transformed to achieve correct normalization and asymptotic behavior before applying the nonlinear transformation.

## Context

This work extends the unitary and analytic modeling approach developed over two decades by the authors and their collaborators for describing the electromagnetic structure of hadrons and nuclei. The approach contrasts with perturbative QCD methods, which fail at low momentum transfer where the running coupling constant α_s becomes large, and with non-perturbative QCD methods that are not well developed for form factor calculations. The models enable predictions for weak processes via the conserved-vector-current (CVC) hypothesis, following the original postulation by Zeldovich (1958) and Feynman and Gell-Mann (1958), and allow extraction of resonance parameters and strange-quark contributions to nucleon structure, with the latter confirmed by SAMPLE and HAPPEX experimental collaborations.
