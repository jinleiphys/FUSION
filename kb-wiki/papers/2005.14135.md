# Astrophysical Implications of Neutron Star Inspiral and Coalescence

> The first inspiral of two neutron stars observed in gravitational waves was remarkably close, allowing the kind of simultaneous gravitational wave and electromagnetic observation that had not been expected for several years. Their merger, followed by a gamma-ray burst and a kilonova, was observed across the spectral bands of electromagnetic telescopes. These GW and electromagnetic observations have led to dramatic advances in understanding short gamma-ray bursts; determining the origin of the heaviest elements; and determining the maximum mass of neutron stars. From the imprint of tides on the gravitational waveforms and from observations of X-ray binaries, one can extract the radius and deformability of inspiraling neutron stars. Together, the radius, maximum mass, and causality constrain the neutron-star equation of state, and future constraints can come from observations of post-merger oscillations. We selectively review these results, filling in some of the physics with derivations and estimates.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that the combined gravitational-wave and electromagnetic observations of GW170817 have provided dramatic advances in understanding short gamma-ray bursts, the origin of heavy elements, and neutron star maximum mass, but the constraints on the equation of state are not yet stringent enough to definitively rule out exotic matter such as quark cores or hyperons.

## Method

The authors selectively review the astrophysical implications of neutron star inspiral and coalescence, filling in underlying physics with derivations and estimates. They discuss the merger outcome dependence on total mass relative to threshold masses, the magnetorotational instability mechanism for magnetic field growth, the relativistic jet model for gamma-ray bursts, and the r-process nucleosynthesis pathway in merger ejecta. The paper synthesizes results from numerical simulations, gravitational-wave data analysis from GW170817, and electromagnetic observations across the spectrum.

## Key numbers

- The total mass of GW190425 is 3.4^{+0.3}_{-0.1} solar masses, with a possibility that one component has mass as large as 2.5 solar masses
- The dynamical timescale for prompt collapse is 0.12 ms times (R/17 km)^{3/2} (M/2.7 solar masses)^{-1/2}
- The available rotational energy from differential rotation is about 10^{53} erg for a remnant with moment of inertia 4 x 10^{45} g cm^2 and angular velocity 7000 s^{-1}
- The gamma-ray burst associated with GW170817 had luminosity less than 4 x 10^{47} erg s^{-1}, orders of magnitude below typical short gamma-ray bursts
- The observed apparent velocity of the late-time jet implies Lorentz factor Gamma approximately 4, consistent with an initial Gamma greater than or approximately 10
- The threshold for pair creation opacity gives a lower limit on the Lorentz factor Gamma greater than 9 for typical parameters
- The r-process abundance peaks at atomic mass numbers approximately 80, 130, and 195
- To form elements from lanthanides through uranium, an electron fraction less than 0.25 is needed
- The LIGO/VIRGO collaboration measured the masses and radii of the two neutron stars in GW170817 to within about 2 km

## Context

This review extends the work of Shibata and Hotokezaka (2019) on merger and mass ejection, Metzger (2019) on kilonovae, Berger (2014) and Meszaros (2019) on gamma-ray bursts, and Lattimer (2017) on neutron-star equation of state constraints. The paper enables future constraints on the neutron-star equation of state from observations of post-merger oscillations and from additional gravitational-wave events, while noting that the single unambiguous NS-NS inspiral (GW170817) and one additional event (GW190425) provide limited statistics for determining merger rates and r-process contribution.
