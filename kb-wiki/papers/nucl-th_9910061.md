# Finite size effects and the order of a phase transition in fragmenting nuclear systems

> We discuss the implications of finite size effects on the determination of the order of a phase transition which may occur in infinite systems. We introduce a specific model to which we apply different tests. They are aimed to characterise the smoothed transition observed in a finite system. We show that the microcanonical ensemble may be a useful framework for the determination of the nature of such transitions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the order of a phase transition observed in finite nuclear systems may differ from that in the thermodynamic limit, and that constraints such as fixed particle number (canonical or microcanonical ensemble) can suppress the signatures of a first-order transition, producing a smooth crossover even when the infinite system would exhibit a first-order transition.

## Method

The authors study the Ising model with fixed magnetization (IMFM) in 2d and 3d, which is equivalent to a lattice gas model (LGM) with strictly fixed particle number. They perform Monte Carlo simulations in both canonical and microcanonical ensembles for systems of linear sizes L=10 to L=48, and compare caloric curves, specific heats, and configuration topologies. They also test the q-state Potts model in 2d (q=4 and q=5) with and without constraints on spin populations to demonstrate the effect of constraints on transition order.

## Key numbers

- Linear dimensions of simulated IMFM systems range from L=10 to L=48 in 3d.
- For the 3d IMFM at density ρ=0.3, caloric curves are shown for L=10, 24, and 48; the slope change between L=24 and L=48 is very small, suggesting the asymptotic limit is close.
- The 2d Potts model test uses a 16×16 system; q=5 (first order in thermodynamic limit) shows the characteristic "S"-shaped microcanonical caloric curve, while q=4 (second order) shows a monotonous rise.
- For the 3d IMFM with L=10 at both ρ=0.3 and ρ=0.5, canonical and microcanonical caloric curves are indistinguishable, with no sign of multivalued energy for fixed temperature.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous finite-size scaling analysis of the IMFM (Carmona, 1999), which suggested a second-order transition at all densities, contradicting the common understanding that the LGM has a first-order transition line except at ρ=0.5. The paper addresses the experimental debate between Pochodzalla (1995) (plateau in caloric curve interpreted as first-order liquid-gas transition) and Trautmann (1999) (monotonous curve with steep rise interpreted as second-order). The results support the possibility that finite-size effects and ensemble constraints can mask first-order signatures, and suggest that microcanonical measurements of negative specific heat (as claimed by D'Agostino, 1999) would be a definitive test for first-order behavior in nuclear fragmentation.
