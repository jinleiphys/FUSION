# Comparison of numerical solutions for Q^2 evolution equations

> Q^2 evolution equations are important not only for describing hadron reactions in accelerator experiments but also for investigating ultrahigh-energy cosmic rays. The standard ones are called DGLAP evolution equations, which are integrodifferential equations. There are methods for solving the Q^2 evolution equations for parton-distribution and fragmentation functions. Because the equations cannot be solved analytically, various methods have been developed for the numerical solution. We compare brute-force, Laguerre-polynomial, and Mellin-transformation methods particularly by focusing on the numerical accuracy and computational efficiency. An efficient solution could be used, for example, in the studies of a top-down scenario for the ultrahigh-energy cosmic rays.

## Key claim

The paper compares the numerical accuracy and computational efficiency of three methods for solving DGLAP evolution equations (brute-force, Laguerre-polynomial, and Mellin-transformation), finding that while the Laguerre method is fastest, it suffers from accuracy problems at small and large x unless a large number of polynomials is used, and the Mellin method requires careful choice of integration parameters for similar x-regions.

## Method

The authors implemented three numerical methods for solving the DGLAP integrodifferential equations for parton distribution functions (PDFs) and fragmentation functions (FFs): a brute-force method using finite differences on a grid of t and x steps, a Laguerre-polynomial expansion method where functions are expanded in Laguerre polynomials of -ln x, and a Mellin-transformation method where the equations become simple multiplications in moment space followed by numerical Mellin inversion via Gauss-Legendre quadrature. They evolved the MRST02 PDFs from Q^2=1 GeV^2 to Q^2=100 GeV^2 and the KKP fragmentation functions from Q^2=2 GeV^2 to Q^2=100 GeV^2, comparing ratios of evolved distributions to a brute-force standard with N_t=200 and N_x=4000.

## Key numbers

- Brute-force method with N_t=200 and N_x=1000 achieves evolution accuracy better than 2% (stated as standard reference)
- Laguerre method with N_Lag=30 takes 0.044 seconds for nonsinglet PDF evolution at 500 x-points, roughly 100 times faster than brute-force with N_t=200, N_x=1000 (5.986 seconds)
- Mellin method with N_GL=20 takes 0.464 seconds for nonsinglet PDF evolution at 500 x-points, several times faster than brute-force
- Brute-force with N_t=200 and N_x=4000 takes 95.634 seconds for nonsinglet PDF evolution at 500 x-points
- Mellin inversion uses integration contour with constants c=1.1 and phi=135 degrees, with z_max=16.5x+3.5 for x region 10^-5 < x < 0.99
- Laguerre method with N_Lag=5, 10, 20, and 30 shows oscillatory behavior at small x for nonsinglet PDF evolution
- Mellin method with N_GL=6, 10, 20, and 50 shows inaccuracy at small and large x for small N_GL values

## Context

This work extends the comparison of DGLAP solution methods previously studied in Toldra (2002) for cosmic ray applications, and the brute-force method developed in Kumano (1998). The Laguerre method was used in Toldra (2002) for cosmic ray studies, while the Mellin method is used in global analyses such as GRSV for polarized PDFs. The paper enables researchers to choose appropriate numerical methods for evolving PDFs and FFs to ultrahigh energies (beyond TeV) needed for studying top-down scenarios of ultrahigh-energy cosmic rays, where accurate evolution at small x is critical.
