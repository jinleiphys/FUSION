# Dual pairing of symmetry groups and dynamical groups in physics

> This article reviews many manifestations and applications of dual representations of pairs of groups, primarily in atomic and nuclear physics. Examples are given to show how such paired representations are powerful aids in understanding the dynamics associated with shell-model coupling schemes and in identifying the physical situations for which a given scheme is most appropriate. In particular, they suggest model Hamiltonians that are diagonal in the various coupling schemes. The dual pairing of group representations has been applied profitably in mathematics to the study of invariant theory. We show that parallel applications to the theory of symmetry and dynamical groups in physics are equally valuable. In particular, the pairing of the representations of a discrete group with those of a continuous Lie group or those of a compact Lie with those of a non-compact Lie group makes it possible to infer many properties of difficult groups from those of simpler groups. This review starts with the representations of the symmetric and unitary groups, which are used extensively in the many-particle quantum mechanics of bosonic and fermionic systems. It gives a summary of the many solutions and computational techniques for solving problems that arise in applications of symmetry methods in physics and which result from the famous Schur-Weyl duality theorem for the pairing of these representations. It continues to examine many chains of symmetry groups and dual chains of dynamical groups associated with the several coupling schemes in atomic and nuclear shell models and the valuable insights and applications that result from this examination.

## Key claim

This review argues that dual representations of pairs of groups, such as a discrete group paired with a continuous Lie group, or a compact Lie group with a non-compact Lie group, are powerful tools for understanding shell-model coupling schemes and for constructing model Hamiltonians that are diagonal in those schemes, but the utility is conditional on the specific physical situation for which a given coupling scheme is most appropriate.

## Method

The paper reviews the mathematical framework of dual group representations, starting with Schur-Weyl duality between symmetric and unitary groups, then extending to unitary-unitary duality and to dual pairs involving compact and non-compact Lie groups. It applies these techniques to derive coupling schemes, branching rules, and tensor product decompositions for atomic and nuclear shell models, including the Interacting Boson Model, the Bohr model, and the microscopic symplectic model, and shows how these schemes diagonalize specific classes of Hamiltonians.

## Key numbers

- The two-level BCS model Hamiltonian is given as $\hat H = \sum_{k=1}^2 \varepsilon_k \hat S_0^k - \sum_{i,k=1}^2 g_{ik}\hat S^i_+ \hat S^k_-$, with two coupling schemes: one diagonalizing Hamiltonians of the form $\hat H_1 = \sum_{k=1}^2 [ \varepsilon_k \hat S_0^k - g_{k}\hat S^k_+ \hat S^k_- ]$ (weak-coupling limit, normal phase) and the other diagonalizing $\hat H_2 = \varepsilon \sum_{k=1}^2 \hat S_0^k - g \sum_{i,k=1}^2 \hat S^i_+ \hat S^k_-$ (strong-coupling limit, superconducting phase).
- The spherical harmonic oscillator basis wave functions are given as $\psi_{nlm}(r,\theta,\varphi) = R_{nl}(r) Y_{lm}(\theta,\varphi)$, with the subgroup chain U(3) $\supset$ SO(3) $\supset$ SO(2) labeled by quantum numbers $n$, $l$, and $m$.
- The SU(1,1) group is constructed from the SO(3)-invariant operators $\hat r^2$, $\hat p^2$, and $\hat{\bf r} \cdot \hat{\bf p} + \hat{\bf p} \cdot \hat{\bf r}$, and its irreps on the Hilbert space of a particle in three-space are uniquely defined by the SO(3) angular momentum quantum number $l$.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Schur (1901) and Weyl (1928) on Schur-Weyl duality, and the subsequent developments by Littlewood, King, Wybourne, and others on character theory and branching rules for Lie groups. It also builds on the application of dual pairs to nuclear physics, including Wigner's U(4) super-multiplet group for classifying shell-model states, and the Interacting Boson Model with its three dynamical symmetries. The review enables the systematic construction of coupling schemes that diagonalize classes of Hamiltonians, and it identifies quasi-dynamical symmetries (as reviewed by Rosensteel and Rowe, 2005, and Cejnar and Jolie, 2010) as a mechanism for understanding why low-energy eigenstates often reside predominantly in one of the possible phases even when Hamiltonians contain mixing terms.
