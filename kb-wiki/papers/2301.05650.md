# Contribution of Nuclear Excitation Electromagnetic Form Factors in ${\rm ^{12}C}$ and ${\rm ^{16}O}$ to the Coulomb Sum Rule

> We report on empirical parameterizations of longitudinal and transverse nuclear excitation electromagnetic form factors in ${\rm ^{12}C}$ and ${\rm ^{16}O}$. We extract the contribution of nuclear excitations to the Normalized Inelastic Coulomb Sum Rule (\csr) as a function of momentum transfer $\bf q$ and find that it is significant (0.29$\pm$0.030 at $\bf q$= 0.22 GeV). The total contributions of nuclear excitations to $S_L({\bf q})$ in ${\rm ^{12}C}$ and ${\rm ^{16}O}$ are found to be equal within the uncertainties. Since the cross sections for nuclear excitations are significant, the radiative tails from nuclear excitations should be included in precise calculations of radiative corrections to quasielastic electron scattering at low $\bf q$ and deep-inelastic electron scattering at large energy transfers $ν$. The parameterizations also serve as a benchmark in testing theoretical modeling of cross sections for excitation of nuclear states in electron and neutrino interactions on nuclear targets at low energies.

## Key claim

The paper provides empirical parameterizations of longitudinal and transverse nuclear excitation electromagnetic form factors for \(^{12}\text{C}\) and \(^{16}\text{O}\), and finds that the contribution of these nuclear excitations to the Normalized Inelastic Coulomb Sum Rule is significant (0.29 ± 0.030 at \(\mathbf{q} = 0.22\) GeV), with the total contributions for both nuclei being equal within uncertainties.

## Method

The authors fit measured longitudinal and transverse form factors for individual nuclear excited states in \(^{12}\text{C}\) (excitation energies below 50 MeV) using Gaussian-based parameterizations as functions of effective momentum transfer \(\mathbf{q}_{\text{eff}}\) or \(\mathbf{q}_{\text{eff}}^2\), and for \(^{16}\text{O}\) they parameterize only longitudinal form factors for states below 12.5 MeV using data from Buti-86 and extract form factors for higher excitation energy regions (12.5–20, 20–35, and 35–55 MeV) from Hotta74 data after subtracting quasielastic contributions using a superscaling model with Pauli suppression. The contributions of these nuclear excitations to the Coulomb Sum Rule \(S_L(\mathbf{q})\) are then computed by summing the squared form factors weighted by the proton number.

## Key numbers

- Contribution of nuclear excitations to the Normalized Inelastic Coulomb Sum Rule: \(0.29 \pm 0.030\) at \(\mathbf{q} = 0.22\) GeV for both \(^{12}\text{C}\) and \(^{16}\text{O}\).
- For the 4.44 MeV \(2^+\) state in \(^{12}\text{C}\), the longitudinal form factor squared is parameterized with \(N_1 = 1.41 \times 10^{-2}\), \(C_1 = 1.125\) fm\(^{-2}\), \(\sigma_1 = 1.71\) fm\(^{-2}\) (using \(\mathbf{q}_{\text{eff}}^2\) in fm\(^{-2}\)).
- For the 9.64 MeV \(3^-\) state in \(^{12}\text{C}\), the longitudinal form factor squared is parameterized with \(N_1 = 5.00 \times 10^{-3}\), \(C_1 = 1.46\) fm\(^{-2}\), \(\sigma_1 = 1.70\) fm\(^{-2}\) (using \(\mathbf{q}_{\text{eff}}^2\) in fm\(^{-2}\)).
- For the 7.65 MeV \(0^+\) state in \(^{12}\text{C}\), the longitudinal form factor squared is parameterized with \(N_1 = 2.8 \times 10^{-3}\), \(C_1 = 0.93\) fm\(^{-1}\), \(\sigma_1 = 0.42\) fm\(^{-1}\) (using \(\mathbf{q}_{\text{eff}}\) in fm\(^{-1}\)).
- For the 6.1299 MeV \(3_1^-\) state in \(^{16}\text{O}\), the longitudinal form factor squared is parameterized with \(N_1 = 1.60\), \(C_1 = 1.00\) fm\(^{-1}\), \(\sigma_1 = 0.45\) fm\(^{-1}\) (using \(\mathbf{q}_{\text{eff}}\) in fm\(^{-1}\)).

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier investigation of the quasielastic contribution to the Coulomb Sum Rule (Bodek and Christy, 2022) by now including the nuclear excitation contributions, which were previously omitted. The empirical parameterizations enable precise calculations of radiative corrections to quasielastic electron scattering at low \(\mathbf{q}\) and deep-inelastic electron scattering at large energy transfers \(\nu\), and serve as a benchmark for testing theoretical models of electron and neutrino scattering on nuclear targets at low energies, such as those by Lovato (2016), Pandey (2015), and microscopic coupled-channel approaches.
