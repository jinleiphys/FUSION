# Lithium Diffusion in the Post-Recombination Universe and Spatial Variation of [Li/H]

> The observed amount of lithium for low metallicity population II stars (known as the Spite plateau) is a factor of $\sim 3-5$ lower than the predictions of the standard cosmology. Since the observations are limited to the local Universe (halo stars, globular clusters and satellites of the Milky Way) it is possible that certain physical processes may have led to the spatial separation of lithium and local reduction of [Li/H]. We study the question of lithium diffusion after the cosmological recombination in sub-Jeans dark matter haloes, taking into account that more than 95% of lithium remains in the singly-ionized state at all times. Large scattering cross sections on the rest of the ionized gas leads to strong coupling of lithium to protons and its initial direction of diffusion coincides with that of H$^+$. In the rest frame of the neutral gas this leads to the diffusion of H$^+$ and Li$^+$ out of overdensities with the trend of reducing [Li/H] in the minima of gravitational wells relative to the primordial value. We quantify this process and argue that, with certain qualifications, it may have played a significant role in creating local lithium deficiency within the primordial dark matter haloes, comparable to those observed along the Spite plateau.

## Key claim

The paper argues that lithium, which remains singly ionized (Li+) after cosmological recombination, diffuses out of gravitational potential wells (against the direction of gravity) because it is tightly coupled to protons via large Rutherford scattering cross sections, potentially creating local lithium deficiencies comparable to the observed Spite plateau, but only under specific conditions such as sub-Jeans dark matter haloes and with the qualification that the effect is small on average.

## Method

The authors solve the Euler equations for multiple cosmological fluid species (H, He, e, p, Li) in the post-recombination universe, treating the diffusion velocities in the regime of small density perturbations within sub-Jeans dark matter haloes. They compute the transport cross sections for neutral-neutral scattering (He-H), ion-neutral scattering (p-H, Li-H, Li-He) using polarization potentials, and ion-ion scattering (p-Li) using the Rutherford formula with a Coulomb logarithm, then evaluate the sign and magnitude of the lithium diffusion velocity relative to hydrogen.

## Key numbers

- The SBBN prediction for primordial lithium is [7Li/H]SBBN = (5.07+0.71-0.62) x 10^-10, while the Spite plateau observational value is (1.23+0.34-0.16) x 10^-10, a factor of 3-5 lower.
- More than 95% of lithium remains in the singly-ionized state (Li+) at all times after recombination.
- The transport cross section for He-H scattering is approximately 100 a_B^2, where a_B is the Bohr radius.
- The Coulomb logarithm for p-Li scattering is ln Lambda ~ 40, weakly dependent on temperature.
- The estimated lithium depletion from diffusion is approximately 1 x 10^-2 x (Delta/200) x (1+z_f/20)^(-3/2), where Delta is the halo overdensity relative to the mean matter density.
- The separatrix for the direction of lithium diffusion (Eq. bracket) is negative for cosmological X_e(T) at all redshifts between recombination and reionization, with the bracket scaling as 5/7 - 300 x (X_e/10^-3) x (ln Lambda/40) x (0.01 eV/T_baryon)^(3/2) < 0.

## Context

This work extends the sparse literature on primordial element diffusion in the early universe, specifically contradicting the earlier linear-regime analysis of Mathews and Lan (which assumed neutral lithium and found lithium accumulates in gravitational wells, worsening the lithium problem). The paper builds on the ionization state calculation of Switzer and Hirata (2005) showing lithium remains ionized, and uses transport cross sections from Dalgarno and from Mason and McDaniel. The mechanism offers a possible explanation for the observed deterioration of the Spite plateau at lowest metallicities (Sbordone et al.) and suggests that future hydrodynamic simulations of early star formation including diffusion effects are needed to confirm the magnitude of lithium depletion.
