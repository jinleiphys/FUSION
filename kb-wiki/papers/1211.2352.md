# Charged Ising Model of Neutron Star Matter

> Background: The inner crust of a neutron star is believed to consist of Coulomb-frustrated complex structures known as "nuclear pasta" that display interesting and unique low-energy dynamics. Purpose: To elucidate the structure and composition of the neutron-star crust as a function of temperature, density, and proton fraction. Methods: A new lattice-gas model, the "Charged-Ising Model" (CIM), is introduced to simulate the behavior of neutron-star matter. Preliminary Monte Carlo simulations on 30^3 lattices are performed for a variety of temperatures, densities, and proton fractions. Results: Results are obtained for the heat capacity, pair-correlation function, and static structure factor for a variety of conditions appropriate to the inner stellar crust. Conclusions: Although relatively simple, the CIM captures the essence of Coulomb frustration that is required to simulate the subtle dynamics of the inner stellar crust. Moreover, the computationally demanding long-range Coulomb interactions have been pre-computed at the appropriate lattice sites prior to the start of the simulation resulting in enormous computational gains. This work demonstrates the feasibility of future CIM simulations involving a large number of particles as a function of density, temperature, and proton fraction.

## Key claim

The Charged Ising Model (CIM), a lattice-gas model that pre-computes long-range Coulomb interactions via Ewald summation, captures the essence of Coulomb frustration in neutron-star crust matter while enabling enormous computational gains, but the authors note that the results are preliminary and limited to 30^3 lattices, and that the existence of pasta phases at very low proton fractions (as suggested by chemical equilibrium) remains uncertain.

## Method

The authors introduce a lattice-gas model (CIM) where nucleons occupy discrete sites of a cubic lattice with lattice spacing a = 1.842 fm, interacting via a short-range nearest-neighbor isospin-dependent nuclear potential and a long-range Coulomb potential between protons treated exactly via Ewald summation. Classical Metropolis Monte Carlo simulations are performed on 30^3 lattices in the canonical ensemble (fixed baryon number and proton fraction) using exchange moves that swap occupancies between two randomly selected sites, with a slow cooling schedule from high temperature to the target temperature to avoid trapping in local minima.

## Key numbers

- Lattice spacing: a = 1.842 fm, corresponding to nuclear matter saturation density rho_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}
- Symmetric nuclear matter binding energy at saturation: -16.5 MeV per nucleon (by construction)
- Pure neutron matter energy at saturation: +15.5 MeV per nucleon (by construction)
- Symmetry energy at saturation density: 32 MeV
- Two-body interaction strengths: v_pn = v_np = -5.500 MeV (attractive), v_pp = v_nn = +5.167 MeV (repulsive)
- Decorrelation times at filling fraction 0.2 and proton fraction 0.3: tau = 7,218 Monte Carlo steps at T = 10 MeV, tau = 5,550 steps at T = 15 MeV
- Filling fraction for main simulations: rho/rho_0 = 0.1875 (approximately 5,000 nucleons on a 30^3 lattice)
- Minimum distance between same-species nucleons on the lattice: r_min = sqrt(2) * a = 2.605 fm
- Pasta structures dissolve at temperatures T greater than or approximately 4-5 MeV for density rho/rho_0 = 0.2 and proton fraction x_p = 0.3

## Context

This work extends earlier fixed-temperature approaches to neutron-star crust matter, particularly the simulations of Horowitz et al. (2004) and the zero-temperature mean-field calculations of Ravenhall et al. (1983), by introducing a computationally efficient lattice-gas model that can systematically study thermal properties. The CIM improves on earlier Ising-model approaches by including the isospin degree of freedom for asymmetric matter and using exact Ewald summation for Coulomb interactions. The results enable future large-scale simulations of the phase diagram as a function of temperature, density, and proton fraction, and may help resolve the question raised by Piekarewicz et al. (2011) of whether pasta formation is possible at the very low proton fractions expected from chemical equilibrium in the inner crust.
