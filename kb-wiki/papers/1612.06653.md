# Monopole transition strength function of ${}^{12}$C in three-$α$ model

> The energy level structure of ${}^{12}$C nucleus at a few MeV above the three-$α$ threshold is still unsatisfactory known. For instance, most microscopic calculations predicted that there exist one $0^+$-state in this energy region besides the well known Hoyle state, while some experimental and theoretical studies show the existing of two $0^+$-states. In this paper, I will take a three-$α$-boson (3$α$) model for bound and continuum states in ${}^{12}$C, and study a transition process from the ${}^{12}$C($0_1^+$) ground state to 3$α$ $0^+$ continuum states by the electric monopole ($E0$) operator. The strength distribution of the process will be calculated as a function of $3α$ energy using the Faddeev three-body theory. The Hamiltonian for the $3α$ system consists of two- and three-$α$ potentials, and some three-$α$ potentials with different range parameters will be examined. Results of the strength function show a double-peaked bump at low energy region, which can be considered as two $0^+$-states. The peak at higher energy may originate from a 3$α$ resonant state. However, it is unlikely that the peak at the lower energy is related to a resonant state, which suggests that it may be due to so called "ghost anomaly". Distributions of decaying particles are also calculated.

# Monopole transition strength function of ${}^{12}$C in three-α model

## Key claim

The E0 transition strength function for ${}^{12}$C calculated in a three-α boson model shows a double-peaked bump at low energy (1–5 MeV above the 3α threshold), which can be interpreted as two 0⁺-states; however, the lower-energy peak (around 1.5 MeV) is unlikely to be a resonant state and may instead arise from a "ghost anomaly" associated with the Hoyle state, while only the higher-energy peak (around 4 MeV) appears consistent with a genuine 3α resonant state.

## Method

The author employs a three-α-boson model using the Faddeev three-body formalism in coordinate space, solving for the wave function of the transition from the ${}^{12}$C(0₁⁺) ground state to 3α 0⁺ continuum states induced by the electric monopole (E0) operator. The Hamiltonian includes the Ali-Bodmer model D α-α potential plus a three-α potential of Gaussian form, with the strength parameter adjusted to reproduce the Hoyle state energy for five different range parameters (b = 2.8, 3.0, 3.45, 3.9, and 4.6 fm). The transition strength function S_E0(E) is calculated as a function of 3α energy E, and the volume integral of the squared wave function is used to assess resonance character.

## Key numbers

- The Hoyle state (${}^{12}$C(0₂⁺)) lies at excitation energy E_x = 7.65 MeV, or E = 0.38 MeV measured from the 3α threshold (7.27 MeV).
- For the Δ(4.6) model, the peak value of S_E0(E) is 26 fm⁴/MeV at E = 3.9 MeV.
- Experimental values from ${}^{12}$C(α,α') reaction at E_α = 386 MeV give two 0⁺-states at E = 1.77(9) MeV with width Γ = 1.45(18) MeV and E = 3.29(6) MeV with Γ = 1.42(8) MeV; the ratio of peak heights (higher to lower) is about 1.1.
- The experimental B(E0;Hoyle) from inelastic electron scattering is 29.9 ± 1.0 fm⁴; the Δ(3.9) model gives 28.9 fm⁴, consistent with this value.
- The B(E0;Hoyle) values for the five models range from 11.8 fm⁴ (Δ(2.8)) to 34.6 fm⁴ (Δ(4.6)).
- The integrated E0 strength in the region (0.8, 6.0) MeV ranges from 3.4 fm⁴ (Δ(2.8)) to 17.5 fm⁴ (Δ(4.6)).
- The 2α resonant state (${}^{8}$Be(0₁⁺)) has E_{r,2α} = 92 keV and Γ_{2α} = 5.57(25) eV.
- The Hoyle state has a very narrow width of 8.3(1.0) eV.
- The sequential decay ratio exceeds 99% for the Hoyle state and remains high up to about E = 2 MeV, then reduces to about 50% at higher energies.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous three-α calculations (Ishikawa 2013, 2014) that studied continuum states up to E = 0.6 MeV for the triple-α reaction rate and Hoyle state decay modes. The results address a longstanding controversy: most microscopic calculations (Uegaki 1979, Kamimura 1980, 2007, Chernykh 2007) predicted one 0⁺-state above the Hoyle state, while the orthogonal-condition model with complex scaling (Kurokawa 2005, 2007, Ohtsubo 2013) and recent microscopic calculations (Funaki 2015, Zhou 2016) found two 0⁺-states. The present calculation supports the existence of two structures but suggests the lower one may be a non-resonant ghost anomaly rather than a true resonance. The results enable future experimental work to distinguish between resonant and ghost-anomaly interpretations by measuring 3α decay distributions, and they highlight the need for better-constrained three-α potentials.
