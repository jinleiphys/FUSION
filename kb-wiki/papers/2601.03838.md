# Thermal Evolution of Shape Coexistence in Mo and Ru Isotopes

> The temperature-driven shape dynamics of isotopic chains of Mo and Ru elements and their impact on decay modes have been investigated in a statistical theoretical framework with macroscopic-microscopic apporach. These isotopes located at the key points in r-process path are known for the rapid structural changes, shape instabilities and shape coexistence that impact the nuclear processes, decay modes and lifetimes. At high temperatures that may exist in stars or in various nuclear reaction processes, these nuclei undergo a variety of shape and deformation changes due to thermal shell quenching effects influencing the decay energies (Q value), and eventually life-time have been studied in detail. Our findings provide insight into the observed shift in the deformation, shapes and coexisting states due to the diminishing nuclear shell effects in hot nuclei, revealing that the structural changes influence the decay processes and significantly in the astrophysically relevant Mo-Ru region especially around A = 100.

# Thermal Evolution of Shape Coexistence in Mo and Ru Isotopes

## Key claim
The temperature-driven quenching of shell effects causes shape-coexisting nuclei in the Mo-Ru region to transition from deformed to spherical configurations at a critical temperature (Tc), which varies with nuclear structure and is highest (around 2.0 MeV) for mid-shell nuclei near N=60 and lowest (around 0.7 MeV) at shell closures N=50 and 82; this thermal shape evolution modifies beta-decay Q-values by up to several hundred keV depending on which shape minima are involved in the decay.

## Method
The authors combined the triaxially deformed Nilsson-Strutinsky macroscopic-microscopic method for ground-state properties with a statistical model based on the grand canonical partition function to compute thermodynamic expectation values at finite temperature. Free energy surfaces F(beta2, gamma, T) were minimized over axial deformation beta2 (0.0 to 0.4) and triaxial angle gamma (-180° to 0°) for 80-124Mo and 84-126Ru isotopes at temperatures from 0.6 to 3.0 MeV. Beta-decay Q-values were calculated for four possible decay paths connecting ground and excited shape minima of parent and daughter nuclei.

## Key numbers
- Critical temperature Tc = 2.0 MeV for 104Mo (maximum value, mid-shell nucleus near N=60)
- Critical temperature Tc = 1.8 MeV for 108Ru (maximum value around N=60)
- Critical temperature Tc = 0.7 MeV at neutron shell closures N=50 and 82 for both isotopic chains
- 92Mo becomes spherical at T = 0.6 MeV
- 108Mo shows oblate minimum at beta2=0.24, gamma=-170° coexisting with triaxial minimum at beta2=0.3, gamma=-140° at T=0 MeV
- 84Ru shows oblate minimum at beta2=0.20, gamma=-180° with shallow triaxial minima at T=0 MeV
- Q-beta for 108Mo Gp→Gd transition = 5.2125 MeV at T=0.8 MeV (experimental: 5.174 MeV)
- Q-beta for 112Mo Gp→Gd transition = 7.8624 MeV at T=0.6 MeV (experimental: 7.78 MeV)
- Q-beta for 112Ru Sp→Gd transition = 4.4125 MeV at T=0.8 MeV (experimental: 4.1 MeV)
- Q-beta+ for 84Ru Gp→Gd transition = 12.5855 MeV at T=0.6 MeV (no experimental value available)
- Inverse level density parameter K = A/a shows maxima near shell closures N=50, 82; values of a ≈ A/8 to A/11
- K becomes approximately constant at 8 MeV for all nuclei at high temperatures around 2 MeV

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous ground-state study of shape coexistence in Mo and Ru isotopes (Aggarwal et al., 2024) by incorporating finite-temperature effects relevant to stellar environments where temperatures can reach up to 2 MeV. The statistical theory of hot nuclei follows the approach initiated by Rajasekaran et al., and the critical temperature concept builds on earlier work by Arumugam et al. (2005). The results enable improved modeling of beta-decay lifetimes in the r-process path around A=100, where rapid shape changes and shape coexistence significantly impact nuclear stability and decay modes. The level density parameters agree with values reported by Chankova et al. (2007) and Rahmatinejad et al. (2020).
