# Neutron tunneling: A new mechanism to power explosive phenomena in neutron stars, magnetars, and neutron star mergers

> Neutron tunneling between neutron-rich nuclei in inhomogeneous dense matter encountered in neutron star crusts can release enormous energy on a short-timescale to power explosive phenomena in neutron stars. In this work we clarify aspects of this process that can occur in the outer regions of neutron stars when oscillations or cataclysmic events increase the ambient density. We use a time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov formalism to determine the rate of neutron diffusion and find that large amounts of energy can be released rapidly. The role of nuclear binding, the two-body interaction and pairing, on the neutron diffusion times is investigated. We consider a one-dimensional quantum diffusion model and extend our analysis to study the impact of diffusion in three-dimensions. We find that these novel neutron transfer reactions can generate energy at the amount of $\simeq 10^{40}-10^{44}$ ergs under suitable conditions.

## Key claim

Neutron tunneling between neutron-rich impurities in neutron star crusts can release enormous energy (10^40-10^44 ergs) on short timescales, but only under the specific condition that cataclysmic events such as crust fractures, tidal forces, or magnetic field reshuffling first create inhomogeneous neutron distributions away from energetic equilibrium.

## Method

The authors used a time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (TDHFB) formalism to simulate neutron diffusion in a one-dimensional system of Woods-Saxon potential wells representing neutron-rich impurities separated by variable distances. They prepared initial wavefunctions for systems of 20, 40, 80, and 160 neutrons with controlled separation energies, then solved the TDHFB equations with a fourth-order Runge-Kutta method after switching off the confining potential and allowing neutrons to tunnel through barriers between neighboring potential wells. The neutron tunneling rate was calculated from the time derivative of the number of neutrons remaining in the initial impurity region.

## Key numbers

- Energy release from neutron tunneling reactions: approximately 10^40 to 10^44 ergs under suitable conditions
- Neutron separation energies for the model systems: 1.55 MeV (N=20), 0.96 MeV (N=40), 0.40 MeV (N=80), and 0.25 MeV (N=160)
- Average tunneling rate for N=160 with D=20 fm separation: 5.16 x 10^-6 c/fm from TDHFB calculation, compared to 9.51 x 10^-6 c/fm from Gamow model
- Average tunneling rate for N=160 with D=100 fm separation: 4.77 x 10^-24 c/fm from TDHFB, compared to 2.93 x 10^-22 c/fm from Gamow model
- For larger impurities with 500 neutrons at 100 fm separation: average tunneling rate approximately 7.23 x 10^-22 c/fm
- For larger impurities with 1000 neutrons at 100 fm separation: average tunneling rate approximately 5.14 x 10^-20 c/fm
- Neutron-neutron interaction increases tunneling rate by a factor of 4-5 when switched off, indicating the interaction suppresses diffusion
- Pairing correlations change tunneling rates by less than 3% for N=160 and less than 5% for N=20

## Context

This work extends the concept of neutron tunneling between neutron-rich nuclei, previously suggested for white dwarfs by Saakyan (1972) and for neutron star crusts by Chugunov (2018, 2019), to explain explosive phenomena in magnetars and fast radio bursts. The authors build on the prediction by PhysRevLett.112.112504 that modifications of local neutron densities above the neutron drip density (approximately 4 x 10^11 g/cm^3) can create attractive interactions between nuclei via interstitial neutrons. The mechanism enables a new explanation for short gamma/X-ray bursts in soft gamma-ray repeaters and anomalous X-ray pulsars, as well as fast radio bursts, by proposing that neutron tunneling following crustal deformation from compression waves or magnetic field evolution can rapidly release energy that seeds high-energy electromagnetic emission.
