# Convergence of the $ppp$ correlation function within the hyperspherical adiabatic basis

> The computation of the three-particle correlation function involving three hadrons started just recently after the first publications of ALICE measurements. Key elements to be considered are the correct description of the asymptotics, antisymmetrization issues and, in most cases, the treatment of the Coulomb interaction. In the case of the $ppp$ correlation function, a first analysis was done where the hyperspherical adiabatic method was used to determine the $ppp$ wave function at different energies. Although the asymptotic behavior, antisymmetrization issues and the treatment of the Coulomb interaction were discussed in detail, the convergence properties of the adiabatic basis were studied at low energies around the formation of the correlation peak determined mainly by the $J^π=1/2^-$ and $3/2^-$ three-body states. Since many and very precise data have been taken or are planned to be measured at energies beyond the peak, we present an analysis of the convergence characteristics of the basis as the energy of the process increases. We show that in order to describe correctly the correlation tail it is necessary to consider three-body states up to $J^π=21/2^-$ whereas higher states can be considered as free. Once those states are incorporated solving the associate dynamical equations, the agreement with the experimental data is found to be excellent.

## Key claim
The hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method, previously shown to converge for the $ppp$ correlation function at low energies around the correlation peak, requires inclusion of three-body states up to $J^\pi=21/2^-$ (corresponding to grand-angular quantum number $K_0=7$) to correctly describe the correlation tail at higher momenta, whereas states above this threshold can be treated as free.

## Method
The three-body scattering wave function was computed using the hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method, where the wave function is expanded in a basis of adiabatic eigenfunctions obtained from the hyperangular part of the Schrodinger equation. The calculation was split into a computed part ($\Psi_s^\mathrm{comp}$) containing radial wave functions for channels with grand-angular quantum numbers $K \leq K_0$, and a free part ($\Psi_s^\mathrm{free}$) for $K > K_0$ using analytic Bessel or Coulomb functions. The Argonne $v_{18}$ proton-proton potential was used, with the Coulomb interaction averaged over hyperangles to yield a $\rho$-dependent form $V_\mathrm{Coul}(\rho)=\frac{16}{\sqrt{2}\pi}\frac{e^2}{\rho}$.

## Key numbers
- Convergence required three-body states up to $J^\pi=21/2^-$; higher states contributed negligibly (variation of order $10^{-4}$ for large $Q_3$)
- For $K_0=7$, the worst-case states ($5/2^-$ and $7/2^-$) required solving 23 coupled differential equations, yielding $23^2=529$ radial wave functions, with about 2500 terms in the hyperspherical harmonic expansion
- The jump from $K_0=2$ to $K_0=5$ produced significant changes in the correlation function for $Q_3$ values larger than about 200 MeV/$c$; further increase to $K_0=7$ gave small corrections
- Source size $\rho_0$ was varied between 2.2 fm and 2.8 fm; the proton source radius was $R_M = (1.24 \pm 0.04)$ fm, giving $\rho_0 = (2.5 \pm 0.1)$ fm
- A $K_\mathrm{max}$ value of about 130 was needed for convergence in the hyperspherical harmonic expansion of the basis functions

## Context
This work extends the initial analysis of the $ppp$ correlation function by Kievsky et al. (2024), which used $K_0=2$ and focused on the low-energy peak dominated by $J^\pi=1/2^-$ and $3/2^-$ states. The present study shows that higher partial waves are necessary to describe the correlation tail at momenta beyond the peak, resolving a discrepancy where earlier calculations approached unity from below while experimental data from the ALICE Collaboration approached from above. This enables accurate theoretical description of three-proton correlation functions at higher energies, where precise ALICE data are available or planned, and demonstrates the efficiency of the adiabatic method compared to direct hyperspherical harmonic expansions that would require solving thousands of coupled equations.
