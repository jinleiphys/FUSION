# Electron-positron vacuum instability in strong electric fields. Relativistic semiclassical approach

> Instability of electron-positron vacuum in strong electric fields is studied. First, falling to the Coulomb center is discussed at $Z>137/2$ for a spinless boson and at $Z>137$ for electron. Then, focus is concentrated on description of deep electron levels and spontaneous positron production in the field of a finite-size nucleus with the charge $Z>Z_{\rm cr}\simeq 170$. Next, these effects are studied in application to the low-energy heavy-ion collisions. Then, we consider phenomenon of "electron condensation" on levels of upper continuum crossed the boundary of the lower continuum $ε=-m$ in the field of a supercharged nucleus with $Z\gg Z_{\rm cr}$. Finally, attention is focused on many-particle problems of polarization of the QED vacuum and electron condensation at ultra-short distances from a source of charge. We argue for a principal difference of cases, when the size of the source is larger than the pole size $r_{\rm pole}$, at which the dielectric permittivity of the vacuum reaches zero, and smaller $r_{\rm pole}$. Some arguments are presented in favor of the logical consistency of QED. All problems are considered within the same relativistic semiclassical approach.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the electron-positron vacuum instability in strong electric fields, including spontaneous positron production and electron condensation, can be consistently described within a single relativistic semiclassical approach, and that this approach provides arguments for the logical consistency of QED, particularly regarding the "zero-charge" problem, provided one distinguishes between source sizes larger or smaller than the pole radius \(r_{\rm pole}\) where the dielectric permittivity reaches zero.

## Method

The author applies a relativistic semiclassical approximation to the Dirac equation (and the Klein-Gordon-Fock equation for bosons) transformed into an effective Schrodinger form, using the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization rule and the imaginary-time method for tunneling probabilities. For supercritical nuclei, the method yields analytic expressions for deep electron level energies, critical charges, and positron production probabilities; for supercharged nuclei with \(Z\gg Z_{\rm cr}\), the many-particle problem of electron condensation is treated via a relativistic Thomas-Fermi method. The approach is also extended to the two-center problem in low-energy heavy-ion collisions and to vacuum polarization at ultra-short distances from a charge source.

## Key numbers

* Critical charge for a spinless boson in the Coulomb field of a point-like nucleus: \(Z_{\rm cr}=1/(2e^2)\simeq 68.5\).
* Critical charge for an electron in the field of a point-like nucleus: \(Z>137\) (falling to the center); for a finite-size nucleus: \(Z_{\rm cr}\simeq 170\) (spontaneous positron production).
* Critical charge range for spontaneous positron production from a finite-size nucleus: \(Z_{\rm cr}=170-173\), depending on charge distribution and \(Z/A\) ratio.
* Schwinger critical field strength for electron-positron pair production: \(E_0=m^2\simeq 1.3\times 10^{16}\) V/cm.
* Typical distance characterizing the vacuum \(K\)-shell electron in a supercritical ion: \(\sim 1/(3m)\gg R_{\rm nucl}\).
* For a uniform static electric field, the pair production probability is \(W\sim e^{-\pi E_0/E}\) with \(E_0=m^2\).

## Context

This review extends the semiclassical treatment of supercritical atoms developed by Migdal, Voskresensky, Popov, and collaborators in the 1970s (Migdal 1976, 1977; Eletskii 1977; Mur 1978; Popov 1978, 1979), and contrasts with numerical approaches such as those of Pieper and Greiner (1969) and more recent work by Popov (2020). It addresses the unresolved experimental status of spontaneous positron production in heavy-ion collisions (GSI experiments 1993-1995 found no line structures) and anticipates renewed experimental searches at upcoming facilities in Germany, Russia, and China. The semiclassical treatment of vacuum polarization and electron condensation at ultra-short distances is presented as a way to argue for the logical consistency of QED, in contrast to the "zero-charge" problem raised by Landau and Pomeranchuk (1955) and Fradkin (1955).
