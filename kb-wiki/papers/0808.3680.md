# Two effects relevant for the study of astrophysical reaction rates: gamma transitions in capture reactions and Coulomb suppression of the stellar enhancement

> Nucleosynthesis processes involve reactions on several thousand nuclei, both close to and far off stability. The preparation of reaction rates to be used in astrophysical investigations requires experimental and theoretical input. In this context, two interesting aspects are discussed: (i) the relevant gamma transition energies in astrophysical capture reactions, and (ii) the newly discovered Coulomb suppression of the stellar enhancement factor. The latter makes a number of reactions with negative Q value more favorable for experimental investigation than their inverse reactions, contrary to common belief.

## Key claim

The paper argues that, contrary to common belief, measuring astrophysical reactions in the direction of negative Q value can sometimes yield results closer to stellar values than measuring the forward (positive Q) direction, due to a newly identified Coulomb suppression effect on the stellar enhancement factor.

## Method

The author uses the statistical Hauser-Feshbach model to calculate stellar enhancement factors f = r*/r^lab across a large range of reactions involving light particles (nucleons, alpha particles) or photons on targets between the proton- and neutron-driplines. The analysis examines the dependence of the stellar enhancement factor on reaction Q value, Coulomb barrier height (determined by nuclear charge Z), and spin selection rules, comparing forward and reverse reaction directions.

## Key numbers

- For neutron capture at 60 keV projectile energy, the maximal contribution to gamma transitions originates from gamma energies of 2-4 MeV, quite independent of target and reaction type
- For the reaction 85Rb(p,n)85Sr, the Q value is -1.847 MeV, providing a specific example where the reverse direction is more favorable for experimental investigation
- More than 1200 cases are found where 1 ≃ f_rev < f_forw across the range of reactions studied

## Context

This work extends the understanding of stellar enhancement factors previously discussed in Rauscher (2003) and addresses the common assumption that measuring forward (positive Q) reactions always gives results closer to stellar values. The Coulomb suppression effect, also discussed in Kiss (2008), enables experimentalists to identify cases where measuring reactions with negative Q values is more favorable, which is particularly relevant for the study of astrophysical reaction rates in nucleosynthesis networks as described by Cowan (1991), Schatz (1998), and Arnould and Goriely (2003).
