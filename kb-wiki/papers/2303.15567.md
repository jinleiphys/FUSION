# Wormhole Model for Neon-20

> A quantum mechanical model for the Neon-20 nucleus is developed that allows for the splitting of a bipyramidal structure of five alpha-partices into an alpha-particle and an Oxygen-16 nucleus. The geometry of the configuration space is assumed to be a 3-dimensional spatial wormhole, and on the wormhole background there is an attractive short-range potential. This leads to a radial Schrödinger equation of the Heun form, which simplifies for threshold bound states to an associated Legendre equation that has explicit solutions. The energies of the true bound states for all spin/parities are numerically calculated, and match those of the well-established $K^π=0^+$, $K^π=0^-$, and certain higher rotational bands of Neon-20.

# Wormhole Model for Neon-20

## Key claim

A quantum mechanical model using a 3-dimensional spatial wormhole as the configuration space for the relative motion of an alpha-particle and an Oxygen-16 cluster can reproduce the observed rotational bands of Neon-20, provided the dimensionless parameter m is fixed to 9 and the energy threshold is calibrated to 15.19 MeV. The model claims that the bipyramidal ground-state configuration of five alpha-particles corresponds to the wormhole throat, and that the observed threshold states near 15-16 MeV are the highest bound states in each rotational band, all at the same energy.

## Method

The authors construct a Lagrangian on an Ellis-Bronnikov wormhole metric with radial coordinate r running from -∞ to ∞ and a 2-sphere throat of radius a. They impose a Z₂ identification to account for alpha-particle indistinguishability, reducing the configuration space to a half-wormhole. The quantum Hamiltonian includes the Laplace-Beltrami operator on the wormhole plus an attractive short-range potential proportional to the Ricci scalar. After separation of variables, the radial Schrödinger equation becomes a confluent Heun equation, which simplifies to an associated Legendre equation at threshold energy (ε=0). The authors fix m=9 (where m² is proportional to the potential strength) and solve the bound state spectrum numerically using a shooting and bisection method, then calibrate the energy scale by fitting to 24 experimentally identified states from the TUNL tables.

## Key numbers

- Wormhole throat radius a = 5.42 fm, derived from the calibration with ℏ²/2μa² = 0.222 MeV, using ℏ = 197.3 MeV fm and reduced mass μ = 2982 MeV
- Dimensionless parameter m fixed to 9 (integer value, originally fitted near 9.01)
- Energy conversion factor ℏ²/2μa² = 0.222 MeV from the best fit to 24 identified states
- Threshold energy at 15.19 MeV (68.5 × 0.222 MeV)
- Ground state band energies (MeV): experiment [0, 1.63, 4.25, 8.78, 15.87] vs model [0, 1.28, 4.25, 8.90, 15.19]
- 1-phonon band energies (MeV): experiment [5.79, 7.16, 10.26, 15.37] vs model [5.04, 6.97, 10.39, 15.19]
- 2-phonon band energies (MeV): experiment [8.7, 9.0, 10.8, 15.2] vs model [8.36, 9.38, 11.71, 15.19]
- Rotational constants B_n (MeV): B_0 = 0.213, B_1 = 0.190, B_2 = 0.170, B_3 = 0.146, B_4 = 0.120
- Cluster separation at bipyramid (r=0): R = 3.65 fm, matching the calculation by Zhou et al.
- Ground state wavefunctions extend from x=0 to approximately x=0.7, corresponding to R between 3.65 fm and approximately 5.3 fm

## Context

This work extends the old idea that Neon-20's ground state is a bipyramid of five alpha-particles (Wheeler, Wefelmeier, and Harvey-Town), and provides a curved-space alternative to conventional Euclidean configuration space models that require cutting out a central ball or introducing singular potentials. The model is an anharmonic extension of the rovibrational analysis by Bijker and Iachello (2021), who incorporated all modes of small oscillation of the bipyramid; the present model has fewer adjustable parameters and can predict ratios of rotational constants. The wormhole geometry was first outlined in an earlier paper by the same authors (Manton and Dunajski, "Emerging" reference) but not analyzed in detail there. The work enables future analysis of scattering states and alpha/Oxygen-16 fusion, and suggests that spatial wormholes could model other nuclei that split asymmetrically into a pair of clusters.
