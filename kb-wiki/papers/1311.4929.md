# Relativistic mean field plus exact pairing approach to open shell nuclei

> Background: Pairing correlations play a critical role in determining numerous properties of open-shell nuclei. Traditionally, they are included in a mean-field description of atomic nuclei through the approximate Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer or Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov formalism. Purpose: We propose a new hybrid ''relativistic-mean-field-plus-pairing'' approach in which pairing is treated exactly so the number of particles is conserved. To verify the reliability of the formalism, we apply it to the study of both ground-state properties and isoscalar monopole excitations of the Tin isotopes. Methods: Accurately-calibrated relativistic mean-field models supplemented by an exact treatment of pairing correlations are used to compute ground-state observables along the isotopic chain in Tin. In turn, ground-state densities are used as input to the calculation of giant monopole resonances through a constrained-relativistic approach. Results: We compute a variety of ground-state observables sensitive to pairing correlations as well as the evolution of giant monopole energies along the isotopic chain in Tin. Whereas ground-state properties are consistent with experiment, we find that pairing correlations have a minor effect on the giant monopole energies. Conclusions: A new mean-field-plus-pairing approach is introduced to compute properties of open-shell nuclei. The formalism provides an efficient and powerful alternative to the computation of both ground-state properties and monopole energies of open-shell nuclei. We find ground-state properties to be well reproduced in this approach. However, as many have concluded before us, we find that pairing correlations are unlikely to provide an answer to the question of ''why is Tin so soft?''

## Key claim

Pairing correlations have a minor effect on the giant monopole resonance energies of Tin isotopes, and are unlikely to explain the observed softening of the isoscalar giant monopole resonance in these nuclei, despite improving ground-state properties.

## Method

The authors combine an accurately-calibrated relativistic mean field (RMF) model with an exact pairing (EP) treatment that conserves particle number, using single-particle energies from the RMF as input to the EP algorithm. The EP problem is solved by diagonalizing the pairing Hamiltonian in a seniority basis, yielding fractional occupancies that are fed back into the RMF calculation until self-consistency is achieved. Ground-state densities from the converged RMF+EP calculation are then used as input to a constrained-RMF approach to compute the centroid energies of the isoscalar giant monopole resonance.

## Key numbers

* The incompressibility coefficient of symmetric nuclear matter is 230 MeV for FSUGold and 271 MeV for NL3.
* The slope of the symmetry energy L is 61 MeV for FSUGold and 118 MeV for NL3.
* The energy required to break a neutron pair in Tin isotopes is about 3 MeV, as inferred from the odd-even staggering of the one-neutron separation energy.
* For the stable A=112-124 Tin isotopes, the predicted one-neutron separation energies agree with experiment, with deviations of about 1 MeV at the two ends of the isotopic chain.

## Context

This work extends the exact pairing approach of Volya (2001) by combining it with relativistic mean-field models (FSUGold and NL3) to study open-shell nuclei, addressing the particle-number violation inherent in BCS and Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov formalisms. The results confirm earlier findings by Li (2008), Khan (2009, 2010), and Vesely (2012) that pairing correlations do not resolve the "why is Tin so soft?" puzzle regarding the giant monopole resonance, and the approach enables future studies of ground-state properties and collective excitations in other open-shell nuclei without relying on number-projection techniques.
