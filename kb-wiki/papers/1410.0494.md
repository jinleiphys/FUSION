# Internal structure of exotic hadrons by high-energy exclusive reactions

> We propose to use high-energy exclusive reactions for probing internal structure of exotic hadron candidates. First, the constituent counting rule of perturbative QCD can be used for finding internal configurations of an exotic hadron candidate. It is because the number of constituents ($n$), which participate in the exclusive reaction, is found by the scaling behavior of the cross section $dσ/dt \propto 1/s^{n-2}$ at large momentum transfer, where $s$ is the center-of-mass energy squared. As an example, we show that the internal structure of $Λ\, (1405)$ should be found, for example, by the reaction $π^- + p \to K^0 + Λ(1405)$. Second, the internal structure of exotic hadron candidates should be investigated by hadron tomography with generalized parton distributions (GPDs) and generalized distribution amplitudes (GDAs) in exclusive reactions. Exotic nature should be reflected in the GPDs which contain two factors, longitudinal parton distributions as indicated by the constituent counting rule and transverse form factors as suggested by the hadron size. The GDAs should be investigated by the two-photon process $γ^* γ\to h\bar h$, for example $h=f_0$ or $a_0$, in electron-positron annihilation. Since the GDAs contain information on a time-like form factor, exotic nature should be found by studying the $h\bar h$ invariant mass dependence of the cross section. The internal structure of exotic hadron candidates should be clarified by the exclusive reactions at facilities such as J-PARC and KEKB.

## Key claim

High-energy exclusive reactions, specifically the constituent counting rule and hadron tomography via generalized parton distributions (GPDs) and generalized distribution amplitudes (GDAs), can reveal whether exotic hadron candidates such as Lambda(1405), f0(980), and a0(980) are ordinary three-quark states or multiquark configurations, but the method relies on the assumption that perturbative QCD scaling sets in at accessible energies (e.g., sqrt(s) = 2 GeV for Lambda(1405) production).

## Method

The authors propose two complementary approaches. First, the constituent counting rule predicts that the differential cross section for a large-angle exclusive reaction scales as dσ/dt ∝ 1/s^(n-2), where n is the total number of valence constituents; measuring the scaling exponent in reactions such as π⁻ + p → K⁰ + Λ(1405) can distinguish between a three-quark (n=10) and a five-quark (n=12) configuration for the Lambda(1405). Second, hadron tomography uses GPDs (measured in virtual Compton scattering) and GDAs (measured in the two-photon process γ*γ → h hbar) to extract both longitudinal parton distributions and transverse form factors, with the GDA cross section as a function of the hadron-pair invariant mass W² being sensitive to the constituent number and size of the exotic hadron.

## Key numbers

- For the reaction π⁻ + p → K⁰ + Λ, the measured scaling exponent is n = 10.1 ± 0.6 at high energies, consistent with the constituent counting rule prediction n = 2+3+2+3 = 10 for ordinary baryon production.
- The perturbative region for the constituent counting rule is assumed to start at sqrt(s) = 2 GeV for the Λ(1405) production reaction.
- For the reaction γ + p → π⁺ + n, the cross section multiplied by s⁷ becomes nearly constant above sqrt(s) = 2.5 GeV, indicating the transition to quark-gluon degrees of freedom.
- The GDA form factor for the cross section eγ → e h hbar is parameterized as F_h(q)(W²) = 1 / [1 + (W² - 4m_h²)/Λ²]^(n-1), with n=2 for ordinary qqbar mesons and n=4 for tetraquark hadrons, evaluated at Q² = 10 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the constituent counting rule, previously validated for ordinary hadrons in experiments at BNL and lepton facilities (e.g., for γ + p → π⁺ + n), to exotic hadron candidates like Λ(1405), f0(980), and a0(980). It also proposes using GPDs and GDAs, tools already applied to nucleon tomography for understanding spin and orbital angular momentum, to probe exotic hadron structure, noting that f0(980) and a0(980) have been suggested as tetraquark or K Kbar molecules based on mass hierarchy and decay-width contradictions with the naive quark model (e.g., the strong-decay width f0 → 2π overestimates data). The approach enables future experiments at J-PARC and KEKB to determine whether these candidates are compact three-quark states or diffuse multiquark/molecular configurations.
