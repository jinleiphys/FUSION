# Solving Einstein equations using deep learning

> Einstein field equations are notoriously challenging to solve due to their complex mathematical form, with few analytical solutions available in the absence of highly symmetric systems or ideal matter distribution. However, accurate solutions are crucial, particularly in systems with strong gravitational field such as black holes or neutron stars. In this work, we use neural networks and auto differentiation to solve the Einstein field equations numerically inspired by the idea of physics-informed neural networks (PINNs). By utilizing these techniques, we successfully obtain the Schwarzschild metric and the charged Schwarzschild metric given the energy-momentum tensor of matter. This innovative method could open up a different way for solving space-time coupled Einstein field equations and become an integral part of numerical relativity.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) with automatic differentiation can numerically solve the Einstein field equations to obtain the Schwarzschild and charged Schwarzschild metrics, but only for static, spherically symmetric vacuum or charged cases, and the method has not yet been extended to time-dependent or space-time coupled geometries.

## Method

The metric tensor components \(g_{00}\) and \(g_{11}\) are each represented by separate deep neural networks with radial coordinate \(r\) as input, using a physically constrained functional form that encodes the weak-field boundary condition at infinity. The residuals of the Einstein field equations (the squared difference between the left-hand side and the energy-momentum tensor) are computed via automatic differentiation and used as the loss function for training with mini-batch stochastic gradient descent (Adam optimizer). To improve accuracy, the radial domain \((10, 300)\) is divided into 3 sub-domains for the Schwarzschild case and 4 sub-domains for the charged Schwarzschild case, with separate neural networks per sub-domain (Distributed Physics Informed Neural Network, DPINN).

## Key numbers

- For the Schwarzschild metric (mass \(M = 2\)), the maximum \(L_2\) error between the network prediction and the analytical solution is approximately \(10^{-3}\) near the gravitational source, decreasing to about \(10^{-5}\) at \(r \rightarrow 300\).
- For the charged Schwarzschild metric (mass \(M = 2\), charge \(Q = 2\)), the maximum error is also approximately \(10^{-3}\).
- The neural networks use 5 hidden layers; the number of hidden neurons per layer is 256 for \(r \in (10, 30)\), 128 for \(r \in (30, 80)\), 64 for \(r \in (80, 300)\) in the Schwarzschild case, and an additional network with 32 neurons for \(r \in (150, 300)\) in the charged case.
- Training uses 1024 residual points per sub-domain, with batch size 8 and 10 epochs; the program runs in less than one hour on a modern laptop.

## Context

This work extends the physics-informed neural network (PINN) approach, previously applied to partial differential equations (Raissi et al., 2019) and many-body Schrödinger equations (Han et al., 2019), to the Einstein field equations. It differs from prior deep learning studies of black hole metrics: Yan (2020) learned metrics from shear viscosity using supervised data, and Luna (2022) used PINNs to learn the Kerr metric from the Teukolsky equation rather than directly from the Einstein field equations. The method enables future applications to time-dependent problems and coupled metric-matter systems such as neutron star structure, where it could be combined with the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation solver to extract the nuclear equation of state (Zhou, 2023; Boehnlein, 2021).
