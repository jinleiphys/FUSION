# Thermodynamics of small superconductors with fixed particle number

> The Variation After Projection approach is applied for the first time to the pairing hamiltonian to describe the thermodynamics of small systems with fixed particle number. The minimization of the free energy is made by a direct diagonalization of the entropy. The Variation After Projection applied at finite temperature provides a perfect reproduction of the exact canonical properties of odd or even systems from very low to high temperature.

## Key claim

The Variation After Projection (VAP) approach, applied for the first time to the pairing Hamiltonian at finite temperature, provides a perfect reproduction of exact canonical properties for both odd and even systems from very low to high temperature, with none of the limitations (such as sharp phase transitions or breakdown at low temperature) seen in other mean-field-based theories.

## Method

The authors minimize the Helmholtz free energy for a projected density operator by varying both the quasiparticle amplitudes (u_k, v_k) and energies (E_k) using a sequential quadratic programming method. The entropy is computed exactly at each iteration by diagonalizing the statistical operator exp(-βĥ)/Z in the Fock space of all many-body configurations with fixed particle number N, using a bit representation of states and a QR algorithm for diagonalization. The method is applied to the Richardson (pairing) Hamiltonian with Ω=10 doubly-folded equidistant levels and pairing strength G=0.4Δε, for systems of N=10 and N=11 particles.

## Key numbers

- Pairing strength G = 0.4 Δε, where Δε is the level spacing unit; system has Ω=10 doubly-folded equidistant levels.
- For N=10 particles, FT-VAP reproduces exact energy, entropy, heat capacity, and average gap from very low temperature up to T=10 (in units of Δε).
- The spin susceptibility χ (normalized by the bulk high-temperature value χ_B = 2 μ_B²/Δε) for both N=10 and N=11 systems is perfectly reproduced by FT-VAP compared to exact results.
- FT-BCS shows a sharp superfluid-to-normal phase transition, while FT-VAP matches the smooth exact behavior; FT-BCS+PAV (Projection After Variation) does even worse than FT-BCS except at very small temperature.

## Context

This work extends the projection method proposed by Esebbag et al. (1993), which had never been practically applied due to its complexity, to finite-temperature thermodynamics of small superconducting systems. It contrasts with prior approaches such as finite-temperature BCS (which shows sharp phase transitions), the Modified BCS theory of Dinh et al. (2003), the Lipkin-Nogami approach at finite temperature (Dinh et al., 2008), and the static-path approximation (Rossignoli et al., 1994; Rossignoli and Ring, 1997) which breaks down at very low temperature. The FT-VAP method enables exact canonical description of small systems with fixed particle number, and the authors suggest it provides a natural extension of FT-BCS or FT-HFB theory for use in nuclear physics within the Energy Density Functional framework at finite temperature.
