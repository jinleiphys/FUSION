# The measurement and interpretation of superallowed 0+ to 0+ nuclear beta decay

> Measurements of the decay strength of superallowed 0+ to 0+ nuclear beta transitions shed light on the fundamental properties of weak interactions. Because of their impact, such measurements were first reported 60 years ago in the early 1950s and have continued unabated ever since, always taking advantage of improvements in experimental techniques to achieve ever higher precision. The results helped first to shape the Electroweak Standard Model but more recently have evolved into sensitively testing that model's predictions. Today they provide the most demanding test of vector-current conservation and of the unitarity of the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa matrix. Here, we review the experimental and theoretical methods that have been, and are being, used to characterize superallowed 0+ to 0+ beta transitions and to extract fundamentally important parameters from their analysis.

# The measurement and interpretation of superallowed 0+ to 0+ nuclear beta decay

## Key claim

Superallowed 0+ to 0+ nuclear beta transitions provide the most precise determination of the CKM matrix element V_ud and the most demanding test of CKM unitarity, currently yielding a unitarity sum of 1.00008±0.00056 that agrees with the Standard Model; however, this precision is limited by small radiative and isospin-symmetry-breaking corrections of order 1% or less, whose uncertainties must be controlled to extract V_ud to better than a part in a thousand.

## Method

The authors review the experimental determination of ft values for superallowed 0+ to 0+ beta transitions from three measured quantities: the total transition energy Q_EC, the half-life t_1/2 of the parent state, and the branching ratio R for the transition. Q_EC values are obtained either from nuclear reactions such as (p,n), (p,gamma) combined with (n,gamma), and (3He,t) reactions, or from on-line Penning trap mass measurements that directly compare cyclotron frequencies of parent and daughter ions. Half-lives are measured using 4pi gas proportional counters with sources produced via on-line isotope separators or magnetic recoil spectrometers to ensure purity. The experimental ft values are then corrected for transition-dependent radiative corrections (delta_R' and delta_NS) and isospin-symmetry-breaking corrections (delta_C) to obtain corrected Ft values, from which the vector coupling constant G_V and ultimately V_ud are extracted.

## Key numbers

- The current unitarity sum of the three top-row CKM matrix elements is 1.00008±0.00056, agreeing with unitarity at the ±0.06% level
- The weak-interaction constant from muon decay is G_F/(hbar c)^3 = (1.1663787 ± 0.0000006) × 10^{-5} GeV^{-2}
- The constant K/(hbar c)^6 = 8120.2787(11) × 10^{-10} GeV^{-4}s appears in the corrected Ft value formula
- Ten superallowed transitions (parents: 14O, 26mAl, 34Cl, 38mK, 42Sc, 46V, 50Mn, 54Co, 62Ga, 74Rb) have ft values known to 0.1% relative precision or better; three more (10C, 22Mg, 34Ar) are known to <0.3%
- The most precise Penning-trap Q_EC measurement achieved relative precision of 7×10^{-6}, corresponding to ±40 eV on the 6044.22 keV Q_EC value for 38mK decay
- The 1960 measurement of 14O gave an ft value of 3060±13 s, standing 1.4 standard deviations from the currently accepted value of 3042.3±2.7 s
- The 1953 measurement of 14O gave ft = 3300±750 s and for 10C gave ft = 5900±2400 s

## Context

This review by Hardy and Towner (2013) updates their earlier survey (Hardy and Towner, 2009) and summarizes six decades of measurements since the first superallowed decay studies by Sherr and Gerhart (1953). The work extends the theoretical framework of the Conserved Vector Current hypothesis proposed by Feynman and Gell-Mann (1958) and the CKM matrix introduced by Cabibbo (1963) and extended by Kobayashi and Maskawa (1973). The results enable the most precise test of CKM unitarity, currently limiting the scope for new physics beyond the Standard Model, with upgraded measurements already underway to improve the ±0.06% precision.
