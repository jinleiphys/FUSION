# Unbound exotic nuclei studied by projectile fragmentation

> We present a simple time dependent model for the excitation of a nucleon from a bound state to a continuum resonant state in a neutron-core complex potential which acts as a final state interaction. The final state is described by an optical model S-matrix so that both resonant and non resonant states of any continuum energy can be studied as well as deeply bound initial states. It is shown that, due to the coupling between the initial and final states, the neutron-core free particle phase shifts are modified, in the exit channel, by an additional phase. The effect of the additional phase on the breakup spectra is clarified. As an example the population of the low energy resonances of $^{11}$Be and of the unbound $^{13}$Be is discussed. Finally, we suggest that the excitation energy spectra of an unbound nucleus might reflect the structure of the parent nucleus from whose fragmentation they are obtained.

## Key claim

The paper presents a time-dependent model for projectile fragmentation that includes the final-state interaction of a neutron with its core via an optical-model S-matrix, showing that the neutron-core free particle phase shifts are modified by an additional phase due to the coupling between initial and final states; this additional phase depends explicitly on the core-target impact parameter and the adiabaticity parameter, and the authors suggest that excitation energy spectra of an unbound nucleus might reflect the structure of the parent nucleus from whose fragmentation they are obtained.

## Method

The authors developed a semiclassical time-dependent perturbation model where a neutron is excited from a bound state to a continuum resonant state via a delta-function neutron-target interaction, with the final state described by an optical-model S-matrix representing the neutron-core interaction. The transition amplitude is calculated as an overlap of initial and final wave functions taken at the core-target impact parameter, boosted by a momentum transfer q along the beam direction, and the cross section is obtained by integrating the excitation probability over core-target impact parameters weighted by the core survival probability. For two-neutron breakup, the model treats one neutron as stripped by the target while the other is elastically scattered and interacts with the core in the final state.

## Key numbers

- The valence neutron in $^{11}$Be is bound by 0.503 MeV with a spectroscopic factor around 0.8 for the 2s state
- The main d$_{5/2}$ strength in $^{11}$Be is centered around 1.25 MeV in the continuum
- The delta-function potential strength used was -4057.59 MeV fm$^3$
- The strong absorption radius R$_s$ is parameterized as approximately 1.4(A$_p^{1/3}$ + A$_t^{1/3}$) fm with a diffuseness parameter a = 0.6 fm
- In $^{14}$B, the valence neutron separation energy S$_n$ = 0.969 MeV while the valence proton separation energy is 17.3 MeV
- The approximate analytical expression for the integral I(k,q) is accurate to within 10% for impact parameters around the strong absorption radius and for neutron-core energies less than 1.5 MeV

## Context

This work extends the transfer-to-the-continuum method developed in Refs. bb-bb4 (2002-2004) for neutron transfer reactions to the projectile fragmentation mechanism, providing a unified theoretical framework for interpreting neutron-core coincidence measurements. The model is tested against experimental data from Fukuda et al. (2004) for $^{11}$Be breakup on $^{12}$C at 70 A.MeV and shows good agreement with both the data and the time-dependent Schrodinger equation calculations of Capel et al. (2004). The approach enables the study of unbound nuclei like $^{13}$Be, which has been previously investigated through multiparticle transfer reactions (Ale83, Ostr92, Belo98) and neutron transfer from deuterons (Kor95, bj), and provides a basis for distinguishing between one-step and two-step mechanisms in two-neutron halo projectile fragmentation.
