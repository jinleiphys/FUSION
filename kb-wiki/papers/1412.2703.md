# Electron-Ion Collider - taking us to the next QCD frontier

> In this talk, I demonstrate that the proposed Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) will be an ideal and unique future facility to address many overarching questions about QCD and strong interaction physics at one place. The EIC will be the world's first polarized electron-proton (and light ion), as well as the first electron-nucleus collider at flexible collision energies. With its high luminosity and beam polarization, the EIC distinguishes itself from HERA and the other fixed target electron-hadron facilities around the world. The EIC is capable of taking us to the next QCD frontier to explore the glue that binds us all.

## Key claim

The Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) will be the first facility capable of exploring the transition from dilute to dense gluon density in nucleons and nuclei, but the existence of saturated gluon matter remains a prediction that the EIC would provide the first unambiguous evidence for, rather than a confirmed phenomenon.

## Method

The paper reviews the proposed EIC design parameters and projected physics reach by comparing its capabilities to existing facilities (HERA, CEBAF, RHIC) and presenting simulated measurements for inclusive deep inelastic scattering (DIS), semi-inclusive DIS (SIDIS), exclusive processes (e.g., J/ψ production), and diffractive cross-section ratios in both e+p and e+A collisions. The projected uncertainties are derived from Monte Carlo simulations assuming the EIC's design luminosity and detector performance, using QCD global analysis frameworks for parton distribution functions (PDFs), transverse-momentum dependent distributions (TMDs), and generalized parton distributions (GPDs).

## Key numbers

- Collision energy range: √s = 20–100 GeV, upgradable to ~140 GeV
- Luminosity: ~10^33–10^34 cm^−2 s^−1
- Beam polarization: ~70% for both electron and nucleon beams
- Quark and antiquark spin contributes ~30% of the proton spin; gluon spin contributes 20–30% from the x-range explored so far
- Nuclear enhancement factor for soft gluon density: proportional to A^1/3, where A is atomic weight
- Expected systematic uncertainty for nuclear structure function ratio at EIC: 2% (total 4%) systematic error, with statistical uncertainty much smaller
- Momentum transfer scale: Q^2 > 1 GeV^2 for existing fixed target data

## Context

This talk extends the science case presented in the EIC White Paper (Accardi et al., 2012) and builds on decades of DIS experiments at SLAC (1960s quark discovery), HERA at DESY (gluon density rise at small x), and fixed-target experiments at CERN (EMC effect). The EIC would unite and extend the programs of CEBAF at Jefferson Lab and RHIC at Brookhaven National Laboratory, enabling precision measurements of gluon saturation, three-dimensional nucleon structure via TMDs and GPDs, and nuclear modifications of parton distributions that current data from EPS09 global fits cannot resolve.
