# Electron Capture $β$-Decay of $^7$Be Encapsulated in C$_{60}$: Origin of Increased Electron Density at the $^7$Be Nucleus

> We offer a new theoretical interpretation for the effect of enhanced electron density at $^7$Be nucleus encapsulated in fullerene C$_{60}$. Our {\it{ab initio}} Hartree-Fock calculations show that electron density at the $^7$Be nucleus in $^7$Be@C$_{60}$ increase due to {\it{}attractive} effective potential well generated by the fullerene. The 2$s$ state in the isolated Be atom turns into 3$s$ state in the joint potential. This new state has higher energy, and slightly larger amplitude at the Be nucleus than the previous 2$s$ state. Moreover the 3$s$ wave function has additional {\it{}node} appeared at the distance $r \simeq 5a_B$ from the center. The node imitates repulsion between the Be electron and the fullerene wall, because the electron has zero probability to occupy this region. Such imitation of the repulsion by means of the node in attractive potential has direct physical analogy in the theory of $α$-$α$ and $N$-$N$ nuclear interactions.

## Key claim
The enhanced electron density at the \(^7\)Be nucleus in \(^7\)Be@C\(_{60}\) arises not from repulsive compression or orbital hybridization, but from the replacement of the Be 2\(s\) state by a 3\(s\) state in the joint attractive potential of the Be atom and the fullerene cage; the resulting 3\(s\) wave function has an additional node that mimics repulsion.

## Method
The authors performed restricted Hartree-Fock (HF) calculations with the 6-31G\(^{**}\) molecular basis set, including electronic correlations at the MP2 level, to optimize the Be position inside C\(_{60}\) and compute the interaction energy. Electron density at the Be nucleus was evaluated using Dunning’s cc-pVTZ basis set, with extrapolation to \(r=0\) via the Kato theorem and verification by adding narrow Gaussian \(s\)-functions. A spherically averaged electrostatic potential extracted from the HF electron density of C\(_{60}\) was used to construct a model potential, and an analytically solvable model of a Coulomb potential combined with a spherical potential layer was developed to illustrate the mechanism.

## Key numbers
- The Be–C\(_{60}\) interaction energy at the HF+MP2 level with counterpoise correction is \(-0.41\) eV (attractive), in contrast to the DFT value of \(+1.05\) eV from Lu et al. (2002).
- The electron density at the Be nucleus in Be@C\(_{60}\) is 35.48 a.u. (atomic units, electrons per cubic Bohr radius), compared to 35.38 a.u. for an isolated Be atom and 34.78 a.u. for metallic Be.
- The relative increase in electron density from metallic Be to Be@C\(_{60}\) is 2.0%, compared to the experimentally measured decay-rate difference of 1.5% at 5 K (Ohtsuki et al., 2007) and 2.1% from half-life data (Kraushaar et al., 1953; Ohtsuki et al., 2007).
- The relative increase in electron density from isolated Be to Be@C\(_{60}\) is 0.28%, in qualitative accord with the 0.17% from DFT calculations (Ohtsuki et al., 2007; Morisato et al., 2008).
- The additional node in the 3\(s\) wave function appears at a distance \(r \simeq 5a_B\) from the center.
- In the analytically solvable model, the spherical potential layer is located between \(R_1 = 5.5 a_B\) and \(R_2 = 7.5 a_B\) with depth \(V = -50\) eV.

## Context
This work extends experimental studies by Ohtsuki et al. (2004, 2007) and Ray et al. (2006) on the electron-capture decay of \(^7\)Be inside C\(_{60}\), which found the largest observed change in half-life due to chemical environment. It contradicts the interpretation of Lu et al. (2002) that the Be–C\(_{60}\) interaction is repulsive and that orbital hybridization causes the density increase, instead showing that the interaction is attractive and that the density enhancement arises from a 2\(s\)-to-3\(s\) state transition in the joint potential. The mechanism has a direct physical analogy in the theory of \(\alpha\)-\(\alpha\) and \(N\)-\(N\) nuclear interactions, where a node in an attractive potential mimics a repulsive core (Saito, 1969; Neudatchin, 1983). This work enables the possibility of controlling the \(^7\)Be decay rate via non-chemical electrostatic interactions, for example by doping C\(_{60}\) with certain atoms.
