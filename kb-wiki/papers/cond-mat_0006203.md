# What can nuclear collisions teach us about the boiling of water or the formation of multi-star systems ?

> Phase transitions in nuclei, small atomic clusters and self-gravitating systems demand the extension of thermo-statistics to ``Small'' systems. The main obstacle is the thermodynamic limit. It is shown how the original definition of the entropy by Boltzmann as the volume of the energy-manifold of the N-body phase space allows a {\em geometrical} definition of the entropy as function of the conserved quantities. Without invoking the thermodynamic limit the whole ``zoo'' of phase transitions and critical points/lines can be unambiguously defined. The relation to the Yang--Lee singularities of the grand-canonical partition sum is pointed out. It is shown that just phase transitions in non-extensive systems give the complete set of characteristic parameters of the transition {\em including the surface tension.} Nuclear heavy-ion collisions are an experimental playground to explore this extension of thermo-statistics

## Key claim

Phase transitions in "small" systems like nuclei, atomic clusters, and self-gravitating systems can be rigorously defined using Boltzmann's original geometric definition of entropy as the volume of the energy manifold in N-body phase space, without invoking the thermodynamic limit; this approach yields the complete set of transition parameters including surface tension, which conventional grand-canonical thermodynamics cannot extract from finite systems.

## Method

The author uses the micro-canonical ensemble with Boltzmann's entropy definition \(S = k \ln W\) where \(W\) is the volume of the \(6N\)-dimensional phase space at sharp energy, and analyzes the curvature (determinant of second derivatives) of the entropy surface \(s(e,n)\) as a function of energy per particle and particle number density. For sodium clusters of 200, 1000, and 3000 atoms, Monte Carlo simulations (MMMC) with realistic interactions at 1 atm external pressure are performed to extract the entropy as a function of energy per atom, from which the convex intruder in the entropy surface reveals the surface entropy and surface tension. The topology of the curvature determinant \(\det(e,n)\) is then used to classify phase transitions: negative determinant indicates a single stable phase, positive determinant indicates a first-order transition with phase separation and surface tension, and zero determinant indicates continuous transitions or multicritical points.

## Key numbers

- For sodium clusters: transition temperature \(T_{tr}\) = 940 K (N=200), 990 K (N=1000), 1095 K (N=3000), compared to bulk value 1156 K
- Latent heat per atom \(q_{lat}\) = 0.82 eV (N=200), 0.91 eV (N=1000), 0.94 eV (N=3000), compared to bulk value 0.923 eV
- Surface tension over transition temperature \(\sigma/T_{tr}\) = 2.75 (N=200), 5.68 (N=1000), 7.07 (N=3000), compared to bulk value 7.41
- Entropy gain for evaporation of one atom \(s_{boil}\) = 10.1 (N=200), 10.7 (N=1000), 9.9 (N=3000), compared to bulk value 9.267 and Trouton's rule value of approximately 10
- Surface entropy per atom \(\Delta s_{surf}\) = 0.55 (N=200), 0.56 (N=1000), 0.45 (N=3000)
- Effective number of surface atoms \(N_{eff}^{2/3}\) = 39.94 (N=200), 98.53 (N=1000), 186.6 (N=3000)

## Context

This work extends the micro-canonical treatment of phase transitions pioneered by the author to "small" non-extensive systems, contrasting with conventional thermodynamics that requires the thermodynamic limit and extensivity as emphasized by Lieb (1997). The approach provides an alternative to non-extensive entropy formulations such as that introduced by Tsallis (1988), and establishes a direct connection between the topology of the micro-canonical entropy surface and Yang-Lee singularities in the grand-canonical partition sum. The method enables the simultaneous determination of transition temperature, latent heat, and surface tension from finite systems, and suggests that nuclear heavy-ion collisions can serve as an experimental laboratory for studying phase transitions in self-gravitating systems, including the statistical formation of multi-star systems under rotation and the fragmentation of objects like Shoemaker-Levy 9.
