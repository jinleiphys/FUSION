# Inclusive prompt photon production in electron-nucleus scattering at small x

> We compute the differential cross-section for inclusive prompt photon production in deeply inelastic scattering (DIS) of electrons on nuclei at small $x$ in the framework of the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) effective theory. The leading order (LO) computation in this framework resums leading logarithms in $x$ as well as power corrections to all orders in $Q_{s,A}^2/Q^2$, where $Q_{s,A}(x)$ is the nuclear saturation scale. This LO result is proportional to universal dipole and quadrupole Wilson line correlators in the nucleus. In the soft photon limit, the Low-Burnett-Kroll theorem allows us to recover existing results on inclusive DIS dijet production. The $k_{\perp}$ and collinearly factorized expressions for prompt photon production in DIS are also recovered in a leading twist approximation to our result. In the latter case, our result corresponds to the dominant next-to-leading order (NLO) perturbative QCD contribution at small $x$. We next discuss the computation of the NLO corrections to inclusive prompt photon production in the CGC framework. In particular, we emphasize the advantages for higher order computations in inclusive photon production, and for fully inclusive DIS, arising from the simple momentum space structure of the dressed quark and gluon "shock wave" propagators in the "wrong" light cone gauge $A^-=0$ for a nucleus moving with $P^{+}_{N} \rightarrow \infty$.

## Key claim
The paper computes the leading-order (LO) differential cross-section for inclusive prompt photon production in deeply inelastic scattering (DIS) of electrons on nuclei at small Bjorken x, within the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) effective theory, and discusses the structure of next-to-leading order (NLO) corrections; the LO result resums leading logarithms in x and power corrections to all orders in Q_{s,A}^2/Q^2, but the authors note that at NLO there are pure alpha_S-suppressed contributions not accompanied by logarithms in x that must be combined with next-to-leading-log JIMWLK evolution for full NLO accuracy.

## Method
The authors compute the amplitude for inclusive prompt photon production in e+A DIS at small x using the CGC effective field theory, employing the dilute-dense power counting where the nuclear target has strong color sources (rho_A ~ 1/g) and the lepton probe is treated perturbatively. The LO computation involves quark-antiquark dipole scattering off the classical color field of the nucleus (Class II processes), with the amplitude expressed in terms of Wilson line correlators in the fundamental representation; the cross-section is obtained by squaring the amplitude and averaging over color sources using the JIMWLK weight functional. The NLO computation is outlined, emphasizing the use of the "wrong" light cone gauge A^- = 0 for the dressed gluon propagator to simplify higher-order calculations.

## Key numbers
- The LO result resums power corrections to all orders in Q_{s,A}^2/Q^2, where Q_{s,A}(x) is the nuclear saturation scale.
- The NLO contributions include terms of order alpha_S ln(x_0/x) that can be as large as the LO contributions, as well as pure alpha_S-suppressed terms.
- In the leading twist approximation, the cross-section becomes proportional to the usual leading twist nuclear gluon distribution evaluated at the scale Q^2 of the virtual photon.
- The soft photon limit (k_gamma -> 0) recovers existing results for inclusive DIS dijet production via the Low-Burnett-Kroll theorem.

## Context
This work extends the CGC framework for inclusive photon production from p+A collisions (Gelis 2002, Benic 2016) to the e+A DIS case at small x, where the gluon distribution dominates over valence quark distributions as seen in HERA e+p data (Abt 1993, Ahmed 1995, Derrick 1993, Derrick 1994, Martin 1994, Lai 1994). The computation enables future quantitative extraction of the nuclear saturation scale Q_{s,A}(x) from e+A measurements at the Electron-Ion Collider, and the NLO framework can be combined with known NLLx JIMWLK evolution equations (Balitsky 2013, Grabovsky 2013, Kovner 2013) and NLLx BK equations (Kovchegov 2006, Balitsky 2008) for precision predictions.
