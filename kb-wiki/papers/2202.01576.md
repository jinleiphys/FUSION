# Formulating bulk viscosity for neutron star simulations

> In order to extract the precise physical information encoded in the gravitational and electromagnetic signals from powerful neutron-star merger events, we need to include as much of the relevant physics as possible in our numerical simulations. This presents a severe challenge, given that many of the involved parameters are poorly constrained. In this paper we focus on the role of nuclear reactions. Combining a theoretical discussion with an analysis connecting to state-of-the-art simulations, we outline multiple arguments that lead to a reactive system being described in terms of a bulk viscosity. The results demonstrate that in order to properly account for nuclear reactions, future simulations must be able to handle different regimes where rather different assumptions/approximations are appropriate. We also touch upon the link to models based on the large-eddy-strategy required to capture turbulence.

## Key claim

The paper argues that nuclear reactions in neutron star merger simulations can be approximated by a bulk viscosity only when the reaction timescale is faster than the numerically resolvable timescale; when reactions are resolvable, the bulk viscosity approximation breaks down and the full reactive system must be evolved directly.

## Method

The authors develop a multi-scale analysis of a reactive three-fluid system (neutrons, protons, electrons) using extended irreversible thermodynamics, starting from the Cattaneo-type relaxation equation for the chemical affinity beta = mu_n - mu_p - mu_e. They derive the conditions under which the fast relaxation of the affinity toward equilibrium can be "integrated out" to yield a Navier-Stokes bulk viscosity closure, and compare this approximation to the full hyperbolic (causal) formulation. The analysis is applied to the APR equation of state from the compOSE database to map the relevant parameter space.

## Key numbers

* The relaxation rate A (inverse timescale) defines the peak frequency of the bulk viscosity resonance; contours of fixed A are plotted in the (temperature, density) plane.
* A numerical angular frequency of 10^7 s^-1 roughly corresponds to a grid spacing of 200 m (current simulations), while 10^9 s^-1 corresponds to roughly 2 m (state of the art in approximately ten years).
* The bulk viscosity approximation is relevant for densities above 10^-2 n_sat (where n_sat is nuclear saturation density) and temperatures above 10 MeV, where current simulations cannot resolve the reactions.
* The bottom two A contours bracket frequencies relevant for bulk gravitational wave generation (1-10 kHz).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of bulk viscosity from nuclear reactions developed by Alford and collaborators (Alford 2010, Alford and Harris 2018, Alford and Harris 2018 on damping) and connects it to the practical needs of numerical relativity simulations of neutron star mergers. The paper directly addresses the "double counting" concern raised by Perego and collaborators (Perego 2019) regarding simultaneous treatment of reactions and bulk viscosity in neutrino-transport schemes. The results enable future simulations to determine, for a given numerical resolution, whether to use a bulk viscosity closure or to evolve the full reactive system, and provide a formal justification for the large-eddy simulation approach to turbulence in this context.
