# Nuclear medium effects from hadronic atoms

> The state of the art in the study of pionic, kaonic and Sigmionic atoms, along with the in-medium nuclear interactions deduced for these hadrons, is reviewed. A special emphasis is placed on recent developments in antikaon-nuclear physics, where a strongly attractive density dependent antikaon-nuclear potential of order 150-200 MeV in nuclear matter emerges by fitting K^- atom data. This has interesting repercussions on antikaon quasibound nuclear states, on the composition of strange hadronic matter and on kaon condensation in self bound hadronic systems.

# Nuclear medium effects from hadronic atoms

## Key claim

The paper reviews evidence that fitting $K^-$ atom data yields a strongly attractive density-dependent antikaon-nuclear potential of order 150–200 MeV in nuclear matter, but notes that the existence of such deep potentials versus shallower alternatives remains an open problem, with the resolution requiring new high-precision measurements of a canonical set of $K^-$ atom data.

## Method

The authors analyze hadronic atom data (pionic, kaonic, and Sigmionic) using optical potentials $V_{\rm opt}^{h}=t_{hN}(\rho)\rho$ that are functionals of nuclear density $\rho(r)$, where $t_{hN}(\rho)$ is a density-dependent in-medium hadron-nucleon $t$ matrix at threshold. For $K^-$ atoms, global fits to strong-interaction level shifts, widths, and yields from X-ray transitions are performed across the periodic table using both density-independent and density-dependent potential forms, including a Fourier-Bessel expansion method. For $\Sigma^-$ atoms, the analysis extracts the real part of the $\Sigma$-nuclear potential from 23 data points, revealing a transition from attraction at low density to repulsion in the nuclear interior.

## Key numbers

- $K^-$-nuclear potential depth from global fits: $-V_{R}^{\bar K}(\rho_0)\sim (150-200)$ MeV at nuclear matter density $\rho_0 = 0.16$ fm$^{-3}$
- Best-fit $\chi^2$ for 65 $K^-$ atom data points: 84 for the deep density-dependent potential (model F), compared to 130 for a shallow $t\rho$ potential
- For the reduced 15-point data set (five targets: C, Si, Ni, Sn, Pb), deep potential gives Re $b(\rho_0)=1.47\pm 0.05$ fm and $\chi^2=26$, while shallow gives Re $b(\rho_0)=0.78\pm 0.13$ fm and $\chi^2=44$
- Pionic atom global fits to 100 data points yield $b_1=-0.104\pm 0.006~m_\pi^{-1}$ for density-independent fits, compared to free-space value $b_1^{\rm free}=-0.0864\pm 0.0010~m_\pi^{-1}$
- $\Sigma$ isoscalar potential from phenomenology: $V_0^{\Sigma}=+30\pm 20$ MeV at $k_F=1.35$ fm$^{-1}$
- Leading-order Tomozawa-Weinberg $\bar K$-nuclear potential: $V_{\rm TW}^{\bar K}=-55$ MeV at $\rho_0$
- In-medium chiral approach yields $V_{R}^{\bar K}(\rho_0)\approx -110$ MeV at $\rho_0$
- Neutron skin parameter from pionic atoms: $\gamma=1.1\pm 0.1$ fm

## Context

This review updates and extends two earlier Physics Reports reviews by the same authors (Friedman and Gal, 1997; Friedman and Gal, 2007) on hadronic atom phenomenology. For pionic atoms, the work confirms the need for density-dependent renormalization of the isovector $s$-wave amplitude $b_1$ as predicted by Weise (2001) from partial restoration of chiral symmetry, consistent with low-energy $\pi$-nucleus scattering data from PSI (Friedman, 2004). For $\Sigma^-$ atoms, the repulsive $\Sigma$-nuclear potential deduced from atomic data agrees with recent Nijmegen ESC08 model calculations (Rijken, 2010) and with chiral EFT and quark-model calculations (Fujiwara, 2007; Polinder, 2006), with implications for the composition of neutron star matter. For $K^-$ atoms, the deep potentials found from global fits are supported by KEK $(K^-,n)$ and $(K^-,p)$ spectra on $^{12}$C (Kishimoto, 2007) but challenged by concerns from Magas et al. (2010) about background reactions. The review enables future work on $\bar K$ quasibound nuclear states, multi-$\bar K$ nuclei, and kaon condensation in neutron stars, while identifying the need for new high-precision $K^-$ atom measurements to resolve the deep versus shallow potential ambiguity.
