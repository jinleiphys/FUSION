# Physics of nuclei: Key role of an emergent symmetry

> Exact symmetry and symmetry-breaking phenomena play a key role in providing a better understanding of the physics of many-particle systems, from quarks and atomic nuclei, to molecules and galaxies. In atomic nuclei, exact and dominant symmetries such as rotational invariance, parity, and charge independence have been clearly established. However, even when these symmetries are taken into account, the structure of nuclei remains illusive and only partially understood, with no additional symmetries immediately evident from the underlying nucleon-nucleon interaction. Here, we show through ab initio large-scale nuclear structure calculations that the special nature of the strong nuclear force determines additional highly regular patterns in nuclei that can be tied to an emergent approximate symmetry. We find that this symmetry is remarkably ubiquitous, regardless of its particular strong interaction heritage, and mathematically tracks with a symplectic group. Specifically, we show for light to intermediate-mass nuclei that the structure of a nucleus, along with its low-energy excitations, respects symplectic symmetry at about 70-80% level, unveiling the predominance of only a few equilibrium shapes, deformed or not, with associated vibrations and rotations. This establishes the symplectic symmetry as a remarkably good symmetry of the strong nuclear force, in the low-energy regime. This may have important implications to studies in astrophysics and neutrino physics that rely on nuclear structure information, especially where experimental measurements are incomplete or not available. A very important practical advantage is that this new symmetry can be utilized to dramatically reduce computational resources required in ab initio large-scale nuclear structure modeling. This, in turn, can be used to pioneer predictions, e.g., for short-lived isotopes along various nucleosynthesis pathways.

## Key claim

Through ab initio large-scale nuclear structure calculations, the authors show that low-lying states of light to intermediate-mass nuclei respect an approximate symplectic Sp(3,R) symmetry at about 70-80% level, but this is a conditional claim: the symmetry is approximate and emerges from the strong nuclear force rather than being imposed, and the dominance of a single symplectic irreducible representation holds for the specific nuclei studied below the calcium region.

## Method

The authors performed ab initio no-core shell model calculations using the symmetry-adapted no-core shell model (SA-NCSM) framework, solving the many-body Schrodinger equation for A particles with realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions derived from chiral effective field theory (EM N3LO and NNLOopt potentials). They reorganized the complete harmonic oscillator basis via a unitary transformation into a symmetry-adapted basis that respects the approximate symplectic Sp(3,R) symmetry, then analyzed the decomposition of nuclear wave functions into symplectic irreducible representations. The calculations were performed for model spaces up to 14 harmonic oscillator major shells with inter-shell distances of 15-20 MeV, and the symplectic content was tested for stability against variations in box size and resolution.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear wave functions respect symplectic Sp(3,R) symmetry at about 70-80% level for light to intermediate-mass nuclei below the calcium region
- The ground state of 20Ne shows a single symplectic irreducible representation dominating the 0+gs, 2+, 4+, 6+, and 8+ rotational band states
- The fragmented giant monopole resonance in 20Ne has a centroid around 29 MeV
- Energies calculated using only a few dominant symplectic irreps reproduce experimental data with errors approximately 100 keV
- For 6Li and 20Ne, calculations used model spaces of 14 and 11 harmonic oscillator major shells respectively, with inter-shell distances of 20 MeV and 15 MeV

## Context

This work extends the Elliott SU(3) model (Elliott, 1958) and the symplectic rotor model (Rosensteel and Rowe, 1977; Rowe, 1985) by demonstrating that the symplectic Sp(3,R) symmetry emerges naturally from first-principles calculations without a priori symmetry assumptions, rather than being imposed as in earlier algebraic models. The results contradict the standard shell model expectation that nuclear structure requires many configurations, showing instead that a few equilibrium shapes dominate. This enables dramatically reduced computational resources for ab initio nuclear structure modeling and opens the possibility for predictions of short-lived isotopes relevant to nucleosynthesis pathways and neutrino physics.
