# Green's Function Knockout Formalism

> Knockout nuclear reactions, in which a nucleon is removed from a nucleus as a result of the collision with another nucleus, have been widely used as an experimental tool, both to populate isotopes further removed from stability, and to obtain information about the single-particle nature of the nuclear spectrum. In order to fully exploit the experimental information, theory is needed for the description of both the structure of the nuclei involved, and the dynamics associated with the nucleon removal mechanisms. The standard approach, using theoretical shell-model spectroscopic factors for the structure description coupled with an eikonal model of reaction, has been successful when used in the context of the removal of valence nucleons in nuclei close to stability. However, it has been argued that the reaction theory might need to be revisited in the case of exotic nuclei, more specifically for highly asymmetric nuclei in which the deficient species (neutrons or protons) is being removed. We present here a new formalism for the nucleon-removal and -addition reaction through knockout and transfer reactions, that treats consistently structure and reaction properties using dispersive optical potentials. In particular, our formalism includes the dynamical effects associated with the removal of a neutron from the projectile, which might explain the long standing puzzle of the quenching of spectroscopic factors in nuclei with extreme neutrons-to-protons ratios.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new Green's Function Knockout (GFK) formalism that treats structure and reaction properties consistently using dispersive optical potentials, including dynamical effects associated with nucleon removal that are neglected in the standard eikonal model with the core spectator approximation. The authors suggest these dissipative effects, particularly for deeply-bound nucleons in highly asymmetric nuclei, might explain the long-standing puzzle of spectroscopic factor quenching observed in knockout experiments but not in transfer reactions.

## Method

The authors derive an inclusive cross section for one-nucleon removal reactions by expressing the sum over final states of the core and the nucleon-target system in terms of the imaginary part of the many-body Green's function, then performing an optical reduction to obtain a one-body hole propagator. The cross section is written in terms of a source term describing hole production and the imaginary part of the hole optical potential, which encodes dissipative effects from core excitation. The formalism is applied to both knockout reactions and transfer reactions such as (p,d) and (d,p), and the authors discuss implementation using either the eikonal approximation or the Distorted Wave Born Approximation for the incoming scattering wave.

## Key numbers

- Hole states from deeply-bound nucleon removal can have widths of tens of MeV, corresponding to decay times of order 10^-23 to 10^-22 s, comparable to collision times in fast knockout experiments.
- The deuteron ground state binding energy used in the (p,d) transfer reaction example is epsilon_d = -2.2246 MeV.
- The eikonal approximation is stated to be accurate for reactions at energies above 60 MeV/nucleon.

## Context

This work extends the Green's Function Transfer (GFT) formalism of Potel et al. (2017), which described one-nucleon addition, to also describe one-nucleon removal processes including knockout and transfer reactions. The formalism directly addresses the puzzle raised by Gade and collaborators (2008, 2014, 2021), who found that standard theory cannot account for up to 80% of spectroscopic factor quenching when the knocked-out particle belongs to the deficient species in highly asymmetric nuclei. The authors note that this quenching is not observed in transfer and quasifree scattering experiments, as discussed by Tostevin et al. (2009), Lee et al. (2010), and others, and that dissipative effects estimated within the intra-nuclear cascade approach by Lee et al. (2011) could account for the discrepancy. The GFK formalism enables a consistent theoretical framework to compare knockout and transfer reaction analyses and may help resolve this long-standing puzzle.
