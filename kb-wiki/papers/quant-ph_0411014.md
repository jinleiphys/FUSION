# Upper and lower bounds on the mean square radius and criteria for occurrence of quantum halo states

> In the context of non-relativistic quantum mechanics, we obtain several upper and lower limits on the mean square radius applicable to systems composed by two-body bound by a central potential. A lower limit on the mean square radius is used to obtain a simple criteria for the occurrence of S-wave quantum halo sates.

## Key claim
The paper derives rigorous upper and lower bounds on the mean square radius for two-body systems bound by a central potential, and uses a lower bound to obtain a simple criterion for identifying S-wave quantum halo states, states where the root-mean-square radius exceeds twice the classical turning point radius.

## Method
The author starts from the radial Schrödinger equation and, through integration by parts and manipulation of the wavefunction normalization condition, derives an identity linking the mean square radius, the energy, the angular momentum, and an integral involving the potential and its derivative. From this identity, upper and lower bounds on the product \(-E\langle r^2\rangle\) are obtained by taking suprema or infima of the function \(W(r) = -(6V(r) + r V'(r)) r^2\). A separate upper bound for the lowest \(\ell\)-wave states (no nodes) is derived using Cauchy-Schwarz and Jost-Pais conditions. The lower bound for S-waves is then applied to define a halo criterion: a state is a halo if its binding energy satisfies \(-2\sigma^2 r_0^2 V(r_0) \le 1\), where \(r_0\) is the classical turning point and \(\sigma=2\) is a chosen threshold factor.

## Key numbers
- For the Wood-Saxon potential modeling nuclear halos, the energy \(E_H\) above which halo states appear is approximately \(E_H \cong -0.22\, A^{-2/5}\) MeV for \(\sigma=2\) (with \(A\) the atomic mass number), and \(E_H \cong -0.25\, A^{-1/2}\) MeV for \(\sigma=1.5\).
- For a Lennard-Jones (10,6) potential with \(R=2.640\) Å (modeling the helium dimer \(^4\)He\(_2\)), the predicted halo threshold energy is \(E_H = -0.82\) \(\mu\)eV, while the experimental binding energy is \(-0.095\) \(\mu\)eV, indicating the dimer is a halo state.
- For the potential \(V_1(r) = -g R^{-2} / (1 + (r/R)^3)\) with \(\ell=0\) and coupling \(g=1.35\) (near the critical value \(g_c \approx 1.34\)), the exact value of \(-E\langle r^2\rangle\) is 0.51418, while the lower bound from Eq. (17) gives 0.5 and the upper bound from Eq. (15) gives 1.0063.
- For the same potential with \(\ell=1\) and \(g=6.945\) (near \(g_c \approx 6.94\)), the exact \(-E\langle r^2\rangle\) is 0.06045, with the upper bound from Eq. (15) at 1.771 and from Eq. (8) at 2.6746.

## Context
This work extends prior inequalities on the mean square radius, such as the Bertlmann-Martin inequality (Bertlmann and Martin, 1980) for \(\ell=0\) states and the general lower bound \(\langle r^2\rangle \ge (2\ell+3)^2/(4\langle T\rangle)\) (Grosse, 1997). The derived lower bound for S-waves, \(-2E_{\ell=0}\langle r^2\rangle_{\ell=0} \ge 1\), generalizes a result previously known only for finite-range potentials (Lassaut and Lombard, 1998). The halo criterion complements earlier studies of halo nuclei (Riisager, 1992; Fedorov and Jensen, 1993; Jensen and Riisager, 2000) and provides a simple analytical tool to predict whether a weakly bound two-body system will exhibit a halo structure, enabling systematic surveys across different potential shapes and mass numbers.
