# Shape coexistence in Lead isotopes in the interacting boson model with Gogny energy density functional

> We investigate the emergence and evolution of shape coexistence in the neutron-deficient Lead isotopes within the interacting boson model (IBM) plus configuration mixing with microscopic input based on the Gogny energy density functional (EDF). The microscopic potential energy surface obtained from the constrained self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method employing the Gogny-D1M EDF is mapped onto the coherent-state expectation value of the configuration-mixing IBM Hamiltonian. In this way, the parameters of the IBM Hamiltonian are fixed for each of the three relevant configurations (spherical, prolate and oblate) associated to the mean field minima. Subsequent diagonalization of the Hamiltonian provides the excitation energy of the low-lying states and transition strengths among them. The model predictions for the $0^{+}$ level energies and evolving shape coexistence in the considered Lead chain are consistent both with experiment and with the indications of the Gogny-EDF energy surfaces.

# Shape coexistence in Lead isotopes in the interacting boson model with Gogny energy density functional

## Key claim

The configuration-mixing interacting boson model (IBM) Hamiltonian parameters can be determined entirely from microscopic Gogny-D1M energy density functional (EDF) mean-field calculations without fitting to experimental data, and the resulting model reproduces the evolution of shape coexistence in neutron-deficient Lead isotopes. However, the offset energies Δᵢ required for spectroscopic calculations differ from those derived directly from the potential energy surface because the intruder configurations gain significant quantum correlation energy through diagonalization that is not captured by the mean-field minima alone.

## Method

Constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations with the Gogny-D1M EDF were performed for ¹⁸²⁻¹⁹²Pb to obtain microscopic potential energy surfaces (PES) as functions of quadrupole deformation parameters β and γ. The PES for each of three configurations (spherical 0p-0h, oblate 2p-2h, and prolate 4p-4h) was mapped onto the coherent-state expectation value of an IBM-2 Hamiltonian, fixing parameters εᵢ, κᵢ, χᵨ,ᵢ, and Cᵦ,ᵢ for each configuration independently. The mixing strengths ωⁱ⁻²ⁱ were determined from the barrier heights between minima, and the offset energies Δᵢ were adjusted using Eq. (6) to account for quantum correlation energies from diagonalization. The full Hamiltonian was then diagonalized in the boson m-scheme basis to obtain excitation energies, B(E2) transition strengths, and spectroscopic quadrupole moments.

## Key numbers

- The three lowest 0⁺ states in ¹⁸⁶Pb lie within a range of 700 keV experimentally
- For ¹⁸⁶Pb, the Δ₂ value derived from the PES is 4.014 MeV, but the value needed in the spectroscopic Hamiltonian is 4.884 MeV (difference of 0.870 MeV representing quantum correlation energy)
- The 2p-2h configuration in ¹⁸⁶Pb gives E₂(0⁺) = -3.676 MeV from diagonalization, with δE₂ = 1.208 MeV from the mean-field minima
- The mixing strength ω⁰² ≈ 50 keV and ω²⁴ ≈ 200 keV from the microscopic mapping, compared to ω⁰² ≈ 10 keV and ω²⁴ ≈ 20-30 keV in phenomenological fits
- The offset energies Δᵢ are approximately 4 MeV for the 2p-2h configuration and 8 MeV for the 4p-4h configuration
- The effective charges used are e₀ = 0.110 eb, e₂ = 0.140 eb, and e₄ = 0.170 eb (taken from Hellemans (2008))
- The experimental 2⁺₁ excitation energy for ¹⁸⁶Pb is 662 keV and for ¹⁸⁸Pb is 724 keV; the experimental 0⁺₃ excitation energy for ¹⁸⁶Pb is 655 keV and for ¹⁸⁸Pb is 725 keV

## Context

This work extends the method of Nomura (2008) for mapping mean-field PES onto IBM Hamiltonians to include configuration mixing, building on the Duval-Barrett (1981, 1982) framework for intruder states in the IBM. It provides a parameter-free microscopic alternative to phenomenological configuration-mixing IBM studies such as Fossion (2003) and Hellemans (2008), which fitted parameters to experimental spectra. The approach enables systematic spectroscopic predictions for shape coexistence in heavy nuclei where full shell-model calculations are intractable, and can be applied to other mass regions where mean-field calculations indicate multiple coexisting minima.
