# Effects of in-medium cross-sections and optical potential on thermal-source formation in p+197Au reactions at 6.2-14.6 GeV/c

> Effects of in-medium cross-sections and of optical potential on pre-equilibrium emission and on formation of a thermal source are investigated by comparing the results of transport simulations with experimental results from the p+{197}Au reaction at 6.2-14.6 GeV/c. The employed transport model includes light composite-particle production and allows for inclusion of in-medium particle-particle cross-section reduction and of momentum dependence in the particle optical-potentials. Compared to the past, the model incorporates improved parameterizations of elementary high-energy processes. The simulations indicate that the majority of energy deposition occurs during the first ~25 fm/c of a reaction. This is followed by a pre-equilibrium emission and readjustment of system density and momentum distribution toward an equilibrated system. Good agreement with data, on the d/p and t/p yield ratios and on the residue mass and charge numbers, is obtained at the time of ~ 65 fm/c from the start of a reaction, provided reduced in-medium cross-sections and momentum-dependent optical potentials are employed in the simulations. By then, the pre-equilibrium nucleon and cluster emission, as well as mean-field readjustments, drive the system to a state of depleted average density, rho/rho_{0} ~ 1/4-1/3 for central collisions, and low-to-moderate excitation, i.e. the region of nuclear liquid-gas phase transition.

## Key claim

Good agreement with experimental data on d/p and t/p yield ratios and residue mass/charge numbers is obtained at ~65 fm/c from the start of the reaction, but only when both reduced in-medium cross-sections and momentum-dependent optical potentials are employed in the simulations; the paper does not claim perfect agreement for the 3He/p ratio, which remains below the data.

## Method

The Boltzmann-equation model (BEM) was used to simulate p+197Au reactions at 6.2-14.6 GeV/c, incorporating light composite-particle production (deuterons, tritons, 3He) via three- and four-body processes. The simulations compared five variants: free versus in-medium cross-sections, with and without momentum-dependent optical potentials, and with or without light composite particle production. Filtered simulation results were compared to experimental data from the ISiS detector array at the Brookhaven AGS, using energy cuts (30 MeV for Z=1, 58 MeV for Z=2) and geometrical acceptance to separate pre-equilibrium from thermal particles.

## Key numbers

- Thermalization time of ~65 fm/c extracted for best agreement between simulations and data, approximately independent of impact parameter and incident momentum
- Majority of energy deposition occurs during the first ~25 fm/c of the reaction
- Average source density reaches rho/rho_0 ~ 1/4-1/3 for central collisions at thermalization
- Momentum-dependent potential increases source excitation energy by ~150-200 MeV compared to density-dependent only case
- Excitation energies up to ~1400 MeV (5-6 AMeV) achieved in simulations with light composite particle production, versus ~800 MeV without clusters
- Maximum excitation energy reached at 18 fm/c for central collisions and 10-12 fm/c for peripheral collisions
- First-stage neutron multiplicity estimated as M_n = 1.93 x M_p from experimental data

## Context

This work extends earlier transport-model studies by Wang et al. (2000) on hadron-induced reactions at a few GeV/c, which found depleted density regions but used simpler parameterizations without momentum-dependent mean-fields or cluster formation. The paper also builds on prior demonstrations by Zhang, Das Gupta and Gale, and by Pan and Danielewicz that momentum-dependent potentials were needed to reproduce collective flow in heavy-ion reactions. The results support the analysis methods of nuclear fragmentation relying on equilibrium concepts within the phase transition region as applied to ISiS data by Berkenbusch et al. and Elliott et al. The study enables future investigation of isospin effects and extension to alpha-particle production in pre-equilibrium emission.
