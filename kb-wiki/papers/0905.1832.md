# Probing particle and nuclear physics models of neutrinoless double beta decay with different nuclei

> Half-life estimates for neutrinoless double beta decay depend on particle physics models for lepton flavor violation, as well as on nuclear physics models for the structure and transitions of candidate nuclei. Different models considered in the literature can be contrasted - via prospective data - with a "standard" scenario characterized by light Majorana neutrino exchange and by the quasiparticle random phase approximation, for which the theoretical covariance matrix has been recently estimated. We show that, assuming future half-life data in four promising nuclei (Ge-76, Se-82, Te-130, and Xe-136), the standard scenario can be distinguished from a few nonstandard physics models, while being compatible with alternative state-of-the-art nuclear calculations (at 95% C.L.). Future signals in different nuclei may thus help to discriminate at least some decay mechanisms, without being spoiled by current nuclear uncertainties. Prospects for possible improvements are also discussed.

## Key claim

Using prospective half-life data in four promising nuclei (Ge-76, Se-82, Te-130, and Xe-136), the standard scenario of light Majorana neutrino exchange with QRPA nuclear matrix elements can be distinguished from at least two nonstandard particle physics models at 95% C.L., while remaining compatible with alternative state-of-the-art nuclear calculations at the same confidence level.

## Method

The authors constructed a null hypothesis based on QRPA calculations for light Majorana neutrino exchange, including the full theoretical covariance matrix (errors and correlations) estimated from 24 variants of input parameters. They generated mock half-life data with 20% experimental uncertainty for seven particle physics models and six nuclear physics models, normalized to a benchmark half-life of 10^26 years for Ge-76. A chi-squared test with N-1 degrees of freedom (where N is the number of nuclei) was used to compute rejection probabilities against the null hypothesis, with a threshold of 95% C.L. for statistical significance.

## Key numbers

- Null hypothesis central logarithmic half-lives (tau^0_i = log10(T_i/y)): Ge-76 = 26.000, Se-82 = 25.480, Te-130 = 25.430, Xe-136 = 25.888
- Null hypothesis 1-sigma errors: Ge-76 = 0.244, Se-82 = 0.270, Te-130 = 0.316, Xe-136 = 0.374
- Null hypothesis correlations between nuclei: Ge-Se = 0.978, Ge-Te = 0.899, Ge-Xe = 0.805, Se-Te = 0.927, Se-Xe = 0.846, Te-Xe = 0.916
- Assumed experimental uncertainty: s_i = 0.08 (corresponding to 20% half-life accuracy)
- Rejection probability for RHC-lambda model with four nuclei: 97.6%
- Rejection probability for KK+1 model with four nuclei: 99.9%
- All six nuclear physics models gave rejection probabilities below 95% C.L. (highest was 87.0% for QRPA (S&K) with g_A=1.00 and UCOM)
- Without theoretical error correlations, no model reached 95% C.L. rejection (highest was 61.8% for KK+1)

## Context

This work extends the quantitative study of model discrimination in neutrinoless double beta decay initiated by Deppisch et al. and Elliott et al., but improves upon it by including the full theoretical covariance matrix estimated by Fogli et al. (2009) for QRPA calculations. The key advance is showing that theoretical error correlations are crucial for discrimination power: without them, no particle physics model could be distinguished from the standard scenario. The analysis enables future experiments (GERDA, MAJORANA, SuperNEMO, CUORE, EXO) to potentially identify the underlying decay mechanism using multi-isotope data, and motivates further theoretical work to evaluate covariance matrices for nonstandard particle physics mechanisms and alternative nuclear models.
