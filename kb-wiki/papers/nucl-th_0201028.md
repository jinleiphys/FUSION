# Coulomb Energy of Nuclei

> The density functional determining the Coulomb energy of nuclei is calculated to the first order in $e^2$. It is shown that the Coulomb energy includes three terms: the Hartree energy; the Fock energy; and the correlation Coulomb energy (CCE), which contributes considerably to the surface energy, the mass difference between mirror nuclei, and the single-particle spectrum. A CCE-based mechanism of a systematic shift of the single-particle spectrum is proposed. A dominant contribution to the CCE is shown to come from the surface region of nuclei. The CCE effect on the calculated proton drip line is examined, and the maximum charge $Z$ of nuclei near this line is found to decrease by 2 or 3 units. The effect of Coulomb interaction on the effective proton mass is analyzed.

# Coulomb Energy of Nuclei

## Key claim

The density functional for the Coulomb energy of nuclei, calculated to first order in \(e^2\), includes a correlation Coulomb energy (CCE) term that contributes significantly to surface energy, mirror-nucleus mass differences, and single-particle spectra; the CCE is shown to shift the proton drip line such that the maximum charge \(Z\) of nuclei near this line decreases by 2 or 3 units, but this result depends on the specific approximation used for the CCE functional and the choice of effective interaction parameters.

## Method

The author constructs the Coulomb energy density functional to first order in \(e^2\) using the Hartree-Fock method with effective forces within density-functional theory, separating the functional into nuclear-force and Coulomb parts. The Coulomb part is decomposed into Hartree, Fock, and correlation Coulomb energy (CCE) terms, where the CCE is calculated from the exact linear response function of protons to an external electric field, with the response function determined by the nuclear-force functional \(F_0\). The CCE is evaluated in semi-infinite nuclear matter using a generalized local density approximation, and the resulting energy per proton is approximated by a simple analytic expression peaked at the nuclear surface.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of Coulomb potential energy to Fermi energy is \(\alpha = e^2 / r_0 \varepsilon_F \simeq 0.03\), where \(\varepsilon_F \simeq 40\) MeV and \(r_0\) is the mean nucleon spacing.
- The CCE per proton is approximated as \(e_c(\rho_p(r)) = D [(\rho_p(r)/\rho_0)(1 - \rho_p(r)/\rho_0)]^{4/3}\) with \(D \simeq 6\) MeV and equilibrium nuclear density \(2\rho_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- The CCE contribution to the total binding energy is \(\Delta E = a_1' Z + a_2' Z^{2/3}\) with \(a_1' \simeq -0.1\) MeV and \(a_2' \simeq 1.0\) MeV.
- The CCE-induced shift of single-particle proton levels near the Fermi level is \(\Delta\varepsilon_p \simeq (0.3-0.4)\) MeV for medium-mass and heavy nuclei.
- Calculated mass differences between mirror nuclei: \(^{15}\)O-\(^{15}\)N = 3.48 MeV (experiment 3.54 MeV); \(^{17}\)F-\(^{17}\)O = 3.56 MeV (experiment 3.54 MeV); \(^{39}\)Ca-\(^{39}\)K = 7.23 MeV (experiment 7.30 MeV); \(^{41}\)Sc-\(^{41}\)Ca = 7.24 MeV (experiment 7.28 MeV); \(^{48}\)Ni-\(^{48}\)Ca = 66.70 MeV (experiment 67.06 MeV).
- The maximum charge \(Z\) of nuclei near the proton drip line decreases by 2 or 3 units when CCE is included.

## Context

This work extends the author's prior studies (Shaginyan and collaborators, late 1990s) on a surface-enhanced Coulomb correlation mechanism that explains a dominant part of the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly, a 30-year-old discrepancy between calculated and experimental mass differences of mirror nuclei first identified by Nolen and Schiffer. The paper provides a consistent density-functional framework for calculating the Coulomb energy, enabling improved predictions of the proton drip line and effective proton mass. The approach contrasts with earlier attempts that introduced charge-dependent forces (as in charge-symmetry-breaking models) to fit the anomaly, and it offers a method to estimate coupling constants for charge-symmetry-violating forces from the remaining small discrepancies in mirror-nucleus mass differences.
