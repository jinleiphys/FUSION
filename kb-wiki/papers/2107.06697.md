# Hot and Dense Matter Equation of State Probability Distributions for Astrophysical Simulations

> We add an ensemble of nuclei to the equation of state for homogeneous nucleonic matter to generate a new set of models suitable for astrophysical simulations of core-collapse supernovae and neutron star mergers. We implement empirical constraints from (i) nuclear mass measurements, (ii) proton-proton scattering phase shifts, and (iii) neutron star observations. Our model is also guided by microscopic many-body theory calculations based on realistic nuclear forces, including the zero-temperature neutron matter equation of state from quantum Monte Carlo simulations and thermal contributions to the free energy from finite-temperature many-body perturbation theory. We ensure that the parameters of our model can be varied while preserving thermodynamic consistency and the connection to experimental or observational data, thus providing a probability distribution of the astrophysical hot and dense matter equation of state. We compare our results with those obtained from other available equations of state. While our probability distributions indeed represent a large number of possible equations of state, we cannot yet claim to have fully explored all of the uncertainties, especially with regard to the structure of nuclei in the hot and dense medium.

## Key claim

The authors construct a new set of equation of state (EOS) models for hot and dense matter that incorporate an ensemble of nuclei and are constrained by nuclear experiments and neutron star observations, but they explicitly state that they cannot yet claim to have fully explored all uncertainties, particularly regarding the structure of nuclei in the hot and dense medium.

## Method

The authors add a distribution of nuclei to a previously developed homogeneous nucleonic matter EOS (Du 2019) using a Helmholtz free energy formalism that includes free neutrons, protons, light nuclei (deuterons, tritons, alpha particles, He-3, Li-4), and heavy nuclei treated as classical Boltzmann particles with excluded volume corrections and Coulomb energy in the Wigner-Seitz approximation. They solve Saha equations for nuclear chemical equilibrium, using experimental nuclear masses where available and theoretical masses from Moller (1995) otherwise, with nuclear partition functions computed from discrete states and a backshifted Fermi-gas level density. The homogeneous matter component combines a virial expansion at low density with a degenerate matter description using a Skyrme interaction (SKχm*) fitted to chiral many-body calculations, interpolated to a high-density EOS constrained by neutron star observations via Markov-chain samples from Steiner (2015).

## Key numbers

- Fiducial EOS parameters: symmetry energy S = 32.8 MeV, symmetry energy slope L = 62.4 MeV, speed of sound parameter φ = 0.9
- Maximum mass number for heavy nuclei is limited to about 340; for symmetric nuclear matter (Ye=0.5) the average mass number reaches this upper limit, while for smaller electron fractions the maximum decreases to 120
- Transition density from inhomogeneous to homogeneous matter is largest near Ye ≈ 0.4, and nuclei always disappear at a baryon density below n0 = 0.16 fm⁻³ (saturation density)
- For Ye=0.1 and T=1.0 MeV, the alpha particle mass fraction rises to around 0.2 for baryon densities between 10⁻⁷ and 10⁻⁶ fm⁻³
- The critical temperature of the gas-liquid phase transition ranges from several to tens of MeV depending on proton fraction

## Context

This work extends the homogeneous nucleonic matter EOS from Du (2019) by adding a full nuclear statistical equilibrium distribution of nuclei, building on the formalism of Hempel (2010) and Shen (2010) for including nuclei in astrophysical EOS tables. The authors compare their results with existing EOS tables including LS220 (Lattimer 1991), SFHO (Steiner 2013), FSU21 (Shen 2011), NRAPR (Schneider 2019), STOS (Shen 1998), and FYSS (Furusawa 2011), finding significant differences in predicted nuclear mass numbers in the inhomogeneous phase. The probability distributions of EOS parameters enable uncertainty quantification in simulations of core-collapse supernovae and neutron star mergers, though the authors note that the nuclear distribution uncertainty is not fully explored and may impact protoneutron star evolution as discussed by Roggero (2018).
