# Order-of-magnitude physics of neutron stars

> We use basic physics and simple mathematics accessible to advanced undergraduate students to estimate the main properties of neutron stars. We set the stage and introduce relevant concepts by discussing the properties of "everyday" matter on Earth, degenerate Fermi gases, white dwarfs, and scaling relations of stellar properties with polytropic equations of state. Then, we discuss various physical ingredients relevant for neutron stars and how they can be combined in order to obtain a couple of different simple estimates of their maximum mass, beyond which they would collapse, turning into black holes. Finally, we use the basic structural parameters of neutron stars to briefly discuss their rotational and electromagnetic properties.

## Key claim

Using only undergraduate-level physics and simple mathematics, the authors show that the basic properties of neutron stars, including their maximum mass, can be estimated to within roughly 20% of exact numerical calculations, provided one accounts for both general relativity and the repulsive strong interaction between nucleons, though the simplest estimates ignoring these effects still yield physically meaningful order-of-magnitude results.

## Method

The authors first derive the equation of state of degenerate Fermi gases in both the non-relativistic and ultra-relativistic limits, then apply these to white dwarfs to obtain scaling relations for polytropic equations of state. For neutron stars, they impose beta equilibrium between neutrons, protons, and electrons, then combine the degenerate Fermi gas pressure with Newtonian gravity to estimate the stellar radius and maximum mass, and finally incorporate general relativity and strong-interaction repulsion through a simple incompressible-star argument using the Schwarzschild radius.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star nucleon density: \(n_{nuc} \sim 2 \times 10^{38} \mathrm{cm^{-3}}\), corresponding to mass density \(\rho \sim 3 \times 10^{14} \mathrm{g/cm^3}\) (derived from the pion Compton wavelength \(\lambdabar_\pi \sim 10^{-13} \mathrm{cm}\))
- White dwarf radius estimate: \(R_{WD} \sim 0.7 \times 10^4 \left(2Z/A\right)^{5/3} (M_\odot/M)^{1/3} \mathrm{km}\) for non-relativistic electrons
- Chandrasekhar mass estimate from simple scaling: \(M_{Ch} \sim 1.7 (2Z/A)^2 M_\odot\); exact polytropic solution gives \(M_{Ch} = 1.4 (2Z/A)^2 M_\odot\)
- Planck mass: \(m_P = (\hbar c/G)^{1/2} = 2.2 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{g}\)
- Neutron beta decay energy release: \(Q = 0.78 \mathrm{MeV} \approx 1.5 m_e c^2\)
- Electron density at transition to relativistic regime: \(n \sim 10^{30} \mathrm{cm^{-3}}\) for electrons, \(n \sim 10^{40} \mathrm{cm^{-3}}\) for nucleons

## Context

This pedagogical paper extends the classic order-of-magnitude approach used by Mahajan (2010) for everyday matter and by Silbar and Reddy (2004) for neutron stars, providing a unified treatment from atoms through white dwarfs to neutron stars. It enables students to understand the Chandrasekhar limit (first found by Stoner, as noted in Holberg 2009) and the Burrows and Ostriker (2014) estimate of the neutron star maximum mass, while clarifying why the simple incompressible-star argument, though unphysical (infinite speed of sound), yields results similar to those from causality constraints.
