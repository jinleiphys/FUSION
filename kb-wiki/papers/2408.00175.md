# The art of modeling nuclear reactions with weakly bound nuclei: status and perspectives

> We give an overview of the theoretical description of nuclear reactions involving weakly-bound nuclei. Some of the more widespread reaction formalisms employed in the analysis of these reactions are briefly introduced, including various recent developments. We put special emphasis on the continuum-discretized coupled-channel (CDCC) method and its extensions to incorporate core and target excitations as well as its application to three-body projectiles. The role of the continuum for one-nucleon transfer reactions is also discussed. The problem of the evaluation of inclusive breakup cross sections is addressed within the Ichimura-Austern-Vincent (IAV) model. Other methods, such as those based on a semiclasical description of the scattering process, are also briefly introduced and some of their applications are discussed and a brief discussion on topics of current interest, such as nucleon-nucleon correlations, uncertainty evaluation and non-locality is presented.

# The art of modeling nuclear reactions with weakly bound nuclei: status and perspectives

## Key claim

The paper reviews theoretical reaction formalisms for weakly-bound nuclei, emphasizing that while the Continuum-Discretized Coupled-Channels (CDCC) method and its extensions remain the most successful framework for incorporating breakup effects, the field still faces open challenges including nucleon-nucleon correlations, uncertainty quantification, and non-locality in optical potentials. The review is necessarily incomplete and biased by the authors' expertise, but aims to show how reaction theory has helped interpret experiments with exotic nuclear species.

## Method

The paper surveys several theoretical approaches for nuclear reactions involving weakly-bound nuclei, with primary focus on the CDCC method and its extensions to include core and target excitations and three-body projectiles. The Ichimura-Austern-Vincent (IAV) model for inclusive breakup cross sections is discussed, along with semiclassical descriptions of scattering. The review also covers the optical model approach, including the Feshbach formalism for polarization potentials, and presents the adiabatic polarization potential derived from second-order perturbation theory for deuteron scattering.

## Key numbers

- Deuteron binding energy: 2.22 MeV, with proton-neutron separation of about 3.8 fm
- Deuteron dipole polarizability extracted from sub-Coulomb elastic scattering on lead: 0.70 ± 0.05 fm³ (Rodning 1982)
- Complete fusion suppression for weakly bound stable nuclei (⁶,⁷Li and ⁹Be): approximately 20-30% compared to tightly bound nuclei
- Woods-Saxon imaginary diffuseness parameter for ⁶He+²⁰⁸Pb at 22 MeV: 1.91 fm, compared to 0.42 fm for ⁴He+²⁰⁸Pb at the same energy
- Woods-Saxon real potential depth for ⁶He+²⁰⁸Pb: 124.8 MeV; for ⁴He+²⁰⁸Pb: 96.44 MeV

## Context

This review extends the long-standing work on deuteron scattering (recognized since the 1960s as a prototype for weakly-bound projectile behavior) and the CDCC method originally introduced by Rawitscher (1974) and refined by the Pittsburgh-Kyushu collaboration (Austern 1987). The paper connects to the pioneering experiments by Tanihata (1985) that first revealed halo nuclei through large interaction cross sections at 800 MeV/nucleon, and to Kobayashi (1988) who found narrow momentum distributions from ¹¹Li fragmentation. The review enables future work on incorporating three-body projectile dynamics, uncertainty evaluation, and non-local potentials into reaction models for exotic nuclei near the driplines.
