# Soret and Dufour effects in hot and dense QCD matter

> The gradients act as invisible engines of transport, converting microscopic imbalances into macroscopic flows, and thus providing deep insights into the dynamics of physical systems. Thermal gradients do not merely drive the flow of heat, but they also set the microscopic constituents of the system into motion. In such scenarios, the constituents of the system not only transport energy but also diffuse collectively under the influence of these gradients. For the very first time, we present a first-principles investigation of the Soret and Dufour effects in hot and dense quantum chromodynamics (QCD) matter. We use the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation under the relaxation time approximation. By incorporating chemical potential and temperature gradients into the kinetic theory framework, we derive explicit expressions for the Dufour coefficient, which quantifies the heat flow due to concentration gradients, and the Soret coefficient, which describes the particle diffusion induced by thermal gradients. These coupled-transport phenomena are traditionally studied in multi-component classical systems at low energy scales. In this study, we follow quasiparticle models for the deconfined phase and the hadron resonance gas model for the confined hadronic phase in the context of heavy-ion collisions. This study provides novel insights into the thermo-diffusion and diffusion-thermo phenomena and opens avenues for incorporating such effects in hydrodynamic modeling and transport simulations of QCD matter.

# Soret and Dufour effects in hot and dense QCD matter

## Key claim
For the first time, the authors derive explicit expressions for the Soret and Dufour coefficients in hot and dense QCD matter using the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation under the relaxation time approximation, but the results are obtained within specific phenomenological models (quasiparticle model for the deconfined phase and hadron resonance gas model for the confined phase) rather than from first-principles lattice QCD, and the relaxation time is taken as momentum-independent.

## Method
The authors solve the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation under the relaxation time approximation, incorporating baryon chemical potential and temperature gradients into the kinetic theory framework. They decompose the single-particle distribution function into equilibrium and nonequilibrium parts, then derive integral expressions for the diffusion coefficient, Soret coefficient, Dufour coefficient, and thermal conductivity. For the quark-gluon plasma phase, they use a quasiparticle model with temperature-dependent effective masses; for the hadronic phase, they use the ideal hadron resonance gas model with all known hadrons and resonances from the Particle Data Group.

## Key numbers
- The Soret coefficient and Dufour coefficient are computed for baryon chemical potential values of μ_B = 0.05, 0.20, 0.40, and 0.60 GeV.
- For the hadronic phase, the hard-sphere scattering cross-section uses meson radius 0.2 fm and baryon radius 0.62 fm.
- In the QGP phase, the relaxation time is given by τ_R = 1/[5.1 T α_s^2 log(1/α_s)(1 + 0.12(2N_f + 1))] with fixed α_s = 0.5.
- The diffusion coefficient D/T and Dufour coefficient D_F/T^2 show saturation around T ≈ 0.13 GeV for μ_B = 0.20 GeV in the hadronic phase.
- For μ_B = 0.40 GeV, a slight dip appears at T ≈ 0.15 GeV in the hadronic phase diffusion and Dufour coefficients.

## Context
This work extends prior studies of thermoelectric transport in QCD matter by Singh et al. (2024, 2025), which investigated Seebeck, magneto-Seebeck, Nernst, Thomson, and magneto-Thomson effects, and by Monnai (2012), who identified baryon-heat cross conductivity in second-order hydrodynamics. The Soret and Dufour effects have been studied in classical multicomponent systems and magnetized electrodynamics plasmas (Garcia 2007), but this is the first application to hot and dense QCD matter. The results enable incorporation of coupled heat and baryon transport into hydrodynamic modeling and transport simulations of heavy-ion collisions, particularly relevant for low-energy experiments at facilities like NICA.
