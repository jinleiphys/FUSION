# A Hierarchical Bayesian Analysis of Neutron-Skin Thicknesses and Implications for the Symmetry-Energy Slope

> Neutron-skin thicknesses provide a sensitive probe of the isovector sector of the nuclear equation of state and its density dependence, commonly characterized by the symmetry-energy slope parameter L. A wide variety of experimental and observational methods have been used to extract neutron skins, ranging from hadronic and electromagnetic probes of finite nuclei to inferences from neutron-star observations. Each approach carries distinct theoretical and systematic uncertainties, complicating global interpretations and obscuring genuine physical trends. In this work we present a hierarchical Bayesian framework for the statistically consistent synthesis of heterogeneous neutron-skin constraints. The neutron-skin thickness is modeled as a smooth latent function of isospin asymmetry and nuclear size, while method-dependent bias parameters and intrinsic nuisance widths are introduced to account for unmodeled experimental and theoretical systematics. Focusing on the tin isotopes, we infer probabilistic neutron-skin trends from 100Sn to 140Sn, finding minimal uncertainties near stability and increasing uncertainties toward the proton-rich and neutron-rich extremes. We assess the consistency of nuclear energy-density functionals and obtain conditional constraints on the symmetry-energy parameters. The resulting posterior exhibits a pronounced compression of the symmetry-energy slope parameter L, reflecting the dominant sensitivity of neutron skins to sub-saturation symmetry pressure. We demonstrate that our hierarchical Bayesian framework provides robust and transparent constraints on the sub-saturation isovector sector of the nuclear equation of state.

## Key claim

The paper presents a hierarchical Bayesian framework that synthesizes heterogeneous neutron-skin constraints from multiple experimental and observational probes, finding that apparent tensions between different methods are not due to large systematic shifts but rather to differences in scatter and model dependence, and that the resulting posterior exhibits a pronounced compression of the symmetry-energy slope parameter L.

## Method

The authors model neutron-skin thickness as a smooth latent function of isospin asymmetry I = (N-Z)/A and nuclear size A^(1/3) using a four-parameter linear model, while introducing method-dependent bias parameters and intrinsic nuisance widths to account for unmodeled experimental and theoretical systematics. They compile 57 neutron-skin measurements from hadronic probes (proton elastic scattering, antiproton annihilation), electromagnetic probes (parity-violating electron scattering, dipole response measurements), mesonic probes (pionic atoms, pion reactions, coherent pion photoproduction), and astrophysical constraints (neutron-star observations), then sample the joint posterior distribution using an affine-invariant ensemble Markov Chain Monte Carlo algorithm. The latent neutron-skin systematics along the tin isotopic chain from 100Sn to 140Sn are used as a calibrated intermediary to assess the consistency of nuclear energy-density functionals and constrain the symmetry-energy slope parameter L.

## Context

This work extends previous efforts to combine neutron-skin extractions from different probes using weighted averages or least-squares fits, which implicitly assume all measurements are unbiased estimators with fully characterized uncertainties. The hierarchical Bayesian framework builds on the established correlation between neutron-skin thicknesses and the symmetry-energy slope parameter L, as developed by Brown (2000), Typel and Brown (2001), Furnstahl (2002), Centelles (2009), and Roca-Maza (2011). The results enable statistically consistent constraints on the sub-saturation isovector sector of the nuclear equation of state and provide a quantitative bridge between finite-nucleus observables and neutron-star properties such as radii and tidal deformabilities, as previously explored by Horowitz and Piekarewicz (2001), Lattimer and Prakash (2007), and Annala (2018).
