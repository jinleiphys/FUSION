# Rotational motion of triaxially deformed nuclei studied by microscopic angular-momentum-projection method II: Chiral doublet band

> In the sequel of the present study, we have investigated the rotational motion of triaxially deformed nucleus by using the microscopic framework of angular-momentum projection. The Woods-Saxon potential and the schematic separable-type interaction are employed as a microscopic Hamiltonian. As the first example nuclear wobbling motion was studied in detail in the part~I of the series. This second part reports on another interesting rotational mode, chiral doublet bands: two prototype examples, $^{128}$Cs and $^{104}$Rh, are investigated. It is demonstrated that the doublet bands naturally appear as a result of the calculation in this fully microscopic framework without any kind of core, and they have the characteristic properties of the $B(E2)$ and $B(M1)$ transition probabilities, which are expected from the phenomenological triaxial particle-rotor coupling model.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that chiral doublet bands appear naturally in a fully microscopic angular-momentum-projection calculation without any core, but only when a specific deformation parameter (β₂ = 0.30, γ = 30°) is chosen; the calculated bands become degenerate at I ≈ 15, while experimentally the degeneracy occurs already at I ≈ 11, and the calculated moments of inertia are too small compared with experiment.

## Method

The authors construct a triaxially deformed mean-field using a Woods-Saxon potential with monopole pairing, then perform full three-dimensional angular-momentum projection to restore rotational symmetry. The projected wave functions are obtained by solving the Hill-Wheeler equation, and electromagnetic transition probabilities are calculated without effective charges for E2 and with an effective spin g-factor of 0.7 × g_s,free for M1. For the odd-odd nuclei ¹²⁸Cs and ¹⁰⁴Rh, the authors block specific quasiparticle states originating from high-j orbits (h₁₁/₂ and g₉/₂) and in some calculations mix two independent signature configurations.

## Key numbers

- For ¹²⁸Cs: deformation parameters β₂ = 0.30, β₄ = 0.0, γ(WS) = 30° (corresponding to γ(den) = 24.0°); average pairing gaps Δ_n = 0.85 MeV and Δ_p = 1.07 MeV.
- For ¹²⁸Cs: the calculated minimum energy difference between the yrast and yrare bands is about 120 keV at spin I = 18.
- For ¹²⁸Cs: the estimated critical spin from cranking moments of inertia is I_c ≈ 12.9.
- For ¹⁰⁴Rh: deformation parameters β₂ = 0.25, β₄ = 0.0; pairing gaps Δ_n = 0.95 MeV and Δ_p = 0.76 MeV; estimated critical spin I_c ≈ 11.8.
- For the even-even core ¹²⁸Xe at γ = 30°: the cranking moment of inertia ratio J_y/(J_x = J_z) is approximately 2.4–2.6 (compared to a factor of 4 for irrotational flow).

## Context

This work extends the microscopic angular-momentum-projection approach previously applied to wobbling motion in part I of the series (Shimada et al., 2018) and to ground-state rotational bands (Shimada et al., 2015, 2016). It directly tests the phenomenological predictions of the triaxial particle-rotor model by Frauendorf and Meng (1997) and the selection rules for electromagnetic transitions derived by Koike, Starosta, and Hamamoto (2004). The results enable future microscopic investigations of how the ideal chiral geometry reflects in observable quantities, though the authors note that the triaxial projected shell model (Bhat et al., 2012) has been more successful in reproducing experimental data.
