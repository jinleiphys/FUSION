# Protoneutron stars within the Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone theory

> We study the structure of newly born neutron stars (protoneutron stars) within the finite temperature Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone theoretical approach including also hyperons. We find that for purely nucleonic stars both finite temperature and neutrino trapping reduce the value of the maximum mass. For hyperonic stars the effect is reversed, because neutrino trapping shifts the appearance of hyperons to larger baryon density and stiffens considerably the equation of state.

## Key claim

For purely nucleonic protoneutron stars, both finite temperature and neutrino trapping reduce the maximum mass, but for hyperonic stars the effect is reversed because neutrino trapping shifts the onset of hyperons to higher baryon density and stiffens the equation of state, leading to a larger maximum mass for the hot protostar than for the cold neutron star.

## Method

The authors used the finite-temperature Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone (BBG) many-body approach with the Argonne V18 nucleon-nucleon potential and phenomenological Urbana three-body forces, extended to include hyperons via the Nijmegen soft-core nucleon-hyperon potential. For finite-temperature calculations with hyperons, they treated hyperons as noninteracting particles (using the Frozen Correlations Approximation for nucleons), while zero-temperature results included interacting hyperons. The equation of state was computed under beta-equilibrium conditions for both neutrino-free and neutrino-trapped matter, and the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations were integrated to obtain stellar mass-radius relations.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of a cold purely nucleonic neutron star: 1.86 solar masses (central density 8.2 times saturation density rho_0 = 0.17 fm^-3)
- Maximum mass of a purely nucleonic protoneutron star at T = 30 MeV without neutrinos: 1.73 solar masses (central density 7.7 rho_0)
- Maximum mass of a purely nucleonic protoneutron star at T = 30 MeV with neutrino trapping: 1.65 solar masses (central density 8.1 rho_0)
- Maximum mass of a cold neutron star with interacting hyperons, neutrino-free: 1.25 solar masses (central density 11.5 rho_0)
- Maximum mass of a cold neutron star with interacting hyperons, neutrino-trapped: 1.48 solar masses (central density 8.8 rho_0)
- Maximum mass of a hyperonic protoneutron star at T = 30 MeV with free hyperons and neutrino trapping: 1.35 solar masses (central density 5.5 rho_0)
- Critical temperature for the liquid-gas phase transition in symmetric nuclear matter: approximately 16 MeV

## Context

This work extends the finite-temperature Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone approach previously applied to nuclear matter (Baldo et al. 1999, 2004) to the study of protoneutron stars, including hyperonic degrees of freedom. The results contradict earlier findings by Prakash et al. (1997), who reported that the maximum mass of protoneutron stars increases with entropy; the authors attribute this difference to the temperature dependence of the non-local single-particle potentials in their microscopic calculation. The finding that hyperonic protostars have larger maximum masses than cold hyperonic neutron stars suggests the possibility of metastable stars that undergo delayed collapse while cooling, as discussed by Prakash et al. (1997) and Pons et al. (1999). The low maximum masses obtained for hyperonic stars (below the canonical 1.44 solar masses) indicate that purely baryonic equations of state may be unrealistic and must be supplemented by a transition to quark matter, as explored in Burgio et al. (2002a, 2002b), Baldo et al. (2003), and Maieron et al. (2004).
