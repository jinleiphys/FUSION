# Interplay of Soft and Hard Interactions in Nuclear Shadowing at High $Q^2$ and Low x

> Nuclear shadowing corrections are dominated by soft interaction and grow as function of $1/x$ more slowly than the single scattering term, which has an essential contribution from hard interaction. Therefore, we predict vanishing nuclear shadowing at very low $x$ provided that $Q^2$ is high and fixed. At the same time, at medium and low $Q^2$, nuclear shadowing grows with $1/x$ as is well known for soft hadronic interactions.

## Key claim

The authors predict that nuclear shadowing will vanish at very low $x$ provided that $Q^2$ is high and fixed, because the soft interactions that dominate shadowing grow more slowly with $1/x$ than the hard single-scattering term; this contradicts the expectation that shadowing continues to increase with $1/x$ at all $Q^2$.

## Method

The authors model the virtual photoabsorption cross section on a nucleon as an average over hadronic fluctuations of the photon, weighted by probabilities $W^{\gamma^*}_h$, and extend this to nuclear targets using a Glauber-type multiple-scattering formalism. They classify fluctuations as either hard (small transverse size $\sim 1/Q^2$, small cross section) or soft (large size $\sim 1/\mu^2$, large cross section), and parameterize the soft-dominated mean square cross section $\langle (\sigma^{hN}_{tot})^2 \rangle$ using a soft Pomeron with intercept $\alpha_P(0) = 1 + \Delta(\mu^2)$, where $\Delta(\mu^2) \approx 0.1$. The proton structure function $F_2^p(x,Q^2)$ is taken from a fit to data, and the single free parameter $N$ is fixed by fitting nuclear shadowing data.

## Key numbers

* The soft Pomeron intercept is taken as $\Delta(\mu^2) \approx 0.1$, from Regge phenomenology of soft hadronic interactions.
* The effective Pomeron intercept describing the $x$-dependence of $F_2^p(x,Q^2)$ at high $Q^2$ is much larger: $\Delta_P(Q^2) \approx 0.3 \div 0.4$.
* The universal parameter $N$ is fixed by fit to $N = 3\ \text{GeV}^{-2}$.
* The observed nuclear shadowing demands $\langle (\sigma_{tot}^{hN})^2 \rangle / \langle \sigma_{tot}^{hN} \rangle \sim 20\ \text{mb}$.
* The hard component contributes to $\langle (\sigma^{hN}_{tot})^2 \rangle$ as $\sim 1/Q^4$, while the soft component contributes as $\sim 1/(\mu^2 Q^2)$, making the soft contribution the leading twist term.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier formalism (Kopeliovich and Povh, 1995) to predict the $x$ and $Q^2$ dependence of nuclear shadowing, and directly contradicts the claim of Ginzburg, Levin, and Mueller (1995) that a small transverse size $\sim 0.2\ \text{fm}$ (corresponding to a cross section $\sim 1\div 1.5\ \text{mb}$) is typical for diffractive dissociation. The results are compared to NMC data (NMC Collaboration, 1995a,b) for carbon and tin, and a scaling variable $n(x,Q^2,A)$ is introduced that collapses shadowing data for different nuclei onto a common curve. The work enables tests of whether nuclear shadowing indeed vanishes at very low $x$ at high $Q^2$, a prediction that can be checked at future colliders like HERA.
