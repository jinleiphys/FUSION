# Correlated Uncertainties in Monte Carlo Reaction Rate Calculations

> Context. Monte Carlo methods have enabled nuclear reaction rates from uncertain inputs to be presented in a statistically meaningful manner. However, these uncertainties are currently computed assuming no correlations between the physical quantities that enter those calculations. This is not always an appropriate assumption. Astrophysically important reactions are often dominated by resonances, whose properties are usually normalized to a well-known reference resonance. This insight provides a basis from which to develop a flexible framework for including correlations in Monte Carlo reaction rate calculations. Aims. The aim of this work is to develop and test a method for including correlations in Monte Carlo reaction rate calculations when the input has been normalized to a common reference. Methods. A mathematical framework is developed for including correlations between input parameters in Monte Carlo reaction rate calculations. The magnitude of those correlations is calculated from the uncertainties typically reported in experimental papers, where full correlation information is not available. The method is applied to four illustrative examples: a fictional 3-resonance reaction, $^{27}$Al(p,$γ$)$^{28}$Si, $^{23}$Na(p,$α$)$^{20}$Ne, and $^{23}$Na($α$,p)$^{26}$Al. Results. Reaction rates at low temperatures that are dominated by a few isolated resonances are found to minimally impacted by correlation effects. However, reaction rates determined from many overlapping resonances can be significantly affected. Uncertainties in the $^{23}$Na($α$,p)$^{26}$Al reaction, for example, increase by up to a factor of 5. This highlights the need to take correlation effects into account in reaction rate calculations, and provide insight into which cases are expected to be most affected by them. The impact of correlation effects on nucleosynthesis is also investigated.

# Correlated Uncertainties in Monte Carlo Reaction Rate Calculations

## Key claim
Monte Carlo reaction rate calculations that assume no correlations between input parameters can underestimate uncertainties by up to a factor of 5 for reactions dominated by many overlapping resonances, but reactions at low temperatures dominated by a few isolated resonances are minimally impacted by correlation effects.

## Method
The author developed a mathematical framework using Cholesky decomposition to generate correlated random variables for resonance strengths that have been normalized to a common reference resonance. The correlation coefficient for each resonance is calculated from the ratio of fractional uncertainties between the reference resonance and the normalized resonance, using the fact that normalized resonances must have larger uncertainties than the reference. The method was applied to four test cases: a fictional 3-resonance reaction, 27Al(p,γ)28Si, 23Na(p,α)20Ne, and 23Na(α,p)26Mg.

## Key numbers
- Uncertainties in the 23Na(α,p)26Mg reaction increase by up to a factor of 5 when correlations are taken into account
- For 27Al(p,γ)28Si, reaction rate uncertainties increase by up to a factor of 2.5 at high temperatures (above 300 MK) when correlations are included
- The reference resonance for 27Al(p,γ)28Si at Er(c.m.) = 391.3 keV has an uncertainty of 6.0% (from Powell 1998)
- For the fictional 3-resonance reaction, resonance 1 has factor uncertainty 2.5 and correlation ρ = 0.60, resonance 2 has factor uncertainty 1.55 and correlation ρ = 0.98, and the reference resonance has factor uncertainty 1.50 and correlation ρ = 1.00
- The 23Na(p,α)20Ne reaction rate at T = 0.01-0.06 GK is lowered by approximately 10% after correcting a sub-threshold resonance input

## Context
This work extends the Monte Carlo reaction rate formalism developed by Longland et al. (2010), Iliadis et al. (2010a, 2010b, 2010c), and summarized in Iliadis et al. (2015) and the Starlib reaction rate library. The method addresses a limitation of previous approaches that assumed no correlations between input parameters, despite the common experimental practice of normalizing resonance strength measurements to well-known reference resonances as described by Iliadis (2001). The framework enables more realistic uncertainty propagation in nucleosynthesis calculations and provides criteria for identifying which reactions are most affected by correlation effects.
