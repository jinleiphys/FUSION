# Deformation Energy Minima at Finite Mass Asymmetry

> A very general saddle point nuclear shape may be found as a solution of an integro-differential equation without giving apriori any shape parametrization. By introducing phenomenological shell corrections one obtains minima of deformation energy for binary fission of parent nuclei at a finite (non-zero) mass asymmetry. Results are presented for reflection asymmetric saddle point shapes of thorium and uranium even-mass isotopes with A=226-238 and A=230-238 respectively.

# Deformation Energy Minima at Finite Mass Asymmetry

## Key claim
By adding phenomenological shell corrections to the liquid drop model deformation energy, the authors obtain minima of the saddle-point energy at a finite (non-zero) mass asymmetry for binary fission of even-mass thorium and uranium isotopes, but the resulting mass numbers of the heavy fragment are systematically lower than experimental values by 6.7% to 10.7%.

## Method
The authors solve an integro-differential equation for the axially symmetric nuclear surface shape without imposing an *a priori* shape parametrization, minimizing the liquid drop energy under constraints of constant volume and a given deformation parameter. Phenomenological shell corrections, adapted from Myers and Swiatecki (1966), are added to the liquid drop deformation energy using a formula based on fragment volumes and magic numbers. The integro-differential equation is solved by successive approximations using a second-order Runge-Kutta method, with separate solutions for the left and right sides of the nucleus to handle reflection asymmetry.

## Key numbers
- The fissility parameter uses coefficients: $a_s = 17.9439$ MeV, $\kappa = 1.7826$, $a_C = 0.7053$ MeV, and $r_0 = 1.2249$ fm.
- Shell correction parameters: $c = 0.2$, $C = 6.2$ MeV, determined by fit to experimental masses and deformations.
- Minima of saddle-point energy occur at mass asymmetry parameters $d_L - d_R$ between about 0.04 and 0.085 for the studied Th and U isotopes.
- The mass number of the most probable heavy fragment from the calculated minima is close to 125, compared to experimental values ranging from 134 to 140 for uranium isotopes (a discrepancy of 6.7% to 10.7% for $A_H$).
- Results are presented for even-mass isotopes: thorium with $A = 226$–238 and uranium with $A = 230$–238.

## Context
This work extends the liquid drop model approach of Strutinsky (1962) and Cohen and Swiatecki (1963) by finding saddle-point shapes without a pre-defined parametrization, and adds shell corrections following the macroscopic-microscopic method of Strutinsky (1967). The results qualitatively reproduce the asymmetric mass division seen in fission, as previously discussed by Wilkins et al. (1976), but the quantitative agreement with experimental fragment masses from Wahl (1988) is limited by the phenomenological shell correction model. The method enables future improvements by incorporating more realistic shell correction models to better match experimental mass asymmetry data.
