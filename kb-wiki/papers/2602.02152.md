# Systematical decomposition of dimension-11 short-range neutrinoless double beta decay operators

> Neutrinoless double beta decay ($0νββ$) may receive sizable contributions from short-range physics beyond the Standard Model. We present a systematical classification of all tree-level ultraviolet completions of the dimension-11 short-range $0νββ$ decay operators, renormalizable scenarios with scalar and fermion mediators are considered. We identify eight distinct topologies and twenty-eight viable diagrams, from which all consistent UV completions are generated by imposing Standard Model gauge invariance. All these models involve a total of 61 new fields beyond the Standard Model and they typically feature fractionally charged fermions and exotic bosons such as dileptons, diquarks, and leptoquarks. We further study a representative model without colored mediators and analyze its implications for the $0νββ$ decay half-life and light neutrino masses. We find that current and future $0νββ$ decay experiments impose stringent constraints. Our systematic decomposition provides a general framework for exploring exotic short-range contributions to $0νββ$ decay in future experiments.

## Key claim

The paper claims to provide a complete classification of all tree-level ultraviolet completions of dimension-11 short-range neutrinoless double beta decay operators, but this completeness is conditional on restricting to renormalizable scenarios with scalar and fermion mediators and excluding models that would also generate the lower-dimension (dimension-9) short-range operators or the Weinberg operator at tree or one-loop level.

## Method

The authors used a diagrammatic approach to generate all tree-level topologies with eight external legs (four quarks, two leptons, two Higgs fields) for dimension-11 short-range neutrinoless double beta decay operators, then assigned Lorentz nature (scalar or fermion) to each line and fixed Standard Model gauge quantum numbers of internal fields by requiring gauge invariance at each vertex. They identified eight distinct topologies and twenty-eight viable diagrams after removing cases where the two external Higgs fields attach to a single three-point vertex (which would introduce a type-II seesaw mediator) and cases requiring non-renormalizable vertices. For each diagram and each operator type, they assigned external fields to legs and solved for the gauge charges of internal mediators, then merged identical fields across all model-diagrams.

## Key numbers

* Eight distinct tree-level topologies were identified for the dimension-11 operators.
* Twenty-eight viable diagrams were constructed after removing seven diagrams where both external Higgs fields attach to a single three-point vertex.
* A total of 61 new fields beyond the Standard Model appear across all models, including fractionally charged fermions and exotic bosons such as dileptons, diquarks, and leptoquarks.
* The decomposition yielded 487 model-diagrams with 3 internal fields, 2065 model-diagrams with 4 internal fields, and 2728 model-diagrams with 5 internal fields.
* The most stringent experimental limit on the $^{136}\textrm{Xe}$ decay half-life is $T^{0\nu}(^{136}\textrm{Xe}) > 3.8 \times 10^{26}$ yr from KamLAND-Zen at 90% confidence level, corresponding to an effective Majorana neutrino mass constraint $|m_{\beta\beta}| < (28-122)$ meV.
* The GERDA limit on $^{76}\text{Ge}$ half-life is $T_{1/2}(^{76}\text{Ge}) > 1.8 \times 10^{26}$ yr, implying $|m_{\beta\beta}| < (79-180)$ meV.

## Context

This work extends the systematic classification of short-range neutrinoless double beta decay operators from dimension-9 (studied at tree level by Bonnet (2012) and at one-loop level by Chen (2021)) to dimension-11, covering the twelve low-energy operators that cannot be obtained from dimension-9 operators. The analysis provides a general framework for exploring exotic short-range contributions to neutrinoless double beta decay in current and future experiments such as KamLAND-Zen and GERDA, and enables phenomenological studies of the 61 new fields (including fractionally charged fermions, dileptons, diquarks, and leptoquarks) at colliders and in low-energy experiments.
