# A New Heavy Flavor Program for the Future Electron-Ion Collider

> The proposed high-energy and high-luminosity Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) will provide one of the cleanest environments to precisely determine the nuclear parton distribution functions (nPDFs) in a wide $x$-$Q^{2}$ range. Heavy flavor production at the EIC provides access to nPDFs in the poorly constrained high Bjorken-$x$ region, allows us to study the quark and gluon fragmentation processes, and constrains parton energy loss in cold nuclear matter. Scientists at the Los Alamos National Laboratory are developing a new physics program to study heavy flavor production, flavor tagged jets, and heavy flavor hadron-jet correlations in the nucleon/nucleus going direction at the future EIC. The proposed measurements will provide a unique way to explore the flavor dependent fragmentation functions and energy loss in a heavy nucleus. They will constrain the initial-state effects that are critical for the interpretation of previous and ongoing heavy ion measurements at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider and the Large Hadron Collider. We show an initial conceptual design of the proposed Forward Silicon Tracking (FST) detector at the EIC, which is essential to carry out the heavy flavor measurements. We further present initial feasibility studies/simulations of heavy flavor hadron reconstruction using the proposed FST.

## Key claim

Heavy flavor hadron measurements at the future Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) can provide discriminating power between competing theoretical models of nuclear modification in deep inelastic scattering, specifically the absorption model and parton energy loss, which cannot be distinguished using only light flavor hadron measurements.

## Method

The authors performed fast simulation studies of a proposed Forward Silicon Tracking (FST) detector for the EIC, embedding track performance into PYTHIA8 simulations for hadron reconstruction studies. They reconstructed charm mesons ($D^{0}$, $D^{\pm}$, $D_{s}^{\pm}$) by clustering charged tracks matching their transverse decay length measured by the FST, requiring at least one kaon track per cluster. The authors scanned a three-dimensional parameter space of pixel pitch, trigger integration time, and material budget per layer to evaluate detector performance dependence.

## Key numbers

- Projected statistical uncertainties for the nuclear modification factor $R_{eA}$ in electron+gold collisions at $\sqrt{s} = 69$ GeV were evaluated for pions, $D$-mesons and $B$-mesons in the forward pseudorapidity region $1.0<\eta<4.5$, with integrated luminosity of 10 $fb^{-1}$ for electron+proton and 500 $nb^{-1}$ for electron+gold collisions.
- The proposed FST achieves better than 70 $\mu m$ spatial resolution for forward tracks, and charged tracks with low transverse momentum can obtain better than 2% momentum resolution.
- With 10 $fb^{-1}$ integrated luminosity in 18 GeV electron on 100 GeV proton collisions, clear signals of $D^{0}$ ($\bar{D^{0}}$), $D^{\pm}$ and $D_{s}^{\pm}$ were found using the FST with 30 $\mu m$ pixel pitch, 500 kHz trigger rate and 0.4% $X_{0}$ radiation length per detector layer.
- Reconstruction statistical uncertainties for $D$-mesons, determined with 10 $fb^{-1}$ integrated luminosity and including 95% tracking efficiency, are better than 0.1%.

## Context

This work extends the heavy flavor program at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) and Large Hadron Collider (LHC) by proposing measurements at the future EIC that can separate cold nuclear matter effects from hot nuclear matter effects. The authors note that competing theoretical models of nuclear modification in DIS reactions, specifically the absorption model and parton energy loss, cannot be distinguished with light flavor hadron measurements alone. The proposed FST detector and heavy flavor program at Los Alamos National Laboratory aims to provide key inputs for interpreting previous and ongoing heavy ion measurements at RHIC and LHC, and will contribute to the EIC detector design and physics case development.
