# Ergodicity of the $Δ_3$ statistic and purity of neutron resonance data

> The $Δ_3(L)$ statistic characterizes the fluctuations of the number of levels as a function of the length of the spectral interval. It is studied as a possible tool to indicate the regular or chaotic nature of underlying dynamics, detect missing levels and the mixing of sequences of levels of different symmetry, particularly in neutron resonance data. The relation between the ensemble average and the average over different fragments of a given realization of spectra is considered. A useful expression for the variance of $Δ_3(L)$ which accounts for finite sample size is discussed. An analysis of neutron resonance data presents the results consistent with a maximum likelihood method applied to the level spacing distribution.

## Key claim

The Dyson-Mehta Δ₃(L) statistic can be used to detect missing levels and contamination in neutron resonance data, but its sensitivity is limited by the finite sample size; the uncertainty in the fraction of missing levels determined from Δ₃(L) is of the same order as the missing fraction itself for spectra with N=100 levels, improving roughly as 1/√N.

## Method

The authors generated ensembles of Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) random matrices of varying dimensions, unfolded the spectra using the analytical semicircular level density, and computed Δ₃(L) exactly without numerical minimization using an explicit algebraic expression. They calculated the ensemble-averaged Δ₃(L), its standard deviation σ across different spectra, and the spectral standard deviation σ_s within single spectra, for pure GOE spectra, spectra with randomly removed levels (depletion), and spectra contaminated by uniformly distributed intruder levels. The Δ₃(L) results were compared with a maximum likelihood method applied to the level spacing distribution for the same depleted GOE spectra and for experimental neutron resonance data.

## Key numbers

- For GOE spectra, the ensemble standard deviation of Δ₃(L) is σ_e = 0.110, independent of L.
- The uncertainty σ for a single spectrum of N levels follows σ ≈ σ_e √(L/N), verified numerically with a fit giving σ = 0.1126 (L/N)^0.5003 − 0.0045 for 0 ≤ L/N ≤ 0.2.
- For a mixture of m=2 independent GOE spectra, the ensemble standard deviation is σ_e = 0.22, and the single-spectrum uncertainty follows σ = 2 σ_e √(L/N).
- For depleted GOE spectra with N=100, the recovered missing fraction x had uncertainties of 2.50% for x=2.91%, 2.81% for x=4.76%, 3.13% for x=8.26%, and 3.06% for x=9.91%; for N=300 the uncertainties dropped to 1.57%, 1.66%, 1.74%, and 1.77% respectively.
- The spectral standard deviation σ_s within a single pure GOE spectrum follows σ_s = σ_e (N − L)/N for the linear region.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of the Δ₃(L) statistic by Georgopoulus (1981), who provided empirical graphs for missing level effects, and by Brody (1981), who suggested a Poisson estimate for uncertainties. It also builds on the maximum likelihood method for missing levels developed by Agvaanluvsan (2003). The paper provides explicit error estimates for Δ₃(L) as a function of sample size and missing fraction, enabling quantitative comparison with neutron resonance data from ongoing high-precision experiments such as those by Koehler (2007). The results show that Δ₃(L) can distinguish between single and double sequences of levels but is not sensitive to the exact mixing fractions, and that the level spacing distribution via maximum likelihood gives comparable results for missing level detection.
