# The Single State Dominance Hypothesis and the Two-Neutrino Double Beta Decay of Mo100

> The hypothesis of the single state dominance (SSD) in the calculation of the two-neutrino double beta decay of Mo100 is tested by exact consideration of the energy denominators of the perturbation theory. Both transitions to the ground state as well as to the 0+ and 2+ excited states of the final nucleus Ru100 are considered. We demonstrate, that by experimental investigation of the single electron energy distribution and the angular correlation of the outgoing electrons, the SSD hypothesis can be confirmed or ruled out by a precise two-neutrino double beta decay measurement (e.g. by NEMO III collaboration).

## Key claim

The Single State Dominance (SSD) hypothesis for the two-neutrino double beta decay of Mo100 is tested by exact treatment of the energy denominators in perturbation theory, rather than the usual approximation of replacing lepton energies with their average value; the authors show that the SSD hypothesis can be confirmed or ruled out by precise measurement of differential decay rates, not just by comparing half-lives.

## Method

The authors performed exact calculations of the SSD hypothesis for 2νββ-decay of Mo100 to the ground state (0+) and excited 0+ and 2+ states of Ru100, using phenomenological beta transition amplitudes derived from measured log ft values of electron capture and single beta decay. They compared three approaches: SSD1 (approximated energy denominators with average lepton energy), SSD2 (exact energy denominators with full lepton energy dependence), and HSD (Higher State Dominance, where higher-lying intermediate states dominate). The single electron energy distributions and angular correlations of outgoing electrons were computed for each approach.

## Key numbers

- For the transition to the 0+ ground state of Ru100: SSD2 half-life = 7.15 × 10^18 years, compared to experimental value (6.82+0.38-0.53 ± 0.68) × 10^18 years (De Silva 1997)
- For the transition to the 0+1 excited state: SSD2 half-life = 4.45 × 10^20 years, compared to experimental value (6.1+1.8-1.1) × 10^20 years (Barabash 1995)
- For the transition to the 2+1 excited state: SSD2 half-life = 1.73 × 10^23 years, with experimental lower limit > 16 × 10^20 years (Barabash 1995)
- The energy difference E1 - Ei for the lowest 1+ intermediate state is -0.343 MeV, with the denominator D(εi,νj) ranging from 0.168 MeV (minimum) to 3.202 MeV (maximum)
- Angular correlation coefficients κ for the ground state transition: -0.627 (SSD) and -0.646 (HSD); for the 0+1 state: -0.487 (SSD) and -0.450 (HSD); for the 2+1 state: +0.153 (SSD) and +0.149 (HSD)
- Exact treatment of energy denominators reduces half-lives by 20 percent for transitions to 0+ states and by 300 percent for transitions to 2+ states

## Context

This work extends the SSD hypothesis originally proposed by Abad, Morales, Nunez-Lagos, and Pacheco (1984) and studied by Griffiths and Vogel (1992), who pointed out the dominance of the ground state of the intermediate nucleus for Mo100. The authors improve upon previous SSD studies by Civitarese and Suhonen (1998) that used approximated energy denominators, showing that this approximation can lead to significant overestimation of half-lives as demonstrated by Semenov, Simkovic, Khruschev, and Domin (2000). The work enables the NEMO III experiment to test the SSD hypothesis through precise measurement of single electron energy distributions and angular correlations, rather than relying solely on half-life comparisons.
