# A Quantum-Mechanical Equivalent-Photon Spectrum for Heavy-Ion Physics

> In a previous paper, we calculated the fully quantum-mechanical cross section for electromagnetic excitation during peripheral heavy-ion collisions. Here, we examine the sensitivity of that cross section to the detailed structure of the projectile and target nuclei. At the transition energies relevant to nuclear physics, we find the cross section to be weakly dependent on the projectile charge radius, and to be sensitive to only the leading momentum-transfer dependence of the target transition form factors. We exploit these facts to derive a quantum-mechanical ``equivalent-photon spectrum'' valid in the long-wavelength limit. This improved spectrum includes the effects of projectile size, the finite longitudinal momentum transfer required by kinematics, and the response of the target nucleus to the off-shell photon.

## Key claim

The authors derive a fully quantum-mechanical equivalent-photon spectrum for heavy-ion collisions that is valid in the long-wavelength limit, finding that the electromagnetic excitation cross section is weakly dependent on the projectile charge radius and sensitive only to the leading momentum-transfer dependence of the target transition form factors, but only for transition energies relevant to nuclear physics and in the mildly relativistic regime where the semi-classical Weizsäcker-Williams method shows significant deviations.

## Method

The authors start from the fully quantum-mechanical cross section for electromagnetic excitation in peripheral heavy-ion collisions derived in the first Born approximation in a previous paper (Benesh, Hayes, and Friar, 1995). They systematically test the sensitivity of this cross section to different parametrizations of the projectile elastic form factor (point, Bessel, Gaussian, and monopole forms) and to different target transition densities (Goldhaber-Teller collective model and shell-model form factors classified by SU(3) symmetry). Based on the finding that only the projectile rms charge radius and the leading-order momentum-transfer dependence of the target form factors matter, they construct simplified expressions for the form factors and integrate the cross section analytically to obtain closed-form equivalent-photon spectra for E1 and E2 transitions.

## Key numbers

* For a 20 MeV dipole excitation of a mass-41 target by a 197-Au projectile at approximately 50 MeV/nucleon, the finite-size projectile cross section is smaller by a factor of 2-3 compared to the point-projectile result.
* For relativistic projectiles with gamma = 2, the reduction in cross section due to projectile finite size is about 7% and decreases as projectile energy increases.
* At the lowest projectile energies, a variation of approximately 20% remains among different projectile form factor parametrizations once the charge radius is fixed.
* The minimum-impact-parameter cutoff used is b_min = (1.34 fm) * (A_P^(1/3) + A_T^(1/3) - 0.75*(A_P^(-1/3) + A_T^(-1/3))).
* The parametrized photo-neutron cross sections used lead to heavy-ion cross sections that are larger by about 5% for Au targets and about 10% for Co targets compared to using direct photo-neutron data.

## Context

This work extends the fully quantum-mechanical treatment of electromagnetic excitation in peripheral heavy-ion collisions from Benesh, Hayes, and Friar (1995) by systematically examining nuclear structure sensitivities. It contradicts the semi-classical Weizsäcker-Williams method (Fermi, 1924; von Weizsäcker, 1934; Williams, 1935) for mildly relativistic collisions with gamma less than 2-3, where significant deviations were found. The new equivalent-photon spectra enable model-independent predictions for electromagnetic processes in relativistic heavy-ion collisions that can be used with measured photoabsorption cross sections in the same fashion as the semi-classical expression, and they explain anomalously small measured cross sections in terms of single-photon exchange without requiring exotic multi-photon mechanisms as suggested by earlier semi-classical analyses (Hill et al., 1988; other authors, 1990s).
