# Collision dynamics at medium and relativistic energies

> Recent results connected to nuclear collision dynamics, from low up to relativistic energies, are reviewed. Heavy ion reactions offer the unique opportunity to probe the complex nuclear many-body dynamics and to explore, in laboratory experiments, transient states of nuclear matter under several conditions of density, temperature and charge asymmetry. From the theoretical point of view, transport models are an essential tool to undertake these investigations and make a connection between the nuclear effective interaction and sensitive observables of experimental interest. In this article, we mainly focus on the description of results of transport models for a selection of reaction mechanisms, also considering comparisons of predictions of different approaches. This analysis can help understanding the impact of the interplay between mean-field and correlation effects, as well as of in-medium effects, on reaction observables, which is an essential point also for extracting information on the nuclear Equation of State. A special emphasis will be given to the review of recent studies aimed at constraining the density behavior of the nuclear symmetry energy. For reactions at medium (Fermi) energies, we will describe light particle and fragment emission mechanisms, together with isospin transport effects. Collective effects characterizing nuclear collision dynamics, such as transverse and elliptic flows, will be discussed for relativistic heavy ion reactions, together with meson production and isotopic ratios.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that transport models are essential for connecting nuclear effective interactions to experimental observables in heavy-ion collisions, but emphasizes that the extraction of robust constraints on the nuclear Equation of State (EOS) depends critically on the interplay between mean-field effects and many-body correlations, as well as in-medium effects, which are not fully captured by any single theoretical approach.

## Method

The paper reviews results from semi-classical transport models, specifically Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) approaches and stochastic mean-field models, alongside molecular dynamics models such as Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics (AMD), applied to heavy-ion collisions from Fermi energies (tens of MeV/nucleon) up to relativistic energies (several hundred MeV/nucleon). For relativistic energies, fully covariant transport approaches based on effective field exchange models are employed, where nucleons and mesons propagate with self-energies that influence particle emission, collective flows, and in-medium nucleon-nucleon inelastic cross sections. The review compares predictions of different transport models for light particle and fragment emission, isospin transport effects, collective flows, and meson production.

## Key numbers

* Beam energies range from few tens of MeV/nucleon (Fermi energy domain) up to several hundred MeV/nucleon (relativistic energy regime)
* During the initial compression phase, densities up to two to three times the saturation density can be reached, depending on beam energy and reaction centrality
* The expansion phase occurs on characteristic time scales of the order of 10-100 fm/c
* Isoscalar collective vibrations in nuclei, collective flows, and meson production have constrained the EOS of charge symmetric matter for densities up to five times the saturation value (Dan2002)

## Context

This review extends the long-standing effort over the past 40 years to probe nuclear matter properties through heavy-ion collisions, building on prior work by Dan2002 on symmetric matter EOS constraints and by Baran2002, Tsang2004, and Betty2009 on isospin effects at Fermi energies. The paper connects to ongoing experimental and theoretical efforts aimed at constraining the density behavior of the nuclear symmetry energy, which influences phenomena from exotic nuclei structure and neutron star crust properties (Lattimer, Burrello2015, Horo) to relativistic heavy-ion reactions and compact star features (Xiao2009, Russotto2016, Baldo2016). The review enables future work by identifying the need to understand how many-body correlations from cluster and fragment formation impact global reaction dynamics and observables used to extract EOS parameters.
