# Method of determination of the most probable coordinate of formation of $α$-particle in $α$-decay

> Method of multiple internal reflections (method MIR) in description of $α$-decay of nucleus in the spherically symmetric approximation is presented in paper. In approach MIR the formalism of calculation of amplitudes of wave function, described moving of $α$-particle from the internal region outside with its tunneling through a realistic radial $α$-nucleus barrier of arbitrary shape, has been constructed at first time. The method MIR gives convergent values for the amplitudes of transmission and reflection, coefficients of penetrability and reflection, obtained in description of leaving of the $α$-particle relatively a potential with barrier in form of a number of rectangular steps (multi-steps potential), with tending this potential to the realistic $α$-nucleus one, and limit values of the amplitudes and coefficients are considered as exact concerning the realistic $α$-nucleus potential (without application of WKB approximation). In approach MIR a dependence of the penetrability coefficient and half-live on the location of the starting point, from where $α$-particle begins to move outside, is opened. From here, we define a radial coordinate of the most probable formation of the $α$-particle inside nucleus before its $α$-decay as such starting point, at moving of the $α$-particle from which outside the half-live, calculated by approach MIR, is maximally closed to its experimental value. For isotopes ${\rm Po}$ with different mass number $A$ we have obtained essentially closer values for half-live, calculated by such approach, to their experimental values in a comparison with half-lives obtained by approach WKB. This result is stable for all studied nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the Method of Multiple Internal Reflections (MIR) can determine the most probable radial coordinate of alpha-particle formation inside a nucleus before alpha-decay by finding the starting point for which the MIR-calculated half-life best matches the experimental value, and that this approach yields half-lives for Polonium isotopes that are "essentially closer" to experimental values than those obtained via the WKB approximation.

## Method

The authors construct a formalism for alpha-decay in a spherically symmetric approximation, where the realistic alpha-nucleus radial barrier is approximated by an arbitrary number of rectangular potential steps. They compute convergent values for transmission and reflection amplitudes, penetrability coefficients, and half-lives by taking the limit as the multi-step potential approaches the realistic barrier shape, without using the WKB approximation. The half-life is then calculated as a function of the starting radial coordinate R_start, and the coordinate yielding the closest match to experimental half-life is identified as the most probable formation point of the alpha-particle.

## Key numbers

- For Polonium isotopes (Z=84) with various mass numbers A, half-lives calculated by the MIR approach are "essentially closer" to experimental values compared to half-lives obtained by the WKB approach in Buck (1993).
- The paper reports that the tunneling time for an alpha-particle through a decay barrier is approximately 10^{-21} seconds.
- The method demonstrates convergence of the series sums for transmission and reflection amplitudes, with the total transmitted and reflected amplitudes satisfying the unitarity condition |sum A_T^n|^2 + |sum A_R^n|^2 = 1.

## Context

This work extends the MIR formalism previously developed by Maydanyuk (2000, 2002) for one-dimensional rectangular barriers and simple radial barriers to realistic alpha-nucleus potentials of arbitrary shape. It addresses the open question of whether interference between waves reflected from different boundaries invalidates the MIR approach for complex barriers, and provides exact solutions that coincide with the standard stationary method. The approach enables calculation of half-lives without the WKB approximation and offers a method to determine the most probable formation coordinate of the alpha-particle inside the nucleus, which was not possible with prior approaches such as those of Buck (1993).
