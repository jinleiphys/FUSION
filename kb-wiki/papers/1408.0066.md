# Collective phenomena in high-energy nuclear collisions

> I review experimental studies of collective phenomena in $\pA$ and A+A collisions presented in the Quark Matter 2014 conference.

## Key claim
The paper reviews experimental studies of collective phenomena in p+A and A+A collisions presented at the Quark Matter 2014 conference, emphasizing that the observed collectivity in small systems such as p+Pb may arise from either initial-state effects, final-state effects, or both, and that the current debate focuses on the effective mechanism behind this apparent collectivity.

## Method
The author summarizes experimental results from RHIC and the LHC on azimuthal harmonics (v1 through v5) measured via two-particle correlation methods and multi-particle cumulants in p+Pb and Pb+Pb collisions. The review also covers event-by-event flow fluctuation measurements using event-shape selection techniques, where events are classified by centrality and further divided according to the reduced flow vector qn from forward calorimeter transverse energy distributions.

## Key numbers
- v2 and v3 values in high-multiplicity p+Pb collisions are sizable at pT > 8 GeV (3-5%), reflecting a significant near-side ridge.
- Multi-particle cumulants v2{4}, v2{6}, v2{8} and v2{LYZ} agree within ±10% over a broad multiplicity range in p+Pb or Pb+Pb collisions.
- The r2 factor for v2 in ultra-central Pb+Pb collisions reaches 0.8, indicating factorization breaking of up to 20%.
- v2 peaks at around 1.5 GeV in ultra-central collisions, while other harmonics peak at 3-4 GeV.
- The difference between v2{4} and v2{6} is less than 1-2%, suggesting limited sensitivity to flow fluctuations given current systematic uncertainties.

## Context
This review extends the line of work on collective flow phenomena in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and the LHC, which has been successfully modeled by relativistic viscous hydrodynamics (Heinz 2013, Luzum 2013). The observation of similar collective behavior in high-multiplicity p+Pb collisions (CMS 2012) challenges the assumption that hydrodynamic descriptions require large system sizes. The results enable future studies of event-shape fluctuations, forward-backward asymmetry in flow vectors, and the response of the medium to jet energy deposition, as well as applications of event-shape selection techniques to other observables such as HBT correlations and chiral magnetic effects.
