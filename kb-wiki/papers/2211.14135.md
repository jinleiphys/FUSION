# Microscopic description of $α$, $2α$, and cluster decays of $^{216-220}$Rn and $^{220-224}$Ra

> Alpha and cluster decays are analyzed for heavy nuclei located above $^{208}$Pb on the chart of nuclides: $^{216-220}$Rn and $^{220-224}$Ra, that are also candidates for observing the $2 α$ decay mode. A microscopic theoretical approach based on relativistic Energy Density Functionals (EDF), is used to compute axially-symmetric deformation energy surfaces as functions of quadrupole, octupole and hexadecupole collective coordinates. Dynamical least-action paths for specific decay modes are calculated on the corresponding potential energy surfaces. The effective collective inertia is determined using the perturbative cranking approximation, and zero-point and rotational energy corrections are included in the model. The predicted half-lives for $α$-decay are within one order of magnitude of the experimental values. In the case of single $α$ emission, the nuclei considered in the present study exhibit least-action paths that differ significantly up to the scission point. The differences in alpha-decay lifetimes are not only driven by Q values, but also by variances of the least-action paths prior to scission. In contrast, the $2 α$ decay mode presents very similar paths from equilibrium to scission, and the differences in lifetimes are mainly driven by the corresponding Q values. The predicted $^{14}$C cluster decay half-lives are within three orders of magnitudes of the empirical values, and point to a much more complex pattern compared to the alpha-decay mode.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a fully microscopic relativistic Energy Density Functional (EDF) approach, with no free parameters adjusted to reproduce decay half-lives, can predict alpha-decay half-lives within one order of magnitude of experimental values for nuclei above lead-208, and that the differences in alpha-decay lifetimes are driven not only by Q values but also by variances in the least-action paths prior to scission.

## Method

The authors performed deformation-constrained Relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations using the DD-PC1 functional and a separable finite-range pairing interaction to compute axially-symmetric potential energy surfaces as functions of quadrupole, octupole, and hexadecupole collective coordinates. Dynamical least-action paths for alpha, two-alpha, and carbon-14 cluster decay modes were determined on these surfaces, with the effective collective inertia calculated using the perturbative cranking approximation and including zero-point energy and rotational energy corrections. The decay half-lives were then obtained using the Wentzel-Kramers-Brillouin (WKB) approximation for barrier penetration.

## Key numbers

- Predicted alpha-decay half-life for radon-216: 185 microseconds (experimental: 45 microseconds)
- Predicted alpha-decay half-life for radon-218: 35 milliseconds (experimental: 35 milliseconds)
- Predicted alpha-decay half-life for radon-220: 40.8 seconds (experimental: 55.6 seconds)
- Predicted alpha-decay half-life for radium-220: 60 milliseconds (experimental: 18 milliseconds)
- Predicted alpha-decay half-life for radium-222: 122 seconds (experimental: 38 seconds)
- Predicted alpha-decay half-life for radium-224: 5.7 days (experimental: 3.6 days)
- Predicted alpha-decay half-life for polonium-212: 0.2 microseconds (experimental: 0.3 microseconds)
- Predicted two-alpha decay half-life for radon-216: log10(T2alpha[s]) = 2.67, with branching ratio log10(BR) = -6.4
- Predicted carbon-14 cluster decay half-life for radium-224: log10(T14C[s]) = 15.87 (experimental: 15.86)
- Predicted carbon-14 cluster decay half-life for radium-222 (2D calculation): log10(T14C[s]) = 13.61 (experimental: 11.01)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous microscopic EDF-based studies of alpha decay in tellurium-104, xenon-108, polonium-212, and radium-224 (Mercier 2020, 2021) to a systematic survey of radon and radium isotopes above lead-208, and provides the first relativistic EDF-based predictions for two-alpha decay and carbon-14 cluster emission in this region. The results enable experimental searches for the predicted two-alpha decay mode, with radon-216 identified as the optimal candidate, and demonstrate that the relativistic EDF approach can describe fission, cluster decay, two-alpha decay, and alpha decay within a unified microscopic framework, complementing earlier Gogny EDF-based cluster decay calculations (Warda 2011).
