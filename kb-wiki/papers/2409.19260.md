# Exploring the Diversity of Nuclear Density through Information Entropy

> This study explores the role of information entropy in understanding nuclear density distributions, including both stable configurations and non-traditional structures such as neutron halos and $α$-clustering. By quantifying the uncertainty and disorder inherent in nucleon distributions in nuclear many-body systems, information entropy provides a macroscopic measure of the physical properties of the system. A more dispersed and disordered density distribution results in a higher value of information entropy. This intrinsic relationship between information entropy and system complexity allows us to quantify uncertainty and disorder in nuclear structures by analyzing various geometric parameters such as nuclear radius, diffuseness, neutron skin, and cluster structural features.

## Key claim
Information entropy can serve as a macroscopic measure to quantify the disorder and complexity of nuclear density distributions, including exotic structures like neutron halos and alpha clustering. The paper establishes a qualitative, intrinsic relationship between higher entropy values and more dispersed or disordered density profiles, but does not provide a specific, quantitative threshold or formula linking entropy to a given geometric parameter.

## Method
The authors computed the information entropy of nuclear density distributions derived from theoretical models. They analyzed how this entropy varies with geometric parameters such as nuclear radius, diffuseness, neutron skin thickness, and features of cluster structures for both stable and exotic nuclei.

## Context
This work extends the application of information entropy, a concept from information theory, to the field of nuclear structure. It builds on prior studies of nuclear density distributions and exotic phenomena like neutron halos and alpha clustering, aiming to provide a new, global observable for characterizing nuclear complexity. The approach could enable future quantitative comparisons between different theoretical models of nuclear structure and experimental density measurements.
