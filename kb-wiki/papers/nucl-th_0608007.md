# Excited-State Density Distributions of Neutron-Rich Unstable Nuclei

> We calculate densities of excited states in the quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA)with Skyrme interactions and volume pairing. We focus on low-energy peaks/bumps in the strength functions of a range of Ca, Ni, and Sn isotopes for J pi = 0+, 1-, and 2+. We define an "emitted-neutron number", which we then use to distinguish localized states from scattering-like states. The degree of delocalization either increases as the neutron-drip line is approached or stays high between the stability line and the drip line. In the 2+ channel of Sn, however, the low-lying states, not even counting surface vibrations, are still fairly well localized on average, even at the neutron drip line.

# Excited-State Density Distributions of Neutron-Rich Unstable Nuclei

## Key claim

The degree of delocalization of low-lying excited states near the neutron drip line depends strongly on both the isotopic chain and the multipolarity channel, and does not universally increase with neutron number; in the 2+ channel of Sn isotopes, low-lying states remain fairly well localized on average even at the neutron drip line, contrary to the trend seen in other channels and isotopic chains.

## Method

The authors used the quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA) with Skyrme interactions (parameter set SkM*) and volume pairing to calculate diagonal density distributions of excited states in spherical Ca, Ni, and Sn isotopes. They defined an "emitted-neutron number" Ne(k) as the integral of the difference between the excited-state neutron density and the ground-state neutron density outside a cutoff radius rc, and used this quantity to distinguish localized (quasibound) states from scattering-like (delocalized) states. Strength-weighted averages of Ne were computed for low-energy peaks in the strength functions for Jpi = 0+, 1-, and 2+ channels.

## Key numbers

- For the 0+ bump below the giant resonance in 50Ca, the average emitted-neutron number was 0.84 in a smaller computational space and 0.89 in a larger space, with individual Ne(k) ranging from 0.77 to 0.90 and 0.85 to 0.92 respectively.
- With a box radius of 25 fm, the average emitted-neutron number for 5 sample states in 50Ca was 0.86.
- The isovector giant quadrupole resonance of 58Ni had the smallest emitted-neutron number among giant resonances, with NeIS = 0.01.
- Giant resonances in Ca, Ni, and Sn isotopes near stability consistently had NeIS and NeIV values on the order of 0.5 or less.
- In the 1- channel of Ca and Ni, Ne increased from 0.1 to about 1 as neutron number increased toward the drip line.
- In the 2+ channel of Sn, Ne increased with N but low-lying states never became less localized than typical giant resonances; surface quadrupole vibrations had Ne(k) less than 0.1.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier investigation of strength functions and transition densities in medium-heavy spherical nuclei from one drip line to the other (Terasaki and Engel, 2006). It introduces a systematic measure of localization for excited states, which had not been previously studied, and builds on predictions of large-strength non-collective transitions to spatially-extended single-particle states in heavy nuclei (Hamamoto, 1996). The results enable future studies of many-particle many-hole state admixtures that could alter the emitted-neutron number, and provide a new window into excitations in exotic neutron-rich nuclei.
