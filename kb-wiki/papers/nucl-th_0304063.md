# Searching for space-time asymmetries in particle production

## Key claim
Unlike particle correlations, in contrast to identical particle correlations, are sensitive to relative space-time asymmetries in particle emission including the sequence of particle emission, but this sensitivity relies on the assumption of sufficiently small phase-space density such that only quantum statistics and final-state interactions influence the correlation.

## Method
The two-particle correlation function is derived using the Bethe-Salpeter amplitude for nonidentical particles, approximated by the stationary scattering solution at equal emission times in the two-particle center-of-mass system. The correlation function is then analyzed separately for positive and negative values of the projection of the relative momentum vector onto the pair velocity vector, yielding the ratio R+/R- which is sensitive to the sign and magnitude of the mean relative time delay between particle emissions.

## Key numbers
- For the K+K- system, the Bohr radius is a = -110 fm
- For the πK system, the Bohr radius is a = ±249 fm
- For the πp system, the Bohr radius is a = ±226 fm
- For the Kp system, the Bohr radius is a = ±84 fm
- For sufficiently relativistic pairs with γv > 0.5, the R+/R- ratio is sensitive to shifts in particle emission times of the order of a few fm/c
- The Coulomb penetration factor substantially deviates from unity only at k* < 22 MeV/c for two protons
- The s-wave singlet scattering length for nucleons is about 20 fm

## Context
This work extends the Kopylov and Podgoretsky (1972) formalism for identical particle interferometry to nonidentical particles, building on the final-state interaction approach of Lednicky and Lyuboshitz. The method was tested using the VENUS 5.14 event generator (Werner, 1993) for Pb+Pb collisions at SPS energy and the RQMD v2.3 transport code for AGS and SPS energies. The technique has already been successfully applied to study proton-deuteron correlations in heavy ion experiments at GANIL (Ghi, 1995; Nou, 1996; Gou, 1996), and enables measurement of mean relative delays in particle emission at time scales as small as 10^-23 s, including sensitivity to transient quark-gluon plasma and strangeness distillation scenarios.
