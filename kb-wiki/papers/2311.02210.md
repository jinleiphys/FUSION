# Causality violations in simulations of large and small heavy-ion collisions

> Heavy-ion collisions, such as Pb-Pb or p-Pb, produce extreme conditions in temperature and density that make the hadronic matter transition to a new state, called quark-gluon plasma (QGP). Simulations of heavy-ion collisions provide a way to improve our understanding of the QGP's properties. These simulations are composed of a hybrid description that results in final observables in agreement with accelerators like LHC and RHIC. However, recent works pointed out that these hydrodynamic simulations can display acausal behavior during the evolution in certain regions, indicating a deviation from a faithful representation of the underlying QCD dynamics. To pursue a better understanding of this problem and its consequences, this work simulated two different collision systems, Pb-Pb and p-Pb at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5.02$ TeV. In this context, our results show that causality violation, even though always present, typically occurs on a small part of the system, quantified by the total energy fraction residing in the acausal region. In addition, the acausal behavior can be reduced with changes in the pre-hydrodynamic factors and the definition of the bulk-viscous relaxation time. Since these aspects are fairly arbitrary in current simulation models, without solid guidance from the underlying theory, it is reasonable to use the disturbing presence of acausal behavior in current simulations to guide improvements towards more realistic modeling. While this work does not solve the acausality problem, it sheds more light on this issue and also proposes a way to solve this problem in simulations of heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

Causality violations are always present in hydrodynamic simulations of both Pb-Pb and p-Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV, but they typically occur in a small part of the system when quantified by the total energy fraction residing in the acausal region, and can be reduced by adjusting pre-hydrodynamic parameters and the bulk-viscous relaxation time definition.

## Method

The authors simulated Pb-Pb and p-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.02 TeV using a hybrid model chain: T_R ENTo for initial energy density, free-streaming pre-equilibrium evolution, MUSIC for second-order relativistic viscous hydrodynamics, Cooper-Frye particlization, and UrQMD hadronic cascade. They evaluated nonlinear causality conditions from Bemfica et al. (2021) at each fluid cell during the hydrodynamic evolution, classifying cells as causal, acausal, or indeterminate, and quantified violations by the energy fraction in acausal cells rather than the fraction of acellular area.

## Key numbers

- At the start of hydrodynamics, Pb-Pb collisions (0-5% centrality) show less than 20% of cells violating causality, carrying approximately 0.3% of the system's energy
- p-Pb collisions (0-5% centrality) show around 36% of acausal cells at the beginning of hydrodynamics
- Reducing free-streaming velocity to 0.85c reduces the energy in acausal cells by approximately 50% at the first time step for both systems
- The hydrodynamic evolution lasts around 12 fm/c for central Pb-Pb collisions and 4.47 fm/c for central p-Pb collisions
- The freeze-out temperature used was 151 MeV
- 2000 minimum bias events were simulated for each system
- Hydrodynamic variables were saved every 0.5 fm/c for Pb-Pb and every 0.25 fm/c for p-Pb

## Context

This work extends the findings of Plumberg et al. (2022), who showed that all considered hybrid models violated causality at initial hydrodynamic stages, and Chiu and Shen (2021), who investigated transport coefficient effects on causality conditions. The authors introduce energy-weighting as a more meaningful quantification of acausality than cell-counting, and demonstrate that the bulk viscosity term zeta/tau_Pi is the primary driver of violations. The results suggest that future Bayesian analyses should incorporate causality constraints when extracting transport coefficients, and that the bulk relaxation time parameter b_Pi could be optimized to reduce acausality without significantly affecting final observables like multiplicity, mean transverse momentum, or elliptic flow v_2.
