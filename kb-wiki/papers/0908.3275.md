# Nuclear Reactions

> Nuclear reactions generate energy in nuclear reactors, in stars, and are responsible for the existence of all elements heavier than hydrogen in the universe. Nuclear reactions denote reactions between nuclei, and between nuclei and other fundamental particles, such as electrons and photons. A short description of the conservation laws and the definition of basic physical quantities is presented, followed by a more detailed account of specific cases: (a) formation and decay of compound nuclei; (b)direct reactions; (c) photon and electron scattering; (d) heavy ion collisions; (e) formation of a quark-gluon plasma; (f) thermonuclear reactions; (g) and reactions with radioactive beams. Whenever necessary, basic equations are introduced to help understand general properties of these reactions. Published in Wiley Encyclopedia of Physics, ISBN-13: 978-3-527-40691-3 - Wiley-VCH, Berlin, 2009.

## Key claim

This encyclopedia entry provides a broad pedagogical overview of nuclear reaction mechanisms, from low-energy compound-nucleus formation to high-energy quark-gluon plasma formation, without presenting a single original experimental result or theoretical advance. The abstract's claim of covering "formation and decay of compound nuclei; direct reactions; photon and electron scattering; heavy ion collisions; formation of a quark-gluon plasma; thermonuclear reactions; and reactions with radioactive beams" is accurate but the treatment is necessarily introductory and qualitative, with basic equations introduced only "whenever necessary" to illustrate general properties rather than to report new findings.

## Method

The paper is a review-style encyclopedia chapter that systematically introduces nuclear reaction concepts through: (1) conservation laws (baryonic number, charge, energy/momentum, angular momentum, parity, isospin); (2) reaction kinematics including Q-value definitions and threshold energy calculations; (3) cross-section formalism with partial-wave expansion; (4) compound-nucleus theory with the statistical evaporation model; (5) resonance phenomena using a square-well potential model. Each section presents standard textbook derivations and references historical experiments (Rutherford 1911, Ghoshal 1950) rather than reporting new measurements.

- The internal nuclear potential V_0 is approximately -35 MeV for the square-well model

## Context

This work is an encyclopedia entry published in the Wiley Encyclopedia of Physics (2009) that synthesizes standard nuclear reaction theory as developed from the 1930s through the 1970s. It extends the compound-nucleus concept of Bohr (1936) and the statistical treatment of nuclear reactions, while referencing the experimental verification by Ghoshal (1950) showing that compound-nucleus decay is independent of formation mode. The chapter enables students and non-specialists to understand the basic framework for describing reactions from low-energy neutron capture to relativistic heavy-ion collisions, but does not present new results or enable specific next-step research.
