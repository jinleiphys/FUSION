# Angular Momentum Transfer in Vela-like Pulsar Glitches

> The angular momentum transfer associated to Vela-like glitches has never been calculated {\em directly} within a realistic scenario for the storage and release of superfluid vorticity; therefore, the explanation of giant glitches in terms of vortices has not yet been tested against observations. We present the first physically reasonable model, both at the microscopic and macroscopic level (spherical geometry, n=1 polytropic density profile, density-dependent pinning forces compatible with vortex rigidity), to determine where in the star the vorticity is pinned, how much of it, and for how long. For standard neutron star parameters ($M=1.4 M_{\odot}, R_s=10$ km, $\dotΩ=\dotΩ_{\rm Vela}=-10^{-10}$ Hz s$^{-1}$), we find that maximum pinning forces of order $f_m\approx10^{15}$ dyn cm$^{-1}$ can accumulate $ΔL_{\rm gl}\approx10^{40}$ erg s of superfluid angular momentum, and release it to the crust at intervals $Δt_{\rm gl}\approx3$ years. This estimate of $ΔL_{\rm gl}$ is one order of magnitude smaller than what implied indirectly by current models for post-glitch recovery, where the core and inner-crust vortices are taken as physically disconnected; yet, it successfully yields the magnitudes observed in recent Vela glitches for {\em both} jump parameters, $ΔΩ_{\rm gl}$ and $Δ\dotΩ_{\rm gl}$, provided one assumes that only a small fraction ($<10%$) of the total star vorticity is coupled to the crust on the short timescale of a glitch. This is reasonable in our approach, where no layer of normal matter exists between the core and the inner-crust, as indicated by existing microscopic calculation. The new scenario presented here is nonetheless compatible with current post-glitch models.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first physically reasonable model to directly calculate the angular momentum transfer in Vela-like pulsar glitches, finding that maximum pinning forces of order \(f_m \approx 10^{15}\) dyn cm\(^{-1}\) can accumulate \(\Delta L_{\rm gl} \approx 10^{40}\) erg s of superfluid angular momentum, which is one order of magnitude smaller than implied by current post-glitch models that assume disconnected core and crust vortices.

## Method

The authors construct a Newtonian model of a neutron star with spherical geometry, an n=1 polytropic density profile, and density-dependent pinning forces compatible with vortex rigidity. They integrate the pinning and Magnus forces along continuous vortex lines throughout the star to determine the critical lag for depinning as a function of cylindrical radius, then use a "snowplow" mechanism where excess vorticity accumulates in a thin sheet at the pinning peak until it is released simultaneously, causing the glitch.

## Key numbers

- Maximum pinning force: \(f_m \approx 10^{15}\) dyn cm\(^{-1}\) at densities \(\rho_m \approx 0.2\rho_0\) (where \(\rho_0 = 2.8 \times 10^{14}\) g cm\(^{-3}\) is nuclear saturation density)
- Accumulated superfluid angular momentum: \(\Delta L_{\rm gl} \approx 10^{40}\) erg s
- Interval between glitches: \(\Delta t_{\rm gl} \approx 3\) years
- Critical lag for depinning at the peak: \(\omega_{\rm max} = 0.01\) Hz, giving \(\Delta t_{\rm gl} = 3.1\) years
- Angular momentum released: \(\Delta L_{\rm gl} = 9.5 \times 10^{39}\) erg s
- Glitch energy: \(\Delta E_{\rm gl} = 6.7 \times 10^{41}\) erg
- Jump in angular velocity: \(\Delta\Omega_{\rm gl} = 1.3 \times 10^{-4}\) Hz (observed average for Vela: \(1.2 \times 10^{-4}\) Hz)
- Jump in angular acceleration relative to steady-state: \(\Delta\dot{\Omega}_{\rm gl}/\dot{\Omega}_{\infty} = 9.3\)
- Coupled fraction during glitch: \(Y_{\rm gl} = 0.05\) (i.e., less than 10% of total star vorticity coupled on short timescale)
- Standard neutron star parameters used: \(M = 1.4 M_{\odot}\), \(R_s = 10\) km, \(\dot{\Omega} = \dot{\Omega}_{\rm Vela} = -10^{-10}\) Hz s\(^{-1}\), superfluid fraction \(Q = 0.95\)

## Context

This work extends the toy-model of Anderson and Itoh (1975) and Pines and Alpar (1980) by incorporating spherical geometry, realistic density profiles, and density-dependent pinning forces, which were previously neglected. The model contradicts the assumption of disconnected core and crust vortices used in post-glitch recovery models by Alpar et al. (1984a, 1984b) and Jones (1990, 1991, 1993), instead adopting continuous vortex lines throughout the star as indicated by microscopic calculations of Zverev et al. (2004). The results enable direct testing of the vortex scenario for giant glitches against observations and are compatible with existing post-glitch recovery models, while also providing a framework for future dynamical simulations using the multifluid formalism of Andersson and Comer (2006).
