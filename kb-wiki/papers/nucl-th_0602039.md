# Femtoscopy in hydro-inspired models with resonances

> Effects of the choice of the freeze-out hypersurface and resonance decays on the HBT interferometry in relativistic heavy-ion collisions are studied in detail within a class of models with single freeze-out. The Monte-Carlo method, as implemented in THERMINATOR, is used to generate hadronic events describing production of particles from a thermalized and expanding source. All well-established hadronic resonances are included in the analysis as their role is crucial at large freeze-out temperatures. We use the two-particle method to extract the correlation functions, which allows us to study the Coulomb effects. We find that the pion HBT data from RHIC are fully compatible with the single freeze-out scenario, pointing at the shape of the freeze-out hypersurface where the transverse radius is decreasing with time. Results for the single-particle spectra for this situation are also presented. Finally, we present predictions for the kaon femtoscopy.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that pion HBT data from RHIC are fully compatible with a single freeze-out scenario, but only when the freeze-out hypersurface has a specific shape where the transverse radius decreases with time (negative parameter a in the Blast-Wave model), contradicting the simpler constant-time or constant-proper-time freeze-out assumptions.

## Method

The authors used the THERMINATOR Monte-Carlo event generator to simulate hadronic production from a thermalized and expanding source with single freeze-out, including all well-established hadronic resonances. They implemented the two-particle method to extract correlation functions, which allowed them to study Coulomb effects directly, and compared four different parametrizations of the freeze-out hypersurface: the Cracow model (constant proper time), standard Blast-Wave (constant laboratory time), and two generalized Blast-Wave models with positive and negative slope parameters a in the t-rho plane.

## Key numbers

* The best agreement with STAR data at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV was achieved with the Blast-Wave model using a=-0.5, T=165.6 MeV, mu_B=28.5 MeV, tau=8.55 fm, rho_max=8.92 fm, and v_T=0.311 c
* The Cracow model used parameters T=165.6 MeV, mu_B=28.5 MeV, tau=10.55 fm, and rho_max=7.53 fm
* The standard Blast-Wave model (a=0) used T=165.6 MeV, mu_B=28.5 MeV, tau=8.17 fm, rho_max=8.21 fm, and v_T=0.341 c
* The positive-slope Blast-Wave model (a=0.5) used T=165.6 MeV, mu_B=28.5 MeV, tau=9.91 fm, rho_max=7.43 fm, and v_T=0.407 c
* Inclusion of resonances increased the HBT radii by about 1 fm compared to calculations without resonances

## Context

This work extends the single-freeze-out approach of Broniowski (2001) and the Blast-Wave model of Schnedermann, Sollfrank, and Heinz (1993) by systematically studying the influence of the freeze-out hypersurface shape on HBT radii. It addresses the RHIC HBT puzzles identified by Lisa, Pratt, Soltz, and Wiedemann (2005), particularly the near-unity R_out/R_side ratio and the constant radii across beam energies. The results enable quantitative constraints on freeze-out geometry from HBT data and provide predictions for kaon femtoscopy that can be tested against future experimental measurements.
