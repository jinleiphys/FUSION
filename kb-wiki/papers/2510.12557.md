# Transport properties of stochastic fluids

> We study heat conduction and momentum transport in the context of stochastic fluid dynamics. We consider a fluid described by model H in the classification of Hohenberg and Halperin. We study both non-critical and critical fluids, and we investigate transport properties in two as well as three dimensions. Our results are based on numerical simulations of model H using a Metropolis algorithm, and we employ Kubo relations to extract transport coefficients. We observe the expected logarithmic divergence of the shear viscosity in a two-dimensional non-critical fluid. At a critical point, we find that the transport coefficients exhibit power-law scaling with the system size $L$. The strongest divergence is seen for the thermal conductivity $κ$ in two dimensions. We find $κ\sim L^{x_κ}$ with $x_κ=1.6\pm 0.1$. The divergence is weaker in three dimensions, $x_κ=1.25 \pm 0.3$, and the scaling exponent for the shear viscosity, $x_η$, is significantly smaller than $x_κ$ in both two and three dimensions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that numerical simulations of stochastic fluid dynamics (model H) using a Metropolis algorithm can extract transport coefficients via Kubo relations, and that at a critical point these coefficients exhibit power-law scaling with system size, with the thermal conductivity showing the strongest divergence.

## Method

The authors performed numerical simulations of model H (a stochastic fluid theory coupling a scalar order parameter to transverse momentum density) on a spatial lattice using a Metropolis algorithm that combines diffusive and stochastic updates. They extracted transport coefficients, shear viscosity and thermal conductivity, using Kubo relations applied to stress tensor and current correlation functions, studying both non-critical and critical fluids in two and three dimensions. For non-critical two-dimensional fluids, they isolated the self-advection contribution to the shear viscosity by considering only the ππ term in the stress tensor.

## Key numbers

- In two-dimensional non-critical fluids, the shear viscosity correction scales as δη = α log(L) with α = 0.0158 from numerical fit, compared to the theoretical prediction 1/(16π) ≈ 0.0198 (for ρ = η₀ = 1 in lattice units)
- At the critical point in two dimensions, thermal conductivity scales as κ ∼ L^{x_κ} with x_κ = 1.6 ± 0.1
- At the critical point in three dimensions, thermal conductivity scales as κ ∼ L^{x_κ} with x_κ = 1.25 ± 0.3
- The epsilon expansion at two loops predicts x_κ = 1.57 in d=2 and x_κ = 0.89 in d=3, with corresponding viscosity exponents x_η = 0.18 (d=2) and x_η = 0.07 (d=3)
- The Kawasaki approximation predicts x_κ = 1 and x_η ≈ 0.054 in three dimensions

## Context

This work extends previous numerical studies of model H by Chattopadhyay et al. (2024) that verified dynamic scaling and measured the dynamic exponent z, now focusing specifically on transport coefficient scaling. The results provide numerical confirmation of theoretical predictions from the epsilon expansion by Adzhemyan et al. (1999) and functional renormalization group studies by Canet et al. (2006, 2011) and Roth et al. (2023, 2024). The measured exponents enable future studies of critical transport behavior in expanding fluids, particularly relevant for understanding the QCD phase diagram endpoint as described by Son (2004).
