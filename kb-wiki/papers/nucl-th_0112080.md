# Strangeness in strongly interacting matter

> This talk is devoted to review the field of strangeness production in (ultra-)relativistic heavy ion collisions within our present theoretical understanding. Historically there have been (at least) three major ideas for the interest in the production of strange hadronic particles: (1) mass modification of the kaons in a (baryon-)dense environment; (2) (early) K+ - production probes the nuclear equation of state (EoS); (3) enhanced strangeness production especially in the (multi-)strange (anti-)baryon channels as a signal of quark gluon plasma (QGP) formation. As a guideline for the discussion I employ the extensive experience with microscopic hadronic transport models. In addition, I elaborate on the recent idea of antihyperon production solely by means of multi-mesonic fusion-type reactions.

## Key claim

This review argues that strangeness production in relativistic heavy ion collisions can be understood within hadronic transport models without invoking quark-gluon plasma formation, but the author notes that a significant discrepancy at AGS energies (where strangeness production is underestimated by about 30% in some models) leaves open the possibility that new nonhadronic physics may be involved.

## Method

The author reviews microscopic hadronic transport approaches (QMD, RQMD, URQMD, RBUU/HSD) that simulate heavy ion collisions using known vacuum cross sections for binary hadronic reactions, string fragmentation for high-energy inelastic collisions, and resonance production. The review compares these model predictions to experimental data from SIS, AGS, and SPS energies, focusing on kaon and (anti-)hyperon yields, and elaborates on a new proposal for antihyperon production via multi-mesonic fusion-type reactions.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of $K^+/\pi^+$ from primary string fragmentation at AGS to SPS energies is approximately 6-8% before hadronic rescattering starts
- Secondary hadronic interactions at SPS energies produce about the same number of strange quarks as primary p+p collisions, yielding a relative enhancement factor of 1.75 compared to p+p
- At AGS energies the experimental enhancement factor for $K^+/\pi^+$ is approximately 3, which cannot be fully explained by cascade-type calculations
- The chemical saturation timescale for kaons in a hadronic transport description exceeds 40 fm/c for equilibrium energy densities up to 2-3 GeV/fm^3
- At SIS energies the saturation time scale exceeds 10^3 fm/c for low energy densities
- The difference in kaon yield between a soft and hard equation of state shrinks from a factor of 3 (as originally proposed by Aichelin and Ko in 1985) down to only 40% when repulsive in-medium potentials for kaons are included

## Context

This review extends the long-standing discussion of strangeness as a probe in heavy ion physics, building on the foundational ideas of Kaplan and Nelson (1986) on kaon mass modification in dense matter, Aichelin and Ko (1985) on subthreshold kaon production as a probe of the nuclear equation of state, and Muller and Rafelski (1982) on strangeness enhancement as a signal of quark-gluon plasma formation. The work contrasts microscopic transport descriptions with thermal model analyses (such as those by Redlich in 2001) and addresses the unresolved question of whether antihyperon production via multi-mesonic fusion reactions (proposed by Greiner and Leupold in 2000) undermines the status of antihyperons as a clean diagnostic tool for QGP formation.
