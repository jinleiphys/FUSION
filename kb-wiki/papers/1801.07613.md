# Violent Nuclear Reactions: Large-Amplitude Nuclear Dynamic Phenomena in Fermionic Systems

> Violent nuclear collisions are open systems which require a non-equilibrium description when the process should be followed from the first instants. The heated system produced in the collision, can no more be treated within an independent-particle picture and additional correlations should be taken into account: they rely to in-medium dissipation and phase-space fluctuations. Their interplay with the one-body collective behaviour activates the transport dynamics: large-amplitude fluctuations and bifurcations in a variety of mechanisms appear, from fusion to neck formation till eventually freezing out the system into several intermediate-mass clusters. Starting from fundamental concepts tested on nuclear matter, a microscopic description is built up to address violent processes occurring in heavy-ion collisions at Fermi energies and in spallation reactions, and it is applied to experimental observables.

## Key claim

Violent nuclear collisions require a non-equilibrium description from the first instants, and the heated system can no longer be treated within an independent-particle picture; additional correlations from in-medium dissipation and phase-space fluctuations must be included, and their interplay with one-body collective behavior activates transport dynamics that can produce large-amplitude fluctuations and bifurcations.

## Method

The paper builds a microscopic description starting from fundamental concepts tested on nuclear matter, using a Boltzmann-Langevin approach to handle fluctuations in the dynamics of fermionic systems. This framework is applied to heavy-ion collisions at Fermi energies (approximately 10 to 50 MeV per nucleon) and to spallation reactions at relativistic energies, connecting the transport description to experimental observables.

## Key numbers

- Heavy-ion collisions at incident energies of around 10 to 50 MeV per nucleon correspond to the Fermi regime where the paper focuses
- Pauli blocking largely suppresses two-body nucleon-nucleon collisions at low energy (from the Coulomb barrier to around 15 MeV per nucleon)
- Two-body collisions become dominant in the participant-spectator regime from about 200 MeV per nucleon to relativistic energies

## Context

This work extends the historical line of research on nuclear fragmentation that began with Bohr (1936) and Serber (1947), and later developed through statistical approaches by Botvina (1985, 1990), Bondorf (1995), and Gross (2001). It addresses long-standing questions that statistical models alone could not resolve, such as the mechanism of light nuclide production in spallation, the relationship between bimodal fragment distributions and phase transitions in heavy-ion collisions, and the rapid kinematics of neck fragmentation. The approach enables connecting microscopic transport dynamics to equilibrium statistical descriptions, and opens the way to studying isospin effects and neutron-proton currents in nuclear reactions.
