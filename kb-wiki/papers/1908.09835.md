# Thermal free energy of large Nf QED in 2+1 dimensions from weak to strong coupling

> In 2+1 dimensions, QED becomes exactly solvable for all values of the fermion charge $e$ in the limit of many fermions $N_f\gg 1$. We present results for the free energy density at finite temperature $T$ to next-to-leading-order in large $N_f$. In the naive large $N_f$ limit, we uncover an apparently UV-divergent contribution to the vacuum energy at order ${\cal O}(e^6 N_f^3)$, which we argue to become a finite contribution of order ${\cal O}(N_f^4 e^6)$ when resumming formally higher-order $1/N_f$ contributions. We find the finite-temperature free energy to be well-behaved for all values of the dimensionless coupling $e^2N_f/T$, and to be bounded by the free energy of $N_f$ free fermions and non-interacting QED3, respectively. We invite follow-up studies from finite-temperature lattice gauge theory at large but fixed $N_f$ to test our results in the regime $e^2N_f/T\gg 1$.

# Thermal free energy of large Nf QED in 2+1 dimensions from weak to strong coupling

## Key claim

The free energy density of QED in 2+1 dimensions (QED3) at finite temperature is well-behaved for all values of the dimensionless coupling \(e^2 N_f / T\) and bounded by the free energy of \(N_f\) free fermions and non-interacting QED3, respectively; however, the authors caution that their numerical results using the in-medium polarization tensor in the naive large \(N_f\) limit may lose validity for \(e^2 N_f / T \gg 16\) at fixed but large \(N_f\), and they invite lattice gauge theory studies to settle this regime.

## Method

The authors compute the free energy density of QED3 to next-to-leading order (NLO) in the large \(N_f\) expansion using the gauge-fixed Euclidean action with Faddeev-Popov ghosts and a resummed photon propagator that includes the photon self-energy \(\Pi_{\mu\nu}\) at leading order in \(1/N_f\). The photon polarization tensor is decomposed into transverse and longitudinal components using a complete tensor basis, and the thermal sums are evaluated analytically where possible and numerically using Gauss-Legendre quadrature for the remaining integrals over Matsubara frequencies and spatial momenta. The vacuum contribution to the free energy is treated separately, revealing an apparent ultraviolet divergence at four-loop order that the authors argue becomes finite after resumming formally higher-order \(1/N_f\) corrections from the fermion self-energy.

## Key numbers

- The Debye mass in 2+1 dimensions is \(m_D^2 = \frac{2 e^2 N_f T \ln 2}{\pi}\).
- The free fermion contribution to the free energy density is \(f_{\rm fermion} = -\frac{3 N_f \zeta(3) T^3}{2\pi}\).
- The ghost contribution to the free energy density is \(f_{\rm ghost} = \frac{\zeta(3) T^3}{2 \pi}\).
- In the weak coupling limit (\(e^2 N_f / T \rightarrow 0\)), the thermal photon contribution \(f_{V,1}\) behaves as \(-\frac{\zeta(3) T^3}{4\pi} + \frac{e^2 N_f T^2}{96} + \mathcal{O}(e^4 N_f^2 T)\).
- In the strong coupling limit (\(e^2 N_f / T \rightarrow \infty\)), \(f_{V,1}\) behaves as \(-\frac{4 \pi^2 T^4}{45 e^2 N_f} + \mathcal{O}(T^5 / (e^4 N_f^2))\).
- At coupling value \(e^2 N_f / T \approx 16\), the normalized pressure is numerically \(3 N_f + 0.333(3)\).
- The apparently divergent vacuum energy at four-loop order scales as \(\mathcal{O}(e^6 N_f^3 / \epsilon)\) in the naive large \(N_f\) limit, which becomes a finite contribution of order \(\mathcal{O}(e^6 N_f^4)\) after resummation.

## Context

This work extends zero-temperature studies of QED3 by Pisarski (1984) and Appelquist (1988) to finite temperature for all values of the coupling, and generalizes techniques previously applied to scalar field theories and Gross-Neveu models in 2+1 dimensions by Romatschke (2019) and DeWolfe (2019). The results provide a non-perturbative benchmark for finite-temperature lattice gauge theory studies of QED3, such as those by Hands (2002), Strouthos (2008), and Karthik (2015), and may inform studies of dualities for related theories in 2+1 dimensions by Son (2015) and Karch (2016). The authors identify an unresolved regime for \(e^2 N_f / T \geq 16\) where their naive large \(N_f\) calculation may not be reliable, and they explicitly invite lattice simulations to test their predictions in this strong-coupling region.
