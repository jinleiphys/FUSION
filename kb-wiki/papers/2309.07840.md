# Recent Progress in Low Energy Neutrino Scattering Physics and Its Implications for the Standard and Beyond the Standard Model Physics

> Neutrinos continue to provide a testing ground for the structure of the standard model of particle physics as well as hints towards the physics beyond the standard model. Neutrinos of energies spanning over several orders of magnitude, originating in many terrestrial and astrophysical processes, have been detected via various decay and interaction mechanisms. At MeV scales, there has been one elusive process, until a few years ago, known as coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering (CEvNS) that was theoretically predicted over five decades ago but was never observed experimentally. The recent experimental observation of the CEvNS process by the COHERENT collaboration at a stopped pion neutrino source has inspired physicists across many subfields. This has vital implications for nuclear physics, high-energy physics, astrophysics, and beyond. CEvNS, being a low-energy process, provides a natural window to study light, weakly-coupled, new physics in the neutrino sector. In this review, we intend to provide the current status of low energy neutrino scattering physics and its implications for the standard and beyond the standard model physics. We discuss the general formalism of calculating the tree-level CEvNS cross section and present estimated theoretical uncertainties on the CEvNS cross section stemming from different sources. We also discuss the inelastic scattering of tens of MeV neutrinos that have implications for supernova detection in future neutrino experiments. We discuss how the CEvNS experiments can be used as a testing ground for the Standard Model (SM) weak physics as well as in searching for the Beyond the Standard Model (BSM) physics signals. Any deviation from the SM predicted event rate either with a change in the total event rate or with a change in the shape of the recoil spectrum, could indicate new contributions to the interaction cross-section.

## Key claim

This review argues that coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering (CEvNS), recently observed by the COHERENT collaboration at a stopped-pion source, provides a natural window to study light, weakly-coupled new physics in the neutrino sector, but any deviation from the Standard Model predicted event rate, either in total rate or recoil spectrum shape, could indicate new contributions to the interaction cross-section.

## Method

The paper reviews the tree-level formalism for calculating the CEvNS cross section, including the weak nuclear form factor derived from proton and neutron density distributions, and presents estimated theoretical uncertainties from nuclear, nucleon, and quark-level sources. It also discusses inelastic neutrino-nucleus scattering at tens of MeV relevant for supernova detection, and surveys how current and proposed CEvNS experiments (primarily at stopped-pion sources) can test Standard Model weak physics and search for beyond-the-Standard-Model signals such as non-standard interactions, neutrino electromagnetic properties, and sterile neutrinos.

## Key numbers

- The COHERENT collaboration announced the first detection of CEvNS at a 6.7σ confidence level using a CsI[Na] scintillating crystal detector at the Spallation Neutron Source (SNS).
- For a pion decay-at-rest source, the maximum neutrino energy is \(E_\nu^\text{max} = m_\mu/2\) where \(m_\mu = 105.65\) MeV; the spectrum includes a monoenergetic 29.8 MeV \(\nu_\mu\) and \(\nu_e\) and \(\bar{\nu}_\mu\) energies up to \(m_\mu/2\).
- Radiative corrections modify the expected neutrino fluxes from around the peak region by 3–4 permille.
- For a \(^{40}\)Ar target, the relative differences in CEvNS cross section predictions between different nuclear model calculations (Payne, Yang, Hoferichter, Helm, Klein-Nystrand) are less than 5% over the whole energy range \(E \lesssim 55\) MeV.
- The estimated total theoretical uncertainty on the CEvNS cross section for \(^{40}\)Ar is 0.58% at 10 MeV incident neutrino energy, 1.65% at 30 MeV, and 4.05% at 50 MeV.

## Context

This review extends the work of Freedman (1974), who first predicted CEvNS but called its experimental detection an "act of hubris" due to grave experimental difficulties, and the COHERENT collaboration (2017), which achieved the first detection. It also builds on prior studies of weak nuclear form factors from parity-violating electron scattering (Donnelly, 1989) and on detailed nuclear structure calculations by Payne (2019), Yang (2019), Hoferichter (2020), and Van Dessel (2020). The review enables future searches for beyond-the-Standard-Model physics using CEvNS, including non-standard interactions, neutrino electromagnetic properties, sterile neutrino searches, and dark sector physics such as vector portal models and axion-like particles.
