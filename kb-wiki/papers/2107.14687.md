# Finite-temperature infinite matter with effective-field-theory-inspired energy-density functionals

> Finite-temperature infinite matter is analyzed with the recently introduced effective-fieldtheory(EFT)-inspired YGLO (Yang-Grasso-Lacroix-Orsay) and ELYO (extended Lee-Yang, Orsay) functionals, which are designed to describe very low-density regimes in symmetric (YGLO) and in pure neutron (YGLO and ELYO) matter. The article deals with neutron matter and aims to verify whether the use of these functionals allows us to correctly incorporate finite-temperature effects. We compare our results for some relevant thermodynamical quantities with the corresponding ones computed with a chosen reference ab-initio model, namely the many-body-perturbation-theory scheme. We validate the reliability of both EFT-inspired functionals at least at rather low densities and not too high temperatures and we discuss the effects related to the effective mass. We conclude that, at the present stage, the ELYO functional, having a higher neutron effective mass around saturation (closer to ab-initio values), allows us to describe finite-temperature properties more satisfactorily, in better agreement with ab-initio predictions up to higher densities and temperatures, compared to YGLO.

# Finite-temperature infinite matter with effective-field-theory-inspired energy-density functionals

## Key claim
The ELYO functional, which has a higher neutron effective mass around saturation (closer to ab-initio values), describes finite-temperature properties of pure neutron matter more satisfactorily than YGLO, showing better agreement with many-body-perturbation-theory predictions up to higher densities and temperatures. However, this validation is limited to "rather low densities and not too high temperatures," and the YGLO functional's very low effective mass at higher densities causes strong deviations from ab-initio results.

## Method
The authors extended the zero-temperature EFT-inspired YGLO and ELYO energy-density functionals to finite temperatures and computed the entropy per nucleon, free energy per nucleon, pressure, and thermal index for pure neutron matter. They solved the density equation using Fermi-Dirac distributions with density-dependent effective masses and compared their results against many-body-perturbation-theory (MBPT) predictions from Keller et al., which used chiral two- and three-nucleon interactions up to next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order.

## Key numbers
- Neutron-neutron s-wave scattering length: a_s = -18.9 fm for pure neutron matter (used in both YGLO and ELYO)
- Neutron-neutron p-wave scattering length: a_p = 0.63 fm (used in ELYO, corresponding to the AV4 interaction)
- YGLO(FP) neutron effective mass at saturation density (rho_0 = 0.16 fm^-3): m_n*/m = 0.500
- ELYO neutron effective mass at saturation density: higher than YGLO, closer to ab-initio predictions (exact value not given in text)
- Modified YGLO case with hand-tuned splitting parameter: m_n*/m = 0.95 at rho_0 = 0.16 fm^-3
- ELYO splitting parameter W_1 = -0.176 (corrected value, not -0.163 as in Table II of Ref. drop2)
- YGLO(FP) splitting parameter W = -0.0764 (from Ref. yglonuclei, used throughout this work)
- Temperatures studied: up to 20 MeV
- Densities studied: up to approximately 0.16 fm^-3, with focus on regime below 0.05 fm^-3

## Context
This work extends the zero-temperature YGLO functional (Yang-Grasso-Lacroix-Orsay, introduced in 2016) and ELYO functional (extended Lee-Yang, Orsay, introduced in 2017) to finite temperatures, benchmarking against the many-body-perturbation-theory calculations of Keller et al. (2020). The study addresses the need for consistent finite-temperature equations of state for proto-neutron stars, core-collapse supernovae, and neutron-star mergers, where the common approximation of a constant thermal index (between 1 and 2) has proven inadequate (Bauswein et al., 2010; Constantinou et al., 2014). The results validate the EFT-inspired approach at low densities and moderate temperatures, while identifying the effective mass as the key limitation for the YGLO functional at higher densities, motivating future inclusion of p-wave contributions.
