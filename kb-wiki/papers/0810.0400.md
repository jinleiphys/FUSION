# Strongly interacting Fermi systems in 1/N expansion: From cold atoms to color superconductivity

> We investigate the 1/N expansion proposed recently as a strategy to include quantum fluctuation effects in the nonrelativistic, attractive Fermi gas at and near unitarity. We extend the previous results by calculating the next-to-leading order corrections to the critical temperature along the whole BCS-BEC crossover. We demonstrate explicitly that the extrapolation from the mean-field approximation, based on the $1/N$ expansion, provides a useful approximation scheme only on the BCS side of the crossover. We then apply the technique to the study of strongly interacting relativistic many-fermion systems. Having in mind the application to color superconductivity in cold dense quark matter, we develop, within a simple model, a formalism suitable to compare the effects of order parameter fluctuations in phases with different pairing patterns. Our main conclusion is that the relative correction to the critical temperature is to a good accuracy proportional to the mean-field ratio of the critical temperature and the chemical potential. As a consequence, it is significant even rather deep in the BCS regime, where phenomenologically interesting values of the quark-quark coupling are expected. Possible impact on the phase diagram of color-superconducting quark matter is discussed.

# Strongly interacting Fermi systems in 1/N expansion: From cold atoms to color superconductivity

## Key claim

The 1/N expansion provides a useful approximation scheme for strongly interacting Fermi systems only on the BCS side of the BCS-BEC crossover, not near unitarity or in the BEC regime; the relative correction to the critical temperature in relativistic superconductors is proportional to the mean-field ratio of the critical temperature and the chemical potential, making it significant even deep in the BCS regime where realistic quark-quark couplings occur.

## Method

The authors generalize the nonrelativistic attractive Fermi gas to N copies of two fermion flavors, bosonize the theory via a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation, and expand the thermodynamic potential, gap, and chemical potential simultaneously in powers of 1/N. They compute next-to-leading order (NLO) corrections to the critical temperature along the BCS-BEC crossover using explicit expressions derived from the gap and number equations, avoiding self-consistency problems by evaluating all derivatives at the mean-field values. For relativistic systems, they apply the same formalism to a class of Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL)-type models, introducing a new quantum number to ensure the Cooper pair transforms as a singlet under the extended symmetry, and determine the critical temperature via the Thouless criterion.

## Key numbers

- At unitarity, the NLO critical temperature based on expansion of 1/T gives T_c = 0.14 ε_F, compared to the Monte Carlo result T_c = 0.152(7) ε_F from Burovski (2006, 2008)
- The NLO result from Veillette (2007) for the critical temperature at unitarity is T_c/ε_F = 0.4964 - 1.31/N, which becomes negative when extrapolated to N=1
- The NLO result from Nikolic and Sachdev (2007) for the inverse critical temperature at unitarity is ε_F/T_c = 2.014 + 5.317/N

## Context

This work extends the 1/N expansion approach developed by Nikolic and Sachdev (2007) and Veillette (2007) for the unitary Fermi gas by calculating NLO corrections to the critical temperature across the entire BCS-BEC crossover. It critically compares the 1/N expansion with the Nozieres-Schmitt-Rink (NSR) theory (1985) and Monte Carlo simulations by Burovski (2006, 2008), finding that the 1/N expansion is reliable only on the BCS side. The relativistic application to color superconductivity in dense quark matter extends earlier work using NSR theory by Nishida (2005) and Abuki (2006), and the pseudogap approximation by He (2007). The formalism enables comparison of fluctuation effects in different pairing patterns, potentially modifying the QCD phase diagram for color-superconducting quark matter.
