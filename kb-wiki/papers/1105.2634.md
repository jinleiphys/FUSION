# On Alfvén hypothesis about nuclear hydromagnetic resonances

## Key claim

The paper re-examines Hannes Alfvén's old hypothesis that atomic nuclei can respond with hydromagnetic (MHD) vibrations, but shifts the focus from predicting resonance energies to using the observed mass-number dependence of giant resonances to *infer the magnitude of the intranuclear magnetic field*, provided that some observed giant resonances are indeed of hydromagnetic nature, a condition the authors explicitly state rather than assert as proven.

## Method

The authors construct a continuum-mechanical MHD model of an atomic nucleus as an incompressible, perfectly conducting sphere with a frozen-in magnetic field, deriving the frequency spectrum of node-free hydromagnetic oscillations via an energy method. They compute the mass-number dependence of the MHD resonance energies, $E_\ell(MHD) \propto A^{-1/3}$, and equate this to the empirical scaling of known giant resonances to solve for the internal magnetic field strength $B$. The model assumes a toroidal axisymmetric magnetic field configuration and uses standard nuclear parameters ($r_0 = 1.2 \times 10^{-13}$ cm, nucleon mass $m = 1.66 \times 10^{-24}$ g).

## Key numbers

- The spectral factor $s_\ell$ for hydromagnetic oscillations lies in the range $2 < s_\ell < 6$ for multipole degrees $2 < \ell < 4$.
- The empirical giant resonance energies satisfy $30 < E_{\rm GR} A^{1/3} < 90$ (in MeV), corresponding to $5 \leq E_{\rm GR} \leq 15$ MeV.
- From equating the theoretical and empirical scaling, the inferred intranuclear magnetic field strength is $3.0 \times 10^{17} \leq B \leq 9.0 \times 10^{17}$ G.
- The dipole magnetic field of a nucleon, estimated from the nuclear magneton $\mu_N = 5.05 \times 10^{-24}$ erg/G and quark-core radius $0.3 < R_N < 0.6$ fm, ranges from $5.0 \times 10^{16}$ to $5.0 \times 10^{17}$ G.

## Context

This work revives and extends Alfvén's original 1957 proposal (Alfvén, 1957) that nuclei can exhibit hydromagnetic resonances, which had been largely neglected in favor of the liquid-drop model (Gamow) and later shear-elastic models of giant resonances (Bohr, 1974). The paper connects nuclear MHD vibrations to asteroseismology of neutron stars (where similar MHD mode theory has been developed) and to the r-process nucleosynthesis in super-strong magnetic fields of magnetars. It suggests that the volume term of nuclear binding energy may have a magnetic origin and that internal magnetic fields could influence nuclear shapes and shell structure, opening a link between laboratory nuclear physics and magnetar astrophysics.
