# Complete experiments in pseudoscalar meson photoproduction revisited

> By exploiting the underlying symmetries of the relative phases of the pseudoscalar meson photoproduction amplitude, we determine all the possible sets of four double-spin observables that resolve the phase ambiguity of the amplitude in transversity basis up to an overall phase. The present results corroborate the original findings by Chiang and Tabakin [Phys. Rev. C 55, 2054 (1997)]. However, it is found that the completeness condition of four double-spin observables to resolve the phase ambiguity holds only when the relative phases do not meet the condition of equal magnitudes. In situations where this condition occurs, it is shown that one needs extra chosen observables, resulting in the minimum number of observables required to resolve the phase ambiguity to reach up to eight, depending on the particular set of four double-spin observables considered. Furthermore, a way of gauging when the condition of equal magnitudes occurs is provided.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the standard claim that four double-spin observables suffice to resolve the phase ambiguity of the pseudoscalar meson photoproduction amplitude in transversity basis holds only when the relative phases do not satisfy a condition of equal magnitudes; when this condition occurs, the minimum number of required observables can reach up to eight.

## Method

The author directly solves for the magnitudes and phases of the four basic transversity amplitudes \(M_j = r_j e^{i\phi_j}\) using bilinear combinations of observables grouped into beam-target, beam-recoil, and target-recoil asymmetries. By classifying all possible sets of four double-spin observables into four cases (2+2, 2+1+1, 3+1, and 4 from one group), the author derives the discrete phase ambiguities for each pair type and applies the consistency relation \(\phi_{12} + \phi_{23} + \phi_{34} = \phi_{14}\) to test whether the possible solutions are linearly independent or degenerate.

## Key numbers

* The completeness condition of four double-spin observables fails when the relative phases meet the condition of equal magnitudes; in that situation, up to seven chosen double-spin observables are needed to resolve the phase ambiguity.
* In the particular case where the relative phases vanish, eight chosen double-spin observables are required to resolve the phase ambiguity.
* For the (2+2) case, a pair of observables of type \((O^m_{n+}, O^m_{n-})\) leads to a four-fold phase ambiguity, while a pair of type \((O^m_{1\nu}, O^m_{2\nu'})\) leads to a two-fold ambiguity.
* For the (2+2) case with two pairs from item (i), there are \(4 \times 4 = 16\)-fold phase ambiguities; with two pairs from item (ii), \(2 \times 2 = 4\)-fold; with one from each, \(4 \times 2 = 8\)-fold.

## Context

This work extends and corroborates the original findings of Chiang and Tabakin (1997), who showed that eight carefully chosen observables suffice for a complete experiment, contradicting the earlier claim by Barker, Donnachie, and Storrow (1975) that nine observables were required. The paper also relates to the work of Moravcsik (1984) on resolving discrete phase ambiguities with a geometrical interpretation. The results enable model-independent extraction of baryon resonances from photoproduction data, an effort currently in progress by Workman et al. (2014), Workman et al. (2017), and Fernandez-Ramirez et al. (2018).
