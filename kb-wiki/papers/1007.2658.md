# Inference of weak nuclear collectivity from atomic masses

> I explore weakly-collective singly-closed shell nuclei with high-j shells where active valence neutrons and particle-particle correlations may be the dominant collective degree of freedom. The combination of large and close-lying proton and neutron pairing gaps extracted from experimental masses seems to charaterize the origin of the weak collectivity observed in Ni and Sn superfluids with $N\approx Z$. The trend of $E2$ transition strengths, i.e., $B(E2; 2^+_1\rightarrow 0^+_1)$ values, in these nuclei is predicted from proton and neutron pairing-gap information. The agreement with the Ni isotopes is excellent and recent experimental results support the trend in the Sn isotopes. This work emphasizes the importance of atomic masses in elucidating nuclear-structure properties. In particular, it indicates that many-body microscopic properties such as nuclear collectivity could be directly inferred from more macroscopic average properties such as atomic masses.

## Key claim
Weakly-collective E2 transition strengths in Ni and Sn isotopes with N≈Z can be predicted from proton and neutron pairing gaps extracted from atomic masses, using a newly defined "relative pairing gap" Δr and "pairing-gap collective strength" Δc. The claim is hedged by the assumption that only the fully-occupied f7/2 and g9/2 orbits contribute to the collective motion, and that this assumption may not hold for very neutron-rich Sn isotopes.

## Method
Proton and neutron pairing gaps (Δp, Δn) were extracted from experimental atomic masses using the symmetric five-point mass difference formula, which assumes pairing is the only non-smooth contribution to nuclear masses. A relative pairing gap Δr = |Δp² - Δn²|^(1/2) was defined, combining a resonant factor (Δp - Δn) and an energy factor (Δp + Δn). A pairing-gap collective strength Δc = 2Ω/Δr was then defined, where 2Ω = (2j+1) is the average particle number for the f7/2 (Ni) or g9/2 (Sn) orbits, and compared to experimental B(E2; 2⁺₁→0⁺₁) values.

## Key numbers
- Δr = 0.985 MeV for ⁵⁸Ni, decreasing to minimum values of Δr = 0.635 MeV for ⁶⁰Ni and Δr = 0.608 MeV for ⁶²Ni, then increasing to Δr = 1.555 MeV at ⁶⁸Ni.
- Δr = 0.498 MeV at ¹¹⁰Sn (sharp minimum), with values of 0.791 MeV at ¹⁰⁸Sn, 0.799 MeV at ¹¹²Sn, and a maximum of 1.300 MeV at ¹¹⁶Sn.
- Δr values for ¹⁰⁴Sn and ¹⁰⁶Sn are 1.076 MeV and 1.010 MeV, respectively.
- The B(E2; 2⁺₁→0⁺₁) value for ¹⁰⁶Sn is 13.1(2.6) W.u. from Ekström et al., showing a decreasing trend compared to ¹⁰⁸Sn.
- The energy difference between 2⁺₁ states in ⁵⁸Ni and ⁶⁶Ni is approximately 30 keV, while the difference in their Δr values is 150 keV.

## Context
This work extends the Grodzins-Raman empirical formula (Grodzins, 1962; Raman, 2001) relating B(E2) values to 2⁺₁ energies, proposing a new connection between atomic masses and collectivity. It addresses the conflict between large-scale shell-model calculations (Honma, 2004; Ansari and Ring, 2005) that predict an inverse parabolic B(E2) trend peaking at midshell, and recent experimental results by Jungclaus (2009) showing a minimum at ¹¹⁶Sn supporting the N=66 subshell closure. The work enables inference of nuclear collectivity from mass measurements, with the key prediction that ¹¹⁰Sn should show the strongest collectivity among Sn isotopes, requiring further experimental confirmation.
