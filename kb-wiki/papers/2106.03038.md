# Systematic study of surface properties for Ne, Na, Mg, Al and Si isotopes in a coherent density fluctuation model using the relativistic mean field formalism

> We have systematically studied the surface properties, such as symmetric energy, neutron pressure, and symmetry energy curvature coefficient for Ne, Na, Mg, Al, and Si nuclei from the proton to neutron drip-lines. The Coherent Density Fluctuation Model (CDFM) is used to estimate these quantities taking the relativistic mean-field densities as inputs. The Brückner energy density functional is taken for the nuclear matter binding energy and local density approximation is applied for its conversion to coordinate space. The symmetry energy again decomposed to the volume and surface components within the liquid drop model formalism to the volume and surface parts separately. Before calculating the surface properties of finite nuclei, the calculated bulk properties are compared with the experimental data, whenever available. The NL3* parameter set with the BCS pairing approach in an axially deformed frame-work is used to take care of the pairing correlation when needed. The deformed density is converted to its spherical equivalent with a two Gaussian fitting, which is used as an input for the calculation of weight function in the CDFM approximation. With the help of the symmetric energy for the recently isotopes $^{29}$F, $^{28}$Ne, $^{29,30}$Na and $^{31,35,36}$Mg are considered to be within the {\it island of inversion} emphasized {\bf [Phys. Lett. B 772, 529 (2017)]}. Although we get large symmetric energies corresponding to a few neutron numbers for this isotopic chain as expected, an irregular trend appears for all these considered nuclei. The possible reason behind this abnormal behavior of symmetry energy for these lighter mass nuclei is also included in the discussion, which gives a direction for future analysis.

## Key claim
The symmetry energy, neutron pressure, and symmetry energy curvature coefficient for Ne, Na, Mg, Al, and Si isotopes show an irregular trend across the isotopic chains, particularly for nuclei in the "island of inversion" region, even though large symmetry energies are obtained for certain neutron numbers as expected.

## Method
The authors used the Coherent Density Fluctuation Model (CDFM) with relativistic mean-field (RMF) densities as input, employing the NL3* parameter set with BCS pairing in an axially deformed framework. The deformed densities were converted to spherical equivalents using a two-Gaussian fitting procedure, and the Brückner energy density functional was used for nuclear matter binding energy with local density approximation applied for conversion to coordinate space. The symmetry energy was further decomposed into volume and surface components within the liquid drop model formalism.

## Key numbers
- The expected neutron drip-lines for the isotopic chains are at mass number A = 34 for Ne, A = 39 for Na, A = 40 for Mg, A = 47 for Al, and A = 48 for Si.
- Shape transitions occur at neutron number N = 14 for Ne, N = 20 for Na and Mg, and N = 28 for Si isotopes.
- The two-Gaussian fitting coefficients c1, a1, c2, and a2 are tabulated for all isotopes; for example, for 20Ne: c1 = -3.04522, a1 = 0.333589, c2 = 3.41182, a2 = 0.313962.
- The parameter gamma = 0.3 was used in the density dependence of symmetry energy, following reference Anto18.

## Context
This work extends the CDFM approach previously applied by Bhuyan et al. (2018, 2020) and Antonov et al. to lighter-mass nuclei in the Ne, Na, Mg, Al, and Si isotopic chains. The study specifically addresses the "island of inversion" region identified in Phys. Lett. B 772, 529 (2017), where experimental binding energies deviate from shell model calculations, and where Patra and Praharaj (1991) predicted large ground-state deformations later verified by Tanihata et al. The results enable future analysis of the abnormal behavior of symmetry energy in lighter-mass nuclei and provide input for neutron star equation of state studies through the connection between neutron skin thickness and symmetry energy.
