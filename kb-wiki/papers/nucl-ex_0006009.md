# On the reliability of the theoretical internal conversion coefficients

> Possible sources of uncertainties in the calculations of the internal conversion coefficients are studied. The uncertainties induced by them are estimated.

## Key claim

The uncertainties in theoretical internal conversion coefficients (ICC) are systematically estimated for the first time, but the authors caution that for precise experimental data (accuracy of a few percent), these uncertainties must be included in the analysis, and that the "best" ICC values come from the Hartree-Fock atomic model, which is not yet available in tabulated form.

## Method

The authors catalogued and numerically evaluated seven distinct sources of uncertainty in ICC calculations: atomic model choice, nuclear deformation and nonsphericity, higher-order perturbation theory effects, atomic binding energy errors, isotopic mass dependence, chemical environment effects, and intranuclear conversion (nuclear structure effect). For each source, they performed systematic calculations across a range of atomic numbers (Z=26 to Z=87), transition energies (30-500 keV), multipolarities (M1-M4, E1-E4), and atomic shells, quantifying the maximum percent change in ICC induced by varying the relevant parameter within realistic bounds.

## Key numbers

- For the atomic model, differences between Hartree-Fock (HF) and Hartree-Fock-Slater (HFS) ICC varied from less than 1% on the K-shell, 2-3% on the L-subshells, up to approximately 30% on the outermost shell (based on 57 experimental data items across 8 transitions in 6 isotopes).
- Vacuum polarization changes ICC by less than 0.1% for Z=26 and less than 1% for Z=80.
- For the K-shell of Cs-55 at 50 keV transition energy, varying the binding energy by +/-10 eV changed ICC by up to 0.46% (for E4 multipolarity).
- For the K-shell of Fr-87 at 200 keV, varying the mass number by +/-10 changed ICC by up to 0.56% (for M1 multipolarity).
- Chemical effects on total ICC were at most 0.7% for Fe-26, 0.5% for Br-35 and Cs-55, and 0.1-0.2% for I-53, Sm-62, Os-76, At-85, and Fr-87.
- For non-hindered transitions, intranuclear conversion changed ICC from negligible (approximately 10^-2%) for low Z and most multipolarities, up to 10% for high Z and M1 multipolarity.
- For hindered transitions, intranuclear conversion changed ICC by a factor of up to 20 (e.g., the 84 keV E1 transition in Pa-231 with hindrance factor 9x10^5).

## Context

This work systematizes and quantifies uncertainties that were previously only partially addressed in the major ICC tables of Rose (1978), Band (1978), and Hager (1968), and extends the analysis to include chemical and isotopic effects often neglected in earlier studies. The results enable more realistic error propagation in spin-parity assignments and multipole mixing analyses, and recommend that for precise work, users should calculate ICC directly using the Hartree-Fock model (as demonstrated by Band (1990) and Dragoun (1981)) rather than relying on interpolated tables, especially near shell thresholds where discontinuities occur.
