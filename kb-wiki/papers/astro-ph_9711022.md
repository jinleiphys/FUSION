# Nucleosynthesis in Low- and Intermediate-Mass Stars: An Overview

> An overview of the main phases of the evolution of low- and intermediate-mass stars is presented, and the different types of nucleosynthesis operating from the pre-main sequence up to and including the asymptotic giant branch phase described. The surface abundance modifications brought by each nucleosynthesis process is also briefly discussed.

## Key claim

Low- and intermediate-mass stars (0.5 to 6-8 solar masses) contribute to galactic chemical enrichment through multiple nucleosynthesis processes operating from the pre-main sequence through the asymptotic giant branch phase, but quantitative predictions still disagree with observations due to poor understanding of mixing processes and mass loss rates.

## Method

The paper reviews the structural evolution of single low- and intermediate-mass stars across five phases (pre-main sequence, main sequence, red giant branch, core helium burning, and asymptotic giant branch) and describes the nucleosynthesis operating in each phase. It identifies four non-explosive hydrogen-burning modes (pp-chains, cold CNO cycles, NeNa and MgAl chains) and analyzes the helium-burning shell nucleosynthesis during thermal pulses on the AGB, including the production of neutrons via the 13C(alpha,n)16O reaction for s-process elements.

## Key numbers

- Stars with initial masses between ~0.08 and ~0.5 solar masses end as He white dwarfs; stars between ~0.5 and 6-8 solar masses end as C-O white dwarfs
- Hydrogen ignites in the core at T6 ~ 12-25 (where T6 is temperature in units of 10^6 K)
- The interpulse period on the AGB lasts several 10^4 years, while thermal pulses liberate 10^2 to 10^6 times the energy provided by the H-burning shell over several tens of years
- The C-O core mass at the AGB phase ranges between 0.5 and 1.2 solar masses
- The nucleosynthesis and energy production on the AGB are confined to a region comprising less than 3% of the total mass of the star
- Hot bottom burning activates at temperatures above T6 ~ 50 in stars more massive than about 4 solar masses
- The NeNa and MgAl chains become mainly active at T6 > 40

## Context

This invited review extends the work of Maeder and Meynet (1989) on stellar evolution mass limits and the nucleosynthesis reviews by Arnould and Mowlavi (1993) and Arnould, Mowlavi and Champagne (1995). It synthesizes findings on surface abundance modifications from first and second dredge-ups, third dredge-up in AGB stars, and hot bottom burning, while identifying persistent disagreements between model predictions and observations that point to shortcomings in mixing prescriptions and mass loss rates. The paper enables future work on constraining the poorly understood transition from the AGB to the post-AGB phase and the final yields of low- and intermediate-mass stars.
