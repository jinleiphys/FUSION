# Mass tomography at different momentum ranges in quark-gluon plasma

> We here show that at lower momentum (i.e., $p_\perp \sim 10$GeV) single particle suppression for different types of probes exhibit a clear mass hierarchy, which is a direct consequence of the differences in the energy loss, rather than the differences in the initial distributions. On the other hand, we predict that the mass hierarchy is not expected at high momentum (i.e., $p_\perp \sim 100$GeV); i.e., while we surprisingly predict that suppression for charged hadrons will be somewhat smaller than the suppression for heavy mesons, we find that this difference will be a consequence of fragmentation functions, not the finite mass effects. That is, apart from the fragmentation functions, the probes of different masses exhibit nearly the same suppression in the high momentum region. We also argue that the same insensitivity on the probe types also appears for jets. In particular, the experimental data in the momentum regions where they exist for both types of probes, show similar suppressions of charged hadrons and inclusive jet data. Interestingly, we also find that our state-of-the-art suppression predictions for high momentum single particles are also in agreement with the jet suppression data, where the reasons behind this agreement yet remain to be understood. Finally, the available jet data also show (though with large error bars) an overlap between b jets (heavy) and inclusive jets (light) probes. Consequently, our results suggest that single particles in the momentum region below 50 GeV present an excellent tool for mass tomography, while high momentum single particles and (possibly) jets are somewhat insensitive to the details of the interaction with quark-gluon plasma.

## Key claim
The paper shows that at lower momentum (p⊥ ~ 10 GeV) single-particle suppression exhibits a clear mass hierarchy due to differences in energy loss, not initial distributions; however, at high momentum (p⊥ ~ 100 GeV) this mass hierarchy is not expected, and any difference between charged hadrons and heavy mesons arises from fragmentation functions rather than finite mass effects.

## Method
The authors used a state-of-the-art dynamical energy loss formalism that removes the static approximation and treats both radiative and collisional energy loss in a finite-size dynamical QCD medium, including finite magnetic mass, running coupling, path-length fluctuations, and multi-gluon fluctuations. They computed the nuclear modification factor R_AA for light hadrons, D mesons, B mesons, and non-prompt J/psi in Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV and 5.02 TeV, using no free parameters (all parameters from standard literature values). Predictions were generated as functions of transverse momentum and number of participants, and compared with available experimental data from ATLAS and CMS.

## Key numbers
- At lower momentum (p⊥ ~ 10 GeV), clear mass hierarchy is observed: D meson and non-prompt J/psi suppression differ significantly, with the difference dominated by energy loss rather than initial distributions (Fig. 2 left panel).
- At high momentum (p⊥ ~ 100 GeV), predictions for charged hadrons, D mesons, and B mesons nearly overlap (Fig. 1 right panel, 60 < p⊥ < 95 GeV region).
- For p⊥ > 100 GeV at 5.02 TeV, charged hadron R_AA is predicted to be more than 10% higher than heavy meson R_AA at p⊥ > 150 GeV, attributed to fragmentation function effects (Fig. 3 left panel).
- The same suppression predictions apply to both 2.76 TeV and 5.02 TeV collision energies (Fig. 4).
- Single particle and jet suppression data show similar R_AA values within error bars (Fig. 5 left panel, comparing ATLAS and CMS data).
- Theoretical single-particle predictions agree with inclusive jet and b-jet data at high momentum (Fig. 6 and Fig. 7).

## Context
This work extends the dynamical energy loss formalism of Djordjevic (2012) and Djordjevic and Heinz (2008) to systematically test mass tomography across a wide momentum range, contradicting the earlier expectation from AdS-CFT approaches (Horowitz 2010, 2011) that a clear mass hierarchy persists at high momentum. It also clarifies that the difference between D meson and non-prompt J/psi suppression at lower momentum is due to energy loss, not initial distributions, contrary to suggestions by ALICE (2015). The results enable future experimental efforts to focus on B meson suppression below 50 GeV for mass tomography, while noting that high-momentum single particles and jets are insensitive to probe mass.
