# Total Energy in Supernova Neutrinos and the Tidal Deformability and Binding Energy of Neutron Stars

> The energy radiated in supernova neutrinos is a fundamental quantity that is closely related to the gravitational binding energy of a neutron star. Recently the tidal deformability of neutron stars was constrained by gravitational wave observations. By considering several equations of state, we find a strong correlation between the tidal deformability and neutron star binding energy. We use this correlation to sharpen predictions of the binding energy of neutron stars and the total neutrino energy in supernovae. We find a minimum binding energy for a neutron star formed in a supernova of $\sim1.5\times 10^{53}$ ergs. Should the neutrino energy in a supernova be significantly below this value, it would strongly suggest new unobserved particles are carrying away some of the supernova energy. Alternatively, if the neutrino energy is observed above $\sim 6\times 10^{53}$ ergs, it would strongly imply the formation of a (perhaps surprisingly) massive neutron star.

## Key claim

The paper finds a strong linear correlation between the tidal deformability of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star and the gravitational binding energy of neutron stars of fixed mass, which allows them to sharpen predictions for the total energy radiated in supernova neutrinos. The key hedge is that this correlation is derived from a set of twenty equations of state and is presented as a linear least-squares fit with associated 1-sigma and 2-sigma error bands, not as an exact physical law.

## Method

The authors solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for twenty different equations of state (both relativistic and non-relativistic) to compute neutron star mass, radius, and binding energy. They then calculate the dimensionless tidal deformability Lambda for a 1.4 solar mass star by solving the differential equation for the second tidal Love number. Finally, they perform linear least-squares fits of binding energy versus Lambda_{1.4} at fixed neutron star masses of 1.174, 1.4, and 2.0 solar masses, using the SciPy curve_fit routine with Levenberg-Marquardt minimization.

## Key numbers

- Minimum binding energy for a 1.174 solar mass neutron star: greater than approximately 1.5 x 10^53 erg (2-sigma lower bound of 1.52 x 10^53 erg for Lambda_{1.4}=580)
- For a 1.4 solar mass star with Lambda_{1.4}=190 (GW170817 central value), binding energy is 2.76 +/- 0.053 x 10^53 erg (1-sigma), with 2-sigma range [2.656, 2.869] x 10^53 erg
- For a 1.4 solar mass star with Lambda_{1.4}=580, binding energy is 2.45 +/- 0.076 x 10^53 erg (1-sigma), with 2-sigma range [2.302, 2.606] x 10^53 erg
- For a 2.0 solar mass star with Lambda_{1.4}=190, binding energy is 6.49 +/- 0.134 x 10^53 erg (1-sigma), with 2-sigma range [6.223, 6.760] x 10^53 erg
- The GW170817 constraint on Lambda_{1.4} at 90% confidence is 190 (+390, -120)
- The lowest observed neutron star mass is 1.174 +/- 0.004 solar masses (Martinez 2015)
- The total neutrino energy from SN1987A was constrained to 2-4 x 10^53 erg (Hirata 1987, Bionta 1987)

## Context

This work extends the relationship between neutron star binding energy and compactness explored by Sun et al. (2020) by connecting it directly to the tidal deformability measured by gravitational wave observations. The correlation enables sharper predictions for the total neutrino energy from the next galactic supernova, which can be measured by large dark matter detectors via neutrino-nucleus elastic scattering (such as Xenon1T, DARWIN, DarkSide-20k, and ARGO). If the observed neutrino energy falls significantly below the predicted minimum of about 1.5 x 10^53 erg, it would suggest new particles (sterile neutrinos, axions, or light dark matter) are carrying away energy, as previously discussed by Particlebounds and PhysRevLett.60.1793; if it exceeds about 6 x 10^53 erg, it would imply formation of a very massive neutron star, possibly related to the GW190814 2.6 solar mass compact object (Abbott 2020).
