# A QCD chiral critical point at small chemical potential: is it there or not?

> For a QCD chiral critical point to exist, the parameter region of small quark masses for which the finite temperature transition is first-order must expand when the chemical potential is turned on. This can be tested by a Taylor expansion of the critical surface (m_{u,d},m_s)_c(mu). We present a new method to perform this Taylor expansion numerically, which we first test on an effective model of QCD with static, dense quarks. We then present the results for QCD with 3 degenerate flavors. For a lattice with N_t=4 time-slices, the first-order region shrinks as the chemical potential is turned on. This implies that, for physical quark masses, the analytic crossover which occurs at mu=0 between the hadronic and the plasma regimes remains crossover in the mu-region where a Taylor expansion is reliable, i.e. mu less than or similar to T. We present preliminary results from finer lattices indicating that this situation persists, as does the discrepancy between the curvature of T_c(m_c(mu=0),mu) and the experimentally observed freeze-out curve.

## Key claim

For a QCD chiral critical point to exist at small chemical potential, the first-order transition region at small quark masses must expand when the chemical potential is turned on; this paper tests that condition via a Taylor expansion of the critical surface and finds that, for a lattice with N_t=4 time-slices, the first-order region shrinks, implying that the analytic crossover at mu=0 persists for mu less than or similar to T, with the caveat that this conclusion relies on a Taylor expansion whose reliability is limited to that mu range.

## Method

The authors perform lattice QCD simulations with three degenerate flavors of standard staggered fermions on an 8^3 x 4 lattice, determining the critical quark mass at mu=0 via the Binder cumulant of the chiral condensate set to the 3d Ising value 1.604. They then extract the curvature of the critical surface dm_c/d(mu^2) at mu=0 using three methods: analytic continuation from imaginary chemical potential (method A), direct measurement of derivative operators at mu=0 (method B), and a new noisy reweighting technique to small imaginary mu (method C), with the latter tested first on a 3d 3-state Potts model that mimics heavy-quark QCD.

## Key numbers

* For the Potts model on a 72^3 lattice, the slope of the critical line at mu=0 is d(m/T)/d(mu/T)^2 = 0.593(8) from method B and 0.589(7) from method C, consistent with the global fit value 0.585(3).
* For N_f=3 QCD on an 8^3 x 4 lattice, the curvature of the pseudo-critical temperature is c_10 = 0.746(1), giving T_0(m_0^c,mu)/T_0(m_0^c,0) = 1 - 0.637(1) (mu/(pi T_0))^2.
* The curvature of the critical surface for N_f=3 QCD is c_1' = -0.105(15), giving m_c(mu)/m_c(0) = 1 - 3.3(5) (mu/(pi T))^2.
* The critical bare quark mass at mu=0 for N_f=3 QCD is a m_0^c = 0.0263(3) on N_t=4, and the ratio m_pi^c/T_c decreases from 1.680(4) on N_t=4 to 0.954(12) on N_t=6.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier study (de Forcrand et al., JHEP, 2007) which used only method A with limited statistics, and provides a cross-check of the critical point determination by Fodor and Katz (2004) using reweighting. The finding that the first-order region shrinks with chemical potential contradicts the scenario needed for a QCD critical point at small mu, and the large cutoff effects observed when moving from N_t=4 to N_t=6 lattices suggest the transition becomes much weaker in the continuum limit, pushing any possible critical point to larger mu values. The discrepancy between the curvature of T_c(mu) and the experimentally observed freeze-out curve persists on finer lattices.
