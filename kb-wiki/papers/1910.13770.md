# On the Topic of Emergence from an Effective Field Theory Perspective

> Effective Field Theories have been used successfully to provide a "bottom-up" description of phenomena whose intrinsic degrees of freedom behave at length scales far different from their effective degrees of freedom. An example is the emergent phenomenon of bound nuclei, whose constituents are neutrons and protons, which in turn are themselves composed of more fundamental particles called quarks and gluons. In going from a fundamental description that utilizes quarks and gluons to an effective field theory description of nuclei, the length scales traversed span at least two orders of magnitude. In this article we provide an Effective Field Theory viewpoint on the topic of emergence, arguing on the side of reductionism and weak emergence. We comment on Anderson's interpretation of constructionism and its connection to strong emergence.

## Key claim
The paper argues that effective field theories (EFTs) provide a rigorous framework for understanding emergent phenomena in nuclear physics, and that this framework is fully consistent with reductionism and weak emergence (where higher-level truths are in principle deducible from lower-level constituents), while strong emergence (where such deduction is impossible even in principle) fails Popper's falsifiability test and is therefore unscientific.

## Method
The authors review the essential ingredients for constructing an EFT: identification of effective degrees of freedom, separation of length/energy scales, identification of symmetries, and a power counting scheme. They then apply this framework to examples from nuclear physics (chiral perturbation theory for nucleons and pions from QCD), particle physics (Fermi's theory of beta decay, Heisenberg-Euler theory), and cosmology (the Standard Model as an EFT of some grander theory). Finally, they analyze the philosophical positions of Anderson (1972) and Ellis (2016) on emergence, using the EFT formalism to argue that weak emergence is natural and strong emergence is not scientifically testable.

## Key numbers
* The length scales traversed from quarks and gluons (sub-femtometer) to nuclei (10s of femtometers) span at least two orders of magnitude.
* Nuclear excitations involve energies of tenths of MeV, while probing quark-gluon substructure requires multi-GeV energies, i.e., three orders of magnitude larger.
* The Planck energy scale is given as 1.2 x 10^25 MeV, relative to which the Standard Model is a low-energy EFT.
* The W and Z boson masses are approximately M_W ~ 80 GeV, setting the breakdown scale of Fermi's four-fermion theory.
* The electron mass is m_e = 0.511 MeV, setting the scale for Heisenberg-Euler theory (omega << m_e).
* Chiral perturbation theory is applicable at energies less than or approximately 250 MeV.
* EFT calculations of nuclei up to the mid-mass region (approximately 50 nucleons) are now possible.

## Context
This paper extends the philosophical discussion of emergence in physics, building on Anderson's (1972) "More is different" and responding to Ellis's (2016) arguments for strong emergence in biology and consciousness. The authors contrast their EFT-based reductionist view with strong emergence claims, using examples from chiral perturbation theory (Weinberg, 1978; Gasser, 1983) and nuclear EFT (Epelbaum, 2008) to demonstrate that weak emergence is sufficient to describe phenomena like the Hoyle state in carbon-12 and the chiral limit divergences in QCD. The work enables a clearer distinction between practical computational impossibility (which Anderson acknowledged) and in-principle non-deducibility (which strong emergence requires), and it provides a falsifiability criterion for evaluating emergence claims.
