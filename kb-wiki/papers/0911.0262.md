# Astrophysical Rates for Explosive Nucleosynthesis: Stellar and Laboratory Rates for Exotic Nuclei

> 4 pages, 2 figures (contining 2 subfigures each); Proceedings of 10th Intl. Conference on Nucleus-Nucleus Collisions, Beijing, China, August 2009; to appear in Nuclear Physics A

# Astrophysical Rates for Explosive Nucleosynthesis: Stellar and Laboratory Rates for Exotic Nuclei

## Key claim
The paper argues that standard Hauser-Feshbach statistical model predictions for reaction rates on exotic nuclei may be adequate as crude estimates (within a factor of 10) even when direct capture contributions become important, but only when the compound nucleus has sufficiently high nuclear level density; the general rule that stellar enhancement factors are smaller for reactions with positive Q-value does not hold in cases where low-energy transitions in the exit channel are suppressed by an additional Coulomb or centrifugal barrier.

## Method
The author reviews and compares theoretical predictions for neutron capture cross sections using the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model, a modified Hauser-Feshbach approach that includes nuclear level density dependence at the compound formation energy, and direct capture calculations with energy-dependent spectroscopic factors. The analysis focuses on sequences of Sn and Pb isotopes, examining the energy of maximally contributing gamma rays and the relative importance of direct versus compound capture as a function of neutron separation energy.

## Key numbers
- The maximally contributing gamma energy E_sig for 60 keV neutron capture on Sn and Pb isotopes lies mostly in the interval of 2-4 MeV, with the relation E_sig approximately equal to (2-4) + E_n MeV.
- For the Sn isotopes shown (except N=92), the sum of modified Hauser-Feshbach and direct capture values approximates the unmodified Hauser-Feshbach result within a factor of 10.
- The comparison of direct capture and Hauser-Feshbach 30 keV Maxwellian Averaged Cross Sections shows that the relative importance of direct capture increases with decreasing neutron separation energy.

## Context
This work extends the author's prior studies on stellar enhancement factors (Rauscher 2008) and the energy of highest impact for gamma transitions (Rauscher 2008), and addresses the long-standing difficulty in predicting alpha-potentials (Yalcin, Mohr, Somorjai, Koehl, Avrigeanu) and the recently recognized need for special attention to the proton optical potential (Kiss 2009, Rauscher 2009). The paper introduces the new code SMARAGD, which will include an improved treatment of low nuclear level densities at compound formation and a global direct capture treatment with energy-dependent spectroscopic factors, enabling more reliable rate predictions for explosive nucleosynthesis reaction networks involving exotic nuclei far from stability.
