# Centrality definition in e+A collisions at the Electron-Ion Collider

> In this work, we investigate the feasibility of defining centrality in electron-ion collisions at the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) by examining the correlation between the impact parameter and several observables, including total energy, total transverse momentum, and total number of particles. Using the BeAGLE Monte Carlo generator, we simulate e+Au and e+Ru collisions at different energies and analyze the correlation between the impact parameter and these observables across different kinematic regions. Our findings indicate that the correlation is weak in the central rapidity region but becomes stronger in the forward and far-forward rapidity regions. However, the correlation is not sufficiently robust to allow for precise centrality determination. We conclude that defining centrality in electron-ion collisions is more challenging than in ion-ion collisions, necessitating further studies to develop a robust centrality definition for the EIC.

## Key claim

The correlation between impact parameter and observables (total energy, total transverse momentum, total number of particles) in e+A collisions at the EIC is weak at mid-rapidity but modest at forward and far-forward rapidity; however, the correlation is not sufficiently robust to allow for precise centrality determination, making centrality definition in electron-ion collisions more challenging than in ion-ion collisions.

## Method

The study used version 1.03 of the BeAGLE Monte Carlo generator, a hybrid model combining DPMJet, PYTHIA6, PyQM, FLUKA, and LHAPDF5 to simulate e+A collisions. The authors simulated e+Au collisions at 18x110 GeV and 10x40 GeV, and e+Ru collisions at 18x110 GeV, then analyzed correlations between impact parameter and three observables (mean energy, mean transverse momentum, mean multiplicity) across three kinematic regions: mid-rapidity (-3.5 < eta < 3.5), forward rapidity (4.5 < eta < 5.9), and far-forward rapidity (eta > 6.0).

## Key numbers

- For e+Au at 18x110 GeV, forward and far-forward rapidity results show mean values varying by approximately 30% and 50% respectively with impact parameter changes
- Reducing collision energy or nuclear size reduces energy deposited at far-forward rapidity by about 50%
- The 0-5% and 60-100% centrality selections show about 30% difference in mean impact parameter for forward rapidity and 50% difference for far-forward rapidity
- No difference is observed between central and peripheral collisions at mid-rapidity for any energy or system size

## Context

This work extends prior investigations by Chang (2022) on using forward neutron energy for centrality in e+A collisions, and addresses questions raised in the context of nuclear structure studies by Magdy (2024) and collision geometry studies by Li (2023). The findings highlight that while centrality is well-established in ion-ion collisions using the Glauber model (Miller, 2007), defining it for electron-ion collisions remains an open question that will require further investigation with alternative models for intranuclear cascade and nuclear remnant breakup, and eventual validation with actual EIC experimental data.
