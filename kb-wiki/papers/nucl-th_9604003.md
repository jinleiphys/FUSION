# Effects of the Spin-Orbit and Tensor Interactions on the $M1$ and $E2$ Excitations in Light Nuclei

> The effects of varying the spin-orbit and tensor components of a realistic interaction on $M1$ excitation rates and $B(E2)'s$ are studied on nuclei in the $0p$ and $1s-0d$ shells. Not only the total $M1$ but also the spin and orbital parts separately are studied. The single-particle energies are first calculated with the same interaction that is used between the valence nucleons. Later this stringent condition is relaxed somewhat and the $1s$ level is raised relative to $0d$. For nuclei up to $^{28}Si$, much better results i.e stronger $B(M1)$ rates are obtained by increasing the strength of the spin-orbit interaction relative to the free value. This is probably also true for $^{32}S$, but $^{36}Ar$ presents some difficulties. The effects of weakening the tensor interaction are also studied. On a more subtle level, the optimum spin-orbit interaction in the lower half of the $s-d$ shell, as far as $M1$ excitations are concerned, is substantially larger than the difference $E(J=3/2^+)_1-E(J=5/2^+)_1=5.2~MeV$ in $^{17}O$. A larger spin-orbit splitting is also needed to destroy the triaxiality in $^{22}Ne$. Also studied are how much $M1$ orbital and spin strength lies in an observable region and how much is buried in the grass at higher energies. It is noted that for many nuclei the sum $B(M1)_{orbital}+B(M1)_{spin}$ is very close to $B(M1)_{total}$, indicating that the summed cross terms are very small.

## Key claim

Increasing the spin-orbit strength parameter \(x\) from 1 to 1.5 and decreasing the tensor strength parameter \(y\) from 1 to 0.5 systematically increases the spin \(B(M1)\) strength in light nuclei, but the effect is conditional on the nucleus and shell region: it works well for nuclei up to \(^{28}\text{Si}\), is probably also true for \(^{32}\text{S}\), but \(^{36}\text{Ar}\) presents difficulties.

## Method

The authors performed shell-model calculations for nuclei in the \(0p\) and \(1s-0d\) shells using a realistic \((x,y)\) interaction where \(x\) multiplies the two-body spin-orbit term and \(y\) multiplies the tensor term, with \(x=1, y=1\) giving a fit to Bonn A \(G\)-matrix elements. Single-particle energies were calculated self-consistently with the same interaction used for the residual two-body matrix elements, rather than taken from experiment. For the \(1s-0d\) shell, the \(1s\) single-particle level was later raised by an additional 2 MeV relative to the \(0d\) centroid to correct for deficiencies of harmonic-oscillator wavefunctions.

## Key numbers

* For \(^{12}\text{C}\), increasing \(x\) from 1 to 1.5 (with \(y=1\)) raises the spin \(B(M1)\) from 0.91 to 2.07 \(\mu_N^2\); for \(^{24}\text{Mg}\) from 1.73 to 3.35 \(\mu_N^2\); for \(^{32}\text{S}\) from 2.72 to 8.52 \(\mu_N^2\); for \(^{36}\text{Ar}\) from 2.25 to 5.20 \(\mu_N^2\).
* In \(^{24}\text{Mg}\), decreasing \(y\) from 1 to 0.5 (with \(x=1\)) increases \(B(M1)_{\text{spin}}\) from 1.73 to 1.94 \(\mu_N^2\); with \(x=1.5\) it increases from 3.35 to 4.04 \(\mu_N^2\).
* For \(^{12}\text{C}\) with \(x=1.5, y=0.5\), the calculated \(B(M1)\) to the \(1^+ T=1\) state is 2.54 \(\mu_N^2\), compared to the experimental value of 2.63(8) \(\mu_N^2\).
* The spin-orbit splitting in \(^{17}\text{O}\) calculated with \(x=1, y=1\) is 5.562 MeV, close to the empirical splitting of 5.2 MeV.
* Raising the \(1s\) level by 2 MeV in \(^{24}\text{Mg}\) with \(x=1.5, y=0.5\) increases the spin \(B(M1)\) summed to the first 10 states from 1.79 to 3.38 \(\mu_N^2\), approaching the experimental value of 5.86 \(\mu_N^2\).
* For \(^{20}\text{Ne}\) with \(x=1, y=1\), the isoscalar \(B(E2)\) to the lowest \(2^+\) state at 2.92 MeV is 299.4 \(e^2\text{fm}^4\); the total sum is 315.1 \(e^2\text{fm}^4\).

## Context

This work extends the shell-model approach of Cohen and Kurath (1965) in the \(p\) shell and Wildenthal and Brown (1980s) in the \(sd\) shell, who obtained excellent fits using empirical effective interactions that implicitly include core polarization and relativistic effects. By starting from a realistic \((x,y)\) interaction and varying its spin-orbit and tensor components, the authors isolate the role of these specific interaction terms in \(M1\) and \(E2\) excitations. The results enable future studies to disentangle the contributions of the spin-orbit and tensor interactions from other effects such as exchange currents, and to test predictions of Dirac phenomenology (Walecka model) and universal scaling arguments (Brown and collaborators, 1980s) for the weakening of the tensor interaction inside nuclei.
