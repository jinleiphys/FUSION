# R-Process Nucleosynthesis in Dynamically Ejected Matter of Neutron Star Mergers

> Although the rapid neutron-capture process, or r-process, is fundamentally important for explaining the origin of approximately half of the stable nuclei with A > 60, the astrophysical site of this process has not been identified yet. Here we study r-process nucleosynthesis in material that is dynamically ejected by tidal and pressure forces during the merging of binary neutron stars (NSs) and within milliseconds afterwards. For the first time we make use of relativistic hydrodynamical simulations of such events, defining consistently the conditions that determine the nucleosynthesis, i.e., neutron enrichment, entropy, early density evolution and thus expansion timescale, and ejecta mass. We find that 10^{-3}-10^{-2} solar masses are ejected, which is enough for mergers to be the main source of heavy (A > 140) galactic r-nuclei for merger rates of some 10^{-5} per year. While asymmetric mergers eject 2-3 times more mass than symmetric ones, the exact amount depends weakly on whether the NSs have radii of ~15 km for a "stiff" nuclear equation of state (EOS) or ~12 km for a "soft" EOS. R-process nucleosynthesis during the decompression becomes largely insensitive to the detailed conditions because of efficient fission recycling, producing a composition that closely follows the solar r-abundance distribution for nuclei with mass numbers A > 140. Estimating the light curve powered by the radioactive decay heating of r-process nuclei with an approximative model, we expect high emission in the B-V-R bands for 1-2 days with potentially observable longer duration in the case of asymmetric mergers because of the larger ejecta mass.

## Key claim

Binary neutron star mergers can be the main source of heavy (A > 140) galactic r-process nuclei, but this conclusion depends on merger rates of some 10^{-5} per year and on the ejecta mass being 10^{-3} to 10^{-2} solar masses; the r-process nucleosynthesis during decompression becomes largely insensitive to detailed conditions because of efficient fission recycling.

## Method

The authors performed three-dimensional general relativistic Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics simulations of symmetric (1.35-1.35 solar mass) and asymmetric (1.2-1.5 solar mass) neutron star mergers, using the Shen et al. (1998) equation of state with neutron star radii of about 15 km and the Lattimer & Swesty (1991) LS220 EOS with radii of about 12 km. They extracted density, electron fraction, entropy, and temperature histories for ejected fluid elements and fed these into a full reaction network of 5000 species from protons up to Z=110, including fission recycling, to compute the nucleosynthetic yields. The temperature evolution was postprocessed by assuming adiabatic flow with shock heating, and the light curve was estimated using a semi-analytic one-zone model.

## Key numbers

- Ejecta mass of about 3 x 10^{-3} solar masses for the symmetric 1.35-1.35 solar mass merger, and about 6 x 10^{-3} solar masses for the asymmetric 1.2-1.5 solar mass merger (using the Shen et al. 1998 EOS)
- With the LS220 EOS, ejecta masses of about 2 x 10^{-3} solar masses (symmetric) and 6 x 10^{-3} solar masses (asymmetric)
- Electron fraction of ejecta ranges between 0.015 and 0.050, with about 30 percent of the mass having Y_e about 0.015
- Average expansion velocity of about 0.31c for the symmetric system and about 0.23c for the asymmetric system
- Total heating energy of about 2 x 10^{49} erg (symmetric) and about 4 x 10^{49} erg (asymmetric), corresponding to 3.4 MeV per nucleon in both cases
- Peak B-V-R band luminosity of about 4 x 10^{41} erg per second for about one day (symmetric), with the asymmetric system producing emission nearly twice as long
- Yield of about (3-5) x 10^{-5} solar masses per event of europium isotopes 151 and 153

## Context

This work extends earlier parametrized studies of r-process nucleosynthesis in neutron star merger ejecta by Freiburghaus et al. (1999), Goriely et al. (2005), and Arnould et al. (2007), which used simple decompression models or treated neutron enrichment and temperature as free parameters. The authors confirm that fission recycling makes the abundance distribution for A > 140 robust and similar to the solar r-abundance pattern, as previously suggested by Goriely et al. (2005) and Arnould et al. (2007). The results enable predictions for kilo-nova electromagnetic counterparts that could provide the first in-situ observation of freshly produced r-process material, and they motivate further fully relativistic simulations including neutrino transport and magnetic fields.
