# Systematic Study on the $α$-particle preformation factor in the theory of $α$-decay based on the Tabular Prior-data Fitted Network (TabPFN)

> A hybrid approach combining the Tabular Prior-data Fitted Network (TabPFN) with the Coulomb and Proximity Potential Model (CPPM) is developed to investigate $α$-particle preformation factors $P_α$ and their impact on $α$-decay half-lives. The TabPFN model, trained on 498 nuclei, accurately learns the relationship between nuclear structure properties and $P_α$, achieving a root mean square deviation of $σ_{\mathrm{rms}} = 0.211$. The predicted factors reveal clear odd-even staggering and shell closure effects, and exhibit linear correlations with both $Q_α^{-1/2}$ and the fragmentation potential $V_{\mathrm{frag}}$. When incorporated into CPPM calculations, the machine-learning-based $P_α$ values significantly improve half-life predictions. Similar improvements are also obtained when deformation effects are included in the potential barrier description. The capability of the model is further demonstrated through predictions for superheavy nuclei ($Z = 117$--120), suggesting $N = 184$ as a potential neutron magic number.

## Key claim
The TabPFN machine learning model, when trained on 498 nuclei and combined with the Coulomb and Proximity Potential Model (CPPM), can predict alpha-particle preformation factors with a root mean square deviation of 0.211, but this accuracy is conditional on including all nine nuclear structure features (mass number, proton number, neutron number, angular momentum, pairing effect, proton parity, neutron parity, quadrupole deformation, and Q-value) as inputs.

## Method
The authors extracted experimental alpha-particle preformation factors from 498 nuclei (172 even-even, 261 odd-A, and 65 odd-odd) by setting the theoretical preformation factor to unity in the CPPM framework and comparing calculated half-lives to experimental values from NUBASE2016. They then trained the TabPFN Transformer-based architecture on these extracted factors using ten nuclear descriptors as input features, employing in-context learning across both samples and attributes. The model's performance was evaluated using root-mean-square deviation between predicted and experimental logarithmic preformation factors, with the best-performing configuration (TabPFN12) incorporating all nine physical features plus the Q-value.

## Key numbers
- Root mean square deviation of the full TabPFN12 model across the entire dataset of 498 nuclei: sigma_rms = 0.211
- For even-even nuclei specifically, TabPFN12 achieved sigma_rms = 0.153, compared to 0.544 from the empirical formula of He (2021) and 0.548 from Deng (2021)
- Including the pairing effect delta reduced sigma_rms from 0.409 (TabPFN1) to 0.266 (TabPFN2), a decrease of 34.9%
- When deformation effects were included in the potential barrier description, the TabPFN12 model achieved sigma_rms = 0.232
- The linear regression between experimental preformation factors and fragmentation potential gave: log10 P_alpha^exp = -0.119 V_frag - 0.119

## Context
This work extends the approach of Deng (2021), who extracted preformation factors via a semi-empirical formula, by replacing the empirical formula with a machine learning framework that achieves substantially lower deviations (63.8% improvement in overall sigma_rms). The study also builds on prior machine learning applications to alpha-decay, including the artificial neural network work of You (2024) and the Bayesian neural network approach of Jin (2023), but differs by focusing specifically on preformation factor prediction rather than direct half-life prediction. The results enable improved half-life predictions for superheavy nuclei (Z = 117-120) and suggest N = 184 as a potential neutron magic number, providing a predictive tool for identifying shell closures in superheavy regions.
