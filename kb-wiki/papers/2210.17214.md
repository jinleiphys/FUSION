# The Fusion-by-Diffusion model as a tool to calculate cross sections for the production of superheavy nuclei

> This article summarizes recent progress in our understanding of the reaction mechanisms leading to the formation of superheavy nuclei in cold and hot fusion reactions. Calculations are done within the Fusion-by-Diffusion (FBD) model using the new nuclear data tables by Jachimowicz et al. [At. Data Nucl. Data Tables 138, 101393 (2021)]. The synthesis reaction is treated in a standard way as a three-step process (i.e., capture, fusion, and survival). Each reaction step is analyzed separately. Model calculations are compared with selected experimental data on capture, fissionlike and fusion cross sections, fusion probabilities, and evaporation residue excitation functions. The role of the angular momentum in the fusion step is discussed in detail. A set of fusion excitation functions with corresponding fusion probabilities is provided for cold and hot synthesis reactions.

## Key claim

The Fusion-by-Diffusion (FBD) model, using new nuclear data tables by Jachimowicz et al. (2021), can reproduce experimental capture, fusion, and evaporation residue cross sections for both cold and hot fusion reactions leading to superheavy nuclei, provided that the "injection point" distance is parametrized separately for each reaction class with two fitted parameters per class.

## Method

The synthesis reaction is treated as a three-step process: capture, fusion, and survival. Capture cross sections are calculated using a Gaussian barrier distribution parametrized from systematics of about 50 heavy nuclear systems. Fusion probabilities are obtained by solving the Smoluchowski diffusion equation, where the system starts from an "injection point" on the potential energy surface and must overcome an internal barrier to reach the compound nucleus configuration. Survival probabilities are computed using standard statistical methods with Weisskopf neutron emission widths and transition-state fission widths, assuming competition only between neutron emission and fission.

## Key numbers

- For cold fusion reactions, the injection point distance parametrization is: s_inj = 0.878 fm - 0.294 × (E_cm - B_0) fm/MeV, derived from 27 reactions.
- For hot fusion reactions, the injection point distance parametrization is: s_inj = 3.291 fm - 0.196 × (E_cm - B_0) fm/MeV, derived from 24 reactions.
- The error corridor for both injection point parametrizations is estimated to be ±1 fm.
- For cold fusion reactions, the excitation energy of the compound nucleus is usually in the range of 10 to 20 MeV.
- For hot fusion reactions, the excitation energy is usually in the range of 30 to 40 MeV, with dominant evaporation channels being 3n and 4n.
- Calculated and fitted mean barrier values for both cold and hot reactions do not differ by more than 2 MeV.
- For the 50Ti + 208Pb reaction, the asymmetric saddle dominates at lower partial waves, while the symmetric saddle starts to dominate at l ≈ 25.

## Context

This work extends the FBD model developed by the authors over the past two decades (Siwek-Wilczynska et al. 2005, 2011) by incorporating new nuclear data tables from Jachimowicz et al. (2021) and providing updated injection point systematics for both cold and hot fusion reactions. The model enables predictions of fusion probabilities and evaporation residue cross sections for unstudied projectile-target combinations, though the authors caution that the parametrizations should be used for interpolation rather than extrapolation beyond the range of studied E_cm - B_0 values. The approach contrasts with Langevin-type equation solutions such as those by Boilley (2013), which also found approximately linear behavior of the injection point distance as a function of energy.
