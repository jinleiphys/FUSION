# Dark Side of the Neutron?

> We discuss our recently proposed interpretation of the discrepancy between the bottle and beam neutron lifetime experiments as a sign of a dark sector. The difference between the outcomes of the two types of measurements is explained by the existence of a neutron dark decay channel with a branching fraction 1%. Phenomenologically consistent particle physics models for the neutron dark decay can be constructed and they involve a strongly self-interacting dark sector. We elaborate on the theoretical developments around this idea and describe the efforts undertaken to verify it experimentally.

# Dark Side of the Neutron?

## Key claim

The 4.0 sigma discrepancy between bottle and beam neutron lifetime measurements (879.6 ± 0.6 s versus 888.0 ± 2.0 s) can be explained by a neutron dark decay channel with a branching fraction of approximately 1%, provided that the final state dark particle mass lies between 937.900 MeV and 939.565 MeV to avoid violating nuclear stability constraints from beryllium-9.

## Method

The authors compare the two experimental approaches: bottle experiments trap neutrons and measure the exponential decay of neutron counts over time, yielding the total neutron lifetime; beam experiments count the protons produced from neutron decays and infer the lifetime from the proton production rate. If a neutron decay channel exists that does not produce a proton in the final state, the beam measurement (which counts only proton-producing decays) will give a longer apparent lifetime than the bottle measurement. The authors construct effective field theories and two explicit particle physics models (Model 1: n → χγ; Model 2: n → χφ) with new dark fermions and scalars, matching the effective Lagrangians to the full models to compute dark decay rates.

## Key numbers

- Bottle experiment average neutron lifetime: τ_n^bottle = 879.6 ± 0.6 s (based on five experiments, PDG average)
- Beam experiment average neutron lifetime: τ_n^beam = 888.0 ± 2.0 s (based on two experiments, PDG average)
- Discrepancy significance: 4.0 sigma between bottle and beam measurements
- Required branching fraction for neutron dark decay: Br(n → p + anything) ≈ 99%, implying 1% dark decay branching
- Allowed dark particle mass range from beryllium-9 stability: 937.900 MeV < M_f < 939.565 MeV
- Neutron separation energy in beryllium-9: S_n(^9Be) = 1.664 MeV
- For Model 1 with m_χ = 937.9 MeV: M_Φ / sqrt(|λ_q λ_χ|) ≈ 400 TeV
- For Model 2 with m_χ = 937.9 MeV, m_φ ≈ 0, m_χ̃ = 2 m_n: M_Φ / sqrt(|λ_q λ_χ̃ λ_φ|) ≈ 300 TeV
- SNO/KamLAND constraint on neutron invisible decay: τ_(n→invisible) > 5.8 × 10^29 years
- Los Alamos UCN search for n → χγ: excluded Br ≈ 1% at 2.2 sigma significance for photon energies 0.782 MeV < E_γ < 1.664 MeV
- Los Alamos UCN search for n → χ e+e-: excluded Br ≈ 1% for E_(e+e-) ≳ 2 m_e + 100 keV with nearly 100% confidence
- Neutron separation energy in lithium-11: S_n(^11Li) = 0.396 MeV
- Neutron separation energy in beryllium-11: S_n(^11Be) = 0.502 MeV
- Constraint from beryllium-11 decay rates: m_χ̃ > 939.064 MeV

## Context

This work extends the long-standing discrepancy between bottle and beam neutron lifetime measurements, first noted by Green (no year given) and documented by the Particle Data Group (Tanabashi 2018). The authors propose that the 1% difference arises from neutron decays into dark sector particles, rather than underestimated systematic errors. The proposal has inspired theoretical developments in neutron star physics (McKeen 2018, Baym 2018, Motta 2018), self-interacting dark matter models (Cline 2018, Karananas 2018), hadron dark decays (Barducci 2018), and low-scale baryogenesis (Bringmann 2018, Elor 2018). Alternative explanations include neutron-mirror neutron oscillations (Berezhiani 2018) and Fierz interference terms (Ivanov 2018). Experimental searches at Los Alamos UCN (Tang 2018, Sun 2018) and CERN-ISOLDE (Pfutzner 2018) have begun testing the predictions, with ongoing beam experiments at NIST and J-PARC expected to further constrain or confirm the scenario.
