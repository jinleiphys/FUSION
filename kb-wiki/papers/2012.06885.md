# Comparison of semiclassical transfer to continuum model with Ichimura-Austern-Vincent model in medium energy knockout reactions

> The full quantum mechanical (QM) model of inclusive breakup of Ichimura-Austern-Vincent (IAV) is implemented in this paper to calculate breakup from heavy radioactive nuclei on a $^9$Be target at intermediate energies. So far it had been implemented and applied only to low energy reactions with light projectiles. The IAV model is successful in predicting absolute cross sections among other observables. In order to get insight on the content of the model in the case of the complicated heavy-ion reactions, results are compared with those of the semiclassical transfer to the continuum (TC) model. Because the TC is based on analytical formulae the dynamics of the breakup as it is contained in the rather involved IAV formalism will become more transparent. Heavy-ion reactions at high energies ($>$50A.MeV) are demanding from the computational point of view because of the high number of partial waves involved, typically around 100. The TC constitutes a useful alternative to the full QM calculations whenever predictions and/or estimates are necessary. It allows also for a systematic, fast evaluation of breakup observables. In the applications of both methods we use state-of-the art optical potentials and structure information. Excellent agreement is found between the calculated results of both methods and with available experimental data which shows that the qualitative and quantitative understanding of most aspects of one nucleon breakup is well under control.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the semiclassical Transfer to the Continuum (TC) model produces results in excellent quantitative agreement with the full quantum mechanical Ichimura-Austern-Vincent (IAV) model for one-nucleon knockout reactions from heavy radioactive nuclei on a $^9$Be target at intermediate energies (above 50A.MeV), validating the semiclassical approximation for the relative motion in this regime. The authors hedge that this agreement holds specifically for the reactions studied and that the TC model shows slightly more extended tails in non-elastic breakup momentum distributions compared to IAV.

## Method

The authors implemented the full quantum mechanical Ichimura-Austern-Vincent (IAV) model for inclusive breakup of heavy radioactive projectiles ($^{14}$O and $^{16}$C) on a $^9$Be target at intermediate energies (53A.MeV and 75A.MeV), extending its application beyond the low-energy, light-projectile cases where it had previously been used. They compared these IAV calculations with results from the semiclassical Transfer to the Continuum (TC) model, which treats the relative motion of the reaction partners as a classical trajectory, using state-of-the-art optical potentials and structure information including the AB n-target potential and single-folding core-target potentials.

## Key numbers

* Total single-particle cross section for $^{14}$O neutron knockout (1$p_{3/2}$, S$_n$=23.12 MeV): IAV gives 13.72 mb, TC gives 12.65 mb, with experimental value 14 mb
* Total single-particle cross section for $^{14}$O proton knockout (1$p_{1/2}$, S$_p$=4.63 MeV): IAV gives 33.91 mb, TC gives 30.5 mb, with experimental value 58 mb
* Total single-particle cross section for $^{16}$C neutron knockout from 2$s_{1/2}$ state (S$_n$=4.25 MeV): IAV gives 58.42 mb, TC gives 47.7 mb, with experimental value 36 mb
* Total single-particle cross section for $^{16}$C neutron knockout from 1$d_{5/2}$ state (S$_n$=4.99 MeV): IAV gives 36.29 mb, TC gives 26.9 mb, with experimental value 46 mb
* Total single-particle cross section for $^{16}$C proton knockout (1$p_{3/2}$, S$_p$=22.56 MeV): IAV gives 7.45 mb, TC gives 7.48 mb, with experimental value 18 mb
* The maximum number of partial waves required for IAV calculations at these energies was around 100, compared to at most 30 for low-energy cases
* Reduction factors R$_f$ = $\sigma_{exp}/(C^2S \sigma_{IAV})$ ranged from 0.3 to 1.4 across the four reactions studied

## Context

This work extends the IAV model, previously applied only to low-energy reactions with light projectiles such as deuteron (Jin, 2015) and $^{6,7}$Li (Jin, 2017, 2018), to higher energies and heavier projectiles, addressing the computational challenge of handling approximately 100 partial waves. The comparison validates the semiclassical TC model as a fast, accurate alternative to full quantum mechanical calculations for medium-energy knockout reactions, which is important for interpreting knockout data from exotic nuclei and understanding discrepancies between experimental cross sections and eikonal model predictions noted by Tostevin and Gade. The results enable future simplifications of quantum mechanical implementations by using semiclassical approximations for relative motion wave functions, and the authors plan to extend these calculations to other knockout reactions to help resolve systematic differences between experimental and theoretical cross sections.
