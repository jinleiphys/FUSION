# Quark-Meson-Coupling (QMC) model for finite nuclei, nuclear matter and beyond

> The Quark-Meson-Coupling model, which self-consistently relates the dynamics of the internal quark structure of a hadron to the relativistic mean fields arising in nuclear matter, provides a natural explanation to many open questions in low energy nuclear physics, including the origin of many-body nuclear forces and their saturation, the spin-orbit interaction and properties of hadronic matter at a wide range of densities up to those occurring in the cores of neutron stars. Here we focus on four aspects of the model (i) a full comprehensive survey of the theory, including the latest developments, (ii) extensive application of the model to ground state properties of finite nuclei and hypernuclei, with a discussion of similarities and differences between the QMC and Skyrme energy density functionals, (iii) equilibrium conditions and composition of hadronic matter in cold and warm neutron stars and their comparison with the outcome of relativistic mean-field theories and, (iv) tests of the fundamental idea that hadron structure changes in-medium.

## Key claim

The Quark-Meson-Coupling (QMC) model provides a natural explanation for nuclear saturation and many-body forces by self-consistently coupling the internal quark structure of hadrons to relativistic mean fields, but the key mechanism is that the effective scalar coupling constant for the sigma meson to the composite nucleon decreases with density due to a "scalar polarizability", a feature that the abstract does not explicitly quantify.

## Method

The authors use the MIT bag model to describe quark confinement in hadrons, coupling the light quarks (u, d, s) to classical scalar (sigma), vector (omega), and isovector (rho) meson fields that are constant over the bag volume. The nucleon effective mass is expanded as a quadratic function of the applied scalar field, introducing a scalar polarizability parameter d that reduces the effective sigma-nucleon coupling with increasing density. For finite nuclei, a Born-Oppenheimer approximation is used to treat slowly varying fields, and the spin-orbit interaction is derived from the isoscalar magnetic moment of the nucleon plus Thomas precession.

## Key numbers

* Scalar polarizability for the nucleon: d = 0.1797 (dimensionless, from fit to MIT bag model with bag radius 1.0 fm)
* Free nucleon bag radius: R_N^free = 1.0 fm (chosen by matching lattice QCD calculations of the isovector squared charge radius at large pion masses)
* Bag model parameters for R_N^free = 1.0 fm: B = 0.284 fm^{-4}, Z_0 = 1.771, alpha_c = 0.560, m_s = 326 MeV (with F_s = 1) or m_s = 289 MeV (with F_s = 0.758)
* Hyperon masses from the fit (F_s = 1): M_Lambda = 1132 MeV, M_Sigma = 1180 MeV, M_Xi = 1353 MeV (experimental: 1116, 1195, 1315 MeV respectively)
* Weights for hyperon scalar couplings relative to nucleon: w_Lambda = 0.7114, w_Sigma = 0.5847, w_Xi = 0.3400; tilde-w_Lambda = 0.68956, tilde-w_Sigma = 0.6629, tilde-w_Xi = 0.3688
* Isoscalar magnetic moment of the nucleon at sigma = 0: mu_IS(0) = 0.88 mu_N; isovector magnetic moment at sigma = 0: mu_IV(0) = 4.7 mu_N

## Context

This review extends the original QMC model of Guichon (1988) by providing a comprehensive theoretical survey and applying it to finite nuclei, hypernuclei, and neutron star matter. It contrasts the QMC approach with Quantum Hadrodynamics (QHD), where saturation requires very large scalar fields, and with Skyrme energy density functionals, noting similarities and differences. The model automatically generates many-body forces for all hadrons including hyperons with no new parameters, enabling predictions for neutron star maximum masses around two solar masses when hyperons are included (Rikovska-Stone, 2006), and has been used to derive an energy density functional for nuclear structure calculations (Stone, 2016; Stone, 2017).
