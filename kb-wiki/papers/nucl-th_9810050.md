# Prediction of Thermonuclear Reaction Rates in Astrophysics

> Recent improvements and remaining problems in the prediction of thermonuclear rates are reviewed. The main emphasis is on statistical model calculations, but the challenge to include direct reactions close to the driplines is also briefly addressed. Further theoretical as well as experimental investigations are motivated.

## Key claim

The paper reviews that the new generation of Hauser-Feshbach statistical models can make reliable predictions of thermonuclear cross sections, but only when the nuclear level density is sufficiently high; below a critical temperature, averaging over too few resonances makes the Hauser-Feshbach approach inappropriate and it will misjudge the cross section.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework for computing thermonuclear reaction rates, focusing on the statistical (Hauser-Feshbach) model and its ingredients: optical potentials, gamma widths, level densities, and isospin effects. It discusses the conditions under which the statistical model applies versus when direct reactions or single resonances dominate, and describes the new NON-SMOKER code which incorporates updated parametrizations for these ingredients.

## Key numbers

- The effective energy window for neutron reactions is given by the width of the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution peak; for charged particles, the Gamow peak is broader and shifted to higher energies.
- The new level density study achieved improved predictions in the mass range 20 ≤ A ≤ 245 using an energy-dependent level density parameter with microscopic corrections from nuclear mass models (Rauscher 1997).
- The McFadden and Satchler (1966) alpha-nucleus potential failed in one case: the recently measured 144Sm(alpha,gamma)148Gd low-energy cross section (Somorjai 1998).
- The new global alpha-nucleus potential approach achieved accuracy comparable to the McFadden and Satchler (1966) potential (Rauscher 1998).

## Context

This review extends the line of work on statistical model calculations for astrophysical reaction rates, building on earlier approaches by Holmes (1976), Woosley (1978), and Cowan (1991). It quantifies the applicability range of the Hauser-Feshbach method and clarifies the boundary between different reaction mechanisms, motivating further experimental investigations over a large mass range to improve parameter systematics, especially for unstable isotopes near the driplines.
