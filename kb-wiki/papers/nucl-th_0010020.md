# Reaction and break-up cross sections of 11Li at 0.8 and 0.28 GeV/u

> In this paper we calculate reaction and breakup cross sections for the two- neutron halo nucleus of $^{11}$Li using the optical limit of Glauber theory. Calculations are presented and compared to experimental data at 0.8 and 0.28 GeV/u on a series of targets. The $^{11}$Li nucleus is described as a three-body system, a core plus two neutrons, with a phenomenological neutron-core potential and a density dependent neutron-neutron interaction of zero range. Three different wave functions are constructed which have different $(2s_{1/2})^2$ and $(1p_{1/2})^2$ two-neutron components but correspond to the same binding energy close to the experimental value. We show that the agreement with all the experimental observables is achieved only if the 11Li wave function contains about 30% of $(2s_{1/2})^2$ configuration.

# Reaction and break-up cross sections of 11Li at 0.8 and 0.28 GeV/u

## Key claim

The agreement with all experimental observables for reaction and two-neutron removal cross sections of 11Li on various targets at 0.8 and 0.28 GeV/u is achieved only if the 11Li ground state wave function contains about 30% of (2s₁/₂)² configuration, but this conclusion is conditional on the use of the optical limit of Glauber theory which may overestimate cross sections by a few percent and does not fully account for the diffractive breakup contribution at the lower energy.

## Method

The authors calculate reaction and two-neutron removal cross sections using the optical limit of Glauber theory with a finite-range nucleon-nucleon profile function, describing 11Li as a three-body system (⁹Li core plus two valence neutrons) in a two-neutron pairing model. Three different wave functions (F₁, F₂, F₃) are constructed with varying (2s₁/₂)² and (1p₁/₂)² two-neutron components but the same two-neutron separation energy of 0.36 MeV, using a zero-range density-dependent neutron-neutron interaction with parameters fitted to the nuclear matter gap from Gogny effective interactions and to the two-neutron separation energy of ¹⁴C. Cross sections are computed for targets of ¹²C, ²⁷Al, ⁶³Cu, and ²⁰⁸Pb at incident energies of 0.8 and 0.28 GeV/u, including Coulomb contributions for heavy targets via a low-energy dipole model.

## Key numbers

- 11Li wave function F₂ contains 27% (2s₁/₂)² and 52% (1p₁/₂)² configurations, with a 11Li rms radius of 3.4 fm (assuming ⁹Li radius 2.32 fm) or 3.3 fm (assuming ⁹Li radius 2.18 fm)
- For ¹¹Li + ¹²C at 0.8 GeV/u, the F₂ wave function gives σ_R = 1.04 b and σ_{-2n} = 0.22 b, in agreement with experimental values (σ_R = 1.04 ± 0.02 b, σ_{-2n} = 0.22 ± 0.01 b from Tanihata et al.)
- The nucleon-nucleon cross section used is σ_NN = 4.1 fm² at 0.8 GeV/u and 3.1 fm² at 0.28 GeV/u; the range of the profile function is r₀ = 0.64 fm at 0.8 GeV and 1.41 fm at 0.28 GeV
- The ⁹Li rms radius fitted to the measured reaction cross section of 796 ± 6 mb on ¹²C at 0.8 GeV/u is 2.18 fm with r₀ = 0.64 fm, compared to 2.32 fm with r₀ = 0
- For ¹¹Li + ²⁰⁸Pb at 0.8 GeV/u, the total two-neutron removal cross section (nuclear + Coulomb) is 1.22 b, close to the measured value of 1.3 ± 0.1 b
- At 0.28 GeV/u, the calculated two-neutron removal cross section for ¹¹Li + ¹²C is 0.2 b, in agreement with the stripping contribution extracted by Zinser et al.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of 11Li structure that used Faddeev calculations (Thompson and Zukhov, 1998) or pairing models (Vinh Mau and Pacheco, 1996; Bertsch and Esbensen, 1998) to determine the configuration mixing in the two-neutron halo wave function. The results agree with the conclusion of Al-Khalili et al. (1996) and Garrido et al. (1997) that about 30% (2s₁/₂)² component is needed, but reveal a strong model dependence of the extracted 11Li radius, smaller in the pairing model than in Faddeev calculations, which the authors attribute to continuum discretization effects. The work enables future studies to use reaction and breakup cross sections as more stringent constraints on halo structure than radii alone, and highlights the need for improved treatment of the diffractive breakup contribution at lower incident energies.
