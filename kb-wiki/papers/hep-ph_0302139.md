# Multiparticle correlations from momentum conservation

> Using a generating-function formalism, we compute the contribution of momentum conservation to multiparticle correlations between the emitted particles in high-energy collisions. In particular, we derive a compact expression of the genuine M-particle correlation, for arbitrary M.

## Key claim
Momentum conservation induces genuine M-particle correlations that scale as 1/N^{M-1} (where N is the total number of emitted particles), the same scaling as short-range correlations, despite being a long-range effect affecting all particles. This result holds to leading order in 1/N using a saddle-point approximation of a generating function.

## Method
The author introduces a generating function G(x_1,...,x_N) of the multiparticle probability distributions, where the logarithm of G yields the cumulants (genuine correlations). For N large, the generating function integral is evaluated via a saddle-point approximation, showing that the leading contribution to all cumulants comes from the saddle-point value N*F(k_0). The two- and three-particle cumulants are then computed explicitly by expanding the saddle-point condition order by order in x/N.

## Key numbers
- The M-particle cumulant scales as 1/N^{M-1}, where N is the total number of emitted particles
- Two-particle cumulant: f_c(p_1, p_2) = -D (p_1·p_2) / (N ⟨p²⟩), where D is the spatial dimension
- Three-particle cumulant: f_c(p_1, p_2, p_3) = -D/(N²⟨p²⟩) (p_1·p_2 + p_1·p_3 + p_2·p_3) + D²/(N²⟨p²⟩²) [(p_1·p_2)(p_1·p_3) + (p_1·p_2)(p_2·p_3) + (p_1·p_3)(p_2·p_3)]
- For non-isotropic distributions, the two-particle cumulant generalizes to f_c(p_1, p_2) = -Σ_i (p_1)_i (p_2)_i / (N ⟨(p)_i²⟩), summing over principal axes

## Context
This work extends earlier calculations of two-particle correlations from momentum conservation by Borghini (2000) and Danielewicz (1988) to arbitrary order M. The result shows that momentum conservation correlations scale identically to short-range correlations from quantum effects (Bose-Einstein/Fermi-Dirac), resonance decays, or (mini)jets, meaning they can be removed as efficiently as short-range correlations in collective flow analyses using cumulant methods (Borghini, 2001). However, this also means momentum conservation may bias measurements of HBT parameters, especially their dependence on average particle momentum, since the correlation strength increases with momentum.
