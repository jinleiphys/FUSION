# Auxiliary counterterms and their role in effective field theory

> Effective field theories include contact-range interactions (or counterterms) for two reasons: representing the unknown short-range physics in a model independent manner and ensuring the cutoff independence of observables. Both are intertwined: cutoff independence alone (modulo truncation errors) already generates counterterms encoding physical information not present in the known long-range physics. Yet, there is also residual cutoff dependence, which is smaller than the uncertainties that are achievable within the effective field theory description and thus can be safely neglected in most settings. If one insists on exact cutoff independence though, new counterterms will be required, but they encode no new physical information and are thus what one could call redundant, or auxiliary, counterterms. It happens that auxiliary counterterms are still useful for solving certain inconsistencies that appear during renormalization or for improving the convergence of the effective field theory expansion. Examples of these use cases are discussed, including the interpretation of the improved actions or the relation between perturbative and non-perturbative renormalization.

## Key claim

Auxiliary counterterms, contact-range operators that enforce exact cutoff independence but carry no new physical information, are generally unnecessary for practical EFT calculations, yet they become useful for resolving renormalization inconsistencies (such as the one identified by Epelbaum et al. (2018)) and for improving the convergence of the EFT expansion, as illustrated by the improved actions approach of Contessi et al. (2023-2025).

## Method

The paper analyzes the role of auxiliary counterterms in effective field theories by examining two regularization schemes for pionless EFT: power divergence subtraction (PDS) and delta-shell cutoff regularization. It derives the scaling behavior of the contact-range couplings \(C_{2n}\) under rescaling of the light scales \(Q = \{k, 1/a_0\}\) and the cutoff, showing that each coupling contains a redundant piece entering at lower order than the piece encoding new physical information. The analysis is then extended to resolve the inconsistency between the \(\hbar\) expansion and the hard-cutoff limit reported by Epelbaum et al. (2018), and to clarify the relation between perturbative and non-perturbative renormalization for singular potentials.

## Key numbers

* In pionless EFT with PDS, the spurious contribution to the shape parameter at N2LO is \(v_2^{\mathrm{N}^2\mathrm{LO}} = \frac{1}{\Lambda - 1/a_0} (r_0/2)^2\), scaling as \(1/\Lambda\).
* In delta-shell regularization, the LO contact coupling scales as \(C_0 \sim 1/\lambda\) (i.e. \(1/Q\)), while the redundant part of \(C_4\) scales as \(1/\lambda^3\) and the physical part as \(1/\lambda^2\).
* The LO phase shift in delta-shell regularization has absolute cutoff dependence \(\mathcal{O}(k^2 r_c)\) and relative cutoff dependence \(\mathcal{O}(k r_c)\).
* The condition for the cutoff radius to keep cutoff dependence within the EFT error is \(r_c \leq \mathcal{O}(1/m_\pi)\), where \(m_\pi\) is the pion mass.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of redundant counterterms introduced by Valderrama (2016) and applies it to the inconsistency in the \(\hbar\) expansion identified by Epelbaum et al. (2018), providing a solution originally sketched in a comment by Valderrama (2019). It also connects to the improved actions approach of Contessi et al. (2023-2025), where subleading contacts are included at leading order to improve few-body convergence. The paper clarifies that the apparent incompatibility between perturbative BPHZ renormalization and non-perturbative renormalization of singular potentials (as formulated by Beane (2000), Pavon Valderrama (2005), and Nogga (2005)) only appears in the \(r_c \to 0\) limit, while for any finite cutoff the two approaches are equivalent.
