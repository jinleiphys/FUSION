# Poincaré Invariant Three-Body Scattering at Intermediate Energies

> The relativistic Faddeev equation for three-nucleon scattering is formulated in momentum space and directly solved in terms of momentum vectors without employing a partial wave decomposition. The equation is solved through Padé summation, and the numerical feasibility and stability of the solution is demonstrated. Relativistic invariance is achieved by constructing a dynamical unitary representation of the Poincaré group on the three-nucleon Hilbert space. Based on a Malfliet-Tjon type interaction, observables for elastic and break-up scattering are calculated for projectile energies in the intermediate energy range up to 2 GeV, and compared to their nonrelativistic counterparts. The convergence of the multiple scattering series is investigated as a function of the projectile energy in different scattering observables and configurations. Approximations to the two-body interaction embedded in the three-particle space are compared to the exact treatment.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that converged three-body scattering calculations using the relativistic Faddeev equation are numerically feasible at projectile energies up to 2 GeV laboratory kinetic energy, but only for a simplified spin-independent Malfliet-Tjon type interaction; the numerical accuracy degrades from about 1% at 1 GeV to about 3% at 2 GeV, and the authors do not present observables beyond 1.5 GeV because the model potential is too simple for higher energies.

## Method

The relativistic Faddeev equation for three-nucleon scattering was formulated in momentum space and solved directly in terms of momentum vectors without partial wave decomposition, using Padé summation for convergence. Relativistic invariance was achieved by constructing a dynamical unitary representation of the Poincaré group on the three-nucleon Hilbert space, with two-body interactions embedded via addition to the square of the mass operator to maintain phase-shift equivalence with the nonrelativistic interaction. The calculation employed a Malfliet-Tjon type interaction (superposition of attractive and repulsive Yukawa potentials supporting a deuteron bound state) and solved the integral equation in five variables using cubic Hermite splines near singularities and Gauss-Legendre quadrature elsewhere.

## Key numbers

- Optical theorem consistency check: total cross sections from sum of elastic and breakup cross sections agree with those from the optical theorem to about 1% or better up to 1 GeV projectile energy, increasing to about 3% at 2 GeV.
- At 1 GeV projectile energy, a q-grid size of N_q = 50 is sufficient for convergence; at 0.2 GeV N_{x_q} = 24 points suffice, at 0.5 GeV at least 28 points are needed, and at 1 GeV a minimum of 36 points in the x_q-grid is required.
- Estimated partial wave requirements for a three-boson system: at E_lab = 200 MeV, l_max = 5, lambda_max = 7, J_max = 12; at E_lab = 1 GeV, l_max = 14, lambda_max = 15, J_max = 29, yielding 125 l-lambda combinations for J=5 at 1 GeV versus 30 at 200 MeV.
- Deuteron binding energy used: E_d = 2.23 MeV.
- At 200 MeV, the exclusive breakup cross section at a specific configuration (theta_1 = theta_2 = 37 degrees, phi_12 = 180 degrees) shows relativistic effects increasing the central peak at S approximately 140 MeV by a factor of about 1.5 for the full partial wave calculation and about 2 for the full 3D calculation.

## Context

This work extends the nonrelativistic momentum-space Faddeev calculations without partial waves (Hang1, bound3d) to the relativistic domain, building on the Poincaré invariant formulation detailed in Lin (2007). It contrasts with partial-wave-based relativistic Faddeev calculations below pion-production threshold (Witala1, Witala2, Witala3) by demonstrating that direct vector integration avoids the prohibitive proliferation of partial waves at higher energies. The results enable future studies with realistic spin-dependent NN interactions (AV18, CD-Bonn, Nijmegen) in the intermediate energy regime, though the present work uses only a simplified bosonic model.
