# High-Energy Nuclear Physics with Lorentz Symmetry Violation

> If textbook Lorentz invariance is actually a property of the equations describing a sector of the excitations of vacuum above some critical distance scale, several sectors of matter with different critical speeds in vacuum can coexist and an absolute rest frame (the vacuum rest frame) may exist without contradicting the apparent Lorentz invariance felt by "ordinary" particles (particles with critical speed in vacuum equal to $c$, the speed of light). Sectorial Lorentz invariance, reflected by the fact that all particles of a given dynamical sector have the same critical speed in vacuum, will then be an expression of a fundamental sectorial symmetry (e.g. preonic grand unification or extended supersymmetry) protecting a parameter of the equations of motion. Furthermore, the sectorial Lorentz symmetry may be only a low-energy limit, in the same way as the relation $ω$ (frequency) = $c_s$ (speed of sound) $k$ (wave vector) holds for low-energy phonons in a crystal. In this context, phenomena such as the absence of Greisen-Zatsepin-Kuzmin cutoff for protons and nuclei and the stability of unstable particles (e.g. neutron, several nuclei...) at very high energy are basic properties of a wide class of noncausal models where local Lorentz invariance is broken introducing a fundamental length. Observable phenomena are expected at very short wavelength scales, even if Lorentz symmetry violation remains invisible to standard low-energy tests. We present a detailed discussion of the implications of Lorentz symmetry violation for very high-energy nuclear physics.

## Key claim

If Lorentz invariance is only a low-energy property of a sector of vacuum excitations above a critical distance scale, then several sectors of matter with different critical speeds can coexist, and phenomena such as the absence of the Greisen-Zatsepin-Kuzmin cutoff for protons and nuclei and the stability of unstable particles at very high energy become basic properties of a wide class of noncausal models where local Lorentz invariance is broken by introducing a fundamental length.

## Method

The paper derives a deformed dispersion relation parameterized by a fundamental length scale a (naturally identified with the Planck length) and a positive constant alpha between 10^-1 and 10^-2, then applies this kinematics to very high-energy cosmic rays, composite hadrons, and nuclei. The analysis considers how the deformation term alpha (k a)^3/2 competes with the mass term at high wave vectors, and extends the discussion to soliton models using a perturbed Phi^4 theory to estimate how nonlocality modifies the internal structure and kinematics of composite objects.

## Key numbers

- For a fundamental length a approximately 10^-33 cm, the critical energy where cross sections fall for a proton colliding with a 10^-3 eV cosmic microwave background photon is approximately 5 x 10^18 eV, assuming alpha approximately 1/12.
- For a approximately 10^-30 cm, the critical energy for the same process can be as low as approximately 10^17 eV; for a approximately 10^-35 cm, it would be approximately 5 x 10^19 eV.
- For a approximately 10^-33 cm, the fall of cross sections for a very high-energy gamma ray occurs above approximately 10^18 eV for collisions with 10^-3 eV photons, above approximately 10^17 eV for 10^-6 eV photons, and above approximately 10^16 eV for 10^-9 eV photons.
- For a approximately 10^-33 cm, the energy scale above which cosmic rays cannot deposit most of their energy in the atmosphere is around approximately 10^22 eV.
- For a approximately 10^-33 cm, the maximum velocity in the deformed kinematics occurs at approximately 10^19 eV for protons; for a approximately 10^-35 cm at approximately 10^20 eV; for a approximately 10^-30 cm at approximately 3 x 10^17 eV.
- For a approximately 10^-33 cm, the energy above which parton models for hadrons are expected to fail is approximately 10^19 eV; for a approximately 10^-35 cm, approximately 10^20 eV; for a approximately 10^-30 cm, approximately 3 x 10^17 eV.
- For a approximately 10^-33 cm and a soliton size Delta approximately 10^-13 cm, the energy scale where departure from standard relativity becomes leading is approximately 2 x 10^19 eV for a approximately 10^-33 cm, approximately 2 x 10^20 eV for a approximately 10^-35 cm, and approximately 5 x 10^17 eV for a approximately 10^-30 cm.
- For a approximately 10^-33 cm and alpha approximately 0.1 for protons, photodisintegration of nuclei by cosmic background photons would be inhibited above approximately N^(2/3) x 10^19 eV if the nucleus is treated as a single particle.
- For a approximately 10^-33 cm and alpha approximately 0.1, the maximum energy attainable by a nucleus made of N nucleons is approximately N^(1/2) x 10^19 eV if alpha scales as N^(-2).

## Context

This work extends the author's previous proposals (Gonzalez-Mestres, 1997a, 1997b, 1997c, 1997d) on Lorentz symmetry violation from nonlocal dynamics at the Planck scale, and contrasts with earlier nonlocal models by Redei (1967) and Blokhintsev (1966) that applied nonlocality only to interaction Hamiltonians rather than particle propagators. The paper enables a framework for interpreting ultra-high-energy cosmic ray events above 10^20 eV, which contradict standard relativistic predictions of a sharp GZK cutoff (Greisen, 1966; Zatsepin and Kuzmin, 1966) and challenge fits by Dova, Epele and Hojvat (1997). It also opens the question of whether nuclei, hadrons, and atoms follow the same deformed kinematics as elementary particles, with implications for future experimental tests using cosmic-ray observations.
