# Representing Equations of State With Strong First-Order Phase Transitions

> Parametric representations of the high-density nuclear equation of state are used in constructing models for interpreting the astrophysical observations of neutron stars. This study explores how accurately equations of state with strong first-order phase transitions can be represented using spectral or piecewise analytic methods that assume no {\it{a priori}} knowledge of the location or the strength of the phase transition. The model equations of state used in this study have phase transitions strong enough to induce a gravitational instability that terminates the sequence of stable neutron stars. These equations of state also admit a second sequence of stable stars with core matter that has undergone this strong first-order phase transition (possibly driven by quark deconfinement). These results indicate that spectral representations generally achieve somewhat higher accuracy than piecewise analytic representations having the same number of parameters. Both types of representation show power-law convergence at approximately the same rate.

## Key claim

Spectral and piecewise analytic parametric representations of neutron-star equations of state, which assume no prior knowledge of the location or strength of a first-order phase transition, can accurately represent equations of state with phase transitions strong enough to induce gravitational instability and a disconnected branch of stable hybrid stars, with spectral representations generally achieving somewhat higher accuracy than piecewise analytic representations having the same number of parameters.

## Method

The study constructed causal Chebyshev polynomial spectral representations and causal piecewise analytic representations of the ACB4(ΔP) and ACB5(ΔP) model equations of state, which describe hadron-quark phase transitions with varying transition widths parameterized by ΔP. Optimal parameter values were determined by minimizing a dimensionless error residual χ using the Levenberg-Marquardt method over the pressure range p_min = 1.23592430 × 10^32 erg/cm^3 to p_max = 1.13139676 × 10^36 erg/cm^3. The study also introduced two-zone representations that split the pressure domain at the phase transition point, using separate spectral or piecewise analytic representations below and above this point with three additional parameters for the transition location and properties.

## Key numbers

- The ACB4 equations of state trigger gravitational instability terminating the stable hadronic neutron star sequence at approximately 2.0 M⊙, while ACB5 does so at approximately 1.4 M⊙.
- The lower pressure bound used for representations was p_min = 1.23592430 × 10^32 erg/cm^3, corresponding to density ε_min = 5.41165156 × 10^13 g/cm^3 (about one fifth normal nuclear density).
- The upper pressure bound was p_max = 1.13139676 × 10^36 erg/cm^3, corresponding to central pressures of maximum mass quark stars from ACB4(ΔP) equations of state.
- For spectral representations of the discontinuous ΔP = 0 ACB4 and ACB5 equations of state, χ decreased monotonically from χ ≈ 0.5 for N_parms = 1 to χ ≈ 0.06 for N_parms = 10.
- The velocity parameter at the matching point was Υ_min = 13.7751914.
- ACB4(ΔP) equations of state with ΔP < 0.05 trigger gravitational instability while admitting stable composite hadron-quark stars; ACB5(ΔP) equations of state with ΔP < 0.02 do the same.

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Lindblom (2024) and Lindblom (2024b) that tested spectral and piecewise analytic representations of neutron-star equations of state with phase transitions, but which were criticized for not including phase transitions strong enough to trigger gravitational instability and allow disconnected sequences of stable higher-density stars. The study uses the ACB4 and ACB5 hadron-quark phase transition models introduced by Paschalidis (2018) and their generalizations by Blaschke (2020) incorporating mixed-phase regions. The results enable the use of these parametric representations for solving the relativistic inverse stellar structure problem with equations of state that exhibit strong first-order phase transitions, potentially relevant for interpreting neutron-star observations from x-ray and gravitational wave measurements.
