# Maximum Entropy Analysis of the Spectral Functions in Lattice QCD

> First principle calculation of the QCD spectral functions (SPFs) based on the lattice QCD simulations is reviewed. Special emphasis is placed on the Bayesian inference theory and the Maximum Entropy Method (MEM), which is a useful tool to extract SPFs from the imaginary-time correlation functions numerically obtained by the Monte Carlo method. Three important aspects of MEM are (i) it does not require a priori assumptions or parametrizations of SPFs, (ii) for given data, a unique solution is obtained if it exists, and (iii) the statistical significance of the solution can be quantitatively analyzed. The ability of MEM is explicitly demonstrated by using mock data as well as lattice QCD data. When applied to lattice data, MEM correctly reproduces the low-energy resonances and shows the existence of high-energy continuum in hadronic correlation functions. This opens up various possibilities for studying hadronic properties in QCD beyond the conventional way of analyzing the lattice data. Future problems to be studied by MEM in lattice QCD are also summarized.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the Maximum Entropy Method (MEM) can reliably extract hadronic spectral functions from lattice QCD data without requiring a priori assumptions or parametrizations of the spectral shape, provided the data have sufficient statistical quality and the Bayesian inference framework is properly applied.

## Method

The authors apply MEM to reconstruct spectral functions from imaginary-time correlation functions obtained via quenched lattice QCD Monte Carlo simulations on a \(20^3 \times 24\) lattice at \(\beta_{lat}=6.0\). The method uses Bayes' theorem to combine a likelihood function (Gaussian for the lattice data) with a prior probability based on the Shannon-Jaynes entropy, which incorporates a default model \(m(\omega)\). The most probable spectral function is found by maximizing \(Q = \alpha S - L\), where \(\alpha\) controls the balance between entropy and likelihood, and the final output is obtained by averaging over \(\alpha\) using Bryan's singular value decomposition algorithm.

## Key numbers

- The lattice used is \(20^3 \times 24\) with \(\beta_{lat}=6.0\) for quenched QCD simulations at \(T=0\).
- The number of temporal lattice sites \(N_\tau\) is 24, and the number of data points used in MEM is \(N = \tau_{max}/a - \tau_{min}/a + 1\), which is of order 10.
- The frequency space is discretized into \(N_\omega\) pixels of size \(\Delta \omega \sim O(10\ \text{MeV})\), with \(N_\omega \sim O(10^3)\).
- The covariance matrix \(C_{ij}\) is defined with normalization factor \(1/[N_{conf}(N_{conf}-1)]\), where \(N_{conf}\) is the total number of gauge configurations.

## Context

This work extends earlier attempts to extract spectral functions from lattice QCD, which relied on specific ansätze such as pole-plus-continuum parametrizations (e.g., Shuryak (1993) and the QCD-TARO collaboration). The MEM approach was previously applied in condensed matter physics (e.g., Jarrell and Gubernatis (1996)) and nuclear physics (Koonin et al.), and the authors adapt it to QCD to enable first-principles study of hadronic properties beyond static quantities like masses and decay constants. The method opens the possibility of analyzing medium modifications of hadrons at finite temperature and baryon chemical potential, relevant to heavy-ion collision experiments at CERN SPS.
