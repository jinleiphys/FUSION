# Shape Deformations in Atomic Nuclei

## Key claim

The ground states of some nuclei are described by spherical densities and mean fields while others exhibit deformed shapes, representing spontaneous symmetry breaking; however, the observed rotational spectra in essentially all known cases uniquely identify the existence of both axial- and R-symmetry, with only a single known exception (the wobbling mode in ¹⁶³Lu) clearly exhibiting triaxial shape.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework for nuclear deformation and rotation, including the cranking model for moments of inertia, the SU₃ model for collective rotation within a harmonic oscillator shell, and the Nilsson diagram for single-particle states in axially-symmetric quadrupole-deformed potentials. The analysis connects observed rotational band structures, E2 transition probabilities, and intrinsic quadrupole moments to deformation parameters, and discusses the role of BCS pair correlations in reducing moments of inertia from rigid-body values.

## Key numbers

- Moments of inertia observed in ground-state bands of well-deformed even-even nuclei are systematically a factor of 2–3 smaller than the values for rigid rotation of the deformed body.
- For axially-symmetric quadrupole deformation, the relation between deformation parameters is β ≈ 1.06 δ to leading order.
- Superdeformation corresponds to ω⊥:ωz = 2:1 with δosc = 0.6 (prolate) or δosc = −0.75 (oblate).
- The ratio R = E(4⁺₁)/E(2⁺₁) observed in even-even nuclei has a sharp peak around R = 3.3, indicating an axially-symmetric rotor.
- The neutron drip line has been experimentally reached in oxygen isotopes, with ²⁴O₁₆ being the heaviest element inside the drip line.
- The Iπ = 1⁻ state in ²²⁴Ra₁₃₆ is observed at 0.216 MeV, indicating incipient octupole instability.
- In ¹⁶O, a rotational band begins at 6.05 MeV; in ⁴⁰Ca, at 5.21 MeV.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on nuclear deformation and rotation from Bohr and Mottelson (1975) and Rowe and Spring (1980), and connects to the BCS theory of pair correlations applied to nuclei by Bohr, Mottelson, and Pines (1958). The paper enables further study of shape coexistence, superdeformed bands at high spin as observed by Janssens and Khoo (1991), and the exploration of exotic shapes in nuclei far from stability using radioactive ion beam facilities and advanced gamma-ray detector systems.
