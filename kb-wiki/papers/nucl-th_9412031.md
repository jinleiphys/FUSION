# Scaling with no phase transition

## Key claim
The observation of scaling relations in scaled factorial moments (SFMs) from ultra-relativistic nuclear collisions would not by itself signal that the hot matter has passed through a phase transition; such scaling could also arise if the system approaches the critical temperature from below without crossing into the high-temperature phase, or if the equation of state exhibits a fast but smooth transition with no actual phase change.

## Method
The author analyzes the Ginzburg-Landau mean field theory of phase transitions used by Mohanty and Kataria (1994) and by Hwa and collaborators (1992), in which the free energy density depends on an order parameter with quadratic, quartic, and sextic terms. The author demonstrates that the scaling relation \(F_q \propto F_2^{(q-1)^{1.33}}\) derived by Mohanty and Kataria does not require the system to cross a phase transition, but can occur under conditions where the quadratic coefficient becomes small without changing sign, or where the critical temperature is approached from below at infinite energy density.

## Key numbers
- The scaling relation claimed by Mohanty and Kataria (1994) is \(F_q \propto F_2^{(q-1)^{1.33}}\), where \(F_q\) is the scaled factorial moment of order \(q\).
- The Ginzburg-Landau free energy density used is \(F[\phi] = a'(T-T_c) |\phi|^2 + b |\phi|^4 + c |\phi|^6\), with a second-order phase transition for \(b>0\) and first-order for \(b<0\).

## Context
This paper directly contradicts the claims of Mohanty and Kataria (1994) and Hwa and Nazirov (1992) that scaling of SFMs would signal passage through a phase transition. The work extends the Ginzburg-Landau mean field analysis to show that the scaling can be produced without any phase transition, for example when the system approaches \(T_c\) from below but never crosses into the high-temperature phase. This result implies that experimental observation of such scaling in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions cannot be taken as evidence for quark-gluon plasma formation, and that additional observables are needed to distinguish true phase transitions from smooth crossovers.
