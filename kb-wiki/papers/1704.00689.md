# Microscopic Theory of Nuclear Fission

> We describe the fission dynamics of $^{240}$Pu within an implementation of the Density Functional Theory (DFT) extended to superfluid systems and real-time dynamics. We demonstrate the critical role played by the pairing correlations, which even though are not the driving force in this complex dynamics, are providing the essential lubricant, without which the nuclear shape evolution would come to a screeching halt. The evolution is found to be much slower than previously expected in this fully non-adiabatic treatment of nuclear dynamics, where there are no symmetry restrictions and all collective degrees of freedom (CDOF) are allowed to participate in the dynamics.

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that pairing correlations, while not the driving force in fission dynamics, act as an essential "lubricant" that enables shape evolution toward scission; without them, the nuclear shape evolution would halt. The fully non-adiabatic treatment, with no symmetry restrictions and all collective degrees of freedom allowed to participate, yields a saddle-to-scission time much longer than previously expected.

## Method
The authors performed time-dependent superfluid local density approximation (TDSLDA) simulations of induced fission of $^{240}$Pu, initialized near the outer fission barrier with an excitation energy of about 8 MeV. The energy density functional (EDF) was based on the SLy4 interaction supplemented with a pairing anomalous contribution, and the equations were discretized on a 3D spatial lattice with a momentum cutoff of approximately 500 MeV/c. No symmetry restrictions were imposed, and the meanfield and pairing potentials were determined self-consistently from instantaneous nucleon densities at each time step.

## Key numbers
- Saddle-to-scission time for $^{240}$Pu with normal pairing strength: approximately 14,000 fm/c.
- Saddle-to-scission time for $^{240}$Pu with artificially enhanced pairing strength: approximately 1,400 fm/c (about ten times faster).
- Average atomic mass of the light fission fragment from simulation: $A_L \approx 105.3$, compared to systematic data $A^S_L \approx 100.6$.
- Average neutron number of the light fragment: $N_L \approx 63.5$, compared to systematic data $N^S_L \approx 61$.
- Average charge of the light fragment: $Z_L \approx 39.7$, matching the systematic value $Z_L^S \approx 39.7$.
- Total kinetic energy of fission fragments from simulation: 181.6 MeV, compared to the systematic value 177.3 MeV.
- Estimated number of post-scission neutrons emitted: 2-3, in reasonable agreement with experiment.
- Excitation energy of the initial $^{240}$Pu nucleus: about 8 MeV, corresponding to induced fission $^{239}$Pu(n,f) with a neutron kinetic energy of about 1.5 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the Density Functional Theory (DFT) framework to superfluid systems and real-time dynamics, building on the Superfluid Local Density Approximation (SLDA) developed by the authors and collaborators (Bulgac 2002, 2003; ARNPS 2013). It directly contradicts the much shorter saddle-to-scission times predicted by phenomenological models and semi-microscopic approaches that impose symmetry restrictions or limit collective degrees of freedom. The results enable future fully microscopic studies of fission fragment distributions, total kinetic energies, and neutron emission without parameter fitting, and demonstrate the recently described mechanism of phase-locking in superfluid systems (Bulgac 2017).
