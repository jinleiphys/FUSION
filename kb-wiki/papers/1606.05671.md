# Uncertainties in the production of $p$ nuclei in massive stars obtained from Monte Carlo variations

> Nuclear uncertainties in the production of $p$ nuclei in massive stars have been quantified in a Monte Carlo procedure. Bespoke temperature-dependent uncertainties were assigned to different types of reactions involving nuclei from Fe to Bi. Their simultaneous impact was studied in postprocessing explosive trajectories for three different stellar models. It was found that the grid of mass zones in the model of a 25 $M_\odot$ star, which is widely used for investigations of $p$ nucleosynthesis, is too crude to properly resolve the detailed temperature changes required for describing the production of $p$ nuclei. Using models with finer grids for 15 $M_\odot$ and 25 $M_\odot$ stars with initial solar metallicity, it was found that most of the production uncertainties introduced by nuclear reaction uncertainties are smaller than a factor of two. Since a large number of rates were varied at the same time in the Monte Carlo procedure, possible cancellation effects of several uncertainties could be taken into account. Key rates were identified for each $p$ nucleus, which provide the dominant contribution to the production uncertainty. These key rates were found by examining correlations between rate variations and resulting abundance changes. This method is superior to studying flow patterns, especially when the flows are complex, and to individual, sequential variation of a few rates.

## Key claim

The paper quantifies nuclear reaction rate uncertainties in the production of p nuclei in massive stars using a Monte Carlo method, finding that most production uncertainties are smaller than a factor of two when using stellar models with finer mass-zone grids, but that the widely used 25 solar mass model grid is too crude to properly resolve the temperature changes required for describing p-nucleus production.

## Method

The authors applied a Monte Carlo framework (PizBuin) to simultaneously vary reaction rates within predefined, temperature-dependent uncertainty limits for nuclei from Fe to Bi, using postprocessing explosive trajectories from three different stellar models (15 and 25 solar mass stars with finer grids). They ran 10,000 Monte Carlo iterations, each time drawing random variation factors from uniform distributions based on combined experimental and theoretical uncertainties, and then analyzed the resulting abundance distributions and correlations between rate variations and final abundances to identify key rates.

## Key numbers

* Most production uncertainties from nuclear reaction uncertainties are smaller than a factor of two for the 15 solar mass and 25 solar mass models with finer grids.
* The standard 25 solar mass model grid was found to be too crude to properly resolve temperature changes required for p-nucleus production.
* 10,000 Monte Carlo iterations were confirmed as sufficient for statistical significance.
* The reaction network included about 3800 nuclei from Fe to Bi.
* Theory uncertainty factors for (n,gamma) reactions were set to 2.0 for both upper and lower limits.
* Theory uncertainty factors for (alpha,gamma), (alpha,n), and (alpha,p) reactions were set to 2.0 for the upper limit and 10.0 for the lower limit.
* Theory uncertainty factors for (p,gamma) and (p,n) reactions were set to 2.0 for the upper limit and 3.0 for the lower limit.
* Weak interaction rates were assigned a theory uncertainty factor of 10 for both upper and lower limits.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the gamma process in massive stars (e.g., Arnould 1976, Rayet et al. 1995, Rauscher et al. 2002) by providing a systematic quantification of nuclear uncertainties rather than relying on manual inspection of reaction flows or sequential variation of a few rates. The Monte Carlo approach enables identification of key rates through correlation analysis, which is superior to studying flow patterns when flows are complex, and the results can be propagated into Galactic Chemical Evolution models to better understand the origin of p nuclei and long-lived radioactivities such as 92Nb, 146Sm, and 97,98Tc.
