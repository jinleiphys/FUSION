# Bulk pressure in fluid-dynamical simulations of Pb-Pb and p-Pb collisions at the LHC energies

> State-of-the-art fluid dynamical simulations of relativistic heavy-ion collisions employ initial state models which result in a rather strong radial flow. In order to fit the experimental observables, a non-negligible bulk viscosity of the QGP and/or hadronic matter is required. We examine modern parametrizations of the bulk viscosity to entropy density ratio $ζ/s$ used in recent fluid dynamical simulations, and explore the relative magnitude of the associated bulk viscous corrections in space-time, for Pb-Pb and p-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.76$ TeV LHC energy with a state-of-the-art initial state provided by TRENTo model. It turns out that, at a typical particlization hypersurface, the effect of bulk viscosity out-competes the one of shear viscosity, and in a significantly large part of the space-time volume, the bulk pressure strongly counteracts the equilibrium pressure, thus the effective pressure approaches zero. The latter potentially challenges the applicability of fluid-dynamical modelling of heavy ion-ion and proton-ion collisions at the LHC energies.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that in state-of-the-art fluid-dynamical simulations of Pb-Pb and p-Pb collisions at LHC energies, modern parametrizations of bulk viscosity produce a bulk pressure that counteracts the equilibrium pressure so strongly that the effective pressure approaches zero in a large part of the space-time volume, potentially challenging the applicability of fluid-dynamical modelling. The authors hedge that this conclusion depends on the specific parametrization used, with Param. II being the most problematic and Param. III being "much more comfortable for hydrodynamic modelling."

## Method

The authors used the 3+1 dimensional relativistic viscous hydrodynamic code vHLLE with initial conditions from the TRENTo model (reduced thickness ansatz) to simulate Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=2.76 TeV and p-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=5.02 TeV. They implemented three different temperature-dependent parametrizations of the bulk viscosity to entropy density ratio zeta/s (Param. I from Ryu (2017), Param. II from Schenke (2019), and Param. III from Schenke (2020)) and compared the relative magnitude of bulk viscous corrections versus shear viscous corrections across the space-time evolution.

## Key numbers

- Param. I: zeta/s peaks at approximately 0.3 around T_p=180 MeV, with a narrow peak
- Param. II: zeta/s peaks at B_norm=0.24 at T_peak=165 MeV, with a broad peak (B_width=1.5, T_width=50 MeV)
- Param. III: zeta/s peaks at B_n=0.13 at T_mu=160 MeV, with B_1=10 MeV and B_2=120 MeV
- For 20-30% central Pb-Pb collisions with Param. II, the fraction of fluid cells where bulk pressure is at least -0.5 of equilibrium pressure rises to nearly 100% and stays there throughout the remaining hydrodynamic expansion
- For 20-30% central Pb-Pb collisions with Param. II, the fraction of fluid cells where Pi < -0.8p peaks at 50% around tau=6 fm/c
- For p-Pb collisions with Param. II, the fraction of cells where Pi < -0.8p is higher than in Pb-Pb and does not drop with evolution time
- The shear viscosity was fixed at eta/s=0.12 for all viscous simulations

## Context

This work extends the study by Byres (2019), which first showed that modern zeta/s parametrizations could produce negative effective pressure but only used Gaussian initial conditions appropriate for high-multiplicity pp collisions. The authors apply the same analysis to realistic TRENTo initial conditions for Pb-Pb and p-Pb collisions at LHC energies. The results connect to causality studies by Cheng (2021) and Plumberg (2021), which showed that the ratio Pi/(epsilon+p) enters into causality constraints for DNMR viscous hydrodynamics, with the sufficient condition requiring this ratio to be much less than 1. The findings raise concerns for Bayesian analyses attempting to constrain zeta/s from model-to-data comparisons, as the hydrodynamic output may become unreliable in the parameter space where cavitation (negative effective pressure) dominates.
