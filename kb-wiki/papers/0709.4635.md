# Color superconductivity in dense quark matter

> Matter at high density and low temperature is expected to be a color superconductor, which is a degenerate Fermi gas of quarks with a condensate of Cooper pairs near the Fermi surface that induces color Meissner effects. At the highest densities, where the QCD coupling is weak, rigorous calculations are possible, and the ground state is a particularly symmetric state, the color-flavor locked (CFL) phase. The CFL phase is a superfluid, an electromagnetic insulator, and breaks chiral symmetry. The effective theory of the low-energy excitations in the CFL phase is known and can be used, even at more moderate densities, to describe its physical properties. At lower densities the CFL phase may be disfavored by stresses that seek to separate the Fermi surfaces of the different flavors, and comparison with the competing alternative phases, which may break translation and/or rotation invariance, is done using phenomenological models. We review the calculations that underlie these results, and then discuss transport properties of several color-superconducting phases and their consequences for signatures of color superconductivity in neutron stars.

## Key claim

At asymptotically high densities where the QCD coupling is weak, the ground state of dense quark matter is the color-flavor locked (CFL) phase, but the weak-coupling calculations that rigorously establish this are only quantitatively reliable at chemical potentials μ ≳ 10⁸ MeV, many orders of magnitude above the densities found in neutron star interiors.

## Method

The authors derive and solve the QCD gap equation for color-superconducting phases using the 2PI effective action formalism in Nambu-Gorkov space, approximating the fermion self-energy at one-loop order with gluon propagators in the hard-dense loop approximation. They analyze the quasiparticle excitation spectrum by computing the eigenvalues of the matrix ℳℳ† for different pairing patterns, and evaluate the condensation energy from the pressure difference between superconducting and normal phases. The gap equation is solved at weak coupling including the effects of unscreened magnetic gluon exchanges and wave function renormalization.

## Key numbers

- The weak-coupling expansion parameter for log(Δ/μ) is at best g, not g²; the leading term is of order 1/g, with log g and g⁰ terms also calculated, while O(g log g) and O(g) terms remain uncalculated
- The weak-coupling calculations become quantitatively reliable only for g(μ) < 0.8, which corresponds to μ ≳ 10⁸ MeV, meaning densities 15-16 orders of magnitude greater than those at the centers of compact stars
- In the CFL phase, the eigenvalues of ℳℳ† are λ₁ = 4 (1-fold degenerate) and λ₂ = 1 (8-fold degenerate), giving one quasiparticle with gap 2Δ and eight with gap Δ
- In the 2SC phase, the eigenvalues are λ₁ = 1 (4-fold degenerate) and λ₂ = 0 (5-fold degenerate), giving four gapped quasiparticles with gap Δ and five ungapped quasiparticles
- The condensation energy density at T=0 is δP = (μ²/4π²) Σᵣ Tr[𝒫ᵣ] λᵣ Δ², giving δP ∝ 12Δ²_CFL for CFL and δP ∝ 4Δ²_2SC for 2SC
- In BCS theory, the critical temperature T_c = 0.57Δ

## Context

This review extends the earlier work on color superconductivity by Alford, Rajagopal, and Wilczek (1999) and Schafer (1999) by providing a comprehensive weak-coupling analysis of the QCD gap equation and quasiparticle properties. The calculations provide a rigorous foundation from which to extrapolate downward in chemical potential using effective field theory, and enable quantitative comparison between competing phases such as CFL and 2SC. The results guide phenomenological models that address the strongly coupled regime at μ ∼ 500 MeV relevant to neutron stars, and establish benchmarks for understanding the effects of strange quark mass and neutrality constraints on the QCD phase diagram.
