# Trapped Fermions Through Kolmogorov-Arnold Wavefunctions

> We investigate a variational Monte Carlo framework for trapped one-dimensional mixture of spin-$\frac{1}{2}$ fermions using Kolmogorov-Arnold networks (KANs) to construct universal neural-network wavefunction ansätze. The method can, in principle, achieve arbitrary accuracy, limited only by the Monte Carlo sampling and was checked against exact results at sub-percent precision. For attractive interactions, it captures pairing effects, and in the impurity case it agrees with known results. We present a method of systematic transfer learning in the number of network parameters, allowing for efficient training for a target precision. We vastly increase the efficiency of the method by incorporating the short-distance behavior of the wavefunction into the ansätz without biasing the method.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a variational Monte Carlo framework using Kolmogorov-Arnold networks (KANs) can achieve sub-percent precision for trapped one-dimensional spin-1/2 fermions, but the method's accuracy is ultimately limited by Monte Carlo sampling statistics and the number of spline knots, not by the network architecture itself.

## Method

The authors construct a neural-network wavefunction ansatz for spin-1/2 fermions in a one-dimensional harmonic trap using two Slater determinants of harmonic oscillator eigenstates multiplied by a Jastrow factor parametrized by a Kolmogorov-Arnold network (KAN). The KAN uses quadratic splines with K knots to represent single-variable functions, and the ansatz explicitly incorporates the short-distance cusp behavior from the delta-function interaction via an additional term Omega. Energy minimization is performed using the ADAM algorithm with Metropolis Monte Carlo sampling, and the number of spline knots is systematically doubled during training to improve precision.

## Context

This work substantially improves upon the authors' previous variational Monte Carlo study of the same system (Bedaque et al., 2024) by introducing Kolmogorov-Arnold networks as the wavefunction ansatz and incorporating short-distance cusp behavior explicitly. The method extends neural-network quantum state approaches originally developed for spin systems (Carleo and Troyer, 2017) and later applied to continuous systems with Coulomb forces (Paulinet et al., 2019; FermiNet, 2020), now adapted for short-range interactions relevant to nuclear physics. The results enable systematic transfer learning across coupling strengths and particle numbers, and the exponential scaling of precision with spline knots suggests the method can achieve "exact" Monte Carlo status in one dimension, with extension to three dimensions identified as the next step.
