# Unified description of structure and reactions: implementing the Nuclear Field Theory program

> The modern theory of the atomic nucleus results from the merging of the liquid drop (Niels Bohr and Fritz Kalckar) and of the shell model (Marie Goeppert Meyer and Axel Jensen), which contributed the concepts of collective excitations and of independent-particle motion respectively. The unification of these apparently contradictory views in terms of the particle-vibration (rotation) coupling (Aage Bohr and Ben Mottelson) has allowed for an ever increasingly complete, accurate and detailed description of the nuclear structure, Nuclear Field Theory (NFT, developed by the Copenhagen-Buenos Aires collaboration) providing a powerful quantal embodiment. In keeping with the fact that reactions are not only at the basis of quantum mechanics (statistical interpretation, Max Born) , but also the specific tools to probe the atomic nucleus, NFT is being extended to deal with processes which involve the continuum in an intrinsic fashion, so as to be able to treat them on an equal footing with those associated with discrete states (nuclear structure). As a result, spectroscopic studies of transfer to continuum states could eventually use at profit the NFT rules, extended to take care of recoil effects. In the present contribution we review the implementation of the NFT program of structure and reactions, setting special emphasis on open problems and outstanding predictions.

## Key claim

Nuclear Field Theory (NFT), originally developed for bound-state nuclear structure, is being extended to treat continuum processes on an equal footing with discrete states, enabling spectroscopic studies of transfer to continuum states using NFT rules extended to include recoil effects. The authors review the implementation of this program with emphasis on open problems and outstanding predictions, noting that the validity of NFT predictions has received strong experimental confirmation for light halo nuclei.

## Method

The authors apply the particle-vibration coupling (PVC) mechanism within the NFT framework, using independent-particle and collective vibrational states as basis states implemented with a SLy4 effective interaction and an Argonne v14(¹S₀) pairing potential. HFB provides the quasiparticle spectrum while QRPA generates density and spin modes, and renormalization processes (self-energy, vertex corrections, phonon renormalization, and phonon exchange) are calculated to obtain dressed particles and the induced pairing interaction. For reactions, the authors use simultaneous and successive transfer processes corrected by non-orthogonality and Pauli principle effects, implemented in the software Cooper, with global optical potentials for elastic scattering except for the ¹¹Li(p,t)⁹Li reaction where an empirical potential was employed.

## Key numbers

* The halo radius of ¹¹Li is 3.55 ± 0.1 fm, compared to the ⁹Li core radius of approximately 2.69 fm
* The halo Cooper pair is bound to the ⁹Li core by approximately 400 keV, with the bare pairing interaction contributing only about 100 keV
* The induced pairing interaction accounts for close to 85% of Cooper pair binding in ¹¹Li, and about 50% in open shell nuclei along the stability valley
* A consistent fraction of approximately 8% of the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn (TRK) sum-rule associated with the giant dipole resonance is brought down by tens of MeV in ¹¹Li due to the neutron skin effect
* The first excited (0⁺)* halo state of ¹²Be lies at 2.25 MeV, with a 1⁻ state observed at 0.460 MeV above it (2.71 MeV above ground state)
* The ¹¹Li ground state transition absolute differential cross section depends on the |(s₁/₂,p₁/₂)₁⁻ ⊗ GDPR;0⁺⟩ ⊗ |p₃/₂(π)⟩ component of the wavefunction through normalization

## Context

This work extends the Nuclear Field Theory program developed by the Copenhagen-Buenos Aires collaboration (Bes et al., 1974-1977) to unify structure and reactions, building on the particle-vibration coupling framework of Bohr and Mottelson (1975). The paper specifically addresses the pairing anti-halo effect identified by Bennaceur et al. (2000) and Hamamoto and Mottelson (2004), showing that the exchange of low-lying collective dipole modes (pygmy resonances) provides the mechanism for Cooper pair binding in halo nuclei like ¹¹Li. The results enable quantitative testing of NFT wavefunctions through absolute two-nucleon transfer cross sections at the 10% uncertainty level, as demonstrated by comparison with the experimental data of Tanihata et al. (2008) and Potel et al. (2010, 2013), and open questions about the extension of the Axel-Brink hypothesis to halo systems and the calculation of optical potentials for exotic nuclei.
