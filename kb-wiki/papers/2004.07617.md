# Inelasticity resulting from rapidity spectra analysis

> In this work we study the pseudorapidity spectra o charged particles produced in proton+proton and proton+antiproton interactions in a wide energy range using the non-extensive Tsallis approach. We evaluate the inelasticity coefficients of the discussed reactions which remain approximately independent of the collision energy.

## Key claim

The inelasticity coefficient in proton+proton and proton+antiproton collisions is approximately independent of collision energy, remaining constant at K ≈ 0.50 ± 0.02 over the energy range 17.3 GeV to 8000 GeV, with a dispersion of σ = 0.05.

## Method

The authors fit pseudorapidity distributions of charged particles from NA49, UA5, UA7, CMS, and TOTEM experiments using a Tsallis non-extensive statistics formula with three parameters: longitudinal temperature T, mean transverse mass μT, and non-extensivity parameter q. The parameter q is fixed from multiplicity fluctuations via the negative binomial distribution, and μT is derived from the known energy dependence of average transverse momentum ⟨pT⟩, leaving only the longitudinal temperature T as a free parameter in the fits. The inelasticity coefficient K is then calculated by integrating the fitted pseudorapidity spectra weighted by particle energy up to the maximum pseudorapidity ηmax = ln(√s/μT).

## Key numbers

- Inelasticity coefficient ⟨K⟩ = 0.50 ± 0.02, with dispersion σ = 0.05, over the energy range 17.3 GeV to 8000 GeV.
- Longitudinal temperature energy dependence: T(√s) = 0.16 (√s)^0.77 (units: GeV).
- Multiplicity energy dependence: N(√s) = 2.73 (√s)^0.36.
- Average transverse momentum parameterization: ⟨pT⟩(s) = 0.413 − 0.0171 ln s + 0.00143 ln² s (units: GeV/c), with s in GeV².
- Negative binomial distribution shape parameter: k⁻¹ = −0.104 ± 0.004 + (0.058 ± 0.001) ln √s.
- Specific heat ratio extracted from the emitting center model: κ = 1.64.

## Context

This work extends earlier estimates of inelasticity from cosmic ray experiments (Zatsepin, 1940s; Cocconi, 1958) and from the Bologna-CERN-Frascati Collaboration at ISR (Basile, 1980) by applying a Tsallis non-extensive approach to a wider energy range up to 8000 GeV. The result that inelasticity remains constant at K ≈ 0.5 contradicts some QCD-inspired models that predicted a decrease with energy (Wlodarczyk, 1995; Beggio, 2020) and others that predicted an increase (Shabelski, 1991). The analysis enables a model-independent extraction of inelasticity from rapidity spectra using only one free parameter (longitudinal temperature), and supports the use of constant inelasticity in cosmic ray cascade simulations and in statistical/hydrodynamical models of multiparticle production (Carruthers, 1983; Shuryak, 1984).
