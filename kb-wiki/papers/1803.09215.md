# Electrical Resistivity and Hall Effect in Binary Neutron-Star Mergers

> We examine the range of rest-mass densities, temperatures and magnetic fields involved in simulations of binary neutron-star mergers and identify the conditions under which the ideal-magnetohydrodynamics approximation breaks down and hence the magnetic-field decay should be accounted for. We use recent calculations of the conductivities of warm correlated plasma in envelopes of compact stars and find that the magnetic-field decay timescales are much larger than the characteristic timescales of the merger process for lengthscales down to a meter. Because these are smaller than the currently available resolution in numerical simulations, the ideal-magnetohydrodynamics approximation is effectively valid for all realistic simulations. At the same time, we find that the Hall effect can be important at low densities and low temperatures, where it can induce a non-dissipative rearrangement of the magnetic field. Finally, we mark the region in temperature and density where the hydrodynamic description breaks down.

## Key claim

The ideal-magnetohydrodynamics approximation remains valid for all realistic binary neutron-star merger simulations because magnetic-field decay timescales are much larger than the characteristic merger timescales for lengthscales down to one meter, which is smaller than current numerical resolution; however, the Hall effect can become important at low densities and low temperatures, inducing non-dissipative magnetic-field rearrangement.

## Method

The authors used recent calculations of the electrical conductivity tensor for warm correlated plasma in compact star envelopes (Harutyunyan 2016a, Harutyunyan 2016b) to compute Ohmic diffusion timescales and Hall timescales across the range of rest-mass densities, temperatures, and magnetic fields encountered in binary neutron-star merger simulations. They extracted characteristic lengthscales of magnetic-field and density variations from an ideal general-relativistic magnetohydrodynamics simulation of an equal-mass binary merger using the Illinois GRMHD code with a finest resolution of approximately 227 meters, then compared the derived dissipation timescales to the typical dynamical timescale of the merger process (approximately 10 milliseconds).

## Key numbers

- Ohmic diffusion timescale at low densities: tau_d approximately 5 x 10^11 (rho / 1 g cm^-3)^0.1 (T / 1 MeV)^0.8 (lambda_B / 1 km)^2 seconds, which is much larger than the merger timescale of 10 milliseconds
- Lower limit on Ohmic diffusion timescale for lambda_B = 1 meter, rho approximately 10^-3 g cm^-3, and T = 0.1 MeV: tau_d approximately 10^4 seconds
- Hall timescale expression: tau_B approximately 1.2 x 10^2 x B_13^-1 (rho / 1 g cm^-3) (lambda_B / 1 km)^2 seconds, where B_13 = B / (10^13 G)
- Hall timescale reaches 10 milliseconds for lambda_B = 1 kilometer at densities rho less than or equal to 10^-4 g cm^-3, and for lambda_B = 1 meter at densities rho less than or equal to 10^2 g cm^-3
- Transition from Ohmic to Hall-dominated regime occurs at densities rho less than or equal to 10^11 B_13 g cm^-3, independent of lengthscale

## Context

This work extends prior resistive magnetohydrodynamics simulations by Dionysopoulou (2012, 2015) that used simplified density-dependent conductivities, replacing them with realistic temperature- and composition-dependent conductivities from Harutyunyan (2016a). The finding that ideal magnetohydrodynamics remains valid for current resolutions contradicts the previous expectation that nearly zero conductivity in low-density regions would make resistive effects important. The identification of regimes where the Hall effect is relevant enables future dissipative simulations to include this non-dissipative magnetic-field rearrangement, and the mapping of where the magnetohydrodynamic description breaks down guides the need for kinetic plasma descriptions in future work.
