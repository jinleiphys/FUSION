# Deep Inelastic Scattering with Application to Nuclear Targets: Lectures at the 1985 Los Alamos School on Relativistic Dynamics and Quark Nuclear Physics

> This paper is essentially a verbatim reconstruction of lectures that I gave at the Los Alamos School on Relativistic Dynamics and Quark Nuclear Physics in 1985. They were published in the school proceedings, but the book is not widely available. The Los Alamos School took place at the height of the first wave of interest in the quark substructure of nuclei, stimulated by the 1983 discovery of the EMC Effect. Interest in this subject has been increasing for years and the prospect of a dedicated Electron Ion Collider within the decade guarantees even greater attention to quarks and gluons in nuclei among both theorists and experimentalists. Recently, to my surprise, I learned that copies of my old lectures have been circulating and been found useful by the relatively few people who know about them. The are, of course, dated: experiments have far outstripped what was available 37 years ago and theory has progressed too. However, the rest frame derivation of the parton model, the derivation and discussion of the convolution formalism for nucleons, nucleon correlations, and other, virtual, constituents of nuclei, and sections on scaling violation and the operator product expansion have aged pretty well and seem to still be useful. With the help and encouragement of Richard Milner, I have recreated the LaTeX files necessary to post the 1985 Lectures on the arXiv, making them available to the nuclear and particle physics community. Apart from correcting some typographical errors, I have made no attempt to edit, improve, or update these lectures. I hope readers will nevertheless find them useful.

## Key claim

These 1985 lectures argue that deep inelastic scattering (DIS) can probe quark distributions in nuclei, and that the EMC effect, a ∼15% difference between nuclear and nucleon structure functions, indicates that the typical length scale for quark propagation in nuclear ground states is longer than in free nucleons, with the rescaling analysis providing a model-independent QCD-based interpretation.

## Method

The lectures derive the parton model from a target-rest-frame perspective using light-cone coordinates, showing that in the Bjorken limit the dominant contribution comes from light-like separations (ξ² → 0) rather than short distances. They then develop the convolution formalism for nuclear targets, where the nucleus is treated as a collection of nucleons plus possible exotic constituents (pions, multiquark bags, Δs), and contrast this with the rescaling approach based on the operator product expansion and QCD scale transformation properties.

## Key numbers

- The EMC effect shows a ∼15% difference between iron and deuteron structure functions in a region where Fermi motion effects are negligible (1982 Paris Conference result)
- The string tension in QCD is ∼1 GeV/fm, so separating a quark costs ∼1 GeV per additional fermi beyond equilibrium
- The N–Δ mass difference is 300 MeV, representing the energy cost to flip a quark’s spin relative to the other two in a nucleon
- The scaling variable x = Q²/2Mν ranges from 0 to M_T/M ≈ A for a nucleus of mass number A, making the distinction between x and x_T significant for nuclear targets

## Context

These lectures were delivered at the 1985 Los Alamos School on Relativistic Dynamics and Quark Nuclear Physics, immediately following the 1983 discovery of the EMC Effect (European Muon Collaboration, 1983). They extend the parton model treatments found in Feynman (1972) and Close (1979) to nuclear targets, and provide a pedagogical bridge to more advanced QCD methods from Gross (1975 Les Houches) and Llewellyn Smith (1980). The work enables future analysis of the A-dependence of the EMC effect as a probe of QCD at finite nuclear density, anticipating the need for a dedicated Electron Ion Collider.
