# Proceedings of the Sixth International Workshop on Multiple Partonic Interactions at the Large Hadron Collider

> Multiple Partonic Interactions are often crucial for interpreting results obtained at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). The quest for a sound understanding of the dynamics behind MPI - particularly at this time when the LHC is due to start its "Run II" operations - has focused the aim of this workshop. MPI@LHC2014 concentrated mainly on the phenomenology of LHC measurements whilst keeping in perspective those results obtained at previous hadron colliders. The workshop has also debated some of the state-of-the-art theoretical considerations and the modeling of MPI in Monte Carlo event generators. The topics debated in the workshop included: Phenomenology of MPI processes and multiparton distributions; Considerations for the description of MPI in Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD); Measuring multiple partonic interactions; Experimental results on inelastic hadronic collisions: underlying event, minimum bias, forward energy flow; Monte Carlo generator development and tuning; Connections with low-x phenomena, diffraction, heavy-ion physics and cosmic rays. In a total of 57 plenary talks the workshop covered a wide range of experimental results, Monte Carlo development and tuning, phenomenology and dedicated measurements of MPI which were produced with data from the LHC's Run I. Recent progress of theoretical understanding of MPI in pp, pA and AA collisions as well as the role of MPI in diffraction and small-x physics were also covered. The workshop forstered close contact between the experimental and theoretical communities. It provided a setting to discuss many of the different aspects of MPI, eventually identifying them as a unifying concept between apparently different lines of research and evaluating their impact on the LHC physics programme.

# Proceedings of the Sixth International Workshop on Multiple Partonic Interactions at the Large Hadron Collider

## Key claim

The workshop aimed to consolidate the understanding of Multiple Partonic Interactions (MPI) as a unifying concept across apparently different lines of research, with the goal of evaluating their impact on the LHC physics programme as Run II was about to begin. The proceedings present a snapshot of current phenomenological and theoretical progress rather than a single definitive result, with many open questions remaining regarding the consistent treatment of double parton distributions and their QCD evolution.

## Method

The workshop consisted of 57 plenary talks covering experimental results from LHC Run I, Monte Carlo generator development and tuning, phenomenology of MPI processes, and dedicated measurements of multiparton distributions. Theoretical discussions addressed the description of MPI in Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD), connections with low-x phenomena, diffraction, heavy-ion physics, and cosmic rays. The workshop fostered close contact between experimental and theoretical communities to evaluate the impact of MPI on the LHC physics programme.

## Key numbers

- The CMS measurement of the effective cross section in W+2-jet events at sqrt(s)=7 TeV gave sigma_eff = 20.7 +/- 0.8 (stat) +/- 6.6 (syst) mb, consistent with Tevatron and ATLAS results.
- CMS observed a signal yield of 446 +/- 23 events for prompt double J/psi production from a common vertex in pp collisions at sqrt(s)=7 TeV using an integrated luminosity of 4.73 fb^{-1}, with evidence for excess at |Delta y| > 2.6.
- In the conventional versus single-ladder-splitting study for double quarkonium production, the ratio R = sigma(2v1)/sigma(2v2) varied between 0.4 and 1.3 when scanning sqrt(s) from 0.2 to 13 TeV and heavy particle masses M from 3 to 126 GeV.

## Context

This workshop proceedings extends the series of MPI@LHC meetings and builds upon earlier measurements from the Tevatron (CDF and D0 collaborations) and LHC Run I. The theoretical discussions address unresolved issues in the factorisation of double parton scattering cross sections, particularly the proper scale evolution of double parton distributions and the separation of DPS from SPS contributions. The work enables future LHC Run II measurements to test predictions such as the scale dependence of sigma_eff, and motivates further theoretical work on generalised double parton distributions and the role of parton splitting mechanisms as studied by Blok et al. (2014) and Gaunt et al. (2014).
