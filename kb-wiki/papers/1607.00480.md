# Towards laboratory detection of topological vortices in superfluid phases of QCD

> Topological defects arise in a variety of systems, e.g. vortices in superfluid helium to cosmic strings in the early universe. There is an indirect evidence of neutron superfluid vortices from glitches in pulsars. One also expects that topological defects may arise in various high baryon density phases of quantum chromodynamics (QCD), e.g. superfluid topological vortices in the color flavor locked (CFL) phase. Though vastly different in energy/length scales, there are universal features, e.g. in the formation of all these defects. Utilizing this universality, we investigate the possibility of detecting these topological superfluid vortices in laboratory experiments, namely heavy-ion collisions. Using hydrodynamic simulations, we show that vortices can qualitatively affect the power spectrum of flow fluctuations. This can give unambiguous signal for superfluid transition resulting in vortices, allowing for check of defect formation theories in a relativistic quantum field theory system, and the detection of superfluid phases of QCD. Detection of nucleonic superfluid vortices in low energy heavy-ion collisions will give opportunity for laboratory controlled study of their properties, providing crucial inputs for the physics of pulsars.

# Towards laboratory detection of topological vortices in superfluid phases of QCD

## Key claim

Using hydrodynamic simulations, the authors show that superfluid vortices formed via the Kibble mechanism during a phase transition to superfluid QCD phases (such as the color flavor locked phase or nucleonic superfluidity) can qualitatively affect the power spectrum of flow fluctuations in heavy-ion collisions, providing an unambiguous signal for the superfluid transition. The claim is conditional on the assumption that the universal features of defect formation across vastly different energy scales allow extrapolation from condensed matter systems to relativistic quantum field theory systems, and that the qualitative patterns in the flow power spectrum are almost model independent.

## Method

The authors performed 3+1 dimensional hydrodynamic simulations of a partonic system using a two-fluid picture (superfluid and normal fluid components), evolving separate energy-momentum tensors with a perfect fluid form and an ideal gas equation of state for quarks and gluons. Initial velocity profiles for the superfluid component were taken from vortex solutions with a coherence length and circulation velocity, while the normal fluid component was given an opposite velocity profile to satisfy local linear momentum conservation at the time of vortex formation via the Kibble mechanism. The power spectrum of flow fluctuations was calculated from the azimuthal anisotropy of transverse fluid momentum in the central rapidity region, extracting root mean square values of Fourier coefficients \(v_n^{rms}\) for cases with no vortex, one vortex, a vortex-vortex pair, and a vortex-antivortex pair.

## Key numbers

- Central energy density \(\epsilon_0\) corresponds to initial temperature \(T_0 = 25\) MeV and quark chemical potential \(\mu_q = 500\) MeV.
- Superfluid transition temperature \(T_c = 50\) MeV, giving coherence length \(\xi = 0.7\) fm and vortex velocity \(v_0 = 0.3\) (in units where \(c=1\)).
- Initial plasma region radius of 3.0 fm with skin width 0.3 fm; expected number of superfluid vortices is about 2 based on Kibble mechanism probability of 1/4 per correlation domain.
- Power spectrum results shown at \(\tau - \tau_0 = 1.68\) fm (with \(\tau_0 = 1.0\) fm).
- For non-central collisions, initial spatial eccentricity = 0.6; vortex-antivortex pair located at \(x = \pm 1.5\) fm on the x-axis.
- Nucleonic superfluidity critical temperature range: 0.2 MeV to 5 MeV; density range: \(10^{-3} \rho_0\) to few times \(\rho_0\) (where \(\rho_0\) is nuclear saturation density).
- Temperatures of about 17 MeV (with \(\rho \sim 0.4 \rho_0\)) reported in Au-Au collisions at 150 MeV/nucleon lab energy; temperatures of order 4-5 MeV reported in Au-Au collisions at E/A = 50 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the proposal by some of the same authors (Das, Dave, De, Srivastava, earlier papers) to detect phase transitions via density fluctuations from topological defect formation in pulsar timing and gravitational wave emission, now applying the same universality to heavy-ion collisions. It contrasts with the existing heavy-ion collision literature, which has focused primarily on signals related to the quark-hadron transition, by instead targeting superfluid phases such as the color flavor locked phase (CFL) and nucleonic superfluidity. The results enable experimental searches at upcoming facilities FAIR and NICA, and could provide laboratory-controlled studies of nucleonic superfluid vortices that are believed to explain pulsar glitches, complementing astrophysical observations from LIGO and other gravitational wave detectors.
