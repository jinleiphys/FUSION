# Alpha-decay from $^{44}$Ti: A study of the microscopic clusterization

> Microscopic determination of alpha-decay half-lives requires structure and reaction calculations. The structure part is given by the microscopic distribution of the constituent nucleons, while the relative motion of the product's decay provides the reaction part. This paper studies the clusterization of the $0^+$ excited states of $^{44}$Ti arising from the nucleonic degrees of freedom. The continuum spectra of proton and neutron are incorporated through the Gamow Shell Model formalism. The alpha-like wave function is calculated in the weak-coupling approximation. Gaussian effective interaction in each pair of nucleons is included. The $0^+$ ground and excited states are compared with experiment and shell model calculations. The wave function amplitudes are obtained and discriminated by their resonant and non-resonant contributions. The influence of a four-body truncated basis is analyzed. Inclusion of the continuum spectra produces a gain in the excited states of a few hundred keV of energy. One candidate for alpha decay was identified near the experimental unresolved state $(0,2)^+$ of 6.8 MeV excitation energy, with a lower limit for the half-life of $\approx 0.8$ ns.

# Alpha-decay from $^{44}$Ti: A study of the microscopic clusterization

## Key claim
The paper identifies a candidate for alpha decay from $^{44}$Ti near the experimental unresolved $(0,2)^+$ state at 6.8 MeV excitation energy, but only provides a lower limit for the half-life of approximately 0.8 ns; the actual half-life may be longer because the four-body spectroscopic factor, which could increase the value, was not yet included in the calculation.

## Method
The authors used the Gamow Shell Model formalism to incorporate continuum spectra of protons and neutrons, calculating alpha-like wave functions in the weak-coupling approximation with a Gaussian effective interaction between each pair of nucleons. They constructed three single-particle representations (bound basis, pole basis, and complete basis) to separately assess the influence of resonant and non-resonant continuum on the four-body $0^+$ states of $^{44}$Ti. The two-body residual interaction was optimized separately for $T=1$ and $T=0$ channels using experimental low-lying states of $^{42}$Ca, $^{42}$Sc, and $^{42}$Ti, and the four-body wave functions were expanded in a basis built from correlated neutron-neutron and proton-proton eigenfunctions.

## Key numbers
- The experimental ground state energy of $^{44}$Ti is $-33.423$ MeV; the uncorrelated four-body ground-state energy from $^{42}$Ca plus $^{42}$Ti is $-24.679$ MeV, requiring $8.744$ MeV of correlation energy from the proton-neutron interaction.
- The $0^+_2$ state is calculated at $5.337$ MeV excitation energy, only $210$ keV above the alpha threshold ($\alpha+^{40}$Ca threshold at $-28.301$ MeV, $Q_\alpha=-5.127$ MeV).
- For the $0^+_3$ candidate state, the single-particle half-life lower limit is $0.786$ ns (using the non-truncated basis) or $1.746$ ns (using an alternative mean-field from Yamaya (1993)).
- The $0^+_5$ state has a single-particle width of $0.193\times10^{-4}$ MeV, corresponding to a half-life of $0.024$ fs, orders of magnitude faster than prompt gamma decay.
- Inclusion of the full continuum (complete basis versus pole basis) produces an average over-binding energy gain of about $200$ keV for the excited states.
- The four-body basis size reduces from $1437$ states (complete basis, Case I) to $76$ states (truncated basis, Case II) when two-neutron states are limited to $10$ MeV excitation energy.

## Context
This work extends the unified framework for alpha-decay calculations presented by Id Betan (2012) by incorporating non-resonant continuum into the single-particle representation and replacing separable forces with an effective Gaussian interaction. It builds on experimental evidence of alpha structure in $^{44}$Ti from multiple reactions reported by Frekers (1976, 1983) and Yamaya (1990), and addresses the astrophysical importance of $^{44}$Ti in core-collapse supernovae as studied by The (1998), Nassar (2006), Vockenhuber (2007), and Larsen (2012). The calculation supports a $0^+$ assignment for the experimental unresolved $(0,2)^+$ state at $6810(60)$ keV from the National Nuclear Data Center, and the next step is to compute the full alpha-decay half-life using the four-body spectroscopic factor.
