# A metric of Yukawa potential as an exact solution to the field equations of general relativity

## Key claim
By constructing a specific energy-momentum tensor for the strong force, the field equations of general relativity admit the Yukawa potential line element as an exact non-vacuum solution, not merely an approximate vacuum solution at short range. The matter that produces the strong force may have negative energy density, in which case the metric contains no Schwarzschild-like singularity.

## Method
The author first reviews that the Yukawa potential line element approximately satisfies the vacuum Einstein equations for short distances (βr ≪ 1). To obtain an exact solution, a diagonal energy-momentum tensor is defined with components proportional to αβe^{-βr}/r² and αβ²e^{-βr}/r. This tensor is inserted into the Einstein field equations, which reduce to a system of differential equations for the metric functions μ(r) and ν(r). Solving these equations yields the exact Yukawa metric form e^{-ν} = 1 - α e^{-βr}/r + c/r, where the c/r term can account for Coulomb repulsion and is set to zero when only strong forces are considered.

## Key numbers
- The Yukawa potential line element is given by e^{-ν} = 1 - α e^{-βr}/r, where β = 1/R and R specifies the range of the force.
- The energy-momentum tensor components are: T₀⁰ = T₁¹ = -(αβ/κ)(e^{-βr}/r²) and T₂² = T₃³ = (αβ²/2κ)(e^{-βr}/r).
- The approximate solution holds for βr ≪ 1, i.e., r ≪ R, where R is the range of the Yukawa force.
- In the limit R → ∞, the Yukawa metric reduces to the Schwarzschild metric.

## Context
This work extends the idea that strong interaction may be described by general relativity at short range, building on prior approximate treatments where the Yukawa line element satisfied vacuum field equations only for βr ≪ 1. The author explicitly references the standard geodesic equations from Bergmann and Lawden, and the field equation formalism from Landau. The result enables a fully relativistic description of strong force effects using a non-vacuum energy-momentum tensor, with the possibility of negative matter density avoiding singularities, and opens the door to determining α from scattering processes or α-decays.
