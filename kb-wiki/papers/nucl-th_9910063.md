# Numerical renormalization using dimensional regularization: a simple test case in the Lippmann-Schwinger equation

> Dimensional regularization is applied to the Lippmann-Schwinger equation for a separable potential which gives rise to logarithmic singularities in the Born series. For this potential a subtraction at a fixed energy can be used to renormalize the amplitude and produce a finite solution to the integral equation for all energies. This can be done either algebraically or numerically. In the latter case dimensional regularization can be implemented by solving the integral equation in a lower number of dimensions, fixing the potential strength, and computing the phase shifts, while taking the limit as the number of dimensions approaches three. We demonstrate that these steps can be carried out in a numerically stable way, and show that the results thereby obtained agree with those found when the renormalization is performed algebraically to four significant figures.

## Key claim

Dimensional regularization can be implemented numerically in the Lippmann-Schwinger equation for a separable potential that produces logarithmic divergences, provided the potential strength is renormalized by fixing a bound-state energy; the resulting phase shifts agree with those from algebraic renormalization to four significant figures, but the method only works for logarithmic divergences and cannot straightforwardly handle power-law divergences.

## Method

The authors solve the partial-wave Lippmann-Schwinger equation in \(D = 3 - \epsilon\) dimensions by replacing the three-dimensional momentum measure \(k^2 dk\) with \(k^{2-\epsilon} dk\). For a separable potential with form factor \(g(k) = 1/(k^2 + \beta^2)^{1/4}\), they impose a renormalization condition that the amplitude has a pole at the deuteron binding energy \(E_B = -2.2246\) MeV, which fixes the bare coupling \(\lambda_\epsilon\) as a function of \(\epsilon\). They then compute on-shell phase shifts numerically for several \(\epsilon\) values and extrapolate to \(\epsilon \rightarrow 0\), using either a contour-rotation method or a K-matrix approach with principal-value integration.

## Key numbers

* The bare coupling \(\lambda_\epsilon\) extracted from the bound-state condition behaves as \(1/\lambda_\epsilon \propto m/\epsilon\) in the limit \(\epsilon \rightarrow 0\), with the slope equal to the nucleon mass \(m\).
* Phase shifts at \(E_{\rm lab} = 24\) MeV converge to \(100.56^\circ\) (numerical) and \(100.55^\circ\) (algebraic) as \(\epsilon \rightarrow 0\), agreeing to four significant figures.
* Phase shifts at \(E_{\rm lab} = 96\) MeV converge to \(79.031^\circ\) (numerical) and \(79.028^\circ\) (algebraic) as \(\epsilon \rightarrow 0\).
* Phase shifts at \(E_{\rm lab} = 352\) MeV converge to \(60.952^\circ\) (numerical) and \(60.950^\circ\) (algebraic) as \(\epsilon \rightarrow 0\).
* The numerical integration used \(k_m = 50\) fm\(^{-1}\) and quadrature counts of \(n_0=16\), \(n_1=60\), \(n_3=15\) for the K-matrix method, and \(n_0=16\), \(n_1=80\), \(n_2=10\), \(n_3=15\) with contour angle \(\phi=0.7\) for the contour-rotation method.

## Context

This work extends the dimensional regularization technique previously applied to a Schwinger-Dyson equation by Schreiber, Sizer, and Williams (1998) to the non-perturbative Lippmann-Schwinger equation. It provides a proof-of-principle that numerical dimensional regularization can replace cutoff-based regulators in integral equations with logarithmic divergences, avoiding the non-locality and gauge-invariance complications of form-factor methods (Chretien and Peierls, 1954; Ohta, 1989). The approach enables future applications to effective field theory treatments of neutron-deuteron scattering in the doublet channel (Bedaque, Hammer, and van Kolck, 1999) and to the Bethe-Salpeter equation for pion-nucleon scattering (Nieland and Tjon, 1968; Lahiff and Afnan, 1999), where form-factor ambiguities are problematic.
