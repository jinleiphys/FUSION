# Determination of shear forces inside the proton

> We report on the first determination of the shear forces quarks inside the proton from experimental data on deeply virtual Compton scattering. The maximum shear force of approximately 40 MeV/fm occurs near 0.6 fm from the proton center, indicating where confinement forces may be strongest. On the macroscopic scale of the earth surface, this force corresponds to the weight of a mass of about 650 kg. The shear forces in the proton reverse direction at r = 0.45 fm from the center.

## Key claim

The paper reports the first determination of shear forces on quarks inside the proton from experimental deeply virtual Compton scattering data, with the caveat that the results carry significant systematic uncertainties due to the limited kinematic range of the data and rely on parameterizations to cover the full kinematic space.

## Method

The authors extracted the gravitational form factor D(t) from a global fit to CLAS beam-spin asymmetry and cross section data on deeply virtual Compton scattering, using a dispersion relation to connect the real and imaginary parts of the Compton form factor. They then related D(t) to the shear force distribution s(r) through the static energy-momentum tensor in the Breit frame, performing a Fourier transform of the fitted multipole form of D(t) to obtain the radial dependence.

## Key numbers

- Maximum shear force of 40 ± 20 MeV/fm occurs near 0.55 fm from the proton center
- Shear forces reverse direction at r ≈ 0.45 fm from the center
- On the macroscopic scale of the earth surface, the maximum shear force corresponds to the weight of a mass of approximately 650 kg
- Fit parameters for D(t) multipole form: D = -1.47 ± 0.06 ± 0.14, M² = 1.02 ± 0.13 ± 0.21 GeV², α = 2.76 ± 0.23 ± 0.48

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous determination of the pressure distribution inside the proton (Burkert 2018) by extracting the shear forces from the same D(t) form factor. The results enable future comparisons with model predictions such as Polyakov (2018) and will be refined by ongoing CLAS12 experiments with extended kinematic coverage and higher statistical precision.
