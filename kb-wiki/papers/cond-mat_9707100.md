# Fragmentation Phase Transition in Atomic Clusters IV -- Liquid-gas transition in finite metal clusters and in the bulk --

> Within the micro-canonical ensemble phase transitions of first order can be identified without invoking the thermodynamic limit. We show for the liquid-gas transition of sodium, potassium, and iron at normal pressure that the transition temperature, the latent heat as well as the interface surface tension approach for some 1000 atoms the experimental bulk values. No excursion to the thermodynamic limit is neccessary. It is often easier to identify the transition in mesoscopic systems than in the infinite system. It is shown that the microcanonical T(E) contains important information about the transition which becomes suppressed in the canonical ensemble. The implications for our understanding of phase transitions in general are discussed. For these finite metallic systems it is essential to allow for a fragmentation of the system. Our result demonstrates further that microcanonical Metropolis Monte Carlo sampling (MMMC) is well suited to establish the link between the fragmentation phase transition in small systems like nuclei and atomic clusters and the liquid-gas transition of infinite matter.

# Fragmentation Phase Transition in Atomic Clusters IV -- Liquid-gas transition in finite metal clusters and in the bulk

## Key claim

Within the micro-canonical ensemble, the liquid-gas transition temperature, latent heat, and surface tension for sodium, potassium, and iron clusters of approximately 1000 atoms approach the experimental bulk values without invoking the thermodynamic limit, provided the system is allowed to fragment. The authors claim that the transition can be identified more easily in mesoscopic systems than in the infinite system because the convex intruder in the entropy, which signals the first-order transition, disappears in the thermodynamic limit as it scales as N^{-1/3}.

## Method

The authors performed micro-canonical Metropolis Monte Carlo (MMMC) simulations of Na, K, and Fe clusters containing 200 to 3000 atoms at a constant pressure of 1 atm. The fragments were treated as spherical particles with translational, rotational, and intrinsic degrees of freedom; their ground-state binding energies were taken from experiment, and internal densities of states were calculated from experimentally known specific heats of bulk solid/liquid matter. From the micro-canonical caloric curve T_P(E/N) and the Maxwell construction of beta_P(E/N) = 1/T_P, the transition temperature T_tr, specific latent heat q_lat, and specific entropy loss Delta s_surf (linked to intra-phase surface creation) were simultaneously determined, and the surface tension gamma was extracted via gamma = T_tr * Delta s_surf * N / (4 pi r_ws^2 N_eff^{2/3}).

## Key numbers

- For sodium clusters with N = 200 and 1000 atoms, the back-bending of the micro-canonical caloric curve T_P(epsilon) occurs at epsilon_2 approximately 0.7-0.8 eV/atom, where the thermodynamic temperature drops suddenly due to rapid increase in fragment number.
- For sodium with N >= 3000, the multi-fragmentation dip moves to approximately 1.1-1.2 eV/atom and merges with the vaporisation dip.
- The surface tension parameter sigma = 4 pi r_ws^2 gamma for the clusters approaches the experimental bulk values for systems of approximately 1000 atoms.
- The experimental bulk surface tension at the melting point has an uncertainty of approximately 5-10% (Iida and Gutherie, 1993), while the extrapolation to the boiling point has an estimated error of +/- 50% for the temperature derivatives.
- The liquid-drop parameter a_s = sigma(T=0) determined from ground-state binding energies of real clusters (Brechignac et al., 1995) is about 30% larger than the values extrapolated from the experimental surface tension at melting.

## Context

This work extends the micro-canonical thermodynamics approach developed in papers I-III of this series (Gross et al., 1994-1996) for the fragmentation phase transition in atomic clusters, applying it to the liquid-gas transition in metal clusters at constant pressure. The method contrasts with conventional molecular dynamics approaches (Allen and Tildesley, 1985) that struggle with metallic binding due to long-range many-body interactions, and with the Andersen constant-pressure ensemble (Andersen, 1980) which treats volume as an additional degree of freedom rather than fixing it by the thermodynamic pressure condition. The results demonstrate that the micro-canonical caloric curve anomaly, previously linked to phase transitions in hot nuclei (Gross, 1972; Gross, 1975) and in the Gaussian ensemble (Challa and Hetherington, 1988), provides a practical method to determine all first-order transition parameters in finite systems without requiring the thermodynamic limit. The work enables microscopic calculation of surface tension in liquid metals and establishes the connection between the fragmentation phase transition in small systems and the bulk liquid-gas transition.
