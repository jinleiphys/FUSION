# Generator coordinate method for transition-state dynamics in nuclear fission

> Since its beginnings, fission theory has asumed that low-energy induced fission takes place through transition-state channels at the barrier tops. Neverthess, up to now there is no microscopic theory applicable to those conditions. We suggest that modern reaction theory is suitable for this purpose, and propose a methodology based on a configuration-interaction framework using the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM). Simple reaction-theoretic models are constructed with the Gaussian Overlap Approximation (GOA) to parameterize both the dynamics within the channels and their incoherent couplings to states outside the barrier. The physical characteristics of the channels examined here are their effective bandwidths and the quality of the coupling to compound-nucleus states as measured by the transmission factor $T$. We also investigate the spacing of GCM states with respect to their degree of overlap. We find that a rather coarse mesh provides an acceptable accuracy for estimating the bandwidths and transmission factors. The common numerical stability problem in using the GCM is avoided due to the choice of meshes and the finite bandwidths of the channels. The bandwidths of the channels are largely controlled by the zero-point energy with respect to the collective coordinate in the GCM configurations.

# Generator coordinate method for transition-state dynamics in nuclear fission

## Key claim

The authors propose that modern reaction theory, implemented via the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) with the Gaussian Overlap Approximation (GOA), can provide a microscopic foundation for the transition-state hypothesis of nuclear fission, but only if the statistical aspects of decay and the momentum matching between channel and reservoir states are properly accounted for. The methodology avoids the common numerical stability problems of GCM by using coarse meshes, which the authors show provide acceptable accuracy for estimating bandwidths and transmission factors.

## Method

The authors construct a configuration-interaction framework using GCM with Gaussian wave packets along a collective coordinate, parameterizing the Hamiltonian matrix with kinetic and potential terms under the GOA. They replace the standard Hill-Wheeler diagonalization with a complex-energy Green's function approach, adding imaginary decay matrices at the channel endpoints to represent couplings to compound-nucleus states and post-barrier states. The transmission factor T is computed via the Datta formula T = Tr(Gamma_a G Gamma_b G*) for one-dimensional chains of 4 or 7 GCM configurations, examining flat channels, parabolic barriers, and two crossing diabatic channels.

## Key numbers

- Overlap between neighboring GCM configurations in the 4-state flat channel model: N_{k,k+1} = 0.28, with mesh spacing Delta q / s = sqrt(5)
- Integrated transmission I_T for flat channel model A_4: 1.69 (in units of E_K); for A_7: 1.65 (less than 10% change)
- Integrated transmission for parabolic barrier model B_4: 0.61; for B_7: 0.56
- Integrated transmission for two-channel crossing model C_8: 3.12; for C^a_8 (ignoring inter-channel coupling): 2.54 (20% smaller)
- Average energy <E>/E_K for flat channel A_4: 1.17; for A_7: 1.11
- Standard deviation sigma(E/E_K) for flat channel A_4: 0.81; for A_7: 0.77
- Diagonal energies of GCM states taken as E_K = 5/4, absorption strength gamma = 1.0 in the baseline models
- Parabolic barrier curvature parameter V_2 = -1/2, with 4 eigenenergies ranging from -0.4 to 3.3

## Context

This work extends the transition-state hypothesis originally formulated by Bohr and Wheeler (1939) and used in modern evaluations by Capote et al. (2009) and Chadwick et al. (2006, 2011), providing a microscopic reaction-theoretic alternative to the phenomenological one-dimensional barrier penetration assumption. It builds on the authors' earlier simplified configuration-interaction approach (Bertsch and Hagino, 2021) and contrasts with prior GCM fission studies by Goutte et al. (2005), Reinhard et al. (2016), and Tao et al. (2017) that mapped the Hamiltonian onto a two-dimensional Schrodinger equation, thereby losing the statistical aspects of decay. The methodology enables future calculations of fission excitation functions using realistic mean-field Hamiltonians, with the key open question being the detailed microscopic treatment of the coupling between transition-state configurations and compound-nucleus reservoirs.
