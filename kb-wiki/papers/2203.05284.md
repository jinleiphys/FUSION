# Training and Projecting: A Reduced Basis Method Emulator for Many-Body Physics

> We present the reduced basis method as a tool for developing emulators for equations with tunable parameters within the context of the nuclear many-body problem. The method uses a basis expansion informed by a set of solutions for a few values of the model parameters and then projects the equations over a well-chosen low-dimensional subspace. We connect some of the results in the eigenvector continuation literature to the formalism of reduced basis methods and show how these methods can be applied to a broad set of problems. As we illustrate, the possible success of the formalism on such problems can be diagnosed beforehand by a principal component analysis. We apply the reduced basis method to the one-dimensional Gross-Pitaevskii equation with a harmonic trapping potential and to nuclear density functional theory for $^{48}$Ca, achieving speed-ups of more than x150 in both cases when compared to traditional solvers. The outstanding performance of the approach, together with its straightforward implementation, show promise for its application to the emulation of computationally demanding calculations, including uncertainty quantification.

# Training and Projecting: A Reduced Basis Method Emulator for Many-Body Physics

## Key claim

The reduced basis method (RBM) can serve as a general emulator for many-body nuclear physics problems, achieving speed-ups of more than 150 times compared to traditional solvers, provided that the solution manifold can be sufficiently embedded in a linear subspace, a condition that can be diagnosed beforehand by principal component analysis (PCA) showing exponential decay of singular values.

## Method

The authors construct reduced basis emulators by first computing exact "snapshot" solutions for a small number of parameter values, then building a low-dimensional basis using either the Lagrange approach (using the snapshots directly) or proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) via PCA of a larger set of snapshots. For each new parameter value, the emulator finds approximate coefficients by projecting the governing equations onto the reduced subspace using the Galerkin method, solving a low-dimensional system instead of the full high-dimensional problem. The method is applied to the one-dimensional Gross-Pitaevskii equation with harmonic trapping and to nuclear density functional theory (DFT) for Ca-48 using a Skyrme energy density functional.

## Key numbers

- Speed-up of more than 150 times for both the Gross-Pitaevskii equation and nuclear DFT when compared to traditional solvers
- For the Gross-Pitaevskii equation with n=2 basis elements: relative RMSE of 1.0e-1 (Lagrange), 1.2e-2 (POD), 1.5e-2 (POD+Greedy); with n=8: 1.3e-5 (Lagrange), 1.2e-6 (POD), 2.0e-8 (POD+Greedy)
- For Ca-48 DFT average particle energy with POD basis: RMSE of 5.9e-3 (n=2), 6.1e-4 (n=4), 1.7e-4 (n=8)
- Gross-Pitaevskii emulation error less than 2.5% in the repulsive phase (q greater than or equal to 0) using a Lagrange basis with four training points
- Ca-48 neutron skin thickness emulation error less than 0.8% for extrapolated values of L_sym and K_NM outside the training zone
- By separating the DFT Hamiltonian into affine and non-affine parts, speed-ups of more than 250 times achieved for a reduced basis of two elements
- For Gross-Pitaevskii with n=2: speed-up of almost 150 times; n=4: 40 times; n=8: 5 times
- For DFT with n=2: 6 times faster; n=4: 4 times; n=8: 2 times (without precomputation)

## Context

This work extends the eigenvector continuation (EC) approach developed by Frame et al. (2018) for bound states and by Furnstahl et al. (2020) for nuclear scattering, by connecting it to the broader reduced basis method formalism from the applied mathematics literature (Quarteroni et al., 2015; Hesthaven et al., 2016). The authors show that the Galerkin projection formulation generalizes EC and can be applied to nonlinear problems like the Gross-Pitaevskii equation and to coupled many-body systems like nuclear DFT. The PCA diagnostic for testing low-dimensional manifolds enables practitioners to determine feasibility before building emulators, and the speed-ups demonstrated make large-scale uncertainty quantification studies (previously considered inaccessible) feasible for a wide range of energy density functionals.
