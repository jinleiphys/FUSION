# Nuclear Equation of State

> Nuclear equation of state plays an important role in the evolution of the Universe, in supernova explosions and, thus, in the production of heavy elements, and in stability of neutron stars. The equation constrains the two- and three-nucleon interactions and the quantum chromodynamics in nonperturbative regime. Despite the importance of the equation, though, its features had remained fairly obscure. The talk reviews new results on the equation of state from measurements of giant nuclear oscillations and from studies of particle emission in central collisions of heavy nuclei.

## Key claim

The nuclear equation of state (EOS) can be constrained by combining data from giant monopole resonances and from anisotropic flow in heavy-ion collisions, but no single EOS simultaneously describes both sideward and elliptic flow anisotropies across all beam energies, indicating that the extracted pressure-density relation carries systematic uncertainties.

## Method

The incompressibility of symmetric nuclear matter was extracted from measurements of isoscalar giant monopole resonances (L=0) in medium-to-heavy nuclei, isolated by analyzing alpha-particle scattering at very forward angles. For supranormal densities, the pressure-density relation was inferred by comparing transport-theory simulations (solving the Boltzmann equation with parametrized energy functionals) to experimental data on sideward flow and elliptic flow in Au+Au collisions over a range of beam energies, using the discrepancy between the two flow observables to define a band of allowed pressures.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear matter density: $\rho_0 = 0.16$ fm$^{-3}$ (approximately 1/(6 fm$^3$)).
- Volume term in the Weizsacker binding-energy formula: -16 MeV per nucleon.
- Incompressibility of $^{144}$Sm: $K^{Sm} = 138$ MeV, extracted from the L=0 peak at excitation energy 15.5 MeV.
- Incompressibility of infinite nuclear matter inferred from samarium data: $K \sim 210$ MeV (using $K^{Sm} \simeq 0.67 K$).
- Benchmark incompressibility from a parabola through the two known points on the energy-per-nucleon curve: $K \sim 290$ MeV.
- Threshold mass for gravitational collapse of an iron core: $M_{th} \sim 1.5 M_\odot$.
- Critical temperature for the hadron-to-quark-gluon transition in baryonless matter: $T_c \sim 170$ MeV.
- In transport simulations, an EOS labeled with incompressibility $K=167$ MeV yields adequate sideward anisotropy at lower beam energies but too little at higher energies; an EOS with $K=380$ MeV yields too much anisotropy at virtually all energies.
- The constraints on pressure from flow are limited to densities higher than twice the normal density.

## Context

This talk reviews progress on the nuclear EOS from giant resonance measurements (e.g., Itoh (2001) on $^{144}$Sm) and from flow studies in Au+Au collisions (Danielewicz (2001)). It extends earlier work by using the discrepancy between sideward and elliptic flow to define a band of allowed pressures, which rules out extreme models such as the relativistic NL3 model and models assuming a phase transition at relatively low densities. The results enable tighter constraints on the EOS for supernova simulations and neutron-star modeling, and they identify the isospin dependence of the EOS and the detection of quark-gluon plasma as the next frontiers.
