# Validation and improvement of the ZPC parton cascade inside a box

> Cascade solutions of the Boltzmann equation suffer from causality violation at large densities and/or scattering cross sections. Although the particle subdivision technique can reduce the causality violation, it alters event-by-event correlations and fluctuations and is also computationally expensive. Here we evaluate and then improve the accuracy of the ZPC parton cascade for elastic scatterings inside a box without using parton subdivision. We first test different collision schemes for the collision times and ordering time and find that the default collision scheme does not accurately describe the equilibrium momentum distribution at large opacities. We then find a specific collision scheme that can describe very accurately the equilibrium momentum distribution as well as the time evolution towards equilibrium, even at large opacities. We also calculate the shear viscosity and the $η/s$ ratio of the parton systems and confirm that the new collision scheme is more accurate. In addition, we use a novel parton subdivision method to obtain the "exact" evolution of the system. This subdivision method is valid for such box calculations and is so much more efficient than the standard subdivision method that we use a subdivision factor of $10^6$ in this study.

## Key claim

The default collision scheme of the ZPC parton cascade does not accurately describe the equilibrium momentum distribution at large opacities, but a specific scheme using the minimum of the two global-frame collision times as both the collision time and ordering time can describe the equilibrium distribution and time evolution very accurately even at extreme opacities, without requiring parton subdivision.

## Method

The authors tested twelve different collision schemes for the ZPC parton cascade, which differ in how the collision time(s) and ordering time are chosen in the global frame after Lorentz transformation from the two-parton center-of-mass frame. They simulated elastic scatterings of massless gluons in a box with periodic boundaries, using both forward-angle and isotropic differential cross sections, and compared the resulting transverse momentum distributions, time evolution of mean transverse momentum and its variance, and collision rates against theoretical expectations and results from a novel, computationally efficient parton subdivision method with a subdivision factor of 10^6.

## Key numbers

- For forward-angle scatterings at T=0.5 GeV and σ=2.6 mb (opacity χ=2.0), the default ZPC scheme's final pT distribution deviates significantly from the exact subdivision result, while the new scheme matches it closely.
- At extreme opacity (T=0.7 GeV, σ=10 mb, χ=41), the new scheme's results remain close to subdivision results and theoretical expectations, while the default scheme deviates substantially.
- The ratio of final variance of pT from the new scheme to the theoretical value deviates by at most about 3% across all tested opacities.
- The optimal effectiveness factor fσ for matching the new scheme's time evolution of mean pT to subdivision results ranges from 0.84 (isotropic scatterings at T=0.7 GeV, σ=2.6 mb) to 1.00 (low opacity cases).
- The collision rate per volume from the default ZPC scheme at T=0.5 GeV and σ=2.6 mb is much lower than the theoretical expectation, while the new scheme's rate is much closer.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of causality violation in cascade simulations (Kodama 1983, Kortemeyer 1995, Zhang 1998) and the ZPC parton cascade (Gyulassy 1997, Zhang 1997) used in the AMPT model (Lin 2004). It addresses the inaccuracy of the default ZPC collision scheme at high opacities, which was previously mitigated only by computationally expensive parton subdivision (Wong 1982, Molnar 2000). The new scheme enables accurate box calculations without subdivision, and the novel subdivision method (volume reduction instead of particle multiplication) allows efficient "exact" benchmarks. This enables future studies of transport coefficients and equilibration in dense partonic systems relevant to heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC.
