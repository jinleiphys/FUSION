# Emergent phenomena and partonic structure in hadrons

> Modern facilities are poised to tackle fundamental questions within the Standard Model, aiming to reveal the nature of confinement, its relationship to dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB) - the origin of visible mass - and the connection between these two, key emergent phenomena. There is strong evidence to suggest that they are intimately connected with the appearance of momentum-dependent masses for gluons and quarks in QCD, which are large in the infrared: $m_g \sim 500\,$MeV and $M_q\sim 350\,$MeV. DCSB, expressed in the dynamical generation of a dressed-quark mass, has an enormous variety of verifiable consequences, including an enigmatic result that the properties of the (almost) massless pion are the cleanest expression of the mechanism which is responsible for almost all the visible mass in the Universe. This contribution explains that these emergent phenomena are expressed with particular force in the partonic structure of hadrons, e.g. in valence-quark parton distribution amplitudes and functions, and, consequently, in numerous hadronic observables, so that we are now in a position to exhibit the consequences of confinement and DCSB in a wide range of hadron observables, opening the way to empirical verification of their expression in the Standard Model.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the origin of visible mass in the Universe is inseparable from dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB) and confinement, and that these emergent phenomena are expressed with particular force in the partonic structure of hadrons. The authors hedge that while the trace anomaly shows gluons produce the entirety of the proton mass in the chiral limit, this conclusion is only valid when the operator and wave function are defined at a resolving scale much larger than the proton mass, and that any understanding of the proton's mass must simultaneously explain why the trace anomaly vanishes in the pion state.

## Method

The authors review continuum-QCD analyses using Dyson-Schwinger equations and Bethe-Salpeter equations to study the dressed-quark and dressed-gluon propagators, the pion's Bethe-Salpeter amplitude, and the projection of the pion's Poincaré-covariant wave function onto the light front to obtain parton distribution amplitudes (PDAs). They describe how top-down analyses of the gauge-sector gap equations and bottom-up schemes fitting data in the matter sector have recently been united, demonstrating that the renormalisation-group-invariant running interaction predicted by gauge-sector analyses coincides with that required to describe ground-state hadron observables.

## Key numbers

- Dynamically generated gluon mass at infrared momenta: \(m_g(k^2=0) \approx 0.5\) GeV
- Dressed light-quark mass function at far infrared momenta: \(M(0) \approx 0.3\) GeV
- The gluon mass function drops to less than 5% of its infrared value by \(k^2 = 4\) GeV\(^2\)
- The emergence of a length-scale \(\varsigma \approx 0.5\) fm characterising changes in analytic structure of propagators
- The mass-gap in pure-gauge QCD from lattice simulations: \(\Delta \gtrsim 1.5\) GeV, with \(\Delta^2/m_\pi^2 \gtrsim 100\)
- The pion's leptonic decay constant and the scalar function in the dressed-quark propagator satisfy \(f_\pi E_\pi(k;0) = B(k^2)\) in the chiral limit
- The leading-order, leading-twist pQCD result for \(Q^2 F_\pi(Q^2)\) underestimates the full computation by just 15% on \(Q^2 \gtrsim 8\) GeV\(^2\)
- The charged pion form factor computed in one reference gives \(r_\pi = 0.66\) fm, compared to the experimental value \(r_\pi = 0.672 \pm 0.008\) fm

## Context

This work extends the continuum-QCD programme of Roberts and collaborators (2010, 2011, 2013, 2015, 2016) that uses Dyson-Schwinger and Bethe-Salpeter equations to connect confinement, DCSB, and partonic structure. It contradicts the notion that static-quark flux tubes measured in quenched lattice QCD are relevant for confinement in the light-quark realm, arguing instead that confinement is a dynamical phenomenon involving light-particle creation and annihilation effects. The paper enables empirical verification of these emergent phenomena at modern and planned facilities such as an electron ion collider (EIC) and CEBAF, and unifies the previously separate top-down and bottom-up approaches to continuum-QCD as demonstrated by Binosi (2014).
