# Neutron star crusts from mean field models constrained by chiral effective field theory

> We investigate the structure of neutron star crusts, including the crust-core boundary, based on new Skyrme mean field models constrained by the bulk-matter equation of state from chiral effective field theory and the ground-state energies of doubly-magic nuclei. Nuclear pasta phases are studied using both the liquid drop model as well as the Thomas-Fermi approximation. We compare the energy per nucleon for each geometry (spherical nuclei, cylindrical nuclei, nuclear slabs, cylindrical holes, and spherical holes) to obtain the ground state phase as a function of density. We find that the size of the Wigner-Seitz cell depends strongly on the model parameters, especially the coefficients of the density gradient interaction terms. We employ also the thermodynamic instability method to check the validity of the numerical solutions based on energy comparisons.

# Neutron star crusts from mean field models constrained by chiral effective field theory

## Key claim

The authors find that traditional Skyrme mean field models cannot accommodate the density dependence of nuclear equations of state derived from chiral effective field theory, so they introduce additional interaction terms proportional to a higher power of the Fermi momentum to enable accurate reproduction of the bulk-matter equation of state. The size of the Wigner-Seitz cell depends strongly on the model parameters, especially the coefficients of the density gradient interaction terms.

## Method

The authors developed new Skyrme mean field parametrizations (Skχ414, Skχ450, Skχ500) by fitting to the isospin-asymmetric nuclear matter equation of state from chiral effective field theory at second order in perturbation theory up to n = 2 n0, together with ground-state energies of doubly-magic nuclei. They studied nuclear pasta phases using both the liquid drop model and the Thomas-Fermi approximation with a parameterized density profile, comparing the energy per nucleon for spherical nuclei, cylindrical nuclei, nuclear slabs, cylindrical holes, and spherical holes to obtain the ground state phase as a function of density. They also employed the thermodynamic instability method to check the validity of numerical solutions based on energy comparisons.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density n0 from the three Skyrme models: 0.1697 fm^-3 (Skχ414), 0.1562 fm^-3 (Skχ450), 0.1679 fm^-3 (Skχ500)
- Saturation energy per particle B: 16.1987 MeV (Skχ414), 15.9262 MeV (Skχ450), 15.9895 MeV (Skχ500)
- Nuclear incompressibility K: 243.19 MeV (Skχ414), 239.53 MeV (Skχ450), 238.16 MeV (Skχ500)
- Symmetry energy Sv: 32.3456 MeV (Skχ414), 30.6346 MeV (Skχ450), 29.1167 MeV (Skχ500)
- Symmetry energy slope parameter L: 51.9307 MeV (Skχ414), 42.0518 MeV (Skχ450), 40.7415 MeV (Skχ500)
- Maximum neutron star mass for all three parametrizations: 2.1 solar masses
- Crust-core transition densities from the BBP formalism using various nuclear mass models range from 0.03505 fm^-3 (D1S model) to 0.05196 fm^-3 (FRDM model)
- Liquid drop model transition densities between geometries for Skχ450: 3DN-2DN at 0.0634 fm^-3, 2DN-1DN at 0.0736 fm^-3, 1DN-2DB at 0.0837 fm^-3, 2DB-3DB at 0.0859 fm^-3, 3DB-Uniform at 0.0878 fm^-3
- Surface tension parameters for the three models: σ0 = 1.311, 1.186, 1.233 MeV-fm^-2 and q = 40.362, 46.748, 69.413 for n3lo414, n3lo450, n3lo500 respectively

## Context

This work extends previous studies of neutron star crusts using phenomenological liquid drop models (Ravenhall 1983, Douchin 2001) and the Thomas-Fermi approximation (Oyamatsu 1993) by incorporating constraints from chiral effective field theory equations of state. It builds on recent efforts by Brown (2013), Bulgac (2015), and Rrapaj (2016) who used low-density neutron matter from chiral EFT to constrain mean field models, but the present study includes the full isospin-asymmetric matter equation of state at second order in perturbation theory up to n = 2 n0. The new Skyrme parametrizations enable simultaneous study of finite nuclei, inhomogeneous nuclear matter in neutron star crusts, and the mass-radius relation of neutron stars, with all three models satisfying the 2.0 solar mass maximum neutron star mass constraint from observations (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013).
