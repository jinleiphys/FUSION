# Realization of a Four Parameter Family of Generalized One-Dimensional Contact Interactions by Three Nearby Delta Potentials with Renormalized Strengths

> We propose a new method to construct a four parameter family of quantum-mechanical point interactions in one dimension, which is known as all possible self-adjoint extensions of the symmetric operator $T=-Δ\lceil C^{\infty}_{0}({\bf R} \backslash\{0\})$. It is achieved in the small distance limit of equally spaced three neighboring Dirac's $δ$ potentials. The strength for each $δ$ is appropriately renormalized according to the distance and it diverges, in general, in the small distance limit. The validity of our method is ensured by numerical calculations. In general cases except for usual $δ$, the wave function discontinuity appears around the interaction and one can observe such a tendency even at a finite distance level.

## Key claim

The authors claim that the full four-parameter family of one-dimensional point interactions (all self-adjoint extensions of the kinetic operator on the line with a point removed) can be realized as the small-distance limit of three equally spaced Dirac delta potentials, provided the strengths of the deltas and an intervening constant vector potential are renormalized with specific power-law divergences in the inter-delta spacing; the abstract omits the separate treatment required for the case where the (2,1) element of the transfer matrix vanishes (gamma = 0).

## Method

The authors place three Dirac delta potentials at positions x = -a, 0, +a with strengths v_-, v_0, v_+ and add a constant vector potential A between the two outer deltas. They compute the total transfer matrix V_a(A,k) connecting the wavefunction and its derivative across the three-delta system. By expanding the matrix elements in powers of the spacing a and demanding that V_a converge to a general SL(2,R) matrix (parameterized by alpha, beta, gamma, delta) as a goes to zero, they derive the required divergent scaling of v_0 (proportional to 1/a^2 for gamma nonzero) and of v_+, v_- (proportional to 1/a), with finite parts fixed by the target parameters. For gamma = 0, a separate scaling is given with v_+, v_- proportional to 1/a and v_0 held constant. Numerical eigenvalue and eigenfunction comparisons for a finite box confirm the approximation at finite a = 0.2.

## Key numbers

* For gamma != 0, the middle delta strength must scale as v_0(a) = gamma / a^2; the side delta strengths as v_+(a) = -1/a + (alpha+1)/gamma and v_-(a) = -1/a + (delta+1)/gamma; the vector potential as A(a) = theta/(2a) with theta fixed.
* For gamma = 0, the side delta strengths scale as v_+(a) = (alpha-1)/(2a) and v_-(a) = (delta-1)/(2a); the middle delta strength is constant: v_0 = 4 beta / (alpha+delta+2).
* Numerical tests use endpoints X_2 = -X_1 = 15.0 and inter-delta distance a = 0.2; for the case alpha=3, beta=-2, gamma=-7, delta=5, the exact point-interaction wave numbers are k_10 = 0.894964 and k_13 = 1.130869, while the three-delta approximation gives k_10 = 0.905264 and k_13 = 1.142775.
* For the gamma=0 case with alpha=5, beta=3, delta=0.2, the exact seventh eigenstate has k_7 = 0.775671 and the three-delta approximation gives k_7 = 0.775312.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous construction of the epsilon potential (a two-parameter subclass) via three deltas (Cheon and Shigehara 1998; Shigehara et al. 1998) to the full four-parameter family, including cases without time-reversal symmetry. It improves upon earlier realizations by Seba (1986), Chernoff and Hughes (1993), Carreau (1993), and Roman and Tarrach (1996), which required non-local pseudopotentials, delta-squared terms, or doubly logarithmic renormalization; the present scheme uses only ordinary delta potentials with simple power-law renormalization, making it more experimentally accessible. The work enables the physical simulation of any one-dimensional point interaction (including those with wavefunction discontinuities) using a finite-range potential that can be approximated numerically or possibly realized in engineered quantum systems.
