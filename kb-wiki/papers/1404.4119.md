# Momentum anisotropy in nuclear collisions from quantum mechanics

> We point out that the intrinsic relationship between space and momentum in quantum physics through the uncertainty principle has potential implications for momentum anisotropy in heavy-ion collisions. Using a harmonic oscillator potential we calculate the elliptic anisotropy and find it to be sizeable compared to elliptic flow measurements in nuclear collisions. Our results question the validity of the completely hydrodynamic interpretation of anisotropic flow data, and highlight the importance of including quantum physics in hydrodynamic calculations which has largely been neglected so far.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the intrinsic quantum mechanical connection between space and momentum via the uncertainty principle can produce a sizeable elliptic anisotropy ($v_2$) in heavy-ion collisions, questioning the purely hydrodynamic interpretation of anisotropic flow data, but the results are explicitly qualitative and based on simplified potentials (harmonic oscillator and square well) rather than a full QCD treatment.

## Method

The authors calculate the momentum anisotropy of a thermally equilibrated system of independent particles confined in an anisotropic two-dimensional harmonic oscillator potential, then suddenly released. They compute the canonical ensemble averages for both nonrelativistic particles (mass M=0.3 GeV) and massless (ultrarelativistic) particles, using analytic formulas for the harmonic oscillator and numerical diagonalization of the Hamiltonian in a large basis (~10^5 states) for the massless case. The elliptic anisotropy is characterized by the Fourier coefficient $v_2 = \langle \cos 2\phi \rangle$ and its transverse momentum dependence $v_2(p_T)$.

## Key numbers

- For a nonrelativistic system with $\langle r_x^2\rangle^{1/2}\sim 1$ fm, temperature $T=0.2$ GeV, and mass $M=0.3$ GeV, the average elliptic anisotropy $\bar v_2 \sim {\cal O}(10^{-2})$ at spatial eccentricity $\varepsilon=0.3$.
- The approximate analytic relation for the harmonic oscillator near the classical limit: $\bar v_2 \approx \frac{\hbar^2 }{12 k_BTM \langle r_x^2 \rangle}\cdot\frac{\varepsilon}{1+\varepsilon}$.
- For the infinite square well near the classical limit: $\bar v_2 \approx \frac{0.18}{\sqrt{TM \langle r_x^2 \rangle}} \frac{\varepsilon}{1+\varepsilon / 2}$.
- For Au+Au collisions at impact parameter $b=8$ fm, with $\langle r_x^2\rangle^{1/2}=1.5$ fm and $\langle r_y^2\rangle^{1/2}=2.2$ fm at $T=0.2$ GeV, the massless particle $v_2$ peaks near $p_T \sim T$ and decreases to about 0.5% at high $p_T$.
- The nonrelativistic formula with $M = 2.5T$ captures the massless particle $\bar v_2$ very well.
- For cold atom systems (fermionic lithium at $T\sim 1$ $\mu$K, trap dimensions $r_x\sim 20$ $\mu$m, $r_y\sim 100$ $\mu$m), the estimated quantum anisotropy is $\bar v_2\sim 10^{-5}$, which is negligible.

## Context

This work extends the observation from atomic Bose-Einstein condensation studies (Anderson) that momentum distributions have opposite anisotropy compared to the trapping potential anisotropy, applying it to heavy-ion collisions. It directly challenges the purely hydrodynamic interpretation of elliptic flow data (hydro) and the applicability of hydrodynamics at short length scales of ${\cal O}(1$ fm$)$. The results suggest that quantum effects must be included in hydrodynamic calculations, especially for small systems such as proton-proton and proton-nucleus collisions at the Large Hadron Collider, and open questions remain about how to incorporate quantum mechanics into relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics.
