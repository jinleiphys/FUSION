# Entanglement Spheres and a UV-IR connection in Effective Field Theories

> We show that long-distance quantum correlations probe short-distance physics. Two disjoint regions of the latticized, massless scalar field vacuum are numerically demonstrated to become separable at distances beyond the negativity sphere, which extends to infinity in the continuum limit. The size of this quantum coherent volume is determined by the highest momentum mode supported in the identical regions, each of diameter $d$. More generally, effective field theories (EFTs), describing a system up to a given momentum scale $Λ$, are expected to share this feature -- entanglement between regions of the vacuum depends upon the UV-completion beyond a separation proportional to $Λ$. Through calculations extended to three-dimensions, the magnitude of the negativity at which entanglement becomes sensitive to UV physics in an EFT (lattice or otherwise) is conjectured to scale as $\sim e^{-Λd}$, independent of the number of spatial dimensions. It is concluded that two-region vacuum entanglement at increasing separations depends upon the structure of the theory at increasing momentum scales. This phenomenon may be manifest in perturbative QCD processes.

## Key claim

Long-distance quantum correlations in the vacuum of a massless scalar field probe short-distance physics: the size of the quantum coherent volume (entanglement sphere) between two disjoint regions is determined by the highest momentum mode supported in those regions, and the threshold negativity at which entanglement becomes sensitive to UV physics scales as \(\sim e^{-\Lambda d}\), independent of the number of spatial dimensions.

## Method

The authors performed numerical lattice computations of the logarithmic negativity between pixelated spherical regions of the massless scalar field vacuum in one, two, and three spatial dimensions. They computed the two-point correlation matrices \(\hat{G}_{i,j} = \langle \hat{\phi}_i \hat{\phi}_j\rangle\) and \(\hat{H}_{i,j} = \langle \hat{\pi}_i \hat{\pi}_j\rangle\) for disjoint regions of diameter \(d\) on a lattice with spacing \(a\), then extracted the symplectic eigenvalues of the partially transposed covariance matrix to obtain the logarithmic negativity. The separability of regions beyond the negativity sphere was verified using the necessary and sufficient Gaussian state separability criterion of Ref. [PhysRevLett.87.167904], which acts as a flow maintaining separability while simplifying the entanglement structure.

## Key numbers

* Negativity decay constant in one dimension: \(\beta_{1D} = 2.82(3) \sim 2\sqrt{2}\)
* Negativity decay constant in two dimensions: \(\beta_{2D} = 5.29(4)\)
* Negativity decay constant in three dimensions: \(\beta_{3D} = 7.6(1)\)
* Entanglement sphere scaling coefficient in one dimension: \(\gamma_{1D} = 1.114(2)\), with \(\tilde{r}_{\Nslash}/d \sim (\gamma/a) d\)
* Entanglement sphere scaling coefficient in two dimensions: \(\gamma_{2D} = 0.60(2)\)
* Entanglement sphere scaling coefficient in three dimensions: \(\gamma_{3D} = 0.43(2)\)
* Product \(\beta_D \gamma_D\) is approximately constant across dimensions: \(\beta_{1D}\gamma_{1D} = 3.14(3)\), \(\beta_{2D}\gamma_{2D} = 3.2(1)\), \(\beta_{3D}\gamma_{3D} = 3.2(1)\), leading to the conjecture \(\mathcal{N}_{\Nslash} \sim e^{-\pi d/a} \sim e^{-\Lambda d}\)
* For a UV cutoff at the Planck mass \(\Lambda = M_{\rm pl}\), proton-sized regions become separable beyond \(\sim 6\) km; atomic-sized regions beyond \(\sim 10^{11}\) km
* For a UV cutoff \(\Lambda \sim 1\) TeV, proton-sized regions become separable beyond \(\sim 500\) fm; atomic-sized regions beyond \(\sim 5\) mm

## Context

This work extends the speculation of Botero and Reznik (2004) on the role of short wavelength modes in vacuum entanglement, and the numerical lattice studies of Klco and Savage (2020) on two-dimensional negativity spheres. It provides numerical evidence for a UV-IR connection in effective field theories that violates the standard EFT lore that IR physics can be systematically recovered from a low-energy EFT with a UV cutoff. The results suggest that precision studies of large-scale entanglement in the quantum vacuum can probe short-distance physics, with potential implications for perturbative QCD processes, nuclear EFTs around the chiral limit, and beyond-the-standard-model physics. The work also identifies a linearly scaling IR truncation \(L \sim \Lambda\) necessary to capture vacuum inseparability, distinct from the volume scaling \(L \sim \Lambda^{-3}\) associated with black hole entropy bounds from Cohen, Kaplan, and Nelson (1998).
