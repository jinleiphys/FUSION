# Hyperbolic triangle in the special theory of relativity

> The vector form of a Lorentz transformation which is separated with time and space parts is studied. It is necessary to introduce a new definition of the relative velocity in this transformation, which plays an important role for the calculations of various invariant physical quantities. The Lorentz transformation expressed with this vector form is geometrically well interpreted in a hyperbolic space. It is shown that the Lorentz transformation can be interpreted as the law of cosines and sines for a hyperbolic triangle in hyperbolic trigonometry. So the triangle made by the two origins of inertial frames and a moving particle has the angles whose sum is less than $180 ^o$.

## Key claim

The Lorentz transformation can be interpreted as the law of cosines and sines for a hyperbolic triangle in hyperbolic trigonometry, where the triangle formed by the two origins of inertial frames and a moving particle has angles whose sum is less than 180 degrees. This geometric interpretation relies on introducing a new definition of relative velocity that varies from point to point and from vector to vector, not just the usual relative velocity between origins.

## Method

The author derives a vector form of the Lorentz transformation by separating time and space components and introducing a new relative velocity vector defined as the difference between the position vectors in two frames divided by the time coordinate. Using the metric with basis vectors satisfying \( \hat{h} \cdot \hat{h} = 1 \) and spatial basis vectors satisfying \( \hat{i} \cdot \hat{i} = -1 \), the transformation equations for position, time, infinitesimal displacement, and energy-momentum are expressed in covariant vector form. The Lorentz transformation is then mapped onto hyperbolic trigonometry by identifying the rapidity parameter \( \vartheta \) (where \( \gamma = \cosh \vartheta \), \( \gamma v = \sinh \vartheta \), \( v = \tanh \vartheta \)) and showing that the energy and momentum transformations obey hyperbolic cosine and sine laws.

## Key numbers

- The Lorentz factor \( \gamma \) is assigned to \( \cosh \vartheta \), \( \gamma v \) to \( \sinh \vartheta \), and \( v \) to \( \tanh \vartheta \), where \( v \) is the usual relative velocity between origins.
- The energy of a particle in the primed frame is given by \( E' = m \cosh \beta = m / \sqrt{1 - u'^2} \), where \( \beta \) is the hyperbolic angle in the primed frame and \( u' \) is the particle speed in that frame.
- The invariant mass squared is \( m^2 = E'^2 + \mathbf{p}' \cdot \mathbf{p}' = E^2 + \mathbf{p} \cdot \mathbf{p} \), where the spatial metric gives \( \mathbf{p} \cdot \mathbf{p} = -p^2 \).
- The triangle formed by the two origins and a moving particle has angles whose sum is less than \( 180^\circ \).

## Context

This work extends the known result that the velocity space of special relativity is a Lobatchewsky (hyperbolic) space, as previously discussed by Laptev, by providing a more explicit geometric interpretation using hyperbolic trigonometry. The author shows that the Lorentz transformation for energy and momentum can be expressed as the hyperbolic law of cosines and sines, which enables a geometric understanding of the Thomas precession as the time rate of the angle defect. The paper builds on standard treatments of Lorentz transformations found in textbooks such as Goldstein and Jackson, but reformulates them in a vector form that avoids the unwanted gamma factors present in component-based approaches.
