# Solving the three-dimensional Skyrme Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov problem using the mixed-basis method

> Background: The symmetry-unrestricted Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) simulation is important for describing various quantum many-body systems. However, the HFB problem in Cartesian coordinate space is numerically challenging. Purpose: For describing ground states without imposing axial symmetry and looking ahead to future extension for dynamics with full time dependence, we present a numerically efficient implementation of the three-dimensional (3D) HFB code. Methods: We develop a 3D Skyrme HFB code based on the mixed-basis representation (HFBmix) which consists of two harmonic-oscillator (HO) bases in the x- and y-directions, and finite-difference (FD) basis in the z-direction in solving the nuclear 3D HFB problem. Results: The results show very well agreement among all the three codes (HFBmix, HO3D, and hfodd). Especially for the HF calculations, the differences in total energies are on the order of a few keV for the lightest O and Mg nuclei. The HFBmix is applied to spherical, prolate, and triaxial systems, and gives the same quadrupole moments for the deformed nuclei as those of the HO-based calculations. Feasibility of the HFBmix is demonstrated in the fission isomer and barrier calculations of 240Pu. Conclusions: The HFBmix is useful for solving the nuclear 3D HFB problem for its numerical efficiency. Future work will include the analysis of deformed drip-line systems and systematic potential-energy surface calculation for fission-path analysis as well as the time-dependent extension of the HFBmix code for dynamics calculations.

## Key claim

The paper presents a numerically efficient implementation of the three-dimensional (3D) Skyrme Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) code using a mixed-basis representation (HFBmix) that combines two harmonic-oscillator (HO) bases in the x- and y-directions with a finite-difference (FD) basis in the z-direction, achieving agreement with existing codes to within a few tens of keV for total energies while enabling calculations for systems without axial symmetry.

## Method

The authors developed a 3D Skyrme HFB code (HFBmix) that represents quasi-particle wave functions in a mixed basis consisting of HO basis states in the x- and y-directions (with quantum numbers n_x and n_y satisfying n_x + n_y ≤ N_max) and a finite-difference grid in the z-direction (with N_z grid points and spacing dz). The HFB equation is solved iteratively in this mixed basis, starting from initial wave functions from a Woods-Saxon potential, with densities linearly mixed (75% from the previous iteration) for stable convergence. The code imposes no spatial symmetry, uses nine-point finite-difference formulas for derivatives in z, and solves the 3D Poisson equation for the Coulomb potential with Dirichlet boundary conditions.

## Key numbers

- For HF calculations of ¹⁶O and ²⁴Mg, differences in total energies between HFBmix and hfodd are on the order of a few keV for the lightest O and Mg nuclei.
- For ¹⁶O with N_max=11 and dz=0.6 fm, the total energy from HFBmix is -128.476 MeV, compared to -128.450 MeV from hfodd (difference of 26 keV).
- For ²⁰⁸Pb with N_max=14 and dz=0.9 fm, the total energy from HFBmix is -1635.052 MeV, compared to -1634.148 MeV from hfodd (difference of 904 keV).
- For ²⁴Mg with N_max=13 and dz=0.833 fm, the total energy from HFBmix is -196.792 MeV, compared to -196.762 MeV from hfodd (difference of 30 keV).
- The quadrupole moment Q₂₀ for ²⁴Mg from HFBmix is 47.0 fm² (β₂=0.601), compared to 46.8 fm² (β₂=0.599) from hfodd.
- For ²⁴⁰Pu, the fission barrier height from HFBmix is approximately 6.0 MeV, consistent with hfodd results.
- The HFB calculation for ¹²⁰Sn with the mixed basis includes 676 proton and 772 neutron quasi-particle states for 0 < E_k < 60 MeV, compared to 574 proton and 616 neutron states using the 3D HO basis.

## Context

This work extends the mixed-basis approach previously applied to the Gogny HFB method (Hashimoto 2012, Hashimoto 2013) to the Skyrme energy density functional, providing a numerically efficient alternative to full 3D HO basis codes like hfodd (Dobaczewski 2009) and HO3D. The method is particularly suited for systems with one or two spatially extended dimensions, such as fissioning nuclei or nuclear pasta, and enables future extensions to time-dependent dynamics and systematic potential-energy surface calculations for fission-path analysis.
