# Angular momentum conservation in heavy ion collisions at very high energy

> The effects of angular momentum conservation in peripheral heavy ion collisions at very high energy are investigated. It is shown that the initial angular momentum of the quark-gluon plasma should enhance the azimuthal anisotropy of particle spectra (elliptic flow) with respect to the usual picture where only the initial geometrical eccentricity of the nuclear overlap region is responsible for the anisotropy. In hydrodynamical terms, the initial angular momentum entails a non trivial dependence of the initial longitudinal flow velocity on the transverse coordinates. This gives rise to a non-vanishing vorticity in the equations of motion which enhances the expansion rate of the supposedly created fluid compensating for the possible quenching effect of viscosity. A distinctive signature of the vorticity in the plasma is the generation of an average polarization of the emitted hadrons, for which we provide analytical expressions. These phenomena might be better observed at LHC, where the initial angular momentum density will be larger and where we envisage an increase of the elliptic flow coefficient v_2 with respect to RHIC energies.

## Key claim

Angular momentum conservation in peripheral heavy ion collisions requires an initial azimuthal anisotropy of the longitudinal flow velocity, which generates non-vanishing vorticity and enhances elliptic flow beyond what is expected from initial geometrical eccentricity alone; this effect may compensate for the quenching effect of viscosity and could be better observed at LHC where the initial angular momentum density will be larger.

## Method

The authors set up an oversimplified hydrodynamical scheme assuming instantaneous thermalization within an infinitesimally thin slab at time t=0, with the two colliding nuclei treated as hard spheres or Woods-Saxon distributions. They derived the initial longitudinal flow velocity profile from momentum density conservation, computed the resulting vorticity, and evaluated the ratio of the vorticity-driven expansion term to the energy-density-gradient term in the Euler equation at t=0. They also calculated the elliptic flow coefficient and polarization for a rigidly spinning hadron gas using statistical hadronization.

## Key numbers

- For Au-Au collisions at RHIC energy √s_NN = 200 GeV and impact parameter b = 5 fm, the initial orbital angular momentum L0 ≈ 5 × 10^5 (in natural units).
- For Pb-Pb collisions at LHC energy √s_NN = 5.5 TeV, L0 ≈ 1.4 × 10^7, almost two orders of magnitude larger than at RHIC.
- The angular momentum J of the interaction region for Au-Au at √s_NN = 200 GeV reaches a maximum of about 7.2 × 10^4 at impact parameter 2.5 fm, which is 29% of the initial orbital angular momentum L0 at that impact parameter.
- The ratio of the vorticity term to the energy-density-gradient term along x at t=0 for hard sphere nuclei at b = 6 fm is about 20% near the collision center x=0 and increases at larger x.
- For a spherical spinning plasma with ω/T = 0.03 at chemical freeze-out temperature T = 165 MeV and radius 10.1 fm, the total angular momentum J_ω ≈ 10^4, of the same order as J at RHIC energies.

## Context

This work extends the standard hydrodynamical description of peripheral heavy ion collisions, which typically uses the Bjorken scaling hypothesis with longitudinal flow velocity depending only on z (Heinz, 2002; Hirano, 2002), by incorporating angular momentum conservation through a transverse-dependent longitudinal velocity. It addresses the recent finding by Heinz and Song (2006) that minimal viscosity strongly affects elliptic flow and requires modifications of initial conditions or equation of state. The paper also provides analytical expressions for hadron polarization from fluid vorticity, building on the authors' earlier study of the ideal relativistic spinning gas (Becattini, Piccinini, Rizzo, 2007) and extending earlier work on polarization in heavy ion collisions (Wang, 1991; Torrieri, 2006). The results suggest that including angular momentum may help reconcile hydrodynamical models with RHIC data and predict enhanced elliptic flow at LHC.
