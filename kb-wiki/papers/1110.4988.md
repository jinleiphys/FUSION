# Pade approximation of the S-matrix as a way of locating quantum resonances and bound states

> It is shown that the spectral points (bound states and resonances) generated by a central potential of a single-channel problem, can be found using rational parametrization of the S-matrix. To achieve this, one only needs values of the S-matrix along the real positive energy axis. No calculations of the S-matrix at complex energies or a complex rotation are necessary. The proposed method is therefore universal in that it is applicable to any potential (local, non-local, discontinuous, etc.) provided that there is a way of obtaining the S-matrix (or scattering phase-shifts) at real collision energies. Besides this, combined with any method that extracts the phase-shifts from the scattering data, the proposed rational parametrization technique would be able to do the spectral analysis using the experimental data.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that bound states and resonances of any single-channel central potential can be located by constructing a Pade approximant to the S-matrix using only its values on the real positive energy axis, without requiring complex-energy calculations or complex rotation; the method's universality is conditional on having a way to obtain the S-matrix or scattering phase-shifts at real collision energies.

## Method

The S-matrix is computed at a discrete set of real momenta \(k_n\) on a finite interval \([k_{\text{min}}, k_{\text{max}}]\). A rational function \(\tilde S_\ell(k)\) is constructed in the form of a ratio of two polynomials of equal degree \(N\), with coefficients determined by solving a linear system that forces \(\tilde S_\ell(k)\) to match the exact S-matrix at those points. The poles of \(\tilde S_\ell(k)\) are then found as the complex roots of the denominator polynomial; meaningful physical poles are distinguished from spurious ones by repeating the calculation with different \(N\) and identifying poles that remain stable in position.

## Key numbers

* For the exponential well potential \(V(r) = -154.06\exp(-r/0.76)\) MeV (triplet proton-neutron S-wave), the bound state at \(E = -2.2244674752\) MeV was reproduced to eight decimal places as \(-2.2244674718\) MeV using \(N=10\) fitting points on the interval \(E_1=1\) MeV to \(E_N=10\) MeV.
* For the singlet proton-neutron exponential potential \(V(r) = -104.20\exp(-r/0.73)\) MeV, the virtual state at \(E = -0.0660644719\) MeV was reproduced to ten decimal places using \(N=7\) fitting points on the same interval.
* For the exponential hump potential \(V(r) = 7.5 r^2 \exp(-r)\) (in units \(\hbar=m=1\)), the first five resonance poles were reproduced to high accuracy using \(N=30\) fitting points uniformly placed on the energy interval \(1 \le E \le 10\) (units unspecified but consistent with \(\hbar=m=1\)).
* Instabilities in the linear system began to appear when more than 70 fitting points were placed on the interval \(1 \le E \le 10\) for the exponential hump potential.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing effort to unify the treatment of bound and resonant states as S-matrix poles, contrasting with methods that require complex-energy calculations or complex rotation (e.g., the Jost-function method of Rakityansky (2006) and the complex rotation approach reviewed in Kukulin (1989)). The proposed Pade approximation technique is similar in spirit to earlier uses of rational approximation for analytic continuation, such as the Pade approximation of the Titchmarsh-Weyl m-function used by Weyl (2003) for locating resonances, and the use of numerically calculated potentials at real points within complex rotation by Massen (2005). The method enables spectral analysis of potentials that are non-analytic, non-local, or energy-dependent, and in principle could be applied directly to experimental phase-shift data.
