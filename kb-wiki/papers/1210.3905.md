# Heavy-quark transport coefficients in a hot viscous quark-gluon plasma medium

> The heavy-quark (HQ) transport coefficients have been estimated for a viscous quark-gluon plasma medium, utilizing a recently proposed quasi-particle description based on realistic QGP equation of state (EoS). Interactions entering through the equation of state significantly suppress the temperature dependence of the drag coefficient of QGP as compared to that of an ideal system of quarks and gluons. Inclusion of shear and bulk viscosities through the corrections to the thermal phase space factors of the bath particles alters the magnitude of the drag coefficient and the enhancement is significant at lower temperatures. The competition between the effects of realistic EoS and dissipative corrections through phase space factor the former eventually dictate how the drag coefficient would behave as a function of temperature, and how much quantitatively digress from the ideal case. The observations suggest significant impact of both the realistic equation of state, and the viscosities, on the HQs transport at RHIC and the LHC collision energies.

## Key claim

The heavy-quark drag coefficient is significantly suppressed when a realistic lattice QCD equation of state (EoS) is used instead of an ideal gas EoS, but inclusion of shear and bulk viscosities through phase-space corrections enhances the drag coefficient, especially at lower temperatures; the net effect is that the realistic EoS dominates the temperature dependence, producing a drag coefficient that is lower than the ideal case at temperatures relevant for RHIC and LHC.

## Method

The authors compute heavy-quark (charm) drag and diffusion coefficients using a Langevin framework with elastic scattering processes (gluon+charm, light-quark+charm, strange-quark+charm). The thermal bath is described by a quasi-particle model with effective fugacities \(z_g\) and \(z_q\) fitted to (2+1)-flavor lattice QCD pressure data, and viscous corrections to the equilibrium distribution functions are added for both shear viscosity (\(\eta\)) and bulk viscosity (\(\zeta\)) using first-order gradient expansion. The drag and diffusion coefficients are then evaluated as thermal averages of momentum transfer weighted by the squared invariant amplitudes.

## Key numbers

* The ratio \(\gamma_{\eta}/\gamma_{Id}\) (shear-viscous drag over ideal drag) at \(T=0.25\) GeV shows a reduction at low momentum (below about 2 GeV) and an increase at higher momentum, with the magnitude of the ratio decreasing as temperature rises to \(T=0.4\) GeV.
* The ratio \(\gamma_{\zeta}/\gamma_{Id}\) (bulk-viscous drag over ideal drag) at \(T=0.25\) GeV shows enhancement at all momenta, with the enhancement increasing with \(\zeta/s\); the ratio decreases when temperature is raised to \(T=0.4\) GeV.
* The combined ratio \(\gamma_{\eta+\zeta}/\gamma_{Id}\) at \(T=0.25\) GeV is enhanced with increasing momentum, with the enhancement being faster at low momentum and slower at high momentum; the magnitude is lower at \(T=0.4\) GeV than at \(T=0.25\) GeV.
* The ratio \(\gamma^{eos}/\gamma_{Id}\) (realistic EoS drag over ideal drag) is less than unity at low temperature and increases with temperature, approaching unity in the Stefan-Boltzmann limit.
* The ratio \(D^{eos}_{\eta+\zeta}/D_{Id}\) (diffusion coefficient with realistic EoS and both viscosities over ideal diffusion) is significantly smaller than unity for temperatures below about 300 MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations of heavy-quark drag and diffusion that used perturbative QCD or ideal-gas equations of state (e.g., Moore and Teaney (2005), Bair et al. (2008), Jeon (2008)) by incorporating a realistic lattice QCD equation of state via the quasi-particle model of Chandra (2012) and by adding shear and bulk viscous corrections to the thermal distributions following the approach of Dusling (2010). The results enable more realistic modeling of heavy-flavor suppression (\(R_{AA}\)) and elliptic flow at RHIC and LHC, where both the non-ideal equation of state and the small viscosities of the QGP are known to be important.
