# Sensitivity of neutron star observables to microscopic nuclear parameters of realistic equations of state

> The equation of state of matter at supranuclear densities governs the astrophysical observables of neutron stars. A realistic, though complex, description is provided by the Chiral-Mean-Field model, which depends on many microscopic nuclear-physics parameters. We present a Fisher-information-inspired analysis of the sensitivity of neutron-star observables to the parameters of the Chiral-Mean-Field model at $β$-equilibrium using SLy as a crust. We then compute neutron-star sequences and extract masses, radii, compactnesses, and tidal deformabilities. From the logarithmic derivatives of these observables with respect to each nuclear parameter, we construct a dimensionless, Fisher-inspired sensitivity matrix and perform a principal-component analysis to identify the effective combinations of nuclear parameters that most strongly affect neutron-star observables. Although the ranking depends mildly on the observable, the three most important nuclear parameters are the vacuum value of the dilaton field $χ_0$ (which sets the overall scale of the scalar potential and trace-anomaly contribution), the scalar singlet strength $g_{1}^X$ (which controls the overall scalar attraction through the baryon effective masses), and the $k_0$ quadratic scalar term (which governs the curvature of the scalar potential). This framework provides a reproducible, data-driven approach to quantify parameter sensitivities in dense-matter models and to guide future Bayesian inference of nuclear information from multi-messenger astrophysical observations.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that, although the Chiral-Mean-Field model depends on 21 microscopic nuclear parameters, the response of neutron-star observables (mass, radius, compactness, tidal deformability) is organized into a low-dimensional structure dominated by at most three principal directions, with the vacuum dilaton field value χ₀, the scalar singlet coupling g₁^X, and the quadratic scalar coefficient k₀ being the three most influential individual parameters, but this ranking depends mildly on which observable is considered.

## Method

The authors used the Chiral-Mean-Field (CMF) model in its C4 parametrization within the MUSES ecosystem to generate core equations of state for β-equilibrated npe matter, matched to an SLy crust. They computed neutron-star sequences by solving the perturbed Einstein equations for non-rotating stars using the QLIMR module, extracting masses, radii, compactnesses, and tidal deformabilities. From logarithmic derivatives of these observables with respect to each of the 21 nuclear parameters varied one-at-a-time about a fiducial point, they constructed a dimensionless Fisher-inspired sensitivity matrix and performed a principal-component analysis to identify the effective parameter combinations that most strongly affect neutron-star observables.

## Key numbers

* Fiducial parameter values include χ₀* = 401.93 MeV, g₁^X* = -7.324, k₀* = 2.373, m_N* = 937.24 MeV, and m₀* = 150 MeV (full set of 21 parameters listed in Table II).
* Saturation density window used for parameter viability: n_sat ∈ [0.10, 0.20] fm⁻³ and binding energy E_B ∈ [-20, -10] MeV.
* The leading principal components typically contain ∼10–11 non-negligible components from the original parameter set, with recurrent contributions from {χ₀, g₁^X, k₀, k₁, k₂, k₃, f_π, m_N, m_K} and subleading contributions from isoscalar-vector quantities such as g_{Nω} and m_ω.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the Chiral-Mean-Field model (Dexheimer 2008, Dexheimer 2009) by performing the first local sensitivity analysis of the CMF C4 model for npe matter, varying parameters across scalar, vector, explicit-symmetry-breaking, and baryon-meson interaction sectors simultaneously, unlike prior work that held the scalar sector fixed while varying vector parameters (Dexheimer 2015, Dexheimer 2018). The framework provides a reproducible, data-driven approach to quantify parameter sensitivities in dense-matter models and can guide future Bayesian inference of nuclear information from multi-messenger astrophysical observations, building upon the MUSES ecosystem (Reinke Pelicer 2025) and complementing phenomenological approaches such as speed-of-sound parameterizations (Lindblom 2010, Greif 2018) and Gaussian process methods (Landry 2020).
