# Effects of single-particle potentials on the level density parameter

> The new definition of the energy dependence for the level density parameter including collective effects depends strongly on the semi-classical approach. For this method, defining an accurate single-particle potential is of great importance. The effect of the single-particle potential terms, which are central, spin-orbit, harmonic oscillator, Woods-Saxon and Coulomb potential, both for spherical and deformed cases, on the level density parameter was investigated by examining the local success of the global parameterizations of eight different combinations of these terms. Among these combinations, the sum of the central, spin-orbit, harmonic oscillator and Coulomb potentials, gives the most accurate predictions compared with experimental data. The local selections of the global parameterizations show that the single-particle models, which are based on Woods-Saxon potential as the main term, are more suitable candidates than the models based on harmonic oscillator potential to extrapolate away far from stability. Also it can be concluded that the contribution of the Coulomb interaction, both around the closed and open shells is not neglectable.

## Key claim

The paper shows that while a harmonic-oscillator-based single-particle potential (Model 2: central + spin-orbit + harmonic oscillator + Coulomb) gives the best global fit to experimental level density data across the entire mass range, Woods-Saxon-based potentials are selected more frequently for nuclei far from stability, suggesting they are more suitable for extrapolation to exotic nuclei. The authors qualify that this conclusion depends on the specific potential parameters used and that the Coulomb interaction is essential for nuclei around both closed and open shells.

## Method

The authors computed the asymptotic level density parameter using a semi-classical formula for the single-particle level density at the Fermi energy, including shell and pairing corrections, for eight different combinations of single-particle potential terms (central, spin-orbit, harmonic oscillator, Woods-Saxon, Coulomb, and their deformed variants). They calculated goodness-of-fit estimators for 1136 nuclei by comparing predicted cumulative level schemes with experimental data and by comparing predicted average resonance spacings with data for 289 stable isotopes. A "Model 0" was constructed by selecting, for each nucleus, the potential combination that gave the lowest local chi-squared contribution.

## Key numbers

* Model 2 (central + spin-orbit + harmonic oscillator + Coulomb) achieved the best global fit with f_rms = 1.51 and f_lev = 1.32 for 1136 nuclei.
* Model 0 (local selections among the eight models) achieved f_rms = 1.30 and f_lev = 1.00.
* For 289 stable isotopes, harmonic-oscillator-based models (Models 1+2+5+6) were selected for 56.4% of nuclei, while Woods-Saxon-based models (Models 3+4+7+8) were selected for 43.6%.
* For the remaining 847 isotopes (including unstable ones), Woods-Saxon-based models were selected for 73.9% of nuclei, while harmonic-oscillator-based models were selected for only 26.1%.
* The spin-orbit coupling constant was taken as C = -0.3 MeV/hbar^2, and the potential well depth was 50 MeV for both harmonic oscillator and Woods-Saxon potentials.
* The Woods-Saxon potential used radius parameter r_0 = 1.25 fm and diffuseness a = 0.5 fm.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous semi-classical level density model (Canbula 2014) by systematically testing eight different single-particle potential combinations, whereas the earlier work used only harmonic oscillator with central and Coulomb terms. The study contradicts the expectation that the more realistic Woods-Saxon potential would outperform the harmonic oscillator globally, finding instead that harmonic-oscillator-based models give better global fits. The results enable improved global parameterizations for reaction calculations involving nuclei far from stability, where experimental level density data are scarce, and identify which potential combinations are most suitable for specific mass regions.
