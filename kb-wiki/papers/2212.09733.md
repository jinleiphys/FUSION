# Prospects for constraining twin stars with next-generation gravitational-wave detectors

> Neutron star equations of state with strong phase transitions may support twin stars, hybrid and hadronic stars with the same mass but different tidal deformabilities. The presence of twin stars in the population of merging neutron stars produces distinctive gaps in the joint distribution of binary tidal deformabilities and chirp masses. We analyze a simulated population of binary neutron star mergers recovered with a network of next-generation (XG) ground-based gravitational-wave detectors to determine how many observations are needed to infer, or rule out, the existence of twin stars. Using a hierarchical inference framework based on a simple parametric twin-star model, we find that a single week of XG observations may suffice to detect a tidal deformability difference of several hundred between twins and measure the mass scale at which twins occur to within a few percent. For less pronounced twins, XG observations will place a stringent upper bound on the tidal deformability difference.

# Prospects for constraining twin stars with next-generation gravitational-wave detectors

## Key claim

Next-generation (XG) gravitational-wave detectors may detect twin stars (hadronic and hybrid neutron stars with the same mass but different tidal deformabilities) at 90% confidence after as few as ~100 binary neutron star observations, corresponding to roughly one week of observing time, but only for equations of state with a tidal deformability difference between twins of several hundred; for less pronounced twins, XG observations will place a stringent upper bound on the tidal deformability difference rather than definitively detecting them.

## Method

The authors construct constant-sound-speed equations of state with first-order phase transitions that produce stable twin stars, then simulate populations of binary neutron star mergers detected by current (HLV), upgraded (A+), and next-generation (XG) gravitational-wave detector networks. They apply Bayesian hierarchical inference using a parametric model for the mass-tidal deformability relation parameterized by the twin-star mass scale M_t and the tidal deformability difference ΔΛ between twins, recovering posterior distributions for these parameters from the simulated populations.

## Key numbers

- A single week of XG observations (~100 detections) yields a log Bayes factor less than -6 favoring twin stars for the SKI5_2009 equation of state (ΔΛ = 940)
- After one month of XG observations, M_t and ΔΛ are determined to within 1% and 15% uncertainty respectively for SKI5_2009
- After one month of XG observations, ΔΛ larger than ~100 can be ruled out at 90% confidence for equations of state with higher onset densities (SK272_2506 and SK272_3505)
- The XG network detects ~5800 binary neutron star mergers per year within its range, compared to 3 per year for HLV and 15 per year for A+
- The SKI5_2009 equation of state has ΔΛ = 940 and M_t = 1.32 solar masses; SKI5_2006 has ΔΛ = 340 and M_t = 1.36 solar masses; SK272_2506 has ΔΛ = 80 and M_t = 1.45 solar masses; SK272_3505 has ΔΛ = 40 and M_t = 1.91 solar masses
- After two years of A+ observations, uncertainties on M_t and ΔΛ are 15% and 68% respectively for SKI5_2009

## Context

This work extends the population-level approach of Chatziioannou and Han (2020), who tackled the related problem of inferring the radius difference between hybrid and hadronic stars with LIGO and Virgo, to next-generation detectors. Previous studies by Paschalidis and Yagi (2018), Montana and Tolos (2019), Christian and Zacchi (2019), Pang and Dietrich (2020), and Wang and Shi (2022) demonstrated that parametric equations of state supporting twin stars could satisfy constraints from GW170817 and pulsar mass measurements, while Christian and Schaffner-Bielich (2020) argued that radius measurements of PSR J0030+0451 rule out twin stars with phase transition onset below 1.7 times nuclear saturation density. The results enable future XG observations to directly constrain the phase structure of dense matter by either detecting twin stars or placing upper bounds on the tidal deformability difference between hadronic and hybrid stars.
