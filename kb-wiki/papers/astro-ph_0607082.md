# Guaranteed and Prospective Galactic TeV Neutrino Sources

> Recent observations, particularly from the HESS Collaboration, have revealed rich Galactic populations of TeV gamma-ray sources, including a collection unseen in other wavelengths. Many of these gamma-ray spectra are well measured up to ~10 TeV, where low statistics make observations by air Cerenkov telescopes difficult. To understand these mysterious sources, especially at much higher energies--where a cutoff should eventually appear--new techniques are needed. We point out the following: (1) For a number of sources, it is very likely that pions, and hence TeV neutrinos, are produced; (2) As a general point, neutrinos should be a better probe of the highest energies than gamma rays, due to increasing detector efficiency; and (3) For several specific sources, the detection prospects for km^3 neutrino telescopes are very good, about 1-10 events/year, with low atmospheric neutrino background rates above reasonable energy thresholds. Such signal rates, as small as they may seem, will allow neutrino telescopes to powerfully discriminate between models for the Galactic TeV sources, with important consequences for our understanding of cosmic-ray production.

## Key claim

The paper argues that several known Galactic TeV gamma-ray sources are very likely to produce pionic (hadronic) gamma rays and therefore TeV neutrinos, and that for several specific sources the detection prospects for km^3 neutrino telescopes are about 1-10 events per year with low atmospheric neutrino background rates above reasonable energy thresholds, though the authors caution that these signal rates are small and that actual detector performance may not be fully understood until calibration.

## Method

The authors empirically calculate the expected neutrino-induced muon and shower spectra for known Galactic TeV gamma-ray sources, assuming the observed gamma-ray spectra are pionic in origin. They use the measured gamma-ray spectral parameters (flux normalization and spectral index) from HESS observations, apply the relation between gamma-ray and neutrino spectra from pion decay, and compute detectable event rates for a km^3 neutrino telescope using standard neutrino-nucleon cross sections, muon energy loss, and Earth attenuation. They consider a range of possible high-energy exponential cutoffs in the neutrino spectra and compare signal rates to atmospheric neutrino backgrounds.

## Key numbers

- Vela Jr. (RX J0852.0-4622): gamma-ray spectrum dPhi/dE = 21 x 10^-12 (E/TeV)^-2.1 TeV^-1 cm^-2 s^-1; expected nu_mu-induced muon rates for a km^3 Mediterranean detector with neutrino spectrum cutoffs of 50, 25, and 10 TeV are shown in figures, with the source significantly detectable within a few years for higher cutoffs
- Vela X (pulsar wind nebula): if the entire measured flux is pionic, expected nu_mu-induced muon rate N(E_mu > 1 TeV) ~ 4.5 yr^-1 in a km^3 Mediterranean detector
- Galactic Center diffuse emission: angle-integrated photon flux dPhi/dE = 5.2 x 10^-12 (E/TeV)^-2.29 TeV^-1 cm^-2 s^-1; expected muon rates for neutrino spectrum cutoffs of 20 and 50 TeV are shown, suggesting the source is near the threshold of discovery for the lifetime of a km^3 Mediterranean detector
- General source with phi_gamma = 20 x 10^-12 TeV^-1 cm^-2 s^-1 at 1 TeV: for spectral indices Gamma = 1.8, 2.0, 2.2, 2.4 with E_nu^cut = 50 TeV, and for cutoffs of 100, 50, 25, 10 TeV with Gamma = 2.1, the differential muon spectra for one year of IceCube observation are shown in a figure
- Atmospheric neutrino background: steeply falling spectrum ~ E_nu^-3.5 above 1 TeV; atmospheric nu_e flux is about 1/20 of the nu_mu background

## Context

This work extends the approach of Lipari (2006) and Kappes, Hinton, Stegmann, and Aharonian (2006) by empirically calculating neutrino detection prospects from observed TeV gamma-ray spectra, rather than relying on theoretical production models. The paper builds on HESS observations of Galactic TeV sources including Vela Jr., Vela X, the Galactic Center diffuse emission, RX J1713-3946, and unidentified sources, and shows that neutrino telescopes like IceCube and a future km^3 Mediterranean detector can discriminate between leptonic and hadronic production models. The results enable future maximum likelihood analyses of neutrino event energies to probe the highest-energy behavior of TeV sources and test whether these sources are the long-sought sites of Galactic cosmic-ray proton acceleration.
