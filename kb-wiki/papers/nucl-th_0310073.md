# Resource Note on Photofission of Nuclei

> Open-source data exists, in widely scattered places, on photofission of the important nuclear isotopes ^{235}U and ^{239}Pu. This data is useful for studies aimed at detecting these materials at ports of entry. An introductory survey is given to access that data.

# Resource Note on Photofission of Nuclei

## Key claim
Open-source photofission data for 235U and 239Pu exists but is widely scattered across the literature; this note provides an introductory survey to help researchers access that data for port-of-entry detection studies, without claiming to present new experimental results.

## Method
The author compiled and summarized existing open-source data on three categories: photon cross-sections on 235U and 239Pu, photofission product yields and distributions, and delayed neutron energy spectra from photofission. The survey covers experimental work from the 1970s through early 2000s, including studies by Berman’s collaboration, the Gent group in Belgium, and Russian and Japanese groups.

## Key numbers
- Photon cross-sections for 235U and 239Pu were measured for photon energies up to 20 MeV, with separate fission cross sections for 1, 2, and 3 neutrons (Berman collaboration, 1980s).
- Photofission product studies on 235U used photon energies in the 12–30 MeV range (Gent group, starting 1976).
- A Japanese group used 20, 30, and 60 MeV bremsstrahlung on both 235U and 239Pu and found results within 10% agreement with calculated values using published photonuclear cross-section data.
- A Russian group from Obminsk used photon energies up to 11 MeV to study yields for odd nuclei including 235U and 239Pu.
- The French Atomic Energy Commission performed delayed neutron counting from incident photons with energies up to 18 MeV for assaying transuranics in waste drums.

## Context
This resource note extends earlier classical surveys of photofission data (e.g., the “Atlas of Photoneutron Cross Sections Obtained with Monoenergetic Photons”) and supports detection schemes such as that of Little et al., who proposed using photon beams of up to 10 MeV to survey freight and detect delayed neutrons from photofission. The compiled data enables evaluation of what type of light source is needed for future photofission-based detection systems and can be complemented by neutron-induced fission data on nuclei with one fewer neutron.
