# Thermal fission rates with temperature dependent fission barriers

> The fission processes of thermal excited nuclei are conventionally studied by statistical models which rely on inputs of phenomenological level densities and potential barriers. Therefore the microscopic descriptions of spontaneous fission and induced fission are very desirable for a unified understanding of various fission processes. We propose to study the fission rates, at both low and high temperatures, with microscopically calculated temperature-dependent fission barriers and collective mass parameters. The fission barriers are calculated by the finite-temperature Skyrme-Hartree-Fock+BCS method. The mass parameters are calculated by the temperature-dependent cranking approximation. The thermal fission rates can be obtained by the imaginary free energy approach at all temperatures, in which fission barriers are naturally temperature dependent. The fission at low temperatures can be described mainly as a barrier-tunneling process. While the fission at high temperatures has to incorporate the reflection above barriers. Our results of spontaneous fission rates reasonably agree with other studies and experiments. The temperature dependencies of fission barrier heights and curvatures have been discussed. The temperature dependent behaviors of mass parameters have also been discussed. The thermal fission rates from low to high temperatures with a smooth connection have been given by different approaches. \item[Conclusions] Since the temperature dependencies of fission barrier heights and curvatures, and the mass parameters can vary rapidly for different nuclei, the microscopic descriptions of thermal fission rates are very valuable. Our studies without free parameters provide a consistent picture to study various fissions such as that in fast-neutron reactors, astrophysical environments and fusion reactions for superheavy nuclei.

## Key claim

The authors propose that thermal fission rates from low to high temperatures can be described consistently using microscopically calculated temperature-dependent fission barriers and collective mass parameters, without free parameters, but the study is restricted to one-dimensional fission pathways and moderate temperatures up to T=1.5 MeV, excluding viscosity and dissipative effects important at higher temperatures.

## Method

The fission barriers were calculated using the finite-temperature Skyrme-Hartree-Fock+BCS method with the SkM* interaction and mixed pairing in axially-symmetric coordinate spaces including octupole deformations. Collective mass parameters were obtained from the temperature-dependent cranking approximation. Thermal fission rates were computed using the imaginary free energy (ImF) approach at all temperatures, with the WKB method for low-temperature quantum tunneling and a parabolic barrier approximation for high-temperature reflection above barriers.

## Key numbers

- Spontaneous fission lifetime of $^{260}$Fm: calculated 4.25×10$^{-3}$ s versus experimental 5.8×10$^{-3}$ s, with E$_0$=0.65 MeV
- Spontaneous fission lifetime of $^{240}$Pu: calculated 2.73×10$^{22}$ s versus experimental 3.6×10$^{18}$ s, with E$_0$=0.92 MeV
- Spontaneous fission lifetime of $^{292}$Fl: calculated 8.56×10$^{4}$ s, with E$_0$=0.46 MeV
- Spontaneous fission lifetime of $^{278}$Cn: calculated 6.39×10$^{-5}$ s, with E$_0$=0.90 MeV
- Transition temperature T$_c$ = 0.24 MeV for ω$_b$=1.5 MeV, separating quantum tunneling from thermal decay regimes
- At T=0.75 MeV, mass parameters become irregular and increase significantly due to pairing phase transition near the critical temperature

## Context

This work extends previous microscopic studies of thermal fission barriers in superheavy nuclei by Pei (2009) and Pei (2010) to include full thermal fission rate calculations, and complements statistical model approaches such as the Bohr-Wheeler transition-state theory and Kramers theory that rely on phenomenological level densities and potential barriers. The results enable parameter-free microscopic predictions for fission in fast-neutron reactors, astrophysical environments, and hot-fusion reactions for superheavy nuclei, though multi-dimensional calculations including triaxial deformations and dissipation remain needed for quantitative agreement with experiment.
