# Advanced density matrix renormalization group method for nuclear structure calculations

> We present an efficient implementation of the Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG) algorithm that includes an optimal ordering of the proton and neutron orbitals and an efficient expansion of the active space utilizing various concepts of quantum information theory. We first show how this new DMRG methodology could solve a previous $400$ KeV discrepancy in the ground state energy of $^{56}$Ni. We then report the first DMRG results in the $pf+g9/2$ shell model space for the ground $0^+$ and first $2^+$ states of $^{64}$Ge which are benchmarked with reference data obtained from Monte Carlo shell model. The corresponding correlation structure among the proton and neutron orbitals is determined in terms of the two-orbital mutual information. Based on such correlation graphs we propose several further algorithmic improvement possibilities that can be utilized in a new generation of tensor network based algorithms.

# Advanced density matrix renormalization group method for nuclear structure calculations

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that a DMRG implementation incorporating quantum-information-based orbital ordering and active space expansion can resolve a previous 400 keV discrepancy in the ground state energy of ⁵⁶Ni, and can treat nuclei in valence spaces exceeding exact diagonalization limits, though for ⁶⁴Ge in the extended pf+g9/2 space the ground state energy convergence is too slow to provide a reliable extrapolation without significantly more block states.

## Method
The two-site DMRG algorithm was implemented with a preprocessing phase that optimizes orbital ordering by minimizing an entanglement distance based on two-orbital mutual information, and a CI-based Dynamically Extended Active Space (CI-DEAS) procedure. The production phase used the Dynamic Block State Selection (DBSS) approach with large fixed numbers of block states, and ground state energies were extrapolated to the truncation-free limit using a fit function E(M)=E(M→∞)+X₁M^{X₂}. Calculations were performed for ²⁸Si, ⁵⁶Ni, and ⁶⁴Ge in the pf shell, and for ⁶⁴Ge in the extended pf+g9/2 valence space.

## Key numbers
- For ⁵⁶Ni in the pf shell (KB3 interaction, basis dimension 1.1×10⁹): the lowest variational energy with M=4096 block states was E_GS = -78.451 MeV, extrapolated to E_GS(M→∞) = -78.463(4) MeV, compared to the exact value E_exact = -78.465 MeV (error of a few keV, resolving the previous 400 keV discrepancy).
- For ⁶⁴Ge in the pf shell (GXPF1A interaction): lowest variational energy with M=4096 states was E_GS = -304.163 MeV, extrapolated to E_GS(M→∞) = -304.182(3) MeV, compared to E_exact = -304.183 MeV.
- For ⁶⁴Ge in the pf+g9/2 space (dimension 1.7×10¹⁴): the extrapolated ground state energy gave a lower bound of -307.4 MeV and an upper bound of -305.5 MeV, with the MCSM reference energy E_MCSM = -306.066 MeV lying between these bounds.
- For ⁵⁶Ni: extrapolated 2⁺ excitation energy ΔE = 5.121(3) MeV (exact 5.125 MeV).
- For ⁶⁴Ge in pf shell: extrapolated 2⁺ excitation energy ΔE = 0.907(2) MeV (exact 0.906 MeV).
- For ⁶⁴Ge in pf+g9/2 space: extrapolated 2⁺ excitation energy ΔE = 0.90(2) MeV, compared to MCSM values of 0.919 MeV (without reordering) and 0.890 MeV (with reordering).

## Context
This work extends the DMRG methodology previously applied to nuclear structure in particle-hole basis (phDMRG) by Dukelsky et al. (2002, 2004) and in the j-coupling scheme (JDMRG) by Dukelsky et al. (2004) and Pittel et al. (2006), which had difficulties beyond exact diagonalization limits. It directly addresses the 400 keV discrepancy found by Papenbrock et al. (2005) for ⁵⁶Ni. The approach adapts quantum-information-based techniques from quantum chemistry DMRG (White 1999, Legeza et al. 2003, Rissler et al. 2006, Barcza et al. 2011) to nuclear structure. The results enable future applications of tensor network methods (tree-tensor network states) to medium and heavy nuclei, and the entanglement analysis provides a new tool for understanding correlation structures in nuclear wave functions.
