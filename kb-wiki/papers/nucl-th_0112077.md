# A microscopic framework for dynamical supersymmetry in nuclei

> We discuss and explore new aspects of the generalized Dyson mapping of nuclear collective superalgebras composed of an arbitrary fermion-pair algebra and a set of single-fermion creation/annihilation operators. It is shown that a direct consequence of the particular mapping procedure is the conservation of the total number of ideal particles in the resulting boson-fermion system. This provides a microscopic framework for the phenomenological supersymmetric models based on the U(6/2Omega) dynamical superalgebras. Attention is paid to the mapping of single-fermion creation and annihilation operators whose detailed form cannot be determined on the phenomenological level. We derive the general expansion of the single-fermion images that result from the similarity transformation employed to ensure non-redundant bosonisation in the ideal space. The method is then illustrated in an application to the SO(4) collective algebra, a natural extension of the SU(2) seniority model.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the generalized Dyson mapping of nuclear collective superalgebras, composed of an arbitrary fermion-pair algebra and single-fermion operators, conserves the total number of ideal particles (bosons plus fermions) in the resulting boson-fermion system, providing a microscopic foundation for phenomenological supersymmetric models based on U(6/2Ω) dynamical superalgebras. However, this conservation holds only for Hamiltonians composed of operators belonging to the collective algebra, and the mapping is nonunitary, requiring subsequent similarity transformations.

## Method

The authors employ the Usui operator formalism to map the real fermionic Hilbert space onto an ideal boson-fermion space, using supercoherent states to justify the mapping. They derive explicit operator images for bifermion creation/annihilation operators and single-fermion creation/annihilation operators, then apply a similarity transformation (the "bosonisation" transformation) to eliminate unwanted ideal-fermion-pair terms from the bifermion images. A second similarity transformation ("hermitisation") is discussed to restore hermiticity of the mapped operators within the physical subspace, though the authors note that matrix elements can be computed directly using left and right eigenstates without explicit hermitisation.

## Key numbers

- The single-particle basis has dimension 2Ω, where Ω is the number of single-particle states (the number is even due to Kramers degeneracy)
- The bifermion creation operators are defined with normalization condition (1/2) χ^{μν}_i χ_{μν}^j = g δ_i^j, where g is a common normalization factor
- The structure constants c^{jl}_{ik} = (1/g) χ_i^{αβ} χ^j_{βγ} χ_k^{γδ} χ^l_{δα} satisfy symmetry relations c^{jl}_{ik} = c^{jl}_{ki} = c^{lj}_{ik} = (c^{ik}_{jl})^*
- The similarity transformation S_B^{-1} has an expansion with terms that increase the number of ideal fermions by ΔN = 2k = 0, +2, +4, +6, ..., with k_max ≤ Ω
- The mapped Hamiltonian (Eq. Hami) contains terms: (u + g w^i_i) constant, (χ^j_{μσ} χ_i^{νσ} w^i_j) fermion mean field, (g v^j_i - c^{jk}_{il} w^l_k) boson mean field, (1/2 c^{mk}_{ij} v^l_m) boson-boson interaction, and (χ^k_{μσ} χ_i^{νσ} v^j_k) boson-fermion interaction

## Context

This work extends the generalized Dyson mapping approach pioneered by Navrátil, Geyer, and Dobaczewski (1990s) for nuclear collective superalgebras, building on earlier fermion-boson mapping techniques by Klein (1980s) and the Usui operator method. The paper provides a microscopic justification for phenomenological supersymmetric models (Iachello, 1980s; Balantekin, 1980s; Van Isacker, 1990s) that have been experimentally verified in the quartet of 194,195Pt and 195,196Au nuclei (Metz, 1999; Gröger, 2000). The results enable calculation of single-particle transfer matrix elements relevant to experimental identification of supersymmetry in nuclei, and are illustrated for the SO(4) collective algebra as an extension of the SU(2) seniority model, which was used in the first attempt by Kaup (1980s) to investigate microscopic justification for phenomenological supersymmetry.
