# Thermal and geometric normal modes of spectral fluctuations in heavy-ion collisions

> The transverse momentum spectrum of charged particles in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions fluctuates event-by-event, encoding signatures of underlying collective dynamics. Such fluctuations originate from a combined effect of thermal and geometric fluctuations in the initial state. We present a direct decomposition of these spectral fluctuations through principal component analysis performed on the joint covariance structure of normalized spectrum, mean transverse momentum and elliptic flow squared. The first two leading modes explain 99.5\% of the total variance, and are orthogonally rotated by imposing physical constraints motivated by the initial state thermal and geometric response. The resulting thermal and geometric modes bear direct analogy with the vibrational normal modes of a linear triatomic molecule. The thermal mode entirely drives the experimentally measured $v_0(p_T)$, while the geometric mode contributes substantially to $v_{02}(p_T)$ in non-central collisions, providing a transparent explanation of its characteristic low-$p_T$ sign change. The study establishes the first physically motivated interpretation of principal component modes in the field of heavy-ion collisions and provides an experimental window into the thermo-geometric structure of the QGP initial state.

# Thermal and geometric normal modes of spectral fluctuations in heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim
Event-by-event fluctuations of the transverse momentum spectrum in heavy-ion collisions can be decomposed into two physically distinct normal modes, thermal and geometric, via an orthogonal rotation of principal component modes, but only when the principal component analysis is performed on an augmented joint covariance matrix that includes the normalized spectrum, mean transverse momentum, and elliptic flow squared, thereby establishing a connection to experimentally measurable observables.

## Method
The author simulated 5000 Pb+Pb collisions at √sNN = 5.02 TeV using TRENTO for fluctuating initial conditions and MUSIC for viscous hydrodynamics with constant shear viscosity to entropy ratio η/s = 0.08. Principal component analysis was performed on the normalized joint covariance matrix of the event-by-event normalized spectrum N(pT), mean transverse momentum [pT], and elliptic flow squared v2^2. The first two principal modes, which explain 99.5% of the total variance, were orthogonally rotated by imposing a maximal coherence criterion to align them with physical thermal and geometric bases.

## Key numbers
- The first two leading principal components explain 99.5% of the total variance in the joint covariance matrix.
- Pearson correlation coefficient between [pT] and entropy density s at fixed eccentricity squared ε2^2 is r = 0.93 for 30-40% centrality.
- Pearson correlation coefficient between [pT] and eccentricity squared ε2^2 at fixed entropy density is r = 0.72 for 30-40% centrality.
- The geometric component carries 75% of the total weight in v02(pT) for 30-40% centrality.
- The normalization coefficient α relating the thermal mode eT(pT) to v0(pT) is approximately 0.9 for 30-40% centrality.
- The mixing coefficients for extracting the geometric mode from experimental observables are β1 ≈ 2 and β2 ≈ 0.3 for 30-40% centrality.

## Context
This work extends prior studies establishing that mean transverse momentum fluctuations reflect effective temperature variations (Gardim 2019, Samanta 2023) and that elliptic flow is linearly related to initial eccentricity (Ollitrault 1992, Voloshin 1994). It builds on the recently proposed observable v02(pT) (Parida 2025) and the Bożek correlator between [pT] and v2^2 (Bozek 2016). The paper provides the first physically motivated interpretation of principal component modes in heavy-ion collisions, addressing the issue raised by Liu (2020) that principal components carry no a priori physical meaning. This decomposition enables direct experimental access to the thermo-geometric structure of the quark-gluon plasma initial state through measurements of v0(pT) and v02(pT).
