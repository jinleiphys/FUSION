# Are valence quarks rotating?

> We suggest to compare the deep inelastic scattering structure functions measured in the unpolarized charged-lepton scattering off a transversely polarized proton and off a longitudinally polarized proton at larger Bjorken variable $x$, one may get a direct evidence concerning the valence quark orbital angular momentum in the proton. This possible rotating effect is estimated by using a proton-ensemble model.

## Key claim

Comparing the unpolarized deep inelastic scattering structure function measured off a transversely polarized proton versus off a longitudinally polarized proton at large Bjorken x may provide direct evidence for valence quark orbital angular momentum, provided that the rotating effect is large enough to be distinguished from unity in the ratio of the two structure functions.

## Method

The author constructs a "proton-ensemble model" that separates the collective rotational motion of partons from their intrinsic motion via an adiabatic approximation, replacing the rotating proton with an equivalent non-rotating proton carrying the rotational velocity. The distribution of these equivalent protons is projected onto the light-front plus-component to obtain a smearing function, which is then convolved with the valence quark distribution in the non-rotating proton. The ratio of the structure function for a transversely polarized target to that for a longitudinally polarized target (or to the unpolarized structure function) is computed for x > 0.4 using a simple valence quark parametrization.

## Key numbers

- Maximum rotating momentum values used: \(k_{R,max} = 20, 50, 100, 200\) MeV, corresponding to maximum rotating velocities \(v_{R,max} = 0.02, 0.05, 0.11, 0.21\) (in natural units \(\hbar=c=1\)).
- Estimated contributions of parton orbital angular momentum to the proton spin for these four cases: \(L/(\frac12\hbar) = 8\%, 20\%, 40\%, 80\%\) respectively.
- The valence quark distribution used for the estimate: \(xV(x,Q^2) = 3B^{-1}(0.5,4) x^{0.5} (1-x)^3\), where \(B\) is the Beta function.

## Context

This work extends the logic of the first EMC effect (Fermi motion of nucleons in nuclei distorting quark distributions at large x, observed by the European Muon Collaboration) to the case of valence quark orbital motion inside a single proton. The author suggests that existing experimental data may already contain the proposed signal, and that measuring the ratio \(R_I = F_{2,T}/F_{2,L}\) or \(R_{II} = F_2/F_{2,L}\) at x > 0.4 could provide a direct test of the "spin crisis" explanation involving orbital angular momentum, without requiring the unknown current quark mass.
