# Mass signature of supernova $ν_μ$ and $ν_τ$ neutrinos in the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory

> Core-collapse supernovae emit of order $10^{58}$ neutrinos and antineutrinos of all flavors over several seconds, with average energies of 10--25 MeV. In the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO), which begins operations this year, neutrinos and antineutrinos of all flavors can be detected by reactions which break up the deuteron. For a future Galactic supernova at a distance of 10 kpc, several hundred events will be observed in SNO. The $ν_μ$ and $ν_τ$ neutrinos and antineutrinos are of particular interest, as a test of the supernova mechanism. In addition, it is possible to measure or limit their masses by their delay (determined from neutral-current events) relative to the $\barν_e$ neutrinos (determined from charged-current events). Numerical results are presented for such a future supernova as seen in SNO. Under reasonable assumptions, and in the presence of the expected counting statistics, a $ν_μ$ or $ν_τ$ mass down to about 30 eV can be simply and robustly determined. If zero delay is measured, then the mass limit is independent of the distance $D$. At present, this seems to be the best possibility for direct determination of a $ν_μ$ or $ν_τ$ mass within the cosmologically interesting range. We also show how to separately study the supernova and neutrino physics, and how changes in the assumed supernova parameters would affect the mass sensitivity.

## Key claim

Under reasonable assumptions about supernova parameters and with the expected counting statistics at SNO, a $\nu_\mu$ or $\nu_\tau$ mass down to about 30 eV can be simply and robustly determined; if zero delay is measured, the mass limit is independent of the distance to the supernova.

## Method

The authors compare the average arrival time of neutral-current events in SNO (sensitive to all neutrino flavors, including $\nu_\mu$ and $\nu_\tau$) with the average arrival time of $\bar{\nu}_e$ events from charged-current reactions, using either SuperKamiokande or SNO light-water data as the massless reference. They model the supernova neutrino emission with Fermi-Dirac energy spectra (temperatures 8 MeV for $\nu_x$, 5 MeV for $\bar{\nu}_e$, 3.5 MeV for $\nu_e$) and an exponential luminosity decay with time constant 3 s, then use Monte Carlo simulations to determine the statistical significance of the delay caused by a finite neutrino mass.

## Key numbers

* For a Galactic supernova at 10 kpc, SNO will detect about 485 neutral-current events on deuterons from all flavors, of which about 82% are from $\nu_x$ neutrinos (the sum of $\nu_\mu$, $\nu_\tau$, and their antiparticles).
* The mass sensitivity for $\nu_\mu$ or $\nu_\tau$ is about 30 eV (rounded from 27.5 eV) when using the SuperKamiokande $\bar{\nu}_e$ reference, and about 35 eV when using the SNO $\bar{\nu}_e$ reference.
* The time delay for a neutrino of mass $m$ (in eV) and energy $E$ (in MeV) over distance $D$ (in 10 kpc) is $\Delta t(E) = 0.515 (m/E)^2 D$ seconds.
* The average arrival time of the reference events for $t_{max} = 9$ s is $\langle t \rangle_R = 2.57$ s, with a width of 2.12 s.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analysis for SuperKamiokande (Beacom and Vogel, 1998), where a mass sensitivity down to about 45 eV was found using neutral-current excitation of $^{16}$O. The SNO detector offers complementary sensitivity because the lower characteristic energy of the neutral-current deuteron breakup (about 32 MeV after cross-section weighting) gives larger delays for a given mass, despite the smaller event sample. The paper improves upon earlier estimates by other groups (Seckel, Krauss, Burrows, and others) that claimed minimal detectable masses ranging from 10 eV to 200 eV, by providing a quantitative Monte Carlo treatment of finite statistics. This technique enables a direct determination of $\nu_\mu$ or $\nu_\tau$ mass in the cosmologically interesting range below about 100 eV, which is currently inaccessible to laboratory experiments.
