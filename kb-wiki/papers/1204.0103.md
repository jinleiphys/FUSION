# Investigation of hadron multiplicities and hadron yield ratios in heavy ion collisions

> Here we thoroughly discuss some weak points of the thermal model which is traditionally used to describe the hadron multiplicities measured in the central nucleus-nucleus collisions. In particularly, the role of conservation laws, the values of hard-core radii along with the effects of the Lorentz contraction of hadron eigen volumes and the hadronic surface tension are systematically studied. It is shown that for the adequate description of hadron multiplicities the conservation laws should be modified, whereas for the description of hadron yield ratios the conservation laws are not necessary at all. Also here we analyzed the usual criteria for the chemical freeze-out and found that none of them is robust. A new chemical freeze-out criterion of constant entropy per hadron equals to 7.18 is suggested and a novel effect of adiabatic chemical hadron production is discussed. Additionally, we found that the data for the center of mass energies above 10 GeV lead to the temperature of the nil hadronic surface tension coefficient of about $T_0 = 147 \pm 7$ MeV. This is a very intriguing result since a very close estimate for such a temperature was obtained recently within entirely different approach. We argue that these two independently obtained results evidence that the (tri)critical temperature of the QCD phase diagram is between 140 and 154 MeV. In addition, here we suggest to consider the pion and kaon hard-core radii as new fitting parameters. Such an approach for the first time allows us to simultaneously describe the hadron multiplicities and the Strangeness Horn and get a very high quality fit of the available experimental data.

## Key claim

The thermal model's standard conservation laws for baryon charge and isospin projection, when applied with fixed initial values, lead to physically unrealistic freeze-out volumes and fail to describe hadron multiplicities at center-of-mass energies above 10 GeV; however, these conservation laws can be neglected entirely for describing hadron yield ratios, which remain well-reproduced.

## Method

The authors fitted a Boltzmann gas thermal model with hard-core repulsion (Van der Waals type) to experimental hadron yield ratios and multiplicities from AGS to RHIC energies (√s_NN = 2.7 to 200 GeV), using χ² minimization. They systematically varied the treatment of conservation laws (baryon charge, isospin projection, strangeness), introduced separate hard-core radii for mesons and baryons, and incorporated Lorentz contraction of hadron eigen volumes to study their effects on freeze-out parameters and the Strangeness Horn.

## Key numbers

- New chemical freeze-out criterion: constant entropy per hadron s/ρ ≈ 7.18, deviating within ±8% across two orders of magnitude in collision energy.
- Temperature of nil hadronic surface tension coefficient: T₀ = 147 ± 7 MeV, extracted from data at center-of-mass energies above 10 GeV.
- (Tri)critical temperature of QCD phase diagram estimated to lie between 140 and 154 MeV, based on the T₀ result and an independent approach.
- At √s_NN = 200 GeV, the initial baryon charge B_init and isospin projection |I₃_init| should be about 10 times smaller than at AGS energies to describe multiplicities.
- For √s_NN = 6.3 GeV: T = 139 MeV, μ_b = 503 MeV, χ²/NDF = 4.8/4 for hadron yield ratios.
- For √s_NN = 130 GeV: T = 169 MeV, μ_b = 31 MeV, χ²/NDF = 3.4/9 for hadron yield ratios.

## Context

This work critically extends the thermal model approach of Andronic et al. (2009) by identifying flaws in the standard treatment of conservation laws and freeze-out criteria. It introduces a novel adiabatic chemical freeze-out criterion (constant entropy per hadron) and demonstrates that treating pion and kaon hard-core radii as free parameters allows, for the first time, a simultaneous high-quality fit of hadron multiplicities and the Strangeness Horn. The results enable improved extraction of the QCD critical temperature and motivate further studies of energy-dependent initial conditions in heavy-ion collisions.
