# The lowest-radiation environments in the Solar System: new opportunities for underground rare-event searches

> We study neutrino, muon, and gamma-ray fluxes in extraterrestrial environments in our Solar System via semi-analytical estimates and Monte Carlo simulations. In sites with negligible atmosphere, we find a strong reduction in the cosmic-ray-induced neutrino and muon fluxes relative to their intensities on Earth. Neutrinos with energies between 50 MeV and 100 TeV show particularly strong suppression, by as much as 10$^3$, even at shallow depths. The solar neutrino suppression increases as the square of the site's distance from the Sun. Natural radiation due to nuclear decay is also expected to be lower in many of these locations and may be reduced to effectively negligible levels in the liquid water environments. The sites satisfying these characteristics represent an opportunity for greatly extending the physics reach of underground searches in fundamental physics, such as searches for WIMP Dark Matter, neutrinoless double-beta decay, the diffuse supernova neutrinos, and neutrinos from nearby supernova. As a potential near-term target, we propose a measurement of muon and gamma-ray fluxes in an accessible underground lunar site such as the Mare Tranquillitatis Pit to perform a first measurement of the prompt component in cosmic-ray-induced particle production, and to constrain lunar evolution models.

## Key claim

The paper claims that extraterrestrial environments with negligible atmospheres (such as the Moon, asteroids, icy moons, and human-made ice balls) can reduce cosmic-ray-induced neutrino and muon fluxes by orders of magnitude (up to 10^3-10^4) relative to Earth, but this suppression applies primarily to the decay-in-flight component from pions and kaons; the prompt component from ultra-short-lived particles like charmed hadrons is not suppressed and dominates at high energies, and solar neutrino suppression scales as the square of the site's distance from the Sun.

## Method

The authors combined semi-analytic calculations using coupled transport equations for nucleons, pions, kaons, muons, and neutrinos with Geant4 Monte Carlo simulations (version 4.10.08, NuBeam physics list) of primary cosmic ray protons impinging on spherical models of the Earth, Moon, and ice balls. Primary protons were generated with energies from 0.3 GeV to 30 TeV with a flat-in-log spectrum and re-weighted to the local interstellar spectrum, and diagnostic spherical shells at various depths recorded secondary particle fluxes, with 3-flavor neutrino oscillation applied using PDG best-fit parameters.

## Key numbers

- Neutrino fluxes between 50 MeV and 100 TeV show suppression by as much as 10^3 at shallow depths on the Moon relative to Earth
- The prompt component parameterization uses R_nu = 8 x 10^-4 as a reference value, with R_nu in the range 0.1-0.5 times this value providing agreement with pQCD calculations
- Lunar lava tube overburdens are approximately 100 m (with Mare Tranquillitatis Pit having horizontal conduits with roughly twice this overburden)
- Five meters of rock in silicates provides roughly 10 nuclear inelastic scattering lengths, reducing primary proton flux by a factor of about 5 x 10^-5
- The muon energy loss parameter alpha ranges from 2 to 2.8 MeV g^-1 cm^2, and xi = 2.5 x 10^5 g/cm^2
- Chondritic meteorite concentrations: 7.4 ppb U, 29 ppb Th, and 550 ppm elemental K (0.012% abundance of ^40K in K)
- Bulk Earth estimates: K concentration half of chondritic, U and Th concentrations about three times chondritic values

## Context

This work extends schematic estimates from the 1980s by Shapiro (1985), Cherry (1985), and Petschek (1985) that first considered cosmic-ray-induced neutrino fluxes on the Moon, and more recent work by Gaspert (2023) and Solomey (2022) on extraterrestrial sites for dark matter detection. The paper provides the first comprehensive study combining semi-analytic and Monte Carlo simulations covering both neutrinos and muons across different energy ranges at various extraterrestrial sites, and it proposes a near-term measurement at the Mare Tranquillitatis Pit on the Moon to measure muon and gamma-ray fluxes and constrain lunar evolution models.
