# The Constituent Quark Model

> In this chapter we give a pedagogical introduction to the constituent quark model. The explanation of magnetic moments of the nucleons was crucial to introduce an effective quark mass for light quarks that nowadays are understood as an effect of Spontaneous Chiral Symmetry Breaking in QCD. We give an overview of the first applications of the model and an introduction to the most modern developments studying states beyond the naive quark model as tetraquarks and pentaquarks.

## Key claim

This chapter provides a pedagogical introduction to the constituent quark model, arguing that the effective quark mass for light quarks, originally introduced to explain nucleon magnetic moments, is now understood as a consequence of Spontaneous Chiral Symmetry Breaking in QCD, and that the model has two main variants: the naive constituent quark model (with one-gluon-exchange and confinement) and the chiral constituent quark model (which includes Goldstone boson exchange).

## Method

The authors present the historical development and theoretical framework of the constituent quark model, starting with the classification of mesons and baryons using SU(2) and SU(3) flavor symmetry groups and the construction of their wave functions. They then describe the Hamiltonian of the naive constituent quark model including one-gluon-exchange and confinement interactions, followed by the introduction of dynamical quark mass and Goldstone boson exchange from Spontaneous Chiral Symmetry Breaking. The chapter concludes with extensions to tetraquarks, pentaquarks, and the Resonating Group Method applied to the nucleon-nucleon system.

## Key numbers

* The constituent quark model explains baryon magnetic moments using an effective quark mass larger than the current quark mass generated by the Higgs mechanism, with the dynamical quark mass evolving from the current quark mass at high momentum to a constituent quark mass at low momentum.
* The mixing angle for vector mesons is close to ideal mixing at θ_V ≈ 35.4°, giving |ω⟩ ≈ (1/√2)|uū + dđ⟩ and |φ⟩ ≈ -|s\bar{s}⟩.
* The mixing angle for pseudoscalar mesons θ_P lies in the range -10° to -20°, far from ideal mixing, indicating η and η' are almost pure octet and pure singlet, respectively.
* The top quark has a very short lifetime of about 10^{-25} s when decaying into bW⁺, so hadrons containing the t-quark do not bind.

## Context

This chapter extends the foundational work of Gell-Mann (1964) and Zweig (1964) on the quark model classification of hadrons, and builds upon earlier constituent quark model developments that explained the hard core of the nucleon-nucleon interaction. It enables future work on exotic multiquark states (tetraquarks and pentaquarks) and molecular structures of recently discovered exotic states, particularly through the chiral constituent quark model version that becomes important for describing possible molecular configurations.
