# Two-nucleon emission in neutrino and electron scattering from nuclei: the modified convolution approximation

> The theoretical formalism of inclusive lepton-nucleus scattering in the two-nucleon emission channel is discussed in the context of a simplified approach, the modified convolution approximation. This allows one to write the 2p2h responses of the relativistic Fermi gas as a folding integral of two 1p1h responses with the energies and momenta transferred to each nucleon. The idea behind this method is to introduce different average momenta for the two initial nucleons in the matrix elements of the two-body current, with the innovation that they depend on the transferred energies and momenta. This method treats exactly the two-body phase space kinematics, and reduces the formulae of the response functions from seven-dimensional integrals over momenta to much simpler three-dimensional ones. The applicability of the method is checked by comparing with the full results within a model of electroweak meson-exchange currents. The predictions are accurate enough, especially in the low-energy threshold region where the average momentum approximation works the best.

## Key claim

The modified convolution approximation (MCA) allows the 2p2h response functions of the relativistic Fermi gas to be written as a folding integral of two 1p1h responses, using kinematics-dependent average momenta for the initial nucleons; the method is accurate enough for practical applications, especially in the low-energy threshold region where the average momentum approximation works best, but it is an approximation that must be checked against full calculations.

## Method

The authors start from the full 2p2h hadronic tensor for a relativistic Fermi gas, which involves a seven-dimensional integral over particle and hole momenta. They introduce the MCA by splitting the energy-conserving delta function into two parts, one for each nucleon, and then replacing the elementary pair response function with its value at average hole momenta that depend on the energy and momentum transferred to each nucleon. This reduces the calculation to a three-dimensional integral over the momentum and energy transferred to one nucleon, involving the product of two analytic 1p1h Lindhard functions. The average hole momentum is defined as the mean of the minimum allowed energy and the Fermi energy, and its direction is determined by energy conservation for each nucleon.

## Key numbers

- The full 2p2h response calculation involves a seven-dimensional integral over momenta; the MCA reduces this to a three-dimensional integral.
- The maximum momentum transfer to the first nucleon is given by \((k_1)_{\rm max} = \sqrt{(\omega+E_F)^2-m_N^2}+k_F\).
- The 1p1h response function is zero outside the interval \(\omega_i^{\rm min} = \sqrt{(k_F-k_i)^2+m_N^2} -E_F\) to \(\omega_i^{\rm max} = \sqrt{(k_F+k_i)^2+m_N^2} -E_F\).
- The 1p1h response function in dimensionless form is \(R_{\rm 1p1h} = \frac{\pi}{\kappa}\xi_F (1-\psi^2)\theta(1-\psi^2)\), where \(\kappa = q/(2m_N)\) and \(\xi_F = \epsilon_F-1\).
- The average hole energy is \(\langle E \rangle = (\epsilon_0+\epsilon_F)m_N/2\), with \(\epsilon_0 = {\rm Max}\{ \kappa\sqrt{1+1/\tau} - \lambda, \epsilon_F-2\lambda\}\).
- The constant average momentum used in prior work (Nie11, Gil97, Car92) is \(\sqrt{3/5}k_F\); the MCA uses a kinematics-dependent average instead.

## Context

This work extends the frozen nucleon approximation (Rui17), which sets initial nucleon momenta to zero and fails near the two-nucleon emission threshold, by introducing a kinematics-dependent average momentum that correctly describes the low-energy region. The MCA provides a test of the constant average momentum approximation used in Nie11, Gil97, and Car92 for photo-absorption and electron/neutrino scattering, which had only been checked for a single kinematics in Car92. The method enables faster computation of 2p2h responses for neutrino oscillation experiments, where integration over the neutrino flux makes the full seven-dimensional calculation computationally expensive.
