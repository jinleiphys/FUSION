# Mesoscopic Fluctuations in Quantum Dots, Nanoparticles and Nuclei

> We discuss mesoscopic effects in quantum dots, nanoparticles and nuclei. In quantum dots, we focus on the statistical regime of dots whose single-electron dynamics are chaotic. Random matrix theory methods, developed to explain the statistics of neutron resonances in compound nuclei, are useful in describing the mesoscopic fluctuations of the conductance in such dots. However, correlation effects beyond the charging energy are important in almost-isolated dots. In particular, exchange and residual interactions are necessary to obtain a quantitative description of the mesoscopic fluctuations. Pairing correlations are important in metallic nanoparticles and nuclei. Nanoparticles smaller than \~ 3 nm and nuclei are close to the fluctuation-dominated regime in which the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer theory is not valid. Despite the large fluctuations, we find signatures of pairing correlations in the heat capacity of nuclei. These signatures depend on the particle-number parity of protons and neutrons

## Key claim

Mesoscopic fluctuations in quantum dots, nanoparticles, and nuclei can be described by random matrix theory methods originally developed for compound nuclei, but correlation effects beyond the constant interaction model, specifically exchange interactions and pairing correlations, are necessary for quantitative agreement with experiments, particularly in almost-isolated dots and in systems where the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer theory is not valid.

## Method

The paper reviews the application of random matrix theory (Gaussian orthogonal ensemble, Gaussian unitary ensemble, and Gaussian symplectic ensemble) to describe statistical fluctuations of conductance in chaotic quantum dots, using the constant interaction model as a baseline. For quantum dots, the universal Hamiltonian is introduced, which adds an exchange interaction term \(-J_s \mathbf{S}^2\) and a Cooper channel term to the constant interaction model, and a spin-projection technique is used to derive closed-form expressions for conductance in the presence of exchange interactions. For nuclei and nanoparticles, quantum Monte Carlo methods are used to calculate thermal properties beyond mean-field approximations, focusing on signatures of pairing correlations in the heat capacity.

## Key numbers

- Nanoparticles smaller than approximately 3 nm are in the fluctuation-dominated regime where BCS theory is not valid.
- The dimensionless Thouless conductance for a ballistic quantum dot is \(g_T \sim \sqrt{N}\), where \(N\) is the number of electrons.
- The resonance width in a quantum dot is \(\Gamma = (d / 2\pi) \sum_c T_c\), where \(d\) is the mean level spacing.
- The peak spacing in the constant interaction model is \(\Delta_2 = e^2/C + (\epsilon_{N+1} - \epsilon_N)\), with the charging energy \(e^2/C\) typically much larger than the mean level spacing \(d\).
- For a typical quantum dot with gas constant \(r_s \sim 1.2\), the RPA estimate of the exchange interaction constant is \(J_s \sim 0.3 \, d\).
- At \(J_s = 0.3 \, d\), the ground-state probability of spin \(S=1\) for an even number of electrons is about 20\%.

## Context

This review extends the application of random matrix theory from compound nuclei (Wigner, 1951; Dyson, 1962) to mesoscopic systems, building on the Bohigas-Giannoni-Schmit conjecture (1984) that classically chaotic systems exhibit RMT fluctuations. The work addresses discrepancies between the constant interaction plus RMT model and experiments on peak spacing statistics (Sivan, 1996; Simmel, 1997; Patel, 1998; Luscher, 2001) and peak height distributions (Patel, 1998), showing that exchange interactions and residual correlations are necessary for quantitative agreement. The discussion of pairing correlations in nanoparticles and nuclei enables future studies of finite-size effects in systems where BCS mean-field theory fails, using quantum Monte Carlo methods to go beyond the mean-field approximation.
