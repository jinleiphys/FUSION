# Theoretical overview of jet quenching

> 8 pages, 2 figures. Accepted for publication in Nuclear Physics A as conference proceedings for Quark Matter 2012

## Key claim

Jet quenching is an established experimental reality, but the theoretical programme to use quenched jets as quantitative medium probes is still ongoing; the paper argues that most relevant questions have been identified and are being answered, but essential challenges remain in consistently formulating and implementing decoherence effects, colour flow modifications, and large broadening effects into Monte Carlo generators.

## Method

The paper reviews recent theoretical developments in the perturbative QCD description of jet quenching, focusing on: (1) relaxing approximations in single-gluon emission formalisms (BDMPS-Z, GLV, AMY, Higher-Twist) by computing the large-x limit and using Soft Collinear Effective Theory; (2) analysing QCD (de)coherence using a q-qbar antenna setup to study interference patterns from multiple emitters; (3) examining large transverse momentum broadening effects that transport soft gluons to large angles; (4) performing colour-differential calculations of medium-induced radiation to study modifications of colour connections; and (5) comparing theoretical predictions with experimental data across multiple observables.

## Key numbers

- Decoherence time: τ_d = (1/(q̂ θ_q\bar{q}²))^(1/3), where θ_q\bar{q} is the antenna aperture and q̂ is the typical transverse momentum acquired per unit pathlength
- Medium formation timescale: τ_med ∼ 0.1 fm before the hot, dense coloured medium forms
- LHC jet modifications observed up to energies in excess of 300 GeV
- Transverse momentum collected during classical broadening stage: ∼ q̂L, independent of gluon energy
- Soft gluons with energies ω ≤ √(q̂L) are transported to large angles via the jet collimation mechanism
- Colour-modified structures account for over half of the radiated spectrum in the colour-differential calculation

## Context

This conference proceedings overview extends the pre-LHC formulations of jet quenching (BDMPS-Z, GLV, AMY, Higher-Twist) that were compared in Armesto (2011) and found to have large discrepancies when extended beyond their strict applicability domains. It builds on the antenna decoherence work initiated by Mehtar-Tani (2010) and independently by Casalderrey-Solana (2011), and on the jet collimation mechanism proposed by Casalderrey-Solana (2010) and Qin (2010). The paper enables future work by outlining the pathway to embed faithful QCD branching descriptions into realistic hydrodynamic medium implementations, with the MARTINI Monte Carlo generator (Schenke, 2009) providing a framework for such developments.
