# $2+1$ dimensional Floquet systems and lattice fermions: Exact bulk spectral equivalence

> A connection has recently been proposed between periodically driven systems known as Floquet insulators in continuous time and static fermion theories in discrete time. This connection has been established in a $(1+1)$-dimensional free theory, where an explicit mapping between the spectra of a Floquet insulator and a discrete-time Dirac fermion theory has been formulated. Here we investigate the potential of static discrete-time theories to capture Floquet physics in higher dimensions, where so-called anomalous Floquet topological insulators can emerge that feature chiral edge states despite having bulk bands with zero Chern number. Starting from a particular model of an anomalous Floquet system, we provide an example of a static discrete-time theory whose bulk spectrum is an exact analytic match for the Floquet spectrum. The spectra with open boundary conditions in a particular strip geometry also match up to finite-size corrections. However, the models differ in several important respects. The discrete-time theory is spatially anisotropic, so that the spectra do not agree for all lattice terminations, e.g. other strip geometries or on half spaces. This difference can be attributed to the fact that the static discrete-time model is quasi-one-dimensional in nature and therefore has a different bulk-boundary correspondence than the Floquet model.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the bulk quasi-energy spectrum of a (2+1)-dimensional anomalous Floquet topological insulator can be exactly analytically reproduced by a static discrete-time lattice fermion theory, but only for periodic boundary conditions and for a specific strip geometry; the correspondence fails for other lattice terminations because the static model is quasi-one-dimensional and spatially anisotropic, leading to a different bulk-boundary correspondence than the Floquet model.

## Method

Starting from a specific (2+1)-dimensional Floquet model on a square lattice with a four-step driving protocol (Rudner 2013), the authors construct a static Hamiltonian whose eigenvalues are given by \(\epsilon_s = \frac{1}{T}\sin(\epsilon' T)\), where \(\epsilon'\) is a \(\pi\)-shifted version of the Floquet quasi-energy. They use a staggered discrete-time derivative on a temporal lattice with a two-site unit cell and lattice spacing \(T/2\) to avoid fermion doubling, and compare the spectra of the Floquet system and the static discrete-time theory under both periodic and open boundary conditions in a strip geometry.

## Key numbers

* The Floquet quasi-energy spectrum under periodic boundary conditions is given by \(\cos(\epsilon T) = \frac{1}{4}[3 + \cos JT - (1-\cos JT)\cos k_+ - (1-\cos JT)\cos k_- - (1-\cos JT)\cos k_+ \cos k_-]\), where \(k_\pm \equiv (k_x \pm k_y)\) and \(J\) is the hopping amplitude.
* The static Hamiltonian eigenvalues under periodic boundary conditions are \(\zeta = \pm \frac{1}{T}\sqrt{\frac{3+m^2}{4} - \frac{1-m^2}{4}\cos k_+ - \frac{1-m^2}{4}\cos k_- - \frac{1-m^2}{4}\cos k_+ \cos k_-}\), with \(m = \cos(JT/2)\).
* The Floquet system exhibits phase transitions at drive periods \(T\) that are odd multiples of \(\pi/J\).
* The OBC spectra are shown for \(JT = 1.5\pi\) (with chiral edge modes) and \(JT = 0.5\pi\) (without edge modes), using 6 transverse lattice sites in the \(x_-\) direction.

## Context

This work extends the (1+1)-dimensional equivalence between Floquet insulators and discrete-time Dirac fermion theories established by Iadecola (2023) to (2+1) dimensions, where anomalous Floquet topological insulators (Rudner 2013) can exhibit chiral edge states despite having bulk bands with zero Chern number. The results show that while a bulk spectral equivalence exists, the static discrete-time model is spatially anisotropic and quasi-one-dimensional, preventing a full matching of edge spectra for all lattice terminations. This opens the question of whether a more general equivalence can be found that captures the full bulk-boundary correspondence of Floquet systems in higher dimensions.
