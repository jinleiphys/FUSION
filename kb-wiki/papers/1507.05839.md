# Bose-Einstein condensates in neutron stars

> In the two decades since the appearance of the book "Bose-Einstein Condensation" in 1995, there have been a number of developments in our understanding of dense matter. After a brief overview of neutron star structure and the Bose-Einstein condensed phases that have been proposed, we describe selected topics, including neutron and proton pairing gaps, the physics of the inner crust of neutron stars, where a neutron fluid penetrates a lattice of nuclei, meson condensates, and pairing in dense quark matter. Especial emphasis is placed on basic physical effects and on connections to the physics of cold atomic gases.

# Bose-Einstein condensates in neutron stars

## Key claim

The paper reviews how various Bose-Einstein condensed phases, including neutron and proton pairing, meson condensates, and quark pairing, may exist in neutron stars, but emphasizes that reliable predictions for many of these phases remain challenging due to strong correlations and limited experimental data, particularly for meson condensates and non-S-wave pairing.

## Method

The authors provide a theoretical review of condensed phases in neutron star matter, organized by baryon number of the condensed boson. They discuss neutron and proton pairing gaps using BCS theory and beyond-mean-field approaches (including Gor'kov-Melik-Barkhudarov corrections), meson condensates (pions and kaons) via chiral effective theories and mean-field approximations, and quark pairing in the color-flavor locked state using weak-coupling QCD. Connections are drawn throughout to ultracold atomic gas experiments as analogues for dense matter phenomena.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star central densities: up to ~10¹⁵ g cm⁻³
- Neutron drip density: ~4 × 10¹¹ g cm⁻³ (about one thousandth of nuclear density)
- Nuclear saturation density: ρ₀ ≈ 0.16 fm⁻³
- Neutron star radius: ~12 km (for observed mass range)
- Neutron S-wave scattering length: a_S ≈ -18.5 fm; effective range: r_e ≈ 2.7 fm
- Low-density neutron pairing gap (Gor'kov-Melik-Barkhudarov result): Δ = (2/e)^(7/3) E_F exp(-π/(2k_F|a_s|)), which is approximately 0.45 of the BCS value
- Proton fraction in outer core: ~5%
- Critical temperature for QCD transition (zero baryon density): T_c ≈ 170 MeV
- Estimated critical quark chemical potential: μ_c ≈ 500 MeV, corresponding to baryon density ρ ≈ 10 ρ₀
- Neutron pairing transition temperature estimate (Migdal): ~1 MeV (10¹⁰ K)
- Number of neutrons per unit cell in inner crust: up to ~10³
- Neutron superfluid density can be a factor of 10 or more smaller than the density of neutrons between nuclei (Chamel calculations)

## Context

This review extends the discussion of Bose-Einstein condensation in dense matter since the 1995 book "Bose-Einstein Condensation" (Pethick, Smith, and colleagues). It builds on foundational work by Migdal (1950s) on neutron superfluidity, Ginzburg and Kirzhnits on transition temperatures, Hoffberg et al. on triplet-state pairing, and Kaplan and Nelson (1980s) on kaon condensation. The paper connects neutron star physics to ultracold atomic gas experiments, particularly regarding the Gor'kov-Melik-Barkhudarov suppression of pairing gaps (later appreciated through atomic Fermi gas work by Heiselberg, Pethick, and colleagues), and draws analogies between pion condensation and spin-density waves in dipolar atomic gases (e.g., dysprosium experiments by Lev and colleagues). Open questions include reliable calculations of pairing gaps at supranuclear densities, the threshold density for meson condensates, and the neutron superfluid density in the inner crust including both band structure and pairing effects.
