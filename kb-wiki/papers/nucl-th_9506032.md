# Saturation properties and incompressibility of nuclear matter: A consistent determination from nuclear masses

> Starting with a two-body effective nucleon-nucleon interaction, it is shown that the infinite nuclear matter model of atomic nuclei is more appropriate than the conventional Bethe-Weizsacker like mass formulae to extract saturation properties of nuclear matter from nuclear masses. In particular, the saturation density thus obtained agrees with that of electron scattering data and the Hartree-Fock calculations. For the first time using nuclear mass formula, the radius constant $r_0$=1.138 fm and binding energy per nucleon $a_v$ = -16.11 MeV, corresponding to the infinite nuclear matter, are consistently obtained from the same source. An important offshoot of this study is the determination of nuclear matter incompressibility $K_{\infty}$ to be 288$\pm$ 28 MeV using the same source of nuclear masses as input.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the infinite nuclear matter (INM) model, which incorporates the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem, is more appropriate than conventional Bethe-Weizsacker (BW) mass formulae for extracting saturation properties of nuclear matter from nuclear masses, and that for the first time the saturation density and binding energy per nucleon can be consistently determined from the same source (nuclear masses) using a mass formula.

## Method

The authors improved the INM model by using better finite-difference expressions for neutron and proton Fermi energies, which achieved a cleaner decoupling of finite-size effects from the infinite nuclear matter part. They then performed two separate least-squares fits to 1371 nuclear masses from the Wapstra and Audi mass table: the first fit (Eq. 6) determined the finite-size coefficients (Coulomb coefficient \(a_C^I\) and surface coefficient \(a_s^I\)), and the second fit (Eq. 9) determined the infinite nuclear matter coefficients (volume coefficient \(a_v^I\) and symmetry coefficient \(a_{\beta}^I\)). The incompressibility \(K_{\infty}\) was then derived by comparing the INM model results with those from a BW-like mass formula fit to the same masses, using a relation that accounts for the different densities at which the two models operate.

## Key numbers

*   Radius constant \(r_0 = 1.138\) fm and saturation density \(\rho_{\infty} = 0.1620\) fm\(^{-3}\), obtained from the INM model fit to 1371 nuclear masses.
*   Volume coefficient (binding energy per nucleon) \(a_v^I = -16.108\) MeV, obtained from the same INM model fit.
*   Nuclear matter incompressibility \(K_{\infty} = 288 \pm 28\) MeV, determined by combining the INM and BW model fits to the same set of nuclear masses.
*   Coulomb coefficient from the INM fit: \(a_C^I = 0.7593\) MeV.
*   The \(\chi_{rms}\) for the two INM model fits (Eq. 6 and Eq. 9) were 371 keV and 372 keV, respectively, using 1371 masses.

## Context

This work extends the INM model approach of Satpathy and collaborators (1990s) by improving the treatment of Fermi energies, and directly addresses the long-standing "\(r_0\) paradox" where BW mass formula fits yielded a radius constant \(r_0 \approx 1.22\) fm, inconsistent with the value of 1.12-1.13 fm from electron scattering data (deShalit, 1960s) and Hartree-Fock calculations (Gogny, 1970s). The authors demonstrate that their INM model resolves this paradox by yielding \(r_0 = 1.138\) fm, consistent with electron scattering. The determination of \(K_{\infty} = 288 \pm 28\) MeV from nuclear masses provides a new, independent constraint on this fundamental property, which is important for nuclear structure and astrophysics.
