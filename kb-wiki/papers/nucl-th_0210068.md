# Analytical expressions of the dispersive contributions of the nuclear optical model

## Key claim
Analytical solutions of dispersion relations for the nuclear optical model potential have been found for both imaginary volume and surface potentials, assuming a Brown-Rho shape for the volume term and a Brown-Rho shape multiplied by a decreasing exponential for the surface term. The solutions are valid for any even value of the exponent appearing in these functional forms, which extends previous work by VanderKam et al. (2000) that had limited the energy range of validity for given analytical expressions.

## Method
The authors derived analytical expressions for the dispersive correction terms ΔV_V(E) and ΔV_S(E) by solving the subtracted dispersion relation integral (equation 2) using contour integration and residue calculus. For the surface potential, the integrand was expressed in terms of poles and residues, with contributions evaluated using exponential integral functions E1 and Ei. For the volume potential, the exponential factor is absent, and the sum of individually divergent pole contributions was evaluated as a finite quantity by taking the proper limit. The solutions assume energy-independent geometry and symmetry of the imaginary potential about the Fermi energy.

## Key numbers
- The volume imaginary potential is parameterized as W_V(E) = A_V * (E-E_P)^n / [(E-E_P)^n + (B_V)^n] for E ≥ E_P, with n even (n=2 proposed by Brown and Rho (1981), n=4 suggested by Mahaux and Sartor (1987))
- The surface imaginary potential is parameterized as W_S(E) = A_S * exp(-C_S|E-E_P|) * (E-E_P)^m / [(E-E_P)^m + (B_S)^m] for E ≥ E_P, with m even (m=2,4)
- The complex poles of the integrand are given by p_j = B * exp(i * (2j-1)/m * π) for j = 1,...,m
- The dispersive correction for the surface potential involves the exponential integral functions E1(-p_j C) and Ei(±C E_±)
- The dispersive correction for the volume potential involves logarithmic terms ln(-p_j), ln(E_+), and ln(|E_-|)

## Context
This work extends the dispersive optical model framework developed by Mahaux and co-workers (1984-1991) and provides analytical solutions to the dispersion integral relations that were previously treated numerically by Quesada, Capote, Molina, and Lozano (2001). The analytical expressions improve upon the work of VanderKam, van der Sluys, and van der Woude (2000), who made approximations limiting the energy range of validity for surface dispersive integrals with m=4. The compact formulae are designed for easy implementation in optical model parameter search codes, enabling more efficient phenomenological OMP parameter determination from experimental data.
