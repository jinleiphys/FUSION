# Classic Calculations of Static Properties of the Nucleons reexamined

> Classic calculations of the magnetic moments mu_p and mu_n of the nucleons using the traditional exponential kernel show instability with respect to variations of the Borel mass as well as arbitrariness with respect to the choice of the onset of perturbative QCD. The use of a polynomial kernel, the coefficients of which are determined by the masses of the nucleon resonances stabilizes the calculation and provides much better damping of the unknown contribution of the nucleon continuum. The method is also applied to the evaluation of the coupling gA of proton to the axial current and to the strong part of the neutron-proton mass difference Delta M_np. All these quantities depend sensitively on the value of the 4-quark condensate < 0 | qqqq | 0 > and the value < 0 | qqqq | 0 > ~ 1.5< 0 | qq | 0 >^2 reproduces the experimental results.

## Key claim

Classic QCD sum-rule calculations of nucleon static properties using the traditional exponential kernel are unstable with respect to variations of the Borel mass and arbitrary with respect to the choice of the onset of perturbative QCD; replacing the exponential kernel with a polynomial kernel whose coefficients are fixed by the masses of nucleon resonances stabilizes the calculation and yields results that depend sensitively on the four-quark condensate parameter kappa, with a single value kappa approximately 1.6 reproducing all four experimental quantities.

## Method

The author reexamines the three-point function of Balitsky and Yung (1991) for nucleon magnetic moments and the polarization amplitude of Belyaev and Kogan (1984) for the axial-vector coupling, replacing the standard exponential Borel kernel with a polynomial kernel p0(t) = 1 - a1 t - a2 t^2. The coefficients a1 = 0.807 GeV^-2 and a2 = -0.160 GeV^-4 are chosen to minimize the polynomial on the interval 2 GeV^2 to 3 GeV^2 where the nucleon resonances lie, damping the unknown continuum contribution to below 6% relative to the value at the nucleon mass. The dispersion integral is then evaluated with the polynomial kernel, the unknown single-pole contributions are eliminated by a factor (m_N^2 - t), and the result is expressed in terms of the four-quark condensate parameter kappa.

## Key numbers

- Proton magnetic form factor: F_m^p = 1.50 kappa, yielding F_m^p = 2.40 for kappa = 1.6 (experimental value 2.71).
- Neutron magnetic form factor: F_m^n = -1.26 kappa, yielding F_m^n = -2.01 for kappa = 1.6 (experimental value -1.91).
- Axial-vector coupling: g_A = 0.39 + 0.61 kappa, yielding g_A = 1.36 for kappa = 1.6 (experimental value 1.27).
- Strong part of neutron-proton mass difference: delta M_np = (m_d - m_u) * U with U = 1.03 kappa - 0.57, yielding delta M_np = 2.6 MeV for kappa = 1.6 (experimental value 2.6 +/- 0.5 MeV).
- The exponential kernel result for F_m^p varies by a factor of 2.8 when the Borel mass M^2 varies between 0.8 GeV^2 and 1.4 GeV^2.

## Context

This work extends the polynomial-kernel method previously applied by Nasrallah (2014) to the nucleon mass and by Nasrallah and Schilcher (2014) to the neutron-proton mass difference, now applied to the classic sum-rule calculations of nucleon magnetic moments by Balitsky and Yung (1991) and Ioffe and Smilga (1984), and to the axial-vector coupling calculations of Belyaev and Kogan (1984) and Eletsky, Petrov, and Meledina (1991). The key advance is that a single value of the four-quark condensate parameter kappa approximately 1.6 simultaneously reproduces the experimental values of the proton and neutron magnetic moments, the axial coupling, and the strong part of the neutron-proton mass difference, resolving the instability and arbitrariness inherent in the traditional exponential-kernel approach. The result enables more reliable extraction of nucleon static properties from QCD sum rules and constrains the value of the four-quark condensate, which has been estimated to vary between 1 (vacuum dominance) and 4 in the literature.
