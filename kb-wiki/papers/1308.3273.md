# Nuclear Jacobi and Poincaré Transitions at High Spins and Temperatures: Account~of~Dynamic~Effects~and~Large-Amplitude Motion

> We present a theoretical analysis of the competition between so-called nuclear Jacobi and Poincaré shape transitions in function of spin - at high temperatures. The latter condition implies the method of choice - a realistic version of the nuclear Liquid Drop Model (LDM), here: the Lublin-Strasbourg Drop (LSD) model. We address specifically the fact that the Jacobi and Poincaré shape transitions are accompanied by the flattening of total nuclear energy landscape as function of the relevant deformation parameters what enforces large amplitude oscillation modes that need to be taken into account. For that purpose we introduce an approximate form of the collective Schrödinger equation whose solutions are used to calculate the most probable deformations associated with both types of transitions and discuss the physical consequences in terms of the associated critical-spin values and transitions themselves.

## Key claim

The paper argues that Jacobi and Poincaré shape transitions in hot rotating nuclei are accompanied by a flattening of the total nuclear energy landscape, which enforces large-amplitude oscillation modes that must be taken into account via a collective Schrödinger equation; the static equilibrium shapes and the most probable (dynamical) deformations may differ considerably under these conditions, affecting predictions of critical-spin values.

## Method

The authors employ the Lublin-Strasbourg Drop (LSD) model, a realistic version of the nuclear Liquid Drop Model, to calculate macroscopic nuclear energies as functions of deformation and spin at high temperatures where shell effects are suppressed. They introduce a deformation-dependent congruence energy term and solve an approximate form of the collective Schrödinger equation in curvilinear coordinates to obtain collective wave functions, from which the most probable deformations associated with both Jacobi and Poincaré transitions are derived.

## Key numbers

- LSD model parameters fitted to measured atomic masses (from Ref. [KPD03]): volume energy coefficient \(b_{\rm vol.} = -15.4920\) MeV, surface energy coefficient \(b_{\rm surf.} = 16.9707\) MeV, curvature energy coefficient \(b_{\rm curv.} = 3.8602\) MeV, charge radius parameter \(r_0^{\rm ch} = 1.21725\) fm, and Coulomb diffuseness correction \(C_4 = 0.9181\) MeV.
- The volume energy is parametrized as \(E_{\rm vol.}(Z,N) = b_{\rm vol.}\,(1 - \kappa_{\rm vol.} I^2)\,A\) with \(\kappa_{\rm vol.} = 1.8601\).
- The surface energy is parametrized as \(E_{\rm surf.}(N,Z;\alpha) = b_{\rm surf.}\,(1 - \kappa_{\rm surf.} I^2)\,A^{2/3} B_{\rm surf.}(\alpha)\) with \(\kappa_{\rm surf.} = 2.2938\).
- The curvature energy is parametrized as \(E_{\rm curv.}(N,Z;\alpha) = b_{\rm curv.}\,(1 - \kappa_{\rm curv.} I^2)\,A^{1/3} B_{\rm curv.}(\alpha)\) with \(\kappa_{\rm curv.} = -2.3764\).
- The Coulomb energy term includes a factor \(\frac{3}{5} e^2 Z^2 / (r_0^{\rm ch} A^{1/3})\) multiplied by the deformation-dependent \(B_{\rm Coul.}(\alpha)\).
- The rotational energy is \(E_{\rm rot.}(Z,N;\alpha) = \frac{\hbar^2}{2\mathcal{J}(Z,N;\alpha)} L(L+1)\).
- The deformation-independent term includes an electron binding energy parametrization \(-0.00001433 \, Z^{2.39}\).
- The paper discusses critical spin values for fission, \(L < L_{\rm fiss.}\), and notes that the fast evolution of shapes with spin occurs in steps of \(\Delta L = 2\hbar\).

## Context

This work extends the LSD model approach previously applied to Jacobi transitions in nuclei such as \(^{46}\)Ti (Maj et al., 2001, 2004), \(^{42}\)Ca (Kmiecik et al., 2005), \(^{47}\)V (Pietri et al., 2010), and \(^{88}\)Mo (Ciemala et al., 2011), as well as to Poincaré transitions in Barium nuclei (Mazurek et al., 2011). It builds on earlier Liquid Drop Model calculations by Cohen, Plasil, and Swiatecki (1974) and Sierk (1986) that addressed static properties of nuclear potential energies, and introduces dynamic effects via the collective Schrödinger equation to account for large-amplitude shape fluctuations. The results enable improved predictions of critical spins for competing shape transitions and can be tested experimentally through Giant Dipole Resonance measurements and fission-fragment mass distributions.
