# Off-Shell NN Potential and Triton Binding Energy

> The NONLOCAL Bonn-B potential predicts 8.0 MeV binding energy for the triton (in a charge-dependent 34-channel Faddeev calculation) which is about 0.4 MeV more than the predictions by LOCAL NN potentials. We pin down origin and size of the nonlocality in the Bonn potential, in analytic and numeric form. The nonlocality is due to the use of the correct off-shell Feynman amplitude of one-boson-exchange avoiding the commonly used on-shell approximations which yield the local potentials. We also illustrate how this off-shell behavior leads to more binding energy. We emphasize that the increased binding energy is not due to on-shell differences (differences in the fit of the NN data or phase shifts). In particular, the Bonn-B potential reproduces accurately the $ε_1$ mixing parameter up to 350 MeV as determined in the recent Nijmegen multi-energy NN phase-shift analysis. Adding the relativistic effect from the relativistic nucleon propagators in the Faddeev equations, brings the Bonn-B result up to 8.2 MeV triton binding. This leaves a difference of only 0.3 MeV to experiment, which may possibly be explained by refinements in the treatment of relativity and the inclusion of other nonlocalities (e.~g., quark-gluon exchange at short range). Thus, it is conceivable that a realistic NN potential which describes the NN data up to 300 MeV correctly may explain the triton binding energy without recourse to 3-N forces; relativity would play a major role for this result.

# Off-Shell NN Potential and Triton Binding Energy

## Key claim

The nonlocal Bonn-B potential predicts 8.0 MeV triton binding energy, about 0.4 MeV more than local NN potentials, and this increase is due specifically to the use of correct off-shell Feynman amplitudes in one-boson-exchange rather than on-shell approximations. Adding relativistic nucleon propagators in the Faddeev equations brings the result to 8.2 MeV, leaving only 0.3 MeV discrepancy with experiment, suggesting that a realistic NN potential fitting data up to 300 MeV may explain triton binding without three-nucleon forces if relativity is properly included.

## Method

The authors performed charge-dependent 34-channel Faddeev calculations using the Bonn-B potential and compared the results to those from local potentials such as Paris. They isolated the origin of nonlocality by analytically expanding the relativistic one-pion-exchange Feynman amplitude and comparing it to the local approximation, showing that higher-order momentum terms (particularly the -k²/(4M²)Q₀(z) term) damp the tensor potential off-shell. They also examined the effect of the M/√(E_q0 E_k) factor applied to the Feynman amplitude and demonstrated that making the pion and rho tensor forces local in the Bonn potential produces results close to the Paris potential.

## Key numbers

- Bonn-B potential predicts 8.0 MeV triton binding energy in a charge-dependent 34-channel Faddeev calculation
- Local NN potentials predict 7.62 ± 0.01 MeV triton binding energy (Friar et al., 1993)
- Adding relativistic nucleon propagators in the Faddeev equations brings Bonn-B result to 8.2 MeV (Sammarruca et al., 1992)
- The remaining difference to the experimental triton binding energy is 0.3 MeV
- Bonn-B potential reproduces the ε₁ mixing parameter accurately up to 350 MeV as determined by the Nijmegen multi-energy NN phase-shift analysis (Stoks et al., 1993)
- The half off-shell potential comparison uses q₀ fixed at 153 MeV, corresponding to 50 MeV lab energy

## Context

This work extends the program of understanding triton binding energy discrepancies between local potentials like Paris (Lacombe et al., 1980) and nonlocal potentials like Bonn (Machleidt, 1989). The authors demonstrate that the nonlocality in the Bonn potential arises from using the full relativistic Feynman amplitude for one-boson-exchange rather than the on-shell approximations that yield local potentials, and that this off-shell behavior leads to a weaker off-shell tensor force which reduces quenching in the three-body system. The results suggest that a proper treatment of nonlocality and relativity may explain the triton binding energy without invoking three-nucleon forces, and the nonlocality from the M/√(E_q0 E_k) factor was further investigated by Gloeckle and Witala (1994).
