# Hyperspherical cluster model for bosons: application to sub-threshold halo states in helium drops

> To describe long-range behaviour of one particle removed from a few- or a many-body system, a hyperspherical cluster model has been developed. It has been applied to the ground and first excited states of helium drops with five, six, eight and ten atoms interacting via a two-body soft gaussian potential. Convergence of the hyperspherical cluster harmonics expansion is studied for binding energies, root-mean-squared radii and overlaps of the wave functions of two helium drops differing by one atom. It was shown that with increasing model space the functional form of such overlaps at large distances converges to the correct asymptotic behaviour. The asymptotic normalization coefficients that quantify the overlaps' amplitudes in this region are calculated. It was also shown that in the first excited state one helium atom stays far apart from the rest forming a two-body molecule, or a halo. The probability of finding the halo atom in the classically-forbidden region of space depends on the definition of the latter and on the number of atoms in the drop. The total norm of the overlap integrals, the spectroscopic factor, represents the number of partitions of a many-body state into a chosen state of the system with one particle removed. The spectroscopic factors have been calculated and their sum rules are discussed giving a further insight into the structure of helium drops.

## Key claim

The hyperspherical cluster model (HCM), reformulated to avoid numerical instabilities of its original version, can describe the long-range behavior of one particle removed from a bosonic system, and its application to helium drops shows that the first excited state of these drops forms a two-body molecule (halo) where one atom stays far apart from the rest, but the probability of finding the halo atom in the classically-forbidden region depends on the definition of that region and on the number of atoms in the drop.

## Method

The HCM selects specific hyperspherical harmonics (HH) states responsible for the behavior of one particle in the classically forbidden region, constructing them by symmetrizing the product of a known HH for the (A-1)-body core and a relative hyperangular function. For the K_c=0 case, the core HH is a constant, and the hyperradial potentials are calculated using coordinate transformations and Raynal-Revai coefficients. The coupled hyperradial Schrödinger equations are solved using a Lagrange-Laguerre mesh method for helium drops with A=5,6,8,10 atoms interacting via a two-body soft Gaussian potential.

## Key numbers

- Ground state binding energies for A=5: 1.9452 K (full HH) vs 1.9386 K (HCM) at K_max=14, a deviation of 0.3%
- Ground state binding energies for A=10: 18.910 K (full HH) vs 18.902 K (HCM) at K_max=12, a deviation of 0.04%
- First excited state binding energy for A=5: 0.83050 K (full HH) vs 0.80119 K (HCM) at K_max=24, a deviation of 3.7%
- First excited state binding energy for A=10: 15.736 K (full HH) at K_max=10 vs 15.713 K (HCM) at K_max=14, a deviation of 0.2%
- Ground state r.m.s. radius for A=5: 7.0251 a.u. (converged at K_max=14)
- First excited state r.m.s. radius for A=5: 13.044 a.u. (converged at K_max=60)
- Ground state r.m.s. radius for A=10: 5.2910 a.u. (converged at K_max=12)
- First excited state r.m.s. radius for A=10: 6.1412 a.u. (converged at K_max=22)
- Average distance between two bosons in the ground state: 11 a.u. for A=5, 10 a.u. for A=6, 8.7 a.u. for A=8, 7.9 a.u. for A=10

## Context

This work extends the hyperspherical cluster model originally proposed by Timofeyuk (2007) for fermionic systems, reformulating it to avoid numerical problems from summing terms differing by more than 16 orders of magnitude. It applies the model to bosonic helium drops, building on earlier studies by Gattobigio et al. (2014) and Kievsky et al. (2014) that showed shallow excited states persist below the one-atom removal threshold in helium drops with more than three atoms. The results enable calculation of asymptotic normalization coefficients and spectroscopic factors for these halo states, providing input for understanding single-particle long-range behavior in both nuclear physics (where overlaps are used for nucleon-removal reactions) and atomic physics (where they are called Dyson orbitals and used in photoelectron spectroscopy).
