# Chiralspin symmetry and QCD at high temperature

> It has been found very recently on the lattice that at high temperature at vanishing chemical potential QCD is increasingly SU(2)_CS and SU(2N_F) symmetric. We demonstrate that the chemical potential term in the QCD Lagrangian has precisely the same symmetry. Consequently the QCD matter beyond the chiral restoration line on the T - μplane is at least approximately SU(2)_CS and SU(2N_F) symmetric.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the quark chemical potential term in the QCD Lagrangian is invariant under SU(2)_CS and SU(2N_F) symmetries, meaning that QCD matter beyond the chiral restoration line on the T-μ plane should be at least approximately SU(2)_CS and SU(2N_F) symmetric, with the symmetries becoming more exact at higher temperatures and chemical potentials.

## Method

The author analyzes the symmetry properties of different parts of the QCD Lagrangian, splitting the quark-gluon interaction into temporal (chromo-electric) and spatial (chromo-magnetic plus kinetic) components. The chemical potential term μ Ψ̅ γ₄ Ψ is shown to transform as μ Ψ† Ψ under unitary transformations, establishing its invariance under SU(2)_CS and SU(2N_F) transformations. The argument builds on prior lattice evidence that truncation of near-zero Dirac modes at zero temperature reveals these symmetries, and that high-temperature lattice simulations at vanishing chemical potential show their emergence.

## Key numbers

- At the highest available temperature of 380 MeV, SU(2)_CS and SU(4) breaking effects in spatial correlation functions are at the level of 5%
- The symmetries emerge at a temperature of approximately 2 T_c on the T-μ phase diagram
- The paper discusses QCD matter at high temperature T ~ 400 MeV as approximately SU(2)_CS- and SU(4)-symmetric

## Context

This work extends lattice findings by Glozman (2014, 2015) showing SU(2)_CS and SU(4) symmetries emerge at high temperature at vanishing chemical potential, and earlier truncation studies by Denissenya (2014, 2015) at zero temperature. The paper argues that the chemical potential term reinforces rather than breaks these symmetries, contradicting the notion of a conventional quark-gluon plasma at these temperatures and instead proposing a "stringy fluid" of chiral quarks connected by chromo-electric fields. This enables future lattice studies at finite chemical potential to test the predicted symmetry pattern.
