# Progress on 3+1D Glasma simulations

> We review our progress on 3+1D Glasma simulations to describe the earliest stages of heavy-ion collisions. In our simulations we include nuclei with finite longitudinal extent and describe the collision process as well as the evolution of the strongly interacting gluonic fields in the laboratory frame in 3+1 dimensions using the colored particle-in-cell method. This allows us to compute the 3+1 dimensional Glasma energy-momentum tensor, whose rapidity dependence can be compared to experimental pion multiplicity data from RHIC. An improved scheme cures the numerical Cherenkov instability and paves the way for simulations at higher energies used at LHC.

# Progress on 3+1D Glasma simulations

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a classical leading-order Color Glass Condensate simulation with nuclei of finite longitudinal extent can produce Gaussian rapidity profiles in the energy density that agree qualitatively with experimental pion multiplicity data from RHIC, even without invoking quantum evolution or next-to-leading order effects. However, the shape of these profiles depends strongly on the infrared regulator \(m\), indicating that the screening length \(\lambda_D \propto 1/m\) plays an important role in generating the deviation from boost invariance.

## Method

The authors perform 3+1 dimensional simulations of heavy-ion collisions in the laboratory frame using the colored particle-in-cell (CPIC) method, where hard partons within the nuclei are represented as recoil-less auxiliary particles with time-dependent color charges coupled to classical Yang-Mills fields on a lattice. The nuclei are given a finite longitudinal extent via a Gaussian profile of width \(L = m_N R / \sqrt{s_{NN}}\), and the McLerran-Venugopalan (MV) model provides the stochastic distribution of color charges. An improved semi-implicit numerical scheme replaces the standard leapfrog discretization with time-averaged plaquette terms to cure the numerical Cherenkov instability that previously required prohibitively fine lattice spacing along the beam axis.

## Key numbers

- The longitudinal thickness of the nuclei is set to \(L = m_N R / \sqrt{s_{NN}}\) with nucleon mass \(m_N \approx 1\ \text{GeV}\), nuclear radius \(R\), and collision energy \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}\).
- The saturation momentum scales as \(Q_s^2 \approx (\sqrt{s_{NN}})^{0.25}\ \text{GeV}^2\), and the MV model parameter \(\mu\) is determined from \(0.75\, g^2 \mu \simeq Q_s\) with coupling \(g \approx 2\).
- Ultraviolet modes are regulated by a cutoff \(\Lambda = 10\ \text{GeV}\); the infrared regulator \(m\) is varied from 0.2 to \(0.8\ \text{GeV}\).
- Simulations use SU(2) gauge group on a lattice with \(2048 \times 192^2\) cells in a volume of \((6\ \text{fm})^3\).
- For \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\ \text{GeV}\) at RHIC, the Gaussian width of the local rest frame energy density profile at \(\tau_0 = 1\ \text{fm}/c\) is \(\sigma_\eta = 2.34\) for \(m = 0.2\ \text{GeV}\), \(\sigma_\eta = 1.66\) for \(m = 0.4\ \text{GeV}\), and \(\sigma_\eta = 1.28\) for \(m = 0.8\ \text{GeV}\).
- The experimental pion multiplicity width at RHIC is \(\sigma_\text{exp} = 2.25\); the Landau model predicts \(\sigma_\text{Landau} = \sqrt{\ln \gamma} \approx 2.15\).
- The system enters free-streaming evolution with longitudinal velocity \(v_z \approx z/t\) already at times \(\tau_0 \gtrsim 0.3\ \text{fm}/c\).

## Context

This work extends the standard boost-invariant Glasma formulation (Kovner 1995, Krasnitz 1998, Lappi 2011) by introducing nuclei with finite longitudinal extent, going beyond the approximation of infinitesimally thin discs that is valid only at infinite collision energy. It contrasts with approaches that break boost invariance through fluctuations on top of boost-invariant backgrounds (Gelis 2013, Fukushima 2011, Berges 2012) or through JIMWLK evolution (Schenke 2016), showing that classical-level effects from finite nuclear thickness can already generate rapidity-dependent observables. The results enable future comparisons with LHC data at higher energies using the improved semi-implicit solver, and may serve as initial conditions for subsequent hydrodynamic simulations that could refine the rapidity profile widths.
