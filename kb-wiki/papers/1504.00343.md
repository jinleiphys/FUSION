# Dissipative effects on quarkonium spectral functions

> Quarkonium at finite temperature is described as an open quantum system whose dynamics are determined by a potential $V_R({\bf x})$ and drag coefficient $η$, using a path integral with a non-local term. Path-integral Monte Carlo calculations determine the Euclidean Green function for this system to an accuracy greater than one part in a thousand and the maximum entropy method is used to determine the spectral function; challenges facing any kind of deconvolution are discussed in detail with the aim of developing intuition for when deconvolution is possible. Significant changes to the quarkonium spectral function in the $1S$ channel are found, suggesting that any description of quarkonium at finite temperature, using a potential, must also carefully consider the effect of dissipation.

## Key claim

Significant changes to the quarkonium spectral function in the 1S channel are found when dissipation is included, suggesting that any description of quarkonium at finite temperature using a potential must also carefully consider the effect of dissipation; the authors hedge that the results are for a specific open quantum system model with a Cornell-like potential and drag coefficients matched to a temperature of 285 MeV.

## Method

The authors model quarkonium above deconfinement as an open quantum system using a path integral with a non-local term derived from an influence functional, with dynamics determined by a renormalized potential V_R(x) and a drag coefficient η. Path-integral Monte Carlo calculations with 2^11+1 discretized time steps are used to compute the Euclidean Green function G(τ) to an accuracy greater than one part in a thousand, and the maximum entropy method with simulated annealing is then applied to deconvolve the spectral function from these Green functions.

## Key numbers

- The Cornell-like potential uses α_s = 0.499, σ = 0.16 GeV^2, and r_min = 0.4 GeV^{-1}
- Drag coefficients η = 0, 0.0729 GeV, and 0.1458 GeV were used, corresponding to spatial diffusion coefficients 2πD_c = ∞, 5, and 2.5 respectively at T = 285 MeV
- The relative difference ΔG/G between Green functions from spectral functions with widths 0.01 GeV and 0.05 GeV has a maximum of about one part in a thousand for the finite-temperature Green function at β = (1.5T_c)^{-1}
- For the zero-temperature-like Green function G(τ) over the range τ = 0.5 GeV^{-1} to τ = 9.6 GeV^{-1}, |ΔG|/G has a maximum of about 1%
- The test deconvolution used 15 data points with random Gaussian noise of standard deviation 10^{-6} GeV
- Without dissipation (η=0), the deconvolved spectral function has a ground state width of 0.25 GeV
- With 2πTD = 5 (η=0.0729 GeV), the width increases to 1 GeV, corresponding to a lifetime of 0.197 fm/c

## Context

This work extends the open quantum system approach to quarkonium developed by Young (2010) and builds on the influence functional formalism of Feynman and Hibbs, Caldeira and Leggett (1982), and Grabert, Schramm, and Ingold (1988). The results enable simultaneous consideration of the competing effects of drag and momentum diffusion on J/ψ survival rates in heavy-ion collisions, as previously studied by Young (2008, 2011). The paper also discusses challenges facing deconvolution of spectral functions from lattice QCD calculations, noting that significantly different spectral functions often differ by very little in their Laplace transforms.
