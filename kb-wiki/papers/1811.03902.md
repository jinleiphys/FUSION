# Semiclassical and quantum shell-structure calculations of the moment of inertia

> Shell corrections to the moment of inertia (MI) are calculated for a Woods-Saxon potential of spheroidal shape and at different deformations. This model potential is chosen to have a large depth and a small surface diffuseness which makes it resemble the analytically solved spheroidal cavity in the semiclassical approximation. For the consistent statistical-equilibrium collective rotations, the MI is obtained within the cranking model in an approach which goes beyond the quantum perturbation approximation based on the non perturbative energy spectrum. For the calculation of the MI shell corrections $δΘ$, the Strutinsky smoothing procedure is used to obtain the average occupation numbers of the particle density generated by the resolution of the Woods-Saxon eigenvalue problem. One finds that the major-shell structure of $δΘ$, as determined in the adiabatic approximation, is rooted, for large as well as for small surface deformations, in the same inhomogenuity of the distribution of single-particle states near the Fermi surface as the energy shell corrections $δE$. This fundamental property is in agreement with the semiclassical results $δΘ\propto δE$ obtained analytically within the non perturbative periodic orbit theory for any potential well, in particular for the spheroidal cavity, and for any deformation, even for large deformations where bifurcations of the equatorial orbits play a substantial role. Since the adiabatic approximation, $ω\ll Ω$, with $\hbar Ω$ the distance between major nuclear shells, is easily obeyed even for large angular momenta typical for high-spin physics at large particle numbers, our model approach seems to represent a tool that could be useful for the description of such nuclear systems.

## Key claim

The shell corrections to the moment of inertia (MI) for a Woods-Saxon potential with large depth and small surface diffuseness are shown to be proportional to the energy shell corrections, δΘ ∝ δE, at the level of major shell or major sub-shell structures, though a strict proportionality as predicted by semiclassical periodic orbit theory for integrable systems is not expected for this partially non-integrable potential.

## Method

The authors solve the Schrödinger equation for a Woods-Saxon potential of spheroidal shape by expanding eigenfunctions in a deformed axially-symmetric harmonic-oscillator basis. The moment of inertia is calculated within the cranking model using the generalized rigid-body (GRB) expression Θ ≈ m ∫ d r r⊥² ρ(r), where the quantum particle density ρ(r) is separated into a smooth part and a shell correction via the Strutinsky smoothing procedure. The MI shell corrections δΘ are then compared with energy shell corrections δE obtained from the same single-particle spectrum, and the results are interpreted using the semiclassical improved stationary phase method for periodic orbit theory (ISPMR).

## Key numbers

- The proportionality coefficient between MI and energy shell corrections for the spheroidal cavity is given by δΘ_scl / Θ_TF = (5 δE_scl) / (3 N ε_F), where Θ_TF = m (a² + b²) N/5 is the Thomas-Fermi (rigid-body) moment of inertia.
- The adiabatic approximation condition ω ≪ Ω is stated, with ℏΩ being the distance between major nuclear shells, which is easily obeyed even for large angular momenta typical for high-spin physics at large particle numbers.
- The semiclassical small parameter is ℏ/S ∼ N^{-1/3}, where S is the action integral for the dominating periodic orbits.

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical periodic orbit theory (POT) approach of Magner et al. (2014, 2015, 2017) from integrable systems like the harmonic oscillator and spheroidal cavity to the more realistic Woods-Saxon potential, which is partially non-integrable. It builds on the Strutinsky shell-correction method (Strutinsky, 1967; Brack and Damgaard, 1972) and the cranking model (Inglis, 1954; Bohr and Mottelson, 1975; Ring and Schuck, 1980), and is intended as a preliminary step toward a full description of rotating nuclear systems including pairing correlations, which are neglected here. The approach enables application to high-spin physics at large deformations and large particle numbers where the quantum perturbation expansion (Inglis cranking formula) fails.
