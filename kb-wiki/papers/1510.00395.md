# Pinning down the superfluid and measuring masses using pulsar glitches

> Pulsars are known for their superb timing precision, although glitches can interrupt the regular timing behavior when the stars are young. These glitches are thought to be caused by interactions between normal and superfluid matter in the crust of the star. However, glitching pulsars such as Vela have been shown to require a superfluid reservoir that greatly exceeds that available in the crust. We examine a model in which glitches tap the superfluid in the core. We test a variety of theoretical superfluid models against the most recent glitch data and find that only one model can successfully explain up to 45 years of observational data. We develop a new technique for combining radio and X-ray data to measure pulsar masses, thereby demonstrating how current and future telescopes can probe fundamental physics such as superfluidity near nuclear saturation.

## Key claim

Only one superfluid model (SFB) can successfully explain up to 45 years of glitch data from pulsars like Vela, and this model requires that the superfluid reservoir extends into the stellar core rather than being confined to the crust, with the additional condition that the pulsar's interior temperature must be low enough for the core neutrons to be superfluid.

## Method

The authors computed total and partial moments of inertia for neutron stars built using three nuclear equations of state (BSk20, BSk21, and APR), then compared the superfluid reservoir available in each model against the observed glitch activity parameter G = 2 tau_c * <A> for nine pulsars. They performed neutron star cooling simulations using standard neutrino emission processes to determine interior temperatures at each pulsar's age, and used these temperatures to evaluate which superfluid models satisfy the condition T < T_c at the densities required to match the observed glitch activity. For pulsars with X-ray surface temperature measurements, they converted surface temperature to interior temperature using standard envelope relations assuming an iron composition.

## Key numbers

* Vela pulsar requires a superfluid reservoir comprising 6.9% of the total moment of inertia (4.3 x 1.6%), based on 19 glitches over 45 years of observation
* PSR J0537-6910 requires a superfluid reservoir comprising 3.9% of the total moment of inertia (4.3 x 0.9%), based on 45 glitches over 13 years
* Vela's observed activity parameter G = 1.62 +/- 0.03% and characteristic age tau_c = 11,300 years
* PSR J0537-6910 has G = 0.875 +/- 0.005% and characteristic age tau_c = 4,930 years
* Using the BSk20 equation of state, Vela's mass is determined to be 1.51 +/- 0.04 solar masses and PSR J0537-6910's mass is 1.83 +/- 0.04 solar masses
* The SFB superfluid model provides a maximum relative moment of inertia of 2.9% for an old and cold 1.4 solar mass neutron star
* Vela pulsar is 11,000 (+5000/-5600) years old with a surface temperature of 9.3 x 10^5 K
* PSR J0537-6910 has an age of 2000 (+3000/-1000) years

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Link et al. (1999), who found that crust superfluid could explain glitches, but addresses the finding by Andersson et al. (2012) and Chamel (2013) that entrainment effects increase the required moment of inertia by a factor of approximately 4.3, making the crust reservoir insufficient. The paper also contrasts with the proposal by Piekarewicz et al. (2014) and Steiner et al. (2015) that unusually thick crusts could provide enough moment of inertia, noting that those solutions conflict with observationally inferred neutron star radii of approximately 11.8 +/- 0.9 km. The new technique combining glitch data with temperature-dependent superfluidity enables mass measurements for isolated pulsars, which previously could only be measured for pulsars in binary systems, and demonstrates how future facilities like the Square Kilometer Array and Athena+ can probe nuclear physics near saturation density.
