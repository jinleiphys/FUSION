# Optimized Perturbation Theory at Finite Temperature --- Renormalization and Nambu-Goldstone theorem in O(N) phi^4 Theory ---

> The optimized perturbation theory (OPT) at finite temperature recently developed by the present authors is reviewed by using O(N) phi^4 theory with spontaneous symmetry breaking. The method resums automatically higher loops (including the hard thermal loops) at high $T$ and simultaneously cures the problem of tachyonic poles at relatively low $T$. We prove that (i) the renormalization of the ultra-violet divergences can be carried out systematically in any given order of OPT, and (ii) the Nambu-Goldstone theorem is satisfied for arbitrary $N$ and for any given order of OPT.

# Optimized Perturbation Theory at Finite Temperature, Renormalization and Nambu-Goldstone theorem in O(N) phi^4 Theory

## Key claim

The optimized perturbation theory (OPT) at finite temperature can simultaneously resum hard thermal loops at high T and eliminate tachyonic poles at low T, while maintaining systematic renormalization of ultraviolet divergences and preserving the Nambu-Goldstone theorem order by order for arbitrary N, provided the mass decomposition is applied to both the Lagrangian and its counter terms.

## Method

The authors start from the O(N) phi^4 Lagrangian and introduce a new mass parameter m^2 via the identity mu^2 = m^2 - (m^2 - mu^2), assigning m^2 = O(delta^0) and chi = (m^2 - mu^2) = O(delta) in a modified loop expansion. They then construct the thermal effective potential and impose either the principle of minimal sensitivity (PMS) or the fastest apparent convergence (FAC) condition to determine the optimal m, which becomes a function of temperature and the field expectation value. Renormalization is carried out before resummation using the MS scheme with counter terms fixed at T=0, and the Nambu-Goldstone theorem is proven by noting that the effective potential retains manifest O(N) invariance at each loop order.

## Key numbers

- The critical quark mass ratio below which multiple solutions appear is m_q^crit / m_q^phys = 0.08, corresponding to m_pi^crit / m_pi^phys = 0.08, with m_pi^phys = 140 MeV.
- The critical temperature for m_q^crit / m_q^phys = 0.08 is T_c approximately 170 MeV.
- At high T where T^2 >> m^2, the analytic solution for the optimal mass is m^2(T) = mu^2 + (lambda/12) T^2.
- Vacuum parameters for m_sigma^peak = 550 MeV: mu^2 = -284^2 MeV^2, lambda = 73.0, h = 123^3 MeV^3, kappa = 255 MeV.
- Vacuum parameters for m_sigma^peak = 750 MeV: mu^2 = -375^2 MeV^2, lambda = 122, h = 124^3 MeV^3, kappa = 325 MeV.
- Vacuum parameters for m_sigma^peak = 1000 MeV: mu^2 = -469^2 MeV^2, lambda = 194, h = 125^3 MeV^3, kappa = 401 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the optimized perturbation theory approach previously applied to various quantum systems (Stevenson, 1981) and first considered for finite-temperature field theory by Okopinska (1987) and Banerjee and Mallik (1991). It addresses two known failures of naive perturbation theory at finite temperature: the breakdown from hard thermal loops at high T (Braaten and Pisarski, 1990) and the emergence of tachyonic poles at low T (Weinberg, 1974). The method enables systematic calculation of spectral functions and the chiral condensate in the O(4) sigma model as a low-energy effective theory of QCD, and it provides a framework to study the nature of the chiral phase transition, though the authors note that the apparent first-order transition near the chiral limit is likely an artifact of the mean-field approximation, as renormalization group analyses and lattice simulations (Kanaya, 1994) indicate a second-order transition for the O(4) model.
