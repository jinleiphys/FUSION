# Gravitomagnetism and Pulsar Beam Precession near a Kerr Black Hole

> A rotating black hole causes the spin-axis of a nearby pulsar to precess due to geodetic and gravitomagnetic frame-dragging effects. The aim of our theoretical work here is to explore how this spin-precession can modify the rate at which pulses are received on earth. Towards this end, we obtain the complete evolution of the beam vectors of pulsars moving on equatorial circular orbits in the Kerr spacetime, relative to asymptotic fixed observers. We proceed to establish that such spin-precession effects can significantly modify observed pulse frequencies and, in specific, we find that the observed pulse frequency rises sharply as the orbit shrinks, potentially providing a new way to locate horizons of Kerr black holes, even if observed for a very short time period. We also discuss implications for detections of sub-millisecond pulsars, pulsar nulling, quasi-periodic oscillations, multiply-peaked pulsar Fourier profiles and how Kerr black holes can potentially be distinguished from naked singularities.

## Key claim

The paper claims that gravitomagnetic spin-precession of a pulsar's spin-axis near a rotating Kerr black hole can significantly modify the observed pulse frequency, with the frequency rising sharply as the orbit shrinks, potentially providing a new way to locate black hole horizons even if observed for a very short time period. This claim is conditional on the pulsar being modeled as a test spinning particle on equatorial circular orbits, with spin-curvature coupling neglected.

## Method

The authors model a pulsar as a test spinning particle following the Fermi-Walker transport law for its intrinsic spin angular momentum, moving on equatorial circular Killing orbits in the Kerr spacetime. They use the Frenet-Serret tetrad formalism to compute the spin-precession frequency for both static Killing observers (spatially fixed) and stationary Killing observers (moving on circular orbits), then derive the complete time evolution of the beam vector relative to asymptotic fixed observers. The analysis is purely analytical, employing the results of Iyer and Vishveshwara (1993) and Chakraborty et al. (2017a, 2017b) for the precession frequencies in the strong-field regime.

## Key numbers

* For a pulsar on an equatorial circular geodesic around a Kerr black hole, the spin-precession frequency depends on the black hole mass M, spin parameter a, and orbital radius r; the Kepler frequencies for co-rotating and counter-rotating orbits are given by Ω_K± = M^(1/2) / (a M^(1/2) ± r^(3/2)).
* The Gravity Probe B mission measured a geodetic precession frequency of 6601.8 ± 18.3 marc-s yr^(-1) and a gravitomagnetic frame-dragging frequency of 37.2 ± 7.2 marc-s yr^(-1) (1 marc-s = 4.848 × 10^(-9) radians), compared to GR predicted values of 6606.1 marc-s yr^(-1) and 39.2 marc-s yr^(-1) respectively.
* For a millisecond pulsar near a Schwarzschild black hole with mass in the range 10^3–10^6 M_⊙, at about 20 M_BH away, shifts in pulse arrival times accumulate to about 2.5 μs every ~35 s (from Li et al. 2019).
* Spin-curvature coupling causes a millisecond pulsar near a black hole (r ≲ 50 M_BH, M_BH ≈ 10^3–10^6 M_⊙) to exhibit non-planar motion, with path length variations of ±5 km translating to timing changes of order ±10 μs (from Singh et al. 2014).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of spin-precession for test gyroscopes in stationary spacetimes developed by Iyer and Vishveshwara (1993) and the recent analyses of gyroscopic precession along timelike geodesics in Kerr spacetime by Bini et al. (2016, 2017). It also builds on studies of pulsar spin-precession in binaries by Stairs et al. (2004) and Clifton and Weisberg (2008), and on the analysis of spin-curvature coupling effects on pulsar motion near black holes by Singh et al. (2014) and Li et al. (2019). The work enables future studies of how gravitomagnetic spin-precession modifies observed pulse profiles, potentially allowing distinction between Kerr black holes and naked singularities, and providing a new method to locate black hole horizons.
