# Explaining observations of rapidly rotating neutron stars in LMXBs

> In a previous paper [M. E. Gusakov, A. I. Chugunov, and E. M. Kantor, Phys. Rev. Lett. 112, 151101 (2014)], we introduced a new scenario that explains the existence of rapidly rotating warm neutron stars (NSs) observed in low-mass X-ray binaries (LMXBs). Here it is described in more detail. The scenario takes into account the interaction between superfluid inertial modes and the normal (quadrupole) $m=2$ $r$-mode, which can be driven unstable by Chandrasekhar-Friedman-Schutz (CFS) mechanism. This interaction can only occur at some fixed "resonance" stellar temperatures; it leads to formation of the "stability peaks" which stabilize a star in the vicinity of these temperatures. We demonstrate that a NS in LMXB spends a substantial fraction of time on the stability peak, that is, in the region of stellar temperatures and spin frequencies, that has been previously thought to be CFS unstable with respect to excitation of $r$-modes. We also find that the spin frequencies of NSs are limited by the CFS instability of normal (octupole) $m=3$ $r$-mode rather than by $m=2$ $r$-mode. This result agrees with the predicted value of the cutoff spin frequency $\sim 730$ Hz in the spin distribution of accreting millisecond X-ray pulsars. In addition, we analyze evolution of a NS after the end of the accretion phase and demonstrate that millisecond pulsars can be born in LMXBs within our scenario. Besides millisecond pulsars, our scenario also predicts a new class of LMXB descendants - hot and rapidly rotating nonaccreting NSs ("hot widows"/HOFNARs). Further comparison of the proposed theory with observations of rotating NSs can impose new important constraints on the properties of superdense matter.

## Key claim

The existence of rapidly rotating warm neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binaries can be explained by resonance interaction between the normal quadrupole m=2 r-mode and superfluid inertial modes, which occurs only at specific fixed stellar temperatures and creates "stability peaks" that stabilize the star in regions previously thought to be CFS unstable.

## Method

The authors model the thermorotational evolution of a canonical 1.4 solar mass neutron star with radius 10 km, using a polytropic equation of state and including shear viscosity from electron scattering as the primary dissipation mechanism. They solve coupled equations for the r-mode amplitude, stellar spin frequency, and internal temperature, incorporating accretion torque, gravitational wave emission, neutrino cooling, and deep crustal heating, while treating the nonlinear saturation amplitude as alpha_sat = 10^-4. The resonance interaction between the normal m=2 r-mode and superfluid inertial modes is introduced at specific temperatures where mode frequencies match.

## Key numbers

- Cutoff spin frequency for accreting millisecond X-ray pulsars: approximately 730 Hz, consistent with the octupole m=3 r-mode instability limiting spin frequencies to 600-700 Hz at T^infinity ~ 10^8 K
- For a canonical neutron star (M=1.4 M_sun, R=10 km), the mean density is approximately 6.646 x 10^14 g/cm^3
- Gravitational radiation timescale for m=2 r-mode: tau_GR0 approximately 46.4 s; for m=3 r-mode: tau_GR0 approximately 1250 s (both normalized to M=1.4 M_sun, R=10 km, nu=1 kHz)
- Shear viscosity damping timescale for m=2 r-mode: tau_S0 approximately 2.2 x 10^5 s; for m=3 r-mode: tau_S0 approximately 2.4 x 10^4 s (both at T^infinity_8 = 1)
- Accretion torque parameter: Omega_dot_acc approximately 3.73 x 10^-6 s^-1 yr^-1 for p=1, M_dot = 10^-10 M_sun/yr, canonical star
- Equilibrium temperature from accretion heating alone: T_eq^infinity approximately 1.078 x 10^8 K for M_dot = 3.0 x 10^-10 M_sun/yr
- Saturation amplitude assumed: alpha_sat = 10^-4 for all modes

## Context

This work extends the scenario first introduced in Gusakov, Chugunov, and Kantor (2014) and addresses the long-standing paradox that many rapidly rotating warm neutron stars in LMXBs lie outside the standard r-mode stability region calculated under realistic assumptions about superdense matter. Previous attempts to resolve this paradox either required artificial enhancement of damping mechanisms (as in Andersson and Kokkotas 2001, Haskell et al. 2012) or invoked nonlinear saturation at unrealistically small amplitudes (as in Haskell et al. 2011, Mahmoodifar and Strohmayer 2013). The new resonance interaction mechanism allows explanation of all observed sources within standard minimal assumptions about superdense matter properties, and predicts a new class of LMXB descendants: hot and rapidly rotating nonaccreting neutron stars ("hot widows"/HOFNARs).
