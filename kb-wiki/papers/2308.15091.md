# Impact of quadrupole deformation on intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions

> This study employs the isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck model to simulate intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions between prolate nuclei $^{24}$Mg. The emphasis is on investigating the influence of centrality and orientation in several collision scenarios. The final-state particle multiplicities and anisotropic flows are primarily determined by the eccentricity and the area of the initial overlap. This not only provides feedback on the collision systems, but also, to some extent, provides a means to explore the fine structure inside deformed nuclei. Additionally, non-polarized collisions have been further discussed. These results contribute to the understanding of the geometric effects in nuclear reactions, and aid in the exploration of other information on reaction systems, such as the equation of state and nuclear high-momentum tail.

## Key claim

The study claims that in intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions of deformed prolate nuclei, the final-state particle multiplicities and anisotropic flows are primarily determined by the eccentricity and area of the initial overlap, but this relationship breaks down for body-body collisions at larger impact parameters due to the longer reaction time and viscosity among nucleons, unlike in high-energy reactions where geometric effects are more cleanly captured.

## Method

The authors used the isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (IBUU) transport model to simulate collisions of prolate $^{24}$Mg nuclei at 0.5 GeV/nucleon. Instead of the traditional deformed Woods-Saxon density, they initialized the nucleon spatial distribution using relativistic mean field (RMF) calculations with the DD-PC1 interaction including Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer pairing. They simulated tip-tip, body-body, and spherical-spherical collision scenarios at impact parameters of 0 fm (ultracentral), 4 fm (semi-central), and 6 fm (peripheral), averaging 300,000 events for each scenario.

## Key numbers

- Quadrupole deformation $\beta_2 = 0.53$ for $^{24}$Mg, with triaxial deformation $\beta_{2,2}=0$
- Saturation density $\rho_0 = 0.17\,\mathrm{fm}^{-3}$; ultracentral collisions reach twice this value
- Impact parameters used: $b=0\,\text{fm}$ (ultracentral), $b=4\,\text{fm}$ (semi-central), and $b=6\,\text{fm}$ (peripheral)
- In tip-body collisions ($\theta=90^\circ$), geometric overlap eccentricity equals 0.195; smoothing out the density peaks at $z = \pm 2\,\text{fm}$ reduces eccentricity to 0.175, corresponding to a 10% decrease in $v_2$ strength
- Linear response coefficient $k \approx 0.0675$ for body-body ultracentral collisions
- For non-polarized collisions, the deformation effect reaches a distinguishable 10% only when $l = 25$ events are combined (over 1,200 particles); with spectator screening (Avg.2), detection is possible with $l = 10$ (500 particles)
- The deformation effect $f_1(\beta) = 1.76 \times 10^{-4}$ from AMPT at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\,\text{GeV}$; from IBUU at 0.5 GeV/nucleon, $f_1(\beta) = 0$ for Avg. and $4.00 \times 10^{-5}$ for Avg.2
- $\langle\epsilon_2^2(\beta)\rangle = 0.036$ from RMF density, compared to $\langle\epsilon_2^2\rangle = 0.239 \beta_2^2 = 0.067$ from Woods-Saxon density in Jia (2022)

## Context

This work extends the study of nuclear deformation effects from ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, where such effects have been studied using the AMPT model by Zhang (2022) and Jia (2022), down to intermediate energies using the IBUU transport model. The use of RMF-calculated densities rather than the traditional Woods-Saxon form provides more realistic nuclear structure information. The results enable future extraction of the equation of state from ultracentral deformed nucleus-nucleus collisions and suggest that neural network methods, as used by Yang (2023) for density-related studies, could map collective flows to nuclear density profiles. The study also provides a reference for eliminating deformation interference when exploring other nuclear properties such as symmetry energy, neutron skin, bubble structure, and high-momentum tail.
