# Imposing multi-physics constraints at different densities on the Neutron Star Equation of State

> Neutron star matter spans a wide range of densities, from that of nuclei at the surface to exceeding several times normal nuclear matter density in the core. While terrestrial experiments, such as nuclear or heavy-ion collision experiments, provide clues about the behaviour of dense nuclear matter, one must resort to theoretical models of neutron star matter to extrapolate to higher density and finite neutron/proton asymmetry relevant for neutron stars. In this work, we explore the parameter space within the framework of the Relativistic Mean Field model allowed by present uncertainties compatible with state-of-the-art experimental data. We apply a cut-off filter scheme to constrain the parameter space using multi-physics constraints at different density regimes: chiral effective field theory, nuclear and heavy-ion collision data as well as multi-messenger astrophysical observations of neutron stars. Using the results of the study, we investigate possible correlations between nuclear and astrophysical observables.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that imposing a sequential "cut-off filter" of multi-physics constraints at different density regimes, chiral effective field theory at low densities, heavy-ion collision data at intermediate densities, and multi-messenger astrophysical observations at high densities, can systematically restrict the parameter space of Relativistic Mean Field models for the neutron star equation of state, but the constraints from heavy-ion collisions are acknowledged to be model-dependent and require confirmation by independent experimental efforts.

## Method

The authors generate a large set of neutron star equations of state by randomly varying the coupling constants of the Relativistic Mean Field model within their allowed uncertainty ranges, defined by nuclear empirical parameters at saturation density. They then apply a binary cut-off filter scheme: each equation of state is assigned a probability of 1 if it satisfies all imposed physical constraints simultaneously, and 0 otherwise. The constraints are applied sequentially by density regime: chiral effective field theory results for pure neutron matter at densities 0.5–1.4 n₀, heavy-ion collision data from KaoS, FOPI, and ASY-EOS experiments at intermediate densities 1–3 n₀, and multi-messenger astrophysical observations (pulsar mass measurements and gravitational wave tidal deformability limits) at high densities.

## Key numbers

- Maximum neutron star mass must exceed 2.08 ± 0.07 M⊙, from the revised measurement of pulsar PSR J0740+6620 (Fonseca 2021)
- Effective tidal deformability upper limit Λ < 720 from the GW170817 event, using the low-spin highest posterior density interval (Abbott 2019)
- The tidal deformability constraint implies R₁.₄M⊙ < 13.5 km for a 1.4 M⊙ neutron star
- Chiral effective field theory constraints apply in the baryon density range 0.5–1.4 n₀, where n₀ ≈ 0.16 fm⁻³
- Heavy-ion collision constraints from KaoS indicate the equation of state at 2–3 n₀ must be soft, with incompressibility K of approximately 200 MeV or less
- From the ASY-EOS experiment, the power-law coefficient γ describing the density dependence of the symmetry energy is γ = 0.72 ± 0.19
- The FOPI experiment constrains the binding energy of symmetric nuclear matter in the density region n_b/n₀ ∼ 1.4–2.0

## Context

This work extends the cut-off filter approach of Hornick et al. (2018), who imposed limits on isovector saturation parameters using chiral effective field theory and multi-messenger astrophysical data, by additionally varying all nuclear parameters within their uncertainties and incorporating heavy-ion collision constraints at intermediate densities. The study contrasts with Bayesian inference schemes using piecewise polytropes or spectral parametrizations (e.g., Annala 2018, Tews 2018, Capano 2020) that overlook the role of underlying nuclear physics degrees of freedom, and with meta-modelling approaches (Margueron 2018) whose validity at high densities is questionable. The results enable systematic exploration of correlations between nuclear empirical parameters (such as symmetry energy and its slope) and neutron star observables (radii and tidal deformability) within a realistic phenomenological model framework.
