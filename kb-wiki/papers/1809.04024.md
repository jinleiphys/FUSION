# Production of Light Nuclei at Thermal Freezeout in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We revisit the problem of the production of light atomic nuclei in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. While their production systematics is well produced by hadro-chemical freezeout at temperatures near the QCD pseudo-critical temperature, their small binding energies of a few MeV per nucleon suggest that they cannot survive as bound states under these conditions. Here, we adopt the concept of effective chemical potentials in the hadronic evolution from chemical to thermal freezeout (at typically $T_{\rm fo}$$\simeq$100\,MeV), which, despite frequent elastic rescatterings in hadronic matter, conserves the effective numbers of particles which are stable under strong interactions, most notably pions, kaons and nucleons. It turns out that the large chemical potentials that build up for antibaryons result in thermal abundances of light nuclei and antinuclei, formed at thermal freezeout, which essentially agree with the ones evaluated at chemical freezeout. Together with their transverse-momentum spectra, which also indicate a kinetic freezeout near $T_{\rm fo}$, this provides a natural explanation for their production systematics without postulating their survival at high temperatures.

## Key claim
The paper argues that light nuclei and antinuclei observed in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions can be formed at thermal freezeout (around 100 MeV) rather than at chemical freezeout (around 160 MeV), because effective chemical potentials that conserve baryon and antibaryon numbers separately during the hadronic phase preserve the chemical-freezeout abundance ratios down to lower temperatures, thereby avoiding the puzzle of nuclei surviving at temperatures far above their binding energies.

## Method
The authors adopt the concept of partial chemical equilibrium from chemical freezeout (T_ch ≈ 160 MeV) to thermal freezeout (T_fo ≈ 100 MeV), introducing effective chemical potentials for hadrons stable under strong interactions (pions, kaons, nucleons) and a separate effective chemical potential for antibaryons that conserves antibaryon number despite large annihilation cross sections. They compute densities of light nuclei (deuteron, triton, ³He, ⁴He) and their antinuclei using Boltzmann distributions with chemical potentials set to A·μ_N for nuclei and A·(−μ_N + μ_B^eff) for antinuclei, then compare antiparticle-to-particle ratios and absolute yields to STAR and ALICE data at both chemical and kinetic freezeout. Transverse-momentum spectra are calculated using a standard blastwave parametrization with temperature fixed to 100 MeV and flow parameters adjusted to fit the data.

## Key numbers
- Chemical freezeout temperature: T_ch ≈ 160 MeV; thermal freezeout temperature: T_fo ≈ 100 MeV
- Baryon chemical potential at chemical freezeout: μ_B^ch ≈ 25 MeV at top RHIC energy (√s = 200 GeV), ≈ 1 MeV at LHC energies (√s of several TeV)
- Effective antibaryon chemical potential reaches close to 800 MeV at the lowest temperatures
- Surface flow velocity for central Au-Au at RHIC: β_s = 0.780c with exponent n = 1.2; for central Pb-Pb at LHC: β_s = 0.866c with exponent n = 0.8
- Deuteron mass: 1875.6 MeV (spin degeneracy 3); triton mass: 2808.9 MeV (degeneracy 2); ³He mass: 2809.3 MeV (degeneracy 2); ⁴He mass: 3728.3 MeV (degeneracy 1)
- Antiparticle-to-particle ratios at LHC are compatible with 1, reflecting near baryon-antibaryon symmetry at mid-rapidity
- Absolute yields vary over about 6 orders of magnitude between antideuterons and anti-⁴He at LHC

## Context
This work extends the partial chemical equilibrium framework previously developed by Rapp (2002) and Rapp (2000) for antibaryon conservation in hadronic matter, and applies it to the specific puzzle of light nucleus production in heavy-ion collisions. The statistical hadronization model at chemical freezeout (Andronic 2010, Andronic 2016) successfully describes nucleus yields but cannot explain their survival at high temperatures; coalescence models (Kapusta 1980, Scheibl 1998) require extra parameters. The present approach shows that chemical-freezeout ratios are preserved down to thermal freezeout without additional parameters, and suggests that nuclear bound states can form after kinetic freezeout in a "nuclear freezeout" stage. A related work by Oliinychenko (2018) using hadronic transport appeared shortly before submission.
