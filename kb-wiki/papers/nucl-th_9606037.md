# The Multichannel Quark Model

> We discuss the non-relativistic multichannel quark model and describe the techniques developed to solve the resulting equations. We then investigate some simple solutions to demonstrate how the model unifies meson-meson scattering with meson spectroscopy, thereby greatly extending the domain of applicability of the naive quark model. In the limits of narrow resonance widths and no quark exchange, it reproduces the standard quark model spectroscopy and Breit-Wigner phase description. Outside those limits s-channel resonance masses are lowered by their two-meson couplings, the line-shapes of wide resonances are significantly altered, and the equivalent Breit-Wigner masses and widths show an energy dependence. Because meson-meson interactions are due to coherent s-channel resonance production and t-channel quark exchange (though other interactions can readily be added), the multichannel equations model experimental resonance production and decay in a way that the usual eigenvalue equations cannot.

## Key claim

The multichannel quark model unifies meson-meson scattering with meson spectroscopy by embedding the quark-model eigenvalue equation into a coupled-channel matrix formulation, thereby extending the naive quark model's domain of applicability. However, the model's quantitative predictions require implementation of more accurate vertex models, inclusion of all two-meson channels, and retuning of Hamiltonian parameters to experimental data.

## Method

The authors construct a non-relativistic coupled-channel Schrodinger equation that couples two-meson scattering states (e.g., K-pi) to s-channel quark-antiquark (q-qbar) resonance states via a transition potential V_ABR, and also includes t-channel quark exchange potentials derived from variational q-qbar-q-qbar calculations. The resulting matrix equation is solved numerically using Noumerov techniques to obtain energy-dependent wavefunctions, from which S-matrix parameters, phase shifts, and resonance masses and widths are extracted.

## Key numbers

- Quark model parameters used: m_u = m_d = 0.375 GeV, m_s = 0.650 GeV, b = 0.782 GeV/fm, alpha_c = 0.857, C = -0.581 GeV, alpha_h = 0.840, sigma_h = 0.700 GeV
- Resulting naive quark model meson masses (in GeV): m_pi = 0.133, m_K = 0.494, m_rho = 0.775, m_K* = 0.913, m_phi = 1.019, m_b1 = 1.230, m_K1 = 1.389, m_h1' = 1.512, m_aJ = 1.280, m_KJ = 1.430, m_fJ' = 1.550
- For the K0 scalar meson with a width of 55 MeV (toy value), the two-channel phase shift passes through 90 degrees at 1.412 GeV, down from the bare eigenenergy of 1.430 GeV
- For the K0 with width 280 MeV (experimental value), the ground state mass is 1.355 GeV, a full 75 MeV below the bare eigenenergy of 1.430 GeV
- First and second radial excitations of the K0 appear at 2.020 and 2.553 GeV, respectively 27 and 12 MeV below their eigenenergies
- K2-K0 mass splitting of 22 MeV arises from d-wave versus s-wave K-pi couplings; K0-K1 mass difference of 17 MeV arises from K-pi versus K*-pi couplings
- The transition potential range parameter r_0 = 3.0 GeV^{-1} (approximately 0.6 fm)

## Context

This work extends the naive quark model spectroscopy approach (which treats mesons as stable eigenstates of a q-qbar Hamiltonian) by incorporating finite-width effects through coupling to two-meson scattering channels. The model builds on earlier square-well approximations by Weinstein (1991, 1993) and complements the Born-approximation quark-exchange potential work by Barnes and Swanson. The multichannel framework enables study of how resonance masses are lowered by two-meson couplings, how Breit-Wigner parameters become energy-dependent for wide resonances, and how different decay channels break degeneracies (e.g., K0, K1, K2 splitting). The approach can be extended to study whether annihilation vertices are dominated by 3P0 or 3S1 (hybrid) mechanisms, and to investigate the interpretation of nearby resonances in different channels as the same underlying state.
