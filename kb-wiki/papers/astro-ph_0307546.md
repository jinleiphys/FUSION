# Neutrino Nucleosynthesis

> We study neutrino process nucleosynthesis in massive stars using newly calculated cross sections, an expanded reaction network, and complete and self-consistent models of the progenitor star. We reevaluate the production of light isotopes from abundant progenitors as well as that of rare, heavy, proton-rich isotopes. In particular, new results are given for B11, F19, La138, and Ta180. The production of these isotopes places limits on neutrino spectrum and oscialltions.

## Key claim

The neutrino process can produce rare, heavy, proton-rich isotopes like $^{138}$La and $^{180}$Ta in massive stars, but the yields depend sensitively on the assumed neutrino spectrum and on whether neutrino oscillations occur; the production of $^{138}$La is dominated by charged-current reactions on $^{138}$Ba and is particularly sensitive to the $\nu_e$ temperature, making it a potential "neutrino thermometer" that could probe the mixing angle $\theta_{13}$.

## Method

The authors performed full $\nu$-process calculations using the KEPLER code for 15 and 25 $M_\odot$ progenitor stars evolved with mass loss, incorporating an expanded reaction network that includes all heavy elements through bismuth. Neutrino cross sections were calculated as two-step processes: first, charged- and neutral-current responses were evaluated using shell-model diagonalizations for $p$- and $sd$-shell nuclei and random phase approximation (RPA) for heavier nuclei; second, the statistical model SMOKER was used to calculate branching ratios for proton, neutron, alpha, and gamma emission from excited states. The calculations included all dynamics, explosive and $\gamma$-process nucleosynthesis, and all network reactions that could destroy parent or daughter isotopes.

## Key numbers

- $^{11}$B production factor relative to solar (normalized to $^{16}$O) in a 15 $M_\odot$ star: 1.90 with $\nu_e$ temperature 4 MeV and $\mu/\tau$ neutrino temperature 6 MeV; 3.99 with $\mu/\tau$ neutrino temperature 8 MeV (using new Kolbe rates)
- $^{19}$F production factor in a 15 $M_\odot$ star: 0.64 with $\nu_e$ temperature 4 MeV and $\mu/\tau$ neutrino temperature 6 MeV (Kolbe rates), compared to 0.83 using Haxton rates from Woo90
- $^{138}$La production factor in a 15 $M_\odot$ star: 0.97 with $\nu_e$ temperature 4 MeV; 2.46 with $\nu_e$ temperature 8 MeV
- $^{180}$Ta production factor in a 15 $M_\odot$ star: 2.75 with $\nu_e$ temperature 4 MeV; 6.03 with $\nu_e$ temperature 8 MeV
- Required cross section $\sigma_{\odot,42}$ for solar production of $^{51}$V: $3.0 \times 10^{-42}$ cm$^2$ (from $^{52}$Fe via $(\nu_e, e^- n)$)
- Required cross section $\sigma_{\odot,42}$ for solar production of $^{55}$Mn: $2.0 \times 10^{-42}$ cm$^2$ (from $^{56}$Ni via $(\nu_e, e^- p)$)
- Assumed neutrino temperatures: 4 MeV for $\nu_e$ and $\bar{\nu}_e$, 6 MeV for $\mu$ and $\tau$ neutrinos and their antiparticles (Fermi-Dirac distributions with zero chemical potential)
- Total supernova neutrino energy assumed: $3 \times 10^{53}$ erg, with exponential decay time-scale of 3 s

## Context

This work extends the earlier $\nu$-process calculations of Woo90, who evaluated productions in a 20 $M_\odot$ star without mass loss and with a reaction network limited to zinc, by incorporating mass loss, an expanded network through bismuth, and improved neutrino cross sections using the SMOKER statistical model. The new results for $^{11}$B and $^{19}$F are compared to recent observational data: Prochaska, Howk, and Wolfe (2003) observed a solar B/O ratio in a high-redshift galaxy, arguing for primary production via the $\nu$-process, while Cunha et al. (2003) found low F/O ratios in $\omega$ Centauri stars consistent with $\nu$-process models. The sensitivity of $^{138}$La and $^{180}$Ta yields to $\nu_e$ temperature opens the possibility of using these isotopes as probes of neutrino oscillations, particularly the mixing angle $\theta_{13}$, following the suggestion of Goriely et al. (2001) that charged-current reactions on $^{138}$Ba dominate $^{138}$La production.
