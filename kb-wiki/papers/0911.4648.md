# Microscopic-Macroscopic Approach for Binding Energies with Wigner-Kirkwood Method

> The semi-classical Wigner-Kirkwood $\hbar$ expansion method is used to calculate shell corrections for spherical and deformed nuclei. The expansion is carried out up to fourth order in $\hbar$. A systematic study of Wigner-Kirkwood averaged energies is presented as a function of the deformation degrees of freedom. The shell corrections, along with the pairing energies obtained by using the Lipkin-Nogami scheme, are used in the microscopic-macroscopic approach to calculate binding energies. The macroscopic part is obtained from a liquid drop formula with six adjustable parameters. Considering a set of 367 spherical nuclei, the liquid drop parameters are adjusted to reproduce the experimental binding energies, which yields a {\it rms} deviation of 630 keV. It is shown that the proposed approach is indeed promising for the prediction of nuclear masses.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the semi-classical Wigner-Kirkwood (WK) $\hbar$ expansion, carried out up to fourth order, can serve as a reliable alternative to the conventional Strutinsky smoothing scheme for calculating shell corrections in the microscopic-macroscopic approach to nuclear binding energies, particularly for deformed nuclei where the WK method avoids the practical difficulties of requiring a discrete single-particle spectrum extending well above the Fermi energy.

## Method

The authors used the Wigner-Kirkwood $\hbar$ expansion up to fourth order to compute the smooth (averaged) energy for neutrons and protons moving in a Woods-Saxon potential including spin-orbit and Coulomb interactions, with three deformation degrees of freedom ($\beta_2$, $\beta_4$, $\gamma$). The quantum mechanical energy was obtained by diagonalising the one-body Hamiltonian in an axially symmetric deformed harmonic oscillator basis with 14 shells, and the shell correction was taken as the difference between the quantum and WK energies. Pairing correlations were included via the Lipkin-Nogami scheme with odd-nucleon blocking, and the macroscopic part of the binding energy came from a liquid drop formula with six adjustable parameters fitted to experimental binding energies of 367 spherical nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The liquid drop parameters were adjusted to reproduce experimental binding energies for a set of 367 spherical nuclei, yielding an rms deviation of 630 keV.
- The Woods-Saxon central potential strength parameters are $U_0 = 53.754$ MeV and $U_1 = 0.791$, with half-density radius parameters $r_0 = 1.19$ fm, $c_1 = 0.116$, and $c_2 = 0.235$ fm, and diffuseness $a = 0.637$ fm.
- The spin-orbit potential uses $\lambda_0 = 29.494$ and $U_2 = 0.162$, with the same radius and diffuseness as the central potential.
- For a system of approximately 126 particles, the Thomas-Fermi energy is of order $10^3$ MeV, the second-order ($\hbar^2$) correction contributes a few tens of MeV, and the fourth-order ($\hbar^4$) correction contributes about 1 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the microscopic-macroscopic approach of Moller et al. (1995, 1997), Pomorski and Dudek (2003), and Myers and Swiatecki (1996) by replacing the Strutinsky averaging method with the Wigner-Kirkwood $\hbar$ expansion, which avoids the need for a discrete single-particle spectrum extending well into the continuum. The approach is intended to enable more reliable mass predictions for nuclei far from stability, where the Strutinsky method becomes problematic, and the authors note that typical Mic-Mac models yield better than about 0.7 MeV rms deviation in masses.
