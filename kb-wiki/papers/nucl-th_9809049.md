# Stability of the E2/M1 ratio at the T-matrix pole

> We show how the stability of the E2/M1 ratio, evaluated at the T-matrix pole, can be understood given a much wider variation at the K-matrix pole.

# Stability of the E2/M1 ratio at the T-matrix pole

## Key claim
The E2/M1 ratio evaluated at the T-matrix pole is stable across different models because the dominant contribution to the electric quadrupole (E2) amplitude at that pole comes from the Born-term part of the parameterization, which is essentially fixed, even though the same ratio evaluated at the K-matrix pole can vary by a factor of five.

## Method
The authors fitted the pion photoproduction database in the 250–450 MeV energy range using a multipole parameterization of the form M = α(1 + iT_πN) + βT_πN, where T_πN is the πN T-matrix, α includes projected Born terms, and β accounts for the energy dependence of the dominant M1 amplitude. They then continued the fitted parameters into the complex energy plane to extract the E2 and M1 residues at the T-matrix pole, comparing results from two different databases that gave very different K-matrix pole ratios.

## Key numbers
- The K-matrix pole E2/M1 ratio varied by a factor of five between the two fits to different databases.
- The term iα_E2/β_M1 at the T-matrix pole is about 0.05 in modulus and is predominantly imaginary.
- The fits covered the restricted energy range of 250–450 MeV, spanning the delta resonance.

## Context
This work extends the observation by the Mainz group (HDT, 1990s) that the E2/M1 ratio is particularly stable when evaluated at the T-matrix pole position, whereas models gave K-matrix ratios differing by factors of two or three. The stability was subsequently verified by the VPI and RPI groups. The authors provide a simple parameterization-based explanation for this stability, showing that the T-matrix pole ratio probes different parts of the photoproduction amplitude than the K-matrix pole ratio, and note that the more difficult question of separating bare/dressed or resonant/background contributions must still be addressed for comparison with quark models such as those by Buchmann (1990s) and Aznauryan (1990s).
