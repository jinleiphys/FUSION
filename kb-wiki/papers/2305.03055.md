# The maximum mass and deformation of rotating strange quark stars with strong magnetic fields

> We study the structure and total energy of a strange quark star (SQS) endowed with a strong magnetic field with different rotational frequencies. The MIT bag model is used, with the density-dependent bag constant for the equation of state (EOS). The EOS is computed considering the Landau quantization effect regarding the strong magnetic fields (up to $5\times10^{17}$ G) in the interior of the strange quark star. Using the LORENE library, we calculate the structural parameters of SQS for different setups of magnetic field strengths and rotational frequencies. In each setup, we perform calculations for $51$ stellar configurations, with specified central enthalpy values. We investigate the configurations with the maximum gravitational mass of SQS in each setup. Our models of SQSs are compared in the maximum gravitational mass, binding energy, compactness, and deformation of the star. We show that the gravitational mass might exceed $2.3 M_\odot$ in some models, which is comparable with the mass of the recently detected ``black widow'' pulsar \emph{PSR J0952-0607} and the mass of \emph{GW190814} detected by the LIGO/Virgo collaboration. The deformation and maximum gravitational mass of SQS can be characterized by simple functions that have been fitted to account for variations in both magnetic field strength and frequency. Rapidly rotating strange stars have a minimum gravitational mass given by the equatorial mass-shedding limit.

## Key claim

The gravitational mass of a strange quark star (SQS) with a strong magnetic field and rapid rotation might exceed 2.3 solar masses in some models, but this result depends on the specific combination of magnetic field strength and rotational frequency; the maximum mass reaches 2.80 solar masses only at the highest rotation (1200 Hz) and strongest magnetic field (approximately 5 x 10^17 G) considered.

## Method

The authors used the MIT bag model with a density-dependent bag constant to compute the equation of state (EOS) for strange quark matter, incorporating Landau quantization effects due to magnetic fields up to 5 x 10^17 G. They solved the Einstein field equations in stationary, axisymmetric spacetime using the LORENE library, which employs spectral methods, for 51 stellar configurations per setup with central enthalpy values from 0.01 c^2 to 0.51 c^2. They varied the rotational frequency (0, 400, 800, and 1200 Hz) and the central magnetic field strength (from 0 to approximately 5 x 10^17 G) to find the maximum gravitational mass and deformation in each setup.

## Key numbers

- Maximum gravitational mass of non-magnetized non-rotating SQS: 2.35 solar masses
- Maximum gravitational mass of non-magnetized SQS at 1200 Hz: 2.73 solar masses
- Maximum gravitational mass of magnetized SQS at 1200 Hz with central magnetic field approximately 4.51 x 10^17 G: 2.80 solar masses
- Maximum deformation parameter a = R_eq/R_pol = 1.55 for SQS with B_c approximately 5 x 10^17 G and f = 1200 Hz
- Fitting coefficient for mass increase with magnetic field: b = 4.31 x 10^-38 G^-2
- Fitting coefficient for mass increase with rotation: c = 8.57 x 10^-8 s^2
- Fitting coefficient for deformation with magnetic field: tilde b = 1.54 x 10^-37 G^-2
- Fitting coefficient for deformation with rotation: tilde c = 2.39 x 10^-7 s^2
- Maximum gravitational mass at 400 Hz with B_c approximately 5.15 x 10^17 G: 2.40 solar masses
- Maximum gravitational mass at 800 Hz with B_c approximately 5.11 x 10^17 G: 2.51 solar masses

## Context

This work extends studies of magnetized strange quark stars by Chatterjee (2015) and rotating strange stars by Gondek-Rosinska (2000), incorporating both strong magnetic fields and rapid rotation simultaneously. The results show that the maximum gravitational masses (up to 2.80 solar masses) can explain the mass of the black widow pulsar PSR J0952-0607 (approximately 2.35 solar masses) and the lower mass object in GW190814 (2.5 to 2.67 solar masses) detected by LIGO/Virgo. The fitted functions for mass and deformation as functions of magnetic field and rotation enable future comparisons with observational data from pulsar timing and gravitational wave detections to constrain the equation of state of strange quark matter.
