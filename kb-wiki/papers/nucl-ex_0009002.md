# Stability and instability of a hot and dilute nuclear droplet

> The diabatic approach to collective nuclear motion is reformulated in the local-density approximation in order to treat the normal modes of a spherical nuclear droplet analytically. In a first application the adiabatic isoscalar modes are studied and results for the eigenvalues of compressional (bulk) and pure surface modes are presented as function of density and temperature inside the droplet, as well as for different mass numbers and for soft and stiff equations of state. We find that the region of bulk instabilities (spinodal regime) is substantially smaller for nuclear droplets than for infinite nuclear matter. For small densities below 30% of normal nuclear matter density and for temperatures below 5 MeV all relevant bulk modes become unstable with the same growth rates. The surface modes have a larger spinodal region, reaching out to densities and temperatures way beyond the spinodal line for bulk instabilities. Essential experimental features of multifragmentation, like fragmentation temperatures and fragment-mass distributions (in particular the power-law behavior) are consistent with the instability properties of an expanding nuclear droplet, and hence with a dynamical fragmentation process within the spinodal regime of bulk and surface modes (spinodal decomposition).

## Key claim

The spinodal region for bulk instabilities is substantially smaller for finite nuclear droplets than for infinite nuclear matter, with the critical temperature reduced to 6 MeV for a soft equation of state, and the observed fragmentation temperatures of about 5 MeV are consistent with spinodal decomposition after expansion rather than with the critical point of the liquid-gas phase transition.

## Method

The authors reformulated the diabatic approach to collective nuclear motion in the local-density approximation to treat normal modes of a spherical nuclear droplet analytically, using an expansion of the displacement potential in terms of multipoles with a complete orthonormal basis set. They calculated collective mass and stiffness tensors within a consistent harmonic approximation, then solved the eigenvalue equation for adiabatic isoscalar modes (τ=0 limit) to obtain frequencies and growth rates as functions of density, temperature, mass number, and equation of state (soft SkM* and stiff SIII Skyrme forces).

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature for bulk instabilities: T_crit = 6 MeV for soft equation of state (SkM*)
- Critical temperature for bulk instabilities: T_crit = 8 MeV for stiff equation of state (SIII)
- Typical growth rates for bulk instabilities: γ ≈ 5 MeV, corresponding to growth times ℏ/γ ≈ 40 fm/c
- Growth rates for stiff EOS are larger by almost a factor of 2 compared to soft EOS
- Below 30% of normal nuclear matter density (ϱ_0 = 0.16 fm⁻³) and temperatures below 5 MeV, all relevant bulk modes become unstable with the same growth rates
- Surface tension parameters: a_s = 17 MeV, r_0 = 1.2 fm, β = 0.006 MeV⁻² (soft EOS) or 0.008 MeV⁻² (stiff EOS)
- Equilibrium nuclear density of homogeneous droplet: ϱ_eq ≈ 0.85 ϱ_0

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of spinodal instabilities in infinite nuclear matter by Heiselberg, Pethick and Ravenhall (1990s) and finite droplets by Kolomietz and Shlomo (1990s) and others, providing a more systematic analysis of eigenmodes as functions of densities and temperatures relevant to multifragmentation. The results enable a dynamical interpretation of multifragmentation as spinodal decomposition within the spinodal regime of bulk and surface modes, consistent with experimental features like fragmentation temperatures and power-law fragment-mass distributions (σ ≈ 2.0) observed by the ALADIN collaboration, and suggest a novel mechanism for fission at high excitation energies through a low-density stage where the fission barrier vanishes.
