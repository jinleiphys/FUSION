# Abrupt Change in Radiation-Width Distribution for 147Sm Neutron Resonances

> We obtained total radiation widths of s-wave resonances through R-matrix analysis of 147Sm(n,gamma) cross-sections. Distributions of these widths differ markedly for resonances below and above 300 eV, in stark contrast to long-established theory. We show that this change, as well as a similar change in the neutron-width distribution reported previously, are reflected in abrupt increases in both the average 147Sm(n,gamma) cross section and fluctuations about the average near 300 eV. Such effects could have important consequences for applications such as nuclear astrophysics and nuclear criticality safety.

# Abrupt Change in Radiation-Width Distribution for 147Sm Neutron Resonances

## Key claim

Total radiation widths extracted from R-matrix analysis of 147Sm(n,gamma) cross sections show an abrupt change in both the shape and average value of the distribution near En = 300 eV, in stark contrast to the theoretical expectation that these quantities should remain essentially constant across the resonance range and beyond. The effect is established with very high statistical confidence (greater than 99.9% by some tests), and occurs at the same energy as previously reported abrupt changes in the alpha-particle strength-function ratio and the reduced-neutron-width distribution, making it virtually certain that a significant departure from standard theory has been observed.

## Method

The authors measured 147Sm(n,gamma) cross sections using the Detector for Advanced Neutron Capture Experiments (DANCE), a 4pi gamma-ray detector comprising 160 BaF2 scintillators at the Los Alamos Neutron Science Center, with a 10.4-mg metallic sample enriched to 97.93% in 147Sm. Spin-separated yields for the two s-wave spin states (J=3 and J=4) were determined using gamma-ray multiplicity information, and the R-matrix code SAMMY was used to fit the cross-section data and extract capture kernels, from which total radiation widths were derived using previously known neutron widths from Mizon (1981). The analysis was limited to 62 resonances (out of 112 observed below 700 eV) meeting the criterion Gamma_n >= Gamma_gamma/2 to ensure reliable width extraction.

## Key numbers

- Average radiation width for the lower-energy region (0-300 eV): <Gamma_gamma> = 52.0 +/- 1.1 meV, with distribution degrees of freedom nu_gamma = 241 +/- 42
- Average radiation width for the upper-energy region (300-700 eV): <Gamma_gamma> = 59.6 +/- 2.0 meV, with distribution degrees of freedom nu_gamma = 51.0 +/- 6.5
- Standard deviation of the Gamma_gamma distribution: sigma_N = 4.67 +/- 0.81 meV (lower region) and sigma_N = 11.7 +/- 1.5 meV (upper region)
- Neutron width distribution degrees of freedom: nu_n = 1.04 (+0.33/-0.31) for 0-300 eV and nu_n = 2.67 (+0.60/-0.57) for 300-700 eV
- Average s-wave resonance spacing: D0 = 5.78 +/- 0.44 eV (0-300 eV) and 6.18 +/- 0.40 eV (300-700 eV)
- s-wave neutron strength function: 10^4 S0 = 4.56 +/- 0.94 (0-300 eV) and 4.53 +/- 0.81 (300-700 eV)
- The cross section in the upper-energy region is approximately 30% larger than calculated using parameters for the lower-energy region
- The median test rejects the null hypothesis of equal medians at 99.8% confidence; the variance test rejects equal variances at 99.9% confidence
- The Smirnov and Cramer-von Mises two-sample tests reject the null hypothesis of same population with >99% and >99.9% confidence, respectively

## Context

This work extends previous observations by Koehler (2004) of an abrupt change in the alpha-particle strength-function ratio for 147Sm and by Koehler (2007) of a change in the reduced-neutron-width distribution shape near 300 eV, both of which remained unexplained. The current results establish that three independent deviations from standard nuclear statistical model expectations occur in the same nuclide at the same energy, with the radiation-width data providing a particularly clean test because Gamma_gamma distributions are expected to be very narrow (with approximately 100 degrees of freedom) and should remain constant across the resonance region according to Porter-Thomas theory (Porter 1956). The findings challenge the standard assumption that neutron widths follow a Porter-Thomas distribution and that photon strength functions are smooth functions of energy, with potential consequences for nuclear astrophysics and nuclear criticality safety calculations that rely on statistical model predictions.
