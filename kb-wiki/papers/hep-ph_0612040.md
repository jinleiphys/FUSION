# Parity violation and the nature of charges

> The origin of parity violation in physics is still unknown. At the present time, it is introduced in the theory by requiring that the SU(2) subgroup entering the description of interactions involves the left components. In the present contribution, one elaborates upon a suggestion made by Landau that particles and antiparticles could be like "stereo-isomeric" molecules, which would naturally provides parity violation. Particles and antiparticles could thus be combinations of the parity doublets associated with a chiral symmetry realized in the Wigner-Weyl mode. Consequences of such a description and the possible problems it could raise are examined.

## Key claim

Parity violation may not be a fundamental property of interactions but rather an apparent effect arising because particles and antiparticles are not eigenstates of parity, being instead combinations of parity doublets from a chiral symmetry realized in the Wigner-Weyl mode.

## Method

The paper elaborates on Landau’s suggestion that particles and antiparticles could be analogous to stereo-isomeric molecules, and develops a framework where they are associated with parity doublets of a chiral symmetry. A toy model inspired by the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model is constructed, replacing spin projection with helicity, to examine how parity violation and charge conjugation emerge from an internal chiral substructure.

## Key numbers

- The toy model Lagrangian density includes a mass term \(m \; \bar{\psi}(x) \gamma . \epsilon \; \gamma_5 \psi(x)\) where \(\epsilon^\mu\) (with \(\epsilon^2 = -1\)) represents the polarization of the underlying chiral structure.
- Solutions to the toy model equation require the condition \(\epsilon \cdot p = 0\), corresponding to decoupling between the spin of the chiral structure and the momentum of the system.
- Matrix elements of the axial current for a particle yield \(\bar{u}(p)\gamma_\mu u(p)\) and for its antiparticle yield \(-\bar{v}(p)\gamma_\mu v(p)\).

## Context

This work extends Landau’s (1957) suggestion that particles and antiparticles differ like stereo-isomeric molecules, and contrasts with the standard model’s approach of assigning parity violation to the \(SU(2)_L\) subgroup. It also contrasts with left-right symmetric models (e.g., Pati and Salam, 1974) that restore parity at high energies via an \(SU(2)_R\) subgroup. The paper enables a rethinking of whether parity symmetry needs restoration, and suggests that the difference between vector and axial currents could vanish at some point.
