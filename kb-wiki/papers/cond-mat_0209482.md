# On the Microscopic Foundation of Thermo-Statistics

> The most complicated phenomena of equilibrium statistics, phase separations and transitions of various order and critical phenomena, can clearly and sharply be seen even for small systems in the topology of the curvature of the microcanonical entropy $S_{B}(E,N)=\ln[W(E,N)]$ as function of the conserved energy, particle number etc.. Also the equilibrium of the largest possible interacting many-body systems like self-gravitating systems is described by the topology of the entropy surface $S_{B}(E,N,L)$ where $L$ is the angular momentum. Conventional (canonical) statistical mechanics describes phase transitions only in the ``thermodynamic limit''(homogeneous phases of ``infinite'' systems interacting with short-range interactions). In this paper I present two examples of phase transitions of first order: the liquid to gas transition in a small atomic cluster and the condensation of a rotating self-gravitating system into single stars or into multi-star systems like double stars and rings. Such systems cannot be addressed by ordinary canonical thermo-statistics. I also give a geometric illustration how an initially non-equilibrized ensemble approaches the microcanonical equilibrium distribution.

## Key claim

Phase transitions of first order, including liquid-gas transitions in small atomic clusters and condensation of rotating self-gravitating systems, can be sharply identified through the topology of the microcanonical entropy curvature even for finite systems, contrary to the canonical ensemble which requires the thermodynamic limit for such phenomena.

## Method

The author uses Boltzmann's microcanonical entropy \(S_B(E,N) = \ln[W(E,N)]\) based on the uniform filling of the energy shell in phase space, and analyzes the curvature of the entropy surface via the Hessian matrix of second derivatives with respect to energy and particle number. Phase transitions of first order are identified by a positive maximal eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\) of the Hessian, corresponding to a convex intruder in the entropy and a negative heat capacity. Two model systems are studied: sodium clusters of 200, 1000, and 3000 atoms at constant pressure of 1 atm, and rotating self-gravitating N-body systems with conserved angular momentum.

## Key numbers

- For Na clusters at 1 atm external pressure: transition temperatures \(T_{tr}\) are 940 K (N=200), 990 K (N=1000), 1095 K (N=3000), compared to bulk value 1156 K.
- Latent heat per atom \(q_{lat}\): 0.82 eV (N=200), 0.91 eV (N=1000), 0.94 eV (N=3000), bulk 0.923 eV.
- Boiling entropy \(s_{boil}\): 10.1 (N=200), 10.7 (N=1000), 9.9 (N=3000), bulk 9.267.
- Surface entropy \(\Delta s_{surf}\): 0.55 (N=200), 0.56 (N=1000), 0.45 (N=3000).
- Surface tension divided by transition temperature \(\sigma/T_{tr}\): 2.75 (N=200), 5.68 (N=1000), 7.07 (N=3000), bulk 7.41.
- Number of surface atoms \(N_{surf}\): 39.94 (N=200), 98.53 (N=1000), 186.6 (N=3000).

## Context

This work extends the microcanonical geometric formulation of statistical mechanics developed by Gross and collaborators (Gross 1995, 2001) to non-extensive systems including self-gravitating systems, which cannot be treated by canonical ensembles as argued by Thirring (1970) and Lynden-Bell (1999). It contradicts the conventional Yang-Lee theory (Yang and Lee 1952) which requires the thermodynamic limit for phase transitions, and provides a framework for understanding negative heat capacities observed experimentally in nuclear fragmentation (Chbihi 1995, d'Agostino 2000) and atomic clusters (Schmidt 2000, 2001). The approach enables classification of phase transitions in small systems and opens the study of inhomogeneous phase separation in astrophysical contexts.
