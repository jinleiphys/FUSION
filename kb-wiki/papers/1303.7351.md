# Comparing and contrasting nuclei and cold atomic gases

> The experimental revolution in ultracold atomic gas physics over the past decades have brought tremendous amounts of new insight to the world of degenerate quantum systems. Here we compare and constrast the developments of cold atomic gases with the physics of nuclei since many concepts, techniques, and nomenclatures are common to both fields. However, nuclei are finite systems with interactions that are typically much more complicated than those of ultracold atomic gases. The simularities and differences must therefore be carefully addressed for a meaningful comparison and to facilitate fruitful crossdisciplinary activity. Universal results from atomic physics should have impact in certain limits of the nuclear domain. In particular, with advances in the trapping of few-body atomic systems we expect a more direct exchange of ideas and results.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while many-body concepts such as BEC and BCS states are applicable in both nuclear physics and ultracold atomic gases, the interactions and finite particle numbers in nuclei can obscure the clear meaning these concepts have in cold atoms; however, universal results from atomic physics should have impact in certain limits of the nuclear domain, particularly with advances in trapping few-body atomic systems.

## Method

The authors present a comparative review of condensates, paired states, and few-body physics across nuclear and cold atomic gas systems, using mean-field theory, BCS pairing theory, and the one-body and two-body density matrix formalism as unifying frameworks. They analyze relevant dimensionless parameters such as density times scattering length cubed (na³) and compare length and energy scales, degrees of freedom, and interactions between the two fields. Specific case studies include bosonic alpha-particle condensates in nuclei, Efimov physics, and BCS-BEC crossover in neutron-rich matter and light halo nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The condensate fraction c_f for a Gaussian mean-field wave function with center-of-mass separation decreases from 1 for N=2 toward about 0.57 for large N.
- For attractive boson interactions (a<0), quasi-stable condensate solutions exist if N|a|/b < 0.65, where b is the trap length of the external harmonic potential.
- The dimensionless quantity na³ relates to the classical mean free path λ by na³ ~ a/(4πλ); mean-field structure occurs when λ ≫ L₀ (na³ ≪ 1) and strong correlations when λ ≃ L₀ (na³ ≃ 0.1–10 for atoms where a/L₀ ∼ 10–100, and na³ ∼ 1 for nuclei away from resonance).
- For atomic ⁴He, only about 10% of a given sample will be in the condensed state due to strong correlations.

## Context

This review extends the long-standing cross-disciplinary exchange between nuclear and atomic physics, building on foundational work in BEC theory (London 1938, Landau 1941), BCS theory (Cooper 1956, Bardeen Cooper and Schrieffer 1957), and the definition of off-diagonal long-range order (Yang 1962). The paper specifically addresses how concepts from infinite systems, such as condensation and superfluidity, transfer to finite systems like nuclei and trapped atomic gases with very few atoms, and it discusses Efimov physics as a prime example of nuclear physics concepts successfully transferred to cold atoms. The work enables more direct exchange of ideas between the two fields, particularly as experimental techniques advance for trapping few-body atomic systems.
