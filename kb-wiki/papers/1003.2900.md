# True ternary fission of superheavy nuclei

> We found that a true ternary fission with formation of a heavy third fragment (a new type of radioactivity) is quite possible for superheavy nuclei due to the strong shell effects leading to a three-body clusterization with the two doubly magic tin-like cores. The simplest way to discover this phenomenon in the decay of excited superheavy nuclei is a detection of two tin-like clusters with appropriate kinematics in low-energy collisions of medium mass nuclei with actinide targets. The three-body quasi-fission process could be even more pronounced for giant nuclear systems formed in collisions of heavy actinide nuclei. In this case a three-body clusterization might be proved experimentally by detection of two coincident lead-like fragments in low-energy U+U collisions.

## Key claim

True ternary fission (simultaneous decay into three fragments of comparable mass) is predicted to be possible for superheavy nuclei due to strong shell effects, but only under the condition that the third fragment is heavy (mass number around 30) and the two heavy fragments are doubly magic tin-like cores; the phenomenon has not yet been observed experimentally.

## Method

The authors used the two-center shell model (TCSM) to calculate the multi-dimensional adiabatic potential energy surface for the superheavy nucleus ²⁹⁶116, extending the model to three-core configurations by introducing a third fragment between the two heavy cores. They restricted the calculation to symmetric ternary fission with two equal cores and equal dynamic deformations of all fragments, using the shape parametrization of Wu, Maruhn, and Greiner (1984) for axially symmetric ternary fission. The potential energy was computed as a function of elongation, mass of the third fragment, and dynamic deformation, with shell corrections obtained via the Strutinsky procedure.

## Key numbers

- For ²⁹⁶116, the ternary fission valley corresponds to a third fragment of mass A₃ ≈ 30 and two heavy fragments of A₁ = A₂ ≈ 130 (the "tin–sulfur–tin" combination).
- For ²⁴⁸Cm, the macroscopic (LDM) potential energy shows binary fission dominates; the shell correction deepens the ground state by about 3 MeV but does not change the potential energy distinctively.
- For ²⁹⁶116, the ternary fission barrier height is "quite comparable" with the regular fission barrier, because ternary fission starts from the shape isomeric state located outside the first (highest) saddle point.

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical studies of ternary fission using the liquid drop model (Swiatecki, 1958) and the three-center shell model (Degheidy and Maruhn, 1979), which showed that shell effects could reduce ternary fission barriers. It also builds on the two-center shell model calculations of Zagrebaev, Karpov, Aritomo, Naumenko, and Greiner (2007). The authors propose that true ternary fission could be observed experimentally in low-energy collisions of medium-mass nuclei with actinide targets (e.g., ⁶⁴Ni + ²³⁸U) or in spontaneous fission of superheavy nuclei (Oganessian, 2007), and that three-body quasi-fission might be detected in low-energy U+U collisions via coincident lead-like fragments.
