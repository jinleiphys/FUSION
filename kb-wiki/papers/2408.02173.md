# A critical assessment of the current implementations of the Generator Coordinate Method

> The generator coordinate method (GCM) was introduced in nuclear physics by Wheeler and independently by Peierls and their collaborators in 1950's and it is still one of the mostly used approximations for treating nuclear large amplitude collective motion (LACM). GCM was inspired by similar methods introduced in molecular and condensed matter physics in the late 1920's, after the Schrödinger equation became the tool of choice to describe quantum phenomena. The interest in the 1983 extension of GCM suggested by Reinhard, Cusson and Goeke, which includes the internal excitations (absent in the initial GCM formulation), was revived in recent years. Unfortunately this newer version of time-dependent GCM (TDGCM) framework has flaws, which prevents it from describing correctly many anticipated features, in particular interference and entanglement, which can play an important role in fission and many-nucleon transfer reactions. I present here an alternative formulation, the enhanced GCM (eGCM), which is free of difficulties encountered in previous GCM implementations and which is relevant for fission and many-nucleon transfer in heavy-ion reactions, and which can be used in either static or time-dependent eGCM formulations. In the eGCM framework the corresponding many-body waves functions have a much more complex structure and this framework is equivalent to a configuration interaction (CI) approach in the continuum for nuclear reactions. eGCM is aimed to be used in the microscopic description of heavy-ion reactions and fission in particular.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the time-dependent Generator Coordinate Method (TDGCM) extension proposed by Reinhard, Cusson and Goeke (1983) has a fundamental flaw: it evaluates norm and Hamiltonian overlaps only at equal times, which causes vanishing overlaps between different fission trajectories and prevents correct description of interference and entanglement. The author proposes an enhanced GCM (eGCM) that includes overlaps at different times, analogous to a Feynman path integral.

## Method

The author formulates eGCM by replacing the equal-time norm and Hamiltonian overlaps of standard TDGCM with generalized overlaps evaluated at different times along different trajectories, solving an extended Hill-Wheeler equation. The many-body wave functions are constructed as superpositions of time-dependent Slater determinants generated by time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) trajectories, each labeled by initial shape parameters (quadrupole Q20 and octupole Q30 deformations) and a running time variable. The method uses canonical wave functions to reduce the number of required quasiparticle states from hundreds of thousands to a few hundred, and proposes using the icosahedral group with 60 angles for angular momentum projection.

## Key numbers

- For a typical fission simulation box of 32^2 x 64 fm^3 with lattice constant l=1 fm, the maximum momentum cutoff is p_max = hbar * pi / l ≈ 600 MeV/c
- The number of quadrupole and octupole deformations needed along the rim of the outer fission barrier is N_Q ≤ 15
- For fragment separation s from approximately 7 fm to 22 fm with Δs = 0.25 fm, N_s = 60 different fragment separations are needed per trajectory
- The number of canonical wave functions needed is N_cwfs < 200-300 for protons and neutrons respectively
- The estimated dimension of norm overlap matrices with angular momentum projection is N_eGCM = 60 angles x 200 cwfs x 60 separations x 15 shapes = 7,200,000
- Without angular momentum projection, the dimension is N_eGCM = 200 x 60 x 15 = 180,000
- Differences between number-projected and number-unprojected densities in induced fission TDDFT are at most 1% during the entire time-evolution

## Context

This work extends the original GCM introduced by Wheeler and collaborators (Hill 1953, Griffin 1957) and the time-dependent extension by Reinhard, Cusson and Goeke (1983), which has been recently implemented by Regnier (2019), Hasegawa (2020), Marevic (2023, 2024), and Li (2023, 2024). The eGCM framework is designed to describe interference and entanglement in fission and heavy-ion reactions, addressing the failure of previous implementations to produce expected quantum mixing between different trajectories. The approach connects to the Feynman-Vernon influence functional formalism and the work of Peierls (1957, 1962) on symmetry restoration, and is enabled by recent TDDFT simulations of fission by Bulgac (2016, 2019, 2020) that demonstrated dissipative non-equilibrium dynamics beyond the adiabatic approximation.
