# Dihadron Correlation in Jets Produced in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The difference between the structures of jets produced in heavy-ion and hadronic collisions can best be exhibited in the correlations between particles within those jets. We study the dihadron correlations in jets in the framework of parton recombination. Two types of triggers, $π^+$ and proton, are considered. It is shown that the recombination of thermal and shower partons makes the most important contribution to the spectra of the associated particles at intermediate $p_T$. In $pp$ collisions the only significant contribution arises from shower-shower recombination, which is negligible in heavy-ion collisions. Moments of the associated-particle distributions are calculated to provide simple summary of the jet structures for easy comparison with experiments.

# Dihadron Correlation in Jets Produced in Heavy-Ion Collisions

## Key claim

The structure of jets produced in heavy-ion collisions is drastically different from that in proton-proton collisions, specifically because the recombination of thermal partons with shower partons dominates the associated-particle spectra at intermediate transverse momentum (p_T), whereas in pp collisions only shower-shower recombination contributes significantly. This difference is shown to be independent of the degree of jet quenching (energy loss), since the suppression factor ξ cancels in the ratio defining the associated particle distribution.

## Method

The authors calculate dihadron correlations within a single jet using the parton recombination framework, where hadrons are formed by combining thermal partons (parameterized by an exponential distribution fitted to low-p_T data) with shower partons (determined from fragmentation functions via the recombination formula). Two-particle distributions for π⁺π⁺, π⁺π⁻, and π⁺p (and their permutations with proton triggers) are computed by considering four or five partons that recombine, with the joint parton distributions F₄ and F₅ built from combinations of thermal (T) and shower (S) partons, omitting TT contributions as thermal background. The associated particle distribution for a given trigger is obtained by taking the ratio of the two-particle distribution to the single-particle trigger distribution, integrated over the trigger momentum range 4-6 GeV/c.

## Key numbers

- Suppression factor ξ = 0.07 for central Au+Au collisions at √s = 200 GeV, due to energy loss in the dense medium
- Thermal parton distribution parameters: C = 23.2 GeV⁻¹, T = 0.317 GeV, for central Au+Au at midrapidity and √s = 200 GeV
- For π⁺ trigger in central Au+Au, the moments of the associated particle distribution (integrated from 0.5 to 4.5 GeV/c) are: M₀ = 1.394 (average number), M₁ = 1.707 (total scalar p_T), M₂ = 2.703 (total p_T²)
- For proton trigger in central Au+Au, the moments are: M₀ = 0.882, M₁ = 0.999, M₂ = 1.450
- For π⁺π⁺ correlation only, the moments in central Au+Au are: M₀ = 0.428, M₁ = 0.498, M₂ = 0.754; in central d+Au: M₀ = 0.200, M₁ = 0.223, M₂ = 0.331; in peripheral d+Au: M₀ = 0.089, M₁ = 0.101, M₂ = 0.153
- Ratio of central Au+Au to peripheral d+Au moments for π⁺π⁺ is approximately 4.8-4.9 for n = 0, 1, 2
- The ratio of central Au+Au to peripheral d+Au spectra exceeds 5 around p_T = 2 GeV/c and remains large throughout the intermediate p_T range

## Context

This work extends the parton recombination framework previously developed by Hwa and Yang (2003-2004) for single-particle spectra, which explained features where fragmentation models failed, including the species dependence of the Cronin effect in d+Au collisions. The study directly addresses the experimental observation from RHIC (STAR collaboration, 2003) that jet structures in heavy-ion collisions differ from those in pp collisions, contrary to what a simple fragmentation model with surface emission would predict. The results enable future experimental comparisons once particle identification improves, and the authors argue that the recombination approach is more physically cogent than medium-modified fragmentation functions, which would lose the advantage of universality. The formalism uses shower parton distributions (SPDs) determined at fixed Q = 10 GeV/c, with the authors noting that Q² evolution of SPDs remains an open problem.
