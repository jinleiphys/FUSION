# Two-fluid model for a rotating trapped Fermi gas in the BCS phase

> We investigate the dynamical properties of a superfluid gas of trapped fermionic atoms in the BCS phase. As a simple example we consider the reaction of the gas to a slow rotation of the trap. It is shown that the currents generated by the rotation can be understood within a two-fluid model similar to the one used in the theory of superconductors, but with a position dependent ratio of normal and superfluid densities. The rather general result of this paper is that already at very low temperatures, far below the critical one, an important normal-fluid component appears in the outer regions of the gas. This renders the experimental observation of superfluidity effects more difficult and indicates that reliable theoretical predictions concerning other dynamical properties, like the frequencies of collective modes, can only be made by taking into account temperature effects.

## Key claim

The paper shows that in a trapped BCS-phase Fermi gas under slow rotation, a significant normal-fluid component appears in the outer regions of the gas even at temperatures far below the critical temperature, which makes experimental detection of superfluidity more difficult and implies that temperature effects must be included in theoretical predictions for collective modes.

## Method

The author applies a two-fluid model, analogous to that used in superconductivity, to a trapped fermionic gas in the BCS phase under slow rotation. The Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) equations are solved in the semiclassical limit (Wigner-Kirkwood expansion, linear order in ℏ) using the Thomas-Fermi (local density) approximation, allowing the gap Δ₀(r) and densities to vary spatially. The current density is decomposed into normal and superfluid components, and the phase φ(r) of the superfluid velocity is determined from the continuity equation.

## Key numbers

- Temperature of 0.03 T_F (where T_F = k_B ε_F) has been reached experimentally in trapped ⁶Li gases, low enough to realize the BCS phase.
- The local critical temperature is defined as T_c(r) = 0.57 Δ₀(r; T=0).
- At T = 2 ℏω̄ ≈ 0.36 T_c (with T_c ≈ 5.5 ℏω̄), a normal fluid component appears near the surface of the trap.
- At T = 4 ℏω̄ ≈ 0.72 T_c, the normal-fluid component starts to extend over the whole volume.
- For a trap with ω_x/ω_y = 0.7, the zero-temperature moment of inertia is Θ(T=0) ≈ 0.12 Θ_rigid.
- The calculation uses N = 36000 atoms with interaction strength g = -ℏ² l_ho/m, giving Δ ≈ 0.2 ε_F at the trap center at zero temperature.

## Context

This work extends the earlier calculation by Urban and Schuck (2004) which used a constant gap Δ and neglected spatial dependence of normal and superfluid fractions. The paper contradicts the assumption that zero-temperature theoretical predictions can be directly compared with experimental measurements of collective modes, as done by Bartenstein (2004) and Kinast (2004). The results enable predictions of temperature-dependent effects in other observables sensitive to the surface, such as the twist mode response studied by Grasso, Urban, and Vinas (2004), and motivate generalization of the two-fluid model to the dynamic case via time-dependent HFB equations.
