# Validating Simple Dynamical Simulations of the Unitary Fermi Gas

> We present a comparison between simulated dynamics of the unitary fermion gas using the superfluid local density approximation (SLDA) and a simplified bosonic model, the extended Thomas Fermi (ETF) with a unitary equation of state. Small amplitude fluctuations have similar dynamics in both theories for frequencies far below the pair breaking threshold and wave vectors much smaller than the Fermi momentum, and the low frequency linear responses match well for surprisingly large wave vectors, even up to the Fermi momentum. For non-linear dynamics such as vortex generation, the ETF provides a semi-quantitative description of SLDA dynamics as long as the fluctuations do not have significant power near the pair breaking threshold, otherwise the dynamics of the ETF cannot be trusted. Nonlinearities in the ETF tends to generate high-frequency fluctuations, and with no normal component to remove this energy from the superfluid, features like vortex lattices cannot relax and crystallize as they do in the SLDA. We present a heuristic diagnostic for validating the reliability of ETF dynamics by considering the approximate conservation of square of the gap: $\int|Δ|^2$.

# Validating Simple Dynamical Simulations of the Unitary Fermi Gas

## Key claim
The extended Thomas Fermi (ETF) bosonic model provides a semi-quantitative description of superfluid local density approximation (SLDA) dynamics for the unitary Fermi gas only when fluctuations do not have significant power near the pair-breaking threshold; otherwise the ETF dynamics cannot be trusted, and nonlinearities in the ETF tend to generate high-frequency fluctuations that prevent vortex lattices from relaxing and crystallizing as they do in the SLDA.

## Method
The authors compared time-dependent simulations of the unitary Fermi gas using two approaches: the superfluid local density approximation (SLDA), a fermionic density functional theory that requires evolving thousands of single-particle wavefunctions, and the extended Thomas Fermi (ETF) model, a bosonic theory describing Cooper pairs with a single collective condensate wavefunction. They computed and compared static density responses, dynamic linear responses at finite wavevector and frequency, and nonlinear dynamics from stirring simulations that generate vortices, using the Bertsch parameter ξ=0.42 to match the SLDA simulations of Bulgac (2011).

## Key numbers
- The Bertsch parameter ξ=0.3742(5) is the current best fit value obtained by consistently fitting both quantum Monte Carlo and experimental results, used as a reference value
- The SLDA simulations used a 32×32×192 lattice with ~500 particles represented by 70000 wavefunctions, requiring 200 GB for a single step
- The ETF model with ξ=0.42 matches the SLDA static response well even for wavevectors up to q ~ k_F, where k_F is the Fermi momentum
- The phonon dispersion in the ETF gives ω_phonon = c_s q + O(q^3) with sound speed c_s = √(ξ/3) v_F, where v_F = ħk_F/m is the Fermi velocity
- For the SLDA with α=1, the coefficient c_SR = 7/3 independent of β and γ, while c_ω changes from -0.255 to 0.055 as ξ is reduced from 0.42 to 0.37
- The ETF gives c_ω = c_SR = 9/4 for the low-energy effective field theory coefficients
- Using ξ=0.374, the phonon dispersion correction is ω_q ∝ 1 + 0.11 c_ω q²/k_F² and the static response correction is χ(q) ∝ 1 - 0.22 c_SR q²/k_F²

## Context
This work validates the ETF model as a computationally efficient alternative to the full time-dependent SLDA (TDSLDA) simulations of Bulgac (2011), which require supercomputing resources even for modest volumes. The ETF model has been used previously by Kim (2004), Salasnich (2008), and others to analyze expansion modes, breathing frequencies, surface oscillations, cloud collisions, vortex generation, and soliton dynamics in cold atomic gases. The validation played a crucial role in solving the mystery of "heavy solitons" observed by Yefsah (2013), where the ETF was used to demonstrate consistency with vortex rings instead of solitons (Bulgac, 2013). The authors suggest the conservation of ∫|Δ|² as a heuristic diagnostic for validating ETF reliability, and note that the model could be improved by adding a normal component to handle pair-breaking effects.
