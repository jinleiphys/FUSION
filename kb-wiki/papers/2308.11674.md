# Measures of azimuthal anisotropy in high-energy collisions

> Azimuthal anisotropy is a key observation made in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. This phenomenon has played a crucial role in the development of the field over the last two decades. In addition to its interest for studying the quark-gluon plasma, which was the original motivation, it is sensitive to the properties of incoming nuclei, in particular to the nuclear deformation and to the nuclear skin. The azimuthal anisotropy is therefore of crucial importance when relating low-energy nuclear structure to high-energy nuclear collisions. This article is an elementary introduction to the various observables used in order to characterize azimuthal anisotropy, which go under the names of $v_2\{2\}$, $v_3\{2\}$, $v_2\{4\}$, etc. The intended audience is primarily physicists working in the field of nuclear structure.

## Key claim

Azimuthal anisotropy observables in high-energy heavy-ion collisions, originally developed to study the quark-gluon plasma, are also sensitive to the properties of incoming nuclei, specifically nuclear deformation and nuclear skin, thereby providing a crucial link between low-energy nuclear structure and high-energy nuclear collisions. The article is an elementary introduction intended primarily for nuclear structure physicists, and the observables discussed (v₂{2}, v₃{2}, v₂{4}, etc.) are presented with the caveat that they rely on the "flow paradigm" assumption that particles are emitted independently in the intrinsic frame.

## Method

The paper reviews the measurement of azimuthal anisotropy via Fourier coefficients vₙ, defined from the expansion of the azimuthal distribution of outgoing particles. The primary method discussed is two-particle correlations, where the correlator c₂{2} ≡ ⟨e^{2i(φ₁−φ₂)}⟩ is constructed from pairs of particles in the same event, and under the assumption of independent particle emission, v₂{2} is obtained as the square root of this correlator. The article also describes the event-plane method (historically used by STAR) and introduces higher-order cumulants (e.g., v₂{4}) for more precise measurements.

## Key numbers

- Lorentz contraction factor γ ≈ 107 at RHIC and ≈ 2900 at the LHC for Pb+Pb collisions.
- Collision time t_coll ≈ 2 × 10⁻²⁶ s for Pb+Pb at the LHC (R ≈ 7 fm).
- Number of created particles exceeds initial nucleon number by a factor ≈ 20 at top RHIC energy and ≈ 85 at the LHC.
- Root-mean-square error on centrality for central collisions of heavy nuclei is ≈ 2% at top RHIC energy and ≈ 1% at the LHC.
- Quantum uncertainty on impact parameter δb ≈ 4 × 10⁻⁷ fm for Pb+Pb at the LHC.
- Effective temperature T_eff relates to mean transverse momentum by ⟨p_t⟩ ≈ 3 T_eff.
- v₂ in Pb+Pb at the LHC is smaller than the ideal hydrodynamic limit by only ≈ 20%.

## Context

This article extends the line of work connecting nuclear structure to high-energy collisions, following the observation that elliptic flow (v₂) in U+U collisions at RHIC provided the first link between nuclear deformation and heavy-ion observables, as encouraged by Art Poskanzer. It builds on earlier measurements of elliptic flow by STAR (2000) and the discovery of the "ridge" structure in pair correlations by STAR (2004) and CMS (2012). The paper enables nuclear structure physicists to use high-energy collision data to study nuclear deformation and nuclear skin, complementing traditional low-energy nuclear physics approaches.
