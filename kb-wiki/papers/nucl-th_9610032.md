# Superheavy nuclei in selfconsistent nuclear calculations

> The shell structure of superheavy nuclei is investigated within various parametrizations of relativistic and nonrelativistic nuclear mean field models. The heaviest known even-even nucleus 264Hs is used as a benchmark to estimate the predictive value of the models. From that starting point, doubly magic spherical nuclei are searched in the region Z=110-140 and N=134-298. They are found at (Z=114, N=184), (Z=120, N=172), or at (Z=126, N=184), depending on the parametrization.

## Key claim

Depending on the specific mean-field parametrization used, the doubly magic spherical superheavy nucleus is predicted at (Z=114, N=184), (Z=120, N=172), or (Z=126, N=184), but the authors note that the study reveals systematic differences between relativistic and nonrelativistic models that remain unresolved.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent relativistic mean-field (RMF) and nonrelativistic Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) calculations for superheavy nuclei, using a spherical representation with occasional axially deformed counterchecks. They benchmarked the models against the known even-even nucleus 264Hs, then systematically scanned the region Z=110-140 and N=134-298 for spherical doubly magic nuclei, identifying shell closures via peaks in the two-nucleon gaps (second differences of binding energies).

## Key numbers

- The experimental binding energy of 264Hs was used as a benchmark; the relative errors for the Skyrme forces with old standard spin-orbit coupling were about 0.6%, while the isovector-extended spin-orbit forces (SkI3, SkI4) and the RMF forces NL-Z and PL-40 gave better agreement.
- For the doubly magic 208Pb, the proton gap is 8.5 MeV and the neutron gap is 7.8 MeV (computed with SkI1), providing a scale for shell gaps in superheavy nuclei.
- The largest shell gaps in the superheavy region are smaller than in the lead region by about a factor of 2.
- Doubly magic spherical nuclei are predicted at (Z=114, N=184) by SkI4, at (Z=120, N=172) by PL-40 and NL-Z, and at (Z=126, N=184) by SkM* and SkP.

## Context

This work extends earlier macroscopic-microscopic predictions of superheavy nuclei (e.g., Mosel and Greiner, and the work of Patyk and Sobiczewski) by using self-consistent mean-field models that naturally incorporate polarization effects. The study reveals that even among optimized parametrizations (e.g., SkI4 vs. PL-40), conflicting predictions for the doubly magic nucleus remain, highlighting the need for more rigorous testing of nuclear models across the periodic table and for new experimental data on superheavy nuclei to resolve the systematic differences between relativistic and nonrelativistic approaches.
