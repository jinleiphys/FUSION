# The exotic double-charge exchange muon positron conversion in nuclei

> The formalism for the neutrinoless muon positron conversion is investigated in detail and the relevant nuclear matrix elements for light intermediate neutrinos in the case of the experimentally interesting A=27 system are calculated. The nucleus is going to be used as a stopping target in the MECO experiment at Brookhaven, one of the most sensitive probes expected to reach a sensitivity in the branching ratio within the next few years, which will be an improvement of three orders of magnitude. The relevant transition operators are constructed utilizing a variety of mechanisms present in current gauge theories, with emphasis on the intermediate neutrinos, both light and heavy, and heavy SUSY particles. The nuclear wave functions, both for the initial state and all excited final states are obtained in the framework of 1s-0d shell model employing the well-known and tested Wildenthal realistic interaction. In the case of the light intermediate neutrinos the transition rates to all excited final states up to 25 MeV in energy are calculated. We find that the imaginary part of the amplitude is dominant. The total rate is calculated by summing over all these partial transition strengths. We also find that the rate due to the real part of the amplitude is much smaller than the corresponding quantity found previously by the closure approximation.

## Key claim

The paper calculates the nuclear matrix elements for the neutrinoless muon-to-positron conversion in the specific case of a $^{27}$Al target, finding that the imaginary part of the amplitude dominates the total rate and that the real part yields a much smaller rate than previously estimated using the closure approximation.

## Method

The authors constructed nuclear wave functions for both the initial $^{27}$Al ground state and all excited final states of $^{27}$Na up to 25 MeV excitation energy using the 1s-0d shell model with the Wildenthal realistic interaction. They then computed the transition operators for the light intermediate neutrino mechanism, focusing on Fermi and Gamow-Teller type operators, and summed the partial transition strengths to all accessible final states to obtain the total reaction rate.

## Key numbers

- The maximum positron momentum for the $^{27}$Al($\mu^-,e^+$)$^{27}$Na reaction is $(p_e)_{max} = 94.5$ MeV/c.
- The atomic mass difference between initial and final nucleus is $M(^{27}Al)-M(^{27}Na) = -10.6$ MeV.
- The muon binding energy for $^{27}$Al is approximately $\epsilon_b \approx 0.5$ MeV.
- The muon mass is $m_\mu = 105.6$ MeV.
- The existing experimental upper limit for the branching ratio $R_{\mu e^+}$ on $^{48}$Ti is $4.6 \times 10^{-12}$ (TRIUMF) and $4.4 \times 10^{-12}$ (PSI).
- The MECO experiment at Brookhaven is expected to reach a sensitivity of order $10^{-16}$ for the branching ratio.
- The existing upper limit for $\mu^-\to e^-$ conversion on $^{197}$Au is $R_{\mu e^-}^{Au} \leq 5.0 \times 10^{-13}$.
- The existing upper limit for $\mu^-\to e^-$ conversion on $^{48}$Ti is $R_{\mu e^-}^{Ti} \leq 6.1 \times 10^{-13}$.
- The existing upper limit for $\mu^-\to e^-$ conversion on $^{208}$Pb is $R_{\mu e^-}^{Pb} \leq 4.6 \times 10^{-11}$.
- The transition rates were calculated for all excited final states up to 25 MeV in excitation energy.

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical studies of muon-to-positron conversion, which had focused on exclusive ground-state-to-ground-state transitions for $^{40}$Ca and $^{58}$Ni targets (Ericson and collaborators, and Leontaris and Vergados), by performing a full shell-model calculation that includes all excited final states. The calculation is motivated by the upcoming MECO experiment at Brookhaven, which will use a $^{27}$Al target and aims to improve sensitivity by three to four orders of magnitude over existing limits. The results enable comparison between the explicit sum-over-states method and the closure approximation used in earlier work, and provide nuclear matrix elements needed to extract lepton-violating parameters from future experimental data.
