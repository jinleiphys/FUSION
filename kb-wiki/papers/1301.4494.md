# DiracQ: A Quantum Many-Body Physics Package

> We present a software package DiracQ, for use in quantum many-body Physics. It is designed for helping with typical algebraic manipulations that arise in quantum Condensed Matter Physics and Nuclear Physics problems, and also in some subareas of Chemistry. DiracQ is invoked within a Mathematica session, and extends the symbolic capabilities of Mathematica by building in standard commutation and anticommutation rules for several objects relevant in many-body Physics. It enables the user to carry out computations such as evaluating the commutators of arbitrary combinations of spin, Bose and Fermi operators defined on a discrete lattice, or the position and momentum operators in the continuum. Some examples from popular systems, such as the Hubbard model, are provided to illustrate the capabilities of the package.

## Key claim

DiracQ is a software package that extends Mathematica's symbolic capabilities by implementing standard commutation and anticommutation rules for quantum many-body operators, enabling physicists to carry out algebraic manipulations with the same informality they use in hand calculations. The package's fundamental operation is splitting expressions into c-number and q-number parts before applying operator rules, which the authors argue mirrors the physicist's approach of deferring formal definitions until needed.

## Method

The package defines a set of symbols as operators (q-numbers) within a Mathematica session, with built-in properties for Bose, Fermi, position-momentum, and angular momentum operators, while allowing users to define more exotic operators with specific properties. DiracQ separates input expressions into c-number and q-number parts as its first operation, then combines q-numbers using the given commutation or anticommutation rules, and finally writes expressions back in standard input-like notation. The package consists of mutually dependent functions named with a trailing "Q" (e.g., SimplifyQ) that call upon Mathematica's symbolic capabilities for handling non-commuting symbols.

## Key numbers

- The package was developed and tested on Version 8 of Mathematica
- Example includes a 4-site cluster with nearest and next-nearest hopping bonds in the sector of 2 up and 2 down particles for generating numerical Hamiltonian matrices
- The Examplebook includes verification of R. J. Baxter's 1971 proof of integrability of the 8-vertex model, recovering relations between Boltzmann weights for the star triangle relations
- The 1-d Hubbard model S matrix (Shastry 1986) is used to construct an inhomogeneous generalization, with a complex relation verified using DiracQ

## Context

This work extends the tradition of computer algebra systems for physics by focusing specifically on the informal, notation-driven approach that physicists use in quantum many-body calculations, following the philosophy of Dirac's 1927 distinction between c-numbers and q-numbers. The package enables computations that would be extremely tedious by hand, such as verifying the higher conservation laws of the 1-d Hubbard model that are non-quadratic in Fermi operators, and can also generate numerical Hamiltonian matrices for finite clusters as a starting point for numerical calculations. The authors note that future versions could potentially couple DiracQ with open-source programs such as Sage, given the relatively small fraction of Mathematica commands actually used.
