# Divergence-type 2+1 dissipative hydrodynamics applied to heavy-ion collisions

> We apply divergence-type theory (DTT) dissipative hydrodynamics to study the 2+1 space-time evolution of the fireball created in Au+Au relativistic heavy-ion collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=$200 GeV. DTTs are exact hydrodynamic theories that do no rely on velocity gradient expansions and therefore go beyond second-order theories. We numerically solve the equations of motion of the DTT for Glauber initial conditions and compare the results with those of second-order theory based on conformal invariants (BRSS) and with data. We find that the charged-hadron minumum-bias elliptic flow reaches its maximum value at lower $p_T$ in the DTT, and that the DTT allows for a value of $η/s$ slightly larger than that of the BRSS. Our results show that the differences between viscous hydrodynamic formalisms are a significant source of uncertainty in the precise extraction of $η/s$ from experiments.

# Divergence-type 2+1 dissipative hydrodynamics applied to heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

Divergence-type theory (DTT) dissipative hydrodynamics, which does not rely on velocity gradient expansions and therefore goes beyond second-order theories, produces charged-hadron minimum-bias elliptic flow that reaches its maximum at lower transverse momentum than second-order theory (BRSS), and allows for a viscosity-to-entropy ratio slightly larger than that of BRSS. The differences between viscous hydrodynamic formalisms represent a significant source of uncertainty in the precise extraction of eta/s from experiments.

## Method

The authors numerically solved the 2+1 dimensional boost-invariant equations of motion for both divergence-type theory (DTT) and the Baier-Romatschke-Son-Starinets (BRSS) second-order conformal hydrodynamics, using Glauber initial conditions for Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV. They employed a finite difference method with first-order accuracy in temporal grid spacing and second-order accuracy in spatial grid spacing, using the Laine-Schroder equation of state with an analytic crossover and the Cooper-Frye freeze-out prescription implemented in the UVH2+1 code. The evolution of the shear tensor in DTT is governed by a new tensor xi^{alpha gamma} which is symmetric, traceless, transverse, and vanishes in equilibrium, with the shear tensor Pi^{mu nu} calculated from xi^{alpha gamma} including a quadratic correction term.

## Key numbers

- Initialization time tau_0 = 1 fm/c for all simulations
- Initial temperature T_i = 333 MeV for BRSS with eta/s = 0.08; T_i = 328 MeV for DTT with eta/s = 0.12
- Freeze-out temperature T_f = 140 MeV in both models
- At early times (tau - tau_0 < 1 fm/c), entropy production is the same in both models; at late times DTT produces approximately 40 percent more entropy than BRSS
- In DTT, Grad's ansatz becomes unreliable at p_T approximately 3.3 GeV, compared to p_T = 4 GeV in BRSS
- DTT results match experimental data for eta/s less than or equal to 0.18, with theoretical uncertainty estimated at plus or minus 0.07 and experimental uncertainty at plus or minus 0.1, yielding an upper bound eta/s less than or equal to 0.35
- Woods-Saxon parameters: R_0 = 6.4 fm, chi = 0.54 fm, with nuclear thickness function normalization such that integral of delta_A over d^3 x equals 197 for Au nuclei

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous development of divergence-type theory for conformal fields (Peralta-Ramos and Calzetta, 2009) and applies it to heavy-ion collisions, comparing directly with the BRSS second-order formalism developed by Baier, Romatschke, Son, and Starinets (2008) and with Israel-Stewart theory. The results are compared to simulations by Luzum and Romatschke (2008, 2009) who performed detailed Au+Au simulations using conformal hydrodynamics, and to experimental data from the PHENIX and STAR Collaborations. The work enables assessment of theoretical uncertainties in extracting eta/s from hydrodynamic simulations and suggests that DTT may prove useful in cases with large velocity gradients such as shock-waves, while also indicating that neither DTT nor BRSS can reproduce the experimental saturation of elliptic flow at p_T greater than approximately 2.5 GeV, possibly due to limitations of Grad's ansatz for the nonequilibrium distribution function.
