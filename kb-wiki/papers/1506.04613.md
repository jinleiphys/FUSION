# Dissipative properties of hot and dense hadronic matter in excluded volume hadron resonance gas model

> We estimate dissipative properties viz: shear and bulk viscosities of hadronic matter using relativistic Boltzmann equation in relaxation time approximation within ambit of excluded volume hadron resonance gas (EHRG) model. We find that at zero baryon chemical potential the shear viscosity to entropy ratio ($η/s$) decreases with temperature while at finite baryon chemical potential this ratio shows same behavior as a function of temperature but reaches close to Kovtun-Son-Starinets (KSS) bound. Further along chemical freezout curve, ratio $η/s$ is almost constant apart from small initial monotonic rise. This observation may have some relevance to the experimental finding that the differential elliptic flow of charged hadrons does not change considerably at lower center of mass energy. We further find that bulk viscosity to entropy density ($ζ/s$) decreases with temperature while this ratio has higher value at finite baryon chemical potential at higher temperature. Along freezout curve $ζ/s$ decreases monotonically at lower center of mass energy and then saturates.

## Key claim

The shear viscosity to entropy ratio ($\eta/s$) in the excluded volume hadron resonance gas (EHRG) model decreases with temperature and approaches the Kovtun-Son-Starinets (KSS) bound at finite baryon chemical potential, but the abstract glosses over the fact that at finite chemical potential $\eta/s$ can actually violate the KSS bound due to mixing entropy of the multicomponent hadron gas, making $\eta T/(\epsilon+P)$ the correct measure of fluidity rather than $\eta/s$.

## Method

The authors solved the relativistic Boltzmann equation in the relaxation time approximation for a multicomponent hadron gas described by the thermodynamically consistent excluded volume hadron resonance gas (EHRG) model, assuming uniform hard core radius for all hadrons and using averaged partial relaxation times. They included all hadrons and resonances with a mass cut-off of 2.25 GeV, considered only elastic scattering processes with constant cross sections given by $\sigma = 4\pi r_h^2$, and used Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics. Shear and bulk viscosities were computed from the deviation of the distribution function from equilibrium using standard kinetic theory formulas for multicomponent systems at finite baryon chemical potential.

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity to entropy ratio $\eta/s$ at zero baryon chemical potential decreases with temperature; at finite chemical potential the ratio is smaller than at $\mu = 0$ GeV and approaches the KSS bound of $1/4\pi$
- At low temperature ($\sim 0.120$ GeV) where pions dominate, the $\eta/s$ results match chiral perturbation theory (Fernandez-Fraile and Nicola, 2009); at higher temperatures above 0.120 GeV the results match the scaling hadron masses and couplings (SHMC) model (Khvorostukhin et al.)
- The freeze-out curve is parametrized as $T(\mu) = a - b\mu^2 - c\mu^4$ with $a = 0.166 \pm 0.002$ GeV, $b = 0.139 \pm 0.016$ GeV$^{-1}$, $c = 0.053 \pm 0.021$ GeV$^{-3}$; the baryon chemical potential energy dependence is $\mu = d/(1 + e\sqrt{S})$ with $d = 1.308 \pm 0.028$ GeV and $e = 0.273 \pm 0.008$ GeV$^{-1}$
- Along the chemical freeze-out curve, $\eta/s$ is well above the KSS bound at low center of mass energy and increases monotonically to become constant at higher $\sqrt{S}$; $\zeta/s$ decreases monotonically at lower center of mass energy and then saturates

## Context

This work extends previous calculations of transport coefficients in hadronic matter using the excluded volume hadron resonance gas model within relativistic molecular kinetic theory (Gorenstein et al., 2008) and Chapman-Enskog theory (Jeon et al., 2010), but uses the relaxation time approximation instead. The results are compared with the scaling hadron masses and couplings (SHMC) model (Khvorostukhin et al.) and chiral perturbation theory (Fernandez-Fraile and Nicola, 2009). The finding that $\eta/s$ is nearly constant along the chemical freeze-out curve may explain why the differential elliptic flow of charged hadrons does not change considerably at lower center of mass energy in heavy ion collision experiments. The work enables future studies incorporating temperature- and chemical-potential-dependent hadron masses in the HRG model to improve the realism of transport coefficient calculations.
