# Possible inconsistency between phenomenological and theoretical determinations of charge symmetry breaking in nuclear energy density functionals

> We summarize the recent progress on the determination of the charge symmetry breaking term of nuclear energy density functionals. We point out that the strength of the term determined theoretically is remarkably smaller than that determined phenomenologically, which is still an open question.

# Possible inconsistency between phenomenological and theoretical determinations of charge symmetry breaking in nuclear energy density functionals

## Key claim

The strength of the charge symmetry breaking (CSB) term in nuclear energy density functionals determined theoretically from *ab initio* calculations and QCD sum rules is remarkably smaller than that determined phenomenologically from fits to experimental data, and this discrepancy remains an open question. The authors note that the phenomenological values of the leading-order CSB parameter \(\tilde{s}_0\) range from about -20 to -50 MeV fm³, while theoretical determinations yield values around -5 MeV fm³, a factor of roughly 1/5.

## Method

The authors compare two approaches for determining the parameters of a Skyrme-like zero-range CSB interaction: phenomenological fits to experimental data (including mirror-nuclei mass differences and isobaric analog energies) and theoretical determinations using *ab initio* coupled-cluster (CC) calculations with chiral interactions, Green's function Monte Carlo (GFMC) calculations with the Argonne v18 interaction, and QCD sum rules. For the theoretical determination, the authors exploit the nearly universal linear relationship between the total mass difference of mirror nuclei and the CSB strength parameter \(s_0\) across different Skyrme energy density functionals, allowing extraction of \(s_0\) from *ab initio* results.

## Key numbers

- Phenomenological \(\tilde{s}_0\) for SAMi-ISB: \(-52.6 \pm 1.4\) MeV fm³ (leading-order CSB term only)
- Phenomenological \(\tilde{s}_0\) for SLy4-ISB (leading order): \(-22.4 \pm 4.4\) MeV fm³
- Phenomenological \(\tilde{s}_0\) for SkM*-ISB (leading order): \(-22.4 \pm 5.6\) MeV fm³
- Phenomenological \(\tilde{s}_0\) for SV\(_T\)-ISB (leading order): \(-29.6 \pm 7.6\) MeV fm³
- Phenomenological \(\tilde{s}_0\) for SV\(_T\)-ISB (next-leading order): \(+44 \pm 8\) MeV fm³, with \(\tilde{s}_1 = -56 \pm 16\) MeV fm⁵ and \(\tilde{s}_2 = -31.2 \pm 3.2\) MeV fm⁵
- Theoretical \(\tilde{s}_0\) from N²LO\(_{GO}\) (394) and CC: \(-4.2 \pm 6.5\) MeV fm³ (derived from \(s_0 = -2.1 \pm 3.2\) MeV fm³)
- Theoretical \(\tilde{s}_0\) from N²LO\(_{GO}\) (450) and CC: \(-5.1 \pm 28.5\) MeV fm³ (derived from \(s_0 = -2.5 \pm 14.3\) MeV fm³)
- Theoretical \(\tilde{s}_0\) from AV18-UX and GFMC: \(-6.413 \pm 0.173\) MeV fm³ (derived from \(s_0 = -3.207 \pm 0.086\) MeV fm³)
- Theoretical \(\tilde{s}_0\) from QCD sum rule (Case I and II): \(-15.5^{+8.8}_{-12.5}\) MeV fm³, with \(\tilde{s}_1 = +0.52^{+0.42}_{-0.29}\) MeV fm⁵ (Case I) or \(\tilde{s}_2 = +0.18^{+0.14}_{-0.10}\) MeV fm⁵ (Case II)
- *Ab initio* mass difference \(\Delta E_{\text{tot}}\) for \(^{48}\)Ca and \(^{48}\)Ni without Coulomb: \(0.72 \pm 1.10\) MeV (N²LO\(_{GO}\) (394)) and \(0.86 \pm 4.85\) MeV (N²LO\(_{GO}\) (450))
- CSB contribution to total energy for \(^{10}\)Be: \(-0.0932(6)\) MeV; for \(^{10}\)C: \(0.0918(6)\) MeV (AV18-UX, GFMC)

## Context

This work extends the discussion of isospin symmetry breaking in nuclear interactions, following the original observation by Okamoto (1964) and Nolen and Schiffer (1969) that the Coulomb interaction alone cannot explain mirror-nuclei mass differences. The paper compares phenomenological Skyrme-EDF approaches such as SAMi-ISB by Roca-Maza et al. (2018) and SLy4-ISB, SkM*-ISB, and SV\(_T\)-ISB by Baczyk et al. (2018, 2019) with *ab initio* results from Novario et al. (2023) using coupled-cluster calculations and from Wiringa et al. using GFMC with the AV18 interaction. The inconsistency identified motivates further work to resolve the discrepancy, which is important because CSB contributions can be non-negligible for certain nuclear properties as shown by Naito et al. (2022, 2023).
