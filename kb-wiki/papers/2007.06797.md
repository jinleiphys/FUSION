# Nucleon localization function in rotating nuclei

> Background: An electron localization function was originally introduced to visualize bond structures in molecules. It became a useful tool to describe electron configurations in atoms, molecules and solids. In nuclear physics, a nucleon localization function (NLF) has been used to characterize clusters in light nuclei, fragment formation in fission and pasta phases in the inner crust of neutron stars. Purpose: We use the NLF to study the nuclear response to fast rotation. Methods: We generalize the NLF to the case of nuclear rotation. The extended expressions involve both time-even and time-odd local densities. Since current density and density gradient contribute to the NLF primarily at the surface, we propose a simpler spatial measure given by the kinetic-energy density. Illustrative calculations for the superdeformed yrast band of $^{152}$Dy were carried out by using the cranked Skyrme-Hartree-Fock method. We also employed the cranked harmonic-oscillator model to gain insights into patterns revealed by the NLF at high angular momentum. Results: In a deformed rotating nucleus, several NLFs can be introduced, depending on the definition of the spin-quantization axis and self-consistent symmetries of the system. The oscillating pattern of the NLF can be explained by a constructive interference between the kinetic-energy and particle densities. The nodal pattern seen in the NLF along the major axis of a rotating nucleus comes from single-particle orbits with large aligned angular momentum. The variation of the NLF along the minor axis is traced back to deformation-aligned orbits. Conclusions: The NLF allows a simple interpretation of the shell structure evolution in the rotating nucleus in terms of the angular-momentum alignment of individual nucleons. We expect that the NLF will be useful for the characterization of other collective modes and time-dependent processes.

## Key claim

The nucleon localization function (NLF) can be generalized to rotating nuclei by including both time-even and time-odd local densities, and a simplified version based solely on kinetic-energy density captures the essential shell-structure patterns in the nuclear interior while neglecting surface contributions from current density and density gradients.

## Method

The authors generalized the NLF to the case of nuclear rotation by extending the expressions to include time-even and time-odd local particle densities, spin densities, currents, and spin-current tensor densities. They performed unpaired cranked Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations using the SkM* parametrization for the superdeformed yrast band of 152Dy, with the angular momentum generated via a cranking term -ωĴy, and supplemented these with cranked harmonic-oscillator model calculations to interpret the spatial patterns.

## Key numbers

- The superdeformed yrast configuration of 152Dy has occupation numbers [22,22,21,21]_n ⊗ [16,16,17,17]_p in the four parity-signature blocks.
- The quadrupole moment Q20 was constrained at 42 b for the SD yrast band of 152Dy, with relative variation less than 1% in the frequency range ℏω = 0.2–0.5 MeV.
- The cranked harmonic-oscillator model used frequencies ω⊥ = ωx = ωy = 2ωz and was filled with 60 particles, corresponding to a closed superdeformed supershell Nshell = 6.
- The CHF calculations for 152Dy were carried out up to ℏω = 0.8 MeV, corresponding to angular momentum Iy ≈ 90 ℏ.
- The stretched deformed harmonic-oscillator basis had frequencies ℏωz = 6.246 MeV and ℏω⊥ = 11.200 MeV, with 1013 basis states and HO quanta not exceeding 15 in each direction.

## Context

This work extends the nucleon localization function, previously used to characterize clusters in light nuclei (Reinhard, 2011), fragment formation in fission (Zhang, 2016; Scamps, 2018), and pasta phases in neutron star crusts (Schuetrumpf, 2017), to the study of nuclear rotation. The calculations for superdeformed 152Dy build on prior self-consistent studies of this nucleus (Dobaczewski, 1995; Satula, 1996; Afanasjev, 2010) and demonstrate that the NLF can visualize how individual nucleon orbits with large aligned angular momentum produce characteristic nodal patterns, enabling future applications to other collective modes and time-dependent processes.
