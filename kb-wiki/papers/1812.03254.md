# Nuclear binding energy predictions based on BP neural network

> Nuclear masses are of great importance in nuclear physics and astrophysics. Descriptive experimental data on nuclear masses and the prediction of unknown masses based on residual proton-neutron interactions are a focus in nuclear physics. The accuracy of the residual interaction determines the accuracy of the nuclear mass values, so the study of residual interactions is essential. Before we carry out this study, there are many papers using artificial neural networks in nuclear physics. But no one uses BP neural network to study residual interactions. In this paper, we obtained a description and prediction model for residual interactions based on BP neural network. By combining experimental values with residual interactions model, we successfully calculate the nuclear masses of $A\geq100$. Results demonstrate that the differences between our calculated values and experimental values (AME2003, AME2012 and AME2016) show that the root-mean-squared deviations (RMSDs) are small (comparing with AME2003, the odd-A nuclei RMSD and the even-A nuclei RMSD are 112 keV and 128 keV; comparing with AME2012, the odd-A nuclei RMSD and the even-A nuclei RMSD are 103 keV and 121 keV; comparing with AME2016, the RMSD of odd-A nuclei and even-A nuclei are 106 keV and 122 keV, respectively). In addition, we obtained some predicted masses based on AME2003 and AME2012, the predicted values have good accuracy and compared well with experimental values (AME2012 and AME2016). The results show that the study of residual interactions using the proposed BP neural network method is feasible and accurate. This method is helpful for analyzing and extracting useful information from a large number of experimental values and then providing a reference for discovering physical laws and support for physical experiments.

## Key claim
The paper claims that a BP (back-propagation) neural network can describe and predict residual proton-neutron interactions for nuclei with mass number A >= 100, and that combining this with experimental mass databases yields calculated nuclear masses with root-mean-squared deviations (RMSDs) of about 100-130 keV. The hedge is that the method contains many parameters and humans cannot participate in the prediction process, and that some individual nuclear mass deviations are larger than desired.

## Method
The author used the AME2003, AME2012, and AME2016 databases to compute the residual interaction delta V_{1p-1n} via Eq. (2) for nuclei with A >= 100. A BP neural network with 3 input neurons (proton number Z, neutron number N, and the residual interaction), 15 hidden-layer neurons, and 1 output neuron was built using MATLAB 2015b, trained with a global minimum error of 10^{-8}, learning rate 0.03, and up to 8000 training iterations. After training, the network output residual interactions were used with Eq. (3) to calculate nuclear masses, and RMSDs were computed against the experimental databases.

## Key numbers
- For AME2003: odd-A nuclei RMSD = 112 keV (621 nuclei); even-A nuclei RMSD = 128 keV (618 nuclei)
- For AME2012: odd-A nuclei RMSD = 103 keV (688 nuclei); even-A nuclei RMSD = 121 keV (695 nuclei)
- For AME2016: odd-A nuclei RMSD = 106 keV (708 nuclei); even-A nuclei RMSD = 122 keV (716 nuclei)
- For A >= 10 with AME2003: odd-A RMSD = 224 keV (925 nuclei); even-A RMSD = 241 keV (915 nuclei)
- For A >= 42 with AME2012: odd-A RMSD = 132 keV (920 nuclei); even-A RMSD = 157 keV (934 nuclei)
- For delta V_{1p-2n} with AME2012: RMSD = 126 keV; for delta V_{2p-1n}: RMSD = 149 keV
- Predicted mass excess for 34 nuclei based on AME2003 showed deviations typically in the range of tens to a few hundred keV, with some as low as 6 keV (e.g., $^{99}$Pd) and up to 524 keV ($^{114}$Cs)

## Context
This work extends prior studies of residual proton-neutron interactions, such as the empirical formulas in Ref. [12] (which reported RMSDs of 132 keV for odd-A and 168 keV for even-A nuclei with A >= 100 using AME2003), and the use of Bayesian neural networks by Ref. [30] (which reduced the Duflo-Zuker mass formula RMSD from 503 keV to 286 keV). The BP neural network approach enables study of nuclear masses over a larger region without increasing deviation, and provides predicted masses for unknown nuclei that can be compared with newer experimental data in AME2012 and AME2016, offering a reference for future nuclear physics experiments.
