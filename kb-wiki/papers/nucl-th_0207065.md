# The Structure of Light Nuclei and Its Effect on Precise Atomic Measurements

> My talk will consist of three parts: (a) what every atomic physicist needs to know about the physics of light nuclei [and no more]; (b) what nuclear physicists can do for atomic physics; (c) what atomic physicists can do for nuclear physics. A brief qualitative overview of the nuclear force and calculational techniques for light nuclei will be presented, with an emphasis on debunking myths and on recent progress in the field. Nuclear quantities that affect precise atomic measurements will be discussed, together with their current theoretical and experimental status. The final topic will be a discussion of those atomic measurements that would be useful to nuclear physics.

## Key claim

Nuclear corrections to precise atomic measurements are now calculable with controlled uncertainties because chiral perturbation theory provides a well-founded rationale for the nuclear force, and Green's Function Monte Carlo techniques can solve the Schrödinger equation for light nuclei with numerical errors smaller than 1%. However, the author notes that assigning uncertainties to nuclear results remains the single most difficult aspect of any calculation, and that the interpretation of nuclear physics in atomic measurements becomes difficult to trust at about the 1 kHz level.

## Method

The paper reviews the chiral perturbation theory framework for the nuclear force, which divides the interaction into a long-range part treated via pion-exchange degrees of freedom and a short-range part parameterized from nucleon-nucleon scattering data. The author then describes Green's Function Monte Carlo calculations that solve the many-body Schrödinger equation for nuclei up to ten nucleons, and discusses how nuclear charge form factors and current densities are expanded in moments that enter atomic energy-level calculations through the electron-nucleus Coulomb and hyperfine interactions.

## Key numbers

- Pion masses extracted from nucleon-nucleon scattering data: \(m_{\pi^\pm} = 139.4(10)\) MeV and \(m_{\pi^0} = 135.6(13)\) MeV, agreeing with free-pion masses of 139.57018(35) MeV and 134.9766(6) MeV respectively
- Deuteron quadrupole moment from nuclear measurement: \(Q = 0.282(19)\) fm\(^2\), consistent with molecular measurement \(Q = 0.2860(15)\) fm\(^2\) but with an order-of-magnitude larger error
- Proton charge radius from Rosenfelder's analysis: \(\langle r^2 \rangle_{\rm p}^{1/2} = 0.880(15)\) fm
- D-H isotope shift in the 2S-1S transition: \(\Delta \nu = 670\,994\,334.64(15)\) kHz
- Deuteron wave function radius from second-generation potentials: \(\langle r^2 \rangle_{\rm wfn}^{1/2} = 1.9687(18)\) fm; experimental point-nucleon charge radius: 1.9753(10) fm; difference: 0.0066(21) fm
- Tritium wave function radius from first-generation forces: \(\langle r^2 \rangle_{\rm wfn}^{1/2} = 1.582(8)\) fm (subjective estimate)

## Context

This talk extends the line of work on nuclear corrections to atomic energy levels, building on the chiral perturbation theory framework developed by Weinberg and van Kolck, and on the Green's Function Monte Carlo technique pioneered by Carlson and Pieper. The paper reviews the compilation of nuclear charge and magnetic radii by Sick, and discusses the ongoing PSI muonic hydrogen experiment for a definitive proton radius measurement. It enables future isotope-shift measurements, particularly the tritium-hydrogen shift, to test nuclear wave functions, and identifies hyperfine splitting residuals (tens to hundreds of ppm) as containing unexplored nuclear information in the form of Zemach moments and spin-dependent polarizabilities related to the Drell-Hearn-Gerasimov sum rule.
