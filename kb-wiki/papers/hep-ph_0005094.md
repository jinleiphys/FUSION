# Prospects of Detecting Baryon and Quark Superfluidity from Cooling Neutron Stars

> Baryon and quark superfluidity in the cooling of neutron stars are investigated. Observations could constrain combinations of the neutron or Lambda-hyperon pairing gaps and the star's mass. However, in a hybrid star with a mixed phase of hadrons and quarks, quark gaps larger than a few tenths of an MeV render quark matter virtually invisible for cooling. If the quark gap is smaller, quark superfluidity could be important, but its effects will be nearly impossible to distinguish from those of other baryonic constituents.

## Key claim
Observations of neutron star cooling could constrain combinations of the neutron or Lambda-hyperon pairing gaps and the star's mass, but quark gaps larger than a few tenths of an MeV render quark matter virtually invisible for cooling; if the quark gap is smaller, its effects will be nearly impossible to distinguish from those of other baryonic constituents.

## Method
The authors performed cooling simulations by solving the heat transport and hydrostatic equations including general relativistic effects for neutron stars with four generic compositions: nucleons only (np), nucleons with quark matter (npQ), nucleons and hyperons (npH), and nucleons, hyperons and quarks (npHQ). They adopted pairing gaps for neutrons in the 1S0 and 3P2 channels, protons in the 1S0 channel, Lambdas in the 1S0 channel, and schematic s-wave quark gaps with heights ranging from 0.1 to 100 MeV. The baryonic matter was calculated using a field-theoretic model at the mean field level, while quark matter was calculated using either a bag-like model or the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio quark model.

## Key numbers
- Quark gaps larger than a few tenths of an MeV render quark matter virtually invisible for cooling.
- For the npQ and npHQ models studied, maximum masses are approximately 1.5 solar masses, central baryon densities are approximately 1.35 fm^-3, and volume fractions of quarks at the center are approximately 0.4.
- The threshold for Lambda appearance in the model EOS is 0.54 fm^-3, with the threshold star mass being 1.28 solar masses.
- For npH matter, stars with mass greater than 1.50 solar masses are dense enough that the Lambda 1S0 gap vanishes and undergo fast cooling.

## Context
This work extends previous studies of neutron star cooling with baryon superfluidity (Page 1992, Prakash 1998) and hyperon cooling (Prakash 1998, Schaab et al. 1998) by including quark matter in a mixed phase with hadrons and more accurately treating the Lambda hyperon gap. The results show that a thin layer of unpaired or weakly paired Lambdas controls the cooling, an effect overlooked in prior work due to inadequate zonal resolution. The paper enables future observational constraints on combinations of pairing gaps and stellar masses, but suggests that cooling observations alone will not provide definitive evidence for the existence of quark matter.
