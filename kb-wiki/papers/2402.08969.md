# Systematic many-fermion Hamiltonian input scheme and spectral calculations on quantum computers

> We present a novel input scheme for general second-quantized Hamiltonians of relativistic or non-relativistic many-fermion systems. This input scheme incorporates the fermionic anticommutation relations, particle number variations, and respects the symmetries of the Hamiltonian. Based on our input scheme, we propose a hybrid quantum-classical framework for spectral calculations on future quantum hardwares. We provide explicit circuit designs and the associated gate cost. We demonstrate our hybrid framework by solving the low-lying spectra of ${^{42}}$Ca and ${^{46}}$Ca. Our input scheme provides new pathways to solving the spectra and dynamics of the relativistic and nonrelativistic many-fermion systems via first-principles approaches.

## Key claim

The authors present a Hamiltonian input scheme for many-fermion systems that avoids the complicated decomposition of fermion operators into Pauli strings required by standard transformations (Jordan-Wigner, Bravyi-Kitaev), instead creating quantum walk states directly from the Hamiltonian and Fock states; the scheme is demonstrated only for a simplified pairing-plus-quadrupole model in a restricted valence space, not for full first-principles interactions.

## Method

The authors construct two oracles: an enumerator oracle that applies a monomial of the Hamiltonian to a Fock state and checks validity via error registers, and a matrix element oracle that computes the phase and magnitude of the corresponding few-body matrix element. These oracles are combined into forward and backward isometries that block-encode the Hamiltonian, with a swap operation pruning non-physical walk-state components. The block-encoded Hamiltonian is then used within a symmetry-adapted quantum Krylov subspace diagonalization framework, where Krylov bases are generated from Chebyshev polynomials and matrix elements are evaluated on a quantum computer via the Hadamard test, with the resulting generalized eigenvalue problem solved classically.

## Key numbers

* The gate cost to construct the input scheme for a Hamiltonian with up to two-fermion interactions is \(\widetilde{O}(N_{\rm sp}^5)\), matching the lowest rigorous complexity from prior LCU approaches.
* The low-lying spectra of \(^{42}\)Ca and \(^{46}\)Ca were computed using a pairing-plus-quadrupole Hamiltonian with coupling constants \(g = 0.147439\) MeV and \(\chi = -3.934 \times 10^8\), fitted to the excitation spectrum of \(^{42}\)Ca.
* The ground state energy of \(^{42}\)Ca was found to be \(-2.34280\) MeV, with the \(2^+\) excitation at \(1.52471\) MeV (matching experiment exactly) and the \(4^+/6^+\) excitation at \(2.92714\) MeV (experiment: \(2.7524\) MeV and \(3.18926\) MeV respectively).
* The ground state energy of \(^{46}\)Ca was found to be \(0.868409\) MeV, with the \(2^+\) excitation at \(1.52471\) MeV (experiment: \(1.346\) MeV) and the \(4^+/6^+\) excitation at \(2.92714\) MeV (experiment: \(2.5747\) MeV and \(2.9739\) MeV respectively).
* The required Krylov subspace dimension \(\mathcal{K}\) scales as \(\Theta[(\log |\gamma_0|^{-1} + \log \mathcal{E}^{-1}) \cdot \min(\mathcal{E}^{-1}, \Delta^{-1})]\), where \(\Delta\) is the spectral gap and \(\gamma_0\) the overlap with the ground state.

## Context

This work extends the quantum Krylov subspace diagonalization methods of Huggins (2020) and Kirby (2023) by adding symmetry control over the Krylov bases, enabling access to excited states rather than only the ground state. The input scheme differs from the sparse-matrix approach of Berry (2012) and Gilyen (2019) by restoring natural connections between Hamiltonian matrix elements and Fock states, and from the standard Jordan-Wigner and Bravyi-Kitaev transformations by avoiding Pauli-string decomposition of many-body operators. The demonstration on calcium isotopes uses the pairing-plus-quadrupole interaction of Dreiss (1971) and is limited to the \(0f_{7/2}\) valence space; the authors state the framework is applicable to general first-principles nuclear structure calculations on future fault-tolerant quantum hardware.
