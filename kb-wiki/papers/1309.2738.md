# Non compound nucleus fission events and standard saddle-point statistical model

> The large body of experimental data on the fission fragments anisotropies are analyzed in several heavy-ion induced fission reaction systems. The entrance channel mass asymmetry parameters of these systems put on the both sides of the Businaro-Gallone mass asymmetry parameters. The role of the mass numbers of the projectile and the target in the prediction of a normal or an anomalous behavior in angular anisotropy, as well as the validity of standard saddle-point statistical model are considered. The average contribution of non compound nucleus fission for the systems with an anomalous behavior in anisotropy are also determined. PACS numbers: 25.70.Jj *Corresponding author: s.soheyli@basu.ac.ir

## Key claim

The paper shows that the standard saddle-point statistical model (SSPSM) is valid only for heavy-ion induced fission systems whose target and projectile mass numbers fall below a certain empirical curve; for systems above that curve, non-compound nucleus fission (NCNF) contributions cause anomalously large angular anisotropies that the SSPSM cannot reproduce, regardless of whether the entrance channel mass asymmetry is greater or less than the Businaro-Gallone critical value.

## Method

The authors analyzed a large body of experimental fission fragment angular anisotropy data for heavy-ion induced reactions, comparing measured anisotropies to SSPSM predictions calculated using the rotating finite range model (RFRM) for fission barriers, moments of inertia, and rotational energies. For systems showing anomalous (larger than predicted) anisotropies, they extracted the average NCNF contribution over a defined energy range using the formula \(A_{NCN} = (A_{exp} - A_{SSPSM}) / A_{exp}\). They then plotted these NCNF contributions as functions of projectile and target mass numbers to construct a border diagram separating regions of pure compound nucleus fission from regions with mixed CNF and NCNF events.

## Key numbers

* For the \(^{32}\text{S}+^{197}\text{Au}\) system over the energy range \(1.1 \leq E_{c.m.}/V_b \leq 1.2\), the average NCNF contribution is approximately 44%.
* For the \(^{32}\text{S}+^{208}\text{Pb}\) system over the same energy range (\(1.1 \leq E_{c.m.}/V_b \leq 1.2\)), the average NCNF contribution is 50%.
* For the \(^{40}\text{Ar}+^{208}\text{Pb}\) system over the energy range \(1.05 \leq E_{c.m.}/V_b \leq 1.3\), the predicted average NCNF contribution from the authors' systematics is approximately 87%.
* The NCNF contributions for induced fission of \(^{208}\text{Pb}\) target are zero for projectiles with mass number 20 or less, and non-zero for projectiles with mass number greater than 20.
* The onset of NCNF events occurs with lighter projectiles for heavier targets, and vice versa.

## Context

This work extends systematic studies of fission fragment anisotropies that previously attributed anomalous behaviors to NCNF mechanisms such as quasi fission, fast fission, and pre-equilibrium fission, as modeled by Ramamurthy and Kapoor (1985). The paper contradicts the simple rule that systems with entrance channel mass asymmetry \(\alpha > \alpha_{BG}\) always show normal anisotropies, by cataloging counterexamples (e.g., \(^{9}\text{Be}+^{232}\text{Th}\), \(^{12}\text{C}+^{238}\text{U}\)) in Table I. The resulting border diagram enables experimenters to predict, based solely on projectile and target mass numbers, whether the SSPSM will be applicable or whether NCNF contributions must be accounted for in interpreting angular distribution data.
