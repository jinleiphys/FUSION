# Population synthesis as a probe of neutron star thermal evolution

> The study of thermal emission from isolated, cooling neutron stars plays a key role in probing the physical conditions of both the star crust and the core. The comparison of theoretical models for the star thermal evolution with the surface temperature derived from X-ray observations of sources of different age is one of the main tools to investigate the properties of the interior and constrain the equation of state. Here we propose to use population synthesis studies as an independent approach to test the physics governing the star cooling. Theoretical Log N - Log S distributions depend on the assumed neutron star thermal evolution. We have computed distributions for several different cooling scenarios and found that comparison with the observed Log N - Log S of isolated neutron stars is effective in discriminating among cooling models. Among the eleven cooling models considered in this paper, all of which may reproduce the observed temperature vs. age diagram, only at most three can explain the Log N - Log S distribution of close-by cooling neutron stars. The Log N - Log S test, being a ``global'' one and despite some limitations, appears capable of complementing the standard temperature vs. age test used up to now.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the Log N - Log S distribution of nearby isolated neutron stars can serve as an independent, complementary test to the standard temperature-age diagram for discriminating among neutron star cooling models, and finds that among eleven cooling models that all pass the temperature-age test, only at most three can also explain the observed Log N - Log S distribution.

## Method

The authors performed population synthesis calculations for neutron stars in the solar neighborhood, combining spatial evolution tracks with cooling curves from eleven different models of neutron star thermal evolution. They computed theoretical Log N - Log S distributions for each cooling model using a Monte Carlo approach with 5000 individual tracks, incorporating assumptions about the initial spatial distribution (including contributions from the Gould Belt), kick velocity distribution, mass spectrum (eight mass bins from 1.1 to 1.76 solar masses), and blackbody surface emission, then compared these distributions to the observed Log N - Log S of thirteen isolated neutron stars from ROSAT data.

## Key numbers

- Eleven cooling models were considered, all of which reproduce the observed temperature vs. age diagram; only at most three can explain the Log N - Log S distribution of close-by cooling neutron stars.
- The mass spectrum used eight bins centered at M/M_sun = 1.1, 1.25, 1.32, 1.4, 1.48, 1.6, 1.7, 1.76, with statistical weights of 31.75%, 25.75%, 11%, 28.125%, 0.875%, 1.125%, 0.75%, and 0.625% respectively.
- Two values of the outer Gould Belt radius were tested: 300 pc and 500 pc.
- The time step for spatial evolution tracks was 10^4 years, and results were plotted for ages greater than 10^4 years and temperatures above 10^5 K.
- The observed sample contained thirteen sources; the observational upper limit on the number of fainter sources with flux greater than 0.2 counts per second was also considered.

## Context

This work extends the population synthesis approach developed in Papers I and II (Popov et al., 2003, 2004) by including nuclear medium effects in the cooling code and investigating the contribution of massive progenitors in the Gould Belt. It builds on the cooling calculations of BGV (Blaschke et al., 2004) and the brightness constraint introduced by G05 (Grigorian, 2005), and proposes the Log N - Log S test as a complement to the standard temperature-age test used by Kaminker et al., Tsuruta, and Page and collaborators. The method enables future discrimination between purely hadronic cooling scenarios and hybrid stars with color superconducting quark cores.
