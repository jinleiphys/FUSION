# Machine-learning approach to finite-size effects in systems with strongly interacting fermions

> We investigate the applicability of machine learning techniques in studying the finite-size effects associated with many-body physics. These techniques have an emerging presence in many-body theory as they have been used for interpolations, extrapolations, and in modeling wavefunctions. We will resolve several issues associated with machine learning and many-body calculations such as small datasets, outliers, and discontinuities, for the purpose of extrapolating finite calculations to macroscopic scales. We carry out a systematic investigation of two related systems by developing metrics that aim to avoid spurious effects and capture desired features. This work uses neural networks to extrapolate the Unitary Gas to the thermodynamic limit at zero-range, which is otherwise difficult to reach. The effective mass of strongly interacting neutron matter is also studied and makes use of the non-interacting problem to resolve discontinuous predictions. For this investigation, we also carried out new Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) calculations for a variety of densities and particle numbers. Ultimately, we demonstrate an effective utility for neural networks in this context.

## Key claim

Neural networks can effectively extrapolate finite-size effects to the thermodynamic limit in strongly interacting fermion systems, but only after addressing small-dataset issues (outliers, discontinuities) through careful data augmentation and separate training of energy and momentum networks; the final predictions remain variational upper bounds, not exact values.

## Method

The authors used feed-forward neural networks (FFNNs) with Keras/Tensorflow to extrapolate two systems: the Unitary Gas (UG) and neutron matter (NM). For the UG, they applied linear data augmentation and outlier removal (at N=70 and N=80) before hyperparameter optimization via 5-fold cross-validation. For NM, they performed new Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) calculations and trained separate networks for the quasi-particle energy and the non-interacting momentum, then combined them via the dispersion relation to extract the effective mass.

## Key numbers

- Unitary Gas thermodynamic-limit energy at zero range: ξ = 0.389(1) (dimensionless ratio E/E_FG)
- Leading theoretical UG value from lattice calculations: 0.372(5) (Carlson 2011, Carlson 2012)
- Experimental UG value from Ku et al. (2012): 0.376(4)
- Earlier UG value from Forbes et al. (2012), whose raw data was used: 0.3897(4)
- NM densities studied: n = 0.02, 0.04, 0.06, 0.08, 0.1, 0.12, 0.14, 0.16, 0.18, 0.2 fm⁻³
- NM particle numbers in training dataset: N ∈ [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 19, 27, 33, 45, 57, 70]

## Context

This work extends the finite-size extrapolation techniques of Forbes et al. (2012) for the Unitary Gas and Buraczynski et al. (2019, 2020) for neutron matter, replacing manual fitting with neural-network-based interpolation. It demonstrates that machine learning can handle the small datasets and discontinuities typical of quantum Monte Carlo calculations, enabling systematic extrapolation to the thermodynamic limit. The approach enables future studies of other strongly interacting systems where finite-size effects are significant, such as nuclear matter and ultracold Fermi gases.
