# Tunneling Theory for Tunable Open Quantum Systems of Ultracold Atoms in One-Dimensional Traps

> The creation of tunable open quantum systems is becoming feasible in current experiments with ultracold atoms in low-dimensional traps. In particular, the high degree of experimental control over these systems allows detailed studies of tunneling dynamics, e.g., as a function of the trapping geometry and the interparticle interaction strength. In order to address this exciting opportunity we present a theoretical framework for two-body tunneling based on the rigged Hilbert space formulation. In this approach, bound, resonant and scattering states are included on an equal footing, and we argue that the coupling of all these components is vital for a correct description of the relevant threshold phenomena. In particular, we study the tunneling mechanism for two-body systems in one-dimensional traps and different interaction regimes. We find a strong dominance of sequential tunneling of single particles for repulsive and weakly attractive systems, while there is a signature of correlated pair tunneling in the calculated many-particle flux for strongly attractive interparticle interaction.

## Key claim

The paper presents a rigged Hilbert space (Berggren basis) framework for two-body tunneling in ultracold atoms, arguing that the coupling of bound, resonant, and scattering states is vital for a correct description of threshold phenomena. The claim is hedged by the fact that the method's numerical precision degrades for strongly attractive interactions where the imaginary part of the energy becomes very small, and that quantitative results for pair tunneling could not be obtained due to numerical noise.

## Method

The authors solve the two-body Schrodinger equation for two distinguishable fermions in a one-dimensional finite-depth trap using an expansion in the Berggren basis, which includes S-matrix poles (bound and resonant states) and non-resonant scattering states along a complex-momentum contour. The two-body interaction is modeled by a zero-range delta potential with tunable strength g, and the Hamiltonian matrix is diagonalized using the Davidson algorithm to target the resonant eigenstate. The tunneling rate is computed both from the imaginary part of the complex resonance energy and from an integral formalism that integrates the outward particle flux at large distance.

## Context

This work extends the rigged Hilbert space formalism, previously employed in nuclear physics for the Gamow Shell Model (Michel 2002, Rotureau 2006, Hagen 2006), to ultracold atom systems. It provides an alternative to time-dependent approaches (Lode 2009, Kim 2011) and semiclassical WKB methods (Rontani 2012) that suffer from uncontrolled approximations or unreliability for small decay widths. The framework enables future studies of tunneling in systems with more particles and different trap geometries, and highlights that the WKB approximation used in experimental data analysis (Zuern 2013) may be inadequate for extracting trap parameters.
