# Single and double coincidence nucleon spectra in the weak decay of Lambda hypernuclei

> Recent progress has been experienced in the field of hypernuclear weak decay, especially concerning the ratio of the neutron- to proton-induced Lambda non-mesonic decay rates, G_n/G_p. Theoretical analyses of nucleon coincidence data have been performed in a finite nucleus framework. They led to the extraction of G_n/G_p values in agreement with pure theoretical estimates, thus providing an evidence for the solution of a longstanding puzzle. Here we present an alternative approach to the problem, based on a nuclear matter formalism extended to finite nuclei via the local density approximation. The work is motivated by the exigence to make the determination of G_n/G_p from data less model dependent. One-meson-exchange potentials are used for describing both the one- and two-nucleon induced decays, Lambda N -> n N and Lambda N N -> n N N. For the latter, treated within a microscopic approach, the channels Lambda n n -> n n n and Lambda p p -> n p p are included in addition to the mode Lambda n p -> n n p already considered, in a phenomenological way, in previous studies. The propagation of the final nucleons in the residual nucleus is simulated by an intranuclear cascade code. We evaluate single and double coincidence nucleon spectra for the non-mesonic decay of C-12-Lambda. Through the comparison of our predictions with KEK coincidence data we determine G_n/G_p=0.43 \pm 0.10 for this hypernucleus, confirming previous finite nucleus analyses.

## Key claim

The authors determine the neutron-to-proton ratio of Lambda non-mesonic decay rates for Carbon-12 hypernucleus as Gamma_n/Gamma_p = 0.43 ± 0.10 by comparing their nuclear matter model (extended via local density approximation) with KEK coincidence data, confirming previous finite nucleus analyses but with the caveat that this extraction remains model-dependent and requires careful treatment of final state interactions and two-nucleon induced decay channels.

## Method

The authors use a nuclear matter formalism based on the polarization propagator method, extended to finite nuclei via the local density approximation, with one-meson-exchange potentials (including pi, rho, K, K*, omega, and eta exchange) for both one-nucleon induced (Lambda N -> n N) and two-nucleon induced (Lambda N N -> n N N) weak decays. For the two-nucleon induced decay, they include the channels Lambda n n -> n n n and Lambda p p -> n p p in addition to Lambda n p -> n n p, treated microscopically rather than phenomenologically. The propagation of final nucleons through the residual nucleus is simulated using an intranuclear cascade code, and the calculated single and double coincidence nucleon spectra for Carbon-12 hypernucleus are compared with KEK experimental data.

## Key numbers

- Gamma_n/Gamma_p = 0.43 ± 0.10 for Carbon-12 hypernucleus, extracted from comparison with KEK coincidence data
- The LDA calculation predicts Gamma_n/Gamma_p = 0.285 as the pure theoretical value (from Table 1: Gamma_n = 0.267, Gamma_p = 0.936 in units of free Lambda decay width)
- The two-nucleon induced decay width ratio Gamma_2/Gamma_1 = 0.26 from the LDA calculation
- The total non-mesonic decay width from LDA is Gamma_NM = 1.521 (in units of free Lambda decay width)
- A high nucleon kinetic energy detection threshold of 60 MeV makes two-nucleon induced channel contributions quite small, while a moderate threshold of 30 MeV is used for coincidence spectra

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of Bauer (2003, 2004) and Oset (1985) by applying a nuclear matter formalism with local density approximation to hypernuclear weak decay, providing an alternative to the finite nucleus approach of Parreno (2002) and the earlier analyses of Bauer (2003, 2004). The paper addresses the longstanding Gamma_n/Gamma_p puzzle by confirming the values around 0.3-0.4 previously extracted by Bauer (2005) and Bauer (2005a) from KEK coincidence data, while aiming to make the determination less model dependent. The results enable more reliable extraction of Gamma_n/Gamma_p from future experiments at FINUDA, J-PARC, and HypHI, and highlight that final state interactions must be properly accounted for even in coincidence measurements, contrary to claims by the KEK collaboration (Bhang, Outa, Kang, 2005, 2006).
