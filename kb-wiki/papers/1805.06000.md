# Transport coefficients of leptons in superconducting neutron star cores

> I consider the thermal conductivity and shear viscosity of leptons (electrons and muons) in the nucleon NS cores where protons are in the superconducting state. I restrict the consideration to the case of not too high temperatures $T\lesssim 0.35T_{\mathrm{c}p}$, where $T_{\mathrm{c}p}$ is the critical temperature of the proton pairing. In this case, lepton collisions with protons can be neglected. Charged lepton collision frequencies are mainly determined by the transverse plasmon exchange and are mediated by the character of the transverse plasma screening. In our previous works [Shternin \& Yakovlev, Phys. Rev. D {\bf 75} 103004 (2007); {\bf 78} 063006 (2008)] the superconducting proton contribution to the transverse screening was considered in the Pippard limit $Δ\ll \hbar q v_{\mathrm{F}p}$, where $Δ$ is the proton pairing gap, $v_{\mathrm{F}p}$ is the proton Fermi velocity, and $\hbar q$ is the typical transferred momentum in collisions. However, for large critical temperatures (large $Δ$) and relatively small densities (small $q$) the Pippard limit may become invalid. In the present study I show that this is indeed the case and that the older calculations severely underestimated the screening in a certain range of the parameters appropriate to the neutron star cores. As a consequence, the kinetic coefficients at $T\ll T_{\mathrm{c}p}$ are found to be smaller than in previous calculations.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that previous calculations of lepton transport coefficients in superconducting neutron star cores (Shternin & Yakovlev, 2007, 2008) severely underestimated the transverse plasma screening in a certain parameter range appropriate to neutron star cores, because they assumed the Pippard limit was valid when in fact the London limit or an intermediate regime applies; as a consequence, the thermal conductivity and shear viscosity at temperatures T ≲ 0.35 T_cp are found to be smaller than in those previous calculations.

## Method

The author calculates the thermal conductivity and shear viscosity of electrons and muons in neutron star cores where protons are in the superconducting state, restricting to temperatures T ≲ 0.35 T_cp where lepton-proton collisions can be neglected. The collision frequencies are computed from the transverse part of the electromagnetic interaction, using the full zero-temperature static transverse polarization function Π_t(0,q) = q_M² J(ζ) for superconducting protons, where ζ = q v_Fp/Δ, rather than assuming either the Pippard limit (ζ ≫ 1) or the London limit (ζ ≪ 1). The leading-order contributions to the transport coefficients are then evaluated by integrating over the transferred momentum q with the general form of the screening.

## Key numbers

- The crossover between London and Pippard limits occurs at A ≈ 17.5 for the thermal conductivity problem (n=0) and at A ≈ 6.8 for the shear viscosity problem (n=2), where A = v_Fp q_M/Δ.
- For small A (London limit), the integral I^t_0 = π/(4 q_M³) and I^t_2 = π/(4 q_M), independent of the gap Δ.
- For large A (Pippard limit), I^t_0 = 4A/(9π² q_M³) and I^t_2 = (4A^{1/3}/(9 q_M)) × (2^{2/3} π^{1/3}/3^{5/6}).
- The parameter A can vary in the range 0.1–100 for neutron star core conditions, with A = 1.12 (x_p/0.1)^{5/6} (n_B/n_0)^{5/6} (m*_p/m_u)^{-3/2} (0.5 MeV/Δ).
- For small A and n=0, the Pippard limit overestimates transport coefficients by up to two orders of magnitude.

## Context

This work extends the calculations of lepton transport coefficients in superconducting neutron star cores by Shternin & Yakovlev (2007, 2008), who considered the proton contribution to transverse screening only in the Pippard limit Δ ≪ ħq v_Fp. The present study shows that for large critical temperatures (large Δ) and relatively small densities (small q), the London limit or an intermediate regime applies, and the older calculations severely underestimated screening. The results enable more accurate modeling of thermal conductivity and shear viscosity in neutron star cores, which are important for understanding neutron star cooling and other astrophysical phenomena.
