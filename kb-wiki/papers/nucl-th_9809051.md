# On the macroscopic limit of nuclear collective motion and its relation to chaotic behavior

> In this note we concentrate on slow collective motion of isoscalar type at small but finite excitations, as given in nuclear fission, for instance. We report on microscopic results of transport coefficients, review how they depend on temperature and address the interplay of one and two body viscosity. We examine how transport properties might be influenced by chaotic behavior of nucleonic dynamics.

# On the macroscopic limit of nuclear collective motion and its relation to chaotic behavior

## Key claim

Slow isoscalar collective motion (as in nuclear fission) can be described microscopically through transport coefficients that depend on temperature, with the interplay of one-body and two-body viscosity being non-separable; chaotic nucleonic dynamics influences transport properties primarily through residual interactions rather than through complex mean-field potentials alone.

## Method

The author develops a locally harmonic approximation around a collective variable Q, coupling nucleonic degrees of freedom to the collective motion via linear response theory. The intrinsic response function is computed using a deformed shell model with single-particle widths parameterized by a collisional damping formula, and the collective response is obtained through a self-consistent RPA-like equation with a temperature-dependent coupling constant. Transport coefficients (inertia, friction, stiffness) are extracted from the collective strength distribution by approximating individual peaks as damped oscillators.

## Key numbers

- The time scale of the collective variable Q is longer by a factor of 5 to 10 than the time scale for nucleonic degrees of freedom.
- The collisional damping width is parameterized with Γ₀ = 33 MeV and c = 20 MeV.
- Above T ≈ 1.5 to 2 MeV, the inertia M drops to values close to those of irrotational flow.
- Strutinsky smoothing procedures for the wall formula involve averaging over intervals in energy of the order of 10 to 20 MeV.
- The heat pole width Γ_T is approximately twice the single particle width at the Fermi surface, which in turn is close to 2T.

## Context

This work extends the author's comprehensive review in Phys. Rep. 284 (1997) and addresses the longstanding question of how one-body dissipation (wall formula) and two-body viscosity contribute to nuclear friction at finite temperature. The paper contrasts with approaches that derive the wall formula from independent particle motion in complex potentials (e.g., the Kiev school's Landau-Vlasov equation solutions), arguing that residual interactions cannot be neglected at finite T. The results enable a microscopic foundation for macroscopic nuclear models used in fission dynamics, while identifying the need for better treatment of level repulsion (beyond the self-energy approach) to capture ergodic behavior.
