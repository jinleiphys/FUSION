# Grand Unified Neutrino Spectrum at Earth: Sources and Spectral Components

> We briefly review the dominant neutrino fluxes at Earth from different sources and present the Grand Unified Neutrino Spectrum ranging from meV to PeV energies. For each energy band and source, we discuss both theoretical expectations and experimental data. This compact review should be useful as a brief reference to those interested in neutrino astronomy, fundamental particle physics, dark-matter detection, high-energy astrophysics, geophysics, and other related topics.

## Key claim
This paper presents an updated graphical compilation of the "Grand Unified Neutrino Spectrum" (GUNS) at Earth, covering neutrino energies from meV to PeV, and emphasizes that the spectrum includes a newly recognized component from big-bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) neutrinos that fills the previously empty gap between the cosmic neutrino background and solar neutrinos. The review is explicitly a compact reference rather than a new measurement or theoretical model, and the authors note that the fluxes from geoneutrinos and reactors are not universal because they strongly depend on location.

## Method
The authors compiled theoretical expectations and experimental data for all known neutrino sources at Earth, including the cosmic neutrino background (CNB), BBN neutrinos, solar neutrinos (both nuclear and thermal), geoneutrinos, reactor neutrinos, atmospheric neutrinos, the diffuse supernova neutrino background (DSNB), and high-energy astrophysical neutrinos. They produced two versions of the GUNS plot: one showing neutrino flux as a function of energy and another showing energy flux, with the CNB plotted for a minimal neutrino mass spectrum of m1=0, m2=8.6 meV, and m3=50 meV. The BBN neutrino component was calculated from the decay of primordial neutrons, tritium, and beryllium-7, using data from prior work by Khatri (2010) and Ivanchik (2018).

## Key numbers
- CNB number density: 112 cm⁻³ per flavor (neutrinos plus antineutrinos)
- CNB temperature for massless neutrinos: Tν = 1.945 K = 0.168 meV
- Minimal neutrino mass spectrum used: m1 = 0, m2 = 8.6 meV, m3 = 50 meV
- Maximum of the CNB differential flux dΦν/dE: 2.70 × 10¹² cm⁻² s⁻¹ meV⁻¹ (independent of neutrino mass)
- BBN tritium number density today: nT = 1.2 × 10⁻¹⁴ cm⁻³
- BBN beryllium-7 number density today: n_Be7 = 1.1 × 10⁻¹⁶ cm⁻³
- BBN neutrinos dominate the GUNS in the energy range Eν = 10–200 meV
- Tritium decay endpoint energy: Emax = 18.6 keV, redshifted to ~90 meV today
- Neutron decay endpoint energy: Emax = 782 keV
- Beryllium-7 electron capture produces νe lines at 861.8 keV (89.6% branching) and 384.2 keV (10.4% branching), redshifted to 29.5 eV and 13.1 eV respectively
- Effective number of neutrino degrees of freedom: Neff = 3.045 (standard value)

## Context
This work extends the tradition of the "Grand Unified Photon Spectrum" by Ressell (1989) and updates earlier GUNS plots by Becker (2007) and Spiering (2012). It incorporates the recently recognized BBN neutrino flux from neutron and triton decays, first discussed by Khatri (2010) and Ivanchik (2018), which fills the previously empty energy gap between the CNB and solar neutrinos. The paper serves as a compact reference for neutrino astronomy, fundamental particle physics, dark-matter detection, and high-energy astrophysics, and it enables researchers to quickly understand which neutrino sources dominate at each energy band and what experimental prospects exist for detecting them.
