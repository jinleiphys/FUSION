# Nuclear neutrino energy spectra in high temperature astrophysical environments

> Astrophysical environments that reach temperatures greater than $\sim$ 100 keV can have significant neutrino energy loss via both plasma processes and nuclear weak interactions. We find that nuclear processes likely produce the highest-energy neutrinos. Among the important weak nuclear interactions are both charged current channels (electron capture/emission and positron capture/emission) and neutral current channels (de-excitation of nuclei via neutrino pair emission). We show that in order to make a realistic prediction of the nuclear neutrino spectrum, one must take nuclear structure into account; in some cases, the most important transitions may involve excited states, possibly in both parent and daughter nuclei. We find that the standard technique of producing a neutrino energy spectrum by using a single transition with a Q-value and matrix element chosen to fit published neutrino production rates and energy losses will not accurately capture important spectral features.

# Nuclear neutrino energy spectra in high temperature astrophysical environments

## Key claim
Nuclear weak interactions likely produce the highest-energy neutrinos in astrophysical environments above ~100 keV, but the standard technique of using a single effective Q-value and matrix element fitted to published rates fails to capture important spectral features because excited nuclear states, sometimes in both parent and daughter nuclei, can dominate the high-energy tail of the neutrino spectrum.

## Method
The authors performed shell model calculations using OXBASH with the USDB Hamiltonian for sd-shell nuclei (closed 16O core, unrestricted sd-shell valence space), computing energy levels and transition matrix elements for both charged current (electron/positron capture and emission) and neutral current (de-excitation via neutrino pair emission) processes. They populated initial nuclear states thermally using a modification of the Brink hypothesis, considering individual states up to 12 MeV excitation and assigning remaining statistical weight to an average of higher states, then summed over initial and final states to produce neutrino energy spectra in units of neutrinos per second per baryon per MeV.

## Key numbers
- Temperature threshold for significant nuclear neutrino energy loss: ~100 keV
- Core temperature in final pre-collapse stages: ~0.5 MeV; electron Fermi energy: ~5 MeV
- For 27Si at T~0.22 MeV and ρYe~9.5×10⁵ g/cm³: ground-state-to-ground-state transitions dominate the charged-current spectrum
- For 30P at T~0.17 MeV and ρYe~2.2×10⁶ g/cm³: the first excited state (Ei=0.677 MeV, T=1, J=0) has a superallowed transition to 30Si ground state and dominates the neutrino spectrum from 3 to 3.9 MeV and above 5 MeV
- For 32P at T~0.17 MeV and ρYe~2.2×10⁶ g/cm³: the third excited state (Ei=1.15 MeV) has BGT=0.074 and produces most of the spectrum; the ground-state transition has BGT=0.50×10⁻⁴
- For 28Al at T~0.43 MeV and ρYe~1.0×10⁸ g/cm³: the first isobaric analog state at E=5.94 MeV is the principal source of positron decay neutrinos and very high energy electron capture neutrinos
- Neutral current de-excitation rate: λ≈1.71×10⁻⁴ s⁻¹ × (ΔE/MeV)⁵ × BGT³
- Peak normalized neutral current emission occurs at ΔE/T=6
- For 21Ne: first excited state reaches peak relative effectiveness at T~0.06 MeV; next allowed transition becomes effective at T~0.2 MeV
- Above 10 MeV neutrino energy, neutral current spectra of 27Al and 28Si are nearly identical

## Context
This work extends the study of neutrino emissivity in pre-collapse massive stars by Patton and Lunardini (2015), who used the single-effective-transition technique of Langanke et al. (2001) to generate charged current neutrino spectra. The authors show that this technique fails for nuclei where excited states dominate, as demonstrated explicitly for 30P, 32P, and 28Al. The results enable more realistic predictions of the high-energy neutrino signal from massive stars, potentially detectable months before core collapse as proposed by Odrzywolek et al. (2004) and Asakura et al. (2016). The neutral current de-excitation rates are compared against electron-positron pair annihilation and the photo process from Itoh et al. (1996), and against electron capture on 28Si using the prescription of Misch et al. (2014).
