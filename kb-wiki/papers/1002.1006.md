# Shell evolution and nuclear forces

> We present a quantitative study of the role played by different components characterizing the nucleon-nucleon interaction in the evolution of the nuclear shell structure. It is based on the spin-tensor decomposition of an effective two-body shell-model interaction and the subsequent study of effective single-particle energy variations in a series of isotopes or isotones. The technique allows to separate unambiguously contributions of the central, vector and tensor components of the realistic effective interaction. We show that while the global variation of the single-particle energies is due to the central component of the effective interaction, the characteristic behavior of spin-orbit partners, noticed recently, is mainly due to its tensor part. Based on the analysis of a well-fitted realistic interaction in sdpf-shell model space, we analyze in detail the role played by the different terms in the formation and/or disappearance of N=16, N=20 and N=28 shell gaps in neutron-rich nuclei.

## Key claim

The evolution of shell gaps (N=16, N=20, N=28) in neutron-rich nuclei is a combined effect of the central term (especially its triplet-even channel) and the tensor term of the effective nucleon-nucleon interaction, with the tensor term playing a dominant role specifically for the relative displacement of spin-orbit partners; however, the authors caution that one cannot assign a dominating role to the tensor mechanism in cases where no explicit spin-orbit partners are considered.

## Method

The authors performed a spin-tensor decomposition of a realistic effective shell-model interaction in the 1s0d1p0f model space, separating the two-body matrix elements into central (k=0), vector (k=1), and tensor (k=2) components. They then computed effective single-particle energies (ESPEs) from the monopole part of the Hamiltonian for series of isotopes and isotones, and studied the variation of these ESPEs induced by each component separately, using the realistic interaction of Nowacki and Poves (2009).

## Key numbers

- The decrease of the splitting between neutron 0d3/2 and 1s1/2 orbitals from 28O to 34Si is 2.57 MeV total, composed of 1.87 MeV from the central part and 1.06 MeV from the tensor part.
- The increase of the gap between neutron 0d3/2 and 0f7/2 orbitals from 28O to 34Si is 3.68 MeV total, with central contribution 2.17 MeV and tensor contribution 1.96 MeV.
- The variation of the gap between neutron 0d3/2 and 0f7/2 orbitals from 36S to 40Ca is 0.21 MeV total, resulting from a central contribution of 1.99 MeV nearly cancelled by a tensor contribution of -1.93 MeV.
- The lowering of the gap between proton 0d5/2 and 0d3/2 orbitals from 40Ca to 48Ca is -2.33 MeV total, dominated by a tensor contribution of -2.73 MeV.
- The reduction of the N=28 shell gap (neutron 1p3/2-0f7/2 difference) from 36O to 42Si is 1.60 MeV total, with central 2.03 MeV and tensor -0.67 MeV.
- The reduction of the N=28 shell gap from 44S to 48Ca is 1.81 MeV total, with central 1.31 MeV and tensor 0.68 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of shell evolution initiated by Otsuka et al. (2001, 2005), who proposed respectively the central spin-isospin exchange term and the tensor force as dominant mechanisms. The authors use a spin-tensor decomposition method previously applied by Umeya and Muto (2004, 2006) in smaller model spaces, but contrary to those studies they find a significant first-order tensor effect. The analysis enables quantitative separation of central, vector, and tensor contributions to shell gap evolution, and suggests that the vector term may counteract the tensor term in heavier nuclei, potentially explaining the absence of a characteristic tensor effect in heavy Sn nuclei found by Nowacki (2007).
