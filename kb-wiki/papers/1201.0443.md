# Nucleon-Nucleon Interactions from Dispersion Relations: Coupled Partial Waves

> We consider nucleon-nucleon interactions from chiral effective field theory applying the N/D method. The case of coupled partial waves is now treated, extending Ref. [1] where the uncoupled case was studied. As a result three N/D elastic-like equations have to be solved for every set of three independent partial waves coupled. As in the previous reference the input for this method is the discontinuity along the left-hand cut of the nucleon-nucleon partial wave amplitudes. It can be calculated perturbatively in chiral perturbation theory because it involves only irreducible two-nucleon intermediate states. We apply here our method to the leading order result consisting of one-pion exchange as the source for the discontinuity along the left-hand cut. The linear integral equations for the N/D method must be solved in the presence of L - 1 constraints, with L the orbital angular momentum, in order to satisfy the proper threshold behavior for L>= 2. We dedicate special attention to satisfy the requirements of unitarity in coupled channels. We also focus on the specific issue of the deuteron pole position in the 3S1-3D1 scattering. Our final amplitudes are based on dispersion relations and chiral effective field theory, being independent of any explicit regulator. They are amenable to a systematic improvement order by order in the chiral expansion.

# Nucleon-Nucleon Interactions from Dispersion Relations: Coupled Partial Waves

## Key claim

The authors extend the N/D method combined with chiral perturbation theory (ChPT) to coupled partial waves in nucleon-nucleon scattering, demonstrating that at leading order (one-pion exchange as the sole input for the left-hand cut discontinuity) the approach yields unitary amplitudes without any explicit regulator, but the deuteron pole position requires special treatment to appear consistently across all coupled channels.

## Method

The authors solve three independent N/D equations for each set of coupled partial waves (labeled 11, 12, and 22), writing each partial wave amplitude as t_ij(A) = A^{ℓ_ij} N_ij(A)/D_ij(A). The input is the discontinuity along the left-hand cut, calculated at leading order from one-pion exchange using ChPT, which involves only irreducible two-nucleon intermediate states. For partial waves with orbital angular momentum ℓ ≥ 2, the authors impose ℓ−1 constraints via Castillejo-Dalitz-Dyson (CDD) poles taken to infinity to ensure proper threshold behavior. An iterative procedure is employed: given input phase shifts and mixing angles, the three integral equations for D_ij are solved along the left-hand cut, new amplitudes are computed on the right-hand cut, and the process repeats until convergence (difference between iterations below one per mil).

## Key numbers

- The deuteron pole is found at k_d² = −0.08 m_π² in the ³S₁ amplitude, corresponding to a binding energy E_d ≈ 1.7 MeV
- The ³S₁ scattering length is fixed to a_t = 5.424 ± 0.004 fm, which determines the subtraction constant N₀ = −4π a_t / m
- The onset of the left-hand cut is at A < L ≡ −m_π²/4, where m_π is the pion mass
- The phase space factor in the non-relativistic approximation is ρ(A) = m√A / 4π, with m the nucleon mass

## Context

This work extends the uncoupled partial wave treatment of Albaladejo and Oller (2011) to coupled channels, addressing a limitation of earlier N/D applications to nucleon-nucleon scattering. Unlike the approaches of Wong (1959) and Scotti and Wong (1963, 1965), which used ad-hoc poles below threshold to achieve correct threshold behavior and neglected or partially treated channel coupling, the present method satisfies exact unitarity for all partial waves and uses CDD poles at infinity rather than fitted fictitious poles. The approach is designed for systematic improvement order by order in the chiral expansion, contrasting with the Weinberg scheme where chiral potentials require cutoff regularization and exhibit nonrenormalizability for the one-pion exchange tensor force as shown by Nogga, Timmermans, and van Kolck (2005).
