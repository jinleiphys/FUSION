# Cooling of Neutron Stars with Color Superconducting Quark Cores

> We show that within a recently developed nonlocal chiral quark model the critical density for a phase transition to color superconducting quark matter under neutron star conditions can be low enough for these phases to occur in compact star configurations with masses below 1.3 M_solar. We study the cooling of these objects in isolation for different values of the gravitational mass. Our equation of state (EoS) allows for 2SC quark matter with a large quark gap \~100 MeV for u and d quarks of two colors that coexists with normal quark matter within a mixed phase in the hybrid star interior. We argue that, if the phases with unpaired quarks were allowed, the corresponding hybrid stars would cool too fast. If they occured for M < 1.3 M_solar, as it follows from our EoS, one could not appropriately describe the neutron star cooling data existing by today. We discuss a "2SC+X" phase, as a possibility to have all quarks paired in two-flavor quark matter under neutron star constraints, where the X-gap is of the order of 10 keV - 1 MeV. Density independent gaps do not allow to fit the cooling data. Only the presence of an X-gap that decreases with increase of the density could allow to appropriately fit the data in a similar compact star mass interval to that following from a purely hadronic model. This scenario is suggested as an alternative explanation of the cooling data in the framework of a hybrid star model.

## Key claim

The paper argues that hybrid stars with color superconducting quark cores can explain neutron star cooling data only if the residual pairing gap (X-gap) for formerly unpaired quarks is density-dependent and decreases with increasing density; density-independent gaps or the presence of any unpaired quarks lead to cooling curves that cannot fit the observational data across a realistic range of stellar masses.

## Method

The authors construct hybrid star models using the Argonne V18+δv+UIX* hadronic equation of state (parameterized by Heiselberg and Hjorth-Jensen) matched via Maxwell construction to a nonlocal chiral quark model with a Gaussian formfactor that allows a 2SC–normal quark mixed phase. They compute cooling curves including quark direct Urca, quark modified Urca, quark bremsstrahlung, electron bremsstrahlung, massive gluon-photon decay, and quark pair formation and breaking processes, then compare the resulting surface temperatures to observed neutron star cooling data for different gravitational masses and pairing gap choices.

## Key numbers

- Critical mass for onset of quark core: \(M_c^{\rm quark} = 1.214~M_\odot\)
- Maximum hybrid star mass with 2SC mixed phase: \(1.793~M_\odot\)
- Threshold density for direct Urca process in hadronic model: \(n_c^{\rm DU} \simeq 5.19~n_0\) (\(M_c^{\rm DU} \simeq 1.839~M_\odot\))
- Large 2SC pairing gap: \(\Delta \sim 100\) MeV for u and d quarks of two colors
- X-gap range: \(\Delta_X \sim 10\) keV – 1 MeV
- For density-dependent X-gap ansatz: \(\Delta_c = 1.0\) MeV at \(\mu_c = 330\) MeV, falling to \(\Delta_X = 4.6\) keV at \(\mu_{\rm max} = 507\) MeV (center of maximum mass configuration)
- Mass interval fitting cooling data with density-dependent X-gap: \(M = 1.32\)–\(1.75~M_\odot\) for hadronic model I; \(M = 1.1\)–\(1.7~M_\odot\) for hadronic model II
- Density jump at hadron-quark boundary: from \(n_c^{\rm hadr} \simeq 0.44~{\rm fm}^{-3}\) to \(n_c^{\rm quark} \simeq 0.46~{\rm fm}^{-3}\)

## Context

This work extends the purely hadronic "nuclear medium cooling scenario" of Grigorian, Blaschke, and Voskresensky (2004) by adding color superconducting quark cores, and contrasts with earlier hybrid star cooling studies (Blaschke, Voskresensky, and collaborators) that assumed all quarks gapped with the same gap. The paper shows that the 2SC+X phase with a density-dependent residual gap can reproduce the cooling data over a mass range similar to that of the purely hadronic model, offering an alternative explanation to the hadronic scenario. The authors suggest that future population synthesis tests (comparing Log N–Log S distributions from the ROSAT catalogue) could distinguish between the hadronic and hybrid star cooling models.
