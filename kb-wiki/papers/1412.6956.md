# Non-Extensive Statistics, New Solution to the Cosmological Lithium Problem

> In the primordial Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN), only the lightest nuclides (D, $^3$He, $^4$He, and $^7$Li) were synthesized in appreciable quantities, and these relics provide us a unique window on the early universe. Currently, BBN simulations give acceptable agreement between theoretical and observed abundances of D and $^4$He, but it is still difficult to reconcile the predicted $^7$Li abundance with the observation for the Galactic halo stars. The BBN model overestimates the primordial $^7$Li abundance by about a factor of three, so called the cosmological lithium problem, a long-lasting pending issue in BBN. Great efforts have been paid in the past decades, however, the conventional nuclear physics seems unable to resolve such problem. It is well-known that the classical Maxwell-Boltzmann (MB) velocity distribution has been usually assumed for nuclei in the Big-Bang plasma. In this work, we have thoroughly investigated the impact of non-extensive Tsallis statistics (deviating from the MB) on thermonuclear reaction rates involved in standard models of BBN. It shows that the predicted primordial abundances of D, $^4$He, and $^7$Li agree very well with those observed ones by introducing a non-extensive parameter $q$. It is discovered that the velocities of nuclei in a hot Big-Bang plasma indeed violate the classical Maxwell-Boltzmann (MB) distribution in a very small deviation of about 6.3--8.2%. Thus, we have for the first time found a new solution to the cosmological lithium problem without introducing any mysterious theories. Furthermore, the implications of non-extensive statistics in other exotic high-temperature and density astrophysical environments should be explored, which might offer new insight into the nucleosynthesis of heavy elements.

# Non-Extensive Statistics, New Solution to the Cosmological Lithium Problem

## Key claim
By replacing the classical Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity distribution with a non-extensive Tsallis distribution (parameterized by a single value q), the predicted primordial abundances of D, 4He, and 7Li simultaneously agree with observations, resolving the cosmological lithium problem without invoking exotic particles or new physics. The solution requires that nuclear velocities in the Big-Bang plasma deviate from the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution by a small amount of 6.3–8.2%, corresponding to a non-extensive parameter in the range 1.063 ≤ q ≤ 1.082.

## Method
The authors calculated forward and reverse thermonuclear reaction rates for 17 key BBN reactions using the q-Gaussian velocity distribution from Tsallis statistics, with a coherent treatment of both forward and reverse rates (unlike prior work by Bertulani et al. (2013) which used the classical MB expression for reverse rates). These non-extensive rates were then incorporated into a standard BBN code with up-to-date cosmological parameters (baryon-to-photon ratio from WMAP9, neutron lifetime 885.7 s, three neutrino families) and a reaction network of 34 reactions involving nuclei with A ≤ 9. The predicted abundances of D, 3He, 4He, and 7Li were computed as functions of the non-extensive parameter q and compared to observed primordial abundances.

## Key numbers
- The non-extensive parameter range that reconciles D, 4He, and 7Li abundances with observations is 1.063 ≤ q ≤ 1.082, corresponding to a 6.3–8.2% deviation from the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.
- The baryon-to-photon ratio used is η = (6.203 ± 0.137) × 10^(-10) (from WMAP9).
- The neutron lifetime adopted is τ_n = 885.7 s.
- The number of light neutrino families is N_ν = 2.9840 ± 0.0082 (from CERN LEP), rounded to N_ν = 3.
- For the 2H(d,p)3H reaction with q < 1, the Tsallis reverse rate deviates from the MB reverse rate by about 200 orders of magnitude; for the 3He(n,p)3H reaction, the deviation is about 30 orders of magnitude.
- Even with q = 0.999, the Tsallis reverse rate of 2H(d,p)3H is about 10^10 times larger than the MB reverse rate at 0.2 GK.
- The 3He/H abundance agrees with observation at about the 1.6σ level, while D, 4He, and 7Li agree within 1σ.

## Context
This work extends the approach of Bertulani et al. (2013), who first applied Tsallis statistics to BBN but did not include non-extensive effects on reverse rates and could not solve the lithium problem. The present study provides a coherent treatment of forward and reverse rates and uses a full BBN network rather than a restricted set of reactions. The result contradicts earlier attempts to resolve the lithium problem through conventional nuclear physics (reviewed by Cyburt et al. (2008), Boyd et al., and others) and avoids the need for exotic mechanisms such as long-lived massive X particles (Kusakabe et al., 2013) or neutron-mirror-neutron oscillations (Coc et al., 2013). The work suggests that non-extensive statistics should be explored in other high-temperature, high-density astrophysical environments such as novae, X-ray bursts, and supernovae, potentially offering new insights into heavy-element nucleosynthesis.
