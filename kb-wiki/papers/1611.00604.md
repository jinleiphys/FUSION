# Three-body model for the two-neutron decay of $^{16}$Be

> While diproton decay was first theorized in 1960 and first measured in 2002, it was first observed only in 2012. The measurement of $^{14}$Be in coincidence with two neutrons suggests that $^{16}$Be does decay through the simultaneous emission of two strongly correlated neutrons. In this work, we construct a full three-body model of $^{16}$Be (as $^{14}$Be + n + n) in order to investigate its configuration in the continuum and in particular the structure of its ground state. In order to describe the three-body system, effective n-$^{14}$Be potentials were constructed, constrained by the experimental information on $^{15}$Be. The hyperspherical R-matrix method was used to solve the three-body scattering problem, and the resonance energy of $^{16}$Be was extracted from a phase shift analysis. In order to reproduce the experimental resonance energy of $^{16}$Be within this three-body model, a three-body interaction was needed. For extracting the width of the ground state of $^{16}$Be, we use the full width at half maximum of the derivative of the three-body phase shifts and the width of the three-body elastic scattering cross section. Our results confirm a dineutron structure for $^{16}$Be, dependent on the internal structure of the subsystem $^{15}$Be.

## Key claim

The authors confirm a dineutron structure for the ground state of $^{16}$Be, but this conclusion depends on the internal structure of the subsystem $^{15}$Be; specifically, the dineutron configuration is only supported when the ground state of $^{15}$Be is a $1d_{5/2}$ state, while an alternative $2s_{1/2}$ ground state for $^{15}$Be washes out the resonance signal entirely.

## Method

The authors constructed a full three-body model of $^{16}$Be as $^{14}$Be + n + n using the hyperspherical R-matrix method to solve the three-body scattering problem. Effective n-$^{14}$Be potentials were built with Woods-Saxon shapes constrained by experimental information on $^{15}$Be, with shell model calculations used to supplement limited experimental data. A three-body interaction was introduced to reproduce the experimental resonance energy of $^{16}$Be, and the width was extracted from both the full width at half maximum of the derivative of the three-body phase shifts and the three-body elastic scattering cross section.

## Key numbers

- The experimental ground state energy of $^{16}$Be is 1.35 MeV with a width of 0.8 MeV (measured with respect to the $^{14}$Be threshold)
- The lowest state in $^{15}$Be is an $l=2$ state at 1.8 MeV with a width of 575 $\pm$ 200 keV
- The three-body model with a $1d_{5/2}$ ground state for $^{15}$Be and a three-body force (model D3B) reproduces the $^{16}$Be ground state at 1.35 MeV
- Without the three-body force (model D), the $^{16}$Be ground state appears at 1.84 MeV
- Without the NN interaction (model DNN), the $^{16}$Be ground state shifts to 3.14 MeV
- The three-body wavefunction for model D3B is 37% $K=0$, $l_x=l_y=0$, 30% $K=2$, $l_x=l_y=0$, and 13% $K=4$, $l_x=l_y=0$
- Results are converged within 0.05 MeV by $K_{max}=28$

## Context

This work extends three-body models previously applied to two-proton decay (Grigorenko, 2001) and to the two-neutron decay of $^{26}$O (Grigorenko, 2013; Hagino, 2014) to the case of $^{16}$Be, which was first observed to undergo dineutron decay in an experiment at the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory (Spyrou, 2012). The study addresses controversy over whether the $^{16}$Be decay is truly correlated dineutron emission versus phase-space three-body decay (Spyrou, 2012 Comment and Response). The results enable future comparisons with experimental data on two-neutron angular correlations and provide a framework for interpreting other two-neutron emitters such as $^{26}$O and $^{24}$O.
