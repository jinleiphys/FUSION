# Analysing direct photon spectra and elliptic flow from heavy ion collision measurements at the top RHIC energy within the integrated hydrokinetic model

> The integrated HydroKinetic Model (iHKM) is applied to analyse the results of direct photon spectra and elliptic flow measurements in 200A GeV Au+Au collisions at RHIC for the three centrality bins. We detect the strong centrality dependence of photon elliptic flow as $v_2(p_T)$-coefficient increases towards peripheral collisions. The photon production in the model is accumulated from the different sources along with the process of relativistic heavy ion collision developing. Those include the primary hard photons from the parton collisions at very early stage of the process, the photons generated at the pre-thermal phase of matter evolution, then thermal photons at equilibrated quark-gluon stage together with radiation displaying a confinement and, finally, from the hadron gas phase. Along the way a hadronic medium evolution is treated in two distinct, in a sense opposite, approaches: chemically equilibrated and chemically frozen system expansion. We find that similar as it was found in iHKM for the LHC energies, a description of the direct photon spectra and elliptic flows is significantly improved if an additional portion of the photon radiation, that is associated with hadronization processes, is included into consideration.

## Key claim

The integrated HydroKinetic Model (iHKM) can describe direct photon spectra and elliptic flow at top RHIC energy (200A GeV Au+Au collisions) only if an additional, hypothetical photon radiation associated with the hadronization process is included; without this contribution, the model fails to reproduce the measured photon yields and flow simultaneously.

## Method

The authors applied the integrated HydroKinetic Model (iHKM) to 200A GeV Au+Au collisions at RHIC for three centrality bins (0-20%, 20-40%, 40-60%). Photon production was accumulated from five sources: prompt hard photons from parton collisions, pre-thermal phase photons, thermal photons from equilibrated quark-gluon plasma, photons from the hadronization surface layer, and hadron gas photons. The hadronic medium evolution was treated in two opposite approaches: chemically equilibrated (iHKM with hydrodynamic stage extended to T=100 MeV) and chemically frozen (original HKM with continuous particle generation after hadronization at T=165 MeV). The hadronization photon emission was modeled using a phenomenological thermal-like prescription with a single free parameter α = d_c γ_hadr = 0.04.

## Key numbers

- Collision energy: √s_NN = 200A GeV for Au+Au collisions at RHIC
- Initial time τ₀ = 0.1 fm/c; thermalization time τ_th = 1.0 fm/c; relaxation parameter τ_rel = 0.25 fm/c
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio η/S = 0.08 (minimal value)
- GLISSANDO initial state weight coefficient α = 0.18 (compared to 0.24 at LHC energies)
- Hadronization temperature T_h = 165 MeV (corresponding to energy density ε = 0.5 GeV/fm³)
- Hadronization photon emission parameter α = d_c γ_hadr = 0.04 (providing best description for 0-20% centrality)
- QCD scales for prompt photon calculation: Q_fact = Q_ren = Q_frag = 0.5 p_T
- Momentum range for photon spectra: 0.5 GeV/c < p_T < 6 GeV/c
- Prethermal photon emission time window: 0.1 fm/c < τ < 1.0 fm/c
- Strong centrality dependence of photon elliptic flow v₂(p_T) increasing towards peripheral collisions

## Context

This work extends the iHKM analysis previously applied to LHC energies (2.76 TeV and 5.02 TeV Pb+Pb collisions) by Naboka et al. (2018) to the top RHIC energy, addressing the "direct photon flow puzzle" observed by the PHENIX Collaboration. The model had already successfully described bulk hadron observables at RHIC (particle yields, spectra, elliptic flows, HBT radii) in prior work by the same group (ihkm-rhic). The key finding, that including hadronization-stage photon radiation significantly improves data description, mirrors the authors' earlier LHC results, suggesting a common mechanism across collision energies. This enables future systematic studies of photon production mechanisms across the full RHIC-to-LHC energy range.
