# A Relativistic Symmetry in Nuclei: Its origins and consequences

> We review the status of quasi-degenerate doublets in nuclei, called pseudospin doublets, which were discovered about thirty years ago and the origins of which have remained a mystery, until recently. We show that pseudospin doublets originate from an SU(2) symmetry of the Dirac Hamiltonian which occurs when the sum of the scalar and vector potentials is a constant. Furthermore, we survey the evidence that pseudospin symmetry is approximately conserved in nuclear spectra and eigenfunctions and in nucleon-nucleus scattering for a Dirac Hamiltonian with realistic nuclear scalar and vector potentials.

# A Relativistic Symmetry in Nuclei: Its origins and consequences

## Key claim
Pseudospin doublets in nuclei originate from an SU(2) symmetry of the Dirac Hamiltonian that occurs when the sum of the scalar and vector potentials is constant; however, this exact symmetry limit cannot be realized in nuclei because if V_S + V_V = constant, there are no Dirac bound valence states and hence nuclei cannot exist.

## Method
The paper reviews the relativistic origin of pseudospin symmetry by analyzing the Dirac Hamiltonian with external scalar and vector potentials, showing that the SU(2) pseudospin generators commute with the Hamiltonian when V_S + V_V is constant. The symmetry is then tested against realistic relativistic mean field (RMF) calculations and experimental data for spherical nuclei, particularly 208Pb, and applied to magnetic dipole transitions and nucleon-nucleus scattering.

## Key numbers
- Pseudospin binding energy splittings in 208Pb for the 0h9/2-1f7/2 doublet: 1.073 MeV (experimental) vs 2.575 MeV (RMF)
- Pseudospin binding energy splittings in 208Pb for the 0g7/2-1d5/2 doublet: 1.791 MeV (experimental) vs 4.333 MeV (RMF)
- Pseudospin binding energy splittings in 208Pb for the 1f5/2-2p3/2 doublet: -0.328 MeV (experimental) vs 0.697 MeV (RMF)
- Pseudospin binding energy splittings in 208Pb for the 1d3/2-2s1/2 doublet: 0.351 MeV (experimental) vs 1.247 MeV (RMF)
- For 800 MeV proton scattering on 208Pb, pseudospin breaking is at most of order 10%, a factor of three lower than the spin breaking

## Context
This work resolves a mystery that persisted for thirty years since the discovery of pseudospin doublets by Arima (1969) and Hecht and Adler (1969). The relativistic interpretation extends earlier non-relativistic treatments by Bohr (1982) and explains features of deformed nuclei including superdeformation and identical bands studied by Stephens (1990) and Twin (1991). The connection to QCD sum rules via Furnstahl (1997) links pseudospin symmetry to chiral symmetry breaking, suggesting that relativistic effective field theories may be needed to capture qualitative nuclear physics aspects missed by non-relativistic theories.
