# On the eikonal approach to nuclear diffraction dissociation

> The study of nuclear breakup of halo and weakly bound particles has been one of the key ingredients in the understanding of exotic nuclei during the last thirty years. One of the most used methods to analyse data, in particular absolute breakup cross sections, has been the eikonal approximation. Here we revise critically the formalisms used for calculating the diffraction dissociation part of nuclear breakup and show that there is a formula that can be applied to breakup on any target, while a most commonly used formula must be restricted to light targets as it contains also the effect of Coulomb breakup calculated to first order in the sudden approximation which is well known for not being accurate.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a commonly used eikonal formula for nuclear diffraction dissociation cross sections (Eq. 44 in the text) actually includes Coulomb breakup calculated in the sudden approximation, which is known to be inaccurate; therefore this formula should be restricted to light targets, while an alternative formula (Eq. 33) correctly isolates the nuclear elastic breakup part and can be applied to any target.

## Method

The authors derive and compare two eikonal expressions for the nuclear elastic breakup cross section of a halo or weakly bound projectile, starting from a three-body eikonal amplitude that includes both neutron-target and core-target interactions. They separate the amplitude into a nuclear part (depending on the neutron-target profile function) and a recoil part (depending on the core-target interaction), then show that the recoil amplitude reduces to the standard dipole Coulomb breakup amplitude in the sudden limit. By tracing the derivation steps, they demonstrate that one widely used formula (Eq. 44) automatically includes this sudden-approximation Coulomb contribution, while the other (Eq. 33) does not.

## Key numbers

- The adiabaticity parameter is defined as \(\bar{\omega} = (\varepsilon_k - \varepsilon_0) b_c / (\hbar v)\), and the sudden limit corresponds to \(\bar{\omega} \to 0\) where \(\bar{\omega} K_1(\bar{\omega}) \to 1\) and \(\bar{\omega} K_0(\bar{\omega}) \to 0\).
- The classical momentum transfer for Coulomb interaction is \(\mathcal{K}_\perp(b_c) = 2 Z_P Z_T e^2 / (\hbar v b_c^2) \mathbf{b}_c\).
- The mass ratios are \(\beta_1 = m_n / m_p\) and \(\beta_2 = m_c / m_p = 1 - \beta_1\), where \(m_p = m_n + m_c\).

## Context

This work critically revises the eikonal formalism used for nuclear breakup of halo nuclei over the last thirty years, following the approaches of Yabana (1992), Esbensen (1995), and Bertsch and Gupta (1998). It extends the method of Bonaccorso and Brink (1991, 1992) for consistently calculating nuclear and Coulomb breakup, and it clarifies that the commonly used formula (Eq. 44 from those earlier works) is unreliable for heavy targets because it includes Coulomb breakup in the sudden approximation. The paper enables more accurate separation of nuclear and Coulomb contributions in future analyses of breakup data, particularly for heavy targets, and points to the methods of Alvarez-Rodriguez (2004) and Rav (2015) for proper treatment of Coulomb breakup to all orders.
