# Thermodynamically Anomalous Regions and Possible New Signals of Mixed Phase Formation

> Using an advanced version of the hadron resonance gas model we have found indications for irregularities in data for hadrons produced in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. These include an abrupt change of the effective number of degrees of freedom, a change of the slope of the ratio of lambda hyperons to protons at laboratory energies 8.6--11.6 AGeV, as well as highly correlated plateaus in the collision-energy dependence of the entropy per baryon, total pion number per baryon, and thermal pion number per baryon at laboratory energies 6.9-11.6 AGeV. Also, we observe a sharp peak in the dimensionless trace anomaly at a laboratory energy of 11.6 AGeV. On the basis of the generalized shock-adiabat model we demonstrate that these observations give evidence for the anomalous thermodynamic properties of the mixed phase at its boundary to the quark-gluon plasma. We argue that the trace-anomaly peak and the local minimum of the generalized specific volume observed at a laboratory energy of 11.6 AGeV provide a signal for the formation of a mixed phase between the quark-gluon plasma and the hadron phase. This naturally explains the change of slope in the energy dependence of the yield of lambda hyperons per proton at a laboratory energy of 8.6 GeV.

## Key claim

Using an advanced hadron resonance gas model, the authors find evidence for thermodynamic irregularities in heavy-ion collision data that they interpret as signals of QGP-hadron mixed phase formation, but they acknowledge that the trace-anomaly peak at 11.6 AGeV rests on only one data point and that the error bars for entropy per baryon and total pion number are still too large to draw definite conclusions.

## Method

The authors employed a multicomponent hadron resonance gas model (HRGM) with hard-core repulsion (different radii for pions, kaons, other mesons, and baryons) to fit 111 independent hadron-multiplicity ratios measured at 14 collision energies from AGS, SPS, and RHIC, extracting thermodynamic quantities at chemical freeze-out. They then interpreted the observed irregularities using the generalized shock-adiabat model for central nuclear collisions, which predicts plateaus in entropy per baryon and pion multiplicities when matter exhibits anomalous thermodynamic properties at a phase transition.

## Key numbers

- Global fit quality: chi-squared/dof = 1.16 for 111 independent multiplicity ratios at 14 values of center-of-mass energy sqrt(s_NN) = 2.7, 3.3, 3.8, 4.3, 4.9, 6.3, 7.6, 8.8, 9.2, 12, 17, 62.4, 130, 200 GeV
- At sqrt(s_NN) = 4.9 GeV (E_lab = 11.6 AGeV): temperature T = 127.0 MeV, baryonic chemical potential mu_B = 530.8 MeV, chi-squared/dof = 0.5/5 = 0.11
- At sqrt(s_NN) = 4.3 GeV (E_lab = 8.6 AGeV): temperature T = 95.3 MeV, mu_B = 586.7 MeV, chi-squared/dof = 0.35/2 = 0.175
- Entropy density increases by a factor of four in the laboratory energy range E_lab = 8.6 - 11.6 GeV, while temperature changes from 95 to 127 MeV and mu_B drops from 586 to 531 MeV
- Ratio s/T^3 increases by 70% for a 30% increase in laboratory energy
- Low-energy plateaus (E_lab = 6.9-11.6 GeV) with M=3: R_{s/rho_B} = 11.31, R_{rho_pi^{th}/rho_B} = 0.461, R_{rho_pi^{tot}/rho_B} = 0.892, chi-squared/dof = 0.531
- High-energy plateaus (E_lab = 30-40 GeV) with M=3: chi-squared/dof = 0.34
- Hard-core radii from best global fit: R_b = 0.2 fm, R_m = 0.4 fm, R_pi = 0.1 fm, R_K = 0.38 fm

## Context

This work extends the hadron resonance gas model studies of Bugaev et al. (2013) and Oliinychenko et al. (2012) that achieved the most successful description of hadronic multiplicities, and applies the generalized shock-adiabat model developed by Bugaev et al. (1989, 1990, 1991) to interpret irregularities as signals of mixed-phase formation. The observed plateaus at lower energies (6.9-11.6 AGeV) are predicted by the shock-adiabat model, while a second set of plateaus at higher energies (30-44 GeV) coincides with the region where the Kink, Strangeness Horn, and Step signals of deconfinement onset were previously claimed by Gazdzicki et al. (2010) and Rafelski et al. The results suggest that further measurements near sqrt(s_NN) = 4.9 GeV could confirm the trace-anomaly peak and that the high-energy plateaus might indicate a second phase transition.
