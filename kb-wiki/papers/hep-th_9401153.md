# Nonperturbative Light-Front QCD

> In this work the determination of low-energy bound states in Quantum Chromodynamics is recast so that it is linked to a weak-coupling problem. This allows one to approach the solution with the same techniques which solve Quantum Electrodynamics: namely, a combination of weak-coupling diagrams and many-body quantum mechanics. The key to eliminating necessarily nonperturbative effects is the use of a bare Hamiltonian in which quarks and gluons have nonzero constituent masses rather than the zero masses of the current picture. The use of constituent masses cuts off the growth of the running coupling constant and makes it possible that the running coupling never leaves the perturbative domain. For stabilization purposes an artificial potential is added to the Hamiltonian, but with a coefficient that vanishes at the physical value of the coupling constant. The weak-coupling approach potentially reconciles the simplicity of the Constituent Quark Model with the complexities of Quantum Chromodynamics. The penalty for achieving this perturbative picture is the necessity of formulating the dynamics of QCD in light-front coordinates and of dealing with the complexities of renormalization which such a formulation entails. We describe the renormalization process first using a qualitative phase space cell analysis, and we then set up a precise similarity renormalization scheme with cutoffs on constituent momenta and exhibit calculations to second order. We outline further computations that remain to be carried out. There is an initial nonperturbative but nonrelativistic calculation of the hadronic masses that determines the artificial potential, with binding energies required to be fourth order in the coupling as in QED. Next there is a calculation of the leading radiative corrections to these masses, which requires our renormalization program. Then the real struggle of finding the right extensions to perturbation theory to study the strong-coupling behavior of bound states can begin.

## Key claim

The authors argue that low-energy bound states in Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) can be recast as a weak-coupling problem by using a bare Hamiltonian with nonzero constituent masses for quarks and gluons, an artificial confining potential that vanishes at the physical coupling, and light-front coordinates, provided that the running coupling never leaves the perturbative domain. The claim is conditional: the approach works only if the coupling constant remains small at all scales, which requires the constituent masses to cut off the infrared growth of the running coupling.

## Method

The authors construct a light-front QCD Hamiltonian with massive quarks and gluons, impose momentum-space cutoffs that eliminate zero longitudinal momentum modes (producing a trivial vacuum), and add an artificial stabilizing and confining potential whose coefficient vanishes at the physical coupling value. They then apply a similarity renormalization scheme to transform the cutoff Hamiltonian into a band-diagonal form, removing cutoff dependence to second order in perturbation theory, and identify counterterms for longitudinal infrared divergences that may encode confinement and chiral symmetry breaking.

## Key numbers

- The binding energies of hadrons are required to be fourth order in the coupling constant, analogous to the situation in Quantum Electrodynamics.
- The longitudinal momentum cutoff is set such that each constituent satisfies \(k^+_i > \epsilon\), with \(\epsilon\) a small positive constant, limiting the maximum number of constituents in any state to \(P^+/\epsilon\).
- The physical coupling \(g_s\) is defined as the running coupling at the hadron mass scale, with the running scale given by \(\Lambda \approx \Lambda_{QCD} e^{c/g_\Lambda^2}\) for small running \(g_\Lambda\), where \(c\) is a positive constant.

## Context

This work extends the light-front approach to QCD pioneered by Dirac (1949) and the infinite momentum frame ideas of Feynman and Weinberg (1966), aiming to reconcile the Constituent Quark Model with QCD. It builds on the similarity renormalization scheme developed by Glazek and Wilson (1993) and on earlier light-front QCD calculations by the authors and colleagues (Zhang 1993, Harindranath 1993, Perry 1993). The paper enables a program where perturbative calculations to second order produce an effective Hamiltonian resembling the Constituent Quark Model, with higher-order computations intended to approach the true strong-coupling behavior of QCD bound states.
