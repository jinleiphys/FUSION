# Some Calculations for Cold Fusion Superheavy Elements

> The Q value and optimal exciting energy of the hypothetical superheavy nuclei in cold fusion reaction are calculated with relativistic mean field model and semiemperical shell model mass equation(SSME) and the validity of the two models is tested. The fusion barriers are also calculated with two different models and reasonable results are obtained. The calculations can give useful references for the experiments in the superheavy nuclei synthesized in cold fusion reactions.

# Some Calculations for Cold Fusion Superheavy Elements

## Key claim

The relativistic mean field (RMF) model and the semiempirical shell model mass equation (SSME) can provide useful estimates of Q values, optimal excitation energies, and fusion barriers for cold fusion reactions leading to superheavy nuclei, but the RMF model is more accurate than SSME for binding energies, and both models become less reliable as the atomic number of the compound nucleus increases, particularly in the neutron-rich region.

## Method

The authors calculated ground-state Q values using the RMF model with NL3 force parameters and the SSME, taking measured binding energies for target and projectile nuclei from the literature. Fusion barriers were computed using two approaches: a simple Coulomb cutoff formula from Smolanczuk (2001) and a new empirical formula fitted to GLDM data on 170 fusion reactions by Moustabchir et al. (2004). Optimal excitation energies were obtained by approximating the equilibrium-point binding energy with the ground-state binding energy, then taking the difference between the binding energies of the compound nucleus and its two-neutron evaporation residue.

## Key numbers

- For the reaction $^{48}Ca + ^{208}Pb \rightarrow ^{255}No$, the RMF Q value is 156.8 MeV, the SSME Q value is 162.5 MeV, and the reference value from Smolanczuk (2001) is 153.6 MeV.
- The RMF binding energy for $^{256}No$ is 1895.7 MeV, which differs from the experimental value of 1898.6 MeV by -2.9 MeV; the SSME binding energy for the same nucleus is 1890.1 MeV, differing by -8.5 MeV.
- For $^{258}Rf$, the RMF binding energy is 1903.0 MeV (experimental: 1904.6 MeV, difference -1.6 MeV), while the SSME binding energy is 1896.1 MeV (difference -8.5 MeV).
- The fusion barrier for $^{48}Ca + ^{208}Pb$ is 178.7 MeV from the Coulomb cutoff formula (eq. 1) and 192.4 MeV from the GLDM-based formula (eq. 2), a difference of 13.7 MeV.
- For $^{68}Zn + ^{208}Pb$, the two fusion barrier formulas give nearly identical values: 275.6 MeV (eq. 1) and 275.8 MeV (eq. 2), a difference of 0.2 MeV.
- The optimal excitation energies from RMF and SSME models differ from experimental data by about 1 to 4 MeV across the reactions studied.

## Context

This work extends the quantum mechanical fragmentation theory (QMFT) approach of Gupta et al. (2003) by providing numerical predictions for Q values, fusion barriers, and optimal excitation energies for the target-projectile combinations listed in that prior work. The calculations test the RMF model and SSME against the macroscopic-microscopic model of Smolanczuk (2001) for cold fusion reactions leading to superheavy elements. The results are intended to guide future experiments at laboratories such as Dubna, Berkeley, RIKEN, and GANIL, though the authors note that more experimental data are needed to develop more powerful models for accurate Q-value calculations in the neutron-rich region.
