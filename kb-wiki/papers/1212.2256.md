# Could quantum decoherence and measurement be deterministic phenomena?

> The apparent random outcome of a quantum measurement is conjectured to be fundamentally determined by the microscopic state of the macroscopic measurement apparatus. The apparatus state thus plays the role of a hidden variable which, in contrast with variables characterizing the measured microscopic system, is shown to lead to a violation of Bell's inequalities and to agree with standard quantum mechanics. An explicit realization of this interpretation is proposed for a primitive model of measurement apparatus inspired by Mott: in the case of an alpha-particle spherical-wave detection in a cloud chamber, the direction of the observed linear track is conjectured to be determined by the position of the atoms of the gas filling the chamber. Using a stationary-state coupled-channel Born expansion, a reduction of the spherical wave function is shown to be necessary to compensate the flux loss due to scattering on the chamber atoms. Being highly non local, this interpretation of quantum mechanics is finally argued to open the way to faster-than-light information transfer.

## Key claim

The paper conjectures that the apparent random outcome of a quantum measurement is fundamentally determined by the microscopic state of the macroscopic measurement apparatus (apparatus hidden variables), rather than by hidden variables characterizing the measured microscopic system; this interpretation is shown to violate Bell's inequalities in agreement with standard quantum mechanics while remaining deterministic, but the authors argue it opens the way to faster-than-light information transfer.

## Method

The authors first demonstrate mathematically that apparatus hidden variables (characterizing the measurement devices rather than the measured particles) reproduce the standard quantum mechanical correlation for the singlet spin state, thereby violating Bell's inequalities. They then revisit Mott's (1929) model of alpha-particle spherical-wave detection in a cloud chamber, using a stationary-state coupled-channel Born expansion to show that the presence of gas atoms reduces the spherical wave function amplitude by a factor |C|^2 per obstacle, and that for N aligned obstacles the spherical-wave flux scales as |C|^(2N), which they interpret as a model for wave-function reduction.

## Key numbers

- The spherical-wave probability flux without obstacle is F_without = 4π (ħk/m_α) ≡ 4π v_α, where v_α is the alpha-particle velocity.
- The reduction factor for the spherical wave in the presence of one obstacle is |C|^2 = [1 + (1/2)∫_0^π dθ sinθ |I_0(θ)|^2 + (1/2)(v'_α/v_α)∫_0^π dθ sinθ |I_1(θ)|^2]^{-1}, with |C|^2 < 1.
- For N successive aligned obstacles, the spherical-wave flux is approximately F_spherical ≈ |C|^(2N) F_without.
- The typical thermal agitation energy of molecules in a cloud chamber (a few meV) is much lower than the typical alpha-particle kinetic energy E_α (a few MeV).

## Context

This work extends the deterministic interpretation of quantum measurement proposed by Gaspard (2001) and Gaspard and Nagaoka (1999), who suggested that the internal state of a macroscopic system determines which outcome of decoherence occurs. It revisits Mott's (1929) model of alpha-particle tracks in a cloud chamber, which explained the appearance of linear tracks from a spherical wave, and reinterprets it as a deterministic mechanism where the positions of gas atoms determine the observed track direction. The paper also relates to decoherence approaches by Zurek (1981, 1991, 2002) but goes further by attempting to explain particular outcomes rather than just the emergence of statistical mixtures. The authors argue that if correct, this interpretation enables faster-than-light communication, contradicting the standard view that quantum randomness preserves special relativity (Eberhard, 1978).
