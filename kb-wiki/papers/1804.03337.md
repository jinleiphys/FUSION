# On the origin of asymmetric fission of actinides

> Nuclear fission of heavy (actinide) nuclei results predominantly in asymmetric mass-splits. Without quantum shells, which can give extra binding energy to these mass-asymmetric shapes, the nuclei would fission symmetrically. The strongest shell effects are in spherical nuclei, so naturally the spherical "doubly-magic" ${^{132}}$Sn nucleus (${Z=50}$ protons), was expected to play a major role. However, a systematic study of fission has shown that the heavy fragments are distributed around ${Z=52}$ to 56, indicating that ${^{132}}$Sn is not the only driver. Reconciling the strong spherical shell effects at ${Z=50}$ with the different ${Z}$ values of fission fragments observed in nature has been a longstanding puzzle. Here, we show that the final mass asymmetry of the fragments is also determined by the extra stability of octupole (pear-shaped) deformations which have been recently confirmed experimentally around $^{144}$Ba (${Z=56}$), one of very few nuclei with shell-stabilized octupole deformation. Using a modern quantum many-body model of superfluid fission dynamics, we found that heavy fission fragments are produced predominantly with ${52-56}$ protons, associated with significant octupole deformation acquired on the way to fission. These octupole shapes favouring asymmetric fission are induced by deformed shells at ${Z=52}$ and 56. In contrast, spherical "magic" nuclei are very resistant to octupole deformation, which hinders their production as fission fragments. These findings may explain surprising observations of asymmetric fission of lighter than lead nuclei.

## Key claim
The final mass asymmetry in actinide fission is determined not primarily by spherical shell effects at Z=50 (doubly-magic ¹³²Sn), but by the extra stability of octupole (pear-shaped) deformations acquired by fragments on the way to fission, induced by deformed shells at Z=52 and 56. Spherical magic nuclei are resistant to octupole deformation, which hinders their production as fission fragments.

## Method
The authors performed time-dependent energy-density functional calculations including nuclear superfluidity, following the dynamical evolution of all single-particle wavefunctions (196 neutrons and 126 protons) for ²⁴⁰Pu and seven other actinides (²³⁰Th, ²³⁴,²³⁶U, ²⁴⁶Cm, ²⁵⁰Cf, ²⁵⁸Fm). They started from configurations in the asymmetric fission valley and tracked the evolution until scission, computing fragment proton and neutron numbers, deformations, and total kinetic energies.

## Key numbers
- Heavy fragments in ²⁴⁰Pu fission are produced with an average of ⟨Z⟩ ≈ 53.8 protons and ⟨N⟩ ≈ 85.2 neutrons
- Scission in ²⁴⁰Pu occurs at t ≈ 20 zs (1 zs = 10⁻²¹ s), with times up to 90 zs depending on initial configuration
- Heavy fragment at scission in ²⁴⁰Pu has quadrupole deformation β₂ ≈ 0.16 and octupole deformation β₃ ≈ 0.22; light fragment has β₂ ≈ 0.64 and β₃ ≈ 0.4
- Heavy fragments are produced predominantly with Z = 52–56 protons across all studied actinides
- Sn fragments from ²⁵⁸Fm symmetric fission have octupole moments 2 to 3 times smaller at scission than fragments with Z ≈ 55

## Context
This work resolves a longstanding puzzle (Schmidt 2018) where spherical shell effects at Z=50 (¹³²Sn) could not explain the observed heavy fragment distribution around Z=52–56 from systematic studies (Schmidt 2000). The findings extend recent experimental confirmation of octupole deformation in ¹⁴⁴Ba (Bucher 2016, 2017; Gaffney 2013) and may explain surprising observations of asymmetric fission in lighter-than-lead nuclei (Andreyev 2010). The results enable improved predictions of fission properties for exotic nuclei relevant to astrophysical r-process nucleosynthesis.
