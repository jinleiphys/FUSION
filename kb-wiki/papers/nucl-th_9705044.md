# A statistical theory of the mean field

> A statistical theory of the mean field is developed. It is based on the proposition that the mean field can be obtained as an energy average. Moreover, it is assumed that the matrix elements of the residual interaction, obtained after the average interaction is removed, are random with the average value of zero. With these two assumptions one obtains explicit expressions for the mean field and the fluctuation away from the average. The fluctuation is expanded in terms of more and more complex excitations. Using the randomness of the matrix elements one can then obtain formulas for the contribution to the error from each class of complex excitations and a general condition for the convergence of the expansion is derived. It is to be emphasized that no conditions on the nature of the system being studied are made. Making some simplifying assumptions a schematic model is developed. This model is applied to the problem of nuclear matter. The model yields a measure of the strength of the effective interaction. It turns out to be three orders of magnitude less than that calculated using a potential which gives a binding energy of about -7 MeV/nucleon demonstrating the strong damping of the interaction strength induced by the averaging process.

## Key claim

The mean field can be obtained as an energy average, and the matrix elements of the residual interaction (after removing the average interaction) are random with an average value of zero; under these assumptions, the mean field energy is always lower than the Hartree-Fock energy, and the effective interaction strength in nuclear matter is reduced by about three orders of magnitude compared to the bare interaction.

## Method

The authors develop a statistical formalism in which the Hilbert space is partitioned into a P-space (low-momentum physics) and a Q-space (rapidly varying components). An energy average is performed using a smoothing function characterized by a parameter epsilon, which shifts the poles of excited states away from the ground state energy. The corrections to the mean field energy are expanded in terms of increasingly complex particle-hole excitations (2p-2h, 4p-4h, etc.), and the randomness of the Q-space matrix elements is used to derive expressions for the fluctuations (errors) from each class of excitations. A schematic model is then applied to infinite nuclear matter using a projection operator that restricts the P-space to the Hartree-Fock determinant.

## Key numbers

* The effective interaction strength in nuclear matter is three orders of magnitude less than that calculated using a potential which gives a binding energy of about -7 MeV/nucleon.
* The binding energy of about -7 MeV/nucleon is cited as the value obtained from a potential used for comparison.
* The parameter epsilon is introduced as a free parameter characterizing the energy averaging width, with no specific numerical value assigned in the text.
* The expansion parameter for the series of corrections is identified through the relation between successive fluctuation terms, but no explicit numerical value is given.

## Context

This work extends the statistical theory of nuclear reactions developed by Feshbach (1992) and Kawai, Kerman, and McVoy (1975) to the problem of the mean field in many-body systems. It provides a formal justification for why the mean field approximation works across diverse physical systems, regardless of the nature of the interparticle interaction. The approach differs from the customary method based on the Pauli principle and dynamical correlations, offering instead a statistical framework that yields a spectroscopic factor consistent with independent estimates from Fantoni (no year given in text). The formalism enables future calculations of the mean field and its fluctuations for any system, with the nuclear matter application serving as an exploratory test case.
