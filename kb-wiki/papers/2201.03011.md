# Quantum statistical fluctuation of energy and its novel pseudo-gauge dependence

> We discuss the quantum statistical fluctuations of energy in subsystems of hot relativistic gas for both spin-zero and spin half particles. We explicitly show the system size dependence of the quantum statistical fluctuation of energy. Our results show that with decreasing system size quantum statistical fluctuations increase substantially. As the consistency of the framework, we also argue that the quantum statistical fluctuations give rise to the known result for statistical fluctuation of energy in the canonical ensemble if we consider the size of the subsystem to be sufficiently large. For a spin-half particle quantum fluctuations show some interesting novel features. We show that within a small sub-system quantum statistical fluctuation of energy for spin half particles depends on the various pseudo-gauge choices of the energy-momentum tensor. Interestingly, for sufficiently large subsystems quantum fluctuations obtained for different pseudo-gauge choices converge and we recover the canonical-ensemble formula known for statistical fluctuations of energy. Our calculation is very general and can be applied to any branch of physics whenever one deals with a thermal system. As a practical application, we argue that our results can be used to determine a coarse-graining scale to introduce the concept of classical energy density or fluid element relevant for the strongly interacting matter, in particular for small systems produced in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper claims that quantum statistical fluctuations of energy in a small subsystem of a hot relativistic gas depend on the pseudo-gauge choice of the energy-momentum tensor for spin-half particles, but only when the subsystem is small; for sufficiently large subsystems, fluctuations from different pseudo-gauge choices converge to the canonical-ensemble result. The hedge is that this pseudo-gauge dependence is a novel feature that appears specifically for fermions and only at small scales.

## Method

The author derives analytical formulas for the variance of the spatially smeared time-time component of the energy-momentum tensor operator within a Gaussian subsystem of a thermal system, using quantum field theory for both real scalar fields and Dirac fields. For spin-half particles, four different pseudo-gauge choices of the energy-momentum tensor are considered: canonical, Belinfante-Rosenfeld (BR), de Groot-van Leeuwen-van Weert (GLW), and Hilgevoord-Wouthuysen (HW). Thermal averaging is performed using Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac distribution functions, and the large-volume limit is taken by representing the Dirac delta function as a Gaussian limit.

## Key numbers

* For a scalar field in the large-volume limit, the volume-scaled normalized fluctuation satisfies \(V_a \sigma_{n,\phi}^2 = T^2 C_{V,\phi} / \varepsilon^2\), where \(V_a = a^3 (2\pi)^{3/2}\) is the effective volume of the Gaussian subsystem.
* For spin-half particles in the large-volume limit, the fluctuations from all pseudo-gauge choices converge to \(V_a \sigma_{n,\psi,Can}^2 = T^2 C_{V,\psi} / \varepsilon_{Can}^2\), matching the canonical-ensemble result.
* Numerical estimates are presented for temperatures in the range 100 MeV to 400 MeV and particle masses from 0 MeV to 1000 MeV, with a total degeneracy factor of 40 (typical for three-flavor QCD matter).

## Context

This work extends the author's prior studies (Das 2021) on quantum statistical fluctuations in hot relativistic gases by explicitly analyzing pseudo-gauge dependence for spin-half particles. It builds on the framework of spatially smeared operators introduced by Chen (2018) and the concept of pseudo-gauge transformations discussed in the context of hyperon polarization in heavy-ion collisions by Hehl (1976), Speranza (2020), and Florkowski (2018). The results enable determination of a coarse-graining scale for defining classical energy density or fluid elements in small systems produced in heavy-ion collisions, particularly at RHIC and LHC.
