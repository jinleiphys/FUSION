# Breakup effects in the $^{16}{\rm C}+p$ and $^{16}{\rm C}+d$ reactions

> We analyze the $^{16}{\rm C}+p$ and $^{16}{\rm C}+d$ reactions within the four- and five-body Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (CDCC) method. The $^{16}$C nucleus is described by a $^{14}{\rm C}+n+n$ configuration in hyperspherical coordinates. This description reproduces fairly well several $^{16}$C low-lying states. First we analyze the $2^+\rightarrow 0^+$ $E2$ transition amplitude, which confirms that an effective charge must be introduced to reproduce the experimental value. Then, proton and deuteron elastic and inelastic scattering are investigated by including $^{16}$C pseudostates, which simulate the $^{14}{\rm C}+n+n$ continuum. In $^{16}{\rm C}+d$, the deuteron breakup is taken into account with $p+n$ two-body pseudostates. A fair agreement with experiment is obtained without any fitting parameter. Breakup effects are in general small, but improve the agreement with experiment.

## Key claim

The paper shows that a three-body (14C+n+n) description of 16C, combined with the Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (CDCC) method, can reproduce experimental elastic and inelastic scattering data for 16C+p and 16C+d reactions without any adjustable parameters, but the agreement is qualified by the need for an effective charge to reproduce the B(E2) transition probability and by some underestimation of the 16C+d elastic cross section in the 30°–60° angular range.

## Method

The authors describe 16C as a three-body 14C+n+n system using hyperspherical coordinates and solve the three-body Schrödinger equation with the Lagrange-mesh method, using the Minnesota potential for n+n and a potential from Horiuchi and Suzuki (2006) for 14C+n. For the scattering calculations, they apply the four-body (3+1) CDCC method for 16C+p and the five-body (3+2) CDCC method for 16C+d, where the continuum of 16C is represented by pseudostates and the deuteron breakup is included via p+n pseudostates; the coupled-channel equations are solved using the R-matrix method with Lagrange meshes.

## Key numbers

- The 16C beam energy is 24 MeV/nucleon, corresponding to center-of-mass energies of 22.59 MeV for 16C+p and 42.67 MeV for 16C+d.
- For the 16C+p elastic scattering, full CDCC calculations with the Koning-Delaroche (KD) potential agree with experimental data except in the range θ ∼ 30°–40°, where the model slightly underestimates the data.
- For the 16C+d elastic scattering, the full five-body calculations involve a total of 504 channels and reproduce the shape of the data reasonably well, but the magnitude is underestimated in the angular range 30°–60°.
- The B(E2,2+→0+) transition probability in 16C requires an effective charge of δe/e ≈ 0.3 ± 0.1 to reproduce the experimental value of 4.34^{+2.27}_{-1.85} e²·fm⁴ from Jiao et al. (2020).
- The three-body model gives proton and neutron matrix elements of Mp = 0.173 fm² and Mn = 14.93 fm² for the E2 transition.
- For 16C+p inelastic scattering at 33 MeV/nucleon, the calculated cross sections up to about 2° are nearly double those reported in Khoa and Oertzen (2019).

## Context

This work extends the CDCC method to three-body projectiles (16C as 14C+n+n) scattering on both a structureless proton target and a two-body deuteron target, following the formalism previously applied to 11Li+d scattering by Descouvemont (2020). The calculations complement experimental data from Jiao et al. (2020) for 16C+p and 16C+d elastic and inelastic scattering, and confirm earlier findings by Horiuchi and Suzuki (2006) that a three-body model of 16C requires an effective charge to reproduce the B(E2) value. The results enable future studies of breakup effects in reactions involving weakly-bound neutron-rich nuclei and highlight the need for proper optical potentials and data at larger scattering angles to further constrain the models.
