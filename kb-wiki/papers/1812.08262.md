# Iterative approaches to the self-consistent nuclear energy density functional problem. Heavy ball dynamics and potential preconditioning

> Large-scale applications of energy density functional (EDF) methods depend on fast and reliable algorithms to solve the associated non-linear self-consistency problem. When dealing with large single-particle variational spaces, existing solvers can become very slow, and their performance dependent on manual fine-tuning of numerical parameters. In addition, convergence can sensitively depend on particularities of the EDF's parametrisation under consideration. Using the widely-used Skyrme EDF as an example, we investigate the impact of the parametrisation of the EDF, both in terms of the operator structures present and the size of coupling constants, on the convergence of numerical solvers. We focus on two aspects of the self-consistency cycle, which are the diagonalisation of a fixed single-particle Hamiltonian on one hand and the evolution of the mean-field densities and potentials on the other. Throughout the article we use a coordinate-space representation, for which the behaviour of algorithms can be straightforwardly analysed. We propose two algorithmic improvements that are easily implementable in existing solvers, heavy-ball dynamics and potential preconditioning. We demonstrate that these methods can be made virtually parameter-free, requiring no manual fine-tuning to achieve near-optimal performance except for isolated cases. The combination of both methods decreases substantially the CPU time required to obtain converged results. The improvements are illustrated for the MOCCa code that solves the self-consistent HFB problem in a 3d coordinate space representation for parametrisations of the standard Skyrme EDF at next-to-leading order in gradients and its extension to next-to-next-to-leading order.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that two algorithmic improvements, heavy-ball dynamics for the diagonalisation subproblem and potential preconditioning for the self-consistent field subproblem, can substantially reduce CPU time for solving the self-consistent nuclear energy density functional problem, and can be made virtually parameter-free requiring no manual fine-tuning except for isolated cases, though the effectiveness depends on the operator structure and coupling constant sizes of the specific Skyrme EDF parametrisation.

## Method

The authors analyse the convergence behaviour of the self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) problem in a 3D coordinate-space representation using Lagrange mesh derivatives, focusing separately on the diagonalisation of the fixed single-particle Hamiltonian and the evolution of mean-field densities and potentials between iterations. They implement heavy-ball dynamics (a momentum-based gradient descent variant) for the partial diagonalisation of the single-particle Hamiltonian, and potential preconditioning (a transformation of the potential-vector update) for the self-consistent field iterations, testing both on the MOCCa code for Skyrme EDFs at next-to-leading order (NLO) and next-to-next-to-leading order (N2LO) in gradients.

## Key numbers

- The coordinate-space mesh uses a typical choice of \(N_X = N_Y = N_Z = 40\), giving \(N_m = 128000\) mesh points and \(2 N_m = 256000\) single-particle wave functions for each nucleon species.
- The effective HF basis size is denoted \(\Omega\), which is much smaller than the full basis dimension of \(2 N_b\) (where \(N_b\) is the number of single-particle orbitals), enabling the two-basis method to limit the HFB problem to a small number of states.
- The combination of heavy-ball dynamics and potential preconditioning "decreases substantially the CPU time required to obtain converged results" (no specific numerical speedup factor is given in the truncated text).

## Context

This work extends prior studies of self-consistent mean-field solvers for Skyrme EDFs, particularly the EV8 code (Bonche 2005) and the MOCCa code (Ryssens 2015), which can fail to converge efficiently for extended N2LO functionals (Becker 2017). The proposed methods are designed to be independent of the choice of EDF or numerical representation, enabling systematic large-scale calculations across the nuclear chart without case-by-case parameter tuning, and are applicable to other frameworks such as those using plane-wave bases (van Dalen 2014) or finite-range interactions.
