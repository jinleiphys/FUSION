# Nuclear ground-state masses and deformations: FRDM(2012)

> We tabulate the atomic mass excesses and binding energies, ground-state shell-plus-pairing corrections, ground-state microscopic corrections, and nuclear ground-state deformations of 9318 nuclei ranging from $^{16}$O to $A=339$. The calculations are based on the finite-range droplet macroscopic model and the folded-Yukawa single-particle microscopic model. Relative to our FRDM(1992) mass table in {\sc Atomic Data and Nuclear Data Tables} [{\bf 59} 185 (1995)], the results are obtained in the same model, but with considerably improved treatment of deformation and fewer of the approximations that were necessary earlier, due to limitations in computer power. The more accurate execution of the model and the more extensive and more accurate experimental mass data base now available allows us to determine one additional macroscopic-model parameter, the density-symmetry coefficient $L$, which was not varied in the previous calculation, but set to zero. Because we now realize that the FRDM is inaccurate for some highly deformed shapes occurring in fission, because some effects are derived in terms of perturbations around a sphere, we only adjust its macroscopic parameters to ground-state masses. The values of ten constants are determined directly from an optimization to fit ground-state masses of 2149 nuclei ranging from $^{16}$O to $^{265}_{106}$Sg and $^{264}_{108}$Hs. The error of the mass model is 0.5595~MeV. We also provide masses in the FRLDM, which in the more accurate treatments now has an error of 0.6618 MeV. But in contrast to the FRDM, it is suitable for studies of fission and has been extensively so applied elsewhere, with FRLDM(2002) constants. The FRLDM(2012) fits 31 fission barrier heights from $^{70}$Se to $^{252}$Cf with a root-mean-square deviation of 1.052 MeV.

## Key claim

The FRDM(2012) mass model achieves an overall error of 0.5595 MeV when fit to 2149 ground-state masses, but this error is only 0.3549 MeV for nuclei with neutron number N ≥ 65, and the model is explicitly stated to be inaccurate for highly deformed shapes occurring in fission because some effects are derived as perturbations around a sphere.

## Method

The authors performed macroscopic-microscopic calculations using the finite-range droplet model (FRDM) for the macroscopic energy and the folded-Yukawa single-particle potential for microscopic shell-plus-pairing corrections. Ten macroscopic-model constants were determined by a maximum-likelihood optimization to fit ground-state masses of 2149 nuclei from ¹⁶O to ²⁶⁵Sg and ²⁶⁴Hs, with the density-symmetry coefficient L now treated as a free parameter (previously set to zero). Ground-state deformations were minimized over a five-dimensional shape space including axial asymmetry, octupole, and hexadecapole degrees of freedom, with considerably fewer approximations than in the FRDM(1992) calculation.

## Key numbers

- Overall model error (σ_th) for FRDM(2012): 0.5595 MeV for the full region of 2149 nuclei included in the adjustment
- FRDM(2012) error for nuclei with N ≥ 65: 0.3549 MeV
- FRLDM(2012) overall error: 0.6618 MeV; for N ≥ 65: 0.5181 MeV
- FRLDM(2012) fits 31 fission barrier heights from ⁷⁰Se to ²⁵²Cf with root-mean-square deviation of 1.052 MeV
- The mass table covers 9318 nuclei from ¹⁶O to A = 339
- For 529 new masses in AME2003 not known when FRDM(1992) constants were determined, the error was only 0.462 MeV (compared to 0.669 MeV with respect to the AME1989 database used for FRDM(1992))

## Context

This work extends the authors' FRDM(1992) mass table (Moller, 1995) by using the same model but with considerably improved treatment of deformation, fewer approximations, and a more extensive experimental mass database that allowed determination of the additional density-symmetry coefficient L. The FRDM(2012) is presented as the preferred model for ground-state masses, while the FRLDM(2012) is recommended for fission studies because the FRDM is inaccurate for highly deformed shapes. The results enable improved predictions for masses of nuclei far from stability, superheavy element decay properties, and various nuclear-structure quantities such as separation energies and beta-decay properties that will be published separately.
