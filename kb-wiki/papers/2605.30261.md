# Qubit-efficient variational algorithm for nuclear structure

> In this work, we compare three qubit-mapping strategies to study the structure of the nuclear ground state within the shell model description employing the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) approach. Although the initial point for different mappings is a Hamiltonian matrix in many-body particle basis or Slater determinant (SD) basis, the structure of the trial wavefunction and resource counts are different for each mapping. These three mappings are tested for a mid $p$-shell nucleus $^{10}$B and compared the quantum resources required to find the ground state for each mapping. Further, we extend the qubit-efficient mapping to study the ground state of one more mid $p$-shell nucleus $^{12}$C. We run circuits up to 26-qubits representing their ground states on a noisy simulator (IBM's FakeFez backend) and quantum hardware ($ibm\_fez$). The best post-error mitigated results from the hardware for $^{10}$B ground state is obtained following SD to qubit mapping with a percent error of 0.21 \%. The percent errors for the same state following cSD and pnSD mapping are 3.37 and 8.88 \%, respectively. On the other hand, following the cSD mapping, the post-error mitigated ground state energy of $^{12}$C is 6.82 \% away from the exact result. We further evaluate the fidelity of the VQE wavefunctions obtained from hardware with respect to the shell model wavefunctions for the cSD mapping. This cSD mapping can be useful for scaling the VQE algorithm for complex nuclei across different mass regions in terms of qubit efficiency.

## Key claim

The compact Slater determinant (cSD) mapping reduces qubit requirements for nuclear shell model VQE calculations at the cost of more variational parameters, but the SD mapping achieves the highest hardware accuracy (0.21% error) for the ground state of 10B despite requiring more qubits, provided the Hamiltonian can be grouped into only four commuting Pauli groups.

## Method

The authors compared three qubit-mapping strategies for the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) applied to nuclear shell model calculations: Slater determinant (SD) mapping where each many-particle state maps to one qubit; proton-neutron Slater determinant (pnSD) mapping where proton and neutron SDs are mapped separately; and compact Slater determinant (cSD) mapping where the Hamiltonian matrix is padded to the nearest power-of-two dimension and converted to Pauli strings. For each mapping, they constructed trial wavefunctions with different circuit structures, performed VQE optimization using COBYLA and SLSQP optimizers on a noiseless simulator, then transpiled the optimized circuits for execution on IBM's FakeFez noisy simulator and the ibm_fez quantum hardware, applying zero-noise extrapolation (ZNE) for error mitigation.

## Key numbers

- For 10B ground state (3+), SD mapping on ibm_fez hardware achieved post-error mitigated percent error of 0.21% relative to the exact shell model reference energy of -41.504 MeV
- For 10B ground state, cSD mapping on ibm_fez hardware achieved post-error mitigated percent error of 3.37% relative to the exact result
- For 10B ground state, pnSD mapping on ibm_fez hardware achieved post-error mitigated percent error of 8.88% relative to the exact result
- For 12C ground state (0+), cSD mapping on ibm_fez hardware achieved post-error mitigated percent error of 6.82% relative to the exact shell model reference energy of -71.045 MeV
- The SD mapping for 10B required 26 qubits, 25 parameters, 451 Pauli terms grouped into 4 commuting groups, with circuit depth 102 (noiseless) and 211 (hardware)
- The cSD mapping for 10B required 5 qubits, 35 parameters, 528 Pauli terms grouped into 122 commuting groups, with circuit depth 21 (noiseless) and 61 (hardware)
- The cSD mapping for 12C required 6 qubits, 66 parameters, 2072 Pauli terms grouped into 365 commuting groups, with circuit depth 34 (noiseless) and 97 (hardware)
- Wavefunction fidelity from ibm_fez hardware for cSD mapping was F = 0.942 for 10B ground state and F = 0.915 for 12C ground state (10000 shots, no error mitigation)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous VQE approach (Sarma and Stevenson, 2026) that used many-body configuration-to-qubit mapping with low-depth circuits but required more qubits, by introducing two new mapping strategies (pnSD and cSD) that reduce qubit counts. The cSD mapping enables study of 12C, which would require 51 qubits under SD mapping or 30 qubits under pnSD mapping, placing those approaches beyond typical classical simulation capabilities. The results demonstrate that the cSD mapping with hardware-efficient ansatzes can scale VQE calculations to more complex nuclei across different mass regions, though the SD mapping remains advantageous for hardware execution time due to its minimal number of commuting Pauli groups.
