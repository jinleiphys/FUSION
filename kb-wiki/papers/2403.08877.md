# Collapsar disk outflows I: Viscous hydrodynamic evolution in axisymmetry

> We investigate mass ejection from accretion disks formed during the collapse of rapidly-rotating Wolf-Rayet stars. The neutrino-cooled, black hole (BH) accretion disk system that forms at the center of the star -- and the ensuing outflows -- provide the conditions for these systems to be candidate $r$-process element production sites and potential progenitors of broad-lined Type Ic (Ic-BL) supernovae. Here we present global, long-term axisymmetric hydrodynamic simulations of collapsar disks that include angular momentum transport through shear viscosity, neutrino emission and absorption, a 19-isotope nuclear reaction network and nuclear statistical equilibrium solver, a pseudo-Newtonian BH with mass and spin modified by accreted matter, and self-gravity. Starting from a stellar profile collapsed in spherical symmetry, our models capture disk formation self-consistently, and are evolved until after the shock wave -- driven by disk winds -- reaches the surface of the star. None of our models achieve sufficient neutronization to eject significant amounts of $r$-process elements (detailed nucleosynthesis calculations will follow in a companion paper). Sufficient $^{56}$Ni is produced to power a typical type Ic-BL supernova light curve, but the average asymptotic velocity is a factor $\sim 2-3$ times too slow to account for the typical line widths in type Ic-BL supernova spectra. The gap in neutrino emission between BH formation and shocked disk formation, and the magnitude of the subsequent peak in emission, would be observable diagnostics of the internal conditions of the progenitor in a galactic collapsar. Periodic oscillations of the shocked disk prior to its expansion are also a potential observable through their impact on the the neutrino and gravitational wave signals.

## Key claim

The authors find that none of their axisymmetric collapsar disk models achieve sufficient neutronization to eject significant amounts of r-process elements, and while enough 56Ni is produced to power a typical Type Ic-BL supernova light curve, the average asymptotic outflow velocity is a factor of approximately 2-3 times too slow to account for the typical line widths observed in Type Ic-BL supernova spectra.

## Method

The authors perform global, long-term axisymmetric hydrodynamic simulations of collapsar disks using the FLASH code, starting from stellar profiles evolved to black hole formation with the spherically-symmetric GR1D code. The simulations include angular momentum transport via shear viscosity parameterized with the alpha prescription, neutrino emission and absorption via a three-species leakage scheme, a 19-isotope nuclear reaction network with nuclear statistical equilibrium solver, a spinning pseudo-Newtonian black hole potential with mass and spin updated by accreted matter, and self-gravity. Two Wolf-Rayet progenitor models (16TI and 35OC from Woosley 2006) are evolved with variations in viscosity parameter (alpha = 0.01, 0.03, 0.1) and equation of state (SFHo and DD2).

## Context

This work extends previous global collapsar simulations by Harikae (2009), Lopez-Camara (2009), Lindner (2010), Obergaulinger (2017), and Just (2022) by including self-consistent disk formation from a collapsed stellar profile, long-term evolution until shock breakout, and a 19-isotope nuclear network. The results contradict the hypothesis that collapsars are significant r-process production sites, at least for the progenitors and axisymmetric treatment considered here. The companion paper will present detailed nucleosynthesis calculations, and the predicted neutrino emission gap between black hole formation and shocked disk formation could serve as an observable diagnostic of progenitor internal conditions in a galactic collapsar.
