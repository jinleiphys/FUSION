# Higgs Boson Production from Black Holes at the LHC

> If the fundamental Planck scale is near a TeV, then TeV scale black holes should be produced in proton-proton collisions at the LHC where \sqrt{s} = 14 TeV. As the temperature of the black holes can be ~ 1 TeV we also expect production of Higgs bosons from them via Hawking radiation. This is a different production mode for the Higgs boson, which would normally be produced via direct pQCD parton fusion processes. In this paper we compare total cross sections and transverse momentum distributions dσ/dp_T for Higgs production from black holes at the LHC with those from direct parton fusion processes at next-to-next-to-leading order and next-to-leading order respectively. We find that the Higgs production from black holes can be larger or smaller than the direct pQCD production depending upon the Planck mass and black hole mass. We also find that dσ/dp_T of Higgs production from black holes increases as a function of p_T which is in sharp contrast with the pQCD predictions where dσ/dp_T decreases so we suggest that the measurement of an increase in dσ/dp_T as p_T increases for Higgs (or any other heavy particle) production can be a useful signature for black holes at the LHC.

# Higgs Boson Production from Black Holes at the LHC

## Key claim

If the fundamental Planck scale is near a TeV, then TeV-scale black holes produced in proton-proton collisions at the LHC (√s = 14 TeV) could emit Higgs bosons via Hawking radiation, and this production mode can be larger or smaller than direct perturbative QCD parton fusion processes depending on the values of the Planck mass and black hole mass. The transverse momentum distribution dσ/dp_T for Higgs production from black holes increases as a function of p_T (up to about 1 TeV), in sharp contrast to the decreasing pQCD predictions, providing a potential signature for black hole production.

## Method

The authors computed the total cross section and transverse momentum distribution for Higgs boson production from black holes at the LHC by multiplying the black hole production cross section (using parton distribution functions and the geometric cross section for black hole formation) with the number of Higgs bosons emitted from a single black hole via Hawking radiation, using the thermal emission formula with grey body factors. They compared these results with the direct pQCD Higgs production cross section at next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO) and the p_T differential cross section at next-to-leading order (NLO), using the effective Lagrangian approach in the large top-quark mass limit with CTEQ6M and MRST parton density sets.

## Key numbers

- LHC center-of-mass energy: √s = 14 TeV
- Black hole temperature: ~ 1 TeV
- Black hole masses considered: M_BH = 3, 4, 5, 6, 10 TeV
- Planck masses considered: M_P = 1, 2, 3 TeV
- Higgs mass range considered: 100 GeV/c^2 ≤ M_H ≤ 300 GeV/c^2 (with specific calculations for M_H = 120, 160, 200 GeV)
- Number of extra dimensions used: d = 4
- Black hole evaporation time used: t_f = 10^{-3} fm
- Top quark mass used: m_t = 173.4 GeV/c^2
- Fermi constant: G_F = 1.16639 GeV^{-2} = 4541.68 pb
- For M_P = 1 TeV and M_BH = 3, 4, 5 TeV, the p_T differential cross section for Higgs production from black hole emission is larger than that from direct NLO parton fusion processes when p_T ≥ 50 GeV

## Context

This work extends the study of TeV-scale black hole phenomenology at colliders, building on earlier calculations of black hole production cross sections by Dimopoulos and Landsberg (2001) and Giddings and Thomas (2002), and on Hawking radiation calculations by Kanti and March-Russell (2002). It compares the black hole production channel with the standard NNLO pQCD Higgs production calculations by Harlander and Kilgour (2002) and Ravindran, Smith, and van Neerven (2003). The paper enables experimental searches at the LHC to use the distinctive rising p_T distribution of heavy particles as a signature for black hole production, complementing existing Monte Carlo tools by Landsberg (2005) and Harris (2005) for detector simulations.
