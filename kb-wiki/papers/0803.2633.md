# Can the quark model be relativistic enough to include the parton model?

> Since quarks are regarded as the most fundamental particles which constitute hadrons that we observe in the real world, there are many theories about how many of them are needed and what quantum numbers they carry. Another important question is what keeps them inside the hadron, which is known to have space-time extension. Since they are relativistic objects, how would the hadron appear to observers in different Lorentz frames? The hadron moving with speed close to that of light appears as a collection of Feynman's partons. In other words, the same object looks differently to observers in two different frames, as Einstein's energy-momentum relation takes different forms for those observers. In order to explain this, it is necessary to construct a quantum bound-state picture valid in all Lorentz frames. It is noted that Paul A. M. Dirac studied this problem of constructing relativistic quantum mechanics beginning in 1927. It is noted further that he published major papers in this field in 1945, 1949, 1953, and in 1963. By combining these works by Dirac, it is possible to construct a Lorentz-covariant theory which can explain hadronic phenomena in the static and high-speed limits, as well as in between. It is shown also that this Lorentz-covariant bound-state picture can explain what we observe in high-energy laboratories, including the parton distribution function and the behavior of the proton form factor.

## Key claim

By combining four of Dirac's major papers (1927, 1945, 1949, 1963) into a single Lorentz-covariant harmonic oscillator formalism, the quark model and Feynman's parton model can be shown to be two different limiting cases of one covariant bound-state entity, valid for all values of momentum-to-mass ratio (p/m).

## Method

The authors construct a Lorentz-covariant quantum bound-state model using four-dimensional harmonic oscillator wave functions that satisfy Dirac's c-number time-energy uncertainty relation (no time-like excitations) and transform under Lorentz boosts as squeeze transformations in the light-cone coordinate system. They apply this covariant oscillator formalism to a two-quark system, defining space-time separation variables x = (x_a - x_b)/(2√2) and total momentum variables, then examine how the wave functions in both space-time and momentum-energy appear in different Lorentz frames.

## Key numbers

- The Lorentz boost transformation in light-cone coordinates takes the form u' = e^(η/2) u, v' = e^(-η/2) v, where the product uv = (1/2)(z² - t²) remains invariant
- The ground-state wave function under boost becomes ψ_η(z,t) = (1/π)^(1/2) exp{-1/4[e^(-η)(z+t)² + e^η(z-t)²]}
- The normal frequencies of the coupled oscillator system are ω₁ = e^η ω and ω₂ = e^(-η) ω, with ω = √(K/m)
- The coupling parameter η is given by exp(η) = √[(2A+C)/(2A-C)] for the case of two identical oscillators coupled by strength C

## Context

This review paper extends the earlier work of Yukawa (1953) on hadrons with space-time extension, Hofstadter and McAllister (1955) who measured the proton's finite charge distribution via electron-proton scattering, and Feynman (1969) who proposed the parton model for fast-moving hadrons. The authors connect Dirac's four separate contributions (1927, 1945, 1949, 1963) to construct a formalism that can explain both the static quark model and the high-energy parton picture, as well as intermediate phenomena such as the proton form factor. The work enables a unified understanding of how a hadron's localized probability distribution appears to observers in different Lorentz frames, from rest to ultra-relativistic speeds.
