# The quest for the neutrino mass spectrum

> This article provides a (semi-)popular introduction to the phenomenology of neutrino masses.

## Key claim

Neutrino oscillations and neutrinoless double beta decay together can determine the absolute neutrino mass scale and the mass hierarchy, but only if the solar neutrino deficit solution and the character of the hierarchy (direct or inverse) are first identified by oscillation experiments.

## Method

The authors review the phenomenology of neutrino masses by combining constraints from neutrino oscillation experiments (solar, atmospheric, and accelerator) with limits from neutrinoless double beta decay. They analyze three mass scenarios, hierarchical, degenerate, and inverse hierarchical, and show how the effective Majorana mass measurable in double beta decay relates to oscillation parameters such as mass-squared differences and mixing angles, using the results of their earlier work with A. Smirnov.

## Key numbers

- The most stringent limit on the effective Majorana mass from the Heidelberg–Moscow experiment is ⟨m⟩ < 0.35 eV.
- For the hierarchical scheme with small mixing MSW or vacuum solar solutions, the CHOOZ experiment implies ⟨m⟩ < 2 × 10⁻³ eV in the range favored by atmospheric neutrinos.
- For the large mixing MSW solution in the hierarchical scheme, ⟨m⟩ ≈ 10⁻³ eV is expected, with a day-night asymmetry of 0.07 at future oscillation experiments.
- For the inverse hierarchy with small mixing MSW solution, ⟨m⟩ = (5–8) × 10⁻² eV is predicted, testable by the 1-ton version of GENIUS.
- For the inverse hierarchy with large mixing MSW or vacuum oscillations, ⟨m⟩ < 8 × 10⁻² eV is expected.
- For degenerate schemes, a ΛCHDM cosmological model with Ω_m = 0.5 and h = 0.6 implies an overall mass scale of about 0.5 eV, yielding ⟨m⟩ = 0.2–0.5 eV for large mixing MSW or vacuum oscillations.
- Future satellite experiments MAP and Planck are estimated to be sensitive to Σ m_ν = 0.5–0.25 eV.
- For four-neutrino schemes, ⟨m⟩ can be as large as O(10⁻³) eV.

## Context

This review extends the analysis of Klapdor-Kleingrothaus, Päs, and Smirnov (1999) on the relation between neutrino oscillation parameters and the effective Majorana mass in double beta decay. It contrasts the hierarchical spectrum (motivated by simple see-saw models and analogies with the quark sector) with degenerate schemes (motivated by the possibility of neutrinos as hot dark matter) and inverse hierarchy. The paper enables future experiments, Super-Kamiokande, SNO, KAMLAND, MINOS, MINIBOONE, GENIUS, MAP, and Planck, to jointly reconstruct the neutrino mass spectrum and test unified theories beyond the Standard Model.
