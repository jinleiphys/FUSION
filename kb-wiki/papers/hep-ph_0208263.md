# Harmony of the Froissart Theorem in Fundamental Dynamics of Particles and Nuclei

> It has been shown that the great ancient Pythagorean ideas have found themselves in the latest researches in high energy elementary particles and nuclear physics. In this respect we concern and discuss the mathematical, physical and geometrical aspects of the famous Froissart theorem and in this way one establishes a link of this theorem to the mathematics and ideas elaborated in the Pythagorean school. A harmony of the Froissart theorem in fundamental dynamics of particles and nuclei has been displayed. We argue that a harmony of the Froissart theorem allow us to hear the new notes of {\it ``the music of the spheres"} just in the Pythagoreans sense.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the Froissart theorem, which bounds high-energy scattering cross sections, embodies Pythagorean ideas of harmony, measure, and number in its mathematical, physical, and geometrical structure, specifically by showing that the total cross section of two-body interaction is bounded by the area of a sphere whose radius is the effective range of the forces.

## Method

The author reviews the standard derivation of the Froissart theorem for elastic two-body scattering, using partial wave expansions, analyticity in the cosine of the scattering angle (Lehmann ellipse), Cauchy representations, and unitarity bounds on partial waves. He then generalizes the theorem to n-body forces by considering O(D)-invariant scattering amplitudes in a (3n-3)-dimensional space, using hyperspherical harmonics, Gegenbauer polynomials, and analogous analyticity assumptions to derive an upper bound on the total cross section for n-body interactions.

## Key numbers

* The two-body total cross section is bounded by \(\sigma_{ab}^{tot}(s) < 4\pi R_2^2(s)\), where the effective radius \(R_2(s) \sim \frac{9}{8 m_\pi} \ln (s/s_0)\) as \(s \rightarrow \infty\), with \(m_\pi\) the pion mass.
* The semi-major axis of the Lehmann ellipse for the imaginary part of the two-body amplitude is \(z_0(s) = 1 + \epsilon_M(s)\) with \(\epsilon_M(s) = t_0/(2\mathbf{q}^2)\) and \(t_0 = 4 m_\pi^2\).
* The partial wave expansion for two-body scattering converges for orbital momenta up to \(L = [\ln \tilde{P}_2(s) / \sqrt{2\epsilon(s)}]\), with \(\tilde{P}_2(s) \sim \tilde{c}_2 s^{9/4}\) at large \(s\).
* The generalized n-body total cross section is bounded by \(\sigma_n^{tot}(s) < S_{D-1} [R_n(s)]^{D-1}\), where \(D = 3n-3\) and \(S_{D-1}\) is the surface area of a unit \((D-1)\)-dimensional sphere.

## Context

This work extends the Froissart theorem (Froissart, 1961) from two-body scattering to n-body forces, building on analyticity results by Lehmann (1958), Martin, and Sommer, and on the Jost-Lehmann-Dyson representation. The author connects the bound to the holographic principle discussed in Planck-scale physics (references cited as [16]), and argues that the geometrical form of the bound (area of a sphere) differs from Froissart's original semiclassical picture of a disk. The generalization enables a unified treatment of multiparticle interactions within axiomatic quantum field theory.
