# The Fate of the Initial State Fluctuations in Heavy Ion Collisions. III The Second Act of Hydrodynamics

> Hydrodynamical description of the "Little Bang" in heavy ion collisions is surprisingly successful, mostly due to the very small viscosity of the Quark-Gluon plasma. In this paper we systematically study the propagation of small perturbations, also treated hydrodynamically. We start with a number of known techniques allowing for analytic calculation of the propagation of small perturbations on top of the expanding fireball. The simplest approximation is the "geometric acoustics", which substitutes the wave equation by mechanical equations for the propagating "phonons". Next we turn to the case in which variables can be separated, in which case one can obtain not only the eikonal phases but also amplitudes of the perturbation. Finally, we focus on the so called Gubser flow, a particular conformal analytic solution for the fireball expansion, on top of which one can derive closed equations for small perturbations. Perfect hydrodynamics allows all variables to be separated and all equations to be solved in terms of known special functions. We can thus collect the analytical expression for all the harmonics and reconstruct the complete Green function of the problem. In the viscous case the equations still allow for variable separation, but one of the equations has to be solved numerically. We still can collect all the harmonics and show real-time perturbation evolution, observing viscosity-induced changes in the spectra and the correlation functions of secondaries. We end up by comparing the calculated angular shape of the correlation function to the STAR experimental data, and find, for sufficiently large viscosity, a surprisingly good agreement.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the angular shape of two-particle correlation functions observed in heavy ion collisions can be reproduced by a viscous hydrodynamic treatment of small perturbations on top of an expanding fireball, provided the viscosity is sufficiently large. The authors hedge that their results capture qualitative behavior rather than producing accurate numbers directly comparable to experimental data, due to assumptions of central collisions, conformal equation of state, and small perturbations.

## Method

The authors study the propagation of small perturbations on top of the Gubser flow, a conformal analytic solution for fireball expansion with longitudinal and transverse flows. They derive linearized hydrodynamic equations for temperature perturbations around this background, achieving separation of variables in the inviscid case and solving analytically using associated Legendre polynomials and spherical harmonics. For the viscous case, they still separate variables but solve one equation numerically, then sum all harmonics to reconstruct the complete Green function and compute the real-time perturbation evolution.

## Key numbers

- Sound velocity is approximately 1/2 (c_s ~ 1/2) in natural units
- Freezeout time tau_FO ~ 2R, where R is nuclear size about 6 fm for Au nuclei
- Sound horizon H_s ~ R, meaning response at large angles of O(±1 radian) from the perturbation
- Speed of sound in the hadronic era is near-constant c_s ≈ 0.4
- For conformal EoS, speed of sound c_s = 1/√3
- The authors predict a minimum at m ~ 7 and maximum at m ~ 9 harmonics in the power spectrum
- A factor 2 matter compression leads to only 15% increase in wave speed

## Context

This paper is the third in a series by Staig and Shuryak studying the propagation of perturbations on top of the "Little Bang" fireball. It extends earlier work by Casalderrey-Solana and Shuryak (2005) on sound propagation in expanding fireballs, and the first paper of this series by Shuryak (2009) which introduced "sound circles" from point-like perturbations. The work builds on the Gubser flow solution by Gubser (2010) and the linearized perturbation equations by Gubser and Yarom (2010). The results enable comparison with experimental two-particle correlation data from STAR, and suggest that measuring three-point correlation functions could distinguish between "minimal" and "maximally coherent" scenarios of initial state fluctuations.
