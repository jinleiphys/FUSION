# Nambu's Nobel Prize, the $σ$ meson and the mass of visible matter

> The electroweak Higgs boson has been discovered in ongoing experiments at the LHC, leading to a mass of this particle of 126 GeV. This Higgs boson mediates the generation of mass for elementary particles, including the mass of elementary (current) quarks. These current-quark masses leave 98% of the mass of the atom unexplained. This large fraction is mediated by strong interaction, where instead of the Higgs boson the $σ$ meson is the mediating particle. Though already discovered in 1957 by Schwinger, the $σ$ meson has been integrated out in many theories of hadron properties because it had not been observed and was doubted to exist. With the observation of the $σ$ meson in recent experiments on Compton scattering by the nucleon at MAMI (Mainz) it has become timely to review the status of experimental and theoretical researches on this topic.

## Key claim

The sigma meson, not the Higgs boson, mediates the generation of approximately 98% of the mass of visible matter through strong interaction, with the Higgs field responsible for only the remaining 2% from current-quark masses. This claim depends on the interpretation that the sigma meson creates constituent-quark masses of 326 MeV in the chiral limit (with the Higgs field turned off), which sum to a nucleon mass near 1 GeV.

## Method

The paper reviews the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model and the linear sigma model (LσM) as effective theories of low-energy QCD, deriving constituent-quark masses from the Delbourgo-Scadron relation M = (2π/√N_c) f_π^cl. Experimental confirmation is obtained by comparing NJL-model predictions for nucleon magnetic moments and electromagnetic polarizabilities (extracted from Compton scattering data at MAMI in Mainz) with measured values, using the sigma meson as a t-channel exchange particle in the scattering process.

## Key numbers

- Current-quark masses: m_u^0 ~ 3-5 MeV, m_d^0 ~ 7-9 MeV (scale-dependent)
- Constituent-quark mass in chiral limit: M = 326 MeV, from f_π^cl = 89.8 MeV and the Delbourgo-Scadron relation
- Sigma meson mass in chiral limit: m_σ^cl = 2M = 652 MeV
- Sigma meson mass including explicit symmetry breaking: m_σ = 666 MeV, from m_σ^2 = (m_σ^cl)^2 + m_π^2
- Constituent-quark masses for magnetic moment calculation: m_u = 331 MeV, m_d = 335 MeV (derived from m_σ = 666 MeV with m_d - m_u ≈ 4 MeV)
- Predicted nucleon magnetic moments: μ_p^theor = 2.831, μ_n^theor = -1.875 (in nuclear magnetons)
- Experimental nucleon magnetic moments: μ_p^exp = 2.79285, μ_n^exp = -1.91304
- Discrepancy between predicted and experimental magnetic moments: Δμ_p = -0.038, Δμ_n = -0.038 (1.4% for proton, 2.0% for neutron)
- Electroweak Higgs boson mass: 126 GeV
- Electroweak vacuum expectation value: v = 246 GeV
- Pion decay constant in chiral limit: f_π^cl = 89.8 MeV
- Meson-quark coupling constant from NJL: g = 2π/√3 = 3.62
- Self-coupling constant: λ = 8π^2/3 = 26.3
- Mass parameter μ = 460 MeV

## Context

This review extends the work of Schwinger (1957), who first introduced the sigma meson, and the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model (1961), which provided the theoretical framework for spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking in strong interactions. The paper contrasts with lattice QCD approaches (Alvarez-Ruso et al., 2013) that explain nucleon mass through current-quark and gluon energies, arguing instead that the sigma meson provides a more appropriate effective description. The experimental confirmation via Compton scattering at MAMI (Galler et al., 2001; Wolf et al., 2001) and subsequent theoretical analysis (Schumacher, 2010, 2011, 2013) enables a unified understanding of mass generation where the sigma meson serves as the strong-interaction analog of the electroweak Higgs boson, with the formal equivalence between the LσM and the Glashow-Weinberg-Salam theory noted for the scalar sector.
