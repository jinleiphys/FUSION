# Hot QCD equations of state and relativistic heavy ion collisions

> We study two recently proposed equations of state (EOS) which are obtained from high temperature QCD, and show how they can be adapted to use them for making predictions for relativistic heavy ion collisions. The method involves extracting equilibrium distribution functions for quarks and gluons from the EOS, which in turn will allow a determination of the transport and other bulk properties of the quark gluon plasma. Simultaneously, the method also yields a quasi particle description of interacting quarks and gluons. The first EOS is perturbative in the QCD coupling constant and has contributions of $O(g^5)$. The second EOS is an improvement over the first, with contributions upto $ O(g^6 ln(\frac{1}{g}))$; it incorporates the nonperturbative hard thermal contributions. The interaction effects are shown to be captured entirely by the effective chemical potentials for the gluons and the quarks, in both the cases. The chemical potential is seen to be highly sensitive to the EOS. As an application, we determine the screening lengths which are, indeed the most important diagnostics for QGP. The screening lengths are seen to behave drastically differently depending on the EOS considered., and yield, therefore, a way to distinguish the two equations of state in heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The authors show that two perturbative equations of state for hot QCD (EOS1 up to O(g⁵) and EOS2 up to O(g⁶ ln(1/g))) can be mapped onto effective chemical potentials for quarks and gluons, but the resulting screening lengths behave drastically differently between the two EOS, providing a way to distinguish them in heavy ion collisions. The abstract's claim of "adapting" the EOS for predictions is qualified by the fact that the method works only for temperatures above 2T_c and that the extracted distribution functions depend on the renormalization scale, which must be traded for a dependence on T/T_c.

## Method

The authors extract equilibrium distribution functions for gluons and quarks from two perturbative high-temperature QCD equations of state (EOS1 up to O(g⁵) and EOS2 up to O(g⁶ ln(1/g))) by expanding the grand canonical partition function in a Taylor series in the dimensionless effective chemical potentials βμ_g and βμ_f up to third order. The interaction contributions ΔP_g and ΔP_f are isolated by subtracting the ideal gas parts from the total pressure, and the resulting cubic equations are solved for the effective chemical potentials. Using these distribution functions, the Debye screening mass is computed via semiclassical transport theory, and the screening length (inverse Debye mass) is compared for the two EOS and with lattice results.

## Key numbers

- For EOS1, the ratio R = P/(P_g^I + P_q^I) deviates from the ideal value 1 by 10–45 percent.
- For EOS2, the ratio R deviates from the ideal value 1 by only 2–8 percent.
- For EOS2, the effective gluon chemical potential βμ_g remains less than 0.1 in magnitude for temperatures from 2T_c to 13T_c.
- For EOS2, the effective quark chemical potential βμ_f has a maximum magnitude of approximately 0.25 at T = 2T_c, about a factor four smaller than the corresponding value from EOS1.
- For EOS2, the relative screening length R_{h/id} stays at zero between 2T_c and 12–13T_c, indicating perfect screening from the gluonic sector.
- For EOS1, the relative screening length R_{h/g} (gluonic contribution to total) is ≥ 0.7 throughout the temperature range.
- Lattice results give screening lengths of approximately 0.7 fm near T_c and dropping to approximately 0.4 fm near 2T_c; EOS1 results are 3–10 times higher than these lattice values.
- Transition temperatures used for comparison: T_c = 270 MeV (pure QCD), 203 MeV (N_f=2), 195 MeV (N_f=3).

## Context

This work extends the perturbative QCD equations of state derived by Arnold and Zhai (2000) and Zhai and Kastening (2000) for EOS1, and by Kajantie et al. (2003) for EOS2, by providing a method to extract distribution functions and compute transport properties. It contrasts with earlier semiclassical approaches by Blaizot and Iancu (2002) and Kelly et al. (1994) that used effective mean color fields, and with lattice-based studies by Allton et al. (2005) that extracted effective masses but not Debye masses. The results enable future work on viscosity, entropy density, and color response functions in heavy ion collisions, particularly at LHC energies where temperatures of 2–3T_c are expected.
