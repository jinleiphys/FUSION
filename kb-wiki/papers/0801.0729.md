# Effective Field Theory and Finite Density Systems

> This review gives an overview of effective field theory (EFT) as applied at finite density, with a focus on nuclear many-body systems. Uniform systems with short-range interactions illustrate the ingredients and virtues of many-body EFT and then the varied frontiers of EFT for finite nuclei and nuclear matter are surveyed.

# Effective Field Theory and Finite Density Systems

## Key claim

Effective field theory (EFT) provides a systematic, model-independent framework for nuclear many-body physics that can be improved order-by-order with theoretical error bars, but the practical realization of this program at finite density remains in its infancy, with multiple frontiers and open questions about power counting, regularization schemes, and the treatment of unnaturally large scattering lengths.

## Method

The authors review the application of EFT to finite-density nuclear systems by first illustrating the ingredients of many-body EFT using uniform systems with short-range interactions, including the construction of the most general Lagrangian consistent with symmetries, the declaration of a regularization and renormalization scheme (such as dimensional regularization with minimal subtraction or power divergence subtraction), and the establishment of a power counting based on ratios of physical scales. They then survey applications to finite nuclei and nuclear matter, including chiral EFT interactions as input to conventional many-body wave function methods, lattice calculations, perturbative chiral EFT calculations with softened interactions, and density functional theory cast in EFT form using effective actions.

## Context

This review extends the EFT framework for nuclear systems discussed in earlier reviews by Burgess (2007), Bernard and Meissner (2006), and Bedaque and van Kolck (2002) to finite-density applications. It surveys the frontiers of EFT-based calculations for many-body nuclei and nuclear matter, including the use of chiral EFT interactions with conventional many-body methods, lattice calculations, and density functional theory. The work enables systematic connections between QCD and nuclear properties, controlled extrapolations to regimes with limited data (such as neutron stars and rare isotopes), and the assessment of theoretical truncation errors in nuclear many-body calculations.
