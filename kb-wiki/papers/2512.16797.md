# Relativistic superfluid profiles near critical surfaces

> Landau's two-fluid model of superfluidity ceases to apply in regions where the condensate amplitude exhibits rapid spatial variation, such as vortex cores or in the vicinity of container walls. A recently proposed relativistic Gross-Pitaevskii-type framework treats the condensate as an independent scalar degree of freedom, enabling a controlled analysis of such regimes. We use it to construct stationary superflows close to the superfluid-normal phase boundary, and examine their stability. We obtain an exact expression for Landau's critical velocity and show that the standard Newtonian profiles (such as the near-vortex condensate depletion or the boundary-layer decay) persist unmodified in the relativistic setting. We further analyse a genuinely relativistic configuration in which an accelerated superfluid develops a phase boundary induced by Tolman temperature gradients.

## Key claim

The authors show that standard Newtonian superfluid profiles (such as condensate depletion near vortex cores and boundary-layer decay) persist unmodified in the relativistic setting, but they also identify that the naive Landau critical velocity obtained from maximizing the pressure is not the true stability boundary: superfluidity actually breaks down at a lower velocity v_c/√3, which represents the genuine Landau critical velocity in this model.

## Method

The authors employ a recently developed relativistic Gross-Pitaevskii-type framework (Mitra 2020, Buza 2024) that treats the condensate amplitude σ as an independent scalar degree of freedom, rather than fixing it algebraically by local thermodynamic variables as in Landau's two-fluid model. They construct stationary superflow configurations near the superfluid-normal phase boundary by imposing equilibrium conditions derived from maximizing a thermodynamic potential Φ = S + α⋆^I Q_I, which yields the conditions that u^μ/T is a Killing vector and μ/T is constant. The stability of these configurations is then analyzed by requiring that the information current E^μ be future-directed non-spacelike, which produces inequalities that determine the true critical velocity.

## Key numbers

- The condensate amplitude in uniform equilibrium is given by σ̄(T,μ,v) = √[(m₀(T_c − T) − μ²v²)/λ] when m₀T_c ≥ m₀T + μ²v², and zero otherwise, where T_c is the critical temperature and m₀, λ are positive constants.
- The naive critical velocity from pressure maximization is v_c = √[m₀(T_c − T)/μ²].
- The true Landau critical velocity, below which superflow is stable, is v_c/√3, meaning superfluidity breaks down at v = v_c/√3 rather than at v = v_c.
- The information current for gradient perturbations of σ is TE^μ_{∇σ̇} = (∇_νσ̇ ∇^νσ̇) u^μ/2 − u^ν ∇_νσ̇ ∇^μσ̇, which is always future-directed non-spacelike.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic Gross-Pitaevskii framework developed by Mitra (2020) and Buza (2024), which itself builds on the Son-Nicolis formalism (Son 2002, Nicolis 2011). The analysis of equilibrium and stability follows the same procedure as Gavassino (2022, 2023, 2025) for identifying configurations that serve as local late-time attractors. The results enable the study of genuinely relativistic configurations where accelerated superfluids develop phase boundaries induced by Tolman temperature gradients, going beyond the Newtonian profiles for vortex cores (Landau and Lifshitz) and boundary layers (Fetter and Walecka 1971) that are shown to persist unmodified in the relativistic setting.
