# Quantum Algebras in Nuclear Structure

> Quantum algebras are a mathematical tool which provides us with a class of symmetries wider than that of Lie algebras, which are contained in the former as a special case. After a self-contained introduction to the necessary mathematical tools ($q$-numbers, $q$-analysis, $q$-oscillators, $q$-algebras), the su$_q$(2) rotator model and its extensions, the construction of deformed exactly soluble models (Interacting Boson Model, Moszkowski model), the use of deformed bosons in the description of pairing correlations, and the symmetries of the anisotropic quantum harmonic oscillator with rational ratios of frequencies, which underly the structure of superdeformed and hyperdeformed nuclei, are discussed in some detail. A brief description of similar applications to molecular structure and an outlook are also given.

## Key claim

Quantum algebras provide a class of symmetries wider than that of Lie algebras, which are contained as a special case when the deformation parameter q is set to unity; the paper reviews whether these deformed symmetries can describe nuclear systems that lie outside the realm of ordinary Lie symmetries, but the review itself does not claim a single definitive experimental validation.

## Method

The paper presents a self-contained review of the mathematical tools of quantum algebras (q-numbers, q-analysis, q-oscillators, q-algebras) and then applies them to nuclear structure problems. It covers the su_q(2) rotator model and its extensions, deformed exactly soluble models (Interacting Boson Model, Moszkowski model), the use of deformed bosons for pairing correlations, and the symmetries of the anisotropic quantum harmonic oscillator with rational ratios of frequencies underlying superdeformed and hyperdeformed nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The q-number is defined as [x] = (q^x - q^{-x})/(q - q^{-1}), with examples [0]=0, [1]=1, [2]=q+q^{-1}, [3]=q^2+1+q^{-2} (Section 2).
- For q real (q=e^tau), the q-deformed harmonic oscillator energy eigenvalues are E(n) = (hbar omega/2) * sinh(tau(n+1/2))/sinh(tau/2) (Equation 10.11).
- For q a phase factor (q=e^{i tau}), the eigenvalues are E(n) = (hbar omega/2) * sin(tau(n+1/2))/sin(tau/2) (Equation 10.12).
- The Q-number is defined as [x]_Q = (Q^x - 1)/(Q - 1), with examples [0]_Q=0, [1]_Q=1, [2]_Q=Q+1, [3]_Q=Q^2+Q+1 (Section 6).
- For the Q-deformed oscillator, the commutation relation is [b, b^dagger] = Q^N (Equation 11.11).
- The generalized deformed oscillator structure function Phi(x) yields energy eigenvalues E(n) = (hbar omega/2)([n]+[n+1]) (Equation 12.11).
- For small tau, the q-oscillator energy expansion is E(n)/(hbar omega) = (n+1/2)(1 +/- tau^2/24) -/+ (tau^2/6)(n+1/2)^3 + ... (Equation 13.2).
- For small T, the Q-oscillator potential expansion includes terms up to x^6, with coefficients given in Equations 13.5 and 13.7.

## Context

This review extends the mathematical framework of quantum algebras (introduced by Drinfeld, Jimbo, and others) into nuclear structure physics, building on earlier work on q-deformed harmonic oscillators by Biedenharn, Macfarlane, and others. It enables future microscopic descriptions of rotational and vibrational nuclear spectra, pairing correlations, and the symmetries of superdeformed and hyperdeformed nuclear bands, while also noting analogous applications to molecular structure.
