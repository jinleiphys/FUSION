# Solar and Supernova Constraints on Cosmologically Interesting Neutrinos

> The sun and core-collapse supernovae produce neutrino spectra that are sensitive to the effects of masses and mixing. Current results from solar neutrino experiments provide perhaps our best evidence for such new neutrino physics, beyond the standard electroweak model. I discuss this evidence as well as the limited possibilities for more conventional explanations. If the resolution of the solar neutrino problem is $ν_e \to ν_μ$ oscillations, standard seesaw estimates of $m_{ν_τ}$ suggest a cosmologically interesting third-generation neutrino. I discuss recent nucleosynthesis arguments that lead to an important constraint on this possibility.

# Solar and Supernova Constraints on Cosmologically Interesting Neutrinos

## Key claim

If the solar neutrino problem is resolved by $\nu_e \to \nu_\mu$ oscillations via the MSW mechanism, standard seesaw estimates place the tau neutrino mass near or within the cosmologically interesting range (approximately 1 to a few eV), but this possibility may be severely constrained by r-process nucleosynthesis arguments that require the r-process to occur deep within Type II supernovae, where even modest $\nu_\tau$ mixing angles could destroy the conditions necessary for the r-process.

## Method

The paper reviews the current status of solar neutrino measurements from the $^{37}$Cl experiment, Kamiokande II/III, Superkamiokande, and the SAGE/GALLEX gallium experiments, comparing observed fluxes to standard solar model (SSM) predictions. It then examines the MSW matter-enhanced oscillation mechanism as a solution, deriving the survival probability via the Landau-Zener formalism, and explores the consequences for neutrino mass estimates using the seesaw mechanism. The author also investigates a nonstandard solar model alternative involving core mixing on $^3$He equilibration timescales, and reviews constraints from r-process nucleosynthesis in Type II supernovae, including the effects of neutrino interactions on the neutron-rich environment required for the r-process.

## Key numbers

- Observed $^8$B flux from Superkamiokande (306 days): $\phi(^8$B) = $2.44 \pm 0.06^{+0.05}_{-0.09} \cdot 10^6$ /cm$^2$s
- Corresponding $^{37}$Cl result: $2.55 \pm 0.25$ SNU, yielding $\phi(^8$B) $\lesssim (2.30 \pm 0.22 \pm 0.05) \cdot 10^6$ /cm$^2$s
- Best-fit MSW small mixing angle solution: $\sin^2 2\theta_v \sim 0.005$ and $\delta m^2 \sim 6 \cdot 10^{-6}$ eV$^2$
- SSM $^8$B neutrino flux has been significant only in the last $10^9$ years, with $\phi(^8$B) $\sim \phi_o e^{\tau/\tau_o}$, $\tau_o \sim 0.9$ Gy
- SSM $^3$He equilibrium abundance: $X^{eq}_3 \sim 7 \cdot 10^{-4} X_1 \, T^{-6}_7$
- $^3$He equilibration time: $\tau^{eq}_3 \sim {1 \over X_1} \, T^{-10}_7$
- 99% of $^3$He equilibrium reached at r $\sim 0.1 R_\odot$ after $\sim 5 \cdot 10^6$ years of SSM burning
- Neutrino temperatures in supernova: $T_{\nu_\mu, \bar \nu_\mu, \nu_\tau, \bar \nu_\tau} \sim 8$ MeV, $T_{\bar \nu_e} \sim 5$ MeV, $T_{\nu_e} \sim 4$ MeV
- r-process conditions require neutron density $\rho_n \sim 10^{20}$/cm$^3$, temperature $T \sim (1-3)\cdot 10^9$ K, timescale $t \sim 1$ sec
- SSM luminosity rise over solar lifetime: $\sim 44\%$
- Core temperature required by observed $^8$B depletion: $T_c \sim 0.96 T^{\mathrm{SSM}}_c$

## Context

This paper extends the long-standing solar neutrino problem first identified by the $^{37}$Cl experiment (Davis, 1960s-1990s) and later confirmed by Kamiokande (Hirata et al., 1989-1996), SAGE, and GALLEX. It builds on the MSW mechanism (Mikheyev, Smirnov, Wolfenstein, 1978-1986) as the favored particle physics solution, with the Landau-Zener survival probability derived by Bethe (1986), Haxton (1986), and Parke (1986). The work challenges the conventional argument that no nonstandard solar model can reproduce the observed flux pattern, identifying a specific class of solutions involving core mixing on $^3$He equilibration timescales, related to earlier concerns about the "solar spoon" overstability (Roxburgh, 1970s) and recent explorations by other authors (1997). The r-process discussion connects to the hot bubble model (Woosley et al., 1994) and the neutrino process nucleosynthesis (Woosley, Haxton et al., 1990; Domagatskii et al., 1990), enabling future constraints on tau neutrino properties from supernova observations and motivating further work on three-dimensional solar hydrodynamics and the SNO experiment.
