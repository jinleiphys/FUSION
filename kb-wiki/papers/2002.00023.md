# Consistency checks for two-body finite-volume matrix elements: II. Perturbative systems

> Using the general formalism presented in Refs. [1,2], we study the finite-volume effects for the $\mathbf{2}+\mathcal{J}\to\mathbf{2}$ matrix element of an external current coupled to a two-particle state of identical scalars with perturbative interactions. Working in a finite cubic volume with periodicity $L$, we derive a $1/L$ expansion of the matrix element through $\mathcal O(1/L^5)$ and find that it is governed by two universal current-dependent parameters, the scalar charge and the threshold two-particle form factor. We confirm the result through a numerical study of the general formalism and additionally through an independent perturbative calculation. We further demonstrate a consistency with the Feynman-Hellmann theorem, which can be used to relate the $1/L$ expansions of the ground-state energy and matrix element. The latter gives a simple insight into why the leading volume corrections to the matrix element have the same scaling as those in the energy, $1/L^3$, in contradiction to earlier work, which found a $1/L^2$ contribution to the matrix element. We show here that such a term arises at intermediate stages in the perturbative calculation, but cancels in the final result.

## Key claim

The paper derives a $1/L$ expansion of the finite-volume matrix element for a two-scalar ground state coupled to a scalar current, finding that the leading volume correction scales as $1/L^3$ rather than $1/L^2$ as claimed in earlier work (Detmold 2014), and shows that the $1/L^2$ term arises at intermediate stages but cancels in the final result.

## Method

The authors use the general finite-volume formalism of Briceno (2015) and Baroni (2018) for the $2 + \mathcal{J} \to 2$ matrix element, truncating to $S$-wave interactions in a periodic cubic volume with side length $L$. They expand the finite-volume quantization condition and matrix element relation in powers of $1/L$ through $\mathcal{O}(1/L^5)$, using the effective range expansion for the scattering amplitude and threshold expansions of finite-volume functions $F_{\text{pv}}$ and $G_{\text{pv}}$. The result is cross-checked through a numerical study of the all-orders formalism, an independent perturbative calculation, and consistency with the Feynman-Hellmann theorem.

## Key numbers

* The ground-state energy expansion: $E_0(L) = 2m + \frac{4\pi a}{m L^3} \left[ 1 - \mathcal{I} \frac{a}{\pi L} + (\mathcal{I}^2 - \mathcal{J}) \frac{a^2}{\pi^2 L^2} + \left( \frac{2\pi^4 r}{a} - \frac{\pi^4}{m^2 a^2} - (\mathcal{I}^3 - 3\mathcal{I}\mathcal{J} + \mathcal{K}) \right) \frac{a^3}{\pi^3 L^3} \right] + \mathcal{O}(1/L^7)$, where $a$ is the scattering length, $r$ the effective range, and $\mathcal{I} = -8.913633$, $\mathcal{J} = 16.532316$, $\mathcal{K} = 8.401924$.
* The matrix element expansion: $L^3 \langle E_0,L | \mathcal{J}(0) | E_0,L \rangle = \frac{g}{m} \left[ 1 - \frac{2\pi^4}{m^2 a^2} \left( (1 - m a \mathcal{T}) \frac{a^3}{\pi^3 L^3} - (1 - 2 m a \mathcal{T}) \mathcal{I} \frac{a^4}{\pi^4 L^4} + (1 - 3 m a \mathcal{T}) (\mathcal{I}^2 - \mathcal{J}) \frac{a^5}{\pi^5 L^5} \right) \right] + \mathcal{O}(1/L^6)$, where $g$ is the scalar charge and $\mathcal{T} \equiv m r + \frac{64 \pi m^2}{g} \mathcal{F}_0$ with $\mathcal{F}_0$ the threshold two-particle form factor.
* The geometric constants are $\mathcal{I} = -8.913633$, $\mathcal{J} = 16.532316$, $\mathcal{K} = 8.401924$, evaluated to high precision in Luscher (1986) and Beane (2007).

## Context

This work extends the finite-volume formalism for $2 + \mathcal{J} \to 2$ matrix elements developed by Briceno (2015) and Baroni (2018) to the perturbative threshold regime, providing a systematic $1/L$ expansion. It contradicts the earlier non-relativistic quantum mechanics result of Detmold (2014), which found a $1/L^2$ leading correction, and shows that such a term cancels in the final result. The expansion enables lattice QCD practitioners to extract the threshold two-particle form factor $\mathcal{F}_0$ from finite-volume matrix element calculations, and the Feynman-Hellmann theorem provides a consistency check relating the matrix element expansion to the well-known energy expansion from Luscher (1986), Beane (2007), and others.
