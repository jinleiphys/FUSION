# Towards exotic nuclei via binary reaction mechanism

> Assuming a binary reaction mechanism, the yield of isotopes near the heaviest $N=Z$ neutron-deficit nucleus $^{100}$Sn is studied with a microscopic transport model. The large influence of nuclear shell structure and isotope composition of the colliding nuclei on the production of exotic nuclei is demonstrated. It is shown that the reaction $^{54}$Fe+$^{106}$Cd seems to be most favourable for producing primary exotic Sn isotopes which may survive if the excitation energy in the entrance reaction channel is less than about 100 MeV. In the case of large differences in the charge (mass) numbers between entrance and exit channels the light fragment yield is essentially fed from the decay of excited primary heavier fragments. The existence of optimal energies for the production of some oxygen isotopes in the binary mechanism is demonstrated for the $^{32}$S+$^{197}$Au reaction.

## Key claim

The yield of exotic nuclei near the doubly-magic \(^{100}\)Sn depends strongly on the shell structure and isotope composition of the colliding nuclei, and the reaction \(^{54}\)Fe+\(^{106}\)Cd appears most favourable for producing primary exotic Sn isotopes, but these can only survive if the excitation energy in the entrance channel is less than about 100 MeV.

## Method

The authors used a microscopic transport model assuming a binary reaction mechanism, where the colliding nuclei form a dinuclear system that exchanges nucleons, energy, and angular momentum. The average dissipated energy and its partition between primary fragments were calculated by solving the equation for the single-particle density matrix in a \(\tau\)-approximation, and then the charge and mass distributions of primary fragments were obtained from a master equation for nucleon transfer probabilities. Secondary (observable) isotope distributions were estimated by applying a statistical decay model to every excited primary fragment.

## Key numbers

- The reaction \(^{54}\)Fe+\(^{106}\)Cd gives the largest primary yield of \(^{100}\)Sn among the reactions considered, including \(^{56}\)Fe+\(^{106}\)Cd, \(^{64}\)Zn+\(^{106}\)Cd, \(^{58}\)Ni+\(^{106}\)Cd, \(^{58}\)Ni+\(^{112}\)Sn, and \(^{40}\)Ca+\(^{106}\)Cd.
- Using a \(^{54}\)Fe target instead of \(^{56}\)Fe in the reaction with \(^{106}\)Cd increases the primary yield of neutron-deficit Sn isotopes by an order of magnitude.
- Primary neutron-deficit Sn isotopes can only survive de-excitation if the excitation energy is less than about 100 MeV.
- For the \(^{32}\)S+\(^{197}\)Au reaction, the feeding effect increases the yield of \(^{15}\)O by about two orders of magnitude compared to the primary yield.
- The optimal colliding energy for producing light exotic nuclei in binary reactions is between 15 and 25 MeV/A.
- The angular momentum window \(\Delta J\) for forming a particular primary isotope is 10–20 \(\hbar\) in the energy range 8–50 MeV/A.
- The interaction time \(t_{int}\) is about \(10^{-21}\) s, and the time step \(\Delta t\) used in the transition probability calculation is \(10^{-22}\) s.

## Context

This work extends the binary reaction mechanism approach, previously applied to deep inelastic transfer reactions at lower energies (e.g., as described by Schmidt (1994) and in the transport model of Adamian et al.), to the intermediate energy range (8–50 MeV/A) for producing exotic nuclei near \(^{100}\)Sn. The study contrasts with high-energy fragmentation methods used at GSI (where 7 events of \(^{100}\)Sn were observed in 277 hours) and at GANIL (where 11 nuclei were identified in 44 hours), showing that the binary mechanism can give higher production rates at intermediate energies. The results enable optimization of reaction partner and bombarding energy for producing specific exotic isotopes, and highlight the need for self-consistent treatment of dissipation, energy sharing, and nucleon transfer in future model development.
