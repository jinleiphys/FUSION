# Size of the thermal source in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> The dependence of the size of the thermal source on the centrality in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions is studied. The interaction region consists of a well defined thermalized core, and of an outer mantle where the production scales with the number of participants. The thermal source builds up in the region with the largest density of participants in the transverse plane. Particle production in the thermalized core is enhanced in comparison to the wounded nucleon model. The change of the degree of strangeness saturation with centrality is also discussed. We perform an estimate of high pT jet absorption finding that an increase of the absorption in the thermal core is compatible with the data.

# Size of the thermal source in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

The interaction region in Au+Au collisions at RHIC energies can be separated into a thermalized core (where particle production per participant pair is enhanced by 65% relative to p+p collisions) and an outer mantle (where production scales with the number of participants), with the thermalized core appearing only when the density of wounded nucleons in the transverse plane exceeds a threshold of 2 fm⁻² at √s = 200 GeV.

## Method

The author uses a Glauber Monte Carlo model with Woods-Saxon nuclear density profiles to calculate the density of wounded nucleons in the transverse plane, then defines a core-mantle boundary by a cutoff density d_cut. Particle production is modeled as the sum of a wounded-nucleon-scaled contribution from the mantle and an enhanced contribution from the core, with the enhancement parameter α fitted to charged particle pseudorapidity density data from BRAHMS, PHENIX, and PHOBOS. The same geometric decomposition is applied to strangeness production (using K⁻/π⁻ ratios from PHENIX) and to jet absorption, where the survival probability depends on the integrated density along the jet trajectory.

## Key numbers

- At √s = 200 GeV: cutoff density d_cut = 2 fm⁻² and enhancement parameter α = 0.65 (corresponding to 65% enhancement per participant pair in the core)
- At √s = 130 GeV: cutoff density d_cut = 2.2 fm⁻² and enhancement parameter α = 0.5
- The thermalized core appears at centralities corresponding to N_part ≈ 40 participants
- For central collisions at zero impact parameter, the thermal core contributes up to 95% of total particle production
- The K⁻/π⁻ ratio in the thermal core is taken as 0.16 from a statistical hadronization model fitted to central collisions
- For jet absorption: absorption coefficient k = 0.12 fm with formation length τ = 0.2 fm reproduces PHENIX data when absorption is proportional to wounded nucleon density; with increased density in the core (factor 1+α), k = 0.09 fm and τ = 0.2 fm reproduces data across all centralities
- Nuclear density parameters: a = 0.535 fm, R_A = 1.12 A^(1/3) fm; cross sections σ = 42 mb at √s = 200 GeV and 41 mb at √s = 130 GeV

## Context

This work extends the wounded nucleon model (Bialas, Bleszynski, Czyz, 1976) by introducing a geometric core-mantle separation to explain the observed deviation from N_part scaling in Au+Au collisions at RHIC, previously noted by the PHOBOS collaboration (Back, 2004) and others. The approach contrasts with single-thermal-source models that assume uniform freeze-out parameters across the entire interaction region (Xu, Rafelski; STAR collaboration). The core-mantle picture enables a unified description of charged particle multiplicity, strangeness saturation, and jet suppression within a common geometric framework, and suggests that fluctuations in the core size may become important for smaller systems such as Cu+Cu collisions.
