# Renormalisation in Quantum Mechanics

> This lecture provides an introduction to the renormalisation group as applied to scattering of two nonrelativistic particles. As well as forming a framework for constructing effective theories of few-nucleon systems, these ideas also provide a simple example which illustrates general features of the renormalisation group.

# Renormalisation in Quantum Mechanics

## Key claim
The renormalisation group (RG) applied to nonrelativistic two-particle scattering provides a tractable framework that illustrates all general features of RG flows, including fixed points and power counting, without the complications of full quantum field theory. The lecture demonstrates that the effective-range expansion for nucleon-nucleon scattering emerges naturally from the RG analysis of contact interactions.

## Method
The author constructs an RG equation for the two-body scattering potential by demanding that the off-shell K-matrix be independent of the momentum cut-off Λ, then rescaling all dimensioned quantities in units of Λ. Fixed points of the resulting RG equation are identified, and perturbations around them are classified by their RG eigenvalues to establish a power counting scheme. The analysis focuses on S-wave scattering with contact interactions, using a simple momentum cut-off regulator.

## Key numbers
- The basic loop integral for S-wave scattering contains a linear divergence: \( M \int q^2 dq/(p^2-q^2) \sim -M \int dq \) for large q
- The trivial fixed point has eigenvalues \( \nu = 2(l+m+n)+1 \) for perturbations of the form \( \hat{k}^{\prime 2l} \hat{k}^{2m} \hat{p}^{2n} \), all positive (stable fixed point)
- The nontrivial fixed point has eigenvalues \( \nu = 2n-1 \) for energy-dependent perturbations, including one negative eigenvalue (unstable fixed point)
- The effective-range expansion is given by \( 1/K(p) = (M/2\pi^2) \sum_{n=0}^\infty C_n p^{2n} = -(Mp/4\pi)(-1/a + (1/2)r_e p^2 + \cdots) \)

## Context
This lecture extends the RG methods developed by Wilson (1970s) for critical phenomena to the simpler setting of nonrelativistic two-body scattering, following the approach of Birse, McGovern, and Richardson (1999). It provides a pedagogical introduction to effective field theory techniques used in nuclear physics, where the RG analysis of contact interactions reproduces the effective-range expansion first applied to the nucleon-nucleon interaction by Bethe (1949). The work enables systematic construction of effective theories for few-nucleon systems and cold atomic gases, and connects to the Efimov effect in three-body systems with infinite two-body scattering length (Efimov, 1970).
