# TeV gamma-rays from photo-disintegration/de-excitation of cosmic-ray nuclei

> It is commonly assumed that high-energy gamma-rays are made via either purely electromagnetic processes or the hadronic process of pion production, followed by decay. We investigate astrophysical contexts where a third process (A*) may dominate, namely the photo-disintegration of highly boosted nuclei followed by daughter de-excitation. Starbust regions such as Cygnus OB2 appear to be promising sites for TeV gamma-ray emission via this mechanism. A unique feature of the A* process is a sharp energy minimum ~ 10 TeV/(T/eV) for gamma-ray emission from a thermal region of temperature T. We also check that a diffuse gamma-ray component resulting from the interaction of a possible extreme-energy cosmic-ray nuclei with background radiation is well below the observed EGRET data. The A* mechanism described herein offers an important contribution to gamma-ray astronomy in the era of intense observational activity.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that photo-disintegration of highly boosted cosmic-ray nuclei followed by daughter de-excitation (the "A*" process) can dominate TeV gamma-ray emission in certain astrophysical environments, specifically starburst regions containing hot stars and accelerated nuclei. The abstract's claim of dominance is hedged: the authors state the process "may dominate" and that it "is likely operative" in environments with both accelerated nuclei and hot starlight, not that it universally replaces other mechanisms.

## Method

The authors calculate the gamma-ray production rate from the Giant Dipole Resonance (GDR) photo-disintegration of high-energy nuclei interacting with a thermal photon background, using a narrow-width approximation for the GDR cross-section. They derive the inverse mean-free-path for photo-disintegration, the resulting gamma-ray spectrum from daughter de-excitation, and the expected flux at Earth, applying these calculations to the Cygnus OB2 starburst region. They also estimate the diffuse gamma-ray component from cosmogenic photo-disintegration of extreme-energy cosmic-ray nuclei on the cosmic microwave and infrared backgrounds.

## Key numbers

- Gamma-ray energy from the A* process: \(\elab \sim 20\,{\rm TeV}/({\rm T/eV})\), where T is the ambient photon temperature
- Required nuclear boost factor: \(\Gamma_A \sim 7\times 10^6\,({\rm T/eV})\), corresponding to energy \(\ealab \sim 7\,{\rm PeV}/({\rm T/eV})\) per nucleon
- GDR parameters: \(\sgdr = 1.45\,A\times 10^{-27} {\rm cm}^2\), \(\ggdr = 8\,{\rm MeV}\), \(\egdr = 42.65 A^{-0.21}\) MeV for \(A > 4\)
- Mean-free-path for photo-disintegration in a thermal photon field: \(\lambda_A^{\rm BE}\sim 5\times 10^{13}\,{\rm cm}/(A\,({\rm T/eV})^3) \sim 3\,{\rm AU}/(A\,({\rm T/eV})^3)\)
- For Cygnus OB2: nuclear mfp \(\lambda_A = (56/A)\times 10^{23}\) cm, starburst radius \(\Rsb = 10\) pc, distance \(d = 1.7\) kpc
- Predicted gamma-ray flux from Cygnus OB2: \({\cal F}_\gamma = 2\times 10^{-10}\,A\,{\cal F}_A\), where \({\cal F}_A\) is the nuclear flux in the PeV region
- Example: iron flux \({\cal F}_{56}=6\times 10^{-5}{\rm /cm^2/s}\) above a TeV yields gamma-ray flux at Earth of about 3% of the Crab flux (\(\Fcrab(\elab>0.35\,{\rm TeV})=10^{-10}{\rm /cm^2/s}\))
- Cosmogenic gamma-ray energy density from the A* process is more than three orders of magnitude below the EGRET bound of \(\omega_{\rm cas}\sim 2\times 10^{-6} {\rm eV/cm^3}\)

## Context

This work revives and extends the A* process first calculated by Stecker (1969) for nuclear photo-disintegration and later proposed for de-excitation gamma-rays by Moskalenko and collaborators (BKM87), which had been overlooked by the gamma-ray community. The mechanism provides an alternative to the standard electromagnetic (synchrotron and inverse Compton) and hadronic (pion production and decay) processes for TeV gamma-ray production, with a unique spectral signature: a sharp flattening below \(\sim 20\,{\rm TeV}/({\rm T/eV})\) and a quasi power-law above, producing "orphan" gamma-ray sources with suppressed GeV emission. The paper enables future observations with CANGAROO, HESS, MAGIC, and VERITAS to distinguish among the three competing mechanisms per source, and identifies Cygnus OB2 as a promising site for testing the A* process.
