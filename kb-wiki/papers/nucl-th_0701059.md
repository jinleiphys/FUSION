# Fluid Dynamics as Diagnostic Tool for Heavy Ion Collisions

> Ultra-Relativistic Heavy Ion Collisions at an energy $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 65 {\rm GeV}$ are studied in a three-dimensional Fluid Dynamical model. The results of a hydrodynamical evolution using the PIC-method are shown. The importance and diagnostic value of a proper reaction plane determination is emphasized, and the time development of collective measurables is presented.

# Fluid Dynamics as Diagnostic Tool for Heavy Ion Collisions

## Key claim
The three-dimensional fluid dynamical model using the Particle-in-Cell (PIC) method can describe ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 65$ GeV, but the calculated directed flow coefficient $v_1$ is larger than experimental data because the study uses constant-time hypersurfaces without freeze-out calculation or impact parameter averaging, both of which would smooth distributions and reduce $v_1$ and $v_2$.

## Method
The authors applied a three-dimensional perfect fluid dynamical model using the Particle-in-Cell (PIC) method, which combines Eulerian pressure calculations on a grid with Lagrangian marker particles for current transfers. The initial state was computed using an effective 1D Yang-Mills coherent field model with a "Firestreak"-type transverse splitting, where projectile and target matter interpenetrate, exchange color charge, and form strings that expand until kinetic energy converts to string tension of 6-8 GeV/fm. Flow coefficients were evaluated at constant-time hypersurfaces assuming massless quarks in a QGP phase, without performing freeze-out calculations.

## Key numbers
- Collision energy: $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 65$ GeV for Au+Au collisions
- Impact parameter: $b = 0.7\,(R_1 + R_2)$
- Effective string tension: $6-8$ GeV/fm, producing initial streaks of $4-8$ fm length
- Energy density shown at two evolution times: $t_1 = 0.3$ fm/c and $t_2 = 2.7$ fm/c after the start of hydrodynamical evolution
- Both $v_1$ and $v_2$ flow coefficients approximately double in magnitude between $t_1 = 0.3$ fm/c and $t_2 = 2.7$ fm/c

## Context
This work extends the fluid dynamical approach of Amsden and Harlow (1960s) and the ultra-relativistic upgrades by Nix and Strottman (1980s-1990s), using the PIC method with recent improvements that eliminate "ringing instabilities" by assigning marker particles random positions. The study builds on predictions of a "third flow component" as a QGP signature from Csernai (1999, 2004), which should appear as a deviation from linear increase in the directed flow coefficient $v_1(y)$ near midrapidity. The results enable future studies of freeze-out hypersurface effects and comparisons with experimental data from Wang (2007), though the authors note that proper reaction plane determination including forward-backward orientation is essential to observe these effects.
