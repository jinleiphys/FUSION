# The Density Matrix Renormalization Group and the Shell Model

> We summarize our recent efforts to develop the Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG) method into a practical truncation strategy for large-scale nuclear shell model calculations. Following an overview of the essential features of the DMRG, we discuss the changes we have implemented for its use in nuclei. In particular, we have found it useful to develop an angular-momentum conserving variant of the method (the JDMRG). We then summarize the principal results we have obtained to date, first reporting test results for $^{48}$Cr and then more recent test results for $^{56}$Ni. In both cases we consider nucleons limited to the 2p-1f shell. While both calculations produce a high level of agreement with the exact shell model results, the fraction of the complete space required to achieve this high level of agreement is found to go down rapidly as the size of the full space grows.

## Key claim

The Density Matrix Renormalization Group (DMRG) method, when implemented with an angular-momentum-conserving variant (JDMRG), can accurately reproduce exact shell-model results for nuclei in the 2p-1f shell, but the fraction of the full space required to achieve high accuracy decreases rapidly as the size of the full space grows, as seen by comparing results for \({}^{48}\)Cr and \({}^{56}\)Ni.

## Method

The authors developed an angular-momentum-conserving variant of the DMRG method (JDMRG) for nuclear shell-model calculations, using a three-block strategy that grows neutron and proton blocks separately. The method involves a warmup stage to make an initial guess for the optimal states, followed by iterative sweeps back and forth through the single-particle levels, constructing the reduced density matrix of the enlarged block in the presence of the medium and retaining the eigenstates with the largest eigenvalues. Calculations were performed for \({}^{48}\)Cr and \({}^{56}\)Ni using valence neutrons and protons outside a \({}^{40}\)Ca core, with the KB3 interaction for \({}^{48}\)Cr and the GXPF1A interaction for \({}^{56}\)Ni, and results were compared with exact diagonalization.

## Key numbers

- For \({}^{48}\)Cr, the exact ground-state energy is \(-32.953\) MeV; with \(m=200\) retained states, the JDMRG gives \(-32.947\) MeV, using 12,076 states out of 41,355 \(0^+\) states (about 29% of the full \(0^+\) space).
- For \({}^{48}\)Cr, the exact \(2_1^+\) excitation energy is 0.806 MeV; with \(m=180\), the JDMRG gives 0.855 MeV using 15,148 states out of 182,421 \(2^+\) states.
- For \({}^{48}\)Cr, the exact \(4_1^+\) excitation energy is 1.823 MeV; with \(m=180\), the JDMRG gives 1.989 MeV using 21,628 states out of 246,979 \(4^+\) states.
- For \({}^{56}\)Ni, the exact ground-state energy is \(-205.709\) MeV; with \(m=120\) retained states, the JDMRG gives \(-205.652\) MeV, using 106,383 states out of 15,443,685 \(0^+\) states (about 0.7% of the full \(0^+\) space).
- For \({}^{56}\)Ni, the exact \(2_1^+\) excitation energy is 2.600 MeV; with \(m=100\), the JDMRG gives 2.942 MeV using 423,265 states out of 71,109,189 \(2^+\) states.
- For \({}^{56}\)Ni, the exact \(4_1^+\) excitation energy is 3.688 MeV; with \(m=100\), the JDMRG gives 4.090 MeV using 701,502 states out of 105,537,723 \(4^+\) states.

## Context

This work extends the DMRG method, originally developed for low-dimensional quantum lattices by White, to nuclear shell-model calculations, following earlier applications by Papenbrock and Dean (2005) who found poor convergence for \({}^{56}\)Ni in the m-scheme. The JDMRG variant was first applied in nuclear physics in the context of the Gamow Shell Model by Ploszajczak and collaborators, and the present results improve on preliminary calculations for \({}^{48}\)Cr reported by Pittel and Thakur (2008). The finding that the fraction of the space required decreases with problem size suggests the method may become practical for even larger shell-model problems, and the authors plan to test it on odd-mass nuclei around \({}^{56}\)Ni and on heavier nuclei by breaking up large single-particle orbitals.
