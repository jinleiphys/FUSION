# Collisional energy loss and the suppression of high $p_T$ hadrons

> We calculate nuclear suppression factor ($R_{AA}$) for light hadrons by taking only the elastic processes and argue that in the measured $p_T$ domain of RHIC, collisional rather than the radiative processes is the dominant mechanism for partonic energy loss.

## Key claim

The authors argue that, contrary to the prevailing assumption in the field, collisional (elastic) energy loss is the dominant mechanism for partonic energy loss in the measured transverse momentum range of light hadrons at RHIC, rather than radiative processes. This claim is conditional on the parton energy being below a certain threshold (E_c), which for RHIC energies falls within the range needed to produce the observed hadrons.

## Method

The authors solve the Fokker-Planck equation to dynamically evolve parton spectra in a longitudinally expanding quark-gluon plasma described by Bjorken hydrodynamics, including only elastic (2-to-2) scattering processes. They compute the transport coefficients (drag and diffusion) for partons propagating through the plasma and then calculate the nuclear suppression factor R_AA for neutral pions by folding the evolved parton distributions with fragmentation functions (KKP parametrization) and parton distribution functions (CTEQ with EKS98 shadowing).

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature T_i = 450 MeV and transition temperature T_c = 190 MeV used in the calculation
- Strong coupling constant α_s = 0.3 used throughout
- Maximum average parton energy required to produce a 13 GeV pion at √s = 200 GeV is about 26 GeV
- For lower beam energy of 62.4 (130) A GeV, the maximum average parton energy required to produce a 13 GeV pion is 16 (22) GeV
- The initial parton distribution is parametrized with p_0 = 1.75 and α = 8

## Context

This work extends the analysis of collisional energy loss revisited by Alam et al. (2005) and Roy et al. (2006), who demonstrated that elastic loss can be as important as radiative loss in certain energy ranges. It contradicts the standard assumption used in radiative-loss-only jet tomography models that successfully reproduced light hadron suppression at RHIC (d'Enterria, 2005), but which failed to explain the non-photonic single electron data from PHENIX without requiring non-perturbatively large coupling or excessive transport coefficients (Wicks et al., 2006; Armesto et al., 2005). The results enable a re-evaluation of jet quenching models that must include both collisional and radiative processes, with the relative importance depending on the collision energy and parton momentum.
