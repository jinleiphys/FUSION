# Bayesian Inference of Hybrid Star Properties from Future High-Precision Measurements of Their Radii

> Future high-precision X-ray and gravitational-wave observations of neutron stars (NSs) are expected to constrain NS radii with uncertainties as small as $σ\simeq 0.1$~km. Such unprecedented precision offers a unique opportunity to extract new information about the nature and equation of state (EOS) of supradense matter in NS cores. Using mock radius data with uncertainties ranging from $σ= 1.0$ to $0.1$~km, together with a flexible meta-model NS EOS that allows for a first-order hadron-quark phase transition, we perform a Bayesian statistical analysis to assess the impact of radius measurements on EOS constraints. We find that high-precision radius measurements, particularly for massive NSs, significantly tighten constraints on the hadron-quark transition density $ρ_t$, the quark matter mass fraction in NS cores, and several parameters characterizing the EOS of supranuclear hadronic matter, although the degree of improvement depends on the assumed prior range of $ρ_t$. In contrast, even with the highest precision considered, NS radii -- including those of massive stars -- remain largely insensitive to the stiffness of quark matter, independent of the measurement accuracy or the prior range adopted for $ρ_t$.

## Key claim

High-precision radius measurements of massive neutron stars (with uncertainty as small as σ ≃ 0.1 km) can significantly tighten constraints on the hadron-quark transition density ρ_t and the quark matter mass fraction in neutron star cores, but even at the highest precision considered, neutron star radii remain largely insensitive to the stiffness of quark matter, independent of measurement accuracy or the prior range adopted for ρ_t.

## Method

The authors performed a Bayesian statistical analysis using mock radius data for 2.0 M⊙ neutron stars with uncertainties ranging from σ = 1.0 km down to 0.1 km, coupled with a meta-model equation of state that combines a hadronic npeμ matter description with the Constant Sound Speed (CSS) model for quark matter via a first-order hadron-quark phase transition. The hadronic EOS was parameterized by six nuclear matter parameters (K₀, J₀, K_sym, J_sym, L, E_sym(ρ₀)) and the quark sector by three parameters (transition density ρ_t, phase transition strength Δε/ε_t, and quark matter speed of sound squared C²_qm/c²), with Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling using the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm to generate posterior probability distribution functions. Two prior ranges for the transition density were compared: ρ_t/ρ₀ = 1.0–6.0 (case A) and ρ_t/ρ₀ = 3.0–6.0 (case B), the latter informed by recent Beam Energy Scan experiments at RHIC.

## Key numbers

- Radius measurement uncertainties considered: σ = 1.0 km, 0.5 km, and 0.1 km, with the highest precision (σ ≃ 0.1 km) corresponding to expected future X-ray and gravitational-wave observations
- The prior range for the hadron-quark transition density ρ_t/ρ₀ was set to 1.0–6.0 (case A) and 3.0–6.0 (case B), with the lower bound of 3.6ρ₀ motivated by the maximum central baryon density reached in √s_NN = 3 GeV Au+Au collisions at RHIC
- The minimum maximum mass (M_TOV) required for EOS filtering was set to 1.97 M⊙, based on the mass of PSR J0348+0432 (M = 2.01 ± 0.04 M⊙)
- The prior range for the slope of the nuclear symmetry energy L was 30–90 MeV, with a fiducial value of L ≈ 60 ± 20 MeV from approximately 100 independent analyses
- The prior range for the speed of sound squared in quark matter C²_qm/c² was 0.0–1.0
- The prior range for the phase transition strength Δε/ε_t was 0.2–1.0

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous Bayesian analyses using the same meta-model EOS framework (Li 2024, Xie 2020, Zhang 2019) by coupling the minimal hadronic neutron star EOS to the CSS model for quark matter via a first-order phase transition, and by systematically varying the precision of mock radius measurements to anticipate future observations from instruments like eXTP (launch ~2030), NewATHENA (launch ~2037), and third-generation gravitational-wave detectors such as the Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer. The study is motivated by the expectation that future measurements can achieve radius uncertainties as small as σ ≤ 0.2 km at 90% credibility for neutron stars in the mass range 1.00–1.97 M⊙ (Walker 2024), compared to current precision where the radius of PSR J0740+6620 is 12.92^{+2.09}_{-1.13} km at 68% credibility (Dittmann 2024). The results provide a reference for future studies with more sophisticated hadron-quark transition models and can help refine observational strategies for upcoming high-precision neutron star radius measurements.
