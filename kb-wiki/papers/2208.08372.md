# Evidence for intrinsic charm quarks in the proton

> The theory of the strong force, quantum chromodynamics, describes the proton in terms of quarks and gluons. The proton is a state of two up quarks and one down quark bound by gluons, but quantum theory predicts that in addition there is an infinite number of quark-antiquark pairs. Both light and heavy quarks, whose mass is respectively smaller or bigger than the mass of the proton, are revealed inside the proton in high-energy collisions. However, it is unclear whether heavy quarks also exist as a part of the proton wavefunction, which is determined by non-perturbative dynamics and accordingly unknown: so-called intrinsic heavy quarks. It has been argued for a long time that the proton could have a sizable intrinsic component of the lightest heavy quark, the charm quark. Innumerable efforts to establish intrinsic charm in the proton have remained inconclusive. Here we provide evidence for intrinsic charm by exploiting a high-precision determination of the quark-gluon content of the nucleon based on machine learning and a large experimental dataset. We disentangle the intrinsic charm component from charm-anticharm pairs arising from high-energy radiation. We establish the existence of intrinsic charm at the 3-standard-deviation level, with a momentum distribution in remarkable agreement with model predictions. We confirm these findings by comparing to very recent data on Z-boson production with charm jets from the LHCb experiment.

## Key claim

The paper claims evidence for intrinsic charm quarks in the proton at the 3-standard-deviation level, but this evidence is conditional on the large-\(x\) region (\(x \gtrsim 0.2\)); for smaller momentum fractions the result is compatible with zero once missing higher-order uncertainties (MHOUs) are accounted for.

## Method

The NNPDF collaboration performed a global QCD analysis of parton distribution functions (PDFs) using machine learning (neural networks) and a large experimental dataset including deep-inelastic scattering, Tevatron, and LHC data. They extracted the charm PDF in the 4-flavor number scheme (4FNS) from data, then used the EKO evolution code to invert the matching conditions and obtain the 3-flavor number scheme (3FNS) intrinsic charm PDF, comparing results obtained with NNLO and N\(^3\)LO matching to estimate MHOUs.

## Key numbers

* Intrinsic charm (3FNS) momentum fraction at \(m_c = 1.51\) GeV: \(0.62 \pm 0.28_{\rm pdf} \pm 0.54_{\rm mhou}\) percent.
* 4FNS charm momentum fraction at \(Q = 1.65\) GeV and \(m_c = 1.51\) GeV (default): \(0.87 \pm 0.23_{\rm pdf}\) percent.
* 4FNS perturbative charm momentum fraction at \(Q = 1.65\) GeV and \(m_c = 1.51\) GeV: \(0.346 \pm 0.005_{\rm pdf} \pm 0.44_{\rm mhou}\) percent.
* Evidence for intrinsic charm is established at the 3-standard-deviation level, based on the local pull in the large-\(x\) region.

## Context

This work extends previous inconclusive searches for intrinsic charm, such as the NNPDF3.1 study (Ball et al., 2016) which found only loose constraints on the charm PDF and relied heavily on EMC data. The present analysis shows that the dominant constraint now comes from collider data, especially LHCb \(W\) and \(Z\) production data, and that the result is stable under variations of dataset, parametrization basis, and charm mass. The findings enable future studies to refine the intrinsic charm component by reducing MHOUs and incorporating additional LHC measurements.
