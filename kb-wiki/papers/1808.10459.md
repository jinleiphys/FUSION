# Fingerprints of heavy element nucleosynthesis in the late-time lightcurves of kilonovae

> The kilonova emission observed following the binary neutron star merger event GW170817 provided the first direct evidence for the synthesis of heavy nuclei through the r-process. The late-time transition in the spectral energy distribution to near-infrared wavelengths was interpreted as indicating the production of lanthanide nuclei, with atomic mass number A>140. However, compelling evidence for the presence of heavier third-peak r-process elements (e.g., gold, platinum) or translead nuclei remains elusive. At early times (~days) most of the r-process heating arises from a large statistical ensemble of beta-decays, which thermalize efficiently while the ejecta is still dense, generating a heating rate that is reasonably approximated by a single power-law. However, at later times (weeks to months), the decay energy input can possibly be dominated by a discrete number of alpha-decays, 223Ra (half-life t_{1/2}=11.43d), 225Ac (t_{1/2}=10.0d, following the beta-decay of 225Ra with t_{1/2}=14.9d), and the fissioning isotope 254Cf (t_{1/2}=60.5d), which liberate more energy per decay and thermalize with greater efficiency than beta-decay products. Late-time nebular observations of kilonovae which constrain the radioactive power provide the potential to identify signatures of these individual isotopes, thus confirming the production of heavy nuclei. In order to constrain the bolometric light to the required accuracy, multi-epoch & wide-band observations are required with sensitive instruments like the James Webb Space Telescope. In addition, by comparing the nuclear heating rate obtained with an abundance distribution following the Solar r abundance pattern, to the bolometric lightcurve of AT2017gfo, we find that the yet-uncertain r abundance of 72Ge plays a decisive role in powering the lightcurve, if one assumes that GW170817 has produced a full range of the Solar r abundances down to A~70.

## Key claim

The late-time (weeks to months) bolometric lightcurve of a kilonova can reveal the production of heavy r-process nuclei, including translead actinides and third-peak elements, through the distinctive heating signatures of individual alpha-decaying isotopes (e.g., 223Ra, 225Ac) and the fissioning isotope 254Cf, provided that multi-epoch, wide-band observations with sufficient sensitivity (e.g., with the James Webb Space Telescope) are obtained; however, the authors note that the yet-uncertain solar r-abundance of 72Ge plays a decisive role in powering the lightcurve if GW170817 produced a full range of solar r-abundances down to A~70.

## Method

The authors calculated the total r-process heating rate for several ejecta models with different electron fraction distributions (models A-D and A1), using a nuclear reaction network with two sets of nuclear masses (FRDM and DZ31) and including beta-decays, alpha-decays, and spontaneous fission. They compared these heating rates to the bolometric lightcurve of AT2017gfo, and also computed heating rates assuming the solar r-abundance pattern for different minimum mass numbers, using two abundance sets from Sneden, Cowan, and Gallino (2008) and Goriely (1999).

## Key numbers

- 223Ra half-life: 11.43 days; 225Ac half-life: 10.0 days (following 225Ra beta-decay with half-life 14.9 days); 254Cf half-life: 60.5 days
- Model A1 produces translead nuclei with A~220-230 at a level of a few times 10^-5, a factor of ~4-10 more than model A
- A fraction Y(254Cf) = 2 x 10^-6 produces a lightcurve bump between 50-300 days
- The steepening of AT2017gfo bolometric luminosity at t~10 days places an upper limit of less than or similar to 10^-5 for total abundance of translead nuclei with A=222-225
- For solar r-abundances with Amin=69 using the S1 set, at least ~20% of the S1 72Ge abundance is needed to match the GW170817 lightcurve for ejecta mass less than or similar to 0.05 solar masses
- JWST NIRcam could detect luminosities down to ~5 x 10^37 erg/s (0.6-4 micron band, S/N=10, 10^4 s integration) for a merger at 100 Mpc; MIRI could constrain luminosity to ~2 x 10^38 erg/s (5-14 micron band)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of kilonova lightcurves from GW170817 (Smartt et al. 2017; Cowperthwaite et al. 2017; Waxman et al. 2017) by focusing on the late-time nebular phase, where individual radioactive decays can dominate the heating. It builds on earlier predictions of r-process heating in neutron star mergers (Metzger, Martinez-Pinedo, Darbha et al. 2010; Korobkin et al. 2012) and the potential role of 254Cf fission (Wanajo et al. 2014; Zhu et al. 2018). The results enable future observations with JWST to distinguish between different nuclear compositions of merger ejecta, potentially confirming the production of actinides and constraining the solar r-abundance pattern down to A~70.
