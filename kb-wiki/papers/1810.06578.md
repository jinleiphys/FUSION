# Strong- vs. weak-coupling pictures of jet quenching: a dry run using QED

> High-energy partons ($E \gg T$) traveling through a quark-gluon plasma lose energy by splitting via bremsstrahlung and pair production. Regardless of whether or not the quark-gluon plasma itself is strongly coupled, an important question lying at the heart of philosophically different approaches to energy loss is whether the high-energy partons of an in-medium shower can be thought of as a collection of individual particles, or whether their coupling to each other is also so strong that a description as high-energy `particles' is inappropriate. We discuss some possible theorists' tests of this question for simple situations (e.g. an infinite, non-expanding plasma) using thought experiments and first-principles quantum field theory calculations (with some simplifying approximations). The physics of in-medium showers is substantially affected by the Landau-Pomeranchuk-Midgal (LPM) effect, and our proposed tests require use of what might be called `next-to-leading order' LPM results, which account for quantum interference between consecutive splittings. The complete set of such results is not yet available for QCD but is already available for the theory of large-$N_f$ QED. We therefore use large-$N_f$ QED as an example, presenting numerical results as a function of $N_fα$, where $α$ is the strength of the coupling at the relevant high-energy scale characterizing splittings of the high-energy particles.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the validity of a weakly-coupled "individual particle" description of high-energy partons in an in-medium shower can be tested by computing the relative size of next-to-leading-order corrections from overlapping formation times, and that for large-\(N_f\) QED these corrections are of order \(N_f \alpha\) with a coefficient of \(-1.302\) when the renormalization scale is chosen as \(\mu = (\hat q E)^{1/4}\), but the result carries significant ambiguity from scale variation.

## Method

The authors compute the next-to-leading-order (NLO) correction to the average charge stopping distance \(\ell_{\rm stop}\) and to the dimensionless ratio \(\sigma / \ell_{\rm stop}\) (width over mean of the deposited charge distribution) for high-energy particles in an infinite, static medium, using large-\(N_f\) QED as a proxy theory. They use previously available NLO LPM splitting rates from Arnold, Iqbal, and Rase (2018) and solve the resulting kinetic equations for the shower evolution, expanding systematically in the coupling \(\alpha\) to isolate the effect of overlapping formation times from the leading-order independent-splitting picture.

## Key numbers

- The relative NLO correction to the charge stopping length is \(\Delta \ell_{\rm stop} / \ell_{\rm stop}^{(0)} = -1.302 \, N_f \alpha_{\overline{\rm MS}}(\mu_0)\) for the central choice \(\mu_0 = (\hat q E)^{1/4}\).
- The parametric estimate for the formation length of nearly-democratic bremsstrahlung is \(l_{\rm form} \sim \sqrt{x(1-x)E / \hat q}\) for gluon bremsstrahlung and \(\sim \sqrt{(1-x)E / (x \hat q)}\) for photon bremsstrahlung.
- The typical transverse momentum scale for high-energy splittings is \(Q_\perp \sim (\hat q E)^{1/4}\).
- The parametric estimate for the stopping length is \(\ell_{\rm stop} \sim \alpha^{-1} \sqrt{E / \hat q}\).
- The parametric estimate for the width \(\sigma\) of the deposited charge distribution is \(\sigma \sim \ell_{\rm stop} \sim \alpha^{-1} \sqrt{E / \hat q}\).

## Context

This work extends the program of Baier et al. (1997) and Zakharov (1996) (BDMPS-Z formalism) by computing corrections to the independent-splitting picture that arise from overlapping formation times, which are not captured by the leading-order BDMPS-Z rates. It builds on leading-log studies by Blaizot, Iancu, and Wu (2013-2014) who found that soft-gluon overlap corrections in QCD can be absorbed into an effective \(\hat q\), and it provides a complementary test using the dimensionless ratio \(\sigma / \ell_{\rm stop}\) that is insensitive to \(\hat q\) and avoids the double-log enhancement present in QCD. The results enable future work to assess whether the weak-coupling picture of jet quenching is quantitatively reliable for realistic values of the coupling in QCD, once the analogous QCD calculations become available.
