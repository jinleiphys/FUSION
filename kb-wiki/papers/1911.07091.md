# Studying strong phase transitions in neutron stars with gravitational waves

> The composition of neutron stars at the extreme densities reached in their cores is currently unknown. Besides nuclear matter of normal neutrons and protons, the cores of neutron stars might harbor exotic matter such as deconfined quarks. In this paper we study strong hadron-quark phase transitions in the context of gravitational wave observations of inspiraling neutron stars. We consider upcoming detections of neutron star coalescences and model the neutron star equations of state with phase transitions through the Constant-Speed-of-Sound parametrization. We use the fact that neutron star binaries with one or more hadron-quark hybrid stars can exhibit qualitatively different tidal properties than binaries with hadronic stars of the same mass, and hierarchically model the masses and tidal properties of simulated populations of binary neutron star inspiral signals. We explore the parameter space of phase transitions and discuss under which conditions future observations of binary neutron star inspirals can identify this effect and constrain its properties, in particular the threshold density at which the transition happens and the strength of the transition. We find that if the detected population of binary neutron stars contains both hadronic and hybrid stars, the onset mass and strength of a sufficiently strong phase transition can be constrained with 50-100 detections. If the detected neutron stars are exclusively hadronic or hybrid, then it is possible to place lower or upper limits on the transition density and strength.

## Key claim

If the detected population of binary neutron stars contains both hadronic and hybrid stars, the onset mass and strength of a sufficiently strong phase transition can be constrained with 50-100 detections; if the detected neutron stars are exclusively hadronic or hybrid, it is possible to place lower or upper limits on the transition density and strength, respectively.

## Method

The authors model neutron star equations of state with sharp hadron-quark phase transitions using the Constant-Speed-of-Sound (CSS) parametrization, which specifies the critical pressure (or baryon number density) at which the transition occurs, the strength of the transition (energy density jump), and the speed of sound in the high-density phase. They simulate populations of binary neutron star inspiral signals with different mass distributions and employ a hierarchical model that uses the chirp radius parameter, a combination of the chirp mass and the effective tidal deformability, to distinguish binaries containing hybrid stars from purely hadronic binaries based on deviations from an equation-of-state-insensitive relation.

## Key numbers

* The transition mass Mtrans for the DBHF_1032 model is 0.34 Msolar, with a transition radius Rtrans of 14.66 km and a maximum mass Mmax of 2.0 Msolar.
* The transition mass Mtrans for the DBHF_3004 model is 1.78 Msolar, with a transition radius Rtrans of 13.14 km and a maximum mass Mmax of 2.14 Msolar.
* The transition mass Mtrans for the SFHo_1031 model is 0.20 Msolar, with a transition radius Rtrans of 17.81 km and a maximum mass Mmax of 2.0 Msolar.
* The transition mass Mtrans for the SFHo_3003 model is 1.31 Msolar, with a transition radius Rtrans of 12.02 km and a maximum mass Mmax of 2.15 Msolar.
* The radius of a 1.4 Msolar star for the purely hadronic DBHF baseline is 13.41 km; for the purely hadronic SFHo baseline it is 11.97 km.
* The systematic error in radius inference from using hadronic-based equation-of-state-insensitive relations on hybrid stars can exceed 2 km for certain systems (e.g., DBHF_2010), but in all cases studied the error is smaller than the 3.7 km statistical uncertainty from GW170817 using the same method.
* A sufficiently strong phase transition can be constrained with 50-100 detections if the population contains both hadronic and hybrid stars.

## Context

This work extends investigations of hadron-quark phase transitions in neutron stars using gravitational wave observations, building on the CSS parametrization framework of Alford (2013) and the chirp radius concept introduced by Wade (2014). It contrasts with studies that assume purely hadronic equations of state, such as those used in the analysis of GW170817 by the LIGO and Virgo collaborations (2017), and addresses the potential systematic bias in radius estimates when phase transitions are ignored, as previously discussed by Steiner (2017) in the context of quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries. The results enable future gravitational wave detectors to constrain the threshold density and strength of phase transitions from populations of binary neutron star inspirals.
