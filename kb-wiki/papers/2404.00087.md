# Quantum simulation of entanglement and hadronization in jet production: lessons from the massive Schwinger model

> The possible link between entanglement and thermalization, and the dynamics of hadronization are addressed by studying the real-time response of the massive Schwinger model coupled to external sources. This setup mimics the production and fragmentation of quark jets, as the Schwinger model and QCD share the properties of confinement and chiral symmetry breaking. By using quantum simulations on classical hardware, we study the entanglement between the produced jets, and observe the growth of the corresponding entanglement entropy in time. This growth arises from the increased number of contributing eigenstates of the reduced density matrix with sufficiently large and close eigenvalues. We also investigate the physical nature of these eigenstates, and find that at early times they correspond to fermionic Fock states. We then observe the transition from these fermionic Fock states to meson-like bound states as a function of time. In other words, we observe how hadronization develops in real time. At late times, the local observables at mid-rapidity (such as the fermion density and the electric field) approach approximately constant values, suggesting the onset of equilibrium and approach to thermalization.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that in the massive Schwinger model, the entanglement entropy between two back-to-back jets grows over time and approaches a maximally entangled state, but only within a restricted subspace of the full Hilbert space; the authors observe a real-time transition from fermionic Fock states (partonic degrees of freedom) to meson-like bound states (hadronization), with local observables at mid-rapidity approaching constant values suggestive of thermalization.

## Method

The authors simulate the real-time evolution of the massive Schwinger model coupled to external sources that mimic back-to-back quark jets, using exact diagonalization on lattices of size N ≤ 20 and tensor network methods (DMRG for ground states, TDVP for time evolution) on lattices of size 80 ≤ N ≤ 100. They compute the Schmidt decomposition of the state across a left-right bipartition of the lattice to extract the entanglement spectrum, and analyze the electric charge distribution and Fock-state overlaps of the Schmidt vectors to track the transition from partonic to hadronic degrees of freedom.

## Key numbers

- Entangleness reaches approximately 0.9 at late times, indicating the onset of a maximally entangled state (Fig. 5, N=100, m=1/(4a), g=1/(2a)).
- For lattice size N=16, the maximal overlap of leading Schmidt vectors with Fock states stays close to 1 at all times at weak coupling (m=2/a, g=1/(2a)), but decreases during time evolution at strong coupling (m=1/(2a), g=2/a), signaling hadronization (Fig. 7).
- At late times, the averaged fermion condensate and electric field energy in the central region reach the same constant value regardless of the averaging region size L (Fig. 10, N=100, m=1/(4a), g=1/(2a)).
- The second Rényi entropy transitions from an area law (independent of subsystem size) to a volume law (linear dependence on subsystem size) after the external sources cross the subsystem boundaries (Fig. 12, N=80).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the massive Schwinger model with external sources by Casher (1974), Loshaj (2011), Kharzeev (2012, 2013), and Florio (2023), and addresses open questions about the link between entanglement and thermalization raised by Kharzeev (2017) in the context of QCD evolution at small Bjorken x. The results provide a first-principles demonstration of hadronization in real time and the emergence of a maximally entangled state in a confining theory, enabling future quantum simulations of jet fragmentation on quantum computers as the required circuit depth becomes accessible.
