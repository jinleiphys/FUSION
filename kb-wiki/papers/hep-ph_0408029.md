# Challenges in Hadron Physics

> In this talk, I address some open problems in hadron physics and stress their importance for a better understanding of QCD in the confinement regime.

# Challenges in Hadron Physics

## Key claim
The parity and width of the exotic $\Theta^+ (1540)$ pentaquark remain undetermined, and resolving these requires specific experimental measurements that avoid theoretical assumptions about naturalness of partial waves. A unified description of hadron resonances combining quark model predictions with coupled-channel dynamics is proposed as a necessary step to distinguish genuine quark-model states from dynamically generated resonances.

## Method
The parity of the $\Theta^+$ is studied via spin correlation coefficient $A_{xx}$ and spin transfer coefficient $D_{xx}$ in the reaction $\vec p \vec p \to \Theta^+ \Sigma^+$, assuming spin 1/2 for the $\Theta^+$. The width is constrained by analyzing $K^+ d \to K^0 p p$ cross sections using the JÃ¼lich kaon-nucleon model extended to include a $\Theta^+ (1540)$ with variable width, and by studying $K^+ Xe \to K^0 p X$ with kinematical cuts mimicking the DIANA experiment. Hyperon resonances are generated as meson-baryon bound states using a unitarized chiral perturbation theory approach with SU(3) symmetry breaking parameterized by a variable $x$.

## Key numbers
- The COSY-TOF collaboration gives $\Gamma_{\Theta^+} \le (18 \pm 4)$ MeV from proton-proton collisions.
- Analysis of $K^+ d \to K^0 p p$ data constrains the $\Theta^+ (1540)$ width to be less than 1 MeV.
- For $K^+ Xe \to K^0 p X$, the $\chi^2$/dof values are 2.3 (no $\Theta^+$), 2.7 (positive parity, 1 MeV width), and 2.9 (negative parity, 1 MeV width).
- The $\Delta$ pole is located at $(1210, i53)$ MeV from unitarized chiral perturbation theory.
- The $\rho$ tensor coupling is $\kappa_\rho \simeq 6.3$.
- In the SU(3) breaking analysis, the singlet pole sits at 1390 MeV and one octet pole at 1426 MeV (both $I=0$), combining to form the $\Lambda(1405)$.
- The SU(3) limit uses $M_0 = 368$ MeV, $m_0 = 1151$ MeV, and $a_0 = -2.148$.
