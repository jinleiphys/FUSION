# Method for Solving the Many-Body Bound State Nuclear Problem

> We present a method based on hyperspherical harmonics to solve the nuclear many-body problem. It is an extension of accurate methods used for studying few-body systems to many bodies and is based on the assumption that nucleons in nuclei interact mainly via pairwise forces.This leads to a two-variable integro-differential equation which is easy to solve. Unlike methods that utilize effective interactions, the present one employs directly nucleon-nucleon potentials and therefore nuclear correlations are included in an unambiguous way. Three body forces can also be included in the formalism. Details on how to obtain the various ingredients entering into the equation for the A-body system are given. Employing our formalism we calculated the binding energies for closed and open shell nuclei with central forces where the bound states are defined by a single hyperspherical harmonic. The results found are in agreement with those obtained by other methods.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method based on hyperspherical harmonics that extends accurate few-body techniques to many-body nuclear systems, under the assumption that nucleons interact mainly via pairwise forces, leading to a two-variable integro-differential equation that is easy to solve; the authors note that their calculated binding energies for closed and open shell nuclei using central forces, where bound states are defined by a single hyperspherical harmonic, agree with results obtained by other methods.

## Method

The authors transform the many-body Schrödinger equation into polar coordinates in the D-dimensional space (where D = 3(A-1)) spanned by the particle coordinates in the center-of-mass frame, using the hyperradius r and hyperspherical harmonics (HHs) as the angular basis. They start from the hypercentral approximation (HCA) where only the ground harmonic polynomial of minimal degree L_m is retained, then extend the wave function by including two-body correlations via a two-body amplitude F(r_ij, r) in the form Ψ = D_{L_m}(Ω) Σ_{i<j} F(r_ij, r), which leads to the Integro-Differential Equation Approach (IDEA), a two-variable integro-differential equation that accounts for pairwise correlations without requiring a large number of coupled equations.

## Key numbers

- The hyperradius is defined as r² = (2/A) Σ_{i<j} (x_i − x_j)², with the factor 2 introduced so that for two particles r² = (x₁ − x₂)².
- The dimension of the space is D = 3(A−1) for an A-body system.
- The ground state corresponds to the minimum degree L_m = Σ_{j=1}^{A} (2n_j + ℓ_j), which is the sum over radial and orbital quantum numbers of all occupied harmonic oscillator states.
- The hypercentral potential V(r) is given by V(r) = [A(A−1)/2] ∫ H*_{L_m}(Ω) V(r_ij) H_{L_m}(Ω) dΩ, where the HH is normalized to ∫ |H_{L_m}(Ω)|² dΩ = 1.

## Context

This work extends the hyperspherical harmonics method, previously applied to few-body systems, to many-body nuclei by introducing the IDEA formalism that reduces the problem to a two-variable integro-differential equation. It contrasts with Hartree-Fock methods using effective interactions (e.g., Skyrme forces as in the work of Bender and Heenen) and with the Independent Particle Model (IPM) of Goeppert-Mayer and Jensen, by employing realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials directly and including two-body correlations unambiguously. The approach enables future calculations of binding energies for heavier nuclei using realistic potentials, with the possibility of incorporating three-body forces into the same formalism.
