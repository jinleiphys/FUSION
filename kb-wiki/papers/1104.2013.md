# A maximum likelihood method to correct for missed levels based on the $Δ_3(L)$ statistic

> The $Δ_3(L)$ statistic of Random Matrix Theory is defined as the average of a set of random numbers $\{δ\}$, derived from a spectrum. The distribution $p(δ)$ of these random numbers is used as the basis of a maximum likelihood method to gauge the fraction $x$ of levels missed in an experimental spectrum. The method is tested on an ensemble of depleted spectra from the gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE), and accurately returned the correct fraction of missed levels. Neutron resonance data and acoustic spectra of an aluminum block were analyzed. All results were compared with an analysis based on an established expression for $Δ_3(L)$ for a depleted GOE spectrum. The effects of intruder levels is examined, and seen to be very similar to that of missed levels. Shell model spectra were seen to give the same $p(δ)$ as the GOE.

# A maximum likelihood method to correct for missed levels based on the Δ₃(L) statistic

## Key claim

The distribution p(δ) of the individual δ₃ⁱ(L) values (whose average is the standard Δ₃(L) statistic) can be parameterized as a function of the missed-level fraction x, enabling a maximum likelihood estimate of x that is more precise than the existing method based on the Bohigas-Pato expression for Δ₃(L,x), though the method becomes inconclusive for some experimental spectra and cannot distinguish missed levels from intruder levels.

## Method

The author generated ensembles of 3000 GOE matrices of dimension 3000, unfolded them, randomly dropped a fraction x of levels (0.00 ≤ x ≤ 0.30 in steps of 0.01), and contracted the spectrum to restore unit level density. For each depleted spectrum of size D=1000, the set of D−L−1 values δ₃ⁱ(L) was computed for L=5 to 90. The cumulative distribution N(δ) was parameterized with a three-parameter fit using an error function of log δ, and those parameters were themselves fit as smooth functions of x for each L, yielding a continuous probability density p(δ,x). The method was tested on depleted GOE ensembles and applied to neutron resonance data from 7 isotopes and acoustic spectra of aluminum blocks, with results compared to the Bohigas-Pato expression Δ₃(L,x)=x²(L/x)/15+(1−x)²Δ₃^GOE(L/(1−x)).

## Key numbers

- For depleted GOE spectra with D=1000, the MLM returned x with σ_x ≈ 0.006 for the range 20 ≤ L ≤ 40, averaged over x from 0.00 to 0.30.
- For the full 93-level ¹⁵⁸Gd dataset, the MLM gave x = 0.121 ± 0.024, corresponding to 93/(1−x) = 105 ± 3 initial levels, with 13 ± 3 missed.
- For the 91-level ¹⁵²Sm full dataset, the MLM gave x = 0.081 ± 0.024, corresponding to 99 ± 3 initial levels, with 8 ± 3 missed.
- For the 81-level ²³⁶U full dataset, the MLM gave x = 0.124 ± 0.027, corresponding to 10 ± 2 missed levels; the lowest 69 levels gave x = 0.038 ± 0.030, corresponding to 3 ± 2 missed.
- For ¹³⁵U j=3 subset (1436 levels), the MLM gave x = 0.053 ± 0.010, corresponding to 20 ± 4 missed; for the j=4 subset (1732 levels), x = 0.045 ± 0.008, corresponding to 26 ± 5 missed.
- For acoustic data from aluminum blocks, the MLM returned x = 0.204 ± 0.012 (set 1), 0.221 ± 0.014 (set 2), and 0.234 ± 0.014 (set 4); three other sets gave x > 0.30.
- The Bohigas-Pato expression gave accurate mean x but with σ_x much larger than the MLM (dashed line in Fig. 7 top panel shows σ_x from this method).

## Context

This work extends the use of the Δ₃(L) statistic for completeness testing beyond the spectral average approach of Bohigas and Pato (2004), who gave the expression Δ₃(L,x) for incomplete spectra. It also builds on the maximum likelihood method of Agvaanluvsan et al. (2003), which used the nearest-neighbor spacing distribution, and on earlier analyses of neutron resonance data by Mulhall (2007, 2009) using both Δ₃(L) and the Agvaanluvsan MLM. The method enables more precise estimation of missed-level fractions for spectra with 80–250 levels, though it cannot distinguish missed levels from intruder levels (which produce nearly identical effects on p(δ)), and it fails for some datasets where the x vs. L curve is not flat. The shell model spectra (Wildenthal USD interaction in the sd model space) were shown to give the same p(δ) as the GOE, consistent with the established result that shell model fluctuations follow RMT.
