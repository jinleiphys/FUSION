# Emergent symplectic symmetry in atomic nuclei: Ab initio symmetry-adapted no-core shell model

> Exact symmetry and symmetry-breaking phenomena play a key role in gaining a better understanding of the physics of many-particle systems, from quarks and atomic nuclei, through molecules and galaxies. In nuclei, exact and dominant symmetries such as rotational invariance, parity, and charge independence have been clearly established. Beyond such symmetries, the nature of nuclear dynamics appears to exhibit a high degree of complexity, and only now, we show the fundamental role of an emergent approximate symmetry in nuclei, the symplectic Sp(3,R) symmetry, as clearly unveiled from ab initio studies that start from realistic interactions. In this article, we detail and enhance our recent findings presented in Physical Review Letters 124 (2020) 042501, that establish Sp(3,R) as a remarkably good symmetry of the strong interaction, and point to the predominance of a few equilibrium nuclear shapes (deformed or not) with associated vibrations and rotations that preserve the symplectic Sp(3,R) symmetry. Specifically, we find that the structure of nuclei below the calcium region in their ground state, as well as in their low-lying excited states and giant resonances, respects this symmetry at the 60-80% level.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the symplectic Sp(3,R) symmetry is an emergent approximate symmetry of the strong interaction in nuclei below the calcium region, with nuclear ground states, low-lying excited states, and giant resonances respecting this symmetry at the 60-80% level, though the symmetry is approximate and the exact percentage varies by nucleus and state.

## Method

The authors used the ab initio symmetry-adapted no-core shell model (SA-NCSM) with realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions (NNLOopt, N3LO-EM, JISP16, NNLOsat) to solve the many-body Schrodinger equation for nuclei from helium to titanium. They constructed many-particle basis states labeled by SU(3) quantum numbers (lambda, mu) and total intrinsic spin S, then reorganized these into Sp(3,R) irreducible representations that correspond to equilibrium nuclear shapes with associated vibrations and rotations. The model space was systematically down-selected using Sp(3,R) considerations, and observables were extrapolated to the infinite-shell limit using the Shanks transformation.

## Key numbers

* The structure of nuclei below the calcium region respects Sp(3,R) symmetry at the 60-80% level in ground states, low-lying excited states, and giant resonances.
* In 20Ne, a single most predominant symplectic irrep (8,0) makes up about 70% or more of the ground state.
* The centroid of the fragmented giant monopole resonance in 20Ne is around 29 MeV.
* In 8He, the lowest two 0+ states are made of a predominant shape contributing at the 40-55% level and a secondary shape with about 20% contribution.
* For 12C, the matter rms radius deviates only by 1% when using a selected interaction compared to the full N3LO-EM interaction.

## Context

This work extends earlier algebraic models that assumed Sp(3,R) as an effective symmetry for nuclei, such as the symplectic rotor model of Rosensteel and Rowe (1977) and the Elliott SU(3) model (1958), by demonstrating that this symmetry naturally emerges from first-principle calculations without symmetry-based approximations. The results enable predictions for open-shell deformed intermediate-mass nuclei such as Ne and Mg isotopes, and target short-lived isotopes along nucleosynthesis pathways where experimental measurements are incomplete. The SA-NCSM approach also allows for drastically reduced computational resources compared to conventional no-core shell model calculations, making heavier nuclei accessible.
