# Three-body resonances in He-6, Li-6, and Be-6, and the soft dipole mode problem of neutron halo nuclei

> Using the complex scaling method, the low-lying three-body resonances of $^6$He, $^6$Li, and $^6$Be are investigated in a parameter-free microscopic three-cluster model. In $^6$He a 2$^+$, in $^6$Li a 2$^+$ and a 1$^+$, and in $^6$Be the 0$^+$ ground state and a 2$^+$ excited state is found. The other experimentally known 2$^+$ state of $^6$Li cannot be localized by our present method. We have found no indication for the existence of the predicted 1$^-$ soft dipole state in $^6$He. We argue that the sequential decay mode of $^6$He through the resonant states of its two-body subsystem can lead to peaks in the excitation function. This process can explain the experimental results in the case of $^{11}$Li, too. We propose an experimental analysis, which can decide between the soft dipole mode and the sequential decay mode.

# Three-body resonances in He-6, Li-6, and Be-6, and the soft dipole mode problem of neutron halo nuclei

## Key claim

The predicted 1- soft dipole mode in He-6 does not exist according to a direct search for three-body resonant poles of the scattering matrix at complex energies; the experimentally observed structures attributed to this mode can instead be explained by sequential decay through the resonant states of the two-body subsystem (He-5 plus neutron), without requiring any collective soft dipole excitation.

## Method

The author used the complex scaling method (CSM) within a microscopic three-cluster model (alpha + N + N) to search for three-body resonances in He-6, Li-6, and Be-6. The trial wave function was expanded in products of tempered Gaussian functions of the Jacobi coordinates in a variational approach, using the Minnesota nucleon-nucleon interaction with parameters fixed to reproduce the alpha + N phase shifts and N + N phase shifts, leaving no free parameters. Complex scaling rotated the Hamiltonian into the complex coordinate plane, converting resonant states into square-integrable eigenfunctions whose complex eigenvalues (E - i Gamma/2) were extracted from the stationary points of theta-trajectories.

## Key numbers

* He-6 2+ state: theoretical energy E = 0.74 MeV, width Gamma = 0.06 MeV; experimental E = 0.822 +/- 0.025 MeV, Gamma = 0.113 +/- 0.020 MeV
* Li-6 2+ state (higher): theoretical E = 1.59 MeV, Gamma = 0.28 MeV; experimental E = 1.696 +/- 0.015 MeV, Gamma = 0.54 +/- 0.020 MeV
* Li-6 1+ state: theoretical E = 5.71 MeV, Gamma = 3.89 MeV; experimental E = 1.95 +/- 0.05 MeV, Gamma = 1.5 +/- 0.2 MeV (theoretical values are pushed higher due to model underbinding)
* Be-6 0+ ground state: theoretical E = 1.52 MeV, Gamma = 0.16 MeV; experimental E = 1.371 MeV, Gamma = 0.092 +/- 0.006 MeV
* Be-6 2+ state: theoretical E = 2.81 MeV, Gamma = 0.87 MeV; experimental E = 3.04 +/- 0.05 MeV, Gamma = 1.16 +/- 0.06 MeV
* He-6 two-neutron separation energy in the model: 0.670 MeV (experimental value: 0.975 MeV)
* Predicted sequential decay 1- structures in He-6: at approximately 1.9 MeV and 5 MeV excitation energy (0.975 + 0.89 and 0.975 + 4 MeV, using the He-5 3/2- and 1/2- resonance energies)
* Li-11 two-neutron separation energy: 0.295 +/- 0.035 MeV; predicted sequential decay structures at approximately 0.7 and 1.1 MeV excitation energy

## Context

This work extends the author's previous microscopic three-cluster model of A=6 nuclei (Csoto 1993) by applying the complex scaling method to directly search for three-body resonances, rather than using indirect bound-state approximations or real-energy phase shift analyses. The results contradict the soft dipole mode predictions of Ikeda (1987) and Suzuki and Ikeda (1988), and are consistent with the real-energy analyses of Danilin and Zhukov (1993) and Ferreira et al. (1993) who also found no indication of a 1- state in He-6. The sequential decay explanation proposed here offers an alternative to the soft dipole interpretation of experimental data from Sakuta et al. (1993) and Kobayashi (1992), and the author suggests that complete kinematical measurements of core-neutron angular correlations, such as those from Sackett et al. (1993), could distinguish between the two mechanisms.
