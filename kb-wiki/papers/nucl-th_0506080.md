# Modeling Nuclear Properties with Support Vector Machines

> We have made initial studies of the potential of support vector machines (SVM) for providing statistical models of nuclear systematics with demonstrable predictive power. Using SVM regression and classification procedures, we have created global models of atomic masses, beta-decay halflives, and ground-state spins and parities. These models exhibit performance in both data-fitting and prediction that is comparable to that of the best global models from nuclear phenomenology and microscopic theory, as well as the best statistical models based on multilayer feedforward neural networks.

## Key claim

Support vector machines (SVMs) can provide global statistical models of nuclear masses, beta-decay halflives, and ground-state spins and parities with predictive performance comparable to the best phenomenological and microscopic nuclear models, as well as to multilayer feedforward neural networks, but only after careful tuning of SVM parameters and subdivision of nuclei into even-oddness classes.

## Method

The authors applied SVM regression (using ANOVA-decomposed radial-basis-function kernels for masses, and simpler RBF kernels for halflives) and SVM classification (for spins and parities) to separate training, validation, and test sets drawn from experimental nuclear data. For each property, nuclei were divided into four classes based on even-oddness of proton number Z and neutron number N (EE, EO, OE, OO), and separate SVM models were trained for each class, with performance evaluated by root-mean-square error (regression) or classification accuracy.

## Key numbers

- Mass model (ANOVA-RBF, gamma=2.5, d=8): rms errors on test sets of 0.99 MeV (EE class, 48 nuclides), 0.62 MeV (EO class, 45 nuclides), 0.88 MeV (OE class, 46 nuclides), and 0.97 MeV (OO class, 45 nuclides)
- Best single SVM mass model (SVM-S, gamma=2.5, d=8): rms error 0.70 MeV on training set O, 0.75 MeV on validation set NB, and 1.41 MeV on test set N
- Beta-decay halflife model (RBF kernel, cutoff at 10^6 s): rms error in log10(halflife) on test sets of 1.20 (EE, 10 nuclides), 1.22 (EO, 15 nuclides), 1.18 (OE, 13 nuclides), and 1.34 (OO, 17 nuclides)
- Parity classification: perfect (100%) on training sets for EO, OE, and OO classes; test set accuracies up to 97.1% (EO class) and 97.8% (OO class)

## Context

This work extends the application of machine learning to nuclear systematics beyond earlier multilayer perceptron studies (Clark and collaborators, 1990s-2000s) by introducing support vector machines, which offer principled control of model complexity and guaranteed global optimization. The SVM mass models are compared to the FRDM model of Moller and Nix (1992, 1995) and the HFB2 model of Pearson and collaborators, achieving competitive rms errors around 0.7-1.0 MeV. The halflife models show improvement by about a factor of 2 in rms error over earlier neural-network work (1995), but the authors note that more recent multilayer perceptron models (2005) still outperform their SVM halflife models, indicating room for further refinement.
