# Relativistic hybrid stars with sequential first-order phase transitions in light of multimessenger constraints

> In this work, we consider the properties of compact stars in which quark matter has low- and high-density phases that are separated by a first-order phase transition. Thus, unlike the commonly considered case of a single phase transition from hadronic to quark matter, our models of hybrid stars contain sequential phase transitions from hadronic matter to low- and then to high-density quark matter phases. We extend our previous study of the parameter space of hybrid stars with a single phase transition to those with sequential phase transitions, taking into account the constraints on the mass and radius of neutron stars from the NICER experiment, the experimental inferences of the neutron skin thickness of the lead nucleus by the PREX-II experiment, and constraints on the tidal deformability from the gravitational-wave event GW170817. We determine the range of the masses for which both twin and triplet configurations, i.e., identical-mass stars with two and three different values of radii, arise.

# Relativistic hybrid stars with sequential first-order phase transitions in light of multimessenger constraints

## Key claim

Hybrid stars can host two sequential first-order phase transitions (hadronic to low-density quark matter, then to high-density quark matter), producing "triplet" configurations where three stars share the same mass but have different radii, but only if the nucleonic equation of state is sufficiently stiff (high values of the symmetry energy slope Lsym and skewness Qsat) and the sound speed in the high-density quark phase is close to the causal limit.

## Method

The authors construct hybrid star equations of state by combining a covariant density-functional model for hadronic matter with a constant-sound-speed (CSS) parameterization for two sequential quark matter phases. They reduce the six-dimensional parameter space of the double CSS model to two physical parameters, the maximum mass of the purely hadronic branch and the maximum mass of the highest-density quark branch, by imposing matching conditions that ensure triplet configurations exist over a finite mass range. The resulting mass-radius relations are tested against constraints from NICER (PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620), the PREX-II neutron skin measurement, and the tidal deformability limit from GW170817.

## Key numbers

- For subcanonical-mass twins/triplets with stiff nucleonic EoS (Qsat = 900 MeV, Lsym = 105 MeV) and M_N_max = 1.3 solar masses, the Q2 branch requires s2 ≈ 1.0 for M_Q2_max = 2.1 solar masses and s2 ≈ 0.7 for M_Q2_max = 2.0 solar masses
- The mass range for triplet stars ΔM_triplet is typically ~10^-3 solar masses, one order of magnitude smaller than the twin mass range ΔM_twin
- The radius difference ΔR_1.3 for N-Q2 pairs is at most about 1.0 km; differences for N-Q1 or Q1-Q2 pairs are even smaller
- For high-mass twins/triplets with Qsat = 900 MeV, Lsym = 45 MeV and M_N_max = 1.70 solar masses, acceptable models require (M_Q2_max/solar masses, s2) pairs of (2.1, 1.0) or (2.0, 0.8)
- The transition density for the subcanonical case is ρ_tran ≈ 2 ρ_sat (specifically 1.89 ρ_sat for the example with Lsym=105 MeV); for the high-mass case it is ρ_tran = 2.41 ρ_sat
- Almost no twin/triplet solutions exist for nucleonic models with Qsat = -500 or -100 MeV in the high-mass regime

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study of hybrid stars with a single phase transition (Li, Sedrakian, and Alford 2021) to the case of sequential first-order phase transitions, following the theoretical framework for multiple quark phases proposed by Alford, Han, and Prakash (2017). The sequential transitions are motivated by the possibility of a 2SC-to-CFL transition within quark matter, as studied in NJL model computations by Blaschke et al. (2010, 2013) and Bonanno and Sedrakian (2012). The results show that stiff nucleonic equations of state favored by the PREX-II experiment (Reed et al. 2021) can be reconciled with the softness required by GW170817 if a low-density phase transition to quark matter occurs, and that future radius measurements with 5% accuracy (as NICER aims to achieve) or tidal deformability measurements with errors in the few hundred range could distinguish between twin and triplet configurations.
