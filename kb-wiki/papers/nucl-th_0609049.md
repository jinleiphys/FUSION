# Fusion hindrance and roles of shell effects in superheavy mass region

> We present the first attempt of systematically investigating the effects of shell correction energy for a dynamical process, which includes fusion, fusion-fission and quasi-fission processes. In the superheavy mass region, for the fusion process, shell correction energy plays a very important role and enhances the fusion probability when the colliding partner has a strong shell structure. By analyzing the trajectory in three-dimensional coordinate space with the Langevin equation, we reveal the mechanism of the enhancement of the fusion probability caused by `cold fusion valleys'. The temperature dependence of shell correction energy is considered.

# Fusion hindrance and roles of shell effects in superheavy mass region

## Key claim
Shell correction energy enhances fusion probability in superheavy systems when the colliding partner has a strong shell structure, but this enhancement is conditional on the temperature dependence of the shell correction energy and the deformation of the compound nucleus ground state. The "cold fusion valleys" that guide trajectories toward compound nucleus formation are shown to evolve dynamically as the deformation parameter changes, and a "second pocket" at the turning point can trap trajectories and block fission only when the compound nucleus ground state is deformed rather than spherical.

## Method
The authors employed a three-dimensional Langevin equation with fluctuation-dissipation dynamics in a nuclear deformation space parameterized by two-center parameters (distance between potential centers, fragment deformation, and mass asymmetry). They calculated trajectories from the contact point through the fusion, quasi-fission, and fusion-fission processes using potential energy surfaces from the liquid drop model with and without shell correction energy, including temperature-dependent shell correction via the Ignatyuk parameterization. The fusion probability was estimated as the probability of forming a compound nucleus in competition with quasi-fission events for head-on collisions (l=0).

## Key numbers
- For the system $^{284}$114 at z=1.6 (near contact point), the potential energy at $\alpha=0.46$ (corresponding to $^{76}$Ge+$^{208}$Pb) is V=6.0 MeV, while at $\alpha=0.24$ (corresponding to $^{108}$Ru+$^{176}$Yb) it is V=14.8 MeV, showing the large negative shell correction for the Pb-target combination.
- The shell correction energy at the ground state of $^{276}$114 (deformed) is about 5.0 MeV.
- The fusion probability for forming $^{276}$114 (deformed compound nucleus) is larger than for $^{292}$114 (spherical compound nucleus) when both start at $\alpha=0.67$ with $E^{*}=40$ MeV, due to the presence of a second pocket at the deformed turning point.
- The temperature dependence of shell correction energy follows $\Phi(T)=\exp(-aT^2/E_d)$ with $a=A/8$ and $E_d=20$ MeV, consistent with Ignatyuk (1975) parameterization.
- Fusion hindrance becomes significant for systems with atomic number Z greater than 102, based on liquid drop model calculations.

## Context
This work extends the author's previous study (Aritomo 2005) that focused on fusion hindrance from a macroscopic liquid drop perspective, by now incorporating microscopic shell effects. It builds on the extra-push model of Swiatecki (1981) and the fluctuation-dissipation dynamics approach (Aritomo 2004), and addresses the "cold fusion valleys" suggested by Greiner (1999, 2000). The results explain why cold fusion reactions using Pb targets (Oganessian 1975) are advantageous for synthesizing superheavy elements, and identify a key difficulty: spherical compound nuclei with Z>110 lack the second pocket that assists fusion in deformed systems. The temperature-dependent shell correction treatment follows Ignatyuk (1975) and is applied to dynamical calculations for the first time in this context, enabling more realistic modeling of fusion-fission competition in the superheavy region.
