# An Effective Field Theory for the Three-Body System

> We study the scattering of a particle from a bound pair in an effective field theory using a distorted-wave renormalisation group method to find the power-counting for the three-body force terms. We find that three-body terms appear at lower orders than naively expected. They start with a marginal term that varies as a logarithm rather than a power of the energy scales in the problem. The marginal term has important implications for the three-body problem in nuclear physics.

## Key claim

Three-body force terms in the effective field theory appear at next-to-leading order rather than the naively expected next-to-next-to-leading order, because the leading term is marginal and varies as a logarithm rather than a power of the energy scales.

## Method

The authors applied a distorted-wave renormalisation group (DWRG) method to the three-body system, using the zero-energy analytic solutions of the Skorniakov-Ter-Martirosian equation to determine the short-range asymptotic behaviour of the three-body wavefunction. They then constructed the power-counting for three-body couplings by studying the scaling behaviour of perturbations around the logarithmic evolution found in the DWRG equation, working in the KSW counting scheme where the two-body leading term scales as Q^{-1}.

## Key numbers

- The Efimov parameter s0 is approximately 1, appearing in the short-range asymptotic wavefunction solution psi(r23, r1) = N(ka2) f(theta) sin(s0 ln(kR) + eta(R0))
- The leading three-body term occurs at next-to-leading order in the expansion (where two-body leading term occurs at Q^{-1}), rather than the naively expected next-to-next-to-leading order
- The three-body force expansion takes the form D_{n,m} p^{2n} a2^{-m} Lambda0^{-2n-m}, where p is on-shell momentum, n and m are non-negative integers, D_{n,m} are dimensionless parameters of order one, and Lambda0 is the breakdown scale of the EFT

## Context

This work extends the KSW effective field theory for two-body systems to the three-body problem, building on the Efimov effect (Efimov, 1970) and the Skorniakov-Ter-Martirosian equation (Skorniakov and Ter-Martirosian, 1957). The DWRG method was previously developed by Barford and Birse (2002) for treating long-range physics exactly. The result explains why different phenomenological nucleon-nucleon potentials produce predictions lying on a single "Phillips line" (Phillips, 1968), showing that their differences can be expressed in a single effective parameter R0, the scale appearing in the marginal term of the three-body potential.
