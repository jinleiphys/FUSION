# Strange Particle Freeze-Out

> We reconsider thermal conditions of the central fireball presumed to be the source of abundantly produced strange (anti-)baryons in S -> W collisions at 200 GeV A. We show that it is possible to completely fix the freeze-out temperature of strange particles in terms of the central rapidity kaon to Lambda particle abundance ratio at fixed, high transverse mass using a non-equilibrium hadronization model and the measured quark fugacities.

## Key claim
The paper shows that a hadron gas (HG) source cannot explain the observed strange particle abundances in S→W collisions at 200 GeV A, and that the freeze-out temperature of strange particles can be determined from the central-rapidity kaon-to-Lambda ratio at fixed high transverse mass, using a non-equilibrium hadronization model with measured quark fugacities.

## Method
The authors use a statistical hadronization model that allows for chemical non-equilibrium via a strangeness saturation factor γ_s and separate meson/baryon abundance factors C_M^s and C_B^s. They impose strangeness conservation (equal numbers of s and anti-s quarks) and solve for the baryochemical potential μ_B as a function of freeze-out temperature T_f, using the measured quark fugacities λ_q=1.48±0.05 and λ_s≈1 from WA85 data. They then relate the required ratio R_C^s = C_M^s/C_B^s to the observable K_s/(Λ+Σ^0) ratio at high transverse mass.

## Key numbers
- The measured quark fugacities are λ_q=1.48±0.05 and λ_s=1.03±0.05 from WA85 data.
- The strangeness saturation factor is γ_s=0.7±0.1 from WA85 results.
- The apparent temperature from m_⊥-spectra is T=232±5 MeV, which is incompatible with a hadron gas source (hatched region in Fig. 1 lies at lower T).
- For S→W collisions, a value R_K ≈ 0.05 at m_⊥ > 1.9 GeV would correspond to a freeze-out temperature T_f ≈ 0.15 GeV.
- The non-equilibrium parameter R_C^s ≈ 0.4 at this low temperature implies strange baryon yields are enhanced by a factor of 2.5 relative to naive thermal expectations.

## Context
This work extends earlier analyses by Letessier et al. (1993, 1994) that showed a hadron gas model cannot fit WA85 data, and contradicts the two-temperature hadronization model of Redlich (1993). It builds on the proposal by Rafelski (1991) that strange particles arise from direct QGP disintegration, supported by the finding λ_s≈1. The paper enables future experiments (e.g., Pb–Pb at 170 GeV A) to determine the hadronization temperature by measuring the K_s/(Λ+Σ^0) ratio at high transverse mass, thereby distinguishing between a low-temperature rapidly hadronizing QGP and a sequential evaporation scenario.
