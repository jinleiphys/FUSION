# Direct observation of hydrodynamization and local prethermalization

> Hydrodynamics accurately describes relativistic heavy-ion collision experiments well before local thermal equilibrium is established. This unexpectedly rapid onset of hydrodynamics -- which takes place on the fastest available timescale -- is called hydrodynamization. It occurs when an interacting quantum system is quenched with an energy density that is much greater than its initial energy density. During hydrodynamization, energy gets redistributed across very different energy scales. Hydrodynamization precedes local equilibration among momentum modes, which is local prethermalization to a generalized Gibbs ensemble in nearly integrable systems or local thermalization in non-integrable systems. Many theories of quantum dynamics postulate local (pre)thermalization, but the associated timescale has not been quantitatively studied. Here we use an array of 1D Bose gases to directly observe both hydrodynamization and local prethermalization. After we apply a Bragg scattering pulse, hydrodynamization is evident in the fast redistribution of energy among distant momentum modes, which occurs on timescales associated with the Bragg peak energies. Local prethermalization can be seen in the slower redistribution of occupation among nearby momentum modes. We find that the time scale for local prethermalization in our system is inversely proportional to the momenta involved. During hydrodynamization and local prethermalization, existing theories cannot quantitatively model our experiment. Exact theoretical calculations in the Tonks-Girardeau limit show qualitatively similar features.

## Key claim

Using an array of 1D Bose gases with point-contact interactions, the authors directly observe both hydrodynamization (fast energy redistribution among distant momentum modes) and local prethermalization (slower redistribution among nearby momentum modes) after a Bragg scattering quench, finding that existing theories cannot quantitatively model the experiment during these processes.

## Method

The experiment starts with nearly zero-temperature 1D Bose gases of 87Rb atoms confined in a 2D optical lattice with axial trapping. A Bragg scattering pulse in the Kapitza-Dirac limit is applied to quench the system with high energy density, and the momentum distribution is measured as a function of time after the pulse using time-of-flight imaging. The authors analyze the time evolution of kinetic energy in different momentum ranges to characterize hydrodynamization, and extract local prethermalization timescales from the evolution of the momentum dividing line p50 that splits the central peak atoms in half.

## Key numbers

- Hydrodynamization oscillation period: Thd ~ 2π/ωhd = 33 μs, where ωhd = ħ(2k)²/m
- The scale of collision energies in the ultracold atom system differs from relativistic heavy-ion collisions by 18 orders of magnitude
- For the experimental data at γ̄₀ = 2.3, the intermediate momentum curves (p̄ = 0.7 to 1.5) show a rapid rise with characteristic time ~ 2/ωhd
- The energy changes during hydrodynamization are about 2.5 times greater in the Tonks-Girardeau theory than in the experiment
- The experimental local prethermalization time constant τp₅₀ = C/(k√ε₀) with C_exp = 2.6 C_th, where ε₀ is the average pre-quench energy per particle
- The time for the TG gas to reach the local generalized Gibbs ensemble is about twice τp₅₀, which is ~5 times faster than the time for Bragg scattered components to traverse the wavelength associated with pᵐ₅₀

## Context

This work extends theoretical studies of hydrodynamization in high-energy physics (Florkowski 2018, Schenke 2021), cosmology (Mottola 1997), and photo-excited correlated metals (Kollar 2011) by providing the first direct experimental observation of the phenomenon. The results enable testing of generalized hydrodynamics (GHD) (Castro-Alvaredo 2016, Bertini 2016, Schemmer 2019, Malvania 2021) during local prethermalization, suggesting GHD may work even before the local GGE is satisfied, analogous to hydrodynamics working after hydrodynamization but before local thermalization in quark-gluon plasma.
