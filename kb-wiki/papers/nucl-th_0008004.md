# Many-body perturbation calculation of spherical nuclei with a separable monopole interaction: I. Finite nuclei

> We present calculations of ground state properties of spherical, doubly closed-shell nuclei from $^{16}$O to $^{208}$Pb employing the techniques of many-body perturbation theory using a separable density dependent monopole interaction. The model gives results in Hartree-Fock order which are of similar quality to other effective density-dependent interactions. In addition, second and third order perturbation corrections to the binding energy are calculated and are found to contribute small, but non-negligible corrections beyond the mean-field result. The perturbation series converges quickly, suggesting that this method may be used to calculate fully correlated wavefunctions with only second or third order perturbation theory. We discuss the quality of the results and suggest possible methods of improvement.

# Many-body perturbation calculation of spherical nuclei with a separable monopole interaction: I. Finite nuclei

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a separable, density-dependent monopole interaction can be fitted to finite-nucleus properties and yields a perturbation series that converges quickly enough that second- or third-order many-body perturbation theory may provide fully correlated wavefunctions across the periodic table, although the Hartree-Fock binding energies are systematically somewhat worse than those from Skyrme parameterizations, and the spin-orbit splittings are systematically too small.

## Method

The authors solve the Hartree-Fock problem in a spherical harmonic oscillator basis for 14 doubly closed-shell nuclei from 16O to 208Pb, using a separable monopole interaction with attractive, repulsive, and derivative terms plus a phenomenological spin-orbit force. They then evaluate the second-order and third-order vacuum-amplitude diagrams (particle-particle, hole-hole, and particle-hole terms) directly by summing over the particle and hole states obtained from the Hartree-Fock solution, with the interaction parameters fitted to experimental binding energies (to second order) and charge radii, charge densities, single-particle energies, and spin-orbit splittings (to Hartree-Fock order).

## Key numbers

- Binding energies for most of the 14 nuclei are reproduced to within approximately 2% of experimental values (from Audi and Wapstra 1995 mass table).
- 16O is notably under-bound; its calculated charge radius is too large by about 5%.
- The second-order correlation energy is roughly constant across the periodic table; a dimensionless strength parameter kappa is defined as the sum over 2p2h excitations of the squared matrix element divided by the square of the energy denominator.
- In 48Ca, the second-order correction is much larger than in 40Ca due to a specific monopole excitation between f7/2 neutron and proton states with a particularly small energy denominator.
- The Hartree-Fock calculation was iterated until the energy converged to within 1 keV, using 12 expansion coefficients per single-particle wavefunction.

## Context

This work revisits the approach of the Kerman group at MIT from the late 1960s (Kerman 1969, Breuer 1969, Rouben 1969, Zipper 1970, Riihimaki 1970), who attempted to parameterize the nucleon-nucleon interaction to be weak enough for perturbation theory but achieved only moderate success. The present interaction is designed as an effective interaction with parameters fitted directly to finite-nucleus properties, in contrast to realistic potentials such as the Argonne v18 (Wiringa 1995) or Bonn (Machleidt 1987) potentials which have hard repulsive cores and require non-perturbative methods. The results are compared to several Skyrme parameterizations (SIII, SkP, SLy4, SkI4) and to experimental charge densities from Friedrich (1995). The method enables future inclusion of higher multipole forces (l=1,2,...) for deformed nuclei and excited states, and the correlation structure suggests that pairing-like ground-state correlations arise naturally from the perturbation theory.
