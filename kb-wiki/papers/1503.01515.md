# Systematic calculations of $α$-decay half-lives with an improved empirical formula

> Based on the recent data in NUBASE2012, an improved empirical formula for evaluating the $α$-decay half-lives is presented, in which the hindrance effect resulted from the change of the ground state spins and parities of parent and daughter nuclei is included, together with a new correction factor for nuclei near the shell closures. The calculated $α$-decay half-lives are found to be in better agreements with the experimental data, and the corresponding root-mean-square (rms) deviation is reduced to $0.433$ when the experimental $Q$-values are employed. Furthermore, the $Q$-values derived from different nuclear mass models are used to predict $α$-decay half-lives with this improved formula. It is found that the calculated half-lives are very sensitive to the $Q$-values. Remarkably, when mass predictions are improved with the radial basis function (RBF), the resulting rms deviations can be significantly reduced. With the mass prediction from the latest version of Weizsäcker-Skyrme (WS4) model, the rms deviation of $α$-decay half-lives with respect to the known data falls to $0.697$.

## Key claim

The authors present an improved empirical formula for calculating alpha-decay half-lives that includes a parity-dependent hindrance factor and a correction for nuclei near shell closures, achieving a root-mean-square (rms) deviation of 0.433 when experimental Q-values are used, which is better than previous formulas but still relies on semi-empirical corrections rather than a fully microscopic treatment.

## Method

The authors fit a modified Royer-type formula to 341 ground-state-to-ground-state alpha transitions from the NUBASE2012 database, introducing a term d^(1-(-1)^l) to account for parity-dependent hindrance of the emitted alpha particle and an empirical correction S = 0.5 for nuclei near the Z=82 and N=126 shell closures. They then use Q-values from eight nuclear mass models (FRDM, WS4, HFB-27, RMF, KTUY, Duflo-Zuker, Bhagwat, and DZ10/DZ31) to predict half-lives, and apply the radial basis function (RBF) approach to improve mass predictions before recalculating half-lives.

## Context

This work extends the empirical formula approach of Royer (2000) and Dong et al. (2010) by adding parity-dependent hindrance and shell-correction terms, improving upon the formulas of Denisov and Khudenko (2009) and Royer (2010) which gave rms deviations of 0.536 and 0.561 respectively on the same data set. The use of the radial basis function approach, introduced by Wang et al. (2011), demonstrates a method to significantly reduce rms deviations when theoretical mass models are used to predict Q-values, enabling more reliable half-life predictions for superheavy elements and nuclei far from stability where experimental data are unavailable.
