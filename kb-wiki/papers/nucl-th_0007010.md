# Calculating the Fierz Transformation for Higher Orders

> We consider the higher-order Fierz transformation, which corresponds to expanding a product of $\barψΓψ$ terms into a sum of products of Dirac densities and currents. It is shown that the Fierz transformation can be obtained by solving a large system of linear equations with fractional complex coefficients, which is practical at least up to fourth power.

# Calculating the Fierz Transformation for Higher Orders

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that higher-order Fierz transformations (up to fourth order) can be obtained by solving large systems of linear equations with fractional complex coefficients using standard Gauss elimination, but the practical feasibility depends on reducing the problem dimension through symmetrization and symmetry requirements, the full fourth-order system of 16^4 equations is too large for practical solution without such reductions.

## Method
The authors treat the Fierz transformation as a system of linear equations obtained by comparing coefficients in the spinor-component space, using the 16 Dirac matrices {1, γ5, γμ, γ5γμ, σμν} as a basis. For second and third order, a Mathematica implementation evaluates scalar products with symbolic four-component spinors and extracts coefficients sequentially. For fourth order, a Fortran-90 code with a fractional complex number data type performs Gauss elimination, encoding spinor component combinations into 8-bit integer indices to build the equation system.

## Key numbers
- The second-order Fierz transformation involves 16^2 = 256 equations for 16×16 = 256 unknown coefficients.
- The third-order system has dimension 16^3, and the fourth-order system has dimension 16^4, giving a complexity ratio of 1:16:256 when going from second to fourth order.
- For the symmetrized case with identity matrices on the left-hand side, the second-order decomposition yields coefficients: 1/4 for the 1·1 term, 1/4 for γ5·γ5, 1/4 for γμ·γμ, -1/4 for γ5γμ·γ5γμ, and 1/8 for σμν·σμν.
- The fourth-order symmetrized case reduces the number of independent terms by a factor of 4! = 24 due to permutation symmetry.

## Context
This work extends the original Fierz transformation (Fierz, 1937) to higher orders, motivated by nonlinear self-coupling of meson fields in relativistic mean-field theories as reviewed by Hoch (year not specified in text). The method generalizes earlier approaches by Takahashi (year not specified) and SU(n) generalizations by Zong (year not specified). The results enable practical computation of exchange matrix elements in terms of densities and currents for relativistic nuclear structure calculations up to fourth order, with the main remaining challenge being the expression of transformations through meaningful couplings of the Γ matrices.
