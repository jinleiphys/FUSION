# The relationship between particle freeze-out distributions and HBT radius parameters

> The relationship between pion and kaon space-time freeze-out distributions and the HBT radius parameters in high-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions is investigated. We show that the HBT radius parameters in general do not reflect the R.M.S. deviations of the single particle production points. Instead, the HBT radius parameters are most closely related to the curvature of the two-particle space-time relative position distribution at the origin. We support our arguments by studies with a dynamical model (RQMD 2.4).

## Key claim

HBT radius parameters obtained from a Gaussian fit to the correlation function do not measure the root-mean-square (R.M.S.) deviations of single-particle freeze-out positions; instead they are equivalent to the curvature of the two-particle relative position distribution at the origin, which can be substantially smaller than the R.M.S. size when the source has non-Gaussian tails.

## Method

The authors derive analytically that a maximum-likelihood Gaussian fit to the correlation function yields HBT radii determined by the second derivative of the source function of relative production points at zero relative distance. They then test this relationship using the RQMD v2.4 event generator for central (b < 3 fm) Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s) = 200 GeV/nucleon, calculating three-dimensional correlation functions for pions and kaons at mid-rapidity (|y| < 0.5) and fitting them with the Bertsch-Pratt out-side-long parameterization.

## Key numbers

- For pions with 0 < p_T < 200 MeV/c and |y| < 0.5, the single-particle R.M.S. deviations are substantially larger than the fitted HBT radii in all three directions (side, out, long), while the Gaussian widths of the two-particle position difference distributions are very close to the fitted HBT radii.
- The Gaussian fit to the two-particle difference distribution is performed in the region of ±0.5 R.M.S. of that distribution.
- For kaons, the R.M.S. deviations and fitted Gaussian widths of the two-particle relative distance distribution are very similar in the transverse directions, but in the longitudinal direction the difference between HBT radii and R.M.S. values remains significant.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of HBT interferometry beyond the static Gaussian source approximation reviewed by Heinz and Jacak (1999) and Wiedemann and Heinz (1999). It contradicts the naive interpretation that fitted HBT radii directly correspond to the variance of the particle spatial distribution at freeze-out, showing instead that the radii reflect only the behavior of close pairs. The results enable more careful interpretation of experimental HBT measurements in heavy-ion collisions, particularly for separating geometric size from expansion dynamics and resonance decay contributions. The analytical derivation relating HBT radii to the second derivative of the source function builds on the Fourier transform relations used by Brown and Danielewicz (1998) and Wiedemann and Heinz (1997).
