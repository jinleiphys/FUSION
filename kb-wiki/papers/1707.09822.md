# On the radiation beaming of bright X-ray pulsars and constraints on neutron star mass-radius relation

> The luminosity of accreting magnetised neutron stars can largely exceed the Eddington value due to appearance of accretion columns. The height of the columns can be comparable to the neutron star radius. The columns produce the X-rays detected by the observer directly and illuminate the stellar surface, which reprocesses the X-rays and causes additional component of the observed flux. The geometry of the column and the illuminated part of the surface determines the radiation beaming. Curved space-time affects the angular flux distribution. We construct a simple model of the beam patterns formed by direct and reflected flux from the column. We take into account the possibility of appearance of accretion columns, whose height is comparable to the neutron star radius. We argue that depending on the compactness of the star the flux from the column can be either strongly amplified due to gravitational lensing, or significantly reduced due to column eclipse by the star. The eclipses of high accretion columns result in specific features in pulse profiles. Their detection can put constraints on the neutron star radius. We speculate that column eclipses are observed in X-ray pulsar V 0332+53, leading us to the conclusion of large neutron star radius in this system ($\sim15\,{\rm km}$ if $M\sim 1.4M_\odot$). We point out that the beam pattern can be strongly affected by scattering in the accretion channel at high luminosity, which has to be taken into account in the models reproducing the pulse profiles.

## Key claim

The authors argue that for accreting magnetized neutron stars with super-Eddington luminosities, the height of accretion columns can become comparable to the neutron star radius, and that eclipses of these tall columns by the neutron star produce sharp dips in X-ray pulse profiles; detection of such features can constrain the neutron star radius, with the specific suggestion that column eclipses observed in V 0332+53 imply a large neutron star radius of approximately 15 km if the mass is about 1.4 solar masses.

## Method

The authors constructed a simplified model of X-ray beam patterns from super-critical X-ray pulsars, treating the accretion column as an extended source with height-dependent luminosity distribution and angular beaming determined by relativistic electron scattering at the column walls. They calculated the total observed flux as the sum of direct radiation from the column and reflected radiation from the neutron star surface, using numerical solutions of photon trajectories in the Schwarzschild metric to account for gravitational light bending and lensing. The model also estimates the optical thickness of the accretion flow at the magnetospheric surface and its potential influence on the beam pattern at high luminosities.

## Key numbers

* Neutron star radius in V 0332+53 estimated at approximately 15 km if the mass is about 1.4 solar masses
* The magnetospheric radius is given by R_m = 2.5 × 10^8 Λ B_12^(4/7) L_37^(-2/7) M_1.4^(1/7) R_6^(10/7) cm, where Λ is a constant less than 1
* The radius of the base of the accretion channel is approximately 6.3 × 10^4 Λ^(-1/2) B_12^(-2/7) L_37^(1/7) M_1.4^(-1/14) R_6^(11/14) cm
* The Eddington luminosity for a typical neutron star is L_Edd approximately 2 × 10^38 erg/s
* For a neutron star of mass 1.4 solar masses and radius 10 km, the maximum observed latitude is about 136 degrees, corresponding to approximately 86 percent of the total neutron star surface
* The optical thickness of the free-fall region due to Thomson scattering is given by τ_ff = (1.7 × 10^5 / l_0) L_37 / β_ff (1 - x/(d/2))
* The optical thickness due to scattering in the accretion envelope is approximately τ_e(λ) ≈ 1.4 L_37^(6/7) B_12^(2/7) / β(λ) × (cos λ_0 / cos λ)^3
* The fraction of point source luminosity intercepted by the accretion channel can be as high as 5 to 15 percent depending on source location, initial beaming, and neutron star compactness

## Context

This work extends previous models of pulse profiles for X-ray pulsars that considered only hot spots on the neutron star surface (Pechenick 1983, Poutanen 2006, Viironen 2010) or small accretion columns with heights less than 1 km (Mészáros 1978, Riffert 1988, Kraus 2001). The authors incorporate the recently recognized possibility that accretion columns can reach heights comparable to the neutron star radius (Postnov 2013) and include the reprocessed (reflected) component from the neutron star surface, which was mentioned in interpretations of V 0332+53 and 4U 0515+63 (Sasaki 2012). The model enables constraints on neutron star mass-radius relations through detection of column eclipse features in pulse profiles, and points to the need for including scattering in the accretion channel at high luminosity for accurate pulse profile modeling.
