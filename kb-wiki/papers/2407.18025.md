# Alpha-decay from $^{44}$Ti: Microscopic alpha half-life calculation using normalized spectroscopic factor

> The microscopic description of alpha decay from the nucleons' degree of freedom involves a two-step process. The first consists of the clusterization of neutron and proton pairs; the second involves the tunneling process. A robust protocol for calculating the normalized spectroscopic factor, as defined by Fliessbach, and its error is established and used for calculating the alpha-width for the $0^+$ states of the nucleus $^{44}$Ti. The Gamow Shell Model is used to calculate the structure part of the alpha-decay, while the Gamow wave function determines the reaction part. The conventional and normalized spectroscopic factors are calculated for the ground and excited $0^+$ states of $^{44}$Ti and the alpha-width and half-life of the excited states. A near alpha-threshold state has an alpha half-life of 5 $μ$sec. The normalization does not appreciably modify the ground-state clusterization, while the excited states do. The non-resonant continuum significantly increases the clustering of some of the excited states, particularly the $T=2$ state. The normalized formation amplitude looks like a single-particle wave function.

## Key claim

The Fliessbach normalization procedure does not appreciably modify the ground-state clusterization of $^{44}$Ti, but it does significantly affect the excited states; the non-resonant continuum substantially increases clustering for some excited states, particularly the $T=2$ state, though the calculated alpha width for that state is twenty times larger than the experimental value, suggesting the clusterization may be overestimated.

## Method

The authors used the Gamow Shell Model to calculate the structure part (spectroscopic factor) and Gamow wave functions for the reaction part (single-particle width) of alpha decay from $^{44}$Ti. They established a robust protocol for calculating the normalized spectroscopic factor as defined by Fliessbach, using an expansion of the norm kernel in shifted Gaussian functions with a systematic determination of the cutoff parameter $\nu_{\rm{max}}$ based on the condition $s_\nu^L < 1$, and assigned errors via Gaussian fitting of histograms over the three-dimensional parameter space $(\Delta R, R_{\rm{max}}, \nu_{\rm{max}})$. The formation amplitude was expressed in terms of single-particle configurations from correlated two-neutron and two-proton states, with the complete basis including both resonant and non-resonant continuum.

## Key numbers

- The normalized spectroscopic factor $\mathcal{S}$ for the ground state ($0_1^+$) of $^{44}$Ti is $0.021(2)$ in the complete basis.
- The normalized spectroscopic factor for the $0_5^+$ ($T=2$) state is $0.4933(20)$ in the complete basis, the largest among the six $0^+$ states.
- The near alpha-threshold state $0_2^+$ has an alpha half-life of $0.48 \times 10^{-5}$ seconds (approximately 5 $\mu$sec).
- The calculated alpha width for the $0_5^+$ ($T=2$) state is $0.74(20) \times 10^{-5}$ MeV, which is twenty times larger than the experimental value of $0.35 \pm 0.07$ eV from Freedman (1978).
- The $0_4^+$ state has a calculated half-life of $0.43 \times 10^{-14}$ seconds (4.3 fs).
- The $0_3^+$ state has a calculated half-life of $0.24 \times 10^{-7}$ seconds (24 ns).
- The single-particle width for the $0_2^+$ state is $0.13(2) \times 10^{-14}$ MeV.
- The single-particle width for the $0_5^+$ state is $0.15(4) \times 10^{-4}$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Id Betan (2012) by complementing the resonant model space with the non-resonant continuum, replacing the schematic separable force with an effective Gaussian interaction, and incorporating the missing proton-neutron interaction. It uses the $0^+$ states of $^{44}$Ti as a benchmark, which is of interest in nuclear astrophysics for understanding formation through the $\alpha$-Ca reaction in core-collapse supernova environments (Robertson, 2012). The calculation shows that many-body Hilbert space truncation, while valid for spectra and wave functions, is not valid for clustering calculations, and that the tensor interaction may be essential for properly describing the neutron-proton threshold. An application to the alpha decay of $^{104}$Te is reported as in progress.
