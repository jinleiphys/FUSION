# Exploring the accurate nuclear potential

> We have constructed empirical formulae for fusion and interaction barrier heights using experimental values available in the literature. Fusion excitation function measurements are used for the former and back angle quasi-elastic excitation function for the latter case. The fusion barriers so obtained have been compared with various model predictions such as Bass potential, Christenson and Winther, Broglia and Winther, Aage Winther, Siwek-Wilczynska and J.Wilczynski, Skyrme energy density function model, and the Sao Paulo optical potential along with experimental results. The comparison allows us to find the best model, which is found to be the Broglia and Winther model. Further, to examine its predictability, the Broglia and Winther model parameters are used to obtain total fusion cross sections showing good agreement with the experimental values for beam energies above the fusion barriers. Thus, this model can be useful for planning any experiments, especially ones aiming for super heavy elements. Similarly, current interaction barrier heights have also been compared with the Bass potential model predictions. It shows that the present model calculations are much lower than the Bass potential model predictions. We believe the current interaction barrier model prediction will be a good starting point for future quasi-elastic scattering experiments. Whereas both the Broglia and Winther model and our interaction barrier model will have practical implications in carrying out physics research near the Coulomb barrier energies.

## Key claim

The authors construct empirical polynomial formulae for fusion and interaction barrier heights from experimental data, and then compare these empirical barriers to predictions from several theoretical models. They find that the Broglia and Winther (BW) model provides the best overall agreement with the empirical fusion barriers, but this conclusion is qualified by the fact that the empirical formulae are themselves derived from the same experimental data used for the comparison, and the BW model's parameters are then used to reproduce fusion cross sections only for beam energies above the fusion barrier.

## Method

The authors collected published experimental fusion barrier heights (from fusion excitation function measurements) and interaction barrier heights (from back-angle quasi-elastic excitation function measurements) for a range of projectile-target systems. They plotted these barrier heights against the dimensionless parameter \(z = Z_p Z_t / (A_p^{1/3} + A_t^{1/3})\) and fitted the data with third-degree polynomials to obtain empirical formulae for \(B_{fu}\) and \(B_{int}\). These empirical barriers were then compared with predictions from the Bass, Christenson and Winther (CW), Broglia and Winther (BW), Aage Winther (AW), Siwek-Wilczynska and Wilczynski (SWW), Skyrme energy density function (SEDFM), and Sao Paulo optical potential (SPP) models.

## Key numbers

*   Empirical fusion barrier formula: \(B_{fu} = -0.22 + (0.85)z + (0.001)z^2 - (1.75 \times 10^{-6})z^3\) MeV, valid for \(8 \leq z \leq 278\).
*   Empirical interaction barrier formula: \(B_{int} = -13.47 + (1.26)z - (0.002)z^2 + (4.03 \times 10^{-6})z^3\) MeV, valid for \(59 \leq z \leq 313\).
*   For the system \(^{12}\)C+\(^{14}\)N (\(z = 8.93\)), the experimental fusion barrier is 7.00 MeV; the BW model predicts 7.11 MeV, while the Bass model predicts 5.19 MeV.
*   For the system \(^{84}\)Kr+\(^{238}\)U (\(z = 313.14\)), the experimental interaction barrier is 333 MeV; the Bass model prediction is not given in the text for this specific system, but the text states the present model calculations are much lower than the Bass potential model predictions.
*   The Broglia and Winther model uses a Woods-Saxon potential with depth \(V_0 = 16\pi a \gamma R_p R_t / (R_p + R_t)\), diffuseness \(a = 0.63\) fm, and surface energy coefficient \(\gamma_0 = 0.95\) MeV fm\(^{-2}\) with \(k_s = 1.8\).

## Context

This work extends the empirical approach of Vaz (1981) and others by constructing separate polynomial fits for fusion and interaction barriers from a larger set of experimental data, and by systematically comparing them to multiple theoretical models including the Bass (1974, 1977), Christenson and Winther (1976), Broglia and Winther (1991), Aage Winther (1995), Siwek-Wilczynska and Wilczynski (2004), Skyrme energy density function (Zanganeh, 2012), and Sao Paulo optical potential (Freitas, 2008) models. The results enable future experiments near the Coulomb barrier, particularly for planning superheavy element synthesis (which requires accurate fusion barriers) and quasi-elastic scattering experiments (which require accurate interaction barriers), by providing a simple empirical estimate and identifying the BW model as the most reliable among the tested theoretical approaches.
