# Nuclear Effective Field Theories: Reverberations of the early days

> Effective field theories are recognized nowadays as the framework to describe low-energy nuclear structure and reactions consistently with the underlying theory of the strong interactions, QCD. It was not always so. As we celebrate the 30 years of the first publications, I collect here some memories from those early days. I also discuss some of the key issues that were raised back then and, despite all progress, are still with us. Dedicated to the memory of Steven Weinberg, whose ideas will not die, wherever they may lead.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while chiral effective field theory (Chiral EFT) has become the standard framework for low-energy nuclear physics over the past 30 years, fundamental consistency issues identified in the early 1990s, particularly regarding renormalization, power counting, and the role of few-body forces, remain unresolved despite widespread adoption of the approach for fitting data.

## Method

The author presents a historical and conceptual review of the development of nuclear EFT, beginning with Weinberg's 1990 proposal to apply chiral perturbation theory to nuclear forces. The method involves constructing nuclear potentials as sums of irreducible diagrams ordered by a power-counting scheme based on the ratio of momentum scales Q/M_QCD, where M_QCD ~ 1 GeV, and then solving the Schrödinger equation with these potentials truncated at a given order. The paper traces how early calculations of two-nucleon phase shifts, three-nucleon forces, and reactions like pion photoproduction were performed, and contrasts the original EFT philosophy with later "hybrid" approaches that treat chiral potentials as black boxes.

## Key numbers

* The characteristic nonperturbative QCD scale M_QCD ~ 1 GeV sets the breakdown scale of the EFT expansion.
* The pion mass m_pi ~ 140 MeV defines the typical momentum scale Q where the expansion is applied.
* The nucleon mass m_N = O(M_QCD) provides an m_N/Q enhancement in reducible diagrams that causes the breakdown of perturbation theory for two or more nucleons.
* The leading-order potential includes one-pion exchange and one contact interaction in each S wave, with next-to-leading-order corrections vanishing under parity invariance.
* The N^3LO two-nucleon potential contains seven additional two-nucleon contact interactions and the first two-pion exchange force consistent with chiral symmetry.
* A fit to the Nijmegen partial-wave analysis was performed for three different cutoff values, though the paper notes they could not match the fitting standard of chi^2/dof ~ 1 up to pion production threshold.

## Context

This paper extends the historical narrative of nuclear EFT from Weinberg's foundational papers (Weinberg 1990, 1991, 1992) and the author's own early work with Ordóñez (1992, 1993, 1995) and Beane (1994, 1995). It contrasts the original EFT philosophy, where power counting and renormalization consistency are paramount, with the later approach of groups like Epelbaum (2008) and Machleidt (2011) who prioritized fitting two-nucleon data to high precision. The paper enables future work by identifying four unresolved issues: the iteration of singular potentials beyond leading order (the "Cohen syndrome"), the relative importance of few-body versus two-body forces, consistency of reaction kernels with the potential, and the role of the Delta isobar as an explicit degree of freedom.
