# Correlated Gaussians and low-discrepancy sequences

> Within the Correlated Gaussian Method the parameters of the Gaussian basis functions are often chosen stochastically using pseudo-random sequences. We show that alternative low-discrepancy sequences, also known as quasi-random sequences, provide bases of better quality.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that using low-discrepancy (quasi-random) sequences to select the parameters of Gaussian basis functions in the Correlated Gaussian Method yields bases of better quality than the conventional approach using pseudo-random sequences, though the improvement is shown primarily through numerical convergence tests rather than a formal proof.

## Method

The author compares the convergence of the ground-state energy for two few-body systems (Ps- and a triton-like system tdμ) when the Gaussian basis parameters are chosen using either pseudo-random sequences or low-discrepancy (quasi-random) sequences. The quality of the basis is assessed by plotting the relative energy error (E - E_x)/|E_x| as a function of the basis size n, with separate curves for the pseudo-random and quasi-random sampling strategies.

## Key numbers

- For the Ps- system, with basis sizes up to n = 400, the quasi-random sequence achieves a relative energy error below 0.0001 (10^-4) while the pseudo-random sequence remains above 0.001 (10^-3) at the same basis size.
- For the tdμ system, with basis sizes up to n = 200, the quasi-random sequence reaches a relative energy error near 0.0001 (10^-4) while the pseudo-random sequence remains above 0.001 (10^-3) at the same basis size.

## Context

This work extends the standard Correlated Gaussian Method, where basis parameters are typically chosen stochastically using pseudo-random sequences, by introducing low-discrepancy sequences as an alternative sampling strategy. The results suggest that quasi-random sequences can provide more efficient basis sets for few-body calculations, potentially enabling more accurate solutions with smaller basis sizes in nuclear physics and atomic physics applications.
