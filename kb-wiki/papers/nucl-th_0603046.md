# Multifragmentation vs. Evaporation vs. Binary-Decay in Fragment Production

> This paper presents part of an internal LANL Progress Report on completion of the "S" and "G" versions of the improved Cascade-Exciton Model (CEM03.01) and the Los Alamos Quark-Gluon String Model (LAQGSM.03.01) codes. The "S" versions consider fragmentation of compound nuclei produced after the preequilibrium stage of reactions for excitation energies above 2A MeV using the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) by Botvina et al. ("S" stands for SMM), while the "G" versions describe evaporation/fission stages of reactions using the fission-like binary-decay model GEMINI of Charity et al. ("G" stands for GEMINI) instead of using the the Generalized Evaporation Model GEM2 of Furihata incorporated into the standard versions of these codes. We present here an analysis of the recent 660 MeV p + 129I and 3.65 GeV p + 112Sn JINR measurements, of the new COSY data on 1.2 GeV p + (13 nuclei from Al to Th), of the 300 MeV and 1 GeV p + 56Fe data measured at GSI in inverse kinematics, and of the new GSI data on 1 GeV/nucleon 124Xe and 136Xe + Pb. To better understand the mechanisms of fragment production, we discuss several calculated but not-yet-measured kinematic characteristics of products of these reactions, which are predicted to be quite different by SMM, GEMINI, and GEM2. We find these kinematic quantities to be potentially useful in differentiating these reaction mechanisms if they can be measured in future experiments.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the "S" (Statistical Multifragmentation Model) and "G" (GEMINI binary-decay) versions of the CEM03.01 and LAQGSM03.01 codes can both describe measured fragment yields from several proton-induced and heavy-ion reactions, but the available experimental data on production cross sections alone are insufficient to distinguish whether multifragmentation or binary-decay mechanisms are responsible for fragment production in the intermediate mass region.

## Method

The authors implemented the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) of Botvina et al. and the fission-like binary-decay model GEMINI of Charity et al. into the Cascade-Exciton Model (CEM03.01) and Los Alamos Quark-Gluon String Model (LAQGSM03.01) codes without parameter fitting, creating "S" and "G" versions respectively. They compared calculated mass distributions, cross sections, and kinematic quantities (mean parallel velocity, mean production angle, forward-backward ratio, mean kinetic energy) against experimental data from 660 MeV p + 129I, 3.65 GeV p + 112Sn, 300 MeV and 1 GeV p + 56Fe, 1.2 GeV p on 13 targets from Al to Th, and 1 GeV/nucleon 124Xe and 136Xe + Pb reactions.

## Key numbers

* 660 MeV p + 129I: Standard CEM03.01 fails to describe isotopes with mass numbers 26 < A < 63; both "S" and "G" versions reproduce these products, with results very similar except for light fragments 10 < A < 20 where no experimental data exist.
* 300 MeV p + 56Fe: Standard versions strongly underestimate fragments with A < 32; "S" version provides more energetic light fragments than "G" version, with differences diminishing for isotopes with Z >= 20.
* 1 GeV p + 56Fe: Standard CEM03.01 describes cross sections better than "S" or "G" versions, especially for products with 5 < A < 16 and Z < 10.
* 3.65 GeV p + 112Sn: For fragments with 8 <= A <= 16, "G" version predicts yield about a factor of five higher than standard CEM03.01, while "S" version predicts almost two orders of magnitude more than CEM03.01.
* 1.2 GeV p + Ag: High-energy tail contributions (T > 100 MeV) to total cross sections are about 17% for p, 3% for d, 1% for t, 2% for 3He, and 0.4% for 4He.
* 1 GeV/A 124Xe + 208Pb: Predictions for yields of isotopes with 15 < A < 31 differ by up to an order of magnitude or more between standard, "S", and "G" versions.

## Context

This work extends the authors' development of CEM03.01 and LAQGSM03.01 event generators for use in MCNP6, MARS, and MCNPX transport codes, addressing deficiencies in describing light- and medium-mass fragments from intermediate-mass targets that cannot fission or fragment using the GEM2 model of Furihata. The study compares two alternative approaches: multifragmentation via SMM (Botvina et al.) and binary-decay via GEMINI (Charity et al.), finding that both can describe available production cross sections but that kinematic quantities (mean kinetic energy, production angles, forward-backward ratios) predicted differently by the models could distinguish mechanisms if measured in future experiments. The paper enables future experimental work to identify the dominant reaction mechanisms for fragment production in the intermediate mass region.
