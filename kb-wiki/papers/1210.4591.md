# The Oblique Basis Method from an Engineering Point of View

> The oblique basis method is reviewed from engineering point of view related to vibration and control theory. Examples are used to demonstrate and relate the oblique basis in nuclear physics to the equivalent mathematical problems in vibration theory. The mathematical techniques, such as principal coordinates and root locus, used by vibration and control theory engineers are shown to be relevant to the Richardson - Gaudin pairing-like problems in nuclear physics.

# The Oblique Basis Method from an Engineering Point of View

## Key claim
The oblique basis method, which uses non-orthogonal basis sets to treat strongly coupled competing modes in nuclear many-body systems, is mathematically analogous to the normal mode analysis in mechanical vibration theory and the Evans root-locus method in control theory; the Richardson-Gaudin pairing equations for identical particles are shown to be formally equivalent to the stability equation \(L(s) = -1/K\) from control theory.

## Method
The paper reviews the oblique basis method by comparing two exactly solvable nuclear interactions, Elliott's SU(3) quadrupole-quadrupole model and the Richardson-Gaudin pairing interaction, with mechanical vibration problems involving mass-spring systems. It derives the generalized eigenvalue problem for non-orthogonal bases in nuclear physics and shows its structural similarity to the normal mode eigenvalue problem \([k]\vec{X}_i - \omega_i^2 [m]\vec{X}_i = 0\) in engineering. The Richardson-Gaudin equations for the T=1 proton-neutron pairing Hamiltonian are presented, and the identical-particle limit of these equations is shown to match the form of the Evans root-locus equation \(L(s) = -1/K\) used for stability analysis in control theory.

## Key numbers
- The T=1 proton-neutron pairing Hamiltonian includes a pairing strength \(g\) and isospin breaking parameter \(\Delta\), with eigenvalues given by \(E = \sum_{\alpha=1}^M e_\alpha + \frac{\Delta}{2}\sum_{\beta=1}^{M+T_0+t} w_\beta + \sum_j \varepsilon_j \left[\frac{v_j}{2}(2+\Delta)-\Delta t_j\right] + \frac{g}{2} T_0(T_0-1)\).
- The Richardson-Gaudin equations for the pairing problem involve variables \(z_j = 2\varepsilon_j\) relating to single-particle energies, with the coupling constant \(g\) appearing in the equation \(\frac{1}{g} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^L \frac{v_i+2t_i-2}{z_i-e_\alpha} + \sum_{\alpha'(\neq\alpha)=1}^M \frac{2}{e_{\alpha'}-e_\alpha} - \sum_{\beta=1}^{M+T_0+t} \frac{1}{\omega_\beta-e_\alpha}\).
- The identical-particle Richardson equation reduces to \(\frac{1}{g} = \sum_{i=1}^L \frac{1}{e_\alpha-z_i} + \sum_{\alpha'(\neq\alpha)=1}^M \frac{2}{e_{\alpha'}-e_\alpha}\), which matches the form of the Evans root-locus equation \(L(s) = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{A_i}{s-p_i} = -\frac{1}{K}\).

## Context
This work extends the oblique basis approach developed by Gueorguiev and collaborators (2001, 2005, 2007) for treating strong mixing between competing collective modes in nuclei, specifically the Elliott SU(3) quadrupole-quadrupole interaction and the pairing interaction. It draws a novel analogy between the Richardson-Gaudin pairing equations (Richardson, 1962; Gaudin, 1976) and the Evans root-locus method (Evans, 1948) from control theory, suggesting that techniques such as principal coordinates and root-locus analysis from engineering can be applied to nuclear pairing problems. The paper enables future cross-disciplinary collaboration by identifying the mathematical equivalence between the generalized eigenvalue problem in nuclear structure and the normal mode analysis in mechanical vibrations, and between the Richardson-Gaudin equations and control theory stability analysis.
