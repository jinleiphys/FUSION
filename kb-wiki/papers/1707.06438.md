# Weinberg eigenvalues for chiral nucleon-nucleon interactions

> We perform a comprehensive Weinberg eigenvalue analysis of a representative set of modern nucleon-nucleon interactions derived within chiral effective field theory. Our set contains local, semilocal, and nonlocal potentials, developed by Gezerlis, Tews et al. (2013); Epelbaum, Krebs, and Meißner (2015); and Entem, Machleidt, and Nosyk (2017) as well as Carlsson, Ekström et al. (2016), respectively. The attractive eigenvalues show a very similar behavior for all investigated interactions, whereas the magnitudes of the repulsive eigenvalues sensitively depend on the details of the regularization scheme of the short- and long-range parts of the interactions. We demonstrate that a direct comparison of numerical cutoff values of different interactions is in general misleading due to the different analytic form of regulators; for example, a cutoff value of $R=0.8$ fm for the semilocal interactions corresponds to about $R=1.2$ fm for the local interactions. Our detailed comparison of Weinberg eigenvalues provides various insights into idiosyncrasies of chiral potentials for different orders and partial waves. This shows that Weinberg eigenvalues could be used as a helpful monitoring scheme when constructing new interactions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that Weinberg eigenvalues provide a quantitative diagnostic for comparing chiral nucleon-nucleon interactions across different regularization schemes, but the attractive eigenvalues behave similarly for all interactions while the repulsive eigenvalues depend sensitively on the details of the short- and long-range regularization, meaning that direct comparison of numerical cutoff values between different interaction families is generally misleading.

## Method

The authors solved the eigenvalue equation \(G_0(W)V |\Psi_\nu(W)\rangle = \eta_\nu(W) |\Psi_\nu(W)\rangle\) in a partial-wave representation for the free-space propagator, using Gaussian quadrature momentum grids and the Sokhotski-Plemelj theorem to handle poles at positive energies. They computed Weinberg eigenvalues for four chiral NN potential families (local GT+, semilocal EKM, nonlocal sim, and nonlocal EMN) at multiple chiral orders and cutoff values, analyzing the energy dependence from \(E=0\) to \(300\) MeV in various partial-wave channels including \(^1S_0\) and \(^3S_1-^3D_1\).

## Key numbers

- For the semilocal EKM potentials, a cutoff value of \(R_0 = 0.8\) fm corresponds to about \(R_0 = 1.2\) fm for the local GT+ potentials, based on comparison of short-range regulator functions in momentum space.
- The distance \(r^*\) where the long-range regulator function takes the value \(f_\text{long}(r^*, R_0)=1/2\) is \(r^*_{\text{GT+}} = 1.10\) fm for \(R_0=1.2\) fm and \(r^*_{\text{EKM}} = 1.19\) fm for \(R_0=0.8\) fm.
- For the semilocal EKM potentials, the mapping between momentum-space cutoff \(\Lambda\) and coordinate-space cutoff \(R_0\) gives \(\Lambda = 493\) MeV for \(R_0 = 0.8\) fm and \(\Lambda = 329\) MeV for \(R_0 = 1.2\) fm.
- For the local GT+ potentials, the corresponding mapping gives \(\Lambda = 614\) MeV for \(R_0 = 0.8\) fm and \(\Lambda = 409\) MeV for \(R_0 = 1.2\) fm.
- The phase shifts in the \(^1S_0\) channel are well reproduced using only \(n_\text{max} \sim 5-10\) Weinberg eigenvalues in the elemental phase shift expansion.

## Context

This work extends the Weinberg eigenvalue analysis originally developed by Weinberg (1963) for understanding bound states and Born series convergence, and applies it to modern chiral EFT potentials including those by Gezerlis, Tews et al. (2013), Epelbaum, Krebs, and Meissner (2015), Entem, Machleidt, and Nosyk (2017), and Carlsson, Ekstrom et al. (2016). The results enable a systematic comparison of regularization artifacts across different interaction families and suggest that Weinberg eigenvalues could serve as a monitoring scheme when constructing new chiral interactions, complementing previous applications of the method to nuclear matter and pairing by Ramanan and Urban (2013) and Srinivas and Ramanan (2016).
