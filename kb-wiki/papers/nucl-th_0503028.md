# Effects of jet quenching on the hydrodynamical evolution of quark-gluon plasma

> We study the effects of jet quenching on the hydrodynamical evolution of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) fluid created in a heavy-ion collision. In jet quenching, a hard QCD parton, before fragmenting into a jet of hadrons, deposits a fraction of its energy in the medium, leading to suppressed production of high-pT hadrons. Assuming that the deposited energy quickly thermalizes, we simulate the subsequent hydrodynamic evolution of the QGP fluid. For partons moving at supersonic speed, v_p > c_s, and sufficiently large energy loss, a shock wave forms leading to conical flow [1]. The PHENIX Collaboration recently suggested that observed structures in the azimuthal angle distribution [2] might be caused by conical flow. We show here that conical flow produces different angular structures than predicted in [1] and that, for phenomenologically acceptable values of parton energy loss, conical flow effects are too weak to explain the structures seen by PHENIX [2].

## Key claim

The authors show that, for phenomenologically acceptable values of parton energy loss (14 GeV/fm at a reference entropy density), conical flow effects from a supersonic parton traversing the quark-gluon plasma are too weak to explain the sharp azimuthal structures observed by the PHENIX Collaboration, and that the predicted Mach cone angle is not sharply defined due to the inhomogeneous and expanding background medium.

## Method

The authors model the quark-gluon plasma as an ideal fluid with vanishing net baryon density, using the (2+1)-dimensional relativistic hydrodynamic code AZHYDRO with longitudinal boost invariance. A fast parton moving at the speed of light deposits energy into the medium along its trajectory according to a phenomenological energy loss rate proportional to the local entropy density, with the deposited energy treated as a source term in the energy-momentum conservation equations. The resulting hydrodynamic evolution is simulated for central Au+Au collisions at RHIC, and the azimuthal distribution of pions at freeze-out is computed using the Cooper-Frye prescription.

## Key numbers

- Reference parton energy loss: \(dE/dx|_0 = 14\) GeV/fm at a reference entropy density \(s_0 = 140\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- Peak initial energy density: \(\varepsilon_0 = 30\) GeV/fm\(^3\) at initial proper time \(\tau_0 = 0.6\) fm/\(c\)
- Critical temperature for phase transition: \(T_c = 164\) MeV
- Freeze-out temperature: \(T_\mathrm{fo} = 100\) MeV
- Squared speed of sound in hadronic sector: \(c_s^2 \approx 0.15\)
- Width of Gaussian source for energy deposition: \(\sigma = 0.35\) fm (and 0.7 fm in some simulations)
- \(\phi\)-independent constant \((dN_{\pi^-}/dy d\phi)_\mathrm{no\ jet} = 27\) from central collisions without jets

## Context

This work extends the linearized hydrodynamic analysis of Mach cone formation in a static background by Shuryak (2005) to a full (2+1)-dimensional hydrodynamic simulation of an expanding fireball. The authors find that the conical flow pattern predicted by Shuryak (2005) is too weak to explain the azimuthal structures reported by the PHENIX Collaboration (Adler, 2005), and that the shoulders in the angular distribution arise from a combination of directed momentum transfer and backsplash rather than a clean Mach cone. The results also contradict the color wake field scenario of Stocker (2005) and Ruppert and Muller (2005), as the authors conclude that conical flow is unlikely to produce the sharp structures seen in the data.
