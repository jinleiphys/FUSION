# General Relativistic effects in the structure of massive white dwarfs

> In this work we investigate the structure of white dwarfs using the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations and compare our results with those obtained from Newtonian equations of gravitation in order to put in evidence the importance of General Relativity (GR) for the structure of such stars. We consider in this work for the matter inside white dwarfs two equations of state, frequently found in the literature, namely, the Chandrasekhar and Salpeter equations of state. We find that using Newtonian equilibrium equations, the radii of massive white dwarfs ($M>1.3M_{\odot}$) are overestimated in comparison with GR outcomes. For a mass of $1.415M_{\odot}$ the white dwarf radius predicted by GR is about 33\% smaller than the Newtonian one. Hence, in this case, for the surface gravity the difference between the general relativistic and Newtonian outcomes is about 65\%. We depict the general relativistic mass-radius diagrams as $M/M_{\odot}=R/(a+bR+cR^2+dR^3+kR^4)$, where $a$, $b$, $c$ and $d$ are parameters obtained from a fitting procedure of the numerical results and $k=(2.08\times 10^{-6}R_{\odot})^{-1}$, being $R_{\odot}$ the radius of the Sun in km. Lastly, we point out that GR plays an important role to determine any physical quantity that depends, simultaneously, on the mass and radius of massive white dwarfs.

## Key claim

General relativistic effects do not greatly affect the maximum mass of white dwarfs (reducing it by only about 3%), but they significantly reduce the radius of massive white dwarfs (M > 1.3 solar masses) compared to Newtonian calculations, with the effect becoming dramatic near the Chandrasekhar limit.

## Method

The authors solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for static, spherically symmetric white dwarfs using the Chandrasekhar equation of state for a fully degenerate relativistic electron gas, and compared the results with Newtonian and special-relativistic equilibrium equations. They integrated the hydrostatic equilibrium equations using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta method with central pressure as the initial condition, and also performed fits for the Salpeter equation of state with various compositions (He, C, O) and for mu_e = 2.154.

## Key numbers

- For a mass of 1.415 solar masses, the general relativistic radius is about 33% smaller than the Newtonian radius (1145 km vs 1708 km)
- For the same mass of 1.415 solar masses, the general relativistic surface gravity differs from the Newtonian value by about 65%
- The general relativistic maximum mass is 1.4154 solar masses, compared to 1.4546 solar masses in the Newtonian case (about 3% reduction)
- The general relativistic minimum radius is 1021 km, about three times larger than the Newtonian minimum radius of 329 km
- For the most massive white dwarf observed (EUVE J1659+440, mass 1.41 +/- 0.04 solar masses), the Newtonian radius is 37% larger than the general relativistic radius
- The central density for a 1.415 solar mass white dwarf in general relativity is 1.61 x 10^10 g/cm^3, about four times larger than the Newtonian value of 4.08 x 10^9 g/cm^3

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Kaplan (1949) and Chandrasekhar (1964) who first discussed general relativistic effects on white dwarf stability, and by Cohen et al. (1970) who studied oscillation periods. It contrasts with recent work on rotating white dwarfs by Rueda et al. (2010-2013) and Boshkayev et al. (2015), who found smaller differences between Newtonian and general relativistic results for rotating cases. The paper provides a fitting formula for the general relativistic mass-radius relation that can be used to calculate other stellar properties such as magnetic dipole field, moment of inertia, and gravitational redshift.
