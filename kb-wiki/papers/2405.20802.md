# First evidence for chiral wobbling of triaxial nuclei

> The appearance of wobbling motion and chirality in rotating triaxial nuclei is explained. The discovery of a new mode, chiral wobbling, in $^{74}$Br, is commented.

# First evidence for chiral wobbling of triaxial nuclei

## Key claim
The paper explains that the odd-odd nucleus $^{74}$Br exhibits a new rotational mode called "chiral wobbling," where the total angular momentum precesses around a tilted axis in a triaxial nucleus that also displays chiral partner bands. The claim is hedged by noting that only one of the expected two chiral wobbling bands (B3) was observed in the experiment, with the chiral wobbling partner band not detected.

## Method
The authors analyzed prompt double and triple gamma coincidence data from the AFRODITE array following a fusion-evaporation reaction populating $^{74}$Br. Lifetimes of excited states were determined from Doppler shifts in coincidence spectra, and absolute reduced transition probabilities were extracted using angular distribution from oriented state (ADO) ratios, linear polarization values, and level lifetimes. A model coupling a triaxial rotor with a proton $h_{11/2}$ hole and a neutron $h_{11/2}$ particle was used to reproduce the observed energies and transition probabilities.

## Key numbers
- The partial level scheme for $^{74}$Br includes rotational bands B1 (yrast $\Delta I=1$ band), B2 (chiral partner $\Delta I=1$ band at somewhat higher energy), and B3 (chiral wobbling band).
- Transition intensities in the level scheme are normalized to the 383 keV transition as 100.
- The assignment of B3 as a chiral wobbling band is established by enhanced $\Delta I=1$ $E2$ transition probabilities to B1 (explicit numerical values for these transition probabilities are not provided in the text).
- The mass region for chiral partner bands includes $A=80$, 110, 130, and 190 (the $^{74}$Br nucleus lies in the $A=80$ region).

## Context
This work extends the study of wobbling motion in triaxial nuclei, previously restricted to a few even-even nuclei and odd-A nuclei (reviewed by Frauendorf (2024)), into the domain of odd-odd nuclei where chirality coexists with wobbling. The concept of nuclear chirality in rotating triaxial nuclei was first proposed by Frauendorf and Meng (1997), and chiral partner bands have since been identified in mass regions around $A=80$, 110, 130, and 190 (reviewed by Meng (2024)). The discovery of chiral wobbling in $^{74}$Br affirms the existence of chirality in the $A=80$ mass region and opens the possibility of searching for the missing chiral wobbling partner band in future experiments.
