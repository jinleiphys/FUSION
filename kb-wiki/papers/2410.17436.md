# Subshell gaps and onsets of collectivity from proton and neutron pairing gap correlations

> Throughout the nuclear chart, particle-hole correlations give rise to giant resonances and, together with the proton-neutron interaction, deformation and rotational bands. In order to shed light on many-body correlations in open-shell nuclei, I explore macroscopic properties that could manifest from the collective behavior of protons and neutrons. Intuitively, the correlation of proton and neutron Cooper pairs can be inferred from the respective pairing gaps, that can precisely be extracted from the AME 2020 atomic mass evaluation through odd-even atomic mass differences. This work shows that the combination of large and close-lying proton and neutron pairing gaps is sensitive to onsets of collectivity and subshell gaps in superfluid nuclei, away from major shell closures. Trends of reduced transition probabilities or B(E2) values -- which describe the collective overlap between the wave functions of initial and final nuclear states -- are revealed in overall agreement with data. Specially interesting is the peak of collectivity in the tin isotopes at $^{110}$Sn, instead of at midshell, as expected by large-scale shell-model calculations; a situation that has astounded the nuclear physics community for quite some time.

## Key claim

The correlation of proton and neutron pairing gaps, quantified by the relative neutron-proton pairing gap Δ_r, is sensitive to onsets of collectivity and subshell gaps in superfluid nuclei away from major shell closures, showing sharper distinctions than excitation energies or separation energies for identifying structural changes within isotopic chains.

## Method

Proton and neutron pairing gaps Δ_p and Δ_n were extracted from the AME 2020 atomic mass evaluation using symmetric five-point mass difference formulas applied to even-even Ni, Cd, Sn, and Te isotopes. The relative neutron-proton pairing gap Δ_r was defined as the absolute value of (Δ_p² - Δ_n²), combining a resonant factor (Δ_p - Δ_n) that measures proximity of the pairing gaps and an energy factor (Δ_p + Δ_n) that accounts for the energy available before breaking Cooper pairs. Trends of Δ_r were compared with experimental B(E2; 0₁⁺ → 2₁⁺) values and 2₁⁺ excitation energies from the 2016 tables of B(E2) values.

## Key numbers

- For Ni isotopes, Δ_r ≈ 0.6 MeV for ⁶⁰,⁶²,⁶⁴Ni, indicating similar collectivity
- For Cd isotopes, sharp minima at ¹¹⁸-¹²⁰Cd with Δ_r values of 0.32-0.34 MeV
- For Sn isotopes, sharp minimum at ¹¹⁰Sn with Δ_r = 0.63 MeV, rather than at midshell
- For Te isotopes, minimum at ¹²²Te with Δ_r = 0.38 MeV
- For ⁵⁸Ni, Δ_r = 0.985 MeV; for ⁶⁰Ni, Δ_r = 0.635 MeV; for ⁶²Ni, Δ_r = 0.608 MeV
- For ¹⁰⁸Sn, Δ_r = 0.791 MeV; for ¹¹²Sn, Δ_r = 0.799 MeV; for ¹¹⁶Sn, Δ_r = 1.300 MeV
- For ¹⁰⁴Sn, Δ_r = 1.076 MeV; for ¹⁰⁶Sn, Δ_r = 1.010 MeV
- For ⁶⁸Ni, Δ_r = 1.555 MeV, corresponding to the smallest E2 strength in the Ni isotopes

## Context

This work extends the author's previous study (Orce 2014) by incorporating the 2020 Atomic Mass Evaluation and 2016 tables of B(E2) values, which substantially improve earlier compilations in accuracy and range. The approach has been supported by more precise calculations using generalized seniority and large-scale shell-model calculations by Maheshwari (2016, 2019) and Zuker (2021), which converge on the Lipkin-Nogami pairing formalism. The peak of collectivity in tin isotopes at ¹¹⁰Sn rather than at midshell contradicts expectations from large-scale shell-model calculations and has been an open puzzle in the nuclear physics community; the work enables further exploration of alpha-type correlations, which are known to be relevant in tin isotopes from studies by Sandulescu (2012) and Baran (2016).
