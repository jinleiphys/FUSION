# About the parity-non-conserving asymmetry in n+p--->d+gamma

> The parity-non-conserving (pnc) asymmetry in \vec{n} + p \to d+γat thermal energies has recently been calculated using effective field-theory methods. A comparison of this calculation with much more elaborate calculations performed in the 70's is made. This allows one to assess the validity of this new approach as presently used. It is found to overshoot the almost exact calculations by a factor close to 2 for the contribution involving the ^3S_1 component of both the initial and final states. This is much larger than anticipated by the authors. This discrepancy is analyzed and found to originate from the over-simplified description of the deuteron and capture states which underlies the new approach. The claim that earlier determinations of the sign would be in error is also examined. It is found that the sign discrepancy is most probably due, instead, to the fact that the pion-nucleon interaction referred to by the authors corresponds to a parity-non-conserving potential with a sign opposite to what is currently used. Some estimates and constraints relative to the pnc piNN coupling, h^1_π, which the above asymmetry is dependent on, are reviewed. Further details are given in an Appendix.

## Key claim

The effective field theory calculation of the parity-non-conserving asymmetry in n + p → d + γ at leading order overshoots the almost exact calculations from the 1970s by a factor close to 2 for the contribution involving the ³S₁ component of both the initial and final states, which is much larger than the 30% uncertainty claimed by the authors of that calculation.

## Method

The paper compares the recent effective field theory calculation by Kaplan et al. (2000) with much more elaborate calculations performed in the 1970s using realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials (Hamada-Johnston, Reid-soft-core, de Tourreil-Sprung, and Paris potential models). The author derives analytic expressions for the asymmetry using zero-range wave functions (which reproduce the effective field theory result) and then progressively improves them using Hulthen wave functions and Danilov's dispersion relation approach to quantify the effects of finite-range forces, short-range repulsion, and tensor force contributions.

## Key numbers

- The zero-range (effective field theory leading order) result gives A_γ = -0.187 h¹_π (using g²_πNN/4π = 14.4, m_π = 0.7 fm⁻¹, M_N = 4.76 fm⁻¹, μ_p - μ_n = 4.706, α = 0.232 fm⁻¹, a_s = -23.7 fm)
- Realistic potential model results: Hamada-Johnston gives A_γ = -0.109 h¹_π, Reid-soft-core gives A_γ = -0.114 h¹_π, de Tourreil-Sprung gives A_γ = -0.107 h¹_π
- The total reduction factor from the zero-range value to realistic values is approximately 1.9-2.0
- The finite-range effect from Hulthen wave functions gives a reduction factor of 1.33
- The Danilov approach with effective range r_t = 1.765 fm gives a reduction factor of approximately 1.3 for the scattering state
- Short-range repulsion in S-states gives an extra reduction factor of approximately 1.15-1.20
- Repulsion in the ³P₁ state gives a reduction factor of approximately 1.25-1.30
- The relation from earlier calculations is A_γ = -0.11 h¹_π with an uncertainty of about 5%
- The updated theoretical range for the pnc coupling is 0 < h¹_π < 2.5 × 10⁻⁷
- The ILL experiment gives h¹_π = (1.4 ± 4.4) × 10⁻⁷
- The ¹⁸F limit gives h¹_π ≤ 1.3 × 10⁻⁷

## Context

This work extends the earlier calculations of the pnc asymmetry by Tadić (1970s), Desplanques (1970s), McKellar, Gari, and Morimatsu, which used realistic NN potentials and found the asymmetry to be rather independent of the strong interaction model. It directly contradicts the recent effective field theory calculation by Kaplan et al. (2000), which obtained a larger value with opposite sign (A_γ = +0.17 h¹_π). The paper enables a proper extraction of the pnc πNN coupling h¹_π from the upcoming LANSCE measurement by showing that the effective field theory approach as currently used is unreliable for this short-range process, and that the earlier benchmark calculations should be used instead.
