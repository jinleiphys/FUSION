# Nuclear limits on properties of pulsars and gravitational waves

> Pulsars are among the most mysterious astrophysical objects in the Universe and are believed to be rotating neutron stars formed in supernova explosions. They are unique testing grounds of dense matter theories and gravitational physics and also provide links among nuclear physics, particle physics and General Relativity. Neutron stars may exhibit some of the most extreme and exotic characteristics that could not be found elsewhere in the Universe. Their properties are largely determined by the equation of state (EOS) of neutron-rich matter, which is the chief ingredient in calculating neutron star structure and properties of related phenomena, such as gravitational wave emission from deformed pulsars. Presently, the EOS of neutron-rich matter is still very uncertain mainly due to the poorly known density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy especially at supra-saturation densities. Nevertheless, significant progress has been made recently in constraining the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy mostly at sub-saturation densities using terrestrial nuclear reactions. While there are still some uncertainties especially at supra-saturation densities, these constraints could provide useful information on the limits of the global properties of pulsars and the gravitational waves to be expected from them. Here we review our recent work on constraining properties of pulsars and gravitational radiation with data from terrestrial nuclear laboratories.

# Nuclear limits on properties of pulsars and gravitational waves

## Key claim

The density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy, partially constrained by terrestrial heavy-ion reaction data at sub-saturation densities, can provide useful limits on the global properties of pulsars and the gravitational waves expected from them, although significant uncertainties remain especially at supra-saturation densities where the symmetry energy could be much softer than current extrapolations.

## Method

The authors used the isospin and momentum-dependent MDI interaction within the IBUU04 transport model to constrain the nuclear symmetry energy from isospin diffusion data in heavy-ion reactions. They then applied the resulting equation of state (EOS) with two limiting parameterizations (x=0 and x=-1) as boundaries consistent with terrestrial data to solve the general relativistic structure equations for both static and rapidly rotating neutron stars, using the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation and its extensions for rotating configurations.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear matter saturation density: ρ₀ ≈ 0.16 fm⁻³
- Incompressibility K₀ of cold symmetric nuclear matter at saturation: 211 MeV
- Symmetry energy at saturation density: 31.62 MeV (MDI), 32.60 MeV (APR), 33.71 MeV (DBHF+Bonn B)
- Slope parameter L of symmetry energy: 62.1 MeV (x=0) and 107.4 MeV (x=-1)
- Curvature parameter K_sym of symmetry energy: -88.6 MeV (x=0) and 94.1 MeV (x=-1)
- Maximum baryon density reached in ¹³²Sn+¹²⁴Sn reactions at 400 MeV/nucleon: about 2 times normal nuclear matter density
- High density phase duration in that reaction: about 15 fm/c from 5 to 20 fm/c
- Fastest observed pulsar J1748-2446ad: rotational frequency ν = 716 Hz
- Kepler frequency threshold for pulsars above 1 M⊙: 1000 Hz
- Unconfirmed X-ray burst oscillation from XTE J1739-285: 1122 Hz

## Context

This work reviews and extends the authors' previous studies (Krastev, Li, and collaborators, 2008-2009) on constraining neutron star properties using terrestrial nuclear data. It builds upon the experimental constraints on the symmetry energy from isospin diffusion (Tsang, 2004) and neutron skin measurements, and connects to the broader effort of using heavy-ion reactions to inform astrophysics, as reviewed by Danielewicz, Lacey, and Lynch (2002). The partially constrained EOS enables predictions for pulsar structure and gravitational wave emission, while the uncertain high-density behavior of the symmetry energy remains a key open question for future experiments with radioactive beams.
