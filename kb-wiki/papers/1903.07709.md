# Open Heavy-Flavor Production in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The ultra-relativistic heavy-ion programs at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider and the Large Hadron Collider have evolved into a phase of quantitative studies of Quantum Chromodynamics at very high temperatures. The charm and bottom hadron production offer unique insights into the remarkable transport properties and the microscopic structure of the Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP) created in these collisions. Heavy quarks, due to their large masses, undergo Brownian motion at low momentum, provide a window on hadronization mechanisms at intermediate momenta, and are expected to merge into a radiative-energy loss regime at high momentum. We review recent experimental and theoretical achievements on measuring a variety of heavy-flavor observables, characterizing the different regimes in momentum, extracting pertinent transport coefficients and deducing implications for the "inner workings" of the QGP medium.

## Key claim

The paper reviews how heavy-flavor observables (charm and bottom hadrons) provide unique and comprehensive insights into the transport properties and microscopic structure of the Quark-Gluon Plasma across three distinct momentum regimes, but the actual extraction of transport coefficients like the spatial diffusion coefficient \(2\pi T \mathcal{D}_s\) remains model-dependent and subject to significant uncertainties from bulk evolution models, hadronization mechanisms, and the interplay of elastic versus radiative energy loss.

## Method

The authors compile and compare experimental data on the nuclear modification factor \(R_{\mathrm{AA}}\) and elliptic flow \(v_2\) of \(D\)-mesons from RHIC (\(\sqrt{s_{_{\mathrm{NN}}}}=200\) GeV) and LHC (\(\sqrt{s_{_{\mathrm{NN}}}}=5.02\) TeV) collisions, and confront these data with multiple theoretical models including the TAMU model (non-perturbative elastic \(T\)-matrix interactions), PHSD (microscopic transport with elastic interactions), Nantes (MC@sHQ with pQCD elastic and radiative interactions), BAMPS parton cascade, and a Bayesian analysis from the Duke group. The review also surveys lattice-QCD results for the heavy-quark spatial diffusion coefficient and compares them to model calculations based on different elastic interaction schemes in the QGP.

## Key numbers

* The pseudo-critical transition temperature for QGP formation is established at \(T_{\mathrm{pc}} = 155-160\) MeV from lattice-QCD.
* The specific shear viscosity \(\eta/s\) extracted from light-hadron data is around 0.1-0.3, close to the conjectured quantum lower bound of \(1/4\pi\).
* The jet transport parameter \(\hat{q}/T^3 \simeq 3-6\) at a parton energy scale of 10 GeV from pQCD calculations.
* \(D\)-meson elliptic flow \(v_2\) peaks above 15% at \(p_T \simeq 3\) GeV/\(c\) and levels off around 5% for \(p_T \gtrsim 10\) GeV/\(c\) in 30-50% Pb+Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{_{\mathrm{NN}}}}=5.02\) TeV.
* The TAMU model gives \(2\pi T \mathcal{D}_s\) increasing from 3-4 at \(T_{\mathrm{pc}}\) to 8-10 at \(2T_{\mathrm{pc}}\); the Bayesian analysis by the Duke group extracts lower values near 2, also rising with temperature.
* The thermalization time of heavy quarks is estimated as \(\sim 1/(2m_Q) \sim 0.1\) fm/\(c\) for production, and a thermalization time of \(\tau_0 = 1\) fm/\(c\) is assumed for the bulk medium.

## Context

This review extends the quantitative study of QGP properties using heavy-flavor probes beyond earlier work on light-flavor hadrons and jet quenching, building on the foundational transport framework of Svetitsky (1987) and Rapp (2009). It synthesizes experimental results from RHIC (STAR collaboration) and LHC (ALICE and CMS collaborations) and compares them to theoretical approaches including the TAMU model (He 2014), PHSD (Song 2015), Nantes (Gossiaux 2009), and lattice-QCD calculations (Banerjee 2011, Ding 2012, Kaczmarek 2014). The work enables future precision measurements with upgraded detectors (ALICE, sPHENIX) to better constrain the temperature dependence of the heavy-quark diffusion coefficient and to distinguish between elastic and radiative energy loss mechanisms across momentum regimes.
