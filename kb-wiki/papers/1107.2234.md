# Nuclear astrophysics: the unfinished quest for the origin of the elements

> Half a century has passed since the foundation of nuclear astrophysics. Since then, this discipline has reached its maturity. Today, nuclear astrophysics constitutes a multidisciplinary crucible of knowledge that combines the achievements in theoretical astrophysics, observational astronomy, cosmochemistry and nuclear physics. New tools and developments have revolutionized our understanding of the origin of the elements: supercomputers have provided astrophysicists with the required computational capabilities to study the evolution of stars in a multidimensional framework; the emergence of high-energy astrophysics with space-borne observatories has opened new windows to observe the Universe, from a novel panchromatic perspective; cosmochemists have isolated tiny pieces of stardust embedded in primitive meteorites, giving clues on the processes operating in stars as well as on the way matter condenses to form solids; and nuclear physicists have measured reactions near stellar energies, through the combined efforts using stable and radioactive ion beam facilities. This review provides comprehensive insight into the nuclear history of the Universe and related topics: starting from the Big Bang, when the ashes from the primordial explosion were transformed to hydrogen, helium, and few trace elements, to the rich variety of nucleosynthesis mechanisms and sites in the Universe. Particular attention is paid to the hydrostatic processes governing the evolution of low-mass stars, red giants and asymptotic giant-branch stars, as well as to the explosive nucleosynthesis occurring in core-collapse and thermonuclear supernovae, gamma-ray bursts, classical novae, X-ray bursts, superbursts, and stellar mergers.

## Key claim

This review provides a comprehensive overview of nuclear astrophysics and nucleosynthesis, but the abstract's claim of "maturity" is qualified by the paper's emphasis on unresolved puzzles, most notably the "lithium problem," where predicted primordial lithium abundances disagree with observations at the 4-5 sigma level, indicating that fundamental questions about the origin of the elements remain unanswered.

## Method

The paper synthesizes results from multiple disciplines: theoretical astrophysics (stellar evolution models, multidimensional supercomputer simulations), observational astronomy (spectroscopy, cosmic microwave background measurements from WMAP), cosmochemistry (analysis of presolar grains in meteorites, solar wind samples from Genesis and Stardust missions), and nuclear physics (direct cross section measurements of reactions at stellar energies using stable and radioactive ion beam facilities). The authors review nucleosynthesis processes from Big Bang nucleosynthesis through hydrostatic burning in low-mass stars, red giants, and AGB stars, to explosive nucleosynthesis in supernovae, gamma-ray bursts, novae, X-ray bursts, and stellar mergers.

## Context

This review summarizes more than 50 years of progress since the foundational papers by Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler and Hoyle (1957) and Cameron (1957), which identified stars as the sites of nucleosynthesis. It extends earlier reviews by incorporating results from WMAP (Hinshaw 2009, Komatsu 2009), presolar grain analyses (Nittler 2008, Gyngard 2010, Nguyen 2010), and improved solar abundance determinations using 3-D hydrodynamic models (Asplund 2005, 2009). The paper enables future work by identifying key open questions, particularly the lithium problem in Big Bang nucleosynthesis, and by providing a comprehensive framework for interpreting new experimental data from radioactive ion beam facilities and space-borne observatories.
