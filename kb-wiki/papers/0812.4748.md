# Dynamical Origin of Power Spectra

> We discuss a possible origin of Tsallis' statistics from the correlation among constituents which reduces the phase space of the system. We also show that in the system of coupled linear harmonic oscillators can exhibit a Tsallis type behavior.

## Key claim

The paper argues that Tsallis-type power-law distributions can arise dynamically from strong correlations among constituents that reduce the accessible phase space, and that such distributions may represent a prethermalized state that emerges before full thermalization, rather than requiring the system to be in true thermal equilibrium.

## Method

The authors derive the Tsallis distribution from a variational principle that minimizes the energy of correlated q-body clusters under constraints on the total number of particles and the total number of clusters. They then test this scenario with two numerical models: a toy model of particles undergoing cluster-forming and energy-exchanging collisions, and a system of N coupled linear harmonic oscillators with random coupling matrices, where they compute the long-time average single-particle energy spectra.

## Key numbers

- In the toy model, the ratio r of cluster-forming collisions to energy-exchanging collisions was varied from r = 0.00001 to r = 0.1; for r = 0.01 the distribution converged 10 times faster than for r = 0 (the Boltzmann case).
- For the coupled harmonic oscillator system with N = 100 and semi-diagonal random coupling (alpha = 0.2), the inclusive spectrum was fitted by a Tsallis distribution with q = 1.05 and T = 0.9 times the average energy per particle.
- In the toy model, the Tsallis distribution is valid only for q less than 3/2, otherwise the energy expectation value diverges.

## Context

This work extends the concept of prethermalization, previously studied in quantum scalar fields by Berges (2004) and in the Hamiltonian mean field model, to provide a dynamical mechanism for Tsallis statistics. The authors argue that their correlated-cluster picture differs from Tsallis' original approach by minimizing the energy of correlated subsystems rather than maximizing a generalized entropy. The results suggest that Tsallis-type distributions may appear as quasi-stationary states in systems with long-range or semi-local interactions, and that the parameter q may evolve in time toward the Boltzmann-Gibbs limit q = 1.
