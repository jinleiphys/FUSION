# Effective Field Theory for Finite Systems with Spontaneously Broken Symmetry

> We extend effective field theory to the case of spontaneous symmetry breaking in genuinely finite quantum systems such as small superfluid systems, molecules or atomic nuclei, and focus on deformed nuclei. In finite superfluids, symmetry arguments alone relate the spectra of systems with different particle numbers. For systems with non-spherical intrinsic ground states such as atomic nuclei or molecules, symmetry arguments alone yield the universal features of the low-lying excitations as vibrations that are the heads of rotational bands. The low-lying excitations in deformed nuclei differ from those in molecules because of symmetry properties caused by pairing.

## Key claim

The paper extends effective field theory (EFT) to genuinely finite quantum systems with emergent symmetry breaking, where the ground state exhibits the full symmetry of the Hamiltonian but mean-field calculations break it. The key hedge is that the construction works only when the energy scales satisfy the hierarchy $\xi \ll \omega \ll \Lambda$, where $\xi$ is the rotational scale, $\omega$ the vibrational scale, and $\Lambda$ the breakdown scale; for deformed nuclei this hierarchy is only marginally met.

## Method

The authors construct an EFT by parameterizing the coset space ${\cal G}/{\cal H}$ with Nambu-Goldstone fields, singling out purely time-dependent zero modes (global rotations or phase rotations) that are treated separately from spatially dependent small-amplitude fluctuations. Invariants are built from the derivatives of the unitary transformation $U$ that generates the symmetry breaking, and the effective Lagrangian is constructed from these invariants using power counting based on the separation of energy scales. The Hamiltonian obtained by Legendre transformation is then quantized, with the zero modes generating rotational bands and the Nambu-Goldstone modes yielding harmonic oscillator spectra.

## Key numbers

- For rare-earth nuclei: rotational energy spacing $\xi \approx 80$ keV, lowest vibrational band head $\omega \approx 1$ MeV, pair-breaking scale $\Lambda \approx 2-3$ MeV
- For linear molecules such as CO$_2$: rotational scale $\xi \approx 1$ cm$^{-1}$, vibrational scale $\omega \approx 500$ cm$^{-1}$, electronic excitation scale $\Lambda \approx 10{,}000$ cm$^{-1}$
- For finite superfluids with broken $U(1)$ symmetry: the chemical potential $\mu$ and the energy $\Omega$ of long-wavelength excitations scale as $\mu \sim \Omega \sim N^{-1/3}$ for harmonically trapped noninteracting bosons, and as $\mu \sim \Omega \sim N^{-2/3}$ for finite uniform systems
- The moment of inertia of a deformed nucleus scales as $\propto A^{5/3}$, making the rotational frequency tend to zero like $A^{-5/3}$, faster than the wave number $\propto A^{-1/3}$ of massless modes

## Context

This work extends the EFT framework for spontaneous symmetry breaking, previously developed for infinite systems such as ferromagnets (Leutwyler 1994, Roman 1999) and QCD on finite lattices (Leutwyler 1987, Gasser 1988), to genuinely finite systems like deformed nuclei and molecules. It generalizes an earlier effective theory proposed by Papenbrock (2011) and re-derives results from the Bohr-Mottelson collective model and the interacting boson model (Arima and Iachello 1975) using only symmetry arguments. The approach enables model-independent predictions for low-lying spectra and transitions, and could address long-standing problems such as the overprediction of electromagnetic interband transitions in collective nuclear models (Garrett 2001, Rowe).
