# The lattice gas model with isospin-dependent interactions

> In this paper we continue the investigation of the lattice gas model. The main improvement is that we use two strengths for bonds: one between like particles and another between unlike particle to implement the isospin-dependence of nuclear force. The main effect is the elimination of unphysical clusters, like the dineutron or diproton. It is therefore a better description of nuclear system. Equation of state in mean field theory is obtained for nuclear matter as well as for $N\ne Z$ systems. Through numerical and analytical calculation we show that the new model maintains all the important features of the older model. We study the effect of the Coulomb interaction on multifragmentation of a compound system of A=86, Z=40 and also for A=197, Z=79. For the first case the Coulomb interaction has small effect. For the latter case the effect is much more pronounced but typical signatures of the lattice gas model such as a minimum (maximum) in the value of $τ$ ($S_2$) are still obtained but at a much lower temperature.

# The lattice gas model with isospin-dependent interactions

## Key claim

The improved lattice gas model with separate bond strengths for like particles (set to zero) and unlike particles (set to -5.33 MeV) eliminates unphysical clusters such as dineutrons and diprotons while preserving all the important qualitative features of the older single-bond model, including the characteristic minimum in the extracted exponent τ and maximum in the second moment S₂ as signals of a phase transition.

## Method

The authors place n nucleons (nₚ protons and nₙ neutrons) into N cubic lattice boxes at a prescribed freeze-out density, using a Metropolis algorithm to sample spatial configurations according to the lattice gas Hamiltonian with εₚₙ = -5.33 MeV and εₚₚ = εₙₙ = 0. Momenta are assigned from a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution at a given temperature, and clusters are defined by the condition that the relative kinetic energy between two nucleons in neighbouring cells is less than the bond strength. The equation of state is derived in the Bragg-Williams mean-field approximation, and Monte-Carlo simulations (1000 events per temperature) are performed for finite systems on a 7³ lattice to extract the specific heat Cᵥ, the second moment S₂, and the exponent τ from the cluster size distribution.

## Key numbers

- The neutron-proton bond strength is set to εₚₙ = -5.33 MeV, fixed by the binding energy of nuclear matter.
- The critical density in the Bragg-Williams mean-field approximation is ρ_c = 0.5 ρ₀, where ρ₀ is normal nuclear density.
- For a compound system of A=197, Z=79, the minimum in τ and maximum in S₂ shift from 4.8 MeV (without Coulomb) to about 2.4 MeV (with Coulomb).
- For a compound system of A=86, Z=40, the Coulomb interaction has a small effect, shifting the minimum in τ and maximum in S₂ to a slightly lower temperature.
- The critical temperature for an infinitely large lattice with one kind of bond is T_c = 1.1275 |εₚₙ|, approximately 6 MeV.
- In the Bethe-Peierls approximation, the critical temperature for the two-bond model (εₚₙ = -5.33 MeV, εₚₚ = 0) lies between 4 and 6 MeV, compared to between 6 and 8 MeV for the single-bond model.

## Context

This work extends the lattice gas model previously developed by Pan and Das Gupta (1995, 1996) and Das Gupta, Pan, and Tsang (1996), which used identical bond strengths for all nucleon pairs. The improvement addresses a known deficiency: the older model produced unphysical bound clusters like dineutrons and diprotons. By setting like-particle bonds to zero, the model becomes a better description of nuclear systems while maintaining the same qualitative signatures of a liquid-gas phase transition. The study also builds on earlier work by Das Gupta and Pan (1996) on Coulomb effects in multifragmentation, showing that for very large systems (A=394, Z=158) the minimum in τ disappears entirely due to Coulomb repulsion. The results enable future hybrid approaches where lattice gas initial conditions are propagated by molecular dynamics to account for Coulomb forces during cluster formation, as suggested by comparison with molecular dynamics calculations using forces from Strachan and Dorso (1997).
