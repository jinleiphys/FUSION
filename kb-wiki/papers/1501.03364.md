# Heat capacity of low density neutron matter: from quantum to classical regimes

> The heat capacity of neutron matter is studied over the range of densities and temperatures prevailing in neutron-star crusts, allowing for the transition to a superfluid phase at temperatures below some critical temperature $T_{sf}$ and including the transition to the classical limit. Finite temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov equations (FTHFB) are solved and compared to existing approximate expressions. In particular, the formula given by Levenfish and Yakovlev is found to reproduce the numerical results with a high degree of accuracy for temperatures $T\leq T_{sf}$. In the non-superfluid phase, $T\geq T_{sf}$, the linear approximation is valid only at temperature $T\ll T_{{\rm F} n}$ ($T_{{\rm F} n}$ being the Fermi temperature of the neutron gas) which is rarely the case in the shallow layers of the neutron star's crust. A non-perturbative interpolation between the quantal and the classical regimes is proposed here. The heat capacity, conveniently parametrized solely in terms of $T_{sf}$, $T_{{\rm F} n}$, and the neutron number density $n_n$, can be easily implemented in neutron-star cooling simulations.

## Key claim
The paper claims that the heat capacity of dilute neutron matter in neutron-star crusts can be accurately parametrized by a simple analytical expression depending only on the superfluid critical temperature, the Fermi temperature, and the neutron number density, but this expression is necessary because the commonly used linear approximation (valid for strongly degenerate neutrons) fails in the shallow crustal layers where neutrons may be non-degenerate or even classical.

## Method
The authors solved the finite-temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (FTHFB) equations self-consistently for homogeneous neutron matter, using a Skyrme-type effective interaction (LNS functional) for the mean field and a zero-range pairing interaction calibrated to microscopic Brueckner calculations of the \(^1\)S\(_0\) pairing gap. They computed the neutron heat capacity from the entropy density derived from the quasiparticle spectrum, covering both the superfluid phase (below the critical temperature \(T_{sf}\)) and the normal phase (above \(T_{sf}\)), and compared their numerical results to the analytical formula of Levenfish and Yakovlev (1994) and to the classical limit.

## Key numbers
- The linear approximation for the heat capacity of non-superfluid neutrons, \(C_V^{n\,(FG)} = \frac{\pi^2}{2} n_n \frac{T}{T_{Fn}}\), is valid only for \(T \ll T_{Fn}\); deviations exceed 30% at the characteristic temperature \(T_{cl} = 3 T_{Fn}/\pi^2\).
- At high temperatures \(T \gg T_{Fn}\), the heat capacity approaches the classical limit \(C_V^{n\,(cl.)} = \frac{3}{2} n_n\).
- The critical temperature for superfluidity is given by \(T_{sf} = \frac{e^\xi}{\pi} \Delta_n(T=0)\) with \(\xi \approx 0.577\) (Euler-Mascheroni constant).
- The temperature dependence of the pairing gap is fitted as \(\Delta_n(T) = \Delta_n(T=0) \sqrt{1 - (T/T_{sf})^{3.23}}\) for \(T \leq T_{sf}\).
- The zero-temperature pairing gap is parametrized with parameters: \(k_{max} = 1.37\) fm\(^{-1}\), \(\Delta_0 = 3.37968\) MeV, \(k_1 = 0.556092\) fm\(^{-1}\), \(k_2 = 1.38236\) fm\(^{-1}\), \(k_3 = 0.327517\) fm\(^{-1}\).
- The phonon-induced correction to the heat capacity in the normal phase is negligible (less than 1%) for dilute neutron matter.

## Context
This work extends the finite-temperature treatment of neutron matter from the high-density core regime (where the weak-coupling BCS approximation of Levenfish and Yakovlev (1994) is accurate) to the low-density crustal regime near the neutron drip point. It provides a unified analytical formula that interpolates between the quantum degenerate regime and the classical regime, enabling more realistic neutron-star cooling simulations. The results are consistent with the LNS Skyrme functional of Cao (2006) and with quantum Monte Carlo calculations of Gandolfi (2012), and the parametrization can be adapted to include the reduction of the pairing gap due to nuclear clusters as studied by Chamel (2010) and others.
