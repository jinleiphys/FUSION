# The Structure of the Hadron-Quark Reaction Zone

> Hadron-quark combustion in dense matter is a central topic in the study of phases in compact stars and their high-energy astrophysics. We critically review the literature on hadron-quark combustion, dividing them into a "first wave" that treats the problem as a steady-state burning with or without constraints of mechanical equilibrium, and a "second wave" which uses numerical techniques to resolve the burning front and solves the underlying Partial Differential Equations for the chemistry of the burning front under less restrictive conditions. We detail the inaccuracies that the second wave amends over the first wave, and highlight crucial differences between various approaches in the second wave. We also include results from time-dependent simulations of the reaction zone that include a hadronic EOS, neutrinos, and self-consistent thermodynamics without using parameterized shortcuts.

# The Structure of the Hadron-Quark Reaction Zone

## Key claim
The paper argues that time-dependent numerical simulations of hadron-quark combustion yield radically different results from earlier steady-state semi-analytic models, with front speeds up to six orders of magnitude higher and the possibility of quenching due to neutrino effects. However, the authors qualify that these results depend on the specifics of the equation of state and the treatment of neutrino transport, and that the quenching from neutrino absorption may be an artifact of the trapped-neutrino approximation used in their simulations.

## Method
The authors critically review the literature on hadron-quark combustion, dividing it into a "first wave" of steady-state semi-analytic treatments (using either mechanical equilibrium or hydrodynamic jump conditions) and a "second wave" of time-dependent numerical simulations that solve coupled partial differential equations for hydrodynamics, weak reaction rates, diffusion, and neutrino transport. They present results from their own time-dependent simulations that include a hadronic equation of state, neutrinos with a diffusion scheme, and self-consistent thermodynamics without parameterized shortcuts, resolving the reaction zone at sub-centimeter scales.

## Key numbers
- The conversion of hadronic matter to strange quark matter can release approximately 100 MeV per baryon converted, based on the bag model equation of state.
- Scaling to a whole neutron star of about 10^57 baryons yields about 10^53 erg of binding energy released.
- Olinto's steady-state formula gives burning front speeds up to 20 km/s.
- Second-wave simulations find burning speeds of 0.001c to 0.01c, which is six orders of magnitude higher than typical first-wave speeds.
- The reaction zone has a length scale of 0.1 cm to 1 cm, while the whole compact star has a length scale of 10 km.
- The deconfinement timescale is tau_d ~ 10^{-23} s, and the sonic time across a 1 cm interface is t_s ~ 10^{-11} s.
- The weak interaction timescale for the nonleptonic reaction is tau = 10^{-8} s.
- The initial hadronic density used in the neutrino absorption simulations was n_B = 0.2 fm^{-3}.

## Context
This review extends the work of Witten (1984) on the qualitative description of neutron star conversion to quark matter, and critically compares the first-wave approaches of Olinto (1987), Benvenuto et al. (1989), Lugones et al. (1994), and Drago et al. (2015) with the second-wave numerical simulations initiated by Niebergal et al. (2010, 2011). The paper highlights that Coll's condition, used by Herzog et al. (2011) and others as a heuristic for combustion, is not justified on thermodynamic grounds. The work enables future studies using adaptive mesh refinement techniques to bridge the disparate scales between the reaction zone (0.1-1 cm) and the full compact star (10 km), and suggests that multidimensional simulations are needed to explore neutrino-driven instabilities analogous to Rayleigh-Taylor instabilities.
