# Inelastic and elastic parton scatterings in the strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma

> We investigate the role of inelastic processes in the strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma (sQGP) based on the effective dynamical quasi-particle model (DQPM). In the DQPM the non-perturbative properties of the sQGP at finite temperature $T$ and baryon chemical potential $μ_B$ are described in terms of strongly interacting off-shell partons (quarks and gluons) with dynamically generated spectral functions whose properties are adjusted to reproduce the lQCD EoS for the QGP in thermodynamic equilibrium. For the first time the massive gluon radiation processes from the off-shell quark-quark ($q+q$) and quark-gluon ($q+g$) scatterings are calculated explicitly within leading order Feynman diagrams with effective propagators and vertices from the DQPM without any further approximations. We present the results for the energy and temperature dependencies of the total and differential radiative cross sections and compare them to the corresponding elastic cross sections. We show that our results reproduce the pQCD calculations in the limit of zero masses and widths of quasiparticles. Also we study the $μ_B$ dependence of the inelastic cross sections. Moreover, we present estimates for the transition rate and relaxation time of radiative versus elastic scatterings in the sQGP.

## Key claim

The authors calculate for the first time massive gluon radiation from off-shell quark-quark and quark-gluon scatterings using explicit leading-order Feynman diagrams with effective propagators and vertices from the Dynamical QuasiParticle Model (DQPM) without any further approximations, finding that inelastic processes in the strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma are strongly suppressed relative to elastic scatterings at thermal energies due to the large mass of emitted gluons.

## Method

The authors compute the total and differential cross sections for the inelastic processes \(q+q \to q+q+g\) and \(q+g \to q+g+g\) using leading-order Feynman diagrams with DQPM effective propagators and vertices, evaluating all \(t\), \(u\), and \(s\) channel diagrams and their interferences numerically using the FeynCalc package. The quasiparticle masses and widths are taken from the DQPM parametrization fitted to lattice QCD entropy density data, and the calculations are performed on-shell (at the pole position of spectral functions) for partons in thermal equilibrium at finite temperature \(T\) and baryon chemical potential \(\mu_B\). The results are compared to elastic cross sections and to perturbative QCD (pQCD) calculations in the limit of zero masses and widths.

## Key numbers

- The DQPM running coupling \(\alpha_s\) at \(\mu_B=0\) increases significantly approaching \(T_c \approx 0.158\) GeV, with the parametrization given by \(g^2(T,\mu_B=0) = d((s(T,0)/s^{QCD}_{SB})^e - 1)^f\) where \(d=169.934\), \(e=-0.178434\), \(f=1.14631\).
- The strange quark mass is set as \(M_s(T,\mu_B) = M_{u/d}(T,\mu_B) + \Delta M\) with \(\Delta M \simeq 30\) MeV.
- The critical temperature at finite \(\mu_B\) is parametrized as \(T_c(\mu_B) = T_c \sqrt{1 - \alpha\mu_B^2}\) with \(T_c \approx 0.158\) GeV and \(\alpha \simeq 0.974\) GeV\(^{-2}\).
- For \(\mu_B=0\), the DQPM gives \(M_q = \frac{2}{3} M_g\) and \(\gamma_q = \frac{4}{9} \gamma_g\).
- On-shell elastic cross sections differ from off-shell ones by less than 20%.

## Context

This work extends the DQPM framework, previously applied to elastic scatterings and jet transport coefficients by Grishmanovskii et al. (2022), to include radiative processes for the first time without soft-gluon approximations. It goes beyond the Gunion-Bertsch (1981) approximation and the soft-gluon approximation used by Song et al. (2022) for DQPM, and contrasts with pQCD-based approaches such as BAMPS (Uphoff et al., 2014) that use constant \(\alpha_s=0.3\). The results enable quantification of when radiative processes become relevant in the sQGP and provide input for transport models like PHSD (Cassing et al., 2008; Linnyk et al., 2015) for heavy-ion collision simulations.
