# Captures of Hot and Warm Sterile Antineutrino Dark Matter on EC-decaying Ho-163 Nuclei

> Capturing low-energy electron antineutrinos on radioactive Ho-163 nuclei, which decay into Dy-163 via electron capture (EC), is a noteworthy opportunity to detect relic sterile antineutrinos. Such hypothetical particles are more or less implied by current experimental and cosmological data, and they might be a part of hot dark matter or a candidate for warm dark matter in the Universe. Using the isotope Ho-163 as a target and assuming reasonable active-sterile antineutrino mixing angles, we calculate the capture rate of relic electron antineutrinos against the corresponding EC-decay background in the presence of sterile antineutrinos at the sub-eV or keV mass scale. We show that the signature of hot or warm sterile antineutrino dark matter should in principle be observable, provided the target is big enough and the energy resolution is good enough.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the signature of hot or warm sterile antineutrino dark matter captured on EC-decaying Ho-163 nuclei should in principle be observable, but only if the target is big enough, the energy resolution is good enough, and the gravitational clustering effect is significant enough, conditions that are extremely challenging and, for the warm case, likely unattainable in the foreseeable future.

## Method

The authors calculate the capture rate of relic electron antineutrinos (including sterile components) on Ho-163 nuclei via the thresholdless reaction \(\overline{\nu}_e + ^{163}{\rm Ho} + e^- \to ^{163}{\rm Dy}^* \to ^{163}{\rm Dy} + E_i\), using a (3+2) mixing scheme for sub-eV sterile antineutrinos and a (3+1) scheme for keV sterile antineutrinos. They convolute the monoenergetic capture signals with a Gaussian energy resolution function and compare them to the EC-decay background spectrum of Ho-163, using the known atomic levels, binding energies, and the half-life of Ho-163 (4570 yr) to normalize rates. Gravitational clustering of relic antineutrinos around Earth is modeled via a power-law overdensity parameter \(\zeta_k \simeq 1 + A (m_k / 1\ {\rm eV})^\Omega\) fitted to results from Wong (2008).

## Key numbers

* The Q-value of the Ho-163 EC decay is taken as \(Q \simeq 2.5\ {\rm keV}\) (constrained to \(2.3\ {\rm keV} \leq Q \leq 2.8\ {\rm keV}\)).
* For hot sterile antineutrinos in the (3+2) scheme, the authors assume \(m_4 \simeq 0.2\ {\rm eV}\), \(m_5 \simeq 0.4\ {\rm eV}\), and mixing angles \(\theta_{14} \simeq \theta_{15} \approx 10^\circ\) giving \(|V_{e4}| \simeq 0.171\) and \(|V_{e5}| \simeq 0.174\).
* The average number density of each relic antineutrino species today is \(\langle n_{\overline{\nu}_k} \rangle \simeq 56\ {\rm cm}^{-3}\).
* Gravitational clustering overdensity parameters from Wong (2008) are \(\zeta_k = \{1.5 \pm 0.1, 3.75 \pm 0.65, 8.2 \pm 1.8, 16 \pm 4\}\) for \(m_k = \{0.15, 0.3, 0.45, 0.6\}\ {\rm eV}\).
* For hot sterile antineutrinos, an energy resolution \(\Delta < 0.1\ {\rm eV}\) is required to distinguish signals from background; given \(\zeta_5 = 10\), a target of \(10^2\ {\rm kg}\) (or \(10^3\ {\rm kg}\)) of Ho-163 could yield one event per year (or ten events per year).
* For warm sterile antineutrino DM, the authors fix \(m_4 \simeq 2.0\ {\rm keV}\) and \(|V_{e4}|^2 \simeq 5.0 \times 10^{-7}\); the local DM density is \(\rho^{\rm local}_{\rm DM} \simeq 0.3\ {\rm GeV\ cm}^{-3}\), giving \(n_{\overline{\nu}_4} \simeq 5 \times 10^4\ (3\ {\rm keV}/m_4)\ {\rm cm}^{-3}\).
* For warm sterile antineutrinos, the required energy resolution is \(\Delta \sim {\cal O}(0.1)\ {\rm keV}\), but a target of 600 tons of Ho-163 is needed for \(|V_{e4}|^2 \sim {\cal O}(10^{-6})\) to obtain one event per year.

## Context

This work extends the proposal by Lusignoli (2010) and Li and Xing (2011) to detect the active component of the cosmic antineutrino background (C\(\overline{\nu}\)B) using Ho-163, now applied to sterile antineutrino components. It complements the approach of Li and Xing (2011) and Liao (2011) for capturing keV sterile neutrinos on beta-decaying H-3 and Ru-106 nuclei, but addresses the antineutrino case which requires EC-decaying targets. The paper enables future experimental design by quantifying the required target masses and energy resolutions for detecting hot (sub-eV) versus warm (keV) sterile antineutrino dark matter, while highlighting that the warm case is almost hopeless with current technology.
