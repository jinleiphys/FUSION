# Weak interaction rates of $sd$-shell Urca pairs in stellar environment using a shell-model approach

> In this work, the weak interaction rates of $sd$-shell nuclei in the hot and dense stellar environment are calculated using the shell model. The {\it ab initio} effective interactions are employed for the calculation of Gamow-Teller strengths and weak rates in addition to the phenomenological interaction. The weak rates are evaluated in a fine grid of density and temperature. The electron capture and $β^-$-decay rates of Urca pairs of nuclei with $A = 23, 25, 29, 31$ and 33 are evaluated as a function of $\log_{10} ρY_e$ and the corresponding Urca density is obtained. The screening effects are also included for the evaluation of these stellar weak rates. In addition, the intrinsic cooling strength is evaluated for $A=29,31$ and 33 Urca pairs in neutron star crusts. The stellar weak rates, along with the neutrino energy emitted and the gamma heat production, are tabulated for $A = 29-39$ nuclei.

## Key claim

The weak interaction rates for sd-shell Urca pairs are calculated using both phenomenological (USDB) and ab initio (CEFT, DJ16A) effective interactions, and the authors find that when available experimental excitation energies and Gamow-Teller strengths are incorporated into the shell-model calculations, the rates become nearly independent of the effective interaction employed, allowing for a common assignment of Urca densities.

## Method

Gamow-Teller strengths and weak interaction rates (electron capture, beta-minus decay, positron capture, and beta-plus decay) were calculated using the shell model with three effective interactions: the phenomenological USDB interaction, and the ab initio Daejeon 16A (DJ16A) and chiral effective field theory (CEFT) interactions. For nuclei with A=31 and 33, the SDPF-MU interaction extending into the sd-pf shell model space was also used. The weak rates were evaluated on a fine grid of density (log10 ρYe from 8.0 to 10.5) and temperature (log10 T from 8.0 to 9.2 in steps of 0.2), including screening effects from degenerate electrons, and the Urca density was determined as the density where electron capture and beta-minus decay rates coincide.

## Key numbers

- Urca density for (23Na, 23Ne) pair: log10(ρYe) = 8.96(±0.01) with screening effects, common to CEFT, DJ16A, and USDB interactions when experimental data are included
- Urca density for (25Mg, 25Na) pair: log10(ρYe) = 8.82(±0.01) with screening effects
- Urca density for (25Na, 25Ne) pair: log10(ρYe) = 9.59(±0.01) at high temperatures (log10 T ≥ 8.4)
- Urca density for (29Mg, 29Na) pair: log10(ρYe) = 10.33(±0.01) with screening effects, and 10.28(±0.01) without screening effects
- Experimental B(GT) value for 23Na(3/2+ g.s.) to 23Ne(5/2+ g.s.) transition: 0.038±0.006
- Experimental B(GT) value for 23Na(3/2+ g.s.) to 23Ne(1/2+ at 1.017 MeV) transition: 0.048±0.007
- Experimental B(GT) value for 23Na(3/2+ g.s.) to 23Ne(3/2+ at 1.823 MeV) transition: 0.041±0.008
- Experimental B(GT) value for 23Na(3/2+ g.s.) to 23Ne(5/2+ at 2.315 MeV) transition: ≤0.021
- Experimental B(GT) value for 29Mg to 29Na ground-state to ground-state transition: 0.038
- Beta-decay Q value for (31P, 31Si) pair: 1.49 MeV
- B(GT) value for 31Al(5/2+ g.s.) to 31Mg(3/2+1) transition: maximum 0.17 (USDB), minimum 0.12 (SDPF-MU)

## Context

This work extends previous shell-model calculations of weak interaction rates for sd-shell nuclei with A=17-28 by Suzuki et al. (2016) to heavier sd-shell nuclei with A=29-39, and introduces ab initio effective interactions (CEFT and DJ16A) alongside the phenomenological USDB interaction. The study builds on the formalism developed by Fuller, Fowler, and Newman (1980-1985) and Oda et al. (1994) for weak interaction rates, and addresses the importance of Urca pairs in cooling neutron star crusts as identified by Schatz et al. (2014) and Deibel et al. (2016). The results enable more reliable modeling of Urca cooling processes in O-Ne-Mg stellar cores and neutron star crusts, particularly for the (31Al, 31Mg) pair identified as important for neutron star crust cooling.
