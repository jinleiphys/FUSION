# Adiabatic Hydrodynamization and the Emergence of Attractors: a Unified Description of Hydrodynamization in Kinetic Theory

> "Attractor" solutions for the pre-hydrodynamic, far-from-equilibrium, evolution of the matter produced in relativistic heavy ion collisions have emerged as crucial descriptors of the rapid hydrodynamization of quark-gluon plasma (QGP). Adiabatic Hydrodynamization (AH) has been proposed as a framework with which to describe, explain, and predict attractor behavior that draws upon an analogy to the adiabatic approximation in quantum mechanics. In this work, we systematize the description of pre-hydrodynamic attractors in kinetic theory by showing how to use the AH framework to identify these long-lived solutions to which varied initial conditions rapidly evolve, demonstrating the robustness of this framework. In a simplified QCD kinetic theory in the small-angle scattering limit, we use AH to explain both the early- and late-time scaling behavior of a longitudinally expanding gluon gas in a unified framework. In this context, we show that AH provides a unified description of, and intuition for, all the stages of what in QCD would be bottom-up thermalization, starting from a pre-hydrodynamic attractor and ending with hydrodynamization. We additionally discuss the connection between the notions of scaling behavior and adiabaticity and the crucial role of time-dependent coordinate redefinitions in identifying the degrees of freedom of kinetic theories that give rise to attractor solutions. The tools we present open a path to the intuitive explanation of how attractor behavior arises and how the attractor evolves in all stages of the hydrodynamization of QGP in heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The Adiabatic Hydrodynamization (AH) framework, drawing on an analogy to the adiabatic approximation in quantum mechanics, provides a systematic method to identify and describe attractor solutions in kinetic theory; the authors demonstrate this by showing that AH explains both the early- and late-time scaling behavior of a longitudinally expanding gluon gas in a simplified QCD kinetic theory with a small-angle scattering collision kernel, though they note that this simplified kernel makes the hydrodynamization time unrealistically long and that their goal is to establish the formalism rather than explain the rapid timescale seen in QCD.

## Method

The authors work with a kinetic theory description of a gluon gas using the small-angle scattering approximation, where the collision kernel takes a Fokker-Planck form. They assume boost invariance and translation invariance in the transverse plane, and study a longitudinally expanding system in Milne coordinates. The AH framework is applied by constructing an effective time-evolution operator, finding its instantaneous eigenstates and eigenvalues, and showing that the adiabaticity criterion (the ratio of the matrix element of the time derivative of eigenstates to the eigenvalue gap) is satisfied, which allows the identification of long-lived slow modes that constitute the attractor solution.

## Key numbers

- The hydrodynamization time in RHIC collisions is estimated to be as short as 0.4 fm/c to 0.6 fm/c, with the hydrodynamic fluid formed at that time having a temperature of approximately 500 MeV to 350-380 MeV (from prior work by Shen (2010) and Shen (2012)).
- The authors use a coupling value of g ~ 2, corresponding to alpha_s ~ 0.3 and g^2 N_c ~ 12, which in QCD kinetic theory yields hydrodynamization time scales less than 1 fm/c (from prior work by Kurkela (2015)).
- The simplified collision kernel uses lambda_0 = 4 pi alpha_s^2 N_c^2, where alpha_s is the strong coupling constant.

## Context

This work extends the Adiabatic Hydrodynamization framework introduced by Brewer (2019) and the subsequent analysis by Brewer, Scheihing-Hitschfeld, and Yin (2022) (referred to as BSY), which demonstrated that time-dependent coordinate rescalings can make the evolution exactly adiabatic in the earliest stages of hydrodynamization. The present paper generalizes this approach to describe all stages of bottom-up thermalization in a unified framework, from the pre-hydrodynamic attractor through to hydrodynamization, using a simplified collision kernel. The tools developed here are intended to be applied in future work to the full QCD Effective Kinetic Theory collision kernel and to systems including transverse expansion, which would enable a physical description of how and why hydrodynamization occurs in heavy-ion collisions.
