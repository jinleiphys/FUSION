# Constraints from $v_2$ fluctuations for the initial state geometry of heavy-ion collisions

> The ability to accurately compute the series of coefficients $v_n$ characterizing the momentum space anisotropies of particle production in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions as a function of centrality is widely regarded as a triumph of fluid dynamics as description of the bulk matter evolution. A key ingredient to fluid dynamical modeling is however the initial spatial distribution of matter as created by a yet not completely understood equilibration process. A measurement directly sensitive to this initial state geometry is therefore of high value for constraining models of pre-equilibrium dynamics. Recently, it has been shown that such a measurement is indeed possible in terms of the event by event probability distribution of the normalized $v_n$ distribution as a function of centrality, which is to high accuracy independent on the details of the subsequent fluid dynamical evolution and hence directly reflects the primary distribution of spatial eccentricities. We present a study of this observable using a variety of Glauber-based models and argue that the experimental data place very tight constraints on the initial distribution of matter and rule out all simple Glauber-based models.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the event-by-event probability distribution of normalized $v_2$ fluctuations as a function of centrality is directly sensitive to the initial state geometry and independent of the subsequent fluid dynamical evolution, but that none of the simple Glauber-based models tested can reproduce the centrality dependence observed by ATLAS, ruling them out as valid representations of the initial state.

## Method

The authors computed the normalized fluctuations of $v_2$ by evaluating the spatial eccentricity $\varepsilon_2$ from Monte Carlo Glauber model initial states, using various assumptions for nucleon distributions (Woods-Saxon, hard sphere, sheet), interaction types (nucleon-nucleon or constituent quark scattering), and entropy density parametrizations (binary collisions, wounded nucleons, or a mixture). They compared the resulting probability distributions $P(\delta \varepsilon_2)$ with ATLAS data across centrality classes from 0-5% to 35-40%, and tested sensitivity to parameters such as surface diffuseness, matter spot size, and multiplicity fluctuations.

## Key numbers

- Woods-Saxon parameters: $c = 6.61$ fm (radius), $z = 0.51$ fm (skin thickness) for Pb nuclei
- Constituent quark Gaussian radius: $0.6$ fm around each nucleon position
- Nucleon-nucleon cross section: $\sigma_{NN} = 64$ mb; constituent quark cross section: $1/9$ of this value
- Matter spot size parameter $\sigma$ tested at $0.4$ fm, $0.6$ fm, $1.0$ fm, and a Gaussian distribution with width $\Delta \sigma = 0.3$ fm centered at $0.6$ fm
- Number of events per centrality class: approximately $20,000$
- Centrality classes studied: 0-5%, 5-10%, 10-15%, 15-20%, 20-25%, 25-30%, 30-35%, 35-40%

## Context

This work extends the finding of Niemi (2012) that the relation $v_2 = C_2 \varepsilon_2$ holds accurately in individual collisions, making $P(\delta v_2)$ equivalent to $P(\delta \varepsilon_2)$ and thus a direct probe of initial geometry. The study contradicts the ability of simple Glauber-based models (wounded nucleon, binary collision, or mixtures) to describe the ATLAS data, and suggests that QCD-based initial state models incorporating non-linear dynamics or sub-nucleon color fluctuations (such as the pQCD+saturation model of Eskola (2000) and Paatelainen (2013), or CGC-based models) are needed. The results enable tighter constraints on pre-equilibrium dynamics and disentangling initial geometry from transport properties in heavy-ion collisions.
