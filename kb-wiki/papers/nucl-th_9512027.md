# Monte Carlo computation of pair correlations in excited nuclei

> We present a novel quantum Monte Carlo method based on a path integral in Fock space, which allows to compute finite-temperature properties of a many-body nuclear system with a monopole pairing interaction in the canonical ensemble. It enables an exact calculation of the thermodynamic variables such as the internal energy, the entropy, or the specific heat, from the measured moments of the number of hops in a path of nuclear configurations. Monte Carlo calculations for a single-shell $(h_{11/2})^6$ model are consistent with an exact calculation from the many-body spectrum in the seniority model.

## Key claim

The paper presents a novel quantum Monte Carlo method based on a path integral in Fock space that enables exact finite-temperature calculations of thermodynamic variables for a many-body nuclear system with a monopole pairing interaction in the canonical ensemble, with the caveat that the method is demonstrated only for a single-shell model and validated against the seniority model.

## Method

The method uses a path integral in Fock space to compute finite-temperature properties of a nuclear many-body system with a monopole pairing interaction, working in the canonical ensemble. Thermodynamic variables such as internal energy, entropy, and specific heat are calculated from the measured moments of the number of hops in a path of nuclear configurations. Monte Carlo calculations were performed for a single-shell (h11/2)^6 model and compared with an exact calculation from the many-body spectrum in the seniority model.

## Key numbers

- The method was applied to a single-shell (h11/2)^6 model system
- The results were consistent with an exact calculation from the many-body spectrum in the seniority model

## Context

This work extends the application of quantum Monte Carlo techniques to nuclear pairing problems, specifically addressing the monopole pairing interaction in the canonical ensemble. The method enables exact finite-temperature calculations that were previously difficult to perform for such systems. The approach is validated against the seniority model, which provides an exact reference for the single-shell case, and opens the possibility for extending such calculations to more complex nuclear systems.
