# Classical Lagrangian Model of the Pauli Principle

> A classical Lagrangian model of the Pauli potential is introduced. It is shown that the kinematic kinetic energy ($\sum \frac{1}{2} m v^2$) in the model approximately reproduces the energy of a free Fermi gas at low temperatures and at densities relevant in nuclear collisions with moderate beam energies. Differences between canonical and kinematic quantities are pointed out. The Pauli potential can be used in transport simulations.

# Classical Lagrangian Model of the Pauli Principle

## Key claim
The kinematic kinetic energy in a classical Lagrangian model with a Pauli potential approximately reproduces the energy of a free Fermi gas at low temperatures (up to about 5 MeV) and at densities up to about 1.5 times standard nuclear matter density, but the fit overestimates the specific heat.

## Method
The authors introduced a Lagrangian Pauli potential depending on relative coordinates and relative velocities between nucleons, with an exponential form in phase-space separation. They performed Metropolis Monte Carlo sampling over canonical phase space for a system of 60 nucleons at fixed positions, using a two-step procedure that first samples in (x, v) space and then maps to (x, p) space, to compute thermal averages of the kinematic kinetic energy at various temperatures and densities.

## Key numbers
- Pauli potential parameters: V0 = 1.17 GeV, r = 1.5 fm, v = 0.163c
- Temperature range studied: up to approximately 5 MeV
- Density studied: around 0.04 fm^-3 for a given type of nucleon with a given spin orientation
- Number of nucleons used in calculations: N = 60
- Quadratic least-square fit to the temperature dependence of kinematic kinetic energy per nucleon at standard nuclear matter density gave reduced chi-squared approximately 0.81; a linear fit gave reduced chi-squared approximately 1.23
- The model reproduces Fermi-gas behavior up to densities corresponding to about 1.5 times standard nuclear matter density

## Context
This work extends the classical Pauli potential approach originally proposed by Wilets and collaborators (1977) and later developed by Dorso and collaborators (1987), Boal and collaborators (1988), and Peilert and collaborators (1992). Unlike previous Hamiltonian-based implementations, the authors present a Lagrangian formulation that allows direct calculation of kinematic quantities (as opposed to canonical momenta), which are important for thermodynamics and observables such as cross sections. The model enables future applications to nuclear collision simulations where fragmentation or evaporation at low temperatures plays an important role.
