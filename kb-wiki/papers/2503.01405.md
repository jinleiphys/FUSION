# Neutrinos from dense environments

> Neutrinos from dense environments are unique laboratories for astrophysics, particle physics and many-body physics. They tell us about the last stages of the gravitational core-collapse and the explosion of massive stars. These elusive particles are also tightly linked to heavy elements synthesis in gravitational core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers, or play a pivotal role at the MeV epoch during the Universe expansion. We highlight theoretical and observational aspects of this interesting domain, in particular for the future measurement of neutrinos from the next core-collapse supernova, and of the diffuse supernova background, whose discovery might lie in the forthcoming future.

# Neutrinos from dense environments

## Key claim
Neutrinos from dense environments serve as unique laboratories for astrophysics, particle physics, and many-body physics, but the paper emphasizes that understanding how neutrinos propagate and change flavor in these environments is fundamental both theoretically and for future observations, with the caveat that many aspects of flavor conversion phenomena still require further attention despite almost two decades of theoretical study.

## Method
The paper reviews theoretical and observational aspects of neutrinos in dense environments, including core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers. It discusses neutrino flavor evolution mechanisms including the Mikheev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein (MSW) effect, neutrino-neutrino interactions, shock wave effects, and turbulence, as well as the application of Bayesian likelihood analyses to neutrino event data from SN1987A and simulated future observations. The review covers neutrino non-radiative two-body decay limits, the diffuse supernova neutrino background (DSNB), and the role of neutrinos in r-process nucleosynthesis.

## Key numbers
- 24 $\bar{\nu}_e$ events detected from SN1987A by Kamiokande-II, Irvine-Michigan-Brookhaven, and Baksan Scintillator Telescope
- Lower bound on neutrino lifetime-to-mass ratio $\tau/m > 2.4 \times 10^5$ s/eV at 68% C.L. (inverted mass ordering) from SN1987A data
- Matter densities in gravitational core-collapse supernovae reach from $10^{10}$ g/cm$^3$ to a few $10^{14}$ g/cm$^3$ in central regions
- Neutrino fluxes produce up to $10^{57}$-$10^{58}$ neutrinos with tens of MeV
- For a supernova at 10 kpc: up to 250 $\bar{\nu}_e$ events during 50 seconds in Super-Kamiokande, 110 events over 40 seconds in DUNE, and 10 $\nu_x$ events in JUNO
- For a future galactic supernova at 10 kpc: several hundred events in HALO-2 or KamLAND, $3 \times 10^3$ events in DUNE, $8 \times 10^3$ events in JUNO, $10^4$ in Super-Kamiokande, a few $10^4$ in Hyper-Kamiokande, and $10^6$ in IceCube
- Pre-SN neutrinos observable at $3\sigma$ in KamLAND for a 25 $M_{sun}$ star at 690 kpc
- Core-collapse supernova rate: $1.63 \pm 0.46$ events per century from combined estimates
- DSNB expected events in ten years: 10-20 in SK-Gd, 10-40 in JUNO, 5-10 in DUNE, 25-145 in Hyper-Kamiokande
- DSNB sensitivity to non-radiative two-body decay in window $\tau/m \in [10^9, 10^{11}]$ s/eV
- Combined SK-I to SK-IV DSNB analysis found 1.5 $\sigma$ excess; SK-Gd increased to 2.3 $\sigma$

## Context
This review extends the theoretical framework developed by Pantaleone (1992) on neutrino-neutrino interactions in supernovae and builds upon the delayed neutrino-heating mechanism first suggested by Bethe and Wilson (1985). It connects to observational milestones including SN1987A (observed by Kamiokande-II, IMB, and Baksan in 1987) and GW170817 (the first concomitant observation of gravitational waves from a binary neutron star merger, a short gamma-ray burst, and a kilonova). The paper enables future work on discriminating supernova models and flavor mechanisms through Bayesian analyses, and positions upcoming experiments (Hyper-Kamiokande starting 2027, JUNO starting 2025, DUNE starting 2029-2032) to potentially discover the DSNB and resolve open questions about the core-collapse supernova explosion mechanism, r-process nucleosynthesis sites, and neutrino properties including non-radiative decay.
