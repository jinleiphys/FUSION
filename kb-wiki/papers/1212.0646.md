# Biased Showers - a common conceptual Framework for the Interpretation of High P_T Observables in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> After the start of the LHC, a plethora of novel observables for jet tomography in heavy-ion collisions has appeared. Many of these studies have yielded counter-intuitive null results of apparently unmodified jets, which have sparked (sometimes exotic) theoretical efforts to explain these findings. However, it has to be realized that almost all current high P_T observables measure conditional probabilities of events, not probabilities. Thus, the correct starting point for their theoretical understanding is Bayes' formula, and the biases introduced by the conditioning are crucial to understanding the outcome. Once this is introduced properly into the modelling process, the counter-intuitive results are seen to find a natural explanation in terms of various biases and the puzzles largely disappear. In this work, a conceptual framework to classify the various observables according to the types of bias they are sensitive to is presented and illustrated with a large number of case studies ranging from simple jet finding to 2+1 dihadron triggered correlations.

## Key claim

Almost all high \(P_T\) observables in heavy-ion collisions measure conditional probabilities rather than unconditional probabilities, so Bayes' formula is the correct starting point for their theoretical understanding; once biases introduced by trigger conditions are properly incorporated into modeling, the initially counter-intuitive null results (such as apparently unmodified jets) find a natural explanation and the puzzles largely disappear.

## Method

The author uses the in-medium shower evolution code YaJEM (YaJEM-DE version) to compute medium-modified fragmentation functions, embedding hard parton pairs generated from leading-order pQCD into a hydrodynamical description of the medium. The simulation samples hard vertices according to the binary overlap profile, evolves parton showers through the medium using transport coefficients \(\hat{q}\) and \(\hat{e}\) that depend on the local energy density and flow, and then applies various trigger conditions (hadron or jet triggers) to the final-state hadrons to study how biases distort the observed shower properties.

## Key numbers

- The primary parton production spectrum falls approximately like a power \(1/p_T^n\) with \(n=7..8\) at RHIC kinematics and \(n=4..5\) at the LHC
- For a fragmenting 20 GeV quark, the leading hadron typically carries only about 15% of the original parton energy, whereas an ideal jet clustering recovers typically 95% of the energy
- The transport coefficients are parameterized as \(\hat{q}[\hat{e}](\zeta) = K_Q[K_E] \cdot 2 \cdot [\epsilon(\zeta)]^{3/4} (\cosh \rho(\zeta) - \sinh \rho(\zeta) \cos\psi)\) with \(K_Q = 0.8 K\) and \(K_E = 0.1 K\) (corresponding to about a 10% elastic energy loss contribution), and \(K\) is fit to the nuclear suppression factor \(R_{AA}\) in 0-10% central Au-Au collisions at RHIC
- The mean lifetime of a virtual parton \(b\) from parent \(a\) is \(\langle \tau_b \rangle = E_b/Q_b^2 - E_b/Q_a^2\)

## Context

This work extends the discussion of biases in triggered correlations from Renk (2012) and provides a unified conceptual framework to classify observables ranging from simple jet finding to 2+1 dihadron triggered correlations. It builds on earlier jet tomography approaches using nuclear suppression factors \(R_{AA}\) (PHENIX, 2002-2007) and dihadron correlations \(I_{AA}\) (STAR, 2003-2006), and addresses the puzzling LHC results showing apparently unmodified jet properties (ATLAS, 2010; CMS, 2011) by demonstrating that biases from trigger conditions naturally explain these findings. The framework enables systematic comparison of different observables by viewing their various trigger conditions as variations of the bias factor, allowing tomographic information to be extracted from how observables respond to changes in bias.
