# Inhomogeneous condensation in the Gross-Neveu model in noninteger spatial dimensions $1 \leq d < 3$

> The Gross-Neveu model in the $N \to \infty$ approximation in $d=1$ spatial dimensions exhibits a chiral inhomogeneous phase (IP), where the chiral condensate has a spatial dependence that spontaneously breaks translational invariance and the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ chiral symmetry. This phase is absent in $d=2$, while in $d=3$ its existence and extent strongly depends on the regularization and the value of the finite regulator. This work connects these three results smoothly by extending the analysis to non-integer spatial dimensions $1 \leq d <3$, where the model is fully renormalizable. To this end, we adapt the stability analysis, which probes the stability of the homogeneous ground state under inhomogeneous perturbations, to non-integer spatial dimensions. We find that the IP is present for all $d<2$ and vanishes exactly at $d=2$. Moreover, we find no instability towards an IP for $2\leq d<3$, which suggests that the IP in $d=3$ is solely generated by the presence of a regulator.

## Key claim

The inhomogeneous phase (IP) is present for all spatial dimensions \(d < 2\) and vanishes exactly at \(d = 2\); for \(2 \leq d < 3\) there is no instability toward an IP, suggesting that the IP observed in \(d = 3\) studies is solely an artifact of finite regulators rather than a genuine feature of the renormalized theory.

## Method

The author performs a stability analysis of the Gross-Neveu model in the \(N \to \infty\) (mean-field) approximation at zero temperature, extending the bosonic two-point function and wave-function renormalization to noninteger spatial dimensions \(1 \leq d < 3\). The model is renormalized using a spatial momentum cutoff with the vacuum expectation value \(\mstarZero\) as the renormalization condition, and the homogeneous effective potential is computed in closed form for arbitrary \(d\). The stability against inhomogeneous perturbations is probed by evaluating the two-point function \(\Gamma^{(2)}\) at the homogeneous minimum \(\mstarMin\) as a function of bosonic momentum \(q\).

## Key numbers

- The critical chemical potential \(\mu_c(d)\) marks the homogeneous phase transition; for \(d=1\) the critical point is at \((\mu,T)/\mstarZero \approx (0.608, 0.318)\), evolving to \((\mu,T)/\mstarZero = (1.0, 0)\) at \(d=2\).
- For \(d=1\), the two-point function diverges logarithmically at \(q = 2\mu\) for all \(\mu > \mu_c\); for \(1 < d < 2\) the divergence vanishes but a cusp (negative minimum) is retained, preserving the instability at \(\mu_c\).
- At \(d=2\), the two-point function is constant and zero for all \(q \leq 2\mu\), indicating degeneracy between homogeneous and inhomogeneous configurations at \((\mu,T) = (\mu_c, 0)\).
- The wave-function renormalization \(Z\) becomes negative for \(\mu > \mu_c\) when \(d < 2\), signaling a moat regime for all chemical potentials in that range.

## Context

This work connects previous results on the Gross-Neveu model in integer spatial dimensions: the IP is present in \(d=1\) (Thies 2003, Schnetz 2004, Koenigstein 2021), absent in \(d=2\) (Urlichs 2007, Buballa 2020, Pannullo 2023), and regulator-dependent in \(d=3\) (Nickel 2009, Partyka 2008, Pannullo 2022). By treating the number of spatial dimensions as a continuous parameter, the author identifies the dimensionality itself as the sole cause of the IP disappearance at \(d=2\). The analysis extends the homogeneous phase diagram study of Inagaki (1994) to inhomogeneous phases and moat regimes, and enables future investigations of how the IP emerges or vanishes as a function of dimension without regulator artifacts.
