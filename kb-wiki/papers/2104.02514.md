# Nuclear pasta and symmetry energy in the relativistic point-coupling model

> Nonuniform structure of low-density nuclear matter, known as nuclear pasta, is expected to appear not only in the inner crust of neutron stars but also in core-collapse supernova explosions and neutron-star mergers. We perform fully three-dimensional calculations of inhomogeneous nuclear matter and neutron-star matter in the low-density region using the Thomas-Fermi approximation. The nuclear interaction is described in the relativistic mean-field approach with the point-coupling interaction, where the meson exchange in each channel is replaced by the contact interaction between nucleons. We investigate the influence of nuclear symmetry energy and its density dependence on pasta structures by introducing a coupling term between the isoscalar-vector and isovector-vector interactions. It is found that the properties of pasta phases in the neutron-rich matter are strongly dependent on the symmetry energy and its slope. In addition to typical shapes like droplets, rods, slabs, tubes, and bubbles, some intermediate pasta structures are also observed in cold stellar matter with a relatively large proton fraction. We find that nonspherical shapes are unlikely to be formed in neutron-star crusts, since the proton fraction obtained in $β$ equilibrium is rather small. The inner crust properties may lead to a visible difference in the neutron-star radius.

## Key claim

The paper claims that nonspherical pasta shapes (rods, slabs, tubes, bubbles) are unlikely to form in the inner crust of neutron stars under beta equilibrium because the proton fraction is too small, even though such shapes do appear in matter with a fixed, larger proton fraction. This conclusion is conditional on the specific set of relativistic point-coupling models used, which were generated from the PC-PK1 parametrization by varying the symmetry energy slope.

## Method

The authors performed fully three-dimensional Thomas-Fermi calculations in a cubic cell (60 fm side length, 64 grid points per direction) with periodic boundary conditions, using the relativistic mean-field point-coupling (RMF-PC) model with the PC-PK1 parametrization. To study the effect of symmetry energy, they generated a set of models with different symmetry energy slope values (L = 40 to 113 MeV) by introducing a coupling term between isoscalar-vector and isovector-vector interactions, while fixing the symmetry energy at a density of 0.12 fm^-3. They calculated pasta structures for both stellar matter with fixed proton fraction and neutron-star matter in beta equilibrium at zero temperature.

## Key numbers

- The PC-PK1 parametrization predicts saturation density ρ0 = 0.153 fm^-3, energy per nucleon E0 = -16.12 MeV, incompressibility K = 238 MeV, symmetry energy Esym = 35.6 MeV, and slope L = 113 MeV.
- The generated models cover L values of 40, 50, 60, 70, 80, 90, 100, and 113 MeV, with corresponding symmetry energies at saturation density ranging from 31.69 MeV (L=40) to 35.61 MeV (L=113).
- The cell size used was 60 fm with 64 grid points in each direction, giving a grid spacing of 0.9375 fm.
- At a typical density of ρB = 0.05 fm^-3, there are about 10800 nucleons in the cubic cell.
- For symmetric matter (Yp=0.5), droplet structures appear at ρB = 0.014 fm^-3, and the transition from droplets to rods occurs at ρB > 0.02 fm^-3.
- The energy difference between bcc and fcc droplet lattices is only a few keV per nucleon near the droplet-to-rod transition.
- Enlarging grid points from 64 to 128 leads to an energy difference within a few keV.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of nuclear pasta using the Thomas-Fermi approximation, particularly the self-consistent Thomas-Fermi calculations by Bao et al. (2015) which found that nonspherical pasta phases may appear for small L values (e.g., L=40 MeV) but not for L ≥ 80 MeV. The fully three-dimensional approach without assumed geometric symmetry allows the observation of intermediate pasta structures that would be missed in Wigner-Seitz calculations. The results enable better predictions for neutron-star crust structure and radius, which can be compared with constraints from gravitational-wave events like GW170817 and NICER observations of PSR J0030+0451.
