# The Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method

> The LIT approach is reviewed both for inclusive and exclusive reactions. It is shown that the method reduces a continuum state problem to a bound-state-like problem, which then can be solved with typical bound-state techniques. The LIT approach opens up the possibility to perform ab initio calculations of reactions also for those particle systems which presently are out of reach in conventional approaches with explicit calculations of many-body continuum wave functions. Various LIT applications are discussed ranging from particle systems with two nucleons up to particle systems with seven nucleons.

# The Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method

## Key claim

The LIT method reduces a continuum state problem to a bound-state-like problem, enabling *ab initio* calculations of reaction cross sections for particle systems where explicit many-body continuum wave functions are currently out of reach; however, the method requires careful choice of the width parameter σ_I and sufficient spatial extent of the LIT function to resolve narrow resonances, and the inversion procedure must be checked for stability against varying the number of basis functions.

## Method

The method solves the equation (H - E_0 - σ_R - i σ_I) |Ψ̃_i⟩ = Θ_i |0⟩, where the complex energy ensures a localized, square-integrable solution. The norm ⟨Ψ̃_i|Ψ̃_i⟩ equals the Lorentz integral transform L_i(σ_R, σ_I) of the response function R_i(ω,q). By computing L_i for many σ_R values at fixed σ_I, one inverts the transform to obtain R_i(ω,q) using an expansion in basis functions χ_m(ω',α_j) = ω'^{α_1} exp(-α_2 ω'/m) with linear coefficients determined by a best fit. For exclusive reactions, two LIT equations are solved, one with Θ_i|0⟩ and one with V|Φ(E_f)⟩ as right-hand sides, and the overlap ⟨Ψ̃_V|Ψ̃_i⟩ yields the LIT of the final-state interaction function.

## Key numbers

- For the deuteron photodisintegration test with the AV14 NN potential, inversion with M_max = 25 and 26 gave essentially identical results, showing stability.
- For a fictitious np system with a narrow resonance at E_np = 0.48 MeV and width Γ = 270 keV, the LIT method required R_max ≥ 30 fm for errors below about 1%, and R_max = 50 fm reduced the relative error by about a factor ten.
- With σ_I = 1 MeV and R_max = 30 fm, the resonance cross section was described with high accuracy; with R_max = 50 fm, considerable improvement was obtained in the second resonance region.
- Reducing σ_I from 1 MeV to 0.5 MeV enhanced the relative error of L_γ^d by at least a factor of five for the same R_max values.
- For σ_I = 0.1 MeV, even R_max = 300 fm was not completely sufficient to resolve the cross section at higher energies.
- σ_I = 2 MeV (about seven times larger than Γ = 270 keV) was still sufficient for resonance resolution.
- For the ^4He total photoabsorption cross section, the 3N force led to a considerable reduction of the peak cross section compared to the AV18 NN-only calculation.
- For ^6He, the LIT calculation with semirealistic NN forces showed a double-peak structure in the photoabsorption cross section.
- For ^7Li, the LIT calculation with the AV4' semirealistic NN potential showed quite good agreement with experimental data from Ahrens.

## Context

This review extends the earlier Stieltjes transform approach proposed by Efros (1985), which suffered from serious inversion problems. The LIT method enables *ab initio* calculations of electroweak cross sections for systems from A=2 to A=7, including the first realistic calculations of three- and four-body total photoabsorption cross sections (Efros et al., 2000; Doron et al., 2006) and inelastic neutral-current neutrino scattering off ^4He (Gazit and Nussinov, 2007). The method opens the possibility to perform continuum reaction calculations for any system where bound-state techniques are available, with applications ranging from inclusive electron scattering to exclusive reactions such as ^4He(γ,n)^3He and ^4He(e,e'p)^3H.
