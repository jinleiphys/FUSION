# An algebraic solution of the multichannel problem applied to low energy nucleon-nucleus scattering

> Compound resonances in nucleon-nucleus scattering are related to the discrete spectrum of the target. Such resonances can be studied in a unified and general framework by a scattering model that uses sturmian expansions of postulated multichannel interactions between the colliding nuclei. Associated with such expanded multichannel interactions are algebraic multichannel scattering matrices. The matrix structure of the inherent Green functions not only facilitates extraction of the sub-threshold (compound nucleus) bound state spin-parity values and energies but also readily gives the energies and widths of resonances in the scattering regime. We exploited also the ability of the sturmian-expansion method to deal with non-local interactions to take into account the strong non-local effects introduced by the Pauli principle. As an example, we have used the collective model (to second order) to define a multichannel potential matrix for low energy neutron-C12 scattering allowing coupling between the 0+ (ground), 2+ (4.4389 MeV), and 0+ (7.64 MeV) states. The algebraic S matrix for this system has been evaluated and the sub-threshold bound states as well as cross sections and polarizations as functions of energy are predicted. The results are reflected in the actual measured data, and are shown to be consistent with expectations as may be based upon a shell model description of the target and of the compound nucleus.

# An algebraic solution of the multichannel problem applied to low energy nucleon-nucleus scattering

## Key claim

The sturmian expansion method provides an algebraic solution for multichannel nucleon-nucleus scattering that can simultaneously predict sub-threshold bound states, resonance energies and widths, and cross sections, but only when the multichannel interaction potentials can be represented by finite-rank separable expansions and the collective model deformation is taken to second order.

## Method

The authors constructed multichannel potential matrices using the Tamura collective model to second order in deformation, with central, spin-orbit, spin-spin, and ℓ²-dependent components, for neutron-¹²C scattering coupling the 0⁺ ground state, 2⁺ (4.4389 MeV), and 0⁺ (7.64 MeV) states. They represented these potentials using finite-rank separable expansions based on second-generation sturmian functions (Weinberg states) derived from first-generation sturmians at a fixed negative energy of -1 MeV, then solved the coupled-channel Lippmann-Schwinger equations algebraically to obtain the S-matrix. Pauli principle violations were handled by adding orthogonalizing pseudo-potentials (OPP) with λ ≈ 100 MeV to eliminate forbidden 0s₁/₂ and 0p₃/₂ configurations.

## Key numbers

- First-generation sturmians were evaluated at a fixed negative energy of B = -1 MeV, independent of channel
- The target states used were 0⁺ (ground), 2⁺ (4.4389 MeV), and 0⁺ (7.64 MeV) in ¹²C
- Neutron scattering calculations were performed for laboratory energies up to 5 MeV
- The OPP parameter was set to λ ≈ 100 MeV, described as "sufficiently large" for low-energy scattering
- The collective model deformation was taken to second order (β² terms) due to strong collectivity of the nucleus

## Context

This work extends the multichannel scattering formalism developed by the Padova group (Canton, Pisent, and collaborators, 1986-1995) based on earlier work by Rawitscher and Delic (1982, 1984), which uses sturmian expansions to create separable representations of interaction potentials. The approach contrasts with phenomenological R-matrix analyses such as that by Rice (1980) for n-¹²C scattering, which specified background scattering from an optical potential with shell-model partial widths. The algebraic solution enables rapid identification of narrow resonances without requiring a super-fine energy grid, and the authors note that future work will replace the collective model with microscopic shell-model specifications of target nuclei folded with effective two-nucleon forces.
