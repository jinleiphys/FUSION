# Mean field and beyond description of nuclear structure with the Gogny force: A review

> Nowadays, the Gogny force is a referent in the theoretical description of nuclear structure phenomena. Its phenomenological character manifests in a simple analytical form that allows for implementations of techniques both at the mean field and beyond all over the nuclide chart. Over the years, multiple applications of the standard many-body techniques in an assorted set of nuclear structure applications have produced results which are in a rather good agreement with experimental data. The agreement allows for a simple interpretation of those intriguing phenomena in simple terms and gives confidence on the predictability of the interaction. The present status on the implementation of different many body techniques with the Gogny force is reviewed with a special emphasis on symmetry restoration and large amplitude collective motion.

## Key claim

This review argues that the Gogny force, despite its phenomenological character and simple analytical form, enables implementations of both mean-field and beyond-mean-field techniques across the entire nuclear chart, producing results in rather good agreement with experimental data. The hedge is that while the agreement allows for simple interpretation of nuclear phenomena and gives confidence in predictability, the review acknowledges that computational demands limit applicability for medium and heavy nuclei, especially when several collective coordinates are included in symmetry-projected Generator Coordinate Method calculations.

## Method

The paper reviews multiple many-body techniques implemented with the Gogny force, including Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations, Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA), fermion-to-boson mapping to the Interacting Boson Model (IBM), and the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) for large amplitude collective motion. The authors describe computer codes developed by their group (HFBax, Atb, HFBaxT, PNPax, GCMax, HFBTri) that implement these methods using an expansion on axially symmetric harmonic oscillator bases, with the HFB equation solved via a second order gradient method. For the IBM mapping, constrained mean-field energy surfaces from Gogny-D1M calculations are mapped onto bosonic coherent states to determine IBM Hamiltonian parameters without phenomenological adjustment to experimental data.

## Key numbers

* The IBM mapping study of Ge and Se isotopes covers nuclei from 66-94Ge and 68-96Se, with configuration mixing included for 66,70-74,90-94Ge and 68-76,90-96Se
* The computed E(2+1) energies for Ge and Se nuclei show a pronounced peak at N=50 and decrease as N approaches 40
* The maximum B(E2; 2+1 -> 0+1) value is reached around N=40 where deformation is largest
* The experimental B(E2; 0+2 -> 2+1) value is very large around N=38 or 40, a feature not reproduced by the calculations
* The HFBax code can handle fission up to the scission point and beyond (two well separated fragments) without numerical instability
* The HFBTri triaxial code can use basis sizes up to 20 shells

## Context

This review extends the line of work on Gogny force applications that began with the D1 family of parametrizations (D1, D1', D1S, D1N, D1M, D1P, DS280, D1M*) and includes newer parametrizations like the D2 family and those including tensor terms (D1ST, D1MT, D1ST2a, D1ST2b, D1MT2c, GT2). The paper builds upon earlier QRPA reviews by Peru and Martini (2014) and prior GCM calculations by Bender (2003), Niksic (2011), and Rodriguez-Guzman (2002a). The IBM mapping procedure, introduced by Nomura (2008), enables computationally economic systematic description of spectroscopic properties in regions where experimental data are scarce, such as exotic neutron-rich nuclei. The review enables future work by documenting the status of computer codes (HFBtho, HFBodd, and the authors' own codes) that are either published or available upon request, and by identifying open challenges such as the need for angular momentum projection to fix systematic deviations in B(E3) predictions for near-spherical nuclei.
