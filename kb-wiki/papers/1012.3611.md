# Symmetries in Nuclei

> The use of dynamical symmetries or spectrum generating algebras for the solution of the nuclear many-body problem is reviewed. General notions of symmetry and dynamical symmetry in quantum mechanics are introduced and illustrated with simple examples such as the SO(4) symmetry of the hydrogen atom and the isospin symmetry in nuclei. Two nuclear models, the shell model and the interacting boson model, are reviewed with particular emphasis on their use of group-theoretical techniques.

## Key claim

This review argues that dynamical symmetries (spectrum generating algebras) provide a powerful framework for solving the nuclear many-body problem, but the symmetry is always approximate: the Hamiltonian is not fully invariant under the larger algebra but is instead a combination of Casimir operators of both the larger and smaller algebras, so that eigenstates remain unchanged while eigenvalues split.

## Method

The paper introduces the mathematical concept of symmetry and dynamical symmetry in quantum mechanics, using the SO(4) symmetry of the hydrogen atom and isospin symmetry in nuclei as illustrative examples. It then reviews two major nuclear models, the nuclear shell model and the interacting boson model, with emphasis on their group-theoretical techniques, showing how dynamical symmetry breaking splits but does not admix eigenstates.

## Key numbers

- Neutron mass: \(m_{\rm n}c^2 = 939.55\) MeV; proton mass: \(m_{\rm p}c^2 = 938.26\) MeV (isospin symmetry context)
- Hydrogen atom energy levels: \(E(n) = -R_{\rm H}/n^2\) with degeneracy \(n^2\) per principal quantum number \(n\)
- Hydrogen atom degeneracy from SO(4) symmetry yields \(E(p) = -R_{\rm H}/(2p+1)^2\) with \(p = 0, 1/2, 1, \dots\)
- Isobaric multiplet mass equation (IMME): \(E(M_T) = \kappa_0 + \kappa_1 M_T + \kappa_2 M_T^2\) for states with good isospin \(T\) and projection \(M_T\)

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on dynamical symmetries in nuclear physics, building on Pauli's (1926) algebraic solution of the hydrogen atom and Wigner's (1937, 1957) introduction of isospin and the isobaric multiplet mass equation. It enables future applications of group-theoretical methods to the shell model and interacting boson model, and provides a framework for understanding symmetry breaking in nuclear spectra, as illustrated by the mirror nuclei comparison of Leary (1997) for \(^{49}\)Cr and \(^{49}\)Mn.
