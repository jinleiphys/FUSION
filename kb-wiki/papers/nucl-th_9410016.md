# Spectroscopic Amplitudes for One--Nucleon Transfer Between 1p0f--Shell Nuclei

> Spectroscopic amplitudes are calculated for one--nucleon transfer between low--lying, normal--parity states of nuclei in the lower part of the 1p$0\,$f--shell. Calculations are performed using shell--model wave functions obtained from the diagonalization procedure of a nuclear Hamiltonian in the space given by the complete set of states generated from the 1p$_{3/2}$, 1p$_{1/2}$, $0\,$f$_{7/2}$ and $0\,$f$_{5/2}$ orbits. The Hamiltonian contains one and two body interactions derived recently by Richter \etal. Sum rules for one--nucleon pick--up and stripping reactions are given. The selectivity in excitation of the final states induced by one--nucleon pick--up or stripping is discussed.

## Key claim

Spectroscopic amplitudes for one-nucleon transfer between low-lying normal-parity states of nuclei in the mass range 41 ≤ A ≤ 47 are calculated using shell-model wave functions from the full 1p₃/₂, 1p₁/₂, 0f₇/₂, and 0f₅/₂ model space, but the upper mass limit is constrained by the long CPU time and disc quota required on the CONVEX C3 computer to solve the eigenvalue problem for heavier nuclei.

## Method

The authors diagonalized a nuclear Hamiltonian in the complete 1p0f-shell model space generated from the 1p₃/₂, 1p₁/₂, 0f₇/₂, and 0f₅/₂ orbits using the program RITSSCHIL, employing the mass-dependent FPD6 two-body interactions derived by Richter et al. (1991). Spectroscopic amplitudes for one-nucleon pick-up and stripping were then calculated from the resulting eigenfunctions using the formalism of Towner (1977) and Ichimura et al. (1973), with explicit formulae for the partial spectroscopic amplitudes given in the appendix. Sum rules for one-nucleon pick-up and stripping in the full 1p0f shell-model space were derived, extending the earlier sum rules of Macfarlane and French (1960) which were limited to one or two active orbits.

## Key numbers

- Mass range of calculated nuclei: 41 ≤ A ≤ 47, constrained by computational limitations on the CONVEX C3 computer
- For neutron pick-up from ⁴³Ca, 94% of the total strength is deposited in the six lowest-lying states of ⁴²Ca, with the first 0⁺1 state absorbing 25% and the first 6⁺1 state absorbing 33% of the total strength
- For neutron pick-up from ⁴⁴Ca, 91% of the total strength is deposited in the first 7/2⁻3/2 state of ⁴³Ca
- For neutron pick-up from ⁴⁵Sc, 50% of the total strength is distributed among the first 2⁺1 (7%), 6⁺1 (8%), 4⁺1 (7%), 1⁺1 (6%), and 7⁺1 (22%) states of ⁴⁴Sc
- The spectroscopic factor for the isobaric analog of ⁴⁴Ca₍ₛ.ₛ.₎ in ⁴⁴Sc (0⁺2, 2.783 MeV) extracted from ⁴⁵Sc(³He,α)⁴⁴Sc is 0.5 and from ⁴⁵Sc(d,t)⁴⁴Sc is 1.1, while the calculated value is 0.55
- For neutron stripping on ⁴⁶Ca, 23% of the total strength is deposited in the first 7/2⁻7/2 (7%), first 3/2⁻7/2 (11%), and second 1/2⁻7/2 (5%) states of ⁴⁷Ca
- For neutron stripping on ⁴⁵Sc, only 12.9% of the total strength is distributed among the seven lowest states of ⁴⁶Sc
- Calculated spectroscopic factor for ⁴¹Ca ground state (7/2⁻): 1.81 (experimental: 1.46 from (p,d) at 26.5 MeV, 1.60 from (³He,α) at 18 MeV)
- Calculated spectroscopic factor for ⁴³Ca ground state (7/2⁻): 3.64 (experimental: 3.3 from (p,d) at 26.5 MeV, 3.5 from (³He,α) at 18 MeV)
- Calculated spectroscopic factor for ⁴⁴Sc 2⁺ ground state: 0.35 (experimental: 0.35 from (d,t) at 19.5 MeV)
- Calculated spectroscopic factor for ⁴⁴Sc 7⁺ state at 0.968 MeV: 1.09 (experimental: 1.29 from (d,t) at 19.5 MeV)

## Context

This work extends the systematic calculation of spectroscopic amplitudes from lighter 0p-shell nuclei (Cohen and Kurath 1967, 1970; Kurath and Millener 1975; Kwaśniewicz and Jarczyk 1985) and 1s0d-shell nuclei (Conze and Monakos 1979; McGrory and Wildenthal 1973) into the lower 1p0f-shell region, using the recently derived FPD6 interactions of Richter et al. (1991). The results enable comparison with experimental spectroscopic factors from transfer reactions such as (d,t), (d,p), (p,d), and (³He,α), and the authors note that the distribution of total strength among residual 1p0f-shell nuclei is similar to that for lighter nuclei. The paper also discusses the ongoing discrepancy between spectroscopic factors from transfer reactions and those from (e,e'p) reactions, citing de Witt Huberts (1990) and theoretical work by Pandharipande (1989), Benhar et al. (1989), and Van Neck et al. (1991) on short-range correlations and final-state interaction effects.
