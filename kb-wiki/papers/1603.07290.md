# Empirical residual neutron-proton interaction in odd-odd nuclei

> Two types of average neutron-proton interaction formulas are compared: In the first type, neutron-proton interactions for even-even and odd-$A$ nuclei extracted from experimental binding energies show a smooth behavior as a function of mass number $A$ and are dominated by the contribution from the symmetry energy. Whereas in the second type large systematic staggering is seen between even-$A$ and odd-$A$ nuclei. This deviation is understood in term of the additional neutron-proton interaction in odd-odd nuclei relative to the neighboring even-even and odd-$A$ systems. We explore three possible ways to extract this additional interaction from the binding energy difference of neighboring nuclei. The extracted interactions are positive in nearly all cases and show weak dependence on the mass number. The empirical interactions are also compared with theoretical values extracted from recent nuclear mass models where large unexpected fluctuations are seen in certain nuclei. The reproduction of the residual neutron-proton interaction and the correction of those irregular fluctuations can be a good criterion for the refinement of those mass models.

## Key claim
The paper shows that the additional residual neutron-proton interaction in odd-odd nuclei, extracted from binding energy differences, is positive in nearly all cases and shows weak dependence on mass number, but large unexpected fluctuations appear when the same quantity is extracted from recent nuclear mass models, particularly the HFB model.

## Method
The authors compared two families of formulas for extracting average neutron-proton (np) interactions from experimental binding energies: the δV_np family (Eqs. 1-4) and the V_1n-1p family (Eq. 5). They then defined three different formulas (δ^(1)_np, δ^(2)_np, δ^(3)_np in Eqs. 9-11) to isolate the residual np interaction in odd-odd nuclei by taking differences between binding energies of neighboring even-even, odd-A, and odd-odd nuclei. These empirical values were compared with those extracted from three theoretical mass models: a macroscopic-microscopic (mac-mic) formula, a 33-term shell-model mass formula, and the HFB-24 mass formula.

## Key numbers
- The mean δ_np values extracted from experimental data are around 300 keV for all three extraction methods.
- For available superheavy nuclei, δ_np values are mostly below 200 keV.
- For the shell-model mass formula, δ^(1)_np is as low as -0.38 MeV for the N=81 nucleus 132Sb, compared to the experimental value of 0.11 MeV.
- For the HFB mass formula, δ^(1)_np = 5.2 MeV for 18F compared to the experimental result of 1.97 MeV.
- Fluctuations in the shell-model mass formula occur at mass regions around A=150 and 220 (up to ±0.5 MeV) and in unknown superheavy nuclei (up to ±1 MeV).
- Fluctuations for unknown nuclei from the HFB mass formula are mostly around ±2 MeV, reaching ±4 MeV in a few cases around A=300.
- Fluctuations from the mac-mic model reach up to ±2 MeV in the superheavy region.

## Context
This work extends previous studies of average np interactions extracted from binding energies, particularly the δV_np filter studied by Zhang (1989) and the V_1n-1p filter proposed by Bass (1971) and recently applied by Zhao and collaborators (2011-2015). The paper clarifies the systematic deviations between the two families of formulas and provides a method to isolate the residual np interaction in odd-odd nuclei. The results enable refinement of nuclear mass models, including the macroscopic-microscopic formula of Wang (2014), the shell-model mass formula of Duflo and Zuker (1995), and the HFB mass formula of Goriely, Hilaire, and Peru (2013), by providing a criterion to correct irregular fluctuations in predicted binding energies of neighboring even and odd nuclei.
