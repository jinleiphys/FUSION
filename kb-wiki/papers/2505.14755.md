# Discovery of the anti-glitch in PSR J1835$-$1106

> We report the detection of an anti-glitch with a fractional frequency change of $Δν/ν=-3.46(6)\times10^{-9}$ in the rotation-powered pulsar PSR J1835$-$1106 at MJD 55813, based on timing observations collected with the Nanshan 26-m and Parkes 64-m radio telescopes from January 2000 to July 2022. A comparison of the average pulse profiles within $\pm300$ d of the event reveals no significant morphological changes. We also estimate the angular velocity lag between the normal and superfluid components at the time of the glitch, showing that one of the superfluid glitch models is incompatible with PSR J1835$-$1106 due to its insufficient spin-down rate and angular velocity lag. The wind braking scenario offers a viable alternative, consistent with the observed spin-down behavior, glitch amplitude, and post-glitch recovery. High-cadence, high-sensitivity monitoring of similar events is essential to distinguish between internal (superfluid) and external (wind-related) glitch mechanisms.

## Key claim

The paper reports the detection of an anti-glitch (a sudden decrease in spin frequency) in the rotation-powered pulsar PSR J1835$-$1106, which is only the third such event observed in a rotation-powered pulsar rather than in a magnetar. The authors argue that the event cannot be explained by the superfluid vortex creep model (the Delta V effect) because the estimated angular velocity lag between the normal and superfluid components is far too small, and instead propose that a temporary increase in particle wind luminosity is a viable alternative.

## Method

The authors analyzed timing observations of PSR J1835$-$1106 collected with the Nanshan 26-m and Parkes 64-m radio telescopes from January 2000 to July 2022. They used PSRCHIVE for coherent dedispersion and profile folding, and TEMPO2 with the JPL DE440 ephemeris to fit a Taylor-series rotation phase model and extract glitch parameters. They compared average pulse profiles within a 600-day window centered on the anti-glitch epoch to search for morphological changes, and estimated the angular velocity lag between the crust and superfluid using the preceding normal glitch as a reference.

## Key numbers

- Fractional frequency change of the anti-glitch: Delta nu / nu = -3.46(6) x 10^-9 at MJD 55813(9)
- Pre-glitch spin frequency: nu = 6.0273153061(3) Hz at epoch MJD 51895
- Post-glitch spin frequency (first interval): nu = 6.0271780300(3) Hz at epoch MJD 54019
- Post-glitch spin frequency (second interval): nu = 6.0269456679(2) Hz at epoch MJD 57614
- Permanent change in spin-down rate: Delta nu-dot / nu-dot = 0.47(3) x 10^-3
- Estimated angular velocity lag between normal and superfluid components: 1.4 x 10^-3 rad/s, far below the 1 rad/s threshold required for the Delta V effect
- Estimated relative change in magnetospheric charge density: Delta rho / rho_GJ = 3.08 x 10^-4
- Inferred increase in particle wind luminosity during the anti-glitch: beta^2 approximately 1.036 (3.6% increase)
- Expected cumulative frequency change from glitch activity if the statistical relation nu-dot_g = 0.01 |nu-dot| holds: 5.14 x 10^-6 Hz over 7958 days; observed total: 1.66 x 10^-7 Hz (factor of 32 smaller)

## Context

This work extends the small sample of anti-glitches in rotation-powered pulsars, following the radiatively quiet anti-glitch in PSR B0540-69 reported by Tuo et al. (2024) and the series of anti-glitch episodes in PSR J1522-5735 reported by Zhou et al. (2024). The paper rules out the Delta V effect proposed by Khriplovich (2014) for this pulsar due to insufficient angular velocity lag, and instead favors the wind braking scenario proposed by Tong et al. (2013), which has previously been applied to magnetar anti-glitches such as 1E 2259+586. The results motivate high-cadence, multi-frequency monitoring to distinguish between internal superfluid and external wind-related mechanisms, with future observations from the enhanced X-ray Timing and Polarimetry mission (eXTP) expected to expand the sample.
