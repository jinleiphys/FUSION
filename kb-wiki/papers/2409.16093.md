# Going against the flow: Revealing the QCD degrees of freedom in hadronic collisions

> In collisions between heavy nuclei, such as those at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN, hydrodynamic models have successfully related measured azimuthal momentum anisotropies to the transverse shape of the collision region. For an elliptically shaped interaction area, the hydrodynamic pressure gradient is greater along the minor axis, resulting in increased particle momentum in that direction - a phenomenon known as positive elliptic flow. In this paper, we demonstrate that in smaller systems, such as proton-proton and peripheral ion-ion collisions, microscopic models for final state interactions, can produce anisotropies where the elliptic flow is negative - that is, the momentum is largest along the major axis, contrary to hydrodynamic predictions. We present results from two distinct microscopic models: one based on repulsion between string-like fields and another based on effective kinetic theory. Negative elliptic flow is a solid prediction of the string interaction model while in the model based on kinetic theory it is linked to a finite interaction range. Consequently, an experimental determination of the sign of elliptic flow, will provide novel insights into the degrees of freedom governing strong nuclear interactions in high-energy collisions and the way in which they interact.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that microscopic models of final-state interactions can produce negative elliptic flow (momentum largest along the major axis of the collision region) in small systems such as proton-proton and peripheral ion-ion collisions, contrary to the positive elliptic flow predicted by hydrodynamic models. The sign of elliptic flow is a solid prediction of the string interaction model, while in kinetic theory it is linked to a finite interaction range and appears in a regime where the theory is formally not applicable.

## Method

The authors employed two distinct microscopic models: a string interaction model based on repulsion between parallel string-like fields (implemented in the Pythia event generator with the shoving model and Angantyr extension) and a parton cascade approach (ALPACA) solving the AMY effective kinetic theory for gluons with elastic 2-to-2 scattering. They studied elliptic flow in toy-model two-hotspot and four-hotspot initial states, then extended to realistic initial states using saturation-based initial conditions for kinetic theory and Pythia-based initial states for the string model, calculating the elliptic flow coefficient v2 as a function of system size.

## Key numbers

* String model: string radius sqrt(⟨R²⟩) = 1 fm; repulsive force per unit length f(d⊥) = (g κ d⊥ / R²) exp(-d⊥²/4R²) with string tension κ ≈ 1 GeV/fm
* Kinetic theory toy model: elastic scattering cross section σ = 4π fm² (chosen for illustration, unrealistically large for QCD); interaction range r = sqrt(σ/π)
* Kinetic theory realistic scenario: pp collisions at sqrt(s) = 5.02 TeV; initial gluon number fixed at 16; sign change of v2 occurs at R0 ≈ 0.2 fm
* String model realistic scenarios: ¹²C-¹²C and ²d-¹⁹⁷Au collisions show transition from negative to positive v2 as function of Npart (number of participating nucleons)

## Context

This work extends the study of anisotropic flow from large heavy-ion systems, where hydrodynamic models (Ollitrault, 1992) successfully describe positive elliptic flow, into small collision systems where QGP signatures have recently been observed (CMS, 2010; ALICE, 2012). The paper builds on prior work showing that both models converge to hydrodynamic behavior in the large-system limit (Kurkela, 2018; Bierlich, 2020), and challenges the universality of hydrodynamic behavior by demonstrating that microscopic degrees of freedom can produce qualitatively different signatures. The results enable experimental tests of the relevant degrees of freedom in QCD by measuring the sign of elliptic flow in small systems, and suggest complementary measurements in cold atom systems (Brandstetter, 2023) where system size and interaction range can be controlled.
