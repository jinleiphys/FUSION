# Curvilinear coordinates and curvature in radiative transport

> We derive a general expression for the streaming term in radiative transport equa- tions and other transport problems when formulated in curvilinear coordinates, emphasizing coordinate systems adapted to the geometry of the domain and the directional dependence of particle transport. By parametrizing the angular vari- able using a local orthonormal frame, we express directional derivatives in terms of curvature-related quantities that reflect the geometry of underlying spatial man- ifolds. Our formulation highlights how the interaction between coordinate choices and curvature influences the streaming operator, offering geometric interpretations of its components. The resulting framework offers intuitive insight into when and how angular dependence can be simplified and may guide the selection of coordinate systems that balance analytical tractability and computational efficiency.

## Key claim

The paper derives a general expression for the streaming term in radiative transport equations when formulated in curvilinear coordinates, showing that the directional derivatives can be expressed in terms of curvature-related quantities of the underlying spatial manifolds, with the hedge that the derivation assumes the orthonormal vector fields n, t, and b are differentiable and form an integrable orthonormal frame.

## Method

The authors parametrize the angular variable Omega using a local orthonormal frame (n, t, b) and apply the chain rule to express Omega dot nabla Psi in terms of partial derivatives with respect to the angular coordinates mu and omega. They then compute the terms Omega dot nabla mu and Omega dot nabla omega by relating them to curvature quantities of integral curves (kappa^n, kappa^t, kappa^b) and the shape operator of surfaces orthogonal to the vector fields, using Frobenius theorem and Weingarten's theorem.

## Key numbers

* For cylindrical coordinates with n = (0,0,1), t = (cos(phi), sin(phi), 0), b = (-sin(phi), cos(phi), 0): the term t dot kappa^b equals 1/rho, giving Omega dot nabla omega = -sqrt(1-mu^2) sin(omega)/rho
* The general expression for Omega dot nabla mu contains a term (1-mu^2)C(r,omega) where C(r,omega) is the curvature of the surface X^n in direction of the projected Omega, plus a term mu sqrt(1-mu^2)(hat-Omega_parallel dot kappa^n)
* The general expression for Omega dot nabla omega contains a term sqrt(1-mu^2)[cos(omega)(b dot kappa^t) - sin(omega)(t dot kappa^b)] plus mu(t dot nabla_n b)

## Context

This work extends the derivations of Freimanis (no year given) who calculated the streaming term for arbitrary spatial coordinates but restricted to two specific choices of the angular frame, whereas the present paper imposes fewer restrictions on n, t, and b and focuses on geometric interpretations. The framework provides intuitive guidance for selecting coordinate systems that balance analytical tractability and computational efficiency, and may simplify derivations that previously required tedious geometric arguments as seen in Bell and Glasstone (no year given) and the helical coordinate work (no year given).
