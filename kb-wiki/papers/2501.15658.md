# Shear Viscosity of Collider-Produced QCD Matter II: Comparing a Multi-Component Chapman-Enskog Framework with AMPT in Full Equilibrium

> Transport properties of the quark-gluon plasma are instrumental to testing perturbative quantum chromodynamics and understanding the extreme conditions of relativistic heavy-ion collisions. This study presents an analytical investigation of the shear viscosity $η$ and the shear viscosity-to-entropy density ratio $η/s$ of the QGP using a novel multi-component Chapman-Enskog framework assuming full thermalization. The approach incorporates species-specific contributions from gluons and (anti-)quarks into the plasma shear viscosity, temperature-dependent running parameters for the Debye mass and strong coupling, and a time-dependent cooling model. Our findings show that both $η$ and $η/s$ are enhanced by the inclusion of (anti-)quarks with gluons, and the parameters decrease over time due to the cooling and expansion of the QGP. These results align with perturbative QCD predictions, offering a more optimistic representation of QGP transport properties under dynamic conditions. This multi-component framework is compared with a multi-phase transport model that treats the QGP as a gluon gas with (anti-)quark augmentation.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a multi-component Chapman-Enskog framework for the quark-gluon plasma, which includes species-specific contributions from gluons and (anti-)quarks with temperature-dependent running parameters, yields shear viscosity and shear viscosity-to-entropy density ratio values that are enhanced compared to one-component descriptions and align more closely with perturbative QCD predictions, but this agreement is conditional on assuming full thermalization and using a time-dependent cooling model.

## Method

The author derived analytical expressions for shear viscosity of a three-component (gluon, quark, antiquark) QGP using the Chapman-Enskog method with linearized collision kernels for massless Maxwell-Boltzmann-distributed partons under anisotropic scatterings. Temperature-dependent running parameters for the strong coupling constant and Debye screening mass were incorporated, and a Newtonian cooling model with exponential temperature decay from 550 MeV to 150 MeV over 10 fm/c was applied to study time evolution. The results were compared with the AMPT transport model, which uses a constant cross section approximation.

## Key numbers

* Shear viscosity and eta/s are enhanced when quarks are included (N_f=3) compared to the gluon-only case (N_f=0) across the temperature range studied.
* The cooling model uses boundary conditions T=550 MeV at t=0 fm/c and T=150 MeV at t=10 fm/c, with temperature given by T(t)=550 MeV * exp(-1.204c/fm * t).
* The KSS lower bound of eta/s = 1/(4pi) is used as a reference line throughout the study.
* The AMPT model uses a constant total cross section of sigma_AMPT = 3 mb for comparison, with data from LHC (Pb+Pb at 2760A GeV) and RHIC (Au+Au at 200A GeV).
* The Debye mass is given by m_D^2 = (1/3)(3 + N_f/2)g^2 T^2, and the running coupling is defined by 1/g^2(T) = (9/8pi^2)ln(T/Lambda_T) + (4/9pi^2)ln[2 ln(T/Lambda_T)] with Lambda_T = 30 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous multi-component Chapman-Enskog framework (MacKay 2024) for anisotropic scatterings to a three-component QGP with running QCD parameters, and compares it with the AMPT transport model (Lin 2014, MacKay 2022) which treats the QGP as an effective gluon gas with constant cross sections. The results contradict the "wrong" trends seen in AMPT when compared to Bayesian analyses of experimental data and perturbative QCD, particularly at lower temperatures. The framework enables more accurate modeling of QGP transport properties for comparison with experimental anisotropic flow measurements from RHIC and LHC, and could be extended to include chemical non-equilibrium effects and partial fugacities.
