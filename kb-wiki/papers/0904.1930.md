# The Pt isotopes: comparing the Interacting Boson Model with Configuration Mixing and the Extended Consistent-Q formalism

> The role of intruder configurations in the description of energy spectra and B(E2) values in the Pt region is analyzed. In particular, we study the differences between Interacting Boson Model calculations with or without the inclusion of intruder states in the even Pt nuclei $^{172-194}$Pt. As a result, it shows that for the description of a subset of the existing experimental data, i.e., energy spectra and absolute B(E2) values up to an excitation energy of about 1.5 MeV, both approaches seem to be equally valid. We explain these similarities between both model spaces through an appropriate mapping. We point out the need for a more extensive comparison, encompassing a data set as broad (and complete) as possible to confront with both theoretical approaches in order to test the detailed structure of the nuclear wave functions.

## Key claim

For the even Pt isotopes \(^{172-194}\)Pt, the Interacting Boson Model with configuration mixing (IBM-CM) and the standard Interacting Boson Model using the extended Consistent-Q formalism (ECQF) reproduce equally well the experimental excitation energies and absolute \(B(E2)\) values for states below about 1.5 MeV, despite the IBM-CM including explicit proton 2p-2h intruder excitations while the ECQF does not. The authors explain this similarity through an appropriate mapping and renormalization of the interaction, but caution that a more extensive comparison with a broader data set is needed to discriminate between the two model spaces.

## Method

The authors performed Interacting Boson Model calculations for even Pt isotopes \(^{172-194}\)Pt using two approaches: (1) the IBM-CM, which includes mixing between regular proton 4h configurations (\(N\) bosons) and intruder proton 6h-2p configurations (\(N+2\) bosons) with a Hamiltonian of the extended Consistent-Q form in each subspace, and (2) the ECQF, which uses only the regular \(N\)-boson space without explicit intruder excitations. For the IBM-CM, eight free parameters per isotope were fitted to experimental excitation energies and absolute \(B(E2)\) values using a \(\chi^2\) minimization with the MINUIT package, while the ECQF fit used four free parameters per isotope determined from energy ratios and \(B(E2)\) ratios. The resulting energy spectra and \(B(E2)\) values from both models were then compared with each other and with experimental data for states up to approximately 1.5 MeV excitation energy.

## Key numbers

- The IBM-CM Hamiltonian parameters include \(\Delta^{N+2} = 1400\) keV and mixing strengths \(w_0^{N,N+2} = w_2^{N,N+2} = 50\) keV, fixed for all isotopes.
- For \(^{180}\)Pt, the experimental \(B(E2;4^+_1 \rightarrow 2^+_1)\) value from De Voigt et al. (1990) is \(140 \pm 30\) W.u., while a more recent measurement by Williams et al. (2006) gives \(260 \pm 32\) W.u.
- The ECQF fit for \(^{180}\)Pt yields parameters \(\zeta = 0.57\), \(\chi = -0.90\), \(\varepsilon = 480.9\) keV, \(\kappa = -13.3\) keV, and effective charge \(e = 2.17 \sqrt{\text{W.u.}}\)
- The IBM-CM fit for \(^{180}\)Pt yields parameters \(\varepsilon_N = 999.3\) keV, \(\kappa_N = -37.34\) keV, \(\kappa'_{N+2} = 6.57\) keV, \(\kappa_{N+2} = -25.14\) keV, \(\chi_{N+2} = -0.32\), \(e_N = 1.29 \sqrt{\text{W.u.}}\), and \(e_{N+2} = 1.94 \sqrt{\text{W.u.}}\)
- For \(^{172}\)Pt and \(^{174}\)Pt, no \(B(E2)\) values are known, so effective charges could not be determined.

## Context

This work extends earlier IBM-CM calculations by Harder et al. (1997) for the Pt nuclei, which reproduced the evolution of low-lying states qualitatively but did not calculate \(B(E2)\) values, and the ECQF calculations by McCutchan et al. (2005) which treated the Pt nuclei without explicit intruder excitations. The study shows that for states below 1.5 MeV, the two approaches are empirically equivalent, but the authors point out that clear differences appear for higher-lying states, motivating a forthcoming paper that will compare the models against a more complete data set including \(\alpha\)-decay hindrance factors, \(g(2^+_1)\) factors, isotopic shifts, and E0 decay properties.
