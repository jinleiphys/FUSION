# Partial Dynamical SU(3) Symmetry in Deformed Nuclei

> We discuss characteristic features of SU(3) partial dynamical symmetry in relation to nuclear spectroscopy and compare with previous broken-SU(3) calculations for ^{168}Er.

# Partial Dynamical SU(3) Symmetry in Deformed Nuclei

## Key claim
The SU(3) partial dynamical symmetry (PDS) provides a description of deformed nuclei where the ground band and gamma band states remain solvable with good SU(3) symmetry, while the beta band is mixed, offering an alternative to complete symmetry breaking approaches such as the Warner-Casten-Davidson (WCD) or consistent-Q formalism (CQF) calculations for 168Er.

## Method
The authors constructed an Interacting Boson Model (IBM) Hamiltonian consisting of boson-pair operators P†0 and P†2 with coefficients h0 and h2, plus an O(3) rotational term. For the specific condition h0 = -5h2, the Hamiltonian is not an SU(3) scalar but retains a subset of solvable eigenstates with good SU(3) symmetry belonging to the ground and γkK=2k bands. The E2 transition operator included both the SU(3) quadrupole generator Q(2) and a (2,2) tensor Π(2), allowing analytic expressions for B(E2) ratios between solvable states.

## Key numbers
- For 168Er (N=16), the K=02 band contains 9.6% (26,0) and 2.9% (24,4) admixtures into the dominant (28,2) irrep.
- The K=02 band contains 12.4% γ2K=0 and 0.1% β2 admixtures into the β mode.
- The B(E2) ratios for γ→g transitions are parameter-free predictions of SU(3) PDS, independent of both α and θ in the E2 operator.

## Context
This work extends the partial dynamical symmetry concept introduced by Leviatan (1996) to SU(3) in deformed nuclei, contrasting with the complete SU(3) breaking approaches of Warner, Casten, and Davidson (WCD) and the consistent-Q formalism (CQF). The results support recent theoretical claims by Casten and Brenner and experimental findings by Lehmann regarding the importance of double-γ components in the lowest K=0 excited band. The approach enables analytic predictions for E2 transitions between solvable states while allowing mixing in other bands, preserving some of the advantages of exact dynamical symmetry.
