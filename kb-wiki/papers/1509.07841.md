# Nuclear rotation in the continuum

> ${\textbf{Background:}}$ Atomic nuclei often exhibit collective rotational-like behavior in highly excited states, well above the particle emission threshold. What determines the existence of collective motion in the continuum region, is not fully understood. ${\textbf{Purpose:}}$ In this work, by studying the collective rotation of the positive-parity deformed configurations of the one-neutron halo nucleus $^{11}$Be, we assess different mechanisms that stabilize collective behavior beyond the limits of particle stability. ${\textbf{Method:}}$ To solve a particle-plus-core problem, we employ a non-adiabatic coupled-channel formalism and the Berggren single-particle ensemble, which explicitly contains bound states, narrow resonances, and the scattering continuum. We study the valence-neutron density in the intrinsic rotor frame to assess the validity of the adiabatic approach as the excitation energy increases. ${\textbf{Results:}}$ We demonstrate that collective rotation of the ground band of $^{11}$Be is stabilized by (i) the fact that the $\ell=0$ one-neutron decay channel is closed, and (ii) the angular momentum alignment, which increases the parentage of high-$\ell$ components at high spins; both effects act in concert to decrease decay widths of ground-state band members. This is not the case for higher-lying states of $^{11}$Be, where the $\ell=0$ neutron-decay channel is open and often dominates. ${\textbf{Conclusion:}}$ We demonstrate that long-lived collective states can exist at high excitation energy in weakly bound neutron drip-line nuclei such as $^{11}$Be.

## Key claim

Long-lived collective rotational states can exist at high excitation energy in weakly bound neutron drip-line nuclei such as \(^{11}\)Be, but this stabilization depends on the \(\ell=0\) one-neutron decay channel being closed and on angular momentum alignment increasing high-\(\ell\) components at high spins; these conditions do not hold for higher-lying states where the \(\ell=0\) channel is open.

## Method

The authors solved a particle-plus-core problem using a non-adiabatic coupled-channel formalism with the Berggren single-particle ensemble, which explicitly includes bound states, narrow resonances, and the scattering continuum. They modeled \(^{11}\)Be as a valence neutron coupled to a deformed \(^{10}\)Be core using a deformed Woods-Saxon potential with a spherical spin-orbit term, and studied the valence-neutron density in the intrinsic rotor frame to assess the validity of the adiabatic approach as excitation energy increases.

## Key numbers

- The \(4^+_1\) level in \(^{10}\)Be has a width of 121 keV.
- The optimized Woods-Saxon pseudo-potential parameters are: radius \(R_0 = 2.08\) fm, diffuseness \(a = 1.1\) fm, strength \(V_0 = -59.36\) MeV, spin-orbit strength \(V_{\rm so} = 15.09\) MeV, and quadrupole deformation \(\beta_2 = 0.53\).
- States in the favorite yrast band of \(^{11}\)Be are predicted to have small neutron widths of the order of 200 keV.
- For the unfavored band, neutron widths are larger, around \(\Gamma = 0.7\) MeV.
- Removing the non-resonant part from the Berggren ensemble for the \(7/2^+\) yrast state yields a width about 17% larger than the value obtained in the full basis.
- The average single-particle time scale for a light nucleus (\(A \approx 10\)) is estimated at \(T_{\rm s.p.} \approx 1.3 \times 10^{-22}\) sec, corresponding to a decay width threshold of \(\Gamma > 3.5\) MeV for \(A \approx 10\).
- The coupled-channel equations were solved up to a maximal radius of \(R_{\rm max} = 30\) fm with a rotation radius for exterior complex-scaling of \(R_{\rm rot} = 15\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the non-adiabatic coupled-channel method previously applied to deformed proton emitters (Kruppa 2000, Barmore 2000) and to the resonant spectrum of dipolar anions (Fossez 2015). It complements large-scale no-core shell model calculations (Maris 2015, Caprio 2013) that found a decoupled \(K^\pi = 1/2^+\) band in \(^{11}\)Be as an 11-body closed quantum system. The results suggest that continuum coupling plays a minor role in the yrast sequence of \(^{11}\)Be but can be appreciable for yrare states where s-wave decay is allowed, and the question of whether collective nuclear states apply to very broad resonances with half-lives shorter than the single-particle time scale remains open for future investigation.
