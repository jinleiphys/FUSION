# Kinetic-theory description of isoscalar dipole modes

> A semiclassical model, based on a solution of the Vlasov equation for finite systems with moving-surface, is employed to study the isoscalar dipole modes in nuclei. It is shown that, by taking into account the surface degree of freedom, it is possible to obtain an exact treatment of the centre of mass motion. It is also shown that a method often used to subtract the spurious strength in RPA calculations does not always give the correct result. An alternative analytical formula for the intrinsic strength function is derived in a simple confined-Fermi-gas model. In this model the intrinsic isoscalar dipole strength displays essentially a two-resonance structure, hence there are two relevant modes. The interaction between nucleons couples these two modes and changes the compressibility of the system. The evolution of the strength profile is then studied as a function of the compressibility of the nuclear fluid. Comparison with available data favours values of the incompressibility parameter of nuclear matter smaller than those suggested by the analysis of the monopole ``breathing'' mode.

# Kinetic-theory description of isoscalar dipole modes

## Key claim

The semiclassical Vlasov-equation model with moving-surface boundary conditions provides an exact treatment of centre-of-mass motion and yields an intrinsic isoscalar dipole strength function with a two-resonance structure; however, even after accurate subtraction of spurious centre-of-mass strength using a newly derived analytical formula, the model fails to reconcile the monopole and dipole data simultaneously, favouring an incompressibility parameter smaller than that suggested by monopole breathing-mode analyses.

## Method

The authors solve the linearized Vlasov equation for finite spherical nuclei with moving-surface boundary conditions, treating the nucleus as a confined Fermi gas of non-interacting nucleons in a spherical cavity with perfectly reflecting but movable walls. They derive an exact analytical expression for the intrinsic isoscalar dipole response function by separating the centre-of-mass contribution from the physical intrinsic response, then introduce a schematic separable dipole-dipole residual interaction to modify the nuclear compressibility. The incompressibility parameter is determined by fitting the calculated monopole resonance energy to experimental data, and the resulting dipole strength functions are compared with experimental data for 208Pb, 116Sn, and 90Zr.

## Key numbers

- The Fermi-gas incompressibility parameter is K_FG = 6 epsilon_F, with epsilon_F corresponding to R = 1.2 A^(1/3) fm, giving K_FG approximately 200 MeV.
- The intrinsic energy-weighted sum rule for the isoscalar dipole operator is (3 hbar^2)/(14 pi m) A R^4.
- The mean excitation energy from the confined Fermi gas model is hbar sqrt(3/2 * K_FG / (m <r^2>)), with <r^2> = 3/5 R^2.
- Fitting the monopole resonance data yields K_mon = 180 MeV.
- The confined Fermi gas strength integrated up to hbar omega = 40 MeV exhausts about 90% of the energy-weighted sum rule; integration to 100 MeV exhausts about 99%.
- The low-energy peak in 208Pb appears at about 13 MeV in the model.

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical Vlasov-equation approach of Abrosimov, Dellafiore, and Matera (2000) for finite systems with moving surfaces, and contrasts with fixed-surface models of Brink (1985) that produce spurious centre-of-mass dissipation. The results are compared with RPA calculations by Colo et al. (2000), Hamamoto et al. (1998), and Shlomo et al. (1999), as well as relativistic approaches by Vretenar et al. (1999) and Piekarewicz (2000). The model reproduces the two-resonance structure and A^(-1/3) scaling of peak positions seen in experimental data from Clark et al. (2001), but fails to simultaneously describe monopole and dipole data, a limitation shared with quantal RPA calculations. The work enables future studies of nuclear compressibility using isoscalar dipole modes as a complementary probe to the monopole breathing mode.
