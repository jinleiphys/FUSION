# Screened alpha decay in dense astrophysical plasmas and magnetars

> This paper shows that ultrastrong magnetic fields (such as those of magnetars) and dense astrophysical plasmas can reduce the half life of alpha decaying nuclei by many orders of magnitude. In such environments the conventional Geiger-Nuttall law is modifed so that all half lives are shifted to dramatically lower values. Those effects, which have never been investigated before, may have significant implications on the universal abundances of heavy radioactive elements and the cosmochronological methods that rely on them.

## Key claim

Ultrastrong magnetic fields (such as those of magnetars) and dense astrophysical plasmas can reduce the half-life of alpha-decaying nuclei by many orders of magnitude, modifying the conventional Geiger-Nuttall law so that all relevant half-lives are shifted to dramatically lower values; the effect is largest for nuclei with low decay energy Q_n, because the classical turning point is larger and the screening potential deviates most strongly from the unscreened one at large distances.

## Method

The author applies the Thomas-Fermi (TF) theory of multielectron screening, previously used for astrophysical fusion reactions, to alpha decay in three environments: terrestrial neutral atoms, magnetar-strength magnetic fields (B up to ~10^15 G), and dense stellar plasmas (s and r process sites). For each environment, the screening enhancement factor (SEF) is computed as the ratio of the screened to unscreened WKB penetration factor, using both the sudden limit (parent electron cloud undisturbed) and the adiabatic limit (all participants remain neutral). In the magnetic case, Kadomtsev's TF equation for superstrong fields is integrated numerically; in the plasma case, Mitler's screened Coulomb potential and Salpeter's formula for degenerate electron gases are adapted to alpha decay.

## Key numbers

- For uranium isotopes in magnetar fields, the half-life of 238U (terrestrial half-life 4.46 × 10^9 y) and 235U (terrestrial half-life 0.7 × 10^9 y) can be reduced by up to four orders of magnitude at fields near the model's upper limit (~10^15 G).
- In dense astrophysical plasmas, screened half-lives for 238U can be up to nine orders of magnitude smaller than the unscreened ones.
- The screening energy shift for a supermagnetized heavy atom in the adiabatic limit is given by U_TF^AL = 0.0136 b^(2/5) [Z^(9/5) - (Z-2)^(9/5) - 2^(9/5)] keV, where b = B/(2.351 × 10^9 G).
- For completely degenerate electron gases, the screening energy shift is U_e^AL = 0.0176 (ρ/μ_e)^(1/3) [Z^(5/3) - (Z-2)^(5/3) - 2^(5/3)] keV, independent of temperature.
- The model for magnetar fields is valid under the condition Z^(4/3) << b << 2Z^3 (or b << 4.25Z^3 per another study), and the relativistic parameter Λ = Z e^2 / (2 m_e c^2 R_B) must satisfy Λ << 1, equivalent to Z^(4/5) b^(2/5) << 38286.

## Context

This work extends the Thomas-Fermi screening formalism previously applied to astrophysical fusion reactions (Liolios, 2001) to alpha decay for the first time, and introduces the novel effects of superstrong magnetic fields (magnetars, as reviewed by Pavlov et al., 2002) and dense stellar plasmas on alpha-decay half-lives. The results contradict the assumption that alpha decay is negligible in heavy-element nucleosynthesis during the s and r processes (Clayton, 1968; Hoyle, 1960), and suggest that screened alpha decay could compete with neutron capture and beta decay in determining heavy-element abundances. The modified Geiger-Nuttall law enables the use of existing half-life data and plots by applying a shift of log10(f_alpha^(-1)) for magnetar or plasma environments.
