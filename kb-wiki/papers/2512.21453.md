# Spontaneous fission half-lives for heavy and super-heavy nuclei from phenomenological models

> A phenomenological model is proposed for a systematic description of the spontaneous fission (SF) half-lives $T_{\rm SF}$ of heavy and super-heavy nuclei. Based on the effective tunneling barrier (ETB), the proposed approach reproduces the SF half-lives of 79 known nuclei with an average deviation of 0.8, which is $17\%$ smaller than that of the linear correlation approach recently proposed in [N. S. Moiseev, N. V. Antonenko and G. G. Adamian, Phys. Rev. C 112, 034607 (2025)]. For superheavy nuclei with $45\leqslant N-Z \leqslant 61$, the predicted SF half-lives from these two different phenomenological models are in good agreement with each other. The ETB calculations implies that the $β$-decay energy affects the SF half-lives of nuclei far from the $β$-stability line. For superheavy nuclei around the magic number $N=184$, the predicted $T_{\rm SF}$ of $^{304}$120 is much shorter than that of $^{298}$Fl. With predicted values of about $10 \sim 160$ ms for $T_{\rm SF}$, the unmeasured SHN $^{293}119 $ could survive for long enough to reach the focal-plane detector in detection systems like the gas-filled recoil separator SHANS in Lanzhou.

# Spontaneous fission half-lives for heavy and super-heavy nuclei from phenomenological models

## Key claim
The proposed effective tunneling barrier (ETB) model reproduces spontaneous fission half-lives of 79 known nuclei with an average deviation of 0.8 orders of magnitude, which is 17% smaller than the linear correlation approach of Moiseev et al. (2025), but the model's reliability is limited to nuclei within the range where the truncation on beta-decay energy prevents unphysical predictions for nuclei far from the beta-stability line.

## Method
The authors construct an effective tunneling barrier (ETB) defined as U = (V_top - Q_sf + Delta) - Q_beta/x, where V_top is the Coulomb barrier between symmetric fission fragments, Q_sf is the spontaneous fission Q-value, Delta is the shell gap from ground-state energy differences, Q_beta is the total beta-decay energy, and x is the fissionability parameter. The spontaneous fission half-life is then expressed as log10 T_SF = c1 U + c2 U^2 + Delta_res, where Delta_res incorporates pairing effects and an oscillatory sine-function correction from the alpha-decay energy Q_alpha. Model parameters are fitted to 79 known nuclei from NUBASE2020, and predictions are compared with the MAA linear correlation formula of Moiseev et al. (2025) and the XRG formula of Ren et al. (2008).

## Key numbers
- Average deviation between ETB predictions and experimental data for 79 known nuclei: 0.800 orders of magnitude
- Average deviation for 42 even-even nuclei with ETB: 0.783, which is 28% smaller than XRG (Ren 2008) and 13% smaller than MAA (Moiseev 2025)
- Average deviation for all 79 nuclei with MAA formula: 0.959
- Shell gap values from WS4 model: Delta = 3.40 MeV for 272Ds, 5.58 MeV for 298Fl, and 3.76 MeV for 304120
- Effective tunneling barrier height for 236U: U = 28.8 MeV; for 246Cm: U = 19.5 MeV (difference of 9.3 MeV)
- Predicted T_SF for unmeasured 293119: approximately 10 to 160 milliseconds
- Separation distance d = 2.12 fm for even-even nuclei and d = 1.83 fm for nuclei with unpaired nucleons
- RMS error of WS4+RBF alpha-decay energy predictions for superheavy nuclei: 0.22 MeV

## Context
This work extends the phenomenological approach to spontaneous fission half-lives by introducing an effective tunneling barrier that incorporates beta-decay energy, addressing limitations of earlier models. It improves upon the XRG formula of Ren et al. (2008), which achieved average deviation of 0.98 for even-even nuclei using relative Coulomb barrier height, and the MAA linear correlation formula of Moiseev, Antonenko, and Adamian (2025), which correlated log10 T_SF with Q_alpha and neutron excess. The ETB model explains why 236U has a half-life nine orders of magnitude longer than 246Cm despite similar fission barrier heights, a puzzle highlighted by Heßberger. The predictions for 293119 suggest it could survive long enough (10-160 ms) to reach focal-plane detectors in systems like the SHANS separator in Lanzhou, guiding future superheavy element synthesis experiments.
