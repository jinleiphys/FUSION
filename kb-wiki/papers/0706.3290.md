# Multiplicity Distributions in Canonical and Microcanonical Statistical Ensembles

> The aim of this paper is to introduce a new technique for calculation of observables, in particular multiplicity distributions, in various statistical ensembles at finite volume. The method is based on Fourier analysis of the grand canonical partition function. Taylor expansion of the generating function is used to separate contributions to the partition function in their power in volume. We employ Laplace's asymptotic expansion to show that any equilibrium distribution of multiplicity, charge, energy, etc. tends to a multivariate normal distribution in the thermodynamic limit. Gram-Charlier expansion allows additionally for calculation of finite volume corrections. Analytical formulas are presented for inclusion of resonance decay and finite acceptance effects directly into the system partition function. This paper consolidates and extends previously published results of current investigation into properties of statistical ensembles.

## Key claim
The paper introduces a new analytical technique, based on Fourier analysis of the grand canonical partition function, to calculate multiplicity distributions in canonical and microcanonical ensembles at finite volume. The authors show that while any equilibrium distribution tends to a multivariate normal distribution in the thermodynamic limit, their method allows for the calculation of finite volume corrections via Gram-Charlier expansion, which the abstract does not specify as a primary focus.

## Method
The method identifies the generalized grand canonical partition function (GGPF) as the characteristic function of a statistical system. By performing a Taylor expansion of the cumulant generating function and applying Laplace's asymptotic expansion, the authors separate contributions to the partition function by their power in volume. This allows them to derive analytical formulas for the canonical and microcanonical multiplicity distributions, including the scaled variance, and to incorporate resonance decay and finite acceptance effects directly into the partition function.

## Key numbers
- The scaled variance of the particle number distribution in the canonical ensemble is given by the formula $\omega = \det |\tilde \kappa_2| / ( \kappa_1^{N} \det| \kappa_2| )$, where $\tilde \kappa_2$ is the $4 \times 4$ covariance matrix and $\kappa_2$ is the $3 \times 3$ covariance matrix of conserved charges.
- The asymptotic canonical partition function for a system with three conserved charges is approximated as $\mathcal{Z}^{Q^j} \simeq Z \exp \left(-\xi^j \xi_{j}/2 \right) / \left( (2\pi V)^{3/2} \det| \sigma| \right)$, where $\xi^j$ is a dimensionless measure of the distance from the mean charge vector.
- The paper demonstrates that the method reproduces the analytical results for the scaled variance in the thermodynamic limit from prior work by Begun et al. (2004) and others.

## Context
This work consolidates and extends previous investigations into the properties of statistical ensembles, particularly the finding by Begun et al. (2004) that the scaled variance of multiplicity distributions differs between the grand canonical, canonical, and microcanonical ensembles even for large systems. The new technique provides a more mathematically efficient and computationally faster alternative to Monte Carlo methods and the microscopic correlator approach used by Begun et al. (2004) and others. It enables the calculation of baseline fluctuations for heavy-ion collision experiments, against which signals of a phase transition or a critical point, as proposed by Stephanov et al. (1998), can be identified.
