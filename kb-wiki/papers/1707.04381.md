# Neutron-skin thickness determines the surface tension of a compressible nuclear droplet

> We systematically investigate the neutron-skin thickness of neutron-rich nuclei within a compressible droplet model, which includes several parameters characterizing the surface tension and the equation of state (EOS) of asymmetric nuclear matter as well as corrections due to the surface diffuseness. Such a systematic analysis helps towards constraining the EOS parameters of asymmetric nuclear matter and the poorly known density dependence of the surface tension; the latter is estimated with help of available experimental data for the neutron and proton density distributions and the nuclear masses. Validity of the present approach is confirmed by calculating realistic density distributions of Ca, Ni, Zr, Sn, Yb, and Pb isotopes within a microscopic Skyrme-Hartree-Fock+BCS method for various sets of the effective nuclear force. Our macroscopic model accompanied by the diffuseness corrections works well in the sense that it well reproduces the evolution of the microscopically deduced neutron-skin thickness with respect to the neutron number for selected sets of the effective nuclear force. We find that the surface tension of the compressible nuclear droplet is a key to bridging a gap between microscopic and macroscopic approaches.

# Neutron-skin thickness determines the surface tension of a compressible nuclear droplet

## Key claim

The neutron-skin thickness of neutron-rich nuclei can be described by a compressible droplet model that includes surface diffuseness corrections, and the density dependence of the surface tension (parameter χ) is the key factor that bridges microscopic and macroscopic approaches, but only for certain Skyrme effective interactions, as the SkI3 and LNS interactions fail to reproduce the microscopic results.

## Method

The authors used a compressible droplet model that expresses the neutron-skin thickness as a sum of a volume term (containing the primary factor C, which depends on the density dependence of surface tension χ) and a surface diffuseness correction term. They determined the primary factor C by fitting to experimental neutron-skin thickness data for stable Sn and Pb isotopes from proton-elastic scattering measurements (Terashima 2008, Zenihiro 2010), then compared the macroscopic model predictions against microscopic Skyrme-Hartree-Fock+BCS calculations for Ca, Ni, Zr, Sn, Yb, and Pb isotopes using nine different Skyrme interactions (SkM*, SLy4, SkI3, KDE0v1, LNS, SkT1-3, and SV-sym32).

## Key numbers

- Primary factor C = 1.06^{+0.08}_{-0.07} determined from experimental data for stable Sn and Pb isotopes, with symmetry energy coefficient S₀ = 32 ± 4 MeV
- Surface widths b_q range between approximately 0.6 and 1 fm for all isotopes studied
- Surface tension parameters used: σ₀ = 1 MeV fm⁻², C_sym = 1.9 (from global mass fit by Koura 2005)
- For SkM* and SLy4 interactions, the extracted χ values are close to the Fermi-gas model prediction χ = 4/3
- Lχ values of 50–70 MeV for most interactions produce good agreement; SkI3 and LNS give Lχ > 70 MeV and show poor reproduction of microscopic results
- The macroscopic model reproduces microscopic results up to asymmetry parameter δ ∼ 0.2

## Context

This work extends the compressible droplet model of Iida (2004) by adding surface diffuseness corrections between neutrons and protons, following the approach of Warda (2009) for surface width effects. The study enables a new way to constrain the equation of state parameters of asymmetric nuclear matter, particularly the slope parameter L of the symmetry energy, by using the density dependence of surface tension χ as a bridge between macroscopic thermodynamics and microscopic mean-field calculations. The results suggest that Skyrme interactions can be tested for thermodynamic consistency, complementing the selection criteria of Brown (2013) based on neutron-skin thickness of doubly magic nuclei.
