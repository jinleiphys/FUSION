# Neutron Star Radii from Laboratory Experiments

> Our present knowledge of the nuclear equation of state is briefly reviewed in this article intended for a wider readership. Particular emphasis is given to the asymmetric-matter equation of state required for modeling neutron stars, neutron-star mergers, and r-process nucleosynthesis. Recent analyses based on combining information obtained from nuclear theory, heavy-ion collisions and astrophysical observations confine the obtained radii of the canonical 1.4-solar-mass neutron star to values between 12 km and 13 km. The remaining uncertainty is primarily related to missing information in the density interval between nuclear saturation density and about twice that value which, however, is accessible with laboratory experiments.

## Key claim

The paper reviews how combining nuclear theory, heavy-ion collision data, and astrophysical observations constrains the radius of a canonical 1.4-solar-mass neutron star to between 12 km and 13 km, but notes that the remaining uncertainty is primarily due to missing information in the density interval between nuclear saturation density and about twice that value, which is accessible with laboratory experiments.

## Method

The authors review the current state of knowledge of the nuclear equation of state by synthesizing results from three domains: nuclear structure studies of ground-state properties and excitations, heavy-ion collision experiments measuring collective flows and meson production, and astrophysical observations including gravitational wave signals from GW170817 and pulse profile measurements from NICER. They describe how Bayesian inference methods are used to combine constraints from these different sources, with particular emphasis on the symmetry energy and its density dependence as characterized by the slope parameter L.

## Key numbers

- Radius of canonical 1.4-solar-mass neutron star: R1.4 = 12.6 ± 1.1 km (90% confidence limit) from Legred et al. (2021)
- Radius R1.4 = 12.45 ± 0.65 km (68% credibility) from Miller et al. (2021) including lower-density information
- Nuclear saturation density: ρ0 = 2.7 × 10^14 g cm^-3, corresponding to nucleon number density n_sat = 0.16 fm^-3
- Symmetry energy at density 0.10 fm^-3: E_sym(0.10 fm^-3) = 24.8 ± 0.5 MeV from χ^2 analysis of nuclear structure data
- Weighted averages from Oertel et al. (2017) compilation of 53 results: E_sym(ρ0) = 31.7 ± 3.2 MeV and L = 58.7 ± 28.1 MeV
- Neutron skin thickness of 208Pb from PREX-2: ΔR_np = 0.28 ± 0.07 fm, leading to E_sym(ρ0) = 38.1 ± 4.7 MeV (Reed et al., 2021)
- Neutron skin thickness of 48Ca from CREX: ΔR_np = 0.121 ± 0.026 (exp) ± 0.024 (model) fm
- Nuclear incompressibility from Le Fevre et al. (2016): K0 = 190 ± 30 MeV
- Pressure at 2ρ0 from heavy-ion collisions: 7.5–13 MeV fm^-3
- Constraint on slope parameter from pion spectral ratios: 42 MeV < L < 117 MeV (Estee et al., 2021)

## Context

This review extends the synthesis approach of earlier compilations such as Oertel et al. (2017) and builds on the multi-messenger era opened by the GW170817 neutron star merger observation. The paper highlights the tension between the PREX-2 measurement of the 208Pb neutron skin (implying a stiff symmetry energy) and the CREX measurement for 48Ca (implying a softer symmetry energy), which remains unresolved. The work points to the need for new laboratory experiments probing the density range between saturation and twice saturation, particularly through heavy-ion collision studies of elliptic flow ratios and pion production, as well as proposed measurements like MREX at Mainz.
