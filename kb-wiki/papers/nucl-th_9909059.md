# Role of nucleon resonance excitation in $ϕ$ meson photoproduction

> The resonance effects are investigated in the $ϕ$ meson photoproduction near threshold through a quark model approach with an effective Lagrangian. The diffractive contribution is consistently estimated by the {\it t}-channel Pomeron exchange. Another non-diffractive process, {\it t}-channel $π^0$ exchange is also included. The numerical result shows that the Pomeron exchange plays dominant role in the $ϕ$ meson photoproduction, while the cross sections of the non-diffractive processes, i.e. {\it s}- and {\it u}-channel excitations, and {\it t}-channel $π^0$ exchange, are quite small. In the polarization observables, we find that large asymmetries are produced in the backward direction by the interferences from the {\it s}- and {\it u}-channel resonances, while in the forward direction, only very small asymmetries are generated. Meanwhile, we find that the effects from the $π^0$ exchange are generally negligible.

# Role of nucleon resonance excitation in φ meson photoproduction

## Key claim

The authors find that while Pomeron exchange dominates the cross section for φ meson photoproduction near threshold, the s- and u-channel nucleon resonance excitations produce large asymmetries in polarization observables at backward angles, even though their contribution to the cross section is small. The paper hedges that these results are obtained in the SU(6)⊗O(3) symmetry limit of the non-relativistic constituent quark model, and that configuration mixings (which are expected at threshold energies) could significantly alter the predictions at large angles.

## Method

The authors model γp → φp using a t-channel Pomeron exchange (treated as a C=+1 isoscalar photon) for the diffractive contribution, plus t-channel π⁰ exchange and s- and u-channel nucleon resonance excitations described via an effective quark-meson Lagrangian with two parameters (a and b). The intermediate baryon states are treated in the non-relativistic constituent quark model with SU(6)⊗O(3) symmetry, including eight low-lying nucleon resonances explicitly (S₁₁(1535), D₁₃(1520), P₁₃(1720), F₁₅(1680), P₁₁(1440), P₁₁(1710), P₁₃(1900), F₁₅(2000)) and treating higher states as degenerate in the harmonic oscillator basis.

## Key numbers

- The Pomeron-constituent-quark coupling is determined as β₀ = 1.27 GeV⁻¹ from data at Eγ = 6.45 GeV
- The quark-meson coupling parameters from fitting sparse data give a = −0.035 ± 0.166 and b′ ≡ b−a = −0.338 ± 0.075; the authors fix |a| = 0.15 and |b′| = 0.3 for calculations
- The π⁰ exchange couplings used are g²πNN/4π = 14 and g²φπγ = 0.143
- The cut-off energy scale for the Pomeron-off-shell-quark vertex is μ₀ = 1.2 GeV
- The Pomeron trajectory slope is α′ = 0.25 GeV⁻²
- At Eγ = 2.0 GeV, the beam polarization asymmetry increases by about a factor of 3 around 110° when resonance contributions are included
- The beam-target double polarization asymmetry reaches approximately −0.7 at 180° in the full calculation with a = −0.15 and b′ = +0.3
- The pure Pomeron exchange gives a beam-target asymmetry of about −0.4 at large angles

## Context

This work extends the study of φ meson photoproduction beyond the well-established diffractive (Pomeron exchange) regime at low momentum transfer and high energies, motivated by new facilities (JLAB, ELSA, GRAAL, SPring-8) enabling measurements at low energy (Eγ ∼ 2 GeV) and/or high momentum transfer. The quark model approach avoids the large number of unknown φNN* coupling parameters that would appear in a hadronic-level treatment. The authors note that a strangeness knock-out model by Titov suggested that a small ss̄ component (∼5%) in the proton might produce large asymmetries (25–45%) in the beam-target observable at small angles, and the present work shows that resonance contributions alone produce only small asymmetries at forward angles, making the forward region a potential probe for such strangeness content. The authors also reference their own submitted work (NPA99) for detailed treatment of configuration mixing effects and Pomeron gauge invariance.
