# Shell Structures and Chaos in Deformed Nuclei and Large Metallic Clusters

> A reflection-asymmetric deformed oscillator potential is analysed from the classical and quantum mechanical point of view. The connection between occurrence of shell structures and classical periodic orbits is studied using the ''removal of resonances method'' in a classical analysis. In this approximation, the effective single particle potential becomes separable and the frequencies of the classical trajectories are easily determined. It turns out that the winding numbers calculated in this way are in good agreement with the ones found from the corresponding quantum mechanical spectrum using the particle number dependence of the fluctuating part of the total energy. When the octupole term is switched on it is found that prolate shapes are stable against chaos whereas spherical and oblate cases become chaotic. An attempt is made to explain this difference in the quantum mechanical context by looking at the distribution of exceptional points which results from the matrix structure of the respective Hamiltonians. In a similar way we analyse the modified Nilsson model and discuss its consequences for nuclei and metallic clusters.

# Shell Structures and Chaos in Deformed Nuclei and Large Metallic Clusters

## Key claim

The paper claims that in a reflection-asymmetric deformed oscillator potential, prolate shapes remain quasi-integrable and exhibit shell structure when an octupole term is added, whereas spherical and oblate shapes become chaotic; this classical result is used to explain quantum shell structures, but the authors note that the spectrum alone cannot distinguish between an additional octupole deformation and a pure but larger quadrupole deformation.

## Method

The authors analyzed a reflection-asymmetric axially deformed oscillator potential (including quadrupole and octupole terms) using both classical and quantum mechanical approaches. Classically, they applied the "removal of resonances" method from secular perturbation theory to average the Hamiltonian over fast phases, reducing the problem to an effectively separable form for prolate deformations. Quantum mechanically, they diagonalized the Hamiltonian in a basis of asymptotic quantum numbers (n⊥, nz, Λ) and extracted the fluctuating part of the total energy as a function of particle number N and octupole strength λ, comparing winding numbers from classical periodic orbits with Fourier transforms of the quantum level density.

## Key numbers

- For the superdeformed case (b=2), the winding number becomes 5:2 at λ = 0.66 λ_crit and 3:1 at λ = 0.8 λ_crit, where λ_crit is the critical octupole strength at which the potential becomes unbounded.
- The quantum shell structures occur at approximately λ/λ_crit = 0.63 and λ/λ_crit = 0.76, with an error of only 5% compared to the classical predictions.
- For the prolate case, the winding number formula ω_ρ/ω_z = (b/2)(1/√(1+λ/λ_crit) + 1/√(1-λ/λ_crit)) applies for b ≥ 1.5.
- The special oblate case b ≈ 0.58 requires the smallest octupole strength to become chaotic.
- For the modified Nilsson model with v_ll = 0.07, chaotic behavior is expected for N ≥ 3 when b=2, while for b=1.05 the onset occurs only as high as N ≥ 20.

## Context

This work extends the periodic orbit theory of Gutzwiller (1971) and the semiclassical approach of Balian and Bloch (1972) to deformed nuclei and metallic clusters, building on earlier findings by Heiss (1994) that prolate octupole-deformed systems exhibit quantum shell structure. The authors connect their results to the modified Nilsson model used for deformed nuclei and to the work of Knight (1984) on metallic cluster abundance spectra. The paper enables further investigation of supershell effects and provides a classical foundation for distinguishing orderly from chaotic motion in many-body systems, while noting that additional experimental information beyond the spectrum is needed to distinguish octupole deformation from enhanced quadrupole deformation.
