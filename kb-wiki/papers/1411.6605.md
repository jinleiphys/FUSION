# Probing superfast quarks in nuclei through dijet production at the LHC

> We investigate dijet production from proton-nucleus collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) as a means for observing superfast quarks in nuclei with Bjorken $x>1$. Kinematically, superfast quarks can be identified through directly measurable jet kinematics. Dynamically, their description requires understanding several elusive properties of nuclear QCD, such as nuclear forces at very short distances, as well as medium modification of parton distributions in nuclei. In the present work, we develop a model for nuclear parton distributions at large $x$ in which the nuclear dynamics at short distance scales are described by two- and three-nucleon short range correlations (SRCs). Nuclear modifications are accounted for using the color screening model, and an improved description of the EMC effect is reached by using a structure function parametrization that includes higher-twist contributions. We apply QCD evolution at the leading order to obtain nuclear parton distributions in the kinematic regime of the LHC, and based on the obtained distributions calculate the cross section for dijet production. We find not only that superfast quarks can be observed at the LHC, but also that they provide sensitivity to the practically unexplored three-nucleon SRCs in nuclei. Additionally, the LHC can extend our knowledge of the EMC effect to large $Q^2$ where higher-twist effects are negligible.

## Key claim

Dijet production in proton-nucleus collisions at the LHC can probe superfast quarks (Bjorken x > 1) and provide sensitivity to three-nucleon short range correlations, provided that the transverse jet momenta and pseudorapidities are selected to isolate scattering off these high-momentum partons, and that the nuclear parton distributions are modeled using two- and three-nucleon SRCs with color screening medium modifications.

## Method

The authors develop a model for nuclear parton distribution functions at large x by decomposing the nuclear light cone fraction distribution into mean field, two-nucleon SRC, and three-nucleon SRC contributions, using the deuteron momentum distribution scaled by the experimentally determined factor a2(A) for two-nucleon SRCs. Nuclear medium modifications are incorporated via the color screening model with higher-twist contributions to describe the EMC effect, and the nuclear PDFs are evolved at leading-order QCD to LHC kinematics. The differential cross section for dijet production in pA collisions is then calculated using the standard factorization formula with partonic scattering amplitudes at leading order, relating the jet rapidities and transverse momentum to the parton momentum fractions x_p and x_A.

## Key numbers

- For lead-proton collisions at 4 TeV beam energy per proton, the average center-of-mass energy per nucleon is sqrt(s_NN^avg) ≈ 5.02 TeV.
- The scaling factor a2(A) for two-nucleon SRCs is approximately 5.6 for heavy nuclei such as iron-56; the authors use a2(208Pb) = 5.6.
- The typical Fermi momentum of heavy nuclei is around 250 MeV/c.
- Two-nucleon SRCs dominate the momentum distribution from k_F up to approximately 650 MeV/c.
- The mean field momentum distribution strength above k_F is less than 1% in magnitude.
- Approximately 25% of nucleons in a heavy nucleus are in a two-nucleon SRC.

## Context

This work extends prior theoretical efforts on superfast quarks in nuclei (Frankfurt 1988, Sargsian 2002) and proposes a new experimental channel, dijet production at the LHC, as an alternative to deep inelastic scattering at Jefferson Lab (Dudek 2012, Arrington 2006) and earlier contradictory measurements at CERN (Benvenuti 1994) and FNAL (Vakili 1999). The model builds on established studies of two-nucleon SRCs from quasi-elastic scattering experiments (Frankfurt 1993, Egiyan 2003, 2005, Fomin 2011, Arrington 2012) and uses the color screening model to describe the EMC effect. The approach enables probing three-nucleon SRCs, which have remained practically unexplored, and allows study of the EMC effect at large Q^2 where higher-twist contributions become negligible.
