# Right-left asymmetry of radiation from fission induced by polarised neutrons

> The effect of the right-left asymmetry is considered in the angular distribution of gamma quanta from fission of U-235 by polarised thermal neutrons, which depends on the polarisation of the neutrons with respect to the gamma-fission plane. Electric dipole radiation from fission fragments arising due to the Strutinsky-Denisov induced polarisation mechanism may give rise to such an effect. Earlier, this mechanism was shown to fit the non-statistical part observed in the gamma spectrum from spontaneous fission of Cf-252. The calculated value of the magnitude of the asymmetry parameter is on the level of 10$^{-4}$. That is in agreement with the current experimental data. A crucial experiment to give a more definite picture of the concrete mechanism would be determination of the energy of the quanta responsible for the asymmetry. Detection of the quanta with the energy of ~5 MeV approaching the giant dipole resonance is needed in order to identify prompt gamma rays emitted at the stage of fissioning.

## Key claim

The right-left asymmetry in gamma emission from fission of U-235 by polarized thermal neutrons can be explained by the Strutinsky-Denisov induced polarization mechanism, where electric dipole radiation from rotating fission fragments produces the effect. The calculated asymmetry parameter of about 10$^{-4}$ agrees with current experimental data, but the mechanism applies specifically to prompt gamma rays emitted within approximately 10$^{-19}$ s after scission, not to the bulk of gamma radiation emitted later from fully accelerated fragments.

## Method

The author calculates the electric dipole radiation arising from post-rupture oscillations of fission fragments using the Strutinsky-Denisov mechanism, where the pear-like shape of fragments (described by quadrupole and octupole deformations) induces a polarization dipole moment. The angular distribution of this dipole radiation is transformed from the intrinsic fragment frame to the laboratory frame, accounting for rotation of the fragment symmetry axis with angular velocity $\omega$ and dissipation with lifetime $\tau_{dis} \approx 10^{-19}$ s. The asymmetry parameter $R$ is then derived as a function of the observation angle and compared to experimental data from Danilyan et al.

## Key numbers

- Asymmetry parameter calculated value: on the level of 10$^{-4}$, in agreement with experimental values $R^{exp}(35^\circ) = (1.5 \pm 0.4) \times 10^{-4}$, $R^{exp}(57^\circ) = (2.3 \pm 0.4) \times 10^{-4}$, and $R^{exp}(90^\circ) = (-0.2 \pm 0.6) \times 10^{-4}$ for U-235 fission by 90% longitudinally polarized thermal neutrons.
- Number of electric dipole quanta per fission from the induced polarization mechanism: $N_d = 0.014$ fission$^{-1}$, calculated with resonance frequency $\hbar\omega_0 \approx 5$ MeV and dissipation lifetime $\tau_{dis} \approx 10^{-19}$ s.
- Anisotropy parameter $a \approx 0.0013$ derived from $N_d = 0.014$ fission$^{-1}$ and total gamma multiplicity $N_\gamma \approx 8$ fission$^{-1}$.
- For gamma quanta with energy $E_\gamma > 2.5$ MeV (approximately 0.3 quanta per fission), the predicted asymmetry parameter $R$ reaches the level of 10$^{-2}$.
- Bremsstrahlung energy from fragments estimated as $I_\gamma = 2.0 \times 10^{-5}$ MeV, yielding $10^{-5}$ to $2 \times 10^{-4}$ bremsstrahlung quanta per fission, too low to produce a noticeable asymmetry effect.
- Fragment rotation angular velocity estimated as $\omega \approx 2.1 \times 10^{19}$ s$^{-1}$ for spin $I = 1$, giving $\cos 2\delta \approx 0.24$ with $\gamma \approx 10^{19}$ s$^{-1}$.

## Context

This work extends the Strutinsky-Denisov induced polarization mechanism, previously shown to fit the non-statistical part of the gamma spectrum from spontaneous fission of Cf-252 (Ploeg et al.), to explain the right-left asymmetry observed by Danilyan et al. in U-235 fission induced by polarized thermal neutrons. The mechanism differs from the ROT effect observed in ternary fission (where rotation of the fission axis after light charged particle emission causes asymmetry) because gamma emission occurs from rotating fragments rather than from rotation of the inter-fragment axis. The paper predicts that detecting hard gamma quanta with energy around 5 MeV (approaching the giant dipole resonance) would provide a crucial test of the mechanism and enable direct study of the early-stage fission dynamics, specifically the snapping-back of the nuclear surface and dissipation in large-amplitude collective motion.
