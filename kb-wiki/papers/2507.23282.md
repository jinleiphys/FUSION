# Mapped $spdf$ interacting boson model for quadrupole-octupole collective states in nuclei

> Dipole bosons are introduced in the interacting boson model (IBM) by means of the self-consistent mean-field method. The constrained mean-field calculations employing a given nuclear energy density functional yield the potential energy surfaces in terms of the axially-symmetric quadrupole-octupole, dipole-quadrupole, and dipole-octupole deformations. By mapping these energy surfaces onto the expectation values of the IBM Hamiltonian in the coherent state of the interacting $s$, $p$, $d$, and $f$ bosons, strength parameters of the $spdf$-IBM Hamiltonian are determined. In an illustrative application to octupole-deformed actinides $^{218-230}$Ra and $^{220-232}$Th, it is shown that effects of including $p$ bosons in the IBM mapping are to lower significantly negative-parity yrast levels, and to improve descriptions of observed energy-level systematic in nearly spherical and transitional nuclei, and of the behaviors of the reduced electric dipole transitions and intrinsic dipole moments with neutron number.

## Key claim
Including dipole ($p$) bosons in the mapped interacting boson model (IBM) significantly lowers calculated negative-parity yrast levels and improves descriptions of energy-level systematics and electric dipole transition properties in nearly spherical and transitional actinide nuclei, but the improvement is conditional on the specific parametrization of the dipole-dipole interaction and the mapping procedure that reproduces the softness of the potential energy surfaces in the dipole deformation.

## Method
The author performed constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) self-consistent mean-field calculations using the Gogny-D1M energy density functional to generate potential energy surfaces (PESs) in terms of axial quadrupole ($\beta_2$), octupole ($\beta_3$), and dipole ($\beta_1$) deformations for $^{218-230}$Ra and $^{220-232}$Th. These fermionic PESs were then mapped onto the expectation values of an $spdf$-IBM Hamiltonian in the coherent state basis, determining the Hamiltonian's strength parameters. The resulting $spdf$-IBM Hamiltonian was numerically diagonalized to compute excitation energies and electric transition probabilities, with results compared to both experimental data and simpler $sdf$-IBM calculations from Nomura (2020).

## Key numbers
- The $p$-boson energy $\epsilon_p$ is systematically lower in magnitude than the $f$-boson energy and is of the same order as or lower than the $d$-boson energy for many nuclei; for example, in $^{218}$Ra, $\epsilon_p = -701$ keV while $\epsilon_f = -672$ keV and $\epsilon_d = 363$ keV.
- The $\chi_{pp}$ parameter varies substantially from $N=132$ to $136$, with values up to 1.50 (e.g., $^{218}$Ra and $^{220}$Th), indicating dipole correlations can be as pronounced as octupole correlations in transitional nuclei.
- The $E1$ boson effective charge is fixed at $e_1 = 0.0054$ $e$b$^{1/2}$ for the $spdf$-boson model, while a constant value $e_1 = 0.008$ $e$b is used in the $sdf$-boson model.
- The $E2$ and $E3$ effective charges are determined per nucleus using overall factors $e_2^{(0)} = 0.12$ $e$b and $e_3^{(0)} = 0.06$ $e$b$^{3/2}$ to match experimental $B(E2;2^+_1 \to 0^+_1)$ and $B(E3;3^-_1 \to 0^+_1)$ orders of magnitude.

## Context
This work extends the mapped IBM framework developed by Nomura and collaborators (2013, 2014, 2020) by introducing dipole ($p$) bosons into the model space, which were previously omitted in the $sdf$-IBM approach. The study addresses a known deficiency of the $sdf$-IBM in reproducing electric dipole ($E1$) transition properties, which had been attributed to missing boson degrees of freedom. The inclusion of $p$ bosons builds on earlier theoretical work by Engel (1985, 1987) and Kusnezov (1989, 1990) on the $spdf$-IBM group structure, and on the demonstration by Otsuka (1986, 1988) that dipole pairs are essential building blocks in deformed actinides. The results enable more accurate calculations of negative-parity states and $E1$ transitions in octupole-deformed nuclei, and provide a foundation for future studies of reflection-asymmetric nuclear shapes and their impact on fundamental symmetry violations such as atomic electric dipole moments.
