# Quantum Optics and Heavy Ion Physics

## Key claim

The paper argues that the diffraction theory approach to nuclear scattering, originally developed for small momentum transfer processes, provides a geometrical substrate that can be extended to heavy ion collisions through a "used nucleon model" that interpolates between the wounded nucleon model and the total collision counting model. Additionally, the author suggests that coherent states, familiar from quantum optics, may be present in the pion output of heavy ion collisions despite the apparently chaotic nature of the collisions.

## Method

The author derives a generalized multiplicity formula for particle production in nuclear collisions by introducing a parameter μ that represents the efficiency of successive collisions, where the first collision produces one unit, the second produces μ, the third produces μ², and so on. This is applied to a single projectile colliding with a nucleus of A constituents, using the binomial probability distribution for n inelastic collisions, and then extended to the case of two colliding nuclei. Separately, the author reviews the Hanbury Brown-Twiss intensity interferometry experiment and the quantum optical formalism of field correlation functions, drawing parallels to the statistical analysis of boson emission in heavy ion collisions.

## Key numbers

- 19.3 GeV/c protons incident on Cu and Pb produced diffraction patterns for elastic scattering that were well described by the diffraction theory, as measured by the Cocconi group at CERN
- 180 MeV deuterons from the Berkeley 184" cyclotron produced forward-travelling 90 MeV neutrons via the stripping process explained by Serber
- The average multiplicity formula for the "used nucleon model" reduces to the wounded nucleon model when μ = 0, and to the average number of collisions (Ax) when μ → 1
- For a coherent state, the photon count distribution is Poissonian: p(n) = |β|²ⁿ e^{-|β|²} / n!
- For pure noise (Gaussian amplitude distribution), the photon count distribution is geometric: p(n) = (1/(1+⟨n⟩)) (⟨n⟩/(1+⟨n⟩))ⁿ
- The Hanbury Brown-Twiss experiment with visible light showed a correlation effect that, with very high resolving power in counters, approaches an increase of a factor of two in coincidence rates for very short time delays

## Context

This paper extends the author's own earlier work on nuclear diffraction theory (Glauber, 1950s) and quantum optical coherence (Glauber, 1963), connecting these to heavy ion physics. The diffraction theory approach is contrasted with the optical model of the nucleus and is shown to successfully describe elastic and inelastic scattering data from Bellettini et al. at CERN. The "used nucleon model" generalizes the wounded nucleon model of Bialas, Bleszinski, and Czyz (1976) and the total collision counting approach used by RHIC collaborations. The quantum optical analysis of photon statistics, including the Hanbury Brown-Twiss effect (Hanbury Brown and Twiss, 1956) and its interpretation by Purcell, is applied to suggest that coherent states may survive in heavy ion pion production, potentially explaining why measured correlation functions for like pions do not rise to the value 2 for vanishing relative momenta.
