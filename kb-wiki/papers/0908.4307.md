# Theory and Applications of Coulomb Excitation

> Because the interaction is well-known, Coulomb excitation is one of the best tools for the investigation of nuclear properties. In the last 3 decades new reaction theories for Coulomb excitation have been developed such as: (a) relativistic Coulomb excitation, (b) Coulomb excitation at intermediate energies, and (c) multistep coupling in the continuum. These developments are timely with the advent of rare isotope facilities. Of special interest is the Coulomb excitation and dissociation of weakly-bound systems. I review the Coulomb excitation theory, from low to relativistic collision energies. Several applications of the theory to situations of interest in nuclear physics and nuclear astrophysics are discussed.

## Key claim

Coulomb excitation is one of the best tools for investigating nuclear properties because the electromagnetic interaction is well-known, but the semiclassical approximation is valid only when the Sommerfeld parameter is large (eta >> 1) and the feedback from intrinsic excitations to relative motion can be neglected; at high energies a quantum treatment using the eikonal approximation may be necessary for some observables such as angular distributions.

## Method

The paper reviews the semiclassical theory of Coulomb excitation from low to relativistic collision energies, where the projectile follows a classical Rutherford trajectory and the time-dependent electromagnetic field induces nuclear transitions. The interaction Hamiltonian is expressed in terms of electric multipole moments of the target nucleus, and transition amplitudes are computed as Fourier components of the time-dependent interaction along the unperturbed trajectory. The formalism is extended to include relativistic Coulomb excitation, intermediate-energy collisions, and multistep coupling in the continuum, with applications to weakly-bound systems and nuclear astrophysics.

## Key numbers

- The Sommerfeld parameter is defined as eta = Z1 Z2 e^2 / (hbar v); for Z1 Z2 > 137 or v << c, eta can easily reach values eta >> 1.
- The classical distance of closest approach is a = 2 a0 = 2 Z1 Z2 e^2 / (m v^2), and the Coulomb barrier energy is E_B = Z1 Z2 e^2 / (R1 + R2).
- The adiabaticity parameter is zeta = omega a0 / v; when zeta > 1 transition probabilities are small.
- For a typical nucleus of intermediate mass A = 120, the ratio of electric to magnetic disintegration constants lambda_E / lambda_M is approximately 100, independent of multipole order L.
- The condition for long-wavelength radiation in nuclei is kappa R << 1, equivalent to hbar omega << 165 A^{-1/3} MeV, which is usually fulfilled.
- For a frontal collision at theta = 180 degrees exciting a J=0 to J=2 transition, the differential cross section is d sigma / d Omega = m0 E |Q_if|^2 / (18 hbar^2 Z_T^2), independent of projectile charge.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Alder and Winther (1975) on low-energy Coulomb excitation and the earlier comprehensive review by Alder et al. (1956), incorporating developments from the last three decades including relativistic Coulomb excitation, intermediate-energy collisions, and multistep coupling in the continuum. The theoretical framework is timely with the advent of rare isotope facilities and enables the study of weakly-bound systems and nuclear astrophysics reactions through Coulomb dissociation. The paper focuses on the electromagnetic interaction while discussing nuclear structure models only schematically, mainly for collective properties such as giant and pigmy resonances.
