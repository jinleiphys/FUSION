# Derivation of Covariant Dissipative Fluid Dynamics in the Renormalization-group Method

> We derive generic relativistic hydrodynamical equations with dissipative effects from the underlying Boltzmann equation in a mechanical and systematic way on the basis of so called the renormalization-group (RG) method. A macroscopic frame vector is introduced to specify the frame on which the macroscopic dynamics is described. Our method is so mechanical with only few ansatz that our method give a microscopic foundation of the available hydrodynamical equations, and also can be applied to make a reduction of the kinetic equations other than the simple Boltzmann equation.

## Key claim

The authors derive relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics from the Boltzmann equation using the renormalization-group (RG) method, claiming that the resulting equations depend on the choice of a macroscopic frame vector and that the constraints by Stewart (rather than Eckart) must be taken when the particle-flow frame is chosen, with the resulting equation differing from Stewart's original proposal.

## Method

The authors apply the RG method to the relativistic Boltzmann equation for a classical system of identical particles with conserved particle number, introducing a macroscopic frame vector to define a covariant coordinate system that separates fast and slow dynamics. They perform a perturbative expansion in a small parameter epsilon (identified with the ratio of average particle distance to mean free path), solve the zeroth-order and first-order equations, and use the envelope method to eliminate secular terms, obtaining hydrodynamic equations for the five slow variables (temperature, chemical potential, and flow velocity). The dissipative currents are then expressed in terms of thermodynamic forces, and transport coefficients are given in Kubo-like formulas.

## Key numbers

- The transport coefficients are given by Kubo formulas: bulk viscosity zeta = (1/T) * integral_0^infinity ds <Pi(0), Pi(s)>_eq, heat conductivity lambda = -(1/3T^2) * integral_0^infinity ds <Q^mu(0), Q_mu(s)>_eq, and shear viscosity eta = (1/10T) * integral_0^infinity ds <Pi^{munu}(0), Pi_{munu}(s)>_eq, where the inner product uses the equilibrium distribution function.
- The reduced mass is defined as z = m/T, with particle density n = (2pi)^{-3} * 4pi * m^3 * exp(mu/T) * {z^{-1} * K_2(z)} and energy density epsilon = m * n * {K_3(z)/K_2(z) - z^{-1}}, where K_2 and K_3 are modified Bessel functions.
- The ratio of heat capacities is gamma = 1 + {z^2 + 3 * hat{h} - (hat{h} - 1)^2}^{-1}, with reduced enthalpy per particle hat{h} = (epsilon + n*T)/(n*T).

## Context

This work extends the RG method previously applied to the non-relativistic Boltzmann equation (Env007, Env008) to the relativistic case, providing a microscopic foundation for the phenomenological dissipative fluid dynamics of Eckart (Hen001), Landau (Hen002), and Israel-Stewart (Mic004). The authors address shortcomings of the Chapman-Enskog method (Chapman) and Grad's fourteen-moment method (Grad), which they argue contain ad-hoc assumptions, and also improve upon van Kampen's reduction method (Mic005) which was not covariant. The results enable systematic derivation of hydrodynamic equations with microscopic transport coefficients and natural initial conditions for applications in relativistic heavy-ion collisions and astrophysics.
