# Global prediction of nuclear charge density distributions using deep neural network

> A deep neural network (DNN) has been developed to generate the distributions of nuclear charge density, utilizing the training data from the relativistic density functional theory and incorporating available experimental charge radii of 1014 nuclei into the loss function. The DNN achieved a root-mean-square (rms) deviation of 0.0193 fm for charge radii on its validation set. Furthermore, the DNN can improve the description in both the tail and central regions of the charge density, enhancing agreement with experimental findings. The model's predictive capability has been further validated by its agreement with recent experimental data on charge radii. Finally, this refined model is employed to predict the charge density distributions in a wider range of nuclide chart, and the parameterized charge densities, charge radii, and higher-order moments of charge density distributions are given, providing a robust reference for future experimental investigations.

# Global prediction of nuclear charge density distributions using deep neural network

## Key claim

A deep neural network trained on relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) charge densities and incorporating experimental charge radii of 1014 nuclei into the loss function can produce charge density distributions with improved accuracy in both the central and tail regions, but the model fails to reproduce certain anomalous features such as the kink structure in Ca isotopes and the sharp decline in charge radii near Z=80.

## Method

The authors constructed a six-layer fully connected neural network (input layer, four hidden layers with 20, 100, 100, and 20 neurons using Tanh activation, and an output layer of 17 neurons) to predict Fourier-Bessel (FB) coefficients of nuclear charge density distributions. Training used RCHB calculations with the PC-PK1 functional as the target, with a two-step procedure: first learning the FB coefficients via mean square error loss, then incorporating experimental charge radii into the loss function with a weight parameter lambda=0.7. The dataset of 1014 nuclei was split 8:2 into training and validation sets.

## Key numbers

- Root-mean-square deviation of 0.0193 fm for charge radii on the validation set (DNN), compared to 0.0430 fm for RCHB theory
- Root-mean-square deviation of 0.0183 fm for charge radii on the training set (DNN)
- For newly measured charge radii on four isotopic chains (Z=28, 46, 80, 83), DNN achieved an rms deviation of 0.0273 fm versus 0.0374 fm for RCHB
- The error between proton numbers calculated from the FB normalization and actual values was about 0.2%
- Learning rate of 5 x 10^{-3} for the first training step and 5 x 10^{-4} for the second step
- Training converged within 10,000 epochs, taking approximately one hour on an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3070 Ti

## Context

This work extends machine learning approaches to nuclear charge density distributions, building on prior studies using artificial neural networks (Akkoyun 2013), Bayesian neural networks (Utama 2016, Dong 2022), convolutional neural networks (Su 2023), and kernel ridge regression (Ma 2022) for charge radii predictions. It differs from earlier work by Yang (2021) and Shang (2022) by providing a unified description of both charge density and charge radius across the entire nuclide chart. The model enables predictions for nuclei listed in AME2020 with Z>=8, providing parameterized charge densities, charge radii, and higher-order moments as a reference for future experiments, though the authors note that interpreting neural network results and reproducing anomalous isotopic trends remain open challenges.
