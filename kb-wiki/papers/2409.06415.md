# Simulating continuous-space systems with quantum-classical wave functions

> Most non-relativistic interacting quantum many-body systems, such as atomic and molecular ensembles or materials, are naturally described in terms of continuous-space Hamiltonians. The simulation of their ground-state properties on digital quantum computers is challenging because current algorithms require discretization, which usually amounts to choosing a finite basis set, inevitably introducing errors. In this work, we propose an alternative, discretization-free approach that combines classical and quantum resources in a global variational ansatz, optimized using the framework of variational Monte Carlo. We introduce both purely quantum as well as hybrid quantum-classical ansatze and benchmark them on three paradigmatic continuous-space systems that are either very challenging or beyond the reach of current quantum approaches: the one-dimensional quantum rotor model, a system of Helium-3 particles in one and two dimensions, and the two-dimensional homogeneous electron gas. We embed relevant constraints such as the antisymmetry of fermionic wave functions directly into the ansatz. Many-body correlations are introduced via backflow transformations represented by parameterized quantum circuits. We demonstrate that the accuracy of the simulation can be systematically improved by increasing the number of circuit parameters and study the effects of shot noise. Furthermore, we show that the hybrid ansatz improves the ground-state energies obtained using the purely classical wave function.

## Key claim
The authors propose a discretization-free variational method that combines classical and quantum resources to simulate continuous-space quantum many-body systems, eliminating basis-set truncation errors that plague conventional quantum algorithms. The approach is demonstrated on systems that are "either very challenging or beyond the reach of current quantum approaches," with the caveat that accuracy depends systematically on the number of circuit parameters and that the method is benchmarked using noise-free statevector simulations rather than actual quantum hardware.

## Method
The wave function is parameterized as a product of a classical ansatz (e.g., Jastrow or Slater determinant) and a quantum circuit output, where the log-amplitude or backflow coordinate transformation is given by the expectation value of an observable measured on a parameterized quantum circuit. The ansatz is optimized using variational Monte Carlo with stochastic reconfiguration, where particle configurations are sampled via Markov Chain Monte Carlo and gradients are computed via parameter-shift rules. For fermionic systems, antisymmetry is enforced through a Slater determinant of single-particle orbitals, with many-body correlations introduced via backflow transformations parameterized by quantum circuits.

## Key numbers
- For the 1D quantum rotor model with N=4 rotors, the PQC₂ ansatz with L=8 layers yields relative energy errors comparable to an MPS simulation with local Hilbert space cutoff M=3, while requiring only 4 qubits instead of at least 12 qubits for the discretized approach.
- The PQC₂ ansatz with L=2 layers already outperforms the classical Jastrow wave function for N=4 rotors, and accuracy improves systematically with increasing circuit depth up to L=8 layers.
- The hybrid quantum-classical ansatz (PQC₂ + Jastrow) improves upon the pre-optimized classical Jastrow wave function for N=4 rotors, converging quickly to the exact ground state after 500 iterations of classical pre-optimization followed by quantum optimization.

## Context
This work extends variational Monte Carlo methods (McMillan 1965, Becca 2017) by introducing parameterized quantum circuits as ansatze for continuous-space systems, contrasting with second-quantized hybrid methods (Yuan 2021, Huang 2023) that suffer from basis truncation errors. The approach enables simulation of fermionic systems such as Helium-3 and the homogeneous electron gas, which are beyond the reach of current quantum devices (Shepherd 2012, Su 2021), and provides a pathway to ground-state simulations on near-term hardware by eliminating the need for discretization and expensive basis-set extrapolations.
