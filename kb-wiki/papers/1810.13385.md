# The Statistical Model of Nuclear Reactions: Open Problems

> Several experiments [1-3] show significant deviations from predictions of the statistical model of nuclear reactions. We summarize unsuccessful recent theoretical efforts to account for such disagreement in terms of a violation of orthogonal invariance caused by the Thomas-Ehrman shift. We report on numerical simulations involving a large number of gamma decay channels that also give rise to violation of orthogonal invariance but likewise do not account for the discrepancies. We discuss the statistical model in the light of these results.

# The Statistical Model of Nuclear Reactions: Open Problems

## Key claim

The statistical model of nuclear reactions fails to account for experimental deviations in neutron resonance data from platinum isotopes, the nuclear data ensemble, and ⁹⁶Mo, and violations of orthogonal invariance due to channel coupling, whether from the Thomas-Ehrman shift or from many gamma channels, cannot explain these discrepancies.

## Method

The authors performed numerical simulations of compound-nucleus reactions using a Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE) Hamiltonian coupled to one neutron channel and 400 gamma channels (200 E1 and 200 M1), constructed by coarse-graining final states for ⁹⁶Mo resonances with spin/parity values 2⁺, 3⁺, 1⁻, 2⁻, 3⁻, and 4⁻. They compared the resulting distributions of partial neutron widths, partial gamma widths, and total gamma decay widths against the Porter-Thomas distribution (PTD) and against experimental data from Koehler (2013). They also tested a model with unrealistically strong neutron channel coupling to see if modified partial-width distributions could reproduce the experimental total-width distributions.

## Key numbers

- The data for ¹⁹²Pt and ¹⁹⁴Pt reject the validity of the Porter-Thomas distribution with 99.997% statistical significance (158 and 411 isolated resonances analyzed, respectively).
- A reanalysis of the nuclear data ensemble rejects the PTD with 99.97% statistical significance.
- Simulated average total gamma widths for ⁹⁶Mo resonances: 165.5 meV (2⁺), 157.5 meV (3⁺), 191.2 meV (1⁻), 172.8 meV (2⁻), 169.2 meV (3⁻), 153.8 meV (4⁻).
- Experimental average total gamma widths for ⁹⁶Mo resonances: 206 (31) meV (2⁺), 240 (58) meV (3⁺), 670 (225) meV (1⁻), 374 (115) meV (2⁻), 404 (100) meV (3⁻), 361 (106) meV (4⁻).
- The ratio of experimental to simulated average total width for 1⁻ states exceeds three.
- The simulation used 401 total channels: one neutron channel, 200 E1 channels, and 200 M1 channels.
- The coarse-graining threshold for final states was 2.79 MeV excitation energy.

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical efforts by Volya (2015), Bogomolny (2017), and Fanto (2017) who investigated whether the Thomas-Ehrman shift could cause deviations from the PTD; those efforts were unsuccessful for realistic parameters. The paper contradicts the expectation that many weak gamma channels might alter width distributions, showing instead that the central limit theorem forces narrow total-width distributions regardless of the partial-width distribution form. The results point toward the need for additional experimental tests of the statistical model and raise the question of whether the Brink-Axel hypothesis applies to all significant gamma transitions in neutron resonance decay. The authors suggest that a small number of strong gamma transitions to low-lying states might resolve the discrepancies, as previously proposed by Koehler (2013).
