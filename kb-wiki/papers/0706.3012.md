# Unbound exotic nuclei studied by projectile fragmentation

> We call "projectile fragmentation" of neutron halo nuclei the elastic breakup (diffraction) reaction, when the observable studied is the neutron-core relative energy spectrum. This observable has been measured in relation to the Coulomb breakup on heavy target and recently also on light targets. Such data enlighten the effect of the neutron final state interaction with the core of origin. Projectile fragmentation is studied here by a time dependent model for the excitation of a nucleon from a bound state to a continuum resonant state in a neutron-core complex potential which acts as a final state interaction. The final state is described by an optical model S-matrix so that both resonant and non resonant states of any continuum energy can be studied as well as deeply bound initial states. It turns out that due to the coupling between the initial and final states, the neutron-core free particle phase shifts are modified, in the exit channel, by an additional phase. Some typical numerical calculations for the relevant observables are presented and compared to experimental data. It is suggest that the excitation energy spectra of an unbound nucleus might reflect the structure of the parent nucleus from whose fragmentation they are obtained.

## Key claim

The paper suggests that the excitation energy spectra of an unbound nucleus might reflect the structure of the parent nucleus from whose fragmentation they are obtained, but the authors note that uncertainties in the interpretation of experimental results compared to structure calculations motivated their work, and the calculated absolute cross sections underestimate experimental data by a factor of 2 in one case.

## Method

The authors use a time-dependent perturbation model for the excitation of a nucleon from a bound state to a continuum resonant state in a neutron-core complex potential, which acts as a final state interaction. The final state is described by an optical model S-matrix so that both resonant and non-resonant states of any continuum energy can be studied, as well as deeply bound initial states. The projectile-target relative motion is treated semiclassically using a straight-line trajectory with constant velocity, and the core survival probability is parameterized by an S-matrix function of the core-target distance of closest approach.

## Key numbers

- The valence neutron in 11Be is bound by 0.503 MeV, with the wave function mainly a 2s state with spectroscopic factor around 0.8 and a small d5/2 component whose main strength is in the continuum centered around 1.25 MeV
- For the 11Be+12C reaction at 69 A.MeV, the delta-function potential strength was chosen as -4057.59 MeV fm3
- For the 14Be+12C reaction at 250 A.MeV, the calculation underestimates the absolute experimental cross section by a factor of 2
- The strong absorption radius is defined as Rs ≈ 1.4(Ap1/3 + At1/3) fm with a diffuseness parameter a = 0.6 fm
- The spectroscopic factor for the s initial state in 11Be is C2S = 0.84

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for describing elastic breakup (diffraction dissociation) of neutron halo nuclei, building on earlier time-dependent approaches by Bonaccorso and Brink (various years) and the sudden approximation method. The model is tested against experimental data from Fukuda et al. (2004) for 11Be fragmentation on 12C and from Simon et al. for 14Be fragmentation, and the results are compared to calculations by Capel et al. who solved the time-dependent Schrodinger equation numerically on a three-dimensional spherical mesh. The paper enables future studies of the interplay between structure and reaction effects in unbound nuclei, particularly for understanding the position of the neutron dripline.
