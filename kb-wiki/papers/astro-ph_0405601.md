# Cooling of a New Born Compact Star with QCD Phase Transition

> We study the cooling behaviour of an isolated strange quark star, using an equation of state derived from perturbative QCD up to second order in strong coupling constant, and we compare it with that of a neutron star. After an initial rapid cooling, a quark star may undergo the QCD phase transition to become a neutron star. We propose several signatures for such a scenario: a large amount of energy can be released due to latent heat, a long duration $γ$-ray source, and a second neutrino burst after a supernova explosion.

# Cooling of a New Born Compact Star with QCD Phase Transition

## Key claim

The authors propose that a newly formed strange quark star, after an initial rapid cooling phase, may undergo a QCD phase transition to become a neutron star, releasing latent heat of order 10^53 erg. This scenario could produce observable signatures including a long-duration gamma-ray source and a second neutrino burst after a supernova explosion, but the authors note that the results depend on assuming a constant phase transition temperature throughout the star and on the choice of equation of state parameters.

## Method

The authors model the cooling of an isolated strange quark star using an equation of state derived from perturbative QCD up to second order in the strong coupling constant, following the approach of Fraga et al. (2001). They calculate the thermal evolution by solving the heat balance equation for a uniform-temperature quark star, including contributions from pion production, quark URCA processes, electron-positron pair production, and thermal radiation. When the star cools to the assumed phase transition temperature T_p, the star converts to a neutron star, and the subsequent cooling follows standard neutron star thermal evolution with a crust model based on the heat conduction equation.

## Key numbers

- Phase transition temperature T_p is assumed constant at either 1 MeV or 10 MeV for comparison, based on lattice QCD calculations showing T_p drops from 140 MeV at zero density to about 50 MeV at 1.5 times nuclear matter density (rho_0 = 0.17 fm^{-3})
- For the chosen parameter set (Lambda/mu = 2.473), the maximum gravitational mass is 1.516 solar masses with baryonic mass 1.60 solar masses and radius 8.54 km
- Latent heat released during phase transition is of order 10^53 erg, depending only weakly on the nuclear matter equation of state
- Photon luminosity during the phase transition is maintained in the range 10^48 to 10^54 erg/s with duration from 10^{-3} s up to 10^4 s
- Gamma-ray luminosity can reach 10^{38-39} erg/s with duration of approximately 10^{13-14} s
- Initial temperature of the newborn strange star is approximately 40 MeV (about 4 x 10^{11} K)
- For Lambda/mu = 2.473, the conversion energy is typically 10^53 erg
- Strange quark matter is absolutely stable for Lambda >= 2.88 mu, with baryonic number density at zero pressure about 1.52 n_0
- Strange stars are more stable than neutron stars for Lambda greater than about 2.7 mu

## Context

This work extends the study of strange star cooling by including the possibility of a QCD phase transition from quark matter to hadronic matter, building on the Witten (1984) proposal that strange quark matter may be the absolute ground state. The authors compare their results with prior work by Usov (1998, 2001, 2002) on rapid cooling of strange stars via pion and electron-positron pair production, and with Aguilera et al. on second neutrino bursts. The paper enables future observational searches for signatures of quark stars using X-ray detectors such as Chandra and XMM, and gamma-ray observatories like Integral, by predicting specific luminosity and duration ranges for phase transition signals.
