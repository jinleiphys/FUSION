# Recent Progress in Two-proton Radioactivity

> During the last few decades, rare isotope beam facilities have provided unique data for studying the properties of nuclides located far from the beta-stability line. Such nuclei are often accompanied by exotic structures and radioactive modes, which represent the forefront of nuclear research. Among them, two-proton (2p) radioactivity is a rare decay mode found in a few highly proton-rich isotopes. The 2p decay lifetimes and properties of emitted protons hold invaluable information regarding the nuclear structures in the presence of a low-lying proton continuum; as such, they have attracted considerable research attention. In this review, we present some of the recent experimental and theoretical progress regarding the 2p decay, including technical innovations for measuring nucleon--nucleon correlations and developments in the models that connect their structural aspects with their decay properties. This impressive progress should play a significant role in elucidating the mechanism of these exotic decays, probing the corresponding components inside nuclei, and providing deep insights into the open quantum nature of dripline systems.

## Key claim

This review paper surveys recent experimental and theoretical progress in two-proton (2p) radioactivity, but emphasizes that only four long-lived ground-state 2p emitters have been confirmed to date, and the mechanisms and properties of this exotic decay remain largely unknown because the complicated three-body process requires fine-tuned studies for each individual system.

## Method

The authors review experimental techniques including in-flight decay for short-lived emitters (half-lives below picoseconds), implantation decay methods for longer-lived nuclei, and optical time projection chambers (OTPC-CCD) for direct visualization of particle tracks. They also survey theoretical approaches that treat internal nuclear structure and asymptotic regions separately, as well as models connecting structural aspects with decay properties through three-body descriptions of the core-plus-two-proton system.

## Key numbers

- Four long-lived ground-state 2p emitters have been identified: $^{45}$Fe (half-life 4.7$^{+3.4}_{-1.4}$ ms at GANIL, 3.2$^{+2.6}_{-1.0}$ ms at GSI), $^{54}$Zn (half-life 3.7$^{+2.2}_{-1.0}$ ms), $^{48}$Ni (half-life 2.1$^{+1.4}_{-0.4}$ ms), and $^{67}$Kr (half-life 7.4(30) ms, branching ratio 37(14)%)
- $^{67}$Kr has a 2p decay energy Q$_{2p}$ = 1.690(17) MeV, making it the heaviest 2p emitter discovered
- $^{11}$O exhibits 2p decay with a ground-state decay width of 2.31(14) MeV and Q$_{2p}$ = 4.25(6) MeV
- $^{45}$Fe has Q$_{2p}$ = 1.80(20) MeV with half-life 2.45(23) ms
- $^{6}$Be has Q$_{2p}$ = 1.372(5) MeV with decay width 92(6) keV
- $^{19}$Mg has Q$_{2p}$ = 0.76(6) MeV with half-life 4.0(15) ps

## Context

This review extends earlier comprehensive reviews by Blank (2008) and Pfutzner (2012) on two-proton radioactivity, covering developments since those works. It summarizes experimental innovations at facilities including GANIL, GSI, RIKEN, NSCL/MSU, and RIBLL, and discusses theoretical challenges in describing the three-body decay process where the Coulomb barrier strongly suppresses the wave function at large distances. The work enables future searches for additional 2p emitter candidates and deeper understanding of open quantum systems near the proton dripline, with implications for astro-nuclear (2p,gamma) and (gamma,2p) processes relevant to waiting point nuclei in element synthesis cycles.
