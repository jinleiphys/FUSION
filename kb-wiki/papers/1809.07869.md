# Current Status of Very-Large-Basis Hamiltonian Diagonalizations for Nuclear Physics

> Today there are a plethora of many-body techniques for calculating nuclear wave functions and matrix elements. I review the status of that reliable workhorse, the interacting shell model, a.k.a. configuration-interaction methods, a.k.a. Hamiltonian diagonalization, and survey its advantages and disadvantages. With modern supercomputers one can tackle dimensions up to about 20 billion! I discuss how we got there and where we might go in the near future.

## Key claim
The interacting shell model (configuration-interaction method) remains a reliable workhorse for nuclear many-body calculations, capable of handling basis dimensions up to about 20 billion on modern supercomputers, but the paper is a review that surveys advantages and disadvantages rather than presenting a single new result.

## Method
The paper reviews the configuration-interaction (CI) method, also called the interacting shell model, which expands nuclear wave functions in a basis of Slater determinants and solves the eigenvalue equation H c = E c. It categorizes CI calculations into phenomenological (semi-phenomenological with fixed cores and valence spaces) and ab initio (no-core shell model, NCSM) approaches, and discusses basis choices including M-scheme, J-scheme, and symmetry-adapted bases. The review covers convergence and extrapolation strategies for NCSM calculations, including exponential fitting and infrared/ultraviolet parameter methods.

## Key numbers
- Modern calculations find extremal eigenvalues of matrices exceeding dimensions of 2 × 10^10 (20 billion), as demonstrated in PhysRevC.97.034328.
- M-scheme bases can have sparsity as small as ~10^-6 (fraction of nonzero matrix elements).
- Early shell model calculations solved 25 × 25 matrices as the height of computation (PhysRev.105.1563).

## Context
This review extends the long history of shell model calculations from the early work of PhysRev.105.1563 to modern capabilities, and contrasts CI methods with size-extensive approaches such as coupled clusters (Hagen 2010). It enables future work by discussing promising directions including natural orbitals (Constantinou 2017), symmetry-adapted bases using SU(3) (PhysRevLett.111.252501) and Sp(3,R) (McCoy), and novel extrapolation techniques using machine learning (Negoita 2018). The paper is a conference summary with no original numerical results.
