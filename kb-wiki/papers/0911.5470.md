# Nuclear constraints on the core-crust transition density and pressure of neutron stars

> Using the equation of state of asymmetric nuclear matter that has been recently constrained by the isospin diffusion data from intermediate-energy heavy ion collisions, we have studied the transition density and pressure at the inner edge of neutron star crusts, and they are found to be 0.040 fm^{-3} <= ρ_{t}<= 0.065 fm^{-3} and 0.01 MeV/fm^{3} <= P_{t} <= 0.26 MeV/fm^{3}, respectively, in both the dynamical and thermodynamical approaches. We have further found that the widely used parabolic approximation to the equation of state of asymmetric nuclear matter gives significantly higher values of core-crust transition density and pressure, especially for stiff symmetry energies. With these newly determined transition density and pressure, we have obtained an improved relation between the mass and radius of neutron stars based on the observed minimum crustal fraction of the total moment of inertia for Vela pulsar.

## Key claim
Using the equation of state (EOS) of asymmetric nuclear matter constrained by isospin diffusion data from heavy-ion collisions, the core-crust transition density and pressure of neutron stars are found to be \(0.040\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3} \leq \rho_t \leq 0.065\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3}\) and \(0.01\ \mathrm{MeV/fm}^3 \leq P_t \leq 0.26\ \mathrm{MeV/fm}^3\), respectively, in both the dynamical and thermodynamical approaches. The abstract glosses over the fact that these ranges are conditional on the slope parameter \(L\) of the symmetry energy being constrained to \(86 \pm 25\ \mathrm{MeV}\) from the same heavy-ion data, and that the parabolic approximation to the EOS yields significantly higher values, especially for stiff symmetry energies.

## Method
The authors used a momentum-dependent MDI interaction (based on the modified finite-range Gogny effective interaction) to model the EOS of asymmetric nuclear matter, exactly the same interaction used in analyzing isospin diffusion data from heavy-ion reactions. They applied both the dynamical method (stability condition against small periodic density perturbations, including density-gradient and Coulomb terms) and the thermodynamical method (stability conditions on pressure and chemical potential) to locate the transition density \(\rho_t\) and pressure \(P_t\) at the inner edge of neutron star crusts. The results were compared with those from the widely used parabolic approximation \(E(\rho,\delta) = E(\rho,\delta=0) + E_{\mathrm{sym}}(\rho)\delta^2\), and the constrained \(L\) value was used to derive the final ranges.

## Key numbers
- Transition density: \(0.040\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3} \leq \rho_t \leq 0.065\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3}\) (from both dynamical and thermodynamical approaches, using the full MDI EOS constrained by isospin diffusion data).
- Transition pressure: \(0.01\ \mathrm{MeV/fm}^3 \leq P_t \leq 0.26\ \mathrm{MeV/fm}^3\) (from the dynamical approach with the full MDI EOS).
- Slope parameter of symmetry energy: \(L = 86 \pm 25\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (extracted from isospin diffusion data, used to constrain the above ranges).
- For the Vela pulsar, using \(\rho_t = 0.065\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3}\) and \(P_t = 0.26\ \mathrm{MeV/fm}^3\) together with the observed crustal moment-of-inertia fraction \(\Delta I/I > 0.014\), the minimum radius is \(R \geq 4.7 + 4.0 M/M_\odot\ \mathrm{km}\); for a canonical \(1.4 M_\odot\) neutron star, the radius is predicted to exceed \(10.5\ \mathrm{km}\).
- Using the full MDI EOS with \(x=0\) and \(x=-1\) (constrained by heavy-ion experiments), the radius of a \(1.4 M_\odot\) neutron star is tightly constrained between \(11.9\ \mathrm{km}\) and \(13.2\ \mathrm{km}\).

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of neutron star crust-core transitions (e.g., Kubis (2007) using the thermodynamical approach with the parabolic approximation) by incorporating the full EOS of asymmetric nuclear matter constrained by terrestrial isospin diffusion data from heavy-ion collisions (Tsang et al., 2004; Chen et al., 2005; Li et al., 2005). The results improve upon previous constraints on the mass-radius relation for neutron stars, such as those derived by Link et al. (1999) using \(\rho_t = 0.075\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3}\) and \(P_t = 0.65\ \mathrm{MeV/fm}^3\), by providing more stringent limits based on both laboratory and astrophysical data. This enables more precise modeling of neutron star properties, including the crustal fraction of the moment of inertia for pulsars like Vela.
