# Quantum corrections to the stress-energy tensor in thermodynamic equilibrium with acceleration

> We show that the stress-energy tensor has additional terms with respect to the ideal form in states of global thermodynamic equilibrium in flat spacetime with non-vanishing acceleration and vorticity. These corrections are of quantum origin and their leading terms are second order in the gradients of the thermodynamic fields. Their relevant coefficients can be expressed in terms of correlators of the stress-energy tensor operator and the generators of the Lorentz group. With respect to previous assessments, we find that there are more second order coefficients and that all thermodynamic functions including energy density receive acceleration and vorticity dependent corrections. Notably, also the relation between ρand p, that is the equation of state, is affected by acceleration and vorticity. We have calculated the corrections for a free real scalar field -- both massive and massless -- and we have found that they increase, particularly for a massive field, at very high acceleration and vorticity and very low temperature. Finally, these non-ideal terms depend on the explicit form of the stress-energy operator, implying that different stress-energy tensor of the scalar field -- canonical or improved -- are thermodynamically inequivalent.

## Key claim

The stress-energy tensor in global thermodynamic equilibrium with non-vanishing acceleration and vorticity acquires quantum corrections at second order in the gradients of the thermodynamic fields, contradicting the common belief that deviations from the ideal form only arise from dissipative processes; these corrections affect all thermodynamic functions including the equation of state, and their coefficients depend on the specific form of the stress-energy operator (canonical versus improved), making them thermodynamically inequivalent.

## Method

The authors start from the general covariant density operator for relativistic quantum statistical mechanics, which for global equilibrium in flat spacetime reduces to an exponential of a linear combination of the 10 Poincaré group generators with constant coefficients. They perform a perturbative expansion of the mean value of the stress-energy tensor operator in powers of the antisymmetric tensor \(\varpi_{\mu\nu}\) (the exterior derivative of the four-temperature field), using the homogeneous equilibrium state as the zeroth-order reference. The expansion coefficients are expressed as correlators of the stress-energy tensor with the Lorentz group generators, and the resulting corrections are decomposed into terms proportional to the acceleration \(a^\mu\) and vorticity \(\omega^\mu\) fields. The calculation is carried out explicitly for a free real scalar field, both massive and massless.

## Key numbers

* For a free real scalar field, the quantum corrections to the stress-energy tensor are proportional to the dimensionless ratios \(\hbar a / (c K T)\) and \(\hbar \omega / (K T)\), where \(a\) is acceleration, \(\omega\) is vorticity, and \(T\) is proper temperature.
* The corrections increase at very high acceleration and vorticity and very low temperature, particularly for a massive field.
* The adimensional scales \(\hbar a / (c K T)\) and \(\hbar \omega / (K T)\) are, for the vast majority of physical systems, much less than 1, making the perturbative expansion feasible.

## Context

This work extends the classification of second-order gradient corrections to the stress-energy tensor in conformal hydrodynamics, previously studied by Romatschke (2010) and others, and by kinetic theory (Denicol, 2012), where some "thermodynamic" coefficients were calculated for conformal field theories by Moore (2013). The paper shows that such non-dissipative corrections are a general consequence of quantum relativistic statistical mechanics, not limited to conformal theories, and that they survive at equilibrium. The results enable the calculation of these corrections for any quantum field theory and imply that the equation of state itself is modified by acceleration and vorticity, with potential applications to heavy-ion collisions and astrophysical systems.
