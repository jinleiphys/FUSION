# An introduction to Bootstrap for nuclear physics

> This guide aims at providing a general introduction to bootstrap methods. By using simple examples taken from nuclear physics, I discuss how such a method can be used to quantify error bars of an estimator. I also investigate the use of bootstrap to estimate parameters of a simple liquid drop model. In particular, I investigate how the method compares with standard techniques based on likelihood estimator and how to take into account correlations to better evaluate confidence interval of parameters.

## Key claim

This guide demonstrates that bootstrap methods, particularly non-parametric bootstrap (NPB), provide a reliable alternative to standard likelihood-based techniques for estimating error bars and confidence intervals in nuclear physics, but the method's validity depends on the assumption of uncorrelated residuals; when residuals are strongly correlated, a block-bootstrap extension is necessary to avoid underestimating parameter uncertainties.

## Method

The author applies NPB to estimate confidence intervals for the mean of an exponential distribution and for the Pearson correlation coefficient between the slope of the symmetry energy L0 and neutron skin thickness in 208Pb and 100Sn, using 63 nuclear functionals from Skyrme, Gogny, and relativistic families. For regression analysis, the author fits a five-parameter liquid drop model to experimental binding energies from the AME2012 database (excluding nuclei with A<14 and experimental errors above 100 keV), then applies NPB by resampling residuals to generate new binding energy datasets and refitting. To handle correlated residuals, the author introduces block bootstrap (BB) with block lengths determined from lag-plot analysis of the averaged residuals as a function of mass number A.

## Key numbers

- For the mean of an exponential distribution with true mean 1/lambda = 1/2, NPB on a sample of N=10 gives mu* = 0.489 with 68% confidence interval [-0.146, +0.159], compared to the standard error sigma_M = 0.154
- Pearson coefficient between L0 and neutron skin thickness in 208Pb: r = 0.91 with 95% confidence interval [-0.05, +0.03] using Fisher transformation; NPB gives r* = 0.92 with 95% interval [-0.04, +0.04]
- For 100Sn with 5 outlier Skyrme functionals added, NPB gives r* = 0.58 with 95% confidence interval [-0.59, +0.25], compared to the direct Pearson estimate r = 0.68 with Fisher-based interval [-0.15, +0.11]
- Liquid drop model parameters from least-squares fit: a_v = 15.69 +/- 0.025 MeV, a_s = 17.75 +/- 0.08 MeV, a_c = 0.713 +/- 0.002 MeV, a_a = 23.16 +/- 0.06 MeV, delta = 11.8 +/- 0.9 MeV
- Block bootstrap with block length l=16 gives larger uncertainties: a_v = 15.70 +/- 0.14 MeV, a_s = 17.82 +/- 0.44 MeV, a_c = 0.713 +/- 0.009 MeV, a_a = 23.20 +/- 0.35 MeV, delta = 11.78 +/- 0.80 MeV
- Lag-plot analysis shows residuals are correlated over approximately 8 mass units (gamma(p) >= 0.5 for p up to 8)

## Context

This work extends the application of bootstrap methods in theoretical nuclear physics, following earlier uses by Nie (2000), Per (2014), Bertsch (2017), and Muir (2018), and provides a pedagogical introduction with nuclear-physics examples. The author compares NPB results to standard maximum likelihood estimation and Fisher transformation methods (Bar 1989, Dob 2014), and demonstrates that block bootstrap can address the correlated-residual problem identified in liquid drop model fits by Bertsch (2017). The method enables practitioners to obtain parameter uncertainties and correlation matrices without numerical derivatives in parameter space, as required by the Hessian-based approach of Roca (2015).
