# Estimating centrality in heavy-ion collisions using Transfer Learning technique

> In this study, we explore the applicability of Transfer Learning techniques for estimating collision centrality in terms of the number of participants ($N_{\rm part}$) in high-energy heavy-ion collisions. In the present work, we leverage popular pre-trained CNN models such as VGG16, ResNet50, and DenseNet121 to determine $N_{\rm part}$ in Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s}=200$ GeV on an event-by-event basis. Remarkably, all three models achieved good performance despite the pre-trained models being trained for databases of other domains. Particularly noteworthy is the superior performance of the VGG16 model, showcasing the potential of transfer learning techniques for extracting diverse observables from heavy-ion collision data.

## Key claim

Transfer learning using pre-trained CNN models (VGG16, ResNet50, DenseNet121) can estimate the number of participants ($N_{\rm part}$) in Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s}=200$ GeV with good accuracy, despite the models being originally trained on the ImageNet dataset of natural images rather than heavy-ion collision data. The VGG16 model achieves performance comparable to a custom CNN trained from scratch, with the hedge that all models perform worse for peripheral collisions due to lower particle multiplicity.

## Method

The authors generated 180K minimum-bias Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s}=200$ GeV using the string melting version of the AMPT (a multi-phase transport) model. Charged hadrons within mid-rapidity ($|\eta| < 1$) and with transverse momentum above 0.2 GeV/c were used to construct two-dimensional $p_{\rm T}$-weighted ($\eta - \phi$) histograms with $32 \times 32$ bins per event, which were resized to $32 \times 32$ pixel images and normalized to [0,1]. Three pre-trained CNN architectures (VGG16, ResNet50, DenseNet121) were adapted for regression by replacing their classification heads with custom fully connected layers; the models were first trained using fixed feature extraction (freezing convolutional base weights) and then fine-tuned with a reduced learning rate of 0.00001.

## Key numbers

- VGG16 after fine-tuning: MSLE = 0.0602, $R^2$ = 0.9834 for predicting $N_{\rm part}$ in Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s}=200$ GeV
- ResNet50 after fine-tuning: MSLE = 0.0754, $R^2$ = 0.9784
- DenseNet121 after fine-tuning: MSLE = 0.0767, $R^2$ = 0.9820
- Custom CNN from prior work (Basak 2023): MSLE = 0.0592, $R^2$ = 0.9847 (comparable to VGG16)
- Best performance achieved with $64 \times 64$ bin histograms: MSLE = 0.0576, $R^2$ = 0.9852 for fine-tuned VGG16
- For 0-10% central collisions, MSLE improved from 0.0066 ($16 \times 16$ bins) to 0.0025 ($128 \times 128$ bins)
- Training used 150K events (75% training, 25% validation) with batch size 64 and up to 100 epochs

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Basak 2023) that used Deep Neural Networks and CNNs trained from scratch to estimate $N_{\rm part}$ from AMPT-simulated Au+Au collisions. It also builds on prior machine learning approaches for impact parameter estimation in heavy-ion collisions, including work by Li et al. (2020, 2021) using CNNs and LightGBM, and by Xiang et al. (2022) using MLP and CNN for Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s}=200$ GeV. The study demonstrates that transfer learning can achieve comparable performance to custom-trained deep learning models while requiring less training data and computational resources, opening the possibility of applying pre-trained models to extract other observables from heavy-ion collision data.
