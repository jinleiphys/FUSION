# Nuclear stability and the Fold Catastrophe

> A geometrical analysis of the stability of nuclei against deformations is presented. In particular, we use Catastrophe Theory to illustrate discontinuous changes in the behavior of nuclei with respect to deformations as one moves in the N - Z space. We construct a minimalistic deformation model using the microscopic-macroscopic approach. A third-order phase transition is found in the liquid-drop model, which translates to a complete loss of stability (using the Fold catastrophe) when shell effects are included. The analysis is found to explain the instability of known fissile nuclei and also justify known decay chains of heavy nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper uses Catastrophe Theory to show that incorporating shell effects into the liquid-drop model transforms a third-order phase transition (where the nucleus simply switches between spherical and deformed shapes) into a complete loss of stability described by the Fold catastrophe, which can explain the instability of known fissile nuclei and the behavior of heavy-nuclei decay chains.

## Method

The authors construct a minimalistic deformation model using the microscopic-macroscopic approach, considering only the quadrupole deformation parameter (beta_2) up to third order. They first analyze the liquid-drop model alone, finding a third-order phase transition at the fissility parameter x = 1. They then incorporate shell corrections using the Nilsson modified harmonic oscillator potential, with shell effects added as a correction to the liquid-drop energy via the Strutinsky smearing procedure. The resulting deformation energy is mapped to the Fold catastrophe form u^3 + tu, and the condition for loss of stability (t > 0) is expressed as an inequality f(N,Z) > 0 in the N-Z plane.

## Key numbers

- The liquid-drop model constants used: a_C = 0.7053, a_s = 17.944 MeV, k_s = 1.782
- The third-order phase transition in the liquid-drop model occurs when the fissility parameter x = 1, which gives N_0 from the equation x(N_0) = (a_C/a_s)(Z^2/A)(1/(1 - k_s I^2)) = 1
- Calculated fission barriers for U238: 7.75 MeV (experimental: 5.50 MeV for E_b, 6.30 MeV for E_a)
- Calculated fission barrier for Th232: 9.57 MeV (experimental: 6.70 MeV for E_b, 5.80 MeV for E_a)
- Calculated fission barrier for Po208: 15.29 MeV (experimental: 19.9 MeV)
- Ronen's fissile rule: nuclei with 90 <= Z <= 100 and 2Z - N = 43 +/- 2 are fissile

## Context

This work extends the application of Catastrophe Theory, developed by Thom (1970s), to nuclear stability analysis. It builds on the macroscopic-microscopic approach described in "Shape and Shells in Nuclear Structure" (Nilsson and Ragnarsson) and the liquid-drop model parameterization from Myers. The analysis explains the instability of known fissile nuclei and justifies observed decay chains of heavy nuclei, particularly alpha decays, while also showing that beta decays temporarily increase the instability measure f(N,Z) before subsequent alpha decays reduce it. The approach enables a geometric classification of nuclear stability regions in the N-Z plane.
