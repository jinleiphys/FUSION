# Exact-exchange relativistic density functional theory in three-dimensional coordinate space

> The exact-exchange relativistic density functional theory (Ex-RDFT) of atomic nuclei has been solved in three-dimensional lattice space for the first time. The exchange energy is treated within the framework of the orbital-dependent relativistic Kohn-Sham density functional theory, wherein the local Lorentz scalar and vector potentials are derived using the relativistic optimized effective potential method. The solutions of binding energies, charge radii, and density distributions are benchmarked against the traditional relativistic Hartree-Fock approach for spherical and axially deformed nuclei. Furthermore, the triaxial neutron-rich $^{104-120}\text{Ru}$ isotopes are investigated with the exchange correlations, which is beyond the current capacity of the traditional relativistic Hartree-Fock approach. The results notably indicate the $γ$-softness of these neutron-rich nuclei, which is consistent with experimental observations. This novel approach establishes a foundation for the study of nuclei without imposing any symmetry restrictions employing relativistic density functional with exchange correlations.

## Key claim

The exact-exchange relativistic density functional theory (Ex-RDFT) has been solved in three-dimensional coordinate space for the first time, enabling the study of nuclei with arbitrary deformation including triaxial shapes, which is beyond the capacity of the traditional relativistic Hartree-Fock approach. The method is benchmarked against RHF results and shows agreement within 0.33 MeV in binding energy and 0.036 fm in charge radius for heavy nuclei, with the caveat that the relativistic Krieger-Li-Iafrate approximation is employed for the orbital-shift terms.

## Method

The authors solve the relativistic Kohn-Sham equation in three-dimensional coordinate space using the inverse Hamiltonian method for iteration and the Fourier spectral method for spatial derivatives to avoid fermion doubling. The exchange energy is treated via the orbital-dependent relativistic optimized effective potential method with the RKLI approximation, using the PKO2 effective interaction as the two-body interaction. The BCS method with a delta pairing force is employed for pairing correlations, and the framework is benchmarked against spherical and axially-deformed RHF calculations solved with the Dirac Woods-Saxon basis.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy difference between Ex-RDFT and RHF for \(^{208}\)Pb: less than 0.33 MeV
- Charge radius difference between Ex-RDFT and RHF for \(^{208}\)Pb: less than 0.036 fm
- Ground-state binding energy of \(^{16}\)O: -126.63 MeV (Ex-RDFT) vs -126.76 MeV (RHF)
- Ground-state binding energy of \(^{208}\)Pb: -1635.68 MeV (Ex-RDFT) vs -1636.01 MeV (RHF)
- Charge radius of \(^{208}\)Pb: 5.555 fm (Ex-RDFT) vs 5.519 fm (RHF)
- Ground-state quadrupole deformation of \(^{20}\)Ne: \(\beta = 0.531\) (Ex-RDFT) vs 0.520 (RHF)
- Ground-state energy difference for \(^{20}\)Ne between Ex-RDFT and RHF: 0.18 MeV
- Charge radius difference for \(^{20}\)Ne between Ex-RDFT and RHF: 0.020 fm
- Coordinate space discretization: 26 grid points with step sizes 1.0 fm along x, y, and z directions
- Convergence criteria: energy uncertainties less than \(10^{-9}\) MeV\(^2\) and maximum density change less than \(10^{-7}\) fm\(^{-3}\)

## Context

This work extends the exact-exchange relativistic density functional theory previously developed for spherical nuclei (Zhao 2023) to three-dimensional coordinate space, enabling studies of arbitrary nuclear deformations. It builds upon the relativistic density functional theory framework (Serot 1986, Ring 1996, Vretenar 2005, Meng 2006) and the three-dimensional coordinate space techniques for solving the Dirac equation (Ren 2017, Li 2020). The method enables the first study of triaxial neutron-rich Ru isotopes with exchange correlations, which was previously impossible with the relativistic Hartree-Fock approach (Geng 2020, Geng 2022). The results show \(\gamma\)-softness in Ru isotopes consistent with experimental observations (Stachel 1982, Stachel 1984, Aysto 1990, Shannon 1994, Soderstrom 2013, Abusara 2017, Shi 2018, Yang 2023), and future work aims to incorporate \(\pi\)-exchange and \(\rho\)-tensor couplings to understand tensor-force effects.
