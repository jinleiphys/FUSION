# Towards an unbiased jet energy loss measurement

> The modifications imprinted on jets due to their interaction with Quark Gluon Plasma (QGP) are assessed by comparing samples of jets produced in nucleus-nucleus collisions and proton-proton collisions. The standard procedure ignores the effect of bin migration by comparing specific observables for jet populations at the same reconstructed jet transverse momentum ($p_T$). Since jet $p_T$ is itself modified by interaction with QGP, all such comparisons confound QGP induced modifications with changes that are simply a consequence of comparing jets that started out differently. The quantile matching procedure introduced by Brewer et al. directly estimates average fractional jet energy loss ($Q_{AA}$) and can thus mitigate this $p_T$ migration effect. In this work, we validate the procedure in more realistic scenarios that include medium response. We study the evolution of $Q_{AA}$ with jet radius, its sensitivity to minimum particle $p_T$ and medium response as implemented in two different models for jet evolution in heavy-ion collisions. Further, we use this procedure to establish that the difference between inclusive jet and $γ+$jet nuclear modification factors ($R_{AA}$) is dominated by differences in the spectral shape, leaving the colour charge of the jet initiating parton with a lesser role to play. Additionally, we compare $Q_{AA}$ to an experimentally proposed proxy for fractional jet energy loss, $S_{loss}$, showing that both quantities are similar, although the former provides a more clear physical interpretation. Finally, we show the size of the $p_T$ migration correction for four different substructure observables and how to reliably use the quantile procedure experimentally to improve existing measurements.

## Key claim

The quantile matching procedure (Brewer et al.) provides a valid proxy for average fractional jet energy loss even when medium response is included and for multiple jet radii, but the agreement between the quantile-based energy loss estimate and the direct photon-based reference is only reasonable (not exact), with systematic differences observed.

## Method

The authors validate the quantile matching procedure by comparing its estimate of average fractional jet energy loss to a direct reference obtained from gamma+jet events, where the photon transverse momentum serves as a proxy for the initial parton transverse momentum. They generate event samples using the JEWEL 2.3 Monte Carlo event generator and the Hybrid Model, both for proton-proton and lead-lead collisions at sqrt(s) = 5.02 TeV, with jets reconstructed using the anti-kt algorithm for varying jet radii. The quantile procedure equates upper cumulative jet cross sections between pp and AA spectra to establish a mapping p_T^q(p_T^v), from which the fractional energy loss proxy 1 - Q_AA = 1 - p_T^q/p_T^v is computed.

## Key numbers

- JEWEL samples consist of 10^6 dijet events and 10^6 gamma+jet events at sqrt(s) = 5.02 TeV for both pp and PbPb (0-10% centrality) collisions
- Hybrid Model samples consist of approximately 10^6 dijet events at sqrt(s) = 5.02 TeV for pp and PbPb (0-5% centrality) collisions
- Minimum transverse momentum cutoff for hard scattering particles is 20 GeV in JEWEL and 100 GeV in the Hybrid Model
- Jet reconstruction uses minimum p_T cuts of 30 GeV (JEWEL) and 130 GeV (Hybrid Model), with |y^jet| <= 2.8
- Photon tagging requires p_T >= 50 GeV and |y| <= 2.37
- Final state particles are filtered with p_T >= 0.5 GeV and |y| <= 3.3
- For gamma+jet events at large p_T, nuclear effects (isospin and nPDFs) translate into a factor of approximately 1.5 difference in R_AA

## Context

This work extends the quantile matching procedure introduced by Brewer et al. (2018) by validating it in more realistic scenarios that include medium response, which was absent in the original validation using Z+jet events. The study also compares the quantile-based energy loss proxy Q_AA to the experimentally proposed proxy S_loss used by ATLAS (2023), finding both quantities are similar but Q_AA provides a clearer physical interpretation. The results enable unbiased jet energy loss measurements that correct for the p_T migration bias inherent in standard R_AA comparisons, and the authors demonstrate how to apply the quantile procedure to four different jet substructure observables.
