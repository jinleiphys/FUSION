# Resonance expansions in quantum mechanics

> The goal of this contribution is to discuss various resonance expansions that have been proposed in the literature.

# Resonance expansions in quantum mechanics

## Key claim

The paper reviews and compares several distinct resonance expansion formalisms (Berggren, Mondragon, Berggren-like, time-dependent proper-pole, and full-pole expansions) for finite-range potentials, noting that these expansions are formal and must be understood within a rigged Hilbert space framework, with the background integral often non-negligible and the integrands diverging exponentially on the infinite arc of the complex k-plane.

## Method

The authors derive completeness relations for Gamow (resonance) states by deforming the contour of the standard bound-plus-continuum completeness relation in the complex k-plane, extracting pole contributions from the fourth quadrant. They then compare Berggren's expansion (which includes a finite number of resonances plus a background integral) with expansions that include proper resonance poles only, and with a full-pole expansion that incorporates anti-resonance states via the Cauchy expansion of the outgoing Green function. Time-dependent expansions are obtained by deforming the contour of the Laplace-transformed retarded Green function, and the steepest descent method is applied to wave-packet propagation to show how resonance poles contribute to transient phenomena.

## Key numbers

- The Berggren expansion includes \(N_{\rm b}\) bound states and \(N_{\rm B}\) resonances lying between the real axis and the contour \(\Gamma_{\rm B}\), plus a background integral over \(\Gamma_{\rm B}\).
- The proper resonance poles satisfy \({\rm Re}\, k_p > {\rm Im}\, k_p\).
- The Gamow states in the time-dependent proper-pole expansion are normalized according to \(\int_0^R dr \, u_p^2(r) + i \frac{u_p^2(R)}{2k_p} = 1\), where \(R\) is the radial coordinate from which the potential vanishes.
- The full-pole expansion (Eq. 16) is valid for \(r, r' < R\) and includes an infinite sum over all poles \(n = -\infty\) to \(\infty\).
- The steepest descent method yields an expression involving the complex error function \(w(z) = \exp(-z^2) {\rm erfc}(-i z)\), with argument \(y_n = -e^{-i\pi/4} k_n t^{1/2}\).

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Gamow (1928) on alpha decay and Siegert (1939) on resonance expansions of the scattering function, as well as Berggren's completeness relation (1968) widely used in nuclear physics. The paper clarifies that Berggren's expansion is a formal expression valid only when sandwiched between wave functions that fall off faster than any exponential, and that the "Hardy axiom" assumed in some prior work (Bohm, 1981; Berggren and Lind, 1993) is flawed because quantum mechanical wave functions diverge exponentially on the infinite arc. The steepest-descent treatment provides a practical computational framework for transient phenomena, with the \(w\)-function serving as the elementary transient mode propagator.
