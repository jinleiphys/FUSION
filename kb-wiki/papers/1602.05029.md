# Occupation numbers of spherical orbits in self-consistent beyond-mean-field methods

> We present a method to compute the number of particles occupying spherical single-particle (SSP) levels within the energy density functional (EDF) framework. These SSP levels are defined for each nucleus by performing self-consistent mean-field calculations. The nuclear many-body states, in which the occupation numbers are evaluated, are obtained with a symmetry conserving configuration mixing (SCCM) method based on the Gogny EDF. The method allows a closer comparison between EDF and shell model with configuration mixing in large valence spaces (SM-CI) results, and can serve as a guidance to define physically sound valence spaces for SM-CI calculations. As a first application of the method, we analyze the onset of deformation in neutron-rich $N=40$ isotones and the role of the SSP levels around this harmonic oscillator magic number, with particular emphasis in the structure of $^{64}$Cr.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method to compute spherical single-particle (SSP) occupation numbers within the symmetry conserving configuration mixing (SCCM) framework based on the Gogny energy density functional, enabling quantitative comparison with shell-model configuration mixing (SM-CI) results. The method's utility is demonstrated by showing that beyond-mean-field correlations, particularly angular momentum projection and shape mixing, are essential to capture the deformed ground state of $^{64}$Cr, which appears spherical at the mean-field level due to the $N=40$ harmonic oscillator shell closure.

## Method

The authors first perform constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations with the Gogny D1S interaction to obtain intrinsic states as a function of axial quadrupole deformation $\beta_2$. These states are then projected onto good particle number and angular momentum (PNAMP), and finally mixed via the generator coordinate method (GCM) to produce the SCCM many-body states. Spherical single-particle orbits are defined by diagonalizing the density matrix of the spherical HFB solution for each nucleus, and the occupation numbers of these orbits in the correlated SCCM states are evaluated using the one-body operator $\hat{n}_\alpha$ within the GCM expectation value formalism.

## Key numbers

* For $^{64}$Cr, the prolate minimum in the particle-number and angular momentum projected (PNAMP) potential energy surface lies approximately 3 MeV lower than the spherical configuration.
* In the SCCM ground state of $^{64}$Cr, the neutron $1g_{9/2}$ orbit has an occupation of approximately 2.6 particles, the $2d_{5/2}$ orbit approximately 0.9 particles, and the $1f_{5/2}$ orbit shows approximately 1.91 holes relative to the spherical HFB configuration.
* The neutron $2p_{1/2}$ orbit shows approximately 0.91 holes and the $2p_{3/2}$ orbit approximately 0.36 holes in the SCCM ground state of $^{64}$Cr.
* For protons in $^{64}$Cr, the $1f_{7/2}$ orbit accommodates roughly one less proton than the four valence protons of the spherical case, with an SCCM occupation of approximately 3.2 particles.
* The $Z=28$ proton gap between the $1f_{7/2}$ orbit and the $2p_{3/2}$, $1f_{5/2}$, $2p_{1/2}$ pseudo-spin triplet is approximately 6 MeV across the $N=40$ isotones.

## Context

This work extends previous efforts to connect energy density functional (EDF) and shell model frameworks, such as comparisons of Gogny-based EDF with SM calculations for $^{48}$Cr (Rodríguez et al., 1995) and cranked intrinsic state studies for magnesium isotopes (Rodríguez et al., 2015). The method enables quantitative validation of valence spaces used in large-scale shell model calculations, specifically supporting the inclusion of the $1g_{9/2}$ and its quasi-SU(3) partner $2d_{5/2}$ in the neutron valence space for $N=40$ isotones as used in SM-CI calculations (Lenzi et al., 2010). The results also show good agreement with the pseudo+quasi SU(3) model predictions for $^{64}$Cr (Zuker et al., 2015), suggesting that the dominant physics of deformation in this region can be captured by algebraic models based on Elliott's SU(3).
