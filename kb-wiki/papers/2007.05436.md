# Complex Paths Around The Sign Problem

> The Monte Carlo evaluation of path integrals is one of a few general purpose methods to approach strongly coupled systems. It is used in all branches of Physics, from QCD/nuclear physics to the correlated electron systems. However, many systems of great importance (dense matter inside neutron stars, the repulsive Hubbard model away from half-filling, dynamical and non-equilibrium observables) are not amenable to the Monte Carlo method as it currently stands due to the so-called "sign-problem". We review a new set of ideas recently developed to tackle the sign problem based on the complexification of field space and the Picard-Lefshetz theory accompanying it. The mathematical ideas underpinning this approach, as well as the algorithms so far developed, are described together with non-trivial examples where the method has already been proved successful. Directions of future work, including the burgeoning use of machine learning techniques, are delineated.

## Key claim

The paper reviews a set of ideas based on complexification of field space and Picard-Lefschetz theory to tackle the sign problem in Monte Carlo evaluations of path integrals, but notes that for non-abelian gauge theories the thimble structure is more complicated because critical points are not isolated but form continuous manifolds due to gauge symmetry, requiring modifications to Picard-Lefschetz theory.

## Method

The authors describe algorithms for deforming integration contours in field space using holomorphic gradient flow, which maps the original real integration domain to a middle-dimensional manifold in complex space where the sign problem is mitigated. They detail several computational approaches for the Jacobian associated with this change of variables, including stochastic estimators for the phase, two estimators (W1 and W2) for log det J, and a Metropolis method that incorporates the Jacobian into the effective action without explicit computation. These methods are applied to real-time field theory, heavy-dense QCD, and 2D QED as case studies.

## Key numbers

- The stochastic estimator for the phase of the Jacobian has computational cost scaling as O(N x N_R), where N is proportional to spacetime volume and N_R is the number of random vectors
- The W1 estimator for log det J has computational cost O(N^2), and the W2 estimator has O(N) complexity, compared to O(N^3) for the full Jacobian
- For real-time correlators in 1+1 dimensional phi^4 theory, time separations up to |t-t'| = 4 beta have been achieved on a lattice with N_t=8, N_beta=2, and N_x=8
- Heavy-dense QCD studies were performed on spatial volumes up to 3^3 - 4^3 with N_c=3
- 2D QED studies achieved the cold limit showing Silver Blaze behavior on a 14x10 lattice

## Context

This review extends the work on Lefschetz thimbles initiated by Cristoforetti et al. (2012) and the generalized thimble method, addressing computational bottlenecks that prevented study of all but the smallest models. The methods described enable computation of real-time correlators (which are inaccessible to conventional lattice methods and where complex Langevin converges to wrong results for time separations beyond inverse temperature, as shown by Berges (2006)) and provide a framework for tackling sign problems in dense matter systems, the repulsive Hubbard model, and non-equilibrium observables. Future directions include the use of machine learning techniques and extending to larger lattices and longer time separations.
