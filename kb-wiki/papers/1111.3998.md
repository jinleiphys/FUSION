# Many-body approximations for atomic binding energies

> We benchmark three standard approximations for the many-body problem -- the Hartree-Fock, projected Hartree-Fock, and random phase approximations -- against full numerical configuration-interaction calculations of the electronic structure of atoms, from Li through to Ne. These configuration-interaction calculations used up to $2 \times 10^8$ uncoupled basis states, equivalent to $ 10^7$ coupled basis states (configuration state functions.) Each method uses exactly the same input, i.e., the same single-particle basis and Coulomb matrix elements, so any differences are strictly due to the approximation itself. Although it consistently overestimates the ground state binding energy, the random phase approximation has the smallest overall errors; furthermore, we suggest it may be useful as a method for efficient optimization of single-particle basis functions.

# Many-body approximations for atomic binding energies

## Key claim

The random phase approximation (RPA) consistently overestimates the ground state binding energy for second-row atoms from Li through Ne, yet it has the smallest overall errors among the three approximations tested when benchmarked against full configuration-interaction (CI) calculations using identical input. The authors suggest RPA may be useful as a method for efficient optimization of single-particle basis functions, despite its systematic overestimation.

## Method

The authors performed full configuration-interaction (CI) calculations using the BIGSTICK code for neutral atoms Li through Ne, using Slater-type orbital (STO) basis sets CVB1, CVB2, and CVB3 with up to 22 single-particle orbits. They then compared these numerically exact results against Hartree-Fock (HF), angular-momentum projected Hartree-Fock (PHF), and random phase approximation (RPA) calculations using the identical single-particle basis and Coulomb matrix elements, ensuring that any differences arise strictly from the approximation method itself. The Hamiltonian was restricted to the nonrelativistic Coulomb interaction, and all calculations used the same orthonormalized single-particle basis and antisymmetrized two-body matrix elements.

## Key numbers

- Full CI spaces reached up to $2 \times 10^8$ uncoupled basis states (M-scheme), equivalent to $10^7$ coupled basis states (configuration state functions)
- For carbon with the CVB3 basis, the uncoupled CI space dimension was $2.5 \times 10^8$
- RPA ground state energy for Li with CVB3: -7.4774 hartree, compared to CI value of -7.4741 hartree (difference of 0.0033 hartree)
- RPA ground state energy for Ne with CVB1: -128.9307 hartree, compared to CI value of -128.8278 hartree (difference of 0.1029 hartree)
- RPA ground state energy for carbon with CVB3: -37.8753 hartree, compared to CI value of -37.8378 hartree
- HF ground state energy for carbon with CVB3: -37.6717 hartree, compared to CI value of -37.8378 hartree
- Experimental ionization potential for carbon: 0.4140 hartree; CI with CVB3 gave 0.4133 hartree
- Experimental electron affinity for carbon: 0.0464 hartree; CI with CVB2 gave 0.0446 hartree
- For basis optimization of carbon CVB1, the minimum energy scale factor was 0.981 for CI and 0.989 for RPA, while HF gave 0.892 and PHF gave 0.908

## Context

This work extends prior benchmarking of many-body approximations for the nuclear many-body problem by Johnson and collaborators (2002, 2003) to atomic electronic structure, using full CI as the numerically exact reference. The authors note that previous studies of these atoms using CI, HF, PHF, and RPA generally focused on computing physical properties rather than isolating errors intrinsic to the approximation method itself. The results enable using RPA as a computationally cheap proxy for full CI during basis set optimization, which is significant because CI calculations in large dimension spaces are tedious and time-consuming, and a basis optimized for HF may not be optimal for CI.
