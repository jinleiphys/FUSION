# Estimation of the Shear Viscosity from 3FD Simulations of Au+Au Collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=$ 3.3--39 GeV

> An effective shear viscosity in central Au+Au collisions is estimated in the range of incident energies 3.3 GeV $\le \sqrt{s_{NN}}\le$ 39 GeV. The simulations are performed within a three-fluid model employing three different equations of state with and without the deconfinement transition. In order to estimate this effective viscosity, we consider the entropy produced in the 3FD simulations as if it is generated within the conventional one-fluid viscous hydrodynamics. It is found that the effective viscosity within different considered scenarios is very similar at the expansion stage of the collision: as a function of temperature ($T$) the viscosity-to-entropy ratio behaves as $η/s \sim 1/T^4$; as a function of net-baryon density ($n_B$), $η/s \sim 1/s$, i.e. it is mainly determined by the density dependence of the entropy density. The above dependencies take place along the dynamical trajectories of Au+Au collisions. At the final stages of the expansion the $η/s$ values are ranged from $\sim$0.05 at highest considered energies to $\sim$0.5 at the lowest ones.

# Estimation of the Shear Viscosity from 3FD Simulations of Au+Au Collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 3.3–39 GeV

## Key claim

The effective shear viscosity extracted from three-fluid dynamics (3FD) simulations of central Au+Au collisions is very similar across different equations of state (hadronic, first-order phase transition, and crossover) at the expansion stage, following eta/s ~ 1/T^4 as a function of temperature and eta/s ~ 1/s as a function of net-baryon density. However, the authors caution that the results should be considered only as order-of-magnitude estimations due to numerical fluctuations and the approximate nature of the extraction method.

## Method

The authors performed 3FD simulations of central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 3.3–39 GeV using three different equations of state: a purely hadronic EoS, one with a first-order deconfinement phase transition, and one with a smooth crossover transition. To estimate an effective shear viscosity, they associated the entropy produced in the 3FD simulations with the entropy production expected from conventional one-fluid viscous hydrodynamics, assuming only shear viscosity is nonzero and defining a baryon-flow velocity and energy-density-weighted temperature from the three-fluid fields.

## Key numbers

- The eta/s ratio at the final stages of expansion ranges from approximately 0.05 at the highest considered energy (sqrt_s_NN = 39 GeV) to approximately 0.5 at the lowest energy (sqrt_s_NN = 3.3 GeV).
- The temperature dependence of eta/s along dynamical trajectories follows approximately (T_0/T)^4, where T_0 = 71 MeV.
- The kinematic viscosity eta/n_B as a function of net-baryon density follows approximately n_0/n_B, where n_0 = 0.15 fm^{-3} is the normal nuclear density, with numerical factors ranging from about 2 n_0 at low sqrt_s_NN to about 0.5 n_0 at the highest considered sqrt_s_NN.
- At high temperatures above approximately 160 MeV in collisions with sqrt_s_NN > 10 GeV, the eta/s ratio falls noticeably below the conjectured lower bound of 1/(4 pi).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous 3FD analysis (Ivanov 2014) which found that different equations of state equally well describe STAR data on integrated elliptic flow at sqrt_s_NN = 7.7–39 GeV, and provides a quantitative explanation for that similarity in terms of effective viscosity. The results are compared with hybrid model analyses (Karpenko 2015) that found eta/s approximately 0.1–0.2 at BES-RHIC energies, and with theoretical expectations (Kestin 2008) that viscosity should rise at lower energies due to prolonged hadronic phase. The extracted eta/s values do not contradict those findings because the ratio is strongly temperature dependent, and the present work reports values at the final expansion stage rather than averages. The small eta/s values at high energies in the hadronic scenario are attributed to an artificial enhancement of friction forces based on proton-proton cross sections, required to reproduce observed baryon stopping.
