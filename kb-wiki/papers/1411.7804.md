# Three-dimensional mesh calculations for covariant density functional theory

> In contrast to the non-relativistic approaches, three-dimensional (3D) mesh calculations for the {\it relativistic} density functional theory have not been realized because of the challenges of variational collapse and fermion doubling. We overcome these difficulties by developing a novel method based on the ideas of Wilson fermion as well as the variational principle for the inverse Hamiltonian. We demonstrate the applicability of this method by applying it to $^{16}$O, $^{24}$Mg, and $^{28}$Si nuclei, providing detailed explanation on the formalism and verification of numerical implementation.

## Key claim

The authors overcome the two major obstacles to three-dimensional (3D) mesh calculations for relativistic density functional theory, variational collapse and fermion doubling, by combining the inverse Hamiltonian method with a Wilson fermion approach, and demonstrate the method's applicability to spherical and deformed nuclei without assuming any spatial symmetry.

## Method

The authors discretize the Dirac equation on a 3D lattice and solve it self-consistently using the relativistic point-coupling model. To avoid variational collapse (the tendency of iterative solutions to dive into the Dirac sea), they replace the standard imaginary-time evolution with an evolution operator based on the inverse Hamiltonian, \(e^{T/(h-W)}\), which monotonically increases the expectation value of \(h^{-1}\) and converges to the lowest positive-energy state. To eliminate the fermion doubling problem (spurious low-energy high-momentum solutions arising from finite-difference approximations of the first derivative), they add a Wilson term proportional to \(\beta \Delta\) to the Hamiltonian, which pushes the spurious states to higher energies while minimally affecting physical states.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{16}\)O, \(^{24}\)Mg, and \(^{28}\)Si nuclei, the method is demonstrated to work, providing detailed verification of numerical implementation.
- The Wilson parameter \(R = 0.2\) is used in the dispersion relation comparison (Fig. 3), showing that the spurious minimum at \(k = \pi/a\) is lifted.
- The mesh spacing is denoted \(a\) and the box size \(L\); the 3-point finite-difference formula \(\partial_x \psi(x_i) = [\psi(x_{i+1}) - \psi(x_{i-1})]/(2a)\) is used for the kinetic term.
- In 3D, there are seven spurious solutions for each physical solution, corresponding to combinations of physical and spurious modes along the \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\) axes.

## Context

This work extends the inverse Hamiltonian method previously developed for spherical relativistic Hamiltonians (Hagino, 2010) to full 3D coordinate space, and adapts the Wilson fermion technique from lattice QCD (Wilson, 1977; Kogut, 1983) to nuclear structure calculations. Prior CDFT calculations for deformed nuclei relied on basis expansion methods (Zhou, 2010; Lu, 2011, 2012, 2014; Zhao, 2012; Li, 2012; Chen, 2012), while non-relativistic Skyrme DFT calculations had already been performed on 3D meshes using the imaginary time method (Davies, 1980; Bonche, 2005). This work enables, for the first time, fully 3D coordinate-space CDFT calculations without spatial symmetry restrictions, opening the door to flexible descriptions of arbitrary nuclear deformations including non-axial and reflection-asymmetric shapes.
