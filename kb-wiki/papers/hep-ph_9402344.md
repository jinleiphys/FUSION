# Changes in the radius of a nucleon in interaction with another nucleon

> We consider a two-nucleon system described by two different skyrmion models that provide attraction for the central NN potential. One of these models is based on the product ansatz and the other on dilaton coupling. Within these models we ask the question, To what degree does the nucleon swell or shrink when the internucleon separation distance is appropriate to attraction or repulsion? We find typically swelling of 3 to 4 percent for central attraction of some 40 to 50 MeV.

## Key claim

Within two different skyrmion models that provide central attraction for the NN potential, the nucleon swells by 3 to 4 percent when the internucleon separation distance corresponds to attraction of some 40 to 50 MeV, but this swelling is modest and well within the empirical limit that the radius change is at most about 4 percent.

## Method

The authors studied a two-nucleon system using two skyrmion models: one based on the product ansatz with admixture of baryon resonances (Delta(1232) and N(1440) Roper), and another coupling the skyrmion to a dilaton field to incorporate the QCD trace anomaly. For each model, they calculated the central NN potential and the ratio of the interacting-nucleon root-mean-square radius to the free-nucleon radius as a function of internucleon separation distance R, with the second nucleon treated as a spectator for the radius evaluation.

## Key numbers

- For the usual Skyrme model with product ansatz and baryon-resonance admixtures, the central NN potential reaches an attraction of about -11 MeV and the corresponding change in the nucleon radius is about 4 percent.
- For the dilaton-coupled skyrmion model, the central attraction reaches values deeper than -40 MeV and the radius change is about 3 percent.
- The empirical limit on nucleon radius change in the nucleus is |r/r0 - 1| less than or equal to 0.04 (about 4 percent).
- Shrinking of the nucleon sets in for internucleon separations R less than about 1.5 fm.

## Context

This work extends earlier skyrmion studies by Oka, Liu, and Yu (1986) and Kalbermann and Eisenberg (1987) that found only shrinking when skyrmions repel each other, since those models contained no attraction. The paper provides quantitative support for the general argument by Frankfurt and Strikman (1985, 1988) that an attractive potential should be accompanied by nucleon swelling. The results enable future studies of nuclear form factors, where the third-power scaling of radius changes at moderate momentum transfer could be probed with new data from facilities such as CEBAF.
