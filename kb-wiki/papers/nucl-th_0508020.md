# Relativistic Continuum Hartree Bogoliubov Theory for Ground State Properties of Exotic Nuclei

> The Relativistic Continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory, which properly takes into account the pairing correlation and the coupling to (discretized) continuum via Bogoliubov transformation in a microscopic and self-consistent way, has been reviewed together with its new interpretation of the halo phenomena observed in light nuclei as the scattering of particle pairs into the continuum, the prediction of the exotic phenomena -- giant halos in nuclei near neutron drip line, the reproduction of interaction cross sections and charge-changing cross sections in light exotic nuclei in combination with the Glauber theory, better restoration of pseudospin symmetry in exotic nuclei, predictions of exotic phenomena in hyper nuclei, and new magic numbers in superheavy nuclei, etc. Recent investigations on new effective interactions, the density dependence of the interaction strengthes, the RMF theory on the Woods-Saxon basis, the single particle resonant states, and the resonant BCS (rBCS) method for the pairing correlation, etc. are also presented in some details.

# Relativistic Continuum Hartree Bogoliubov Theory for Ground State Properties of Exotic Nuclei

## Key claim

The Relativistic Continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory, which treats pairing correlations and coupling to the continuum via Bogoliubov transformation in a self-consistent microscopic framework, can describe halo phenomena as the scattering of particle pairs into the continuum and predict giant halos in neutron-drip-line nuclei, but the spherical symmetry assumption leads to discrepancies of about 10 MeV in binding energy for deformed nuclei and fails to reproduce the parabolic charge-radius trend in mid-mass calcium isotopes.

## Method

The RCHB theory solves the relativistic mean-field (RMF) equations and the Bogoliubov transformation simultaneously in coordinate space, using a discretized continuum via box boundary conditions. Pairing correlations are treated with a finite-range or zero-range pairing force, and the effective meson-nucleon interactions include nonlinear self-coupling terms (e.g., PK1, NL3) or density-dependent couplings (e.g., DD-ME1). Ground-state properties are calculated for spherical even-even nuclei across isotopic chains from the proton drip line to the neutron drip line.

## Key numbers

- Binding energies for Pb isotopes from A=182 to A=214 are reproduced within 3-4 MeV (less than 1% of experimental values) for most effective interactions; the rms deviation sigma for PK1 is 0.00033 (relative deviation).
- The neutron drip line nuclei are predicted as: 28O, 72Ca, 98Ni, 136Zr, 174Sn, and 266Pb.
- For Ca isotopes, the neutron skin thickness in 208Pb with PK1 and NL3 interactions yields a minimum value S = 0.16 fm; with S271 and Z271 interactions, S = 0.11 fm.
- The charge radius coefficient r_Z = 1.631(11) fm from a Z^{1/3} fit to 536 nuclei with A >= 40, with isospin parameter b = 0.062(9).
- The one-neutron separation energy for 19C is calculated as -0.003 MeV (predicted unbound) while experimentally it is bound; for 21C the calculated S_n is positive (predicted bound) while experimentally it is unbound.
- Charge radii from RCHB reproduce experimental data within 1.5% for even-even Ca, Ni, Zr, Sn, and Pb isotopes.

## Context

This review extends the RCHB framework developed by Meng et al. (1996, 1998) and applies it systematically to isotopic chains from Li to Pb, building on earlier RMF calculations by Sharma et al. (1992) and the discovery of the 11Li halo by Tanihata (1985). The work contradicts the earlier prediction by Fayans et al. (2000) and Im et al. (2000) that 70Ca is the last bound Ca isotope, finding instead that 72Ca is bound due to the disappearance of the N=50 magic number from halo effects. The proposed Z^{1/3} law for charge radii with isospin dependence (Zhang et al., 2002) improves upon the traditional A^{1/3} law and the modified formulas of Pomorski et al. (1993) and Dobaczewski et al. (1996). The results enable predictions of giant halos, new magic numbers near the drip line, and provide constraints for density-dependent effective interactions such as DD-ME1 (Niksic et al., 2002).
