# Physics with a very first low-energy beta-beam

> We describe the importance of having low-energy (10-100 MeV) neutrino beams produced through the decay of boosted radioactive ions (``beta-beams''). We focus on the interest for neutrino-nucleus interaction studies and their impact for astrophysics, nuclear and particle physics. In particular, we discuss the relation to neutrinoless double-beta decay. Finally, we mention the status as far as the feasibility of low-energy beta-beams is concerned.

# Physics with a very first low-energy beta-beam

## Key claim
Low-energy beta-beams (10–100 MeV) produced by boosting radioactive ions to Lorentz factors γ = 5–15 can provide a flexible tool for neutrino-nucleus interaction studies, and these measurements are closely connected to constraining the nuclear response needed for neutrinoless double-beta decay half-life predictions, though the feasibility of a small dedicated storage ring is still under study within the Eurisol Design Study.

## Method
The paper reviews the concept of beta-beams, where boosted radioactive ions decay to produce well-known electron (anti)neutrino beams, and focuses on the low-energy scenario (γ = 5–15) as distinct from the original high-energy proposal (γ = 60–100) by Zucchelli (2002). It presents calculated neutrino-nucleus interaction rates for deuteron, oxygen, iron, and lead targets using cross sections from prior literature, comparing a small storage ring (150 m straight sections, 450 m total length) to a large ring (2.5 km straight sections, 7 km total length), with detectors at 10 m distance. The paper also demonstrates the connection between neutrino-nucleus one-body operators and the two-body operators in neutrinoless double-beta decay by rewriting the neutrino exchange potential in momentum space and using multipole decomposition.

## Key numbers
- Low-energy beta-beam energy range: 10–100 MeV.
- Lorentz factor range for low-energy scenario: γ = 5–15.
- Small storage ring: 150 m straight sections, 450 m total length; large ring: 2.5 km straight sections, 7 km total length.
- For γ = 14, with a small ring and detector at 10 m: ν + deuteron (35 ton detector) gives 2363 events/year; ν + oxygen (952 ton) gives 6054 events/year; ν + iron (250 ton) gives 20768 events/year; ν + lead (360 ton) gives 103707 events/year.
- For the large ring under the same conditions: ν + deuteron gives 180 events/year; ν + oxygen gives 734 events/year; ν + iron gives 1611 events/year; ν + lead gives 7922 events/year.
- Planned radioactive ion beam intensities: 10^11–10^13 ions/s.
- Present limit on neutrinoless double-beta decay effective Majorana mass: about 0.2 eV; future experiments aim at 50 meV.

## Context
This work extends the original beta-beam concept proposed by Zucchelli (2002) for CP violation searches at high γ (60–100) to a low-energy regime (γ = 5–15) first suggested by Volpe (2004). It directly addresses the limited number of neutrino-nucleus measurements available (essentially on three light nuclei) and the discrepancies seen in cross sections for carbon-12 and lead-208. The paper enables future experimental programs to constrain neutrinoless double-beta decay half-life predictions by measuring the same nuclear response (isospin and spin-isospin operators) via neutrino-nucleus interactions, complementing existing constraints from beta-decay, muon capture, charge-exchange reactions, and two-neutrino double-beta decay.
