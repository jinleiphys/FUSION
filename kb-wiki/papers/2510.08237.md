# On fluctuation properties of MACS

> The Maxwellian Average Cross Section (MACS) is usually calculated with help of the statistical codes that do not take into account fluctuations of individual resonance parameters. The actual MACS can substantially deviate from its expectation value. This work focuses on description of various sources and aspects of these fluctuations. Simulated resonance sequences considering all the fluctuations involved in the statistical model are used for this purpose. Contribution of various sources of the fluctuations and neutrons with different orbital momenta are thoroughly investigated and described. Impact of available cross section data at low neutron energies is also checked. Based on this analysis simple empirical formulae for estimating relative fluctuations of MACS using solely $s$-wave resonance spacing $D_0$ and the temperature of the stellar environment are derived. It is shown that real nuclei follow the proposed formula well. The expected MACS fluctuations increase with $D_0$ and can be significant. While for isotopes with $D_0$ in eV range the possible deviation from calculated values are small, for nuclei with $D_0$ in the keV range the full-width half-maximum of the expected distribution is larger than about 25\% and 10\% for astrophysically relevant temperatures of $kT=8$ and 30 keV. At least for these nuclei the predictions from statistical codes must be taken with caution.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the Maxwellian Average Cross Section (MACS) calculated using statistical Hauser-Feshbach codes carries an inherent uncertainty from fluctuations of individual resonance parameters, and that for nuclei with s-wave resonance spacing \(D_0\) in the keV range, the full-width half-maximum of the expected MACS distribution exceeds about 25% at \(kT=8\) keV and 10% at \(kT=30\) keV, meaning statistical-code predictions must be treated with caution for such nuclei.

## Method

The authors generated 500–2000 random sequences of neutron resonances for each parameter set, using average resonance parameters (level densities, neutron strength functions, average radiative widths) and expected fluctuation properties (Porter-Thomas for neutron widths, \(\chi^2_\nu\) or DICEBOX-simulated distributions for radiative widths, and Wigner or Poisson nearest-neighbor spacing for resonance positions). They calculated \(\sigma_{\rm MACS}\) from each sequence via Eq. (4), summing over resonances up to \(E_n = 10kT\), and studied the relative standard deviation \(\delta_{\rm MACS}\) as a function of \(D_0\), \(kT\), and the combination of neutron strength functions and average radiative widths, using both an artificial nucleus based on \(^{96}\)Zr and real nuclei.

## Key numbers

* For nuclei with \(D_0\) in the keV range, the full-width half-maximum of the expected MACS distribution is larger than about 25% at \(kT=8\) keV and larger than about 10% at \(kT=30\) keV.
* The effective number of levels \(N_{\rm eff}\) for \(\ell=0\) is approximated by \(N_{\rm eff} = 2600 \times kT \times D_0^{-1.03}\), where \(kT\) is in keV and \(D_0\) in eV.
* For \(\ell=1\), \(N_{\rm eff} = 14500 \times kT \times D_0^{-1.08}\); for \(\ell=2\), \(N_{\rm eff} = 32000 \times kT \times D_0^{-1.06}\).
* The cumulative contribution to the MACS reaches approximately 99.9% for \(E_n < 8kT\), and the fraction from \(E_n > 10kT\) reaches at maximum only about \(10^{-4}\).
* The three combinations of strength function and average radiative width used for the artificial nucleus were: \(S_\ell = 0.4 \times 10^{-4}\) with \(\langle\Gamma_\gamma\rangle = 500\) meV; \(S_\ell = 6.0 \times 10^{-4}\) with \(\langle\Gamma_\gamma\rangle = 50\) meV; and \(S_\ell = 1.5 \times 10^{-4}\) with \(\langle\Gamma_\gamma\rangle = 200\) meV.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Rochman (2017) that partly illustrated the impact of resonance-parameter fluctuations on MACS, and it provides a systematic quantification of these fluctuations across a wide range of \(D_0\) (1 eV to 30 keV) and astrophysically relevant temperatures (\(kT = 5\)–100 keV). The derived empirical formulas for \(\delta_{\rm MACS}\) as a function of \(D_0\) and \(kT\) enable practitioners to estimate the inherent uncertainty in Hauser-Feshbach predictions without performing full Monte Carlo simulations, and they highlight the need for direct measurements (activation or time-of-flight) for nuclei with \(D_0\) in the keV range.
