# Search For Gravitational Redshifted Absorption Lines In LMXB Serpens X-1

> The equation of state for ultra-dense matter can be tested from observations of the ratio of mass to radius of neutron stars. This could be measured precisely from the redshift of a narrow line produced on the surface. X-rays bursts have been intensively searched for such features, but so far without detection. Here instead we search for redshifted lines in the persistent emission, where the accretion flow dominates over the surface emission. We discuss the requirements for narrow lines to be produced, and show that narrow absorption lines from highly ionized iron can potentially be observable in accreting low mass X-ray binaries (low B field) which have either low spin or low inclination so that Doppler broadening is small. This selects Serpens X-1 as the only potential candidate persistent LMXB due to its low inclination. Including surface models in the broad band accretion flow model predicts that the absorption line from He-like iron at 6.7 keV should be redshifted to $\sim$ 5.1 - 5.7 keV (10 - 15 km for $1.4\mathrm{M_\odot}$) and have an equivalent width of 0.8 - 8.0 eV for surface temperatures of 7 - 10 $\times$ 10$^6$ K. We use the high resolution Chandra grating data to give a firm upper limit of 2 - 3 eV for an absorption line at $\sim 5$ keV. We discuss possible reasons for this lack of detection (the surface temperature and the geometry of the boundary layer etc.). Future instruments with better sensitivity are required in order to explore the existence of such features.

## Key claim

The paper searches for gravitationally redshifted absorption lines from highly ionized iron in the persistent (non-burst) emission of the low-mass X-ray binary Serpens X-1, rather than in X-ray bursts where such features have been sought previously. The authors predict that a He-like iron absorption line at 6.7 keV should be redshifted to approximately 5.1-5.7 keV with an equivalent width of 0.8-8.0 eV, but their Chandra data place a firm upper limit of 2-3 eV, ruling out the higher end of these predictions.

## Method

The authors used Suzaku broadband X-ray data to model the continuum emission of Serpens X-1 as a combination of a disk component (kerrbb) and a Comptonized boundary layer (nthcomp), then added neutron star atmosphere models at two effective temperatures (7 × 10⁶ K and 10 × 10⁶ K) to predict the equivalent width of the Fe XXV absorption line. They then performed a blind search for absorption lines in high-resolution Chandra HETGS MEG±1 grating data over the energy range 3.94-6.09 keV (redshift z = 0.1-0.7), fitting a power-law continuum plus a Gaussian line with fixed intrinsic width of 80 eV and stepping the line center energy in 0.01 keV increments. The 3σ detection threshold was determined through Monte Carlo simulations accounting for the look-elsewhere effect.

## Key numbers

- Predicted redshifted energy of Fe XXV absorption line: 5.1-5.7 keV, corresponding to neutron star radii of 10-15 km for a 1.4 M⊙ mass
- Predicted equivalent width of Fe XXV line: 0.8 eV for surface temperature 7 × 10⁶ K; 7.7 eV for surface temperature 10 × 10⁶ K
- Predicted equivalent width of Fe XXVI Lyα line: 0.05 eV at 7 × 10⁶ K; 2.0 eV at 10 × 10⁶ K
- Firm upper limit from Chandra data: 2-3 eV for an absorption line at approximately 5 keV
- 3σ upper limit on equivalent width at z = 0.3: approximately -3 eV
- Maximum observed Δχ² in the blind search: 5.41, compared to the 3σ threshold of 15.37
- Estimated exposure time needed to detect the 0.8 eV line at 3σ confidence: 3-4 Ms

## Context

This work extends the search for gravitationally redshifted surface lines from neutron stars beyond X-ray bursts, where Cottam (2002) reported detections in EXO 0748-676 that were not reproduced in more sensitive data by Cottam (2008) and were later ruled out by spin measurements from Galloway (2010). The authors follow the Astro-H white paper on LMXBs (2014) and use atmosphere models from Rauch (2008) with relativistic transfer functions from Baubock (2013). The non-detection enables future instruments with better sensitivity to explore these features, and the results constrain the surface temperature of Serpens X-1 to be below approximately 10 × 10⁶ K or require alternative explanations such as boundary layer geometry effects.
