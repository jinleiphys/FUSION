# Transport Model Simulations of Projectile Fragmentation Reactions at 140 MeV/nucleon

> The collisions in four different reaction systems using $^{40,48}$Ca and $^{58,64}$Ni isotope beams and a Be target have been simulated using the Heavy Ion Phase Space Exploration and the Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics models. The present study mainly focuses on the model predictions for the excitation energies of the hot fragments and the cross sections of the final fragments produced in these reactions. The effects of various factors influencing the final fragment cross sections, such as the choice of the statistical decay code and its parameters have been explored. The predicted fragment cross sections are compared to the projectile fragmentation cross sections measured with the A1900 mass separator. At $E/A=140$ MeV, reaction dynamics can significantly modify the detection efficiencies for the fragments and make them different from the efficiencies applied to the measured data reported in the previous work. The effects of efficiency corrections on the validation of event generator codes are discussed in the context of the two models.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that at 140 MeV/nucleon, reaction dynamics can significantly modify detection efficiencies for fragments, making them different from the efficiencies applied to previously published measured data; therefore, comparing calculated results directly to published corrected data may be inappropriate, and filtered calculations should instead be compared to uncorrected raw experimental cross sections.

## Method

The authors simulated four projectile fragmentation reaction systems (40,48Ca and 58,64Ni beams on a 9Be target at 140 MeV/nucleon) using the macroscopic-microscopic Heavy Ion Phase Space Exploration (HIPSE) model and the fully microscopic Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics (AMD) model. Primary fragments from both models were subsequently decayed using the GEMINI statistical evaporation code, and the predicted final fragment cross sections were compared to projectile fragmentation cross sections measured with the A1900 mass separator at the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory. The effects of the choice of statistical decay code and its parameters (particularly the level density parameter) on the final fragment distributions were explored, and the influence of detector efficiency corrections on model validation was assessed by comparing filtered model results to raw experimental data.

## Key numbers

- Four reaction systems were studied: 40Ca, 48Ca, 58Ni, and 64Ni beams on 9Be targets at E/A = 140 MeV/nucleon.
- The experimental campaign yielded more than 1400 measured cross sections (1379 fragments plus 111 pick-up) from eight different reaction systems.
- In AMD simulations, the average excitation energy per nucleon saturates around 4 MeV after removal of about 10 nucleons.
- HIPSE model parameters were extrapolated to 140 MeV/nucleon as alpha_a = 0.55, x_ex = 0.09, and x_coll = 0.18.
- AMD simulations used free two-nucleon collision cross sections with a cut-off at 150 mb for the in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section.
- Approximately 100,000 simulated events per reaction were used for HIPSE and 20,000 for AMD.
- The level density parameter a = A/10 MeV^-1 was used as the default for sequential decay calculations, with comparisons also shown for a = A/12 MeV^-1 and a = A/8 MeV^-1.

## Context

This work extends previous projectile fragmentation experiments at the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory (2002-2005) and provides benchmark-quality data sets for testing reaction models, going beyond phenomenological approaches such as the EPAX parameterization (Summerer, 2000) and the Abrasion-Ablation model (Bowman, 1973; Gaimard, 1991). The study enables more rigorous validation of transport simulation codes used in accelerator design and radiation shielding (such as PHITS, MARS, and MCNPX) by demonstrating that proper treatment of detection efficiency corrections is essential when comparing dynamical model predictions to experimental data. The results also highlight the need for better understanding of the de-excitation (evaporation) stage of nuclear collisions and suggest that measured momentum distributions may provide important constraints on transport quantities such as the symmetry energy term of the nuclear equation of state and in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections.
