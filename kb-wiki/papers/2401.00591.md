# Magnetic dipole $γ$-ray strength functions of heavy nuclei in the configuration-interaction shell model

> A low-energy enhancement (LEE) has been observed in the deexcitation $γ$-ray strength function ($γ$SF) of compound nuclei. The LEE has been a subject of intense experimental and theoretical interest since its discovery, and, if the LEE persists in heavy neutron-rich nuclei, it would have significant effects on calculations of r-process nucleosynthesis. Standard configuration-interaction (CI) shell-model calculations in medium-mass nuclei have attributed the LEE to the magnetic dipole $γ$SF but such calculations are computationally intractable in heavy nuclei. We review a combination of beyond-mean-field many-body methods within the framework of the CI shell model that enables the calculation of $γ$SF in heavy nuclei, and discuss the recent theoretical identification of a LEE in the magnetic dipole $γ$SF of lanthanide isotopes.

## Key claim

The paper reviews a combination of beyond-mean-field many-body methods (shell-model Monte Carlo and static-path approximation plus random-phase approximation) that enables the calculation of gamma-ray strength functions in heavy nuclei, and reports the first theoretical identification of a low-energy enhancement in the magnetic dipole gamma-ray strength function of lanthanide isotopes, with the caveat that the LEE is observed specifically at temperatures corresponding to neutron resonance energies and its strength varies with neutron number.

## Method

The authors combine the shell-model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method, which calculates imaginary-time response functions in model spaces too large for conventional diagonalization, with the maximum entropy method for analytic continuation to real time. The prior strength function required for the maximum entropy method is provided by either the static-path approximation plus random-phase approximation (SPA+RPA) or the SPA alone, where the SPA+RPA includes large-amplitude static fluctuations of auxiliary fields plus small time-dependent fluctuations around each static configuration. The method is applied to calculate M1 gamma-ray strength functions for isotopic chains of samarium and neodymium nuclei using a valence model space of the 50-82 shell plus the 1f7/2 orbital for protons and the 82-126 shell plus the 0h11/2 and 1g9/2 orbitals for neutrons.

## Key numbers

- Temperature of T = 0.22 MeV used for the imaginary-time M1 response function calculations in samarium isotopes.
- A peak observed around 6 MeV in the M1 gamma-ray strength function near the ground state, interpreted as the spin-flip mode.
- A structure around 1-3 MeV in the ground-state M1 strength function, consistent with the scissors mode in heavy deformed nuclei.
- A pronounced peak at omega approximately 0 MeV at temperatures corresponding to neutron resonance energies, which leads to a low-energy enhancement in gamma-ray decay.
- A small peak at omega approximately 2 MeV in the resonance-energy M1 strength function, interpreted as a scissors mode built on top of excited states.
- The LEE in the deexcitation strength function f_M1 follows an exponential decay approximately e^{-E_gamma/T_B}, with fitted T_B values approximately constant over a range of initial energies.

## Context

This work extends prior configuration-interaction shell-model calculations that attributed the low-energy enhancement to the magnetic dipole gamma-ray strength function in medium-mass nuclei (Schwengner 2013, Brown 2014, Karampagia 2017, Schwengner 2017, Midtbo 2018) to heavy lanthanide nuclei where conventional diagonalization methods are computationally intractable. The method enables microscopic calculations of gamma-ray strength functions in heavy nuclei, which is necessary for comparing with experimental observations of the LEE in rare-earth nuclei (Simon 2016, Naqvi 2019, Guttormsen 2022) and for assessing its impact on r-process nucleosynthesis calculations. Future work requires calculation of the E1 gamma-ray strength function for detailed comparison with Oslo method experiments that cannot separate E1 and M1 components.
