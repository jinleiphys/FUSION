# Wave-function-based emulation for nucleon-nucleon scattering in momentum space

> Emulators for low-energy nuclear physics can provide fast & accurate predictions of bound-state and scattering observables for applications that require repeated calculations with different parameters, such as Bayesian uncertainty quantification. In this paper, we extend a scattering emulator based on the Kohn variational principle (KVP) to momentum space (including coupled channels) with arbitrary boundary conditions, which enable the mitigation of spurious singularities known as Kohn anomalies. We test it on a modern chiral nucleon-nucleon (NN) interaction, including emulation of the coupled channels. We provide comparisons between a Lippmann-Schwinger equation emulator and our KVP momentum-space emulator for a representative set of neutron-proton (np) scattering observables, and also introduce a quasi-spline-based approach for the KVP-based emulator. Our findings show that while there are some trade-offs between accuracy and speed, all three emulators perform well. Self-contained Jupyter notebooks that generate the results and figures in this paper are publicly available.

## Key claim

The paper extends a scattering emulator based on the Kohn variational principle (KVP) to momentum space with coupled channels and arbitrary boundary conditions, demonstrating that while there are trade-offs between accuracy and speed, all three tested emulators (KVP standard method, KVP quasi-spline method, and Lippmann-Schwinger equation emulator) perform well for nucleon-nucleon scattering observables.

## Method

The authors build a reduced-order model emulator by constructing trial scattering wave functions as linear combinations of high-fidelity solutions (snapshots) to the partial-wave Schrodinger equation at selected training parameter values, then applying the constrained Kohn variational principle to obtain stationary approximations to scattering matrices. The emulator is implemented in momentum space using the reactance (K) matrix formalism, with matrix elements evaluated either by direct numerical integration (standard method) or by a global Glockle spline interpolation method. The emulator is tested on the Reinert et al. semilocal momentum-space (SMS) chiral potential at N4LO+ with cutoff 450 MeV, varying the contact low-energy constants (LECs) as parameters.

## Key numbers

- Basis size of nbasis = 2 * nlecs, where nlecs is the number of LECs per partial wave (nlecs = 3 for 1S0, nlecs = 6 for 3S1-3D1)
- Total of N = 80 momentum quadrature points used for the high-fidelity simulator, split across three segments (0-3 fm^-1, 3-6 fm^-1, 6-infinity fm^-1)
- Laboratory energy range of 0.1 to 350 MeV with 350 points for observables and 3500 points for partial wave plots
- Relative error for KVP standard method approximately 10^-12 for 1S0 phase shifts
- Relative error for KVP Glockle method approximately 10^-6 to 10^-5 for 1S0 phase shifts
- Relative error for NVP emulator approximately 10^-13 for 1S0 phase shifts
- Simulator accuracy tested against analytical Gaussian separable potential produced relative errors of approximately 10^-7 or better
- Simulator speed roughly 4 times slower when doubling mesh from N=80 to N=160 quadrature points

## Context

This work extends previous explorations of emulators for nucleon-nucleon scattering by Furnstahl (2020), Drischler (2021), and Melendez (2021) from coordinate-space wave functions to momentum-space wave functions and coupled channels. The KVP momentum-space emulator is compared to the Lippmann-Schwinger equation emulator using the Newton variational principle introduced by Melendez (2021). The approach enables fast Bayesian uncertainty quantification for chiral effective field theory Hamiltonians, where repeated calculations with different parameter values are needed, and builds on the reduced basis method (also known as eigenvector continuation in nuclear theory) developed by Frame (2017) and Konig (2019).
