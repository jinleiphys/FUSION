# Structure of and E2 transition in $^{16}$C in a $^{14}$C+n+n model

> A three-body model of $^{14}$C+n+n is applied to study the energy spectrum and the hindered $E$2 transition in $^{16}$C. A realistic two-nucleon potential is used for the valence neutrons. Both spin singlet and triplet components for the neutrons are taken into account. The three-body problem with a Pauli constraint is solved in a stochastic variational method. For the n-$^{14}$C potential chosen to reproduce the properties of $^{15}$C, the low-lying energy spectrum agrees reasonably well with experiment, but the ground state is predicted to be about 1 MeV high. The calculated $B(E2$; $2_1^+ \to 0^+_1)$ value is about twice the measured value if the polarization charge of the valence neutrons is taken to be the same as that required to fit the $^{15}$C data. The correlated motion of the valence neutrons is displayed through the two-neutron density distribution.

## Key claim

The three-body model of ¹⁴C+n+n reproduces the low-lying energy spectrum of ¹⁶C reasonably well, but the ground state is predicted about 1 MeV too high, and the calculated B(E2; 2₁⁺ → 0₁⁺) value is about twice the measured value when the polarization charge of the valence neutrons is taken to be the same as that required to fit the ¹⁵C data.

## Method

The authors solved the three-body problem of ¹⁴C plus two valence neutrons using a stochastic variational method with correlated Gaussian basis functions, imposing a Pauli constraint to exclude occupied orbits in the ¹⁴C core. A realistic two-nucleon potential (G3RS) was used for the neutron-neutron interaction, and three different n-¹⁴C potentials (sets A, B, C) were tested, all fitted to reproduce the ½₁⁺ and ⁵⁄₂₁⁺ states of ¹⁵C. Both spin-singlet and spin-triplet components of the valence neutrons were included, and the E2 transition operator was evaluated with a polarization charge δ determined from the ¹⁵C data.

## Key numbers

- Ground-state energy of ¹⁶C calculated as −4.40 MeV (set B) versus experimental −5.469 MeV, a discrepancy of about 1 MeV.
- Experimental B(E2; 2₁⁺ → 0₁⁺) in ¹⁶C is 0.63 ± 0.12 e² fm⁴ (about 0.26 W.u.).
- Calculated B(E2) value with δ = 0.159 (set B) is 1.25 e² fm⁴, about twice the measured value.
- For purely spin-singlet wave functions (a = b = 0), the B(E2) would be 1.51 e² fm⁴; the S = 1 admixture (a² = 0.08, b² = 0.25) reduces it to 1.25 e² fm⁴.
- The B(E2; ⁵⁄₂₁⁺ → ½₁⁺) in ¹⁵C is 0.97 ± 0.02 e² fm⁴ (0.44 W.u.).
- The B(E2) value of ¹⁴C is 3.74 e² fm⁴ (1.87 W.u.).
- Excitation energy of the 2₁⁺ state in ¹⁶C is 1.77 MeV, compared to 7.01 MeV in ¹⁴C.
- Point nucleon radius r(¹⁶C) for the ground state is 2.63 fm (set B).
- Spin-singlet probability P_{S=0} is 0.92 for the 0₁⁺ state and 0.75 for the 2₁⁺ state.

## Context

This work extends the previous three-body model of Horiuchi and Suzuki (2005) by including both spin-singlet and spin-triplet components and using a realistic nucleon-nucleon potential instead of an effective interaction. The study addresses the anomalously small B(E2) value in ¹⁶C, which deviates from the gradual trend seen in ¹⁰C, ¹²C, and ¹⁴C; the ¹⁶C value is much smaller than that of ¹⁴C despite having a much lower excitation energy. The results suggest that core distortion or excitation beyond the ¹⁴C+n+n model may be needed to fully explain the hindered transition, as the calculation overestimates the B(E2) by a factor of about two even with the most complete treatment within the model.
