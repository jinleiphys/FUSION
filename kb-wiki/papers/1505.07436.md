# On the compactness of neutron stars

> Recent progress in the determination of both masses and radii of neutron stars are starting to place stringent constraints on the dense matter equation of state. In particular, new theoretical developments together with improved statistical tools seem to favor stellar radii that are significantly smaller than those predicted by models using purely nucleonic equations of state. Given that the underlying equation of state must also account for the observation of $2M_{\odot}$ neutron stars, theoretical approaches to the study of the dense matter equation of state are facing serious challenges. In response to this challenge, we compute in a model-independent way the underlying equation of state associated with an assumed mass-radius template similar to the "common radius" assumption used in recent studies. Once such a mass-radius template is adopted, the equation of state follows directly from the implementation of Lindblom's algorithm; assumptions on the nature or composition of the dense stellar core are not required. By analyzing mass-radius profiles with a maximum mass consistent with observation and common radii in the 8 to 11 km range, a lower limit on the stellar radius of a $1.4M_{\odot}$ neutron star of $R_{\rm NS}\!\gtrsim\!10.7$ km is obtained in order to prevent the equation of state from violating causality.

## Key claim

By assuming a "common radius" mass-radius template and applying Lindblom's inversion algorithm, the authors derive a model-independent lower limit on the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star of about 10.7 km, required to prevent the underlying equation of state from violating causality while still supporting a 2 solar mass neutron star.

## Method

The authors adopt a mass-radius template based on their FSUGarnet relativistic density functional, then generate additional profiles (R8–R11) by shifting the template to common radii of 8–11 km for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star. They apply Lindblom's algorithm, which inverts the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to extract the unique equation of state from each assumed mass-radius relation, without any assumptions about the composition of the stellar core. They then compute the speed of sound for each extracted equation of state to determine the maximum mass that can be supported before the equation of state becomes superluminal (acausal).

## Key numbers

- Lower limit on the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: \(R_{\rm NS}\gtrsim 10.7\) km, required to support a 2 solar mass neutron star with a causal equation of state.
- Critical masses for the R8–R11 templates: \(M_{\rm crit}/M_{\odot} = 2.06\) (R11), 1.83 (R10), 1.57 (R9), 1.26 (R8).
- The microscopic model of Wiringa, Fiks, and Fabrocini violates causality beyond \(M_{\rm crit}\approx 1.96 M_{\odot}\).
- FSUGarnet predicts: \(M_{\rm max} = (2.07\pm0.02) M_{\odot}\) and \(R_{1.4} = (13.0\pm0.1)\) km.
- FSUGarnet predicts a pressure for pure neutron matter at saturation density of \(P_{\rm PNM} = (2.60\pm0.08)\) MeV/fm\(^3\).
- The common-radius value from Guillot and collaborators was revised to \(R_{\rm NS} = (9.4\pm1.2)\) km.

## Context

This work extends the inverse-stellar-structure approach of Lindblom (1992) to test the "common radius" hypothesis from Guillot et al. (2013, 2014), which suggested neutron star radii as low as 9–11 km. The authors find that such small radii are difficult to reconcile with the observed 2 solar mass neutron stars from Demorest et al. (2010) and Antoniadis et al. (2013) without violating causality. The derived 10.7 km lower limit is consistent with the upper limit of Guillot et al. (2014) and fits within the \(10.8^{+0.5}_{-0.4}\) km range from Ozel et al. (2015), enabling future observational-theoretical synergy to further constrain the dense matter equation of state.
