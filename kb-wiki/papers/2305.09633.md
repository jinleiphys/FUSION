# Chiral perturbation theory of the hyperfine splitting in (muonic) hydrogen

> The ongoing experimental efforts to measure the hyperfine transition in muonic hydrogen prompt an accurate evaluation of the proton-structure effects. At the leading order in $α$, which is $O(α^5)$ in the hyperfine splitting (hfs), these effects are usually evaluated in a data-driven fashion, using the empirical information on the proton electromagnetic form factors and spin structure functions. Here we perform a first calculation based on the baryon chiral perturbation theory (B$χ$PT). At leading orders it provides a prediction for the proton polarizability effects in hydrogen (H) and muonic hydrogen ($μ$H). We find large cancellations among the various contributions leading to, within the uncertainties, a zero polarizability effect at leading order in the B$χ$PT expansion. This result is in significant disagreement with the current data-driven evaluations. The small polarizability effect implies a smaller Zemach radius $R_\mathrm{Z}$, if one uses the well-known experimental $1S$ hfs in H or the $2S$ hfs in $μ$H. We, respectively, obtain $R_\mathrm{Z}(\mathrm{H}) = 1.010(9)$ fm, $R_\mathrm{Z}(μ\mathrm{H}) = 1.040(33)$ fm. The total proton-structure effect to the hfs at $O(α^5)$ is then consistent with previous evaluations; the discrepancy in the polarizability is compensated by the smaller Zemach radius. Our recommended value for the $1S$ hfs in $μ\text{H}$ is $182.640(18)\,\mathrm{meV}.$

## Key claim

The leading-order baryon chiral perturbation theory (BχPT) prediction for the proton polarizability effect in the hyperfine splitting of hydrogen and muonic hydrogen is effectively zero within uncertainties, in significant disagreement with current data-driven dispersive evaluations; however, when combined with a correspondingly smaller extracted Zemach radius, the total proton-structure effect at O(α⁵) becomes consistent with previous evaluations.

## Method

The authors performed a first calculation of the proton polarizability contribution to the hyperfine splitting in hydrogen (H) and muonic hydrogen (μH) using manifestly Lorentz-invariant baryon chiral perturbation theory (BχPT) at leading order (O(p³) in the chiral power counting). They computed the spin-dependent non-Born forward doubly-virtual Compton scattering amplitudes, S₁ and S₂, from one-loop πN diagrams, and introduced a novel decomposition into contributions from longitudinal-transverse (σLT) and helicity-difference (σTT) photoabsorption cross sections to improve convergence and uncertainty estimation. The polarizability effect was evaluated by integrating these amplitudes over the full momentum range, with a 30% uncertainty assigned based on the high-momentum cutoff dependence of the σLT and σTT contributions.

## Key numbers

- LO BχPT polarizability effect in 1S H hfs: 0.69(2.03) peV
- LO BχPT polarizability effect in 1S μH hfs: 6.8(11.4) μeV
- Extracted Zemach radius from H 1S hfs: RZ(H) = 1.010(9) fm
- Extracted Zemach radius from μH 2S hfs: RZ(μH) = 1.040(33) fm
- Recommended value for 1S hfs in μH: 182.640(18) meV
- Previously extracted Zemach radius from μH 2S hfs (data-driven): RZ(μH) = 1.082(37) fm
- Previously evaluated data-driven polarizability effect in μH 2S hfs: 8.0(2.6) μeV
- Difference in Wilson coefficient between μH and H polarizability (BχPT): Δc₄ = 0.09(0.46)
- Difference in Wilson coefficient between μH and H polarizability (data-driven): Δc₄ = -0.27(1.53)

## Context

This work extends the successful BχPT approach previously applied to the μH Lamb shift polarizability effect (Alarcon 2013) to the hyperfine splitting, providing a model-independent alternative to the data-driven dispersive evaluations of Carlson (2008), Faustov (2006), and Tomalak (2018). The results contradict the conventional dispersive evaluations that rely on empirical spin structure functions g₁ and g₂, suggesting that cancellations between σLT and σTT contributions and between elastic and inelastic low-Q² contributions may be underestimated in the data-driven approach. The small polarizability effect enables a more precise extraction of the Zemach radius from upcoming ppm-precision measurements of the 1S hfs in μH by the CREMA, FAMU, and J-PARC collaborations, and allows for combined analyses of H and μH data to disentangle Zemach radius and polarizability contributions as proposed by Antognini (2022).
