# Charge radii and structural evolution in Sr, Zr, and Mo isotopes

> The evolution of the ground-state nuclear shapes in neutron-rich Sr, Zr, and Mo isotopes, including both even-even and odd-A nuclei, is studied within a self-consistent mean-field approximation based on the D1S Gogny interaction. Neutron separation energies and charge radii are calculated and compared with available data. A correlation between a shape transition and a discontinuity in those observables is found microscopically. While in Sr and Zr isotopes the steep behavior observed in the isotopic dependence of the charge radii is a consequence of a sharp prolate-oblate transition, the smooth behavior found in Mo isotopes has its origin in an emergent region of triaxiality.

## Key claim

The steep increase in charge radii observed in Sr and Zr isotopes near neutron number N=60 is microscopically correlated with a sharp prolate-oblate shape transition, whereas the smoother charge-radius evolution in Mo isotopes originates from an emergent region of triaxiality; however, the exact neutron number at which the transition occurs depends on subtle energy differences (less than 0.5 MeV) between nearly degenerate oblate and prolate minima.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using the finite-range, density-dependent Gogny D1S interaction, expanding wave functions in a harmonic oscillator basis. They computed one-neutron and two-neutron separation energies and charge radii for even-even and odd-A Sr, Zr, and Mo isotopes from N=50 to N=68, using the equal filling approximation for odd-A nuclei. Constrained calculations generated potential energy curves (axial) and Q-gamma potential energy surfaces (triaxial) to map shape evolution.

## Key numbers

- The charge radius jump in Sr isotopes occurs experimentally between N=59 and N=60, while the calculation places it between N=58 and N=59; the oblate and prolate minima at N=59 in Sr are separated by less than 0.5 MeV.
- In Zr isotopes, the experimental charge radius jump occurs between N=59 and N=60, while the calculation places it between N=60 and N=61; the oblate and prolate minima at N=60 in Zr are separated by 0.2 MeV.
- For Sr and Zr isotopes at N=60, the transition involves a change from oblate deformations with Q ~ 2 barns to prolate deformations with Q ~ 5 barns.
- The prolate deformation parameter beta is approximately 0.4 for Sr isotopes at N=60, while the oblate deformation is approximately -0.2.
- The charge radius correction from the finite size of the proton is taken as 0.80 fm^2, and from the neutron as -0.12 fm^2.

## Context

This work extends previous mean-field studies of shape transitions in the A=100-110 region, including calculations by Moller et al. (1995) using the finite-range droplet model, Skalski et al. using Woods-Saxon potentials, and relativistic mean-field calculations by Lalazissis et al. It also complements large-scale studies of quadrupole correlation energies by Bender et al. (2006) and Delaroche et al. using both Skyrme and Gogny interactions. The results demonstrate that charge radii are more sensitive indicators of shape transitions than separation energies in regions of shape coexistence, and that triaxial degrees of freedom are essential to reproduce the smooth behavior in Mo isotopes, enabling predictions for unexplored neutron-rich isotopes.
