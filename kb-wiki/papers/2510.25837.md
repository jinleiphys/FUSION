# Deriving a parton shower for jet thermalization in QCD plasmas

> Jet quenching - the modification of high-energy jets in the quark-gluon plasma - has been extensively studied through weakly coupled scattering amplitudes embedded in parton-shower frameworks. These models, often combined with bulk hydrodynamic evolution, successfully describe a wide range of observables, though they typically rely on assumptions of rapid thermalization and simplified treatments of medium response. Parallel to these developments, jet thermalization has been investigated within the finite-temperature QCD effective kinetic theory, which provides our best microscopic understanding of equilibration in heavy-ion collisions. Early studies of linearized perturbations have highlighted both the promise and the limitations of current approaches, as existing MC implementations face challenges - particularly in the treatment of recoils and particle merging. Building on this foundation, we introduce a new parton-shower algorithm that exactly reproduces the dynamics of the linearized EKT, enabling a first-principles description of jet thermalization with proper inclusion of recoils, holes, quantum statistics, and merging processes.

## Key claim
The authors introduce a new parton-shower Monte Carlo algorithm that exactly reproduces the dynamics of the linearized QCD effective kinetic theory (EKT), enabling a first-principles description of jet thermalization with proper inclusion of recoils, holes, quantum statistics, and merging processes, going beyond previous approximate treatments that assumed rapid thermalization or simplified medium response.

## Method
The authors reformulate the linearized Boltzmann equation of finite-temperature QCD effective kinetic theory as a parton-shower evolution equation, where the no-collision probability (Sudakov factor) and real-collision terms include splitting, merging, recoil, and hole particles. The algorithm samples next splitting times from the Sudakov factor, selects among competing channels (splitting vs. merging) in proportion to their rates, and iterates for all new particles until the propagation length is exceeded, with hole particles tracked via sign labels.

## Key numbers
- Single 100 GeV gluon initial condition used for high-energy limit demonstration
- Infrared cutoff of E_min = 1 GeV used for the full EKT evolution (lower than the typical ~5 GeV cutoff in phenomenological applications)
- Propagation lengths up to L = 2 fm shown for two-particle correlation results
- Asymptotic late-time distribution given by δf_eq(p,x) = δT ∂_T n_B(p) with p_0/V = (4π²/30)T³δT

## Context
This work extends the finite-temperature QCD effective kinetic theory (EKT) framework of Arnold, Moore, and Yaffe (2002) and the linearized perturbation studies by Schlichting and Soudi (2020) and Zhou et al. (2024). It addresses limitations of existing Monte Carlo implementations such as Zapp et al. (2008), Auvinen et al. (2009), and Schenke et al. (2009), which used approximate treatments of recoils and holes, neglected 2→1 merging processes, and omitted quantum statistical effects. The algorithm enables first-principles coupling of jet evolution to hydrodynamic backgrounds and provides access to multi-particle correlations beyond the molecular chaos assumption.
