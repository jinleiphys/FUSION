# Theoretical analysis of a possible observation of the chiral magnetic effect in Au + Au collisions within the RHIC beam energy scan program

> In terms of the hadron-string-dynamics (HSD) approach we investigate the correlation function in the azimuthal angle $ψ$ of charged hadrons that is expected to be sensitive to a signal of local strong parity violation. Our analysis of Au+Au collisions is based on the recent STAR data within the RHIC Beam-Energy-Scan (BES) program. The HSD model reasonably reproduces STAR data for $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=$7.7 GeV, while there are some deviations from the experiment at the collision energy of 11.5 GeV and an increase of deviations between theory and experiment at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=$39 GeV. For reference, the results for $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=$ 200 GeV are given as well. The role of the retarded electromagnetic field is discussed and a compensation effect for the action of its electric and magnetic components is pointed out. We conclude that the recent RHIC BES data at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=$7.7 and 11.5 GeV can be understood on the hadronic level without involving the idea of a strong parity violation; however, at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}\sim$40 GeV and above one needs to take into consideration explicit partonic (quark-qluon) degrees-of-freedom for a proper treatment of the dynamics.

# Theoretical analysis of a possible observation of the chiral magnetic effect in Au + Au collisions within the RHIC beam energy scan program

## Key claim

The HSD transport model reproduces the STAR azimuthal correlation data at 7.7 GeV and reasonably at 11.5 GeV without invoking parity violation, but fails at 39 GeV and above, indicating that partonic degrees of freedom become essential for a proper description at higher energies; the authors conclude that the low-energy BES data can be understood on a purely hadronic level, leaving no room for the chiral magnetic effect at those energies.

## Method

The authors used the Hadron-String-Dynamics (HSD) transport approach, which treats nuclear collisions in terms of quasiparticles with finite width beyond the on-shell Boltzmann equation, to calculate the two-body azimuthal correlator ⟨cos(ψα+ψβ−2ΨRP)⟩ for charged pions in Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 7.7, 11.5, 39, and 200 GeV. They compared these calculations to preliminary STAR Beam-Energy-Scan data and also extended HSD to include retarded electromagnetic fields solved from Maxwell equations in vacuum, studying the time evolution of the magnetic and electric fields and their back-reaction on particle propagation.

## Key numbers

- At √sNN = 7.7 GeV, the HSD results for oppositely and same-charged pion correlations practically coincide and show a large enhancement in very peripheral collisions, with the centrality distributions well reproduced by the calculations.
- At √sNN = 11.5 GeV, the calculated value at the maximum (centrality 70%) decreases by a factor of 3 compared to 7.7 GeV; experimental points at this large centrality are not available.
- At √sNN = 39 GeV, the measured same-charged and oppositely charged pion lines are clearly separated (positive for same-charged, negative for oppositely charged) but strongly suppressed; the HSD model cannot describe this picture.
- The maximal magnetic field at √sNN = 7.7 GeV for impact parameter b = 10 fm is about 0.13 mπ², which is about an order of magnitude lower than at √sNN = 200 GeV, but persists about 20 times longer.
- The statistics of calculated events range from 3×10³ events at 5% centrality to 600×10³ events at 70% centrality, depending on energy.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous HSD-based study of the chiral magnetic effect at top RHIC energy (Toneev et al., 2010) and tests the prediction from a phenomenological model (Toneev and Voronyuk, 2010) that the CME should disappear below √sNN ∼ 20 GeV. The results contradict interpretations that the STAR correlation data at all BES energies require parity violation, and instead support the need for partonic dynamics as implemented in the Parton-Hadron-String-Dynamics (PHSD) approach for energies above 40 GeV. The compensation effect between transverse electric and magnetic field components identified here challenges astrophysical claims that strong magnetic field effects observed in other contexts should also appear in heavy-ion collisions.
