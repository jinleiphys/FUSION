# Elliptic Flow and HBT in non-central Nuclear Collisions

> Elliptic flow and HBT are studied for non-central relativistic nuclear collisions. Azimuthal asymmetries show up in both elliptic flow and HBT radii and are calculated in both collisionless and hydrodynamic limits relevant for peripheral and central collisions respectively. Determining the reaction plane and measuring the HBT radii as function of the angle between the reaction plane and the particle momenta can determine the physical quantities as source sizes, deformations, emission times, duration of emission and opacities. Comparison to SPS data and predictions for RHIC and LHC energies are given. The centrality dependence with and without a phase transition to a quark-gluon plasma is discussed - in particular, how the physical quantities are expected to display a qualitative different behavior in case of a phase transition.

## Key claim

Elliptic flow and HBT radii both exhibit azimuthal asymmetries in non-central relativistic nuclear collisions, and a combined analysis can determine source sizes, deformations, emission times, duration of emission, and opacities; the centrality dependence of these quantities is expected to show qualitatively different behavior if a phase transition to a quark-gluon plasma occurs.

## Method

The authors derive analytical formulas for elliptic flow in both the collisionless (free-streaming) limit, using the Boltzmann equation with a first-order correction from particle collisions, and the hydrodynamic limit, assuming equipotential transverse flow on a deformed Gaussian source. HBT radii are calculated for deformed sources in both transparent and opaque limits, where opacity is modeled via Glauber absorption with a mean free path parameter. The results are compared to SPS data from the NA49 experiment for semicentral Pb+Pb collisions.

## Key numbers

* Elliptic flow parameter v2 for protons at SPS energies (b ~ R ~ 7 fm) is v2 ~ 0.1 or less, with pions having smaller values.
* In the hydrodynamic fit to proton elliptic flow, the deformation at freeze-out is delta ~ 0.07, a factor ~6 smaller than the initial deformation of the collision zone.
* The average transverse flow squared from apparent temperature slopes is <u_perp^2> ~ 0.15 c^2 for central S+S collisions and ~ 0.3 c^2 for central Pb+Pb collisions at SPS energies.
* For the hydrodynamic flow parametrization with two transverse velocities, the difference <u_x^2> - <u_y^2> ~ 0.015, an order of magnitude smaller than the average transverse flow squared (~0.15).
* The transport cross sections used are sigma^{pi N} ~ 30 mb and sigma^{NN} ~ 40 mb, with transport cross sections taken as half the total cross section.
* The rapidity density of nucleons from NA49 is dN_N/dy ~ 30 for semicentral Pb+Pb collisions.
* For b = R, the initial Gaussian radii are R_x ~ 2 fm, R_y ~ 3 fm, and deformation delta ~ 0.4.
* The apparent temperature formula is T_app ~ T + (1/2) m <u_perp^2> for large particle masses m and small transverse flow.
* For opaque sources with lambda_mfp/R = 1, numerical calculation gives g_s ~ 1.4 and g_o ~ 0.9; for a black-body disk source, g_s = 4/3 and g_o ~ 0.2.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of directed and elliptic flow using hydrodynamics by Ollitrault (1992) and the cascade code RQMD, which had been compared to AGS and SPS data with some success but could not explain the transverse momentum and rapidity dependences for pions and protons. The authors provide simple analytical formulas that complement numerical approaches and enable a combined analysis of HBT and anisotropic flow to extract source properties at freeze-out. The results enable predictions for RHIC and LHC energies and provide a framework for detecting a phase transition to a quark-gluon plasma through the centrality dependence of quantities such as the ratio of elliptic flow to deformation and the duration of emission.
