# On model emulation and closure tests for 3+1D relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> In nuclear and particle physics, reconciling sophisticated simulations with experimental data is vital for understanding complex systems like the Quark Gluon Plasma (QGP) generated in heavy-ion collisions. However, computational demands pose challenges, motivating using Gaussian Process emulators for efficient parameter extraction via Bayesian calibration. We conduct a comparative analysis of Gaussian Process emulators in heavy-ion physics to identify the most adept emulator for parameter extraction with minimal uncertainty. Our study contributes to advancing computational techniques in heavy-ion physics, enhancing our ability to interpret experimental data and understand QGP properties.

## Key claim

The paper claims that Gaussian Process emulators using principal component analysis (PCGP and PCSK methods from the BAND collaboration's `surmise` package) provide more accurate predictions and better uncertainty estimation for 3+1D relativistic heavy-ion collision simulations than the standard Scikit-learn GP emulator, but this conclusion is conditional on the specific training setup and observable groups tested, and the Scikit GP's poorer performance may be due to not having optimized its kernel and parameters.

## Method

The authors trained three types of Gaussian Process emulators (PCGP, PCSK, and Scikit GP) on 970 design points from a 20-dimensional parameter space, using event-by-event simulations of Au+Au collisions at 7.7, 19.6, and 200 GeV from the iEBE-MUSIC framework. They validated emulator accuracy using the remaining 30 design points, computing the root-mean-square fractional error and a metric for uncertainty estimation quality across 544 experimental observables grouped by collision energy and observable type. The study also tested logarithmic transformation of multiplicity observables, PCA transformation of functional parameters (rapidity loss, shear viscosity, bulk viscosity), and active learning by adding 70 points from posterior distributions.

## Key numbers

- Training dataset contained 1000 design points from Maximum Projection Latin Hypercube Design, with 970 used for training and 30 for validation
- 1000 minimum bias events were simulated at 200 GeV and 2000 minimum bias events at 19.6 and 7.7 GeV for each design point
- The 20-dimensional parameter space included parameters with priors such as $B_G$ in [1,25] GeV$^{-2}$, $\eta_0$ in [0.001,0.3], and $\zeta_{\rm max}$ in [0,0.2]
- The bulk viscosity parametrization required six principal components, while shear viscosity and rapidity loss each required three principal components to explain 99 percent of variance
- For the 7.7 GeV $\langle p_{\rm T}\rangle$ dataset, the quality measure $\mathcal{H}$ decreased from 0.5 to approximately 0 for training sizes of 300 or more points
- For the 200 GeV ${\rm d}N/{\rm d}y$ dataset with 700 or more training points, Scikit GP showed almost a factor of two larger uncertainty compared to PCGP and PCSK
- Particle yield $\mathrm{d}N/\mathrm{d}y$ at 7.7 GeV showed emulator uncertainties up to 100 percent in peripheral centrality bins, while most other points were in the 2-20 percent range

## Context

This work extends previous Bayesian calibration studies in heavy-ion physics that used Gaussian Process emulators, such as those by Bernhard (2019), JETSCAPE (2020), Nijs (2020), and Shen (2023), by systematically comparing three emulator implementations. The study introduces PCA transformation for functional model parameters (rapidity loss, shear viscosity, bulk viscosity) as a more systematic alternative to the approach used in JETSCAPE (2020) where additional sampled points were fed to emulators. The findings enable more reliable parameter extraction for the RHIC Beam Energy Scan program and suggest that the BAND collaboration's `surmise` package emulators (PCGP and PCSK) are preferable for future Bayesian analyses of heavy-ion collision data.
