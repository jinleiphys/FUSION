# Uncertainty quantification for $μ\to e$ conversion in nuclei: charge distributions

> Predicting the rate for $μ\to e$ conversion in nuclei for a given set of effective operators mediating the violation of lepton flavor symmetry crucially depends on hadronic and nuclear matrix elements. In particular, the uncertainties inherent in this non-perturbative input limit the discriminating power that can be achieved among operators by studying different target isotopes. In order to quantify the associated uncertainties, as a first step, we go back to nuclear charge densities and propagate the uncertainties from electron scattering data for a range of isotopes relevant for $μ\to e$ conversion in nuclei, including $^{40,48}$Ca, $^{48,50}$Ti, and $^{27}$Al. We provide as central results Fourier-Bessel expansions of the corresponding charge distributions with complete covariance matrices, accounting for Coulomb-distortion effects in a self-consistent manner throughout the calculation. As an application, we evaluate the overlap integrals for $μ\to e$ conversion mediated by dipole operators. In combination with modern ab-initio methods, our results will allow for the evaluation of general $μ\to e$ conversion rates with quantified uncertainties.

## Key claim

The paper propagates uncertainties from electron scattering data to extract nuclear charge distributions with quantified covariance matrices for isotopes relevant to $\mu\to e$ conversion, but the authors note that the resulting uncertainties are only a first step and that the data situation for several isotopes is poor, with many original measurements lost or poorly documented, limiting the precision achievable.

## Method

The authors performed Fourier-Bessel fits to elastic electron-nucleus scattering cross sections for $^{40,48}$Ca, $^{48,50}$Ti, and $^{27}$Al, using a phase-shift analysis (distorted-wave Born approximation) that accounts for Coulomb-distortion effects by solving the Dirac equation numerically. For $^{27}$Al, which has non-zero spin, they subtracted contributions from higher Coulomb multipoles and magnetic form factors using estimates from nuclear shell-model and ab-initio in-medium similarity renormalization group calculations. The fits were performed both with and without additional constraints from muonic-atom Barrett moments, and systematic uncertainties were estimated by scanning over different numbers of Fourier-Bessel coefficients and cutoff radii.

## Key numbers

- For $^{40}$Ca and $^{48}$Ca: charge distributions extracted with quantified uncertainties from electron scattering data, with two variants (with and without Barrett-moment constraints) showing tension at small radii for $^{40}$Ca and at larger distances for $^{48}$Ca
- For $^{48}$Ti and $^{50}$Ti: charge distributions extracted with appreciably larger uncertainties than for calcium, based on relative measurements from Frosch (1968) referenced to the authors' $^{48}$Ca results
- For $^{27}$Al: charge distribution extracted from data at $E=250$ MeV and $E=500$ MeV from Li (1974), with $L>0$ corrections estimated from IMSRG calculations using two chiral Hamiltonians (DeltaN2LO_GO and N2LO_sat)
- The current experimental limit for $\mu\to e$ conversion in titanium is Br[$\mu\to e$, Ti] $< 6.1 \times 10^{-13}$ from Wintz (1998), and for gold Br[$\mu\to e$, Au] $< 7 \times 10^{-13}$ from SINDRUM-II (2006)
- The limit for $\mu\to e\gamma$ is Br[$\mu\to e\gamma$] $< 4.2 \times 10^{-13}$ from MEG (2016), and for $\mu\to 3e$ is Br[$\mu\to 3e$] $< 1.0 \times 10^{-12}$ from SINDRUM (1987)

## Context

This work extends the approach of DeVries (1987) by providing Fourier-Bessel parameterizations of nuclear charge densities with complete covariance matrices, which were absent in the original compilation. The results enable quantified uncertainty propagation for $\mu\to e$ conversion overlap integrals, particularly for dipole operators, and complement the correlation analysis between charge and neutron distributions pioneered by Hagen (2015) for $^{48}$Ca and Payne (2019) for $^{40}$Ar. In combination with modern ab-initio methods, these charge distributions will allow evaluation of general $\mu\to e$ conversion rates with quantified uncertainties, which is critical for distinguishing different beyond-Standard-Model scenarios at upcoming experiments like Mu2e and COMET.
