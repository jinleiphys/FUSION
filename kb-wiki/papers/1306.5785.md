# BCS-BEC Crossover and the Unitary Fermi Gas

> The crossover from weak coupling Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) pairing to a Bose-Einstein condensate (BEC) of tightly bound pairs, as a function of the attractive interaction in Fermi systems, has long been of interest to theoretical physicists. The past decade has seen a series of remarkable experimental developments in ultracold Fermi gases that has realized the BCS-BEC crossover in the laboratory, bringing with it fresh new insights into the very strongly interacting unitary regime in the middle of this crossover. In this review, we start with a pedagogical introduction to the crossover and then focus on recent progress in the strongly interacting regime. While our focus is on new theoretical developments, we also describe three key experiments that probe the thermodynamics, transport and spectroscopy of the unitary Fermi gas. We discuss connections between the unitary regime and other areas of physics -- quark-gluon plasmas, gauge-gravity duality and high temperature superconductivity -- and conclude with open questions about strongly interacting Fermi gases.

## Key claim

The BCS-BEC crossover is a continuum from weak-coupling BCS pairing to Bose-Einstein condensation of tightly bound pairs, with the unitary Fermi gas at the midpoint representing the most strongly interacting regime; the authors emphasize that the unitary regime has the highest ratio of superfluid transition temperature to Fermi energy ever observed in any fermionic superfluid, but note that perturbative treatments break down near unitarity where the Ginzburg critical region is of order unity.

## Method

The authors present a pedagogical review of the BCS-BEC crossover, starting with mean-field theory plus Gaussian fluctuations (the Nozieres-Schmitt-Rink approach) to provide a qualitatively correct picture across the entire crossover, then describe field-theoretical methods including renormalization group, 1/N and epsilon expansions, and quantum Monte Carlo simulations for the unitary regime where mean-field theory is quantitatively inadequate. They also discuss three key experiments probing thermodynamics, transport, and spectroscopy of the unitary Fermi gas, and connect the unitary regime to other areas including quark-gluon plasmas, gauge-gravity duality, and high-temperature superconductivity.

## Key numbers

- Superfluid transition temperature at unitarity: \(T_c/E_F \simeq 0.15 - 0.2\), the highest ratio ever observed in any fermionic superfluid
- Pairing energy gap at unitarity: \(\Delta \simeq 0.5 E_F\)
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio at unitarity: \(\eta/s \sim 0.2\), approaching a lower bound from gauge-gravity duality
- Ground state energy ratio at unitarity: \(\xi_s = E_0/(3 N E_F/5) \simeq 0.37\) (best estimate), compared to mean-field estimate of 0.59
- Chemical potential at unitarity: \(\mu = \xi_s E_F\)
- Mean-field estimate of \(T_c/E_F\) at unitarity from MFT plus Gaussian fluctuations: \(\simeq 0.2\), exceeding best numerical estimates of 0.15
- Non-interacting BEC transition temperature for dimers: \(T_c = [n/2\zeta(3/2)]^{2/3}\pi/m \simeq 0.22 E_F\)
- Dimer scattering length in BEC limit: exact four-body solution gives \(a_{dd}=0.6a\), while mean-field overestimates as \(a_{dd}^{\rm MF}=2a\)
- Critical velocity at unitarity: \(v_c \sim v_F\), with a maximum through the crossover

## Context

This review extends the foundational theoretical work on BCS-BEC crossover by Eagles (1969), Leggett (1980), and Nozieres and Schmitt-Rink (1985), and connects it to the experimental realization in ultracold Fermi gases achieved in 2004-2005 by groups including Regal et al., Zwierlein et al., Kinast et al., and Bourdel et al. The unitary Fermi gas provides a controlled laboratory system for studying strongly interacting fermions with connections to quark-gluon plasmas, high-temperature superconductivity, and gauge-gravity duality; the review enables future work on open questions including the existence of a pairing pseudogap above \(T_c\) at unitarity and the behavior of fermions in optical lattices.
