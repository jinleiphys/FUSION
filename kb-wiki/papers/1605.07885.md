# Radii and binding energies in oxygen isotopes: a puzzle for nuclear forces

> We present a systematic study of both nuclear radii and binding energies in (even) oxygen isotopes from the valley of stability to the neutron drip line. Both charge and matter radii are compared to state-of-the-art {\it ab initio} calculations along with binding energy systematics. Experimental matter radii are obtained through a complete evaluation of the available elastic proton scattering data of oxygen isotopes. We show that, in spite of a good reproduction of binding energies, {\it ab initio} calculations with conventional nuclear interactions derived within chiral effective field theory fail to provide a realistic description of charge and matter radii. A novel version of two- and three-nucleon forces leads to considerable improvement of the simultaneous description of the three observables for stable isotopes, but shows deficiencies for the most neutron-rich systems. Thus, crucial challenges related to the development of nuclear interactions remain.

## Key claim

Despite the good reproduction of binding energies in oxygen isotopes by conventional chiral effective field theory interactions (the EM interaction), these forces fail to describe charge and matter radii; a newer interaction (NNLO$_{\text{sat}}$) improves radii for stable isotopes but shows deficiencies for the most neutron-rich systems, indicating that crucial challenges remain for nuclear force development.

## Method

The authors performed a systematic reanalysis of elastic proton scattering data for oxygen isotopes (16O, 18O, 20O, 22O) using a microscopic density-dependent optical model potential (the Jeukenne-Lejeune-Mahaux potential) within the distorted wave Born approximation to extract matter radii. They compared these experimental radii and binding energies to state-of-the-art ab initio calculations using two many-body approaches (self-consistent Green's function and in-medium similarity renormalization group) with two different nuclear interactions: the standard EM chiral interaction and the newer NNLO$_{\text{sat}}$ interaction.

## Key numbers

- For 16O, the charge radius r_ch was estimated to be 2.730(25) fm and 2.737(8) fm from different analyses
- The difference in charge radius between 17O and 16O, Delta r_ch, is -0.008(7) fm; between 18O and 16O it is +0.074(8) fm
- The proton charge radius used for unfolding is 0.877(7) fm
- For 18O, the matter radius extracted from (p,p) analysis is r_m = 2.75(10) fm
- For 20O, the matter radius from (p,p) analysis is r_m = 2.9(1) fm
- For 22O, matter radii from (p,p) analysis are 2.96 fm (with Sly4 densities) and 3.03 fm (with D1S densities), with uncertainty of 0.1 fm
- Radii computed with EM are systematically smaller than those with NNLO$_{\text{sat}}$ by 0.2-0.3 fm
- EM underestimates evaluated data by about 0.3-0.4 fm for all isotopes
- NNLO$_{\text{sat}}$ shows a discrepancy of about 0.2 fm in 22O

## Context

This work extends previous studies that established the crucial role of three-nucleon forces in reproducing the neutron drip line at 24O (Otsuka 2010, Hagen 2012, Holt 2013, Hergert 2013) but could not simultaneously describe nuclear radii (Cipollone 2015). The paper tests the newer NNLO$_{\text{sat}}$ interaction (Ekstrom 2015), which includes experimental constraints from light nuclei in its optimization, against the standard EM interaction (Entem and Machleidt 2003). The results enable systematic testing of nuclear forces along complete isotopic chains and highlight the need for joint theory-experiment analyses of both binding energies and radii for reliable structure and reaction calculations.
