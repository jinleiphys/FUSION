# A comprehensive study of decay modes associated with Pb isotopes

> Decay modes in Pb isotopes within the range 176$\leq$A$\leq$266 are investigated by the calculation of half-lives using several empirical formulas. These formulas along with various theoretical treatments are first tested to reproduce experimental half-lives and known decay modes of Pb isotopes, which are in consequence applied to estimate half-lives and decay modes of unknown Pb isotopes. A comparison between $α$-decay and weak-decay from the stable to drip-line isotopes is canvassed which leads to the excellent match with experimental data and applicability of applied empirical formulas. In addition, the full chain of Pb isotopes is probed as potential daughter candidates of cluster emission from superheavy nuclei which ensued the predominant role of $^{208}$Pb and nearby isotopes in probable cluster emission.

## Key claim

The paper claims that empirical formulas for α-decay and weak-decay half-lives, when combined with Q-values from the WS4 mass model, can reliably predict decay modes and half-lives for unknown Pb isotopes across the range 176≤A≤266, but this reliability is explicitly conditioned on the formulas having been first tested against experimental data for known Pb isotopes and on the WS4 model having the lowest root-mean-square error among the considered mass models.

## Method

The authors calculated Q-values for α-decay, β⁻-decay, and electron capture (EC) using the WS4 mass model after comparing its performance against RMF variants (NL3*, TMA, FSU-Gold, FSU-Garnet), FRDM, and RCHB models. They then applied four empirical formulas (NMSF, NMHF, MTNF, QF) to compute α-decay half-lives and the Fiset-Nix formula for β⁻ and EC half-lives, using minimum angular momentum selection rules for α transitions. For cluster decay, they used the universal decay law (UDL) to compute half-lives for various cluster emissions from superheavy nuclei where Pb isotopes serve as daughter nuclei.

## Key numbers

- WS4 mass model achieved the lowest RMSE for Q-values among all tested models: 0.36 MeV for Qα, 0.29 MeV for Qβ⁻, and 0.53 MeV for QEC for Pb isotopes.
- For ¹⁷⁸Pb, the experimental α-decay half-life is log₁₀T₁/₂ = −3.92 s, with calculated values ranging from −3.31 s (MTNF) to −4.12 s (QF).
- For ²⁰⁴Pb, the experimental α-decay half-life is ≥24.64 s, while calculated values range from 28.32 s (QF) to 37.26 s (NMSF).
- For ²⁰⁹Pb, the experimental β⁻-decay half-life is log₁₀T₁/₂ = 4.07 s, with a calculated value of 5.68 s.
- For ²¹⁰Pb, the experimental β⁻-decay half-life is log₁₀T₁/₂ = 8.80 s, with a calculated value of 8.03 s.
- For ²¹⁵Pb, the experimental β⁻-decay half-life is log₁₀T₁/₂ = 2.17 s, with a calculated value of 3.00 s.
- For ¹⁸⁵Pb, the branching ratio for α-decay is 99.96% and for β⁺/EC is 0.04%; for ¹⁹²Pb, the branching ratio for α-decay is 5.50% and for β⁺/EC is 94.50%.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of α-decay in Pb isotopes using the Coulomb and proximity potential model (Santosh 2015), quantum-mechanical tunneling (Hosseini 2019), and the generalized liquid drop model (Liu 2020), as well as β-decay measurements for ²¹⁵–²¹⁸Pb beyond N=126 by Caballero-Folch (2017). The study enables systematic predictions of half-lives and dominant decay modes for all Pb isotopes from the proton drip-line to the neutron drip-line, and identifies ²⁰⁸Pb and nearby isotopes as the most favorable daughter candidates for cluster emission from superheavy nuclei, which can guide future experimental searches for cluster radioactivity.
