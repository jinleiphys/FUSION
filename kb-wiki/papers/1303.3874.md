# Neutrinos in Cosmology and Astrophysics

> We briefly review the recent developments in neutrino physics and astrophysics which have import for frontline research in nuclear physics. These developments, we argue, tie nuclear physics to exciting developments in observational cosmology and astrophysics in new ways. Moreover, the behavior of neutrinos in dense matter is itself a fundamental problem in many-body quantum mechanics, in some ways akin to well-known issues in nuclear matter and nuclei, and in some ways radically different, especially because of nonlinearity and quantum de-coherence. The self-interacting neutrino gas is the only many body system driven by the weak interactions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that neutrino flavor evolution in dense astrophysical environments (the early universe and core-collapse supernovae) is a fundamental many-body quantum mechanics problem that ties nuclear physics to observational cosmology and astrophysics, but the standard "separation of scales" approach (using Boltzmann transport at high densities and Schrodinger-like equations at low densities) fails in some cases, such as the "neutrino halo" effect.

## Method

The authors review the current state of neutrino physics and its connections to cosmology and astrophysics, focusing on how neutrino flavor evolution is treated in dense environments. They describe the need for full neutrino quantum kinetic equations (QKEs) derived from quantum field theory by Vlasenko, Fuller, and Cirigliano, which reduce to the Boltzmann equation in the high-density limit and a Schrodinger-like equation in the low-density limit. The paper surveys five key cosmological observables (CMB baryon-to-photon ratio, primordial deuterium abundance, primordial helium abundance from Silk damping, the effective number of relativistic species Neff, and the sum of neutrino masses from large-scale structure) and discusses core-collapse supernova neutrino transport, collective oscillations, and nucleosynthesis implications.

## Key numbers

- Baryon-to-photon ratio from CMB: eta approximately 6.11 x 10^-10
- Standard model prediction for Neff: 3.046 (excess over 3 from e+ e- pair annihilation into neutrino pairs near the BBN epoch)
- Future CMB experiments expected to measure Neff to 10% precision
- Future weak gravitational lensing experiments may constrain sum of neutrino masses to less than 0.1 eV
- Total energy carried by neutrinos in a core-collapse supernova: 10^53 ergs (compared to 10^51 ergs in optical and kinetic energy)
- Number of neutrinos produced in a core-collapse supernova: approximately 10^58
- Photon decoupling temperature: T_CMB approximately 0.2 eV

## Context

This review extends the discussion of neutrino flavor evolution in astrophysical environments, building on prior work by Raffelt and Sigl (1992) and Strack and Burrows (2005) on quantum kinetic equations, and by Duan (2010) on collective neutrino oscillations. The paper connects neutrino physics to Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) and r-process nucleosynthesis in core-collapse supernovae, noting that the neutrino-driven wind is a candidate r-process site (Meyer, 1992) but current hydrodynamical simulations do not reach the extreme conditions necessary for the r-process. The work enables future connections between neutrino flavor dynamics, observational cosmology (especially Planck satellite results), and nuclear astrophysics studies of element formation.
