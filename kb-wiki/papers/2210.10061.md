# Collinear Parton Dynamics Beyond DGLAP

> Renormalization group evolution equations describing the scale dependence of quantities in quantum chromodynamics (QCD) play a central role in the interpretation of experimental data. Arguably the most important evolution equations for collider physics applications are the Dokshitzer-Gribov-Lipatov-Altarelli-Parisi (DGLAP) equations, which describe the evolution of a quark or gluon fragmenting into hadrons, with only a single hadron identified at a time. In recent years, the study of the correlations of energy flow within jets has come to play a central role at collider experiments, necessitating an understanding of correlations, going beyond the standard DGLAP paradigm. In this Letter we derive a general renormalization group equation describing the collinear dynamics that account for correlations in the fragmentation. We compute the kernel of this evolution equation at next-to-leading order (NLO), where it involves the $1\to 3$ splitting functions, and develop techniques to solve it numerically. We show that our equation encompasses all previously-known collinear evolution equations, namely DGLAP and the evolution of multi-hadron fragmentation functions. As an application of our results, we consider the phenomenologically-relevant example of energy flow on charged particles, computing the energy fraction in charged particles in $e^+e^- \to$ hadrons at NNLO. Our results are an important step towards improving the understanding of the collinear dynamics of jets, with broad applications in jet substructure, ranging from the study of multi-hadron correlations, to the description of inclusive (sub)jet production, and the advancement of modern parton showers.

## Key claim

The authors derive a general renormalization group equation for collinear parton dynamics that accounts for correlations in fragmentation, going beyond the standard DGLAP paradigm which only tracks a single hadron at a time; the equation is computed at next-to-leading order (NLO) where it involves the complete structure of the 1→3 splitting functions, and the authors show it encompasses all previously-known collinear evolution equations including DGLAP and multi-hadron fragmentation functions.

## Method

The authors study the renormalization of "track functions," universal non-perturbative objects that track the energy fraction of a subset of hadrons produced in fragmentation. They compute an auxiliary jet function differential in both the energy fraction of charged particles and the mass of all particles, then integrate out angular variables in the 1→3 splitting functions using sector decomposition to handle overlapping divergences, canceling poles between one-loop 1→2 and tree-level 1→3 splitting functions. The resulting evolution kernels are computed for all partonic channels in QCD and in N=4 super-Yang-Mills theory, and numerical solution algorithms using Fourier series, moments, and wavelets are developed.

## Key numbers

- The evolution kernel for N=4 SYM at O(a^2) involves a term -25 ζ_3 a^2 T(x) (where a = α_s/(4π)) for the single-track contribution.
- The NLO evolution kernel for the 1→3 splitting in N=4 SYM contains terms with coefficients 4a (for the leading-order [1/z]_+ distribution) and 16a^2 (for the NLO corrections involving ζ_2 and logarithms).
- The initial condition for track function evolution is set at μ = 100 GeV, extracted from Pythia.
- The distribution of energy fraction w in charged particles in e+e- → hadrons is computed at LO, NLO, and NNLO, with NNLO including only logarithmic terms (not constants), showing a large reduction in scale uncertainty when NLO evolution is included.

## Context

This work extends the DGLAP framework (Gribov 1972, Dokshitzer 1977, Altarelli 1977) to include multiparton correlations in collinear fragmentation, which is needed for modern jet substructure studies at the LHC. It builds on the track function formalism introduced by Chang (2013) and the moment-based analysis by Li (2021) and Jaarsma (2022), and provides the first complete set of 1→3 collinear evolution equations. The results enable improved calculations of observables on charged particles (tracks), description of inclusive (sub)jet production for small jet radii, and advancement of next-generation parton shower algorithms.
