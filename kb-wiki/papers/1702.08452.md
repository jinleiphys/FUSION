# Cooling of neutron stars in soft X-ray transients

> Thermal states of neutron stars in soft X-ray transients (SXRTs) are thought to be determined by "deep crustal heating" in the accreted matter that drives the quiescent luminosity and cooling via emission of photons and neutrinos from the interior. In this study, we assume a global thermal steady-state of the transient system and calculate the heating curves (quiescent surface luminosity as a function of mean accretion rate) predicted from theoretical models, taking into account variations in the equations of state, superfluidity gaps, thickness of the light element layer and a phenomenological description of the direct Urca threshold. We further provide a statistical analysis on the uncertainties in these parameters, and compare the overall results with observations of several SXRTs, in particular the two sources containing the coldest (SAX J1808.4-3658) and the hottest (Aql X-1) neutron stars. Interpretation of the observational data indicates that the direct Urca process is required for the most massive stars and also suggests small superfluid gaps.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the coldest neutron stars in soft X-ray transients (SXRTs), such as SAX J1808.4-3658, cannot be explained without invoking the direct Urca process for enhanced neutrino cooling, and that the observational data favor small superfluid gaps, but these conclusions depend on the assumed equation of state, the thickness of the light-element layer, and the phenomenological treatment of the direct Urca threshold.

## Method

The authors assume a global thermal steady-state for transiently accreting neutron stars and solve the heat balance equation between deep crustal heating and cooling via photon and neutrino emission, using the NSCool code to compute heating curves (quiescent surface luminosity as a function of mean accretion rate). They vary the nuclear equation of state (APR, HHJ, SLy4, NL3), the neutron and proton superfluid gap models (including a phenomenological Gaussian parametrization), the thickness of the light-element layer in the envelope, and a phenomenological broadening/shifting of the direct Urca threshold. A Markov chain Monte Carlo statistical analysis is performed to constrain these parameters against observational data from 24 SXRTs, focusing on the coldest source SAX J1808.4-3658 and the hottest source Aql X-1.

- Direct Urca onset masses for the four equations of state: APR 2.01 Msolar, HHJ 1.87 Msolar, SLy4 2.03 Msolar, NL3 0.82 Msolar (Table II)
- Maximum masses: APR 2.18 Msolar, HHJ 2.17 Msolar, SLy4 2.05 Msolar, NL3 2.77 Msolar (Table II)
- Deep crustal heating power: L_dh ≈ 6.03 × 10^33 erg/s × (Mdot / 10^-10 Msolar/yr) × (Q / 1 MeV) (Equation 4)
- For a 2.1 Msolar APR star with direct Urca threshold lowered by beta=0.7, the quiescent surface luminosity decreases by approximately 39% compared to the original value; for HHJ the decrease is approximately 21%
- The statistical analysis finds the peak probability for the direct Urca threshold density around n_B^dU = 0.5-0.6 fm^-3 for both SLy4 and HHJ equations of state
- The analysis indicates proton singlet critical temperatures below T_cps^max ≈ 10^8 K, much lower than the (2-3) × 10^9 K values often assumed in isolated neutron star cooling studies

## Context

This work extends the "minimal cooling" paradigm of Page (2004) and the thermal evolution models of Yakovlev (2002, 2003) for accreting neutron stars, which previously assumed no direct Urca process. It directly addresses the challenge posed by Heinke (2006) who found that SAX J1808.4-3658 is much colder than expected from the minimal model, and builds on the discovery of two approximately 2 Msolar neutron stars by Demorest (2010) and Antoniadis (2013). The results enable future joint constraints on the dense matter equation of state, superfluid gaps, and the direct Urca threshold from combined observations of both cold and hot accreting neutron stars, and suggest that the proton singlet gaps inferred from accreting systems may be smaller than those derived from isolated neutron star cooling studies such as Page (2010) and Beloin (2017).
