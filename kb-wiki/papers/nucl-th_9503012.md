# Dynamical fluctuations in the one particle density - comparison of different approaches

> Diffusion coefficients are obtained from linear response functions and from the quantal fluctuation dissipation theorem. They are compared with the results of both the theory of hydrodynamic fluctuations by Landau and Lifschitz as well as the Boltzmann-Langevin theory. Sum rules related to conservation laws for total particle number, momentum and energy are demonstrated to hold true for fluctuations and diffusion coefficients in the quantum case.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that diffusion coefficients derived from the quantal fluctuation dissipation theorem (the approach of Hofmann, Kiderlen, and Tsekhmistrenko) coincide with those from Landau-Lifschitz hydrodynamic fluctuation theory and from Boltzmann-Langevin theory, but only when the equilibrium fluctuation contribution \(\tilde\sigma^{\rm st}\) is used and the additional contribution \(\Delta\sigma\) (related to conserved quantities) is either neglected or vanishes exactly.

## Method

The author derives diffusion coefficients from linear response functions and the quantal fluctuation dissipation theorem for a system described by linearized equations of motion for averages of dynamical variables. These are compared with the correlation functions of fluctuating forces from Landau-Lifschitz hydrodynamics and from Boltzmann-Langevin theory (Ayik and Grégoire 1990, Randrup and Remaud 1990). For the hydrodynamic case, the diffusion coefficients are computed both from the general formula using stationary fluctuations and directly from the fluctuating force correlation functions. For the Boltzmann-Langevin comparison, the classical limit of the fluctuation dissipation theorem is applied to the linearized Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) equation with a Markovian collision term.

## Key numbers

- The temperature range of interest for quantum effects in nuclear matter is below 15 MeV, where homogeneous nuclear matter can become mechanically unstable against isothermal density variations (the isothermal spinodal region).
- The density range investigated for quantum effects in a previous work (Kiderlen and Hofmann) was between one tenth of up to two times the saturation density.
- The diffusion coefficient in the classical limit for a local Markov collision term is given by \(\tilde d(\p_1,\r_1,\p_2,\r_2) = \delta(\r_1-\r_2)[(n^0_{p_1}\bar n^0_{p_1}/\tau_{\p_1})h^3\delta(\p_1-\p_2) - \frac{1}{2}(I_{\p_1\p_2}n^0_{p_2}\bar n^0_{p_2} + I_{\p_2\p_1}n^0_{p_1}\bar n^0_{p_1})]\).

## Context

This work extends the approach of Hofmann, Kiderlen, and Tsekhmistrenko (1994) for determining diffusion coefficients from the fluctuation dissipation theorem, and establishes its equivalence to the hydrodynamic fluctuation theory of Landau and Lifschitz (1959) and to the Boltzmann-Langevin theory of Ayik and Grégoire (1990) and Randrup and Remaud (1990). The paper shows that sum rules from conservation laws hold for the quantum case, resolving apparent violations found in earlier work by Hofmann, Kiderlen, and Tsekhmistrenko (1994). This enables the use of equilibrium-based diffusion coefficients as approximations for systems not too far from equilibrium, which is computationally more tractable than the full non-equilibrium Boltzmann-Langevin expressions for realistic six-dimensional nuclear systems.
