# Effective Potential Study of the Chiral Phase Transition in a QCD-like Theory

> We construct the effective potential for a QCD-like theory using the auxiliary field method. The chiral phase transition exhibited by the model at finite temperature and the quark chemical potential is studied from the viewpoint of the shape change of the potential near the critical point. We further generalize the effective potential so as to have quark number and scalar quark densities as independent variables near the tri-critical point.

## Key claim

The authors construct an effective potential for a QCD-like theory using the auxiliary field method and a bilocal external source field, claiming this approach allows them to study the shape change of the potential near the critical point and to generalize the potential to include quark number and scalar quark densities as independent variables near the tri-critical point. The hedge is that the method relies on a particular choice of the source field (proportional to the inverse kernel) and on the stationary-phase approximation, and the numerical results are obtained under simplifying assumptions (neglecting the vector and scalar parts of the mass function and using a covariant-like ansatz).

## Method

The authors start from the QCD-like theory, which is the renormalization-group improved ladder approximation for the Schwinger-Dyson equation of QCD, and integrate out the gluon field. They introduce a bilocal auxiliary field to make the action bilinear in the quark fields, then obtain the effective potential within the stationary-phase approximation. To study the potential away from the extremum, they introduce a bilocal external source field \(J(p,k)\) coupled quadratically to the self-energy, specifically choosing \(J(p,k) = -c K^{-1}(p,k)\) with a parameter \(c\), and solve the Schwinger-Dyson equation with this source to scan the potential shape from a trivial to a symmetry-breaking configuration. They extend the potential to finite temperature and chemical potential using the imaginary time formalism, and further generalize it via a Legendre transformation to obtain a Landau potential with the quark number density as an independent order parameter.

## Key numbers

* The pion decay constant \(f_\pi = 93\) MeV is used to fix the model parameter \(\Lambda_{\rm QCD} \simeq 776\) MeV, with the IR cutoff set at \(\ln(p_{IF}^2/\Lambda_{\rm QCD}^2) = 0.1\).
* The quark condensate in the vacuum is \(|\langle \overline{\psi}\psi \rangle|^{1/3} \simeq 236\) MeV.
* The transition point at zero chemical potential is \(T_c \simeq 114\) MeV; at zero temperature, \(\mu_c \simeq 472\) MeV.
* The tri-critical point is located at \((T_t, \mu_t) \simeq (97, 203)\) MeV.
* At \(T = 80\) MeV and \(\mu = 280\) and \(290\) MeV, two non-trivial solutions for the mass function exist, indicating a first-order transition; the transition point at \(T = 80\) MeV is \(\mu = 282\) MeV, and at \(T = 100\) MeV it is \(\mu = 187\) MeV.
* The UV cutoff is set at \(\Lambda^2/\Lambda_{\rm QCD}^2 = 2.0 \times 10^4\).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of the chiral phase transition in QCD-like theories using the Schwinger-Dyson equation and the Cornwall-Jackiw-Tomboulis (CJT) potential, as done by Bender et al. (1989, 1994) and Kondo et al. (2000). It contrasts with the CJT approach by using the auxiliary field method with a source field, which yields a local minimum rather than a saddle point, as noted by Root et al. (1991). The results enable future studies of the phase diagram with multiple order parameters, including the diquark condensate for color superconductivity, and provide a framework for investigating the QCD critical point with a finite current quark mass, as suggested by Fujii and Ohtani (2004).
