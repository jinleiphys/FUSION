# NSE abundance data

> Novel method of calculating Nuclear Statistical Equilibrium is presented. Basic equations are carefully solved using arbitrary precision arithmetic. Special interpolation procedure is then used to retrieve all abundances using tabulated results for neutrons and protons, together with basic nuclear data. Proton and neutron abundance tables, basic nuclear data and partition functions for nuclides used in calculations are provided. Simple interpolation algorithm using pre-calculated p and n abundances tabulated as a functions of kT, rho and Ye is outlined. Unique properties of this method are: (1) ability to pick-up out of NSE selected nuclei only (2) computational time scaling linearly with number of re-calculated abundances (3) relatively small amount of stored data: only two large tables (4) slightly faster than solving NSE equations using traditional Newton-Raphson methods for small networks (few tens of species); superior for huge (800-3000) networks (5) do not require initial guess; works well on random input (6) can tailored to specific application (7) ability to use third-party NSE solvers to obtain fully compatible tables (8) encapsulation of the NSE code for bug-free calculations. Range of applications for this approach is possible: coverage test of traditional NSE Newton-Raphson codes, generating starting values, code-to-code verification and possible replacement of the old legacy procedures in supernova simulations.

# NSE abundance data

## Key claim

The paper presents a method for computing Nuclear Statistical Equilibrium abundances that avoids the convergence problems of traditional Newton-Raphson solvers by pre-tabulating only proton and neutron abundances, then using interpolation on the final abundances of individual species rather than on the interpolated p and n values themselves. The method is claimed to be "slightly faster" than Newton-Raphson for small networks but "superior" for large networks of 800-3000 species, though the actual speed advantage depends on the number of species being recalculated.

## Method

The author solves the standard NSE equations (mass conservation and electron fraction conservation) using arbitrary precision arithmetic in Mathematica to generate tables of proton abundance X_p and neutron abundance X_n as functions of temperature kT, density rho, and electron fraction Y_e. For a given query point, the algorithm identifies the eight surrounding grid points in the three-dimensional parameter space, computes the abundance X_k of each desired nucleus at those eight points using the exact NSE formula with stored nuclear data (partition functions, binding energies, mass numbers), and then performs trilinear interpolation on those eight X_k values to obtain the abundance at the query point. The nuclear data includes temperature-dependent partition functions calculated directly from measured excited states and spins in the Mathematica IsotopeData database.

## Key numbers

- Temperature range covered: kT = 0.2 MeV to 1.0 MeV (approximately T = 2 x 10^9 K to 10^11 K)
- Density range covered: rho = 10^2 to 10^13 g/cm^3
- Electron fraction range covered: Y_e = 0.0 to 1.0
- To obtain all abundances above X_min = 10^-6, at least nuclides up to ^61Zn (Z=30, A=61) are required, corresponding to 621 species
- To obtain all abundances above X_min = 10^-30, at least nuclides up to ^87Nb (Z=41, A=87) are required, corresponding to 970 species
- Networks of 800 to 3000 nuclides were tested
- For Y_e = 0.5 at kT = 0.4 MeV and rho = 10^7 g/cm^3, the most primitive NSE including only protons and neutrons is "not useful" and inclusion of alpha particles still gives p and n abundances "wrong by few orders of magnitude"
- At very high temperatures above kT approximately 0.5 MeV (T_9 approximately 5.8), the solution reduces to X_n = 1 - Y_e and X_p = Y_e
- For Y_e = 0.45, the cold catalyzed matter state is pure ^58Fe; for Y_e = 0.5, it is ^56Ni

## Context

This work extends the standard NSE formalism used in astrophysical simulations, particularly addressing limitations of Newton-Raphson solvers as implemented in codes by Hix and Thielemann (1996, 1999). The method is designed for applications including pre-supernova stars after silicon burning, thermonuclear supernovae, core-collapse supernovae, and protoneutron stars. The approach enables coverage testing of traditional NSE codes, generation of starting values for Newton-Raphson solvers, code-to-code verification, and potential replacement of legacy procedures in supernova simulations. The author notes that the method can use third-party NSE solvers (such as webnucleo or the FXT NSE code) to generate compatible tables, and that results were checked against available codes and results from Seitenzahl et al. (2008) with good agreement.
