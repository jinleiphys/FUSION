# Effect of liquid drop model parameters on nuclear liquid gas phase transition

> The phenomenon of liquid-gas phase transition occurring in heavy ion collisions at intermediate energies is a subject of contemporary interest. In statistical models of fragmentation, the liquid drop model is generally used to calculate the ground state binding energies of the fragments. It is well known that the surface and symmetry energy of the hot fragments at the low density freeze out can be considerably modified. In addition to this, the level density parameter also has a wide variation. The effect of variation of these parameters is studied on fragmentation observables which are related to the nuclear liquid gas phase transition. The canonical thermodynamical model which has been very successful in describing the phenomenon of fragmentation is used for the study. The shift in transition temperature owing to the variation in liquid drop model parameters has been examined.

## Key claim

The paper systematically examines how variations in liquid drop model parameters (surface energy, symmetry energy, and level density parameter) shift the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition temperature, finding that only the surface energy coefficient has a significant effect, while the symmetry energy and level density parameter have almost no impact on the transition temperature.

## Method

The authors used the Canonical Thermodynamical Model (CTM) to calculate fragmentation of a system with mass number A0=67 and proton number Z0=32, expected from central collisions of 58Ni with 9Be. They computed the temperature derivative of total multiplicity (dM/dT) as a function of temperature for different values of the surface energy coefficient (as0), symmetry energy coefficient (Csym), and level density parameter (ε0), identifying the peak of dM/dT as the transition temperature. The surface energy was modeled with a temperature-dependent form a_s(T)=a_s0{(T_c^2-T^2)/(T_c^2+T^2)}^5/4, and the freeze-out volume was kept constant at 6V0.

## Key numbers

- Transition temperature shifts by about 2 MeV when the surface energy coefficient (a_s0) is varied from 15 to 21 MeV (Fig. 1)
- Using temperature-independent surface energy shifts the transition temperature peak by about 1 MeV to the right compared to the temperature-dependent form (Fig. 3)
- The exponent α1 in the temperature-dependent surface energy function (varied from 1.05 to 1.45) has insignificant effect on the transition temperature (Fig. 4a)
- Varying the critical temperature T_c (with α1 fixed) produces only a small shift in transition temperature to the right as T_c increases (Fig. 4b)
- Symmetry energy coefficient variation from 15 to 30 MeV has almost no effect on the phase transition for four fragmenting sources with Z0=32 and A0=67, 73, 77, and 90 (Fig. 5)
- Level density parameter ε0 variation from 8 to 16 MeV produces absolutely no shift in transition temperature; only the magnitude of the multiplicity derivative changes slightly (Fig. 6)

## Context

This work extends prior studies of nuclear liquid-gas phase transitions using statistical models such as the Canonical Thermodynamical Model (Das) and the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (Bondorf et al.), which have confirmed the transition in the temperature range of 5 to 6 MeV. The effect of surface and asymmetry terms on isotopic scaling and mean neutron-to-proton ratios had been studied in the SMM framework (Ogul et al.), but their effect on the phase transition itself had not been examined. The results establish that the surface energy term is the dominant parameter controlling the transition temperature, motivating more precise microscopic calculations of its temperature and density dependence for better characterization of the phase transition.
