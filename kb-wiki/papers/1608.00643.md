# Contrasting Freezeouts in Large Versus Small Systems

> We study the data on mean hadron yields and contrast the chemical freezeout conditions in p+p, p+Pb and Pb+Pb at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) energies. We study several schemes for freezeout that mainly differ in the way strangeness is treated: i. strangeness freezes out along with the non-strange hadrons in complete equilibrium (1CFO), ii. strangeness freezes out along with non-strange hadrons with an additional parameter $γ_S$ accounting for non-equilibrium production of strangeness (1CFO+$γ_S$), and iii. strangeness freezes out earlier than non-strange hadrons and in thermal equilibrium (2CFO). A comparison of the chisquares of the fits indicate a dependence of the freezeout scheme on the system size. The minimum bias p+p and different centralities of p+Pb and peripheral Pb+Pb data prefer 1CFO$+γ_S$ with $γ_S$ approaching unity as we go from p+p to central p+Pb and peripheral Pb+Pb. On the other hand, the mid-central to central Pb+Pb data prefer 2CFO over 1CFO+$γ_S$. Such system size dependence of freezeout scheme could be an indication of the additional interaction in Pb+Pb over p+Pb and p+p.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the preferred chemical freezeout scheme for describing hadron yields at LHC energies depends on system size: the two-freezeout (2CFO) scheme works best for large systems (Pb+Pb with mid-rapidity charged multiplicity above about 100), while the single-freezeout with strangeness non-equilibrium factor (1CFO+γ_S) works best for small systems (p+p and p+Pb). This conclusion is based on comparing fits with equal numbers of free parameters (1CFO+γ_S and 2CFO+FS), not merely on the number of fit parameters.

## Method

The authors fit a hadron resonance gas model in the grand canonical ensemble to mid-rapidity yields of pions, kaons, protons, φ mesons, Ξ baryons, Ω baryons, and Λ baryons (where available) from p+p at √s=7 TeV, p+Pb at √s=5.02 TeV, and Pb+Pb at √s=2.76 TeV across all available centralities. They compare four freezeout schemes: single freezeout in complete equilibrium (1CFO, 2 parameters), single freezeout with a strangeness non-equilibrium factor γ_S (1CFO+γ_S, 3 parameters), separate freezeout surfaces for strange and non-strange hadrons (2CFO, 4 parameters), and 2CFO with entropy conservation between the two surfaces (2CFO+FS, 3 parameters). The χ² values are compared directly between schemes with the same number of free parameters (1CFO+γ_S and 2CFO+FS) to discriminate between the physical scenarios.

## Key numbers

- For p+p min-bias: χ²/NDF = 4.7 for 1CFO+γ_S versus 9 for 2CFO+FS (both 3 parameters)
- For p+Pb 80-100% centrality: χ²/NDF = 2.7 for 1CFO+γ_S versus 7 for 2CFO+FS
- For Pb+Pb 00-10% centrality: χ²/NDF = 2.6 for 1CFO+γ_S versus 1.0 for 2CFO+FS
- For Pb+Pb 20-40% centrality: χ²/NDF = 3.1 for 1CFO+γ_S versus 0.6 for 2CFO+FS
- The freezeout temperature for strange hadrons in Pb+Pb and for all hadrons in p+p and p+Pb stays around 155-160 MeV
- Non-strange hadrons in Pb+Pb freezeout at temperatures about 10% lower than the strange freezeout surface
- The extracted γ_S in p+Pb varies from 0.95 for (10-20)% centrality to 0.80 for (80-100)% centrality
- The transition between preferred schemes occurs at dN_ch/dη approximately 100

## Context

This work extends previous studies of freezeout in small systems (Chatterjee, 2015) that found 1CFO+γ_S performs better than 2CFO for p+p collisions, and contrasts with the established success of 2CFO for Pb+Pb collisions at LHC energies (Bellwied, 2013; Chatterjee, 2015). The results suggest that the microscopic mechanism behind 2CFO (early freezeout of strangeness due to falling kaon-to-pion ratio in a cooling fireball with sufficient hadronic interactions) requires a large fireball with long lifetime, while small systems undergo sudden freezeout with all hadrons together. The extracted non-strange freezeout temperatures in Pb+Pb (around 144 MeV) agree with those from fluctuation analyses of Au+Au at RHIC (Alba, 2014; Bellwied, 2015), while the strange freezeout temperatures align with lattice QCD hadronization temperatures from strange-quark number susceptibilities (Bazavov, 2014).
