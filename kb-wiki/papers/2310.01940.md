# A new perspective on thermal transition in QCD

> Motivated by the picture of partial deconfinement developed in recent years for large-$N$ gauge theories, we propose a new way of analyzing and understanding thermal phase transition in QCD. We find nontrivial support for our proposal by analyzing the WHOT-QCD collaboration's lattice configurations for SU(3) QCD in $3+1$ spacetime dimensions with up, down, and strange quarks. We find that the Polyakov line (the holonomy matrix around a thermal time circle) is governed by the Haar-random distribution at low temperatures. The deviation from the Haar-random distribution at higher temperatures can be measured via the character expansion, or equivalently, via the expectation values of the Polyakov loop defined by the various nontrivial representations of SU(3). We find that the Polyakov loop corresponding to the fundamental representation and loops in the higher representation condense at different temperatures. This suggests that there are (at least) three phases, one intermediate phase existing in between the completely-confined and the completely-deconfined phases. Our identification of the intermediate phase is supported also by the condensation of instantons: by studying the instanton numbers of the WHOT-QCD configurations, we find that the instanton condensation occurs for temperature regimes corresponding to what we identify as the completely-confined and intermediate phases, whereas the instantons do not condense in the completely-deconfined phase. Our characterization of confinement based on the Haar-randomness explains why the Polyakov loop is a good observable to distinguish the confinement and the deconfinement phases in QCD despite the absence of the $\mathbb{Z}_3$ center symmetry.

## Key claim

The authors propose that thermal QCD exhibits three phases, complete confinement, partial deconfinement, and complete deconfinement, characterized by the deviation of the Polyakov line distribution from the Haar-random distribution, even though the Z3 center symmetry is absent due to dynamical quarks.

## Method

The authors analyzed lattice QCD configurations from the WHOT-QCD collaboration for SU(3) QCD with 2+1 flavors of dynamical quarks on a 32^3 spatial volume at seven temperatures between 174 MeV and 697 MeV, using a fixed lattice spacing of approximately 0.07 fm. They constructed the probability distribution of the Polyakov line eigenvalues across all spatial points and measured deviations from the Haar-random distribution using character expansion, computing expectation values of Polyakov loops in the fundamental, adjoint, rank-2 symmetric, and rank-3 symmetric representations. They also examined the topological charge distribution from the same configurations to study instanton condensation.

## Key numbers

*   The lattice spacing is approximately 0.07 fm, with up and down quarks heavier than physical mass (m_pi/m_rho approximately 0.63, compared to the real-world value of approximately 0.18) and approximately physical strange-quark mass.
*   The spatial volume is 32^3, yielding 32768 Polyakov line samples per configuration and 98304 eigenvalues per configuration.
*   The Polyakov loop in the fundamental representation is consistent with zero at T = 174 MeV, the lowest temperature studied.
*   The Polyakov loops in higher representations (adjoint, rank-2 symmetric, rank-3 symmetric) become nonzero at temperatures T greater than or approximately 348 MeV.
*   The topological charge distribution shows multiple peaks including nonzero Q at temperatures T less than or equal to 279 MeV, while at T = 348 MeV only a single sharp peak at Q = 0 remains with almost vanishing peaks at Q = plus or minus 1.

## Context

This work extends the framework of partial deconfinement, previously developed for large-N gauge theories by Hanada and collaborators (2016-2023), to real-world SU(3) QCD with dynamical quarks. The analysis builds on the theoretical understanding that the Polyakov line distribution reflects gauge redundancy, as established by Hanada (2020), and connects to earlier weak-coupling analyses by Sundborg (1999) and Aharony (2003) that suggested two phase transitions. The results enable a new characterization of QCD phases using Polyakov loops in various representations and instanton condensation, and suggest that the Gross-Witten-Wadia transition (1980) may have a finite-N counterpart in QCD.
