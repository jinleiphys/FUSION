# Random Matrices and Chaos in Nuclear Spectra

> We speak of chaos in quantum systems if the statistical properties of the eigenvalue spectrum coincide with predictions of random-matrix theory. Chaos is a typical feature of atomic nuclei and other self-bound Fermi systems. How can the existence of chaos be reconciled with the known dynamical features of spherical nuclei? Such nuclei are described by the shell model (a mean-field theory) plus a residual interaction. We approach the question by using a statistical approach (the two-body random ensemble): The matrix elements of the residual interaction are taken to be random variables. We show that chaos is a generic feature of the ensemble and display some of its properties, emphasizing those which differ from standard random-matrix theory. In particular, we display the existence of correlations among spectra carrying different quantum numbers. These are subject to experimental verification.

## Key claim

Chaos in nuclei (defined as agreement of spectral fluctuations with random-matrix theory) is a generic feature of the two-body random ensemble (TBRE), even though the TBRE has far fewer independent random variables than the Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE) and is not unitarily invariant. The authors show that the matrices representing the geometry and symmetries of the shell model are themselves the agents that cause chaos, for almost all choices of the two-body interaction matrix elements.

## Method

The authors analyze the nuclear shell-model Hamiltonian restricted to a single major shell (the sd-shell) with degenerate single-particle energies, so that the Hamiltonian is determined entirely by the two-body residual interaction. They introduce the two-body random ensemble (TBRE), where the reduced two-body matrix elements v(α) are taken as Gaussian-distributed random variables with zero mean and common second moment. The Hamiltonian matrix for fixed quantum numbers J (spin, isospin, parity) is then a linear combination H_μν(J) = Σ_α v(α) C_μν(J,α), where the coefficient matrices C_μν(J,α) are fixed by the shell-model geometry and are independent of the specific interaction. The spectral fluctuation properties (nearest-neighbor spacing distribution and Δ₃ statistic) of the resulting eigenvalues are compared with GOE predictions.

## Key numbers

- The nuclear data ensemble consists of 1726 spacings from slow-neutron and proton scattering data, used to determine the NNS distribution and Δ₃ statistic, showing agreement with GOE predictions.
- In the sd-shell, the number of independent two-body matrix elements α ranges from 1 to 63.
- For a single j-shell with half-integer j, the number of independent two-body matrix elements ranges from 1 to j + 1/2.
- The Hamiltonian matrix for J=2, T=0 states of 12 nucleons in the sd-shell has dimension 874; its NNS distribution and Δ₃ statistic agree well with GOE predictions.
- In ²³⁸U, neutron resonances show typical spacings of 10 eV and widths typically smaller than 1 eV, compared to single-particle s-wave level spacings around 1 MeV and decay widths around 100 keV.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of quantum chaos from few-degree-of-freedom systems (such as the Sinai billiard studied by Bohigas, Giannoni, and Schmit (1984)) to the many-body context of atomic nuclei. It addresses the apparent contradiction between the regular features of the nuclear shell model (magic numbers, rotational bands) and the chaotic spectral fluctuations observed at neutron threshold and in shell-model calculations. The TBRE was originally introduced by French and Wong (1970) and by Bohigas and Flores (1971); the present paper uses it to show that chaos is generic in the shell model and that the coefficient matrices C_μν(J,α), not the specific values of the interaction, are responsible for producing GOE-like fluctuations. This enables future theoretical work on the analytical properties of the TBRE, particularly in the limit of large j-shells, and suggests that correlations among spectra with different quantum numbers are experimentally testable.
