# Twist-averaged boundary conditions for nuclear pasta Hartree-Fock calculations

> Background: Nuclear pasta phases, present in the inner crust of neutron stars, are associated with nucleonic matter at sub-saturation densities arranged in regular shapes. Those complex phases, residing in a layer which is approximately 100 m thick, impact many features of neutron stars. Theoretical quantum-mechanical simulations of nuclear pasta are usually carried out in finite 3D boxes assuming periodic boundary conditions (PBC). The resulting solutions are affected by spurious finite-size effects. Purpose: In order to remove spurious finite-size effects, it is convenient to employ twist-averaged boundary conditions (TABC) used in condensed matter, nuclear matter, and lattice QCD applications. In this work, we study the effectiveness of TABC in the context of pasta phases simulations within nuclear density functional theory. Methods: We perform Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations in three dimensions by implementing Bloch boundary conditions. The TABC averages are obtained by means of Gauss-Legendre integration over twist angles. Results: We benchmark the TABC for a free nucleonic gas and apply it to simple cases such as the rod and slab phases, as well as to more elaborate P-surface and gyroidal phases. Conclusions: We demonstrate that by applying TABC reliable results can be obtained from calculations performed in relatively small volumes. By studying various contributions to the total energy, we gain insights into pasta phases in mid-density range.

# Twist-averaged boundary conditions for nuclear pasta Hartree-Fock calculations

## Key claim

Twist-averaged boundary conditions (TABC) can reduce spurious finite-size effects in self-consistent Hartree-Fock calculations of nuclear pasta phases, enabling reliable results from relatively small simulation volumes containing hundreds of nucleons, but the method's effectiveness depends on the dimensionality of the averaging and the specific pasta shape.

## Method

The authors implemented Bloch boundary conditions in the 3D Skyrme-Hartree-Fock code Sky3D, using the SLy6 Skyrme energy density functional with a proton fraction of X_P=1/3 and a uniform electron background for charge neutrality. The twist-averaged expectation values were computed by Gauss-Legendre quadrature over twist angles between zero (periodic boundary conditions) and pi (anti-periodic boundary conditions), using N_GL=4 integration points in each direction. The method was benchmarked on a free nucleonic gas and then applied to rod, slab, P-surface, and gyroid pasta phases, with guiding potentials used during the first 200 HF iterations to converge to the desired shapes.

## Key numbers

- For the free nucleonic gas at total density ρ=0.15625 fm⁻³ (k_F≈1.32 fm⁻¹), the exact average kinetic energy is E_kin,∞≈21.7786 MeV; TABC with 2, 4, or 10 Gauss-Legendre points reduces finite-size fluctuations by approximately an order of magnitude compared to periodic boundary conditions.
- For the rod phase at ρ=0.0358 fm⁻³ with particle numbers 100 ≤ A ≤ 1200, TABC reduces total energy fluctuations from ≈0.04 MeV/A (PBC) to below 0.01 MeV/A for A>200; kinetic and potential energy fluctuations drop from ≈0.4 MeV/A to below 0.1 MeV/A.
- For the slab phase at ρ=0.0715 fm⁻³, TABC reduces the range of kinetic and potential energy fluctuations from ≈1.1 MeV/A (PBC) to ≈0.056 MeV/A.
- For the slab phase with varying box length L_z, a clear minimum in total energy is found at L_z≈20 fm with E/A=-10.89 MeV.
- The P-surface reaches its total energy minimum at box length L=21.65 fm; the gyroid minimum occurs at approximately L=28 fm.
- The largest gyroid calculation used a 29 fm box with 1734 particles, near the limit of the Sky3D solver.
- For the rod phase, the finite-volume error was reduced to ΔE=0.02 MeV/A, a factor of more than three improvement over PBC.

## Context

This work extends previous Skyrme-Hartree-Fock pasta calculations by Schuetrumpf et al. (2013, 2014, 2015) that used periodic boundary conditions and were affected by spurious finite-size effects. The TABC method, previously applied in condensed matter (Gros 1992, 1996; Lin), nucleonic matter (Hagen 2014), and lattice QCD (Tiburzi 2005; Briceno 2014), as well as to neutron star crust models (Carter; Chamel 2007) and Quantum Monte Carlo (Gulminelli), is here adapted to self-consistent HF pasta calculations. The results enable future applications including TABC extension to adaptive multi-resolution 3D HF solvers (Pei 2014) and Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations for superfluid pasta phases and complex nucleonic topologies such as fission.
