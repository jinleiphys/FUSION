# Weak and strong coupling equilibration in nonabelian gauge theories

> We present a direct comparison studying equilibration through kinetic theory at weak coupling and through holography at strong coupling in the same set-up. The set-up starts with a homogeneous thermal state, which then smoothly transitions through an out-of-equilibrium phase to an expanding system undergoing boost-invariant flow. This first apples-to-apples comparison of equilibration provides a benchmark for similar equilibration processes in heavy-ion collisions, where the equilibration mechanism is still under debate. We find that results at weak and strong coupling can be smoothly connected by simple, empirical power-laws for the viscosity, equilibration time and entropy production of the system.

## Key claim

The paper presents a direct, "apples-to-apples" comparison of equilibration dynamics in nonabelian gauge theories at weak coupling (kinetic theory) and strong coupling (holography) by using a setup that avoids the difficulty of defining far-from-equilibrium initial conditions: the system starts in thermal equilibrium and is driven out of equilibrium by a smooth metric pulse, rather than being initialized in a non-equilibrium state. The authors find that results at weak and strong coupling can be smoothly connected by simple empirical power-laws for viscosity, equilibration time, and entropy production, but this connection is empirical and not derived from first principles.

## Method

The authors consider a gauge theory initially in global thermal equilibrium at temperature \(T_i\) in flat Minkowski space, then apply a smooth, one-parameter family of metric pulses (Eq. 4) that transitions the system to an expanding boost-invariant (Bjorken) flow geometry. At weak coupling, they solve the full leading-order kinetic theory for SU(N) gauge bosons including elastic \(2\leftrightarrow 2\) and inelastic \(1\leftrightarrow 2\) collisions with isotropic screening and Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal suppression, using a discretized phase-space grid with Monte Carlo sampling of collision integrals. At strong coupling, they use holography (gauge/gravity duality) for \(\mathcal{N}=4\) SYM, solving Einstein's equations in 5-dimensional anti-de-Sitter spacetime with the same boundary metric, starting from a black brane geometry. Both approaches compute the stress-energy tensor and entropy density evolution, which are compared to each other and to hydrodynamic predictions from Navier-Stokes and second-order (rBRSSS) hydrodynamics.

## Key numbers

- The metric pulse parameter \(\alpha\) controls the rapidity of the transition; the space-time is curved only in the region \(|t T_i-1| \propto 1/\alpha\).
- For free-streaming (non-interacting) evolution at late times, the equilibrium entropy density scales as \(s(t)/s_i = (\pi/(4 t T_i))^{4/3}\) and the pressure anisotropy as \(P_L/P_\perp = 2/(t T_i)^2\), showing the system never reaches equilibrium.
- For Navier-Stokes hydrodynamics at late times, the entropy density follows \(s(t)/s_i = \chi/(t T_i) \left(1 - (2\eta/s) / ((t T_i)^{2/3} \chi^{1/3})\right)\), where \(\chi = S_{eq}(t\rightarrow\infty)/S_{eq,i}\) is the total entropy produced.
- For BRSSS (second-order) hydrodynamics at late times, the entropy density includes a further correction: \(s(t)/s_i = \chi/(t T_i) \left(1 - (2\eta/s)/((t T_i)^{2/3} \chi^{1/3}) + (2\eta^2(2+C_\lambda-C_\tau))/(3 s^2 (t T_i)^{4/3} \chi^{2/3})\right)\).
- The pressure anisotropy at late times for BRSSS is \(P_L/P_T = 1 - (8\eta/s)/((t T_i)^{2/3} \chi^{1/3}) + (16\eta^2(3+C_\lambda-C_\tau))/(3 s^2 (t T_i)^{4/3} \chi^{2/3})\).

## Context

This work extends the parametric "Bottom-up" thermalization picture of Baier (2000) and the quantitative weak-coupling kinetic theory calculations of Kurkela and collaborators (2011-2015), as well as the strong-coupling holographic studies of Chesler and Yaffe (2008-2009) and Heller (2011-2013). By using a common metric-driven setup starting from equilibrium, it enables a direct comparison that was previously hindered by the difficulty of characterizing far-from-equilibrium initial conditions in both frameworks. The results provide a benchmark for understanding equilibration in heavy-ion collisions, where the coupling is neither very small nor very large, and suggest that empirical power-laws may interpolate between the weak- and strong-coupling regimes.
