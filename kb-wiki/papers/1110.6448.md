# Probing Novel Scalar and Tensor Interactions from (Ultra)Cold Neutrons to the LHC

> Scalar and tensor interactions were once competitors to the now well-established V-A structure of the Standard Model weak interactions. We revisit these interactions and survey constraints from low-energy probes (neutron, nuclear, and pion decays) as well as collider searches. Currently, the most stringent limit on scalar and tensor interactions arise from 0+ -> 0+ nuclear decays and the radiative pion decay pi -> e nu gamma, respectively. For the future, we find that upcoming neutron beta decay and LHC measurements will compete in setting the most stringent bounds. For neutron beta decay, we demonstrate the importance of lattice computations of the neutron-to-proton matrix elements to setting limits on these interactions, and provide the first lattice estimate of the scalar charge and a new average of existing results for the tensor charge. Data taken at the LHC is currently probing these interactions at the 10^-2 level (relative to the standard weak interactions), with the potential to reach the 10^-3 level. We show that, with some theoretical assumptions, the discovery of a charged spin-0 resonance decaying to an electron and missing energy implies a lower limit on the strength of scalar interactions probed at low energy.

## Key claim

Scalar and tensor interactions, once competitors to the Standard Model V-A structure, are currently most stringently constrained by 0+ -> 0+ nuclear decays (for scalar) and radiative pion decay pi -> e nu gamma (for tensor), but upcoming neutron beta decay and LHC measurements will compete to set the most stringent bounds, with the caveat that lattice-QCD estimates of nucleon matrix elements at the 10-20% level are required to fully exploit the increased experimental sensitivity.

## Method

The authors performed a comprehensive survey of constraints on scalar and tensor beyond-Standard-Model (BSM) interactions from low-energy probes (neutron, nuclear, and pion decays) and collider searches, using an effective field theory framework with gauge-invariant operators at the TeV scale. They provided the first lattice-QCD estimate of the nucleon scalar charge g_S and a new average of existing results for the tensor charge g_T, and used these to translate experimental bounds on the Fierz interference term b and neutrino asymmetry parameter B into constraints on BSM couplings. They also analyzed LHC data to obtain collider constraints and projected future sensitivities.

## Key numbers

- Scalar charge from lattice QCD: g_S = 0.8 (4) (first lattice estimate)
- Tensor charge from lattice QCD: g_T = 1.05 (35) (new average of existing results)
- Current lower bound on effective scale for scalar interactions from nuclear beta decay: Lambda_S > 4.7 TeV
- Current lower bound on effective scale for tensor interactions from radiative pion decay: Lambda_T > 5 TeV
- LHC currently probes scalar and tensor interactions at the 10^-2 level relative to standard weak interactions, with potential to reach 10^-3 level
- Future LHC constraints associated with effective scales Lambda_{S,T} ~ 7 TeV
- Standard Model Fierz interference term: b^{SM} = -1.35(1) x 10^-3
- Standard Model neutrino asymmetry term: b_nu^{SM} = -1.27(1) x 10^-3
- Experimental sensitivity required for future neutron-decay experiments to compete with collider bounds: delta b, delta b_nu ~ 10^-3
- Lattice-QCD precision needed to exploit increased experimental sensitivity: 10-20% on nucleon matrix elements

## Context

This work extends previous analyses of scalar and tensor interactions by providing the first lattice-QCD estimate of the scalar charge and a new average of tensor charge results, enabling more precise constraints from neutron beta decay. The authors survey constraints from low-energy probes (including nuclear beta decay, radiative pion decay, and pion decay) and collider searches, finding that future neutron-decay experiments with sensitivities delta b, delta b_nu ~ 10^-3 will compete with LHC bounds, while sensitivities of 10^-4 would provide unmatched discovery potential. The paper builds on prior work by Ando (2004) and Gudkov (2005) on correlation coefficients in neutron decay, and by various lattice collaborations (RBC/UKQCD, LHPC, QCDSF, ETMC, CLS, HSC) on nucleon matrix elements.
