# All orders breakup of heavy exotic nuclei in a semiclassical model

> We present a semiclassical and mostly analytical model of elastic neutron breakup reactions for exotic projectiles. Both nuclear and Coulomb induced reactions are considered and the potentials are treated to all orders in the interactions. Furthermore we introduce a technique which allows the use of the full Coulomb potential, thus including all multipoles besides the dipole. Results for deeply bound states as well as for halo initial states are presented and it is shown that experiments on heavy targets would be well suited to study exotic nuclei with tightly bound valence nucleons.

## Key claim

The paper presents a semiclassical, mostly analytical model for elastic neutron breakup that treats both nuclear and Coulomb interactions to all orders and includes the full Coulomb potential beyond the dipole approximation; the authors show that for tightly bound valence nucleons (separation energies above approximately 10 MeV and angular momentum ℓ ≥ 2) on heavy targets, Coulomb breakup becomes negligible compared to nuclear breakup, making heavy targets well suited for studying such nuclei.

## Method

The projectile is treated as a two-body system (core plus valence neutron) and the core-target relative motion is approximated by a straight-line trajectory with constant velocity. The neutron-target interaction is included via an eikonal phase, while the Coulomb potential is handled by splitting it into a short-range screened part (treated in the sudden approximation) and a long-range part (treated to first order in time-dependent perturbation theory), yielding an all-multipole, all-orders expression for the Coulomb breakup amplitude. The total breakup cross section is obtained by integrating over impact parameter, weighted by the core-target survival probability, and includes both elastic breakup and stripping contributions.

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical formalism of Bonaccorso and Brink (2002, 2003) by including the full Coulomb potential beyond the dipole approximation and using numerical Woods-Saxon wave functions instead of asymptotic tails. It addresses the debate raised by Mukha (2005) on the reliability of spectroscopic factors from peripheral reactions, and provides an analytical alternative to the numerical Schrödinger equation approaches of Esbensen (1995) and Capel (2004). The results enable future studies of proton breakup and of medium-mass to heavy exotic nuclei such as \(^{46}\)Ar, where the eikonal method has been found to fail on light targets (Gade, 2005).
