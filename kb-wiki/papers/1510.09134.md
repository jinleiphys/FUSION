# Inverse magnetic catalysis from the properties of the QCD coupling in a magnetic field

> We compute the vacuum one-loop quark-gluon vertex correction at zero temperature in the presence of a magnetic field. From the vertex function we extract the effective quark-gluon coupling and show that it grows with increasing magnetic field strength. The effect is due to a subtle competition between the color charge associated to gluons and the color charge associated to quarks, the former being larger than the latter. In contrast, at high temperature the effective thermo-magnetic coupling results exclusively from the contribution of the color charge associated to quarks. This produces a decrease of the coupling with increasing field strength. We interpret the results in terms of a geometrical effect whereby the magnetic field induces, on average, a closer distance between the (electrically charged) quarks and antiquarks. At high temperature, since the effective coupling is proportional only to the color charge associated to quarks, such proximity with increasing field strength makes the effective coupling decrease due to asymptotic freedom. In turn, this leads to a decreasing quark condensate. In contrast, at zero temperature both the effective strong coupling and the quark condensate increase with increasing magnetic field. This is due to the color charge associated to gluons dominating over that associated to quarks, with both having the opposite sign. Thus, the gluons induce a kind of screening of the quark color charge, in spite of the quark-antiquark proximity. The implications of these results for the inverse magnetic catalysis phenomenon are discussed.

## Key claim

The effective quark-gluon coupling in vacuum grows with increasing magnetic field strength due to the color charge associated to gluons dominating over that associated to quarks, whereas at high temperature the coupling decreases with field strength because only the quark color charge contributes; this difference provides a mechanism to understand inverse magnetic catalysis, though the paper does not address the non-perturbative details of how the change in behavior occurs across intermediate temperatures.

## Method

The authors computed the one-loop quark-gluon vertex correction at zero temperature in the presence of a constant magnetic field, using the weak-field expansion of the Schwinger proper-time fermion propagator in the chiral limit. They evaluated the QED-like and pure QCD Feynman diagrams in the symmetric three-momentum configuration (back-to-back quark-antiquark pair) and in the static limit (P→0) with large gluon virtuality Q², extracting the effective coupling from the longitudinal components of the vertex. The results were compared with the previously computed high-temperature thermo-magnetic coupling from Ayala (2015).

## Key numbers

- The vacuum effective coupling correction contains the color factor combination: [1+ln(4)]C_F − [7+3ln(4)]/10 C_A, where for N=3 the gluon color charge (C_A=3) dominates over the quark color charge (C_F=4/3).
- The magnetic field-dependent correction to the vertex at T=0 is proportional to (qB)/Q², with the expansion parameter for perturbative validity being qB ≪ Q².
- The high-temperature effective coupling correction is proportional to C_F only, with the function M²(T,m,qB) = (qΣ·B)/(16π²)[ln(2) − (π/2)(T/m)], which is negative definite at high temperature.

## Context

This work extends the high-temperature analysis of Ayala (2015), which showed that the quark-gluon coupling decreases with magnetic field and that gluon color charge cancels at high T, to the zero-temperature vacuum case. The results provide a perturbative explanation consistent with lattice QCD findings that the quark condensate increases with magnetic field at T=0 (magnetic catalysis) but decreases near the transition temperature (inverse magnetic catalysis), as reported by Bali (2012) and Fodor (2013). The paper establishes that the sign of the first derivative of α_s with respect to magnetic field matches the condensate behavior at both extremes, but leaves open the non-perturbative question of how the coupling changes across intermediate temperatures.
