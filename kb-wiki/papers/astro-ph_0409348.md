# Compact objects for everyone: I. White dwarf stars

> Based upon previous discussions on the structure of compact stars geared towards undergraduate physics students, a real experiment involving two upper-level undergraduate physics students, a beginning physics graduate, and two advanced graduate students was conducted. A recent addition to the physics curriculum at Florida State University, "The Physics of Stars", sparked quite a few students' interests in the subject matter involving stellar structure. This, coupled with "Stars and Statistical Physics" by Balian and Blaizot and "Neutron Stars for Undergraduates" by Silbar and Reddy, is the cornerstone of this small research group who tackled solving the structure equations for compact objects in the Summer of 2004. Through the use of a simple finite-difference algorithm coupled to Microsoft Excel and Maple, solutions to the equations for stellar structure are presented in the Newtonian regime appropriate to the physics of white dwarf stars.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that the structure equations for white dwarf stars in the Newtonian regime can be solved by upper-level undergraduate and beginning graduate students using simple finite-difference algorithms implemented in Microsoft Excel and Maple, making the problem accessible to motivated high-school students. The authors emphasize that their results are limited to Newtonian stars, explicitly excluding general-relativistic corrections needed for neutron stars.

## Method
The authors derived the equations of hydrostatic equilibrium for a spherically symmetric, non-rotating, cold white dwarf star, assuming a zero-temperature Fermi gas equation of state for the degenerate electrons. They implemented a finite-difference algorithm to solve the coupled differential equations for pressure and enclosed mass, using both Microsoft Excel and Maple. The equations were scaled to natural mass and length scales to handle the enormous range of physical scales (e.g., electron mass versus solar mass).

## Key numbers
- The natural mass scale for a white dwarf with electron-to-baryon ratio \(Y_e = 1/2\) is \(M_0 = 2.650\,M_\odot\) (solar masses).
- The natural radius scale for a white dwarf with \(Y_e = 1/2\) is \(R_0 = 8\,623\) km.
- For a toy model of a 1 \(M_\odot\) white dwarf, the non-relativistic prediction for the radius is \(R_{\rm NR} = 7\,162\) km, while the correct relativistic dispersion relation gives a smaller radius of \(R_{\rm NR} = 4\,968\) km.
- The dimensionless gravitational coupling between two nucleons is \(\alpha_{\rm G} = 5.906 \times 10^{-39}\).
- The Chandrasekhar limiting mass is stated as \(M_{Ch} \equiv 1.44 M_{\odot}\).

## Context
This work extends pedagogical approaches to compact object physics previously developed by Balian and Blaizot ("Stars and Statistical Physics") and Silbar and Reddy ("Neutron Stars for Undergraduates") by providing a hands-on computational experiment suitable for undergraduate students. The paper focuses exclusively on Newtonian white dwarf stars, reserving the treatment of neutron stars (which require general relativity) for a future publication. It enables students to directly compute mass-radius relationships and understand the role of special relativity in setting the Chandrasekhar limit, contrasting with the non-relativistic treatment of Fowler (1926) that predicted no upper mass limit.
