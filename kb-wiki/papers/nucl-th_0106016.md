# Ambiguities in the implementation of the impulse approximation for the response of many-fermion systems

> Within the impulse approximation the response of a many body system at large momentum tranfer can be written in a simple and transparent form, allowing to directly relate the inclusive scattering cross section to the properties of the target ground state. Although the physics assumptions underlying impulse approximation are well defined, their implementation involves ambiguities that may cause significant differences in the calculated responses. We show that, while minimal use of the impulse approximation assumptions naturally leads to write the response in terms of the target spectral function, the widely used alternative definition in terms of the momentum distribution involves a more extended use of the same assumtpions. The difference between the responses resulting from the two procedures is illustrated by two examples.

## Key claim

While the impulse approximation (IA) assumptions can be stated unambiguously, their implementation leads to two distinct definitions of the many-body response, one using the spectral function and one using the momentum distribution, that produce significantly different numerical results, particularly in nuclear matter where the average removal energy is large.

## Method

The authors derived the inclusive response S(q,ω) of a many-fermion system to a scalar probe within the impulse approximation using two different procedures. The first procedure, which makes minimal use of IA assumptions, expresses the response in terms of the target spectral function P(k,E) by factorizing the N-body propagator after using the Schrödinger equation to eliminate one propagator. The second procedure applies the IA factorization to both propagators appearing in the time-ordered product, leading to an expression in terms of the momentum distribution n(k) that loses all information about the removal energy distribution. The two formulations were compared for infinite nuclear matter at equilibrium density and for liquid ³He.

## Key numbers

- Infinite nuclear matter at equilibrium density ρ = 0.16 fm⁻³: average removal energy ⟨E⟩ = 61.9 MeV, compared to Fermi energy ε_F = 16 MeV and Fermi momentum k_F = 1.33 fm⁻¹
- At momentum transfer |q| = 5 fm⁻¹ in nuclear matter, 50% of the spectral function strength at |k| = 3 fm⁻¹ resides at removal energy E > 200 MeV
- Liquid ³He at equilibrium density ρ = 0.01635 Å⁻³: Fermi energy ε_F = 2.47 °K, with peak half-width at |q| = 10 Å⁻¹ of roughly 200 °K and removal energy shift of ~10 °K
- The nuclear matter response at |q| = 5 fm⁻¹ has a half-width of ~250 MeV, compared to ε_F = 16 MeV and ⟨ε⟩ = 61.9 MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior calculations of nuclear matter spectral functions using Correlated Basis Function perturbation theory (Benhar, Fabrocini, Fantoni 1989) and addresses ambiguities in the IA framework used in analyses of electron-nucleus scattering data. The paper shows that the momentum-distribution-based definition used by Carraro and Koonin (1990) for liquid helium responses, while successful at very high momentum transfer (|q|/k_F > 10) near the peak, is inadequate for nuclear systems where removal energy effects are substantial. The spectral-function formulation is identified as the necessary starting point for quantitative studies of final state interactions, which are known to dominate nuclear responses at low energy transfer.
