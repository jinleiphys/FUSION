# Accuracy of the Faddeev Random Phase Approximation for Light Atoms

> The accuracy of the Faddeev random phase approximation (FRPA) method is tested by calculating the total and ionization energies of a set of light atoms up to Ar. Comparisons are made with the results of coupled-cluster singles and doubles (CCSD), third-order algebraic diagrammatic construction [ADC(3)], and with the experiment. It is seen that even for two-electron systems, He and Be-2+, the inclusion of RPA effects leads to satisfactory results and therefore it does not over-correlate the ground state. The FRPA becomes progressively better for larger atomic numbers where it gives about 5 mH more correlation energy and it shifts ionization potentials by 2-10 mH, with respect to its sister method ADC(3). The corrections for ionization potentials consistently reduce the discrepancies with the experiment.

# Accuracy of the Faddeev Random Phase Approximation for Light Atoms

## Key claim

The Faddeev Random Phase Approximation (FRPA) method yields satisfactory results for two-electron systems (He and Be²⁺) without over-correlating the ground state, and becomes progressively better for larger atomic numbers where it gives approximately 5 mH more correlation energy than its sister method ADC(3), though for the near-degenerate Be atom the FRPA in its present form shows a limitation, missing about 9 mH of correlation energy compared to full configuration interaction.

## Method

The authors computed total binding energies and ionization energies for a set of closed-shell and closed-subshell atoms and ions up to argon (Z ≤ 18) using the FRPA method, which includes two-particle–one-hole and two-hole–one-particle configurations in the self-energy and groups these configurations in terms of particle-vibration couplings where collective vibrations are calculated using RPA. Calculations were performed using correlation-consistent polarized valence Gaussian basis sets (cc-pvXz and cc-pcvXz) with extrapolations to the basis set limit using the relation E_X = E_∞ + A X⁻³, and results were compared to coupled-cluster singles and doubles (CCSD), third-order algebraic diagrammatic construction [ADC(3)], and experimental values.

## Key numbers

- For He, FRPAc misses 1 mH (2%) of the correlation energy, with total binding energy -2.9029 H compared to experimental -2.9037 H
- For Be, FRPAc gives total binding energy -14.6436 H, missing approximately 9 mH compared to CCSD (-14.6522 H) and approximately 15 mH compared to experiment (-14.6674 H)
- For Ne, FRPAc gives total binding energy -128.9381 H, within 0.2 mH of experiment (-128.9383 H)
- For Mg, FRPAc gives total binding energy -200.052 H, within 2 mH of experiment (-200.054 H)
- For Ar, FRPAc gives total binding energy -527.548 H, within 4 mH of experiment (-527.544 H)
- For Z ≥ 10, FRPA predicts about 5 mH more binding than FTDAc/ADC(3)c
- The rms error for FRPAc across all systems is 9.5 mH (3.4 mH when excluding Be)
- For Ne in cc-pcvDz basis, the total correlation energy is 233 mH, and FRPA halves the discrepancy between FTDA and full configuration interaction
- The correction to correlation energies from extra core orbits was approximately 40 mH for Ne and approximately 300 mH for Mg

## Context

This work extends the FRPA calculations previously applied to the Ne atom by Barbieri (2007) to other closed-shell and closed-subshell systems, testing the accuracy of the approach for light atoms. The FRPA method is closely related to the ADC(3) formalism of Schirmer (1983) and the quasi-particle DFT formalism proposed by Van Neck (2006); when the Tamm-Dancoff approximation is used instead of RPA, FRPA reduces exactly to ADC(3). The approach addresses limitations of the GW approximation, which exaggerates correlations for outer valence orbits in atoms as found by Barbieri (2007), by including interactions in the particle-particle and hole-hole channels alongside the particle-hole channel. The results enable bridging calculations of finite and extended electron systems, though for near-degenerate systems like Be, further improvements such as self-consistency in the polarization propagator or inclusion of fifth-order ADC(5) terms may be needed.
