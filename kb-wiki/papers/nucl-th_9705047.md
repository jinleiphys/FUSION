# Effect of viscosity on one dimensional hydrodynamic flow and direct photons from 200 AGeV S+Au collisions at CERN SPS

> We have analysed the direct photon data obtained by the WA80 collaboration in 200 A GeV S+Au collision at CERN SPS, in a one dimensional hydrodynamical model. Two scenario was considered: (i) formation of quark-gluon plasma and (ii) formation of hot hadronic gas. For both the scenario, ideal as well as extremely viscous fluid was considered. It was found that direct photon yield from QGP is not affected much whether the fluid is treated as ideal or extremely viscous. The yield however differ substantially if hadron gas is produced. Both the scenario do not give satisfactory description of the data.

## Key claim

The paper finds that neither the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) scenario nor the hot hadronic gas scenario, whether treated as ideal or extremely viscous fluids, gives a satisfactory description of the WA80 direct photon data for 200 AGeV S+Au collisions at CERN SPS. The abstract's claim that "both the scenario do not give satisfactory description of the data" glosses over the condition that this conclusion holds only within the one-dimensional, boost-invariant hydrodynamic model without transverse expansion or pre-equilibrium photon contributions.

## Method

The author performed one-dimensional, longitudinal boost-invariant hydrodynamic calculations for two scenarios: phase transition (PT) to QGP and no phase transition (NPT) with hot hadronic gas. For each scenario, both ideal fluid flow (zero viscosity) and maximal viscous flow (where the total viscous force equals the pressure) were considered, using the bag equation of state for QGP and a pion gas equation of state for the hadronic phase. Direct photon yields were computed by convoluting thermal photon emission rates from Kapusta et al. (1991) and Xiong et al. (1992) with the space-time evolution, using initial times of 0.3, 0.6, and 1.0 fm for the PT scenario and 1, 2, and 3 fm for the NPT scenario.

## Key numbers

- Pion multiplicity used: dN/dy = 225 for central S+Au collisions
- Critical temperature for QGP phase transition: T_c = 160 MeV
- Freeze-out temperature: T_F = 100 MeV
- In the PT scenario, initial temperatures for ideal fluid ranged from 203 MeV (tau_i=1.0 fm) to 304 MeV (tau_i=0.3 fm); for viscous fluid from 160 MeV (tau_i=1.0 fm) to 226 MeV (tau_i=0.3 fm)
- In the NPT scenario, initial temperatures for ideal fluid ranged from 283 MeV (tau_i=3.0 fm) to 407 MeV (tau_i=1.0 fm); for viscous fluid from 218 MeV (tau_i=3.0 fm) to 287 MeV (tau_i=1.0 fm)
- The PT scenario underpredicts the WA80 data by a factor of 10 or more at large p_T

## Context

This work extends the author's previous analysis (Chaudhuri, 1995) of the preliminary WA80 data, which had shown that a viscous hadron gas could explain the earlier results. The revised WA80 data (Albrecht et al., 1996) now gives upper limits at 90% confidence rather than absolute yields, and the present analysis shows that neither the QGP scenario nor the hadronic gas scenario can describe these revised data, regardless of viscosity. The paper suggests that unconventional scenarios, such as a very soft equation of state in the mixed phase as argued by Xiong and Shuryak (1994), may be needed to explain the data, and emphasizes that accurate viscosity coefficients for hot hadronic matter are of primary importance for future calculations.
