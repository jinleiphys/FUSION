# Thermal conduction and thermopower of inner crusts of magnetized neutron stars

> We compute the thermal conductivity and thermoelectric power (thermopower) of the inner crust of compact stars across a broad temperature-density domain relevant for proto-neutron stars, binary neutron-star mergers, and accreting neutron stars. The analysis covers the transition from a semi-degenerate to a highly degenerate electron gas and assumes temperatures above the melting threshold of the nuclear lattice, such that nuclei form a liquid. The transport coefficients are obtained by solving the Boltzmann kinetic equation in the relaxation-time approximation, fully incorporating the anisotropies generated by non-quantizing magnetic fields. Electron scattering rates include (i) dynamical screening of the electron-ion interaction in the hard-thermal-loop approximation of QED, (ii) ion-ion correlations within a one-component plasma, and (iii) finite nuclear-size effects. As an additional refinement, we evaluate electron-neutron scattering induced by the coupling of electrons to the anomalous magnetic moment of free neutrons; this contribution is found to be subdominant throughout the parameter range explored. To assess the sensitivity of transport coefficients to the underlying microphysics, we perform calculations for several inner-crust compositions obtained from different nuclear interactions and many-body methods. Across most of the crust, variations in relaxation times and in the components of the anisotropic thermal-conductivity and thermopower tensors reach up to factors 3-4 and 1.5-2, respectively, with the exception of the region where pasta phases are expected. These results provide updated, composition-dependent microphysical inputs for dissipative magneto-hydrodynamic simulations of warm neutron stars and post-merger remnants, where anisotropic heat and charge transport are of critical importance.

## Key claim

The thermal conductivity and thermopower of the inner crust of neutron stars are computed for temperatures above the melting threshold (liquid phase), incorporating non-quantizing magnetic fields and several microphysical refinements; the results show that variations among different crust compositions lead to differences of up to factors 3-4 in relaxation times and 1.5-2 in transport tensor components across most of the crust, except where pasta phases are expected.

## Method

The authors solved the Boltzmann kinetic equation in the relaxation-time approximation for electrons, including electron-ion and electron-neutron scattering, with the full anisotropy from non-quantizing magnetic fields. Electron-ion scattering incorporated dynamical screening via the hard-thermal-loop approximation of QED, ion-ion correlations through the structure factor of a one-component plasma, and finite nuclear-size effects via a nuclear form factor; electron-neutron scattering via the neutron's anomalous magnetic moment was evaluated but found subdominant. Transport coefficients were computed for five different inner-crust compositions (NV, D1M, D1M*, Bsk24, Sly9) obtained from different nuclear interactions and many-body methods.

## Key numbers

- Relaxation time and Hall parameter variations among compositions reach up to factors of 3-4 across most of the crust.
- Components of the anisotropic thermal-conductivity and thermopower tensors vary by factors of 1.5-2 among compositions across most of the crust.
- The Hall parameter ω_cτ is of order unity throughout the inner crust for a magnetic field B_{12} = 100 (where B_{12} ≡ B/(10^{12} G)).
- Anisotropy becomes negligible for B_{13} ≲ 13 (i.e., B ≲ 1.3 × 10^{13} G).

## Context

This work extends the formalism previously developed for electrical and thermal conductivities in the outer crust by Harutyunyan et al. (2016, 2024) to the inner crust, where free neutrons and heavier nuclear clusters introduce additional microphysics. It complements the recent calculation of electrical conductivity in the inner crust by Harutyunyan et al. (2024) and provides updated, composition-dependent inputs for dissipative magneto-hydrodynamic simulations of warm neutron stars and post-merger remnants, where anisotropic heat and charge transport are critical. The results enable quantification of thermoelectric effects in magnetic field evolution, building on earlier proposals for dynamo-driven magnetic field amplification by Dolginov (1980), Blandford (1983), and others.
