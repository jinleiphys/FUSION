# Latest results for the antikaon-nucleon optical potential

> The key question of this letter is whether the K-nucleus optical potential is deep, as it is prefered by the phenomenological fits to kaonic atoms data, or shallow, as it comes out from unitary chiral model calculations. The current experimental situation is reviewed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the existing experimental claims of deeply bound antikaon-nucleon states are mutually inconsistent and can all be explained by conventional nuclear absorption mechanisms without invoking bound states, leaving the fundamental question of whether the K-nucleus optical potential is deep (up to 600 MeV) or shallow (40-60 MeV) unresolved.

## Method

The authors review the current experimental situation regarding claims of deeply bound K- states with nucleons, including results from KEK, FINUDA, OBELIX, and DISTO experiments. They then present their own reanalysis of these data using Monte Carlo simulations that account for K- absorption from low-lying atomic orbits by two or three nucleons, including Fermi motion, recoil effects, and final state interactions with the daughter nucleus.

## Key numbers

- Deep K-nucleus optical potentials from phenomenological fits to kaonic atoms can be up to 600 MeV deep in the center of the nucleus
- Shallow chiral unitary model potentials give 40-60 MeV attraction at nuclear matter density, with an imaginary part also about 40-60 MeV at nuclear matter density
- The experiment studied the (K-,p) reaction on 12C with fast kaons at 1 GeV/c momentum

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analyses (Magas et al., 2009; Yamagata-Sekihara et al., 2010) that showed experimental signals for deeply bound K- clusters can be explained without invoking bound states. The paper contradicts claims from FINUDA (2005), OBELIX (1996), and DISTO (2010) experiments. The analysis enables future experiments with higher statistics, specifically E15 at J-PARC and AMADEUS at DAΦNE, to properly interpret their data by first understanding conventional K- absorption mechanisms.
