# Reflections on Chiral Symmetry within QCD

> That chiral symmetry is a crucial feature of the strong force was realized before the discovery of Quantum Chromodynamics. However, the full power it exerts on the structure of the nucleon became apparent only afterwards. We present a high-level and somewhat personal overview of its role in almost every aspect of proton structure, from its mass and spin to the asymmetry of its antimatter content and its strange quark content. The lessons learned from studying the proton are also vital with respect to the modern challenge of the nature of baryon excited states.

## Key claim

Chiral symmetry, realized through the Goldstone mode with pions coupled to quarks at the bag surface, provides a quantitatively successful framework for understanding almost every aspect of proton structure, from its mass and spin to the asymmetry of its antimatter content and its strange quark content, though the author presents this as a high-level and somewhat personal overview rather than a definitive proof.

## Method

The paper reviews the development and applications of the cloudy bag model (CBM), which restores chiral symmetry to the MIT bag model by coupling pions to quarks at the bag surface, with a finite-size source characterized by a bag radius between 0.8 and 1.0 fm. The author then discusses how this framework, combined with finite range regularization (FRR) for extrapolating lattice QCD data and Hamiltonian effective field theory for studying baryon resonances, yields predictions for nucleon structure observables including parton distribution functions, form factors, sigma commutators, and spin content.

## Key numbers

- Bag radius of 0.8 to 1.0 fm preferred by the cloudy bag model analysis of the Δ resonance and neutron charge distribution
- σπN (contribution of u and d quarks to nucleon mass) from chiral analysis of CLS lattice data: approximately 52 MeV with error of order 4 MeV (Owa et al., 2023)
- Strange sigma commutator σs from FRR analysis of lattice data: between 20 and 60 MeV (Young, 2009; Shanahan, 2012), roughly an order of magnitude smaller than the earlier belief of 300 MeV
- Strange magnetic moment of the proton G_M^s = -0.046 ± 0.019 μ_N (Leinweber, 2004)
- Strange electric form factor of the proton G_E^s(0.1 GeV^2) = +0.001 ± 0.004 ± 0.004 (Leinweber, 2006)
- Leading non-analytic behavior of the n'th moment of anti-strange quark distribution: m_K^(2n+2) ln m_K^2; for the n'th moment of strange quark distribution: m_K^2 ln m_K^2

## Context

This review extends the author's decades-long work on the cloudy bag model (Miller, Theberge, and Thomas, 1979; Thomas, 1982) and its applications to nucleon structure, including the prediction of an excess of anti-down over anti-up quarks in the proton sea (Thomas, 1983), later confirmed by the New Muon Collaboration (1991) and Drell-Yan measurements at Fermilab (NuSea, 2001; FNAL E906, 2022). The paper also discusses how finite range regularization (Leinweber, 1999; Donoghue, 1998) enabled extrapolation of lattice QCD results from large quark masses to the physical point, and how Hamiltonian effective field theory (Hall, 2013) revealed the evolution of the Λ(1405) resonance from a three-quark state to a K̅N bound state as pion mass varies. Future work at JLab 12 GeV and the electron-ion collider will probe nucleon structure with higher precision, with chiral symmetry remaining essential for interpretation.
