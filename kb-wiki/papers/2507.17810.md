# Decoding the proton's gluonic density with lattice QCD-informed machine learning

> We present a first machine learning-based decoding of the gluonic structure of the proton from lattice QCD using a variational autoencoder inverse mapper (VAIM). Harnessing the power of generative AI, we predict the parton distribution function (PDF) of the gluon given information on the reduced pseudo-Ioffe-time distributions (RpITDs) as calculated from an ensemble with lattice spacing $a\! \approx\! 0.09$ fm and a pion mass of $M_π\! \approx\! 310$ MeV. The resulting gluon PDF is consistent with phenomenological global fits within uncertainties, particularly in the intermediate-to-high-$x$ region where lattice data are most constraining. A subsequent correlation analysis confirms that the VAIM learns a meaningful latent representation, highlighting the potential of generative AI to bridge lattice QCD and phenomenological extractions within a unified analysis framework.

# Decoding the proton's gluonic density with lattice QCD-informed machine learning

## Key claim
The authors present the first machine learning-based extraction of the gluon parton distribution function (PDF) from lattice QCD using a variational autoencoder inverse mapper (VAIM), finding that the resulting gluon PDF is consistent with phenomenological global fits within uncertainties, particularly in the intermediate-to-high-$x$ region ($0.2 \lesssim x \lesssim 0.7$) where the lattice reduced pseudo-Ioffe-time distributions (RpITDs) are most constraining; the agreement holds for all five global fits compared (CJ22, CT18, JAM22, MSHT20, NNPDF4.0) with a slight exception for JAM22 over the narrow range $0.5 \lesssim x \lesssim 0.6$.

## Method
The authors use the pseudo-PDF method to relate lattice-calculated reduced pseudo-Ioffe-time distributions (RpITDs) to the lightcone gluon PDF through a perturbative matching relation, employing a variational autoencoder inverse mapper (VAIM) trained on 400,000 synthetic data samples of PDF shape parameters ($\alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta$) and their corresponding RpITDs. The VAIM architecture consists of four residual blocks with 1000 units per layer, and after training, lattice RpITDs from an ensemble with lattice spacing $a \approx 0.09$ fm and pion mass $M_\pi \approx 310$ MeV are fed into the observable space to generate predicted PDF parameters via Monte Carlo ensembling of Jackknife samples. The resulting gluon PDF is compared against five global QCD fits (CJ22, CT18, JAM22, MSHT20, NNPDF4.0) and validated through a closure test comparing input lattice RpITDs with those reconstructed from the VAIM-predicted PDF parameters.

## Key numbers
- Lattice spacing: $a \approx 0.09$ fm; pion mass: $M_\pi \approx 310$ MeV
- Gluon PDF presented at scale $\mu = 2$ GeV, normalized as $xg(x,\mu)/\langle x \rangle_g(\mu)$
- Most constraining $x$-region: $0.2 \lesssim x \lesssim 0.7$, where lattice RpITDs have highest sensitivity
- RpITDs computed for spatial separations $z \in [1a, 5a]$ and momenta $P_z \in \frac{2\pi}{aL} \times [1, 7]$, yielding 35 RpITD observables
- Lowest momentum used: $P_z \approx 0.44$ GeV
- Training data: 400,000 samples split 80/10/10 into training/validation/test sets
- PDF parameter ranges: $\alpha \in \mathcal{U}(-0.9,1)$, $\beta \in \mathcal{U}(0,10)$, $\gamma, \delta \in \mathcal{U}(-5,5)$
- RpITD bounds for training: $\mathcal{M}(\nu, z^2) \in [-0.5, 1]$
- VAIM architecture: 4 residual blocks, 1000 units per layer, 256-dimensional latent space
- Learning rate: $10^{-6}$ using Adam optimizer
- Closure test: average point-by-point deviation below $1\sigma$, maximum deviation within $2\sigma$

## Context
This work extends prior lattice QCD calculations of gluon distributions in the nucleon using the pseudo-PDF method by groups such as HadStruc (2021, 2022) and Fan et al. (2022), as well as earlier VAIM applications to inverse problems in hadron structure by Kriesten et al. (2023) and Almaeen et al. (2024). The study builds on recent efforts to use neural networks for PDF reconstruction from lattice data, including work by Karpie et al. (2019), Del Debbio et al. (2020), and others. The results open a pathway for embedding lattice QCD observables directly into global phenomenological analyses, enabling future unified pheno-lattice frameworks that can incorporate forthcoming lattice information with improved statistical precision to reduce $x$-dependent PDF uncertainties.
