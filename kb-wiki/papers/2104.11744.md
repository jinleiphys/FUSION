# "In-System" Fission-Events: An Insight into Puzzles of Exoplanets and Stars?

> In expansion of our recent proposal (Physics, 2020, 2, 213-276) that the solar system's evolution occurred in two stages -- during the first stage, the gaseous giants formed (via disk instability), and, during the second stage (caused by an encounter with a particular stellar-object leading to "in-system" fission-driven nucleogenesis), the terrestrial planets formed (via accretion) -- we emphasize here that the mechanism of formation of such stellar-objects is generally universal and therefore encounters of such objects with stellar-systems may have occurred elsewhere across galaxies. If so, their aftereffects may perhaps be observed as puzzling features in the spectra of individual stars (such as idiosyncratic chemical enrichments) and/or in the structures of exoplanetary systems (such as unusually high planet densities or short orbital periods). This paper reviews and reinterprets astronomical data within the "fission-events framework." Classification of stellar systems as "pristine" or "impacted" is offered.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that "in-system" fission-driven nucleogenesis, triggered by encounters with a particular type of stellar object, may be a universal mechanism that explains puzzling features in exoplanetary systems (such as unusually high planet densities or short orbital periods) and idiosyncratic chemical enrichments in stellar spectra, but this is presented as a reinterpretation of existing data rather than a tested hypothesis.

## Method

The authors review and reinterpret astronomical data within their previously proposed "fission-events framework," which posits a two-stage solar system evolution: first, gaseous giant formation via disk instability, then terrestrial planet formation via accretion triggered by an encounter with a stellar object that caused in-system fission-driven nucleogenesis. They extend this framework to other stellar systems and offer a classification of stellar systems as either "pristine" or "impacted."

## Key numbers

Review-type paper: no original numerics.

## Context

This paper expands on the authors' earlier proposal (Tito and Pavlov, 2020, Physics, 2, 213-276) that the solar system underwent a two-stage evolution involving fission-driven nucleogenesis. The work reinterprets existing astronomical observations of exoplanetary systems and stellar chemical abundances through the lens of this fission-events framework, offering a classification scheme for stellar systems. It enables future observational searches for signatures of such encounters in exoplanet demographics and stellar abundance patterns, though it does not provide quantitative predictions or testable numerical constraints.
