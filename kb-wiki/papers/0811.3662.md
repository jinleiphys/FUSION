# Low-mass lepton pair production at large transverse momentum

> We study the transverse momentum distribution of low-mass lepton pairs produced in hadronic scattering, using the perturbative QCD factorization approach. We argue that the distribution at large transverse momentum, $Q_T \gg Q$, with the pair's invariant mass $Q$ as low as $Q \sim Λ_{\mathrm{QCD}}$, can be systematically factorized into universal parton-to-lepton pair fragmentation functions, parton distributions, and perturbatively calculable partonic hard parts evaluated at a short distance scale $\sim {\cal O}(1/Q_T)$. We introduce a model for the input lepton pair fragmentation functions at a scale $μ_0\sim 1$ GeV, which are then evolved perturbatively to scales relevant at RHIC. Using the evolved fragmentation functions, we calculate the transverse momentum distributions in hadron-hadron, hadron-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus collisions at RHIC. We also discuss the sensitivity of the transverse momentum distribution of low-mass lepton pairs to the gluon distribution.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the perturbative QCD factorization formalism for low-mass lepton pairs at large transverse momentum remains valid even when the pair's invariant mass \(Q\) is as low as \(\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}\), provided that the parton-to-virtual-photon fragmentation functions are treated as non-perturbative input at an initial scale \(\mu_0 \sim 1\) GeV, rather than being fully perturbative as required when \(Q \gg \Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}\).

## Method

The authors reorganize the perturbative QCD cross section for virtual photon production into a "direct" contribution and a "fragmentation" contribution, where the latter resums large logarithms \(\alpha_s \ln(Q_T^2/Q^2)\) into parton-to-virtual-photon fragmentation functions via inhomogeneous evolution equations. For the non-perturbative regime \(Q \sim \Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}\), they introduce a model for the input fragmentation functions at scale \(\mu_0 = 1\) GeV, combining a lowest-order QED contribution (with a mass parameter \(\lambda\) to regulate infrared sensitivity) and a vector meson dominance (VMD) contribution using \(\rho\) meson parameters and pion fragmentation functions. These input functions are then evolved perturbatively to scales relevant at RHIC, and the resulting cross sections are computed for hadron-hadron, hadron-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus collisions.

## Key numbers

- The invariant mass range considered for low-mass lepton pairs is \(100 \leq Q \leq 300\) MeV, as measured by the PHENIX collaboration at RHIC.
- The initial scale for the non-perturbative input fragmentation functions is chosen as \(\mu_0 = 1\) GeV.
- The vector meson dominance model uses \(m_V = m_\rho\), with \(f_\rho^2/4\pi = 2.2\).
- The cross section for low-mass dileptons is approximately related to the prompt photon cross section by a factor \(\frac{\alpha_{\mathrm{em}}}{3\pi} \ln\left(Q_{\mathrm{max}}^2/Q_{\mathrm{min}}^2\right)\), where \(\alpha_{\mathrm{em}}\) is the fine structure constant.
- The transverse momentum region of interest is \(Q_T \leq 5\) GeV, where real photon measurements are difficult due to neutral hadron backgrounds.
- The center-of-mass energy for the proton-proton calculations is \(\sqrt{s} = 200\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the perturbative QCD factorization approach for Drell-Yan lepton pairs at high transverse momentum, previously developed by Qiu (2001) and Berger (2001) for the regime \(Q_T \gg Q \gg \Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}\), down to the regime where \(Q \sim \Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}\) as low as 100 MeV. It is motivated by measurements from the PHENIX collaboration at RHIC (Akiba) and goes beyond an earlier theoretical study by Aurenche (1988) by providing a more complete treatment of the fragmentation contribution and more extensive phenomenological studies. The approach is similar in spirit to the treatment of virtual photon structure functions at HERA (Bussey). The results enable the use of low-mass lepton pairs as a probe of nuclear gluon distributions in heavy-ion collisions and as a means to access lower transverse momenta than possible with real prompt photons.
