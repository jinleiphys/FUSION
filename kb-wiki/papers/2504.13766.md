# The impact of new ($α$, n) reaction rates on the weak s-process in metal-poor massive stars

> Massive stars are significant sites for the weak s-process (ws-process). $^{22}$Ne and $^{16}$O are, respectively, the main neutron source and poison for the ws-process. In the metal-poor stars, the abundance of $^{22}$Ne is limited by the metallicity, so that the contribution of $^{22}$Ne($α$, n)$^{25}$Mg reaction on the s-process is weaker. Conversely, the $^{17}$O($α$, n)$^{20}$Ne reaction becomes more prominent in these stars due to the most abundant $^{16}$O in all metallicities. In this work, we calculate the evolution of four metal-poor models ($Z=10^{-3}$) for the Zero-Age Main-Sequence (ZAMS) masses of $M ({\rm ZAMS})=$ 15, 20, 25, and 30 M$_{\odot}$ to investigate the effect of reaction rates on the ws-process. We adopt the new $^{17}$O($α$, n)$^{20}$Ne and $^{17}$O($α, γ$)$^{21}$Ne reaction rates suggested by Best et al. (2013) and $^{22}$Ne($α$, n)$^{25}$Mg and $^{22}$Ne($α, γ$)$^{26}$Mg from Wiescher et al. (2023). The yields of the s-process isotope with updated reaction rates are compared with the results using default reaction rates from JINA REACLIB. We find that the new $^{17}$O+$α$ reaction rates increase the ws-process mainly in all the stages, while the new $^{22}$Ne+$α$ reaction rates only increase the ws-process in C and Ne burning stages. Updating these new reaction rates would increase the production of ws-process isotopes by tens of times. We also note that for more massive stars, the enhancement by new $^{17}$O+$α$ reaction rates become more significant.

## Key claim

The new $^{17}$O+$\alpha$ reaction rates from Best et al. (2013) increase the weak s-process in all burning stages of metal-poor massive stars, while the new $^{22}$Ne+$\alpha$ reaction rates from Wiescher et al. (2023) only increase the weak s-process during carbon and neon burning stages, with the enhancement from the $^{17}$O+$\alpha$ rates becoming more significant for more massive stars.

## Method

The authors used the Modules for Experiments in Stellar Astrophysics (MESA, version 12778) to evolve four metal-poor stellar models with Z=10^{-3} and ZAMS masses of 15, 20, 25, and 30 solar masses from the zero-age main sequence until iron core collapse. They then performed post-processing nucleosynthesis calculations using the WinNet code with a network of about 2000 isotopes, comparing four reaction recipes that combine the new $^{17}$O+$\alpha$ rates from Best et al. (2013) and $^{22}$Ne+$\alpha$ rates from Wiescher et al. (2023) against the default JINA REACLIB rates.

## Key numbers

- The ($\alpha$, n)/($\alpha$, $\gamma$) ratio for $^{22}$Ne+$\alpha$ from Wiescher et al. (2023) is 1.2 to 2.0 times higher than REACLIB values below 1.5 GK, and increases to several tens of times above 1.5 GK
- The ($\alpha$, n)/($\alpha$, $\gamma$) ratio for $^{17}$O+$\alpha$ from Best et al. (2013) is similar to REACLIB below 0.7 GK but increases to several tens of times in the carbon, neon, and oxygen layers
- The 25 solar mass model has a He core mass of 7.80 solar masses, a CO core mass of 4.96 solar masses, and a mass cut M(V/U_max) of 1.84 solar masses
- The neutron excess (eta = 1-2Y_e) in the weak s-process region is 10^{-3} to 10^{-4}, increasing to about 0.2 in the center
- Updating the new reaction rates increases the production of weak s-process isotopes by tens of times

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the weak s-process in massive stars by Raiteri et al. (1991, 1992, 1993), The et al. (2007), and Limongi and Chieffi (2018), specifically addressing the discrepancy between observed weak s-process elements in metal-poor stars and earlier theoretical predictions by Woosley and Weaver (1995) and Baraffe et al. (1992). The study investigates the combined impact of $^{17}$O+$\alpha$ and $^{22}$Ne+$\alpha$ reaction rates in non-rotating metal-poor stars, complementing previous work on rotating massive stars by Frischknecht et al. (2012), Nishimura et al. (2017), and Choplin et al. (2018). The results enable more accurate predictions of weak s-process yields from metal-poor massive stars for galactic chemical evolution models.
