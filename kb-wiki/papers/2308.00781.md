# Theory of the (Heavy-Quark) Exotic Hadrons: A Primer for the Flavor Community

> Scores of exotic hadrons, particularly tetraquarks and pentaquarks in the heavy-quark sector, have been observed in the past 20 years, and more continue to be discovered to this day. Unlike mesons and baryons, such exotics are not mandated to exist under our current understanding of QCD, meaning that each new discovery presents fresh insights into the expansive possibilities of strong-interaction physics. Even the basic architecture of these multiquark states remains an open question. Here we discuss the merits and deficiencies of their best-known dynamical descriptions, recognizing that the eventual universal model for exotics will almost certainly require the synthesis of more than one fundamental paradigm.

# Theory of the (Heavy-Quark) Exotic Hadrons: A Primer for the Flavor Community

## Key claim
The paper argues that no single theoretical paradigm explains all of the more than 60 observed heavy-quark exotic hadrons, and that the eventual universal model will almost certainly require the synthesis of more than one fundamental dynamical description. The author emphasizes that even basic questions such as the internal architecture of these multiquark states remain unresolved, despite two decades of experimental discoveries.

## Method
The paper surveys five major dynamical paradigms for tetraquarks (with pentaquark analogues): hybrid mesons, hadronic molecules, diquark-antidiquark states, hadroquarkonium, and threshold/rescattering/cusp effects. For each paradigm, the author evaluates its successes and deficiencies by comparing predicted spectra, decay patterns, and quantum numbers against the 64 observed heavy-quark exotic candidates as of June 2023. The analysis also incorporates results from lattice QCD simulations and the Weinberg compositeness criterion.

## Key numbers
- 64 observed heavy-quark exotics (tetraquarks and pentaquarks) reported by experimental collaborations as of June 2023
- 49 of these are in the hidden-charm sector, 5 in the hidden-bottom sector, 4 with a single c quark, 1 with a single b quark, 4 with valence content c cbar c cbar, and 1 with two c quarks
- m_X(3872) - m_D0 - m_D*0 = -40 ± 90 keV, making X(3872) bound by less than the deuteron binding energy of 2.2452(2) MeV
- LHCb data yields Z > 0.15 for X(3872), indicating a state that is largely but not entirely molecular
- Lattice QCD predicts a 1−− hybrid state at 4285 ± 14 MeV
- m_X(3915) - m_Ds - m_Ds ≅ -15 MeV and m_Pc(4312) - m_Sigmac+ - m_D0 ≅ -7 MeV
- m_Zc+(3900) - m_D+ - m_D*0 ≅ 10 MeV and m_Zb+(10610) - m_B0 - m_B*+ ≅ +3 MeV
- A naive count estimates well over 100 more exotics awaiting discovery

## Context
This paper extends the line of work on exotic hadron phenomenology that began with the discovery of X(3872) by Belle (2003) and the foundational quark-model papers of Gell-Mann (1964) and Zweig (1964), which already noted multiquark possibilities. It builds upon the Snowmass 2021 white paper on multiquark hadrons (Brambilla et al., 2022), which noted that both compact and molecular configurations may appear in Nature. The paper enables future work by identifying which experimental measurements, particularly transitions between exotic states and branching ratios to quarkonium versus open-flavor pairs, will be essential for determining which exotics share similar underlying structures.
