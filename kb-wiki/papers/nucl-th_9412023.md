# Inclusive Pion Double Charge Exchange above 0.5 GeV

> A cascade model has been developed to study pion induced multichannel reactions (quasielastic, SCX, DCX, absorption and pion production) at pion energies above 0.5 GeV. Special attention has been paid to pion double charge exchange. Pion production is a determinant feature in the high energy pion nucleus reactions, and the DCX signal not related to pion production is sizeable only at forward angles and for high energy outgoing pions. The contribution to DCX of the conventional mechanism, with two quasielastic SCX steps decreases very fast as a function of the energy and reaches very low values at energies above 0.7 GeV. This opens the opportunity of having sizeable contributions of exotic mechanisms that are negligible at the delta resonance energies.

# Inclusive Pion Double Charge Exchange above 0.5 GeV

## Key claim

The conventional sequential mechanism for inclusive pion double charge exchange (DCX), proceeding via two quasielastic single charge exchange (SCX) steps, decreases very fast with energy and reaches very low values at energies above 0.6 GeV, opening the opportunity for sizeable contributions from exotic mechanisms (such as meson exchange currents or six-quark bags) that are negligible at delta resonance energies but may not decrease as rapidly at high energies.

## Method

A Monte Carlo cascade simulation was developed to model pion-induced multichannel reactions (quasielastic scattering, single charge exchange, double charge exchange, absorption, and pion production) on nuclei. The simulation uses elementary pi-N cross sections from Arndt's phase shifts for elastic and charge-exchange scattering, parametrized two- and three-nucleon absorption probabilities from the imaginary part of the pion self-energy, and parametrized single-pion production cross sections for the five independent channels induced by charged pions. Pions are propagated stepwise through the nuclear medium with random selection of interaction type, scattering angles, and energies, including Pauli blocking and Fermi motion of nucleons.

## Key numbers

- The quasielastic peak for pi+ scattering on 12C at 0.5 GeV is well reproduced in both size and width at all angles, using a Fermi momentum of 250 MeV throughout the nucleus.
- The integrated DCX cross section (with a cut requiring final pion energy within 150 MeV of beam energy) decreases by almost 3 orders of magnitude from 0.5 GeV to 1.3 GeV, while the quasielastic channel changes by about a factor of 2 and SCX loses one order of magnitude over the same range.
- For pi+ + 40Ca at 600 MeV and above, DCX is totally dominated by pion production except for the small region of phase space where pion production is forbidden.
- For pi+ + 208Pb at 1200 MeV, quasifree DCX is hardly visible in the double differential cross section due to the smallness of the pi-N charge exchange cross section at this energy.
- The two-body absorption mechanism becomes dominant at high energies, with the three-body absorption decreasing again as pion energy increases above the resonance region.

## Context

This work extends earlier cascade model calculations by Salcedo et al. (1988) and Vicente-Vacas et al. (1990) that described pi-nucleus inclusive reactions around the delta resonance, now applied to energies above 0.5 GeV where distortion is weaker and the semiclassical approximation is more justifiable. The results are compared with experimental data from Zumbro et al. (1987) for quasielastic scattering and from Petrov et al. (1988) for single charge exchange at 0.5 GeV, and with the cascade-exciton model (CEM) of Mashnik et al. The paper is motivated by ongoing measurements at ITEP (Moscow) and predicts that the best place to isolate a clean non-production DCX signal is at forward angles and for high-energy outgoing pions. The findings are consistent with earlier exclusive DCX calculations by Oset et al. (1994) showing deep minima in the sequential contribution at energies around 700 and 1300 MeV, and suggest that inclusive DCX measurements at these energies could reveal exotic mechanisms such as meson exchange currents (Mikkel et al.), six-quark bags (Miller et al.), or mechanisms involving more than two nucleons (Jeanneret et al.).
