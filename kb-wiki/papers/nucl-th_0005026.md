# Cross sections for Coulomb and nuclear breakup of three-body halo nuclei

> All possible dissociation cross sections for the loosely bound three-body halo nuclei $^6$He (n+n+$α$) and $^{11}$Li (n+n+$^{9}$Li) are computed as functions of target and beam energy. Both Coulomb and nuclear interactions are included in the same theoretical framework. The measurements agree with the calculations for energies above 100 Mev/nucleon. The largest cross sections correspond to final states with zero or three particles for heavy and with two neutrons for light targets.

## Key claim

The authors compute all possible dissociation cross sections for the three-body halo nuclei \(^{6}\)He and \(^{11}\)Li on C, Cu, and Pb targets, treating Coulomb and nuclear interactions within the same theoretical framework; the calculations agree with measurements only for beam energies above 100 MeV/nucleon, with the largest cross sections corresponding to final states with zero or three particles for heavy targets and with two neutrons for light targets.

## Method

The reaction is described by three independent collisions of one projectile constituent (participant) at a time, with the remaining two particles treated as spectators. The participant-target interaction is described by the optical model, while simultaneous interactions of additional constituents with the target are handled via a black-sphere model that determines absorption based on whether the constituent lies inside a cylinder of radius equal to the sum of target and constituent radii. For charged participants, the long-range Coulomb interaction is split into a low-momentum-transfer part (treated as elastic scattering of the core) and a high-momentum-transfer part (included in the participant absorption and diffraction), with an adiabatic cutoff applied to remove virtual excitations at large impact parameters.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{6}\)He on Pb at 240 MeV/nucleon: \(\sigma_{nnc} = 940\) mb, \(\sigma_{nc} = 96\) mb, \(\sigma_{c} = 113\) mb (computed); experimental values are \(650 \pm 110\) mb, \(320 \pm 90\) mb, and \(180 \pm 100\) mb respectively.
- For \(^{11}\)Li on Pb at 280 MeV/nucleon: \(\sigma_{nnc} = 1476\) mb, \(\sigma_{nc} = 338\) mb, \(\sigma_{c} = 116\) mb (computed); experimental values are \(1000 \pm 350\) mb, \(1000 \pm 350\) mb, and \(\leq 70\) mb respectively.
- For \(^{6}\)He on C at 240 MeV/nucleon: \(\sigma_{nnc} = 43\) mb, \(\sigma_{nc} = 93\) mb, \(\sigma_{c} = 37\) mb (computed); experimental values are \(30 \pm 5\) mb, \(127 \pm 14\) mb, and \(33 \pm 23\) mb respectively.
- For \(^{11}\)Li on C at 280 MeV/nucleon: \(\sigma_{nnc} = 64\) mb, \(\sigma_{nc} = 142\) mb, \(\sigma_{c} = 22\) mb (computed); experimental values are \(60 \pm 20\) mb, \(170 \pm 20\) mb, and \(50 \pm 10\) mb respectively.
- Coulomb dissociation cross section for \(^{11}\)Li on Pb at 800 MeV/nucleon: 765 mb (this work), compared to 960 mb from Johansen (1990) and 610–660 mb from Suzuki (1990).
- Coulomb dissociation cross section for \(^{11}\)Li on Cu at 800 MeV/nucleon: 81 mb (this work), compared to 86–92 mb from Suzuki (1990).
- For \(^{11}\)Li on Pb at 180, 280, and 800 MeV/nucleon: Coulomb dissociation cross sections of 2050 mb, 1401 mb, and 765 mb respectively (this work), compared to 2128 mb, 1429 mb, and 971 mb from Cobis (1998).
- Root-mean-square neutron-core distance: \(\langle r_{cn}^2 \rangle^{1/2} = 4.2\) fm for \(^{6}\)He and 5.9 fm for \(^{11}\)Li.

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical studies of two-neutron halo dissociation, such as the eikonal calculations by Bertsch (1998) for \(^{11}\)Li and \(^{6}\)He on carbon, and the three-body continuum calculations by Cobis (1998) for Coulomb dissociation of \(^{11}\)Li. It provides the first systematic treatment of all possible final-state channels (including core destruction) within a single model that simultaneously includes Coulomb and nuclear interactions, enabling direct comparison with experimental data from Kobayashi (1989), Blank (1993), Zinser (1997), and Aumann (1999). The results encourage new measurements of the predicted absolute cross sections for specific particle multiplicities.
