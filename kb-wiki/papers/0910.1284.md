# Microscopic calculation of 240Pu scission with a finite-range effective force

> Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations of hot fission in $^{240}\textrm{Pu}$ have been performed with a newly-implemented code that uses the D1S finite-range effective interaction. The hot-scission line is identified in the quadrupole-octupole-moment coordinate space. Fission-fragment shapes are extracted from the calculations. A benchmark calculation for $^{226}\textrm{Th}$ is obtained and compared to results in the literature. In addition, technical aspects of the use of HFB calculations for fission studies are examined in detail. In particular, the identification of scission configurations, the sensitivity of near-scission calculations to the choice of collective coordinates in the HFB iterations, and the formalism for the adjustment of collective-variable constraints are discussed. The power of the constraint-adjustment algorithm is illustrated with calculations near the critical scission configurations with up to seven simultaneous constraints.

## Key claim

The hot-scission line in $^{240}\mathrm{Pu}$ is identified in quadrupole-octupole-moment coordinate space using Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations with the D1S finite-range effective interaction, but the identification of scission configurations requires extremely small variations of the constraints (steps of $1\ \mathrm{b}^{2}$ in hexadecapole moment) and the introduction of a neck-particle-number constraint to obtain smooth, continuous energy curves.

## Method

HFB calculations were performed with the newly-implemented code FRANCHBRIE using the D1S finite-range effective interaction in a deformed harmonic-oscillator basis with axial symmetry and z-signature symmetry imposed. The nucleus was driven to scission by imposing multiple constraints on the quadrupole ($Q_{20}$), octupole ($Q_{30}$), dipole ($Q_{10}$), and hexadecapole ($Q_{40}$) moments, as well as on proton and neutron numbers, using a linear-constraint method with an iterative convergence algorithm that mixes generalized densities between iterations and adjusts Lagrange multipliers. A neck-particle-number operator $\hat{Q}_N$ was introduced to smoothly control the scission transition. A benchmark calculation for $^{226}\mathrm{Th}$ was performed and compared to results from Dubray et al. (2008).

## Key numbers

- For $^{240}\mathrm{Pu}$ at quadrupole moment $Q_{20}=300\ \mathrm{b}$ (cold fission), a barrier of height approximately $4.0\ \mathrm{MeV}$ relative to the fission-valley minimum separates the fission and fusion valleys.
- For $^{240}\mathrm{Pu}$ at $Q_{20}=370\ \mathrm{b}$ (hot fission), a drop of $7.6\ \mathrm{MeV}$ occurs in going from $\langle \hat{Q}_{40}\rangle =190\ \mathrm{b}^{2}$ to $189\ \mathrm{b}^{2}$.
- For $^{240}\mathrm{Pu}$ at $Q_{20}=300\ \mathrm{b}$ (cold fission), a drop of $2.7\ \mathrm{MeV}$ occurs in going from $\langle \hat{Q}_{40}\rangle =110\ \mathrm{b}^{2}$ to $109\ \mathrm{b}^{2}$.
- The oscillator frequencies that minimize the HFB energy for $^{240}\mathrm{Pu}$ follow $\hbar\omega_0 = 8.4345 - 0.0021668\, Q_{20}$ (in MeV, with $Q_{20}$ in barns) and $\omega_\perp/\omega_z = 1.7041 + 0.0028743\, Q_{20}$.
- The HFB convergence criterion reached $\varepsilon < 10^{-4}$ after 156 iterations for the cold-fission point at $\langle \hat{Q}_{40}\rangle =110\ \mathrm{b}^{2}$.

## Context

This work extends the microscopic description of fission developed by the group at Bruyeres-le-Chatel (Berger et al., 1984; Goutte et al., 2005; Dubray et al., 2008) by introducing a newly-implemented HFB code with a finite-range effective interaction and examining in detail the technical aspects of near-scission calculations. The identification of the hot-scission line in $^{240}\mathrm{Pu}$ and the demonstration that a neck-particle-number constraint yields smooth energy curves at scission enable future work on a more microscopic definition of scission and fission-fragment properties, including interaction energies between fragments and quantities such as excitation energy, shape, kinetic energy, emitted-neutron multiplicity, and angular momentum.
