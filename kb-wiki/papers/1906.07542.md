# Limits on assigning a shape to a nucleus

> The interpretation of nuclear observables in the laboratory frame in terms of the intrinsic deformation parameters beta and gamma is a classical theme in nuclear structure. Here we use the quadrupole invariants (Kumar), calculated within the framework of the configuration-interaction shell model, to clarify the meaning and limitations of nuclear shapes. We introduce a novel method that enables us to calculate accurately higher-order invariants and, therefore, the fluctuations in both beta and gamma. We find that the shape parameter beta often has a non-negligible degree of softness, and that the angle gamma is usually characterized by large fluctuations, rendering its effective value not meaningful. Contrary to common belief, we conclude that doubly magic nuclei are not spherical because the notion of a well-defined shape does not apply to them.

## Key claim

The paper argues that assigning a well-defined shape (characterized by the Bohr-Mottelson parameters β and γ) to a nucleus is often not meaningful, because the fluctuations in β are frequently non-negligible and the fluctuations in γ are almost invariably large; contrary to common belief, doubly magic nuclei are not spherical because the notion of a well-defined shape does not apply to them.

## Method

The authors use the configuration-interaction (CI) shell model to compute expectation values of quadrupole invariants up to sixth order (Q^2 through Q^6) for the ground states and selected excited 0+ states of even-even nuclei in the mass region A ~ 20–76. They introduce a novel method based on the projected Lanczos strength function technique, iteratively applying the axial quadrupole operator Q_20 to generate doorway states and extracting the required invariants from sum rules. From these invariants they calculate the effective values of β and γ and their variances (fluctuations).

## Context

This work extends the method of quadrupole invariants introduced by Kumar (1972) and applied by Cline (1986) and others, using the CI shell model to compute higher-order invariants that were previously inaccessible. It contradicts recent claims by Ge76, Ge76a, and Se76 that the A=76 isobars of germanium and selenium are rigid triaxial, showing instead that they exhibit large γ fluctuations. The findings also challenge the common characterization of doubly magic nuclei as spherical, and suggest that nuclear states are better described by a probability distribution of intrinsic shapes (Alhassid 2018) rather than by single values of β and γ.
