# $^{40}$Ca transverse response function from coupled-cluster theory

> We present calculations of the $^{40}$Ca transverse response function obtained from coupled-cluster theory used in conjunction with the Lorentz integral transform method. We employ nuclear forces derived at next-to-next-to leading order in chiral effective field theory with and without $Δ$ degrees of freedom. We first benchmark this approach on the $^4$He nucleus and compare both the transverse sum rule and the response function to earlier calculations based on different methods. As expected from the power counting of the chiral expansion of electromagnetic currents and from previous studies, our results retaining only one-body term underestimate the experimental data for $^4$He by about $20\%$. However, when the method is applied to $^{40}$Ca at the same order of the expansion, response functions do not lack strength and agree well with the world electron scattering data. We discuss various sources of theoretical uncertainties and comment on the comparison of our results with the available experiments.

# $^{40}$Ca transverse response function from coupled-cluster theory

## Key claim

The authors present the first *ab initio* calculation of the transverse response function for $^{40}$Ca using coupled-cluster theory with the Lorentz integral transform method, retaining only one-body currents. While one-body currents underestimate the $^4$He data by about 20% as expected, the $^{40}$Ca results at the same order of the chiral expansion do not lack strength and agree well with world electron scattering data, suggesting that two-body currents may play a different role in medium-mass nuclei than in light nuclei.

## Method

The transverse response function is computed using coupled-cluster theory with single and double excitations (CCSD) combined with the Lorentz integral transform method, which reformulates the sum over continuum final states into a bound-state-like equation with a source term. Nuclear forces are taken at next-to-next-to-leading order in chiral effective field theory using the NNLOsat potential (without $\Delta$ degrees of freedom) and the $\Delta$NNLO$_{\rm GO}$(450) potential (with $\Delta$ degrees of freedom), both with a 450 MeV/c regulator cutoff. The calculations are performed in a model space of 15 major oscillator shells with an additional $e_{3\rm max} \le 16$ cut on the three-body potential, and the response is obtained by inverting the Lorentz integral transform using an exponential basis expansion.

## Key numbers

- $^4$He transverse response at $q=300$ and $400$ MeV/c shows approximately 20% missing strength relative to experimental data when using only one-body currents, consistent with expectations from chiral power counting.
- For $^{40}$Ca at $q=300$ MeV/c, the response functions from NNLOsat and $\Delta$NNLO$_{\rm GO}$(450) potentials agree well with the world data re-analysis by Jourdan (1996), with differences between the two chiral orders at the level of a few percent.
- The number of multipoles needed for convergence in $^{40}$Ca grows from 4 at $q=100$ MeV/c to 12 at $q=400$ MeV/c (for $^4$He these are 3 and 6, respectively).
- The proton separation energy used for inversion is $\omega_{th}=6.9$ MeV for NNLOsat and $\omega_{th}=8$ MeV for $\Delta$NNLO$_{\rm GO}$(450).
- Experimental data sets for $^{40}$Ca at $q=400$ MeV/c (Williamson, 1997) and $q=410$ MeV/c (Meziani, 1984) differ by up to 30% in the quasielastic peak, with the authors' calculations agreeing considerably better with Williamson (1997).

## Context

This work extends the LIT-CC method, previously applied to the longitudinal response function for $^4$He and $^{40}$Ca (Sobczyk, 2020; Sobczyk, 2021), to the transverse channel for the first time in medium-mass nuclei. It benchmarks against Green's Function Monte Carlo calculations for light nuclei up to $^{12}$C (Lovato, 2014; Lovato, 2015; Lovato, 2017; Lovato, 2020) and hyperspherical harmonics calculations for $^4$He (Bacca, 2006), finding consistent underestimation of $^4$He data due to the omission of two-body currents. The results for $^{40}$Ca enable future inclusion of two-body currents and study of many-body truncation effects, and highlight the need for new experimental measurements to resolve the tension between existing Rosenbluth-separated data sets, which is relevant for constraining theoretical predictions for neutrino oscillation experiments.
