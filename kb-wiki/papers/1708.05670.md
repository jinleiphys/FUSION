# Measurement of the Lorentz-FitzGerald Body Contraction

## Key claim

The paper argues that a first measurement of the Lorentz-FitzGerald body contraction is feasible using back-scattering of a probing laser beam from a relativistic flying electron cloud mirror generated by an ultra-intense laser pulse, but only if the electron cloud density is sufficiently high for coherent scattering and the acceleration process is understood as a dynamical transfer between inertial frames rather than a purely kinematic Lorentz transformation.

## Method

The author proposes accelerating an electron cloud to ultrarelativistic speed by firing an ultra-intense ultra-short laser pulse at a thin micron foil, creating a relativistic mirror. A second, low-intensity probing laser beam is then back-scattered from this moving electron cloud mirror. The condition for coherent upscattering of the reflected light into the X-ray regime depends on the Lorentz-factor of the electron cloud and the electron mean separation within the cloud, where the Lorentz-FitzGerald contraction of the cloud's thickness in the direction of motion modifies the effective electron density and thus the coherent scattering condition.

## Key numbers

- The Lorentz-factor for the electron cloud is given by $\gamma_e = 1/\sqrt{1-\beta^2}$, where $\beta = v/c$, and the emerging electron cloud is compressed relative to the original foil thickness by a factor of $\gamma_e$.
- For coherent upscattering of the reflected light into the X-ray regime, the condition is $\gamma_e^\mathrm{max} \lesssim (\lambda/d_e)^a$ with $a \simeq 1/3$, where $\lambda$ is the probing laser wavelength and $d_e$ is the electron mean separation within the moving mirror cloud.
- The paper notes that relativistic atomic nuclei at the LHC achieve $\gamma_\mathrm{HI} \simeq 5000$, and suggests that comparable $\gamma_e$ values could become available at Extreme Light Infrastructure (ELI) facilities in Europe.
- The visible light in coherent scattering is stepped up in energy by a factor proportional to $\gamma^2$, as pointed out by Einstein in the 1905 Special Relativity paper.

## Context

This work extends the Lorentz-Bell pedagogical approach to Special Relativity, which relies on acceleration to dynamically transfer a body between inertial frames, as championed by Bell (1976) and earlier by Lorentz. The paper contrasts this with Einstein's 1905 approach that sidestepped acceleration entirely. The proposed experiment would provide the first direct measurement of the Lorentz-FitzGerald body contraction, which the author argues has never been experimentally observed despite being invoked to explain the null result of the Michelson-Morley experiment. The work builds on prior proposals for relativistic electron mirrors as compact X-ray sources (Boulanov; Kiefer) and on the author's own textbook treatment (Rafelski, 2017).
