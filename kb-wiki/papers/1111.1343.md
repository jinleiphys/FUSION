# Nuclear condensate and helium white dwarfs

> We consider a high density region of the helium phase diagram, where the nuclei form a Bose-Einstein condensate rather than a classical plasma or a crystal. Helium in this phase may be present in helium-core white dwarfs. We show that in this regime there is a new gapless quasiparticle not previously noticed, arising when the constraints imposed by gauge symmetry are taken into account. The contribution of this quasiparticle to the specific heat of a white dwarf core turns out to be comparable in a range of temperatures to the contribution from the particle-hole excitations of the degenerate electrons. The specific heat in the condensed phase is two orders of magnitude smaller than in the uncondensed plasma phase, which is the ground state at higher temperatures, and four orders of magnitude smaller than the specific heat that an ion lattice would provide, if formed. Since the specific heat of the core is an important input for setting the rate of cooling of a white dwarf star, it may turn out that such a change in the thermal properties of the cores of helium white dwarfs has observable implications.

## Key claim

The authors show that in a Bose-Einstein condensate of helium nuclei (alpha particles) in the core of a helium white dwarf, a previously unnoticed gapless quasiparticle mode exists, arising from the interplay of gauge invariance and dynamical electrons. The specific heat of the condensed phase is two orders of magnitude smaller than in the uncondensed plasma phase and four orders of magnitude smaller than in an ion lattice, which could significantly alter white dwarf cooling rates, but the authors hedge that whether this explains the early termination of the observed helium white dwarf sequence "is not yet clear."

## Method

The authors construct an effective field theory of helium nuclei (treated as pointlike bosons), electrons, and photons at densities where the interparticle spacing satisfies \(R_N \ll l \ll a_0\). They integrate out the electrons to obtain a photon polarization tensor, fix unitary gauge, and expand the Lagrangian to quadratic order in fluctuations around the condensate. By solving the resulting dispersion relations for the mixed \(h\)-\(A_0\)-\(\mathbf{A}^L\) sector, they identify the quasiparticle poles, including a gapless mode with linear dispersion that depends on the electron screening mass and the ion plasma frequency.

## Key numbers

* The critical mass density for the onset of Bose-Einstein condensation is estimated to lie between \(2 \times 10^4\ \mathrm{g/cm^3}\) and \(2 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{g/cm^3}\), depending on the estimate of the critical temperature.
* The gapless mode has a linear dispersion relation \(p_0 = (m_A/m_s)\,p\) with a small imaginary part suppressed by a factor \(\pi x/(4v_F) \ll 1\); numerically \(m_A/(v_F m_s) \approx 0.0012\,(1+m^2/k_F^2)^{1/4}\sqrt{a_0/l}\).
* The gapped mode (plasma oscillation) has energy \(p_0 \approx 500\ \mathrm{eV}\,(1+m^2/k_F^2)^{-1/4}(a_0/l)\), corresponding to about \(5.8\times 10^6\ \mathrm{K}\,(1+m^2/k_F^2)^{-1/4}(a_0/l)\).
* The ion plasma frequency scale \(m_A\) is approximately \(1.3\times 10^4\ \mathrm{K}\,(a_0/l)^{3/2}\), which for a reference density of \(5\times 10^5\ \mathrm{g/cm^3}\) (\(a_0/l \sim 35\)) gives \(2.6\times 10^6\ \mathrm{K}\).
* The specific heat in the condensed phase is two orders of magnitude smaller than in the uncondensed plasma phase and four orders of magnitude smaller than in an ion lattice.

## Context

This work extends the proposal of Gabadadze et al. (2008, 2009) that helium nuclei in white dwarf cores may Bose-condense, by computing the full quasiparticle spectrum beyond the Thomas-Fermi approximation. The authors find a new gapless mode missed in those earlier studies (Gabadadze 2008, 2009; Mosquera 2010) because gauge-invariance constraints on the electron polarization tensor were not fully accounted for. The results enable more realistic modeling of helium white dwarf cooling, potentially explaining the early termination of the helium white dwarf sequence observed by Soszynski et al. (2009), though the authors caution that this remains an open question.
