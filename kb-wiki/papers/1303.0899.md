# Neutrinoless double beta decay and lepton number violation at the LHC

> We compare the discovery potential of the LHC for lepton number violating (LNV) signals with the sensitivity of current and future double beta decay experiments, assuming 0νββdecay is dominated by heavy particle exchange. We consider charged scalar, leptoquark and diquark mechanisms of 0νββ-decay, covering the 0νββ-decay operators with both, the smallest and largest, possible rates. We demonstrate, if 0νββ-decay were found with a half-life below $10^{26}-10^{27}$ ys a positive signal should show up at the LHC, except for some particular cases of the leptoquark mechanism, and vice versa, if the LHC does not find any hints for LNV, a "short-range" explanation for a finite 0νββ-decay half-life will be ruled out in most cases. We argue, if a positive LNV signal were found at the LHC, it is possible to identify the dominant contribution to 0νββ. Two different kinds of observables which could provide such "model discriminating" power are discussed: Different invariant mass peaks and the charge asymmetry.

# Neutrinoless double beta decay and lepton number violation at the LHC

## Key claim

If neutrinoless double beta decay were found with a half-life below 10^26–10^27 years, a positive lepton number violating signal should show up at the LHC, except for some particular cases of the leptoquark mechanism; conversely, if the LHC does not find any hints for lepton number violation, a "short-range" explanation for a finite neutrinoless double beta decay half-life will be ruled out in most cases.

## Method

The authors compare the discovery potential of the LHC for lepton number violating signals with the sensitivity of current and future double beta decay experiments, assuming neutrinoless double beta decay is dominated by heavy particle exchange. They consider charged scalar, leptoquark, and diquark mechanisms of neutrinoless double beta decay, covering the decay operators with both the smallest and largest possible rates. They calculate production cross sections for the relevant scalar bosons at the LHC using CalcHEP and MadGraph 5, and compare these to the parameter space excluded or reachable by double beta decay experiments for half-lives between 10^25 and 10^27 years.

## Context

This work extends the general decomposition of the dimension-9 neutrinoless double beta decay operator given by Bonnet (2012), which identified several new diagrams requiring exotic particles such as diquarks or colored fermions with fractional charges. The paper builds on searches by ATLAS (2012) and CMS (2012) for like-sign dilepton plus jets events, which were interpreted in the left-right symmetric model. The results enable experimentalists to distinguish between different lepton number violating models using invariant mass peaks and charge asymmetry, should a positive signal be found at the LHC.
