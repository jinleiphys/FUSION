# The proton radius: From a puzzle to precision

> 3 pp, solicited article for News&Views, Science Bulletin, accepted for publication, abstract only for the arXiv version

## Key claim

The proton radius puzzle is now considered solved, as recent measurements of the electronic Lamb shift and low-energy electron-proton scattering, combined with dispersion-theoretical analyses of electron scattering data, consistently yield a small proton radius around 0.84 fm, in agreement with the original muonic hydrogen value.

## Method

The authors review the history of proton charge radius determinations from electron scattering and from the Lamb shift in both electronic and muonic hydrogen. They highlight that dispersion relations, which incorporate unitarity, crossing, and analyticity, provide a model-independent framework for analyzing nucleon form factor data, and that such analyses have consistently favored a small radius when including the two-pion continuum contribution.

## Key numbers

- Muonic hydrogen Lamb shift measurement gave a small radius of 0.84184(67) fm, differing by 5σ from the then-standard CODATA value (Pohl 2010).
- Dispersion-theoretical reanalysis of Mainz Microtron (MAMI) electron-proton scattering data gave a small radius of 0.84(1) fm (Lorenz 2012).
- A refined dispersion analysis including two-photon exchange gave a radius of 0.840 (0.828 – 0.855) fm (Lorenz 2014).
- New electronic Lamb shift measurements: 0.8335(95) fm (Beyer 2017), 0.877(13) fm (Fleurbaey 2018), and 0.833(10) fm (Bezginov 2019).
- New low-energy electron-proton scattering gave 0.831(7)(12) fm (JLab 2019).
- The 2019 CODATA compilation lists the proton charge radius as 0.8414(19) fm.

## Context

This paper extends the dispersion-theoretical approach pioneered by the Karlsruhe group (Hohler 1976) and further developed by the Bonn-Mainz group (Mergell 1995, Hammer 1996), which had already anticipated the puzzle in 2007 (Belushkin 2007) by showing that a large radius of 0.88–0.90 fm could hardly be accommodated by form factor data under unitarity and analyticity constraints. The convergence of new electronic Lamb shift measurements and low-energy scattering data with the muonic hydrogen value now enables a path toward increased precision in the determination of this fundamental quantity, with improved dispersion analyses incorporating the latest two-pion continuum determination (Hoferichter 2016) and complementary muon scattering experiments planned.
