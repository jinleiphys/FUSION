# Generative deep-learning reveals collective variables of Fermionic systems

> Complex processes ranging from protein folding to nuclear fission often follow a low-dimension reaction path parameterized in terms of a few collective variables. In nuclear theory, variables related to the shape of the nuclear density in a mean-field picture are key to describing the large amplitude collective motion of the neutrons and protons. Exploring the adiabatic energy landscape spanned by these degrees of freedom reveals the possible reaction channels while simulating the dynamics in this reduced space yields their respective probabilities. Unfortunately, this theoretical framework breaks down whenever the systems encounters a quantum phase transition with respect to the collective variables. Here we propose a generative-deep-learning algorithm capable of building new collective variables highly representative of a nuclear process while ensuring a differentiable mapping to its Fermionic wave function. Within this collective space, the nucleus can evolve continuously from one of its adiabatic quantum phase to the other at the price of crossing a potential energy barrier. This approach applies to any Fermionic system described by a single Slater determinant, which encompasses electronic systems described within the density functional theory.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a generative deep-learning algorithm that builds new collective variables for Fermionic systems, ensuring a differentiable mapping to the Slater determinant wave function; this allows the nucleus to evolve continuously between adiabatic quantum phases, but the height of the resulting potential barrier varies with a standard deviation of 2.1 MeV depending on the training initialization.

## Method

The authors used a set of 200 quadrupole-constrained Hartree-Fock states of ¹⁶O, parameterized via the Thouless theorem relative to a Karcher mean pivotal Slater determinant in the Grassmannian manifold. They applied principal component analysis (PCA) to reduce the Thouless coordinates to 20 components, then trained a variational autoencoder (VAE) to further compress to a single latent variable λ, ensuring differentiability through the use of differentiable activation functions.

## Key numbers

- The Fubini-Study distance between quantum states B and C is two orders of magnitude higher than typical between other neighboring states.
- The PCA compression to 20 principal components yields a root mean square error lower than 10⁻⁷ for the Z matrices in the training set.
- The root mean square reconstruction error on the Hartree-Fock energy is below 0.3% of the ground state energy for training, validation, and test sets.
- The root mean square errors on the quadrupole and octupole moments are below 0.01% of their respective variation range.
- The height of the new potential barrier between configurations B and C is 13.2 MeV, with a standard deviation of 2.1 MeV across multiple VAE training runs.

## Context

This work extends the constrained Hartree-Fock approach to nuclear collective variables (Bender 2003) by addressing quantum phase transitions that cause discontinuities in the adiabatic path, which previous methods either smoothed pragmatically (Ho 1996, Hurd 2010) or attempted to avoid through local path modifications (Lau 2022). The method enables the use of the time-dependent generator coordinate method for predicting half-lives of metastable states and reaction probabilities, and is applicable to any Fermionic system described by a single Slater determinant, including electronic systems in density functional theory.
