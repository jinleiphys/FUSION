# Bayesian optimization in ab initio nuclear physics

> Theoretical models of the strong nuclear interaction contain unknown coupling constants (parameters) that must be determined using a pool of calibration data. In cases where the models are complex, leading to time consuming calculations, it is particularly challenging to systematically search the corresponding parameter domain for the best fit to the data. In this paper, we explore the prospect of applying Bayesian optimization to constrain the coupling constants in chiral effective field theory descriptions of the nuclear interaction. We find that Bayesian optimization performs rather well with low-dimensional parameter domains and foresee that it can be particularly useful for optimization of a smaller set of coupling constants. A specific example could be the determination of leading three-nucleon forces using data from finite nuclei or three-nucleon scattering experiments.

# Bayesian optimization in ab initio nuclear physics

## Key claim
Bayesian optimization performs rather well for low-dimensional parameter domains (up to D=12) in chiral effective field theory, but the authors explicitly hedge that it is foreseen to be "particularly useful for optimization of a smaller set of coupling constants" such as leading three-nucleon forces, rather than a general replacement for existing optimization methods across all parameter dimensions.

## Method
The authors applied Bayesian optimization using Gaussian process surrogate models with three kernel choices (squared exponential, Matern 3/2, Matern 5/2), two acquisition functions (expected improvement and lower confidence-bound), and with or without automatic relevance determination. They benchmarked 12 BayesOpt variants on six test functions in D=2,4,8 dimensions using data profiles, then applied the method to optimize the 12 low-energy constants at next-to-next-to-leading order in chiral effective field theory using proton-neutron scattering data from the Granada database with laboratory energies below 75 MeV, comparing performance against the POUNDERs algorithm.

## Key numbers
- 12 low-energy constants (LECs) were optimized at next-to-next-to-leading order in chiral effective field theory
- Proton-neutron scattering data used with laboratory energies of the incident proton beam below 75 MeV
- Each evaluation of the objective function took "a couple of seconds on a standard desktop computer"
- Evaluating the 4,096 corners in the hypercube of the corresponding parameter domain took "a couple of hours"
- For test functions in D=2, d(50) ~ 0.7, meaning approximately 70% of problems converged at the tau=0.1 level within 50 function evaluations
- At 150 function evaluations, more than 80% of test functions in D=2 were converged
- Maximum of 250 function calls per solver and problem was allowed
- N=2D starting points were used for each D-dimensional domain (e.g., N=24 for D=12)
- Convergence criterion used tau=0.1 (90% reduction in function value) and occasionally tau=0.01

## Context
This work extends the application of Bayesian optimization, popularized in the 1990s by Mockus and Jones and recently applied to hyperparameter tuning of deep neural networks by Snoek (2012), into ab initio nuclear physics where chiral effective field theory models contain 10-30 unknown low-energy constants. The authors note that prior work by Elhatisari (2016), Ekstrom (2015), Lapoux (2016), and Hagen (2016) indicated that nucleon-nucleon scattering data and properties of very light nuclei do not contain enough information to constrain all parameter directions, motivating the need for optimization strategies that can handle computationally expensive evaluations of observables from finite nuclei or three-nucleon scattering. The work enables future application of Bayesian optimization to determine leading three-nucleon forces using data from finite nuclei or three-nucleon scattering experiments, where each model evaluation may require hours to days on a supercomputer.
