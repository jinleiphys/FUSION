# Neutrino oscillations in the formal theory of scattering

> Scattering theory in the Gell-Mann and Goldberger formulation is slightly extended to render a Hamiltonian quantum mechanical description of the neutrino oscillations.

## Key claim

The standard neutrino oscillation formula can be derived from a Hamiltonian quantum-mechanical treatment by extending the Gell-Mann and Goldberger formulation of scattering theory to long-baseline experiments, provided the beam preparation time is sufficiently short and the neutrino masses are sufficiently small relative to the neutrino momentum.

## Method

The authors extend the Gell-Mann and Goldberger formal scattering theory to finite times longer than the beam preparation time τ = ε⁻¹, using the T2K experiment as an example. They evaluate the transition rate for the process π⁺ n → μ⁺ μ⁻ p by expanding the Hamiltonian in the exponential factors to first order in H₀ and the resolvent to second order in H_I, then summing over intermediate neutrino mass eigenstates. The condition |mⱼ² ± mᵢ²|/(2|p_ν|) ≪ ε is imposed to approximate the energy denominators as mass-independent, yielding the standard oscillation formula for the ratio of muon-counting rates at different distances.

## Key numbers

- The beam preparation time τ in T2K is shorter than approximately 1 μs, while the time t for neutrinos to travel from Tokai to Kamioka is greater than about 1 ms, making t more than 10³ times greater than τ.
- The leading correction to the standard oscillation formula from using the approximate G_ji is expected to be smaller than cτ/L, which currently means smaller than 10⁻³ in T2K.
- The far detector distance L_far is approximately 300 km and the near detector distance L_near is approximately 280 m in T2K.
- The condition for the approximation to hold is |mⱼ² ± mᵢ²|/(2|p_ν|) ≪ ε, where ε = τ⁻¹ and the neutrino momentum is on the order of 1 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the Gell-Mann and Goldberger (1953) formulation of scattering theory to provide a Hamiltonian treatment of neutrino oscillations, filling a deficit noted in prior approaches. It builds on the theoretical framework of Pontecorvo (1967), Bilenky and Pontecorvo (1977), Kayser (1981), Rich (1993), and Giunti, Kim, Lee, and Lee (1993), and is consistent with the summary by Akhmedov and Kopp (2010). The derivation is limited to vacuum oscillations and does not include matter effects as treated by Wolfenstein (1977) and Mikheev and Smirnov (1986); the authors note that precise calculations require understanding of cutoff dependence and that evaluation of full cross sections remains a formidable task involving wave packets, density matrices, and experimental cuts.
