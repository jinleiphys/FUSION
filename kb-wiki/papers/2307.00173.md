# Source Function from Two-Particle Correlation Through Deblurring

> In heavy-ion collisions, low relative-velocity two-particle correlations have been a tool for assessing space-time characteristics of particle emission. Those characteristics may be cast in the form of a relative emission source related to the correlation function through the Koonin-Pratt (KP) convolution formula that involves the relative wave-function for the particles in its kernel. In the literature, the source has been most commonly sought by parametrizing it in a Gaussian form and fitting to the correlation function. At times the source was more broadly imaged from the function, still employing a fitting. Here, we propose the use of the Richardson-Lucy (RL) optical deblurring algorithm for deducing the source from a correlation function. The RL algorithm originally follows from probabilistic Bayesian considerations and relies on the intensity distributions for the optical object and its image, as well as the convolution kernel, being positive definite, which is the case for the corresponding quantities of interest within the KP formula.

## Key claim

The Richardson-Lucy (RL) optical deblurring algorithm, originally developed for Bayesian image restoration, can be adapted to infer the relative emission source function from two-particle correlation functions in heavy-ion collisions, provided the source and kernel are discretized and detector resolution effects are folded into the kernel.

## Method

The authors discretize the isotropic source function \(S(r)\) into \(M\) bins over a radial range up to \(r_{\text{max}} = 56\ \text{fm}\) and compute the Koonin-Pratt (KP) convolution kernel \(K(q,r)\) from angle-averaged squared relative wave functions for deuteron-alpha pairs, using phenomenological potentials fitted to measured phase shifts. The RL iterative deblurring algorithm is then applied to invert the KP relation, with detector resolution accounted for by convolving the kernel with a Gaussian of width \(\sigma_q\) in relative momentum. Optimal parameters (\(M\) and \(\sigma_q\)) are selected by minimizing \(\chi^2\) per degree of freedom between the measured and restored correlation functions.

## Key numbers

- The source distance range is limited to \(r_{\text{max}} = 56\ \text{fm}\) and divided into \(M \sim 14\) bins, yielding a source resolution of \(r_{\text{max}}/M \sim 4\ \text{fm}\).
- For the experimental \(d\)-\(\alpha\) correlation from \(^{40}\text{Ar}+^{27}\text{Al}\) collisions at \(E/A = 44\ \text{MeV}\), the optimal detector resolution parameter is \(\sigma_q = 3.7\ \text{MeV}/c\) (from \(\chi^2\) minimization) compared to an independent estimate of \(\sigma_q \simeq 3\ \text{MeV}/c\).
- The correlation function features a narrow resonance peak at \(q \sim 40\ \text{MeV}/c\) (the \(J^\pi=3^+\) resonance in \(^6\text{Li}\)) and a broad hump around \(q \sim 80\ \text{MeV}/c\) (overlapping \(2^+\) and \(1^+\) resonances).
- The regularization parameter used in the RL algorithm is \(\lambda = 0.015\).
- The restored source approaches low values by \(r \sim 10\ \text{fm}\) more abruptly than a Gaussian source with radius \(R_0 = 4.5\ \text{fm}\), but exhibits a tail at larger \(r\) that the Gaussian lacks.

## Context

This work extends the imaging approach to source extraction from correlation functions, previously pursued by Brown and Danielewicz (1997) and the E895 Collaboration (2003), by replacing direct inversion or parametric fitting with the Bayesian RL deblurring algorithm. The method is demonstrated on deuteron-alpha correlations measured by Ghetti et al. (2006) and builds on earlier Gaussian source fitting by Boal and Shillcock (1986) and others. The RL algorithm had previously been ported to nuclear physics for detector inefficiency and reaction-plane corrections by Danielewicz (2022); this work applies it to the distinct problem of source imaging from the KP formula. The approach enables non-parametric source extraction with quantified uncertainties, and the authors suggest it may be useful for other particle combinations and correlation measurements.
