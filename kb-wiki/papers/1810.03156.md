# Predictions of nuclear $β$-decay half-lives with machine learning and their impacts on $r$ process

> Nuclear $β$ decay is a key process to understand the origin of heavy elements in the universe, while the accuracy is far from satisfactory for the predictions of $β$-decay half-lives by nuclear models up to date. In this letter, we pave a novel way to accurately predict $β$-decay half-lives with the machine-learning based on the Bayesian neural network, in which the known physics has been explicitly embedded, including the ones described by the Fermi theory of $β$ decay, and the dependence of half-lives on pairing correlations and decay energies. The other potential physics, which is not clear or even missing in nuclear models nowadays, will be learned by the Bayesian neural network. The results well reproduce the experimental data with a very high accuracy and further provide reasonable uncertainty evaluations in half-life predictions. These accurate predictions for half-lives with uncertainties are essential for the $r$-process simulations.

## Key claim

The authors claim that a Bayesian neural network (BNN) approach, which embeds known physics from the Fermi theory of beta decay alongside pairing and decay-energy dependence, can predict nuclear beta-decay half-lives with significantly higher accuracy than existing nuclear models, achieving a root-mean-square deviation of only 0.20 in log10(half-life) for nuclei with half-lives under 1 second. The hedge is that this high accuracy is achieved for nuclei along or near the r-process path, and the method's predictive power relies on the BNN learning the "missing physics" that current nuclear models cannot capture.

## Method

The authors first constructed a simple two-parameter formula based on the Fermi theory of beta decay, using Q_beta values from the WS4 mass model and fitting parameters a=4.96 and b=8.51 to experimental half-lives from NUBASE2016. They then employed a Bayesian neural network (BNN) to model the residuals between the experimental log10(half-life) and the predictions of this simple formula. Two BNN variants were tested: BNN-I2 with inputs (Z, N) and BNN-I4 with inputs (Z, N, delta, Q_beta), where delta encodes pairing type and Q_beta is the decay energy; the networks had 30 and 20 hidden neurons respectively and were trained on 900 randomly selected nuclei from NUBASE2016.

## Key numbers

*   The simple two-parameter Fermi-theory formula reproduces known half-lives with a root-mean-square deviation sigma_rms(log10 T_1/2) = 0.81, comparable to the FRDM+QRPA model's 0.82.
*   For nuclei with half-lives shorter than 1 second (those along or near the r-process path), the BNN-I4 approach achieves sigma_rms(log10 T_1/2) = 0.20, meaning predictions are within a factor of 1.58 of experimental data.
*   When three artificial new half-life data points per isotope were added towards the neutron-drip line for N=126 isotones, the uncertainties of BNN-I4 predictions decreased by about a factor of 3 in the unknown region.

## Context

This work extends the application of machine learning to nuclear physics, specifically following the Bayesian neural network approach previously used for nuclear mass predictions by Utama (2016) and Niu (2018). It directly addresses the long-standing difficulty of predicting beta-decay half-lives, where models such as the gross theory (Takahashi 1969, Tachibana 1990), quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) approaches (Engel 1999, Moller 2003, Marketin 2016), and the shell model (Langanke 2003) have typically reproduced data only within a few orders of magnitude. The improved half-life predictions with quantified uncertainties enable more reliable r-process simulations, particularly for the A > 140 region where uncertainties are currently large, and demonstrate that a few additional measurements towards the neutron-drip line could substantially reduce these uncertainties.
