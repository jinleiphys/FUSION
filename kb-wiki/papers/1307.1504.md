# Bag vs. NJL models for color-flavor-locked strange quark matter

> We compare the mass-radius relationship of strange stars obtained in two theoretical frameworks describing the color-flavor-locking state of dense quark matter: The semi-empirical MIT model and a self-consistent approach using the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model. In the simplest MIT model extended to include pairing, one can make the equation of state stiffer by increasing the gap parameter so that larger maximum masses for these objects can be reached. In the NJL model, however, such an effect is not possible. To increase the gap parameter within the NJL model to values comparable to those considered in the MIT case, a noticeably increase of the diquark-coupling-constant strength is needed, but this in turn softens the equation of state producing a lower maximum star mass. This behaviour is interpreted as signalling the system crossover at high diquark coupling from a BCS regime to a BEC one, a process that cannot be reproduced within the simple MIT prescription.

# Bag vs. NJL models for color-flavor-locked strange quark matter

## Key claim
The MIT bag model and the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model produce qualitatively different mass-radius relationships for strange stars in the color-flavor-locked (CFL) phase: increasing the gap parameter stiffens the equation of state in the MIT model, allowing larger maximum masses, but in the NJL model the same increase requires a stronger diquark coupling that actually softens the equation of state, producing a lower maximum star mass. This behavior signals a crossover from a BCS regime to a BEC regime at high diquark coupling that the MIT prescription cannot reproduce.

## Method
The authors compared the thermodynamic potentials for CFL quark matter in the MIT bag model (where the gap parameter Δ is fixed by hand) and in the NJL model (where the gap is obtained self-consistently from the gap equation ∂Ω/∂Δ = 0). They computed the equation of state (energy density vs. pressure) for both models using zero quark masses, then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain mass-radius relations for strange stars. For the NJL model, they used diquark coupling constants G = 4.32, 5.15, and 7.10 GeV⁻², corresponding to gaps Δ = 10, 25, and 100 MeV at μ = 500 MeV respectively; for the MIT model they used fixed Δ values of 10 and 100 MeV with a bag constant B = 58 MeV/fm³.

## Key numbers
- Pulsar J1614-2230 has a measured mass M = 1.97 ± 0.04 M⊙ from Shapiro delay (Demorest 2010).
- In the NJL model, coupling constants G = 4.32, 5.15, and 7.10 GeV⁻² produce gaps Δ = 10, 25, and 100 MeV at μ = 500 MeV respectively.
- The critical coupling for the BCS-to-BEC crossover in the CFL-NJL model is Gcr ≈ 7.2 GeV⁻² (Israel).
- In the MIT model, the maximum crust mass supported by a strange star of mass ~1.4 M⊙ is estimated at ~10⁻⁵ M⊙ with thickness ~200 m (Alcock 1986).
- New data on black widow systems suggest compact star masses M = 2.1 – 2.9 M⊙ (Heavy1, Heavy2).

## Context
This work extends the comparison of MIT bag model and NJL model approaches to CFL strange quark matter, building on prior studies of color superconductivity in strange stars (Alford 2001, German). It directly contradicts the common assumption in MIT-based calculations (e.g., Alford 2001, German) that increasing the gap parameter always stiffens the equation of state and raises maximum masses. The results show that the NJL model, which treats the gap self-consistently, predicts a BCS-to-BEC crossover at high diquark coupling that limits maximum masses, suggesting that MIT calculations using large fixed gaps (e.g., Delta1, Delta2, Delta3) should be revisited. The authors note that an extended NJL model including vector interactions could stiffen the equation of state, but this depends on an unknown coupling strength.
