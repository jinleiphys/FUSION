# Reanalysis of $^{13}$N($p,γ$)$^{14}$O reaction and its role in stellar CNO cycle

> Within the framework of the modified potential cluster model with forbidden states, the $^{13}$N($p,γ$)$^{14}$O reaction rate and the astrophysical $S$-factor are considered. It is shown that the first $p^{13}$% N resonance determines the $S$-factor and contributions of the $M1$ and $E2$ transitions are negligible at energies $E<1$ MeV, but are significant at high energies. The $S$-factor strongly depends on the $^{3}S_{1}$ resonance parameters. The influence of the width of \ the $^{3}S_{1}$ resonance on $S$% -factor is demonstrated. The reaction rate is calculated and an analytical approximation for the reaction rate is proposed. A comparison of our calculation with existing data is addressed. Results of our calculations for the $^{13}$N($p,γ)^{14}$O reaction rate provide the contribution to the steadily improving reaction rate database libraries. Our calculations of the $% ^{13}$N($p,γ)^{14}$O reaction rate along with results for the rates of $^{14}$N($% p,γ)^{15}$O and $^{12}$C$(p,γ)^{13}$N processes provide the temperature range $0.13<T_{9}<0.97$ for the conversion of CNO cycle to the HCNO cycle. Our results demonstrate that at early stages of a nova explosion at temperatures about $0.1$ $T_{9}$ and at late stages of evolution of supermassive stars at temperatures about $1.0$ $T_{9}$ the ignition of the HCNO cycle could occur at much lower densities of a stellar medium.

## Key claim

The astrophysical S-factor of the 13N(p,γ)14O reaction is dominated by the first 3S1 resonance, and the contributions of M1 and E2 transitions are negligible at energies below 1 MeV but become significant at higher energies; the S-factor strongly depends on the width of the 3S1 resonance, and the reaction rate calculations provide a temperature range of 0.13 < T9 < 0.97 for the conversion of the CNO cycle to the hot CNO cycle, potentially enabling ignition of the HCNO cycle at much lower stellar densities than previously considered.

## Method

The authors used the modified potential cluster model with forbidden states, classifying orbital states according to Young diagrams to account for the Pauli principle. They constructed two-parameter Gaussian potentials for the p-13N interaction in each partial wave, reproducing experimental resonance energies and widths from three different datasets (Ajzenberg 1991, Tilley 1995, and the recent 2019 neutron-knockout experiment). The Schrödinger equation was solved numerically to obtain wave functions for scattering and bound states, and total cross sections were calculated using standard expressions for electric and magnetic multipole transition matrix elements.

## Key numbers

- First 3S1 resonance energy: 536(12) keV (from the 2019 experiment), with alternative values of 528(2) keV and 545(10) keV from earlier compilations; width consistently 38(2) keV across all three datasets
- Astrophysical S-factor at zero energy S(0) = 7.9(2) keV·b for the 536 keV resonance, 8.4(2) keV·b for the 528 keV resonance, and 7.4(2) keV·b for the 545 keV resonance
- Temperature range for CNO-to-HCNO cycle conversion: 0.13 < T9 < 0.97
- Excitation energies of higher resonances considered: 5.710(20) MeV (0- state, width 400(45) keV), 5.920(10) MeV (1+ state, width <12 keV), 6.284(9) MeV (3- state, width 25(3) keV), 6.609(10) MeV (2+ state, width <5 keV), 6.767(11) MeV (2- state, width <90(5) keV), 7.768(10) MeV (2+ state, width 68(6) keV), and 9.755(10) MeV (2+ state, width 229(51) keV)

## Context

This work extends previous calculations of the 13N(p,γ)14O reaction using potential cluster models, shell models, and R-matrix approaches (Funck 1987, Huang 2010, and others) by incorporating the most recent experimental data on 14O resonance parameters from Tilley (1995) and the 2019 neutron-knockout experiment. The results contribute to the NACRE and NACRE II reaction rate databases and, when combined with rates for 14N(p,γ)15O and 12C(p,γ)13N, enable determination of the temperature range for the transition between the standard CNO cycle and the hot CNO cycle, which is relevant for nova explosions and supermassive star evolution.
