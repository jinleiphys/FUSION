# Thermodynamic versus Dynamical Description of the Neutron-Star Crust-Core Instability: Implications for Crustal Observables

> We investigate the crust-core transition in neutron stars using both thermodynamic and dynamical descriptions of the instability. In the thermodynamic approach, the transition is identified through the vanishing of a generalized incompressibility coefficient signaling the onset of a bulk spinodal instability. In contrast, the dynamical approach based on the relativistic random-phase approximation (RPA) incorporates Coulomb screening and finite-size effects that determine the instability at finite wavelength. Using a family of covariant energy density functionals spanning a broad range of symmetry-energy slopes, we show that the dynamical treatment systematically predicts lower transition densities and pressures compared to the thermodynamic approach. We further demonstrate that the RPA instability develops at a characteristic length scale set by the competition among bulk, Coulomb, and surface effects. Most importantly, we show that these differences propagate directly into neutron-star observables. Because the thermodynamic approach predicts larger transition pressures, it generates thicker crusts and significantly larger crustal fractions of the stellar moment of inertia than the dynamical-RPA framework -- with important implications for the interpretation of pulsar glitches and other crust-sensitive neutron-star observables.

# Neutron-Star Crust-Core Instability: Thermodynamic versus Dynamical Description

## Key claim

The dynamical (RPA) treatment of the crust-core transition systematically predicts lower transition densities and pressures than the thermodynamic approach, with the discrepancy growing for models with stiffer symmetry energies (larger slope parameter L). These differences propagate directly into neutron-star observables: the thermodynamic approach generates thicker crusts and significantly larger crustal fractions of the stellar moment of inertia, with important implications for interpreting pulsar glitches.

## Method

The authors compare two approaches to the crust-core instability using a family of covariant energy density functionals (FSUGold2 and eight systematically varied interactions) spanning symmetry-energy slopes L = 47-113 MeV. The thermodynamic approach identifies the transition through the vanishing of a generalized incompressibility coefficient K_mu, signaling a bulk spinodal instability in the zero-momentum limit. The dynamical approach uses the relativistic random-phase approximation (RPA) to analyze stability against finite-wavelength density fluctuations, incorporating Coulomb screening, finite-size effects, and surface contributions through the longitudinal dielectric function.

## Key numbers

- Transition densities from the thermodynamic approach are systematically higher than from the dynamical-RPA approach across the full range of symmetry-energy slopes L = 47-113 MeV
- The critical momentum transfer q_cc at the dynamical instability shows non-monotonic behavior with L, reflecting competition among bulk, Coulomb, and surface effects
- Transition pressure P_cc exhibits a broad maximum at intermediate L values, with the thermodynamic approach predicting larger pressures than the dynamical approach
- The maximum crustal fraction of the stellar moment of inertia occurs at L approximately 75 MeV for the thermodynamic approach and L approximately 65 MeV for the dynamical treatment
- For neutron stars of mass M = 1.2 M_sun and M = 1.8 M_sun, the thermodynamic approach systematically predicts larger I_crust/I values than the dynamical approach
- The Vela glitch constraint of at least 1.6% of the stellar moment of inertia (without entrainment) is satisfied by all models; with entrainment requiring 7-9%, the constraint becomes significantly more restrictive, especially for massive stars

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Kubis (2006) on thermodynamic stability of neutron-star matter and the RPA-based dynamical approach developed by Horowitz (2000) and Carriere (2002). The authors use the FSUGold2 family of functionals from Chen (2014) and Reed (2021) to systematically vary the symmetry-energy slope. The results connect directly to the interpretation of Vela pulsar glitches, where earlier work by Link (1999) established the 1.6% moment-of-inertia requirement, and studies by Chamel (2012) and Andersson (2012) showed that crustal entrainment raises this requirement to 7-9%. The findings enable more precise predictions for crustal observables and help assess whether the crustal superfluid alone can account for large glitches, particularly for massive neutron stars.
