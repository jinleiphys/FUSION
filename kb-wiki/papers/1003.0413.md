# Coupling Relativistic Viscous Hydrodynamics to Boltzmann Descriptions

> Models of relativistic heavy ion collisions typically involve both a hydrodynamic module to describe the high density liquid-like phase and a Boltzmann module to simulate the low density break-up phase which is gas-like. Coupling the prescriptions is more complicated for viscous prescriptions if one wants to maintain continuity of the entire stress-energy tensor and currents. Derivations for the viscosity for a gas are reviewed, which then lead to expressions for changes in the phase space occupation based on simple relaxation-time pictures of viscosity. These expressions are shown to consistently reproduce the non-equilibrium components of the stress-energy tensor. An algorithm for generating a Monte Carlo sampling of particles with which to initiate the Boltzmann calculations is also presented.

## Key claim

The paper presents a detailed prescription for coupling relativistic viscous hydrodynamics to Boltzmann transport at the interface between the two modules, but the authors explicitly hedge that the simple relaxation-time-based calculations of viscosity are "not meant to be competitive with more realistic treatments" and are included only to provide context for the algorithm that alters phase-space densities to be microscopically consistent with the relaxation-time picture.

## Method

The authors derive expressions for bulk and shear viscosities of a dilute hadronic gas using both Kubo relations and a kinematic approach based on the evolution of phase-space densities under velocity gradients, assuming a single momentum-independent relaxation time. They then present an algorithm for generating Monte Carlo samples of particles to initialize Boltzmann calculations, using a linear transformation of momenta from a thermal distribution to reproduce the non-equilibrium components of the stress-energy tensor from the viscous hydrodynamic module.

## Key numbers

- Bulk viscosity for a hadronic gas at temperatures around 150 MeV is negligible, with the equal-time fluctuation \(\sigma_\zeta^2\) being small throughout the temperature range and returning to zero at low temperatures.
- Shear viscosity is given by \(\eta = \beta \Delta\tau \sum_\ell \int \frac{d^3p}{(2\pi\hbar)^3} \frac{p_x^2 p_y^2}{E(\mathbf{p},m_\ell)^2} e^{-\beta E(\mathbf{p},m_\ell)-\alpha_\ell}\).
- The relaxation time is approximated as \(\Delta\tau = 1/(\sigma \rho_{\text{tot}})\) with a fixed cross section of 20 mb.
- The interface temperature window for switching from hydrodynamics to Boltzmann is given as \(140 \lesssim T \lesssim 165\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends prior approaches to coupling hydrodynamics and Boltzmann modules, which previously treated only ideal (non-viscous) hydrodynamics at the interface, by incorporating viscous corrections to maintain continuity of the full stress-energy tensor. The paper specifically references the Cooper-Frye freeze-out formula (Cooper and Frye, 1974) and notes that non-equilibrium effects at freeze-out can modify elliptic flow \(v_2\) by parametrically non-negligible factors, as shown by Dusling (2009). The algorithm enables more accurate determination of \(\eta/s\) from comparisons of hydrodynamic simulations with heavy-ion collision data by properly accounting for the 50% systematic effect introduced by the decoupling dynamics.
