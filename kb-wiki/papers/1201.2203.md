# Equation of State in a Strongly Interacting Relativistic System

> We study the evolution of the equation of state of a strongly interacting quark system as a function of the diquark interaction strength. We show that for the system to avoid collapsing into a pressureless Boson gas at sufficiently strong diquark coupling strength, the diquark-diquark repulsion has to be self-consistently taken into account. In particular, we find that the tendency at zero temperature of the strongly interacting diquark gas to condense into the system ground state is compensated by the repulsion between diquarks if the diquark-diquark coupling constant is higher than a critical value $λ_C=7.65$. Considering such diquark-diquark repulsion, a positive pressure with no significant variation along the whole strongly interacting region is obtained. A consequence of the diquark-diquark repulsion is that the system maintains its BCS character in the whole strongly interacting region.

# Equation of State in a Strongly Interacting Relativistic System

## Key claim

For a strongly interacting quark system to avoid collapsing into a pressureless boson gas at sufficiently strong diquark coupling, the diquark-diquark repulsion must be self-consistently taken into account; the tendency at zero temperature of the strongly interacting diquark gas to condense into the system ground state is compensated by the repulsion between diquarks only if the diquark-diquark coupling constant exceeds a critical value of λ_C = 7.65.

## Method

The authors performed a mean-field analysis of a four-fermion interaction Lagrangian density, introducing a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to obtain the gap parameter Δ and chemical potential μ from the coupled gap equation and number density constraint at zero temperature. They solved these equations numerically for a relativistic regime with fixed Fermi momentum P_F/Λ = 0.3 and mass m/Λ = 0.2, using a momentum cutoff Λ = 602.3 MeV. The diquark-diquark repulsion was incorporated by adding a λΔ^4 term to the free energy, with the coupling constant λ estimated from a modified P-matrix formalism.

## Key numbers

- Critical diquark-diquark coupling constant: λ_C = 7.65; for λ > λ_C the pressure remains positive and the system maintains BCS character for all g-values, while for λ < λ_C the BEC dynamics can be reached and pressure becomes negative.
- Estimated diquark-diquark repulsion coupling: λ = 27.8, taken from Diquarks (using a modified P-matrix formalism of Jaffe and Low).
- Critical coupling for BCS-BEC crossover in the free-diquark gas: g̃_cr ~ 1.1 (where g̃ = gΛ^2), corresponding to the threshold where μ < m is satisfied.
- Momentum cutoff: Λ = 602.3 MeV, with quark mass m/Λ = 0.2 and Fermi momentum P_F/Λ = 0.3.
- Bag constant: B^{1/4} = 145 MeV, used in the energy density and pressure calculations.
- For the free-diquark gas, the pressure decreases to negative values in the BEC regime (g̃ > g̃_cr), indicating instability.
- For the self-interacting diquark gas with λ = 27.8, the pressure remains positive and nearly constant across the whole strongly interacting region.

## Context

This work extends the study of the BCS-BEC crossover in color superconductivity, building on earlier mean-field analyses of four-fermion interactions (R-BCS-BEC-1) and the observation by Wilczek that diquark-diquark repulsion arises from cross-channel unfavorable correlations between quarks belonging to different diquarks. The authors show that previous studies of the BCS-BEC crossover in strong interactions had ignored this repulsive interaction, which fundamentally changes the understanding of the phenomenon. The results are relevant for the equation of state of quark matter in neutron star cores, particularly for high-mass stars such as PSR J1614-2230 (Hessels, 2010; Demorest, 2010), and the approach differs from that of Diquarks, where the diquark repulsive potential was modeled using an ad hoc Gaussian distribution rather than treated as a dynamical variable through the gap equation. The work enables future investigation of magnetic field effects (Ferrer, 2010) and inhomogeneous phases such as density waves, quarkyonic chiral spirals, and the Fulde-Ferrel state in moderate-density quark matter.
