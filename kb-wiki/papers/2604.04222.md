# Causality, the Kovtun-Son-Starinets bound, and a novel sum rule for spectral densities

> We directly show that the local ratio of the shear viscosity to the entropy density for Unruh radiation at a finite distance from the horizon is universal and satisfies the relation $ η/s = 1/(4πc_s^2) $, which involves the speed of sound $ c_s $. Since $ c_s^2 \leq 1 $ by causality, this establishes the close connection between the famous Kovtun-Son-Starinets bound and causality. Moreover, we show that the ratio of bulk to shear viscosity saturates another well-known bound for the bulk viscosity, predicted within holographic approach. We also show that the condition of isotropy of thermal radiation in the Rindler space leads to a novel sum rule relating the $ c^{(0)}(μ) $ and $ c^{(2)}(μ) $ spectral densities, and we explicitly demonstrate its validity for conformal field theory and free massive Dirac fields in any number of dimensions. The sum rule provides the validity of Pascal law and bears some similarity with Burkhardt-Cottingham sum rule for spin-dependent parton distributions. Our result suggests a new perspective on dissipative transport phenomena in media undergoing extreme acceleration, such as quark-gluon plasma created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

# Causality, the Kovtun-Son-Starinets bound, and a novel sum rule for spectral densities

## Key claim

The local ratio of shear viscosity to entropy density for Unruh radiation at a finite distance from the horizon satisfies η/s = 1/(4π c_s²), which directly links the Kovtun-Son-Starinets bound to causality because the speed of sound c_s must be less than the speed of light; the KSS bound would be violated only if c_s > c, implying superluminal phonons and a breakdown of causality.

## Method

The authors analyze quantum fields in Euclidean Rindler space, using the spectral representation for the correlator of two stress-energy tensors in terms of spin-0 and spin-2 spectral densities c^(0)(μ) and c^(2)(μ). They compute temperature derivatives of the energy-momentum tensor components at the Unruh temperature T = T_U (where the Rindler cone unfolds into flat space) using perturbation theory in the modular Hamiltonian, then impose isotropy of thermal radiation to derive a sum rule relating the two spectral densities. From the spectral representation, they obtain expressions for shear viscosity η, bulk viscosity ζ, specific heat c_V, and entropy density s, and evaluate their ratios.

## Key numbers

- The local ratio η/s = 1/(4π c_s²), where c_s² ≤ 1 by causality, establishing the KSS bound η/s ≥ 1/(4π) as a consequence of causality.
- The ratio η/c_V = 1/(4π) (in units where ℏ = k_B = 1), which is the most general form of the relation.
- The bulk-to-shear viscosity ratio ζ/η = 2(1/(d-1) - c_s²), saturating the holographic bound for bulk viscosity.
- For massless Dirac fields in four dimensions, the energy-momentum tensor is ⟨T^μ_ν⟩ = (7π²T⁴/60 + T²a²/24 - 17a⁴/(960π²)) · diag(1, -1/3, -1/3, -1/3), vanishing at T = T_U = a/(2π).
- For quark-gluon plasma, experimental estimates give 0.05 < η/s < 0.2, while using c_s² ∼ 1/3 in the derived formula gives η/s ∼ 0.24.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Chirco (2010) and Lapygin (2025), who demonstrated saturation of the KSS bound by thermal radiation in Rindler space using linear-response theory, and generalizes the results of Prokhorov and Teryaev (prep1) to local transport properties at finite distance from the horizon. The derived relation between the KSS bound and causality parallels the holographic result of Brigante (2008), where a weaker viscosity bound was broken by superluminal gravitons in Gauss-Bonnet gravity, but here the argument is made outside the holographic context. The novel sum rule relating c^(0)(μ) and c^(2)(μ) spectral densities bears similarity to the Burkhardt-Cottingham sum rule for spin-dependent parton distributions in QCD. The results suggest new perspectives on dissipative transport in media undergoing extreme acceleration, such as quark-gluon plasma created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions.
