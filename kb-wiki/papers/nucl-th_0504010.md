# The Hagedorn thermostat

> A system H with a Hagedorn-like mass spectrum imparts its unique temperature TH to any other system coupled to it. An H system radiates particles in preexisting physical and chemical equilibrium. These particles form a saturated vapor at temperature TH. This coexistence describes a first order phase transition. An H system is nearly indifferent to fragmentation into smaller H systems. A lower mass cut-off in the spectrum does not significantly alter the general picture

## Key claim

A system with a Hagedorn-like mass spectrum acts as a perfect thermostat and particle reservoir, imposing its unique temperature T_H on any coupled system; this behavior describes a first-order phase transition at a single temperature fixed by the bag pressure, rather than over a range of temperatures as in ordinary fluids.

## Method

The authors use microcanonical statistical mechanics to couple a Hagedorn system H (with level density rho_H(E) proportional to exp(E/T_H)) to a harmonic oscillator and to an ideal vapor of non-interacting particles. They derive the most probable energy partition and particle density by maximizing the total level density with respect to excitation energy and particle number, showing the vapor acquires exactly the canonical distribution at temperature T_H. They also analyze H's stability against fragmentation by maximizing the level density with respect to fragment mass and number.

## Key numbers

- The most probable kinetic energy per particle in the vapor is (3/2) T_H, provided total energy E >= mN + (3/2) N T_H.
- The most probable particle density of the saturated vapor is n_H = (m T_H / 2 pi)^(3/2) exp(-m/T_H), independent of volume.
- For free fragment mass, the most probable fragment mass is m = (3/2) T_H, giving vapor concentration N/V = (3/(4 pi e))^(3/2) T_H^3.
- The radiation pressure exerted on the bag by pions is p_rad ~ 0.02 B, where B is the bag constant.
- The upper limit for the bag dissolution time is tau ~ [3 pi exp(m/T_H) E_0] / [g_m m^2 T_H^2 R_0^2], where g_m is the degeneracy, R_0 the initial bag radius, and E_0 the initial bag energy.
- The decoupling volume where H completely evaporates is V_d ~ (1/n_H) E / (m + (3/2) T_H).

## Context

This work extends the Hagedorn mass spectrum concept (Hagedorn 1965, 1968) and the MIT bag model (Chodos 1974) by demonstrating that a Hagedorn system is a perfect thermostat, not merely a system with a limiting temperature. It explains the common value of hadronization temperatures from the statistical hadronization model (Becattini), inverse slopes of transverse mass spectra (Alexopoulos 2002, Gazdzicki 2004), and lattice QCD transition temperatures at low baryonic density. The results enable interpreting observed hadron spectra as originating from a Hagedorn-like system and provide a framework for understanding the hadron-to-parton phase transition as a first-order coexistence at a single temperature.
