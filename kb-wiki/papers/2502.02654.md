# Theory of resonances

> We give a pedagogical introduction to the theory of resonances, focusing specifically on the spectrum of excited strongly interacting particles. After providing historical context starting from the atomic theory, we summarize the status of theoretical and experimental research. We discuss then the methodology to determine universal resonance parameter through the analytical properties of the transition amplitudes. For this, the main aspects of the $S$-matrix theory are introduced including some explicit calculations in simplified cases. In the last section, we summarize several frequently used amplitude types also making a connection to Effective Field Theories, as well as provide first glimpse into some more advanced applications to further resonance properties.

## Key claim

This pedagogical review argues that the only universal, reaction-independent way to extract resonance parameters (mass, width, couplings) is through the analytic properties of the S-matrix, specifically the pole positions on unphysical Riemann sheets, rather than through simple "bump hunting" in cross sections.

## Method

The paper introduces the S-matrix formalism, deriving the partial-wave unitarity condition for two-body scattering of scalar fields, which yields a branch cut along the real energy axis above threshold. It then shows how resonance poles appear as conjugate pairs on unphysical Riemann sheets, with the pole position giving the complex energy sqrt(s*) = M - i Gamma/2 and the residue giving the coupling to each final state. Several amplitude parametrizations are presented: Breit-Wigner for narrow isolated resonances, Flatte for coupled-channel effects near thresholds, K-matrix for general two-body unitarity, and unitarized chiral perturbation theory approaches.

## Key numbers

* The paper shows a figure comparing hydrogen atom excited states (calculated via Rydberg formula) with nucleon excited states (J^P = 1/2^+) taken from Particle Data Group central values.
* The Particle Data Group currently lists around 150 excited mesons and around 50 confirmed excited baryons.
* The two-body unitarity condition is derived for the elastic region 4m^2 < s < 9m^2, where only two-body intermediate states (N=2) contribute.
* The Breit-Wigner cross section formula contains the term Gamma^2/4 in the numerator and (sqrt(s)-M)^2 + Gamma^2/4 in the denominator.
* The Flatte parametrization includes a branching function proportional to sqrt((s-(M1+M2)^2)(s-(M1-M2)^2)/(4s)) for decays to heavier states.

## Context

This review extends the pedagogical tradition of S-matrix theory for hadron spectroscopy, building on the foundational work of Heisenberg (1943) and Wigner (1946) on resonances. It connects to modern effective field theory approaches such as Chiral Perturbation Theory (Weinberg 1978, Gasser and Leutwyler 1984) and their unitarized extensions (UCHPT, N/D, Inverse Amplitude Methods). The paper enables practitioners to understand how lattice QCD results (Mai 2022) and experimental data from facilities like Jefferson Laboratory, ELSA, and CLAS can be analyzed to extract universal resonance parameters through analytic continuation of scattering amplitudes.
