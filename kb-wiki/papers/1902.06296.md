# How nuclear jets form and disintegrate into clusters in heavy-ion collisions

> The most extreme deformations that can be explored in heavy-ion collisions at Fermi-energies are collimated flows of nuclear matter which recall jet dynamics. From microphysics to the cosmological scale, jets are rather common topologies. In nuclear physics, pioneering works focused on the breakup of these structures, resulting into early nuclear-fission models in analogy to the droplet formation in viscous liquids; such view became emblematic to explain surface-energy effects and surface instability by analogy with the Rayleigh instability. Through a dynamical approach based on the Boltzmann-Langevin equation, well adapted to out-of-equilibrium conditions, we explored the possibility that nuclear jets could arise in heavy-ion collisions from different conditions than those leading to fission or neck fragmentation, and that they can breakup from mechanisms that are almost unrelated to cohesive properties.

## Key claim

The authors argue that nuclear jets, collimated streams of nuclear matter formed in heavy-ion collisions at Fermi energies, disintegrate into clusters primarily through volume (spinodal) instabilities rather than surface (Plateau-Rayleigh) instabilities, contrary to earlier analogies with fission and neck fragmentation. This conclusion is conditional on the use of a full Boltzmann-Langevin treatment that includes fluctuations and two-body correlations; a simplified "collisionless" approach that suppresses these correlations fails to produce the jet mechanism.

## Method

The authors employed the Boltzmann-Langevin-One-Body (BLOB) model, which solves the Boltzmann-Langevin equation in full phase space by introducing correlations beyond the kinetic-equation level through colliding phase-space portions of extended size. They simulated head-on collisions of 36Ar on 58Ni at beam energies of 52 and 74 A MeV, using a simplified SKM* effective interaction with compressibility modulus k=200 MeV. For comparison, they also performed a "collisionless" calculation where numerical noise served as the only fluctuation seed, and they compared the resulting dispersion relations to analytic expressions for spinodal (volume) and Plateau-Rayleigh (surface) instabilities.

## Key numbers

- The jet mechanism was observed in BLOB calculations for 36Ar+58Ni head-on collisions at 52 and 74 A MeV, producing clusters with element number up to Z~10.
- The density in the jet tail dropped below ρ_sat/4 (where ρ_sat is saturation density), as shown in the BLOB calculation at 74 A MeV.
- The average temperature of the breaking-up system was T=3 MeV, used to evaluate analytic dispersion relations.
- The finite-temperature correction to surface tension at T=3 MeV was negligible, α(T)≈1.
- The system transitions from mostly fusion below 25 A MeV to disintegration into clusters and light particles above 90 A MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of nuclear fission and neck fragmentation (Vandenbosch 1973; Baran 2005; Di Toro 2006) by showing that a distinct jet mechanism exists in heavy-ion collisions at Fermi energies, where volume instabilities dominate over surface effects. The authors note that experimental signatures of nuclear jets have been observed in previous experiments (Lautesse 2006; Francalanza in preparation) but were difficult to interpret. The BLOB approach (Napolitani 2013) enables this analysis by implementing fluctuations and dissipation in full phase space, and the results are consistent with a parallel study using stochastic TDHF approaches (De La Mota 2019). This work enables future studies of cluster formation mechanisms in nuclear reactions where density drops to very low values, with implications for understanding the competition between volume and surface instabilities along the equation-of-state landscape.
