# Quark Masses: An Environmental Impact Statement

> We investigate worlds that lie on a slice through the parameter space of the Standard Model over which quark masses vary. We allow as many as three quarks to participate in nuclei, while fixing the mass of the electron and the average mass of the lightest baryon flavor multiplet. We classify as "congenial" worlds that satisfy the environmental constraint that the quark masses allow for stable nuclei with charges one, six, and eight, making organic chemistry possible. Whether a congenial world actually produces observers depends on a multitude of historical contingencies, beginning with primordial nucleosynthesis, which we do not explore. Such constraints may be independently superimposed on our results. Environmental constraints such as the ones we study may be combined with information about the a priori distribution of quark masses over the landscape of possible universes to determine whether the measured values of the quark masses are determined environmentally, but our analysis is independent of such an anthropic approach. We estimate baryon masses as functions of quark masses and nuclear masses as functions of baryon masses. We check for the stability of nuclei against fission, strong particle emission, and weak nucleon emission. For two light quarks with charges 2/3 and -1/3, we find a band of congeniality roughly 29 MeV wide in their mass difference. We also find another, less robust region of congeniality with one light, charge -1/3 quark, and two heavier, approximately degenerate charge -1/3 and 2/3 quarks. No other assignment of light quark charges yields congenial worlds with two baryons participating in nuclei. We identify and discuss the region in quark-mass space where nuclei would be made from three or more baryon species.

## Key claim

The paper investigates which values of the light quark masses allow for stable nuclei with atomic numbers one, six, and eight (hydrogen, carbon, and oxygen), thereby making organic chemistry possible; the authors classify such worlds as "congenial" but explicitly note that whether a congenial world actually produces observers depends on historical contingencies like primordial nucleosynthesis that they do not explore.

## Method

The authors fix the electron mass to its value in our world and adjust the QCD scale so that the average mass of the lightest baryon flavor multiplet equals the nucleon mass (940 MeV). They estimate baryon masses as functions of quark masses using first-order flavor SU(3) perturbation theory, and estimate nuclear masses using either analog nuclei from our world (with Coulomb corrections) or a generalized Weizsacker semi-empirical mass formula with a degenerate Fermi gas model for kinematic flavor symmetry violation. They then check nuclear stability against fission, strong particle emission analogous to alpha decay, and weak nucleon emission.

## Key numbers

* The band of congeniality for two light quarks with charges 2/3 and -1/3 is roughly 29 MeV wide in their mass difference, with our own world lying comfortably away from the edges.
* The electron mass is fixed to its value in our world: m_e = 0.511 MeV.
* The average mass of the lightest baryon flavor multiplet is fixed to the nucleon mass in our world: M_N = 940 MeV.
* In our world, the up quark mass is m_u = 1.5-3 MeV, the down quark mass is m_d = 2.5-5.5 MeV, and the strange quark mass is m_s = 95 +/- 25 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the environmental (anthropic) approach previously applied to the cosmological constant by Weinberg (1987) and refined by Martel, Shapiro, and Weinberg, and to the Higgs vacuum expectation value by other authors. The paper provides a framework for determining which regions of quark-mass parameter space permit carbon-based chemistry, independent of any specific a priori distribution over a multiverse. The results enable future studies that could combine these nuclear-physics constraints with information about primordial nucleosynthesis and astrophysical processes to determine whether the measured quark masses are environmentally selected.
