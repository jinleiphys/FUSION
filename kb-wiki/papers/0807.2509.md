# Challenge on the Astrophysical R-process Calculation with Nuclear Mass Models

> Our understanding of the rapid neutron capture nucleosynthesis process in universe depends on the reliability of nuclear mass predictions. Initiated by the newly developed mass table in the relativistic mean field theory (RMF), in this paper the influence of mass models on the $r$-process calculations is investigated assuming the same astrophysical conditions. The different model predictions on the so far unreachable nuclei lead to significant deviations in the calculated r-process abundances.

## Key claim

The paper shows that different nuclear mass models (RMF, FRDM, ETFSI-Q, HFB-13) produce significantly different r-process abundance patterns even when the same astrophysical conditions are assumed, indicating that the nature of the r-process is complicated by the interplay between nuclear physics and astrophysical environment.

## Method

The authors performed site-independent r-process calculations using a configuration of 16 components with neutron densities ranging from 10^20 to 10^28 cm^-3 at a temperature of about 10^9 K, assuming (n,gamma) <-> (gamma,n) equilibrium. They used the same astrophysical conditions (the best case obtained from the RMF mass table) and the same beta-decay properties from Moller (2003) while varying only the nuclear mass input among four models: the newly developed RMF mass table, FRDM, ETFSI-Q, and HFB-13.

## Key numbers

- The RMF Lagrangian achieves a prediction power of about 0.65 MeV for one neutron separation energy S_n, comparable to highly parameterized mass models, using less than 10 parameters fitted to doubly magic nuclei
- The shell gap energy at N=126 in the RMF model is about 2 MeV larger than that in other models (FRDM, ETFSI-Q, HFB-13)
- In the RMF simulation, the r-process path jumps directly from 169Pr to 185Pr (15 mass units) due to the predicted shell structure and shape transition
- The first oblate nuclides for Z=60-63 appear at mass numbers 172-177 in the RMF predictions, while in the HFB-13 model the corresponding mass numbers are 178-179
- The r-process path in the RMF simulation runs about 1-2 mass units towards the neutron drip line compared to other simulations

## Context

This work extends the investigation of mass model dependence on r-process calculations initiated by previous studies such as Cowan (1991), Kartz (1993), and SMG (2007), who noted that different mass models agree on known data but disagree towards the neutron-rich side. The paper uses the newly developed RMF mass table from the authors' own work (RMFBCS) and compares it with FRDM, ETFSI-Q, and HFB-13 models. The results show that precise mass measurements of neutron-rich nuclides with accuracy less than 100 keV are needed to determine shell evolution at N=82 and 126 and shape transition locations, which would constrain mass models and improve understanding of r-process nucleosynthesis.
