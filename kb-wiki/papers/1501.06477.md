# Exploring the properties of the phases of QCD matter - research opportunities and priorities for the next decade

> This document provides a summary of the discussions during the recent joint QCD Town Meeting at Temple University of the status of and future plans for the research program of the relativistic heavy-ion community. A list of compelling questions is formulated, and a number of recommendations outlining the greatest research opportunities and detailing the research priorities of the heavy-ion community, voted on and unanimously approved at the Town Meeting, are presented. They are supported by a broad discussion of the underlying physics and its relation to other subfields. Areas of overlapping interests with the "QCD and Hadron Structure" ("cold QCD") subcommunity, in particular the recommendation for the future construction of an Electron-Ion Collider, are emphasized. The agenda of activities of the "hot QCD" subcommunity at the Town Meeting is attached.

## Key claim

This white paper presents community-voted recommendations for the future of relativistic heavy-ion physics, asserting that an Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) is the highest priority for future construction after FRIB, while also recommending a program to complete the search for the QCD critical point and exploit jet-based probes of the quark-gluon plasma at RHIC and LHC energies.

## Method

The document summarizes discussions from the joint QCD Town Meeting at Temple University (September 2014), where 244 registrants from the "hot QCD" and "cold QCD" subcommunities participated in parallel and joint sessions. Four recommendations were formulated, presented, and voted on by the community, with the authors drawing on presentations, community white papers, and additional material to provide a coherent picture of the field's status and future priorities.

## Key numbers

* 244 registrants participated in the 2014 joint QCD Town Meeting (108 from "hot QCD", 136 from "cold QCD"), representing about 10% stronger participation than the corresponding 2007 meeting at Rutgers University
* The EIC design aims for variable center-of-mass energies from approximately 20 to 100 GeV, upgradable to approximately 140 GeV
* The EIC design targets high collision luminosity of approximately 10^33 to 10^34 cm^-2 sec^-1
* The EIC design aims for polarized beams of approximately 70% polarization for electrons, protons, and light nuclei
* Recent measurements at RHIC combined with perturbative QCD analyses show that gluons carry approximately 20-30% of the proton's helicity
* The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio (eta/s) of the QGP has been determined to within less than a factor of 2, with present uncertainty conservatively estimated as plus or minus 50%
* The transverse momentum diffusion parameter (q-hat/T^3) has been constrained to within a factor of 2-3, representing an improvement of about an order of magnitude

## Context

This white paper extends the 2007 Nuclear Physics Long Range Plan, which designated the EIC as "embodying the vision for reaching the next QCD frontier," and responds to the 2013 NSAC Subcommittee report declaring an EIC "absolutely essential." It builds on discoveries from RHIC (which established the QGP as an almost perfect liquid) and the LHC heavy-ion program, and recommends completing the RHIC scientific mission with upgrades including the sPHENIX jet detector and low-energy luminosity upgrades, continued U.S. participation in the LHC heavy-ion program, and strong investment in theoretical efforts including the Topical Collaborations in Nuclear Theory program initiated in the 2007 Long Range Plan.
