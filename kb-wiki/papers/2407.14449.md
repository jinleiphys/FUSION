# QCD phase diagram in the $T-eB$ plane for varying pion mass

> We study the effect of a varying pion mass on the quantum chromodynamics (QCD) phase diagram in the presence of an external magnetic field, aiming to understand it, for the first time, using Nambu\textendash Jona-Lasinio like effective models. We compare results from both its local and nonlocal versions. In both cases, we find that the inverse magnetic catalysis (IMC) near the crossover is eliminated with increasing pion mass, while the decreasing trend of crossover temperature with increasing magnetic field persists for pion mass values at least up to $440$ MeV. Thus, the models are capable of capturing qualitatively the results found by lattice QCD (LQCD) for heavy (unphysical) pions. The key feature in the models is the incorporation of the effect of a reduction in the coupling constant with increasing energy. Along with reproducing the IMC effect, it enables models to describe the effects of heavier current quark masses without introducing additional parameters. For the local NJL model, this agreement depends on how the parameters of the model are fit at the physical point. In this respect, the nonlocal version, which, due to its formulation, automatically exhibits the IMC effect around the crossover region, captures the physics more naturally. We further use the nonlocal framework to determine the pion mass beyond which the IMC effect around the transition region does not exist anymore.

## Key claim

For the first time using Nambu–Jona-Lasinio like effective models, the authors show that increasing the pion mass eliminates the inverse magnetic catalysis (IMC) effect near the crossover, while the decreasing trend of crossover temperature with increasing magnetic field persists for pion masses at least up to 440 MeV, qualitatively matching lattice QCD results for heavy (unphysical) pions.

## Method

The authors studied the QCD phase diagram in the temperature–magnetic field plane using both local and nonlocal Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (NJL) models, varying the current quark mass (and hence the pion mass) while keeping the scale parameter Λ and coupling constant G fixed. They computed the chiral condensate as a function of temperature and magnetic field using the Feynman-Hellmann theorem, with the nonlocal model employing a Gaussian form factor and the local model using a parameterized running coupling constant to reproduce inverse magnetic catalysis. The pion mass at which IMC disappears was determined from the nonlocal framework.

## Key numbers

* In the nonlocal model, the IMC effect disappears around a pion mass of 215 MeV, with a 1.4% spread and very small eB dependence.
* For the 2+1-flavor local model, the IMC effect disappears between 220 and 340 MeV of pion mass, except for eB = 0.2 GeV² where the IMC effect survives up to the highest pion mass tested.
* The decreasing trend of crossover temperature with increasing magnetic field persists for pion mass values at least up to 440 MeV.
* The magnetic field strength in heavy-ion collision experiments is approximately 0.02–0.3 GeV².

## Context

This work extends lattice QCD studies by D'Elia et al. (2018) and Endrodi (2019), who observed that at pion masses heavier than the physical value, the IMC effect disappears while the decreasing trend of crossover temperature persists. The authors use effective NJL models to provide an analytical understanding of these lattice observations, finding that the nonlocal NJL model captures the physics more naturally than the local version. This enables future studies of how the running of the coupling constant in effective models can describe heavier current quark masses without introducing additional parameters.
