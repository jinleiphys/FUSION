# Quantum Dissipation in Open Harmonic Systems: Operator Solution

> A finite number of harmonic oscillators coupled to infinitely many environment oscillators is fundamental to the problem of understanding quantum dissipation of a small system immersed in a large environment. Exact operator solution as a function of time is given to this problem, by using diagonalized dynamical variable of the entire system, the small system plus the environment. The decay law of prepared initial configuration is worked out in greatest detail. A clear separation of the exponential- and the power-law decay period is made possible by our method. Behavior of physical quantities at asymptotically late times can be understood in terms of the overlap probability of the system variable with the diagonal variable of the entire system.

## Key claim

The paper provides an exact operator solution for a finite number of harmonic oscillators coupled to an infinite environment of oscillators, enabling a clear separation of exponential and power-law decay periods. The key qualifier is that this separation and the enhanced remnant fraction at low temperatures depend critically on the presence of a gap in the environment spectrum (ω_c > 0), which the authors contrast with the gapless case (ω_c = 0) treated by Ullersma.

## Method

The authors diagonalize the full Hamiltonian (system plus environment) by solving the eigenvalue problem for the potential matrix using an ansatz analogous to the Lippmann-Schwinger equation, with an out-state boundary condition. They then construct the canonical transformation to diagonal variables, express the Heisenberg operator solutions for q(t) and p(t) in terms of initial operators, and derive the function g(t) that controls the semi-classical time evolution. The decay law is analyzed by deforming the contour integral for g(t) into the second Riemann sheet, separating the pole contribution (exponential decay) from the branch-cut contribution (power-law decay).

## Key numbers

- The condition for no isolated (bound) spectrum is f(ω_c^(2-)) > ω_c^2, with consistency requiring ω_0 > ω_c.
- The power-law decay of g(t) at late times is ∝ t^(-α-1), where α > -1 characterizes the threshold behavior r(ω) ∝ (ω - ω_c)^α.
- For a typical example with r(ω) = c(ω - ω_c)^α (0 < α < 1) and ω̄ ≫ Max(ω_c, T), the temperature-dependent part of the asymptotic occupation number is n^β ≈ (c/ω̄^3) Γ(α+1) e^(-βω_c) T^(α+1).
- The transient time t_* from exponential to power-law decay is t_* ≈ (1/γ) ln( ω̄^3 / [2c Γ(α+1) γ^(α+1)] ), where γ = -Im z_0 is the decay rate.
- The overlap probability satisfies the unitarity relation ∫_{ω_c}^∞ dω |⟨0|Ψ(ω)⟩|^2 = ∫_{ω_c}^∞ dω 2ω H(ω) = 1.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous path integral approach (Joichi, Matsumoto, Yoshimura, 1996) to arbitrary initial states including system-environment correlations and non-thermal environments, and generalizes the classical work of Ullersma (1966) by including a gap in the environment spectrum. The operator solution enables computation of correlation functions and asymptotic quantities in terms of the overlap probability |⟨0|Ψ(ω)⟩|^2, which has a universal character independent of initial states. The results enable analysis of unstable particle decay in medium, where the presence of a gap (ω_c = 2 × daughter mass) yields an enhanced remnant fraction at low temperatures that does not suffer from the usual Boltzmann suppression factor.
