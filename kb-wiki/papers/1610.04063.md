# Combining symmetry breaking and restoration with configuration interaction: a highly accurate many-body scheme applied to the pairing Hamiltonian

> Background: Ab initio many-body methods have been developed over the past ten years to address mid-mass nuclei... As progress in the design of inter-nucleon interactions is made, further efforts must be made to tailor many-body methods. Methods: We formulate a truncated configuration interaction method that consists of diagonalizing the Hamiltonian in a highly truncated subspace of the total N-body Hilbert space. The reduced Hilbert space is generated via the particle-number projected BCS state along with projected seniority-zero two and four quasi-particle excitations. Furthermore, the extent by which the underlying BCS state breaks U(1) symmetry is optimized in presence of the projected two and four quasi-particle excitations... The quality of the newly designed method is tested against exact solutions of the so-called attractive pairing Hamiltonian problem. Results: By construction, the method reproduce exact results for N=2 and N=4. For N=(8,16,20) the error on the ground-state correlation energy is less than (0.006, 0.1, 0.15) % across the entire range of inter-nucleon coupling defining the pairing Hamiltonian and driving the normal-to-superfluid quantum phase transition. The presently proposed method offers the advantage to automatically access the low-lying spectroscopy, which it does with high accuracy. Conclusions: The numerical cost of the newly designed variational method is polynomial (N$^6$) in system size. It achieves an unprecedented accuracy on the ground-state correlation energy, effective pairing gap and one-body entropy as well as on the excitation energy of low-lying states of the attractive pairing Hamiltonian. This constitutes a strong enough motivation to envision its application to realistic nuclear Hamiltonians in view of providing a complementary, accurate and versatile ab initio description of mid-mass open-shell nuclei in the future.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a truncated configuration interaction method, combining particle-number projected BCS states with projected seniority-zero two- and four-quasi-particle excitations and an optimized symmetry-breaking parameter, achieves unprecedented accuracy on the ground-state correlation energy of the attractive pairing Hamiltonian, with errors below 0.15% for N=20 across all coupling strengths. The hedge is that this high accuracy is demonstrated only for the schematic pairing Hamiltonian, not yet for realistic nuclear Hamiltonians.

## Method

The authors formulate a truncated configuration interaction method that diagonalizes the Hamiltonian in a subspace of the total N-body Hilbert space spanned by the particle-number projected BCS state plus projected seniority-zero two- and four-quasi-particle excitations built from that BCS state. The extent of U(1) symmetry breaking in the underlying BCS state is optimized as a variational parameter (the auxiliary coupling g_aux) in the presence of the projected excitations, extending the restricted variation-after-projection method. The method is tested against exact solutions of the attractive pairing Hamiltonian at half-filling (N=Ω) with equally spaced single-particle levels.

## Key numbers

- For N=8, the error on the ground-state correlation energy is less than 0.006% across the entire range of inter-nucleon coupling.
- For N=16, the error on the ground-state correlation energy is less than 0.1% across the entire range of inter-nucleon coupling.
- For N=20, the error on the ground-state correlation energy is less than 0.15% across the entire range of inter-nucleon coupling.
- The numerical cost of the method scales polynomially as N^6 in system size.
- For rare-earth nuclei, the typical pairing strength is g/Δe ~ 0.5, where Δe ~ 500 keV is the spacing between successive pairs of degenerate levels.

## Context

This work extends the particle-number projected many-body perturbation theory approach of Lacroix and Gambacurta (2012) by moving from perturbative to non-perturbative diagonalization and by optimizing the symmetry-breaking order parameter. It improves upon the polynomial similarity transformation (PoST) method of Dukelsky and collaborators (2016), which achieved less than 1% error on the correlation energy, by reaching errors below 0.15% for N=20. The method enables future application to realistic nuclear Hamiltonians for ab initio description of mid-mass open-shell nuclei, complementing existing approaches such as Gorkov self-consistent Green's function theory, multi-reference in-medium similarity renormalization group, and Bogoliubov coupled cluster theory.
