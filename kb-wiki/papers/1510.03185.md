# Continuum discretized BCS approach for weakly bound nuclei

> The Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) formalism is extended by including the single-particle continuum in order to analyse the evolution of pairing in an isotopic chain from stability up to the drip line. We propose a continuum discretized generalized BCS based on single-particle pseudostates (PS). These PS are generated from the diagonalization of the single-particle Hamiltonian within a Transformed Harmonic Oscillator (THO) basis. The consistency of the results versus the size of the basis is studied. The method is applied to neutron rich Oxygen and Carbon isotopes and compared with similar previous works and available experimental data. We make use of the flexibility of the proposed model in order to study the evolution of the occupation of the low-energy continuum when the system becomes weakly bound. We find a larger influence of the non-resonant continuum as long as the Fermi level approaches zero.

## Key claim

The paper extends the BCS formalism to include the single-particle continuum via a discretized pseudostate basis generated from a Transformed Harmonic Oscillator (THO) basis, finding that the non-resonant continuum has a larger influence on occupation as the Fermi level approaches zero, but only in extreme weakly-bound situations where the resonant continuum still dominates.

## Method

The single-particle continuum is discretized by diagonalizing the core-valence Hamiltonian in a THO basis, producing a finite set of pseudostates that represent both bound and unbound states. A generalized BCS formalism is then solved using these pseudostates with a Density Dependent Delta Interaction (DDDI) for the pairing interaction, without distinguishing resonant and non-resonant parts of the continuum. The method is applied to neutron-rich Oxygen and Carbon isotopes, using a single-particle potential fitted to reproduce the spectra of \(^{17}\)O and \(^{13}\)C respectively, with pairing strengths constrained by the two-neutron separation energies of the stable isotopes.

## Key numbers

- Two-neutron separation energies for oxygen isotopes from \(^{18}\)O to \(^{28}\)O are calculated and compared with experimental data from the National Nuclear Data Center (NNDC); the continuum threshold in \(^{17}\)O is at 4.14 MeV.
- For \(^{22}\)O, the total occupation in the continuum for the most weakly-bound case (central potential strength \(V = -47.5\) MeV) is 0.4048 (6.75%) for \(s_{1/2}\), 0.0002 (less than 0.01%) for \(d_{5/2}\), and 0.0023 (0.04%) for \(d_{3/2}\).
- The energy cut-off for the pseudostates is set at 20 MeV; the basis size used for all calculations is \(N = 100\) pseudostates per partial wave.
- For carbon isotopes, the \(d_{3/2}\) resonance is at 2.2 MeV and a broad \(f_{7/2}\) resonance around 10 MeV; \(^{24}\)C is found to be unbound.
- Convergence of the average pairing gap \(\Delta_{\rm av.}\) for \(^{22}\)O is demonstrated for basis sizes \(N = \{60, 70, 80, 90, 100, 150, 200\}\).

## Context

This work extends the continuum BCS approach of Sandulescu et al. (1997) and the continuum single-particle level density (CSPLD) method of Id Betan et al. (2008, 2012) by introducing a THO basis for discretization, which treats resonant and non-resonant continuum on the same footing without separate treatment. The results for carbon isotopes agree well with the Richardson-equation solutions of Id Betan et al. (2012b) and with experimental binding energies. The method enables systematic studies of pairing in weakly-bound nuclei approaching the drip line, and the flexibility of the THO basis allows optimization of computational cost for future applications to heavier systems such as nickel and tin isotopes.
