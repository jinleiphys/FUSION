# Quantum computation in fermionic thermal field theories

> Thermal properties of quantum fields at finite temperature are crucial to understanding strongly interacting matter and recent development in quantum computing has provided an alternative and promising avenue of study. In this work, we study thermal field theories involving only fermions using quantum algorithms. We first delve into the presentations of fermion fields via qubits on digital quantum computers alongside the quantum algorithms such as quantum imaginary time evolutions employed to evaluate thermal properties of generic quantum field theories. Specifically, we show numerical results such as the thermal distribution and the energy density of thermal field theories for Majorana fermions in 1+1 dimensions using quantum simulators. In addition to free field theory, we also study the effects of interactions resulting from coupling with a spatially homogeneous Majorana field. In both cases, we show analytically that thermal properties of the system can be described using phase-space distributions, and the quantum simulation results agree with analytical and semiclassical expectations. Our work is an important step to understand thermal fixed points, preparing for quantum simulation of thermalization in real time.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that quantum imaginary time evolution (QITE) on quantum simulators can accurately reproduce the Fermi-Dirac distribution for Majorana fermions in 1+1 dimensions at finite temperature, but only for 4 qubits in the free-field case; the energy density requires more qubits to match the continuum limit, and the agreement holds only for spatially homogeneous systems where analytical phase-space distributions are available.

## Method

The authors discretize a 1+1-dimensional Majorana fermion field theory on a spatial lattice with periodic boundary conditions, mapping the N fermion sites to N qubits via the Jordan-Wigner transformation. They then apply the quantum imaginary time evolution (QITE) algorithm to prepare thermal states, using the Trotter decomposition to approximate the non-unitary imaginary-time evolution operator as a sequence of unitary operations. Thermal expectation values of observables such as the phase-space distribution and energy density are computed by sampling over computational basis states using the minimally entangled typical thermal state (METTS) method, and the results are compared with exact diagonalization and analytical Fermi-Dirac distributions.

## Context

This work extends the application of quantum algorithms, specifically QITE, to fermionic thermal field theories, building on prior studies of thermal states in quantum field theory using QITE by Czajka (2021, 2022) and Davoudi (2022). It focuses on Majorana fermions in 1+1 dimensions as a minimal testbed, contrasting with earlier work on Dirac fermions, and aims to validate quantum simulation against analytical Fermi-Dirac distributions and semiclassical expectations. The results enable future quantum simulations of real-time thermalization and thermal fixed points in strongly interacting matter, such as quarks in QCD.
