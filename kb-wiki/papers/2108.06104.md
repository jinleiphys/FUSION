# High-precision nuclear chronometer for the cosmos

> Nuclear chronometer, which predicts the ages of the oldest stars by comparing the present and initial abundances of long-lived radioactive nuclides, provides an independent dating technique for the cosmos. A new nuclear chronometer called Th-U-X chronometer is proposed, which imposes stringent constraints on the astrophysical conditions in the $r$-process simulation by synchronizing the previous Th/X, U/X and Th/U chronometers. The astrophysical uncertainties of nuclear chronometer are significantly reduced from more than $\pm2$ billion years to within 0:3 billion years by the Th-U-X chronometer. The proposed chronometer is then applied to estimate the ages of the six metal-poor stars with observed uranium abundances, and the predicted ages are compatible with the cosmic age 13.8 billion years predicted from the cosmic microwave background radiation, but in contradictory with the new cosmic age 11.4 billion years from the gravitational lenses measurement.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a "Th-U-X chronometer" that synchronizes the Th/X, U/X, and Th/U chronometers to impose stringent constraints on astrophysical conditions in r-process simulations, reducing astrophysical uncertainties from more than ±2 billion years to within 0.3 billion years, but only for those specific astrophysical conditions where the three chronometers give consistent ages.

## Method

The authors performed r-process nucleosynthesis simulations using a parametric high-entropy wind (HEW) model for the neutrino-driven wind scenario of core-collapse supernovae, with the NucNet tools solving coupled differential equations for nuclear abundances. They varied the electron abundance Y_e and the maximum entropy S_final as the two key astrophysical parameters, and used the WS4 mass model as the primary nuclear physics input, with additional simulations using DZ28, FRDM2012, and HFB24 mass models for uncertainty analysis. For each star, they searched for the specific combination of Y_e and S_final where the ages derived from Th/X, U/X, and Th/U chronometers become consistent (within 0.2 Gyr), thereby defining the synchronized Th-U-X chronometer.

## Key numbers

* Astrophysical uncertainty reduced from more than ±2 billion years to within 0.3 billion years for the Th-U-X chronometer
* For star J2038-0023 with Y_e=0.45, synchronization achieved at S_final=302, with differences among the three chronometers smaller than 0.2 Gyr
* Th-U-X chronometer ages for J2038-0023 vary from 13.7 to 14.0 Gyr across astrophysical conditions ranging from (Y_e=0.40, S_final=260) to (Y_e=0.48, S_final=315)
* Variation in Th-U-X ages from different nuclear mass models is about 0.9 Gyr for J2038-0023
* Ages of six metal-poor stars estimated by Th-U-X chronometer range from 11 to 17 Gyr, with uncertainties from astrophysical conditions of 0.2-0.3 Gyr and from nuclear physics inputs of 0.8-1.6 Gyr
* The cosmic age of 13.8 billion years from microwave background radiation is compatible with all six stars, while the 11.4 billion year age from gravitational lens measurement is contradicted by stars CS31082-001 and CS29497-004

## Context

This work extends previous nuclear chronometer studies by Goriely (1999), Cayrel (2001), Wanajo (2002), Otsuki (2003), and Schatz (2002), which suffered from uncertainties of more than 2 billion years from astrophysical conditions and nuclear physics inputs. The Th-U-X chronometer resolves the "dual crisis" identified by Schatz (2002) where ages from different chronometers for the same star could differ by more than 10 billion years. The method enables customized initial elemental abundances for individual metal-poor stars rather than assuming identical initial conditions as in Johnson (2001) and Niu (2009), and provides a technique that can filter out unreasonable astrophysical conditions even when the r-process site remains unidentified.
