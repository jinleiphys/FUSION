# Phase Transition Study meets Machine Learning

> In recent years, machine learning (ML) techniques have emerged as powerful tools for studying many-body complex systems, and encompassing phase transitions in various domains of physics. This mini review provides a concise yet comprehensive examination of the advancements achieved in applying ML to investigate phase transitions, with a primary focus on those involved in nuclear matter studies.

# Phase Transition Study meets Machine Learning

## Key claim
This mini-review argues that machine learning techniques, including supervised, unsupervised, and generative approaches, can effectively identify and characterize phase transitions in nuclear matter, from the nuclear liquid-gas transition to the QCD deconfinement transition, but the success depends on the specific observable (e.g., final-state particle spectra versus position-space configurations) and the degree of realism in the simulation chain (e.g., inclusion of hadronic afterburner effects reduces event-by-event classification accuracy).

## Method
The authors survey applications of supervised learning (fully connected neural networks, convolutional neural networks), unsupervised learning (autoencoders, confusion schemes), and generative models (generative adversarial networks, variational autoregressive networks, normalizing flows) to phase transition problems in condensed matter, nuclear physics, and lattice quantum field theory. For nuclear systems specifically, they review deep neural network extraction of nuclear temperature from charge multiplicity distributions, autoencoder-based identification of liquid-gas coexistence events in heavy-ion collisions, and CNN-based classification of QCD transition type (crossover vs. first-order) using final-state pion spectra from hydrodynamic simulations.

## Key numbers
- For the square-lattice Ising model, a fully connected neural network yields critical temperature \(T^*/J = 2.266 \pm 0.002\) and critical exponent \(\nu \approx 1.0 \pm 0.2\), consistent with the exact value \(T_c/J = 2/\ln(1+\sqrt{2})\).
- The same network applied to the triangular-lattice Ising model gives \(T_c/J = 3.65 \pm 0.01\) and \(\nu \approx 1.0 \pm 0.3\).
- For the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition in \(^{40}\)Ar on \(^{27}\)Al and \(^{48}\)Ti at 47 MeV/nucleon, the confusion scheme yields a limiting temperature of \(9.24 \pm 0.04\) MeV, consistent with \(9.0 \pm 0.4\) MeV from traditional caloric curve analysis (Wada et al., 1999).
- A CNN trained on final-state pion spectra from hydrodynamic simulations achieves above 95% event-by-event classification accuracy for distinguishing crossover from first-order QCD transition.
- Bayesian inference using elliptic flow and mean transverse kinetic energy of protons from heavy-ion collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 2-10\) GeV constrains the nuclear matter equation of state up to 4 times nuclear saturation density.

## Context
This review extends the pioneering application of neural networks to phase transitions in condensed matter (Carleo and Troyer, 2017) and the confusion scheme for unsupervised phase classification (Nieuwenburg et al., 2017) into nuclear physics domains. It connects to prior work on nuclear liquid-gas phase transition probes (Pochodzalla et al., 1995; Ma et al., 1983; Natowitz et al., 1989) and QCD phase diagram studies (Fukushima and Hatsuda, 2010). The reviewed methods enable future experimental identification of the QCD critical point in heavy-ion collisions at facilities such as CBM, and provide tools for constraining the dense nuclear matter equation of state from both collider and neutron star observations.
