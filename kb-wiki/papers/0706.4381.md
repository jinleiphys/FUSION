# Neutrino Emission From Direct Urca Processes in Pion Condensed Quark Matter

> We study neutrino emission from direct Urca processes in pion condensed quark matter. In compact stars with high baryon density, the emission is dominated by the gapless modes of the pion condensation which leads to an enhanced emissivity. While for massless quarks the enhancement is not remarkable, the emissivity is significantly larger and the cooling of the condensed matter is considerably faster than that in normal quark matter when the mass difference between $u$- and $d$-quarks is sizable.

## Key claim

Neutrino emissivity from direct Urca processes in pion condensed quark matter is significantly enhanced only when the mass difference between u- and d-quarks is sizable; for massless quarks the enhancement over normal quark matter is not remarkable, and the cooling of the condensed matter is considerably faster only in the massive case.

## Method

The authors calculated the neutrino emissivity from direct Urca processes (beta-decay and electron capture) in two-flavor quark matter with a charged pion condensate, using the Fermi effective theory of weak interactions and the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model for the quark propagator in the condensed phase. They derived the retarded W-boson polarization tensor from the full quark propagator including Bogoliubov coefficients, then evaluated the emissivity integrals analytically in the low-temperature limit and numerically as a function of temperature up to the critical temperature, focusing on the gapless regime where the baryon chemical potential exceeds the pion condensate. The specific heat was computed from the entropy density, and the cooling rate was obtained by integrating the energy balance equation.

## Key numbers

* The four-fermion coupling constant used is \( G = G_F \cos\theta_C \approx 1.13488 \times 10^{-11} \) MeV\(^{-2}\).
* For numerical calculations, the baryon chemical potential is set to \(\mu_B = 900\) MeV, the isospin chemical potential to \(\mu_I = 150\) MeV, and the zero-temperature pion condensate to \(\Delta_0 = 100\) MeV.
* The critical temperature is given by \( T_c = 0.57 \Delta_0 \) according to BCS theory, i.e., \( T_c \approx 57 \) MeV for \(\Delta_0 = 100\) MeV.
* In the massive case with \( m_u = 75 \) MeV and \( m_d = 125 \) MeV, the enhancement factor for the emissivity relative to normal quark matter is 1.2 over the whole temperature range.
* The emissivity near \( T_c \) is given by \(\epsilon \approx \left(1 - \frac{\Delta^2}{\mu_u \mu_d}\right) \frac{1371}{2520} \pi G^2 \mu_e \mu_u \mu_d \left[ \left(1 + \frac{\mu_d}{\mu_u}\right) \kappa + \frac{m_d^2 - m_u^2}{2 \mu_u \mu_e} \right] T^6\), where \(\kappa\) is a phenomenological parameter reflecting Fermi momentum reduction.

## Context

This work extends studies of neutrino cooling in dense matter from nuclear matter (where direct Urca requires proton fraction above about 11%, as shown by Lattimer (1991)) and from normal quark matter (where Iwamoto (1982) derived the \(T^6\) emissivity) to the case of pion condensed quark matter. It contrasts with color superconducting phases such as the color-flavor locked (CFL) phase (Alford (1999)) where neutrino emission is exponentially suppressed at low temperature, and with gapless two-flavor color superconductors (g2SC) where the emissivity scales as \(T^6\). The results enable predictions for the cooling curves of compact stars that may contain pion condensed quark matter, providing a potential observational signature to distinguish this phase from normal quark matter or nuclear matter.
