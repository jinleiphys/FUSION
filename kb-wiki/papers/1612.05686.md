# Recent progress on intrinsic charm

> Over the past $\sim\!\! 10$ years, the topic of the nucleon's nonperturbative or $\textit{intrinsic}$ charm (IC) content has enjoyed something of a renaissance, largely motivated by theoretical developments involving quark modelers and PDF fitters. In this talk I will briefly describe the importance of intrinsic charm to various issues in high-energy phenomenology, and survey recent progress in constraining its overall normalization and contribution to the momentum sum rule of the nucleon. I end with the conclusion that progress on the side of calculation has now placed the onus on experiment to unambiguously resolve the proton's intrinsic charm component.

## Key claim

The abstract states that progress on the theoretical side has placed the onus on experiment to resolve the proton's intrinsic charm component, but the full text reveals that the most recent QCD global analysis finds a stringent upper limit of ⟨x⟩_IC < 0.1% at the 5σ level, and that even when including the suggestive EMC data the best-fit value is only ⟨x⟩_IC = 0.13 ± 0.04%, a significantly reduced magnitude relative to earlier analyses like CT14 (2014).

## Method

The paper surveys three theoretical approaches to intrinsic charm: the original BHPS scalar framework (Brodsky, Hoyer, Peterson, and Sakai, 1980) which treats the proton's transition to a |uudc\bar{c}⟩ Fock state using old-fashioned perturbation theory; a meson-baryon model (MBM) from Hobbs (2015) that describes the nucleon fluctuating into intermediate states such as Λ_c D^* and computes charm quark distributions via convolution of splitting functions with quark-level distributions within the hadrons; and a QCD global analysis from Hobbs, Melnitchouk, Londergan, and Jimenez-Delgado (2015) that uses the JR14 fitting framework with O(α_s) charm structure function formalism, incorporating higher-twist, target mass, and nuclear corrections to allow less restrictive kinematic cuts (Q^2 ≥ 1 GeV^2, W^2 ≥ 3.5 GeV^2) across 4296 data points from 26 independent data sets.

## Key numbers

- The MBM cutoff parameter fixed by fitting pp → Λ_c + X hadroproduction data from ISR yielded Λ = (3.0 ± 0.2) GeV.
- The QCD global analysis without EMC data found ⟨x⟩_IC < 0.1% at the 5σ level.
- When including EMC data, the full global fit gave ⟨x⟩_IC = 0.13 ± 0.04%.
- The EMC data alone favored ⟨x⟩_IC = 0.3-0.4%, but the EMC points were poorly fit with χ^2/datum = 4.3.
- The CT14 analysis found IC as large as ⟨x⟩_IC ∼ 2-3% could be accommodated depending on the IC scenario.
- The NNPDF analysis (Ball et al., 2015) found ⟨x⟩_IC = 0.7 ± 0.3% for their "Fitted" charm distribution.
- The proposed AFTER@CERN fixed-target pp experiment would operate at √s = 115 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the decades-long line of investigation into intrinsic charm that began with the BHPS paper (Brodsky, Hoyer, Peterson, and Sakai, 1980) and includes subsequent model-based analyses by Pumplin (2006) and QCD global fits by the CT14 collaboration (2014). The MBM approach provides a more physically motivated alternative to the BHPS scalar framework, incorporating hadronic degrees of freedom and allowing c/̄c asymmetries. The most recent global analysis (Hobbs, Melnitchouk, Londergan, and Jimenez-Delgado, 2015) places significantly tighter constraints on IC than earlier work, but the tension between the EMC data and lower-Q^2 HERA data (2012) leaves open questions that could be resolved by future measurements at an electron-ion collider, the AFTER@CERN experiment, or LHCb forward Z+charm-jet production (Williams, 2015).
