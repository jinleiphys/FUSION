# Baryon resonances and strong decays

> Constituent quark models provide a reasonable description of the baryon mass spectra. However, even in the light- and strange-flavor sectors several intriguing shortcomings remain. Especially with regard to strong decays of baryon resonances no consistent picture has so far emerged, and the existing experimental data cannot be explained in a satisfactory manner. Recently first covariant calculations with modern constituent quark models have become available for all pi, eta, and K decay modes of the low-lying light and strange baryons. They generally produced a remarkable underestimation of the experimental data for partial decay widths. We summarize the main results and discuss their impact on the classification of baryon resonances into flavor multiplets. These findings are of particular relevance for future efforts in the experimental investigation of baryon resonances.

# Baryon resonances and strong decays

## Key claim
Modern constituent quark models (CQMs) that produce reasonable baryon mass spectra systematically underestimate experimental partial decay widths for pi, eta, and K decay modes of low-lying light and strange baryons when calculated covariantly without additional fitting, revealing that the direct predictions are not compatible with phenomenology.

## Method
The authors performed covariant calculations of pi, eta, and K decay widths using relativistic CQMs within the point-form spectator model (PFSM), treating baryon resonances as bound three-quark eigenstates of the invariant mass operator and computing decay amplitudes as transition matrix elements between these bound states. They compared the direct theoretical predictions to experimental data from the Particle Data Group (PDG) and used the systematic pattern of results to reassign baryon resonances into SU(3) flavor octet and decuplet multiplets.

## Key numbers
- Theoretical partial decay widths for octet baryon resonances systematically underestimate experimental data by similar amounts across each octet, with only a few exceptions such as Lambda(1670) -> Sigma pi and Lambda(1690) -> Sigma pi coming out too large.
- The N(1650) -> N eta decay represents a notorious difficulty for CQMs, with the theoretical result being an exception to the general underestimation pattern.
- For the J^P = 1/2^- sector of Sigma resonances, the CQMs produce three lower-lying eigenstates, while only one established resonance with at least three-star status (Sigma(1750)) is reported, whose partial width for decay into Sigma pi is quoted as less than 8% of the total width.
- The Sigma(1620) has J^P determined to be 1/2^- and the Sigma(1560) has no definite J^P assignment; both have only two-star status.
- The Lambda(1810) is interpreted as a flavor singlet rather than an octet, in agreement with Matagne and Stancu (2006) but differing from the PDG classification.

## Context
This work extends covariant decay calculations by Melde, Plessas, and collaborators (2005-2007) using the point-form spectator model, and finds results surprisingly similar to those from the Bonn group using an instanton-induced CQM within the Bethe-Salpeter equation framework (Metsch 2004, Migura 2007). The systematic underestimation of decay widths contrasts with earlier nonrelativistic CQM calculations (Stancu 1989, Capstick 1993, Krassnigg 1999, Plessas 1999, Theussl 2000) that produced results scattering around experimental data, often overestimating widths. The findings enable future efforts to improve CQMs by including explicit couplings to decay channels, which would give baryon states finite widths and require a complete reconstruction of the models.
