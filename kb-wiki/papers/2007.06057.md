# On the nature of GW190814 and its impact on the understanding of supranuclear matter

> The observation of a compact object with a mass of $2.50-2.67M_{\odot}$ on August 14, 2019, by the LIGO Scientific and Virgo collaborations (LVC) has the potential to improve our understanding of the supranuclear equation of state. While the gravitational-wave analysis of the LVC suggests that GW190814 likely was a binary black hole system, the secondary component could also have been the heaviest neutron star observed to date. We use our previously derived nuclear-physics-multimessenger astrophysics framework to address the nature of this object. Based on our findings, we determine GW190814 to be a binary black hole merger with a probability of $>99.9\%$. Even if we weaken previously employed constraints on the maximum mass of neutron stars, the probability of a binary black hole origin is still $\sim 81\%$. Furthermore, we study the impact that this observation has on our understanding of the nuclear equation of state by analyzing the allowed region in the mass-radius diagram of neutron stars for both a binary black hole or neutron star--black hole scenario. We find that the unlikely scenario in which the secondary object was a neutron star requires rather stiff equations of state with a maximum speed of sound $c_s\geq \sqrt{0.6}$ times the speed of light, while the binary black hole scenario does not offer any new insight.

## Key claim

The authors determine that GW190814 was a binary black hole merger with a probability greater than 99.9% when using their full multimessenger framework, and still approximately 81% even when weakening previously employed constraints on the maximum mass of neutron stars; the abstract's claim of "binary black hole merger with a probability of >99.9%" depends on including an upper limit on the maximum neutron star mass derived from GW170817.

## Method

The authors applied their previously developed Nuclear Physics Multimessenger Astrophysics (NMMA) framework, which uses Bayesian inference to combine constraints from chiral effective field theory calculations, quantum Monte Carlo simulations, heavy pulsar mass measurements, NICER X-ray observations, gravitational-wave data from GW170817 and GW190425, and kilonova modeling of AT2017gfo. They compared the posterior distribution for the maximum neutron star mass from this framework with the posterior for the mass of GW190814's secondary component under different spin assumptions, calculating the probability that the secondary mass lies below or above the maximum neutron star mass for four scenarios.

## Key numbers

- The secondary component of GW190814 has a mass of 2.50-2.67 solar masses
- Probability that GW190814 was a binary black hole merger: greater than 99.9% (with GW170817 upper limit on maximum mass) and approximately 81% (without that upper limit)
- Probability that GW190814 was a neutron star-black hole merger: less than 0.1% (with GW170817 upper limit) and approximately 19% (without that upper limit)
- For the neutron star-black hole scenario with the GW170817 upper limit enforced, the maximum neutron star mass is constrained to 2.53^{+0.07}_{-0.06} solar masses and the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is 12.6^{+0.4}_{-0.5} km
- For the neutron star-black hole scenario without the GW170817 upper limit, the maximum neutron star mass is 2.68^{+0.14}_{-0.11} solar masses and the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is 12.7^{+0.6}_{-0.5} km
- For the binary black hole scenario with the GW170817 upper limit, the maximum neutron star mass is 2.18^{+0.15}_{-0.13} solar masses and the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is 11.8^{+0.9}_{-0.8} km
- For the binary black hole scenario without the GW170817 upper limit, the maximum neutron star mass is 2.29^{+0.26}_{-0.21} solar masses and the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is 12.0^{+0.9}_{-0.9} km
- The neutron star-black hole scenario requires a maximum speed of sound inside neutron stars of at least sqrt(0.6) times the speed of light

## Context

This work extends the NMMA framework developed by Dietrich (2020) to analyze GW190814, which was detected by LIGO and Virgo and reported by Abbott (2020). The results are consistent with and strengthen the conclusions of the LVC analysis, Most (2020), and Essick (2020), all of which found the binary black hole scenario to be strongly favored. The analysis provides the most complete multimessenger treatment of GW190814 to date, incorporating nuclear physics constraints, pulsar masses, NICER data, gravitational-wave observations, and kilonova modeling simultaneously. If future observations confirm the neutron star-black hole scenario, it would require revisiting current understanding of the equation of state at supranuclear densities and might indicate that chiral effective field theory breaks down below 1.5 times nuclear saturation density.
