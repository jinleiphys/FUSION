# Trees and Forests in Nuclear Physics

> We present a simple introduction to the decision tree algorithm using some examples from nuclear physics. We show how to improve the accuracy of the classical liquid drop nuclear mass model by performing Feature Engineering with a decision tree. Finally, we apply the method to the Duflo-Zuker model showing that, despite their simplicity, decision trees are capable of improving the description of nuclear masses using a limited number of free parameters.

## Key claim

Decision trees, despite their simplicity and explainability, can improve the accuracy of nuclear mass models such as the liquid drop model and the Duflo-Zuker model by learning patterns in the residuals, but the improvement depends on careful feature engineering and the choice of the number of leaves to avoid overfitting.

## Method

The authors applied the CART decision tree algorithm to the residuals (difference between experimental binding energies and model predictions) of the Bethe-Weizsacker liquid drop model and the Duflo-Zuker DZ10 mass model. They used the nuclear mass table AME2016, discarding data with experimental uncertainty larger than 100 keV, and trained decision trees with features N, Z, and A to minimize the mean squared error, using k-fold cross-validation to select the optimal number of leaves.

## Key numbers

- The liquid drop model alone has a root mean square (RMS) deviation of approximately 3.0 MeV (from Figure 2 caption, left panel).
- The DZ10 model alone has an RMS deviation roughly one order of magnitude smaller than the liquid drop model (from text, Figure 2 caption).
- In the two-variable synthetic example, a decision tree with 3 leaves achieved an MSE of 0.073, while a tree with 7 leaves achieved an MSE of 0 (from Table 1).
- In the single-variable synthetic example, the optimal split point was found at x* = 0.493, with leaf values of 0.003 and 1.007.

## Context

This work extends the application of machine learning techniques to low-energy nuclear physics, following previous guides on bootstrap techniques by the same authors (Pastore, 2019). It builds on the ISNET workshop series and the associated focus issue in Journal of Physics G (Ireland, 2015). The paper enables further use of decision trees and ensemble methods like Random Forests for improving nuclear mass predictions, and demonstrates that even simple models can benefit from feature engineering without requiring complex neural networks.
