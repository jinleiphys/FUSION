# Microscopic description of complex nuclear decay: multimodal fission

> Our understanding of nuclear fission, a fundamental nuclear decay, is still incomplete due to the complexity of the process. In this paper, we describe a study of spontaneous fission using the symmetry-unrestricted nuclear density functional theory. Our results show that the observed bimodal fission can be explained in terms of pathways in multidimensional collective space corresponding to different geometries of fission products. We also predict a new phenomenon of trimodal spontaneous fission for some rutherfordium, seaborgium, and hassium isotopes.

## Key claim

The observed bimodal fission in fermium and transfermium nuclei can be explained by pathways in multidimensional collective space corresponding to different fragment geometries, and trimodal spontaneous fission is predicted for some rutherfordium, seaborgium, and hassium isotopes, based on symmetry-unrestricted nuclear density functional theory calculations.

## Method

The authors used the symmetry-unrestricted Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov solver HFODD with the SkM* energy density functional and a seniority pairing force fitted to experimental gaps in 252Fm. They computed two-dimensional energy surfaces in the deformation spaces {Q20, Q30} and {Q20, Q40} for even-even nuclei with 98 ≤ Z ≤ 108 and 154 ≤ N ≤ 160, then identified one-dimensional fission pathways by following local energy minima as functions of the driving quadrupole moment Q20. Spontaneous fission half-lives were estimated from the WKB expression for the double-humped potential barrier assuming one-dimensional tunneling along Q20, with collective inertia parameters and zero-point energy corrections calculated from the cranking model.

## Key numbers

- The asymmetric trajectory aEF bifurcates away from Q30=0 near Q20 ≈ 150 b, while the bifurcation between symmetric elongated (sEF) and symmetric compact (sCF) fragments occurs near Q20 ≈ 225 b in 258Fm.
- The total kinetic energy distributions of fission fragments in bimodal fission have two peaks centered around 200 and 233 MeV.
- The existence of a small outer barrier in 256Fm increases the fission half-life by more than four orders of magnitude compared to that of 258Fm.
- A single HFODD run with all self-consistent symmetries broken takes about 60 minutes of CPU time, requiring about 3 CPU-years for the full fission pathway analysis of 24 nuclei.
- The first barrier is reduced by several MeV due to triaxial degrees of freedom.
- Calculations assumed zero-point energies E0 = 0.3 MeV and 0.5 MeV for the ground state energy.

## Context

This work extends earlier macroscopic-microscopic method studies of bimodal fission by Broglia (1986), Moller (1987), and others, as well as symmetry-restricted density functional theory calculations by Warda (2002, 2006) and Bonneau (2006). The symmetry-unrestricted approach allows simultaneous inclusion of elongation, triaxiality, and reflection-asymmetry, which previous studies did not consider. The results enable future improvements including multidimensional inertia tensors, direct minimization of the collective action in multidimensional space, and extension to nonadiabatic effects.
