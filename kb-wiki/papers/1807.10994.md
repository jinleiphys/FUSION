# Spin tensor and its role in non-equilibrium thermodynamics

> It is shown that the description of a relativistic fluid at local thermodynamic equilibrium depends on the particular quantum stress-energy tensor operator chosen, e.g., the canonical or symmetrized Belinfante stress-energy tensor. We argue that the Belinfante tensor is not appropriate to describe a relativistic fluid whose macroscopic polarization relaxes slowly to thermodynamic equilibrium and that a spin tensor, like the canonical spin tensor, is required. As a consequence, the description of a polarized relativistic fluid involves an extension of relativistic hydrodynamics including a new antisymmetric rank-two tensor as a dynamical field. We show that the canonical and Belinfante tensors lead to different predictions for measurable quantities such as spectrum and polarization of particles produced in relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The description of a relativistic fluid at local thermodynamic equilibrium is not pseudo-gauge invariant; the canonical and Belinfante stress-energy tensors lead to different predictions for measurable quantities such as particle spectra and polarization in heavy-ion collisions, and the Belinfante tensor is inappropriate when macroscopic polarization relaxes slowly compared to the hydrodynamic time scale.

## Method

The authors derive the local thermodynamic equilibrium density operator using entropy maximization with constraints on mean densities of conserved currents, first with the Belinfante stress-energy tensor alone and then with the canonical tensors including an explicit spin tensor constraint. They perform pseudo-gauge transformations between the canonical and Belinfante formulations to show that the resulting density operators differ unless the spin potential equals the thermal vorticity and the symmetric gradient of the four-temperature vanishes. They then analyze the conditions for stationarity and global equilibrium, and discuss the extension of relativistic hydrodynamics to include the spin tensor as a dynamical field.

## Key numbers

- The thermal vorticity is defined as \(\varpi_{\lambda\nu} = \frac{1}{2} (\nabla_\nu \beta_\lambda - \nabla_\lambda \beta_\nu)\), where \(\beta\) is the four-temperature vector.
- The symmetric part of the gradient of the four-temperature vector is \(\xi_{\lambda\nu} = \frac{1}{2} (\nabla_\nu \beta_\lambda + \nabla_\lambda \beta_\nu)\).
- The spin tensor introduces 6 independent components that must be determined from 6 additional partial differential equations: \(\partial_\lambda \spt^{\lambda,\mu\nu} = T^{\nu\mu} - T^{\mu\nu}\).
- For the free Dirac field, the canonical spin tensor is \(\wspt^{\mu,\lambda\nu}_C = \frac{i}{8}\, {\overline {\widehat \psi}} \{\gamma^\mu , [\gamma^\lambda,\gamma^\nu] \} {\widehat \psi}\), which is completely antisymmetric.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of spin tensor significance from earlier theoretical studies (Hehl, 1970s; Becattini and Tinti, 2010s) into the phenomenology of relativistic heavy-ion collisions, where the STAR collaboration (2017) measured global polarization of particles. The authors show that the spin tensor becomes necessary when spin density relaxes slowly, in contrast to the Belinfante formulation which assumes instantaneous equilibration. This enables the development of extended relativistic hydrodynamics with spin (Florkowski, 2018; Florkowski et al., 2018) and provides a framework for calculating polarization observables that differ from predictions based solely on thermal vorticity.
