# Proposal for a Nuclear Light Source

> The paper considers a principal possibility of creating a nuclear light source of the vacuum ultra violet (VUV) range based on the $^{229}$Th nucleus. This nuclear light source can help to solve two main problems -- excitation of the low-lying $^{229m}$Th isomer and precision measurement of the nuclear isomeric transition energy. The Thorium nuclear light source is based on the nuclei implanted in a thin dielectric film with a large bandgap. While passing an electric current through the sample, the $^{229}$Th nuclei are excited to the low energy isomeric state $3/2^+(8.19\pm0.12$ eV) in the process of inelastic scattering of conduction electrons. The subsequent spontaneous decay of $^{229m}$Th is followed by the emission of $γ$ quanta in the VUV range. The luminosity of the Thorium nuclear light source is approximately $10^5$~photons/s per 1~A of current and per 1~ng of $^{229}$Th. The suggested scheme to obtain $γ$ radiation from the $^{229m}$Th isomer can be considered as a kind of nuclear analogue of the optical radiation from the usual metal-insulator-semiconductor (MIS) junction.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a nuclear light source based on \(^{229}\)Th can be realized by exciting the isomer via inelastic scattering of conduction electrons in a thin dielectric film, but the scheme depends critically on the dielectric having a bandgap larger than the isomeric transition energy (8.19 ± 0.12 eV) and on the absence of additional electronic defect states in the bandgap that would quench the photon output.

## Method

The authors propose implanting \(^{229}\)Th nuclei into a thin (10–20 nm) dielectric film with a large bandgap (e.g., SiO\(_2\) or MgF\(_2\)), sandwiched between a metal contact and a doped semiconductor or metal substrate. An electric current is passed through the sample; conduction electrons with kinetic energies around 9–10 eV inelastically scatter off the \(^{229}\)Th nuclei, exciting them to the \(3/2^+\) isomeric state at 8.19 eV. The subsequent spontaneous \(\gamma\) decay of \(^{229m}\)Th in the VUV range is detected, with the internal conversion channel suppressed because the dielectric bandgap exceeds the isomeric energy. The \(\gamma\) activity is calculated using rate equations that incorporate the Purcell effect from the layered structure and the refractive index of the medium.

## Key numbers

- Isomeric transition energy: \(E_{\text{is}} = 8.19 \pm 0.12\) eV (from Peik (2021)).
- Luminosity of the nuclear light source: approximately \(10^5\) photons/s per 1 A of current and per 1 ng of \(^{229}\)Th.
- Isomer excitation cross section for inelastic electron scattering: (2–6) \(\times 10^{-26}\) cm\(^2\) for electron energies around 10 eV.
- Radiative half-life of the isomer in vacuum: \(T_{1/2} = 20\)–100 min (for \(B_{\text{W.u.}}(M1) = 0.03\) or 0.006–0.008, at \(E_{\text{is}} = 8.2\) eV).
- Dielectric film thickness: 10–20 nm; breakdown voltage for 10 nm SiO\(_2\) is 40 V (dielectric strength ~4 GV/m).
- Alpha activity of 1 ng \(^{229}\)Th: 7 Bq; background from alpha particles in the 15 nm dielectric layer is approximately \(10^3\) photons/s per 1 ng \(^{229}\)Th.
- Maximum \(^{229}\)Th loading in a 1 cm\(^2\) × 15 nm SiO\(_2\) film: about 10 ng.
- Expected detector count rate: \(10^4\)–\(10^5\) counts/s for 1 A current, detector quantum efficiency \(\gtrsim 10^{-1}\), and photon collection angle \(\approx 2\pi\).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical proposal for exciting \(^{229m}\)Th via inelastic electron scattering (Tkalya (2020)) and the experimental demonstration of gamma decay from \(^{229m}\)Th implanted in a large-bandgap dielectric (Kraemer (2022)). It aims to solve two key problems: non-resonant excitation of the isomer (avoiding the need for precise laser tuning) and precision measurement of the isomeric transition energy. The scheme is a nuclear analogue of a metal-insulator-semiconductor (MIS) junction light source. The paper builds on prior studies of the Purcell effect on \(^{229m}\)Th decay (Tkalya (2018)) and on the suppression of internal conversion in wide-gap dielectrics (Tkalya (2000), Dessovic (2014)). It enables a practical path toward a nuclear clock and a \(\gamma\)-ray laser, provided that defect states in the dielectric bandgap can be avoided, as studied by Borisyuk (2018) and Nickerson (2020).
