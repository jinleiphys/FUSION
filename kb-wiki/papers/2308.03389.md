# Quasiparticle Cosmology

> We consider thermodynamics of the Universe within a quasiparticle approach where the collective dynamics of a system is governed by the thermal mass of the constituents. The spacetime dependence of this thermal mass leads to a negative contribution to the pressure and a positive contribution to the energy density, similar to the effect due to dark energy. We propose a mechanism based on thermodynamic arguments to quantify this contribution from the thermal vacuum of the system. For a sufficiently large spacetime variation of the thermal mass, the effective pressure can become negative and could mimic a dark energy equation of state. We validate our framework using results from renormalizable interacting scalar field theory and demonstrate an application on QCD axion.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that requiring thermodynamic consistency for a system of quasiparticles with temperature-dependent mass naturally produces a vacuum energy term \(B(T)\) that can mimic dark energy, but the numerical values obtained for QCD axions are many orders of magnitude smaller than the observed cosmological constant, so the mechanism is only a qualitative demonstration rather than a viable dark energy candidate.

## Method

The author constructs a quasiparticle thermodynamic framework where particle masses depend on temperature (and thus on spacetime through the Universe's expansion). Starting from an effective Hamiltonian with a temperature-dependent zero-point energy \(E_0^*\), the pressure and energy density are modified by a term \(B(T)\) that is fixed by demanding consistency with the thermodynamic relation \(\rho = T\,dP/dT - P\). The framework is validated by comparing its predictions for pressure against perturbative results from \(\lambda\phi^4\) scalar field theory, and then applied to QCD axions using a parametrized thermal mass fitted to NJL, PNJL, and lattice QCD calculations.

## Key numbers

- Observed cosmological constant energy density: \(\rho_\Lambda \simeq 2.5 \times 10^{-11}\,\text{eV}^4\) (from astrophysical data, Eq. 4).
- Vacuum energy densities from particle physics scales: QCD \(\sim 10^{34}\,\text{eV}^4\), electroweak \(\sim 10^{45}\,\text{eV}^4\), Planck \(\sim 10^{108}\,\text{eV}^4\) (Eqs. 7-9).
- For QCD axion with zero-temperature mass \(m_0 = 6\times 10^{-4}\,\text{eV}\) and present CMB temperature \(T_0 = 6.6\times 10^{-4}\,\text{eV}\), the framework yields \(B(T_0) \simeq 10^{-30}\,\text{eV}^4\).
- By adjusting the parametrization so that the thermal mass drops sharply at \(T_0\) rather than at \(T_c \sim 188\,\text{MeV}\), the value improves to \(B(T_0) \simeq 10^{-15}\,\text{eV}^4\).

## Context

This work extends the quasiparticle approach previously applied to quark-gluon plasma thermodynamics (Gorenstein 1995, Jeon 1994, Romatschke 2011) into a cosmological setting, and connects to earlier studies of dark matter with variable masses that noted thermodynamic inconsistency and proposed "dark entropy production" (Casas 1991, Garcia-Bellido 1992). The framework offers a new thermodynamic perspective on the cosmological constant problem but does not yet produce observationally relevant numbers; the author suggests future work should incorporate the temperature-dependent \(B(T)\) into Friedmann equations and compare with observational data.
