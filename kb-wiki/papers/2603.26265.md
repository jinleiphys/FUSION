# Inelastic nucleon-nucleus scattering from a microscopic point of view

> We apply to the nucleon-nucleus inelastic process a fully coherent microscopic multiple scattering approach. Our study addresses the complexities inherent in characterizing inelastic scattering events, offering a comprehensive theoretical model grounded in the reaction theory. The approach is based on the distorted-wave approximation and requires the knowledge of three potentials, which give the initial and final distorted wave functions and the transition potential. All of them are derived just like the microscopic optical potential for elastic nucleon-nucleus scattering we derived in previous papers of ours within the framework of the Watson multiple scattering theory and adopting the impulse approximation. The potentials are obtained by folding nonlocal ab initio nuclear densities from the No-Core Shell Model (NCSM) with a nucleon-nucleon $t$ matrix computed with a chiral interaction consistent with the one used in the calculation of the density. The only difference in the formal expressions of the three potentials resides in the nuclear density, where we use the ground and excited state densities of the target and the transition density. By extending methods traditionally applied to elastic scattering, we incorporate the effects of inelastic transitions enabling an accurate description of the experimental differential cross section. The predictive power of our numerical results is benchmarked against empirical data of inelastic proton scattering off $^{12}$C, for the transition to the $2^+$ state at 4.44 MeV, in a range of projectile energies of 65-300 MeV. The generally good description of the experimental cross sections as functions of the scattering angle gives clear evidence of the reliability and robustness of a model that does not contain any free adjustable parameters.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a fully microscopic, parameter-free model based on the distorted-wave impulse approximation (DWIA) can describe inelastic proton scattering cross sections, but the approach is tested only for the specific transition to the first \(2^+\) state of \(^{12}\)C at 4.44 MeV and for projectile energies between 65 and 300 MeV, leaving its general applicability to other nuclei and transitions as an open question.

## Method

The authors extend their previous microscopic optical potential for elastic scattering to inelastic processes using the distorted-wave approximation, which requires three potentials: the initial distorting potential (elastic optical potential), the final distorting potential, and the transition potential. All three potentials are derived at first order in the Watson multiple scattering theory with the impulse approximation, by folding nonlocal ab initio one-body nuclear densities from the No-Core Shell Model (NCSM) with a nucleon-nucleon \(t\) matrix computed from a chiral interaction (Entem et al., N\(^4\)LO with 500 MeV cutoff) consistent with the one used for the densities. The only difference among the three potentials lies in the density matrix: the ground-state density, the excited-state density, and the transition density are used respectively.

## Key numbers

* Differential cross sections for inelastic proton scattering off \(^{12}\)C to the \(2^+\) state at 4.44 MeV are calculated and compared to experimental data at projectile energies of 65, 120, 135, 185, 200, 250, and 300 MeV.
* The NCSM calculation used \(N_{max} = 8\) excitations and a harmonic oscillator frequency \(\hbar \omega = 16\) MeV.
* The Similarity Renormalization Group (SRG) cutoff was set to \(\lambda_{\mathrm{SRG}} = 2.0\) fm\(^{-1}\).
* The three-nucleon force low-energy constants were fixed at \(c_D = -1.8\) and \(c_E = -0.31\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous microscopic optical potential for elastic nucleon-nucleus scattering (Vorabbi et al., 2015-2024) to inelastic transitions, providing a fully coherent framework without free parameters. It contrasts with earlier phenomenological approaches such as the \(g\)-folding model with NCSM densities by Kim et al. (2007), which used a phenomenological interaction and lacked full consistency between the nuclear structure and reaction calculations, and with the microscopic coupled-channel calculations by Tomita et al. (2015) that employed a different folding procedure. The approach enables future applications to other nuclei and transitions using the same ab initio framework, potentially allowing systematic studies of inelastic scattering across the nuclear chart.
