# Deconfinement of strangeness and freeze-out from charge fluctuations

> We use Lattice QCD calculations of fluctuations and correlations of various conserved charges to show that the deconfinement of strangeness takes place in the chiral crossover region of QCD; however, inside the quark-gluon plasma strange quarks remain strongly interacting at least up to temperatures twice the QCD crossover temperature. Further, we discuss how the freeze-out parameters of heavy-ion collisions can be determined in a model-independent way through direct comparisons between experimentally measured higher order cumulants of conserved charges and corresponding Lattice QCD calculations. Utilizing the preliminary data from the STAR and PHENIX experiments we illustrate this method. Although, the Lattice QCD based determinations of the freeze-out parameters utilizing data sets of different experiments and different observables are currently not consistent with each other, it is tantalizing to see that all the observed freeze-out parameters lie very close to the chiral/deconfinement crossover region of QCD.

## Key claim

Using lattice QCD calculations of fluctuations and correlations of conserved charges, the authors show that the deconfinement of strangeness occurs in the chiral crossover region of QCD, but that inside the quark-gluon plasma strange quarks remain strongly interacting at least up to temperatures twice the QCD crossover temperature; the freeze-out parameters of heavy-ion collisions can be determined in a model-independent way through direct comparisons between experimentally measured higher order cumulants of conserved charges and corresponding lattice QCD calculations, though current results from different experiments and observables are not yet consistent with each other.

## Method

The authors performed lattice QCD calculations of generalized susceptibilities (derivatives of the pressure with respect to chemical potentials) for baryon number, strangeness, and electric charge. They constructed specific combinations of these susceptibilities (v1 and v2) that vanish identically for an uncorrelated hadron gas to probe the deconfinement of strangeness, and compared ratios of baryon-strangeness and charge-strangeness correlations to predictions from non-interacting quark gas and one-loop resummed Hard Thermal Loop perturbation theory. For freeze-out determination, they used a Taylor expansion of charge densities in powers of chemical potentials up to next-to-leading order, imposing strangeness neutrality and isospin asymmetry constraints, and compared lattice QCD results for cumulant ratios (R31^Q as a thermometer and R12^Q as a baryometer) with preliminary STAR and PHENIX experimental data.

## Key numbers

- Chiral crossover temperature: T_c = 154(9) MeV
- For temperatures T_c ≲ T ≲ 2T_c, lattice QCD results for second order baryon/charge-strangeness correlations are far from values expected for non-interacting quarks
- Next-to-leading order contributions to mu_Q and mu_S are below 10% and well controlled for baryon chemical potential mu_B ≲ 200 MeV (corresponding to RHIC energies down to sqrt(s_NN) ≳ 19.6 GeV)
- Around T ≈ 157 MeV, lattice QCD results give mu_S/mu_B ≈ 0.24
- Average freeze-out temperature determined from STAR data averaged over sqrt(s_NN) = 19.6-200 GeV: T^f = 158(7) MeV
- The chiral/deconfinement crossover region as a function of mu_B: T_c(mu_B) = (154(9) - [0.0066(7)/154(9)] mu_B^2) MeV

## Context

This work extends lattice QCD calculations of the QCD phase diagram by providing evidence that strangeness deconfinement coincides with the chiral crossover, and introduces a model-independent method for determining freeze-out parameters that contrasts with traditional statistical model fits (such as those by Cleymans et al. (2005) and the Hadron Resonance Gas model). The results enable direct comparisons between first-principle lattice QCD calculations and heavy-ion collision experiments from RHIC (STAR and PHENIX) and future facilities like FAIR and NICA, though the authors caution that effects of finite system sizes and acceptance cuts (discussed by Bzdak et al. (2012)) must be addressed before the grand canonical ensemble approach can be fully validated for heavy-ion collision conditions.
