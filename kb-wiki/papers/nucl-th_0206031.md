# A consistent approximation scheme beyond RPA for bosons

> In this paper, we develop a consistent extension of RPA for bosonic systems. In order to illustrate the method, we consider the case of the anharmonic oscillator. We compare our results with those obtained in mean-field and standard RPA approaches, with the exact ones and show that they are very close to the exact ones.

## Key claim
The paper develops a consistent extension of the random phase approximation (RPA) for bosonic systems that removes contradictions present in renormalized RPA (r-RPA), such as violations of the virial theorem and the Heisenberg uncertainty principle, by self-consistently calculating expectation values of three- and four-operator products rather than factorizing them.

## Method
The authors apply a Dyson-Schwinger approach to a one-dimensional anharmonic oscillator with Hamiltonian H = p²/2 + μ²X²/2 + gX⁴. They start from a Bogoliubov-transformed mean-field basis and then go beyond standard RPA by determining expectation values like ⟨x³⟩, ⟨p²x⟩, ⟨x⁴⟩, ⟨p⁴⟩, and ⟨p²x²⟩ self-consistently via the integration of Green's functions, rather than using a mean-field ground state or a factorization approximation for these higher-order terms. The basis parameters ω and s are determined by generalized gap equations derived from minimizing the ground state energy.

## Key numbers
*   For μ²/g = -1, the expectation value ⟨b†bbb⟩ is -0.033 and ⟨b†b†bb⟩ is 0.026, demonstrating that these three-operator correlations are non-zero in the extended approach.
*   The mean-field approximation shows a maximum discrepancy of about 10% from the exact ground state energy.
*   The "phase transition" in the model, where the shift parameter s jumps from a non-zero value to zero, occurs at approximately μ²/g ≈ -6.8.

## Context
This work extends the standard RPA and renormalized RPA (r-RPA) approaches for bosonic systems, which were previously applied to a λΦ⁴-theory by Hansen et al. (2002) and found to have problems with covariance and self-consistency. The method is tested on the anharmonic oscillator, a standard test ground previously used for coupled cluster techniques by Bishop and Flynn (1988) and for early SCRPA attempts by Dukelsky and Schuck (1991). The results are very close to the exact solution, suggesting the method could be applied to quantum field theory to cure problems identified in the work of Hansen et al. (2002), though the authors note it cannot compete with the Coupled Cluster Method (CCM) in precision for quantum chemistry or solid-state physics.
