# Parity violating asymmetry with nuclear medium effects in deep inelastic $\vec e$ scattering

> Recently at JLab using polarised electron beam on unpolarised deuteron target measurements have been performed for the parity violating asymmetry($A_{PV}$) and there are future plans to measure this asymmetry using various nuclear targets. In this paper, we study $A_{PV}$ in nuclear targets like $^{12}C$, $^{56}Fe$ and $^{208}Pb$, in a local density approximation using spectral function which takes into account Fermi motion, binding energy correction and nucleon correlations. Furthermore, the pion and rho cloud contributions have also been taken into account. The present model has been used earlier to study medium effects in electromagnetic as well as weak interaction induced processes in the DIS region.

## Key claim

The authors calculate the parity-violating asymmetry \(A_{PV}\) in deep inelastic scattering of polarized electrons from nuclear targets (\(^{12}\text{C}\), \(^{56}\text{Fe}\), \(^{208}\text{Pb}\)) using a local density approximation with a nucleon spectral function that includes Fermi motion, binding energy corrections, and nucleon correlations, plus pion and rho meson cloud contributions; the results show that nuclear medium effects modify \(A_{PV}\) significantly compared to the free nucleon case, especially at intermediate and high Bjorken \(x\), and that non-isoscalarity in \(^{56}\text{Fe}\) and \(^{208}\text{Pb}\) further alters the asymmetry.

## Method

The parity-violating asymmetry \(A_{PV}\) is expressed in terms of nuclear structure functions \(F_{1,2}^{\gamma}\) (electromagnetic) and \(F_{1,2,3}^{\gamma Z}\) (weak-electromagnetic interference), which are computed by convoluting free-nucleon parton distribution functions (CTEQ6.6) with a nucleon spectral function derived in a local density approximation. The spectral function accounts for Fermi motion, binding energy, and nucleon correlations via a relativistic nucleon self-energy in nuclear matter; additionally, pion and rho meson cloud contributions to \(F_2\) are included using a many-body field theoretical approach. Target mass corrections are applied using the formalism of Schienbein et al., and the model is applied to \(^{12}\text{C}\), \(^{56}\text{Fe}\), and \(^{208}\text{Pb}\) at \(Q^2 = 5\ \text{GeV}^2\) and beam energy \(E = 6.06\ \text{GeV}\).

## Key numbers

- For \(^{12}\text{C}\), the kinetic energy per nucleon is 20.2 MeV and the binding energy per nucleon is 7.6 MeV (Table I).
- For \(^{56}\text{Fe}\), the kinetic energy per nucleon is 30.0 MeV and the binding energy per nucleon is 8.8 MeV (Table I).
- For \(^{208}\text{Pb}\), the kinetic energy per nucleon is 32.7 MeV and the binding energy per nucleon is 7.8 MeV (Table I).
- The asymmetry \(A_{PV}\) is computed at \(Q^2 = 5\ \text{GeV}^2\) and electron beam energy \(E = 6.06\ \text{GeV}\) for all three targets (Fig. 5); the results show that nuclear medium effects (spectral function plus meson cloud) modify \(A_{PV}\) relative to the free isoscalar nucleon case, with the deviation increasing for heavier nuclei.
- The ratio \(a_3/a_2\) is presented for \(^{56}\text{Fe}\) and \(^{208}\text{Pb}\) at \(Q^2 = 5\ \text{GeV}^2\) (Fig. 4), showing deviations from the free isoscalar nucleon result due to non-isoscalarity and nuclear medium effects.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of nuclear medium effects in deep inelastic scattering (EMC effect) and neutrino scattering on nuclei by the same authors (Athar et al., 2006; Sajjad Athar et al., 2011; Haider et al., 2012) to the parity-violating asymmetry \(A_{PV}\) in polarized electron scattering. It directly addresses the need for theoretical calculations of nuclear modifications in \(A_{PV}\) emphasized by Cloet et al. (2012) in view of planned JLab 12 GeV experiments with nuclear targets (Fe, Au, Pb). The results enable future comparisons with high-precision JLab measurements to test the Standard Model and search for physics beyond the Standard Model, and also complement mean-field calculations using the NJL model (Nambu, 1960; Cloet et al., 2005).
