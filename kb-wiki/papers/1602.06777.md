# Modification of the Nuclear Landscape in the Inverse Problem Framework using the Generalized Bethe-Weizsäcker Mass Formula

> The dependence on the structure functions and Z, N numbers of the nuclear binding energy is investigated within the inverse problem(IP) approach. This approach allows us to infer the underlying model parameters from experimental observation, rather than to predict the observations from the model parameters. The IP was formulated for the numerical generalization of the semi-empirical mass formula of BW. It was solved in step by step way based on the AME2012 nuclear database. The established parametrization describes the measured nuclear masses of 2564 isotopes with a maximum deviation less than 2.6 MeV, starting from the number of protons and number of neutrons equal to 1. The set of parameters $\{a_{i}\}$, $i=1,\dots, {\mathcal{N}}_{\rm{param}}$ of our fit represent the solution of an overdetermined system of nonlinear equations, which represent equalities between the binding energy $E_{B,j}^{\rm{Expt}}(A,Z)$ and its model $E_{B,j}^{\rm{Th}}(A,Z,\{a_{i}\})$, where $j$ is the index of the given isotope. The solution of the overdetermined system of nonlinear equations has been obtained with the help of the Aleksandrov's auto-regularization method of Gauss-Newton(GN) type for ill-posed problems. The efficiency of the above methods was checked by comparing relevant results with the results obtained independently. The explicit form of unknown functions was discovered in a step-by-step way using the modified least $χ^{2}$ procedure, that realized in the algorithms which were developed by Aleksandrov to solve nonlinear systems of equations via the GN method, lets us to choose between two functions with same $χ^{2}$ the better one. In the obtained generalized model the corrections to the binding energy depend on nine proton (2, 8, 14, 20, 28, 50, 82, 108, 124) and ten neutron (2, 8, 14, 20, 28, 50, 82, 124, 152, 202) magic numbers as well on the asymptotic boundaries of their influence.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the inverse problem framework, using the Aleksandrov auto-regularization Gauss-Newton method, can extract a generalized Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula directly from experimental nuclear mass data, achieving a maximum deviation of less than 2.6 MeV for 2564 isotopes, but the solution depends on the specific choice of magic numbers and asymptotic boundaries discovered through a step-by-step least-chi-squared procedure.

## Method

The authors formalized the nuclear mass problem as an inverse problem, solving an overdetermined system of nonlinear equations that equate the experimental binding energies to a generalized Bethe-Weizsäcker model. They used the AME2012 nuclear database with 2564 measured isotopes (excluding extrapolated masses marked with #) and applied the Aleksandrov dynamic auto-regularization method of Gauss-Newton type (REGN) to obtain stable solutions. The explicit form of unknown functions in the generalized mass formula was discovered step-by-step using a modified least chi-squared procedure that selects between functions with the same chi-squared value.

## Key numbers

* Maximum deviation of the parametrization from measured nuclear masses: less than 2.6 MeV for 2564 isotopes
* Number of experimental nuclear masses used: 2564, including the Hydrogen atom
* Nine proton magic numbers identified: 2, 8, 14, 20, 28, 50, 82, 108, 124
* Ten neutron magic numbers identified: 2, 8, 14, 20, 28, 50, 82, 124, 152, 202

## Context

This work extends the classic Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula (Weizsäcker 1935, Bethe 1936) by applying an inverse problem methodology rather than forward modeling, building on earlier latent regularity analysis by Aleksandrov and Gadjokov (1971). The approach provides a complement to macroscopic-microscopic models such as the finite-range droplet model (Moller 1995) and microscopic density functional theory calculations, and enables predictions for nuclei far from stability, including superheavy elements, where direct measurements are not yet possible.
