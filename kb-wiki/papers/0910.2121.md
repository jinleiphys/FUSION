# Astrophysical Implication of Low E(2^+_1) in Neutron-rich Sn Isotopes

> The observation and prediction of unusually depressed first excited 2^+_1 states in even-A neutron - rich isotopes of semi-magic Sn above 132Sn provide motivations for reviewing the problems related to the nuclear astrophysics in general. In the present work, the beta-decay rates of the exotic even Sn isotopes (134,136Sn) above the 132Sn core have been calculated as a function of temperature (T). In order to get the necessary ft values, B(GT) values corresponding to allowed Gamow Teller (GT-) beta-decay have been theoretically calculated using shell model. The total decay rate shows decrease with increasing temperature as the ground state population is depleted and population of excited states with slower decay rates increases. The abundance at each Z value is inversely proportional to the decay constant of the waiting point nucleus for that particular Z. So the increase in half-life of isotopes of Sn, like 136Sn, might have substantial impact on the r-process nucleosynthesis.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the unusually low energy of the first excited 2⁺ state in neutron-rich Sn isotopes, particularly as predicted by the SMPN interaction for ¹³⁶Sn, leads to a temperature-dependent increase in beta-decay half-lives that could substantially impact r-process nucleosynthesis, but only if the SMPN interaction's prediction of depressed 2⁺₁ energies is correct rather than the constant energies predicted by the CWG interaction.

## Method

The authors performed untruncated shell model calculations in the π(gdsh) ⊕ ν(hfpi) valence space above the ¹³²Sn core using both the empirical SMPN and effective CWG (1+2)-body Hamiltonians to obtain B(GT) values for allowed Gamow-Teller β⁻-decay transitions. They then calculated beta-decay rates as a function of temperature for ¹³⁴Sn and ¹³⁶Sn by summing over thermally populated excited states of the mother nucleus and allowed transitions to daughter states, using the OXBASH code for the shell model calculations.

## Key numbers

- For ¹³⁴Sn: experimental half-life is 1.050 s, Q-value is 7.37 MeV; saturated half-life values at high temperature are approximately 5 s with SMPN and approximately 7.5 s with CWG
- For ¹³⁶Sn: experimental half-life is 0.250 s, Q-value is 8.37 MeV; saturated half-life at high temperature is approximately 2.2 s with both interactions
- The E(2⁺₁) value for ¹³⁶Sn predicted by SMPN is 578 keV, compared to 733 keV predicted by CWG
- The difference [E(2⁺₁)(¹³⁶Sn) - E(2⁺₁)(¹³⁸Te)] for N=86 is 108 keV with SMPN, consistent with the Casten-Sherrill trend; for CWG this difference is 377 keV
- For N=84, the difference [E(2⁺₁)Sn - E(2⁺₁)Te] is 119 keV
- Deviation from laboratory half-life begins at approximately 0.1 MeV temperature (approximately 10⁹ K)
- Decay rates from the first and second excited 2⁺ states are two orders of magnitude slower than ground-state decay at kT = 1 MeV

## Context

This work extends the Casten-Sherrill systematics (Casten and Sherrill) which noted the decreasing energy difference between Sn and Te 2⁺₁ states near N=84, and applies it to the astrophysical r-process context where ¹³⁶Sn is known as a classical "waiting point" nucleus for the A=130 solar system abundance peak. The calculations use shell model interactions from Sarkar (SMPN) and Brown (CWG) and build on the formalism of Kar et al. for temperature-dependent beta-decay rates. The results suggest that if the SMPN predictions of depressed 2⁺₁ energies are correct, the increased half-lives of Sn isotopes at r-process temperatures could alter abundance calculations for the A=130 peak and the generation of more neutron-rich species.
