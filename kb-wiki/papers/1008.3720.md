# Quantum effects on the phase diagram of nuclear-like systems

> A Path Integral Monte Carlo method is used to investigate the thermodynamics of nuclear like systems. Systems composed of bosons or fermions interracting via a Lennard-Jones potential with periodic boundary conditions were simulated and the corresponding phase diagrams are constructed. The Path Integral Monte Carlo appears to be a powerfull tool for investigating quantum effects in nuclear multifragmentation phenomena.

## Key claim

Path Integral Monte Carlo simulations of 16-particle Lennard-Jones systems show that both bosonic and fermionic quantum fluids have a common critical point at 10 MeV, with quantum effects becoming negligible above this temperature. The abstract's claim that PIMC is "powerful" is hedged by the paper's explicit acknowledgment that the fermion sign problem is mitigated only because the simulations are performed for "highly excited" systems where it "shouldn't play here a big role."

## Method

The author performed Path Integral Monte Carlo simulations of 16 particles interacting via a truncated and long-range-corrected Lennard-Jones 6-12 potential, placed in a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions. Both bosonic (symmetric wavefunctions) and fermionic (antisymmetric wavefunctions) systems were simulated by applying the appropriate permutation sums to the distinguishable-particle path integral, using the primitive approximation for the density matrix. Pressure was computed from the virial expression, and isothermal pressure-volume curves were constructed for temperatures from 6 to 10 MeV, from which Maxwell constructions yielded the liquid-gas coexistence regions.

## Key numbers

- Lennard-Jones parameters: σ = 2.55 fm, ε = 25.03 MeV, chosen to roughly describe binding energies of various size nuclei.
- System size: 16 quantum particles in a cubic recipient with periodic boundary conditions.
- Temperature range of simulated isotherms: 6 to 10 MeV.
- Critical point temperature for both bosonic and fermionic 16-particle systems: 10 MeV.
- Extrapolated critical temperature for an infinite nuclear system: 15.8 MeV, obtained by multiplying the 10 MeV value by a factor of 1.58 from prior work (phd).

## Context

This work extends classical Monte Carlo simulations of Lennard-Jones fluids (phd, ggnhm) to quantum systems, addressing the question of how quantum statistics (bosonic vs. fermionic) affects the liquid-gas phase diagram of nuclear-like systems. The paper builds on prior studies of nuclear liquid-gas phase transitions (siemens), Coulomb effects (lee-mek, prl), finite-size effects in Hartree-Fock theories (jaqaman, satpathy), and cluster-type statistical multifragmentation models such as SMM, MMMC, and MMM (smm, mmmc, mmm). The finding that bosonic and fermionic systems share a common critical point at 10 MeV, with quantum effects vanishing above it, suggests that for highly excited nuclear systems above the critical temperature, quantum statistics may be neglected, enabling simpler classical treatments.
