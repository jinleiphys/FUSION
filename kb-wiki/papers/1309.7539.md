# The Astrophysical S-factor of the 12C(α,γ)16O Reaction at Solar Energies

> The astrophysical S-factor of the 4He-12C radiative capture is calculated in the potential model at the energy range 0.1-2.0 MeV. Radiative capture 12C(α,γ)16O is extremely relevant for the fate of massive stars and determines if the remnant of a supernova explosion becomes a black hole or a neutron star. Because this reaction occurs at low-energies the experimental measurements is very difficult and perhaps impossible. In this paper, radiative capture of the 12C(α,γ)16O reaction at very low-energies is taken as a case study. In comparison with other theoretical methods and available experimental data, excellent agreement is achieved for the astrophysical S-factor of this process.

## Key claim

The authors calculate the astrophysical S-factor for the \(^{12}\text{C}(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}\text{O}\) reaction using a potential model with Woods-Saxon potentials, finding that their result for the E2 transition at 300 keV (84.97 keV·b) agrees reasonably with several experimental evaluations, though they note that a cluster-model calculation by Dubovichenko et al. gives a much lower value of 16.0 keV·b.

## Method

The authors solve the radial Schrödinger equation for bound and continuum states of the \(^{12}\text{C}+\alpha\) system using Woods-Saxon central and spin-orbit potentials plus a Coulomb potential, with parameters adjusted to reproduce the ground-state binding energy of \(^{16}\text{O}\). They then compute the astrophysical S-factor for the E2 radiative capture transition using the RADCAP computer code, which evaluates electric multipole matrix elements from the radial wavefunctions. The calculation covers the center-of-mass energy range 0.1–2.0 MeV.

## Key numbers

* Astrophysical S-factor for the E2 transition at \(E_{\text{cm}} = 300\) keV: **84.97 keV·b** (this work, 2013).
* For comparison, experimental evaluations at 300 keV: Schürmann et al. (2012) – 73.4 keV·b; Oulebsir et al. (2012) – 50 ± 19 keV·b; Hammer et al. (2005) – 81 ± 22 keV·b; Kunz et al. (2001) – 85 ± 30 keV·b; Redder et al. (1987) – 80 ± 25 keV·b.
* Cluster-model calculation by Dubovichenko et al. gives S(300) = 16.0 keV·b.
* Woods-Saxon potential parameters used: \(V_0 = -51.8\) MeV, \(R_0 = 2.41\) fm, \(a_0 = 0.644\) fm, \(V_{S0} = 39.54\) MeV, \(R_{S0} = 2.291\) fm, \(a_{S0} = 0.644\) fm, \(R_C = 2.41\) fm.

## Context

This work extends previous potential-model calculations of the \(^{12}\text{C}(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}\text{O}\) reaction, which is critical for determining the carbon-to-oxygen ratio in stellar nucleosynthesis and thus the fate of massive stars. The authors compare their result with experimental data from Tatischeff, Redder, Dyer, and Tornow, and with evaluations by Schürmann (2012), Oulebsir (2012), Hammer (2005), Kunz (2001), and Redder (1987). They also contrast their approach with the cluster-model calculation of Dubovichenko et al., which yields a significantly lower S-factor. The calculation enables improved extrapolation of the S-factor to solar energies where direct measurement is extremely difficult.
