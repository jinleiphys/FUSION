# Perspective on Tsallis Statistics for Nuclear and Particle Physics

> This is a concise introduction to the topic of nonextensive Tsallis statistics meant especially for those interested in its relation to high energy proton-proton, proton-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus collisions. The three types of Tsallis statistics are reviewed. Only one of them is consistent with the fundamental hypothesis of equilibrium statistical mechanics. The single particle distributions associated with it, namely Boltzmann, Fermi-Dirac, and Bose-Einstein, are derived. These are not equilibrium solutions to the conventional Boltzmann transport equation which must be modified in a rather nonintuitive manner for them to be so. Nevertheless the Boltzmann limit of the Tsallis distribution is extremely efficient in representing a wide variety of single particle distributions in high energy proton-proton, proton-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus collisions with only three parameters, one of them being the so-called nonextensitivity parameter $q$. This distribution interpolates between an exponential at low transverse energy, reflecting thermal equilibrium, to a power law at high transverse energy, reflecting the asymptotic freedom of QCD. It should not be viewed as a fundamental new parameter representing nonextensive behavior in these collisions.

## Key claim

The Tsallis distribution is an efficient phenomenological parametrization of single-particle spectra in high-energy collisions, but the parameter \(q\) should not be interpreted as evidence of fundamental nonextensive behavior; it merely controls the transition from exponential (thermal) to power-law (QCD asymptotic freedom) behavior and encapsulates complicated physics that has not been derived from QCD.

## Method

The paper reviews the three types of Tsallis statistics (Types I, II, III) and tests each against the fundamental hypothesis of equilibrium statistical mechanics using the microcanonical ensemble. Only Type III, which defines operator averages as \(\langle \mathcal{O} \rangle = \sum_i p_i^q \mathcal{O}_i / \sum_j p_j^q\), is consistent with equal a priori probabilities. The single-particle distributions for fermions, bosons, and classical particles are derived from Type III, and their consistency with the Boltzmann transport equation and a Fokker-Planck equation is examined. The phenomenological success of the classical Boltzmann limit of the Tsallis distribution in fitting experimental data from proton-proton, proton-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus collisions is then summarized.

## Key numbers

- The Tsallis distribution fits charged hadron spectra over up to **14 orders of magnitude** in yield (Wong 2015).
- The parameter \(q\) must be adjusted to **4 significant figures**, ranging from **1.109** for ATLAS at 0.9 TeV to **1.151** for CMS at 7 TeV in proton-proton collisions.
- These \(q\) values correspond to power-law exponents \(1/(q-1)\) of **9.21** and **6.60**, respectively.
- Dimensional analysis of parton scattering predicts \(n=4\) and therefore \(q=4/3\) if \(n = q/(q-1)\), but realistic parton distribution functions increase \(n\) significantly, making \(q\) much closer to 1 for hadrons; jets have \(n \sim 4.5\) to **5.5** (Wong 2015, Wong 2013).

## Context

This perspective extends the work of Tsallis (1988) on nonextensive entropy and the three types of statistics proposed by Tsallis (1998). It directly addresses the widespread use of Tsallis-like distributions in high-energy phenomenology by experiments such as STAR (2007), PHENIX (2011), ALICE (2011), and CMS (2012), and by phenomenological studies such as Wong (2015) and Zheng (2015). The paper clarifies that only Type III is physically consistent with equilibrium statistical mechanics, and it cautions against interpreting the fitted \(q\) parameter as evidence of nonextensivity, instead recommending that the power-law exponent \(n\) be reported as more physically revealing. The work enables a more critical evaluation of Tsallis fits and points to the need for a genuine QCD derivation of the observed spectral shapes.
