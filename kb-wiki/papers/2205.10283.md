# Relativistic mean-field theories for neutron-star physics based on chiral effective field theory

> We describe and implement a procedure for determining the couplings of a Relativistic Mean-Field Theory (RMFT) that is optimized for application to neutron star phenomenology. In the standard RMFT approach, the couplings are constrained by comparing the theory's predictions for symmetric matter at saturation density with measured nuclear properties. The theory is then applied to neutron stars which consist of neutron-rich matter at densities ranging up to several times saturation density, which allows for additional astrophysical constraints. In our approach, rather than using the RMFT to extrapolate from symmetric to neutron-rich matter and from finite-sized nuclei to uniform matter, we fit the RMFT to properties of uniform pure neutron matter obtained from chiral effective field theory. Chiral effective field theory incorporates the experimental data for nuclei in the framework of a controlled expansion for nuclear forces valid at nuclear densities and enables us to account for theoretical uncertainties when fitting the RMFT. We construct four simple RMFTs that span the uncertainties provided by chiral effective field theory for neutron matter, and are consistent with current astrophysical constraints on the equation of state. Our RMFTs can be used to model the properties of neutron-rich matter across the vast range of densities and temperatures encountered in neutron stars and their mergers.

## Key claim

The authors construct Relativistic Mean-Field Theories (RMFTs) for neutron-star physics by fitting their couplings to chiral effective field theory (chiEFT) predictions for pure neutron matter, rather than to symmetric nuclear matter data alone, and they show that the resulting models can span the chiEFT uncertainty band while remaining consistent with astrophysical constraints on the equation of state.

## Method

The authors fit six RMFT coupling constants (g_sigma, g_omega, g_rho, b, c, b_1) to chiEFT calculations of zero-temperature pure neutron matter from Tews et al. (2018) over the density range 0.5 to 2 times nuclear saturation density, using the Gandolfi-Carlson-Reddy parametrization to sample the chiEFT uncertainty band. They simultaneously fit to standard symmetric nuclear matter properties (saturation density 0.16 fm^-3, binding energy -16 MeV, incompressibility 240 +/- 20 MeV) and then screen the resulting RMFTs against the astrophysical constraint that the maximum neutron star mass must be consistent at the 2-sigma level with the heaviest observed neutron star (2.072 +/- 0.066 solar masses). The crust is attached by matching to the GPPVA(TM1e) crustal equation of state at a transition density near 0.4 times saturation density.

## Key numbers

- The four selected RMFTs (QMC-RMF1 through QMC-RMF4) have saturation densities between 0.157 and 0.162 fm^-3, binding energies between -16.3 and -16.1 MeV, and incompressibilities between 230 and 279 MeV.
- Maximum neutron star masses for the four models range from 1.95 to 2.21 solar masses.
- The chiEFT neutron matter data used for fitting covers the density range 0.5 to 2 times saturation density (n_0 = 0.16 fm^-3).
- The crust matching occurs at a baryon density of approximately 0.4 times saturation density (0.064 fm^-3).
- The chiEFT calculations employed local interactions at next-to-next-to-leading order (N^2LO) with a breakdown scale in the range 500-600 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Hornick et al. (2018), who checked RMFT parametrizations for agreement with chiEFT for neutron matter, by directly fitting RMFT couplings to chiEFT predictions rather than to symmetric nuclear matter data. The authors note that commonly used RMFTs such as GM1, NL3, IU-FSU, and SFHo show disagreement with chiEFT at densities below 1.5 times saturation density. The resulting RMFTs are intended for use in neutron star phenomenology including r-mode spindown, neutron star cooling, and merger simulations, and are made publicly available on the CompOSE website and in a GitLab repository.
