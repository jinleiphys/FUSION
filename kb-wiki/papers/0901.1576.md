# Kinetics vs hydrodynamics: generalization of Landau/Cooper-Frye prescription for freeze-out

> The problem of spectra formation in hydrodynamic approach to A+A collisions is considered within the Boltzmann equations. It is shown analytically and illustrated by numerical calculations that the particle momentum spectra can be presented in the Cooper-Frye form despite freeze-out is not sharp and has the finite temporal width. The latter is equal to the inverse of the particle collision rate at points $(t_σ({\bf r},p),{\bf r})$ of the maximal emission at a fixed momentum $p$. The set of these points forms the hypersurfaces $t_σ({\bf r},p)$ which strongly depend on the values of $p$ and typically do not enclose completely the initially dense matter. This is an important difference from the standard Cooper-Frye prescription (CFp), with a common freeze-out hypersurface for all $p$, that affects significantly the predicted spectra. Also, the well known problem of CFp as for negative contributions to the spectra from non-space-like parts of the freeze-out hypersurface is naturally eliminated in this improved prescription.

## Key claim

The Cooper-Frye prescription for freeze-out can be generalized to describe continuous particle emission with a finite temporal width, provided the freeze-out hypersurface is allowed to depend on particle momentum; the standard approach using a common hypersurface for all momenta is too rough and leads to negative contributions from non-space-like parts.

## Method

The authors start from the Boltzmann equation and rewrite it in integral form using the escape probability for a particle to leave the system without collisions. They apply a generalized relaxation time approximation where the local equilibrium distribution function is used to compute gain and loss terms, then use the saddle point method to evaluate the momentum spectrum integral. The saddle point condition defines a momentum-dependent freeze-out hypersurface \(t_\sigma({\bf r},p)\) where the emission function has its maximum, and the temporal width of emission is shown to equal the inverse of the collision rate at that point.

## Key numbers

- The temporal width of the emission zone at the point of maximum is \(D = \frac{e}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \frac{1}{R} \approx \tau_{\rm rel}\), where \(R\) is the collision rate.
- For a specific numerical example at \(\tau = 16.5\) fm/c, \(r = 0\), \(p_T = 0.2\) GeV, \(p_L = 0\): the collision rate is \(R \approx 0.217\) c/fm, giving a theoretical width \(D \approx 5.00\) fm, which agrees with the hydro-kinetic model (HKM) numerical result \(D_{HKM} \approx 4.95\) fm.
- The maximal emission regions for different \(p_T\) are crossed by isotherms with temperatures ranging from 80 MeV (low momenta) to 135 MeV (high momenta).

## Context

This work extends the hydro-kinetic approach developed in Sinyukov et al. (2008) (PRC) and the idea of duality between continuous emission and Cooper-Frye form proposed in earlier work (dual). It addresses the well-known problem of negative contributions to spectra from non-space-like parts of the freeze-out hypersurface, which was previously handled by ad hoc prescriptions such as those in Sinyukov (1998) (Sin4) and Bugaev (2005). The generalized prescription enables more accurate modeling of freeze-out in relativistic heavy-ion collisions by accounting for the momentum dependence of the decoupling hypersurface, and it resolves the conflict between the sudden freeze-out assumption and the gradual nature of particle liberation.
