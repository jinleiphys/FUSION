# Fluctuations and Correlations from Microscopic Transport Theory

> The multiplicity fluctuations in A+A collisions at SPS and RHIC energies are studied within the HSD transport approach. We find a dominant role of the fluctuations in the nucleon participant number for the final fluctuations. In order to extract physical fluctuations one should decrease the fluctuations in the participants number. This can be done considering very central collisions. The system size dependence of the multiplicity fluctuations in central A+A collisions at the SPS energy range -- obtained in the HSD and UrQMD transport models -- is presented. The results can be used as a `background' for experimental measurements of fluctuations as a signal of the critical point. Event-by-event fluctuations of the $K/π$, $K/p$ and $p/π$ ratios in A+A collisions are also studied. Event-by-event fluctuations of the kaon to pion number ratio in nucleus-nucleus collisions are studied for SPS and RHIC energies. We find that the HSD model can qualitatively reproduce the measured excitation function for the $K/π$ ratio fluctuations in central Au+Au (or Pb+Pb) collisions from low SPS up to top RHIC energies. The forward-backward correlation coefficient measured by the STAR Collaboration in Au+Au collisions at RHIC is also studied. We discuss the effects of initial collision geometry and centrality bin definition on correlations in nucleus-nucleus collisions. We argue that a study of the dependence of correlations on the centrality bin definition as well as the bin size may distinguish between these `trivial' correlations and correlations arising from `new physics'.

## Key claim

The HSD transport model can qualitatively reproduce the measured excitation function for K/π ratio fluctuations in central A+A collisions from low SPS up to top RHIC energies, but only if the dominant fluctuations from the nucleon participant number are suppressed by selecting very central collisions; the model overestimates preliminary STAR data for the proton to pion ratio at RHIC energies.

## Method

The authors performed event-by-event simulations of A+A collisions using the Hadron-String-Dynamics (HSD) transport model, with some comparisons to the Ultra-Relativistic-Quantum-Molecular-Dynamics (UrQMD) model. They calculated the scaled variance ω for multiplicity fluctuations and the dynamical fluctuation measure σ_dyn for K/π, K/p, and p/π ratios, applying experimental acceptances from the NA49 and STAR Collaborations. For forward-backward correlations, they computed the correlation coefficient ρ as a function of pseudorapidity gap η_gap for different centrality bin definitions and bin sizes.

## Key numbers

- Scaled variance ω_P^{targ} for target participant fluctuations in In+In collisions at E_lab=158 AGeV reaches values above 0.8 for semi-peripheral collisions with N_P^{proj} around 20, decreasing to near zero for the most central collisions
- HSD results for σ_dyn^{Kπ} in central Pb+Pb collisions at SPS energies (E_lab=20-158 AGeV) range from approximately -0.5×10^{-3} to 1.5×10^{-3}, with UrQMD giving values about 40% smaller at the lowest SPS energy
- At 160 AGeV, switching from NA49 to STAR acceptance causes a jump in σ_dyn^{Kp} of 3%
- Forward-backward correlation coefficient ρ for 10% centrality classes at √s=200 GeV in Au+Au collisions shows values up to approximately 0.6 for small η_gap, decreasing to about 0.4 for η_gap=1.5

## Context

This work extends transport model studies of fluctuations by Konchakovski et al. (2006) and provides a hadronic baseline for the NA61/SHINE experimental program searching for the QCD critical point at SPS energies (Gazdzicki, 2006). The results contradict statistical model predictions (Gorenstein et al., 2007) which show different energy dependence for scaled variance versus dynamical fluctuations. The forward-backward correlation analysis demonstrates that geometrical fluctuations from centrality bin definition can mimic signals that might otherwise be attributed to new physics, as previously discussed by the STAR Collaboration (2007). The findings enable experimentalists to subtract trivial hadronic backgrounds when searching for critical point signals in fluctuation measurements.
