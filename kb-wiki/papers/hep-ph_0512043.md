# Stressed pairing in conventional color superconductors is unavoidable

> At sufficiently high densities, cold dense three-flavor quark matter is in the color-flavor locked (CFL) phase, in which all nine quarks pair in a particularly symmetric fashion. Once the heaviness of the strange quark (mass $m_s$) and the requirements of electric and color neutrality are taken into account, the CFL pattern of color superconductivity requires the pairing of quarks that would, in the absence of pairing, have Fermi momenta that differ by of order $m_s^2/μ$, with $μ$ the quark number chemical potential. This means that at sufficiently small $μ$, the ``stress'' on the pairing is large enough that the system can lower its energy by breaking pairs, resulting in some unconventional color superconductor which includes gapless excitations, spatial inhomogeneity, counter-propagating currents, or all three. In this paper we ask whether there is some less symmetric but still conventional pattern of pairing that can evade the stress. In other words, is there a pattern of pairing in which, once electric and color neutrality are imposed by suitable chemical potentials, pairing only occurs among those quarks whose Fermi momenta would be equal in the absence of pairing? We use graph-theoretical methods to classify 511 patterns of conventional color superconducting pairing, and show that none of them meet this requirement. All feel a stress, and all can be expected to become unstable to gapless modes at a density comparable to that at which the CFL phase becomes unstable.

## Key claim

The authors show that, among all 511 possible patterns of conventional BCS pairing in three-flavor quark matter that are antisymmetric in both color and flavor, none can avoid "stressed pairing", meaning that in every case, once electric and color neutrality are imposed, the quarks that pair have different effective chemical potentials, so the pairing inevitably incurs a free energy cost proportional to \(m_s^4\) that will destabilize the phase at sufficiently low density.

## Method

The authors use graph-theoretical methods to classify all 511 patterns of conventional color-superconducting pairing that are antisymmetric in color and flavor and have total angular momentum \(J=0\). For each pattern, they impose electric and color neutrality via three chemical potentials \(\mu_e\), \(\mu_3\), and \(\mu_8\), and then check whether the effective chemical potentials of paired quarks can be made equal (the condition for stress-free pairing). They expand the free energy to leading order in \(m_s^2/\mu\) and the charge chemical potentials, and derive the neutrality conditions in the form \(\sum_i \nu_i q_{ik}=0\), where \(\nu_i\) is the common Fermi momentum of the component containing quark \(i\) and \(q_{ik}\) are the charge assignments.

## Key numbers

* The splitting between effective chemical potentials of paired quarks in the CFL phase is of order \(m_s^2/\mu\), where \(m_s\) is the strange quark mass and \(\mu\) is the quark number chemical potential.
* The CFL phase becomes unstable when \(m_s^2/\mu > 2\Delta\), where \(\Delta\) is the gap parameter (estimated to be 10–100 MeV).
* The authors classify 511 distinct patterns of conventional color-superconducting pairing.
* The free energy cost of stressed pairing is of order \((m_s^2/\mu)^2 \mu^2 = m_s^4\), while the free energy benefit of pairing is of order \(\Delta^2 \mu^2\).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the CFL phase and its instability at lower densities, previously studied by Alford, Rajagopal, and Wilczek (1998) and by Alford, Kouvaris, and Rajagopal (2003–2005). The gapless CFL (gCFL) phase, proposed by Alford, Kouvaris, and Rajagopal (2003), is known to be unstable toward counter-propagating currents or spatial inhomogeneity, as shown by Huang, Shovkovy, and others (2004) and by Casalbuoni, Gatto, and others (2004). The present paper rules out the possibility that a less symmetric but still conventional BCS phase could evade this instability, forcing future work to focus on unconventional non-BCS phases such as crystalline color superconductors (Larkin-Ovchinnikov-Fulde-Ferrell type) or meson supercurrent phases (Kryjevski, Kaplan, and others, 2005).
