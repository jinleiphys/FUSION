# Configurational entropy as a probe of the stability condition of compact objects

> We systematically extend the statement that the configurational entropy provides an alternative approach to studying gravitational stability of compact objects, carried out in the previous work of M. Gleiser and N. Jiang, Phys. Rev. D {\bf 92}, 044046 (2015). Inspired by that paper, we try to answer the crucial question: Is there any one-to-one correspondence between the minimum of the configurational entropy and the stability point for each realistic equation of state? In view of the above question, we focus on neutron stars, quark stars, as well as on the third family of compact stars (hybrid stars), where a possible phase transition may lead to the existence of twin stars (stars with equal mass but different radius). In each case, we use a large set of equations of state investigating the possibility to find correlations between the stability region obtained from the traditional perturbation methods to the one obtained by the minimum of configurational entropy. We found that the suggested prediction of the stability by the minimization of the configurational entropy, concerning neutron stars and quark stars, does not have, at least quantitatively, universal validity. However, in several cases it qualitatively predicts the existence of the stability point. In conclusion, the configurational entropy, can be considered as an additional tool which enters into the quiver for studying the stability of compact objects such as for example neutron and quark stars, but taking into account that the accuracy of the predictions depends on the specific character of each equation of state.

## Key claim

The configurational entropy (CE) does not provide a universal quantitative predictor of gravitational stability for neutron stars and quark stars, contrary to earlier suggestions by Gleiser and Jiang (2015); the correlation between the minimum of the CE and the stability point depends strongly on the specific equation of state (EOS), and in many cases no minimum exists at the stability point.

## Method

The authors solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for a large set of hadronic EOSs, four interacting quark matter EOSs, and six hybrid EOSs producing twin stars (equal mass, different radius). For each stellar configuration, they computed the density profile ρ(r) from the TOV solution, performed a Fourier transform to obtain F(k), constructed the normalized modal fraction f̃(k) using the longest physical mode |k_min| = π/R, and integrated to obtain the configurational entropy S_C. They compared the central density at which S_C reaches a minimum with the stability point determined by the traditional method (TM), which identifies stability with dM/dε_c > 0 and the maximum mass configuration.

## Key numbers

- For hadronic EOSs, the minimum of the CE occurs at central densities far higher than those corresponding to the stability point; in many cases no minimum appears at all.
- For the free Fermi gas EOS, the CE minimum and the TM stability point nearly coincide, but this is the only exception among all hadronic EOSs tested.
- For twin stars (hybrid stars), the first stability point coincides with the first CE minimum with less than 2% error, but the authors attribute this to an artificial discontinuity in the EOS at the phase transition.
- For the second stability point in twin stars, the CE minimum appears at central densities two to three times larger than the TM stability point.
- For quark stars, using four EOSs with parameter λ̄ = (1, 2, 5, 16) and B_eff = 150 MeV fm⁻³, the CE minimum and TM stability point do not coincide.

## Context

This work systematically extends the approach of Gleiser and Jiang (2015), who found that CE minimization predicted critical masses for white dwarfs, boson stars, and the Fermi gas neutron star with a few percent accuracy. The present study tests that claim across a much wider range of realistic EOSs for neutron stars, quark stars, and hybrid twin stars, using the traditional stability condition dM/dε_c > 0 from Haensel (2007) as the reference. The results show that CE is only a qualitative tool whose accuracy depends on the specific EOS, and the authors suggest future work on rapidly rotating or anisotropic stars to further investigate the correlation.
