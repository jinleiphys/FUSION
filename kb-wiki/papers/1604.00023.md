# Correlations and fluctuations of pions at the LHC

> The intriguing possibility of Bose-Einstein condensation of pions at the LHC is examined with the use of higher order moments of the multiplicity distribution. The scaled variance, skewness and kurtosis are calculated for the pion system. The obtained results show that the normalized kurtosis has a significant increase for the case of the pion condensation.

## Key claim
The normalized kurtosis of the pion multiplicity distribution shows a significant increase if a Bose-Einstein condensate of pions is present at the LHC, but this signal is calculated for primary pions only and a realistic estimate must also include resonance decays.

## Method
The author calculates higher-order moments (scaled variance, skewness, and kurtosis) of the pion multiplicity distribution analytically, using thermodynamic parameters obtained from previous fits to LHC data. The fluctuations are computed separately for the condensate level (p=0), non-condensate levels (p≠0), and the total system (p≥0) in the infinite volume limit, with the sum over momentum levels replaced by an integral except for the condensate level which is treated separately.

## Key numbers
- The experimentally measured pion spectra at the LHC are about 25-50% higher at low transverse momenta (p_T < 200 MeV) than predictions from existing models that worked well at RHIC.
- About 5% of pions are in the Bose-Einstein condensate according to the non-equilibrium parameters obtained in the author's previous analysis (Begun 2015).
- Fluctuations on the p=0 level are an order of magnitude larger than fluctuations on the p≠0 levels.
- For a Poisson distribution (random emission), ω = S·σ = κ·σ² = 1; for a Gaussian distribution, ω = 1 but S·σ = κ·σ² = 0.

## Context
This work extends the author's previous studies (Begun 2015, Begun 2014) on non-equilibrium scenarios for pion production at the LHC, where standard hadron gas models still show a three-sigma deviation for protons (Stachel 2013). The possibility of pion Bose-Einstein condensation was motivated by ALICE collaboration reports of large coherent pion emission from multi-pion correlation studies (ALICE-corr), and the observation that pion spectra are not sensitive enough to judge the existence of BEC. The next step would be to include resonance decays in a realistic estimate (Begun 2016) to determine whether the fluctuation signal can be detected experimentally.
