# Three Puzzles from Nuclear Astrophysics

> I discuss three open problems in astrophysics where nuclear physics can make important contributions: the solar abundance problem, dark matter particle detection, and the origin of the r-process elements.

# Three Puzzles from Nuclear Astrophysics

## Key claim
The paper argues that three seemingly distinct puzzles in astrophysics, the solar abundance problem, dark matter detection, and the r-process site, each have nuclear physics connections that may offer resolution pathways, but each remains an open problem with significant uncertainties. The solar abundance problem specifically may be resolved by planetary formation processes that deposited metal-depleted gas onto the Sun after it developed a radiative core, invalidating the Standard Solar Model assumption of zero-age homogeneity.

## Method
The paper reviews three separate astrophysical puzzles through the lens of nuclear physics contributions. For the solar abundance problem, the author compares high-metallicity (GS98-SFII) and low-metallicity (AGSS09-SFII) Standard Solar Models against helioseismic data and neutrino flux measurements, and discusses nonstandard solar models with accretion. For dark matter detection, the author presents a general Galilean-invariant effective field theory for WIMP-nucleon interactions with 11 couplings, deriving the corresponding nuclear response functions beyond the standard spin-independent/spin-dependent dichotomy. For the r-process, the author examines a neutrino-driven r-process in the helium shells of metal-poor core-collapse supernovae using the Kepler code and reaction networks, including neutrino oscillation effects.

## Key numbers
- The high-metallicity GS98-SFII SSM has Z_ini ~ 0.0169, while the low-metallicity AGSS09-SFII SSM has Z_ini ~ 0.0122, a reduction that makes the Sun more consistent with local neighborhood stars
- The high-metallicity GS98-SFII SSM 8B neutrino flux is ~22% higher than the low-metallicity AGSS09-SFII SSM flux
- The excess metal in the planets is similar to the net difference in GS98-SFII and AGSS09-SFII SSM convective-zone metal, about 40 Earth masses
- The CN neutrino fluxes are ~2.9 x 10^8 /cm^2/s for 13N and ~2.2 x 10^8 /cm^2/s for 15O
- The weighted ratio of 15O to 8B fluxes can determine core C+N abundance to ~13%, compared to the current debate over differences in Z_ini of >= 30%
- The dominant uncertainty in the flux ratio equation is 9.9% from nuclear physics, particularly the S-factors for 7Be(p,gamma) and 14N(p,gamma)
- The estimated rate of r-process events in the early galaxy is ~10^-2 per year, typical of core-collapse supernovae
- The neutrino-driven r-process in He shells works for metallicities of 10^-4 < Z < 10^-3 in Fe-core supernovae between 11-16 solar masses and for very massive progenitors of 49-75 solar masses
- Neutron abundances of ~10^19 /cm^3 were produced and maintained for long times in the He shell r-process scenario

## Context
This review extends the ongoing debate over the solar abundance problem between the GS98-SFII and AGSS09-SFII models (Serenelli 2009, Haxton and Serenelli 2008), and discusses nonstandard solar models with accretion (Serenelli, Haxton, and Pena-Garay 2011) that are constrained by helioseismic and neutrino measurements. For dark matter, the paper builds on the effective field theory approach of Fitzpatrick et al. (2012) to expand beyond the standard spin-independent/spin-dependent treatment, identifying six independent nuclear response functions rather than two. For the r-process, the paper revisits the neutrino-driven wind mechanism (Woosley 1992) and its difficulties (Roberts 2010), then presents an alternative early r-process in He shells of metal-poor supernovae (Banerjee et al.) that could complement neutron star mergers (Argast et al.) to explain both metal-poor halo star abundances and galactic chemical evolution. The work enables future measurements by SNO+ to directly compare solar core and surface compositions, and suggests that the six nuclear response functions for dark matter provide experimental dials for determining WIMP properties.
