# A further update on possible crises in nuclear-matter theory

> The ancient problem of the saturation of symmetric nuclear matter is reviewed with an update on the status of the crises that were identified at an early stage by John Clark. We discuss how the initial problem with variational calculations providing more binding than the two hole-line contribution for the same interaction was overcome by calculations including three hole-line contributions without however reproducing the empirical nuclear saturation properties. It is argued that this remaining problem is still open because many solutions have been proposed or ad hoc adjustments implemented without generating universal agreement on the proper interpretation of the physics. The problem of nuclear saturation therefore persists leading to the necessity of an analysis of the way the nuclear saturation properties are obtained from experimental data. We clarify the role of short-range correlations and review results for nuclear saturation when such ingredients are completely taken into account using the Green's function method. The role of long-range correlations is then analyzed with special emphasis on the importance of attractive pion-dominated excitation modes which inevitably lead to higher saturation densities than observed. Because such modes have no counterpart in finite nuclear systems, it is therefore argued that they should not be considered when calculating nuclear matter properties. The remaining open question is then whether long-range correlations in finite nuclei which in turn have no counterpart in infinite matter, represent the remaining missing ingredient in this analysis.

# A further update on possible crises in nuclear-matter theory

## Key claim

The paper argues that the long-standing nuclear saturation problem remains unresolved because long-range correlations associated with attractive pion-dominated excitation modes, which inevitably lead to higher saturation densities than observed in infinite nuclear matter, have no counterpart in finite nuclei and therefore should be excluded from nuclear-matter calculations; the remaining open question is whether long-range correlations in finite nuclei that have no counterpart in infinite matter represent the missing ingredient.

## Method

The paper reviews the history of the nuclear saturation problem, starting with the first crisis (disagreement between variational and lowest-order Brueckner calculations) resolved by three hole-line calculations, and the second crisis (failure to reproduce empirical saturation properties) which persists. The author analyzes results from self-consistent Green's function (SCGF) calculations that treat short-range correlations (SRC) fully, compares them with three hole-line results from Song (1998), and examines dispersive optical model (DOM) analyses of finite nuclei, particularly Ca-40, to constrain the role of SRC versus long-range correlations (LRC). The paper also evaluates the impact of chiral perturbation theory interactions and three-body forces.

## Key numbers

- Empirical saturation density: 0.16 nucleons per fm^3 (corresponding to k_F = 1.33 fm^-1) and binding energy of 16 MeV per particle
- DOM analysis of Ca-40 yields 10.6% of protons having momenta above 1.4 fm^-1
- DOM binding energy for Ca-40: 7.91 MeV per nucleon, compared to experimental 8.55 MeV per nucleon, leaving about 0.64 MeV per nucleon for higher-body interactions
- Kinetic energy contribution from DOM: 22.64 MeV per nucleon
- Three-body interaction expectation value from DOM analysis: repulsive 1.28 MeV per nucleon
- GFMC calculations for light nuclei require about 1.5 MeV per nucleon attractive three-body force
- SCGF calculation for AV18 interaction at saturation: about 4 MeV per nucleon short of the empirical 16 MeV per nucleon binding

## Context

This paper extends the analysis of nuclear saturation crises originally identified by John Clark (1979), building on the three hole-line calculations of Day (1978, 1981) and Song (1998), and the SCGF work of Dewulf (2003), Frick (2005), Rios (2009), and Baldo (2012). The author argues against the standard assumption that long-range correlations in finite nuclei and infinite matter are commensurate, specifically challenging the inclusion of pion-exchange ring diagrams that were calculated by Dickhoff (1982, 1984). The paper contrasts the AV18 interaction with soft chiral perturbation theory interactions, noting that chiral interactions fail to generate sufficient high-momentum nucleons, leading to too-small radii and overbinding in finite nuclei as reported by Soma (2014) and Hagen (2014). The work enables a re-evaluation of which correlations should connect infinite matter calculations to finite nuclei, and identifies the role of finite-nucleus phonon contributions (surface vibrations, giant resonances) as an open question.
