# Dissociation of quarkonium in hot QCD medium: Modification of the inter-quark potential

> We have studied the dissociation of heavy quarkonium states in a hot QCD medium by investigating the medium modifications to a heavy quark potential. Our model shows that in-medium modification causes the screening of the charge in contrast to the screening of the range of the potential. We have then employed the medium-modified potential to estimate the dissociation pattern of the charmonium and bottomonium states and also explore how the pattern changes as we go from the perturbative to nonperturbative domain in the Debye mass. The results are in good agreement with the other current theoretical works both from the spectral function analysis and the potential model study.

## Key claim

The paper claims that in-medium modification of the heavy quark potential leads to screening of the charge (rather than screening of the range of the potential), producing a long-range Coulomb tail. The dissociation temperatures obtained depend strongly on the choice of Debye mass, and the results agree with other potential model studies only when the perturbative leading-order Debye mass is used, while nonperturbative corrections yield unrealistically low dissociation temperatures.

## Method

The authors start from the Cornell potential (Coulomb plus linear string term) and introduce medium modifications via a dielectric permittivity in Fourier space, using the static limit of the gluon self-energy. They compute the inverse Fourier transform to obtain a real-space potential that contains both a Yukawa term and a long-range Coulombic tail with an effective charge proportional to \(2\sigma/m_D^2\). Using this potential, they solve the Schrödinger equation (analytically for the asymptotic Coulomb form, numerically for the full potential) to obtain binding energies for charmonium and bottomonium states. Dissociation temperatures are determined by the condition that the binding energy equals the average thermal energy (either \(3T\) for a lower bound or \(T\) for an upper bound), using three different forms of the Debye mass: leading-order perturbative (\(m_D^{\rm LO}\)), nonperturbative (\(m_D^{\rm NP}\)), and lattice-parametrized (\(m_D^L\)).

## Key numbers

- String tension \(\sigma = 0.184\ {\rm GeV}^2\) used throughout.
- Critical temperatures: \(T_c = 270\ {\rm MeV}\) for pure gauge QCD, \(203\ {\rm MeV}\) for two-flavor QCD, and \(197\ {\rm MeV}\) for three-flavor QCD.
- Using \(m_D^{\rm LO}\) in pure QCD: \(J/\psi\) dissociates at \(1.1\ T_c\) (lower bound) to \(1.4\ T_c\) (upper bound); \(\Upsilon\) dissociates at \(1.4\ T_c\) to \(1.8\ T_c\); \(\psi'\) dissociates at \(0.8\ T_c\) to \(1.0\ T_c\); \(\Upsilon'\) dissociates at \(1.0\ T_c\) to \(1.3\ T_c\).
- Using \(m_D^{\rm LO}\) in three-flavor QCD: \(J/\psi\) dissociates at \(1.2\ T_c\) to \(1.6\ T_c\); \(\Upsilon\) dissociates at \(1.6\ T_c\) to \(2.0\ T_c\).
- Using the lattice-parametrized Debye mass \(m_D^L\) in pure QCD: \(J/\psi\) dissociates at \(0.8\ T_c\) to \(1.0\ T_c\); \(\Upsilon\) dissociates at \(1.0\ T_c\) to \(1.3\ T_c\).
- Using the full potential (including finite-range terms) with \(m_D^{\rm LO}\) increases dissociation temperatures by approximately 10-20% compared to the asymptotic Coulomb approximation.
- For three-flavor QCD with \(T_c = 192\ {\rm MeV}\) and \(m_D^L\): upper bound dissociation temperatures are \(\psi' \le 0.9\ T_c\), \(J/\psi = 1.2\ T_c\), \(\Upsilon' = 1.1\ T_c\), \(\Upsilon = 1.6\ T_c\).

## Context

This work extends the potential model approach to quarkonium dissociation by modifying the full Cornell potential (not just its Coulomb part) with a dielectric function, in contrast to earlier studies that only screened the Coulomb term. The results are compared with the spectral function analyses of Asakawa (2004), Datta (2004), Umeda (2005), and Iida (2006), and with the potential model studies of Mocsy and Petreczky (2007, 2008) and Alberico et al. (2007, 2008). The paper finds good quantitative agreement with Mocsy and Petreczky (2007) when using the perturbative Debye mass, but the nonperturbative Debye mass from Kajantie et al. (2003) gives dissociation temperatures too low to match lattice spectral function results, leaving open the question of how to reconcile potential model and lattice approaches.
