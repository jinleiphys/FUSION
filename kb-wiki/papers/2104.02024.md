# Quantum algorithms for transport coefficients in gauge theories

> In the future, ab initio quantum simulations of heavy ion collisions may become possible with large-scale fault-tolerant quantum computers. We propose a quantum algorithm for studying these collisions by looking at a class of observables requiring dramatically smaller volumes: transport coefficients. These form nonperturbative inputs into theoretical models of heavy ions; thus, their calculation reduces theoretical uncertainties without the need for a full-scale simulation of the collision. We derive the necessary lattice operators in the Hamiltonian formulation and describe how to obtain them on quantum computers. Additionally, we discuss ways to efficiently prepare the relevant thermal state of a gauge theory.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that transport coefficients of gauge theories (such as shear and bulk viscosity of the quark-gluon plasma) can be computed on quantum computers using dramatically smaller lattice volumes than full heavy-ion collision simulations, requiring only L ~ 1 fm boxes rather than the ~10 fm needed for full scattering calculations, but this advantage depends on the validity of the hydrodynamic factorization and the ability to prepare thermal states at temperatures ~200 MeV.

## Method

The authors derive lattice operators for the energy-momentum tensor Tμν in the Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian formulation of gauge theories, providing both naive O(a) and clover-improved O(a²) discretizations. They describe how to extract transport coefficients from quantum simulations using either the transverse current autocorrelation function (TCAF) method, where a shear wave decays exponentially with a rate proportional to viscosity, or the Green-Kubo relation, which requires the correct normalization of the stress tensor operator. The paper also discusses methods for preparing thermal states of gauge theories on quantum computers, noting that high-temperature states near 200 MeV are markedly easier to prepare than low-temperature states.

## Key numbers

- Required lattice volume for transport coefficient calculations: L ~ 1 fm, compared to ~10 fm needed for full heavy-ion collision simulations
- Required lattice spacing: a ~ 0.1 fm for studying matter at ~200 MeV
- Estimated qubit cost reduction factor of ~10³ compared to full scattering simulations, based on the hadronic tensor decomposition example from Lamm (2019)
- Thermalization timescale suggested by experimental data and theoretical models: ~1 fm
- Current theoretical uncertainties on QGP transport coefficients: O(1)
- Shear viscosity extracted from TCAF method via exponential decay: e^(-η/(ρ k²) t)
- Green-Kubo relation for shear viscosity: η = (V/T) ∫₀^∞ ⟨T₁₂(t) T₁₂(0)⟩ dt
- Green-Kubo relation for bulk viscosity: ζ = (V/(9T)) ∫₀^∞ ⟨T_ii(t) T_jj(0)⟩ dt

## Context

This work extends the approach of Lamm (2019) for decomposing scattering simulations into nonperturbative inputs convolved with perturbative expressions, applying it to heavy-ion collisions via transport coefficients rather than full collision dynamics. It addresses the limitations of Euclidean lattice field theory, which suffers from sign problems at finite density and ill-posed analytic continuation for Minkowski observables as discussed by deForcrand (2010) and Tripolt (2018). The paper enables future quantum simulations of hydrodynamic properties of gauge theories without requiring the ~10⁶ qubits and ~10² time steps estimated for full heavy-ion collision simulations by de Jong (2020), instead targeting the transport coefficients that serve as nonperturbative inputs into hydrodynamic models of the quark-gluon plasma used at LHC and RHIC.
