# Hadron Structure Studied with the Electromagnetic Probe - from Giant Resonances to Meson Production

> The development of theoretical photonuclear physics over the last 40 years is illustrated by a few selected examples highlighting a number of important issues like collective motion in nuclei, the role of subnuclear degrees of freedom, relativity and meson production.

# Hadron Structure Studied with the Electromagnetic Probe – from Giant Resonances to Meson Production

## Key claim

The paper argues that theoretical photonuclear physics over the past 40 years has achieved deep insights into nuclear structure through selected examples, but the abstract's claim of illustrating "a number of important issues" glosses over the fact that the selection is explicitly governed by the scientific work of Michael Danos and the author's own research interests, and is not a comprehensive review.

## Method

The author presents a historical and pedagogical overview of theoretical developments in photonuclear physics, organized around four themes: the dynamic collective model of giant resonances (coupling rotation-vibration degrees of freedom with giant resonance degrees of freedom), subnuclear degrees of freedom (isobar configurations and meson exchange currents), relativistic effects in the Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule for the deuteron, and eta meson photoproduction on the deuteron. Each theme is illustrated by comparing theoretical calculations with experimental data from specific nuclei and reactions.

## Key numbers

- The deuteron's anomalous magnetic moment is κ_d = -0.143, yielding a GDH sum rule prediction of I^{GDH}_d = 0.65 μb, more than two orders of magnitude smaller than the nucleon values.
- The sum of proton and neutron GDH contributions is I^{GDH}_p(∞) + I^{GDH}_n(∞) = 438 μb.
- The photodisintegration channel contributes -413 μb to the deuteron GDH integral at about 500 MeV convergence; without relativistic contributions this would be -619 μb.
- The ΔΔ-component probability in the deuteron is P_{ΔΔ} = 0.97%.

## Context

This paper extends the line of work initiated by Danos (1958) on the splitting of the giant dipole resonance in deformed nuclei and the dynamic collective model developed by Danos and Greiner (1964). It also builds on the nuclear isobar configuration approach developed by Arenhövel, Danos, and Williams (1970) and the meson exchange current framework. The paper enables future work on short-range nuclear structure, relativistic effects in nuclear wave functions, subnuclear degrees of freedom in the context of QCD, polarization observables, and meson production on light nuclei.
