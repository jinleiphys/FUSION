# The Inner Crust and its Structure

> In this chapter we discuss some possible physical pictures that describe the constitution of the inner crust of compact objects. Different relativistic models both with constant couplings and density dependent ones are used. We calculate the liquid-gas phase transition in asymmetric nuclear matter from the thermodynamic and dynamic instabilities. The equations of state used to describe the crust are related to the crust-core transition properties. Cold and warm pasta phases with and without alpha particles are constructed. The influence of the pasta phase and its internal structure on the diffusion coefficients associated with Boltzman transport equations used to simulate the evolution of protoneutron stars are shown. Finally, the possible existence of bare quark stars and the effects of strong magnetic fields on quark matter are considered. Open questions are pointed out.

## Key claim

This chapter reviews multiple relativistic mean-field (RMF) models for the inner crust of compact stars, showing that the crust-core transition density and the extent of the pasta phase depend strongly on the chosen model and on whether thermodynamic or dynamic stability criteria are used; the authors emphasize that the simpler thermodynamic spinodal gives a good estimate for cold beta-equilibrium matter but overestimates the transition density compared to the dynamical spinodal and self-consistent pasta calculations.

## Method

The authors calculate the liquid-gas phase transition in asymmetric nuclear matter using both thermodynamic (binodal and spinodal surfaces) and dynamic (Vlasov formalism) instability analyses within several RMF models with constant couplings (NL3 parameter set) and density-dependent couplings (TW parameter set). They construct cold and warm pasta phases (droplets, rods, slabs, tubes, bubbles) using two methods: a coexistence-phase (CP) method based on Gibbs conditions with a parametrized surface energy, and a self-consistent Thomas-Fermi (TF) approximation. The influence of alpha particles is included by adding a Lagrangian density for alpha clusters with a coupling to the omega meson to mimic excluded volume effects. Diffusion coefficients for neutrino transport are computed from the pasta-phase structure using cross sections for neutral-current scattering and charged-current absorption reactions.

## Key numbers

- The crust thickness estimated from Haensel (2007) ranges from 1.01 km to 0.29 km depending on the equation of state and stellar mass.
- For the TW model, the symmetry energy is 32.76 MeV and its slope L is 55.30 MeV; for NL3, the symmetry energy is 37.34 MeV and L is 118.30 MeV.
- The pasta phase appears at densities on the order of 0.001 to 0.1 fm^{-3} in neutral nuclear matter and at a smaller density range in beta-equilibrium stellar matter.
- The alpha particle binding energy used is B_alpha = 28.3 MeV.
- Diffusion coefficients D_2, D_3, and D_4 are calculated for baryon densities above 0.005 fm^{-3} and temperatures from 0 to 50 MeV; for D_2 at T=5 MeV and Y_L=0.4, the homogeneous and pasta-phase curves cross at rho = 0.12 fm^{-3}.
- The kink in pasta-phase diffusion coefficients occurs at densities between 0.01 and 0.015 fm^{-3}.

## Context

This chapter extends prior work on the inner crust of neutron stars by Pethick (1995) and Link (1999) relating transition pressure and density to crust mass and moment of inertia, and by Muller (1995) on the liquid-gas phase transition. It builds on earlier pasta-phase calculations by Watanabe (2008) using quantum molecular dynamics and by Newton (2009) using 3D Skyrme-Hartree-Fock at finite temperature. The results enable improved modeling of protoneutron star evolution during the Kelvin-Helmholtz phase by providing diffusion coefficients that account for the pasta-phase structure, and they identify open questions regarding the role of light clusters beyond alpha particles and the need for better experimental constraints on the symmetry energy slope L.
