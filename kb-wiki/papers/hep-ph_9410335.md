# Spin Dependent Structure Functions of Nucleons and Nuclei

> We review recent progress in the understanding of the spin structure of the nucleon. For the free nucleon the issues addressed include the status of the Bjorken and Ellis-Jaffe sum-rules and the role of the axial anomaly. We outline recent work connecting the quark models familiar from hadron spectroscopy to the spin and flavour dependence of the parton distributions. Finally we review the current understanding of nuclear spin structure functions and particularly the extraction of the neutron spin structure function from deuteron data.

# Spin Dependent Structure Functions of Nucleons and Nuclei

## Key claim
After applying a correction for non-perturbative QCD effects at low $Q^2$, all experiments are consistent with the fraction of nucleon spin carried by quarks being $\Sigma \sim 42 \pm 14\%$, rather than the value near zero that originally motivated the term "spin crisis." The Bjorken sum-rule has been verified at the 10% level (at $1\sigma$), but the Ellis-Jaffe sum-rule still shows a roughly $1\sigma$ deviation after these corrections.

## Method
The paper reviews experimental measurements of the spin-dependent structure function $g_{1N}$ from the EMC, SMC, and SLAC E142/E143 collaborations, comparing them to the Bjorken and Ellis-Jaffe sum-rules. It then presents a theoretical framework connecting quark model wave functions (using the MIT bag model with a two-quark intermediate state) to the shape and flavour dependence of parton distributions, including the Close-Thomas mechanism for the $x \to 1$ limit. For nuclear targets, the author compares a full off-shell calculation of the deuteron spin structure function (using a covariant $D \to NN$ vertex and phenomenological $N \to q$ (di-quark) vertices) against the simpler convolution approximation and constant depolarisation factor.

## Key numbers
- Bjorken sum-rule prediction at $Q^2 = 10$ GeV$^2$: $S^p - S^n = 0.185$
- Ellis-Jaffe sum-rule predictions at $10$ GeV$^2$ ($3$ GeV$^2$): $S_{EJ}^p = 0.171 \pm 0.004$ ($0.161 \pm 0.004$), $S_{EJ}^n = -0.014 \pm 0.004$ ($-0.010 \pm 0.004$)
- Original EMC measurement: $S^p = 0.126 \pm 0.010 \pm 0.015$ at $10.7$ GeV$^2$, giving $\Sigma = 17\% \pm 9\% \pm 14\%$
- SMC proton result: $S^p = 0.136 \pm 0.011 \pm 0.011$ at $10$ GeV$^2$; combined with earlier data: $S^p = 0.145 \pm 0.008 \pm 0.011$
- SLAC E143 proton result: $S^p = 0.129 \pm 0.004 \pm 0.010$ at $3$ GeV$^2$; deuteron: $0.043 \pm 0.004 \pm 0.004$; neutron: $-0.035 \pm 0.0096 \pm 0.011$
- After non-perturbative correction, average spin carried by quarks: $\Sigma = 42 \pm 14\%$
- Axial charges from $\beta$-decay: $\Delta q_3 = 1.257 \pm 0.003$, $\Delta q_8 = 0.59 \pm 0.02$
- The anomaly correction needed to resolve the Ellis-Jaffe discrepancy: $\delta \approx 5\%$, corresponding to $\Delta g \sim 1.2$ at $10$ GeV$^2$ or $\Delta g \sim 1/2$ at a quark model scale ($\lesssim 1$ GeV$^2$)
- Deuteron d-state probability range: $4$ to $7\%$, with modern non-local potentials preferring $5 \pm 1\%$; a $1\%$ variation in $\omega_D$ gives roughly $10\%$ error in $g_{1n}$ at $x = 0.1$

## Context
This 1994 review summarises the state of spin structure function measurements following the EMC "spin crisis" and the first SMC and SLAC E142/E143 results. It extends the work of Close and Thomas (1988) on the flavour and spin dependence of parton distributions from quark model wave functions, and the work of Efremov and Teryaev and Altarelli and Ross (ETAR) on the role of the axial anomaly. The nuclear structure function analysis builds on the off-shell calculations of Melnitchouk, Piller, and Thomas (1994) for the deuteron, and earlier convolution approaches by Tokarev (1994) and Kaptari and Umnikov. The paper identifies open questions including the $x \to 0$ extrapolation (Regge behaviour vs. logarithmic corrections for the singlet component, as discussed by Bass and Landshoff), higher twist corrections, the need to determine whether $g_{1n}$ becomes positive at intermediate and large $x$, and the role of shadowing and meson exchange corrections for polarised nuclear targets.
