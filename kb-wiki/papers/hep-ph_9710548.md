# Chiral Perturbation Theory and Nucleon Polarizabilities

> Compton scattering offers in principle an intriguing new window on nucleon structure. Existing experiments and future programs are discussed and the state of theoretical understanding of such measurements is explored.

# Chiral Perturbation Theory and Nucleon Polarizabilities

## Key claim

Low-energy Compton scattering provides a window into nucleon structure through electric and magnetic polarizabilities, and chiral perturbation theory at one-loop order yields predictions for these quantities that agree remarkably well with existing experimental data, though higher-order calculations (including explicit Delta(1232) degrees of freedom) are necessary to resolve discrepancies in the spin polarizability and to achieve consistent power counting.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework connecting Compton scattering amplitudes to nucleon polarizabilities, then applies heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory (HBChPT) at one-loop (O(p^3)) and two-loop (O(p^4)) order to compute the electric polarizability α_E, magnetic polarizability β_M, and spin polarizability γ for both proton and neutron. A separate calculation incorporates the Delta(1232) resonance as an explicit degree of freedom using a projected Rarita-Schwinger formalism, treating the mass splitting Δ = m_Δ − m_N as an additional small parameter in a generalized chiral expansion at O(ε^3). The paper also discusses virtual Compton scattering (VCS) as a probe of generalized, momentum-dependent polarizabilities.

## Key numbers

- Proton electric polarizability (experimental, SAL and MAMI): α_E^p = 12.1 ± 0.8 ± 0.5 × 10^{-4} fm^3
- Proton magnetic polarizability (experimental, SAL and MAMI): β_M^p = 2.1 ∓ 0.8 ∓ 0.5 × 10^{-4} fm^3
- Neutron electric polarizability (experimental, ORNL Pb transmission): α_E^n = 12.6 ± 1.5 ± 2.0 × 10^{-4} fm^3
- Neutron magnetic polarizability (experimental, ORNL Pb transmission): β_M^n = 3.2 ∓ 1.5 ∓ 2.0 × 10^{-4} fm^3
- One-loop HBChPT prediction (proton and neutron): α_E^p = α_E^n = 12.2 × 10^{-4} fm^3, β_M^p = β_M^n = 1.22 × 10^{-4} fm^3
- One-loop HBChPT spin polarizability prediction: γ = 4.5 × 10^{-4} fm^4 (opposite sign to sum-rule value of approximately −1 × 10^{-4} fm^4)
- O(p^4) HBChPT predictions: α_E^p = 10.5 ± 2.0, α_E^n = 13.4 ± 1.5, β_M^p = 3.5 ± 3.6, β_M^n = 7.8 ± 3.6 (all × 10^{-4} fm^3)
- O(ε^3) calculation with explicit Delta(1232): α_E = 12.2 (nucleon loop) + 0 (Delta pole) + 4.2 (Delta loop) = 16.4 × 10^{-4} fm^3; β_M = 1.2 (nucleon loop) + 7.2 (Delta pole) + 0.7 (Delta loop) = 9.1 × 10^{-4} fm^3
- Backward spin-polarizability (LEGS group): γ_π = (−28.0 ± 2.8 ± 2.5) × 10^{-4} fm^4
- Anomaly contribution to backward spin-polarizability: γ_π^{anomaly} = −44 × 10^{-4} fm^4
- Quark model prediction for proton electric polarizability: α_E ≈ 35 × 10^{-4} fm^3 (too large)
- Quark model prediction for α_E^p − α_E^n: ≈ 3.8 × 10^{-4} fm^3

## Context

This review extends the program of applying chiral perturbation theory to nucleon electromagnetic properties, building on the heavy baryon formalism developed by Bernard, Kaiser, and Meissner (mid-1990s) and the earlier constituent quark model calculations that overpredicted polarizabilities. The paper identifies a key tension: the one-loop chiral prediction for the spin polarizability γ has the opposite sign from the value extracted from single-pion photoproduction multipole analyses via the Drell-Hearn-Gerasimov sum rule. The inclusion of the Delta(1232) as an explicit degree of freedom (work by Holstein, Hemmert, and Kambor) partially resolves this but destroys agreement for α_E and β_M, motivating the need for O(ε^4) calculations. The paper also introduces virtual Compton scattering as a new frontier that will measure generalized, momentum-dependent polarizabilities, with approved experimental programs at MAMI, CEBAF, and BATES; a particularly interesting prediction from chiral perturbation theory is a temporary rise in β_M(q̄) at low momentum transfer, in contrast to quark model predictions of a monotonic decrease.
