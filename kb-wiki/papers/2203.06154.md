# Hierarchy Configuration Interaction: Combining Seniority Number and Excitation Degree

> We propose a novel partitioning of the Hilbert space, hierarchy configuration interaction (hCI), where the excitation degree (with respect to a given reference determinant) and the seniority number (i.e., the number of unpaired electrons) are combined in a single hierarchy parameter. The key appealing feature of hCI is that each hierarchy level accounts for all classes of determinants whose number share the same scaling with system size. By surveying the dissociation of multiple molecular systems, we found that the overall performance of hCI usually exceeds or, at least, parallels that of excitation-based CI. For higher orders of hCI and excitation-based CI, the additional computational burden related to orbital optimization usually do not compensate the marginal improvements compared with results obtained with Hartree-Fock orbitals. The exception is orbital-optimized CI with single excitations, a minimally correlated model displaying the qualitatively correct description of single bond breaking, at a very modest computational cost.

## Key claim

The authors propose hierarchy configuration interaction (hCI), which combines excitation degree and seniority number into a single parameter, and find that hCI usually exceeds or at least parallels excitation-based CI in performance, with the caveat that for higher orders the additional computational burden of orbital optimization does not compensate for marginal improvements over results obtained with Hartree-Fock orbitals.

## Method

The authors implemented hCI by adapting the CIPSI (configuration interaction using a perturbative selection made iteratively) algorithm to select determinants with a given maximum hierarchy parameter h = (e + s/2)/2, where e is excitation degree and s is seniority number. They computed potential energy curves for dissociation of six molecular systems (HF, F2, N2, ethylene, H4, and H8) using both canonical Hartree-Fock orbitals and orbital-optimized CI, with the cc-pVDZ basis set and frozen core approximation. Convergence of non-parallelity error, distance error, vibrational frequencies, and equilibrium geometries to the full CI limit was evaluated as a function of the number of determinants.

## Key numbers

- hCI2 and CISD both scale as Ndet = order(Nbas^4); hCI3 and CISDT both scale as Ndet = order(Nbas^6)
- For HF, F2, and ethylene, hCI1 and hCI1.5 yield lower non-parallelity errors than the much more expensive CISDT method
- oo-CIS recovers 15%-40% of correlation energy around equilibrium geometries and 25%-65% at dissociation
- Second-order perturbation correction for ground state energy convergence was set below 0.01 millihartree
- Orbital optimization convergence threshold: maximum orbital rotation gradient below 0.1 millihartree
- Gradient component replacement threshold g0 = 1 microhartree for avoiding saddle point solutions

## Context

This work extends the dichotomy between excitation-based CI (which recovers dynamic correlation at polynomial cost but struggles with static correlation) and seniority-based CI (effective for static correlation but with exponential scaling). Prior hybrid approaches combining excitation and seniority truncations were explored by Alcoba (2014) and Bytautas (2015), but hCI is distinguished by its single hierarchy parameter and polynomial scaling. The method enables systematic improvement over excitation-based CI for bond-breaking problems and suggests future extensions to coupled-cluster methods analogous to the excitation-seniority truncation, as well as applications to excited states and quantum Monte Carlo trial wave functions.
