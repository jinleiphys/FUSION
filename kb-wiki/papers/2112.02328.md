# Nuclear potentials relevant to the symmetry energy in chiral models

> We employ the extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio, linear-$σ$ models, and the density-dependent model with chiral limits to work out the mean fields and relevant properties of nuclear matter. To have the constraint from the data, we reexamine the Dirac optical potentials and symmetry potential based on the relativistic impulse approximation (RIA). Unlike the extended NJL and the density-dependent models with the chiral limit in terms of the vanishing scalar density, the extended linear-$σ$ model with a sluggish changing scalar field loses the chiral limit at the high density end. The various scalar fields can characterize the different Schrödinger-equivalent potentials and kinetic symmetry energy in the whole density region and the symmetry potential in the intermediate density region. The drop of the scalar field due to the chiral restoration results in a clear rise of the kinetic symmetry energy. The chiral limit in the models gives rise to the softening of the symmetry potential and thereof the symmetry energy at high densities.

# Nuclear potentials relevant to the symmetry energy in chiral models

## Key claim
The chiral limit in extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) and density-dependent models produces a significant reduction of the scalar field and stiffening of the equation of state, while the extended linear-σ model with a sluggish scalar field loses the chiral limit at high densities and degenerates into a usual relativistic mean-field model; the chiral limit also causes a coincident softening of the symmetry potential and symmetry energy at high densities.

## Method
The authors solved the extended NJL model (NJL350 with momentum cutoff 350 MeV), the extended linear-σ model in the strong coupling limit (SCL), the density-dependent model with chiral limit (SLCd), and the relativistic mean-field model FSUgold in the mean-field approximation. They then used the relativistic impulse approximation (RIA) to compute Dirac optical potentials, Schrödinger-equivalent potentials, and symmetry potentials from the scalar and vector densities of each model, with forward NN elastic scattering amplitudes determined from experimental NN phase shifts.

## Key numbers
- Incompressibilities: SCL 279 MeV, NJL350 262 MeV, SLCd 230 MeV, FSUgold 230 MeV
- Saturation densities: SCL 0.145 fm⁻³, NJL350 0.16 fm⁻³, SLCd 0.16 fm⁻³, FSUgold 0.145 fm⁻³
- The symmetry energy slope L from PREX-II is cited as L = 106 ± 37 MeV, and from charged pion spectra as 42 < L < 117 MeV
- The global average of 28 independent analyses gave L = 59 ± 16 MeV (Li 2013); an updated average gave L = 58.7 ± 28.1 MeV (Oertel 2016)
- The ²⁰⁸Pb neutron skin thickness from weak-interaction electron scattering is 0.283 ± 0.071 fm

## Context
This work extends prior studies of chiral symmetry in nuclear matter using the NJL model (NJL 1961), linear-σ model (Schwinger 1957, Gell-Mann and Lévy 1960), and Brown-Rho scaling (Song 2001, Jia 2007). It contrasts with the usual relativistic mean-field approach (e.g., FSUgold) and earlier RIA studies of symmetry potentials (Chen 2005, Li 2006). The results enable constraints on the symmetry energy at high densities by linking chiral restoration to the stiffening of the equation of state and softening of the symmetry potential, relevant to neutron star observations such as the 2 M⊙ constraint.
