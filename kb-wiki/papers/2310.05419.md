# On the Microscopic Level Density Models for Nuclei Near Z=28 Shell Closure

> A comprehensive test of level density models for explaining the decay of excited compound nuclei, 54 Mn, 56 Fe, 58 Co, 60 Ni, 61 Ni and 63 Cu, in the energy range of 28 - 36 MeV has been performed. The compound nuclei of interest in the desired ranges are populated using 6 Li based transfer reactions. The proton decay spectrum for each excitation energy bins has been measured. The measured proton spectrum has been reproduced using statistical model calculations with different level density models. A variance minimised approach has been employed for analysing the prediction capability of different level density models. This approach has been converged to Gogny Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov(HFB) microscopic level density model and which is attributed as the most accurate model for the desired nuclei.

## Key claim
The Gogny Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) microscopic level density model is the most accurate among tested models for reproducing measured proton decay spectra from compound nuclei near the Z=28 shell closure, specifically in the excitation energy range of 28-36 MeV where multiparticle emission is prominent. The claim is hedged by the statement that this conclusion applies to the particular mass region studied and that phenomenological models can also reproduce the data for specific isotopes, though lacking the same physical basis.

Proton decay spectra from the excited compound nuclei were measured using silicon detector telescopes in coincidence with projectile-like fragments, and the experimental spectra were compared with statistical model calculations using TALYS-1.9 employing five different level density models: Fermi Gas Model, Constant Temperature Model, Back-Shifted Fermi Gas Model, Generalized Superfluid Model, and microscopic (Skyrme-HFB and Gogny-HFB) models. A chi-squared minimization approach was used to quantify the agreement between experimental and calculated spectra for each model.

## Context
This work extends previous studies of nuclear level densities by testing microscopic models against experimental data for nuclei near the Z=28 shell closure, a region where shell effects and multiparticle emission are significant. The study uses the variance-minimized approach to compare the Gogny-HFB and Skyrme-HFB microscopic models against conventional phenomenological models (Fermi Gas, Constant Temperature, Back-Shifted Fermi Gas, Generalized Superfluid) as implemented in the TALYS-1.9 code with RIPL-3 parameterization. The results enable improved predictions for nuclear reaction cross sections relevant to fusion reactor structural materials (Cr, Fe, Ni, Mn, Co) and for nuclear astrophysics r-process calculations, where microscopic models are preferred for nuclei far from stability where empirical parameterizations are unreliable.
