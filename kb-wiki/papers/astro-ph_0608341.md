# Sensitivity of p-Process Nucleosynthesis to Nuclear Reaction Rates in a 25 Solar Mass Supernova Model

> The astrophysical p process, which is responsible for the origin of the proton rich stable nuclei heavier than iron, was investigated using a full nuclear reaction network for a type II supernova explosion when the shock front passes through the O/Ne layer. Calculations were performed with a multi-layer model adopting the seed of a pre-explosion evolution of a 25 solar mass star. The reaction flux was calculated to determine the main reaction path and branching points responsible for synthesizing the proton rich nuclei. In order to investigate the impact of nuclear reaction rates on the predicted p-process abundances, extensive simulations with different sets of collectively and individually modified neutron-, proton-, alpha-capture and photodisintegration rates have been performed. These results are not only relevant to explore the nuclear physics related uncertainties in p-process calculations but are also important for identifying the strategy and planning of future experiments.

## Key claim

The paper systematically investigates how uncertainties in nuclear reaction rates affect predicted p-process abundances in a 25 solar mass supernova model, finding that while most p-nuclei abundances change by less than a factor of two under global rate variations, specific isotopes show strong sensitivity to particular reaction channels, especially ($\gamma,\alpha$) rates for heavy p-nuclei above A=140 and ($\gamma$,p) rates for light p-nuclei like $^{92}$Mo and $^{96}$Ru.

## Method

The authors used a full nuclear reaction network of over 20,000 reactions connecting about 1,800 nuclei, simulating p-process nucleosynthesis in 14 different mass layers of the O/Ne burning zone of a 25 solar mass star during a type II supernova shock front. They calculated time-integrated reaction fluxes to identify main reaction paths and branching points, then performed sensitivity studies by collectively and individually modifying neutron-, proton-, alpha-capture, and photodisintegration rates within uncertainty limits, comparing the resulting normalized overproduction factors to those obtained with standard Hauser-Feshbach rates from the NON-SMOKER code.

## Key numbers

- Peak temperatures in the Ne/O layer range from 1.7 < T$_9$ < 3.3 (where T$_9$ is temperature in 10$^9$ K)
- The reaction network comprises more than 20,000 reactions connecting about 1,800 nuclei from hydrogen to bismuth
- 35 p-nuclei were investigated, from $^{74}$Se to $^{196}$Hg
- Global enhancement or reduction of all neutron-induced rates by a factor of three changes overall p-nuclei abundances by less than a factor of two on average
- The $^{144}$Sm($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{158}$Gd reaction rate was measured nearly one order of magnitude lower than Hauser-Feshbach predictions
- Critical peak temperature for ($\gamma,\alpha$) reactions is T$_9$=2.6, below which these rates are too weak to cause rapid depletion of high-Z isotopes
- The $^{96}$Ru($\gamma,\alpha$)$^{92}$Mo reaction shows significant flux in the p-process path

## Context

This work extends previous p-process studies by Rayet et al. (1995) and Arnould and Goriely (2003) by focusing specifically on nuclear physics uncertainties rather than global astrophysical model variations. The authors use the same 25 solar mass progenitor model and seed abundances as Rayet et al. (1995) and compare with results from Collaboration for Research on the Origin of the Elements (2000) and Wasserburg et al. (2002). The study identifies critical reaction rates needing experimental investigation, particularly ($\gamma,\alpha$) reactions on neutron-deficient isotopes near closed shells, and confirms that the long-standing underproduction of $^{92,94}$Mo and $^{96,98}$Ru cannot be resolved by nuclear rate uncertainties alone, pointing instead to alternative production scenarios such as neutrino-induced processes or the rp process in accreting neutron stars.
