# Nuclear excitation by electron capture in optical-laser-generated plasmas

> The process of nuclear excitation by electron capture in plasma environments generated by the interaction of ultra-strong optical lasers with solid-state samples is investigated theoretically. With the help of a plasma model we perform a comprehensive study of the optimal parameters for most efficient nuclear excitation and determine the corresponding laser setup requirements. We discern between the low-density plasma regime, modeled by scaling laws, and the high-density regime, for which we perform particle-in-cell calculations. As nuclear transition case study we consider the 4.85 keV nuclear excitation starting from the long-lived $^{93\mathrm{m}}$Mo isomer. Our results show that the optimal plasma and laser parameters are sensitive to the chosen observable and that measurable rates of nuclear excitation and isomer depletion of $^{93\mathrm{m}}$Mo should be already achievable at laser facilities existing today.

## Key claim

Nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC) in optical-laser-generated plasmas can achieve measurable rates of isomer depletion for $^{93\mathrm{m}}$Mo at laser facilities existing today, but the optimal plasma and laser parameters are sensitive to the chosen observable (whether maximizing the NEEC rate or the total number of excited nuclei).

## Method

The authors developed a plasma model combining microscopic NEEC cross sections (calculated using Multi-Configurational-Dirac-Fock atomic wave functions and Dirac equation continuum solutions) with macroscopic plasma conditions including charge state distributions and electron flux. They distinguished between the low-density plasma regime modeled by scaling laws (using ponderomotive and short-scale length temperature scalings) and the high-density regime for which particle-in-cell (PIC) calculations were performed. The total number of excited nuclei was computed by integrating the NEEC rate over the plasma volume and lifetime, with the plasma lifetime approximated by a spherical expansion model and validated against a hydrodynamic expansion model.

## Key numbers

- The nuclear transition energy for the $^{93\mathrm{m}}$Mo isomer trigger state is 4.85 keV, with the isomeric state at 2424 keV and a subsequent decay cascade containing a 1478 keV photon as a signature of isomer depletion.
- The NEEC resonance width is approximately 100 neV, determined by the nuclear linewidth.
- The isomer fraction $f_{\text{iso}} \approx 10^{-5}$ can be embedded in solid-state Niobium foils via the $^{93}_{41}$Nb(p,n)$^{93\mathrm{m}}_{\phantom{m} 42}$Mo reaction using intense ($\ge 10^{14}$ protons/s) beams.
- The plasma lifetime for spherical plasmas is given by $\tau_{\text{p}} = R_{\text{p}} \sqrt{m_{\text{i}}/(T_{\text{e}} \bar{Z})}$, where $R_{\text{p}}$ is the plasma radius, $m_{\text{i}}$ the ion mass, $T_{\text{e}}$ the electron temperature, and $\bar{Z}$ the average charge state.
- The ponderomotive scaling law gives $T_{\text{e}} \approx 3.6 I_{16} \lambda_{\mu}^2$ keV, where $I_{16}$ is laser intensity in units of $10^{16}$ W/cm$^2$ and $\lambda_{\mu}$ the wavelength in microns.
- The short-scale length scaling law gives $T_{\text{e}} \approx 8 (I_{16} \lambda_{\mu}^2)^{1/3}$ keV.
- The absorption coefficient $f$ saturates at 10-15% for high irradiances and steep density profiles, but can reach up to 70% for moderate intensities and intermediate scale lengths.
- The reduced transition probability $B(E2)$ for the nuclear transition was calculated as 3.5 Weisskopf units (W.u.).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of NEEC in XFEL-generated plasmas (Gunst 2014, Gunst 2015), where NEEC was shown to exceed direct nuclear photoexcitation by approximately six orders of magnitude, to the case of optical-laser-generated plasmas. The authors build on earlier theoretical work on nuclear excitation in optical laser-generated plasmas (Harston 1999, Gosselin 2004, Gosselin 2007, Morel 2004) and a recent letter (Wu 2018) showing NEEC as the dominant mechanism for a broad parameter range. The study enables the design of experiments at existing high-power laser facilities for controlled energy storage and release via nuclear isomers, motivated by the recent experimental observation of isomer depletion via NEEC for the 4.85 keV transition in $^{93\mathrm{m}}$Mo (Chiara 2018).
