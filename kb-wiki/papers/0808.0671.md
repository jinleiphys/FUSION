# Thickness of the strangelet-crystal crust of a strange star

> It has recently been pointed out that if the surface tension of quark matter is low enough, the surface of a strange star will be a crust consisting of a crystal of charged strangelets in a neutralizing background of electrons. This affects the behavior of the surface, and must be taken into account in efforts to observationally rule out strange stars. We calculate the thickness of this ``mixed phase'' crust, taking into account the effects of surface tension and Debye screening of electric charge. Our calculation uses a generic parametrization of the equation of state of quark matter. For a reasonable range of quark matter equations of state, and surface tension of order a few MeV/fm^2, we find that the preferred crystal structure always involves spherical strangelets, not rods or slabs of quark matter. We find that for a star of radius 10 km and mass 1.5 Msolar, the strangelet-crystal crust can be from zero to hundreds of meters thick, the thickness being greater when the strange quark is heavier, and the surface tension is smaller. For smaller quark stars the crust will be even thicker.

## Key claim

The paper calculates that for a strange star of radius 10 km and mass 1.5 solar masses, the strangelet-crystal crust can range from zero to hundreds of meters thick, with the thickness being greater when the strange quark is heavier and the surface tension is smaller; the authors qualify that for smaller quark stars the crust will be even thicker.

## Method

The authors model the mixed-phase crust as a lattice of Wigner-Seitz cells containing quark matter (spheres, rods, or slabs) in a degenerate electron gas, solving the Poisson equation in the Thomas-Fermi approximation with Debye screening. They use a generic parametrization of the quark matter equation of state expanded to second order in the electron chemical potential, and determine the favored geometry by minimizing the enthalpy per quark at each external pressure. The crust thickness is then obtained from the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation assuming the crust is thin compared to the stellar radius.

## Key numbers

- For a star of radius 10 km and mass 1.5 solar masses, the strangelet-crystal crust thickness ranges from zero to hundreds of meters.
- The critical surface tension for droplet formation ranges from 0.533 MeV/fm^2 (for Debye length 4.82 fm and charge density 0.0445 fm^{-3}) to 11.6 MeV/fm^2 (for Debye length 6.82 fm and charge density 0.124 fm^{-3}).
- For a Debye length of 4.82 fm and charge density 0.0791 fm^{-3}, the critical pressure for the mixed phase is 1370 MeV^4.
- For surface tension 0.3 MeV/fm^2, crust thicknesses range from 9 m (Debye length 4.82 fm, charge density 0.0445 fm^{-3}) to over 460 m (Debye length 6.82 fm, charge density 0.124 fm^{-3}).
- For surface tension 10.0 MeV/fm^2, the only nonzero crust thickness reported is 39 m (Debye length 6.82 fm, charge density 0.124 fm^{-3}).

## Context

This work extends the earlier estimate by Jaikumar, Reddy, and Steiner (2005), who assumed zero surface tension and neglected Debye screening and found a crust thickness of 40-100 m. The present calculation includes surface tension and Debye screening, and finds that the preferred crystal structure always involves spherical strangelets rather than rods or slabs, contrary to the conjecture of a multi-layer "nuclear pasta" structure. The results enable more realistic modeling of strange star observables including cooling behavior, photon emission spectra, glitches, and seismic vibration frequencies that could distinguish strange stars from neutron stars.
