# Photon and dilepton production at the Facility for Antiproton and Ion Research and the beam energy scan program at the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider using coarse-grained microscopic transport simulations

> We present calculations of dilepton and photon spectra for the energy range $E_{\text{lab}}=2-35$ $A$GeV which will be available for the Compressed Baryonic Matter (CBM) experiment at the future Facility for Anti-Proton and Ion Research (FAIR). The same energy regime will also be covered by phase II of the Beam Energy Scan at the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider (RHIC-BES). Coarse-grained dynamics from microscopic transport calculations of the Ultra-relativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) model is used to determine temperature and chemical potentials, which allows for the use of dilepton and photon-emission rates from equilibrium quantum-field theory calculations. The results indicate that non-equilibrium effects, the presence of baryonic matter and the creation of a deconfined phase might show up in specific manners in the measurable dilepton invariant mass spectra and in the photon transverse momentum spectra. However, as the many influences are difficult to disentangle, we argue that the challenge for future measurements of electromagnetic probes will be to provide a high precision with uncertainties much lower than in previous experiments. Furthermore, a systematic study of the whole energy range covered by CBM at FAIR and RHIC-BES is necessary to discriminate between different effects, which influence the spectra, and to identify possible signatures of a phase transition.

## Key claim

The authors argue that disentangling non-equilibrium effects, baryonic matter influences, and possible signatures of a deconfined phase in dilepton and photon spectra at FAIR and RHIC-BES energies will require high-precision measurements with uncertainties much lower than in previous experiments, combined with a systematic study across the full energy range from 2 to 35 AGeV.

## Method

The authors use coarse-grained dynamics from the Ultra-relativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) transport model to extract local energy and baryon densities at each space-time point, then apply a hadron gas equation of state to determine temperature and baryochemical potential. Thermal dilepton and photon emission rates from equilibrium quantum field theory are employed, using in-medium spectral functions for the rho and omega mesons from a hadronic many-body calculation, supplemented by meson gas contributions including bremsstrahlung and strangeness-bearing reactions. Non-equilibrium effects are incorporated via effective energy densities accounting for momentum-space anisotropies and by extracting pion and kaon chemical potentials from the transport simulations.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy range covered: \(E_{\text{lab}} = 2-35\) AGeV for FAIR/CBM and overlapping fixed-target energies of 7.7, 9.1, 11.5, 14.5, and 19.6 AGeV for RHIC-BES phase II
- Critical temperature used to separate hadronic and partonic emission: \(T_c \approx 170\) MeV
- Maximum allowed pion chemical potential: 140 MeV; maximum allowed kaon chemical potential: 450 MeV
- The photon parametrization from the rho meson is limited to baryon chemical potentials lower than 400 MeV and momenta larger than 0.2 GeV/c
- The system needs roughly 10 fm/c after the beginning of the collision for transverse and longitudinal pressures to become approximately equal

## Context

This work extends the coarse-graining approach previously applied to dilepton production at SPS and SIS 18 energies (Endres 2014, Endres 2015) into the unexplored energy window between 2 and 35 AGeV, which will be covered by the CBM experiment at FAIR and phase II of the RHIC Beam Energy Scan. The calculations provide a baseline for interpreting future measurements and aim to discriminate between effects from high baryochemical potentials, phase transition signals, and non-equilibrium dynamics. The approach contrasts with pure transport models (which struggle with broad resonances and multi-particle interactions) and pure hydrodynamic models (which face issues with boost invariance and thermalization times at these lower energies).
