# An integral-free representation of the Dyson series using divided differences

> The Dyson series is an infinite sum of multi-dimensional time-ordered integrals, which serves as a formal representation of the quantum time-evolution operator in the interaction-picture. Using the mathematical tool of divided differences, we introduce an alternative representation for the series that is entirely free from both time ordering and integrals. In this new formalism, the Dyson expansion is given as a sum of efficiently-computable divided differences of the exponential function, considerably simplifying the calculation of the Dyson expansion terms, while also allowing for time-dependent perturbation calculations to be performed directly in the Schr{ö}dinger-picture. We showcase the utility of this novel representation by studying a number of use cases. We also discuss several immediate applications.

## Key claim

The Dyson series can be rewritten as a sum of efficiently-computable divided differences of the exponential function, entirely eliminating both time-ordering operators and multi-dimensional integrals, provided the perturbation Hamiltonian is first decomposed into a sum of generalized permutation operators multiplied by exponentials in time.

## Method

The authors decompose the time-dependent perturbation Hamiltonian \(V(t)\) into a sum of generalized permutation operators, each written as a product of a diagonal operator and a permutation operator, and further expand each diagonal operator as an exponential sum in time. Using this decomposition, they explicitly evaluate the time-ordered integrals of the standard Dyson series by applying the mathematical tool of divided differences, yielding a closed-form expression for each term in the series. The resulting representation expresses the interaction-picture time-evolution operator as a sum over states and multi-indices of coefficients that are divided differences of the exponential function, calculable with computational complexity proportional to \(q^2\) for the \(q\)-th order term.

## Key numbers

* The divided differences of any function with \(q\) inputs can be calculated with \(q(q-1)/2\) basic operations (Section II).
* The \(q\)-th order contribution to the transition amplitude for the non-Hermitian Hamiltonian \(H = H_0 + \gamma F e^{iEt}\) (with \(F\) a permutation matrix satisfying \(F|z_{\mathrm{in}}\rangle = |z_{\mathrm{fin}}\rangle\) and \(F^2 = \mathbb{1}\)) is given by \(A^{(q)}(z_{\mathrm{in}} \to z_{\mathrm{fin}}, t) = \gamma^q e^{-itE_{z_{\mathrm{in}}}} e^{-it[qE, (q-1)E, \ldots, E, 0]}\) for odd \(q\), and can be computed with \(O(q^2)\) basic operations (Example 1).
* For the two-level system \(H = \omega_0 \sigma_z + g \sigma_x \cos \omega t\), numerically-exact calculations using the divided-differences method were verified against 4th-order Runge-Kutta simulations for the case \(\omega_0 = 2\omega = 200\) (Figure 1 caption).
* For the harmonic oscillator with anharmonic perturbation \(H(t) = \omega(\hat{a}^\dagger\hat{a} + 1/2) + (1/4)\Gamma\cos(\Omega t)(1/(2m\omega))^2(\hat{a}^\dagger + \hat{a})^4\), the first-order (\(q=1\)) coefficient \(\beta^{(i_1)}_n\) is given explicitly as \(\beta^{(i_1)}_n = \gamma e^{-itE_{n+i_1}} (D_{i_1})_{nn} (e^{-it[\Omega - i_1\omega, 0]} + e^{-it[-(\Omega + i_1\omega), 0]})\) where \(\gamma = (1/8)\Gamma(1/(2m\omega))^2\) (Example 3).

## Context

This work extends the divided-differences approach previously applied to time-independent Hamiltonians (Kalev and Hen, 2020) to the time-dependent case, providing an integral-free alternative to the standard Dyson series (Dyson, 1949) that avoids the cumbersome multi-dimensional time-ordered integrals. The new formulation enables time-dependent perturbation calculations to be performed directly in the Schrodinger-picture without switching to the interaction-picture, and the authors demonstrate its utility for computing transition amplitudes beyond Fermi's golden rule (Merzbacher), for highly oscillating two-level systems relevant to quantum gates (Giscard) and Bloch-Siegert systems (Frasca), and for infinite-dimensional systems such as the driven harmonic oscillator. The representation also naturally lends itself to quantum algorithms for Hamiltonian simulation via the linear combination of unitaries (LCU) lemma (Berry), building on prior work on quantum simulation of time-dependent Hamiltonians (Chen, 2021).
