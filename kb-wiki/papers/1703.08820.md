# Interesting effect of the nd -> p(nn) reaction

> The reaction nd -> p(nn) produce two slow neutrons in the final state instead of deuteron. In the first view we can take into account the binding energy e ~ 2.23 MeV but this approach can not explain all features which are observed in the momentum spectrum of the secondaries protons. We need to suppose that two slow neutrons in the final state form the intermediate system which has own distribution of internal Fermi momentum.

# Wiki Page: Interesting effect of the nd -> p(nn) reaction

## Key claim
The momentum spectrum of secondary protons from the quasi-elastic charge-exchange reaction nd → p(nn) is shifted toward lower momenta relative to the elastic np → pn peak by about 6.5 MeV/c, which is 2.5 times larger than can be explained by the deuteron binding energy (≈2.23 MeV) alone. The authors argue that this excess energy loss implies the two final-state neutrons form an intermediate system with its own internal Fermi-momentum distribution, rather than a simple spectator-neutron picture.

## Method
The experiment used the Delta-Sigma spectrometer at the Laboratory of High Energy Physics (Dubna) with a neutron beam (T_n = 0.55–2.0 GeV) incident on alternating H₂ and D₂ cryogenic targets. Proton momentum spectra from charge-exchange reactions were recorded near zero degrees using a magnetic spectrometer with multi-wire proportional chambers, a time-of-flight system to reject deuteron background from np → dπ⁰, and a target-surrounding veto detector (DTS) to suppress inelastic events from Δ(1232) resonance production. The relative shift between the elastic np → pn and quasi-elastic nd → p(nn) peaks was extracted both by fitting each spectrum with a double-Gaussian function and by a novel parametrization method that divides the D₂ spectrum by the H₂ spectrum and finds the shift that makes the slope of the ratio zero, using only the right (high-momentum) halves of the peaks to avoid inelastic contamination.

## Key numbers
- The deuteron binding energy is ε_bind ≈ 2.23 MeV, but the observed energy loss δE at T_n = 800 MeV is ≈ 5.4 MeV, which is 2.5 times larger than this binding energy.
- At T_n = 0.8 GeV, the shift between the elastic and quasi-elastic peaks measured by the parametrization method is δP = 6.42 ± 2.51 MeV/c; the double-Gaussian fit gives δP = 5.7 ± 2.4 MeV/c.
- At T_n = 1.8 GeV, the parametrization method gives δP = 6.01 ± 3.06 MeV/c; the double-Gaussian fit gives δP = 11.2 ± 3.4 MeV/c (the latter is inflated by inelastic background).
- The energy-equivalent shift at T_n = 800 MeV is δE ≈ 5.4 MeV, derived from E·dE = P·dP.
- The veto detector DTS suppresses inelastic background by a factor of ≈5, with a charged-particle registration efficiency of 92% and neutral-particle efficiency of 67%, giving a total efficiency of ≈80%.
- The inelastic contamination in the right half of the proton spectrum at T_n = 2.0 GeV with DTS active is 0.85% for H₂ and 3.64% for D₂.
- The beam momentum spread is σP/P ≈ 3.3%, and the spectrometer momentum resolution is σP/P ≈ 0.7% with angular resolution σθ ≈ 1.2 mrad.
- The time-of-flight system achieves a time resolution of ≈300 ps, with a left-right arm dispersion of ≈1.8 ns.

## Context
This work extends and quantifies an effect first hinted at by the Los Alamos group (Bjork et al., early 1970s), who observed a shift of ≈7 MeV in the pd → n(pp) reaction at 647 and 800 MeV but with ≈10 MeV uncertainty. A related measurement by Bonner et al. (1970s) using the nd → p(nn) reaction at T_n = 794 MeV also implicitly contained the shift, though the authors did not discuss it directly. The present paper provides a systematic measurement across eight beam energies (0.55–2.0 GeV) with improved precision and a new analysis method that isolates the shift from inelastic backgrounds. The result challenges the simple impulse-approximation picture where the spectator neutron is at rest and the entire momentum transfer goes to the recoil neutron; instead, it suggests that the two final-state neutrons form a correlated system with an internal momentum distribution, enabling future studies of the nn interaction near threshold and the validity of the Hulthen wavefunction description of the deuteron.
