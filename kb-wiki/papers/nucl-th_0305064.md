# Hydrodynamical description of collective flow

> I review how hydrodynamical flow is related to the observed flow in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions and how initial conditions, equation of state and freeze-out temperature affect flow in hydrodynamical models.

## Key claim

This review argues that hydrodynamical models have been surprisingly successful in reproducing elliptic flow data at RHIC up to transverse momentum of 1.5 GeV, but this success leaves two complementary puzzles: either the system does not thermalize yet still produces flow close to the hydrodynamical prediction, or the system does thermalize and the mechanism responsible remains unknown.

## Method

The paper reviews how collective flow observables in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions are related to hydrodynamical model calculations, focusing on the Fourier expansion of azimuthal particle distributions into directed flow (v1) and elliptic flow (v2) coefficients. It examines how the choice of initial conditions (participant scaling versus binary collision scaling), the nuclear equation of state (including phase transitions), and the freeze-out temperature affect the buildup and final magnitude of flow anisotropies in ideal relativistic fluid dynamics.

## Key numbers

- At SPS energies, the final particle multiplicity scales with the number of participants (WA98).
- In simulations of Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s)=130 GeV, the initial time for hydrodynamical evolution has varied from tau0=0.2 to 1 fm.
- A delay of 3.5 fm/c in thermalization was estimated to reduce v2 by 50% (Kolb 2000a).
- The momentum anisotropy increases roughly by 25% after the system is completely hadronized (at tau-tau0 approximately 6.5 fm/c), whereas the anisotropy of pions increases by 70%.
- The differential elliptic flow of charged particles is reproduced up to pT=1.5 GeV.
- The centrality dependence of elliptic flow works below impact parameter b approximately 6 fm.

## Context

This review extends the work of Ollitrault (1992) on the relationship between initial spatial asymmetry and elliptic flow, and Kolb (2000a) on the time evolution of momentum anisotropy and its connection to phase transitions. It enables future work to constrain the nuclear equation of state and initial conditions by simultaneously reproducing flow data, HBT radii, and pseudorapidity dependence, while noting that open questions remain about the thermalization mechanism and the description of the pre-equilibrium stage.
