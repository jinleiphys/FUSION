# The proton charge radius

> Nucleons (protons and neutrons) are the building blocks of atomic nuclei, and are responsible for more than 99\% of the visible matter in the universe. Despite decades of efforts in studying its internal structure, there are still a number of puzzles surrounding the proton such as its spin, and charge radius. Accurate knowledge about the proton charge radius is not only essential for understanding how quantum chromodynamics (QCD) works in the non-perturbative region, but also important for bound state quantum electrodynamics (QED) calculations of atomic energy levels. It also has an impact on the Rydberg constant, one of the most precisely measured fundamental constants in nature. This article reviews the latest situation concerning the proton charge radius in light of the new experimental results from both atomic hydrogen spectroscopy and electron scattering measurements, with particular focus on the latter. We also present the related theoretical developments and backgrounds concerning the determination of the proton charge radius using different experimental techniques. We discuss upcoming experiments, and briefly mention the deuteron charge radius puzzle at the end.

## Key claim

The proton charge radius puzzle, a 5–7σ discrepancy between muonic hydrogen measurements (≈0.84 fm) and CODATA values (≈0.8775 fm), remains unresolved despite major progress; the review emphasizes that a strict three-dimensional charge density for the proton cannot be defined due to relativistic effects, and only a two-dimensional transverse charge density has a proper probabilistic interpretation.

## Method

The paper reviews the determination of the proton charge radius from two experimental techniques: elastic electron–proton scattering (measuring the electric form factor \(G_E\) via differential cross sections) and atomic hydrogen spectroscopy (extracting the radius from Lamb shift measurements combined with QED calculations). It discusses the theoretical framework linking form factors to charge densities, including the Sachs form factors, the Breit frame, and the infinite-momentum frame, and examines the role of generalized parton distributions (GPDs) and transverse momentum dependent parton distributions (TMDs) in understanding the proton's electromagnetic structure.

## Key numbers

- Muonic hydrogen Lamb shift measurement by the CREMA collaboration (2010): \(r_p = 0.84184(67)\) fm, from the \(2S_{1/2}(F=1)\) to \(2P_{3/2}(F=2)\) transition.
- Updated CREMA result (2013) combining two transitions: \(r_p = 0.84087(39)\) fm.
- 2010 CODATA recommended value: \(r_p = 0.8775(51)\) fm, based on electron scattering and ordinary hydrogen spectroscopy.
- Pre-2010 electron scattering results: \(r_p = 0.805(11)\) fm (Hand et al., 1963) and \(r_p = 0.862(12)\) fm (Simon et al., 1980), differing by about 7% relative.
- Melnikov and van Ritbergen (2000) extraction from hydrogen Lamb shift: \(r_p = 0.883(14)\) fm.
- Bernauer et al. (2010) from Mainz electron scattering: \(r_p = 0.8791(79)\) fm.
- Zhan et al. (2011) from Jefferson Lab: \(r_p = 0.875(10)\) fm.
- First proton charge radius from Hofstadter (1950s): \(0.74 \pm 0.24\) fm.

## Context

This review extends the earlier reviews by Pohl et al. (2013) and Carlson (2015) on the proton charge radius puzzle, which originated from the 2010 muonic hydrogen result. It focuses on recent progress from lepton scattering experiments and theoretical developments, including the work of Miller (2019) and Jaffe (2020) on the limitations of defining a three-dimensional charge density for relativistic systems. The paper enables future experiments at Jefferson Lab and the Electron-Ion Collider to resolve the puzzle and briefly notes a similar deuteron charge radius puzzle.
