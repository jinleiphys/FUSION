# Very Narrow Resonances in Spherical Proton Emitting Nuclei

> The separation energy and half-life of some heavy proton emitting nuclei, and the single-particle structure of the unbound 11-N, have been evaluated by implementing a careful numerical treatment to solve Schrodinger equation in a continuum discretization context. The basic scheme behind the method consists in using the ground-state proton emitter in connection with an isolated single-particle resonance.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a continuum discretization method for solving the Schrödinger equation can reliably calculate half-lives and separation energies of very narrow single-particle resonances in spherical proton-emitting nuclei, but the method works best for narrow resonances and has greater uncertainty for broad resonances such as the \( \frac{1}{2}^{+} \) ground state of \( ^{11}\mathrm{N} \).

## Method

The authors treat the ground-state proton emitter as an isolated single-particle resonance of a proton+core system. They use orthogonal and complementary projectors to decompose the scattering solution of the single-particle Hamiltonian, then solve the Schrödinger equation iteratively by sweeping successive energy ranges with improving integration step sizes until the resonant energy and width stabilize. The central potential is a Woods-Saxon form plus a spin-orbit term with a Woods-Saxon derivative, and the Coulomb term is a uniformly charged sphere; the potential parameters are adjusted so that the stabilized resonant energy reproduces the experimental \( Q \) value.

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical methods for calculating proton-emitter half-lives and spins, such as the WKB method used by Davids (year not given) and Maglione (year not given), and the continuum discretization approach previously developed by the same authors (Leite and Teruya, year not given). The results agree well with experimental data for heavy proton emitters and with the data of N11 (year not given) and Lep (year not given) for \( ^{11}\mathrm{N} \), though the method struggles with broad resonances. The approach enables more complex nuclear structure calculations beyond the single-particle picture, such as evaluating deformation or configuration mixing effects.
