# Unified QMF equation of state for neutron star matter: Static and dynamic properties

> We construct a set of unified equations of state based on the quark mean field (QMF) model, calibrated to different values of nuclear symmetry energy slope at the saturation density ($L_0$), with the aim of exploring both the static properties and dynamical behavior of neutron stars (NSs), and building a coherent picture of their internal structure. We assess the performance of these QMF models in describing the mass-radius relation, the cooling evolution of isolated NSs and X-ray transients, and the instabilities (e.g., the r-mode). In comparison to relativistic mean field (RMF) models formulated at the hadronic level, the QMF model predicts heavier nuclear clusters and larger Wigner-Seitz cell sizes in the NS crust, while the density of the free neutron gas remains largely similar between the two approaches. For the cooling of isolated NSs, the thermal evolution is found to be insensitive to both the many-body model and the symmetry energy slope in the absence of the direct Urca (dUrca) process. However, when rapid cooling via the dUrca process is allowed, in the case of large $L_0$ values (e.g., $L_0 \gtrsim 80$ MeV) in our study, the QMF model predicts a longer thermal relaxation time. Both the QMF and RMF models can reproduce cooling curves consistent with observations of X-ray transients (e.g., KS 1731--260) during their crustal cooling phase, although stellar parameters show slight variations depending on the model and symmetry energy slope. Within our unified framework, a larger $L_0$ value generally results in a wider instability window, while increasing the stellar mass tends to suppress the instability window. We also provide simple power-law parameterizations that quantify the dependence of bulk and shear viscosities on the symmetry energy slope for nuclear matter at saturation density.

## Key claim

The quark mean-field (QMF) model, when calibrated to different values of the nuclear symmetry energy slope at saturation density ($L_0$), predicts heavier nuclear clusters and larger Wigner-Seitz cell sizes in the neutron star crust compared to relativistic mean-field (RMF) models, while the free neutron gas density remains similar; however, the thermal evolution of isolated neutron stars is insensitive to both the many-body model and the symmetry energy slope unless the direct Urca process is allowed, in which case QMF predicts a longer thermal relaxation time for large $L_0$ values ($L_0 \gtrsim 80$ MeV).

## Method

The authors constructed unified equations of state for neutron star matter using the quark mean-field (QMF) model, where meson couplings were fitted to reproduce empirical values of the symmetry energy slope $L_0$ (40, 60, and 80 MeV) while keeping other nuclear saturation properties fixed. They solved the Klein-Gordon equations for meson fields within Wigner-Seitz cells using the Thomas-Fermi approximation to describe the crust, including five nuclear pasta geometries (droplets, rods, slabs, tubes, bubbles), and used the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for global neutron star properties. Thermal evolution simulations were performed using the NSCool code for isolated neutron stars and the crustcool code for X-ray transients, while bulk and shear viscosities were computed from Urca processes and particle scattering to evaluate r-mode instability windows.

- For dUrca bulk viscosity: $\xi_{l}^{\text{dUrca}} = 8.553 \times 10^{24} (M_n^*/M_n)(M_p^*/M_p)(\rho_e/\rho_0)^{1/3} (T_9^4/\omega_4^2)(C_l/100\text{ MeV})^2 \Theta_{npl}$ g cm$^{-1}$ s$^{-1}$, where $T_9 = T/(10^9$ K) and $\omega_4 = \omega/(10^4$ s$^{-1})$

- Neutron $^3P_2$ maximum critical temperature: $0.5 \times 10^9$ K

## Context

This work extends previous unified equation of state studies using the quark mean-field model (Zhu et al., 2018; Miao et al., 2020) by systematically comparing static and dynamic neutron star properties across different symmetry energy slopes, and contrasts with relativistic mean-field models (Walecka, 1974; Niksic et al., 2011) that treat nucleons as point-like particles. The unified framework enables self-consistent calculations of crust-core transition, cooling evolution, and r-mode instabilities, building on prior crustal cooling studies of X-ray transients such as KS 1731-260 (Merritt et al., 2016) and providing power-law parameterizations for viscosities that can be used in future Bayesian inference of nuclear matter properties from multi-messenger neutron star observations.
