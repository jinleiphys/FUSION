# Shell-model study of exotic Sn isotopes with a realistic effective interaction

> We report on a shell-model study of Sn isotopes beyond N=82 employing a realistic effective interaction derived from the CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon potential renormalized through use of the V-low-k approach. At present, the most exotic Sn isotope for which some experimental information exists is 134Sn with an N/Z ratio of 1.68. It is the aim of our study to compare the results of our calculations with the available experimental data and to make predictions for the neighboring heavier isotopes which may be within reach of the next generation of radioactive ion beam facilities. The very good agreement between theory and experiment obtained for 134Sn gives confidence in the predictive power of our realistic shell-model calculations.

# Shell-model study of exotic Sn isotopes with a realistic effective interaction

## Key claim
The realistic shell-model calculations, using a CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon potential renormalized via the V-low-k approach with no adjustable parameters, reproduce the available experimental data for 134Sn very well, but the predictive power for heavier isotopes up to 140Sn depends on the assumption that the effective single-particle energies remain nearly constant from N=82 to N=90, contrary to claims of a shell closure at N=90 by Sarkar (2010).

## Method
The authors performed shell-model calculations for even tin isotopes from 134Sn to 140Sn using doubly magic 132Sn as a closed core, with valence neutrons occupying the six levels (0h9/2, 1f7/2, 1f5/2, 2p3/2, 2p1/2, 0i13/2) of the 82-126 shell. The effective interaction was derived from the CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon potential renormalized via the V-low-k approach with a cutoff momentum of Λ=2.2 fm⁻¹, and the two-body matrix elements were calculated using the Q-box folded-diagram expansion including all diagrams up to second order within a harmonic-oscillator basis with oscillator parameter 7.88 MeV for the A=132 region.

## Key numbers
- First 2⁺ state in 134Sn lies at 726 keV excitation energy, the lowest first-excited 2⁺ level observed in a semi-magic even-even nucleus across the chart of nuclides.
- Calculated B(E2; 2⁺₁ → 0⁺₁) = 1.64 W.u. versus experimental 1.42 ± 0.20 W.u. in 134Sn.
- Calculated B(E2; 6⁺₁ → 4⁺₁) = 0.82 W.u. versus experimental 0.89 ± 0.17 W.u. in 134Sn.
- Calculated binding energy of 134Sn = 5.92 MeV versus experimental 5.916 ± 0.150 MeV.
- Calculated one-neutron separation energy of 134Sn = 3.55 MeV versus experimental 3.545 ± 0.152 MeV.
- Calculated one-neutron separation energies for 136Sn, 138Sn, and 140Sn are 3.55, 3.53, and 3.50 MeV respectively.
- The 2p1/2 single-particle state in 133Sn is at 1.363 MeV excitation energy, about 300 keV lower than the previous accepted value from Hoff (1996).
- The difference between the <(f7/2)²|V_eff|(f7/2)²> matrix elements for 0⁺ and 2⁺ is about 0.4 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the realistic shell-model calculations of Covello (2007) for 134Sn to heavier isotopes up to 140Sn, using updated single-particle energies from the recent Penning trap mass measurement of Dworschak (2008) and the (d,p) reaction study of Jones (2010) on 133Sn. The results contradict the claim of a shell closure at N=90 by Sarkar (2010) using empirical interactions, and instead show nearly flat excitation energy curves for yrast 2⁺, 4⁺, and 6⁺ states, consistent with the calculations of Kartamyshev (2007). The predictions for binding and separation energies suggest the region remains far from the neutron drip line, and the calculations enable future experiments at next-generation radioactive ion beam facilities to test the predicted spectroscopic properties of 136Sn, 138Sn, and 140Sn.
