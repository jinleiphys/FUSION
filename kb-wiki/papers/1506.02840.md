# Quantifying jet transport properties via large $p_T$ hadron production

> Nuclear modification factor $R_{AA}$ for large $p_T$ single hadron is studied in a next-to-leading order (NLO) perturbative QCD (pQCD) parton model with medium-modified fragmentation functions (mFFs) due to jet quenching in high-energy heavy-ion collisions. The energy loss of the hard partons in the QGP is incorporated in the mFFs which utilize two most important parameters to characterize the transport properties of the hard parton jets: the jet transport parameter $\hat q_{0}$ and the mean free path $λ_{0}$, both at the initial time $τ_0$. A phenomenological study of the experimental data for $R_{AA}(p_{T})$ is performed to constrain the two parameters with simultaneous $χ^2/{\rm d.o.f}$ fits to RHIC as well as LHC data. We obtain for energetic quarks $\hat q_{0}\approx 1.1 \pm 0.2$ GeV$^2$/fm and $λ_{0}\approx 0.4 \pm 0.03$ fm in central $Au+Au$ collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV, while $\hat q_{0}\approx 1.7 \pm 0.3$ GeV$^2$/fm, and $λ_{0}\approx 0.5 \pm 0.05$ fm in central $Pb+Pb$ collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76$ TeV. Numerical analysis shows that the best fit favors a multiple scattering picture for the energetic jets propagating through the bulk medium, with a moderate averaged number of gluon emissions. Based on the best constraints for $λ_{0}$ and $τ_0$, the estimated value for the mean-squared transverse momentum broadening is moderate which implies that the hard jets go through the medium with small reflection.

## Key claim

The paper extracts both the jet transport parameter $\hat q_{0}$ and the mean free path $\lambda_{0}$ simultaneously from fits to nuclear modification factor data, finding that the best-fit values lie between the single-scattering and infinite-number-scattering limits, which favors a multiple-scattering picture with moderate average number of gluon emissions.

## Method

The authors compute the nuclear modification factor $R_{AA}$ for large $p_T$ single hadrons using a next-to-leading order (NLO) perturbative QCD parton model with medium-modified fragmentation functions (mFFs) that incorporate parton energy loss via the high-twist approach. The mFFs depend on two independent parameters: the jet transport parameter $\hat q_{0}$ and the mean free path $\lambda_{0}$, both defined at initial time $\tau_0 = 0.6$ fm, with the bulk medium evolution given by a (3+1)-dimensional ideal hydrodynamic model. Simultaneous $\chi^2$/d.o.f fits to experimental $R_{AA}(p_T)$ data from RHIC and LHC are performed to constrain both parameters.

## Key numbers

* For central Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV: $\hat q_{0}\approx 1.1 \pm 0.2$ GeV$^2$/fm and $\lambda_{0}\approx 0.4 \pm 0.03$ fm for energetic quarks at initial temperature T=373 MeV.
* For central Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76$ TeV: $\hat q_{0}\approx 1.7 \pm 0.3$ GeV$^2$/fm and $\lambda_{0}\approx 0.5 \pm 0.05$ fm for energetic quarks at initial temperature T=473 MeV.
* The average transverse momentum broadening squared at initial time in the fireball center: $\langle q_T^2\rangle \approx 0.44 \pm 0.11$ GeV$^2$ at RHIC and $\langle q_T^2\rangle \approx 0.85 \pm 0.20$ GeV$^2$ at LHC.
* The scaled jet transport parameter $\hat q_0/T^3_0$ falls within the range extracted by the Jet Collaboration (Burke et al., 2014) but is considerably smaller than values from strong-coupled AdS/CFT calculations (Liu et al., 2006; Zhang et al., 2012) and lattice calculations (Majumder et al., 2012; Panero et al., 2013).

## Context

This work extends phenomenological extractions of jet transport properties by simultaneously constraining both $\hat q_0$ and $\lambda_0$, whereas previous studies such as the Jet Collaboration (Burke et al., 2014) extracted only $\hat q_0$ for a fixed initial energy of 10 GeV. The extracted mean free path values are consistent with theoretical estimates using a K-factor of 2.5-4.0 for the leading-order elastic cross section, suggesting a strongly interacting QGP. The results enable more precise modeling of jet quenching and can be compared with future extractions from other observables such as dihadron correlations or jet substructure measurements.
