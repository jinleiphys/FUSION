# Computing nuclear response functions with time-dependent coupled-cluster theory

> We compute nuclear response functions by solving the time-dependent A-body Schrödinger equation, recording the time-dependent transition moment and extracting spectral information via Fourier transforms. The solution of the time-dependent many-body problem accounts for correlations on top of the mean field by taking advantage of a time-dependent formulation of coupled-cluster theory. As a validation, we focus on electric dipole transitions in $^4$He and $^{16}$O and compare moments of the response function distribution to the results of an equivalent static framework, finding negligible discrepancies. We investigate how proton and neutron densities evolve in time, and we see the traditional picture of soft and giant dipole resonances as collective oscillations of protons and neutrons emerging from our calculations in $^{16}$O and $^{24}$O. This method also allows us to investigate the behavior of the nucleus in the presence of a strong electric field. In that regime, the behavior of the system becomes chaotic. Qualitatively, the spectral information obtained in this limit is in line with previous time-dependent mean-field results.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that time-dependent coupled-cluster (TDCC) theory can compute nuclear response functions for electric dipole transitions in medium-mass nuclei, with results matching static coupled-cluster calculations to within about 1% for sum rules, but only when the perturbation strength is kept small (epsilon = 0.1 MeV/fm) to remain within the linear perturbative regime.

## Method

The authors solve the time-dependent A-body Schrodinger equation using the time-dependent coupled-cluster ansatz truncated at the singles-and-doubles (CCSD) level, starting from a Hartree-Fock reference state with a static single-particle basis. They apply a Gaussian-shaped electric dipole pulse at t=0, evolve the system for up to 2000 fm/c using adaptive implicit time integration from the CVODE package, record the time-dependent dipole moment, and extract the response function via discrete Fourier transform with a cosine windowing function. The chiral nucleon-nucleon interaction NNLO_opt is employed, and results are compared to static Lorentz integral transform (LIT) coupled-cluster calculations for validation.

## Key numbers

- Perturbation strength epsilon = 0.1 MeV/fm is used for linear response calculations, which keeps the expectation value of J^2 below 10^-6 for ^4He at N_max=4
- Total simulation time T_max = 2000 fm/c gives an energy resolution of approximately 0.6 MeV
- The giant dipole resonance peak appears at about 28 MeV for both ^4He and ^16O, consistent with no-core shell model calculations predicting 26.5 MeV for ^4He
- Two low-lying excited states in ^16O are resolved at 8 and 10 MeV, consistent with experimental 1- states at 7.116 and 9.585 MeV
- For ^16O at N_max=8, the dipole polarizability shows about 1% deviation between linear and cubic-spline interpolation methods
- Time step is 0.2 fm/c for the first 6 fm/c (during the pulse) and 1 fm/c thereafter, resolving excitation energies exceeding 200 MeV
- Absolute tolerance for CVODE is set to 10^-9, relative tolerance to 10^-8

## Context

This work extends time-dependent coupled-cluster theory beyond the spherically symmetric calculations of Pigg (2012) to axially symmetric systems in medium-mass nuclei, building on the bivariational formulation by Kvaal (2012). The approach provides an alternative to time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) methods used by Nakatsukasa (2016), Maruhn (2014), and Simenel (2025) for nuclear dynamics, while enabling ab initio treatment of correlations beyond mean field. The method enables future studies of nuclear density fluctuations and behavior in strong electromagnetic fields, and implementations without symmetry restrictions are anticipated.
