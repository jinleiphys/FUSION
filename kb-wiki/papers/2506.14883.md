# A NICER view of the 1.4 solar-mass edge-on pulsar PSR J0614-3329

> Four neutron star radius measurements have already been obtained by modeling the X-ray pulses of rotation-powered millisecond pulsars observed by the Neutron Star Interior Composition ExploreR (NICER). We report here the radius measurement of PSR J0614-3329 employing the same method with NICER and XMM-Newton data using Bayesian Inference. For all different models tested, including one with unrestricted inclination prior, we retrieve very similar non-antipodal hot regions geometries and radii. For the preferred model, we infer an equatorial radius of $R_{\rm eq}=10.29^{+1.01}_{-0.86}\,$km for a mass of $M=1.44^{+0.06}_{-0.07} \, M_{\odot}$ (median values with equal-tailed $68\%$ credible interval), the latter being essentially constrained from radio timing priors obtained by MeerKAT. A more complex model, fitting the data equally well, resulted in a consistent inferred radius. We find that, for all different models, the pulse emission originates from two hot regions, one at the pole and the other at the equator. The resulting radius constraint is consistent with previous X-ray and gravitational wave measurements of neutron stars in the same mass range. Equation of state inferences, including previous NICER and gravitational wave results, slightly soften the equation of state with PSR J0614$-$3329 included and shift the allowed mass-radius region toward lower radii by $\sim 300\,$m, which is compatible with previous analyses to within less than one standard deviation.

## Key claim
The paper reports a radius measurement of PSR J0614-3329 using pulse profile modeling, finding an equatorial radius of 10.29 km for a mass of 1.44 solar masses, but the abstract's claim of consistency with previous measurements is qualified by the fact that including this new measurement shifts the allowed mass-radius region toward lower radii by approximately 300 meters, which is compatible with previous analyses to within less than one standard deviation.

## Method
The authors performed Bayesian inference using the X-PSI package to model energy-dependent X-ray pulse profiles from NICER (1.090 Ms effective exposure) and XMM-Newton (11.36 ks PN, 14.42 ks MOS) observations of PSR J0614-3329. They tested multiple hot region geometry models (single temperature with unshared parameters, single temperature plus a permanent dark spot, and two-temperature with unshared parameters) using nested sampling with MultiNest, incorporating radio timing priors on mass and inclination from MeerKAT Shapiro delay measurements. The analysis used a fully ionized hydrogen atmosphere model and marginalized over phase-independent background parameters for each instrument.

## Key numbers
- Equatorial radius for the preferred model: R_eq = 10.29 (+1.01, -0.86) km (median with 68% credible interval)
- Mass for the preferred model: M = 1.44 (+0.06, -0.07) solar masses, constrained from MeerKAT radio timing priors
- The inclusion of PSR J0614-3329 shifts the allowed mass-radius region toward lower radii by approximately 300 meters
- NICER effective exposure after filtering: 1.090 Ms from 1.6 Ms raw data
- Distance prior from optical photometry: normal distribution with mean 585 pc and standard deviation 45 pc
- Spin frequency of the pulsar: 318 Hz
- Neutral hydrogen column density prior: uniform from 0.001 to 10 x 10^20 cm^-2

## Context
This work extends the NICER pulse profile modeling program, which previously measured radii for four other millisecond pulsars including PSR J0030+0451 (Riley et al. 2019, Miller et al. 2019) and PSR J0740+6620 (Riley et al. 2021, Miller et al. 2021, Salmi et al. 2022). The tight mass and inclination constraints from MeerKAT radio timing (Miles et al. 2025) enabled a robust radius measurement that would otherwise suffer from degeneracies. The result is consistent with previous X-ray and gravitational wave measurements (GW170817 from Abbott et al. 2018) and, when combined with those, slightly softens the equation of state constraints, enabling tighter joint inferences on dense nuclear matter properties.
