# Neutron skin thickness in droplet model with surface width dependence: indications of softness of the nuclear symmetry energy

> We analyze the neutron skin thickness in finite nuclei with the droplet model and effective nuclear interactions. The ratio of the bulk symmetry energy J to the so-called surface stiffness coefficient Q has in the droplet model a prominent role in driving the size of neutron skins. We present a correlation between the density derivative of the nuclear symmetry energy at saturation and the J/Q ratio. We emphasize the role of the surface widths of the neutron and proton density profiles in the calculation of the neutron skin thickness when one uses realistic mean-field effective interactions. Next, taking as experimental baseline the neutron skin sizes measured in 26 antiprotonic atoms along the mass table, we explore constraints arising from neutron skins on the value of the J/Q ratio. The results favor a relatively soft symmetry energy at subsaturation densities. Our predictions are compared with the recent constraints derived from other experimental observables. Though the various extractions predict different ranges of values, one finds a narrow window L ~ 45-75 MeV for the coefficient L that characterizes the density derivative of the symmetry energy which is compatible with all the different empirical indications.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the ratio of the bulk symmetry energy J to the surface stiffness coefficient Q, which drives neutron skin thickness in the droplet model, is correlated with the slope L of the symmetry energy at saturation; using experimental neutron skin data from 26 antiprotonic atoms, the authors find that the symmetry energy is relatively soft at subsaturation densities, with a narrow window of L approximately 45-75 MeV compatible with all empirical indications, though the various extractions predict different ranges.

## Method

The authors used the droplet model (DM) to compute neutron skin thickness for nuclei from 40Ca to 238U, incorporating a surface width correction term proportional to (b_n^2 - b_p^2) that is usually neglected. They extracted the surface stiffness coefficient Q from asymmetric semi-infinite nuclear matter calculations using the extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) method for 18 Skyrme, Gogny, and relativistic mean-field (RMF) effective interactions. The DM predictions including surface width contributions were compared with self-consistent ETF calculations of finite nuclei and with experimental neutron skin thicknesses measured in antiprotonic atoms by Trzcinska et al. (2001, 2004).

## Key numbers

* The experimental neutron skin data from 26 antiprotonic atoms (40Ca to 238U) follow a linear trend with relative neutron excess I = (N-Z)/A: ΔR_np = (0.90 ± 0.15) I + (-0.03 ± 0.02) fm, as reported by Trzcinska et al. (2001, 2004).
* The slope L of the symmetry energy at saturation is constrained to a narrow window L ~ 45-75 MeV, compatible with all different empirical indications.
* The J/Q ratio for the 18 interactions ranges from 0.34 (SVI force) to 1.48 (NL1 force), with J values from 26.83 MeV (SGII) to 45.12 MeV (NL2) and Q values from 29.4 MeV (NL1) to 78.4 MeV (SVI).
* The surface width contribution slope σ^sw with respect to I lies within a band bounded by σ^sw = 0.3 J/Q + 0.07 fm and σ^sw = 0.3 J/Q - 0.05 fm; Skyrme forces have σ^sw below 0.25 fm, while RMF models have σ^sw above 0.25 fm.

## Context

This work extends the droplet model analysis of neutron skins by Swiatecki (2005) and the self-consistent mean-field studies of Brown (2000), Typel and Brown (2001), and Centelles et al. (2002), which showed linear correlations between neutron skin thickness in 208Pb and the slope of the neutron equation of state. The paper uses the experimental antiprotonic atom data of Trzcinska et al. (2001, 2004) as a benchmark, and compares its constraints on L with those from other observables such as isospin diffusion in heavy-ion reactions (Tsang et al., 2004; Chen et al., 2005; Li et al., 2005) and collective excitations (Piekarewicz, 2002; Li et al., 2007; Trippa et al., 2008). The results enable tighter constraints on the density dependence of the symmetry energy, relevant for neutron star structure and supernova dynamics.
