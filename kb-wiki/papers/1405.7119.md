# Nuclear Structure and the Fate of Core Collapse (Type II) Supernova

> For a long time Gerry Brown and his collaborator Hans Bethe considered the question of the final fate of a core collapse (Type II) supernova. Recalling ideas from nuclear structure on Kaon condensate and a soft equation of state of the dense nuclear matter they concluded that progenitor stars with mass as low a 17-18M$_\odot$ (including supernova 1987A) could collapse to a small mass black hole with a mass just beyond 1.5M$_\odot$, the upper bound they derive for a neutron star. We discuss another nuclear structure effect that determines the carbon to oxygen ratio (C/O) at the end of helium burning. This ratio also determines the fate of a Type II supernova with a carbon rich progenitor star producing a neutron star and oxygen rich collapsing to a black hole. While the C/O ratio is one of the most important nuclear input to stellar evolution it is still not known with sufficient accuracy. We discuss future efforts to measure with gamma-beam and TPC detector the 12C(a,g)16O reaction that determines the C/O ratio in stellar helium burning.

# Nuclear Structure and the Fate of Core Collapse (Type II) Supernova

## Key claim

The carbon-to-oxygen ratio (C/O) at the end of helium burning, determined by the poorly constrained 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction, may determine whether a Type II supernova progenitor produces a neutron star (carbon-rich) or collapses to a black hole (oxygen-rich), but the extrapolated astrophysical S-factors S_E1(300) and S_E2(300) remain ambiguous and cannot be resolved by current world data.

## Method

The paper reviews the current "world data" on the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction, focusing on published S_E2 values extracted from angular distribution measurements at center-of-mass energies below 1.7 MeV. Using chi-squared analysis, the data are shown to aggregate into two distinct groups, leading to two different extrapolations of S_E2(300). The author also discusses the analogous ambiguity in S_E1(300) from 16N beta-decay data and describes ongoing and proposed experiments using gamma-ray beams and a Time Projection Chamber (TPC) detector at the HIgammaS facility to measure the time-reversed 16O(gamma,alpha)12C reaction.

## Key numbers

- Progenitor stars with mass as low as 17-18 solar masses (including supernova 1987A) could collapse to a black hole with mass just beyond 1.5 solar masses, the upper bound derived for a neutron star via kaon condensate and a soft equation of state.
- The "world data" on S_E2 extrapolate to two distinct values at 300 keV: approximately 60 keVb or approximately 154 keVb.
- The S_E1(300) ambiguity yields two minima at approximately 10 keVb and approximately 80 keVb from 16N beta-decay data.
- Stellar evolution theory requires the C/O ratio with an uncertainty of 5%, requiring measurements in the Gamow window at 300 keV.
- Recent gamma-ray measurements of the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction achieved luminosities of order 10^35 cm^-2 sec^-1 with integrated luminosities close to one inverse femtobarn.

## Context

This work extends the earlier considerations of Bethe and Brown (Scientific American, 1985) and Brown (1988, 1992, 1994) on kaon condensates and the maximum neutron star mass, which led to the conclusion that low-mass black holes (just above 1.5 solar masses) may be common. The paper identifies a new nuclear structure effect, the C/O ratio from helium burning, as an additional determinant of supernova fate, building on the foundational work of Fowler on the anthropic principle and the triple-alpha process. The unresolved ambiguities in S_E1(300) and S_E2(300) must be addressed by ongoing experiments at HIgammaS and proposed facilities HIgammaS2 and ELI-NP before stellar evolution models can achieve the required 5% precision in the C/O ratio.
