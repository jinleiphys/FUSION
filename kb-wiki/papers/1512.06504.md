# Residue cross sections of $^{50}$Ti-induced fusion reactions based on the two-step model

> $^{50}$Ti-induced fusion reactions to synthesize superheavy elements are studied systematically with the two-step model developed recently, where fusion process is divided into approaching phase and formation phase. Furthermore, the residue cross sections for different neutron evaporation channels are evaluated with the statistical evaporation model. In general, the calculated cross sections are much smaller than that of $^{48}$Ca-induced fusion reactions, but the results are within the detection capability of experimental facilities nowadays. The maximum calculated residue cross section for producing superheavy element $Z=119$ is in the reaction $^{50}$Ti+$^{247}$Bk in $3n$ channels with $σ_{\rm res}(3n)=0.043$ pb at $E^{*}$ = 37.0 MeV.

# Residue cross sections of $^{50}$Ti-induced fusion reactions based on the two-step model

## Key claim
The calculated residue cross sections for $^{50}$Ti-induced fusion reactions to synthesize superheavy elements $Z=117-121$ are systematically smaller than those from $^{48}$Ca-induced reactions by nearly one order of magnitude, but the values for producing elements up to $Z=119$ remain within the current experimental detection limit of approximately 0.03 pb. The predictions depend on a fitted reduction factor $f$ applied to the shell correction energy in the fission barrier calculation, which was calibrated using available experimental data for $^{48}$Ca and $^{50}$Ti reactions.

## Method
The authors applied the two-step model, which divides the fusion process into a sticking (approaching) phase and a formation phase, to calculate fusion probabilities for $^{50}$Ti-induced reactions. The sticking probability was computed using an empirical Gaussian barrier distribution with parameters $C=0.095$ and a linear barrier shift $\Delta B = 1.225Z - 98.95$ fitted to experimental capture cross sections for $^{50}$Ti+$^{208}$Pb, $^{50}$Ti+$^{209}$Bi, and $^{50}$Ti+$^{244}$Pu. The survival probability after neutron evaporation was calculated using the HIVAP statistical evaporation code, with a reduction factor $f$ applied to the shell correction energy in the fission barrier ($B_f = B_{\text{LD}} - f \cdot E_{\text{shell}}$), where $f$ was determined by fitting experimental residue cross sections for $^{48}$Ca+$^{249}$Bk ($f=0.45$), $^{48}$Ca+$^{208}$Pb ($f=0.72$), and $^{50}$Ti+$^{208}$Pb ($f=0.77$), then extrapolated to $^{50}$Ti+$^{249}$Bk as $f=0.48$.

## Key numbers
- Maximum residue cross section for producing $Z=119$: $\sigma_{\text{res}}(3n) = 0.043$ pb at $E^* = 37.0$ MeV for $^{50}$Ti+$^{247}$Bk.
- Maximum residue cross section for producing $Z=118$: $\sigma_{\text{res}}(2n) = 0.053$ pb at $E^* = 28.0$ MeV for $^{50}$Ti+$^{244}$Cm, and $\sigma_{\text{res}}(3n) = 0.040$ pb at $E^* = 37.1$ MeV for $^{50}$Ti+$^{246}$Cm.
- Maximum residue cross section for producing $Z=117$: $\sigma_{\text{res}}(3n) = 0.044$ pb at $E^* = 37.3$ MeV for $^{50}$Ti+$^{243}$Am.
- Maximum residue cross section for producing $Z=120$: $\sigma_{\text{res}}(3n) = 0.012$ pb (12.2 fb) at $E^* = 35.3$ MeV for $^{50}$Ti+$^{252}$Cf.
- Maximum residue cross section for producing $Z=121$: $\sigma_{\text{res}}(2n) = 0.004$ pb (4 fb) at $E^* = 26.5$ MeV for $^{50}$Ti+$^{252}$Es.
- The experimental detection limit for residue cross sections is stated as approximately 0.03 pb (30 fb).
- For $^{50}$Ti+$^{249}$Bk producing $Z=119$, the present result (0.033 pb) is compared to 0.57 pb from the fusion-by-diffusion model (Siwek-Wilczynska et al.) and 0.11 pb from the dinuclear system model (Zagrebaev et al.).

## Context
This work extends the two-step model, previously applied to $^{48}$Ca-induced reactions by Shen et al. (2011), to $^{50}$Ti projectiles for synthesizing superheavy elements beyond $Z=118$, where $^{48}$Ca projectiles become impractical due to target availability. The calculated cross sections are compared with predictions from the fusion-by-diffusion model (Siwek-Wilczynska et al.) and the dinuclear system model (Zagrebaev et al.), showing generally lower values that are more consistent with the analytical expression of Liu and Bao (2013). The results indicate that while $^{50}$Ti-induced reactions can produce elements $Z=117-119$ within current experimental capabilities, the synthesis of $Z=120$ and $121$ will require future improvements in detection sensitivity, as the predicted cross sections fall below the present 30 fb limit.
