# Chaotic scattering on surfaces and collisional damping of collective modes

> The damping of hot giant dipole resonances is investigated. The contribution of surface scattering is compared with the contribution from interparticle collisions. A unified response function is presented which includes surface damping as well as collisional damping. The surface damping enters the response via the Lyapunov exponent and the collisional damping via the relaxation time. The former is calculated for different shape deformations of quadrupole and octupole type. The surface as well as the collisional contribution each reproduce almost the experimental value, therefore we propose a proper weighting between both contributions related to their relative occurrence due to collision frequencies between particles and of particles with the surface. We find that for low and high temperatures the collisional contribution dominates whereas the surface damping is dominant around the temperatures $\sqrt{3}/2π$ of the centroid energy.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that both collisional damping (from interparticle collisions) and surface damping (from chaotic scattering off the nuclear surface) each individually reproduce almost the experimental damping value of hot giant dipole resonances, but the correct physical description requires a proper weighting between both contributions based on their relative occurrence due to collision frequencies; the authors find that collisional damping dominates at low and very high temperatures, while surface damping is dominant around temperatures of \(\sqrt{3}/2\pi\) times the centroid energy.

## Method

The authors calculate the largest Lyapunov exponent for classical 3-D closed billiards with quadrupole and octupole deformations to characterize chaotic surface scattering, then derive a unified response function starting from a kinetic equation with a non-Markovian relaxation time approximation for collisional damping. They incorporate the Lyapunov exponent as an imaginary frequency shift in the Lindhard response function within a local density approximation, and weight the two damping mechanisms by the ratio of interparticle collision frequency to surface collision frequency.

## Key numbers

- The Lyapunov exponent for quadrupole deformation increases immediately with deformation, while for octupole deformation it increases more slowly and remains always smaller; the octupole deformation shows parts with negative curvature at smaller deformation parameters than quadrupole, but no significant enhancement of chaotization.
- Finite size scaling factors for collisional damping: \(\xi_{\rm Sn}=0.9199\) for \(^{120}\)Sn and \(\xi_{\rm Pb}=0.9250\) for \(^{208}\)Pb.
- The surface collision frequency is given by \(\nu_{\rm surf}=1.5 T n_0 v_F r_0^2/a_5\), independent of nucleus size and linearly dependent on temperature.
- The temperature where surface contributions become important is \(T_c={\sqrt{3} \over 2 \pi}\omega\), corresponding to approximately 3.7 MeV for the IVGDR.
- The weighting factor \(\zeta(T)\) equals 1 at zero and high temperatures, with a minimum at \(T_c\) where surface contributions are significant.

## Context

This work extends the debate between two lines of thought on damping mechanisms in excited nuclei: one assuming collisions as the physical reason for damping via Fermi liquid approaches (e.g., KAM69, SAB84, ATS94, KPS95, KPS96, KTO96, HNP96, TKL97, KLT97, FMW98, KLP98, MWF97, AYG98, GTO98), and the other considering surface oscillations and finite level density (e.g., SHB75, ESB83, ESB84, BBB91, OBB96, KST97, OBB97, KLL98, KIN98). The paper questions the hypothesis by Swiatecki that negative curved surfaces of octupole deformations would induce an additional chaotic mechanism of dissipation, finding instead that octupole deformation does not lead to any special enhancement of the Lyapunov exponent compared to quadrupole mode. The unified response function enables a description that reproduces experimental data for hot isovector giant dipole resonances across the accessible temperature range, with results comparable to the damping curve of Ormand (1997).
