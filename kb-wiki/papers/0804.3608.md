# Distinction between sequential and direct three-body decays

> We discuss the three-body decay mechanisms of many-body resonances. Sequential decays proceed via two-body configurations after emission of the third particle. In direct decay all three particles leave simultaneously their interaction regions. The intermediate paths within the interaction regions are not observables and only accessible through models. The momentum distributions carry, apart from polarization, all possible information about decay modes and resonance structure. In this context we discuss detailed results for the decay of the $^{9}$Be($5/2^-$) resonance.

# Distinction between sequential and direct three-body decays

## Key claim

The paper argues that the decay of the $^{9}$Be($5/2^-$) resonance is predominantly direct (not sequential), with only a small fraction (about 3%) proceeding via the $^{8}$Be($0^+$) intermediate state, and no decays via the $^{8}$Be($2^+$) or $^{5}$He($p$) structures, contradicting earlier experimental claims that 86% of the decay proceeds through the $^{8}$Be($2^+$) channel. The authors emphasize that intermediate paths within the interaction region are not observables and that mode separation becomes model-dependent when signatures are not distinctly different.

## Method

The authors use a three-body model with Faddeev equations and the hyperspherical expansion method combined with complex scaling to compute the resonance wave function of $^{9}$Be as an $\alpha+\alpha+n$ system. Two-body interactions reproduce low-energy $\alpha-\alpha$ and $n-\alpha$ scattering data, while a phenomenological three-body potential fine-tunes the resonance energy. The adiabatic potentials are computed as functions of the hyperradius $\rho$, and the wave function is analyzed at large distances to classify decay modes as direct or sequential based on the asymptotic behavior of the complex-scaled wave function.

## Key numbers

- The $^{9}$Be($5/2^-$) resonance lies at 0.856 MeV above the three-particle breakup threshold, with a width of $i 0.4$ keV (excitation energy 2.429 MeV).
- About 96% of the wave function at large distance maintains the structure related to the pocket at small distance (direct decay), while about 3% is located in the component related to the $^{8}$Be($0^+$) ground state structure.
- The most probable distance between the two $\alpha$-particles when the system emerges from underneath the barrier at $\rho = 30$ fm is about 18 fm, showing no spatial $^{8}$Be structure; for the $^{8}$Be($0^+$) component, the $\alpha$-$\alpha$ distance is about 3.3 fm independent of $\rho$.
- For the small $^{5}$He-like component, the distance between neutron and one $\alpha$-particle is about 2.8 fm while the distance to the other $\alpha$-particle is about 27 fm.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of three-body decay classification developed by Garrido (2007) and Alvarez-Rodriguez (2007), and directly addresses conflicting interpretations of $^{9}$Be($5/2^-$) decay data. The experimental investigation by Papka (2007) claimed that 86% of the decay proceeds via the $^{8}$Be($2^+$) channel, in agreement with Descouvemont (2001), but in contrast to Grigorenko (2005) who found a "democratic" (phase-space determined) decay of about 94% with a small $^{5}$He contribution. The present results support the direct decay picture and imply that reaction rates for astrophysical applications based on derived branching ratios via sequential channels are not meaningful or misleading. The geometric visualization method proposed here can be applied to other three-body decays, such as $^{12}$C decay into three $\alpha$-particles as studied by Alvarez-Rodriguez (2007).
