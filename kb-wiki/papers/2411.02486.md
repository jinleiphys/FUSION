# Scalable Quantum Simulations of Scattering in Scalar Field Theory on 120 Qubits

> Simulations of collisions of fundamental particles on a quantum computer are expected to have an exponential advantage over classical methods and promise to enhance searches for new physics. Furthermore, scattering in scalar field theory has been shown to be BQP-complete, making it a representative problem for which quantum computation is efficient. As a step toward large-scale quantum simulations of collision processes, scattering of wavepackets in one-dimensional scalar field theory is simulated using 120 qubits of IBM's Heron superconducting quantum computer ibm_fez. Variational circuits compressing vacuum preparation, wavepacket initialization, and time evolution are determined using classical resources. By leveraging physical properties of states in the theory, such as symmetries and locality, the variational quantum algorithm constructs scalable circuits that can be used to simulate arbitrarily-large system sizes. A new strategy is introduced to mitigate errors in quantum simulations, which enables the extraction of meaningful results from circuits with up to 4924 two-qubit gates and two-qubit gate depths of 103. The effect of interactions is clearly seen, and is found to be in agreement with classical Matrix Product State simulations. The developments that will be necessary to simulate high-energy inelastic collisions on a quantum computer are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that variational quantum circuits, informed by physical symmetries and locality, can simulate wavepacket scattering in one-dimensional scalar field theory on 120 qubits of IBM's Heron processor, with results in qualitative agreement with classical Matrix Product State simulations, but only for the specific case of low-energy elastic collisions where the interaction strength is small enough that the variational compression remains accurate.

## Method

The authors map a one-dimensional lattice scalar field theory with a quartic interaction term onto qubits using the Jordan-Lee-Preskill digitization, with 2 qubits per spatial site. They use a scalable variational circuit algorithm to compress vacuum preparation, wavepacket initialization, and time evolution into shallow brickwall circuits, optimizing parameters by minimizing local infidelity against classically computed target states for small system sizes and extrapolating to larger systems. The simulations are executed on IBM's ibm_fez superconducting quantum processor using 120 qubits, with a new error mitigation strategy based on simulating vacuum evolution to subtract hardware noise from the measured observables.

## Key numbers

- 120 qubits of IBM's Heron superconducting quantum computer ibm_fez were used for the scattering simulations
- Circuits contained up to 4924 two-qubit gates with two-qubit gate depths of 103
- One second-order Trotter step for n_q=2 can be implemented with a two-qubit gate depth of 20
- The digitization uses n_q=2 qubits per spatial site, giving 2^2 = 4 allowed field values per site
- The local infidelity metric I_d uses reduced density matrices spanning d spatial sites to measure state preparation quality

## Context

This work extends the program of Jordan, Lee, and Preskill (2011, 2012, 2018) on quantum simulations of scalar field theory scattering, building on prior variational approaches by Farrell et al. (2023, 2024) for state preparation and time evolution. It is the first demonstration of wavepacket scattering in an interacting quantum field theory on a quantum computer, and the techniques developed are intended to enable future simulations of high-energy inelastic scattering events in more complicated theories where classical methods based on Matrix Product States are expected to fail due to large entanglement generation.
