# Production of the entire range of r-process nuclides by black hole accretion disc outflows from neutron star mergers

> We consider $r$-process nucleosynthesis in outflows from black hole accretion discs formed in double neutron star and neutron star -- black hole mergers. These outflows, powered by angular momentum transport processes and nuclear recombination, represent an important -- and in some cases dominant -- contribution to the total mass ejected by the merger. Here we calculate the nucleosynthesis yields from disc outflows using thermodynamic trajectories from hydrodynamic simulations, coupled to a nuclear reaction network. We find that outflows produce a robust abundance pattern around the second $r$-process peak (mass number $A \sim 130$), independent of model parameters, with significant production of $A < 130$ nuclei. This implies that dynamical ejecta with high electron fraction may not be required to explain the observed abundances of $r$-process elements in metal poor stars. Disc outflows reach the third peak ($ A \sim 195$) in most of our simulations, although the amounts produced depend sensitively on the disc viscosity, initial mass or entropy of the torus, and nuclear physics inputs. Some of our models produce an abundance spike at $A = 132$ that is absent in the Solar system $r$-process distribution. The spike arises from convection in the disc and depends on the treatment of nuclear heating in the simulations. We conclude that disc outflows provide an important -- and perhaps dominant -- contribution to the $r$-process yields of compact binary mergers, and hence must be included when assessing the contribution of these systems to the inventory of $r$-process elements in the Galaxy.

## Key claim

Disc outflows from black hole accretion discs formed in neutron star mergers can produce the entire range of r-process nuclides from mass number A ~ 80 to uranium and thorium, with a robust abundance pattern around the second r-process peak (A ~ 130) that is independent of model parameters, though the production of third-peak elements (A ~ 195) depends sensitively on disc viscosity, initial mass or entropy of the torus, and nuclear physics inputs.

## Method

The authors evolved long-term hydrodynamic simulations of black hole accretion discs in axisymmetric spherical polar coordinates using the FLASH3 code, including an alpha viscosity prescription, a pseudo-Newtonian potential, and neutrino emission and absorption via a leakage scheme. They inserted 10,000 passive tracer particles per model to extract thermodynamic trajectories (density, temperature, electron fraction) and coupled these to a nuclear reaction network containing 7,360 nuclei from nucleons to 313Ds, including fission and charged-current neutrino interactions on free nucleons.

## Key numbers

- Baseline model S-def produces ejecta with average electron fraction at T=5 GK of 0.237, average entropy of 19.9 kB per baryon, and average final mass number of 119
- Ejected mass fraction ranges from 1.36% (model alpha0.01) to 18.05% (model chi0.8) of the initial disc mass
- Disc outflow velocities lie in the range (0.2-1.6) x 10^9 cm/s, much slower than typical dynamical ejecta
- Total lanthanide plus actinide mass fraction at 1 day ranges from 0.25% (model alpha0.01) to 8.85% (model alpha0.10)
- Average expansion timescale at T=5 GK ranges from 52 ms (model alpha0.10) to 227 ms (model M10)
- The 132-peak anomaly (A=132 abundance spike) appears in 11.8% of trajectories in the baseline model, ranging from 0% to 24.2% across models

## Context

This work extends the nucleosynthesis calculations of Just (2014) by systematically varying disc parameters and including nuclear heating feedback, and contrasts with earlier studies of dynamical ejecta from mergers (e.g., Goriely 2011, Korobkin 2012, Bauswein 2013) that focused on highly neutron-rich material with electron fraction below 0.1. The results imply that disc outflows may provide a dominant contribution to r-process yields from compact binary mergers, potentially explaining the observed abundances in metal-poor stars without requiring high electron fraction dynamical ejecta as suggested by Wanajo (2014). The findings enable more realistic predictions for kilonova emission and Galactic chemical evolution models that must now include both dynamical ejecta and disc outflows.
