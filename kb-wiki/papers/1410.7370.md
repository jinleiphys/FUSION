# Universality in one-dimensional fermions at finite temperature: Density, pressure, compressibility, and contact

> We present finite-temperature, lattice Monte Carlo calculations of the particle number density, compressibility, pressure, and Tan's contact of an unpolarized system of short-range, attractively interacting spin-1/2 fermions in one spatial dimension, i.e., the Gaudin-Yang model. In addition, we compute the second-order virial coefficients for the pressure and the contact, both of which are in excellent agreement with the lattice results in the low-fugacity regime. Our calculations yield universal predictions for ultracold atomic systems with broad resonances in highly constrained traps. We cover a wide range of couplings and temperatures and find results that support the existence of a strong-coupling regime in which the thermodynamics of the system is markedly different from the noninteracting case. We compare and contrast our results with identical systems in higher dimensions.

## Key claim

The paper presents finite-temperature lattice Monte Carlo calculations of the thermodynamics of the Gaudin-Yang model (one-dimensional, attractively interacting spin-1/2 fermions) and claims universal predictions for ultracold atomic systems with broad resonances in highly constrained traps, but the results are limited to unpolarized systems and the universality is conditional on the interaction being well approximated by a contact interaction in dilute regimes.

## Method

The authors placed a two-species fermion system on a Euclidean space-time lattice of extent N_x x N_tau with periodic spatial and anti-periodic temporal boundary conditions, using a Trotter-Suzuki decomposition followed by a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to write the grand-canonical partition function as a path integral over an auxiliary field. They evaluated the path integral using Metropolis-based Monte Carlo methods, taking 5000 de-correlated samples per data point to achieve statistical uncertainties of order 3-4 percent. The calculations covered lattice sizes from N_x = 50 to 100, with N_x = 81 used for the main results, and the continuum limit was approached by increasing beta while keeping lambda^2 = beta g^2 fixed.

## Key numbers

- Density n/n_0 shows maximal deviation from non-interacting behavior around beta mu = ln z approximately -1 for all lambda > 0, with the effect more pronounced at larger lambda
- Statistical uncertainty of order 3-4 percent from 5000 de-correlated samples per data point
- Temporal lattice spacing tau = 0.05 (in lattice units) chosen to balance discretization effects with computational efficiency
- Thermal wavelength lambda_T approximately 3.5 to 7.0 used in calculations
- Second-order virial coefficient b_2 ranges from -0.035 at lambda = 1.0 to 3.61 at lambda = 3.0 (lattice results)
- Leading-order contact virial coefficient c_2 ranges from 0.63 at lambda = 1.0 to 57.7 at lambda = 3.0 (lattice results)
- Fit parameter alpha for density equation of state ranges from 0.24(1) at lambda = 1.0 to 1.525(1) at lambda = 3.0
- Fit parameter gamma for density equation of state ranges from 0.46(6) at lambda = 1.0 to 0.76(1) at lambda = 3.0
- For the contact at large beta mu, zeta_1 = 0.35(1) is nearly lambda-independent, while zeta_2(lambda) approximately equals a + b lambda with a = -0.34(1) and b = 0.701(5) at beta mu = 10
- Analytic continuum result for b_2: b_2 = -1/sqrt(2) + exp(lambda^2/4)/(2 sqrt(2)) [1 + erf(lambda/2)]
- Analytic continuum result for c_2: c_2 = lambda^2/4 + (sqrt(pi)/8) exp(lambda^2/4) lambda^3 [1 + erf(lambda/2)]
- Largest systematic uncertainties due to finite beta are on the order of 10 percent, most prominent around the maximum in n/n_0 at strongest couplings (lambda = 4)

## Context

This work extends the finite-temperature Monte Carlo approach previously applied to three-dimensional unitary Fermi gases by Drut and collaborators (2012) and to two-dimensional systems by Enss (2012), now applied to one-dimensional fermions. The results provide a benchmark for many-body methods, as the Gaudin-Yang model is exactly solvable via the Bethe ansatz at zero temperature (Batchelor and collaborators, Takahashi) but finite-temperature studies require the thermodynamic Bethe ansatz with uncontrolled truncations. The calculations enable predictions for ultracold atom experiments in highly elongated optical traps using modulated potentials, and are readily generalizable to higher numbers of fermion species expected to become experimentally available.
