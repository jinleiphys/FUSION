# Comment on a recent attempt to explain the `GSI anomaly' by initial-state e.m. spin-rotation coupling

## Key claim
The proposed solution of the GSI anomaly by spin precession of decaying heavy ions in the storage ring magnetic field (Lambiase, Papini, Scarpetta, 2013) is dubious because the uncertainty in the computed electron-capture decay-rate modulation frequency is at least of order 10^7 Hz, far exceeding the 1 Hz modulation frequency reported in the GSI experiment.

## Method
The author examines the spin precession frequency formula proposed by Lambiase et al., which combines contributions from the electron magnetic moment and the nuclear magnetic moment with coefficients depending on the relativistic Lorentz factor of the electron relative to the nucleus and the nuclear gyromagnetic ratio. The author then evaluates the required precision in these parameters to achieve the claimed cancellation that would bring the precession frequency down to the observed ~1 Hz level, comparing these requirements against the actual experimental knowledge of the parameters.

## Key numbers
- The electron magnetic moment contribution to the precession frequency is of order |μ_e| B ≈ 3.3 × 10^10 Hz for B ≈ 1.2 T at the GSI storage ring.
- The nuclear magnetic moment contribution is of order μ_p B ≈ 2.5 × 10^7 Hz.
- The reported modulation period is ~7 s, corresponding to a frequency P_GSI ~ 1 Hz.
- To achieve cancellation, (γ_{e|n} - 1) must be known accurately to at least three digits, and the nuclear gyromagnetic ratio g_nucl must be known accurately to at least seven digits.
- In practice, (γ_{e|n} - 1) is estimated semiclassically to a single digit only, and g_nucl(^122I) = 0.94 ± 0.03 (Green, 1986), known to a single digit only.
- A 0.1% variation of γ_{e|n} induces approximately 4 × 10^7 Hz variation in the computed frequency.

## Context
This comment responds to the proposal by Lambiase, Papini, and Scarpetta (2013) in Physics Letters B and Annals of Physics, which attempted to explain the GSI anomaly, the ~7 s modulation of electron-capture decay rates observed in hydrogen-like heavy ions at the GSI Experimental Storage Ring (GSI, 2008). Earlier work by Faber (2010) and Pavli (2010) had concluded that spin precession could not explain the modulation due to the large frequencies involved. The comment also notes that implanted neutral atoms not exposed to external magnetic fields show no evidence for GSI-like modulation (Vetter, 2008; Faestermann, 2009). The author concludes that magnetic moments of order 10^{-11} μ_B, many orders of magnitude below atomic and nuclear moments, would be required to explain the anomaly by spin precession (Gal, 2010).
