# The 115 GeV signal from nuclear physics

> According standard models of nuclei, steepest variations of binding energy at the drip lines should happen for nuclear masses of 45, 68, 92, 115, 175 and 246 GeV. We explore the coincidence of these masses with another ones from well known HEP research and we wonder how much, of it, is an experimental measurement coming from low energy research.

# The 115 GeV signal from nuclear physics

## Key claim

The paper suggests that the series of nuclear masses where steepest variations of binding energy occur at the drip lines (45, 68, 92, 115, 175, and 246 GeV) coincides with masses of known or suspected elementary particles, but the author explicitly cannot assess how biased the selection of these particular masses is, noting that an exhaustive list of deviations from background for the 1-300 GeV range is lacking.

## Method

The author studies the difference between proton and neutron drip line masses using the classical Weizsacker semi-empirical mass formula with standard parameter values (a1=15.75 MeV, a2=17.8 MeV, a3=0.711 MeV, a4=23.0 MeV), solving the second-degree equation for the proton drip line and the third-degree equation for the stability line to obtain the corresponding stable nucleus mass. The resulting function d(A0)=N-N0 is analyzed numerically, and the correspondence between magic numbers at the proton and neutron drip lines is tabulated. The author then compares these nuclear mass values (converted to GeV using 1 u = 0.9315 GeV) with known particle masses and historical experimental signals from LEP2, UA1, and L3.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear masses at drip lines: approximately 44, 68, 91.2, 115, 175, and 246 GeV (corresponding to approximately 47, 73, 98, 123, 187, and 264 atomic mass units)
- Standard Weizsacker parameters used: a1 = 15.75 MeV, a2 = 17.8 MeV, a3 = 0.711 MeV, a4 = 23.0 MeV
- The function d(A0) reaches its highest value for A0 approximately 300, with a value of -0.815 when proton and neutron masses are equal
- The equidistance property k=k' holds within 2% for mid-range masses
- Conversion constant: 1 u = 0.9315 GeV

## Context

This paper extends the observation that historical experimental signals at CERN (the 40-45 GeV deviation from UA1 in 1984, the 68-70 GeV deviation from L3 around 1998, and the 115 GeV deviation from ALEPH at LEP2 closure) coincide with nuclear mass values predicted by the Weizsacker formula at the drip lines. The author proposes a "Lamb's Balance Conjecture" as a theoretical framework where recoil corrections to bound states of a relativistic two-body system could explain how elementary particle masses influence nuclear magic numbers at the drip lines. The work builds on the FRDM (1992) and other nuclear mass models from Uno et al., and suggests that if the signals are taken seriously, they point toward a non-minimal Higgs sector (2DHM, Zee models) rather than the Minimal Standard Model or MSSM, and could be compatible with dimensional deconstruction models by Georgi et al.
