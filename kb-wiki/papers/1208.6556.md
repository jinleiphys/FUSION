# Lattice methods for strongly interacting many-body systems

> Lattice field theory methods, usually associated with non-perturbative studies of quantum chromodynamics, are becoming increasingly common in the calculation of ground-state and thermal properties of strongly interacting non-relativistic few- and many-body systems, blurring the interfaces between condensed matter, atomic and low-energy nuclear physics. While some of these techniques have been in use in the area of condensed matter physics for a long time, others, such as hybrid Monte Carlo and improved effective actions, have only recently found their way across areas. With this topical review, we aim to provide a modest overview and a status update on a few notable recent developments. For the sake of brevity we focus on zero-temperature, non-relativistic problems. After a short introduction, we lay out some general considerations and proceed to discuss sampling algorithms, observables, and systematic effects. We show selected results on ground- and excited-state properties of fermions in the limit of unitarity. The appendix contains details on group theory on the lattice.

# Lattice methods for strongly interacting many-body systems

## Key claim

Lattice field theory methods, traditionally used in quantum chromodynamics, are increasingly applicable to non-relativistic few- and many-body systems at zero temperature, but the practical viability of these methods depends critically on overcoming signal-to-noise problems and overlap problems that grow exponentially with Euclidean time, particularly for systems with attractive interactions where bound states in the variance degrade the signal.

## Method

The authors review several Monte Carlo algorithms for sampling auxiliary-field configurations in non-relativistic lattice calculations: Determinantal Monte Carlo (DMC), which uses localized Metropolis updates; Hybrid Monte Carlo (HMC), which introduces fictitious momentum fields and molecular dynamics for global updates; heat-bath approaches that exploit open boundary conditions to make the fermion determinant identically equal to 1; and open-ended imaginary-time evolution with re-weighting and branching random walks. They analyze the signal-to-noise ratio using the Lepage prescription, showing that the noise is controlled by the ground-state energy of a two-particle system with attractive interactions, and discuss the cumulant expansion as an alternative to importance sampling for mitigating distribution overlap problems.

## Key numbers

- DMC cost per sweep scales as \(N N^{2}_{\tau} V^3\), where \(N\) is the number of particles, \(N^{}_{\tau}\) is the number of lattice points in imaginary time, and \(V = N^{3}_{x}\) is the spatial volume; with Fourier acceleration this becomes \(N N^{2}_{\tau} V^2 \log V\)
- HMC cost per sweep scales as \(N N^{}_{\tau} V^2\), or \(N N^{}_{\tau} V \log V\) with Fourier acceleration
- The rule of thumb for acceptance rate in DMC and branching algorithms is 40-60%
- For the single-particle correlator in a two-flavor theory with attractive interaction, the signal-to-noise ratio at large Euclidean time \(\beta\) behaves as \(\sqrt{N_{\mbox{cfg}}} (Z_0^{(1)}/\sqrt{Z_0^{(2)}}) e^{\beta/2 E_0^{(2)}}\), where \(E_0^{(2)} < 0\) for an attractive bound state
- The number of configurations required for the central limit theorem to hold must satisfy \(N_\mathrm{cfg} \gg e^{\beta (2E_0^{(3)}-3E_0^{(2)})}\) when the third moment is considered
- Results are shown for 50 unitary fermions in a harmonic trap using \(10^6\) configurations with cumulant expansion truncated at order \(n^{}_{max}=4\)

## Context

This topical review extends the lattice field theory methodology from Lattice QCD (pioneered by Creutz, Cabibbo and Marinari, and others) into non-relativistic strongly correlated systems, bridging condensed matter, atomic physics, and low-energy nuclear physics. The heat-bath approach with open boundary conditions builds on work by Lee and collaborators (2006-2012) and eliminates the fermion sign problem for zero-temperature calculations, though it introduces an overlap problem that requires large numbers of configurations (approximately \(10^8\)). The open-ended imaginary-time evolution with branching random walks was first developed by PhysRevLett.74.3652 and PhysRevB.55.7464 in the mid-1990s. The signal-to-noise analysis follows the Lepage prescription (1989) for nucleon correlators in QCD, and the cumulant expansion method was demonstrated for unitary fermions by Nicholson and collaborators (2012). The review enables future calculations of ground-state properties of fermions at unitarity and other strongly interacting systems, with the key open question being how to systematically control the exponential degradation of signal at large Euclidean times.
