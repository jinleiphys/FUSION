# Towards the chiral phase transition in the Roberge-Weiss plane

> We discuss the interplay between chiral and center sector phase transitions that occur in QCD with an imaginary quark chemical potential $μ=i(2n+1) πT/3$. Based on a finite size scaling analysis in (2+1)-flavor QCD using HISQ fermions with a physical strange quark mass and a range of light quark masses, we show that the endpoint of the line of first-order Roberge-Weiss (RW) transitions between center sectors is second order for light quark masses $m_l\ge m_s/320$, and that it belongs to the $3$-d, $Z(2)$ universality class. The operator for the chiral condensate behaves like an energy-like operator in an effective spin model for the RW phase transition. As a consequence, for any non-zero value of the quark mass, the chiral condensate will have an infinite slope at the RW phase transition temperature, $T_{RW}$. Its fluctuation, the disconnected chiral susceptibility, behaves like the specific heat in $Z(2)$ symmetric models and diverges in the infinite volume limit at the RW phase transition temperature $T_{RW}$ for any non-zero value of the light quark masses. Our analysis suggests the critical temperatures for the RW phase transition and the chiral phase transition coincide in the RW plane. On lattices with temporal extent $N_τ=4$, we find in the chiral limit $T_χ=T_{RW}=195(1)~$MeV.

## Key claim

The endpoint of the first-order Roberge-Weiss (RW) transition line in (2+1)-flavor QCD with an imaginary chemical potential is second order for light quark masses \(m_l \ge m_s/320\) and belongs to the 3-d, \(Z(2)\) universality class, but this conclusion is explicitly limited to lattices with temporal extent \(N_\tau=4\) and no continuum extrapolation is performed.

## Method

The authors performed lattice QCD simulations using the HISQ action with a physical strange quark mass and light-to-strange mass ratios \(H = m_l/m_s\) in the range \(1/320\) to \(1/27\), corresponding to pion masses from about 40 MeV to 135 MeV. They carried out finite-size scaling analyses of the order parameter \(M\) (related to the Polyakov loop), its susceptibility \(\chi_M\), and cumulant ratios \(K_2\) and \(B_4\) on lattices of size \(N_\sigma^3 \times 4\) with \(N_\sigma = 16, 24, 32\), fitting the data to universal scaling functions of the 3-d, \(Z(2)\) universality class obtained from an improved Ising model.

## Key numbers

* The RW transition endpoint temperature for \(H=1/27\) is \(T_{RW} = 202.4(1)\) MeV on \(N_\tau=4\) lattices.
* The RW transition endpoint temperature for \(H=1/160\) is \(T_{RW} = 197.3(1)\) MeV on \(N_\tau=4\) lattices.
* The RW transition endpoint temperature for \(H=1/320\) is \(T_{RW} = 196.4(1)\) MeV on \(N_\tau=4\) lattices.
* In the chiral limit, the critical temperature for both the chiral and RW phase transitions is \(T_\chi = T_{RW} = 195(1)\) MeV on \(N_\tau=4\) lattices.
* The Binder cumulant crossing point at the critical temperature is \(B_4(T_{RW},\infty) = 1.606(2)\) for the 3-d, \(Z(2)\) universality class.
* The ratio \(K_2\) at the critical temperature in the infinite volume limit is \(K_2(T_{RW},\infty) = 0.240(3)\) for the 3-d, \(Z(2)\) universality class.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the Roberge-Weiss endpoint order as a function of quark mass, building on earlier studies by de Forcrand and Philipsen (2010) and Philipsen and Pinke (2014) that mapped the phase diagram in the imaginary chemical potential plane. The finding that the RW endpoint is second order for \(m_l \ge m_s/320\) and belongs to the 3-d, \(Z(2)\) universality class, together with the observation that the chiral condensate behaves like an energy-like operator at the RW transition, implies that the chiral and RW critical temperatures coincide in the RW plane. This enables future studies to use the RW plane as a controlled setting for investigating the chiral phase transition, and the authors note that their \(T_{RW}\) value at \(H=1/27\) is consistent with scaling of the quark number density and Lee-Yang edge singularities extracted by analytic continuation to real chemical potential.
