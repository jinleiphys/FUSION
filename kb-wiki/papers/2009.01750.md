# Electron capture in stars

> Electron captures on nuclei play an essential role for the dynamics of several astrophysical objects. The capture rate can be derived in perturbation theory where allowed nuclear transitions (Gamow-Teller transitions) dominate, except at the higher temperatures achieved in core-collapse supernovae where also forbidden transitions contribute significantly to the rates. There has been decisive progress in recent years in measuring Gamow-Teller (GT) strength distributions using novel experimental techniques based on charge-exchange reactions. These measurements provide not only data for the GT distributions of ground states for many relevant nuclei, but also serve as valuable constraints for nuclear models which are needed to derive the capture rates for the many nuclei, for which no data exist yet. In particular models are needed to evaluate the stellar capture rates at finite temperatures, where the capture can also occur on excited nuclear states. There has also been significant progress in recent years in the modelling of stellar capture rates. This has been made possible by advances in nuclear many-body models as well as in computer soft- and hardware. Specifically to derive reliable capture rates for core-collapse supernovae a dedicated strategy has been developed based on a hierarchy of nuclear models specifically adapted to the abundant nuclei and astrophysically conditions present at the various collapse conditions. This manuscript reviews the experimental and theoretical progress achieved recently in deriving stellar electron capture rates. It also discusses the impact these improved rates have on the various astrophysical objects. (Abridged)

## Key claim

Electron capture rates in stars can be derived in perturbation theory where allowed Gamow-Teller transitions dominate, except at the higher temperatures achieved in core-collapse supernovae where forbidden transitions also contribute significantly; the review emphasizes that experimental measurements of Gamow-Teller strength distributions serve as constraints for nuclear models needed to derive rates for the many nuclei where no data exist, particularly at finite temperatures where capture can occur on excited nuclear states.

## Method

The authors review experimental techniques based on charge-exchange reactions at intermediate beam energies (above 100 MeV), including (n,p), (d,2He), and (t,3He) reactions, which extract Gamow-Teller strength distributions through the proportionality between differential cross sections at zero momentum transfer and transition strengths calibrated against known beta-decay transitions. For theoretical modeling, they describe a hierarchy of approaches: large-scale diagonalization shell model calculations for nuclei in the mass range A=20-65, a hybrid model combining Shell Model Monte Carlo calculations of partial occupation numbers with RPA calculations for nuclei in the mass range A=65-110, and a parametric study for about 2700 heavier nuclei, all validated against experimental charge-exchange data where available.

## Key numbers

- Electron capture becomes relevant at densities above about 10^9 g cm^-3 and temperatures ranging from 10^8 K in electron-capture supernovae to above 10^10 K in advanced core collapse of massive stars
- The Ikeda sum rule connects Gamow-Teller strengths in the beta-minus and beta-plus directions: S_beta-(GT) - S_beta+(GT) = 3(N-Z)
- Only about 50-60% of the sum-rule strength is observed in the Gamow-Teller resonance at excitation energies below about 20 MeV, referred to as the quenching phenomenon
- Experimental systematic uncertainties in Gamow-Teller strength extraction are reduced to about 10% through relative normalization techniques
- The uncertainty in Gamow-Teller strength extraction due to tensor force interference is approximately 0.03-0.035 ln(B(GT)), amounting to about 20% uncertainty for B(GT)=0.01
- The hybrid model has been applied to about 200 nuclei in the mass range A=65-110, and a parametric study based on smooth trends in occupation numbers at about 1 MeV temperature yielded rates for another 2700 nuclei

## Context

This review extends the pioneering work of Bethe, Brown, Applegate, and Lattimer (1979) who derived capture rates based on a single Gamow-Teller transition in the Independent Particle Model, and the subsequent rate tables of Fuller, Fowler, and Newman (1980-1985) for nuclei in the mass range A=21-60. It supersedes the Bruenn (1985) prescription that assumed vanishing capture rates for nuclei with neutron numbers N>40 due to Pauli blocking, which was the default in supernova simulations until the early 2000s. The improved rates, based on shell model calculations for sd and pf shell nuclei and the hybrid model for heavier nuclei, have been incorporated into supernova simulations showing that capture on nuclei dominates over capture on free protons during the entire collapse, leading to stronger deleptonization and smaller temperatures and entropies compared to previous simulations. The review also discusses implications for the fate of O-Ne-Mg cores in intermediate-mass stars and for nucleosynthesis in thermonuclear supernovae, noting that the electron capture on 20Ne constitutes an unusual case dominated by a second-forbidden ground-state-ground-state transition recently measured experimentally by Kirsebom, Jones, and collaborators (2019).
