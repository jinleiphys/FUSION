# Prolate dominance of nuclear shape caused by a strong interference between the effects of spin-orbit and l^2 terms of the Nilsson potential

> The origin of the dominance of prolate shapes over oblate ones of the ground states of atomic nuclei is investigated with the Nilsson-Strutinsky method. The number of prolate nuclei among all the deformed even-even nuclei is calculated as a function of the strengths of the spin-orbit and the l^2 terms of the Nilsson potential. The latter simulates a square-well like radial profile of the mean potential. The ratio of prolate nuclei is 86% with the standard strengths corresponding to the actual atomic nuclei. By weakening the spin-orbit potential, the ratio oscillates strongly, having a local minimum value of 45% with a half of the standard strength and a local maximum value of 78% without the spin-orbit potential.

## Key claim

The prolate dominance of nuclear ground-state shapes (86% of well-deformed even-even nuclei) arises from a strong interference between the spin-orbit and l^2 terms of the Nilsson potential, not from either term acting independently; weakening the spin-orbit strength by 50% actually reverses the dominance to produce more oblate than prolate nuclei (45% prolate).

## Method

The authors performed Nilsson-Strutinsky calculations for 1843 even-even nuclei between the proton and neutron drip lines, scanning reduction factors f_ll (for the l^2 term) and f_ls (for the spin-orbit term) over a square grid from -1 to 1 in steps of 0.125. For each combination of reduction factors, they computed potential energy surfaces versus quadrupole deformation epsilon_2 (with optimized hexadecapole deformation epsilon_4) and classified ground states as prolate or oblate only when both well-developed minima existed, using a smearing procedure to separate valleys. The ratio of prolate nuclei R_p was defined as the number of prolate ground states divided by the total number of nuclei with both prolate and oblate minima.

## Key numbers

- With standard strengths (f_ll = f_ls = 1), the ratio of prolate nuclei R_p = 86% among approximately 900 even-even nuclei having both prolate and oblate minima.
- At f_ll = 1 and f_ls = 0.5 (half the standard spin-orbit strength), R_p drops to 45% (more oblate than prolate nuclei).
- At f_ll = 1 and f_ls = 0 (no spin-orbit term), R_p rises to 78%.
- At f_ll = 1 and f_ls = -0.5, R_p = 44%; at f_ll = 1 and f_ls = -1, R_p = 81%.
- The minimum R_p across the entire parameter space is 40% at (f_ll, f_ls) = (-1, -0.125).
- For a pure harmonic oscillator (f_ls = f_ll = epsilon_4 = 0 with weakened pairing), R_p = 55%.
- Including hexadecapole deformation increases R_p from 82% to 86% at the standard point.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of prolate dominance by Frisk (1990), who attributed the effect to the square-well-like radial profile of the mean potential, and by Tajima, Takahara, and Onishi (1996), who observed a correlation between prolate dominance and the appearance of high-j intruder orbitals from the spin-orbit potential. The results show that the prolate dominance of actual nuclei arises from a specific combination of the l^2 and spin-orbit strengths that coincides with the conditions for pseudo-spin symmetry discussed by Hecht and Adler (1969) and Arima, Harvey, and Shimizu (1969). The findings enable future studies of how these terms might change in neutron-rich unstable nuclei and comparisons with Woods-Saxon potential calculations.
