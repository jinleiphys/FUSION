# Nuclear Equation of state for Compact Stars and Supernovae

> The equation of state (EoS) of hot and dense matter is a fundamental input to describe static and dynamical properties of neutron stars, core-collapse supernovae and binary compact-star mergers. We review the current status of the EoS for compact objects, that have been studied with both ab-initio many-body approaches and phenomenological models. We limit ourselves to the description of EoSs with purely nucleonic degrees of freedom, disregarding the appearance of strange baryonic matter and/or quark matter. We compare the theoretical predictions with different data coming from both nuclear physics experiments and astrophysical observations. Combining the complementary information thus obtained greatly enriches our insights into the dense nuclear matter properties. Current challenges in the description of the EoS are also discussed, mainly focusing on the model dependence of the constraints extracted from either experimental or observational data (specifically, concerning the symmetry energy), the lack of a consistent and rigorous many-body treatment at zero and finite temperature of the matter encountered in compact stars (e.g. problem of cluster formation and extension of the EoS to very high temperatures), the role of nucleonic three-body forces, and the dependence of the direct URCA processes on the EoS.

## Key claim

This review paper surveys the current status of nuclear equations of state for compact stars and supernovae, but explicitly limits its scope to purely nucleonic degrees of freedom, disregarding strange baryonic matter and quark matter. The authors note that current challenges include model dependence of constraints extracted from experimental and observational data, particularly concerning the symmetry energy, and the lack of a consistent many-body treatment at zero and finite temperature.

## Method

The authors review both ab-initio many-body approaches (Bethe-Brueckner-Goldstone expansion, Dirac-Brueckner Hartree-Fock, variational methods, chiral effective field theory, self-consistent Green's function) and phenomenological models (self-consistent mean-field models using Skyrme or Gogny interactions, relativistic models with baryon and meson fields, and liquid-drop models). They compare theoretical predictions with data from nuclear physics experiments and astrophysical observations, discussing constraints from both sources.

## Key numbers

- The approximate range of thermodynamic variables for the most general EoS is: \(10^{-11} \lesssim n_B \lesssim\) few fm\(^{-3}\), \(0 \leq T \lesssim 150\) MeV, and \(0 < Y_e < 0.6\)
- Nuclear statistical equilibrium is typically achieved for temperatures \(T \gtrsim 0.5\) MeV
- Electron-capture reactions are not in equilibrium for baryon densities below \(n_B \approx 10^{-3} - 10^{-4}\) fm\(^{-3}\) (or mass-energy densities below \(\rho_B \approx\) few \(10^{11}\) g cm\(^{-3}\))
- For cold neutron stars, temperature is below 1 MeV, making the zero-temperature approximation valid
- The safe maximum density for chiral perturbation theory applications is around the saturation value \(n_0\)
- The fss2 quark-model interaction reproduces the triton binding energy within 300 keV
- In symmetric nuclear matter, three-body forces produce a shift in binding energy of about +1 MeV and of -0.01 fm\(^{-3}\) in density

## Context

This review extends the line of work on nuclear equations of state for compact objects, comparing results from multiple many-body methods including the Bethe-Brueckner-Goldstone approach, Dirac-Brueckner Hartree-Fock, variational methods (notably the Akmal-Pandharipande-Ravenhall EoS from 1998), and chiral effective field theory. It discusses constraints from both nuclear physics experiments and astrophysical observations, and identifies open challenges including the model dependence of symmetry energy constraints, the problem of cluster formation at finite temperature, the role of three-body forces, and the dependence of direct URCA processes on the EoS. The paper enables future work by providing a comprehensive overview of available EoSs and their applications in core-collapse supernovae, neutron star structure, and binary merger simulations.
