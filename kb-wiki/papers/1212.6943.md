# Relic neutrinos: Physically consistent treatment of effective number of neutrinos and neutrino mass

> We perform a model independent study of the neutrino momentum distribution at freeze-out, treating the freeze-out temperature as a free parameter. Our results imply that measurement of neutrino reheating, as characterized by the measurement of the effective number of neutrinos $N_ν$, amounts to the determination of the neutrino kinetic freeze-out temperature within the context of the standard model of particle physics where the number of neutrino flavors is fixed and no other massless (fractional) particles arise. At temperatures on the order of the neutrino mass, we show how cosmic background neutrino properties i.e. energy density, pressure, particle density, are modified in a physically consistent way as a function of neutrino mass and $N_ν$.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the measured non-integer effective number of neutrinos \(N_\nu\) can be interpreted as a determination of the neutrino kinetic freeze-out temperature \(T_k\), treated as a free parameter, within the standard model of particle physics where the number of neutrino flavors is fixed at three and no other massless particles arise.

## Method

The authors perform a model-independent parametric study by treating the neutrino kinetic freeze-out temperature \(T_k\) as a free parameter, distinct from the chemical freeze-out temperature \(T_{ch}\). They use conservation of comoving neutrino number and conservation of entropy in the \(e^\pm,\gamma,\nu\) plasma between chemical and kinetic freeze-out to solve for the neutrino fugacity \(\Upsilon_\nu\) and the photon-to-neutrino temperature ratio as functions of \(T_k\). The resulting non-equilibrium Fermi-Dirac distribution is then evolved using the Einstein-Vlasov equation for free-streaming neutrinos in a flat FRW spacetime.

## Key numbers

* The standard theoretical effective number of neutrinos after reheating is \(N_{\nu}^{\rm th}=3.046\) (Mangano 2005).
* Planck data (CMB only) gives \(N_\nu=3.36\pm 0.34\); with a prior from the Hubble constant, Planck gives \(N_\nu=3.62\pm 0.25\).
* Nine-year WMAP observations give \(N_\nu=3.55^{+0.49}_{-0.48}\) at BBN; an independent BBN study gives \(N_\nu=3.71^{+0.47}_{-0.45}\) (Steigman 2012).
* The photon-to-neutrino temperature ratio after reheating is given by \(T_\gamma/T_\nu = a \Upsilon_\nu^{b} (1 + c\sigma^2 + O(\sigma^3))\) with \(a = (11/4)^{1/3} \approx 1.4010\), \(b \approx 0.367\), and \(c \approx -0.0209\).
* A numerical fit from Birrell (2013) gives \(\Upsilon_\nu = 0.420 (T_\gamma/T_\nu)^{2.57}\) to within 1% over the range \(0.4 \leq \Upsilon_\nu \leq 1\).

## Context

This work extends the standard treatment of neutrino freeze-out based on the Einstein-Boltzmann equation with two-body scattering (Lopez 1998, Gnedin 1997, Mangano 2005) by introducing a distinct kinetic freeze-out temperature as a free parameter, motivated by the possibility that additional interactions (e.g., neutrino-plasmon scattering) could delay freeze-out. The approach uses methods developed for particle freeze-out in quark-gluon plasma hadronization (Letessier 2005, Kuznetsova 2006). The results enable a self-consistent study of the combined effects of a non-integer effective number of neutrinos and nonzero neutrino masses on cosmological observables, a problem noted as difficult by Lesgourgues (book).
