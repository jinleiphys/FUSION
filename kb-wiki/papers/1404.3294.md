# Relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> The field of relativistic heavy-ion collisions is introduced to the high-energy physics students with no prior knowledge in this area. The emphasis is on the two most important observables, namely the azimuthal collective flow and jet quenching, and on the role fluid dynamics plays in the interpretation of the data. Other important observables described briefly are constituent quark number scaling, ratios of particle abundances, strangeness enhancement, and sequential melting of heavy quarkonia. Comparison is made of some of the basic heavy-ion results obtained at LHC with those obtained at RHIC. Initial findings at LHC which seem to be in apparent conflict with the accumulated RHIC data are highlighted.

## Key claim

This pedagogical review introduces relativistic heavy-ion collisions to high-energy physics students, emphasizing that while quark-gluon plasma (QGP) has been discovered at RHIC, its precise properties remain to be established and the QCD phase diagram is largely unknown. The paper highlights that initial findings at LHC appear in apparent conflict with accumulated RHIC data, particularly regarding constituent quark number scaling.

## Method

The paper surveys the standard model of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions, where the initial state is constructed using the Glauber model or color-glass-condensate approaches, the intermediate evolution uses causal relativistic imperfect fluid dynamics (Müller-Israel-Stewart theory) with a QCD equation of state spanning partonic and hadronic phases, and the final freezeout stage employs relativistic Boltzmann transport theory. The author focuses on two principal observables, azimuthal collective flow (Fourier-decomposed into harmonic coefficients \(v_n\)) and jet quenching (measured via the nuclear modification factor \(R_{AA}\)), and briefly describes constituent quark number scaling, particle abundance ratios, strangeness enhancement, and sequential melting of heavy quarkonia.

## Key numbers

- Lattice QCD predicts a crossover transition at vanishing baryon chemical potential around \(T \sim 150\)-170 MeV (Borsanyi 2010, Borsanyi 2013)
- Energy density and pressure rise rapidly around \(T = 160\) MeV, approaching the Stefan-Boltzmann limit \(\epsilon = 3P\) at high temperature
- The jet quenching parameter \(\hat{q}\) estimated in leading-order QCD is \(\simeq 2.2\) GeV\(^2\)/fm, while phenomenological fits to RHIC data give \(\mathcal{O}(10)\) GeV\(^2\)/fm
- High-\(p_T\) pion and eta yields in AuAu central collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV are suppressed by a factor of \(\sim 5\) relative to scaled \(pp\) yields
- LHC PbPb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 2.76\) TeV show dijet imbalance in CMS data (Chatrchyan 2011)
- SPS and RHIC data on strange baryon production cover the centre-of-mass energy range from 2 GeV to 200 GeV (Cleymans 1998, Andronic 2008)

## Context

This review extends the pedagogical tradition of introducing relativistic heavy-ion physics to newcomers, building on textbooks by Satz, Florkowski, Vogt, Yagi, Letessier, Wong, Csernai, and Muller. It synthesizes results from RHIC (Adams 2005) and LHC (Chatrchyan 2011) and references the lattice QCD equation of state calculations by Borsanyi (2010, 2013). The paper enables students to understand the current tension between RHIC-era constituent quark number scaling and LHC results from ALICE (2012), and highlights open questions about the precise determination of \(\eta/s\) and the role of event-by-event fluctuations in initial conditions.
