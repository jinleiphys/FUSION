# Nuclear Physics --- at the frontiers of knowledge

> (transcript of a talk given by Prof.~Feshbach:) Nuclear physics has been and will be a major factor in science and technology. It makes unique and important contributions to medicine, to industry and to other sciences. Interaction with other physics has been strong. Astrophysics and mesoscopic physics are notable examples. But what I shall talk about today are labeled {\bf``universals"}. This refers to results which transcend the limits of a given subject providing fundamental principles which inform not only nuclear physics but all of science. It is the universals which take nuclear physics research from the parochial albeit fascinating studies of nuclear reactions and structure to the development and formulation of concepts of significance for all of the physical sciences.

## Key claim

Nuclear physics research produces "universals" -- results that transcend the subject and provide fundamental principles applicable to all small systems (systems with a number of interacting particles much less than 10^23 but greater than one), even though the abstract presents this as a general statement without noting that the claim is illustrated primarily through analogies to mesoscopic physics and metallic clusters.

## Method

The paper is a transcribed talk that surveys the history and future of nuclear physics, organizing the discussion around "universals" -- phenomena common to small systems. It reviews collective motion (shell model, deformed nuclei, giant resonances), nuclear reactions (direct, compound, heavy-ion), statistical methods and the optical model, weak interactions, and then examines three new frontiers: the nucleon's internal structure via QCD, relativistic heavy-ion collisions and the quark-gluon plasma, and radioactive beam nuclei near the dripline.

## Key numbers

- 30% of the nucleon spin resides in quarks, -10% in strange sea quarks, with the remainder in gluons or angular momentum contributions
- For stable nuclei, neutron and proton radii are equal to within roughly 0.1 fm
- At the AGS, Au + Au collisions produce baryon densities thought to be as high as 10 times normal nucleon density (10ρ₀)
- AGS beam energies are 11.6A GeV; SPS heavy ion energies are 160A GeV; RHIC colliding beams have energies of 100A x 100A GeV
- Ground state energies calculated from first principles: ²H -2.22 MeV (theory -2.22), ³H -8.48 MeV (theory -8.47(2)), ⁴He -28.3 MeV (theory -28.31), ⁵He -27.2 MeV (theory -26.5(2)), ⁶Li -32.0 MeV (theory -32.4(9))

## Context

This talk by Feshbach (1995) extends the long-standing nuclear physics tradition of identifying universal concepts, drawing explicit parallels to mesoscopic physics (metallic clusters, quantum dots, small conductors) as pioneered by Knight et al. (1984) and de Heer, Knight, Chou, and Cohen (1987). It enables future work on three frontiers: the quark-gluon plasma to be studied at RHIC (scheduled for 1999), radioactive beam nuclei approaching the dripline, and the internal structure of hadrons via QCD. The paper also notes that statistical methods and the Fadeev formalism have successfully calculated light-nuclei ground state energies, providing unique information on the nucleon-nucleon potential.
