# Signatures of phase transitions in nuclei at finite excitation energies

> The mean-field approximation predicts pairing and shape phase transitions in nuclei as a function of temperature or excitation energy. However, in the finite nucleus the singularities of these phase transitions are smoothed out by quantal and thermal fluctuations. An interesting question is whether signatures of these transitions survive despite the large fluctuations. The shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) approach enables us to calculate the statistical properties of nuclei beyond the mean-field approximation in model spaces that are many orders of magnitude larger than spaces that can be treated by conventional diagonalization methods. We have extended the SMMC method to heavy nuclei and used it to study the transition from vibrational (spherical) to rotational (deformed) nuclei in families of rare-earth isotopes. We have calculated collective enhancement factors of level densities as a function of excitation energy and found that the decay of the vibrational and rotational enhancements is well correlated with the pairing and shape phase transitions, respectively.

## Key claim

The mean-field approximation predicts pairing and shape phase transitions in nuclei as a function of temperature or excitation energy, but in finite nuclei the singularities of these transitions are smoothed out by quantal and thermal fluctuations; the paper demonstrates that signatures of these transitions survive despite the large fluctuations, as the decay of vibrational and rotational collective enhancement factors of level densities is well correlated with the pairing and shape phase transitions, respectively.

## Method

The authors used the shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method, which employs a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to express the imaginary-time many-body propagator as a functional integral over auxiliary fields, enabling calculations in model spaces many orders of magnitude larger than those accessible by conventional diagonalization. They extended SMMC to heavy rare-earth nuclei and calculated the thermal observable ⟨J²⟩_T (the expectation value of the total angular momentum squared) as a function of temperature to distinguish vibrational from rotational collectivity. The microcanonical level density was obtained from the canonical partition function via a saddle-point approximation, and collective enhancement factors K = ρ_SMMC/ρ_HFB were computed by comparing SMMC total state densities with intrinsic Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) densities.

## Key numbers

- For ¹⁶²Dy, the ground-state moment of inertia extracted from the slope of ⟨J²⟩_T versus temperature is I_g.s. = 35.5 ± 3.3 MeV⁻¹, in agreement with the experimental value I_g.s. = 3/E_{2⁺} = 37.2 MeV⁻¹.
- The SMMC state density of ¹⁶²Dy was compared with experimental results including level counting at low energies, neutron resonance data, and data measured by the Oslo method.
- For the samarium isotopes ¹⁴⁸–¹⁵⁴Sm, ⟨J²⟩_T as a function of temperature shows a crossover from vibrational (¹⁴⁸Sm) to rotational (¹⁵⁴Sm) behavior, with SMMC results in good agreement with experimental data using a backshifted Bethe formula level density.
- In the HFB approximation, ¹⁴⁸Sm is spherical in its ground state (T=0), ¹⁵⁰Sm acquires a small deformation, and ¹⁵⁴Sm is well-deformed; ¹⁵²Sm is known as an X(5) nucleus defining the phase transition between spherical and axially-deformed nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the SMMC method to heavy nuclei, overcoming technical challenges that previously limited applications to medium-mass nuclei (Alhassid 2008). It builds on the Landau theory of nuclear shape transitions (Alhassid 1992) and the static path approximation plus random phase approximation (Lauritzen 1990, Attias 1995). The results enable microscopic calculations of statistical properties in the configuration-interaction shell model for heavy deformed nuclei, where the geometric Bohr Hamiltonian and interacting boson model have been successful phenomenologically but a microscopic description in a truncated spherical shell model was a major challenge. The findings open the way to studying how collective enhancement factors of level densities correlate with pairing and shape phase transitions in families of rare-earth isotopes.
