# Unitary fermions and Luscher's formula on a crystal

> We consider the low-energy particle-particle scattering properties in a periodic simple cubic crystal. In particular, we investigate the relation between the two-body scattering length and the energy shift experienced by the lowest-lying unbound state when this is placed in a periodic finite box. We introduce a continuum model for s-wave contact interactions that respects the symmetry of the Brillouin zone in its regularisation and renormalisation procedures, and corresponds to the na{ï}ve continuum limit of the Hubbard model. The energy shifts are found to be identical to those obtained in the usual spherically symmetric renormalisation scheme upon resolving an important subtlety regarding the cutoff procedure. We then particularize to the Hubbard model, and find that for large finite lattices the results are identical to those obtained in the continuum limit. The results reported here are valid in the weak, intermediate and unitary limits, and can be used for the extraction of scattering information ,via exact diagonalisation or Monte Carlo methods, of two-body systems in realistic periodic lattices.

# Unitary fermions and Luscher's formula on a crystal

## Key claim

The energy shifts for two fermions in a periodic cubic box, obtained using a contact interaction regularised with cubic symmetry (respecting the Brillouin zone), are identical to those from the standard spherically symmetric renormalisation scheme, but only after resolving a subtlety: the cutoff used in renormalisation must be taken as the infinite-volume limit of the lattice cutoff, not the finite-volume cutoff itself.

## Method

The authors derive the Bethe-Goldstone equation for two particles in a periodic simple cubic lattice using a kernel subtraction method, then apply it to both the naive continuum limit of the Hubbard model and the Hubbard model itself. For the continuum model, they regularise the s-wave contact interaction with a cubic cutoff that respects the Brillouin zone symmetry, renormalise the coupling constant in favour of the scattering length, and compute the energy shift of the lowest unbound state at zero total momentum. For the Hubbard model, they compute the scattering length from the on-site interaction U and the Watson integral, then derive the energy shift from the Bethe-Goldstone equation in the finite lattice.

## Key numbers

- The zero-energy integral for the cubic-cutoff regularisation scheme gives \(I_0(0) = -15.3482484734169\ldots \Lambda + \mathcal{O}(1/\Lambda)\), where \(\Lambda\) is the ultraviolet cutoff.
- The leading finite-size correction to the energy shift in the weak-coupling limit is \(\Delta E \approx \frac{4\pi \hbar^2}{m} \frac{a}{L^3} \left[1+2.837297 \frac{a}{L}\right]\), identical to Luscher's formula.
- The Watson integral for the Hubbard model dispersion has numerical value \(J W(0) = -0.12636655\ldots \equiv \omega_0\).
- In the unitary limit (unnaturally large scattering length), the energy shift is \(\Delta E \approx -\frac{4\pi^2 \hbar^2}{mL^2}\left[0.09590\ldots + \mathcal{O}(1/aL)\right]\).
- The sum \(s_1\) (the finite part of the lattice sum after subtraction) is \(-1.23945\ldots\), and the standard Luscher sum is \(-8.91363\ldots\).

## Context

This work extends Luscher's formula (Luscher, 1986) and the pionless effective field theory treatment of Beane et al. (2004) to the case of a periodic cubic lattice, where the Brillouin zone has cubic rather than spherical symmetry. The authors show that the naive continuum limit of the Hubbard model reproduces the standard Luscher result only when the renormalisation cutoff is taken as the infinite-volume limit \(\Lambda(\infty)\), not the finite-volume cutoff \(\Lambda(L_s)\). The results enable extraction of scattering lengths from exact diagonalisation or Monte Carlo simulations of two-body systems in realistic periodic lattices, including optical lattices and condensed matter systems, without needing to project onto s-wave channels. The authors note that generalisations to graphene-like lattices and the calculation of the Bertsch parameter for the Hubbard model are natural next steps.
