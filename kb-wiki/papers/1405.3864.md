# A Hybrid Strong/Weak Coupling Approach to Jet Quenching

> We propose and explore a new hybrid approach to jet quenching in a strongly coupled medium. The basis of this phenomenological approach is to treat physics processes at different energy scales differently. The high-$Q^2$ processes associated with the QCD evolution of the jet from production as a single hard parton through its fragmentation, up to but not including hadronization, are treated perturbatively. The interactions between the partons in the shower and the deconfined matter within which they find themselves lead to energy loss. The momentum scales associated with the medium (of the order of the temperature) and with typical interactions between partons in the shower and the medium are sufficiently soft that strongly coupled physics plays an important role in energy loss. We model these interactions using qualitative insights from holographic calculations of the energy loss of energetic light quarks and gluons in a strongly coupled plasma, obtained via gauge/gravity duality. We embed this hybrid model into a hydrodynamic description of the spacetime evolution of the hot QCD matter produced in heavy ion collisions and confront its predictions with jet data from the LHC. The holographic expression for the energy loss of a light quark or gluon that we incorporate in our hybrid model is parametrized by a stopping distance. We find very good agreement with all the data as long as we choose a stopping distance that is comparable to but somewhat longer than that in ${\cal N}=4$ supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory. For comparison, we also construct alternative models in which energy loss occurs as it would if the plasma were weakly coupled. We close with suggestions of observables that could provide more incisive evidence for, or against, the importance of strongly coupled physics in jet quenching.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a hybrid phenomenological model for jet quenching that treats high-energy jet evolution perturbatively (via DGLAP splitting) while modeling the soft interactions between jet partons and the strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma using insights from holographic calculations, with the claim that this hybrid approach can describe LHC jet data if the stopping distance parameter is chosen to be comparable to but somewhat longer than that in N=4 supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory.

## Method

The authors construct a Monte Carlo simulation where a hard parton undergoes vacuum DGLAP splitting with a spacetime structure assigned to each splitting vertex based on the formation time tau = 2E/Q^2. Each parton in the shower then loses energy while propagating through a hydrodynamically evolving medium, with the energy loss rate taken from holographic calculations of falling strings in strongly coupled plasma, parametrized by a stopping distance x_stop = (1/(2*kappa_sc)) * (E_in^(1/3) / T^(4/3)). The model is compared to LHC data on jet R_AA, dijet asymmetry, and jet fragmentation function ratios across different centrality classes.

## Key numbers

- The stopping distance in strongly coupled N=4 SYM is given by x_stop = (1/(2*kappa_sc)) * (E_in^(1/3) / T^(4/3)), where kappa_sc = 1.05 * lambda^(1/6) with lambda the 't Hooft coupling
- The energy loss rate for a parton in strongly coupled plasma is dE/dx = -(4/pi) * (x^2 / x_stop^2) * (1 / sqrt(x_stop^2 - x^2)) * E_in
- The formation time assigned to each splitting vertex is tau = 2 * E / Q^2
- The medium temperature range explored is T ~ 150-600 MeV
- The delay time for the string worldsheet to fully split after a hard vertex is of order 1/(pi*T)
- The width of the lowest non-hydrodynamical quasinormal mode is Gamma_1 = 2*pi*T ~ T/0.16
- Hydrodynamic modes with q ~ pi*T dissipate over a time ~ (0.5-0.6)/T for eta/s ~ 2/(4*pi)

## Context

This work extends the holographic approach to jet quenching pioneered by Chesler et al. (2008, 2014) and Gubser et al. (2008) by embedding the strongly coupled energy loss mechanism into a full jet shower evolution with hydrodynamic medium modeling. It contrasts with purely perturbative approaches to jet quenching such as those based on Baier et al. (1996, 1998) and Gyulassy et al. (2000), and with earlier hybrid attempts by Marquet et al. (2009), Betz et al. (2011), and Ficnar et al. (2012). The model enables future tests of whether strongly coupled physics is essential for describing jet quenching by comparing predictions for observables that could provide more incisive evidence for or against the importance of strong coupling.
