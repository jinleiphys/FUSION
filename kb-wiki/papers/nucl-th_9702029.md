# A self-consistent treatment of the dynamics of stable and unstable collective modes

> We address the dynamics of damped collective modes in terms of first and second moments. The modes are introduced in a self-consistent fashion with the help of a suitable application of linear response theory. Quantum effects in the fluctuations are governed by diffusion coefficients D_{μν}. The latter are obtained through a fluctuation dissipation theorem generalized to allow for a treatment of unstable modes. Numerical evaluations of the D_{μν} are presented. We discuss briefly how this picture may be used to describe global motion within a locally harmonic approximation.

## Key claim

The paper develops a self-consistent linear response framework for damped collective modes that works for both stable and unstable modes, using a generalized fluctuation-dissipation theorem to obtain diffusion coefficients; the authors note that the locally harmonic approximation (LHA) may encounter limits at smaller temperatures, and that for unstable modes the effective stiffness is reduced in magnitude compared to the static stiffness, weakening the instability.

## Method

The authors start from a Hamiltonian of the form \(\hat H^{(2)} = \hat H_0 + (k/2) \hat F \hat F\) and use linear response theory to derive the collective response function \(\chi_{\rm coll}(\omega) = \chi(\omega) / (1 + k \chi(\omega))\). They then reduce the full response to a single damped oscillator form \(\chi_{\rm osc}(\omega) = -1/(M^F) \cdot 1/(\omega^2 + i\omega\Gamma - \varpi^2)\) and obtain transport coefficients (inertia, friction, stiffness) for the collective coordinate \(q(t)\). For fluctuations, they quantize the collective variable via a Bohm-Pines-type Hamiltonian with a subsidiary condition \(k \hat F = \hat Q - Q_0\), and derive differential equations for the second moments \(\Sigma_{qq}, \Sigma_{qp}, \Sigma_{pp}\) with diffusion coefficients \(D_{qp}, D_{pp}\) obtained from a fluctuation-dissipation theorem generalized to unstable modes through analytic continuation.

## Key numbers

* The static stiffness \(C(0)\) drops dramatically above \(k_B T = 2\;{\rm MeV}\) in nuclear physics, where shell structure diminishes.
* The sum rule for inertia gives \(1/M \le S^{(1)}_{qq} = i k^2 / \hbar \langle [\hat{\dot F}, \hat F] \rangle \equiv 1/m_0\), so the collective inertia cannot be smaller than the irrotational-flow liquid-drop inertia \(m_0\).
* The effective stiffness satisfies \(C \geq C(0)\); for unstable modes with \(C = -|C|\) and \(C(0) = -|C(0)|\), one obtains \(1/|C| \geq 1/|C(0)| \cdot 1/(1 - |C(0)|/\chi(0)) \geq 1/|C(0)|\).
* The static response \(\chi(0)\) is assumed positive and larger than \(|C(0)|\) for the unstable case.

## Context

This work extends the Kramers (1940) transport-equation approach to include quantum effects and self-consistency, building on earlier treatments of quantal equations for stable modes by Hofmann and Sollacher (1989) and by Dekker (1991). It generalizes the fluctuation-dissipation theorem to unstable modes, following techniques used by Ingold (1989) and by Ankerhold, Grabert, and Ingold (1995), but applied here to diffusion coefficients in a transport equation. The locally harmonic approximation (LHA) framework, previously developed for nuclear fission by Hofmann and collaborators (1990, 1992), is used to handle large-scale collective motion where functional-integral methods are not applicable. The results enable the description of global motion (e.g., decay from a metastable minimum) within a transport-equation picture that includes both damping and quantum fluctuations.
