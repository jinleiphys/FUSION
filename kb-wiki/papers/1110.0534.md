# Theory of Antineutrino Monitoring of Burning MOX Plutonium Fuels

> This letter presents the physics and feasibility of reactor antineutrino monitoring to verify the burnup of plutonium loaded in the reactor as a Mixed Oxide (MOX) fuel. It examines the magnitude and temporal variation in the antineutrino signals expected for different MOX fuels, for the purposes of nuclear accountability and safeguards. The antineutrino signals from reactor-grade and weapons-grade MOX are shown to be distinct from those from burning low enriched uranium. Thus, antineutrino monitoring could be used to verify the destruction of plutonium in reactors, though verifying the grade of the plutonium being burned is found to be more challenging.

# Theory of Antineutrino Monitoring of Burning MOX Plutonium Fuels

## Key claim

Reactor antineutrino monitoring can verify the destruction of plutonium in MOX fuels because the antineutrino signals from reactor-grade and weapons-grade MOX are distinct from those of low enriched uranium; however, distinguishing the grade of plutonium being burned is more challenging and may only be feasible when MOX constitutes the majority of the reactor core.

## Method

The authors performed reactor burn simulations using the Monteburns code, which couples the Monte Carlo neutron transport code MCNP to the burn code CINDER`90 (63 neutron energy groups, up to 3400 nuclides including 638 isomers). They benchmarked against a standard H.B. Robinson Unit 2 PWR fuel assembly (2.56% enriched fresh LEU, burnup 23.81 GWd/MTU) and then simulated four MOX loadings: 100% reactor-grade MOX, 100% weapons-grade MOX, 33.3% reactor-grade MOX with 66.7% fresh LEU, and 33.3% weapons-grade MOX with 66.7% fresh LEU. The antineutrino signals were computed using the recently improved spectra of Mueller et al. for each actinide.

## Key numbers

- Fissioning 239Pu emits only about 42% as many antineutrinos per fission as 238U and 65% as many as 235U (Table 1 context).
- The benchmark HBR2 assembly was irradiated to a burnup of 23.81 GWd/MTU, and predicted spent fuel isotopic inventories matched measurement within about 5%.
- For 100% MOX cores, the reactor-grade plutonium signal is about 7% larger than that for weapons-grade plutonium.
- For 33.3% MOX cores, the signal difference between reactor-grade and weapons-grade plutonium is reduced to about 2%.
- The overall uncertainty in the antineutrino flux emitted from the reactor is about 5% if the thermal power is known.
- The antineutrino detection cross section per fission for 239Pu is 5.97 × 10⁻⁴³ cm², compared to 6.61 × 10⁻⁴³ cm² for 235U and 10.10 × 10⁻⁴³ cm² for 238U (Table 1).

## Context

This work extends prior studies by Borovoi et al. on antineutrino monitoring of reactors for verifying reactor power and fuel isotopic content. It specifically addresses the feasibility of monitoring MOX fuels, which have been used in thermal reactors since the 1960s and are now loaded in many commercial thermal reactors at one-third MOX fractions. The results enable potential verification of plutonium destruction in reactors for nuclear safeguards purposes, though the authors note that distinguishing plutonium grade requires MOX to be the majority of the core, and that real reactor cores with mixed fresh and cycled fuel will require further study.
