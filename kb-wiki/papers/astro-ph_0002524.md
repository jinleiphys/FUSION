# Do strange stars exist in the Universe?

> Definitely, an affirmative answer to this question would have implications of fundamental importance for astrophysics (a new class of compact stars), and for the physics of strong interactions (deconfined phase of quark matter, and strange matter hypothesis). In the present work, we use observational data for the newly discovered millisecond X-ray pulsar SAX J1808.4-3658 and for the atoll source 4U 1728-34 to constrain the radius of the underlying compact stars. Comparing the mass-radius relation of these two compact stars with theoretical models for both neutron stars and strange stars, we argue that a strange star model is more consistent with SAX J1808.4-3658 and 4U 1728-34, and suggest that they are likely strange star candidates.

## Key claim

Based on observational data from the millisecond X-ray pulsar SAX J1808.4-3658 and the atoll source 4U 1728-34, a strange star model is more consistent with the inferred mass-radius relations than neutron star models, but the present uncertainties in accretion mechanisms and disk-magnetosphere interaction do not allow a definitive ruling out of the neutron star possibility.

## Method

The author compared theoretical mass-radius relations for neutron stars (using several realistic equations of state: conventional models BBB1 and BBB2, a model with hyperons, and a model with kaon condensates) and for strange stars (using the MIT bag model with B = 90 MeV/fm^3 and two parameterizations of the Dey et al. (1998) equation of state, SS1 and SS2) against observationally constrained mass-radius regions for SAX J1808.4-3658 and 4U 1728-34. For SAX J1808.4-3658, the radius upper limit was derived from the requirement that the inner accretion disk radius lies between the stellar surface and the co-rotation radius, using X-ray flux variations during the 1998 outburst. For 4U 1728-34, constraints came from the Titarchuk and Osherovich (1999) model of kilohertz quasi-periodic oscillations, requiring the inner disk radius to exceed both the stellar radius and the radius of the last stable circular orbit.

## Key numbers

- For SAX J1808.4-3658: pulse period of 2.49 milliseconds; distance of 4 kpc; peak X-ray luminosity of 6 x 10^36 erg/s in bright state; X-ray luminosity lower than 10^35 erg/s in quiescence; surface dipolar magnetic moment less than 10^26 G cm^3; orbital period of 2 hours; maximum X-ray flux F_max approximately 3 x 10^-9 erg cm^-2 s^-1; minimum detectable flux F_min approximately 2 x 10^-11 erg cm^-2 s^-1; flux ratio F_max/F_min approximately 100; radius upper limit R < 27.5 (F_min/F_max)^(2/7) (P/2.49 ms)^(2/3) (M/M_sun)^(1/3) km.
- For 4U 1728-34: spin frequency of 364 Hz; lower kHz QPO frequency separation decreases from 349.3 +/- 1.7 Hz to 278.7 +/- 11.6 Hz; inner disk radius constraint R <= r_o approximately 8.86 a_k^(2/3) (M/M_sun)^(1/3) km with a_k = 1.03; for a non-rotating star, r_o >= 3 R_s = 8.86 (M/M_sun) km.
- For strange quark matter stability: energy per baryon of strange quark matter must be less than or equal to approximately 930 MeV; for non-interacting quarks (alpha_c = 0), bag constant B in the range 57-91 MeV/fm^3 for m_s = 0, and B in the range 57-75 MeV/fm^3 for m_s = 150 MeV.
- For the Dey et al. (1998) equation of state: normal nuclear matter density n_0 = 0.16 fm^-3; effective quark mass parameterization M_q = m_q + 310 * sech(nu n_B/n_0) MeV; SS1 corresponds to nu = 0.333; SS2 corresponds to nu = 0.286.

## Context

This work extends the search for observational signatures of strange stars, building on the strange matter hypothesis formulated by Witten (1984) and Bodmer (1971). The analysis uses the mass-radius constraint method developed by Li et al. (1999a) for SAX J1808.4-3658 and the kHz QPO model of Titarchuk and Osherovich (1999) for 4U 1728-34. The paper identifies SAX J1808.4-3658 and 4U 1728-34 as likely strange star candidates, joining previously proposed candidates including the X-ray burster 4U 1820-30 (Bombaci, 1997), the bursting X-ray pulsar GRO J1744-28 (Cheng et al., 1998b), and the X-ray pulsar Her X-1 (Dey et al., 1998). Future work could test these candidates with improved accretion models and observations, and strange stars have also been speculated to model gamma-ray bursters and soft gamma-ray repeaters.
