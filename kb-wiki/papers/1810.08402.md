# From Asymmetric to Symmetric Fission in the Fermium Isotopes within the Time-Dependent GCM Formalism

> Predicting the properties of neutron-rich nuclei far from the valley of stability is one of the major challenges of modern nuclear theory. In heavy and superheavy nuclei, a difference of only a few neutrons is sufficient to change the dominant fission mode. A theoretical approach capable of predicting such rapid transitions for neutron-rich systems would be a valuable tool to better understand r-process nucleosynthesis or the decay of super-heavy elements. In this work, we investigate for the first time the transition from asymmetric to symmetric fission through the calculation of primary fission yields with the time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM). We choose here the transition in neutron-rich Fermium isotopes, which was the first to be observed experimentally in the late seventies and is often used as a benchmark for theoretical studies. We compute the primary fission fragment mass and charge yields for 254 Fm, 256 Fm and 258 Fm from the TDGCM under the Gaussian overlap approximation. The static part of the calculation (generation of a potential energy surface) consists in a series of constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations based on the D1S, D1M or D1N parameterizations of the Gogny effective interaction in a two-center harmonic oscillator basis. The 2-dimensional dynamics in the collective space spanned by the quadrupole and octupole moments is then computed with the finite element solver FELIX-2.0. The available experimental data and the TDGCM post-dictions are consistent and agree especially on the position in the Fermium isotopic chain at which the transition occurs. The main limitation of the method lies in the presence of discontinuities in the 2-dimensional manifold of generator states.

## Key claim

The time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) under the Gaussian overlap approximation (GOA) can qualitatively reproduce the rapid transition from asymmetric to symmetric fission in the Fermium isotopic chain, with the transition occurring at the correct neutron number N=156, but the mass-by-mass yield values may differ from experiment by up to 2% in absolute value and the intermediate nucleus 256Fm does not reproduce the double-humped experimental shape.

## Method

The authors performed constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using the D1S, D1M, and D1N parameterizations of the Gogny effective interaction in a two-center harmonic oscillator basis to generate potential energy surfaces in the collective space spanned by the quadrupole moment Q20 and octupole moment Q30. They then solved the TDGCM+GOA collective Schrodinger equation using the finite element solver FELIX-2.0, simulating the collective evolution over 20 zs with an initial state built as a superposition of collective eigen-modes in the first potential well, and extracted primary fission fragment mass yields from the time-integrated flux through a frontier defined by the neck operator isoline QN = 7.5, convoluted with a Gaussian of width sigma = 4 mass units.

## Key numbers

- The potential energy surfaces were computed on a grid spanning [0,450] x [0,100] (in barn units) with mesh steps h20 = 2 b and h30 = 1 b^(3/2)
- The inner fission barrier heights (B_I) relative to the potential minimum are 13.3 MeV for 254Fm, 12.4 MeV for 256Fm, and 11.8 MeV for 258Fm using the D1S interaction
- The GCM ground-state energies (E_0,GCM) in the first well are -1883.0 MeV for 254Fm, -1893.6 MeV for 256Fm, and -1903.7 MeV for 258Fm
- After 20 zs of propagation, 34% of the total norm crossed the frontier for 254Fm, 34% for 256Fm, and 28% for 258Fm
- The time step used in the evolution was dt = 2 x 10^(-4) zs (10^(-21) s), and the propagation ran up to 20 zs
- The absolute numerical convergence of the mass yields (normalized to 200%) is expected to be of the order of 0.06%

## Context

This work extends the TDGCM+GOA approach, previously applied to actinide nuclei by Goutte (2005), Younes (2012), and Regnier (2016), to the neutron-rich Fermium isotopes to test its ability to predict rapid structural changes in fission yields. The transition from asymmetric to symmetric fission in Fermium isotopes was first observed experimentally in the late 1970s by Harbour (1973), Gindler (1977), and others, and has been studied theoretically using static approaches by Lustig (1980), Cwiok (1989), and Warda (2002, 2006), as well as scission-point models by Schmidt (2018) and Langevin calculations by Asano (2004), which failed to reproduce the transition. The TDGCM results enable future studies of fission yield evolution far from the valley of stability, relevant for r-process nucleosynthesis, though the method is limited by discontinuities in the 2-dimensional manifold of generator states.
