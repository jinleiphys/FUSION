# Effect of Coulomb Screening Length on Nuclear Pasta Simulations

> We study the role of the effective Coulomb interaction strength and length on the dynamics of nucleons in conditions according to those in a neutron star's crust. Calculations were made with a semi-classical molecular dynamics model, studying isospin symmetric matter at sub-saturation densities and low temperatures. The electrostatic interaction between protons interaction is included in the form of a screened Coulomb potential in the spirit of the Thomas-Fermi approximation, but the screening length is artificially varied to explore its effect on the formation of the non-homogeneous nuclear structures known as "nuclear pasta". As the screening length increases, we can a transition from a one-per-cell pasta regime (due exclusively to finite size effects) to a more appealing multiple pasta per simulation box. This shows qualitative difference in the structure of neutron star matter at low temperatures, and therefore, special caution should be taken when the screening length is estimated for numerical simulations.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the commonly used screening length of λ = 10 fm for the Thomas-Fermi screened Coulomb potential in particle-based simulations of neutron star crust matter is too small to produce physically realistic nuclear pasta structures; there exists a critical screening length λ_c between 10 fm and 15 fm (depending on density) below which the observed structures are artificial "pseudo-pasta" caused by finite-size effects and periodic boundary conditions rather than genuine Coulomb-nuclear competition.

## Method

The authors performed semi-classical molecular dynamics simulations using the Pandharipande two-body potentials for nucleon-nucleon interactions, with a screened Coulomb (Yukawa) potential between protons. They simulated isospin-symmetric matter (2500 protons and 2500 neutrons) in cubic boxes with periodic boundary conditions at sub-saturation densities (ρ = 0.005, 0.03, 0.05, and 0.08 fm⁻³), cooling systems from T = 1.6 MeV to T = 0.001 MeV using a Nosé-Hoover thermostat. The screening length λ was varied from 0 fm (no Coulomb) to 20 fm (and up to 50 fm for the lowest density), and the resulting structures were analyzed using Minkowski functionals (surface area, mean curvature, Euler characteristic) and pair correlation functions.

## Key numbers

- The screening length from quantum field theory calculations is λ ≈ 100 fm, while previous simulations used λ = 10 fm for computational feasibility.
- The critical screening length λ_c lies between 10 fm and 15 fm for the Pandharipande potential, depending on density.
- For all λ < 10 fm, the pressure of the configurations is negative, indicating artificial structures due to periodic boundary conditions.
- For λ > 10 fm, the pressure becomes positive, signaling that Coulomb interaction begins to play its intended role.
- The nearest-neighbor distance in clusters remains at r = 1.7 fm and 1.9 fm regardless of λ, showing short-range structure is governed by the nuclear potential.
- At ρ = 0.005 fm⁻³, the average size of "gnocchi" clusters stabilizes for λ ≥ 20 fm, with an average relative error of approximately 8%.
- The cutoff radius for the nuclear potentials is r_c = 5.4 fm, and the equilibrium density is ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³ with binding energy E(ρ₀) = 16 MeV/nucleon and compressibility of 250 MeV.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of nuclear pasta formation using dynamical models, particularly addressing the arbitrary choice of screening length λ = 10 fm made by Maruyama (2012) and Horowitz (2004) for computational reasons. The authors had previously shown (Dor12) that topological tools (Minkowski functionals) can classify pasta structures, and earlier static studies by Watanabe (2003) and Maruyama (2005) found that screening broadens the density range for pasta phases but did not include dynamical effects. The paper demonstrates that the commonly used λ = 10 fm lies in a transition regime where structures are not well-characterized pasta phases, and that only for λ = 20 fm do the structures stabilize into the expected pasta morphologies (gnocchi, spaghetti, lasagna). This finding enables future simulations to choose screening lengths that properly recover Thomas-Fermi physics while remaining computationally tractable, though the critical value depends on the nuclear interaction model used.
