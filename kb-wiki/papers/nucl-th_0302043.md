# Short versus long range interactions and the size of two-body weakly bound objects

> Very weakly bound systems may manifest intriguing "universal" properties, independent of the specific interaction which keeps the system bound. An interesting example is given by relations between the size of the system and the separation energy, or scaling laws. So far, scaling laws have been investigated for short-range and long-range (repulsive) potentials. We report here on scaling laws for weakly bound two-body systems valid for a larger class of potentials, i.e. short-range potentials having a repulsive core and long-range attractive potentials. We emphasize analogies and differences between the short- and the long-range case. In particular, we show that the emergence of halos is a threshold phenomenon which can arise when the system is bound not only by short-range interactions but also by long-range ones, and this for any value of the orbital angular momentum $\ell$. These results enlarge the image of halo systems we are accustomed to.

# Short versus long range interactions and the size of two-body weakly bound objects

## Key claim

The paper shows that scaling laws relating the size of weakly bound two-body systems to their separation energy hold for a broader class of potentials than previously studied, specifically short-range potentials with a repulsive core and long-range attractive potentials, and that halos can emerge as a threshold phenomenon for any orbital angular momentum ℓ, not only for s- and p-waves as in the short-range case.

## Method

The authors analyze the two-body Schrödinger equation for spherically symmetric potentials, using both general theoretical arguments (inequalities and invariance properties) and approximate wavefunction forms (Hankel functions for short-range tails, spherical Bessel functions for long-range interiors). They compare numerical results for specific potentials, including a cut Kratzer potential with a repulsive core and a square well without one, to extract the coefficients c_ℓ in the scaling laws ⟨r²⟩_ℓ ≈ c_ℓ / |E_ℓ| or similar forms, as the binding energy approaches zero.

## Key numbers

- For short-range potentials, the scaling law for s-states gives ⟨r²⟩_0 ≈ c_0 / |E_0| with c_0 = 1/2 in the universal limit.
- The one-neutron halo ¹¹Be has a separation energy of about 0.5 MeV and an average distance between the halo neutron and the core of about 7 fm.
- The (⁴He)₂ dimer has a predicted dissociation energy of 0.1 μeV and a measured average distance between the two atoms of about 52 Å.
- For long-range potentials, the scaling law for all ℓ is ⟨r²⟩_ℓ ≈ c_ℓ[V] / |E_ℓ|, with c_0 = 3 for the Kratzer potential, the Coulomb attractive potential, and the harmonic oscillator.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of scaling laws for two-body systems, which had been limited to short-range potentials defined by only two parameters (depth and range) and to repulsive long-range interactions such as the Coulomb potential (Author, past; Author, euro; Author, R). The authors build on their own recent investigation (Lombard and Volpe, 2003) to include repulsive cores and attractive long-range potentials. The results enlarge the conventional image of halo systems, previously associated with particles spending most of their time outside the classically allowed region, by showing that halos can also arise from long-range interactions where the particle remains mainly inside the classical region, and that this can occur for any ℓ.
