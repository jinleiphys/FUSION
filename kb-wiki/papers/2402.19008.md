# Investigation of the determination of nuclear deformation using high-energy heavy-ion scattering

> Background: Nuclear deformation provides a crucial characteristic of nuclear structure. Conventionally, the quadrupole deformation length of a nucleus, $δ_{2}$, has often been determined based on a macroscopic model through a deformed nuclear potential with the deformation length $δ^{\rm (pot)}_{2}$, which is determined to reproduce the nuclear scattering data. This approach assumes $δ_{2}=δ^{\rm (pot)}_{2}$ although there is no theoretical foundation. Purpose: We clarify the relationship between $δ_{2}$ and $δ^{\rm (pot)}_{2}$ for high-energy heavy-ion scattering systematically to evaluate the validity of the conventional approach to determine the nuclear deformation. Method: The deformation lengths for the $^{12}$C inelastic scattering by $^{12}$C, $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca, and $^{208}$Pb targets at $E/A$ = 50--400 MeV are examined. First, we perform microscopic coupled-channel (CC) calculations to relate $δ_{2}$ of the deformed density into the inelastic scattering cross section. Second, we use the deformed potential model to determine $δ^{\rm (pot)}_{2}$ so as to reproduce the microscopic CC result. We then compare $δ^{\rm (pot)}_{2}$ with $δ_{2}$. Results: We find that $δ^{\rm (pot)}_{2}$ is about 20--40 \% smaller than presumed $δ_{2}$, showing strong energy and target dependence. Further analysis, which considers higher-order deformation effects beyond the derivative model, reveals that $δ^{\rm (pot)}_{2}$ is still about 15--35 \% smaller than $δ_{2}$. Conclusion: Our results suggest that one needs to be careful when the deformed potential model for the high-energy heavy-ion scattering is used to extract the nuclear deformation. The conventional approach may underestimate the deformation length $δ_2$ systematically.

## Key claim

The conventional assumption that the quadrupole deformation length of the nuclear density, δ₂, equals the deformation length of the nuclear potential, δ₂^(pot), is shown to be invalid for high-energy heavy-ion scattering; δ₂^(pot) systematically underestimates δ₂ by 20–40% in the deformed potential model and by 15–35% even when higher-order deformation effects beyond the derivative model are included.

## Method

The authors performed microscopic coupled-channel (CC) calculations using the deformed density (DD) model, where the transition density of ¹²C is characterized by a known δ₂ = -1.564 fm, to compute angular-integrated inelastic scattering cross sections for the 2₁⁺ state of ¹²C. They then used the deformed potential (DP) model, fitting δ₂^(pot) to reproduce those same cross sections, and compared the extracted δ₂^(pot) with the input δ₂. The analysis was repeated with a higher-order DP2 model that goes beyond the first-order derivative approximation for the coupling potential.

## Key numbers

- δ₂^(pot) is about 20–40% smaller than the presumed δ₂ across all systems studied.
- In the higher-order DP2 model, δ₂^(pot) remains about 15–35% smaller than δ₂.
- The input deformation length for ¹²C is |δ₂| = 1.564 fm, with radius parameter R₀ = 2.1545 fm and diffuseness a = 0.425 fm.
- Incident energies span E/A = 50–400 MeV for ¹²C scattering on ¹²C, ¹⁶O, ⁴⁰Ca, and ²⁰⁸Pb targets.
- For ¹²C + ¹²C at E/A = 121.1 MeV, the calculated σ_theo(2₁⁺) = 27 mb underestimates the experimental σ_exp(2₁⁺) = 43 ± 3 mb.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Bee96 and Kho00, which examined the δ₂ vs. δ₂^(pot) relationship mainly below E/A < 100 MeV and found significant underestimation, into the high-energy regime (E/A = 50–400 MeV) where experimental data are scarce. The microscopic CC calculations with the complex G-matrix interaction MPa, previously validated by Furumoto (2013, 2018) and Watanabe (2015, 2017), provide reliable predictions that enable this systematic comparison. The results enable future experimental determinations of nuclear deformation from high-energy heavy-ion scattering to apply a correction factor rather than assuming δ₂ = δ₂^(pot), and motivate new measurements of inelastic cross sections to refine microscopic potentials.
