# Observable Consequences of a Scalar Boson Coupled only to Neutrinos

> We have examined the consequences of assuming the existence of a light scalar boson, weakly coupled to neutrinos, and not coupled to any other light fermions. For a range of parameters, we find that this hypothesis leads to the development of neutrino clusters which form in the early Universe and which provide gravitational fluctuations on scales small compared to a parsec (i.e., the scale of solar systems). The existence of such clustering produces an effect which would appear as a negative mass squared for the electron neutrino in Tritium beta decay, without conflicting with other experiments. The neutrino masses arising in unified gauge theories would then be very much larger than the masses extracted from experiments within the solar system.

# Observable Consequences of a Scalar Boson Coupled Only to Neutrinos

## Key claim

A light scalar boson coupled only to neutrinos, with a coupling constant and mass that evade existing experimental constraints, would cause neutrinos to form self-bound clusters in the early Universe; these clusters would produce an apparent negative mass-squared for the electron neutrino in Tritium beta decay experiments, while the true vacuum neutrino masses from unified gauge theories would be much larger than those inferred from solar-system measurements.

## Method

The authors apply Quantum Hadrodynamics (QHD) formalism, originally developed for nuclear matter, to a relativistic Fermi gas of neutrinos interacting via scalar exchange. They solve the self-consistent equation for the effective neutrino mass in a medium, derive constraints from Tritium beta decay endpoint data, neutrinoless double beta decay limits, primordial nucleosynthesis bounds, and supernova neutrino observations, and estimate the annihilation rate and cooling rate of neutrino clusters via scalar bremsstrahlung.

## Key numbers

- The density of relic neutrinos required to produce the observed Tritium beta decay endpoint excess is approximately $5 \times 10^{15} / \text{cm}^3$, corresponding to a Fermi momentum $k_F = 13 \, \text{eV}/c$.
- To suppress neutrinoless double beta decay, the parameter $A$ must satisfy $A > 10^9$, ensuring the effective neutrino mass remains negligible at momenta up to $k/m^o \sim 10^8$.
- The scalar boson mass is bounded by $m_\phi < 10^{-17} \, \text{eV}/c^2$, implying a range greater than $10^{12} \, \text{cm}$.
- The coupling constant must satisfy $\tilde{\alpha} = g^2/4\pi < 10^{-18}$ to keep the mean neutrino annihilation rate below $10^{-18} / \text{s}$.
- The neutrino cluster energy density is estimated as $4 \times 10^{16} \, \text{eV}/\text{cm}^3 = 7 \times 10^{-17} \, \text{g}/\text{cm}^3$, giving an extra mass interior to Jupiter's orbit of $1.3 \times 10^{26} \, \text{g} = 10^{-7} M_\odot$.

## Context

This work extends the study of scalar-mediated neutrino interactions, building on the QHD framework of Serot and Walecka (1986) and the analysis of additional neutrino degrees of freedom in primordial nucleosynthesis by Kolb, Turner, and Walker (1986). The proposed scalar boson is similar in many respects to the Majoron proposed by Chikashige, Mohapatra, and Peccei (1981) and by Gelmini and Roncadelli (1981). The scenario offers a potential explanation for the negative mass-squared results from the five modern Tritium beta decay experiments (Weinheimer et al., 1993; Holzschuh et al., 1992; Kawami et al., 1991; Stoeffl, 1992; Robertson et al., 1991) and suggests that neutrino clustering could provide seeds for stellar formation, enabling further studies of solar system dynamics and dark matter contributions.
