# Helicity and nuclear $β$ decay correlations

> We present simple derivations of nuclear $β$-decay correlations with an emphasis on the special role of helicity. This provides a good opportunity to teach students about helicity and chirality in particle physics through exercises using simple aspects of quantum mechanics. In addition, this paper serves as an introduction to nuclear $β$-decay correlations from both a theoretical and experimental vantage. This article can be used to introduce students to ongoing experiments searching for hints of new physics in the low-energy precision frontier.

## Key claim

The paper presents accessible derivations of nuclear β-decay correlations using only elementary quantum mechanics, showing that these correlations arise simply from conservation of angular momentum and the left-handed chirality of emitted leptons, without requiring the full formalism of quantum field theory or trace techniques with Dirac γ matrices.

## Method

The authors derive angular correlation formulas for β decay by expressing helicity states as linear combinations of spin-up and spin-down states along a quantization axis, then applying conservation of angular momentum and the known left-handed chirality of weak-interaction leptons. They extend this approach to derive the Fierz interference term and the electron-antineutrino correlation coefficient for different current types (scalar, vector, axial-vector, tensor), using the general Lee-Yang Hamiltonian expressed in terms of Dirac γ matrices and chirality projectors.

## Key numbers

- For the β asymmetry in ⁶⁰Co decay: the correlation coefficient A = -1 for J → J-1 transitions
- The electron polarization in β decay is P_e = -p/E, confirmed experimentally by Frauenfelder (1957) and others
- For pure Fermi transitions with vector currents, the e-ν correlation coefficient a = +1; for scalar currents, a = -1
- For pure Gamow-Teller transitions with axial-vector currents, a = -1/3; for tensor currents, a = +1/3
- Present limits on non-Standard Model couplings (C_S, C_S′, C_T, C_T′) are of order 10%, as reported by Severijns (2006)

## Context

This work extends the pedagogical approach of Sakurai (1964) and Konopinski (1966) by providing derivations accessible to students who have completed introductory quantum mechanics, without requiring quantum field theory. The paper connects to the historical experiments of Wu (1957) that established parity violation, and to the subsequent determination of the V-A structure of weak interactions. It enables students to understand contemporary experiments searching for new physics through precision measurements of β-decay correlations, such as those searching for scalar or tensor currents beyond the Standard Model.
