# What is the real meaning of the Froissart theorem ?

> The Froissart bounds for amplitudes and cross sections are explained and reconsidered to clarify the role of different assumptions. It is the physical conditions of unitarity and of no massless exchanges, together with mathematical properties of the Legendre functions, that imply much softer high-energy asymptotics for elastic amplitudes at physical angles than for the same amplitudes at nonphysical angles. The canonical log-squared boundary for σ_{tot} appears only under the additional hypothesis that the amplitude at any nonphysical angle cannot grow faster than some power of energy. The Froissart results are further shown to admit some reinforcement. Comparison of the familiar and new Froissart-like restrictions with the existing data on σ_{tot} and diffraction slope at all available energies (including LHC) does not allow yet to unambiguously determine the asymptotic behavior of σ_{tot}, but shows that its current increase cannot be saturated (i.e., maximally rapid).

# What is the real meaning of the Froissart theorem?

## Key claim

The Froissart theorem does not by itself require total cross sections to grow no faster than log-squared in energy; that specific bound only appears under the additional, never-justified assumption that the amplitude at any nonphysical angle cannot grow faster than some power of energy. The truly general consequence of unitarity and the absence of massless exchanges is that physical amplitudes have much softer high-energy asymptotics than nonphysical ones.

## Method

The paper re-derives the Froissart bounds using only unitarity and the absence of massless particles, without assuming dispersion relations. It uses the partial-wave expansion and the Legendre function of the second kind to obtain a bound on partial-wave amplitudes from analyticity in the momentum-transfer plane, then determines a critical angular momentum L where the unitarity bound and the analyticity bound intersect. The asymptotic behavior of the total cross section and elastic amplitudes follows from the energy dependence of L, which in turn depends on the growth of the amplitude on a contour in the nonphysical region.

## Key numbers

- The forward/backward amplitude is bounded by L², where L is the critical angular momentum.
- The fixed-angle amplitude is bounded by L^(3/2) / (sin θ)^(1/2).
- For fixed physical (negative) t, the amplitude is bounded by L^(3/2) (s/|t|)^(1/4).
- Under the enhanced bound using a stricter inequality for Legendre functions, the relation for L becomes e^(α₀ L) √L = (1/2) B₀(s), giving a smaller L than the original Froissart derivation.
- The ratio σ_tot / b at LHC energies shows a definite increase compared to its nearly constant behavior from 10 GeV to 100 GeV.

## Context

This work re-examines the foundational assumptions of the original Froissart paper (1961) and the Gribov-Froissart representation. It extends the analysis of Azimov (2011) which provided a modified derivation without dispersion relations. The paper shows that the canonical log-squared bound is not a direct consequence of unitarity, and that phenomenological linearity of hadronic Regge trajectories provides indirect evidence for cross sections growing faster than log-squared. The observed increase of σ_tot/b at LHC energies (TOTEM collaboration data) suggests that the current growth of σ_tot is not saturated, meaning it does not follow the maximally rapid allowed behavior. This opens the question of whether hadrons at high energies become completely transparent, analogous to the Reggeon description with unit intercept where the target radius increases indefinitely.
