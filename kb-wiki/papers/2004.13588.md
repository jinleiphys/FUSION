# The Lagrange-mesh R-matrix method for inhomogenous equations

> The Lagrange-mesh $R$-matrix method is generalized to inhomogeneous equations. This method is numerically stable and efficient. It can be directly used for transfer reactions with the formalism discussed by Ascuitto and Glendenning [Phys. Rev. 181,1396 (1969)] and for inclusive breakup reactions modeled by Ichimura, Austern, and Vincent [Phys. Rev. C 32, 431 (1985)]. We first present a simple example to assess the method. Then the application to the $^{93}$Nb($d$,$pX$) non-elastic breakup is discussed.

## Key claim

The Lagrange-mesh R-matrix method is generalized to solve inhomogeneous Schrödinger equations, and is shown to be numerically stable and efficient for applications such as transfer reactions and inclusive breakup reactions, converging faster than the Green's function method and requiring fewer mesh points than the Numerov method.

## Method

The authors extend the standard R-matrix formalism to inhomogeneous equations by introducing a Bloch operator to ensure hermiticity of the Hamiltonian in the internal region and continuity of the derivative at the channel boundary. The wave function in the internal region is expanded over Lagrange functions based on Legendre polynomials, which simplify matrix element calculations through Gauss quadrature. The S-matrix for the inhomogeneous equation is derived from the continuity condition at the channel radius, and the method is tested on an analytical example and applied to the nonelastic breakup reaction ⁹³Nb(d,pX).

## Key numbers

- For the analytical example: reduced mass μ = 929.4254 MeV, center-of-mass energy E_cm = 12.74 MeV, with potential parameters V_r = 77.3 MeV, R_r = 5.21 fm, a_r = 0.77 fm, W_v = 6.1 MeV, R_w = 6.03 fm, a_w = 0.47 fm, W_s = 8.4 MeV, R_s = 6.21 fm, a_s = 0.77 fm.
- For the analytical example with channel radius a = 15 fm, the R-matrix method with N = 20 mesh points agrees well with the Numerov method (step size 0.05 fm), while the Green's function method requires at least N = 80 quadrature points for comparable accuracy.
- The R-matrix method is about 6 times faster than the Green's function method in CPU time for solving a large number of inhomogeneous equations.
- For the ⁹³Nb(d,pX) reaction at E_lab = 25.5 MeV with outgoing proton energy 14 MeV, a channel radius a ≥ 80 fm is needed for convergence at small angles.
- For the nonelastic breakup calculation with n-⁹³Nb subsystem energy E_n = 8.7 MeV and π/k_n ≈ 5 fm, the R-matrix method requires N ≈ 48-80 mesh points for a = 80 fm, compared to 1600 points needed by the Numerov method with step size 0.05 fm.

## Context

This work extends the Lagrange-mesh R-matrix method, previously applied to homogeneous scattering problems by Descouvemont (2010) and Baye (2015), to inhomogeneous equations relevant for transfer reactions using the formalism of Ascuitto and Glendenning (1969) and for inclusive breakup reactions using the model of Ichimura, Austern, and Vincent (1985). The method enables more efficient computation of nonelastic breakup cross sections than the Green's function or Numerov approaches, and can be extended to multi-channel problems and non-local interactions.
