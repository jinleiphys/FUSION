# Bose-Einstein correlations in cascade processes and non-extensive statistics

> We discuss the effect of nonextensivity of the emitting source on the Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC). This is done numerically by comparing cascade hadronization model (CAS), which is known to exhibit fractal structure in both space-time and phase-space, with its equivalent obtained from the information theory approach (MaxEnt), in which hadronization proceeds uniformly in the phase-space. To this end we have developed a new method of accounting for BEC in Monte Carlo event generators, which preserves all kinematics of the hadronization process.

# Bose-Einstein correlations in cascade processes and non-extensive statistics

## Key claim

The authors claim that Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) are sensitive to the nonextensivity parameter q and the cascade development time τ, but when comparing a fractal cascade hadronization model (CAS) with an extensive information-theory model (MaxEnt), the differences in BEC patterns are weak and depend primarily on the number of elementary phase-space cells and their multiplicities rather than on the detailed structure of the hadronizing source.

## Method

The authors developed a new Monte Carlo algorithm for incorporating Bose-Einstein correlations that preserves the energy-momenta, total multiplicities, and total charge of produced secondaries by reassigning charges to particles based on momentum-distance weights P(ij). They compared two hadronization models: a cascade model (CAS) with fractal space-time structure, and a maximum-entropy model (MaxEnt) representing uniform phase-space hadronization, using two choices for the weight function, constant P=0.5 and a Gaussian form depending on momentum and space-time separations.

## Key numbers

- For constant weight P=0.5 at M=100 GeV: CAS gives γ=0.97, λ=1.25, R=1.25 fm; MaxEnt gives γ=0.88, λ=1.26, R=1.10 fm (from exponential fit C₂(Q) = γ·[1 + λ exp(-R·Q)])
- For constant weight P=0.5 at M=10 GeV: CAS gives γ=0.84, λ=1.52, R=0.97 fm; MaxEnt gives γ=0.72, λ=1.92, R=0.79 fm
- For Gaussian weight with τ=0.2 fm at M=100 GeV: CAS gives γ=0.97, λ=0.21, R=0.46 fm; MaxEnt gives γ=1.02, λ=0.33, R=1.32 fm
- For Gaussian weight with τ=0.2 fm at M=10 GeV: CAS gives γ=0.76, λ=1.53, R=0.67 fm; MaxEnt gives γ=0.80, λ=1.58, R=0.94 fm
- For Gaussian weight with τ=1/M at M=100 GeV: CAS gives γ=0.93, λ=0.55, R=0.65 fm (MaxEnt not reported)
- Real source size for CAS with τ=0.2 fm grows from 0.29 fm at M=10 GeV to 1.61 fm at M=100 GeV; for τ=1/M from 0.12 fm to 0.62 fm

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by the same authors (Utyuzh, Wilk, Wlodarczyk, 2000) that demonstrated BEC sensitivity to the nonextensivity parameter q in cascade processes, but did not compare nonextensive and extensive approaches directly. It also builds on prior work by Bialas (1990) on fractal structure in hadronic sources and by Csorgo and collaborators (1996) on continuous emission from hydrodynamically expanding sources. The new algorithm enables direct comparison between different hadronization models, which was not possible with earlier "afterburner" methods (Csorgo, 1996) that changed original events, or with weighting procedures (Wibig, 1999) that were too complicated. The results suggest that BEC measurements may reflect elementary cell structure rather than source size, and the authors indicate that a full three-dimensional analysis will be presented elsewhere.
