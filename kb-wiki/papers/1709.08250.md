# Ground States via Spectral Combing on a Quantum Computer

> A new method is proposed for determining the ground state wave function of a quantum many-body system on a quantum computer, without requiring an initial trial wave function that has good overlap with the true ground state. The technique of Spectral Combing involves entangling an arbitrary initial wave function with a set of auxiliary qubits governed by a time dependent Hamiltonian, resonantly transferring energy out of the initial state through a plethora of avoided level crossings into the auxiliary system. The number of avoided level crossings grows exponentially with the number of qubits required to represent the Hamiltonian, so that the efficiency of the algorithm does not rely on any particular energy gap being large. We give an explicit construction of the quantum gates required for the realization of this procedure and explore the results of classical simulations of the algorithm on a small quantum computer with up to 8 qubits. We show that for certain systems and comparable results, Spectral Combing requires fewer quantum gates to realize than the Quantum Adiabatic Algorithm.

## Key claim

Spectral Combing can find the ground state of a quantum many-body system on a quantum computer without requiring an initial trial wave function with good overlap with the true ground state, and its efficiency does not rely on any particular energy gap being large, unlike the Quantum Adiabatic Algorithm.

## Method

The algorithm entangles an arbitrary initial wave function of the target system with a set of auxiliary "comb" qubits governed by a time-dependent Hamiltonian whose energy scale monotonically decreases. As the comb spectrum is dragged through the target spectrum, avoided level crossings resonantly transfer energy from the target to the comb. The procedure is iterated: after time evolution, the comb spins are measured and reset to their ground state, with the coupling parameters rescaled by a factor η < 1 for subsequent iterations.

## Key numbers

- For the 3-spin Ising model with h = 2.0, starting from a random initial state with only 0.1% overlap with the true ground state, the algorithm achieved 99% overlap after successive iterations.
- For the 3-spin Ising model with h = 0.5, using 2000 Trotter steps distributed over 2 combs, the method improved ground state overlap for a sample of 800 possible initial states and comb measurements.
- For the Ising model with an external magnetic field, Spectral Combing required approximately 50 Trotter steps per comb, compared to the Quantum Adiabatic Algorithm whose gate cost scaled as Δ⁻² (where Δ is the minimum energy gap).
- The gate cost per Trotter step was estimated as 21 (for N_t = 3) and 28 (for N_t = 4) for the QAA, and 283 (for N_t = 3) and 347 (for N_t = 4) for Spectral Combing with N_c = 3 comb qubits.

## Context

This work extends the family of quantum algorithms for ground state preparation, offering an alternative to Quantum Phase Estimation (Nielsen and Chuang, 2010) and the Quantum Adiabatic Algorithm (Farhi et al., 2000, 2001). The method is designed for systems where good trial wave functions are unknown or where adiabatic paths encounter small energy gaps, such as the unitary fermion gas, the Hubbard model away from half filling, QCD at finite baryon density, and frustrated systems like spin glasses. The authors suggest Spectral Combing could serve as a preconditioner for Quantum Phase Estimation, and note that further work is needed to determine scaling properties for larger system sizes, as has been done for other algorithms (Wecker et al., 2014).
