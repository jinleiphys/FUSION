# Neutrino Emission from Neutron Stars

> We review the main neutrino emission mechanisms in neutron star crusts and cores. Among them are the well-known reactions such as the electron-positron annihilation, plasmon decay, neutrino bremsstrahlung of electrons colliding with atomic nuclei in the crust, as well as the Urca processes and neutrino bremsstrahlung in nucleon-nucleon collisions in the core. We emphasize recent theoretical achievements, for instance, band structure effects in neutrino emission due to scattering of electrons in Coulomb crystals of atomic nuclei. We consider the standard composition of matter (neutrons, protons, electrons, muons, hyperons) in the core, and also the case of exotic constituents such as the pion or kaon condensates and quark matter. We discuss the reduction of the neutrino emissivities by nucleon superfluidity, as well as the specific neutrino emission produced by Cooper pairing of the superfluid particles. We also analyze the effects of strong magnetic fields on some reactions, such as the direct Urca process and the neutrino synchrotron emission of electrons. The results are presented in the form convenient for practical use. We illustrate the effects of various neutrino reactions on the cooling of neutron stars. In particular, the neutrino emission in the crust is critical in setting the initial thermal relaxation between the core and the crust. Finally, we discuss the prospects of exploring the properties of supernuclear matter by confronting cooling simulations with observations of the thermal radiation from isolated neutron stars.

## Key claim

This review systematically catalogs all known neutrino emission mechanisms in neutron star crusts and cores, but the actual cooling rate of any given neutron star depends critically on uncertain properties such as the equation of state, the presence and strength of nucleon superfluidity, the possible existence of exotic matter (pion/kaon condensates or quark matter), and the internal magnetic field strength, none of which are uniquely determined by current theory.

## Method

The authors compiled and unified the theoretical expressions for neutrino emissivities from all relevant reactions: electron-positron annihilation, plasmon decay, neutrino bremsstrahlung from electron-nucleus collisions in the crust, the direct and modified Urca processes in the core, neutrino bremsstrahlung from nucleon-nucleon collisions, and the specific emission from Cooper pairing of superfluid baryons. They then incorporated the effects of nucleon superfluidity (which suppresses some reactions and enables the Cooper-pair emission) and strong magnetic fields (which enable synchrotron neutrino emission), and illustrated the impact of each mechanism on simulated neutron star cooling curves.

## Key numbers

- Mean neutron star density: approximately 7 × 10^14 g cm^-3, several times the standard nuclear density ρ0 = 2.8 × 10^14 g cm^-3.
- Central density can reach (10–20) ρ0.
- The direct Urca process, if allowed, enhances neutrino luminosity by 5–6 orders of magnitude compared to the standard modified Urca processes.
- The outer crust extends to density ρd ≈ 4 × 10^11 g cm^-3; the inner crust extends from ρd to about 0.5 ρ0.
- The ion-coupling parameter for crystallization is Γm ≈ 172; the corresponding melting temperature is Tm ≈ 1.32 × 10^7 Z^(5/3) (ρ12 Ye)^(1/3) K.
- The ion plasma temperature is Tp ≈ 7.83 × 10^9 (Z Ye ρ12 / Ai)^(1/2) K.
- The electron degeneracy temperature is T0 ≡ me c^2 / kB ≈ 5.930 × 10^9 K.
- The muon threshold occurs when the electron chemical potential exceeds mμ c^2 = 105.7 MeV.
- Critical temperatures for neutron and proton superfluidity in the core range from about 10^8 K to about 10^10 K depending on the microscopic model.
- For quark matter, pairing of different quarks could produce kB Tc up to 50 MeV, about one-tenth of the quark Fermi energy (~500 MeV).

## Context

This review extends and unifies earlier treatments of neutrino emission in neutron stars, including the works of Bisnovatyi-Kogan (1989), Pethick (1992), Itoh et al. (1996), and Yakovlev et al. (1999b). It specifically incorporates recent theoretical developments such as band-structure effects from electron scattering in Coulomb crystals, the neutrino emission from Cooper pairing of superfluid particles, and the effects of strong magnetic fields on the direct Urca process and electron synchrotron emission. The results are presented in a form suitable for implementation in neutron star cooling codes, enabling future comparisons between cooling simulations and observations of thermal radiation from isolated neutron stars to constrain the properties of supernuclear matter.
