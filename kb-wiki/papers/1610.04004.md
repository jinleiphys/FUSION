# $K^- N$ amplitudes below threshold constrained by multinucleon absorption

> Six widely different subthreshold $K^- N$ scattering amplitudes obtained in SU(3) chiral-model EFT approaches by fitting to low-energy and threshold data are employed in optical-potential studies of kaonic atoms. Phenomenological terms representing $K^-$ multinucleon interactions are added to the EFT inspired single-nucleon part of the $K^-$-nucleus optical potential in order to obtain good fits to kaonic-atom strong-interaction level shifts and widths across the periodic table. Introducing as a further constraint the fractions of single-nucleon $K^-$ absorption at rest from old bubble-chamber experiments, it is found that only two of the models considered here reproduce these absorption fractions. Within these two models, the interplay between single-nucleon and multinucleon $K^-$ interactions explains features observed previously with fully phenomenological optical potentials. Radial sensitivities of kaonic atom observables are also re-examined, and remarks are made on the role of `subthreshold kinematics' in absorption at rest calculations.

# $K^- N$ amplitudes below threshold constrained by multinucleon absorption

## Key claim

Only two of six widely different subthreshold $K^- N$ scattering amplitudes derived from SU(3) chiral-model EFT approaches (the Prague and Kyoto-Munich models) reproduce the experimentally measured fractions of single-nucleon $K^-$ absorption at rest from bubble-chamber experiments, when these fractions are used as an additional constraint beyond standard fits to kaonic-atom strong-interaction level shifts and widths across the periodic table.

## Method

Six different $K^- N$ s-wave center-of-mass amplitudes from four chiral SU(3) meson-baryon coupled-channel dynamical models at next-to-leading order (Kyoto-Munich, Prague, Murcia with two variants M1 and M2, and Bonn with two variants B2 and B4) were employed as the single-nucleon part of the $K^-$-nucleus optical potential. A phenomenological multinucleon term of the form $B(\rho/\rho_0)^\alpha$ was added, and the full optical potential was constructed self-consistently using a subthreshold kinematics algorithm that maps local nuclear density to the Mandelstam variable $\sqrt{s}$. The fractions of single-nucleon versus multinucleon absorption at rest were calculated from the overlap integrals of the imaginary potential with the kaonic atom wave functions and compared to bubble-chamber data.

## Key numbers

- Experimental multinucleon absorption-at-rest fraction: 0.25$\pm$0.05, derived from three bubble-chamber experiments: Davis et al. (1968) reported 0.26$\pm$0.03 for a mixture of C, F and Br; Moulder et al. (1971) reported 0.28$\pm$0.03 for Ne; Vander Velde-Wilquet et al. (1977) reported 0.19$\pm$0.03 for C.
- $\chi^2$ values for 65 kaonic-atom data points using single-nucleon potentials alone ranged from 1174 (B2) to 3548 (M2); adding the multinucleon term reduced $\chi^2$ to between 105 (B4) and 125 (P).
- For the acceptable Prague (P) and Kyoto-Munich (KM) models with $\alpha$ fixed to 1.0: Re$B$ = $-$1.3$\pm$0.2 fm (P) and $-$0.9$\pm$0.2 fm (KM); Im$B$ = 1.5$\pm$0.2 fm (P) and 1.4$\pm$0.2 fm (KM).
- For the unacceptable models with $\alpha$ fixed: B2 ($\alpha$=0.3, Re$B$=2.4$\pm$0.2 fm, Im$B$=0.8$\pm$0.1 fm); B4 ($\alpha$=0.3, Re$B$=3.1$\pm$0.1 fm, Im$B$=0.8$\pm$0.1 fm); M1 ($\alpha$=0.3, Re$B$=0.3$\pm$0.1 fm, Im$B$=0.8$\pm$0.1 fm); M2 ($\alpha$=1.0, Re$B$=2.1$\pm$0.2 fm, Im$B$=1.2$\pm$0.2 fm).
- For carbon, about 75% of absorptions take place from the upper atomic state, based on a radiation yield of 0.07$\pm$0.013.
- The subthreshold energy range accessed by the density-to-energy transformation reaches densities as high as 50% of $\rho_0$ = 0.17 fm$^{-3}$; kaonic atoms are insensitive to densities above 60% of $\rho_0$.

## Context

This work extends the self-consistent subthreshold kinematics approach developed by Cieply et al. (2011a, 2011b) and Friedman and Gal (2012, 2013) by introducing bubble-chamber absorption-at-rest fractions as a new constraint on the $K^- N$ amplitudes. It contradicts the assumption that all six chiral-model amplitudes (from IHW 2012, Cieply and Smejkal 2012, Geng and Oset 2013, and Mai and Meissner 2015) are equally valid for kaonic-atom calculations, showing that only the Prague and Kyoto-Munich amplitudes satisfy the absorption constraint. The results explain features previously observed with fully phenomenological optical potentials (Friedman, Gal, and Batty 1993; Batty, Friedman, and Gal 1997; Friedman and Gal 2007), particularly the compression of the real part of the potential and the radial sensitivity of kaonic atoms being limited to surface densities. This enables future microscopic treatments of the multinucleon part of the $K^-$-nucleus optical potential.
