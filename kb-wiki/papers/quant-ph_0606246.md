# Stochastic Schroedinger equation from optimal observable evolution

> In this article, we consider a set of trial wave-functions denoted by $| Q \right>$ and an associated set of operators $A_α$ which generate transformations connecting those trial states. Using variational principles, we show that we can always obtain a quantum Monte-Carlo method where the quantum evolution of a system is replaced by jumps between density matrices of the form $D = |Q_a> <Q_b|$, and where the average evolutions of the moments of $A_α$ up to a given order $k$, i.e. $< A_{α_1} >$, $< A_{α_1} A_{α_2} >$,..., $< A_{α_1} ... A_{α_k} >$, are constrained to follow the exact Ehrenfest evolution at each time step along each stochastic trajectory. Then, a set of more and more elaborated stochastic approximations of a quantum problem is obtained which approach the exact solution when more and more constraints are imposed, i.e. when $k$ increases. The Monte-Carlo process might even become exact if the Hamiltonian $H$ applied on the trial state can be written as a polynomial of $A_α$. The formalism makes a natural connection between quantum jumps in Hilbert space and phase-space dynamics. We show that the derivation of stochastic Schroedinger equations can be greatly simplified by taking advantage of the existence of this hierarchy of approximations and its connection to the Ehrenfest theorem. Several examples are illustrated: the free wave-packet expansion, the Kerr oscillator, a generalized version of the Kerr oscillator, as well as interacting bosons or fermions.

## Key claim

The paper shows that a hierarchy of stochastic approximations to quantum dynamics can be systematically constructed by imposing that the average evolution of moments of selected operators up to order \(k\) follows the exact Ehrenfest theorem at each time step, with the Monte-Carlo process becoming exact if the Hamiltonian applied to the trial state is a polynomial of those operators.

## Method

The author starts from a set of trial wave-functions \(|Q\rangle\) and associated generators \(A_\alpha\) that produce transformations between them. Using variational principles on the average action, stochastic contributions (gaussian noises for second moments, higher-order noises for higher moments) are introduced as variational parameters, leading to a hierarchy where at level \(k\) the average evolution of all moments of \(A_\alpha\) up to order \(k\) matches the exact Ehrenfest evolution. The formalism is then applied to derive explicit stochastic Schroedinger equations for the free wave-packet expansion, the Kerr oscillator, a generalized Kerr oscillator, and interacting bosons or fermions.

## Key numbers

* Free wave-packet expansion: initial width \(\langle x^2 \rangle = 1/(2\eta)\) with \(\eta = m\omega/\hbar\); exact evolution gives \(\langle x^2 \rangle = 1/(2\eta) + (\hbar^2 \eta / (2m^2)) t^2\).
* Free wave-packet expansion: the Hamiltonian after release is \(H = p^2/(2m) = -(\hbar\omega/4)(a^+-a)^2\), a polynomial of order 2 in creation/annihilation operators.
* Kerr oscillator and generalized Kerr oscillator: mentioned as illustrative examples but no specific numerical results are given in the provided text.
* Interacting bosons or fermions: mentioned as examples but no specific numerical results are given in the provided text.

## Context

This work extends variational principles for trial wave-functions (Blaizot 1986, Feldmeier 2000) into a stochastic framework, building on prior stochastic Schroedinger equation approaches (Diosi 1985, Gisin 1984, Ghirardi 1990, Dalibard 1992, Dum 1992, Gisin 1992, Carmichael 1993, Diosi 1994, Imamoglu 1994, Breuer 1995, Rigo 1996, Castin 1996, Plenio 1998, Gardiner 2000, Breuer 2002, Stockburger 2002) and recent exact Monte-Carlo methods for many-body systems (Carusotto 2001, Juillet 2002, Juillet 2004, Breuer 2004, Lacroix 2005). The hierarchy enables systematic improvement of stochastic approximations and provides a direct connection between quantum jumps in Hilbert space and phase-space dynamics, simplifying the derivation of stochastic Schroedinger equations for complex systems.
