# Description of nucleon transfer reactions at intermediate energies within the impulse picture

> Background: At intermediate energies, transfer reactions are suppressed because the momentum-matching condition is difficult to satisfy. In the standard distorted wave Born approximation (DWBA), a high momentum component of the transferred particle is required to match the large momentum transfer. Purpose: We investigate the applicability of the distorted wave impulse approximation (DWIA) for describing ($p,d$) transfer reactions at intermediate energies by performing a comparative study with the standard DWBA. DWIA, which has been successful for knockout reactions, is expected to provide an alternative reaction mechanism at this energy region. Methods: Both DWBA and DWIA formalisms are applied to the $^{16}$O($p,d$){}$^{15}$O reaction at 200~MeV. In DWBA, the reaction is described as a neutron pickup, while in DWIA, it is treated as a quasi-elastic scattering from a preformed deuteron cluster in the target. Results: The DWBA calculation is in good agreement with the experimental data, reproducing both the angular distribution and the absolute magnitude of the cross section with a reasonable spectroscopic factor. In contrast, the DWIA calculation, while qualitatively reproducing the trend of the angular distribution, severely underestimates the cross section by about two orders of magnitude. Conclusions: Our findings suggest that conventional DWBA provides a more suitable description for the $^{16}$O($p,d$){}$^{15}$O reaction at 200~MeV. The failure of DWIA in this case, unlike its success in knockout reactions, raises open questions about its applicability to transfer reactions. This motivates the need for systematic investigations to delineate the applicability of both reaction mechanisms under various conditions.

## Key claim

The distorted wave impulse approximation (DWIA), which treats the reaction as quasi-elastic scattering from a preformed deuteron cluster, severely underestimates the cross section for the 16O(p,d)15O reaction at 200 MeV by about two orders of magnitude, whereas the standard distorted wave Born approximation (DWBA) reproduces both the angular distribution and absolute magnitude with a reasonable spectroscopic factor.

## Method

Both DWBA and DWIA formalisms were applied to the 16O(p,d)15O reaction at an incident energy of 200 MeV. In DWBA, the reaction was described as a neutron pickup using the prior-form transition matrix with zero-range approximation; in DWIA, the reaction was treated as a quasi-elastic scattering between the incident proton and a preformed deuteron cluster in the target, using the asymptotic momentum approximation (AMA) and the p-d elastic scattering cross section data as input for the elementary process.

## Key numbers

- The DWIA calculation underestimates the experimental cross section by a factor of approximately 100 (scaling factor of 100 required to match data in the region 4° < θ < 15°).
- The PWIA calculation underestimates the experimental cross section by a factor of approximately 20 (scaling factor of 20 required to match data in the same angular region).
- The absorption effect (difference between PWIA and DWIA scaling factors) makes the cross section smaller by about a factor of five.
- The zero-range interaction strength D0 used in DWBA was set to 125 MeV·fm^(3/2).
- The momentum transfer q at θ = 0° is approximately 0.9 fm^(-1); at θ = 40°, q is approximately 2.5 fm^(-1).
- The deuteron momentum distribution has a node around k_d^(0) = 0.8 fm^(-1).

## Context

This work extends the foundational concept explored in Kam00 and Ues02, which applied a similar impulse-picture idea to (p,d) reactions but restricted to forward-angle kinematics. The study directly compares DWIA and DWBA for the 16O(p,d)15O reaction at 200 MeV, finding that DWIA, successful for knockout reactions such as (p,pN) (Wakasa, 2017), (p,pα) (Yoshida, 2019), and (p,pd) (Samanta, 1982; Terashima, 2018), fails for this transfer reaction. The results raise open questions about the applicability of DWIA to transfer reactions and motivate systematic investigations to delineate the applicability of both reaction mechanisms under various conditions.
