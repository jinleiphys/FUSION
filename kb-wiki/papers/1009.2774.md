# Anomaly and a QCD-like phase diagram with massive bosonic baryons

> We study a strongly coupled $Z_2$ lattice gauge theory with two flavors of quarks, invariant under an exact $\mathrm{SU}(2)\times \mathrm{SU}(2) \times \mathrm{U}_A(1) \times \mathrm{U}_B(1)$ symmetry which is the same as QCD with two flavors of quarks without an anomaly. The model also contains a coupling that can be used to break the $\mathrm{U}_A(1)$ symmetry and thus mimic the QCD anomaly. At low temperatures $T$ and small baryon chemical potential $μ_B$ the model contains massless pions and massive bosonic baryons similar to QCD with an even number of colors. In this work we study the $T-μ_B$ phase diagram of the model and show that it contains three phases : (1) A chirally broken phase at low $T$ and $μ_B$, (2) a chirally symmetric baryon superfluid phase at low $T$ and high $μ_B$, and (3) a symmetric phase at high $T$. We find that the nature of the finite temperature chiral phase transition and in particular the location of the tricritical point that seperates the first order line from the second order line is affected significantly by the anomaly.

# Anomaly and a QCD-like phase diagram with massive bosonic baryons

## Key claim
The paper claims that the temperature-baryon chemical potential phase diagram of a strongly coupled Z2 lattice gauge theory with two flavors contains three phases and that the location of the tricritical point separating first-order from second-order chiral phase transitions is significantly affected by the strength of the U_A(1) anomaly, but this conclusion is hedged by the fact that the model is a toy model with bosonic baryons and an even number of colors, not real QCD.

## Method
The authors study a Z2 lattice gauge theory on a four-dimensional hypercubic lattice with two-component Grassmann fields representing two flavors of quarks, using a world line formulation that avoids the sign problem. They perform Monte Carlo simulations using the directed path algorithm on L^3 × 4 lattices with L = 12, 16, 24, 32, measuring chiral current susceptibility (Y_C), axial current susceptibility (Y_A), and baryon current susceptibility (Y_B) to identify spontaneous symmetry breaking and determine the order of phase transitions via finite-size scaling analysis.

## Key numbers
- At c = 0 (no anomaly) and μ_B = 0.0, the finite temperature chiral phase transition is first order with T_c ≈ 2.168 on L = 32.
- At c = 0 and μ_B = 0.8, the superfluid-to-symmetric transition is second order in the 3d XY universality class with T_c = 1.7879(6) and ν fixed to 0.6715.
- At c = 0, the three-phase coexistence point M is located at roughly T = 1.70(5) and μ_B = 0.57(1).
- At c = 0.3 (large anomaly) and μ_B = 0.0, the chiral transition is second order in the 3d O(4) universality class with T_c = 2.4753(4) and ν fixed to 0.745.
- At c = 0.3 and μ_B = 0.7, the superfluid-to-symmetric transition is second order in the 3d XY universality class with T_c = 2.0811(6).
- At c = 0.02 and μ_B = 0.3, the chiral transition shows a two-state signal characteristic of first order, while at μ_B = 0.1 it fits O(4) scaling, giving a tricritical point at roughly μ_B ∼ 0.25(5).
- The renormalized baryon mass M_B is bounded in the range 0.8 ≥ M_B ≥ 0.5 for the chosen parameters m_B = 0.1 and ω_D = 1.0.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous construction of a two-flavor QCD-like model with SU(2) × SU(2) × U_A(1) × U_B(1) symmetry in a world line formulation (Chandrasekharan 2007, 2008) by adding massive bosonic baryons and studying the full T-μ_B phase diagram. It contrasts with mean-field studies of NJL and PNJL models (Buballa 2003, Ratti 2005) by providing ab initio Monte Carlo results without the sign problem, and with two-color lattice QCD studies (Kogut 1999, 2001) where baryons are massless due to extra symmetries. The work enables future studies of how the anomaly strength affects the location of the QCD critical point, and suggests that the points B and M in the phase diagram may be more robust than the tricritical point C, which is strongly anomaly-dependent.
