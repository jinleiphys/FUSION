# Effects of Minijets on Common Observables in Heavy-Ion Collisions with Uncommon Implications

> In this brief review of the observable effects of minijets in heavy-ion collisions the main points emphasized are that the quadruple moment $v_2(p_T,b)$ and the hadronic ($π$ and $p$) spectra at low $p_T$ can both be reproduced by minijet contributions to the recombination of thermal and shower partons. Without using hydrodynamics the minijet approach does not trace the evolution of the expanding system. The thermal distribution of the medium partons at the time of hadronization is assumed, but rapid thermalization initially is not required so as to allow minijets to leave their footprints on the system in the final state. Azimuthal anisotropy due to minijets is directly calculated in the momentum space without any fluid assumption relating the spatial eccentricity to $v_2$. There are no more parameters used, compared to the hydro approach in fitting the data on $v_2$ and $p_T$ spectra. Thus both approaches satisfy the sufficiency condition for a viable description of the dynamical process involved.

## Key claim

Minijet contributions to the recombination of thermal and shower partons can reproduce both the elliptic flow \(v_2(p_T,b)\) and the hadron spectra at low \(p_T\) without using hydrodynamics, but the thermal distribution of the medium partons at hadronization is assumed rather than derived, and the approach does not trace the evolution of the expanding system.

## Method

The single-particle distribution for hadrons at mid-rapidity is separated into a \(\phi\)-independent base component (from thermal parton recombination) and a \(\phi\)-dependent ridge component (from enhanced thermal partons near a semihard parton trajectory). The azimuthal anisotropy is calculated directly in momentum space using a surface segment function \(S(\phi,b)\) that relates the spatial ellipse of the initial overlap region to the momentum anisotropy of final-state hadrons, without invoking fluid dynamics or spatial eccentricity. The \(p_T\) dependence of the base and ridge components is expressed via exponential distributions with different inverse-slope temperatures, and the second Fourier harmonic \(v_2\) is obtained from the ratio of the ridge to the base as a function of \(p_T\) and centrality.

## Key numbers

- The background temperature \(T_0 = 0.245\) GeV is the single adjustable parameter used to fit \(v_2(p_T,b)\) for both pions and protons at 0-5% centrality.
- The common exponential temperature for hadron spectra is \(T = 0.283\) GeV, determined phenomenologically.
- The ridge temperature \(T_R\) is approximately 0.32 GeV, higher than the base temperature \(T_0\).
- The minijet approach reproduces \(v_2\) data for \(N_{\rm part} > 100\) and for \(p_T < 2\) GeV/c without adjustable parameters in the centrality dependence.
- The equilibration time \(\tau_0 = 0.6\) fm/c is used in typical hydro calculations, but is not required in the minijet approach.

## Context

This paper reviews the minijet approach developed by Hwa and collaborators (Hwa 2013, Hwa and Yang 2008, Hwa and Zhu 2011) as an alternative to the standard hydrodynamical description of heavy-ion collisions (Kharzeev and Heinz 2007, Teaney 2003). It extends earlier work on the ridge phenomenon (Putschke 2007, Daugherity 2008, Takahashi 2009) and the recombination model (Hwa and Yang 2003, Hwa and Yang 2004) by showing that minijets can account for both the \(p_T\) spectra and the azimuthal anisotropy \(v_2\) without invoking flow or pressure gradients. The approach enables a complementary perspective on the role of semihard partons in the low-\(p_T\) region, suggesting that the "standard model" of hydrodynamics may be incomplete, especially at higher LHC energies where minijet densities are larger.
