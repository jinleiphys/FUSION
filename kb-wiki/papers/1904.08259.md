# The analytic structure of thermodynamic systems with repulsive interactions

> Thermodynamic properties of systems with repulsive interactions, are considered in the grand canonical ensemble. The analytic structure of the excluded-volume model in the complex plane of the system chemical potential (fugacity) is elaborated, based on the fact that the pressure function can be given in terms of the Lambert W-function. Even though the excluded volume model has no phase transitions at real values of the chemical potential, it does exhibit a branch cut singularity in the complex plane, thus limiting the convergence range of the Taylor expansion in the chemical potential. Close similarities to analytic properties of the other models with repulsive interactions, such as a cluster expansion model, the mean-field model, and the ideal Fermi gas model, are pointed out. As an example, repulsive baryonic interactions in a hadron gas, with a focus on the fugacity/virial and Taylor expansion methods used in lattice QCD, are presented. The asymptotic behavior of the Fourier expansion coefficients in these various models suggests that the singular part of net baryonic density can to leading order be universally expressed in terms of polylogarithms.

## Key claim

Even though the excluded volume model has no phase transitions at real values of the chemical potential, it does exhibit a branch cut singularity in the complex plane, thus limiting the convergence range of the Taylor expansion in the chemical potential. The asymptotic behavior of the Fourier expansion coefficients in various repulsive interaction models suggests that the singular part of net baryonic density can to leading order be universally expressed in terms of polylogarithms.

## Method

The authors analytically solved the excluded volume model for a single-component Maxwell-Boltzmann gas using the Lambert W-function representation of the pressure, then extended this to a hadron resonance gas with repulsive baryonic interactions (EV-HRG model). They compared the analytic structure, Taylor expansion convergence radii, and Fourier coefficient asymptotics across four models: the excluded volume model, the mean-field model, the cluster expansion model, and the ideal Fermi gas, all evaluated in the grand canonical ensemble at finite baryon chemical potential.

## Key numbers

- The branch point of the single-component excluded volume model occurs at \(z = z_{\rm br} \equiv -e^{-1}\) in the complex fugacity plane, with \(|R(z)| \to e\) as \(z \to z_{\rm br}\)
- For the EV-HRG model at \(T = 155\) MeV with \(b = 1\) fm\(^3\) and \(b \, \phi_B(T=155\ {\rm MeV}) \simeq 0.026\), the branch points are at \(\mu_B^{\rm br}/T \simeq \pm 2.634 \pm i \pi\) and the radius of convergence is \(r_{\mu/T} \simeq 4.1\) (i.e. \(r_{\mu} \simeq 635\) MeV)
- The large-\(k\) asymptotic behavior of Fourier coefficients differs by power-law factor: EV model gives \(b_k \sim k^{-1/2}\), mean-field model gives \(b_k \sim k^{-3/2}\), cluster expansion model gives \(b_k \sim k^{-1}\), and ideal Fermi gas gives \(b_k \sim k^{-3/2}\)
- All models with repulsive interactions predict \(r_{\mu/T} \sim 3-5\) at \(T > 140\) MeV, reaching a minimum value of \(r_{\mu/T}^{\rm min} = \pi\) at \(T \simeq 190-200\) MeV

## Context

This work extends the analytic understanding of repulsive interaction models previously studied for Lee-Yang zeros by Hauge and Hemmer (1963) and for the excluded volume model by Rischke et al. (1991) and Noronha-Hostler et al. (2012). The results directly inform the interpretation of lattice QCD extrapolation methods: the Taylor expansion method used by Allton et al. (2002), Gavai and Gupta (2008), and Bazavov et al. (2017), and the Fourier coefficient method applied to lattice data by Vovchenko et al. (2017). The finding that convergence radii are limited by complex singularities unrelated to phase transitions means that lattice QCD practitioners must be cautious when interpreting apparent convergence failure as evidence for a critical point. The universal polylogarithmic form of the singular part of net baryon density across all four models suggests a deeper mathematical structure that could guide future analytic continuation strategies.
