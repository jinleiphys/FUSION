# The neutron star mass, distance, and inclination from precision timing of the brilliant millisecond pulsar J0437$-$4715

> The observation of neutron stars enables the otherwise impossible study of fundamental physical processes. The timing of binary radio pulsars is particularly powerful, as it enables precise characterization of their (three-dimensional) positions and orbits. PSR~J0437$-$4715 is an important millisecond pulsar for timing array experiments and is also a primary target for the Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer (NICER). The main aim of the NICER mission is to constrain the neutron star equation of state by inferring the compactness ($M_p/R$) of the star. Direct measurements of the mass $M_p$ from pulsar timing therefore substantially improve constraints on the radius $R$ and the equation of state. Here we use observations spanning 26 years from Murriyang, the 64-m Parkes radio telescope, to improve the timing model for this pulsar. Among the new precise measurements are the pulsar mass $M_p=1.418\pm 0.044$ $M_{\odot}$, distance $D=156.96 \pm 0.11$ pc, and orbital inclination angle $i=137.506 \pm 0.016^\circ$, which can be used to inform the X-ray pulse profile models inferred from NICER observations. We demonstrate that these results are consistent between multiple data sets from the Parkes Pulsar Timing Array (PPTA), each modeled with different noise assumptions. Using the longest available PPTA data set, we measure an apparent second derivative of the pulsar spin frequency and discuss how this can be explained either by kinematic effects due to the proper motion and radial velocity of the pulsar or excess low-frequency noise such as a gravitational-wave background.

## Key claim

The paper reports precise measurements of the pulsar mass, distance, and orbital inclination for PSR J0437-4715 using 26 years of Parkes radio telescope data, but the results depend on the specific noise model assumed and the data set used, with the most precise values coming from the PPTA-DR3 data set after achieving statistically white and Gaussian residuals.

## Method

The authors analyzed timing observations from the Parkes Pulsar Timing Array (PPTA) spanning up to 22 years, using generalized least-squares fitting in the tempo2 software package with noise models that included white-noise parameters and Gaussian random processes for achromatic red noise, dispersion measure variations, and band noise. They compared results from three data releases (PPTA-DR1e, PPTA-DR2e, and PPTA-DR3) and produced an ad hoc PPTA-DR2.5 data set by reprocessing pulse profiles to achieve statistically white residuals. The pulsar mass was derived from Shapiro delay combined with the binary mass function, the distance from the Shklovskii effect and parallax, and the orbital inclination from annual-orbital parallax (Kopeikin terms) and Shapiro delay.

## Key numbers

* Pulsar mass from PPTA-DR3: M_p = 1.418 ± 0.044 M_sun
* Distance from Shklovskii effect (PPTA-DR3): D = 156.98 ± 0.15 pc
* Distance from Shklovskii effect (PPTA-DR2e): D = 156.96 ± 0.11 pc
* Orbital inclination angle (PPTA-DR3): i = 137.506 ± 0.016 degrees
* Companion mass (PPTA-DR3): M_c = 0.221 ± 0.004 M_sun
* Orbital period derivative (PPTA-DR2e): dot_P_b = 3.7329 ± 0.0015 × 10^(-12)
* Parallax (PPTA-DR2e): pi = 6.43 ± 0.04 mas
* Spin frequency second derivative (PPTA-DR2e): ddot_f = -61 ± 15 × 10^(-30) s^(-3)
* Radial velocity derived from ddot_f: v_r = -75 ± 18 km/s
* Periastron advance (PPTA-DR2e): dot_omega = 0.0134 ± 0.0006 deg/yr

## Context

This work extends the timing analysis of PSR J0437-4715 from earlier PPTA studies by Reardon (2016) and the PPTA collaboration (2013, 2021), achieving improved precision through better noise modeling that produced statistically white residuals for the first time. The measurements provide Gaussian priors for the NICER mission's X-ray pulse profile modeling, which aims to constrain the neutron star radius and equation of state, complementing prior NICER results for PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620. The detection of a second derivative of spin frequency enables a radial velocity estimate, though this may be contaminated by low-frequency noise such as a gravitational-wave background.
