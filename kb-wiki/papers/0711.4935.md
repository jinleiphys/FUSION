# Study of event-by-event fluctuations in heavy ion collisions

> We propose Kolmogorov-Smirnov test as a means for recognising event-by-event fluctuations of rapidity distributions in relativistic heavy ion collisions. Such fluctuations may be induced by the spinodal decomposition of the rapidly expanding system during the 1st order quark-hadron phase transition.

# Study of event-by-event fluctuations in heavy ion collisions

## Key claim
The Kolmogorov-Smirnov test can detect non-statistical event-by-event fluctuations in rapidity distributions that may be induced by spinodal decomposition during a first-order quark-hadron phase transition, but the authors caution that if such fluctuations are observed, it does not automatically imply they are caused by droplets.

## Method
The authors applied the Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) test to pairs of simulated heavy-ion collision events, computing the quantity Q = 1 - H from the maximum difference between empirical rapidity distributions. They first validated the method on events generated from two different Gaussian distributions with varying mean values, then applied it to events generated by a Monte Carlo droplet generator that simulates particle emission from fragments emerging from spinodal decomposition, varying droplet size and the fraction of particles originating from droplets.

## Key numbers
- For Gaussian test distributions with means differing by 0.01 (mu = 1.99 vs 2.00, sigma = 0.1), the KS test clearly recognized the events were not drawn from a single distribution; multiplicities ranged from 8 to 535.
- Droplet sizes tested were 50 fm^3 and 10 fm^3 at temperature T = 175 MeV, using samples of 10^5 event pairs from 10^5 simulated events.
- Non-statistical fluctuations were present even when only 20% of particles came from droplets (compared to 60% case), and vanished for the 0% case.

## Context
This work extends prior studies of spinodal decomposition in heavy ion collisions (Tomasik, Scavenius) by proposing a specific statistical test to identify the resulting event-by-event fluctuations. The method is intended to be applied to experimental data from the NA49 collaboration. The authors note that while the KS test can detect fluctuations, alternative physical mechanisms beyond droplet formation could also produce such signals, leaving the interpretation as an open question.
