# Quark-Hadron Duality in Structure Functions

> While quark-hadron duality is well-established experimentally, the current theoretical understanding of this important phenomenon is quite limited. To expose the essential features of the dynamics behind duality, we use a simple model in which the hadronic spectrum is dominated by narrow resonances made of valence quarks. We qualitatively reproduce the features of duality as seen in electron scattering data within our model. We show that in order to observe duality, it is essential to use the appropriate scaling variable and scaling function. In addition to its great intrinsic interest in connecting the quark-gluon and hadronic pictures, an understanding of quark-hadron duality could lead to important benefits in extending the applicability of scaling into previously inaccessible regions.

## Key claim

The authors qualitatively reproduce the features of Bloom-Gilman duality using a simple model where the hadronic spectrum consists of narrow resonances made of valence quarks, but they caution that the model uses scalar quarks and an infinitely heavy antiquark target, so the results are not numerically comparable to nucleon data.

## Method

The authors construct a relativistic quantum-mechanical model of a light scalar quark confined to an infinitely massive scalar antiquark by a linear potential V²(r) = α r², which yields analytic solutions via the Klein-Gordon equation. They compute the inclusive inelastic electron scattering structure function as a sum over transition form factors from the ground state to excited resonances, then compare the averaged hadronic result to the free-quark scaling curve using a constituent-quark scaling variable x_cq and scaling function S_cq that account for both target and quark masses.

## Key numbers

- Constituent quark mass m = 0.33 GeV and string constant α^(1/4) = 0.4 GeV were used as phenomenological parameters
- The n=2, 4, 6, and 8 moments M_n all rise to within about 10% of their asymptotic values by Q² = 1 GeV²
- At Q² = 0, the elastic peak contributes about 44% of the asymptotic value of M₂
- At Q² = 0.5 GeV², the elastic peak accounts for about 22% of the area under the scaling curve
- The elastic peak becomes negligible for Q² ≥ 2.0 GeV²
- Scaling sets in by Q² = 5 GeV², with only a trace of resonance structure visible at that value

## Context

This work extends the seminal observation of Bloom-Gilman duality (Bloom and Gilman, 1970) by providing a theoretical framework to understand when and why duality holds, motivated by high-precision Jefferson Lab data that confirmed duality to low Q² values. The model connects Bloom-Gilman duality in electron scattering to duality in heavy quark decays, where exact duality occurs in the heavy quark limit, and demonstrates that using the appropriate scaling variable (Barbieri et al., 1970s) and scaling function is essential for observing duality at low Q². This enables future work to extend the model to three-quark systems, include spin-dependent effects, and study the quantitative breakdown of duality between the scaling regime and low energy.
