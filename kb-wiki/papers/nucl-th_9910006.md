# Describing the nucleon and its form factors at high q**2

> The nucleon form factors are calculated using a non-relativistic description in terms of constituent quarks. The emphasis is put on the reliability of present numerical methods used to solve the three-body problem in order to correctly reproduce the expected asymptotic behavior of form factors. Nucleon wave functions obtained in the hyperspherical formalism or employing Faddeev equations have been considered. While a q**(-8) behavior is expected at high q for a quark-quark force behaving like 1/r at short distances, it is found that the hypercentral approximation in the hyperspherical formalism (K=0) leads to a q**(-7) behavior. An infinite set of waves is required to get the correct behavior. Solutions of the Faddeev equations lead to the q**(-8) behavior. The amplitude of the corresponding term however depends on the number of partial waves retained in the Faddeev amplitude. The convergence to the asymptotic behavior has also been studied. Sizeable departures are observed in some cases at squared momentum transfers as high as 50 (GeV/c)**2. It is not clear whether these departures are of the order 1/q or 1/q**2 log q relatively to the dominant contribution and whether the bad convergence results from truncations in the calculations. From a comparison with the most complete Faddeev results, a q**2 validity range is obtained for the calculation made in the hyperspherical formalism or in the Faddeev approach with the minimum number of amplitudes.

## Key claim

The paper shows that non-relativistic constituent quark models can reproduce the expected \(q^{-8}\) asymptotic behavior of nucleon form factors at high momentum transfer, but only if the three-body problem is solved with sufficient numerical accuracy; the hypercentral approximation (K=0) in the hyperspherical formalism incorrectly yields a \(q^{-7}\) behavior, while Faddeev solutions give the correct \(q^{-8}\) behavior, though the amplitude of that term depends on the number of partial waves retained.

## Method

The authors solved the non-relativistic three-body Schrodinger equation for the nucleon using the Bhaduri et al. quark-quark potential, which includes a Coulomb-like term, a confining term, and a spin-spin interaction. They compared two numerical approaches: the hyperspherical harmonic formalism (truncated to K=0 and K=2) and the Faddeev equations (with 2 and 8 amplitudes in the Faddeev amplitude expansion). The nucleon electromagnetic form factors were then computed from the resulting wave functions and analyzed for their asymptotic power-law behavior at high squared momentum transfer \(q^2\).

## Key numbers

- The quark-quark potential parameters are: \(\kappa = \kappa_{\sigma} = 102.67\ \text{MeV fm}\), \(a = 0.0326\ \text{MeV}^{-1/2}\ \text{fm}^{1/2}\), \(r_0 = 0.4545\ \text{fm}\), quark mass \(m_q = 337\ \text{MeV}\), and \(D = 913.5\ \text{MeV}\).
- The nucleon mass obtained from the Faddeev approach with 2 amplitudes is 1031 MeV; with 8 amplitudes it is 1020 MeV; from the hyperspherical formalism (K=0,2) it is 1039 MeV.
- The square matter radius is \(0.219\ \text{fm}^2\) for both Faddeev calculations and \(0.218\ \text{fm}^2\) for the hyperspherical formalism.
- The mixed symmetry probability \(P_{S'}\) is 1.0% for the 2-amplitude Faddeev calculation, 2.1% for the 8-amplitude Faddeev calculation, and 1.5% for the hyperspherical formalism.
- Sizeable departures from the asymptotic behavior are observed at squared momentum transfers as high as \(50\ (\text{GeV}/c)^2\) in some cases.

## Context

This work extends earlier non-relativistic constituent quark model calculations of nucleon form factors, which had often relied on harmonic oscillator wave functions (Isgur and Karl, 1978) that produce an exponential falloff rather than the power-law behavior expected from perturbative QCD (Brodsky and Lepage, 1980). The paper directly compares the hyperspherical harmonic formalism (used by the Valencia group) and the Faddeev approach (used by the Grenoble group) for the same quark-quark force from Bhaduri et al. (1981). The results enable a quantitative assessment of how truncations in each numerical method affect the onset of asymptotic scaling, and they provide a \(q^2\) validity range for calculations using the hyperspherical formalism or the minimal Faddeev approach.
