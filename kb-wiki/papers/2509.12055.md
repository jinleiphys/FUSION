# Nuclear Beavers

> Nuclear physics is a very abstract field with little accessibility for wider audiences, and yet it is a field of physics with far reaching implications for everyday life. The Nuclear Beavers demonstration is a hands-on experience that offers an intuitive lens into nuclear structure and decay. We aim to provide a more accessible entry point for students and educators by substituting complex nuclear structures and interactions with tactile building blocks following well-defined rules, thereby opening nuclear physics concepts to the general public.

# Nuclear Beavers

## Key claim

The Nuclear Beavers demonstration uses wooden blocks to provide an intuitive, hands-on model of nuclear structure and decay, but the authors acknowledge that this is a simplified pedagogical tool that does not capture all nuclear phenomena (for example, fission is excluded because it would require over 200 blocks).

## Method

The authors designed a tactile demonstration where red and blue wooden blocks represent protons and neutrons, respectively, and are stacked on a vibrating table to simulate nuclear stability and decay. Participants build nuclei by following rules that mimic the nuclear shell model, magic numbers, and the Coulomb force, then apply vibrations to the table to induce gamma decay (blocks falling to lower levels), beta decay (blocks falling to change orientation or level), and nucleon emission (blocks falling off entirely). The demonstration is accompanied by a companion website and supplemental material with detailed building rules.

## Key numbers

- Lithium-5 (⁵Li) is used as an example nucleus, with 5 total nucleons (protons and neutrons combined).
- The 0s₁/₂ shell holds 2 nucleons, calculated as (1/2 × 2) + 1 = 2.
- The 0p₃/₂ shell holds 4 nucleons, calculated as (3/2 × 2) + 1 = 4.
- The nuclear landscape shown includes all known nuclei up to ⁴⁸Ca.
- Beryllium-6 (⁶Be) decays by emitting two protons simultaneously; carbon-8 (⁸C) decays by emitting four protons in pairs of two protons.

## Context

This work extends prior pedagogical approaches by Constan (2010), which demonstrated nuclear collisions without highlighting structure, and Whittaker (2013), which illustrated quark-to-nucleus structure but only in a static picture. The Nuclear Beavers demonstration complements both by allowing dynamic construction and decay of nuclei through vibration, enabling students to explore shell structure, magic numbers, beta decay, gamma decay, and nucleon emission in a tactile, interactive format. The authors suggest this demonstration can be used in lecture environments and encourage students to submit their own "discoveries" via the companion website.
