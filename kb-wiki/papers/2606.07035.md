# Solution of the Equation-of-Motion Phonon Method eigenvalue problems on the D-Wave quantum annealer

> The solution of large-scale eigenvalue problems is crucial in nuclear many-body theory, where Hamiltonian matrices often reach extremely large dimensions. Quantum computing opens new perspectives for addressing such demanding problems. Although the Quantum Phase Estimation algorithm offers, in principle, a systematic route to matrix diagonalization, its practical deployment demands levels of coherence and error correction that current quantum hardware cannot yet support. A viable near-term strategy is instead to exploit quantum annealing, which enables the recasting of eigenvalue problems into quadratic unconstrained binary optimization formulations that can be addressed by existing annealing-based processors. Here, we propose a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm that combines quantum annealing and classical deflation to iteratively extract the full eigenspectrum of both standard and generalized eigenvalue problems. We benchmark this method on eigenvalue problems arising from the Equation of Motion Phonon Method performing calculations on real quantum hardware. Our approach illustrates the capabilities and limitations of near-term quantum devices in addressing nuclear eigenvalue problems.

## Key claim

The paper presents a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm that combines quantum annealing on the D-Wave processor with classical deflation to iteratively extract the full eigenspectrum of both standard and generalized eigenvalue problems, but the method is benchmarked only on small-scale matrices arising from the Equation-of-Motion Phonon Method (EMPM) and the authors acknowledge that the approach illustrates the capabilities and limitations of near-term quantum devices rather than offering a practical advantage over classical solvers.

## Method

The authors reformulate the Rayleigh quotient minimization for eigenvalue problems as a Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) problem by discretizing continuous variables using binary encoding with a precision vector, then solve the QUBO on the D-Wave quantum annealer. After extracting the dominant eigenpair (maximum magnitude eigenvalue and corresponding eigenvector) via QUBO-based optimization, they apply classical deflation techniques, Hotelling, Orthogonal Projection, or Householder deflation, to remove the contribution of the found eigenpair from the matrix, enabling iterative extraction of subsequent eigenpairs. For generalized eigenvalue problems of the form \(A\ket{v} = \lambda B\ket{v}\), the algorithm replaces the standard Rayleigh quotient with the generalized Rayleigh quotient and updates the matrix as \(H = A - \lambda B\) instead of \(H = A - \lambda I\).

## Key numbers

* The D-Wave Advantage system used has more than 5,000 qubits and 15-way connectivity.
* The binary encoding precision vector of length \(b\) is defined as \(\bra{p} = \left( -1, \frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2^2}, \frac{1}{2^3}, \ldots, \frac{1}{2^{b-1}} \right)\), and the resulting QUBO problem has size \(n \cdot b\) for an \(n\)-variable optimization problem.
* The initial spectral shift parameter \(\lambda\) is chosen as the trace average \(\lambda = \text{tr}(A) / n\), with bounds on eigenvalues given by \(m - s\sqrt{n-1} \le \lambda_{\text{min}} \le m - \frac{s}{\sqrt{n-1}}\) and \(m + \frac{s}{\sqrt{n-1}} \le \lambda_{\text{max}} \le m + s\sqrt{n-1}\), where \(m\) is the mean and \(s\) the standard deviation of the spectrum.

## Context

This work extends the QUBO-based quantum annealing approach for eigenvalue problems introduced by Krakoff et al. (2022), which was limited to computing only the lowest-energy eigenpair, by adding classical deflation to recover the full eigenspectrum. The method is benchmarked on Hamiltonian matrices from the Equation-of-Motion Phonon Method (EMPM), a nuclear many-body technique developed by De Gregorio et al. (2021, 2022) that constructs multiphonon states to capture collective correlations. The approach enables future exploration of whether quantum annealing can complement classical iterative eigensolvers such as the Lanczos algorithm (Lanczos, 1950) and Davidson algorithm (Davidson, 1975) for large-scale nuclear structure calculations, though the current demonstration is limited to small matrices.
