# Semi-automatic Calculations of Multi-loop Feynman Amplitudes with AmpRed

> We present a Mathematica package AmpRed for the semi-automatic calculations of multi-loop Feynman amplitudes with high efficiency and precision. AmpRed implements the methods of integration by parts and differential equations in the Feynman-parameter representation. It allows for the calculations of general parametric integrals (which may not have momentum-space correspondences). Various user-friendly tools for multi-loop calculations, such as those to construct and solve differential equations for Feynman integrals, are provided. It can also deal with tensor algebras in non-relativistic field theories. Interfaces to some packages, like QGRAF and FORM, are also provided.

## Key claim

AmpRed implements integration-by-parts (IBP) reduction and differential-equation methods in the Feynman-parameter representation rather than momentum space, which the authors argue is advantageous because parametric integrals respect Lorentz symmetry at the integrand level, avoid irreducible scalar products, and allow systematic asymptotic expansions for boundary conditions. The package is described as "semi-automatic," meaning the user must provide some input and guidance, particularly for complex multi-loop integrals where the reduction may be incomplete.

## Method

The package uses two methods for integral reduction: Method I introduces auxiliary external momenta and propagators (with negative indices) to mimic the standard momentum-space IBP approach, while Method II works entirely with non-negative indices and generates symbolic reduction rules from a Gröbner basis of polynomial equations derived from parametric IBP identities. For master integrals, AmpRed constructs differential equations in the Feynman-parameter representation, introduces an auxiliary scale via a delta-function insertion for single-scale integrals, and solves the system numerically by substituting epsilon with pure numbers and restoring the epsilon dependence through fitting. Boundary conditions are determined by matching asymptotic solutions to asymptotic expansions of the master integrals, which are themselves expressed as parametric integrals and evaluated recursively until the F-polynomial has at most n+1 terms, at which point analytic evaluation is possible.

## Context

This work extends the parametric IBP and differential-equation methods developed by Chen (2019, 2020) and Chen (2023) into a unified Mathematica package. It provides an alternative to momentum-space reduction packages such as FIRE (Smirnov, 2008; 2014; 2019), Kira (Maierhofer, 2017; Klappert, 2020), and Reduze (Studerus, 2009; von Manteuffel, 2012), as well as to the differential-equation package AMFlow (Liu, 2022). The parametric approach is claimed to be the only one offering a systematic algorithm for asymptotic expansions (Pak, 2010), which enables natural determination of boundary conditions for differential equations. The package enables future calculations of multi-loop Feynman amplitudes where momentum-space methods become prohibitively expensive due to intermediate expression swell.
