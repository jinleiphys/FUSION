# Second order dissipative fluid dynamics from kinetic theory

> We derive the equations of second order dissipative fluid dynamics from the relativistic Boltzmann equation following the method of W. Israel and J. M. Stewart. We present a frame independent calculation of all first- and second-order terms and their coefficients using a linearised collision integral. Therefore, we restore all terms that were previously neglected in the original papers of W. Israel and J. M. Stewart.

# Second order dissipative fluid dynamics from kinetic theory

## Key claim

The authors derive the full set of second-order dissipative fluid-dynamic equations from the relativistic Boltzmann equation using the Israel-Stewart 14-moment method, restoring all second-order terms and coefficients that were neglected in the original work of Israel and Stewart. The calculation is performed in a frame-independent manner using a linearized collision integral, meaning the results are valid for any choice of hydrodynamic frame (Eckart or Landau-Lifshitz) without imposing the matching conditions that earlier treatments used to discard certain terms.

## Method

The derivation follows the method of Grad (1949) as generalized to relativistic systems by Israel and Stewart (1979), expanding the single-particle distribution function around local equilibrium to second order in deviations using a polynomial expansion in four-momentum up to quadratic order. The 14-moment approximation is used to close the hierarchy of moment equations from the Boltzmann equation, and the production terms from the collision integral are evaluated using a linearized collision integral with a general collision tensor decomposition. The resulting relaxation equations for bulk viscous pressure, particle/energy diffusion currents, and shear stress tensor are written in a frame-independent form, with all transport coefficients expressed in terms of thermodynamic integrals and collision tensor components.

## Key numbers

- Bulk viscosity relaxation time: $\tau_{\Pi} = -\psi_4 / \mathcal{C}_{\Pi}$, where $\psi_4$ is a combination of thermodynamic integrals $J_{nq}$ and $\mathcal{C}_{\Pi}$ is the bulk collision coefficient
- Shear viscosity coefficient: $\eta = I_{31} / \mathcal{C}_{\pi}$, where $I_{31}$ is an equilibrium thermodynamic integral and $\mathcal{C}_{\pi}$ is the shear collision coefficient
- Shear relaxation time: $\tau_{\pi} = -\psi_3 / \mathcal{C}_{\pi}$, with $\psi_3 = J_{52}/J_{42}$
- Heat conductivity coefficient: $\kappa = h \beta_0^2 D_{31} / (\mathcal{C}_W J_{31})$, where $h = (e_0 + p_0)/n_0$ is the enthalpy per particle
- In the ultrarelativistic limit ($m/T \rightarrow 0$, $e = 3p$, $\Pi = 0$): $\beta_{\pi} \rightarrow 3/(4p_0)$, $\beta_W = \beta_V \rightarrow 5 n_0/(4 \beta_0 p_0^2)$, $\beta^{\lambda}_{WW} \rightarrow 9/5$, $\beta^{\lambda}_{VV} \rightarrow 7/5$, $\delta_2 \rightarrow 4\beta_{\pi}/3$

## Context

This work extends the original Israel-Stewart formalism (Israel and Stewart, 1979) by restoring all second-order terms that were previously neglected, providing a complete frame-independent derivation of second-order dissipative fluid dynamics from kinetic theory. The results enable more accurate modeling of relativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC, LHC, and FAIR by including previously omitted couplings between dissipative currents (e.g., bulk-shear coupling, vorticity terms) that may be important in rapidly expanding systems. The paper also provides explicit ultrarelativistic limits of the transport coefficients, facilitating direct comparison with phenomenological models and numerical simulations of relativistic fluid dynamics.
