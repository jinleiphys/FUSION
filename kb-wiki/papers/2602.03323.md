# Neutron skin thickness and its volume and surface contributions

> Accurate determination of the neutron skin thickness ($ΔR_{\mathrm{np}}$) in finite nuclei is crucial for constraining the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy. In this work, we systematically investigate $ΔR_{\mathrm{np}}$ in the transuranium berkelium (Bk) isotopic chain using the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (DRHBc). Our results reveal a general increase of $ΔR_{\mathrm{np}}$ with neutron number $N$, which exhibits anti-kinks at the shell closures $N = 184, 258$ due to the shell effects. By decomposing $ΔR_{\mathrm{np}}$ into volume and surface contributions through two-parameter Fermi (2pF) fits to angle-averaged DRHBc densities, we find that the volume term accounts for as much as $68\%$ in most nuclei, whereas the surface term dominates only near the proton drip line for $N < 142$. Nuclear deformation is shown to slightly reduce the central radius $R_c$ while significantly enhancing the surface diffuseness $a$, resulting in a notable increase in $ΔR_{\mathrm{np}}$ that is largely driven by the surface term. Moreover, by extracting 2pF parameters along the symmetry axis ($θ= 0^\circ$) and perpendicular to it ($θ= 90^\circ$), we examine the anisotropy of $ΔR_{\mathrm{np}}$. In prolate deformed nuclei, a pronounced directional dependence emerges: although the nucleus elongates along the symmetry axis, $ΔR_{\mathrm{np}}$ is substantially larger in the perpendicular direction. This anisotropy is weak for oblate nuclei near shell closures. The anisotropy of $ΔR_{\rm np}$ is attributed mainly to the volume term, which remains the dominant contribution in most nuclei regardless of direction. These findings provide new insights into the interplay between deformation, shell structure, and the neutron skin in finite nuclei.

## Key claim

The authors decompose the neutron skin thickness in transuranium berkelium isotopes into volume and surface contributions using two-parameter Fermi fits to densities from deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum calculations, finding that the volume term dominates at 68% in most nuclei while the surface term dominates only near the proton drip line for neutron numbers below 142, with the caveat that these results depend on the specific density functional (PC-PK1) and the fitting procedure.

## Method

The authors performed deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (DRHBc) calculations for odd-A berkelium isotopes from the proton drip line to the neutron drip line using the PC-PK1 density functional and a zero-range pairing force. They fitted the self-consistent density distributions with a two-parameter Fermi (2pF) distribution using the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm, then extracted the neutron skin thickness and its volume and surface components using the surface expansion formalism relating the equivalent sharp radius R and surface width b. For comparison, they also performed spherical relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) calculations and examined directional dependence by fitting densities along the symmetry axis (θ = 0°) and perpendicular to it (θ = 90°).

## Key numbers

- The volume term accounts for as much as 68% of the neutron skin thickness in most Bk nuclei on the neutron-rich side
- The surface term dominates over the volume term only for neutron numbers N < 142, near the proton drip line
- Deformation enhances the surface diffuseness a by up to 40% compared to the spherical case
- The DRHBc calculation for 208Pb gives a neutron skin thickness of ΔR_np = 0.257 fm, consistent with the PREX-II experimental result of 0.283 ± 0.071 fm
- The DRHBc mass table reproduces available binding energy data with a root-mean-square deviation of σ = 1.433 MeV and charge radii with σ = 0.033 fm
- The charge radius of 208Pb has been accurately measured as r_ch = 5.5010(9) fm

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous DRHBc study of Bk isotopes (Huang 2025) that established the deformation properties of these nuclei, and builds on the surface expansion formalism introduced by Hasse and Myers (1988) and applied to neutron skin decomposition by Warda (2010) and Centelles (2010). The study enables systematic investigation of how deformation and shell structure jointly determine neutron skin thickness in heavy deformed nuclei, and provides a framework for constraining the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy through the slope parameter L, which has been shown to correlate linearly with ΔR_np in 208Pb (Roca-Maza 2011).
