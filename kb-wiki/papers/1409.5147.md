# Survival of charmonia in a hot environment

> A colorless c-cbar dipole emerging from a heavy ion collision and developing the charmonium wave function can be broken-up by final state interactions (FSI) propagating through the hot medium created in the collision. We single out two mechanisms of charmonium attenuation: (i) Debye color screening, called melting; and (ii) color-exchange interaction with the medium, called absorption. The former problem has been treated so far only for charmonia at rest embedded in the medium, while in practice their transverse momenta at the LHC are quite high, <p_T^2>=7-10 GeV^2. We demonstrate that a c-cbar dipole may have a large survival probability even at infinitely high temperature. We develop a procedure of Lorentz boosting of the Schroedinger equation to a moving reference frame and perform the first realistic calculations of the charmonium survival probability employing the path-integral technique, incorporating both melting and absorption. These effects are found to have comparable magnitudes. We also calculated the FSI suppression factor for the radial excitation psi(2S) and found it to be stronger than for J/psi, except large p_T, where psi(2S) is relatively enhanced. The azimuthal asymmetry parameter v_2 is also calculated.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a moving charmonium can have a large survival probability even at infinitely high temperature, contrary to previous treatments that assumed charmonia at rest would be completely dissociated by Debye screening. The authors show that both Debye screening ("melting") and color-exchange interactions ("absorption") contribute comparably to charmonium attenuation, and that the survival probability depends strongly on the charmonium's transverse momentum.

## Method

The authors developed a Lorentz-boosted Schrodinger equation to describe a colorless c-cbar dipole propagating through a hot medium, using path-integral techniques to account for the dipole's evolving transverse separation. They employed the Cornell potential for the binding interaction and a Debye-screened potential from lattice QCD for melting effects, while modeling absorption via an imaginary potential proportional to the transport coefficient q-hat and the dipole's velocity. The survival probability was calculated by convolving the Green function with initial and final charmonium wave functions for lead-lead collisions at LHC energies.

## Key numbers

- Mean transverse momentum squared of J/psi at LHC: <p_T^2> = 7-10 GeV^2
- String tension in Cornell potential: sigma = 1 GeV/fm
- Cornell potential parameter: alpha ≈ 0.471
- Time for medium to reach equilibrium: t_0 = 1 fm
- Trial values of transport coefficient q_0 used in calculations: 0.5, 1, 2, and 4 GeV^2/fm
- Mean quark velocity squared in charmonium rest frame: <v^2> ≈ 0.2, giving <lambda^2> = 0.017
- Path lengths for maximal melting demonstration: L = 1, 3, and 5 fm

## Context

This work extends previous treatments of charmonium suppression in heavy ion collisions, which had only considered charmonia at rest in the medium (Satz, Karsch et al.) or in a moving homogeneous plasma (Matsui, Escobedo et al.). The authors introduce a Lorentz-boosting procedure that allows realistic calculations for the high-p_T charmonia observed at LHC, and incorporate both melting and absorption mechanisms that previous work treated separately. The path-integral technique builds on earlier work by Kopeliovich et al. (1991) for cold nuclear matter, now generalized to hot deconfined media. This enables future extraction of medium transport properties from charmonium data at LHC.
