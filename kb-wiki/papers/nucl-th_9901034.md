# Coulomb dissociation of 19C

> We aim to re-investigate the structure of the neutron rich exotic nucleus ^{19}C through studies of its Coulomb breakup reactions, assuming that its excitation is to states in the low energy continuum. The method used retains all finite-range effects associated with the interactions between the breakup fragments and can use realistic wave functions for this possibly halo nucleus. We apply the method to compute the longitudinal momentum distribution of ^{18}C, exclusive neutron angular distribution and relative energy spectrum of ^{19}C following Coulomb breakup of ^{19}C on heavy targets at beam energies below 100 MeV/nucleon. The calculated results are compared with recently available experimental data. In the majority of the few cases, the data favour ^{18}C(0^+) \otimes 1s_{1/2} as the probable ground state configuration of ^{19}C, with a one-neutron separation energy of 0.53 MeV. However, it appears that the existing experimental data do not allow to draw a clear conclusion about the ground state configuration of this nucleus.

# Coulomb dissociation of 19C

## Key claim

The existing experimental data on Coulomb breakup of 19C do not allow a clear conclusion about the ground state configuration of this nucleus, although in the majority of the few available cases the data favor a 18C(0+) ⊗ 1s1/2 configuration with a one-neutron separation energy of 0.53 MeV, implying a neutron halo structure.

## Method

The author applies a fully quantum mechanical, non-perturbative theoretical model of elastic Coulomb breakup that retains all finite-range effects associated with the interactions between the breakup fragments and includes initial and final state interactions to all orders. The method assumes that only the charged core interacts with the target via a point Coulomb interaction and that the important projectile excitations are to the low-energy continuum, treated adiabatically. The author computes the longitudinal momentum distribution of 18C, the exclusive neutron angular distribution, and the relative energy spectrum of 19C following Coulomb breakup on heavy targets (Pb and Ta) at beam energies below 100 MeV/nucleon, comparing results with four different ground state configurations for 19C.

## Key numbers

- One-neutron separation energy of 0.53 MeV derived indirectly from the RIKEN experiment (Nakamura 1998) for the 18C(0+) ⊗ 1s1/2 configuration
- Width (FWHM) of the parallel momentum distribution of 18C: 27 MeV/c for option (a), 71 MeV/c for option (b), 83 MeV/c for option (c), and 41 MeV/c for option (d), the MSU data (Bazin 1995) give a width of 41 ± 3 MeV/c
- Total Coulomb breakup cross section on Pb at 67 MeV/nucleon: 0.75 b calculated with option (d) versus 1.2 ± 0.11 b from the RIKEN experiment
- Peak height of the experimental excitation energy spectrum at RIKEN: approximately 1 barn/MeV at an excitation energy of almost 800 keV
- Neutron angular distribution FWHM from GANIL (Liegard 1998): 120 ± 18 MeV/c, with cross section magnitude approximately 1.5 b/sr at forward angles
- RMS sizes for the four configurations: 3.45 fm (option a), 3.00 fm (option b), 2.96 fm (option c), and 3.20 fm (option d); the 18C core RMS size is 2.9 fm

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical calculations by Banerjee, Thompson, and Tostevin (1998) on Coulomb breakup of 19C and addresses discrepancies among experimental results from MSU (Bazin 1995), GSI (Baumann 1998), GANIL (Marques 1996; Liegard 1998), and RIKEN (Nakamura 1998). The calculations contradict the findings of Ridikas et al. (1997) who obtained a reasonable fit to the 18C parallel momentum distribution with Jπ = 3/2+ or 5/2+ ground state assignments using a separation energy of 0.24 MeV. The paper enables future experimental measurements with better statistics and quality to resolve the ground state configuration of 19C, and highlights the need for accurate estimates of nuclear breakup contributions and Coulomb-nuclear interference in extracting pure Coulomb dissociation cross sections.
