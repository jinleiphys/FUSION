# A quantum-classical co-processing protocol towards simulating nuclear reactions on contemporary quantum hardware

> Quantum computers hold great promise for arriving at exact simulations of nuclear dynamical processes (e.g., scattering and reactions) that are paramount to the study of nuclear matter at the limit of stability and to explaining the formation of chemical elements in stars. However, quantum simulations of the unitary (real) time dynamics of fermionic many-body systems require a currently prohibitive number of reliable and long-lived qubits. We propose a co-processing algorithm for the simulation of real-time dynamics in which the time evolution of the spatial coordinates is carried out on a classical processor, while the evolution of the spin degrees of freedom is carried out on a quantum processor. This hybrid algorithm is demonstrated by a quantum simulation of the scattering of two neutrons performed at the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory's Advanced Quantum Testbed. We show that, after implementation of error mitigation strategies to improve the accuracy of the algorithm in addition to the use of either circuit compression techniques or tomography as methods to elucidate the onset of decoherence, this initial demonstration validates the principle of the proposed co-processing scheme. We anticipate that a generalization of this present scheme will open the way for (real-time) path integral simulations of nuclear scattering.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate a hybrid quantum-classical co-processing protocol for simulating nuclear reactions, where the spatial evolution of two neutrons is computed classically while the spin evolution is executed on a quantum processor, validating the principle only after implementing multiple error mitigation strategies and using either circuit compression or tomography to manage decoherence effects.

## Method

The authors factorize the Hamiltonian into spin-independent (spatial) and spin-dependent terms using a Trotter decomposition, then evolve the spatial coordinates classically via Verlet integration of Newton's equation while constructing short-time spin propagators from the leading-order chiral effective field theory potential. These spin propagators are compiled into quantum circuits and executed on a two-qubit superconducting processor at Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory's Advanced Quantum Testbed, with the spin state probabilities measured after concatenating time-ordered operators. Error mitigation strategies including readout calibration, randomized compiling, and state purification were applied, and circuit compression via Cartan's KAK decomposition or a reinitialization procedure using state tomography were explored as methods to reduce circuit depth.

## Key numbers

* Spatial evolution used N = 1000 time steps of size Δt = 0.005 MeV⁻¹ for the Verlet integration.
* Spin evolution reduced to N_s = 20 coarse time steps from T_0 = 0 to T_{N_s} = 19, with each coarse step ΔT = 50 Δt = 0.25 MeV⁻¹.
* Each U_j operator circuit contained three CNOT and eight U_3 gates, with circuits simulating longer times increasing linearly in depth.
* Circuit compression reduced all circuits to at most three CNOT gates, enabling 40 time steps for finer dynamics.
* Total variation distance (TVD) was used to quantify the difference between experimental and ideal probability distributions.

## Context

This work extends the Trotter decomposition approach first introduced by Holland (2020) for modeling neutron spin evolution on a superconducting qudit processor, and applies it to a full hybrid scheme with classical spatial dynamics. Prior quantum algorithms for nuclear dynamics have focused on linear-response functions (Roggero, 2019; Roggero, 2020; Baroni, 2022), radiative processes (Bedaque, 2022), and Green's functions (Anthony), but this is the first application of quantum computing to simulate nuclear reactions using a real-time approach. The protocol enables future path integral simulations of nuclear scattering, though challenges remain including sampling from real-time path integrals and the fermion sign problem (Troyer).
