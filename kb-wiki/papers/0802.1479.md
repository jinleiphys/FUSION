# Density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock calculation of $^{16}$O+$^{208}$Pb fusion cross sections

> We present a fully microscopic study of the $^{16}$O+$^{208}$Pb fusion using the density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock theory. The calculated fusion cross-sections are in good agreement with the experimental data for the entire energy range indicating that the incorporation of dynamical effects is crucial in describing heavy-ion fusion.

## Key claim

The density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock (DC-TDHF) method, which contains no free parameters or normalization, yields fusion cross-sections for the \(^{16}\)O+\(^{208}\)Pb system that are in good agreement with experimental data across the entire energy range, but only when the coordinate-dependent mass (dynamical effects) is included via a scaled potential; using unscaled potentials with constant reduced mass fails at the lowest sub-barrier energies.

## Method

The authors performed fully three-dimensional TDHF calculations for the \(^{16}\)O+\(^{208}\)Pb system using the Skyrme SLy4 effective interaction, with no symmetry restrictions. At each time step during the TDHF evolution, the instantaneous density was used to perform a static Hartree-Fock minimization (density constraint) to extract the ion-ion interaction potential \(V(R)\) and the coordinate-dependent mass \(M(R)\). The fusion cross-sections were then obtained by solving the Schrödinger equation with the scaled potential \(U(\bar{R})\) (which incorporates the coordinate-dependent mass via a point transformation) using the incoming wave boundary condition method.

## Key numbers

* The DC-TDHF potential barriers were calculated at three center-of-mass energies: \(E_{\mathrm{c.m.}} = 76\) MeV, 78 MeV, and 90 MeV.
* The Cartesian simulation box was 60 fm along the collision axis and 30 fm in the other two directions, with an initial separation of 24 fm between the two nuclei.
* Density constraint calculations were performed every 10 fm/c, for a total of 40 calculations.
* The numerical accuracy of static binding energies and the deviation from point Coulomb energy in the initial state was on the order of 50–150 keV.
* For the \(^{16}\)O+\(^{16}\)O system, the energy was tripled from 12 MeV to 36 MeV with very small energy dependence observed in the potential barriers.

## Context

This work extends the DC-TDHF method, previously applied to the \(^{64}\)Ni+\(^{132}\)Sn system (Umar and Oberacker, 2006, 2007) and the \(^{64}\)Ni+\(^{64}\)Ni system (Umar and Oberacker, 2008), to the \(^{16}\)O+\(^{208}\)Pb system, which is a benchmark for coupled-channels calculations. The results contradict the adequacy of standard phenomenological coupled-channels approaches (e.g., those by Esbensen, 2006; Hagino and Watanabe, 2007) that require ad hoc modifications such as compression potentials or neck formation to fit sub-barrier data; instead, the microscopic DC-TDHF method naturally incorporates these dynamical effects. This enables future fully microscopic studies of heavy-ion fusion without phenomenological parameters, and suggests that the energy dependence of the potential barrier is a key feature for understanding deep sub-barrier fusion.
