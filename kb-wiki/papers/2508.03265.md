# Nonlinear analysis of causality for heat flow in heavy-ion collisions: constraints from equation of state

> The present work investigates the causal parameter space of the Mueller-Israel-Stewart second-order theory for heat-conducting fluids in the Eckart frame for one-dimensional fluid flow in systems with finite baryon density. It is shown that this parameter space is highly constrained and particularly sensitive to the equation of state and second-order transport coefficients. Through numerical analysis of the characteristic equations, the present analysis identifies regions of strong hyperbolicity, weak hyperbolicity, and non-hyperbolicity, mapping the boundaries of causality violation as functions of the heat flux to energy density ratio $q/\varepsilon$ and relaxation parameters. The present work also explores the causality conditions using a realistic lattice QCD-based equation of state. Using the Navier-Stokes approximation, an estimate is made of the heat flow magnitude to assess causality criteria for one-dimensional heat conduction in heavy-ion collisions. The present calculations reveal unrealistically large heat flux values ($|{\bf{q}}|/\varepsilon \approx 330$--$811$) for typical RHIC conditions when using thermal conductivity estimates from kinetic theory models, suggesting either significant overestimation of transport coefficients or breakdown of the fluid approximation in these extreme conditions. The pressure gradient corrections reduce the heat flow by approximately 15\% but do not resolve the causality concerns.

## Key claim

The causal parameter space of the Mueller-Israel-Stewart second-order theory for heat-conducting fluids in the Eckart frame is highly constrained and particularly sensitive to both the equation of state and second-order transport coefficients; the abstract glosses over the finding that for typical RHIC conditions, heat flux values derived from kinetic theory estimates are unrealistically large (|q|/epsilon approximately 330-811), suggesting either significant overestimation of transport coefficients or breakdown of the fluid approximation.

## Method

The authors performed a numerical analysis of the characteristic equation derived from the Mueller-Israel-Stewart second-order theory for one-dimensional heat-conducting fluid flow in the Eckart frame, solving for the characteristic velocities v as functions of the normalized heat flux q/epsilon, the relaxation parameter lambda (where beta_1 = lambda * 5/(4p)), and the squared speed of sound c_s^2. They mapped regions of strong hyperbolicity, weak hyperbolicity, and non-hyperbolicity for both constant-speed-of-sound equations of state and a realistic lattice QCD-based equation of state from Borsanyi (2014), and estimated heat flow magnitudes using the Navier-Stokes approximation with thermal conductivity estimates from kinetic theory models.

## Key numbers

* For a constant-speed-of-sound EoS with c_s^2 = 1/3 (ultra-relativistic limit) and lambda = 1, the causal region is limited to |q/epsilon| less than or approximately 0.18, consistent with Hiscock and Lindblom (1988).
* For the same EoS with c_s^2 = 1/3 but lambda = 10, the causal region expands to |q/epsilon| less than or approximately 0.25.
* Using the Navier-Stokes approximation with kinetic theory estimates of thermal conductivity, the calculated heat flux values for typical RHIC conditions are |q|/epsilon approximately 330-811, which are unrealistically large.
* Pressure gradient corrections reduce the heat flow by approximately 15% but do not resolve the causality concerns.
* The speed of sound from lattice QCD exhibits a dip around approximately 130 MeV, approaching the massless limit c_s^2 = 1/3 from below at high temperature.

## Context

This work extends the linear-regime causality analyses of Hiscock and Lindblom (1988) and others into the nonlinear regime for heat-conducting fluids, and is the first to incorporate a realistic lattice QCD-based equation of state into the causality analysis of the Mueller-Israel-Stewart theory. The findings constrain the applicability of second-order dissipative hydrodynamics for systems with finite baryon density, particularly relevant for the RHIC Beam Energy Scan program and upcoming FAIR and NICA experiments, and suggest that current kinetic theory estimates of thermal conductivity may be significantly overestimated or that the fluid approximation breaks down under extreme conditions.
