# Cluster formation near midrapidity -- can the mechanism be identified experimentally?

> The formation of weakly bound clusters in the hot and dense environment at midrapidity is one of the surprising phenomena observed experimentally in heavy-ion collisions from a low center of mass energy of a few GeV up to a ultra-relativistic energy of several TeV. Three approaches have been advanced to describe the cluster formation: coalescence at kinetic freeze-out, cluster formation during the entire heavy-ion collision by potential interaction between nucleons and deuteron production by hadronic reactions. We identify experimental observables, which can discriminate these production mechanisms for deuterons.

## Key claim

The paper identifies experimental observables, specifically the shape of the rapidity distribution and the low-transverse-momentum slope of the transverse momentum spectrum, that can discriminate between three competing deuteron production mechanisms (coalescence at freeze-out, potential-interaction clustering, and hadronic collision production), but notes that current data tentatively favor the MST + kinetic scenario only with caution, and that more precise low-\(p_T\) measurements are needed.

## Method

The authors used the Parton-Hadron-Quantum Molecular Dynamics (PHQMD) microscopic transport approach, which implements all three deuteron production mechanisms in a single framework: coalescence at kinetic freeze-out using momentum and distance cuts (\(\Delta P < 0.285\) GeV, \(\Delta R < 3.575\) fm); potential-interaction clustering via the advanced Minimum Spanning Tree (aMST) algorithm selecting bound clusters with negative binding energy; and kinetic production via inelastic hadronic reactions (\(NN\pi \to d\pi\), \(NNN \to dN\)) with finite-size effects. They simulated central Au+Au and Pb+Pb collisions at center-of-mass energies from 3.0 GeV to 200 GeV and compared the resulting rapidity and transverse momentum distributions of deuterons.

## Key numbers

* Coalescence radii used: relative momentum \(\Delta P < 0.285\) GeV and relative distance \(\Delta R < 3.575\) fm, with a spin degeneracy factor of 3/8 applied.
* At midrapidity, only about 20% of MST-identified deuterons are also identified as coalescence deuterons (before the 3/8 factor).
* For the ratio \(\frac{dN}{dy}(y=0)/\frac{dN}{dy}(y=0.6)\) at \(\sqrt{s}=3\) GeV, the authors obtain 1.25; for higher energies, the ratio \(\frac{dN}{dy}(y=0)/\frac{dN}{dy}(y=1)\) is 1.5.
* The MST + kinetic scenario under-predicts the deuteron yield by about 15% in all rapidity intervals compared to E864 data at \(E_{Lab}=11\) AGeV (\(\sqrt{s}=4.9\) GeV).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of deuteron production in heavy-ion collisions by comparing all three mechanisms within the same transport framework, building on prior PHQMD calculations (Aichelin 2019, Coci 2023) and SMASH-based kinetic studies (Oliinychenko 2018, Staudenmaier 2021). It addresses the "ice in the fire" puzzle, how weakly bound clusters survive the hot, dense midrapidity environment, and enables experimental discrimination between production mechanisms, which was previously impossible because all three approaches reproduced the same transverse momentum data. The analysis suggests that future low-\(p_T\) measurements, achievable with modern detectors, can resolve the mechanism and thus explain how deuterons survive the hadronic expansion phase.
