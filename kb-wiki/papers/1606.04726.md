# Adiabatic fission barriers in superheavy nuclei

> Using the microscopic-macroscopic model based on the deformed Woods-Saxon single-particle potential and the Yukawa-plus-exponential macroscopic energy we calculated static fission barriers $B_{f}$ for 1305 heavy and superheavy nuclei $98\leq Z \leq 126$, including even - even, odd - even, even - odd and odd - odd systems. For odd and odd-odd nuclei, adiabatic potential energy surfaces were calculated by a minimization over configurations with one blocked neutron or/and proton on a level from the 10-th below to the 10-th above the Fermi level. The parameters of the model that have been fixed previously by a fit to masses of even-even heavy nuclei were kept unchanged. A search for saddle points has been performed by the "Imaginary Water Flow" method on a basic five-dimensional deformation grid, including triaxiality. Two auxiliary grids were used for checking the effects of the mass asymmetry and hexadecapole non-axiallity. The ground states were found by energy minimization over configurations and deformations. We find that the non-axiallity significantly changes first and second fission barrier in many nuclei. The effect of the mass - asymmetry, known to lower the second, very deformed barriers in actinides, in the heaviest nuclei appears at the less deformed saddles in more than 100 nuclei. It happens for those saddles in which the triaxiallity does not play any role, what suggests a decoupling between effects of the mass-asymmetry and triaxiality. We studied also the influence of the pairing interaction strength on the staggering of $B_f$ for odd- and even-particle numbers. Finally, we provide a comparison of our results with other theoretical fission barrier evaluations and with available experimental estimates.

## Key claim

The authors calculate adiabatic fission barriers for 1305 heavy and superheavy nuclei (Z=98-126) using a microscopic-macroscopic model, finding that triaxiality significantly changes first and second fission barriers in many nuclei, and that mass-asymmetry effects appear at less deformed saddles in more than 100 nuclei, but only where triaxiality does not play a role.

## Method

The authors used the deformed Woods-Saxon single-particle potential with Yukawa-plus-exponential macroscopic energy, keeping all model parameters fixed from previous fits to even-even heavy nuclei masses. For odd and odd-odd nuclei, adiabatic potential energy surfaces were calculated by minimizing over configurations with one blocked neutron or proton on levels from the 10th below to the 10th above the Fermi level. Saddle points were searched using the "Imaginary Water Flow" method on a basic five-dimensional deformation grid (including β20, β22, β40, β60, β80), with two auxiliary grids used to check effects of mass asymmetry and hexadecapole nonaxiality.

## Key numbers

- 1305 heavy and superheavy nuclei with proton numbers 98 ≤ Z ≤ 126 and neutron numbers 134 ≤ N ≤ 192 were studied
- The basic five-dimensional deformation grid contained 29250 points, interpolated to 50735286 points with step 0.01 in each dimension
- For mass-asymmetry studies, a seven-dimensional grid of 76800 deformations was interpolated to 1690730496 points
- The largest barrier lowering by mass-asymmetry was approximately 0.77 MeV for Z=113, N=163
- For Z=113 isotopes, the maximum barrier lowering by triaxiality exceeded 1.5 MeV near N≈165
- For Z=118, N=165, the mass-asymmetry effect from minimization was 0.44 MeV, similar to 0.46 MeV from the Imaginary Water Flow method
- For Z=113, N=163, the minimization method gave 1.25 MeV effect while the Imaginary Water Flow method gave 0.76 MeV, a difference of approximately 0.5 MeV
- Saddles with quadrupole nonaxiality were found for about 900 nuclei, constituting more than 70% of all fission barriers

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous microscopic-macroscopic model calculations, which were applied mainly to even-even nuclei (Kowal, 2009), to odd-A and odd-odd superheavy systems. The study systematically covers a wider range of isotopes than prior work, including comparisons with the Los Alamos microscopic-macroscopic model and recent self-consistent models (Gorbar, 2015). The results enable improved predictions for fission rates in superheavy nuclei, which are relevant for understanding production cross sections of evaporation residues in heavy ion reactions, particularly the growth of total cross sections around Z=118 discussed by Oganessian (2012).
