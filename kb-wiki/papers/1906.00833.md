# Turning the nuclear energy density functional method into a proper effective field theory: reflections

> Nuclear energy density functionals (EDFs) have a long history of success in reproducing properties of nuclei across the table of the nuclides. They capture quantitatively the emergent features of bound nuclei, such as nuclear saturation and pairing, yet greater accuracy and improved uncertainty quantification are actively sought. Implementations of phenomenological EDFs are suggestive of effective field theory (EFT) formulations and there are hints of an underlying power counting. Multiple paths are possible in trying to turn the nuclear EDF method into a proper EFT. I comment on the current situation and speculate on how to proceed using an effective action formulation.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while nuclear energy density functionals (EDFs) have been phenomenologically successful, turning them into a proper effective field theory (EFT) remains an open challenge; the author speculates that an effective action formulation may provide a path forward, but cautions that this is a personal assessment rather than a comprehensive review.

## Method

The author surveys the current landscape of nuclear EDF methods and their relationship to EFT, reviewing progress in lattice QCD, ab initio chiral EFT calculations, phase-space-based power counting studies, density matrix expansion (DME) approaches that incorporate pions, and pionless EFT formulations. The paper then outlines a proposed bottom-up strategy using an effective action formalism to construct a proper EFT for nuclear EDFs, drawing on the phenomenological successes of existing functionals as a guide.

## Key numbers

- The upper bound for 5% accuracy ab initio calculations with realistic Hamiltonians has grown from A=14 to A of order 80 within the last half-dozen years
- For 208Pb, percent-level ab initio calculations would mean an error of order 15 MeV in binding energy, while the best EDFs are at the 1 MeV level or below
- Root mean square residuals for binding energies: UNEDF-2 reference gives 1.98 MeV; N2LOΔ gives 1.26 MeV; N2LOΔ+3N gives 1.72 MeV (from Table 1)
- The Δ resonance mass is approximately 300 MeV (MΔ - MN ≈ 300 MeV)
- The best EDFs reproduce nuclear masses at the 1 MeV level or below

## Context

This paper extends the author's earlier work (Drut and Furnstahl, 2009) on ab initio density functional theory for nuclei, and comments on developments since then including chiral EFT Hamiltonians from Epelbaum (2008) and Machleidt (2011), the DME-based EDF work by Holt, Kaiser, and collaborators (2013), and the UNEDF-2 functional. The paper identifies the gap in the EFT hierarchy for describing ground state properties of nuclei, where EDFs remain the phenomenological method of choice, and suggests that an effective action formulation could provide a systematic path forward for constructing a proper EFT.
