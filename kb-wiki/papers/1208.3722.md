# Too massive neutron stars: The role of dark matter?

> The maximum mass of a neutron star is generally determined by the equation of state of the star material. In this study, we take into account dark matter particles, assumed to behave like fermions with a free parameter to account for the interaction strength among the particles, as a possible constituent of neutron stars. We find dark matter inside the star would soften the equation of state more strongly than that of hyperons, and reduce largely the maximum mass of the star. However, the neutron star maximum mass is sensitive to the particle mass of dark matter, and a very high neutron star mass larger than 2 times solar mass could be achieved when the particle mass is small enough. Such kind of dark-matter- admixed neutron stars could explain the recent measurement of the Shapiro delay in the radio pulsar PSR J1614-2230, which yielded a neutron star mass of 2 times solar mass that may be hardly reached when hyperons are considered only, as in the case of the microscopic Brueckner theory. Furthermore, in this particular case, we point out that the dark matter around a neutron star should also contribute to the mass measurement due to its pure gravitational effect. However, our numerically calculation illustrates that such contribution could be safely ignored because of the usual diluted dark matter environment assumed. We conclude that a very high mass measurement of about 2 times solar mass requires a really stiff equation of state in neutron stars, and find a strong upper limit (<= 0.64 GeV) for the particle mass of non-self- annihilating dark matter based on the present model.

## Key claim

The paper finds that dark matter inside a neutron star would soften the equation of state more strongly than hyperons do, but that a very high neutron star mass larger than 2 solar masses could be achieved when the dark matter particle mass is small enough (below about 0.64 GeV for strongly-interacting dark matter), provided the dark matter is non-self-annihilating and behaves as a fermion gas with a repulsive interaction.

## Method

The authors model a neutron star as a two-fluid system: ordinary nuclear/hypernuclear matter described by the microscopic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach with three-body forces, and dark matter treated as a self-interacting Fermi gas with a free parameter for the interaction strength. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equations using the sum of the two components' pressures and energy densities, varying the dark matter particle mass from 0.01 GeV to 10 GeV and the interaction strength between the weak (300 GeV scale) and strong (100 MeV scale) regimes. They also estimate the gravitational mass contribution from a possible dark matter halo around the star using the Roche lobe size of the PSR J1614-2230 binary system and four different Galactic dark matter density profiles.

## Key numbers

* The maximum mass of a neutron star without dark matter is 2.29 solar masses; with strongly-interacting dark matter of mass 10 GeV it drops to 0.39 solar masses, and with weakly-interacting dark matter of the same mass it drops to 0.34 solar masses.
* For a dark matter particle mass of 0.01 GeV, the maximum mass reaches 2.96 solar masses for both strongly-interacting and weakly-interacting dark matter, with a corresponding radius of 17.3 km.
* The measured mass of PSR J1614-2230 is 1.97 +/- 0.04 solar masses from Shapiro delay.
* The upper limit on the dark matter particle mass is 0.64 GeV for strongly-interacting dark matter and 0.16 GeV for weakly-interacting dark matter, based on the requirement that a hyperon star can reach 2 solar masses.
* The estimated dark matter density at the location of PSR J1614-2230 (7.44 kpc from the Galactic center) averages 0.474 GeV/cm^3 across four density profiles, yielding a halo mass contribution of about 10^-24 solar masses, which is negligible.

## Context

This work extends the study of dark-matter-admixed neutron stars by Leung et al. (2011), Ciarcelluti and Sandin (2011), and Goldman (2011), who used different dark matter models, by systematically varying the dark matter particle mass and interaction strength. It directly addresses the "hyperon puzzle" identified in Brueckner theory calculations by Burgio et al. (2010, 2011), where hyperon stars have maximum masses below 1.4 solar masses, in conflict with the 2-solar-mass measurement of PSR J1614-2230 by Demorest et al. (2010). The result enables future work using two-fluid TOV equations with more realistic dark matter equations of state to determine the internal configuration of dark-matter-admixed neutron stars.
