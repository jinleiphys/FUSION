# Current Developments in Nuclear Density Functional Methods

> Density functional theory (DFT) became a universal approach to compute ground-state and excited configurations of many-electron systems held together by an external one-body potential in condensed-matter, atomic, and molecular physics. At present, the DFT strategy is also intensely studied and applied in the area of nuclear structure. The nuclear DFT, a natural extension of the self-consistent mean-field theory, is a tool of choice for computations of ground-state properties and low-lying excitations of medium-mass and heavy nuclei. Over the past thirty-odd years, a lot of experience was accumulated in implementing, adjusting, and using the density-functional methods in nuclei. This research direction is still extremely actively pursued. In particular, current developments concentrate on (i) attempts to improve the performance and precision delivered by the nuclear density-functional methods, (ii) derivations of density functionals from first principles rooted in the low-energy chromodynamics and effective theories, and (iii) including effects of low-energy correlations and symmetry restoration. In this study, we present an overview of recent results and achievements gained in nuclear density-functional methods.

## Key claim

Nuclear density functional theory (DFT) and its energy-density functional (EDF) implementation can describe ground-state properties and low-lying excitations of medium-mass and heavy nuclei with impressive precision, but the exact derivation of the density functional from first principles is impractical because it would require solving the many-body problem exactly; instead, functionals must be modelled using physical intuition, general theoretical arguments, experiment, and exact calculations for simple systems.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical foundations of nuclear EDF methods, including the two-step variational principle that underlies DFT, and the classification of EDFs into local, quasilocal, nonlocal, and nonlocal-nonlocal forms. It compares nonrelativistic EDFs (Skyrme, Gogny, BCP) and relativistic EDFs (meson-exchange and point-coupling models), and discusses the Negele-Vautherin density-matrix expansion (DME) that establishes equivalence between nonlocal and quasilocal EDFs. Applications covered include mass calculations with pairing correlations, collective quadrupole correlations treated via configuration mixing and the 5D collective Hamiltonian, fission-path calculations, giant resonances in deformed nuclei via RPA and QRPA, and time-dependent mean-field dynamics.

## Key numbers

- RMS deviation for 2149 nuclear masses using the quasilocal Skyrme functional HFB-17: 798 keV.
- RMS deviation for 2149 nuclear masses using the nonlocal Gogny functional D1M: 581 keV.
- Second-order DME derived from Gogny D1S gives precision of order 1% for proton RMS radii and binding energies of doubly magic nuclei.
- Relative deviations between experimental and theoretical charge radii and binding energies for 12 spherical nuclei calculated with relativistic functionals are below 1%.
- Neutron drops confined in Woods-Saxon potentials of depths V0 = -25 MeV and -35.5 MeV were used to test Skyrme functionals.

## Context

This review extends the long line of work on nuclear DFT that began with the Hohenberg-Kohn (1964) and Kohn-Sham (1965) theorems, and the development of Skyrme functionals (Ben03), Gogny functionals (Dec80), and relativistic EDFs (Rin96, Lal04a). It enables systematic improvement of nuclear EDFs through higher-order DME expansions (Car10, Dob10), better treatment of collective correlations via configuration mixing and projection techniques (Sab07, Del10), and first-principles derivations from low-energy chromodynamics and effective theories. Open questions include formal aspects of DFT for self-bound systems, consistent inclusion of low-energy correlations, and extension to exotic nuclei and astrophysical applications.
