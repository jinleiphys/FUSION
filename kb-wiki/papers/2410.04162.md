# Detecting spacelike vacuum entanglement at all distances and promoting negativity to a necessary and sufficient entanglement measure in many-body regimes

> Though known to be present, the accessibility of spacelike vacuum entanglement capable of being a fundamental resource for quantum information processing has remained in question at distances beyond the scale of vacuum fluctuations in massive fields. For a broad subclass of physical many-body mixed Gaussian states, including the free scalar field vacuum, the logarithmic negativity is here shown to be a necessary and sufficient measure of entanglement and to be entirely accessible by pairs of single-mode detectors in the continuum. By deriving exact and optimal detection profiles, entanglement resources in the massive field are demonstrated to be available at all distances.

# Detecting spacelike vacuum entanglement at all distances and promoting negativity to a necessary and sufficient entanglement measure in many-body regimes

## Key claim

For a broad subclass of physical many-body mixed Gaussian states including the free scalar field vacuum, the logarithmic negativity is shown to be a necessary and sufficient measure of entanglement and to be entirely accessible by pairs of single-mode detectors in the continuum, with entanglement resources in the massive field demonstrated to be available at all distances. The key qualifier is that this holds for AB-symmetric, phi-pi-uncorrelated covariance matrices satisfying positive-semidefinite field and negative-semidefinite momentum region-region blocks, and that the detection profiles must be derived from the partial transpose eigenspace rather than from equal-field-and-momentum-profile assumptions used in prior work.

## Method

The authors use lattice regularization of the free scalar field vacuum in one spatial dimension, representing the state as a Gaussian state fully characterized by its covariance matrix. They analytically derive semidefinite properties of the region-region covariance matrix blocks (positive for field-field, negative for momentum-momentum) and use symplectic transformations to reorganize the partially transposed covariance matrix into block-diagonal form. Optimal collective detection mode profiles are then calculated from the dominant negativity-contributing subspace of the partial transpose eigensystem, enabling the design of beamsplitter interactions that swap the collective field mode with a single-mode detector.

## Key numbers

- The minimum negativity supported by the lattice scales as $\mathcal{N} \sim e^{-\Lambda' d}$, where $d$ is the number of lattice sites per region and $\Lambda'$ is a UV-scale parameter, persisting from the massless regime.
- At large separations, the negativity decays as $\mathcal{N} \sim e^{-(m\tilde{r})^2}$, where $m$ is the field mass and $\tilde{r}$ is the number of lattice sites between regions.
- For $md \gg 1$, the negativity transitions to a regime of $\mathcal{N} \sim e^{-mr}$ before the quadratic exponential decay for $m\tilde{r} \gtrsim md$.
- Calculations use pixelations of $d \gtrsim 100$ oscillators per region to approximate the continuum, with minimal lattice configurations $\tilde{r}_{\text{min}}, d_{\text{min}} \leq 10$.
- The optimal profiles shown in Figure 1 use $d=1000$ oscillators per region.

## Context

This work extends the direct significance of the negativity entanglement measure from low-dimensional or highly symmetric systems (two-mode, $(1_A \times n_B)$-mode, pure, isotropic, or bisymmetric states as studied by Peres (1996), Horodecki (1996), Simon (2000), and others) to a structural class of physical many-body Gaussian quantum states. It provides analytic support for previous numerical observations of the entanglement sphere coinciding with the negativity sphere by Klco (2023) and extends beyond the work of Reznik (2003) and subsequent studies that reported critical distances around the Compton wavelength scale beyond which no operational entanglement resources were available. The results enable the design of detectors capable of connecting to the field as a quantum resource for quantum information processing and may contribute to entanglement-guided design of quantum simulation algorithms for nuclear and particle physics applications.
