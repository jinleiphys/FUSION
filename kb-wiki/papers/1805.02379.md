# Charged Particle Emissions in High-Frequency Alternative Electric Fields

> Proton emission, $α$ decay, and cluster radioactivity play an important role in nuclear physics. We show that high-frequency alternative electric fields could deform Coulomb barriers that trap the charged particle, and raise the possibility of speeding up charged particle emissions. They could also cause anisotropic effects in charged particle emissions, and introduce additional terms in the Geiger-Nuttall laws. Our study may further suggest that, for proton emitters like $^{166}$Ir, when the electric field is strong, the dominant decay mode could be changed from $α$ decay to proton emission. As high-frequency alternative electric fields correspond to high-frequency laser fields in the dipole approximation, our study could be viewed as a benchmark for future theoretical studies of charged particle emissions in realistic laser fields.

## Key claim

High-frequency alternating electric fields can deform Coulomb barriers and significantly enhance charged particle emission rates, potentially changing the dominant decay mode of certain proton emitters like $^{166}$Ir from alpha decay to proton emission, but only when the field intensity exceeds a critical threshold (quantified by the dimensionless parameter $D = S_0/R_{d0}$).

## Method

The authors solve the time-dependent Schrodinger equation for the relative motion of cluster and daughter nuclei in a high-frequency alternating electric field, using the Hennenberger transformation to obtain a static effective potential. They apply the WKB approximation to compute barrier penetrabilities for proton emission, alpha decay, and cluster radioactivity, treating the Coulomb and centrifugal potentials while neglecting nuclear interactions. The formalism is applied to several representative nuclei including $^{159}$Re, $^{212}$Po, $^{242}$Cm, and $^{166}$Ir, with both spherical and axially deformed nuclei considered.

## Key numbers

- For the proton emitter $^{159}$Re with $L=5$, at $D=1$ the differential penetrability varies by approximately one order of magnitude from $\Theta=0$ to $\Theta=\pi/2$
- For the alpha emitter $^{212}$Po at $D=1$, the differential penetrability varies by approximately four orders of magnitude from $\Theta=0$ to $\Theta=\pi/2$
- For the $^{34}$Si emitter $^{242}$Cm at $D=1$, the differential penetrability varies by more than twenty orders of magnitude from $\Theta=0$ to $\Theta=\pi/2$
- Critical $D$ values: $D_{p,\text{crit}} \approx 2$ for proton emission from $^{166}$Ir, $D_{\alpha,\text{crit}} \approx 1$ for alpha decay channels of both $^{166}$Ir and $^{242}$Cm, and $D_{c,\text{crit}} \approx 0.6$ for $^{34}$Si radioactivity from $^{242}$Cm
- For $^{166}$Ir with $\beta_{2d}=0.3$, nuclear deformation increases proton-emission penetrability by about 20% and alpha-decay penetrability by about one order of magnitude
- For $^{242}$Cm with $^{34}$Si cluster deformation $\beta_{2c} \approx 0.2$, the overall increase in penetrability is more than 50 times
- At $D=1.5$, corrections from shifted Geiger-Nuttall laws are about 2 times for proton emission, 40 times for alpha decay, and five orders of magnitude for cluster radioactivity
- For $^{166}$Ir, the ratio $D_p/D_\alpha \sim 15$; at $D_\alpha \approx 0.5$, the proton-emission channel gets enhanced by about five orders of magnitude
- For $^{242}$Cm, $D_c/D_\alpha \sim 0.17$; at $D_\alpha \approx 3$, the alpha-decay channel increases by about five orders of magnitude while the $^{34}$Si channel enhances by less than one order

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of alpha decay in strong electromagnetic fields by Cortes (2011), Misicu (2013), Kopytin (2014), and Delion (2017) to include proton emission and cluster radioactivity in a unified framework. It builds on the unified decay rules approach developed by Delion (2006), Ni (2008), Qi (2009), and Poenaru (2011). The results suggest that high-frequency laser fields (in the dipole approximation) could be used to manipulate decay modes of proton-rich nuclei, with potential applications for decontaminating alpha-radioactive nuclear waste. The authors note that achieving such high laser intensities in the laboratory frame is challenging, and propose using laser-nucleus collisions as suggested by Burvenich (2006) to achieve high field intensities in the nucleus rest frame via the relativistic Doppler effect.
