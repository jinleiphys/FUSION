# Dispersion relations: foundations

> We give a pedagogical introduction to the founding ideas of dispersion relations in particle physics. Starting from elementary mechanical systems, we show how the physical principle of causality is closely related to the mathematical property of analyticity, and how both are implemented in quantum mechanical scattering theory. We present a personal selection of elementary applications such as the relation between hadronic production amplitudes or form factors to scattering, and the extraction of resonance properties on unphysical Riemann sheets. More advanced topics such as Roy equations for pion--pion scattering and dispersion relations for three-body decays are briefly touched upon.

## Key claim

Dispersion relations are a powerful tool for analyzing quantum mechanical scattering, decay, or production amplitudes because they are founded on very general principles, causality first of all, which translates into analyticity, that apply beyond perturbative settings, making them especially valuable for strong interactions at low energies where quantum chromodynamics is inaccessible to perturbation theory.

## Method

The paper begins by establishing the connection between causality and analyticity using the classical forced damped harmonic oscillator, showing that the response function is analytic in the upper half of the complex frequency plane precisely when the Green's function vanishes for negative time arguments. It then extends this reasoning to non-relativistic quantum mechanical scattering, demonstrating that the S-matrix element is analytic except for a right-hand cut above threshold and bound-state poles, and derives a dispersion relation relating its real and imaginary parts. The method is further applied to relativistic quantum field theory using Cutkosky rules to calculate the discontinuity of Feynman diagrams, and to the non-perturbative pion vector form factor using unitarity to relate its imaginary part to the pion-pion P-wave scattering amplitude.

## Key numbers

- For the forced damped harmonic oscillator, the eigenfrequencies are omega_{1/2} = +/- sqrt(omega_0^2 - gamma^2) - i gamma, with Im omega_{1/2} < 0 due to the damping factor gamma > 0.
- For the one-loop two-propagator Feynman diagram, the imaginary part is Im M(s) = (1/2) * sigma(s)/(16 pi), where sigma(s) = sqrt(1 - 4M^2/s) is the phase space factor.
- The pion charge radius from the elastic Omnès representation is <(r_pi^V)^2>_{el.} = 0.419 fm^2, compared to the experimental value <(r_pi^V)^2>_{exp.} = 0.429(4) fm^2 (Colangelo 2018).
- The pion vector form factor normalization is F_pi^V(s=0) = 1, the pion's charge in units of e.

## Context

This pedagogical review extends the classic Kramers-Kronig relations (Kramers 1927, de L. Kronig 1926) from electrodynamics to quantum mechanical and quantum field theoretical amplitudes, showing how causality implies analyticity in the upper half-plane. The work builds on the foundational S-matrix analyticity program (Omnes 1971) and provides the basis for modern applications such as Roy equations for pion-pion scattering and dispersion relations for three-body decays. The Omnès solution (Omnes 1958) for the pion form factor, which resums pi-pi rescattering to all orders, enables precision determinations of hadronic properties and provides a framework for incorporating inelastic effects from channels such as 4pi, pi-omega, and K-bar-K.
