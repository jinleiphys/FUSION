# Supernova Neutrino-Nucleus Physics and the r-process

> This talk reviews three inputs important to neutrino-induced nucleosynthesis in a supernova: 1) "standard" properties of the supernova neutrino flux, 2) effects of phenomena like neutrino oscillations on that flux, and 3) nuclear structure issues in estimating cross sections for neutrino-nucleus interactions. The resulting possibilities for neutrino-induced nucleosynthesis -- the neutrino-process -- in massive stars are discussed. This includes two relatively recent extensions of neutrino-process calculations to heavier nuclei, one focused on understanding the origin of 138La and 180Ta and the second on the effects following r-process freezeout. From calculations of the neutrino post-processing of the r-process distribution, limits can be placed on the neutrino fluence after freezeout and thus on the dynamic timescale for the expansion of the "hot bubble."

## Key claim

The neutrino process (ν-process) can account for the observed abundances of several rare isotopes like ¹¹B, ¹⁹F, and ¹³⁸La, and the neutrino post-processing of r-process material provides a "neutrino fingerprint" that circumstantially supports the hot bubble as the r-process site, though the ¹⁸⁰Ta overproduction may be resolved if most of the yield cascades to the ground state rather than the isomeric state.

## Method

The paper reviews three interconnected inputs for neutrino-induced nucleosynthesis: standard supernova neutrino flux properties (including flavor-dependent spectral temperatures), effects of neutrino oscillations on that flux (particularly matter-enhanced MSW effects and the unknown mixing angle θ₁₃), and nuclear structure issues in estimating neutrino-nucleus cross sections (comparing shell model, continuum RPA, and effective operator approaches). For the ν-process, the author describes calculations by Woosley et al. and Heger et al. that embed neutrino reactions in supernova models with explosive nucleosynthesis networks, and for r-process post-processing, the method works backward from the observed r-process distribution by subtracting calculated neutrino-induced modifications to infer the freeze-out distribution.

## Key numbers

- Effective Fermi-Dirac temperatures from supernova models: T(ν_μ) ∼ T(ν_τ) ∼ T(ν̄_μ) ∼ T(ν̄_τ) ∼ 8 MeV, T(ν_e) ∼ 3.5 MeV, T(ν̄_e) ∼ 4.5 MeV
- Solar neutrino solution: θ₁₂ ∼ π/6 and δm₁₂² ∼ 7 × 10⁻⁵ eV² (from SNO)
- Atmospheric neutrino results: δm₂₃² ∼ (2-3) × 10⁻³ eV² with θ₂₃ ∼ π/4 (from Super-Kamiokande)
- K2K experiment: δm₂₃² ∼ (1.5-3.9) × 10⁻³ eV² under maximal mixing assumption
- Neutrino fluence after r-process freeze-out: ∼ (0.045-0.015) × 10⁵¹ ergs/(100 km)²
- Neutrino fluence values from abundance fits: N=82 peak 0.031 × 10⁵¹ ergs/(100 km)²/flavor; N=126 peak 0.015 × 10⁵¹ ergs/(100 km)²/flavor
- Neutrino flux decay timescale: τ_ν ∼ 3 seconds
- r-process freeze-out temperature range: (3-1) × 10⁹ K, corresponding to radius (600-1000) km and time ∼ 10 seconds after core collapse
- Ne-shell radius for ν-process: ∼ 20,000 km
- Approximately 1/300 of nuclei in the Ne zone interact with neutrinos; for heavy r-process nuclei the interaction probability is approximately unity

## Context

This review extends the ν-process calculations of Woosley et al. (various years) for lighter isotopes like ¹⁹F and ¹¹B to heavier nuclei, specifically addressing ¹³⁸La and ¹⁸⁰Ta production via charged-current and neutral-current reactions, and introduces the concept of neutrino post-processing of the r-process distribution following freeze-out as developed by Qian (various years). The work connects new neutrino oscillation parameters from SNO, Super-Kamiokande, and KamLAND to supernova nucleosynthesis, showing how the unknown mixing angle θ₁₃ and the mass hierarchy could affect ν-process yields and r-process conditions. The results enable constraints on supernova dynamics including the freeze-out radius and dynamic timescale, and motivate further studies of neutrino oscillation effects on nucleosynthetic fingerprints.
