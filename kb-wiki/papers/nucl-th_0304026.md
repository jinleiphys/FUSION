# Color Superconductivity in Compact Stars and Gamma Ray Bursts

> We study the effects of color superconductivity on the structure and formation of compact stars. We show that it is possible to satisfy most of recent observational boundaries on masses and radii if a diquark condensate forms in a hybrid or a quark star. Moreover, we find that a huge amount of energy, of the order of $10^{53}$ erg, can be released in the conversion from a (metastable) hadronic star into a (stable) hybrid or quark star, if the presence of a color superconducting phase is taken into account. Accordingly to the scenario proposed in Astrophys.J.586(2003)1250, the energy released in this conversion can power a Gamma Ray Burst. This mechanism can explain the recent observations indicating a delay, of the order of days or years, between a few Supernova explosions and the subsequent Gamma Ray Burst.

# Color Superconductivity in Compact Stars and Gamma Ray Bursts

## Key claim
Color superconductivity can reconcile apparently contradictory observational constraints on compact star masses and radii, and simultaneously provide the energy (up to ~10^53 erg) needed to power Gamma Ray Bursts from the conversion of metastable hadronic stars into hybrid or quark stars, provided the vacuum pressure (bag constant) lies in the narrow range 160 MeV ≲ B^1/4 ≲ 170 MeV and the superconducting gap reaches a maximum of ~0.15 GeV at a chemical potential between 0.4 and 0.6 GeV.

## Method
The authors construct equations of state for beta-stable matter using the Glendenning-Moszkowski (GM1, GM3) relativistic hadronic model for the hadronic phase and a MIT-bag model with a chemical-potential-dependent Color-Flavor Locking (CFL) gap for the quark phase, connected via Gibbs equilibrium conditions. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain mass-radius relations, and compute quantum tunneling nucleation times for quark droplet formation in metastable hadronic stars to determine the critical mass for conversion and the energy released.

## Key numbers
- Energy released in hadronic-to-hybrid/quark star conversion: up to 2.90 × 10^53 erg (290 foe) for GM1 with B^1/4 = 170 MeV, σ = 40 MeV/fm^2, and Δ_4 gap (quark star case)
- Maximum superconducting gap values: 70 to 150 MeV, depending on the chiral gap parameter (Δ_χ from 300 to 600 MeV)
- Critical mass M_cr for 1-year nucleation time: ranges from 0.45 M_⊙ (GM1, B^1/4 = 160 MeV, σ = 10 MeV/fm^2) to 1.79 M_⊙ (GM1, B^1/4 = 180 MeV, σ = 30 MeV/fm^2)
- Bag constant range for viable GRB mechanism: 160 MeV ≲ B^1/4 ≲ 170 MeV
- Hybrid/quark star masses: 1.2–1.8 M_⊙ with radii ~8.5–10 km
- Surface tension values used: 10 to 40 MeV/fm^2
- Critical density for mixed phase formation: > 4ρ_0 for Z/A ~ 0.3

## Context
This work extends the scenario of Drago, Lavagno, and Pagliara (2003, Astrophys.J.586,1250) associating delayed Gamma Ray Bursts with the conversion of metastable hadronic stars into quark-containing stars, and builds on the CFL phase calculations of Alford, Rajagopal, and Wilczek (1999) and the mass-radius studies of Alford and collaborators (2001) using a constant gap. The authors show that a chemical-potential-dependent gap can satisfy observational constraints from Sanwal et al. (2002), Cottam et al. (2002), Quaintrell et al. (2003), Heinke et al. (2003), Dey et al. (1998), Li et al. (1999), and Burwitz et al. (2002), which had appeared contradictory. The mechanism enables a natural explanation for the days-to-years delay between supernova explosions and subsequent GRBs observed by Amati et al. (2000) and Reeves et al. (2002), and is testable against the supranova model of Vietri and collaborators.
