# Anthropics of Aluminum-26 Decay and Biological Homochirality

> Results of recent experiment reinstate feasibility to the hypothesis that biomolecular homochirality originates from beta decay. Coupled with hints that this process occurred extraterrestrially suggests aluminum-26 as the most likely source. If true, then its appropriateness is highly dependent on the half-life and energy of this decay. Demanding that this mechanism hold places new constraints on the anthropically allowed range for multiple parameters, including the electron mass, difference between up and down quark masses, the fine structure constant, and the electroweak scale. These new constraints on particle masses are tighter than those previously found. However, one edge of the allowed region is nearly degenerate with an existing bound, which, using what is termed here as `the principle of noncoincident peril', is argued to be a strong indicator that the fine structure constant must be an environmental parameter in the multiverse.

## Key claim

The paper argues that if biomolecular homochirality originates from beta decay of aluminum-26 in interstellar space, then the half-life and decay energy of this isotope place new anthropic constraints on fundamental parameters; however, one edge of the allowed parameter region is nearly degenerate with an existing bound, which the authors interpret as evidence that the fine structure constant must be an environmental parameter in the multiverse.

## Method

The authors model the half-life of aluminum-26 using the standard beta decay formula with a Fermi integral for a second-forbidden transition, calibrating the normalization to the observed half-life of 717,000 years. They compute the decay energy using the semi-empirical mass formula (SEMF) for binding energies of aluminum-26 and magnesium-26, with a recalibration shift to match the observed energy release, and express the energy in terms of the electron mass, proton mass, up-down quark mass difference, and fine structure constant. They then Taylor expand the half-life around observed parameter values and compare the resulting allowed region to preexisting anthropic bounds from hydrogen stability and the proton-proton chain exothermicity condition.

## Key numbers

* The observed half-life of aluminum-26 is 717,000 years, with the decay energy parameter w0 = 2.3 (dimensionless, in units of electron mass).
* The first-order Taylor expansion gives: t1/2 = 717,000 yr (1 + 7.29 δme/me0 + 26.03 δΔm/Δm0 - 25.72 δmp/mp0 - 75.32 δα/α0 + 4 δv/v0).
* The decay will not occur at all when w0 < 1, which translates to the condition: me + Δm < 3.68 (mp/mp0) (1.97 α/α0 - 0.97) MeV.
* The experimentally established chiral asymmetry from polarized electrons is of order 3 × 10^{-4}.

## Context

This work extends the hypothesis of Cline (2005) that aluminum-26 decay in molecular clouds could imprint biomolecular homochirality, which was recently supported by the experimental demonstration of chirality induction by polarized electrons in Dreiling and Gay (2014). The paper compares its new anthropic constraints to preexisting bounds from hydrogen stability and the proton-proton chain exothermicity condition from Hogan (2000) and closely related work, and argues that the near-degeneracy of one boundary with an existing bound supports the multiverse interpretation of the fine structure constant.
