# Delayed outflows from black hole accretion tori following neutron star binary coalescence

> Expulsion of neutron-rich matter following the merger of neutron star (NS) binaries is crucial to the radioactively-powered electromagnetic counterparts of these events and to their relevance as sources of r-process nucleosynthesis. Here we explore the long-term (viscous) evolution of remnant black hole accretion disks formed in such mergers by means of two-dimensional, time-dependent hydrodynamical simulations. The evolution of the electron fraction due to charged-current weak interactions is included, and neutrino self-irradiation is modeled as a lightbulb that accounts for the disk geometry and moderate optical depth effects. Over several viscous times (~1s), a fraction ~10% of the initial disk mass is ejected as a moderately neutron-rich wind (Y_e ~ 0.2) powered by viscous heating and nuclear recombination, with neutrino self-irradiation playing a sub-dominant role. Although the properties of the outflow vary in time and direction, their mean values in the heavy-element production region are relatively robust to variations in the initial conditions of the disk and the magnitude of its viscosity. The outflow is sufficiently neutron-rich that most of the ejecta forms heavy r-process elements with mass number A >130, thus representing a new astrophysical source of r-process nucleosynthesis, distinct from that produced in the dynamical ejecta. Due to its moderately high entropy, disk outflows contain a small residual fraction ~1% of helium, which could produce a unique spectroscopic signature.

## Key claim

Over several viscous times (~1 s), approximately 10% of the initial disk mass is ejected as a moderately neutron-rich wind (Ye ~ 0.2) powered by viscous heating and nuclear recombination, with neutrino self-irradiation playing a sub-dominant role; the outflow properties are relatively robust to variations in initial disk conditions and viscosity magnitude, but the authors note that their models employ approximations including a pseudo-Newtonian potential, a lightbulb neutrino treatment, and alpha-viscosity.

## Method

The authors performed two-dimensional, axisymmetric time-dependent hydrodynamical simulations using the FLASH3.2 code on a spherical polar grid, evolving the equations of mass, momentum, energy, and lepton number conservation. They included charged-current weak interactions in the optically-thin regime to track electron fraction evolution, modeled neutrino self-irradiation via a lightbulb prescription accounting for disk geometry and moderate optical depth, and approximated angular momentum transport using an alpha-viscosity parameterization with a Shakura-Sunyaev prescription. The simulations used a pseudo-Newtonian potential for the black hole gravity and the Helmholtz equation of state with nuclear statistical equilibrium for neutrons, protons, and alpha particles.

## Key numbers

- Ejected mass fraction: ~10% of the initial disk mass is unbound over ~1 s (viscous timescale)
- Electron fraction of outflow: Ye ~ 0.2 (moderately neutron-rich)
- Initial torus mass range explored: 0.01 to 0.1 solar masses (Mt0 = 0.01, 0.03, 0.10 Msun)
- Black hole masses used: 3 and 10 solar masses
- Initial torus radii: 50 km and 75 km
- Viscosity parameter alpha values: 0.01, 0.03, and 0.10
- Initial electron fractions explored: 0.05, 0.10, and 0.15
- Initial entropies: 6, 8, and 10 kB per baryon
- Mass-flux-weighted electron fraction range across models: 0.14 to 0.21
- Mass-flux-weighted entropy range: 16 to 19 kB per baryon
- Expansion timescale range: 38 to 193 ms
- Ejecta mass to accreted mass ratio (Mej/Macc): 0.04/0.92 to 0.22/0.78
- Residual helium fraction in outflows: ~1%
- Characteristic outflow velocity: ~2.2 x 10^4 km/s (from table values ~2.2 in units of 10,000 km/s)
- Characteristic outflow density: ~1.6 x 100 g/cm^3 at 10^9 cm radius (from standard model)

## Context

This work extends the height-integrated models of Metzger et al. (2009) (MPQ09), who estimated 20-50% of initial disk mass could be unbound with Ye ~ 0.1-0.4, and the axisymmetric simulations of Lee et al. (2009), which confirmed helium evaporation but did not explicitly follow weak interactions. The results provide a new astrophysical source of r-process nucleosynthesis distinct from dynamical ejecta, with most ejecta forming heavy r-process elements with mass number A > 130, and enable more detailed predictions for kilonova light curves and nucleosynthetic yields from binary neutron star mergers, building on the opacity calculations of Kasen et al. (2013) and Barnes & Kasen (2013).
