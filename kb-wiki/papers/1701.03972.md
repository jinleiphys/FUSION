# Ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions: the first billion seconds

> I first review the early history of the ultrarelativistic heavy ion program, starting with the 1974 Bear Mountain Workshop, and the 1983 Aurora meeting of the U.S. Nuclear Science Committee, just one billion seconds ago, which laid out the initial science goals of an ultrarelativistic collider. The primary goal, to discover the properties of nuclear matter at the highest energy densities, included finding new states of matter -- the quark-gluon plasma primarily -- and to use collisions to open a new window on related problems of matter in cosmology, neutron stars, supernovae, and elsewhere. To bring out how the study of heavy ions and hot, dense matter in QCD has been fulfilling these goals. I concentrate on a few topics, the phase diagram of matter in QCD, and connections of heavy ion physics to cold atoms, cosmology, and neutron stars.

## Key claim

The ultrarelativistic heavy ion program has succeeded in producing nuclear matter at energy densities several orders of magnitude beyond that in nuclei at rest and has discovered a strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma that behaves collectively, though the original goal was to discover new states of matter including a quark-gluon plasma rather than making a quark-gluon plasma being *the* goal.

## Method

The paper reviews the early history of the ultrarelativistic heavy ion program from the 1974 Bear Mountain Workshop through the 1983 Aurora NSAC meeting that laid out initial science goals for RHIC, then surveys subsequent developments in the QCD phase diagram of hot dense matter, connections to ultracold atomic physics, cosmology, and neutron star physics. The author concentrates on the phase diagram of matter in QCD and connections of heavy ion physics to cold atoms, cosmology, and neutron stars, drawing on lattice gauge calculations, theoretical models of color superconductivity, and observational constraints from neutron star masses.

## Key numbers

- The 1983 Aurora meeting occurred approximately 10^9 seconds before the QM2015 conference, whence the paper's title
- RHIC initial luminosity was set at 10^25 cm^-2 sec^-1 with a possible upgrade to 10^28 cm^-2 sec^-1
- First collisions at RHIC (Au on Au) occurred in 2000, 17 years after the 1983 Aurora meeting
- First experimental collisions of Pb on Pb at the LHC at 2.76 TeV/A took place in November 2010, 26 years after the 1984 ECFA-CERN meeting
- Lattice gauge calculations indicate a rapid crossover from hadronic to quark-gluon phase at temperature T ~ 150-155 MeV
- Cold atomic fermonic ^6Li at unitarity has viscosity to entropy ratio eta/s less than 0.4, comparable with RHIC value less than 0.2
- Neutron star PSR J1614-2230 has mass M = 1.97 ± 0.04 solar masses
- Neutron star PSR J0348+0432 has mass M = 2.01 ± 0.04 solar masses
- Neutron star radii are approximately 10-12 km for some dozen neutron stars

## Context

This review extends the historical narrative of the ultrarelativistic heavy ion program from the Bear Mountain Workshop (1974) and the Aurora NSAC meeting (1983) through the RHIC and LHC eras, updating the scientific motivations and achievements. The paper connects heavy ion physics to ultracold atomic systems (noting similarities despite twenty orders of magnitude difference in energy scales), cosmology (the smooth QCD crossover implying no first-order hadronization transition in the early universe), and neutron star physics (where recent observations of two-solar-mass neutron stars by Demorest (2010) and Antoniadis (2013) constrain the equation of state). The work enables continued refinement of the QCD phase diagram through beam energy scans at RHIC and SPS searching for the Asakawa-Yazaki critical point, and supports the development of quark-hadron continuity and percolation pictures of the deconfinement transition.
