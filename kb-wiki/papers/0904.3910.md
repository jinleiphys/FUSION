# Systematic Study of Fission Barriers of Excited Superheavy Nuclei

> A systematic study of fission-barrier dependence on excitation energy has been performed using the self-consistent finite-temperature Hartree-Fock+BCS (FT-HF+BCS) formalism with the SkM* Skyrme energy density functional. The calculations have been carried out for even-even superheavy nuclei with Z ranging between 110 and 124. For an accurate description of fission pathways, the effects of triaxial and reflection-asymmetric degrees of freedom have been fully incorporated. Our survey demonstrates that the dependence of isentropic fission barriers on excitation energy changes rapidly with particle number, pointing to the importance of shell effects even at large excitation energies characteristic of compound nuclei. The fastest decrease of fission barriers with excitation energy is predicted for deformed nuclei around N=164 and spherical nuclei around N=184 that are strongly stabilized by ground-state shell effects. For nuclei 240Pu and 256Fm, which exhibit asymmetric spontaneous fission, our calculations predict a transition to symmetric fission at high excitation energies due to the thermal quenching of static reflection asymmetric deformations.

## Key claim
The dependence of isentropic fission barriers on excitation energy changes rapidly with particle number, indicating that shell effects remain important even at the large excitation energies characteristic of compound nuclei formed in hot-fusion reactions. The fastest barrier decrease is predicted for nuclei around N=164 (deformed) and N=184 (spherical) that are strongly stabilized by ground-state shell effects.

## Method
The authors performed systematic finite-temperature Hartree-Fock+BCS (FT-HF+BCS) calculations using the SkM* Skyrme energy density functional with a density-dependent delta pairing interaction. They employed the symmetry-unrestricted solver HFODD to treat triaxial and reflection-asymmetric degrees of freedom along constrained fission pathways defined by the mass quadrupole moment Q20, assuming an isentropic (constant entropy) rather than isothermal description of the fission process.

## Key numbers
- Calculations performed for 48 even-even superheavy nuclei with Z ranging 110-124 and N ranging 166-188
- For 240Pu at kT_g.s.=1.0 MeV (E*=13.82 MeV), both saddle points are reduced by 2-2.5 MeV compared to zero temperature
- At kT_g.s.=2 MeV, 240Pu has E*=70.88 MeV and barriers become very small due to thermal melting of shell effects
- For 256Fm, kT_g.s.=1.0 MeV corresponds to E*=14.93 MeV and kT_g.s.=1.5 MeV corresponds to E*=39.20 MeV
- The maximum of the inverse barrier damping parameter gamma_D^{-1} is predicted for N=176 and 178
- Small gamma_D^{-1} values are found for N=166 and 168, indicating rapid barrier decrease around 280112 (deformed superheavy region)
- For Z=122 and 124, largest barrier damping is expected around N=182 and 184 (spherical N=184 magic gap region)

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous study (Pei 2009) by performing a global systematic survey of fission barrier damping in superheavy nuclei. The calculations use the finite-temperature density functional theory approach, building on earlier work on thermal shape transitions (Diebel 1981, Faber 1984) and the importance of triaxiality at saddle points (Larsson 1972, Burvenich 2004). The results provide microscopic barrier damping parameters that can be used to model the survival probability of superheavy compound nuclei in heavy-ion fusion experiments, particularly for the hot-fusion reactions using 48Ca beams (Oganessian 2007).
