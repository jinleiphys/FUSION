# High-$K$ multi-particle bands and pairing reduction in $^{254}$No

> The multi-particle states and rotational properties of two-particle bands in $^{254}$No are investigated by the cranked shell model (CSM) with pairing correlations treated by a particle-number conserving (PNC) method. For the first time, the rotational bands on top of two-particle $K^π=3^+,8^-$ and $10^+$ states and the pairing reduction are studied theoretically in $^{254}$No. The experimental excitation energies and moments of inertia for the multi-particle state are reproduced well by the calculation. Better agreement with the data are achieved by including the high-order deformation $\varepsilon_{6}$ which leads to enlarged $Z=100$ and $N=152$ deformed shell gaps. The rise of the $J^{(1)}$ in these two-particle bands compared with the ground-state band is attributed to the pairing reduction due to the Pauli blocking effects.

## Key claim

For the first time, the rotational bands built on top of two-particle \(K^{\pi}=3^{+},8^{-}\) and \(10^{+}\) states in \(^{254}\)No are studied theoretically, and the rise in kinematic moment of inertia \(J^{(1)}\) for these bands compared with the ground-state band is attributed to pairing reduction due to Pauli blocking effects, with better agreement to experimental data achieved by including the high-order deformation \(\varepsilon_{6}\).

## Method

The cranked shell model (CSM) Hamiltonian with monopole and quadrupole pairing correlations was solved using a particle-number conserving (PNC) method in a truncated Fock space, which exactly conserves particle number and accounts for Pauli blocking. The Nilsson parameters optimized for the \(A=250\) mass region were used with deformation parameters \(\varepsilon_{2}=0.26\), \(\varepsilon_{4}=0.02\), and \(\varepsilon_{6}=0.042\). The Hamiltonian was diagonalized in a cranked many-particle configuration (CMPC) space constructed from proton \(N=4,5,6\) and neutron \(N=6,7\) shells, with effective pairing strengths \(G_{0}=0.25\) MeV and \(G_{2}=0.02\) MeV for both protons and neutrons.

## Key numbers

- The experimental excitation energy of the \(K^{\pi}=3^{+}\) two-proton state at \(0.988\) MeV is reproduced by the PNC-CSM calculation with \(\varepsilon_{6}=0.042\), giving \(1.154\) MeV (without \(\varepsilon_{6}\) the \(3^{+}\) state is not the lowest two-particle state).
- The \(K^{\pi}=8^{-}\) isomer at \(1.297\) MeV is best reproduced by the two-proton configuration \(\pi\frac{7}{2}^{-}[514]\otimes\pi\frac{9}{2}^{+}[624]\) with calculated energy \(1.272\) MeV (with \(\varepsilon_{6}=0.042\)).
- The \(K^{\pi}=10^{+}\) state at \(2.013\) MeV is calculated at \(2.526\) MeV with the configuration \(\nu\frac{9}{2}^{-}[734]\otimes\nu\frac{11}{2}^{-}[725]\).
- The four-particle \(K^{\pi}=14^{+}\) state at \(2.928\) MeV is calculated at \(2.991\) MeV; the \(K^{\pi}=16^{+}_{1}\) state is calculated at \(3.215\) MeV, \(0.287\) MeV above the experimental value.
- The kinematic moment of inertia \(J^{(1)}\) for the two-particle bands shows a \(20\%\sim25\%\) increase compared to the ground-state band at low rotational frequency.
- Without pairing, all two-particle bands and the ground-state band have similar \(J^{(1)}\) values of \(65-70\ \hbar^{2}\text{MeV}^{-1}\).

## Context

This work provides the first detailed theoretical investigation of rotational bands built on high-\(K\) multi-particle states in \(^{254}\)No, extending prior studies that focused mainly on the yrast band (e.g., Ren (2002), Zhang (2013), He (2009)) and on the excitation energies of high-\(K\) isomers (Liu (2011)). The calculations confirm the importance of the \(\varepsilon_{6}\) deformation for reproducing the correct ordering of two-particle states, consistent with the configuration-constrained potential-energy surfaces calculations of Liu (2011). The results enable future experimental and theoretical work to resolve the ongoing dispute over the configuration of the \(8^{-}\) isomer and the spin assignment of the four-particle isomer, and they provide a framework for studying pairing reduction in superheavy nuclei.
