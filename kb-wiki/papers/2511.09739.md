# CP violation

> CP violation, which involves breaking the combined charge-conjugation (C) and parity (P) symmetries, is essential for understanding the observed matter-antimatter asymmetry in the universe. It is a key feature of the Standard Model, originating from complex phases in the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa quark mixing matrix. Despite the successes of the SM, the amount of CP violation it predicts is not enough to explain baryogenesis, prompting searches for new sources of CP violation in other areas of particle physics. This article offers a pedagogical introduction to the theoretical foundations of C, P, and T symmetries and their combinations. It also emphasizes the importance of CP violation in modern particle physics and how it can be used to explore New Physics.

## Key claim

This pedagogical article reviews the theoretical foundations of C, P, and T symmetries and their combinations, emphasizing that while CP violation in the Standard Model originates from complex phases in the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa quark mixing matrix, the amount of CP violation it predicts is not enough to explain baryogenesis, prompting searches for new sources of CP violation in other areas of particle physics.

## Method

The article systematically introduces discrete symmetry transformations starting from classical non-relativistic mechanics, then extending to relativistic mechanics and electrodynamics, and finally to non-relativistic and relativistic quantum mechanics and quantum field theory. It derives transformation properties of fields, currents, and the Dirac equation under C, P, T, and their combinations, using the mode decomposition of scalar, vector, and fermion field operators to show how creation and annihilation operators transform.

## Key numbers

- The time-reversal operator squared (\({\cal T}^2\)) has eigenvalue \(+1\) for bosonic systems and \(-1\) for fermionic systems, leading to Kramer's degeneracy theorem for fermions.
- The charge conjugation matrix in the Dirac representation takes the form \({\cal C} = i\gamma^2 \gamma^0\).
- The parity transformation changes a contravariant four-vector into a covariant one: \(x^\mu = (t, \mathbf{r}) \xrightarrow{P} x_\mu = (t, -\mathbf{r})\).

## Context

This article provides a pedagogical introduction to discrete symmetries, extending the classical treatment of symmetries in electrodynamics to quantum field theory, referencing standard textbooks such as Sakurai (2011) and Peskin (1995) for quantum mechanics and QFT foundations, and Branco (1999) for CP violation. It enables readers to understand how CP violation arises in the Standard Model and how experimental tests of discrete symmetries can probe New Physics beyond the Standard Model, motivated by the Sakharov conditions (Sakharov, 1967) for baryogenesis.
