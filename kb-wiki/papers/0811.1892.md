# Nuclear effects in proton decay

> An experimental observation of proton decay would be a spectacular proof of grand unification. Currently, the best constraint on the proton lifetime for the p->e+ pi0 decay channel, coming from the Super-Kamiokande experiment, reaches 8*10^33 years. To improve the measurement, much bigger detectors should be constructed. Moreover, a better description of the bound-nucleon states and of the propagation of the proton-decay products through nuclear matter have to be developed. In this article special attention is paid to the argon nucleus because a liquid argon detector is a promising candidate for the future large apparatus.

## Key claim
The paper argues that improving the sensitivity of proton decay searches requires a more accurate description of nuclear effects than the commonly used local Fermi gas model; using a spectral function that accounts for shell structure and short-range correlations can significantly alter the predicted momentum distributions of decay products, but it is not yet clear whether this difference persists when intranuclear cascades are included.

## Method
The authors simulate the two-body decay of a proton bound in an argon nucleus into a kaon and antineutrino, comparing two nuclear models: the local Fermi gas model (which ignores shell structure and nucleon correlations) and a spectral function (which includes shell structure and short-range correlations). They generate 20,000 events for each model and also incorporate the Bertini cascade implemented in GEANT4 to simulate rescattering of decay products through the nucleus.

## Key numbers
- The current best constraint on the proton lifetime for the \(p\to e^{+}\pi^{0}\) decay channel from Super-Kamiokande is \(8.0\times 10^{33}\) years (with an earlier limit of \(1.6\times 10^{33}\) years).
- For the \(p\to \bar{\nu}K^{+}\) channel, the Super-Kamiokande limit is \(2.3\times 10^{33}\) years, with an efficiency below 9%.
- Using the spectral function instead of the local Fermi gas model lowers the peak of the kaon momentum distribution by approximately 20% and extends the allowed momentum range.
- The ICARUS experiment uses 600 tons of liquid argon; the proposed LAGUNA project considers a 100 kton liquid argon detector.

## Context
This work extends the study of nuclear effects in proton decay simulations, building on prior spectral function calculations for argon by Ankowski (2008) and for calcium that showed good accuracy in electron scattering. It contrasts the local Fermi gas model (used in GEANT4) with realistic spectral functions from Benhar, Farina, and Nakamura (2008) and Bus, Leitner, Mosel, and Alvarez-Ruso (2007). The results are relevant for designing future large detectors such as Hyper-Kamiokande (550 kton water), UNO, and the LAGUNA project’s liquid argon option, which could cover the full range of predicted lifetimes for the \(p\to \bar{\nu}K^{+}\) mode within 10 years.
