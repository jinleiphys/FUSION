# Non Trivial Effects in Heavy Nuclear Systems due to Charge Rearrangement

> In this report we propose that a charge rearrangement within heavy ions may occur during fusion reactions, lowering the Coulomb barrier as the nuclear systems approach each other. In this situation a modified Gamow factor has been calculated, which emphasizes the importance of spatial degrees of freedom in nuclear matter.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a charge rearrangement within heavy ions, modeled as an induced electric dipole, may occur during fusion reactions and lower the Coulomb barrier as the systems approach each other; the effect is strongly dependent on the specific system and thermodynamic variables, and the authors note that the charge fraction participating in the rearrangement cannot be derived within the present model and must be estimated from other considerations.

## Method

The authors model charge rearrangement inside a heavy ion as an induced electric dipole moment, solving the scalar Schrödinger equation in parabolic coordinates for a proton incident on a heavy ion with this dipole correction. They derive a modified Gamow factor from the resulting hypergeometric confluent solutions, taking the limit of small azimuthal outgoing angle θ to satisfy the dipole approximation. The dipole moment is estimated semi-classically from an E1 transition in ¹⁷⁹Hf, using the gamma-ray energy as the work done in a Yukawa potential to obtain p_z = 0.122 e·fm.

## Key numbers

- For the reaction ¹⁷⁹Hf + p, the estimated dipole moment from an E1 transition (E_γ = 214.3 keV, half-life 1.86 ns) is p_z = 0.122 e·fm.
- At kT = 3 keV, the azimuthal outgoing angle θ = 0.00012 rad; at kT = 10 keV, θ = 0.00039 rad.
- In the limit β → 0 (no charge rearrangement), the modified Gamow factor reduces to the standard expression G_f = exp(-2π Z₁ Z₂ e² / ħ v).

## Context

This work extends the standard point-like Coulomb approximation for fusion reactions, which the authors note has shown anomalies near the Coulomb barrier (citing C. Tenreiro and others) and in p-element abundances in supernovae (citing C. Rolfs). The proposed charge rearrangement mechanism could contribute to understanding the electron screening puzzle in laboratory measurements (citing H. Assenbaum and others) and may affect reaction networks in stellar interiors, particularly for heavy-element burning at lower energies. The authors plan to develop molecular-dynamics-like simulations to estimate the charge fraction participating in the rearrangement, and note that spin effects and higher-order multipole moments are left for future work.
