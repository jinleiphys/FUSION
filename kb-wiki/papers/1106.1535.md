# Stability of fragments and study of participant-spectator matter at peak center-of-mass energy

> We simulate the central reactions of nearly symmetric, and asymmetric systems, for the energies at which the maximum production of IMFs occurs (E$_{c.m.}^{peak}$).This study is carried out by using hard EOS along with cugnon cross section and employing MSTB method for clusterization. We study the various properties of fragments. The stability of fragments is checked through persistence coefficient, gain term and binding energy. The information about the thermalization and stopping in heavy-ion collisions is obtained via relative momentum, anisotropy ratio, and rapidity distribution. We find that for a complete stopping of incoming nuclei very heavy systems are required. The mass dependence of various quantities (such as average and maximum central density, collision dynamics as well as the time zone for hot and dense nuclear matter) is also presented. In all cases (i.e., average and maximum central density, collision dynamics as well as the time zone for hot and dense nuclear matter) a power law dependence is obtained.

## Key claim

At the peak center-of-mass energy for maximum intermediate mass fragment (IMF) production, the degree of stopping and thermalization in heavy-ion collisions depends strongly on system mass; very heavy systems are required for complete stopping, while lighter systems exhibit significant transparency, and all studied mass-dependent quantities (density, collision dynamics, dense-matter time zone) follow a power-law dependence on composite mass.

## Method

Central collisions (b = 0.0 fm) of seven systems from 20Ne+20Ne to 197Au+197Au were simulated using the Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) model with a hard equation of state and energy-dependent Cugnon nucleon-nucleon cross section. The phase space was clusterized using the Minimum Spanning Tree method with binding energy check (MSTB), with a binding energy threshold of -4.0 MeV for fragments with three or more nucleons. Fragment stability was quantified via persistence coefficient, gain term, and binding energy per nucleon, while thermalization and stopping were characterized using relative momentum, anisotropy ratio, and rapidity distribution.

## Key numbers

- Incident energies at peak IMF production: 24 AMeV (20Ne+20Ne), 46 AMeV (40Ar+45Sc), 69 AMeV (58Ni+58Ni), 77 AMeV (86Kr+93Nb), 96 AMeV (129Xe+118Sn), 124 AMeV (86Kr+197Au), and 104 AMeV (197Au+197Au)
- Persistence coefficient reaches asymptotic value of 0.8 after approximately 130 fm/c for all fragment types
- Binding energy per nucleon for heavy fragments at 200 fm/c: -5 to -7 MeV
- Power-law exponent for participant matter fraction: tau = 0.15 ± 0.016 (nearly mass-independent)
- Power-law exponent for spectator matter fraction: tau = -0.46 ± 0.039
- Power-law exponent for super-participant matter fraction: tau = 0.46 ± 0.06
- Power-law exponent for maximal average density: tau = 0.08 ± 0.02
- Power-law exponent for maximal maximum density: tau = 0.034 ± 0.008
- Power-law exponent for time zone with average density above normal nuclear density: tau = 0.15 ± 0.05
- Power-law exponent for total allowed nucleon-nucleon collisions: tau = 1.28 ± 0.054

## Context

This work extends the experimental findings of Sisan et al. (2001), who observed that peak IMF multiplicity follows a power law with exponent 0.7 and that peak energy increases linearly with system mass, by examining the nuclear dynamics at those specific peak energies. It builds on prior QMD simulations by Dhawan et al. (2006) on stopping in intermediate-energy collisions and by Sood and Puri (2006) on thermalization and participant-spectator matter, confirming that heavier systems achieve greater equilibration. The results enable systematic parametrization of density, collision dynamics, and dense-matter lifetime at peak IMF energies, providing a framework for predicting these quantities for other systems via the reported power-law exponents.
