# Derivation and assessment of strong coupling core-particle model from the Kerman-Klein-Dönau-Frauendorf theory

> We review briefly the fundamental equations of a semi-microscopic core-particle coupling method that makes no reference to an intrinsic system of coordinates. We then demonstrate how an intrinsic system can be introduced in the strong coupling limit so as to yield a completely equivalent formulation. It is emphasized that the conventional core-particle coupling calculation introduces a further approximation that avoids what has hitherto been the most time-consuming feature of the full theory, and that this approximation can be introduced either in the intrinsic system, the usual case, or in the laboratory system, our preference. A new algorithm is described for the full theory that largely removes the difference in complexity between the two types of calculation. Comparison of the full and approximate theories for some representative cases provides a basis for the assessment of the accuracy of the traditional approach. We find that for well-deformed nuclei, e.g. 157Gd and 157Tb, the core-coupling method and the full theory give similar results.

## Key claim

The conventional strong coupling core-particle model can be derived as an approximation to the more complete Kerman-Klein-Dönau-Frauendorf (KKDF) theory by assuming that the physical states of the odd nucleus can be expanded using only the physical (positive-energy) solutions of the zero-excitation limit, rather than the full set of physical and unphysical solutions required for mathematical completeness in the exact theory. For well-deformed nuclei such as 157Gd and 157Tb, the core-coupling method and the full theory give similar results.

## Method

The authors start from the KKDF equations of motion for odd nuclei, which are expressed in the laboratory frame and require solving a sequence of eigenvalue problems with a projection technique to select physical solutions from a set twice as large as the number of physical states. They transform these equations to an intrinsic coordinate system for axially symmetric deformed nuclei, then introduce the approximation that the wavefunction can be expanded using only the physical solutions of the Hartree-Bogoliubov equations obtained at zero rotational excitation. This reduces the problem to a single diagonalization of a core-particle coupling matrix. The authors also describe a new algorithm that simplifies the full KKDF calculation by largely removing its computational disadvantage relative to the approximate method.

## Key numbers

- For 157Gd and 157Tb, the core-coupling method and the full KKDF theory give similar results (no numerical deviations reported in the truncated text).
- The number of solutions in the full KKDF model is twice as great as the number of physical states being described, due to the inclusion of pairing interactions.
- The pairing gap is defined as \(2\Delta_a\sqrt{2j_a+1} \cong 2\Delta\sqrt{2j_a+1}\) in the limit of constant pairing matrix element.
- The quadrupole interaction strength is expressed as \(F_{abcd}(2) = -\kappa_2 F_{ab}F_{dc}\) with the intrinsic quadrupole moment \(Q_0\) defined by \(\sum_{bd}F_{db}q^{(2,0)}_{00}(db) \equiv Q_0\).

## Context

This work extends the earlier Kerman-Klein theory of collective motion and the Dönau-Frauendorf (1980s) formulation for odd nuclei by explicitly connecting it to the traditional strong coupling core-particle model, a connection that Ring and Schuck (1980) had described as a mystery. The paper also builds on an earlier paper by one of the authors (Klein, 1970s) that showed how core-particle coupling models could be understood as approximations to Kerman-Klein theory, but which went unnoticed. The new algorithm described here largely removes the computational advantage of the approximate core-particle model over the full KKDF theory, enabling more practical applications of the exact formalism.
