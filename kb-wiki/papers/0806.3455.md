# Role of multifragmentation in spallation reactions

> In nuclear reactions induced by hadrons and ions of high energies, nuclei can disintegrate into many fragments during a short time (~100 fm/c). This phenomenon known as nuclear multifragmentation was under intensive investigation last 20 years. It was established that multifragmentation is an universal process taking place in all reactions when the excitation energy transferred to nuclei is high enough, more than 3 MeV per nucleon, independently on the initial dynamical stage of the reactions. Very known compound nucleus decay processes (sequential evaporation and fission), which are usual for low energies, disappear and multifragmentation dominates at high excitation energy. For this reason, calculation of multifragmentation must be carried on in all cases when production of highly excited nuclei is expected, including spallation reactions. From the other hand, one can consider multifragmentation as manifestation of the liquid-gas phase transition in finite nuclei. This gives way for studying nuclear matter at subnuclear densities and for applications of properties of nuclear matter extracted from multifragmentation reactions in astrophysics. In this contribution, the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM), which combines the compound nucleus processes at low energies and multifragmentation at high energies, is described. The most important ingredients of the model are discussed.

## Key claim

Multifragmentation is a universal decay mode that dominates over sequential evaporation and fission in all nuclear reactions where the excitation energy transferred to the nucleus exceeds 3 MeV per nucleon, regardless of the initial dynamical stage; the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) provides a natural extension of conventional cascade-evaporation calculations by including compound-nucleus processes at low energies and simultaneous break-up at high energies.

## Method

The paper describes the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM), which assumes statistical equilibrium of an excited nuclear system at a low-density freeze-out volume parameterized as V = V₀ + V_f, where V₀ is the normal-density volume and V_f is the free volume for fragment translation. The model enumerates all break-up channels composed of nucleons and excited fragments, treating light nuclei (A ≤ 4) as elementary particles and heavier fragments as heated nuclear liquid drops with free energies parameterized by bulk, surface, Coulomb, and symmetry terms. At low excitation energies a microcanonical treatment with limited partitions (multiplicity M ≤ 3) is used, while at high energies the code transitions to a grand-canonical ensemble; after Monte-Carlo generation of a partition, Coulomb acceleration and propagation of fragments are simulated, followed by secondary de-excitation via Fermi break-up for light fragments (A ≤ 16) and modified Weisskopf evaporation plus fission for heavier fragments.

## Key numbers

- Multifragmentation dominates when excitation energy exceeds 3 MeV per nucleon, independently of the initial dynamical stage.
- The disintegration time for multifragmentation is approximately 100 fm/c.
- The freeze-out volume is parameterized with normal nuclear density ρ₀ ≈ 0.15 fm⁻³.
- The binding energy of infinite nuclear matter W₀ = 16 MeV; surface coefficient B₀ = 18 MeV; critical temperature T_c = 18 MeV; symmetry energy parameter γ = 25 MeV; Coulomb parameter r₀ = 1.17 fm.
- Multifragmentation channels account for 10–15% of the total cross section in high-energy hadron-nucleus reactions and about twice that in high-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions.
- The model describes nuclear matter at temperatures T ≈ 3–8 MeV and densities ρ ≈ 0.1–0.3 ρ₀ in the freeze-out volume.

## Context

This work extends the statistical compound-nucleus concept introduced by Bohr (1936) to high excitation energies where sequential evaporation models (e.g., the GEMINI code) fail to describe multifragmentation data, as demonstrated by experiments from the ALADIN, EOS, ISIS, INDRA, and other collaborations. The SMM is included in complex transport codes such as GEANT4 (CERN) and provides a framework for studying the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition, with applications to the equation of state of stellar matter and supernova nucleosynthesis, building on prior work by Bondorf (1994), Yariv, and the INCL4 collaboration.
