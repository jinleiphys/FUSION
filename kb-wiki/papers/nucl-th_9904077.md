# Mass number dependence in Lambda hypernuclei by means of the Hypervirial Theorems

> The Hypervirial Theorems are applied to a wide class of single particle nuclear potentials, in order to study the mass number dependence of various quantities in Lambda hypernuclei. A very efficient model is assumed for the radius parameter of the potentials and the limitations of the whole method are discussed.

# Mass number dependence in Lambda hypernuclei by means of the Hypervirial Theorems

## Key claim

The Hypervirial Theorems (HVT) can generate analytic series expansions for binding energies, kinetic and potential energies, and mean-square radii of Lambda hypernuclei across a wide class of single-particle potentials, provided the radius parameter follows a specific mass-number-dependent form \( r_0 = r_1 + r_2 A^{-2/3} \). The method is accurate only for deeply bound states in relatively heavy nuclei (\(20 < A < 120\)) and for potentials that are deep and wide enough that the expansion parameter \(s\) remains sufficiently small.

## Method

The author applies the Hypervirial Theorems to the Schrödinger equation for a class of central potentials of the form \(V(r) = -D f(r/R)\), where \(f\) is an even analytic function. Series expansions for energy eigenvalues \(E_{nl}\), potential energy expectation \(\langle V \rangle_{nl}\), kinetic energy \(\langle T \rangle_{nl}\), and mean-square radius \(\langle r^2 \rangle_{nl}\) are derived as power series in the parameter \(s = \sqrt{\hbar^2 / (2\mu D R^2)}\). The radius parameter is then taken as \(r_0 = r_1 + r_2 A^{-2/3}\), leading to an expansion of \(s\) in powers of \(A^{-1/3}\), and the resulting series for each observable is truncated at order \(A^{-1}\). Explicit expressions are given for the Gaussian potential and the reduced Poeschl-Teller potential.

## Key numbers

- Potential depth for the Gaussian potential fitted to experimental data: \(D = 32.45\ \text{MeV}\)
- Radius parameter coefficients for the Gaussian potential: \(r_1 = 1.438\), \(r_2 = -1.118\)
- Binding energy for the 1s state (Gaussian potential): \(E_{1s} = -D \left[ 1 - 6 s_0 A^{-1/3} + \frac{15}{2} s_0^2 A^{-2/3} + \left( \frac{25}{8} s_0^3 + 6 b s_0 \right) A^{-1} \right]\)
- Binding energy for the 1p state (Gaussian potential): \(E_{1p} = -D \left[ 1 - 10 s_0 A^{-1/3} + \frac{35}{2} s_0^2 A^{-2/3} + \left( \frac{105}{8} s_0^3 + 10 b s_0 \right) A^{-1} \right]\)
- Binding energy for the 1d state (Gaussian potential): \(E_{1d} = -D \left[ 1 - 14 s_0 A^{-1/3} + \frac{63}{2} s_0^2 A^{-2/3} + \left( \frac{273}{8} s_0^3 + 14 b s_0 \right) A^{-1} \right]\)
- Binding energy for the 2s state (Gaussian potential): \(E_{2s} = -D \left[ 1 - 14 s_0 A^{-1/3} + \frac{75}{2} s_0^2 A^{-2/3} + \left( \frac{315}{8} s_0^3 + 14 b s_0 \right) A^{-1} \right]\)
- Mean-square radius for the 1s state (Gaussian potential): \(\langle r^2 \rangle_{1s} = R^2 \left[ 3 s_0 A^{-1/3} + \frac{405}{32} s_0^2 A^{-2/3} + \frac{3(135 s_0^3 - 16 b s_0)}{16} A^{-1} \right]\)
- Mean-square radius for the 1p state (Gaussian potential): \(\langle r^2 \rangle_{1p} = R^2 \left[ 5 s_0 A^{-1/3} + \frac{1365}{32} s_0^2 A^{-2/3} + \frac{5(273 s_0^3 - 16 b s_0)}{16} A^{-1} \right]\)
- Valid mass number range for the derived formulas: \(20 < A < 120\)

## Context

This work extends the Hypervirial Theorem approach previously applied to nuclear potentials (Liolios, 1998; Grypeos et al., 1998) by incorporating a mass-number-dependent radius parameter of the form \(r_0 = r_1 + r_2 A^{-2/3}\), which was shown by Millener (1990) and Lalazissis (1994) to provide much better fits to experimental Lambda hypernuclear binding energies than a constant radius parameter. The derived analytic expressions for the Gaussian potential reproduce the numerical results of Lalazissis (1994) and are validated against experimental data from Pile (1991). The method enables calculation of the oscillator level spacing \(\hbar \omega_\Lambda\) via the mean-square radius, which in turn allows computation of the Debye-Waller factor for recoilless Lambda production (Grypeos et al., 1998). The formalism can be extended to nucleon-nucleus interactions with a different radius parameter model.
