# Finite Temperature Quark Matter and Supernova Explosion

> We study the equation of state of quark matter at finite temperature, using a confinement model in which chiral symmetry remains broken in the deconfined phase. Implications for type II supernova explosion and for the structure and evolution of the proto-neutron star are discussed.

# Finite Temperature Quark Matter and Supernova Explosion

## Key claim

Using the Color Dielectric Model (CDM), a confinement model where chiral symmetry remains broken in the deconfined phase, the authors show that a phase transition to quark matter can occur at densities only slightly above nuclear saturation density, softening the equation of state enough to enable a prompt supernova explosion while still yielding a maximum neutron star gravitational mass (1.59 solar masses) compatible with observations, unlike the phenomenological BCK equation of state.

## Method

The authors compute the finite-temperature equation of state of hadronic matter (described by a Walecka-type relativistic mean-field model with HS81 parameters) and quark matter (described by the Color Dielectric Model, with effective quark masses of order 100 MeV). The phase transition is treated using Glendenning's Gibbs construction for two conserved charges (baryon number and electric charge), imposing chemical equilibrium under beta decay and deconfinement, mechanical equilibrium (equal pressures), and fixed lepton fractions to account for neutrino trapping in the proto-neutron star.

## Key numbers

- Mixed phase begins at baryon density 0.23 fm^{-3} in symmetric matter at low temperatures, with no quark matter present in heavy nuclei.
- Pure quark matter phase is reached at densities slightly larger than 2 times nuclear saturation density (2 rho_0) even at zero temperature.
- The equation of state is soft from rho = 0.17 fm^{-3} to rho = 0.34 fm^{-3}, then becomes considerably stiffer than the BCK equation of state above rho = 0.34 fm^{-3}.
- Maximum gravitational mass for a non-rotating cold star in the CDM is 1.59 solar masses (compared to the 1.44 solar mass limit of PSR 1913+16).
- Extra energy at disposal for the shock wave is about 8 foe (8 x 10^{51} erg) relative to the pure Walecka model.
- Maximum baryonic mass for the proto-neutron star remains near 1.82 solar masses, essentially independent of electron fraction and entropy.

## Context

This work extends earlier phenomenological studies of phase transitions in supernovae by Gentile et al. (1993), who did not include temperature or electron fraction dependence, by using a specific quark model (CDM) with finite-temperature Gibbs construction, following the technique developed by Glendenning (1992) and previously applied to nuclear liquid-gas transitions by Muller and Serot (1995). The results contrast with MIT bag model predictions, where the transition starts at much higher densities and the mixed phase extends over a broader range. The paper enables dynamical simulations of supernova explosions using a quark matter equation of state that simultaneously allows prompt explosions and neutron star masses above 1.44 solar masses, and suggests that energetic neutrino emission during the first seconds of the proto-neutron star's life is possible without a discontinuous jump in binding energy.
