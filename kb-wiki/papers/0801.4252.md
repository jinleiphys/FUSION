# Why does the recently proposed simple empirical formula for the lowest excitation energies work so well?

> It has recently been shown that a simple empirical formula, in terms of the mass number and the valence nucleon numbers, is able to describe the main trends of the lowest excitation energies of the natural parity even multipole states up to $10^+$ in even-even nuclei throughout the entire periodic table. In an effort to understand why such a simple formula is so capable, we investigate the possibility of associating each term of the empirical formula with the specific part of the measured excitation energy graph.

## Key claim
The six-parameter empirical formula $E_x = \alpha A^{-\gamma} + \beta_p e^{-\lambda_p N_p} + \beta_n e^{-\lambda_n N_n}$ can describe the lowest excitation energies of natural parity even multipole states up to $10^+$ in even-even nuclei because each of its three terms can be associated with a specific part of the measured excitation energy graph: the first term corresponds to the rotational-band energies of mid-shell nuclei, while the second and third terms correspond to excitation energy differences along isotonic and isotopic chains, respectively.

## Method
The authors analyzed the measured lowest excitation energies of $2^+$, $4^+$, $6^+$, $8^+$, and $10^+$ states in even-even nuclei from the compilation of Raman et al. (2001) and the Table of Isotopes by Firestone et al. (1999). They extracted the first term $\alpha A^{-\gamma}$ by considering nuclei near the doubly mid-shell region where valence nucleon numbers are large, making the exponential terms negligible. They then extracted the parameter products $2\beta_n\lambda_n$ and $2\beta_p\lambda_p$ by evaluating excitation energy differences between adjacent nuclei along isotopic chains (for neutron number changes) and isotonic chains (for proton number changes), respectively, and compared these products with average measured energy differences.

## Key numbers
- For the $2_1^+$ state: $\alpha = 68.37$ MeV, $\gamma = 1.34$, $\beta_p = 0.83$ MeV, $\beta_n = 1.17$ MeV, $\lambda_p = 0.42$, $\lambda_n = 0.28$, fitted to 557 data points.
- For the $4_1^+$ state: $\alpha = 268.04$ MeV, $\gamma = 1.38$, $\beta_p = 1.21$ MeV, $\beta_n = 1.68$ MeV, $\lambda_p = 0.33$, $\lambda_n = 0.23$, fitted to 430 data points.
- For the $6_1^+$ state: $\alpha = 598.17$ MeV, $\gamma = 1.38$, $\beta_p = 1.40$ MeV, $\beta_n = 1.64$ MeV, $\lambda_p = 0.31$, $\lambda_n = 0.18$, fitted to 375 data points.
- For the $8_1^+$ state: $\alpha = 1438.59$ MeV, $\gamma = 1.45$, $\beta_p = 1.34$ MeV, $\beta_n = 1.50$ MeV, $\lambda_p = 0.26$, $\lambda_n = 0.15$, fitted to 309 data points.
- For the $10_1^+$ state: $\alpha = 2316.85$ MeV, $\gamma = 1.47$, $\beta_p = 1.36$ MeV, $\beta_n = 1.65$ MeV, $\lambda_p = 0.21$, $\lambda_n = 0.14$, fitted to 265 data points.
- Average excitation energy difference along isotopic chains for $2_1^+$: $0.99 \pm 0.02$ MeV, compared to $2\beta_n\lambda_n = 0.66$ MeV.
- Average excitation energy difference along isotonic chains for $2_1^+$: $0.70 \pm 0.04$ MeV, compared to $2\beta_p\lambda_p = 0.70$ MeV.

## Context
This work extends the empirical formula previously proposed by Ha and Cha (2007), Yoon, Ha, and Cha (2007), and Kim, Ha, and Cha (2008) for describing lowest excitation energies of natural parity even multipole states in even-even nuclei. The authors provide a physical interpretation for each term of the formula by connecting the first term to the rotational band model of Bohr and Mottelson (1975) for deformed nuclei near the doubly mid-shell region, and by relating the exponential terms to energy differences along isotopic and isotonic chains. This analysis enables future work to understand why such a simple mass-number and valence-nucleon-number dependent formula captures the essential physics of collective excitations across the entire nuclear chart, and may guide the development of similar empirical approaches for other nuclear observables.
