# How to identify a Strange Star

> Contrary to young neutron stars, young strange stars are not subject to the r-mode instability which slows rapidly rotating, hot neutron stars to rotation periods near 10 ms via gravitational wave emission. Young millisecond pulsars are therefore likely to be strange stars rather than neutron stars, or at least to contain significant quantities of quark matter in the interior.

## Key claim

Young millisecond pulsars with rotation periods below 5-10 milliseconds are likely to be strange stars (or neutron stars with significant quark matter content) because, contrary to young neutron stars, young strange stars are not subject to the r-mode instability that slows rapidly rotating hot neutron stars to periods near 10 ms via gravitational wave emission.

## Method

The author calculated the critical angular velocity for the onset of the r-mode instability as a function of temperature by solving the equation where the sum of the inverse timescales for gravitational wave growth, shear viscosity damping, and bulk viscosity damping equals zero. Timescales were derived using analytic formulae from Kokkotas and Stergioulas (1998) for uniform density stars, with shear and bulk viscosities for strange quark matter taken from Heiselberg and Pethick (1993) and Madsen (1992) respectively, and compared to neutron star viscosities from the modified URCA and direct URCA processes.

## Key numbers

- For strange stars, the transition between shear and bulk viscosity damping occurs only after cooling to around 4.6 x 10^6 K (with a dependence on strong coupling constant and strange quark mass), compared to about 10^9 K for neutron stars
- The critical rotation frequency for strange stars is reduced to between 0.26 and 0.32 of the Kepler frequency for strange quark masses of 100 MeV and 200 MeV respectively, corresponding to rotation periods of 3 milliseconds or faster
- For neutron stars with modified URCA processes, the rotation rate is reduced to 7-8% of the Keplerian limit
- The bulk viscosity of strange quark matter is larger than that of neutron star matter by many orders of magnitude, with a typical value of zeta approximately 1.6 x 10^30 [93(Delta v/v_0)^2 + 0.29 T_9^2] g cm^-1 s^-1 for m_s=100 MeV, mu_d=300 MeV, and omega=2 x 10^3 s^-1
- The cooling timescale for a strange star to reach 4.6 x 10^6 K is about 2 x 10^5 years, compared to less than a year for neutron stars to cool to 10^9 K

## Context

This work extends the r-mode instability analysis of Andersson (1998), Friedman and Morsink (1998), Lindblom, Owen, and Morsink (1998), and Kokkotas and Stergioulas (1998) from neutron stars to strange stars, using the corrected bulk viscosity calculations from Madsen (1992) that were several orders of magnitude larger than earlier values from Sawyer (1989). The paper enables the identification of strange stars through observations of young millisecond pulsars, and re-opens the possibility of forming millisecond pulsars by accretion-induced collapse of white dwarfs, which was ruled out for neutron stars by the r-mode instability.
