# Bose-Enhancement and Pauli-Blocking Effects in Transport Models

> The ability of semi-classical transport models to correctly simulate Pauli blocking and Bose enhancement is discussed. In the context of simple quantum mechanical systems, it is shown that using $(1 \pm f)$ enhancements is inadequate to describe systems far from equilibrium. The relaxation of $(1 + f)$ descriptions toward equilibrium is studied both in the context of simple models, including a hot pion gas confined to a box. We give simple estimates for the characteristic number of collisions for a system to relax toward equilibrium.

# Bose-Enhancement and Pauli-Blocking Effects in Transport Models

## Key claim

Semi-classical transport models that incorporate quantum statistics via $(1 \pm f)$ phase-space occupation factors are inadequate for systems far from equilibrium, particularly during the emission stage; however, if sufficient rescattering occurs (on the order of several collisions per particle), such models can relax toward correct equilibrium distributions, though they systematically underestimate total multiplicities.

## Method

The authors compare the $(1 \pm f)$ method, which modifies in-medium cross sections by factors $(1 \pm f_c)(1 \pm f_d)$, against full $n$-body symmetrization for simple quantum systems, including a hot pion gas in a box. They derive analytic expressions for mean occupations under both treatments for bosons and fermions, then simulate the relaxation of $(1+f)$-populated systems toward equilibrium using a Monte Carlo collision algorithm where particles are randomly removed and placed into levels weighted by $d_k (1+n_k) \eta_k$. They extract characteristic relaxation times by linearizing the evolution equations and solving the coupled differential equations for small deviations from equilibrium.

## Key numbers

- For a system with 8 levels spaced by $\Delta E = T/8$ and 40 particles, the initial $(1+f)$ prescription underpopulates the ground state, but collisions restore equilibrium; the characteristic number of collisions for approach to equilibrium $\tau_d$ is approximately $T/(E_2 - E_1)$, where $E_1$ and $E_2$ are ground and first excited state energies.
- For a cubic box of length $L = 10$ fm with temperature $T = 175$ MeV and 200 pions, the estimate of $\tau_d$ is six collisions per particle; after five collisions per particle, symmetrization enhancements for low energies are halfway to equilibrium values.
- In sulphur-on-lead collisions at 200 GeV incident laboratory energy, pions experience on the order of a half dozen collisions; for upcoming lead beams that number could roughly double.

## Context

This work extends the Nordheim (1928) approach of incorporating $(1 \pm f)$ factors into the Boltzmann equation, and the Einstein coefficient formulation of stimulated emission and absorption. It directly addresses the limitations of the $(1 \pm f)$ method used in prior transport simulations by Bauer et al. (1984, 1992) and others. The paper enables quantitative understanding of when semi-classical simulations can be trusted for relativistic heavy-ion collisions, particularly for the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) scheduled for 1999, and identifies conditions under which super-radiant pion sources might require full $n$-body symmetrization treatments as developed by Pratt (1993). For proton-induced collisions with negligible reinteraction, the authors conclude $(1+f)$ models are completely inappropriate and full $n$-particle symmetrization must be used.
