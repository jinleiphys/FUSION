# Nuclear medium cooling scenario in the light of new Cas A cooling data and the 2 M_sun pulsar mass measurements

> Recently, Elshamounty et al. performed a reanalysis of the surface temperature of the neutron star in the supernova remnant Cassiopeia A on the basis of Chandra data measured during last decade, and added a new data point. We show that all reliably known temperature data of neutron stars including those belonging to Cassiopea A can be comfortably explained in our "nuclear medium cooling" scenario of neutron stars. The cooling rates account for medium-modified one-pion exchange in dense matter, polarization effects in the pair-breaking-formation processes operating on superfluid neutrons and protons paired in the 1S_0 state, and other relevant processes. The emissivity of the pair-breaking-formation process in the 3P_2 state is a tiny quantity within our scenario. Crucial for a successful description of the Cassiopeia A cooling proves to be the thermal conductivity from both, the electrons and nucleons, being reduced by medium effects. Moreover, we exploit an EoS which stiffens at high densities due to an excluded volume effect and is capable of describing a maximum mass of 2.1 M_sun, thus including the recent measurements of PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0348+0432.

## Key claim

The authors show that all reliably known neutron star surface temperature data, including the new Cassiopeia A data point from Elshamounty et al. (2013), can be explained within their "nuclear medium cooling" scenario, provided that the thermal conductivity from both electrons and nucleons is reduced by medium effects and that the equation of state is stiffened at high densities to accommodate the 2 solar mass pulsar measurements.

## Method

The authors employ a nuclear medium cooling scenario that includes medium-modified one-pion exchange (MOPE) for nucleon-nucleon interactions, polarization effects in pair-breaking-formation processes for superfluid neutrons and protons paired in the 1S0 state, and a very small 3P2 neutron pairing gap motivated by Schwenk and Friman (2003). They incorporate the lepton thermal conductivity from Shternin and Yakovlev (2007) and suppress the baryon thermal conductivity using MOPE effects, while extending the Heiselberg-Hjorth-Jensen equation of state with an excluded volume correction to reach a maximum mass of 2.06 solar masses.

## Key numbers

- Maximum neutron star mass of 2.06 solar masses for the new HDD equation of state, including the recent measurements of PSR J1614-2230 (1.97 +/- 0.04 solar masses) and PSR J0348-0432 (2.01 +/- 0.04 solar masses)
- Critical density for pion condensation taken as 3 times nuclear saturation density (n0 = 0.16 fm^-3), corresponding to neutron star masses above 1.32 solar masses for the HHJ(delta=0.2) equation of state
- The ratio of medium modified Urca emissivity to standard modified Urca emissivity scales as 3 (n/n0)^(10/3) [Gamma(n)/Gamma(n0)]^6 / [omega*(n)/m_pi]^8
- The electron thermal conductivity parameterization: kappa_e = 8.5 x 10^21 (p_F,e / fm^-1)^2 f_e erg s^-1 cm^-1 K^-1, with f_e approximately 2.7/(e^(1.3T/T_cp)-1) for T less than T_cp
- The baryon thermal conductivity suppression factor: kappa_b = kappa_b^SY (omega*(n)/m_pi)^3 (Gamma(n0)/Gamma(n))^4 (n0/n)
- Excluded volume parameters: alpha = 0.02, beta = 6, sigma = 4, corresponding to quark core radius r_q approximately 0.2 to 0.3 fm

## Context

This work extends the nuclear medium cooling scenario previously applied by Blaschke et al. (2011) to include the new Cassiopeia A data from Elshamounty et al. (2013) and to satisfy the 2 solar mass pulsar constraints from Demorest et al. (2010) and Antoniadis et al. (2013). It contrasts with the "minimal cooling" paradigm of Page et al. (2010) and the approach of Yakovlev et al. (2010), which both attribute the Cas A cooling to a large 3P2 neutron pairing gap around 0.5 to 0.9 x 10^9 K; instead, this scenario uses a tiny 3P2 gap following Schwenk and Friman (2003) and relies on medium-modified processes and reduced thermal conductivity. The new HDD equation of state enables future studies of hadron-quark phase transitions in massive neutron stars.
