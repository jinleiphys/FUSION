# What can we learn from NJL-type models about dense matter?

> The merits and limitations of the Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model as a model for strong interactions at nonzero density are critically discussed. We present several examples, demonstrating that, while in general the results should not be trusted quantitatively, the NJL model is a powerful theoretical tool for getting new insights and ideas about the QCD phase diagram and the dense-matter equation of state.

## Key claim

The NJL model cannot make quantitative predictions about the QCD phase diagram or dense-matter equation of state, but it is a powerful tool for generating new insights and ideas that inspire more serious theoretical and experimental investigations.

## Method

The paper critically reviews NJL-model calculations of the QCD phase diagram at nonzero temperature and chemical potential, including the prediction of a critical endpoint and inhomogeneous chiral condensates. It also examines NJL-model equations of state for quark matter in compact stars, comparing them with hadronic models and discussing the role of vector and diquark couplings.

## Key numbers

- The NJL model predicted a first-order chiral phase transition at low temperatures and high chemical potential, with a crossover at low chemical potential, in agreement with lattice QCD results.
- The critical endpoint (CEP) position depends sensitively on model parameters; for moderate values of the vector coupling, the first-order phase boundary and CEP disappear completely.
- Two compact stars with masses of about 2 solar masses have been discovered (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013), challenging but not ruling out the presence of quark-matter cores.
- By choosing relatively large couplings in the vector and diquark channels, NJL-model calculations can simultaneously reach maximum masses above 2 solar masses and allow a quark-matter core.

## Context

This work extends the pioneering NJL-model phase diagram calculation of Asakawa and Yazaki (1989), which was the first prediction of a critical endpoint. It contrasts with lattice QCD, which cannot be applied at nonzero chemical potential due to the sign problem. The paper enables future work on inhomogeneous phases (inspired by NJL results and also studied in Dyson-Schwinger QCD by Muller 2013) and motivates constructing nucleons and nuclear matter within a single NJL framework, as attempted by Rezaeian (2005), Lawley (2006), and Wang (2010).
