# Analysis of NN Amplitudes up to 2.5 GeV: An Optical Model and Geometric Interpretation

> We analyse the SM97 partial wave amplitudes for nucleon--nucleon (NN) scattering to 2.5 GeV, in which resonance and meson production effects are evident for energies above pion production threshold. Our analyses are based upon boson exchange or quantum inversion potentials with which the sub-threshold data are fit perfectly. Above 300 MeV they are extrapolations, to which complex short ranged Gaussian potentials are added in the spirit of the optical models of nuclear physics and of diffraction models of high energy physics. The data to 2.5 GeV are all well fit. The energy dependences of these Gaussians are very smooth save for precise effects caused by the known $Δ$ and N$^\star$ resonances. With this approach, we confirm that the geometrical implications of the profile function found from diffraction scattering are pertinent in the regime 300 MeV to 2.5 GeV and that the overwhelming part of meson production comes from the QCD sector of the nucleons when they have a separation of their centres of 1 to 1.2 fm. This analysis shows that the elastic NN scattering data above 300 MeV can be understood with a local potential operator as well as has the data below 300 MeV.

# Analysis of NN Amplitudes up to 2.5 GeV: An Optical Model and Geometric Interpretation

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that elastic NN scattering data from 300 MeV to 2.5 GeV can be described using a local potential operator with the same quality as data below 300 MeV, by adding complex short-ranged Gaussian optical potentials to background potentials that perfectly fit sub-threshold data. The analysis confirms that meson production predominantly originates from the QCD sector of the nucleons when their centers are separated by 1 to 1.2 fm, and that the geometrical implications of the profile function from diffraction scattering remain valid in the 300 MeV to 2.5 GeV regime.

## Method

The authors start with either boson exchange potentials (specifically the nonlinear one solitary boson exchange potential OSBEP) or quantum inversion potentials that perfectly reproduce NN scattering data below 300 MeV. For energies above 300 MeV, they add complex short-ranged Gaussian optical potentials (real and imaginary) to these background potentials, solving a relativistic Schrodinger equation with partial wave expansions up to L=6. The Gaussian ranges are varied between 0.5 and 1.2 fm, and the potential strengths are adjusted to fit the SM97 partial wave amplitudes from SAID for proton-proton scattering up to 2.5 GeV and neutron-proton scattering up to 1.6 GeV.

## Key numbers

- The analysis covers NN scattering data up to 2.5 GeV, with confidence in partial wave amplitudes to about 1.75 GeV
- The inversion potentials reproduce continuous phase shift functions to better than 0.02 degrees in every partial wave
- Core radii of the SM94 proton-proton inversion potentials are approximately 0.8 fm, with classical turning points ranging from about 0.5 fm at the highest energy to about 1 fm at low energies
- The imaginary parts of the optical potentials show central peak values of approximately 625 MeV in the D-2 channel and approximately 900 MeV in the F-3 channel, reflecting Delta and N-star resonance contributions
- Gaussian range values between 0.5 and 1.2 fm are used, spanning radii from the little bag (0.5 fm) to the MIT bag (1.2 fm)
- With a channel-independent range of 0.7 to 0.85 fm, the optical models have evenly distributed strengths in the dominating L=0-3 channels
- Maximum loss of flux occurs in a range of 1 to 1.2 fm
- The P-3-0 channel shows maximum absorption at approximately 1 GeV, corresponding to an invariant mass of approximately 2.325 GeV

## Context

This work extends the optical model approach previously applied by Neudatchin et al. (1991) for NN scattering up to 6 GeV, but now using the high-precision SM97 partial wave amplitudes from Arndt et al. (1997) available via SAID. The analysis builds upon boson exchange models (Poppe, 1987; Elster, 1988; Sammarruca, 1990; Valcarce, 1994) and quantum inversion potentials (Kohlhoff, 1994; Sander, 1996, 1997), and provides a geometric connection between low-energy potential models and high-energy diffraction models based on Regge theory with pomeron exchange (Collins, 1977; Jackiw, 1974; Kamran, 1984; Donnachie, 1996; Mattig, 1994). The results enable a unified geometric interpretation of NN scattering across all energies, suggesting that meson production is a genuine QCD effect localized at the confinement surface of the nucleon, consistent with boundary condition models (Jaffe, 1979; Lomon, 1982) and the Moscow potential approach (Moscow, 1984).
