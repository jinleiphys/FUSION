# Level Density in the Complex Scaling Method

> It is shown that the continuum level density (CLD) at unbound energies can be calculated with the complex scaling method (CSM), in which the energy spectra of bound states, resonances and continuum states are obtained in terms of $L^2$ basis functions. In this method, the extended completeness relation is applied to the calculation of the Green functions, and the continuum-state part is approximately expressed in terms of discretized complex scaled continuum solutions. The obtained result is compared with the CLD calculated exactly from the scattering phase shift. The discretization in the CSM is shown to give a very good description of continuum states. We discuss how the scattering phase shifts can inversely be calculated from the discretized CLD using a basis function technique in the CSM.

## Key claim

The continuum level density (CLD) at unbound energies can be calculated using the complex scaling method (CSM) with a finite basis expansion, without requiring any smoothing technique such as the Strutinsky procedure, provided the scaling angle \(\theta\) is chosen large enough that the imaginary parts of the discretized continuum eigenvalues exceed the intervals between them.

## Method

The authors apply the complex scaling transformation to the Hamiltonian and its asymptotic counterpart, diagonalizing them in a finite basis of Gaussian functions with geometrically progressing range parameters. Using the extended completeness relation, the Green functions are expressed as sums over bound states, resonances, and discretized continuum states, yielding the level density as a sum of Lorentzian functions rather than delta functions. The continuum level density (CLD) is then obtained by subtracting the level density of the asymptotic Hamiltonian from that of the full Hamiltonian, and the scattering phase shift is recovered by integrating the CLD over energy.

## Key numbers

- For the simple CGKPM potential with \(\hbar^2/\mu = 1\) MeV/fm\(^2\), the three lowest \(1^-\) resonances are at \(1.1710 - i0.0049\) MeV, \(2.0175 - i0.4863\) MeV, and \(2.5588 - i1.7378\) MeV.
- In the simple potential model, the CLD calculated with \(\theta = 10^\circ, 15^\circ, 20^\circ\) is indistinguishable from the exact CLD derived from the scattering phase shift.
- For the \(^4\)He+\(n\) system, the CLD and phase shifts for the \(3/2^-\), \(1/2^-\), and \(1/2^+\) states calculated with \(\theta = 35^\circ\) show very good quantitative agreement with the exact phase shifts.
- For the \(\alpha+\alpha\) system, the CLD for the \(0^+\), \(2^+\), and \(4^+\) states shows a sharp peak corresponding to each resonance, and the integrated phase shifts are nearly identical to those from scattering solutions.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Kruppa and Arai (1998, 1999) and Arai (1999), who calculated the CLD using a Strutinsky smoothing procedure that exhibited strong dependence on smoothing parameters. By employing the complex scaling method (CSM) and the extended completeness relation originally proposed by Berggren (1968) and developed by Myo et al. (1998), the authors eliminate the need for smoothing entirely. The method enables the calculation of scattering phase shifts directly from the eigenvalues of the complex-scaled Hamiltonian without solving the scattering problem, and it is demonstrated for both short-range (CGKPM potential) and long-range (Coulomb) interactions. The authors suggest that the method should be applicable to coupled-channel systems, for which the extended completeness relation has been proven by Giraud et al. (2004), while the three-body problem remains open.
