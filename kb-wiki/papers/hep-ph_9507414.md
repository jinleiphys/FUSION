# Reheating and Thermalization, Linear Vs. Non-Linear Relaxation

> We consider the case of a scalar field, the inflaton, coupled to both lighter scalars and fermions, and the study the relaxation of the inflaton via particle production in both the linear and non-linear regimes. This has an immediate application to the reheating problem in inflationary universe models. The linear regime analysis offers a rationale for the standard approach to the reheating problem, but we make a distinction between relaxation and thermalization. We find that particle production when the inflaton starts in the {\it non-linear} region is typically a far more efficient way of transfering energy out of the inflaton zero mode and into the quanta of the lighter scalar than single particle decay. For the non-linear regime we take into account self-consistently the evolution of the expectation value of the inflaton field coupled to the evolution of the quantum fluctuations. An exhaustive numerical analysis reveals that the distribution of produced particles is far from thermal, and the effect of open channels. In the fermionic case, Pauli blocking begins to hinder the transfer of energy into the fermion modes very early on in the evolution of the inflaton. We examine the implications of our results to the question of how to calculate the reheating temperature of the universe after inflation.

## Key claim

The paper argues that particle production via non-linear relaxation (induced amplification with back-reaction) is typically far more efficient at transferring energy out of the inflaton zero mode into lighter scalar quanta than single-particle decay (linear relaxation), but the distribution of produced particles remains far from thermal, and the time scales for dissipation via particle production may be widely different from the time scales for collisional thermalization.

## Method

The authors study a scalar inflaton field coupled to lighter scalars and fermions in Minkowski space, assuming relaxation time scales are much shorter than the expansion time scale. For linear relaxation, they solve the equation of motion for the inflaton expectation value via Laplace transform, analyzing the analytic structure of the retarded self-energy at one-loop order. For non-linear relaxation, they evolve the inflaton expectation value self-consistently coupled to the quantum fluctuations of the lighter fields, performing exhaustive numerical analysis of particle production and energy transfer.

## Key numbers

- The decay rate for the inflaton into two sigma particles is given by \(\Gamma = g^2 \Phi_0^2 / (8\pi M) \sqrt{1 - 4M_\sigma^2/M^2}\) for \(M > 2M_\sigma\), with the Breit-Wigner approximation valid for times \(\leq \Gamma^{-1} \ln(\Gamma / M_\sigma)\)
- At zero temperature, for \(M < 2M_\sigma\) the long-time behavior of the inflaton expectation value decays as \(t^{-3/2}\), specifically \(\delta_{\rm cut}(t) \propto \cos(2M_\sigma t + 3\pi/4) / (M_\sigma t)^{3/2}\)
- The finite-temperature width is \(\Gamma(T) = \Gamma(0)[1 + 2 n_b(M/2)]\) where \(\Gamma(0) = g^2 \Phi_0^2 / (8\pi M) \sqrt{1 - 4M_\sigma^2/M^2}\)
- For large temperature, the real part of the self-energy behaves as \(\Sigma(s,\beta) = g^2 \Phi_0^2 M_\sigma T / (\pi s^2) [1 - \sqrt{1 + s^2/(4M_\sigma^2)}] + \mathcal{O}(T^0)\)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of non-linear relaxation and induced amplification previously studied for self-interacting scalar fields (Boyanovsky et al., 1995) and anticipated by Kofman, Linde, and Starobinsky (1994) as a mechanism that would modify the standard picture of reheating based on single-particle decay (Linde, 1990; Abbott et al., 1982; Kirilova et al., 1983). The paper distinguishes relaxation of the expectation value from thermalization, noting that the damping rate from the imaginary part of the self-energy (often identified as a "thermalization rate" in the literature, e.g., Landsman, 1988) actually describes only the approach to equilibrium of the expectation value, while thermalization requires a Boltzmann equation with collision terms. The fermionic Pauli blocking phenomenon found here is similar to that observed by Kluger et al. (1992) in studies of fermionic back-reaction in strong electric fields.
