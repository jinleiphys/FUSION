# Electric Dipole Moments of the Atoms, Molecules, Nuclei and Particles

> A permanent electric dipole moment (EDM) of a particle or system is a separation of charge along its angular-momentum axis and is a direct signal of T-violation and, assuming CPT symmetry, CP violation. For over sixty years EDMs have been studied, first as a signal of a parity-symmetry violation and then as a signal of CP violation that would clarify its role in nature and in theory. Contemporary motivations include the role that CP violation plays in explaining the cosmological matter-antimatter asymmetry and the search for new physics. Experiments on a variety of systems have become ever-more sensitive, but provide only upper limits on EDMs, and theory at several scales is crucial to interpret these limits. Nuclear theory provides connections from Standard-Model and Beyond-Standard-Model physics to the observable EDMs, and atomic and molecular theory reveal how CP-violation is manifest in these systems. EDM results in hadronic systems require that the Standard Model QCD parameter of $\barθ$ must be exceptionally small, which could be explained by the existence of axions - also a candidate dark-matter particle. Theoretical results on electroweak baryogenesis show that new physics is needed to explain the dominance of matter in the universe. Experimental and theoretical efforts continue to expand with new ideas and new questions, and this review provides a broad overview of theoretical motivations and interpretations as well as details about experimental techniques, experiments, and prospects. The intent is to provide specifics and context as this exciting field moves forward.

## Key claim

This review argues that permanent electric dipole moments (EDMs) provide a direct experimental probe of CP violation beyond the Standard Model, which is required to explain the cosmological matter-antimatter asymmetry, but all current experiments have yielded only upper limits consistent with zero, and the Standard Model CKM contribution to any observable EDM is many orders of magnitude smaller than these limits.

## Method

The authors present a comprehensive review of EDM theory and experiment, covering the connections from fundamental Standard Model and Beyond-Standard-Model physics through effective field theory at the electroweak scale (approximately 300 GeV) to low-energy parameters, and then through atomic, molecular, and nuclear structure calculations to experimentally accessible P-odd/T-odd observables. They compile experimental results from neutron, atomic (Cs, Tl, Xe, Hg, Ra), molecular (TlF, YbF, ThO, HfF+), and particle (muon, tau, Lambda hyperon) systems, and discuss the theoretical interpretation in terms of the QCD theta parameter, CKM matrix phase, and effective-field-theory Wilson coefficients.

## Key numbers

* Neutron EDM upper limit: 3.6 × 10^{-26} e cm at 95% confidence level (Afach 2015)
* Mercury-199 atomic EDM upper limit: 7.4 × 10^{-30} e cm at 95% confidence level (Graner 2016)
* ThO molecular electron EDM upper limit: 9.7 × 10^{-29} e cm at 95% confidence level (Baron 2013)
* HfF+ molecular ion electron EDM upper limit: 1.6 × 10^{-28} e cm at 95% confidence level (Cairncross 2017)
* Cesium atomic EDM: (-1.8 ± 6.9) × 10^{-24} e cm, giving 95% upper limit 1.4 × 10^{-23} e cm (Murthy 1989)
* Thallium atomic EDM: (-4.0 ± 4.3) × 10^{-25} e cm, giving 95% upper limit 1.1 × 10^{-24} e cm (Regan 2002)
* Standard Model CKM contribution to neutron EDM estimated at (1-6) × 10^{-32} e cm (Seng 2014)
* Muon EDM upper limit: 1.8 × 10^{-19} e cm at 95% confidence level (Bennett 2008)

## Context

This review extends and updates the comprehensive reviews of EDM experiments and theory by Pospelov and Ritz (2005), Engel, Ramsey-Musolf, and van Kolck (2013), and others, providing a broad overview of theoretical motivations including electroweak baryogenesis and the strong CP problem. The work enables future experimental efforts by detailing state-of-the-art techniques for magnetic shielding, comagnetometry, and ultra-cold neutron sources, and by establishing the effective-field-theory framework for interpreting results across different systems in terms of a common set of low-energy CP-violating parameters.
