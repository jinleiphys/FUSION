# Demonstrating quantum computing with the quark model

> The use of quantum computing to solve a problem in quantum mechanics is illustrated, step by step, by calculating energies and transition amplitudes in a nonrelativistic quark model. The quantum computations feature the use of variational quantum imaginary time evolution implemented using automatic differentiation to determine ground and excited states of charmonium. The calculation of transition amplitudes is illustrated utilizing the Hadamard test. Examples of readout and gate error mitigation are included.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that variational quantum imaginary time evolution (VQITE) implemented with automatic differentiation can successfully compute ground and excited state energies and transition amplitudes for charmonium in a nonrelativistic quark model using a two-qubit quantum computer, but only when the basis is truncated to four harmonic oscillator states and the oscillator parameter is tuned to minimize truncation error.

## Method

The author encodes a four-state harmonic oscillator basis for charmonium (ηc, J/ψ, hc) into two qubits, rewrites the Hamiltonian matrix as sums of Pauli operator products, and applies variational quantum imaginary time evolution using the Pennylane library's automatic differentiation to compute energy eigenvalues. Transition amplitudes for M1 transitions are calculated using wave function overlap circuits and the swap test, while E1 transition amplitudes are computed using the Hadamard test, with readout error mitigation applied using calibration matrices from the ibmq_manila noise model.

## Key numbers

- Charmonium model parameters: αs = 0.5461, b = 0.1425 GeV², mc = 1.4794 GeV, σ = 1.0946 GeV
- Ground state energy for ¹S₀ channel from truncated Hamiltonian: 0.395 fm⁻¹ (mass 2982 MeV)
- Ground state energy for ³S₁ channel from truncated Hamiltonian: 0.753 fm⁻¹ (mass 3090 MeV)
- Ground state energy for ¹P₁ channel from truncated Hamiltonian: 2.783 fm⁻¹ (mass 3516 MeV)
- Squared M1 transition amplitude 1³S₁→1¹S₀: 0.9939(2) on ideal quantum simulator with 20000 shots
- Squared M1 transition amplitude 2³S₁→1¹S₀: 0.0041(1) on ideal quantum simulator with 20000 shots
- E1 transition amplitude 1¹P₁→1¹S₀: 0.3929(37) fm with 1000 shots, 0.3900(13) fm with 10000 shots
- Readout error mitigation improved 1³S₁→1¹S₀ squared overlap from 0.9385(1) to 0.9813(1)

## Context

This work extends the quarkonium quantum computation of an earlier study (2022) that only considered spin-averaged S-wave states, by including spin-dependent interactions and P-wave calculations, as well as transition amplitudes. The paper uses the quark model parameters of Barnes et al. (2005) and follows the variational quantum imaginary time evolution approach of McArdle (2019). The demonstration enables physicists and instructors to use quantum computing for nuclear physics problems without requiring extensive chemistry background, and provides a pedagogical template for implementing error mitigation strategies on real quantum hardware.
