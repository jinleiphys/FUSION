# Observation of Universal Expansion Anisotropy from Cold Atoms to Hot Quark-Gluon Plasma

> Azimuthal anisotropy has been ubiquitously observed in high-energy proton-proton, proton-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus (heavy-ion) collisions, shaking the early belief that those anisotropies require an intense phase of multiple interactions between the created particles. This work reports a study of anisotropic expansion of cold $^{6}$Li Fermi gases, initially trapped in an anisotropic potential, as a function of the interaction strength that can be readily tuned by an external magnetic field. It is found that the expansion anisotropy builds up quickly at small interaction strength, without the need of a large amount of interactions. An unexpected and quantitative universal scaling of the expansion anisotropy is observed for the first time between cold atom and heavy-ion systems as a function of the number of collisions per particle or opacity ($n_{\rm coll}$), despite their vast differences in scale and physics. The expansion isotropy in both the cold atom gases and heavy-ion collisions increases smoothly and shows no sign of saturation in the observed opacity range, with an approximate power-law dependence of $\sqrt{n_{\rm coll}}$, characteristic of random walks. This universality potentially unifies a variety of vastly different physical systems, from weakly interacting dilute gases to the strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma of the early universe.

## Key claim
The paper claims a universal scaling of expansion anisotropy as a function of opacity (average number of collisions per particle) across cold atom gases and hot quark-gluon plasma, but the universality requires accounting for differences in the definition of the anisotropy parameter and nonlinear eccentricity corrections before the data collapse onto a common curve.

## Method
Cold $^{6}$Li Fermi gases were prepared in an anisotropic optical dipole trap with two different beam crossing angles (10° and 35°), yielding initial aspect ratios of 1/11.4 and 1/3.17 respectively. The interaction strength was tuned via an external magnetic field near a Feshbach resonance at 834 G, and the gas was released from the trap to expand freely. The root-mean-square sizes along axial and transverse directions were extracted from absorption images at multiple expansion times, and the squared velocity components were obtained by fitting the late-time ballistic expansion to extract the anisotropy parameter $v_2$. For heavy-ion collisions, existing data from Au+Au collisions at RHIC and Pb+Pb collisions at LHC were analyzed using the same opacity variable, defined as the average number of collisions per particle traversing the medium.

## Key numbers
- Trapping frequencies: $\omega_r/2\pi = 2137$ Hz (radial), $\omega_z/2\pi = 187$ Hz (axial), giving aspect ratio $\beta = 1/11.4$ for the 10° crossing angle
- Atom number per spin state: $N \approx 5.2 \times 10^5$; temperature $T = 4.6$ $\mu$K; Fermi temperature $T_F = 6.4$ $\mu$K; degeneracy $T/T_F = 0.72$
- For the 35° crossing angle: atom number $\sim 2.4 \times 10^5$ per spin state; degeneracy $T/T_F = 0.69$; aspect ratio $\beta = 1/3.17$
- Eccentricities: $\varepsilon_2 = 0.98$ (10° crossing) and $\varepsilon_2 = 0.82$ (35° crossing)
- At $B = 831$ G near resonance, the aspect ratio reversed at $\sim 0.8$ ms and reached $\beta \approx 1.95$ at 2.0 ms
- The $v_2$ parameter reached values well above 0.5 near the Feshbach resonance at 834 G
- For heavy-ion collisions, the parton-parton cross section used was $\sigma = 3$ mb

## Context
This work extends the observation of anisotropic expansion from heavy-ion collisions, where large elliptic flow was first observed at RHIC (STAR Collaboration, 2005; PHENIX Collaboration, 2004) and later in small systems like proton-proton and proton-nucleus collisions (CMS Collaboration, 2010; ALICE Collaboration, 2012), to cold atom systems where interaction strength can be systematically tuned. The study builds on suggestions by He et al. (2015), Kurkela et al. (2018), and others that intense interactions may not be necessary for anisotropy generation, and on previous cold atom experiments showing anisotropic expansion in strongly interacting Fermi gases (O'Hara et al., 2002) and elliptic flow in few-fermion systems (Brandstetter et al., 2023). The universal scaling as a function of opacity potentially unifies descriptions of anisotropic expansion from weakly interacting dilute gases to the strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma, and enables future studies using cold atoms as emulators for nuclear collision dynamics across the full range of interaction intensities.
