# A non-equilibrium microscopic description of spallation

> We investigate the prompt emission of few intermediate-mass fragments in spallation reactions induced by protons and deuterons in the 1 GeV range. Such emission has a minor contribution to the total reaction cross section, but it may overcome evaporation and fission channels in the formation of light nuclides. The role of mean-field dynamics and phase-space fluctuations in these reactions is investigated through the Boltzmann-Langevin transport equation. We found that a process of frustrated fragmentation and re-aggregation is a prominent mechanism of production of IMFs which can not be assimilated to the statistical decay of a compound nucleus. Very interestingly, this process may yield a small number of IMF in the exit channel, which may even reduce to two, and be wrongly confused with ordinary asymmetric fission. This interpretation, inspired by nuclear-spallation experiments, can be generalised to heavy-ion collisions approaching the fragmentation threshold.

## Key claim

The paper argues that intermediate-mass fragment (IMF) emission in spallation reactions induced by 1 GeV protons and deuterons arises from a process of frustrated fragmentation and re-aggregation, not from statistical decay of a compound nucleus; this mechanism can produce as few as two fragments and be mistaken for asymmetric fission, but it follows a shorter timescale and originates from a low-density spinodal instability rather than from neck formation.

## Method

The authors solved the Boltzmann-Langevin transport equation in full phase space using the BLOB approach, which replaces the average collision integral with a treatment involving extended phase-space agglomerates of test particles to simulate wave packets and introduce nucleon-nucleon correlations stochastically. Initial conditions were prepared by a simplified cascade treatment that evolved momentum space while freezing configuration space until the projectile left the target, producing an excited target nucleus as the starting point for the dynamical evolution. The dynamical transport was coupled to a statistical evaporation model after the last fragment separation (around 400-600 fm/c) to continue the decay until the system cooled completely.

## Key numbers

- The average bound mass loss rate was studied for a $^{208}$Pb target as a function of reduced impact parameter $b_{\textrm red}$ (0: central, 1: peripheral); central-to-intermediate collisions produce a large mass loss with weak dependence on $b_{\textrm red}$.
- The heavy target undergoes isotropic expansion reaching subsaturation densities and excitation energies exceeding 3 MeV per nucleon in the interval from 50 to 100 fm/c.
- Inhomogeneities arise in the bulk and form a mottling pattern at around 200 fm/c; the latest fragments separate in an interval from 400 to 600 fm/c for all systems studied.
- The calculation used a Skyrme-like (SKM*) effective interaction with a soft isoscalar equation of state of compressibility $k=200$ MeV and a free nucleon-nucleon cross section with a cutoff at 100 mb.
- For the $^{136}$Xe$+p$ system at 1$A$GeV, the zero-angle inclusive velocity spectra for $^{13}$C and $^{20}$F showed a combination of concave and convex contributions attributed to binary fission and multifragmentation, respectively.

## Context

This work extends the dynamical description of spallation beyond the traditional Serber (1947) scheme of a fast excitation stage followed by statistical decay, which had been successful for total cross sections but incomplete for IMF production. The authors address experimental contradictions from measurements of $^{136}$Xe$+p$ at 1$A$GeV by Napolitani (2011) and Gorbinet (2012), where event-by-event correlations showed mostly two or three fragments with large mass asymmetry, at variance with traditional multifragmentation scenarios. The BLOB approach, previously developed by Napolitani (2013) for heavy-ion collisions, is here applied to spallation and enables future studies of heavy-ion collisions approaching the fragmentation threshold.
