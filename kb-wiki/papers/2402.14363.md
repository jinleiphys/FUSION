# Bayesian Model Averaging (BMA) for nuclear data evaluation

> To ensure agreement between theoretical calculations and experimental data, parameters to selected nuclear physics models, are perturbed, and fine-tuned in nuclear data evaluations. This approach assumes that the chosen set of models accurately represents the `true' distribution. Furthermore, the models are chosen globally, indicating their applicability across the entire energy range of interest. However, this approach overlooks uncertainties inherent in the models themselves. As a result, achieving satisfactory fits to experimental data within certain energy regions for specific channels becomes challenging, as the evaluation is constrained by the deficiencies of the selected models. In this work, we propose that instead of selecting globally a winning model set and proceeding with it as if it was the `true' model set, we instead, take a weighted average over multiple models within a BMA framework, each weighted by its posterior probability. The method involves executing a set of TALYS calculations by randomly varying multiple nuclear physics models and their parameters to yield a vector of calculated observables. Next, the likelihood function was computed at each considered incident energy point for selected cross sections by comparing the vector of calculated observables with that of the selected differential experimental data. As the cross sections and elastic angular distributions were updated locally on a per-energy-point basis, the approach typically results in discontinuities or "kinks" in the curves, and these were addressed using spline interpolation. The proposed BMA method was applied to the evaluation of proton induced reactions on $^{58}$Ni within 1 - 100 MeV. The results demonstrate favorable comparisons with experimental data, as well as with the TENDL-2021 evaluation.

## Key claim

Instead of selecting a single "winning" model set globally for the entire energy range and treating it as the true model, the authors propose taking a weighted average over multiple nuclear reaction models within a Bayesian Model Averaging (BMA) framework, with weights assigned locally at each incident energy based on the likelihood function, which introduces discontinuities ("kinks") that are subsequently smoothed using spline interpolation.

## Method

The authors generated a set of TALYS calculations by randomly varying 52 different nuclear reaction models (including level density, pre-equilibrium, mass, and gamma-ray strength function models) together with their parameters to produce a vector of calculated observables. The likelihood function was computed at each incident energy point for selected cross sections by comparing the vector of calculated observables with differential experimental data from EXFOR, and these likelihood values were used as weights to compute posterior distributions and weighted means for the quantities of interest (reaction cross sections, residual production cross sections, and elastic angular distributions) on a per-energy-point basis. The resulting discontinuities in the cross section and angular distribution curves were addressed using spline interpolation.

## Key numbers

- The BMA method was applied to proton-induced reactions on $^{58}$Ni within the energy range 1 - 100 MeV.
- A total of 52 different nuclear reaction models in the TALYS code were considered, including 4 JLM optical models, 6 level density models, 4 pre-equilibrium models, 4 mass models, and 8 gamma-ray strength function models.
- For experimental data sets lacking reported uncertainties, a 10% relative uncertainty was assigned to each data point when such data sets were the only experiments available for the considered channel.

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian Monte Carlo (BMC) approach used in TENDL evaluations and the iterative Bayesian Monte Carlo (iBMC) method applied in Alhassan (2022) for p+$^{111}$Cd, which relied exclusively on parameter variation around pre-selected models. The authors demonstrate that varying only model parameters around a single model set (as in traditional approaches) can be insufficient to reproduce experimental data, as shown with $^{59}$Co(p,3n) cross sections, and that different level density models exhibit varying strengths in different energy regions. The BMA approach enables local model selection at each incident energy, providing more flexibility than global model selection methods used in prior work by Koning and Rochman, and could be applied to other nuclear data evaluations where model deficiencies limit the ability to fit experimental data across the full energy range.
