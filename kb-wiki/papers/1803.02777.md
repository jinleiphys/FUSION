# Mapping the sensitivity of hadronic experiments to nucleon structure

> Determinations of the proton's collinear parton distribution functions (PDFs) are emerging with growing precision due to increased experimental activity at facilities like the Large Hadron Collider. While this copious information is valuable, the speed at which it is released makes it difficult to quickly assess its impact on the PDFs, short of performing computationally expensive global fits. As an alternative, we explore new methods for quantifying the potential impact of experimental data on the extraction of proton PDFs. Our approach relies crucially on the Hessian correlation between theory-data residuals and the PDFs themselves, as well as on a newly defined quantity --- the sensitivity --- which represents an extension of the correlation and reflects both PDF-driven and experimental uncertainties. This approach is realized in a new, publicly available analysis package PDFSense, which operates with these statistical measures to identify particularly sensitive experiments, weigh their relative or potential impact on PDFs, and visualize their detailed distributions in a space of the parton momentum fraction $x$ and factorization scale $μ$. This tool offers a new means of understanding the influence of individual measurements in existing fits, as well as a predictive device for directing future fits toward the highest impact data and assumptions. Along the way, many new physics insights can be gained or reinforced. As one of many examples, PDFSense is employed to rank the projected impact of new LHC measurements in jet, vector boson, and $t\bar{t}$ production and leads us to the conclusion that inclusive jet production at the LHC has a potential for playing an indispensable role in future PDF fits. These conclusions are independently verified by preliminarily fitting this experimental information and investigating the constraints they supply using the Lagrange multiplier technique.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a new statistical quantity called the "sensitivity" \(S_f\) that extends the standard Hessian correlation \(C_f\) by incorporating both PDF-driven uncertainties and experimental uncertainties (including correlated systematic errors), enabling assessment of experimental data's impact on PDFs without performing computationally expensive global fits.

## Method

The authors construct normalized residual variations \(\delta_{i,l}^{\pm}\) from the differences between residuals evaluated at eigenvector PDF sets \(\vec{a}_{l}^{\pm}\) and the central PDF set \(\vec{a}_{0}\), normalized by the root-mean-square residual \(\langle r_{0}\rangle_{E}\) for each experiment. These variations are used to define the sensitivity parameter \(S_f\), which is computed for each data point with respect to a chosen PDF-dependent observable (such as the Higgs production cross section). The method is implemented in a publicly available analysis package called PDFSense, which visualizes the sensitivity distributions in the space of parton momentum fraction \(x\) and factorization scale \(\mu\).

- The CTEQ-TEA dataset includes 3287 raw data points from the CT14HERA2 analysis (Tables 1 and 2) augmented by 734 raw data points from newer LHC data (Table 3), mapped to 5227 typical \(\{x,\mu\}\) values.
- For the Higgs production cross section \(\sigma_{H^{0}}(14\,\mathrm{TeV})\), 310 data points with the highest absolute correlation \(|C_f|\) range between 0.42 and 1.0, while 310 data points with the highest sensitivity \(|S_f|\) have \(|S_f| > 0.21\).
- The CT14HERA2 PDF set uses \(N=28\) PDF parameters in the Hessian method.
- The short-distance cross sections for Higgs boson production via gluon fusion are known up to N\(^3\)LO with a scale uncertainty of about 3\%.

## Context

This work extends the Hessian correlation technique introduced by Pumplin (2001) and Nadolsky (2001) for quantifying PDF-mediated correlations between observables, and addresses limitations of existing approaches like Hessian profiling (Camarda, 2015) and Bayesian reweighting (Ball, 2010; Ball, 2011) that depend strongly on the assumed statistical tolerance or reweighting factors. The sensitivity method enables rapid assessment of which experiments most constrain PDF-dependent predictions (such as Higgs production) without performing full global fits, and has been applied to select datasets for the next generation of CTEQ-TEA analysis, to explore physics potential at a future Electron-Ion Collider (Accardi, 2012; Boer, 2011), and to investigate high-energy data's potential for lattice-calculable quantities (Lin, 2017).
