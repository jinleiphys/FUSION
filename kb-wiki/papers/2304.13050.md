# Do we have enough evidence to invalidate the mean-field approximation adopted to model collective neutrino oscillations?

> Recent body of work points out that the mean-field approximation, widely employed to mimic the neutrino field within a neutrino-dense source, might give different results in terms of flavor evolution with respect to the correspondent many-body treatment. In this paper, we investigate whether such conclusions derived within a constrained framework should hold in an astrophysical context. We show that the plane waves, commonly adopted in the many-body literature to model the neutrino field, provide results that are crucially different with respect to the ones obtained using wavepackets of finite size streaming with a non-zero velocity. The many-body approach intrinsically includes coherent and incoherent scatterings. The mean-field approximation, on the other hand, only takes into account the coherent scattering in the absence of the collision term. Even if incoherent scatterings are included in the mean-field approach, the nature of the collision term is different from that in the many-body approach. Because of this, if only a finite number of neutrinos is considered, as often assumed, the two approaches naturally lead to different flavor outcomes. These differences are further exacerbated by vacuum mixing. We conclude that existing many-body literature, based on closed neutrino systems with a finite number of particles, is neither able to rule out nor assess the validity of the mean-field approach adopted to simulate the evolution of the neutrino field in dense astrophysical sources, which are open systems.

## Key claim

The paper argues that existing many-body neutrino simulations, which use closed systems with a small number of particles (O(10–100)) and plane waves, cannot rule out or validate the mean-field approximation for astrophysical sources, because these simulations model intrinsically different physical systems (closed vs. open) and the results depend crucially on the assumption of plane waves rather than finite-size wavepackets.

## Method

The authors compare the flavor evolution predicted by the many-body Schrödinger equation (including both coherent and incoherent scattering) with that from the mean-field approach (coherent scattering only) for simple systems of 1–7 neutrinos confined in a box with periodic boundary conditions. They first use plane waves (stationary, extended over the whole volume) and then repeat the calculation with localized wavepackets of finite size moving at various velocities, tracking the flavor transition probability of a neutrino initially in the νₑ state.

## Key numbers

* For a system of 1 νₑ interacting with N ν_μ (N = 1, 3, 7) with no vacuum mixing, the flavor transition probability follows P¹_{eμ}(t) = [4N/(N+1)²] sin²[((N+1)/2) t], where t is in units of μ₀⁻¹ (μ₀ = √2 G_F (1−cosΘ)/V, with V the normalization volume).
* For 1 νₑ interacting with N ν_x (maximal superposition of νₑ and ν_μ), the probability is P_{eμ}(t) = [N(N+1)/(N+1)²] sin²[((N+1)/2) t].
* In the wavepacket simulations with 3 νₑ and 3 ν_μ (zero velocity), the flavor evolution matches the (1+1) plane-wave case; as velocity increases (in units of L μ₀), the evolution converges to the zero-velocity case but with a self-interaction strength lower by a factor of 3.
* The neutrino density in a core-collapse supernova after decoupling is O(10²⁸) cm⁻³.

## Context

This work extends the debate on the validity of the mean-field approximation for collective neutrino oscillations, which was questioned by many-body studies such as Friedland (2003) and Rrapaj (2019). The authors show that the apparent discrepancies arise because the many-body Hamiltonian inherently includes incoherent scattering (proportional to G_F² N) while the mean-field Hamiltonian includes only coherent forward scattering (proportional to G_F² N²); in the limit N → ∞ the incoherent term becomes negligible. The paper also demonstrates that the plane-wave assumption (common in many-body literature) gives qualitatively different results from finite-size wavepackets, and concludes that future work must model open systems with an infinite number of neutrinos streaming through an interaction volume to properly test the mean-field approximation for astrophysical sources.
