# The impact of dark matter on tidal signatures in neutron star mergers with Einstein Telescope

> If dark matter (DM) accumulates inside neutron stars (NS), it changes their internal structure and causes a shift of the tidal deformability from the value predicted by the dense-matter equation of state (EOS). In principle, this shift could be observable in the gravitational-wave (GW) signal of binary neutron star (BNS) mergers. We investigate the effect of fermionic, non-interacting DM when observing a large number of GW events from DM-admixed BNSs with the precision of the proposed Einstein telescope (ET). Specifically, we study the impact on the recovery of the baryonic EOS and whether DM properties can be constrained. For this purpose, we create event catalogues of BNS mock events with DM fraction up to 1%, from which we reconstruct the posterior uncertainties with the Fisher matrix approach. Using this data, we perform joint Bayesian inference on the baryonic EOS, DM particle mass, and DM particle fraction in each event. Our results reveal that when falsely ignoring DM effects, the EOS posterior is biased towards softer EOSs, though the offset is rather small. Further, we find that within our assumptions of our DM model and population, ET will likely not be able to test the presence of DM in BNSs, even when combining many events and adding Cosmic Explorer (CE) to the next-generation detector network. Likewise, the potential constraints on the DM particle mass will remain weak because of degeneracies with the fraction and EOS.

## Key claim
The paper finds that even with the high precision of the proposed Einstein Telescope, detecting the presence of fermionic non-interacting dark matter in binary neutron star mergers will likely not be possible, and constraints on the dark matter particle mass will remain weak due to degeneracies with the dark matter fraction and the baryonic equation of state.

## Method
The authors created mock event catalogs of 500 binary neutron star mergers with dark matter fractions up to 1%, using the Fisher matrix approach with the gwfast package to approximate posterior uncertainties on tidal deformability that would be recovered by the Einstein Telescope. They performed joint Bayesian inference on the baryonic equation of state, dark matter particle mass, and dark matter particle fraction for each event, using a metamodel to generate 5000 nucleonic equation of state candidates and modeling dark matter as a relativistic Fermi gas of non-interacting spin-one-half particles.

## Key numbers
- Dark matter fraction range: 0.01% to 1% of the total gravitational mass of the neutron star
- Dark matter particle mass grid: 12 values log-uniformly distributed between 170 MeV and 3000 MeV
- 500 mock binary neutron star events per catalog, each with signal-to-noise ratio larger than 100
- Two injected baryonic equations of state: a stiffer one with canonical tidal deformability Lambda_1.4 = 534 and a softer one with Lambda_1.4 = 293
- Bias in the recovered canonical tidal deformability when ignoring dark matter effects: 10 to 20 units
- For uniform dark matter fraction distribution, discrepancy between the analysis without dark matter and the true equation of state becomes noticeable after 50 to 100 events
- For log-uniform dark matter fraction distribution, the 95% credible region excludes the true equation of state only after approximately 150 events or more

## Context
This work extends studies of dark matter effects on neutron star structure (Bertone 2007, Ivanytskyi 2019, Giangrandi 2022) by investigating whether next-generation gravitational-wave detectors can distinguish dark matter contributions from the baryonic equation of state. It builds on previous work showing that current gravitational-wave measurements lack sufficient precision to identify the small tidal deformability offset induced by dark matter, and that next-generation X-ray telescopes face similar limitations (Rutherford 2022). The results indicate that even with the Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer, disentangling dark matter properties from the baryonic equation of state will remain challenging, motivating the need for additional observational channels or different dark matter models.
