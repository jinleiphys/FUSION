# Antisymmetrized, translationally invariant theory of the nucleon optical potential

> Earlier work showed how a nucleon optical model wave function could be defined as a projection of a many-nucleon scattering state within a translationally invariant second quantised many-body theory. In this paper an optical potential operator that generates this optical model wave function is defined through a particular off-shell extension of the elastic transition operator. The theory is express explicitly in terms of the many-nucleon Hamiltonian in a mixed representation in which localised target nucleus states feature. No reference to a mean-field concept is involved in the definition. It is shown that the resulting optical model operator satisfies the requirements of rotational and translational invariance and has standard behaviour under the time reversal transformation. The contributions to the optical potential from two different exchange mechanisms are expressed in terms of an effective Hamiltonian involving a nucleon-number conserving one-body interaction. In the weak-binding limit the method reduces to a a version of Feshbach's projection operator formulation of the optical potential with a truncated nucleon-nucleon potential including exchange terms and recoil corrections. Definitions of the nucleon single-particle Green's function and the corresponding Dyson self-energy modified by corrections for translational invariance are presented and different definitions of the optical potential operator are compared.

# Antisymmetrized, translationally invariant theory of the nucleon optical potential

## Key claim

The paper defines a nucleon optical potential operator that is explicitly translationally invariant and antisymmetrized, without any reference to a mean-field concept, through a particular off-shell extension of the elastic transition operator. The theory is expressed in terms of the many-nucleon Hamiltonian in a mixed representation with localized target nucleus states, and the resulting optical potential satisfies rotational and translational invariance as well as standard time-reversal behavior.

## Method

The optical model wave function is defined as a Fock-space matrix element between a many-nucleon scattering state and a bra describing the target ground state with its center of mass at the origin. The optical potential operator is then defined through a relation with the off-shell elastic transition operator $\hat{\mathcal{T}}_{0,0}(E+i\epsilon)$, given by $\hat{V}^\mathrm{opt} = \hat{\mathcal{T}}_{0,0} - \hat{V}^\mathrm{opt} \hat{g}_0 \hat{\mathcal{T}}_{0,0}$, where $\hat{g}_0$ is the free Green's function in barycentric space. The transition operator is decomposed into contributions from direct and exchange scattering (expressed via a one-body effective interaction $\mathcal{V}$) and heavy-particle stripping terms, and the full theory is recast in an extended barycentric space that includes target intrinsic states.

## Key numbers

- Reduced mass of the nucleon-target system: $\mu_{mA} = \frac{A}{(A+1)}m$, where $m$ is the nucleon mass and $A$ is the target mass number.
- On-shell energy relation: $E = \frac{\hbar^2 k_0^2}{2\mu_{mA}}$, where $k_0$ is the incident nucleon momentum in the overall center-of-mass system.
- Estimate of target center-of-mass displacement during collision: $\frac{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|}{A}$, which decreases like $A^{-2/3}$ for large $A$.
- Kinetic energy of incident nucleon: $\epsilon_{\mathbf{k}_0} = \frac{\hbar k_0^2}{2m}$.

## Context

This work extends the translationally invariant formulation of the nucleon optical model wave function introduced by Johnson (2017) by defining the corresponding optical potential operator. It contrasts with standard many-body approaches that rely on the mass operator from the nucleon single-particle Green's function, as reviewed by Dickhoff and Charity (2018), which typically ignore full translational invariance except for very light targets. The theory bridges the gap between many-body theory and standard nuclear reaction theory, and in the weak-binding limit reduces to a modified version of Feshbach's (1958) projection operator formulation with exchange terms and recoil corrections included. The work shares techniques with Redish and Villars (1970) but does not develop a perturbation theory about a mean field.
