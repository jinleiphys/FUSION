# Thermoelectric effect and Seebeck coefficient for hot and dense hadronic matter

> We investigate the thermoelectric effect for baryon rich plasma produced in heavy ion collision experiments. We estimate the associated Seebeck coefficient for the hadronic matter. Using kinetic theory within relaxation time approximation we calculate the Seebeck coefficient of a hadronic medium with a temperature gradient. The calculation is performed for hadronic matter modeled by hadron resonance gas model with hadrons and resonance states up to a cutoff in the mass as 2.25 GeV. We argue that the thermoelectric current produced by such effect can produce magnetic field in heavy ion collision experiments.

## Key claim

The authors argue that a temperature gradient in baryon-rich hadronic matter produced in heavy ion collisions can generate a thermoelectric current, which in turn can produce a magnetic field; this effect requires a finite baryon chemical potential because at zero baryon density the contributions from particles and antiparticles cancel.

## Method

Using kinetic theory within the relaxation time approximation, the authors calculate the Seebeck coefficient for a multicomponent hadronic system modeled by the hadron resonance gas (HRG) model, including all hadrons and resonances with masses up to a cutoff of 2.25 GeV. The relaxation times for each species are estimated assuming hard-sphere scattering with a uniform radius of 0.3 fm, and the total Seebeck coefficient is obtained as a weighted average of the individual species' Seebeck coefficients weighted by their electrical conductivities.

## Key numbers

- Mass cutoff for hadrons and resonances: 2.25 GeV (baryons up to 2.252 GeV, mesons up to 2.011 GeV)
- Hard-sphere radius for all mesons and baryons: 0.3 fm
- Temperature range studied: 80 MeV to 160 MeV
- Baryon chemical potential values studied: 60 MeV, 80 MeV, 100 MeV, and 150 MeV (also shown up to 250 MeV in figures)
- For a single massless species at zero chemical potential, the Seebeck coefficient is approximately 9 (dimensionless)
- For comparison, copper has a Seebeck coefficient of 1.05 micro-eV/K (approximately 10^-2) and semiconductors have approximately 0.4 meV/K (approximately 4)

## Context

This work extends the study of transport coefficients in heavy-ion collisions, previously focused on shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, thermal conductivity, and electrical conductivity, to the thermoelectric (Seebeck) effect, which has not been studied in this context before. The calculation uses the HRG model, which has been successful in explaining particle ratios at chemical freeze-out (HRG1, HRG2) and has been used previously for transport coefficients by various authors (Moritz Greif, Prakash and Venu, Wiranata and Prakash, Kapusta and Chakraborty, and others). The results are relevant for baryon-rich collisions expected at FAIR and NICA facilities, and the authors suggest the thermoelectric current could generate magnetic fields in heavy ion collision experiments.
