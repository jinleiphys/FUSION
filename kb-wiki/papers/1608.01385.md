# Improving Predictions with Reliable Extrapolation Schemes and Better Understanding of Factorization

> We investigate two distinct sources of uncertainty in low-energy nuclear physics calculations and develop ways to account for them. Harmonic oscillator basis expansions are widely used in ab-initio nuclear structure calculations. Finite computational resources usually require that the basis be truncated before observables are fully converged, necessitating reliable extrapolation schemes. We show that a finite oscillator basis effectively imposes a hard-wall boundary condition. We accurately determine the position of the hard-wall as a function of oscillator space parameters, derive extrapolation formulas for the energy and other observables, and discuss the extension of this approach to higher angular momentum. Nucleon knockout reactions have been widely used to study and understand nuclear properties. Such an analysis implicitly assumes that the effects of the probe can be separated from the physics of the target nucleus. This factorization between nuclear structure and reaction components depends on the renormalization scale and scheme, and has not been well understood. But it is potentially critical for interpreting experiments and for extracting process-independent nuclear properties. We use similarity renormalization group (SRG) transformations to systematically study the scale dependence of factorization for the simplest knockout process of deuteron electrodisintegration. We find that the extent of scale dependence depends strongly on kinematics, but in a systematic way. Based on examination of the relevant overlap matrix elements, we are able to qualitatively explain and even predict the nature of scale dependence based on the kinematics under consideration.

## Key claim
The paper investigates two distinct sources of uncertainty in low-energy nuclear physics: truncation errors in harmonic oscillator basis expansions for ab-initio structure calculations, and the scale dependence of factorization between nuclear structure and reaction components in nucleon knockout reactions. The author shows that a finite oscillator basis effectively imposes a hard-wall boundary condition and that the scale dependence of factorization in deuteron electrodisintegration depends strongly on kinematics in a systematic way, with relatively weak scale dependence at quasi-free kinematics that becomes progressively stronger away from that region.

## Method
For the oscillator basis truncation problem, the author analytically determines the effective hard-wall position as a function of oscillator space parameters and derives infrared extrapolation formulas for energy and other observables, exploiting the duality of the harmonic oscillator to account for ultraviolet cutoff errors. For the factorization study, the author uses similarity renormalization group (SRG) transformations to systematically evolve the nuclear interaction and study the scale dependence of factorization in deuteron electrodisintegration, examining relevant overlap matrix elements to explain and predict the nature of scale dependence based on kinematics.

## Key numbers
- The hard-wall position is determined as a function of oscillator space parameters, enabling infrared extrapolation formulas for energy and other observables
- The AV18 potential is fit to nucleon scattering data up to 350 MeV and to deuteron bound state properties including binding energy, radii, and quadrupole moment
- The AV18 potential achieves chi-squared per degree of freedom approximately 1 for np, nn, and pp scattering

## Context
This work extends the recent demonstration that errors from basis truncation in harmonic oscillator expansions can be understood through infrared and ultraviolet cutoffs induced by a truncated basis, building on the framework of Furnstahl, More, and Papenbrock (2014) and Konig, Bogner, Furnstahl, More, and Papenbrock (2014). The factorization study using SRG transformations on deuteron electrodisintegration, published in More, Konig, Furnstahl, and Hebeler (2015), provides a systematic framework for understanding scale dependence in knockout reactions that is critical for interpreting experiments and extracting process-independent nuclear properties. This enables improved uncertainty quantification in both ab-initio structure calculations and reaction theory.
