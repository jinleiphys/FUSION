# Multicomponent Superfluids and Superconductors in Dense Nuclear and Quark Matter

> Matter at intermediate baryon densities and low temperatures is notoriously hard to tackle theoretically. Whereas lattice methods cannot cover more than rather small densities, perturbative methods are only applicable at much higher densities. The regime of intermediate chemical potential at low temperatures in the QCD-phase diagram is therefore out of reach of first-principle methods, thus we have to rely on stellar objects to investigate dense nuclear and quark matter at low temperatures. Compact stars can serve as an unique laboratory for this regime. Due to their fast rotation and high magnetic field, phenomena like hydrodynamic instabilities and the formation of vortices/flux tubes become of phenomenological interest. In my thesis, I investigate these multicomponent systems in a consistent multi-fluid treatment. By starting from a fieldtheoretical, bosonic model, the phase structure of a two-fluid system, e.g. consisting of superfluid neutrons and superconducting protons, is explored. Consequently, hydrodynamic instabilities, which might serve as trigger for pulsar glitches, are calculated for these two-fluid systems. By incorporating a gauge field and taking into account the charge of one scalar field, the influence of a superfluid on the magnetic phase structure of a superconductor is studied. In the last part, color superconducting quark matter, which can effectively be described as a multicomponent (color-)supercondcutor, is investigated.

## Key claim

This thesis investigates multicomponent superfluids and superconductors in dense nuclear and quark matter using a consistent multi-fluid field-theoretical treatment, but the results are derived from effective bosonic models rather than first-principles QCD, and the applicability to compact stars depends on the validity of these approximations in a regime where lattice methods and perturbative QCD are both unavailable.

## Method

The author starts from a field-theoretical bosonic model with two scalar fields coupled via density and derivative couplings (producing the entrainment effect) to explore the phase structure of a two-fluid system of superfluid neutrons and superconducting protons. Hydrodynamic instabilities are calculated by analyzing sound modes and Goldstone dispersion relations, while critical magnetic fields are derived from a temperature-dependent Ginzburg-Landau potential. For color-superconducting quark matter, a Ginzburg-Landau approach for three massless quark flavors is employed to compute color-magnetic flux tube profiles and identify the type-II regime.

## Key numbers

* Baryon onset of nuclear matter occurs at baryon chemical potential μ_B = 922.7 MeV, derived from nucleon mass m_N = 939 MeV reduced by binding energy E_B = -16.3 MeV
* QCD crossover temperature at low densities is approximately T_QCD = 150 MeV
* Typical neutron star radius R ≈ 12 km with masses M ≈ 1-3 M_⊙ (solar masses), typical value M ≈ 1.4 M_⊙
* Average neutron star density ρ ≈ 7 × 10^14 g cm^{-3} ≈ 2.8 ρ_0, where ρ_0 = 2.5 × 10^14 g cm^{-3} is nuclear ground state density
* Conversion factor: qB = 0.1 GeV^2 corresponds to B = 1.7 × 10^19 G (Gauss)
* Record pulsar rotation period: 1.39 ms for PSR J1748-244ad, with equatorial speed approximately 24% of light speed
* Measured neutron star masses: PSR J1614-2230 at M = 1.97 ± 0.04 M_⊙ and PSR J0348+0432 at M = 2.01 ± 0.03 M_⊙ (both at 68% confidence)
* Surface magnetic fields B ≈ 10^12 G, with magnetars reaching B ≈ 10^15 G and theoretical core stability limit B ≈ 10^18-10^20 G
* Neutron star temperatures after supernova: T ≈ 10^11 K (≈ 10 MeV), cooling to keV range within days

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for multicomponent superfluids in neutron stars previously developed by Schmitt and collaborators, and addresses the regime of intermediate baryon densities inaccessible to lattice QCD (due to the sign problem) and perturbative methods. The results enable predictions for pulsar glitch triggers via hydrodynamic two-stream instabilities, and identify new color-magnetic defects (including a preferred flux tube configuration in CFL quark matter and magnetic domain walls in the 2SC phase) that could produce detectable gravitational waves from compact star deformations, building on prior work by Alford (1997) on color-flavor locking and by Duncan and Thompson (1992) on magnetars.
