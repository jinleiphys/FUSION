# The calorimetric spectrum of the electron-capture decay of $^{163}$Ho. The spectral endpoint region

> The electron-neutrino mass (or masses and mixing angles) may be directly measurable in weak electron-capture decays. The favoured experimental technique is "calorimetric". The optimal nuclide is $^{163}$Ho, and several experiments (ECHo, HOLMES and NuMECS) are currently studying its decay. The most relevant range of the calorimetric-energy spectrum extends for the last few hundred eV below its endpoint. It has not yet been well measured. We explore the theory, mainly in the cited range, of electron capture in $^{163}$Ho decay. A so far neglected process turns out to be most relevant: electron-capture accompanied by the shake-off of a second electron. Our two main conclusions are very encouraging: the counting rate close to the endpoint may be more than an order of magnitude larger than previously expected; the "pile-up" problem may be significantly reduced.

## Key claim

The counting rate in the endpoint region of the calorimetric spectrum of 163Ho electron-capture decay may be more than an order of magnitude larger than previously expected due to the previously neglected process of electron-capture accompanied by shake-off of a second electron, provided the theoretical predictions for these two-hole contributions are correct.

## Method

The authors extended a simple theoretical framework for electron-capture decay in 163Ho, originally developed for single-hole final states, to include two-hole final states produced by shake-up and shake-off processes. They applied the sudden approximation and the method of Intemann and Pollock to compute the shape of shake-off contributions using non-relativistic Coulomb wave functions with effective nuclear charges chosen to reproduce Hartree-Fock mean radii, and compared their predictions to preliminary data from the ECHo and NuMECS experiments in the N-region of the spectrum.

## Key numbers

- The Q-value for 163Ho decay is Q = 2833 eV (with statistical uncertainty 30 eV and systematic uncertainty 15 eV), as recently measured.
- The two-hole contributions at Ec near Q are a factor of approximately 40 larger than the single-hole contribution at the same energy.
- In the pessimistic scenario where all double-hole contributions are divided by 40, the quadratic fit to the Kurie plot yields Q = 2833.65 eV, correct to 2.3 parts in 10^4 relative to the input Q = 2833 eV.
- For the optimistic scenario, the linear fit to the Kurie plot gives Q = 2827.56 eV, wrong by about 0.19 percent relative to the true Q = 2833 eV, while the quadratic fit gives Q = 2833.14 eV, correct to 5 parts in 10^5.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical treatment of 163Ho electron-capture decay by Robertson (2015) who first pointed out that two-hole contributions should not be negligible, and by Faessler et al. (2015) who computed one-, two-, and three-hole contributions to the calorimetric spectrum. The authors find that the predictions of Faessler et al. for the normalization of various spectral features appear untrustworthy when compared to preliminary ECHo and NuMECS data, and that the shake-off contributions (which Faessler et al. did not compute) may dominate the endpoint region. The results suggest that the pile-up problem may be significantly reduced and that a quadratic fit with one extra parameter could allow extraction of the neutrino mass even if the two-hole contributions are not perfectly understood theoretically.
