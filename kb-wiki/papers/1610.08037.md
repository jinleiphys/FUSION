# On wounded constituents in nuclear collisions

> In this talk we summarize the main results of our recent paper, Phys. Rev. C94, 014902, where we explore predictions of the wounded quark model for particle production and the properties of the initial state formed in ultra-relativistic collisions of atomic nuclei.

# On wounded constituents in nuclear collisions

## Key claim
The wounded quark model, where subnucleonic degrees of freedom are taken as the relevant sources of particle production, yields an approximately linear scaling of charged particle multiplicity with the number of wounded constituents across a wide range of collision systems and energies; however, at RHIC energies the ratio for p+p collisions is about 30% higher than the band for A+A collisions, indicating that the scaling is not perfectly universal.

## Method
The authors used the GLISSANDO Monte Carlo code, modified to simulate wounded constituents (quarks) rather than wounded nucleons, to compute the number of wounded constituents \(Q_{\rm W}\) for various collision systems. They compared the ratio \(dN_{\rm ch}/d\eta / Q_{\rm W}\) against experimental data from RHIC and LHC for A+A and p+p collisions, and also computed fireball eccentricities in p+p collisions using Gaussian smearing of entropy density from individual sources.

## Key numbers
- At LHC energies, the optimal number of constituents per nucleon is three.
- At RHIC energies, the p+p point for \(dN_{\rm ch}/d\eta / Q_{\rm W}\) is about 30% higher than the band for A+A collisions.
- For p+p collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=7\) TeV, both ellipticity \(\varepsilon_2\) and triangularity \(\varepsilon_3\) are large and do not depend strongly on multiplicity.

## Context
This work extends the wounded quark model originally proposed by Bialas (1977) and Eremin (2003) for RHIC data, and applies it to LHC energies and to p+p collisions. It contrasts with the standard wounded nucleon model of Bialas (1976), which requires an additional binary collision component to describe centrality dependence. The results enable the study of collectivity in small systems (p+p collisions) using the same framework as for larger A+A collisions, as pursued by Bozek (2011-2013).
