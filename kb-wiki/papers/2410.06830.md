# Magnetic coupling through flux branching of adjacent type-I and -II superconductors in a neutron star

> The inner and outer cores of neutron stars are believed to contain type-I and -II proton superconductors, respectively. The type-I superconductor exists in an intermediate state, comprising macroscopic flux-free and flux-containing regions, while the type-II superconductor is flux-free, except for microscopic, quantized flux tubes. Here, we show that the inner and outer cores are coupled magnetically, when the macroscopic flux tubes subdivide dendritically into quantized flux tubes, a phenomenon called flux branching. An important implication is that up to $\sim 10^{12} (r_1/10^6 \, {\rm cm}) \, {\rm erg}$ of energy are required to separate a quantized flux tube from its progenitor macroscopic flux tube, where $r_1$ is the length of the macroscopic flux tube. Approximating the normal-superconducting boundary as sharp, we calculate the magnetic coupling energy between a quantized and macroscopic flux tube due to flux branching as a function of, $f_1$, the radius of the type-I inner core divided by the radius of the type-II outer core. Strong coupling delays magnetic field decay in the type-II superconductor. For an idealised inner core containing only a type-I proton superconductor and poloidal flux, and in the absence of ambipolar diffusion and diamagnetic screening, the low magnetic moments ($\lesssim 10^{27} \, {\rm G \, cm^3}$) of recycled pulsars imply $f_1 \lesssim 10^{-1.5}$.

## Key claim

The inner and outer cores of neutron stars are magnetically coupled when macroscopic flux tubes in the type-I superconductor subdivide dendritically into quantized flux tubes in the type-II superconductor, a process called flux branching. This coupling is energetically favorable under a wide range of plausible parameters, but only when the ratio of the type-I inner core radius to the type-II outer core radius falls within specific ranges that depend on the size of the macroscopic flux tubes.

## Method

The authors apply the sharp-interface model of Landau (1943) and Andrew (1948) to calculate the surplus free energy of a flux tree consisting of concatenated branching cells, where a macroscopic normal region (trunk) branches into four smaller normal regions per level. They compare this free energy to the free energy of alternative configurations without branching, where a fixed number of rectilinear normal regions of uniform radius thread both superconductors. The free energy is minimized analytically for a linear profile of the scaled surface energy across the core, with branching assumed to occur predominantly near the type-I-II interface.

- Up to approximately 10^12 (r_1/10^6 cm) erg of energy are required to separate a quantized flux tube from its progenitor macroscopic flux tube, where r_1 is the length of the macroscopic flux tube.
- For an idealized inner core with only a type-I proton superconductor and poloidal flux, and in the absence of ambipolar diffusion and diamagnetic screening, the low magnetic moments (less than or equal to 10^27 G cm^3) of recycled pulsars imply f_1 less than or equal to 10^{-1.5}, where f_1 is the radius of the type-I inner core divided by the radius of the type-II outer core.

## Context

This work extends the flux branching formalism developed by Landau (1943) and Andrew (1948) for type-I superconductors in uniform applied magnetic fields to the astrophysical context of adjacent type-I and type-II proton superconductors in neutron star cores. It addresses the open question of how magnetic field lines connect across the type-I-II interface, which was previously studied by Haber (2017) and Wood (2022) in the context of type-1.5 superconductivity mediated by neutron superfluid interactions. The results enable quantitative predictions for magnetic field decay timescales in neutron stars and observational constraints on the size of the type-I superconducting inner core from recycled pulsar magnetic moments.
