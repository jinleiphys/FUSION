# Large-Scale Calculations of $β$-Decay Rates and Implications for $r$-Process Nucleosynthesis

> Nuclear $β$ decay is a key element of the astrophysical rapid neutron capture process ($r$-process). In this paper, we present state-of-the-art global $β$-decay calculations based on the quantified relativistic nuclear energy density functional theory and the deformed proton-neutron quasiparticle random-phase approximation. Our analysis considers contributions from allowed and first-forbidden transitions. We used two point-coupling functionals with carefully calibrated time-odd terms and isoscalar pairing strength. The new calculations display consistent results for both employed functionals, especially near the neutron drip line, suggesting slower $β$ decays past the $N=126$ neutron shell closure than in commonly used $β$-decay models. The new rates, along with the existing rates based on the latest non-relativistic calculations, are found to slow down the synthesis of heavy elements in the $r$-process and significantly reduce the contribution of neutron-induced fission.

# Large-Scale Calculations of β-Decay Rates and Implications for r-Process Nucleosynthesis

## Key claim

The new relativistic energy density functional calculations predict slower β decays past the N=126 neutron shell closure than commonly used β-decay models, but this conclusion is conditional on the choice of functional: the two employed point-coupling functionals (DD-PC1 and DD-PCX) show consistent results with each other and with the Skyrme SkO' model, particularly near the neutron drip line, while discrepancies with the spherical D3C* model and the phenomenological FRDM model are more pronounced.

## Method

The authors performed global β-decay half-life calculations for 4598 nuclides between Z=20 and Z=110 using relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory with axially-deformed, reflection-invariant nuclear shapes, combined with the deformed proton-neutron quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA) linear response framework. Two point-coupling energy density functionals (DD-PC1 and DD-PCX) were employed with calibrated time-odd terms and isoscalar pairing strength, including both allowed Gamow-Teller (1+) and first-forbidden (0-, 1-, and 2-) transitions, with odd-A and odd-odd nuclei treated by self-consistent quasiparticle blocking within the equal-filling approximation.

## Key numbers

- The calculations cover 4598 individual nuclides between Z=20 and Z=110, starting near the valley of stability and ending at the two-neutron drip line.
- For DD-PCX, the mean relative error in half-lives is under 5% at T1/2^exp,max = 10^4 s, with a maximum discrepancy of 20% for nuclei with shorter experimental half-lives; for DD-PC1 the maximum discrepancy is 32%.
- The mean Qβ residual for DD-PC1 is μ = -0.300 MeV (underestimation), while for DD-PCX it is μ = 0.157 MeV (overestimation), with 1σ deviation not exceeding 1 MeV across 959 nuclei with experimental data.
- Approximately 30 nuclei for each functional required manual correction for negative strength contributions from β+ mixing in odd-A and odd-odd nuclei.
- The super-allowed decay constant used was K = 6137 s, with the contour integration discretized using a 30-point Gauss-Laguerre quadrature and a 20-point Chebyshev grid for electron energy integration.

## Context

This work extends the global β-decay calculations of Ney (2020) using the Skyrme SkO' functional with deformed QRPA, and the spherical relativistic matrix-QRPA calculations of Marketin (2016) with the D3C* functional, while also comparing to the phenomenological FRDM+QRPA tables of Moller (2003, 2019). The new rates, combined with existing non-relativistic calculations, slow down heavy element synthesis in r-process simulations and significantly reduce the contribution of neutron-induced fission, suggesting that β-delayed fission may be comparable to neutron-induced fission in some scenarios. This enables more consistent evaluations of nuclear physics input for r-process nucleosynthesis with controlled extrapolation, particularly for nuclei beyond current experimental reach.
