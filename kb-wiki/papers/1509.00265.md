# Electric dipole polarizability: from few- to many-body systems

> We review the Lorentz integral transform coupled-cluster method for the calculation of the electric dipole polarizability. We benchmark our results with exact hyperspherical harmonics calculations for 4He and then we move to a heavier nucleus studying 16O. We observe that the implemented chiral nucleon-nucleon interaction at next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order underestimates the electric dipole polarizability.

## Key claim

The Lorentz integral transform coupled-cluster method truncated to singles-and-doubles excitations (LIT-CCSD) can reliably calculate the electric dipole polarizability from few- to many-body nuclei, but the chiral nucleon-nucleon interaction at next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order systematically underestimates the experimental value in heavier systems such as 16O.

## Method

The authors compute the electric dipole polarizability via a continued fraction of Lanczos coefficients derived from the Lorentz integral transform coupled-cluster formalism, avoiding uncertainties from inversion of the transform. They benchmark the method against exact hyperspherical harmonics calculations for 4He, then apply it to 16O using a chiral nucleon-nucleon interaction at next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order, truncating the cluster expansion at singles-and-doubles excitations (LIT-CCSD).

## Key numbers

- 4He: converged LIT-CCSD polarizability of 0.0815 fm^3, compared to EIHH result of 0.0831 fm^3 and no-core-shell-model result of 0.0822(5) fm^3 using the same interaction
- 4He: with 15 Lanczos coefficients the error relative to the converged value is 0.3%
- 16O: converged LIT-CCSD polarizability of 0.461 fm^3, compared to experimental value of 0.585(9) fm^3 from Ahrens (1975)

## Context

This work extends the Lorentz integral transform coupled-cluster method previously applied to response functions (Bacca 2013, LITCC) to direct calculation of the electric dipole polarizability in medium-mass nuclei. The benchmark in 4He agrees with exact hyperspherical harmonics calculations (Barnea 2000, Goerke 2012) and no-core-shell-model results (Stetcu 2009). The discrepancy in 16O points to the inadequacy of two-body chiral interactions for this observable, motivating the inclusion of three-nucleon forces as developed by Ekstroem (2015) to improve agreement with experiment.
