# Description of Induced Nuclear Fission with Skyrme Energy Functionals: II. Finite Temperature Effects

> Understanding the mechanisms of induced nuclear fission for a broad range of neutron energies could help resolve fundamental science issues, such as the formation of elements in the universe, but could have also a large impact on societal applications in energy production or nuclear waste management. The goal of this paper is to set up the foundations of a microscopic theory to study the static aspects of induced fission as a function of the excitation energy of the incident neutron, from thermal to fast neutrons. To account for the high excitation energy of the compound nucleus, we employ a statistical approach based on finite-temperature nuclear density functional theory with Skyrme energy densities, which we benchmark on the 239 Pu(n,f) reaction. We compute the evolution of the least-energy fission pathway across multidimensional potential energy surfaces with up to five collective variables as a function of the nuclear temperature, and predict the evolution of both the inner and outer fission barriers as a function of the excitation energy of the compound nucleus. We show that the coupling to the continuum induced by the finite temperature is negligible in the range of neutron energies relevant for many applications of neutron-induced fission. We prove that the concept of quantum localization introduced recently can be extended to T > 0, and we apply the method to study the interaction energy and total kinetic energy of fission fragments as a function of the temperature for the most probable fission. While large uncertainties in theoretical modeling remain, we conclude that finite-temperature nuclear density functional may provide a useful framework to obtain accurate predictions of fission fragment properties.

## Key claim

The paper establishes that finite-temperature nuclear density functional theory (FT-DFT) with Skyrme energy densities can provide a useful framework for predicting fission fragment properties in induced fission, but only under the assumption that the temperature must be constant across the potential energy surface, and with the caveat that large uncertainties in theoretical modeling remain.

## Method

The authors performed finite-temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (FT-HFB) calculations using the SkM* parametrization of the Skyrme pseudopotential, with a density-dependent mixed surface-volume pairing force fitted on the 3-point formula of the odd-even mass difference in 240Pu. They computed constrained HFB solutions across multidimensional potential energy surfaces with up to five collective variables (axial quadrupole, triaxial quadrupole, axial octupole, axial hexadecapole, and neck operator) for the 239Pu(n,f) reaction, using the DFT solvers HFODD and HFBTHO with a basis of 1100 states and maximum oscillator shell Nmax = 31.

## Key numbers

- First fission barrier height for 240Pu with SkM* functional: EA = 7.65 MeV
- One-neutron separation energy for 240Pu: S(N) = 7.09 MeV
- Temperature range studied: 0 to 1.75 MeV, corresponding to maximal excitation energies of about E* ≈ 80 MeV for the compound nucleus
- Maximum neutron kinetic energies corresponding to the first barrier: En ≈ 73 MeV
- Cut-off energy for quasi-particles in density calculations: Ecut = 60 MeV
- Range parameter for neck operator: aN = 1.0 fm

## Context

This work extends the zero-temperature DFT study of 239Pu(n,f) fission presented in the authors' previous paper (Schunck 2014) to include finite-temperature effects, building on earlier FT-HFB applications by McDonnell (2013), Sheikh (2009), and Pei (2009). The paper establishes that the concept of quantum localization can be extended to T > 0 and that coupling to the continuum induced by finite temperature is negligible for neutron energies relevant to applications, enabling future microscopic calculations of fission fragment properties as functions of incident neutron energy.
