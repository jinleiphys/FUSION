# Generalized Fluid Models of the Braginskii Type. Part 2. The Boltzmann Operator

> In our previous paper (Hunana et al. 2022) we have employed the Landau collisional operator together with the moment method of Grad and considered various generalizations of the Braginskii model, such as a multi-fluid formulation of the 21- and 22-moment models valid for general masses and temperatures, where all of the considered moments are described by their evolution equations (with fully non-linear left-hand-sides). Here we consider the same models, however, we employ the Boltzmann operator and calculate the collisional contributions via expressing them through the Chapman-Cowling collisional integrals. These ``integrals'' just represent a useful mathematical technique/notation introduced roughly 100 years ago, which (in the usual semi-linear approximation) allows one to postpone specifying the particular collisional process and finish all of the calculations with the Boltzmann operator. We thus consider multi-fluid 21- and 22-moment models which are valid for a large class of elastic collisional processes describable by the Boltzmann operator. Reduction into the 13-moment approximation recovers the models of Schunk and Burgers. We only focus on the particular cases of hard spheres, Coulomb collisions, purely repulsive inverse power force $|K|/r^ν$ and attractive force $-|K|/r^ν$ with repulsive rigid core (or potential $V(r)=δ(r)- |c|/r^n$, so that the particles bounce from each other when they meet), but other cases can be found in the literature. In the Appendix, we introduce the Boltzmann operator in a way suitable for newcomers and we discuss a surprisingly simple recipe how to calculate the collisional contributions with analytic software.

## Key claim

This paper extends the multi-fluid 21- and 22-moment models previously developed with the Landau collisional operator (Hunana et al. 2022) to the Boltzmann collisional operator, making them valid for a large class of elastic collisional processes describable by the Boltzmann operator, not just Coulomb collisions in fully ionized weakly-coupled plasmas.

## Method

The author employs the moment method of Grad to construct multi-fluid 21- and 22-moment models, expressing all collisional contributions through Chapman-Cowling collisional integrals Ω(l,j)ab. These integrals allow postponing specification of the particular collisional process until after the general fluid model is derived. The author then evaluates the Chapman-Cowling integrals for specific cases: hard spheres, Coulomb collisions (with corrections to the Coulomb logarithm), purely repulsive inverse power forces |K|/rν, and attractive forces −|K|/rν with a repulsive rigid core (potential V(r)=δ(r)−|c|/rn). The models are valid for arbitrary masses and temperatures of all species, with the restriction that differences in bulk velocities between species must be sufficiently smaller than their thermal velocities.

## Key numbers

- The Landau operator yields 2lnΛ for the Coulomb logarithm, whereas the Boltzmann operator yields the more precise ln(Λ2+1), allowing extension to moderately-coupled plasmas (2 ≤ lnΛ ≤ 10)
- For hard spheres, the differential cross-section is σab(gab,θ) = (ra+rb)2/4, a constant independent of relative velocity and scattering angle
- The repulsive force case ν=2 corresponds to Coulomb collisions, ν=5 to Maxwell molecules, and ν=∞ to hard spheres
- The attractive case ν=3 (or n=2) is reproduced in the paper, and numerical integrals are verified for attractive Maxwell molecules ν=5 and London force ν=7

## Context

This work extends the multi-fluid 21- and 22-moment models from Hunana et al. (2022) (Part 1), which used the Landau collisional operator and were valid only for fully ionized weakly-coupled plasmas. By employing the Boltzmann operator, the models now apply to partially ionized plasmas where neutral particles are present and multiple collisional interactions must be considered. Reduction to the 13-moment approximation recovers the models of Schunk (1975, 1977) and Burgers (1969). The formulation through Chapman-Cowling integrals enables future application to other collisional processes by simply providing the appropriate integrals, without recalculating the underlying fluid model.
