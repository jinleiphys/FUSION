# Understanding experimentally-observed fluctuations

> We discuss two topics on the experimental measurements of fluctuation observables in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. First, we discuss the effects of the thermal blurring, i.e. the blurring effect arising from the experimental measurement of fluctuations in momentum space as a proxy of the thermal fluctuations defined in coordinate space, on higher order cumulants. Second, we discuss the effect of imperfect efficiency of detectors on the measurement of higher order cumulants. We derive effective formulas which can carry out the correction of this effect for higher order cumulants based on the binomial model.

# Understanding experimentally-observed fluctuations

## Key claim
The experimentally-observed fluctuations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions are not identical to thermal fluctuations defined in statistical mechanics due to differences in phase space (thermal blurring) and detector inefficiency; the paper derives practical correction formulas that allow comparison between these different fluctuations, but the thermal blurring effect must be considered seriously when comparing event-by-event fluctuations at rapidity windows comparable to the thermal width.

## Method
The authors analyze two distinct effects that distort experimental fluctuation measurements relative to theoretical thermal fluctuations. First, they examine thermal blurring arising from the mismatch between momentum-space rapidity (measured by detectors) and coordinate-space rapidity (used in statistical mechanics), using a blastwave model with temperature T and transverse velocity β to compute particle number distributions per unit rapidity. Second, they derive new efficiency correction formulas based on the binomial model that express cumulants of the original particle number Q in terms of mixed cumulants of observed particle numbers, with coefficients depending on particle charges a_i and detector efficiencies ε_i.

## Key numbers
- For pions at kinetic freezeout (T ≈ 100 MeV, β = 0.6–0.7), the thermal width in rapidity space is as large as 1.0, which is comparable with the maximum coverage of the STAR detector Δy = 1.0
- For nucleons at kinetic freezeout, w = m/T ≈ 9, while for pions w ≈ 1.5
- The new efficiency correction formulas reduce numerical cost from ~M^n (proportional to M to the power n) to proportional to M for all orders of cumulants, where M is the number of different particle species and n is the cumulant order

## Context
This work extends earlier studies on fluctuation observables in relativistic heavy-ion collisions by Asakawa (2000) and Jeon (2000), who recognized the approximate correspondence between momentum-space and coordinate-space fluctuations under Bjorken boost-invariant evolution. The thermal blurring analysis builds on Ohnishi (2016) and Kitazawa (2015), who discussed that this effect gives characteristic Δy dependence of higher-order cumulants and can be regarded as part of diffusion before kinetic freezeout. The efficiency correction formulas improve upon prior methods by Luo (2014) and Bzdak (2013), which used factorial moments with numerical cost scaling as M^n, enabling practical corrections with momentum-dependent efficiency in experimental analyses.
