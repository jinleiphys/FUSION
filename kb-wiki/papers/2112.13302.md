# Cooling of Isolated Neutron Stars with Pion Condensation: Possible Fast Cooling in a Low-Symmetry-Energy Model

> We studied thermal evolution of isolated neutron stars (NSs) including the pion condensation core, with an emphasis on the stiffness of equation of state (EOS). Many temperature observations can be explained by the minimal cooling scenario which excludes the fast neutrino cooling process. However, several NSs are cold enough to require it. The most crucial problem for NS cooling theory is whether the nucleon direct Urca (DU) process is open. The DU process is forbidden if the nucleon symmetry energy is significantly low. Hence, another fast cooling process is required in such an EOS. As the candidate to solve this problem, we consider the pion condensation. We show that the low-symmetry-energy model can account for most cooling observations including cold NSs, with strong neutron superfluidity. Simultaneously, it holds the $2~M_{\odot}$ observations even if the pion condensation core exists. Thus, we propose the possibility of pion condensation, as an exotic state to solve the problem in low-symmetry-energy EOSs. We examined the consistency of our EOSs with other various observations as well.

## Key claim

The authors propose that pion condensation can serve as an alternative fast cooling mechanism in isolated neutron stars when the nucleon direct Urca process is forbidden due to a low symmetry energy, provided that the underlying equation of state is stiff enough at high densities to still support a 2 solar mass neutron star.

## Method

The authors constructed equations of state by adding the energy density and pressure gains from pion condensation (using a SU(2) chiral symmetry model with Landau-Migdal parameter g'=0.5) to three standard-nuclear-matter EOSs: TOGASHI (L=30.0 MeV), TM1e (L=40 MeV), and TM1 (L=111.1 MeV). They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain mass-radius relations and then computed cooling curves using a spherically symmetric relativistic stellar evolutionary code, incorporating slow cooling processes, the nucleon direct Urca process, the pion Urca process, and nucleon superfluidity with several pairing models.

## Key numbers

- TOGASHI+pi maximum mass: 2.09 solar masses, consistent with the 2.14 solar mass measurement of PSR J0740+6620
- TOGASHI+pi radius for 1.4 solar mass: 10.97 km
- TM1e+pi maximum mass: 1.88 solar masses, inconsistent with the 2 solar mass constraint
- TM1+pi maximum mass: 1.97 solar masses, inconsistent with the 2 solar mass constraint
- Charged pion condensation phase appears at baryon density approximately 1.6 times nuclear saturation density (nuclear saturation density is 2.66 x 10^14 g/cm^3 for TOGASHI)
- The threshold mass for direct Urca in TOGASHI is greater than the maximum mass of 2.21 solar masses, meaning the DU process is forbidden for all masses
- Pion Urca neutrino emissivity is around 10^24-25 T_9^6 erg cm^-3 s^-1, where T_9 is temperature in units of 10^9 K

## Context

This work extends the minimal cooling scenario of Page (2004, 2009) which explains most isolated neutron star observations but fails to account for cold objects like PSR J0205+6449 in 3C58 and RX J0007.0+7302. The authors build on their previous study (Dohi 2019) which showed that the low-symmetry-energy TOGASHI EOS cannot explain cold neutron stars without exotic matter. The paper enables future tests of pion condensation models against upcoming NICER observations and pulsar glitch data, while showing that the hyperon puzzle (the difficulty of supporting 2 solar mass stars with exotic matter) can be avoided if the standard-nuclear EOS is sufficiently stiff at high densities.
