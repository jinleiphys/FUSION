# Hadronic Multi-Particle Final State Measurements with CLAS at Jefferson Lab

> Precision measurements in the neutrino sector are becoming increasingly feasible due to the development of relatively high-rate experimental capabilities. These important developments command renewed attention to the systematic corrections needed to interpret the data. Hadronic multi-particle final state measurements made using CLAS at Jefferson Lab, together with a broad theoretical effort that links electro-nucleus and neutrino-nucleus data, will address this problem, and will elucidate long-standing problems in intermediate energy nuclear physics. This new work will ultimately enable precision determinations of fundamental quantities such as the neutrino mixing matrix elements in detailed studies of neutrino oscillations.

# Hadronic Multi-Particle Final State Measurements with CLAS at Jefferson Lab

## Key claim

Multi-hadron final state measurements from CLAS at Jefferson Lab, combined with a theoretical framework linking electro-nucleus and neutrino-nucleus data, can reduce systematic uncertainties in neutrino oscillation experiments and enable precision determinations of neutrino mixing matrix elements, provided that models describing elementary reactions on protons and neutrons are developed or adapted to describe both electron beam and neutrino beam interactions simultaneously.

## Method

The CLAS spectrometer in Hall B at Jefferson Lab was used to detect multiple final-state charged and neutral hadrons over a wide range of angles and momenta from electron beam interactions with various targets (hydrogen, deuterium, helium-3, helium-4, carbon, iron, lead, and polarized ammonia). The detector employed a toroidal magnetic field with six sectors, drift chambers for tracking, time-of-flight counters for hadron identification, Cerenkov counters and electromagnetic calorimeters for electron identification, and calorimeters for neutron and neutral pion detection. Data were acquired at beam energies of 0.5-5.7 GeV at a design luminosity of 10^34 cm^-2 s^-1, with a trigger selecting scattered electron events at 3-4 kilohertz and data transfer rates exceeding 20 megabytes per second.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy range: 0.5-5.7 GeV from CEBAF, with a sample shown at 4.232 GeV
- Design luminosity: 10^34 cm^-2 s^-1
- Trigger rate: 3-4 kilohertz for scattered electron events
- Data transfer rate: exceeds 20 megabytes per second (approximately 1 terabyte per day)
- Annual trigger collection: 10 to 30 billion triggers
- Missing mass resolution: 10-20 MeV for identifying unmeasured hadrons
- Drift chamber angular coverage: 8 to 142 degrees in scattering angle
- Forward electron identification angular range: 8 to 45 degrees
- Lower practical Q^2 limit: approximately 0.1 GeV^2
- Resonance mass range visible in data: 1.2-2.2 GeV
- Neutrino energy range for maximal oscillation probability in planned experiments: 0.5-3.0 GeV

## Context

This work extends the long tradition of electron scattering experiments (from the 1950s discovery that protons are not pointlike, through deep inelastic scattering scaling, to form factor and spin structure measurements) by moving beyond inclusive measurements to multi-hadron final state detection. The paper addresses the need for improved systematic corrections in high-rate, long-baseline neutrino oscillation experiments (planned in the U.S. and Japan) that use nuclear targets, where effects such as Fermi motion, Pauli blocking, coherent pion production, hadron absorption, and hadron formation lengths must be understood. The approach enables a cross-disciplinary study linking electron and neutrino scattering data, with the potential to improve understanding of both neutrino physics and intermediate energy nuclear physics, including determination of hadron formation lengths (a subject of one CLAS program involving nuclear targets).
