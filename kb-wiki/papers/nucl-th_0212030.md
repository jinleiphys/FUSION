# Perspectives of Nuclear Physics

> The organizers of this meeting have asked me to present perspectives of nuclear physics. This means to identify the areas where nuclear physics will be expanding in the next future. In six chapters a short overview of these areas will be given, where I expect that nuclear physics willdevelop quite fast: A. Quantum Chromodynamics and effective field theories in the confinement region; B. Nuclear structure at the limits; C. High energy heavy ion collisions; D. Nuclear astrophysics; E. Neutrino physics; F. Test of physics beyond the standard model by rare processes. After a survey over these six points I will pick out a few topics where I will go more in details. There is no time to give for all six points detailed examples. I shall discuss the following examples of the six topics mentionned above: 1. The perturbative chiral quark model and the nucleon $Σ$-term, 2. VAMPIR (Variation After Mean field Projection In Realistic model spaces and with realistic forces) as an example of the nuclear structure renaissance, 3. Measurement of important astrophysical nuclear reactions in the Gamow peak, 4. The solar neutrino problem. As examples for testing new physics beyond the standard model by rare processes I had prepared to speak about the measurement of the electric neutron dipole moment and of the neutrinoless double beta decay. But the time is limited and so I have to skip these points, although they are extremely interesting.

## Key claim

This paper presents a personal perspective on six areas where nuclear physics is expected to expand rapidly, but the author explicitly notes that time constraints forced the omission of detailed discussion on two planned topics (the electric neutron dipole moment and neutrinoless double beta decay), despite their acknowledged extreme interest.

## Method

The author surveys six broad frontiers: QCD and effective field theories in the confinement region, nuclear structure at the limits, high-energy heavy-ion collisions, nuclear astrophysics, neutrino physics, and tests of physics beyond the standard model via rare processes. From these six areas, four specific examples are selected for more detailed treatment: the perturbative chiral quark model and the nucleon Σ-term, the VAMPIR approach for nuclear structure, measurement of astrophysical reactions in the Gamow peak, and the solar neutrino problem.

## Key numbers

- The nucleon Σ-term is defined via the relation σ^π_N ≡ m̂ ⟨p| ū u + d̄ d |p⟩, where the average light-quark mass m̂ = 7 MeV and the strange-quark mass m̂_s = 175 MeV.
- The Gell-Mann–Oakes–Renner relation gives B ≡ -⟨0| ū u |0⟩ = m^2_π / (2 m̂) = 1.4 GeV.
- The axial coupling constant g_A = 1.25 is used to fit the model parameter ρ via g_A = (5/3)[1 - 2ρ²/(1 + 3ρ²/2)].
- The mean squared charge radius of the nucleon's quark content is ⟨r²_charge⟩ = 0.6 ± 0.1 fm².
- The condition for inverse beta decay in core-collapse supernovae is m_e c² + ε_F ≥ (m_n - m_p)c² = 1.293 MeV.
- The inner Fe/Ni core density at which neutrinos become trapped is about 10¹² g/cm³.
- The upper limit on the neutron electric dipole moment is d_n ≲ 10⁻²⁶ e·cm.
- The lower limit on the neutrinoless double beta decay lifetime for ⁷⁶Ge is of order 10²⁵ years.
- Neutrino mass differences are estimated as 10⁻³ to 10⁻² eV between the lowest and second-lowest masses, and 10⁻¹ to 1 eV between the second and third masses.
- The s-process requires neutron fluxes of about 10⁸ neutrons per cm² per second; the r-process requires fluxes about 14 orders of magnitude larger.

## Context

This review extends the tradition of survey talks at nuclear physics conferences, covering topics from lattice QCD (where chiral symmetry implementation has been solved in principle within the last two years, per the author's lattice colleagues) to the solar neutrino problem resolved by SNO and Super-Kamiokande (Nobel prizes 2002 to Davis and Koshiba). The paper enables future work by identifying open questions such as the nature of the quark-gluon plasma phase transition (first or second order), the stalled supernova shock problem (where Langanke's suggestion of increased electron retention is noted), and the need for three-dimensional supernova simulations.
