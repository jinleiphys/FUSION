# Spectral Functions for the Quark-Meson Model Phase Diagram from the Functional Renormalization Group

> We present a method to obtain spectral functions at finite temperature and density from the Functional Renormalization Group. Our method is based on a thermodynamically consistent truncation of the flow equations for 2-point functions with analytically continued frequency components in the originally Euclidean external momenta. For the uniqueness of this continuation at finite temperature we furthermore implement the physical Baym-Mermin boundary conditions. We demonstrate the feasibility of the method by calculating the mesonic spectral functions in the quark-meson model along the temperature axis of the phase diagram, and at finite quark chemical potential along the fixed-temperature line that crosses the critical endpoint of the model.

## Key claim

The authors present a method to obtain mesonic spectral functions at finite temperature and density from the Functional Renormalization Group, but emphasize that this is only the first iteration step in a procedure that does not yet provide a non-trivial width for resonance peaks; full self-consistency would require an iterative scheme that feeds the momentum-dependent 2-point functions back into the flow equations.

## Method

The method uses a thermodynamically consistent truncation of the flow equations for mesonic 2-point functions, where the analytic continuation from Euclidean to real time is performed directly on the flow equations using 3-dimensional spatial regulators that avoid spurious poles. The Matsubara sums are evaluated explicitly, then the external frequency is analytically continued via \(p_0 \rightarrow -i(\omega + i\epsilon)\) after exploiting the periodicity of bosonic and fermionic occupation numbers, implementing the physical Baym-Mermin boundary conditions. The flow equations for the effective potential are solved first on a grid in field space, and then the flow equations for the real and imaginary parts of the pion and sigma 2-point functions are integrated at the fixed global minimum of the effective potential.

## Key numbers

* Quark mass in vacuum: \(m_\psi = 299\ \mathrm{MeV}\) at vanishing temperature and chemical potential.
* Pion mass in vacuum: \(m_\pi = 138\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
* Sigma meson mass in vacuum: \(m_\sigma = 509\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
* Chiral order parameter (pion decay constant): \(\sigma_0 \equiv f_\pi = 93.5\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
* Critical endpoint of the quark-meson model phase diagram: \(\mu = 293\ \mathrm{MeV}\) and \(T = 10\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
* Pseudo-critical temperature for the crossover at \(\mu = 0\): \(T_c \approx 175\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
* UV cutoff scale: \(\Lambda = 1000\ \mathrm{MeV}\); maximum accessible temperature: \(T_{\mathrm{max}} \approx \Lambda / 2\pi \approx 170\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
* Infrared scale where flow is stopped: \(k_{\mathrm{IR}} = 40\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
* Small imaginary part kept in analytic continuation: \(\epsilon = 1\ \mathrm{MeV}\).

## Context

This work extends previous results for the \(O(4)\) linear-sigma model in the vacuum by Kamikado (2013) to finite temperature and density with dynamical quarks within the quark-meson model, which serves as a low-energy effective model for 2-flavor QCD. The approach provides an alternative to maximum entropy methods (MEM) used in lattice QCD by Asakawa (2000) and in FRG studies by Haas (2013), as well as to Padé approximants used by Schmidt (2011), by performing the analytic continuation explicitly on the level of the flow equations. The method enables the calculation of real-time observables such as spectral functions at finite chemical potential without encountering the fermion sign-problem that limits lattice QCD, and can be extended to quark and gluonic spectral functions as an alternative to analytically continued Dyson-Schwinger equations by Strauss (2012).
