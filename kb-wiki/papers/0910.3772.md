> I answer the question in the title for the relativistic Bose gas at finite chemical potential using numerical lattice simulations, complemented with analytical understanding.

# Can complex Langevin dynamics evade the sign problem?

## Key claim
For the relativistic Bose gas at finite chemical potential, complex Langevin dynamics successfully reproduces the correct physics including the Silver Blaze problem and a phase transition, even when the sign problem is severe; however, the author cautions that applying the method "easily" to other theories with a complex action is "slightly too optimistic."

## Method
The author performed numerical lattice simulations of the relativistic Bose gas using complex Langevin equations, complexifying the two real scalar fields into four real fields and integrating the stochastic differential equations with Gaussian noise. The results were compared with analytical mean field calculations using a Gaussian factorization of the two-point functions, and with the phase quenched theory solved via real Langevin dynamics. Simulations used lattices of size N^4 with N=4,6,8,10, parameters m=λ=1, and Langevin time step ε=5×10^{-5}.

## Key numbers
- Critical chemical potential μ_c ≈ 1.15, determined from the mean field condition m^2+4λ⟨|φ|^2⟩+2(1-cosh μ)=0 with m=λ=1
- Density ⟨n⟩ is strictly zero for μ < μ_c ≈ 1.15 in the thermodynamic limit, demonstrating the Silver Blaze feature
- Langevin time step ε=5×10^{-5} was used with no instabilities observed
- Typical Langevin time range for runs was 0 < θ < 250
- At μ=0.5, the memory of initial conditions is quickly erased because the minimal value of A_p is well separated from zero

## Context
This work extends the author's previous study of complex Langevin dynamics in QCD with static quarks and one-link models (Aarts 2008), where excellent results were found for all chemical potential values. The relativistic Bose gas provides a simpler four-dimensional lattice field theory that still exhibits a sign problem, a Silver Blaze problem (Cohen 2003), and a phase transition, allowing both numerical simulation and perturbative analytical insight into the algorithm (Aarts 2009). The results enable further application of complex Langevin dynamics to other models with complex actions, such as the three-dimensional XY model at nonzero chemical potential and extended QCD with static quarks.
