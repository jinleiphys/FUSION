# A Quantum Simulation Approach to Implementing Nuclear Density Functional Theory via Imaginary Time Evolution

> The quantum imaginary time evolution (QITE) algorithm is a direct implementation of the classical imaginary time evolution algorithm on quantum computer. We implement the QITE algorithm for the case of nuclear Hartree-Fock equations in a formalism equivalent to nuclear density functional theory. We demonstrate the algorithm in the case of the helium-4 nucleus with a simplified effective interaction of the Skyrme kind and demonstrate that the QITE, as implemented on simulated quantum computer, gives identical results to the classical algorithm.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the quantum imaginary time evolution (QITE) algorithm, implemented on a simulated quantum computer, reproduces the classical imaginary time evolution results for the nuclear Hartree-Fock equations in the specific case of helium-4 with a simplified Skyrme interaction, but only when using a small basis expansion (up to four oscillator states) and a simplified interaction that neglects the $t_1$, $t_2$, and $W_0$ terms.

## Method

The authors encode the radial wave function of the helium-4 nucleus in a 3D isotropic oscillator basis, with expansion coefficients stored as a quantum state vector. They implement the non-unitary imaginary time evolution operator by decomposing it into a sum of Pauli matrix products, storing the coefficients in an ancillary state, and applying controlled Pauli gates followed by Hadamard gates to extract the evolved state. The algorithm is executed on a classical simulator using Qiskit with 10000 shots per measurement, iterating the imaginary time evolution until self-consistency is achieved.

## Key numbers

- Ground state energy of helium-4 for $N=1$ (2 expansion states): classical ITE gives $-29.1567$ MeV after 40 iterations; quantum ITE gives $-29.14$ MeV after 40 iterations
- Ground state energy of helium-4 for $N=2$ (4 expansion states): classical ITE gives $-34.8215$ MeV after 60 iterations; quantum ITE gives $-34.8$ MeV after 60 iterations
- Imaginary time step used: $\Delta\tau/\hbar = 0.005$ MeV$^{-1}$
- Optimized oscillator length for $N=1$ case: $1/b = 1.5284$ fm
- Optimized oscillator length for $N=2$ case: $1/b = 2.0635$ fm
- Skyrme parameters used: $t_0 = -1090.0$ MeV fm$^3$ and $t_3 = 17288.0$ MeV fm$^6$
- Self-consistency achieved after 20 iterations ($N=1$ classical) and 35 iterations ($N=2$ classical); quantum ITE required 50 iterations for $N=2$ self-consistency

## Context

This work extends the application of quantum imaginary time evolution, originally developed for quantum chemistry (Motta et al., 2020), to nuclear density functional theory using a Skyrme-type effective interaction. The authors build on prior nuclear physics quantum computing work using variational quantum eigensolvers for the deuteron (Dumitrescu et al., 2018) and shell-model calculations (Perez-Obiol et al., 2022), but note that their Hartree-Fock approach is limited to nearly uncorrelated states. The algorithm could serve as a state preparation method for more sophisticated quantum simulations of highly-correlated nuclear systems, or as a starting point for symmetry restoration algorithms (Lacroix et al., 2022).
