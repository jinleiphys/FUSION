# Geometry and symmetries of multi-particle systems

> The quantum dynamical evolution of atomic and molecular aggregates, from their compact to their fragmented states, is parametrized by a single collective radial parameter. Treating all the remaining particle coordinates in d dimensions democratically, as a set of angles orthogonal to this collective radius or by equivalent variables, bypasses all independent-particle approximations. The invariance of the total kinetic energy under arbitrary d-dimensional transformations which preserve the radial parameter gives rise to novel quantum numbers and ladder operators interconnecting its eigenstates at each value of the radial parameter. We develop the systematics and technology of this approach, introducing the relevant mathematics tutorially, by analogy to the familiar theory of angular momentum in three dimensions. The angular basis functions so obtained are treated in a manifestly coordinate-free manner, thus serving as a flexible generalized basis for carrying out detailed studies of wavefunction evolution in multi-particle systems.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the quantum dynamics of any multi-particle aggregate (atomic, molecular, or nuclear) can be exactly parametrized by a single collective radial parameter (the hyper-radius \(R\)), with all remaining degrees of freedom treated democratically as angular coordinates, thereby bypassing independent-particle approximations; the authors hedge that this approach is "tutorial" and "systematic" rather than providing completed calculations, and that the practical range of hyper-angular momentum quantum numbers \(\lambda\) remains modest only for low-lying channels or specific energy ranges.

## Method

The authors develop hyperspherical coordinates for \(N\)-particle systems in \(d = 3(N-1)\) dimensions, separating the hyper-radius \(R\) from hyper-angular coordinates, and exploit the invariance of the kinetic energy under \(d\)-dimensional rotations (the group \(SO(d)\)) to construct hyperspherical harmonics and ladder operators without solving partial differential equations in any specific coordinate system. They introduce Jacobi coordinate trees to represent alternative fragmentation channels, and outline how the Schrödinger equation (Eq. 5) can be expanded in hyperspherical harmonics to yield coupled ordinary differential equations in \(R\), with the effective charge operator \(Z(\hat{\mathbf{R}})\) coupling different channels.

## Key numbers

* The hyper-radius for an \(N\)-body aggregate is defined as \(R = \left( \sum_{i=1}^N M_i r_i^2 / \mathcal{M} \right)^{1/2}\), where \(\mathcal{M} = \sum_{i=1}^N M_i\) (Eq. 1).
* The angular Laplacian eigenvalue in \(d\) dimensions is \(\lambda(\lambda + d - 2)\), with integer \(\lambda\).
* The number of independent rotations (rank) in \(d\) dimensions is \(\ell = [d/2]\) (largest integer not exceeding \(d/2\)).
* For \(N\) particles in three-dimensional space, the total number of infinitesimal rotation operators is \(d(d-1)/2 = (3N-3)(3N-4)/2\).
* The factor \(R^{(3N-4)/2}\) in the Schrödinger equation reduces to unity for the hydrogen atom where \(N=2\).
* For the helium atom (three particles, six internal coordinates), the hyper-radius is \(R = (x_1^2 + y_1^2 + z_1^2 + x_2^2 + y_2^2 + z_2^2)^{1/2}\).

## Context

This work extends the hyperspherical approach pioneered by Delves (1958, 1960) for nuclear shell-model calculations and by Macek (1968) for doubly excited states of two-electron atoms, and generalizes it to arbitrary \(N\)-particle systems including both electrons and nuclei. The paper builds on prior algebraic treatments of \(d\)-dimensional rotations by Wybourne (1974) and Cornwell (1984), and aims to provide a coordinate-independent framework for constructing hyperspherical harmonics that can be applied to atoms, molecules, and nuclear aggregates, enabling ab initio calculations of wavefunction evolution from compact to fragmented states without solving partial differential equations in specific coordinate systems.
