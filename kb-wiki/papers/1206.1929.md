# Derivation of relativistic hydrodynamic equations consistent with relativistic Boltzmann equation by renormalization-group method

> We review our work on the application of the renormalization-group method to obtain first- and second-order relativistic hydrodynamics of the relativistic Boltzmann equation (RBE) as a dynamical system, with some corrections and new unpublished results. For the first-order equation, we explicitly obtain the distribution function in the asymptotic regime as the invariant manifold of the dynamical system, which turns out to be nothing but the matching condition defining the energy frame, i.e., the Landau-Lifshitz one. It is argued that the frame on which the flow of the relativistic hydrodynamic equation is defined must be the energy frame, if the dynamics should be consistent with the underlying RBE. A sketch is also given for derivation of the second-order hydrodynamic equation, i.e., extended thermodynamics, which is accomplished by extending the invariant manifold so that it is spanned by excited modes as well as the zero modes (hydrodynamic modes) of the linearized collision operator. On the basis of thus constructed resummed distribution function, we propose a novel ansatz for the functional form to be used in Grad moment method; it is shown that our theory gives the same expressions for the transport coefficients as those given in the Chapman-Enskog theory as well as the novel expressions for the relaxation times and lengths allowing natural interpretation.

## Key claim

The renormalization-group method applied to the relativistic Boltzmann equation uniquely leads to first-order relativistic hydrodynamics in the energy (Landau-Lifshitz) frame, provided the macroscopic-frame vector is taken to be the flow velocity itself with no momentum dependence; alternative frames like the particle frame require an unnatural momentum-dependent normalization that undermines the macroscopic interpretation.

## Method

The authors rewrite the relativistic Boltzmann equation using a flow velocity four-vector and introduce a small parameter epsilon representing the Knudsen number to treat spatial inhomogeneity as a perturbation. They apply the renormalization-group method to construct an invariant manifold of the dynamical system, expanding the distribution function around the local equilibrium Juttner function and renormalizing secular terms into the hydrodynamic variables (temperature, chemical potential, flow velocity). For second-order hydrodynamics, the invariant manifold is extended to include excited modes of the linearized collision operator in addition to the five zero modes.

## Key numbers

- The linearized collision operator L has exactly five zero modes: four corresponding to p^mu (mu = 0,1,2,3) and one corresponding to 1 x m (mass), as given in Eq. (1-039).
- The zeroth-order solution is the Juttner distribution function: f^(eq)_p = (1/(2pi)^3) exp[(mu - p^mu u_mu)/T], with u^mu u_mu = 1.
- The inner product defined in Eq. (1-034) uses the factor (p . u) and satisfies positive-definiteness: <phi, phi> = sum_p (1/p^0)(p . u) f^(eq)_p (phi_p)^2 > 0 for nonzero phi_p.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous applications of the renormalization-group method to the relativistic Boltzmann equation (Tsumura 2009, Tsumura 2011) and provides a systematic derivation that resolves ambiguities in defining the flow velocity for relativistic hydrodynamics. The approach contrasts with the Eckart (particle) frame, which is known to produce unphysical instabilities (Hiscock and Lindblom 1985), and with the Grad moment method, for which the authors propose a novel ansatz for the distribution function based on their resummed solution. The derived transport coefficients match those from the Chapman-Enskog method while also providing new expressions for relaxation times and lengths, enabling a natural physical interpretation and addressing the causality problem of first-order equations.
