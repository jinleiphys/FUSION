# The Pion in Electromagnetic and Weak Neutral Current Nuclear Response Functions

> The impact of pionic correlations and meson--exchange currents in determining the (vector) response functions for electroweak quasielastic lepton scattering from nuclei is discussed. The approach taken builds on previous work where the Fermi gas model is used to maintain consistency in treating forces and currents (gauge invariance) and to provide a Lorentz covariant framework. Results obtained in first-order perturbation theory are compared with infinite-order summation schemes (HF and RPA) and found to provide quite successful approximations for the quasielastic response functions. The role of pionic correlations in hardening the responses R_L and R_T is investigated in some detail, including studies of the relative importance of central and tensor pieces of the force and of exchange and self-energy diagrams; in addition, their role in significantly modifying the longitudinal parity-violating response R_{AV}^L is explored. The MEC are shown to provide a small, but non-negligible, contribution in determining the vector responses.

## Key claim

Pionic correlations and meson-exchange currents provide moderate corrections to the free relativistic Fermi gas (RFG) model for quasielastic electroweak nuclear response functions, with first-order perturbation theory proving quite accurate compared to infinite-order summation schemes (HF and RPA) for the kinematics studied, though the authors note this conclusion is restricted to the specific classes of diagrams considered and to the pionic degrees of freedom alone.

## Method

The authors employ a relativistic Fermi gas model as the baseline nuclear model and compute pionic correlation effects through self-energy and exchange diagrams in first-order perturbation theory, using the static one-pion-exchange potential (OPEP) with a monopole πNN vertex function characterized by a cutoff parameter Λ_π. They compare these first-order results with infinite-order summation schemes: the Hartree-Fock (HF) approximation for self-energy diagrams and the random phase approximation (RPA) for exchange diagrams, using continued fractions for the latter. The electromagnetic longitudinal (R_L) and transverse (R_T) response functions, as well as the weak neutral current longitudinal parity-violating response (R_AV^L), are computed for symmetric nuclear matter (Z=N=A/2) at momentum transfers q = 300, 500, and 1000 MeV/c.

## Key numbers

- Fermi momentum k_F = 225 MeV/c (corresponding roughly to ^12C) used as the default value throughout the calculations
- Cutoff parameter Λ_π = 1300 MeV used as the default value for the πNN vertex monopole form factor
- The isoscalar/isovector magnetic moment ratio squared (μ_S/μ_V)^2 ≈ 0.035, which greatly suppresses the isoscalar contribution compared to the isovector contribution in the transverse channel
- The exchange diagram is found to be three times stronger in the τ=0 (isoscalar) channel than in the τ=1 (isovector) channel for the charge response
- The self-energy contribution to the longitudinal response is shown at q = 300, 500, and 1000 MeV/c, with the effect decreasing as q increases and becoming negligible at 1000 MeV/c
- The HF longitudinal response at q = 300, 500, and 1000 MeV/c shows that first-order results practically coincide with the HF result, with the energy shift being at most a few MeV and only perceptible at low q

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Alberico et al., 1993, ref. [15]) of pionic effects in nuclear response functions, building on the relativistic Fermi gas model developed by Donnelly and collaborators (ref. [16]). It addresses the long-standing question of pionic degrees of freedom in nuclear structure, contrasting with the relativistic mean-field approach of Walecka and collaborators (ref. [13]) where pions do not enter in lowest order, and with variational approaches (refs. [7,8]) that suggest over half of nuclear matter binding energy may be ascribed to the pion. The finding that first-order perturbation theory works well for quasielastic kinematics despite the strong pionic interaction enables future work to incorporate heavier mesons within the same framework, and the significant modification of the weak neutral current longitudinal response R_AV^L by correlations opens a path for experimental tests of these predictions.
