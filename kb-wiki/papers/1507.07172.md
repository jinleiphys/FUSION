# The many faces of strange matter: compact stars, cosmic rays, and dark matter

> The state of cold bulk matter at around nuclear density depends on the fundamental strong interaction between quarks at low-energy scale, so-called non-perturbative quantum chromo-dynamics. Such kind of matter is conjectured to be condensed matter of 3-flavour (u, d and s) quark clusters in this note, being manifested in the form of compact stars, cosmic rays, and even dark matter.

## Key claim

The paper conjectures that cold bulk matter at around nuclear density is condensed matter of 3-flavour (u, d, and s) quark clusters, which could manifest as compact stars, cosmic rays, and even dark matter; however, this depends on whether the color coupling between quarks remains non-perturbative at a few nuclear densities, and the existence of such matter requires a minimal baryon number of about \(10^9\) for 3-flavour symmetry to be energetically favored.

## Method

The author reviews theoretical arguments for the existence of strange matter, considering two or three formation channels: first-order QCD phase transition in the early Universe producing relic strange nuggets with baryon numbers around \(10^{9-49}\); collapse events of normal baryonic matter (core-collapse of massive stars, O-Ne-Mg degenerate cores, or white dwarfs) that could produce strange compact remnants and ejected strangelets; and mergers of binary strange stars that could produce strange nuggets as cosmic rays. The paper contrasts strange quark-cluster matter (with localized quarks in clusters) against strange quark matter (with itinerant quarks), noting that the former has a stiffer equation of state and a strangeness barrier on the surface.

## Key numbers

- Minimal baryon number for 3-flavour symmetry: \(A_{\rm min} \simeq 10^9\), required for relativistic electrons to be inside the strange object.
- Baryon number of conjectured relic strange nuggets from the early Universe: \(10^{\sim (9-49)}\).
- Current masses of light quarks (u, d, s) are smaller than \(\sim 100\) MeV, while heavy quarks (c, t, b) have masses \(> 1\) GeV.
- The electron Compton wavelength \(\lambda_{\rm c} = h/m_{\rm e}c = 0.024\) Å is the scale above which weak interaction (neutronization and strangenization) becomes important.

## Context

This work extends the speculation by Bodmer (1970s) about "collapsed nuclei" with strangeness being energetically favored for large baryon number, and builds on earlier studies of bulk matter composed of almost free u, d, and s quarks (Witten 1984; Farhi and Jaffe 1984) and their astrophysical manifestations (Alcock, Farhi, and Olinto 1986; Haensel, Zdunik, and Schaeffer 1986). The paper introduces the concept of strange quark-cluster matter as an alternative to strange quark matter, which could help explain observations of X-ray bursters and the symbiotic X-ray system 4U 1700+24, and suggests that future facilities like FAST could provide evidence for strange matter.
