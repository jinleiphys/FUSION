# Angular Momentum-Resolved Inelastic Electron Scattering for Nuclear Giant Resonances

> Giant resonances (GRs) provide crucial insights into nuclear physics and astrophysics. Exciting GRs using particles like electrons is effective, yet the angular momentum (AM) transfer of electrons, including both intrinsic spin and orbital degrees of freedom in inelastic scattering, has never been studied. Here, we investigate AM transfer in GRs excited by plane-wave and vortex electrons, developing a comprehensive AM-resolved inelastic electron scattering theory. We find that even plane-wave electrons can model-independently extract transition strengths of higher multipolarity by selecting specific AM states of scattered electrons. Additionally, relativistic vortex electrons with orbital angular momentum (OAM) $\pm1$ can be efficiently generated. Vortex electrons can also be used to extract GR transition strength as in the plane-wave case, regardless of the position of nucleus relative to the beam axis. Furthermore, relativistic vortex electrons with larger OAM can be generated for on-axis nuclei due to AM conservation. Our method offers new perspectives for nuclear structure research and paves the way for generating vortex particles.

# Angular Momentum-Resolved Inelastic Electron Scattering for Nuclear Giant Resonances

## Key claim

Even plane-wave electrons can model-independently extract transition strengths of higher multipolarity giant resonances by selecting specific angular momentum states of scattered electrons, and relativistic vortex electrons with orbital angular momentum ±1 can be efficiently generated through the scattering process itself, without requiring ultra-intense laser devices to create pre-vortex particles.

## Method

The authors develop an angular-momentum-resolved inelastic electron scattering theory that incorporates both intrinsic spin and orbital angular momentum degrees of freedom, extending the traditional plane-wave framework. They compute transition amplitudes for plane-wave-to-vortex and vortex-to-vortex scattering using Bessel wave functions for electrons, with transition strengths determined by the fully self-consistent quasiparticle random phase approximation model based on covariant density functional theory for the nucleus ¹⁶O. The differential cross sections are decomposed into Coulomb and transverse electric multipole components for E1, E2, and E3 transitions.

## Key numbers

- Incident electron energy ε_i = 400 MeV used for calculations of differential cross sections for E1, E2, and E3 transitions in ¹⁶O
- For plane-wave electrons exciting E1 transitions, the scattered polar angle θ_p' with maximum differential cross section is approximately 0.004°, with a full width at half maximum of about 0.008°
- For oxygen gas target with thickness ρ_s = 228 mg/cm², the estimated number of vortex electrons with OAM m'_l = -1 generated per unit time per unit solid angle is approximately N'_e ≃ 0.27 × N_e, where N_e is the number of incident longitudinally spin-polarized electrons per unit time
- For vortex electron scattering with the nucleus off-axis, a specific impact parameter κb = 1.8 ħ is used where channels with m' = -1/2 and 3/2 are most probable
- For E2 transition extraction using vortex electrons with m' = 5/2, the scattered polar angle θ_p' = 0.2° is examined

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior demonstration that vortex γ photons with intrinsic orbital angular momentum enable selective probing of isovector giant resonances with higher multipolarities (Lu 2023). It addresses the longstanding challenge that existing inelastic electron scattering theories (Barber 1962, Eisenberg 1976) inadequately address angular momentum conservation and selection rules, which has contributed to large spreads in reported transition probabilities and resonance widths from experimental studies (Bertrand 1976, Pitthan 1979). The method enables model-independent extraction of transition strengths for higher multipolarity giant resonances and opens a new pathway for generating relativistic vortex electrons through electron-nucleus interactions, with implications for nuclear structure research, nuclear astrophysics, and strong field laser physics.
