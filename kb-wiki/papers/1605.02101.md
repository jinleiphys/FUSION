# Quasiparticle anisotropic hydrodynamics for central collisions

> We use quasiparticle anisotropic hydrodynamics to study an azimuthally-symmetric boost-invariant quark-gluon plasma including the effects of both shear and bulk viscosities. In quasiparticle anisotropic hydrodynamics, a single finite-temperature quasiparticle mass is introduced and fit to the lattice data in order to implement a realistic equation of state. We compare results obtained using the quasiparticle method with the standard method of imposing the equation of state in anisotropic hydrodynamics and viscous hydrodynamics. Using these three methods, we extract the primordial particle spectra, total number of charged particles, and average transverse momentum for various values of the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio eta/s. We find that the three methods agree well for small shear viscosity to entropy density ratio, eta/s, but differ at large eta/s. We find, in particular, that when using standard viscous hydrodynamics, the bulk-viscous correction can drive the primordial particle spectra negative at large p_T which is clearly unphysical. Such a behavior is not seen in either anisotropic hydrodynamics approach, irrespective of the value of eta/s.

## Key claim

The quasiparticle anisotropic hydrodynamics (aHydroQP) method, which introduces a temperature-dependent quasiparticle mass fitted to lattice QCD data, agrees with standard anisotropic hydrodynamics (aHydro) and second-order viscous hydrodynamics (vHydro) for small shear viscosity to entropy density ratio (eta/s), but differs at large eta/s; crucially, only vHydro produces unphysical negative primordial particle spectra at high transverse momentum (p_T) due to bulk-viscous corrections, while both anisotropic hydrodynamics approaches avoid this pathology regardless of eta/s.

## Method

The authors extended previous 0+1d quasiparticle anisotropic hydrodynamics work to 1+1d for an azimuthally-symmetric, boost-invariant quark-gluon plasma, using a relaxation-time approximation for the collisional kernel. They derived dynamical equations from moments of the Boltzmann equation for three approaches: aHydroQP (with a temperature-dependent quasiparticle mass fit to lattice data from the Wuppertal-Budapest collaboration), aHydro (standard method imposing equation of state via conformal factorization), and vHydro (second-order viscous hydrodynamics). They solved the resulting partial differential equations numerically and applied anisotropic Cooper-Frye freeze-out to compute primordial particle spectra, total charged particle number, and average transverse momentum for pions, kaons, and protons.

## Key numbers

- For shear viscosity to entropy density ratio \(4\pi\eta/s = 1\), the three methods (aHydroQP, aHydro, vHydro) agree well for neutral pion, kaon, and proton spectra as a function of transverse momentum.
- For \(4\pi\eta/s = 3\), differences between the methods become visible in the particle spectra.
- For \(4\pi\eta/s = 10\), vHydro produces unphysical negative primordial particle spectra at large p_T, while neither aHydroQP nor aHydro show this behavior.
- Total charged particle number and average transverse momentum for pions, kaons, and protons are compared as functions of \(4\pi\eta/s\) across all three methods.

## Context

This work extends the quasiparticle anisotropic hydrodynamics framework of Alqahtani (2015) from 0+1d to 1+1d systems, building on prior anisotropic hydrodynamics developments by Martinez (2010), Florkowski (2010), and Nopoush (2014). It compares against the standard method of imposing a realistic equation of state in anisotropic hydrodynamics developed by Nopoush (2015) and against second-order viscous hydrodynamics approaches from the literature (e.g., Muronga 2001, Romatschke 2007, Song 2008). The results enable more reliable phenomenological predictions for heavy-ion collisions at LHC energies, particularly for central Pb-Pb and p-Pb collisions, by providing a method that avoids unphysical negative spectra at high p_T that plague standard viscous hydrodynamics at large eta/s.
