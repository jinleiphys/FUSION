# Prospects for study of the effect of the electronic screening on the $α$ decay in the storage rings

> Study of the role of the electron screening in the $α$ decay is advanced, aimed at the experimental test in the storage rings. To this end, systematic calculation of the effect in heavy ions of the nuclei within the Ra to Po domain of the $α$ emitters is conducted within the adiabatic approach. The obtained effect is to slow down the decay by an amount within the percent value. It is of the opposite sign comparing to what is predicted within the conventional frozen shell model. The reason for this divergence is demonstrated. Testing this difference experimentally in storage ring facilities is proposed. It is of great interest for advancing the astrophysical fundamental research.

## Key claim
The electron screening effect on alpha decay is predicted to slow down the decay by about 0.1% (negative effect) within the adiabatic approach, which is opposite in sign and an order of magnitude smaller than the positive effect of about 1% predicted by the conventional frozen shell model. The authors propose that this sign difference can be tested experimentally in storage rings by comparing decay rates of bare nuclei versus helium-like or neon-like ions.

## Method
The authors performed systematic calculations of the electron screening effect on alpha decay for nuclei in the Ra to Po region using the adiabatic approach, where the electronic wavefunctions evolve dynamically as the alpha particle moves through the shell. They computed the effective electronic potential from Dirac electron wavefunctions using the RAINE package, and compared the resulting decay rate changes with those from the frozen shell model where electrons are held fixed in their initial atomic orbitals.

## Key numbers
- For neutral $^{226}$Ra (Q = 4.87063 MeV), the relative change in decay rate Y = -0.246% (adiabatic approach)
- For neutral $^{212}$Rn (Q = 6.385 MeV), Y = -0.086%
- For $^{226}$Ra, the adiabatic effective potential starts from zero at the nuclear surface, while the frozen shell potential starts from -0.767 keV at R = 0
- The energy difference Delta B for $^{226}$Ra is -38.805 keV; the frozen shell potential at R = 0 is -39.572 keV
- About 82% of the total screening effect comes from K-shell electrons
- For $^{226}$Ra in He-like ions, Y = -0.201%; for Ne-like ions, Y = -0.236%
- For $^{212}$Rn in He-like ions, Y = -0.071%; for Ne-like ions, Y = -0.082%
- For radium isotopes with half-lives of seconds to minutes, Y ranges from -0.060% ($^{220}$Ra, Q = 7.595 MeV) to -0.091% ($^{222}$Ra, Q = 6.681 MeV)
- For $^{213}$Fr (Q = 6.9051 MeV, T1/2 = 34.6 s), Y = -0.082%
- For $^{211}$Po (Q = 7.5945 MeV, T1/2 = 0.516 s), Y = -0.061%

## Context
This work extends the adiabatic approach first proposed by Karpeshin (2013) for treating electron screening in alpha decay, and directly contradicts the frozen shell model calculations by Patyk (1999) for radon isotopes, which predicted a positive effect of about 1%. The authors note that a previous experiment at GSI on H-like $^{213}$Fr ions (2013) measured a half-life of 34(6) s, compatible with the neutral atom value but with large statistical uncertainty. The proposed storage ring experiments using Schottky spectroscopy could achieve 0.1% precision, enabling a definitive test between the two models and providing information relevant to astrophysical nuclear reaction rates in stellar plasmas.
