# Probing the Fundamental Symmetries of the Early Universe: The Low Energy Frontier

> Searching for the fundamental symmetries that characterize the particle physics of the early universe lies at the forefront of particle physics, nuclear physics, and cosmology. In this talk, I review low energy probes of these symmetries and discuss what they may teach us about what lies beyond the fundamental symmetries of the Standard Model.

## Key claim

Low-energy precision electroweak studies can probe the fundamental symmetries of the early universe and reveal physics beyond the Standard Model, but the Standard Model itself cannot explain the observed baryon asymmetry because its electroweak phase transition is not strongly first order given experimental Higgs mass bounds, and its CP-violation via the CKM matrix is highly suppressed by the Jarlskog invariant and multiple powers of quark Yukawa couplings.

## Method

The paper reviews three categories of low-energy precision probes: searches for electric dipole moments (EDMs) of particles to test electroweak baryogenesis scenarios; studies of weak decays (beta-decay, CKM unitarity tests) and parity-violating electron scattering to detect virtual effects of new forces; and measurements of the Michel spectrum in muon decay and searches for lepton flavor violation to constrain new interactions. The analysis uses effective four-fermion Lagrangians and radiative correction frameworks to compare experimental results with Standard Model predictions.

## Key numbers

- The baryon asymmetry of the universe is Y_B ~ 10^{-10}, determined from WMAP analysis of the cosmic microwave background and Big Bang Nucleosynthesis
- The Planck scale M_P ~ 2 x 10^{18} GeV is sixteen orders of magnitude larger than the electroweak symmetry breaking scale M_wk
- The electroweak symmetry-breaking temperature is T_wk ~ 100 GeV
- The extracted value of V_ud from superallowed beta-decay is 0.97377(11)(15)(19), where the three errors are experimental plus nuclear structure, nuclear Coulomb distortion, and hadronic structure uncertainty
- The weak charges of the electron and proton satisfy |Q_W^{e,p}| less than or approximately 0.1, making them sensitive to new forces
- New EDM searches will improve sensitivity by two to four orders of magnitude beyond current limits
- The muon anomalous magnetic moment a_mu shows a deviation from the Standard Model prediction that is most likely within supersymmetry at large tan(beta)

## Context

This talk reviews the low-energy precision frontier as complementary to the energy frontier (Fermilab, LHC, ILC) for probing physics beyond the Standard Model. It extends the work of Sakharov (1967) on baryogenesis conditions and addresses questions from the NSAC subcommittee "Tribble Report" (2005). The paper discusses how precision measurements can test electroweak baryogenesis scenarios (which the Standard Model fails to explain due to Higgs mass constraints) and can probe supersymmetry, left-right symmetric models, and other new physics. The results enable future experimental programs including new EDM searches, the Q-Weak experiment at Jefferson Lab, and muon-to-electron conversion searches, which together can constrain or rule out specific baryogenesis scenarios.
