# Lowest Order Constrained Variational Calculation of Structure Properties of Protoneutron Star

> We calculate the structure properties of protoneutron star such as equation of state, maximum mass, radius and temperature profile using the lowest order constrained variational method. We show that the mass and radius of protoneutron star decrease by decreasing both entropy and temperature. For the protoneutron star, it is shown that the temperature is nearly constant in the core and drops rapidly near the crust.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the mass and radius of a protoneutron star decrease with decreasing entropy and temperature, and that the temperature is nearly constant in the core and drops rapidly near the crust, but these results are conditional on the specific choice of the Argonne V18 potential and the lowest order constrained variational method, which yields equations of state that differ from those of prior calculations by Gondek et al. and Strobel et al.

## Method

The authors used the lowest order constrained variational method with the Argonne V18 nucleon-nucleon potential to compute the equation of state of protoneutron star matter, including contributions from leptons and nucleons up to the two-body cluster term. They then numerically integrated the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation to obtain the gravitational mass, radius, and temperature profile for lepton-rich protoneutron stars (with lepton fraction Y = 0.3 or 0.4 and entropy s = 1 or 2 k_B) and for beta-stable protoneutron stars along both isentropic and isothermal paths. At low densities, they supplemented their calculations with equations of state from Gondek et al. and Strobel et al.

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior studies of neutron star matter (Bordbar et al., 2005-2008) by applying the lowest order constrained variational method with the Argonne V18 potential to the hot, lepton-rich environment of a protoneutron star. The results are compared with those of Gondek et al. (2001) and Strobel et al. (2003), showing that the equations of state from Strobel et al. are stiffer than those obtained here, while the results of Gondek et al. are in closer agreement for the case s = 2 k_B and Y = 0.3. The calculations enable predictions of the maximum mass and radius of protoneutron stars at different entropies and temperatures, which are relevant for determining whether a protoneutron star evolves into a stable neutron star or collapses to a black hole.
