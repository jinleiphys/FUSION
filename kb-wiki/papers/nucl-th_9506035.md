# Effective Field Theories

> Three lectures on effective field theory given at the Seventh Summer School in Nuclear Physics, Seattle June 19-30 1995.

## Key claim

Effective field theory provides a systematic framework for constructing low-energy approximations to more fundamental theories by expanding in operators consistent with low-energy symmetries, where the expansion parameter is the ratio of the experimental energy scale to the physical scales of the system. The key insight is that for processes at energy E much smaller than a scale Lambda, the effects of high-energy physics appear only through irrelevant operators whose coefficients are suppressed by powers of 1/Lambda, making the theory predictive despite containing an infinite number of operators.

## Method

The paper develops effective field theory through dimensional analysis of operators in the Lagrangian, classifying operators as relevant (dimension less than 4), marginal (dimension 4), or irrelevant (dimension greater than 4) based on how their effects scale with energy. The method is illustrated through constructing effective Lagrangians for Rayleigh scattering of light by neutral atoms, low-energy neutrino interactions via the Fermi interaction, and the binding of charmonium to nuclei, where the coefficients of irrelevant operators are estimated by matching to the underlying theory or by dimensional analysis using the physical scales of the system (such as atomic size r0 or the weak scale M_W). The paper also discusses how quantum corrections and renormalization affect the classification of operators, particularly how marginal operators can become relevant or irrelevant through loop effects.

## Key numbers

- Fine structure constant alpha = 1/137 (dimensionless)
- Bohr radius a0 = 1/(alpha * m_e), where m_e = 0.511 MeV
- Ground state binding energy of hydrogen E0 = (1/2) * alpha^2 * m_e (approximately 13.6 eV)
- Photon energy for X-ray diffraction: E_gamma > alpha * m_e = O(10 keV)
- Photon energy for atomic transitions: E_gamma < alpha^2 * m_e = O(10 eV)
- Correction to hydrogen binding energy from b quark mass: O(m_e^2/m_b^2) ~ 10^-8, with m_b = 5 GeV
- Fine structure constant at 100 GeV center-of-mass energy: alpha = 1/128
- W boson mass: 80 GeV; Z boson mass: 91 GeV
- Neutrino cross-section scaling: sigma_nu ~ G^2 * s, where G has dimension -2 and is roughly g^2/M_W^2
- Muon mass: 106 MeV; muon lifetime: 2.2 * 10^-6 seconds
- Tau mass: 1777 MeV; tau lifetime: 3.0 * 10^-13 seconds
- Observed branching ratio for tau -> e + antineutrino_e + neutrino_tau: 18.01 +/- 0.18%
- Proton lifetime lower bound for p -> e+ + pi0: 1.3 * 10^32 years
- Neutron-antineutron oscillation lower bound: 1.2 * 10^8 seconds
- QCD scale Lambda_QCD = 200 MeV
- Charmonium binding energy to nuclei estimate: E_B ~ Lambda_QCD^4 / (alpha_s * m_c)^3, where m_c is the charm quark mass

## Context

This paper presents three pedagogical lectures on effective field theory given at the Seventh Summer School in Nuclear Physics in 1995, building on the foundational work of Ken Wilson and the modern treatments by Polchinski (1992) and Georgi (1994). The lectures extend the effective field theory approach to nonrelativistic systems with a Fermi surface, explaining the success of Landau liquid theory and the emergence of superconductivity from marginal interactions. The work enables practitioners to systematically compute low-energy observables from high-energy theories through matching, and to understand why renormalizable theories naturally emerge when there is a large hierarchy of physical scales.
