# Solar Neutrinos: Where We Are, What We Need

> This talk compares standard model predictions with the results of solar neutrino experiments. Here `standard model' means the combined standard model of minimal electroweak theory plus a standard solar model. I emphasize the importance of recent analyses in which the neutrino fluxes are treated as free parameters, independent of any constraints from solar models, and the stunning agreement between the predictions of standard solar models and helioseismological measurements. In order to interpret solar neutrino experiments more accurately in terms of fundamental physics and astronomy, we need improved improved nuclear physics data. I describe the five most important nuclear physics problems whose solution is required for understanding the precise implications of solar neutrino experiments.

# Solar Neutrinos: Where We Are, What We Need

## Key claim
The combined predictions of the standard solar model and the minimal electroweak theory disagree with the results of all four pioneering solar neutrino experiments, and this disagreement persists even when neutrino fluxes are treated as free parameters independent of any solar model constraints. The paper argues that either at least three of the four operating experiments have yielded misleading results, or physics beyond the standard electroweak model is required to change neutrino energy spectra or flavor content after production.

## Method
The author compares measured event rates from the chlorine (Homestake), Kamiokande, GALLEX, and SAGE experiments with predictions from the combined standard model (standard solar model plus minimal electroweak theory). He reviews model-independent analyses by Hata, Bludman, and Langacker (1994), Heeger and Robertson (1996), and Hata and Langacker (1997) that treat neutrino fluxes as free parameters constrained only by the solar luminosity, and compares standard solar model sound-speed predictions with helioseismological measurements from Basu and collaborators (1996). The paper then identifies the five most important nuclear reaction cross sections requiring improved measurements for precise interpretation of solar neutrino experiments.

## Key numbers
- Chlorine experiment measured rate: 2.55 ± 0.25 SNU, compared to predicted 9.5 (+1.2, -1.4) SNU, a factor of ~3.6 less than predicted.
- The partial 8B neutrino contribution to the chlorine rate, inferred from Kamiokande observations above 7 MeV, is 3.2 ± 0.45 SNU, exceeding the total observed chlorine rate of 2.55 ± 0.25 SNU.
- Average observed gallium experiment rate (GALLEX and SAGE): 70.5 ± 7 SNU, compared to a standard model prediction of 73 SNU from pp and pep neutrinos alone, leaving no room for the expected 34 ± 4 SNU from 7Be neutrinos.
- Model-independent analysis by Heeger and Robertson (1996) found inconsistency with standard neutrino assumptions at 99.5% C.L. with the luminosity constraint, and at 94% C.L. without it.
- Standard solar model sound speeds agree with helioseismological measurements to 0.1% rms (Bahcall-Pinsonneault model with diffusion), and the Christensen-Dalsgaard et al. (1996) model agrees to better than 0.2% everywhere.
- The 8B neutrino flux scales as T^24 with central temperature; maximum temperature differences of ~0.2% from helioseismology produce flux changes much less than required to resolve the solar neutrino problems.
- The 7Be(p,gamma)8B cross section at low energies (~20 keV) is known experimentally to only about 4 parts in 19 (1 sigma).
- The pp reaction rate uncertainty of 2% is dominated by meson-exchange corrections to the nuclear matrix element.

## Context
This talk extends the long-standing solar neutrino problem first identified by the chlorine experiment (Davis, 1964-1994) and sharpens it by showing that the three separate discrepancies (absolute rate, chlorine-Kamiokande incompatibility, and missing 7Be neutrinos in gallium experiments) cannot be resolved by solar model changes, especially given the precise helioseismological confirmation of standard solar models by Basu and collaborators (1996) and Christensen-Dalsgaard et al. (1996). The paper builds on the 1997 Seattle Workshop on Solar Fusion Reactions (Bahcall, Haxton, Parker, Robertson) and the collective report submitted to Reviews of Modern Physics (Adelberger et al., 1998). It identifies the five most critical nuclear physics measurements needed to interpret the upcoming generation of experiments (Super-Kamiokande, SNO, GNO, BOREXINO, ICARUS) for fundamental physics and astronomy.
