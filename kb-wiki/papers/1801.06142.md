# Neutron moderation spectrum considering inelastic scattering

> For the first time an analytic expression was obtained for the inelastic neutron scattering law with an isotropic neutron source within the gas model, considering moderating medium temperature as a parameter. The inelastic scattering law is obtained, based on the solution of the kinematic problem of neutron inelastic scattering on a nucleus in laboratory coordinate system (L-system) in general case. I.e. in case not only a neutron but also a nucleus have arbitrary velocity vector in L-system. Analytic expressions are found for the neutron flux density and moderation spectrum in reactor fissile medium, both in case of the elastic scattering law, obtained earlier by the authors, and in case of the inelastic scattering law obtained in this paper. Both elastic and inelastic scattering laws are considered to be dependent on the medium temperature. The obtained expressions for neutron moderation spectra enable reinterpretation of physical nature of the processes that determine the shape of neutron spectrum in a wide energy range.

## Key claim

For the first time, an analytic expression for the inelastic neutron scattering law is derived within the gas model, treating the moderating medium temperature as a parameter and assuming an isotropic neutron source; the derivation relies on averaging over the spherically-symmetric angular distributions of both the neutron source and the thermal motion of moderator nuclei, and the resulting scattering law is valid only when the compound nucleus decomposition is isotropic in the center-of-mass system.

## Method

The authors solved the kinematic problem of neutron inelastic scattering on a nucleus in the laboratory system, where both the neutron and the nucleus have arbitrary velocity vectors, using the gas model for the moderating medium. They introduced a non-stationary laboratory frame (L'-system) where the target nucleus is initially at rest, transformed to the center-of-mass system, and applied conservation of momentum with a non-zero reaction thermal effect (gamma-quantum energy). The average neutron energy after inelastic scattering was obtained by sequentially averaging over the isotropic angular distributions of the neutron source, the thermal motion of moderator nuclei (Maxwell distribution), and the isotropic inelastic scattering in the center-of-mass system, yielding a probability density function for the scattered neutron energy.

## Key numbers

* The average kinetic energy of moderating medium nuclei is given by \(\overline{E}_N^{(L)} = \frac{3}{2}kT\), where \(k\) is Boltzmann's constant and \(T\) is the moderating medium temperature.
* The inelastic scattering law probability density is \(P(E_1) = 1 / \left[ \left( E_{10}^{(L)} + \frac{1}{A}\cdot\frac{3}{2}kT \right) \widetilde{\alpha}_2^2 (1-\widetilde{\alpha}_1) \right]\), where \(E_{10}^{(L)}\) is the neutron energy before scattering, \(A\) is the nucleus mass number, and \(\widetilde{\alpha}_1\), \(\widetilde{\alpha}_2\) are inelastic scattering coefficients defined as \(\widetilde{\alpha}_1^2 = ((AB-1)/(AB+1))^2\) and \(\widetilde{\alpha}_2^2 = ((AB+1)/(A+1))^2\) with \(B^2 = 1/2\).
* The gamma-quantum energy \(E_\gamma\) in inelastic scattering is confined to the range \(0 \leq E_\gamma \leq \frac{A}{A+1} \cdot \frac{(V_{10}^{(L')})^2}{2} < E_f\), where \(E_f\) is the fission threshold energy of the compound nucleus.

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior elastic-scattering-based neutron moderation theory (Rusov, 2017) by incorporating inelastic scattering, which is essential for moderating media containing heavy nuclei such as uranium-238 where inelastic scattering becomes significant above several hundred keV. The derived analytic expressions for the neutron flux density and moderation spectrum, accounting for both elastic and inelastic scattering laws dependent on medium temperature, enable reinterpretation of the physical processes determining neutron spectra in a wide energy range and provide a theoretical foundation for investigating reactor emergency modes, soliton fission reactors, impulse reactors, neutron boosters, subcritical assemblies, and natural nuclear reactors such as the georeactor.
