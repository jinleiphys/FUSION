# Applications of the dynamical generator coordinate method to quadrupole excitations

> We apply the dynamical generator coordinate method (DGCM) with a conjugate momentum to a nuclear collective excitation. To this end, we first discuss how to construct a numerically workable scheme of the DGCM for a general one-body operator. We then apply the DGCM to the quadrupole vibration of $^{16}$O using the Gogny D1S interaction. We show that both the ground state energy and the excitation energies are lowered as compared to the conventional GCM with the same number of basis functions. We also compute the sum rule values for the quadrupole and monopole operators, and show that the DGCM yields more consistent results than the conventional GCM to the values from the double commutator. These results imply that the conjugate momentum is an important and relevant degree of freedom in collective motions.

## Key claim

The dynamical generator coordinate method (DGCM) yields lower ground-state and excitation energies and more consistent sum-rule values than the conventional GCM for quadrupole excitations in \(^{16}\)O, but only when the model space is large enough that the cutoff approximation for the one-body operator remains unitary; the improvement is conditional on the collective subspace being constructed with a conjugate momentum that satisfies the canonical commutation relation.

## Method

The authors constructed a dynamical path \(|\beta, p_\beta\rangle = e^{i p_\beta \hat{\beta}} |\beta\rangle\) using the constrained Hartree-Fock method with the Gogny D1S interaction, where \(\hat{\beta}\) is the dimensionless quadrupole operator. They applied a cutoff to the one-body operator in a finite harmonic-oscillator basis (\(N_0=10\)) to maintain unitarity of the transformation, then solved the Hill-Wheeler equation for both the DGCM (using 25 points in the \(\beta\)-\(p_\beta\) plane) and the conventional GCM (using 25 points in \(\beta\) only). Hamiltonian and norm kernels were evaluated with the generalized Wick theorem, and eigenvalues of the norm kernel below \(10^{-5}\) were removed to handle overcompleteness.

## Key numbers

* Ground-state energy: GCM gives \(-129.682\) MeV, DGCM gives \(-129.765\) MeV for \(^{16}\)O with the Gogny D1S interaction.
* First excited-state energy: GCM gives \(-107.993\) MeV, DGCM gives \(-108.140\) MeV.
* Second excited-state energy: GCM gives \(-92.260\) MeV, DGCM gives \(-104.475\) MeV.
* Quadrupole sum-rule ratio for the ground state: GCM gives 1.0005, DGCM gives 1.0047 (ideal value is 1).
* Quadrupole sum-rule ratio for the second excited state: GCM gives 0.7309, DGCM gives 1.0512.
* Monopole sum-rule ratio for the ground state: GCM gives 0.3855, DGCM gives 0.9176.
* Monopole sum-rule ratio for the first excited state: GCM gives 0.6533, DGCM gives 0.9926.
* The smallest eigenvalue of the DGCM norm kernel is O(\(10^{-8}\)), while in the GCM there are 10 eigenvalues below \(10^{-13}\).

## Context

This work extends the dynamical GCM formalism of Goeke and Reinhard (1980) from formal theory to practical numerical implementation for quadrupole vibrations, following the authors' earlier application to particle-number projection (Hizawa and Hagino, 2021). It contrasts with conventional GCM calculations (Bender 2003, Yao 2014, and many others) that use only a collective coordinate without its conjugate momentum, and with the self-consistent collective coordinate method (Marumori 1980, Matsuo 1984) which treats coordinates and momenta on equal footing but does not explicitly mix configurations. The results enable future applications to large-amplitude collective motions such as nuclear fission within the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov framework, and open the possibility of studying entanglement entropy in collective subspaces.
