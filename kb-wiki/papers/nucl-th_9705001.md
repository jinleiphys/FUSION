# Probing the Structure of Halo Nuclei

> Our understanding of halo nuclei has so far relied on high-energy scattering and reactions, but a number of uncertainties remain. I discuss in general terms the new range of observables which will be measured by experiments around the Coulomb barrier, and how some details of the reaction mechanisms still need to be clarified.

# Probing the Structure of Halo Nuclei

## Key claim
While high-energy scattering and reactions have provided the initial understanding of halo nuclei, significant uncertainties remain about their detailed structure; experiments around the Coulomb barrier will provide new observables, but the reaction mechanisms at these lower energies still need to be clarified.

## Method
The paper reviews existing high-energy experimental probes of halo nuclei (fragmentation momentum distributions and Coulomb breakup) and discusses the theoretical challenges in interpreting them. It then surveys the prospects for low-energy reactions near the Coulomb barrier, including elastic scattering, fusion, transfer reactions, and inelastic scattering, identifying where current three-body and four-body reaction models are insufficient and what new measurements could resolve structural ambiguities.

## Key numbers
- $^6$He and $^{11}$Li are the clearest examples of two-neutron halo nuclei, and $^{11}$Be for one-neutron halos.
- For $^{12}$Be, a three-body model with an inert $^{10}$Be core gives valence neutrons mainly in $(p_{1/2})^2$ configuration with about 25% admixture of $(sd)^2$ configurations; including core excitation changes this to mainly $(sd)^2$ with only 10% $(p_{1/2})^2$ and ~40% core-excited components.
- Fusion reductions of up to 40% have been found for $^{6,7}$Li + $^9$Be and $^{6,7}$Li + $^{12}$C at energies from near the barrier to approximately twice the barrier.
- The continuum region $0 < E < 3$ MeV must be accurately calculated for three-body n+n+core systems to examine the possible soft-dipole mode.
- The 320 keV separation of the first excited state in $^{11}$Be must be resolved experimentally to observe depletion effects in elastic scattering.

## Context
This work extends the discussion of halo nuclear structure from the early high-energy experiments of Tanihata (1988) and the fragmentation studies of Kobayashi (1988) and Orr (1992) toward lower-energy regimes. It identifies specific open questions, such as the role of intruder levels, pairing correlations, core excitation (as studied by Csoto (1993) and Hoffman (1997)), and the existence of soft dipole resonances. The paper enables future experimental programs at low-energy radioactive beam facilities by specifying which measurements (elastic scattering, fusion, transfer, inelastic) could discriminate between competing theoretical models, such as the inert-core versus core-coupled models for $^{12}$Be.
