# Recent development of complex scaling method for many-body resonances and continua in light nuclei

> The complex scaling method (CSM) is a useful similarity transformation of the Schrödinger equation, in which bound-state spectra are not changed but continuum spectra are separated into resonant and non-resonant continuum ones. Because the asymptotic wave functions of the separated resonant states are regularized by the CSM, many-body resonances can be obtained by solving an eigenvalue problem with the $L^2$ basis functions. Applying this method to a system consisting of a core and valence nucleons, we investigate many-body resonant states in weakly bound nuclei very far from the stability lines. Non-resonant continuum states are also obtained with the discretized eigenvalues on the rotated branch cuts. Using these complex eigenvalues and eigenstates in CSM, we construct the extended completeness relations and Green's functions to calculate strength functions and breakup cross sections. Various kinds of theoretical calculations and comparisons with experimental data are presented.

## Key claim

The complex scaling method (CSM) provides a unified framework for treating bound states, resonances, and non-resonant continuum states in many-body nuclear systems by rotating the coordinate and momentum axes into the complex plane, but its practical application requires that the interaction potentials maintain analyticity under the transformation and that the scaling angle \(\theta\) exceeds the resonance angle \(\theta_r = \tan^{-1}(\gamma_r/\kappa_r)\) to regularize divergent resonance wave functions.

## Method

The authors apply the complex scaling transformation \(U(\theta)\) to the many-body Schrödinger equation, replacing coordinates \(\mathbf{r}\) by \(\mathbf{r}e^{i\theta}\) and momenta \(\mathbf{k}\) by \(\mathbf{k}e^{-i\theta}\), then diagonalize the resulting non-Hermitian Hamiltonian \(H^\theta\) using \(L^2\) basis functions such as Gaussian or harmonic oscillator functions. For systems consisting of a core plus valence nucleons, they employ the cluster-orbital shell model (COSM) to construct many-body basis states and obtain complex eigenvalues that separate bound states (real negative), resonances (complex with \(E = E_r - i\Gamma/2\)), and discretized non-resonant continuum states on rotated branch cuts. They further construct complex-scaled Green's functions from the extended completeness relation to calculate strength functions and breakup cross sections.

## Key numbers

- The scaling angle \(\theta\) must satisfy \(\theta > \theta_r = \tan^{-1}(\gamma_r/\kappa_r)\) for a resonance with momentum \(\kappa_r - i\gamma_r\) to regularize the divergent asymptotic wave function.
- For a Gaussian potential, the CSM is applicable with \(\theta < \pi/4\) to maintain analyticity.
- The continuum spectra are rotated clockwise by an angle of \(2\theta\) from the positive real energy axis starting from each threshold.
- The typical range of the nucleon-nucleon interaction is given by the one-pion exchange as around 1.4 fm.
- The average binding energy per nucleon in stable nuclei is about 8 MeV.

## Context

This review extends the CSM framework originally proposed by Aguilar, Combes, and Balslev (1971) and Simon (1972) from atomic and molecular physics to nuclear many-body systems, building on earlier applications in nuclear cluster models by Kruppa (1988) and Csoto (1994). The approach is complementary to the Gamow shell model (GSM) developed by Michel (2002) and others, which uses Berggren's extended completeness relation with complex contour deformation for single-particle basis states. The CSM enables the unified description of bound, resonant, and continuum states in weakly bound nuclei far from stability, particularly for Borromean systems such as \(^6\)He and \(^{11}\)Li, and allows calculation of scattering observables including breakup cross sections and strength functions via the complex-scaled Lippmann-Schwinger equation.
