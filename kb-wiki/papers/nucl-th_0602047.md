# Strangeness Chemical Equilibration in QGP at RHIC and LHC

> We study, in the dynamically evolving QGP fireball formed in relativistic heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, the growth of strangeness yield toward and beyond the chemical equilibrium. We account for the contribution of the direct strangeness production and evaluate the thermal-QCD strangeness production mechanisms. The specific yield of strangeness per entropy, s/S, is the primary target variable. We explore the effect of collision impact parameter, i.e., fireball size, on kinetic strangeness chemical equilibration in QGP. Insights gained in study the RHIC data with regard to the dynamics of the fireball are applied to the study strangeness production at the LHC. We use these results and consider the strange hadron relative particle yields at RHIC and LHC in a systematic fashion. We consider both the dependence on s/S and directly participant number dependence.

## Key claim

The specific yield of strangeness per entropy, \(s/S\), is shown to be a nearly model-independent observable for thermal strangeness production in QGP, with the final value at RHIC converging to \(s/S \simeq 0.033\) in central collisions, though the paper acknowledges that this result depends on the assumption that entropy is conserved during the strangeness production period and that initial conditions (such as initial temperature and direct strangeness yield) have only minor impact on the final result.

## Method

The authors formulate kinetic equations describing the growth of specific strangeness per entropy \(s/S\) in a dynamically expanding QGP fireball, using thermally averaged cross sections for gluon fusion (\(gg \to s\bar s\)) and quark-antiquark annihilation (\(q\bar q \to s\bar s\)) with a multiplicative \(K=1.7\) factor to account for higher-order QCD effects. They evolve the system in proper time assuming entropy conservation, using a running QCD coupling constant \(\alpha_s\) fixed to \(\alpha_s(m_{Z^0})=0.118\) and a strange quark mass \(m_s(\mu=2\ \text{GeV})=0.10\) GeV, and model the volume expansion with both longitudinal and transverse components to derive the time dependence of temperature and strangeness occupancy \(\gamma_s\).

## Key numbers

- The specific strangeness per entropy in most central Au–Au collisions at RHIC (\(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=200\) GeV) converges to \(s/S \simeq 0.033\).
- In most peripheral RHIC reactions (participant number \(\langle A\rangle = 6.3\)), the observed value is \(s/S = 0.019\), implying a thermal enhancement factor of \(1.9 \pm 0.3\) for central collisions.
- The entropy yield at RHIC is \(S \simeq 35,000\) for Au–Au reactions at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=200\) GeV, with central rapidity yield \(dS/dy \simeq 5000\); for LHC the central rapidity entropy yield is assumed to be about 4 times greater than at RHIC.
- The initial thermalization time assumed is \(\tau_0 = 0.25\) fm for RHIC and \(\tau_0 = 0.1\) fm for LHC, with a range explored \(0.1 < \tau_0 < 1.5\) fm/\(c\).
- The QCD coupling at the critical temperature is \(\alpha_s(T_c) = 0.50 \pm 0.04\) at \(T_c = 0.16\) GeV, with running parameter \(C = 0.760 \pm 0.002\).
- The strange quark mass used is \(m_s(\mu=2\ \text{GeV}) = 0.10\) GeV, with a 25% uncertainty; a more recent PDG evaluation recommends a 10% smaller central value.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of strangeness chemical equilibration in QGP, notably the early treatment by Kapusta (1986) which used different dense matter evolution models and statistical parameters not appropriate for RHIC and LHC energies, and the authors' own earlier schematic model (Rafelski, 1999). The paper serves as a theoretical companion to phenomenological analyses of RHIC data (Rafelski, 2004; Letessier, 2005) and extrapolates to LHC predictions, where prior work (Rafelski, 2005) was based on parametric final-state strangeness yields. The results enable direct connection between QGP evolution and experimental hadron yields, particularly for multistrange hadrons and the \(K^+/\pi^+\) ratio, and distinguish the kinetic strangeness production mechanism from the "canonical enhancement" model (Braun-Munzinger, 2003) which derives enhancement from hadronic phase chemical equilibrium.
