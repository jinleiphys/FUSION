# Dynamical Phase Transitions in models of Collective Neutrino Oscillations

> Collective neutrino oscillations can potentially play an important role in transporting lepton flavor in astrophysical scenarios where the neutrino density is large, typical examples are the early universe and supernova explosions. It has been argued in the past that simple models of the neutrino Hamiltonian designed to describe forward scattering can support substantial flavor evolution on very short time scales $t\approx\log(N)/(G_Fρ_ν)$, with $N$ the number of neutrinos, $G_F$ the Fermi constant and $ρ_ν$ the neutrino density. This finding is in tension with results for similar but exactly solvable models for which $t\approx\sqrt{N}/(G_Fρ_ν)$ instead. In this work we provide a coherent explanation of this tension in terms of Dynamical Phase Transitions (DPT) and study the possible impact that a DPT could have in more realistic models of neutrino oscillations and their mean-field approximation.

## Key claim

The tension between earlier claims of fast flavor evolution on timescales \(t \approx \log(N)/(G_F\rho_ν)\) and later exact-solvable-model results giving \(t \approx \sqrt{N}/(G_F\rho_ν)\) is resolved by identifying a Dynamical Phase Transition (DPT) in the spin model; however, the fast oscillations seen in some models arise only when the global \(SU(2)\) flavor invariance of the neutrino Hamiltonian is explicitly broken, and are therefore not directly relevant to physical neutrino systems.

## Method

The author maps the two-flavor neutrino forward-scattering Hamiltonian onto an \(SU(2)\) spin model with all-to-all Heisenberg coupling, then studies quantum quenches from a product initial state (half spins up, half down) to various final Hamiltonians. For the single-angle (isotropic) case, the dynamics is solved analytically via total angular momentum eigenstates and numerically with Time-Evolving Block Decimation (TEBD) using Matrix Product States; for the two-beam geometry with \(SU(2)\)-breaking perturbations, the Loschmidt echo rate function and half-chain entanglement entropy are computed to characterize Dynamical Phase Transitions.

## Key numbers

* For the single-angle \(SU(2)\)-invariant model, the time to reach the minimum of flavor persistence scales as \(t_P/\tau_L = 2.10(5)\) with \(\tau_L = \mu^{-1}\sqrt{N}\), and the minimum persistence value extrapolates to \(p_{min}=0.357(2)\) in the thermodynamic limit.
* The crossing time of the two Loschmidt echoes in the single-angle model is \(t^*/\tau_L = 1.34(2)\).
* For the \(SU(2)\)-broken two-beam model (Bell et al.), the time to the first minimum of flavor persistence follows \(\mu t_P = 2.04(5)\log(N) + 1.6(1)\), and the Loschmidt echo crossing time scales as \(t^*/\tau_F = 1.56(4)\) with \(\tau_F = \mu^{-1}\log(N)\).
* The half-chain entanglement entropy in the single-angle model reaches its maximum on a timescale \(t_{ent}(N) = a\sqrt{N} + b/\sqrt{N} + c/N\) with \(a\tau_L = 1.16(4)\); in the \(SU(2)\)-broken model, the entropy peaks occur at \(t^1_{ent}/\tau_F = 1.3(1)\) and \(t^2_{ent}/\tau_F = 3.9(1)\).

## Context

This work resolves a long-standing discrepancy between the fast timescale \(\tau_F \approx \mu^{-1}\log(N)\) reported by Bell, Rawlinson and Sawyer (2003) and Sawyer (2004) and the slower \(\tau_L \approx \mu^{-1}\sqrt{N}\) found by Friedland and Lunardini (2003) for the \(SU(2)\)-invariant model. By showing that the fast oscillations require explicit \(SU(2)\) breaking, the paper clarifies that the physical neutrino Hamiltonian (which preserves \(SU(2)\) flavor symmetry) does not support the fast collective speedup. The companion paper Roggero (2021) extends these results to intermediate densities where the vacuum term can reintroduce fast oscillations through a DPT, and the framework enables future study of multi-angle effects and connections to dynamical order parameters.
