# Liquid-gas phase transition and its order in finite nuclei

> The liquid-gas phase transition in finite nuclei is studied in a heated liquid-drop model where the drop is assumed to be in thermodynamic equilibrium with the vapour emanated from it. Changing pressure along the liquid-gas coexistence line of the systems, symmetric or asymmetric, suggests that the phase transition is a continuous one. This is further corroborated from the study of the thermal evolution of the entropy at constant pressure.

## Key claim

The liquid-gas phase transition in finite nuclei, both symmetric and asymmetric, is a continuous (second-order) transition, in contrast to the first-order transition seen in symmetric infinite nuclear matter; this conclusion is drawn from the continuous change of pressure along the coexistence line and the continuous thermal evolution of entropy at constant pressure, with the finite size and Coulomb effects being the dominant factors.

## Method

The authors employed a heated liquid-drop model in which a finite nucleus (e.g., \(^{186}\)Re) is treated as a spherical liquid drop in thermodynamic equilibrium with its own vapour, conserving total baryon number and isospin. The free energy of the system was constructed from a finite-temperature Thomas-Fermi framework using a modified Seyler-Blanchard interaction for infinite nuclear matter, supplemented by Coulomb free energies (uniform sphere for liquid, spherical shell for gas) and a temperature- and asymmetry-dependent surface free energy. Coexistence between liquid and gas phases was enforced by equating the neutron and proton chemical potentials and the pressures in both phases, and the resulting isotherms, isospin fractionation, heat capacity, and entropy evolution were analyzed.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{186}\)Re at \(T = 7\) MeV, the minimum liquid-drop mass along the coexistence line is \(A^l = 24\); at \(T = 10\) MeV, the limiting liquid-drop mass is \(A^l = 130\).
- The surface energy coefficient is given by \(\sigma(X=0) = 18\) MeV, with \(a_s = 28.66\) MeV and the critical temperature of symmetric nuclear matter \(T_c = 15\) MeV.
- The heat capacity per particle \(C_V\) for \(^{186}\)Re at a freeze-out volume \(V = 10 V_0\) (with \(V_0\) from radius parameter \(r_0 = 1.16\) fm) shows a broad bump with a maximum at \(T \sim 10\) MeV, corresponding to a liquid part with \(A^l \approx 80\).
- At constant pressure \(P = 0.06\) MeV fm\(^{-3}\), the entropy per particle for \(^{186}\)Re rises continuously in the temperature range 8.4 to 10.3 MeV, indicating the phase transition domain.
- At very high temperature, the heat capacity saturates at 1.5, corresponding to a pure classical gas.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of liquid-gas phase transitions from infinite nuclear matter to finite nuclei, addressing conflicting predictions from prior models: the EOS group (Elliot et al., 2002) and ISiS group (Klein et al., 2001) found evidence for a continuous transition, while lattice-gas models (Gulminelli et al., 1999; Pan et al., 1998) were compatible with a first-order transition. The study builds on the relativistic mean-field calculations of asymmetric nuclear matter by Muller and Serot (1995) and the nonrelativistic work of Kolomietz et al. (2001), showing that finite size and Coulomb effects make even symmetric nuclei behave like two-component systems, leading to a continuous transition. The results enable experimental tests, such as the predicted preponderance of proton-rich isotopes in energetic heavy-ion collisions from symmetric medium-heavy nuclei.
