# Large Lorentz Scalar and Vector Potentials in Nuclei

> In nonrelativistic models of nuclei, the underlying mass scales of low-energy quantum chromodynamics (QCD) are largely hidden. In contrast, the covariant formulations used in relativistic phenomenology manifest the QCD scales in nuclei through large Lorentz scalar and four-vector nucleon self-energies. The abundant and varied evidence in support of this connection and the consequences are reviewed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that large Lorentz scalar and vector nucleon self-energies (several hundred MeV in heavy nuclei) are natural manifestations of low-energy QCD mass scales, and that the success of nonrelativistic nuclear phenomenology provides little direct evidence about these covariant potentials because the leading-order scalar and vector contributions cancel in the nonrelativistic central potential.

## Method

The authors review evidence from covariant density functional theory (DFT) applied to nuclei, where energy functionals of the ground-state scalar density and baryon current are extremized to produce Dirac single-particle Hamiltonians with local scalar and vector fields. They apply naive dimensional analysis (NDA) and naturalness principles, which prescribe counting powers of the pion decay constant \(f_\pi \approx 94\) MeV and a larger mass scale \(\Lambda\) in effective Lagrangians or energy functionals, to test whether coefficients in relativistic mean-field models are consistent with QCD scale expectations.

## Key numbers

* Scalar and vector potentials \(\Phi\) and \(W\) are always larger than 300 MeV in the interior of a nucleus, except when a large isoscalar tensor coupling is included.
* With an isoscalar tensor coupling, the scalar and vector potentials can be reduced to roughly 250 MeV.
* The leading scalar and vector terms at equilibrium density \(\rho_B^0\) are each predicted by NDA to be of order \(\rho_B^0 / f_\pi^2 \approx 150\) MeV.
* The pion decay constant is \(f_\pi \approx 94\) MeV.

## Context

This review extends the work of Friar, Lynn, and Madland (1996), who first pointed out the signature of low-energy QCD scales in relativistic point-coupling model coefficients, and subsequent analyses by Furnstahl (1997), Rusnak (1997), and others that tested NDA power counting in both relativistic and nonrelativistic mean-field models. The paper argues that the EFT/DFT perspective abrogates longstanding criticisms of relativistic approaches by Brodsky (1984), Negele (1985), Brown (1987), and others, and that a covariant treatment with distinct scalar and four-vector self-energies offers greater physical insight than nonrelativistic formulations.
