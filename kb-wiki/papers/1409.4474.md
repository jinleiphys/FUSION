# On the two remaning issues in the gauge-invariant decomposition problem of the nucleon spin

> The question whether the total gluon angular momentum in the nucleon can be decomposed into its spin and orbital parts without conflict with the gauge-invariance principle has been an object of long-lasting debate. Despite a remarkable progress achieved through the recent intensive researches, the following two issues still remains to be clarified more transparently. The first issue is to resolve the apparent conflict between the proposed gauge-invariant decomposition of the total gluon angular momentum and the textbook statement that the total angular momentum of the photon cannot be gauge-invariantly decomposed into its spin and orbital parts. We show that this problem is also inseparably connected with the uniqueness or non-uniqueness problem of the nucleon spin decomposition. The second practically more important issue is that, among the two physically inequivalent decompositions of the nucleon spin, i.e. the "canonical" type decomposition and the "mechanical" type decomposition, which can we say is more physical or closer to direct observation ? In the present paper, we try to answer both these questions as clearly as possible.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the apparent conflict between gauge-invariant decomposition of gluon angular momentum and textbook statements about photon angular momentum is resolved by recognizing that the decomposition requires fixing a Lorentz frame; the key additional condition that uniquely selects a physical decomposition for the nucleon is Lorentz-boost invariance along the direction of the nucleon momentum, not gauge invariance alone.

## Method

The author compares several gauge-invariant decompositions of the nucleon spin, the "canonical" type (including the Chen decomposition and the Bashinsky-Jaffe decomposition) and the "mechanical" type, by analyzing their transformation properties under Lorentz boosts along the nucleon momentum direction. He examines different definitions of the physical component of the gluon field (light-cone gauge motivated, temporal-gauge motivated, spatial axial-gauge motivated, and Coulomb-gauge motivated) and tests which ones remain invariant under a boost along the nucleon momentum. He then connects this analysis to the standard definition of the longitudinally polarized gluon distribution from deep-inelastic scattering (DIS) and shows that only the light-cone gauge motivated definition satisfies the required boost invariance.

## Key numbers

- The 1-loop anomalous dimension for the gluon spin operator is the same for all three axial-gauge motivated definitions: \(1 + \frac{\alpha_S}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{\beta_0}{\varepsilon}\), where \(\beta_0 = 11 - 2 n_f / 3\).
- Under a Lorentz boost with velocity \(v\) along the nucleon momentum direction, the light-cone gauge motivated physical component \(A^k_{phys} = \frac{1}{D^+} F^{+k}\) is invariant, while the temporal-gauge motivated component transforms as \(\frac{1}{D^0 - v D^3} (F^{0k} - v F^{3k})\) and is not boost-invariant.
- The first moment of the longitudinally polarized gluon distribution \(\Delta G\) is expressed as \(\frac{1}{2 P^+} \langle P S | 2 \text{Tr} [ \epsilon^{jk}_\perp F^{j+}(0) A^k_{phys}(0) ] | P S \rangle\), with \(A^k_{phys}(0) = -\frac{1}{2} \int d\xi^- \epsilon(\xi^-) \mathcal{L}[0,\xi^-] F^{+k}(\xi^-) \mathcal{L}[\xi^-,0]\).

## Context

This work extends the debate on nucleon spin decomposition initiated by Jaffe and Manohar (1990) and the Chen et al. (2008, 2009) gauge-invariant decomposition, addressing criticisms by Ji, Xu, and Zhao (2012, 2013) and Lorce (2013, 2014) that there exist infinitely many such decompositions. The paper resolves the uniqueness problem by showing that the Lorentz-boost invariance condition selects the light-cone gauge motivated decomposition as the one relevant for DIS measurements, analogous to how the paraxial laser beam direction enables separate measurement of photon spin and orbital angular momentum as discussed by Van Enk and Nienhuis (1994). This enables a clear distinction between the "canonical" and "mechanical" decompositions and identifies which one corresponds to observable quantities in high-energy scattering experiments.
