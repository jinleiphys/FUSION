# Zipf's law in Multifragmentation

> We discuss the meaning of Zipf's law in nuclear multifragmentation. We remark that Zipf's law is a consequence of a power law fragment size distribution with exponent $τ\simeq 2$. We also recall why the presence of such distribution is not a reliable signal of a liquid-gas phase transition.

# Zipf's law in Multifragmentation

## Key claim
Zipf's law in nuclear multifragmentation is merely a mathematical consequence of a power-law fragment size distribution with exponent τ ≃ 2, and its observation is neither a new nor independent signal of critical behavior, nor an unambiguous signal of a liquid-gas phase transition.

## Method
The authors derive analytically that the Zipf exponent λ = 1/(τ − 1) for a power-law cluster size distribution, then verify this relation numerically using random partitions of an integer N with a prescribed power-law cluster size distribution. They also perform bond-percolation calculations with N = 100 occupied sites, fitting the mean size of clusters of rank r as a function of distance to the critical bond parameter ε = p_c − p, and compare the χ² deviation for fits with λ free versus λ fixed to 1.

## Key numbers
- For a power-law cluster size distribution with τ = 2.2, the best fit of the mean size versus rank gives λ ≃ 0.86.
- In bond-percolation calculations with N = 100, the minimum χ² occurs at λ ≃ 0.90 for a corresponding cluster size distribution slope τ ≃ 2.16, agreeing with the relation λ = 1/(τ − 1) within 4%.
- The fit with λ fixed to 1 (strict Zipf's law) violates the relation by 26% and gives an incorrect localization of the critical point.
- For the lattice-gas model with Coniglio-Klein clusters, the expected Zipf exponent is λ ≃ 0.83.
- For finite samplings with N ≃ 100 and 2 ≤ τ ≤ 3, the relation λ = 1/(τ − 1) is fulfilled within a few percent.

## Context
This work extends the discussion initiated by Ma (2004) who suggested that Zipf's law is a good signal of the liquid-gas phase transition in the isospin-dependent lattice gas model, and by subsequent experimental observations in nuclear fragmentation data (Ma, 2004; Dabrowska, 2004). The authors show that power-law cluster size distributions with τ ≃ 2 appear at the critical points of many theories (Fischer droplets, lattice-gas, percolation, Lennard-Jones fluids) but also in super-critical regions, so Zipf's law cannot serve as an unambiguous phase transition signal. This enables a more cautious interpretation of Zipf-like scaling in multifragmentation experiments.
