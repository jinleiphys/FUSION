# Vector Color Transparency

> Color transparency (CT) in high momentum transfer $(e,e' \vec p)$ reactions is explored. The spin of the proton and photon are treated explicitly, hence the name ``Vector CT". The Dirac distorted wave impulse approximation is used as a starting point; then CT effects are embedded. A hadronic basis is used to describe the struck proton as a wavepacket of physical baryon resonances. The effects of the wavepacket expansion on the normal component of the ejectile polarization, which vanishes in the limit of full CT, are investigated. This formalism is also applied to study CT effects in total cross sections, individual separated nuclear response functions, Fermi motion of the initial nucleon, non-zero size of the initial wavepacket and the effects of relativistic lower components. We show that including CT reduces the violations of current conservation (CC), a typical problem in calculations of this kind. The energy dependence of the normal polarization in $(e,e' \vec p)$ reactions is found to be slow. However, a measurement of the normal transverse response in a heavy nucleus, such as $^{208}Pb$ seems to afford the opportunity to see CT at quite low momentum transfers. The effects of Fermi motion are investigated, and choosing the momentum of the struck nucleon to be large leads to significant violations of CC.

## Key claim

Color transparency (CT) effects in high momentum transfer \((e,e' \vec p)\) reactions are explored by explicitly treating the spin of the proton and photon, showing that the normal component of the ejectile polarization vanishes in the limit of full CT but that a measurement of the normal transverse response in a heavy nucleus such as \({}^{208}\)Pb may allow observation of CT at quite low momentum transfers, even though the energy dependence of the normal polarization is found to be slow.

## Method

The authors use the Dirac distorted wave impulse approximation as a starting point and embed CT effects by describing the struck proton as a wavepacket of physical baryon resonances in a hadronic basis. They compute the nuclear current matrix element using four-component Dirac spinors for both bound and scattered states, with the scattered wavefunction obtained from an eikonal solution of the Dirac equation incorporating scalar and vector optical potentials. The formalism is applied to compute total cross sections, individual separated nuclear response functions, the normal component of the ejectile polarization, and the effects of Fermi motion and relativistic lower components.

## Key numbers

- The dipole form factors used are \(G_E(q^2) = (1 - q^2 / 0.71\,\text{GeV}^2)^{-2}\) and \(G_M(q^2) = (1 + \kappa) G_E(q^2)\) with \(\kappa = 1.79\) (the anomalous magnetic moment of the proton).
- Current conservation violations are maintained at the \(<10\%\) level or better in the calculation.
- The normal component of the polarization \(P_n^0\) vanishes in the limit of full CT; the energy dependence of this normal polarization in \((e,e' \vec p)\) reactions is found to be slow.
- A measurement of the normal transverse response in a heavy nucleus such as \({}^{208}\)Pb seems to afford the opportunity to see CT at quite low momentum transfers.

## Context

This work extends the earlier multiple-scattering Glauber framework for color transparency developed by Greenberg and Miller (1993) by incorporating full spin dependence and relativistic Dirac wavefunctions, whereas previous calculations of CT had totally neglected spin effects. The formalism is designed to provide theoretical predictions for an approved CEBAF experiment (Saha and collaborators) that will measure the unpolarized cross section and the normal component of the ejected proton's polarization, enabling direct comparison with experimental results. The approach uses the QHD model of Horowitz and Serot (1981) for relativistic bound state wavefunctions and the relativistic impulse approximation for optical potentials, building on the Dirac phenomenology developed in the early 1980s by Clark, Hama, Mercer, and others.
