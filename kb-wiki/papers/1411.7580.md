# Bottomonium in the plasma: lattice results

> We present results on the heavy quarkonium spectrum and spectral functions obtained by performing large-scale simulations of QCD for temperatures ranging from about 100 to 500 MeV, in the same range as those explored by LHC experiments. We discuss our method and perspectives for further improvements towards the goal of full control over the many systematic uncertainties of these studies.

## Key claim

The fundamental bottomonium S-wave state (Υ) survives up to at least twice the critical temperature, while excited states dissolve, consistent with CMS observations; however, the analysis still faces significant systematic uncertainties from the spectral function reconstruction method and the use of unphysically heavy light quarks.

## Method

The authors performed lattice QCD simulations with two active light quarks (with mπ/mρ ≈ 0.4) and one heavier quark near the strange mass, for temperatures from about 100 to 500 MeV. Bottomonium correlators were computed using the NRQCD effective field theory, which removes the heavy quark rest mass and allows coarser lattices. Spectral functions were then reconstructed from the Euclidean-time correlators using the Maximum Entropy Method (MEM), with systematic checks against the choice of default model and the number of time slices.

## Key numbers

- Temperature range explored: approximately 100 to 500 MeV, covering 0.76 Tc to 1.9 Tc.
- Tuned spin-averaged 1S kinetic mass: M2(‾1S) = 9560(110) MeV, consistent with the experimental value of 9444.7(8) MeV.
- The heavy quark mass corresponds to ω ∼ 2M ∼ 8 GeV for b quarks, much larger than the maximum temperature T < 0.5 GeV.
- At the highest accessible temperature T/Tc = 1.90, thermal modifications in the P-wave χb1 channel are much larger than in the S-wave Υ channel.

## Context

This work extends earlier lattice studies of bottomonium with two active flavors (Aarts 2011, Aarts 2012) by including a dynamical strange quark, finding broadly consistent results. The results provide a theoretical baseline for interpreting heavy quarkonium suppression observed at LHC experiments (CMS, ALICE, PHENIX), and enable future studies toward physical light-quark masses and full control over spectral function reconstruction, including cross-checks with the novel Bayesian method of Burnier and Rothkopf (2013).
