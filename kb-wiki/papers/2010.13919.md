# Extension of the Hauser-Feshbach Fission Fragment Decay Model to Multi-Chance Fission

> The Hauser-Feshbach fission fragment decay model, $\mathtt{HF^3D}$, which calculates the statistical decay of fission fragments, has been expanded to include multi-chance fission, up to neutron incident energies of 20 MeV. The deterministic decay takes as input pre-scission quantities - fission probabilities and the average energy causing fission - and post-scission quantities - yields in mass, charge, total kinetic energy, spin, and parity. From these fission fragment initial conditions, the full decay is followed through both prompt and delayed particle emissions, allowing for the calculation of prompt neutron and $γ$ properties, such as multiplicity and energy distributions, both independent and cumulative fission yields, and delayed neutron observables. In this work, we describe the implementation of multi-chance fission into the $\mathtt{HF^3D}$ model, and show an example of prompt and delayed quantities beyond first-chance fission, using the example of neutron-induced fission on $^{235}$U. This expansion represents significant progress in consistently modeling the emission of prompt and delayed particles from fissile systems.

## Key claim

The Hauser-Feshbach Fission Fragment Decay model (HF³D) implemented in the BeoH code has been extended to handle multi-chance fission up to 20 MeV incident neutron energy, but the authors state that the work demonstrates viability of the extension rather than fully optimized input yield models, with more thorough optimizations deferred to future work.

## Method

The pre-scission stage (multi-chance fission probabilities and average excitation energy causing fission) is calculated using the statistical Hauser-Feshbach code CoH, which computes fission cross sections for each chance channel. The post-scission stage uses the BeoH code with the HF³D model, which takes parametrized initial fission fragment distributions (mass yield, charge yield, total kinetic energy, spin, and parity) and performs deterministic statistical Hauser-Feshbach decay calculations for each fragment, tracking both prompt neutron and gamma emission as well as delayed neutron emission through beta-decay chains using the ENDF/B-VIII.0 decay data library.

## Key numbers

- Multi-chance fission probabilities are calculated for ²³⁵U(n,f) from thermal to 20 MeV incident energy, with first-, second-, third-, and fourth-chance fission channels included
- The average excitation energy causing fission for each chance channel increases essentially linearly, but the probability-weighted average across all channels remains relatively low (approximately 10 MeV) due to cooling by pre-fission neutron emission
- The total kinetic energy (TKE) parametrization uses a piecewise linear function with a break point at energy E₀, with parameters a, b, d fitted to experimental data
- Mass yield parametrizations are separately constrained for ²³⁵U(n,f), ²³⁴U(n,f), and ²³³U(n,f) as functions of incident neutron energy; around 19-20 MeV when fourth-chance fission opens, the ²³³U(n,f) parametrization is used with A_c=234 shifted to A_c-1=233
- The spin cut-off parameter and charge distribution factors were tuned to chain yield data from the England and Rider evaluation for A = 132, 133, 135, 136, 143, 144, 145, 148, along with prompt and delayed average neutron multiplicities from ENDF/B-VIII.0

## Context

This work extends the deterministic HF³D model previously studied by Okumura et al. (2018) for ²³⁵U(n,f) below the second-chance fission threshold, where they reproduced prompt observables including independent yields, TKE distribution, average prompt neutron multiplicity as a function of incident energy and mass, neutron multiplicity distribution, and isomeric ratios. The extension enables consistent calculation of prompt and delayed fission observables up to 20 MeV incident energy, addressing the known discrepancy between independently evaluated fission quantities noted by Jaffke et al. (2018). The model can now be applied to major actinides beyond ²³⁵U in future work, with further optimization of the input yield models still underway.
