# Structural Change of Cooper Pairs and Momentum-dependent Gap in Color Superconductivity

> The two-flavor color superconductivity is studied over a wide range of baryon density with a single model. We pay a special attention to the spatial-momentum dependence of the gap and to the spatial-structure of Cooper pairs. At extremely high baryon density (O(10^{10} rho_0) with rho_0 being the normal nuclear matter density), our model becomes equivalent to the usual perturbative QCD treatment and the gap is shown to have a sharp peak near the Fermi surface due to the weak-coupling nature of QCD. On the other hand, the gap is a smooth function of the momentum at lower densities (O(10 rho_0)) due to strong color magnetic and electric interactions. To study the structural change of Cooper pairs from high density to lower density, quark correlation in the color superconductor is studied both in the momentum space and in the coordinate space. The size of the Cooper pair is shown to become comparable to the averaged inter-quark distance at low densities. Also, effects of the momentum-dependent running coupling and the antiquark pairing, which are both small at high density, are shown to be non-negligible at low densities. These features are highly contrasted to the standard BCS superconductivity in metals.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the momentum dependence of the superconducting gap in two-flavor color superconductivity undergoes a qualitative structural change from a sharp peak near the Fermi surface at extremely high baryon density (∼10¹⁰ ρ₀) to a smooth function of momentum at lower densities (∼10 ρ₀), with the condition that this transition arises from the interplay between weak-coupling QCD at high density and strong color magnetic and electric interactions at lower density.

## Method

The authors solve the one-loop Schwinger-Dyson (gap) equation for the momentum-dependent superconducting gap Δ₊(k) in the improved ladder approximation, using a static gluon propagator with Debye screening for the electric part and Landau damping for the magnetic part, together with an infrared-safe running coupling g²(q,k). They solve the coupled gap equations for both quark and antiquark pairing channels (Δ₊ and Δ₋) numerically over a wide range of baryon chemical potential, from μ = 2¹²Λ (∼1.6 TeV) down to lower densities, and compute the quark occupation numbers, momentum-space and coordinate-space correlation functions, and the coherence length from the resulting gap functions.

## Key numbers

- At extremely high baryon density ρ_B = 1.1 × 10¹¹ ρ₀ (μ = 2¹²Λ ≃ 1.6 TeV), the gap has a very narrow peak at the Fermi surface with a plateau width of 2Δ₊(k=μ)
- The color-electric interaction enhances the gap by about a factor of 10 compared to the magnetic interaction alone at high density
- The asymptotic solution for k ≫ μ gives a power-law decay Δ₊(k) ∝ k^(−λ) with λ = 1 + √(1 − g²/3π²), which at weak coupling (g ≪ 1) behaves as 1/k²
- The weak-coupling analytic solution near the Fermi surface is Δ₊(μ) ≅ 2b μ exp(−3π²/√2 g) with b = (2/N_f)(32π/g²)
- The Debye mass is m_D² = (N_f/2π²) g² μ² and the Landau damping scale is M² = (π/4) m_D²
- The running coupling parameters are Λ = 400 MeV and p_c² = 1.5 Λ², determined from vacuum meson properties
- At low densities (∼10 ρ₀), the Cooper pair size becomes comparable to the averaged inter-quark distance

## Context

This work bridges two previously separate theoretical approaches to color superconductivity: the perturbative one-gluon-exchange Schwinger-Dyson analysis valid at asymptotically high densities (Son 1998, Schaefer, Hong, Pisarski and Rischke) and the mean-field 4-Fermi model studies applicable at lower densities (Alford, Rajagopal and Wilczek 1998; Rapp, Schäfer, Shuryak and Velkovsky 1998; Berges and Rajagopal; Diakonov). The paper extends earlier investigations of momentum-dependent gaps by Iwasaki, Horie, and Matsuzaki, and enables future studies of the transition from weak-coupling BCS-like behavior to strong-coupling color superconductivity where the scale hierarchy between the gap and the Fermi momentum breaks down.
