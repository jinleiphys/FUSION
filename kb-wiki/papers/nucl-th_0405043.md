# Hidden evidence of non-exponential nuclear decay

> The framework to describe natural phenomena at their basics being quantum mechanics, there exist a large number of common global phenomena occurring in different branches of natural sciences. One such global phenomenon is spontaneous quantum decay. However, its long time behaviour is experimentally poorly known. Here we show, that by combining two genuine quantum mechanical results, it is possible to infer on this large time behaviour, directly from data. Specifically, we find evidence for non-exponential behaviour of alpha decay of 8Be at large times from experiments.

# Hidden evidence of non-exponential nuclear decay

## Key claim

By combining the Fock-Krylov method for survival amplitude with the Beth-Uhlenbeck result linking density of states to the energy derivative of scattering phase shifts, the authors infer non-exponential behavior of alpha decay of 8Be at large times directly from experimental scattering data, without requiring direct decay measurements at those times.

## Method

The authors use experimental D-wave phase shifts from alpha-alpha elastic scattering to extract the spectral function of the 8Be(2+) resonance via the Beth-Uhlenbeck relation, which identifies the continuum probability density of states with the energy derivative of the scattering phase shift. They parametrize the phase shift data near threshold with an energy-dependent Breit-Wigner form, compute the survival amplitude as a Fourier transform of this spectral function, and evaluate the survival probability numerically. The large-time behavior is extracted from the threshold exponent of the spectral function.

## Key numbers

- The survival probability of the 8Be(2+) state at large times behaves as P(t) ~ 1/t^6.36
- The best-fit parameters for the phase shift parametrization are: kappa = 6.36, beta = 0.00359 GeV^-1, Gamma_0 = 0.0009 GeV, E_0 = 7.45838 GeV
- The deviation from exponential decay begins around 30 lifetimes after the onset of decay, by which time the sample has depleted by approximately 13 orders of magnitude (e^-30)
- The fitted exponent kappa = 6.36 corresponds to an effective l-value of 2.68, compared to the theoretical expectation of 2l+1 = 5 for l=2

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of Fock and Krylov for quantum decay by connecting it to the Beth-Uhlenbeck result from statistical mechanics, which had been overlooked in the context of unstable nuclear states. The method enables inference of large-time decay behavior from scattering data, circumventing the experimental impossibility of direct measurement at large times (as noted by Rutherford, Butt, and Norman in earlier work). The result contradicts the expectation stated in Nature (2002) that nuclear decays are unlikely to show deviations from exponential behavior, and enables future studies of non-exponential decay in other resonances where threshold phase shift data are available.
