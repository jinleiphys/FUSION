# A Rotation/Magnetism Analogy for the Quark-Gluon Plasma

> In peripheral heavy ion collisions, the Quark-Gluon Plasma that may be formed often has a large angular momentum per unit energy. This angular momentum may take the form of (local) rotation. In many physical systems, rotation can have effects analogous to those produced by a magnetic field; thus, there is a risk that the effects of local rotation in the QGP might be mistaken for those of the large genuine magnetic fields which are also known to arise in these systems. Here we use the gauge-gravity duality to investigate this, and we find indeed that, with realistic parameter values, local rotation has effects on the QGP (at high values of the baryonic chemical potential) which are not only of the same kind as those produced by magnetic fields, but which can in fact be substantially larger. Furthermore, the combined effect of rotation and magnetism is to change the shape of the main quark matter phase transition line in an interesting way, reducing the magnitude of its curvature; again, local rotation contributes to this phenomenon at least as strongly as magnetism.

## Key claim
Using gauge-gravity duality, the paper finds that local rotation in the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) at high baryonic chemical potential has effects of the same kind as those produced by magnetic fields, but can be substantially larger; ignoring local rotation could therefore lead to serious errors when estimating magnetic field effects on the plasma.

## Method
The authors employ the gauge-gravity (holographic) correspondence, mapping the rotating, magnetized QGP to a dyonic (electrically and magnetically charged) AdS-Kerr-Newman black hole with a topologically spherical event horizon. They derive a holographic dictionary relating boundary parameters (temperature, baryonic chemical potential, magnetic field, specific angular momentum, specific entropy) to black hole quantities, then solve the resulting pair of algebraic equations numerically for the temperature as a function of specific angular momentum and magnetic field, holding specific entropy and the ratio mu_B/T fixed.

## Key numbers
- The critical endpoint temperature at zero magnetic field and zero angular momentum is taken as T_0^CEP ≈ 140 MeV, with mu_B_0^CEP ≈ 280 MeV (giving Gamma ≈ 2).
- Magnetic fields are considered from zero up to 15 B_pi^2 (units of the squared pion mass), with B_pi^2 from Skokov et al.
- Specific angular momentum a is varied from zero up to 50 fm.
- At a = 0, B = 0: T = 140 MeV; at a = 50 fm, B = 0: T = 88.3 MeV; at a = 0, B = 15 B_pi^2: T = 132.0 MeV; at a = 50 fm, B = 15 B_pi^2: T < 40 MeV.
- The effect of increasing a completely dominates the effect of increasing B: at a = 30 fm, B = 0, T = 121.1 MeV; at a = 0, B = 10 B_pi^2, T = 136.7 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the study of magnetic field effects on the QGP phase diagram, such as the inverse magnetic catalysis found in lattice computations by Bali et al., and the analysis of angular momentum in heavy-ion collisions by Deng et al. and Yin et al. It contrasts with the case of shearing angular momentum studied by McInnes (2016), where increasing angular momentum sometimes increased temperature. The results indicate that local rotation must be accounted for in beam energy scan experiments (RHIC, GSI-FAIR, NICA) when interpreting the location of the critical endpoint and the curvature of the phase transition line in peripheral collisions.
