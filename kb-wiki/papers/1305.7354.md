# Effects of fermionic dark matter on properties of neutron stars

> By assuming that only gravitation exists between dark matter (DM) and normal matter (NM), we study the effects of fermionic DM on the properties of neutron stars using the two-fluid Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff formalism. It is found that the mass-radius relationship of the DM admixed neutron stars (DANSs) depends sensitively on the mass of DM candidates, the amount of DM, and interactions among DM candidates. The existence of DM in DANSs results in a spread of mass-radius relationships that cannot be interpreted with a unique equilibrium sequence. In some cases, the DM distribution can surpass the NM distribution to form DM halo. In particular, it is favorable to form an explicit DM halo, provided the repulsion of DM exists. It is interesting to find that the difference in particle number density distributions in DANSs and consequently in star radii caused by various density dependencies of nuclear symmetry energy tends to disappear as long as the repulsion of accumulated DM is sufficient. These phenomena indicate that the admixture of DM in neutron stars can significantly affect the astrophysical extraction of nuclear equation of state by virtue of neutron star measurements. In addition, the effect of the DM admixture on the star maximum mass is also investigated.

## Key claim

The mass-radius relationship of dark-matter admixed neutron stars (DANSs) depends sensitively on the mass of fermionic dark matter candidates, the amount of dark matter, and interactions among dark matter candidates; the existence of dark matter results in a spread of mass-radius relationships that cannot be interpreted with a unique equilibrium sequence, and the difference in star radii caused by various density dependencies of nuclear symmetry energy tends to disappear when the repulsion of accumulated dark matter is sufficient.

## Method

The authors used the two-fluid Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff formalism, assuming only gravitational interaction between dark matter and normal matter, to model static, spherically symmetric neutron stars containing fermionic dark matter. For normal matter, they employed relativistic mean-field models (density-dependent SLC and SLCd, and nonlinear IU-FSU) with beta equilibrium and charge neutrality, while dark matter was modeled as a fermion field with scalar and vector meson interactions described by a renormalizable Lagrangian. The two-fluid TOV equations were solved with equal central energy densities for dark matter and normal matter as a baseline, then varied.

## Key numbers

- For a dark matter candidate mass of 0.5 GeV, the minimum mass of DANSs is at least 1.32 solar masses (SLC model) or 1.38 solar masses (IU-FSU model) when central energy densities are equal.
- For a 1 GeV free dark matter candidate, the maximum mass of a DANS with the SLC model is reduced from 2.02 solar masses (no dark matter) to 1.82 solar masses; with IU-FSU, from 1.94 solar masses to 1.44 solar masses.
- For a pure dark matter star with candidate mass 0.5 GeV, the maximum mass is 2.5 solar masses and the corresponding radius is 32.46 km, derived from the scaling relation M_max = 0.627 solar masses * (1 GeV / M_D)^2 and R_D = 8.115 km * (1 GeV / M_D)^2.
- For a neutron star and a DANS both having mass 1.35 solar masses and total radius 12.76 km (SLC model, 0.5 GeV dark matter), the visible radius of the normal matter core in the DANS is 9 km, yielding gravitational redshifts of 0.206 for the normal neutron star and 0.340 for the DANS.

## Context

This work extends the two-fluid approach of Sandin and Ciarcelluti (2009) and Leung et al. (2011) by considering a broad variety of fermionic dark matter masses and interactions, including scalar and vector meson exchanges, rather than only mirror dark matter or free particles. The results show that dark matter admixture can significantly affect the astrophysical extraction of the nuclear equation of state from neutron star measurements, and that the presence of dark matter with repulsive interactions favors the formation of explicit dark matter halos, which could be distinguished from normal neutron stars through gravitational redshift measurements.
