# Strangeness in Neutron Stars

> It is generally agreed on that the tremendous densities reached in the centers of neutron stars provide a high-pressure environment in which numerous novel particles processes are likely to compete with each other. These processes range from the generation of hyperons to quark deconfinement to the formation of kaon condensates and H-matter. There are theoretical suggestions of even more exotic processes inside neutron stars, such as the formation of absolutely stable strange quark matter, a configuration of matter even more stable than the most stable atomic nucleus, iron. In the latter event, neutron stars would be largely composed of pure quark matter, eventually enveloped in a thin nuclear crust. No matter which physical processes are actually realized inside neutron stars, each one leads to fingerprints, some more pronounced than others though, in the observable stellar quantities. This feature combined with the unprecedented progress in observational astronomy, which allows us to see vistas with remarkable clarity that previously were only imagined, renders neutron stars to nearly ideal probes for a wide range of physical studies, including the role of strangeness in dense matter.

## Key claim

The paper argues that strangeness in the form of hyperons, kaon condensates, H-dibaryons, and deconfined strange quarks likely appears in neutron star cores at densities only a few times nuclear saturation density, but the actual realization of each process depends on uncertain in-medium properties and the specific stellar mass and rotation frequency.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical models for the equation of state of neutron star matter that incorporate strangeness-bearing components, including hyperons (Lambda, Sigma, Xi), kaon condensates, H-dibaryons, and deconfined three-flavor quark matter. It connects these models to observable stellar quantities such as mass-radius relations, moment of inertia, braking index, spin frequency distributions in accreting systems, and cooling curves, comparing predictions with data from radio telescopes and X-ray satellites.

## Key numbers

- Hyperons may appear at densities of just ~2 rho_0, where rho_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3} is the baryon number density of ordinary nuclear matter; the total hyperon population may be as large as 20%.
- The effective K- mass in dense matter may drop to m*_K- ~ 200 MeV at rho ~ 3 rho_0, based on an analysis of KaoS data from Ni+Ni collisions; the vacuum K- mass is m_K = 495 MeV.
- K- condensation could reduce the maximum neutron star mass from ~2 M_sun to about 1.5 M_sun.
- H-dibaryons with a vacuum mass of about 2.2 GeV and a moderately attractive medium potential of about -30 MeV could form a Bose condensate in neutron stars with limiting mass M = 1.444 M_sun (the Hulse-Taylor pulsar PSR 1913+16).
- Quark deconfinement in rotating neutron stars is predicted to occur at frequencies around 200 Hz (angular velocities ~1370 s^{-1}), with a possible spin-up era lasting ~10^8 years; about 10% of the ~25 solitary millisecond pulsars could be in the quark transition epoch.
- For absolutely stable strange quark matter, bag constants of B = 57.5 MeV/fm^3 (B^{1/4} = 145 MeV) and B = 85.3 MeV/fm^3 (B^{1/4} = 160 MeV) give energy per baryon of E/A = 829 MeV and 915 MeV, respectively, compared to 930.4 MeV for ^{56}Fe.
- The density of the nuclear crust of a strange star is always less than neutron drip density (4 x 10^{11} g/cm^3).
- Color superconductivity gaps and critical temperatures are estimated to be on the order of ~100 MeV.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on dense matter equations of state by Glen (1985, 1997, 1998, 1999) and Weber (1999) that incorporates strangeness degrees of freedom, and it connects to experimental constraints from the KaoS collaboration (Barth 1997) and Li (1997) on kaon masses. The paper enables future observational tests of strangeness in neutron stars through pulsar timing anomalies, spin frequency distributions in low-mass X-ray binaries (as observed with RXTE), and thermal cooling data, and it discusses the possibility of absolutely stable strange quark matter originally proposed by Bodmer (1971) and Witten (1984).
