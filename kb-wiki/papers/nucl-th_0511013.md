# The effects of deformation and pairing correlations on nuclear charge form factor

> A set of moderately deformed $s-d$ shell nuclei is employed for testing the reliability of the nuclear ground state wave functions which are obtained in the context of a BCS approach and offer a simultaneous consideration of deformation and pairing correlations effects. In this method, the mean field is assumed to be an axially symmetric Woods-Saxon potential and the effective two-body interaction is a monopole pairing force. As quantities of main interest we have chosen the nuclear form factors, the occupancies of the active (surface) orbits and the Fermi sea depletion, which provide quite good tests for microscopic descriptions of nuclei within many body theories. For our comparisons with results emerging from other similar methods, an axially deformed harmonic oscillator field is also utilized.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a BCS approach built on an axially deformed Woods-Saxon potential, with pairing strengths scaled to empirical separation energies, can simultaneously account for deformation and pairing correlations in moderately deformed s-d shell nuclei and reproduce experimental charge form factors significantly better than a deformed harmonic oscillator basis, especially for momentum transfers above 2 fm\(^{-1}\). However, the method systematically overestimates the Fermi sea depletion (by 40-130% compared to older experimental data), which the authors attribute to the absence of other correlation types and to the parameterization of the Woods-Saxon potential originally being tuned for spherical nuclei.

## Method

The authors construct BCS ground states for \(^{24,26}\)Mg, \(^{28}\)Si, and \(^{32}\)S using an axially symmetric Woods-Saxon mean field (parameterized after Tanaka (1979)) and a monopole pairing force whose strengths are fitted separately for protons and neutrons to reproduce empirical pairing gaps \(\Delta_{pp}=\Delta_{nn}=12.84/A^{1/2}\) MeV. The deformed Woods-Saxon single-particle wave functions are expanded in a deformed harmonic oscillator (DHO) basis, and the point-proton density is computed from the BCS occupation probabilities. The charge form factor is then obtained via the Born approximation with the momentum transfer \(\mathbf{q}\) taken along the symmetry axis. For comparison, the same quantities are also computed using a pure DHO basis and using a Slater determinant (no pairing) in the Woods-Saxon field.

## Key numbers

*   Deformation parameters \(\beta\) used for the four nuclei: \(\beta(^{24}\text{Mg}) = 0.416\) (from Lalazi), \(\beta(^{26}\text{Mg}) = 0.296\), \(\beta(^{28}\text{Si}) = -0.328\), \(\beta(^{32}\text{S}) = 0.186\).
*   Experimental charge radii used: \(r_c(^{24}\text{Mg}) = 3.126\) fm, \(r_c(^{26}\text{Mg}) = 3.083\) fm, \(r_c(^{28}\text{Si}) = 3.177\) fm, \(r_c(^{32}\text{S}) = 3.282\) fm.
*   Fermi sea depletion (in percent) from the present WS+BCS method: \(^{24}\text{Mg}\): 16.8%, \(^{26}\text{Mg}\): 12.6%, \(^{28}\text{Si}\): 15.3%, \(^{32}\text{S}\): 11.1%.
*   For \(^{24}\text{Mg}\), varying \(\beta\) from 0.38 to 0.69 (from Stone (2001)) changed the Fermi sea depletion only slightly, from 17% to 17.5%; for \(^{32}\text{S}\), varying \(\beta\) from 0.19 to 0.29 changed it from 11.11% to 11.31%.
*   Pairing gaps used: \(\Delta_{pp}(^{24}\text{Mg}) = 2.62\) MeV, \(\Delta_{pp}(^{26}\text{Mg}) = 2.52\) MeV, \(\Delta_{pp}(^{28}\text{Si}) = 2.43\) MeV, \(\Delta_{pp}(^{32}\text{S}) = 2.27\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the deformed Woods-Saxon BCS approach previously applied by Simkovic et al. and Kosmas et al. to double-beta decay and other electroweak processes, now testing it against elastic electron scattering data for s-d shell nuclei. It directly compares with spherical shell-model calculations of Potbhare (using the U and PW interactions) and with the experimental form factor data of Sinha (1973), de Vries (1987), Soundranayagam (1988), and Wesselborg (1997). The results enable future extension of these BCS ground states to deformed RPA calculations for neutrino-nucleus reactions and double-beta decay in non-spherical systems, as noted by Sarriguren (2003) and Simkovic et al.
