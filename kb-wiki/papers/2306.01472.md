# Corrections to Local Density Approximation for superfluid trapped fermionic atoms from the Wigner-Kirkwood $\hbar$ expansion

> A semiclassical second-order differential equation for the inhomogeneous local gap $Δ(r)$ is derived from a strict second-order $\hbar$ expansion of the anomalous pairing tensor and compared with a similar equation given by Simonucci et al. The second-order normal density matrix is given as well. Several extra gradient terms are revealed. Second-order expressions at finite temperature are given for the first time. The corresponding Ginzburg-Landau equation is presented and it is shown that, compared to the equation of Baranov and Petrov, an extra second-order gradient term is present. Applications to the pairing gap in cold atoms in a harmonic trap are presented.

## Key claim

The paper derives a strict second-order $\hbar$ expansion of the anomalous pairing tensor for superfluid trapped fermionic atoms, revealing that the previously published Local Phase Density Approximation (LPDA) equation of Simonucci et al. (2014) contains only one of several gradient correction terms, and that the Ginzburg-Landau equation of Baranov and Petrov (1998) misses an extra second-order gradient term.

## Method

The authors perform a Wigner-Kirkwood $\hbar$ expansion of the generalized density matrix for inhomogeneous superfluid Fermi systems, extending previous zero-temperature work to finite temperature for the first time. They compute the second-order corrections to the pairing tensor and normal density matrix in phase space, then integrate over momentum to obtain local densities and a second-order differential equation for the inhomogeneous local gap $\Delta(r)$. The resulting equation is solved numerically for fermionic atoms in a spherical harmonic trap and compared to full Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations.

## Key numbers

- For $\mu = 35$ (trap units, corresponding to approximately 14,300 particles) at $T=0$, the LDA overestimates the gap in the center; the full $\hbar^2$ correction reproduces the HFB reduction better than LPDA, which is "hardly distinguishable" from LDA on the scale of the graphs.
- For $\mu = 50$ (trap units, approximately 42,000 particles) at $T=0$, the LDA works "very well except near the surface," while the $\hbar^2$ corrected gap "follows more closely the HFB gap than the LDA" and becomes "too large around the classical turning point."
- At finite temperature $T=1$ for $\mu=35$ and $T=3$ for $\mu=50$, both LPDA and full $\hbar^2$ calculations "are quite successful in reproducing the general behavior of the HFB gap, both in the center and in the tail," while LDA "fails badly."
- The coupling constant is fixed to $g = -1$ in trap units, giving dimensionless interaction strength $k_F(r=0)a_F \approx -0.67$ for $\mu=35$ and $-0.80$ for $\mu=50$.
- The Ginzburg-Landau equation derived near $T_c$ contains the coefficient $K = 7\zeta(3)/(8\pi^2) \approx 0.1066$ in the gradient terms.

## Context

This work extends the Wigner-Kirkwood $\hbar$ expansion of the generalized density matrix first derived by Taruishi and Schuck (1992) for superfluid systems, and later reconsidered by Ullrich and Gross, Csordas et al. (2010), and Pei et al. (2015). It provides a systematic comparison with the LPDA equation of Simonucci and Strinati (2014) and the Ginzburg-Landau equation of Baranov and Petrov (1998), showing that the full $\hbar^2$ expansion contains additional gradient terms beyond those in both prior approaches. The results enable improved semiclassical treatments of large superfluid Fermi systems (such as cold atoms in traps with very large particle numbers) where full quantum HFB calculations become numerically challenging, and provide finite-temperature expressions for the first time.
