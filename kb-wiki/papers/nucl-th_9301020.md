# Comment on Shadow model for sub-barrier fusion applied to light systems

> We demonstrate that the cross sections derived from the "shadow model" for reactions between light nuclei disagree with low-energy laboratory data and exhibit unphysical behavior at energies below those for which data exist. As a consequence, the large thermonuclear reaction rates obtained by Scalia and Figuera [Phys. Rev. C46, 2610 (1992)] are wrong.

# Comment on "Shadow model for sub-barrier fusion applied to light systems"

## Key claim

The shadow model of Scalia and Figuera (1992) produces cross sections for light-nuclei reactions that disagree with low-energy laboratory data and exhibit an unphysical increase of the astrophysical S-factor at energies below those for which data exist, making the model's extrapolated thermonuclear reaction rates incorrect.

## Method

The authors transformed the fusion cross sections calculated from the shadow model equations (Eqs. 3-9 of Scalia and Figuera 1992, using the parameters from Table I of that reference) into the astrophysical S-factor defined as S(E) = sigma(E) * E * exp(2*pi*eta(E)), where eta is the Sommerfeld parameter. They then compared these S-factors with experimental data for the 3He(3He,2p)4He reaction from Krauss et al. (1987) and with predictions from a microscopic model by Typel et al. (1991). They also examined the other reactions considered in the original shadow model paper.

## Key numbers

- The microscopic model of Typel et al. (1991) yields S(0) ≈ 5.3 MeV·b for the 3He(3He,2p)4He reaction, in close agreement with the value used in the standard solar model.
- The most effective energy under solar conditions for the 3He(3He,2p)4He reaction is E0 ≈ 22 keV.
- The shadow model predicts an unphysical, dramatic increase of the S-factor at energies smaller than those for which data are available, leading to reaction rates at solar temperatures that are significantly higher than the standard values.

## Context

This comment refutes the claim by Scalia and Figuera (1992) that thermonuclear reaction rates for solar hydrogen burning are substantially larger than those adopted in the Standard Solar Model of Bahcall (1983). The authors demonstrate that the conventional Gamow barrier penetration, as confirmed by numerous measurements compiled by Fowler (1984) and by the microscopic calculation of Typel et al. (1991), correctly describes low-energy cross sections. The shadow model's six-parameter fit appears to reproduce cross sections on logarithmic plots but fails when transformed to the S-factor, and its extrapolation to lower energies is unjustified. This work reaffirms the standard extrapolation methods used in nuclear astrophysics.
