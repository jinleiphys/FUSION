# Quantum-mechanical description of angular motion of fission fragments at scission

> The quantum-mechanical description of the collective angular motion in a system of two touching fission fragments is proposed. The main peculiarities of excitation spectrum and the structure of the wave functions are investigated. As found, the angular motion approximately corresponds to independent vibrations of fragments around the pole-to-pole configuration. The model allows us to explain the experimentally observed lack of correlation between the angular momenta of fission fragments. Additionally the correlation between angular momentum and fragment mass is primarily linked to the change of fragments deformation. The saw-tooth behavior of angular momentum distribution with respect to the fragment mass is well explained.

# Quantum-mechanical description of angular motion of fission fragments at scission

## Key claim

The angular motion of two touching fission fragments at scission approximately corresponds to independent small-amplitude vibrations of each fragment around the pole-to-pole configuration, rather than the previously assumed bending and wriggling modes. This picture explains the experimentally observed lack of correlation between fragment angular momenta and the saw-tooth behavior of angular momentum versus fragment mass, with the key condition that the ground state of the angular vibrational spectrum decisively contributes to angular momentum generation because the lowest excited states lie above 1 MeV.

## Method

The authors construct a quantum-mechanical Hamiltonian for a dinuclear system (DNS) consisting of two axially-symmetric deformed fragments at touching, including kinetic energy terms for fragment rotation and DNS rotation as a whole, plus a potential energy composed of Coulomb and nuclear interactions. The Hamiltonian is diagonalized in a basis of tripolar spherical harmonics for the case of spontaneous fission of an even-even nucleus with total angular momentum I=0. The resulting wave functions are analyzed to extract distributions of angular momenta, K quantum numbers (projection onto the separation axis), and correlations between fragment spins for the system 102Zr + 150Ce from spontaneous fission of 252Cf.

## Key numbers

- The lowest excited angular vibrational state lies at energy larger than 1 MeV, as in Shneidman (2003)
- For 102Zr + 150Ce with deformations (beta_Zr, beta_Ce) = (0.32, 0.2), (0.7, 0.7), and (0.7, 0.2), excitation spectra are calculated and shown in Figure 1
- The distribution of K values extends up to K approximately 4 for the lowest states
- For 102Zr(beta=0.7) + 150Ce(beta=0.24), the average angular momenta of fragments are calculated with constraints I_min = 0, 2, 4, etc. (in units of hbar)
- For 92Kr(beta=0.24) + 160Sm(beta=0.8), the average angular momentum is calculated as a function of fragment mass
- For 102Zn(beta=0.6) + 150Ce(beta=0.5), the average angular momentum is calculated as a function of fragment mass
- For 120Cd(beta=0.8) + 132Sn(beta=0), the average angular momentum is calculated as a function of fragment mass

## Context

This work extends the classical analysis of angular vibrational modes at scission by Nix (1965) and the quantum-mechanical treatment of bending modes by Zielinska (1974) and Rasmussen (1969). It directly addresses recent experimental observations by Wilson (2021) showing absence of angular momentum correlation between fission fragments and saw-tooth behavior of average spin versus fragment mass. The model provides an alternative to the statistical approach of Randrup (2021, 2022) and Dossing (2024), which attribute angular momentum increase to excitation energy while assuming fragment deformation remains near ground state; instead, this work proposes that most available energy at scission is stored as deformation energy, linking angular momentum to fragment deformation changes. The results enable interpretation of the saw-tooth pattern in angular momentum as a consequence of the saw-tooth behavior of neutron multiplicity versus fragment mass, as observed by Gook (2014).
