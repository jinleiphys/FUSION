# Particle-particle correlations and the space-time structure of heavy ion collisions

> The present status of the use of two-particle intensity interferometry as a diagnostic tool to study the space-time dynamics of intermediate energy heavy ion collisions is examined. Calculations for the two-proton and two-pion correlation functions are presented and compared to experiment. The calculations are based on the nuclear Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck transport theory.

## Key claim

Two-particle correlation functions at small relative momentum can be calculated from one-body transport theory (the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck equation) under the assumption that final-state interactions between the two detected particles dominate and that interactions with the emitting source and all remaining particles can be neglected, but the extracted source sizes from two-pion correlations can be significantly distorted by pion absorption and shadowing effects if these kinematic effects are not included.

## Method

The author solves the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) transport equation using the test particle method to obtain the time evolution of the single-particle phase space distribution function \(f(\vec r,\vec p,t)\). For two-proton correlations, this distribution is inserted into the Koonin formula (Equation 1) which integrates the product of single-particle emission functions with the square of the two-particle relative wave function, where the relative wave function includes Coulomb, strong (Reid soft core potential), and antisymmetrization effects. For two-pion correlations, an extended BUU code propagates protons, neutrons, Delta and N* resonances, and pions, and the correlation function is computed by comparing correlated pairs (fixed reaction plane) to uncorrelated background events (random rotation of reaction plane about the beam axis).

## Key numbers

- For the reaction \(^{14}\)N + \(^{27}\)Al at E/A = 75 MeV, two-proton correlation functions are shown for three gates on total pair momentum \(|\vec P|\); the beam momentum per nucleon is \(p_{\text{beam}} \approx 375\) MeV/c.
- For the reaction La + La at E/A = 1.35 GeV, single pion spectra are calculated for central collisions (\(b \leq 2.8\) fm) with detection angle \(90^\circ \pm 30^\circ\) in the center of mass.
- For the reaction p + C and p + Pb at 1.6 GeV incident energy, two-pion (\(\pi^+,\pi^-\)) correlation functions are computed as a function of invariant mass \(M_{\text{inv}}\) of the pion pair.
- The two-proton relative wave function shows a peak at \(q \approx 20\) MeV/c due to the \(^2\)He resonance.
- For a simple Gaussian source parameterization with zero lifetime, the strong interaction has dominant effect for source radii around \(r_0 = 2.5\) fm due to the \(^2\)He resonance; for \(r_0 < 20\) fm the correlation function becomes increasingly sensitive to antisymmetrization and strong interaction effects.
- Varying the in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section by only 10% produces differences in calculated two-proton correlation functions that should be experimentally measurable.

## Context

This work extends the use of intensity interferometry, originally introduced by Hanbury Brown and Twiss (1954, 1956) for astronomy and first applied to subatomic physics by Goldhaber et al. (1960) for pion correlations in \(p\bar{p}\) annihilation. Koonin (1977) proposed using two-proton intensity interferometry to obtain pictures of heavy ion collisions. The calculations build on prior BUU transport theory developments by Bertsch et al. (1984), Kruse et al. (1985), and others, and extend the work of Gong et al. (1990, 1991) on computing two-proton correlation functions from transport theory. The results enable quantitative extraction of source sizes and in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections from correlation function measurements, while demonstrating that pion shadowing effects must be accounted for to avoid extracting incorrect source sizes from two-pion correlations.
