# Quantum State Preparation with Resolution Refinement

> We introduce a method called resolution refinement that allows one to bootstrap eigenstate preparation on a quantum computer. We first prepare an eigenstate of a low-resolution Hamiltonian using any method of choice. The eigenstate is then lifted to higher resolution and adiabatically evolved to produce the corresponding eigenstate of a higher-fidelity Hamiltonian. We give examples of resolution refinement applied to both single-particle basis states as well as a spatial lattice grid. For basis refinement, we compute few-body ground states of the Busch model for interacting particles in a harmonic trap in one dimension. For lattice refinement, we compute Hartree-Fock nuclear states for a central Woods-Saxon potential in three dimensions, and we compute bound states and continuum states in a multi-species Hubbard model of fermions in one dimension. In all cases, the method is efficient and requires an adiabatic evolution time that scales with the inverse of the energy gap times the square root of the system size. We show that this very favorable scaling arises from the fact that resolution refinement does not make large changes to the structure or energies of the low-energy eigenstates.

## Key claim

Resolution refinement bootstraps eigenstate preparation on a quantum computer by first preparing an eigenstate of a low-resolution Hamiltonian, then lifting it to higher resolution and adiabatically evolving to produce the corresponding eigenstate of a higher-fidelity Hamiltonian; the method's efficiency relies on the fact that resolution refinement does not make large changes to the structure or energies of the low-energy eigenstates, so the minimum energy gap along the adiabatic path remains comparable to the physical energy gap rather than shrinking dramatically.

## Method

The authors first prepare an eigenstate of a low-resolution Hamiltonian (using any method of choice) within a small model space, then apply a prolongation operator P that lifts every many-body basis state from the low-resolution space to a high-resolution space. Adiabatic evolution is performed with time duration T from the shifted and prolonged low-resolution Hamiltonian P(H_low - mu)P^dagger to the shifted high-resolution Hamiltonian H_high - mu, using interpolation functions cos^2[theta(t)] and sin^2[theta(t)] with theta(t) = (pi t)/(2T). For basis refinement, single-particle harmonic oscillator orbitals are used with truncation parameters n_max=2 (low) and n_max=10 (high); for lattice refinement, a coarse lattice with spacing 2a is lifted to a fine lattice with spacing a by distributing each |1> state on a coarse site to an equal superposition of 2^d states on surrounding fine lattice sites.

## Key numbers

- For the Busch model with K=N=2 fermions and coupling g=1: ground state energy at low resolution (n_max=2) is 1.3782, at high resolution (n_max=10) is 1.3264, and the exact analytic value is 1.3067 (all in units of hbar omega)
- Energy gap Delta E for Busch model: approximately 0.7 for K=N=2, 0.6 for K=N=3, and 0.5 for K=N=4 (in units of hbar omega)
- For lattice refinement of nuclear states: fine lattice spacing a = (150 MeV)^{-1} = 1.32 fm, coarse lattice spacing 2a = (75 MeV)^{-1} = 2.63 fm; nucleon mass m = 938.92 MeV; diffuseness parameter alpha = 0.5 fm
- Woods-Saxon potential parameters: fine lattice uses R_0 = 1.5 fm and V_0 = 50 MeV; coarse lattice uses R_0 = 1.8 fm and V_0 = 40 MeV
- Energy gap Delta E for nuclear lattice calculations (He-4, O-16, Mg-24, Si-28, Ca-40) is between 10 and 15 MeV
- For Ca-40 lattice refinement, the overlap probability starts at 8 x 10^{-6} and rises by five orders of magnitude
- For the one-dimensional Hubbard model cluster states, the energy gap Delta E for each Hamiltonian is about 4 or 5 MeV
- The adiabatic evolution time T scales as O(sqrt(N)/(sqrt(epsilon) Delta E_min)) for linear interpolation, where N is the number of particles and target fidelity is 1-epsilon

## Context

This work extends the principles of low-energy effective field theory and renormalization group methods (Bogner 1999, Bogner 2006, Hergert 2012, Hagen 2015, Ekstrom 2015) to quantum computing, acting as an inverse renormalization group transformation from low to high resolution. The method addresses scaling limitations of existing quantum algorithms: variational quantum eigensolvers (Peruzzo 2014) face exponentially vanishing gradients for large systems (McClean 2018), adiabatic state preparation (Albash 2018) can require exponentially increasing runtime, and filtering methods like quantum phase estimation (Kitaev 1995, Abrams 1999) and the rodeo algorithm (Choi 2020) have success probabilities that decrease exponentially with system size. The authors suggest combining resolution refinement with filtering algorithms such as the rodeo algorithm (Patkowski 2025) to achieve exponential convergence at late times, and note that extension to lattice gauge theories is beyond the present scope.
