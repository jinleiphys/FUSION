# Self-Consistent Pushing and Cranking Corrections to the Meson Fields of the Chiral Quark-Loop Soliton

> We study translational and spin-isospin symmetry restoration for the two-flavor chiral quark-loop soliton. Instead of a static soliton at rest we consider a boosted and rotating hedgehog soliton. Corrected classical meson fields are obtained by minimizing a corrected energy functional which has been derived by semi-classical methods ('variation after projection'). We evaluate corrected meson fields in the region 300 MeV \le M \le 600 MeV of constituent quark masses M and compare them with the uncorrected fields. We study the effect of the corrections on various expectation values of nuclear observables such as the root-mean square radius, the axial-vector coupling constant, magnetic moments and the delta-nucleon mass splitting.

# Self-Consistent Pushing and Cranking Corrections to the Meson Fields of the Chiral Quark-Loop Soliton

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that performing a "variation after projection" (self-consistent pushing and cranking) for the chiral quark-loop soliton yields corrected meson fields that differ from the static hedgehog fields, and that these corrections significantly affect nuclear observables; however, the authors explicitly state that they do not include pushing and cranking corrections to the observables themselves, which must be added before comparison with experimental data.

## Method

The authors start from the two-flavor chiral quark-loop (NJL) model with hedgehog meson fields restricted to the chiral circle, treating mesons as mean fields while fully including the polarized Dirac sea. Instead of minimizing the static mean-field energy and adding correction terms afterward (projection after variation), they minimize a corrected energy functional that includes translational zero-point energy, rotational zero-point energy, and a cranking term for definite isospin, using a variational representation of the profile function with spline interpolation and an effective pion mass parameter for the asymptotic behavior. The corrected meson profiles are determined separately for nucleons (T=1/2) and Delta isobars (T=3/2) by minimizing the corrected energy functional as a function of constituent quark mass M in the range 300 MeV to 600 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Constituent quark mass range studied: 300 MeV ≤ M ≤ 600 MeV
- At M ≥ 480 MeV, the total energy correction for the nucleon exceeds one half of the soliton energy (mean-field energy roughly 1230 MeV)
- The expectation value of T^2 for the hedgehog soliton is 9/4 (from 3 valence quarks, each with spin 1/2)
- Above M ≈ 380 MeV, the moment of inertia for the Delta isobar is roughly 15% larger than for the nucleon
- The effective pion mass squared follows approximately: m_tilde_pi^2 ≈ 1.14 m_pi^2 - 0.56 Omega^2, from a fit in the region -6 ≤ (Omega/m_pi)^2 ≤ 1
- The experimental nucleon mass is reached at M ≈ 350 MeV, but the predicted Delta mass is remarkably smaller than the experimental value in the whole mass region
- The valence quark energy dominates up to M ≈ 500 MeV for the corrected soliton, compared to M ≈ 420 MeV for the static soliton
- The valence quark energy becomes negative at M ≈ 750 MeV in the uncorrected case

## Context

This work extends the chiral quark-loop soliton approach by implementing a "variation after projection" method, in contrast to previous studies that performed projection after variation (i.e., calculating static mean-field configurations first and adding correction terms afterward). The semi-classical pushing and cranking approximations follow the techniques used in non-relativistic many-particle physics (Ring 1980) and are analogous to the Skyrme model approach (Adkins 1983). The authors compare their zero-point energies with RPA calculations of mesonic quantum fluctuations (Weigel 1994), noting that only bound fluctuations were considered in that work. The corrected meson fields enable a more consistent treatment of symmetry restoration, but the authors emphasize that a full treatment must also include pushing and cranking corrections to the observables themselves, which will be the subject of a forthcoming analysis.
