# Spectroscopic features of low-energy excitations in skin nuclei

> Systematic studies of dipole and other multipole excitations in stable and exotic nuclei are discussed theoretically. Exploring the relation of the strengths of low-energy dipole and quadrupole pygmy resonances to the thickness of the neutron (proton) skin a close connection between static and dynamic properties of the nucleus is observed. The fine structure of low-energy dipole strength in 138Ba nucleus is revealed from E1 and spin-flip M1 strengths distributions.

# Spectroscopic features of low-energy excitations in skin nuclei

## Key claim
The paper argues that the strength of low-energy dipole (PDR) and quadrupole (PQR) pygmy resonances is closely connected to the thickness of the neutron or proton skin, which is determined by the neutron-to-proton ratio N/Z. However, the authors qualify that in N=82 nuclei, the magnetic (M1) contribution must be identified and subtracted from the observed low-energy dipole strength to isolate the pure electric PDR strength associated with the nuclear skin.

## Method
The authors employ a theoretical framework combining self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoljubov (HFB) calculations for the nuclear ground state with the quasiparticle-phonon model (QPM) for excited states, using density functional theory (DFT) to express the interaction part in terms of Wood-Saxon potentials. Excitations are described by QRPA phonons, and the QPM provides multiconfiguration mixing with wave functions consisting of one-, two-, and three-phonon configurations. The method is applied to investigate dipole and multipole excitations in N=50, N=82, and Z=50 nuclei, with detailed calculations for tin isotopes and the 138Ba nucleus.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous microscopic studies (Tsoneva and Lenske, 2008; Tsoneva, Lenske, and Stoyanov, 2004) on pygmy resonances and nuclear skin phenomena, building on earlier predictions from hydrodynamic and collective models (Suzuki et al., 1990; Van Isacker et al., 1992). The systematic theoretical investigation across isotopic and isotonic chains enables comparison with an increasing number of photon-scattering experiments (Tonchev et al., 2010; Volz et al., 2006; Schwengner et al., 2008) and provides a framework for identifying the distinct electric character of the PDR mode separate from the giant dipole resonance and magnetic contributions.
