# Revealing the structure of light pseudoscalar mesons at the Electron-Ion Collider

> How the bulk of the Universe's visible mass emerges and how it is manifest in the existence and properties of hadrons are profound questions that probe into the heart of strongly interacting matter. Paradoxically, the lightest pseudoscalar mesons appear to be the key to the further understanding of the emergent mass and structure mechanisms. These mesons, namely the pion and kaon, are the Nambu-Goldstone boson modes of QCD. Unravelling their partonic structure and the interplay between emergent and Higgs-boson mass mechanisms is a common goal of three interdependent approaches -- continuum QCD phenomenology, lattice-regularised QCD, and the global analysis of parton distributions -- linked to experimental measurements of hadron structure. Experimentally, the foreseen electron-ion collider will enable a revolution in our ability to study pion and kaon structure, accessed by scattering from the "meson cloud" of the proton through the Sullivan process. With the goal of enabling a suite of measurements that can address these questions, we examine key reactions to identify the critical detector system requirements needed to map tagged pion and kaon cross sections over a wide range of kinematics. The excellent prospects for extracting pion structure function and form factor data are shown, and similar prospects for kaon structure are discussed in the context of a worldwide programme. Successful completion of the programme outlined herein will deliver deep, far-reaching insights into the emergence of pions and kaons, their properties, and their role as QCD's Goldstone boson modes.

## Key claim

The paper argues that measuring the partonic structure of pions and kaons at the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) via the Sullivan process can reveal how emergent hadronic mass (EHM) and Higgs-boson mass generation interfere, but the success of this programme depends on identifying critical detector system requirements and validating the extrapolation from off-mass-shell pions to on-shell structure.

## Method

The authors examine key reactions for tagged pion and kaon production through the Sullivan process at the EIC, identifying detector system requirements needed to map cross sections over a wide kinematic range. They combine continuum Schwinger function methods (CSMs), lattice-regularised QCD, and global analysis of parton distributions to predict observables such as elastic electromagnetic form factors and parton distribution functions. The analysis includes evaluating the feasibility of extracting pion and kaon structure functions and form factors from simulated EIC data.

## Key numbers

- The Higgs-generated valence-quark current masses account for less than 2% of the proton mass, while more than 98% of visible mass emerges from strong interactions within QCD (emergent hadronic mass, EHM).
- In the chiral limit, the trace anomaly expectation value for the proton is 94% of the measured proton mass.
- The sum of valence-quark and valence-antiquark current masses in the kaon amounts to 20% of its measured mass, four times more than in the pion.
- EHM+Higgs-boson interference produces 95% of the pion's mass and 80% of the kaon's mass.
- The pion valence-quark distribution function at large x follows (1-x)^beta with beta=2 at the hadronic scale, growing to beta=2+delta under evolution.
- The ratio u_K(x)/u_pi(x) from continuum predictions and lattice QCD results differs by approximately 5% at the scale zeta_5 = 5.2 GeV.
- A Minkowski space analysis finds the valence-quark probability in the pion state is about 70%, with the remainder in higher Fock-space components.

## Context

This paper extends the theoretical and experimental framework for studying pion and kaon structure, building on continuum QCD phenomenology (e.g., Chang 2013, Chen 2018, Cui 2020), lattice-regularised QCD (e.g., Sufian 2019, Lin 2020), and earlier global analyses of parton distributions (e.g., Aicher 2010). It directly addresses the need for new precision data to test predictions from these approaches, as the last pion structure function experiment interpretable in terms of valence-quark distributions was completed more than thirty years ago (Conway 1989) and the lone K-/pi- structure function experiment was performed forty years ago (Badier 1980). The programme outlined enables future EIC measurements to probe the interplay between EHM and Higgs-boson mass generation, and to test predictions for glue and sea distributions in pions and kaons for which no empirical information currently exists.
