# Fission Barriers of Compound Superheavy Nuclei

> The dependence of fission barriers on the excitation energy of the compound nucleus impacts the survival probability of superheavy nuclei synthesized in heavy-ion fusion reactions. In this work, we investigate the isentropic fission barriers by means of the self-consistent nuclear density functional theory. The relationship between isothermal and isentropic descriptions is demonstrated. Calculations have been carried out for $^{264}$Fm, $^{272}$Ds, $^{278}$112, $^{292}$114, and $^{312}$124. For nuclei around $^{278}$112 produced in "cold fusion" reactions, we predict a more rapid decrease of fission barriers with excitation energy as compared to the nuclei around $^{292}$114 synthesized in "hot fusion" experiments. This is explained in terms of the difference between the ground-state and saddle-point temperatures. The effect of the particle gas is found to be negligible in the range of temperatures studied.

## Key claim
The paper predicts that isentropic fission barriers in superheavy compound nuclei decrease more rapidly with excitation energy for nuclei produced in "cold fusion" reactions (around $^{278}$112) than for those produced in "hot fusion" reactions (around $^{292}$114), a difference explained by the variation in ground-state versus saddle-point temperatures. The authors emphasize that the isentropic description is physically more appropriate than the isothermal one for an isolated fissioning nucleus, and that self-consistent finite-temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations show the two descriptions are practically equivalent when self-consistency is maintained.

## Method
The authors performed self-consistent finite-temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (FT-HFB) calculations using the SkM* energy density functional and a density-dependent delta pairing interaction, implemented in the axial coordinate-space solver HFB-AX and the symmetry-unrestricted solver HFODD. Isentropic fission pathways were computed by fixing the entropy S at the value corresponding to the free energy minimum at a given temperature T, then inverting the S(T) relation numerically via interpolation. Calculations were carried out for five nuclei: $^{264}$Fm, $^{272}$Ds, $^{278}$112, $^{292}$114, and $^{312}$124, with the fission barrier height extracted as a function of excitation energy $E^*$.

## Key numbers
- For $^{272}$Ds and $^{278}$112 (cold fusion), the damping parameter $\gamma_D^{-1}$ = 17.2 MeV and 10.8 MeV, respectively.
- For $^{292}$114 (hot fusion), $\gamma_D^{-1}$ = 30 MeV; for $^{312}$124, $\gamma_D^{-1}$ = 20.2 MeV.
- In $^{264}$Fm, at $S(kT_{g.s.}=0.5)$ the barrier increases by about 0.6 MeV compared to $S=0$; at $S(kT_{g.s.}=1.5)$ the barrier is reduced to 0.9 MeV.
- Triaxiality reduces the fission barrier of $^{264}$Fm by approximately 3-4 MeV at low excitation energies, but this effect becomes negligible at the largest excitations considered.
- The external particle gas contributes only about 0.2 MeV (0.9 MeV) to the kinetic energy at $kT$ = 1.25 MeV (1.5 MeV); the number of gas neutrons increases from 0.01 at $kT$ = 1 MeV to 0.21 at $kT$ = 1.5 MeV.
- For $^{312}$124, no isentropic fission barrier is calculated at $E^* \sim 80$ MeV.

## Context
This work extends prior self-consistent studies of zero-temperature fission pathways in superheavy nuclei (Bur04, Ben98a, Sta05) by incorporating finite-temperature effects via the FT-HFB method, which had previously been applied to heated nuclei (Egi00, Mar03, Kha07, Min08). The isentropic approach follows the theoretical framework of Die81, who argued that fission of an isolated compound nucleus should be treated as an isentropic rather than isothermal process. The results enable more reliable theoretical estimates of compound nucleus survival probability in heavy-ion fusion reactions, replacing phenomenological damping factors (Arm00a, Den00, Itk02, Lov07) with microscopically calculated barrier damping parameters and neutron decay rates extracted from the particle gas component.
