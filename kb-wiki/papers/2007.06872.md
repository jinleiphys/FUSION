# From nuclei to neutron stars: simple binding energy computer modelling in the classroom (Part 1)

> We present a simple activity based on the liquid-drop model which allows secondary school students to explore the uses of mathematical models and gain an intuitive understanding of the concept of binding energy, and in particular the significance of positive binding energy. Using spreadsheets provided as Supplementary Material, students can perform simple manipulations on the different coefficients of the model to understand the role of each of its five terms. Students can use the spreadsheets to determine model parameters by optimising the agreement with real atomic mass data. %This will subsequently be used to predict the limit of existence of the Segré chart and to find the minimum mass of a neutron star. This activity can be used as the starting point of a discussion about theoretical models, their validation when it comes to describing experimental data and their predictive power towards unexplored regimes.

## Key claim

The paper presents a classroom activity based on the liquid-drop model that allows secondary school students to explore mathematical models and binding energy concepts, with the caveat that the model's predictions for the limits of nuclear stability (such as the maximum mass number for an isotopic chain) are not fully reliable due to approximations and the nature of nuclear stability itself.

## Method

The authors developed two Microsoft Excel and OpenOffice spreadsheets (Worksheet_Instructor and Worksheet_Student) that implement the Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula based on the liquid-drop model, which expresses binding energy per nucleon as the sum of volume, surface, Coulomb, asymmetry, and pairing terms. Students perform parameter optimisation by manually adjusting coefficients (a_V, a_A, a_P) to match experimental binding energy data for specific isotopic chains such as oxygen, calcium, and lead, using trial-and-error with visual feedback from graphs. The activity then extends to predicting the maximum bound nucleus for each isotopic chain by increasing neutron number until binding energy becomes negative.

## Key numbers

- The neutron rest mass energy is m_n c^2 = 939.565 MeV and the proton rest mass energy is m_p c^2 = 938.272 MeV
- Typical binding energy per particle of a medium-to-heavy nucleus is approximately 8 MeV
- More than 2400 nuclear binding energies per particle have been measured with accuracy greater than 0.005%
- The liquid-drop model coefficients provided are: a_V = 15.8 MeV, a_S = 18.3 MeV, a_C = 0.714 MeV, a_A = 23.2 MeV, a_P = 12.0 MeV
- For calcium (Z=20), the model predicts a maximum mass number A_max = 140; for lead (Z=82), A_max = 636
- The nuclear radius parameter is r_0 = 1.2 fm
- The typical accuracy of the liquid-drop model is 0.1 MeV per particle

## Context

This work extends the Binding Blocks educational outreach project developed at the University of York, which previously used LEGO brick towers to visualise experimental nuclear data (Pastore et al., 2017) and developed A-level activities (Wright et al., 2017). The activity enables students to understand parameter optimisation, model validation, and prediction, and serves as a foundation for a follow-up paper (Part 2) that applies the same model to neutron star physics, bridging nuclear physics and astrophysics.
