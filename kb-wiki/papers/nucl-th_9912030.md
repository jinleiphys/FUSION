# Low- and intermediate-energy nucleon-nucleon interactions and the analysis of deuteron photodisintegration within the dispersion relation technique

> The nucleon-nucleon interaction in the region of the nucleon kinetic energy up to 1000 MeV is analysed together with the reaction $γd \to pn$ in the photon energy range $E_γ=0-400$ MeV. Nine nucleon-nucleon $s$-channel partial amplitudes are reconstructed in the dispersion relation $N/D$ method: $^1S_0$, $^3S_1-^3D_1$, $^3P_0$, $^1P_1$, $^3P_1$, $^3P_2$, $^1D_2$, $^3D_2$ and $^3F_3$. Correspondingly, the dispersive representation of partial amplitudes $NΔ\to pn$, $NN^* \to pn$ and $NNπ\to pn$ is given. Basing on that, we have performed parameter-free calculation of the amplitude $γd \to pn$, taking into account: $(i)$ pole diagram, $(ii)$ nucleon-nucleon final-state rescattering $γd \to pn \to pn$, and $(iii)$ inelastic final-state rescatterings $γd \to NΔ(1232) \to pn$, $γd \to NN^*(1400) \to pn$ and $γd \to NNπ\to pn$. The $γd \to pn$ partial amplitudes for nine above-mentioned channels are found. It is shown that the process $γd \to pn \to pn$ is significant for the waves $^1S_0$, $^3P_0$, $^3P_1$, at $E_γ =50 -100$ MeV, while $γd \to NΔ\to pn$ for the waves $^3P_2$, $^1D_2$,$^3F_3$ dominates at $E_γ > 300$ MeV. Meson exchange current contributions into the deuteron disintegration are estimated: they are significant at $E_γ=100-400$ MeV.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the dispersion relation N/D method can simultaneously describe nucleon-nucleon scattering up to 1000 MeV and deuteron photodisintegration up to 400 MeV, with the key condition that the inelasticities in the nucleon-nucleon amplitude are primarily due to baryon excitations (such as NΔ and NN*) rather than prompt meson production, and that the photodisintegration calculation is parameter-free once the nucleon-nucleon vertex functions are fixed from phase shift data.

## Method

The authors reconstruct nine nucleon-nucleon s-channel partial amplitudes (¹S₀, ³S₁-³D₁, ³P₀, ¹P₁, ³P₁, ³P₂, ¹D₂, ³D₂, and ³F₃) using the dispersion relation N/D method, where the N-function contains left-hand singularities from meson exchanges and the D-function contains right-hand singularities from unitarity cuts. For channels with significant inelasticity (¹D₂, ³P₂, ³F₃), a two-channel formalism is employed coupling NN to NΔ(1232) states, with vertex functions parametrized as sums of poles along the left-hand cut and fitted to phase shift data. The deuteron photodisintegration amplitude is then calculated as a sum of the pole diagram, nucleon-nucleon final-state rescattering, and inelastic final-state rescatterings through NΔ(1232), NN*(1400), and NNπ intermediate states, using the previously determined vertex functions without additional free parameters.

## Key numbers

- Nucleon-nucleon interaction analyzed up to nucleon kinetic energy T = 1000 MeV
- Deuteron photodisintegration analyzed for photon energies Eγ = 0–400 MeV
- The process γd → pn → pn (elastic final-state rescattering) is significant for the ¹S₀, ³P₀, and ³P₁ waves at Eγ = 50–100 MeV
- The process γd → NΔ → pn dominates for the ³P₂, ¹D₂, and ³F₃ waves at Eγ > 300 MeV
- Meson exchange current contributions are significant at Eγ = 100–400 MeV
- The deuteron vertex functions obtained from the N/D method successfully describe deuteron form factors up to Q² = 2 GeV²/c²

## Context

This work extends the dispersion relation analysis of deuteron photodisintegration previously carried out by Anisovich and collaborators (Alex1, Alex2) from low energies into the region Eγ ≈ 250–400 MeV where Δ(1232) and N*(1400) production becomes important. It complements the extensive studies of deuteron photodisintegration using quantum mechanical and Feynman diagram approaches by Gross (GrossHol), Laget (Laget), and Levchuk (Levchuk), particularly by providing a framework where meson exchange currents arise naturally from anomalous t- and u-channel singularities rather than being inserted as separate diagrams. The successful description of deuteron elastic form factors using the same dispersion relation technique (Akms) motivates this application to the two-nucleon breakup reaction, and the approach enables future extension to other two-nucleon processes with controlled analytic properties.
