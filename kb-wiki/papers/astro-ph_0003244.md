# Lower Limit on Radius as a Function of Mass for Neutron Stars

> While model-independent limits are always interesting, a limit on neutron star radius as a function of mass attains special interest in light of recent interpretations of the periodic as well as quasi-periodic oscillations (QPOs) in brightness of X-rays emitted from neutron stars that are accreting matter from a low-mass companion. Here we derive such a limit based only on well accepted principles. We discuss our limit in connection with a recent interpretation of X-ray pulsations from SAX J1808.4-3658 as indicating a strange-star candidate, and show that this object can also be a normal neutron star, though one whose central core has very high density. The most plausible high-density phase of hadronic matter, which is also expected to be very compressible, is quark matter. So an alternative to the strange star interpretation of SAX J1808.4-3658 is that it is a hybrid neutron star.

## Key claim

The paper derives a model-independent lower limit on neutron star radius as a function of mass, based only on general relativity, causality, and minimal stability constraints. The limit shows that the X-ray pulsar SAX J1808.4-3658, previously interpreted as a strange-star candidate, can also be a normal neutron star with a very dense central core, specifically a hybrid neutron star containing quark matter.

## Method

The author performed a variational search over equations of state that satisfy four minimal constraints: Einstein's general relativistic equations for stellar structure, non-negative pressure-density gradient (Le Chatelier's principle), causal sound speed (less than the speed of light), and continuous matching to the low-density equation of state of Baym, Pethick, and Sutherland (1971) with no bound state at any density. The search minimized the radius at fixed mass, allowing a constant-pressure region above a baryon density of 0.1625 fm⁻³ to account for ignorance of matter above saturation density. The resulting limit was then compared to the mass-radius constraint derived from QPO models of SAX J1808.4-3658 by Li et al. (1999).

## Key numbers

- The minimum radius as a function of mass is approximated by the polynomial: R ≥ (3.1125 − 0.44192 x + 2.3089 x² − 0.38698 x³) km, for 1 ≤ x ≡ M/M⊙ ≤ 2.5.
- If the most massive neutron star in nature is 2 M⊙, the radius of all neutron stars must exceed 8.37 km.
- The central density of the minimum-radius star for a canonical 1.44 M⊙ star is about 26 times nuclear density.
- The Kepler period for a 1.44 M⊙ neutron star at the limiting radius is about 0.3 ms.

## Context

This work extends the author's earlier model-independent search for a minimum Kepler period (Glendenning 1992) and an even earlier analysis of gravitational redshift limits (Lindblom 1984). The derived mass-radius constraint directly addresses the interpretation of SAX J1808.4-3658 by Li et al. (1999), who proposed it as a strange-star candidate because explicit neutron star models failed their QPO-derived constraint. The result enables future discrimination between neutron stars and exotic stars (self-bound strange stars) through either rotational period measurements or X-ray pulsation analyses, though the author notes that the Kepler frequency formula for rotating relativistic stars remains model-dependent.
