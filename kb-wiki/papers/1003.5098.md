# Photonuclear Reactions induced by Intense Short Laser Pulses

> A measurement of the decay in time of nuclei excited by an intense short laser pulse of energy E(0) yields the Fourier transform of the autocorrelation function of the associated scattering matrix. We determine the optimal length (in time) of the pulse and evaluate the time-decay function using random-matrix theory. That function is shown to contain information not otherwise available. We approximate that function in a manner that is useful for the analysis of data. For E(0) below the threshold energy E(n) of the first neutron channel, the time-decay function is exponential in time t while it is the product of an exponential and a power in t for E(0) > E(n). The comparison of the measured decay functions in both energy domains yields an unambiguous and novel test of random-matrix theory in nuclei.

# Photonuclear Reactions induced by Intense Short Laser Pulses

## Key claim

For excitation energies below the first neutron threshold, the time-decay function of nuclei excited by a short intense laser pulse is purely exponential; above that threshold, the decay is the product of an exponential and a power law in time, providing an unambiguous and novel experimental test of random-matrix theory (RMT) in nuclei, provided the pulse bandwidth satisfies the condition that the average level spacing d is much smaller than the bandwidth Delta E, which in turn is much smaller than the giant dipole resonance width.

## Method

The authors model the excitation of compound-nucleus (CN) resonances by a short laser pulse as a coherent superposition of scattering wave functions with an envelope function g(E) centered at energy E0 and bandwidth Delta E. They relate the long-time part of the scattered flux in a given exit channel to the Fourier transform of the S-matrix autocorrelation function, which is evaluated analytically using the random-matrix theory approach of Verbaarschot (1985). The resulting time-decay function is approximated by factoring out an exponential due to gamma decay, leaving a residual function F(t) that depends on the transmission coefficients of the entrance, exit, and neutron channels and on whether the neutron channel is open or closed.

## Key numbers

- Average level spacing d of CN resonances is approximately 10 eV; typical resonance width is about 1 eV.
- Optimal laser pulse bandwidth Delta E is in the range 10 keV to 100 keV, corresponding to pulse duration Delta t approximately 0.5 x 10^(-19) s to 0.5 x 10^(-20) s.
- For s-wave neutrons, the transmission coefficient T_n is approximately 0.6; for p-wave neutrons at 50 keV above threshold, T_n is approximately 0.1 to 0.2.
- In numerical simulations with Lambda = 49 gamma channels and all p_gamma_i equal to T, for T = 0.0004 and T_n = 0.2, the fitted exponent a_1 for neutron decay is approximately 1.46 (close to 3/2) and for gamma decay is approximately 0.48 (close to 1/2); the exponential decay constant a_2 is approximately 0.02, which is about 50 times T.
- For T = 0.0016 and T_n = 0.4, a_1 for neutron decay is approximately 1.39 (close to 3/2) and for gamma decay is approximately 0.51 (close to 1/2); a_2 is approximately 0.077, again about 50 times T.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for coherent resonance formation and non-exponential decay developed by Hart et al. (1990), Dietz et al. (1992), and Hart et al. (1992), which was previously tested in microwave billiard experiments by Alt et al. (1995), Mitchell et al. (2010), and Dietz et al. (2010). The authors specifically address photonuclear reactions induced by intense laser pulses, motivated by the upcoming Extreme Light Infrastructure (ELI) project. The proposed measurement of the time-decay function in nuclei would provide a direct test of RMT predictions in nuclear systems, complementing earlier work on the autocorrelation function of photodissociation cross sections by Fyodorov (1998) and Gorin et al. (2004, 2005). The results enable experimental analysis using only a few parameters (average level spacing, transmission coefficients in the entrance, exit, and neutron channels) without requiring knowledge of individual gamma-channel transmission coefficients.
