# Neutrino Constraints on the Dark Matter Total Annihilation Cross Section

> In the indirect detection of dark matter through its annihilation products, the signals depend on the square of the dark matter density, making precise knowledge of the distribution of dark matter in the Universe critical for robust predictions. Many studies have focused on regions where the dark matter density is greatest, e.g., the Galactic Center, as well as on the cosmic signal arising from all halos in the Universe. We focus on the signal arising from the whole Milky Way halo; this is less sensitive to uncertainties in the dark matter distribution, and especially for flatter profiles, this halo signal is larger than the cosmic signal. We illustrate this by considering a dark matter model in which the principal annihilation products are neutrinos. Since neutrinos are the least detectable Standard Model particles, a limit on their flux conservatively bounds the dark matter total self-annihilation cross section from above. By using the Milky Way halo signal, we show that previous constraints using the cosmic signal can be improved on by 1-2 orders of magnitude; dedicated experimental analyses should be able to improve both by an additional 1-2 orders of magnitude.

## Key claim

By using the Milky Way halo signal rather than the cosmic (extragalactic) signal, the authors show that previous constraints on the dark matter total self-annihilation cross section can be improved by 1–2 orders of magnitude, with the caveat that the halo signal is less sensitive to uncertainties in the dark matter density distribution, especially for flatter halo profiles.

## Method

The authors calculate the neutrino flux from dark matter annihilations in the whole Milky Way halo, considering three different halo density profiles (Moore, NFW, and Kravtsov) normalized to match the Milky Way's rotation curve. They assume dark matter particles annihilate only into neutrino pairs (the least detectable Standard Model final state) and compare the predicted neutrino signal to the measured atmospheric neutrino spectrum from the Fréjus, Super-Kamiokande, and AMANDA detectors, setting an upper limit on the annihilation cross section by requiring the signal not to double the atmospheric background in a given energy bin.

## Key numbers

- The canonical enhancement factor for the whole-sky halo average signal is \( {\cal J}_{Ave} \simeq 5 \), with differences between profiles less than a factor of 2.
- The canonical enhancement factor for the isotropic halo component (minimal signal in all directions) is \( {\cal J}_{Iso} \simeq 0.5 \).
- The canonical enhancement factor for the halo angular component (within a 30° half-angle cone around the Galactic Center for neutrinos above 10 GeV) is \( {\cal J}_{Ang} \simeq 25 \).
- The ratio of the halo signal to the cosmic signal is approximately \( 10^5 \times ({\cal J}_{\Delta \Omega} / f_0) \), independent of cross section and dark matter mass.
- The cosmic intensity multiplier normalization \( f_0 \) is taken as \( 10^5 \) for the canonical constraint, with values ranging from \( 0.2 \times 10^5 \) (Kravtsov) to \( 5 \times 10^5 \) (Moore) for different profiles.
- The local dark matter density at the solar circle is \( \rho(R_{sc}) = 0.3 \) GeV cm\(^{-3}\) for the NFW profile, with values of 0.27 and 0.37 GeV cm\(^{-3}\) for Moore and Kravtsov profiles, respectively.
- The solar circle distance is \( R_{sc} = 8.5 \) kpc.
- The energy bin width used for the halo signal is \( \Delta \log_{10} E = 0.3 \) around \( E = m_\chi \); for the cosmic signal, a bin width of \( \Delta \log_{10} E = 0.5 \) is used.
- The atmospheric neutrino data are available up to \( 10^5 \) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Beacom, Bell, and Mack (2007), who used the cosmic (extragalactic) dark matter annihilation signal to set upper limits on the total annihilation cross section via neutrinos. The authors show that the Milky Way halo signal is generally larger and more certain than the cosmic signal, especially for flatter halo profiles, and that it can improve constraints by 1–2 orders of magnitude. The results are applicable to future dedicated analyses by neutrino experiments such as Super-Kamiokande, AMANDA, and IceCube, and also to gamma-ray studies of dark matter.
