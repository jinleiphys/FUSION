# Fragment Formation in Central Heavy Ion Collisions at Relativistic Energies

> We perform a systematic study of the fragmentation path of excited nuclear matter in central heavy ion collisions at the intermediate energy of $0.4 AGeV$. The theoretical calculations are based on a Relativistic Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck ($RBUU$) transport equation including stochastic effects. A Relativistic Mean Field ($RMF$) approach is used, based on a non-linear Lagrangian, with coupling constants tuned to reproduce the high density results of calculations with correlations. At variance with the case at Fermi energies, a new fast clusterization mechanism is revealed in the early compression stage of the reaction dynamics. Fragments appear directly produced from phase-space fluctuations due to two-body correlations. In-medium effects of the elastic nucleon-nucleon cross sections on the fragmentation dynamics are particularly discussed. The subsequent evolution of the primordial clusters is treated using a simple phenomenological phase space coalescence algorithm. The reliability of the approach, formation and recognition, is investigated in detail by comparing fragment momentum space distributions {\it and simultaneously} their yields with recent experimental data of the $FOPI$ collaboration by varying the system size of the colliding system, i.e. its compressional energy (pressure, radial flow). We find an excellent agreement between theory and experiment in almost all the cases and, on the other hand, some limitations of the simple coalescence model. Furthermore, the temporal evolution of the fragment structure is explored with a clear evidence of an earlier formation of the heavier clusters, that will appear as interesting $relics$ of the high density phase of the nuclear Equation of State ($EoS$).

## Key claim

At the intermediate energy of 0.4 AGeV, fragments in central heavy ion collisions are formed directly from phase-space fluctuations due to two-body correlations during the early compression stage, rather than through the spinodal-instability mechanism that dominates at Fermi energies; this mechanism is conditional on the presence of a fast expansion that hinders the low-density nucleation and spinodal growth timescales.

## Method

The authors solved the Relativistic Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (RBUU) transport equation including stochastic effects via a discrete test-particle mapping of phase space, using a Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) approach with a non-linear Lagrangian tuned to reproduce Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) results at high density. They applied a phase-space coalescence algorithm with fixed coalescence parameters (4.5 fm in coordinate space, 1.5 1/fm in momentum space) to identify clusters at a freeze-out time of 90 fm/c, and compared the resulting fragment yields and rapidity distributions with FOPI collaboration data for central collisions of Ca+Ca, Ni+Ni, Zr+Zr, Ru+Ru, and Au+Au systems at 0.4 AGeV.

## Key numbers

* Charge distributions at mid-rapidity for Au+Au, Zr+Zr, and Ni+Ni at 0.4 AGeV were reproduced using coalescence parameters of 4.5 fm (coordinate) and 1.5 1/fm (momentum), fixed across all systems.
* In-medium neutron-proton cross sections were shown at Fermi momenta k_F = 0.0, 1.1, 1.34, and 1.7 fm^{-1}, corresponding to densities of approximately 0, 0.5, 1, and 2 times saturation density (rho_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}).
* The compression modulus of the soft EoS used was 200 MeV.
* The freeze-out time for cluster identification was 90 fm/c.
* The rms radius of deuterons is 1.96 fm, compared to 1.61 fm for tritons and 1.74 fm for He fragments.

## Context

This work extends the study of fragmentation from the Fermi-energy regime, where spinodal instabilities drive a liquid-gas phase transition (Chomaz, Colonna, and Randrup, 2004), to the higher-energy regime of 0.4 AGeV where a new fast clusterization mechanism operates in the compression phase. The approach builds on prior non-relativistic transport models such as Quantum Molecular Dynamics (Aichelin, 1991) and Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (Chen, Ko, and Li, 2004) that suggested high-density clusterization, and enables detailed comparison with FOPI collaboration data (Reisdorf and the FOPI collaboration, 2004) to test the reliability of the coalescence model and to extract information about the high-density nuclear Equation of State from heavier fragments as relics of the dense phase.
