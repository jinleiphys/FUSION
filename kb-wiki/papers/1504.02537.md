# Nature of Radiation-Induced Defects in Quartz

> Although quartz ($\rm α$-form) is a mineral used in numerous applications wherein radiation exposure is an issue, the nature of the atomistic defects formed during radiation-induced damage have not been fully clarified. Especially, the extent of oxygen vacancy formation is still debated, which is an issue of primary importance as optical techniques based on charged oxygen vacancies have been utilized to assess the level of radiation damage in quartz. In this paper, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations are applied to study the effects of ballistic impacts on the atomic network of quartz. We show that the defects that are formed mainly consist of over-coordinated Si and O, as well as Si--O connectivity defects, e.g., small Si--O rings and edge-sharing Si tetrahedra. Oxygen vacancies, on the contrary, are found in relatively low abundance, suggesting that characterizations based on $E^{\prime}$ centers do not adequately capture radiation-induced structural damage in quartz. Finally, we evaluate the dependence on the incident energy, of the amount of each type of the point defects formed, and quantify unambiguously the threshold displacement energies for both O and Si atoms. These results provide a comprehensive basis to assess the nature and extent of radiation damage in quartz.

## Key claim

The paper argues that oxygen vacancies (and thus E' centers) constitute only a small fraction of all radiation-induced defects in quartz, contrary to the common assumption that they are the primary damage indicator. The authors show that over-coordinated Si and O species dominate, and that the fraction of oxygen vacancies decreases with increasing incident energy, suggesting that optical measurements based on E' centers do not adequately capture the full extent of structural damage.

## Method

Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were performed using the LAMMPS code with a combined BKS and ZBL potential to model ballistic impacts in alpha-quartz supercells. A randomly selected atom (the primary knock-on atom, PKA) was accelerated with a given kinetic energy (300, 600, or 900 eV), and the resulting collision cascade was tracked for 3 ps with a variable time step, followed by 50 ps of relaxation at 300 K. For each incident energy, 100 independent cascade simulations were run to obtain statistical data on defect formation and annihilation.

## Key numbers

- Oxygen vacancies constitute only 3.6%, 3.4%, and 3.1% of all point defects at incident energies of 300, 600, and 900 eV, respectively.
- Extrapolated to higher energies, the oxygen vacancy fraction decreases to 2.2% at 10 keV and 1.5% at 100 keV.
- The average threshold displacement energy for oxygen is 28.9 eV, with a minimum of 20 eV along the <001> direction.
- The average threshold displacement energy for silicon is 70.5 eV, with a minimum of 30 eV along the <-110> direction.
- The number of each defect type follows a power-law dependence on incident energy: N_d = A * E^m, with exponents m = 0.72 for Si^III, 0.80 for O^I, 0.89 for Si^V, and 0.88 for O^III.

## Context

This work extends prior MD simulations of radiation damage in silicates (Delaye, Weber 1997, Kubota 2003, Mota 2005, Yu 2009) by focusing specifically on crystalline alpha-quartz, which had received limited attention. The findings contradict the common experimental practice of using E' center (charged oxygen vacancy) concentrations to assess radiation damage, as established by Arnold (1965) and Fischer (1983), and instead support earlier experimental hints by Devine (1983, 1984) that many other defects accompany each E' center. The quantified threshold displacement energies and defect fractions enable more accurate Monte Carlo predictions of radiation damage in quartz for nuclear power plant and other radiation-exposure applications.
