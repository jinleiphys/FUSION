# Kaon spectrum revisited

> The European Organization for Nuclear Research (CERN) has recently approved a world-unique QCD facility in which an updated version of the external M2 beam line of the CERN SPS in conjunction with a universal spectrometer of the COMPASS experiment is used. One of its main goals is to use highly intense and energetic kaon beams to map out the complete spectrum of excited kaons with an unprecedented precision; having a broad impact not only on low-energy QCD phenomenology, but also on many high-energy particle processes where excited kaons appear, such as the study of CP violation in heavy-meson decays studied at LHCb and Belle~II. In support of the experimental effort, the kaon spectrum is computed herein using a constituent quark model which has been successfully applied to a wide range of hadronic observables, from light to heavy quark sectors, and thus the model parameters are completely constrained. The model's prediction can be used as a template against which to compare the already collected data and future experimental findings, in order to distinguish between conventional and exotic kaon states. We also compare our results with those available in the literature in order to provide some general statements, common to all calculations.

## Key claim

The authors compute the kaon spectrum using a constituent quark model whose parameters are completely constrained by fits to a wide range of hadronic observables, from light to heavy quark sectors, rather than being tuned specifically to kaon data. The predictions are intended as a template to distinguish conventional quark-antiquark states from exotic kaon states in upcoming COMPASS++ experiments, but the model's spectrum is finite because bound-state energies cannot exceed a threshold of 2591 MeV.

## Method

The authors solve the Schrodinger equation for quark-antiquark bound states using the Rayleigh-Ritz variational method with Gaussian trial functions whose ranges follow a geometric progression. The quark-antiquark interaction includes a chiral potential from Goldstone boson exchanges (pion, kaon, eta, sigma), a one-gluon exchange potential with a scale-dependent strong coupling constant, and a screened confinement potential that becomes constant at large distances. Model parameters are taken from previous fits to meson, baryon, hadron-hadron, and multiquark phenomenology, with values listed in Table 1 of the paper.

## Key numbers

* Ground state kaon K(495): theoretical mass 481 MeV, experimental 495 MeV (deviation less than 25 MeV)
* First excited 0- state K(1460): theoretical mass 1512 MeV, experimental 1482 +/- 16 MeV
* K*(896) ground state (1-): theoretical mass 900 MeV, experimental 896 MeV
* K*(1680) (1-): theoretical mass 1787 MeV (1^3D_1 assignment) or 1676 MeV (2^3S_1), experimental 1718 +/- 18 MeV
* K_0*(1430) scalar (0+): theoretical mass 1305 MeV, experimental 1425 +/- 50 MeV
* K_0*(1950) scalar (0+): theoretical mass 1894 MeV, experimental 1944 +/- 18 MeV
* K_1(1270) axial (1+): theoretical mass 1370 MeV, experimental 1253 +/- 7 MeV
* K_1(1400) axial (1+): theoretical mass 1455 MeV, experimental 1403 +/- 7 MeV
* K_2(1770) (2-): theoretical mass 1760 MeV, experimental 1773 +/- 8 MeV
* K_2(1820) (2-): theoretical mass 1854 MeV, experimental 1819 +/- 12 MeV
* Maximum bound-state energy threshold: 2591 MeV (m_q + m_qbar + V_thr)

## Context

This work extends the constituent quark model approach previously applied by the same group to hadron phenomenology (Vijande 2004, Segovia 2013) and provides predictions for the upcoming COMPASS++ kaon beam facility at CERN, which will map the excited kaon spectrum with unprecedented precision. The results are compared with other theoretical frameworks including Godfrey and Isgur (1985), Ebert et al. (2009), and Pang et al. (2017), as well as with the 25 strange mesons listed by the Particle Data Group (2020), of which only 14 are well established. The model does not predict the K_0*(700) as a conventional quark-antiquark state, consistent with interpretations of it as a dynamically generated four-quark resonance, and leaves the K*(1410) without a conventional assignment due to its anomalously low mass relative to theoretical expectations.
