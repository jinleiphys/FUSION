# Introduction to superfluidity -- Field-theoretical approach and applications

> In this pedagogical introduction, I discuss theoretical aspects of superfluidity and superconductivity, mostly using a field-theoretical formalism. While the emphasis is on general concepts and mechanisms behind superfluidity, I also discuss various applications in low-energy and high-energy physics. Besides some introductory and standard topics such as superfluid helium and superfluidity in a simple scalar field theory, the lecture notes also include more advanced chapters, for instance discussions of the covariant two-fluid formalism and Cooper pairing with mismatched Fermi surfaces.

# Introduction to superfluidity, Field-theoretical approach and applications

## Key claim

This pedagogical review argues that superfluidity and superconductivity are fundamentally distinguished not by the bosonic or fermionic nature of the underlying particles, but by whether the spontaneously broken symmetry is global (superfluid) or local (superconductor). The author notes that this distinction is the "only fundamental difference" between the two phenomena, while acknowledging that the best-known examples (superfluid helium and electronic superconductors) happen to be bosonic and fermionic respectively.

## Method

The lecture notes develop superfluidity using a field-theoretical formalism, starting with a phenomenological two-fluid model for superfluid helium and then constructing a microscopic bosonic field theory with spontaneous symmetry breaking. For fermionic systems, the author derives the mean-field gap equation for Cooper pairing and computes the Meissner mass for gauge bosons in superconductors. The treatment covers both non-relativistic systems (helium, ultracold atomic gases) and relativistic systems (neutron matter, quark matter), using natural units throughout.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature of superfluid helium-4: approximately 2.2 K
- Critical temperature for ultracold atomic superfluids: order of 10⁻⁷ K
- Critical temperature for neutron matter (from astrophysical observations): order of 10⁸ K
- Critical temperature for quark matter (theoretical estimate): up to about 10¹¹ K
- Phonon dispersion parameters for superfluid helium: speed of sound c = 238 m s⁻¹, roton gap Δ = 8.9 K, roton momentum p₀ = 2.1 × 10⁻¹⁹ g cm s⁻¹, roton effective mass m = 1.72 × 10⁻²⁴ g
- Critical temperature computed from two-fluid model with total density ρ = 0.147 g cm⁻³: T_c ≈ 2.47 K
- Phonon contribution to pressure: P_ph = π² T⁴ / (90 c³)
- Phonon entropy density: s_ph = 2π² T³ / (45 c³)
- Phonon specific heat: c_V,ph = 2π² T³ / (15 c³) = 3 s_ph
- Roton pressure (low-temperature approximation): P_rot ≈ √(m/(2π³)) p₀² T³/² e^{-Δ/T}
- Roton normal-fluid density (w→0 limit): ρ_n,rot ≈ √(m/(2π³)) e^{-Δ/T} p₀⁴ / (3 T¹/²)

## Context

This work provides a pedagogical bridge between the phenomenological two-fluid model of Landau (1941) and Tisza (1938) and modern field-theoretical treatments of superfluidity across energy scales. It extends the standard textbook discussions of superfluid helium and BCS theory (Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer, 1957) to include relativistic superfluids in compact stars, color-superconducting quark matter (Alford, 2007), and the BCS-BEC crossover observed in ultracold fermionic gases (Giorgini, 2008; Ketterle, 2008). The covariant two-fluid formalism builds on work by Carter (1989) and Alford (2012), while the discussion of mismatched Fermi surfaces follows Chandrasekhar (1962) and Clogston (1962) and has applications in ultracold atoms, dense quark matter, and chiral symmetry breaking.
