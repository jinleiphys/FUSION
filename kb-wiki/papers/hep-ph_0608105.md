# Hadron Physics at J-PARC

> An outline is explained for hadron-physics projects at J-PARC, which is considered to be one of the flagship facilities in hadron physics from 2008. The facility provides an intensity frontier with 50 GeV proton beam for nuclear and particle physics. It could cover a wide range of hadron physics from strongly interacting many-body systems with an extended hadronic degree of freedom, strangeness, to new forms of hadrons and hadronic matters. These studies lead not only to create innovative fields of hadron physics but also, possibly, to understand fundamental interactions because of recent progress on AdS/CFT correspondence. At the first stage of the J-PARC operation, hadron topics are mainly on strangeness nuclear physics such as hypernuclei, kaonic nuclei, and possible pentaquark hadrons. Then, the studies could be extended to exotic hadron searches, chiral dynamics in nuclear medium, structure functions, hard exclusive processes, hadron physics in neutrino scattering, and spin structure of the nucleon. With major upgrades of the facility, extensive studies could be done for the nucleon spin, heavy-ion physics, and hadron physics at a high-energy neutrino factory.

## Key claim

J-PARC will be one of the flagship hadron-physics facilities from 2008, providing an intensity frontier with a 50 GeV proton beam that could cover a wide range of hadron physics, but the actual first-stage program is mainly limited to strangeness nuclear physics (hypernuclei, kaonic nuclei, and possible pentaquark hadrons), with other topics requiring later stages or major upgrades.

## Method

The paper reviews the planned hadron-physics program at J-PARC, organized into first-stage experiments using secondary kaon and pion beams for strangeness nuclear physics, second-stage studies of exotic hadrons, chiral dynamics in nuclear medium, structure functions via Drell-Yan processes, hard exclusive processes, and hadron physics in neutrino scattering, and future projects after major upgrades including polarized proton beams, heavy-ion beams, and a neutrino factory. The author surveys specific proposed experiments, their beamlines, and the theoretical motivations for each.

## Key numbers

- J-PARC provides a 50 GeV proton beam (30 GeV in the beginning) for nuclear and particle physics.
- The KEK-E325 experiment measured a 9% mass shift for rho and omega mesons in nuclear medium, with parameter k = 0.092 ± 0.002 in the density-dependent mass formula m(rho)/m(0) = 1 - k rho/rho_0.
- The probed x region for Drell-Yan at J-PARC is x > 0.2, complementary to LHC's small-x region (x ~ 10^-5).
- For the pentaquark Theta+ search, pion beams with momenta 1.87, 1.92, and 1.97 GeV/c will be used in the K1.8 beamline, and a low-momentum (475 MeV/c) K+ beam will investigate s-channel formation.
- The expected experimental error on the strange-quark contribution to nucleon spin (Delta s) from neutrino scattering at J-PARC is about 0.03, much smaller than the BNL-E734 experiment.
- Nuclear exclusion effects in neutrino scattering on oxygen at E_nu ~ 1 GeV are estimated at about 8% decrease in cross section at small Q^2.
- The BNL-EVA experiment studied pA -> pp (A-1) up to 14 GeV; theoretical calculations indicate a significant increase of color transparency from 10 GeV to 50 GeV in the J-PARC energy region.

## Context

This review extends the program of strangeness nuclear physics previously pursued at KEK-PS (KEK-E325, KEK-E471) and complements high-energy hadron physics at RHIC and LHC by focusing on the large-x region (x > 0.2) and lower-temperature, high-density nuclear matter. The paper enables future experimental proposals at J-PARC by identifying specific measurements that would resolve controversies from prior experiments, such as the pentaquark Theta+ (reported at SPring-8), strange tribaryons S0(3115) and S+(3140) (KEK-PS), and the need for independent confirmation of the FINUDA K-pp bound state claim.
