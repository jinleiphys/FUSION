# Identification of the slow E3 transition 136mCs -> 136Cs with conversion electrons

> We performed at ISOLDE the spectroscopy of the decay of the 8- isomer in 136Cs by and conversion-electron detection. For the first time the excitation energy of the isomer and the multipolarity of its decay have been measured. The half-life of the isomeric state was remeasured to T1/2 = 17.5(2) s. This isomer decays via a very slow 518 keV E3 transition to the ground state. In addition to this, a much weaker decay branch via a 413 keV M4 and a subsequent 105 keV E2 transition has been found. Thus we have found a new level at 105 keV with spin 4+ between the isomeric and the ground state. The results are discussed in comparison to shell model calculations.

## Key claim

The 8- isomer in 136Cs decays primarily via a 518 keV E3 transition to the ground state, which is "by far the slowest E3 transition known in this mass range" at only 6.35(7) x 10^-6 W.u., and the authors find that a shell-model calculation can reproduce this extreme slowness only if the neutron effective charge is taken as zero (e_nu = 0).

## Method

The authors produced 136mCs via 1.4 GeV proton-induced fission on a uranium carbide target at ISOLDE, mass-separated A=136, and implanted the beam into an aluminum catcher foil. They simultaneously measured gamma rays with a HPGe detector and conversion electrons with a liquid-nitrogen-cooled Si(Li) detector, then extracted internal conversion coefficients (alpha_K, alpha_L, alpha_M+N) for the 518 keV and 413 keV transitions by comparing relative intensities of gamma and electron lines. The half-life was remeasured with a HPGe detector at the ISOLDE monitoring tape station, and shell-model calculations were performed using the SN100PN interaction diagonalized with the code NuShell.

## Key numbers

- Half-life of the 8- isomer: T_1/2 = 17.5(2) s (a factor of 10 more precise than the previous values of 19(2) s and 17(2) s)
- Excitation energy of the 8- isomer: 517.9(1) keV, determined from the measured gamma-ray energy
- B(E3; 8- -> 5+) = 6.97(8) x 10^-3 e^2 fm^6, corresponding to 6.35(7) x 10^-6 W.u., the slowest E3 transition in this mass range
- Experimental conversion coefficients for the 518 keV transition: alpha_K = 1.84(4) x 10^-2, alpha_L = 3.73(10) x 10^-3, alpha_M+N = 9.35(38) x 10^-4, all agreeing with theoretical E3 values and ruling out M4
- For the 413 keV transition: alpha_K = 0.490(47), alpha_L = 0.122(15), alpha_M+N = 3.1(11) x 10^-2, consistent with M4 and ruling out E5
- The 413 keV transition is a factor of about 800 weaker than the 518 keV ground-state transition
- New level at 104.8(3) keV with spin 4+, fed by the 413 keV M4 transition and decaying via a 105 keV E2 transition to the ground state
- Shell-model calculation with e_nu = 0 yields B(E3) = 0.0068 e^2 fm^6 = 6.1 x 10^-6 W.u., giving a partial half-life of 17.5 s for the E3 branch

## Context

This work provides the first measurement of the excitation energy and multipolarity of the 8- isomer in 136Cs, a nucleus where previously only the ground state (5+) and the isomer (8-) were known from earlier work by Ravn (1970s) and Thibault (1970s). The results are relevant for neutrinoless double-beta decay calculations of 136Xe -> 136Ba, as discussed by Akimov (2005) and Suhonen (1998), because wave functions of the intermediate nucleus 136Cs are needed. The extreme hindrance of the E3 transition (only two slower E3 transitions known in the entire chart of nuclides, in 177mLu and 179m2Hf) and the need to invoke a zero neutron effective charge in the shell-model calculation pose a puzzle for nuclear structure theory, and the authors suggest that Coulomb excitation, transfer reactions (135Cs(d,p), 137Ba(d,3He)), and neutron capture (135Cs(n,gamma)) should be used to find more levels in this poorly explored nucleus.
