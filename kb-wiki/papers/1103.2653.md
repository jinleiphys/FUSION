# Poles, the only true resonant-state signals, are extracted from a worldwide collection of partial wave amplitudes using only one, well controlled pole-extraction method

> Each and every energy dependent partial-wave analysis is parameterizing the pole positions in a procedure defined by the way how the continuous energy dependence is implemented. These pole positions are, henceforth, inherently model dependent. To reduce this model dependence, we use only one, coupled-channel, unitary, fully analytic method based on the isobar approximation to extract the pole positions from the each available member of the worldwide collection of partial wave amplitudes which are understood as nothing more but a good energy dependent representation of genuine experimental numbers assembled in a form of partial-wave data. In that way, the model dependence related to the different assumptions on the analytic form of the partial-wave amplitudes is avoided, and the true confidence limit for the existence of a particular resonant state, at least in one model, is established. The way how the method works, and first results are demonstrated for the S11 partial wave.

## Key claim

The authors propose that by applying a single, well-controlled pole-extraction method (the Carnegie-Mellon-Berkeley (CMB) coupled-channel model) to all published partial wave analyses, the model dependence inherent in each analysis's own analytic continuation procedure can be avoided, establishing a "true confidence limit" for the existence of a resonant state at least in one model.

## Method

The authors use the Zagreb realization of the CMB model, a three-channel (πN, ηN, and an effective π²N channel), unitary, fully analytic isobar model, to fit published partial wave amplitudes from six different analyses (KH80, VPI/GWU, Giessen, EBAC, Jülich, and Dubna-Mainz-Taipei). They treat these partial wave amplitudes as energy-dependent representations of experimental data, extract pole positions by analytically continuing the channel propagator into the complex energy plane using a Pietarienen expansion, and determine the optimal number of poles by minimizing reduced χ² while requiring visual resemblance to the data.

## Key numbers

* For the S11 partial wave, fitting only the πN elastic channel from KH80 with three resonances yields dressed pole positions at (1513, -2Im=71) MeV, (1661, -2Im=148) MeV, and (1903, -2Im=90) MeV.
* For the S11 partial wave, fitting only the πN elastic channel from WI08 with three resonances yields dressed pole positions at (1492, -2Im=89) MeV, (1646, -2Im=95) MeV, and (2684, -2Im=822) MeV.
* For the S11 partial wave, fitting only the πN elastic channel from GWU-SES with three resonances yields dressed pole positions at (1500, -2Im=106) MeV, (1646, -2Im=119) MeV, and (2598, -2Im=210) MeV.
* The reduced χ² values for the three-resonance fits range from 0.043 (WI08) to 2.252 (GWU-SES) for single-channel fits.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Höhler (1980) who introduced the speed-plot technique for pole extraction and distinguished Breit-Wigner parameters from true pole positions, and addresses the continuum ambiguity problem discussed by Ceci et al. (2006) where including inelastic channels is necessary to eliminate ambiguities in coupled-channel formalisms. The method enables a systematic comparison of pole positions from all major partial wave analyses on an equal footing, and the authors demonstrate that the S11(1535) and S11(1650) resonances are unambiguously seen in all analyzed data, while suggesting the existence of an additional S11 state near 1800 MeV consistent with photo-production results from Chen et al. (2003) and Yang et al. (2003).
