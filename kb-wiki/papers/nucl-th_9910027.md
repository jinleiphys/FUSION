# Breakup of $^8$B and the $^7$Be($p,γ)^8$B reaction

> The calculated rate of events in some of the existing solar neutrino detectors is directly proportional to the rate of the $^7$Be($p,γ)^8$B reaction measured in the laboratory at low energies. However, the low-energy cross sections of this reaction are quite uncertain as various measurements differ from each other by 30-40 %. The Coulomb dissociation process which reverses the radiative capture by the dissociation of $^8$B in the Coulomb field of a target, provides an alternate way of accessing this reaction. While this method has several advantages (like large breakup cross sections and flexibility in the kinematics), the difficulties arise from the possible interference by the nuclear interactions, uncertainties in the contributions of the various multipoles and the higher order effects, which should be considered carefully. We review the progress made so far in the experimental measurements and theoretical analysis of the breakup of $^8$B and discuss the current status of the low-energy cross sections (or the astrophysical $S$-factor) of the $^7$Be($p,γ)^8$B reaction extracted therefrom. The future directions of the experimental and theoretical investigations are also suggested.

# Breakup of $^8$B and the $^7$Be(p,γ)$^8$B reaction

## Key claim

The Coulomb dissociation method can provide a reliable extraction of the astrophysical S-factor $S_{17}(0)$ for the $^7$Be(p,γ)$^8$B reaction, but only when data are taken at very forward angles (≤ 4°) and low relative energies (< 0.75 MeV), where nuclear effects and E2 contributions are minimal; at larger angles or higher energies, the point-like projectile approximation breaks down and Coulomb-nuclear interference becomes substantial.

## Method

The authors review experimental measurements and theoretical analyses of $^8$B breakup on $^{208}$Pb and $^{58}$Ni targets at beam energies from 25.8 MeV/nucleon to 51.9 MeV/nucleon. They compare semiclassical (Alder-Winther) calculations with full quantum mechanical distorted wave Born approximation (DWBA) calculations that use a single-folding procedure to determine Coulomb and nuclear form factors from fragment-target interactions, avoiding the point-like projectile approximation and collective model prescriptions. The breakup cross sections are related to the radiative capture cross sections via the equivalent photon method and detailed balance, from which the astrophysical S-factor $S_{17}$ is extracted.

## Key numbers

- RIKEN-I data (46.5 MeV/nucleon $^8$B on $^{208}$Pb): best-fit $S_{17}$ values from pure E1 Coulomb dissociation are $(17.58 \pm 2.26)$ eV barn at $E_{cm}$ = 0.6 MeV, $(14.07 \pm 2.67)$ eV barn at $E_{cm}$ = 0.8 MeV, and $(15.59 \pm 3.49)$ eV barn at $E_{cm}$ = 1.0 MeV; extrapolation to zero energy gives $S_{17}(0) = (15.5 \pm 2.80)$ eV barn.

- The first RIKEN measurement (Motobayashi et al., 1994) assuming pure E1 excitation predicted $S_{17}(0) = 16.7 \pm 3.2$ eV barn, compared to the standard solar model value of $22.4 \pm 2.0$ eV barn used by Bahcall and Pinsonneault (1995).

- For the Notre Dame experiment (25.8 MeV $^8$B on $^{58}$Ni), the ratio of integrated breakup cross section of $^7$Be to Rutherford elastic scattering is $(8.1 \pm 0.8^{+2.0}_{-0.5}) \times 10^{-3}$; the DWBA calculation including three-body kinematics gives $7.0 \times 10^{-3}$, in close agreement.

- Low-energy cross sections of the $^7$Be(p,γ)$^8$B reaction from direct measurements disagree by 30-40% in absolute magnitude.

## Context

This review extends the work of Motobayashi et al. (1994) (RIKEN-I) and Kikuchi et al. (1997) (RIKEN-II) on Coulomb dissociation of $^8$B, and addresses the discrepancies between the extracted $S_{17}(0)$ values and the standard solar model value of Bahcall and Pinsonneault (1995). The authors show that earlier semiclassical analyses (Shyam, 1996) using the point-like projectile approximation become inaccurate beyond 4°, and that the Notre Dame experiment (Johanning et al., 1996) at subCoulomb energies cannot reliably determine the E2 component because Coulomb-nuclear interference makes the E1 component dominant at all angles. The work enables future extraction of a reliable $S_{17}(0)$ by identifying the kinematical regime where the Coulomb dissociation method's assumptions are well fulfilled, and provides the theoretical tools (full DWBA with single-folding) needed for such analyses.
