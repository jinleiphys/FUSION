# Structures in the energy distribution of the scission neutrons: finite neutron-number effect

> The scission neutron kinetic energy spectrum is calculated for $^{236}U$ in the frame of the dynamical scission model. The bi-dimensional time dependent Schrödinger equation with time dependent potential is used to propagate each neutron wave function during the scission process which is supposed to last $1\times 10^{-22}$ sec. At the end, we separate the unbound parts and continue to propagate them as long as possible (in this case $50\times 10^{-22}$ sec) in the frozen fragments approximation. At several time intervals, the Fourier transforms of these wave packets are calculated in order to obtain the corresponding momentum distributions which lead to the kinetic energy distributions. The evolution of these distributions in time provides an interesting insight into the separation of each neutron from the fissioning system and asymptotically gives the kinetic energy spectrum of that particular neutron. We group the results in substates with given projection $Ω$ of the angular momentum on the fission axis to study its influence on the spectrum. Finally, the sum over all $Ω$ values is compared with a typical evaporation spectrum as well as with recent precise measurements in the reaction $^{235}U(n_{th},f)$. Structures are present both in the scission-neutron spectrum and in the data.

## Key claim

The scission neutron kinetic energy spectrum for \(^{236}\)U, calculated using the dynamical scission model, exhibits non-smooth structures due to the finite number of neutrons that contribute (only 35 non-negligible terms), and these structures are also present in recent high-statistics experimental data for the reaction \(^{235}\)U(n\(_{th}\),f), though the data oscillate less than the calculation.

## Method

The authors solve the two-dimensional time-dependent Schrodinger equation with a time-dependent potential to propagate each neutron wave function during the scission process, assumed to last \(1\times 10^{-22}\) sec. After scission, the unbound parts of the wave packets are separated and propagated for up to \(50\times 10^{-22}\) sec in the frozen fragments approximation. Fourier transforms in cylindrical coordinates (combining Hankel and one-dimensional Fourier transforms) are calculated at several time intervals to obtain momentum and kinetic energy distributions for individual neutron states grouped by projection \(\Omega\) of angular momentum on the fission axis.

## Key numbers

- Scission process duration: \(1\times 10^{-22}\) sec; post-scission propagation up to \(T_{max} = 50\times 10^{-22}\) sec (\(5\times 10^{-21}\) sec)
- Spatial grid: \([0, 84\ \text{fm}] \times [-128\ \text{fm}, 128\ \text{fm}]\) with \(\Delta \rho = \Delta z = 1/8\) fm; time step \(\Delta t = 1/128 \times 10^{-22}\) sec
- Minimum neck radius before scission: 1.6 fm; minimum distance between fragment surfaces after scission: 0.6 fm
- Average scission neutron multiplicity: 0.6 neutrons per fission event (25% of total prompt fission neutron multiplicity)
- Potential depth \(V_0 = 40.2\) MeV; average kinetic energy of unbound neutrons immediately after scission is around 30 MeV for \(\Omega=1/2\), around 23 MeV for \(\Omega=5/2\), and around 21 MeV for \(\Omega=7/2\)
- At \(T=50\times 10^{-22}\) sec, average kinetic energy is around 3 MeV for \(\Omega=5/2\) and around 3.5 MeV for \(\Omega=7/2\)
- Total spectrum maximum around 0.7 MeV with exponentially decreasing tail to 8 MeV
- \(\Omega=1/2\) gives the dominant contribution (65%) to the total spectrum
- Number of non-negligible neutron states contributing: 35 total (21 for \(\Omega=1/2\), 8 for \(\Omega=3/2\), 4 for \(\Omega=5/2\), 2 for \(\Omega=7/2\))

## Context

This work extends the dynamical scission model previously developed by Carjan and collaborators (2013) to calculate kinetic energy spectra of scission neutrons for a fixed fragment mass division (\(A_L=96\)), complementing earlier studies of angular distributions (Carjan et al., 2016). The results are compared with recent high-statistics measurements by Al-Adili and collaborators (2016) for the reaction \(^{235}\)U(n\(_{th}\),f) and with Weisskopf evaporation spectra at nuclear temperatures of 1.0 and 0.9 MeV. The finding that scission neutrons can reproduce the high-energy tail of the prompt fission neutron spectrum, which evaporation spectra cannot, addresses a point raised by Kornilov (2010). The work enables future studies incorporating simultaneous fragment separation and re-absorption of unbound neutrons to achieve more quantitative agreement.
