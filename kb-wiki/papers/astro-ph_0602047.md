# Strangeness in Compact Stars

> Astrophysicists distinguish between three different types of compact stars. These are white dwarfs, neutron stars, and black holes. The former contain matter in one of the densest forms found in the Universe. This feature, together with the unprecedented progress in observational astronomy, makes such stars superb astrophysical laboratories for a broad range of exciting physical studies. This article studies the role of strangeness for compact star phenomenology. Strangeness is carried by hyperons, mesons, H-dibaryons, and strange quark matter, and may leave its mark in the masses, radii, cooling behavior, surface composition and the spin evolution of compact stars.

## Key claim

Strangeness, carried by hyperons, mesons, H-dibaryons, and strange quark matter, may significantly affect the masses, radii, cooling behavior, surface composition, and spin evolution of compact stars, but the equation of state at densities above nuclear saturation is extremely uncertain, so these effects depend strongly on the assumed nuclear model and the in-medium properties of strange particles.

## Method

The authors review theoretical models for the equation of state (EoS) of neutron star matter incorporating strangeness, including relativistic mean-field models (Walecka-type) for nucleons and hyperons, the MIT bag model for quark matter, and models for color-flavor locked (CFL) and two-flavor superconducting (2SC) quark phases. They apply the Gibbs conditions for phase equilibrium between hadronic and quark matter, enforce chemical equilibrium and electric charge neutrality, and solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations within general relativity to compute stellar structure.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star matter at core densities ranges from a few times \(2.5\times 10^{14}\) g/cm³ (nuclear saturation density) to ten to twenty times higher.
- The saturation density of normal nuclear matter is \(n_0 = 0.16\) fm⁻³, corresponding to an energy density of \(\epsilon_0 = 140\) MeV/fm³.
- Ignoring interactions, \(\Lambda\) hyperons appear at densities as low as \(2 n_0\) (from \(k_{F_n} \geq \sqrt{m_\Lambda^2 - m_n^2} \simeq 3\) fm⁻¹, with \(m_\Lambda = 1116\) MeV and \(m_n = 939\) MeV).
- The critical proton fraction for nucleon direct Urca processes is about 11% (or about 0.15 with muons present).
- Estimates predict onset of \(\pi^-\) condensation at densities around \(n^\pi \sim 2 n_0\) (0.16 fm⁻³), and \(K^-\) condensation threshold at least as high as \(4 n_0\).
- For the H-dibaryon with vacuum mass about 2.2 GeV and optical potential \(U_{\rm H} = -30\) MeV, a condensate could form if the limiting star mass is about \(M = 1.444\, M_\odot\) (Hulse-Taylor pulsar PSR 1913+16); with \(U_{\rm H} = +30\) MeV, formation requires masses greater than about \(1.6\, M_\odot\).
- Bag constants of \(B = 57\) MeV/fm³ (\(B^{1/4} = 145\) MeV) and \(B = 85\) MeV/fm³ (\(B^{1/4} = 160\) MeV) give strange quark matter energy per baryon of \(E/A = 829\) MeV and 915 MeV, respectively, corresponding to absolutely bound strange quark matter.
- The ratio of crustal moment of inertia to total moment of inertia for strange stars, \(I_{\rm crust}/I_{\rm total}\), varies between \(10^{-3}\) and \(10^{-5}\) at maximum mass.
- Observed pulsar glitches have relative frequency changes \(\Delta \Omega/\Omega = (10^{-9} - 10^{-6})\).
- The superfluid gap in color superconducting quark matter at astrophysically relevant chemical potentials (\(\mu < 1000\) MeV) is between 50 and 100 MeV.

## Context

This paper reviews and extends the work on strange matter in compact stars, building on earlier studies of hyperonic matter (Glen 1985), H-dibaryon condensates (Glen 1998), and color superconductivity (Rajagopal 2001, Alford 2001). It discusses how strangeness affects neutron star phenomenology including cooling via direct Urca processes (Lattimer 1991, Prakash 1992), glitch mechanisms in strange stars (Glen 1992), and the possibility of converting neutron stars into strange stars (Sakai 1997, Faessler 1997). The review enables future observational tests of the strange quark matter hypothesis through pulsar timing, cooling data, and cosmic ray experiments such as AMS and ECCO.
