# Nuclear Fission: from more phenomenology and adjusted parameters to more fundamental theory and increased predictive power

> Two major recent developments in theory and computational resources created the favorable conditions for achieving a microscopic description of nuclear fission almost eighty years after its discovery in 1939 by Hahn and Strassmann (1930). The first major development was in theory, the extension of the Time-Dependent Density Functional Theory (TDDFT) to superfluid fermion systems. The second development was in computing, the emergence of powerful enough supercomputers capable of solving the complex systems of equations describing the time evolution in three dimensions without any restrictions of hundreds of strongly interacting nucleons. Even though the available nuclear energy density functionals (NEDFs) are phenomenological still, their accuracy is improving steadily and the prospects of being able to perform calculations of the nuclear fission dynamics and to predict many properties of the fission fragments, otherwise not possible to extract from experiments, are within reach, all without making recourse anymore to uncontrollable assumptions and simplified phenomenological models.

# Nuclear Fission: from more phenomenology and adjusted parameters to more fundamental theory and increased predictive power

## Key claim
The authors argue that the Time-Dependent Superfluid Local Density Approximation (TDSLDA), an extension of Time-Dependent Density Functional Theory (TDDFT) to superfluid fermion systems, can now provide a microscopic description of nuclear fission dynamics, but they acknowledge that the available nuclear energy density functionals (NEDFs) remain phenomenological and their accuracy, while improving, still limits predictive power.

## Method
The authors performed TDSLDA simulations of induced fission of 240Pu in three dimensions without symmetry restrictions, using a phenomenological NEDF in the particle-hole channel complemented with pairing correlations. The time evolution was solved from saddle-to-scission configurations, tracking the density, pairing field magnitude, and pairing field phase for both neutrons and protons. They also developed a new NEDF called SeaLL1 with only 7 uncorrelated parameters, fitted to nuclear masses and charge radii.

## Key numbers
- Induced fission of 240Pu with normal pairing strength: saddle-to-scission time up to 14,000 fm/c (approximately 47 × 10^-21 s)
- Induced fission of 240Pu with enhanced pairing strength: saddle-to-scission time about 1,400 fm/c
- SeaLL1 NEDF: rms deviation for masses of even-even nuclei from AME2012 of 1.74 MeV
- SeaLL1 NEDF: rms deviation for charge radii of 0.034 fm
- Light fragment at scission emerges with ratio of major to minor axes close to 3/2

## Context
This work extends the microscopic description of nuclear fission beyond the liquid drop model of Bohr and Wheeler (1939) and the single-particle shell model of Mayer and Jensen, addressing the long-standing challenge of describing fission dynamics from first principles. The authors contrast their TDSLDA approach with the stochastic mean-field model of Ayik and the TDHF calculations of Tanimura, arguing that those approaches rely on ad hoc initial conditions that undermine predictive power. The development of the SeaLL1 functional and the TDSLDA framework enables future calculations of fission fragment properties such as excitation energies that are otherwise impossible to measure or compute, and opens the possibility of describing superheavy nuclear systems with charges Z ≈ 200 discussed at the FUSION17 workshop by Mittig.
