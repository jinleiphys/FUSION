# A short walk through the physics of neutron stars

> In this work we shortly review several aspects of the physics of neutron stars. After the introduction we present a brief historical overview of the idea of neutron stars as well as of the theoretical and observational developments that followed it from the mid 1930s to the present. Then, we review few aspects of their observation discussing, in particular, the different types of telescopes that are used, the many astrophysical manifestations of these objects, and several observables such as masses, radii or gravitational waves. Finally, we briefly summarize some of theoretical issues like their composition, structure equations, equation of state, and neutrino emission and cooling.

## Key claim

This paper is a pedagogical review that surveys the observational and theoretical landscape of neutron star physics, rather than presenting a single new result. The author explicitly states that a complete review is impossible within the scope of the lecture, so the work offers only a "brush-stroke" overview of the field.

## Method

The paper follows the structure of a lecture given at the school "Rewriting Nuclear Physics Textbooks" at the University of Pisa in July 2017. It first provides a historical overview of neutron star theory and observation from the 1930s to the present, then reviews observational methods including telescopes across the electromagnetic spectrum, neutrino observatories, and gravitational wave detectors. Finally, it summarizes theoretical aspects such as composition, structure equations, equation of state, neutrino emission, and cooling.

## Key numbers

- Neutron stars have mass numbers of order A ~ 10^56-10^57, radii of about 10-12 km, and masses in the range M ~ 1-2 M_sun (where M_sun ~ 2 x 10^33 g)
- Oppenheimer and Volkoff (1939) found stable static neutron stars could not have masses larger than ~0.7 M_sun for non-interacting neutrons
- The Hulse-Taylor binary pulsar has measured masses M_p = 1.4408 ± 0.0003 M_sun and M_c = 1.3873 ± 0.0003 M_sun
- Millisecond pulsar PSR J1614-2230 has mass M_p = 1.928 ± 0.017 M_sun
- Millisecond pulsar PSR J0348+0432 has mass M_p = 2.01 ± 0.04 M_sun
- Analysis of quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries indicates neutron star radii in the range 10.4-12.9 km (Steiner et al.) or ~10 km or less (Guillot et al.)
- The fastest known pulsar, PSR J1748-2446ad, has a rotational period of 1.39595482 ms
- Surface magnetic field strengths range from 10^8-10^9 G (millisecond pulsars), ~10^12 G (normal pulsars), to 10^14-10^15 G (magnetars)
- Normal nuclear saturation density is rho_0 = 2.8 x 10^14 g/cm^3
- Glitches show relative rotational frequency increases Delta-Omega/Omega from ~10^-10 to ~5 x 10^-6
- Gravitational redshift measurements support a neutron star redshift range of 0.2 ≤ z ≤ 0.5, with highest concentration in 0.25 ≤ z ≤ 0.35

## Context

This review extends the pedagogical tradition of neutron star physics overviews, referencing foundational works such as Baade and Zwicky (1934), Oppenheimer and Volkoff (1939), and the historical development through Cameron (1959), Tsuruta and Cameron (1966), and the discovery of the first pulsar by Bell and Hewish (1968). The paper enables newcomers to the field to understand the interdisciplinary nature of neutron star research, connecting nuclear physics, general relativity, and observational astronomy. It highlights open questions including the true behavior of the nuclear equation of state at very high densities, the resolution of conflicting radius measurements from Steiner et al. and Guillot et al., and the unresolved mechanism for generating strong magnetic fields in neutron stars.
