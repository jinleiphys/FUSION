# Exotic Stable Branches with Efficient TOV Sequences

> Modern inference schemes for the neutron star equation of state (EoS) require large numbers of stellar models constructed with different EoS, and these stellar models must capture all the behavior of stable stars. I introduce termination conditions for sequences of stellar models for cold, non-rotating neutron stars that can identify all stable stellar configurations up to arbitrarily large central pressures along with an efficient algorithm to build accurate interpolators for macroscopic properties. I explore the behavior of stars with both high- and low-central pressures. Interestingly, I find that EoS with monotonically increasing sound-speed can produce multiple stable branches (twin stars) and that large phase transitions at high densities can produce stable branches at nearly any mass scale, including sub-solar masses, while still supporting stars with $M > 2\,M_\odot$. I conclude with some speculation about the astrophysical implications of this behavior.

## Key claim

The paper introduces termination conditions for sequences of cold, non-rotating neutron star models that can identify all stable stellar configurations up to arbitrarily large central pressures, finding that equations of state with monotonically increasing sound-speed can produce multiple stable branches (twin stars) and that large phase transitions at high densities can produce stable branches at nearly any mass scale including sub-solar masses, while still supporting stars with M > 2 solar masses.

## Method

The author solves the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for sequences of central pressures using an enthalpy formulation, which provides a factor of 5-10 speedup compared to the standard formulation. An adaptive recursive bisection algorithm places additional stellar models only where errors in interpolated curves are large, achieving an order of magnitude reduction in computational cost per equation of state compared to uniform grid placements in ln(p_c). The algorithm terminates when the n=1 radial mode loses stability at high central pressures, which signals the final spiral towards the fixed point mass M_circlearrowleft.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of neutron star stability and twin stars by Harrison (1965), Bardeen (1966), and Sorkin (1981, 1982), who established the static stability criteria for stellar models. The paper contradicts claims in the literature that twin stars robustly imply the presence of a phase transition with non-monotonic sound-speed, showing instead that monotonically increasing sound-speed can produce twin stars. The work also extends categorization schemes for twin-star branches by Alford (2013) and Christian (2017), and enables more efficient Bayesian inference for the neutron star equation of state by reducing computational cost per equation of state proposal. The findings suggest that sub-solar mass compact objects with small tidal deformability may not be unambiguous evidence for black holes, as previously argued by Crescimbeni (2024) and Golomb (2024).
