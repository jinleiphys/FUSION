# N* Spectroscopy from Lattice QCD: The Roper Explained

> This brief review focuses on the low-lying even- and odd-parity excitations of the nucleon obtained in recent lattice QCD calculations. Commencing with a survey of the 2014-15 literature we'll see that results for the first even-parity excitation energy can differ by as much as 1 GeV, a rather unsatisfactory situation. Following a brief review of the methods used to isolate excitations of the nucleon in lattice QCD, and drawing on recent advances, we'll see how a consensus on the low-lying spectrum has emerged among many different lattice groups. To provide insight into the nature of these states we'll review the wave functions and electromagnetic form factors that are available for a few of these states. Consistent with the Luscher formalism for extracting phase shifts from finite volume spectra, the Hamiltonian approach to effective field theory in finite volume can provide guidance on the manner in which physical quantities manifest themselves in the finite volume of the lattice. With this insight, we will address the question; Have we seen the Roper in lattice QCD?

## Key claim

The Roper resonance has been partially observed in lattice QCD, but only the component that resembles a constituent quark model state; the full resonance is dynamically generated with only a small bare-state component, and the finite-volume lattice results sit on energy eigenstates dominated by that bare-state component rather than on the low-lying scattering states.

## Method

The authors review lattice QCD calculations of the nucleon spectrum from the CSSM, JLab Hadron Spectrum Collaboration, Cyprus group, and χQCD collaboration, comparing results obtained using different interpolating field bases and analysis methods. They use Hamiltonian Effective Field Theory (HEFT) to connect finite-volume lattice energy levels to infinite-volume resonance properties, predicting which states three-quark operators are most likely to excite based on the bare-state component of each finite-volume eigenstate.

## Key numbers

- Results for the first even-parity excitation of the nucleon at \(m_\pi^2 \sim 0.15\) GeV\(^2\) ranged from 1.5 GeV (χQCD collaboration) to 2.5 GeV (Cyprus group) in early 2014
- The Cyprus group's results shifted from \(\sim 2.5\) GeV to \(\sim 1.8\) GeV between versions of their preprint
- The CSSM collaboration used RMS smearing radii from 0.1 to 1.5 fm, extending beyond the node at 0.83 fm in the first even-parity excitation wave function
- CSSM results used approximately 29,500 propagators on PACS-CS configurations with spatial length \(L \simeq 3.0\) fm
- Cyprus group volumes had length \(L \simeq 2.8\) fm; JLab HSC volumes had length \(L \simeq 2\) fm
- In the ground state nucleon, the bare state provides \(\sim 80\%\) of the basis-state contributions
- At the lightest quark masses, the first and second most probable states have four to five times the bare-state strength of the third most-probable state

## Context

This review extends the work of the χQCD collaboration (Liu et al., 2014), the Cyprus group (Alexandrou et al., 2013, 2014), the CSSM collaboration (Mahbub et al., 2010, 2013), and the JLab Hadron Spectrum Collaboration (Edwards et al., 2011), showing how a consensus on the low-lying nucleon excitation spectrum has emerged after the Cyprus group revised their earlier results using the Athens Model Independent Analysis Scheme. The HEFT approach (Hall et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2015) provides a framework for connecting finite-volume lattice results to infinite-volume resonance properties, analogous to the Luscher formalism, and enables future lattice simulations to constrain the HEFT model from first principles of QCD to determine how QCD gives rise to the Roper resonance.
