# Phase Transitions, Inhomogeneous Horizons and Second-Order Hydrodynamics

> We use holography to study the spinodal instability of a four-dimensional, strongly-coupled gauge theory with a first-order thermal phase transition. We place the theory on a cylinder in a set of homogeneous, unstable initial states. The dual gravity configurations are black branes afflicted by a Gregory-Laflamme instability. We numerically evolve Einstein's equations to follow the instability until the system settles down to a stationary, inhomogeneous black brane. The dual gauge theory states have constant temperature but non-constant energy density. We show that the time evolution of the instability and the final states are accurately described by second-order hydrodynamics. In the static limit, the latter reduces to a single, second-order, non-linear differential equation from which the inhomogeneous final states can be derived.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that second-order hydrodynamics accurately describes both the time evolution and the final stationary, inhomogeneous states resulting from the spinodal instability in a strongly coupled gauge theory with a first-order thermal phase transition, even when spatial gradients are large and second-order gradient corrections are as large as the equilibrium pressure.

## Method

The authors use holography (gauge/string duality) with a five-dimensional Einstein-plus-scalar model whose dual is a four-dimensional strongly coupled gauge theory deformed by a dimension-three scalar operator. They place the theory on a cylinder with compactified spatial direction, initialize homogeneous unstable states in the spinodal region of the phase diagram, perturb them with a specific Fourier mode, and numerically evolve Einstein's equations to follow the Gregory-Laflamme-type instability until the system settles into a stationary, inhomogeneous black brane. The boundary stress tensor extracted from the dynamical metric is then compared to predictions from second-order hydrodynamic constitutive relations.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature of the first-order phase transition: \(T_c = 0.247 \Lambda\)
- Initial state energy density: \(\mathcal{E}/\Lambda^4 \simeq 0.0096\), with temperature \(T_i \simeq 0.251 \Lambda\) and entropy density \(s_i = 0.037 \Lambda^3\)
- Perturbation wavenumber: \(k = 3(2\pi/L) \simeq 1.3 T_i\), with growth rate \(\Gamma = 0.0247 \Lambda\) from the sound dispersion relation
- Final state temperature: \(T_f = 0.250 \Lambda\)
- Domain size in final state: \(\Delta z = L/3 \simeq 0.4/T_f\)
- Growth rate of the \(n=3\) mode agrees with the linearized prediction within 4%
- Final average entropy density is only 1% larger than the initial one
- Quasi-normal mode decay rate: \(\Gamma_{\rm QNM} \sim c\, \pi T\) with \(c \simeq 3.4\)
- Squared sound speed in the unstable region: \(|c_s|^2 \simeq 0.03\)

## Context

This work extends previous holographic studies of hydrodynamics in strongly coupled theories with large gradients (Chesler 2010, Heller 2011, Casalderrey-Solana 2013, Attems 2016) to the case of a first-order phase transition. It provides the first complete first-principles dynamical evolution of a spinodal instability in a strongly coupled gauge theory and the first full dynamical evolution of a Gregory-Laflamme-type instability in asymptotically anti-de Sitter space. The results enable future studies of phase separation dynamics and the thermodynamics of inhomogeneous stationary states, and suggest exploring stronger first-order transitions where the sound speed can become of order unity.
