# Energy partition between splitting fission fragments

> From the microscopic view, the energy partition between two fission fragments are associated with the splitting of wave functions of an entangled fissioning system, in contrast to most fission models using an explicit statistical partition of excitation energies by invoking level densities of fragments. The dynamical fission evolution is described within the time-dependent Hartree-Fock+BCS framework. Excitation energies of isotopic fission fragments are obtained with the particle-number projection method after the dynamical splitting of $^{238}$U. The resulting excitation energies of light and heavy fragments illustrate the appearance of sawtooth structures. We find that the paring strengths have significant influences on the partition of excitation energies. Furthermore, excitation energies of isotopic fragments increase with increasing neutron numbers, suppressing the production of neutron-rich beams in rare-isotope beam facilities.

# Energy partition between splitting fission fragments

## Key claim

The excitation energy partition between fission fragments arises from quantum entanglement and the splitting of wave functions of the entangled fissioning system, rather than from a statistical equilibrium based on level densities. The sawtooth structure in excitation energies of isotopic fragments is explained by positive slopes in the energy distributions, though the slopes are overestimated and would be alleviated by fluctuation effects.

## Method

The dynamical fission evolution of $^{238}$U beyond the saddle point is described using the time-dependent Hartree-Fock+BCS (TD-BCS) framework with Sky3D solver, using SkM$^{*}$ and UNEDF1 Skyrme forces with mixing-type pairing interactions. After the dynamical splitting, excitation energies of isotopic fission fragments are obtained via double particle-number projection (PNP) on both the total space and a partial space, where the projected binding energy of each fragment is subtracted from its ground state energy. The calculations include variations in pairing strengths (factors of 0.0, 0.9, and 1.2 relative to standard values) to study their influence on energy partition.

## Key numbers

- Total kinetic energy (TKE) from SkM$^{*}$ calculations: 168.9 MeV; from UNEDF1 calculations: 159.5 MeV; experimental average TKE from photofission of $^{238}$U is around 170 MeV
- TKE for varying pairing strengths: 174.2 MeV (factor 0.0, TDHF), 169.6 MeV (factor 0.9), 160.3 MeV (factor 1.2)
- Fission yield peak location: around A=136 with SkM$^{*}$ force; around A=138 with UNEDF1 force; half-widths about 8 particles
- Peak location shifts to A=142 when pairing strength increased by factor 1.2; peak around A=132 with TDHF (zero pairing)
- Cutoff applied at $5\times10^{-4}$ for PNP calculations when denominator becomes tiny

## Context

This work extends the microscopic TD-DFT approach to fission energy partition, building on previous studies of $^{240}$Pu fission (Qiang 2023) that showed light fragments acquire more excitation than heavy fragments. It contrasts with conventional statistical models that invoke level densities (Albertsson 2020, Schmidt 2016) and shape-dependent level densities, which slightly underestimate sawtooth slopes. The results have practical implications for estimating beam intensities at rare-isotope beam facilities such as FRIB, RIBF, and HIAF, where the finding that heavier isotopes have higher excitation energies (leading to more neutron evaporation) suggests suppression of neutron-rich beam production.
