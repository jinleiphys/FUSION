# Factorization breaking for higher moments of harmonic flow

> We study correlations between harmonic flow vectors squared measured at different transverse momenta. One of the flow harmonics squared is taken at a fixed transverse momentum and correlated to the momentum averaged harmonic flow squared of the same order. Such four particle correlators, dependent on transverse momentum, have been recently measured experimentally. Factorization coefficients based on the ratio of such four-particle correlators allow the independent measurement of the flow vector and flow magnitude factorization breaking coefficient. Moreover, the correlation of the angles of flow harmonics as a function of transverse momentum can be extracted. Results are compared to preliminary data of the ALICE Collaboration. We also present predictions for the momentum dependent factorization breaking coefficient between mixed flow harmonics. The correlators with squares of mixed harmonics can serve as a way to independently measure the flow vector, flow magnitude, and flow angle correlations, and could be used to gain additional information on the fluctuating initial state and the dynamics in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that four-particle correlators involving harmonic flow vectors squared can separately measure flow vector, flow magnitude, and flow angle decorrelations as a function of transverse momentum, but the model calculations for semi-central collisions (30-40% centrality) show significantly stronger decorrelation than preliminary ALICE data, suggesting substantial non-flow contributions may be present in the experimental results.

## Method

The authors used a boost-invariant version of the hydrodynamic model MUSIC to simulate Pb+Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV, with initial entropy distributions from both a two-component Glauber Monte Carlo model and the TRENTO model, using a constant shear viscosity to entropy density ratio of 0.08. They computed factorization breaking coefficients for flow vectors squared, flow magnitudes squared, and flow angle correlations as functions of transverse momentum, comparing these to preliminary ALICE Collaboration data. For mixed harmonics, they calculated correlation coefficients between higher powers of flow vectors (e.g., V_2^4 and V_4(p)^2) and extracted flow angle decorrelations from the ratio of vector and magnitude correlators.

## Key numbers

* For central collisions (0-5% centrality), the model results for the elliptic flow vector squared factorization breaking coefficient r_2;2(p) are similar to preliminary ALICE data across the transverse momentum range
* For semi-central collisions (30-40% centrality), the model shows much stronger decorrelation for flow vectors squared than the data, with the main difference appearing for p > 2.0 GeV
* The magnitude decorrelation accounts for roughly one half of the flow vector decorrelation, expressed as [1 - r_n;2(p)] approximately equal to 2[1 - r_n^{v_n^2}(p)]
* The deviation of the experimental approximation factor from 1 is less than 6 x 10^{-3} for 0.5 GeV < p < 3.0 GeV
* For mixed harmonics, the correlation coefficient between V_2^4 and V_4(p)^2 is smaller than for the lower powers V_2^2 and V_4(p), with the flow magnitude decorrelation accounting for about one half of the flow vector decorrelation in semi-central collisions

## Context

This work extends previous studies of flow decorrelation in transverse momentum by Gardim (2012), Kozlov (2014), and Bozek (2018), and parallels similar analyses of longitudinal flow decorrelation by Jia (2014) and the ATLAS Collaboration (2017). The paper enables experimental separation of flow vector, flow magnitude, and flow angle decorrelations using four-particle correlators, which can provide additional constraints on fluctuating initial state models and hydrodynamic evolution in heavy-ion collisions. The authors note that the discrepancy between model and data for semi-central collisions requires further study to disentangle genuine flow fluctuation differences from remaining non-flow effects.
