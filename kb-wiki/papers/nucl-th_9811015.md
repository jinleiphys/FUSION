# Modelling the many-body dynamics of heavy ion collisions: Present status and future perspective

> Basic problems of the semiclassical microscopic modelling of strongly interactingsystems are discussed within the framework of Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD). This model allows to study the influence of several types of nucleonic interactions on a large variety of observables and phenomena occurring in heavy ion collisions at relativistic energies.It is shown that the same predictions can be obtained with several -- numerically completely different and independently written -- programs as far as the same model parameters are employed and the same basic approximations are made. Many observables are robust against variations of the details of the model assumptions used. Some of the physical results, however, depend also on rather technical parameters like the preparation of the initial configuration in phase space. This crucial problem is connected with the description of the ground state of single nuclei,which differs among the various approaches. An outlook to an improved molecular dynamics scheme for heavy ion collisions is given.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that several independently written Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) programs yield the same physical predictions when the same model parameters and basic approximations are employed, but that some results depend on technical parameters like the preparation of the initial configuration in phase space, particularly the description of the ground state of single nuclei.

## Method

The authors compare multiple numerical realizations of the QMD model, which represents each nucleon as a coherent state (Gaussian wave packet) with six time-dependent parameters and evolves the many-body system via a generalized variational principle yielding Hamilton-like equations of motion. The model includes a Hamiltonian with Skyrme-type contact interactions, a finite-range Yukawa potential, momentum-dependent interactions, and Coulomb forces, with binary collisions treated stochastically using free nucleon-nucleon cross sections and Pauli blocking. The authors systematically examine how differences in the description of the initial ground state nuclei, interaction ranges, and other technical parameters affect observables in heavy ion collisions at relativistic energies.

## Key numbers

- The compressibility for a hard equation of state is kappa = 380 MeV and for a soft equation of state is kappa = 200 MeV
- The initial test particles are randomly distributed in a coordinate space sphere of radius R = 1.12 A^(1/3) fm
- The saturation density condition fixes the binding energy at E/A = -16 MeV at rho = rho_0
- For the system Au(1 AGeV)+Au at impact parameter b=3 fm with hard equation of state, the directed flow p_x^dir rises from 98 +/- 3 MeV/c to 110 +/- 3 MeV/c when changing the reference system from nucleon-nucleon CM to Lab system
- The pion number N_pi falls from 64 +/- 1 to 60 +/- 1 under the same reference system change
- Pauli blockers in VUU and QMD show efficiencies of about 94-96 percent

## Context

This work extends the Quantum Molecular Dynamics approach developed by Aichelin (1991) and compares it with one-body transport models like VUU (Kruse, 1985), BUU (Bertsch, 1984), and Landau-Vlasov (Gregoire, 1987). The paper addresses conflicting results reported in comparisons of different QMD numerical realizations (QMDvsQMD) and demonstrates that most discrepancies arise from different descriptions of the initial ground state nuclei rather than from the collision dynamics themselves. The findings enable future development of improved molecular dynamics schemes for heavy ion collision simulations, particularly by clarifying which technical parameters must be carefully controlled to obtain reliable predictions.
