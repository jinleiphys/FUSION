# Nuclear Reactions in the Crusts of Accreting Neutron Stars

> X-ray observations of transiently accreting neutron stars during quiescence provide information about the structure of neutron star crusts and the properties of dense matter. Interpretation of the observational data requires an understanding of the nuclear reactions that heat and cool the crust during accretion, and define its nonequilibrium composition. We identify here in detail the typical nuclear reaction sequences down to a depth in the inner crust where the mass density is 2E12 g/cm^3 using a full nuclear reaction network for a range of initial compositions. The reaction sequences differ substantially from previous work. We find a robust reduction of crust impurity at the transition to the inner crust regardless of initial composition, though shell effects can delay the formation of a pure crust somewhat to densities beyond 2E12 g/cm^3. This naturally explains the small inner crust impurity inferred from observations of a broad range of systems. The exception are initial compositions with A >= 102 nuclei, where the inner crust remains impure with an impurity parameter of Qimp~20 due to the N = 82 shell closure. In agreement with previous work we find that nuclear heating is relatively robust and independent of initial composition, while cooling via nuclear Urca cycles in the outer crust depends strongly on initial composition. This work forms a basis for future studies of the sensitivity of crust models to nuclear physics and provides profiles of composition for realistic crust models.

## Key claim

The paper identifies the detailed nuclear reaction sequences in accreting neutron star crusts down to a density of 2 × 10¹² g/cm³, finding a robust reduction of crust impurity at the transition to the inner crust regardless of initial composition, though shell effects can delay the formation of a pure crust to densities beyond 2 × 10¹² g/cm³; the exception is initial compositions with A ≥ 102 nuclei, where the inner crust remains impure with an impurity parameter Qimp ≈ 20 due to the N = 82 shell closure.

## Method

The authors used a full nuclear reaction network including electron captures, β-decays, neutron captures, neutron dissociations, and pycnonuclear fusion reactions to track the compositional evolution of an accreted fluid element as it is compressed by increasing pressure in a plane-parallel slab model with constant gravity. They employed the FRDM mass model for nuclear masses, QRPA-fY weak interaction rates, TALYS statistical model for neutron capture rates, and pycnonuclear fusion rates from Be to Si, running calculations for four different initial compositions (pure ⁵⁶Fe, extreme X-ray burst ashes, KEPLER burst ashes, and superburst ashes) up to a column depth of about 2 × 10¹⁶ g/cm².

## Key numbers

- The calculation starts at a density of 1.4 × 10⁹ g/cm³ and reaches a final density of 2.4 × 10¹² g/cm³, corresponding to a column depth of 2.1 × 10¹⁶ g/cm²
- An accreted fluid element takes about 24,000 years to reach the end of the calculation at column depth 2 × 10¹⁶ g/cm²
- For initial ⁵⁶Fe composition, the first electron capture sequence (⁵⁶Fe → ⁵⁶Cr) occurs at ρ = 4.9 × 10⁹ g/cm³ and μe = 6.2 MeV
- Neutron drip (appearance of free neutrons) occurs around ρ = 6 × 10¹¹ g/cm³
- The first pycnonuclear fusion reaction (⁴⁰Mg + ⁴⁰Mg → ⁸⁰Cr) begins at ρ = 1.1 × 10¹² g/cm³
- For initial compositions with A ≥ 102 nuclei, the inner crust impurity parameter is Qimp ≈ 20
- The neutron mass fraction at the end of the calculation reaches 46% for the ⁵⁶Fe initial composition
- The accretion rate used is 0.3 times the Eddington accretion rate, with local Eddington accretion rate = 8.8 × 10⁴ g cm⁻² s⁻¹

## Context

This work extends the pioneering studies of Bisnovatyi-Kogan (1979), Sato (1979), and Haensel (1990) by using a full reaction network that allows multiple species and includes shell structure, going beyond the simplified single-species, equilibrium models of those earlier works. It builds on the network calculations of Gupta (2007, 2008) that first included electron captures with excited states and superthreshold electron capture cascades, and on Schatz (2014) that discovered nuclear Urca cycles in the outer crust. The results provide composition profiles for realistic crust models that can be used to interpret X-ray observations of quiescent neutron stars and to constrain dense matter properties, thermal conductivity, and the need for additional shallow heating sources.
