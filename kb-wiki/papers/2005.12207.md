# Scattering Observables from One- and Two-Body Densities: Formalism and Application to $γ\,{}^3$He Scattering

> We introduce the transition-density formalism, an efficient and general method for calculating the interaction of external probes with light nuclei. One- and two-body transition densities that encode the nuclear structure of the target are evaluated once and stored. They are then convoluted with an interaction kernel to produce amplitudes, and hence observables. By choosing different kernels, the same densities can be used for any reaction in which a probe interacts perturbatively with the target. The method therefore exploits the factorisation between nuclear structure and interaction kernel that occurs in such processes. We study in detail the convergence in the number of partial waves for matrix elements relevant in elastic Compton scattering on $^3$He. The results are fully consistent with our previous calculations in Chiral Effective Field Theory. But the new approach is markedly more computationally efficient, which facilitates the inclusion of more partial-wave channels in the calculation. We also discuss the usefulness of the transition-density method for other nuclei and reactions. Calculations of elastic Compton scattering on heavier targets like $^4$He are straightforward extensions of this study, since the same interaction kernels are used. And the generality of the formalism means that our $^3$He densities can be used to evaluate any $^3$He elastic-scattering observable with contributions from one- and two-body operators. They are available at https://datapub.fz-juelich.de/anogga.

# Scattering Observables from One- and Two-Body Densities: Formalism and Application to γ³He Scattering

## Key claim

The transition-density formalism factorises nuclear structure from the reaction mechanism for external-probe scattering, enabling the same pre-computed one- and two-body densities to be reused for any elastic-scattering observable on ³He, provided the probe interacts perturbatively and the energy is large compared to the nuclear binding scale (so that collective "rescattering" contributions are negligible). The method is fully consistent with previous Chiral Effective Field Theory calculations but is markedly more computationally efficient, speeding up evaluation of Compton matrix elements by a factor of 10 or more.

## Method

One- and two-body transition densities are computed once from momentum-space Faddeev solutions of the non-relativistic Schrödinger equation for ³He, using two combinations of NN and 3N interactions (AV18+Urbana-IX and chiral Idaho N³LO at 500 MeV with a O(Q³) 3N interaction). These densities, defined in momentum space as functions of Jacobi momenta, Faddeev angular-momentum quantum numbers, momentum transfer, and energy, are stored in machine-independent hdf5 format. For any given reaction, the densities are then convoluted with the appropriate one- and two-body interaction kernels (current operators in the momentum-spin basis) to produce scattering amplitudes and observables.

## Key numbers

- Computational speed-up factor of "10 or more" for evaluation of Compton matrix elements compared to the traditional approach of refs. Shukla (2008), Choudhury (2007), and Margaryan (2018).
- Densities are provided for photon energies up to approximately 120 MeV, the pion-production threshold below which elastic Compton scattering is considered.
- Two interaction combinations used: AV18 with Urbana-IX 3N interaction (relatively "hard") and chiral Idaho N³LO at cutoff 500 MeV with O(Q³) χEFT 3N interaction of variant "b" from Nogga (2005).
- Densities publicly available at https://datapub.fz-juelich.de/anogga.

## Context

This work extends the factorisation approach used in lattice QCD (where gauge configurations are computed once and reused) and in shell-model calculations using one-body density operators (Burrows, 2017-2020), but goes beyond those by allowing for momentum transfer and defining densities in momentum space for three-body systems. The formalism enables straightforward extension to elastic Compton scattering on ⁴He and heavier targets using the same interaction kernels, and provides a path to calculations of electro-disintegration, beta decay, inelastic neutrino scattering, and charged-pion photoproduction. The densities are tested against previous ³He Compton scattering calculations by Shukla (2008), Choudhury (2007), and Margaryan (2018), which used a "traditional" approach with significantly higher computational cost.
