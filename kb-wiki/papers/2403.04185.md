# The stochastic relativistic advection diffusion equation from the Metropolis algorithm

> We study an approach to simulating the stochastic relativistic advection-diffusion equation based on the Metropolis algorithm. We show that the dissipative dynamics of the boosted fluctuating fluid can be simulated by making random transfers of charge between fluid cells, interspersed with ideal hydrodynamic time steps. The random charge transfers are accepted or rejected in a Metropolis step using the entropy as a statistical weight. This procedure reproduces the expected strains of dissipative relativistic hydrodynamics in a specific (and non-covariant) hydrodynamic frame known as the density frame. Numerical results, both with and without noise, are presented and compared to relativistic kinetics and analytical expectations. An all order resummation of the density frame gradient expansion reproduces the covariant dynamics in a specific model. In contrast to all other numerical approaches to relativistic dissipative fluids, the dissipative fluid formalism presented here is strictly first order in gradients and has no non-hydrodynamic modes. The physical naturalness and simplicity of the Metropolis algorithm, together with its convergence properties, make it a promising tool for simulating stochastic relativistic fluids in heavy ion collisions and for critical phenomena in the relativistic domain.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the stochastic relativistic advection-diffusion equation can be simulated using the Metropolis algorithm in a specific non-covariant hydrodynamic frame (the "density frame"), which is strictly first order in gradients and has no non-hydrodynamic modes, but only for fluids where the underlying interactions are not required to be fully Lorentz invariant.

## Method

The authors implement a Metropolis algorithm that alternates between ideal hydrodynamic time steps (advecting charge with the fluid flow) and random charge transfers between neighboring fluid cells, where each transfer is accepted or rejected based on the change in entropy of the system. The dissipative current in the density frame is derived from the covariant Landau-Lifshitz frame by using the ideal equations of motion to replace time derivatives with spatial derivatives, yielding a constitutive relation that is first order in spatial gradients only. Numerical correlation functions from the Metropolis algorithm for an equilibrated boosted fluid are compared to relativistic kinetic theory and analytical expectations.

## Key numbers

- The shear viscosity to entropy ratio from Bayesian fits is given as approximately η/s ≈ 2 ℏ/(4π k_B), indicating a strongly coupled medium with relaxation rates of order ∼ k_B T/ℏ.
- The diffusion coefficient in the density frame parallel to the flow is σ_∥(β⁰, v)/β⁰ = Tσ(β)/γ², where γ is the Lorentz factor of the fluid velocity.
- The diffusion coefficient perpendicular to the flow is σ_⟂(β⁰, v)/β⁰ = Tσ(β), independent of the Lorentz factor.

## Context

This work extends the Metropolis-based approach to stochastic hydrodynamics previously applied to non-relativistic diffusion and the O(4) critical point by Florio et al. (2021) and to Model B and Model H dynamics by Chattopadhyay et al. (2023, 2024). The density frame formalism was developed by Armas and Jain (2020) as an alternative to Israel-Stewart (Israel, 1976; Israel and Stewart, 1979) and BDNK (Bemfica, Disconzi, and Noronha, 2017; Kovtun, 2019) approaches to relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics. The method enables simulation of stochastic relativistic fluids in heavy ion collisions and for critical phenomena in the relativistic domain, particularly for small colliding systems where fluctuations become important.
