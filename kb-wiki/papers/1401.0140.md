# Hadron Polarizabilities

> Electromagnetic polarizabilities describe the response of a system to the application of an external quasi-static electric or magnetic field. In this article experimental and theoretical work addressing the polarizabilities of the light hadrons is examined.

## Key claim

Electromagnetic polarizabilities of light hadrons (proton, neutron, charged pion) provide the leading-order correction to the Thomson scattering amplitude and are fundamental structure properties, but their extraction requires careful separation of higher-order effects and relies on dispersion-relation codes or effective-field-theory calculations to handle photon energies in the 50–100 MeV range where polarizability effects become sizable without bringing in large higher-order contributions.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental determinations of hadron polarizabilities via Compton scattering (real and virtual), slow-neutron electromagnetic scattering in the Coulomb field of heavy nuclei, and quasi-free Compton scattering on neutrons bound in deuterons. On the theoretical side, it examines calculations using heavy-baryon chiral perturbation theory (HBChPT) at orders \(q^3\) and \(q^4\), relativistic chiral perturbation theory (RChPT) with infrared regularization, dispersion-relation sum rules (Baldin sum rule and Bernabeu-Ericson-Ferro Fontan-Tarrach sum rule), and constituent quark models.

## Key numbers

- Proton electric polarizability: \(\alpha_E^p = (11.2 \pm 0.4) \times 10^{-4} \, \text{fm}^3\) (PDG average from multiple Compton scattering experiments)
- Proton magnetic polarizability: \(\beta_M^p = (2.5 \mp 0.4) \times 10^{-4} \, \text{fm}^3\) (PDG average)
- Baldin sum rule for proton: \(\alpha_E^p + \beta_M^p = (13.69 \pm 0.14) \times 10^{-4} \, \text{fm}^3\) (Babusci 1997), \((14.0 \pm 0.5) \times 10^{-4} \, \text{fm}^3\) (Levchuk 1999), \((13.8 \pm 0.4) \times 10^{-4} \, \text{fm}^3\) (Olm)
- Neutron electric polarizability from slow-neutron scattering: \(\alpha_E^n = (12.0 \pm 1.5 \pm 2.0) \times 10^{-4} \, \text{fm}^3\) (Schmiedmayer 1991)
- Neutron electric polarizability PDG average: \(\alpha_E^n = (11.6 \pm 1.5) \times 10^{-4} \, \text{fm}^3\)
- Neutron magnetic polarizability PDG average: \(\beta_M^n = (3.7 \pm 2.0) \times 10^{-4} \, \text{fm}^3\)
- Baldin sum rule for neutron: \(\alpha_E^n + \beta_M^n = (15.2 \pm 0.5) \times 10^{-4} \, \text{fm}^3\) (Levchuk 1999)
- HBChPT at \(q^3\): \(\alpha_{E,\text{HB},q^3}^p = 12.6 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{fm}^3\), \(\beta_{M,\text{HB},q^3}^p = 1.26 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{fm}^3\)
- HBChPT at \(q^4\): \(\alpha_{E,\text{HB},q^4}^p = (10.5 \pm 2.0) \times 10^{-4} \, \text{fm}^3\), \(\beta_{M,\text{HB},q^4}^p = (3.5 \pm 3.6) \times 10^{-4} \, \text{fm}^3\) (Bernard 1993)
- Thomson cross sections: electron 0.665 barn, pion 8.84 \(\mu\)barn, proton 197 nbarn

## Context

This review extends the body of work on hadron polarizabilities that began with the concept from classical electrodynamics and was applied to hadrons via Compton scattering (Klein 1955, Friar 1988, Holstein 1990, 1992). It summarizes experimental results from Federspiel (1991), Zieger (1992), MacGibbon, and the TAPS collaboration for the proton, and from Schmiedmayer (1991), Rose (1990), Kossert (2002), and Lundin (2002) for the neutron. The theoretical framework builds on chiral perturbation theory (Gasser 1983, 1987) and dispersion-relation approaches (Gell-Mann 1954, Baldin sum rule). The review enables future work on spin-dependent polarizabilities, virtual Compton scattering for polarizability distributions within the proton, and precision determinations of other observables such as the Lamb shift in muonic hydrogen and the hadronic light-by-light contribution to the muon anomalous magnetic moment.
