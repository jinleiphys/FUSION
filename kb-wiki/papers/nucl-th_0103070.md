# Nuclear binding energies: Global collective structure and local shell-model correlations

> Nuclear binding energies and two-neutron separation energies are analyzed starting from the liquid-drop model and the nuclear shell model in order to describe the global trends of the above observables. We subsequently concentrate on the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) and discuss a new method in order to provide a consistent description of both, ground-state and excited-state properties. We address the artefacts that appear when crossing mid-shell using the IBM formulation and perform detailed numerical calculations for nuclei situated in the 50-82 shell. We also concentrate on local deviations from the above global trends in binding energy and two-neutron separation energies that appear in the neutron-deficient Pb region. We address possible effects on the binding energy, caused by mixing of low-lying $0^{+}$ intruder states into the ground state, using configuration mixing in the IBM framework. We also study ground-state properties using a deformed mean-field approach. Detailed comparisons with recent experimental data in the Pb region are amply discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the linear drop in two-neutron separation energies (S₂n) across isotopic chains arises naturally from both the asymmetry term in the liquid-drop model and the quadratic term in the shell-model binding-energy expression, and that the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) can reproduce this global linear trend while also capturing local deviations (≈200–300 keV or less) caused by configuration mixing, provided one adds an ad hoc global Hamiltonian to account for the bulk binding energy.

## Method

The authors first derive the linear S₂n behavior from the liquid-drop model (LDM) by expanding the semi-empirical mass formula around a reference mass number, showing the asymmetry term dominates the slope. They then connect this to the shell-model seniority scheme, where a quadratic term in the number of valence nucleons (β term) produces the same linear S₂n drop. Finally, they implement the IBM-1 with a Hamiltonian containing a global part (Casimir operators of U(6), giving a quadratic BE in boson number N) and a local part (the standard IBM Hamiltonian with ε_d, κ, χ, κ′ parameters), and apply this to the 50–82 neutron shell and to the neutron-deficient Pb region, using both configuration-mixing IBM and a macroscopic-microscopic (deformed mean-field) approach.

## Key numbers

- The LDM parameters used are: a_V = 15.85 MeV, a_C = 0.71 MeV, a_S = 18.34 MeV, a_A = 23.22 MeV (Wapstra values).
- The pairing energy term is given as BE_pairing = −11.46 A^(−1/2) MeV, causing a relative S₂n variation of ≈100 keV over ΔA = 20.
- Local deviations from global S₂n trends are about 200–300 keV or less; experimental accuracy in the Pb region is typically 30–40 keV.
- For the U(5) limit near the vibrational case, the local contribution to S₂n is constant: S₂n = 5κ for particles, S₂n = −5κ for holes.
- For the SU(3) limit, the local S₂n for particles is S₂n = −δ(8N + 2) and for holes S₂n = δ(8N + 10), with δ < 0.
- For the O(6) limit, the local S₂n for particles is S₂n = ζ(2N + 3) and for holes S₂n = −ζ(2N + 5), with ζ < 0.
- The overlap between the exact ground state and the U(5) basis state (n_d = 0) for N = 8 and κ/ε_d = 0.03 is 0.913 (χ = 0), 0.876 (χ = −1), and 0.833 (χ = −√7/2).
- Near the SU(3) limit, the overlap between the exact ground state and the SU(3) basis state for N = 8 remains >0.9 even for |ε_d|/|κ| = 10 (specifically 0.942).

## Context

This work extends the global mass descriptions of the liquid-drop model (Wapstra 1958, 1971) and macroscopic-microscopic methods (Moller 1981, 1988, 1995, 1997) by focusing on local correlations (≈200–300 keV) that recent high-precision trap measurements (ISOLTRAP, MISTRAL; Bollinger 1996, Raimann 1997) can now resolve. It builds on the IBM binding-energy analysis of Garcia-Ramos (2001) and applies the consistent-Q formalism (Warner 1982) to the 50–82 shell and the neutron-deficient Pb region, enabling future tests of intruder-state mixing and shape coexistence using both configuration-mixing IBM and macroscopic-microscopic potential-energy surfaces.
