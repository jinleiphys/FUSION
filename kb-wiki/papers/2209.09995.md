# Theoretical discrepancies in the nucleon spin structure and the hyperfine splitting of muonic hydrogen

> Two groups, ours (Mainz) and Bochum, have recently been re-evaluating the spin polarizabilities and spin structure functions at low $Q$, using the baryon chiral perturbation theory (B$χ$PT), the manifestly-covariant counterpart of the heavy-baryon chiral perturbation theory (HB$χ$PT). Whilst the two groups agree that the B$χ$PT framework works better than HB$χ$PT in this sector, their quantitative results disagree in some of the quantities; most notably, the proton spin polarizabilities $γ_0$ and $δ_{LT}$. These discrepancies are especially intriguing in light of new experimental data coming from the Jefferson Lab "Spin Physics Program". The preliminary data on the proton are reported by Karl Slifer in a plenary session of this workshop. Another theoretical discrepancy is emerging in the proton-polarizability contribution to the hyperfine splitting (hfs) in hydrogen and muonic hydrogen. Our B$χ$PT calculation shows a significantly smaller effect than the state-of-the-art data-driven evaluations based on empirical spin structure functions. The smaller polarizability contribution leads to a smaller Zemach radius of the proton. This discrepancy could be relevant for the planned first-ever measurement of the ground-state hfs in muonic hydrogen.

## Key claim

The paper identifies two unresolved theoretical discrepancies in low-energy nucleon spin structure: (1) between the Mainz and Bochum groups' baryon chiral perturbation theory (BχPT) calculations of the proton spin polarizabilities γ₀ and δLT, and (2) between the BχPT prediction and data-driven dispersive evaluations of the proton-polarizability contribution to the hyperfine splitting (hfs) in hydrogen and muonic hydrogen, where the BχPT result is "considerably smaller" and "comparable with 0."

## Method

The authors compute forward doubly-virtual Compton scattering (VVCS) on the nucleon using baryon chiral perturbation theory (BχPT) at next-to-next-to-leading order (O(p⁷/²)), including pion, nucleon, and Δ(1232)-isobar fields. They compare their results for spin polarizabilities (γ₀, δLT) and generalized Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn (GDH) integrals (I₁, IA) with the analogous BχPT calculation by the Bochum group, with older heavy-baryon χPT (HBχPT) calculations, with the empirical MAID model, and with recent Jefferson Lab experimental data. For the hfs polarizability contribution, they compare their BχPT prediction with data-driven dispersive evaluations based on empirical spin structure functions.

## Key numbers

- The proton spin polarizability δLT(Q²) shows "significant discrepancy" between Mainz and Bochum BχPT calculations, especially for the proton; the Bochum result deviates from MAID by "factors 2 or 3" in the generalized GDH integrals I₁ and IA.
- The polarizability contribution to the hfs in μH from data-driven evaluations yields Δpol = 234 ppm, arising from cancellation between a 1089 ppm contribution from 4I₁(Q²) and a −855 ppm contribution from F₂²(Q²).
- The BχPT prediction for the polarizability contribution to hfs is "considerably smaller" than the data-driven value and "comparable with 0," implying a smaller Zemach radius of the proton.
- The GDH sum rule at Q²=0 gives I₁(0) = IA(0) = −¼ κN, with the proton anomalous magnetic moment κp ≈ 1.793.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous BχPT calculations (Lensky 2014, Alarcon 2020) and contrasts with the Bochum group's BχPT calculation (Bernard 2012, Thurmann 2020), which yields different quantitative results despite using the same framework. The discrepancy in the polarizability contribution to hfs is relevant for the planned first-ever measurement of ground-state hfs in muonic hydrogen by the CREMA, FAMU, and J-PARC/Riken collaborations, where precise theory predictions are needed to narrow the frequency search range. The paper suggests that existing real Compton scattering data (Gryniuk 2015, 2016) and off-forward Compton scattering data (Lensky 2015) could help resolve these discrepancies without requiring new experiments.
