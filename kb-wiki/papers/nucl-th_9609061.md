# Studies in the statistical and thermal properties of hadronic matter under some extreme conditions

> The thermal and statistical properties of hadronic matter under some extreme conditions are investigated using an exactly solvable canonical ensemble model. A unified model describing both the fragmentation of nuclei and the thermal properties of hadronic matter is developed. Simple expressions are obtained for quantities such as the hadronic equation of state, specific heat, compressibility, entropy, and excitation energy as a function of temperature and density. These expressions encompass the fermionic aspect of nucleons, such as degeneracy pressure and Fermi energy at low temperatures and the ideal gas laws at high temperatures and low density. Expressions are developed which connect these two extremes with behavior that resembles an ideal Bose gas with its associated Bose condensation. In the thermodynamic limit, an infinite cluster exists below a certain critical condition in a manner similar to the sudden appearance of the infinite cluster in percolation theory. The importance of multiplicity fluctuations is discussed and some recent data from the EOS collaboration on critical point behavior of nuclei can be accounted for using simple expressions obtained from the model.

## Key claim

The paper claims that an exactly solvable canonical ensemble model can unify the description of nuclear multifragmentation and the thermodynamic properties of hadronic matter, with the intermediate regime between a degenerate Fermi gas and an ideal Maxwell-Boltzmann gas exhibiting behavior analogous to an ideal Bose gas with Bose-Einstein condensation. The hedge is that this analogy holds only when the model parameters are chosen such that cluster yields follow a power law, and the critical behavior depends on the specific choice of the exponent tau.

## Method

The authors construct a canonical partition function for fragmenting nuclear matter using a weight function that depends on thermodynamic variables (volume V and temperature T) and a cluster-size-dependent factor beta_k = k^tau. The parameters x and y in the weight are determined by requiring consistency with the low-temperature degenerate Fermi gas limit (with Skyrme interactions) and the high-temperature ideal Maxwell-Boltzmann gas limit. The partition function is computed recursively, and thermodynamic functions (pressure, internal energy, specific heat, compressibility, entropy) are derived from derivatives of ln Z_A. The grand canonical limit is used to analyze critical point behavior, and the model is compared to ideal Bose-Einstein gas laws and percolation theory.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear density: rho_0 = 0.17 nucleons fm^{-3}
- Binding energy per particle at zero temperature and normal density: about 8.0 MeV/nucleon
- Incompressibility at T=0: kappa = 96 + 144 sigma MeV; for sigma = 1, kappa = 240 MeV
- Critical exponent tau chosen for calculations: tau = 2.5
- Phase instability first appears at rho = 0.5 rho_0, T = 24 MeV
- From EOS collaboration data: critical charge multiplicity <m>_c = 26 +/- 1 for gold (Z=79), yielding tau = 2.262 +/- 0.013 from the model
- Percolation theory gives tau = 2.14 +/- 0.06 for the same data
- Critical exponent tau for percolation: 2.21

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous exactly solvable canonical ensemble model for nuclear multifragmentation (Mekjian 1990, Chase 1994) to include thermodynamic properties such as equation of state, specific heat, and compressibility. It connects to thermal models (Goodman 1984), statistical models (Gross 1990), and percolation models (Campi 1992, Bauer 1986) of nuclear fragmentation. The model enables analysis of critical point behavior in nuclear collisions, as demonstrated by comparison with EOS collaboration data on gold multifragmentation, and provides a framework for understanding the relationship between multiplicity fluctuations and phase transitions in finite nuclear systems.
