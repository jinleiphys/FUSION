# Dynamical and sequential decay effects on isoscaling and density dependence of the symmetry energy

> The isoscaling properties of the primary and final products are studied via isospin dependent quantum molecular dynamics (IQMD) model and the followed sequential decay model GEMINI, respectively. It is found that the isoscaling parameters $α$ of both primary and final products keep no significant change for light fragments, but increases with the mass for intermediate and heavy products. The dynamical effects on isoscaling are exhibited by that $α$ value decreases a little with the evolution time of the system, and opposite trend for the heavy products. The secondary decay effects on isoscaling are reflected in the increasing of the $α$ value for the final products which experiences secondary decay process. Furthermore the density dependence of the symmetry energy has also been explored, it is observed that in the low densities the symmetry energy coefficient has the form of $C_{sym}(ρ)\sim C_{0}(ρ/ρ_{0})^γ$, where $γ= 0.7 \sim 1.3$ for both primary and final products, but $C_{0}$ have different values for primary and final products. It is also suggested that it might be more reasonable to describe the density dependence of the symmetry energy coefficient by the $C_{sym}(ρ/ρ_{0})\approx C_{1}(ρ/ρ_{0})^{γ_{soft}} + C_{2}(ρ/ρ_{0})^{γ_{stiff}}$ with $γ_{soft}\leq 1$, $γ_{stiff}\geq 1$ and $C_{1}, C_{2}$ constant parameters.

## Key claim

The isoscaling parameter α shows no significant change for light fragments but increases with mass for intermediate and heavy products, and the density dependence of the symmetry energy coefficient in the low-density region follows C_sym(ρ) ∼ C_0(ρ/ρ_0)^γ with γ = 0.7–1.3 for both primary and final products, though C_0 differs between primary and final products.

## Method

The authors used the isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics (IQMD) model to simulate central collisions of ⁴⁰Ca + ⁴⁰Ca and ⁴⁸Ca + ⁴⁸Ca at 35 MeV/nucleon with impact parameter b = 1 fm, recording fragments at different reaction times from 160 to 400 fm/c. Primary fragments from IQMD were then fed into the GEMINI sequential decay code as an afterburner to produce final products, allowing comparison of isoscaling behavior before and after secondary decay. The symmetry energy coefficient was extracted from the isoscaling parameter α using the relation α = 4(C_sym/T)[(Z_1/A_1)² − (Z_2/A_2)²], and the density dependence was studied by correlating C_sym with the average central density of clusters.

## Key numbers

- Incident energy: 35 MeV/nucleon for both ⁴⁰Ca + ⁴⁰Ca and ⁴⁸Ca + ⁴⁸Ca reactions
- Impact parameter: b = 1 fm
- Reaction times studied: 160, 200, 240, 320, and 400 fm/c
- Gaussian wave packet width L = 2.16 fm²
- Coalescence radius r_min ≤ 3.5 fm
- Normal nuclear matter density ρ_0 = 0.16 fm⁻³
- Symmetry energy coefficient in IQMD: C_0 = 16.5 MeV for primary products
- Symmetry energy coefficient in IQMD+GEMINI: C_0 = 26 MeV for final products
- γ range for density dependence: 0.7 to 1.3 for both primary and final products
- Average excitation energies per nucleon for ⁴⁰Ca + ⁴⁰Ca: from 4.812 MeV at 160 fm/c to 3.584 MeV at 400 fm/c
- Average excitation energies per nucleon for ⁴⁸Ca + ⁴⁸Ca: from 5.511 MeV at 160 fm/c to 4.179 MeV at 400 fm/c
- Average temperatures ⟨T⟩: from 7.180 MeV at 160 fm/c to 6.226 MeV at 400 fm/c
- Ratio α_GEMINI/α_IQMD ≈ 1.2 for products with charge number Z > 8

## Context

This work extends previous isoscaling studies in multifragmentation reactions (Tsang 2001, Botvina 2002) by explicitly separating dynamical effects (via time evolution in IQMD) from sequential decay effects (via GEMINI). It builds on the relation between isoscaling parameter α and symmetry energy coefficient established in the grand-canonical statistical multifragmentation model (Tsang 2001) and the expanding-emitting source model (Botvina 2002). The finding that a single power-law form C_sym(ρ) = C_0(ρ/ρ_0)^γ cannot describe the full density range, requiring a combination of soft (γ ≤ 1) and stiff (γ ≥ 1) terms, provides guidance for future experimental extraction of the density dependence of the symmetry energy from isoscaling data.
