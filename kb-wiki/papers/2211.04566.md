# Lattice-QCD-based equations of state at finite temperature and density

> The equation of state (EoS) of QCD is a crucial input for the modeling of heavy-ion-collision (HIC) and neutron-star-merger systems. Calculations of the fundamental theory of QCD, which could yield the true EoS, are hindered by the infamous Fermi sign problem which only allows direct simulations at zero or imaginary baryonic chemical potential. As a direct consequence, the current coverage of the QCD phase diagram by lattice simulations is limited. In these proceedings, two different equations of state based on first-principle lattice QCD (LQCD) calculations are discussed. The first is solely informed by the fundamental theory by utilizing all available diagonal and non-diagonal susceptibilities up to $\mathcal{O}(μ_B^4)$ in order to reconstruct a full EoS at finite baryon number, electric charge and strangeness chemical potentials. For the second, we go beyond information from the lattice in order to explore the conjectured phase structure, not yet determined by LQCD methods, to assist the experimental HIC community in their search for the critical point. We incorporate critical behavior into this EoS by relying on the principle of universality classes, of which QCD belongs to the 3D Ising Model. This allows one to study the effects of a singularity on the thermodynamical quantities that make up the equation of state used for hydrodynamical simulations of HICs. Additionally, we ensure that these EoSs are valid for applications to HICs by enforcing conditions of strangeness neutrality and fixed charge-to-baryon-number ratio.

## Key claim

The paper presents two lattice-QCD-based equations of state for heavy-ion collisions: one constructed solely from first-principles lattice data using Taylor expansions up to order mu_B^4 in all three conserved charge chemical potentials, and another that incorporates a conjectured critical point via a non-universal mapping from the 3D Ising model, with the caveat that the critical point parameters are illustrative and not predictive.

## Method

The first equation of state (BQS EoS) was constructed by Taylor-expanding the QCD pressure in baryon, electric charge, and strangeness chemical potentials using diagonal and non-diagonal susceptibilities from the Wuppertal-Budapest collaboration up to O(mu_B^4), then parametrizing the coefficients over a temperature range from 30 to 800 MeV with smooth merging to the Hadron Resonance Gas model at low T and the Stefan-Boltzmann limit at high T. The second equation of state (BES EoS) incorporated critical behavior by mapping the 3D Ising model free energy onto the QCD phase diagram via a linear transformation, matching Taylor coefficients to lattice QCD at mu_B=0, and imposing strangeness neutrality with fixed charge-to-baryon-number ratio. Thermodynamic quantities were then derived from the pressure through standard relations.

## Key numbers

- Taylor expansion coefficients used up to O(mu_B^4) from lattice susceptibilities, covering baryon chemical potential up to mu_B <= 420 MeV for BES-II energies
- Temperature range for parametrization: 30 <= T <= 800 MeV
- Critical point placed at (T_C approx 143.2 MeV, mu_{B,C}=350 MeV) with angular parameters alpha_1=3.85 degrees, alpha_2=93.85 degrees, and scale parameters omega=1, rho=2
- Isentropic trajectories shown for s/n_B = 420, 144, 70, 30, corresponding to collision energies sqrt(s_NN)=200, 62.4, 27, 14.5 GeV
- 3D Ising model critical exponents: alpha=0.11, beta=0.326, delta=4.8; normalization constants M_0=0.605, h_0=0.364
- Curvature parameter of the chiral transition line: kappa=-0.0149 from Bellwied (2015)
- Switching temperature overlap region: Delta T=17 MeV

## Context

This work extends the lattice-QCD-based equation of state framework developed by Parotto (2018) and Noronha-Hostler (2019) by enforcing strangeness neutrality and fixed charge-to-baryon-number ratio, conditions relevant for heavy-ion collisions at RHIC's Beam Energy Scan program. The BQS EoS provides a first-principles baseline using susceptibilities from Borsanyi (2018), while the BES EoS enables hydrodynamic simulations to test the effects of a conjectured critical point on experimental observables, with the open-source code allowing users to vary the critical point location and scale parameters to compare with data from BES-II.
