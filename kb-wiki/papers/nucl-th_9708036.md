# Quantum Tunneling in Nuclear Fusion

> Recent theoretical advances in the study of heavy ion fusion reactions below the Coulomb barrier are reviewed. Particular emphasis is given to new ways of analyzing data, such as studying barrier distributions; new approaches to channel coupling, such as the path integral and Green function formalisms; and alternative methods to describe nuclear structure effects, such as those using the Interacting Boson Model. The roles of nucleon transfer, asymmetry effects, higher-order couplings, and shape-phase transitions are elucidated. The current status of the fusion of unstable nuclei and very massive systems are briefly discussed.

## Key claim

This review argues that subbarrier heavy-ion fusion cross sections are significantly enhanced beyond one-dimensional barrier-penetration predictions due to multidimensional quantum tunneling effects, but emphasizes that simplified coupled-channels codes (e.g., CCFUS) should be used only for qualitative understanding, as their approximations (constant coupling, Wong's formula) can overestimate cross sections and ignore important couplings such as double-phonon states.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical approaches to subbarrier fusion including the standard coupled-channels formalism with incoming-wave boundary conditions, simplified eigen-channel models that decouple the equations under constant-coupling and no-Coriolis approximations, and path integral methods using influence functionals. It also describes the extraction of barrier distributions from the second energy derivative of \(E\sigma\) and the inversion of experimental data to obtain effective one-dimensional barriers via Abelian integrals.

## Key numbers

- The Hill-Wheeler formula for transmission through a parabolic barrier: \(T_0(E) = [1 + \exp(-2\pi(E-V_{B0})/\hbar\Omega)]^{-1}\), where \(V_{B0}\) is the s-wave barrier height and \(\Omega\) the curvature.
- The Wong formula for the fusion cross section above the barrier: \(\sigma(E) = (\hbar\Omega r_0^2 / 2E) \log[1 + \exp(2\pi(E-V_{B0})/\hbar\Omega)]\).
- The classical limit of the Wong formula: \(\sigma(E) = \pi r_0^2 (1 - V_{B0}/E)\).
- For a two-channel problem with coupling strength \(F\) and excitation energy \(Q\), the eigenvalues are \(\lambda_\pm = \frac{1}{2}(-Q \pm \sqrt{Q^2 + 4F^2})\).
- For coupling to an harmonic mode, the total transmission probability is \(T_{\rm cc}(E) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (F^{2n}/Q^{2n}n!) \exp(-F^2/Q^2) T(E-n|Q|+F^2/|Q|)\).
- The effective radius \(R^2(E)\) is given by \(R^2(E) = r_0^2 [1 - (4/\alpha r_0^2) \int_0^E dE' T_0(E')(E-E') / \int_0^E dE' T_0(E')]\), where \(\alpha\) is the curvature of the s-wave barrier.

## Context

This review extends the earlier one-dimensional fusion model of Vaz, Alexander, and Satchler (1981) and the inversion analysis of Balantekin, Koonin, and Negele (1983) which demonstrated the inadequacy of single-barrier descriptions. It systematizes the coupled-channels approach developed by Dasso, Landowne, and collaborators and the barrier-distribution method introduced by Rowley, Satchler, and Stelson (1991). The work enables quantitative exploration of anharmonic couplings, nucleon transfer effects, and fusion with unstable nuclei, while identifying open problems such as the treatment of double-phonon states and the energy dependence of the effective radius.
