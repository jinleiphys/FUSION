# Classical fields and heavy ion collisions

> This is a review of numerical applications of classical gluodynamics to heavy ion collisions. We recall some results from calculations of gluon production, discuss their implications for heavy ion phenomenology, and outline a strategy to calculate the number of quark pairs produced by these classical fields.

## Key claim

This review argues that classical gluodynamics can be used to describe bulk particle production in heavy ion collisions when the saturation scale Qs is large enough to justify weak-coupling methods, but the numerical value of the saturation scale remains a free parameter that must be fixed from experimental data, leading to two different phenomenological scenarios.

## Method

The paper reviews numerical solutions of the Yang-Mills equations in the forward light cone using the Hamiltonian formalism with boost-invariant initial conditions, which reduces the problem to a 2+1 dimensional gauge theory with an adjoint scalar field. For quark pair production, the Dirac equation is solved numerically in the background color field of the two nuclei, using a 1+1-dimensional toy model to handle the longitudinal dimension before extending to the full 3+1-dimensional calculation.

## Key numbers

- The saturation scale Qs is estimated to grow as Qs^2(x) ~ x^{-lambda} with lambda ≈ 0.3, based on fits to HERA deep inelastic scattering data
- For strong fields with g^2 μ RA ≥ 50, the dimensionless energy ratio f_E and multiplicity ratio f_N become approximately independent of the field strength parameter and lattice spacing
- In the hydro scenario, the saturation scale is g^2 μ ≈ 1.9 GeV; in the free streaming scenario, g^2 μ ≈ 1.4 GeV
- For the LHC at sqrt(s) = 5500 GeV, the prediction is approximately 3000 particles per unit rapidity at central rapidities (with N_ch ≈ 2/3 N_tot)
- The thermalization time from perturbative estimates is τ_0 ≥ 3 fm, while the saturation scale argument gives τ_0 ~ 1/Qs ~ 0.2 fm

## Context

This review extends the McLerran-Venugopalan model (McLerran and Venugopalan, 1994) for classical color fields in heavy ion collisions, building on numerical methods developed by Krasnitz, Nara and Venugopalan (1998, 2001). The work contrasts with perturbative estimates of thermalization such as the bottom-up scenario (Baier et al., 2000) and addresses the need to understand how a purely gluonic system transforms into a quark-gluon plasma, with future work requiring treatment of longitudinal dimensions and initial conditions from the JIMWLK equation.
