# Large Logarithms in the Beam Normal Spin Asymmetry of Elastic Electron--Proton Scattering

> We study a parity-conserving single-spin beam asymmetry of elastic electron-proton scattering induced by an absorptive part of the two-photon exchange amplitude. It is demonstrated that excitation of inelastic hadronic intermediate states by the consecutive exchange of two photons leads to logarithmic and double-logarithmic enhancement due to contributions of hard collinear quasi-real photons. The asymmetry at small electron scattering angles is expressed in terms of the total photoproduction cross section on the proton, and is predicted to reach the magnitude of 20-30 parts per million. At these conditions and fixed 4-momentum transfers, the asymmetry is rising logarithmically with increasing electron beam energy, following the high-energy diffractive behavior of total photoproduction cross section on the proton.

# Large Logarithms in the Beam Normal Spin Asymmetry of Elastic Electron–Proton Scattering

## Key claim

The beam normal single-spin asymmetry (SSNA) in elastic electron–proton scattering receives logarithmic and double-logarithmic enhancement from hard collinear quasi-real photons when inelastic hadronic intermediate states are excited in the two-photon exchange amplitude, but only for the case of normal (transverse) beam polarization; this enhancement does not occur for target SSNA or longitudinally polarized electrons due to gauge invariance constraints.

## Method

The authors compute the parity-conserving single-spin beam asymmetry from the absorptive (imaginary) part of the two-photon exchange amplitude using a rank-3 leptonic tensor contracted with a hadronic tensor describing the non-forward nucleon Compton amplitude. They isolate the collinear photon kinematics where one intermediate photon is nearly collinear to its parent electron, extract the resulting logarithmic singularities via a subtraction procedure, and relate the forward Compton amplitude to the total photoproduction cross section on the proton using the optical theorem. Numerical integration is performed for resonance contributions using a Breit–Wigner factor, and analytic integration yields the master formula for small scattering angles.

## Key numbers

- The beam SSNA is predicted to reach a magnitude of 20–30 parts per million (ppm) at small electron scattering angles.
- The master formula for the beam SSNA at small Q² contains terms of order log²(Q²/m_e²) and log(Q²/m_e²), where m_e is the electron mass.
- For the kinematics of the SLAC E158 experiment, the contribution from the elastic intermediate proton state is suppressed by "a few orders of magnitude" compared to inelastic excitations.
- At beam energies of 3 GeV, contributions from the resonance region (W² < 4 GeV²) are estimated at 10–20% of the total, decreasing below 1% at higher energies.
- For beam energies of 45 GeV, more than 95% (80%) of the result comes from the upper 1/2 (3/4) of the W²-integration range.
- The total photoproduction cross section on the proton is taken as approximately 0.1 mb for the analytic integration.
- An exponential suppression factor exp(-B Q²/2) is applied with B = 8 GeV⁻² to account for the Q² dependence of the nucleon Compton amplitude.
- At fixed Q² = 0.05 GeV², the asymmetry is approximately constant over a wide energy range up to √s = 500 GeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations by Afanasev and Merenkov (2003) that predicted beam SSNA at the level of a few ppm assuming only elastic proton intermediate states, and by De Rujula et al. who noted the role of the imaginary part of the two-photon exchange amplitude for normal target polarization. The authors show that inelastic hadronic intermediate states produce much larger asymmetries (20–30 ppm) than the elastic-only calculations, and that the effect is qualitatively different from the target SSNA due to gauge invariance. The results are relevant for parity-violating electron scattering experiments (such as E158 at SLAC and Jefferson Lab experiments) where the parity-conserving beam SSNA is a source of systematic corrections, and the predicted energy independence at fixed Q² makes the asymmetry measurable at higher energies including those from the Jefferson Lab 12-GeV upgrade.
