# Stars and quark stars in bumblebee gravity

> In this work, the interior spacetime of stars is built in a Lorentz symmetry breaking model called bumblebee gravity. Firstly, we calculated the modified Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation in this context of modified gravity. Then we show that the bumblebee field, responsible for the symmetry breaking, increases the star mass-radius relation when it assumes its vacuum expectation value. When compared to the general relativity mass-radius relation, a Lorentz symmetry breaking context, like the bumblebee gravity, could provide more massive stars, surpassing the $2.5 M_{\odot}$ limit as the interior of the star is described by quark matter with the MIT bag model. Also, we investigate the stability of the solution with the MIT bag equation of state in this context of modified gravity.

# Stars and quark stars in bumblebee gravity

## Key claim

The bumblebee gravity model, with Lorentz symmetry breaking parameter ℓ, can produce quark stars with masses exceeding the 2.5 M⊙ limit that general relativity cannot reach with the MIT bag model, but this enhancement only occurs for specific values of ℓ (up to about ℓ = 10⁻¹) and requires relaxing the Solar system upper bound of ℓ < 10⁻¹⁵ to much larger values due to the high pressures and densities inside compact stars.

## Method

The authors derived the modified Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation for hydrostatic equilibrium in bumblebee gravity, using the Casana et al. Schwarzschild-like metric as the exterior solution and matching it to an interior perfect fluid solution. They solved the modified TOV equation numerically for two equations of state: an incompressible constant-density fluid (as a didactic case) and quark matter described by the MIT bag model with bag constants B = 40 MeV/fm³ and B = 60 MeV/fm³, assuming the bumblebee field is frozen at its vacuum expectation value with no coupling to matter.

## Key numbers

- For B = 40 MeV/fm³ and ℓ = 1.9 × 10⁻², the bumblebee model predicts a quark star with mass M = 2.53 M⊙ and radius R = 13.75 km, compared to M = 2.45 M⊙ and R = 13.51 km in general relativity (ℓ = 0)
- For B = 60 MeV/fm³ and ℓ = 1.9 × 10⁻², the model gives M = 2.0 M⊙ and R = 10.87 km, while general relativity gives M = 1.94 M⊙ and R = 10.66 km
- The central pressure for the B = 40 MeV/fm³ case drops from 187.12 MeV/fm³ (ℓ = 0) to 22.45 MeV/fm³ (ℓ = 1.9 × 10⁻²)
- The central density for the B = 40 MeV/fm³ case drops from 711.05 MeV/fm³ (ℓ = 0) to 217.06 MeV/fm³ (ℓ = 1.9 × 10⁻²)
- For the constant-density case with ρ₀ = 2 GeV/fm³ and R = 3.2 km, the bumblebee field decreases the central pressure as ℓ increases, but the approximation breaks for ℓ > 10⁻³
- The speed of sound in the MIT bag model fluid is constant at c_s = c/√3, satisfying causality

## Context

This work extends the study of compact stars in modified gravity, specifically within the bumblebee gravity framework that spontaneously breaks Lorentz symmetry. It contrasts with general relativity, which with the MIT bag model cannot exceed about 2.4 M⊙ for static noncharged stars, and therefore cannot explain the compact object in the GW190814 event (mass M = 2.59⁺⁰·⁰⁸₋₀·₀₉ M⊙). The authors note that two prior attempts to derive interior solutions from the same Schwarzschild-like exterior metric (Liu and Panotopoulos et al., both 2024) contained incorrect modified TOV equations. This work enables the possibility that the GW190814 compact object could be a quark star in bumblebee gravity, and it provides the necessary stability analysis via the dM/dρ_c criterion and the adiabatic index.
