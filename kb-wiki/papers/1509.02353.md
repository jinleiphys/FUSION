# Multipole modes in deformed nuclei within the finite amplitude method

> Background: To access selected excited states of nuclei, within the framework of nuclear density functional theory, the quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) is commonly used. Purpose: We present a computationally efficient, fully self-consistent framework to compute the QRPA transition strength function of an arbitrary multipole operator in axially-deformed superfluid nuclei. Methods: The method is based on the finite amplitude method (FAM) QRPA, allowing fast iterative solution of QRPA equations. A numerical implementation of the FAM-QRPA solver module has been carried out for deformed nuclei. Results: The practical feasibility of the deformed FAM module has been demonstrated. In particular, we calculate the quadrupole and octupole strength in a heavy deformed nucleus $^{240}$Pu, without any truncations in the quasiparticle space. To demonstrate the capability to calculate individual QRPA modes, we also compute low-lying negative-parity collective states in $^{154}$Sm. Conclusions: The new FAM implementation enables calculations of the QRPA strength function throughout the nuclear landscape. This will facilitate global surveys of multipole modes and beta decays, and will open new avenues for constraining the nuclear energy density functional.

## Key claim

The paper presents a computationally efficient, fully self-consistent framework for computing QRPA transition strength functions of arbitrary multipole operators in axially-deformed superfluid nuclei, using the finite amplitude method (FAM) without any truncations in the quasiparticle space, but with the caveat that the method requires explicit linearization of coordinate-space fields for the K ≠ 0 case rather than the numerical derivative used in earlier FAM implementations.

## Method

The authors extended the FAM-QRPA approach to axially deformed nuclei by implementing an iterative solver based on the HFBTHO code, which solves Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov equations in a transformed harmonic oscillator basis. For the K ≠ 0 case, the induced fields (δh, δΔ, and δΔ̅) were linearized explicitly rather than computed via numerical derivatives, allowing treatment of operators with arbitrary multipolarity LK without mixing densities with different magnetic quantum numbers K. The method uses simplex-y symmetry and the Broyden iterative method for convergence, and can be parallelized with MPI across multiple CPU cores.

## Key numbers

- For the spherical test nucleus ²⁰O with SkM* EDF and Nsh=15 oscillator shells, relative differences between various K-modes were at the level of O(10⁻⁵) or smaller
- For ²⁴⁰Pu with SLy4 EDF and Nsh=20, the HFB ground state had deformation β=0.28, neutron pairing gap Δn=0.43 MeV, and proton pairing gap Δp=0.32 MeV
- For ²⁴⁰Pu, the mixed pairing force strength was V₀=-283.45 MeV·fm³
- For ¹⁵⁴Sm with the same computational setup as ²⁴⁰Pu, the HFB ground-state quadrupole deformation was β=0.32, with Δn=0.30 MeV and Δp=0.53 MeV
- The lowest Kπ=0⁻ octupole state in ¹⁵⁴Sm was predicted at ω₁=0.4168 MeV with B(E3)=8.70 W.u., compared to the experimental value B(E3;0₁⁺→3₁⁻)=10(2) W.u.
- The lowest Kπ=1⁻ octupole state in ¹⁵⁴Sm was predicted at ω₁=0.9014 MeV with B(E3)=2.01 W.u.
- A typical strength function calculation with 20 oscillator shells on a multicore Intel Sandy Bridge 2.6 GHz processor system takes about 1000 CPU hours

## Context

This work extends the FAM-QRPA approach from the earlier K=0 case of Stetcu et al. (2011) to arbitrary multipolarity LK, enabling calculations in axially deformed superfluid nuclei without the quasiparticle space truncations required by traditional matrix QRPA (MQRPA) methods such as those of Yoshida et al. (2011) and Terasaki et al. (2011). The new implementation enables global surveys of multipole strength functions across the nuclear landscape, facilitates studies of beta decay rates, and provides a tool for constraining the isovector sector of nuclear energy density functionals using experimental data on multipole strength in deformed nuclei.
