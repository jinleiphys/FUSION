# Bottomonium suppression using a lattice QCD vetted potential

> We estimate bottomonium yields in relativistic heavy-ion collisions using a lattice QCD vetted, complex-valued, heavy-quark potential embedded in a realistic, hydrodynamically evolving medium background. We find that the lattice-vetted functional form and temperature dependence of the proper heavy-quark potential dramatically reduces the dependence of the yields on parameters other than the temperature evolution, strengthening the picture of bottomonium as QGP thermometer. Our results also show improved agreement between computed yields and experimental data produced in RHIC 200 GeV/nucleon collisions. For LHC 2.76 TeV/nucleon collisions, the excited states, whose suppression has been used as a vital sign for quark-gluon-plasma production in a heavy-ion collision, are reproduced better than previous perturbatively-motivated potential models; however, at the highest LHC energies our estimates for bottomonium suppression begin to underestimate the data. Possible paths to remedy this situation are discussed.

## Key claim

Using a lattice-QCD-vetted complex heavy-quark potential (rather than perturbatively motivated models) dramatically reduces the dependence of bottomonium suppression yields on parameters other than the temperature evolution, strengthening the case for bottomonium as a QGP thermometer; however, at the highest LHC energies (5.02 TeV) the estimates begin to underestimate the data, suggesting that either the assumed mechanism for the imaginary part (full assignment to gluo-dissociation) overestimates suppression or that a regeneration component is needed.

## Method

The authors combined a lattice-QCD-vetted, complex-valued heavy-quark potential (parametrized via a generalized Gauss law with a Cornell vacuum form and hard-thermal-loop permittivity) with a 3+1d anisotropic hydrodynamics (aHydro) simulation of the bulk QGP evolution. The real and imaginary parts of the binding energies for bottomonium states (1S, 2S, 3S, 1P, 2P) were obtained by solving the Schrödinger equation with the complex potential on a 256^3 grid, using the anisotropic Debye mass computed self-consistently from the aHydro evolution. The suppression factor R_AA was computed by integrating the instantaneous decay rate (from the imaginary part of the binding energy) over proper time, with formation times taken from vacuum binding energies, and averaging over transverse momentum, centrality, and spatial rapidity.

## Key numbers

* For the Upsilon(1S) state, the formation time used was 0.2 fm/c; for Upsilon(2S) it was 0.4 fm/c; for Upsilon(3S) it was 0.6 fm/c; for chi_b(1P) it was 0.4 fm/c; for chi_b(2P) and chi_b(3P) it was 0.6 fm/c.
* The initial proper time for plasma evolution was set to tau_0 = 0.3 fm/c at both RHIC and LHC energies.
* The final proper time was defined as the time when the local effective temperature dropped below T_d = 192 MeV.
* The grid spacing for Upsilon(nS) states was a = 0.15 fm on N = 256^3 grid points; for chi_b(mP) states the spacing was a = 0.175 fm.
* The binary mixing factor for initial energy density profiles was alpha = 0.15.
* The longitudinal profile parameters were Delta_varsigma = 2.3 and sigma_varsigma = 1.6.
* For states with Re[E_bind] <= 0, a decay rate of 10 GeV was used to quickly suppress fully unbound states.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of bottomonium suppression that used perturbatively motivated potentials (e.g., Laine 2006, Dumitru 2007, Burnier 2009) by incorporating a lattice-QCD-vetted complex potential extracted from Wilson line correlators (Burnier 2015, Burnier 2016). It also builds on anisotropic hydrodynamics frameworks (Martinez 2010, Florkowski 2010, Strickland 2014) to include non-equilibrium corrections from early-time pressure anisotropy. The results enable future work using open-quantum-systems approaches (Akamatsu 2011, Rothkopf 2013, Brambilla 2016) to disentangle gluo-dissociation from Landau damping contributions to the imaginary part, and to include regeneration via rate equations (Du 2017).
