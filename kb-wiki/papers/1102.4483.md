# Isospin effects in disappearance of flow as a function of colliding geometry

> We study the effect of isospin degree of freedom on the balance energy (E$_{bal}$) as well as its mass dependence throughout the mass range 48-270 for two sets of isobaric systems with N/Z = 1 and 1.4 at different colliding geometries ranging from central to peripheral ones. Our findings reveal the dominance of Coulomb repulsion in isospin effects on E$_{bal}$ as well as its mass dependence throughout the range of the colliding geometry.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the isospin dependence of balance energy (E_bal) and its mass dependence across colliding geometries is dominated by Coulomb repulsion, not by the symmetry energy; when Coulomb is artificially reduced, neutron-rich systems actually have *lower* E_bal than neutron-deficient ones, reversing the trend seen with full Coulomb.

## Method

The authors used the isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics (IQMD) model to simulate symmetric collisions of isobaric systems with N/Z = 1.0 and 1.4 across a mass range of 48–270. They divided colliding geometry into four reduced impact parameter bins (BIN 1 through BIN 4) and extracted E_bal for each system and geometry. To isolate the role of Coulomb, they repeated the calculations with reduced Coulomb potential and compared the resulting E_bal values and power-law exponents.

## Key numbers

- Power-law exponent τ for N/Z = 1.0 (τ₁.₀) and N/Z = 1.4 (τ₁.₄) at BIN 1: τ₁.₀ = –0.17 ± 0.02, τ₁.₄ = –0.14 ± 0.01.
- At BIN 2: τ₁.₀ = –0.17 ± 0.02, τ₁.₄ = –0.19 ± 0.02.
- At BIN 3: τ₁.₀ = –0.24 ± 0.03, τ₁.₄ = –0.26 ± 0.01.
- At BIN 4: τ₁.₀ = –0.31 ± 0.03, τ₁.₄ = –0.29 ± 0.02.
- Systems studied span combined mass A = 48 to A = 270, with N/Z = 1.0 (e.g., ²⁴Mg+²⁴Mg, ⁵⁸Cu+⁵⁸Cu, ⁷²Kr+⁷²Kr, ⁹⁶Cd+⁹⁶Cd, ¹²⁰Nd+¹²⁰Nd, ¹³⁵Ho+¹³⁵Ho) and N/Z = 1.4 (e.g., ²⁴Ne+²⁴Ne, ⁵⁸Cr+⁵⁸Cr, ⁷²Zn+⁷²Zn, ⁹⁶Zr+⁹⁶Zr, ¹²⁰Sn+¹²⁰Sn, ¹³⁵Ba+¹³⁵Ba).

## Context

This work extends earlier findings by Pak (1997) and Gautam (2010) that neutron-rich systems have higher E_bal than neutron-deficient ones at all colliding geometries, with the effect stronger at peripheral geometries. By systematically varying the Coulomb interaction, the authors demonstrate that the enhancement at peripheral geometries is due to Coulomb repulsion rather than the symmetry energy, and that the symmetry energy’s effect is uniform across both mass and impact parameter. This clarifies the separate roles of Coulomb and symmetry energy in isospin-dependent flow disappearance, enabling more precise constraints on the symmetry energy from future experiments with neutron-rich beams.
