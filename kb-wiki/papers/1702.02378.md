# Anisotropic flow of thermal photons at RHIC and LHC

> We calculate elliptic and triangular flow parameters of thermal photons using an event-by-event hydrodynamic model with fluctuating initial conditions at 200A GeV Au+Au collisions at RHIC and at 2.76A TeV Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC for three different centrality bins. The photon elliptic flow shows strong centrality dependence where $v_2(p_T)$ increases towards peripheral collisions both at RHIC and at the LHC energies. However, the triangular flow parameter does not show significant dependence to the collision centrality. The elliptic as well as the triangular flow parameters found to underestimate the PHENIX data at RHIC by a large margin for all three centrality bins. We calculate $p_T$ spectrum and anisotropic flow of thermal photons from 200A GeV Cu+Cu collisions at RHIC for 0--20\% centrality bin and compare with the results with those from Au+Au collisions. The production of thermal photon is found to decrease significantly for Cu+Cu collisions compared to Au+Au collisions. However, the effect of initial state fluctuation is found to be more pronounced for anisotropic flow resulting in larger $v_2$ and $v_3$ for Cu+Cu collisions. We study the correlation between the anisotropic flow parameters and the corresponding initial spatial anisotropies from their event by event distributions at RHIC and at the LHC energies. The linear correlation between $v_2$ and $ε_2$ is found be stronger compared to the correlation between $v_3$ and $ε_3$. In addition, the correlation coefficient is found to be larger at LHC than at RHIC.

# Anisotropic flow of thermal photons at RHIC and LHC

## Key claim
The event-by-event hydrodynamic model with fluctuating initial conditions systematically underestimates both the elliptic flow (v2) and triangular flow (v3) of direct photons measured by PHENIX at RHIC for all three centrality bins studied (0-20%, 20-40%, 40-60%), indicating that a "photon v3 puzzle" exists alongside the previously known photon v2 puzzle. The disagreement persists despite the model successfully describing hadronic flow observables.

## Method
The authors used a (2+1)-dimensional ideal hydrodynamic model with Monte Carlo Glauber fluctuating initial conditions, distributing initial entropy density around wounded nucleons using a 2D Gaussian with width sigma = 0.4 fm. Photon production from the quark-gluon plasma phase was calculated using complete leading-order plasma rates, while the hadronic phase contribution used a parameterization from earlier work. The anisotropic flow coefficients v2 and v3 were computed with respect to the participant plane angle, averaging over 400 random events per centrality bin at both RHIC (200A GeV Au+Au) and LHC (2.76A TeV Pb+Pb) energies.

## Key numbers
- Initial formation time tau0 = 0.17 fm/c for Au+Au at RHIC and 0.14 fm/c for Pb+Pb at LHC, taken from the EKRT minijet saturation model
- Inelastic nucleon-nucleon cross-section sigma_NN = 42 mb at RHIC and 64 mb at LHC
- Freeze-out temperature = 160 MeV; transition temperature from plasma to hadronic phase = 170 MeV
- The pT spectrum for Cu+Cu collisions at 200A GeV is found to be about a factor of 5-10 smaller than for Au+Au collisions at the same energy
- Correlation coefficient c(epsilon2, v2) ranges from approximately 0.5 to 0.7 for pT values of 1, 2, and 3 GeV/c at RHIC, and from approximately 0.6 to 0.8 at LHC for the same pT range

## Context
This work extends earlier hydrodynamic studies of thermal photon production using fluctuating initial conditions (Chatterjee et al., 2015; Chatterjee et al., 2016) to a systematic centrality-dependent analysis of both elliptic and triangular flow at RHIC and LHC. The results confirm and deepen the "photon v2 puzzle" first identified in comparisons of hydrodynamic calculations to PHENIX direct photon data, and newly demonstrate a similar v3 puzzle. The correlation analysis between initial spatial eccentricities and final momentum anisotropies builds on methods developed for hadronic flow by previous authors (Hannu et al., 2010). The calculations provide predictions for Cu+Cu collisions that could be tested against future PHENIX data, and establish baseline expectations for thermal photon anisotropic flow at LHC energies where ALICE data are becoming available.
