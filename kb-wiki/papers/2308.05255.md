# Examining the justification for the introduction of a fermion localization function

> Becke and Edgecombe suggested in 1990 a theoretical tool to describe electron localization in atoms and molecules, an idea which was borrowed by a large number of nuclear theorists since 2011 to describe nucleon localization in nuclear systems. I argue here that these arguments are highly questionable and cannot be used in interacting systems, where effects beyond the naive mean field or the simple Hartree-Fock framework are important and the inclusion of correlations induced by particle interactions is necessary in order to introduce such a localization function. I also describe several aspects of the exchange and irreducible 2-body density matrices, which depend on the character and strength of the 2-particle interaction and, which can be useful in justifying the derivation of an appropriate energy density functional.

## Key claim

The fermion localization function (FLF) introduced by Becke and Edgecombe (1990) and widely used in nuclear physics since 2011 is ill-justified for interacting systems because it is derived from the Hartree-Fock approximation and cannot capture clustering effects that arise from correlations induced by particle interactions, particularly when effects beyond mean field are important.

## Method

The author analyzes the mathematical derivation of the FLF from the 2-body number density for fermions with the same spin and isospin in the limit of vanishing separation, showing it depends only on Pauli correlations and the kinetic energy density. He then examines the irreducible 2-body density matrix and its sum rules, demonstrating that the FLF is essentially a measure of the accuracy of the Thomas-Fermi approximation to the kinetic energy density rather than a true measure of clustering. The argument is supported by contrasting the FLF approach with known behavior of strongly correlated systems including the unitary Fermi gas, dilute neutron matter, and nuclei with short-range correlations (SRCs).

## Key numbers

- The FLF is defined as $C_{\tau,\sigma}({\bm r}) = [1 + (D_{\tau,\sigma}({\bm r})/(\frac{3}{5}(6\pi^2)^{5/3}{\text n}^{2/3}_{\tau,\sigma}({\bm r})))^2]^{-1}$, where $D_{\tau,\sigma}({\bm r})$ contains kinetic energy density, gradient terms, and current density terms
- For $^{16}$O, the FLF allegedly shows a "spatial shell-like" structure with average radius of about 3 fm and an "inner density depression" with radius of about 1.25 fm
- The trace of the exchange plus irreducible 2-body density matrices satisfies $\sumint_{\xi,\zeta} [ {\text n}_{ex}+{\text n}_{corr} ] = -N/2$, independent of the particle interaction
- The irreducible 2-body density matrix trace satisfies $-\frac{N}{2} < -\frac{N}{2}(1 - N/N_{sp}) \leq \sumint {\text n}_{corr} \leq 0$, with minimum achieved for $n_k = N/N_{sp}$ and maximum for $n_k = 0$ or $1$
- For a unitary Fermi gas, the 2-body number density behaves as ${\text n}_2({\bm r}_1,{\bm r}_2) \propto 1/|{\bm r}_1-{\bm r}_2|^2$ for $|{\bm r}_1-{\bm r}_2| < |a|$, related to a 1-body momentum distribution $n_p \propto 1/p^4$

## Context

This paper argues against the widespread use of the FLF in nuclear physics, which was introduced by Reinhard (2011) and subsequently used by many groups including Zhang (2016), Schuetrumpf (2017), Sadhukhan (2017), Scamps (2018), Jerabek (2018), Giuliani (2019), Li (2020), Umar (2021), Matsumoto (2022), Ren (2022), and others. The work extends the author's previous studies of pairing correlations and SRCs in Fermi systems (Bulgac 2002, 2007, 2011a, 2019, 2022) and connects to the long-standing understanding of nuclear clustering from Morinaga (1956), Ravenhall (1983), and the JLAB experiments on SRCs by Hen (2014, 2017) and Cruz-Torres (2018, 2021). The paper suggests that proper treatment of clustering requires the irreducible 2-body density matrix rather than the 1-body FLF, and that a generalized FLF including pairing correlations (Eq. 5) still suffers from fundamental limitations.
