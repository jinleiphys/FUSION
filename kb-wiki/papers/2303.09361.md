# Calculation of Dynamical Response Functions Using a Bound-state Method

> We investigate a method to extract response functions (dynamical polarisabilities) directly from a bound-state approach applied to calculations of perturbation-induced reactions. The use of a square-integrable basis leads to a response in the form of a sum of $δ$ functions. We integrate this over energy and fit a smooth function to the resulting stepwise-continuous one. Its derivative gives the final approximation to the physical response function. We show that the method reproduces analytical results where known, and analyse the details for a variety of models. We apply it to some simple models, using the Stochastic Variational Method as the numerical method. Although we find that this approach, and other numerical techniques, have some difficulties with the threshold behaviour in coupled-channel problems with multiple thresholds, its stochastic nature allows us to extract robust results even for such cases.

# Calculation of Dynamical Response Functions Using a Bound-state Method

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that response functions (dynamical polarisabilities) can be extracted directly from a bound-state approach by integrating the discrete sum of delta functions obtained from a square-integrable basis, fitting a smooth function to the resulting stepwise-continuous integrated response, and differentiating; the method reproduces analytical results where known, but the authors note that both this approach and other numerical techniques have difficulties with threshold behavior in coupled-channel problems with multiple thresholds.

## Method

The authors replace the standard Lorentz Integral Transform (LIT) approach with a direct calculation of the integrated response function T(epsilon) = integral from 0 to epsilon of F(epsilon') d-epsilon', where F is the response function. They diagonalize the Hamiltonian in a square-integrable basis (harmonic oscillator or Gaussian basis functions, and later the Stochastic Variational Method), obtaining eigenenergies and eigenstates; the discrete response is a sum of delta functions at those eigenenergies. They then fit a smooth function (a Pade approximant of the form T(omega) = (sum_i a_i omega^i)/(1 + sum_i b_i omega^i) with n=4) to the stepwise-continuous integrated response, and differentiate that fit to obtain the final approximation to the physical response function. The method is benchmarked against an analytically solvable Poschl-Teller potential in one dimension and applied to deuteron photodisintegration with the Argonne V18 potential.

## Key numbers

* For the one-dimensional Poschl-Teller potential with lambda=1, the bound state energy is -1/2 (dimensionless units) and the ground state wavefunction is psi_0(x) = (1/sqrt(2))*(1/cosh(x)).
* The integrated response saturates at the norm squared of x|psi_0>, which equals pi^2/12 ≈ 0.822467.
* For the three-dimensional Poschl-Teller potential with lambda=1.8, the single bound state lies at dimensionless energy -0.32.
* In the deuteron photodisintegration calculation, the authors compare their results with those of Bampa et al. (2011) and find that differences become visible only when zooming in by factors of 10, with deviations appearing around 40 MeV energy transfer.
* The Gaussian basis calculation achieves comparable accuracy with 20 basis states as the harmonic oscillator basis achieves with approximately 200 orthonormal states.

## Context

This work extends the Lorentz Integral Transform (LIT) method developed by Leidemann et al. (2009, 2013, 2020) and Efros et al. (2004, 2007, 2012, 2019), which uses a transformation of the response function leading to inhomogeneous Schrodinger equations followed by an inverse transform. The authors note that LIT inversion is robust for single-channel problems such as deuteron photodisintegration (Bampa et al., 2011) but more challenging in multi-channel cases. The new integrated-response approach takes advantage of the stochastic nature of the Stochastic Variational Method (Suzuki, 1998; Mitroy, 2013) rather than requiring the regularly-spaced spectra of hyperspherical harmonics or no-core-shell-model bases. The method enables calculation of response functions and polarisabilities for systems where bound-state methods are applicable, including cases with multiple open channels where the authors find that all methods struggle to some extent.
