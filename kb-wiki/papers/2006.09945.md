# X(3872) Transport in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The production of the $X(3872)$ particle in heavy-ion collisions has been contemplated as an alternative probe of its internal structure. To investigate this conjecture, we perform transport calculations of the $X(3872)$ through the fireball formed in nuclear collisions at the LHC. Within a kinetic-rate equation approach as previously used for charmonia, the formation and dissociation of the $X(3872)$ is controlled by two transport parameters, i.e., its inelastic reaction rate and thermal-equilibrium limit in the evolving hot QCD medium. While the equilibrium limit is controlled by the charm production cross section in primordial nucleon-nucleon collisions (together with the spectra of charm states in the medium), the structure information is encoded in the reaction rate. We study how different scenarios for the rate affect the centrality dependence and transverse-momentum ($p_T$) spectra of the $X(3872)$. Larger reaction rates associated with the loosely bound molecule structure imply that it is formed later in the fireball evolution than the tetraquark and thus its final yields are generally smaller by around a factor of two, which is qualitatively different from most coalescence model calculations to date. The $p_T$ spectra provide further information as the later decoupling time within the molecular scenario leads to harder spectra caused by the blue-shift from the expanding fireball.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the internal structure of the X(3872) particle can be probed through its production in heavy-ion collisions, but the differences in final yields between a molecular and tetraquark scenario are only about a factor of two, which is much smaller than the up to two orders of magnitude predicted by most coalescence model calculations.

## Method

The authors solve a kinetic-rate equation for the X(3872) abundance through the hadronic phase of a fireball formed in Pb-Pb collisions at the LHC, using a thermal fireball model with blastwave expansion. The transport is controlled by two parameters: the thermal-equilibrium limit (determined by charm-quark conservation and the spectrum of open-charm states) and the inelastic reaction rate (which encodes structure information). The molecular scenario uses a large reaction rate of 300-500 MeV at the initial temperature, while the tetraquark scenario uses a smaller rate of 50-80 MeV, with the temperature dependence varied as a power law.

## Key numbers

- Molecular scenario reaction rate at initial temperature T0=180 MeV: Gamma0_mol = 300-500 MeV
- Tetraquark scenario reaction rate at T0=180 MeV: Gamma0_tet = 50-80 MeV
- Final yield of tetraquark is about a factor of 2 larger than for the molecule in central Pb-Pb collisions at 5 TeV
- For 40-60% central collisions, the molecular yield is dN/dy = (1.15 +/- 0.2) x 10^(-4) when varying the temperature exponent n=1-5
- Charm cross section in pp collisions at 5 TeV: dsigma_ccbar/dy = 0.8 mb (baseline) or 1.1 mb (including missing charm-baryon states)
- Nuclear shadowing can reduce total yields by up to 40% in central collisions due to a 20% suppression of the charm cross section
- Canonical suppression effects in the most peripheral (60-80%) centrality bin can reduce the equilibrium limit by up to 40%

## Context

This work extends the thermal-rate equation framework previously used for charmonium and bottomonium transport (Grandchamp 2003, Zhao 2011, Du 2017) to the exotic X(3872) particle. It differs from instantaneous coalescence models (Cho 2010, Sun 2017, Zhang 2020) which predict much larger yields for the molecular scenario, and from the statistical hadronization model (Andronic 2019) which gives structure-independent yields. The results enable experimental discrimination between molecular and tetraquark interpretations through centrality dependence and transverse-momentum spectra of the X(3872) in LHC Pb-Pb collisions.
