# Beta delayed neutron emission of $N=84$ $^{132}$Cd

> Using the time-of-flight technique, we measured the beta-delayed neutron emission of $^{132}$Cd. From our large-scale shell model (LSSM) calculation using the N$^3$LO interaction [Z.Y. Xu et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 131, 022501 (2023)], we suggest the decay is dominated by the transformation of a neutron in the $g_{7/2}$ orbital, deep below the Fermi surface, into a proton in the $g_{9/2}$ orbital. We compare the beta-decay half-lives and neutron branching ratios of nuclei with $Z<50$ and $N\geq82$ obtained with our LSSM with those of leading "global" models such as Finite-Range Droplet Model (FRDM). Our calculations match known half-lives and neutron branching ratios well and suggest that current leading models overestimate the yet-to-be-measured half-lives. Our model, backed by the $^{132}$Cd decay data presented here, offers robust predictive power for nuclei of astrophysical interest such as $r$-process waiting points.

## Key claim

The beta-delayed neutron emission of $^{132}$Cd was measured for the first time using the time-of-flight technique, and the authors suggest that the decay is dominated by the transformation of a neutron in the $g_{7/2}$ orbital deep below the Fermi surface into a proton in the $g_{9/2}$ orbital, based on large-scale shell model (LSSM) calculations using the N$^3$LO interaction. The authors hedge that their model, backed by the $^{132}$Cd decay data, offers robust predictive power for nuclei of astrophysical interest such as $r$-process waiting points, but note that current leading global models overestimate the yet-to-be-measured half-lives.

## Method

The authors produced $^{132}$Cd at the ISOLDE facility at CERN using the Isotope Separation On-Line (ISOL) technique with a 1.4 GeV proton beam inducing fission in a UC$_x$ target, selectively ionized using the Resonance Ionization Laser Ion Source (RILIS). They measured beta-delayed gamma rays with four high-purity Ge clover detectors and beta-delayed neutron time-of-flight spectra using the Versatile Array of Neutron Detectors at Low Energy (VANDLE) consisting of 26 plastic scintillator modules arranged in an arc of 104.5 cm radius. The neutron energy distribution was obtained using a fitting procedure with a GEANT4 Monte Carlo simulation of the detector response, calibrated using the beta-delayed neutron emission of $^{17}$N and $^{49}$K.

## Key numbers

- Total number of $^{132}$Cd decays observed: $2.2 \times 10^5$
- Neutron branching ratio of $^{132}$Cd: 100% (deduced from lack of gamma transitions in $^{132}$In)
- Photo-peak efficiency of the HPGe array: 3.2% at 1 MeV
- VANDLE array acceptance: 12.0% of $4\pi$, with total detection efficiency 7.8% at 1 MeV
- Largest neutron peak observed at approximately 2.0 MeV (54 ns time-of-flight)
- Two largest GT transitions correspond to states in $^{132}$In at 4.8 and 4.9 MeV, with B(GT) values of 0.14(2) and 0.18(2) respectively (log(ft)=4.65(7) and 4.54(6))
- 31(6)% of the strength below the main transition corresponds to log(ft) values larger than 5 (candidates for First Forbidden transitions)
- LSSM calculation predicts 17.1% of strength populates states through FF transitions, within two sigma of experimental value
- LSSM calculation predicts main GT transition at 4.9 MeV with transition strength 0.35, matching observed B(GT)=0.32(3) of the two largest transitions combined
- For $N=84$ even-even $^{128}$Ru and $^{130}$Pd, LSSM predicts half-lives about two times shorter than FRDM-QRPA values
- FF transitions represent between 5% in $^{128}$Ru and 26% in $^{133}$In along $N=84$ (compared to 31% and 48% in D3C*, 60% and 75% in DDPC1)
- New half-lives and neutron branching ratios may change the second abundance peak (at $A\sim130$) by 20% and the third peak (at $A\sim190$) and actinides by approximately 10%

## Context

This work extends the investigation of beta decay in the $Z<50$, $N\geq82$ region previously studied for $^{133}$In by Xu (2023), where the decay strength was shown to be dominated by the $\nu g_{7/2}\rightarrow\pi g_{9/2}$ transformation. The measurement of $^{132}$Cd provides the first beta-delayed neutron spectroscopy for this nucleus, testing the hypothesis that this transformation dominates the decay of all $N=84$ isotones. The results contradict predictions from global models such as FRDM-QRPA (Moller, 2019) and DF3-CQRPA (Borzov, 2016), which systematically under-predict neutron branching ratios for $^{132,133}$Cd and overestimate half-lives for even-even $N=82,84$ isotones. The LSSM calculations, anchored by experimental data, enable more reliable predictions for $r$-process waiting points and show that including these new half-lives and neutron branching ratios in $r$-process abundance calculations for neutron star mergers can modify abundance patterns across the second and third abundance peaks and actinides by up to 20%.
