# Pion Condensation in Baryonic Matter: from Sarma Phase to Larkin-Ovchinnikov-Fudde-Ferrell Phase

> We investigated two pion condensed phases in the frame of the two flavor Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model at finite baryon density: the homogeneous and isotropic Sarma phase and inhomogeneous and anisotropic Larkin-Ovchinnikov-Fudde-Ferrell(LOFF) phase. At small isospin chemical potential $μ_I$, the Sarma state is free from the Sarma instability and magnetic instability due to the strong coupling and large enough effective quark mass. At large $μ_I$, while the Sarma instability can be cured via fixing baryon density $n_B$ to be nonzero, its magnetic instability implies that the LOFF state is more favored than the Sarma state. In the intermediate $μ_I$ region, the stable ground state is the Sarma state at higher $n_B$ and LOFF state at lower $n_B$.

## Key claim

The paper argues that in pion condensed matter at finite baryon density, the Sarma phase (homogeneous and isotropic) can be the stable ground state at small isospin chemical potential due to strong coupling and large effective quark mass, but at large isospin chemical potential the LOFF phase (inhomogeneous and anisotropic) is more favored; the abstract's claim of stability for the Sarma phase is conditional on the isospin chemical potential being below a critical value \(\mu_I^0 \simeq 230\) MeV.

## Method

The authors used the two-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model at finite baryon and isospin chemical potentials in mean-field approximation, solving the coupled gap equations for the effective quark mass \(m\) and pion condensate \(\Delta\) at zero temperature. They evaluated the thermodynamic potential, quasiparticle dispersion relations, and occupation numbers to characterize the Sarma phase, and calculated the susceptibility \(\kappa_\Delta\) to test thermodynamic stability and the Meissner mass squared to test magnetic (dynamical) stability. The LOFF phase was constructed as a spatially non-uniform condensate and compared energetically with the Sarma phase.

## Key numbers

- The critical isospin chemical potential for pion condensation is \(\mu_I^c = m_\pi = 138\) MeV, exactly the vacuum pion mass.
- The critical isospin chemical potential separating BEC and BCS regimes is \(\mu_I^0 = 230\) MeV for the chosen parameter set (current quark mass \(m_0 = 5\) MeV, coupling \(G = 4.93\) GeV\(^{-2}\), cutoff \(\Lambda = 653\) MeV); this value varies between 230 and 270 MeV when the effective quark mass in vacuum ranges from 300 to 500 MeV.
- At \(\mu_I = 150\) MeV (below \(\mu_I^0\)), the susceptibility \(\kappa_\Delta\) is always positive in the whole gapless region, indicating thermodynamic stability of the Sarma phase.
- The baryon number density in the gapless phase is given by expressions such as \(n_B = (p_2^3 - p_1^3)/(3\pi^2)\) for type 1 Sarma state, where \(p_1\) and \(p_2\) are the gapless node momenta.

## Context

This work extends studies of pion condensation at finite isospin density in the NJL model (He 2005, He 2006) to include nonzero baryon density, and builds on earlier investigations of the Sarma phase in color superconductivity (CSC2, CSC3) and asymmetric fermion superfluids (Pao 2005, Son 2005). The phase diagram obtained is similar to that proposed by Son and Stephanov (2005) for cold polarized Fermi gas. The results enable future studies of pion condensation under conditions relevant to neutron stars and heavy ion collisions, where both baryon and isospin densities are nonzero, and suggest that the LOFF phase should be considered as a candidate ground state at large isospin chemical potential.
