# Nucleon - Nucleon Interactions at Short Distances

> Despite the progress made in understanding the NN interactions at long distances based on effective field theories, the understanding of the dynamics of short range NN interactions remains as elusive as ever. One of the most fascinating properties of short range interaction is its repulsive nature which is responsible for the stability of strongly interacting matter. The relevant distances, $\le 0.5$ fm, in this case are such that one expects the onset of quark-gluon degrees of freedom with interaction being dominated by QCD dynamics. We review the current status of the understanding of the QCD dynamics of NN interactions at short distances, highlight outstanding questions and outline the theoretical foundation of QCD description of hard NN processes. We present examples of how the study of the hard elastic NN interaction can reveal the symmetry structure of valence quark component of the nucleon wave function and how the onset of pQCD regime is correlated with the onset of color transparency phenomena in hard $pp$ scattering in the nuclear medium. The discussions show how the new experimental facilities can help to advance the knowledge about the QCD nature of nuclear forces at short distances.

## Key claim

Despite the abstract's suggestion that QCD dynamics dominate at short distances (≤0.5 fm), the paper argues that the understanding of the QCD dynamics of NN interactions at short distances remains "very incomplete," with no new experimental data taken since the mid-1990s, and that the observed oscillatory energy dependence of scaled cross sections and large polarization asymmetries indicate interference between short-range and long-range (spatially extended) configurations rather than a pure perturbative QCD regime.

## Method

The paper reviews the history of QCD approaches to short-range NN interactions, including dimensional counting rules, helicity conservation predictions, and the quark-interchange model. It then develops a theoretical framework for hard NN scattering using helicity amplitudes (φ₁ through φ₅) and derives expressions for polarization observables in the lab frame, including single, double, and triple polarization asymmetries, with their limiting forms under helicity conservation. The analysis compares these theoretical predictions against existing experimental data on elastic pp and pn scattering cross sections, spin asymmetry Aₙₙ, and nuclear transparency measurements from hard pp scattering in nuclear targets.

## Key numbers

- The repulsive core of NN interaction operates at distances ≤0.5 fm, where per nucleon binding energy would be 1500 MeV without repulsion (Stability Theorem)
- The Aₙₙ polarization experiment at p_Lab = 11.75 GeV/c for 90° center-of-mass elastic pp scattering found that the cross section for protons scattering with parallel spins normal to the scattering plane is four times that for antiparallel spins
- The s¹⁰-weighted elastic pp cross section shows oscillatory energy dependence with irregularities on a level of 60% in a region where the hard elastic pp cross section changes by eight orders of magnitude
- Nuclear transparency T_pp initially grows with proton momentum above Glauber theory predictions, then falls back to values apparently below Glauber prediction at p_Lab ≈ 14 GeV
- The broad peak in s¹⁰-weighted pp cross section occurs at s ≈ 25 GeV², where Aₙₙ produces its largest asymmetry and nuclear transparency drops
- At s ≈ 18-20 GeV², the pQCD prediction of Aₙₙ is closest to data and the onset of color transparency (less attenuation) is observed

## Context

This review extends the phenomenological approaches of One Boson Exchange Potential (OBEP) models and the phase-shift analyses by the SAID and Nijmegen groups, which describe NN interactions up to lab kinetic energies of 350 MeV but provide no QCD constraints. The paper highlights that the quark counting rules (Matveev-Muradyan-Tavkhelidze, Brodsky-Farrar) predict s⁻¹⁰ energy dependence for fixed-angle NN scattering, but the data show oscillatory deviations that may arise from interference with the Landshoff three-gluon exchange mechanism or near-threshold s-channel strangeness and charm production. The work enables future experimental programs at J-PARC (Japan), PANDA (Germany), NICA (Russia), and HIAF (China) to test the onset of perturbative QCD regime and color transparency in hard NN scattering, and to investigate the hidden color component of the NN system predicted by Harvey, Obukhovsky, and others.
