# Electromagnetic structure of A=2 and 3 nuclei and the nuclear current operator

> Different models for conserved two- and three-body electromagnetic currents are constructed from two- and three-nucleon interactions, using either meson-exchange mechanisms or minimal substitution in the momentum dependence of these interactions. The connection between these two different schemes is elucidated. A number of low-energy electronuclear observables, including (i) $np$ radiative capture at thermal neutron energies and deuteron photodisintegration at low energies, (ii) $nd$ and $pd$ radiative capture reactions, and (iii) isoscalar and isovector magnetic form factors of $^3$H and $^3$He, are calculated in order to make a comparative study of these models for the current operator. The realistic Argonne $v_{18}$ two-nucleon and Urbana IX or Tucson-Melbourne three-nucleon interactions are taken as a case study. For $A$=3 processes, the bound and continuum wave functions, both below and above deuteron breakup threshold, are obtained with the correlated hyperspherical-harmonics method. Three-body currents give small but significant contributions to some of the polarization observables in the $^2$H($p,γ$)$^3$He process and the $^2$H($n,γ$)$^3$H cross section at thermal neutron energies. It is shown that the use of a current which did not exactly satisfy current conservation with the two- and three-nucleon interactions in the Hamiltonian was responsible for some of the discrepancies reported in previous studies between the experimental and theoretical polarization observables in $pd$ radiative capture.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that discrepancies between theoretical and experimental polarization observables in $pd$ radiative capture, previously reported in studies by the same authors (Viviani et al., 2000), were caused by the use of a nuclear electromagnetic current that did not exactly satisfy current conservation with the two- and three-nucleon interactions in the Hamiltonian; when properly conserved currents are employed, these discrepancies are removed.

## Method

The authors constructed conserved two- and three-body electromagnetic currents from the Argonne $v_{18}$ two-nucleon and Urbana IX three-nucleon interactions using two approaches: meson-exchange mechanisms and minimal substitution in the momentum dependence of the interactions. Bound and continuum wave functions for $A=3$ processes were obtained with the correlated hyperspherical-harmonics method, and the currents were tested by calculating low-energy electronuclear observables including $np$ radiative capture, deuteron photodisintegration, $nd$ and $pd$ radiative captures, and magnetic form factors of $^3$H and $^3$He.

## Key numbers

- The theoretical prediction for the $nd$ radiative capture total cross section at thermal neutron energies exceeded the experimental value by 14% in previous work; with the present conserved current model, the overprediction is reduced to 9%.
- The astrophysical $S$-factor for $pd$ radiative capture at zero energy is calculated to be within 1% of the value extrapolated from cross section measurements in the range approximately 2 to 20 keV (Luna et al., 2002).
- Calculations for $pd$ radiative capture were extended up to center-of-mass energies of 20 MeV.

## Context

This work extends previous studies by the same group (Viviani et al., 1996, 2000; Marcucci et al., 1998) on $A=3$ radiative capture reactions and trinucleon form factors, which used currents that did not exactly satisfy current conservation. The paper also relates to alternative approaches by Arenhövel and collaborators (Buc85; Aren00; Aren04) for $A=2$ reactions, and by Golak et al. (2000), Skibinski et al. (2003), Schadow et al. (2001), and Deltuva et al. (2004) for three-nucleon systems, where three-body currents were either not considered or did not exactly satisfy current conservation with the adopted Hamiltonian. The present work enables more reliable calculations of electromagnetic observables in few-nucleon systems by providing conserved current operators consistent with realistic nuclear interactions.
