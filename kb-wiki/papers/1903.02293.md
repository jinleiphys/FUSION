# Bayesian techniques and applications to QCD

> Realizing the full potential of interconnecting the large amounts of data created in physics experiments, phenomenological models and theory simulations requires robust tools for statistical inference. Here I review a particularly promising branch, Bayesian statistics, which over the past decade has found manifold use in high-energy physics. After a brief introduction to Bayesian statistics I will present two concrete examples, where Bayesian thinking has led to progress in understanding strongly interacting matter: unfolding problems in the form of lattice QCD spectral functions (in spirit similar to detector corrections), as well as the efficient estimation of quark-gluon-plasma parameters from a systematic comparison of experimental heavy-ion collision data and phenomenological models.

# Bayesian techniques and applications to QCD

## Key claim
Bayesian statistics provides a flexible and robust framework for statistical inference in QCD that avoids the subjectivity often attributed to it, because prior information is treated as domain knowledge with uncertainties properly encoded via hyperparameters in hierarchical models. The paper argues that modern computational tools now make full Bayesian analysis (sampling the full posterior distribution rather than just its maximum) readily accessible.

## Method
The paper reviews Bayesian inference and presents two concrete applications in QCD: (1) extracting spectral functions from lattice QCD correlation functions via Bayesian reconstruction (an ill-posed inverse Laplace transform problem), where the BR prior is used with the MC-STAN Hamiltonian Monte Carlo sampler to obtain the full posterior distribution; (2) estimating quark-gluon-plasma parameters from heavy-ion collision data by chaining phenomenological models (initial conditions, hydrodynamics, hadronization) and using Gaussian processes to efficiently approximate the likelihood from ~100 training simulations on a Latin hypercube.

## Key numbers
- Lattice QCD spectral reconstruction used N_omega = 1000 frequency bins with N_tau = 32 correlator data points, running 40 MC chains of 2500 iterations each (500 warmup steps)
- The BR prior uses a Gamma distribution: p_BR(rho|m,alpha) = Gamma[alpha+1, alpha/m] with a half-Gaussian hyperprior p(alpha) = HalfNormal[0,10]
- The QGP parameter estimation study (Bernhard 2016) selected 9 free parameters among all model parameters: 4 related to initial conditions (p entropy deposition, k shape fluctuations, w Gaussian nucleon width, normalization) and 5 QGP properties (T_switch hadronization temperature, eta/S_HRG specific viscosity in hadronic phase, eta/S_min minimal specific viscosity at T_switch, slope of specific viscosity, zeta/S magnitude of specific bulk viscosity)
- The Gaussian process kernel uses three hyperparameters: sigma_GP (correlation strength), correlation lengths l_k, and sigma_n (training data uncertainty)

## Context
This review extends the line of Bayesian methods applied to QCD inverse problems, building on the Maximum Entropy Method (Asakawa 2000) and the BR prior (Burnier 2013) for spectral function reconstruction. For QGP parameter estimation, it follows the first genuinely Bayesian study by Bernhard (2016) and subsequent work by Auvinen (2017). The paper enables future work on incorporating analytic properties of QCD spectra into priors (potentially via machine learning), improving information content of lattice correlators through anisotropic lattices or modified time contours (Pawlowski 2016), and comprehensive Bayesian parameter estimation for heavy-ion collision models.
