# Sensitive dependence of isotope and isobar distribution of limiting temperatures on symmetry energy

> The mass, isotope, and isobar distributions of limiting temperatures for finite nuclei are investigated by using a thermodynamics approach together with the Skyrme energy density functional. The relationship between the width of the isotope (isobar) distribution of limiting temperatures and the stiffness of the density dependence of the symmetry energy clearly is observed. The nuclear symmetry energy with smaller slope parameter $L_{\rm{sym}}$ causes a wider the isotope (isobar) distribution of limiting temperatures. The widths of the isotope (isobar) distributions of limiting temperatures could be useful observables for exploring the information of the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy at finite temperatures.

## Key claim

The width of the isotope (isobar) distribution of limiting temperatures is correlated with the stiffness of the density dependence of the symmetry energy, specifically with the slope parameter \(L_{\rm{sym}}\), such that a softer symmetry energy (smaller \(L_{\rm{sym}}\)) produces a wider distribution; however, the neutron-proton effective mass splitting also influences the width, and the symmetry energy cannot be uniquely constrained by only two data points in the isotope or isobar chain.

## Method

The authors used a thermodynamic liquid-drop model in thermal, mechanical, and chemical equilibrium with surrounding vapor, employing the Skyrme energy density functional with 29 different Skyrme interactions that have a fixed incompressibility range (\(K_{\infty}=230\pm30\) MeV) but varied slope parameters \(L_{\rm{sym}}\) and curvature parameters \(K_{\rm{sym}}\). They calculated limiting temperatures for nuclei along isotope chains (e.g., Sn and Sr) and isobar chains (e.g., \(^{93}A\)) and fitted the resulting distributions with a three-parameter Gaussian function to extract the distribution width \(\sigma\). The correlation between \(\sigma\) and \(L_{\rm{sym}}\) was then examined, and the influence of the effective mass splitting was also assessed.

## Key numbers

- The critical temperature for infinite nuclear matter was taken as 17 MeV, referenced from Natowitz (2002) where \(T_c=16.6\pm 0.86\) MeV.
- 29 sets of Skyrme interactions were selected with incompressibility \(K_{\infty}=230\pm30\) MeV and values of \(L_{\rm{sym}}\) ranging from \(-31.17\) MeV (SkM1) to 160.74 MeV (SkI1) at zero temperature.
- For the Skz-series interactions, which share the same isoscalar part, \(L_{\rm{sym}}\) values range from 4.89 MeV (Skz4) to 62.40 MeV (Skz-1) at zero temperature.
- The isotope distribution of limiting temperatures for Sn isotopes intercrosses around \(^{116}\)Sn, the \(\beta\)-stability isotope of Sn.
- For the Sr isotope chain, the SkT5 interaction (\(L_{\rm{sym}}=100.11\) MeV at zero temperature) reproduced the experimental data for \(^{80,93}\)Sr reasonably well, while Skz-family interactions overestimated the data.
- The width \(\sigma\) of the isotope distribution for Sr shows a clear correlation with \(L_{\rm{sym}}\): softer symmetry energy (smaller \(L_{\rm{sym}}\)) yields a wider distribution, with fluctuations in the range \(L_{\rm{sym}}=48\) to 65 MeV attributed to the effective mass splitting.

## Context

This work extends the earlier study by Li and Liu (2004), which showed that the isotope distribution of limiting temperatures depends on the isospin-dependent part of the interaction but did not separate isoscalar and isovector effects. The present paper isolates the role of the symmetry energy by using Skyrme interactions with identical isoscalar parts (the Skz series) and demonstrates that the distribution width \(\sigma\) is a useful observable for constraining the density dependence of the symmetry energy at finite temperatures. The results suggest that at least one more experimental datum point (e.g., \(^{83}\)Sr–\(^{86}\)Sr or \(^{93}_{40}A\)–\(^{93}_{42}A\)) is needed to determine the width and thus better constrain the symmetry energy, building on the experimental data from the S254 experiment at GSI (Sfienti, 2009).
