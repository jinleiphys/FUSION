# Aspects of Chiral Symmetry

> This article is an attempt to a pedagogical introduction and review into the elementary concepts of chiral symmetry in nuclear physics. Effective chiral models such as the linear and nonlinear sigma model will be discussed as well as the essential ideas of chiral perturbation theory. Some applications to the physics of ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions will be presented.

## Key claim

This pedagogical review argues that chiral symmetry, while an approximate symmetry of QCD due to small but finite quark masses, is spontaneously broken in the ground state, explaining the small pion mass and the success of relations such as the Goldberger-Treiman relation; the abstract glosses over the fact that the symmetry is only approximate and that the spontaneous breaking is essential to reconcile the symmetry with the observed hadron mass spectrum.

## Method

The paper introduces the Lagrangian formulation of quantum field theory and derives Noether currents for vector and axial-vector transformations of massless fermion fields, establishing the SU(2)_V × SU(2)_A symmetry. It then constructs the linear sigma model as a chirally invariant effective theory involving pions, sigma mesons, and nucleons, and discusses how explicit symmetry breaking via finite quark masses and spontaneous symmetry breaking via a non-zero vacuum expectation value of the sigma field generate the pion mass and the Goldstone boson nature of the pion.

## Key numbers

- Pion decay constant: f_π = 93 MeV, determined from experiment via the matrix element <0|A_μ^a(x)|π^b(q)> = -i f_π q_μ δ^{ab} e^{-iq·x}
- Axial-vector coupling constant of the nucleon: g_a = 1.25, from weak beta decay of the neutron
- Goldberger-Treiman relation prediction: g_{πNN} = g_a M_N / f_π ≈ 12.6, compared to experimental value g_{πNN}^{exp} = 13.4
- Light quark masses: 5–10 MeV, compared to QCD scale Λ_QCD ≈ 200 MeV
- ρ meson mass: m_ρ = 770 MeV; a_1 meson mass: m_{a_1} = 1260 MeV; predicted relation m_{a_1} = √2 m_ρ

## Context

This review extends the pedagogical tradition of introducing chiral symmetry concepts in nuclear physics, building on the foundational work of Gell-Mann and Levy (1960) who introduced the linear sigma model, and on the development of chiral perturbation theory. The paper enables experimentalists working in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions to understand how chiral symmetry restoration at high temperature or density would lead to degenerate meson masses and the loss of the pion's Goldstone boson nature, which is a major goal of the heavy ion program.
