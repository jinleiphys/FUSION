# Magnetic field evolution of X-ray emitting radio-quiet pulsars

> The intense magnetic fields present in neutron stars are closely linked to their observed temperature and spectral characteristics, timing properties, including spin period and its derivatives. Therefore, a comprehensive theoretical analysis of magnetic field evolution is essential for understanding how the strength of the magnetic field change over time. The decay rate of magnetic field in isolated, non-accreting neutron stars can be assessed by evaluating the second derivative of the spin frequency. Another method to estimate this rate involves monitoring an increase in thermal emission beyond what is expected from standard cooling processes, assuming no additional heating mechanisms are present. Our findings indicate that for X-ray emitting isolated neutron stars, the evolution rate of spin period derivative aligns with the dissipation rate of magnetic energy from the dipolar field, provided that a substantial portion of the released energy is emitted as X-rays. The time scale of magnetic field decay is found to be much shorter than typical age of radio pulsars.

## Key claim

The paper claims that for X-ray emitting isolated neutron stars (the "Magnificent Seven"), the evolution rate of the spin period derivative aligns with the dissipation rate of magnetic energy from the dipolar field, provided that a substantial portion of the released energy is emitted as X-rays and no additional heating mechanisms are present.

## Method

The authors estimated the instantaneous magnetic field decay timescale from the observed braking index using the magneto-dipole equation, assuming an exponential decay of the field. They then independently estimated the decay timescale by equating the magnetic energy dissipation rate to the observed X-ray luminosity, assuming all released energy is converted to Ohmic heating of the crust and emitted as thermal X-rays. The two timescales were compared for the six members of the Magnificent Seven with detected pulsations, using spin frequency and its derivatives from Bogdanov and Ho (2024) and X-ray luminosities from Potekhin et al. (2020).

## Key numbers

- For RX J0720.4-3125: magnetic field B = 2.5 × 10^13 G, frequency derivative dot-nu = -1.02 × 10^-15 Hz s^-1, braking index n = 221, magnetic field decay timescale tau = 3.4182 × 10^4 years.
- For RX J0720.4-3125: the X-ray luminosity calculated from the spin-evolution timescale is approximately 6 × 10^32 erg s^-1 (using total neutron star volume) or 0.6 × 10^32 erg s^-1 (if field confined to crust), compared to the observed luminosity range of (1.1-3.2) × 10^32 erg s^-1.
- For RX J0806.4-4123: braking index n = 1077, decay timescale tau = 6.3489 × 10^4 years.
- For RX J1308.6+2127: braking index n = 157, decay timescale tau = 3.7923 × 10^4 years.
- For RX J2143.0+0654: braking index n = 378, decay timescale tau = 3.8453 × 10^4 years.
- For RX J1856.6-3754: braking index n = 425, decay timescale tau = 3.5304 × 10^4 years.
- For RX J0420.0-5022: braking index n = 45, decay timescale tau = 1.8117 × 10^5 years.
- For RX J1605.3+3249: braking index n = 4, decay timescale tau = 2.3077 × 10^5 years.
- The ratio L_r/L_X (spin-down luminosity to X-ray luminosity) is about two orders of magnitude less than unity for all M7 sources except RX J0420.0-5022 (ratio 4.6481) and RX J1605.3+3249 (ratio 6.3896).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Bogdanov and Ho (2024), who reported the first measurement of the second derivative of the spin period for RX J0720.4-3125 (ddot-P = -4.1 × 10^-25 s s^-2, braking index n ≈ 675). The authors compare their results to the magneto-thermal evolution simulations of Vigano et al. (2013), which predicted braking indices of about n ≈ 68 for similar objects. The paper challenges the fallback disk model of Ertan et al. (2014), which predicted a braking index of n ≈ 80, nearly an order of magnitude lower than the observed value. The results enable predictions of braking indices (expected to be a few hundred) and second frequency derivatives (10^-28 to 10^-27 Hz s^-2) for the remaining M7 sources, and suggest that magnetic field decay timescales of 10^4-10^5 years are much shorter than typical radio pulsar ages.
