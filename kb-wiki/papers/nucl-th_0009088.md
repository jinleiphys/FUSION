# Two-neutron separation energies, binding energies and phase transitions in the interacting boson model

> In the framework of the interacting boson model the three transitional regions (rotational-vibrational, rotational-$γ$-unstable and, vibrational-$γ$-unstable transitions) are reanalyzed. A new kind of plot is presented for studying phase transitions in finite systems such as atomic nuclei. The importance of analyzing binding energies and not only energy spectra and electromagnetic transitions, describing transitional regions is emphasized. We finally discuss a number of realistic examples.

# Two-neutron separation energies, binding energies and phase transitions in the interacting boson model

## Key claim
Phase transitions in atomic nuclei can be identified through anomalies in two-neutron separation energies (S₂ₙ) when crossing a critical point along a chain of isotopes, but the observability of such anomalies depends critically on whether the control parameter varies linearly or quadratically with boson number. The authors show that fitting only energy spectra without also fitting binding energies can lead to parameter sets that reproduce excited states well but give binding energies differing by up to about 1 MeV.

## Method
The authors use the interacting boson model (IBM-1) with a Hamiltonian that can describe the three transitional regions: U(5)-SU(3) (spherical to deformed), U(5)-O(6) (spherical to γ-unstable), and SU(3)-O(6) (deformed to γ-unstable). They compute binding energies and two-neutron separation energies both by exact diagonalization in the laboratory frame and using the intrinsic-state (coherent state) formalism. For realistic cases, they fit Hamiltonian parameters to experimental energy spectra of Gd, Pd, and Pt isotope chains and then compare the resulting calculated S₂ₙ values with experimental data.

## Key numbers
- The critical control parameter for the phase transition is given by ξ_c = N/(5N + χ² − 8), which for large boson number approaches ξ_c ≈ 0.2.
- For the Gd isotopes, using χ = −√7/2 gave poor S₂ₙ agreement; using χ = −0.6 and κ = 19.2 keV with a linear background S₂ₙ^{lin} = 16.75 − 0.607 N_ν (MeV) gave good agreement.
- For the Pd isotopes, using χ = 0 gave poor S₂ₙ agreement; using χ = −0.3 and ε_d = 70 keV with a linear background S₂ₙ^{lin} = 20.46 − 0.835 N_ν (MeV) gave good agreement.
- For the Pt isotopes, using χ = 0 and ξ = 0 gave poor S₂ₙ agreement; using ε_d = 25.7 keV, κ = 33.5 keV, and κ′ = 15.2 keV with a linear background S₂ₙ^{lin} = 11.85 − 0.649 N_ν (MeV) gave good agreement.
- For ¹⁴⁸Sm, different parameter sets that all reproduce the energy spectrum give binding energies of 0.939 MeV (Scholten 1978), 1.571 MeV (Chou 1997), and 2.253 MeV (also Chou 1997), differing by up to about 1.3 MeV.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of phase transitions in the IBM by Casten, Kusnezov, and Zamfir (1999) and by Jolie, Cejnar, and Dobeš (1999), who analyzed phase transitions using energy spectra and electromagnetic transitions. The authors introduce a new type of plot (binding energy versus control parameter for multiple boson numbers) that is particularly suited to finite systems where the control parameter cannot be varied externally. The paper shows that binding energy analysis can discriminate between parameter sets that give equivalent descriptions of spectra, a point previously underappreciated in IBM studies such as those by Scholten (1978, 1980), Stachel (1982), and Casten (1978). This enables more consistent IBM descriptions of long isotope chains and provides a framework for interpreting new mass measurements from facilities accessing unstable nuclei.
