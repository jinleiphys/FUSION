# Centrality dependence of multiplicity fluctuations from a hydrodynamical approach

> As one of the possible signals for the whereabouts of the critical point on the QCD phase diagram, recently, the multiplicity fluctuations in heavy-ion collisions have aroused much attention. It is a crucial observable of the Beam Energy Scan program of the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider. In this work, we investigate the centrality dependence of the multiplicity fluctuations regarding the recent measurements from STAR Collaboration. By employing a hydrodynamical approach, the present study is dedicated to the noncritical aspects of the phenomenon. To be specific, in addition to the thermal fluctuations, finite volume corrections, and resonance decay at the freeze-out surface, the model is focused on the properties of the hydrodynamic expansion of the system and the event-by-event initial fluctuations. It is understood that the real signal of the critical point can only be obtained after appropriately subtracting the background, the latter is investigated in the present work. Besides the experimental data, our results are also compared to those of the hadronic resonance gas, as well as transport models.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a significant portion of measured multiplicity fluctuations in heavy-ion collisions arises from noncritical sources (thermal fluctuations, finite volume corrections, resonance decay, hydrodynamic expansion, and event-by-event initial fluctuations), and that the true signal of the QCD critical point can only be isolated after subtracting these background contributions. The authors explicitly state their approach does not include critical physics, so their results provide an estimate of the background rather than a direct signal of the critical point.

## Method

The authors used the SPheRIO hydrodynamic code (an ideal relativistic fluid model based on the Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics algorithm) to simulate Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV. Each fluid element (SPH particle) on the freeze-out surface is treated as a quantum grand canonical ensemble, and the Cooper-Frye sudden freeze-out is assumed at constant temperature. The model incorporates thermal fluctuations, finite volume corrections, and resonance decay via a generating function method, with initial conditions generated by NeXuS and averaged over events in each centrality bin.

## Key numbers

- Simulations used 980 events for 0-5% centrality, 1039 events for 6-15%, 1138 events for 15-25%, 1112 events for 25-35%, 994 events for 35-45%, 966 events for 45-55%, and 985 events for 60-80% centrality windows
- The centrality dependence of dynamical fluctuations becomes more pronounced when the number of participants is less than 100
- For the quantity $\kappa\sigma^2$ (kurtosis times variance), the hydrodynamic results do not show a significant difference from those of the Hadron Resonance Gas model, and the effect of system expansion pushes results in a "wrong" direction relative to STAR data
- The products $\kappa\sigma^2$ and $S\sigma$ (skewness times standard deviation) are evaluated as ratios of particle number cumulants, where $\kappa\sigma^2 = 1$ for an ideal Poissonian distribution

## Context

This work extends previous studies using the Hadron Resonance Gas model (which considered thermal fluctuations, finite volume correction, and resonance decay) by adding the effects of hydrodynamic expansion and event-by-event initial condition fluctuations. The results are compared to STAR Collaboration data from the Beam Energy Scan program at RHIC, as well as to UrQMD transport model calculations and HRG model results. The paper builds on a recent attempt (Ma et al., 2019) to evaluate multiplicity fluctuations using a hydrodynamical model, and the authors note that progress has been made very recently about implementing canonical or microcanonical systems in a hydrodynamical approach. The inability of the hydrodynamic model to reproduce the measured $\kappa\sigma^2$ suggests that the experimentally observed non-monotonical dependence of kurtosis on centrality might carry crucial information about the critical point, motivating further study with equations of state that include finite baryon density effects.
