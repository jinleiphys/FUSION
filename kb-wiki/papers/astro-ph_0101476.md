# Element Synthesis in Stars

> Except for 1H, 2H, 3He, 4He, and 7Li, originating from the Big Bang, all heavier elements are made in stellar evolution and stellar explosions. Nuclear physics, and in many cases nuclear structure far from stability, enters in a crucial way. Therefore, we examine in this review the role of nuclear physics in astrophysics in general and in particular how it affects stellar events and the resulting nucleosynthesis. Stellar modeling addresses four major aspects: 1. energy generation and nucleosynthesis, 2. energy transport via conduction, radiation or possibly convection, 3. hydrodynamics/hydrostatics, and finally 4. thermodynamic properties of the matter involved. Nuclear Physics enters via nuclear reaction cross sections and nuclear structure (affecting the composition changes and nuclear energy generation), neutrino-nucleon and neutrino-nucleus cross sections (affecting neutrino opacities and transport), and e.g. the equation of state at and beyond nuclear densities which creates a relation between the nuclear many-body problem and the hydrodynamic response like pressure and entropy. In the following we review these four topics by highlighting the role and impact of nuclear physics in each of these aspects of stellar modeling. The main emphasis is put on the connection to element synthesis.

## Key claim

This review argues that nuclear physics, especially nuclear structure far from stability, weak interaction rates, and the equation of state at supranuclear densities, is a crucial input for stellar modeling and nucleosynthesis, but the authors note that many aspects of the explosion mechanism in core-collapse supernovae remain uncertain and depend on the interplay of multiple nuclear inputs that are not yet fully constrained.

## Method

The paper reviews four major aspects of stellar modeling, energy generation and nucleosynthesis, energy transport, hydrodynamics/hydrostatics, and thermodynamic properties, and examines how nuclear reaction cross sections, neutrino-nucleus cross sections, and the nuclear equation of state enter each aspect. Specific astrophysical applications are discussed: stellar evolution, type II and type Ia supernovae, novae and X-ray bursts (the rp-process), and possible sites of the r-process, with emphasis on how nuclear physics uncertainties affect the predicted nucleosynthesis yields.

## Key numbers

- The total energy released in a core-collapse supernova is 2–3 × 10^53 erg, equal to the gravitational binding energy of a neutron star.
- In stellar evolution, fusion reactions transform hydrogen to iron-group nuclei, releasing eventually 8.7 MeV of binding energy per nucleon.
- Ne-burning occurs at temperatures above 1.5 × 10^9 K due to a small Q-value of about 4 MeV; Si-burning occurs at temperatures in excess of 3 × 10^9 K.
- Stars with masses greater than 8 solar masses develop an onion-like composition and produce a collapsing core; less massive stars end as C/O white dwarfs that do not exceed the Chandrasekhar mass.
- In type Ia supernova models, central ignition densities of 1.37 and 2.12 × 10^9 g cm^-3 were tested, with deflagration speeds of 0.015, 0.03, or 0.05 times the sound speed.
- The rp-process waiting point nuclei include 25Si (half-life 0.22 s), 29S (0.187 s), 34Ar (0.844 s), 38Ca (0.439 s), and later 64Ge (64 s), 68Se (96 s), 74Kr (17 s).
- The rp-process bottleneck at 56Ni requires minimum temperatures around 10^9 K, maximum temperatures below 2 × 10^9 K, and densities exceeding 10^6 g cm^-3.

## Context

This review extends earlier work on stellar nucleosynthesis by integrating recent advances in nuclear physics, including shell model calculations for weak interaction rates (Langanke 2000) and statistical model predictions for reaction cross sections (Rauscher 1999, 2000). It discusses how improved electron capture rates from large-scale shell model diagonalization (Dean 1998, Langanke 2000) affect predictions for type Ia supernova yields, and how uncertainties in the r-process site, whether supernovae or neutron star mergers, are highlighted by observations of r-process/Fe ratios varying by more than a factor of 100 in low-metallicity stars (Sneden 2000, Truran 2000). The paper enables future work by identifying key nuclear inputs, such as masses far from stability, pygmy resonances in E1-strength, and fission barriers, that must be better constrained to model element synthesis reliably.
