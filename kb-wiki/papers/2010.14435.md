# Investigation of low-lying resonances in breakup of halo nuclei within the time-dependent approach

> We investigate the Coulomb breakup of $^{11}$Be halo nuclei on a heavy target from intermediate (70 MeV/nucleon) to low energies (5 MeV/nucleon) within the non-perturbative time-dependent approach. The convergence of the computational scheme is demonstrated in this energy range including \textit{n}+$^{10}$Be low-lying resonances in different partial and spin states. We have found a considerable contribution of the $5/2^{+}$ resonance ($E_{r}$= 1.23 MeV) to the breakup cross section at 30 MeV/nucleon and lower, while at higher energies, the resonant states $3/2^{-}$ and $3/2^{+}$ (with $E_{r}$= 2.78 and 3.3 MeV) make most visible contributions. The obtained results are in good agreement with experimental data available at 69 and 72 MeV/nucleon. Comparison with the existing theoretical calculations of other authors for 20 and 30 MeV/nucleon is also made. The developed computational scheme opens new possibilities in the investigation of the Coulomb, as well as nuclear, breakup of other halo nuclei on heavy and light targets.

## Key claim

The non-perturbative time-dependent approach for Coulomb breakup of halo nuclei, previously validated at intermediate energies, is extended to low beam energies (down to 5 MeV/nucleon) and shown to converge; the contribution of low-lying resonances to the breakup cross section becomes increasingly important as beam energy decreases, with the $5/2^{+}$ resonance dominating at 30 MeV/nucleon and below, while at higher energies the $3/2^{-}$ and $3/2^{+}$ resonances contribute more visibly.

## Method

The authors numerically integrate the time-dependent Schrodinger equation for the halo neutron relative to the $^{10}$Be core in the projectile rest frame, using a non-perturbative algorithm with a discrete-variable representation on a two-dimensional angular grid and sixth-order finite differences on a quasiuniform radial grid up to 1200 fm. The time-dependent Coulomb interaction between the projectile and the $^{208}$Pb target is treated without multipole expansion, and the projectile follows a classical straight-line trajectory. The breakup cross section is computed by projecting the final wave packet onto continuum states, either with Bessel functions or with eigenfunctions of the neutron-core Hamiltonian that include resonant states.

## Key numbers

- The $5/2^{+}$ resonance has $E_{r}=1.23$ MeV and width $\Gamma=100$ keV (theory) or $E_{r}=1.281$ MeV and $\Gamma=120$ keV (experiment).
- The $3/2^{-}$ resonance has $E_{r}=2.789$ MeV and $\Gamma=240$ keV (theory) or $E_{r}=2.898$ MeV and $\Gamma=120$ keV (experiment).
- The $3/2^{+}$ resonance has $E_{r}=3.367$ MeV and $\Gamma=3$ keV (theory) or $E_{r}=2.387$ MeV and $\Gamma=8$ keV (experiment).
- At 69 MeV/nucleon, including the three resonances increases the breakup cross section at relative energy $E=1.2$ MeV by 25% (from 0.383 to 0.479 b/MeV) and at $E=3.3$ MeV by about 3% (from 0.036 to 0.037 b/MeV).
- The breakup cross section at 10 MeV/nucleon converges to within about 1% for an angular grid with $N=81$ points and an impact parameter integration range $b_{min}=12$ fm to $b_{max}=400$ fm.
- The ground state of $^{11}$Be is bound at -0.503 MeV, and the $1/2^{-}$ excited state is bound at -0.183 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the non-perturbative time-dependent approach previously applied to $^{11}$Be, $^{15}$C, and $^{17}$F at higher beam energies (Melezhik et al., 1999; 2003; 2001) down to 5 MeV/nucleon, a region relevant for facilities such as HIE-ISOLDE at CERN and ReA12 at MSU. It improves upon earlier calculations that omitted resonant states (e.g., the 1999 and 2003 works) and provides comparisons with the distorted-wave Born approximation (DWBA) at 30 MeV/nucleon (2002) and the dynamical eikonal approximation at 20 MeV/nucleon (2005; 2006), showing significant deviations at lower energies. The method enables systematic study of low-lying resonances in halo nuclei via breakup reactions and can be extended to include nuclear breakup on light targets.
