## Key claim
The $H$ dibaryon exists as a multichannel bound state in the strangeness $S=-2$ sector, with two bound states identified below the $\Lambda\Lambda$ threshold (one deeply bound and one shallow) and resonances near the $\Sigma\Sigma$ and $\Sigma^*\Sigma^*$ thresholds, but the existence and properties depend on which strategy is used to fix the low-energy constants and on the cutoff value, with strategy~1 yielding only virtual states while strategy~2 produces bound states.

## Method
The authors constrain the $S$-wave baryon-baryon interaction using SU(6) symmetry within a nonrelativistic effective field theory (NREFT), where the leading-order Lagrangian contains only two independent parameters ($\tilde{a}$ and $\tilde{b}$). These parameters are determined using two inputs: the physical $NN$ scattering lengths (in the $^3S_1$ and $^1S_0$ channels) and the lattice QCD $\Omega\Omega$ scattering length from the HAL QCD Collaboration. Two strategies are employed to relate the parameters to scattering lengths: strategy~1 uses the SU(6)-averaged baryon mass, while strategy~2 uses physical masses for $m_N$, $m_\Delta$, and the lattice value for $m_\Omega$. The coupled-channel scattering problem is solved for the four-channel isoscalar $J=0$, $Q=0$, $S=-2$ sector, and poles of the $T$-matrix are searched on the complex energy plane across various Riemann sheets.

## Context
This work extends the SU(6) symmetry approach to baryon-baryon interactions previously applied to $NN$, $\Delta\Delta$, and $\Omega\Omega$ scattering by Richardson (2024) and builds on the SU(6) framework developed by Kaplan (1995). It addresses the long-standing debate over the $H$ dibaryon, first predicted by Jaffe (1976), whose nature has remained unsettled despite extensive theoretical work (including chiral effective field theory studies by Haidenbauer, 2011, and lattice QCD calculations by the NPLQCD and HAL QCD collaborations) and experimental searches (including the J-PARC E42 experiment). The results provide clear signatures for experimental verification through distinct enhancements in $\Lambda\Lambda$ invariant mass distributions, and the predicted poles can be tested in reactions producing $\Lambda\Lambda$ pairs, such as those studied at J-PARC.
