# Constraining the (gamma,pi) amplitude for E2 N->delta

> A recent analysis of pion photo-production multipoles using p(g,pi0) data from Mainz is repeated, successively adding constraints from other observables and varying the number of fitted partial waves. The original analysis is shown to have been underconstrained and suffered from ambiguities. The inclusion of additional observables in our analysis results in a very different, but stable, ratio of E2/M1 multipoles at the delta that is consistent with the Mainz data set.

# Constraining the (γ,π) amplitude for E2 N→Δ

## Key claim

The ratio of E2/M1 multipoles at the Δ resonance extracted from Mainz data alone is unreliable and underconstrained; when additional observables are included, the ratio becomes stable but significantly lower than the original Mainz result, contradicting the apparent agreement between the Mainz and LEGS values.

## Method

The authors repeated the energy-independent analysis of the Mainz p(γ,π⁰) data using S and P-wave multipoles with phases fixed to the VPI[SM95] πN scattering solution, then performed an energy-dependent K-matrix-like parameterization of the (γ,π) multipoles. They successively added constraints from π⁺ cross sections, beam asymmetries, Bonn target asymmetries, and Khar'kov target and recoil asymmetries, while increasing the number of fitted partial waves from S-P through S-F waves.

## Key numbers

- Original Mainz result for R_π⁰ at 340 MeV: −2.31 (%) ± 0.54(stat+sys) from S and P-wave fit to only 2 observables
- With σ(π⁺) and Σ(π⁺) data added, S-P wave fit gave R_EM = −(3.27 ± 0.35)%, but extending to S-F waves gave −(5.66 ± 0.63)%, showing instability
- With σ, Σ, target (T) and recoil (P) asymmetries added, S-P wave fit gave R_EM = −(1.40 ± 0.24)%, and S-F wave fit gave −(3.39 ± 0.38)%
- With all constraints including older Bonn σ(π⁰) data, the result stabilized at R_EM = −(0.58 ± 0.08)% for S-P waves and −(0.58 ± 0.12)% for S-F waves

## Context

This work extends the multipole analysis controversy between the Mainz group (Beck 1997) and the LEGS Collaboration, who reported ratios of −2.5% and −2.9% respectively despite incompatible underlying data. The authors demonstrate that the Mainz analysis suffered from the Donnachie (1973) ambiguity, where higher partial wave strength can appear in lower partial waves when too few observables are fitted. The results enable a more reliable extraction of the E2/M1 ratio for N→Δ transitions, which discriminates between nucleon structure models, and highlight the need for complete sets of polarization observables as discussed by Chiang and Tabakin (1997).
