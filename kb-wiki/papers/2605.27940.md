# A Question of Shape: New Mechanism Governing Superheavy Nuclei Survival

> We demonstrate that hot superheavy nuclei do not retain spherical shapes, as traditionally assumed, but instead equilibrate in deformed, often oblate or triaxial, configurations at finite excitation energy. This behavior arises from a mechanism analogous to the Jahn-Teller effect: spherical systems exhibit high single-particle degeneracy near the Fermi surface, causing their shell corrections to damp out significantly faster with temperature than those of deformed shapes. Using a finite-temperature framework, we reveal a thermally induced inversion of the potential-energy landscape in the Z = 118-120 region, where deformed minima become energetically favored at U = 30-50 MeV. This shape inversion fundamentally alters the competition between neutron evaporation and fission. We derive a deformation-dependent correction to the survival probability, revealing a systematic bias in estimates based on spherical ground-state properties. Our results identify a finite-temperature structural effect that calls for a revision of current models of superheavy-nucleus synthesis and decay.

# A Question of Shape: New Mechanism Governing Superheavy Nuclei Survival

## Key claim

Hot superheavy nuclei do not retain spherical shapes as traditionally assumed, but instead equilibrate in deformed (often oblate or triaxial) configurations at finite excitation energy, due to a thermally induced inversion of the potential-energy landscape that occurs specifically in the Z = 118–120 region at excitation energies U ≈ 30–50 MeV.

## Method

The authors employ a macroscopic-microscopic finite-temperature framework where the effective potential energy is decomposed into a macroscopic liquid-drop part (assumed temperature-independent) and a microscopic part that accounts for thermal damping of shell and pairing correlations via the free-energy correction. For each deformation, a local canonical ensemble is assumed with a deformation-dependent temperature determined self-consistently by energy conservation, and the potential energy surface is computed on a (β₂₀, β₂₂) grid with step 0.025 up to deformation 0.25 for isotopes of element Z = 118. The damping parameter E_d^GS is extracted from the logarithmic derivative of the microscopic correction with respect to excitation energy, averaged over U* = 10–50 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The thermally induced shape inversion occurs at excitation energies U ≈ 30–50 MeV for nuclei in the Z = 118–120 region.
- For the well-deformed nucleus ²⁷⁵Ds (β₂₀^GS = 0.21), the damping parameter E_d^GS = 30.82 MeV, indicating slow damping of shell effects.
- For the transitional nucleus ²⁹⁶Ts (β₂₀^GS = 0.04), E_d^GS = 24.06 MeV.
- For the spherical nucleus ³⁰⁴120 (β₂₀^GS = 0), E_d^GS = 19.27 MeV, indicating rapid damping.
- For 353 nuclei in the Z = 98–120 region, E_d^GS ≈ 30 MeV for |β₂₀^GS| > 0.15, and varies from 17 to 30 MeV for near-spherical deformations.
- The reduction factor from shell damping for a typical microscopic correction of −6 MeV is of order 0.02–0.01 for the energy range 30–50 MeV, using E_d^GS ≈ 30 MeV and T ≈ 1 MeV.
- The compensatory factor F from the deformation-dependent level density is approximately 5–10 when the equilibrium deformation shifts to β^eq ≈ 0.15, based on a 5% increase in the level density parameter.

## Context

This work extends the macroscopic-microscopic approach previously applied by Rahmatinejad (2024) to superheavy nuclei, and identifies a nuclear analogue of the Jahn-Teller effect (Jahn 1937) as the mechanism driving thermal shape transitions. The results contradict the standard assumption that hot compound nuclei retain spherical shapes, and call for revision of current models of superheavy-nucleus synthesis and decay (Oganessian 2000, 2006; Hofmann 2007, 2012; Düllmann 2010). The deformation-dependent correction to the survival probability derived here enables improved predictions for evaporation-residue formation in hot-fusion reactions.
