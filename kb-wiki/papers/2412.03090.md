# A deep neural network approach to solve the Dirac equation

> We extend the method from [Naito, Naito, and Hashimoto, Phys. Rev. Research 5, 033189 (2023)] to solve the Dirac equation not only for the ground state but also for low-lying excited states using a deep neural network and the unsupervised machine learning technique. The variational method fails because of the Dirac sea, which is avoided by introducing the inverse Hamiltonian method. For low-lying excited states, two methods are proposed, which have different performances and advantages. The validity of this method is verified by the calculations with the Coulomb and Woods-Saxon potentials.

# A deep neural network approach to solve the Dirac equation

## Key claim
The paper extends the unsupervised deep neural network method of Naito, Naito, and Hashimoto (2023) to solve the Dirac equation for both ground and low-lying excited states, using the inverse Hamiltonian method to avoid variational collapse caused by the Dirac sea. The method is validated for Coulomb and Woods-Saxon potentials, but the accuracy of the orthonormal method degrades for higher excited states due to error accumulation from lower-state wave functions.

## Method
The radial Dirac equation is discretized on a mesh (logarithmic for Coulomb, uniform for Woods-Saxon potentials) and solved using a fully connected deep neural network with two hidden layers of 16 units each and softplus activation functions. The ground state is obtained by minimizing the expectation value of the inverse Hamiltonian operator, which avoids the variational collapse by inverting the spectrum around a chosen parameter epsilon'. Low-lying excited states are computed either by adjusting the epsilon' parameter in the inverse Hamiltonian method or by constructing wave functions orthogonal to all lower-energy states (orthonormal method), with the large component F output by the DNN and the small component G computed via the Dirac equation.

## Key numbers
- For the hydrogen atom (kappa = -1), the inverse Hamiltonian method achieves relative energy errors of 1.79 x 10^-7 for n=1 and 5.68 x 10^-4 to 7.42 x 10^-4 for n=2 through n=6.
- For the orthonormal method on hydrogen (kappa = -1), relative energy errors range from 6.00 x 10^-4 (n=2) to 2.16 x 10^-3 (n=6).
- The inverse Hamiltonian method uses 1700 mesh points (M = 1700) with box sizes from 20 a.u. (n=1) to 100 a.u. (n=6) for hydrogen.
- Time per epoch for the inverse Hamiltonian method on hydrogen is 0.0553 to 0.0570 seconds; for the orthonormal method it is 0.111 to 0.136 seconds.
- For Woods-Saxon potentials, the scalar-vector sum U(r) uses parameters V' = -71.28 fm, r_0 = 1.233 fm, a = 0.615 fm, and lambda_n = 11.12.

## Context
This work extends the unsupervised DNN approach of Naito, Naito, and Hashimoto (2023) from non-relativistic quantum mechanics to the relativistic Dirac equation, addressing the variational collapse problem that arises from the Dirac sea. The inverse Hamiltonian method was previously introduced by PhysRevC.82.057301 for nuclear structure calculations, and the orthonormal method adapts the excited-state technique from Naito, Naito, and Hashimoto (2023) to the Dirac framework. The method enables future applications to relativistic nuclear structure calculations and atomic physics where the Dirac equation is essential, including systems with more complex potentials or multiple particles.
