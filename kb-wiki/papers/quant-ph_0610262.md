# An application of renormalization in Hilbert space at phase transition points in strongly interacting systems

> We introduce an algorithm aimed to reduce the dimensions of Hilbert space. It is used here in order to study the behaviour of low energy states of strongly interacting quantum many-body systems at first order transitions and avoided crossings. The method is tested on different frustrated quantum spin ladders with two legs. The role and importance of symmetries are investigated by using different bases of states.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a Hilbert-space renormalization algorithm that reduces the dimension of the many-body Hilbert space step by step, and shows that at first-order phase transition points (level crossings) the renormalized coupling constant remains constant over a large range of space dimensions, thereby signaling the transition; however, the stability is limited by numerical pathologies at the exact crossing point and by the approximation inherent in the projection method.

## Method

The authors start from a Hamiltonian \(H = H_0 + g H_1\) acting in a Hilbert space of dimension \(N\). Using the Feshbach projection method, they eliminate one basis state at a time, reducing the space to dimension \(N-1\) while requiring that the ground-state eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\) remains unchanged; this condition determines a renormalized coupling constant \(g^{(N-1)}\). The procedure is iterated down to small dimensions, and the Lanczos algorithm is used at each step to obtain the ground state and its energy. The method is applied to frustrated two-leg spin-1/2 ladders with \(L=6\) rungs (12 sites), using both an SU(2) basis and an SO(4) basis, with the coupling \(g = J_t\) (the rung coupling) as the renormalized parameter.

## Key numbers

* For a ladder with \(L=6\) in the SU(2) basis, the ground-state crossing with the first excited state occurs at \(J_t/J_l \simeq 1.23\) (Fig. 2a).
* At \(J_t = 10\) (well above the crossing), the spectrum remains stable down to Hilbert space dimension \(N \leq 100\) from an initial dimension \(N = 924\) (Fig. 2b, 2c).
* At the exact crossing point \(J_t = 5\) in the SU(2) basis, the coupling constant stays constant only over a smaller interval of \(N\) due to numerical instability; in the close neighborhood with \(J_c = 3.8 \neq J_l\), stability extends over a much larger range of \(N\) (Figs. 2e, 2f).
* In the SO(4) basis at the same crossing point \(J_t = 5\), the ground-state energy and \(J_t\) remain stable over the entire reduction procedure (Figs. 3b, 3c), while the first excited state shows stepwise plateaus.
* For \(J_t \ge 6\), the three lowest excited states become degenerate, corresponding to a continuous transition line; this degeneracy is preserved over the whole range of \(N\) in the SO(4) basis (Figs. 4a, 4b).
* At an avoided crossing near \(J_t = 6.6891\), the spectrum and \(J_t\) are stable down to \(N \simeq 200\) (Fig. 5c).

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier development of a Hilbert-space reduction algorithm (Khalil and Richert, 2005) and tests it at phase transition points, complementing established methods such as the Real Space Renormalization Group (Wilson, 1975; White, 1992) and the Density Matrix Renormalization Group (White, 1992; Schollwöck, 2005). The algorithm is applied to frustrated two-leg spin ladders previously studied by Lin et al. (1997) and Gelfand et al. (1998), and the results show that first-order transition points can be identified by the constancy of the renormalized coupling constant. The approach enables the detection of phase transitions in strongly interacting quantum systems without requiring large-scale diagonalization, though avoided crossings remain difficult to locate because the coupling becomes complex at the exceptional point.
