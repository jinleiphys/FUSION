# Diffractive vector meson photo-production in oxygen-oxygen and neon-neon ultraperipheral collisions at energies available at the CERN Large Hadron Collider

> The energy-dependent hotspot model is used to predict cross sections for vector-meson diffractive photo-nuclear production off oxygen ($γ$O) and neon ($γ$Ne) that can be extracted from ultra-peripheral O--O and Ne--Ne collisions, recently recorded at the LHC. In both cases, two models are used to describe the nuclear shapes. Woods-Saxon prescriptions for O and Ne as well as an alpha-cluster description of O and a bowling-pin-like shape for Ne, according to the PGCM formalism. Predictions are presented for the dependence on the centre-of-mass energy of the photon--nucleus system, as well as on Mandelstam-$t$, of the cross sections for the coherent and the incoherent photo-nuclear production of $ρ^{0}$ and J/$ψ$ vector mesons. Furthermore, the rapidity dependence of the ultra-peripheral cross section is reported for all cases. It is found that the incoherent process provides a measurable signature for the approach to the gluon-saturation regime, and that the simultaneous determination of $ρ^{0}$ and J/$ψ$ coherent and incoherent production provides a strong constraint on nuclear models for both O and Ne.

## Key claim

The energy-dependent hotspot model predicts that the incoherent cross section for vector meson photo-production off oxygen and neon provides a measurable signature of the approach to gluon saturation, but only if the nuclear structure model is sufficiently constrained; the simultaneous measurement of coherent and incoherent production of both rho0 and J/psi vector mesons is required to distinguish between competing nuclear models (Woods-Saxon versus alpha-cluster for oxygen, Woods-Saxon versus PGCM bowling-pin-like shape for neon).

## Method

The energy-dependent hotspot model, based on the Good-Walker formalism and the dipole model, was used to compute coherent and incoherent cross sections for diffractive photo-nuclear production of rho0 and J/psi vector mesons off oxygen and neon targets. For each nucleus, two nuclear shape models were employed: a Woods-Saxon three-parameter Fermi distribution and either an alpha-cluster tetrahedron model (for oxygen) or a PGCM bowling-pin-like shape (for neon). The nuclear dipole cross section was constructed from nucleons containing energy-dependent numbers of hotspots, with hotspot positions sampled from the corresponding nuclear thickness profiles, and the UPC cross sections were obtained by folding the photo-nuclear cross sections with the appropriate photon fluxes.

## Key numbers

- Centre-of-mass energy per nucleon pair for O-O and Ne-Ne collisions: 5.36 TeV
- Woods-Saxon parameters for oxygen: radius 2.608 fm, skin depth 0.513 fm, deformation omega = -0.051
- Woods-Saxon parameters for neon: radius 2.791 fm, skin depth 0.698 fm, deformation omega = -0.168
- Alpha-cluster tetrahedron side length for oxygen: 3.42 fm, reproducing measured rms radius of 2.699 fm
- Gaussian smearing width for alpha-cluster vertices: sigma = 0.1 fm
- For oxygen incoherent production at mid-rapidity, the Woods-Saxon model predicts a flat or slowly increasing cross section with energy, while the alpha-cluster model predicts a decreasing cross section for rho0
- For neon, the PGCM model predicts that the incoherent rho0 cross section decreases with increasing energy, while the Woods-Saxon model predicts a larger cross section
- For J/psi in O-O UPCs, the Woods-Saxon model predicts very similar coherent and incoherent cross sections, while the alpha-cluster model predicts they differ by around 40%
- The Mandelstam-t range where measurable differences between nuclear models appear is (0.1-2) GeV^2

## Context

This work extends the energy-dependent hotspot model previously applied to Pb-Pb and Xe-Xe UPCs (Cepila et al., 2016-2023) to the newly available O-O and Ne-Ne collision data recorded at the LHC in July 2025. The predictions are compared with similar calculations by Mantysaari et al. (2023) and Goncalves et al. (2022), finding agreement in shape and magnitude for coherent J/psi production before the first diffractive dip. The results demonstrate that these light-ion collisions serve as a test-bed for future studies at the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) currently under construction, and that multi-differential measurements of the energy dependence at different Mandelstam-t ranges can constrain nuclear structure models while also potentially revealing the saturation-induced decrease of the incoherent cross section at high energies.
