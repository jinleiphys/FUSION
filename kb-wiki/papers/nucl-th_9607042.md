# Hadronic Ratios and the Number of Projectile Participants. Thermal hadron production in Si-Au collisions

> We investigate the dependence of hadronic ratios on the number of projectile participants using a thermal model incorporating exact baryon number and strangeness conservation. A comparison is made with results from $Au-Au$ collisions obtained at the BNL-AGS.

# Hadronic Ratios and the Number of Projectile Participants. Thermal hadron production in Si-Au collisions

## Key claim

The thermal resonance gas model with exact baryon number and strangeness conservation (rather than average conservation via chemical potentials) can reproduce the E866 collaboration's preliminary data on hadronic ratios as a function of projectile participants in Au-Au collisions at BNL-AGS, though the model predicts a steeper rise for the K+/π+ ratio than the experimental data indicate.

## Method

The authors use a thermal resonance gas model at fixed temperature and fixed baryon density, projecting the partition function onto exact values of baryon number B and strangeness S using integral representations that yield modified Bessel functions. Particle densities are computed with exact conservation factors replacing the usual fugacities, and all resonances are allowed to decay into stable particles using branching ratios from the Particle Data Booklet.

## Key numbers

- Temperature: T ≈ 100 MeV
- Baryon density range: B/V ≈ 0.02 – 0.05 fm⁻³
- Baryon chemical potential (grand canonical equivalent): μ_B ≈ 540 MeV
- Baryon number B = 2N_pp, where N_pp is the number of projectile participants, with the factor 2 reflecting the symmetry of the Au-Au collision system

## Context

This work extends the thermal model analyses of Stachel (1995) and Elliott (1995) by implementing exact baryon number and strangeness conservation rather than the grand canonical ensemble approach with chemical potentials, following the formalism of Hagedorn (1971) and subsequent treatments by Hagedorn and Redlich, Greiner, Gorenstein, and Cleymans-Satz-Werner. The results enable systematic comparison of small-system corrections to thermal hadron production, showing that exact conservation effects must be accounted for before considering more dynamical models.
