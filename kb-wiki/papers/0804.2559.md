# Spin degrees and Polarization Observables in Electromagnetic Reactions

> After a brief introduction into the general importance of polarization observables for the analysis of a reaction, the basic density matrix formalism for the description of polarization phenomena is outlined and illustrated by explicit examples for spin one-half and one. Then various electromagnetic processes like photoreactions and electron scattering are discussed in some detail. Finally, the question of how to determine a complete set of polarization observables providing the maximum information on a reaction is considered.

# Spin Degrees and Polarization Observables in Electromagnetic Reactions

## Key claim

A complete set of $2n-1$ properly chosen polarization observables should allow determination of all independent $T$-matrix elements for a given reaction, except for possible discrete ambiguities that require additional observables for resolution; the paper demonstrates this criterion explicitly for deuteron electrodisintegration, where 11 observables suffice to determine 6 independent matrix elements despite the existence of 36 linearly independent observables.

## Method

The paper develops the density matrix formalism for describing polarization phenomena in electromagnetic reactions, treating spin-one-half particles, photons, and massive spin-1 particles using irreducible tensor operators. For the specific case of deuteron electrodisintegration with a longitudinal virtual photon, the author constructs a diagrammatic representation of interference terms between T-matrix elements and identifies minimal sets of observables that form independent connected graphs without closed loops, allowing explicit inversion to extract all matrix elements.

## Key numbers

- For a reaction with parity conservation and N total T-matrix elements, the number of independent matrix elements reduces to n = N/2 when N is even, or n = (N-1)/2 when N is odd.
- For the simplest case of scalar particle absorption on a spin-1/2 target with parity conservation, there are 2 independent complex matrix elements requiring 3 observables for determination (with 4 linearly independent observables available).
- For deuteron electrodisintegration with longitudinal virtual photon, there are 12 T-matrix elements reducing to 6 independent matrix elements under parity conservation, yielding 36 linearly independent observables.
- A complete set for deuteron electrodisintegration comprises 11 observables, with explicit solutions showing 12 observables reduced to 10 by two quadratic constraint equations, plus one additional observable to fix the remaining matrix element.

## Context

This work extends the formalism of Arenhoevel and Leidemann (2000) on polarization observables in deuteron electrodisintegration, providing a systematic criterion for determining whether a chosen set of $2n-1$ observables is complete. The paper builds on the density matrix approach developed by Fano (1957) and Tolhoek (1961) for describing statistical ensembles in quantum mechanics, and applies it to electromagnetic reactions including photoreactions and electron scattering. The results enable future experimental programs to design minimal sets of polarization measurements that extract maximum dynamical information from reactions, and provide theoretical predictions for complete sets of observables over large ranges of energy and momentum transfer.
