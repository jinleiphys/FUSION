# Systematics on ground-state energies of nuclei within the neural networks

> One of the fundamental ground-state properties of nuclei is binding energy. In this study, we have employed artificial neural networks (ANNs) to obtain binding energies based on the data calculated from Hartree-Fock-Bogolibov (HFB) method with the two SLy4 and SKP Skyrme forces. Also, ANNs have been employed to obtain two-neutron and two-proton separation energies of nuclei. Statistical modeling of nuclear data using ANNs has been seen as to be successful in this study. Such a statistical model can be possible tool for searching in systematics of nuclei beyond existing experimental nuclear data.

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that a feed-forward artificial neural network (ANN) can reproduce binding energies, two-neutron separation energies, and two-proton separation energies for Sr, Xe, Er, and Pb isotopes, with the ANN trained on approximately 1300 other isotopes and tested on these four chains that the network had never seen. The claim is hedged by the fact that the ANN is trained on theoretical Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) results rather than experimental data, so the ANN's success is in reproducing the HFB systematics, not necessarily experimental ground-state energies.

## Method
The authors used a feed-forward ANN with a 2-16-16-1 architecture (two input neurons for neutron number N and proton number Z, two hidden layers of 16 neurons each, one output neuron for binding energy or separation energy) and a back-propagation algorithm with Levenberg-Marquardt optimization. The network was trained on binding energies of approximately 1300 even-even isotopes calculated from the HFB method using two Skyrme forces (SLy4 and SKP), then tested on Sr, Xe, Er, and Pb isotopic chains that were excluded from training. The same trained network was then used to predict two-neutron and two-proton separation energies for these four chains.

## Key numbers
- The ANN architecture was 2-16-16-1 with 304 adjustable weights and no bias.
- Minimum mean square error (MSE) values during training ranged between 0.00002 and 0.01; during testing, MSE values ranged between 0.00003 and 0.005.
- The training set consisted of approximately 1300 isotopes, excluding Sr, Xe, Er, and Pb isotopes.
- The ANN successfully reproduced the abrupt decreases in two-neutron separation energies at magic neutron numbers N=50, 82, and 126.

## Context
This work extends the use of artificial neural networks to nuclear mass systematics, following prior applications such as Athanas (2000) for nuclear mass systematics, Costiris (2009) for beta decay half-lives, and Akkoyun (2013) for nuclear charge radii. The study builds on large-scale HFB ground-state calculations by Dobaczewski (2005) using the HFBTHO code with SKP and SLy4 Skyrme parameters. The results suggest that ANNs could serve as a fast statistical tool for predicting nuclear ground-state properties in regions beyond experimental data, though the method is limited by the accuracy of the underlying HFB calculations used for training.
