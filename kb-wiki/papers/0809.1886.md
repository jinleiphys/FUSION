# Volume Effects for Pion Two-Point Functions in Constant Electric and Magnetic Fields

> We compute finite volume effects relevant for lattice QCD simulations using background fields. Focusing on constant electric and magnetic fields on a periodic lattice, we determine volume corrections to pion two-point functions using chiral perturbation theory. Among such corrections are the finite volume shifts to the electric and magnetic polarizabilities, which are numerically shown to be non-negligible. We additionally find that all terms in the single-particle effective action can be renormalized by infrared effects. This includes Born couplings to the pion current and total charge-squared, which can be renormalized due to the nature of gauge invariance on a torus.

## Key claim

Finite volume corrections to pion two-point functions in constant electric and magnetic fields are non-negligible for lattice QCD simulations, and all terms in the single-particle effective action, including Born couplings to the pion current and total charge-squared, can be renormalized by infrared effects due to the nature of gauge invariance on a torus, contrary to what one might expect from infinite-volume reasoning.

## Method

The author used chiral perturbation theory at one-loop order to compute finite volume corrections to charged and neutral pion two-point functions in constant electric and magnetic fields on a spatially periodic torus of side length L with infinite time extent. For the electric field case, a time-dependent gauge potential was employed and the Schwinger proper-time method was used to sum Born couplings to all orders before expanding to second order in the field strength; for the magnetic field case, the external field was treated perturbatively from the outset using coordinate-space perturbation theory. The ultraviolet-regulated sums over periodic momentum modes were expressed in terms of standard finite-volume functions (I, J, and K functions) to isolate the infrared corrections.

## Key numbers

* The finite volume correction to the neutral pion charge-squared coupling is \(\d_L[Q^2_{\pi^0}] = -\frac{1}{2 f^2} m_\pi^4 I_{5/2}(m_\pi)\), where \(f = 130\) MeV is the chiral-limit pion decay constant.
* The finite volume correction to the charged pion charge-squared coupling is \(\d_L[Q^2_{\pi^\pm}] = -\frac{2}{3 f^2} \left[ 2 I_{1/2}(m_\pi) + m_\pi^2 I_{3/2}(m_\pi) \right]\).
* The finite volume correction to the neutral pion electric polarizability is \(\d_L[\alpha^{\pi^0}_E] = \frac{5 \alpha_{f.s.}}{24 f^2 m_\pi} m_\pi^2 \left[ 2 I_{5/2}(m_\pi) + J_{7/2}(m_\pi) \right]\), where \(\alpha_{f.s.} = e^2 / 4\pi\) is the fine-structure constant.
* The finite volume correction to the charged pion electric polarizability is \(\d_L[\alpha^{\pi^\pm}_E] = \frac{\alpha_{f.s.}}{3 f^2 m_\pi} m_\pi^2 I_{5/2}(m_\pi)\).
* The finite volume correction to the neutral pion magnetic polarizability is \(\d_L[\beta_M^{\pi^0}] = -\frac{\alpha_{f.s.}}{24 f^2 m_\pi} m_\pi^2 \left[ 2 I_{5/2}(m_\pi) + 7 K_{9/2}(m_\pi) \right]\).
* The finite volume correction to the charged pion magnetic polarizability is \(\d_L[\beta_M^{\pi^\pm}] = -\frac{5 \alpha_{f.s.}}{12 f^2 m_\pi} m_\pi^4 I_{7/2}(m_\pi)\).
* Numerical estimates show non-negligible finite volume dependence for pion masses \(\lesssim 350\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the finite-volume chiral perturbation theory framework of Gasser and Leutwyler (1987, 1988) to the case of background electromagnetic fields, and is directly relevant to lattice QCD simulations using the classical external field method (e.g., Lee et al. 2005, Detmold et al. 2008, Aubin et al. 2008). The infrared renormalization of charge-squared couplings found here is the coordinate-space analog of the zero-frequency Compton scattering tensor renormalization originally found by Detmold (2006) and further studied by Hu (2007). The results enable lattice practitioners to correct for finite-volume artifacts when extracting pion electric and magnetic polarizabilities from background-field simulations, and demonstrate that as pion masses approach the physical point on fixed-size lattices, isolating infinite-volume physics will become increasingly challenging.
