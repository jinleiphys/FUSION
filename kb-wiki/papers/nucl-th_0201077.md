# Two-Particle Resonant States in a Many-Body Mean Field

> A formalism to evaluate the resonant states produced by two particles moving outside a closed shell core is presented. The two particle states are calculated by using a single particle representation consisting of bound states, Gamow resonances and scattering states in the complex energy plane (Berggren representation). Two representative cases are analysed corresponding to whether the Fermi level is below or above the continuum threshold. It is found that long lived two-body states (including bound states) are mostly determined by either bound single-particle states or by narrow Gamow resonances. However, they can be significantly affected by the continuum part of the spectrum.

## Key claim

The paper presents a formalism for evaluating two-particle resonant states in a many-body mean field using the Berggren representation, showing that long-lived two-body states are mostly determined by bound single-particle states or narrow Gamow resonances, but can be significantly affected by the continuum part of the spectrum. The key hedge is that the method only works for two-particle resonances lying in an "allowed" region of the complex energy plane that is free from zeroth-order poles, and that the physical meaning of complex energies is valid only for isolated, narrow resonances.

## Method

The authors solve the Schrodinger equation with outgoing boundary conditions to obtain Gamow resonances as poles of the S-matrix in the complex energy plane, using the Berggren representation which consists of bound states, Gamow resonances, and scattering states on a contour in the complex energy plane. The two-particle TDA equations are converted into a dispersion relation using separable forces, and the single-particle states are generated using a Woods-Saxon potential with parameters corresponding to the double closed shell nucleus 78Ni. The formalism is applied to two representative cases: one where the Fermi level is below the continuum threshold (using WS1 potential) and one where it is above (using WS2 potential with reduced depth).

## Key numbers

- The Woods-Saxon potential parameters for WS1: V0 = 40 MeV, r0 = 1.27 fm, a = 0.67 fm, Vso = 21.43 MeV
- The N=50 shell gap is about 3.6 MeV between the lowest particle state (1d5/2) and the highest hole state (0g9/2)
- For WS2, the potential depth is reduced to V0 = 37 MeV
- The rectangular contour used for WS1 has vertices: a = 0.5 MeV, b = 9 MeV, c = -4 MeV, d = 20 MeV
- The allowed region for two-particle energies comprises complex energies (Er, Ei) such that 1 MeV < Er < 9 MeV and -4 MeV < Ei < 0 MeV
- Convergence within six digits requires 10 Gaussian points per MeV on most contour segments and 5 points per MeV on the last segment, giving Ng = 225 scattering states
- The ground state of 80Ni is assumed to have an energy gap of 1.4 MeV, giving a ground state energy of -3 MeV
- For WS2, the contour parameters are a = 0.1 MeV, b = 13 MeV, c = -6 MeV, d = 26 MeV, and the strengths G_lambda are 10% larger than in the WS1 case
- For WS2, a bound state appears at G = 0.023 MeV with energy -0.104 MeV

## Context

This work extends the Berggren representation formalism (Berggren, 1968; Liotta et al.) from one-particle to two-particle systems, solving the problem that two-particle basis energies can cover the entire complex energy plane of interest. The method enables shell model calculations in the continuum for nuclei near or beyond the drip line, where the Fermi level may be immersed in the continuum. The authors note that previous work on halo nuclei (Maruhn et al.) and studies using separable forces (Almberger et al., Epelbaum et al.) provided background, but the role of single-particle resonances and the continuum in heavy nuclei was not fully understood. The formalism allows future microscopic description of resonances induced by two-body interactions in the background of a many-body mean field.
