# Exploring laser-driven neutron sources for neutron capture cascades and the production of neutron-rich isotopes

> The production of neutron-rich isotopes and the occurrence of neutron capture cascades via laser-driven (pulsed) neutron sources are investigated theoretically. The considered scenario involves the interaction of a laser-driven neutron beam with a target made of a single type of seed nuclide. We present a comprehensive study over $95$ seed nuclides in the range $3\le Z \le 100$ from $^7_3$Li to $^{255}_{100}$Fm. For each element, the heaviest sufficiently-long-lived (half life $> 1$ h) isotope whose data is available in the recent ENDF-B-VIII.0 neutron sublibrary is considered. We identify interesting seed nuclides with good performance in the production of neutron-rich isotopes where neutron capture cascades may occur. The effects of the neutron number per pulse, the neutron-target interaction size and the number of neutron pulses are also analyzed. Our results show the possibility of observing up to $4$ successive neutron capture events leading to neutron-rich isotopes with $4$ more neutrons than the original seed nuclide. This hints at new experimental possibilities to produce neutron-rich isotopes and simulate neutron capture nucleosynthesis in the laboratory. With several selected interesting seed nuclides in the region of the branching point of the $s$-process ($^{126}_{51}$Sb, $^{176}_{71}$Lu and $^{187}_{75}$Re) or the waiting point of the $r$-process (Lu, Re, Os, Tm, Ir and Au), we expect that laser-driven experiments can shed light on our understanding of nucleosynthesis.

## Key claim

The paper theoretically demonstrates that laser-driven pulsed neutron sources, with sufficiently high neutron fluxes (on the order of $10^{19}$ n/cm$^2$/s per pulse), can produce up to 4 successive neutron capture events in a single-component target, enabling the production of neutron-rich isotopes that are 4 neutrons heavier than the original seed nuclide, provided the neutron source delivers $10^{12}$ neutrons per pulse at a 1 Hz repetition rate over $10^4$ shots.

## Method

The authors developed a one-dimensional model accounting for successive radiative neutron capture, damping of the incident neutron beam, and loss of nuclei by transmutation and radioactive decay. They considered 95 seed nuclides from $^7_3$Li to $^{255}_{100}$Fm, selecting for each element the heaviest isotope with half-life greater than 1 hour available in the ENDF/B-VIII.0 neutron sublibrary. The model was applied to a rectangular target with interacting surface area 25 $\mu$m and thickness 100 $\mu$m, irradiated by neutron beams with average energies between 50 keV and 10 MeV, assuming a Gaussian energy spectrum with 10% relative width.

## Key numbers

- Up to 4 successive neutron capture events can be observed, producing neutron-rich isotopes with 4 more neutrons than the original seed nuclide.
- The scenario assumes $10^{12}$ neutrons per pulse ($10^{12}$ n/pl) at a repetition rate of 1 Hz, over $10^4$ shots.
- The achievable neutron flux from laser-driven sources is approximately $10^{19}$ n/cm$^2$/s, compared to the highest conventional source (European Spallation Source ESS) expected to reach around $10^{18}$ n/cm$^2$/s.
- The capture and loss parameters $\mu_i$ and $\eta_i$ are estimated to be in the range $\lessapprox 10^{-6}$ to $10^{-4}$.
- The study covers seed nuclides with atomic numbers $3 \le Z \le 100$, from $^7_3$Li to $^{255}_{100}$Fm.

## Context

This work extends theoretical studies of neutron capture nucleosynthesis by proposing laser-driven neutron sources as a laboratory tool to simulate astrophysical processes, specifically the s-process branching points and r-process waiting points. The authors identify seed nuclides such as $^{126}_{51}$Sb, $^{176}_{71}$Lu, and $^{187}_{75}$Re (s-process branching points) and Lu, Re, Os, Tm, Ir, and Au (r-process waiting points) as promising candidates. The results suggest that Petawatt-class laser facilities like the Extreme Light Infrastructure (ELI) could enable experiments that produce neutron-rich isotopes (e.g., $^{248}_{95}$Am, $^{258}_{99}$Es, $^{259}_{99}$Es) in regimes not previously accessible in the laboratory, potentially improving understanding of nucleosynthesis pathways.
