# Geant4 hadronic physics status and validation for large HEP detectors

> Optimal exploitation of hadronic final states played a key role in successes of all recent collider experiment in HEP, and the ability to use hadronic final states will continue to be one of the decisive issues during the analysis phase of the LHC experiments. Monte Carlo techniques facilitate the use of hadronic final states, and have been developed for many years. We will give a brief overview of the physics underlying hadronic shower simulation, discussing the three basic types of modeling; data driven, parametrization driven, and theory driven modeling at the example of Geant4. We will confront these different types of modeling with the stringent requirements posed by the LHC experiments on hadronic shower simulation, and report on the current status of the validation effort for large HEP applications. We will address robustness, and CPU and physics performance evaluations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that all three modeling techniques (data driven, parameterization driven, and theory driven) are needed to satisfy LHC experiment requirements optimally, but notes that parameterization driven modeling has proven to allow tuning for test-beam energies and is the most CPU performant option for calorimeter simulation, while theory driven modeling promises safe extrapolation beyond test-beam energies.

## Method

The paper surveys the hadronic physics models implemented in Geant4 as of 2003, categorizing them into data driven models (based on evaluated nuclear data libraries such as ENDF/B-VI, JENDL3.3, and FENDL2.2), parameterized models (rewrites of GEANT3.21/GEISHA models), and theory driven models (parton string models above 5 GeV center-of-mass energy, intra-nuclear transport models below 5 GeV, exciton pre-compound models below 100 MeV, and evaporation models). Validation results from multiple LHC detector test-beam simulations are presented, including ATLAS end-cap calorimetry comparisons.

## Key numbers

- Neutron data driven models are limited to kinetic energies up to 20 MeV, with extensions up to 30 MeV or 150 MeV for some isotopes
- The data driven isotope production model has an upper limit of applicability of 100 MeV nucleon kinetic energy
- Parton string models apply for center-of-mass energies above approximately 5 GeV
- Exciton based pre-compound models are provided for energies below approximately 100 MeV
- A systematic error of 15% was added to isotope production simulation results to account for extrapolation errors in total cross-sections
- The Binary Cascade model was first released for public use beginning in 2003

## Context

This paper extends the GEANT3.21 hadronic simulation framework (described by the GEANT3 collaboration) by implementing a modular, object-oriented approach in Geant4 that allows combining data driven, parameterized, and theory driven models. The work enables LHC experiments (ATLAS, CMS, LHCb, ALICE) to validate hadronic shower simulations against test-beam data, with validation efforts coordinated by the LHC computing grid project's simulation sub-project led by Fabiola Gianotti. The paper reports that Geant4 hadronic physics has become widely used with predictive power as good or better than GEANT3 at its best.
