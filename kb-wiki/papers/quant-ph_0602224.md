# Anomalously Slow Cross Symmetry Phase Relaxation, Thermalized Non-Equilibrated Matter and Quantum Computing Beyond the Quantum Chaos Border

> Thermalization in highly excited quantum many-body system does not necessarily mean a complete memory loss of the way the system was formed. This effect may pave a way for a quantum computing, with a large number of qubits $n\simeq 100$--1000, far beyond the quantum chaos border. One of the manifestations of such a thermalized non-equilibrated matter is revealed by a strong asymmetry around 90$^\circ $ c.m. of evaporating proton yield in the Bi($γ$,p) photonuclear reaction. The effect is described in terms of anomalously slow cross symmetry phase relaxation in highly excited quantum many-body systems with exponentially large Hilbert space dimensions. In the above reaction this phase relaxation is about eight orders of magnitude slower than energy relaxation (thermalization).

## Key claim

Thermalization in a highly excited quantum many-body system does not necessarily erase all memory of how the system was formed; the cross symmetry phase relaxation width β in the Bi(γ,p) photonuclear reaction is about eight orders of magnitude smaller than the spreading width Γ_spr, meaning phase memory can persist far longer than energy relaxation (thermalization).

## Method

The authors analyzed experimental proton angular distributions from the Bi(γ,p) photonuclear reaction induced by 24-MeV bremsstrahlung, for outgoing proton energies ε = 2–8 MeV. They fitted the angular distributions using a statistical-model expression for the double differential cross section that includes S-matrix elements with different total spins and parities, parameterized by the ratio β/Γ_cn (cross symmetry phase relaxation width to compound-nucleus decay width) and three transmission-coefficient ratios (A = T^{L=2}/T^{L=1}, B = T^{l'=1}/T^{l'=0}, C = T^{l'=2}/T^{l'=0}). The fit extracted β/Γ_cn, and Γ_cn was estimated from systematics to obtain β in eV.

## Key numbers

- The fit to the experimental angular distributions gave β/Γ_cn = 0.11.
- The compound-nucleus decay width Γ_cn for Bi at excitation energy 14 MeV was estimated as Γ_cn ≃ 0.1 eV, yielding β ≃ 0.01 eV.
- The spreading width Γ_spr for Bi at 14 MeV excitation is about 2 MeV (from standard nuclear physics estimates) or about 4.5 MeV (from the dipole giant resonance width for Bi).
- The phase relaxation time τ_ph = ħ/β ≃ 6 × 10^{-14} sec, while the thermalization time ħ/Γ_spr ≃ 3 × 10^{-22} sec, making phase relaxation about eight orders of magnitude slower than energy relaxation.
- The average level spacing D of the Bi compound nucleus at 14 MeV excitation is D ≃ 10^{-16} MeV, giving N_eff ≃ Γ_spr/D ≃ 10^{16} and Γ_cn/D ≃ 10^9.
- The Heisenberg time ħ/D ≃ 10^{-5} sec, about eight orders of magnitude longer than τ_ph.
- The nuclear "temperature" extracted from the exponential fit of the scaled angle-integrated spectrum for ε ≤ 8 MeV was T = 0.55 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the concept of "thermalized non-equilibrated matter" introduced by Kun (1994, 1997) and builds on earlier analysis by Flores, Kun, and Seligman (2005) of forward-peaked angular distributions in nucleon-induced reactions, where phase relaxation times were up to five orders of magnitude longer than energy relaxation times. The present photonuclear result shows an even more pronounced effect (eight orders of magnitude), and because the entrance channel is electromagnetic, it more closely resembles loading mechanisms in proposed quantum computers. The finding suggests that quantum computing with n ≃ 100–1000 qubits could operate beyond the "quantum chaos border" described by Georgeot and Shepelyansky (2000, 2001), provided such long-lived phase-coherent states can be engineered.
