# Can Thermalization in Heavy Ion Collisions be Described by QCD Diagrams?

> The onset of thermalization in heavy ion collisions in the weak coupling framework can be viewed as a transition from the initial state Color Glass Condensate dynamics, characterized by the energy density scaling like $ε\sim 1/τ$ with $τ$ the proper time, to the hydrodynamics-driven expansion of the quark-gluon plasma with $ε\sim 1/τ^{4/3}$ (or higher power of $1/τ$ for the boost non-invariant case). We argue that, at any order of the perturbative expansion in the QCD coupling constant, the gluon field generated in an ultrarelativistic heavy ion collision leads to energy density scaling as $ε\sim 1/τ$ for late times $τ\gg 1/Q_s$. Therefore it is likely that thermalization and hydrodynamic description of the gluon system produced in heavy ion collisions can not result from perturbative QCD diagrams at these late times. At earlier times with $τ\sim 1/Q_s$ the subleading corrections to $ε$ in $1/τ$ expansion (terms scaling like $\sim 1/τ^{1+Δ}$ with $Δ>0$) may become important possibly leading to hydrodynamic-like behavior of the gluon system. Still, we show that such corrections do not contribute to the particle production cross section, and are likely to be irrelevant for physical observables. We generalize our results by including massless quarks into the system. Thus, it appears that the apparent thermalization of quarks and gluons, leading to success of Bjorken hydrodynamics in describing heavy ion collisions at RHIC, can only be attributed to the non-perturbative QCD effects.

## Key claim

The paper argues that, at any order of perturbative QCD expansion in the coupling constant, the gluon field generated in an ultrarelativistic heavy ion collision yields an energy density scaling as epsilon ~ 1/tau for late times tau >> 1/Q_s, making it impossible for thermalization and hydrodynamic behavior to result from perturbative QCD diagrams at those late times; the apparent thermalization observed at RHIC can only be attributed to non-perturbative QCD effects.

## Method

The author calculates the energy-momentum tensor of the lowest-order classical gluon field in covariant gauge, then generalizes to arbitrary orders of perturbative QCD by writing the most general boost-invariant form of the energy-momentum tensor and analyzing the tau-dependence of the energy density. The argument relies on the observation that tau-dependent corrections to the gluon field enter only through powers of gluon virtuality k^2 in momentum space, each power giving a factor of 1/tau, so that at late times the leading epsilon ~ 1/tau scaling dominates and subleading corrections (scaling as 1/tau^(1+Delta) with Delta>0) are negligible; the analysis is extended to include rapidity-dependent distributions and massless quarks.

## Key numbers

- The initial-state Color Glass Condensate dynamics gives energy density scaling as epsilon ~ 1/tau, while Bjorken hydrodynamics with ideal gas equation of state gives epsilon ~ 1/tau^(4/3).
- For the lowest-order classical gluon field, the energy density at late times (tau >> 1/<k_T>) is epsilon ~ (1/tau) * dE_T/(d eta d^2 b), with longitudinal pressure p_3 ~ 0.
- The condition for the quasi-classical McLerran-Venugopalan regime requires rapidity Y > ln(1/alpha_s) and Y < 1/alpha_s.
- Hydrodynamic models at RHIC require equilibration at times t <~ 0.5 fm/c.

## Context

This work extends the Color Glass Condensate/saturation formalism (McLerran-Venugopalan model, Kovchegov et al.) and directly challenges the "bottom-up" thermalization scenario of Baier, Mueller, Schiff, and Son (2001), which predicted thermalization via multiple rescattering processes on timescales ~ 1/alpha_s^(13/5) Q_s. The paper also contrasts with arguments by Arnold, Lenaghan, and Moore (2003) about plasma instabilities potentially shortening thermalization times, and with the suggestion by Arnold, Lenaghan, Moore, and Yaffe (2004) that isotropization may occur faster than full thermalization. The conclusion that only non-perturbative QCD effects can explain the early thermalization needed for Bjorken hydrodynamics at RHIC points toward mechanisms such as those discussed by Dunne and Kovchegov (2004).
