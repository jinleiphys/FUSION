# The hottest superfluid and superconductor in the Universe: Discovery and nuclear physics implications

> We present recent work on using astronomical observations of neutron stars to reveal unique insights into nuclear matter that cannot be obtained from laboratories on Earth. First, we discuss our measurement of the rapid cooling of the youngest neutron star in the Galaxy; this provides the first direct evidence for superfluidity and superconductivity in the supra-nuclear core of neutron stars. We show that observations of thermonuclear X-ray bursts on neutron stars can be used to constrain properties of neutron superfluidity and neutrino emission. We describe the implications of rapid neutron star rotation rates on aspects of nuclear and superfluid physics. Finally, we show that entrainment coupling between the neutron superfluid and the nuclear lattice leads to a less mobile crust superfluid; this result puts into question the conventional picture of pulsar glitches as being solely due to the crust superfluid and suggests that the core superfluid also participates.

## Key claim

Observations of the rapid cooling of the Cassiopeia A neutron star provide the first direct evidence for superfluidity and superconductivity in the supra-nuclear core of neutron stars, but this interpretation depends on the assumption that the observed temperature decline is due to passive neutrino cooling rather than other mechanisms.

## Method

The authors analyzed ten years of Chandra X-ray Observatory temperature measurements of the Cassiopeia A neutron star (age approximately 330 years) and compared them to theoretical cooling models with different superfluid states (normal matter, proton superfluid only, and neutron/proton superfluid). They also examined thermonuclear X-ray burst recurrence times in low-mass X-ray binaries to infer core temperatures, analyzed r-mode instability damping mechanisms for rapidly rotating neutron stars, and calculated the effects of entrainment coupling between neutron superfluid and crust lattice on pulsar glitch dynamics using a general relativistic multifluid model.

## Key numbers

- Cassiopeia A neutron star age: approximately 330 years (supernova estimated in 1681 ± 19)
- Steady temperature decline of four percent observed over 10 years of Chandra observations (1999-2010)
- For neutron triplet pairing gap with maximum critical temperature T_nt,max ≤ 8×10^8 K, there can be two thermally stable core temperature values for a single observed accretion luminosity
- Factor of less than 3 difference in inferred core temperature when L_acc ∼ (0.2-9)×10^37 ergs s^-1 and T_nt,max = 4.3×10^8 K
- Crust mode resonance at 600 Hz (typical frequency of first overtone of pure crustal modes)
- For Vela pulsar glitches explained solely by crust superfluid, moment of inertia ratio must satisfy I_n/I ≳ 0.016 × (<m_n*>/m_n) ≈ 0.07, compared to I_n/I ∼ 1% when entrainment is not considered

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of neutron star cooling developed by Tsuruta (1998), Yakovlev and Pethick (2004), Page et al. (2006), and Yakovlev et al. (2011), and provides the first observational confirmation of core superfluidity predicted by Migdal (1959), Haensel et al. (2007), and Lattimer (2012). The entrainment results contradict the conventional picture of pulsar glitches as solely due to crust superfluid (Link et al. 1999) and suggest core superfluid participation, while the r-mode analysis challenges the standard instability window that would otherwise prevent the existence of observed millisecond pulsars like the 716 Hz pulsar discovered by Hessels et al. (2006).
