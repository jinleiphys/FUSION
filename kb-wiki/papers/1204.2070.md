# On general relativistic uniformly rotating white dwarfs

> The properties of uniformly rotating white dwarfs (RWDs) are analyzed within the framework of general relativity. Hartle's formalism is applied to construct the internal and external solutions to the Einstein equations. The WD matter is described by the relativistic Feynman-Metropolis-Teller equation of state which generalizes the Salpeter's one by taking into account the finite size of the nuclei, the Coulomb interactions as well as electroweak equilibrium in a self-consistent relativistic fashion. The mass $M$, radius $R$, angular momentum $J$, eccentricity $ε$, and quadrupole moment $Q$ of RWDs are calculated as a function of the central density $ρ_c$ and rotation angular velocity $Ω$. We construct the region of stability of RWDs ($J$-$M$ plane) taking into account the mass-shedding limit, inverse $β$-decay instability, and the boundary established by the turning-points of constant $J$ sequences which separates stable from secularly unstable configurations. We found the minimum rotation periods $\sim 0.3$, 0.5, 0.7 and 2.2 seconds and maximum masses $\sim 1.500$, 1.474, 1.467, 1.202 $M_\odot$ for $^{4}$He, $^{12}$C, $^{16}$O, and $^{56}$Fe WDs respectively. By using the turning-point method we found that RWDs can indeed be axisymmetrically unstable and we give the range of WD parameters where it occurs. We also construct constant rest-mass evolution tracks of RWDs at fixed chemical composition and show that, by loosing angular momentum, sub-Chandrasekhar RWDs (mass smaller than maximum static one) can experience both spin-up and spin-down epochs depending on their initial mass and rotation period while, super-Chandrasekhar RWDs (mass larger than maximum static one), only spin-up.

## Key claim

Uniformly rotating white dwarfs can reach maximum masses up to ~1.500, 1.474, 1.467, and 1.202 solar masses for ⁴He, ¹²C, ¹⁶O, and ⁵⁶Fe respectively, but these values depend on the specific equation of state used (the relativistic Feynman-Metropolis-Teller EOS) and are subject to stability constraints from mass-shedding, inverse beta-decay, and secular axisymmetric instability boundaries.

## Method

The authors applied Hartle's formalism to solve the Einstein equations up to second order in the angular velocity, constructing internal and external solutions for uniformly rotating white dwarfs. The white dwarf matter was described using the relativistic Feynman-Metropolis-Teller equation of state, which accounts for finite nuclear size, Coulomb interactions, and electroweak equilibrium. Mass, radius, angular momentum, eccentricity, and quadrupole moment were calculated as functions of central density and rotation angular velocity, and stability regions were mapped using the mass-shedding limit, inverse beta-decay instability, and the turning-point method for constant angular momentum sequences.

## Key numbers

- Maximum rotating masses: 1.500 M⊙ (⁴He), 1.474 M⊙ (¹²C), 1.467 M⊙ (¹⁶O), 1.202 M⊙ (⁵⁶Fe)
- Minimum rotation periods: 0.284 s (⁴He), 0.501 s (¹²C), 0.687 s (¹⁶O), 2.195 s (⁵⁶Fe)
- Non-rotating maximum masses from the RFMT EOS: 1.40906 M⊙ (⁴He), 1.38603 M⊙ (¹²C), 1.38024 M⊙ (¹⁶O), 1.10618 M⊙ (⁵⁶Fe)
- Critical densities for inverse beta-decay: 1.39×10¹¹ g/cm³ (⁴He), 3.97×10¹⁰ g/cm³ (¹²C), 1.94×10¹⁰ g/cm³ (¹⁶O), 1.18×10⁹ g/cm³ (⁵⁶Fe)
- Pycnonuclear fusion threshold for ¹²C: ρ_pyc ~ 9.26×10⁹ g/cm³ for 10 Gyr reaction time, ρ_pyc ~ 1.59×10¹⁰ g/cm³ for 0.1 Myr reaction time
- Ratio of rotating to non-rotating maximum mass (k factor): 1.0646 (⁴He), 1.0632 (¹²C), 1.0626 (¹⁶O), 1.0864 (⁵⁶Fe)

## Context

This work extends the non-rotating white dwarf calculations of RotD2011 (based on the RFMT EOS from RotC2011) to uniformly rotating configurations in full general relativity, going beyond earlier Newtonian and post-Newtonian studies by authors such as Chandrasekhar (1931), Salpeter (1961), and Hamada (1961). The only prior general relativistic treatment of rotating white dwarfs was by Arutyunyan (1971) using the Chandrasekhar EOS. The results enable more accurate modeling of super-Chandrasekhar white dwarfs and their evolution tracks, showing that sub-Chandrasekhar rotating white dwarfs can experience both spin-up and spin-down epochs while losing angular momentum, while super-Chandrasekhar ones only spin up.
