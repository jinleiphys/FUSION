# Neutron star masses: dwarfs, giants and neighbors

> We discuss three topics related to the neutron star (NS) mass spectrum. At first we discuss the possibility to form low-mass ($ M \stackrel{<}{\sim} 1 M_{\odot}$) and suggest this is possible only due to fragmentation of rapidly rotating proto-NSs. Such low-mass NSs should have very high spatial velocities which could allow identification. A critical assessment of this scenario is given. Secondly, we discuss mass growth due to accretion for NSs in close binary systems. With the help of numerical population synthesis calculations we derive the mass spectrum of massive ($M > 1.8 M_{\odot}$) NSs. Finally, we discuss the role of the mass spectrum in population studies of young cooling NSs. We formulate a kind of {\it mass constraint} which can be helpful, in our opinion, in discussing different competive models of the thermal evolution of NSs.

## Key claim

Low-mass neutron stars (below about 1 solar mass) can form only through fragmentation of rapidly rotating proto-neutron stars, but this mechanism has internal problems and its realization in nature is "not very promising"; the authors critically assess the scenario rather than endorsing it.

## Method

The authors review the fragmentation mechanism for low-mass neutron stars, perform numerical population synthesis calculations using the "Scenario Machine" code to derive the mass spectrum of massive neutron stars (above 1.8 solar masses) formed via accretion in close binaries, and formulate a "mass constraint" for testing theoretical cooling curves against observations of young isolated neutron stars.

## Key numbers

- Low-mass neutron stars could form with masses about 0.8–1 solar masses from an initial object of 1.2–1.3 solar masses via fragmentation; ejected fragments could have masses about 0.2–0.5 solar masses.
- The maximum mass for a rapidly rotating neutron star is assumed to be 3.45 solar masses, following Ouyed (2004).
- The formation rate of massive neutron stars (above 1.8 solar masses) from the population synthesis is 6.7 × 10⁻⁷ per year, corresponding to about 10,000 such objects in the Galaxy.
- Secondary components of double neutron star systems have masses in the narrow range 1.18–1.39 solar masses, based on nine known systems.
- The pulsar J0751+1807 has mass 2.1 solar masses with uncertainties +0.4 and -0.5 at 95% confidence.
- For 4U 1636-536, a mass estimate of 1.9–2.1 solar masses is given; for Vela X-1 the range is 1.8–2.4 solar masses; for 4U 1700-37 the mass is 2.4 ± 0.27 solar masses.

## Context

This work extends discussions of neutron star mass spectra from earlier studies such as Woosley et al. (2002), Fryer and Kalogera (2001), and Timmes et al. (1996), which found no objects below 1–1.2 solar masses in standard formation models. The fragmentation mechanism was originally suggested by Bethe et al. (1988) and later developed by Imshennik (1992). The population synthesis calculations build on the "Scenario Machine" code described in Lipunov et al. (1996). The mass constraint formulated here provides a criterion for testing competing models of neutron star thermal evolution, particularly relevant for interpreting observations of the Magnificent Seven and other young nearby cooling neutron stars.
