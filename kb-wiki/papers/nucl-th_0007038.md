# Microscopic dynamics of a phase transition: equilibrium vs out-of-equilibrium regime

> We present for the first time to the nuclear physics community the Hamiltonian Mean Field (HMF) model. The model can be solved analytically in the canonical ensemble and shows a second-order phase transition in the thermodynamic limit. Numerical microcanonical simulations show interesting features in the out-of-equilibrium regime: in particular the model has a negative specific heat. The potential relevance for nuclear multifragmentation is discussed.

## Key claim

The Hamiltonian Mean Field (HMF) model, though analytically a second-order phase transition in the thermodynamic limit, can produce a negative specific heat and a backbending caloric curve in microcanonical simulations when the system is not perfectly equilibrated, mimicking signatures often interpreted as evidence of a first-order phase transition.

## Method

The authors performed microcanonical numerical simulations of the HMF model, a system of N fully-coupled classical spins on a unit circle, by integrating the Hamilton equations of motion with a fourth-order symplectic algorithm. They started the system in a far-from-equilibrium "water bag" initial condition (all rotators at angle zero with velocities from a constant distribution) and followed the dynamics for times up to t=2×10⁶, computing temperature, magnetization, kinetic energy fluctuations, and specific heat from time averages. They compared results at short times (t=3000) and long times (t=2×10⁶) against the analytical canonical solution.

## Key numbers

- Critical energy density: U_c = 0.75 (E/N), corresponding to critical temperature T_c = 0.5 (Boltzmann constant k=1)
- System size: N=500 particles, chosen to be of the same order as the typical number of nucleons in nuclear multifragmentation
- Time step: δt = 0.2, with relative energy conservation error smaller than ΔE/E = 10⁻⁵
- For U=0.7 near the critical point, relaxation to equilibrium required times t > 5×10⁵, while for U=0.8 and U=0.4 equilibrium was almost reached by t=3000
- In the out-of-equilibrium regime (t=3000), the specific heat C_V assumed negative values for energies in the range 0.5 < U ≤ U_c

## Context

This work introduces the HMF model, originally developed by Ruffo (1994), to the nuclear physics community as a tool for studying phase transitions in finite systems, analogous to the role of the Ising model in statistical mechanics. The paper extends prior work on nuclear multifragmentation (ALADIN collaboration, INDRA collaboration, EOS collaboration) and addresses the debated question of whether multifragmentation is a first-order or second-order phase transition, as discussed by Gross, Bondorf, Campi, D'Agostino, Moretto, and others. The finding that non-equilibrium effects can produce negative specific heat in a second-order transition directly challenges the interpretation of D'Agostino (2000) that such negative heat capacity is a definitive signature of a first-order phase transition. The model enables future dynamical microcanonical studies of phase transitions in finite systems, with the quasi-stationary states potentially linked to the non-extensive statistical mechanics framework proposed by Tsallis.
