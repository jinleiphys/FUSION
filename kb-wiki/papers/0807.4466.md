# What can we learn from the Interacting Boson Model in the limit of large boson numbers?

> Over the years, studies of collective properties of medium and heavy mass nuclei in the framework of the Interacting Boson Approximation (IBA) model have focused on finite boson numbers, corresponding to valence nucleon pairs in specific nuclei. Attention to large boson numbers has been motivated by the study of shape/phase transitions from one limiting symmetry of IBA to another, which become sharper in the large boson number limit, revealing in parallel regularities previously unnoticed, although they survive to a large extent for finite boson numbers as well. Several of these regularities will be discussed. It will be shown that in all of the three limiting symmetries of the IBA [U(5), SU(3), and O(6)], energies of 0+ states grow linearly with their ordinal number. Furthermore, it will be proved that the narrow transition region separating the symmetry triangle of the IBA into a spherical and a deformed region is described quite well by the degeneracies E(0_2^+)=E(6_1^+), E(0_3^+) =E(10_1^+), E(0_4^+)=E(14_1^+), the energy ratio E(6_1^+) /E(0_2^+) turning out to be a simple, empirical, easy-to-measure effective order parameter, distinguishing between first- and second-order transitions. The energies of 0+ states near the point of the first order shape/phase transition between U(5) and SU(3) will be shown to grow as n(n+3), where n is their ordinal number, in agreement with the rule dictated by the relevant critical point symmetries studied in the framework of special solutions of the Bohr Hamiltonian. The underlying dynamical and quasi-dynamical symmetries are also discussed.

## Key claim
In the large boson number limit of the Interacting Boson Approximation (IBA) model, the energies of 0+ states in all three dynamical symmetries [U(5), SU(3), O(6)] grow linearly with their ordinal number, while near the first-order phase transition between U(5) and SU(3) they follow an n(n+3) rule; these regularities survive to a large extent for finite boson numbers as well.

## Method
The authors performed analytical calculations of 0+ bandhead energies in the SU(3) and O(6) limits of the IBA using the relevant Casimir operators and irreducible representations, and compared these to numerical IBA calculations using the standard two-parameter Hamiltonian (ζ, χ) with the IBAR code for large boson numbers up to N_B = 250. They also compared the IBA results to analytical predictions from critical point symmetries E(5), X(5), Z(5), Z(4), and X(3) derived from special solutions of the Bohr Hamiltonian with infinite square well potentials.

## Key numbers
- In all three IBA dynamical symmetries (U(5), SU(3), O(6)) at large boson number, 0_n+ bandhead energies follow E = A n (linear growth with ordinal number n)
- Near the first-order phase transition between U(5) and SU(3), 0_n+ state energies follow n(n+3), with normalized energies: 0_2+ = 1.0, 0_3+ = 2.48, 0_4+ = 4.62, 0_5+ = 7.13, 0_6+ = 9.85 (from IBA calculation with N_B = 250)
- The degeneracies E(0_2+) = E(6_1+), E(0_3+) = E(10_1+), and E(0_4+) = E(14_1+) characterize the coexistence region in the IBA symmetry triangle
- The ratio E(6_1+)/E(0_2+) serves as an order parameter: for χ = -√7/2 (first-order transition) it shows a sharp maximum just before the critical point, while for χ = 0 (second-order transition) it behaves smoothly
- Experimental data for N=90 isotones (Nd, Sm, Gd, Dy) show a clear maximum in E(6_1+)/E(0_2+) just before N=90, while data around 134Ba show smooth behavior

## Context
This work extends the study of shape/phase transitions in the IBA, originally discussed by Feng (1981) using catastrophe theory, and connects to the critical point symmetries E(5) and X(5) introduced by Iachello (2000, 2001) as special solutions of the Bohr Hamiltonian. The results reveal that the degeneracies E(0_2+)=E(6_1+) and related ones, which are hallmarks of X(5), characterize the coexistence region in the IBA symmetry triangle, and the ratio E(6_1+)/E(0_2+) provides a simple empirical order parameter distinguishing first-order from second-order transitions. The findings enable further investigation into the underlying symmetries of the Alhassid-Whelan arc of regularity, with a recent conjecture by Macek of a partial SU(3) dynamical symmetry underlying this arc.
