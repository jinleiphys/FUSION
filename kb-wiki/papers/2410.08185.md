# Anomalous $U(1)_A$ couplings and the Columbia plot

> When the quark masses are lighter than those in QCD, the standard lore is that a chiral transition of first order must emerge for three, light flavors. Recently, however, numerical simulations on the lattice suggest that the chiral transition is of second order in the chiral limit. Using an extended linear sigma model in the mean field approximation, we study the relation between terms which break the anomalous, $U(1)_A$ symmetry and the order of the chiral phase transition, especially how a chiral transition of second order can arise for three, massless flavors. We note that in an (unphysical) region of the "Columbia" phase diagram, when the strange quark mass is light and negative, corresponding to topological angle $θ=π$, the $CP$ symmetry is spontaneously broken.

## Key claim

Using an extended linear sigma model in the mean-field approximation, the authors show that the order of the chiral phase transition for three massless quark flavors is controlled entirely by the strength of the lowest-order anomalous 't Hooft determinant coupling; a second-order transition in the three-flavor chiral limit is only possible when this coupling vanishes identically, not because the $U(1)_A$ symmetry is restored.

## Method

The authors use an extended linear sigma model (eLSM) with vector and axial-vector mesons, solved at the mean-field level with fermionic one-loop corrections and a Polyakov loop potential. They fit the model parameters to 29 physical quantities including meson masses, decay widths, decay constants, and the pseudocritical temperature, for different combinations of the three anomalous couplings $\xi_1$, $\xi_1^1$, and $\xi_2$ that break $U(1)_A$. They then vary the explicit symmetry breaking parameters $h_N$ and $h_S$ (proportional to light and strange quark masses) at fixed other parameters to map out the Columbia plot for each parameter set.

## Key numbers

- The averaged effective anomaly coupling $\bar{\xi}_\text{eff}$ is 1.507 GeV for fits with only $\xi_1$, 1.589 GeV for only $\xi_2$, and 1.505 GeV for $\xi_1$ and $\xi_2$ together.
- The reduced $\chi^2_\text{red}$ values for the best 100 fits range from 1.98 (for $\xi_1^1$ only) to 2.37 (for all three couplings), with $\xi_1$ only giving 2.09 and $\xi_2$ only giving 2.23.
- The representative parameter sets use $\xi_1 = 1.50$ GeV for the left Columbia plot, $\xi_2 = 278.79$ GeV$^{-2}$ for the middle plot, and $\xi_2 = 1093.00$ GeV$^{-2}$ for the right plot.
- The deconfining critical endpoint for heavy quarks occurs at current quark mass $m_{\rm deconf} \sim 2$ GeV.
- Lattice simulations find no evidence for a first-order transition for pions as light as $m_\pi < 50$ MeV (staggered fermions) or $m_\pi < 100$ MeV (Wilson fermions).

## Context

This work extends the long-standing debate about the order of the chiral phase transition in QCD, originally studied by Pisarski and Wilczek (1983) using $4-\epsilon$ expansions, which predicted a first-order transition for three massless flavors. Recent lattice simulations by Bazavov et al. (2017), Dini et al. (2021), and Kuramashi et al. (2020) found no evidence for a first-order transition, while functional renormalization group studies by Fejos and collaborators (2021, 2024) found a stable infrared fixed point for $N_f \geq 2$ when the anomaly is restored. The present work provides a proof-of-concept for the conjectures of Pisarski (2024) that the first-order region in the Columbia plot can be small or vanishing if higher-order anomalous operators dominate over the 't Hooft determinant. The results enable future studies of how the axial anomaly is realized at finite temperature and how the Columbia plot depends on the microscopic structure of anomalous correlations.
