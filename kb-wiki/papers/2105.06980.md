# A NICER View of the Massive Pulsar PSR J0740+6620 Informed by Radio Timing and XMM-Newton Spectroscopy

> We report on Bayesian estimation of the radius, mass, and hot surface regions of the massive millisecond pulsar PSR J0740$+$6620, conditional on pulse-profile modeling of Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer X-ray Timing Instrument (NICER XTI) event data. We condition on informative pulsar mass, distance, and orbital inclination priors derived from the joint NANOGrav and CHIME/Pulsar wideband radio timing measurements of arXiv:2104.00880. We use XMM European Photon Imaging Camera spectroscopic event data to inform our X-ray likelihood function. The prior support of the pulsar radius is truncated at 16 km to ensure coverage of current dense matter models. We assume conservative priors on instrument calibration uncertainty. We constrain the equatorial radius and mass of PSR J0740$+$6620 to be $12.39_{-0.98}^{+1.30}$ km and $2.072_{-0.066}^{+0.067}$ M$_{\odot}$ respectively, each reported as the posterior credible interval bounded by the 16% and 84% quantiles, conditional on surface hot regions that are non-overlapping spherical caps of fully-ionized hydrogen atmosphere with uniform effective temperature; a posteriori, the temperature is $\log_{10}(T$ [K]$)=5.99_{-0.06}^{+0.05}$ for each hot region. All software for the X-ray modeling framework is open-source and all data, model, and sample information is publicly available, including analysis notebooks and model modules in the Python language. Our marginal likelihood function of mass and equatorial radius is proportional to the marginal joint posterior density of those parameters (within the prior support) and can thus be computed from the posterior samples.

## Key claim
The paper reports Bayesian estimates of the mass and equatorial radius of the massive millisecond pulsar PSR J0740+6620, conditional on the assumption that surface hot regions are non-overlapping spherical caps of fully-ionized hydrogen atmosphere with uniform effective temperature. The radius prior is truncated at 16 km to ensure coverage of current dense matter models, and the results depend on informative priors from radio timing and XMM-Newton spectroscopy.

## Method
The authors performed Bayesian pulse-profile modeling of NICER X-ray Timing Instrument event data, conditioned on informative pulsar mass, distance, and orbital inclination priors derived from joint NANOGrav and CHIME/Pulsar wideband radio timing measurements. They used XMM-Newton European Photon Imaging Camera spectroscopic event data to inform the X-ray likelihood function, and employed the X-ray Pulse Simulation and Inference (XPSI) framework for relativistic ray-tracing including multiple imaging effects. The analysis assumed surface hot regions as non-overlapping spherical caps of fully-ionized hydrogen atmosphere with uniform effective temperature, with conservative priors on instrument calibration uncertainty.

## Key numbers
- Equatorial radius: 12.39 km with credible interval -0.98/+1.30 km (16% and 84% quantiles)
- Mass: 2.072 solar masses with credible interval -0.066/+0.067 solar masses (16% and 84% quantiles)
- Surface hot region temperature: log10(T [K]) = 5.99 with credible interval -0.06/+0.05 for each hot region
- NICER on-source exposure time: 1,602,683.761 seconds
- XMM-Newton effective exposure times: 6.81 ks (pn), 17.96 ks (MOS1), and 18.7 ks (MOS2)
- Prior support for radius truncated at 16 km
- Neutral hydrogen column density prior: uniform from 0 to 10^21 cm^-2

## Context
This work extends the pulse-profile modeling methodology previously applied to PSR J0030+0451 by Riley (2019) and Miller (2019), which delivered mass and radius constraints at the ~10% level for an isolated pulsar. The analysis of PSR J0740+6620, the highest well-constrained mass neutron star, enables stronger constraints on the dense matter equation of state when combined with the mass measurement from radio timing by Fonseca (2020). The results enable follow-on equation of state inference carried out in a companion paper by Raaijmakers (2021), and can be combined with constraints from gravitational wave observations and nuclear physics experiments.
