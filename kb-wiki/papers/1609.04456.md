# Extraction of the proton charge radius from experiments

> Static properties of hadrons such as their radii and other moments of the electric and magnetic distributions can only be extracted using theoretical methods and not directly measured from experiments. As a result, discrepancies between the extracted values from different precision measurements can exist. The proton charge radius, $r_p$, which is either extracted from electron proton elastic scattering data or from hydrogen atom spectroscopy seems to be no exception. The value $r_p = 0.84087(39)$ fm extracted from muonic hydrogen spectroscopy is about 4% smaller than that obtained from electron proton scattering or standard hydrogen spectroscopy. The resolution of this so called proton radius puzzle has been attempted in many different ways over the past six years. The present article reviews these attempts with a focus on the methods of extracting the radius.

## Key claim

The proton charge radius is an extracted quantity, not directly measured, and the 4% discrepancy between the muonic hydrogen value (0.84087(39) fm) and the electron-proton scattering or standard hydrogen spectroscopy values may be resolved by incorporating relativistic corrections (including Lorentz boosts) and careful treatment of finite-size corrections in the extraction methods, rather than by new physics.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical methods used to extract the proton charge radius from two experimental approaches: (1) electron-proton elastic scattering, where the radius is obtained from the slope of the electric form factor \(G_E^p(q^2)\) at \(q^2=0\) using the relation \(\langle r_p^2 \rangle = -6/G_E^p(0) \cdot dG_E^p(q^2)/dq^2|_{q^2=0}\); and (2) atomic spectroscopy (both electronic and muonic hydrogen), where the radius is fitted by comparing precisely measured transition energies with theoretical calculations that include quantum electrodynamics corrections and finite-size corrections (FSC) such as the third Zemach moment. The review examines specific theoretical approaches including perturbative and nonperturbative treatments of FSC, the Breit equation with form factors, and reanalyses of scattering data using different form factor parametrizations (dipole, Taylor expansion, and fluctuating proton size models).

## Key numbers

- Muonic hydrogen Lamb shift measurement: \(\Delta E = E_{2P_{3/2}}^{f=2} - E_{2S_{1/2}}^{f=1} = 206.2949(32)\) meV, leading to extracted proton radius \(r_p = 0.84087(39)\) fm (about 4% smaller than the CODATA value of 0.8768(69) fm)
- Alternative extraction using Breit equation with form factors gave \(r_p = 0.83594(46)\) fm from the expression \(\Delta E = 209.16073 + 0.1174 r_p - 4.2585 r_p^2 + 0.0203 r_p^3\) meV
- Nonperturbative Dirac equation calculation gave \(\Delta E = 209.9505 - 5.2345 r_p^2 + 0.0361 r_p^3\) meV, yielding a radius within errors of the muonic hydrogen value 0.84184(67) fm
- Electronic hydrogen spectroscopy: \(f_{1S-2S} = 2466061413187.34(84)\) kHz and \(f_{2S_{1/2} - 8D_{5/2}} = 770649561581.1(5.9)\) kHz, with Rydberg constant \(R_{\infty} = 10973731.568539(55)\) m\(^{-1}\)
- Fluctuating proton size model gave \(r_p = 0.864\) fm before relativistic corrections, reducing to 0.844 fm after applying the Lorentz boost

## Context

This review extends the ongoing effort to resolve the "proton radius puzzle" first highlighted by the muonic hydrogen measurement of Pohl et al. (2010) and later reinforced by Antognini et al. (2013). It systematically compares the theoretical extraction methods used in both scattering and spectroscopy, including the perturbative Friar approach (Friar, 1979), the Breit equation method of the authors themselves (Kelkar et al., 2015), and nonperturbative checks by Miller (2013). The review also discusses reanalyses of scattering data by Horbatsch and Higinbotham (2013) and the fluctuating proton model by Terry and collaborators (2015). The work enables a clearer understanding of which theoretical uncertainties (relativistic corrections, frame dependence, finite-size treatment) must be resolved before planned experiments such as MUSE and PRad can definitively settle the puzzle.
