# Solution of few-body problems with the stochastic variational method: I. Central forces

> This paper presents a fortran program to solve diverse few-body problems with the stochastic variational method. Depending on the available computational resources the program is applicable for $N=2-3-4-5-6-...$-body systems with $L=0$ total orbital momentum. The solution with the stochastic variational method is ``automatic'' and universal. One defines the system (number of particles, masses, symmetry, interaction, etc. ) and the program finds the ground state energy and wave function. The examples include nuclear (alpha particle: four-body, $^6$He: six-body), atomic (td$μ^-$ and $e^+e^-e^+e^-$) and subnuclear (the nucleon and the delta in a nonrelativistic quark model) systems. The solutions are accurate for excited states as well, and even the Efimov-states can be studied. The program is available from the author (e-mail:varga@rikaxp.riken.go.jp).

## Key claim

The stochastic variational method (SVM) provides an "automatic" and universal solution for few-body problems with central forces, but the accuracy and applicability depend on available computational resources and the method is limited to systems with total orbital angular momentum \(L=0\).

## Method

The authors implemented a Fortran program that solves the many-body Schrödinger equation by expanding the wave function in a correlated Gaussian basis and optimizing the nonlinear parameters through a random trial-and-error procedure. The basis is built incrementally by generating random candidate parameters, solving the generalized eigenvalue problem for each candidate, and selecting the parameter set that yields the lowest ground state energy. The program handles antisymmetrization for fermions and bosons and computes matrix elements analytically for several potential forms (Coulomb, Gaussian, Yukawa) or numerically for arbitrary central potentials.

## Key numbers

- \(td\mu^{-}\) molecule ground state energy: first four digits reproduced on a basis size of \(K=50\); fifth digit reached after three refinement cycles (atomic units).
- Positronium molecule (\(e^+e^-e^+e^{-}\)): results on \(K=50\) basis after refinement cycles are very close to those of other calculations using \(K=300\) correlated Gaussians.
- Alpha particle ground state energy calculated with the Malfliet-Tjon V interaction (MTV); results compared to other calculations in Table VI.
- \(^{6}\)He ground state energy calculated with the central, spin-isospin dependent Minnesota potential using a predefined basis.
- Nucleon and delta masses in a nonrelativistic quark model: three-quark problem solved with one-gluon exchange potential using quark mass 337 MeV; basis size required is "very small."
- Efimov states in a three-boson system: first three bound states found with a predefined basis of \(n=20\) and parameters \(a_0=0.1\), \(x_0=2.4\); ground state energy \(-4.81\) (units not specified); ground state root mean square radius about 1.5 fm; first excited state radius about 6000 fm; basis extends to \(a_0 x_0^n = 0.1 \times 2.4^9 = 1674990\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the rigorous few-body techniques for \(N=3,4\) systems (e.g., Faddeev equations, Green's function Monte Carlo by Carlson (1987), and the work of Glöckle and Kamada (1993)) to larger systems of strongly correlating particles, up to \(N=6\) or more depending on computational resources. The SVM approach enables accurate solutions for nuclear systems (alpha particle, \(^{6}\)He), Coulombic systems (\(td\mu^{-}\), positronium molecule), and subnuclear systems (nonrelativistic quark model of baryons), and it can also study Efimov states with extreme spatial extension. The method enables future applications to mesoscopic systems (few ions in a trap, few electrons in a quantum dot) and can be extended to other baryons with suitable modifications of the symmetrization.
