# Robust extrapolation using physics-related activation functions in neural networks for nuclear masses

> Given the importance of nuclear mass predictions, numerous models have been developed to extrapolate the measured data into unknown regions. While neural networks -- the core of modern artificial intelligence -- have been recently suggested as powerful methods, showcasing high predictive power in the measured region, their ability to extrapolate remains questionable. This limitation stems from their `black box' nature and large number of parameters entangled with nonlinear functions designed in the context of computer science. In this study, we demonstrate that replacing such nonlinear functions with physics-related functions significantly improves extrapolation performance and provides enhanced understanding of the model mechanism. Using only the information about neutron (N) and proton (Z) numbers without any existing global mass models or knowledge of magic numbers, we developed a highly accurate model that covers light nuclei (N, Z > 0) up to the drip lines. The extrapolation performance was rigorously evaluated using the outermost nuclei in the measurement landscape, and only the data in the inner region was used for training. We present details of the method and model, along with opportunities for future improvements.

# Robust extrapolation using physics-related activation functions in neural networks for nuclear masses

## Key claim
Replacing conventional computer-science activation functions (e.g., ReLU) with physics-related activation functions (PAF) in a neural network significantly improves extrapolation performance for nuclear mass predictions, even when using only neutron (N) and proton (Z) numbers as inputs and without any existing global mass models or knowledge of magic numbers. The improvement is demonstrated under a rigorous evaluation where only inner-region data are used for training and the outermost nuclei in the measurement landscape are reserved for testing.

## Method
A feed-forward neural network was constructed using activation functions drawn from physics, including multiplication, division, logarithm, sine, and functions of N and Z such as N^x and Z^x (Table 1). Sparse learning with L0 regularization was applied to reduce over-parametrization, and two additional penalty terms were introduced during training: a bound penalty to keep predictions in the extrapolation region within a physically reasonable range (mass excess between -0.1 and 2 u), and a soft Garvey-Kelson (GK) relation penalty to guide predictions without forcing exact satisfaction. Sixteen models were trained as a deep ensemble to estimate uncertainty.

## Key numbers
- PAF network interpolation RMS: 207 keV (N,Z >= 8) / 255 keV (N,Z > 0)
- PAF network extrapolation RMS: 308 keV (N,Z >= 8) / 396 keV (N,Z > 0)
- PAF network total RMS: 258 keV (N,Z >= 8) / 328 keV (N,Z > 0)
- DenseNet (conventional DNN) extrapolation RMS: 1048 keV (N,Z >= 8) / 1173 keV (N,Z > 0)
- ConvNet extrapolation RMS: 531 keV (N,Z >= 8) / 658 keV (N,Z > 0)
- Duflo-Zuker total RMS: 428 keV (N,Z >= 8)
- WS4 total RMS: 295 keV (N,Z >= 8)
- FRDM total RMS: 606 keV (N,Z >= 8)
- Bound penalty range: mass excess [-0.1, 2] u, with B1=0.95 u, B2=1.05 u
- GK penalty deviation range: [-0.5, 0.5] u, with G=0.5 u
- Training data: 1,736 of 2,548 total AME2020 data points; 435 for interpolation evaluation; 377 for extrapolation evaluation

## Context
This work extends the equation learning approach of Martius (2016) and Sahoo (2018), who replaced activation functions with scientific functions and used sparse learning, but it departs from their goal of obtaining a simple analytical equation. Instead, the authors show that a moderate level of sparsity combined with physics-related activation functions yields accurate extrapolation without requiring a simple formula. The method contradicts the common finding that conventional deep neural networks (e.g., DenseNet, ConvNet) fail to extrapolate nuclear masses beyond the training region, as shown by Kim (2024) and others. The PAF network successfully predicts both proton and neutron drip lines and shows inflection points at magic numbers in the outputs of certain activation functions, suggesting interpretability. Future work could explore novel activation functions, alternative penalty formulations, and application to other network structures such as convolutional neural networks or Transformers.
