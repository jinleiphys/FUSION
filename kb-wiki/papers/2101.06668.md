# Unruh effect and information entropy approach

> Total entropy generated by the Unruh effect is calculated within the framework of information theory. In contrast to previous studies, here the calculations are done for the finite time of existence of the non-inertial reference frame. In this case only the finite number of particles is produced. Dependence on mass of the emitted particles is taken into account. Analytic expression for the entropy of radiated boson and fermion spectra is derived. We study also its asymptotics corresponding to limiting cases of low and high acceleration. The obtained results can be further generalized to other intrinsic degrees of freedom of the emitted particles, such as spin and electric charge.

## Key claim

The total entropy generated by the Unruh effect is calculated using information theory for a finite time of existence of the non-inertial reference frame, which implies a finite number of emitted particles; the authors assume that the energy spectrum of emitted particles follows an exponential (Schwinger-like) form derived from the structure of the density matrix, rather than from a full dynamical calculation.

## Method

The authors start from the Schmidt-decomposed vacuum state for bosons and fermions in a 1+1-dimensional Rindler spacetime, tracing over the inaccessible (inside-horizon) modes to obtain the density matrix for outgoing radiation. They treat the particle number cutoff N as finite (reflecting finite observation time) and introduce an exponential probability density function p(E) for the energy of emitted quanta. Using Shannon entropy for the joint distribution over particle number n and energy E, they derive an analytic expression for the total Unruh entropy and study its asymptotic limits for low and high acceleration (temperature).

## Key numbers

- Unruh temperature is given by T = a/(2π) in Planck units (c = G = ħ = kB = 1).
- For fermions, the maximum occupation number is N = 2; for bosons, N can take any positive integer value (examples shown with N = 100 and N = 1000).
- In the low-temperature limit (T → 0), the leading-order entropy is H(n,E|N,T) ≈ −⟨ln(dE/T)⟩_{E_p} + 1 + (1/4)(1 + 2m/T) exp(−m/T), with the entropy reaching a maximum at m/T ≈ 0.5.
- In the high-temperature limit (T → ∞), the entropy expansion is H(n,E|N,T) = −⟨ln dE⟩_{E_p} + 1 + ln(M − m) + ln N − [(N²+2)(M²+m²) + (N²−7)Mm]/(72T²) + O(1/T³).
- The entropy maximum at small m/T increases with rising M/T ratio and becomes more pronounced as N increases.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the Unruh effect (Unruh, 1976) by considering finite observation time and finite particle number, in contrast to the usual assumption of everlasting acceleration. It builds on the information-theoretic approach rather than the algebraic approach of Bisognano and Wichmann (1975) or the path-integral method of Unruh and Weiss (1984). The results can be applied to multiparticle production in ultrarelativistic hadronic and heavy-ion collisions, where the Unruh effect has been invoked as a thermalization mechanism by Bialas and Czyz (1978), Gribov and Shuryak (1979), and others; the finite-time treatment enables more realistic particle emission spectra.
