# Properties of Skyrme force as a residual interaction in beyond mean-field theories

> In an effort to find an effective interaction which can consistently be used for both the mean-field part and the residual part in beyond mean-field theories, properties of the Skyrme interactions as a residual interaction are investigated. The time-dependent density-matrix theory (TDDM) is used as a beyond mean-field theory and the ground states of $^{16}$O and $^{40}$Ca are calculated using the five standard parametrizations of the Skyrme interaction which differs in density and momentum dependence. It is found that the Skyrme interaction which has strong density dependence and weak momentum dependence induces substantial ground-state correlations comparable to the results of other theoretical calculations.

## Key claim

The Skyrme parametrization SVI, which has the strongest density dependence and weakest momentum dependence among the five standard sets tested, induces ground-state correlations in 16O and 40Ca that are comparable to shell-model and other theoretical calculations, suggesting it is a candidate for consistent use in both mean-field and beyond mean-field channels, but only a few percent reduction of its parameters is needed to reproduce the original Hartree-Fock ground-state energy.

## Method

The time-dependent density-matrix theory (TDDM) was used to calculate correlated ground states of 16O and 40Ca by solving coupled equations for the one-body density matrix and the correlated part of the two-body density matrix. The three-body t3 term of the Skyrme force was reduced to an effective density-dependent two-body interaction that respects exchange properties, and the adiabatic method (with T = 2400 fm/c) was used for 16O while a gradient method was used for 40Ca. Five standard Skyrme parametrizations (SII, SIII, SIV, SV, SVI) were tested, using only single-particle states around the Fermi level: the 1p3/2, 1p1/2, and 1d5/2 states for 16O, and the 2s1/2, 1d3/2, 1d5/2, and 1f7/2 states for 40Ca.

## Key numbers

- For 16O with SVI: proton occupation probabilities are 0.920 (1p3/2), 0.846 (1p1/2), and 0.104 (1d5/2); correlation energy is -28.0 MeV
- For 16O with SVI: total energy decrease from ground-state correlations is 10.5 MeV, which is 8.3% of the total Hartree-Fock energy
- For 16O with SVI in exact diagonalization approach: correlation energy is -27.2 MeV (in parentheses)
- For 16O with SIII: proton occupation probabilities are 0.961 (1p3/2), 0.932 (1p1/2), and 0.048 (1d5/2); correlation energy is -14.1 MeV
- For 16O with SV: proton occupation probabilities are 0.985 (1p3/2), 0.978 (1p1/2), and 0.018 (1d5/2); correlation energy is -7.1 MeV
- For 40Ca with SVI: proton occupation probabilities are 0.936 (1d5/2), 0.765 (1d3/2), 0.944 (2s1/2), and 0.179 (1f7/2); correlation energy is -56.5 MeV
- For 40Ca with SVI: total energy decrease from ground-state correlations is 23.9 MeV, which is 7.0% of the ground state energy in Hartree-Fock
- A 2.8% reduction of SVI parameters (t0, t1, t2, t3) for 16O gives the original Hartree-Fock ground-state energy; for 40Ca a 2.1% reduction is needed
- Shell-model calculations (Utsuno) for 16O give occupation probabilities of 0.920 (1p3/2), 0.820 (1p1/2), and 0.071 (1d5/2)

## Context

This work extends the consistent TDDM calculations of Barton et al. (2021), who used Skyrme parametrizations without density dependence (SV and SHZ2) and found small ground-state correlations, by analyzing why some Skyrme forces induce weak correlations and identifying SVI as a candidate for consistent use. The results for SVI are comparable to shell-model calculations (Utsuno) and perturbation calculations (Adachi), and the study enables future consistent TDDM simulations for low-energy phenomena where a limited number of single-particle states around the Fermi level are involved. The finding that SIII only induces moderate correlations when using the properly exchange-respecting density-dependent interaction (Equation t32) contradicts earlier variational shell-model work (Otsuka) that used the simpler density-dependent form (Equation t31) and found strong correlations.
