# Inclusive electron scattering in the quasielastic region with Korea-IBS-Daegu-SKKU density functional

> With the framework of KIDS (Korea-IBS-Daegu-SKKU) density functional model, the isoscalar and isovector effective masses of nucleon and the effect of symmetry energy in nuclear medium are investigated in inclusive $(e,e')$ reaction in quasielastic region. The effective masses are varied in the range $(0.7 \sim 1.0)M$ with free nucleon mass $M$, and the symmetry energy is varied within the uncertainty allowed by nuclear data and neutron star observation. The wave functions of nucleons inside target nucleus are generated by solving Hartree-Fock equation with adjusting equation of state, binding energy and radius of various stable nuclei, and effective mass of nucleon in the KIDS model. With the obtained wave functions, we calculate the differential cross section for the inclusive $(e,e')$ reaction and compare the theoretical results with Bates, Saclay, and SLAC experimental data. Our model describes experimental data better at SLAC-type high incident electron energy than those measured from Bates and Saclay. The influence of the effective mass and symmetry energy appears to be precise on the longitudinal cross section.

# Inclusive electron scattering in the quasielastic region with Korea-IBS-Daegu-SKKU density functional

## Key claim
The KIDS density functional model describes inclusive (e,e') cross sections better at SLAC-type high incident electron energy (2.02 GeV) than at lower energies from Bates and Saclay experiments, and the isoscalar effective mass in the range (0.9–1.0)M is favored over smaller values, but the symmetry energy dependence is weak (at most 5% difference) and appears primarily in the longitudinal cross section.

## Method
The authors solved the Hartree-Fock equation with the KIDS density functional to generate bound-state nucleon wave functions for target nuclei (12C, 40Ca, 56Fe, 197Au, 208Pb), adjusting the equation of state, binding energy, radius, and effective mass. They then calculated inclusive (e,e') differential cross sections in the plane wave Born approximation using relativistic single-particle wave functions from the Dirac equation with scalar and vector potentials derived from the non-relativistic potentials, ensuring gauge invariance by using identical potentials for initial and final states. The theoretical results were compared with experimental data from Bates, Saclay, and SLAC for incident electron energies up to 2.02 GeV.

## Key numbers
- Effective mass values varied in the range (0.7–1.0)M, where M is the free nucleon mass; isoscalar effective masses of 0.70M (KIDS0-m*77 and SLy4), 0.90M (KIDS0-m*99), 0.99M (KIDS0), 1.01M (KIDS-A), and 0.98M (KIDS-C) were used.
- For SLAC kinematics at incident electron energy 2.02 GeV and scattering angle 15°, the four-momentum transfer squared around the peak was Q² = 0.25 (GeV/c)².
- For Bates data on 40Ca, Q² around the peak was 0.22 (GeV/c)² at 330 MeV and 0.19 (GeV/c)² at 628 and 375 MeV.
- For Saclay data on 208Pb, Q² around the peak was 0.19 (GeV/c)² at 310 MeV and 0.17 (GeV/c)² at 485 and 354 MeV.
- Difference between KIDS0, KIDS-A, and KIDS-C models (varying symmetry energy) was at most 5% around the peak (ω ~ 150 MeV).
- Compression modulus K0 values: 240 MeV (KIDS0), 230 MeV (KIDS-A), 250 MeV (KIDS-C).
- Symmetry energy slope parameter L: 49 MeV (KIDS0), 66 MeV (KIDS-A), 58 MeV (KIDS-C).

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous study of exclusive (e,e'p) reactions (Gil et al., 2021) to inclusive (e,e') scattering, which probes all nucleons in a nucleus rather than only those near the Fermi surface. The KIDS model allows independent control of the equation of state, bulk nuclear properties, and effective mass, unlike Skyrme force models surveyed by Dutra et al. (2012) where effective mass varies from 0.58M to 1.12M. The results show that inclusive scattering favors larger isoscalar effective masses (0.9–1.0)M over smaller ones (0.7–0.8)M, consistent with the exclusive reaction findings, while the symmetry energy dependence is weak. The work enables future constraints on the density dependence of symmetry energy through precise measurements of the longitudinal cross section.
