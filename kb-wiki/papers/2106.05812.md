# Field excitation in fuzzy dark matter near a strong gravitational wave source

> The axion-like particles with ultralight mass ($\sim10^{-22}$eV) can be a possible candidate of dark matter, known as the fuzzy dark matter (FDM). These particles form Bose-Einstein condensate in the early Universe which can explain the dark matter density distribution in galaxies at the present time. We study the time evolution of ultralight axion-like field in the near region of a strong gravitational wave (GW) source, such as binary black hole merger. We show that GWs can lead to the generation of field excitations in a spherical shell about the source that eventually propagate out of the shell to minimize the energy density of the field configuration. These excitations are generated toward the end of the merger and in some cases even in the ringdown phase of the merger, therefore it can provide a qualitatively distinct prediction for changes in the GW waveform due to the presence of FDM. This would be helpful in investigating the existence of FDM in galaxies.

## Key claim

Gravitational waves from binary black hole mergers can generate field excitations in fuzzy dark matter via parametric resonance, producing a spherical shell of excited field around the source. These excitations arise toward the end of the merger and in some cases even in the ringdown phase, which may provide a qualitatively distinct prediction for changes in the gravitational waveform that could help investigate the existence of fuzzy dark matter in galaxies.

## Method

The authors numerically solved the Klein-Gordon equation for an ultralight axion-like field (mass ~10^-22 eV) minimally coupled to an oscillating spacetime metric, using a two-dimensional torus geometry with periodic boundary conditions. For the continuous gravitational wave case, they used a monochromatic waveform with frequency 250 Hz and strain amplitude ~0.019-0.026. For the binary black hole merger case, they constructed a phenomenological time-varying gravitational waveform modeled after GW150914 parameters, with strain amplitude and frequency increasing until merger and decaying during ringdown.

## Key numbers

- Axion-like field mass taken as m = 10^-22 eV
- Gravitational wave frequency for continuous case: f_GW = 250 Hz, giving ω' = 1.05 × 10^10 in dimensionless units
- Strain amplitude at distance r_0 = 677 km from source: ε = 0.019 (+0.007/-0.009), estimated from GW150914 parameters (h_e = 1.0 × 10^-21, r_e = 410 (+160/-180) Mpc)
- Lattice spacing Δx = Δy = 12 km, time step Δt = 2 × 10^-5 s, with N = 200 lattice points per direction (total area 5.76 × 10^6 km^2)
- For continuous GWs, resonance growth of specific momentum modes (k_x ≈ ω/2, k_y ≈ 0, corresponding to ~0.5 peV) began at t ≈ 0.9 s, with full excitation achieved at t ≈ 1.7 s for β = π/10 and t ≈ 2.6 s for β = π/200
- Total energy density of the field achieved roughly two orders of magnitude growth from initial value under sustained spacetime oscillations
- For the BBH merger waveform, two parameter sets were used: Set-I (t_0 = 0.425 s, h_d = 300.0 s^-1) and Set-II (t_0 = 0.43 s, h_d = 500.0 s^-1), with ringdown phase lasting longer for Set-I
- The resonance condition for the continuous case follows k_x^2 + k_y^2 + m^2 ≈ (nω/2)^2, which for ω >> m reduces to k_x^2 + k_y^2 ≈ (nω/2)^2

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Dave and Digal, 2021) on parametric resonance of scalar fields under spacetime oscillations, applying it specifically to fuzzy dark matter near gravitational wave sources. It contrasts with superradiance-based mechanisms studied by Brito et al. (2015) and others, which require rotating black holes or neutron stars and produce bosonic clouds through different physical processes. The results provide a possible explanation for the suppression in strain amplitude and frequency of gravitational waves in the ringdown phase observed by Sukanta (2020), suggesting that energy transfer from gravitational waves to the field could cause such suppression. This opens a new possibility for indirect detection of fuzzy dark matter by studying discrepancies between expected and measured gravitational waveforms, particularly toward the end of merger and in the ringdown phase.
