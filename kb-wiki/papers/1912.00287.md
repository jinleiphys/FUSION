# Nuclear Fission Dynamics: Past, Present, Needs, and Future

> Recent developments in theoretical modeling and in computational power have allowed us to make significant progress on a goal not achieved yet in nuclear theory: a fully microscopic theory of nuclear fission. The complete microscopic description remains a computationally demanding task, but the information that can be provided by current calculations can be extremely useful to guide and constrain phenomenological approaches. First, a truly microscopic framework that can describe the real-time dynamics of the fissioning system can justify or rule out assumptions and approximations incompatible with an accurate quantum treatment or with our understanding of the inter nucleon interactions. Second, the microscopic approach can be used to obtain trends such as: the excitation energy sharing mechanism between fission fragments (FFs) with increasing excitation energy of the fissioning system, the angular momentum content of the FFs, or even to compute observables that cannot be otherwise calculated in phenomenological approaches or even measured, as in the case of astronomical environments. Merely the characterization of the trends would be of great importance for various application. We present here arguments that a truly microscopic approach to fission does not support the assumption of adiabaticity of the large amplitude collective motion in fission, particularly starting from the outer saddle down to the scission configuration.

# Nuclear Fission Dynamics: Past, Present, Needs, and Future

## Key claim

The adiabatic assumption for large amplitude collective motion in fission, particularly from the outer saddle down to the scission configuration, is strongly violated in fully microscopic time-dependent superfluid local density approximation (TDSLDA) simulations; the collective motion is instead strongly overdamped, with the collective flow energy being almost negligible until scission, contradicting the widely used assumption that the nucleus follows the lowest potential energy surface.

## Method

The authors performed fully unrestricted TDSLDA simulations of nuclear fission, solving the time-dependent Bogoliubov-de Gennes equations for quasiparticle wave functions using several nuclear energy density functionals (NEDFs) including SLy4, SLy4*, SkM*, and SeaLL1. The collective kinetic energy was separated from the intrinsic energy using a hydrodynamic decomposition of the total energy density functional, allowing direct evaluation of the degree of adiabaticity during the saddle-to-scission evolution. The simulations tracked the evolution of quadrupole and octupole moments, pairing fields, and excitation energy sharing between fission fragments without imposing any restrictions on the collective degrees of freedom.

## Key numbers

- Collective speed from saddle-to-scission: v_coll/c ≈ 0.002 to 0.004, significantly smaller than the Fermi velocity v_F/c ≈ 0.25
- Saddle-to-scission time: 1 to 3 × 10^3 fm/c for realistic pairing, and O(10^4) fm/c for SLy4 NEDF
- Time to cross an avoided level crossing: Δt ≪ ℏ/Δε ≈ 400 fm/c, where Δε = 1/ρ_sp(ε_F) is the average single-particle energy level spacing at the Fermi level
- For transitions between potential energy surfaces separated by O(10) keV, the required crossing time for adiabaticity would be ≈ 20,000 fm/c or longer
- When pairing strength was artificially increased by a factor of 3 to 4 times the realistic value, the saddle-to-scission evolution time decreased by a factor of approximately 10

## Context

This work extends and provides microscopic justification for the overdamped Brownian motion model of fission (Weidenmuller 1984, Randrup 2011-2013) and partially supports the scission-point model (Wilkins 1972, 1976; Lemaitre 2015, 2019), while contradicting the adiabatic assumption underlying the generator coordinate method (GCM) introduced by Hill and Wheeler (1953) and Griffin (1957), as well as the adiabatic time-dependent Hartree-Fock (ATDHF) method (Baranger 1972, 1978; Villars 1978). The results demonstrate that the collective potential energy surface and inertia tensor, essential elements of many simplified microscopic approaches, become irrelevant for the saddle-to-scission dynamics. The findings enable future microscopic calculations of fission fragment properties including excitation energy sharing, angular momentum distributions, and observables relevant for r-process nucleosynthesis that cannot be directly measured.
