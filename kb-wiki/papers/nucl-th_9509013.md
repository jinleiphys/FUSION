# Causality Violations in Cascade Models of Nuclear Collisions

> Transport models have successfully described many aspects of intermediate energy heavy-ion collision dynamics. As the energies increase in these models to the ultrarelativistic regime, Lorentz covariance and causality are not strictly respected. The standard argument is that such effects are not important to final results; but they have not been seriously considered at high energies. We point out how and why these happen, how serious of a problem they may be and suggest ways of reducing or eliminating the undesirable effects.

## Key claim

Cascade models of nuclear collisions at ultrarelativistic energies violate causality and Lorentz covariance because they use non-retarded interactions with discrete timesteps, leading to superluminous information transport that can approach velocities of $\sqrt{\sigma/\pi}/\Delta t_{\text{step}}$, where $\sigma$ is the parton-parton cross section and $\Delta t_{\text{step}}$ is the simulation timestep. The authors argue that these violations are not negligible at high energies, contrary to the standard argument that such effects do not significantly affect final results.

## Method

The authors performed 3+1 dimensional parton cascade simulations of 100 GeV/nucleon proton-gold collisions using fully relativistic kinematics, with quarks treated as massive particles and both quarks and gluons allowed to be off-shell. Initial conditions were determined from standard parton distribution functions $f(x,Q^2)$ with a minimum $x$-value and $Q^2$ as model parameters, and only QCD processes with two incoming and two outgoing partons were implemented with phenomenological screening masses to avoid divergent cross sections. The authors compared several methods for reducing causality violations: blocking superluminous collisions, suppressing low-energy collisions below 0.4 GeV c.m.-energy, introducing a simple time delay of $|\Delta r_{\text{lab}}|/(2c)$, downscaling cross sections by a factor of 1/5 while increasing particle number by a factor of 5, and using a wee-parton initialization that smears the nuclear pancake to approximately 1 fm thickness.

## Key numbers

- Maximum transverse signal velocity can reach $\sqrt{\sigma/\pi}/\Delta t_{\text{step}}$, where $\Delta t_{\text{step}}$ is of order a few thousandths of fm/$c$
- For $\sigma = 0.5$ fm$^2$, information travels outwards proportional to simulation time (ballistic regime); for $\sigma = 0.2$ fm$^2$, causality violations are "still rather moderate"
- The gluon-gluon cross section cutoff was chosen at $s \approx 0.25$ GeV$^2$, corresponding to $\sigma \le 0.45$ fm$^2$
- Timestep length used in simulations: 0.0002 fm/$c$
- Approximately 9000 partons generated per testrun with chosen $Q^2$ and minimum $x$-value
- Isolated scattering events observed with signal velocities from 50$c$ up to 1360$c$
- After initial fast expansion, the outer boundary of information spreading travels at approximately 0.17$c$
- Maximum distance of a scattering event from the beam axis: 2.5 fm
- With velocity restriction, collision rate drops to 49% of original in early stages and to 8% in later stages
- Downscaling cross sections by factor $n=5$ reduces maximum signal velocity by factor $\approx 0.45$
- Lorentz-contracted nucleon thickness: approximately $8 \times 10^{-3}$ fm; gold nucleus thickness: $6 \times 10^{-2}$ fm
- Wee-parton initialization reduces parton density by approximately a factor of 20

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical transport theory approach of Greiner, Geiger, and collaborators to ultrarelativistic energies, where the authors identify fundamental causality and Lorentz covariance problems that were not seriously considered at lower energies. The paper builds on earlier internuclear cascade calculations by Kodama (showing frame-dependence of collision numbers and time-ordering) and the stochastic collision method of Lang and Danielewicz (which avoids time-ordering problems but does not solve superluminous shock-waves). The findings enable future development of retarded interaction frameworks and suggest that wee-parton initialization and cross-section downscaling are promising directions for reducing causality violations in parton cascade simulations.
