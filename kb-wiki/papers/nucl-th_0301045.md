# Neutrino Opacities in Neutron Stars with Kaon Condensates

> The neutrino mean free paths in hot neutron-star matter are obtained in the presence of kaon condensates. The kaon-induced neutrino absorption process, which is allowed only in the presence of kaon condensates, is considered for both nondegenerate and degenerate neutrinos. The neutrino mean free path due to this process is compared with that for the neutrino-nucleon scattering. While the mean free path for the kaon-induced neutrino absorption process is shown to be shorter than the ordinary two-nucleon absorption process by several orders of magnitude when temperature is not very high, the neutrino-nucleon scattering process has still a dominant contribution to the neutrino opacity. Thus, the kaon-induced neutrino absorption process has a minor effect on the thermal and dynamical evolution of protoneutron stars.

## Key claim
The kaon-induced neutrino absorption process (KA) in neutron-star matter with kaon condensates yields a mean free path shorter than the ordinary two-nucleon absorption process by several orders of magnitude at low temperatures, but the neutrino-nucleon scattering process (S) still dominates the total neutrino opacity, making KA a minor contributor to protoneutron star thermal and dynamical evolution.

## Context
This work extends the study of kaon condensation in neutron stars by Muto, Yasuhira, Tatsumi, and Iwamoto, building on the finite-temperature equation of state developed by Yasuhira and Tatsumi (2001) and the delayed collapse scenario proposed by Baumgarte et al. (1996). The KA process is related to the kaon-induced Urca process previously identified as a rapid cooling mechanism for kaon-condensed neutron stars by Brown (1988) and Tatsumi (1988). The results enable more accurate modeling of neutrino transport during protoneutron star deleptonization and cooling, particularly for assessing whether kaon condensation significantly alters the thermal evolution timescale compared to the normal-phase neutrino-nucleon scattering and two-nucleon absorption processes studied by Sawyer and Soni (1979) and Friman and Maxwell (1979).
