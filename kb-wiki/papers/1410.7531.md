# Structure of hypernuclei in relativistic approaches

> We review the relativistic mean-field approach to various aspects of hypernuclei. It was Brockmann and Weise who first applied this approach to hypernuclei. At that time, it had been already observed experimentally that the spin-orbit splittings in hypernuclei are significantly smaller than that in ordinary nuclei. The relativistic approach was suitable for a discussion of spin-orbit splittings in hypernuclei, as the spin-orbit interaction is naturally emerged with the relativistic framework. Since this seminal work, many developments have been done and the relativistic approach has been applied both to the ground state and to excited states of hypernuclei. We summarize those developments in the Chapter. We shall concentrate on $Λ$-hypernuclei and do not discuss $Σ$ hypernuclei, since the theoretical treatment is rather similar to each other.

## Key claim

The relativistic mean-field approach, first applied to hypernuclei by Brockmann and Weise, is particularly suitable for explaining the experimentally observed small spin-orbit splittings in hypernuclei because the spin-orbit interaction emerges naturally within the relativistic framework, and the tensor coupling between the \(\Lambda\) hyperon and the \(\omega\) meson field largely cancels the Thomas precession term.

## Method

The authors review both the meson-exchange and point-coupling versions of the relativistic mean-field (RMF) model for \(\Lambda\)-hypernuclei. In the meson-exchange version, nucleons and hyperons interact via exchange of \(\sigma\), \(\omega\), and \(\rho\) mesons, with the \(\Lambda\) hyperon coupling only to the isoscalar \(\sigma\) and \(\omega\) fields plus a tensor coupling term. In the point-coupling version, zero-range contact interactions replace meson exchange, and the Dirac equations for nucleons and the \(\Lambda\) hyperon are solved self-consistently using an equal-filling approximation (except in studies of magnetic moments). The total binding energy includes RMF energy, center-of-mass correction, and pairing energy for open-shell cores.

## Key numbers

* The spin-orbit splitting between the \(1p_{1/2}\) and \(1p_{3/2}\) hyperon states in \(^{13}_{\Lambda}\)C was determined experimentally to be \(152\pm54 \pm36\) keV, which is smaller than the spin-orbit splitting in ordinary nuclei by a factor of 20-30.
* The empirical depth parameter of a Woods-Saxon potential fitted to \(\Lambda\) binding energies is about \(V_0 = 30\) MeV, approximately 2/3 of the depth parameter for nucleons.
* In the point-coupling RMF model with the PCY-S4 parameter set, the overall fit to experimental \(\Lambda\) binding energies is good except for a few deformed hypernuclei.
* For \(^{17}_{\Lambda}\)O with the \(\Lambda\) in the \(1s_{1/2}\) state, the RMF magnetic moment is \(-0.660\) n.m. (Schmidt value: \(-0.613\) n.m.); with tensor coupling included, it becomes \(-0.611\) n.m., close to the Schmidt value.
* For \(^{41}_{\Lambda}\)Ca with the \(\Lambda\) in the \(1s_{1/2}\) state, the RMF magnetic moment is \(-0.682\) n.m. (Schmidt: \(-0.613\) n.m.); with tensor coupling, it becomes \(-0.611\) n.m.
* Time-odd fields in triaxial RMF calculations separate single-particle energies of time-reversal partner states by up to 100 keV.

## Context

This review extends the seminal work of Brockmann and Weise (1977), who first applied the relativistic approach to hypernuclei, and the subsequent demonstration by Noble (1980) of the importance of tensor coupling for spin-orbit splittings. It summarizes developments in ground-state properties (binding energies, spin-orbit splittings, magnetic moments, deformation, neutron drip-line extension) and excited states, building on earlier non-relativistic Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations (Rayet, 1976; 1981) and cluster model studies (Motoba, 1985). The work enables future applications to \(M1\) transition strengths in \(\Lambda\) hypernuclei and to multi-\(\Lambda\) hypernuclei and neutron stars.
