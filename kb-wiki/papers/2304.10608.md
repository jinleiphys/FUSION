# Executive Summary of the Topical Program: Nuclear Isomers in the Era of FRIB

> We report on the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB) Theory Alliance topical program "Nuclear Isomers in the Era of FRIB". We outline the many ways isomers influence and contribute to nuclear science and technology, especially in the four FRIB pillars: properties of rare isotopes, nuclear astrophysics, fundamental symmetries, and applications for the nation and society. We conclude with a resolution stating our recommendation that the nuclear physics community actively pursue isomer research. A white paper is forthcoming.

# Executive Summary of the Topical Program: Nuclear Isomers in the Era of FRIB

## Key claim

The nuclear physics community should actively pursue isomer research at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB) because isomers influence all four FRIB science pillars (properties of rare isotopes, nuclear astrophysics, fundamental symmetries, and applications), though the paper presents this as a recommendation rather than a demonstrated result, with a full white paper forthcoming.

## Method

The authors organized and report on a topical program workshop held at FRIB, Michigan State University, May 9-20, 2022, with 32 participants and 27 formal presentations from institutions in the US, UK, Germany, and China. The workshop covered past and present experimental and theoretical efforts to study and characterize nuclear isomers, with discussions organized around the four FRIB science pillars.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear isomers are defined as metastable states with half-lives t1/2 >= 10^-9 s, compared to typical nuclear excited states with t1/2 ~ 10^-12 s
- The longest-lived known isomer is 180mTa with t1/2 > 10^16 y, while the 180Ta ground state has an 8.15-hour half-life
- Isomer excitation energies range from a few eV (229mTh) to over 10 MeV (208Pb, 152Er)
- The 229Th isomer is a prime candidate for a nuclear clock with 1-2 orders of magnitude greater precision than the best atomic clocks

## Context

This executive summary reports on a 2022 topical program workshop that extends prior isomer research including the work of Walker (1999) on energy traps, Misch (2020) on astromers, and Campbell (2012) on nuclear clocks. The paper outlines how the new generation of radioactive ion beam facilities, particularly FRIB, create opportunities for isomer discovery and characterization across nuclear structure, astrophysics, fundamental symmetries, and applications including nuclear medicine (99mTc for imaging), nuclear batteries, and gamma-ray lasers. The authors recommend that facilities enable and the community prioritize isomer research through both targeted experiments and experiments of opportunity.
