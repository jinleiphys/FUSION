# Supernovae as the Site of the r-Process: Implications for Gamma-Ray Astronomy

> We discuss how detection of gamma-ray emission from the decay of r-process nuclei can improve our understanding of r-process nucleosynthesis. We find that a gamma-ray detector with a sensitivity of 10**(-7)/cm**2/s at 100-700 keV may detect the emission from the decay of Sb125, Cs137, Ce144, Eu155, and Os194 produced in a future Galactic supernova. In addition, such a detector may detect the emission from the decay of Sn126 in the Vela supernova remnant and the diffuse emission from the decay of Sn126 produced by past supernovae in our Galaxy. The required detector sensitivity is similar to what is projected for the proposed Advanced Telescope for High Energy Nuclear Astrophysics (ATHENA). Both the detection of gamma-ray emission from the decay of several r-process nuclei (e.g., Sb125 and Os194) produced in future Galactic supernovae and the detection of emission from the decay of Sn126 in the Vela supernova remnant would prove that supernovae are a site of the r-process. Furthermore, the former detection would allow us to determine whether or not the r-process nuclei are produced in relative proportions specified by the solar r-process abundance pattern in supernova r-process events. Finally, detection of diffuse emission from the decay of Sn126 in our Galaxy would eliminate neutron star/neutron star mergers as the main source for the r-process nuclei near mass number A=126.

# Supernovae as the Site of the r-Process: Implications for Gamma-Ray Astronomy

## Key claim

If a gamma-ray detector with sensitivity of approximately 10^(-7) gamma rays per square centimeter per second in the 100-700 keV energy range can be developed, detection of emission from specific r-process nuclei (such as Sb-125 and Os-194) in a future Galactic supernova, or from Sn-126 in the Vela supernova remnant, would prove that supernovae are a site of the r-process. The claim is conditional on achieving this detector sensitivity, similar to that projected for the proposed ATHENA mission, and on the assumption that the supernova occurs within approximately 10 kpc.

## Method

The authors estimated the average mass of radioactive r-process nuclei produced per supernova by scaling from solar r-process abundances, the total mass and age of the Galaxy, and the Galactic supernova frequency. They then calculated expected gamma-ray fluxes from the decay of five relatively short-lived r-process nuclei (Sb-125, Cs-137, Ce-144, Eu-155, Os-194) for a future supernova at 10 kpc, from the long-lived nucleus Sn-126 in the Vela supernova remnant at 200 pc, and from diffuse Sn-126 decay emission in the Galactic center direction, using nuclear decay parameters from the Table of Isotopes.

## Key numbers

- Required detector sensitivity: approximately 10^(-7) gamma rays per square centimeter per second at energies 100-700 keV
- Expected flux from Sb-125 decay (428 keV line) from a supernova at 10 kpc: 4.8 x 10^(-7) gamma rays per square centimeter per second
- Expected flux from Os-194 decay (328 keV line) from a supernova at 10 kpc: 1.6 x 10^(-7) gamma rays per square centimeter per second
- Expected flux from Ce-144 decay (134 keV line) from a supernova at 10 kpc: 2.9 x 10^(-7) gamma rays per square centimeter per second
- Expected flux from Sn-126 decay (666 keV line) in the Vela supernova remnant at 200 pc: 2.2 x 10^(-7) gamma rays per square centimeter per second
- Expected diffuse flux from Sn-126 decay (666 keV line) in the Galactic center direction: 5.0 x 10^(-7) gamma rays per square centimeter per second per radian
- Lifetime of Sn-126: 1.44 x 10^5 years
- Lifetime of Cs-137: 43.4 years
- Lifetime of Ce-144: 1.12 years
- Distance to Vela pulsar: approximately 125-500 pc
- Age of Vela pulsar: approximately 10^4 years
- Estimated Galactic supernova frequency: approximately one every 30 years
- Estimated Galactic NS-NS merger frequency: approximately one every 10^6 years

## Context

This work extends the earlier gamma-ray signature predictions of Meyer and Howard (1991), who estimated fluxes from r-process nuclei in SN 1987A, and builds on the foundational r-process theory of Burbidge et al. (1957) and Cameron (1957). The paper addresses the long-standing mystery of the r-process site by proposing specific observational tests using gamma-ray astronomy. Detection of diffuse Sn-126 emission would eliminate neutron-star-neutron-star mergers as the main source for r-process nuclei near mass number 126, while detection from a future supernova or the Vela remnant would confirm supernovae as an r-process site. The authors also connect their predictions to a scenario with two distinct supernova source frequencies (one every 30 years and one every 300 years) proposed in their earlier work (Qian, Vogel, and Wasserburg 1998) to explain meteoritic data on I-129/I-127 and Hf-182/Hf-180 ratios from Wasserburg, Busso, and Gallino (1996).
