# Photon and neutron production as in-situ diagnostics of proton-boron fusion

> Short-pulse, ultra high-intensity lasers have opened new regimes for studying fusion plasmas and creating novel ultra-short ion beams and neutron sources. Diagnosing the plasma in these experiments is important for optimizing the fusion yield but difficult due to the picosecond time scales, 10s of micron-cubed volumes and high densities. We propose to use the yields of photons and neutrons produced by parallel reactions involving the same reactants to diagnose the plasma conditions and predict the yields of specific reactions of interest. In this work, we focus on verifying the yield of the high-interest aneutronic proton-boron fusion reaction $^{11}{B}(p,2α){}^4{He}$, which is difficult to measure directly due to the short stopping range of the produced $α$s in most materials. We identify promising photon-producing reactions for this purpose and compute the ratios of the photon yield to the $α$ yield as a function of plasma parameters. In beam fusion experiments, the ${}^{11}{C}$ yield is an easily-measurable observable to verify the $α$ yield. In light of our results, improving and extending measurements of the cross sections for these parallel reactions are important steps to gaining greater control over these laser-driven fusion plasmas.

## Key claim

The authors propose using yields of photons and neutrons from parallel nuclear reactions as in-situ diagnostics to infer the yield of the aneutronic proton-boron fusion reaction ¹¹B(p,2α)⁴He, which is difficult to measure directly because the produced alpha particles have short stopping ranges in most materials; the yield ratio method eliminates normalization unknowns such as local reactant density, effective reaction volume, and time.

## Method

The authors computed yield ratios of candidate photon- and neutron-producing reactions to the alpha yield from ¹¹B(p,2α)⁴He as functions of plasma parameters, using Maxwellian ion distributions to model the directly-irradiated plasma and beam-target kinematics for the fast-ion population. They evaluated cross sections from the EXFOR and JANIS databases for primary reactions (proton-initiated on ¹¹B, ¹⁰B, and ¹⁴N) and secondary reactions (alpha-initiated on the same nuclei), identifying promising diagnostic reactions based on exothermicity, definite product energy, and measurable yields. For beam-target experiments, they identified radioactive nuclide yields (particularly ¹¹C) as easily measurable proxies for the alpha yield.

## Key numbers

* The ¹¹B(p,2α)⁴He cross section reaches approximately 0.8 b at 0.6 MeV center-of-mass energy, significantly higher than DD or DT fusion reactions due to the higher charge of boron.
* The ¹¹B(p,γ)¹²C reaction has a peak cross section of 3×10⁻⁶ b at 14 MeV center-of-mass energy, with Q = 15.9 MeV.
* The ¹⁰B(p,γ)¹¹C reaction has a peak cross section of 0.0003 b at 6 MeV center-of-mass energy, with Q = 8.69 MeV.
* The ¹⁰B(α,n)¹³N reaction has a peak cross section of 0.1 b at 4.6 MeV center-of-mass energy, with Q values of 1 and 3.51 MeV.
* The ¹¹C nuclide has a half-life of 1.22×10³ s (beta-plus decay), making it measurable in beam-target experiments.
* The ¹³N nuclide has a half-life of 598 s (beta-plus decay).
* The ¹⁸F nuclide has a half-life of 6.59×10³ s (beta-plus decay).

## Context

This work extends the use of yield ratios from inertial-confinement fusion diagnostics (Frenje 2020) to short-pulse laser-driven proton-boron fusion experiments, where direct alpha measurement is problematic due to short stopping ranges. The authors previously used the ratio between alpha and ¹¹C yields to determine the ¹¹B(p,2α)⁴He reaction yield more accurately in beam-target experiments (McCary 2022), and here they demonstrate its utility for direct irradiation experiments. The results identify ¹¹B(p,γ)¹²C and ¹⁰B(p,γ)¹¹C as the most promising photon-producing diagnostics for direct irradiation experiments, and confirm ¹¹C as the best proxy in beam-target experiments, while calling for improved cross section measurements of these parallel reactions to enable greater control over laser-driven fusion plasmas.
