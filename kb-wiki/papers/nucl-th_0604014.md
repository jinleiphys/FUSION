# Dissipative hydrodynamics in 2+1 dimension

> In 2+1 dimension, we have simulated the hydrodynamic evolution of QGP fluid with dissipation due to shear viscosity. Comparison of evolution of ideal and viscous fluid, both initialised under the same conditions e.g. same equilibration time, energy density and velocity profile, reveal that the dissipative fluid evolves slowly, cooling at a slower rate. Cooling get still slower for higher viscosity. The fluid velocities on the otherhand evolve faster in a dissipative fluid than in an ideal fluid. The transverse expansion is also enhanced in dissipative evolution. For the same decoupling temperature, freeze-out surface for a dissipative fluid is more extended than an ideal fluid. Dissipation produces entropy as a result of which particle production is increased. Particle production is increased due to (i) extension of the freeze-out surface and (ii) change of the equilibrium distribution function to a non-equilibrium one, the last effect being prominent at large transverse momentum. Compared to ideal fluid, transverse momentum distribution of pion production is considerably enhanced. Enhancement is more at high $p_T$ than at low $p_T$. Pion production also increases with viscosity, larger the viscosity, more is the pion production. Dissipation also modifies the elliptic flow. Elliptic flow is reduced in viscous dynamics. Also, contrary to ideal dynamics where elliptic flow continues to increase with transverse momentum, in viscous dynamics, elliptic flow tends to saturate at large transverse momentum. The analysis suggest that initial conditions of the hot, dense matter produced in Au+Au collisions at RHIC, as extracted from ideal fluid analysis can be changed significantly if the QGP fluid is viscous.

# Dissipative hydrodynamics in 2+1 dimension

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that including shear viscosity in 2+1 dimensional hydrodynamic simulations of quark-gluon plasma (QGP) significantly alters the evolution compared to ideal fluid dynamics, but the author notes that these first-order (acausal) results should be considered less reliable than second-order results, which will be presented in a later publication.

## Method

The author solved the energy-momentum conservation equations for a baryon-free QGP fluid in 2+1 dimensions with longitudinal boost-invariance, using the first-order (Eckart/Landau-Lifshitz) dissipative hydrodynamic formalism with only shear viscosity included. The SHASTA-FCT algorithm was used to evolve the fluid from initial conditions at τ_i=0.6 fm, with the shear stress tensor components calculated from spatial gradients using the ideal equations of motion to avoid time-derivative terms. Particle spectra were computed via the Cooper-Frye freeze-out prescription with a non-equilibrium correction to the distribution function proportional to the shear stress tensor.

## Key numbers

- Initial equilibration time: τ_i = 0.6 fm
- Initial central energy density: 25 GeV/fm^3
- Initial temperature: 350 MeV
- Critical temperature for phase transition: T_c = 164 MeV
- Bag pressure: B^{1/4} = 230 MeV
- Two values of shear viscosity to entropy ratio used: η/s = 0.08 (ADS/CFT estimate) and η/s = 0.135 (perturbative estimate)
- Freeze-out temperature: T_F = 0.158 GeV
- Impact parameter for Au+Au collisions: b = 6.8 fm (corresponding to 16-24% centrality)
- At p_T = 3 GeV, pion production increased by a factor of 3 for η/s = 0.08 and a factor of 5 for η/s = 0.135
- Non-equilibrium correction to particle production becomes comparable to equilibrium contribution beyond p_T = 5 GeV for η/s = 0.08 and beyond p_T = 4.5 GeV for η/s = 0.135
- Sound attenuation length at freeze-out: Γ_s ≈ 0.2 fm (compared to 1.4 fm in blast wave models)

## Context

This work extends the ideal hydrodynamic simulations of QGP at RHIC, which successfully explained Au+Au collision data using the EOS-Q equation of state (QGP3, 2005), by incorporating shear viscosity to address deviations from ideal behavior observed in peripheral collisions and at forward rapidity (Heinz, 2004). The author developed the AZHYDRO-KOLKATA code building on the publicly available AZHYDRO code, solving the first-order dissipative equations in 2+1 dimensions, whereas prior viscous hydrodynamic work was limited to 1+1 dimensions (Muronga, 2004; Chaudhuri, 2005) or used blast wave models (Teaney, 2004). The results enable future extraction of initial conditions for viscous QGP from experimental data and provide a baseline for comparison with second-order (Israel-Stewart) dissipative hydrodynamics, which the author notes are more reliable due to the causality problems inherent in first-order theories.
