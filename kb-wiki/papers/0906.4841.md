# Towards Quantum Transport for Nuclear Reactions

> Nonequilibrium Green's functions represent a promising tool for describing central nuclear reactions. Even at the single-particle level, though, the Green's functions contain more information that computers may handle in the foreseeable future. In this study, we investigate whether all the information contained in the Green's functions is necessarily relevant when describing the time evolution of nuclear reactions. For this, we carry out mean-field calculations of slab collisions in one dimension.

## Key claim

The authors investigate whether the full eight-dimensional information contained in nonequilibrium Green's functions is necessary for describing the time evolution of nuclear reactions, finding that only a limited range of arguments in the density matrix is practically important for forward-time evolution, with the scale set by the inverse of Fermi momentum.

## Method

The authors performed mean-field (time-dependent Hartree-Fock) calculations of nuclear slab collisions in one spatial dimension, using the density matrix formalism with a Skyrme interaction. They evolved the density matrix using a split-operator method with fast Fourier transforms, and tested the importance of off-diagonal elements by applying a strong imaginary superoperator potential that suppresses matrix elements away from the diagonal axis at various cutoff distances.

## Key numbers

- For adiabatic preparation of the initial state, values of the switching parameter tau greater than or approximately equal to 20 fm/c lead to a very good approximation of the mean-field ground state.
- At incident center-of-mass energies between approximately 0.6 MeV/nucleon and 15 MeV/nucleon, slabs pass through each other, become excited, and may break up.
- At center-of-mass energies above approximately 15 MeV/nucleon, the system breaks into a multitude of fragments (multifragmentation).
- When matrix elements are suppressed from |x - x'| = 5 fm onward, some changes in the density compared to the standard evolution begin to emerge at late times.
- No perceptible difference in the evolution of density was found when elements had been eliminated at distances |x - x'| greater than or approximately equal to 10 fm, even when fragments crossed periodic boundaries and collided again.
- Fusion occurs at incident center-of-mass energy of 0.1 MeV/nucleon in the absence of Coulomb interactions.

## Context

This work extends the nonequilibrium Green's function approach to nuclear reactions, which has been used primarily for derivations in nuclear physics and was previously solved directly for uniform interpenetrating nuclear matter by Danielewicz (1984). The study addresses the computational challenge that the Green's functions method involves handling a vast amount of information that would overwhelm computing systems, as noted by the authors. The results suggest that simulating central nuclear reactions in three dimensions using nonequilibrium Green's functions may be feasible, and the adiabatic preparation method for initial states could enable consistent treatment of nuclear structure and reactions.
