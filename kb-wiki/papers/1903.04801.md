# Chiral phase transition temperature in (2+1)-Flavor QCD

> We present a lattice QCD based determination of the chiral phase transition temperature in QCD with two degenerate, massless quarks and a physical strange quark mass. We propose and calculate two novel estimators for the chiral transition temperature for several values of the light quark masses, corresponding to Goldstone pion masses in the range of $58~{\rm MeV}\lesssim m_π\lesssim 163~{\rm MeV}$. The chiral phase transition temperature is determined by extrapolating to vanishing pion mass using universal scaling analysis. Finite volume effects are controlled by extrapolating to the thermodynamic limit using spatial lattice extents in the range of $2.8$-$4.5$ times the inverse of the pion mass. Continuum extrapolations are carried out by using three different values of the lattice cut-off, corresponding to lattices with temporal extent $N_τ=6,\ 8$ and $12$. After thermodynamic, continuum and chiral extrapolations we find the chiral phase transition temperature $T_c^0=132^{+3}_{-6}$ MeV.

## Key claim

The chiral phase transition temperature in (2+1)-flavor QCD with two massless light quarks and a physical strange quark mass is determined to be 132 MeV with asymmetric error bars (+3, -6) MeV, which is about 25 MeV lower than the pseudocritical crossover temperature at physical quark masses. The result depends on the assumption that the transition is second-order and controlled by 3-D O(4) universality class scaling, and the analysis finds no evidence for a nonzero critical light-to-strange quark mass ratio.

## Method

The authors performed lattice QCD calculations using the Highly Improved Staggered Quark (HISQ) action with Symanzik improved gluon action, varying the light quark mass ratio H = m_l/m_s from 1/160 to 1/20 (corresponding to Goldstone pion masses 58-163 MeV) on lattices with temporal extents N_tau = 6, 8, and 12 for continuum extrapolation. They defined two novel estimators for the chiral transition temperature: T_delta from the condition H*chi_M/M = 1/delta (where delta is the critical exponent) and T_60 from the temperature where the chiral susceptibility reaches 60% of its peak value on the low-temperature side, both of which approach the true chiral transition temperature more rapidly than the peak of the susceptibility. Finite-volume effects were controlled using spatial extents of 2.8-4.5 times the inverse pion mass, and thermodynamic, continuum, and chiral extrapolations were performed using universal O(4) scaling analysis.

## Key numbers

- Chiral phase transition temperature: T_c^0 = 132 (+3, -6) MeV after thermodynamic, continuum, and chiral extrapolations
- Pseudocritical crossover temperature at physical quark masses: T_pc = 156.5 +/- 1.5 MeV (from Bazavov et al. 2018)
- Goldstone pion mass range studied: 58 MeV less than or equal to m_pi less than or equal to 163 MeV
- Light quark mass ratio range: H = m_l/m_s from 1/160 to 1/20
- Lattice temporal extents used for continuum extrapolation: N_tau = 6, 8, and 12
- Spatial lattice extent ratio: N_sigma/N_tau from 4 to 8
- RMS pion masses from taste violations at physical quark masses: 400 MeV (N_tau=6), 300 MeV (N_tau=8), 200 MeV (N_tau=12)
- Kaon decay constant used for normalization: f_K = 156.1/√2 MeV
- Critical exponent delta for O(4) universality class: 4.824
- Systematic shift from including or excluding N_tau=6 data: 2-3 MeV

## Context

This work provides the first lattice-QCD-based determination of the chiral phase transition temperature T_c^0 in (2+1)-flavor QCD with controlled thermodynamic, continuum, and chiral extrapolations, extending previous pseudocritical temperature determinations such as Bazavov et al. (2018) which found T_pc = 156.5 MeV at physical quark masses. The result is about 20-30 MeV lower than the pseudocritical temperature, consistent with predictions from QCD-inspired model calculations by Berges (1997) and Braun (2005). The two novel estimators (T_delta and T_60) proposed here will be useful for future lattice QCD calculations using other fermion actions, and the result sets an upper bound on the temperature of a possible critical point at nonzero baryon chemical potential being searched for in heavy-ion collision experiments.
