# Angular momentum of fission fragments

> The strong deformation present immediately after scission has consequences for the angular momentum population of the fragments as well as the angular distribution of their decay radiation. We find that the usual spin-cutoff parameterization describes very well the angular momentum distribution associated with the deformation of the fragments at the scission point. Depending on the deformation, its contribution can comparable to the thermal contribution to the angular momentum of the newly formed fragments. The $M$-distribution of the angular momentum is highly polarized and gives rise to large anisotropies in the subsequent gamma cascade. We treat in detail a typical gamma cascade in a daughter nucleus, following usual model assumptions except for the anisotropy of the initial state. In principle, the observed anisotropy can provide information on the relative amounts of deformation and thermal energy present at the scission point.

## Key claim

The usual spin-cutoff parameterization describes the angular momentum distribution from deformation at the scission point very well, and depending on the deformation its contribution can be comparable to the thermal contribution to the angular momentum of newly formed fragments; the M-distribution is highly polarized and gives rise to large anisotropies in the subsequent gamma cascade, which in principle can provide information on the relative amounts of deformation and thermal energy present at the scission point.

## Method

The authors constructed deformed configurations using the Gogny D1S energy functional in the Hartree-Fock approximation, constraining on the mass quadrupole operator to obtain wave functions for six fission fragment nuclei prominent in 235U(n,f). They computed the mean-square angular momentum from the Hartree-Fock formula and analyzed the angular momentum distribution by projection, comparing it to the spin-cutoff formula. They then modeled a typical gamma cascade in a daughter nucleus, using both a simplified cascade starting from a pure aligned state and a more realistic stochastic treatment of the level spectrum with the constant-temperature formula and Brink-Axel strength function for electric dipole transitions.

## Key numbers

- For the six fission fragment nuclei (96Kr, 96Sr, 96Zr, 140Te, 140Xe, 140Ba), the linear relationship between mean-square angular momentum and deformation is approximately ⟨J²⟩ ≈ (0.3 ± 0.05) A^(3/2) β, where angular momentum is in units of ħ.
- For a lighter post-scission fragment of mass A=105 from 239Pu(n,f), the deformation was in the range β = 0.55-0.7, giving coherent angular momentum ⟨J²⟩ = 175-220, much larger than statistical modeling estimates of ~100.
- For the realistic cascade starting from (J,M) = (8,0) at excitation energy E* = 8 MeV, the anisotropy coefficients were c2 = -0.2 for dipole distributions and c2 = 0.4 for quadrupole distributions.
- Measured anisotropy coefficients from 252Cf spontaneous fission were c2 ≈ 0.1 for 144Ba, 0.3 for 110Ru, and -0.3 for 105Mo.

## Context

This work extends the treatment of fission fragment angular momentum beyond the statistical modeling that ignores deformation effects, which fails to reproduce average angular momenta by as much as a factor of two (Stetcu et al., 2014). The authors build on earlier work by Bonneau et al. (2007) that studied the relationship between deformation and angular momentum using different modeling assumptions, and they reference time-dependent density functional theory calculations by Bulgac et al. (2015, 2018) that provide deformation parameters for post-scission fragments. The results enable using prompt gamma angular distributions as an observable to learn about the division of excitation energy in newly formed fission fragments, though the authors note that combining quasiparticle angular momentum with deformation will reduce the anisotropy and that relationship needs quantitative understanding.
