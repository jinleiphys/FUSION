# Magnetic shift of the chemical freezeout and electric charge fluctuations

> We discuss the effect of a strong magnetic field on the chemical freezeout points in the ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collision. As a result of the inverse magnetic catalysis or the magnetic inhibition, the crossover onset to hot and dense matter out of quarks and gluons should be shifted to a lower temperature. To quantify this shift we employ the hadron resonance gas model and an empirical condition for the chemical freezeout. We point out that the charged particle abundances are significantly affected by the magnetic field so that the electric charge fluctuation is largely enhanced especially at high baryon density. The charge conservation partially cancels the enhancement but our calculation shows that the electric charge fluctuation and the charge chemical potential could serve as a magnetometer. We find that the fluctuation exhibits a crossover behavior rapidly increased for eB >~ (0.4GeV)^2, while the charge chemical potential has better sensitivity to the magnetic field.

## Key claim
The chemical freezeout curve in heavy-ion collisions is shifted to lower temperatures by a strong magnetic field, consistent with inverse magnetic catalysis, but this shift is non-trivial because the hadron resonance gas model predicts an *upward* shift if electric charge conservation is not imposed. The electric charge fluctuation and charge chemical potential could serve as a magnetometer, with the fluctuation exhibiting a rapid crossover increase for eB ≳ (0.4 GeV)^2.

## Method
The authors used the hadron resonance gas (HRG) model with the particle list from THERMUS-V3.0, imposing the freezeout condition E/N = 0.9–1.0 GeV. They implemented strangeness conservation (n_S = 0) and electric charge conservation (B/(2Q) = 1.2683, fixed for heavy nuclei) by introducing finite strangeness and charge chemical potentials μS and μQ, and computed the electric charge susceptibility χ_Q along the resulting freezeout curves for B=0 and eB=(0.5 GeV)^2.

## Key numbers
- The freezeout temperature parametrization T_f(μB) = a – b μB^2 – c μB^4 yields for B=0 and E/N=0.9 GeV: a = 0.1519 GeV, b = 0.1347 GeV^{-1}, c = 0.05976 GeV^{-3}; for B=0 and E/N=1.0 GeV: a = 0.1618 GeV, b = 0.1367 GeV^{-1}, c = 0.04705 GeV^{-3}.
- For eB=(0.5 GeV)^2 and E/N=0.9 GeV: a = 0.1418 GeV, b = 0.1253 GeV^{-1}, c = 0.1849 GeV^{-3}; for eB=(0.5 GeV)^2 and E/N=1.0 GeV: a = 0.1555 GeV, b = 0.1362 GeV^{-1}, c = 0.0565 GeV^{-3}.
- At eB=(0.5 GeV)^2, χ_Q is twice enhanced for small μB and, for μB ~ 0.6 GeV, it can be four times as large as that at B=0.
- The charge chemical potential μQ at eB=(0.5 GeV)^2 reaches values of order ~0.1 GeV at large μB, compared to ~0.01 GeV at B=0.
- The fluctuation χ_Q is almost flat up to eB ~ 0.15 GeV^2 and shows a rapid increase for eB ≳ (0.4 GeV)^2.

## Context
This work extends the hadron resonance gas model analysis of chemical freezeout (Cleymans 1998, Cleymans 2005) to include strong magnetic fields, and connects to lattice QCD findings of inverse magnetic catalysis (Bali 2011, Bali 2012, Bali 2014). The authors show that the HRG model can reproduce the downward shift of the freezeout temperature seen in lattice simulations, but only when electric charge conservation is properly enforced. The results enable experimental searches for magnetic field effects in heavy-ion collisions through centrality-differentiated measurements of thermal parameters and charge fluctuations, particularly at facilities like FAIR, NICA, and JPARC.
