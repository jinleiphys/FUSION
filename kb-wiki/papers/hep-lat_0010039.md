# Introduction to stochastic error correction methods

> We propose a method for eliminating the truncation error associated with any subspace diagonalization calculation. The new method, called stochastic error correction, uses Monte Carlo sampling to compute the contribution of the remaining basis vectors not included in the initial diagonalization. The method is part of a new approach to computational quantum physics which combines both diagonalization and Monte Carlo techniques.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that truncation error from any subspace diagonalization can be eliminated by using Monte Carlo sampling to compute the contribution of basis vectors not included in the initial diagonalization, but the method's convergence depends on the starting eigenvectors being close to the exact eigenvectors (for the series method) or, for the stochastic Lanczos method, does not require that proximity.

## Method

The authors introduce two varieties of stochastic error correction (SEC): a series method that expands the exact eigenvector in inverse powers of an energy parameter \(E\) around a subspace-diagonalized starting point, and a non-series stochastic Lanczos method that uses matrix diffusion Monte Carlo to compute matrix elements of Krylov vectors generated from the subspace eigenvectors. The series method calculates first- and second-order corrections via Monte Carlo sampling of random basis vectors, while the stochastic Lanczos method constructs an auxiliary subspace and evaluates the operator \(P_S[P_Z(H-a)P_Z]^n P_S\) recursively using random walkers.

## Key numbers

- For \(\phi^4\) theory in \(2+1\) dimensions with \(N_{\max}=10\) and \(L=3\pi\) (momentum cutoff \(\Lambda=3.33\mu\)), QSE diagonalization with 500 Fock states and first-order series SEC shows a second-order phase transition near \(\frac{g}{4!}=0.9\).
- For compact U(1) in \(2+1\) dimensions on a \(4\times4\) lattice at \(x=1\), the ground state energy extrapolated to \(L_{\max}^2=\infty\) is \(E_0 = -7.442(4)\) (in units of \(e^2/2\)), compared to Green's function Monte Carlo result \(-7.4432(5)\).
- For the \(4\times4\) Hubbard model with 5 electrons per spin at \(U=4t\), QSE with 1000 states and first-order SEC gives ground state energy \(-1.8302(1)\) (relative to Fermi sea at \(U=0\)), compared to exact value \(-1.8309\).
- For the Hubbard model at \(U=5t\) with 2000 states, QSE+SEC gives \(-2.7231(1)\) versus exact \(-2.7245\).
- For compact U(1), the lightest \(0^-\) glueball mass is \(3.03(2)\) (units of \(e^2/2\)), agreeing with GFMC result \(3.01(6)\).

## Context

This work extends the quasi-sparse eigenvector (QSE) diagonalization method introduced by the same group (Lee et al., 2000) by adding Monte Carlo sampling to correct truncation errors, combining diagonalization and Monte Carlo techniques for quantum systems. The method is demonstrated on three test problems: \(\phi^4\) theory (comparing with results from Magruder and from Chang's duality argument), compact U(1) lattice gauge theory (comparing with Green's function Monte Carlo results from Hamer et al.), and the Hubbard model (comparing with exact diagonalization, stochastic diagonalization from De Raedt, and projector quantum Monte Carlo from Husslein et al.). The approach enables computation of excited states with the same quantum numbers as lower states, a feature not available in typical Monte Carlo methods, and reduces the fermion sign problem by using subspace diagonalization as a starting point.
