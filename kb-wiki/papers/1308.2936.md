# Equivalence Principle and Bound Kinetic Energy

> We consider the role of the internal kinetic energy of bound systems of matter in tests of the Einstein equivalence principle. Using the gravitational sector of the standard model extension, we show that stringent limits on equivalence principle violations in antimatter can be indirectly obtained from tests using bound systems of normal matter. We estimate the bound kinetic energy of nucleons in a range of light atomic species using Green's function Monte Carlo calculations, and for heavier species using a Woods-Saxon model. We survey the sensitivities of existing and planned experimental tests of the equivalence principle, and report new constraints at the level of between a few parts in $10^{6}$ and parts in $10^{8}$ on violations of the equivalence principle for matter and antimatter.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that tests of the Einstein equivalence principle (EEP) using bound systems of normal matter can indirectly constrain EEP violations in antimatter, because the anomalous gravitational redshift produced by the EEP-violating coefficient \(\bar{c}^{w}_{00}\) in the bound kinetic energy of nucleons couples to the sum of matter and antimatter anomaly parameters \(\beta^{w}+\beta^{\bar{w}}\).

## Method

The authors use the gravitational sector of the Standard Model Extension (SME) to derive the Hamiltonian for a non-gravitationally bound system of particles, showing that the bound kinetic energy \(T_{\rm int}\) acquires a term proportional to \(\bar{c}^{w}_{00}\) and the gravitational potential \(U\). They estimate the bound kinetic energies of protons and neutrons in light nuclei (up to \(A=12\)) using Green's function Monte Carlo (GFMC) many-body calculations, and for heavier species using a single-particle Woods-Saxon potential model with parameters from Schwierz et al. (2007) and nuclide data from Audi et al. (2003). They then perform a multivariate normal analysis on an ensemble of existing EEP tests, including matter-wave interferometer, clock comparison, and torsion pendulum experiments, to extract limits on the isotropic EEP-violating SME coefficients.

## Key numbers

* Bound kinetic energies for \({}^{12}\)C from GFMC: \(T_{\rm int}^{\rm p}=219\) MeV and \(T_{\rm int}^{\rm n}=219\) MeV; from Woods-Saxon: \(T_{\rm int}^{\rm p}=145\) MeV and \(T_{\rm int}^{\rm n}=153\) MeV.
* Bound kinetic energies for \({}^{6}\)Li from GFMC: \(T_{\rm int}^{\rm p}=77\) MeV and \(T_{\rm int}^{\rm n}=78\) MeV; from Woods-Saxon: \(T_{\rm int}^{\rm p}=64\) MeV and \(T_{\rm int}^{\rm n}=65\) MeV.
* New constraints on EEP violation for matter and antimatter are reported at levels between a few parts in \(10^{6}\) and parts in \(10^{8}\).
* Global limit on \(\beta^{e+p-n} = -0.013 \pm 0.021\) (\(\times 10^{6}\)).
* Global limit on \(\beta^{\bar{e}+\bar{p}-\bar{n}} = 1.1 \pm 1.8\) (\(\times 10^{6}\)).
* Global limit on \((\bar{c}^{e})_{TT} = -0.014 \pm 0.028\) (\(\times 10^{6}\)).
* Global limit on \((\bar{c}^{n})_{TT} = 1.1 \pm 1.4\) (\(\times 10^{6}\)).
* Torsion pendulum tests constrain specific combinations of \(\beta\) parameters at the level of \(10^{-12}\), having constrained \(\Delta g/g\) to the level of \(10^{-14}\).

## Context

This work extends the prior analysis of Hohensee et al. (2011), which used a simpler Fermi gas model for nucleon kinetic energies, by incorporating more realistic nuclear models (GFMC and Woods-Saxon) that account for angular momentum, Coulomb effects, and spin-orbit interactions. The results connect the SME framework to older representations of EEP violation based on neutron excess and baryon number from Damour (1996). The improved constraints on SME coefficients enable future work to reduce model-dependent limitations, with better nuclear modeling potentially improving limits by up to eight orders of magnitude, and new EEP tests using matter-waves (DropTower, lithium, kasevich), clocks (STEQuest), or macroscopic masses (SR-Poem, GG) expected to eliminate these limitations.
