# Fock-Bargmann space and classic trajectories

> In recent years, a new approach to the theory of nuclear reactions leading to a break-down of the interacting subsytems into various channels has been developed. This approach was named the Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamic (AMD), and its main idea lies in the description of the nucleons by wave packets in which the antisymmetrization effects (but not other quantum effects) are accounted for. In the present review, the basic principles of AMD are illustrated on the examples of simplest nuclear systems, and the results are compared with those provided by an exact quantum-mechanical description in the Fock-Bargmann space. The applicability region of AMD is discussed, in particular, in the cases of systems with discrete spectrum, and a relation between the classical AMD trajectories and the quantum distributions is established. At the same time, a new interpretation of Brink orbitals and Slater determinants built on them as eigenfunctions of the coordinate operator defined in the Fock-Bargmann space is proposed. It is shown that these functions form the cluster geometry of nuclear systems.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics (AMD) approach, which describes nucleons by wave packets accounting only for antisymmetrization effects, can be rigorously related to exact quantum mechanics through the Fock-Bargmann space; however, this correspondence holds only in the quasi-classical limit and requires additional corrections for wave-packet spreading at low energies where antisymmetrization effects become significant.

## Method

The authors construct wave functions in the Fock-Bargmann space using Brink-Bloch orbitals and Slater determinants, then derive the probability distributions in phase space by multiplying the squared wave functions by the Bargmann measure. They compute classical Hamilton functions by taking expectation values of quantum Hamiltonians over Brink-Bloch orbitals and applying the least action principle to obtain classical equations of motion and phase trajectories. The quantum probability distributions and classical phase trajectories are compared for one-dimensional systems including free motion, harmonic oscillators, and interacting fermions with a Gaussian potential.

## Key numbers

- The minimal energy of a one-dimensional wave packet in free motion is 1/4 (in units where oscillator length, nucleon mass, and Planck constant are set to unity)
- For the one-dimensional harmonic oscillator, the classical Hamilton function is H = (1/2)(eta^2 + xi^2) + 1/2, with continuous spectrum limited to E > 1/2
- The singlet fermion pair has a minimum of the Hamilton function at xi = +/- 1.13, eta = 0, with E_min = 0.11
- The triplet fermion pair has minimal energy equal to 1/4, with a transition from bounded to unbounded motion at E = 3/4
- For the harmonic oscillator at large n, the probability distribution concentrates around the circle (xi^2 + eta^2)/2 = n, with dispersion sqrt(n)
- The Wigner function for the n=1 harmonic oscillator takes negative values inside the circle xi^2 + eta^2 = 1

## Context

This review extends the earlier work of Filippov (1999) on the connection between AMD trajectories and Fock-Bargmann space wave functions, and builds upon the original AMD formulation by Horiuchi (1991). The paper establishes that Brink-Bloch orbitals (Brink, 1966) are eigenfunctions of the coordinate operator in Fock-Bargmann space, and shows that the density matrix elements for multi-fermion systems correspond to SU(3) Clebsch-Gordan coefficients. This work enables a systematic comparison between classical AMD trajectories and exact quantum distributions, particularly for understanding the limitations of the AMD approach at low energies where antisymmetrization effects dominate and wave-packet spreading cannot be neglected.
