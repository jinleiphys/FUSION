# Dynamical Simulation of Nuclear "Pasta": Soft Condensed Matter in Dense Stars

> More than twenty years ago, it was predicted that nuclei can adopt interesting shapes, such as rods or slabs, etc., in the cores of supernovae and the crusts of neutron stars. These non-spherical nuclei are referred to as nuclear "pasta". In recent years, we have been studying the dynamics of the pasta phases using a method called quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) and have opened up a new aspect of study for this system. Our findings include: dynamical formation of the pasta phases by cooling down the hot uniform nuclear matter; phase diagrams in the density versus temperature plane; structural transitions between the pasta phases induced by compression and elucidation of the mechanism by which they proceed. In the present article, we given an overview of the basic physics and astrophysics of the pasta phases and review our works for readers in other fields.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the authors' quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations showing that nuclear "pasta" phases (non-spherical nuclei such as rods and slabs) can form dynamically in supernova cores and neutron star crusts, but the actual presence of these phases in stars depends on whether the dynamical processes leading to their formation actually occur, not just on energetic favorability.

## Method

The authors used quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations to study the dynamical behavior of nuclear matter at subnuclear densities, cooling hot uniform nuclear matter to form pasta phases and compressing the system to induce structural transitions between different pasta shapes. They also employed a simple incompressible liquid-drop model with a Wigner-Seitz approximation to calculate the total energy density (surface plus Coulomb contributions) for five nuclear shapes: spheres, cylinders, slabs, cylindrical holes, and spherical holes.

## Key numbers

- The normal nuclear saturation density is given as ρ₀ = 0.165 nucleons fm⁻³ ≈ 3 × 10¹⁴ g cm⁻³
- The surface tension used in the liquid-drop model is σ = 0.73 MeV fm⁻²
- At densities below 0.215 ρ₀, spherical nuclei give the lowest surface-plus-Coulomb energy; with increasing density the most stable shape changes through cylinder, slab, cylindrical hole, spherical hole, and finally uniform matter
- The static structure factor S_nn(q) for the rod phase at ρ = 0.175 ρ₀, x = 0.3, and T = 1 MeV shows a peak around q = 0.25–0.3 fm⁻¹ where the cross section exceeds one hundred times that for uniform matter
- The pasta phases can occupy half of the total mass of neutron star crusts and 10–20% of the mass of supernova cores in the later stage of collapse

## Context

This work extends the original predictions by Ravenhall et al. (1983) and Hashimoto et al. (1984) that non-spherical nuclei could be energetically stable in dense stellar matter, and addresses the dynamical question of whether such phases actually form. The authors' QMD simulations build on earlier static calculations and open the possibility of incorporating pasta-phase effects into supernova explosion simulations, particularly for neutrino transport (where coherent scattering cross sections are enhanced) and for the equation of state used in core-collapse models. The paper also discusses implications for pulsar glitches (via the vortex pinning model and starquake model) and neutron star cooling (via modified URCA processes and bremsstrahlung).
