# Galactic discrete sources of high energy neutrinos

> We review recently developed models of galactic discrete sources of high energy neutrinos. Some of them are based on a simple rescaling of the TeV $γ$-ray fluxes from recently detected galactic sources, such as, shell-type supernova remnants or pulsar wind nebulae. Others present detailed and originally performed modeling of processes occurring close to compact objects, i.e. neutron stars and low mass black holes, which are supposed to accelerate hadrons close to dense matter and radiation fields. Most of the models considered in this review optimistically assume that the energy content in relativistic hadrons is equal to a significant part of the maximum observable power output in specific sources, i.e. typically $\sim 10%$. This may give a large overestimation of the neutrino fluxes. This is the case of models which postulate neutrino production in hadron-photon collisions already at the acceleration place, due to the likely $e^\pm$ pair plasma domination. Models postulating neutrino production in hadron-hadron collisions avoid such problems and therefore seem to be more promising. The neutrino telescopes currently taking data have not detected any excess from discrete sources yet, although some models could already be constrained by the limits they are providing.

## Key claim

Most models of galactic high-energy neutrino sources optimistically assume that the energy content in relativistic hadrons equals about 10% of the maximum observable power output in specific sources, which may lead to large overestimations of neutrino fluxes; models postulating neutrino production in hadron-hadron collisions avoid problems associated with hadron-photon collisions at the acceleration site and appear more promising.

## Method

The authors review models of galactic discrete neutrino sources, including those based on rescaling TeV gamma-ray fluxes from shell-type supernova remnants and pulsar wind nebulae, as well as detailed modeling of hadron acceleration near neutron stars and low-mass black holes. They evaluate neutrino production mechanisms through hadron-hadron (pp) and hadron-photon (pγ) collisions, and compute expected muon neutrino event rates for neutrino telescopes using detector effective areas, neutrino cross sections, and Earth absorption probabilities.

## Key numbers

- For a source at D = 5 kpc, detection of 10 events of 100 TeV neutrinos in a 0.1 km² detector in one year requires a luminosity Lν ∼ 10³⁶ erg/s
- At 100 TeV, the probability that a neutrino produces a muon above 10 GeV is Pν→μ ∼ 10⁻⁴
- The model of supernova shock waves predicts about 100 events in a 1 km² detector from a supernova at 10 kpc if proton acceleration efficiency is close to 10%
- For a pulsar with initial period P₀ = 10 ms and no polar cap heating, the neutrino spectrum below 10⁴ GeV is dNν/dEν = 3.220 × 10⁻⁵ (Eν/1 GeV)⁻¹·⁶⁸⁰ GeV⁻¹ m⁻² s⁻¹
- For a pulsar with P₀ = 5 ms and no polar cap heating, the neutrino spectrum below 10⁴ GeV is dNν/dEν = 4.470 × 10⁻⁵ (Eν/1 GeV)⁻¹·⁴⁸⁰ GeV⁻¹ m⁻² s⁻¹
- The Beall and Bednarek (2002) model predicts up to 10⁴–10⁵ events in a 1 km² detector from a supernova at 10 kpc during the first year for pulsars with millisecond periods and surface magnetic fields of a few 10¹² G
- For a pulsar with P = 3 ms and B₁₂ = 4, the acceleration efficiency ξ = 1.4 × 10⁻⁴, yielding 12.2 events from Fe-M→νμ in the horizontal direction
- For a pulsar with P = 10 ms and B₁₂ = 100, ξ = 3.6 × 10⁻³, yielding 648.0 events from Fe-M→νμ in the horizontal direction
- The Crab nebula neutrino flux is dNν/dEν = 1.85 × 10⁻¹⁰ (Eν/10³ GeV)⁻²·⁶ GeV⁻¹ m⁻² s⁻¹ for Eν < 10⁵ GeV
- Pulsar wind nebulae could yield 1 to about 12 events per year in a 1 km² detector in the energy range Eν ∼ 1–100 TeV

## Context

This review extends earlier works on galactic neutrino sources, such as Gaisser et al. (1995) and Learned and Mannheim (2000), by incorporating recent TeV gamma-ray observations from shell-type supernova remnants and pulsar wind nebulae. The paper enables future comparisons between model predictions and limits from operating neutrino telescopes like AMANDA, MACRO, and Super-Kamiokande, as well as upcoming cubic-kilometer detectors such as IceCube and KM3NeT. The authors note that most models optimistically assume ∼10% hadronic energy content, which may overestimate fluxes, and that models based on hadron-hadron collisions appear more promising than those relying on hadron-photon collisions at acceleration sites.
