# Covariant energy density functionals: nuclear matter constraints and global ground state properties

> The correlations between global description of the ground state properties (binding energies, charge radii) and nuclear matter properties of the state-of-the-art covariant energy density functionals have been studied. It was concluded that the strict enforcement of the constraints on the nuclear matter properties (NMP) defined in Ref.\ \cite{RMF-nm} will not necessary lead to the functionals with good description of the binding energies and other ground and excited state properties. In addition, it will not substantially reduce the uncertainties in the predictions of the binding energies in neutron-rich systems. It turns out that the functionals, which come close to satisfying these NMP constraints, have some problems in the description of existing data. On the other hand, these problems are either absent or much smaller in the functionals which are carefully fitted to finite nuclei but which violate some NMP constraints. This is a consequence of the fact that the properties of finite nuclei are defined not only by nuclear matter properties but also by underlying shell effects. The mismatch of phenomenological content, existing in all modern functionals, related to nuclear matter physics and the physics of finite nuclei could also be responsible.

## Key claim

Strict enforcement of nuclear matter property (NMP) constraints does not necessarily lead to functionals that give a good description of binding energies and other ground-state properties of finite nuclei, and it will not substantially reduce uncertainties in predictions for neutron-rich systems. The authors argue that functionals carefully fitted to finite nuclei but violating some NMP constraints often perform better than those satisfying the constraints, because finite-nucleus properties depend not only on nuclear matter properties but also on underlying shell effects.

## Method

The authors performed global relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations restricted to axially reflection-symmetric shapes for even-even nuclei between the two-proton and two-neutron drip lines, using four state-of-the-art covariant energy density functionals (NL3*, DD-ME2, DD-MEδ, DD-PC1) from prior work (Afanasjev and Agbemava 2014). They compared the binding energy predictions of these functionals against experimental data from the AME2012 mass evaluation and analyzed correlations between the functionals' nuclear matter properties (saturation density, energy per particle, incompressibility, symmetry energy, slope of symmetry energy, effective mass) and their performance on finite nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The rms deviations for binding energies of known nuclei range from 2.01 MeV (DD-PC1) to 2.96 MeV (NL3*) in global calculations, with fitted rms deviations from 0.69 MeV (DD-PC1) to 2.33 MeV (DD-MEδ)
- Binding energy spreads ΔE among the four functionals stay within 5-6 MeV for known nuclei, but reach up to 15 MeV near the neutron-drip line
- For the Yb isotope chain, adding the PC-PK1 functional increases ΔE from approximately 16 MeV to approximately 37 MeV at N=162 beyond the FRIB limit
- The DD-MEδ functional, which comes closest to satisfying NMP constraints, has a global rms deviation of 2.29 MeV for binding energies and 0.0329 fm for charge radii
- The NL3* functional has symmetry energy J = 38.68 MeV and slope L0 = 122.6 MeV, both outside the SET2b constraint ranges (J: 30-35 MeV, L0: 30-80 MeV)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of 263 covariant energy density functionals performed in Ref. RMF-nm (2015), which defined NMP constraint sets SET2a and SET2b, by testing whether those constraints translate into good finite-nucleus performance. The authors find that functionals satisfying NMP constraints (like FSUGold and DD-MEδ) face significant problems in describing finite nuclei, while functionals like NL3*, DD-ME2, PC-PK1, and DD-PC1 provide better overall descriptions despite violating some NMP constraints. This contradicts the expectation that strict NMP enforcement would reduce theoretical uncertainties, and it suggests that future mass measurements at rare isotope facilities (FRIB, GANIL, RIKEN, FAIR) will provide only modest improvements to isovector properties of functionals, especially for heavy nuclei with Z ≥ 82.
