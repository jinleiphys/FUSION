# Nuclear Symmetry Energy with Strangeness in Heavy Ion Collision

> The role of anti-kaons in the symmetry energy to be determined in heavy-ion collisions as for instance in such observables as the $π^-/π^+$ ratio is discussed using a simple chiral Lagrangian. It is shown, with some mild assumptions, that kaons, when present in the system, can affect the EoS appreciably for both symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter. For nuclear matter with small asymmetry with which heavy-ion collisions are studied, it may be difficult to distinguish a stiff symmetry energy and the supersoft symmetry energy, even with kaons present. However the effect of kaon is found to be significant such that $μ_n-μ_p \neq 0$ near $x=1/2$, at which the chemical potential difference is zero without kaon amplitude. We present the argument that in order to obtain a reliably accurate equation of state (EoS) for compact-star matter, a much deeper understanding is needed on how the strangeness degrees of freedom such as kaons, hyperons etc. behave in baryonic matter in a Fermi liquid (or possibly a non-Fermi liquid) phase with potential phase changes. It is suggested that such an {\em accurate} treatment could have an important implication on possibly modified gravity.

# Nuclear Symmetry Energy with Strangeness in Heavy Ion Collision

## Key claim
The presence of bound anti-kaons in baryonic matter produced in heavy-ion collisions can modify the nuclear symmetry energy and induce a nonzero neutron-proton chemical potential difference at symmetric matter composition (x=1/2), but for the small asymmetries typical of heavy-ion collisions, it may remain difficult to distinguish a stiff symmetry energy from a supersoft symmetry energy even with kaons present.

## Method
The authors employ a simple chiral Lagrangian at order O(p^2) containing kaon-nucleon and nucleon-nucleon interactions, including the Weinberg-Tomozawa term and the KN sigma term. They compute the kaon chemical potential, the neutron and proton chemical potentials in the presence of a bound s-wave kaon amplitude, and the asymmetry energy per nucleon as a function of proton fraction x and kaon fraction x_K. Two phenomenological parametrizations of the symmetry energy are used for comparison: a standard "MID" (momentum-independent interaction) form from Li et al. and a supersoft form.

## Key numbers
- The kaon condensation threshold density from the simple formula is approximately 6 n_0 when using the lattice value for the KN sigma term (Sigma_KN less than or about 250 MeV), but a more sophisticated hidden local symmetry approach gave n_th approximately 3 n_0.
- The pion decay constant used is f approximately 93 MeV.
- The Fermi energy at saturation is E_F^0 = 37.2 MeV.
- The MID symmetry energy parameters are A = -298.3 MeV, B = 110.9 MeV, gamma = 1.21, F = 3.673, and C = 1.569.
- The supersoft symmetry energy is given by S_ss(n) = 13.1 (n/n_0)^(2/3) + 107 (n/n_0) - 88.4 (n/n_0)^(1.25) MeV.
- The chemical potential difference and asymmetry energy are evaluated at n = 2 n_0 and n = 3 n_0, with an illustrative kaon fraction x_K = 1.
- The initial neutron-proton asymmetry factor delta ranges from 0 for carbon-12 to 0.198 for gold-197 or 0.227 for uranium-238 collisions.

## Context
This work extends the study of nuclear symmetry energy in heavy-ion collisions by incorporating strangeness degrees of freedom, which had received little prior attention. It builds on the framework of Li et al. (LCK) for the symmetry energy parametrization and on earlier work by Lee and Rho (LR1) on kaon-nucleon interactions. The paper connects to the broader problem of compact-star equations of state, noting that the Brown-Bethe scenario of kaon condensation softening the EoS enough to prevent stars above 1.5 solar masses appears challenged by recent measurements of approximately 2 solar-mass stars (2Solar). The authors argue that this conclusion is premature and that a deeper understanding of strangeness in dense matter, including possible topology changes from skyrmions to half-skyrmions as found by the half-skyrmion group, and non-Fermi-liquid effects, is needed before ruling out kaon condensation.
