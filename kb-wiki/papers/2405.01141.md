# Particle-theory input for neutron-star physics

> Understanding the properties and physical phase of the dense strongly interacting matter present in the cores of neutron stars or created in their binary mergers remains one of the most prominent open problems in nuclear astrophysics. While most microscopic analyses have historically relied on solvable phenomenological models of nuclear and quark matter, in recent years a model-independent approach utilizing only controlled ab-initio calculations and astrophysical observations has emerged as a viable alternative. In these lecture notes, I review recent progress in first-principles weak-coupling calculations within high-density quark matter, shedding light on its thermodynamic and transport properties. I cover the most important technical tools used in such calculations, introduce selected highlight results, and explain how this information can be used in phenomenological studies of neutron-star physics. The notes do not offer a self-consistent treatment of the topics covered, but rather aim at filling gaps in existing textbooks on thermal field theory and at connecting the dots in a story developed in several recent research articles, to which the interested reader is directed for further technical details.

## Key claim

These lecture notes argue that first-principles weak-coupling calculations within high-density quark matter, combined with astrophysical observations, now offer a viable model-independent alternative to traditional phenomenological models for studying neutron-star matter, but they do not provide a self-consistent treatment and instead aim to fill gaps in existing textbooks on thermal field theory.

## Method

The author reviews perturbative thermal field theory techniques at finite baryon chemical potential, focusing on the imaginary-time formalism and the Hard Thermal Loop (HTL) effective theory. The method proceeds by expanding the QCD partition function in powers of the coupling constant around a system of noninteracting quarks at zero temperature, then using dimensional reduction and HTL resummation to handle infrared divergences that appear at three-loop order. The derivation of the one-loop gluon self energy in the soft-external-momentum limit is performed explicitly, and the resulting HTL self energies are applied to determine the leading non-analyticity in the strong coupling constant for the pressure of cold and dense quark matter.

## Key numbers

* The leading-order pressure of noninteracting massless quarks at zero temperature is \(p_\text{QCD}^\text{LO} = \frac{N_c}{12\pi^2} \sum_f \mu_f^4\), where \(N_c\) is the number of colors and \(\mu_f\) are flavor-dependent quark chemical potentials.
* The next-to-leading order pressure for massless quarks is \(p_\text{QCD}^\text{NLO} = \left(\frac{N_c}{12\pi^2} - \frac{d_A g^2}{64\pi^4}\right) \sum_f \mu_f^4\), where \(d_A = N_c^2-1\) and \(g\) is the gauge coupling.
* The leading-order electric screening mass is \(m_\text{E}^2 = g^2\left[\frac{C_A+T_F}{3}T^2 + \frac{1}{2\pi^2}\sum_f \mu_f^2\right]\), with \(T_F = N_f/2\).
* The nuclear saturation density is given as \(n_s = 0.16/\text{fm}^3\).

## Context

These lecture notes extend the perturbative thermal field theory methods developed for high-temperature QCD (e.g., by Braaten and Pisarski (1989) and Kajantie et al. (1995)) to the cold and dense regime relevant for neutron-star cores. The work builds on recent first-principles calculations of quark matter thermodynamics by Gorda et al. (2021, 2022, 2023) and Kurkela et al. (2016), and it enables model-independent determinations of the neutron-star matter equation of state by combining perturbative QCD results with astrophysical observations such as mass measurements (Antoniadis 2013, Cromartie 2019) and gravitational-wave signals (LIGO/Virgo 2017).
