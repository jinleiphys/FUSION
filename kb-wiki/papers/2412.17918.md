# Emulation of the final r-process abundance pattern with a neural network

> This work explores the construction of a fast emulator for the calculation of the final pattern of nucleosynthesis in the rapid neutron capture process (the $r$-process). An emulator is built using a feed-forward artificial neural network (ANN). We train the ANN with nuclear data and relative abundance patterns. We take as input the $β$-decay half-lives and the one-neutron separation energy of the nuclei in the rare-earth region. The output is the final isotopic abundance pattern. In this work, we focus on the nuclear data and abundance patterns in the rare-earth region to reduce the dimension of the input and output space. We show that the ANN can capture the effect of the changes in the nuclear physics inputs on the final $r$-process abundance pattern in the adopted astrophysical conditions. We employ the deep ensemble method to quantify the prediction uncertainty of the neutal network emulator. The emulator achieves a speed-up by a factor of about 20,000 in obtaining a final abundance pattern in the rare-earth region. The emulator may be utilized in statistical analyses such as uncertainty quantification, inverse problems, and sensitivity analysis.

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that a feed-forward artificial neural network can emulate the final r-process abundance pattern in the rare-earth region (mass numbers A=150-180) with a speed-up factor of about 20,000, but only under the specific astrophysical trajectories and input distributions used for training, and only for the localized effect of varying β-decay half-lives and one-neutron separation energies of 212 nuclei in that region.

## Method
The authors trained a feed-forward artificial neural network (ANN) using 300,000 samples from nuclear reaction network calculations performed with the PRISM code, varying the β-decay half-lives and one-neutron separation energies of 212 nuclei in the rare-earth region according to prescribed distributions (uniform for S_n, log-uniform for decay rates). The ANN architecture was optimized using neural architecture search with AutoKeras, resulting in a model with convolutional layers followed by fully connected layers, and uncertainty quantification was performed using the deep ensemble method with 10 copies of the network. Training was done on two astrophysical trajectories: a cold neutron-rich dynamical ejecta from a binary neutron star merger and a hot wind trajectory.

## Key numbers
- Speed-up factor of about 20,000 in obtaining a final abundance pattern in the rare-earth region compared to full nuclear reaction network calculations
- 212 nuclei in the rare-earth region were varied as input variables (424 total input variables: S_n and T_{1/2} for each)
- 300,000 training samples used (280,000 for optimization, 20,000 for validation), with 10,000 additional test samples
- S_n uncertainty assumed to be ±0.5 MeV uniformly distributed around FRDM2012 values
- β-decay rate uncertainty set with σ = ln(2) in the log-normal distribution, allowing for a factor of 10 variation
- Ensemble size M=10 for deep ensemble uncertainty quantification
- Mass number range A=150 to A=180 for the rare-earth peak region

## Context
This work extends prior efforts to reduce computational cost in r-process nucleosynthesis studies, which previously used reduced-order models, Gaussian processes, or neural networks (Frame 2018, Drischler 2021, Drischler 2022, Bonilla 2022, Melendez 2022, Garcia 2023, Giuliani 2023, Odell 2024, Lay 2024). It directly addresses the computational bottleneck identified in Monte Carlo studies (Mumpower 2016, Martin 2016, Sprouse 2020) and inverse problem approaches using Markov chain Monte Carlo (Mumpower 2017, Vassh 2021), where full network calculations become prohibitively expensive. The emulator enables future statistical analyses including uncertainty quantification, inverse problems, and sensitivity analysis of nuclear physics inputs on the rare-earth peak formation, building on the astrophysical trajectories and nuclear data from Kiss (2022).
