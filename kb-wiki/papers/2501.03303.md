# Lattice Effective Field Theory Simulations of Nuclei

> Lattice effective field theory applies the principles of effective field theory in a lattice framework where space and time are discretized. Nucleons are placed on the lattice sites, and the interactions are tuned to replicate the observed features of the nuclear force. Monte Carlo simulations are then employed to predict the properties of nuclear few- and many-body systems. We review the basic methods and several theoretical and algorithmic advances that have been used to further our understanding of atomic nuclei.

# Lattice Effective Field Theory Simulations of Nuclei

## Key claim

Lattice effective field theory (NLEFT) provides a complementary *ab initio* approach to nuclear structure that discretizes space and time on a lattice with spacing comparable to 1 fm, resolving only long-distance physics while using Monte Carlo simulations to predict properties of nuclear few- and many-body systems; the review covers recent algorithmic advances that have enabled progress, but notes that higher-order chiral interactions typically produce severe sign problems in Monte Carlo simulations, requiring perturbation theory or wavefunction matching to overcome.

## Method

Nucleons are placed on discrete lattice sites with spatial lattice spacing *a* and temporal spacing *a_t*, using a cubic lattice with periodic boundaries. Chiral effective field theory interactions are implemented via local and nonlocal smearing operators, with pion fields treated as instantaneous interactions rather than dynamical fields. Auxiliary-field Monte Carlo simulations rewrite two-nucleon interactions as couplings between nucleons and fluctuating auxiliary fields using Gaussian integral identities, and Euclidean-time projection is used to extract ground-state properties by applying powers of the transfer matrix to Slater-determinant initial states. For scattering calculations, spherical wall boundaries replace periodic boundary conditions to extract phase shifts and mixing angles.

## Key numbers

- Lattice spacing *a* = 1.32 fm used for N3LO neutron-proton scattering phase shift calculations (Li 2018)
- Phase shifts and mixing angles computed at N3LO for relative momenta up to *p_rel* = 250 MeV (Li 2018)
- Saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter *rho_0* approximately 0.16 fm^{-3} (Lu 2018)
- Computational scaling approximately quadratic in nucleon number for medium-mass nuclei, crossing over to cubic for heavy nuclei
- Simple Hamiltonian with only four adjustable parameters reproduces energies and charge radii of light and medium-mass nuclei (Lu 2018)

## Context

This review extends the line of work beginning with the first lattice calculations of nucleons with short-range interactions (Muller 1999) and the first lattice calculations with dynamical nucleons and chiral EFT (Lee 2004). It builds on earlier NLEFT reviews (Lee 2008, Drut 2012, Lahde 2019) and describes recent advances including the pinhole algorithm for A-body densities (Elhatisari 2017), the pinhole trace algorithm for nuclear thermodynamics (Lu 2019), and wavefunction matching for accelerated perturbation theory convergence (Elhatisari 2022). These developments enable calculations of nuclear structure, scattering, and thermodynamics that were previously inaccessible due to sign problems in Monte Carlo simulations of realistic chiral interactions.
