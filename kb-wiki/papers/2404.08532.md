# Tensor factorization in ab initio many-body calculations: Triaxially-deformed (B) MBPT calculations in large bases

> Whether for fundamental studies or nuclear data evaluations, first-principle calculations of atomic nuclei constitute the path forward. Today, performing \textit{ab initio} calculations (a) of heavy nuclei, (b) of doubly open-shell nuclei or (c) with a sub-percent accuracy is at the forefront of nuclear structure theory. While combining any two of these features constitutes a major challenge, addressing the three at the same time is currently impossible. From a numerical standpoint, these challenges relate to the necessity to handle (i) very large single bases and (ii) mode-6, \textit{i.e.} three-body, tensors (iii) that must be stored repeatedly. Performing second-order many-body perturbation theory(ies) calculations based on triaxially deformed and superfluid reference states of doubly open-shell nuclei up to mass $A=72$, the present work achieves a significant step forward by addressing challenge (i). To do so, the memory and computational cost associated with the handling of large tensors is scaled down via the use of tensor factorization techniques. The presently used factorization format is based on a randomized singular value decomposition that does not require the computation and storage of the very large initial tensor. The procedure delivers an inexpensive and controllable approximation to the original problem, as presently illustrated for calculations that could not be performed without tensor factorization. With the presently developed technology at hand, one can envision to perform calculations of yet heavier doubly open-shell nuclei at sub-percent accuracy in a foreseeable future.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that second-order many-body perturbation theory calculations for triaxially-deformed, doubly open-shell nuclei up to mass A=72 can be performed in large single-particle bases (N up to 4000) by using a randomized singular value decomposition that never requires computing or storing the full many-body tensor explicitly, thereby addressing the challenge of large basis dimensions while deferring the simultaneous challenge of three-body tensors and sub-percent accuracy.

## Method

The authors perform second-order (Bogoliubov) many-body perturbation theory calculations based on triaxially-deformed and superfluid reference states, breaking both U(1) gauge symmetry (particle number) and SU(2) rotational symmetry (both J² and Jz). They apply a tensor factorization format based on randomized singular value decomposition directly in the quasi-particle basis relevant to finite-nuclei calculations, which avoids computing and storing the large initial mode-4 tensor. The factorization is applied to the many-body tensors arising in the perturbative expansion, which are known closed-form functionals of the Hamiltonian tensors and quasi-particle energies.

## Key numbers

- Mass range: calculations performed for doubly open-shell nuclei up to A=72 (specifically up to ⁷²Kr)
- Single-particle basis dimension: N in the range [2000, 4000] for the triaxial calculations
- Effective basis dimension when exploiting spherical symmetry: _sÑ = 240 for N=2720 (emax=14), and _sÑ = 306 for N=3876 (emax=16)
- Compression factor from angular momentum coupling: the two-body interaction tensor size is reduced by 6 orders of magnitude for N=1820 (emax=12)
- Prior spherical calculations reached _sÑ = 216 (from N=2720) for doubly closed-shell nuclei up to ¹³²Sn (Zare, 2022)

## Context

This work extends prior tensor factorization efforts in spherical many-body perturbation theory for doubly closed-shell nuclei (Tichai, 2018) and spherical Bogoliubov MBPT for singly open-shell nuclei (Tichai, 2019) to the much more challenging case of triaxially-deformed, doubly open-shell nuclei where no symmetry reduction of the quasi-particle basis is possible. The approach builds on the randomized SVD factorization format introduced by Tichai (2023) for three-nucleon interactions in momentum basis, but adapts it to work directly in the quasi-particle basis for finite-nuclei calculations. This enables future calculations of heavier doubly open-shell nuclei at sub-percent accuracy, and provides a foundation for extending tensor factorization to non-perturbative methods such as Bogoliubov coupled cluster and to third-order perturbation theory.
