# Merging multidimensional equations of state of strongly interacting matter via a statistical mixture

> We introduce a general method to merge multidimensional equations of state (EoSs) by combining them in a two-fluid equilibrium statistical mixture in the grand canonical ensemble. The merged grand potential density $ω$ is built directly from the input EoSs and the fluid fractions are fixed by minimizing $ω$ at fixed temperature $T$ and baryon chemical potential $μ_B$. Thermodynamic consistency and stability are guaranteed as all thermodynamic quantities are consistently derived from a single merged grand potential $ω(T,μ_B)$ with the correct convexity properties. Our method can accommodate a first-order phase transition and a critical endpoint with mean-field critical exponents. We use this method to merge a van der Waals Hadron-Resonance-Gas EoS with a holographic Einstein-Maxwell-Dilaton EoS that has a critical point and a first-order line. The result is a single EoS, spanning hadronic and deconfined matter over a broad range in $(T,μ_B)$, which can be readily used in heavy-ion hydrodynamic simulations. Our merging method can be generalized to consider a higher dimensional phase diagram (e.g., by considering more chemical potentials) and more than two input EoSs.

# Merging multidimensional equations of state of strongly interacting matter via a statistical mixture

## Key claim

The authors introduce a general method to merge two distinct equations of state (EoSs) into a single thermodynamically consistent EoS by modeling the system as a two-fluid equilibrium statistical mixture in the grand canonical ensemble, where the fluid fractions are determined by minimizing the grand potential density at fixed temperature and baryon chemical potential. The method can accommodate a first-order phase transition and a critical endpoint with mean-field critical exponents, but the specific location and existence of these features depend on the choice of the mixing parameters $a$ and $b$ relative to each other.

## Method

The authors construct a grand potential density $\omega(T,\mu_B;p) = -p P_1 - (1-p) P_2 + a[p\ln p + (1-p)\ln(1-p)] + p(1-p)b$, where $p\in[0,1]$ is an internal variable representing the fraction of the first fluid, $P_1$ and $P_2$ are the input EoS pressures, $a$ is a mixing entropy parameter, and $b$ encodes repulsive interactions between the two fluids. The equilibrium mixing weight $\overline{p}(T,\mu_B)$ is found by minimizing $\omega$ at fixed $T$ and $\mu_B$, and all thermodynamic quantities are then derived from the merged grand potential. The method is applied to merge a van der Waals Hadron-Resonance-Gas (QvdW-HRG) EoS for hadronic matter with a holographic Einstein-Maxwell-Dilaton (EMD) EoS for the quark-gluon plasma, with the parameters $a = T/\Delta V$ and $b = 2T_c/\Delta V$ chosen so that a crossover occurs at high temperature and a first-order line emerges from a critical endpoint at lower temperature.

## Key numbers

- The QvdW-HRG baryonic van der Waals parameters are $a = 329\;\mathrm{MeV\,fm^3}$ and $b = 3.42\;\mathrm{fm^3}$, fixed by reproducing nuclear-matter saturation density $n_0 = 0.16\;\mathrm{fm}^{-3}$ and binding energy $E/A = -16\;\mathrm{MeV}$.
- The liquid-gas critical point in the QvdW-HRG calibration sits at $T_c \simeq 19.7\;\mathrm{MeV}$, $n_c \simeq 0.07\;\mathrm{fm}^{-3}$.
- The merged EoS spans up to $\mu_B \sim 1000\;\mathrm{MeV}$ and $T \sim 600\;\mathrm{MeV}$.
- The critical point in the merged EoS occurs when $b = 2a$, corresponding to $T_c = b\Delta V/2$.

## Context

This work extends previous efforts to merge different EoSs using switching functions (Nonaka 2004, Parotto 2018, Karthein 2021, Kapusta 2021, Kapusta 2022, Kapusta 2025), which the authors argue can produce thermodynamic instabilities due to derivative contributions from the switching function. The new method guarantees thermodynamic consistency and stability by treating the mixing weight as an internal variable determined by minimization. The merged EoS is designed for use in heavy-ion hydrodynamic simulations at RHIC Beam Energy Scan and future FAIR experiments, and the open-source code is made publicly available within the MUSES framework.
