# Nuclear Forces for Precision Nuclear Physics -- a collection of perspectives

> This is a collection of perspective pieces contributed by the participants of the Institute of Nuclear Theory's Program on Nuclear Physics for Precision Nuclear Physics which was held virtually from April 19 to May 7, 2021. The collection represents the reflections of a vibrant and engaged community of researchers on the status of theoretical research in low-energy nuclear physics, the challenges ahead, and new ideas and strategies to make progress in nuclear structure and reaction physics, effective field theory, lattice QCD, quantum information, and quantum computing. The contributed pieces solely reflect the perspectives of the respective authors and do not represent the viewpoints of the Institute for Nuclear theory or the organizers of the program.

# Nuclear Forces for Precision Nuclear Physics – a collection of perspectives

## Key claim
The long-standing discrepancy in lattice QCD calculations of di-nucleon systems at heavy pion masses, where some groups found deeply bound states and others did not, has been resolved in favor of no deeply bound di-nucleons, but only when a variational method with Hermitian correlation matrices is used; earlier results that identified bound states are attributed to false plateaus from asymmetric (non-conjugate) creation and annihilation operators.

## Method
The authors review the history of lattice QCD calculations of two-baryon systems, contrasting the earlier approach using local hexaquark creation operators with momentum-space annihilation operators against the newer variational method that employs a Hermitian matrix of correlation functions built from momentum-space operators. They discuss the generalized eigenvalue problem (GEVP) for extracting the finite-volume spectrum, the Lüscher quantization condition for relating that spectrum to infinite-volume scattering amplitudes, and the need to control discretization effects by using multiple lattice spacings. The perspective also outlines the formalism for extracting two-body matrix elements from finite-volume calculations and the challenges for extending these methods to three-nucleon and four-nucleon systems.

## Key numbers
- The lowest excited-state gap in two-baryon systems is roughly \(p^2/M \approx (2\pi/L)^2/M \approx 20\)–\(50\) MeV for typical lattice volumes, corresponding to a time scale of 4–10 fm for excited-state decay.
- The noise in two-baryon correlation functions overwhelms the signal before \(t \approx 2\) fm at larger-than-physical pion mass, and the signal-to-noise ratio degrades as \(e^{-2(M_N - \frac{3}{2}m_\pi)t}\) at asymptotically large times.
- For the \(H\) dibaryon, the continuum-limit binding energy is 4.6(1.3) MeV, whereas on the coarsest lattice spacing it exceeded 30 MeV, demonstrating large discretization effects.
- The NPLQCD collaboration first reported \(B_{NN} \approx 20\) MeV binding energies for both di-nucleon systems, but their most recent work using momentum-space creation operators finds no evidence for deep bound states, suggesting an O(20 MeV) systematic uncertainty on the NN binding energy.
- Ref. [NPLQCD:2012mex] quoted a \(^3\)He binding energy of \(B \approx 50\) MeV, which is 30 MeV from the quoted proton-deuteron breakup threshold.

## Context
This perspective piece extends the ongoing debate in lattice QCD nuclear physics between the HAL QCD Collaboration (which used the HAL QCD potential method and found no bound di-nucleons at heavy pion masses) and other groups (NPLQCD, Yamazaki, Orginos, Berkowitz, Wagman) that reported deeply bound di-nucleons using asymmetric correlators. The resolution comes from three independent calculations using variational methods with Hermitian correlation matrices: the Mainz group (Francis 2018), the sLapHnn Collaboration (Horz 2020), and the NPLQCD Collaboration (Amarasinghe 2021). The work enables future reliable determinations of two-baryon scattering amplitudes and matrix elements (including electroweak form factors and neutrinoless double-beta decay), but also highlights the need to develop quantization conditions valid on left-hand cuts and to extend the formalism to systems with intrinsic spin and to three-nucleon systems.
