# Systematic Theoretical Search for Dibaryons in a Relativistic Model

> A relativistic quark potential model is used to do a systematic search for quasi-stable dibaryon states in the $u$, $d$, and $s$ three flavor world. Flavor symmetry breaking and channel coupling effects are included and an adiabatic method and fractional parentage expansion technique are used in the calculations. The relativistic model predicts dibaryon candidates completely consistent with the nonrelativistic model.

# Systematic Theoretical Search for Dibaryons in a Relativistic Model

## Key claim
The relativistic quark potential model predicts dibaryon candidates whose masses are "completely consistent" with the nonrelativistic quark delocalization color screening model (QDCSM), but the authors qualify that the mass differences between the two models fluctuate from case to case, averaging about ±50 MeV, and that the H particle (flavor-singlet dibaryon) cannot definitively be concluded to exist as a bound state because the nonrelativistic model finds it unbound by 35 MeV while the relativistic model finds it bound.

## Method
A relativistic quark potential model (LAMP) with scalar linear confinement and a Gaussian color magnetic interaction was used to search for quasi-stable dibaryon states in the u, d, s three-flavor world. The six-quark Hamiltonian was diagonalized in a Dirac cluster basis using a relativistic extension of the fractional parentage expansion technique, with the effective two-baryon potential obtained by varying the delocalization parameter at each separation distance to find the minimum eigenenergy. Flavor symmetry breaking and channel coupling effects were included by considering all possible two-baryon states composed of octet and decuplet baryons.

## Key numbers
- The strange quark mass was set to m_s = 307 MeV, determined by the Lambda-N mass difference (939 MeV for N, 1116 MeV for Lambda).
- The color magnetic interaction strength for massless quarks was fixed as g^2_s(0)A = -1.350 GeV, determined by the N-Delta mass difference (939 MeV vs 1232 MeV).
- The confinement potential parameters were k^2 = 0.9 GeV/fm and r_0 = 0.57 fm, fixed so that the single-quark eigenenergy equals one-third of the average of the nucleon and Delta masses.
- The ratio g_s(m_s)/g_s(0) = 1/1.26 was determined by fitting octet and decuplet baryon masses (theoretical masses: N 939, Delta 1232, Lambda 1116, Sigma 1182, Xi 1333, Sigma* 1376, Xi* 1527, Omega 1683 MeV).
- The relativistic model predicts the d* (IJ=03) state to be at least 15 MeV below the NN pi pi threshold, even taking the highest estimated mass.
- For the alpha = (-600) state, the two versions of the relativistic model (Model A and Model B) differ by 140 MeV; for all other states the difference is less than ±60 MeV.
- The relativistic model gives the H particle about 60 MeV below the Lambda Lambda threshold.
- The nonrelativistic model finds the H particle unbound by 35 MeV.
- The estimated production cross section for pi^± d -> pi^± d* is of order 0.1 microbarn.

## Context
This work extends the nonrelativistic quark delocalization color screening model (QDCSM) developed by the same group (Goldman, Maltman, Stephenson, Ping, Wang, 1990s) to a fully relativistic framework, checking whether dibaryon candidates predicted by the nonrelativistic model are robust against relativistic effects. The relativistic model yields dibaryon masses quite similar to the nonrelativistic model across the u, d, s three-flavor world, which the authors take as mutual confirmation of stability to model details. The H particle candidate was originally predicted by Jaffe (1977); the present work notes that experimental results on doubly strange hypernuclei cast doubt on its existence as a bound state. The d* (IJ=03) state is compared to a similar state obtained in a meson exchange model with small hard-core radius and large NN rho coupling (reference in text). The paper identifies specific reactions for experimental searches, including a proton-induced reaction proposed at TRIUMF (Canada, spokesman S. Yen for E772), and notes that pion beams of the required energy range and intensity are not currently available at any accelerator facility.
