# Superallowed Fermi beta decay and Coulomb mixing in nuclei

> Superallowed $0^+ \to 0^+$ nuclear beta decay provides a direct measure of the weak vector coupling constant, $\GV$. We survey current world data on the nine accurately determined transitions of this type, which range from the decay of $^{10}$C to that of $^{54}$Co, and demonstrate that the results confirm conservation of the weak vector current (CVC) but differ at the 98% confidence level from the unitarity condition for the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa (CKM) matrix. We examine the reliability of the small calculated corrections that have been applied to the data, and conclude that there are no evident defects although the Coulomb correction, $δ_C$, depends sensitively on nuclear structure and thus needs to be constrained independently. The potential importance of a result in disagreement with unitarity, clearly indicates the need for further work to confirm or deny the discrepancy. We examine the options and recommend priorities for new experiments and improved calculations. Some of the required experiments depend upon the availability of intense radioactive beams. Others are possible with existing facilities.

## Key claim
The world data on nine superallowed $0^+ \to 0^+$ beta decays confirm the conserved vector current (CVC) hypothesis but yield a CKM unitarity sum of $0.9968 \pm 0.0014$, which differs from unity at the 98% confidence level, a discrepancy that could indicate physics beyond the Standard Model or, alternatively, undiagnosed inadequacies in the calculated radiative or Coulomb corrections.

## Method
The authors surveyed world data on nine accurately measured superallowed Fermi transitions (from $^{10}$C to $^{54}$Co), combining experimental $Q$-values, half-lives, and branching ratios from over 100 independent measurements. They applied three calculated correction terms, the nucleus-dependent radiative correction $\delta_R$, the nucleus-independent radiative correction $\Delta_R^V$, and the isospin-symmetry-breaking Coulomb correction $\delta_C$, to extract corrected $\mathcal{F}t$-values, then derived $V_{ud}$ and tested CKM unitarity. For $\delta_C$, they averaged results from two independent shell-model calculations (Towner-Hardy-Harvey and Ormand-Brown) and tested the configuration-mixing component $\delta_{C1}$ against experimental branching ratios for non-analogue Fermi transitions to excited $0^+$ states in five nuclei.

## Key numbers
- Average corrected $\mathcal{F}t$-value: $3072.3 \pm 2.0$ s (statistical error $\pm 0.9$ s, systematic error $\pm 1.1$ s added linearly).
- $V_{ud} = 0.9740 \pm 0.0005$, derived using $\Delta_R^V = (2.40 \pm 0.08)\%$.
- CKM unitarity sum: $|V_{ud}|^2 + |V_{us}|^2 + |V_{ub}|^2 = 0.9968 \pm 0.0014$, differing from unity at the 98% confidence level.
- $\chi^2/\nu = 1.1$ for the nine $\mathcal{F}t$-values, verifying CVC at the level of $3 \times 10^{-4}$ fractional uncertainty.
- For $^{42}$Sc, the experimental branching ratio to the excited $0^+$ state is $59(14)$ ppm, yielding $\delta_{C1}^1 = 0.040(9)\%$, in agreement with the theoretical value $0.041\%$.
- For $^{54}$Co, the experimental branching ratio is $45(6)$ ppm, giving $\delta_{C1}^1 = 0.035(5)\%$, matching the theoretical $0.037\%$.
- The leading contributions to the $V_{ud}$ error bar come from $\Delta_R^V$ ($\pm 0.0004$) and $\delta_C$ ($\pm 0.0003$), while experiment alone would contribute only $\pm 0.0001$.

## Context
This work extends the analysis of superallowed Fermi decays previously surveyed by Hardy and Towner (1990) and Towner and Hardy (1995), and updates the treatment of Coulomb corrections using shell-model calculations by Towner, Hardy, and Harvey (1977) and by Ormand and Brown (1995). The paper demonstrates that experimental precision has outstripped theoretical corrections, making further improvements in the unitarity test dependent on refined calculations of $\Delta_R^V$ and $\delta_C$ rather than new measurements on the existing nine transitions. The results motivate independent experimental checks of $\delta_C$ via non-analogue Fermi transitions (as performed by Hagberg et al., 1994) and call for improved neutron-decay measurements and radioactive-beam experiments to expand the sample of well-measured superallowed emitters.
