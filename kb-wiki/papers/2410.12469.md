# Potential of constraining the Fifth Force Using the Earth as a Spin and Mass Source from space

> We explore the potential of conducting an experiment in a low Earth orbit spacecraft and using the Earth as a spin and mass source to constrain beyond-the-standard-model (BSM) long-range spin- and velocity-dependent interactions, which are mediated by the exchange of an ultralight $\left(m_{Z^{\prime}}<10^{-10}\text{eV}\right)$ or massless intermediate vector boson. The high speed of the low Earth orbit spacecraft can enhance the sensitivity to velocity-dependent interactions. The periodicity enables efficient extraction of signals from background noise, thereby improving the experiment's accuracy. Combining these advantages, we demonstrate theoretically that the novel Spacecraft-Earth model can improve existing bounds on these exotic interactions by up to three orders of magnitude, using the China Space Station (CSS) as a representative low-Earth-orbit carrier. Such a model, if successfully implemented, may provide an innovative strategy for detecting ultralight dark matter and yield tighter constraints on certain coupling constants of exotic interactions.

## Key claim

The authors theoretically demonstrate that a low-Earth-orbit spacecraft, using the Earth as a spin and mass source, could improve existing bounds on exotic long-range spin- and velocity-dependent interactions by up to three orders of magnitude, assuming an arbitrary energy sensitivity of \(10^{-20}\) eV for both ground-based and space-based schemes to isolate the improvement from the model itself.

## Method

The authors model the Earth as a source of polarized geoelectrons and unpolarized nucleons, integrating the exotic interaction potentials over the Earth's volume from the core-mantle boundary to the surface for electron-electron interactions, and from the center to the surface for electron-nucleon interactions. They use the China Space Station (CSS) as a representative low-Earth-orbit carrier, with its orbital velocity of about 7.67 km/s and a 90-minute orbital period, and compute the total potential by summing the contributions from each source particle using the relative velocity between the spacecraft and the Earth's rotating source. The expected signals are simulated for six velocity-dependent spin-spin interactions and two spin-velocity interactions, with the Earth's geomagnetic field described by the World Magnetic Model (WMM 2020) and the geoelectron density based on the assumption that Earth's paramagnetism is dominated by unpaired d-shell electrons in iron ions.

## Key numbers

- The nominal orbital velocity of the CSS is about 7.67 km/s, which is more than 20 times the relative speed achievable in ground-based experiments.
- For the interaction \(V_6\) at \(\lambda = 10^{2.5}\) km, the new model yields a bound approximately 2.4 orders of magnitude more restrictive than ground-based schemes.
- For \(V_8\), \(V_{14}\), and \(V_{16}\) at \(\lambda = 10^{2.5}\) km, the constraints are improved by up to 5, 3, and 4 orders of magnitude, respectively.
- For the spin-velocity terms \(\left.V_{4+5}\right|_{V V}\) and \(\left.V_{4+5}\right|_{A A}\) at \(\lambda = 10^{2.5}\) km, the improvement is up to 2.5 orders of magnitude.
- For the odd-parity spin-velocity term \(V_{12+13}\), the improvement is 2 orders of magnitude at \(\lambda = 10^{2.5}\) km.

## Context

This work extends the approach of using the Earth as a massive spin and mass source, previously employed by Hunter et al. (2013) and Heckel et al. (2008) in ground-based experiments, by placing the sensor on a low-Earth-orbit spacecraft to overcome the limitations of fixed positions and low relative velocities. The proposed Spacecraft-Earth model enables periodic signal extraction and the identification of optimal detection regions, which could provide a new strategy for constraining ultralight dark matter candidates and distinguishing between different types of exotic interactions mediated by spin-0 axion-like particles or spin-1 bosons.
