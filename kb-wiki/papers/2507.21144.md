# Hadron Production Processes

> The experimental search for the pion -- proposed in 1935 by Hideki Yukawa as the force carrier of the strong nucleon-nucleon interaction -- was rewarded in 1947 when in cosmic ray photographic emulsion data a charged particle was identified with the proper mass of about 300 times the electron mass, completed three years later by the discovery of the neutral pion. Since then, accelerator-driven pion and meson (photo-)production on the nucleon and the associated production of new baryons have become the key elements for ground-breaking discoveries in numerous areas of particle and nuclear physics, from fundamental symmetries and their breaking to low-energy QCD dynamics, laying also foundations for modern elementary particle physics and the Standard Model. This article is an overview of eight decades of experimental and theoretical meson production physics, from isospin to charm and beyond, forming our understanding of hadrons and their interactions.

## Key claim

This paper provides a broad historical and conceptual overview of eight decades of experimental and theoretical meson production physics, from the 1947 discovery of the charged pion (mass about 300 times the electron mass) through to contemporary studies of charm and beyond, without presenting any new experimental data or original theoretical calculations.

## Method

The authors review the evolution of hadron production processes, covering pion and meson (photo-)production on nucleons and the associated production of new baryons. They trace the development from early cosmic-ray emulsion experiments through accelerator-based studies, discussing how these measurements have informed understanding of fundamental symmetries, symmetry breaking, and low-energy QCD dynamics. The paper is structured as a narrative survey rather than a report of new measurements or calculations.

## Key numbers

- The charged pion was identified in 1947 with a mass of about 300 times the electron mass, as reported in cosmic ray photographic emulsion data by Lattes, Occhialini, and Powell (1947).
- The neutral pion was discovered three years later, in 1950.
- The Omega hyperon (strangeness minus three) was observed by Barnes et al. (1964) with a mass of 1520 MeV, and the Lambda(1520) resonance was later studied via high-precision electroproduction by Qiang et al. (2011).
- The J/psi resonance was discovered in 1974 by the E598 and SLAC-SP-017 collaborations, and the Upsilon resonance at 9.5 GeV was observed in 400 GeV proton-nucleus collisions by the E288 collaboration (1977).
- The top quark was observed in 1995 by the CDF and D0 collaborations in antiproton-proton collisions.
- CP violation in charm decays was observed by LHCb in 2019, and charge-parity symmetry breaking in baryon decays was reported by LHCb in 2025.

## Context

This review extends the historical narrative of meson production from the foundational work of Yukawa (1935) and the experimental discovery of the pion by Lattes, Occhialini, and Powell (1947) through to modern results from LHCb (2019, 2025) on CP violation. It connects the early cosmic-ray and cyclotron-based studies (Gardner and Lattes, 1948) to contemporary topics such as hadronic molecules and multiquark states (Hanhart, 2025), lattice QCD calculations of hadron spectroscopy (Durr et al., 2008; Fodor and Hoelbling, 2012), and coupled-channel formalisms (Oller, 2025). The paper enables a unified perspective on how meson production experiments have shaped the Standard Model and continue to inform open questions in low-energy QCD and hadron structure.
