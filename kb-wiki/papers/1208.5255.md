# Neutrino oscillations in the front form of Hamiltonian dynamics

> Since future, precise theory of neutrino oscillations should include the understanding of the neutrino mass generation and a precise, relativistic description of hadrons, and observing that such a future theory may require Dirac's FF of Hamiltonian dynamics, we provide a preliminary FF description of neutrino oscillations using the Feynman--Gell-Mann-Levy version of an effective theory in which leptons interact directly with whole nucleons and pions, instead of with quarks via intermediate bosons. The interactions are treated in the lowest-order perturbative expansion in the coupling constants in the effective theory, including a perturbative solution of the coupled constraint equations. Despite missing quarks and their binding mechanism, the effective Hamiltonian description is sufficiently precise for showing that the standard oscillation formula results from the interference of amplitudes with different neutrinos in virtual intermediate states. This holds provided that the inherent experimental uncertainties of preparing beams of incoming and measuring rates of production of outgoing particles are large enough for all of the different neutrino intermediate states to contribute as alternative virtual paths through which the long-baseline scattering process can manifest itself. The result that an approximate, effective FF theory reproduces the standard oscillation formula at the level of transition rates for currently considered long-baseline experiments--even though the space-time development of scattering is traced differently and the relevant interaction Hamiltonians are constructed differently than in the commonly used IF of dynamics--has two implications. It shows that the common interpretation of experimental results is not the only one, and it opens the possibility of considering more precise theories taking advantage of the features of the FF that are not available in the IF.

## Key claim

The standard neutrino oscillation formula can be reproduced using Dirac's front form (FF) of Hamiltonian dynamics in an effective theory where leptons interact directly with nucleons and pions, provided that experimental uncertainties in beam preparation and particle detection are large enough for all different neutrino intermediate states to contribute as alternative virtual paths in the long-baseline scattering process.

## Method

The authors constructed a FF Hamiltonian description of neutrino oscillations using the Feynman-Gell-Mann-Levy effective Lagrangian density, in which muon-neutrinos couple to protons, neutrons, pions, and muons via effective four-fermion interactions. They treated the interactions in lowest-order perturbative expansion in the coupling constants \(G_F\) and \(F_\pi\), including a perturbative solution of the coupled constraint equations specific to the FF, and applied the Gell-Mann-Goldberger scattering theory extended to long-baseline experiments to calculate transition rates for the process \(\pi^+ n \rightarrow p \mu \bar\mu\).

## Key numbers

- The long-baseline distance \(L\) between Tokay and Kamioka in the T2K experiment is approximately 300 km.
- The neutrino energies and momenta in T2K are on the order of 1 GeV.
- The upper limit on the FF beam-preparation time \(\tau^+\) corresponds to a pion decay tunnel length of about 100 m, giving \(\epsilon^- = 2/\tau^+\) on the order of at least \(10^{-9}\) eV.
- The differences between FF neutrino "energies" \(p^-_{\nu_i}\) for neutrinos with masses squared on the order of \(10^{-(3 \div 7)}\) eV\(^2\) divided by \(p_\nu^+ \sim 1\) GeV are on the order of \(10^{-(12 \div 16)}\) eV, much smaller than \(\epsilon^- \sim 10^{-9}\) eV.

## Context

This work extends the instant form (IF) description of neutrino oscillations, previously developed using the Gell-Mann-Goldberger scattering theory by Glazek (2012), to Dirac's front form of Hamiltonian dynamics. It uses the same effective Lagrangian density as Feynman, Gell-Mann, and Levy (1958, 1960) that was also employed in the IF analysis. The result shows that the common interpretation of experimental results is not unique and opens the possibility of developing more precise theories that exploit features of the FF not available in the IF, particularly for future theories that must include neutrino mass generation and a precise relativistic description of hadrons.
