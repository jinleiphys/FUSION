# Cooper-Frye Negative Contributions in a Coarse-Grained Transport Approach

> Many models of heavy ion collisions employ relativistic hydrodynamics to describe the system evolution at high densities. The Cooper-Frye formula is applied in most of these models to turn the hydrodynamical fields into particles. However, the number of particles obtained from the Cooper-Frye formula is not always positive-definite. Physically negative contributions of the Cooper-Frye formula are particles that stream backwards into the hydrodynamical region. We quantify the Cooper-Frye negative contributions in a coarse-grained transport approach, which allows to compare them to the actual number of underlying particles crossing the transition hypersurface. It is found that the number of underlying inward crossings is much smaller than the one the Cooper-Frye formula gives under the assumption of equilibrium distribution functions. The magnitude of Cooper-Frye negative contributions is also investigated as a function of hadron mass, collision energy in the range $E_{\rm lab} = 5-160A$ GeV, and collision centrality. The largest negative contributions we find are around 13% for the pion yield at midrapidity at $E_{\rm lab} = 20A$ GeV collisions.

# Cooper-Frye Negative Contributions in a Coarse-Grained Transport Approach

## Key claim

The Cooper-Frye formula's negative contributions (particles that would stream backwards into the hydrodynamic region) are much smaller in actual transport simulations than the equilibrium Cooper-Frye calculation predicts, with the largest negative contributions reaching about 13% for pions at midrapidity at 20A GeV; however, the underlying number of actual inward particle crossings in the transport approach is only about 4% at that same energy, indicating that the equilibrium assumption overestimates the problem.

## Method

The authors used the UrQMD transport model (version 3.3p2) to simulate Au+Au collisions at laboratory energies from 5 to 160A GeV, then coarse-grained the events onto a rectangular grid to extract the energy-momentum tensor and baryon current. From these fields they constructed a hypersurface of constant energy density (0.3 GeV/fm^3) in the Landau rest frame using the Cornelius subroutine, and compared the Cooper-Frye particle spectra (assuming equilibrium Bose/Fermi distributions) on that surface to the actual distributions of UrQMD particles crossing the same surface, both inward and outward.

## Key numbers

- Largest Cooper-Frye negative contribution: approximately 13% of the positive contribution for pion yield at midrapidity at Elab = 20A GeV in central Au+Au collisions
- Actual UrQMD inward crossing fraction at the same energy and conditions: approximately 4% of outward crossings
- Collision energy range studied: Elab = 5–160A GeV
- Grid spacing used: Δt = 0.1 fm/c, Δz = 0.3 fm, Δx = Δy = 1 fm (with finer spacing Δz = 0.1 fm, Δx = Δy = 0.3 fm at 160A GeV)
- Number of events per calculation: N = 1500, Gaussian smearing width σ = 1 fm
- Conservation laws on the hypersurface fulfilled to better than 1% accuracy
- Energy density threshold for hypersurface: ε = 0.3 GeV/fm^3 (also tested at 0.6 GeV/fm^3)

## Context

This work extends earlier findings by Petersen and Huovinen (2012) who reported negative contributions of around 9% at RHIC top energy and 13% at 160A GeV, by systematically investigating the energy dependence down to 5A GeV and comparing to actual transport backscattering. The results contradict the assumption that equilibrium Cooper-Frye negative contributions can be simply compensated by removing backscattered particles in hybrid hydrodynamics+transport models, as the inward crossing distributions in UrQMD differ substantially from the equilibrium negative contributions. This enables improved particlization procedures for hybrid models at lower collision energies, particularly relevant for the FAIR and NICA energy regimes.
