# Nuclear mass predictions based on convolutional neural network

> A convolutional neural network (CNN) is employed to investigate nuclear mass. By introducing the masses of neighboring nuclei and the paring effects at the input layer of the network, local features of the target nucleus are extracted to predict its mass. Then, through learning the differences between the experimental nuclear masses and the predicted nuclear masses by the WS4 model, a new global-local model (CNN-WS4) is developed, which incorporates both the global nuclear mass model and local features. Due to the incorporation of local features, the CNN-WS4 model achieves high accuracy on the training set. When extrapolating for newly emerged nuclei, the CNN-WS4 also exhibits appreciable stability, thereby demonstrating its robustness.

# Nuclear mass predictions based on convolutional neural network

## Key claim
By combining the WS4 global nuclear mass model with a convolutional neural network that extracts local features from neighboring nuclei, the authors achieve a root-mean-square error of 0.070 MeV on the training set, which they state is the highest accuracy among theoretical nuclear mass models. However, this accuracy applies only to nuclei with known experimental masses in the training region, and the extrapolation error for newly emerged nuclei in AME2020 is substantially larger at 0.211 MeV.

## Method
The authors construct a convolutional neural network with two convolutional layers (3x3 kernels, 72 kernels per layer, no pooling) and a fully connected output layer. Input data are organized as 5x5 matrices centered on the target nucleus, with channels for proton number, neutron number, masses of neighboring nuclei, and the nuclear pairing effect delta = [(-1)^N + (-1)^Z]/2. The network is trained to predict the difference between experimental masses (from AME2016 and AME2020) and masses predicted by the WS4 macroscopic-microscopic model, producing the combined CNN-WS4 model.

## Key numbers
- CNN-WS4 model achieves a root-mean-square error of 0.070 MeV on the training set of nuclei with Z >= 8 and N >= 8 from AME2016.
- For extrapolation to newly emerged nuclei in AME2020, the root-mean-square error is 0.211 MeV.
- The CNN-I4 model (without WS4) achieves an RMSE of 0.291 MeV on the training set, a 46% reduction from the CNN-I3 model's 0.535 MeV.
- For the superheavy nuclei region (Z >= 104), the CNN-WS4 model predicts masses with a deviation of 0.046 MeV from experimental values.
- In an 8:2 random train-test split of 3456 nuclei from AME2020, the training set RMSE is 0.095 MeV and the testing set RMSE is 0.171 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the application of machine learning to nuclear mass prediction, building on prior approaches such as the kernel ridge regression method of Wu et al. (2022) which achieved 128 keV accuracy, and the Bayesian machine learning model of Niu et al. (2022) which achieved 84 keV accuracy. The authors explicitly draw inspiration from the Garvey-Kelson local mass relation (Garvey and Kelson, 1966) for designing the 5x5 input matrix of neighboring nuclei. The CNN-WS4 model combines the global WS4 model (Wang et al., 2014) with local feature extraction, and the authors suggest that incorporating additional physical models into the network design could further improve stability and predictive reliability.
