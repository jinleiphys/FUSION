# Uniformly rotating neutron stars in the global and local charge neutrality cases

> In our previous treatment of neutron stars, we have developed the model fulfilling global and not local charge neutrality. In order to implement such a model, we have shown the essential role by the Thomas-Fermi equations, duly generalized to the case of electromagnetic field equations in a general relativistic framework, forming a coupled system of equations that we have denominated Einstein-Maxwell-Thomas-Fermi (EMTF) equations. From the microphysical point of view, the weak interactions are accounted for by requesting the β stability of the system, and the strong interactions by using the σ-ω-ρ nuclear model, where σ, ω and ρ are the mediator massive vector mesons. Here we examine the equilibrium configurations of slowly rotating neutron stars by using the Hartle formalism in the case of the EMTF equations indicated above. We integrate these equations of equilibrium for different central densities and circular angular velocities and compute the mass, polar and equatorial radii, angular momentum, eccentricity, moment of inertia, as well as quadrupole moment of the configurations. Both the Keplerian mass-shedding limit and the axisymmetric secular instability are used to construct the new mass-radius relation. We compute the maximum and minimum masses and rotation frequencies of neutron stars. We compare and contrast all the results for the global and local charge neutrality cases.

## Key claim

The paper extends the authors' previous Einstein-Maxwell-Thomas-Fermi (EMTF) model, which imposes global rather than local charge neutrality, to slowly rotating neutron stars using the Hartle formalism; the key qualifier is that the slow-rotation approximation remains valid because the fractional displacement of constant-density surfaces due to rotation is only about 2-3% even at the maximum rotation frequency.

## Method

The authors integrate the Hartle slow-rotation equations (accurate to second order in angular velocity) for neutron star configurations obtained from the EMTF equations, using the NL3 parameter set for the sigma-omega-rho nuclear model and imposing beta stability. They compute equilibrium sequences for different central densities and angular velocities, applying both the Keplerian mass-shedding limit and the secular axisymmetric instability criterion (via the turning-point method of Friedman, Ipser, and Sorkin) to define the stable region. The results for globally neutral stars (with crust density set to the neutron drip density of about 4.3e11 g cm^-3) are compared to locally neutral stars obtained from the traditional Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff approach.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of non-rotating globally neutral neutron star: 2.67 solar masses, with radius 12.38 km
- Maximum mass of non-rotating locally neutral neutron star: 2.70 solar masses, with radius 12.71 km
- Maximum mass of rotating globally neutral neutron star: 2.76 solar masses (3.37% increase over non-rotating)
- Maximum mass of rotating locally neutral neutron star: 2.79 solar masses (3.33% increase over non-rotating)
- Maximum rotation frequency for globally neutral stars: 1.97 kHz (minimum period 0.51 ms)
- Maximum rotation frequency for locally neutral stars: 1.89 kHz (minimum period 0.53 ms)
- Fractional equatorial radius increase at maximum rotation: 2.26% for globally neutral, 2.75% for locally neutral
- The induced magnetic field from rotation of the charged core is negligible: radial component at poles about 2.9e-16 times the critical magnetic field B_c (4.4e13 G), though the angular component can reach about 100 B_c in the core-crust transition layer

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous EMTF model for static neutron stars (Belvedere et al. 2012, Nuclear Physics A) to the rotating case, contrasting with the traditional TOV approach that imposes local charge neutrality (see e.g. Haensel, Potekhin, and Yakovlev 2007). The results enable more accurate modeling of rapidly rotating neutron stars with realistic microphysics, and the authors note that the Hartle second-order approximation is accurate to within about 4% for maximum mass (Weber and Glendenning 1992) and that including third-order terms improves maximum frequency by less than 1% (Benhar et al. 2005).
