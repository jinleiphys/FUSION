# Phase Transitions In Compact Stars

> We report on a three--month research project for undergraduate students about the mass-radius relation of compact stars. The equation of state used is constrained at low densities by well-established equations of state of the nuclear phase (the solid crust) and then extended to higher densities with a phenomenological, parametric approach. A first order phase transition from hadronic matter to a phase of higher density, assumed to be quark matter is studied in addition. The mass-radius relation is obtained by solving numerically the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation. We derive some conditions for the existence of a third family of compact stars on the form of the equation of state and its different global properties.

## Key claim

The existence of a third family of compact stars (beyond white dwarfs and neutron stars) depends on the equation of state having a sufficiently strong first-order phase transition, specifically requiring that the slope of the quark phase be higher than the slope at the end of the hadronic polytropic phase, with the critical polytropic exponent for the hadronic phase lying between 3.4 and 3.6 for the chosen parameters.

## Method

The authors constructed a parametric equation of state by matching the well-established Baym-Pethick-Sutherland (BPS) and Negele-Vautherin (NV) crust equations of state to a polytropic hadronic phase, a linear mixed phase, and a linear quark phase with slope 1/3 (MIT Bag Model). They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation numerically using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta algorithm to obtain mass-radius relations, varying the polytropic exponent Gamma, the onset density of the phase transition, the length of the mixed phase, and the slope of the mixed phase.

## Key numbers

- Minimum stable neutron star mass: 0.09 solar masses, with maximum radius approximately 260 km and central energy density about 0.52 times normal nuclear density (0.17 fm^-3 = 2.8e14 g/cm^3).
- Maximum polytropic exponent for a causal pure neutron star: Gamma_max = 2.92, giving a maximum mass of about 2.2 solar masses with radius 11 km; a 1.1 solar mass star would have radius about 13 km.
- Minimum polytropic exponent from the Hulse-Taylor pulsar mass constraint (1.44 solar masses): Gamma_min = 2.415, with the most massive star radius as small as 8 km and a 0.7 solar mass star radius about 10 km.
- For stars with a phase transition starting at 2 times normal nuclear density, the maximum allowed Gamma is 4.2.
- The third family of compact stars disappears for polytropic exponents between 3.4 and 3.6 when the mixed phase length is 3 times normal nuclear density and the mixed phase slope is zero.

## Context

This undergraduate research project extends the parametric approach of Kaempfer (1981, 1983) to study the third family of compact stars, which was first proposed by Gerlach (1968) and later investigated by Heintzmann, Hillebrandt and coworkers (1974). The work uses a simplified phenomenological equation of state to map conditions for a third stable branch in the mass-radius relation, complementing more detailed relativistic field theory calculations by Glendenning (2000), Schertler (2000), and others that use Gibbs criteria for phase transitions to quark matter, hyperon matter, or kaon condensed matter. The results enable systematic exploration of which parameter combinations allow a third family, providing guidance for future observational searches to distinguish neutron stars, quark stars, and black holes.
