# Shell evolution and its indication on the isospin dependence of the spin-orbit splitting

> The available experimental data on shell evolution indicate that the strength of the spin-orbit (SO) single-particle potential may be enhanced in neutron-rich nuclei. We observe that such a simple scheme destroys the Harmonic Oscillator (HO) magic numbers N=8 and 20 and generates new SO magic numbers like N=6, 14, 16, 32 and 34. The traditional SO magic numbers like N=28 and 50 and N=14 seen in $^{22}$O are eroded somehow in neutron-rich nuclei due to the sensitivity of larger-$l$ orbitals to the depth of the central potential but they are more robust than the HO magic numbers. The N=82 shell closure persists in neutron-rich nuclei while HO shell closures like N=40 and 70 do not emerge. Both mechanisms contribute to enhancing the N=56 and 90 gaps by splitting the $1d_{5/2}$ and $0g_{7/2}$ and the $0h_{9/2}$ and $1f_{7/2}$ orbitals.

# Shell evolution and its indication on the isospin dependence of the spin-orbit splitting

## Key claim

The strength of the spin-orbit single-particle potential may be enhanced in neutron-rich nuclei (positive isospin-dependence parameter κ_SO), which would destroy the harmonic oscillator magic numbers N=8 and 20 and generate new spin-orbit magic numbers like N=6, 14, 16, 32 and 34, while traditional spin-orbit magic numbers like N=28 and 50 are eroded but more robust than the harmonic oscillator ones.

## Method

The authors performed systematic calculations of single-particle energies using both a harmonic oscillator potential with an isospin-dependent spin-orbit coupling term of the form λ(1+κ_SO(N-Z)/A)ħω l·s and a Woods-Saxon potential with standard and re-fitted parameters, taking κ_SO = κ (standard isospin dependence) or κ_SO = -κ (enhanced spin-orbit in neutron-rich nuclei). They also evaluated potential energy surfaces for selected neutron-rich nuclei using the Woods-Saxon code GAMOW and solved the Woods-Saxon plus pairing Hamiltonian with a Lanczos diagonalization approach for Ca isotopes.

## Key numbers

- The N=20 gap in ²⁸O is 4.2 MeV with standard Woods-Saxon parameters but reduces to 1.2 MeV with κ_SO = -κ.
- The distance between the 0h₁₁/₂ and 1f₇/₂ orbitals decreases from 4.1 MeV in ¹³²Sn to 0.65 MeV in ¹¹⁰Ni with standard parameters, but increases to 2.5 MeV with κ_SO = -κ.
- The N=40 shell gap in ⁶⁰Ca is 4.4 MeV with standard parameters versus 0.43 MeV with κ_SO = -κ; the N=50 gap in ⁷⁰Ca is 0.67 MeV with standard parameters versus 2.3 MeV with κ_SO = -κ.
- The N=16 gap in ²⁴O is measured as 4.86±0.13 MeV; calculations give 3.3 MeV with κ_SO = κ and 4.5 MeV with κ_SO = -κ.
- The N=14 gap in ²⁰O increases from 1.8 MeV (κ_SO = κ) to 2.7 MeV (κ_SO = -κ).
- The N=32 gap in ⁵²Ca is 1.8 MeV with standard parameters and 2.3 MeV with κ_SO = -κ.
- The N=34 gap in ⁵⁴Ca is 0.92 MeV with standard parameters and 1.7 MeV with κ_SO = -κ.
- The ³²Mg ground state is calculated with β₂=0.38 and γ=4.8° using the re-fitted parameter with κ_SO = -κ.

## Context

This work extends the phenomenological Woods-Saxon analysis of shell evolution in neutron-rich nuclei, building on the earlier studies of Bohr and Mottelson (1969) and the standard parameterization of Blomqvist and Wahlborn (1960). It contrasts with the common assumption that the spin-orbit potential has the same isospin dependence as the central potential (κ_SO = κ), and instead supports the possibility of an opposite sign (κ_SO positive) as suggested by Isakov et al. (2002) and consistent with Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations including tensor forces by Zalewski et al. (2009). The results enable a simple phenomenological starting point for shell model calculations in the continuum and for exploring the effects of pairing and deformation on shell structure in neutron-rich nuclei.
