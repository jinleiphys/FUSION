# Time-reversal symmetric resolution of unity without background integrals in open quantum systems

> We present a new complete set of states for a class of open quantum systems, to be used in expansion of the Green's function and the time-evolution operator. A remarkable feature of the complete set is that it observes time-reversal symmetry in the sense that it contains decaying states (resonant states) and growing states (anti-resonant states) parallelly. We can thereby pinpoint the occurrence of the breaking of time-reversal symmetry at the choice of whether we solve Schroedinger equation as an initial-condition problem or a terminal-condition problem. Another feature of the complete set is that in the subspace of the central scattering area of the system, it consists of contributions of all states with point spectra but does not contain any background integrals. In computing the time evolution, we can clearly see contribution of which point spectrum produces which time dependence. In the whole infinite state space, the complete set does contain an integral but it is over unperturbed eigenstates of the environmental area of the system and hence can be calculated analytically. We demonstrate the usefulness of the complete set by computing explicitly the survival probability and the escaping probability as well as the dynamics of wave packets. The origin of each term of matrix elements is clear in our formulation, particularly the exponential decays due to the resonance poles.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new complete set of states for a class of open quantum systems (tight-binding models with semi-infinite leads) that includes both decaying resonant states and growing anti-resonant states in parallel, thereby maintaining explicit time-reversal symmetry in the expansion until the final choice of solving an initial-condition or terminal-condition problem. The key advance is that in the subspace of the central scattering region (the "dot"), the expansion contains contributions from all point-spectrum states with no background integral, unlike previous contour-deformation approaches.

## Method

The authors map the infinite-dimensional eigenvalue problem of the full Hamiltonian onto a quadratic eigenvalue problem in the N-dimensional dot subspace using the Feshbach effective Hamiltonian formalism. They then convert this into a generalized linear eigenvalue problem in a 2N-dimensional space, whose solutions yield all 2N discrete eigenstates (bound, anti-bound, resonant, and anti-resonant states). The resulting resolution of unity in the dot subspace is a sum over these 2N biorthogonal left and right eigenvectors, with no background integral; the full-space resolution of unity adds an integral over unperturbed lead eigenstates that can be evaluated analytically.

## Key numbers

* The effective Hamiltonian in the dot subspace is an N x N matrix, where N is the number of dot sites; the system generally has 2N discrete eigenstates with point spectra.
* The dispersion relation on the leads is E = -2 cos k, with the hopping amplitude on the leads set to t_lead = 1 as the unit of energy.
* The self-energy for each lead attached to dot site i is Sigma_{i alpha}(E) = -(t_{i alpha})^2 e^{ik} |d_i><d_i| = -(t_{i alpha})^2 lambda |d_i><d_i|, where lambda = e^{ik}.
* The wave function on a lead site x_{i alpha} for a discrete eigenstate is given by <x_{i alpha}|psi_n> = t_{i alpha} lambda_n^{x_{i alpha}} <d_i|psi_n>, where lambda_n = e^{ik_n}.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Klaiman (2011) for one-dimensional open quantum systems to a general class of tight-binding models with arbitrary dot-lead configurations. It contrasts with the conventional contour-deformation method of Berggren (1968, 1970, 1996), which arbitrarily splits the continuum integral into selected resonant-state contributions and a background integral, thereby breaking time-reversal symmetry. The new formulation enables clear identification of which point spectrum contributes to each term in the time evolution of survival probability, escaping probability, and wave-packet dynamics, and can be connected to the Landauer formula for electric conductance via the transmission coefficient T_{i alpha, j beta}(E) = (t_{i alpha} t_{j beta})^2 sin^2 k |<d_i|G(E)|d_j>|^2.
