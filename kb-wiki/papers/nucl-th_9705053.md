# About the determination of critical exponents related to possible phase transitions in nuclear fragmentation

> We introduce a method based on the finite size scaling assumption which allows to determine numerically the critical point and critical exponents related to observables in an infinite system starting from the knowledge of the observables in finite systems. We apply the method to bond percolation in 2 dimensions and compare the results obtained when the bond probability p or the fragment multiplicity m are chosen as the relevant parameter.

## Key claim

The finite-size scaling (FSS) method can determine critical exponents for infinite systems from finite-size data, but the extracted exponents depend on whether the bond probability \(p\) or the fragment multiplicity \(m\) is used as the relevant parameter; the exponents obtained with \(m\) are not numerically compatible with those obtained with \(p\), contradicting earlier claims.

## Method

The authors apply the finite-size scaling assumption to 2D bond percolation on a square lattice without periodic boundary conditions, computing the correlation length \(\xi_L\), the normalized second moment \(m_{2L}/L^2\), and the ratio \(m_{2L}/m_{1L}\) for lattice sizes \(L\) up to 1000. For each value of the bond probability \(p\) (in steps of 0.01 over [0.40, 0.48]) they generate \(10^4\) events; for calculations with the reduced multiplicity \(m\) they generate 2500 events per \(m\) value. The asymptotic values of the observables are used to construct universal scaling functions \(Q_O(x)\), which are then fitted to power laws to extract the critical point and exponents \(\nu\), \(\gamma\), and \(\sigma\) (or \(\tilde\nu\), \(\tilde\gamma\), \(\tilde\sigma\) for the multiplicity case).

## Key numbers

- Exact reference values for 2D bond percolation: \(p_c = 0.5\), \(\gamma = 2.389\), \(\nu = 1.333\), \(\sigma = 0.396\).
- For \(p\) as the parameter with open boundaries at \(L=120\): \(p_c = 0.501(3)\), \(\gamma = 2.34(7)\), \(\nu = 1.35(3)\), \(\sigma = 0.42(3)\).
- For \(p\) as the parameter with periodic boundaries at \(L=120\): \(p_c = 0.500(2)\), \(\gamma = 2.28(8)\), \(\nu = 1.35(4)\), \(\sigma = 0.44(3)\).
- For \(m\) as the parameter with open boundaries at \(L=120\): \(m_c = 0.098(3)\), \(\tilde\gamma = 1.98(4)\), \(\tilde\nu = 1.17(3)\), \(\tilde\sigma = 0.50(2)\).
- For \(m\) as the parameter with periodic boundaries at \(L=120\): \(m_c = 0.102(3)\), \(\tilde\gamma = 1.97(5)\), \(\tilde\nu = 1.09(5)\), \(\tilde\sigma = 0.51(3)\).

## Context

This work extends the FSS method previously tested on the 2D and 3D Ising model by Kim, de Souza, and Landau (1996) to the 2D bond percolation problem, which serves as an academic test case for nuclear fragmentation. The results contradict the findings of Elliott et al. (1994, 1997) and Gilkes et al. (1994), who claimed that exponents extracted using the multiplicity parameter are compatible with percolation exponents. The method enables a rigorous path to extract critical exponents from finite nuclear systems, but the authors note that nuclei are too small (300-400 constituents) to reach the asymptotic regime, and that experimental application would require a model that reproduces finite-size observables and is then extrapolated to infinite size.
