# Temperature dependence of shear viscosity of $SU(3)$--gluodynamics within lattice simulation

> In this paper we study the shear viscosity temperature dependence of $SU(3)$--gluodynamics within lattice simulation. To do so, we measure the correlation functions of energy-momentum tensor in the range of temperatures $T/T_c\in [0.9, 1.5]$. To extract the values of shear viscosity we used two approaches. The first one is to fit the lattice data with some physically motivated ansatz for the spectral function with unknown parameters and then determine shear viscosity. The second approach is to apply the Backus-Gilbert method which allows to extract shear viscosity from the lattice data nonparametrically. The results obtained within both approaches agree with each other. Our results allow us to conclude that within the temperature range $T/T_c \in [0.9, 1.5]$ SU(3)--gluodynamics reveals the properties of a strongly interacting system, which cannot be described perturbatively, and has the ratio $η/s$ close to the value ${1}/{4π}$ in $N = 4$ Supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory.

## Key claim
The shear viscosity-to-entropy-density ratio η/s of SU(3)-gluodynamics in the temperature range T/Tc ∈ [0.9, 1.5] is close to the value 1/(4π) from N = 4 Supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory, indicating a strongly interacting system that cannot be described perturbatively; however, the results depend on the choice of spectral function ansatz and the subtraction of ultraviolet contributions, with the strongest systematic uncertainty at the lowest temperature T/Tc = 0.9.

## Method
The authors performed lattice simulations of SU(3)-gluodynamics using the Wilson gauge action on a 16×32³ lattice at eight values of the gauge coupling β corresponding to temperatures T/Tc ≈ 0.9, 0.925, 0.95, 1.0, 1.1, 1.2, 1.35, 1.5. They measured Euclidean correlation functions of the energy-momentum tensor using a clover discretization scheme and a two-level algorithm to achieve high precision. Shear viscosity was extracted via two approaches: (1) fitting the lattice data with physically motivated ansatzes for the spectral function (ρ₁, ρ₂, ρ₃) that combine a hydrodynamic low-frequency part with a perturbative high-frequency part, and (2) applying the nonparametric Backus-Gilbert method to reconstruct the spectral function, with ultraviolet subtraction using the fitted high-frequency parameters.

## Key numbers
- The ratio η/s obtained from the ρ₃ ansatz (including all uncertainties) ranges from 0.57(25) at T/Tc = 0.90 to 0.27(7) at T/Tc = 1.50, with a minimum of 0.15(5) at T/Tc = 1.1.
- At T/Tc = 1.0, the η/s values from different ansatzes are 0.23(3) (ρ₁), 0.21(5) (ρ₂), and 0.24(8) (ρ₃).
- The threshold parameter ω₀/T ranges from 7.0(7) at T/Tc = 1.1 to 9.0(4) at T/Tc = 1.5 across all ansatzes.
- The ultraviolet factor A ranges from 0.818(7) at T/Tc = 1.2 to 1.109(3) at T/Tc = 0.90.
- The correlation functions were measured with uncertainties not larger than 2-3% at the distance T x₀ = 0.5 for all temperatures.
- The entropy density uncertainties are 4-6% for all temperatures; renormalization coefficient uncertainties are ~3% for T/Tc ≥ 1.0, ~6% for T/Tc = 0.925 and 0.95, and ~12% for T/Tc = 0.9.

## Context
This work extends previous lattice studies of shear viscosity in SU(3)-gluodynamics by Karsch (1986), Nakamura (2004), Meyer (2007, 2009), and Mages (2015), as well as SU(2)-gluodynamics studies by Braguta (2013) and Astrakhantsev (2015). The results confirm that SU(3)-gluodynamics near the deconfinement transition behaves as a strongly interacting system with η/s close to the conjectured lower bound 1/(4π) from N = 4 Supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory (Policastro, 2001), in contrast to weak-coupling calculations (Arnold, 2000, 2003) which give η/s ∼ 1. The temperature dependence shows a rapid drop of η/s approaching Tc from above, followed by a slow rise or constant behavior, consistent with various QCD models. This work enables future lattice calculations of shear viscosity in full QCD with dynamical quarks by establishing methodology for handling the ultraviolet subtraction and systematic uncertainties.
