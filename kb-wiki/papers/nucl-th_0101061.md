# Non equilibrium effects in fragmentation

> We study, using molecular dynamics techniques, how boundary conditions affect the process of fragmentation of finite, highly excited, Lennard-Jones systems. We analyze the behavior of the caloric curves (CC), the associated thermal response functions (TRF) and cluster mass distributions for constrained and unconstrained hot drops. It is shown that the resulting CC's for the constrained case differ from the one in the unconstrained case, mainly in the presence of a ``vapor branch''. This branch is absent in the free expanding case even at high energies . This effect is traced to the role played by the collective expansion motion. On the other hand, we found that the recently proposed characteristic features of a first order phase transition taking place in a finite isolated system, i.e. abnormally large kinetic energy fluctuations and a negative branch in the TRF, are present for the constrained (dilute) as well the unconstrained case. The microscopic origin of this behavior is also analyzed.

## Key claim

The caloric curve for a freely expanding Lennard-Jones system lacks a "vapor branch" that is present when the same system is confined by a spherical wall, because the collective expansion motion in the unconstrained case acts as a heat sink that prevents the system from reaching global thermal equilibrium. The characteristic signatures of a first-order phase transition (negative branch in the thermal response function and abnormally large kinetic energy fluctuations) appear in both constrained and unconstrained cases, but only at sufficiently low densities for the constrained system.

## Method

Molecular dynamics simulations were performed for 147-particle Lennard-Jones drops with a 6-12 potential cut off at 3σ, using the Verlet algorithm with a time step of 0.002t₀. For the unconstrained case, the system was allowed to expand freely; for the constrained case, a spherical repulsive wall potential (∼r⁻¹²) was imposed at various radii. Fragment recognition used both the minimum spanning tree (MST) method based on spatial proximity (r_cl = 3σ) and the Early Cluster Recognition Algorithm (ECRA), which finds the most bound density fluctuation in phase space by minimizing the sum of fragment internal energies.

## Key numbers

- System size: 147 particles interacting via Lennard-Jones potential with cut-off radius r_c = 3σ
- Integration time step: t_int = 0.002 t₀, where t₀ = √(σ²m/48ε)
- For the constrained case, microcanonical sampling was performed every 5 t₀ up to a final time of 140,000 t₀
- Two reference freeze-out densities were identified: ρ_fo⁺ ∼ ρ₀/10 (r_wall ∼ 8σ) and ρ_fo⁻ ∼ ρ₀/40 (r_wall = 12σ), where ρ₀ is the equilibrium density
- Collective motion becomes dominant at around E = 2.0 ε (in units of the potential well depth ε)
- The onset of fragmentation occurs in the energy range -2ε ≤ E ≤ 0ε
- For the unconstrained case, the local density at fragmentation time for inner cells remains rather constant at ρ ∼ 0.08 σ⁻³
- For the constrained case at ρ = ρ_fo⁻, the maximum MST-fragment size decreases with increasing energy; for ρ = ρ_fo⁺, the maximum MST-fragment comprises more than 95% of the total mass irrespective of energy

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous molecular dynamics studies of Lennard-Jones fragmentation (Strachan and Dorso, 1997) by systematically comparing constrained and unconstrained boundary conditions. It directly challenges the assumption of global equilibrium used in statistical models such as the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) (Bondorf et al., 1995) and the microcanonical Metropolis Monte Carlo model (Gross, 1997), which employ a freeze-out volume concept. The results enable a critical reexamination of caloric curves extracted from nuclear multifragmentation experiments, particularly those showing vapor branches, and support the need for local equilibrium descriptions that account for radial collective flow rather than global equilibrium assumptions.
