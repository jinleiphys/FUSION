# Exact solutions for pairing interactions

> The exact solution of the BCS pairing Hamiltonian was found by Richardson in 1963. While little attention was paid to this exactly solvable model in the remainder of the 20th century, there was a burst of work at the beginning of this century focusing on its applications in different areas of quantum physics. We review the history of this exact solution and discuss recent developments related to the Richardson-Gaudin class of integrable models, focussing on the role of these various models in nuclear physics.

# Exact solutions for pairing interactions

## Key claim
This review argues that the Richardson exact solution of the BCS pairing Hamiltonian, largely ignored for decades after its 1963 discovery, has been revived since the early 2000s and can now be applied to realistic nuclear structure problems, but only for Hamiltonians restricted to the pairing degree of freedom, without explicit treatment of deformation effects.

## Method
The authors review the Richardson solution for the reduced BCS Hamiltonian with constant pairing strength G acting in a space of doubly-degenerate time-reversed states, where the many-body wave function is constructed as a product of distinct Cooper-pair operators. They then generalize this to the Richardson-Gaudin class of integrable models based on SU(2) algebra, presenting both the rational family (which contains the standard pairing model) and the hyperbolic family, and discuss applications to nuclear structure using single-particle energies from deformed Hartree-Fock or Gogny HFB calculations.

## Key numbers
- For the semi-magic nucleus 144Sm, treating pairing exactly provides a modest increase in pairing correlations of about 0.3 MeV beyond number-projected BCS.
- In non-semi-magic nuclei, number-projected BCS misses about 1 MeV of the exact correlation energy that derives from the Richardson solution.
- For 154Sm, the Hilbert space dimension associated with the residual neutron pairing Hamiltonian is of order 3.9 × 10^53, whereas the exact Richardson approach requires solving a coupled set of 46 non-linear equations.
- For 238U, the hyperbolic model with parameters α = 25.25 MeV and G = 2 × 10^-3 MeV yields a valence space of 148 levels with 46 proton pairs, producing a Hamiltonian size of 4.83 × 10^38 states, solvable via 46 coupled non-linear equations.
- The exact solution for 238U shows a gain in correlation energy of more than 2 MeV beyond mean-field.

## Context
This review extends the historical work of Richardson (1963) and Gaudin on integrable models, which saw little application until the early 2000s when the exact solution was rediscovered in the context of ultrasmall superconducting grains. The paper discusses applications to nuclear structure including the systematic study of Sm isotopes by Dussel et al. (2007) and the treatment of pairing in the continuum by Id Betan. The hyperbolic family of models was first physically realized for p-wave pairing in 2D lattices by Sierra and later applied to heavy nuclei using Gogny HFB results. The review enables future work on self-consistent Hartree-Fock plus exact pairing approaches across large regions of the nuclide chart, and extensions to higher-rank algebras such as SO(5) for T=1 proton-neutron pairing and SO(8) for T=0,1 pairing.
