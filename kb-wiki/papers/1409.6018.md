# Spinodal instability growth in new stochastic approaches

> Are spinodal instabilities the leading mechanism in the fragmentation of a fermionic system? Numerous experimental indications suggest such a scenario and stimulated much effort in giving a suitable description, without being finalised in a dedicated transport model. On the one hand, the bulk character of spinodal behaviour requires an accurate treatment of the one-body dynamics, in presence of mechanical instabilities. On the other hand, pure mean-field implementations do not apply to situations where instabilities, bifurcations and chaos are present. The evolution of instabilities should be treated in a large-amplitude framework requiring fluctuations of Langevin type. We present new stochastic approaches constructed by requiring a thorough description of the mean-field response in presence of instabilities. Their particular relevance is an improved description of the spinodal fragmentation mechanism at the threshold, where the instability growth is frustrated by the mean-field resilience.

## Key claim

The paper presents new stochastic transport approaches (SMF and BLOB) that provide an improved description of spinodal fragmentation at the threshold where instability growth is frustrated by the mean-field resilience, but the validation is limited to nuclear matter in a periodic box and a single finite-system simulation event.

## Method

The authors constructed stochastic transport models based on the Boltzmann-Langevin equation, implementing two numerical strategies: the Stochastic Mean Field (SMF) approach, which imposes fluctuations from an external stochastic force projected on density space, and the Boltzmann-Langevin One-Body (BLOB) model, which introduces fluctuations in full phase space by inducing N-N correlations. They tested these models on nuclear matter in a mechanically unstable region of the equation of state (temperature 3 MeV, density ratio 0.3) using a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions, and compared the resulting dispersion relations to analytical linear-response calculations. They then applied the BLOB model to a finite heavy-ion collision system near the threshold between compound nucleus formation and multifragmentation.

## Key numbers

- Temperature T ≈ 3 MeV and mean-density-to-saturation-density ratio ρ/ρ₀ = 0.3 for the nuclear matter test conditions
- Cubic box edge length l = 39 fm with periodic boundary conditions containing 1584 neutrons and 1584 protons (each represented by 40 test particles)
- At around 100 fm/c the system presents a mottling pattern; at around 150 fm/c a large number of blobs of comparable size A′ appear as signature of the spinodal process
- The leading disturbance wavelength λ′ induces development of blobs of size A′ ≈ ρ′ (λ′/2)³, situated approximately in the region of Neon and Oxygen

## Context

This work extends the line of research on spinodal instabilities as a mechanism for fragment formation in heavy-ion collisions, as suggested by Chomaz (2004), and also relates to applications in compact star physics described by Ducoin (2007). The stochastic approaches build on prior Boltzmann-Langevin formulations by Colonna (1994) and the SMF strategy by Colonna (1998), while the BLOB model follows the approach of Napolitani (2013). The validation of the dispersion relation against analytical solutions enables future application of these transport models to describe nuclear multifragmentation without requiring thermodynamic hypotheses, making the dynamical description consistent with alternative statistical approaches.
