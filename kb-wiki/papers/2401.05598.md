# Astromers: Status and Prospects

> The extreme temperatures and densities of many astrophysical environments tends to destabilize nuclear isomers by inducing transitions to higher energy states. Those states may then cascade to ground. However, not all environments destabilize all isomers. Nuclear isomers which retain their metastable character in pertinent astrophysical environments are known as astrophysically metastable nuclear isomers, or "astromers". Astromers can influence nucleosynthesis, altering abundances or even creating new pathways that would otherwise be inaccessible. Astromers may also release energy faster or slower relative to their associated ground state, acting as heating accelerants or batteries, respectively. In stable isotopes, they may even simply remain populated after a cataclysmic event and emit observable x- or $γ$-rays. The variety of behaviors of these nuclear species and the effects they can have merit careful consideration in nearly every possible astrophysical environment. Here we provide a brief overview of astromers past and present, and we outline future work that will help to illuminate their role in the cosmos.

# Astromers: Status and Prospects

## Key claim
Nuclear isomers that retain their metastable character in astrophysical environments, termed "astromers", can influence nucleosynthesis, alter abundances, create new reaction pathways, and affect energy release timing, but whether a given isomer becomes an astromer depends on the specific competition between internal transition rates and transmutation rates in a given environment, meaning the classification is environment-dependent rather than an intrinsic nuclear property.

## Method
The authors review the history of theoretical treatments of isomers in astrophysics, from Solomon and Sargood's (1978) state-by-state population tracking in 45Sc through Ward and Fowler's (1980) analytical thermalization timescale formulation, Coc et al.'s (1999) network calculations treating individual nuclear levels as separate species, Gupta and Meyer's (2001) directed-graph approach with Dijkstra's algorithm, and Misch et al.'s (2021) matrix-inversion method for computing effective ground-to-isomer transition rates via intermediate states with minimal assumptions. They then survey specific astrophysical environments (s-process, rp-process, r-process) where isomers have been studied, and outline future directions including shell-model predictions and experimental isomer measurements.

## Key numbers
- The 228 keV state in 26Al has a ground-state half-life of ~700 kyr and an isomer half-life of ~6 s, with a thermalization temperature around 35 keV
- The 304.8 keV state in 85Kr has a half-life of 4.5 hours (isomer) versus nearly 11 years (ground state), with an 80% beta-decay branch from the isomer to 85Rb
- The first excited state of 128Sb was measured at 43.9 keV, with a thermalization temperature estimated between 1 and 9 keV
- The 77.2 keV metastable state of 180Ta has a half-life greater than 10^15 yr, while the ground state decays in 8 hr
- The 12.4 keV isomer in 45Sc has a half-life of 326 ms, with a mean lifetime against proton capture of 1 ns and thermal equilibrium achieved within 140 ps

## Context
This review extends the foundational work on astrophysical isomers initiated by Solomon and Sargood (1978) for the A=45 bottleneck in silicon burning, and by Ward and Fowler (1980) for 26Al thermalization. It builds on Gupta and Meyer's (2001) general treatment of isomers as distinct species and Misch et al.'s (2021) precise formulation for computing thermally mediated transitions. The paper surveys recent developments including Fujimoto et al.'s (2020) first r-process isomer study, Misch et al.'s (2021) dynamic population calculations showing astromers in r-process decay, and Hoff et al.'s (2023) measurement of 128Sb's first excited state. The work enables targeted experimental campaigns at radioactive beam facilities and motivates inclusion of isomer-specific treatments in nucleosynthesis simulations across multiple astrophysical environments.
