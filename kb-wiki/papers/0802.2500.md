# Density fluctuations and a first-order chiral phase transition in non-equilibrium

> The thermodynamics of a first-order chiral phase transition is considered in the presence of spinodal phase separation using the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model in the mean field approximation. We focus on the behavior of conserved charge fluctuations. We show that in non-equilibrium the specific heat and charge susceptibilities diverge as the system crosses the isothermal spinodal lines.

## Key claim

In a non-equilibrium first-order chiral phase transition, the net quark number susceptibility and specific heat diverge at the isothermal spinodal lines, not only at the critical end point (CEP); this divergence is a direct consequence of the vanishing pressure derivative at the spinodal, and the susceptibility changes sign inside the spinodal region.

## Method

The authors used the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model in the mean field approximation to compute thermodynamic quantities across a first-order chiral phase transition. They evaluated the net quark number susceptibility and specific heat as functions of quark number density at fixed temperature, focusing on the behavior when the system crosses the isothermal spinodal lines that bound the mechanically unstable region.

## Key numbers

- At T = 50 MeV, the net quark number susceptibility diverges when crossing the isothermal spinodal lines and becomes negative in the region between them.
- The critical exponent at the isothermal spinodal line is gamma = 1/2, with chi_q ~ (mu - mu_c)^(-gamma).
- The critical exponent at the CEP is gamma = 2/3, indicating a stronger divergence at the CEP than at the spinodal lines.

## Context

This work extends the study of conserved charge fluctuations from equilibrium first-order phase transitions to non-equilibrium systems undergoing spinodal decomposition, building on earlier discussions of spinodal instabilities in nuclear matter (Ran, Heiselberg). The results show that enhanced baryon number fluctuations can signal a first-order transition over a broader range of beam energies than the narrow critical region around the CEP, complementing earlier work on the CEP by Schaefer and Wambach and by the authors themselves. The negative specific heat predicted here connects to experimental evidence for anomalous behavior in low-energy nuclear collisions reported by Chomaz and collaborators.
