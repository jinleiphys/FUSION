# Nonlinear optical effects in a nucleus

> Intense laser technologies generate light with unprecedented and growing intensities. The possibility emerges that a nucleus responds nonlinearly to an intense light field, pointing to a yet little explored research area of nuclear nonlinear optics. We consider two-photon and three-photon absorption (with subsequent disintegration) processes of the deuteron, the simplest and the most fundamental nontrivial nucleus, as prototypes of nuclear nonlinear optical effects. Quantitative calculations are performed on these processes and novel observable effects are predicted.

## Key claim

Two-photon and three-photon absorption processes in the deuteron, the simplest nontrivial nucleus, produce angular distributions of the resultant proton (or neutron) that depend sensitively on the photon energy, in contrast to the energy-insensitive dipole shape of one-photon absorption; these nonlinear effects become observable only when the light intensity is sufficiently high, with two-photon rates reaching about 10^-5 s^-1 at 10^22 W/cm^2.

## Method

The authors used the strong field approximation (SFA), also known as the Keldysh-Faisal-Reiss (KFR) theory, to calculate two-photon and three-photon absorption rates and angular distributions for the deuteron. The deuteron ground state wavefunction was numerically calculated using the Reid potential, and continuum states were approximated by Volkov states, the quantum states of a free charged particle in an external electromagnetic field. Calculations were performed for both linearly and circularly polarized light across a range of photon energies below the 2.22 MeV dissociation threshold.

## Key numbers

- Two-photon absorption rate reaches about 10^-5 s^-1 at light intensity 10^22 W/cm^2 for the deuteron.
- Two-photon disintegration rate can reach 10^-1 s^-1 at intensity 10^24 W/cm^2.
- With several milligrams of deuterium and a 10 fs light pulse at 10^22 W/cm^2, the number of protons (neutrons) from two-photon disintegration is on the order of 100 per shot; at 10^24 W/cm^2, this becomes 10^6 per shot.
- At intensity 10^21 W/cm^2, three-photon absorption rates are about 13 orders of magnitude lower than two-photon rates.
- The deuteron ground state binding energy is 2.22 MeV, which is the threshold energy for dissociation.
- For two-photon absorption, the considered photon energy range is (1.11, 2.22) MeV; for three-photon absorption, the range is (0.74, 1.11) MeV.
- For circular polarization, two-photon angular distribution has a shape of sin^4 theta, and three-photon has sin^6 theta, independent of photon energy.

## Context

This work extends the study of nonlinear optical effects from atomic systems (where multiphoton ionization was first predicted by Goeppert-Mayer in the 1930s) to nuclear systems, using the deuteron as the nuclear analog of the hydrogen atom. The authors note that while one-photon photodisintegration of the deuteron has been extensively studied (Rustgi 1960, Ying 1988), nonlinear few-photon absorption in nuclei has been little explored. The predictions are enabled by recent and upcoming intense laser facilities such as the Extreme Light Infrastructure (ELI) in Europe and the Superintense Ultrafast Laser Facility (SULF) in Shanghai, which can generate gamma-ray intensities on the order of 10^22 W/cm^2. This work opens the possibility of studying nuclear nonlinear optics in more complex nuclei and other nonlinear optical effects, and connects to recent work on light-nucleus interactions including collective nuclear quantum optical effects with Mossbauer nuclei (Rohlsberger 2010, 2012; Heeg 2013, 2015) and analyses of intense light effects on alpha decay (Delion 2017, Qi 2019, Palffy 2020), nuclear fission (Qi 2020), and nuclear fusion (Queisser 2019, Lv 2019, Wang 2020).
