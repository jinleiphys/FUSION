# Understanding the baryon and meson spectra

> A brief overview is given of what we know of the baryon and meson spectra, with a focus on what are the key internal degrees of freedom and how these relate to strong coupling QCD. The challenges, experimental, theoretical and phenomenological, for the future are outlined, with particular reference to a program at Jefferson Lab to extract hadronic states in which glue unambiguously contributes to their quantum numbers.

## Key claim

The paper argues that understanding the baryon and meson spectra requires moving beyond simple constituent quark models to incorporate hadronic decay channels as essential degrees of freedom, with the caveat that many excited states may only exist because of their coupling to decay channels rather than as simple quark-model configurations.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental and theoretical efforts to extract baryon and meson resonance parameters from scattering data, focusing on coupled-channel amplitude analyses that decompose cross-sections into partial waves with definite quantum numbers. The Excited Baryon Analysis Center (EBAC) team uses an effective field theory of hadronic interactions developed by Sato and Lee that ensures unitarity, while the Bonn-Gatchina team fits an extensive range of multi-hadron final states including data from Jefferson Lab and ELSA on photoproduction reactions such as gamma p to pi0 p and KY.

## Key numbers

- The EBAC analysis presents N* and Delta* spectra up to 1800 MeV, with all states having 3*-4* provenance
- The Bonn-Gatchina analysis presents states up to 2.1 GeV, including those with 1*-2* ratings
- The X(3872) state is identified as having a width of 1-50 MeV, with its existence tied to the dynamics of the anti-D0 D*0 channel
- States above the open charm threshold have widths of 1-50 MeV decaying to channels such as D-bar D, D-bar D*, D-bar* D*, D_s-bar D_s*

## Context

This review extends the long-standing quark model framework developed by Isgur and Capstick for baryons and by Barnes and Swanson for mesons, while addressing the problem of "missing" baryon states above 1.6 GeV that were predicted by constituent quark models but not observed in pi N scattering. The work builds on coupled-channel approaches advocated by van Beveren and Lutz, and the experimental program at Jefferson Lab using real and virtual photon beams aims to reveal states that are "dark" in pion-nucleon channels but shine in pi pi N and KY final states. The analysis enables future extraction of hadronic states in which glue unambiguously contributes to their quantum numbers.
