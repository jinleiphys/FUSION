# Proton-proton 1S0 pairing in neutron star

> The onset of 1S0 proton spin-singlet pairing in neutron-star matter is studied in the framework of the BCS theory including medium polarization effects. The strong three-body coupling of the diproton pairs with the dense neutron environment and the self-energy effects severely reduce the gap magnitude, so to reshape the scenario of the proton superfluid phase inside the star. The vertex corrections due to the medium polarization are attractive in all isospin-asymmetry range at low density and tend to favor the pairing in that channel. However quantitative estimates of their effect on the energy gap do not give significant changes. Implications of the new scenario on the role of pairing in neutron-star cooling is briefly discussed.

# Proton-proton 1S0 pairing in neutron stars

## Key claim

The inclusion of medium polarization effects (vertex corrections via induced interaction) does not significantly change the previously established result that the proton 1S0 pairing gap remains below the activation threshold of direct URCA processes, meaning proton pairing cannot quench the rapid cooling of neutron stars via dURCA. The strong three-body coupling of diproton pairs with the dense neutron environment and self-energy effects severely reduce the gap magnitude, while the attractive vertex corrections from medium polarization produce only modest enhancements (over 20 percent) at low density.

## Method

The authors solve the BCS gap equation for proton-proton 1S0 pairing in beta-equilibrated neutron star matter, using the Argonne V18 two-body force and a consistent meson-exchange three-body force as the pairing interaction. The induced interaction from medium polarization is calculated within the framework of the Landau-Migdal theory, treating particle-hole collective excitations in asymmetric nuclear matter. The screening effects are incorporated by solving a linearized gap equation in a narrow window around the Fermi surface, with the induced interaction evaluated at the Fermi momentum. Self-energy effects (effective mass and quasi-particle strength) are extracted from a third-order G-matrix BHF calculation.

## Key numbers

- Upper baryonic density limit for 1S0 proton pairing with three-body force included: rho = 0.3 fm^{-3} (from prior work by Zuo et al.)
- Screening effect produces a sensitive enhancement of the gap magnitude: over 20 percent at low density
- At total density rho = 0.08 fm^{-3} (proton Fermi momentum k_F^p = 0.385 fm^{-1}): gap without screening Delta_0 = 0.0157 MeV, gap with screening Delta = 0.027 MeV (including self-energy effects with m*/m = 0.90 and Z = 0.59)
- At total density rho = 0.10 fm^{-3} (k_F^p = 0.420 fm^{-1}): Delta_0 = 0.0205 MeV, Delta = 0.036 MeV (m*/m = 0.85, Z = 0.64)
- At total density rho = 0.12 fm^{-3} (k_F^p = 0.475 fm^{-1}): Delta_0 = 0.0165 MeV, Delta = 0.027 MeV (m*/m = 0.82, Z = 0.65)
- Induced interaction is always attractive in the full asymmetry range from symmetric nuclear matter to pure neutron matter for total density rho <= 0.1 fm^{-3}, always repulsive for rho >= 2.2 fm^{-3}

## Context

This work extends previous calculations of 1S0 proton pairing in neutron stars by Zuo et al. (2008) that included three-body forces, and applies the induced interaction theory developed by Babu and Brown (1973), Sjoberg (1973), and Backman (1979) to the asymmetric nuclear matter case relevant for neutron stars. The induced interaction was previously found to reduce spin-singlet pairing in pure neutron matter (Clark et al., 1976) but to be attractive in symmetric nuclear matter (Cao et al., 2006). The present results show that for proton-proton pairing in neutron star matter, the vertex corrections are attractive at low density but do not significantly change the overall scenario: the pairing range remains below the dURCA threshold, so proton pairing cannot attenuate rapid cooling. The self-energy effects further reduce the gap magnitude, making it rather small on nuclear scale, though the thermodynamic consequences for long-era cooling may still be significant due to exponential dependence on Delta/kT.
