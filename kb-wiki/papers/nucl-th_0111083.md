# Synthesis of Superheavy elements and Theory of Atomic Nucleus

> The connection of synthesis of long-lived superheavy nuclei and existence of the island of stability with the choice of realistic nuclear potential is discussed: the behaviour of the average field of nuclei is an important element of nuclear models. It is also shown that it is just Dubna that possesses the priority both in the recent synthesis of a superheavy nucleus with charge Z=114 and in its theoretical prediction made 35 years ago. Possible sizes of the island of stability of superheavy nuclei are considered.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the theoretical prediction of a new proton closed shell at Z=114, made in 1966 using a realistic (finite, diffuse) nuclear potential with parameters fixed from independent nuclear reactions, was the first correct prediction and was experimentally confirmed by the synthesis of element 114 at Dubna in 1998; the authors assert that predictions based on the harmonic-oscillator (Nilsson) potential were unreliable and that Dubna holds priority for both the theoretical prediction and the experimental synthesis.

## Method

The authors solved the Schroedinger equation for a spherical Woods-Saxon potential (with a finite depth, a diffuse surface, and a spin-orbit interaction of the Heisenberg form) using a semianalytic method that represents radial wave functions as Hermite polynomials times a Gaussian, with a correcting logarithmic function. The potential parameters (V0 = 53 MeV, r0 = 1.27 fm, a = 0.67 fm, and a spin-orbit strength depending on N and Z) were fixed from data on low-lying states of near-magic nuclei, one-nucleon transfer reactions, and elastic and inelastic nucleon scattering. For deformed nuclei, the potential was generalized to an axially symmetric Woods-Saxon form with quadrupole and hexadecapole deformations, and the wave functions were expanded in the spherical basis.

## Key numbers

* The first theoretical prediction (Gareev, Kalinkin, Sobiczewski, 1966) identified Z=114 and N=184 as the new magic numbers for superheavy nuclei, using the realistic potential.
* The fission barrier for the doubly magic nucleus Z=114, N=184 was estimated as Vf ≈ 10 MeV in the 1966 work; a later more accurate calculation (using the Strutinsky shell-correction method) gave Vf ≈ 7-8 MeV.
* The potential parameters used were V0 = 53 MeV, r0 = 1.27 fm, a = 0.67 fm, and the spin-orbit strength κ = 0.263(1 + (N-Z)/A).
* The synthesis of the 114th element was achieved at Dubna in the reaction 48Ca + 244Pu, producing the isotope 289114 (and later 287114, 288114, 289114, and 292116).
* A decay chain from element 118 (293118) was reported from Berkeley, produced in the reaction 86Kr + 208Pb.

## Context

This paper extends the authors' 1966 work (Gareev, Kalinkin, Sobiczewski, Phys. Lett. 1966) that first predicted Z=114 as a magic number using a realistic Woods-Saxon potential, contradicting the earlier expectation (based on the Nilsson scheme) that the next closed proton shell after Z=82 would be Z=126. The work also describes the generalization of the realistic potential to deformed nuclei, which was used in the Bogoliubov-Soloviev model to calculate properties of rare-earth and transuranium nuclei. The paper argues for priority over later predictions by Meldner (1967) and others, and criticizes reviews by Herrmann and by Hofmann and Munzenberg for misrepresenting the historical record. The results enable further experimental searches for the island of stability around Z=114, N=184 and provide a validated framework for calculating fission barriers of superheavy nuclei.
