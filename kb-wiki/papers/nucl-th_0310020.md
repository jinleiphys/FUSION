# Giant Resonances from TDHF

> A method of calculating giant resonance strength functions using Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock techniques is described. An application to isoscalar giant monopole resonances in spherical nuclei is made, thus allowing a comparison between independent 1-, 2- and 3-Dimensional computer codes.

# Giant Resonances from TDHF

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations using modest box sizes combined with Gaussian smoothing can reproduce continuum-level giant resonance strength functions, provided the smoothing width is chosen to be physically reasonable (e.g., 1 MeV). The method is validated by comparing independent 1-dimensional (spherical), 2-dimensional (axial), and 3-dimensional TDHF codes for isoscalar giant monopole resonances (ISGMR) in spherical nuclei.

## Method
The authors solve the TDHF equations by first obtaining static Hartree-Fock single-particle wavefunctions, then evolving them in time under a Gaussian-shaped external perturbation with spatial form \(F(r) = W_0 \sum_i r_i^2\) to excite ISGMR modes. The strength function is extracted as the Fourier transform of the time-dependent expectation value of the spatial operator, divided by the Fourier transform of the time profile. To handle the discretization artifacts from finite-box calculations (e.g., 45 fm box), a Gaussian convolution with 1 MeV width is applied to smooth the discrete strength function, enabling comparison with continuum calculations performed in a 1000 fm box.

## Key numbers
- Time evolution used time steps of \(\Delta t = 1\) MeV/c up to a maximum time of \(t_\mathrm{max} = 2^{11}\) MeV/c for both continuum and discrete calculations.
- Continuum calculation used a 1000 fm box; discrete calculation used a 45 fm box.
- Smoothing Gaussian width was 1 MeV.
- For the SkM* Skyrme interaction calculation in \(^{16}\)O, the 1-D calculation used a 24 fm radius spherical box; the 2-D calculation used a cylinder of height 24 fm and radius 24 fm.
- A box size of 720 fm with 0.2 fm mesh was feasible for spherically-symmetric 1-D TDHF calculations seventeen years prior (Chomaz et al., 1987).

## Context
This work extends the TDHF approach to giant resonances previously developed by Stringari and Vautherin (1979) and Chomaz et al. (1987), and is motivated by renewed interest in TDHF for calculations without spatial symmetry assumptions (Nakatsukasa, 2002). The method enables practical TDHF calculations of giant resonances in deformed systems and with different effective interactions, including finite-range forces, while providing a natural path to beyond-RPA (nonlinear) approaches. The validation of independent 1-D, 2-D, and 3-D codes for the same ISGMR in \(^{16}\)O establishes the feasibility of using modest box sizes with smoothing for higher-dimensional calculations where true continuum calculations are difficult.
