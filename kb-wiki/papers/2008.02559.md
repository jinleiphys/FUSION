# Centrality, transverse momentum and collision energy dependence of the Tsallis parameters in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> The thermodynamic properties of matter created in high-energy heavy-ion collisions have been studied in the framework of the non-extensive Tsallis statistics. The transverse momentum ($p_{\rm T}$)~spectra of identified charged particles (pions, kaons, protons) and all charged particles from the available experimental data of Au-Au collisions at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) energies and Pb-Pb collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) energies are fitted by the Tsallis distribution. The fit parameters, $q$ and $T$ measure the degree of deviation from an equilibrium state and the effective temperature of the thermalized system, respectively. The $p_{\rm T}$~spectra are well described by the Tsallis distribution function from peripheral to central collisions for the wide range of collision energies, from $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}$ = 7.7 GeV to 5.02 TeV. The extracted Tsallis parameters are found to be dependent on the particle species, collision energy, centrality, and fitting ranges in $p_{\rm T}$. For central collisions, both $q$ and $T$ depend strongly on the fit ranges in $p_{\rm T}$. For most of the collision energies, $q$ remains almost constant as a function of centrality, whereas $T$ increases from peripheral to central collisions. For a given centrality, $q$ systematically increases as a function of collision energy whereas $T$ has a decreasing trend. A profile plot of $q$ and $T$ with respect to collision energy and centrality shows an anti-correlation between the two parameters.

## Key claim

The Tsallis distribution function provides a good description of transverse momentum spectra from peripheral to central collisions across a wide range of collision energies (from 7.7 GeV to 5.02 TeV), but the extracted parameters q and T depend strongly on the fitting range in pT for central collisions, and the quality of fits degrades for heavier particles and at higher pT ranges.

## Method

The authors fitted the thermodynamically consistent form of the Tsallis distribution (Eq. 6 in the paper) to published transverse momentum spectra of identified charged particles (pions, kaons, protons) and all charged particles from STAR, PHENIX, and ALICE experiments for Au-Au collisions at RHIC energies (7.7, 11.5, 14.5, 19.6, 27, 39, 62.4, and 200 GeV) and Pb-Pb collisions at LHC energies (2.76 and 5.02 TeV). The fits were performed for multiple centrality bins and for different pT fitting ranges, extracting the Tsallis parameters q (entropy index measuring deviation from equilibrium) and T (effective temperature), along with the normalization volume V.

## Key numbers

- Collision energy range covered: from sqrt(s_NN) = 7.7 GeV to 5.02 TeV
- For fits up to pT of 2 GeV/c, the maximum deviations of data from fits are within approximately 10-15% for low-energy RHIC data
- For fits to higher pT ranges (up to 10-12 GeV/c) at 62.4 and 200 GeV, maximum deviations reach up to about 30%
- For central collisions at LHC energies, q decreases from peripheral to central collisions, while at RHIC energies q is independent of centrality within uncertainty
- For pions and kaons, q shows an increasing trend with sqrt(s_NN) for both central and peripheral collisions; for protons at LHC energies, q values are found lower than unity
- T decreases with increasing collision energy for pions, but increases for kaons and protons in central collisions
- At mid-pT region (5-10 GeV/c), q and T values are similar within uncertainties across all collision energies

## Context

This work extends previous studies of Tsallis statistics in proton-proton collisions (Azmi 2015, Wong 2015, Trambak 2018) to heavy-ion collisions across a broad energy range, complementing traditional blast-wave model fits (STAR 2009, ALICE 2013) which cannot describe spectra at mid-to-high pT. The systematic mapping of Tsallis parameters as functions of centrality, collision energy, and fitting range enables future extraction of kinetic freeze-out temperatures by combining the effective temperature T with collective flow velocity through the relation T = T_kin + (1/2) m beta_T^2, as used by PHENIX (2004) and Wei (2016).
