# The role of finite value of strange quark mass $(m_{s}\neq0)$ and baryon number density $(n)$ on the stability and maximum mass of strange stars

> This study describes the impact of non-zero value of strange quark mass $(m_{s})$ and number density of baryons $(n)$ on the structure, stability and maximum mass of strange stars. We derive an exact relativistic solution of the Einstein field equation using the Tolman-IV metric potential and modified MIT bag model EoS, $p_{r}=\frac{1}{3}(ρ-4B')$, where $B'$ is a function of bag constant $B$, $m_{s}$ and baryon number density $(n)$. Following CERN's findings, transition of phase from hadronic matter to Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP) may occur at high densities in presence of favourable conditions. The standard MIT bag model, with a constant $B$, fails to explain such transition properly. Introducing a finite $m_{s}$ and Wood-Saxon parametrisation for $B$, dependent on baryon number density $(n)$, provides a more realistic EoS to address such phase transition. Both $m_{s}$ and $n$ constrain the EoS, making it softer as $m_{s}$ increases. Solutions to the TOV equations reveal that for massless strange quarks, maximum mass is 2.01 $M_{\odot}$ and corresponding radius is 10.96 Km when $n=0.66~fm^{-3}$. These values decrease to 1.99 $M_{\odot}$ and 1.96 $M_{\odot}$, with corresponding radii of 10.88 Km and 10.69 Km for $m_{s}=50$ and $100~MeV$ respectively having same $n$ value. It is interesting to note that a corelation exists between $n$ and $m_{s}$. The hadronic to quark matter transition occurs at higher values of $n$, when $m_{s}$ increases such as $n\geq0.484,~0.489$ and $0.51~fm^{-3}$ for $m_{s}=50$ and $100~MeV$ respectively. Beyond these values, the energy per baryon $(\mathcal{E_{B}})$ drops below $930.4~MeV$, indicating a complete transition to quark matter. For physical analysis, we have considered $n~(=0.578~fm^{-3})$ which lies in the stable region with $B(n)=70~MeV/fm^{3}$. The model provides a viable description of strange stars, satisfying all necessary physical requirements.

## Key claim

The paper claims that introducing a finite strange quark mass and a baryon-number-density-dependent bag constant (via a Wood-Saxon parametrization) provides a more realistic equation of state for strange stars than the standard MIT bag model with constant B, and that both parameters soften the equation of state, reducing the maximum mass and radius; however, the results are conditional on the specific choice of bag constant (B(n)=70 MeV/fm³) and baryon number density (n=0.578 fm⁻³) used for the physical analysis.

## Method

The authors derive an exact relativistic solution of the Einstein field equations using the Tolman-IV metric potential and a modified MIT bag model equation of state, p_r = (1/3)(ρ - 4B'), where B' depends on the bag constant B, the strange quark mass m_s, and the baryon number density n. They incorporate a Wood-Saxon parametrization for the density-dependent bag constant B(n) and solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations numerically to obtain mass-radius relations for different values of m_s (0, 50, 100 MeV) and B(n) (57.55, 70, 95.11 MeV/fm³).

## Key numbers

- For massless strange quarks (m_s=0) at n=0.66 fm⁻³, maximum mass is 2.01 M_⊙ and corresponding radius is 10.96 km.
- For m_s=50 MeV at n=0.66 fm⁻³, maximum mass is 1.99 M_⊙ and radius is 10.88 km.
- For m_s=100 MeV at n=0.66 fm⁻³, maximum mass is 1.96 M_⊙ and radius is 10.69 km.
- The hadronic to quark matter transition occurs at n ≥ 0.484 fm⁻³ for m_s=0, n ≥ 0.489 fm⁻³ for m_s=50 MeV, and n ≥ 0.51 fm⁻³ for m_s=100 MeV, based on energy per baryon dropping below 930.4 MeV.
- For the physical analysis, the authors adopt n=0.578 fm⁻³ with B(n)=70 MeV/fm³.
- For B(n)=70 MeV/fm³ and m_s=0, maximum mass is 1.82 M_⊙ and radius is 9.94 km; for m_s=50 MeV, 1.81 M_⊙ and 9.87 km; for m_s=100 MeV, 1.78 M_⊙ and 9.72 km.
- For B(n)=95.11 MeV/fm³ and m_s=0, maximum mass is 1.56 M_⊙ and radius is 8.53 km; for m_s=50 MeV, 1.55 M_⊙ and 8.48 km; for m_s=100 MeV, 1.53 M_⊙ and 8.35 km.

## Context

This work extends the standard MIT bag model approach of Farhi and Jaffe (1984) by incorporating a baryon-number-density-dependent bag constant, following the density-dependent B concept of Reinhardt (1986) and Burgio (2002), and by including a finite strange quark mass. The use of the Tolman-IV metric potential builds on the anisotropic stellar modeling of Banerjee (2018), Bhar (2019), and Das (2019). The model enables more realistic predictions for strange star masses and radii that can be compared with observations of compact objects such as 4U 1820-30, 4U 1608-52, EXO 1745-248, Cen X-3, and Her X-1.
