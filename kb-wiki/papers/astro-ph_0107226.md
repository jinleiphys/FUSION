# KeV Astrophysics With GeV Beams; Blazing a New Trail on the Summitt of Nuclear Astrophysics

> GeV beams of light ions and electrons are used for creating a high flux of real and virtual photons, with which some problems in Nuclear Astrophysics are studied. GeV 8B beams are used to study the Coulomb dissociation of 8B and thus the 7Be(p,gamma)8B reaction. This reaction is one of the major source of uncertainties in estimating the 8B solar neutrino flux and a critical input for calculating the 8B Solar neutrino flux. The Coulomb dissociation of 8B appears to provide a viable method for measuring the 7Be(p,gamma)8B reaction rate, with a weighted average of the RIKEN1, RIKEN2, GSI1 and MSU published results of S17(0) = 18.9 +/- 1.0 eV-b. This result however does not include a theoretical error estimated to be +/- 10 %. GeV electron beams on the other hand, are used to create a high flux of real and virtual photons at TUNL-HIGS and MIT-Bates, respectively, and we discuss two new proposals to study the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction with real and virtual photons. The 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction is essential for understanding Type II and Type Ia supernova. It is concluded that virtual and real photons produced by GeV light ions and electron beams are useful for studying some problems in Nuclear Astrophysics.

# KeV Astrophysics With GeV Beams; Blazing a New Trail on the Summit of Nuclear Astrophysics

## Key claim

The Coulomb dissociation of 8B provides a viable method for measuring the 7Be(p,gamma)8B reaction rate, yielding a weighted average of S17(0) = 18.9 +/- 1.0 eV-b from four experiments, but this result does not include a theoretical error estimated to be +/- 10%, and the accuracy of extracted S-factors is limited by the understanding of the Coulomb dissociation process itself.

## Method

GeV 8B beams were fragmented from a 350 A MeV 12C beam at GSI and directed onto a 208Pb target at 254 A MeV to induce Coulomb dissociation, with the breakup products 7Be and proton detected in the KaoS magnetic spectrometer using silicon micro-strip detectors for track reconstruction. For the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction, the author proposes using the inverse 16O(gamma,alpha)12C reaction with real photons from the HIGS facility at TUNL and virtual photons from electron beams at MIT-Bates, detected with an optical readout Time Projection Chamber (TPC) to track the back-to-back alpha and carbon particles.

## Key numbers

- Weighted average S17(0) from RIKEN1, RIKEN2, GSI1, and MSU Coulomb dissociation experiments: 18.9 +/- 1.0 eV-b (theoretical error of +/- 10% not included)
- RIKEN1: S17(0) = 16.9 +/- 3.2 eV-b, with SE2/SE1(0.6 MeV) < 7 x 10^-4
- RIKEN2: S17(0) = 18.9 +/- 1.8 eV-b, with SE2/SE1(0.6 MeV) < 4 x 10^-5
- GSI1: S17(0) = 20.6 +1.2 -1.0 eV-b, with SE2/SE1(0.6 MeV) < 3 x 10^-5
- MSU: S17(0) = 17.8 +1.4 -1.2 eV-b, with SE2/SE1(0.6 MeV) = 4.7 +2.0 -1.3 x 10^-4
- Adopted SE2/SE1(0.6 MeV) from CD experiments: < 3 x 10^-5
- GSI experiment used 8B beam at 254.5 A MeV average energy, 208Pb target thickness 199.7 +/- 0.2 mg/cm^2, with relative energy resolution of 0.11 and 0.22 MeV (1 sigma) at Erel = 0.6 and 1.8 MeV respectively
- For the proposed 16O(gamma,alpha)12C experiment: photodissociation cross section is approximately 6 nb at 0.9 MeV center-of-mass energy (compared to direct cross section of approximately 60 pb), and the proposed TPC would achieve energy resolution of approximately 2%
- Proposed luminosity for the 16O(gamma,alpha)12C experiment: 10^30 sec^-1 cm^-2 with a 100 cm long CO2 target at 76 torr and photon beam of 2 x 10^9 /sec, yielding 0.1 pb^-1 integrated luminosity per day

## Context

This work extends the Coulomb dissociation method for the 7Be(p,gamma)8B reaction previously developed by Baur (1986), Bertulani (1988), and Gai (1995), and reports new GSI results at 254 A MeV that are consistent with lower measured values from RIKEN1, RIKEN2, and MSU experiments. The proposed 16O(gamma,alpha)12C experiments at HIGS and MIT-Bates aim to resolve the long-standing uncertainty in the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction rate, which is essential for understanding Type II and Type Ia supernova outcomes and the carbon-to-oxygen ratio at the end of helium burning, where current R-matrix analyses by Hale (1996) cannot rule out a small S-factor solution of 10-20 keV-b. The optical readout TPC design builds on work by Titt at the Physikalisch Technische Bundesanstalt and the Weizmann Institute, and a similar program with high intensity photon beams and a TPC already exists at the Tokyo Institute of Technology.
