# Neutron star cooling

> The impact of nuclear physics theories on cooling of isolated neutron stars is analyzed. Physical properties of neutron star matter important for cooling are reviewed such as composition, the equation of state, superfluidity of various baryon species, neutrino emission mechanisms. Theoretical results are compared with observations of thermal radiation from neutron stars. Current constraints on theoretical models of dense matter, derived from such a comparison, are formulated.

## Key claim

The paper argues that many different scenarios of neutron star cooling (involving various equations of state, compositions, and superfluidity models) are currently compatible with observations of thermal radiation from isolated neutron stars, but that mild superfluidity with a peak critical temperature between roughly 3 × 10⁸ K and 2 × 10⁹ K at densities below about 8 × 10¹⁴ g cm⁻³ appears to be ruled out because it would produce Cooper-pairing neutrino emission in low-mass stars that would conflict with observed warm sources.

## Method

The authors use a numerical cooling code to compute cooling curves (effective surface temperature versus age) for neutron star models with different masses, equations of state, compositions (nucleon cores, hyperon cores, exotic phases), and phenomenological models of baryon superfluidity (proton singlet-state pairing and neutron triplet-state pairing with density-dependent critical temperatures). They compare these theoretical cooling curves with observational data on the surface temperatures and ages of isolated neutron stars, including sources fitted with hydrogen atmosphere models and blackbody models.

## Key numbers

- The density of saturated nuclear matter is ρ₀ ≈ 2.8 × 10¹⁴ g cm⁻³.
- The core-crust interface is placed at mass density ρ ∼ ρ₀/2.
- Crustal matter contains free neutrons at ρ ≳ 4 × 10¹¹ g cm⁻³.
- For one adopted EOS (Prakash et al. 1988), the direct Urca process opens at ρ > ρ_D = 7.851 × 10¹⁴ g cm⁻³, corresponding to gravitational mass M > M_D = 1.358 M⊙, with maximum mass M_max = 1.977 M⊙.
- The upper limit on surface temperature for RX J1856.4–3754 is T_s^∞ < 0.65 MK.
- For the Cooper-pairing scenario, neutrino luminosity scales as L_ν ∝ T^8; for other enhanced scenarios in massive NSs, L_ν ∝ T^6.
- The mass of an accreted envelope used in some models is ΔM = 10⁻⁸ M⊙, with a decay timescale τ = 4000 years in one variant.
- For the Vela pulsar, the inferred mass is M ≈ 1.47 M⊙ under one specific model (EOS, direct Urca threshold, and superfluidity model).
- The transition from slow to fast cooling in the absence of superfluidity would occur in a very narrow mass interval (0 < M − M_D ≲ 0.001 M⊙).

## Context

This paper reviews and extends the cooling theory reviewed by Yakovlev and Pethick (2004) and Page et al. (2004), comparing multiple cooling scenarios (direct Urca process, pion condensates, kaon condensates, quark matter, Cooper-pairing neutrino emission) against observations. It shows that the direct Urca process in nucleon cores gives the coldest massive neutron stars and the widest range of possible surface temperatures, while the Cooper-pairing scenario in nucleon cores without direct Urca gives the narrowest range. The work enables future observational campaigns to discriminate between these scenarios, particularly by searching for colder neutron stars or by combining cooling data with observations of quiescent thermal emission from neutron stars in soft X-ray transients.
