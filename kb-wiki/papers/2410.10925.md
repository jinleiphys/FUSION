# Solving the Lindblad equation with methods from computational fluid dynamics

> Liouvillian dynamics describes the evolution of a density operator in closed quantum systems. One extension towards open quantum systems is provided by the Lindblad equation. It is applied to various systems and energy regimes in solid state physics as well as also in nuclear physics. A main challenge is that analytical solutions for the Lindblad equation are only obtained for harmonic system potentials or two-level systems. For other setups one has to rely on numerical methods. In this work, we propose to use a method from computational fluid dynamics, the Kurganov-Tadmor central (finite volume) scheme, to numerically solve the Lindblad equation in position-space representation. We will argue, that this method is advantageous in terms of the efficiency concerning initial conditions, discretization, and stability. On the one hand, we study, the applicability of this scheme by performing benchmark tests. Thereby we compare numerical results to analytic solutions and discuss aspects like boundary conditions, initial values, conserved quantities, and computational efficiency. On the other hand, we also comment on new qualitative insights to the Lindblad equation from its reformulation in terms of an advection-diffusion equation with source/sink terms.

## Key claim

The Kurganov-Tadmor central finite volume scheme, adapted from computational fluid dynamics, provides a stable and efficient numerical method for solving the Lindblad equation in position-space representation across a wide range of initial conditions and Lindblad coefficients, though the authors demonstrate this primarily for benchmark systems (square well and harmonic oscillator) rather than for arbitrary nuclear-physics potentials.

## Method

The authors reformulate the Lindblad equation in position-space representation as an advection-diffusion equation with source/sink terms, splitting the density matrix into real and imaginary parts. They then apply the Kurganov-Tadmor central finite volume scheme, which is second-order accurate in space and uses semi-discrete time stepping, to solve this system of partial differential equations on a two-dimensional computational domain representing the coordinates (x,y) of the density matrix. Benchmark tests compare numerical results to analytic solutions for the free particle in a square well potential and the harmonic oscillator, examining boundary conditions, initial values, conserved quantities, and computational efficiency.

## Key numbers

- Mass set to m = 470 MeV (reduced mass of a deuteron), with hbar = k_Boltzmann = 1
- Computational domain set to L x L = 40 fm x 40 fm for the one-dimensional quantum system (density matrix becomes two-dimensional in coordinate space)
- The scheme is second order accurate in space, with errors expected to decrease quadratically with grid spacing Delta x
- The source term proportional to D_pp leads to exponential suppression of off-diagonal density matrix elements with rate proportional to 4 D_pp r^2, where r = (x-y)/2

## Context

This work extends numerical approaches to the Lindblad equation beyond standard methods such as Runge-Kutta, Taylor-series, and Crank-Nicolson schemes (Crank and Nicolson, 1947), which can produce spurious oscillations or norm violations for certain initial conditions and Lindblad coefficients. The Kurganov-Tadmor scheme, previously used in computational fluid dynamics, is applied here for the first time to Lindblad-type equations. The reformulation of the Lindblad equation as an advection-diffusion equation provides new qualitative insights into the dynamics of open quantum systems, and the method enables future studies of thermalization and relaxation times in nuclear-physics contexts, as planned in a separate publication (Rais, 2025).
