# Rationale for the Extrapolation Procedure in Selected Configuration Interaction

> Selected configuration interaction (SCI) methods have emerged as state-of-the-art methodologies for achieving high accuracy and generating benchmark reference data for ground and excited states in small molecular systems. However, their precision relies heavily on extrapolation procedures to produce a final estimate of the exact result. Using the structure of the exact electronic energy landscape, we provide a rationale for the common linear extrapolation of the variational energy as a function of the second-order perturbative correction. In particular, we demonstrate that the energy gap and the coupling between the so-called internal and external spaces are the key factors determining the rate at which the linear regime is reached. Starting from first principles, we also derive a new non-linear extrapolation formula that improves the post-processing of data generated from SCI methods and can be applied to both ground- and excited-state energies.

## Key claim

The linear extrapolation of the variational energy as a function of the second-order perturbative correction is justified from first principles using the structure of the exact electronic energy landscape, but the linear regime is only reached asymptotically when the variational wave function is sufficiently close to an eigenstate; the rate at which this linear regime is attained depends on the energy gap and coupling between the internal and external spaces.

## Method

The authors derive the asymptotic linear relationship between the variational energy and the second-order Epstein-Nesbet perturbative correction by representing the SCI variational wave function as a point on the exact electronic energy landscape, expanding the energy to second order via a Taylor series, and showing that the Newton-Raphson step yields an energy correction that reduces to the perturbative correction under a diagonally dominant approximation for the external-space Hamiltonian. They then construct a two-state model representing the separation of internal and external spaces, from which they derive an analytic non-linear relationship between the variational energy and the perturbative correction, leading to a new parametrised non-linear extrapolation formula.

## Key numbers

* The frozen-core correlation energy of benzene in the cc-pVDZ basis was estimated to be -862.890 millihartree via a five-point linear fit, with a fitting error of 0.266 millihartree and an overall error of order 1 millihartree.
* For water in the cc-pVDZ basis, the exact frozen-core correlation energy is -215.027 millihartree; using a set of only 351 determinants as the largest variational space (with a PT2 value of 7.5e-2 hartree), the non-linear fit estimated -216.192 millihartree (error around 1 millihartree), while the linear fit predicted -204.07 millihartree.
* For the water example, the linear and non-linear weighted fits used 5 points with minimal and maximal values of Ept = 9.5e-3 hartree and Ept = 1.1e-1 hartree, respectively; the 5-point window used a starting point with Ept = 1.6e-3 hartree.

## Context

This work provides the first theoretical justification for the widely observed linear extrapolation of variational energy against perturbative correction in SCI methods, which had previously been used empirically by Holmes (2017), Garniron (2019), Eriksen (2020), and others without formal rationale. The new non-linear extrapolation formula extends the approach of Burton (2021) on the electronic energy landscape and offers a more robust alternative to linear and quadratic fits for less well-converged SCI calculations, enabling more reliable FCI estimates from smaller variational spaces.
