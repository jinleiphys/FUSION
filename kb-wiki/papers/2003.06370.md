# Spallation of r-Process Nuclei Ejected from a Neutron Star Merger

> Neutron star mergers (NSMs) are rapid neutron capture (r-process) nucleosynthesis sites, which eject materials at high velocities, from 0.1c to as high as 0.6c. Thus the r-process nuclei ejected from a NSM event are sufficiently energetic to initiate spallation reactions with the interstellar medium (ISM) particles. With a thick-target model for the propagation of high-speed heavy nuclei in the ISM, we find that spallation reactions may shift the r-process abundance patterns towards solar data, particularly around the low-mass edges of the r-process peaks where neighboring nuclei have very different abundances. The spallation effects depend both on the astrophysical conditions of the r-process nuclei and nuclear physics inputs for the nucleosynthesis calculations and the propagation process. This work extends that of [Wang et al.(2019)] by focusing on the influence of nuclear physics variations on spallation effects.

## Key claim

Spallation reactions with interstellar medium particles can shift r-process abundance patterns from neutron star mergers toward solar data, particularly smoothing the low-mass edges of the r-process peaks, but the magnitude of this effect depends strongly on the initial ejecta velocity (becoming non-negligible above 0.2c) and on the choice of nuclear physics inputs for both nucleosynthesis and spallation cross sections.

## Method

The authors generate initial r-process abundance patterns from neutron star merger ejecta using the nucleosynthesis network code PRISM, then adopt a thick-target model for propagation through the interstellar medium where ionization loss dominates energy loss. They calculate spallation reaction yields using theoretical cross sections from TALYS/1.9 for reactions between heavy nuclei and hydrogen or helium in the interstellar medium, and explore variations by scaling cross sections by a factor of ten and by using different beta-decay rates and neutron-capture rates from alternative databases.

## Key numbers

- Ejecta velocities range from 0.1c to as high as 0.6c, based on kilonova models and neutron star merger simulations.
- For dynamical ejecta at 0.3c, spallation brings approximately 8% average abundance change; at 0.5c, the abundance change can be as high as a factor of 2 for some nuclei.
- For the baseline disk wind simulation at 0.4c, the average positive spallation abundance change is approximately 200% for the wind example versus approximately 40% for the cold dynamical ejecta example.
- For the baseline hot disk wind at 0.3c with TALYS cross sections, spallation produces approximately 30% average abundance change; with beta-decay rates from MKT, the average change drops to approximately 8%.
- Using neutron capture rates from NONSMOKER yields approximately 20% average spallation effect for the same conditions.
- The relevant energy range for spallation reactions is roughly 5–100 MeV per nucleon.
- TALYS cross-section values differ by at most 10 percent when varying input nuclear models, but NONSMOKER calculations differ from TALYS by more than an order of magnitude for the channel 196Pt(p,alpha)193Ir.

## Context

This work extends Wang et al. (2019) by focusing on how variations in nuclear physics inputs (beta-decay rates, neutron-capture rates, spallation cross-section models) affect the spallation reshaping of r-process abundance peaks. The spallation mechanism is analogous to cosmic-ray spallation that fills in the lithium-beryllium-boron valley at the expense of CNO nuclei, as studied in earlier cosmic-ray propagation work. The results motivate new measurements of spallation reactions for key targets (198Pt, 197Au, 196Pt, 195Pt) at energies of approximately 5–100 MeV, potentially accessible at facilities such as FRIB, FAIR, and RIKEN.
