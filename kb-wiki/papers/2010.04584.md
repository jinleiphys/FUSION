# Decay properties of $N^*(1895)$

> The nature of nucleon resonances is still being debated, while much experimental data are accumulated. In this work, we focus on the negative parity resonance $N^*(1895)$ which is located in the scattering region of various meson-baryon coupled channels, and such dynamics can be crucial in understanding its properties. To test the relevance of such hadron dynamics, we investigate the decay properties of $N^*(1895)$ in detail. We examine how a two pole nature of $N^*(1895)$ is compatible with its observed decay properties. Moreover, we find that the resonance decays into final states involving $Λ(1405)$ and $Σ(1400)$, where the latter is not yet observed experimentally. Such decay processes can be useful to study the properties of the aforementioned hyperon resonances.

## Key claim

The paper investigates whether the two-pole nature of the negative-parity nucleon resonance \(N^*(1895)\), found in a previous coupled-channel analysis, is compatible with its observed decay properties, and predicts that the resonance decays into final states involving the hyperon resonances \(\Lambda(1405)\) and the experimentally unobserved \(\Sigma(1400)\), with the caveat that these predictions depend on the specific pole structure and the regularization cut-off used in the triangle-loop calculations.

## Method

The authors use previously determined couplings of the two poles of \(N^*(1895)\) (at \(1801-i96\) MeV and \(1912-i54\) MeV) to pseudoscalar-baryon and vector-baryon channels, obtained from a \(\chi^2\)-fit to \(\pi N\) scattering amplitudes and cross sections for \(\pi^- p \to \eta n\) and \(\pi^- p \to K^0 \Lambda\). They calculate partial decay widths to \(K\Lambda(1405)\) and \(K\Sigma(1400)\) by evaluating triangle-loop diagrams involving intermediate meson-baryon channels, using effective Lagrangians for the meson and baryon vertices, and projecting the amplitudes onto the \(p\)-wave final state. The widths are convolved over the finite widths of the resonances, and a cut-off \(\Lambda = 600-700\) MeV is used to regularize the loop integrals.

## Key numbers

- Two poles associated with \(N^*(1895)\): \(1801-i96\) MeV and \(1912-i54\) MeV (Table in text).
- Partial width for \(N^{*+}_1 \to K^+ \Lambda^*_1\) (lower pole to lower \(\Lambda(1405)\) pole): \(10.4 \pm 1.3\) MeV.
- Partial width for \(N^{*+}_1 \to K^+ \Lambda^*_2\) (lower pole to upper \(\Lambda(1405)\) pole): \(6.4 \pm 0.8\) MeV.
- Partial width for \(N^{*+}_1 \to K^+ \Sigma^{*0}\): \(3.8 \pm 0.5\) MeV.
- Partial width for \(N^{*+}_2 \to K^+ \Lambda^*_1\) (higher pole to lower \(\Lambda(1405)\) pole): \(1.9 \pm 0.1\) MeV.
- Partial width for \(N^{*+}_2 \to K^+ \Lambda^*_2\) (higher pole to upper \(\Lambda(1405)\) pole): \(1.1 \pm 0.2\) MeV.
- Partial width for \(N^{*+}_2 \to K^+ \Sigma^{*0}\): \(4.1 \pm 0.4\) MeV.
- For the superposed \(N^*(1895)\) (mass \(\sim 1895\) MeV, width \(\sim 120\) MeV): \(\Gamma_{N^{*+}(1895)\to K^+ \Sigma^0(1400)} = 6.3 \pm 0.5\) MeV, branching ratio \((5.3 \pm 0.4)\%\).
- For the superposed \(N^*(1895)\): \(\Gamma_{N^{*+}(1895)\to K^+ \Lambda(1405)} = 8.3 \pm 1.3\) MeV, branching ratio \((6.9 \pm 1.1)\%\).
- Branching ratios for the two poles to \(\pi N\): \(9.4\%\) (\(N_1^*\)) and \(13.5\%\) (\(N_2^*\)), compared to experimental range \(2-18\%\).
- Branching ratios to \(\eta N\): \(2.7\%\) (\(N_1^*\)) and \(22.5\%\) (\(N_2^*\)), compared to experimental range \(15-40\%\).
- Branching ratios to \(K \Lambda\): \(10.9\%\) (\(N_1^*\)) and \(24.0\%\) (\(N_2^*\)), compared to experimental range \(13-23\%\).
- Branching ratios to \(K \Sigma\): \(0.7\%\) (\(N_1^*\)) and \(31.9\%\) (\(N_2^*\)), compared to experimental range \(6-20\%\).

## Context

This work extends the coupled-channel analysis of Khemchandani et al. (2013) for nonstrange meson-baryon scattering, where two poles were associated with \(N^*(1895)\), and of Khemchandani et al. (2018) for strangeness \(-1\) systems, where evidence for \(\Sigma(1400)\) was found. The results contradict naive quark model expectations (Isgur, 1978; Bijker, 1994; Hosaka, 1997; Takayama, 1999), which predict the next \(S_{11}\) resonance above 2100 MeV, highlighting the importance of hadron dynamics. The predicted decay widths to \(K\Lambda(1405)\) and \(K\Sigma(1400)\) can be used to guide experimental searches at J-PARC (Noumi, 2017) and to interpret photoproduction data on \(\gamma p \to K^+ \Lambda^*\) and \(K^+ \Sigma^*\) (Kim, 2017).
