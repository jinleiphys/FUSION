# Direct dark matter search by observing electrons produced in neutralino-nucleus collisions

> Exotic dark matter and dark energy together seem to dominate in the Universe. Supersymmetry naturally provides a candidate for the dark matter constituents via the lightest supersymmetric particle (LSP). The most important process for directly detecting dark matter is the LSP-nucleus elastic scattering by measuring the energy of the recoiling nucleus. In the present work we explore a novel process, which has definite experimental advantages, that is the detection of the dark matter constituents by observing the low energy ionization electrons. These electrons, which are produced during the LSP-nucleus collision, may be observed separately or in coincidence with the recoiling nuclei. We develop the formalism and apply it in calculating the ratio of the ionization rate to the nuclear recoil rate in a variety of atoms including Ne-20, Ar-40, Ge-76, Kr-78 and Xe-132 employing realistic Hartree-Fock electron wave functions. The obtained ratios are essentially independent of all parameters of supersymmetry except the neutralino mass, but they crucially depend on the electron energy cut off. These ratios per electron tend to increase with the atomic number and can be as high as 10 per cent. Based on our results it is both interesting and realistic to detect the LSP by measuring the ionization electrons following the-LSP nuclear collisions.

## Key claim
The paper proposes that dark matter (neutralino) detection can be achieved by measuring low-energy ionization electrons produced during neutralino-nucleus collisions, rather than only by measuring nuclear recoil energy. The authors hedge that the ratio of ionization rate to nuclear recoil rate is essentially independent of all supersymmetry parameters except neutralino mass, but crucially depends on the electron energy cutoff.

## Method
The authors developed a formalism for the differential cross section of neutralino-nucleus scattering leading to electron emission, normalized to the standard neutralino-nucleus elastic scattering cross section. They calculated the ratio of ionization rate to nuclear recoil rate for five target atoms (Ne-20, Ar-40, Ge-76, Kr-78, Xe-132) using realistic Roothaan-Hartree-Fock electron wave functions and harmonic oscillator nuclear form factors, then folded the results with a Maxwell-Boltzmann LSP velocity distribution with escape velocity 2.84 times the solar velocity (220 km/s).

## Key numbers
- The ratio of ionization rate to nuclear recoil rate per electron can be as high as 10% for a threshold energy of 200 eV and neutralino mass of 100 GeV.
- For Xe-132 at electron threshold energy near zero, the velocity distribution fraction reaches maximum values of 0.13 for 1s electrons, 0.75 for 2s, 0.76 for 2p, and 0.95 for 3s electrons.
- The quenching factor for nuclear recoils follows the empirical form Q_fac(Q) = 0.256 * [Q/1 keV]^0.153, giving approximately 0.3 at 10 keV recoil energy.
- For Xe-132 with a threshold energy of 0.2 keV, the nuclear form factor reduces the branching ratio by less than 7%.

## Context
This work extends a previous calculation by Vergados and Ejiri (2004) that estimated the ionization rate ratio for Ne-20 using hydrogenic electron wave functions, now employing realistic Hartree-Fock wave functions and multiple target nuclei. The proposed method offers an alternative to standard nuclear recoil experiments such as DAMA, EDELWEISS, and CDMS, and could be realized with gaseous TPC detectors or double beta decay detectors sensitive to low-energy electrons around 1 keV. The results enable coincidence measurements of ionization electrons with nuclear recoils and suggest that inner-shell electron holes could be exploited via X-ray or Auger electron detection.
