# The 5 MeV bump - a nuclear whodunit mystery

> We perform a combined analysis of recent NEOS and Daya Bay data on the reactor antineutrino spectrum. This analysis includes approximately 1.5 million antineutrino events, which is the largest neutrino event sample analyzed to date. We use a double ratio which cancels flux model dependence and related uncertainties as well as the effects of the detector response model. We find at 3-4 standard deviation significance level, that plutonium-239 and plutonium-241 are disfavored as the single source for the the so-called 5 MeV bump. This analysis method has general applicability and in particular with higher statistics data sets will be able to shed significant light on the issue of the bump. With some caveat this also should allow to improve the sensitivity for sterile neutrino searches in NEOS.

## Key claim

The 5 MeV bump in the reactor antineutrino spectrum is largely canceled in a double ratio of NEOS and Daya Bay data, indicating the bump amplitude and position are very similar in both data sets; plutonium-239 and plutonium-241 are disfavored as the single source of the bump at 3-4 standard deviation significance, but the analysis does not conclusively identify which isotope is responsible.

## Method

The analysis uses a double ratio of the NEOS measured prompt spectrum to the Huber+Mueller model prediction divided by the corresponding ratio for the Daya Bay unfolded spectrum, corrected for the different fission fractions between the two experiments using the same flux model. A detector response function for NEOS is estimated from published energy resolution and a model of surface energy losses from escaping positrons and annihilation gamma rays, and its effect on the double ratio is shown to be negligible (less than 0.01). A chi-squared fit is performed to the double ratio with the bump position fixed at 4.9 MeV, testing five hypotheses: the bump originates solely from uranium-235, uranium-238, plutonium-239, plutonium-241, or equally from all isotopes.

## Key numbers

- The analysis includes approximately 1.5 million antineutrino events total: about 1,200,000 from Daya Bay and 300,000 from NEOS.
- The bump in Daya Bay data is described by a Gaussian with central value 4.9 MeV, width 0.55 MeV, and amplitude 10.4%.
- The best fit double ratio at the bump is 1.022 with a chi-squared of 46.7 and 80% goodness of fit (57-1 degrees of freedom).
- For the hypothesis that plutonium-239 is the sole source: chi-squared = 60.3, significance = 3.27 sigma.
- For the hypothesis that plutonium-241 is the sole source: chi-squared = 66.0, significance = 3.92 sigma.
- For uranium-235 as sole source: chi-squared = 46.9, significance = 0.34 sigma.
- For uranium-238 as sole source: chi-squared = 51.6, significance = 1.93 sigma.
- For equal contribution from all isotopes: chi-squared = 49.9, significance = 1.55 sigma.
- The NEOS detector energy resolution is given as sigma(E_prompt) = (0.05 sqrt(E_prompt) + 0.12) MeV.
- The mean range of an 8 MeV positron in scintillator is approximately 4 cm; the mean range of 511 keV annihilation gammas is about 8 cm.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the 5 MeV bump first reported by RENO (Seo 2014, RENO 2015) and confirmed by Daya Bay (An 2015) and Double Chooz (Abe 2015). It directly tests the hypothesis from Hayes (2015) that epithermal fission of plutonium causes the bump, which is disfavored by the results. The double-ratio method cancels flux model uncertainties that limited previous analyses (Huber 2016) and enables future sterile neutrino searches in NEOS using Daya Bay as a far detector, though the authors caution that a detailed detector response model would be needed for oscillation searches. Future measurements at research reactors with nearly pure uranium-235 fission could distinguish between the remaining possibilities.
