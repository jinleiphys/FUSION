# Quantum Monte Carlo methods for nuclear physics

> Quantum Monte Carlo methods have proved very valuable to study the structure and reactions of light nuclei and nucleonic matter starting from realistic nuclear interactions and currents. These ab-initio calculations reproduce many low-lying states, moments and transitions in light nuclei, and simultaneously predict many properties of light nuclei and neutron matter over a rather wide range of energy and momenta. We review the nuclear interactions and currents, and describe the continuum Quantum Monte Carlo methods used in nuclear physics. These methods are similar to those used in condensed matter and electronic structure but naturally include spin-isospin, tensor, spin-orbit, and three-body interactions. We present a variety of results including the low-lying spectra of light nuclei, nuclear form factors, and transition matrix elements. We also describe low-energy scattering techniques, studies of the electroweak response of nuclei relevant in electron and neutrino scattering, and the properties of dense nucleonic matter as found in neutron stars. A coherent picture of nuclear structure and dynamics emerges based upon rather simple but realistic interactions and currents.

## Key claim

Quantum Monte Carlo methods, combined with realistic nuclear interactions and currents, can reproduce many low-lying states, moments, and transitions in light nuclei and predict properties of neutron matter, but the accuracy depends on the specific Hamiltonian used and the method's ability to handle spin-isospin, tensor, spin-orbit, and three-body interactions, with the largest GFMC calculations to date reaching 12C and 16-neutron systems.

## Method

The authors review continuum Quantum Monte Carlo methods applied to nuclear physics, specifically Variational Monte Carlo (VMC), Green's Function Monte Carlo (GFMC), and Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC). These methods use a coordinate representation of the Hamiltonian with realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials such as Argonne v18 and v8' and three-nucleon forces from the Urbana and Illinois series, as well as local chiral effective field theory interactions up to next-to-next-to-leading order. VMC optimizes trial wave functions with Jastrow-like correlations, GFMC projects the ground state via imaginary-time evolution using path integrals, and AFDMC samples spin-isospin degrees of freedom with auxiliary fields to treat larger systems.

## Key numbers

- The Argonne v18 potential fits 1787 pp and 2514 np observables for laboratory energies up to 350 MeV with a final chi-squared per data point of 1.1.
- The largest GFMC calculations are for 12C (540,672 spin-isospin states) and systems of 16 neutrons (65,536 spin-isospin states).
- The Urbana IX three-nucleon potential parameters were determined by fitting the binding energy of 3H and the density of nuclear matter in conjunction with AV18.
- The Illinois-7 model parameters were searched to fit about 20 nuclear ground and low-lying excited states in nuclei with A less than or equal to 10.
- The chiral symmetry breaking scale Lambda_chi is approximately 1 GeV.

## Context

This review extends the work of earlier QMC applications in nuclear physics dating back to the early 1980s for VMC (Lomnitz 1981) and the late 1980s for GFMC (Carlson 1987, 1988), and the introduction of AFDMC in 1999 (Schmidt 1999). It contrasts with other many-body methods such as coupled cluster (Hagen 2014), the no-core shell model (Barrett 2013), similarity renormalization group (Bogner 2010), and self-consistent Green's function (Dickhoff 2004), which can treat a wider variety of nuclear interaction models but are less able to handle the wide range of momentum and energy and phenomena like superfluidity and clustering that QMC methods address. The work enables future studies of heavier nuclei, nuclear matter, and systems including hyperons, as well as applications to low-energy nuclear reactions, electroweak response, and dense matter in neutron stars.
