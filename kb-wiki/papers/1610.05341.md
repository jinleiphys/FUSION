# Hydrodynamic Overview at Hot Quarks 2016

> This presents an overview of relativistic hydrodynamic modeling in heavy-ion collisions prepared for Hot Quarks 2016, at South Padre Island, TX, USA. The influence of the initial state and viscosity on various experimental observables are discussed. Specific problems that arise in the hydrodynamical modeling at the Beam Energy Scan are briefly discussed.

## Key claim

Relativistic viscous hydrodynamics can reproduce experimental flow harmonics in heavy-ion collisions with an extremely small shear viscosity to entropy density ratio, but the exact value of transport coefficients is extremely dependent on the initial state model chosen, and no single observable has yet been found that is sensitive only to the initial state or only to transport coefficients.

## Method

The paper reviews event-by-event relativistic viscous hydrodynamic modeling of heavy-ion collisions, comparing how different initial state models (such as IP-Glasma, EKRT, and Trento) and transport coefficients (shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, and their relaxation times) affect the calculation of flow harmonics, multiparticle cumulants, symmetric cumulants, and event plane correlations. It also discusses specific challenges for hydrodynamic modeling at finite baryon chemical potential relevant to the Beam Energy Scan at RHIC.

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio of approximately η/s ∼ 0.08 is needed to reproduce flow harmonics, close to the conjectured KSS bound of 1/4π.
- Flow harmonic predictions for LHC run 2 were accurate to approximately 5% and later experimentally confirmed.
- The ratio v2{4}/v2{2} is less than 1 in heavy-ion collisions, indicating collective behavior with fluctuations; without fluctuations this ratio would approach 1.
- Symmetric cumulant SC(3,2) (correlating elliptic and triangular flow) appears almost entirely driven by initial eccentricities, while SC(4,2) sensitivity to viscosity largely disappears after proper multiplicity weighing and centrality binning.

## Context

This overview extends the "standard model" of heavy-ion collisions that emerged after the discovery of nearly perfect fluidity at RHIC and the LHC, which includes fluctuating initial conditions, event-by-event relativistic viscous hydrodynamics, and a hadronic afterburner. It discusses the work of Bernhard (2015, 2016) using Bayesian techniques to constrain η/s, and the temperature dependence of viscosity studied by Noronha-Hostler (2008, 2012, 2015). The paper enables future work on separating initial state effects from transport coefficients, particularly through soft-hard event engineering and studies of the QCD critical point at finite baryon density in the Beam Energy Scan.
