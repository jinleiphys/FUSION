# Jost Function for Singular Potentials

> An exact method for direct calculation of the Jost function and Jost solutions for a repulsive singular potential is presented. Within this method the Schrodinger equation is replaced by an equivalent system of linear first-order differential equations, which after complex rotation, can easily be solved numerically. The Jost function can be obtained to any desired accuracy for all complex momenta of physical interest, including the spectral points corresponding to bound and resonant states. The method can also be used in the complex angular-momentum plane to calculate the Regge trajectories. The effectiveness of the method is demonstrated using the Lennard-Jones (12,6) potential. The spectral properties of the realistic inter-atomic He4-He4 potentials HFDHE2 and HFD-B of Aziz and collaborators are also investigated.

## Key claim

The paper presents an exact method for direct calculation of the Jost function and Jost solutions for repulsive singular potentials (those more singular than 1/r^2 at the origin), which the authors' prior method could not handle; the method works for all complex momenta of physical interest including bound, resonant, and scattering states, and can also be extended to the complex angular-momentum plane for Regge trajectories.

## Method

The Schrodinger equation is replaced by an equivalent system of linear first-order differential equations for the functions F_ell^(+/-)(k,r), which are integrated from a small radius r_min outward. Near the origin, where the potential is singular, WKB boundary conditions provide the regular solution and its derivative, which are then used to initialize F_ell^(+/-) via matching formulas. For resonant states (complex k in the fourth quadrant), the integration path is rotated into the complex r-plane along a ray r = b + x exp(i theta) with theta greater than or equal to the resonance angle, ensuring the Jost function f_ell(k) is obtained as the asymptotic limit of F_ell^(-)(k,r) at large x.

## Key numbers

- For the Lennard-Jones (12,6) potential with d=3.56 Angstrom and D=60 cm^{-1}, the lowest resonance in partial wave ell=8 has energy E_res = 9.4943275 cm^{-1} and width Gamma_res = 0.264474 cm^{-1}
- For D=5 cm^{-1} (extremely broad resonance), the lowest ell=8 resonance has E_res = 30 cm^{-1} and Gamma_res = 60 cm^{-1} (width exceeds resonance energy by a factor of 2)
- For the HFDHE2 Aziz He4-He4 potential, the dimer binding energy is 0.8301249029 mK
- For the HFD-B Aziz He4-He4 potential, the dimer binding energy is 1.6854110471 mK
- For the HFDHE2 potential, the lowest resonance in ell=1 has E=0.334 K and Gamma=1.822 K; in ell=5, E=20.478 K and Gamma=42.949 K
- For the HFD-B potential, the lowest resonance in ell=1 has E=0.339 K and Gamma=1.795 K; in ell=5, E=20.518 K and Gamma=42.603 K

## Context

This work extends the Jost function method previously developed by the same authors (Rakityansky, Sofianos, Amos 1996; Sofianos, Rakityansky 1997; Sofianos, Rakityansky, Vermaak 1997; Rakityansky, Sofianos 1998) from regular potentials to repulsive singular potentials such as the Lennard-Jones (12,6) potential. The method is tested against the Complex Rotation method and Quantum Time Delay method of Connor and Smith (1983) and the Finite Range Scattering Wave method of Jang and Light (1993), showing improved stability for broad resonances where the CR method failed. The application to realistic He4-He4 potentials (HFDHE2 and HFD-B of Aziz and collaborators) enables the first systematic study of dimer resonances and Regge trajectories for these systems, which is relevant for understanding low-temperature helium scattering and Bose-Einstein condensation.
