# How EIC can help us to understand heavy-ion collisions

> I give a brief overview of the science cases of the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) with a particular emphasis on the connections to the physics of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) can provide critical insights into the physics of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions, but the connection is bidirectional: heavy-ion physics, particularly through ultraperipheral pA collisions, can also inform EIC science. The paper argues that the overlap extends beyond the commonly cited topics of gluon saturation and jet quenching to include nucleon tomography, proton spin, and proton mass studies.

## Method

The paper is a conference proceeding (Quark Matter 2019) that reviews the main physics cases of the EIC, organized into sections on nucleon tomography (TMDs, GPDs, Wigner distributions), proton spin, jets, gluon saturation, and proton mass. For each topic, the author identifies specific experimental observables at EIC (such as semi-inclusive DIS, deeply virtual Compton scattering, coherent diffractive dijet production, and J/psi photoproduction near threshold) and discusses their relevance to heavy-ion collision physics. The review connects these EIC capabilities to existing work in the Color Glass Condensate framework, RHIC programs, and LHC heavy-ion results.

## Key numbers

- EIC center-of-mass energy range: 20 < sqrt(s) < 140 GeV, with design luminosity ~10^34 cm^-2 s^-1
- EIC luminosity is higher than HERA (sqrt(s)=318 GeV) by a factor of 1000
- EIC provides about 2 orders of magnitude improvement in kinematic coverage (x and Q^2) over fixed-target polarized ep experiments (HERMES, COMPASS)
- The EIcC in China is planned at sqrt(s) less than or equal to 30 GeV
- Only 1% of the proton mass is attributed to the sum of u,d current quark masses, with the remaining 99% from other sources

## Context

This paper extends the discussions from the EIC white paper (Accardi et al., 2012) and the INT workshop proceedings (Aidala et al., 2020), specifically targeting the heavy-ion collision community. It connects EIC physics to ongoing work in the Color Glass Condensate framework (Balitsky, Kovchegov), global analyses of TMDs (Scimemi, Bacchetta groups), and recent extractions of the proton D-form factor from DVCS data (Burkert et al., 2018). The paper enables future cross-community collaboration by identifying specific observables where EIC data can test saturation-based calculations at NLO+NLL precision, which can then be applied to heavy-ion collisions with greater confidence.
