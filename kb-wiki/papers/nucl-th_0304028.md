# The Goldstone bosons in the pairing Hamiltonian: the path integral approach

> As a first step to derive the IBM from a microscopic nuclear hamiltonian, we bosonize the pairing hamiltonian in the framework of the path integral formalism respecting both the particle number conservation and the Pauli principle. Special attention is payed to the role of the Goldstone bosons. We construct the saddle point expansion which reproduces the sector of the spectrum associated to the addition or removal of nucleon pairs.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the Goldstone bosons arising from the (almost) spontaneous breaking of particle-number conservation in the pairing Hamiltonian survive in finite nuclei with the same signatures as in infinite systems, specifically, the Goldstone field lives in the coset space of the broken U(1) symmetry and displays only derivative couplings, but the authors note that the argument is heuristic and that the real justification comes from the saddle-point expansion developed later in the paper.

## Method

The authors bosonize the pairing Hamiltonian using a path integral formalism with Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to introduce auxiliary bosonic fields, then employ a polar representation to separate the Goldstone field. They perform a saddle-point expansion of the effective action, with the expansion parameter \(M = g\Omega/2\) (where \(g\) is the pairing strength and \(\Omega = j+1/2\) is the pair degeneracy of a single j-level), and fix the particle number either via a chemical potential or a projection operator.

## Key numbers

* The expansion parameter is \(M = g\Omega/2\), with \(\Omega = j+1/2\) the pair degeneracy of a single-particle level of angular momentum \(j\).
* The exact energy spectrum for the pairing Hamiltonian is \(E_{n,s} = 2en - gn(\Omega - n + 1) + gs(\Omega - s + 1)\), where \(n\) is the number of pairs and \(s\) the pair seniority, valid for \(n \le \Omega\) and \(s \le \Omega - n\).
* The minimum of the energy as a function of continuous \(n\) occurs at \(\nu_0 = (\Omega + 1)/2 - e/g\); the excitation energy for adding or removing one pair (Goldstone boson) is of order \(g\), while breaking a pair (seniority, Higgs boson) costs energy of order \(g\Omega\).
* A reasonable estimate for \(M\) in the Sn isotopes yields \(M \ge 3\) MeV.
* The saddle-point value of the constant \(\rho\) field is \(\overline{\rho}_0 = n(\Omega - n)\), which is maximal (\(\sim \Omega^2/4\)) for a half-filled level and minimal for \(n=1\) or \(n=\Omega\).
* The chemical potential is \(\mu = g(n - \Omega/2 - 3/8) + e\), and the resulting ground-state energy from the saddle-point plus first-order correction is \(E_{n,0} = 2en - gn(\Omega - n + 3/4) + (g/8)(5\Omega + 9/8)\), reproducing the exact excitation spectrum with a relative error of order \(1/\Omega\).

## Context

This work extends earlier bosonization attempts for the pairing interaction using even Grassmann variables (Palumbo, 1980s) and the mean-field approach of Mukherjee and Nambu (1976), which treated infinite homogeneous systems and did not conserve particle number. The authors aim to provide a microscopic derivation of the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) from a fermionic nuclear Hamiltonian, with the present paper as a first step limited to the pairing interaction (s-bosons). The approach enables future extension to forces of higher multipolarity (d-bosons and beyond), as confirmed by recent work (Palumbo, 2003), and can incorporate level dispersion (Barbaro, 2002).
