# Quantum entanglement patterns in the structure of atomic nuclei within the nuclear shell model

> Quantum entanglement offers a unique perspective into the underlying structure of strongly-correlated systems such as atomic nuclei. In this paper, we use quantum information tools to analyze the structure of light and medium-mass berillyum, oxygen, neon and calcium isotopes within the nuclear shell model. We use different entanglement metrics, including single-orbital entanglement, mutual information, and von Neumann entropies for different equipartitions of the shell-model valence space and identify mode-entanglement patterns related to the energy, angular momentum and isospin of the nuclear single-particle orbitals. We observe that the single-orbital entanglement is directly related to the number of valence nucleons and the energy structure of the shell, while the mutual information highlights signatures of proton-proton and neutron-neutron pairing, as well as nuclear deformation. Proton and neutron orbitals are weakly entangled by all measures, and in fact have the lowest von Neumann entropies among all possible equipartitions of the valence space. In contrast, orbitals with opposite angular momentum projection have relatively large entropies, especially in spherical nuclei. This analysis provides a guide for designing more efficient quantum algorithms for the noisy intermediate-scale quantum era.

## Key claim

The paper systematically maps quantum entanglement patterns in nuclear shell-model ground states across isotopic chains of Be, O, Ne, and Ca, finding that single-orbital entanglement is directly tied to valence nucleon number and subshell energy structure, while mutual information reveals signatures of proton-proton and neutron-neutron pairing as well as nuclear deformation, but the authors note that these patterns are observed within the constraints of specific phenomenological Hamiltonians and valence spaces, not as universal nuclear properties.

## Method

The authors computed ground states of even-even isotopes (Be, O, Ne, Ca) using the nuclear shell model with standard phenomenological Hamiltonians: Cohen-Kurath in the p shell, USDB in the sd shell, and KB3G in the pf shell. They then calculated three entanglement measures, single-orbital von Neumann entropy, mutual information between orbital pairs, and von Neumann entropies for equipartitions of the valence space, using the Jordan-Wigner fermion-to-qubit mapping and partial tracing of reduced density matrices, with all ground states having J=M=0.

## Key numbers

* Single-orbital entropy for the 0f7/2 subshell in 44Ca (4 valence neutrons in pf shell): S_i = 0.998, corresponding to occupation number gamma = 0.477 (nearly maximal entanglement)
* Single-orbital entropy for the 0f7/2 subshell in 50Ca (10 valence neutrons): S_i = 0.184, with occupation gamma = 0.972 (low entanglement due to near-full occupancy)
* Single-orbital entropy for the 1p3/2 subshell in 50Ca: S_i = 0.996, with occupation gamma = 0.465 (nearly maximal entanglement)
* Single-orbital entropy for the 0p3/2 subshell in 8Be: S_i = 0.95 (both proton and neutron shells)
* Single-orbital entropy for the 0p1/2 subshell in 8Be: S_i = 0.85 (both proton and neutron shells)
* Maximum possible entropy for the proton-neutron partition in 8Be: S_max = log2(15) = 3.9, constrained by particle number conservation and M=0

## Context

This work extends prior entanglement studies in nuclear structure, particularly the single- and double-orbital entanglement analysis for 8Be by Stetcu and collaborators, and the DMRG-based entanglement studies by Legeza (2015). It complements the ab initio valence-space entanglement analysis of sd-shell and nickel isotopes by Tichai and collaborators (2022), and the adaptive variational quantum eigensolver study of single-orbital entanglement across p, sd, and pf shells by Perez-Marquez and collaborators (2023). The systematic mapping across isotopic chains provides a guide for designing more efficient quantum algorithms for nuclear shell-model simulations on noisy intermediate-scale quantum devices, and for developing truncation protocols that retain the most relevant degrees of freedom.
