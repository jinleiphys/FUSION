# Heavy flavour measurements with the ALICE experiment at the LHC

> Heavy quarks (charm and beauty) are produced early in the nucleus-nucleus collisions, and heavy flavor survives throughout the later stages. Measurements of heavy-flavor quarks thus provide us with means to understand the properties of the Quark-Gluon Plasma, a hot and dense state of matter created in heavy-ion collisions. Production of heavy-flavor in small collision systems, on the other hand, can be used to test Quantum-chromodynamics models. After a successful completion of the Run-I data taking period, the increased luminosity from the LHC and an upgraded ALICE detector system in the Run-II data taking period allows for an unprecedented precision in the study of heavy quarks. In this article we give an overview of selected recent results on heavy-flavor measurements with ALICE experiment at the LHC.

## Key claim

Heavy-flavor measurements from the ALICE experiment at the LHC provide a probe of Quark-Gluon Plasma properties in nucleus-nucleus collisions and a test of Quantum Chromodynamics models in small collision systems, with the increased luminosity of Run-II and the upgraded ALICE detector enabling unprecedented precision compared to Run-I.

## Method

The paper presents an overview of selected recent results on heavy-flavor (charm and beauty) production measured with the ALICE detector at the LHC. Heavy quarks are identified through their decay products in both nucleus-nucleus (Pb-Pb) and small collision systems (pp and p-Pb). The analysis uses data from the Run-I and Run-II data-taking periods, exploiting the upgraded ALICE detector system and increased LHC luminosity for improved statistical precision.

## Context

This overview extends the program of heavy-flavor measurements from the ALICE collaboration, building on results from the Run-I period (2010-2013) and incorporating the higher statistics available from Run-II (2015-2018). The measurements in heavy-ion collisions aim to constrain the transport properties of the Quark-Gluon Plasma, while results from small systems test perturbative QCD calculations. The paper enables future comparisons with theoretical models and provides a benchmark for upcoming measurements at higher precision.
