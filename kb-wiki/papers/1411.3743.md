# Proton Charge Radius and Precision Tests of QED

## Key claim
The proton radius puzzle remains unsolved as of 2014, with a discrepancy of more than 7 standard deviations between the proton charge radius extracted from muonic hydrogen spectroscopy and the combined result from electron scattering and electronic hydrogen spectroscopy, despite extensive theoretical and experimental scrutiny.

## Method
The paper reviews three principal methods for measuring the proton charge radius: elastic electron-proton scattering (using Rosenbluth separation to extract the electric form factor and its slope at zero momentum transfer), electronic hydrogen spectroscopy (measuring transitions such as 2S-2P for the Lamb shift or 1S-2S combined with higher transitions), and muonic hydrogen spectroscopy (exploiting the muon’s 200 times smaller orbit and correspondingly larger finite-size effect). The author summarizes the current experimental results, categorizes proposed solutions (experimental errors, analysis errors, and new physics), and surveys upcoming experiments.

## Key numbers
- The muon in muonic hydrogen has a 200 times smaller orbit and an approximately 200³ higher probability of being inside the proton compared to the electron.
- The muonic hydrogen spectroscopy results are more than 7 standard deviations away from the combined electron-based result.
- The Mainz scattering experiment measured more than 1400 cross sections, about twice all other existing proton form factor data.
- Scattering extractions typically achieve uncertainties on the order of 1%.
- The muonic spectroscopy results quote more than ten times smaller uncertainties than the electronic measurements.
- The Mainz experiment using initial state radiation aims to lower the current limit for Q²_min by more than an order of magnitude.

## Context
This paper summarizes the state of the proton radius puzzle as of 2014, following the initial discrepancy reported by Bernauer (2010) and Pohl (2010). It extends the discussion of possible solutions including two-photon exchange corrections studied by Arrington (2011) and Bernauer (2011), and proposals for new physics such as light dark matter by Barger (2010) and Batell (2011). The review enables future work by cataloging upcoming experiments including the PRad experiment at Jefferson Lab, the MUSE experiment at Paul Scherrer Institute, and improved spectroscopy measurements from groups at LKB, MPQ, NPL, and York University.
