# Exploring anomalies by many-body correlations

> The quantum anomaly can be written alternatively into a form violating conservation laws or as non-gauge invariant currents seen explicitly on the example of chiral anomaly. By reinterpreting the many-body averaging, the connection to Pauli-Villars regularization is established which gives the anomalous term a new interpretation as arising from quantum fluctuations by many-body correlations at short distances. This is exemplified by using an effective many-body quantum potential which realizes quantum Slater sums by classical calculations. It is shown that these quantum potentials avoid the quantum anomaly but approaches the same anomalous result by many-body correlations. A measure for the quality of quantum potentials is suggested to describe these quantum fluctuations in the mean energy. Consequently quantum anomalies might be a short-cut way of single-particle field theory to account for many-body effects. This conjecture is also supported since the chiral anomaly can be derived by a completely conserving quantum kinetic theory.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that quantum anomalies, traditionally understood as symmetry-breaking effects in quantum field theory, can instead be reinterpreted as arising from many-body correlations at short distances, and that a proper many-body treatment reproduces the same physical results without any anomalous violation of conservation laws.

## Method

The author reformulates the many-body statistical average using an inverse Laplace transform and expands the interaction in a perturbation series, then compares the full quantum result with the classical limit (hbar → 0) to identify anomalous contributions. The analysis is performed for nonrelativistic systems with power-law potentials, examining the dependence on dimensionality D, potential exponent α, and perturbation order n. The author then constructs effective quantum potentials (Kelbg-type and Fermi-type) that reproduce quantum Slater sums via classical calculations, and shows that these potentials have finite values at short distances, thereby removing the divergence that causes the anomaly.

## Key numbers

* For three dimensions with Coulomb interaction (α=2), the anomalous energy shift is ΔH = (1/8) E₀, where E₀ is the characteristic energy scale (Rydberg).
* The binary quantum potential (Kelbg) for Maxwellian correlations has the small-distance limit V₂(0) = E₀ a₀ √π / l, where l = ℏ√(2β/m) is the thermal wavelength.
* The binary quantum potential for Fermi correlations at T=0 has the small-distance limit V₂f(0) = E₀ a₀ π/(4 l_F), where l_F = ℏ/p_F is the inverse Fermi momentum.
* The ratio c = ⟨E₁⟩/⟨E₁ - Eᶜ₁⟩ (used as a quality measure for quantum potentials) is approximately 9.24 for the binary Maxwellian potential, 4.57 for the ternary Maxwellian, 11.10 for the binary Fermi, and 6.46 for the ternary Fermi potential.

## Context

This work extends the author's earlier quantum kinetic derivation of the chiral anomaly (Morawetz 2018) and connects to the Pauli-Villars regularization scheme (Kaku 1991, Bertlmann 2000, Fujikawa 2004). The paper builds on the observation by Fukuda (2008) that the chiral anomaly can be obtained heuristically from a conventional phase-space argument, and on the earlier demonstration by the author (Morawetz 2010) that anomalous propagators in superconductivity can be replaced by a conserving T-matrix theory. The quantum potentials used are based on the Kelbg potential (Kelbg 1964, Ebeling et al. 1967) and the statistical equivalence approach (Morawetz 2001). The work suggests that anomalies in general may be a short-cut of single-particle field theory to account for many-body effects, opening the possibility of simulating strongly correlated quantum systems using classical calculations with effective quantum potentials.
