# Enhanced symmetry energy bears universality of the r-process

> The abundance of about half of the stable nuclei heavier than iron via the rapid neutron capture process or $r$-process is intimately related to the competition between neutron capture and $β$-decay rates, which ultimately depends on the binding energy of neutron-rich nuclei. The well-known Bethe-Weizsäcker semi-empirical mass formula\cite{weiz,bethe} describes the binding energy of ground states -- i.e. nuclei with temperatures of $T\approx0$ MeV -- with the symmetry energy parameter converging between $23-27$ MeV for heavy nuclei. Here we find an unexpected enhancement of the symmetry energy at higher temperatures, $T\approx0.7-1.0$ MeV, from the available data of giant dipole resonances built on excited states. Although these are likely the temperatures where seed elements are created -- during the cooling down of the ejecta following neutron-star mergers\cite{mergersnucleo} or collapsars\cite{collapsar} -- the fact that the symmetry energy remains constant between $T\approx0.7-1.0$ MeV, suggests a similar trend down to $T\approx0.5$ MeV, where neutron-capture may start occurring. Calculations using this relatively larger symmetry energy yield a reduction of the binding energy per nucleon for heavy neutron-rich nuclei and inhibits radiative neutron-capture rates. This results in a substantial close in of the neutron dripline -- where nuclei become unbound -- which elucidates the long sought universality of heavy-element abundances through the $r$-process; as inferred from the similar abundances found in extremely metal-poor stars and the Sun.

# Enhanced symmetry energy bears universality of the r-process

## Key claim

The symmetry energy parameter in the Bethe-Weizsäcker semi-empirical mass formula is unexpectedly enhanced at temperatures of T≈0.7–1.0 MeV (relevant to r-process seed formation) compared to ground-state values, and this enhancement appears constant across that temperature range, suggesting it persists down to T≈0.5 MeV where neutron capture begins. This larger symmetry energy reduces binding energies of neutron-rich nuclei, closes in the neutron dripline, and may explain the observed universality of r-process abundances across extremely metal-poor stars and the Sun.

## Method

The authors extracted the symmetry energy coefficient a_sym(A) from experimental giant dipole resonance (GDR) parameters, centroid energy E_GDR and width Γ_GDR, using the Steinwedel-Jensen hydrodynamic model relation for both ground states (T≈0 MeV) and excited states (T≈0.7–1.0 MeV). GDR cross-section data were obtained from the EXFOR and ENDF databases and fitted with Lorentzian curves to extract E_GDR and Γ_GDR, then a_sym(A) was calculated via Eq. (GDRsymm). The resulting a_sym(A) values were fitted to the leptodermous parametrization a_sym(A)=S_v(1 − (S_s/S_v)A^(−1/3)). The impact on the neutron dripline was evaluated using the Bethe-Weizsäcker SEMF with different symmetry energy values, and neutron-capture cross-section reductions were calculated using the TALYS and EMPIRE nuclear reaction codes.

## Key numbers

- Ground-state fit (T=0): a_sym(A)=35.3(7)(1−1.58(5)A^(−1/3)) MeV, with RMS deviation of 15%
- Excited-state fit (T≈0.7–1.0 MeV): a_sym(A)=46.2(2.4)(1−2.22(14)A^(−1/3)) MeV, with RMS deviation of 6%
- For heavy nuclei at T≈0.7–1.0 MeV, a_sym converges to approximately 30 MeV (compared to 23–27 MeV at T=0)
- The neutron dripline closes in from ²⁵⁴Pt to ²²⁰Pt when using a_sym=30 MeV instead of a_sym=23.7 MeV
- Neutron-capture cross sections in the A≈200 mass region are reduced by a factor of order 10² at the enhanced symmetry energy
- The centroid energy of the GDR increases by approximately 3–5% at T≈0.7–1.0 MeV compared to ground-state values for nearly-spherical nuclei (¹²⁰Sn, ²⁰⁸Pb, ²⁰¹Tl) and deformed nuclei in the A=160–180 region

## Context

This work extends the determination of the symmetry energy from ground-state GDR data (Berman 1975; Orce review) and from binding energy fits of isobaric nuclei (Tian 2013, using the 2012 atomic mass evaluation) to finite-temperature conditions relevant to r-process nucleosynthesis. The enhanced symmetry energy at T≈0.7–1.0 MeV supports earlier arguments by Berman that a large surface-to-volume symmetry energy ratio favors a close-in neutron dripline, constraining r-process reaction networks. The reduction in neutron-capture cross sections by a factor of ~10² in the A≈200 region is consistent with microscopic calculations by Goriely (2011) at T=1.5×10⁹ K. The results enable a potential explanation for the observed universality of r-process abundances between extremely metal-poor stars (Christlieb 2002; Sneden 2008) and the Sun, and motivate new experimental measurements of GDRs built on excited states below T≈0.7 MeV using facilities such as GAMKA in South Africa.
