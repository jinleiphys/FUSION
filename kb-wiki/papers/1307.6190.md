# Symmetry energy impact in simulations of core-collapse supernovae

> We present a review of a broad selection of nuclear matter equations of state (EOSs) applicable in core-collapse supernova studies. The large variety of nuclear matter properties, such as the symmetry energy, which are covered by these EOSs leads to distinct outcomes in supernova simulations. Many of the currently used EOS models can be ruled out by nuclear experiments, nuclear many-body calculations, and observations of neutron stars. In particular the two classical supernova EOS describe neutron matter poorly. Nevertheless, we explore their impact in supernova simulations since they are commonly used in astrophysics. They serve as extremely soft and stiff representative nuclear models. The corresponding supernova simulations represent two extreme cases, e.g., with respect to the protoneutron star (PNS) compactness and shock evolution. Moreover, in multi-dimensional supernova simulations EOS differences have a strong effect on the explosion dynamics. Because of the extreme behaviors of the classical supernova EOSs we also include DD2, a relativistic mean field EOS with density-dependent couplings, which is in satisfactory agreement with many current nuclear and observational constraints. This is the first time that DD2 is applied to supernova simulations and compared with the classical supernova EOS. We find that the overall behaviour of the latter EOS in supernova simulations lies in between the two extreme classical EOSs. As pointed out in previous studies, we confirm the impact of the symmetry energy on the electron fraction. Furthermore, we find that the symmetry energy becomes less important during the post bounce evolution, where conversely the symmetric part of the EOS becomes increasingly dominating, which is related to the high temperatures obtained. ## Key claim

The paper argues that while the symmetry energy significantly influences the electron fraction during core collapse, its impact diminishes during the post-bounce evolution where the symmetric part of the equation of state becomes increasingly dominant due to the high temperatures reached; this nuance is glossed over in the abstract's general statement about symmetry energy impact.

## Method

The authors performed spherically symmetric core-collapse supernova simulations using the AGILE-Boltztran code with general relativistic radiation hydrodynamics and three-flavor Boltzmann neutrino transport, applying the 11.2 solar mass progenitor model from Woosley (2002). They compared simulations using four equations of state: the classical LS220 (soft non-relativistic) and STOS (stiff relativistic mean field) models, the newer DD2 relativistic mean field model with density-dependent couplings, and a quark-bag hybrid EOS (QB) constructed from STOS hadronic matter with a bag constant of 139 MeV and strong coupling constant of 0.7.

## Key numbers

- LS220 and STOS are the two classical supernova EOSs, with LS220 having compressibility 220 MeV and symmetry energy 28.61 MeV, while STOS (based on TM1) has compressibility 281 MeV and symmetry energy 36.89 MeV
- HS(DD2) has saturation density 0.1491 fm^-3, incompressibility 243 MeV, symmetry energy 31.67 MeV, and slope parameter L = 55.04 MeV
- The QB hybrid EOS yields a hybrid star maximum mass of 2.04 solar masses, consistent with the neutron star mass constraint of 2.01 +/- 0.04 solar masses from Antoniadis (2013)
- Neutron star radius constraints from Steiner (2010) indicate R_{1.44 M_sun} = 10.4-12.9 km
- The DD2 EOS gives a 1.4 solar mass neutron star radius of 13.22 km, within 1 km of observational radius constraints
- Spherically symmetric simulations up to 300 ms post-bounce did not obtain explosions for the 11.2 solar mass progenitor

## Context

This work extends previous comparisons of supernova EOSs by Hempel (2012) and Steiner (2013), and is the first application of the DD2 EOS to core-collapse supernova simulations. The paper confirms earlier findings about symmetry energy impact on electron fraction from studies such as those by Lattimer (1991) and Shen (1998), while adding that the symmetric part of the EOS dominates post-bounce evolution. The results enable future multi-dimensional simulations to test whether the DD2 EOS, which satisfies nuclear constraints from Chiral Effective Field Theory (Hebeler 2010, Krueger 2013) and neutron star observations, produces different explosion dynamics compared to the classical but disfavored LS and STOS models.
