# Symmetry breaking and restoration on a fermionic quantum ring

> Background: The Hartree-Fock mean-field approximation is standard in combination with energy density functionals (EDF) that account for some dynamical correlations. Breaking and restoring the symmetries of the system allow for the inclusion of additional static correlations. However, exact solutions to evaluate the effectiveness of these methods are rare. Purpose: To benchmark the Hartree-Fock method with broken and restored rotational symmetry in a system of identical interacting fermions on a one-dimensional quantum ring using model interactions. Method: The ground-state wave function is found using the Hartree-Fock method both with rotational invariance and with the symmetry broken at the mean-field level. Rotational symmetry is then restored with an angular momentum projection method. The ground-state energies are compared to variational Monte Carlo predictions. This is done for a range of different interactions between the particles. Results: Breaking the rotational symmetry in the Hartree-Fock mean-field brings little improvement to the ground-state energy in weakly repulsive systems or attractive systems confined on small rings. Larger improvements are found in strongly repulsive systems and attractive systems on larger rings in which the particles form a self-bound system. Symmetry restoration brought only small improvements in most cases but was able to account for most of the remaining correlation energy (after symmetry breaking) in repulsive systems. Conclusions: The effectiveness of incorporating correlations through rotational symmetry breaking followed by angular momentum projection is demonstrated for one-dimensional quantum rings using model interactions, encouraging generalisations to other symmetries, extensions to higher dimensions, as well as applications in the EDF framework.

## Key claim

Breaking rotational symmetry in the Hartree-Fock mean-field provides significant improvement to ground-state energies only in strongly repulsive systems or attractive systems on large rings where particles form a self-bound system, while symmetry restoration via angular momentum projection accounts for most remaining correlation energy in repulsive systems but brings only small improvements in most cases.

## Method

The authors performed Hartree-Fock calculations on a one-dimensional quantum ring of radius \(R\) with \(N\) identical fermions, both with enforced rotational symmetry (USHF) and with symmetry broken via an imaginary time-step method using an external potential that was progressively removed. Rotational symmetry was then restored by angular momentum projection onto a state with good angular momentum \(M = N/2\) using Monte Carlo integration with the Metropolis algorithm. The resulting ground-state energies were benchmarked against variational Monte Carlo (VMC) calculations using a Jastrow trial wave function optimized with the DIRECT algorithm, for repulsive and attractive Coulomb-like potentials and two nuclear-model potentials (Nuclear 1 and Nuclear 2) at various ring radii and interaction strengths.

## Key numbers

- For \(N=2\) particles with repulsive Coulomb interaction (\(V_0=1\)) at ring radius \(R=1/2\), the VMC ground-state energy was \(2.2503 \pm 0.0008\) (dimensionless), compared to the analytical exact value \(2.2500\) and the USHF value \(2.2732\).
- For \(N=2\) particles with repulsive Coulomb interaction (\(V_0=1\)) at ring radius \(R=\sqrt{23/2}\), the VMC ground-state energy was \(0.19562 \pm 0.00003\) (dimensionless), compared to the analytical exact value \(0.19565\) and the USHF value \(0.20947\).
- The imaginary time step used in the BSHF method was \(d\tau = 10^{-4} / E_{\text{USHF}}\), with \(10^4\) iterations sufficient for convergence.
- The correlation energy accounted for by BSHF and RSHF methods was quantified as the percentage \(\frac{E_{\text{USHF}} - E_{\text{BSHF}}}{E_{\text{USHF}} - E_{\text{VMC}}}\) or \(\frac{E_{\text{USHF}} - E_{\text{RSHF}}}{E_{\text{USHF}} - E_{\text{VMC}}}\) for each system.

## Context

This work extends the benchmark study of Bray and Simenel (2021), which found that USHF gave good approximations for some quantum ring systems but failed for dense systems with strong short-ranged repulsion and attractive systems. The present study systematically evaluates whether rotational symmetry breaking and restoration can recover the missing static correlations in those problematic regimes. The results encourage generalizations to other symmetries, extensions to higher dimensions (e.g., spherium), and applications within the energy density functional framework for nuclear physics.
