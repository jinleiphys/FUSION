# Inverse-kinematics one-neutron pickup with fast rare-isotope beams

> New measurements and reaction model calculations are reported for single neutron pickup reactions onto a fast \nuc{22}{Mg} secondary beam at 84 MeV per nucleon. Measurements were made on both carbon and beryllium targets, having very different structures, allowing a first investigation of the likely nature of the pickup reaction mechanism. The measurements involve thick reaction targets and $γ$-ray spectroscopy of the projectile-like reaction residue for final-state resolution, that permit experiments with low incident beam rates compared to traditional low-energy transfer reactions. From measured longitudinal momentum distributions we show that the $\nuc{12}{C} (\nuc{22}{Mg},\nuc{23}{Mg}+γ)X$ reaction largely proceeds as a direct two-body reaction, the neutron transfer producing bound \nuc{11}{C} target residues. The corresponding reaction on the \nuc{9}{Be} target seems to largely leave the \nuc{8}{Be} residual nucleus unbound at excitation energies high in the continuum. We discuss the possible use of such fast-beam one-neutron pickup reactions to track single-particle strength in exotic nuclei, and also their expected sensitivity to neutron high-$\ell$ (intruder) states which are often direct indicators of shell evolution and the disappearance of magic numbers in the exotic regime.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that fast-beam one-neutron pickup reactions on carbon targets proceed predominantly as direct two-body transfers, making them a viable tool for tracking single-particle strength in exotic nuclei, but the same reaction on beryllium targets is dominated by pickup of strongly-bound core neutrons rather than the weakly-bound valence neutron, limiting its usefulness for quantitative spectroscopy.

## Method

The authors measured one-neutron pickup reactions using a 84 MeV per nucleon 22Mg secondary beam incident on thick 12C and 9Be targets at the NSCL. They identified the 23Mg projectile-like residues using the S800 spectrograph and performed gamma-ray spectroscopy with the SeGA detector array for final-state resolution. Coupled channels Born approximation (CCBA) calculations using the code FRESCO were carried out, incorporating shell-model spectroscopic factors from the USDB interaction for the projectile and known p-shell overlaps for the target nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Inclusive cross section for 12C(22Mg,23Mg+gamma)X: 2.40(19) mb at 84.7 MeV per nucleon mid-target energy
- Inclusive cross section for 9Be(22Mg,23Mg+gamma)X: 2.58(16) mb at 84.2 MeV per nucleon mid-target energy
- Partial cross section for the 5/2+ state at 451 keV in 23Mg: 1.27(14) mb on carbon target, 1.32(12) mb on beryllium target
- Partial cross section for the 7/2+ state at 2051 keV: 0.18(5) mb on carbon, 0.15(4) mb on beryllium
- Partial cross section for the 1/2+ state at 2359 keV: 0.08(5) mb on carbon, 0.13(4) mb on beryllium
- Partial cross section for the 9/2+ state at 2715 keV: 0.10(5) mb on carbon, 0.13(4) mb on beryllium
- Ground state (3/2+) cross section upper limit: less than or equal to 0.77(+0.09/-0.13) mb on carbon, less than or equal to 0.86(+0.08/-0.11) mb on beryllium
- 12C target neutron separation energy: Sn = 18.72 MeV
- 9Be valence neutron separation energy: Sn = 1.665 MeV
- 22Mg beam energy: 84 MeV per nucleon
- 12C target thickness: 149.4 mg/cm2
- 9Be target thickness: 188 mg/cm2

## Context

This work extends the methodology of fast-beam knockout reactions (Gade 2007, Hansen 2003) to the inverse-kinematics neutron pickup channel, complementing earlier studies of proton pickup from 9Be targets (Gade 2008). The results show that carbon targets enable direct two-body pickup suitable for spectroscopy, while beryllium targets are dominated by core pickup, contradicting the simpler valence-neutron picture. The technique enables future measurements of neutron high-ell intruder states in exotic nuclei, which are indicators of shell evolution and magic number disappearance, and which are difficult to populate in low-energy (d,p) transfer reactions due to angular momentum mismatch.
