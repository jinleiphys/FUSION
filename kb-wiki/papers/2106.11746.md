# Optimizing multilayer Bayesian neural networks for evaluation of fission yields

> The Bayesian machine learning is a promising tool for the evaluation of nuclear fission data but its potential capability has not been fully realized. We attempt to optimize the performances of the multilayer Bayesian neural networks for evaluations of fission yields. The influences of adjustments of learning data, activation functions, network structures have been studied. In particular, negative values of net functions have been penalized to avoid non-physical inferences of fission yields. Presently the network with double hidden layers has optimal performances compared to the single-layer or deeper networks. These studies are essential for further developments of precise evaluation methods.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a Bayesian neural network with double hidden layers (16-16 neurons) and a penalized likelihood function to suppress negative values provides optimal performance for evaluating fission yields, but this conclusion is hedged by the observation that deeper networks (3-7 hidden layers) showed no advantage and that the double-layer structure was optimal only for the specific problem and data set studied.

## Method

The authors trained feed-forward Bayesian neural networks on 5350 evaluated fission yield data points from the JENDL library for 30 nuclei, using inputs of fragment mass number, fissioning nucleus charge and mass, and compound nucleus excitation energy. They systematically tested the effects of linear data transformation to the range (-0.9, 0.9), four activation functions (tanh, sigmoid, ReLU, sine), network depths from one to seven hidden layers, and a penalty term added to the likelihood function to penalize negative output values. The posterior distributions were sampled via Markov Chain Monte Carlo, and predictions were made by integrating over the posterior.

## Key numbers

- Total learning data set: 5350 evaluated fission yield data points from JENDL for 30 nuclei.
- Total $\chi_N^2$ values for different activation functions (one hidden layer, 40 neurons): tanh = 5.78$\times$10$^{-6}$, sigmoid = 6.37$\times$10$^{-6}$, ReLU = 8.79$\times$10$^{-6}$, sine = 7.56$\times$10$^{-6}$.
- Total $\chi_N^2$ values for different network structures: 40-neuron single layer = 5.78$\times$10$^{-6}$, 66-neuron single layer = 4.35$\times$10$^{-6}$, 16-16 double layer = 3.43$\times$10$^{-6}$.
- Total $\chi_N^2$ values for deeper networks (all with similar total connection weights to the 16-16 structure): 3 layers (11-12-12) = 4.07$\times$10$^{-6}$, 4 layers (9-10-10-10) = 4.99$\times$10$^{-6}$, 5 layers (9-9-8-8-9) = 4.64$\times$10$^{-6}$, 6 layers (8-8-8-8-7-7) = 5.05$\times$10$^{-6}$, 7 layers (7-7-7-7-7-7-8) = 4.94$\times$10$^{-6}$.
- Normalization of fission yields was kept within an uncertainty of 2%.
- The confidential interval was given at the 95% level for uncertainty quantification.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous demonstration that Bayesian neural networks can evaluate incomplete fission mass yields (Wang and Pei, 2019) and fission charge yields (Qiao et al., 2020). It systematically optimizes network architecture and training choices that were not explored in those earlier studies. The optimized BNN approach is shown to produce evaluations of $^{239}$U fission yields that are consistent with the GEF model and ENDF evaluations, and the method enables high-precision evaluation of fission data where experimental measurements are sparse or have large uncertainties.
