# Dynamical instability of white dwarfs and breaking of spherical symmetry under the presence of extreme magnetic fields

> Massive, highly magnetized white dwarfs with fields up to $10^9$ G have been observed and theoretically used for the description of a variety of astrophysical phenomena. Ultramagnetized white dwarfs with uniform interior fields up to $10^{18}$ G, have been recently purported to obey a new maximum mass limit, $M_{\rm max}\approx 2.58~M_\odot$, which largely overcomes the traditional Chandrasekhar value, $M_{\rm Ch}\approx 1.44~M_\odot$. Such a much larger limit would make these astrophysical objects viable candidates for the explanation of the superluminous population of type Ia supernovae. We show that several macro and micro physical aspects such as gravitational, dynamical stability, breaking of spherical symmetry, general relativity, inverse $β$-decay, and pycnonuclear fusion reactions are of most relevance for the self-consistent description of the structure and assessment of stability of these objects. It is shown in this work that the first family of magnetized white dwarfs indeed satisfy all the criteria of stability, while the ultramagnetized white dwarfs are very unlikely to exist in nature since they violate minimal requests of stability. Therefore, the canonical Chandrasekhar mass limit of white dwarfs has to be still applied.

## Key claim

The paper argues that ultramagnetized white dwarfs with interior fields up to 10^18 G, which have been claimed to reach a maximum mass of about 2.58 solar masses, are very unlikely to exist in nature because they violate multiple stability criteria; the canonical Chandrasekhar mass limit of about 1.44 solar masses must still be applied.

## Method

The authors analyzed the stability of magnetized white dwarfs by evaluating the virial theorem constraint on magnetic field strength, calculating the quadrupole deformation (P_l-deformation) that breaks spherical symmetry, and assessing microscopic instabilities including inverse beta-decay and pycnonuclear fusion reactions. They also considered general relativistic effects by comparing the compactness of the proposed ultramagnetized configurations to neutron star parameters.

## Key numbers

- The virial theorem gives a maximum magnetic field B_max = 2.24 × 10^8 (M/M_sun)(R_sun/R)^2 G; for a 1.44 M_sun white dwarf with radius 3000 km, B_max ≈ 1.7 × 10^13 G
- For the three ultramagnetized configurations examined, the ratio W_B/|W_G| ≈ 235-273, far above the stability requirement W_B/|W_G| ≈ 1
- The quadrupole deformation parameter |epsilon/R| ≈ 195-223 for these configurations, indicating highly oblate shapes and strong breaking of spherical symmetry
- Inverse beta-decay limits magnetic fields to B ≈ 3.6 × 10^16 G (helium), 1.5 × 10^16 G (carbon), 9.1 × 10^15 G (oxygen), and 1.1 × 10^15 G (iron)
- Carbon-carbon pycnonuclear fusion at density 1.6 × 10^10 g cm^-3 occurs at a timescale of 0.1 Myr, limiting the magnetic field to B ≈ 1.1 × 10^16 G
- The maximum mass configuration (2.58 M_sun) would have radius ≈ 70 km, central density ≈ 1.2 × 10^13 g cm^-3, and compactness GM/(c^2 R) ≈ 0.05

## Context

This work extends the analysis of white dwarf stability by Boshkayev et al. (2013) and Rueda et al. (2011), who studied non-magnetized white dwarfs including general relativity, inverse beta-decay, and rotation, and contradicts the claim by Das and Mukhopadhyay (2013) that ultramagnetized white dwarfs can reach 2.58 solar masses. The results show that the virial theorem constraint from Chandrasekhar and Fermi (1953) already prohibits such extreme configurations, and that macroscopic dynamical instabilities set in before microscopic processes like electron captures and pycnonuclear reactions.
