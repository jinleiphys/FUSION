# Hard Probes: After the dust has settled!

> A lot has been learnt in the 15 years since the first data on jet modification at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC). These proceedings will describe the portion of the theory that is unassailable, and attempt to chart a course for the next period of precision measurements and related calculations. In particular, we will focus on the emerging series of higher order calculations, which may reveal the scale, energy and temperature dependence of jet transport coefficients, as well as develop ingredients for a future NLO calculation of jet modification. Connections with the underlying degrees of freedom in the Quark Gluon Plasma (QGP) will be explored. We will discuss the challenges faced by Monte-Carlo event generators and their comparison with a broad spectrum of jet modification data. First principles calculations of transport coefficients, and the determination of new transport coefficients which encapsulate the transition of energy momentum from the jet scale to the medium scale will also be discussed.

## Key claim
The paper argues that while leading-hadron suppression data can be described by multiple competing energy-loss formalisms (each with at least one adjustable parameter), full-jet observables require a unified framework that combines the high-virtuality, low-virtuality, and strongly-coupled regimes, and that no such overarching formalism currently exists.

## Method
The author reviews the three distinct physical regimes of jet evolution in a quark-gluon plasma: the high-energy-high-virtuality phase (described by DGLAP-based approaches such as MATTER and YaJEM), the high-energy-low-virtuality phase (described by BDMPS/AMY-based approaches such as JEWEL and MARTINI), and the low-energy-low-virtuality phase (described by AdS/CFT or hybrid formalisms). The paper then discusses the theoretical definitions of transport coefficients, including the transverse momentum diffusion parameter \(\hat{q}\) and the drag coefficient \(\hat{e}\), and introduces new higher-moment coefficients (\(\hat{q}_4\), \(\hat{e}_2\), \(\hat{e}_4\)) as well as a proposed energy-deposition diffusion coefficient \(\sigma(T,Q)\).

## Key numbers
- The lifetime and transverse size of a parton with energy \(E\) and virtuality \(\mu\) scale as \(\tau \sim E/\mu^2\) and \(\delta x^2 \sim 1/\mu^2 \ll 1/T^2\), where \(T\) is the medium temperature.
- For a 100 GeV jet traversing a 4 fm long QGP at fixed temperature with \(\hat{q} = 1\) GeV\(^2\)/fm, the leading parton loses virtuality much more rapidly than energy; the mean virtuality in the BDMPS regime scales as \(Q^2 \sim \sqrt{\hat{q} E}\).
- The transverse momentum diffusion parameter is defined as \(\hat{q} = \langle p_\perp^2 \rangle / L\) and the drag coefficient as \(\hat{e} = \langle p_z \rangle / L\), where \(L\) is the path length.
- Higher-moment transport coefficients are defined: \(\hat{q}_4 = (\langle p_\perp^4 \rangle - \langle p_\perp^2 \rangle^2)/L\), \(\hat{e}_2 = \langle \delta p_z^2 \rangle / L\), and \(\hat{e}_4 = (\langle \delta p_z^4 \rangle - \langle \delta p_z^2 \rangle^2)/L\).

## Context
This proceedings paper from the Hard Probes 2015 conference reviews the state of jet quenching theory 15 years after the first RHIC data, extending the earlier debates among the GLV, BDMS, Higher-Twist, and AMY approaches (Gyulassy et al., 1999-2000; Baier et al., 1994-1996; Wang and Guo, 2001; Arnold et al., 2001-2002). It contrasts the success of leading-hadron \(R_{AA}\) calculations (shown for CMS data at the LHC) with the unresolved challenges for full-jet observables, and calls for future NLO calculations (e.g., Fickinger et al., 2013) to bridge the gap between the high-virtuality and low-virtuality regimes. The work enables the development of a next-generation Monte-Carlo event generator that would incorporate both type I (momentum-exchange) and type II (energy-deposition) transport coefficients.
