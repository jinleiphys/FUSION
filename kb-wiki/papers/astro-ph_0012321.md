# On the minimum and maximum mass of neutron stars and the delayed collapse

> The minimum and maximum mass of protoneutron stars and neutron stars are investigated. The hot dense matter is described by relativistic (including hyperons) and non-relativistic equations of state. We show that the minimum mass ($\sim$ 0.88 - 1.28 $M_{\sun}$) of a neutron star is determined by the earliest stage of its evolution and is nearly unaffected by the presence of hyperons. The maximum mass of a neutron star is limited by the protoneutron star or hot neutron star stage. Further we find that the delayed collapse of a neutron star into a black hole during deleptonization is not only possible for equations of state with softening components, as for instance, hyperons, meson condensates etc., but also for neutron stars with a pure nucleonic-leptonic equation of state.

## Key claim

The minimum mass of a neutron star (0.878–1.284 solar masses) is set by the earliest protoneutron star stage (0.1–1 second after core bounce), not by cold neutron star models; the maximum mass is limited by the first ~30 seconds of evolution. The delayed collapse of a neutron star into a black hole during deleptonization is possible not only for equations of state with softening components like hyperons, but also for pure nucleonic-leptonic equations of state, provided the initial mass is high enough.

## Method

The authors constructed protoneutron star models at several evolutionary stages (0.1–1 s, 1–3 s, 10–30 s, and cold) using a non-relativistic Thomas-Fermi model and a relativistic Hartree model (with parameter sets GM1, GM3, NL1), both with and without hyperons (Λ, Σ, Ξ, Δ resonances). For the earliest stage (0.1–1 s after core bounce), they imposed a hot shocked envelope with entropy per baryon s = 4–6 and an unshocked core with s = 1–2, with trapped neutrinos at constant lepton number Y_l = 0.4. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to find the minimum and maximum gravitational and baryonic masses for each equation of state and each evolutionary stage.

## Key numbers

- Minimum gravitational mass of a neutron star: 0.878–1.284 solar masses (depending on equation of state and entropy profile), determined by the earliest protoneutron star stage (0.1–1 s after core bounce)
- Minimum baryonic mass: 0.949–1.338 solar masses for the same models
- Maximum gravitational mass of a cold neutron star: 1.699–2.663 solar masses across all equations of state studied
- For the NL1 nucleonic-leptonic equation of state, the maximum baryonic mass at 0.1–1 s is 3.218 solar masses, which exceeds the maximum baryonic mass at 1–3 s (3.162 solar masses), enabling delayed black hole formation
- For the GM3 nucleonic-leptonic equation of state, the maximum baryonic mass at 0.1–1 s is 2.204 solar masses, which is lower than the maximum at 1–3 s (2.212 solar masses), so no delayed collapse occurs
- For hyperonic equations of state, the baryonic mass difference between early (0.1–1 s) and late (1–3 s) protoneutron star stages is 0.146–0.158 solar masses; for pure nucleonic-leptonic equations of state, this difference is less than 0.056 solar masses
- The minimum gravitational mass is nearly unaffected by hyperons, with changes ΔM_G ≤ 0.007 solar masses

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations of neutron star minimum mass by Gondek (1997, 1998), Goussard et al. (1998), and Strobel (1999b), who used only nucleonic-leptonic equations of state, by including hyperons and a wider range of evolutionary stages. It contradicts the previous consensus (e.g., Takatsuka 1995, Bombaci 1996, Prakash 1997, Gondek 1998) that delayed black hole formation during deleptonization requires softening components like hyperons or meson condensates, by showing it can occur for pure nucleonic-leptonic equations of state when the earliest protoneutron star stage is considered. The results enable future time-dependent simulations of neutron star evolution to determine whether the predicted mass windows for delayed collapse are realized, and suggest that a cutoff in the neutrino signal a few seconds after core bounce could indicate black hole formation, though distinguishing between collapse due to deleptonization versus post-bounce accretion remains an open question.
