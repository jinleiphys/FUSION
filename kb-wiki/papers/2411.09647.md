# A unified description of small, peripheral, and large system suppression data from pQCD

> We present quantitative predictions for the nuclear modification factor in both small and peripheral systems from a pQCD-based energy loss model that is constrained by light- and heavy-flavor suppression data from central heavy-ion collisions. We find nearly identical suppression for central $p / d + A$ collisions as for peripheral $A + A$ collisions, quantitatively consistent with the measured 20% suppression of neutral pions produced in $d + \mathrm{Au}$ collisions by PHENIX, but dramatically inconsistent with the measured 20% enhancement of charged hadrons produced in $p + \mathrm{Pb}$ collisions by ATLAS. We demonstrate that this equivalence of central small system suppression and peripheral large system suppression is insensitive to the underlying energy loss model.

## Key claim
The paper claims that pQCD-based energy loss models predict nearly identical suppression in central small systems (p/d + A) and peripheral large systems (A + A), and that this equivalence is insensitive to the underlying energy loss mechanism. However, this prediction is quantitatively consistent with PHENIX d+Au data showing 20% suppression but dramatically inconsistent with ATLAS p+Pb data showing 20% enhancement, suggesting the ATLAS result may be affected by centrality bias from Glauber model normalization.

## Method
The authors constrain a convolved radiative and collisional pQCD energy loss model by fitting the effective strong coupling constant α_s^eff to 245 experimental data points from central heavy-ion collisions at RHIC (0.2 TeV) and LHC (5.02 TeV), including charged hadron, neutral pion, and D meson R_AA measurements from CMS, ATLAS, ALICE, PHENIX, and STAR. They then apply the large-system-constrained model to predict R_AB for central p/d+A and peripheral A+A collisions, incorporating small system size corrections to both radiative (DGLV with short path length correction) and collisional (HTL) energy loss, with IP-Glasma initial conditions and Bjorken expansion. A separate model-agnostic analysis considers parametric energy loss forms ΔE ∝ L^a T^b f(E) for collisional, GLV, BDMPS-Z, and AdS/CFT models.

## Key numbers
- PHENIX measures R_AB ~ 0.75 for neutral pions at p_T = 8–17 GeV in 0–5% centrality d+Au collisions at √s_NN = 0.2 TeV
- ATLAS measures R_AB ~ 1.2 for charged hadrons at p_T = 5–30 GeV in 0–5% centrality p+Pb collisions at √s_NN = 5.02 TeV
- The model predicts ≥ 30% suppression in central p+Pb collisions, inconsistent with the measured ~20% enhancement
- Extracted α_s^eff values range from 0.3 to 0.5 across 14 model variations, an ~25% uncertainty
- Central small systems are ~50% smaller and ~30–50% hotter than peripheral large systems
- Peripheral A+A collisions involve ~5 times as many binary collisions as central p/d+A collisions (O(50) vs O(10))
- Theoretical uncertainty on predicted R_AB for central d+Au and peripheral Au+Au is ≤ 15%; for central p+Pb it is O(10–30%)
- The constant energy loss contour analysis uses f(E=10 GeV) and Debye mass μ_D = 0.5 GeV

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous studies on small system size corrections to radiative (Faraday 2023) and collisional (Faraday 2024) energy loss, and provides the first justifiable extrapolation of any energy loss model to small systems without additional tuning. It contradicts the ATLAS (2022) measurement of charged hadron enhancement in p+Pb collisions, while agreeing with PHENIX (2023) d+Au suppression data and peripheral A+A data from ATLAS (2022) and PHENIX (2012). The results enable future experimental tests using photon- or Z-normalized R_pA measurements (as in PHENIX 2023 and Huss 2020), centrality-averaged R_AA in O+O collisions (Huss 2020, Citron 2018), and self-normalized jet substructure observables (Kolbe 2023), which would minimize centrality bias.
