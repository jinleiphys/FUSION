# Core-collapse supernovae and supernova neutrinos

> Core-collapse supernovae are the terminal explosions of massive stars. After successive phases of nuclear fusion proceeding up to silicon burning, these stars form an iron core that is supported by electron degeneracy pressure. The core eventually collapses to a proto-neutron star, and in most cases the outer layers of the star are ejected by a shock wave, with a kinetic energy of order $10^{51}\,\mathrm{erg}$. Neutrinos and multi-dimensional fluid flow play a key role in extracting energy from the collapsed core to drive the explosion. After adumbrating the astrophysical context of stellar evolution and transient observations, this chapter sketches the modern theory of neutrino-driven supernova explosions, and discusses the key role of nuclear physics and neutrino interaction rates in the supernova problem. It also outlines the role of neutrinos and gravitational waves as probes into the supernova core.

## Key claim

This chapter provides a pedagogical overview of the modern theory of neutrino-driven core-collapse supernovae, but the authors explicitly note that the neutrino-driven mechanism is only thought to power "the vast majority of CCSNe with typical explosion energies," and that unusually high-energy events such as Type Ic-BL supernovae (up to ~10^52 erg) likely require a different mechanism involving gamma-ray burst jets.

## Method

The authors synthesize the established theoretical framework of core-collapse supernova dynamics, from iron core formation through bounce, shock stall, and the neutrino-driven revival mechanism. They review the role of multi-dimensional fluid instabilities (convection and the standing accretion shock instability, SASI) in aiding shock revival, and summarize observational constraints from light curves, spectra, progenitor identifications, and compact object kicks. The chapter also surveys the current state of 3D simulations from multiple groups (CoCoNuT-FMT, Fornax, Chimera, Prometheus-Vertex, 3DnSNe-IDSA, Flash, and Fugra) and discusses the pathways to black hole formation.

## Key numbers

- Explosion kinetic energy of order 10^51 erg for typical core-collapse supernovae
- Gravitational potential energy liberated during collapse: E_grav ~ GM^2/R > 10^53 erg
- Proto-neutron star radius: R ≈ 12 km for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star
- Type IIP supernova explosion energies: range of 0.1 to 2 × 10^51 erg
- Nickel-56 masses in Type IIP supernovae: 5 × 10^-3 to 0.28 solar masses, with mean 0.046 solar masses
- SN 1987A explosion energy: ~1.5 × 10^51 erg, ejecta mass ~14 solar masses, nickel-56 mass 0.07 solar masses
- Type Ic-BL supernova explosion energies: up to ~10^52 erg, about ten times higher than ordinary events
- Neutrino luminosities from the proto-neutron star surface: several 10^52 erg s^-1
- Neutrino heating efficiency in the gain region: ~10%
- Galactic core-collapse supernova rate: 1-3 events per century
- Failed supernova optical transient energy: ~10^47 erg
- Neutron star birth kicks: up to 1000 km s^-1; black hole kicks: several 100 km s^-1
- Core densities at collapse onset: 10^9 to 10^10 g cm^-3
- Post-shock density for rapid neutrino energy losses: ~10^11 g cm^-3
- Neutrino trapping density: > 10^12 g cm^-3
- Shock stall radius: 100-200 km
- Type IIP supernova light curve plateau duration: ~100 days

## Context

This chapter updates and extends the extensive review literature on core-collapse supernovae, including the reviews by Mueller (2020), Burrows (2021), and Janka (2025) for hydrodynamics and explosion outcomes, and the reviews by Mirizzi (2016), Horiuchi (2018), and Mezzacappa (2020) for neutrino aspects. It builds on the foundational neutrino-driven mechanism proposed by Colgate (1966) and shaped by Bethe (1985), and addresses the "red supergiant problem" of Smartt (2009) regarding the paucity of observed explosions from high-mass progenitors. The chapter enables future multi-messenger observations of supernova neutrinos and gravitational waves from Galactic events, and provides the theoretical context for interpreting signals from the next Galactic supernova.
