# Mass Predictions of Atomic Nuclei in the Infinite Nuclear Matter Model

> We present here the mass excesses, binding energies, one- and two- neutron, one and two- proton and α-particle separation energies of 6727 nuclei in the ranges 4 \leq Z \leq 120 and 8 \leq A \leq 303 calculated in the infinite nuclear matter model. Compared to our predictions of 1999 mass table, the present ones are obtained using larger data base of 2003 mass table of Wapstra and Audi and resorting to higher accuracy in the solutions of the η-differential equations of the INM model. The local energy η's supposed to carry signature of the characteristic properties of nuclei are found to possess the predictive capability. In fact η-systematics reveal new magic numbers in the drip-line regions giving rise to new islands of stability supported by relativistic mean field theoretic calculations. This is a manifestation of a new phenomenon where shell-effect overcomes the instability due to repulsive components of the nucleon-nucleon force broadening the stability peninsula. The two-neutron separation energy-systematics derived from the present mass predictions reveal a general new feature for the existence of islands of inversion in the exotic neutron rich regions of nuclear landscape, apart from supporting the presently known islands around 31Na and 62Ti. The five global parameters representing the properties of infinite nuclear matter, the surface, the Coulomb and the pairing terms are retained as per our 1999 mass table. The root-mean-square deviation of the present mass-fit to 2198 known masses is 342 keV, while the mean deviation is 1.3 keV, reminiscent of no left-over systematic effects. This is a substantive improvement over our 1999 mass table having rms deviation of 401 keV and mean deviation of 9 keV for 1884 data nuclei.

## Key claim

The Infinite Nuclear Matter (INM) model achieves a root-mean-square deviation of 342 keV and a mean deviation of only 1.3 keV when fitted to 2198 known nuclear masses, representing a substantive improvement over the authors' 1999 mass table (which had rms deviation of 401 keV for 1884 data nuclei). The authors claim this near-zero mean deviation indicates no leftover systematic effects, and that the local energy eta-systematics reveal new magic numbers in drip-line regions giving rise to new islands of stability.

## Method

The authors apply the Infinite Nuclear Matter (INM) model, which replaces the classical liquid-drop with a sphere of infinite nuclear matter and uses the Extended Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem to separate nuclear energy into three decoupled components: an infinite part E(A,Z), a finite-size part f(A,Z) containing surface, Coulomb, and pairing terms, and a local energy eta(A,Z) that carries shell, deformation, and diffuseness effects. The five global parameters (volume, asymmetry, surface, Coulomb, and pairing coefficients) are retained from the 1999 mass table, while the local energy eta for each nucleus is determined by solving a partial differential equation in (N,Z) space using a hexagonal grid ensemble-averaging procedure with Gaussian weighting, applied to 2198 experimentally known masses from the 2003 Wapstra-Audi mass table. The final mass predictions cover 6727 nuclei in the ranges 4 ≤ Z ≤ 120 and 8 ≤ A ≤ 303, extending up to and slightly beyond the drip lines.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous INM model mass tables from 1988 (3481 nuclei) and 1999 (7208 nuclei), using a larger experimental database (2003 Wapstra-Audi table) and higher-precision solutions of the eta-differential equations. The paper demonstrates that INM model predictions, when used as a reference in comparative studies, show common trends with other mass models (such as the Finite-Range Droplet Model of Moeller and Nix, and the Duflo-Zucker model) in unknown regions, unlike the random divergences seen when other models are compared among themselves. The eta-systematics predict new neutron magic numbers 100, 150, and 164 and proton magic numbers 78, 62, and 90, with new islands of stability around 162Sm, 228Pt, and 254Th, corroborated by Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) theory calculations using the NL3 parameter set. The results also reaffirm shell quenching in the N=82 and N=126 shells, relevant for r-process nucleosynthesis studies.
