# Dynamics and Instabilities of Nuclear Fermi Liquid

> The kinetic theory of the Fermi liquid is applied to finite nuclei. The nuclear collective motion is treated in terms of the observable variables: particle density, current density, pressure etc. The nuclear dynamics is influenced strongly by the Fermi-surface distortion (FSD) effect. The relaxation of collective motion and the width of giant multipole resonances in hot nuclei show a saturated behaviour with temperature caused by the memory effect in the collision integral. The development of the bulk and surface instabilities of the nuclear Fermi-liquid is sensitive to the dynamic effects such as the FSD and the relaxation processes. The instability growth rate is reduced due to the FSD effect and depends on the multipolarity of the particle density and surface fluctuations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the dynamic Fermi-surface distortion (FSD) effect strongly influences nuclear collective motion, relaxation, and instabilities in finite nuclei, but that for the lowest monopole mode the FSD-induced renormalization of nuclear incompressibility is compensated by the consistent treatment of the same effect in the boundary condition, keeping the giant monopole resonance energy close to the traditional liquid-drop model prediction.

## Method

The author applies kinetic theory of Fermi liquid to finite nuclei, starting from the kinetic equation for the phase-space distribution function and transforming it into a set of moment equations for observable variables (density, velocity field, pressure tensor). The dynamic Fermi-surface distortions are included up to multipolarity \(l = 2\), and the equation of motion is reduced to a secular equation for the macroscopic variable \(\beta(t)\) with frequency-dependent stiffness and dissipative terms. The collisional relaxation time is derived from the collision integral including memory effects, and the total width of collective states is obtained by combining contributions from interparticle collisions, one-body dissipation, and particle emission.

## Key numbers

- For the isoscalar mode, the coefficient \(\alpha^{(+)} = 9.2\ \text{MeV}\) and for the isovector mode \(\alpha^{(-)} = 4.6\ \text{MeV}\) in the relation \(\tilde{\tau}_2(T)/\hbar = \alpha^{(\pm)} T^{-2}\) at low temperatures \(T \ll \epsilon_F\).
- The transition temperature for the isovector GDR at realistic \(x = 0.19\) is \(T_{\text{tr}} \approx 4.5\ \text{MeV}\); using \(x=1\) and \(\tau = \tau_2\) gives \(T_{\text{tr}} \approx 10\ \text{MeV}\).
- The static incompressibility is parameterized as \(K = K_\infty + K_\sigma A^{-1/3}\) with \(K_\infty = 220\ \text{MeV}\) and \(K_\sigma = -440\ \text{MeV}\), using \(\rho_0 = 0.17\ \text{fm}^{-3}\), \(\epsilon_F = 40\ \text{MeV}\), \(r_0 = 1.12\ \text{fm}\), and \(\sigma = 1.2\ \text{MeV/fm}^2\).
- The condition for the transition from long to short relaxation time regimes is \(\omega_0 \tau = y_0 = x^{-1/2}\), with \(y_0 = 2.28\) for the isovector GDR at \(x = 0.19\).

## Context

This work extends the nuclear fluid dynamic approach of Kob (year not given), HoEc1 (year not given), and KoPlSh1 (year not given) by incorporating dynamic Fermi-surface distortions up to \(l=2\) and memory effects in the collision integral. It contrasts with the traditional liquid-drop model (LDM) of BoMo (year not given), which neglects Fermi-surface distortion effects, and shows that for the lowest monopole mode the FSD effect is compensated by boundary conditions, while for higher modes (\(n \geq 2\)) a significant upward shift occurs. The results enable improved understanding of giant multipole resonance widths in hot nuclei and the development of bulk and surface instabilities relevant to nuclear fission and multifragmentation.
