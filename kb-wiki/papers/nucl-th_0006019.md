# Microcanonical Lattice Gas Model for Nuclear Disassembly

# Microcanonical Lattice Gas Model for Nuclear Disassembly

## Key claim
Microcanonical calculations in the Lattice Gas Model are no more difficult to implement than canonical calculations, and for systems down to mass number 84, there are no serious departures between microcanonical and canonical results for the observables studied, despite significantly larger temperature fluctuations in the microcanonical ensemble for lighter systems.

## Method
The authors modify the standard canonical Lattice Gas Model (CLGM) by fixing the total energy instead of temperature. Starting from a given configuration with known potential energy, the kinetic energy is determined by energy conservation. A Metropolis switch in configuration space is accepted or rejected based on the ratio of phase space integrals for the kinetic energy, which depends analytically on the number of nucleons and the kinetic energy. After accepting a configuration, momenta are assigned via Monte Carlo sampling of a uniform distribution in momentum space, normalized to the correct kinetic energy. The event temperature is extracted from the kinetic energy using the relation (3/2)AT = E_kin.

## Key numbers
- Bond energy between nearest neighbor neutron and proton: epsilon_np = -5.33 MeV (neutron-neutron and proton-proton bonds set to 0)
- For 84Kr at average temperature <T> = 5.0 MeV, the temperature fluctuation <T^2> - <T>^2 is 6.59 MeV^2 in the microcanonical model
- For 197Au at the same average temperature <T> = 5.0 MeV, the temperature fluctuation is 1.1 MeV^2
- Lattice sizes used: 6^3 for 84Kr, 8^3 for 197Au
- IMF definition: intermediate mass fragments with Z between 3 and 20

## Context
This work extends the Lattice Gas Model approach of Samaddar and Das Gupta (2000) by implementing a microcanonical version (MLGM) alongside the standard canonical version (CLGM). It addresses the common argument that fixed-energy calculations may be more appropriate for nuclear disassembly than fixed-temperature calculations. The results show that for average quantities like the caloric curve and for IMF emission probabilities and fluctuations, the two ensembles give nearly identical results even for intermediate-mass systems. The paper enables future microcanonical studies of nuclear fragmentation without requiring significantly more computational effort than canonical approaches, and it provides a framework for extracting event-by-event temperatures that is attractive from an experimental perspective.
