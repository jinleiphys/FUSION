# Relativistic viscous hydrodynamics for heavy-ion collisions: A comparison between the Chapman-Enskog and Grad methods

> Derivations of relativistic second-order dissipative hydrodynamic equations have relied almost exclusively on the use of Grad's 14-moment approximation to write $f(x,p)$, the nonequilibrium distribution function in the phase space. Here we consider an alternative Chapman-Enskog-like method, which, unlike Grad's, involves a small expansion parameter. We derive an expression for $f(x,p)$ to second order in this parameter. We show analytically that while Grad's method leads to the violation of the experimentally observed $1/\sqrt{m_T}$ scaling of the longitudinal femtoscopic radii, the alternative method does not exhibit such an unphysical behavior. We compare numerical results for hadron transverse-momentum spectra and femtoscopic radii obtained in these two methods, within the one-dimensional scaling expansion scenario. Moreover, we demonstrate a rapid convergence of the Chapman-Enskog-like expansion up to second order. This leads to an expression for $δf(x,p)$ which provides a better alternative to Grad's approximation for hydrodynamic modeling of relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a Chapman-Enskog-like iterative solution of the Boltzmann equation (using the Knudsen number as a small expansion parameter) yields a form for the viscous correction to the distribution function that preserves the experimentally observed \(1/\sqrt{m_T}\) scaling of longitudinal femtoscopic radii, whereas Grad's 14-moment approximation violates this scaling and produces unphysical imaginary radii at high transverse momentum.

## Method

The authors solved the relativistic Boltzmann equation in the relaxation-time approximation iteratively to obtain a Chapman-Enskog-like expansion of the nonequilibrium distribution function \(f(x,p)\) to second order in gradients. They then used this expression for \(\delta f\) to compute hadron transverse-momentum spectra and longitudinal Hanbury-Brown-Twiss (HBT) radii via the Cooper-Frye freezeout prescription, within a one-dimensional Bjorken scaling expansion scenario. For comparison, they performed the same calculations using Grad's 14-moment approximation for \(\delta f\), evolving the system with second-order viscous hydrodynamic equations in both cases.

## Key numbers

* Initial conditions for central Au-Au collisions at RHIC: temperature \(T_0 = 360\) MeV, proper time \(\tau_0 = 0.6\) fm/\(c\), isotropic pressure \(\Phi_0 = 0\).
* Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio: \(\eta/s = 1/4\pi\) (Kovtun-Son-Starinets lower bound).
* Freezeout temperature: \(T = 150\) MeV.
* For large \(z_m = m_T/T\), the viscous correction to \(R_L^2\) in the Chapman-Enskog case scales as \((\delta R_L^2)^{(1)} = -5\tau^2 T \Phi / (4\beta_\pi m_T)\), preserving the \(1/\sqrt{m_T}\) scaling.
* For large \(z_m\), the viscous correction in Grad's approximation scales as \((\delta R_L^2)^{(G)} = -\tau^2 T \Phi / (5\beta_\pi m_T) \cdot (3 + m_T/T)\), which grows as \(m_T/T\) relative to the ideal result.
* In Grad's approximation, \(R_L\) becomes imaginary for pion pair transverse momentum \(K_T \gtrsim 0.9\) GeV/\(c\).

## Context

This work extends the comparison between Grad's 14-moment approximation (standard in relativistic heavy-ion hydrodynamics since Israel (1979) and Muronga (2003)) and the Chapman-Enskog method, which had seldom been applied in this context. The authors build on prior analytical work by Jaiswal (2013) and numerical studies by Teaney (2003) showing that Grad's approximation produces unphysical behavior in HBT radii. The proposed Chapman-Enskog-like form for \(\delta f\) provides a better alternative for hydrodynamic modeling of relativistic heavy-ion collisions, enabling more reliable calculations of femtoscopic observables and hadron spectra at large transverse momentum.
