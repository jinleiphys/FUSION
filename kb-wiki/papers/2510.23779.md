# Microscopic study of the low-energy enhancement in the gamma-decay strength of \(^{50}\)V

> We address the microscopic origin of the low-energy enhancement (LEE) in \(^{50}\)V with large-scale shell-model calculations to obtain $E1$ and $M1$ transitions within the same theoretical framework. The valence space spans the three major shells $sd$, $pf$ and $sdg$ and is treated with the SDPFSDG-MU interaction using the KSHELL code. With a \(1 \hbar ω\) truncation, 3600 energy eigenstates and a basis of $7.02\times10^{6}$ positive and $5.94\times10^{8}$ negative parity states, the calculations yield nearly two million individual dipole transitions. The fourteen lowest experimental levels are reproduced within $0.30$~MeV, the calculated total level density excellently reproduces Oslo-method data up to $E \approx 7.5$~MeV, and the calculated dipole gamma strength function follows the experimental shape -- including the LEE -- for the full gamma-energy range covered by the Oslo experiment. The LEE is shown to be entirely magnetic dipole in origin. Both spin and orbital parts of the \(\hat{M}1\) operator are required to reproduce the LEE, with constructive interference between the spin and orbital parts giving an extra enhancement to the LEE. Reduced one-body transition densities identify $0f_{7/2} \rightarrow 0f_{7/2}$ proton transitions as the principal driver of the LEE.

# Microscopic study of the low-energy enhancement in the gamma-decay strength of \(^{50}\)V

## Key claim
The low-energy enhancement (LEE) in the gamma-decay strength of \(^{50}\)V is entirely magnetic dipole (\(M1\)) in origin, with both spin and orbital parts of the \(M1\) operator required to reproduce the LEE and constructive interference between them giving an extra enhancement. The \(0f_{7/2} \rightarrow 0f_{7/2}\) proton transitions are identified as the principal driver of the LEE.

## Method
Large-scale shell-model calculations were performed using the KSHELL code with the SDPFSDG-MU effective interaction spanning the \(sd\), \(pf\), and \(sdg\) major shells. A \(1\hbar\omega\) truncation was applied, yielding 3600 energy eigenstates from a basis of \(7.02\times10^{6}\) positive-parity and \(5.94\times10^{8}\) negative-parity states, from which nearly two million individual dipole transitions (\(E1\) and \(M1\)) were computed within the same theoretical framework. The \(g_s\) values were quenched by a factor of 0.9, and orbital \(g\) factors of \(g_l = (1.1, -0.1)\) were used for protons and neutrons respectively following the effective \(M1\) operator of Ref. PhysRevC.69.034335.

## Key numbers
- The fourteen lowest experimental levels of \(^{50}\)V are reproduced within a maximum difference of 0.30 MeV and a mean difference of 0.19 MeV.
- The calculated total level density excellently reproduces Oslo-method data up to excitation energy \(E \approx 7.5\) MeV.
- The \(M\)-scheme dimension was \(7.02 \times 10^6\) for positive-parity states and \(5.94 \times 10^8\) for negative-parity states.
- The calculations yielded 959834 \(E1\) transitions and 938347 \(M1\) transitions after excluding transitions with zero gamma energy.
- The calculated ground-state magnetic moment of \(^{50}\)V is \(+2.99 \, \mu_N\), compared to the experimental value of \(+3.35 \, \mu_N\).
- The first calculated negative-parity levels (\(2^-\), \(3^-\), \(4^-\)) appear at 2.16, 2.20, and 2.31 MeV, coinciding with experimental negative levels at 2.16, 2.42, and 2.52 MeV.
- The Lawson parameter used was \(\beta_{\mathrm{CM}} \hbar \omega / A = 10\) MeV.

## Context
This work extends previous shell-model studies of the LEE that either included only \(M1\) transitions (Schwengner et al., PhysRevLett.111.232504 for \(^{90}\)Zr and \(^{94,95,96}\)Mo) or calculated both \(E1\) and \(M1\) in smaller model spaces (Sieja, PhysRevLett.119.052502 for \(^{44}\)Sc; Liddick et al., PhysRevC.100.024624 for \(^{51}\)Ti). The novelty is the use of a three-major-shell valence space supporting both \(E1\) and \(M1\) transitions within the same framework for an odd-odd nucleus. The results enable improved theoretical radiative neutron-capture cross sections for \(^{50}\)V, which is relevant for core-collapse supernova nucleosynthesis and massive star nucleosynthesis calculations where experimental data is unavailable and theoretical rates can vary by an order of magnitude or more.
