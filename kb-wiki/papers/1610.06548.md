# Status and Future of Nuclear Matrix Elements for Neutrinoless Double-Beta Decay: A Review

> The nuclear matrix elements that govern the rate of neutrinoless double beta decay must be accurately calculated if experiments are to reach their full potential. Theorists have been working on the problem for a long time but have recently stepped up their efforts as ton-scale experiments have begun to look feasible. Here we review past and recent work on the matrix elements in a wide variety of nuclear models and discuss work that will be done in the near future. Ab initio nuclear-structure theory, which is developing rapidly, holds out hope of more accurate matrix elements with quantifiable error bars.

## Key claim

The nuclear matrix elements for neutrinoless double-beta decay currently carry uncertainties of roughly a factor of three, and the real theoretical uncertainty is likely larger because the "g_A problem" has been ignored in most analyses; the authors express optimism that chiral effective field theory and ab initio many-body methods will produce reliable matrix elements with quantified uncertainties within the next five or so years.

## Method

The paper reviews calculations of neutrinoless double-beta decay nuclear matrix elements across a wide variety of nuclear models, including the closure approximation (which replaces intermediate-state energies with an average value and reproduces the unapproximated matrix element to within 10%) and the impulse approximation (using free-nucleon current operators). The authors examine the formalism for light-neutrino exchange, heavy-particle exchange mechanisms, and the role of chiral effective field theory in treating short-range physics and pion-exchange contributions.

## Key numbers

- The closure approximation reproduces the unapproximated neutrinoless double-beta matrix element to within 10%, though tests have not included states above 10s of MeV
- The momentum transfer in neutrinoless double-beta decay is about 100 MeV (the average inverse spacing between nucleons)
- The smaller neutrino mass-squared difference from solar experiments is about 75 meV^2, and the larger from atmospheric experiments is about 2400 meV^2
- The axial coupling constant g_A(0) is approximately 1.27 from neutron beta-decay measurements
- The KamLAND-Zen experiment gives an upper limit on m_beta-beta of 61-165 meV, with the uncertainty due almost entirely to the matrix element
- The next generation of experiments aims to fully cover the region m_beta-beta greater than or approximately equal to 10 meV
- Heavy-particle exchange with mass m_H of order 1 TeV can compete with light-neutrino exchange
- The pseudoscalar coupling g_P is related to g_A by the Goldberger-Treiman relation with pion mass m_pi approximately 138 MeV

## Context

This review extends earlier pedagogical treatments of double-beta decay by Avi08 and Ver12, and surveys matrix element calculations across multiple nuclear models including those by Sim99, Rod06, and Hor10. The paper discusses how improved matrix elements would enable better isotope selection for ton-scale experiments (such as KamLAND-Zen, EXO, GERDA, and CUORE) and allow discrimination between light-neutrino exchange and new physics mechanisms (such as left-right symmetric models by Moh80/Moh81, supersymmetric particles by Moh86/Ver87, and Majoron emission by Chi81/Gel81/Geo81). The authors identify ab initio methods (Carlson15, Hebeler15, Hagen14, Hergert16) and chiral effective field theory (Chiral, Machleidt11) as the path forward for quantified uncertainties.
