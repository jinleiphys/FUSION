# An attempt to incorporate separate chemical and thermal freeze outs in hydrodynamics

> From an analysis of various types of data obtained in relativistic nuclear collisions, the following picture has emerged in thermal and hydrodynamical descriptions: as the fluid expands and cools, particles first undergo a chemical freeze out at T_ch.~160-200 MeV then a thermal freeze out at T_th~100-140 MeV. In this paper we show how to incorporate these separate freeze outs consistently in a hydrodynamical code via a modified equation of state (general case) or via a modified Cooper-Frye formula (particular case of T_ch. close to T_th. or few particle species undergoing early chemical freeze-out). The modified equation of state causes faster cooling and may have sizable impact on the predicted values of observables.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates how to incorporate separate chemical and thermal freeze-out temperatures into a hydrodynamical code, showing that the modified equation of state causes faster cooling and may have sizable impact on predicted observables, but only if the chemical and thermal freeze-out temperatures are very different (e.g., 180 and 110 MeV) or if many particle species undergo early chemical freeze-out.

## Method

The authors modify the equation of state in a Bjorken one-dimensional boost-invariant hydrodynamical model to account for particles that undergo chemical freeze-out at a higher temperature than thermal freeze-out, introducing separate conservation laws for chemically frozen species like Lambda and anti-Lambda. For the case where chemical and thermal freeze-out temperatures are close, they instead use a modified Cooper-Frye formula that normalizes the thermal freeze-out spectrum to the particle abundances fixed at chemical freeze-out. The modified equation of state is implemented using a gas of non-interacting resonances with approximations valid when particle densities are low and masses are large compared to temperature.

## Key numbers

- Chemical freeze-out temperature range: T_ch ~ 160-200 MeV (from analysis of SPS data)
- Thermal freeze-out temperature range: T_th ~ 100-140 MeV (from analysis of SPS data)
- For initial conditions T_0 = mu_B0 = 200 MeV, tau_0 = 1 fm, with chemical freeze-out at 180 MeV: mu_B at chemical freeze-out = 210 MeV
- For thermal freeze-out at 110 MeV: thermal freeze-out time is 13 fm for modified equation of state versus 20 fm for unmodified equation of state
- For thermal freeze-out at 110 MeV: baryonic potential is 405 MeV for modified equation of state versus 375 MeV for unmodified equation of state
- For HYLANDER-PLUS calculations: T_th fixed at 139 MeV, T_ch tested at 176 MeV and 184 MeV

## Context

This work extends the picture emerging from thermal and hydrodynamical descriptions of relativistic nuclear collisions at SIS, AGS, and SPS energies, as compiled by Cleymans and Redlich (1999), where separate chemical and thermal freeze-outs are observed. The paper addresses the gap that no hydrodynamical code at the time included both freeze-outs self-consistently, building on prior work by Hung and Shuryak (1998) on hydrodynamical codes and by Bebie, Gerber, Goity, and Leutwyler (1992) on chemical freeze-out in expanding systems. The results enable more accurate predictions for observables that depend on thermal freeze-out volumes, such as particle abundances and correlations, particularly at SPS and RHIC energies.
