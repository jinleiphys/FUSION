# The study of Nuclear binding energy for $A\geq100$ based on Odd-Even staggering of nuclear masses

> The existing nuclear masses formula and nuclear masses model has undoubtedly achieved very good results, but it is still not satisfactory for some nuclear masses. Although there are many studies in Odd-Even staggering (OES) of nuclear masses, but the research on nuclear masses by using the systematicness of OES is indeed very few. Our purpose in this paper is to describe an empirical formula for Odd-Even staggering of nuclear masses that can be useful in describing and predicting nuclear masses. We empirically obtained the formula of odd-Z (odd-N) nuclei and even-Z (even-N) nuclei based on studying the OES of nuclear masses (AME2012). With the proton (neutron) empirical pairing gap from the OES of the binding energies and AME2012 database, the root-mean-square deviation of even-Z nuclei and odd-Z nuclei that we have successfully obtained 208 keV and 238 keV, respectively. The RMSD of even-N nuclei and odd-N nuclei is 222 keV and 240 keV. The result shows that our predicted values are compared well with values in AME2016, and some predicted values agree better with the experimental values. These results demonstrate that our empirical formulas have good accuracy and reliability. Another advantage of these formulas is that they use less known nuclear masses to predict unknown nuclear masses. In addition, this paper also uses BP neural network to study proton Odd-Even staggering of nuclear masses (even-Z and odd-Z nuclei) and neutron Odd-Even staggering of nuclear masses (even-N and odd-N nuclei). The RMSD of even-Z and odd-Z nuclei is 141 keV and 159 keV; the RMSD of even-N and odd-N nuclei is 150 keV and 160 keV. The results show that the RMSD of nuclear masses based on neural network 60-80 keV decrease than that based on empirical formula (the accuracy is increased by about 32%). Accurate nuclear mass is helpful to the research of nuclear physics, nuclear technology and astrophysics.

## Key claim

The paper presents empirical formulas for the Odd-Even staggering (OES) of nuclear masses that can describe and predict nuclear masses for A >= 100, but the formulas are derived specifically from the AME2012 database and the accuracy is limited to root-mean-square deviations of 208-240 keV for the empirical approach, with the caveat that nuclei at magic proton numbers Z=50,82 and magic neutron numbers N=82,126 are excluded from the fitting.

## Method

The author used the three-point formula for proton and neutron pairing gaps (Delta_p^(3) and Delta_n^(3)) from the AME2012 database to study the OES of nuclear masses for nuclei with A >= 100. Empirical formulas of the form (constant * ln A)/A were fitted separately for even-Z, odd-Z, even-N, and odd-N nuclei, excluding magic-number nuclei. The resulting OES values were then used with Eqs. (7) and (8) to calculate nuclear masses from neighboring known masses. Separately, a backpropagation (BP) neural network with the tansig activation function was trained using Z, N, and the OES values as inputs to obtain improved OES predictions.

- RMSD between predicted values (empirical formula, neutron OES) and AME2016 database: 232 keV
- RMSD between predicted values (BP neural network, neutron OES) and AME2016 database: 190 keV

## Context

This work extends the study of Odd-Even staggering of nuclear masses, which has been investigated by several authors (references 23-26 in the paper), but applies it systematically to predict nuclear masses for A >= 100, an approach the author notes has been rarely attempted. The empirical formulas are based on the AME2012 database (Audi et al., 2012) and validated against AME2016 (Audi et al., 2016). The neural network approach builds on prior work using artificial neural networks for nuclear mass prediction, including Bayesian neural network improvements to the Duflo-Zuker mass formula (reference 34, which reduced RMSD from 503 keV to 286 keV) and Levenberg-Marquardt neural network studies (reference 37, which improved a liquid drop formula from 2.455 MeV to 0.235 MeV). The method enables prediction of unknown nuclear masses using only two neighboring known masses, which facilitates extrapolation to neutron-rich and superheavy nuclei.
