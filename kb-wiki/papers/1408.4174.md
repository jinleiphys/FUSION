# Resonance Conversion as a Catalyser of Nuclear Reactions

> It is shown that resonance interal conversion offers a feasible tool for mastering nuclear processes with laser or synchrotron radiation. Physics of the process is discussed in detail in historical aspect. Possible way of experimental applicaytion is shown in the case of the $M1$ 70.6-keV transition in nuclei of $^{169}$Yb. Nuclear transition rate in hydrogenlike ions of this nuclide can be enhanced by up to four orders of magnitude.

## Key claim

Resonance internal conversion (BIC) can enhance nuclear transition rates by up to four orders of magnitude in hydrogenlike ions of \(^{169}\)Yb, but this enhancement requires the nuclear transition energy to be nearly resonant with an atomic electron transition and can be further amplified by an external electromagnetic field.

## Method

The authors develop the theory of bound internal conversion (BIC), where the conversion electron cannot escape the atom and instead occupies an excited discrete atomic state. They derive a resonance factor \(R\) by multiplying the discrete internal conversion coefficient \(\alpha_d\) by a Breit-Wigner resonance factor, and show that the BIC probability can be tuned by applying an external laser or synchrotron field to create a two-photon resonance between the nuclear transition and an atomic electron transition.

## Key numbers

- The 70.6-keV \(M1\) transition in \(^{169}\)Yb differs by about 10 eV from the energy of the atomic \(1s \to 10s\) transition, enabling resonance enhancement.
- Nuclear transition rate in hydrogenlike ions of \(^{169}\)Yb can be enhanced by up to four orders of magnitude.
- Typical nuclear level defect \(\Delta \simeq 1\) keV, while typical \(K\)-hole width \(\Gamma \sim 20\) eV, giving a potential resonance enhancement factor of \((\Delta/\Gamma)^2\) around ten orders of magnitude or more.
- For BIC in outer electron shells, \(\Gamma \sim 10^{-6}\) to \(10^{-5}\) eV.

## Context

This work extends the concept of internal conversion to the resonance (bound) regime, building on earlier predictions by Wheeler (1939) for muonic atoms and by Morita (1958) for NEET (Nuclear Excitation by Electron Transition). The authors propose that resonance BIC can overcome the difficulty of triggering nuclear isomers directly with electromagnetic fields, as attempted for \(^{180\text{m}}\)Ta, by using the electron shell as a resonator. The paper enables experimental study of isomer triggering in laser-produced plasma, specifically for isomers such as \(^{235\text{m}}\)U (76 eV), \(^{201}\)Hg (1.5 keV), and \(^{178}\)Ta (6 keV).
