# First-order derivative of cluster size as a new signature of phase transition in heavy ion collisions at intermediate energies

> The phenomenon of liquid-gas phase transition occurring in heavy ion collisions at intermediate energies is a subject of contemporary interest. Phase transition is usually characterized by the specific behaviour of state variables like pressure, density, energy etc. In heavy ion collisions there is no direct way of accessing these state variables and hence unambiguous detection of phase transition becomes difficult. This work establishes that signatures of phase transition can be extracted from the observables which are easily accessible in experiments and these have similar behaviour as the state variables. The temperature dependence of the first order derivative of the order parameters related to the largest and second largest cluster size (produced in heavy ion collisions) exhibit similar behavior as that of the variation of specific heat at constant volume Cv which is an established signature of first order phase transition. This motivates us to propose these derivatives as confirmatory signals of liquid-gas phase transition. The measurement of these signals in easily feasible in most experiments as compared to the other signatures like specific heat, caloric curve or bimodality. This temperature where the peak appears is designated to be the transition temperature and the effect of certain parameters on this has also been examined.

# First-order derivative of cluster size as a new signature of phase transition in heavy ion collisions at intermediate energies

## Key claim
The temperature dependence of the first-order derivative of the normalized largest cluster size and of the normalized size difference between the largest and second largest cluster exhibit a peak at the same temperature as the specific heat at constant volume, making these derivatives proposed as confirmatory signals of liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear multifragmentation. The authors note that these signals may lose some sharpness after evaporation from hot fragments and that fission in heavy nuclei can distort the signature if Coulomb interaction is included.

## Method
The authors used the canonical thermodynamical model (CTM) to simulate nuclear disassembly of symmetric nuclear matter at statistical equilibrium at freeze-out, with Coulomb interaction switched off and no distinction between neutrons and protons. They calculated the average size of the largest cluster and the normalized variable a2 (the difference between the largest and second largest cluster sizes divided by their sum) as functions of temperature for fixed system size and freeze-out volume, then computed the first-order temperature derivatives of these quantities. The calculations were performed for fragmenting systems of mass A = 50, 100, and 200 at freeze-out volumes of 2V0, 6V0, and 8V0.

## Key numbers
- The transition temperature for a system of mass A = 200 at freeze-out volume Vf = 6V0 is approximately 6 MeV.
- Calculations were performed for fragmenting source sizes of 50, 100, and 200 nucleons.
- Freeze-out volumes used were Vf = 2V0, Vf = 6V0, and Vf = 8V0, where V0 is the normal nuclear volume.
- The transition temperature decreases as the source size is decreased and as the freeze-out volume is increased.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous studies (Mallik 2016, Mallik 2020) that established the derivative of total multiplicity as a signature of liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear multifragmentation. It builds on the established use of the largest cluster size as an order parameter for first-order phase transitions (Gulminelli, Chaudhuri bimodality, Mallik 2014) and on the study of a2 as an order parameter (Lefevre). The proposed derivative-based signatures are presented as easier to measure experimentally compared to established signatures such as specific heat, caloric curve, or bimodality of the largest cluster distribution, which can suffer from ambiguity due to finite-size effects (Mallik 2013). The authors note that extension to nuclei with two kinds of particles is in progress.
