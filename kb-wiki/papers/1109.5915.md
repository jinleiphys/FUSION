# Cold Dense Baryonic Matter and Compact Stars

> Probing dense hadronic matter is thus far an uncharted field of physics. Here we give a brief summary of the highlights of what has been so far accomplished and what will be done in the years ahead by the World Class University III Project at Hanyang University in the endeavor to unravel and elucidate the multifacet of the cold dense baryonic matter existing in the interior of the densest visible stable object in the Universe, i.e., neutron stars, strangeness stars and/or quark stars, from a modest and simplified starting point of an effective field theory modeled on the premise of QCD as well as from a gravity dual approach of hQCD. The core of the matter of our research is the possible origin of the $\sim 99%$ of the proton mass that is to be accounted for and how the "vacuum" can be tweaked so that the source of the mass generation can be uncovered by measurements made in terrestrial as well as space laboratories. Some of the issues treated in the program concern what can be done - both theoretically and experimentally - in anticipation of what's to come for basic physics research in Korea.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the origin of approximately 99% of the proton mass can be traced to spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking in QCD, and that this mass generation mechanism can be probed by studying how hadron masses scale in cold dense baryonic matter, particularly in the interior of compact stars. The authors present a specific scaling modification, the "new Brown-Rho scaling", that arises from a topology change to half-skyrmion matter at densities above normal nuclear matter density, which they claim produces qualitatively different predictions for nuclear forces and compact star structure compared to the original Brown-Rho scaling.

## Method

The authors employ an effective field theory approach based on hidden local symmetry and a dilaton field associated with spontaneously broken scale symmetry, combined with skyrmion crystal simulations on an FCC lattice to model dense baryonic matter. They also use a holographic QCD (hQCD) gravity dual approach to study in-medium meson properties, and construct a three-layer compact star model (nuclear matter, kaon-condensed matter, strange quark matter) using a momentum-dependent interaction (MDI) parametrization for the nuclear equation of state.

## Key numbers

- Proton mass: \( m_p = 938.272013 \pm 0.000023 \) MeV (precisely measured)
- Average light quark mass: \( m_q = \frac{1}{2}(m_u + m_d) \sim 4 \) MeV
- Normal nuclear matter density: \( n_0 \simeq 0.16 \) fm\(^{-3}\)
- Quark condensate contributes approximately 99% of the proton mass (the "correction" \(\Delta\))
- Temperature for hadron melting in heavy-ion collisions: \(\sim 175\) MeV
- Interior density of compact stars estimated to be as high as \(\sim 10\) times nuclear matter density
- Observed neutron star mass: PSRJ1614-2230 at \(1.97 \pm 0.04\) \(M_\odot\)
- Kaon-nucleon sigma term used: \(\Sigma_{KN} \sim 260\) MeV (compatible with lattice results)
- Average excitation energy associated with tensor force: \(\bar{E} \sim 200\) MeV
- Binding energy correction for nuclei: \(|\delta| \lesssim 0.02 m_p\)

## Context

This work extends the Brown-Rho scaling framework first proposed by Brown and Rho (1991), which predicted that hadron masses scale with the in-medium pion decay constant in dense matter. The authors claim their new scaling, derived from half-skyrmion crystal simulations, modifies the original scaling at densities above \(n_{1/2}\) and produces qualitatively different tensor forces and symmetry energy behavior compared to the old scaling. The paper also builds on hidden local symmetry approaches (Harada and Yamawaki, 2003) and holographic QCD models (Sakai and Sugimoto, 2005), and aims to provide constraints for future experiments at FAIR, J-Parc, and the proposed KoRIA facility in Korea.
