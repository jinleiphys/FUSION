# A hybrid model for studying nuclear multifragmentation around Fermi energy domain: Case for central collision of Xe on Sn

> Experimental data for central collisions of $^{129}$Xe on $^{119}$Sn at beam energies of (a) 32 MeV/nucleon, (b) 39 MeV/nucleon, (c) 45 MeV/nucleon and (d) 50 MeV/nucleon are compared with results calculated using a hybrid model. We use a transport model (BUU) to obtain the excitation energy per nucleon in the center of mass of the multifragmenting system. The canonical thermodynamic model is then used to determine the temperature which would lead to this excitation energy. With this temperature we use the canonical thermodynamic model to calculate various experimental data such as multiplicities of different composites, probability distribution of the largest cluster etc. Agreement with data establishes the validity of the model.

# Hybrid model for nuclear multifragmentation around Fermi energy: Central collision of Xe on Sn

## Key claim

The hybrid model, combining a Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) transport calculation with the Canonical Thermodynamical Model (CTM), reproduces experimental charge distributions, largest cluster probability distributions, and average largest cluster size for central collisions of 129Xe on 119Sn at beam energies from 32 to 50 MeV/nucleon, but only when 80% of the total mass is taken as the fragmenting system after pre-equilibrium emission; the agreement depends on this specific choice of mass fraction rather than being derived from first principles.

## Method

The authors first perform a BUU transport calculation for central collisions, using a Lenk-Pandharipande mean-field potential and 100 test particles per nucleon, to obtain the excitation energy per nucleon of the multifragmenting system after pre-equilibrium particles are removed (selecting the 80% most central test particles). They then use the Canonical Thermodynamical Model (CTM) to find the freeze-out temperature that yields that excitation energy for the given mass and charge. With this temperature fixed, CTM calculates fragment multiplicities and largest cluster distributions, and excited fragments are subsequently decayed via a Weisskopf evaporation model.

## Key numbers

- Beam energies studied: 32, 39, 45, and 50 MeV/nucleon for 129Xe on 119Sn.
- Fragmenting system mass taken as 80% of total mass, corresponding to A0 = 198 nucleons.
- BUU calculation uses Ntest = 100 test particles per nucleon.
- Mean-field parameters: A = -2230.0 MeV fm3, B = 2577.85 MeV fm7/2, sigma = 7/6, rho0 = 0.16 fm-3, c = -6.5 MeV fm5/2.
- Time evolution stopped at t = 200 fm/c for excitation energy extraction.
- Excitation energy per nucleon and corresponding freeze-out temperature are plotted as functions of beam energy (Fig. 3, numerical values not given in text).

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior approach where CTM was used with an assumed temperature profile to fit projectile-like fragment data (Mallik et al., 2014) and later showed that temperature profile could be obtained from BUU (Mallik et al., 2014). The hybrid model improves upon purely statistical models (such as SMM by Bondorf et al., 1995) by setting initial conditions from a dynamical calculation rather than from experimental observables or parameterization. It also complements the approach of Barz et al. (1992) who similarly used dynamical input for statistical model initialization. The method enables extraction of freeze-out temperatures without the complications of sequential decay or Fermi motion that affect Albergo formula temperature measurements (Albergo et al., 1985).
