# Light radioactive nuclei capture reactions with phenomenological potential models

> Light radioactive nuclei play an important role in many astrophysical environments. Due to very low cross sections of some neutron and proton capture reactions by these radioactive nuclei at energies of astrophysical interest, direct laboratory measurements are very difficult. For radioactive nuclei such as 8Li and 8B, the direct measurement of neutron capture reactions is impossible. Indirect methods have been applied to overcome these difficulties. In this work we will report on the results and discussion of phenomenological potential models used to determine some proton and neutron capture reactions. As a test we show the results for the 16O(p,g)17F_gs(5/2+) and 16O(p,g)17F_ex(1/2+) capture reactions.We also computed the nucleosynthesis cross sections for the 7Li(n,g)8Li, 8Li(n,g)9Li and 8B(p,g)9C capture reactions.

## Key claim

Phenomenological potential models using Woods-Saxon parameterizations can reproduce the non-resonant part of neutron and proton capture cross sections for light radioactive nuclei, but the method relies on spectroscopic factors and potential depths that must be obtained from indirect sources such as transfer reactions or scattering data from neighboring systems, and the results for the unmeasured reactions (e.g., 8B(p,γ)9C) remain predictions without direct experimental validation.

## Method

The authors used the computer code RADCAP to calculate direct radiative capture cross sections via E1 transitions, employing Woods-Saxon potentials to generate both the continuum scattering wave functions and the bound-state wave functions. For each reaction, the central potential depth was adjusted to reproduce the known binding energy of the final state, while the spin-orbit depth and geometric parameters (r0=1.25 fm, a=0.65 fm) were fixed; spectroscopic factors were taken from prior analyses of transfer reactions or scattering data. The method was first tested on the well-measured 16O(p,γ)17F reactions, then applied to the radioactive-nucleus systems 7Li(n,γ)8Li, 8Li(n,γ)9Li, 7Be(p,γ)8B, and 8B(p,γ)9C.

## Key numbers

* For 16O(p,γ)17F_gs(5/2+): the potential model fit with pole expression gives S(0)=0.319 keV·b with χ²=0.0139, using a binding energy of 0.60 MeV and spectroscopic factor 1.0.
* For 16O(p,γ)17F_ex(1/2+): the potential model fit gives S(0)=10.987 keV·b with χ²=0.2019, using a binding energy of 0.11 MeV and spectroscopic factor 1.0.
* For 7Li(n,γ)8Li: the ground state (2+) has binding energy 2.03 MeV with spectroscopic factor 0.98(15); the first excited state (1+) has binding energy 1.05 MeV with spectroscopic factor 0.48; capture to the first excited state contributes less than 5% of the total cross section.
* For 8Li(n,γ)9Li_gs(3/2-): binding energy 4.06 MeV with spectroscopic factor 0.62(13); scattering potentials were obtained by keeping the same volume integral per nucleon as for the 7Li+n system.
* For 7Be(p,γ)8B_gs(2+): binding energy 0.14 MeV with spectroscopic factor 1.0; the M1 resonance at E_cm=0.63 MeV was reproduced using V_scat=-38.14 MeV and SF=0.7.
* For 8B(p,γ)9C_gs(3/2-): binding energy 1.30 MeV with spectroscopic factor set to 1.0; no experimental data are available for this reaction.

## Context

This work extends the potential-model approach previously applied to stable systems (e.g., Rolfs (1973) for 16O(p,γ)17F) to radioactive nuclei relevant for inhomogeneous big-bang nucleosynthesis and explosive stellar environments, where direct measurements are impossible due to the short half-lives of 8Li (178 ms) and 8B (770 ms). The calculations complement indirect methods such as the ANC method (Gagliardi, 2002) and the Coulomb dissociation method (Baur, 1994), and the results for 8B(p,γ)9C provide predictions that can be compared with future measurements or with the ANC-extracted value from Trache (2002) shown at E=0. The authors note that elastic scattering measurements for 8Li+p and 8B+p at low energy, planned at the RIBRAS facility, would provide more reliable scattering potentials for future calculations.
