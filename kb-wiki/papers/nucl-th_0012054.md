# Liquid-Drop Model and Quantum Resistance Against Noncompact Nuclear Geometries

> The importance of quantum effects for exotic nuclear shapes is demonstrated. Based on the example of a sheet of nuclear matter of infinite lateral dimensions but finite thickness, it is shown that the quantization of states in momentum space, resulting from the confinement of the nucleonic motion in the conjugate geometrical space, generates a strong resistance against such a confinement and generates restoring forces driving the system towards compact geometries. In the liquid-drop model, these quantum effects are implicitly included in the surface energy term, via a choice of interaction parameters, an approximation that has been found valid for compact shapes, but has not yet been scrutinized for exotic shapes.

# Liquid-Drop Model and Quantum Resistance Against Noncompact Nuclear Geometries

## Key claim

Quantum effects from the confinement of nucleonic motion in noncompact geometries (such as an infinite sheet of nuclear matter) generate a strong resistance against such confinement and produce restoring forces that drive the system toward compact shapes; the liquid-drop model's surface energy term implicitly includes these quantum effects for compact shapes via parameter choice, but this approximation has not been validated for exotic geometries.

## Method

The authors modified the Thomas-Fermi formalism of Seyler and Blanchard to account for quantization of the nucleonic momentum component perpendicular to the sheet surface, arising from confinement in a finite-thickness geometry. They calculated the volume energy per nucleon of symmetric nuclear matter in an infinite sheet geometry using the Seyler-Blanchard nucleon-nucleon interaction, with parameters from Myers (1977), and compared results to those obtained with a newer parameterization from Myers and Swiatecki that adds an attractive term inversely proportional to relative momentum and a repulsive density-dependent term. The model thickness of the confining potential well was distinguished from the physical matter thickness, and the energy per nucleon was minimized by varying the Fermi momentum.

## Key numbers

- The volume energy per nucleon for infinite symmetric nuclear matter is -15.677 MeV, with a kinetic Fermi energy of 33.138 MeV (Seyler-Blanchard parameters: C=328.61 MeV, a=0.62567 fm, b=392.48 MeV/c).
- The system becomes unbound (volume energy per nucleon becomes positive) for sheet matter thicknesses of approximately 2.8 fm and below (corresponding to model thickness d < 5.6 fm).
- The difference between model thickness and matter thickness increases linearly from 0 to a maximum of approximately 2.8 fm as model thickness increases from 0 to 5.6 fm, then decreases quasi-hyperbolically to saturation at approximately 2 fm for model thicknesses above 20 fm.
- The Planck constant used is h = 1239.86 MeV fm/c, and the average nucleon mass is taken as M = 938.903 MeV/c^2.

## Context

This work extends the liquid-drop model studies of Seyler and Blanchard (1961) and Myers (1977) by examining the validity of the surface-energy approximation for noncompact geometries, specifically infinite sheets previously studied by Moretto and collaborators in the context of multifragmentation and sheet instability. The results question the applicability of both the standard liquid-drop model and BUU (Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck) calculations to exotic nuclear shapes, and motivate more accurate calculations including surface and Coulomb energies as well as finite temperature effects, with implications for understanding neck matter stability and intermediate-mass fragment production in heavy-ion reactions.
