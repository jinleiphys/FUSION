# Nambu-Jona-Lasinio description of hadronic matter from a Bayesian approach

> A microscopic nuclear matter formalism with explicit chiral symmetry based on the Nambu Jona-Lasinio model is considered to describe nuclear matter. To reproduce nuclear matter properties adequately at the saturation density, four-point and eight-point interactions are introduced. Within a Bayesian inference approach, the parameters of the model are determined by imposing nuclear matter, both experimental and from {\it ab-initio} calculations, and neutron star observational constraints. Nuclear matter properties are well reproduced with an effective mass of 0.75 to 0.8 nucleon mass at the saturation density. At 90% confidence level, the radius of a $1.4 ~\rm M_\odot$ star varies between 11.48 km and 13.20 km, masses as large as $\sim 2.2 ~\rm M_\odot$ are predicted and the radius of a 2 M$_\odot$ star is above 10.5 km. High-density perturbative QCD (pQCD) results exclude equations of state that predict larger maximum masses and radii. The speed of sound increases monotonically with density and reaches values as large as $\sqrt{0.7}c$-$\sqrt{0.8}c$ in the center of massive stars. Several properties such as the polytropic index or the renormalized trace anomaly, that have been proposed to identify the deconfined phase transition, are analyzed. Interestingly, the radius of the obtained posterior that also meets pQCD constraints aligns closely with the mass-radius measurement of the recent PSR J0437-4715, which contrasts with other relativistic mean field model results.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that an extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (eNJL) model with four-point and eight-point interactions, calibrated via Bayesian inference against nuclear matter and neutron star constraints, can reproduce saturation properties and neutron star observables, but only when high-density perturbative QCD (pQCD) constraints are applied do the resulting equations of state align with the recent mass-radius measurement of PSR J0437-4715, contrasting with relativistic mean field model results.

## Method

The authors constructed an extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (eNJL) model for nucleonic matter including scalar, vector, isovector, and crossed eight-point interaction channels, with a sharp three-momentum cutoff regularization. They performed Bayesian inference using nested sampling (PyMultiNest with 1500 live points) to determine the seven coupling parameters and the current quark mass, imposing constraints from symmetric nuclear matter properties at saturation, chiral effective field theory pure neutron matter pressures at three densities, a neutron star maximum mass above 2 solar masses, and pQCD consistency at 7 times saturation density. The resulting posterior was filtered for thermodynamic stability conditions, eliminating less than 3% of samples.

## Key numbers

- Effective nucleon mass at saturation density: 0.75 to 0.8 nucleon mass (939 MeV)
- Radius of a 1.4 solar mass star at 90% confidence level: 11.48 km to 13.20 km
- Maximum neutron star mass predicted: approximately 2.2 solar masses
- Radius of a 2 solar mass star: above 10.5 km
- Speed of sound in massive star centers: reaches values between sqrt(0.7)c and sqrt(0.8)c
- Saturation density constraint: 0.153 plus or minus 0.005 fm^{-3}
- Binding energy at saturation: -16.1 plus or minus 0.2 MeV
- Incompressibility modulus: 230 plus or minus 40 MeV
- Symmetry energy at saturation: 32.5 plus or minus 1.8 MeV
- Pure neutron matter pressure at 0.08 fm^{-3}: 0.521 plus or minus 0.091 MeV fm^{-3}
- Pure neutron matter pressure at 0.12 fm^{-3}: 1.262 plus or minus 0.295 MeV fm^{-3}
- Pure neutron matter pressure at 0.16 fm^{-3}: 2.513 plus or minus 0.675 MeV fm^{-3}

## Context

This work extends the extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (eNJL) model framework first proposed by Koch (1987) and later developed by the authors of Ref. enjl, applying Bayesian inference for the first time to this class of chiral hadronic models. It follows the methodology of Malik (2022) and Malik (2023) for calibrating relativistic mean field models, and contrasts with the Walecka-type hadrodynamics models analyzed in the comprehensive review by Dutra (2014) and Dutra (2016), which found only 2 of 263 parameterizations satisfied all constraints. The results enable future comparisons with NICER and LIGO/Virgo observations, and the analysis of the polytropic index and renormalized trace anomaly provides tools for identifying deconfined phase transitions in dense matter.
