# Electromagnetic proton-neutron mass difference

> We discuss the Cottingham formula and evaluate the proton-neutron electromagnetic mass difference exploiting the state-of-the-art phenomenological input. We decompose individual contributions to the mass splitting into Born, inelastic and subtraction terms. We evaluate the subtraction-function contribution from the experimental input only and the Born term accounting for the modern low-$Q^2$ data.

# Electromagnetic proton-neutron mass difference

## Key claim

The electromagnetic contribution to the proton-neutron mass difference is evaluated as 1.09 ± 0.3 MeV using the Cottingham formula with state-of-the-art phenomenological input, but the uncertainty remains dominated by the subtraction-function contribution, which is constrained by the isovector magnetic polarizability and has an error of 0.30 MeV.

## Method

The Cottingham formula is decomposed into Born, inelastic, and subtraction contributions to the electromagnetic mass shift. The Born term is evaluated using modern proton and neutron electromagnetic form factors from electron scattering data, the inelastic term uses unpolarized structure functions from resonance and deep-inelastic scattering, and the subtraction term is determined by connecting the experimental isovector magnetic polarizability at zero virtuality with higher-Q² behavior via the operator product expansion and Regge phenomenology.

## Key numbers

- Total electromagnetic proton-neutron mass difference: δM^γ_{p-n} = 1.09 ± 0.3 MeV
- Born contribution: δM^{Born}_{p-n} = 0.74 ± 0.01 MeV (proton: 0.53 ± 0.01 MeV, neutron: -0.21 ± 0.01 MeV)
- Inelastic contribution: δM^{inel}_{p-n} = 0.034 ± 0.010 MeV
- Subtraction term: δM^{subt}_{p-n} = 0.33 ± 0.30 MeV (or 0.54 ± 0.46 MeV without operator product expansion input)
- Experimental isovector magnetic polarizability: β^{p-n}_M = (-1.2 ± 1.3) × 10^{-4} fm³
- Experimental total mass difference: δM_{p-n} = -1.29333217(42) MeV

## Context

This work updates the dispersive evaluation of the electromagnetic mass splitting originally formulated by Cottingham (1963) and historically studied by Gasser (1974) and WalkerLoud (2012). It improves upon the Born and subtraction-term uncertainties compared to previous data-driven estimates, and the result agrees within errors with the lattice QCD calculation of Borsanyi (2014) giving δM^γ_{p-n} = 1.00 ± 0.16 MeV. The subtraction-function uncertainty must be reduced to (0.4-0.5) × 10^{-4} fm³ in the isovector magnetic polarizability to compete with lattice precision, and further studies in low-energy effective field theories could clarify the low-Q² region.
