# Short-range correlations in nuclei

> Atomic nuclei are held together by the strong nuclear force acting between protons and neutrons (nucleons). While the long range, averaged part of this force is well described by the nuclear shell model, the short-range and tensor components create a fascinating substructure: pairs of nucleons that momentarily approach each other very closely, acquiring large relative momenta. These short-range correlated (SRC) pairs account for roughly 20% of all nucleons in any nucleus and almost all of the high momentum nucleons. This chapter provides an introduction to SRC pairs: their origin in the nucleon-nucleon tensor force, the experimental methods used to study them, principally deep inelastic and quasielastic electron and proton scattering, and the comprehensive picture that has emerged over the past three decades.

## Key claim

Short-range correlated (SRC) pairs account for roughly 20% of all nucleons in any nucleus and almost all of the high-momentum nucleons, but this statement applies only to the momentary, fluctuating substructure created by the short-range and tensor components of the nuclear force that lie beyond the mean-field shell model description.

## Method

The authors review experimental methods using high-energy electron and proton scattering to study SRC pairs, principally deep inelastic and quasielastic scattering where a hard probe (Q² > 1.5 GeV², x_B > 1.2) knocks out a nucleon from an SRC pair, causing its correlated partner to also be emitted in a back-to-back configuration. Kinematic cuts including θ_rq < 40° and |p_miss| > 0.3 GeV/c are applied to suppress meson-exchange currents, isobar configurations, and final-state interactions. Coincidence detection of the scattered lepton and two outgoing nucleons allows identification of SRC pair type (np vs. pp) and measurement of pair fractions across nuclei from carbon to lead.

## Key numbers

- Roughly 20% of all nucleons in any nucleus belong to SRC pairs
- Typical Fermi momentum k_F ≈ 200–250 MeV/c for medium to heavy nuclei
- Q² > 1.5 GeV² and x_B > 1.2 are the kinematic cuts used to select SRC events
- |p_miss| > 0.3 GeV/c selects nucleons above the Fermi momentum
- In carbon, 96 ± 22% of high-p_miss protons were tagged with a recoiling neutron, and only 9.5 ± 2% with a recoiling proton (Subedi 2008)
- The np to pp pair ratio in carbon after single charge exchange correction is 18 ± 5
- In proton-carbon scattering, 92^{+8}_{-18}% of high-p_miss protons had a correlated neutron partner (Piasetzky 2006)
- The np to pp event ratio in carbon reaches 9.0 ± 2.5 after single charge exchange correction
- SRC pairs have separation r ≤ 1 fm, much smaller than the average inter-nucleon spacing of about 1.7 fm
- In lead (126 neutrons, 82 protons), if 20% of nucleons are in SRC pairs, the high-momentum proton fraction is about 24% while the high-momentum neutron fraction is about 16%

## Context

This chapter reviews and synthesizes three decades of experimental and theoretical work on SRC pairs, extending the early theoretical predictions of Frankfurt and Strikman (1980s) and the first experimental observations from Brookhaven National Lab (Tang 2003, Piasetzky 2006) and Jefferson Lab (Subedi 2008). The work establishes that np SRC pairs dominate over pp pairs by roughly an order of magnitude across all nuclei studied, a consequence of the tensor component of the nucleon-nucleon force that acts on spin-1 np pairs but not on spin-0 pp pairs. These results enable future studies connecting SRC pairs to the EMC effect (modification of nucleon quark-gluon structure inside nuclei) and motivate probe-independent studies using electrons, protons, and photons to put SRC studies on the same rigorous footing as parton distribution functions.
