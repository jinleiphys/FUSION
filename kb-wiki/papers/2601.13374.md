# Trace Anomaly of Cold Dense Matter Constrained by Collective Flow

> The trace anomaly of dense matter, $Δ\equiv 1/3 - P/\varepsilon$, defined through the ratio $w \equiv P/\varepsilon$ of pressure $P$ to energy density $\varepsilon$, quantifies deviations from conformal symmetry and provides a dimensionless measure of the stiffness of the equation of state (EOS) relevant for both neutron stars and heavy-ion collisions. While $Δ(\varepsilon)$ has recently been inferred from neutron star observations, we report the first Bayesian extraction of the trace anomaly from collective flow observables in intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions. By employing transport-model simulations that explicitly decouple the cold matter mean-field potential from thermal effects, we directly constrain the EOS of cold dense matter. Remarkably, the trace anomaly inferred from laboratory flow data agrees quantitatively, within $68\%$ credible intervals, with independent astrophysical posterior bands. This nontrivial agreement demonstrates that heavy-ion collisions and neutron star observations probe the same macroscopic properties in a mutually consistent way, establishing the dense-matter trace anomaly as a composition-insensitive macroscopic bridge observable across widely different physical environments.

# Trace Anomaly of Cold Dense Matter Constrained by Collective Flow

## Key claim
The trace anomaly of cold dense matter, extracted for the first time from collective flow observables in intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions via Bayesian inference, agrees quantitatively within 68% credible intervals with independent astrophysical constraints from neutron star observations, establishing it as a composition-insensitive macroscopic bridge observable across terrestrial and astrophysical environments.

## Method
The author performed Bayesian analyses of the excitation functions of proton directed and elliptic flow measured by the FOPI Collaboration at beam energies from 150 to 1200 MeV/nucleon and the HADES Collaboration at 1230 MeV/nucleon for mid-central Au+Au collisions. A Gaussian-process emulator trained on an isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (IBUU) transport model was used, explicitly decoupling the cold matter mean-field potential from thermal effects treated via collision integrals, with an in-medium baryon-baryon scattering cross-section modification factor X assigned a broad prior range of 0.3 to 2.0. The maximum central baryon density reached at each beam energy was used to evaluate the trace anomaly, with the sound speed squared averaged up to that density according to the relation between the pressure-to-energy-density ratio w and the averaged sound speed.

## Key numbers
- The trace anomaly Δ(ε) extracted from heavy-ion flow data agrees quantitatively with neutron star constraints within 68% credible intervals.
- Beam energies studied range from 150 to 1200 MeV/nucleon (FOPI) plus 1230 MeV/nucleon (HADES) for mid-central Au+Au collisions.
- The in-medium baryon-baryon scattering cross-section modification factor X was assigned a prior range of 0.3 ≤ X ≤ 2.0.
- The energy density at saturation nucleon density ρ₀ ≈ 0.16 fm⁻³ is ε₀ ≈ 150 MeV/fm³.
- The maximum central density reached in collisions ranges from approximately 1.5ρ₀ to over 3ρ₀ depending on beam energy, as shown in the time evolution of central baryon density.

## Context
This work extends recent astrophysical inferences of the trace anomaly from neutron star observations (Fujimoto 2022, Gorda 2023, Al-Mamun 2021) by providing the first Bayesian extraction from laboratory heavy-ion collision data, using collective flow observables that have been analyzed for decades (Pawel 2002, Fuchs 2005, Lynch 2009). The result demonstrates that collective flow and neutron star global observables probe the same macroscopic properties of cold dense matter despite very different dynamical conditions, enabling future cross-validation between next-generation heavy-ion facilities and advanced X-ray timing and gravitational-wave observations of neutron stars. The trace anomaly is shown to be more robust against microscopic modeling uncertainties than direct EOS inference because the dimensionless ratio P/ε removes normalization scales and is insensitive to how energy density is partitioned into kinetic and interaction contributions, as demonstrated analytically via the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem.
