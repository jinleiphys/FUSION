# Hydrodynamic emission of strange and non-strange particles at RHIC and LHC

> The hydrodynamic model is used to describe the single-particle spectra and elliptic flow of hadrons at RHIC and to predict the emission angle dependence of HBT correlations at RHIC and LHC energies.

## Key claim

The hydrodynamic model successfully reproduces the single-particle spectra and elliptic flow of both strange and non-strange hadrons at RHIC, including the heavy Omega hyperons, but it fails to describe two-particle Bose-Einstein (HBT) correlations, overpredicting longitudinal and outward HBT radii while underpredicting the sideward radius (the "RHIC HBT puzzle").

## Method

The author used relativistic ideal fluid hydrodynamics with a common kinetic decoupling temperature to compute single-particle spectra, elliptic flow coefficients, and HBT correlation radii for hadrons produced in Au+Au collisions at RHIC and LHC energies. The model was initialized with constraints from measured charged particle multiplicities, and the emission function at freeze-out was analyzed using the Cooper-Frye prescription to extract the spatial correlation tensor components that determine the HBT radius parameters as functions of azimuthal emission angle relative to the reaction plane.

## Key numbers

- For semiperipheral Au+Au collisions at RHIC, elliptic flow saturates after about 5 fm/c, with thermalization occurring at tau_therm < 1 fm/c
- The estimated energy density at early times is > 10 GeV/fm^3
- The kinetic decoupling temperature that best fits pion and proton data from central collisions is T_f = 130 MeV
- The hydrodynamic model reproduces data for impact parameters b < 7 fm
- For the Omega hyperons, freeze-out at T_f = 130 MeV is consistent with data, while freeze-out directly after hadronization at T_c = 165 MeV is ruled out
- For LHC-type initial conditions with an initial temperature of 2 GeV, the source lives longer and changes from out-of-plane to in-plane elongation at freeze-out

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamic analysis of Kolb, Huovinen, and Heinz (2003) and Heinz and Kolb (2002) by showing that Omega hyperons couple more strongly to the expanding pion fluid during the late hadronic stage than previously expected based on work by H. van Hecke, S. Sorge, and N. Xu (1998). The azimuthal HBT oscillations predicted here provide a new observable that can constrain the total duration of the expansion phase; preliminary STAR data from Lopez (2002) and Lisa (2003) agree with the RHIC prediction of an out-of-plane elongated source at freeze-out, ruling out the longer-lived in-plane elongated source expected at LHC energies. Resolving the "RHIC HBT puzzle" in central collisions remains necessary before quantitative statements can be made.
