# Review of N N Interaction from Lattice QCD

> Historically, lattice studies of QCD have concentrated on understanding the properties and structure of an isolated hadron. Recently, there have efforts at understanding the interactions between hadrons. In this talk I will review two approaches to this problem.

# Review of N N Interaction from Lattice QCD

## Key claim

Lattice QCD studies of hadron-hadron interactions are fundamentally difficult due to multi-hadron states requiring four-point function computations, large spatial lattice extents, and the Euclidean lattice obscuring scattering phase information; however, two complementary approaches, finite-volume scattering length extraction and static heavy-light meson potentials, have been adopted to address these challenges.

## Method

The first approach applies Lüscher’s finite-volume method to extract infinite-volume s-wave scattering lengths from the discrete two-particle energy shifts on the lattice, as demonstrated for the π-π system. The second approach studies two heavy-light mesons with static heavy quarks, which mimics nucleon-nucleon features such as quark exchange, flavor exchange, and color polarization, and allows computation of an adiabatic potential using recently developed “all-to-all” quark propagator techniques.

## Key numbers

- The I=2 ππ scattering length was computed in the quenched approximation and shown as a function of lattice spacing, with a continuum limit result; the current-algebra prediction for mπ = 140 MeV is shown as an open circle for comparison.
- NN scattering lengths are around 10 fm, making the finite-volume approach more problematical than for the ππ system.

## Context

This review extends the finite-volume method of Lüscher (1986, 1991) from the ππ system, where Fukugita et al. (1995, 1999) obtained encouraging results, to the more challenging NN system. The heavy-light meson approach, first studied by Richards (1989) for flavor-exchange processes, has been advanced by Mihaly et al. (1997), Koniuk et al. (1998), and Michael (1999) using all-to-all propagator techniques to seek evidence for nuclear binding. This work enables future lattice studies of nuclear forces and is a key element of the Lattice Hadron Physics Collaboration research program.
