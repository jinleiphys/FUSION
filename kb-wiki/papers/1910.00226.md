# Proper time evolution of magnetic susceptibility in a magnetized quark-gluon plasma

> In ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions, enormous magnetic fields are generated because of fast-moving charged particles. In the presence of these magnetic fields, the spin of particles is aligned either in the parallel or in the antiparallel direction with respect to the direction of the magnetic field. A finite magnetization is thus produced. It is known that a finite magnetic susceptibility, $χ_{m}$, changes the evolution of the energy density of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP), which is believed to be created in these collisions. Depending on whether the system under consideration is a paramagnetic ($χ_{m}>0$) or diamagnetic ($χ_{m}<0$) fluid, it slows down or speeds up the decay of the energy density, and affects other thermodynamic quantities. In general, one expects that the magnetic susceptibility depends on the magnetic field and temperature. Bearing in mind that these parameters evolve with the evolution of the fluid, a nonuniform magnetic susceptibility in this system is thus expected. In this work, we first determine $χ_{m}$ by using a certain analogy to the standard anisotropic kinetic theory, where the one-particle distribution function is replaced by the corresponding anisotropic distribution function. We then determine the proper time dependence of the magnetic susceptibility in the framework of the ideal transverse magnetohydrodynamics. We also study the effect of dissipation on the evolution of $χ_{m}$.

# Proper time evolution of magnetic susceptibility in a magnetized quark-gluon plasma

## Key claim

The magnetic susceptibility of a quark-gluon plasma evolves with proper time and depends on whether the fluid is treated as nondissipative or dissipative; the qualitative temperature dependence of the susceptibility in the range 100–200 MeV coincides with lattice QCD results, but unlike those lattice results the susceptibility here peaks at around 200 MeV and then decreases, because the magnetic field itself decays dynamically as B ~ τ⁻¹.

## Method

The authors construct an anisotropic one-particle distribution function analogous to that used in anisotropic hydrodynamics, replacing the standard isotropic distribution with one containing an anisotropy parameter ξ induced by magnetization. They derive coupled differential equations for the anisotropy parameter ξ and effective temperature λ from the zeroth and first moments of the Boltzmann equation in the relaxation time approximation, separately for nondissipative and dissipative cases. The magnetic susceptibility χₘ is then obtained from the magnetization M computed from the distribution function, using M = χₘ B, and its proper time evolution is determined within the framework of ideal transverse magnetohydrodynamics under Bjorken flow.

## Key numbers

- Initial magnetic field values used: eB̄ = 5 m_π² and eB̄ = 15 m_π², with m_π = 140 MeV.
- Initial temperature T₀ = 400 MeV and initial proper time τ₀ = 0.2 fm/c for the evolution calculations.
- Initial anisotropy parameter ξ(τ₀) = 10⁻⁷ and initial effective temperature λ(τ₀) = 400 MeV.
- Relaxation times τ_{r,0} and τ_r set to 0.3 fm/c or 0.5 fm/c in different calculations.
- Magnetic field evolves as B = B₀ (τ₀/τ), with B₀ = B(τ₀).
- Magnetic susceptibility increases for 0.2 < τ < 1.5 fm/c (corresponding to 400 > T > 180 MeV) and then decreases.
- Qualitative agreement with lattice QCD results for χₘ in the temperature range T ∈ [100, 200] MeV.

## Context

This work extends the anisotropic hydrodynamics framework of Strickland (2010) and Strickland (2017) to a magnetized quark-gluon plasma, using an analogy between the anisotropy induced by magnetization and that arising from viscous effects. The proper time evolution of the magnetic field follows the frozen flux theorem as derived by Pu (2016) and Shokri (2018). The temperature dependence of the magnetic susceptibility is compared with lattice QCD results from D'Elia (2013), where the present work finds a peak at around 200 MeV that is absent in the lattice calculations, attributed to the dynamical decay of the magnetic field. The results enable future studies of how the evolving magnetic susceptibility affects the energy density evolution and spin polarization of particles in heavy-ion collisions.
