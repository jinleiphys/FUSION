# Equilibrium Statistical-Thermal Models in High-Energy Physics

> We review some recent highlights from the applications of statistical-thermal models to different experimental measurements and lattice QCD thermodynamics, that have been made during the last decade. We start with a short review of the historical milestones on the path of constructing statistical-thermal models for heavy-ion physics. We discovered that Heinz Koppe formulated in 1948 an almost complete recipe for the statistical-thermal models. In 1950, Enrico Fermi generalized this statistical approach, in which he started with a general cross-section formula and inserted into it simplifying assumptions about the matrix element of the interaction process that likely reflects many features of the high-energy reactions dominated by density in the phase space of final states. In 1964, Hagedorn systematically analysed the high-energy phenomena using all tools of statistical physics and introduced the concept of limiting temperature based on the statistical bootstrap model. It turns to be quite often that many-particle systems can be studied with the help of statistical-thermal methods. The analysis of yield multiplicities in high-energy collisions gives an overwhelming evidence for the chemical equilibrium in the final state. The strange particles might be an exception, as they are suppressed at lower beam energies. However, their relative yields fulfill statistical equilibrium, as well. We review the equilibrium statistical-thermal models for particle production, fluctuations and collective flow in heavy-ion experiments. We also review their reproduction of the lattice QCD thermodynamics at vanishing and finite chemical potential. During the last decade, five conditions have been suggested to describe the universal behavior of the chemical freeze out parameters.

## Key claim
This review paper argues that equilibrium statistical-thermal models successfully describe particle production, fluctuations, and collective flow in high-energy heavy-ion experiments, and also reproduce lattice QCD thermodynamics at vanishing and finite chemical potential, but the abstract's claim of "overwhelming evidence for chemical equilibrium" is qualified by the observation that strange particles may be an exception, as they are suppressed at lower beam energies, although their relative yields still fulfill statistical equilibrium.

## Method
The paper reviews the historical development of statistical-thermal models from Koppe (1948) and Fermi (1950) through Hagedorn (1964) to modern applications, then surveys comparisons of these models with experimental data on particle multiplicities, ratios, and higher-order moments from AGS, SPS, RHIC, and LHC energies. It also reviews the reproduction of lattice QCD thermodynamics using hadron resonance gas models, and discusses six proposed universal conditions for the chemical freeze-out parameters, including constant energy per particle, constant baryon density, percolation theory, constant normalized entropy, vanishing product of kurtosis and susceptibility, and constant trace anomaly.

## Key numbers
- Koppe's 1948 model predicted meson production in alpha-carbon collisions at 380 MeV, yielding approximately 1.7 × 10⁻⁴ mesons per unit time
- The limiting temperature concept from Hagedorn's statistical bootstrap model (1964) arose from an exponential mass spectrum of hadron resonances
- The paper reports that five (later six) universal conditions have been suggested to describe the universal behavior of chemical freeze-out parameters across a wide range of nucleus-nucleus center-of-mass energies √s_NN
- The temperature of excited daughter nuclei in Koppe's model was measured as approximately 10 MeV at the Berkeley Betatron

## Context
This review extends the line of work initiated by Koppe (1948), Fermi (1950), and Hagedorn (1964) on statistical descriptions of particle production, and builds on the 2003 confrontation of statistical-thermal models with lattice QCD simulations that changed the physical picture from releasing additional degrees of freedom above T_c to the hadron resonance gas having more degrees of freedom than the quark-gluon plasma below T_c. The paper enables future work on locating the QCD critical endpoint through higher-order moments of multiplicity distributions, and reviews three models combining hadronic with partonic phases: quasi-particle model, linear sigma-model with Polyakov-loop potentials, and compressible bag model.
