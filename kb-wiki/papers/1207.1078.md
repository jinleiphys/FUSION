# Symmetry energy effects in the neutron star properties

> The functional form of the nuclear symmetry energy has only been determined in a very narrow range of densities. Uncertainties concern both the low as well as the high density behaviour of this function. In this work different shapes of the symmetry energy, consistent with the experimental data, were introduced and their consequences for the crustal properties of neutron stars are presented. The resulting models are in agreement with astrophysical observations.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that different functional forms of the nuclear symmetry energy, all consistent with experimental data at saturation density, can produce neutron star models that simultaneously satisfy the 2 solar mass maximum mass constraint and the direct Urca cooling constraint for low-mass stars, but only when paired with a sufficiently stiff isoscalar equation of state.

## Method
The authors parameterized both the isoscalar part V(n) and the isovector symmetry energy E_s(n) using Bezier curves, with V(n) following the stiff APR A18+UIX model corrected to saturation properties (binding energy -16 MeV, compressibility 240 MeV). Four different symmetry energy models were constructed sharing the same high-density behavior but with different slopes L at saturation density (40, 60, 90, and 120 MeV). The crust-core transition was determined using three different methods, and the TOV equations were solved in General Relativity to compute neutron star macroscopic properties.

## Key numbers
- Saturation density n0 = 0.16 fm^-3
- Binding energy at saturation: -16 MeV
- Compressibility K0 = 240 MeV
- Symmetry energy at saturation Es(n0) ≈ 30 MeV, with slope L in range 40-120 MeV
- Crust-core transition densities for APR-L40-high-b model: 0.103076 fm^-3 (Q method), 0.11012 fm^-3 (K_mu method), 0.116185 fm^-3 (1-2 method)
- Crust-core transition densities for APR-L120-high-b model: 0.115633 fm^-3 (Q method), 0.142939 fm^-3 (K_mu method), 0.147017 fm^-3 (1-2 method)
- Maximum neutron star mass of about 2 solar masses can be created within this model
- Recent combined laboratory measurements give Es(n0) ≈ 32 MeV, L ≈ 50 MeV with error of a few MeV

## Context
This work extends the parametric approach to nuclear symmetry energy using Bezier functions, building on the APR equation of state (Akmal, Pandharipande, Ravenhall, 1998) and the observation of a 2 solar mass neutron star (Demorest et al., 2010). The models satisfy the direct Urca cooling constraint (Popov et al., 2006) that low-mass neutron stars must not cool by direct Urca processes. The results enable comparison with microscopic approaches and suggest that models with low L values (around 50 MeV) are preferable based on combined laboratory measurements (Lattimer, 2012).
