# Importance of different energy loss effects in jet suppression at RHIC and LHC

> Jet suppression is considered to be an excellent probe of QCD matter created in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions. Our theoretical predictions of jet suppression, which are based on our recently developed dynamical energy loss formalism, show a robust agreement with various experimental data, which spans across different probes, experiments (RHIC and LHC) and experimental conditions (i.e. all available centrality regions). This formalism includes several key ingredients, such as inclusion of dynamical scattering centers, finite size QCD medium, collisional energy loss, finite magnetic mass and running coupling. While these effects have to be included based on theoretical grounds, it is currently unclear what is their individual importance in accurately interpreting the experimental data, in particular because other approaches to suppression predictions commonly neglect some - or all - of these effects. To address this question, we here study the relative importance of these effects in obtaining accurate suppression predictions for D mesons (clear energy loss probe) at top RHIC and LHC energies. We obtain that several different ingredients are responsible for the accurate predictions, i.e. the robust agreement with the data is a cumulative effect of all the ingredients, though inclusion of the dynamical scattering centers has the largest relative importance.

## Key claim

The robust agreement between the authors' dynamical energy loss formalism and experimental jet suppression data is a cumulative effect of all included ingredients, not a single dominant effect; however, inclusion of dynamical scattering centers (modeling medium constituents as moving particles rather than static centers) has the largest relative importance.

## Method

The authors computed D meson nuclear modification factor (R_AA) predictions using a pQCD-based dynamical energy loss formalism that includes radiative and collisional energy losses, finite size (LPM) effects, finite magnetic mass, and running coupling. They systematically removed or modified individual ingredients, starting from the static approximation and sequentially adding dynamical scattering centers, collisional energy loss, finite size effects, finite magnetic mass, and running coupling, to isolate the contribution of each effect to the suppression predictions for central 200 GeV Au+Au collisions at RHIC and 2.76 TeV Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC.

## Key numbers

- At RHIC (200 GeV Au+Au, central), the static approximation (radiative only) yields D meson suppression that is 2–3 times smaller than experimentally observed at the LHC, demonstrating its inadequacy.
- At the LHC (2.76 TeV Pb+Pb, 0–7.5% central), the static approximation leads to 2–3 times smaller suppression than the data.
- Including collisional energy loss in a dynamical medium increases D meson suppression by up to a factor of two compared to dynamical radiative energy loss alone.
- Neglecting the finite size (LPM) effect can lead to as much as two times larger suppression at RHIC and several times larger suppression at the LHC.
- Inclusion of finite magnetic mass leads to a notable ~30% decrease in suppression.
- Running coupling increases suppression by almost a factor of two at lower jet momenta, with no significant difference at higher momenta.
- Effective temperatures used: 304 MeV at LHC (from ALICE) and 221 MeV at RHIC (from PHENIX).

## Context

This work extends the authors' dynamical energy loss formalism (Djordjevic 2009, Djordjevic and Heinz 2008) which previously showed robust agreement with experimental data across probes, experiments, and centralities. It directly addresses the question of which effects are responsible for that agreement, contrasting with other pQCD approaches (e.g., BDMPS, AMY, GLV, DGLV, HT) that commonly neglect some or all of these effects. The findings enable future work to test whether further improvements to the energy loss model are significant for explaining data at higher transverse momenta from upcoming RHIC and LHC runs.
