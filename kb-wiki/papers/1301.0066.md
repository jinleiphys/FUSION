# Proton Mass, Topology Change and Tensor Forces in Compressed Baryonic Matter

> This is a summary of the talks I gave at Korean Physical Society meeting (April 26, 2012, Daejeon, Korea) and the 4th Asian Triangle Heavy Ion Conference (ATHIC) (November 14, 2012, Pusan, Korea). They are based on the series of work done at Hanyang University in the World Class University III Program under the theme of "From Dense Matter to Compact Stars." The program was conceived and executed to understand highly compressed baryonic matter in anticipation of the forthcoming RIB machine "RAON" which is in construction in the Institute for Basic Science (IBS) in Korea. The problems treated ranged from the origin of the proton mass, topological structure of barynic matter, chiral symmetry and conformal symmetry to the EoS of nuclear matter and dense neutron-rich matter and to the maximum mass of neutron stars. The results obtained are new and intriguing and could have an impact on the novel structure of dense matter to be probed in the accelerators "RAON," FAIR etc. and in compact stars.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a substantial chirally-invariant component of the nucleon mass (denoted \(m_0\)) is indicated by the equation of state of nuclear matter and dense neutron-rich matter, and that this mass component plays a key role in compact-star properties; the claim is hedged by the admission that the topology change underlying this picture may be an artifact of the crystal approximation and that quantum (\(1/N_c\)) corrections could alter the qualitative results.

## Method

The author uses a single effective Lagrangian (dHLS: hidden local symmetry with a dilaton field \(\chi\) for scale symmetry) to describe mesons, baryons as skyrmions, and dense baryonic matter in a crystal lattice. The density-dependent parameters of the Lagrangian are extracted from skyrmion crystal calculations that exhibit a topology change from skyrmion matter to half-skyrmion matter at a density \(n_{1/2}\), where the average quark condensate vanishes but chiral symmetry is not restored. These parameters are then used in a double-decimation effective field theory approach (first to \(V_{lowk}\), then to Fermi-liquid theory) to compute the equation of state for nuclear matter and neutron stars.

## Key numbers

- The topology change from skyrmion to half-skyrmion matter occurs at a density \(n_{1/2}\) in the range \(1.3 \lesssim n_{1/2}/n_0 \lesssim 2.0\), where \(n_0 \sim 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\) is nuclear matter saturation density.
- The maximum neutron-star mass predicted is \(2.39 M_\odot\) (with radius 10.90 km) for \(n_{1/2}=2.0 n_0\), and \(2.38 M_\odot\) (radius 10.89 km) for \(n_{1/2}=1.5 n_0\).
- The maximum central density reached for such massive stars is \(\sim 5 n_0\).
- The symmetry energy factor \(E_{sym}\) is calculated from the skyrmion crystal moment of inertia via \(E_{sym}(n) \approx 1/(8\Omega_{cell}(n))\), showing a cusp at \(n_{1/2}=1.3 n_0\) in the crystal calculation.
- The nucleon mass in the parity-doublet model is expressed as \(m_N = m_0 + \bar{m}(\langle\bar{q}q\rangle)\), with the chirally-invariant mass \(m_0 \approx 0.8 m_N\) indicated by the new scaling.

## Context

This work extends the earlier "BR scaling" approach of Brown and Rho (1991) by replacing it with a new scaling ("BLPR") that incorporates a topology change from skyrmion to half-skyrmion matter. It contradicts the naive expectation that the nucleon mass vanishes with the quark condensate, instead supporting the parity-doublet model of DeTar and Kunihiro. The results enable predictions for the equation of state of neutron stars that can produce masses up to \(\sim 2.4 M_\odot\), and suggest that the tensor-force modification could have precursor effects in asymmetric nuclei to be studied at RIB facilities like RAON, FAIR, and NICA. The paper also notes that the vector-manifestation fixed point of hidden local symmetry (HY:PR) predicts that dileptons become "nearly blind" to the quark-condensate part of the \(\rho\) meson mass near chiral restoration, explaining why previous dilepton experiments were inconclusive.
