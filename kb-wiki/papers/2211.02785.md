# The Electron-Ion Collider -- A U.S. facility for the European community to explore the mysteries of the building blocks of matter

> This document is submitted as input to the NuPECC Long Range Plan 2024 by three European members of the EIC Users Group Steering Committee (Vice Chair, one at-large member, and the EU Representative). We submit the document on behalf of the international EIC Users Group (EICUG) community, but we specifically represent 335 European members of the EICUG (25%) based in 80 institutions (30% of the total) located in Armenia, Czech Republic, Finland, France, Germany, Hungary, Ireland, Israel, Italy, Netherlands, Norway, Poland, Slovenia, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, Ukraine, and the United Kingdom. This European involvement is an important driver of the EIC, but can also be beneficial for a number of related ongoing and planned nuclear physics experiments in Europe. In this document, the shared interest regarding scientific questions and detector R&D between the EIC and European nuclear physics communities is outlined. The aim is to highlight how these synergies offer ample opportunities to foster progress at the forefront of nuclear physics.

## Key claim
This document argues that the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC), a U.S. facility, offers substantial synergies with European nuclear physics experiments and detector R&D, and that the 335 European members (25% of the EIC Users Group) from 80 institutions represent a key driver for the project, not merely participants.

## Method
The authors, three European members of the EIC Users Group Steering Committee, submitted this document as input to the NuPECC Long Range Plan 2024. They outline the EIC's physics case, covering partonic structure of nucleons, nuclear structure, and connections to other fields, and describe how specific EIC measurement capabilities complement ongoing and planned European experiments at facilities including Jefferson Lab, CERN, FAIR, and the LHC.

## Key numbers
- The EIC will collide highly polarized electrons with heavy ions and polarized light ions at variable center-of-mass energy between 20 and 140 GeV, with peak luminosity around 10^34 cm^-2 sec^-1.
- 335 European members of the EICUG (25% of total) from 80 institutions (30% of total) across 18 European countries are represented.
- The EIC is on track for first collisions in the early 2030s, with construction start (CD3) targeted for 2025.
- In the ePIC Collaboration, 20% of working group conveners are European, and one member of the interim Steering Committee is European.

## Context
This document extends the EIC White Paper (2012) and the EIC Yellow Report (2021) by specifically addressing the European nuclear physics community's stake in the project. It builds upon the U.S. 2015 Long-Range Plan for Nuclear Physics recommendation of the EIC as the highest priority new facility after FRIB, and the 2018 endorsement by the U.S. National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine. The paper enables European institutions to align their detector R&D and scientific programs, including those at Jefferson Lab, CERN experiments (ALICE, ATLAS, CMS, LHCb), FAIR (PANDA), and RHIC, with EIC capabilities, particularly for studies of parton distributions, gluon saturation, and nuclear structure.
