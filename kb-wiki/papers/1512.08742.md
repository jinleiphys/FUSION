# In-medium jet evolution: interplay between broadening and decoherence effects

> The description of the modifications of the coherence pattern in a parton shower, in the presence of a QGP, has been actively addressed in recent studies. Among the several achievements, finite energy corrections, transverse momentum broadening due to medium interactions and interference effects between successive emissions have been extensively improved as they seem to be essential features for a correct description of the results obtained in heavy-ion collisions. In this work, based on the insights of our previous work [1], we explore the physical interplay between broadening and decoherence, by generalising previous studies of medium-modifications of the antenna spectrum [2, 3, 4] - so far restricted to the case where transverse motion is neglected. The result allow us to identify two quantities controlling the decoherence of a medium modified shower that can be used as building blocks for a successful future generation of jet quenching Monte Carlo simulators: a generalisation of the $Δ_{med}$ parameter of the works of [2, 4] - that controls the interplay between the transverse scale of the hard probe and the transverse resolution of the medium - and of the $Δ_{coh}$ in [1] - that dictates the interferences between two emitters as a function of the transverse momentum broadening acquired by multiple scatterings with the medium.

## Key claim

This work generalizes previous studies of medium-modified antenna spectra, which had been restricted to cases where transverse motion is neglected, by incorporating transverse momentum broadening and finite-energy corrections, thereby bringing the interplay between broadening and decoherence effects under theoretical control. The authors identify two quantities that control decoherence in a medium-modified shower: a generalization of the Δ_med parameter (controlling the interplay between the transverse scale of the hard probe and the transverse resolution of the medium) and the Δ_coh parameter (dictating interferences between two emitters as a function of transverse momentum broadening from multiple scatterings).

## Method

The authors compute the gluon emission spectrum for a non-eikonal QCD antenna (a quark-antiquark pair allowed to undergo Brownian motion in the transverse plane) propagating through a quark-gluon plasma, using a path-integral formalism with Green's functions that include Wilson lines and medium averaging. They separate the total cross-section into direct terms (which factorize into independent sources for gluon emission) and an interference term, generalizing the Δ_med parameter beyond the eikonal limit to account for different transverse coordinates in the amplitude and conjugate amplitude. The resulting spectrum is expressed as a sum of a coherent part (R_coh Δ_coh') and a decoherent part (2J Δ_med'), revealing the interplay between angular ordering regimes and coherence/decoherence effects.

## Key numbers

- The Δ_med parameter in the eikonal limit is given by Δ_med ≈ 1 - exp{ - (1/12) \hat{q} θ_{q\bar{q}} L_+ }, where \hat{q} is the transport coefficient, θ_{q\bar{q}} is the angle between the quark and antiquark, and L_+ is the medium length.
- The Δ_coh parameter is expressed as Δ_coh = 1 + ∫_{x_{2+}}^{L_+} dτ \hat{q}_F (x_q - \bar{x}_q)·(\bar{x}_g - x_g)|_τ exp{ \hat{q}_F ∫_{x_{2+}}^τ dξ (x_q - x_g)·(\bar{x}_g - \bar{x}_q) }, where \hat{q}_F is the transport coefficient characterizing transverse momentum squared transferred per mean free path.
- The vacuum antenna spectrum is dI/dΩ_k = α_s C_F (R_q + R_{\bar{q}} - 2J) = α_s C_F [q_1·q_2]/[(k·q_1)(k·q_2)], with C_F = (N²-1)/(2N) and N=3.

## Context

This work extends the eikonal antenna studies of Mehtar-Tani et al. (2010, 2012) and Casalderrey-Solana et al. (2011) by including transverse Brownian motion and finite medium effects, building on the authors' previous work (Apolinário et al., 2014) that introduced the Δ_coh parameter. The results support the picture of a medium-modified jet whose hard central core remains unmodified, consistent with experimental observations from the LHC, and provide building blocks for future jet quenching Monte Carlo simulators that must simultaneously describe jet energy loss, angular distributions, and fragmentation functions.
