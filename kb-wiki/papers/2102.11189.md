# The smallest fluid on earth

> High energy heavy ion collisions create quark gluon plasmas that behave like almost perfect fluids. Very similar features to those that led to this insight have also been observed in experimental data from collisions of small systems, involving protons or other light nuclei. We describe recent developments aimed at understanding whether, and if so how, systems that produce relatively few particles (orders of magnitude less than in typical heavy ion collisions) and are only one to a few times the size of a proton, can behave like fluids. This involves a deeper understanding of fluid dynamics and its applicability, improvements of our understanding of the initial geometry of the collisions by considering fluctuations of the proton shape, as well as advancements in the calculation of initial state effects within an effective theory of quantum chromodynamics, which can affect the observables that are used to study fluid behavior. We further address open questions and discuss future directions.

## Key claim

High-energy collisions of small systems (proton+proton, proton+lead, etc.) can exhibit fluid-like behavior even when producing orders of magnitude fewer particles than heavy ion collisions, but this conclusion depends on whether the observed azimuthal anisotropies arise from final-state hydrodynamic response to initial geometry or from initial-state effects such as color correlations in dense gluon fields.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical developments across three areas: (1) studies of hydrodynamization using hydrodynamic expansions, Boltzmann equation simulations, and AdS/CFT correspondence to determine when a dilute system can be described by fluid dynamics; (2) inclusion of fluctuating subnucleonic proton structure in the IP-Glasma model (a Color Glass Condensate effective field theory framework) constrained by incoherent diffractive J/psi production data from HERA; and (3) calculations of initial-state momentum anisotropies from the CGC effective field theory that are uncorrelated with the initial geometry.

## Key numbers

- The minimal droplet size for which hydrodynamics can apply is estimated to be approximately 0.15 fm (0.15 × 10^{-15} m) (Romatschke, 2016).
- Pressure anisotropies of order 50% (transverse pressure roughly two times larger than longitudinal pressure) are predicted at approximately 1 fm/c after the collision.
- The subnucleonic hot spot width is tightly constrained to approximately 0.4 fm from Bayesian analyses.
- The total fireball lifetime in small collision systems is often less than 3 fm/c.
- The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio used in kinetic theory calculations is several times 1/4π (the strong coupling limit).
- The IP-Glasma model with three Gaussian hot spots (radius approximately three times smaller than the proton radius) produces significantly larger incoherent diffractive cross sections than a round proton with only color charge fluctuations.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on relativistic fluid dynamics in heavy ion collisions (Heinz, 2013; Gale, 2013; Gajdosova, 2020) to much smaller systems such as p+p, p+Pb, p+Au, d+Au, and 3He+Au collisions at RHIC and LHC. It builds on prior studies of hydrodynamization (Heller, 2013; Romatschke, 2017) and the IP-Glasma model (Schenke, 2012), and uses constraints from HERA data on diffractive vector meson production (Mantysaari, 2016) to inform subnucleonic structure. The developments enable future work at the Electron Ion Collider and have implications beyond nuclear physics, including neutron star mergers and condensed matter systems.
