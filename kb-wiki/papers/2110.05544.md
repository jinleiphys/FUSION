# Effect of the Nuclear Equation of State on Relativistic-Turbulence Induced Core-Collapse Supernovae

> The nuclear equation of state is an important component in the evolution of core collapse supernovae. In this paper we make a survey of various equations of state in the literature and analyze their effect on spherical core-collapse models in which the effects of three-dimensional turbulence is modeled by a general relativistic formulation of Supernova Turbulence in Reduced dimensionality (STIR). We show that the viability of the explosion is quite EOS dependent and that it best correlates with the early-time interior entropy density of the proto-neutron star. We check that this result is not progenitor dependent, although low-mass progenitors show different explosion properties, due to the different pre-collapse nuclear composition. Larger central entropies also induce more vigorous proto-neutron-star convection in our one-dimensional turbulence model, as well as a wider convective layer.

# Effect of the Nuclear Equation of State on Relativistic-Turbulence Induced Core-Collapse Supernovae

## Key claim

The viability of neutrino-driven explosions in spherical core-collapse models that incorporate three-dimensional turbulence via the general relativistic STIR model is strongly dependent on the nuclear equation of state, and this dependence best correlates with the early-time interior entropy density of the proto-neutron star rather than with the strength of convection in the gain region. This correlation holds across different progenitor masses, although low-mass progenitors (9 solar masses) show different explosion properties due to their different pre-collapse nuclear composition.

## Method

The authors performed spherically symmetric, general relativistic simulations of core-collapse supernovae using a modified version of the GR1D code, incorporating the Supernova Turbulence in Reduced dimensionality (STIR) model to account for the effects of three-dimensional turbulent convection via a time-dependent mixing-length theory approach. They simulated four progenitors (9, 15, 20, and 25 solar masses from Sukhbold (2016)) using seven different nuclear equations of state: LS220, APR, KDE0v1, SLy4 (SRO-type), and SFHo, DD2, HShen (RMF-type), calibrating the mixing-length parameter alpha_MLT to 1.48 by comparing to a 3D simulation from Couch (2014) using the SFHo equation of state and a 20 solar mass progenitor.

## Key numbers

- The mixing-length parameter alpha_MLT was calibrated to 1.48, based on comparison of integrated turbulent energy at approximately 135 ms after bounce with a 3D simulation
- The explosion fraction for alpha_MLT = 1.48 lies just outside the 90% confidence interval of observational results from Neustadt (2021), while alpha_MLT = 1.5 is fully consistent with observations
- Simulations used 700 spatial zones with constant 0.3 km resolution in the inner 20 km and logarithmically spaced outer zones extending to 15,000 km
- 18 neutrino energy groups were used, logarithmically spaced from 1 to 280 MeV
- The LS220 equation of state has a nuclear saturation density n_0 of 0.1549 fm^{-3}, symmetry energy J of 28.61 MeV baryon^{-1}, and slope of symmetry energy L of 73.81 MeV baryon^{-1}
- The APR equation of state has n_0 = 0.1600 fm^{-3}, J = 32.59 MeV baryon^{-1}, L = 58.47 MeV baryon^{-1}, and K_0 = 266.00 MeV baryon^{-1}
- The HShen equation of state has the largest symmetry energy slope L = 110.99 MeV baryon^{-1} and the largest nuclear incompressibility K_0 = 281.00 MeV baryon^{-1}
- The DD2 equation of state has the smallest effective nucleon mass ratio m*_n/m_n = 0.5628
- Maximum neutron star masses range from 1.97 solar masses (KDE0v1) to 2.21 solar masses (HShen)

## Context

This work extends the general relativistic STIR model developed by Boccioli (2021) and the original STIR model by Couch (2020) by surveying multiple equations of state, whereas previous studies (Schneider 2019; Yasin 2020) focused only on Skyrme-type equations of state and their nuclear parameters. The study contrasts with earlier work by Ghosh (2021) that examined EOS effects on nucleosynthesis, and with multi-EOS studies by Suwa (2013) and Couch (2013) that considered fewer progenitors. The finding that early-time central entropy correlates with explosion strength, rather than gain-region convection, provides a new diagnostic for understanding explosion outcomes and enables future studies to predict explodability based on proto-neutron star properties.
