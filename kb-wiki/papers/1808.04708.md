# Probing heavy ion collisions using quark and gluon jet substructure with machine learning

> Understanding the inner working of the quark-gluon plasma requires complete and precise jet substructure studies in heavy ion collisions. In this proceeding we discuss the use of quark and gluon jets as independent probes, and how their classification allows us to uncover regions of QCD phase space sensitive to medium dynamics. We introduce the telescoping deconstruction (TD) framework to capture complete jet information and show that TD observables reveal fundamental properties of quark and gluon jets and their modifications in the medium. We draw connections to soft-drop subjet distributions and illuminate medium-induced jet modifications using Lund diagrams. The classification is also studied using a physics-motivated, multivariate analysis of jet substructure observables. Moreover, we apply image-recognition techniques by training a deep convolutional neural network on jet images to benchmark classification performances. We find that the quark gluon discrimination performance worsens in \textsc{Jewel}-simulated heavy ion collisions due to significant soft radiation affecting soft jet substructures. This work suggests a systematic framework for jet studies and facilitates direct comparisons between theoretical calculations and measurements in heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

Quark and gluon jet discrimination performance worsens in heavy ion collisions simulated with Jewel, because significant soft radiation from the medium washes out the substructure features that distinguish quark from gluon jets; this degradation is a possible signature of medium response to jets.

## Method

The author generated quark- and gluon-enriched jet samples using prompt photon production channels (q+gamma and g+gamma) in the Jewel event generator for both pp and PbPb collisions. Three complementary classification approaches were applied: a physics-motivated multivariate analysis using five jet observables (jet mass, radial moments, pT^D, pixel multiplicity), a deep convolutional neural network trained on jet images discretized in rapidity and azimuthal angle, and the telescoping deconstruction (TD) framework which organizes jet information into subjet momentum fraction z and angular distributions at multiple resolutions (T3 order). Classification performance was quantified using receiver-operating-characteristic (ROC) curves.

## Key numbers

* Quark gluon discrimination performance (quantified by ROC curves) worsens in Jewel-simulated AA collisions compared to pp collisions, with the degradation attributed to significant soft radiation affecting soft jet substructures.
* The TD framework at T3 order shows that subjet momentum fraction distributions exhibit a characteristic 1/z functional form, consistent with Altarelli-Parisi splitting functions.
* Pixel multiplicity is identified as the dominant observable for distinguishing jets in pp and AA collisions, reflecting significant soft event activities in heavy ion collisions.
* The collinear-drop observable delta m shows that the difference between quark and gluon jets disappears in AA collisions, suggesting soft radiation washes out flavor-distinguishing features.

## Context

This work extends the study of jet quenching from inclusive observables to detailed jet substructure modifications, building on prior work by Chien (2015) on jet energy redistribution and by Chatrchyan (2013) on quark/gluon jet fractions. The telescoping deconstruction framework connects to soft-drop subjet distributions studied by Larkoski (2014) and to the soft-drop z_g variable used by Sirunyan (2017) to probe medium-induced radiation in heavy ion collisions, which was first explained by Chien (2016). The results enable direct comparisons between theoretical calculations and measurements in heavy ion collisions, and suggest a systematic framework for using quark and gluon jets as independent probes of the quark-gluon plasma.
