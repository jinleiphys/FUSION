# Astrophysical neutrinos: 20th Century and Beyond

> I summarize the first four decades of solar neutrino research and suggest what may be possible to learn with extragalactic neutrinos and with solar neutrinos in the next decade.

## Key claim

Solar neutrino measurements over four decades have consistently shown deficits relative to standard solar model predictions, and helioseismology confirms the solar model to high precision (rms fractional sound-speed difference of 0.001), so the discrepancy requires new particle physics, likely neutrino oscillations, rather than modified solar physics.

## Method

The author reviews the history of solar neutrino detection from the first chlorine experiment (Ray Davis, 1968) through the six experiments reporting results by 2000 (chlorine, Kamiokande, Super-Kamiokande, GALLEX, SAGE, GNO, and SNO). He compares measured event rates to standard solar model predictions (BP98) and uses helioseismological sound-speed measurements to test whether solar physics modifications could explain the neutrino deficits. For extragalactic neutrinos, he discusses the Waxman-Bahcall upper bound on high-energy neutrino fluxes from gamma-ray bursts and the sensitivity of proposed detectors (ANTARES, BAIKAL, ICECUBE, NESTOR) to vacuum neutrino oscillations.

## Key numbers

- Measured solar neutrino rates range from 33% ± 5% of the calculated rate (chlorine) to 58% ± 7% (other experiments), assuming no neutrino oscillations.
- Rms fractional difference between calculated and measured solar sound speeds is 1.1 × 10⁻³ for the entire measured region (0.05 R⊙ to 0.95 R⊙) and 0.7 × 10⁻³ in the solar core (0.05 R⊙ to 0.25 R⊙), where about 95% of solar energy and neutrino flux is produced.
- At 99% CL, the inferred ⁸B neutrino flux at the source (correcting for oscillations) lies between 0.55 and 1.32 times the standard solar model prediction.
- The ⁸B neutrino flux depends on the 25th power of the solar central temperature.
- More than 98% of the calculated standard model solar neutrino flux lies below 1 MeV; ⁸B neutrinos constitute less than 10⁻⁴ of the total flux.
- The p-p neutrinos carry about 91% of the total flux; ⁷Be neutrinos constitute about 7%.
- The p-p neutrino flux is calculated to ±1% accuracy.
- Extragalactic neutrino experiments (ANTARES, BAIKAL, ICECUBE, NESTOR) can reach L/E > 10¹⁸ km/GeV, sensitive to Δm² ≥ 10⁻¹⁷ eV².
- Gamma-ray bursts should produce about 10 events per km² per year from neutrinos with characteristic energies of order 10¹⁴ eV.
- GRBs can test special relativity to 1 part in 10¹⁶ and the weak equivalence principle to 1 part in 10⁶.

## Context

This paper reviews the first four decades of solar neutrino research, extending the work of Pontecorvo (1967) who first suggested using the sun for neutrino oscillation studies, and Davis (1955-1968) who built the first chlorine detector and detected solar neutrinos. The author's own standard solar model calculations (BP98, with Basu and Pinsonneault, 1998) are used as the reference predictions. The paper sets the stage for the SNO experiment's neutral-current to charged-current ratio measurement, which would provide a decisive test of neutrino oscillations, and identifies the measurement of p-p and ⁷Be neutrinos below 1 MeV as the next major challenge. For extragalactic neutrinos, it introduces the Waxman-Bahcall upper bound (1999) that constrains optimistic AGN models by Mannheim (1995), Protheroe (1997), and Halzen and Zas (1997).
