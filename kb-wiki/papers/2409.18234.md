# Coupled-Cluster Calculations of Infinite Nuclear Matter in the Complete Basis Limit Using Bayesian Machine Learning

> Infinite nuclear matter provides valuable insights into the behavior of nuclear systems and aids our understanding of atomic nuclei and large-scale stellar objects such as neutron stars. However, partly due to the large basis needed to converge the system's binding energy, size-extensive methods such as coupled-cluster theory struggle with long computational run times, even using the nation's largest high-performance computing facilities. This research introduces a novel approach to the problem. We propose using a machine learning method to predict the coupled-cluster energies of infinite matter systems in the complete basis limit, leveraging only data collected using smaller basis sets. This method promises to deliver high-accuracy results with significantly reduced run times. The sequential regression extrapolation (SRE) algorithm, based on Gaussian processes, was created to perform these extrapolations. By combining Bayesian machine learning with a unique method of formatting the training data, we can create a powerful extrapolator that can make accurate predictions given very little data. The SRE algorithm successfully predicted the CCD(T) energies for pure neutron matter across six densities near nuclear saturation density, with an average error of 0.0083 MeV/N. The algorithm achieved an average error of 0.038 MeV/A for symmetric nuclear matter. These predictions were made with a time savings of 83.8 node hours for pure neutron matter and 284 node hours for symmetric nuclear matter. Additionally, the symmetry energy at these six densities was predicted with an average error of 0.031 MeV/A and a total time savings of 368 node hours compared to the traditional converged coupled-cluster calculations performed without the SRE algorithm.

## Key claim

The sequential regression extrapolation (SRE) algorithm, based on Gaussian processes, can predict coupled-cluster energies of infinite nuclear matter in the complete basis limit using only training data from smaller basis sets, achieving high accuracy with significantly reduced computational run times. The method relies on the ratio of CCD(T) to MBPT2 correlation energies converging to a constant at large numbers of single-particle states, and the predictions are limited to densities where MBPT2 calculations converge without problems.

## Method

The authors performed CCD(T) (coupled-cluster doubles with perturbative triples) calculations for pure neutron matter and symmetric nuclear matter using the NNLOGO interaction, with 66 neutrons for PNM and 132 nucleons for SNM. They trained a Gaussian process with a rational quadratic kernel (modified by white and constant kernels) on sequential ratios of CCD(T) to MBPT2 correlation energies at increasing numbers of single-particle states, using three training points per density for PNM (M from 186 to 294) and six for SNM (M from 324 to 684). The trained model iteratively predicted subsequent ratios until convergence, and the converged ratio was multiplied by the MBPT2 correlation energy at a large M to approximate the CCD(T) correlation energy in the complete basis limit.

## Key numbers

- Average error for pure neutron matter CCD(T) energies across six densities: 0.0083 MeV/N (0.057% average percent error)
- Average error for symmetric nuclear matter CCD(T) energies across six densities: 0.038 MeV/A (0.21% average percent error)
- Time savings for pure neutron matter: 83.8 node hours for six data points (84.3 node hours for converged calculations versus 0.41 node hours for training data)
- Time savings for symmetric nuclear matter: 284 node hours for six data points (390 node hours for converged calculations versus 106 node hours for training data)
- Average error for symmetry energy across six densities: 0.031 MeV/A (0.084% average percent error)
- Total time savings for symmetry energy predictions: 368 node hours
- Average uncertainty on predictions: 0.0043 MeV/N for PNM, 0.032 MeV/A for SNM
- Average percent error between converged energies and training data at M=294 for PNM: 21.4%
- Average percent error between converged energies and training data at M=358 for SNM: 9.34%

## Context

This work extends the SRE method previously applied to the homogeneous electron gas (Butler et al., 2023) to infinite nuclear matter calculations, addressing the computational bottleneck of large-scale coupled-cluster studies needed for determining the nuclear equation of state. The approach enables computationally feasible studies of infinite nuclear matter across multiple densities and proton fractions, which are crucial for understanding neutron star properties and the symmetry energy, and the authors note the method can be generalized to other many-body methods such as many-body perturbation theory and in-medium similarity renormalization group theory (Hergert, 2017).
