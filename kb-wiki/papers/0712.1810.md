# Flux tubes and the type-I/type-II transition in a superconductor coupled to a superfluid

> We analyze magnetic flux tubes at zero temperature in a superconductor that is coupled to a superfluid via both density and gradient (``entrainment'') interactions. The example we have in mind is high-density nuclear matter, which is a proton superconductor and a neutron superfluid, but our treatment is general and simple, modeling the interactions as a Ginzburg-Landau effective theory with four-fermion couplings, including only s-wave pairing. We numerically solve the field equations for flux tubes with an arbitrary number of flux quanta, and compare their energies. This allows us to map the type-I/type-II transition in the superconductor, which occurs at the conventional kappa = 1/sqrt(2) if the condensates are uncoupled. We find that a density coupling between the condensates raises the critical kappa and, for a sufficiently high neutron density, resolves the type-I/type-II transition line into an infinite number of bands corresponding to ``type-II(n)'' phases, in which n, the number of quanta in the favored flux tube, steps from 1 to infinity. For lower neutron density, the coupling creates spinodal regions around the type-I/type-II boundary, in which metastable flux configurations are possible. We find that a gradient coupling between the condensates lowers the critical kappa and creates spinodal regions. These exotic phenomena may not occur in nuclear matter, which is thought to be deep in the type-II region, but might be observed in condensed matter systems.

## Key claim

The type-I/type-II transition in a proton superconductor coupled to a neutron superfluid is modified by density and gradient interactions: a density coupling raises the critical Ginzburg-Landau parameter kappa above the conventional value of 1/sqrt(2), while a gradient coupling lowers it, and under certain conditions the transition line can split into an infinite series of bands corresponding to stable multi-quantum flux tubes.

## Method

The authors constructed a Ginzburg-Landau effective theory with s-wave pairing and four-fermion couplings for a proton superconductor coupled to a neutron superfluid, including both density coupling (parameter beta = a_pn/a_pp) and gradient entrainment coupling (parameter sigma). They numerically solved the coupled differential equations for cylindrically symmetric flux tubes containing n flux quanta using a finite-element relaxation method, then computed the energy per flux quantum E_n/n for n ranging from 1 to several hundred to determine the energetically favored number of flux quanta.

## Key numbers

* The conventional type-I/type-II transition occurs at kappa = 1/sqrt(2) when the condensates are uncoupled (beta = sigma = 0).
* For nuclear matter, the proton fraction is taken as vevp^2/vevn^2 ≈ 0.05 (5% to 10% range).
* The entrainment parameter sigma is estimated to be of order 10^{-1}, based on the relation sigma = eps * vevn/vevp with eps ≈ 0.03 and vevn^2/vevp^2 ≈ 20.
* Numerical errors in E_n/n were found to be of order 10^{-6}, invisible on the scale of the energy per flux plots.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Buckley et al. (2005) by surveying the full range of density coupling values and including gradient (entrainment) interactions between the proton and neutron condensates, using a simpler direct method based on flux tube energetics. The study was motivated by questions about whether proton superconductivity in neutron star matter is type I or type II, following evidence from long neutron star precession periods that seemed to favor type I (Link, 2003), contrary to the general belief that protons form a type-II superconductor (Baym, Pethick, and Pines, 1969). The results enable future work on calculating the interaction energy U(d) between two flux tubes at finite separation, which could reveal new varieties of type-II superconductors with favored Abrikosov lattice spacings.
