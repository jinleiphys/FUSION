# Extreme Shape Coexistence Observed in $^{70}$Co

> The shape of the atomic nucleus is a property which underpins our understanding of nuclear systems, impacts the limits of nuclear existence, and enables probes of physics beyond the Standard Model. Nuclei can adopt a variety of shapes, including spheres, axially deformed spheroids, and pear shapes. In some regions of the nuclear chart where a spherical nucleus would naively be expected, deformed nuclear states can result from collective action of constituent protons and neutrons. In a small subset of nuclei both spherical and deformed nuclear states have been experimentally observed, a phenomenon termed shape coexistence. We present spectroscopic evidence for the coexistence of $J^π=1+$ spherical and deformed states in $^{70}$Co, separated by less than 275~keV. This close degeneracy of levels with the same $J^π$ and different shapes demonstrates an extreme example of shape coexistence resulting from the interplay of independent particle motion and collective behavior in highly unstable nuclear systems and identifies the Co isotopes as a transition point between deformed ground states observed in the Cr isotopes and spherical configurations observed in the closed-shell Ni isotopes.

# Extreme Shape Coexistence Observed in $^{70}$Co

## Key claim

The paper presents spectroscopic evidence that the low-spin beta-decaying state in $^{70}$Co has $J^\pi = 1^+$ and a strongly deformed character, coexisting with a weakly deformed (near-spherical) $J^\pi = 1^+$ state separated by less than 275 keV. The claim is hedged by noting that the relative ordering of the two states in the calculations depends on model space truncation effects, and that a correction derived from the mirror nucleus $^{51}$Cr places the strongly deformed state a few hundred keV below the weakly deformed state, consistent with experiment.

## Method

The experiment used total absorption spectroscopy (TAS) with the Summing NaI(Tl) (SuN) detector at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams. A beam of $^{70}$Fe was implanted at the center of the SuN detector, and the beta-decay of the $J^\pi = 0^+$ ground state of $^{70}$Fe preferentially populated the low-spin beta-decaying state of $^{70}$Co. The measured beta-decay feeding intensities and Gamow-Teller strength distributions were compared to shell model calculations performed with the NuShellX code using the $fpg9tn$ model space with $t1$ and $t2$ truncations on proton excitations across the Z=28 shell gap.

## Key numbers

- The two $J^\pi = 1^+$ states in $^{70}$Co are separated by less than 275 keV, with the 274 keV gamma-ray deexciting the weakly deformed state populated in $^{70}$Fe beta-decay.
- The low-spin beta-decaying state in $^{70}$Co has a half-life of approximately 470 ms, while the high-spin state has a half-life of approximately 65 ms.
- The strongly deformed $J^\pi = 1^+$ state predominantly feeds a localized set of levels at roughly 6 MeV excitation energy in $^{70}$Ni.
- In the $t2$ truncation calculations, the weakly deformed $J^\pi = 1^+$ state appears 220 keV below the strongly deformed $J^\pi = 1^+$ state.
- The lowest $0^+$ state in $^{70}$Ni is located at 1.57 MeV and is fed with only 0.8% of the total intensity from the strongly deformed $J^\pi = 1^+$ state in calculations.
- The $^{51}$Cr mirror case shows that $t2$ truncation places the strongly deformed band roughly 600 keV too high relative to full space calculations.

## Context

This work extends upon indications from Morales (2017) that shape coexistence may occur in $^{70}$Co, and follows previous TAS measurements of the high-spin beta-decaying state in $^{70}$Co by Spyrou (2016). The results identify the Co isotopes as a transition point between deformed ground states observed in the Cr isotopes and spherical configurations in the closed-shell Ni isotopes, and are relevant to understanding Type II shell evolution as described by Otsuka (2016) and the possible merging of the N=40 and proposed N=50 islands of inversion discussed by Nowacki (2016). Future experiments measuring the lifetime of the 274 keV level or its angular distribution could further confirm the interpretation, and theoretical calculations extending to $t3$ truncation including the $1d_{5/2}$ orbital remain to be carried out.
