# Cross-Section Bootstrap: Unveiling the Froissart Amplitude

> We derive a universal bound on the integrated total scattering cross-section at \emph{finite} energies, expressed in terms of a single low-energy coefficient constrained by the non-perturbative S-matrix Bootstrap. At high energies, the bound is compared with proton-proton scattering data; at low energies, with numerical bootstrap results obtained by directly maximizing the cross-section. We conjecture that the amplitude saturating the cross-section at high energies lies at a strongly-coupled corner of the allowed space of low-energy parameters. This universal amplitude exhibits a rising total cross-section, a shrinking elastic differential cross-section with multiple diffractive minima, and a surprisingly rich spectrum of resonances aligning along Regge trajectories, including Pomeron-like and daughter trajectories, as well as unusual ``singular" trajectories in the forward limit which appear deeply interconnected with Froissart growth. Remarkably, the eikonal representation reveals that the scattering is localized within an annular region that slowly expands with energy, challenging the traditional ``disk" diffraction picture. Our results open the door to theoretical and phenomenological studies of \emph{soft} high-energy hadronic scattering via the S-matrix Bootstrap.

## Key claim

The paper derives a universal upper bound on the *integrated* total scattering cross-section at finite energies, expressed in terms of a single low-energy coefficient that can be constrained by the S-matrix Bootstrap; the bound is not pointwise in energy but applies to an integrated quantity, and its saturation at high energies is conjectured to correspond to a universal "Froissart amplitude" located at a strongly-coupled corner of the allowed space of low-energy parameters.

## Method

The authors derive an analytic bound on the integrated total cross-section for identical scalar particles in any dimension by splitting the partial-wave sum at a cutoff angular momentum L, setting the low-spin partial waves to their unitarity limit (Re S_l = -1), and bounding the high-spin contribution using a positive dispersive relation for the low-energy coefficient c_{2k}(t_0). They then determine the maximal value of c_2(t_0) numerically via the S-matrix Bootstrap, using a multi-foliation basis expansion of the amplitude that enforces maximal analyticity and crossing symmetry, with unitarity imposed as a positive semi-definite condition on partial waves up to L_max = 18 and improved positivity constraints for higher spins. The extremal amplitude at the (min c_0, max c_2) cusp is identified as the Froissart amplitude and studied in the physical region.

## Key numbers

- The analytic bound in four dimensions reduces to the Froissart bound: sigma_tot(s) <= (4 pi / t_0) log^2(s / s_0) as s -> infinity, with t_0 = (2 m_pi)^2.
- The Bootstrap exclusion bound lies within a factor of ten of experimental proton-proton and proton-antiproton scattering data (Fig. 1).
- The direct numerical maximization of the integrated cross-section at finite s yields a bound that differs from the analytic exclusion bound by a factor slightly below five.
- The optimal cutoff angular momentum for saturating the bound scales as L*(s) ~ (1/4) sqrt(s) log s in the high-energy limit.
- The Pomeron-like trajectory B is fitted by a linear Regge trajectory alpha(t) = alpha_0 + alpha' t with alpha_0 ~ 1.1 and alpha' ~ 0.2.
- An independent fit of the growth of the Froissart amplitude yields alpha_0 ~ 1.2.
- The integrated cross-section of the Froissart amplitude lies below both the direct numerical maximization result and the analytic upper bound for each fixed s (Fig. 3).

## Context

This work extends the classical Froissart-Martin bound (Phys. Rev. 123, 1053; Martin bound) by making it quantitative at finite energies, removing Yndurain's (1972) assumption of a finite D-wave scattering length. It builds on the modern non-perturbative S-matrix Bootstrap framework developed by Paulos et al. (2016-2019), Guerrieri et al. (2018-2024), and others, and it connects to earlier Regge theory predictions of Pomeron trajectories and singular trajectories in the forward limit proposed by Schwarz (1968), Oehme (1971-1973), and others. The results enable systematic theoretical and phenomenological studies of soft high-energy hadronic scattering via the S-matrix Bootstrap, potentially explaining the discrepancy between the measured coefficient c in proton-proton scattering (two orders of magnitude below the Froissart bound) and the true asymptotic regime.
