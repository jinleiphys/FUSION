# From quark drops to quark stars: some aspects of the role of quark matter in compact stars

> We review some recent results about the mechanism of deconfinement of hadronic matter into quark matter in cold neutron stars and protoneutron stars. We discuss the role of finite size effects and the relevance of temperature and density fluctuations on the nucleation process. We also examine the importance of surface effects for mixed phases in hybrid stars. A small drop of quark matter nucleated at the core of a compact star may grow if the conversion is sufficiently exothermic. In such a case, it may trigger the burning of the stellar core and even the whole star if quark matter is absolutely stable. We explore the physical processes that occur inside the flame and analyze the hydrodynamic evolution of the combustion front. In the last part of this review, we focus on hybrid stars using the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model with scalar, vector and 't Hooft interactions, paying particular attention to a generalized non-standard procedure for the choice of the 'bag constant'. We also describe the non-radial oscillation modes of hadronic, hybrid and strange stars with maximum masses above $2 M_{\odot}$ and show that the frequency of the $p_1$ and $g$ fluid modes contains key information about the internal composition of compact objects.

## Key claim

This review argues that while finite-size effects (surface tension and curvature energy) strongly suppress the formation of a mixed hadron-quark phase in hybrid stars, they do not preclude the nucleation of quark matter drops that can trigger a full conversion of the star, provided the bulk transition density is reached near the stellar core.

## Method

The author reviews calculations using the multiple reflection expansion (MRE) formalism to incorporate finite-size effects into the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model for three-flavor quark matter, including color superconductivity. Nucleation rates and transition curves are computed for quark drops both out of chemical equilibrium (during the initial deconfinement) and in weak-equilibrium (for mixed phases). The hydrodynamic evolution of the resulting combustion front is discussed, and non-radial oscillation modes of hybrid and strange stars are analyzed using several equations of state.

## Key numbers

- Surface tension α for quark matter in the NJL model is in the range 120–150 MeV fm⁻² for drops out of chemical equilibrium, and 145–165 MeV fm⁻² for drops in chemical equilibrium (for temperatures 0–30 MeV and radii 5–∞ fm).
- Curvature energy γ is in the range 100–110 MeV fm⁻¹ for out-of-equilibrium drops and 95–110 MeV fm⁻¹ for equilibrium drops.
- The critical surface tension above which a mixed phase becomes mechanically unstable is approximately 65 MeV fm⁻², based on Voskresensky (2003).
- Within the combustion flame, weak decays increase the temperature to 60–70 MeV in a timescale of ∼10⁻⁹ s.
- Neutrino emissivity per baryon peaks at 10¹⁰–10¹² MeV/s during the first ∼10⁻¹⁰ s.
- Total energy released as neutrinos per baryon is 30–60 MeV for cold neutron stars and 10–55 MeV for protoneutron stars; for a whole star with ∼10⁵⁸ baryons, this yields roughly ∼10⁵³ erg.
- The laminar deflagration velocity from s-quark diffusion is estimated at v_lam < 10⁴ cm/s; more recent calculations give burning speeds between 0.002c and 0.04c.
- The pulsars PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0348-0432 have masses of (1.97 ± 0.04) M⊙ and (2.01 ± 0.04) M⊙ respectively.

## Context

This review extends earlier work on quark matter nucleation in neutron stars using the MIT bag model (doCarmo 2013) and the NJL model (Lugones 2011, 2013), finding that despite large differences in surface tension between models, the critical nucleation radius remains of order hundreds of fm. The results contradict the possibility of a mixed hadron-quark phase in hybrid stars for the large surface tensions predicted by the NJL model (above 65 MeV fm⁻²), instead favoring a sharp interface. The analysis of non-radial oscillation modes (f, p₁, and g modes) for stars above 2 M⊙ enables future gravitational-wave observations to distinguish between hadronic, hybrid, and strange quark stars.
