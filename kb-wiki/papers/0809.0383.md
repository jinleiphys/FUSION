# Statistical Global Modeling of Beta-Decay Halflives Systematics Using Multilayer Feedforward Neural Networks and Support Vector Machines

> In this work, the beta-decay halflives problem is dealt as a nonlinear optimization problem, which is resolved in the statistical framework of Machine Learning (LM). Continuing past similar approaches, we have constructed sophisticated Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) and Support Vector Regression Machines (SVMs) for each class with even-odd character in Z and N to global model the systematics of nuclei that decay 100% by the beta-minus-mode in their ground states. The arising large-scale lifetime calculations generated by both types of machines are discussed and compared with each other, with the available experimental data, with previous results obtained with neural networks, as well as with estimates coming from traditional global nuclear models. Particular attention is paid on the estimates for exotic and halo nuclei and we focus to those nuclides that are involved in the r-process nucleosynthesis. It is found that statistical models based on LM can at least match or even surpass the predictive performance of the best conventional models of beta-decay systematics and can complement the latter.

## Key claim

Statistical models based on Machine Learning (Artificial Neural Networks and Support Vector Machines) can match or surpass the predictive performance of the best conventional global nuclear models for beta-decay halflives, but the subdivision of data into four parity classes (even-even, even-odd, odd-even, odd-odd) can lead to spurious fluctuations in predictions for isotopic and isotonic chains.

## Method

The authors constructed four separate multilayer feedforward Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) and four Support Vector Regression Machines (SVMs), one for each parity class of proton number Z and neutron number N (EE, EO, OE, OO), to model the systematics of beta-minus decay halflives for nuclei decaying 100% by that mode in their ground states. The ANNs used a [2-5-5-5-1] architecture with hyperbolic-tangent sigmoid activation in hidden layers and a saturated linear output, trained via Levenberg-Marquardt backpropagation with Bayesian regularization. The SVMs employed a composite pA-type kernel with parameters p=1 and d=8, and a control parameter C equal to 3.861 x 10^-3 for all four classes. The models were trained on a subset (NuSet-B) of 838 nuclides from the Nubase2003 evaluation, with halflives ranging from 0.15 x 10^-2 s to 0.20 x 10^6 s, split into learning (80%), validation (10%), and test (10%) sets.

## Key numbers

- Root-mean-square error (sigma_RMSE) for the total ANN models: 0.41 (learning set, 672 nuclides), 0.44 (validation set, 83 nuclides), 0.51 (test set, 83 nuclides)
- Root-mean-square error (sigma_RMSE) for the total SVM models: 0.47 (learning set, 672 nuclides), 0.46 (validation set, 83 nuclides), 0.53 (test set, 83 nuclides)
- For the ANN models in overall mode on nuclides with experimental halflife less than 1 s (252 nuclides): M = 0.02, sigma_M = 0.30, Sigma = 0.30
- For the FRDM+pnQRPA model on nuclides with experimental halflife less than 1 s (184 nuclides): M = 0.03, sigma_M = 0.57, Sigma = 0.57
- For the pnQRPA+ffGT model on nuclides with experimental halflife less than 1 s (184 nuclides): M = -0.08, sigma_M = 0.48, Sigma = 0.49
- The halflives in the NuSet-B dataset range from 0.15 x 10^-2 s for Na-35 to 0.20 x 10^6 s for Pu-247

## Context

This work extends previous statistical modeling approaches to nuclear halflife systematics (Costiris et al. 2008, earlier ANN models) and provides a direct comparison between ANN and SVM algorithms for the same task. The results are compared with conventional "theory-thick" models such as the FRDM+pnQRPA and pnQRPA+ffGT models of Moller and collaborators (2003), as well as the Gross Theory calculations of Takahashi et al. (1973) as implemented by Pfeiffer and coworkers. The models are particularly aimed at providing halflife estimates for neutron-rich nuclei involved in r-process nucleosynthesis, where experimental data are scarce and conventional models show deviations from experiment of at least an order of magnitude.
