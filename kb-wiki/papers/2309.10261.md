# Visualizing quantum coherence and decoherence in nuclear reactions

> Differential cross sections of nuclear reactions often exhibit characteristic oscillations in the angular distribution originated from an interference of two indistinguishable processes. Here we propose a novel method to visualize origins of such oscillations. This is achieved by taking Fourier transform of scattering amplitudes, following the idea in wave optics. We apply this method to elastic scattering of $^{16}$O+$^{16}$O and $^{18}$O+$^{18}$O at energies above the Coulomb barrier. The former system shows strong oscillations in the angular distribution due to the nearside-farside interferences, while the oscillations are largely suppressed in the latter system due to a stronger absorption. We show that the image of the former and the latter systems corresponds to a double-slit and a single-slit problems in quantum mechanics, respectively.

# Visualizing quantum coherence and decoherence in nuclear reactions

## Key claim
The Fourier-transform imaging method, adapted from wave optics and previously applied in particle physics, can visualize the origin of angular oscillations in nuclear reaction cross sections; the method works only when a fitted optical-model scattering amplitude is available, and the analogy to double-slit versus single-slit interference holds specifically for the unsymmetrized (not the full symmetrized) scattering amplitude at energies about 2.6 times the Coulomb barrier.

## Method
The authors take a Fourier transform of the scattering amplitude over a finite angular window (a "lens") centered at a chosen scattering angle, following the formalism of Hashimoto (2023). They apply this to elastic scattering of 16O+16O and 18O+18O at energies above the Coulomb barrier, using optical-model fits to experimental angular distributions: a deep squared Woods-Saxon potential for 16O+16O and the same potential plus a surface imaginary term for 18O+18O. The resulting image intensity I(X,Y) is interpreted as a spatial map on a virtual screen behind the lens, with peak positions corresponding to classical impact parameters.

## Key numbers
- 16O+16O elastic scattering at E_cm = 26.5 MeV (about 2.6 times the Coulomb barrier height of ~10.3 MeV) shows strong angular oscillations; the nearside and farside components cross at around θ = 51 degrees.
- 18O+18O elastic scattering at E_cm = 26 MeV shows largely suppressed oscillations; the farside component is strongly absorbed.
- For Rutherford scattering of 16O+16O at E_cm = 8.8 MeV with θ0 = 90 degrees and Δθ = Δφ = 30 degrees, the image peak is at X = 5.65 fm, close to the classical impact parameter b_cl = 5.24 fm.
- The optical-model fit for 16O+16O uses V0 = 421.28 MeV, RR = 4.12 fm, aR = 1.52 fm, W0 = 157.1 MeV, RW = 4.39 fm, aW = 0.151 fm; for 18O+18O an additional surface imaginary potential is added with Ws = 94.01 MeV, Rs = 5.61 fm, as = 0.734 fm, and the radii are scaled by a factor of 1.04.

## Context
This work extends the Fourier-imaging approach from particle physics (Hashimoto 2023, applied to string scattering and black-hole imaging in AdS/CFT) to low-energy nuclear reactions. It builds on the established nearside-farside decomposition method of Fuller (1975) and the earlier analogy between nearside-farside interference and the double-slit problem discussed by Hussein (1984). The imaging technique provides a visual confirmation that 16O+16O scattering at 26.5 MeV behaves like a double slit (two peaks from nearside and farside components), while 18O+18O at 26 MeV behaves like a single slit (one peak from the nearside component only), with decoherence caused by stronger absorption from the two extra neutrons. The method enables future applications to other interference phenomena in nuclear reactions, such as Coulomb-nuclear interference and barrier-wave-internal-wave interference, as well as to inelastic scattering.
