# Neutrinoless Double-Beta Decay: Status and Prospects

> Neutrinoless double-beta decay is a forbidden, lepton-number-violating nuclear transition whose observation would have fundamental implications for neutrino physics, theories beyond the Standard Model and cosmology. In this review, we summarize the theoretical progress to understand this process, the expectations and implications under various particle physics models, as well as the nuclear physics challenges that affect the precise predictions of the decay half-life. We will also provide a synopsis of the current and future large-scale experiments that aim at discovering this process in physically well-motivated half-life ranges.

# Neutrinoless Double-Beta Decay: Status and Prospects

## Key claim

Neutrinoless double-beta decay remains unobserved, with current half-life limits exceeding 10^26 years; its discovery would confirm lepton-number violation and the Majorana nature of neutrinos, but the interpretation depends on the assumed underlying mechanism, which could range from sub-eV light neutrino exchange to multi-TeV heavy particle exchange.

## Method

This is a comprehensive review article summarizing theoretical progress in understanding neutrinoless double-beta decay, including particle physics motivations (neutrino mass mechanisms, alternative beyond-Standard-Model mechanisms, QCD corrections), nuclear physics challenges (hadronization, nuclear matrix element calculations using multiple many-body approaches), and experimental status (design criteria, current and future large-scale detector technologies). The review covers the connection between the effective Majorana mass, neutrino oscillation parameters, cosmological constraints, and collider searches for lepton-number violation.

## Key numbers

- Current best half-life limits for neutrinoless double-beta decay exceed 10^26 years
- The effective Majorana mass is given by ⟨m_ββ⟩ = |U_ei² m_i|, with current limits around 0.2 eV
- The minimum effective mass in the inverted mass ordering scenario is approximately 0.013 eV, representing a physics goal for current and upcoming experiments
- The half-life for light-neutrino exchange scales as T^{0ν}_{1/2} ≈ 10^{27-28} (0.01 eV / ⟨m_ββ⟩)² years
- For heavy physics mechanisms, the half-life scales approximately as T^{heavy}_{1/2} ∼ 10^{27-28} ( (c̃/M) / TeV )^{10} years
- The phase-space factor G is proportional to Q_{ββ}^5 and is of order 10^{-25} / (y·eV²)
- The nuclear matrix element approximation |M|² ∼ 10 is used for order-of-magnitude estimates
- Current conservative cosmological limits on the sum of neutrino masses Σ are about 0.3 eV
- The projected KATRIN sensitivity for the kinematic neutrino mass m_β is 0.2 eV
- Limits on the short-range operator coefficients ε^X range from 10^{-7} to 10^{-10}
- The induced pseudoscalar correction to the leading Gamov-Teller matrix element is of order q²/(q² + m_π²) ∼ 30% in the light-neutrino case

## Context

This review extends and updates earlier reviews on neutrinoless double-beta decay, including those by Moe (1994), Elliott (2002), Rodejohann (2011), and Dell'Oro (2016). It covers the period when experiments have reached half-life limits in the 10^25–10^26 year range and a worldwide program aiming for two orders of magnitude improvement in sensitivity is being pursued. The review enables comparison of the discovery potential of next-generation experiments (CUPID, KamLAND-Zen, LEGEND, nEXO, NEXT, PANDA-X, SNO+) and connects neutrinoless double-beta decay searches to complementary probes including direct kinematic mass measurements (KATRIN, ECHo, Project-8), cosmological observations (Planck, future Euclid and Square Kilometre Array data), and collider searches for same-sign dilepton events at the LHC.
