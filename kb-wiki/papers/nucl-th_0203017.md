# Three-Nucleon Force Effects in Nucleon Induced Deuteron Breakup: Predictions of Current Models (I)

> An extensive study of three-nucleon force effects in the entire phase space of the nucleon-deuteron breakup process, for energies from above the deuteron breakup threshold up to 200 MeV, has been performed. 3N Faddeev equations have been solved rigorously using the modern high precision nucleon-nucleon potentials AV18, CD Bonn, Nijm I, II and Nijm 93, and also adding 3N forces. We compare predictions for cross sections and various polarization observables when NN forces are used alone or when the two pion-exchange Tucson-Melbourne 3NF was combined with each of them. In addition AV18 was combined with the Urbana IX 3NF and CD Bonn with the TM' 3NF, which is a modified version of the TM 3NF, more consistent with chiral symmetry. Large but generally model dependent 3NF effects have been found in certain breakup configurations, especially at the higher energies, both for cross sections and spin observables. These results demonstrate the usefulness of the kinematically complete breakup reaction in testing the proper structure of 3N forces.

## Key claim

The paper systematically maps three-nucleon force (3NF) effects across the full phase space of nucleon-deuteron breakup, finding large but strongly model-dependent effects in specific kinematic configurations at higher energies, while noting that the total breakup cross section is dominated by quasi-free scattering where 3NF effects are negligible.

## Method

The authors solved rigorous 3N Faddeev equations for nucleon-deuteron breakup using five high-precision nucleon-nucleon (NN) potentials (AV18, CD Bonn, Nijm I, II, and Nijm 93) alone and combined with the two-pion-exchange Tucson-Melbourne (TM) 3NF, with the cutoff parameter adjusted separately for each NN force to reproduce the experimental triton binding energy. Additional calculations combined AV18 with the Urbana IX 3NF and CD Bonn with the TM' 3NF (a chiral-symmetry-consistent modification of TM). The full breakup phase space was scanned at laboratory energies of 13, 65, 135, and 200 MeV, computing the five-fold differential cross section and seven analyzing powers on a grid of angles and energies.

## Key numbers

- Total nd breakup cross section at 13 MeV: 167.5 mb; at 65 MeV: 91.7 mb; at 135 MeV: 56.0 mb; at 200 MeV: 48.2 mb (CD Bonn potential predictions)
- Maximum 3NF effect on the differential cross section at 200 MeV: 133% (without constraints), 115% (with cross-section cut), 115% (with cross-section and energy cut)
- Maximum 3NF effect on the tensor analyzing power A_yy at 200 MeV: 702% (without constraints), 443% (with cross-section cut), 443% (with cross-section and energy cut)
- Maximum 3NF effect on the tensor analyzing power A_xz at 200 MeV: 677% (without constraints), 230% (with cross-section and energy cut)
- Maximum 3NF effect on the differential cross section at 135 MeV: 98% (all cut conditions)
- Maximum 3NF effect on the nucleon analyzing power A_y at 65 MeV: 182% (without constraints), 156% (with cross-section cut)
- 3NF enhancement of the total nd cross section: up to about 5%, nearly independent of the specific 3NF model used
- At about 200 MeV, elastic scattering contributes only about 20% to the total nd cross section
- The grid scan reproduced the total breakup cross section to within 5% (e.g., 167.5 mb calculated vs 159.2 mb scanned at 13 MeV)

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of 3NF effects in elastic Nd scattering (Kuros-Zolnierczuk et al., 2001) to the kinematically more complex breakup reaction, where the five-parameter final state allows more detailed probing of the 3N Hamiltonian. The study uses the same high-precision NN potentials and 3NF models (TM, TM', Urbana IX) previously applied to bound states and elastic scattering, where 3NFs were found necessary to reproduce triton and helium-4 binding energies (Nogga et al., 2000). The results provide guidance for ongoing and future kinematically complete breakup experiments, particularly at KVI Groningen (Bodek et al., 2001; Kistryn et al., 2001), and enable systematic comparison with data in a planned follow-up paper (Part II).
