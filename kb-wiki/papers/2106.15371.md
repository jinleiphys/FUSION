# Phenomenological study of quarkonium suppression and the impact of the energy gap between singlets and octets

> The study of heavy quarkonium suppression in heavy-ion collisions represents an important source of information about the properties of the quark-gluon plasma produced in such collisions. In a previous paper, we have considered how to model the evolution of a quarkonium in such a way that the solution of the resulting equations evolves toward the correct thermal equilibrium distribution for an homogeneous and static medium. We found that it is crucial to take into account the energy gap between singlet and octet configurations when the temperature is not much greater than this energy gap. In this manuscript, we explore in more detail the phenomenological consequences of this observation in the more realistic situation of an expanding system. We consider two different scenarios, based on the same approximation scheme, but on different choices of parameters. In the first case, we rely on a Hard Thermal Loop approximation, while the second case is based on a recent determination of the static potential in lattice QCD. In both cases, we compute the decay width and the nuclear modification factor, both taking the energy gap into account and ignoring it. We find that the impact on the predictions is as large in the expanding medium as it is in the static case. Our conclusion is that this energy gap should be taken into account in phenomenological studies.

## Key claim

The energy gap between color-singlet and color-octet quarkonium configurations significantly modifies the decay width and nuclear modification factor \(R_{AA}\) in an expanding quark-gluon plasma, and this effect is as large in the expanding medium as in the static case; the authors conclude that this energy gap should be included in phenomenological studies, but note that their purpose is only to estimate the effect, not to perform a detailed analysis of experimental data.

## Method

The authors solve a rate equation for the singlet probability \(p_s\) with a decay width \(\Gamma_s\) derived from an open quantum systems framework, using a multiple-scale analysis valid when the binding energy is much larger than the decay width. They compute \(\Gamma_s\) from Eq. (4) including the energy gap between singlet and octet states, and compare it to the static-limit width \(\Gamma_s^0\) from Eq. (5) that ignores the gap; the gluon correlator is estimated using the Hard Thermal Loop (HTL) approximation. Two parameter scenarios are considered: a "perturbative scenario" using a screened Yukawa potential with running coupling and Debye mass from leading-order perturbation theory, and a "lattice scenario" using a potential fitted to lattice QCD data from Lafferty (2019) with Debye masses extracted from that fit. The survival probability is evolved in an expanding medium with a Glauber-model initial temperature profile, assuming adiabatic interaction with the instantaneous temperature.

- In the perturbative scenario, the decay width \(\Gamma_s\) is typically a factor of 2 smaller than \(\Gamma_s^0\) at \(T = 250\ \text{MeV}\), as shown in Fig. 3.
- For \(\Upsilon(1S)\) in the perturbative scenario, the binding energy and decay width are computed for temperatures from 200 to 450 MeV in steps of 50 MeV; the condition \(E_s \gg \Gamma_s\) is fulfilled only for \(\Upsilon(1S)\), not for \(\Upsilon(2S)\).
- In the lattice scenario, the Debye masses used are: \(317 \pm 25\ \text{MeV}\) at \(T = 184.5\ \text{MeV}\), \(437.5 \pm 29.5\ \text{MeV}\) at \(T = 208\ \text{MeV}\), \(445 \pm 30\ \text{MeV}\) at \(T = 219\ \text{MeV}\), and \(534 \pm 33\ \text{MeV}\) at \(T = 257\ \text{MeV}\) (from Table I).
- For \(\Upsilon(1S)\) in the lattice scenario, the decay width is fitted by \(\Gamma_s \sim \tilde{a} T e^{-\tilde{b}/T}\) with parameters: S1: \(\tilde{a} = 22.9\), \(\tilde{b} = 2170\ \text{MeV}\); S2: \(\tilde{a} = 36.9\), \(\tilde{b} = 2370\ \text{MeV}\); S3: \(\tilde{a} = 15.3\), \(\tilde{b} = 1990\ \text{MeV}\) (from Table II).
- For \(\Upsilon(2S)\) in the lattice scenario, the decay width is fitted by \(\Gamma_s \sim a T + b T^2\) with parameters: S1: \(a = -0.285\), \(b = 0.00160\ \text{MeV}^{-1}\); S2: \(a = -0.258\), \(b = 0.00142\ \text{MeV}^{-1}\); S3: \(a = -0.309\), \(b = 0.00178\ \text{MeV}^{-1}\) (from Table II).
- The initial time for plasma interaction is taken as \(t_0 = 0.6\ \text{fm}\), following Alberico (2013).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous static-medium analysis (Blaizot and Escobedo, 2018) to an expanding medium, confirming that the energy-gap effect remains significant. It contrasts with earlier studies that ignored the energy dependence of the decay width, such as those using the GKSL equation in the \(T \gg E\) limit (Brambilla et al., 2016, 2017) or the static imaginary-potential approach (Laine, 2006; Beraudo, 2007). The results enable more realistic phenomenological modeling of bottomonium suppression at the LHC, particularly for \(\Upsilon(1S)\) and \(\Upsilon(2S)\), and highlight the need to incorporate the energy gap in future calculations of the nuclear modification factor \(R_{AA}\).
