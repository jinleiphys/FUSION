# Error Estimates of Theoretical Models: a Guide

> This guide offers suggestions/insights on uncertainty quantification of nuclear structure models. We discuss a simple approach to statistical error estimates, strategies to assess systematic errors, and show how to uncover inter-dependencies by correlation analysis. The basic concepts are illustrated through simple examples. By providing theoretical error bars on predicted quantities and using statistical methods to study correlations between observables, theory can significantly enhance the feedback between experiment and nuclear modeling.

## Key claim

This guide argues that uncertainty quantification in nuclear structure models should combine statistical error estimates (from regression analysis of parameter fits) with systematic error estimates (from model deficiencies), but acknowledges that systematic errors are inherently difficult to assess because the perfect reference model is usually unavailable.

## Method

The authors present a weighted least-squares framework using a chi-squared penalty function, where adopted errors combine experimental, numerical, and theoretical components. They describe how to construct the Hessian, Jacobian, and covariance matrices from the fit, then propagate statistical uncertainties to predicted observables via linearization. For systematic errors, they recommend three strategies: analysis of residual distributions, inter-model comparison across multiple well-calibrated models, and comparison with existing data via root-mean-square deviations.

## Key numbers

- The normalization condition requires that the average chi-squared per degree of freedom, χ²(p₀)/(N_d − N_p), should approach 1, where N_d is the number of data points and N_p is the number of model parameters.
- For an exact model with identical observables and identical adopted errors, the statistical uncertainty scales as ΔA ∼ ΔO^exp / √(N_d), improving as the square root of the number of measurements.
- In the odd-even staggering example with Model A (one parameter), the statistical error on the predicted observable is ΔO_stat = δ/√(N_d), while the systematic error is ΔO_syst = δ, where δ is the staggering magnitude and N_d is the number of data points.
- In the liquid drop model example, N_d = 516 even-even nuclei with 6 ≤ Z ≤ 106 were used as pseudo-data, fitted with a four-parameter (N_p = 4) liquid drop model.

## Context

This guide extends the tradition of uncertainty quantification from atomic physics, where an editorial in Physical Review A (PhysRevA) established expectations for theoretical error estimates. The work is intended to set the stage for a Focus Issue of Journal of Physics G on "Enhancing the interaction between nuclear experiment and theory through information and statistics." The authors reference prior applications of statistical analysis in nuclear modeling, including mass fits by Bertsch et al. (2005) and Toivanen et al. (2008), single-particle energy fits by Kortelainen et al. (2008), and pre-optimization of functionals by Stoi tsov et al. (2010). The guide enables practitioners to compute theoretical error bars on predicted quantities and use correlation analysis to guide future experiments.
