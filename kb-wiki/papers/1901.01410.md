# Origin of the Heaviest Elements: the Rapid Neutron-Capture Process

> The production of about half of the heavy elements found in nature is assigned to a specific astrophysical nucleosynthesis process: the rapid neutron capture process (r-process). Although this idea has been postulated more than six decades ago, the full understanding faces two types of uncertainties/open questions: (a) The nucleosynthesis path in the nuclear chart runs close to the neutron-drip line, where presently only limited experimental information is available, and one has to rely strongly on theoretical predictions for nuclear properties. (b) While for many years the occurrence of the r-process has been associated with supernovae, more recent studies have cast substantial doubts on this environment. Alternative scenarios include the mergers of neutron stars, neutron-star black hole mergers, but possibly also rare classes of supernovae as well as hypernovae/collapsars with polar jet ejecta and also accretion disk outflows related to the collapse of fast rotating massive stars with high magnetic fields. Stellar r-process abundance observations, have provided insights into, and constraints on the frequency of and conditions in the responsible stellar production sites. One of them, neutron star mergers, was just identified and related to the Gravitational Wave event GW170817. High resolution observations, increasingly more precise due to improved experimental atomic data, have been particularly important in defining the heavy element abundance patterns of the old halo stars, and thus determining the extent, and nature, of the earliest nucleosynthesis in our Galaxy. Combining new results and important breakthroughs in the related nuclear, atomic and astronomical fields of science, this review attempts to provide an answer to the question "How Were the Elements from Iron to Uranium Made?" # Origin of the Heaviest Elements: the Rapid Neutron-Capture Process

## Key claim
About half of the heavy elements in nature are produced by the rapid neutron-capture process (r-process), but the full understanding remains incomplete due to two major uncertainties: the nucleosynthesis path runs close to the neutron-drip line where experimental data are scarce, and the long-assumed association with supernovae has been cast into doubt, with neutron star mergers now identified as one confirmed site following the gravitational wave event GW170817.

## Method
This review synthesizes results from nuclear physics (experimental and theoretical studies of neutron-rich nuclei far from stability), atomic physics (laboratory measurements of transition probabilities and lifetimes for neutron-capture elements), stellar spectroscopy (high-resolution abundance observations of metal-poor halo stars), astrophysical modeling of candidate r-process sites (core-collapse supernovae, magneto-rotational supernovae, neutron star mergers, neutron star-black hole mergers, collapsars), and galactic chemical evolution models. The authors compare observed stellar abundance patterns to solar system r-process residuals and to nucleosynthesis calculations from different proposed sites.

## Key numbers
- About half of heavy elements found in nature are assigned to the r-process
- Neutron densities of 10^26 cm^-3 and higher are required for a strong r-process that produces the heaviest elements up to the actinides
- GW170817 had a combined total mass of about 2.74 solar masses (M_sun)
- GW190425 had a combined total mass of approximately 3.4 M_sun
- GW190426 and GW190814 had combined total masses in excess of 7 M_sun and 25 M_sun respectively
- The 2.6 M_sun binary member in GW190814 is debated as either a very massive neutron star or a very small black hole
- Stellar abundance classifications: "r-I" stars have [Eu/Fe] between +0.3 and +1.0 with [Ba/Eu] < 0; "r-II" stars have [Eu/Fe] > +1.0 with [Ba/Eu] < 0
- Early wavelength measurements of spectral lines achieved 1 part per million (ppm) accuracy; Fourier transform spectrometers achieved approximately 0.01 ppm (10 ppb) accuracy
- The 1 m Fourier transform spectrometer at the National Solar Observatory has wavenumber accuracy to 1 part in 10^8 and resolution as small as 0.01 cm^-1

## Context
This review extends the long line of r-process reviews dating back to Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler and Hoyle (1957) and Cameron (1957), and updates earlier comprehensive reviews by Cowan, Thielemann and Truran (1991), Arnould, Goriely and Takahashi (2007), Sneden, Cowan and Gallino (2008), and Thielemann et al. (2017). It incorporates the landmark gravitational wave detection GW170817, which provided the first direct identification of a neutron star merger as an r-process site via its associated kilonova electromagnetic transient. The work enables future studies to constrain the relative contributions of different r-process sites (neutron star mergers, rare supernova classes, collapsars) across galactic chemical evolution, and highlights the need for improved nuclear data for neutron-rich nuclei and continued high-resolution stellar abundance surveys.
