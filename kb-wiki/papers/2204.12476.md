# The Fission Barrier of Heaviest Nuclei From a Macroscopic-Microscopic Perspective

> The concept of fission barrier - a parameter which enters in quantitative estimates of various observables related to nuclear fission - is presented from the point of view of theory based on the picture of nuclear deformation and energy dependent on it. We describe the macroscopic-microscopic method of calculating energy landscapes which is simpler than the selfconsistent mean field approach, and, due to its two-component nature, seems to be easier to adjust to experimental data. We present some models and methods used to find the fission saddles. For the purpose of illustration, we present results on fission barriers in actinides and superheavy nuclei, obtained within one macroscopic-microscopic model. We discuss comparisons with results of other models, including some of the mean-field type.

## Key claim

The fission barrier is not a directly measurable quantum observable but a model-dependent parameter whose value can depend on the reaction type and excitation energy range, leading to inherent uncertainty in calculations. The authors emphasize that there is no sufficiently reliable theory to predict the importance of diabatic versus adiabatic effects at level crossings, which directly translates into uncertainty in the fission barrier height.

## Method

The authors present the macroscopic-microscopic (MM) method for calculating fission barriers, which divides the calculation into a macroscopic part (smooth variation of binding energy with Z and N, based on the liquid-drop model) and a microscopic part (oscillating shell corrections from individual nucleon contributions). The shell correction method follows Strutinsky (1969) and Brack et al. (1972), where the difference between the real binding energy and the liquid-drop value is expressed as a difference between sums of single-particle energies in a phenomenological potential for the quantum one-body density versus the density averaged over shells. The authors discuss the selection of collective variables for deformation space, methods for searching saddles in multidimensional spaces, and illustrate results for actinides and superheavy nuclei from one specific MM model.

## Key numbers

- Shell correction oscillations have amplitude less than or equal to 10 MeV
- Shells in atomic nuclei contain approximately A^(2/3) states, with energy distance between them of about 5-10 MeV (hbar*Omega)
- The Landau-Zener-Stueckelberg probability for diabatic transition at a level crossing is P = exp(-(2*pi/hbar)*V^2/(q_dot*|d/dq(epsilon_2-epsilon_1)|))
- In the overdamped limit (gamma >> omega_A(B)), the fission width becomes Gamma_fiss_overdamped ~ (omega_A(B)*omega_GS/gamma)*exp(-B_A(B)/T)
- The Hill-Wheeler barrier transmission coefficient is T^HL_A(B)(E) = 1/(1+exp[2*pi*(B^d_A(B)-E)/(hbar*omega_A(B))])

## Context

This chapter reviews the macroscopic-microscopic approach to fission barriers, extending the work of Strutinsky (1969) and Brack et al. (1972) on shell correction methods, and provides comparisons with selfconsistent mean-field approaches covered by N. Schunck's contribution to the same volume. The work enables improved estimates of fission rates for superheavy nuclei synthesis around Z=118, r-process nucleosynthesis calculations, and reactor physics applications, while noting that no fundamental theory exists for the regime where excitation energy is comparable to the barrier height. The authors reference the transition state formalism of Wigner (1938) and Bohr and Wheeler (1939), the Kramers (1940) dissipative dynamics approach, and the optical potential method for transmission through multi-humped barriers from Goriely et al. (2009).
