# A Novel Approach to Neutrino-Hydrogen Measurements

> The limited statistics of the available (anti)neutrino-hydrogen (H) interactions has been a longstanding impediment for high-energy neutrino physics. We discuss a practical way to achieve accurate (anti)neutrino-hydrogen measurements, addressing the principal limitations of earlier experiments. Interactions on hydrogen are extracted by subtracting measurements on thin dedicated graphite (pure C) and polypropylene (CH$_2$) targets within a highly segmented low-density detector. A kinematic selection is used to increase the purities to 80-95\% before subtraction. A statistics of ${\cal O}(10^6)$ can be realistically achieved in modern neutrino beams for the various $ν(\bar ν)$-H event topologies. The availability of such samples would allow a precise determination of neutrino and antineutrino fluxes, as well as to directly constrain nuclear effects from a comparison with corresponding measurements on heavy materials within the same detector. The (anti)neutrino fluxes and the nuclear smearing are typically the leading sources of systematic uncertainties in long-baseline oscillation experiments. (Anti)neutrino-hydrogen interactions also provide an ideal tool for a wide range of precision tests of fundamental interactions.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a practical, safe, and inexpensive method to obtain high-statistics (anti)neutrino-hydrogen charged-current interaction samples by subtracting measurements on thin graphite (pure carbon) targets from those on polypropylene (CH₂) targets within a straw tube tracker, achieving purities of 80-95% before subtraction and a statistics of O(10⁶) events in modern neutrino beams, a significant improvement over the approximately 16k ν-H and 9k ν̄-H events from historical bubble chamber experiments.

## Method

The authors simulate (anti)neutrino interactions on CH₂, H, and C targets using three event generators (NuWro as default, with GiBUU and GENIE for cross-checks) and apply GEANT4-based detector smearing consistent with NOMAD data. Interactions on hydrogen are extracted by subtracting measurements on dedicated graphite (pure C) targets from those on polypropylene (CH₂) targets within a highly segmented low-density straw tube tracker (STT) with average density ≤ 0.17 g/cm³, where targets constitute about 97% of the total mass. A kinematic selection using likelihood functions incorporating multi-dimensional correlations among transverse plane variables (including the asymmetry R_mH = (p_T^m - p_T^H)/(p_T^m + p_T^H), the hadron transverse momentum perpendicular to the lepton p_T⊥^H, and the angle θ_νT) is applied to increase the signal-to-background ratio before subtraction.

## Key numbers

- The average density of the STT detector is ≤ 0.17 g/cm³, comparable to liquid deuterium
- The detector can achieve a fiducial hydrogen mass of approximately 0.7 tons from 5 tons of CH₂
- For the cleanest topology ν_μ p → μ⁻ p π⁺, the kinematic selection achieves 96% efficiency with 95% purity
- For ν̄_μ p → μ⁺ n quasi-elastic events, the selection achieves 75% efficiency with 80% purity
- The combined neutron detection efficiency for H events is 80.5% (25% from STT interactions plus 55% from ECAL detection)
- The average momentum resolution for charged tracks is about 5%, with angular resolution about 2 mrad
- The proton reconstruction efficiency is about 93% for H events and 67% for C events
- The difference in acceptance between CH₂ and C targets for final state particles is within 10⁻³

## Context

This work extends the kinematic selection techniques pioneered by the NOMAD experiment (Altegoer 1997, Astier 2001, Naumov 2004) to a novel solid-target subtraction method, addressing the principal limitations of earlier bubble chamber experiments (ANL 12-foot, BNL 7-foot, FNAL E31 and E45, CERN WA21 and WA24) which collected only about 16k ν-H and 9k ν̄-H charged-current interactions total. The approach enables precise determination of neutrino and antineutrino fluxes and direct constraints on nuclear effects, the leading systematic uncertainties in long-baseline oscillation experiments, by comparing hydrogen measurements with corresponding measurements on heavy materials within the same detector.
