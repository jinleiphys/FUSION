# Coleman-Weinberg mechanism for spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking in the massless chiral sigma model

> We study the effect of one-loop corrections from nucleon together with those from boson in the massless chiral sigma model, where we perform the Coleman-Weinberg renormalization procedure. This renormalization procedure has a mechanism of spontaneous symmetry breaking due to radiative corrections in $ϕ^4$ theory. We apply it to the system of nucleon and bosons with chiral symmetry where the negative-mass term of bosons does not exist. Spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking is derived from the contribution of nucleon and boson loops which generates the masses of nucleon, scalar meson, and vector meson dynamically. We find that the renormalization scale plays an important role for the breaking of the symmetry between fermion (nucleon) and boson, and eventually for the chiral symmetry at the same time. In addition, we find that the naturalness restores by means of the introduction of the vacuum fluctuation from both nucleon and boson. Finally we obtain a stable effective potential with the effect of Dirac sea in the chiral model for the first time.

# Coleman-Weinberg mechanism for spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking in the massless chiral sigma model

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the Coleman-Weinberg renormalization procedure, applied to the massless chiral sigma model with both nucleon and boson one-loop corrections, can generate spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking dynamically without a negative-mass term for bosons, provided the renormalization scale is finite. The mechanism relies on the near-cancellation of large nucleon and boson loop contributions, which yields a stable effective potential for the first time in a chirally symmetric model with Dirac sea effects.

## Method

The authors start from the chirally symmetric linear sigma model Lagrangian with nucleon, sigma, pion, and omega meson fields, taking the limit where the boson mass term vanishes. They apply the Coleman-Weinberg renormalization scheme to both the boson loop and the nucleon loop separately before spontaneous symmetry breaking, using a renormalization scale m to avoid logarithmic singularities at the origin. The renormalized one-loop effective potentials for bosons and nucleons are computed and combined, and the condition for a local minimum at the pion decay constant f_pi determines the coupling constant lambda. The parameters are then fixed using the physical masses of the nucleon (939 MeV), omega meson (783 MeV), pion (139 MeV), and f_pi (93 MeV).

## Key numbers

- Nucleon mass M = 939 MeV, omega meson mass m_omega = 783 MeV, pion mass m_pi = 139 MeV, pion decay constant f_pi = 93 MeV (input experimental values)
- Resulting coupling constants: g_sigma = 10.09, g_tilde_omega = 8.419, lambda = 77.07, ratio gamma = 1.038, epsilon = 1.79 x 10^6 MeV^3
- Resulting sigma meson mass m_sigma = 641 MeV
- In the limit m -> infinity, lambda approaches 75.315 and m_sigma approaches 0, indicating restoration of chiral symmetry
- The ratio gamma = |V_B^R / V_F^R| = 3(4 lambda^2 + g_tilde_omega^4) / (8 g_sigma^4) approaches 1 as m -> infinity, meaning perfect cancellation of loop corrections
- Naive dimensional analysis coefficients: kappa_2 = -1.76 (natural, order unity) and kappa_5 = 2.35 (natural, order unity) for the massless chiral sigma model, compared to kappa_5 = -61.97 for the Walecka model (unnatural)

## Context

This work extends the Coleman-Weinberg mechanism (Coleman and Weinberg, 1973) from pure phi^4 theory to a chirally symmetric system of fermions and bosons, addressing the long-standing problem of unstable effective potentials in chirally symmetric renormalization (Boguta, 1983; Matsui, 1981; Lee, 1974). Previous attempts to include Dirac sea effects in the chiral sigma model required large sigma meson masses (about 1125 MeV) and still produced unstable potentials (Jackson, 1982; Lee, 1974). The present model achieves a stable effective potential with natural coefficients in the naive dimensional analysis (Georgi, 1993; Furnstahl, 1997) for the first time, enabling future applications to nuclear matter at finite density and hadronic systems at finite temperature.
