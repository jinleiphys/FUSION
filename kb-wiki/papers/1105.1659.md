# Superheavies: Theoretical incitements and predictions

> It is well known that in fusion reactions one may get only neutron deficient superheavy nuclei located far from the island of stability. The multi-nucleon transfer reactions allow one to produce more neutron enriched new heavy nuclei but the corresponding cross sections are rather low. Neutron capture process is considered here as alternative method for production of long-lived neutron rich superheavy nuclei. Strong neutron fluxes might be provided by nuclear reactors and nuclear explosions in laboratory frame and by supernova explosions in nature. All these cases are discussed in the paper.

# Superheavies: Theoretical incitements and predictions

## Key claim

Neutron capture in multiple "soft" nuclear explosions or in next-generation pulsed reactors could produce macroscopic amounts of long-lived neutron-rich superheavy nuclei near the island of stability, but only if the neutron fluence per pulse is increased by about three orders of magnitude over existing pulsed reactors; the theoretical uncertainty in fusion cross-section predictions for elements 119 and 120 is at least a factor of 3.

## Method

The authors solve a set of coupled differential equations for neutron capture, beta-minus decay, alpha decay, and spontaneous fission along isotopic chains, using theoretical half-lives from the Viola-Seaborg formula for alpha decay, an empirical formula for beta decay, and the Swiatecki systematics with Möller et al. (1995) shell corrections for fission half-lives. They simulate multiple short (1 microsecond) neutron exposures separated by 10-second intervals to model nuclear explosions, and longer millisecond pulses for reactor scenarios, comparing yields against data from the 1952 "Mike" thermonuclear test.

## Key numbers

- Cross sections for element 120 in fusion reactions: approximately 0.04 pb for $^{50}$Ti+$^{249}$Cf and 0.02 pb for $^{54}$Cr+$^{248}$Cm, as estimated by Zagrebaev et al. (2008).
- Neutron fluence in nuclear explosions reaches $10^{25}$ cm$^{-2}$, giving a dimensionless capture factor $x = n\sigma_{n\gamma} \sim 1$ for a capture cross section of about 1 barn.
- In the "Mike" test, a neutron exposure of $1.3 \times 10^{24}$ neutrons/cm$^2$ over 1 microsecond was used to reproduce the observed yields of transuranium nuclei.
- Existing pulsed reactors deliver neutron fluence per pulse up to $10^{16}$ n/cm$^2$, yielding $x \sim 10^{-8}$ and a neutron capture time of about $10^5$ seconds.
- To bypass the Fermium gap and the region near Z=106-108, the required neutron fluence per pulse is $10^{20}$ neutrons/cm$^2$, an increase of about three orders of magnitude over existing pulsed reactors.
- The theoretical uncertainty in fusion cross-section predictions for superheavy elements is at least a factor of 3, arising from fission barrier uncertainties of about 1 MeV changing survival probabilities by a factor of 10.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of fusion reactions for superheavy element synthesis, which using $^{48}$Ca projectiles on actinide targets produced elements up to Z=118 (Oganessian, 2006; Oganessian, 2010) but only neutron-deficient isotopes far from the predicted island of stability at N=184. The authors build on earlier studies of multi-nucleon transfer reactions (Zagrebaev et al., 2006; Zagrebaev et al., 2008) and neutron capture in single nuclear explosions (Seaborg; Dorn, 1962), and propose for the first time that multiple sequential neutron exposures could overcome the "Fermium gap" of short-lived fissioning isotopes at Z=100. The results enable experimental searches for neutron-rich superheavy nuclei using either multiple nuclear explosions or high-intensity pulsed reactors, and inform astrophysical r-process nucleosynthesis studies (Panov et al., 2009).
