# Microscopically constrained mean field models from chiral nuclear thermodynamics

> We explore the use of mean field models to approximate microscopic nuclear equations of state derived from chiral effective field theory across the densities and temperatures relevant for simu- lating astrophysical phenomena such as core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers. We consider both relativistic mean field theory with scalar and vector meson exchange as well as energy density functionals based on Skyrme phenomenology and compare to thermodynamic equa- tions of state derived from chiral two- and three-nucleon forces in many-body perturbation theory. Quantum Monte Carlo simulations of symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter are used to determine the density regimes in which perturbation theory with chiral nuclear forces is valid. Within the theoretical uncertainties associated with the many-body methods, we find that select mean field models describe well microscopic nuclear thermodynamics. As an additional consistency requirement, we study as well the single-particle properties of nucleons in a hot/dense environment, which affect e.g., charged-current weak reactions in neutron-rich matter. The identified mean field models can be used across a larger range of densities and temperatures in astrophysical simulations than more computationally expensive microscopic models.

## Key claim

Select mean field models (both Skyrme-type and relativistic mean field) can accurately reproduce the thermodynamic equations of state derived from chiral effective field theory across densities and temperatures relevant for astrophysical simulations, but only when they are constrained by low-density microscopic calculations and satisfy experimental nuclear matter constraints; the agreement holds within the theoretical uncertainties of the many-body methods.

## Method

The authors compared equations of state from Skyrme energy density functionals and relativistic mean field (RMF) models to microscopic calculations using chiral two- and three-nucleon forces at next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order (N3LO) with a 414 MeV cutoff, computed via many-body perturbation theory (MBPT) at second and third order. They used configuration interaction Monte Carlo (CIMC) simulations of symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter at zero temperature to assess the convergence of MBPT and determine density regimes where perturbation theory is valid. Mean field models were then selected based on agreement with the chiral EOS at low densities and with the 11 infinite-matter constraints from Dutra et al. (2012).

## Key numbers

- For pure neutron matter with N3LO 414 and 450 MeV cutoff interactions, second-order MBPT deviates from CIMC by approximately 2-3%, while NNLO_opt and N3LO 500 MeV interactions show deviations of 6-8%.
- In symmetric nuclear matter, second- and third-order MBPT results are on average 7-10% more bound than CIMC results in the intermediate-density regime.
- The saturation density of nuclear matter is taken as rho_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}.
- Three-body forces become quantitatively important for densities above approximately 0.06 fm^{-3} in pure neutron matter.
- At low densities below 0.04 fm^{-3} in symmetric nuclear matter, second-order MBPT errors exceed approximately 20%.
- Among 240 Skyrme parametrizations benchmarked by Dutra et al. (2012), only 16 satisfied all 11 infinite-matter constraints; among 263 RMF parametrizations, only Z271v5 and Z271v6 met the criteria.
- The Z271v6 RMF model was the only relativistic parametrization that agreed with the microscopic low-density neutron matter constraints.

## Context

This work extends the program of constraining phenomenological nuclear equations of state with chiral effective field theory, following earlier studies by Brown and Schwenk (2013) who refitted Skyrme models to neutron matter calculations, and Dutra et al. (2012) who benchmarked mean field models against experimental constraints. The analysis enables the use of computationally inexpensive mean field models in astrophysical simulations of core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers, where the full microscopic chiral EOS would be too expensive. The identified models can be applied across a larger range of densities and temperatures than the microscopic calculations, while remaining consistent with the low-energy theory.
