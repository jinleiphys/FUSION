# Disappearance of Mott oscillations in sub-barrier elastic scattering of identical heavy ions and the nuclear interaction

> We investigate the possible disappearance of Mott oscillations in the scattering of bosonic nuclei at sub-barrier energies. This effect is universal and happens at a critical value of the Sommerfeld parameter. It is also found that the inclusion of the short-range nuclear interaction has a profound influence on this phenomenon. Thus we suggest that the study of this lack of Mott oscillation, which we call, ``transverse isotropy" is a potentially useful mean to study the nuclear interaction.

## Key claim

The disappearance of Mott oscillations (transverse isotropy) in sub-barrier elastic scattering of identical bosonic nuclei is universal and occurs at a critical Sommerfeld parameter \(\eta_0 = \sqrt{2}\), but the inclusion of the short-range nuclear interaction shifts this transition and can create a second transition energy, making the phenomenon sensitive to the nuclear potential rather than purely Coulombic.

## Method

The authors analytically derived the second derivative of the normalized Mott cross section at \(\theta = 90^\circ\) as a function of the Sommerfeld parameter \(\eta\) for pure Coulomb scattering, finding the critical value \(\eta_0 = \sqrt{2}\). They then numerically computed the same second derivative for the \(^4\)He + \(^4\)He system using two different nuclear potentials: the Ak\"yuz-Winther Woods-Saxon potential and the BFWC double-Gaussian potential, both combined with the Coulomb interaction. The energy dependence of the second derivative was used to identify energies where the cross section becomes flat (transverse isotropy) and to compare with experimental data from Abdullah et al. (2006).

## Key numbers

- Critical Sommerfeld parameter for pure Coulomb Mott scattering: \(\eta_0 = \sqrt{2}\) (dimensionless).
- For \(^4\)He + \(^4\)He, the pure Coulomb transition energy is \(E_{\text{c.m.}} = 0.397\) MeV.
- Using the Ak\"yuz-Winther potential, the Coulomb barrier for \(^4\)He + \(^4\)He is \(V_{\text{B}} = 0.87\) MeV; the nuclear interaction introduces a second transition energy \(E_0' = 1.10\) MeV.
- Using the BFWC potential, the two transition energies are \(E_0 = 0.47\) MeV and \(E_0' = 2.41\) MeV.
- The BFWC potential parameters: attractive Gaussian \(V_{\text{A}} = 122.62\) MeV, \(R_{\text{A}} = 2.132\) fm; repulsive Gaussian \(V_{\text{R}} = 3.0\) MeV, \(R_{\text{R}} = 2.0\) fm; Coulomb radius \(R_{\text{C}} = 5.8\) fm.
- Only two systems satisfy the condition that the transition energy lies below the Coulomb barrier: \(^2\)H + \(^2\)H and \(^4\)He + \(^4\)He; \(^6\)Li + \(^6\)Li nearly satisfies it.

## Context

This work extends earlier suggestions by Freedman et al. (1977) and Pittel and Gmitro (1981) that heavy-ion systems like \(^{12}\)C + \(^{12}\)C and \(^{16}\)O + \(^{16}\)O might show deviations from pure Mott scattering due to the Fermi nature of nucleons, and by Bromley (1977) who reported experimental hints of such deviations. It builds on the concept of "transverse isotropy" introduced by Canto et al. (2001) and is directly motivated by the experimental \(\alpha + \alpha\) data of Abdullah et al. (2006), which showed flat cross sections at energies higher than the pure Coulomb prediction. The results suggest that measuring the transverse isotropy transition at \(E_{\text{c.m.}} = 0.47\) MeV in \(^4\)He + \(^4\)He could precisely determine the long-range part of the nuclear potential, complementing information obtained from high-energy nuclear rainbow scattering as studied by Khoa et al. (2007) and Brandan and Satchler (1997).
