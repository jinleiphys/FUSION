# Characteristics of Chiral Anomaly in View of Various Applications

> In view of the recent applications of chiral anomaly to various fields beyond particle physics, we discuss some basic aspects of chiral anomaly which may help deepen our understanding of chiral anomaly in particle physics also. It is first shown that Berry's phase (and its generalization) for the Weyl model $H =v_{F} \vecσ\cdot \vec{p}(t)$ assumes a monopole form at the exact adiabatic limit but deviates from it off the adiabatic limit and vanishes in the high frequency limit of the Fourier transform of $\vec{p}(t)$ for bounded $|\vec{p}(t)|$. An effective action, which is consistent with the non-adiabatic limit of Berry's phase, combined with the Bjorken-Johnson-Low prescription gives normal equal-time space-time commutators and no chiral anomaly. In contrast, an effective action with a monopole at the origin of the momentum space, which describes Berry's phase in the precise adiabatic limit but fails off the adiabatic limit, gives anomalous space-time commutators and a covariant anomaly to the gauge current. We regard this anomaly as an artifact of the postulated monopole and not a consequence of Berry's phase. As for the recent application of the chiral anomaly to the description of effective Weyl fermions in condensed matter and nuclear physics, which is closely related to the formulation of lattice chiral fermions, we point out that the chiral anomaly for each species doubler separately vanishes for a finite lattice spacing, contrary to the common assumption. Instead a general form of pair creation associated with the spectral flow for the Dirac sea with finite depth takes place. This view is supported by the Ginsparg-Wilson fermion, which defines a single Weyl fermion without doublers on the lattice and gives a well-defined index (anomaly) even for a finite lattice spacing.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the chiral anomaly derived from Berry's phase in effective models of Weyl fermions is an artifact of assuming a point-like monopole in momentum space, which is only valid in the precise adiabatic limit; off this limit, Berry's phase vanishes and no anomalous equal-time commutators or chiral anomaly arise from the mere presence of a Weyl fermion.

## Method

The author exactly solves Berry's original spin-1/2 model in a rotating magnetic field with a specific time dependence, obtaining the exact non-adiabatic geometric phase (holonomy) as a function of the adiabaticity parameter η = 2ħμB/ħω. Using the Bjorken-Johnson-Low (BJL) prescription, the author evaluates equal-time commutation relations from effective actions that either include or exclude a monopole term in momentum space, comparing the results to the exact solution's behavior in the high-frequency (non-adiabatic) limit. The analysis is extended to the lattice formulation of chiral fermions, examining the vanishing of chiral anomaly for each species doubler separately at finite lattice spacing.

## Key numbers

- Berry's phase ratio Ω₊/Ω_mono = (1/2)η² for the upper half-sphere (θ = π/2) in the non-adiabatic limit η → 0, where η = 2ħμB/ħω = μBT/π
- Berry's phase vanishes as Ω₊/Ω_mono ∼ 1/ω² for ω → ∞ with fixed B
- The adiabatic limit corresponds to η → ∞ (T → ∞ or |B| → ∞), giving the monopole value Ω_mono = 2π(1 − cosθ)
- The non-adiabatic limit η → 0 gives Ω₊ → 0 or 4π (trivial constants)

## Context

This work extends the author's prior analyses of chiral anomaly and Berry's phase (Fujikawa, 2005; Fujikawa and Suzuki, 2004) and directly critiques the kinematic derivation of chiral anomaly from Berry's phase proposed by Yamamoto (2015) and earlier work by Niu (1999) and Duval (2006). The paper also connects to the Ginsparg-Wilson fermion formulation (Ginsparg and Wilson, 1982) as a lattice approach that defines a single Weyl fermion without doublers and gives a well-defined index even at finite lattice spacing. The analysis enables a clearer distinction between genuine quantum anomalies and artifacts of adiabatic approximations in condensed matter and nuclear physics applications.
