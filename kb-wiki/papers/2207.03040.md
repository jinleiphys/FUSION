# Investigating the Compton amplitude subtraction function in lattice QCD

> Theoretical predictions of the proton--neutron mass difference and measurements of the proton's charge radius require inputs from the Compton amplitude subtraction function. Model-dependent and non-relativistic calculations of this subtraction function vary significantly, and hence it contributes sizeable uncertainties to the aforementioned physical quantities. We report on the use of Feynman-Hellmann methods in lattice QCD to calculate the subtraction function from first principles. In particular, our initial results show anomalous high-energy behaviour that is at odds with the prediction from the operator product expansion (OPE). Therefore, we investigate the possibility that this unexpected behaviour is due to lattice artifacts, by varying the lattice spacing and volume, and comparing different discretisations of the vector current. Finally, we explore a Feynman-Hellmann implementation that is less sensitive to short-distance contributions and show that the subtraction function's anomalous behaviour can be attributed to these short-distance contributions. As such, this work represents the first steps in achieving a complete understanding of the Compton amplitude subtraction function.

## Key claim

The authors report that their initial lattice QCD calculation of the Compton amplitude subtraction function shows anomalous high-energy behaviour that deviates from the operator product expansion (OPE) prediction, and they attribute this unexpected behaviour to short-distance contributions (lattice artifacts) rather than to a physical OPE-breaking fixed pole.

## Method

The authors used Feynman-Hellmann methods in lattice QCD to calculate the Compton amplitude subtraction function from first principles, implementing both a local vector current and a conserved vector current discretisation. They varied the lattice spacing (0.074 fm, 0.068 fm, 0.058 fm) and volume (2.4 fm, 2.8 fm, 3.3 fm) across three gauge ensembles at the SU(3) flavour symmetric point, and also explored a temporal interlacing technique that introduces a minimum temporal separation between current insertions to suppress short-distance contributions.

## Key numbers

- Lattice spacings used: 0.074 fm (32^3 x 64, L=2.4 fm), 0.068 fm (48^3 x 96, L=3.3 fm), and 0.058 fm (48^3 x 96, L=2.8 fm)
- Pion masses on the three ensembles: 0.47 GeV, 0.41 GeV, and 0.43 GeV
- Local current renormalisation factors (Z_V): 0.86, 0.87, and 0.88 for the three ensembles respectively
- Statistics for the local current calculation ranged from N_meas ~ O(100) to O(1000), with some higher statistics points at N_meas ~ O(10000) for the smallest volume
- For the conserved current implementation on the 32^3 x 64 ensemble, N_meas = 1000

## Context

This work extends the Feynman-Hellmann method previously used to calculate the forward Compton amplitude in lattice QCD (Chambers 2017, Utku Can 2020) to the specific case of the subtraction function. The subtraction function is required as input for the Cottingham sum rule (Cottingham 1963) to predict the proton-neutron mass difference, and for two-photon exchange corrections to the muonic-hydrogen Lamb shift (Tomalak 2015, Afanasev 2017), which is relevant to the proton radius puzzle (Pohl 2010, Bernauer 2010). The authors' investigation of lattice artifacts lays the foundation for future first-principles calculations that could resolve discrepancies among model-dependent calculations (Alarcon 2013, Caprini 2016, Carlson 2011, Miller 2012, Walker-Loud 2012) and clarify whether a J=0 fixed pole (Brodsky 1973, Creutz 1968) exists in the Compton amplitude.
