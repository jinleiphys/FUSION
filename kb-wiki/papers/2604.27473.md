# Quarkonium $p_{\rm T}$ spectra in heavy--ion collisions at LHC energies within a hydrodynamic core--corona framework

> We present a systematic study of the transverse momentum ($p_{\rm T}$) spectra of charmonium (J/$ψ$, $ψ(2S)$) and bottomonium ($Υ(nS)$) states in Pb-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 5.02$ TeV within an analytical relativistic hydrodynamics framework. The medium evolution is described assuming cylindrical symmetry with boost-invariant longitudinal expansion and Hubble-like transverse flow. Quarkonium spectra are evaluated using the Cooper-Frye formalism on a constant-temperature freeze-out hypersurface, supplemented by a core--corona approach to include both thermal and non-thermal contributions. The model describes the measurements from ALICE and CMS over a broad $p_{\rm T}$ range. For charmonium, both the spectra and the $ψ(2S)$/J/$ψ$ ratio are well reproduced, while deviations at high $p_{\rm T}$ for J/$ψ$ indicate additional hard production mechanisms. In the bottomonium sector, the $Υ(nS)$ spectra and their yield ratios are successfully described, consistent with the expected sequential suppression pattern. These results demonstrate that an analytical hydrodynamic approach combined with a core-corona framework provides a unified and transparent description of quarkonium production in heavy--ion collisions at LHC energies.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that an analytical relativistic hydrodynamics solution with cylindrical symmetry, boost-invariant longitudinal expansion, and Hubble-like transverse flow, combined with a core-corona framework, can simultaneously describe charmonium and bottomonium transverse momentum spectra in Pb-Pb collisions at LHC energies, but the model underestimates J/ψ yields at high transverse momentum (p_T > 8 GeV), indicating additional hard production mechanisms not captured by the framework.

## Method

The medium evolution is described using an exact analytical solution of relativistic hydrodynamics assuming cylindrical symmetry, boost-invariant longitudinal expansion, and Hubble-like transverse flow. Quarkonium spectra are evaluated via the Cooper-Frye formalism on a constant-temperature freeze-out hypersurface, with the core component representing thermalized medium and the corona component (parametrized by fitting pp collision data to a power-law function) accounting for non-thermal hard-scattering contributions. The boundary between core and corona is determined from nuclear charge density distribution using a Glauber Monte Carlo approach, and model parameters are extracted via χ² minimization fits to ALICE and CMS data.

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out temperature for charmonium (J/ψ, ψ(2S)): T ≈ 160 MeV with average transverse flow velocity ⟨v_T⟩ ≈ 0.60, from simultaneous fit to ALICE data at forward rapidity (2.5 < y < 4) in 0-90% centrality Pb-Pb collisions at √s_NN = 5.02 TeV, with χ²/ndf = 5.9
- Freeze-out temperature for bottomonium (Υ(1S), Υ(2S), Υ(3S)): T ≈ 224 MeV with average transverse flow velocity ⟨v_T⟩ ≈ 0.56, from fit to CMS data at midrapidity (|y| < 2.4) in 0-100% centrality Pb-Pb collisions at √s_NN = 5.02 TeV, with χ²/ndf = 4.6
- The model describes J/ψ data for p_T < 8 GeV but underestimates yields at p_T > 8 GeV in both midrapidity (0-10% centrality) and forward rapidity (0-90% centrality) Pb-Pb collisions

## Context

This work extends the analytical hydrodynamic framework developed in Hydro_core (2023) to quarkonium production, providing a unified description that contrasts with transport models incorporating dissociation and regeneration dynamically. The extracted freeze-out temperatures are consistent with earlier studies by Gupta (2014) and Bhaduri_T (2022), where bottomonium freeze-out at T ~ 230 MeV was found, significantly higher than the light-hadron chemical freeze-out temperature of ~156 MeV. The results enable systematic comparison between charmonium and bottomonium thermalization properties, showing that bottomonium decouples earlier from the medium while charmonium remains sensitive to later stages with stronger collective flow.
