# Low-energy 17O(n,g)18O reaction within the microscopic potential model and its role for the weak r-process

> The neutron radiative capture reaction $^{17}$O(n,$γ$)18O plays a pivotal role in both nuclear structure studies and astrophysical nucleosynthesis, particularly in the formation of elements during hydrostatic and explosive stellar environments. We calculated the $^{17}$O(n,$γ$)$^{18}$O cross section within the Skyrme Hartree-Fock potential model and analyzed electric dipole E1 transitions to both positive and negative-parity states below the alpha-decay threshold in $^{18}$O. Our cross sections are significantly different from the data available in commonly used libraries. We further investigate the impact of the new calculated cross section on weak r-process nucleosynthesis using large-scale reaction network calculations across a wide range of electron fractions and entropies. Our results show that the $^{17}$O(n, $γ$)$^{18}$O reaction rate significantly influences the production of first r-process peak elements, such as strontium, under specific astrophysical conditions. This study highlights the importance of accurate nuclear dat$ for light isotopes in modeling heavy-element synthesis and provides updated reaction rates for future nucleosynthesis simulations.

## Key claim

The authors calculate the $^{17}$O(n,$\gamma$)$^{18}$O cross section using a microscopic Skyrme Hartree-Fock potential model and find that their results are significantly different from data in commonly used libraries; however, the impact on weak r-process nucleosynthesis is limited to specific astrophysical conditions (moderately neutron-rich regimes with electron fractions $Y_e \approx 0.39$ and entropies around $S=34\,k_B$/baryon), where the reaction rate influences the production of first r-process peak elements such as strontium.

## Method

The authors employed a Skyrme Hartree-Fock potential model (using SLy4 and SkP interactions) to compute the $^{17}$O(n,$\gamma$)$^{18}$O cross section, treating the reaction as electric dipole (E1) transitions from scattering states to bound states in $^{18}$O below the alpha-decay threshold (6.23 MeV). They adjusted two scaling parameters ($\lambda_s$ for scattering potentials and $\lambda_b$ for bound-state potentials) to reproduce single-particle resonance locations and separation energies, and incorporated spectroscopic factors from the $^{17}$O(d,p)$^{18}$O reaction (Li 1976). The resulting cross sections were folded with Maxwell-Boltzmann distributions to obtain astrophysical reaction rates, which were then used in large-scale reaction network calculations (XNet code) across a grid of electron fractions ($0.2 \leq Y_e \leq 0.5$) and entropies ($10 \leq S_0 \leq 100\,k_B$/baryon) with a constant expansion timescale of 10 ms.

## Key numbers

- Thermal neutron capture cross section from literature: $\sigma_{\text{th}} = 0.67 \pm 0.07$ mb (Firestone 2016)
- Neutron separation energy in $^{18}$O: $S_n = 8.045$ MeV
- Alpha-decay threshold in $^{18}$O: $S_\alpha = 6.23$ MeV
- Spectroscopic factor for the $0^+_1$ ground state: $S_F = 1.22$ for the $1d_{5/2}$ configuration (Li 1976)
- Spectroscopic factor for the $1^-_1$ state at 4.46 MeV: $S_F = 0.03$ for the $1p_{3/2}$ configuration (Li 1976)
- A $d_{3/2}$ resonance is located at 240 keV above threshold in the SLy4 interaction
- Sub-threshold resonance adjusted to $-0.068$ MeV below threshold for both SLy4 ($\lambda_s = 0.76$) and SkP ($\lambda_s = 0.70$) interactions
- Sr mass fraction at $Y_e=0.39$, $S=34\,k_B$/baryon differs by more than a factor of 10 between ENDF/B-VIII.1 and REACLIB reaction rates

## Context

This work extends the direct capture calculations of Zhang (2022) for the $^{17}$O(n,$\gamma$)$^{18}$O reaction by using a microscopic Skyrme Hartree-Fock potential instead of phenomenological Woods-Saxon potentials, reducing parameter uncertainties. The authors investigate weak r-process conditions (moderately neutron-rich ejecta) that were not considered by Zhang (2022), who focused on s-process and collapsar/supernova neutron burst scenarios. The results enable more accurate nucleosynthesis simulations for the first r-process peak ($A \approx 80-90$), particularly for disentangling contributions from neutron star mergers (Sprouse 2024, Just 2023) and core-collapse supernovae (Wang 2024, Glas 2018) to observed Sr abundances in metal-poor stars (Montes 2007) and the kilonova associated with GW170817 (Watson 2019).
