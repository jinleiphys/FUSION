# Quantum thermalization of Quark-Gluon Plasma

> The thermalization of quark gluon plasma created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions is a crucial theoretical question in understanding the onset of hydrodynamics, and in a broad sense, a key step to the exploration of thermalization in isolated quantum systems. Addressing this problem theoretically, in a first principle manner, requires a real-time, non-perturbative method. To this end, we carry out a fully quantum simulation on a classical hardware, of a massive Schwinger model, which well mimics QCD as it shares the important properties such as confinement and chiral symmetry breaking. We focus on the real-time evolution of the Wigner function, namely, the two-point correlation function, which approximates quark momentum distribution. In the context of the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis and the evolution of entropy, our solution reveals the emergence of quantum thermalization in quark-gluon plasma with a strong coupling constant, while thermalization fails progressively as a consequence of the gradually increased significance of quantum many-body scar states in a more weakly coupled system. More importantly, we observe the non-trivial role of the topological vacuum in thermalization, as the thermalization properties differ dramatically in the parity-even and parity-odd components of the Wigner function.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that quantum thermalization of a quark-gluon plasma analog emerges in a strongly coupled massive Schwinger model, while thermalization fails progressively in more weakly coupled systems due to the increased significance of quantum many-body scar states; crucially, the thermalization properties differ dramatically between the parity-even and parity-odd components of the Wigner function, revealing a non-trivial role of the topological vacuum.

## Method

The authors performed a fully quantum simulation on classical hardware of the massive Schwinger model in 1+1 dimensions, discretized on a lattice with N=20 grid points and spacing a=1/(2g), using staggered fermions mapped to Pauli matrices via the Jordan-Wigner transformation. They evolved pure quantum states in real time according to the lattice Hamiltonian and computed the expectation values of the Wigner function (the two-point correlation function) for its scalar, pseudoscalar, vector, and axial-vector components at discrete momenta. Thermalization was assessed by comparing long-time averages of these Wigner functions to microcanonical and canonical ensemble averages, using a relative deviation measure normalized by thermal widths.

## Key numbers

- Lattice size: N=20 grid points with spacing a=1/(2g), giving system size Na=10/g ≈ 18 M_meson^{-1} (where M_meson=g/√π is the massless Schwinger meson mass).
- Electric field truncation: Λ=2, yielding Hilbert space dimension (2Λ+1)2^N = 5×2^20 ≈ 5.2×10^6 (reduced by factor ~5 in neutral sector).
- Fermion mass values explored: m=0 (strong coupling case) and m=2g (weak coupling case, with g/m=1/2), with m ranging between 0 and Λ_UV=2g.
- Thermalization criterion: relative deviation Δ_i^2(p,t) < 1 indicates thermalization; for the strong coupling case (m=0) all four Wigner components satisfy this, while for weak coupling (m=2g) the scalar and axial-vector components show Δ_s^2 > 1 and do not thermalize.
- Relaxation time scale: thermalization occurs after a time scale within t ∈ [0, 50]/g for the strong coupling case.

## Context

This work extends the study of eigenstate thermalization hypothesis (ETH) to a gauge theory that mimics QCD, building on prior quantum simulations of the massive Schwinger model on classical devices (Zache 2018, Kharzeev 2020, Florio 2023) and quantum devices (Klco 2018, Farrell 2023). It connects to findings of ETH in SU(2) gauge theory for plaquette and electric energy operators (Yao 2023, Ebner 2023) and the identification of scar states (Hayata 2023). The results enable future investigation of how topological vacuum sectors affect thermalization in QCD-like systems and provide a quantum foundation for the onset of hydrodynamics in heavy-ion collisions.
