# The Azimuthal Asymmetry at large p_t seem to be too large for a ``Jet Quenching''

> We discuss simple generic model of ``jet quenching'' in which matter absorption is defined by one parameter. We show that as absorption grows, the azimuthal asymmetry v_2 grows as well, reaching the finite limit with a simple geometric interpretation. It turns out, that this limit is still below the experimental values for 6 > p_t > 2 GeV, according to preliminary data from STAR experiment at RHIC. We thus conclude that ``jet quenching'' models alone cannot account for the observed phenomenon, and speculate about alternative scenarios.

# The Azimuthal Asymmetry at large p_t seem to be too large for a pure "Jet Quenching"

## Key claim

Even in the limit of arbitrarily strong absorption (jet quenching), the azimuthal asymmetry parameter v₂ reaches a finite geometric limit v₂* that is systematically below the preliminary STAR data for transverse momenta 6 > p_t > 2 GeV, implying that jet quenching models alone cannot account for the observed asymmetry and that additional sources such as collective hydrodynamical flow must contribute.

## Method

The author constructs a generic geometric model in which high-p_t partons are produced via hard collisions in the almond-shaped overlap region of two colliding homogeneous spheres, with production probability proportional to the product of the two nuclear longitudinal thicknesses L₊(x,y)L₋(x,y). The probability for a parton to escape is given by an exponential attenuation factor f = exp[-κ ∫₀^∞ ds (L₋L₊)(x+s n_x, y+s n_y)] where κ is a single free parameter characterizing absorption strength; the azimuthal asymmetry v₂ is then computed as a function of κ and impact parameter, and the limiting value v₂* in the strong-absorption limit is compared to STAR data.

## Key numbers

- For the 0-11% centrality bin: model limiting v₂* = 0.042, while STAR preliminary data give v₂ = 0.12 ± 0.02 at p_t > 3.5 GeV
- For the 11-34% centrality bin: model limiting v₂* = 0.12, while STAR data give v₂ = 0.16 ± 0.02
- For the 34-85% centrality bin: model limiting v₂* = 0.16, while STAR data give v₂ = 0.22 ± 0.02
- The ratio v₂*/s₂ (limiting asymmetry to spatial anisotropy) is confined to the range 0 < v₂*/s₂ < 1/3, while STAR data give v₂/s₂ = 0.5-1
- The escape probability ⟨f⟩ in the strong absorption limit (κ = 0.2 fm⁻³) ranges from 0.018 (most central) to 0.046 (most peripheral)
- The data considered cover the transverse momentum range 6 > p_t > 2 GeV

## Context

This work extends the discussion of jet quenching and azimuthal asymmetry initiated by Wang and Gyulassy (1990s) and by Gyulassy, Vitev, and Wang (2001), who had concluded that a combination of jet quenching and hydrodynamical expansion could approximately describe the data. The paper directly contradicts the sufficiency of pure jet quenching models and instead supports the hydrodynamical interpretation of Kolb, Heinz, and collaborators (2000-2001) that had successfully described v₂ at lower p_t < 2 GeV. The author speculates that collective hydrodynamical flow or baryon junction dynamics, as discussed by Gyulassy, Vitev, Wang, and others (2001), may provide the additional asymmetry needed. A note added in proof reports that final STAR data using four-particle cumulants nearly eliminate the discrepancy, and that later STAR data at 200 GeV/N for p_t = 6-12 GeV show good agreement with the geometric limiting v₂* as noted by Voloshin (2002).
