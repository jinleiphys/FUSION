# Quasifree processes from nuclei: Meson photoproduction and electron scattering

> We have developed a relativistic formalism for studying quasi-free processes from nuclei. The formalism can be applied with ease to a variety of processes and renders transparent analytical expressions for all observables. We have applied it to kaon photoproduction and to electron scattering. For the case of the kaon, we compute the recoil polarization of the lambda-hyperon and the photon asymmetry. Our results indicate that polarization observables are insensitive to relativistic, nuclear target, and distortion effects. Yet, they are sensitive to the reactive content, making them ideal tools for the study of modifications to the elementary amplitude -- such as in the production, propagation, and decay of nucleon resonances -- in the nuclear medium. For the case of the electron, we have calculated the spectral function of He-4. An observable is identified for the clean and model-independent extraction of the spectral function. Our calculations provide baseline predictions for the recently measured, but not yet fully analyzed, momentum distribution of He-4 by the A1-collaboration from Mainz. Our approach predicts momentum distributions for He-4 that rival some of the best non-relativistic calculations to date.

## Key claim

Polarization observables in quasifree kaon photoproduction are insensitive to relativistic, nuclear target, and distortion effects, yet are sensitive to the reactive content of the elementary amplitude, making them ideal for studying medium modifications of nucleon resonances. For electron scattering, the longitudinal component of the hadronic tensor provides a clean, model-independent observable for extracting the spectral function, with the caveat that this requires a Rosenbluth separation of the cross section.

## Method

The authors developed a relativistic plane-wave formalism for quasifree processes from nuclei, using an algebraic trick by Gardner and Piekarewicz (1994) that defines a bound nucleon propagator with the same Dirac structure as the free Feynman propagator. This allows the use of Feynman trace techniques, automated via FeynCalc 1.0 with Mathematica 2.0, to evaluate all observables analytically. The formalism was applied to kaon photoproduction (computing lambda-hyperon recoil polarization and photon asymmetry) and to electron scattering (computing the spectral function of He-4), with no distortion effects incorporated.

## Key numbers

- Photon energy for kaon photoproduction calculations: E_gamma = 1400 MeV, at a missing momentum of p_m = 120 MeV, for nuclei ranging from He-4 to Pb-208.
- The momentum distribution extracted via the factorization approximation for He-4 integrates to 2.9 rather than 2, representing a discrepancy of 45 percent.
- The longitudinal response expression for the spectral function is valid up to small second-order corrections of 1-3 percent.
- Distortion effects for the He-4 momentum distribution are estimated at about 12 percent (Florizone 1998, Schiavilla 1990).
- Theoretical predictions of the He-4 momentum distribution overestimate experimental data by up to 50-60 percent.
- High-quality data for the He-4 momentum distribution is available up to a missing momentum of about 200 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic treatment of quasifree processes from nuclei, building on the bound-state propagator technique of Gardner and Piekarewicz (1994). For kaon photoproduction, it provides baseline predictions for polarization observables that are sensitive to the elementary amplitude models such as Saclay-Lyon (1996), WCC (1990), and MFLS (1998). For electron scattering, it challenges the conventional non-relativistic factorization approach used by Fruet (1984) and addresses off-shell ambiguities identified by the Spanish group of Udias and collaborators (1993-2001). The calculations provide baseline predictions for the recently measured but not yet fully analyzed momentum distribution of He-4 by the A1-collaboration from Mainz (Florizone 1998, 2001; Kozlov 2000, 2001a, 2001b), and enable a robust extraction of the spectral function via the longitudinal response that is free from off-shell ambiguities and negative-energy state contributions.
