# $^{178}$Hg and asymmetric fission of neutron-deficient pre-actinides

> Fission at low excitation energy is an ideal playground to probe the impact of nuclear structure on nuclear dynamics. While the importance of structural effects in the nascent fragments is well-established in the (trans-)actinide region, the observation of asymmetric fission in several neutron-deficient pre-actinides can be explained by various mechanisms. To deepen our insight into that puzzle, an innovative approach based on inverse kinematics and an enhanced version of the VAMOS++ heavy-ion spectrometer was implemented at the GANIL facility, Caen. Fission of $^{178}$Hg was induced by fusion of $^{124}$Xe and $^{54}$Fe. The two fragments were detected in coincidence using VAMOS++ supplemented with a new SEcond Detection arm. For the first time in the pre-actinide region, access to the pre-neutron mass and total kinetic energy distributions, and the simultaneous isotopic identification of one the fission fragment, was achieved. The present work describes the experimental approach, and discusses the pre-neutron observables in the context of an extended asymmetric-fission island located south-west of $^{208}Pb. A comparison with different models is performed, demonstrating the importance of this "new" asymmetric-fission island for elaborating on driving effects in fission.

## Key claim

The paper reports the first measurement of pre-neutron mass and total kinetic energy distributions, together with isotopic identification of one fission fragment, for the neutron-deficient pre-actinide nucleus \(^{178}\)Hg, demonstrating that this system lies within an extended asymmetric-fission island southwest of \(^{208}\)Pb, though the authors caution that multi-Gaussian decomposition of the mass distribution may not yield a unique solution due to experimental and procedural ambiguities.

## Method

Fission of \(^{178}\)Hg was induced via fusion of a \(^{124}\)Xe beam at 4.3 MeV/u with a \(^{54}\)Fe target at the GANIL facility, producing the compound nucleus at an excitation energy of 34 MeV. One fission fragment was detected and identified (post-neutron mass and charge) using the VAMOS++ magnetic spectrometer, while the coincident partner was detected using a newly developed SEcond Detection (SED) arm consisting of a position-sensitive multi-wire proportional counter and a silicon strip detector. The kinematical coincidence \(2v\) method was applied to determine pre-neutron fragment masses and total kinetic energies from the measured velocities of both fragments.

## Key numbers

- The compound nucleus \(^{178}\)Hg was produced at an excitation energy \(E^* = 34\) MeV.
- A total of \(6.8 \times 10^4\) true fission coincidences were retained after event selection.
- The subset used for acceptance-corrected analysis comprised \(1.3 \times 10^4\) events.
- The resolution in pre-neutron mass was about 4 amu (FWHM); the resolution in total kinetic energy was about 6 MeV (FWHM).
- The mean pre-neutron total kinetic energy was 136 MeV with a variance of 8 MeV.
- The set-up efficiency was about 2%.
- The SED position resolution was 1.2 mm (FWHM); its intrinsic timing resolution was 200 ps (FWHM); the silicon strip detector energy resolution was 70 keV (FWHM) for the 8.37 MeV \(\alpha\) line of \(^{230}\)Th.
- The VAMOS++ post-neutron mass resolution was \(\Delta A_{post}/A_{post} \approx 0.8\%\).

## Context

This work extends the study of asymmetric fission in neutron-deficient pre-actinides, following the observation of asymmetric fission in \(^{180}\)Hg by Andreyev (2010) and subsequent mapping of an asymmetric-fission island in the region. The new data for \(^{178}\)Hg supplement the existing set of systems investigated by Itkis (1990s), Nishio (2015), and others, and show mass distributions similar to those of \(^{180}\)Hg and \(^{178}\)Pt at comparable excitation energies. The results enable refined comparisons with models such as the Brownian shape-motion model, SPY2, SPM, and GEF, and help constrain the western boundary of the asymmetric-fission island, which remains to be determined.
