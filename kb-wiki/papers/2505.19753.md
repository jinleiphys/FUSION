# Triaxial shapes in even-even nuclei: A theoretical overview

> Triaxial shapes in even-even nuclei have been considered since the early days of the nuclear collective model. Although many theoretical approaches have been used over the years for their description, no effort appears to have been made for grouping them together and identifying regions on the nuclear chart where the appearance of triaxiality might be favored. In addition, over the last few years, discussion has started on the appearance of small triaxiality in nuclei considered so far as purely axial rotors. In the present work we collect the predictions made by various theoretical approaches and show that pronounced triaxiality appears to be favored within specific stripes on the nuclear chart, with low triaxiality being present in the regions between these stripes, in agreement with parameter-free predictions made by the proxy-SU(3) approximation to the shell model, based on the Pauli principle and the short-range nature of the nucleon-nucleon interaction. The robustness of triaxiality within these stripes is supported by global calculations made in the framework of the Finite-Range Droplet Model (FRDM), which is based on completely different assumptions and possesses parameters fitted in order to reproduce fundamental nuclear properties.

## Key claim

The paper collects predictions from various theoretical approaches and shows that pronounced triaxiality (gamma between 15 and 45 degrees) appears to be favored within specific horizontal and vertical stripes on the nuclear chart, with low triaxiality present in the regions between these stripes, in agreement with parameter-free predictions from the proxy-SU(3) approximation; the robustness of triaxiality within these stripes is supported by global calculations from the Finite-Range Droplet Model (FRDM), which is based on completely different assumptions.

## Method

The authors review and compile existing theoretical predictions for triaxial shapes in even-even nuclei from multiple approaches, including the Rigid Triaxial Rotor Model (RTRM), the Algebraic Collective Model (ACM), critical point symmetries (Y(5), Z(5), Z(4), T(5)), special solutions of the Bohr Hamiltonian, the Triaxial Projected Shell Model (TPSM), Monte Carlo Shell Model (MCSM) calculations, the Interacting Boson Model (IBM), and the proxy-SU(3) symmetry. They compare these predictions to identify regions on the nuclear chart where triaxiality is favored, using the proxy-SU(3) framework as a unifying guide based on the Pauli principle and the short-range nature of the nucleon-nucleon interaction.

## Key numbers

- Maximum triaxiality occurs at gamma = 30 degrees, with pronounced triaxiality defined as gamma values between 15 and 45 degrees.
- The stripes where pronounced triaxiality is favored lie within proton or neutron numbers 22-26, 34-48, 74-80, 116-124, and 172-182.
- Gamma values extracted from RTRM fits to data are usually above 10 degrees, serving as an early sign of triaxiality over extended regions of the nuclear chart.
- The Z(5) critical point symmetry involves a steep harmonic oscillator potential around gamma = 30 degrees, with the prolate-to-oblate transition taking place in Pt-192 to Pt-196.
- The Z(4) critical point symmetry fixes gamma at 30 degrees, with the transition taking place in Xe-128 to Xe-132.
- The Y(5) critical point symmetry describes a shape/phase transition in the gamma variable, with Dy-164 and Er-166,168 suggested as possible candidates.
- TPSM calculations have been summarized for 30 triaxial nuclei ranging from Ge to U.

## Context

This work extends the parameter-free proxy-SU(3) predictions of Bonatsos (2017, 2023, 2025) by comparing them against decades of theoretical work from the Bohr collective model, the Interacting Boson Model, and large-scale shell model calculations. It contradicts the traditional assumption that most deformed nuclei are purely axial rotors, supporting recent suggestions from Monte Carlo Shell Model calculations by Otsuka (2019, 2023) and Triaxial Projected Shell Model calculations by Rouoof (2024) that some amount of triaxiality is present in almost all nuclei. The compilation enables future experimental searches for triaxial nuclei by identifying specific stripes on the nuclear chart where pronounced triaxiality is theoretically favored.
