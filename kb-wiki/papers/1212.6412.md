# Parity- and Time-Reversal Tests in Nuclear Physics

> Nuclear physics tests of parity- and time-reversal invariance have both shaped the development of the Standard Model and provided key tests of its predictions. These studies now provide vital input in the search for physics beyond the Standard Model. We give a brief review of a few key experimental and theoretical developments in the history of this sub-field of nuclear physics as well as a short outlook, focusing on weak decays, parity-violation in electron scattering, and searches for permanent electric dipole moments of the neutron and neutral atoms.

## Key claim

Nuclear physics tests of parity- and time-reversal invariance have historically shaped the Standard Model and now provide vital input for searching beyond-Standard-Model physics, but the paper is a qualitative and retrospective review that keeps technical details to a minimum rather than presenting new experimental results.

## Method

The authors provide a brief historical and qualitative review of key experimental and theoretical developments in nuclear-physics tests of parity (P) and time-reversal (T) invariance, focusing on three areas: weak decays (charged-current interactions), parity-violation in electron scattering (neutral-current interactions), and searches for permanent electric dipole moments (EDMs) of the neutron and neutral atoms. The review covers experiments from the 1950s discovery of parity violation in \(^{60}\)Co beta decay and pion decay through modern precision measurements, including the Perkeo II and UCNA neutron decay experiments, the SLAC polarized electron-deuteron scattering experiment, and the cesium atomic parity-violation measurement.

## Key numbers

- The \(^{60}\)Co beta-decay experiment measured 310 keV electrons from the dominant decay branch, with the crystal cooled to 0.003 K to polarize the nuclei.
- The muon g-factor was measured as \(g = 2.00 \pm 0.10\) in the 1957 Columbia pion-decay experiment.
- Modern muon lifetime measurements have reached 1.0 ppm precision, yielding the Fermi constant to 0.5 ppm; the muon g-factor has been measured to nearly 0.3 ppb, showing a 3.6\(\sigma\) discrepancy with the Standard Model.
- The TWIST collaboration measured muon-decay parity-violating directional asymmetry parameters agreeing with Standard Model expectations at \(\sim 0.1\%\) precision, constraining possible right-handed muon couplings below a part per thousand.
- The Perkeo II and UCNA collaborations have measured the neutron decay asymmetry parameter \(\lambda = g_A/g_V\) with roughly 0.1% precision.
- The 1978 SLAC polarized electron-deuteron scattering experiment measured the parity-violating asymmetry \(A_{PV} \sim 10^{-4}\) and yielded \(\sin^2\theta_W = 0.224 \pm 0.020\).
- The cesium atomic parity-violation measurement determined the weak charge \(Q_W(\mathrm{Cs})\) with experimental error below 0.5% and theoretical error refined over a decade, agreeing with the Standard Model at the 0.5% level.
- Neutron EDM searches constrain the QCD \(\bar{\theta}\) parameter to be no larger than \(\sim 10^{-10}\).
- The \(^{199}\)Hg EDM search (Griffith 2009) also constrains \(\bar{\theta}\) to \(\sim 10^{-10}\).

## Context

This review extends the historical narrative of parity and time-reversal tests in nuclear physics, covering developments from the 1950s Wu experiment (Wu 1957) and Garwin-Lederman-Weinrich experiment (Garwin 1957) through modern precision measurements such as the TWIST muon decay study (Bueno 2011, Bayes 2011), Perkeo II (Mund 2012) and UCNA (2012) neutron decay experiments, and the cesium atomic parity-violation result (Wood 1997). The paper enables future work by highlighting the complementarity of these nuclear-physics tests with beyond-Standard-Model searches at the Large Hadron Collider, and by identifying open questions such as the need for lattice QCD to compute small strange-quark contributions to nucleon structure and the potential for next-generation EDM searches to test electroweak baryogenesis scenarios.
