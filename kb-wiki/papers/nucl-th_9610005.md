# On the Coulomb and higher-order sum rules in the relativistic Fermi gas

> Two different methods for establishing a space-like Coulomb sum rule for the relativistic Fermi gas are compared. Both of them divide the charge response by a normalizing factor such that the reduced response thus obtained fulfills the sum rule at large momentum transfer. To determine the factor, in the first approach one exploits the scaling property of the longitudinal response function, while in the second one enforces the completeness of the states in the space-like domain via the Foldy-Wouthuysen transformation. The energy-weighted and the squared-energy-weighted sum rules for the reduced responses are explored as well and the extension to momentum distributions that are more general than a step-function is also considered. The two methods yield reduced responses and Coulomb sum rules that saturate in the non-Pauli-blocked region, which can hardly be distinguished for Fermi momenta appropriate to atomic nuclei. Notably the sum rule obtained in the Foldy-Wouthuysen approach coincides with the well known non-relativistic one. Only at quite large momentum transfers (say 1 GeV/c) does a modest softening of the Foldy-Wouthuysen reduced response with respect to that obtained in the scaling framework show up. The two responses have the same half-width to second order in the Fermi momentum expansion. However, when distributions extending to momenta larger than that at the Fermi surface are employed, then in both methods the Coulomb sum rule saturates only if the normalizing factors are appropriately modified to account for the high momentum components of the nucleons.

## Key claim

The paper shows that two different methods for constructing a space-like Coulomb sum rule for the relativistic Fermi gas, one based on scaling properties of the longitudinal response and the other on the Foldy-Wouthuysen transformation, yield reduced responses and sum rules that are nearly indistinguishable for Fermi momenta appropriate to atomic nuclei, with only a modest softening of the Foldy-Wouthuysen reduced response appearing at momentum transfers around 1 GeV/c.

## Method

The authors compare two approaches for dividing the charge response by a normalizing factor to obtain a reduced response that satisfies a Coulomb sum rule at large momentum transfer. In the scaling approach, the normalizing factor exploits the scaling property of the longitudinal response function using a relativistic scaling variable. In the Foldy-Wouthuysen approach, completeness of states in the space-like domain is enforced via a unitary transformation that projects out particle-antiparticle excitations. The analysis is extended to energy-weighted and squared-energy-weighted sum rules, and to momentum distributions more general than the step-function Fermi gas.

## Key numbers

- The Foldy-Wouthuysen Coulomb sum rule coincides with the well-known non-relativistic Coulomb sum rule at all momentum transfers.
- At momentum transfers of 1 GeV/c, a modest softening of the Foldy-Wouthuysen reduced response relative to the scaling approach becomes visible.
- The two reduced responses have the same half-width to second order in the Fermi momentum expansion.
- For the harmonic oscillator momentum distribution of 16O, the Fermi momentum extracted from the linear term in the sum rule expansion yields k_F = 1.1 fm^{-1}, lying between values of 1.039 fm^{-1} and 1.22 fm^{-1} obtained from other procedures.
- The variance of the reduced response in both methods is sigma = (kappa * eta_F) / sqrt(5 * (1 + 4*kappa^2)) + O[eta_F^3].

## Context

This work extends earlier non-relativistic Coulomb sum rule treatments (West 1975, Rosenfelder 1980) and relativistic Fermi gas studies (Walecka 1983, Matsui 1983) by comparing two methods for achieving saturation in the space-like region alone. The scaling approach was previously developed by Alberico et al. (1993), while the Foldy-Wouthuysen approach builds on work by Amore, Boffi, and Dean (1995). The paper enables future application of these sum rule methods to finite nuclei with realistic momentum distributions, and provides a framework for testing relativistic effects in electron-nucleus scattering experiments.
