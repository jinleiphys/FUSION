# The Role of Fission in Neutron Star Mergers and its Impact on the r-Process Peaks

> Comparing observational abundance features with nucleosynthesis predictions of stellar evolution or explosion simulations can scrutinize two aspects: (a) the conditions in the astrophysical production site and (b) the quality of the nuclear physics input utilized. We test the abundance features of r-process nucleosynthesis calculations for the dynamical ejecta of neutron star merger simulations based on three different nuclear mass models: The Finite Range Droplet Model (FRDM), the (quenched version of the) Extended Thomas Fermi Model with Strutinsky Integral (ETFSI-Q), and the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) mass model. We make use of corresponding fission barrier heights and compare the impact of four different fission fragment distribution models on the final r-process abundance distribution. In particular, we explore the abundance distribution in the second r-process peak and the rare-earth sub-peak as a function of mass models and fission fragment distributions, as well as the origin of a shift in the third r-process peak position. The latter has been noticed in a number of merger nucleosynthesis predictions. We show that the shift occurs during the r-process freeze-out when neutron captures and β-decays compete and an (n,γ)-(γ,n) equilibrium is not maintained anymore. During this phase neutrons originate mainly from fission of material above A = 240. We also investigate the role of β-decay half-lives from recent theoretical advances, which lead either to a smaller amount of fissioning nuclei during freeze-out or a faster (and thus earlier) release of fission neutrons, which can (partially) prevent this shift and has an impact on the second and rare-earth peak as well.

## Key claim

The shift of the third r-process peak to higher mass numbers observed in neutron star merger nucleosynthesis predictions occurs during the r-process freeze-out when (n,γ)-(γ,n) equilibrium is no longer maintained, and neutrons released primarily from fission of material above A = 240 drive late neutron captures that cause this shift.

## Method

The authors performed r-process nucleosynthesis calculations for 30 representative fluid trajectories from dynamical ejecta of neutron star merger simulations, using the extended nuclear network Winnet with over 6000 isotopes. They compared three nuclear mass models (FRDM, ETFSI-Q, and HFB-14) combined with four different fission fragment distribution models (Kodama and Takahashi 1975, Panov et al. 2001, Panov et al. 2008, and ABLA07), including spontaneous, β-delayed, and neutron-induced fission modes, and tested the effect of recent theoretical β-decay half-life predictions.

## Key numbers

- The third peak is shifted by up to 5 mass units to higher mass numbers compared to solar values for all mass models tested
- At freeze-out time (t = 1.34 s for FRDM, t = 1.36 s for ETFSI-Q, t = 1.24 s for HFB-14), the third peak position is still consistent with solar r-abundances
- At t = 1 s with FRDM, the temperature is 9.5 × 10^8 K and neutron density is 7.44 × 10^26 cm^-3
- The Panov et al. (2008) fission model predicts 19 released neutrons for neutron-induced fission of 274Pu, while ABLA07 predicts 7 neutrons for the same reaction
- The ABLA07 model with FRDM produces underproduction of A = 140-170 nuclei by a factor of about 3
- The Panov et al. (2008) model produces underproduction of the mass region A ≈ 140-170 by a factor of 10 or more
- Fissioning nuclei in FRDM calculations reach up to A = 287, while HFB-14 calculations reach up to A = 300
- Fission fragments are mainly produced in the mass range 100 ≤ A ≤ 160

## Context

This work extends the neutron star merger nucleosynthesis calculations of Korobkin et al. (2012) by testing additional mass models (ETFSI-Q and HFB-14) beyond FRDM, and by implementing newer fission fragment distribution models (Panov et al. 2008 and ABLA07). The shift of the third peak had been noticed in previous merger nucleosynthesis predictions by Freiburghaus et al. (1999), Metzger et al. (2010), Roberts et al. (2011), Korobkin et al. (2012), and Goriely et al. (2013). The results enable future studies to constrain nuclear mass models and fission fragment distributions by comparing predicted abundance patterns with observed solar r-process abundances, particularly for the second peak and rare-earth sub-peak regions.
