# Magnetic fields in heavy ion collisions: flow and charge transport

> At the earliest times after a heavy-ion collision, the magnetic field created by the spectator nucleons will generate an extremely strong, albeit rapidly decreasing in time, magnetic field. The impact of this magnetic field may have detectable consequences, and is believed to drive anomalous transport effects like the Chiral Magnetic Effect (CME). We detail an exploratory study on the effects of a dynamical magnetic field on the hydrodynamic medium created in the collisions of two ultrarelativistic heavy-ions, using the framework of numerical ideal MagnetoHydroDynamics (MHD) with the ECHO-QGP code. In this study, we consider a magnetic field captured in a conducting medium, where the conductivity can receive contributions from the electromagnetic conductivity $σ$ and the chiral magnetic conductivity $σ_χ$. We first study the elliptic flow of pions, which we show is relatively unchanged by the introduction of a magnetic field. However, by increasing the magnitude of the magnetic field, we find evidence for an enhancement of the elliptic flow in peripheral collisions. Next, we explore the impact of the chiral magnetic conductivity on electric charges produced at the edges of the fireball. This initial $σ_χ$ can be understood as a long-wavelength effective description of chiral fermion production. We then demonstrate that this chiral charge, when transported by the MHD medium, produces a charge dipole perpendicular to the reaction plane which extends a few units in rapidity. Assuming charge conservation at the freeze-out surface, we show that the produced charge imbalance can have measurable effects on some experimental observables, like $v_1$ or $\langle \sin ϕ\rangle$. This demonstrates the ability of a MHD fluid to transport the signature of the initial chiral magnetic fields to late times.

## Key claim

The paper presents an exploratory study showing that while the elliptic flow of pions is relatively unchanged by the introduction of a magnetic field, increasing the magnetic field magnitude can enhance elliptic flow in peripheral collisions, with the effect stronger at RHIC than at LHC energies. The authors also demonstrate that chiral charge transported by the magnetohydrodynamic medium produces a charge dipole perpendicular to the reaction plane extending a few units in rapidity, which can have measurable effects on observables like directed flow.

## Method

The authors performed (3+1)-dimensional numerical simulations of ideal relativistic magnetohydrodynamics (MHD) using the ECHO-QGP code in Milne/Bjorken coordinates, with initial energy density distributions from a geometrical Glauber model. The initial magnetic field was computed from point charges moving at constant velocity in a conducting medium with both electromagnetic conductivity and chiral magnetic conductivity, then integrated over Lorentz-contracted nuclei. The authors computed thermal spectra using the Cooper-Frye prescription with local charge conservation at freeze-out, and studied how varying impact parameter, conductivity, freeze-out temperature, and initial magnetic field magnitude affected elliptic flow of pions at mid-rapidity.

## Key numbers

- Initial magnetic field strength of order eB ~ (m_pi)^2 ~ 10^14 T at earliest times after collision
- For Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV with impact parameter b = 8 fm, the enhancement of v_2 at p_T ~ 3 GeV reached up to 12% when the initial magnetic field was multiplied by a factor of 4
- For Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV with b = 8 fm, the enhancement of v_2 at p_T ~ 3 GeV reached up to 4.7% when the initial magnetic field was multiplied by a factor of 4
- The initial time for RHIC simulations was tau_0 = 0.4 fm/c and for LHC simulations tau_0 = 0.2 fm/c
- The freeze-out temperature used was T_f.o. = 154 MeV for both RHIC and LHC simulations
- The electrical conductivity used in initial conditions was sigma = 5.8 MeV and chiral magnetic conductivity sigma_chi = 1.5 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous simplified studies of electromagnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions by Deng (2012), Pang (2016), and Roy (2017) that neglected the back-reaction of the fluid on the fields, and builds on the MHD framework developed by Inghirami (2016) and Das (2017) that included this back-reaction in the ideal conductivity limit. The study enables future development of dissipative-resistive MHD models for more realistic QGP descriptions, and provides a framework for connecting the chiral magnetic effect signal from pre-equilibrium stages to late-time experimental observables like directed flow and charge-dependent azimuthal correlations.
