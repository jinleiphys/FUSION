# Two-Step Model of Fusion for Synthesis of Superheavy Elements

> A new model is proposed for fusion mechanisms of massive nuclear systems where so-called fusion hindrance exists. The model describes two-body collision processes in an approaching phase and shape evolutions of an amalgamated system into the compound nucleus formation. It is applied to $^{48}$Ca-induced reactions and is found to reproduce the experimental fusion cross sections extremely well, without any free parameter. Combined with the statistical decay theory, residue cross sections for the superheavy elements can be readily calculated. Examples are given.

# Two-Step Model of Fusion for Synthesis of Superheavy Elements

## Key claim

The paper proposes a two-step fusion model that reproduces experimental fusion cross sections for ⁴⁸Ca-induced reactions "extremely well, without any free parameter," but the model is completely classical and thus excludes quantum tunneling effects, which limits the lowest energy that can be reached.

## Method

The model separates fusion into two sequential Langevin-dynamical steps: first, the approaching phase of two colliding nuclei passing over the Coulomb barrier is described by a surface friction model (SFM) with radial and tangential friction, yielding a sticking probability and a Gaussian distribution of radial momenta at the contact point; second, shape evolutions of the amalgamated mono-nuclear system toward the spherical compound nucleus are described by a multi-dimensional Langevin equation with the one-body wall-and-window dissipation formula, using a two-center parameterization of nuclear shapes with the relative distance and mass asymmetry coordinates. The fusion probability is the product of the sticking probability from step one and the formation probability from step two, the latter obtained by convolving the formation distribution from step two with the Gaussian initial momentum distribution from step one.

## Key numbers

- For the ⁴⁸Ca-²³⁸U system at L=0, the sticking probability just above the barrier is "almost no probability" (no explicit numerical value given for the probability itself).
- Radial friction coefficient prefactor: K⁰ᵣ = 0.035 in units of 10⁻²¹ s/MeV; tangential friction coefficient prefactor: K⁰ₜ = 0.0001 in the same units.
- For ⁴⁸Ca-²⁴⁴Pu with Möller/3 shell corrections, the calculated maximum 3n residue cross section is 7.39 pb at excitation energy E* = 30.1 MeV; the 4n residue cross section is 6.00 pb at E* = 35.3 MeV.
- For ⁴⁸Ca-²⁴⁸Cm with Möller/3 shell corrections, the calculated maximum 3n residue cross section is 4.56 pb at E* = 30.4 MeV; the 4n residue cross section is 2.98 pb at E* = 35.6 MeV.
- For ⁴⁸Ca-²⁵²Cf with Möller/3 shell corrections, the calculated maximum 3n residue cross section is 0.216 pb at E* = 28.8 MeV; the 4n residue cross section is 0.086 pb at E* = 33.5 MeV.
- Experimental residue cross sections for ⁴⁸Ca-²⁴⁴Pu are approximately 1 pb at laboratory energy 236 MeV for both 3n and 4n channels; for ⁴⁸Ca-²⁴⁸Cm the experimental 4n cross section is 0.6 pb at E* = 35.8 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of fusion hindrance in massive nuclear systems, previously described phenomenologically by the "extra-push" concept of Swiatecki (1982) and by dissipative collision models. The two-step model combines the surface friction model of Frobrich (1984) for the approaching phase with the wall-and-window dissipation formula for shape evolutions, and applies the same multi-dimensional Langevin formalism previously used for dynamical studies of fission. The model enables calculation of residue cross sections for superheavy elements (Z=114, 116, 118) when combined with the statistical decay code HIVAP, and the authors report reasonable agreement with Dubna experiments (2002) using shell correction energies much smaller than previously thought. The authors note the model is now being applied to other massive systems such as ¹⁰⁰Mo-¹⁰⁰Mo.
