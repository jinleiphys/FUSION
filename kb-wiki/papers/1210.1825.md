# Axially deformed solution of the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov equations using the transformed harmonic oscillator basis (II) HFBTHO v2.00d: a new version of the program

> We describe the new version 2.00d of the code HFBTHO that solves the nuclear Skyrme Hartree-Fock (HF) or Skyrme Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov (HFB) problem by using the cylindrical transformed deformed harmonic-oscillator basis. In the new version, we have implemented the following features: (i) the modified Broyden method for non-linear problems, (ii) optional breaking of reflection symmetry, (iii) calculation of axial multipole moments, (iv) finite temperature formalism for the HFB method, (v) linear constraint method based on the approximation of the Random Phase Approximation (RPA) matrix for multi-constraint calculations, (vi) blocking of quasi-particles in the Equal Filling Approximation (EFA), (vii) framework for generalized energy density with arbitrary density-dependences, and (viii) shared memory parallelism via OpenMP pragmas.

## Key claim

The paper presents version 2.00d of the HFBTHO code, which extends the previous version (1.66) with eight new capabilities including finite-temperature HFB, quasi-particle blocking, and generalized Skyrme functionals, while establishing precise numerical benchmarks against other DFT solvers; the authors note that the new version is slightly slower per iteration than version 1.66 when using a single thread due to overhead from generalized functionals and the Broyden method.

## Method

The code solves the Skyrme Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov equations by expanding quasiparticle wave functions in an axial transformed harmonic oscillator (THO) single-particle basis and iteratively diagonalizing the HFB Hamiltonian until self-consistency is achieved. The new version implements the modified Broyden method for convergence acceleration, optional breaking of reflection symmetry, calculation of axial multipole moments up to order 8, finite-temperature HFB formalism, linear constraints using an RPA-matrix approximation, quasi-particle blocking in the Equal Filling Approximation, generalized Skyrme energy density functionals with arbitrary density-dependence, and shared-memory parallelism via OpenMP pragmas.

## Key numbers

* Total energy of 208Pb in spherical HF without Coulomb: -2445.930216 MeV (HFBTHO), agreeing with HOSPHE to within 1 eV and with HFODD to within 2 eV on the Skyrme energy
* Total energy of 208Pb in spherical HF with Coulomb: -1632.591495 MeV (HFBTHO), with discrepancies of order 100 eV between solvers
* Total energy of 120Sn in spherical HFB without Coulomb: -1374.087663 MeV (HFBTHO), with pairing energy -12.646023 MeV and neutron pairing gap 0.910071 MeV
* Total energy of 120Sn in spherical HFB with Coulomb: -1021.265407 MeV (HFBTHO), with pairing energy -11.125231 MeV and neutron pairing gap 0.864875 MeV
* Proton rms radius of 208Pb without Coulomb: 5.250015 fm (HFBTHO), differing from HOSPHE by 0.0002 fm
* Proton rms radius of 120Sn without Coulomb: 4.455761 fm (HFBTHO)
* Typical running time ranges from a few seconds for ground-state configurations in small bases (N_max approx 8-12) to a few minutes for very deformed heavy nuclei with large bases (N_max greater than 20)
* OpenMP speedup demonstrated on 8-core Intel Xeon E5-2670 at 2.6 GHz for 120Sn with N_max=20 shells using SLy4 interaction

## Context

This work extends the original HFBTHO code described by Stoitsov, Dobaczewski, Nazarewicz, and Ring (2005) and provides comprehensive benchmarks against the spherical solver HOSPHE (version 2.00, described by Carlsson et al., 2013 and 2010) and the symmetry-unrestricted solver HFODD (version 2.56, described by Schunck et al., 2013 and 2012, and Dobaczewski et al., 2009). The new capabilities enable calculations of odd-even and odd-odd nuclei via blocking, finite-temperature properties, and generalized energy density functionals such as the UNEDF0 and UNEDF1 parameterizations of Kortelainen et al. (2010, 2012), facilitating future development of nuclear DFT solvers and deeper integration between HFBTHO and HFODD.
