# The r-mode instability in strange stars with a crystalline crust

> The r-mode instability, believed to limit the rotation speed of compact stars, can provide empirical confirmation for the existence of stable deconfined phases of quark matter that are predicted by weak coupling calculations in Quantum Chromodynamics. We construct a model for strange quark stars as heavy as 2 solar masses that are made of superconducting quark matter in the bulk and a thin crystalline quark matter crust. This crystalline quark crust is sufficiently robust to withstand r-mode heating and viscous rubbing for realistic mode amplitudes O(10^{-2}), unlike a crust made of neutron-rich nuclei. The dissipation provided by viscous rubbing at the core-crust boundary is both necessary and sufficient to obtain stable rotation speeds that are consistent with the majority of rapidly spinning pulsars in low mass X-ray binaries. Our analysis implies that while bare strange stars are ruled out by the existence of rapidly spinning pulsars, a strange star with a quark matter crust is a distinct possibility.

# The r-mode instability in strange stars with a crystalline crust

## Key claim

Strange quark stars with a thin crystalline quark matter crust can achieve stable rotation speeds consistent with observed rapidly spinning pulsars in low-mass X-ray binaries, provided the crust is sufficiently rigid to withstand r-mode heating and viscous rubbing at mode amplitudes of order 10^{-2}. This contrasts with bare strange stars, which are ruled out by the existence of rapidly spinning pulsars, and with neutron stars whose nuclear crust would melt under such conditions.

## Method

The authors construct a model of strange quark stars using the CFL-K0 (kaon-condensed color-flavor-locked) phase for the bulk and a CubeX crystalline quark phase for a thin surface crust, solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations with general relativistic corrections. They calculate the r-mode damping timescales from gravitational wave emission, shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, and a new contribution from viscous rubbing at the core-crust boundary, then solve for the critical spin frequency where total damping balances gravitational wave growth. The shear modulus of the crystalline quark crust is taken to be 20-1000 times larger than that of a nuclear crust, and the slippage factor at the core-crust boundary is computed to be nearly 1 for the frequency range of interest.

## Key numbers

- Maximum stellar mass of 2 solar masses is achieved with the equation of state parameters used.
- Two stellar models are constructed: a 1.40 solar mass star with radius 11.14 km and central density 2.71 times nuclear saturation density, and a 1.97 solar mass star with radius 11.71 km and central density 4.93 times nuclear saturation density.
- The crust thickness is less than 1% of the stellar radius, with the core-crust boundary at 0.997 R for the 1.40 solar mass star and 0.993 R for the 1.97 solar mass star.
- The viscous boundary layer thickness d is at least a factor of about 100 smaller than the crust thickness, validating the boundary layer approximation.
- The critical frequency from rubbing friction alone scales as approximately 123 times T_9^{-5/11} Hz, giving about 350 Hz at 10^8 K for the 1.40 solar mass star.
- In contrast, equating gravitational wave timescale to shear viscosity timescale at 10^8 K gives a critical frequency of only about 11 Hz.
- The melting temperature of crystalline quark matter is about 10 MeV, roughly 10 times larger than for a nuclear crust.
- The shear modulus of crystalline quark matter is 20-1000 times larger than that of a nuclear crust.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies showing that bare strange stars cannot explain observed LMXB spin frequencies due to insufficient r-mode dissipation (Madsen 2000, Kokkotas 2001, Rupak 2010). It builds on the finding that nuclear crusts in neutron stars can damp r-modes but would melt at amplitudes of order 10^{-2} (Lindblom 2000, Levin 2000). The authors use the CubeX crystalline quark phase (Rajagopal 2006) and the CFL-K0 equation of state (Alford 2004) to construct stars meeting the 2 solar mass observational constraint. This work enables future studies of shear modes in crystalline quark crusts and their possible connection to seismic vibrations observed in magnetar flares, which pose a challenge for normal quark matter crusts (Watts 2006).
