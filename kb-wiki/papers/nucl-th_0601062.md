# Canonical suppression in microscopic transport models

> We demonstrate the occurrence of canonical suppression associated with the conservation of an U(1)-charge in current transport models. For this study a pion gas is simulated within two different transport approaches by incorporating inelastic and volume-limited collisions $ππ\leftrightarrow K\bar{K}$ for the production of kaon pairs. Both descriptions can dynamically account for the suppression in the yields of rare strange particles in a limited box, being in full accordance with a canonical statistical description.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that two different microscopic transport models (UrQMD and a stochastic cascade) dynamically reproduce the canonical suppression of rare strange particle yields in a finite volume, in full accordance with a canonical statistical description, even though the transport algorithms are based on solving the Boltzmann equation which would naively lead to a grand canonical result.

## Method

A pion gas at T=170 MeV was simulated in a large fixed box (20 fm side length, 8000 fm³ volume) containing a smaller reaction volume of variable size where only the inelastic reactions ππ ↔ K K̅ were allowed, with kaons confined to the small volume. Two transport approaches were used: UrQMD, which uses a geometrical interpretation of cross sections, and a stochastic cascade that calculates collision probabilities via transition rates in small spatial subcells. The kaon density was extracted as a function of the reaction volume size after equilibration.

## Key numbers

- Temperature of the pion heat bath: T = 170 MeV
- Heat bath volume: 8000 fm³, corresponding to 1348 pions with total energy 747.5 GeV
- Constant cross sections for ππ → K K̅ forward reactions: 1 mb, 5 mb, or 10 mb (UrQMD simulations)
- For the stochastic cascade, deviations in temperature and effective fugacity were at most 2 percent
- In UrQMD with typical mesonic cross sections, the discrepancy in kaon yield relative to the theoretical limit was in the range of 10 to 15 percent
- The chemical equilibration time for kaons in the canonical regime is estimated as τ_chem;K ≈ 5 V/(fm² c), with typical reaction volumes of order 100 fm³

## Context

This work extends the dynamical interpretation of canonical suppression previously offered by kinetic master-equation formulations by Ko (2000) and Redlich (2001) to full microscopic transport models. It shows that the canonical suppression effect, long known from statistical models applied at SIS energies by Cleymans (1997, 1998) and Oeschler (2000), is naturally reproduced by transport codes that conserve energy and quantum numbers exactly. The paper enables future investigations into whether chemical equilibrium of kaons can be achieved in heavy ion collisions at intermediate energies, and warns that test-particle methods (as used in parton cascades by Xu 2004) or perturbative methods with virtual particles must be avoided when canonical suppression is relevant.
