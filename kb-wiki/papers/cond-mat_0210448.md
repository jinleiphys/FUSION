# Comment on: "Nonextensivity: from low-dimensional maps to Hamiltonian systems" by Tsallis et al.

## Key claim

Boltzmann's microcanonical entropy, not the canonical Boltzmann-Gibbs distribution, is the correct foundation for equilibrium statistics of non-extensive Hamiltonian systems including small systems like nuclei and self-gravitating systems; Tsallis q-statistics is unnecessary for equilibrium because the microcanonical Boltzmann entropy already handles non-extensivity without modification.

## Method

The author rebuts the critique by Tsallis et al. against using Boltzmann's microcanonical entropy by distinguishing it from the canonical Boltzmann-Gibbs distribution, pointing out that the two are not equivalent at phase separations. The author cites prior work applying microcanonical Boltzmann statistics to hot nuclei, atomic clusters, and self-gravitating systems, and contrasts these results with the canonical q-statistics approach of Tsallis et al., noting that even the HMF-model discussed by Tsallis approaches Boltzmann entropy with q=1 in the equilibrium limit.

## Key numbers

- The logistic map parameter a_c = 1.4101... marks the edge of chaos where S_{q=0.2445...}(t) becomes linear in time, whereas for a > a_c, S_{q=1}(t) rises linearly.
- Negative heat capacity was predicted for small systems like hot nuclei and confirmed experimentally in sodium clusters by Schmidt (2001), though Tsallis et al. offered an alternative explanation using their 1-dim HMF-model.

## Context

This comment extends the author's geometric approach to statistical mechanics developed since 1981 for hot nuclei (Gross 1981, Gross 1995) and atomic clusters, which successfully predicted negative heat capacity and sharp phase transitions in small systems. It contradicts the claim by Tsallis et al. (2002) that non-extensive Hamiltonian systems require Tsallis q-statistics, and instead argues that Boltzmann's microcanonical entropy, as applied to self-gravitating systems by Gross (2002) using Lynden-Bell statistics (Lynden-Bell 1967), is sufficient. The work enables further application of microcanonical Boltzmann statistics to non-extensive equilibrium systems without resorting to alternative entropy forms.
