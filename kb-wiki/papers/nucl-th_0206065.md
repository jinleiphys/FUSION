# Semiclassical evaluation of average nuclear one and two body matrix elements

> Thomas-Fermi theory is developed to evaluate nuclear matrix elements averaged on the energy shell, on the basis of independent particle Hamiltonians. One- and two-body matrix elements are compared with the quantal results and it is demonstrated that the semiclassical matrix elements, as function of energy, well pass through the average of the scattered quantum values. For the one-body matrix elements it is shown how the Thomas-Fermi approach can be projected on good parity and also on good angular momentum. For the two-body case the pairing matrix elements are considered explicitly.

# Semiclassical evaluation of average nuclear one and two body matrix elements

## Key claim
The Thomas-Fermi (TF) approximation, at lowest order in the Wigner-Kirkwood expansion, can accurately reproduce the energy-averaged values of both one-body and two-body nuclear matrix elements, including parity- and angular-momentum-projected cases, when the quantal energies are used as input. The method works for pairing matrix elements as well, but the quality of the agreement depends on the choice of the single-particle potential (harmonic oscillator vs. Woods-Saxon).

## Method
The authors replace the quantum density matrix on the energy shell by its semiclassical counterpart using the Wigner-Kirkwood expansion truncated at the Thomas-Fermi (lowest-order) level. For one-body matrix elements, they compute the Wigner transform of the on-shell density matrix and project onto good parity and angular momentum using two different semiclassical projection schemes (Hasse's method and an alternative plane-wave expansion). For two-body matrix elements, they evaluate the average pairing matrix element by inserting the TF non-local density matrix into the double integral over the two-body interaction, using both a zero-range delta force and the finite-range Gogny D1S force.

## Context
This work extends the semiclassical Thomas-Fermi method, previously used for ground-state properties and level densities (see references in the paper), to the evaluation of energy-averaged one- and two-body matrix elements. It builds on earlier applications of the Wigner-Kirkwood expansion by Migdal and on techniques developed in the context of chaotic dynamics (Hasse, 1980). The approach provides a computationally efficient alternative to Strutinsky averaging for obtaining shell-averaged matrix elements, and it enables systematic studies of pairing correlations in finite nuclei without the need for full quantal calculations. The authors note that the method can be extended to include higher-order $\hbar$ corrections and to treat other two-body operators beyond pairing.
