# Microsopic nuclear level densities by the shell model Monte Carlo method

> The configuration-interaction shell model approach provides an attractive framework for the calculation of nuclear level densities in the presence of correlations, but the large dimensionality of the model space has hindered its application in mid-mass and heavy nuclei. The shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method permits calculations in model spaces that are many orders of magnitude larger than spaces that can be treated by conventional diagonalization methods. We discuss recent progress in the SMMC approach to level densities, and in particular the calculation of level densities in heavy nuclei. We calculate the distribution of the axial quadrupole operator in the laboratory frame at finite temperature and demonstrate that it is a model-independent signature of deformation in the rotational invariant framework of the shell model. We propose a method to use these distributions for calculating level densities as a function of intrinsic deformation.

# Microsopic nuclear level densities by the shell model Monte Carlo method

## Key claim

The shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method can calculate nuclear level densities in heavy nuclei up to the lanthanides, but the method requires stabilization of ill-defined matrices at large inverse temperatures and, for odd-even nuclei, suffers from a sign problem that limits accurate ground-state energy determination to inverse temperatures up to about 4 MeV⁻¹.

## Method

The authors use the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to represent the Gibbs ensemble of a configuration-interaction shell model Hamiltonian as a superposition of non-interacting nucleon ensembles in external auxiliary fields, then integrate over the auxiliary fields via Monte Carlo sampling. The state density is obtained by integrating the thermal energy to compute the partition function, then applying a saddle-point approximation to the inverse Laplace transform of the partition function. For odd-even nuclei, the ground-state energy is determined from a one-parameter fit of the SMMC thermal energy to thermal energy calculated from experimental data, rather than from direct SMMC calculation.

## Key numbers

- The SMMC method permits calculations in model spaces many orders of magnitude larger than spaces treatable by conventional diagonalization methods, which are limited to dimensionalities of approximately 10¹¹.
- For odd-even nuclei, the thermal energy can be calculated only up to β ∼ 4 MeV⁻¹ due to the sign problem from projection on odd particle number.
- The quadrupole distribution for ¹⁵⁴Sm at T = 0.1 MeV shows a skewed distribution qualitatively similar to a prolate rigid rotor; at T = 4 MeV the distribution is close to a Gaussian.
- The SMMC state densities for even-mass samarium and neodymium isotopes show good agreement with level counting data at low excitation energies and with neutron resonance data when available.

## Context

This work extends the SMMC approach to level densities from mid-mass nuclei (Alhassid 1999) to heavy nuclei in the lanthanide region (Alhassid 2008, Ozen 2013), using a proton-neutron formalism with different single-particle orbitals for protons and neutrons. The method enables microscopic calculation of level densities including correlations that are missed by mean-field and combinatorial approaches (Konig 2008). The quadrupole distribution method provides a model-independent signature of deformation within the rotationally invariant shell model framework, and the authors plan to use it to calculate level densities as a function of intrinsic deformation for fission modeling.
