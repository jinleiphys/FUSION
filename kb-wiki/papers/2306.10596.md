# Effect of finite volume on thermodynamics of quark-hadron matter

> The effects of a finite system volume on thermodynamic quantities, such as the pressure, energy density, specific heat, speed of sound, conserved charge susceptibilities and correlations, in hot and dense strongly interacting matter are studied within the parity-doublet Chiral Mean Field (CMF) model. Such an investigation is motivated by relativistic heavy-ion collisions, which create a blob of hot QCD matter of a finite volume, consisting of strongly interacting hadrons and potentially deconfined quarks and gluons. The effect of the finite volume of the system is incorporated by introducing a lower momentum cut-offs in the momentum integrals appearing in the model, the numerical value of the momentum cut-off being related to the de Broglie wavelength of the given particle species. It is found that some of these quantities show a significant volume dependence, in particular those sensitive to pion degrees of freedom, and the crossover transition is generally observed to become smoother in finite volume. These findings are relevant for the effective equation of state used in fluid dynamical simulations of heavy-ion collisions and efforts to extract the freeze out properties of heavy-ion collisions with susceptibilities involving electric charge and strangeness.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that finite system volume significantly affects thermodynamic quantities in hot and dense strongly interacting matter, particularly those sensitive to pion degrees of freedom, and that the crossover transition becomes smoother in finite volume; however, this conclusion is conditional on the specific implementation of a lower momentum cut-off related to the de Broglie wavelength, which is an approximation that neglects surface and curvature effects.

## Method

The authors employ the parity-doublet Chiral Mean Field (CMF) model, which includes both hadronic and deconfined quark degrees of freedom with a Polyakov-loop extension, to compute thermodynamic quantities at zero chemical potentials. Finite volume effects are incorporated by introducing a lower momentum cut-off p_min = pi/R in all momentum integrals, where R is the radius of the spherical volume, based on the argument that the maximum de Broglie wavelength of a particle cannot exceed twice the system size. The results are compared between the full CMF model and a purely hadronic version without excluded volume effects to disentangle contributions from confined versus deconfined degrees of freedom.

## Key numbers

- System volumes correspond to radii R = 2, 4, 5, 8 fm (spherical volume V = 4/3 pi R^3), with the largest radius of 10 fm mentioned from ALICE Collaboration measurements
- The freeze-out volume radii from ALICE Collaboration are as large as 10 fm
- The volume of homogeneity in UrQMD simulations for lead-lead collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 62.4 to 2760 GeV varies from 50 to 250 fm^3
- Excluded volumes used in the model: mesons v_M = 1/8 fm^3, baryons v_B = 1 fm^3
- The speed of sound squared (c_s^2) shows a minimum near T ~ 150 MeV in the full CMF model, with smaller volumes leading to increased c_s^2 values
- The ratio chi_4^Q/chi_2^Q shows strong suppression at low temperatures even for the largest finite system size considered (R = 8 fm)
- A peak in chi_11^{BQ} appears at approximately T = 190 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous finite-volume studies using the Polyakov loop extended Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (PNJL) model by Bhattacharyya (2015) and the hadron resonance gas model, now within the CMF framework that incorporates both hadronic and quark degrees of freedom. The results are relevant for fluid dynamical simulations of heavy-ion collisions and for efforts to extract freeze-out properties from experimental susceptibility measurements, as finite volume effects may complicate comparisons with lattice QCD calculations that are performed in finite volumes. The paper also contrasts finite system volume effects with excluded volume effects studied by Vovchenko (2014, 2016) and Alba (2016), noting that the largest finite-volume effects come from bosonic pions rather than baryons with large hard-core volumes.
