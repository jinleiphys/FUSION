# Gyrohydrodynamics: Relativistic spinful fluid with strong vorticity

> We develop a relativistic (quasi-)hydrodynamic framework, dubbed the gyrohydrodynamics, to describe fluid dynamics of many-body systems with spin under strong vorticity based on entropy-current analysis. This framework generalizes the recently-developed spin hydrodynamics to the regime where the spin density is at the leading order in derivatives but suppressed by another small parameter, the Planck constant $\hbar$, due to its quantum nature. Our analysis shows that the complete first-order constitutive relations of gyrohydrodynamics involve seventeen transport coefficients and are highly anisotropic.

## Key claim

The paper develops a relativistic quasi-hydrodynamic framework called "gyrohydrodynamics" that describes spinful fluids under strong vorticity, where vorticity is treated as zeroth-order in derivatives (rather than first-order as in standard spin hydrodynamics) while spin density is suppressed by the Planck constant \(\hbar\) due to its quantum nature. The framework generalizes spin hydrodynamics to a regime where spin density is at leading order in derivatives but suppressed by \(\hbar\), and the complete first-order constitutive relations involve seventeen transport coefficients and are highly anisotropic.

## Method

The authors employ an entropy-current analysis based on the second law of thermodynamics, using a power-counting scheme where spatial components of thermal vorticity \(\omega_{\mu\nu}\) and spin potential \(\mu^{\nu\rho}\) are counted as \(O(\partial^0)\), while their difference and other gradients are counted as \(O(\partial^1)\), and spin density is suppressed by \(\hbar\) as \(O(\hbar)\). They parametrize the energy-momentum tensor, spin current, and charge current in terms of the fluid velocity \(u^\mu\), vorticity direction \(\hat{\omega}^\mu\), and the transverse projection tensor \(\Xi^{\mu\nu}\), then derive constitutive relations by requiring \(\partial_\mu s^\mu \geq 0\) up to second order in derivatives and spin.

## Key numbers

* Seventeen transport coefficients appear in the complete first-order constitutive relations: three bulk viscosities (\(\zeta_{\parallel}, \zeta_{\perp}, \zeta_{\times}\)), four shear viscosities (\(\eta_{\parallel}, \eta_{\perp}, \eta_{H_{\parallel}}, \eta_{H_{\perp}}\)), three rotational viscosities (\(\gamma_{\parallel}, \gamma_{\perp}, \gamma_{H}\)), four cross viscosities (\(\xi_{\parallel}, \zeta_{H\parallel}, \zeta_{H\perp}, \xi_H\)), and three conductivities (\(\kappa_{\perp}, \kappa_{\parallel}, \kappa_H\))
* Seven of the seventeen transport coefficients are Hall-like and non-dissipative: \(\eta_{H_\perp}, \eta_{H_\parallel}, \gamma_H, \zeta_{H\parallel}, \zeta_{H\perp}, \xi_H, \kappa_H\)
* The dissipative coefficients must satisfy positivity constraints including \(\zeta_{\perp} \geq 0\), \(\zeta_{\parallel} \geq 0\), \(\zeta_{\perp}\zeta_{\parallel} - \zeta_{\times}^2 \geq 0\), \(\eta_{\perp} \geq 0\), \(\eta_{\parallel} \geq 0\), \(\gamma_{\perp} \geq 0\), \(\gamma_{\parallel} \geq 0\), \(\eta_{\parallel}\gamma_{\parallel} - \xi_{\parallel}^2 \geq 0\), \(\kappa_{\perp} \geq 0\), \(\kappa_{\parallel} \geq 0\)

## Context

This work extends the recently-developed spin hydrodynamics framework (Hattori 2019, Bhadury 2020, Fukushima 2020, Gallegos 2021, Hongo 2021, and others) to a regime where vorticity is treated as zeroth-order in derivatives, motivated by the discovery of strong spin polarization in quark-gluon plasma from heavy-ion collisions (STAR 2017). The resulting anisotropic constitutive relations bear similarity to relativistic magnetohydrodynamics (Huang 2011, Grozdanov 2016, Hongo 2020), but unlike magnetohydrodynamics, gyrohydrodynamics exhibits Hall-like transports even in the strict hydrodynamic limit, and these Hall coefficients are even under charge conjugation and can be non-zero for neutral fluids. The framework enables quantitative understanding of spin transports in quark-gluon plasma, supernova explosions, and may help resolve the sign problem of local spin polarization in heavy-ion collisions.
