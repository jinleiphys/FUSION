# Mapping Parton Distributions of Hadrons with Lattice QCD

> The strong force which binds hadrons is described by the theory of quantum chromodynamics (QCD). Determining the character and manifestations of QCD is one of the most important and challenging outstanding issues necessary for a comprehensive understanding of the structure of hadrons. Within the context of the QCD parton picture, the parton distribution functions (PDFs) have been remarkably successful in describing a wide variety of processes. However, these PDFs have generally been confined to the description of collinear partons within the hadron. New experiments and facilities provide the opportunity to additionally explore the three-dimensional structure of hadrons, which can be described by generalized parton distributions (GPDs), for example. In recent years, a breakthrough was made in calculating the Bjorken-$x$ dependence of PDFs in lattice QCD by using large-momentum effective theory (LaMET) and other similar frameworks. The breakthrough has led to the emergence and rapid development of direct calculations of Bjorken-$x$--dependent structure. In this review article, we show some of the recent progress made in lattice QCD in PDFs and GPDs and discuss future challenges.

## Key claim

This review article surveys recent progress in lattice QCD calculations of Bjorken‑$x$‑dependent parton distribution functions (PDFs) and generalized parton distributions (GPDs), emphasizing that while large‑momentum effective theory (LaMET) and related frameworks have enabled direct calculations of the $x$ dependence, the results remain subject to systematic uncertainties from finite volume, finite lattice spacing, and matching at finite hadron momentum.

## Method

The paper reviews two primary lattice approaches for extracting $x$‑dependent parton structure: the LaMET (quasi‑PDF) method and the pseudo‑PDF method. In LaMET, equal‑time spatial correlators (quasi‑PDFs) are computed on the lattice at finite hadron momentum $P_z$, then Fourier‑transformed and matched to light‑cone PDFs via a perturbative kernel up to next‑to‑next‑to‑leading order (NNLO). The pseudo‑PDF method constructs reduced Ioffe‑time pseudo‑distributions (RpITDs) from matrix elements of nonlocal operators, canceling ultraviolet divergences through double ratios, and fits the RpITDs to phenomenologically motivated functional forms. Both methods require renormalization, with improved schemes such as hybrid‑ratio and self‑renormalization used to handle linear divergences and renormalon ambiguities.

## Key numbers

* Maximum hadron momentum $P_z^\text{max}$ used in LaMET calculations ranges 2–3 GeV, with power corrections suppressed as $\mathcal{O}(\Lambda_\text{QCD}^2/(x^2 P_z^2))$ and $\mathcal{O}(\Lambda_\text{QCD}^2/((1-x)^2 P_z^2))$, where $\Lambda_\text{QCD}$ is 0.3–0.5 GeV depending on the lattice.
* The hybrid‑renormalization scheme separates short distances ($z < z_s \approx 0.3$ fm) from long distances ($z \geq z_s$), using an exponential factor $e^{(\delta m + m_0)(z - z_s)}$ to remove linear divergence and renormalon ambiguity.
* NNLO matching has been incorporated in lattice calculations of the valence pion PDF (Gao 2021), while most other lattice PDF calculations use next‑to‑leading order (NLO) matching.
* The functional form for self‑renormalization includes a linear divergence term $\frac{kz}{a\ln(a\Lambda_\text{QCD})}$ and a logarithmic term $\frac{3C_F}{4\pi\beta_0} \ln\left(\frac{\ln(a\Lambda_\text{QCD})}{\ln(\Lambda_\text{QCD}/\mu)}\right)$.

## Context

This review extends earlier summaries of lattice PDF calculations (Lin 2017, Constantinou 2020, Ji 2020) by focusing on the rapid developments since the 2013 LaMET breakthrough (Ji 2013). It covers applications to nucleon, pion, kaon, and $\Delta^+$ baryon PDFs, as well as extensions to twist‑three PDFs, gluon distributions, and GPDs. The work enables future combination of lattice‑QCD results with experimental data in global‑QCD analyses, particularly for kinematic regions where experimental data are sparse (small‑$x$, large‑$x$, heavy‑flavor PDFs), and for three‑dimensional hadron structure studies at the Electron‑Ion Collider.
