# Properties of the largest fragment in multifragmentation: a canonical thermodynamic calculation

> Many calculations for the production of light and intermediate particles resulting from heavy ion collisions at intermediate energies exist. Calculations of properties of the largest fragment resulting from multifragmentation are rare. In this paper we compute these properties and compare them with the data for the case of gold on carbon. We use the canonical thermodynamic model. The model also gives a bimodal distribution for the largest fragment in a narrow energy range.

# Properties of the largest fragment in multifragmentation: a canonical thermodynamic calculation

## Key claim

The canonical thermodynamic model reproduces the average charge of the largest fragment from multifragmentation of gold-on-carbon projectile-like fragments across a wide range of excitation energies, but only if the freeze-out density is allowed to vary with excitation energy rather than held fixed. The model also predicts a bimodal distribution for the largest fragment in a narrow energy window, which the authors connect to a liquid-gas phase transition in finite systems.

## Method

The authors used the canonical thermodynamic model of nuclear disassembly, computing the partition function for a projectile-like fragment (PLF) with given charge Z and neutron number N at a specified temperature and freeze-out density. They calculated the probability distribution of the maximum charge Z_max by constructing restricted partition functions where all composites with charge greater than a given threshold are excluded, then taking differences between successive thresholds. The freeze-out density was parameterized as a function of excitation energy per nucleon to match the experimental data from the EOS collaboration for Au+C reactions.

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out density parameterization: $\rho/\rho_0 = a + b \exp(-c (E^*/A_0))$ with $a=0.17$, $b=0.83$, and $c=0.417$ MeV$^{-1}$
- Fixed freeze-out density calculations used $\rho/\rho_0 = 0.25$ (typical canonical model value) and $\rho/\rho_0 = 0.39$ (value used in EOS collaboration models)
- Excitation energy range covered: from less than 2 MeV per nucleon to 10 MeV per nucleon
- The largest composite included in set 1: 5 isotopes for mass numbers 5-40, 7 isotopes for mass numbers above 40; set 2: 5 isotopes for mass 5-9, 7 isotopes for mass 10-40, 9 isotopes for mass above 40

## Context

This work extends the canonical thermodynamic model (Das Gupta and Mekjian, 1998; Das et al., 2005) to compute properties of the largest fragment, which had rarely been calculated despite extensive work on intermediate mass fragment and light charged particle yields. The calculations are compared with data from the EOS collaboration (Elliott et al., 2003; Hauger et al., 2000, 1998) for Au+C reactions. The bimodality in the largest fragment distribution connects to discussions of liquid-gas phase transitions in finite systems, previously studied using lattice gas or Ising models (Gulminelli and Chomaz, 2005; Krishnamachari et al., 1996; Pleimling and Selke, 2000; Binder, 2003), but here addressed with a nuclear-physics-specific thermodynamic model. The work enables future calculations for other projectile-target systems (La+C, Kr+C) and further exploration of the relationship between bimodality and first-order phase transitions.
