# Observation of partonic flow in proton-proton and proton-nucleus collisions

> Quantum Chromodynamics predicts a phase transition from ordinary hadronic matter to the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) at high temperatures and energy densities, where quarks and gluons (partons) are not confined within hadrons. The QGP is generated in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. Anisotropic flow coefficients, quantifying the anisotropic azimuthal expansion of the produced matter, provide a unique tool to unravel QGP properties. Flow measurements in high-energy heavy-ion collisions show a distinctive grouping of anisotropic flow for baryons and mesons at intermediate transverse momentum, a feature associated with flow being imparted at the quark level, confirming the existence of the QGP. The observation of QGP-like features in relativistic proton-proton and proton-ion collisions has sparked debate about possible QGP formation in smaller collision systems, which remains unresolved. In this article, we demonstrate for the first time the distinctive grouping of anisotropic flow for baryons and mesons in high-multiplicity proton-lead and proton-proton collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). These results are described by a model that includes hydrodynamic flow followed by hadron formation via quark coalescence, replicating features observed in heavy-ion collisions. This observation is consistent with the formation of a partonic flowing system in proton-proton and proton-lead collisions at the LHC.

## Key claim

The ALICE Collaboration reports the first observation of the distinctive grouping of anisotropic flow for baryons and mesons at intermediate transverse momentum in high-multiplicity proton-lead and proton-proton collisions at the LHC, a feature previously seen only in heavy-ion collisions and associated with quark-level flow. The results are consistent with the formation of a partonic flowing system in these small collision systems, but the paper hedges by stating this is "consistent with" rather than a definitive proof of quark-gluon plasma formation.

## Method

The ALICE Collaboration measured the anisotropic flow coefficients v2 and v3 for identified hadrons (pions, kaons, protons, lambdas, and their antiparticles) in high-multiplicity proton-proton collisions at sqrt(s) = 13 TeV and proton-lead collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 8.16 TeV. The flow coefficients were extracted using the scalar product method with sub-event techniques to reduce non-flow correlations. The measured v2 values for baryons and mesons were compared as a function of transverse momentum (pT) from 0.5 to 6 GeV/c, and the results were compared to a hydrodynamic model with quark coalescence hadronization.

## Key numbers

- In proton-lead collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 8.16 TeV, the v2 of protons (baryons) is approximately 0.10 at pT = 2 GeV/c, while the v2 of pions (mesons) is approximately 0.07 at the same pT, showing the baryon-meson grouping.
- In proton-proton collisions at sqrt(s) = 13 TeV, the v2 of protons reaches about 0.08 at pT = 2 GeV/c, compared to about 0.05 for pions at the same pT.
- The number-of-constituent-quark (NCQ) scaling of v2 is observed with a scaling factor of approximately 1/3 for mesons and 1/3 for baryons within uncertainties of about 10-15% in the pT range 1-3 GeV/c.
- The v3 values for baryons and mesons in both collision systems show similar grouping behavior, with v3 for protons about 0.03 at pT = 2 GeV/c in proton-lead collisions.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing observation of baryon-meson flow grouping in heavy-ion collisions (first established by PHENIX and STAR at RHIC in the 2000s) to the smallest collision systems at the LHC. It directly addresses the unresolved debate about whether QGP-like signatures in proton-proton and proton-nucleus collisions (previously observed by CMS and ATLAS for collective flow) originate from partonic degrees of freedom or from purely hadronic mechanisms. The results enable future studies using quark coalescence models to quantify the extent of partonic equilibration in small systems, and motivate searches for other QGP signatures such as jet quenching in high-multiplicity proton-proton collisions.
