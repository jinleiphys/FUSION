# Neutrino Astrophysics

> A review of neutrino astrophysics is presented, including solar and atmospheric neutrinos; neutrino mass and oscillations; the supernova mechanism, supernova neutrino production, and associated nucleosynthesis; cosmological neutrinos and Big Bang nucleosynthesis; neutrino cooling and associated limits on neutrino properties; and high-energy astrophysical neutrinos.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that the "solar neutrino problem", the long-standing discrepancy between predicted and detected solar neutrino fluxes, was resolved not by errors in solar models but by the discovery that neutrinos have mass and undergo flavor oscillations, particularly through the matter-enhanced MSW effect, which transforms about two-thirds of electron neutrinos produced in the Sun into heavy-flavor neutrinos by the time they reach Earth.

## Method

The paper surveys the history and methodology of solar neutrino detection, including radiochemical experiments (Homestake chlorine detector, SAGE, GALLEX/GNO) that measure integrated neutrino capture, and real-time water Cherenkov detectors (Kamiokande, Super-Kamiokande, SNO) and liquid scintillator detectors (Borexino) that record individual neutrino scattering events. SNO's unique design using heavy water allowed simultaneous measurement of charged-current (sensitive only to electron neutrinos), neutral-current (sensitive to all active flavors), and elastic scattering channels, enabling direct determination of both the total solar neutrino flux and its flavor composition. The paper also describes the theoretical framework of vacuum and matter-enhanced neutrino oscillations (MSW mechanism) and the standard solar model used to predict neutrino fluxes.

## Context

This review synthesizes the results from four decades of solar neutrino experiments, from Davis's Homestake chlorine experiment (2002 Nobel Prize) through SNO and Borexino, which collectively demonstrated that neutrino oscillations, not solar model errors, explain the observed flux deficits. The paper extends the discussion of neutrino oscillations first proposed by Pontecorvo (1958) and the MSW matter-enhanced mechanism developed by Wolfenstein, Mikheyev, and Smirnov (1985). The unresolved solar abundance problem between high-metallicity models (GS98-SFII) that agree with helioseismology and low-metallicity models (AGSS09-SFII) that use better photospheric modeling remains an open question. The discovery that neutrinos have mass and mix flavors implies the Standard Model of particle physics is incomplete, and enables future searches for matter effects in supernova neutrinos and cosmological neutrino studies.
