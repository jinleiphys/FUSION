# Signatures of thermalized charm quarks in all charged flow observables

> A long standing question in the field of heavy-ion collisions is whether charm quarks are thermalized within the Quark Gluon Plasma. In recent years, progress in lattice QCD simulations has led to reliable results for the equation of state of a system of 2+1 flavors (up, down, and strange) and 2+1+1 flavors (up, down, strange, and charm). We find that the equation of state strongly affects differential flow harmonics and a preference is seen for thermalized charm quarks at the LHC. Predictions are also made for the event-plane correlations at RHIC AuAu $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV collisions, and the scaling of differential flow observables and factorization breaking for all charged particles at LHC PbPb $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=5.02$ TeV collisions compared to LHC XeXe $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=5.44$ TeV collisions, which could be useful in answering the question: are charm quarks thermalized?

## Key claim

The paper argues that the equation of state (EoS) including thermalized charm quarks (2+1+1 flavors) significantly improves the fit to differential flow harmonics \(v_n(p_T)\) at LHC Run 2 energies compared to the EoS without thermalized charm (2+1 flavors), providing a new method to test charm quark thermalization using only all-charged-particle flow observables rather than D-meson measurements.

## Method

The authors performed event-by-event relativistic viscous hydrodynamics simulations using the v-USPhydro code, with initial conditions from the TRENTO model tuned to IP-Glasma. They compared two lattice QCD equations of state: a 2+1-flavor EoS (up, down, strange quarks thermalized) and a 2+1+1-flavor EoS (including thermalized charm quarks), both parameterized from Wuppertal-Budapest collaboration data and merged with a Hadron Resonance Gas model using the PDG2016+ particle list. The simulations were run for AuAu collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200\) GeV, PbPb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=5.02\) TeV, and XeXe collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=5.44\) TeV, with 35,000-50,000 initial conditions per energy and shear viscosity tuned separately for each EoS to match integrated flow harmonics.

## Key numbers

- The trace anomaly of the 2+1+1-flavor EoS exceeds that of the 2+1-flavor EoS by 14%, 40%, 70%, and 102% at temperatures \(T=300\), 400, 500, and 600 MeV respectively.
- At PbPb 5.02 TeV, the shear viscosity used was \(\eta/s=0.047\) for the 2+1-flavor EoS and \(\eta/s=0.04\) for the 2+1+1-flavor EoS; at RHIC AuAu 200 GeV, \(\eta/s=0.05\) was used for both EoS.
- The \(\langle p_T\rangle\) of protons is \(\sim 3\%\) higher for the 2+1-flavor EoS at XeXe 5.44 TeV and \(\sim 3-5\%\) higher for the 2+1-flavor EoS at PbPb 5.02 TeV.
- The 2+1+1-flavor EoS produces a significantly larger \(v_2(p_T)\) in peripheral PbPb collisions that matches ALICE data, while the 2+1-flavor EoS fails in peripheral collisions.
- For the ratio \(v_2(p_T,Xe)/v_2(p_T,Pb)\) in 30-70% centrality, the 2+1-flavor EoS predicts an increase while the 2+1+1-flavor EoS predicts a suppression.
- In 0-5% central collisions, the factorization breaking \(r_2(p_T^a,p_T^b)\) shows opposite scaling with system size: the 2+1-flavor EoS predicts a decrease from PbPb to XeXe, while the 2+1+1-flavor EoS predicts a significant increase.

## Context

This work extends previous hydrodynamic analyses (Alba 2017) that found negligible effects of charm thermalization on standard observables like spectra and integrated flow harmonics. It contradicts the common assumption that charm quarks only influence thermodynamics near \(T\sim 1.3\) GeV, showing instead that thermalized charm affects the EoS at temperatures below \(T\lesssim 300\) MeV reached at both RHIC and LHC. The paper proposes a new approach distinct from previous studies (Moore 2004, Rapp 2009, Cao 2011) that focused on D-meson flow and nuclear modification factors; instead, it uses all charged hadron flow observables in the soft sector (\(p_T<3\) GeV). The predictions for system-size scaling between PbPb and XeXe collisions and for factorization breaking provide testable signatures that could confirm whether the EoS probed at LHC matches that of the quark epoch in the Early Universe.
