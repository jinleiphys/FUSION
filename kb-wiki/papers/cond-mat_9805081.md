# New approach to Borel summation of divergent series and critical exponent estimates for an N-vector cubic model in three dimensions from five-loop εexpansions

> A new approach to summation of divergent field-theoretical series is suggested. It is based on the Borel transformation combined with a conformal mapping and does not imply the exact asymptotic parameters to be known. The method is tested on functions expanded in their asymptotic power series. It is applied to estimating the critical exponent values for an N-vector field model, describing magnetic and structural phase transitions in cubic and tetragonal crystals, from five-loop εexpansions.

# New approach to Borel summation of divergent series and critical exponent estimates for an N-vector cubic model in three dimensions from five-loop ε expansions

## Key claim
The authors propose a Borel summation method combined with conformal mapping that does not require knowledge of the exact asymptotic parameters (a₀, b₀) of the divergent series, relying instead on the stability of the processed result under variation of these parameters. The method yields critical exponent estimates for the cubic (anisotropic) N-vector model in three dimensions from five-loop ε expansions, where conventional Padé, Padé-Borel, and Padé-Borel-Leroy techniques fail due to the irregular (non-alternating) nature of the exponent series.

## Method
The authors apply a Borel transformation with conformal mapping ω = (√(x+1)-1)/(√(x+1)+1) to the divergent series, introducing an additional parameter λ chosen by minimizing the relative contribution of the last accounted term. Rather than fixing the asymptotic parameters a and b to their exact (unknown) values, the method varies a and b over a wide range and identifies "plateau" regions where the processed result remains stable; the final estimate is obtained by averaging over this stability domain. The procedure is tested on model functions with known asymptotic behavior and on the ground state energy of the unharmonic oscillator before being applied to the five-loop RG series for the cubic model in (4-ε) dimensions at ε=1.

## Key numbers
- For the unharmonic oscillator ground state energy at g=1, the estimate for L=12 is 1.392351, compared to the exact value 1.392352; for L=8 the estimate is 1.392376, which is one order closer to the exact value than the number 1.391655±0.004562 found using Wynn's ε-algorithm.
- For the cubic model at N=2 in three dimensions: η = 0.0350±0.0003, ν = 0.6277±0.0010, γ = 1.2358±0.0040.
- For N=3: η = 0.0375±0.0005, ν = 0.6997±0.0024, γ = 1.3746±0.0020.
- For N=4: η = 0.0365±0.0005, ν = 0.7225±0.0022, γ = 1.4208±0.0030.
- For N=5: η = 0.0358±0.0004, ν = 0.7290±0.0016, γ = 1.4305±0.0040.
- For N=6: η = 0.0354±0.0003, ν = 0.7301±0.0016, γ = 1.4322±0.0040.
- For N=∞ (Ising model with equilibrium magnetic impurities): η = 0.0350±0.0003, ν = 0.7108±0.0010, γ = 1.3993±0.0020.
- The scaling relation γ_sc = ν(2-η) differs from the directly computed γ by no more than 0.2% across all N values.

## Context
This work extends the five-loop RG calculations of Kleinert and Schulte-Frohlinde (1995) for the cubic model in (4-ε) dimensions, who found the critical dimensionality N_c to be less than three in three dimensions, implying control by the cubic fixed point. The method addresses the failure of conventional resummation techniques (Padé, Padé-Borel, Padé-Borel-Leroy) for the irregular critical exponent series, and avoids the need for exact asymptotic parameters which are known only for O(N)-symmetric models (Le Guillou and Zinn-Justin, 1977; Brézin et al., 1977) and the anisotropic quartic quantum oscillator. The results for N=2 agree excellently with earlier Ising model estimates (η=0.035±0.002, ν=0.628±0.001) from Guida and Zinn-Justin (1998), and with the results of Kleinert (1995) using a substantially different method. The approach enables critical exponent estimation for anisotropic models describing phase transitions in real cubic and tetragonal crystals, and may be applicable to divergent series in QCD and QED where conventional techniques fail.
