# Cooling of the Cassiopeia A neutron star and the effect of diffusive nuclear burning

> The study of how neutron stars cool over time can provide invaluable insights into fundamental physics such as the nuclear equation of state and superconductivity and superfluidity. A critical relation in neutron star cooling is the one between observed surface temperature and interior temperature. This relation is determined by the composition of the neutron star envelope and can be influenced by the process of diffusive nuclear burning (DNB). We calculate models of envelopes that include DNB and find that DNB can lead to a rapidly changing envelope composition which can be relevant for understanding the long-term cooling behavior of neutron stars. We also report on analysis of the latest temperature measurements of the young neutron star in the Cassiopeia A supernova remnant. The 13 Chandra observations over 18 years show that the neutron star's temperature is decreasing at a rate of 2-3 percent per decade, and this rapid cooling can be explained by the presence of a proton superconductor and neutron superfluid in the core of the star.

# Cooling of the Cassiopeia A Neutron Star and the Effect of Diffusive Nuclear Burning

## Key claim
Diffusive nuclear burning (DNB) can cause rapidly changing envelope compositions in neutron stars, which affects the relation between surface and interior temperature over long timescales; the observed 2–3% per decade cooling of the Cassiopeia A neutron star can be explained by proton superconductivity and neutron superfluidity in the core, but the absolute temperature normalization depends on the poorly constrained mass and radius of the star.

## Method
The authors calculated new envelope models that include diffusive nuclear burning (DNB) for H-He and He-C compositions, producing updated surface temperature–interior temperature (Ts–Tb) relations. They then analyzed 13 Chandra ACIS-S observations of the Cassiopeia A neutron star over 18 years, fitting carbon atmosphere spectra to extract surface temperatures and cooling rates, with either fixed or variable interstellar absorption column (NH).

## Key numbers
- Cooling rate of the Cassiopeia A neutron star: 2.1 ± 0.2% per decade (1σ) for constant NH, and 2.7 ± 0.3% per decade (1σ) for varying NH, from 13 Chandra observations over 18 years
- Best-fit mass and radius from spectral fitting: M = 1.65 Msun and R = 12.9 km, though these are not strongly constrained
- Best-fit interstellar absorption column for constant NH fit: NH = (1.67 ± 0.03) × 10^22 cm^-2
- Estimated age of the Cassiopeia A neutron star: ≈340 years, from a supernova date of 1681 ± 19
- For accretion rates above 10^-20 Msun yr^-1, hydrogen builds up sufficiently to produce a hydrogen atmosphere after a few hundred years; for lower rates, a carbon spectrum results
- Initial hydrogen in a H-C envelope is consumed within an hour after neutron star formation due to high temperatures

## Context
This work extends prior studies of neutron star cooling by incorporating diffusive nuclear burning into envelope models, which was not considered in earlier Ts–Tb relations by Potekhin et al. (1997, 2003) for pure compositions or by Beznogov et al. (2016) for mixed compositions. The cooling rate measurements update previous analyses of the Cassiopeia A neutron star by Heinke and Ho (2010), Elshamouty et al. (2013), and Ho et al. (2015), and are consistent with upper limits from Posselt and Pavlov (2018). The rapid cooling enables inference of the density dependence of critical temperatures for proton superconductivity and neutron superfluidity, as previously modeled by Page et al. (2011) and Shternin et al. (2011). Future work will use these results to improve constraints on nuclear equation of state models, while ongoing calibration of Chandra ACIS detectors remains important for measurement accuracy.
