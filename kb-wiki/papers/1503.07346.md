# Optimising the neutron environment of Radiation Portal Monitors: a computational optimisation study

> Efficient and reliable detection of radiological or nuclear threats is a crucial part of national and international efforts to prevent terrorist activities. Radiation Portal Monitors (RPMs), which are deployed worldwide, are intended to interdict smuggled fissile material by detecting emissions of neutrons and gamma rays. However, considering the range and variety of threat sources, vehicular and shielding scenarios, and that only a small signature is present, it is important that the design of the RPMs allows these signatures to be accurately differentiated from the environmental background. Using Monte-Carlo neutron-transport simulations of a model helium-3 detector system we have conducted a parameter study to identify the optimum combination of detector shielding and collimation that maximises the sensitivity of RPMs. These structures, which could be simply and cost-effectively added to existing RPMs, can improve the detector response by more than a factor of two relative to an unmodified, bare design. Furthermore, optimisation of the air gap surrounding the helium tubes also improves detector efficiency.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that simple, cost-effective additions of boronated polythene shielding and collimation to existing Radiation Portal Monitors can improve detector response by more than a factor of two relative to an unmodified bare design, but only when the background count is reduced to a relatively greater extent than the threat count.

## Method
Monte-Carlo neutron-transport simulations using MCNPX were performed on a model helium-3 detector system to evaluate the response to both environmental neutron background and a californium-252 threat source. A parameter study varied collimator depth, forward shielding thickness, collimator hole profile, and hole packing density to identify the optimum combination that minimises the detectable threat source strength. Additional simulations optimised the air gap surrounding the helium tubes to improve detector efficiency through albedo cavity effects.

## Key numbers
- The minimum detectable threat source strength for the optimised configuration (5 cm collimator depth, 2 cm extra forward shielding, cylindrical holes occupying 70-80% of exposed surface) was approximately 1.2 × 10³ n/s, compared to 2638 n/s for the bare unshielded system and 1286 n/s for the shielded detector with 5 cm collimator.
- The background normalisation factor N_bkgrd was calculated as 5950 background neutrons per second, based on a nominal neutron flux of 100 n m⁻² s⁻¹ at the midpoint between detectors.
- The helium-3 tubes in the model have length 125 cm, radius 2.52 cm (0.1 cm stainless steel tank), with helium density of 2.5 × 10⁻⁵ atoms per barn-cm, and total helium volume of 2296.12 cm³.
- The detector panels are 156 cm in length and 135 cm in height, spaced 300 cm apart, with each panel containing 13 one-atmosphere helium-3 tubes.
- The threat source was a ²⁵²Cf neutron point source with emission rate of 2000 n/s moving at 2 m s⁻¹ with 2 s acquisition time.

## Context
This work extends prior optimisation studies of helium-3 detector response within polythene box configurations by Tomanin (2013), Reesczirr (2012), and Kouzes (2008), but uses an alternative generic RPM design with performance optimisation for threat source relative to neutron background through shielding and collimation enhancements. The study enables practical improvements to existing deployed RPM systems worldwide, which are critical for detecting smuggled fissile material at ports and border crossings, and addresses the current scarcity of helium-3 by suggesting cost-effective modifications rather than alternative detection technologies.
