# Charge radii of Cl isotopes from x-ray spectroscopy of muonic atoms

> Nuclear charge radii are vital for nuclear and atomic physics, the determination of fundamental constants, and searches for new physics. Muonic atoms, where a single negative muon orbits a nucleus, are sensitive tools for determining nuclear radii due to the large wavefunction overlap of the muon and nucleus. Here we report on a new measurement of the $2, 3, 4p\to1s$ x-ray energies in muonic $^{35,37}$Cl with uncertainties reaching 18 ppm. By employing a large-scale germanium detector array, it was possible to extract these energies from a high statistics dataset using highly enriched samples of only a few tens of milligrams. Combining these results with state-of-the-art atomic and nuclear theory input, the charge radii of the stable chlorine isotopes were determined to be $R(^{35}\text{Cl}) = 3.3333(23)~fm$ and $R(^{37}\text{Cl}) = 3.3444(23)~fm$. This is an order of magnitude more precise and significantly different from previously tabulated values. Our new values solve a discrepancy observed for the charge radius difference in mirror nuclei, agreeing with the overall global trend. The charge radius difference $δ\langle r^2 \rangle (^{37}\text{Cl} - {^{35}\text{Cl}}) = -0.0776(64)~fm^2$ we extract is 25 times more precise than the previous values. This precision is crucial for establishing reference values for future laser spectroscopy measurements of radioactive isotopes.

## Key claim

The charge radii of stable chlorine isotopes extracted from muonic x-ray spectroscopy are an order of magnitude more precise and significantly different from previously tabulated electron-scattering values, resolving a discrepancy in the mirror-nuclei radius-difference trend. The results depend on a model-dependent correction factor (V2) derived from energy density functional calculations rather than from electron-scattering data, introducing a systematic uncertainty of about 0.05%.

## Method

Muonic x-ray energies for the 2p→1s, 3p→1s, and 4p→1s transitions in 35Cl and 37Cl were measured using the GIANT high-purity germanium detector array (19 crystals) at the πE1 beamline of the Paul Scherrer Institute, with enriched samples of a few tens of milligrams. The transition energies were extracted via Bayesian inference fits accounting for fine-structure splitting, with calibration performed using orthogonal distance regression and non-parametric bootstrapping to handle digitizer non-linearity. A hybrid theoretical framework combining QED calculations with the Barrett formalism was developed for medium-mass muonic atoms (3 ≤ Z ≲ 30), and the Barrett equivalent radii were converted to RMS charge radii using V2 correction factors computed from the BSkG family of energy density functionals.

## Key numbers

- R(35Cl) = 3.3333(23) fm (BSkG4 model), compared to literature value 3.3880(170) fm from electron scattering
- R(37Cl) = 3.3444(23) fm (BSkG4 model), compared to literature value 3.3840(170) fm from electron scattering
- δ⟨r²⟩(37Cl − 35Cl) = −0.0776(64) fm², which is 25 times more precise than previous values
- R(37Cl) − R(35Cl) = 0.01163(95) fm (BSkG4 model)
- Muonic x-ray energies measured with uncertainties reaching 18 ppm; for example, the 2p→1s transition in 35Cl is 578.874(16) keV
- V2 correction factors: V2,35Cl = 1.28328(65) and V2,37Cl = 1.28382(65), with ~97% correlation between isotopes

## Context

This work extends the revival of muonic-atom spectroscopy beyond the lightest elements (H, D, He studied by Pohl (2010) and Krauth (2021)) and heavier systems (Zr, Sn, Pb studied by Sun (2025) and Beyer (2025)), providing the first extraction of absolute charge radii from muonic x rays with below-per-mille precision in 30 years for medium-mass nuclei. The new radii solve a discrepancy observed for the charge radius difference in mirror nuclei that was identified by Ohayon (2024), reducing the χ²ν of the mirror shift fit from 2.15 to 1.01, and establish reference values for future laser spectroscopy measurements of radioactive chlorine isotopes via King-plot analysis.
