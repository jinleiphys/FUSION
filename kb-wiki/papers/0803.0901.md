# Heavy Quark Diffusion as a Probe of the Quark-Gluon Plasma

> We report on recent research on the properties of elementary particle matter governed by the strong force at high temperatures, where QCD predicts hadrons to dissolve into the Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP). After a short introduction to the basic elements of QCD in the vacuum, most notably quark confinement and mass generation, we discuss how these phenomena relate to phase changes in strongly interacting matter at high temperature. We briefly review the main experimental findings at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) which provide strong evidence that a QGP has been produced, with unprecedentedly small viscosity and large opacity. We discuss how heavy quarks (charm and bottom) can be utilized to quantitatively probe the transport properties of a strongly coupled QGP (sQGP). The large heavy-quark (HQ) mass allows to set up a Brownian motion approach, which can serve to evaluate different approaches for HQ interactions in the sQGP. The implementation of lattice QCD based HQ potentials generates pre-hadronic resonance structures in HQ scattering in the medium, leading to large interaction rates and small diffusion coefficients. The resonance correlations are strongest close to the critical temperature (T_c), suggesting an intimate connection to the hadronization of the QGP. The implementation of HQ transport into Langevin simulations of an expanding QGP fireball at RHIC enables quantitative comparisons with experiment. The extracted HQ diffusion coefficients are employed for schematic estimates of the shear viscosity, corroborating the notion of a strongly-coupled QGP in the vicinity of T_c.

## Key claim

Heavy quarks (charm and bottom) can serve as quantitative probes of the transport properties of a strongly-coupled Quark-Gluon Plasma (sQGP) via a Brownian motion framework, but the extracted diffusion coefficients and their connection to shear viscosity depend on whether the heavy-quark interactions are modeled using perturbative QCD or nonperturbative approaches based on lattice QCD potentials that generate pre-hadronic resonance structures near the critical temperature.

## Method

The authors implement heavy-quark (HQ) interactions in the QGP using both perturbative QCD and nonperturbative approaches, the latter based on lattice QCD computations of the heavy-quark potential that generate resonance structures in HQ scattering off light quarks. These interactions are used to compute transport coefficients (drag and diffusion), which are then implemented into Langevin simulations of an expanding QGP fireball for Au-Au collisions at RHIC, with hadronization treated via a quark coalescence approach at the phase transition to enable comparison with experimental heavy meson and electron decay spectra.

## Key numbers

- Charm quark mass: m_c ≈ 1.5 GeV/c²; bottom quark mass: m_b ≈ 4.5 GeV/c²
- Critical temperature for QGP transition: T_c = 160-190 MeV (from lattice QCD)
- RHIC collision energy: E_cm ≈ 200 A GeV ≈ 40 TeV for Au-Au collisions (A=197)
- Approximately 5000 particles produced in a central Au-Au collision at RHIC
- Fireball lifetime: about 10⁻²² s
- Stefan-Boltzmann limit for energy density with N_f=3: ε_SB ≈ 15.6 T⁴
- Stefan-Boltzmann limit for entropy density: s ≈ 20.8 T³
- String tension: σ ≈ 1 GeV/fm
- Light quark bare masses: m_u,d ≈ 0.005 GeV/c²; strange quark mass: m_s ≈ 0.12 GeV/c²
- Constituent quark mass from condensate: m_q* ≈ 0.4 GeV
- QCD scale: Λ_QCD = 0.2 GeV
- Quark condensate vacuum expectation value: ⟨0|q̄q|0⟩ ≈ (-250 MeV)³, corresponding to about 4 pairs per fm³

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for heavy-quark transport in the QGP, building on lattice QCD computations of the heavy-quark free energy by Kaczmarek (2005) and Petreczky (2004), as well as the equation of state calculations by Cheng (2007). The approach enables quantitative comparison with RHIC experimental data from the PHENIX and STAR collaborations, and the extracted diffusion coefficients provide schematic estimates of shear viscosity that corroborate the strongly-coupled nature of the QGP near T_c, connecting to prior work on quarkonium dissociation by Digal (2001) and Shuryak (2004).
