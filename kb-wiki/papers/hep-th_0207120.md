# Calculating Vacuum Energies in Renormalizable Quantum Field Theories: A New Approach to the Casimir Problem

> The Casimir problem is usually posed as the response of a fluctuating quantum field to externally imposed boundary conditions. In reality, however, no interaction is strong enough to enforce a boundary condition on all frequencies of a fluctuating field. We construct a more physical model of the situation by coupling the fluctuating field to a smooth background potential that implements the boundary condition in a certain limit. To study this problem, we develop general new methods to compute renormalized one--loop quantum energies and energy densities. We use analytic properties of scattering data to compute Green's functions in time--independent background fields at imaginary momenta. Our calculational method is particularly useful for numerical studies of singular limits because it avoids terms that oscillate or require cancellation of exponentially growing and decaying factors. To renormalize, we identify potentially divergent contributions to the Casimir energy with low orders in the Born series to the Green's function. We subtract these contributions and add back the corresponding Feynman diagrams, which we combine with counterterms fixed by imposing standard renormalization conditions on low--order Green's functions. The resulting Casimir energy and energy density are finite functionals for smooth background potentials. In general, however, the Casimir energy diverges in the boundary condition limit. This divergence is real and reflects the infinite energy needed to constrain a fluctuating field on all energy scales; renormalizable quantum field theories have no place for ad hoc surface counterterms. We apply our methods to simple examples to illustrate cases where these subtleties invalidate the conclusions of the boundary condition approach.

## Key claim

The Casimir energy, when computed in a renormalizable quantum field theory with a smooth background potential that implements a boundary condition only in a singular limit, generally diverges as that limit is taken; this divergence is real and reflects the infinite energy needed to constrain a fluctuating field on all energy scales, meaning that the idealized boundary-condition Casimir energy does not exist as a finite quantity in such theories.

## Method

The authors couple a fluctuating scalar field to a smooth, static background potential \(\sigma(\vec{x})\) and compute the renormalized one-loop vacuum expectation value of the energy density operator \(\hat{T}_{00}\). They express the energy density in terms of the scattering Green's function at coincident points, subtract the first few terms of the Born series to regulate ultraviolet divergences, and add back the corresponding Feynman diagrams with counterterms fixed by standard renormalization conditions. To avoid oscillatory behavior in the boundary-condition limit, they rotate the momentum integral to the positive imaginary axis, rewriting the Green's function as a product of two functions \(g_\ell(it,r)\) and \(h_\ell(it,r)\) that are bounded and real on that axis, and integrate the resulting differential equations numerically.

## Key numbers

* For a boson field in one spatial dimension with \(\sigma(x) = \delta(x-a)+\delta(x+a)\), the total Casimir energy diverges in the limit \(\lambda \to \infty\) (Dirichlet boundary condition), while the energy density at all \(x \ne \pm a\) remains finite and smoothly approaches the boundary-condition result.
* For a Gaussian background peaked around a circle of radius \(a\) in two spatial dimensions, the renormalized total energy diverges in the \(\delta\)-function limit because \(\int d^{2}x[\sigma(r)]^{2} \to \infty\); consequently the surface tension (derivative of energy with respect to \(a\)) also diverges.

## Context

This work extends the methods of Graham, Jaffe, and collaborators (2001, 2002) for computing total Casimir energies to the calculation of energy densities, and builds on the analytic continuation techniques of Brown and collaborators (1979). It directly contradicts the common practice of introducing ad hoc surface-dependent counterterms (as in the \(\zeta\)-function method of Blau, Visser, and Wipf, 1988) by showing that such counterterms have no justification in renormalizable quantum field theory. The results enable a precise understanding of when the idealized boundary-condition approach to the Casimir effect is valid (for forces between rigid surfaces) and when it fails (for quantities like surface tension that depend on the detailed interaction between the field and the material).
