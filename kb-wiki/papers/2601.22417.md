# Enhanced Yield Rate of \textsuperscript{229m}Th via Cascade Decay in Storage Rings and Electron Beam Ion Traps

> The low-energy nuclear isomeric state of \textsuperscript{229m}Th provides a unique bridge between nuclear and atomic physics, enabling applications such as nuclear clocks and precision metrology. However, efficient and controllable production of \textsuperscript{229m}Th remains a major experimental challenge. We propose an efficient scheme to produce the $^{229\mathrm{m}}$Th in storage rings (SRs) and electron beam ion traps (EBITs), using a cascade decay pathway. Highly charged ions are excited to higher nuclear states via nuclear excitation by inelastic electron scattering (NEIES) and nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC), followed by radiative or internal conversion cascades that populate the isomer. Our calculations demonstrate that, under typical SRs and EBITs conditions, optimized indirect excitation pathways significantly enhance \textsuperscript{229m}Th production rate. In particular, NEIES can provide an enhancement of up to four orders of magnitude through cascade de-excitation at high energies, while NEEC can contribute an additional enhancement of up to several tens of times. Such a significant increase in the \textsuperscript{229m}Th yield rate would facilitate its application in various nuclear photonics fields, especially in the development of atomic nuclear clocks.

## Key claim

The authors propose that indirect excitation of higher-lying nuclear states in 229Th (up to 125.44 keV) followed by cascade decay can enhance the production rate of the 229mTh isomer by up to four orders of magnitude via NEIES and by up to several tens of times via NEEC, compared to direct excitation of the isomer, under typical storage ring and electron beam ion trap conditions.

## Method

The authors calculated NEIES and NEEC cross sections and resonance strengths for excitation of 229Th from the ground state to six excited nuclear states (8.36 eV to 125.44 keV), using Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater electronic wave functions and reduced nuclear transition probabilities from the projected shell model and experimental data. They then computed isomer yield rates by convolving these cross sections with Gaussian electron energy distributions characteristic of storage rings (200 mA beam current, 1 cm radius, 1 meV energy spread) and electron beam ion traps, weighting each cascade path by the product of branching ratios for decays that populate the isomeric state.

## Key numbers

* NEIES cross sections for the fifth (97.14 keV) and sixth (125.44 keV) excited states exceed those for direct excitation of the first excited state (8.36 eV) by several orders of magnitude at high electron energies, with the sixth state dominating the total yield rate above its threshold.
* For NEEC at ionic charge state q_i=90, the largest resonance strengths reach approximately 10 b·eV for the third, fifth, and sixth excited states, compared to roughly 0.1 b·eV for direct excitation of the first excited state.
* In a storage ring with 10^8 ions, 200 mA electron beam, 1 cm radius, and 1 meV energy spread, the total NEIES yield rate at high electron energies is enhanced by at least 4 orders of magnitude relative to direct excitation.
* For NEEC in a storage ring, the cascade via the sixth excited state (125.44 keV) yields an isomer production rate of 1.89×10^5 s^-1, a factor of 84.4 enhancement over direct excitation (2.24×10^3 s^-1).
* The NEEC resonance for the second excited state (29.19 keV) at q_i=90 occurs at electron energy 0.822871 keV, yielding an isomer rate of 5.16×10^4 s^-1 (enhancement factor 23.0).

## Context

This work extends theoretical studies of electron-mediated nuclear excitation in 229Th by Zhao (2024) and Xu (2025), which focused on the first and second excited states, by including higher-lying states up to 125.44 keV and their cascade decay pathways. The calculations use reduced transition probabilities from Chen (2025) and experimental branching ratios from Masuda (2019) and the NNDC database. The proposed scheme enables design of future nuclear clock experiments and NEEC confirmation experiments in storage rings and electron beam ion traps, addressing the challenge that NEEC has not yet been experimentally confirmed (Chiara 2018, Wu 2019).
