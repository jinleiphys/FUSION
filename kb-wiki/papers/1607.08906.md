# From the pion cloud of Tomonaga to the electron pairs of Schrieffer: many body wave functions from nuclear physics to condensed matter physics

> It is well known that diverse pieces of models and physical ideas coming from different areas of physics converged in the BCS theory of superconductivity. On the contrary it is little known that the formalism developed in the Tomonaga quantum field theory of the pion-nucleon system was an important ingredient for the development of BCS theory. We discuss the evolution of these ideas in quantum field theory providing an unconventional historical perspective.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the formalism of Sin-Itiro Tomonaga's intermediate-coupling quantum field theory of the pion-nucleon system, rather than being merely a historical curiosity, was a direct and essential ingredient that inspired Schrieffer's construction of the BCS wave function, a connection that remains little known compared to other influences on BCS theory.

## Method

The authors provide a historical and conceptual analysis tracing the evolution of many-body wave functions from Tomonaga's Hartree-approximation treatment of a static nucleon surrounded by a cloud of virtual pions to the BCS wave function for electron pairs in superconductors. They compare the mathematical structures of the two wave functions explicitly, showing how Tomonaga's product form for pion amplitudes maps onto the BCS product form for electron-pair amplitudes, and discuss the subsequent reformulation in second quantization and the relationship to the quasi-chemical equilibrium theory of the Sidney group (Schafroth and Blatt).

## Key numbers

* The BCS wave function describes electron pairs with momenta contained in a "tiny shell around the Fermi momentum," forming a two-fluid system of normal fluid (unpaired electrons) and superfluid (condensed pairs).
* Ogg (1946) claimed observation of persistent currents in dilute alkali-metal-ammonia solutions at temperatures "of the order of 180 C" (approximately 180 K), attributed to Bose-Einstein condensation of trapped electron pairs.
* The paper references pressurized sulfur hydrides with a critical temperature T_c = 203 K as a contemporary system where the BCS-BEC crossover regime is relevant.

## Context

This work extends the historical narrative of BCS theory by highlighting the previously underappreciated role of Tomonaga (1940s) in providing the wave-function formalism, contrasting with the well-known contributions of London, Frohlich, and the Sidney group (Schafroth and Blatt). The paper connects this lineage to the modern problem of the BCS-BEC crossover in high-temperature superconductors and ultracold Fermi gases, suggesting that Tomonaga's intermediate-coupling approach may help understand systems like pressurized sulfur hydrides with T_c = 203 K.
