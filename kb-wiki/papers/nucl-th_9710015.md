# A statistical approach to the theory of the mean field

> We develop a statistical theory of the mean field. It is based on the proposition that the mean field can be obtained as an energy average. Moreover, it is assumed that the matrix elements of the residual interaction are random with the average value of zero. Explicit expressions for the mean field and the fluctuation away from the average are obtained. The fluctuation is expanded in terms of more and more complex excitations. Using the randomness of the matrix elements one can then obtain formulas for the contribution to the error from each class of complex excitations and a general condition for the convergence of the expansion is derived. Making some simplifying assumptions a schematic model is developed and applied to the problem of nuclear matter. It yields a measure of the strength of the effective interaction. The latter turns out to be three orders of magnitude less than that calculated using a potential which gives a binding energy of about -7 MeV/nucleon demonstrating the strong damping of the interaction strength induced by the averaging process.

## Key claim

The mean field can be derived from an energy averaging procedure that smooths out short-time events, and the matrix elements of the residual interaction are assumed to be random with zero average value; the mean field energy is shown to be always lower than the Hartree-Fock energy, but this result holds only if the difference between the mean field energy and the true ground state energy is much smaller than the averaging parameter epsilon.

## Method

The authors partition the Hilbert space into a P-space (simple, smooth wave functions) and a Q-space (complex excitations), then perform an energy average using a smoothing function characterized by a parameter epsilon that shifts the poles of excited states away from the ground state energy. They expand the fluctuations of the mean field in terms of increasingly complex particle-hole excitations (2p-2h, 4p-4h, etc.) and use the randomness of the residual interaction matrix elements to derive expressions for the squared error contributions from each class. A schematic model is developed for infinite nuclear matter (Fermi gas) by restricting the P-space to a single Hartree-Fock determinant and accounting only for the first corrections.

## Key numbers

* The residual effective interaction strength is reduced by about three orders of magnitude compared to the bare interaction calculated from a potential that gives a binding energy of about -7 MeV per nucleon.
* The mean field energy is proportional to the variance beta^2 of the residual effective interaction divided by the averaging parameter epsilon, with the relation: bar_E_0 approximately equals E_HF minus beta^2/epsilon.
* The expansion parameter connecting successive contributions to the squared error is given by the ratio of matrix elements between 2p-2h and 4p-4h states, with the recurrence relation: (delta_E_n)^2 = (1/(E-bar_epsilon_2n)^2)(1/(E-bar_epsilon_2n-2)^2)(delta_E_{n-1})^2 |<psi_2n-2|V|psi_2n>|^4.

## Context

This work extends the statistical theory of nuclear reactions developed by Feshbach (1992, 1980) and Kawai, Kerman and McVoy (1975) to the derivation of the nuclear mean field for bound states. It provides an alternative to customary approaches based on explicit Pauli and dynamical correlations, and enables a framework where the mean field emerges from statistical averaging rather than from detailed treatment of correlations. The approach is applied to nuclear matter as an exploratory calculation, with the authors noting that the theory is general and can be applied to any strongly interacting many-body system.
