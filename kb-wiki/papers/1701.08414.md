# Dependence of weak interaction rates on the nuclear composition during stellar core collapse

> We investigate the influences of the nuclear composition on the weak interaction rates of heavy nuclei during the core collapse of massive stars. The nuclear abundances in nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) are calculated by some equation of state (EOS) models including in-medium effects on nuclear masses. We systematically examine the sensitivities of electron capture and neutrino-nucleus scattering on heavy nuclei to the nuclear shell effects and the single nucleus approximation. We find that the washout of shell effects at high temperatures brings significant change to weak rates by smoothing the nuclear abundance distribution: the electron capture rate decreases by $\sim$20$\%$ in the early phase and increases by $\sim$40$\%$ in the late phase at most, while the cross section for neutrino-nucleus scattering is reduced by $\sim$15$\%$. This is because the open-shell nuclei become abundant instead of those with closed neutron shells as the shell effects disappear. We also find that the single-nucleus description based on the average values leads to underestimations of weak rates. Electron captures and neutrino coherent scattering on heavy nuclei are reduced by $\sim$80$\%$ in the early phase and by $\sim$5$\%$ in the late phase, respectively. These results indicate that NSE like EOS accounting for shell washout is indispensable for the reliable estimation of weak interaction rates in simulations of core-collapse supernovae.

# Dependence of Weak Interaction Rates on the Nuclear Composition during Stellar Core Collapse

## Key claim
The washout of nuclear shell effects at high temperatures (T ~ 2.0–3.0 MeV) significantly alters weak interaction rates during core collapse by smoothing the nuclear abundance distribution, but the magnitude and sign of the change depend on the collapse phase: electron capture rates decrease by ~20% in the early phase and increase by ~40% in the late phase, while neutrino-nucleus scattering cross sections are reduced by ~15%.

## Method
The authors calculated nuclear abundances in nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) using three equation of state (EOS) models: Model FS (full shell effects at all temperatures), Model WS (shell washout via a temperature-dependent factor τ/sinhτ), and Model NS (no shell effects). They folded these abundances with individual electron capture rates from tabulated data (FFN, ODA, LMP, LMSH) and an approximation formula for nuclei without data, and with neutrino-nucleus scattering cross sections, using a thermodynamic trajectory from a spherically symmetric Boltzmann neutrino-radiation hydrodynamics simulation of an 11.2 M⊙ progenitor.

## Key numbers
- Electron capture rate per baryon decreases by ~20% in the early phase and increases by ~40% in the late phase at most when shell washout is included (Model WS vs. Model FS).
- Neutrino-nucleus scattering cross section per baryon is reduced by ~15% around ρB ~ 10^12 g/cm^3 due to shell washout.
- The single-nucleus description based on average values underestimates electron capture rates by ~80% in the early phase compared to the full NSE ensemble.
- The single-nucleus description underestimates neutrino coherent scattering on heavy nuclei by ~5% in the late phase.
- Shell effects wash out completely at T ~ 2.0–3.0 MeV, with the energy spacing parameter εsh = 41 A_i^(-1/3) MeV.
- The number of nuclei contributing to the top 90% of electron capture rate at ρB = 2.0 × 10^12 g/cm^3 is 62 for Model FS, 175 for Model WS, and 188 for Model NS.

## Context
This work extends the multi-nucleus EOS models of Furusawa et al. (2011, 2013, 2017) by systematically comparing the impact of shell effect washout on weak interaction rates, a feature previously neglected in supernova EOS models such as those of Lattimer (1991), Shen (1998, 2011), and Hempel (2010). The results are consistent with Raduta et al. (2016), who reported that shell quenching affects average electron capture rates by up to ~30%. The findings enable more reliable weak interaction rate tables for core-collapse supernova simulations and highlight the need for NSE-like EOS models that account for shell washout, as well as new experimental and theoretical studies of non-magic nuclei at finite densities and temperatures.
