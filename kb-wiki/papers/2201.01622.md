# A Chiral Mean-Field Equation-of-State in UrQMD: Effects on the Heavy Ion Compression Stage

> It is shown that the initial compression in central heavy ion collisions at beam energies of $E_\mathrm{lab}=1-10A$~GeV depends dominantly on the underlying equation of state and only marginally on the model used for the dynamical description. To do so, a procedure to incorporate any equation of state in the UrQMD transport model is introduced. In particular we compare the baryon density, temperature and pressure evolution as well as produced entropy in a relativistic ideal hydrodynamics approach and the UrQMD transport model, where the same equation of state is used in both approaches. Not only is the compression similar if the same equation of state is used in either dynamical model, but it also strongly depends on the actual equation of state. These results indicate that the equation of state can be studied with observables which are sensitive to the initial compression phase and maximum compression achieved in heavy ion collisions at these beam energies.

# A Chiral Mean-Field Equation-of-State in UrQMD: Effects on the Heavy Ion Compression Stage

## Key claim

The initial compression in central heavy ion collisions at beam energies of 1–10 A GeV depends dominantly on the underlying equation of state and only marginally on whether the dynamical description uses a relativistic ideal fluid approach or a microscopic transport model, provided the same equation of state is used in both. The authors show this by introducing a procedure to incorporate any equation of state into the UrQMD transport model, but the agreement between hydrodynamics and transport is not exact for the hadron resonance gas equation of state and begins to deviate at the highest beam energy (10 A GeV) due to transparency effects not captured by one-fluid hydrodynamics.

## Method

The authors implemented the Chiral Mean Field (CMF) equation of state into the QMD (Quantum Molecular Dynamics) part of the UrQMD transport model by computing the effective field energy per baryon from the CMF model at zero temperature and using it in the Hamiltonian equations of motion for baryons. They then compared the time evolution of baryon density, temperature, pressure, and entropy in the central volume of central Au+Au collisions at five beam energies (1.23, 2.0, 4.0, 6.0, and 10.0 A GeV) between the UrQMD-CMF transport model and a 3+1 dimensional ideal relativistic hydrodynamic simulation using the same CMF equation of state, with analogous comparisons for the hadron resonance gas (HRG) equation of state versus UrQMD in cascade mode.

## Key numbers

- Maximum baryon density reached in central Au+Au collisions varies by almost a factor of 2 between the CMF equation of state and the HRG equation of state across the beam energy range 1.23–10.0 A GeV.
- The CMF model predicts a nuclear incompressibility K0 ≈ 267 MeV, binding energy E0/B ≈ −15.2 MeV, and asymmetry energy S0 ≈ 31.9 MeV.
- The chiral phase transition in the CMF model occurs at about 4 times normal nuclear density (4 n0) with a critical endpoint at TCP ≈ 17 MeV.
- Temperatures in the central volume from transport and hydrodynamics agree within 5–10 MeV after a few fm/c for both equations of state.
- The HRG simulations give systematically larger temperatures than the CMF simulations by about 10 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the approach of incorporating realistic equations of state into transport models, going beyond the limited Skyrme potential method used in earlier QMD implementations (Aichelin 1991, Konopka) and the relativistic mean field implementations (Nara 2019, 2020). The CMF model used here has previously been applied to lattice QCD data analysis (Motornenko 2020), cold neutron star description (Motornenko 2019), and hydrodynamic simulations of heavy ion collisions and binary neutron star mergers (Seck 2020). The results enable future studies of flow, cluster production, correlations, and fluctuations as observables sensitive to the equation of state through the initial compression phase, and suggest that the equation of state can be studied with observables sensitive to maximum compression in a nearly model-independent way at these beam energies.
