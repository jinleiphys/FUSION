# Investigation of ground state properties and shape evolution in Hf isotopes using the CDFT approach

> The ground-state properties and shape evolution of even-even hafnium isotopes ranging from $N=80$ to the neutron dripline are thoroughly examined using Covariant Density Functional Theory (CDFT) with density-dependent effective interactions, specifically the parameter sets DD-ME1, DD-ME2, DD-PC1, and DD-PCX. Key nuclear properties, including binding energies, two-neutron separation energies ($S_{2n}$), two-neutron shell gaps ($δS_{2n}$), neutron pairing energies ($E_{pair,n}$), quadrupole deformation parameters ($β_2$), root-mean-square (RMS) charge and matter radii, and neutron skin thickness ($Δr_{np}$), are systematically computed and compared with available experimental results and predictions from various theoretical models. These include the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) framework employing the Skyrme SLy4 interaction, the Finite Range Droplet Model (FRDM), the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (DRHBc) using the PC-PK1 functional, and the relativistic mean-field (RMF) approach with NL3 parameterization. Shell closures at $N=82$ and $N=126$, subshell effects at $N=108$ and $N=152$, and shape transitions with coexistence in $^{192}$Hf and $^{222-236}$Hf are observed. Neutron skin thickness increases with neutron excess, and potential energy surfaces show consistent trends, validating CDFT's reliability for nuclear structure predictions.

## Key claim

The paper claims that Covariant Density Functional Theory (CDFT) reliably predicts ground-state properties and shape evolution across the entire even-even hafnium isotopic chain from N=80 to the neutron dripline, but the actual results show that predictions for the neutron dripline location vary significantly between models, ranging from N≈160 (HFB with SLy4) to N=184 (DRHBc with PC-PK1), with CDFT predicting N≈170.

## Method

The authors performed systematic calculations using four density-dependent CDFT parameter sets (DD-ME1, DD-ME2, DD-PC1, and DD-PCX) within the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov framework, employing a finite-range separable pairing force with parameters G=728 MeV·fm³ and a=0.644 fm calibrated to the Gogny D1S force. They computed binding energies, two-neutron separation energies, shell gaps, neutron pairing energies, quadrupole deformation parameters, RMS charge and matter radii, and neutron skin thickness for even-even hafnium isotopes from ¹⁵²Hf to ²⁷⁰Hf, comparing results with experimental data and predictions from RMF (NL3), DRHBc (PC-PK1), HFB (SLy4), and FRDM models.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy per nucleon peaks at mass number A=172 (N=100) for all CDFT interactions, identifying ¹⁷²Hf as the most stable isotope in the chain
- Neutron dripline for even-even hafnium isotopes occurs around N≈170 for all four CDFT parameterizations, where S₂n becomes negative
- In ¹⁹²Hf and ¹⁹⁴Hf, prolate and oblate minima are found at β₂=±0.15 with energy differences of approximately 0.8 MeV and 0.3 MeV, respectively
- For isotopes ²⁰²–²²⁰Hf, prolate minima lie 2.0–3.0 MeV below oblate minima in DD-ME1 and DD-ME2, and 2.0–5.0 MeV in DD-PC1 and DD-PCX calculations
- For isotopes ²²²–²⁴⁰Hf, prolate–oblate energy differences range from 1.5–2.5 MeV (DD-ME1, DD-PC1) and 2.0–4.0 MeV (DD-ME2, DD-PCX)
- Neutron pairing energy drops to zero at N=82 and N=126, with DD-PCX predicting vanishing pairing energy at N=118 while other functionals show near-zero values

## Context

This work extends systematic CDFT studies of nuclear structure across the isotopic chart, following prior investigations of shape transitions in the A≈190 mass region by Sharma (2025), Dadwal (2017), and others. The results validate CDFT's reliability for predicting ground-state properties of neutron-rich nuclei while revealing model-dependent variations in dripline predictions that highlight the need for experimental data on neutron-rich hafnium isotopes beyond current measurements. The observed subshell effects at N=108 and N=118, and shape coexistence in ¹⁹²Hf and ²²²–²³⁶Hf, provide targets for future experimental studies using radioactive beam facilities.
