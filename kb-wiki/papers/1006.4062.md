# Looking inside neutron stars: Microscopic calculations confront observations

> While QCD appears not to be accurately solvable in the regime of interest for neutron star physics, microscopic calculations are feasible at both low and very high densities. In this work, we propose using the most realistic calculations in these two regimes of nuclear physics and perturbative QCD, and construct equations of state by matching the results requiring thermodynamic consistency. We find that the resulting equations of state --- in contrast to several hadronic ones --- are able to reproduce current observational data on neutron stars without any fine tuning, and allow stable hybrid stars with masses up to 2.1M_{sun}. Using recent observations of star radii, we perform a maximum likelihood analysis to further constrain the equation of state, and in addition show that the effects of rotation on radii and masses should not be neglected in future precision studies.

## Key claim

The paper constructs equations of state (EoS) for neutron star interiors by matching state-of-the-art microscopic calculations at low and high densities, and finds that these EoSs reproduce current observational data without fine tuning, but the authors note that the maximum likelihood analysis favoring a large quark core is tentative due to the scarcity of objects with sufficient data.

## Method

The authors matched hadronic matter EoSs (from nuclear physics calculations up to nuclear saturation density) to quark-gluon matter EoSs (from perturbative QCD calculations at very high densities) requiring thermodynamic consistency, considering both homogeneous phases and mixed phases. They then solved the general relativistic field equations for uniformly rotating stars using these hybrid EoSs, and compared the resulting mass-radius and mass-frequency relations to observational data on 15 millisecond pulsars and 6 stars with known radii, performing a maximum likelihood analysis to constrain the EoS.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: \(n_{\rm sat}=0.16\ {\rm fm}^{-3}\)
- Maximum mass for slowly rotating hybrid stars: up to \(2.1\,M_{\odot}\)
- Maximum mass for hybrid stars with a mixed phase: \(1.93\,M_{\odot}\)
- For hybrid stars with masses of \(1.5\,M_\odot\), radii range from 10.8 to 13.5 km
- Best-fit radius for a \(1.5\,M_\odot\) star: 13.1 km (smallest \(\chi^2\) value)
- Strange quark mass: \(m_s(\bar{\Lambda}=2\,{\rm GeV})=92.2\pm1.3\) MeV
- \(\Lambda_{\tiny \msbar}\) range: 346 MeV to 412 MeV
- Gap parameter \(\Delta\) range: 0 to 100 MeV
- Minimum gravitational mass for newborn neutron stars: \(\sim 1.1 M_{\odot}\)

## Context

This work extends the matching strategy of Kurkela (2009) from high-temperature low-density matter to cold dense matter applicable to neutron stars, and constitutes the first direct comparison of state-of-the-art theoretical predictions with observations of rotating neutron stars. It contradicts hadronic EoSs containing hyperons (Schulze 2006) or kaon condensation (Glendenning 1998), which are ruled out by the data, and provides a framework for future precision studies that must account for rotation effects at frequencies exceeding 600 Hz.
