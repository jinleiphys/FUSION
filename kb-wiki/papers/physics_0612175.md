# Laser acceleration of ion beams

> We consider methods of charged particle acceleration by means of high-intensity lasers. As an application we discuss a laser booster for heavy ion beams provided, e.g. by the Dubna nuclotron. Simple estimates show that a cascade of crossed laser beams would be necessary to provide additional acceleration to gold ions of the order of GeV/nucleon.

# Laser acceleration of ion beams

## Key claim
A cascade of crossed laser beams could provide additional acceleration to heavy ions (e.g., gold) from the Dubna nuclotron on the order of GeV/nucleon, but only if the ion-field interaction is terminated at specific points in the field cycle to avoid cancellation of energy gains by subsequent losses.

## Method
The authors analyze three mechanisms for laser acceleration of ions: light pressure from photon absorption and re-emission, gradient forces from the interaction of ions with the envelope of the laser electric field, and a crossed laser beam geometry that creates a longitudinal accelerating electric field. They derive analytic expressions for the energy gain in the crossed-beam scheme and compute the optimal crossing half-angle and energy gain for fully ionized gold ions (Au$^{79+}$) as a function of laser intensity, field cycles, and initial Lorentz factor.

## Key numbers
- For Au$^{79+}$ ions, the optimal half-crossing angle is $\theta_{\rm max} \approx 14^\circ$ for a flat envelope $g(\eta)=1$, and $\theta_{\rm max}\approx 20^\circ$ for a $\sin^2(\eta/10)$ envelope.
- The maximal energy gain reaches 25 GeV per ion (140 MeV/nucleon) for a laser intensity of the order of $10^{22}$ W/cm$^2$.
- The Dubna nuclotron operates at $\gamma \simeq 4$, corresponding to ultra-relativistic conditions.
- For $^{179}$Au, the nuclear excitation energy is $\varepsilon^* = 72$ keV, requiring an X-ray laser frequency of order 1 MeV for the light pressure mechanism.
- Energy gain saturates with increasing injection energy at about $\gamma_0\approx 20$ for the parameters used.

## Context
This work extends earlier proposals for laser acceleration of charged particles by Haaland (1995) and Salamin (2000, 2003), who considered crossed laser beam schemes for electrons. The authors apply this concept to heavy ion beams from the Dubna nuclotron, where an additional acceleration of a few GeV/nucleon would enable studies of the onset of quark matter formation in heavy-ion collisions. The paper identifies the light pressure mechanism as impractical due to insufficient laser frequency (even for modern X-ray lasers as noted by Ring), and gradient forces as less effective than linear accelerators, leaving the crossed-beam cascade as the most promising approach for a laser booster system.
