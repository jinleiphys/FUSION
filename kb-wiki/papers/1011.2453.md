# Analytic Harmonic Approach to the N-body problem

> We consider an analytic way to make the interacting N-body problem tractable by using harmonic oscillators in place of the relevant two-body interactions. The two body terms of the N-body Hamiltonian are approximated by considering the energy spectrum and radius of the relevant two-body problem which gives frequency, center position, and zero point energy of the corresponding harmonic oscillator. Adding external harmonic one-body terms, we proceed to solve the full quantum mechanical N-body problem analytically for arbitrary masses. Energy eigenvalues, eigenmodes, and correlation functions like density matrices can then be computed analytically. As a first application of our formalism, we consider the N-boson problem in two- and three dimensions where we fit the two-body interactions to agree with the well-known zero-range model for two particles in a harmonic trap. Subsequently, condensate fractions, spectra, radii, and eigenmodes are discussed as function of dimension, boson number N, and scattering length obtained in the zero-range model. We find that energies, radii, and condensate fraction increase with scattering length as well as boson number, while radii decrease with increasing boson number. Our formalism is completely general and can also be applied to fermions, Bose-Fermi mixtures, and to more exotic geometries.

## Key claim

The paper presents an analytic harmonic oscillator approximation for the interacting N-body problem, where two-body interactions are replaced by harmonic oscillators whose parameters (frequency, center position, zero-point energy) are fitted to reproduce the energy spectrum and radius of the corresponding two-body problem. The authors hedge that the accuracy of the oscillator approximation increases as the potentials resemble oscillators, and that for weakly bound structures the approach reveals "correct overall qualitative, and perhaps also semi-quantitative, behaviour."

## Method

The authors construct a general N-body Hamiltonian with quadratic one- and two-body harmonic potentials, then perform a series of coordinate transformations, first translating to eliminate linear terms, then transforming to relative and center-of-mass coordinates, then diagonalizing the potential matrix, and finally scaling and diagonalizing the kinetic energy matrix, to decouple the system into independent harmonic oscillators in new coordinates. For the specific application to N bosons in a trap, the two-body interaction parameters are fitted to reproduce the ground state properties of the zero-range contact interaction model for two particles in a harmonic trap from Busch et al. (1998). The formalism yields analytic expressions for energy eigenvalues, eigenmodes, root-mean-square radii, and the one-body density matrix (from which condensate fraction is extracted).

## Key numbers

- The largest eigenvalue of the one-body density matrix (condensate fraction) is given by λ = (2/(1+√d_x)) × (2/(1+√d_y)) × (2/(1+√d_z)) for three dimensions, where d_x, d_y, d_z are ratios defined from the wavefunction exponent matrix Z.
- The remaining eigenvalues of the density matrix follow λ_n = λ(1-λ)^n, where n is a non-negative integer.
- The ground state energy for a given set of quantum numbers is E = V_shift + ℏ Σ_k (ω̄_x,k (n_x,k + 1/2) + ω̄_y,k (n_y,k + 1/2) + ω̄_z,k (n_z,k + 1/2)).
- The length parameter for each decoupled mode is b_x,k² = ℏ/(m̄_x,k ω̄_x,k).

## Context

This work extends the harmonic oscillator approximation approach used in configuration interaction methods (Goeppert-Mayer 1955, Heyd 1990) and in cold atom studies (Brosens 1997, Tempere 1998, Lemmens 1999, Zaluska-Kotur 2000, Yan 2003, Gajda 2006) by providing a general formalism valid for arbitrary masses and arbitrary quadratic one- and two-body potentials, including cases where center-of-mass motion does not separate trivially. The method enables analytic calculation of energies, radii, density matrices, and condensate fractions for N-boson systems with short-range interactions, and the authors state it can be applied to fermions, Bose-Fermi mixtures, and exotic geometries. The specific application uses the two-body zero-range model results of Busch et al. (1998) to fit oscillator parameters, and the authors note that at small positive scattering length they find a highly fragmented state in both two and three dimensions.
