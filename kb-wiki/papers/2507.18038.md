# Impact of spin polarization on transport and thermodynamic coefficients

> This work investigates the influence of parton spin polarization on effective transport and thermodynamic coefficients in noncentral light- and heavy-ion collisions. To model this influence, I consider two sources of spin polarization: thermal vorticity, induced by angular momentum, and thermal shear, arising from local velocity gradients. Using a novel kinetic theory framework, one finds that transport and thermodynamic coefficients -- including the speed of sound squared $c_{s}^{2}$, specific shear viscosity $η/s$, specific bulk viscosity $ζ/s$, and mean free path $λ$ -- are substantially modified by spin polarization effects. Among the two sources, thermal vorticity-induced spin polarization dominates the modifications to these coefficients. Moreover, both $c_{s}^{2}$ and $ζ/s$ exhibit a nonmonotonic dependence on the collision energy, and the associated scaling behaviors potentially serve as indicators of the critical phenomena of quantum chromodynamics.

## Key claim
Spin polarization from thermal vorticity and thermal shear substantially modifies transport and thermodynamic coefficients in noncentral heavy-ion collisions, with the nonmonotonic energy dependence of the speed of sound squared and specific bulk viscosity potentially serving as indicators of the QCD critical point. The abstract's claim of "substantially modified" coefficients is hedged by the finding that thermal shear only suppresses or enhances the magnitude of coefficient ratios without shifting the positions of their peaks or valleys, meaning the nonmonotonic critical signal is primarily driven by thermal vorticity alone.

## Method
The author uses the AMPT (a multi-phase transport) model to simulate the space-time evolution of noncentral O+O, Cu+Cu, Ag+Ag, and Au+Au collisions at 20-60% centrality. A kinetic theory framework with a spin-dependent phase-space distribution function incorporates two sources of spin polarization: thermal vorticity (case I, VIP) and the sum of thermal vorticity and thermal shear (case II, VIP+SIP). Transport and thermodynamic coefficients are extracted from the energy-momentum tensor computed with the spin-modified distribution functions, and their ratios between spin-polarized and non-spin-polarized cases are analyzed as functions of radius, temperature, and collision energy.

## Key numbers
- The nonmonotonic behavior in the ratios $\tilde{c}_{s}^{2}/c_{s}^{2}$ and $[\tilde{\zeta}/\tilde{s}]/[\zeta/s]$ is observed at $R \geq 3$ fm (except for O+O systems in VIP/non-SP case) and at $T \leq 0.17$ GeV.
- The inflection point of the nonmonotonic ratios restricts the parton chemical potential to $\langle \mu_{p} \rangle \sim$ [0.018, 0.029] GeV, corresponding to collision energies $\sqrt{s_{NN}} \sim$ [19.6, 39.0] GeV.
- Results are shown at an early proper time $\tau = 0.4$ fm for radial and temperature distributions, and integrated over $\tau = 0$ to 6 fm for other analyses.
- The critical temperature is taken as $T_{c} = 0.110$ GeV for the parton chemical potential calculation.

## Context
This work extends the author's previous kinetic theory framework (Wei 2025) that showed spin polarization can be induced via vorticity, by now incorporating both thermal vorticity and thermal shear contributions. The nonmonotonic behavior aligns with predictions from ideal hydrodynamics + UrQMD simulations on information entropy of net proton distributions (Deng 2024) and with RHIC measurements of high-order cumulants and scaling exponents (STAR 2020, 2021, 2022). The results suggest the QCD critical point is likely limited to the energy range $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 19.6$-27 GeV, and the scale-dependent behavior across different system sizes may serve as a potential signature of the QCD critical point.
