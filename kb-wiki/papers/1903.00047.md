# Discrepancy between experimental and theoretical $β$-decay rates resolved from first principles

> $β$-decay, a process that changes a neutron into a proton (and vice versa), is the dominant decay mode of atomic nuclei. This decay offers a unique window to physics beyond the standard model, and is at the heart of microphysical processes in stellar explosions and the synthesis of the elements in the Universe. For 50 years, a central puzzle has been that observed $β$-decay rates are systematically smaller than theoretical predictions. This was attributed to an apparent quenching of the fundamental coupling constant $g_A \simeq $ 1.27 in the nucleus by a factor of about 0.75 compared to the $β$-decay of a free neutron. The origin of this quenching is controversial and has so far eluded a first-principles theoretical understanding. Here we address this puzzle and show that this quenching arises to a large extent from the coupling of the weak force to two nucleons as well as from strong correlations in the nucleus. We present state-of-the-art computations of $β$-decays from light to heavy nuclei. Our results are consistent with experimental data, including the pioneering measurement for $^{100}$Sn. These theoretical advances are enabled by systematic effective field theories of the strong and weak interactions combined with powerful quantum many-body techniques. This work paves the way for systematic theoretical predictions for fundamental physics problems. These include the synthesis of heavy elements in neutron star mergers and the search for neutrino-less double-$β$-decay, where an analogous quenching puzzle is a major source of uncertainty in extracting the neutrino mass scale.

## Key claim

The long-standing puzzle of systematically quenched beta-decay rates in nuclei is resolved from first principles by showing that the apparent reduction of the axial-vector coupling constant g_A arises primarily from two-body weak currents (coupling of the weak force to two nucleons) combined with strong nuclear correlations, rather than from a fundamental modification of the coupling constant itself. The authors demonstrate that first-principles calculations using chiral effective field theories and advanced many-body methods reproduce experimental Gamow-Teller strengths across light to heavy nuclei without invoking a phenomenological quenching factor, though the quenching factor obtained from two-body currents depends on the Hamiltonian employed and ranges from q_2BC = 0.73 to 0.85.

## Method

The authors performed coupled-cluster calculations for the super-allowed Gamow-Teller decay of 100Sn, no-core shell model calculations for light nuclei (A <= 14), and valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group (VS-IMSRG) calculations for medium-mass sd-shell and lower pf-shell nuclei. They employed a family of chiral effective field theory nucleon-nucleon and three-nucleon interactions with consistently derived two-body currents, including a newly developed NN-N4LO+3N_lnl interaction constrained to reproduce the triton half-life.

## Key numbers

- The predicted Gamow-Teller strength for 100Sn decay is in the range 5.2(5) <= |M_GT|^2 <= 7.0(7), overlapping with the systematic evaluation of Batist et al. (2010) and the lower end of the measurement by Hinke et al. (2012)
- The quenching factor from two-body currents ranges from q_2BC = 0.73 to 0.85, consistent with the value q = 0.75(2) from Batist et al. (2010)
- For sd-shell nuclei, VS-IMSRG calculations without two-body currents yield a quenching factor of 0.89(4); with two-body currents included, the linear fit lies close to the diagonal line representing perfect agreement with experiment
- For lower pf-shell nuclei, VS-IMSRG calculations without two-body currents yield a quenching factor of 0.85(3)
- Benchmark calculations between the many-body methods agree to within 5% for the large transition in 14O
- Each 100Sn calculation carries a conservatively estimated uncertainty of about 10%

## Context

This work resolves a 50-year puzzle in nuclear physics, first systematically documented by Wilkinson (1973) and Towner (1987), where observed beta-decay rates were systematically smaller than theoretical predictions, requiring an ad hoc quenching of g_A by a factor of about 0.75. The authors extend prior first-principles calculations by incorporating consistent two-body currents from chiral effective field theory, building on the Green's function Monte Carlo work of Pastore et al. (2017) for light nuclei. This enables systematic ab initio predictions for weak interactions across the nuclear chart, with direct implications for neutrino-less double-beta decay searches (where an analogous quenching puzzle introduces major uncertainty in extracting the neutrino mass scale) and for r-process nucleosynthesis in neutron star mergers.
