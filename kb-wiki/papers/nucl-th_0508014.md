# Scattering theory for arbitrary potentials

> The fundamental quantities of potential scattering theory are generalized to accommodate long-range interactions. New definitions for the scattering amplitude and wave operators valid for arbitrary interactions including potentials with a Coulomb tail are presented. It is shown that for the Coulomb potential the generalized amplitude gives the physical on-shell amplitude without recourse to a renormalization procedure.

## Key claim

The paper presents new definitions for the scattering amplitude and wave operators that are valid for arbitrary potentials, including those with a Coulomb tail, and demonstrates that for the Coulomb potential the generalized amplitude yields the physical on-shell amplitude without requiring any renormalization procedure.

## Method

The authors derive a formal solution to the Schrödinger equation using a surface-integral representation of the T-matrix that depends only on the asymptotic behavior of the participating wave functions. They introduce generalized wave operators of the form Ω^± = [1 + G(E±iε)(H→ - E)] and show that the scattered wave is generated entirely from the leading-order term of the incident distorted wave, with no scattered wave produced from the remaining higher-order terms. The new T-matrix is expressed in both prior form T(k',k) = ⟨ψ⁻_k' | H→ - E | φ⁽⁰⁾⁺_k⟩ and post form T(k',k) = ⟨φ⁽⁰⁾⁻_k' | H← - E | ψ⁺_k⟩, which reduce to the standard definitions for short-range potentials.

## Key numbers

- The Coulomb parameter is defined as γ = z₁z₂μ/k, where z₁ and z₂ are the charges of the particles, μ is the reduced mass, and k is the relative momentum.
- For the pure Coulomb potential, the T-matrix evaluates to (4π z₁z₂)/|k' - k|² × [Γ(1+iγ)/Γ(1-iγ)] × [4k²/|k' - k|²]^(iγ), which is the well-known full on-shell Coulomb T-matrix.
- The scattering amplitude is related to the T-matrix by f(k̂·r̂) = -(μ/2π) T(k',k), where k' = k r̂.

## Context

This work extends the formalism of Dollard (1964) and the renormalization methods of Taylor (1975) and others, which required ad-hoc procedures to remove divergent factors from the on-shell amplitude. The authors build on their recent three-body formalism (Kadyrov et al., 2003, 2004) and correct an earlier result by Barrachina and Macek (1989) regarding the integral equation for the Coulomb scattering wave function. The new surface-integral formulation is particularly suited for practical calculations and provides a foundation for treating three-body Coulomb scattering problems above the breakup threshold.
