# Statistical Fluctuations and Correlations in Hadronic Equilibrium Systems

> This thesis is dedictaed to the study of fluctuation and correlation observables of hadronic equilibrium systems. The statistical hadronization model of high energy physics, in its ideal, i.e. non-interacting, gas approximation will be investigated in different ensemble formulations. The hypothesis of thermal and chemical equilibrium in high energy interaction will be tested against qualitative and quantitative predictions.

# Statistical Fluctuations and Correlations in Hadronic Equilibrium Systems

## Key claim

The statistical hadronization model in its ideal gas approximation can be used to test the hypothesis of thermal and chemical equilibrium in high energy interactions, but the validity of the predictions depends on the choice of ensemble formulation (canonical, grand-canonical, or micro-canonical) and on the treatment of finite-volume effects and acceptance scaling, which introduce systematic corrections to the idealized equilibrium picture.

## Method

The author derives analytic expressions for the first and second moments of particle multiplicity distributions in canonical, grand-canonical, and micro-canonical ensembles of a hadron resonance gas, using the statistical hadronization model in the Boltzmann approximation. A Monte Carlo scheme is employed to transform grand-canonical samples into canonical or micro-canonical samples via a weight factor method, and the convergence of observables such as scaled variances and correlation coefficients is studied as a function of sample size and the volume ratio parameter lambda = V1/Vg. Resonance decay effects are incorporated analytically into the partition function through absolute branching ratios, and the cumulant structure of the system is examined using a kappa-tensor formalism.

## Key numbers

- For a system with observable volume V1 = 50 fm^3, global volume Vg = 75 fm^3, global charge Qg = 10, temperature T = 0.160 GeV, particle mass m = 0.140 GeV, and degeneracy g = 1: the average charge in the observable subsystem is <Q1> ≈ 6.667, the mean particle multiplicity is <N1> ≈ 7.335, and the scaled variance of particle number fluctuations is ω ≈ 0.3896.
- The optimal chemical potential for Monte Carlo sampling is μQ = 0.1896 GeV, where the Monte Carlo weight moments show a strong minimum.
- For a hadron resonance gas with λ = V1/Vg = 0.875, in transverse momentum bin ΔpT,5: the scaled variance of positively charged hadrons is ω+(ΔpT,5) = 0.8069 ± 0.0514, and the correlation coefficient between positively and negatively charged hadrons is ρ+-(ΔpT,5) = -0.0026 ± 0.0421.
- For the same system with 20 Monte Carlo runs of 2×10^5 events each: ω+(ΔpT,5) = 0.8081 ± 0.0149 and ρ+-(ΔpT,5) = -0.0022 ± 0.0125.
- Additional Monte Carlo runs of 1×10^7 events were performed for λ = 0.925, 0.950, and 0.975.

## Context

This thesis extends the micro-correlator approach developed by Begun et al. (2004-2006) and the saddle point expansion method of Becattini (2005) for calculating scaled variances in the thermodynamic limit of hadronic systems. The work provides a unified framework for computing fluctuation and correlation observables across different statistical ensembles, including the effects of resonance decays and limited acceptance. The results enable quantitative tests of thermal and chemical equilibrium hypotheses in high-energy nuclear collisions, and the Monte Carlo weight method developed here can be applied to experimental data from heavy-ion experiments where only a fraction of the full phase space is accessible.
