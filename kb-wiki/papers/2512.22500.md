# Bidirectional Neural Networks for Global Nucleon-Nucleus Optical Model Calculations

> Modern nuclear data evaluation increasingly requires not only accurate scattering calculations, but also efficient methods for uncertainty quantification and parameter optimization, tasks that benefit from differentiable solvers amenable to gradient-based algorithms. I present a neural network emulator based on Bidirectional Liquid Neural Networks (BiLNN) that provides a fully differentiable mapping from optical potential parameters to scattering wave functions. The key innovation enabling generalization across the parameter space is the use of phase-space coordinates $ρ= kr$ that normalize the oscillation wavelength regardless of projectile energy, allowing a single network to span 1 to 200~MeV. Trained on Numerov solutions for twelve target nuclei (\nuc{12}{C} to \nuc{208}{Pb}), both protons and neutrons, and partial waves up to $l=30$, the network achieves an overall relative error of 1.2\%. The predicted wave functions yield accurate $S$-matrix elements and elastic scattering cross sections, reproducing diffraction patterns spanning four orders of magnitude. Importantly, the model extrapolates successfully to nuclei not included in training (\nuc{24}{Mg}, \nuc{63}{Cu}, \nuc{184}{W}) with comparable accuracy, demonstrating that it has learned the physics of the optical model rather than memorizing specific targets. The differentiable nature of the trained model opens the door to gradient-based optimization of optical model parameters and efficient uncertainty quantification.

## Method
The radial SchrÃ¶dinger equation was solved using the Numerov method with the KD02 optical potential (central terms only, omitting spin-orbit) for twelve target nuclei from 12C to 208Pb, both protons and neutrons, at energies randomly sampled from 1 to 200 MeV and partial waves up to l=30. The wave functions were transformed to phase-space coordinates Ï = kr to normalize oscillation wavelength, then used to train a Bidirectional Liquid Neural Network with Closed-form Continuous-time (CfC) layers that processes the radial sequence in both forward and backward directions to incorporate both boundary conditions. The network receives a 9-dimensional feature vector at each radial point including normalized potential, WKB phase information, and target mass, and outputs the real and imaginary parts of the radial wave function.

## Context
This work extends the Koning-Delaroche (KD02) global optical potential parameterization (Koning 2003) by providing a differentiable neural network emulator that can serve as a surrogate for the Numerov solver. It contrasts with reduced-order emulators such as eigenvector continuation (Frame 2018, Furnstahl 2020), the reduced-basis method (Drischler 2023), and wave-function-based coupled-channels emulation (Catacora Rios 2026), which achieve high accuracy (often at or below 0.1%) but require rebuilding a problem-specific basis for each new system. The BiLNN approach trades some accuracy for breadth of applicability, enabling gradient-based optimization of optical model parameters and efficient uncertainty quantification through automatic differentiation, which the author identifies as the principal direction for future work.
