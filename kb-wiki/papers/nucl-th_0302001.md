# Proton vs. neutron halo breakup

> In this paper we show how effective parameters such as effective binding energies can be defined for a proton in the combined nuclear-Coulomb potential, including also the target potential, in the case in which the proton is bound in a nucleus which is partner of a nuclear reaction. Using such effective parameters the proton behaves similarly to a neutron. In this way some unexpected results obtained from dynamical calculations for reactions initiated by very weakly bound proton halo nuclei can be interpreted. Namely the fact that stripping dominates the nuclear breakup cross section which in turn dominates over the Coulomb breakup even when the target is heavy at medium to high incident energies. Our interpretation helps also clarifying why the existence and characteristics of a proton halo extracted from different types of data have sometimes appeared contradictory.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the unexpectedly large nuclear stripping cross sections observed for proton halo nuclei (such as \(^{8}\)B and \(^{17}\)F) on heavy targets, and the dominance of nuclear over Coulomb breakup even at medium to high incident energies, can be explained by defining effective binding energies for the proton that incorporate the combined nuclear-Coulomb potentials of both projectile and target. This effective treatment makes the proton behave similarly to a neutron with a much larger ("normal") binding energy, thereby reducing the phase space for breakup and altering the relative importance of reaction mechanisms.

## Method

The authors introduce an effective binding energy \(\tilde{\varepsilon}_i = \varepsilon_i - \Delta_i\) for the proton, where \(\Delta_i\) includes the Coulomb barrier shift from the proton-core potential (\(Z_P e^2 / R_i\)) and a polarization effect from the target Coulomb potential averaged over the left and right sides of the projectile. Using this effective energy, they compute effective asymptotic wave function parameters (decay constant \(\tilde{\gamma}_i\) and normalization constant \(\tilde{C}_i\)) as for a neutron. They then apply the "transfer to the continuum" model, previously used for neutron breakup, to calculate total breakup cross sections for \(^{17}\)F on \(^{208}\)Pb at 40 A.MeV, comparing them to analogous calculations for the neutron halo nucleus \(^{11}\)Be.

## Key numbers

*   The one-proton removal cross section for \(^{8}\)B on \(^{28}\)Si at around 40 A.MeV was measured as stripping = \(110 \pm 9\) mb and total diffraction = \(112 \pm 12\) mb (from Neg (2001) data cited in the paper).
*   For \(^{8}\)B on \(^{208}\)Pb at 142 A.MeV, the one-proton removal cross section was \(744 \pm 9\) mb, with an estimated 300-450 mb from nuclear breakup and 311 mb from Coulomb breakup (from Blan (2002) data cited in the paper).
*   For \(^{8}\)B on \(^{208}\)Pb at 936 A.MeV, the one-proton removal cross section was \(662 \pm 60\) mb; for \(^{8}\)B on \(^{12}\)C at the same energy, it was \(94 \pm 8\) mb (from Lola (2002) data cited in the paper).
*   The calculated cross sections for \(^{11}\)Be + \(^{208}\)Pb at 40 A.MeV are: Coulomb breakup \(\sigma_C = 2724\) mb, stripping \(\sigma_S = 312\) mb, diffraction \(\sigma_D = 240\) mb.
*   The calculated cross sections for \(^{17}\)F + \(^{208}\)Pb at 40 A.MeV (using effective parameters) are: Coulomb breakup \(\sigma_C = 382\) mb, stripping \(\sigma_S = 131\) mb, diffraction \(\sigma_D = 53\) mb.
*   The effective binding energy shift \(\Delta_i\) for \(^{8}\)B + \(^{208}\)Pb is -2.29 MeV, leading to an effective binding energy \(\tilde{\varepsilon}_i = -2.43\) MeV (compared to the true binding energy of -0.14 MeV).
*   The effective binding energy shift \(\Delta_i\) for \(^{17}\)F + \(^{208}\)Pb is -3.2 MeV, leading to an effective binding energy \(\tilde{\varepsilon}_i = -3.8\) MeV (compared to the true binding energy of -0.6 MeV for the \(1d_{5/2}\) state).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical understanding of halo breakup, which was well-established for neutron halos like \(^{11}\)Be (studied by Anne (1994), Nak (1994), and Mar (2002)), to the puzzling case of proton halos like \(^{8}\)B and \(^{17}\)F. It directly addresses apparent contradictions in the literature regarding the existence and characteristics of a proton halo, as questioned by Kelly (1996) and Gai (1997). The paper provides a physical interpretation for the numerical results of dynamical calculations by Esbensen and Bertsch (1996) and Esbensen and Hencken (1998), which showed that nuclear breakup can dominate over Coulomb breakup for proton halos on targets up to mass 100. It also justifies the use of first-order perturbation theory for proton breakup on heavy targets, as employed by Bertulani and Danielewicz (2002), by showing that the effective binding energy is large enough to suppress higher-order effects. The approach enables a simplified treatment of proton halo reactions using neutron-like models and clarifies how exclusive measurements of proton angular distributions (as done for neutrons by Anne (1994)) could disentangle the dominant reaction mechanisms.
