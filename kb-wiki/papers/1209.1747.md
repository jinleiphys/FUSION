# Correlation femtoscopy of small systems

> The basic principles of the correlation femtoscopy, including its correspondence to the Hanbury Brown and Twiss intensity interferometry, are re-examined. The main subject of the paper is an analysis of the correlation femtoscopy when the source size is as small as the order of the uncertainty limit. It is about 1 fm for the current high energy experiments. Then the standard femtoscopy model of random sources is inapplicable. The uncertainty principle leads to the partial indistinguishability and coherence of closely located emitters that affect the observed femtoscopy scales. In thermal systems the role of corresponding coherent length is taken by the thermal de Broglie wavelength that also defines the size of a single emitter. The formalism of partially coherent phases in the amplitudes of closely located individual emitters is used for the quantitative analysis. The general approach is illustrated analytically for the case of the Gaussian approximation for emitting sources. A reduction of the interferometry radii and a suppression of the Bose-Einstein correlation functions for small sources due to the uncertainty principle are found. There is a positive correlation between the source size and the intercept of the correlation function. The peculiarities of the non-femtoscopic correlations caused by minijets and fluctuations of the initial states of the systems formed in $pp$ and $e^+e^-$ collisions are also analyzed. The factorization property for the contributions of femtoscopic and non-femtoscopic correlations into complete correlation function is observed in numerical calculations in a wide range of the model parameters.

## Key claim

The standard femtoscopy model of random independent sources becomes inapplicable when the source size is as small as the uncertainty limit (about 1 fm for current high-energy experiments), because the uncertainty principle forces partial indistinguishability and coherence of closely located emitters, which modifies the observed femtoscopy scales.

## Method

The authors re-examine the basic principles of correlation femtoscopy and its analogy to Hanbury Brown-Twiss intensity interferometry, then develop a formalism of partially coherent phases for the amplitudes of individual emitters. They apply this formalism to small systems where the distance between emitter centers is comparable to the inverse of the momentum spread, using the overlap integral of wave packets and the thermal de Broglie wavelength as the natural coherence length. The general approach is illustrated analytically for Gaussian source approximations, and numerical calculations are performed to study the factorization of femtoscopic and non-femtoscopic correlations in small systems.

## Key numbers

- The uncertainty limit for source size in current high-energy experiments is about 1 fm.
- The typical resolving width of the correlation function is \(\overline{q}=50\) MeV, which corresponds to a source size of 4 fm.
- For a Gaussian momentum spectrum \(f(\textbf{p})=\frac{1}{(2\pi \Delta p^2)^{3/2}}e^{-\frac{\textbf{p}^2}{2\Delta p^2}}\), the spatial overlap integral between emitters at positions \(\textbf{x}\) and \(\textbf{x}^{\prime}\) is \(I_{x^{\prime}x}=e^{-\frac{\Delta p^2(\textbf{x} - \textbf{x}^{\prime})^2}{2}}\).
- The time-domain smearing due to the uncertainty principle is given by \(G_{x^{\prime}x}^t = e^{-\frac{\Delta p^2(t - t^{\prime})^2}{2}}\).

## Context

This work extends the foundational femtoscopy formalism of Kopylov and Podgoretsky (1960s) and the later development of homogeneity lengths by Sinyukov and Akkelin (1990s) to the regime of very small sources created in \(pp\) and \(e^+e^-\) collisions, where the standard continuous-limit approximation fails. The authors explicitly correct the double-counting problem in the standard derivation of the correlation function for small numbers of emitters, building on the partially coherent phase formalism of Tolstov (1970s). The results enable more accurate femtoscopic analysis of small collision systems, where non-femtoscopic correlations from minijets and initial-state fluctuations must also be accounted for, and where the factorization property between femtoscopic and non-femtoscopic contributions is observed numerically.
