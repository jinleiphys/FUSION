# Supernovae and Neutrinos

> A long-standing problem in supernova physics is how to measure the total energy and temperature of $ν_μ$, $ν_τ$, $\barν_μ$, and $\barν_τ$. While of the highest importance, this is very difficult because these flavors only have neutral-current detector interactions. We propose that neutrino-proton elastic scattering, $ν+ p \to ν+ p$, can be used for the detection of supernova neutrinos in scintillator detectors. It should be emphasized immediately that the dominant signal is on {\it free} protons. Though the proton recoil kinetic energy spectrum is soft, with $T_p \simeq 2 E_ν^2/M_p$, and the scintillation light output from slow, heavily ionizing protons is quenched, the yield above a realistic threshold is nearly as large as that from $\barν_e + p \to e^+ + n$. In addition, the measured proton spectrum is related to the incident neutrino spectrum. The ability to detect this signal would give detectors like KamLAND and Borexino a crucial and unique role in the quest to detect supernova neutrinos. These results are now published: J. F. Beacom, W. M. Farr and P. Vogel, Phys. Rev. D {\bf 66}, 033001 (2002) [arXiv:hep-ph/0205220]; the details are given there.

## Key claim

Neutrino-proton elastic scattering, previously unrecognized as a useful detection channel for low-energy neutrinos, can provide a yield in scintillator detectors nearly as large as the standard inverse beta decay reaction, even after accounting for the severe quenching of scintillation light from slow protons and a realistic detector threshold of 0.2 MeV electron equivalent energy.

## Method

The authors calculate the expected proton recoil spectrum from neutrino-proton elastic scattering for a standard supernova at 10 kpc, using Fermi-Dirac neutrino spectra with temperatures of 3.5 MeV (ν_e), 5 MeV (anti-ν_e), and 8 MeV (ν_μ, ν_τ, and their antiparticles). They apply Birk's Law with dE/dx tables for protons in the KamLAND oil-scintillator mixture to convert true proton kinetic energies into quenched electron-equivalent energies, then perform Monte Carlo simulations of the supernova signal in a 1 kton detector and chi-squared fits to determine how well the total energy and temperature of the four "hot" flavors can be extracted from the measured proton spectrum shape.

## Key numbers

* Total expected yield from ν + p → ν + p above a 0.2 MeV electron-equivalent threshold (true proton energy 1.2 MeV) is 273 events in 1 kton of KamLAND for a standard supernova at 10 kpc, compared to about 310 events from anti-ν_e + p → e+ + n.
* The four hot flavors (ν_μ, ν_τ, anti-ν_μ, anti-ν_τ) contribute 253 of the 273 events above threshold (127 from the two neutrino flavors and 126 from the two antineutrino flavors), while ν_e contributes only 3 events and anti-ν_e contributes 17 events.
* The total energy and temperature of the four hot flavors can each be determined with roughly 10% error in KamLAND, based on Monte Carlo fits to the proton spectrum shape.
* For Borexino (0.3 kton useful volume, hydrogen-to-carbon ratio 1.3:1), the precision on total energy and temperature should be about 20% or better, scaling as 1/√N.
* The supernova binding energy is about 3 × 10^53 ergs, with each of the six neutrino/antineutrino types nominally carrying 1/6 of this, though this equipartition assumption has an uncertainty of at least 50%.
* The proton quenching factor (E_equiv/T_p) is roughly 1/2 at 10 MeV, 1/3 at 6 MeV, and 1/4 at 3 MeV proton kinetic energy.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing problem of measuring the total energy and temperature of ν_μ, ν_τ, anti-ν_μ, and anti-ν_τ from supernovae, which had no experimental solution because these flavors only interact via neutral-current reactions where neutrino energy is typically not measurable. The proposed technique gives detectors like KamLAND and Borexino a crucial role in supernova neutrino detection, complementing charged-current measurements of ν_e and anti-ν_e in other detectors. Prior approaches such as elastic scattering on electrons (Cabrera, 1985) or coherent scattering on whole nuclei have been discussed but never built, while oscillation-based techniques (Barger, 2002; Minakata, 2002) require assuming exact energy equipartition among flavors, which has at least 50% uncertainty. The ability to separately measure total energy and temperature for each flavor will enable model-independent studies of neutrino oscillations and allow determination of the newly-formed neutron star mass.
