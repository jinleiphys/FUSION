# A resolution of the cosmic Lithium problem

> In 1982, Monique and Francois Spite discovered that the 7Li abundance in the atmosphere of old metal-poor dwarf stars in the galactic halo was independent of metallicity and temperature. Since then, 7Li abundance in the Universe has become a subject of intrigue, because there is less of it in Population II dwarf stars (by a factor of 3) than standard big bang nucleosynthesis predicts. Here we show how quark-novae (QNe) occurring in the wake of Pop. III stars, can elegantly produce an A(Li) ~ 2.2 Lithium plateau in Pop. II (low-mass) stars formed in the pristine cloud swept up by the mixed SN+QN ejecta. We also find an increase in the scatter as well as an eventual drop in A(Li) below the Spite plateau values for very low metallicity ([Fe/H] < -3) in excellent agreement with observations. We propose a solution to the discrepancy between the Big Bang Nucleosynthesis 7Li abundance and the Spite plateau and list some implications and predictions of our model.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the Spite plateau of constant lithium abundance in metal-poor Population II stars is not primordial Big Bang Nucleosynthesis lithium, but instead is produced by quark-novae (QNe) occurring after Population III supernovae, with the specific plateau value of A(Li) ~ 2.2 emerging from spallation reactions in the mixed SN+QN ejecta. The model claims to reproduce both the low scatter at higher metallicities and the observed drop and increased scatter at very low metallicities ([Fe/H] < -3), but the results depend on specific choices of fiducial parameters including the time delay between supernova and quark-nova explosions.

## Method

The author simulates spallation reactions between relativistic neutron-rich ejecta from quark-novae (with neutron energies of 5-10 GeV and Lorentz factor ~10) and the expanding ejecta from preceding Population III Type II supernovae, using a layered onion-like model of the SN ejecta composition. The key parameter is the time delay t_delay between the SN and QN explosions, which determines the density of each target layer (56Ni, 32S, 28Si, 24Mg, 20Ne, 16O, 12C) at the moment of neutron impact and thus the spallation efficiency. The spallation products are calculated using Poisson-distributed multiple hits per mean-free-path, with the resulting abundances in the swept-up cloud determining the composition of subsequently formed Population II stars.

## Key numbers

* The Spite plateau lithium abundance is A(Li) ~ 2.2, defined as A(Li) = log(N_7Li/N_H) + 12, observed in stars with effective temperatures 5900 K < T_eff < 6300 K and metallicities -2.8 < [Fe/H] < -2
* The primordial BBN lithium abundance from WMAP is A(Li) = 2.72 (+0.05/-0.06), which is three times higher than the observed Spite plateau value
* The scatter in 7Li abundance among Spite plateau stars is ~0.05 dex
* The QN ejecta has average mass M_QN ~ 10^(-3) M_sun, Lorentz factor Gamma_QN ~ 10, and total kinetic energy E_QN^KE ~ 2 x 10^52 erg
* Typical neutron energy in the QN ejecta is E_QN ~ 10 GeV
* The critical time delays are t_outer ~ 8.3 days and t_inner ~ 6.3 days for fiducial parameters (v_sn = 5000 km/s, M_Ni_SN = 0.1 M_sun)
* For t_delay = 10 days, the number of spallation mean-free-paths in each layer are: N_mfp_Ni ~ 0.69, N_mfp_O ~ 15.10, N_mfp_C ~ 1.64
* The spallation cross section is sigma_sp_A_T = 45 A_T^0.7 mb
* The average neutron+proton multiplicity on 56Ni target is zeta_av ~ 13 for 10 GeV neutrons, yielding spallated nucleons with energy ~0.77 GeV exiting the Ni layer

## Context

This work addresses the long-standing discrepancy between the primordial 7Li abundance predicted by standard Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (Cyburt 2008; Coc and Vangioni 2010) and the lower value observed in the Spite plateau (Spite 1982; Asplund 2006). It extends the author's previous quark-nova model (Ouyed 2013; Ouyed et al. 2011) to the lithium problem, offering an alternative to proposed solutions such as lithium destruction in Population III stars (Sbordone 2010), non-standard BBN models with decaying supersymmetric particles (Jedamzik et al. 2006; Cyburt et al. 2010), modified reaction cross-sections (Chakraborty, Fields and Olive 2011), and diffusion with mixing in stellar photospheres (Richard et al. 2005). The model enables predictions for the behavior of alpha-elements and sulfur abundances at extremely low metallicities that can be tested against future observations of metal-poor stars.
