# Isomer production by multi-photon excitation

> The multi-photon excitation to the $8$-eV nuclear isomeric state $^{229\text{m}}$Th in the direct laser-nucleus interaction is investigated theoretically. We solve the time-dependent Schrödinger equation with the method which allows us to study the $n$-photon absorption in the nuclear excitation in the direct laser-nucleus interaction. Based on the laser facilities available currently or in the near future, we analyze the impact of the laser parameters on the excitation probability of the multi-photon excitation. The possibilities of the $2$-, $3$- and $4$-photon excitations to the isomeric state $^{229\text{m}}$Th from the ground state are discussed in details. Our results show the strong impact of the laser intensity and pulse duration on the multi-photon excitation probability. The onset of high-order effects in the multi-photon excitation in the direct laser-nucleus interaction is also revealed. Our findings open new possibilities to study the multi-photon laser-nucleus interaction in high-power laser facilities.

# Isomer production by multi-photon excitation

## Key claim

The authors theoretically demonstrate that multi-photon excitation (2-, 3-, and 4-photon) of the 8-eV nuclear isomeric state 229mTh from the ground state is possible via direct laser-nucleus interaction, but only under the condition that laser intensities similar to or higher than the current experimental record (1.1 × 10^23 W/cm^2) are used; for nuclear clock applications, lower intensities with longer pulse durations are recommended but remain impractical due to unresolved issues including precise process control and the need for stable light sources with extremely narrow linewidths.

## Method

The authors solve the time-dependent Schrodinger equation for an isolated two-level system (ground state and isomeric state of 229Th) subjected to a Gaussian-shaped laser pulse, using a numerical method that sorts processes by photon absorption number via a tridiagonal transfer matrix. They adopt the excitation energy E_I = 8.35574 eV, lifetime tau_I = 1740 s, reduced transition probability B(M1, I to G) = 0.022 W.u., and magnetic dipole moments mu_G = 0.360 mu_N and mu_I = -0.37 mu_N for the ground and isomeric states respectively. A two-step approximation is introduced to handle long pulse durations by dividing the Gaussian envelope into intervals and using a similarity transformation to reduce matrix multiplication steps.

## Key numbers

- Excitation energy of 229mTh: E_I = 8.35574 eV, lifetime tau_I = 1740 s, reduced transition probability B(M1, I to G) = 0.022 W.u. (Weisskopf unit), taken from PhysRevLett.132.182501
- Magnetic dipole moment of ground state 229Th: mu_G = 0.360 mu_N; isomeric state moment mu_I = -0.37 mu_N, derived from ratio mu_I/mu_G = -1.04 from Thielking (2018)
- Laser intensity of 1.1 × 10^23 W/cm^2 achieved by CoReLS (Yoon 2021); same team obtained 5.5 × 10^22 W/cm^2 in 2019 with pulse duration 19.6 fs and peak power 4 PW
- For 2-photon excitation at I = 10^24 W/cm^2: probability about 10^-1 for pulse duration FWHM of 1000 fs, about 10^-3 for FWHM of 100 fs
- For 2-photon excitation: highest excitation probability about 0.3 across intensities from 10^20 to 10^24 W/cm^2, with corresponding pulse duration proportional to inverse of peak intensity
- For 3-photon excitation at I = 10^24 W/cm^2: probability about 10^-4 for pulse duration FWHM of 1000 fs
- For 4-photon excitation: probability proportional to fourth power of peak laser intensity and square of pulse duration FWHM (when FWHM longer than about 10 fs)

## Context

This work extends the theoretical study of multi-photon nuclear excitation beyond the 1-photon direct excitation recently realized experimentally (PhysRevLett.132.182501, PhysRevLett.133.013201, Zhang 2024) and beyond the hyperfine mixing mechanism studied in PhysRevLett.133.152503. The authors build on the original concept of multi-photon absorption by Goeppert-Mayer (1931) and apply it to the 229Th system, which has the lowest known nuclear excitation energy at about 8 eV. The results enable future experimental studies of multi-photon laser-nucleus interaction at high-power laser facilities such as CoReLS, LMJ, and NIF, though the authors caution that practical nuclear clock applications remain distant due to unresolved control and stability requirements.
