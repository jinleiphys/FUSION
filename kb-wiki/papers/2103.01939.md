# Opportunities of OO and $p$O collisions at the LHC

> This is the summary document of the virtual workshop "Opportunities of OO and $p$O collisions at the LHC", which took place Feb 4-10, 2021. We briefly review the presented perspectives on the physics opportunities of oxygen--oxygen and proton--oxygen collisions. The full workshop program and the recordings are available at cern.ch/OppOatLHC.

# Opportunities of OO and pO collisions at the LHC

## Key claim
Oxygen–oxygen and proton–oxygen collisions at the LHC during Run 3 offer unique discovery opportunities across soft QCD, hard probes, and cosmic-ray physics, but the actual feasibility and performance depend on unresolved accelerator uncertainties (SPS space charge limit, emittance, bunch intensity, ion transmutation) that can only be assessed by a pilot oxygen run.

## Method
The paper is a summary document of a virtual workshop (Feb 4–10, 2021) with 27 invited talks and 8 discussion sessions. It reviews presentations on oxygen beam feasibility at the LHC accelerator complex, theoretical descriptions of initial state and soft dynamics in small systems, experimental projections for hard probes in OO and pO collisions, and the impact of pO data on cosmic-ray modelling. The organisers present a consensus view not officially endorsed by speakers or collaborations.

## Key numbers
- Target luminosity for OO physics run: L_OO ≈ 0.5 nb⁻¹ for ALICE, ATLAS and CMS
- Target luminosity for pO physics run: L_pO ≈ 2 nb⁻¹ for ATLAS/LHCf and LHCb; L_pO > 5 nb⁻¹ for ALICE and CMS
- LHCb minimum physics goals with light hadrons: L_pO ≈ 0.2 nb⁻¹
- LHCf minimum physics program: L_pO ≈ 0.7 nb⁻¹ at low pileup (mu < 0.02)
- Center-of-mass energies: sqrt(s_NN)(OO) = 7 TeV, sqrt(s_NN)(pO) = 9.9 TeV
- Tentative pilot run schedule: 6–8 days total (2–3 days OO commissioning, 1 day OO physics; 0.5–1 days pO commissioning, 2.5–3 days pO physics)
- Luminosity precision achievable via van der Meer scans: 1.5–3% for OO and pO collisions
- ALICE projected systematic uncertainty on minimum bias hadron R_AA: about 5% (statistical uncertainties subdominant)
- ALICE projected sensitivity to Delta p_T ~ 160 MeV shifts in hadron-triggered jet spectra
- ALICE projected 10% accuracy for D meson R_AA and about 30% accuracy (statistics dominated) for v_2 measurements
- ATLAS projections: <5% statistical uncertainties on jet (50 GeV/c < p_T^j < 240 GeV/c) and hadron (p_T^h < 80 GeV/c) R_AA for L_OO ~ 0.5 nb⁻¹
- Model calculations: energy loss signal may be 5–10% for hadrons with p_T > 20 GeV in OO
- Forward kinematics of pO collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 9.9 TeV corresponds to cosmic rays with energy > 10¹⁶ eV
- ALICE projected ~5% uncertainties on hadron R_AA for p_T < 50 GeV/c in OO with less than 3% uncertainty due to interpolated pp reference
- ALICE experimental projections for anisotropic flow coefficients v_n in OO with integrated luminosity L_OO ~ 1 nb⁻¹
- Thermal photon radiation enhancement in OO compared to PbPb: factor of 2
- ALICE projections for strange hadron yields, deuterons, He-3 and hypertritons in OO
- OO femtoscopy: hadron source size of 2–3 fm compared to 1 fm for pp
- LHCb acceptance for identified light-hadron spectra: 2 < eta < 5
- LHCf forward detectors: |eta| > 8.4
- LHCb fixed-target pO at sqrt(s_NN) = 81 GeV with acceptance 3.9 < eta' < 6.9 in oxygen rest-frame
- Z and W± measurements in pO would require L_pO ~ 2 pb⁻¹
- Current 50% spread among models for identified particle spectra in pO collisions

## Context
This workshop summary extends the scientific case established in the CERN Yellow report (Citron et al., 2018) for a short oxygen beam programme in LHC Run 3. It addresses open questions about the transition of collective phenomena from large to small systems, where similar signatures have been observed in peripheral PbPb, pPb, and high-multiplicity pp collisions (Zhao, 2021). The document enables future planning for a pilot oxygen run, identifies necessary accelerator tests (scheduled May 25–29, 2021), and highlights the need for pp references at matching center-of-mass energies to control systematic uncertainties in nuclear modification factor measurements.
