# Equation of State of Decompressed Quark Matter, and Observational Signatures of Quark-Star Mergers

> Quark stars are challenging to confirm or exclude observationally because they can have similar masses and radii as neutron stars. By performing the first calculation of the non-equilibrium equation of state of decompressed quark matter at finite temperature, we determine the properties of the ejecta from binary quark-star or quark star-black hole mergers. We account for all relevant physical processes during the ejecta evolution, including quark nugget evaporation and cooling, and weak interactions. We find that these merger ejecta can differ significantly from those in neutron star mergers, depending on the binding energy of quark matter. For relatively high binding energies, quark star mergers are unlikely to produce r-process elements and kilonova signals. We propose that future observations of binary mergers and kilonovae could impose stringent constraints on the binding energy of quark matter and the existence of quark stars.

## Key claim

The merger ejecta from binary quark-star or quark star-black hole mergers can differ significantly from those in neutron star mergers, and for relatively high quark matter binding energies (e.g., ≳ 50 MeV), such mergers are unlikely to produce r-process elements and kilonova signals; however, the actual outcome depends on the poorly constrained binding energy of quark matter, and the authors propose that future kilonova observations could impose constraints on this binding energy and the existence of quark stars.

## Method

The authors performed the first calculation of the non-equilibrium equation of state of decompressed quark matter at finite temperature, modeling the evolution of merger ejecta as a quark nugget gas (QNG) composed of nuggets and a nucleon gas phase. They accounted for quark nugget evaporation and cooling, weak interactions (neutron-proton conversion via electron/positron capture), and the Coulomb barrier at the nugget surface that suppresses proton reabsorption, evolving the system using differential equations for nugget internal energy, nucleon number, and baryon number. The ejecta evolution was modeled with a homologous expansion profile, using density and pressure tracks from a binary quark-star general relativistic hydrodynamical simulation by Zhu and Rezzolla (2021) as input.

## Key numbers

- The saturation timescale for nugget evaporation in binary quark-star merger ejecta is τ_sat ∼ 1.7 × 10⁻¹³ s × (0.1 fm⁻³ / n_B) × (10 MeV / T)^(1/2) × (A / 10³⁰)^(1/3), much shorter than the ejecta expansion time τ_exp ≳ 10⁻³ s.
- The Coulomb barrier height at the nugget surface is E_c = V(R_s) = 3V_q/4, with V_q ≃ m_s²/(4μ_q) ≈ 8 MeV for strange quark mass m_s = 100 MeV.
- The initial density for homologous expansion was set to ρ₀ = 5 × 10¹² g/cm³, with ejecta mass M_ej = 0.01 M_⊙, ejecta velocity v_ej = 0.1c, and adiabatic index γ = 4/3.
- The critical boundary between QNG and nucleon gas was calculated using the equilibrium condition ρ = m_n n_n^eq + m_p n_p^eq.
- For the bag model, the "standard value" of the bag parameter B = (145 MeV)⁴ gives ΔE = 111 MeV, while B = (212 MeV)⁴ from lattice calculations gives ΔE = -272 MeV (unstable quark matter).
- An upper limit from tidal deformability constraints (GW170817) gives ΔE ≤ 105 MeV (95% confidence level) within the bag model framework.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of quark star mergers by Bauswein et al. (2009), Bauswein et al. (2010), Zhu and Rezzolla (2021), and others, which focused on bulk quark matter at supra-nuclear densities but did not treat the sub-nuclear density regime relevant to ejecta. It also improves upon earlier calculations of quark nugget evaporation in binary quark-star mergers by Dore et al. (2019) and Bauswein et al. (2022), which used a formalism from Alcock et al. (1985) for early-universe nugget evaporation but did not account for saturation of evaporated nucleons. The results enable future numerical simulations of binary quark-star or quark star-black hole mergers to use a self-consistent, evolving thermal adiabatic index rather than the constant values (7/4 or 4/3) used previously, and they provide a framework for using kilonova observations to constrain the quark matter binding energy.
