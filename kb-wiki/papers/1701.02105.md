# Search for the QCD Critical Point with Fluctuations of Conserved Quantities in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions at RHIC : An Overview

> Fluctuations of conserved quantities, such as baryon, electric charge and strangeness number, are sensitive observables in relativistic heavy-ion collisions to probe the QCD phase transition and search for the QCD critical point. In this paper, we review the experimental measurements of the cumulants (up to fourth order) of event-by-event net-proton (proxy for net-baryon), net-charge and net-kaon (proxy for net-strangeness) multiplicity distributions in Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=7.7, 11.5, 14.5, 19.6, 27, 39, 62.4, 200$ GeV from the first phase of beam energy scan program at the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider (RHIC). We also summarize the data analysis methods of suppressing the volume fluctuations, auto-correlations and the unified description of efficiency correction and error estimation. Based on theoretical and model calculations, we will discuss the characteristic signatures of critical point as well as backgrounds for the fluctuation observables in heavy-ion collisions. The physics implications and the future second phase of the beam energy scan (2019-2020) at RHIC will be also discussed.

## Key claim

This review paper summarizes the experimental measurements of cumulants (up to fourth order) of net-proton, net-charge, and net-kaon multiplicity distributions from the first phase of the RHIC Beam Energy Scan program, but the authors note that definitive evidence for the QCD critical point has not yet been found and that the search requires careful modeling of critical phenomena and background contributions, with the burden on experiments to determine the location of the critical point or first-order phase boundary.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental data from the STAR experiment at RHIC for Au+Au collisions at eight beam energies (7.7, 11.5, 14.5, 19.6, 27, 39, 62.4, and 200 GeV) collected during 2010-2014. The analysis uses event-by-event fluctuations of net-proton (as a proxy for net-baryon), net-charge, and net-kaon (as a proxy for net-strangeness) multiplicity distributions, with cumulants up to fourth order calculated after applying centrality bin width correction, volume fluctuation suppression, auto-correlation removal, and efficiency correction with unified error estimation. The experimental results are compared to theoretical calculations from the Hadron Resonance Gas model, Lattice QCD, and various dynamical models (UrQMD, AMPT, JAM) to identify signatures of the QCD critical point.

## Key numbers

- Beam energies studied: sqrt(s_NN) = 7.7, 11.5, 14.5, 19.6, 27, 39, 62.4, and 200 GeV in Au+Au collisions
- Lattice QCD predictions for the QCD critical point location range from (mu_B^E = 285 MeV, T^E = 155 MeV) to (mu_B^E = 504 MeV, T^E = 115 MeV) depending on the calculation method and collaboration
- The ratio chi_6^B/chi_2^B from Lattice QCD shows negative values near and above the transition temperature and positive values around unity at low temperatures
- The NNLO correction to the pressure from Lattice QCD is found to be less than 5% when mu_B/T is less than 2
- In the HRG model, kappa_B * sigma_B^2 = 1 for net-baryon fluctuations under Boltzmann approximation

## Context

This review extends the experimental program of the RHIC Beam Energy Scan (BES) first phase (2010-2014) and sets the stage for the second phase (2019-2020) with STAR detector upgrades. The work builds on theoretical predictions from Stephanov (1998, 1999), Jeon and Koch (1999), and Asakawa (2000) that fluctuations of conserved charges are sensitive to the correlation length near the critical point. The paper discusses comparisons with Lattice QCD calculations from the BNL-Bielefeld-CCNU collaboration and the HotQCD and Wuppertal-Budapest collaborations, as well as model calculations from UrQMD and AMPT. The results enable future searches for the QCD critical point by establishing baselines from the Hadron Resonance Gas model and identifying non-critical background contributions that must be understood before claiming a critical point signal.
