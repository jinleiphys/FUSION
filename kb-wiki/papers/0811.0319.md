# Single and low-lying states dominance in two-neutrino double-beta decay

> A theoretical analysis of the single-state dominance hypothesis for the two-neutrino double-beta decay rates is performed on the examples of the double-beta decays of 100Mo, 116Cd, and 128Te. We also test the validity of an extended low-lying-state dominance that takes into account the contributions of the low-lying excited states in the intermediate nucleus to the double-beta decay rates. This study has been accomplished for all the double-beta emitters for which we have experimental information on their half-lives. The theoretical framework is a proton-neutron quasiparticle random-phase approximation based on a deformed Skyrme Hartree-Fock mean field with pairing correlations. Our calculations indicate that there are not clear evidences for single- or low-lying-state dominance in the two-neutrino double-beta decay. Finally, we investigate the single electron energy distributions of the outgoing electrons in the double-beta decay processes with an exact treatment of the energy denominators, which could help to a more comprehensive analysis of NEMO-3 data.

## Key claim

The paper finds that there is no clear evidence for single-state dominance (SSD) or low-lying-state dominance (LLSD) in two-neutrino double-beta decay, contrary to earlier claims that relied on experimental input to adjust theoretical parameters. The authors show that while SSD appears to hold experimentally within 90% for 100Mo, the theoretical calculations indicate that higher-lying intermediate states contribute significantly to the total matrix element in all cases studied.

## Method

The authors used a proton-neutron quasiparticle random-phase approximation (pnQRPA) based on a deformed Skyrme Hartree-Fock mean field with pairing correlations (SLy4 interaction), including separable residual spin-isospin interactions in both particle-hole and particle-particle channels. They calculated Gamow-Teller strength distributions and two-neutrino double-beta decay matrix elements for 100Mo, 116Cd, and 128Te, comparing contributions from the ground state only (SSD), low-lying states (LLSD), and the full set of intermediate 1+ states. They also computed single electron energy distributions using an exact treatment of the energy denominators.

## Key numbers

- For 100Mo: experimental half-life is 7.1 x 10^18 y, corresponding to a matrix element (M^{2nu beta beta}_{GT})_exp = 0.241 MeV^-1 (using g_A = 1.25); SSD hypothesis is fulfilled experimentally within 90%
- For 116Cd: experimental half-life is 3.0 x 10^19 y, corresponding to (M^{2nu beta beta}_{GT})_exp = 0.127 MeV^-1; no clear evidence for SSD due to spread of data from different sources
- For 128Te: experimental half-life is 2.5 x 10^24 y, corresponding to (M^{2nu beta beta}_{GT})_exp = 0.043 MeV^-1; both SSD1 and SSD2 predictions overestimate the experimental half-life
- For 100Mo: theoretical LLSD calculations up to 2.6 MeV of excitation energy account for only 65% of the experimental matrix element; the remaining 40% comes from higher excitation energy states
- For 116Cd: theoretical LLSD up to 2.5 MeV accounts for only half of the experimental matrix element; the total calculated matrix element agrees well with experiment
- For 100Mo: the GT resonance measured at 13-14 MeV with strength B(GT^-) = 23 is reproduced in the calculation, yielding a peak centered at 16 MeV with similar strength
- For 116Cd: the GT resonance measured at 14.5 MeV with B(GT^-) = 26 is reproduced in the calculation, centered at 16 MeV
- For 128Te: the GT resonance measured at 13.14 MeV with B(GT^-) = 34.24 is reproduced in the calculation, yielding B(GT^-) = 35 centered at 15 MeV

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of the SSD hypothesis by Civitarese and Suhonen (1998) within a pnQRPA formalism, which had found SSD realized in most cases but relied on adjusting single-particle levels and fixing the particle-particle force strength to reproduce experimental EC and beta- decay rates. The present calculations use a selfconsistent deformed Skyrme Hartree-Fock mean field without such adjustments, and find that SSD and LLSD are not clearly supported. The study also extends the analysis to all observed 2nu beta beta emitters, building on earlier running-sum calculations by Simkovic et al. for 76Ge using a Woods-Saxon mean field and by Nakada et al. for 36Ar, 54Fe, and 58Ni using the shell model. The single electron energy distributions with exact energy denominators could help analyze NEMO-3 data.
