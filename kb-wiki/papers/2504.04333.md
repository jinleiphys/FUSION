# Study of nucleon and $Δ$ resonances from a systematic analysis of $K^\astΣ$ photoproduction

> A systematic analysis of the $K^\astΣ$ photoproduction off proton is performed with all the available differential cross section data. We carry out a strategy different from the previous studies of these reactions, where, instead of fixing the parameters of the added resonances from PDG or pentaquark models, we add the resonance with a specific $J^P$ and leave its parameters to be determined from the experimental data. When adding only one resonance, the best result is to add one $N(3/2^-)$ resonance around $2097$ MeV, which substantially reduces the $χ^2$ per degree of freedom to $1.35$. There are two best solutions when adding two resonances, one is to add one $N^\ast(3/2^-)$ and one $N^\ast(7/2^-)$, the other is to add one $N^\ast(3/2^-)$ and one $Δ^\ast(7/2^-)$, leading the $χ^2$ per degree of freedom to be $1.10$ and $1.09$, respectively. The mass values of the $N^\ast(3/2^-)$ in the two resonance solutions are both near $2070$ MeV. Our solutions indicate that the $N(3/2^-)$ resonance around $2080$ MeV is strongly coupled with the $K^\astΣ$ final state and support the molecular picture of $N(2080, 3/2^-)$.

## Key claim

By leaving resonance parameters free rather than fixing them from PDG or pentaquark models, the authors find that a single $N(3/2^-)$ resonance around 2097 MeV provides a good description of $K^\ast\Sigma$ photoproduction data, and that two-resonance solutions (adding either a $N^\ast(7/2^-)$ or a $\Delta^\ast(7/2^-)$) further improve the fit, though the data cannot clearly distinguish between nucleon and $\Delta$ resonances with the same $J^P$.

## Method

The authors performed a tree-level effective Lagrangian analysis of $\gamma p \to K^{\ast 0}\Sigma^+$ and $\gamma p \to K^{\ast +}\Sigma^0$ reactions, including $t$-channel $\kappa$, $K$, $K^\ast$ exchanges, $u$-channel $\Lambda$, $\Sigma$, $\Sigma^\ast(1385)$ exchanges, $s$-channel nucleon and $\Delta(1232)$ exchanges, and an interaction current. They added nucleon or $\Delta$ resonances with specific $J^P$ to the $s$-channel, leaving their masses, widths, and coupling constants as free parameters to be determined from fitting all available differential cross section data from CLAS and CBELSA/TAPS.

## Key numbers

- With only background contributions, $\chi^2/\text{ndf} = 3.40$ for the fit to all differential cross section data.
- Adding one $N^\ast(3/2^-)$ resonance gives the best single-resonance solution with $\chi^2/\text{ndf} = 1.35$, with resonance mass $M_R = 2095.9 \pm 5.7$ MeV and width $\Gamma_R = 66.3 \pm 10.0$ MeV.
- Adding one $N^\ast(3/2^-)$ and one $N^\ast(7/2^-)$ gives $\chi^2/\text{ndf} = 1.10$, with the $N^\ast(3/2^-)$ mass at $2070.3 \pm 3.3$ MeV and the $N^\ast(7/2^-)$ mass at $2433.3 \pm 17.6$ MeV.
- Adding one $N^\ast(3/2^-)$ and one $\Delta^\ast(7/2^-)$ gives $\chi^2/\text{ndf} = 1.09$, with the $N^\ast(3/2^-)$ mass at $2070.6 \pm 3.1$ MeV and the $\Delta^\ast(7/2^-)$ mass at $2437.1 \pm 14.0$ MeV.
- Other single-resonance solutions with $\chi^2/\text{ndf}$ below 2.0 include $N^\ast(1/2^-)$ at $2072.1$ MeV ($\chi^2/\text{ndf}=1.77$), $N^\ast(5/2^-)$ at $2085.9$ MeV ($\chi^2/\text{ndf}=1.67$), $\Delta^\ast(1/2^-)$ at $2081.7$ MeV ($\chi^2/\text{ndf}=1.66$), $\Delta^\ast(3/2^-)$ at $2092.0$ MeV ($\chi^2/\text{ndf}=1.44$), and $\Delta^\ast(5/2^-)$ at $2065.8$ MeV ($\chi^2/\text{ndf}=1.75$).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of $\gamma p \to K^\ast\Sigma$ reactions by Kim et al. (2012, 2013), Wang et al. (2017), and Ben et al. (2023), which fixed resonance parameters from PDG or pentaquark model predictions. The authors' strategy of leaving resonance parameters free allows them to identify a $N(3/2^-)$ resonance near 2080 MeV that is strongly coupled to the $K^\ast\Sigma$ final state, supporting the molecular picture of $N(2080,3/2^-)$ proposed by He et al. (2017), Lin et al. (2018), and Zou (2018) as a strange partner of the $P_c$ pentaquark states. The results provide predictions for polarization observables that can be tested by future experiments.
