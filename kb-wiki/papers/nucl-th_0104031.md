# Interplay of soft and hard processes and hadron $p_T$ spectra in $pA$ and $AA$ collisions

> Motivated by a schematic model of multiple parton scattering within the Glauber formalism, the transverse momentum spectra in $pA$ and $AA$ collisions are analyzed in terms of a nuclear modification factor with respect to $pp$ collisions. The existing data at the CERN Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) energies are shown to be consistent with the picture of Glauber multiple scattering in which the interplay between soft and hard processes and the effect of absorptive processes lead to nontrivial nuclear modification of the particle spectra. Relative to the additive model of incoherent hard scattering, the spectra are enhanced at large $p_T$ (hard) by multiple scattering while suppressed at low $p_T$ (soft) by absorptive correction with the transition occurring at around a scale $p_0\sim 1-2$ GeV/$c$ that separates soft and hard processes. Around the same scale, the $p_T$ spectra in $pp$ collisions also change from an exponential form at low $p_T$ to a power-law behavior at high $p_T$. At very large $p_T\gg p_0$, the nuclear enhancement is shown to decrease like $1/p_T^2$. Implications of these nuclear effects on the study of jet quenching, parton thermalization and collective radial flow in high-energy $AA$ collisions are discussed.

# Interplay of soft and hard processes and hadron $p_T$ spectra in $pA$ and $AA$ collisions

## Key claim

The nuclear modification factor $R_{AB}(p_T)$ in $pA$ and $AA$ collisions can be explained by initial-state multiple parton scattering within a Glauber formalism, without requiring final-state effects such as parton energy loss, provided the transition scale between soft and hard processes lies at $p_0 \sim 1-2$ GeV/$c$. The authors demonstrate that existing CERN SPS data are consistent with this picture, but caution that at RHIC energies the same analysis framework could reveal the onset of parton energy loss.

## Method

The authors develop a schematic Glauber model of multiple parton scattering in which the differential parton-nucleon cross section is taken to have a regularized power-law form $h(p_T) = C/(p_T^2 + p_0^2)^n$, with $p_0$ as an infrared cutoff separating soft and hard processes. They compute the nuclear modification factor $R_A$ up to double-scattering order, including both the double-scattering enhancement and the absorptive correction to single scattering. Experimental hadron spectra from $pp$, $pA$, and $AA$ collisions at CERN SPS energies ($E_{\rm lab}=158$ and $200$ GeV) are fitted with a two-component parameterization (exponential at low $p_T$ plus power-law at high $p_T$) and then compared to the model predictions via the ratio $R_{AB}(p_T)$.

## Key numbers

- The transition scale separating soft and hard processes is $p_0 \sim 1-2$ GeV/$c$, identified as the momentum where $R_{AB}(p_T)=1$ and where $pp$ spectra change from exponential to power-law behavior.
- At very large $p_T \gg p_0$, the nuclear enhancement decreases like $1/p_T^2$.
- Fit parameters for $h^-$ in $pp$ at $E_{\rm lab}=158$ GeV: $p_0 = 2.43$ GeV/$c$, $n = 6.29$, $T_0 = 154$ MeV.
- Fit parameters for $h_0^-$ in $pp$ at $E_{\rm lab}=200$ GeV: $p_0 = 2.27$ GeV/$c$, $n = 6.22$, $T_0 = 154$ MeV.
- Fit parameters for charged hadrons in $p\bar{p}$ at $\sqrt{s}=200$ GeV: $p_0 = 1.75$ GeV/$c$, $n = 4.98$, $T_0 = 154$ MeV.
- The nuclear radius parameter used is $r_0 = 1.14$ fm.

## Context

This work extends the Glauber multiple-scattering formalism previously applied to parton production (Petersson and collaborators; Wang, 1990s) and provides a baseline for distinguishing initial-state effects from final-state effects in heavy-ion collisions. The analysis shows that CERN SPS data on $pA$ and $AA$ collisions are consistent with initial multiple scattering alone, without parton energy loss, contradicting earlier expectations of significant jet quenching at those energies. The framework enables future studies at RHIC to isolate parton energy loss (predicted by Gyulassy-Wang, Baier-Dokshitzer-Peigne-Schiff, Zakharov, and others) and to disentangle collective radial flow (hydrodynamic models) from initial multiple scattering by examining the mass dependence of the nuclear modification factor.
