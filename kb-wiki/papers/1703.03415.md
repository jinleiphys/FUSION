# Pseudomagnetic lens as a valley and chirality splitter in Dirac and Weyl materials

> It is proposed that strain-induced pseudomagnetic fields in Dirac and Weyl materials could be used as valley and chirality sensitive lenses for beams of Weyl quasiparticles. The study of the (pseudo-)magnetic lenses is performed by using the eikonal approximation for describing the Weyl quasiparticles propagation in magnetic and strain-induced pseudomagnetic fields. Analytical expressions for the locations of the principal foci and the focal lengths are obtained in the paraxial approximation in the models with uniform as well as nonuniform effective magnetic fields inside the lens. The results show that the left- and right-handed quasiparticles can be focused at different spatial locations when both magnetic and pseudomagnetic fields are applied. It is suggested that the use of magnetic and pseudomagnetic lenses could open new ways of producing and manipulating beams of chiral Weyl quasiparticles.

## Key claim

Strain-induced pseudomagnetic fields in Dirac and Weyl materials can act as valley- and chirality-sensitive lenses for beams of Weyl quasiparticles, but the maximum control of chiral beams requires the combination of both magnetic and pseudomagnetic fields; the Berry curvature provides only second-order corrections to the chirality splitting in Weyl materials but is crucial for achieving chirality separation in Dirac semimetals.

## Method

The authors used the eikonal (geometrical optics) approximation to describe the propagation of Weyl quasiparticles in the presence of uniform and nonuniform magnetic and strain-induced pseudomagnetic fields, incorporating Berry curvature corrections to the dispersion relation. Analytical expressions for the principal foci and focal lengths were derived in the paraxial approximation by solving the eikonal equation for the abbreviated action, with matching conditions at the lens boundaries. The analysis was performed for both Weyl materials (with two Weyl nodes) and Dirac materials that are hidden Z₂ Weyl semimetals.

## Key numbers

- Characteristic pseudomagnetic field strengths in Dirac and Weyl semimetals range from approximately B₅ ≈ 0.3 T (static torsion on a nanowire of Cd₃As₂) to approximately B₅ ≈ 15 T (bent thin film of Cd₃As₂)
- For Cd₃As₂, the Fermi velocity is v_F ≈ 9.8 eV·Å, and the characteristic magnetic field is |B*| ≈ 14 T for quasiparticle energy ε = 100 meV
- The lens length used in calculations was L = 10⁻² cm
- The focal lengths f_λ for opposite chiralities become different when both magnetic and pseudomagnetic fields are applied, with the splitting appearing at linear order in the fields
- For nonuniform fields, the effective lens size is L_eff = πξ/2, where ξ is the inhomogeneity scale

## Context

This work extends the concept of magnetic lenses (used in cathode ray tubes and electron microscopes, as developed by Busch and Knoll) to the domain of Dirac and Weyl materials, building on prior studies of strain-induced pseudomagnetic fields in graphene and Weyl semimetals by Zubkov (2015), Cortijo (2016), Grushin and Vishwanath (2016), Pikulin (2016), and Liu and Pikulin (2016). The results enable experimental control of chiral beam splitting in condensed matter systems, potentially allowing "chiral distillation" and steady states with nonzero chiral asymmetry, detectable via local probes of electric current densities.
