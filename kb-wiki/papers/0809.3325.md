# Properties of asymmetric nuclear matter in different approaches

> Properties of asymmetric nuclear matter are derived from various many-body approaches. This includes phenomenological ones like the Skyrme Hartree-Fock and relativistic mean field approaches, which are adjusted to fit properties of nuclei, as well as more microscopic attempts like the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approximation, a self-consistent Greens function method and the so-called $V_{lowk}$ approach, which are based on realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions which reproduce the nucleon-nucleon phase shifts. These microscopic approaches are supplemented by a density-dependent contact interaction to achieve the empirical saturation property of symmetric nuclear matter. The predictions of all these approaches are discussed for nuclear matter at high densities in $β$-equilibrium. Special attention is paid to behavior of the isovector component of the effective mass in neutron-rich matter.

## Key claim

The paper systematically compares five many-body approaches (Skyrme Hartree-Fock, density-dependent relativistic mean field, Brueckner-Hartree-Fock, self-consistent Green's function, and Vlowk) for asymmetric nuclear matter, but the microscopic approaches (BHF, SCGF, Vlowk) require an additional density-dependent contact interaction fitted to reproduce the empirical saturation point of symmetric nuclear matter before their predictions for neutron-rich matter can be meaningfully compared.

## Method

The authors perform non-relativistic Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations using the SLy4 parameterization and relativistic mean-field calculations using a density-dependent DDRMF model fitted to Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock results with the Bonn A potential. For the microscopic approaches, they start from the realistic CDBONN nucleon-nucleon interaction and solve the Bethe-Goldstone equation for BHF, extend it with hole-hole propagation terms for SCGF, and apply a unitary-model-operator approach to derive the Vlowk interaction with a cutoff of 2 fm^-1; all three microscopic methods are supplemented by an isoscalar contact interaction (with alpha=0.5) fitted to yield saturation at rho_0=0.16 fm^-3 and E/A=-16.0 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density rho_0: 0.160 fm^-3 for SLy4, 0.178 fm^-3 for DDRMF, 0.374 fm^-3 for BHF without contact term, 0.161 fm^-3 for BHF with contact term, 0.212 fm^-3 for SCGF without contact term, 0.160 fm^-3 for SCGF with contact term, 0.160 fm^-3 for Vlowk with contact term
- Binding energy at saturation E/A(rho_0): -15.97 MeV (SLy4), -16.25 MeV (DDRMF), -23.97 MeV (BHF without ct), -16.01 MeV (BHF with ct), -11.47 MeV (SCGF without ct), -16.06 MeV (SCGF with ct), -16.0 MeV (Vlowk with ct)
- Compressibility modulus K: 230 MeV (SLy4), 337 MeV (DDRMF), 286 MeV (BHF without ct), 214 MeV (BHF with ct), 203 MeV (SCGF without ct), 270 MeV (SCGF with ct), 258 MeV (Vlowk with ct)
- Symmetry energy at saturation a_S(rho_0): 32.0 MeV (SLy4), 32.1 MeV (DDRMF), 51.4 MeV (BHF without ct), 31.9 MeV (BHF with ct), 34.0 MeV (SCGF without ct), 28.3 MeV (SCGF with ct), 21.7 MeV (Vlowk with ct)
- Contact interaction parameters (alpha=0.5): t_0 = -153 MeV fm^3 (BHF), -311 MeV fm^3 (SCGF), -438.1 MeV fm^3 (Vlowk); t_3 = 2720 MeV fm^(3+3alpha) (BHF), 3670 MeV fm^(3+3alpha) (SCGF), 6248 MeV fm^(3+3alpha) (Vlowk)

## Context

This work extends comparisons of nuclear matter properties from phenomenological Skyrme and relativistic mean-field models (such as the SLy4 parameterization of Chabanat and the DDRMF model of Goegelein (2008)) to include three microscopic approaches based on realistic NN interactions. The microscopic methods fail to reproduce empirical saturation without a contact term, a known issue placing them on the Coester band as discussed by Muether (2000); the paper shows that after adding such a term, the symmetry energy predictions vary significantly, with Vlowk yielding only about two-thirds of the experimental value (21.7 MeV versus 32 MeV), indicating that the treatment of isospin T=0 ladder terms is crucial. The results enable assessment of uncertainties in extrapolating nuclear properties to high densities and large asymmetries relevant for neutron star models and radioactive-beam facilities.
