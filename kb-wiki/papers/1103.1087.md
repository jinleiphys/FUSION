# Electric properties of the Beryllium-11 system in Halo EFT

> We compute E1 transitions and electric radii in the Beryllium-11 nucleus using an effective field theory that exploits the separation of scales in this halo system. We fix the leading-order parameters of the EFT from measured data on the 1/2+ and 1/2- levels in Be-11 and the B(E1) strength for the transition between them. We then obtain predictions for the B(E1) strength for Coulomb dissociation of the Be-11 nucleus to the continuum. We also compute the charge radii of the 1/2+ and 1/2- states. Agreement with experiment within the expected accuracy of a leading-order computation in this EFT is obtained. We also discuss how next-to-leading-order (NLO) corrections involving both s-wave and p-wave neutron-Be-10 interactions affect our results, and display the NLO predictions for quantities which are free of additional short-distance operators at this order. Information on neutron-Be-10 scattering in the relevant channels is inferred.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that Halo Effective Field Theory (EFT) can describe electric properties of the Beryllium-11 nucleus at leading order with 30-40% accuracy, consistent with the expected expansion parameter of approximately 0.4, and that next-to-leading-order corrections improve agreement with experimental data.

## Method

The authors construct a Halo EFT with Beryllium-10 core and neutron degrees of freedom, including s-wave and p-wave contact interactions that generate the shallow 1/2+ ground state (500 keV separation energy) and 1/2- excited state (180 keV separation energy) of Beryllium-11. They fix leading-order parameters from the bound-state energies and the B(E1) transition strength between them, then compute charge radii and Coulomb dissociation strength. The EFT expansion parameter is the ratio of core to halo size, approximately 0.4, and calculations are carried out to next-to-leading order for quantities free of additional short-distance operators.

## Key numbers

- Neutron separation energies: 500 keV for the 1/2+ state and 180 keV for the 1/2- state in Beryllium-11, from Ajzenberg-Selove (1990)
- Scattering volume for n-Beryllium-10 in the l=1, J=1/2 channel: a1 = (457 ± 67) fm^3, from Typel and Baur (2004)
- B(E1)(1/2+ → 1/2-) = 0.105(12) e^2 fm^2 from Coulomb excitation (Summers et al. 2007), consistent with 0.116(12) e^2 fm^2 from lifetime measurements (Millener et al. 1983)
- Neutron radius of Beryllium-11 ground state: ⟨r^2⟩^{1/2} = 5.7(4) fm from Palit et al. (2003)
- Charge radius of Beryllium-11: ⟨r_E^2⟩^{1/2} = 2.463(16) fm from atomic physics measurement (Nortershauser et al. 2009)
- Leading-order Halo EFT prediction for Beryllium-11 charge radius: 2.40 fm, which is 2-3% smaller than the experimental value
- Difference ⟨r_c^2⟩_{Be-11} - ⟨r_c^2⟩_{Be-10}: Halo EFT gives 0.19 fm^2 versus experimental 0.51(17) fm^2
- γ0 = 0.15 fm^{-1} and γ1 = 0.09 fm^{-1} from the separation energies
- Leading-order r1 = (-0.54 ± 0.08) fm^{-1}, next-to-leading-order r1 = (-0.72 ± 0.08) fm^{-1}

## Context

This work extends the Halo EFT framework developed by Bertulani et al. (2002) and Bedaque et al. (2003) to electromagnetic properties of Beryllium-11, complementing ab initio calculations by Forssen et al. (2005, 2009) and microscopic descriptions by Millener et al. (1983). The approach enables systematic extraction of asymptotic normalization coefficients from experimental data and establishes correlations between neutron-Beryllium-10 scattering observables and electromagnetic transition strengths. A preliminary version appeared in Hammer and Phillips (2010), and the mechanism for divergence cancellation is shared with the related study of radiative neutron capture on Lithium-7 by Rupak et al. (2011).
