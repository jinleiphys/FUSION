# Space-time structure of particle emission and femtoscopy scales in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions

> The analysis of the spatiotemporal picture of particle radiation in relativistic heavy-ion collisions in terms of correlation femtoscopy scales, emission and source functions allows one to probe the character of evolution of the system created in the collision. Realistic models, like the integrated hydrokinetic model (iHKM), used in the present work, are able to simulate the entire evolution process of strongly interacting matter produced in high-energy nuclear collision. The mentioned model describes all the stages of the system's evolution, including formation of the very initial state and its consequent gradual thermalization, hydrodynamic expansion and afterburner hadronic cascade, that can help researchers to figure out the specific details of the process and better understand the formation mechanisms of certain observables. In the current paper we investigate the behavior of the pion and kaon interferometry radii and their connection with emission functions in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions at the Large Hadron Collider within iHKM. We are focusing on the study of the emission time scales at different energies for both particle species (pions and kaons) aiming to get deeper insight into relation of these scales and the peculiarities of the mentioned system's collective expansion and decay with the experimentally observed femtoscopy radii. One of our main interests is the problem of the total system's lifetime estimation based on the femtoscopy analysis.

# Space-time structure of particle emission and femtoscopy scales in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

The maximal emission times extracted from combined fits to pion and kaon transverse momentum spectra and longitudinal femtoscopy radii do not reflect the apparent dominance of late post-hydrodynamic stage contributions seen in emission time distributions, because the fits include higher-momentum particles (up to 1.0 GeV/c) whose emission peaks closer to the particlization time, while low-momentum particles (below 0.55 GeV/c) yield larger maximal emission times that may serve as upper-limit estimates for the total system lifetime.

## Method

The authors used the integrated hydrokinetic model (iHKM) to simulate central Pb+Pb collisions at LHC energies of 2.76 TeV and 5.02 TeV, which includes pre-thermal dynamics, viscous hydrodynamic expansion, and a final UrQMD hadronic cascade stage. They constructed emission time distributions for pions and kaons by integrating emission functions over transverse radius, and applied analytical fitting formulas for transverse momentum spectra and longitudinal femtoscopy radii to extract maximal emission times under different momentum cuts.

## Key numbers

- For central Pb+Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV (c=0-5%): maximal emission times from combined fits were tau_pi = 8.97 ± 0.04 fm/c and tau_K = 12.73 ± 0.12 fm/c, with temperature T = 138 MeV
- For central Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV (c=0-5%): maximal emission times were tau_pi = 10.34 ± 0.06 fm/c (or 9.44 ± 0.02 fm/c in an alternative fit) and tau_K = 12.65 ± 1.58 fm/c, with temperature T = 144 MeV
- Mean emission times from full model histograms for low-momentum particles (0.2 < p_T < 0.3 GeV/c) at 2.76 TeV: 16.05 fm/c for pions and 18.70 fm/c for kaons
- Low-momentum fits (0.25 < p_T < 0.55 GeV/c) yielded tau_pi = 13.65 ± 0.44 fm/c and tau_K = 16.73 ± 0.49 fm/c, with T = 106 ± 16 MeV
- Switching off the hadronic cascade stage reduced femtoscopy radii by 12% for pions and 25-30% for kaons at 2.76 TeV
- Particlization temperatures used: T_p = 165 MeV (Laine-Schroeder EoS, epsilon_p = 0.5 GeV/fm^3) and T_p = 156 MeV (HotQCD EoS, epsilon_p = 0.27 GeV/fm^3)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Sinyukov et al., 2016; Shapoval et al., 2020) that proposed a method for estimating maximal emission times from combined spectra and femtoscopy fits, which was subsequently used by the ALICE Collaboration in their experimental kaon femtoscopy analysis at 2.76 TeV. The results support the "continuous freeze-out" picture with a prolonged afterburner stage, consistent with experimental observations of K*/K yield ratios from ALICE that show suppression in central collisions due to rescattering effects. The analysis enables improved estimation of the total system lifetime by distinguishing between high-momentum particles (emitted near particlization) and low-momentum particles (emitted later during the hadronic cascade phase).
