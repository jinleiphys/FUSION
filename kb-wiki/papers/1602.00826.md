# Bimodality emerges from transport model calculations of heavy ion collisions at intermediate energy

> This work is a continuation of our effort [Phys. Rev. C 91, 034616 (2015)] to examine if signatures of a phase transition can be extracted from transport model calculations of heavy ion collisions at intermediate energy. A signature of first order phase transition is the appearance of a bimodal distribution in $P_m(k)$ in finite systems. Here P_m(k) is the probability that the maximum of the multiplicity distribution occurs at mass number k. Using a well-known model for event generation (BUU plus fluctuation), we do two cases of central collision: mass 40 on mass 40 and mass 120 on mass 120. Bimodality is seen in both the cases. The results are quite similar to those obtained in statistical model calculations. An intriguing feature is seen. We observe that at the energy where bimodality occurs. other phase transition like signatures appear. There are breaks in certain first order derivatives. We then examine if such breaks appear in standard Botzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) calculations without fluctuations. They do. The implication is interesting. If first order phase transition occurs, it may be possible to recognise that from ordinary BUU calculation. Probably the reason this was not seen already is because this aspect was not investigated before.

## Key claim

Bimodality in the largest-cluster probability distribution \(P_m(k)\), a signature of first-order phase transition in finite systems, emerges from transport model calculations (BUU plus fluctuation) for central collisions of both \({}^{40}\text{Ca}+{}^{40}\text{Ca}\) and \({}^{120}\text{Sn}+{}^{120}\text{Sn}\), and the same transition energy also shows breaks in first-order derivatives that are visible even in standard BUU calculations without fluctuations.

## Method

The authors used a Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) transport model augmented with a fluctuation mechanism (Bauer et al.) to generate event-by-event simulations of central collisions (\(b=0\)) for two symmetric systems: \(A=40\) and \(A=120\). For each event, test particles were propagated in a mean field with hard scattering; clusters were defined by a distance criterion (test particles within 2 fm are part of the same cluster). From 1000 events per beam energy, the probability \(P_m(k)\) that the largest cluster contains \(k\) nucleons was constructed at late times (300 fm/c for the lighter system, 600 fm/c for the heavier). The same systems were also studied with standard BUU (no fluctuations) to examine derivatives of average kinetic and potential energies.

## Key numbers

- For \({}^{40}\text{Ca}+{}^{40}\text{Ca}\), bimodality appears at a beam energy of 42.5 MeV/nucleon, where the two peaks in \(P_m(k)\) are of nearly equal height; at 20 MeV/nucleon the distribution peaks near mass 60 (liquid phase), and at 100 MeV/nucleon it peaks at very low mass (gas phase).
- For \({}^{120}\text{Sn}+{}^{120}\text{Sn}\), bimodality appears at a beam energy of 60.125 MeV/nucleon, with similar liquid-like and gas-like distributions at 20 and 100 MeV/nucleon respectively.
- For \({}^{40}\text{Ca}+{}^{40}\text{Ca}\), the bimodal distribution is confined to a narrow energy range: shown at 41.5, 42.5, 43.5, and 44.5 MeV/nucleon (Fig. 3).
- The cluster recognition used a distance cutoff of 2 fm and a lattice spacing \(l = 1\) fm with \(n=1\).
- Calculations used 1000 events per beam energy and \(\tilde{N} \approx 100\) test particles per nucleon.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous transport-model study (Mallik et al., Phys. Rev. C 91, 034616, 2015) which showed that multiplicity distributions from BUU-plus-fluctuation simulations resemble those from the canonical thermodynamic model (CTM). It directly tests the bimodality signature proposed by Gulminelli and Chomaz for finite systems, previously demonstrated in lattice gas and CTM calculations. The results enable a possible method to estimate freeze-out density in statistical models by matching the bimodality temperature from CTM (which depends on an assumed freeze-out density) to that from the transport model, which has no such free parameter.
