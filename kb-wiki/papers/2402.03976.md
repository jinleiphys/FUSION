# Nuclear Isomers at the Extremes of their Properties

> The longer-lived excited nuclear states, referred as nuclear isomers, exist due to the hindered decays owing to their peculiar nucleonic structural surroundings. Some of these conditions, being exceptionally rare and limited to achieve, elevate certain isomers to the status of extreme and unusual isomers among their kin. For example, the $E5$ coupling of single-particle orbitals is rare and so are $E5$ decaying isomers. This review delves into some of such remarkable isomers scattered across the nuclear landscape while highlighting the possibilities to find more of them. Unique properties of some of them, harbor the potential for transformative applications in medicine and energy. An exciting example is that of the lowest energy isomer known so far in $^{229}$Th, which may help realize the dream of an ultra-precise nuclear clock in the coming decade. These isomers also offer an insight into the extremes of nuclear structure associated with them, which leads to their unusual status in energy, half-life, spin etc. The review attempts to highlight isomers with high-multipolarities, high-spins, high-energies, longest half-lives, extremely low energy, etc. A lack of theoretical understanding of the decay rates, half-lives and moments of these isomers is also pointed out.

# Nuclear Isomers at the Extremes of their Properties

## Key claim
This review argues that certain nuclear isomers achieve "extreme" status due to exceptionally rare conditions such as high-multipolarity couplings, extremely low excitation energies, or very high spins, but the authors note that a complete theoretical understanding of the decay rates, half-lives, and moments of these unusual isomers is still lacking, posing a major challenge to existing nuclear theories.

## Method
The authors survey the nuclear landscape using the Atlas of Nuclear Isomers - Second Edition (Garg 2023) as the primary data source, selecting isomers that exhibit extreme properties in energy, half-life, spin, multipolarity, or decay mode. They categorize these isomers into groups including high-energy isomers, high-multipolarity isomers (M3, M4, E5), extremely low energy isomers, very high-spin isomers, highest quasi-particle isomers, very long-lived isomers, proton-decaying isomers, and beta-decaying isomers, discussing selected examples from each category with reference to their nucleonic structural origins.

## Context
This review extends the compilations of the Atlas of Nuclear Isomers (Jain 2015, Garg 2023) by focusing specifically on isomers at the extremes of their properties, rather than providing a comprehensive catalog. It builds on the theoretical framework of spin isomers first proposed by Weizsacker (1936) and the understanding of isomers in relation to shell structure established by Feenberg (1949) and Bohr. The work highlights the potential for transformative applications, particularly the ²²⁹Th isomer for ultra-precise nuclear clocks as demonstrated by Kraemer (2023), and identifies open questions regarding the theoretical understanding of decay rates and half-lives that future work at radioactive ion beam facilities may address.
