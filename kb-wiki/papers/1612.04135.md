# An estimate of Alpha decay half-life from the poles of S-matrix of an exactly solvable potential

> We develop a versatile and analytically solvable potential which fairly reproduces the combined potential of an $α+$nucleus system resulting from both the attractive nuclear and repulsive electrostatic potentials. The potential is expressed in terms of the radial position, mass and proton number of the $α$-particle and the daughter nucleus and certain parameters governing the depth, height and steepness of the barrier. The potential generated is typically a pocket near the origin and a barrier adjacent to it. The Schrödinger equation with the above mentioned potential is then solved for the wave function. This potential produces discrete positive energy quasibound state known as resonance state. By matching the wave function and its derivative with the regular Coulomb wave function $F_0$ and irregular Coulomb wave function $G_0$, we obtain the S-matrix analytically. The resonance is obtained from the pole in complex energy plane which gives the width of the corresponding time of decay through the imaginary part of the energy pole position. We make a comparative study of the measured half-lives of various nuclei with the calculated half-lives. The calculated values of half-lives closely match with the corresponding experimental results.

## Key claim

The paper claims that an analytically solvable potential, originally used by Fiedeldey and Frahn, can reproduce the combined nuclear and Coulomb potential for alpha-nucleus systems and that the half-lives calculated from S-matrix poles closely match experimental values across a wide range of nuclei, provided the interior steepness parameter \(d_1\) is adjusted within the range \(2.5 < d_1 < 6.5\).

## Method

The authors construct a piecewise potential with a pocket near the origin and a barrier, parameterized by depth, height, steepness, and the mass and charge of the alpha particle and daughter nucleus. They solve the s-wave Schrodinger equation exactly using hypergeometric functions, match the wave function and its derivative to regular and irregular Coulomb wave functions at the boundary \(r = R_0\), and obtain the S-matrix analytically. The resonance energy (Q-value) and width are extracted from the complex pole of the S-matrix in the momentum plane, and the half-life is computed as \(T_{1/2} = \hbar / \Gamma\).

## Key numbers

- For \(^{238}_{92}\)U: experimental Q-value = 4.270 MeV, experimental half-life = \(1.4 \times 10^{17}\) s, calculated half-life = \(5.59 \times 10^{17}\) s, calculated width = \(8.15 \times 10^{-40}\) MeV.
- For \(^{146}_{62}\)Sm: experimental half-life = \(2.2 \times 10^{15}\) s, calculated half-life = \(3.73 \times 10^{15}\) s.
- For \(^{210}_{82}\)Pb: experimental half-life = \(3.7 \times 10^{16}\) s, calculated half-life = \(3.7 \times 10^{16}\) s.
- For \(^{106}_{52}\)Te: experimental Q-value = 4.290 MeV, experimental half-life = \(8.0 \times 10^{-5}\) s, calculated half-life = \(4.02 \times 10^{-5}\) s.
- For \(^{294}_{118}\)Lv: experimental Q-value = 11.810 MeV, experimental half-life = \(1.4 \times 10^{-3}\) s, calculated half-life = \(4.30 \times 10^{-3}\) s.
- The interior steepness parameter \(d_1\) is varied in the range 2.5 to 6.5 across all systems; the exterior steepness parameter \(d_2\) is fixed at 2.

## Context

This work extends the use of analytically solvable potentials for alpha decay, contrasting with the Woods-Saxon potential (which is not analytically solvable) and the Ginocchio potential (where searching for S-matrix poles is tedious). The authors note that the WKB method has problems with the frequency factor, motivating their alternative S-matrix pole approach. The results enable calculation of alpha-decay half-lives for any nucleus from light to superheavy, covering decay times from \(10^{-4}\) s to \(10^{17}\) s, and the formulation is compatible with relativistic mean field theory.
