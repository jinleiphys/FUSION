# Are Antiprotons Forever?

> Up to one million antiprotons from a single LEAR spill have been captured in a large Penning trap. Surprisingly, when the antiprotons are cooled to energies significantly below 1 eV, the annihilation rate falls below background. Thus, very long storage times for antiprotons have been demonstrated in the trap, even at the compromised vacuum conditions imposed by the experimental set up. The significance for future ultra-low energy experiments, including portable antiproton traps, is discussed.

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that antiprotons cooled to sub-eV energies in a Penning trap exhibit an annihilation rate on residual gas that falls below the cosmic-ray background, contradicting the expected \(1/v\) dependence of the annihilation cross section at low energy. The effect is observed even at the compromised vacuum conditions of the setup (estimated residual gas pressure \(\geq 10^{-11}\) Torr), and is distinct from the long storage times achieved by the PS196 collaboration through cryogenic vacuum alone.

## Method
Antiprotons from a single LEAR spill (up to \(10^9\) particles, 200 ns duration, 5.9 MeV) were slowed by a thin degrading foil; those emerging with kinetic energy below 12.5 keV were dynamically captured in a 50 cm long open-end-cap Penning trap (5 cylindrical electrodes, inner diameter 2.8 cm) inside a 6 T superconducting magnet. The captured antiprotons were cooled by a preloaded cloud of \(\approx 10^9\) electrons (cooled to \(\approx 10\) K by synchrotron radiation) and collected in a 1 cm\(^3\) harmonic well at the trap center. Annihilation events on residual gas were monitored by external scintillators (S1–S3 in two-fold coincidence, 4% detection efficiency) during the cooling and storage period, and the energy distribution of released antiprotons was obtained from time-of-arrival spectra.

## Key numbers
- Up to \(10^6\) antiprotons captured from a single LEAR spill, with up to 65% cooled to sub-eV energies and collected in a 1 cm\(^3\) region after \(\approx 600\) seconds.
- Cooling time constant fitted to 175 seconds; after 1500 seconds, approximately 12% of initially captured antiprotons remained in the inner trap.
- The width of the energy distribution of antiprotons released after 1500 seconds was less than 800 meV, with centroid below 1 eV, compatible with cooling to the ambient trap temperature (\(< 15\) K).
- The observed maximum annihilation rate was \(8 \times 10^{-3}\) sec\(^{-1}\); the final rate at \(t = 1500\) sec was significantly lower and indistinguishable from the cosmic-ray background of 1–2 counts per second.
- Residual gas pressure estimated at \(4 \times 10^{-12}\) Torr (using Bracci et al. cross-section as upper bound and 10 K gas temperature), consistent with the upper bound of \(10^{-11}\) Torr.

## Context
This work extends the PS200 collaboration’s effort to measure the gravitational acceleration of antiprotons by demonstrating that sub-eV antiprotons can be stored for long periods even without the fully cryogenic vacuum used by the PS196 collaboration (Feinberg, 1994). The observed reduction in annihilation rate at ultra-low energies contradicts the \(1/v\) dependence predicted by Bracci et al. (1990) for hydrogen targets and the \(1/v^2\) dependence of Morgan and Hughes (1970), and is not explained by the coupled-channel non-adiabatic approach of Voronin (1994). The result enables the practical construction of portable antiproton traps and room-temperature transport systems for future experiments, including ultra-low-energy antiproton physics and secondary particle production.
