# Eliminating experimental bias in anisotropic-flow measurements of high-energy nuclear collisions

> We argue that the traditional event-plane method, which is still widely used to analyze anisotropic flow in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions, should be abandoned because flow fluctuations introduce an uncontrolled bias in the measurement. Instead, one should use an alternative, such as the scalar-product method or cumulant method, which always measures an unambiguous property of the underlying anisotropic flow and therefore eliminates this bias, and does so without any disadvantages. It is known that this correction is important for precision comparisons of traditional v_n measurements requiring better than a few percent accuracy. However, we show that it is absolutely essential for correlations between different harmonics, such as those that have been recently measured by the ATLAS Collaboration, which can differ from the nominally-measured quantity by a factor two or more. We also describe how, using the corrected analysis method, the information from different subevents can be combined in order to optimize the precision of analyses.

## Key claim

The traditional event-plane method for measuring anisotropic flow in high-energy nuclear collisions should be abandoned because event-by-event flow fluctuations introduce an uncontrolled bias, making the measured quantity depend on detector resolution and experimental setup. The scalar-product method or cumulant method always measures an unambiguous root-mean-square value of the flow coefficient, with no disadvantages compared to the traditional approach.

## Method

The authors compare the traditional event-plane method, which correlates the flow vector of particles of interest with the direction of the flow vector in a reference detector, against the scalar-product method, which uses the scalar product of flow vectors without normalizing by the flow-vector magnitude. They evaluate event averages in two steps, first averaging over events with the same underlying flow, then combining bins of flow, to show how flow fluctuations affect each method. For mixed-harmonic correlations, they extend the analysis to show that the event-plane method produces results that can differ from the nominal quantity by a factor of two or more, while a generalized scalar-product method removes this ambiguity.

## Key numbers

- The difference between the high-resolution limit (mean v_n) and low-resolution limit (rms v_n) for individual v_n measurements ranges from 6 to 13%, depending on centrality and harmonic, for Pb-Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV per nucleon pair.
- For v_3, the ratio of rms to mean is close to 2/√π ≈ 1.13, corresponding to Gaussian fluctuations.
- For the mixed-harmonic correlation v_4{Ψ_2}, the STAR collaboration found that v_4{3} (the low-resolution limit) is larger than v_4{Ψ_2} by 20% for mid-central collisions, compared to roughly 5% difference for v_2.
- The ratio of low-resolution to high-resolution limits for correlations between Φ_2 and Φ_4 varies from 1.4 to more than 2, depending on centrality.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies showing that event-by-event flow fluctuations cause the event-plane method to measure a quantity between the mean and rms v_n (Alver 2008, Ollitrault 2009). The paper demonstrates that the problem is far more severe for mixed-harmonic correlations recently measured by the ATLAS Collaboration (Jia 2012), where the ambiguity can reach a factor of two. The authors show that the scalar-product method, introduced by the STAR collaboration (Adler 2002), and cumulant methods (Borghini 2001, Bilandzic 2010) provide unambiguous measurements, and they describe how to combine information from different subevents to optimize precision. This enables reliable comparisons between experiments at different collision energies (RHIC vs. LHC) and between theory and experiment, avoiding the ambiguities that complicated earlier comparisons (Gardim 2012).
