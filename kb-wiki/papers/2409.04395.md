# Suppressing Gauge Drift in Quantum Simulations with Gauge Transformations

> The simulation of quantum lattice gauge theories faces the major challenge of maintaining gauge invariance, as various errors in the simulation push the state of the system out of the physical subspace of the system's exponentially larger Hilbert space. This paper outlines a method, based off of previous work, that uses gauge transformations in two ways. Firstly, the method exploits the Zeno effect by conducting frequent projections to suppress gauge drift. These projections utilize local gauge transformations to destructively interfere unphysical amplitudes via coupling to an ancillary qubit while the physical amplitudes are left untouched, up to a less than unity normalization factor. Secondly, gauge transformations are conducted throughout the time evolution of the system to hamper the speed of gauge drift. This paper demonstrates this method on a pure 1D SU$(2)$ toy model.

## Key claim
The paper claims that combining frequent projective measurements (which use random gauge transformations to destructively interfere unphysical amplitudes) with random gauge transformations applied after every Trotterized time step can effectively suppress gauge drift in digital quantum simulations of non-abelian lattice gauge theories, but the method requires an optimization between projection frequency and computational cost, and failed projections necessitate restarting the entire simulation.

## Method
The method applies a random gauge transformation after every Trotterized time step to slow the rate of gauge drift (method 1, based on Lamm, Lawrence, and Yamauchi (2020)). Additionally, it performs frequent projective measurements that couple the system to an ancillary qubit via a controlled gauge transformation, followed by a Hadamard gate and measurement, leveraging the quantum Zeno effect to suppress unphysical amplitudes by approximately a factor of 1/2 per successful projection (method 2). The method was demonstrated on a pure 1D SU(2) toy model with four sites and periodic boundary conditions, using a Kogut-Susskind lattice approach with a cutoff j_max = 1/2 and an artificial drift operator to simulate gauge drift.

## Key numbers
- The average suppression factor per successful projection was measured as 0.512 ± 0.063, based on 5,050 suppression factors from 50 initializations run for 101 time steps each.
- For the toy model with Δt = 0.01 time units and drift strength ε = 0.01, the combined method (both gauge transformations after each time step and projections after each time step) bounded the expectation value ⟨G²⟩ to small values near zero over 500 time steps.
- The control run (no suppression) showed growing ⟨G²⟩ over 500 time steps, while using only method 1 (gauge transformations after each time step) slowed but did not bound this growth.
- When reusing only two gauge transformations throughout a simulation (alternating all g's), the average ⟨G²⟩ was (17.2 ± 4.3) × 10⁻⁴, compared to (14.8 ± 4.2) × 10⁻⁴ for fully random gauge transformations.

## Context
This work extends the method of Lamm, Lawrence, and Yamauchi (2020), which used gauge transformations after each time step to slow gauge drift but did not fully stop it, and the author's prior work (Ball, 2023) which used e^{-itG²} operators within projections. The combined approach addresses the optimization problem of balancing projection frequency against computational cost, and the calibration phase described enables tuning for simulations run many times. The method is designed for digital quantum simulations of non-abelian lattice gauge theories such as SU(3) quantum chromodynamics, where maintaining gauge invariance is particularly challenging due to the exponentially larger unphysical Hilbert space.
