# Shell Model Description of $^{102-108}$Sn Isotopes

> We have performed shell model calculations for neutron deficient even $^{102-108}$Sn and odd $^{103-107}$Sn isotopes in $sdg_{7/2}h_{11/2}$ model space using two different interactions. The first set of interaction is due to Brown {\it et al.} and second is due to Hoska {\it et al}. The calculations have been performed using doubly magic $^{100}$Sn as core and valence neutrons are distributed over the single particle orbits 1$g_{7/2}$, 2$d_{5/2}$, 2$d_{3/2}$, 3$s_{1/2}$ and 1$h_{11/2}$. In more recent experimental work for $^{101}$Sn [Phys. Rev. Lett. {\bf 105} (2010) 162502], the g.s. is predicted as 5/2$^+$ with excited 7/2$^+$ at 172 keV. We have also performed another two set of calculations by taking difference in single particle energies of 2$d_{5/2}$ and 1$g_{7/2}$ orbitals by 172 keV. The present state-of-the-art shell model calculations predicts fair agreements with the experimental data. These calculations serve as a test of nuclear shell model in the region far from stability for unstable Sn isotopes near the doubly magic $^{100}$Sn core.

## Key claim

The paper reports that large-scale shell model calculations using two different interactions (sn100pn and snet) in the $sdg_{7/2}h_{11/2}$ model space with $^{100}$Sn as a core produce "fair agreement" with experimental data for neutron-deficient $^{102-108}$Sn isotopes, but the agreement is better for even-mass isotopes than for odd-mass isotopes, and the results are sensitive to the single-particle energy difference between the $2d_{5/2}$ and $1g_{7/2}$ orbitals.

## Method

Shell model calculations were performed for even $^{102-108}$Sn and odd $^{103-107}$Sn isotopes using the Nushell code, with valence neutrons distributed over the $1g_{7/2}$, $2d_{5/2}$, $2d_{3/2}$, $3s_{1/2}$, and $1h_{11/2}$ orbitals above a $^{100}$Sn core. Two sets of interactions were used: sn100pn (from Brown et al., based on the CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon potential) and snet (from Hoska et al., based on the renormalized Paris potential). A second set of calculations modified the single-particle energies by setting the $2d_{5/2}$-$1g_{7/2}$ splitting to 172 keV, following the experimental result for $^{101}$Sn from Darby (2010). For $^{108}$Sn, a truncation of maximum two particles in the $1h_{11/2}$ orbital was applied.

## Key numbers

*   For $^{102}$Sn, the calculated $2^+$ state lies at 1.683 MeV (sn100pn) and 1.689 MeV (snet), compared to the experimental value of 1.472 MeV; the $6^+$ state is predicted at 2.063 MeV (sn100pn) and 2.089 MeV (snet).
*   For $^{104}$Sn, the first excited $2^+$ state is overestimated by 234 keV (sn100pn) and 337 keV (snet) from the experimental value of 1.260 MeV; the $6^+$ state is predicted within 70 keV of the experimental 2.257 MeV.
*   For $^{106}$Sn, the $2^+$ level at 1.413 MeV from sn100pn agrees with the experimental 1.208 MeV, while snet overestimates it by 350 keV.
*   For $^{108}$Sn, the sn100pn interaction predicts the $2^+$, $4^+$, $6^+$, $8^+$, and $10^+$ states at 1.186, 2.142, 2.297, 3.502, and 4.051 MeV, respectively, compared to experimental values of 1.207, 2.111, 2.365, 3.561, and 4.256 MeV.
*   For $^{103}$Sn, the splitting between the $5/2^+$ ground state and the $7/2^+$ excited state (experimental 0.168 MeV) is underestimated by approximately 140 keV (sn100pn) and 90 keV (snet), improving to differences of about 60 keV and 85 keV with modified single-particle energies.
*   For $^{105}$Sn, the first $7/2^+$ state at 0.200 MeV is reproduced very well with modified single-particle energies, giving 0.193 MeV (sn100pn mod.) and 0.223 MeV (snet mod.).
*   The $B(E2; 0^+_{g.s} \rightarrow 2^+_1)$ values for $^{106}$Sn are 0.138 $e^2b^2$ (sn100pn) and 0.116 $e^2b^2$ (snet), compared to the experimental 0.240 $e^2b^2$; for $^{108}$Sn the sn100pn value is 0.176 $e^2b^2$ versus the experimental 0.230 $e^2b^2$.

## Context

This work extends previous shell model calculations for neutron-deficient Sn isotopes by Engeland (1995), Covello (1997), and Schubert (1995), and more recent calculations by Dikmen (2009) using CD-Bonn and Nijmegen1 interactions. The calculations test two effective interactions (sn100pn and snet) against accumulated experimental data, including recent results for $^{101}$Sn from Darby (2010). The study enables further refinement of effective interactions and single-particle energies for the $^{100}$Sn region, and the authors note that more experimental data for lighter Sn isotopes are needed for a complete assessment.
