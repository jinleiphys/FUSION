# Disentangling the timescales behind the non-perturbative heavy quark potential

> The static part of the heavy quark potential has been shown to be closely related to the spectrum of the rectangular Wilson loop. In particular the lowest lying positive frequency peak encodes the late time evolution of the two-body system, characterized by a complex potential. While initial studies assumed a perfect separation of early and late time physics, where a simple Lorentian (Breit-Wigner) shape suffices to describe the spectral peak, we argue that scale decoupling in general is not complete. Thus early time, i.e. non-potential effects, significantly modify the shape of the lowest peak. We derive on general grounds an improved peak distribution that reflects this fact. Application of the improved fit to non-perturbative lattice QCD spectra now yields a potential that is compatible with a transition to a deconfined screening plasma.

# Disentangling the timescales behind the non-perturbative heavy quark potential

## Key claim

The authors argue that early-time (non-potential) effects significantly modify the shape of the lowest-lying spectral peak of the Wilson loop, contrary to earlier assumptions of complete scale separation; therefore a simple Lorentzian (Breit-Wigner) fit is inadequate, and an improved skewed peak distribution must be used to extract the heavy quark potential from lattice QCD data.

## Method

The authors derive an analytic expression for the Wilson loop spectral function starting from the general time evolution equation, showing that the lowest-lying peak takes the form of a skewed Lorentzian plus polynomial background terms arising from the coupling of early-time and late-time physics. They then apply this improved fitting function to non-perturbative Wilson loop and Wilson line correlator spectra obtained from quenched lattice QCD at finite temperature using the Maximum Entropy Method (MEM), and extract both the real and imaginary parts of the static heavy quark potential.

## Key numbers

- Lattice QCD data at temperature \(T = 2.33 T_C\) (deconfined phase) with lattice spacing \(a_\sigma = 0.097\ \mathrm{fm}\) (coarse lattice, \(\beta=6.1\)) and \(a_\sigma = 0.039\ \mathrm{fm}\) (fine lattice, \(\beta=7.0\))
- Wilson loop spectra fitted at separations \(r = 0.19\ \mathrm{fm}\) and \(r = 0.39\ \mathrm{fm}\) (Fig. 2)
- Real part of the potential \(V_\square(r)\) from the improved fit becomes only slightly larger than the color-singlet free energy at \(r \sim 0.3\ \mathrm{fm}\), in contrast to the naive Lorentzian fit which showed a strong rise
- For the Wilson line correlator \(V_{||}(r)\), the real part grows until \(r \simeq 0.45\ \mathrm{fm}\) and then flattens off to a value slightly larger than the color-singlet free energies
- Wilson line correlator spectra show well-defined peaks up to distances \(r \simeq 1\ \mathrm{fm}\)

## Context

This work extends the spectral-function approach to the heavy quark potential introduced by Rothkopf (2011), which assumed complete separation of early and late time scales and used a simple Breit-Wigner fit. The authors show that this assumption is not generally valid, and that the improved fitting resolves the counterintuitive large rise in the real part of the potential found in that earlier study. The results are compatible with a deconfined screening plasma scenario, and the method enables non-perturbative extraction of both real and imaginary parts of the potential from lattice QCD, complementing perturbative calculations by Laine et al. (2006) and Beraudo et al. (2007). Future work requires improved signal-to-noise ratios in Euclidean correlator data, for example via the multilevel algorithm of Luscher (2001).
