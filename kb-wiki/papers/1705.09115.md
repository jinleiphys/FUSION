# Experimental status of the nuclear spin scissors mode

> With the Wigner Function Moments (WFM) method the scissors mode of the actinides and rare earth nuclei are investigated. The unexplained experimental fact that in $^{232}$Th a double hump structure is found finds a natural explanation within WFM. It is predicted that the lower peak corresponds to an isovector spin scissors mode whereas the higher lying states corresponds to the conventional isovector orbital scissors mode. The experimental situation is scrutinized in this respect concerning practically all results of $M1$ excitations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the experimentally observed double-hump structure of the scissors resonance in actinides and some rare earth nuclei corresponds to two distinct collective modes: a lower-energy isovector spin scissors mode (spin-up nucleons oscillating against spin-down nucleons) and a higher-energy conventional isovector orbital scissors mode (protons oscillating against neutrons), with the spin scissors mode predicted to have stronger B(M1) transition strength.

## Method

The authors apply the Wigner Function Moments (WFM) method to solve the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) equations including spin dynamics and spin-orbit interaction, extended to incorporate spin-spin interactions and pair correlations. They analyze experimental M1 excitation spectra from nuclear resonance fluorescence and Oslo-type experiments on actinides (Th, Pa, U) and rare earth nuclei, folding the spectra with Lorentzians of increasing width until only two humps remain to extract centroid energies and summed B(M1) strengths for the lower and higher groups. The resulting experimental centroids are compared with the two low-lying eigenvalues from the WFM calculations, which are interpreted as the spin scissors and orbital scissors modes.

## Key numbers

- For 232Th: calculated spin scissors energy 2.30 MeV (experimental 2.15 MeV from Adekola, 1.95 MeV from Siem2); orbital scissors energy 2.93 MeV (experimental 2.99 MeV from Adekola, 2.85 MeV from Siem2)
- For 232Th: calculated spin scissors B(M1) = 2.40 μN² (experimental 2.52(26) μN² from Adekola); orbital scissors B(M1) = 1.42 μN² (experimental 1.74(38) μN² from Adekola)
- For 232Th: calculated splitting ΔE = 0.63 MeV (experimental 0.84 MeV from Adekola, 0.8 MeV from Siem2); ratio B_L/B_U = 1.69 (experimental 1.45 from Adekola, 2.2 from Siem2)
- For 236U: experimental spin scissors energy 2.10 MeV, orbital scissors energy 2.61 MeV; spin scissors B(M1) = 2.01(25) μN², orbital scissors B(M1) = 1.60(26) μN²
- For 238U: experimental spin scissors energy 2.19 MeV, orbital scissors energy 2.76 MeV; spin scissors B(M1) = 2.46(31) μN², orbital scissors B(M1) = 5.13(89) μN²
- For 232Th with g_s=0 (switching off spin part of magnetic dipole operator): spin scissors B(M1) = 0.74 μN², orbital scissors B(M1) = 7.69 μN², compared to g_s≠0 case: spin scissors B(M1) = 2.40 μN², orbital scissors B(M1) = 1.42 μN²

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous WFM calculations (Balbutsev, Molodtsova, Schuck, 2015-2017) that predicted the spin scissors mode, and addresses the long-standing puzzle of the double-hump structure in the scissors resonance first noted by Wesselborg et al. (1990) and later observed by the Oslo group (Guttormsen et al., 2012-2014) in Th, Pa, and U isotopes. The paper reanalyzes experimental data from Adekola et al. (2011) on 232Th, Kuliev et al. (2000) QRPA calculations, and numerous rare earth experiments, finding that the spin scissors interpretation resolves the failure of gamma-deformation explanations for the splitting. The results enable future experimental searches for the spin scissors mode in additional nuclei and motivate finite-temperature extensions of the WFM method to directly compare with heated-nucleus data.
