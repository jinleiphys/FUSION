# A Particle number conserving shell-correction method

> The shell correction method is revisited. Contrary to the traditional Strutinsky method, the shell energy is evaluated by an averaging over the number of particles and not over the single-particle energies, which is more consistent with the definition of the macroscopic energy. In addition, the smooth background is subtracted before averaging the sum of single-particle energies, which significantly improves the plateau condition and allows to apply the method also for nuclei close to the proton or neutron drip lines. A significant difference between the shell correction energy obtained with the traditional and the new method is found in particular for highly degenerated single-particle spectra (as i.e. in magic nuclei) while for deformed nuclei (where the degeneracy is lifted to a large extent) both estimates are close, except in the region of super or hyper-deformed states.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a particle-number-conserving shell-correction method that averages over the number of particles rather than over single-particle energies, which is more consistent with the definition of the macroscopic energy; the author warns that this new prescription should not be used in practical calculations without an appropriate readjusting of the parameters of the mean-field potentials, macroscopic models, and the pairing force.

## Method

The smooth component of the total single-particle energy is obtained by folding the sum of single-particle energies in the space of particle numbers (𝒩-space) using a modified Gauss function, after subtracting an average energy background derived from the harmonic oscillator energy sum rule. The folding is performed in the cubic root of the particle number (𝒩^{1/3}) because the distance between major harmonic oscillator shells is constant in that variable. The resulting smoothed energy is compared with the traditional Strutinsky method, where the smooth energy is obtained by folding the single-particle level density in energy space and conserving particle number only on average.

## Key numbers

- The harmonic oscillator energy sum rule gives the average energy as approximately (1/4)(3𝒩)^{4/3} ℏω₀, with a more accurate estimate adding (1/8)(3𝒩)^{2/3} ℏω₀.
- The distance between major harmonic oscillator shells in 𝒩^{1/3} is approximately 0.7, derived from the relation Δ(𝒩^{1/3}) ≈ 3^{-1/3} ≈ 0.7.
- For the spherical harmonic oscillator, the difference between the new and old shell energies is approximated by ΔE ≈ (1/54) a Δ²/𝒩^{2/3}, where a ≈ 3^{4/3}/4 ℏω₀ and Δ is the degeneracy of the single-particle level.
- For deformed nuclei with degeneracy Δ=2, the difference between methods is negligible (of the order of 0.01 MeV for heavier nuclei).
- For ²⁰⁸Pb with a Saxon-Woods potential, the difference between the new and old smoothed energies is of the order of a few MeV at the Fermi level.
- The optimal smearing parameter for the new method is γ ≈ 0.78 for both protons and neutrons, while for the old Strutinsky method γ_S ≈ 1.20 ℏω₀ for protons and γ_S ≈ 1.05 ℏω₀ for neutrons.

## Context

This work extends the earlier attempts by Strutinsky and Ivanyuk (1975-1984) to replace the original Strutinsky method with averaging in particle-number space, which had reached sufficient accuracy but was never widely used due to complexity. The new method is simpler than the Ivanyuk-Strutinsky approach and gives results close to it, while also improving the plateau condition and allowing application to nuclei near the proton or neutron drip lines. The paper enables future calculations of potential energy surfaces, fission barriers, and alpha-decay Q-values, but the author explicitly warns that the parameters of macroscopic models and mean-field potentials must be readjusted before the new method can be used in practice.
