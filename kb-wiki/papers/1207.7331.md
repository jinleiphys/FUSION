# Dynamical freeze-out in event-by-event hydrodynamics

> In hydrodynamical modeling of the ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions the freeze-out is typically performed at a constant temperature or density. In this work we apply a dynamical freeze-out criterion, which compares the hydrodynamical expansion rate with the pion scattering rate. Recently many calculations have been done using event-by-event hydrodynamics where the initial density profile fluctuates from event to event. In these event-by-event calculations the expansion rate fluctuates strongly as well, and thus it is interesting to check how the dynamical freeze-out changes hadron distributions with respect to the constant temperature freeze-out. We present hadron spectra and elliptic flow calculated using (2+1)-dimensional ideal hydrodynamics, and show the differences between constant temperature and dynamical freeze-out criteria. We find that the differences caused by different freeze-out criteria are small in all studied cases.

## Key claim

The differences in hadron spectra and elliptic flow between constant-temperature freeze-out and a dynamical freeze-out criterion (based on comparing the hydrodynamical expansion rate to the pion scattering rate) are small in all studied cases, even when event-by-event initial density fluctuations are included, provided the initial fluctuation smearing parameter is large enough to avoid unphysical small-scale structures.

## Method

The authors solved (2+1)-dimensional ideal hydrodynamics with longitudinal boost invariance for Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV, using the s95p-v1 equation of state. They compared freeze-out on a constant-temperature hypersurface (T_fo = 140 MeV) with a dynamical criterion where freeze-out occurs when the Knudsen number K = θ/Γ reaches a chosen value (K_fo = 0.5, 1.0, or 2.0), with θ the expansion rate and Γ the pion scattering rate calculated in a hadron-resonance gas using UrQMD cross sections. Particle emission was computed via the Cooper-Frye formula, followed by resonance decays, for both smooth optical Glauber initial conditions and Monte Carlo Glauber fluctuating initial conditions with smearing parameters σ = 0.4 fm and 0.8 fm.

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out surfaces compared for 20-30% central Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV; dynamical criterion yields earlier decoupling at edges (higher temperature) and slightly longer-lived center (lower temperature) than constant T_fo = 140 MeV.
- With smooth initial conditions, pion and proton p_T-spectra and charged-particle elliptic flow v_2 are essentially identical between dynamical and constant-temperature freeze-out.
- Varying the dynamical freeze-out parameter K_fo from 0.5 to 1.0 to 2.0 changes the freeze-out surface size and the proton yield (due to chemical equilibrium until freeze-out), but the effect on spectra is small.
- For fluctuating initial conditions with σ = 0.8 fm, the freeze-out surface is smooth and the spectra and v_2 again show no visible difference between the two criteria.
- With σ = 0.4 fm, the dynamical freeze-out surface develops thin rod-like "horns" and narrow "fins" where the expansion rate becomes negative (compression), which the authors consider unphysical.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Eskola et al. (2007) that found small effects on spectra from dynamical freeze-out with smooth initial profiles, and by Holopainen et al. (2010) on event-by-event hydrodynamics. It addresses the open question of whether the freeze-out criterion matters more when initial density fluctuations cause violent flow development. The results suggest that constant-temperature freeze-out is a sufficient approximation for spectra and elliptic flow in ideal hydrodynamics, but the unphysical small-scale structures seen with small smearing parameters motivate future work with viscous hydrodynamics (Schenke et al., 2010) and hybrid models (Petersen et al., 2008; Song et al., 2010; Hirano et al., 2012; Huovinen et al., 2012) where switching on a constant Knudsen number might reduce sensitivity to the switching parameter.
