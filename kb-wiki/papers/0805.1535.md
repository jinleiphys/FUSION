# Improved radiative corrections for (e,e'p) experiments - A novel approach to multi-photon bremsstrahlung

> Radiative processes lead to important corrections to (e,e'p) experiments. While radiative corrections can be calculated exactly in QED and to a good accuracy also including hadronic corrections, these corrections cannot be included into data analyses to arbitrary orders exactly. Nevertheless consideration of multi-photon bremsstrahlung above the low-energy cut-off is important for many (e,e'p) experiments. To date, higher-order bremsstrahlung effects concerning electron scattering experiments have been implemented approximately by employing the soft-photon approximation (SPA). In this paper we propose a novel approach to multi-photon emission which partially removes the SPA from (e,e'p) experiments. In this combined approach one hard photon is treated exactly; and additional softer bremsstrahlung photons are taken into account resorting to the soft-photon approximation. This partial removal of the soft-photon approximation is shown to be relevant for the missing-energy distribution for several kinematic settings at MAMI and TJNAF energies.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a novel "combined approach" for multi-photon bremsstrahlung in (e,e'p) experiments that treats one hard photon exactly via the full QED matrix element while treating all additional softer photons using the soft-photon approximation (SPA), partially removing the SPA from multi-photon radiative corrections. The authors show this partial removal is relevant for the missing-energy distribution at several kinematic settings at MAMI and TJNAF energies, but note that the approach is only a partial improvement and does not constitute a full exact multi-photon QED treatment.

## Method

The authors developed a Monte Carlo event generator that generates multi-photon bremsstrahlung events according to the SPA distribution, then re-weights each event using the ratio of the exact 1-gamma matrix element to the SPA matrix element for one chosen "hard" photon. The hard photon is selected using four different methods (largest energy, largest deviation of q^2 from elastic, closest q^2 to total q^2, or random), and the remaining photons are treated in SPA. The combined Monte Carlo routine was embedded into the SIMC simulation code for (e,e'p) experiments from TJNAF to compare with standard modified SPA (mSPA) calculations and experimental data.

## Key numbers

- At Q^2 = 0.6 GeV^2 and missing energy E_m = 100 MeV, the modified SPA calculation overestimates the radiative tail by 3.6%; at E_m = 500 MeV the deviation is 25%
- At Q^2 = 2.0 GeV^2 and E_m = 100 MeV, the modified SPA overestimates the radiative tail by 3.9%; at E_m = 500 MeV the deviation is 29%
- At Q^2 = 4.0 GeV^2 and E_m = 100 MeV, the modified SPA overestimates the radiative tail by 3.5%; at E_m = 500 MeV the deviation is 23%
- At Q^2 = 8.0 GeV^2 and E_m = 100 MeV, the modified SPA overestimates the radiative tail by 3.0%; at E_m = 500 MeV the deviation is 17%
- When embedded into SIMC with all corrections, the total yield differs by 4.6% at Q^2 = 0.6 GeV^2, 2.3% at Q^2 = 2.0 GeV^2, 1.4% at Q^2 = 4.0 GeV^2, and 1.4% at Q^2 = 8.0 GeV^2 between the combined approach and standard SIMC mSPA

## Context

This work extends the radiative correction framework for (e,e'p) experiments originally developed by Mo and Tsai (1969) and later refined by Makins and collaborators, which treated single-photon bremsstrahlung exactly but multi-photon emission only in the soft-photon approximation (SPA). The paper also builds on the modified SPA (mSPA) approach suggested by Borie and Drechsel that adjusts kinematics to account for bremsstrahlung backlash approximately. The combined approach enables improved data analysis for nuclear structure experiments at MAMI and TJNAF energies, particularly for the radiative tail of the missing-energy distribution where SPA deviations become significant (up to 29% at high missing energies). The authors note that for photon angular distributions, the peaking approximation used in standard SIMC remains the dominant approximation rather than the SPA itself.
