# Prediction of (p,n) Charge-Exchange Reactions with Uncertainty Quantification

> Background: Charge-exchange reactions are a powerful tool for exploring nuclear structure and nuclear astrophysics, however, a robust charge-exchange reaction theory with quantified uncertainties is essential to extracting reliable physics. Purpose: The goal of this work is to determine the uncertainties due to optical potentials used in the theory for charge-exchange reactions to isobaric analogue states. Method: We implement a two-body reaction model to study (p,n) charge-exchange transitions and perform a Bayesian analysis. We study the (p,n) reaction to the isobaric analog states of $^{14}$C, $^{48}$Ca, and $^{90}$Zr targets over a range of beam energies. We compare predictions using standard phenomenological optical potentials with those obtained microscopically. Results: Charge-exchange cross sections are reasonably reproduced by modern optical potentials. However, when uncertainties in the optical potentials are accounted for, the resulting predictions of charge-exchange cross sections have very large uncertainties. Conclusions: The charge-exchange reaction cross section is strongly sensitive to the input interactions, making it a good candidate to further constrain nuclear forces and aspects of bulk nuclear matter. However, further constraints on the optical potentials are necessary for a robust connection between this tool and the underlying isovector properties of nuclei.

## Key claim

Charge-exchange cross sections for (p,n) reactions to isobaric analogue states are reasonably reproduced by modern optical potentials, but when uncertainties in the optical potentials are accounted for, the resulting predictions have very large uncertainties of three to four orders of magnitude, far exceeding the nominal 30% often cited.

## Method

The authors implemented a two-body reaction model using single-step Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA) to study (p,n) charge-exchange transitions to 0+ isobaric analogue states, mediated by a Lane potential constructed from the difference between proton- and neutron-target optical potentials. They performed a Bayesian analysis using mock elastic scattering data to constrain the phenomenological Koning-Delaroche (KD) optical potential parameters and propagate uncertainties to charge-exchange cross sections, and separately propagated uncertainties from the microscopic Whitehead-Lim-Holt (WLH) global optical potential derived from chiral effective field theory. Calculations were performed for targets of 14C, 48Ca, and 90Zr at beam energies of 25, 35, and 45 MeV using a new reaction code called chexpn.

## Key numbers

- Charge-exchange cross section uncertainties from both the Bayesian and WLH approaches span three to four orders of magnitude, compared to elastic scattering uncertainties that are much smaller
- The ratio of the upper limit to the lower limit of the 95% confidence interval for charge-exchange cross sections is several orders of magnitude larger than for elastic scattering, as shown in Fig. 9 for all three targets at all three beam energies
- For 14C(p,n)14NIAS, experimental data were taken from Taddeucci et al. at Elab = 25.7, 35, and 45 MeV
- For 48Ca(p,n)48ScIAS and 90Zr(p,n)90NbIAS, experimental data came from Doering et al. at Elab = 25, 35, and 45 MeV
- The difference between KD and WLH predictions at forward angles greatly exceeds the nominal 30% value often quoted for reaction uncertainties
- The Bayesian posterior distributions were generated from sixteen separate MCMC samplings, each with 1600 parameter pulls

## Context

This work extends the methodology of Danielewicz et al. (2017) who used a similar charge-exchange framework with Lane potentials to study isobaric analogue state transitions, but without including modifications to the isovector term due to Coulomb interactions. The authors build on uncertainty quantification techniques developed by Lovell et al. (2018) for reactions and apply them to charge-exchange for the first time. The results demonstrate that charge-exchange data provide an extraordinarily sensitive constraint to the isovector component of the optical potential, suggesting that a comprehensive systematic study across a wide range of targets and beam energies could reveal details about isovector nuclear forces. The authors note that uncertainties could be reduced by employing optical potentials expressed directly in terms of isoscalar and isovector components as in Bauge et al. (2001) or Jeukenne et al. (1977), which will be the focus of future work.
