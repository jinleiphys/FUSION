# Complex suppression patterns distinguish between major energy loss effects in Quark-Gluon Plasma

> Interactions of high momentum partons with Quark-Gluon Plasma created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions provide an excellent tomography tool for this new form of matter. Recent measurements for charged hadrons and unidentified jets at the LHC show an unexpected flattening of the suppression curves at high momentum, exhibited when either momentum or the collision centrality is changed. Furthermore, a limited data available for B probes indicate a qualitatively different pattern, as nearly the same flattening is exhibited for the curves corresponding to two opposite momentum ranges. We here show that the experimentally measured suppression curves are well reproduced by our theoretical predictions, and that the complex suppression patterns are due to an interplay of collisional, radiative energy loss and the dead-cone effect. Furthermore, for B mesons, we predict that the uniform flattening of the suppression indicated by the limited dataset is in fact valid across the entire span of the momentum ranges, which will be tested by the upcoming experiments. Overall, the study presented here, provides a rare opportunity for pQCD theory to qualitatively distinguish between the major energy loss mechanisms at the same (nonintuitive) dataset.

# Complex suppression patterns distinguish between major energy loss effects in Quark-Gluon Plasma

## Key claim

The experimentally observed flattening of suppression curves for charged hadrons and unidentified jets at high momentum, and the qualitatively different pattern for B probes, are well reproduced by the dynamical energy loss formalism with no free parameters; these complex patterns arise from an interplay of collisional energy loss, radiative energy loss, and the dead-cone effect, though the B meson predictions for uniform flattening across the entire momentum span remain to be tested by upcoming 5 TeV LHC data.

## Method

The author used the dynamical energy loss formalism, which treats the QGP as consisting of dynamical (moving) partons with finite size, and calculates both collisional and radiative energy losses within the same theoretical framework, including finite magnetic mass and running coupling. The formalism was integrated into a numerical procedure incorporating up-to-date initial parton distributions, fragmentation functions, path-length distributions, and multi-gluon fluctuations, with no free parameters used in comparing model predictions with experimental data. Predictions were generated for charged hadrons, D mesons, and B mesons at LHC energies, using an effective QGP temperature of 304 MeV for 0-40% centrality Pb+Pb collisions.

## Key numbers

- Effective QGP temperature: 304 MeV for 0-40% centrality Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC, with a ±60 MeV uncertainty that leads to a change in R_AA of less than 0.07
- Magnetic to electric mass ratio range: 0.4 < μ_M/μ_E < 0.6, used as the uncertainty band in predictions
- Charm quark mass: 1.2 GeV; bottom quark mass: 4.75 GeV
- Charged hadron R_AA vs. N_part data shown for momentum ranges: 7 < p_T < 9 GeV, 20 < p_T < 23 GeV, and 60 < p_T < 95 GeV
- B probe data shown for non-prompt J/ψ with 6.5 < p_T < 30 GeV and B jets with 80 < p_T < 90 GeV
- Predictions applicable to both 2.76 TeV and 5.02 TeV Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC, as R_AA values at these two energies are predicted to be the same

## Context

This work extends the dynamical energy loss formalism developed by Djordjevic (2009) and Djordjevic and Heinz (2008), which removed the widely used assumption of static scattering centers. The study addresses recent ATLAS measurements of charged hadron and unidentified jet suppression at the LHC (ATLAS Collaboration, 2015) and CMS data for non-prompt J/ψ (CMS Collaboration, 2012) and B jets (CMS Collaboration, 2014), which showed unexpected flattening patterns. The results enable a qualitative test of the dead-cone effect (Dokshitzer and Kharzeev, 2001) and the interplay between collisional and radiative energy loss mechanisms, with predictions for B mesons that can be tested by upcoming 5 TeV Pb+Pb collision data at the LHC.
