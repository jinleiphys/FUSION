# Nuclear astrophysical plasmas: ion distribution functions and fusion rates

## Key claim
Very small deviations from the Maxwellian exponential tail in the ion velocity distribution, while leaving bulk quantities unchanged, can produce dramatic effects on fusion reaction rates; the paper argues that such deviations may arise from several mechanisms including particle correlations, non-local Fokker-Planck dynamics, random micro-fields, and quantum uncertainty relations between energy and momentum in the plasma.

## Method
The authors analyze the Gamow peak formalism for thermonuclear reactions, showing that the penetration factor selects particles in the high-energy tail of the distribution, where only about 0.1% of particles contribute to fusion rates. They parametrize deviations from the Maxwellian using the Tsallis q-exponential distribution exp_q(-E/kT) and demonstrate the effect on the Gamow peak for several key solar fusion reactions. They then review physical mechanisms that can produce non-Maxwellian tails: correlations in the Fokker-Planck equation with higher-order momentum-dependent drift and diffusion coefficients, random electric micro-fields, and the Kadanoff-Baym energy-momentum uncertainty relation in many-body plasmas.

## Key numbers
- For the Sun, the most effective energy for the ³He + ³He → ⁴He + 2p reaction is E₀ = 17.036 kT at kT = 1.293 keV (≈ 11.6 × 10⁶ °K), with Gamow energy E_G = 11.83 MeV.
- The area under the Maxwellian curve within the Gamow peak is of the order of 0.1% of the total area; for the p + ¹⁴N reaction (CNO cycle), contributing particles are few in a million.
- For the p + ¹⁴N reaction, deviations with |1-q| = 0.002 already produce large effects on the rate; for |1-q| = 0.01 the effect exceeds a factor of 10 (the green peak for q>1 is divided by five and the red peak for q<1 is multiplied by five to fit on the same scale).
- Gamow energies for three key reactions: p+p→d+ν+e⁺ (E_G = 493 keV), ³He+³He→⁴He+2p (E_G = 11.83 MeV), p+¹⁴N→¹⁵O+γ (E_G = 45.09 MeV).

## Context
This work extends earlier critical examinations of stellar energy distributions by Atkinson, Kacharov, Clayton, and Haubold, and builds on the authors' own prior work (Coraddu 1998, Degl'Innocenti 1998) showing that small deviations from Maxwellian do not modify stellar core properties or helioseismology constraints but can affect fusion rates. The paper enables quantitative assessment of how non-Maxwellian tails (from superdiffusion or subdiffusion) could alter solar neutrino fluxes, photon luminosity, and mechanical eigenfrequencies, and suggests that deviations might explain excess energy in quasi-stellar objects like Jupiter (Coraddu 2001).
