# Towards a self-consistent dynamical nuclear model

> Density Functional Theory (DFT) is a powerful and accurate tool exploited in Nuclear Physics to investigate the ground-state and some collective properties of nuclei along the whole nuclear chart. Models based on DFT are, however, not suitable for the description of single-particle dynamics in nuclei. Following the field theoretical approach by A. Bohr and B. R. Mottelson to describe nuclear interactions between single-particle and vibrational degrees of freedom, we have undertaken important steps to build a microscopic dynamic nuclear model. In connection to that, one important issue that needs to be better understood is the renormalization of the effective interaction in the particle-vibration approach. One possible way to renormalize the interaction is the so called {\it subtraction method}. In this contribution we will implement the {\it subtraction method} for the first time in our model and study its consequences.

## Key claim

The authors implement the subtraction method for the first time in their particle-vibration coupling (PVC) model to renormalize the effective interaction, but they note that it is not yet demonstrated that this procedure properly renormalizes the theory, and that whether it is equivalent to a full refit of the interaction parameters depends on the nature of the observables studied.

## Context

This work extends the particle-vibration coupling approach developed by the Milano group (Bortignon 1977, Bertsch 1983, ColÃ² 1994) and addresses the renormalization problem previously studied in simplified models (Moghrabi 2010, Moghrabi 2012, Brenna 2014, Yang 2016). The subtraction method was previously introduced by Tselyaev (2007, 2013) and applied by other groups (Litvinova 2007, Gambacurta 2015, Lyutorovich 2015, Lyutorovich 2016) to avoid double counting when going beyond energy density functionals. The work aims to enable a more realistic description of single-particle dynamics, including the fragmentation of single-particle states and the spreading width of giant resonances, which is crucial for reliably estimating beta-decay half-lives and optical potentials for nuclear reactions.
