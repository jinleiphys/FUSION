# Lattice calculation of transport coefficient $\hat{q}$ in pure gluon plasma and (2+1)-flavor QCD plasma

> The transport coefficient $\hat{q}$ is a leading coefficient that controls the modification of the hard parton traversing QGP, and hence, responsible for the suppression of the high transverse momentum (transverse to the beam direction) charged-hadrons in heavy-ion collisions. In this article, we present the first unquenched lattice QCD calculation of $\hat{q}$. The calculation is carried out using (2+1)-flavor of quarks, using the highly improved staggered quark action (HISQ) and tree-level Symanzik improved gauge action. The calculation is performed in a wide range of temperatures, ranging from 200 MeV $<T<$ 800 MeV using MILC code package. We considered a leading-order process where a hard parton scatters off the glue field of a thermal QCD medium by exchanging a Glauber gluon (whose transverse momentum is larger than its longitudinal components). The hard scale associated with the jet parton allows the coupling of the gluon to that parton to be treated in perturbation theory. The coupling of the gluon to the medium is treated non-perturbatively. This non-perturbative part is expressed in terms of a non-local (two-point) field-strength-field-strength operator product which can be Taylor expanded after analytic continuation to the deep Euclidean region. Such an expansion allows us to write $\hat{q}$ in terms of a series of local operators, which are suppressed by factors of the hard parton energy. The calculated $\hat{q}$ and its temperature dependence demonstrates reasonable agreement with the phenomenological extraction carried out by the JET collaboration.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first unquenched lattice QCD calculation of the transport coefficient $\hat{q}$ for a hard quark traversing a (2+1)-flavor QCD plasma, finding reasonable agreement with phenomenological extractions by the JET collaboration within $2\sigma$ uncertainty, but only for a single lattice spacing with aspect ratio $n_s/n_\tau = 4$ and a fixed hard parton light-cone momentum $q^- = 100$ GeV.

## Method

The authors compute $\hat{q}$ using a leading-order formalism where a hard quark exchanges a Glauber gluon with the thermal medium; the gluon-medium coupling is treated non-perturbatively via a Taylor expansion of a non-local field-strength-field-strength operator product after analytic continuation to the deep Euclidean region. They evaluate the first three non-zero local operators (involving $F^{3i}F^{3i} - F^{4i}F^{4i}$ and its covariant derivatives $D_z^2$ and $D_z^4$) on $n_\tau=4$, $n_s=16$ lattices using the MILC code, with clover-leaf discretization for the field strength tensor and a multiplicative renormalization factor $R_{\beta_0}$ from the trace anomaly. For the (2+1)-flavor case, they use the HISQ action and tree-level Symanzik improved gauge action with parameters from the HotQCD and TUMQCD collaborations, with light quark masses set to $m_{u,d}=m_s/20$ corresponding to a pion mass of about 160 MeV.

## Key numbers

- $\hat{q}/T^3 \sim 1.5$–$2.5$ for pure gluon (quenched) plasma on $n_\tau=4$, $n_s=16$ lattices
- $\hat{q}/T^3 \sim 2.5$–$3.5$ for (2+1)-flavor QCD plasma on $n_\tau=4$, $n_s=16$ lattices
- Temperature range covered: 200 MeV $< T <$ 800 MeV
- Hard parton light-cone momentum set to $q^- = 100$ GeV
- Lattice aspect ratio $n_s/n_\tau = 4$ (specifically $n_\tau=4$, $n_s=16$)
- Statistical average over 10000 gauge configurations using the Rational Hybrid Monte-Carlo algorithm
- Strange quark mass set to physical value; degenerate light quark masses $m_{u,d}=m_s/20$

## Context

This work extends the lattice formalism proposed by Majumder (2012) and Benzke (2012) to include dynamical quarks, providing the first unquenched lattice calculation of $\hat{q}$ that can be compared with phenomenological extractions by the JET collaboration (Burke, 2013) and JETSCAPE collaboration (Soltz, 2019). The results enable first-principles constraints on jet energy-loss models in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, though the authors note that calculations on finer lattices are ongoing and the current results are limited to a single lattice spacing.
