# Understanding the nature of baryon resonances

> This presentation opens with a brief review of lattice QCD calculations showing the $2s$ radial excitation of the nucleon sits at approximately 2 GeV, well above the Roper resonance position. We then proceed to reconcile this observation with experimental scattering data. While the idea of dressing quark-model states in a coupled-channel analysis to describe scattering data has been around for decades, it's now possible to bring these descriptions to the finite-volume of lattice QCD for confrontation with lattice-QCD calculations. This combination of lattice QCD and experiment demands that we reconsider our preconceived notions about the quark-model and its excitation spectrum. We close with a discussion of an unanticipated resolution to the missing baryon resonances problem.

# Understanding the nature of baryon resonances

## Key claim

The Roper resonance (nucleon excitation at 1.44 GeV) is not the 2s radial excitation of the quark model; rather, it is generated by coupled-channel rescattering in the πN, σN, and πΔ channels, while the genuine 2s radial excitation of the nucleon sits at approximately 2 GeV in lattice QCD calculations. This reinterpretation resolves the long-standing missing baryon resonances problem by showing that quark-model parameters have been erroneously tuned to place the radial excitation at the Roper mass.

## Method

The authors review lattice QCD calculations using Gaussian-smeared quark sources with opposite signs to create a node in the nucleon wave function, combined with generalized eigenvalue methods, to extract the 2s radial excitation energy. They then use Hamiltonian Effective Field Theory (HEFT), a nonperturbative extension of chiral perturbation theory incorporating the Lüscher formalism, to bring experimental πN scattering data to the finite volume of lattice QCD. HEFT constructs a Hamiltonian matrix from single-particle basis states and non-interacting two-particle states (πN, σN, πΔ) with vertices governed by heavy-baryon chiral perturbation theory and separable potentials, then solves the Schrödinger equation on a finite periodic volume to predict energy eigenvalues and eigenstate compositions for direct comparison with lattice QCD results.

## Key numbers

- The 2s radial excitation of the nucleon sits at 1.9(1) GeV on a lattice volume of 3 fm on a side, well above the Roper resonance at 1.44 GeV.
- The Roper resonance mass is 1.44 GeV (Roper, 1964).
- The overlap between a smeared nucleon interpolating field and a non-interacting pion-nucleon basis state is approximately 10^{-3} on a 3 fm lattice at the lowest nontrivial momentum, based on the χPT estimate of Bär et al. (2016).
- Two HEFT fits are compared: one with bare mass m0 = 1.7 GeV (placing the dressed quark-model state near the Roper) and one with m0 = 2.0 GeV (placing it near 2 GeV); only the m0 = 2.0 GeV fit agrees with lattice QCD results.
- Quark-model predictions (Capstick and Isgur, 1992) place the N 1/2+ 2s at 1.54 GeV, while lattice QCD finds it at 1.90(6) GeV; the N 1/2+ 3s is predicted at 1.77 GeV versus 2.60(7) GeV in lattice QCD; the Δ 3/2+ 2s is predicted at 1.80 GeV versus 2.14(5) GeV in lattice QCD.

## Context

This work extends the consensus emerging around 2015 (Leinweber et al., 2015) that the 2s radial excitation of the nucleon sits at approximately 2 GeV, contradicting the long-held association with the Roper resonance first observed by Roper (1964). The authors build on lattice QCD calculations by the CSSM (Liu et al., 2016), Lang et al. (2016), and earlier work by Mahbub et al. (2010, 2013) and Alexandrou et al. (2014), as well as HEFT developments by Wu et al. (2014, 2017) and Abell et al. (2021, 2023). The resolution of the missing baryon resonances problem, that the proliferation of predicted radial excitations below 2 GeV in the quark model (Capstick and Isgur, 1992) is an artifact of misidentifying the Roper as the 2s state, enables future lattice QCD and HEFT studies to correctly interpret the spectrum of nucleon and Δ excitations, with the 2s radial excitation now associated primarily with the N1/2+(1880) resonance observed in photoproduction.
