# Extended Theory of Finite Fermi Systems: Application to the collective and non-collective E1 strength in $^{208}$Pb

> The Extended Theory of Finite Fermi Systems is based on the conventional Landau-Migdal theory and includes the coupling to the low-lying phonons in a consistent way. The phonons give rise to a fragmentation of the single-particle strength and to a compression of the single-particle spectrum. Both effects are crucial for a quantitative understanding of nuclear structure properties. We demonstrate the effects on the electric dipole states in $^{208}$Pb (which possesses 50% more neutrons then protons) where we calculated the low-lying non-collective spectrum as well as the high-lying collective resonances. Below 8 MeV, where one expects the so called isovector pygmy resonances, we also find a strong admixture of isoscalar strength that comes from the coupling to the high-lying isoscalar electric dipole resonance, which we obtain at about 22 MeV. The transition density of this resonance is very similar to the breathing mode, which we also calculated. We shall show that the extended theory is the correct approach for self-consistent calculations, where one starts with effective Lagrangians and effective Hamiltonians, respectively, if one wishes to describe simultaneously collective and non-collective properties of the nuclear spectrum. In all cases for which experimental data exist the agreement with the present theory results is good.

## Key claim

The Extended Theory of Finite Fermi Systems (ETFFS) improves upon conventional Landau-Migdal theory by explicitly including coupling to low-lying phonons, which compresses the single-particle spectrum and fragments single-particle strength; the authors claim this is necessary for simultaneously describing collective and non-collective nuclear states, but the agreement with experiment is stated as "good" only for cases where experimental data exist, implying the theory's predictive power is validated only where comparison is possible.

## Method

The authors derived the ETFFS within the many-body Green function formalism, starting from the Bethe-Salpeter equation for the response function and applying Landau's renormalization procedure to obtain quasi-particle equations. They then extended the theory by including phonon coupling via the \(g^2\) approximation and a chronological decoupling of diagrams, which introduces energy-dependent self-energies and effective interactions. The resulting equations for the change of quasi-particle density in an external field were solved in coordinate space using a continuum random phase approximation (CRPA) for the electric dipole states in \(^{208}\)Pb, incorporating the most collective low-lying phonons calculated within RPA.

## Key numbers

- The isoscalar electric dipole resonance in \(^{208}\)Pb is obtained at about 22 MeV.
- Low-lying non-collective E1 strength is calculated below 8 MeV, where the isovector pygmy resonance is expected.
- The phonons used in the ETFFS calculation include a \(3^-\) state at 2.61 MeV, \(2^+\) states at 4.09 and 6.44 MeV, and multiple states up to \(8^+\) with energies ranging from 4.04 to 7.22 MeV (see Table 1).
- The single-particle continuum is included at the RPA level, and the spurious E1 state energy is set exactly to zero using the procedure from Kl (1998).

## Context

This work extends the conventional Landau-Migdal theory (TFFS) by incorporating phonon coupling, addressing limitations of earlier self-consistent approaches such as those by Goriely and Khan (2002), who found low-lying E1 strength systematically higher by about 3 MeV compared to data, and relativistic approaches reviewed by Ring (2005), which also overestimated pygmy resonance energies. The ETFFS is closely related to the Quasiparticle Phonon Model (QPM) of Soloviev, as applied by Tsoneva et al. (2004), and the QRPA plus phonon coupling model by Sarchi et al. (2004), but differs by treating the single-particle continuum exactly via a Green function technique in coordinate space. The results enable future self-consistent calculations that start from effective Lagrangians or Hamiltonians to include phonon effects explicitly, rather than relying on bare mean-field RPA.
