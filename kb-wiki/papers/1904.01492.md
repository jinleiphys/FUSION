# Description of the mass-asymmetric fission of the Pt isotopes, obtained in the reaction $^{36}$Ar + $^{142}$Nd within the two-stage fusion-fission model

> The two stages dynamical stochastic model developed earlier for description of fusion-fission reactions is applied to the calculation of mass- and energy-distributions of fission fragments of platinum isotopes in reaction ${\rm ^{36}Ar + ^{142}Nd \to ^{178-x}Pt + xn}$. The first stage of this model is the calculation of the approaching of projectile nucleus to the target nucleus. On the second stage of the model, the evolution of the system formed after the touching of the projectile and target nuclei is considered. The evolution of the system on both stages is described by three-dimensional Langevin equations for the shape parameters of the system. The mutual orientation of the colliding ions and tunneling through the Coulomb barrier in the entrance channel are also taken into account. The potential energy of the system is calculated within the macroscopic-microscopic approach. The calculated mass-energy distributions of fission fragments are compared with the available experimental data. The impact of shell effects, rotation of the system and neutron evaporation on the calculated results is discussed.

## Key claim

The two-stage dynamical stochastic model, which treats the entire fusion-fission process from projectile-target approach through compound-nucleus evolution, reproduces the measured mass distributions of fission fragments from the reaction $^{36}$Ar + $^{142}$Nd → $^{178-x}$Pt + xn at three beam energies, but the calculated width of the total kinetic energy (TKE) distribution is significantly narrower than the experimental width, and the model does not find evidence for two distinct fission modes contributing to the TKE distribution as argued in Andreev (2018).

## Method

The evolution of the system is described in two stages using three-dimensional Langevin equations for shape parameters. In the first stage, the approach of the projectile to the target is calculated, including quadrupole deformations of both ions, the distance between centers of mass, the orientation of the deformed target nucleus, and quantum tunneling through the Coulomb barrier via the WKB approximation. In the second stage, the evolution of the compact system formed after touching is described using Cassini ovaloid shape parameters (elongation, mass asymmetry, and neck radius), with the potential energy calculated within the macroscopic-microscopic shell-correction approach of Strutinsky, and neutron evaporation is treated within a statistical model.

## Key numbers

- Beam energies used: $E_{\rm lab}=153.9$, $168.8$, and $178.8$ MeV, corresponding to center-of-mass energies $E_{\rm cm}=122.78$, $134.68$, and $142.58$ MeV.
- Average angular momentum at the touching point for $E^*=50.5$ MeV: $\langle L \rangle \approx 20.5\ \hbar$.
- Average rotational energy at the touching point: $\langle E_{\rm rot} \rangle \approx 1.89$ MeV.
- Average dissipated energy at the touching point: $\langle E_{\rm dis} \rangle \approx 4.40$ MeV.
- Most probable mass division for the asymmetric component: $A_{\rm L}/A_{\rm H}=79/98$.
- Average calculated TKE: $130.4$ MeV, compared to the experimental main peak TKE$^{\rm high}=133.4$ MeV.
- Calculated width of TKE distribution: $\sigma_{\rm TKE}=11$ MeV, much smaller than the experimental value.
- System with $E_{\rm dis}=40$ MeV lives on average $400$ zs before first neutron evaporation; after evaporation it loses about $10$ MeV, and to evaporate the second neutron it needs about $1500$ zs.

## Context

This work extends the two-stage dynamical stochastic model previously developed by Litnevsky et al. (2014) and Kosenko et al. (2002) to describe fusion-fission reactions with heavy ions, applying it specifically to the reaction $^{36}$Ar + $^{142}$Nd studied experimentally by Andreev (2018). The model contrasts with earlier statistical approaches by Fong (1969) and dynamical models by Adeev (1989), as it treats the entire evolution from projectile-target approach through compound-nucleus formation and fission. The results show that the competition between symmetric and asymmetric fission channels is driven by the enhancement of shell effects during de-excitation via neutron emission, and that the mass and energy distributions are formed mainly at the saddle point rather than following the fission valley bottom.
