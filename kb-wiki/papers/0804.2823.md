# Energy dependence of nucleus-nucleus potential close to the Coulomb barrier

> The nucleus-nucleus interaction potentials in heavy-ion fusion reactions are extracted from the microscopic time-dependent Hartree-Fock theory for mass symmetric reactions $^{16}$O${}+^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca${}+^{40}$Ca, $^{48}$Ca${}+^{48}$Ca and mass asymmetric reactions $^{16}$O$ +^{40,48}$Ca, $^{40}$Ca${}+^{48}$Ca, $^{16}$O+$^{208}$Pb, $^{40}$Ca+$^{90}$Zr. When the center-of-mass energy is much higher than the Coulomb barrier energy, potentials deduced with the microscopic theory identify with the frozen density approximation. As the center-of-mass energy decreases and approaches the Coulomb barrier, potentials become energy dependent. This dependence signs dynamical reorganization of internal degrees of freedom and leads to a reduction of the "apparent" barrier felt by the two nuclei during fusion of the order of $2-3 %$ compared to the frozen density case. Several examples illustrate that the potential landscape changes rapidly when the center-of-mass energy is in the vicinity of the Coulomb barrier energy. The energy dependence is expected to have a significant role on fusion around the Coulomb barrier.

# Energy dependence of nucleus-nucleus potential close to the Coulomb barrier

## Key claim

The nucleus-nucleus interaction potential extracted from time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory becomes energy dependent as the center-of-mass energy decreases toward the Coulomb barrier, leading to a reduction of the apparent barrier height by 2–3% compared to the frozen density approximation; this reduction is attributed to dynamical reorganization of internal degrees of freedom during the approaching phase.

## Method

The authors extracted nucleus-nucleus potentials from TDHF simulations using the Dissipative-Dynamics TDHF (DD-TDHF) method, which reduces the mean-field dynamics to a one-dimensional macroscopic dissipative equation of motion for the relative distance between colliding nuclei. Two TDHF trajectories at slightly different center-of-mass energies (ΔE/E_c.m. ≈ 1–2%) were used to simultaneously determine the potential and friction coefficient, assuming the potential and friction are independent of small energy changes. The potentials were obtained for head-on collisions of spherical nuclei using the Skyrme SLy4d effective force on a three-dimensional mesh with 0.8 fm spacing and 0.45 fm/c time steps.

## Key numbers

- For ¹⁶O+¹⁶O at E_c.m. = 34 MeV (about three times the Coulomb barrier), the DD-TDHF and frozen density potentials are nearly identical, with a barrier difference less than 0.1 MeV
- The dynamical barrier reduction for ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca: V_B^FD = 54.7 MeV, V_B^DD (low E_c.m.) = 53.35 MeV, compared to experimental value 52.8 MeV
- For ¹⁶O+²⁰⁸Pb: V_B^FD = 76.0 MeV, V_B^DD (low E_c.m.) = 74.51 MeV, experimental value 74.52 MeV
- For ⁴⁰Ca+⁹⁰Zr: V_B^FD = 99.8 MeV, V_B^DD (low E_c.m.) = 97.71 MeV, experimental value 96.88 MeV
- The barrier radius for ¹⁶O+¹⁶O: R_B^FD = 8.4 fm, R_B^DD (high E_c.m.) = 8.46 fm, R_B^DD (low E_c.m.) = 8.52 fm
- The difference between FD barrier and lowest DD-TDHF barrier increases approximately linearly with V_B^FD (i.e., with Z_P Z_T)

## Context

This work extends the density-constrained TDHF (DC-TDHF) method of Umar and collaborators (2006) by introducing an alternative macroscopic reduction (DD-TDHF) that also provides access to friction coefficients. The results show that the frozen density approximation, commonly used in macroscopic models such as those by Bass (1977) and Blocki et al. (1977), overestimates Coulomb barriers systematically, with the discrepancy growing for heavier systems. The energy-dependent potentials obtained here can improve coupled-channels calculations of fusion cross sections near the Coulomb barrier, particularly for understanding sub-barrier fusion anomalies observed in high-precision measurements by Jiang et al. (2002–2006) and Dasgupta et al. (2007).
