# Pre-Cluster Dynamics In Multifragmentation

> The initial production and dynamical expansion of hot spherical nuclei are examined as the first stage both in the projectile-multifragmentation and in central collision processes. The initial temperatures, which are necessary for entering the adiabatic spinodal region, as well as the minimum temperatures and densities, which are reached in the expansion, significantly differ for hard and soft equations of state. Additional initial compression, occurring in central collisions leads most likely to a qualitatively different multifragmentation mechanism. Recent experimental data are discussed in relation to the results of the proposed model.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the initial expansion dynamics of hot spherical nuclei, rather than the subsequent fragmentation itself, can explain the temperatures and caloric curves observed in multifragmentation experiments, but only if the system reaches the adiabatic spinodal region and stays there long enough (≥30 fm/c) for instabilities to develop; the mechanism differs qualitatively between peripheral collisions (where turning points occur) and central collisions (where initial compression prevents turning points and leads to an explosion-like breakup).

## Method

The authors model the expansion of a thermalized, spherically symmetric projectile-like nucleus at ground-state density, created in peripheral high-energy collisions according to the abrasion model, and follow its evolution in the temperature-density plane including neutron and proton evaporation. For central collisions, they estimate the initial state from a shock model with compression and neglect evaporation, stopping the calculation at a break-up density of 0.3ρ₀. They compare the calculated trajectories and turning points with experimental caloric-curve data from the ALADIN and FOPI collaborations.

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature of about 5 MeV (E*/A = 2 MeV) is needed for the expanding drop to reach the boundary of the spinodal regime in peripheral collisions.
- Turning points in the expansion occur for initial temperatures up to about 14 MeV and are located around 4 MeV (soft equation of state) and 5 MeV (hard equation of state), independent of initial excitation.
- To enter the spinodal region and stay there for more than 30 fm/c, initial temperatures of about 8 MeV (soft EOS) and 11 MeV (hard EOS) are required.
- For central collisions, an initial compression of 1.5 ρ₀ already eliminates turning points for realistic excitation energies.
- The break-up density used for central collisions is 0.3 ρ₀, obtained from analyzing ALADIN data.
- Turning points for the same initial excitation lie 1–2 MeV higher in temperature and 0.1–0.2 ρ₀ higher in density for the stiff EOS compared to the soft EOS.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Bertsch, Siemens, and Cugnon (1983–1984) on spinodal instabilities as a multifragmentation mechanism, and the expanding emitting source model of Friedman (1990s). It provides a dynamical explanation for the caloric curve plateau discovered by the ALADIN collaboration (Pochodzalla et al., 1995), showing that the plateau arises from turning points in the expansion rather than from a true phase transition. The model enables quantitative comparison with ALADIN and FOPI data and predicts that central collisions, due to initial compression, should show no such plateau, consistent with FOPI observations.
