# Implications of supermassive neutron stars for the form of the equation of state of hybrid stars

> The observations of PSR J0952-0607 and the second object in GW190814 event indicate the possible existence of supermassive neutron stars. In this work, by using the Constant-Sound-Speed (CSS) parametrization to describe the equation of state (EOS) of quark matter, the constraints on the EOS parameters from supermassive hybrid stars are investigated through the Maxwell and Gibbs constructions. It is shown that to support a supermassive hybrid star, a lower transition energy density, a smaller energy density discontinuity and a higher sound speed of quark matter are favored. For the constructed hybrid star EOS model, the maximum mass of the corresponding hybrid stars will not meet the lower mass limit of the second object in GW190814 if the energy density discontinuity takes a value higher than $180~{\rm MeV~fm^{-3}}$. Moreover, it is confirmed that the supermassive neutron star observation can also rule out the existence of twin stars as a supermassive hybrid star requires a relatively small energy density discontinuity. Finally, we give a rough estimate of the lower limit of the dimensionless tidal deformability of neutron stars which ranges from 2 to 3.

## Key claim

The existence of supermassive neutron stars (around 2.5 solar masses) would require a specific combination of equation of state parameters for hybrid stars with a quark core: a lower transition energy density, a smaller energy density discontinuity at the hadron-quark phase transition, and a higher sound speed in quark matter; the abstract's claim that these conditions are "favored" glosses over the fact that the paper shows specific numerical thresholds beyond which a 2.5 solar mass hybrid star cannot be supported.

## Method

The authors construct hybrid star equations of state by combining hadronic EOSs (APR3 for soft, DDME2 for stiff) with the Constant-Sound-Speed (CSS) parametrization for quark matter, connecting the two phases via both Maxwell and Gibbs constructions. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to obtain mass-radius relations and the tidal deformability equations to compute the dimensionless tidal deformability Lambda for hybrid stars, then systematically vary the transition energy density, energy density discontinuity, and quark matter sound speed to determine which parameter combinations can support a 2.5 solar mass star.

## Key numbers

- PSR J0952-0607 mass: 2.35^{+0.17}_{-0.17} solar masses (Romani 2020)
- GW190814 second object mass: 2.59^{+0.08}_{-0.09} solar masses (Abbott 2020)
- Maximum mass of normal neutron stars supported by APR3/DDME2 EOS: up to near 2.4/2.5 solar masses
- Energy density discontinuity threshold: if Delta-epsilon exceeds 180 MeV fm^{-3}, the maximum mass of hybrid stars (Maxwell construction) will not meet the lower mass limit of the GW190814 second object
- For Gibbs construction, Delta-epsilon greater than 180 MeV fm^{-3} induces a similar result
- For hybrid stars with Delta-epsilon = 60 MeV fm^{-3} and transition energy density above 1.6 times saturation density, the stars will not meet the GW190814 lower mass limit if squared sound speed reduces to 0.6
- Lower limit of dimensionless tidal deformability for maximum mass hybrid stars: ranges from 2 to 3
- GW170817 constraint on tidal deformability of 1.4 solar mass neutron star: Lambda_{1.4} = 190^{+390}_{-120}

## Context

This work extends previous studies of supermassive hybrid stars by Blaschke (2021), Christian (2021), Drischler (2021), and others who explored whether compact stars with masses near the GW190814 secondary object could contain quark cores. The paper uses the CSS parametrization (Alford 2013) rather than more detailed quark models like NJL (Benic 2015) or MIT bag (Johnson 1976) to provide a more adjustable parameter space. The results enable future gravitational wave observations, particularly with third-generation detectors like the Einstein Telescope, to constrain the phase transition parameters by measuring the tidal deformability of supermassive neutron stars, though the paper notes that for small tidal deformability values even these detectors may not reach the required precision.
