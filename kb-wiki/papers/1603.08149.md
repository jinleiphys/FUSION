# Understanding transport simulations of heavy-ion collisions at 100 and 400 AMeV: Comparison of heavy ion transport codes under controlled conditions

> Transport simulations are very valuable for extracting physics information from heavy-ion collision experiments. With the emergence of many different transport codes in recent years, it becomes important to estimate their robustness in extracting physics information from experiments. We report on the results of a transport code comparison project. 18 commonly used transport codes were included in this comparison: 9 Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck-type codes and 9 Quantum-Molecular-Dynamics-type codes. These codes have been required to simulate Au+Au collisions using the same physics input for mean fields and for in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections, as well as the same initialization set-up, the impact parameter, and other calculational parameters at 100 and 400 AMeV incident energy. Among the codes we compare one-body observables such as rapidity and transverse flow distributions. We also monitor non-observables such as the initialization of the internal states of colliding nuclei and their stability, the collision rates and the Pauli blocking. We find that not completely identical initializations constitute partly for different evolutions. Different strategies to determine the collision probabilities, and to enforce the Pauli blocking, also produce considerably different results. There is a substantial spread in the predictions for the observables, which is much smaller at the higher incident energy. We quantify the uncertainties in the collective flow resulting from the simulation alone as about $30\%$ at 100 AMeV and $13\%$ at 400 AMeV, respectively. We propose further steps within the code comparison project to test the different aspects of transport simulations in a box calculation of infinite nuclear matter. This should, in particular, improve the robustness of transport model predictions at lower incident energies where abundant amounts of data are available.

## Key claim

The paper reports that even when 18 different transport codes are forced to use identical physics inputs (mean fields, in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections, initialization, impact parameter, and other calculational parameters), the spread in predicted collective flow observables remains substantial at 30% at 100 AMeV and 13% at 400 AMeV, with the lower energy regime being particularly sensitive to differences in initialization strategies, collision probability determination, and Pauli blocking enforcement.

## Method

The authors compared 9 Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU)-type codes and 9 Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD)-type codes simulating Au+Au collisions at 100 and 400 AMeV incident energy with a fixed impact parameter of 7 fm, using identical mean-field potentials and in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections. They monitored one-body observables (rapidity distributions, transverse flow) and non-observables (initialization of internal nuclear states, stability, collision rates, Pauli blocking) across all codes, and also performed calculations with a large impact parameter of 20 fm to study initialization stability without actual nucleus-nucleus collisions.

## Key numbers

* Collective flow uncertainty from simulation alone: about 30% at 100 AMeV incident energy for Au+Au collisions.
* Collective flow uncertainty from simulation alone: about 13% at 400 AMeV incident energy for Au+Au collisions.
* 18 transport codes were compared: 9 BUU-type and 9 QMD-type codes.
* Test particle number per nucleon in BUU calculations was 100.
* Impact parameter for the main comparison was 7 fm; initialization stability was studied at b = 20 fm.

## Context

This work extends the earlier transport code comparison initiated at the European Center for Theoretical Studies for Nuclear Physics and Related Fields (ECT*) in Trento (2004), which compared particle production at 1 AGeV, and a subsequent workshop in Trento (2009) that began comparing bulk observables at lower energies. The present study quantifies model-dependent uncertainties that must be accounted for when extracting the nuclear equation of state from heavy-ion collision data, particularly for the symmetry energy above saturation density where constraints remain uncertain. The authors propose a follow-up "box calculation" of infinite nuclear matter with periodic boundary conditions to isolate and test specific aspects of transport simulations, aiming to improve robustness at lower incident energies where abundant experimental data exist.
