# Examining empirical evidence of the effect of superfluidity on the fusion barrier

> Background: Several Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (TDHFB) calculations predict that the super- fluidity enhances the fluctuations of the fusion barrier. This effect is not fully understood and not yet revealed experimentally. Purpose: The goal of this study is to investigate empirically the effect of the superfluidity on the fusion barrier width. Method: First, the local regression method is introduce and used to determine the barrier distribution more precisely. A second method that requires only the calculation of an integral of the cross section is developed to determine accurately the fluctuations of the barrier. A benchmark is done between this two methods and with the fitting method usually used. This integral method showing a better agreement in a test case, it is applied systematically in a selection of 115 fusion reactions. Results: The fluctuations of the barrier for superfluid systems are on average larger than for magic or semi-magic nuclei. This is due to the deformation effects and the effect of the superfluidity. To disentangle those two effects, we compare the experimental width to the width estimated from a model that takes into account the tunneling, the deformation and the vibration effect. The deviation of the experimental width from this theory for reaction between superfluid nuclei shows that the superfluidity enhance the fusion barrier width. Conclusions: This analysis shows that the predicted effect of the superfluidity on the width of the barrier is real and is of the order of 1 MeV.

# Examining empirical evidence of the effect of superfluidity on the fusion barrier

## Key claim

The analysis shows that the predicted effect of superfluidity on the width of the fusion barrier is real and is of the order of 1 MeV, but this empirical result contradicts the idea of a very strong effect of superfluidity on the fusion barrier, and no effect is found on the barrier height or fusion radius.

## Method

The author developed two new methods to determine the width of the fusion barrier distribution: a local regression method to compute the barrier distribution more precisely than the standard three-point difference formula, and an integral method that avoids calculating the second derivative by using partial integration of the fusion cross section. These methods were benchmarked against simulated data from the CCFULL program and then applied systematically to 115 fusion reactions selected from experimental databases, comparing the experimental barrier width to the width estimated from the Siwek-Wilczynska model (2004) that accounts for tunneling, static deformation, and vibration effects.

## Key numbers

- For the reaction $^{20}$O+$^{20}$O simulated with TDHFB, an amplitude of $\Delta B$=0.4 MeV is found between the maximum and minimum heights of the barrier due to pairing interaction
- For the reaction $^{44}$Ca+$^{44}$Ca, TDHFB calculations find a value of $\Delta B$=2.3 MeV
- The integral method applied to CCFULL test cases yields $\sigma_B$ = 4.18 MeV with collective excitations and $\sigma_B$ = 1.03 MeV without collective excitations
- For the reaction $^{40}$Ar+$^{144}$Sm (S=1), the experimental width is $\sigma^{\rm integ.}_{\rm exp.}$ = 4.39 $\pm$ 0.44 MeV compared to the Siwek-Wilczynska model prediction of $\sigma_{\rm Siw.}$ = 2.31 MeV
- For the reaction $^{40}$Ar+$^{154}$Sm (S=2), the experimental width is $\sigma^{\rm integ.}_{\rm exp.}$ = 5.28 $\pm$ 0.23 MeV compared to $\sigma_{\rm Siw.}$ = 4.27 MeV
- For the reaction $^{32}$S+$^{110}$Pd (S=2), the experimental width is $\sigma^{\rm integ.}_{\rm exp.}$ = 4.69 $\pm$ 0.09 MeV compared to $\sigma_{\rm Siw.}$ = 2.65 MeV
- For the reaction $^{58}$Ni+$^{132}$Sn, using only evaporated residue data gives $\sigma_{\rm exp}$=2.77 MeV and $R_B^{\rm exp}$=7.46 fm, while using complete data (evaporated residue and fusion-fission) gives $\sigma_{\rm exp}$=3.36 MeV and $R_B^{\rm exp}$=10.8 fm

## Context

This work extends the predictions from Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (TDHFB) calculations by Has16, Mag16, and Sek17, which suggested that superfluidity enhances fluctuations of the fusion barrier through the dependence on the initial relative gauge angle between superfluid nuclei. The study provides the first systematic empirical evidence for this effect by analyzing 115 fusion reactions and comparing to the empirical model of Siwek-Wilczynska (2004), which does not include superfluidity. The results enable future microscopic TDHFB calculations to be tested against the specific list of reactions provided where the superfluidity enhancement exceeds 1 MeV, and suggest that more complex methods combining tunneling effects with superfluidity (such as a Density-constraint-TDHFB method) remain to be developed.
