# High-precision determination of radiative corrections to superallowed nuclear beta decays

> Superallowed $0^+\rightarrow 0^+$ transitions between $T=1$ nuclei have been a perfect avenue avenue for determining the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa matrix element $V_{ud}$, which imposes powerful constraints on physics beyond the Standard Model at low energies. For a long time, the precision of $V_{ud}$ has been limited by uncertainties in radiative corrections that arise from non-perturbative strong interaction physics at both the hadronic and nuclear levels. In this talk, I will describe some recent efforts to pin down these corrections by combining dispersive analysis with experimental data, lattice QCD, and nuclear many-body calculations.

## Key claim

The precision of the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa matrix element \(V_{ud}\) extracted from superallowed nuclear beta decays is currently limited by theoretical uncertainties in radiative corrections, not by experimental precision; the talk describes recent efforts to reduce these uncertainties by combining dispersive analysis with experimental data, lattice QCD, and nuclear many-body calculations, but notes that the final results still carry systematic uncertainties from missing non-nucleonic degrees of freedom and unknown low-energy constants.

## Method

The author splits the full radiative correction into a nucleus-independent part \(\Delta_R^V\) (from free neutron decay) and a nucleus-dependent part \(\delta_\text{NS}\). For \(\Delta_R^V\), two methods are used: a dispersion relation analysis that relates the \(\gamma W\)-box diagram to the parity-odd nucleon structure function \(F_3\), and a direct lattice QCD calculation of the four-point correlation function involving electromagnetic and weak currents. For \(\delta_\text{NS}\), a dispersive approach using the No Core Shell Model (NCSM) with chiral two- and three-body forces is applied to compute the nuclear Green's function via the Lanczos strength method, while an alternative effective field theory (EFT) approach splits the loop integral into ultrasoft and potential regions and represents contributions as ground-state matrix elements of two-body potentials.

## Key numbers

- \(\Delta_R^V = 0.02479(21)\) from averaging dispersion relation analyses (Gorchtein 2023)
- \(\Delta_R^V = 0.02439(18)\) from lattice QCD (Ma 2023), agreeing with the dispersion result within 1.5\(\sigma\)
- For \({}^{10}\text{C}\rightarrow {}^{10}\text{B}\), \(\delta_\text{NS}\) evolved from \(-0.345(35)\%\) (shell model, Hardy 2014) to \(-0.400(50)\%\) (dispersive with Fermi gas model, Seng 2018) to \(-0.422(31)\%\) (NCSM, Gennari 2024)
- For \({}^{14}\text{O}\rightarrow {}^{14}\text{N}\), the EFT approach with Quantum Monte Carlo reports \(V_{ud}[^{14}\text{O}] = 0.97364(52)_{\delta_\text{NS}}(20)_{\text{other}}\), compared to \(V_{ud}[^{14}\text{O}] = 0.97405(31)_{\delta_\text{NS}}(20)_{\text{other}}\) from Hardy (2020)

## Context

This work extends the long-standing program of extracting \(V_{ud}\) from superallowed \(0^+\rightarrow 0^+\) transitions (Hardy 2020) by addressing the dominant theory uncertainty from radiative corrections. The dispersive approach (Seng 2018, Gorchtein 2023) and lattice QCD (Feng 2020, Ma 2023) have recently improved the nucleus-independent correction \(\Delta_R^V\), while the first _ab initio_ NCSM calculation of \(\delta_\text{NS}\) (Gennari 2024) for \({}^{10}\text{C}\rightarrow {}^{10}\text{B}\) corrects earlier shell-model calculations that missed quasi-elastic contributions. The parallel EFT approach (Cirigliano 2024) offers a computationally lighter alternative but introduces unknown low-energy constants. The proposed future matching between dispersive and EFT approaches for the two lightest superallowed transitions could resolve these constants and enable precise predictions for all other transitions, further refining the unitarity test of the CKM matrix.
