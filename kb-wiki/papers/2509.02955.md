# Two-flavor chirally imbalanced quark matter beyond large $N_c$

> We investigate a chirally imbalanced medium in the context of the two-flavor Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model using both the large-$N_c$ (LN) and beyond large-$N_c$ (BLN) approximations. To incorporate BLN effects, we consider the optimized perturbation theory (OPT) to the first nontrivial order, which includes two-loop (exchange) contributions. This procedure allows us to explicitly explore how finite $N_c$ corrections affect the thermodynamics as well as the phase diagram of chirally imbalanced quark matter. We then compare the results obtained with a sharp three-dimensional cutoff -- generically referred to as the traditional regularization scheme -- and with an alternative procedure called the medium separation scheme (MSS). In the first case, we observe that the pseudocritical temperature decreases as the chiral chemical potential increases, an effect dubbed inverse chiral catalysis. On the other hand, when considering the MSS regularization, which properly isolates the medium contributions from the vacuum, we find the opposite result. We show that the results obtained with MSS are consistent with well-established LQCD data in both the LN and BLN approximations. Finally, we suggest that to cope with the high-density limit, the standard OPT interpolation prescription must be modified with the inclusion of an extra variational parameter.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the apparent inverse chiral catalysis (decrease of pseudocritical temperature with increasing chiral chemical potential) observed in the two-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model with traditional regularization is an artifact of improper regularization; when the medium separation scheme (MSS) is used instead, the pseudocritical temperature increases with chiral chemical potential, consistent with lattice QCD data, and this result holds for both the large-Nc and beyond-large-Nc approximations.

## Method

The authors study the two-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model with a chiral chemical potential using both the large-Nc (LN) approximation and the optimized perturbation theory (OPT) at first nontrivial order, which includes two-loop exchange contributions to incorporate finite-Nc corrections. They compare results from a sharp three-dimensional cutoff (traditional regularization scheme, TRS) with the medium separation scheme (MSS), which isolates medium contributions from vacuum divergences before regularization. The OPT interpolation prescription is applied to compute thermodynamic quantities and phase diagrams.

## Key numbers

- The pseudocritical temperature for chiral symmetry restoration decreases as the chiral chemical potential increases when using the traditional regularization scheme (inverse chiral catalysis effect)
- The pseudocritical temperature increases with chiral chemical potential when using the medium separation scheme, consistent with lattice QCD results from Braguta et al. (2015, 2016)
- Results obtained with MSS are consistent with well-established lattice QCD data in both the LN and BLN approximations
- The standard OPT interpolation prescription must be modified with an extra variational parameter to cope with the high-density limit

## Context

This work extends previous studies of chirally imbalanced quark matter using the NJL model, particularly the work of Farias et al. (2016) which first achieved correct description of the local NJL model through MSS implementation, and the study by Azeredo et al. (2024) which employed MSS with a Polyakov loop potential. The results contradict earlier NJL model predictions from Ruggieri (2011), Fukushima et al. (2010), and Chao et al. (2013) that showed inverse chiral catalysis, and instead support lattice QCD simulations by Braguta et al. (2015, 2016) showing that the chiral chemical potential enhances quark-antiquark pairing and raises the chiral restoration temperature. The work enables more reliable studies of dense matter in astrophysics and heavy-ion collisions by demonstrating that proper regularization is essential for obtaining physically consistent results from effective models.
