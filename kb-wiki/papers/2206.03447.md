# Evaluations of uncertainties in simulations of propagation of ultrahigh-energy cosmic-ray nuclei derived from microscopic nuclear models

> Photodisintegration is a main energy loss process for ultrahigh-energy cosmic-ray (UHECR) nuclei in intergalactic space. Therefore, it is crucial to understand systematic uncertainty in photodisintegration when simulating the propagation of UHECR nuclei. In this work, we calculated the cross sections using the random phase approximation (RPA) of density functional theory (DFT), a microscopic nuclear model. We calculated the $E1$ strength of 29 nuclei using three different density functionals. We obtained the cross sections of photonuclear reactions, including photodisintegration, with the $E1$ strength. Then, we implemented the cross sections in the cosmic-ray propagation code CRPropa. We found that assuming certain astrophysical parameter values, the difference between UHECR energy spectrum predictions using the RPA calculation and the default photodisintegration model in CRPropa can be more than the statistical uncertainty of the spectrum. We also found that the differences between the RPA calculations and CRPropa default in certain astrophysical parameters obtained by a combined fit of UHECR energy spectrum and composition data assuming a phenomenological model of UHECR sources can be more than the uncertainty of the data.

## Key claim

The systematic uncertainty in photodisintegration cross sections derived from microscopic nuclear models (random phase approximation of density functional theory) can cause differences in predicted ultrahigh-energy cosmic-ray (UHECR) energy spectra and inferred astrophysical source parameters that exceed the statistical uncertainties of current experimental data, but only under certain assumed astrophysical parameter values.

## Method

The authors calculated electric dipole (E1) strength functions for 29 stable nuclei using the random phase approximation (RPA) with three different Skyrme energy-density functionals (SkM*, SLy4, and UNEDF1). These E1 strength functions were input into the TALYS nuclear reaction code to obtain nonelastic cross sections, which were then implemented in the CRPropa 3 cosmic-ray propagation code. The authors simulated UHECR propagation from identical sources emitting five nuclear species (1H, 4He, 14N, 28Si, 56Fe) and performed combined fits of the simulated energy spectrum, mean logarithmic mass, and variance of logarithmic mass to data from the Pierre Auger Observatory, comparing results between the RPA-based cross sections and the default CRPropa photodisintegration model.

## Key numbers

- 29 stable nuclei were calculated, ranging from 10B to 56Fe
- Three density functionals were used: SkM*, SLy4, and UNEDF1
- Peak energies of the giant dipole resonance for 28Si were 18.0 MeV (SkM*), 17.8 MeV (SLy4), and 18.9 MeV (UNEDF1), compared to the CRPropa default value
- The spreading width gamma was set to 2.0 MeV for the continuous strength function calculation
- The E1 strength function was calculated up to 200 MeV
- The fit used 15 data points for the energy spectrum and 20 data points for composition (10 for mean ln A and 10 for variance of ln A) above 10^18.7 eV
- The energy scale shift Delta E/E was allowed to vary uniformly from -0.14 to +0.14
- Best-fit parameters using CRPropa default with SIBYLL-2.3c and TALYS were p = -1.0, log10(R_cut/V) = 18.2, m = 4, and Delta E/E = -8%

## Context

This work extends previous evaluations of photodisintegration model uncertainties in UHECR propagation, which compared the PSB model, PEANUT, TALYS, and Geant4 (as done by DESY (2017), SimProp (2017), and AugerFit (2017)). The study is part of the PANDORA project, which plans to systematically measure photoabsorption cross sections for light to A ~ 60 nuclei using facilities in Japan, Romania, and South Africa. The RPA calculations provide a microscopic alternative to the phenomenological empirical laws used in TALYS, which are known to overestimate peak energies for light nuclei and do not account for nuclear deformation effects. This work enables quantification of how nuclear model uncertainties propagate into astrophysical parameter inference from UHECR observations.
