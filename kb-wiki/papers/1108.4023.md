# Adaptation of the Landau-Migdal Quasiparticle Pattern to Strongly Correlated Fermi Systems

> A quasiparticle pattern advanced in Landau's first article on Fermi liquid theory is adapted to elucidate the properties of a class of strongly correlated Fermi systems characterized by a Lifshitz phase diagram featuring a quantum critical point (QCP) where the density of states diverges. The necessary condition for stability of the Landau Fermi Liquid state is shown to break down in such systems, triggering a cascade of topological phase transitions that lead, without symmetry violation, to states with multi-connected Fermi surfaces. The end point of this evolution is found to be an exceptional state whose spectrum of single-particle excitations exhibits a completely flat portion at zero temperature. Analysis of the evolution of the temperature dependence of the single-particle spectrum yields results that provide a natural explanation of classical behavior of this class of Fermi systems in the QCP region.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the breakdown of standard Fermi-liquid theory in strongly correlated Fermi systems near a quantum critical point (QCP) is driven by topological phase transitions that rearrange the Fermi surface, rather than by second-order phase transitions that suppress the quasiparticle weight. The authors claim that the necessary stability condition for the Landau state is violated at the QCP, triggering a cascade of topological transitions that ultimately produce a flat portion in the single-particle excitation spectrum at zero temperature.

## Method

The authors analyze the Landau-Migdal quasiparticle pattern by examining the necessary stability condition for the Landau state, expressed through the relation between the Fermi velocity and the first harmonic of the Landau interaction function. They study a model with interaction function \(f(q)=\lambda/q\) and a regularized version \(f(q)=\lambda (q^2+\kappa^2)^{-1}\) with \(\kappa=0.07\,p_F\), solving the variational equation \(\delta E(n(p))/\delta n(p)-\mu =0\) numerically using a regularization procedure with a small additional local interaction \(U_0\delta({\bf p}-{\bf p}_1)\). The analysis is complemented by a variational estimate of the ground-state energy for a rearranged momentum distribution and by drawing an analogy to the Thomas-Fermi problem for ionized atoms to illustrate the breakdown of analyticity.

## Key numbers

- The critical value for violation of the necessary stability condition in the model with \(f(q)=\lambda/q\) is \(F^0_1(\rho_{\infty}) = 3\), where \(F^0_1 = f_1 p_F M / \pi^2\).
- In the regularized model with \(f(q)=\lambda (q^2+\kappa^2)^{-1}\) and \(\kappa=0.07\,p_F\), the Pauli principle is satisfied only when the coupling constant \(\lambda\) exceeds a critical value \(\lambda_{\crit}=1.52\,\lambda_{\qcp}\), where \(\lambda_{\qcp}\) corresponds to the first topological transition at the QCP.
- For 3D liquid \(^3\)He, the dimensionless first harmonic extracted from specific-heat data is \(F^0_1 \geq 2.0\); for 2D liquid \(^3\)He films, the effective mass diverges, implying \(F^0_1 > 3.0\).
- In the illustrative model, the Fermi volume expands or swells by as much as 30\% at \(\lambda > \lambda_{\crit}\).

## Context

This work extends the earlier theoretical proposals of Khodel, Shaginyan, and Nozieres (1990s) regarding fermion condensation and Fermi surface swelling, which predicted the flattening of single-particle spectra in strongly correlated Fermi systems. The authors contrast their topological scenario with the collective scenario of Doniach and Engelsberg (1966) and the spin-density wave model, arguing that the QCP is separated from the line of second-order phase transitions, a feature observed experimentally in 2D liquid \(^3\)He by Saunders and collaborators and in heavy-fermion metals by Bud'ko and Steglich. The paper enables a unified theoretical framework for understanding non-Fermi-liquid behavior in systems such as dense \(^3\)He films, heavy-fermion metals, and the 2D electron gas in MOSFETs, where the effective mass diverges at the QCP.
