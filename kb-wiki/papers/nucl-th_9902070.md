# Non-extensive statistics, fluctuations and correlations in high energy nuclear collisions

> Starting from the experimental evidence that high-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions cannot be described in terms of superpositions of elementary nucleon-nucleon interactions, we analyze the possibility that memory effects and long-range forces imply a nonextensive statistical regime during high energy heavy ion collisions. The relevance of these statistical effects and their compatibility with the available experimental data are discussed. In particular we show that theoretical estimates, obtained in the framework of the generalized nonextensive thermostatistics, can reproduce the shape of the pion transverse mass spectrum and explain the different physical origin of the transverse momentum correlation function of the pions emitted during the central Pb+Pb and during the p+p collisions at 158 A GeV.

## Key claim

The paper argues that memory effects and long-range forces in high-energy heavy ion collisions can lead to a nonextensive statistical regime, and that small deviations from standard Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics (quantified by the entropic parameter \(q\)) can reproduce the shape of the pion transverse mass spectrum and explain the different physical origin of transverse momentum correlations in Pb+Pb versus p+p collisions at 158 A GeV, without requiring a collective transverse flow interpretation.

## Method

The authors apply the generalized nonextensive Tsallis thermostatistics, maximizing the Tsallis entropy under constraints of fixed average internal energy and particle number to obtain modified single-particle distribution functions for bosons and fermions. They derive analytical expressions for the transverse mass spectrum and the event-by-event transverse momentum fluctuation measure \(\Phi_{p_\perp}\) (defined by Gazdzicki and Mrowczynski) in terms of the entropic parameter \(q\), temperature \(T\), and chemical potential \(\mu\), and compare these to experimental data from the NA49 Collaboration for central Pb+Pb and p+p collisions at 158 A GeV.

## Key numbers

* The experimental correlation measure for Pb+Pb collisions at 158 A GeV is \(\Phi^{exp}_{p_\perp}=0.6\pm 1\) MeV, while for p+p collisions at the same energy it is \(\Phi^{exp}_{p_\perp}=5\pm 1\) MeV (preliminary).
* With \(T=170\) MeV and \(\mu=60\) MeV, the nonextensive statistics yields \(\Phi_{p_\perp}=5\) MeV for \(q=1.038\); the standard extensive statistics (\(q=1\)) gives \(\Phi_{p_\perp}=24.7\) MeV.
* With \(\mu=0\) MeV, the same \(\Phi_{p_\perp}=5\) MeV is obtained with \(q=1.015\); the standard statistics gives \(\Phi_{p_\perp}=13.6\) MeV.
* The NA49 Collaboration estimated a statistical two-particle correlation contribution of \(\Delta\Phi_{p_\perp}=5\pm 1.5\) MeV and an anti-correlation from two-track resolution of \(\Delta\Phi_{p_\perp}=-4\pm 0.5\) MeV for Pb+Pb data.
* The authors consider small deviations from standard statistics with \(|(q-1)|<0.05\) to be consistent with experimental midrapidity transverse mass distributions.

## Context

This work extends the application of Tsallis nonextensive thermostatistics (Tsallis, 1988) to high-energy nuclear collisions, building on earlier studies that found nonextensive effects in astrophysical self-gravitating systems, the solar neutrino problem, and pion-nucleus scattering. It directly addresses the experimental puzzle from the NA49 Collaboration (Roland and collaborators, 1998) that the transverse momentum fluctuation measure \(\Phi_{p_\perp}\) has a similar magnitude in Pb+Pb and p+p collisions but arises from different physical origins. The paper contrasts its approach with standard interpretations invoking collective transverse flow (Bear and collaborators, 1997) and with the Markovian approximation used in transport models like UrQMD, which was criticized by Bleicher and collaborators (1998) for certain scattering processes. The results enable future experimental searches for negative partial contributions to \(\Phi_{p_\perp}\) at high transverse momenta (above 0.5 GeV) as a signature of nonextensive statistics.
