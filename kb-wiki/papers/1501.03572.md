# Uncertainty Quantification for Nuclear Density Functional Theory and Information Content of New Measurements

> Statistical tools of uncertainty quantification can be used to assess the information content of measured observables with respect to present-day theoretical models; to estimate model errors and thereby improve predictive capability; to extrapolate beyond the regions reached by experiment; and to provide meaningful input to applications and planned measurements. To showcase new opportunities offered by such tools, we make a rigorous analysis of theoretical statistical uncertainties in nuclear density functional theory using Bayesian inference methods. By considering the recent mass measurements from the Canadian Penning Trap at Argonne National Laboratory, we demonstrate how the Bayesian analysis and a direct least-squares optimization, combined with high-performance computing, can be used to assess the information content of the new data with respect to a model based on the Skyrme energy density functional approach. Employing the posterior probability distribution computed with a Gaussian process emulator, we apply the Bayesian framework to propagate theoretical statistical uncertainties in predictions of nuclear masses, two-neutron dripline, and fission barriers. Overall, we find that the new mass measurements do not impose a constraint that is strong enough to lead to significant changes in the model parameters. The example discussed in this study sets the stage for quantifying and maximizing the impact of new measurements with respect to current modeling and guiding future experimental efforts, thus enhancing the experiment-theory cycle in the scientific method.

## Key claim

The new mass measurements of neutron-rich nuclei from the Canadian Penning Trap at Argonne National Laboratory do not impose a constraint strong enough to lead to significant changes in the parameters of the UNEDF1 Skyrme energy density functional, because both the amount of new data and the range of neutron excess probed are insufficient to impact the model appreciably.

## Method

The authors applied Bayesian inference to calibrate the 12-parameter UNEDF1 Skyrme energy density functional using a Gaussian process emulator trained on 200 Latin hypercube samples of DFT calculations with the HFBTHO solver. They constructed the posterior probability distribution for the parameters using Markov chain Monte Carlo, first with the original UNEDF1 dataset of 115 observables and then with an augmented dataset that included 17 new mass measurements from the Canadian Penning Trap. For comparison, they also performed a direct least-squares reoptimization of the functional (called UNEDF1_CPT) that included the new masses, independent of the Gaussian process response surface.

## Key numbers

* The UNEDF1 functional has 12 parameters and was optimized on a dataset of 115 observables (n_d = 115), with each chi-squared evaluation requiring about 5 minutes of CPU time on over 800 cores.
* The root-mean-square deviation for deformed masses improved from 0.721 MeV (UNEDF1) to 0.578 MeV (UNEDF1_CPT), while for spherical masses it worsened from 1.461 MeV to 1.545 MeV.
* The root-mean-square deviation for the CPT masses was 1.064 MeV for UNEDF1 and 0.479 MeV for UNEDF1_CPT.
* The largest relative difference between UNEDF1 and UNEDF1_CPT parameters was 0.6 sigma for the isovector surface coupling constant C_1^{rho Delta rho} and the surface symmetry energy.
* The estimated uncertainty for calculated masses is approximately +/- 2 MeV, consistent with the root-mean-square deviation of 1.9 MeV for even-even nuclei across the nuclear landscape for UNEDF1.
* The uncertainty on the position of the two-neutron dripline is on the order of 15 to 20 nucleons.

## Context

This work extends the UNEDF program of nuclear density functional theory parameter optimization, specifically building on the UNEDF1 functional of Kortelainen (2012) and the covariance analysis reported there. The Bayesian framework enables rigorous uncertainty quantification that goes beyond the linear approximation used in prior covariance studies. The results show that the new CPT mass data, which probe neutron-rich nuclei around 132Sn, provide only weak additional constraints on isovector coupling constants, and the authors concur with Van Schelt (2013) that existing mass models are insufficient for accurate r-process simulations. This work enables future assessments of the information content of new measurements for guiding experimental campaigns at next-generation radioactive ion beam facilities.
