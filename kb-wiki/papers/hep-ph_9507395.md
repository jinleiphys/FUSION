# Partial $U(1)_A$ Restoration and $η$ Enhancement in High-Energy Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We calculate the thermally averaged rates for the $η$-$π$ conversion and $η$ scattering using the Di Vecchia-Veneziano model and t' Hooft model, which incorporate explicitly the $U(1)_A$ anomaly. Assuming an exponential suppression of the $U(1)_A$ anomaly, we also take into account the partial restoration of $U(1)_A$ symmetry at high temperatures. We find that the chemical equilibrium between $η$ and $π$ breaks up considerably earlier than the thermal equilibrium. Two distinct scenarios for the $η$ freeze-out are discussed and the corresponding chemical potentials are calculated. We predict an enhancement of the thermal $η$-production as a possible signal of the partial $U(1)_A$ restoration in high-energy heavy-ion collisions.

# Partial $U(1)_A$ Restoration and $\eta$ Enhancement in High-Energy Heavy-Ion Collisions

## Key claim

The authors predict an enhancement of thermal $\eta$ production in high-energy heavy-ion collisions as a possible signal of partial $U(1)_A$ restoration, but only if the chemical equilibrium between $\eta$ and $\pi$ breaks up considerably earlier than thermal equilibrium, and the enhancement depends on which of two distinct freeze-out scenarios (adiabatic mass relaxation or "quenching") actually occurs.

## Method

The authors calculated thermally averaged rates for $\eta$-$\pi$ conversion and $\eta$ scattering using the Di Vecchia-Veneziano nonlinear $\sigma$-model and the t' Hooft linear $\sigma$-model, both of which incorporate the $U(1)_A$ anomaly explicitly. They modeled the temperature dependence of the $U(1)_A$ anomaly by assuming an exponential suppression $a(T)=a(0)e^{-(T/T_0)^2}$ with $T_0=150$ MeV, which determines the temperature-dependent masses of $\eta$ and $\eta'$ through the $\eta$-$\eta'$ mixing. They then computed thermal and chemical freeze-out temperatures by equating the respective collision time scales to the transverse freeze-out radius $R=6$ fm, and calculated the resulting $\eta$ momentum distributions and $\eta/\pi^0$ ratios under two scenarios for the mass evolution after chemical freeze-out.

## Key numbers

- Chemical freeze-out temperature: $T_{\rm ch} \simeq 168$ MeV, at which $\eta\eta \leftrightarrow \pi\pi$ conversion becomes slow
- Thermal freeze-out temperature: $T_{\rm th} \simeq 139$ MeV, comparable to the decoupling temperature of thermal pions
- $\eta$ mass at chemical freeze-out: $m_\eta(T_{\rm ch}) \simeq 360$ MeV
- $\eta$ mass at thermal freeze-out: $m_\eta(T_{\rm th}) \simeq 413$ MeV
- Ratio of freeze-out times: $\tau_d/\tau_c \simeq 1.53$ from $1+1$ dimensional hydrodynamic evolution with $T_{\rm ch}/T_{\rm th}=1.21$
- Chemical potential factor in Scenario A: $\lambda_\eta = e^{\mu/T_{\rm th}} = 1.58$
- Chemical potential factor in Scenario B: $\lambda_\eta = e^{\mu/T_{\rm th}} \simeq 1.24$
- Zero-temperature $\eta$ mass: $m_\eta^{\rm exp}=547$ MeV; model prediction at $T=0$: $m_\eta = 500$ MeV
- Zero-temperature $\eta'$ mass: $m_{\eta'}^{\rm exp}=958$ MeV; model prediction at $T=0$: $m_{\eta'} = 984$ MeV
- Mixing angle at $T=0$: $\theta = 18.3^\circ$ (model) compared to $\theta^{\rm exp} \simeq 20^\circ$
- $\eta'$ mass at $T=T_{\rm ch}$: $m_{\eta'} \simeq 750$ MeV
- $\eta$ mass difference for trapping: $\Delta m \sim 100 - 200$ MeV

## Context

This work extends the suggestion by Pisarski and Wilczek (1984) and Shuryak (1994) that $U(1)_A$ restoration at high temperatures could have observable consequences in heavy-ion collisions, by providing a quantitative calculation of the thermal and chemical equilibration processes for $\eta$ particles. The authors use the Di Vecchia-Veneziano model (1980) and t' Hooft model (1986) to incorporate the $U(1)_A$ anomaly, and the exponential suppression form for the anomaly follows from Pisarski and Yaffe (1980). The results enable experimental tests via the $\eta/\pi^0$ ratio as a function of transverse momentum, with preliminary data from the WA80 Collaboration (1994) on $S+Au$ collisions at CERN SPS showing a trend consistent with enhancement, though better statistics are needed. The enhanced $\eta$ production may also partially account for the observed dilepton enhancement below 500 MeV invariant mass reported by the CERES Collaboration (1995).
