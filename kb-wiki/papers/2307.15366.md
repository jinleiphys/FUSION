# Ridges in rotating neutron-star properties due to first order phase transitions

> We identify combinations of observables for rotating neutron stars that can one day bear on the question of whether there can be first order phase transitions in the neutron matter therein. We employ the Hartle-Thorne theory for stationary, rotating neutron stars at conventional angular velocities (in the pulsar and millisecond pulsar ranges) and extract three-dimensional sections of the ellipticity or the dynamical angular momentum as function of the star's mass and angular velocity. An eventual first order phase transition in the equation of state (EoS) leaves a clear ridge (nonanalyticity) in these observables, akin to the sudden kink in popular mass-radius diagrams for static stars. Finally, we observe that static neutron stars in General Relativity (GR) will fail to be compact enough for the light ring's position at r=3M to be outside the star, except for the most extreme equations of state. The outer light ring of a rotating star might however be formed unless the EoS softens too much, and its eventual detection can then be used to constrain the EoS (or the gravity theory).

## Key claim

The paper identifies that first-order phase transitions in neutron-star matter produce clear ridges (nonanalyticities) in three-dimensional plots of rotating-star observables such as ellipticity or dynamical angular momentum as functions of stellar mass and angular velocity, but this result depends on the Hartle-Thorne slow-rotation approximation and is demonstrated using equations of state from the nEoS band that have not been filtered by astrophysical constraints.

## Method

The authors employ the Hartle-Thorne perturbative theory for stationary, slowly rotating neutron stars, solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for static stars and then adding rotational corrections to first order in the angular velocity. They compute the ellipticity, angular momentum, and dimensionless spin parameter for families of stars across three different equations of state from the nEoS band, including one with an artificially inserted first-order phase transition, and examine how these observables vary with mass and angular velocity up to 5 radians per millisecond.

## Key numbers

- The fastest known pulsar has a frequency of 716 Hz, corresponding to angular velocity Omega = 4.5 radians per millisecond
- The Keplerian mass-shedding limit is given by Omega_k approximately 0.65 sqrt(M/R^3), which yields Omega_k approximately 240 ((M/M_sun)/(R/km)^3)^(1/2) per millisecond
- The millisecond pulsar PSR J0030+0451 with frequency 205.53 Hz has surface velocity Omega R/c = 0.055, within the slow-rotation approximation
- The fastest rotator at 716 Hz would have Omega R/c of order 15 to 25 percent depending on radius
- For GW170817, the effective spin parameter chi_eff is estimated to lie in the range (-0.01, 0.17)
- The photon ring of the Schwarzschild geometry at r = 3M corresponds to 6.2 km for a canonical 1.4 solar mass neutron star and 10.1 km for a 2.3 solar mass neutron star
- The maximum compact configuration has a lower bound R greater than 2.824 solar masses, close to the photon ring position

## Context

This work extends the known result that first-order phase transitions produce kinks in static mass-radius diagrams (Paschalidis 2017, Bauswein 2018, Han 2018, Most 2018) to the rotating case, proposing that three-dimensional plots of observables like J(M,Omega) or ellipticity(M,Omega) make these features more identifiable than two-dimensional kinks. The paper also connects to the proposed backbending phenomenon due to changes in moment of inertia (Glendenning 1997, Bejger 2016, Franzon 2016) and to universal relations such as the I-Love-Q relations (Yagi 2013, Blazquez-Salcedo 2022). The finding that static neutron stars in General Relativity are generally not compact enough to host a photon ring at r=3M, except for extreme equations of state, suggests that detection of such a ring in rotating stars could constrain both the equation of state and the theory of gravity.
