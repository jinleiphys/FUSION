# Quantum kinetic theory for spin-1/2 fermions in Wigner function formalism

> We give a brief overview of the kinetic theory for spin-1/2 fermions in Wigner function formulism. The chiral and spin kinetic equations can be derived from equations for Wigner functions. A general Wigner function has 16 components which satisfy 32 coupled equations. For massless fermions, the number of independent equations can be significantly reduced due to the decoupling of left-handed and right-handed particles. It can be proved that out of many components of Wigner functions and their coupled equations, only one kinetic equation for the distribution function is independent. This is called the disentanglement theorem for Wigner functions of chiral fermions. For massive fermions, it turns out that one particle distribution function and three spin distribution functions are independent and satisfy four kinetic equations. Various chiral and spin effects such as chiral magnetic and votical effects, the chiral seperation effect, spin polarization effects can be consistently described in the formalism.

## Key claim

For massless spin-1/2 fermions, the 16-component Wigner function and its 32 coupled equations can be reduced to a single independent kinetic equation for one distribution function (the disentanglement theorem), while for massive fermions, one particle distribution function and three spin distribution functions remain independent and satisfy four kinetic equations, but this reduction relies on a semiclassical expansion in powers of ℏ whose validity requires that the product of the spatial variation scale of the electromagnetic field and the momentum variation scale of the Wigner function be much larger than ℏ.

## Method

The authors review the Lorentz-covariant, gauge-invariant Wigner function formalism for spin-1/2 fermions in background Abelian gauge fields, expanding the 4×4 Wigner matrix in the 16 generators of the Clifford algebra. They perform a semiclassical expansion in powers of ℏ of the Wigner function components and of the operators Π^μ and G^μ (which contain the electromagnetic field via spherical Bessel functions of the triangle operator Δ = ∂_p·∂_x). For massless fermions, they show that the chiral Wigner functions J^μ_s decouple into two independent chiral sectors, and prove that among the four components of each chiral Wigner function only one is independent, with the others determined by it and the on-shell condition; for massive fermions, four independent functions (one scalar distribution and three spin distributions) survive.

## Key numbers

- A general Wigner function has 16 components which satisfy 32 coupled equations.
- For massless fermions, the number of independent equations is reduced such that only one kinetic equation for the distribution function is independent (the disentanglement theorem).
- For massive fermions, one particle distribution function and three spin distribution functions are independent and satisfy four kinetic equations.
- The semiclassical expansion is valid when ΔR_F ΔP_W ≫ ℏ, where ΔR_F is the spatial scale for variation of F^{μν}(x) and ΔP_W is the momentum scale for variation of the Wigner function.
- The operators Π^μ and G^μ are expanded to O(ℏ^2) as Π^μ = p^μ − (1/12)ℏ^2 Δ F^{μν}∂_ν^p and G^μ = ∂_x^μ − F^{μν}∂_ν^p + (1/24)ℏ^2 Δ^2 F^{μν}∂_ν^p.

## Context

This review extends the earlier covariant Wigner function formalism developed by Vasak, Gyulassy, and Elze (1987) and the subsequent works on Abelian and non-Abelian gauge fields from the 1980s–1990s, which had not been applied to realistic heavy-ion collision simulations due to the complexity of the coupled multicomponent equations. The disentanglement theorem enables a practical reduction to a single kinetic equation for massless fermions, making it possible to consistently describe chiral and spin effects such as the chiral magnetic effect, chiral vortical effect, chiral separation effect, and spin polarization effects in a unified framework, and opens the door to numerical simulations of chiral kinetic equations and the derivation of spin hydrodynamics from Wigner equations.
