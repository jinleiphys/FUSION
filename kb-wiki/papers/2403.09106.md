# Effects of neck and nuclear orientations on the mass drift in heavy ion collisions

> We clarified that the fusion hindrance in heavy ion collisions is caused by the expansion of the neck bridge at the early stage of collision [Phys. Rev. C 108, 014612 (2023)]; however, our discussion was limited to the trajectory analysis. To get a reliable fusion cross section, it is important to understand the fusion process connecting with multinucleon transfer and also the process depending on the target orientation in detail. Especially, the effects of target orientation on the multinucleon transfer process have not been discussed so far in our model. First, we investigate precisely the start time of the neck expansion relevant to the mass transfer. The main aims of this paper are to discuss the mass drift in the collision with the different target orientations within the dynamical approach in the reaction 32S + 232Th. The orientation effects are incorporated within the framework of the Langevin equation. The start time of the neck expansion was presumed to be 10 zs by analysis in several entrance channels. By taking account of the target nuclear orientation, a strong mass-angle correlation was obtained which is compatible with the experimental data. Not only ``delayed relaxation'' of the neck but also the nuclear orientation effects have an important role in the strong correlation between fragment mass and its emitting angle. The mass evolution toward mass symmetry is slower than the standard mass drift mode assuming an exponential-type function. Particularly, the mass drift of the tip collision follows the slow mass drift mode assuming a Fermi-type function rather than an exponential-type function, which is related to the different features of the maximum neck cross-sectional area in the sticking process.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the start time of the neck expansion in heavy ion collisions is 10 zs (zeptoseconds), and that the mass drift toward symmetry is slower than the standard exponential-type mass drift mode, with the tip collision specifically following a Fermi-type function rather than an exponential-type function, contingent on the target nuclear orientation.

## Method

The authors performed dynamical trajectory calculations using multidimensional Langevin equations with three nuclear deformation parameters (distance between centers, fragment deformation, and mass asymmetry) and the two-center shell model for the adiabatic potential energy. The orientation effects of the deformed 232Th target were incorporated by using axially-symmetric or axially-asymmetric diabatic potentials depending on the initial orientation angle, with a time-dependent restoration from asymmetric to symmetric states. The neck parameter relaxation was modeled with a Fermi-type function characterized by a start time t0 and a variance, and the calculations were compared to experimental mass-angle distributions for 30Si, 48Ti, and 86Kr projectiles on 208Pb targets to determine the appropriate t0 value.

## Key numbers

- The start time of the neck expansion was determined to be 10 zs (1 zs = 10^-21 s) from analysis of several entrance channels.
- For the 232Th target, the initial static deformation parameter delta_i was approximately 0.18, derived from a quadrupole deformation beta_2 of 0.207.
- The relaxation time for the transition from diabatic to adiabatic potential (tau_DA) was 0.1 zs, and the restoration time for axially-asymmetric to axially-symmetric states (tau_res) was 1 zs.
- The neck parameter relaxation variance Delta_epsilon was fixed at 0.1 zs.
- The shell damping energy E_d was selected as 20 MeV.
- The Bass barrier energies V_bass for 30Si, 48Ti, and 86Kr on 208Pb were 128.54 MeV, 195.18 MeV, and 302.63 MeV, respectively, with calculations performed at E_c.m./V_bass ratios of 1.05, 1.173, and 1.084.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Amano et al., 2023) which clarified that fusion hindrance is caused by neck bridge expansion but was limited to trajectory analysis. The paper addresses the previously unconsidered effects of target orientation on multinucleon transfer processes, building on the systematic analysis of mass-angle distributions by Toke et al. (1985) and the standard mass drift curve established in the 1980s for 238U-induced reactions. The findings enable more reliable fusion cross section calculations by connecting the fusion process with multinucleon transfer and orientation-dependent dynamics, and support the recent experimental observation by Tanaka et al. (2021) that mass evolution toward symmetry is slower than the standard mass drift curve for fast quasifission.
