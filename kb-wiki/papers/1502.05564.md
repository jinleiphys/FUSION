# Fission fragment mass yield deduced from density distribution in the pre-scission configuration

> Static self-consistent methods usually allow to determine the most probable fission fragments mass asymmetry. We have applied random neck rupture mechanism to the nuclei in the configuration at the end of fission paths. Fission fragment mass distributions have been deduced from the pre-scission nuclear density distribution obtained from the self-consistent calculations. Potential energy surfaces as well as nuclear shapes have been calculated in the fully microscopic theory, namely the constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogolubov model with the effective Gogny D1S density-dependent interaction. The method has been applied for analysis of fission of Fm-256,258, Cf-252 and Hg-180 and compared with the experimental data.

# Fission fragment mass yield deduced from density distribution in the pre-scission configuration

## Key claim
The authors show that applying a random neck rupture mechanism to pre-scission nuclear density distributions obtained from self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogolubov calculations can reproduce the most probable fragment masses for several fissioning nuclei, but the calculated mass distributions are systematically too narrow because dynamical effects and quantal properties of nucleons are omitted.

## Method
The constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogolubov model with the Gogny D1S effective interaction was used to calculate potential energy surfaces and nuclear shapes in the deformation space of quadrupole and octupole deformations. From the pre-scission configuration at the end of each fission path, the spatial density distribution was extracted and the random neck rupture mechanism of Brosa (1988, 1990) was applied: the probability of neck rupture at a given position along the symmetry axis depends exponentially on the cut energy (surface tension times neck cross-section) divided by the nuclear temperature, where the temperature is derived from the excitation energy at scission.

## Key numbers
- For $^{258}$Fm symmetric fission path: pre-scission point at quadrupole moment $Q_{20}=129$ b; neck contains 4 protons and 6 neutrons; most probable split yields two $^{129}$Sn isotopes.
- For $^{258}$Fm asymmetric fission path: pre-scission point at $Q_{20}=239$ b, $Q_{30}=67$ b$^{3/2}$; neck contains 8 protons and 12 neutrons; most probable fragments are $^{143}$Cs and $^{115}$Rh.
- For $^{256}$Fm asymmetric pre-scission configuration: $Q_{20}=230$ b, $Q_{30}=68$ b$^{3/2}$; neck contains 6 protons and 8 neutrons; nascent fragments close to $^{134}$Te and $^{108}$Mo.
- For $^{252}$Cf pre-scission configuration: $Q_{20}=217$ b, $Q_{30}=66.5$ b$^{3/2}$; nascent fragments close to $^{102}$Zr ($Q_{20}=5.5$ b) and $^{130}$Sn (spherical).
- For $^{180}$Hg pre-scission configuration: $Q_{20}=130$ b, $Q_{30}=30$ b$^{3/2}$; scission point excitation energy taken as maximal available energy of 10.44 MeV; most probable masses $A_H=100$ and $A_L=80$.
- For $^{258}$Fm symmetric path: density distance function $D_{\rho\rho'}$ is four times larger at $Q_2=130$ b compared to neighbouring deformations, indicating scission.

## Context
This work extends the random neck rupture model of Brosa (1983, 1988, 1990) by replacing the macroscopic liquid-drop density with microscopic density distributions from self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogolubov calculations using Gogny forces. It builds on prior microscopic studies of fission in these nuclei by Warda et al. (2012) and Warda and Robledo (2011), and on the explanation of asymmetric fission in $^{180}$Hg by Warda et al. (2012). The method reproduces the most probable fragment masses for $^{256}$Fm, $^{258}$Fm, $^{252}$Cf, and $^{180}$Hg but yields distributions that are too narrow compared to experiment, motivating future inclusion of dynamical effects (e.g., via Adiabatic Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogolubov as in Hashimoto and Goutte, 2005) and quantal treatment of neck nucleons.
