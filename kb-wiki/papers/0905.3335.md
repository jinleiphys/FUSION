# The role of superfluidity in nuclear incompressibilities

> Nuclei are propitious tools to investigate the role of the superfluidity in the compressibility of a Fermionic system. The centroid of the Giant Monopole Resonance (GMR) in Tin isotopes is predicted using a constrained Hartree-Fock Bogoliubov approach, ensuring a full self-consistent treatment. Superfluidity is found to favour the compressibitily of nuclei. Pairing correlations explain why doubly magic nuclei such as $^{208}$Pb are stiffer compared to open-shell nuclei. Fully self-consistent predictions of the GMR on an isotopic chain should be the way to microscopically extract both the incompressibility and the density dependence of a given energy functional. The macroscopic extraction of K$_{sym}$, the asymmetry incompressibility, is questioned. Investigations of the GMR in unstable nuclei are called for. Pairing gap dependence of the nuclear matter incompressibility should also be investigated.

# The role of superfluidity in nuclear incompressibilities

## Key claim
Superfluidity favors the compressibility of nuclei, with pairing correlations explaining why doubly magic nuclei such as 208Pb are stiffer compared to open-shell nuclei. The effect is sizable enough to change the extracted nuclear incompressibility by about 10 MeV, but the claim is hedged by the requirement that pairing must be treated with a full Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) approach rather than a simpler BCS treatment.

## Method
The centroid of the Giant Monopole Resonance (GMR) in Tin isotopes was predicted using a constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (CHFB) approach in coordinate space, ensuring full self-consistency. Skyrme functionals (SLy4 and SkM*) and a zero-range surface pairing interaction were used, with the pairing strength adjusted to reproduce the neutron pairing gap trend in Tin isotopes. The GMR centroid was obtained from the ratio of the m1 sumrule (from the Thouless double commutator) to the m-1 sumrule (from the derivative of the monopole operator under constraint).

## Key numbers
- The GMR centroid in Tin isotopes is located at approximately 16 MeV.
- Pairing effects decrease the predicted GMR centroid by a few hundred keV, which changes the extracted nuclear incompressibility KA by about 10 MeV.
- Using the SLy4 functional (K∞ = 230 MeV, which describes 208Pb GMR well) without pairing overestimates the Sn GMR data; pairing brings predictions closer to experiment.
- The SkM* functional (K∞ = 215 MeV) with pairing effects gives better agreement with the Sn data.
- Non-relativistic fully self-consistent QRPA calculations on Sn isotopes lead to K∞ ≈ 215 MeV.
- 208Pb is stiffer than Sn, Zr, and Sm nuclei by about 20 MeV in K∞, both in non-relativistic and relativistic approaches.
- Pairing effects induce approximately 10 MeV change on the nuclear incompressibility KA in the Sn isotopic chain.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies by Civitarese et al. (1991) which found a negligible effect of superfluidity on nuclear incompressibility but used a non-self-consistent approach. It confirms and extends the QRPA results of Li, Colo, and Meng (2008) showing that pairing decreases the GMR centroid. The paper addresses the puzzle that 208Pb appears stiffer than Sn, Zr, and Sm nuclei, questioning whether the question should be "why Tin are so soft?" (Piekarewicz, 2007) or rather "why 208Pb is so stiff?". It calls for GMR measurements in unstable nuclei such as doubly magic 132Sn, and for extending the macroscopic extraction of Ksym to include pairing effects. The results also suggest that equations of state used for neutron star and supernovae predictions should take pairing into account when providing incompressibility values.
