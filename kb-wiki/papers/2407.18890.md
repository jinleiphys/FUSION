# Bulk viscosity of nuclear matter with pions in the neutrino-trapped regime

> Recent work [B. Fore and S. Reddy, Phys. Rev. C 101 035809 (2020)] has shown that the population of thermal pions could modify the equation of state and transport properties of hot and dense neutron-rich matter and introduce new reaction pathways to change the proton fraction. In this article we study their impact on the bulk viscosity of dense matter, focusing on the neutrino-trapped regime that would be realized in neutron star mergers and supernovae. We find that the presence of a thermal population of pions alters the bulk viscosity by modifying the EoS (via the susceptibilities) and by providing new reaction pathways to achieve beta-equilibrium. In neutron star merger conditions, the bulk viscosity in neutrino-trapped $npeμ$ matter (without pions) has its peak at temperatures of at most a couple MeV and is quite small at temperatures of tens of MeV. We find that thermal pions enhance the low-temperature peak of the bulk viscosity by a factor of a few and shift it to slightly lower temperatures. At higher temperatures, where the pion abundance is large but the bulk viscosity is traditionally small, pions can increase the bulk viscosity by an order of magnitude or more, although it is still orders of magnitude smaller than its peak value.

## Key claim

Thermal pions enhance the bulk viscosity of neutrino-trapped nuclear matter by a factor of a few at the low-temperature peak and by an order of magnitude or more at higher temperatures, although the high-temperature bulk viscosity remains orders of magnitude smaller than its peak value. This result depends on the specific lepton fractions and densities characteristic of neutron star mergers versus supernovae, with the enhancement being more pronounced in merger conditions.

## Method

The authors extended the formalism for bulk viscosity in neutrino-trapped nuclear matter from the three-species case (npe) to matter containing neutrons, protons, electrons, muons, and a thermal population of negatively charged pions (npeμπ⁻). They used a nonrelativistic Skyrme model (NRAPR) for the nucleon sector and a pion self-energy constructed from phase shifts with two fitted parameters (α₀=1, α₁=0.23596) to model pion-nucleon interactions. The bulk viscosity was calculated by solving the coupled rate equations for three independent equilibrating chemical potentials (δμ₁, δμ₂, δμ₃) under small-amplitude density oscillations, with weak interaction rates computed via full phase space integration of Fermi's golden rule without degeneracy approximations.

## Key numbers

- The pion "mass" [Eπ⁻(p=0)] is shifted from its vacuum value of 139.6 MeV to 177.4 MeV at nuclear saturation density n₀ and to 194.0 MeV at 1.6 n₀ (temperature 1 MeV)
- The fitting parameter α₁ for the pion-nucleon pseudopotential was fixed at 0.23596 for all thermodynamic conditions
- In neutron star merger conditions, the conserved electron-type lepton fraction YLe ranges from 0.04 to 0.10 and the muon-type YLμ ranges from 0.01 to 0.07 (from post-processing analysis of simulations)
- Supernovae simulations predict YLe between 0.3 and 0.4
- The study focused on two representative configurations: {YLe, YLμ} = {0.05, 0} for mergers and {0.3, 0} for supernovae
- At temperatures above 1 MeV, the direct Urca process dominates over modified Urca in neutrino-trapped matter
- The strong interaction process n ↔ p + π⁻ (with spectator) is taken as infinitely fast (λ₃ → ∞) for T ≳ 1 MeV

## Context

This work extends the formalism of bulk viscosity in neutrino-trapped nuclear matter developed by Alford et al. (2017, 2019, 2021, 2023) and Harris et al. (2020, 2024) to include thermal pions, building on the earlier demonstration by Fore and Reddy (2020) that thermal pions modify the equation of state and transport properties of hot neutron-rich matter. The results enable more accurate modeling of bulk viscous damping in neutron star merger simulations, where pions were previously neglected, and complement recent studies of bulk viscosity in matter with hyperons (Alford et al., 2020) and quark matter (Alford et al., 2024; Cruz Rojas et al., 2024; Hernandez et al., 2024).
