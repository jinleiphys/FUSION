# A boost to low energy nuclear reactions from preliminary elastic collisions (Carambole collisions)

> In conventional nuclear experiments a beam of accelerated nuclei collides with a target nucleus that is surrounded by other nuclei in a molecule, in condensed matter, or in a plasma environment. It is shown that for low collision energies possible nuclear reactions (including deuterium fusion) are strongly boosted by the environment. The effect originates from a chain of preliminary three elastic collisions which transform the projectile-target experiment into one with colliding beams. Firstly, the projectile-target pair of nuclei undergo elastic scattering in which the projectile shares its energy and momentum with the target nucleus. Then the projectile and target nuclei collide with different heavy nuclei from the environment. These latter collisions change the velocities of the target and projectile nuclei and set them again on the collision course. Finally, the same pair of nuclei collide inelastically, this time giving rise to the nuclear reaction. The increased energy of the target nucleus increases the relative velocity up to sqrt 2 times, resulting in a drastic exponential increase of the probability to penetrate the Coulomb barrier and therefore sharply increasing the likelihood of the nuclear reaction. Applications to laser-induced fusion are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that low-energy nuclear reactions (including deuterium fusion) in condensed matter, molecules, or plasma environments can be strongly boosted by a chain of three preliminary elastic collisions (TEC) between the projectile, target, and two heavy environmental nuclei, which transforms a beam-target experiment into an effective colliding-beams configuration. The authors hedge that the effect applies when the projectile energy is lower than the Coulomb barrier and when the target nucleus is surrounded by heavy environmental nuclei, with the maximum effect achieved when the target deuterium is located at the center of a square formed by heavy atoms.

## Method

The authors develop a semiclassical wave function for the projectile-target pair that accounts for three sequential elastic collisions: first, elastic scattering between projectile and target shares energy and momentum; then the projectile and target each scatter elastically off different heavy environmental nuclei (atoms A and B), changing their directions without significant energy loss; finally, the same pair collides inelastically with increased relative velocity. The probability of the nuclear reaction is calculated by integrating the squared amplitude over final momenta, using the derived wave function and the standard exponential dependence of Coulomb barrier penetration on relative velocity. The ratio of the TEC-initiated reaction probability to the direct collision probability is expressed as a pre-exponential factor times an exponential enhancement factor.

## Key numbers

- The maximum possible relative velocity for two deuterons after TEC is \(v_m = \sqrt{2}\, v_1\), where \(v_1\) is the initial projectile velocity.
- The condition for the theory's applicability requires \(\pi \xi \gg 1\), where \(\xi = (e^2 Z_1 Z_2 / \hbar) \cdot (v_1' v_2' / v_t^3)\); for deuteron-deuteron collisions this gives \(E_1 \le 15\) keV.
- The pre-exponential enhancement factor includes a term \(k \simeq r_{AB}^2 / a^2 \simeq 100\), where \(a\) is the localization length of the target nucleus (estimated as \(\sim 10\%\) of atomic separation).
- The coefficient \({\cal K}\) scales as \({\cal K} \propto r_{AB}^{-4} \propto n^{4/3}\), where \(n\) is the density of the condensed matter environment.
- The energy condition for straight-line propagation between collisions requires \(p_1^2 / (2m_1) \gg Z_p \cdot Z_t \cdot 27\) eV.

## Context

This work extends earlier discussions of enhanced deuterium-deuterium fusion in solids (references cited as "a few publications" and Ichimaru) and the role of target vibrations stimulated by Coulomb interaction (Kuchiev, Altshuler, Flambaum, and others, cited as AFKZ). The TEC mechanism is distinct from simple vibrational enhancement because it exploits elastic rescattering off heavy environmental nuclei to redirect the projectile and target onto a head-on collision course. The paper enables future calculations of fusion rates in specific condensed matter or molecular geometries and discusses applications to laser-induced fusion, with the maximum effect predicted when the target deuterium sits at the center of a square of heavy atoms and the projectile beam is parallel to the square's surface.
