# Hydrodynamic Description of the Quark-Gluon Plasma

> We review the history and success of applying relativistic hydrodynamics to high-energy heavy-ion collisions. We emphasize the important role hydrodynamics has played in the discovery of the quark-gluon plasma and its quantitative exploration.

# Hydrodynamic Description of the Quark-Gluon Plasma

## Key claim
Relativistic hydrodynamics has been remarkably successful in describing not only large heavy-ion collision systems but also much smaller systems such as p+p and p+Pb collisions, despite the unresolved question of how such small and short-lived fireballs could thermalize fast enough for fluid dynamics to become applicable. The authors present this as a "seemingly unreasonable success" that triggered increased theoretical interest in understanding the conditions under which hydrodynamics is applicable.

## Method
The paper reviews the historical development of relativistic hydrodynamic modeling of heavy-ion collisions, from Landau's 1953 framework through modern 3+1D viscous hydrodynamic simulations with event-by-event initial state fluctuations. The authors describe how contemporary models split the collision evolution into three stages: an initial far-off-equilibrium "hydrodynamization" stage described microscopically, a collective expansion stage where dissipative fluid dynamics applies, and a late dilute hadronic rescattering and freeze-out stage simulated via hadronic cascade. The review covers numerical implementations (finite volume methods, smoothed particle hydrodynamics), initial state models (Glauber, Color Glass Condensate, IP-Glasma, EKRT), and extensions including anisotropic hydrodynamics, spin hydrodynamics, and magneto-hydrodynamics.

## Key numbers
- The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio, eta/s, could not be much larger than a few times the lower bound of 1/(4pi), derived at strong coupling in 2001 by Policastro, Son, and Starinets, or the elliptic flow would be underestimated too much.
- The elliptic flow coefficient v2 measured by STAR in Au+Au collisions at RHIC showed absolute values just slightly lower than expectations from ideal hydrodynamics.
- At strong coupling, low-order hydrodynamic constitutive relations become applicable after a time of order of the inverse temperature, with the term "hydrodynamization" coined by Casalderrey-Solana and collaborators (2011) to distinguish applicability of viscous hydrodynamic relations from local thermalization.
- The bottom-up thermalization picture by Baier and collaborators (2000) describes thermalization proceeding in three stages from an initially over-occupied and momentum-anisotropic gluon-dominated state called glasma.

## Context
This review extends and synthesizes the line of work from Landau (1953) through Bjorken (1982), the development of viscous relativistic hydrodynamics by Muller, Israel, and Stewart (1960s-1970s), and the discovery of nearly perfect fluidity at RHIC in the early 2000s. It enables future studies of collectivity in small systems (p+p, p+Pb, d+Au, 3He+Au collisions), connections between flow observables and nuclear structure (including uranium deformation studies by STAR in 2015), and searches for the QCD critical point using fluctuating hydrodynamic simulations. The paper also discusses open questions about the applicability of hydrodynamics in far-off-equilibrium regimes and the role of hydrodynamic attractors explored by Heller and collaborators (2015) and reviewed by Florkowski and collaborators (2017).
