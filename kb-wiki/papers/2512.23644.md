# Recent advancements in the strongly coupled many-body theory for nuclear spectral computation

> Some recent advancements of the nuclear many-body theory and selected results on nuclear giant and pygmy resonances are presented. The theory is compactly reviewed, with a special focus on the emergent scale of the quasiparticle-vibration coupling (qPVC), which carries the order parameter associated with the qPVC vertex, and an efficient treatment of the nuclear many-body problem organized around the qPVC hierarchy. Self-consistent numerical solutions of the relativistic Bethe-Salpeter-Dyson equation for the nuclear response function in medium-heavy nuclei are discussed. The presented update on the pygmy dipole resonance focuses on establishing the formation of its two-component structure as a result of the fragmentation of the low-energy dipole mode due to the qPVC and its mixing with the similarly fragmented giant dipole resonance. The centroid of the isoscalar giant monopole resonance is also linked to qPVC effects, particularly to its sensitivity to the coupling of the collective breathing mode to the lowest quadrupole vibrations, which is enhanced by quadrupole collectivity. The resolution of the long-standing "fluffiness" puzzle regarding the compressibility of open-shell tin isotopes is summarized. The recently developed thermal variant of the superfluid response theory is briefly introduced and continuously linked to the description of the isoscalar monopole response at finite temperature with the prospect of refining the temperature-dependent nuclear equation of state.

## Key claim

The quasiparticle-vibration coupling (qPVC) introduces a new emergent order parameter that dominates the power counting in the strongly coupled nuclear many-body problem, enabling a converging hierarchy of approximations to nucleonic correlation functions, but this hierarchy must be extended beyond the leading order to achieve spectroscopic accuracy.

## Method

The author reviews the relativistic nuclear field theory (RNFT) framework, which solves the relativistic Bethe-Salpeter-Dyson equation for the nuclear response function using self-consistent numerical implementations. The theory organizes configuration complexity through the qPVC hierarchy, progressing from relativistic quasiparticle random phase approximation (RQRPA/REOM^1) through 2q⊗phonon (REOM^2) to 2q⊗2phonon (REOM^3) configurations, with the latter implemented for medium-heavy nuclei including spherical and moderately deformed species. A thermal variant of the superfluid response theory was developed by deriving the equation of motion for the thermally averaged two-point two-fermion correlation function in the Bogoliubov quasiparticle basis with a 4×4 block matrix structure.

## Key numbers

- The isoscalar giant monopole resonance (ISGMR) centroid shifts due to qPVC effects can amount to 1-2 MeV, more pronounced in softer mid-shell nuclei
- The nuclear compression modulus K∞ = 258 for the NL3* effective meson-nucleon interaction parameter set
- The pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) appears as an E1 strength enhancement grouped around 5-7 MeV in heavy nuclei
- The isovector giant dipole resonance (IVGDR) is located at 15-20 MeV
- The critical temperature Tc for vanishing superfluidity lies in the interval 0 ≤ Tc ≤ 1 MeV for the finite-temperature calculations
- The 2q⊗2phonon (6q) configurations in REOM^3 were successfully applied to the dipole response of tin isotopes

## Context

This work extends the relativistic nuclear field theory (RNFT) developed by Litvinova, Ring, and Tselyaev (2007, 2008) and connects to the non-relativistic nuclear field theory (NFT) of Bohr, Mottelson, and Broglia (1969, 1975, 1976) and the quasiparticle-phonon model (QPM) of Soloviev (1992). The resolution of the "fluffiness" puzzle regarding open-shell tin isotope compressibility builds on prior work by Litvinova (2023) and enables systematic studies of temperature-dependent nuclear equation of state. The finite-temperature formalism developed by Bhattacharjee (2024) extends earlier thermal response work by Sommermann (1983) and enables astrophysical applications to beta decay and electron capture rates.
