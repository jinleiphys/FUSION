# What evidences the mass distribution in halo nucleus?

> Detailed analysis of the distribution of nuclear matter in the halo nucleus $^{11}$Be is performed within the microscopic approach based on the flexible variational basis of the functions of the "polarized" orbitals model. It is shown that the proposed approach reproduces, in agreement with the experiment, the most distinguishing features of the mass distribution in this system. Taking into account the results of specific calculations and the exceptionally high reliability of the experimental data on the nuclear density distribution in the "tail" of $^{11}$Be, we make a conclusion about the existence of new structures with the sizes considerably exceeding the size of the nucleus (up to tens of $fm$) and the density being thousands times less that the typical nuclear one.

## Key claim

The microscopic variational calculation reproduces the experimental nuclear matter density of \(^{11}\)Be, particularly the magnitude and slope of the halo tail, and reveals that the system consists of two distinct physical subsystems: the compact core nucleus \(^{10}\)Be and a separate, extended structure of intermediate (between atomic and nuclear) nature, which is a consequence of the Pauli principle acting on all 11 nucleons.

## Method

The author solves the many-particle Schrödinger equation for \(^{11}\)Be using the "polarized orbitals" model, a variational approach in which each single-particle orbital is a harmonic-oscillator function with its own independent variational parameters \((a_i, b_i, c_i)\) for the three spatial directions. The effective central exchange NN-potential is taken as a superposition of five Gaussian terms, and the total wave function is an antisymmetrized product of these orbitals, projected onto the \(J^\pi = \frac{1}{2}^+\) ground state via the Hill-Wheeler integral. The nuclear matter density is then computed by averaging the one-particle spatial density operator with the optimized variational wave function.

## Key numbers

* Root-mean-square radius of nuclear matter in \(^{11}\)Be: \(R^m_{rms} = (2.90 \pm 0.05)\) fm (from prior experiments cited as Ref. [9,10]).
* Root-mean-square radius of the core nucleus \(^{10}\)Be: \(R^m_{rms} = (2.30 \pm 0.02)\) fm (from prior experiments cited as Ref. [9,10]).
* Root-mean-square radius of the valence nucleon: \(R^m_{rms} \sim 7\) fm (from prior work cited as Ref. [11]).
* Diameter of the halo tail: \(D_{halo} \approx 30\) fm (from prior work cited as Ref. [11]).
* Variational parameter for the valence neutron along the \(z\)-axis: \(c_4^n = 5.38\) fm (from Table 1), indicating anomalous spreading.
* Density of the three structures observed in the valence neutron distribution: \((2-5) \times 10^{-5}\) fm\(^{-3}\).
* Energy difference between the ground state and the first excited \(\frac{1}{2}^-\) state: \(\delta E = 0.32\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the microscopic "polarized orbitals" model previously developed by the author (Steshenko, Ref. [14]) to the specific case of the one-neutron halo nucleus \(^{11}\)Be. It directly confronts the experimental data from the RIKEN collaboration (Ref. [8]), which provided highly reliable measurements of the nuclear density in the halo tail. The paper argues that the cluster model (core plus one valence neutron) is too phenomenological and cannot explain the origin of the long-range interaction, whereas the present microscopic approach treats all nucleons equally and reveals that the halo structure is a consequence of the Pauli principle, not simply a weakly-bound valence neutron orbiting a core. This interpretation suggests that halo nuclei of the first kind represent a new class of structures in microphysics, with sizes up to tens of fm and densities thousands of times lower than typical nuclear density.
