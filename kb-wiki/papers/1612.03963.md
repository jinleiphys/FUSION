# Trends and Progress in Nuclear and Hadron Physics: a straight or winding road

> Quantitative calculations of the properties of hadrons and nuclei, with assessed uncertainties, have emerged as competitive with experimental measurements in a number of major cases. We may well be entering an era where theoretical predictions are critical for experimental progress. Cross-fertilization between the fields of relativistic hadronic structure and non-relativistic nuclear structure is readily apparent. Non-perturbative renormalization methods such as Similarity Renormalization Group and Okubo-Lee-Suzuki schemes as well as many-body methods such as Coupled Cluster, Configuration Interaction and Lattice Simulation methods are now employed and advancing in both major areas of physics. New algorithms to apply these approaches on supercomputers are shared among these areas of physics. The roads to success have intertwined with each community taking the lead at various times in the recent past. I briefly sketch these fascinating paths and comment on some symbiotic relationships. I also overview some recent results from the Hamiltonian Basis Light-Front Quantization approach.

## Key claim

Quantitative calculations of hadron and nuclear properties with assessed uncertainties have become competitive with experiment, and the paper suggests we may be entering an era where theoretical predictions are critical for experimental progress, but this claim is hedged by the recognition that the field is still developing methods and that success depends on continued cross-fertilization between relativistic hadronic structure and non-relativistic nuclear structure communities.

## Method

The paper reviews and presents results from the Hamiltonian Basis Light-Front Quantization (BLFQ) approach, which solves the light-front Hamiltonian eigenvalue problem using a two-dimensional harmonic oscillator basis for transverse modes and discretized plane waves for longitudinal modes. Applications include solving QED problems at strong coupling (electron anomalous magnetic moment, positronium spectrum), the Yukawa model with scalar boson exchange, heavy quarkonium spectroscopy with an effective Hamiltonian incorporating confinement and one-gluon exchange, vector meson production in the dipole model, and time-dependent BLFQ (tBLFQ) for electron evolution in strong external electromagnetic fields.

## Key numbers

- Yukawa model ground state mass extrapolated to continuum limit: 1.99933 m_f (fermion mass units), compared to non-relativistic result of 1.99954 m_f, for scalar boson mass m_b = 0.15 m_f, coupling α = 0.3, HO length parameter b = 0.16 m_f
- Charmonium calculations used coupling constant α = 0.3595, confining strength κ = 0.963 GeV, quark mass m_q = 1.492 GeV, gluon mass μ_g = 0.02 GeV
- Saturation scale extracted from Color Glass Condensate simulations: Q^2/g^2μ = 0.32, with lattice size g^2μ = 1.0 GeV, g^2μ L = 100, N = 128 grid points
- tBLFQ transition rate calculations used N_max = 32, K = 32, L = 10 GeV^{-1}, b = 1000 m_e, with initial state energy P^-_{β,i} = 0.455 GeV and final state energy P^-_{β,f} = 0.955 GeV
- For gold nucleus (Z=79) coupling α = 79/137, tBLFQ simulation showed approximately 35% probability of electron excitation to higher longitudinal momentum after evolving 50 GeV^{-1} (roughly 10 fm/c)

## Context

This paper extends the long tradition of non-perturbative many-body methods in nuclear and hadronic physics, building on the pioneering works of Brueckner, Bethe and Goldstone, the Okubo-Lee-Suzuki (OLS) scheme reviewed by Barrett (2013), the Similarity Renormalization Group (SRG) of Glazek and Wegner, and the Coupled-Cluster method of Coester and Kuemmel. The BLFQ approach specifically extends the Discretized Light Cone Quantization (DLCQ) method reviewed by Brodsky (1998) and incorporates techniques from the no-core shell model (NCSM) used in nuclear many-body theory. The results enable future applications to baryons, multi-baryon systems, and real-time evolution of colored objects in heavy-ion collisions within the Color Glass Condensate framework.
