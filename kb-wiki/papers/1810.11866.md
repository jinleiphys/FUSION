# Why do Nilsson quantum numbers remain good at moderate deformations?

> The Nilsson model is a simple microscopic model which has been extensively used over the years for the interpretation of a bulk of experimental results. The single particle orbitals in this model are labeled by quantum numbers which are good in the limit of large nuclear deformations. However, it is generally admitted that these quantum numbers remain good even at moderate deformations. We show that this fact is due to the existence of an underlying approximate symmetry, called the proxy-SU(3) symmetry. The implications of proxy-SU(3) on various aspects of nuclear structure will be discussed.

# Why do Nilsson quantum numbers remain good at moderate deformations?

## Key claim

The Nilsson quantum numbers $K[N n_z \Lambda]$, which are mathematically justified only in the asymptotic limit of large nuclear deformations, remain useful at moderate and even small deformations because an approximate proxy-SU(3) symmetry underlies nuclear shells above 28 nucleons, effectively restoring the SU(3) symmetry of the three-dimensional harmonic oscillator that is otherwise broken by the spin-orbit interaction.

## Method

The authors demonstrate that Nilsson orbitals differing by $\Delta K[\Delta N \Delta n_z \Delta\Lambda] = 0[110]$ possess very large spatial overlaps, as these orbitals share the same angular momentum and spin projections while differing by one quantum along the symmetry axis. They show that replacing each orbital invading a harmonic oscillator shell from above (due to spin-orbit splitting) by its 0[110] counterpart among the orbitals that deserted that shell to the one below approximately restores SU(3) symmetry, with the proxy-SU(3) shell accommodating two fewer nucleons than the original nuclear shell.

## Key numbers

- The proxy-SU(3) shell can accommodate two nucleons less than the original nuclear shell.
- For 10 particles in the U(15) proxy-SU(3) sdg shell, the highest weight irrep is (20,4); for 10 holes (20 particles), the highest weight irrep is (20,0), not the conjugate irrep (4,20) that particle-hole symmetry would predict.
- For 100 nucleons within the proxy-SU(3) pfh shell (starting at 82 nucleons, U(21) symmetry), the highest weight irrep is (36,6); for the 3D harmonic oscillator shell (70-112, U(21) symmetry), the irrep is (30,0).
- For 120 nucleons within the proxy-SU(3) pfh shell (starting at 82, U(21), $N_q=5$), the highest weight irrep is (2,16); for the 3D harmonic oscillator shell (112-168, U(28), $N_q=6$), the irrep is (34,4).
- Regions where 3D harmonic oscillator predictions for $\sqrt{C_2(\lambda,\mu)+3}$ (proportional to deformation $\beta$) fall below proxy-SU(3) predictions are 34-42, 60-72, 96-116, and 146-172 nucleons; the relevant subregions bounded by harmonic oscillator magic numbers are 34-40, 60-70, 96-112, and 146-168.
- Regions where the 3D harmonic oscillator predicts $\gamma \geq 30^\circ$ (oblate shapes) while proxy-SU(3) predicts $\gamma < 30^\circ$ (prolate shapes) are 34-40, 64-70, 104-112, and 158-168.

## Context

This work extends the proxy-SU(3) symmetry framework introduced by the same group (Bonatsos et al., 2017) to explain why Nilsson quantum numbers remain valid away from their asymptotic limit, a fact long observed empirically in the literature (Casten, 1990). The paper builds on the earlier recognition by Cakirli et al. (2005, 2006) that 0[110] pairs of orbitals produce maximal proton-neutron interaction, and on the original Elliott SU(3) model (Elliott, 1958) which is valid only up to the sd shell (20 nucleons). The results enable a group-theoretical understanding of shape coexistence regions on the nuclear chart, particularly near the magic numbers 82 and 126, and provide a framework for comparing competing irreps from the proxy-SU(3) scheme and the three-dimensional harmonic oscillator to predict deformation parameters and shape transitions.
