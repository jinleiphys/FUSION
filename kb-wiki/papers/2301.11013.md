# Comprehensive test of nuclear level density models

> For the last two decades, experimental information on nuclear level densities for about 60 different nuclei has been obtained on the basis of the Oslo method. While each of these measurements has been typically compared to one or a few level density models, a global study including all the measurements has been missing. The present study provides a systematic comparison between Oslo data and six global level density models for 42 nuclei for which $s$-wave resonance spacings are also available. We apply a coherent normalization procedure to the Oslo data for each of the six different models, all being treated on the same footing. Our quantitative analysis shows that the constant-temperature model presents the best global description of the Oslo data, closely followed by the mean-field plus combinatorial model and Hartree-Fock plus statistical model. Their accuracies are quite similar, so that it remains difficult to clearly favour one of these models. When considering energies above the threshold where the experimental level scheme is complete, all the six models are shown to lead to rather similar accuracies with respect to Oslo data. The recently proposed shape method can, in principle, improve the situation since it provides an absolute estimate of the excitation-energy dependence of the measured level densities. We show for the specific case of $^{112}$Cd that the shape method could exclude the Hartree-Fock plus statistical model. Such an analysis remains to be performed for the bulk of data for which the shape method can be applied to the Oslo measurements before drawing conclusions on the general quality of a given nuclear level density model.

## Key claim

The constant-temperature model provides the best global description of Oslo-method nuclear level density data for 42 nuclei, closely followed by the mean-field plus combinatorial model and the Hartree-Fock plus statistical model, but their accuracies are so similar that it remains difficult to clearly favour one model; the shape method applied to a single nucleus (¹¹²Cd) could exclude the Hartree-Fock plus statistical model, but such analysis remains to be performed for the bulk of data.

## Method

The authors performed a systematic comparison between Oslo-method nuclear level density data and six global level density models (constant-temperature, back-shifted Fermi gas, generalized superfluid, Skyrme-Hartree-Fock plus statistical, Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov plus combinatorial, and temperature-dependent Gogny-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov plus combinatorial) for 42 nuclei where s-wave resonance spacings are also available. A coherent normalization procedure was applied to the Oslo data for each model, adjusting the level density to reproduce low-lying discrete levels and the experimental s-wave resonance spacing D₀, then renormalizing the Oslo data to the model prediction at the highest excitation energies to minimize the root-mean-square deviation. The shape method was additionally applied to ¹¹²Cd to provide a model-free estimate of the excitation-energy dependence of the level density.

## Key numbers

* The constant-temperature model gave a global rms deviation σ = 1.45 when including all Oslo data points for 42 nuclei, corresponding to a global ratio of about 45% around the mean value.
* The Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov plus combinatorial model gave σ = 1.47 for all data points, and the Hartree-Fock plus statistical model gave σ = 1.53.
* For energies above the completeness threshold E_lls, the constant-temperature model gave σ = 1.21, while all six models gave σ between 1.21 and 1.34.
* For ¹⁰⁶Pd, the renormalized Oslo level densities at the highest excitation energies differed by a factor of 4.7 between the largest (Hartree-Fock plus statistical) and lowest (constant-temperature) predictions.
* The energy difference ΔE = B_n − E_exp^max between the neutron separation energy and the highest measured data point lies mainly between 1 and 2 MeV, but reaches up to 8 MeV for ⁴⁴Ti.
* For ¹¹²Cd, the f_rms deviations for the six models ranged from 1.04 (constant-temperature) to 1.09 (back-shifted Fermi gas and Hartree-Fock plus statistical).

## Context

This work provides the first systematic global comparison of Oslo-method nuclear level density data against six publicly available level density models, extending prior studies that typically compared measurements to only one or a few models. The analysis builds on the Oslo method developed by Guttormsen (1987, 1996) and Schiller (2000), and uses the shape method proposed by Wiedeking (2021) and applied by Mücher (2022) to provide model-free normalization. The results enable future work to apply the shape method to the full set of Oslo data to discriminate between models, and to improve nuclear reaction calculations for astrophysics and applications that rely on accurate level density predictions far from stability.
