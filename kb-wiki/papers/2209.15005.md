# Non-Gaussian Fluctuation Dynamics

> Recent progress of a general deterministic approach to the non-Gaussian fluctuation dynamics is reviewed, with an emphasis on the derivation of the fluctuation evolution equations and their phenomenological implication in heavy-ion collision experiments.

## Key claim
The paper reviews a general deterministic approach to non-Gaussian fluctuation dynamics that goes beyond equilibrium theory, showing that non-equilibrium effects from critical fluctuations can shift the expected non-monotonic energy dependence of higher-order cumulants toward lower collision energies compared to equilibrium predictions.

## Method
The author derives evolution equations for cumulants of stochastic fields from the Fokker-Planck equation using a systematic truncation scheme based on two small parameters: epsilon (inversely proportional to uncorrelated degrees of freedom, controlling loop expansion) and delta (the Knudsen number, controlling gradient expansion). These equations are then applied to the specific problem of diffusive charge evolution near the QCD critical point, using a multi-point Wigner transform to represent cumulants in wave-vector space, with the correlation length serving as a UV cutoff.

## Key numbers
- The power counting assigns: drift force F_i ~ delta^2, Onsager matrix Q_ij ~ delta^2 epsilon, cumulants G^c_n ~ epsilon^(n-1)
- In the critical regime of Model H: alpha^(n) ~ xi^((n-5)/2), lambda^(n) ~ xi^((n+2)/2), gamma^(n) ~ xi^((n-2)/2), W^c_n ~ xi^((5n-6)/2), partial_t W^c_n ~ q^2 xi^((5n-8)/2)
- Numerical simulations use q = 0.1 and q = 0.3 (arbitrary units) to demonstrate non-equilibrium effects on cumulant contours
- Equilibrium solutions: W_2^eq = 1/alpha', W_3^eq = -alpha''/alpha'^3, W_4^c,eq = (3alpha''^2 - alpha'alpha''')/alpha'^5

## Context
This work extends the equilibrium critical point analysis of Stephanov (2008, 2011) by incorporating non-equilibrium effects of non-Gaussian fluctuations, motivated by the need to interpret results from the RHIC Beam Energy Scan program. The formalism is part of the BEST framework and builds on prior work by An (2021, 2022b) on fluctuation evolution equations. The numerical results are qualitatively consistent with experimental data reported in PhysRevLett.126.092301, but the author cautions that quantitative comparison requires more realistic implementations including freeze-out prescriptions.
