# From supernovae to neutron stars: crust formation time

> A neutron star is born as a hot, lepton-rich protoneutron star (PNS) and cools via neutrino emission, eventually allowing heavy ions in the outer layers to crystallize into a solid crust. We develop a simple analytic estimate for the onset time of this crust formation during the late, post-convective PNS cooling phase. Using a diffusion-based neutrino luminosity and the resulting entropy evolution together with an approximately isentropic interior structure, we obtain the time-dependent density and temperature at the neutrinosphere. We then impose the Coulomb crystallization condition for heavy nuclei, expressed through the Coulomb coupling parameter, and determine when the neutrinosphere temperature first falls below the crystallization threshold evaluated at the neutrinosphere density. This procedure yields closed expressions for the entropy at crystallization and the corresponding crust-formation time, with explicit dependence on the PNS mass and radius, an effective diffusion/cooling normalization, and composition parameters such as the ionic charge $Z$ and heavy-nuclei mass fraction. For canonical microphysics, we find that the first solid phase typically appears at $t_{\mathrm{crust}}\sim 100$-$500\,\mathrm{s}$. These closed-form scalings provide a useful late-time analytic benchmark for the onset of crust formation and clarify its dependence on PNS and composition parameters.

# From supernovae to neutron stars: crust formation time

## Key claim
The first solid crust appears at the neutrinosphere when its temperature falls below the Coulomb crystallization threshold, yielding a typical onset time of 100-500 seconds for canonical parameters. This analytic estimate is derived by extrapolating diffusion-based cooling formulae beyond their calibration interval, so the results should be regarded as useful late-time benchmarks rather than model-specific predictions for individual protoneutron stars.

## Method
The authors combine a diffusion-based analytic solution for neutrino luminosity and entropy evolution (calibrated against detailed cooling calculations up to several times 10 seconds) with an approximately isentropic interior structure and an isothermal outer atmosphere. They impose the Coulomb crystallization condition for heavy nuclei at the neutrinosphere density, expressed through the Coulomb coupling parameter Gamma, and solve for the time when the neutrinosphere temperature first drops below the crystallization threshold. This yields closed-form expressions for the entropy at crystallization and the corresponding crust-formation time as functions of protoneutron star mass and radius, an effective diffusion/cooling normalization, and composition parameters.

- Neutrinosphere density at crystallization: rho_nu = 4.1 x 10^13 g cm^-3 (M_PNS/1.4 M_sun)^(3/10) (R_PNS/10 km)^(-6/5) (g beta/3)^(-3/10) (s/1 k_B/baryon)^(-3/5).
- Neutrinosphere temperature: T_nu = 3.6 x 10^10 K (M_PNS/1.4 M_sun)^(1/5) (R_PNS/10 km)^(-4/5) (g beta/3)^(-1/5) (s/1 k_B/baryon)^(3/5).
- Critical temperature at neutrinosphere density: T_c(rho_nu) = 6.5 x 10^9 K (Gamma/175)^(-1) (x_a/0.3)^(1/3) (Z/40)^(5/3) (M_PNS/1.4 M_sun)^(1/10) (R_PNS/10 km)^(-2/5) (g beta/3)^(-1/10) (s/1 k_B/baryon)^(-1/5).
- Entropy at crystallization: s_crust = 0.12 k_B/baryon (M_PNS/1.4 M_sun)^(-1/8) (R_PNS/10 km)^(1/2) (g beta/3)^(1/8) (Gamma/175)^(-5/4) (x_a/0.3)^(5/12) (Z/40)^(25/12).
- Representative crust-formation times from model parameters: t_crust ~ 100-440 s for the models listed in Suwa (2021) assuming Gamma=175, x_a=0.3, and Z=40.

## Context
This work extends the analytic cooling model of Suwa (2021) to predict crust formation times, complementing fully self-consistent numerical simulations with neutrino radiation transport (Pons et al. 1999; Fischer et al. 2010; Hudepohl et al. 2010; Suwa et al. 2014; Nakazato et al. 2018) that are computationally expensive for broad parameter surveys. The analytic scalings clarify which macroscopic parameters control the result and allow rapid exploration across plausible protoneutron star configurations. The crust onset time provides a useful characteristic transition for later magnetic-field evolution, as the outer layers change from fluid-like to solid and highly conducting, though the paper does not attempt a quantitative model of magnetic-field relaxation.
