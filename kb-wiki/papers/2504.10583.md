# A $ν$ look at the Sun: Probing the conditions of the solar core using $^8$B neutrinos

> In the coming age of precision neutrino physics, neutrinos from the Sun become robust probes of the conditions of the solar core. Here, we focus on $^8$B neutrinos, for which there are already high precision measurements by the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory and Super-Kamiokande. Using only basic physical principles and straightforward statistical tools, we estimate projected constraints on the temperature and density of the $^8$B neutrino production zone compared to a reference solar model. We outline how to better understand the astrophysics of the solar interior using forthcoming neutrino data and solar models. Finally, we note that detailed forward modeling will be needed to develop the full potential of this approach.

## Key claim

Using only basic physical principles and straightforward statistical tools, the authors estimate projected constraints on the temperature and density of the $^8$B neutrino production zone relative to a reference solar model, noting that detailed forward modeling will be needed to develop the full potential of this approach and that several approximations are made in estimating these projected constraints.

## Method

The authors calculate how the spectrum of scattered electrons in Super-Kamiokande depends on $^8$B neutrino production, propagation via the MSW effect, and detection via neutrino-electron elastic scattering. They characterize the $^8$B neutrino production rate profile using a power-law dependence on local temperature, $\Gamma(r) \propto T(r)^\beta$, and average the electron neutrino survival probability over this production zone. The total $^8$B flux measured by SNO is used to normalize expectations, and the recoil electron spectrum is computed including energy resolution effects from Super-K-IV.

## Key numbers

- Total $^8$B neutrino flux measured by SNO: $(5.25 \pm 0.20) \times 10^6 \text{ cm}^{-2} \text{ s}^{-1}$
- The total $^8$B neutrino flux scales as $\phi \propto T_\textrm{c}^{\alpha}$ with $\alpha \approx 24$ (and $\alpha \approx 24 \pm 5$ across contemporary solar models)
- The local temperature exponent for the production rate profile is $\beta \approx 14$ from the temperature dependence alone, with $\beta \approx 27$ needed to match the full calculation
- The MSW mixing parameters used: $\tan^2(2\theta_{12}) = 0.436^{+0.029}_{-0.025}$, $\Delta m^2_{21} = (7.53 \pm 0.18) \times 10^{-5}$ eV$^2$, $\sin^2(\theta_{13}) = 0.022 \pm 0.001$
- The $^8$B neutrino spectrum has a peak near 6.5 MeV and an endpoint near 15 MeV
- The solar core temperature is approximately $k_\mathrm{B} T \approx 1.3$ keV
- SNO has determined the neutral current total $^8$B neutrino flux to better than 4%, which probes the solar core temperature to 0.2% in the absence of other uncertainties (taking into account laboratory and solar modeling uncertainties, the temperature uncertainty is more like 1%)

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Balantekin (1997), Lopes (2013), and Laber-Smith (2022, 2024) that probed solar density using neutrino mixing, but develops the first joint probes in the density-temperature plane. The approach aims to help resolve persistent discrepancies in solar helioseismology and metallicity data, and is enabled by forthcoming experiments including JUNO (starting operations in 2025), Hyper-Kamiokande, and DUNE, which will improve neutrino mixing parameter precision to better than 1% and allow solar neutrino observations to return to probing solar astrophysics rather than neutrino properties.
