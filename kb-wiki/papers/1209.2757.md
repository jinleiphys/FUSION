# Existence and stability of multiple solutions to the gap equation

> We argue by way of examples that, as a nonlinear integral equation, the gap equation can and does possess many physically distinct solutions for the dressed-quark propagator. The examples are drawn from a class that is successful in describing a broad range of hadron physics observables. We apply the homotopy continuation method to each of our four exemplars and thereby find all solutions that exist within the interesting domains of light current-quark masses and interaction strengths; and simultaneously provide an explanation of the nature and number of the solutions, many of which may be associated with dynamical chiral symmetry breaking. Introducing a stability criterion based on the scalar and pseudoscalar susceptibilities we demonstrate, however, that for any nonzero current-quark mass only the regular Nambu solution of the gap equation is stable against perturbations. This guarantees that the existence of multiple solutions to the gap equation cannot complicate the description of phenomena in hadron physics.

## Key claim
The gap equation of QCD possesses many physically distinct solutions for the dressed-quark propagator, but for any nonzero current-quark mass only the regular Nambu solution is stable against perturbations when judged by a criterion based on scalar and pseudoscalar susceptibilities, guaranteeing that multiple solutions do not complicate hadron phenomenology.

## Method
The authors applied the homotopy continuation method, novel to Dyson-Schwinger equation studies, to solve the gap equation with four different kernel Ansatze combining two interaction models (Maris-Tandy and Qin-Chang) with two vertex Ansatze (rainbow-ladder and a truncated Ball-Chiu form). They charted the complete solution set domains as functions of current-quark mass and interaction strength, and assessed stability by solving the inhomogeneous scalar and pseudoscalar Bethe-Salpeter equations to determine whether the lowest mass excitation in each channel has positive mass-squared.

## Key numbers
- For the Maris-Tandy interaction in rainbow-ladder truncation, the domain of reasonable interaction strengths is \(3 \lesssim \mathcal{I} \lesssim 13\) where \(\mathcal{I} = D/\omega^2\); for the Qin-Chang interaction, the domain is \(2 \lesssim \mathcal{I} \lesssim 8\).
- The renormalisation point used throughout is \(\zeta = 19\) GeV.
- The infrared interaction parameters used are \(\omega = 0.4\) GeV for the Maris-Tandy model and \(\omega = 0.5\) GeV for the Qin-Chang model, with \(D\omega = (0.72\ \text{GeV})^3\) and \(D\omega = (0.8\ \text{GeV})^3\) respectively.
- The perturbative-QCD running coupling uses \(\Lambda_{\text{QCD}} = 0.234\) GeV, \(N_f = 4\), and \(m_t = 0.5\) GeV.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies that established multiple solutions to the gap equation (Zong 2004, Chang 2006, Williams 2006) by applying the homotopy continuation method to find all solutions systematically across four realistic kernels. It introduces a stability criterion based on simultaneous positivity of scalar and pseudoscalar susceptibilities, which generalises earlier work using only the chiral susceptibility (Yuan 2006, Zhao 2008, Chang 2008, Qin 2010). The results enable unambiguous selection of the correct solution for computing hadron observables via Bethe-Salpeter equations and clarify that the existence of multiple solutions does not affect phenomenological descriptions.
