# Freeze out of the expanding system

> The freeze out (FO) of the expanding systems, created in relativistic heavy ion collisions, is discussed. We start with kinetic FO model, which realizes complete physical FO in a layer of given thickness, and then combine our gradual FO equations with Bjorken type system expansion into a unified model. We shall see that the basic FO features, pointed out in the earlier works, are not smeared out by the expansion.

## Key claim

The basic features of post-freeze-out distributions found in earlier gradual freeze-out models (particularly the non-thermal, asymmetric shape at low momentum) survive when the system undergoes Bjorken-like expansion during the freeze-out process, provided the freeze-out layer thickness exceeds approximately twice the mean free path.

## Method

The authors combine a kinetic freeze-out model based on the Boltzmann Transport Equation with a Bjorken-type one-dimensional expansion of the system. The freeze-out layer is defined between two parallel hypersurfaces at proper times τ₁ and τ₁+L, where the interacting component of the distribution function evolves via coupled differential equations that include both escape probability and rethermalization terms, solved in the fast rethermalization limit. The model treats the fireball in two phases: pure Bjorken hydrodynamics from τ₀ to τ₁, followed by simultaneous Bjorken expansion and gradual freeze-out from τ₁ to τ₁+L.

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature T_i(τ₀=0.05 fm) = 835 MeV; freeze-out start temperature T_i(τ₁=5 fm) = T_FO = 180 MeV
- Mean free path parameter τ_FO = 0.5 fm
- For freeze-out layer thickness L > 2τ_FO (i.e., L > 1.0 fm), the final post-FO distribution approaches that of an infinitely long freeze-out calculation
- The interacting component energy density evolves as e^i(τ) = e₀(τ₀/τ)^(1+cₒ²) × ((L+τ₁-τ)/L)^(L/τ_FO)

## Context

This work extends earlier gradual freeze-out models by Csernai, Magas, and Molnar (2005-2006) that treated freeze-out in a static layer without expansion, and contrasts with the two extreme approaches used in the field: freeze-out on a zero-thickness hypersurface and volume emission models requiring infinite time and space. The results justify the use of a freeze-out hypersurface in hydrodynamic models for heavy ion collisions, provided the post-freeze-out distributions are taken as non-thermal (asymmetric) rather than thermal, with parameters determined from conservation laws and a volume scaling factor to account for expansion during freeze-out.
