# Neutral $s$ States in Loosely Bound Nuclei

> In reviewing the data that has accumulated in light nuclei we find that the binding energy plays a critical role in describing the variation in energy of $s$ states relative to other states. The behavior of states with zero angular momentum within a few MeV of threshold is qualitatively different from that of neutron states with any other $\ell$ value or of any proton state. This observation is explored for simple Woods-Saxon potentials and is remarkably successful in describing a wealth of experimental data for nuclei with neutron numbers between 5 and 10. The lingering of neutron $s$ states just below threshold is associated with the increases in radii of the neutron density distributions, the neutron halos, and leads to speculations about possible halos in heavier nuclei.

# Neutral s States in Loosely Bound Nuclei

## Key claim

The binding energy plays a critical role in describing the variation in energy of neutron s states relative to other states, and the qualitatively different behavior of neutron s states near threshold (within a few MeV of zero binding) can be largely explained by simple geometric finite-binding effects in a Woods-Saxon potential, without invoking more complex nuclear structure mechanisms such as the tensor force.

## Method

The authors compiled experimental data on the 1/2+ and 5/2+ single-neutron excitations for nuclei with neutron numbers between 5 and 10, spanning isotopes from He to O. They calculated single-particle energies in a Woods-Saxon potential with standard radial parameters, adjusting the central well depth to fit the experimental 5/2+ energies and then predicting the 1/2+ energies in the same potential. The difference between the experimental and calculated 1/2+-5/2+ separations was examined as a function of proton number to isolate residual effects beyond the geometric finite-binding description.

## Key numbers

- The 5/2+ excitation energies range from being bound by approximately 4 MeV to unbound by approximately 3.5 MeV across the surveyed nuclei.
- The same increment in potential depth that increases binding of the 1/2- and 5/2+ states from 0 to 1 MeV causes a change in the s state that is almost an order of magnitude less.
- For cases where the 1/2+ state was near threshold, the calculated energy difference varied by as much as 0.6 MeV between different Woods-Saxon parameter sets (for 9He, 9Be, and 13Be).
- In 9He, the 1/2+ resonance energy is taken as 0.18 (+0.50/-0.18) MeV, with the 5/2+ state at 3.42 +/- 0.78 MeV, giving an experimental energy difference of -3.24 (+0.93/-0.80) MeV.
- In 17O, the 1/2+ state is at -3.27 MeV and the 5/2+ state at -4.14 MeV, giving an energy difference of 0.87 MeV, which matches the Woods-Saxon calculation exactly (0.87 MeV).

## Context

This work extends earlier observations by Talmi and Unna (1960) on the intrusion of the 1s1/2 orbital into the 0p-shell, and by Fortune (1995) on anomalous Coulomb displacement energies for 1/2+ states. The authors show that finite-binding effects in a simple Woods-Saxon potential explain the pattern of level ordering more successfully than the tensor force component emphasized by Otsuka (2005), though the tensor force may contribute at the level of a few hundred keV. The results enable speculation about possible neutron halos in heavier nuclei near N=50 (around 78Ni) and beyond N=126 (around 204Pt or 202Os), where the 2s1/2 or 3s1/2 neutron states may similarly linger near threshold.
