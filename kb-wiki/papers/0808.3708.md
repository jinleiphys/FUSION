# Collectivity-induced quenching of signatures for shell closures

> Mass differences are an often used as signature and measure for shell closure. Using the angular-momentum projected Generator Coordinate Method and the Skyrme interaction SLy4, we analyze the modification of mass differences due to static deformation and dynamic fluctuations around the mean-field ground state.

## Key claim

The quenching of two-nucleon separation energies at shell closures, often interpreted as a reduction of spherical single-particle gaps far from stability, is instead primarily caused by static deformation and dynamic quadrupole correlations that modify the binding energies of the nuclei used to compute the separation energies, not by a genuine reduction of the underlying spherical shell gaps.

## Method

The authors performed systematic calculations of binding energies for even-even nuclei using the angular-momentum projected Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) with the Skyrme energy density functional SLy4 and a density-dependent zero-range pairing interaction. They compared results at three levels of approximation: spherical mean-field states, deformed mean-field minima, and the full projected GCM ground states that include both rotational and vibrational correlations through particle-number and angular-momentum projection followed by configuration mixing over axial quadrupole moments.

## Key numbers

- The spherical mean-field single-particle gap at N=50 decreases from 6.1 MeV in 100Sn to 4.4 MeV in 78Ni, representing a genuine shell quenching due to surface effects on neutron levels.
- Deformation gains up to 2 MeV in binding energy for some Cd (Z=48) and Te (Z=52) isotopes, which increases S2p for Z=50 and decreases it for Z=52.
- The gaps at magic numbers in two-nucleon separation energies are reduced to about half their spherical mean-field values when static deformation and dynamical correlations are included.
- The LN pairing prescription shifts single-particle states away from the Fermi level by at most 200 to 300 keV, insufficient to explain the difference between single-particle levels and -S2p/2.
- Errors of the order of 200 to 300 keV in the calculated trends would not affect the conclusions.

## Context

This work extends the systematic mass calculations of Bender et al. (2005, 2006) by analyzing how beyond-mean-field correlations affect the interpretation of shell closures from mass differences. It contradicts the common assumption that jumps in two-nucleon separation energies directly reflect spherical single-particle gaps, and instead shows that collectivity-induced quenching dominates. The results enable a more careful interpretation of experimental mass measurements, particularly for exotic nuclei where recent data from Hakala et al. (2008) and the AME2003 compilation provide extended isotopic chains. The study also addresses the ongoing debate about whether single-particle energies should be used in fitting energy density functionals, as proposed by Zalewski et al. (2008).
