# The legacy of the experimental hadron physics programme at COSY

> The experimental hadronic physics programme at the COoler SYnchrotron of the Forschungszentrum Juelich terminated at the end of 2014. After describing the accelerator and the associated facilities, a review is presented of the major achievements in the field realized over the twenty years of intense research activity.

## Key claim

This review paper summarizes the major achievements of the hadronic physics program at the COoler SYnchrotron (COSY) over twenty years of operation, but the author explicitly notes that the selection of the ten most influential experiments is "necessarily a very speculative choice" and that the review presents only "the phenomenology required to understand the experimental results at a rather basic level," omitting detailed discussion of systematic uncertainties and theoretical motivation.

## Method

The author, a single reviewer, synthesized results from the several hundred research papers produced by the COSY experimental collaborations (COSY-11, ANKE, WASA, TOF, Big Karl, GEM, MOMO) between approximately 1994 and 2014. The review describes the accelerator facility, its cooling systems (electron cooling up to 600 MeV/c, stochastic cooling from 1.5 to 3.3 GeV/c), and the six principal detector installations, then evaluates experimental results organized by reaction type, including near-threshold meson production, strangeness production, and few-body reactions.

## Key numbers

- COSY maximum circulating beam momentum: 3700 MeV/c for protons and deuterons; maximum extracted proton beam momentum achieved: 3300 MeV/c
- Electron cooling effective up to momenta of 600 MeV/c; stochastic cooling covers 1.5 to 3.3 GeV/c
- Momentum resolution achieved: Δp/p = 10^{-3} to 10^{-5}
- Space charge limit on stored particles: about 2×10^{11}; practical maximum using stacking injection: 6×10^{10}
- COSY ring circumference: approximately 184 m; straight sections: 40 m each; arcs: 52 m each
- Big Karl spectrometer maximum proton momentum: 1080 MeV/c in 3Q2DQ mode; extended to 3240 MeV/c in 3QD mode
- Big Karl momentum acceptance: ±4.5%; momentum resolution: Δp/p ≈ 10^{-4}
- WASA forward detector kinetic energy resolution for protons: σ(T)/T ∼ 1.5-3% for T < 400 MeV
- WASA central calorimeter energy resolution: σ(E)/E ≈ 5%/√(E[GeV])
- WASA pellet target density: on the order of 10^{15} atoms/cm²; pellet diameters: approximately 30 μm
- TOF detector residual pressure: less than 7×10^{-4} mbar
- COSY-11 flight path: approximately 9.4 m between start and stop scintillators
- ANKE forward detector angular acceptance: about 12° horizontal, 3.5° vertical
- ANKE positive detector momentum range: 0.3 to 0.8 GeV/c; forward detector: 0.8 to 3.7 GeV/c
- GEM detector opening angle: about 16.5°; central hole angular size: 1.6°

## Context

This review extends the legacy of the SATURNE program at Saclay (described in a 1998 multi-author volume) by covering the complementary COSY program, which operated as a storage ring with internal targets rather than using external magnetic spectrometers like SATURNE. The paper enables future work by documenting the technical foundations for COSY's ongoing precision physics program, including searches for electric dipole moments of the deuteron and proton and the TRIC experiment on time-reversal violation in proton-deuteron collisions. The author notes that the hadronic physics program terminated at the end of 2014, with the facility shifting toward precision measurements more aligned with current particle physics priorities.
