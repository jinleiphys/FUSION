# Confronting LHC data with the statistical hadronization model

> The most recent data from the CERN LHC are compared with calculations within the statistical hadronization model. The parameters temperature und baryon chemical potential are fitted to the data. The best fit yields a temperature of 156 MeV, slightly below the expectation from RHIC data. Proton yields are nearly three standard deviations below this fit and possible reasons are discussed.

# Confronting LHC data with the statistical hadronization model

## Key claim
The statistical hadronization model fits most ALICE hadron yield data with a temperature of 156 MeV and zero baryon chemical potential, but proton and antiproton yields fall 2.7–2.9 standard deviations below the fit, constituting a "proton anomaly" that currently has no convincing explanation.

## Method
The authors performed a grand-canonical statistical hadronization model fit to published and preliminary ALICE hadron yield data from Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC, using the known hadron spectrum from the Particle Data Group (2008 compilation). The fit minimized chi-squared to extract temperature T and baryon chemical potential μ_b, with the K^0* meson excluded from the fit due to its sensitivity to post-freeze-out modifications.

## Key numbers
- Best-fit temperature: T = 156 MeV with μ_b = 0 MeV and reduced χ² = 2.4
- Proton yield 18.0% below the model (2.7 sigma deviation); antiproton yield 19.4% below (2.9 sigma deviation)
- A fit excluding protons yields T = 158 MeV with reduced χ² < 1
- A model calculation at T = 164 MeV and μ_b = 1 MeV increases antiproton disagreement to about 50%
- Previous fit to preliminary data gave T = 152 MeV with reduced χ² = 4.3
- Predicted chemical freeze-out parameters for LHC from earlier work (Stachel et al., 2013, citing their own prediction): μ_b = 0.8^{+1.2}_{-0.6} MeV and T = 161 ± 4 MeV

## Context
This work extends the statistical hadronization model comparisons previously applied to AGS, SPS, and RHIC data (summarized in a 2010 compilation by the same group), where agreement was very good across all energies. The LHC results confirm the expected leveling-off of temperature near 160 MeV seen at top RHIC energies, but the proton anomaly, already hinted at in RHIC data with larger uncertainties, now reaches nearly 3 sigma significance. The paper discusses two candidate explanations: incomplete hadron spectrum (following Hagedorn resonance gas studies by the authors) and annihilation in the hadronic phase (modeled with UrQMD by Bass, Becattini, and others), but notes that detailed balance arguments from Shuryak and from Cassing (2004) and Pratt (2013) weaken the annihilation case, and that the centrality dependence of the proton-to-pion ratio shows opposite trends at RHIC and LHC. The work enables future tests with higher-precision LHC data and with yields of light (anti-)nuclei, which are well reproduced by the standard model but overpredicted by a factor of 6 in an out-of-equilibrium extension proposed by Rafelski.
