# Fusion rate enhancement due to energy spread of colliding nuclei

> Experimental results for sub-barrier nuclear fusion reactions show cross section enhancements with respect to bare nuclei which are generally larger than those expected according to electron screening calculations. We point out that energy spread of target or projectile nuclei is a mechanism which generally provides fusion enhancement. We present a general formula for calculating the enhancement factor and we provide quantitative estimate for effects due to thermal motion, vibrations inside atomic, molecular or crystal system, and due to finite beam energy width. All these effects are marginal at the energies which are presently measurable, however they have to be considered in future experiments at still lower energies. This study allows to exclude several effects as possible explanation of the observed anomalous fusion enhancements, which remain a mistery.

## Key claim

Energy spread of target or projectile nuclei (due to thermal motion, molecular or crystal vibrations, or finite beam energy width) is a mechanism that generally provides fusion enhancement, but all these effects are marginal at presently measurable energies and cannot explain the observed anomalous fusion enhancements that exceed the adiabatic electron screening limit.

## Method

The authors derive a general formula for the enhancement factor f = ⟨σv_rel⟩/(σ(V)V) by expanding the fusion cross section in powers of the target velocity relative to the projectile velocity, assuming the sudden approximation holds (projectile velocity much larger than target velocity). They apply this formalism to calculate enhancement factors for thermal motion, nuclear vibrations inside atoms and molecules, local vibrations in crystal lattices, and finite beam energy width and straggling, using the exponential dependence of the sub-barrier fusion cross section on relative velocity.

## Key numbers

- For d+d collisions at E=1 keV and room temperature: f-1 ≃ 10⁻⁴
- For d+d collisions at E=1 keV, a 10% enhancement would require kT ≃ 30 eV
- For nuclear motion inside the atom (d+d at E=1 keV): f_at - 1 ≃ 2·10⁻⁵
- For molecular vibrations in D₂ (E_vib=0.19 eV) at E=1 keV: f_mol - 1 ≃ 10⁻⁴
- For LUNA beam energy width Δ≃10 eV at E=1 keV: f-1 ≃ 2·10⁻⁵
- A 10% enhancement from beam width would require Δ ≃ 250 eV
- For polynomial velocity distributions (n=3) to give f₀≃1.1 at E=1 keV: ⟨v²⟩ ≃ 3·10⁻² V₀², corresponding to average energy ~1 keV

## Context

This work addresses the longstanding puzzle of anomalous fusion enhancements observed in sub-barrier reactions, where experimental screening potentials U_ex significantly exceed the adiabatic limit U_ad (e.g., U_ex ≃ 700 eV for deuterium in metals versus expected atomic values). The authors show that energy spread effects, including thermal motion, molecular vibrations (as in D₂ molecules), crystal lattice vibrations, and beam energy width, are all too small to explain the anomalies, which remain unexplained. The study provides a framework for correcting future low-energy experiments and extends earlier work on electron screening by Rolfs (1987), Bracci (1990), and others, while ruling out several proposed explanations for the anomalous enhancements.
