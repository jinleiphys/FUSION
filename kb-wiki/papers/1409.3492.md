# Large low-energy $M1$ strength for $^{56,57}$Fe within the nuclear shell model

> A strong enhancement at low $γ$-ray energies has recently been discovered in the $γ$-ray strength function of $^{56,57}$Fe. In this work, we have for the first time obtained theoretical $γ$ decay spectra for states up to $\approx 8$ MeV in excitation for $^{56,57}$Fe. We find large $B(M1)$ values for low $γ$-ray energies that provide an explanation for the experimental observations. The role of mixed $E2$ transitions for the low-energy enhancement is addressed theoretically for the first time, and it is found that they contribute a rather small fraction. Our calculations clearly show that the high-$\ell$ ($=f$) diagonal terms are most important for the strong low-energy $M1$ transitions. As such types of $0\hbarω$ transitions are expected for all nuclei, our results indicate that a low-energy $M1$ enhancement should be present throughout the nuclear chart. This could have far-reaching consequences for our understanding of the $M1$ strength function at high excitation energies, with profound implications for astrophysical reaction rates.

## Key claim

The paper claims that large $B(M1)$ values at low $\gamma$-ray energies, arising from high-$\ell$ ($=f$) diagonal $0\hbar\omega$ transitions within the nuclear shell model, explain the experimentally observed low-energy enhancement in the $\gamma$-ray strength function of $^{56,57}$Fe; the authors hedge that this mechanism is expected to be present throughout the nuclear chart, but the calculations are limited to positive-parity states up to about 8 MeV excitation and do not include negative-parity states or the shears mode.

## Method

Large-basis shell-model calculations using the GPFX1A Hamiltonian for the $pf$ shell were performed for $^{56}$Fe and $^{57}$Fe, with model spaces including up to 6,046,562 states for $^{56}$Fe and 233,793 negative-parity states for $^{57}$Fe. The complete set of $M1$ and $E2$ matrix elements was calculated for positive-parity states up to about 7.5 MeV excitation, using effective charges of $e_p=1.5$ and $e_n=0.5$ for $E2$ and the effective $M1$ operator of Ref. [effM1]. The calculated transitions were sorted into 200-keV wide energy bins for both initial excitation energy and transition energy to produce average $B(M1)$ matrices and $M1$ strength functions.

## Key numbers

- For $^{56}$Fe, the model space includes a total of 6,046,562 states, with 255 positive-parity states up to 7.5 MeV excitation.
- For $^{57}$Fe, the model space includes 233,793 negative-parity states with truncation to $t=0$ and 1.
- The average contribution from $E2$ transitions is about 22% for $^{56}$Fe and about 15% for $^{57}$Fe.
- The $M1$ strength function for $^{56}$Fe is within about 10% of the larger basis result when using a more restrictive truncation that reduces the level density by a factor of two.
- The shapes of the $\gamma$SF from different excitation-energy regions agree within typically 10% for each 200-keV bin for $E_\gamma<3$ MeV.
- Stretched ($\Delta J=1$) $M1$ transitions give about 30% stronger $B(M1)$ values than non-stretched ($\Delta J=0$) transitions for $^{56}$Fe.

## Context

This work extends the experimental findings of Voinov (2004) and Larsen (2013) on the low-energy enhancement in $^{56,57}$Fe by providing the first theoretical explanation via large-basis shell-model calculations. It contradicts traditional $E1$ models such as those of Kopecky (1990) and Goriely (2002), which predicted decreasing strength at low $\gamma$-ray energies. The mechanism differs from the shears mode suggested by Schwengner (2013) for Mo isotopes and instead identifies high-$\ell$ diagonal $0\hbar\omega$ $M1$ transitions as the origin, which the authors argue should be present in all nuclei and could significantly increase astrophysical ($n,\gamma$) reaction rates for the r-process.
