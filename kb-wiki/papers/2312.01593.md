# Impacts of hexadecapole deformations on the collective energy spectra of axially deformed nuclei

> The hexadecapole deformation, as well as the quadrupole one, influences the low-lying states of finite nuclei. The hexadecapole correlations are often overshadowed by the large quadrupole effects, and hence have not been much investigated. Here we address the relevance of hexadecapole deformations in the calculations of low-energy collective states of heavy nuclei, by using the theoretical framework of the self-consistent mean-field method and the interacting-boson approximation. The interacting-boson Hamiltonian that explicitly includes the quadrupole and hexadecapole collective degrees of freedom is specified by a choice of the energy density functional and pairing interaction. In an illustrative application to axially deformed Gd isotopes, it is shown that the inclusion of the hexadecapole degree of freedom does not affect most of the low-spin and low-lying states qualitatively, but that has notable effects in that it significantly improves the description of high-spin states of the ground-state bands of nearly spherical vibrational nuclei and gives rise to $K^π=4^+$ bands exhibiting strong $E4$ transitions in strongly deformed nuclei.

## Key claim
The inclusion of hexadecapole (g boson) degrees of freedom in the interacting boson model does not qualitatively affect most low-spin and low-lying states, but it significantly improves the description of high-spin states (I ≥ 6⁺) in ground-state bands of nearly spherical vibrational nuclei and produces K^π = 4⁺ bands with strong E4 transitions in strongly deformed nuclei, provided the Hamiltonian parameters are determined from self-consistent mean-field calculations rather than fitted to experiment.

## Method
The authors performed multidimensionally constrained relativistic mean-field (MDC-RMF) calculations using the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov method with the DD-PC1 energy density functional and a separable pairing force of finite range to obtain quadrupole-hexadecapole (β₂-β₄) energy surfaces for ¹⁴⁸–¹⁶⁰Gd isotopes. These SCMF energy surfaces were then mapped onto the energy surfaces of an sdg-interacting boson model (IBM) Hamiltonian containing s, d, and g bosons, using the coherent-state method of Nomura (2008). The mapped Hamiltonian parameters (ε_d, ε_g, κ, χ) were determined without any adjustment to experimental data, and the resulting spectra were compared with those from the standard sd-IBM (without g bosons) and with experimental data.

## Key numbers
- Global minima of SCMF energy surfaces occur at (β₂^min, β₄^min) ≈ (−0.05, 0), (0.15, 0.05), (0.2, 0.1), (0.3, 0.15), (0.3, 0.15), (0.35, 0.15), and (0.35, 0.15) for ¹⁴⁸–¹⁶⁰Gd, respectively.
- Calculated R₄/₂ = E_x(4⁺₁)/E_x(2⁺₁) ratios from sdg-IBM (sd-IBM) are: 1.86 (2.13), 2.15 (2.18), 2.38 (2.36), 2.84 (2.83), 2.99 (3.08), 3.10 (3.24), 3.16 (3.23) for ¹⁴⁸–¹⁶⁰Gd; experimental values are 1.81, 2.02, 2.19, 3.02, 3.24, 3.26, 3.32.
- For ¹⁴⁸Gd, the sdg-IBM reproduces the experimental R₄/₂ = 1.81 (below 2), which the sd-IBM cannot achieve (gives 2.13).
- For ¹⁴⁸Gd, states with I^π = 4⁺₁, 6⁺₁, 8⁺₁, 10⁺₁ have ⟨n̂_g⟩ ≈ 1, and 12⁺₁, 14⁺₁ have ⟨n̂_g⟩ ≈ 1.5.
- For ¹⁵⁴Gd, the B(E4; 4⁺_{K=4⁺} → 0⁺₁) transition is 93 W.u. in sdg-IBM versus 1.3 W.u. in sd-IBM; the experimental B(E4; 4⁺₁ → 0⁺₁) used to fix charges is 38 ± 3 W.u.
- The experimental bandhead energy of the 4⁺₄ state in ¹⁵⁴Gd is 1646 keV.

## Context
This work extends the mapping procedure of Nomura (2008) from the sd-IBM to the sdg-IBM, incorporating hexadecapole degrees of freedom that have been studied phenomenologically by Van Isacker (2010) and others. It builds on earlier SCMF calculations of hexadecapole deformations in rare-earth nuclei by Nazarewicz (1981) and Kumar and Robledo (2023) using Gogny forces. The results enable systematic identification of regions across the nuclear chart where hexadecapole correlations play prominent roles, including experimentally unexplored isotopes, and may inform predictions for neutrinoless double-beta decay matrix elements as noted by Engel (2017).
