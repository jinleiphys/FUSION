# Power Counting Energy Flow Polynomials

> Power counting is a systematic strategy for organizing collider observables and their associated theoretical calculations. In this paper, we use power counting to characterize a class of jet substructure observables called energy flow polynomials (EFPs). EFPs provide an overcomplete linear basis for infrared-and-collinear safe jet observables, but it is known that in practice, a small subset of EFPs is often sufficient for specific jet analysis tasks. By applying power counting arguments, we obtain linear relationships between EFPs that hold for quark and gluon jets to a specific order in the power counting. We test these relations in the parton shower generator Pythia, finding excellent agreement. Power counting allows us to truncate the basis of EFPs without affecting performance, which we corroborate through a study of quark-gluon tagging and regression.

## Key claim

Power counting arguments yield linear relationships between energy flow polynomials (EFPs) that hold for quark and gluon jets to a specific order in the power counting, enabling truncation of the EFP basis without affecting machine learning performance in quark-gluon tagging and regression tasks, though the numerical accuracy of these relations depends on the chosen power counting scheme.

## Method

The authors applied power counting to derive linear relations between EFPs under three different approximations: a strongly-ordered expansion (assuming emissions are strongly ordered in both energy and angle), a 1-collinear expansion (one hard parton plus collinear-soft emissions without strong ordering), and a 2-collinear expansion (two collinear partons plus collinear-soft emissions). They constructed reduced EFP bases up to degree 6 for each scheme, then tested the accuracy of these relations using Pythia 8.226 parton shower simulations of quark and gluon jets from Z-boson associated production at √s = 14 TeV, with anti-kT jets of radius R=0.4 and pTjet ∈ [500,550] GeV.

## Key numbers

* The full set of EFPs includes 1, 3, 8, 23, 66, and 212 prime EFPs at degrees 0 through 6 respectively (cumulative 314 up to degree 6)
* The strongly-ordered basis reduces to 1, 2, 4, 7, 12, and 22 elements by degree (cumulative 49 up to degree 6)
* The 1-collinear basis contains 1, 2, 4, 8, 17, and 37 elements by degree (cumulative 70 up to degree 6)
* The 2-collinear basis requires 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, and 34 elements by degree (cumulative 66 up to degree 6)
* The z²-truncated basis contains 1, 3, 5, 9, 13, and 20 elements by degree (cumulative 52 up to degree 6)
* Jets were selected with pTjet ∈ [500,550] GeV and rapidity |yjet| < 1.7, using angular exponent β=2

## Context

This work extends the pioneering power counting approach of Larkoski (2014) for jet substructure observables to the systematic organization of energy flow polynomials, which were introduced by Komiske (2017) as an overcomplete linear basis for infrared-and-collinear-safe jet observables. The reduced bases enable more efficient machine learning applications of EFPs, which have been used in studies by Komiske (2018), Kasieczka (2019), and others. The approach is complementary to truncation by angular degree alone and provides a first-principles justification for basis reduction that was previously done empirically.
