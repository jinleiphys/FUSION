# On the Theory of Collisions between Atoms and Electrically Charged Particles

> In the fall of 1924, Enrico Fermi visited Paul Ehrenfest at Leyden on a 3-month fellowship from the International Education Board (IEB). Fermi was 23 years old. In his trip report to the IEB, Fermi says he learned a lot about cryogenics and worked on two scientific papers, including the following one. It was submitted in German to Zeitschrift fur Physik. The German version was known to Weizsacker and Williams and cited in the papers (10 years) later in which they extended Fermi's method to the Ultra-Relativistic case. The German version was subsequently translated into a Russian version and perhaps other languages. Fermi's Italian version (printed in Nuovo Cimento) is less widely known and does not appear in the ``Collected Works''. Nevertheless, Persico remarks that this was one of Fermi's favorite ideas and that he often used it in later life. So, we would like to think of this as a late 100th birthday present to the Italian Navigator. We would like to thank Professor T.D. Lee for his encouragement of this project and for interesting discussions about Fermi. Also Tom Rosenblum at the Rockefeller Archives for bringing Fermi's correspondence to our attention and Bonnie Sherwood for typing the original manuscript.

## Key claim

Fermi proposes that the excitation and ionization of atoms by charged particles can be quantitatively derived from the known optical absorption coefficients of the atoms, by treating the time-varying electric field of the passing particle as equivalent to a continuous spectrum of light, with the crucial condition that Fourier components with quantum energy exceeding the particle's kinetic energy are excluded.

## Method

Fermi decomposes the electric field of a charged particle passing at impact parameter \(b\) with velocity \(v\) into its Fourier components, obtaining an equivalent light intensity \(I(\nu)\) expressed in terms of modified Bessel functions. He then uses the known optical absorption coefficient \(\alpha(\nu)\) of the target atom to compute the probability of excitation or ionization via the equivalent light spectrum, integrating over impact parameters to obtain an effective collision radius \(\rho\). The method is applied to three cases: excitation of the mercury resonance line by slow electrons, ionization of helium by alpha particles from Radium-C, and the energy loss (deceleration) of alpha particles in helium.

## Key numbers

- For excitation of the mercury 2537 resonance line by 8-volt electrons: calculated effective radius \(\rho = 0.8 \times 10^{-8}\) cm, compared to an experimental estimate of \(0.4 \times 10^{-8}\) cm from Sponer (1921).
- For ionization of helium by alpha particles from Radium-C (velocity \(v = 1.98 \times 10^9\) cm/s): calculated number of ion pairs per cm = 4800, compared to the experimental value of 4600.
- For the range of alpha particles from Radium-C in helium: calculated range = 37 cm, compared to the experimental value of approximately 33 cm.
- Helium ionization potential: 24.5 volts, corresponding to \(\nu_0 = 6.0 \times 10^{15}\) Hz.
- Helium number density at 15 degrees C: \(n = 2.6 \times 10^{19}\) atoms per cm\(^3\).

## Context

This paper introduces the "equivalent light" or "Fermi method" for treating collisions between charged particles and atoms, which was later extended by Weizsacker and Williams (circa 1935) to the ultra-relativistic case. It contrasts with earlier theories of ionization by collision from Thomson (1912), Bohr (1913, 1915), and Rosseland (1925), which treated atomic electrons as free or bound by quasi-elastic forces and calculated energy transfer directly. Fermi's approach enables the calculation of excitation probabilities, ionization yields, and stopping power from known optical data, providing a unified framework that connects collision phenomena to optical absorption.
