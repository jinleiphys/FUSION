# Predominance of Prolate Nuclear Deformations Emerging from Many-Body Interactions

> A new approach to the old problem of the predominance of prolate deformations among well deformed nuclei is proposed within the shell model framework. The parameter space is explored using the ensemble of random rotationally-invariant interactions. Subsets with rotational energy ratio $E(4^{+})/E(2^{+})$ and the rigid-rotor relation between the quadrupole moment $Q(2^{+})$ and the transition probability $B(E2;2^{+}\to 0^{+}))$ are found exhibiting prolate predominance. We identify matrix elements of the effective forces responsible for the predominance of prolate deformation.

## Key claim
The predominance of prolate over oblate deformations in well-deformed nuclei emerges from the mixing of valence spherical orbitals of the same parity (specifically those with \(\Delta j = 2\)), driven by specific two-body matrix elements in the effective interaction, rather than from single-particle mean-field arguments alone.

## Method
The authors performed shell-model calculations for four neutrons and four protons in a two-orbital space (\(0f_{7/2}\) and \(1p_{3/2}\)), using an ensemble of random rotationally-invariant two-body interactions. They generated 10,000 realizations for each value of the mixing strength \(\lambda = V/\epsilon\) (where \(V\) is the random interaction strength and \(\epsilon\) the orbital spacing), and selected subsets satisfying both a rotational energy ratio criterion \(E(4^+)/E(2^+)\) between 3.0 and 3.6 and an Alaga ratio \(Q(2^+)^2 / B(E2;2^+\to 0^+)\) between 3.90 and 4.30. They then averaged the 30 allowed two-body matrix elements over the prolate rotational samples to identify which matrix elements are responsible for the prolate dominance.

## Key numbers
- For the \(N=Z\) case with normal level ordering and \(\lambda=4.0\), the fraction of prolate configurations among all rotational states is 0.71 (Table I\(a\)).
- For the \(N=Z\) case with inverted level ordering and \(\lambda=4.0\), the prolate fraction rises to 0.81 (Table I\(c\)).
- For the case with four protons and four neutrons in opposite-parity orbitals (\(f_{5/2}\) and \(g_{9/2}\)) and \(\lambda=4.0\), the prolate fraction drops to 0.05 (Table I\(d\)).
- The average value of matrix element #7 (\(\langle ff|V|ff\rangle(41)\)) over prolate samples is 0.869, compared to a full-ensemble average of 0.026 (Table II).
- The average value of matrix element #12 (\(\langle ff|V|pf\rangle(41)\)) over prolate samples is -2.826, compared to a full-ensemble average of -0.010 (Table II).

## Context
This work extends the random-interaction approach of Johnson (1998) and Horoi, Brown, and Zelevinsky (2001) to the specific problem of prolate dominance, which had previously been addressed via single-particle arguments by Lemmer (1960), Castel and Goeke (1976), and most recently Hamamoto and Mottelson (2009). The authors identify the specific two-body matrix elements responsible for the effect, particularly those describing \(f_{7/2} \leftrightarrow p_{3/2}\) single-nucleon transfer, and show that the effect disappears when the two orbitals have opposite parity. The results enable future studies of whether the prolate dominance persists in larger systems or vanishes in the macroscopic limit as predicted by Hamamoto and Mottelson (2009).
