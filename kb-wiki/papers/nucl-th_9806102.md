# VNI-4.1: Simulation of space-time evolution of e^+e^- .... A+B collisions with parton-cascades, parton-hadron conversion, final-state hadron cascades

> VNI is a general-purpose Monte-Carlo event-generator, which includes the simulation of lepton-lepton, lepton-hadron, lepton-nucleus, hadron-hadron, hadron-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus collisions. It uses the real-time evolution of parton cascades in conjunction with a self-consistent hadronization scheme, as well as the development of hadron cascades after hadronization. The causal evolution from a specific initial state (determined by the colliding beam particles) is followed by the time-development of the phase-space densities of partons, pre-hadronic parton clusters, and final-state hadrons, in position-space, momentum-space and color-space. The parton-evolution is described in terms of a space-time generalization of the familiar momentum-space description of multiple (semi)hard interactions in QCD, involving 2 -> 2 parton collisions, 2 -> 1 parton fusion processes, and 1 -> 2 radiation processes. The formation of color-singlet pre-hadronic clusters and their decays into hadrons, on the other hand, is treated by using a spatial criterion motivated by confinement and a non-perturbative model for hadronization. Finally, the cascading of produced pre-hadronic clusters and of hadrons is includes a multitude of 2 -> n processes, and is modeled in paralell to the parton cascade description. This paper gives a brief review of the physics underlying VNI, as well as a detailed description of the program itself. The latter program description emphasizes easy-to-use pragmatism and explains how to use the program (including simple examples), annotates input and control parameters, and discusses output data provided by it.

## Key claim

VNI provides a space-time description of parton cascade evolution and hadronization that is self-consistently controlled by the local parton density, rather than by an arbitrary global momentum cutoff, enabling the study of high-density QCD systems where statistical interactions among nearby particles become important.

## Method

The simulation follows the real-time evolution of a mixed system of partons, pre-hadronic clusters, and hadrons in 7-dimensional phase-space (position, momentum, energy) using a quantum-kinetic transport approach. The parton cascade includes 2-to-2 scatterings, 1-to-2 emissions, and 2-to-1 fusions, while hadronization proceeds via spatial coalescence of color-connected partons into pre-hadronic clusters that decay isotropically into hadrons, followed by a final-state hadron cascade with 2-to-n processes. The initial state is constructed from experimentally measured nucleon structure functions and elastic form-factors, decomposing beam and target particles into their partonic substructure.

## Key numbers

- CPU time for e+e- at sqrt(s) = 100 GeV: 10000 events/hour (Delta t = 35 fm, 133-166 MHz processor)
- CPU time for p+pbar at sqrt(s) = 200 GeV: 5000 events/hour (Delta t = 35 fm)
- CPU time for p+Au at sqrt(s) = 200 GeV/nucleon: 100 events/hour (Delta t = 35 fm)
- CPU time for Au+Au at sqrt(s) = 200 GeV/nucleon: 1 event/hour (Delta t = 35 fm)
- Program memory requirement: 2000 kwords (64-bit words)
- Program size: 25760 lines of main program, plus 49 and 244 lines for two example programs
- Numerical inaccuracies may accumulate for hadronic collisions above sqrt(s) >> 10 TeV and nuclear collisions above sqrt(s) >> 5 TeV/nucleon

## Context

VNI occupies a middle ground between event generators for "clean" reactions (such as PYTHIA, HERWIG, ARIADNE, LEPTO, and ISAJET) that lack space-time variables, and generators for nuclear collisions (such as FRITIOF, DPM, VENUS, RQMD, HIJET, and HIJING) that operate on hadronic or string degrees of freedom rather than partonic ones. The approach enables studies of high-density QCD phenomena relevant to HERA, RHIC, and LHC experiments, including parton saturation at small Bjorken-x and quark-gluon plasma formation in heavy-ion collisions, and extends prior work by Geiger and collaborators on quantum-kinetic theory and cluster hadronization.
