# Polarization in Heavy Ion Physics

> Role of polarization studies in heavy ion physics is discussed with emphasis on the search for quark-gluon plasma formation and studies of its dynamical properties.

## Key claim

Polarization measurements in heavy-ion collisions are a complementary but useful tool for detecting quark-gluon plasma formation and studying its dynamical properties, but the predicted vanishing hyperon polarization relative to the production plane in central nuclear collisions remains an untested hypothesis with no experimental measurements at high energies.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical predictions for hyperon polarization relative to the production plane as a signal of QGP formation, and contrasts these with predictions for polarization relative to the reaction plane arising from large orbital angular momentum in non-central collisions. It then compares these predictions with experimental results from RHIC, particularly the STAR Collaboration measurements of global polarization of Λ and anti-Λ hyperons in Au+Au collisions.

## Key numbers

- Upper limit on global polarization of Λ and anti-Λ hyperons in Au+Au collisions at RHIC: |P_Λ,anti-Λ| ≤ 0.02 at √s_NN = 62.4 and 200 GeV
- Average orbital angular momentum in nuclear collisions at RHIC: ~10^5 (in units where ℏ=1)
- Average orbital angular momentum in nuclear collisions at LHC: ~10^7 (in units where ℏ=1)
- Estimated large interaction cross-section for the strongly interacting transient matter: about 22 mb

## Context

This work extends the early studies by Hoy (1983), Pan (1984), and Stock (1986) who proposed vanishing Λ polarization as a QGP signal, and contrasts with predictions by Liang (2005) of significant (order 0.3) hyperon polarization relative to the reaction plane due to spin-orbital coupling. The paper discusses how the discovery of strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma (sQGP) at RHIC, which behaves as a perfect liquid rather than a weakly-coupled gas, leads to different expectations for polarization and directed flow, and suggests that combined polarization and anisotropic flow measurements at RHIC and LHC could help distinguish between weakly-coupled and strongly-coupled transient matter.
