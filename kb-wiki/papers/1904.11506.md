# Neutrino event generators: foundation, status and future

> Neutrino event generators are an essential tool needed for the extraction of neutrino mixing parameters, the mass hierarchy and a CP violating phase from long-baseline experiments. In this article I first describe the theoretical basis and the approximations needed to get to present-days generators. I also discuss the strengths and limitations of theoretical models used to describe semi-inclusive neutrino-nucleus reactions. I then confront present day's generators with this theoretical basis by detailed discussions of the various reaction processes. Finally, as examples I then show for various experiments results of the generator GiBUU for lepton semi-inclusive cross sections as well as particle spectra. I also discuss features of these cross sections in terms of the various reaction components, with predictions for DUNE. Finally, I argue for the need for a new neutrino generator that respects our present-day knowledge of both nuclear theory and nuclear reactions and is as much state-of-the-art as the experimental equipment. I outline some necessary requirements for such a new generator.

## Key claim

Present-day neutrino event generators rely on approximations (quasiparticle, frozen, and free-particle approximations) that are inconsistent with modern nuclear theory; a new generator is needed that respects state-of-the-art knowledge of both nuclear theory and nuclear reactions to meet the precision requirements of upcoming long-baseline experiments.

## Method

The paper derives the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) transport equation from quantum many-body theory, starting from Dyson equations for Green's functions and introducing the Wigner transform. It then systematically examines the approximations made by existing generators: the quasiparticle approximation (neglecting in-medium spectral functions), the frozen approximation (assuming the nuclear density does not change during the reaction), and the free-particle approximation (neglecting binding potentials and using free dispersion relations). The author confronts each generator's treatment of reaction subprocesses (quasielastic scattering, resonance excitation, deep inelastic scattering, 2p2h interactions) against this theoretical foundation, using the GiBUU generator as an illustrative example.

## Key numbers

- The DUNE energy distribution peaks at about 2 GeV, with tails from zero energy up to 30 GeV.
- The T2K beam energy peak is at about 0.75 GeV.
- At the peak of the oscillated event distribution for T2K (about 0.65 GeV), the true and reconstructed energies differ by about 25%.
- The sensitivity of the electron appearance signal to the CP violating phase delta_CP at the same peak also shows differences of about 25% between curves for different delta_CP values.
- For nuclei with mass number A > 12, the frozen approximation assumes only about 2-3 nucleons are emitted.

## Context

This review extends the critical analysis of neutrino event generators beyond earlier reviews such as Gallagher (2018) and Katori (2016), which focused on cross section data and generator descriptions. The paper argues that generators like GENIE, NEUT, and NuWro use inconsistent treatments where initial-state spectral functions (containing potential and off-shell information) are paired with free-particle final-state propagation, while GiBUU and FLUKA implement consistent potentials throughout. The work enables the development of a new generator that would incorporate off-shell transport, consistent mean fields, and modern nuclear many-body theory, which is required for the precision goals of DUNE and T2HK.
