# From Hyperons to Hypernuclei: A New Route to Unravel Proton Spin Polarization

> Ultra-relativistic nuclear collisions create the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) known as the hottest, least viscous, and most vortical fluid ever produced in terrestrial laboratories. Its vortical structure has been uncovered through the spin polarization of Lambda ($Λ$) hyperons, attributed to the spin-orbit coupling that transfers the system's orbital angular momentum to the quark spin, which is then inherited by hadrons via quark recombination or coalescence. However, $Λ$ polarization reflects primarily the strange-quark component, leaving the spin dynamics of the up and down quarks largely unexplored. Although the proton is an ideal probe, its stability makes direct measurements experimentally challenging. Here, we propose to unravel proton spin polarization via hypertriton ($^3_Λ\text{H}$) measurements, exploiting the fact that spin information is preserved when polarized nucleons and $Λ$ coalesce to form hypertriton. We show that, over a broad range of collision energies, the polarizations of proton, $Λ$, and hypertriton are related by a simple linear scaling law. Since both $Λ$ and hypertriton polarizations can be measured via their self-analyzing weak decays, this linear relation provides a practical experimental avenue for accessing spin polarizations of protons and neutrons-the dominant baryonic degrees of freedom in nuclear collisions.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that proton spin polarization in heavy-ion collisions can be reconstructed from the measurable polarizations of Lambda hyperons and hypertritons via a simple linear scaling relation, validated by model simulations over a broad range of collision energies, but the relation assumes isospin symmetry (proton and neutron polarizations are approximately equal) and neglects higher-order terms since polarizations are typically only a few percent.

## Method

The authors extended the multi-phase transport (AMPT) model to include spin degrees of freedom, computing hadron polarization at hadronization via the thermal vorticity of the quark-gluon plasma using the Cooper-Frye formula, then simulating spin-dependent hadronic rescatterings and resonance decays (including Delta and Sigma resonances) through the hadronic phase until kinetic freeze-out. Hypertriton production was modeled using a spin-dependent coalescence model with a Gaussian Wigner function, where the spin statistical factors encode the polarization transfer from nucleons and Lambda hyperons to the hypertriton. The simulations were performed for non-central Au+Au collisions at center-of-mass energies from 7.7 to 200 GeV.

## Key numbers

- Lambda hyperon polarization at kinetic freeze-out is reduced by approximately 20% relative to its value at hadronization, due to combined effects of resonance feed-down and spin-flip scatterings.
- The spin transfer coefficient for quasi-elastic scatterings such as pi N -> Delta -> pi N is 5/9, reducing nucleon polarization to approximately 5/9 of initial values at hadronization.
- The Lambda separation energy in hypertriton is B_Lambda = 0.164 MeV, used to set the Wigner function parameter sigma_lambda = 6.52 fm.
- The Wigner function parameter sigma_rho = 2.26 fm reproduces the empirical deuteron root-mean-squared radius of sqrt(<r_d^2>) = 1.96 fm.
- The decay parameter of hypertriton is linearly proportional to that of Lambda: alpha_hypertriton = -alpha_Lambda / 2.58.
- The updated decay parameter of Lambda hyperon is alpha_Lambda = 0.732 +/- 0.014.
- The linear relation for proton polarization is P_p = (3 P_hypertriton + P_Lambda) / 4, validated for collision energies sqrt(s_NN) = 7.7-200 GeV.
- The orbital angular momentum in semi-central collisions is on the order of 10^3 hbar.

## Context

This work extends the study of global spin polarization in heavy-ion collisions, previously observed for Lambda hyperons by the STAR Collaboration (2017, 2023), by proposing a method to access proton and neutron spin polarizations which have remained unmeasured due to the stability of these baryons. The approach builds on the understanding that Lambda polarization primarily reflects strange-quark spin (Liang 2004), while proton polarization would probe up and down quark spin dynamics, and it enables future experimental studies at RHIC, the High Intensity heavy-ion Accelerator Facility (HIAF), the Nuclotron-based Ion Collider Facility (NICA), and the Facility for Antiproton and Ion Research (FAIR).
