# Soft triaxial rotor in the vicinity of $γ=π/6$ and its extensions

> The collective Bohr hamiltonian is solved for the soft triaxial rotor around $γ_0=π/6$ with a displaced harmonic oscillator potential in $γ$ and a Kratzer-like potential in $β$. The properties of the spectrum are outlined and a generalization for the more general triaxial case with $0<γ<π/6$ is proposed.

## Key claim

The paper presents an analytic solution of the Bohr collective Hamiltonian for a soft triaxial rotor with a harmonic potential in γ centered at γ₀ = π/6 and a Kratzer-like potential in β, and proposes a generalization to other triaxial shapes with 0 < γ₀ < π/3 using an su(1,1) algebraic approach, though the generalization requires approximations and yields only approximate analytic or numerical solutions.

## Method

The authors separate the Bohr Hamiltonian into β and γ parts using a potential of the form V(β,γ) = V₁(β) + V₂(γ)/β², with a displaced harmonic oscillator in γ and a Kratzer-like potential in β. The γ-angular part is solved to give an extension of the Meyer-ter-Vehn formula with an additive harmonic term, while the β equation is solved analytically for both the Kratzer and Davidson potentials. For the generalization to arbitrary γ₀, the moments of inertia are approximated by their values at γ₀, the wave functions are expanded in a basis of rotational wave functions, and the su(1,1) algebra is used to reduce the secular problem to an algebraic equation.

## Key numbers

- The γ-angular energy contribution is ω_{L,R,n_γ} = √C (2n_γ + 1) + L(L+1) − (3/4)R², where R is the projection of angular momentum on the intrinsic 1-axis.
- For the Kratzer-like β potential, the energy spectrum is ε(n_γ, n_β, L, R) = (A²/4) / (√(9/4 + B + ω_{L,R,n_γ}) + 1/2 + n_β)².
- For the Davidson β potential, the spectrum is ε_D(n_γ, n_β, L, R) = √A_D (2n_β + τ_{L,R,n_γ} + 5/2), where τ satisfies (τ+1)(τ+2) = B_D − ω_{L,K,n_γ}.
- The rigid triaxial rotor (Davydov) model is recovered when B → ∞.

## Context

This work extends the rigid triaxial rotor model of Davydov (1960) and the Meyer-ter-Vehn formula (1975) by introducing soft potentials in both β and γ, providing a more physical description than rigid models. It builds on recent analytic solutions for shape phase transitions introduced by Iachello (2000-2001), such as E(5), X(5), and Y(5), and on prior work by Fortunato (2003) on the γ-angular part. The model enables interpretation of collective spectra in nuclei that lack axial symmetry, though the authors note that applications to experimental data are still preliminary.
