# Importance of the Doppler Effect to the Determination of the Deuteron Binding Energy

> The deuteron binding energy extracted from the reaction ${}^1H(n,γ){}^2H$ is reviewed with the exact relativistic formula, where the initial kinetic energy and the Doppler effect are taken into account. We find that the negligible initial kinetic energy of the neutron could cause a significant uncertainty which is beyond the errors available up to now. Therefore, we suggest an experiment which should include the detailed informations about the initial kinetic energy and the detection angle. It could reduce discrepancies among the recently reported values about the deuteron binding energy and pin down the uncertainty due to the Doppler broadening of $γ$ ray.

## Key claim

The authors argue that even the very small kinetic energy of thermal neutrons (0.025–0.056 eV) in the \(^1H(n,\gamma)^2H\) reaction causes a Doppler broadening of the emitted gamma ray that introduces an uncertainty of up to 25 eV in the extracted deuteron binding energy, far exceeding the reported experimental error of 2.3 eV from the crystal spectrometer.

## Method

The authors derived an exact relativistic formula for the deuteron binding energy from the \(^1H(n,\gamma)^2H\) reaction, incorporating the initial kinetic energy of the neutron and the detection angle of the gamma ray via the Doppler effect. They applied this formula to the measured gamma-ray wavelength of 5.5766988(55) × 10⁻¹³ m from Greene et al., using standard neutron and proton masses, and calculated the binding energy for three special cases of the detection angle (\(\theta = 0, \pi/2, \pi\)) with a neutron kinetic energy of 0.056 eV. They also estimated the expected line width of the gamma-ray spectrum from the Doppler broadening using the angular distribution of thermal neutrons.

## Key numbers

- The measured gamma-ray wavelength from the n-p capture is \(\lambda_{np} = 5.5766988(55) \times 10^{-13}\) m, corresponding to a photon energy of \(\omega = 2.2232552(23)\) MeV.
- For a neutron kinetic energy of 0.056 eV, the calculated deuteron binding energies for three detection angles are: \(E_b = 2.2245601(23)\) MeV for \(\cos\theta = 1\) (source approaching), \(E_b = 2.2245732(23)\) MeV for \(\cos\theta = 0\) (transverse), and \(E_b = 2.2245852(23)\) MeV for \(\cos\theta = -1\) (source receding), giving a spread of about 25 eV.
- For a neutron kinetic energy of 0.025 eV (thermal neutrons at room temperature), the estimated line width from Doppler broadening is 14.0 eV; for 0.056 eV, the line width is 21.1 eV.
- The standard deviation of the 52 Bragg angle measurements in Greene et al. is calculated as \(2.3 \times \sqrt{52} = 16.6\) eV, compatible with the calculated line width.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the deuteron binding energy from the \(^1H(n,\gamma)^2H\) reaction, previously measured by Greene et al., Van et al., Vylov et al., Greenwood et al., Adam et al., and Michael et al., by incorporating relativistic kinematics and the Doppler effect that were neglected in the conventional non-relativistic treatment. The authors show that the Doppler broadening from the neutron's initial kinetic energy can explain the discrepancies among reported values (e.g., a 53–64 eV shift between Vylov et al. and Van et al. or Greenwood et al.) and suggest that selecting a specific detection angle (e.g., \(\theta = \pi/2\)) could reduce the line width and enable more precise measurements.
