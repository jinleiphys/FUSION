# Shell-model based study of the direct capture in neutron-rich nuclei

> The radiative neutron capture rates for isotopes of astrophysical interest are commonly calculated within the statistical Hauser-Feshbach reaction model. Such an approach, assuming a high level density in the compound system, can be questioned in light and neutron-rich nuclei for which only a few or no resonant states are available. Therefore, in this work we focus on the direct neutron-capture process. We employ a shell-model approach in several model spaces with well-established effective interactions to calculate spectra and spectroscopic factors in a set of 50 neutron-rich target nuclei in different mass regions, including doubly-, semi-magic and deformed ones. Those theoretical energies and spectroscopic factors are used to evaluate direct neutron capture rates and to test global theoretical models using average spectroscopic factors and level densities based on the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov plus combinatorial method. The comparison of shell-model and global model results reveals several discrepancies that can be related to problems in level densities. All the results show however that the direct capture is non-negligible with respect to the by-default Hauser-Feshbach predictions and can be even 100 times more important for the most neutron-rich nuclei close to the neutron drip line.

## Key claim

The direct neutron capture process is non-negligible compared to the standard Hauser-Feshbach statistical model predictions for neutron-rich nuclei, and can be up to 100 times more important for the most neutron-rich nuclei near the neutron drip line; however, this conclusion is conditional on the specific shell-model spaces and interactions used, and the comparison reveals discrepancies that can be related to problems in level densities.

## Method

The authors performed large-scale shell-model calculations in multiple model spaces (SDPF-U, LNPS, LNPS-GDS, NI78-II) to compute energy levels and spectroscopic factors for 50 neutron-rich target nuclei spanning doubly-magic, semi-magic, and deformed systems. These theoretical structure inputs were then used within a potential model to evaluate direct neutron capture cross sections, considering E1, E2, and M1 transitions, and compared against global theoretical models using average spectroscopic factors and Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov plus combinatorial level densities.

## Key numbers

- Direct capture cross section ratio to Hauser-Feshbach predictions reaches a factor of 10 after passing the N=50 shell closure in Ni isotopes at incident neutron energy 100 keV
- For the most neutron-rich nuclei close to the neutron drip line, direct capture can be up to 100 times more important than Hauser-Feshbach predictions
- Inter-model root mean square deviation for 50 nuclei at E_n=100 keV is 12.26 for the intrinsic model and 11.34 for the 1p-1h model
- Average spectroscopic factor from shell-model calculations for ground states of even-odd compound nuclei is 0.364, close to the global model value of 0.347
- Mean spectroscopic factor for even-even residual nuclei is approximately 0.6
- Spectroscopic factor for the s_{1/2} orbital ranges from 0.2 in 73Ni to 0.9 in 79Ni
- In 79Ni, a 7/2+ state calculated at 1.22 MeV with spectroscopic factor 0.87 enters the cross section calculation
- Direct capture cross section on odd targets is larger than on even targets by a factor of 1.2 to 3.5
- For 70Cr, global models give a cross section four orders of magnitude smaller than the shell-model prediction at E_n=100 keV

## Context

This work extends the direct capture studies of Xu (2012) and Xu (2014) to neutron-rich nuclei far from stability, where the statistical Hauser-Feshbach model of Goriely (2008) becomes questionable due to low level densities. The shell-model calculations test the global microscopic models using Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov plus combinatorial level densities from Goriely (2008) and provide improved direct capture rates for r-process nucleosynthesis simulations, particularly for neutron star merger scenarios as studied by Goriely (2011, 2015) and Just (2015). The results enable more reliable neutron capture rates for the approximately 5800 nuclei between the proton and neutron drip lines needed for r-process calculations.
