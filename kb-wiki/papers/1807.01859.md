# Heavy-ions collisions and fission dynamics with the time-dependent Hartree-Fock theory and its extensions

> Microscopic methods and tools to describe nuclear dynamics have considerably been improved in the past few years. They are based on the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory and its extensions to include pairing correlations and quantum fluctuations. The TDHF theory is the lowest level of approximation of a range of methods to solve the quantum many-body problem, showing its universality to describe many-fermion dynamics at the mean-field level. The range of applications of TDHF to describe realistic systems allowing for detailed comparisons with experiment has considerably increased. For instance, TDHF is now commonly used to investigate fusion, multi-nucleon transfer and quasi-fission reactions. Thanks to the inclusion of pairing correlations, it has also recently led to breakthroughs in our description of the saddle to scission evolution, and, in particular, the non-adiabatic effects near scission. Beyond mean-field approaches such as the time-dependent random-phase approximation (TDRPA) and stochastic mean-field methods have reached the point where they can be used for realistic applications. We review recent progresses in both techniques and applications to heavy-ion collision and fission.

## Key claim

The time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory and its extensions (including pairing correlations and beyond-mean-field methods like TDRPA and stochastic mean-field) have reached the point where they can be used for realistic applications to heavy-ion collisions and fission dynamics, but the approach remains fundamentally unable to describe quantum tunneling in sub-barrier fusion without invoking an external nucleus-nucleus potential, and the treatment of the relative phase between colliding superfluid nuclei may require beyond-mean-field techniques.

## Method

The paper reviews the derivation of TDHF from multiple formalisms: quantum many-body perturbation theory (leading to Dyson's equation and the self-consistent Hartree-Fock approximation), truncation of the BBGKY hierarchy (setting two-body correlations to zero), the Dirac variational principle (restricting the many-body state to Slater determinants), the Balian-Vénérioni variational principle (optimized for one-body observables), and the path integral formulation (with stationary phase approximation). Extensions covered include the time-dependent random-phase approximation (TDRPA) and stochastic mean-field methods to incorporate quantum fluctuations, as well as the inclusion of pairing correlations via BCS or Bogoliubov approaches to describe superfluid dynamics in fission.

## Key numbers

- The TDHF equation is derived from the Dirac action: \( i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} \az_\alpha(x,t) = \int dy \, h(x,y;t) \az_\alpha(y,t) \) for occupied single-particle states \( 1 \le i \le A \).
- The non-interacting single-particle Green's function is expressed as \( G^0(\vx,\vy,\omega) = \sum_i \az_i^0(\vx) {\az_i^0}^*(\vy) \frac{1}{\omega - e_i^0/\hbar + \text{sign}(e_i^0 - \epsilon_F^0) i\eta} \).
- The proper self-energy in the Hartree-Fock approximation is \( \Sigma^\star_{HF}(x,x') = \frac{-i\delta(t-t')}{\hbar} [ \delta(\vx-\vx') \int d^3x'' G(\vx''t,\vx''t^+) V(\vx-\vx'') - V(\vx-\vx') G(\vx t,\vx' t^+) ] \).
- The one-body density matrix in the HF ground-state is \( \rho(\vx,\vy) = \sum_{i=1}^A \az_i(\vx) \az_i(\vy) \) with the sum over occupied states with \( e_i < \epsilon_F \).

## Context

This review extends the earlier reviews of Negele (1982), Simenel (2012), Lacroix (2014), and Nakatsukasa (2016) on microscopic approaches to nuclear dynamics. It describes how TDHF has become a tool to analyze experimental data for fusion, multi-nucleon transfer, and quasi-fission reactions, and how the inclusion of pairing correlations has enabled breakthroughs in describing the saddle-to-scission evolution in fission, including predictions of total kinetic energy of fission fragments without a scission-point prescription. The paper identifies open problems including the need for a fully microscopic treatment of quantum tunneling in sub-barrier fusion (potentially via the time-dependent generator coordinate method or path integral approaches with complex time), the challenge of describing multi-nucleon transfer channels leading to fragments very different from the average, and the lack of a consistent microscopic non-adiabatic treatment of fission from the compound system to post-scission configuration.
