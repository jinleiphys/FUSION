# Die Erste Stunde (The first hour)

> I recall my "first hour" events following on my meeting in Fall 1968 in the classroom with my academic teacher and thesis mentor Prof. Dr. Dr. h.c. multiple Walter Greiner. My comments focus on the creation of the new "strong fields" domain of physics in Frankfurt. I argue that this was the research field closest to Walter's heart during his lustrous academic career. I will describe the events that lead on to Greiner's course books, Walters actions leading to the rise of Frankfurt School of Theoretical Physics, and show how a stability principle defined his science.

## Key claim

This personal reminiscence argues that the "strong fields" domain of physics, created in Frankfurt beginning in the late 1960s, was the research field closest to Walter Greiner's heart during his career, and that a "stability principle" guided, and sometimes misdirected, his scientific work, particularly his initial resistance to accepting the reality of electron "diving" into the negative continuum and later his opposition to the quark-gluon plasma concept.

## Method

The author recounts his personal experiences as a student and later scientific assistant of Walter Greiner from 1968 to the early 1980s, describing the creation of Greiner's theoretical physics textbook series from student lecture scripts, the development of the "strong field" research program in Frankfurt, and the application of Fano's resonance embedment method to understand positron autoionization when bound electron states dive into the negative energy continuum. The paper also reviews the technical details of the Born-Infeld nonlinear electromagnetism approach that was explored as a possible mechanism to prevent critical binding, and describes the subsequent development of the "charged vacuum" concept.

## Key numbers

- The critical binding energy threshold for electron diving is \(2mc^2 = 1.022\) MeV, the energy at which a bound state enters the negative energy continuum.
- For the Born-Infeld model, the potential depth at the origin scales as \(eV_\mathrm{BI}(0) = - Z^{1/2} \times 0.765\) MeV, with the Born-Infeld radius \(r_\mathrm{BI} = 1.236 r_e = 3.483\) fm derived from the classical electron radius.
- The German special relativity textbook sold 6,300 copies over three editions in 10 years.
- The critical nuclear charge for diving was found to be \(Z > 172\), building on earlier work by Werner and Wheeler (1958) who had considered \(Z\) values substantially higher than 137.

## Context

This paper extends the personal and scientific history of the Frankfurt School of Theoretical Physics, complementing a recent commentary by Biro (2018) on Greiner's life and work. It describes how Greiner's group built on the earlier work of Werner and Wheeler (1958) on superheavy nuclei, and how Greiner adapted the resonance embedment method of Fano (1961) to understand positron autoionization. The work enabled the development of the "charged vacuum" concept (Rafelski, Muller, and Greiner, 1974) and later influenced efforts to modify Einstein's gravity to avoid event horizons, as well as the eventual acceptance of quark-gluon plasma physics at Frankfurt.
