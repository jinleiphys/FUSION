# Neutron radius determination of 133Cs and its impact on the interpretation of CEvNS-CsI measurement

> Proton-$^{133}$Cs elastic scattering at low momentum transfer is performed using an in-ring reaction technique at the Cooler Storage Ring at the Heavy Ion Research Facility in Lanzhou. Recoil protons from the elastic collisions between the internal H$_2$-gas target and the circulating $^{133}$Cs ions at 199.4 MeV/u are detected by a silicon-strip detector. The matter radius of $^{133}$Cs is deduced by describing the measured differential cross sections using the Glauber model. Employing the adopted proton distribution radius, a point-neutron radius of 4.86(21) fm for $^{133}$Cs is obtained. With the newly determined neutron radius, the weak mixing angle sin$^2 θ_W$ is independently extracted to be 0.227(28) by fitting the coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering data. Our work limits the sin$^2 θ_W$ value in a range smaller than the ones proposed by the previous independent approaches, and would play an important role in searching new physics via the high precision CE$ν$NS-CsI cross section data in the near future.

## Key claim

The authors claim that by experimentally determining the point-neutron radius of 133Cs to be 4.86(21) fm via proton elastic scattering in inverse kinematics, they can independently extract the weak mixing angle sin2θW = 0.227(28) from CEνNS-CsI data, but this result is conditional on the precision of the CEνNS data itself and the uncertainty remains too large (about 10%) to effectively probe new physics.

## Method

Proton-133Cs elastic scattering at 199.4 MeV/u was performed in inverse kinematics using the Cooler Storage Ring (CSRe) at HIRFL-Lanzhou, where circulating 133Cs55+ ions interacted repeatedly with an internal H2-gas-jet target. Recoil protons in the laboratory angular range of about 85° to 90° were detected by a double-sided silicon-strip detector (DSSD) with 32 × 32 strips and 1000 μm thickness. The measured small-angle differential cross sections were described using the Glauber multiple-scattering theory with a two-parameter Fermi density distribution, and the point-matter radius was extracted by fitting the half-density radius R and a normalization factor L0 while fixing the diffuseness parameter a = 0.53(3) fm. The point-neutron radius was then derived from the point-matter radius using the adopted point-proton radius of 4.740(5) fm from charge radius data.

## Key numbers

- Point-neutron radius of 133Cs: Rpn = 4.86 ± 0.21 fm, derived from the measured point-matter radius Rpm = 4.811 ± 0.127 fm.
- Point-proton radius of 133Cs: Rpp = 4.740(5) fm, adopted from charge radius data (Angeli 2013).
- Neutron skin of 133Cs: Rpn − Rpp = 0.12(21) fm from this experiment; consistent with 0.14(3) fm derived from parity-violating electron scattering data of 48Ca and 208Pb, and 0.13(4) fm from antiprotonic atom experiments (Trzcinska 2001).
- Weak mixing angle extracted from CEνNS-CsI data using the newly determined neutron radius: sin2θW = 0.227(28), compared to the Standard Model prediction of 0.23857(5) at low momentum transfer.
- The precision of the sin2θW determination improved by a factor of about 2.5 compared to the free two-dimensional fit value of 0.309+0.078−0.063.
- Glauber model input parameters at 200 MeV: σpp = 1.788(20) fm2, σpn = 3.099(27) fm2, αpp = 0.893(17), αpn = 0.325(23), βpp = βpn = 0.528(41) fm2.

## Context

This work directly addresses the long-standing lack of a precision experimental neutron radius for 133Cs, which had been identified by the COHERENT collaboration (Akimov 2021) as the dominant uncertainty in CEνNS-CsI cross section calculations. Previous reported neutron radii for 133Cs spread from 4.6 fm to 6.6 fm, derived from various theoretical models or fits to CEνNS data assuming fixed sin2θW values (Huang 2019, Papoulias 2020, Khan 2019, Cadeddu 2018, 2020, 2021, Coloma 2020, Romeri 2023, Corona 2023). The new measurement enables an independent extraction of sin2θW from CEνNS data without relying on theoretical neutron distribution predictions or combining with atomic parity nonconservation (PNC) data, which had been complicated by theoretical corrections to the PNC amplitude (Cadeddu 2023, Sahoo 2021). Future high-precision CEνNS-CsI data can now use this key neutron radius value to search for new physics in neutrino interactions.
