# Nuclear Equation of State and Neutron Star Cooling

> We investigate the cooling of neutron stars with relativistic and non-relativistic models of dense nuclear matter. We focus on the effects of uncertainties originated from the nuclear models, the composition of elements in the envelope region, and the formation of superfluidity in the core and the crust of neutron stars. Discovery of $2 M_\odot$ neutron stars PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0343+0432 has triggered the revival of stiff nuclear equation of state at high densities. In the mean time, observation of a neutron star in Cassiopeia A for more than 10 years has provided us with very accurate data for the thermal evolution of neutron stars. Both mass and temperature of neutron stars depend critically on the equation of state of nuclear matter, so we first search for nuclear models that satisfy the constraints from mass and temperature simultaneously within a reasonable range. With selected models, we explore the effects of element composition in the evenlope region, and the existence of superfluidity in the core and the crust of neutron stars. Due to uncertainty in the composition of particles in the envelope region we obtain a range of cooling curves that can cover substantial region of observation data.

## Key claim
The cooling curves of neutron stars can cover a substantial region of observational data due to uncertainties in the composition of particles in the envelope region, but the direct Urca process, once activated, causes such rapid neutrino emission that no other effects (superfluidity or exotic states) can slow the temperature drop, making its appearance crucial for constraining the inner structure and equation of state.

## Method
The authors simulated neutron star cooling using both non-relativistic Skyrme force models (SLy4, SkI4, SGI, SV, TOV-min, LS220) and relativistic mean field (RMF) models (IU-FSU, DD-MEδ, SFHo, NLρ, TMA, NL3) for the core, assuming uniform nucleon-lepton matter without exotic degrees of freedom. They solved the thermal evolution equations incorporating the effects of envelope element composition and superfluidity (including pair breaking and formation, PBF) in the core and crust, and compared the resulting cooling curves to observed surface temperatures and photon luminosities of 19 isolated neutron stars.

## Key numbers
- Maximum neutron star masses of ~2 M⊙ from PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0348+0432 are used to select stiff equations of state that satisfy this constraint.
- Nuclear saturation density is n0 ≈ 0.16 fm⁻³.
- For the neutron star in Cassiopeia A (Cas A), the effective temperature at infinity is log(T_s^∞/K) = 6.18^{+0.01}_{-0.01} and the photon luminosity is log(L^∞/erg s⁻¹) = 33.83–33.88, with spin-down age log(t_sd/yr) = 2.518^{+0.007}_{-0.007}.
- The symmetry energy slope L ranges from 45.9 MeV (SLy4) to 118 MeV (NL3) across the selected models.
- The compression modulus K ranges from 219 MeV (DD-MEδ) to 318 MeV (TMA).

## Context
This work extends prior studies of neutron star cooling by incorporating both relativistic and non-relativistic nuclear models that satisfy the 2 M⊙ mass constraint from Demorest (2010) and Antoniadis (2013), and the mass-radius constraints from Steiner, Lattimer, and Brown (2010). It builds on the analysis of kaon condensation and hyperon effects by Lim et al. (2014) and hyperon studies by the same group (2014), and on the successful reproduction of Cas A cooling via pair breaking and formation by Page et al. (2011) and Shternin et al. (2011). The work enables multi-test grounds for nuclear models by requiring simultaneous consistency with both mass-radius relations and observed thermal evolution data.
