# Determining the nuclear neutron distribution from Coherent Elastic neutrino-Nucleus Scattering: current results and future prospects

> Coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering (CE$ν$NS), a process recently measured for the first time at ORNL's Spallation Neutron Source, is directly sensitive to the weak form factor of the nucleus. The European Spallation Source (ESS), presently under construction, will generate the most intense pulsed neutrino flux suitable for the detection of CE$ν$NS. In this paper we quantify its potential to determine the root mean square radius of the point-neutron distribution, for a variety of target nuclei and a suite of detectors. To put our results in context we also derive, for the first time, a constraint on this parameter from the analysis of the energy and timing data of the CsI detector at the COHERENT experiment.

# Determining the nuclear neutron distribution from Coherent Elastic neutrino-Nucleus Scattering: current results and future prospects

## Key claim

The root mean square radius of the point-neutron distribution can be constrained from CEνNS data, but the extracted value depends strongly on the choice of quenching factor and background modeling assumptions, leading to a spread in results beyond those previously considered in the literature. The authors derive the first constraint on this parameter using both energy and timing information from the COHERENT CsI detector, finding that timing information shifts the best-fit toward slightly larger values when using the data-release background parametrization.

## Method

The authors perform a chi-squared fit to the COHERENT CsI data (308.1 live-days, 7.48 GW-hr) binned in photoelectrons and time, using three different quenching factor parametrizations (energy-independent from data release, energy-dependent "Chicago QF" from Collar (2019), and a new "Duke QF" from their own fit to TUNL calibration data) and two background treatments (exponential parametrization from data release and a data-based temporal fit). For future prospects at the European Spallation Source, they simulate event distributions binned in nuclear recoil energy for three detector technologies (cryogenic CsI scintillator, high-pressure gaseous Xe TPC, and p-type point contact Ge) assuming 3 years exposure at 20 m distance, using the Helm form factor parametrization to extract the weak radius and convert to the point-neutron distribution radius via the charge radius and nucleon charge radii.

## Key numbers

- COHERENT CsI best-fit for point-neutron distribution radius: varies between approximately 5.0 fm and 5.5 fm depending on quenching factor and background assumptions (Fig. 3, right panel)
- ESS design power: 5 MW (compared to 1 MW at SNS); proton energy: 2 GeV (compared to 1 GeV at SNS)
- ESS neutrino yield: 0.3 neutrinos of each flavor per proton, approximately 3.2 times larger than SNS yield
- ESS protons on target: 2.8 × 10^23 per calendar year; total for 3-year exposure: 8.4 × 10^23 PoT
- ESS duty factor: 4 × 10^-2 (compared to SNS duty factor of 6 × 10^-4)
- Detector parameters: CsI mass 22.5 kg, threshold 1 keV, energy resolution 30%; Xe mass 20 kg, threshold 0.9 keV, resolution 40%; Ge mass 7 kg, threshold 0.6 keV, resolution 15%
- Steady-state background rates: 10 ckkd for CsI and Xe, 3 ckkd for Ge (before duty factor reduction)
- Proton charge radius squared: 0.70706 fm^2; neutron charge radius squared: -0.1161 fm^2 (from Particle Data Group)

## Context

This work extends previous analyses of neutron distribution radii from CEνNS by Cadeddu (2017, 2018, 2019, 2020), Giunti (2019), and others, but is the first to include both energy and timing information from COHERENT CsI data for this purpose. The timing information provides additional constraints that shift the best-fit value compared to energy-only analyses. The future projections for the European Spallation Source build on the detector concepts and flux simulations from Baxter (2019), but focus specifically on nuclear structure rather than new physics searches, and include the effect of energy scale uncertainty which was not considered in that prior work. The results enable improved tests of nuclear models and constraints on the neutron skin thickness, which connects to the symmetry energy and neutron star structure as discussed by Brown (2000), Horowitz (2000), Centelles (2008), and Tsang (2012).
