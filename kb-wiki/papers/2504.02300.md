# $β$-decay of N=126 isotones for the r-process nucleosynthesis

> The $β$-decay properties of nuclei with neutron number $N = 126$ is investigated in this paper. Two different versions of the proton-neutron quasi particle random phase (pn-QRPA) model were employed to compute $β$-decay rates and half-lives for the N = 126 isotones. The first set of calculation solves the pn-QRPA equations using the schematic model (SM) approach. The Woods-Saxon potential was employed as a mean-field basis. A spherical shape assigned for each waiting point nuclei throughout all simulations. Both allowed Gamow-Teller (GT) and first-forbidden (FF) transitions were considered in the particle-hole (ph) channel. The second set uses the pn-QRPA model in deformed Nilsson basis to calculate $β$-decay rates for allowed GT and unique first-forbidden (U1F) transitions under terrestrial and stellar conditions. Our results are in agreement with shell model findings that first-forbidden transitions lead to a considerable decrement in the calculated half-lives of the isotones. Inclusion of the first-forbidden contribution led to a decent agreement of our computed terrestrial $β$-decay half-lives with measured ones, much better than the previous calculations. The possible implication of the waiting point nuclei on r-process nucleosynthesis is discussed briefly.

## Key claim

The paper claims that including first-forbidden (FF) transitions in pn-QRPA calculations for N=126 isotones significantly reduces computed beta-decay half-lives and improves agreement with measured terrestrial half-lives, but this improvement is conditional on using two different pn-QRPA implementations (spherical Woods-Saxon and deformed Nilsson basis) without any explicit quenching factor.

## Method

Two versions of the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) model were employed. The first (pn-QRPA (WS)) used a spherical Woods-Saxon mean-field potential with separable residual interactions in the particle-hole channel, computing allowed Gamow-Teller (GT) and first-forbidden (FF) transitions of rank 0, 1, and 2. The second (pn-QRPA (N)) used a deformed Nilsson basis with both particle-hole and particle-particle GT forces, computing allowed GT and unique first-forbidden (U1F) transitions under terrestrial and stellar conditions for 17 selected N=126 waiting-point nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The particle-hole interaction strength for allowed GT transitions was taken as chi_GT = 5.2*A^0.7 MeV in the pn-QRPA (WS) model
- The particle-hole interaction strength for rank 0, rank 1, and rank 2 FF transitions were chi_rank0 = 30*A^(-5/3) MeV fm^(-2), chi_rank1 = 99*A^(-5/3) MeV fm^(-2), and chi_rank2 = 350*A^(-5/3) MeV fm^(-2), respectively
- The pn-QRPA (N) model used chi = 4.2/A MeV for allowed GT and chi = 56.16/A MeV fm^(-2) for U1F transitions
- The pair correlation function was chosen as C_n = C_p = 12 / sqrt(A) for open shell nuclei
- The effective ratio of axial and vector coupling constants was taken as (g_A/g_V) = -1.254, with D = 6295 seconds
- For 190Gd, allowed GT rates were roughly factor 4 greater than U1F rates at low stellar temperatures (T9 = 0.01-2 GK), while U1F rates exceeded allowed GT rates by up to a factor 66 at higher T9 values

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical calculations of beta-decay half-lives for N=126 isotones, which have shown significant disagreement compared to calculations for N=50 and N=82 isotones (Suzuki 2012). The authors compare their results against several prior approaches: QRPA-FRDM (Moller 2003), continuum QRPA with density functional theory (Borzov 2006, Borzov 2011), shell model calculations (Suzuki 2012), large-scale shell model (Zhi 2013), and an empirical formula (Zhou 2017). The pn-QRPA approach was able to converge states for excitation energies well over 10 MeV, unlike the Lanczos method used in large-scale shell model calculations which was limited to 100 iterations and insufficient for converging states above 2.5 MeV excitation energies. The computed stellar beta-decay rates and beta-delayed neutron emission probabilities for these waiting-point nuclei are intended for use in r-process nucleosynthesis calculations.
