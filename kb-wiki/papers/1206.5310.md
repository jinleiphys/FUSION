# Meson spectroscopy with unitary coupled-channels model for heavy-meson decay into three mesons

> We develop a model for describing excited mesons decay into three mesons. The properties of the excited mesons can be extracted with this model. The model maintains the three-body unitarity that has been missed in previous data analyses based on the conventional isobar models. We study an importance of the three-body unitarity in extracting hadron properties from data. For this purpose, we use the unitary and isobar models to analyze the same pseudo data of gamma p -> pi+pi+pi-n, and extract the properties of excited mesons. We find a significant difference between the unitary and isobar models in the extracted properties of excited mesons, such as the mass, width and coupling strength to decay channels.

## Key claim

The three-body unitarity, which is missing in conventional isobar models, causes significant differences in the extracted properties of excited mesons (mass, width, and coupling strength to decay channels) when analyzing three-meson final states; the effect is particularly large for broad resonances.

## Method

The author constructed a unitary coupled-channels model for the decay of excited mesons (\(M^*\)) into three pions, incorporating Z-diagrams and a coupled-channels (\(\pi\pi, K\bar K\)) description of \(\pi\pi\) scattering solved via a Lippmann-Schwinger equation. The unitary model was used to generate pseudo data (Dalitz plots) for the reaction \(\gamma p \to \pi^+\pi^+\pi^-n\) at \(E_\gamma=5\) GeV, with \(M^*\to \pi R\) couplings fixed by the \(^3P_0\) quark model. The same pseudo data were then fitted with a conventional isobar model (retaining only the middle diagram of the \(M^*\) decay amplitude and using Breit-Wigner functions), and the extracted \(M^*\) properties from both models were compared.

## Key numbers

- Mass of \(a_1(1230)\): 937 MeV (unitary model) vs. 1096 MeV (isobar model); width: 496 MeV (unitary) vs. 684 MeV (isobar).
- Mass of \(a_2(1320)\): 1263 MeV (unitary) vs. 1269 MeV (isobar); width: 42 MeV (unitary) vs. 56 MeV (isobar).
- Mass of \(\pi_1(1600)\): 1599 MeV (unitary) vs. 1599 MeV (isobar); width: 8 MeV (unitary) vs. 8 MeV (isobar).
- Mass of \(\pi_2(1670)\): 1784 MeV (unitary) vs. 1798 MeV (isobar); width: 456 MeV (unitary) vs. 502 MeV (isobar).
- Mass of \(a_1(1700)\): 1658 MeV (unitary) vs. 1687 MeV (isobar); width: 106 MeV (unitary) vs. 99 MeV (isobar).
- Kinematics used for pseudo data generation: \(E_\gamma = 5\) GeV (laboratory frame), \(t = -0.4\) GeV\(^2\), \(0.8\) GeV \(\le W \le 2\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the conventional isobar model approach used in previous data analyses, such as the CLAS Collaboration analysis of \(\gamma p \to \pi^+\pi^+\pi^-n\) (CLAS, 2009) and the planned GlueX study (GlueX, 2012), by incorporating three-body unitarity that those analyses missed. The results show that for broad resonances like \(a_1(1230)\) and \(\pi_2(1670)\), the unitary model yields significantly different masses and widths compared to the isobar model, while narrow states like \(a_2(1320)\) and \(\pi_1(1600)\) are less affected. This enables more reliable extraction of hadron properties from three-meson final states, which is essential for confirming the existence of exotic mesons such as the \(\pi_1(1600)\).
