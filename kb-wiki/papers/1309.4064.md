# Estimates for temperature in projectile like fragment in geometric and transport models

> Projectile like fragments emerging from heavy ion collision have an excitation energy which is often labeled by a temperature. This temperature was recently calculated using a geometric model. We expand the geometric model to include also dynamic effects using a transport model. The temperatures so deduced agree quite well with values of temperature needed to fit experimental data.

# Estimates for temperature in projectile like fragment in geometric and transport models

## Key claim
The temperature of projectile like fragments (PLFs) can be estimated from a transport model (Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck, BUU) and agrees with a simple geometric parametrization T = 7.5 MeV - [A_s/A_0] * 4.5 MeV, but only when the beam energy is high enough for straight-line trajectories to be valid (at least 140 MeV/nucleon and above).

## Method
The authors used a Thomas-Fermi description of nuclear ground states and then performed BUU transport calculations with test particles (100 per nucleon for small systems, 50 per nucleon for large systems) to simulate collisions. For each impact parameter, they identified the PLF by analyzing the density and energy profiles along the beam direction at late times (50-200 fm/c), then computed the excitation energy of the PLF and converted it to a temperature using the canonical thermodynamic model (CTM). They compared results from the full BUU calculation with a simpler geometric model that uses straight-line cuts to define the PLF shape and excitation energy.

## Key numbers
- The geometric model parametrization is T = 7.5 MeV - [A_s/A_0] * 4.5 MeV, where A_s is the PLF mass and A_0 is the projectile mass.
- For 58Ni on 9Be at 140 MeV/nucleon, BUU calculations yield temperatures of order 6 MeV, with a definitive dependence on A_s/A_0 (temperature falling as A_s/A_0 increases).
- For 124Sn on 119Sn at 600 MeV/nucleon, BUU results are very similar to those at 200 MeV/nucleon, consistent with limiting fragmentation.
- The Lenk-Pandharipande mean field parameters used for large systems are A = -2230.0 MeV fm^3, B = 2577.85 MeV fm^(7/2), sigma = 7/6, rho_0 = 0.16 fm^(-3), and c = -6.5 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the geometric model of Mallik et al. (2012, 2013) by adding dynamical effects via BUU transport calculations, confirming that the simple temperature parametrization derived from Sn on Sn data (Ogul et al., 2004) holds for other systems including 58Ni on 9Be and 58Ni on 181Ta. The results support the concept of limiting fragmentation (independence of beam energy above a threshold) and enable more reliable temperature inputs for the canonical thermodynamic model (CTM) used to predict fragment yields in projectile fragmentation experiments.
