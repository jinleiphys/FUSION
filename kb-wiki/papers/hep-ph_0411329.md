# Particle Production in Proton-Proton and Deuteron-Gold Collisions at RHIC

> We try to understand recent data on proton-proton and deuteron-gold collisions at RHIC, employing a parton model approach called EPOS.

## Key claim

The EPOS parton model, which incorporates an effective treatment of high parton densities via Pomeron-Pomeron interactions, can reproduce the nuclear modification factor in deuteron-gold collisions at RHIC, including its rise above unity, provided that the target excitation parameter \(Z_{\mathrm{T}}\) reaches values of 3-4 in central collisions.

## Method

The authors employ the EPOS energy-conserving multiple scattering approach, which treats soft and hard interactions consistently using parton ladders (Pomerons). High parton densities are handled by absorbing multiple ladder interactions into effective projectile and target excitations, parameterized by a variable \(Z\) that quantifies the number of additional partons available for multiple interactions. The excitation mass is distributed as \(1/M^{2(1-\mu)}\), and string parameters such as mean transverse momentum and strangeness suppression depend on \(Z\) via \(f = f_{0} \min(Z_{\max}, 1 + aZ)\).

## Key numbers

- In central deuteron-gold collisions, the target excitation parameter \(Z_{\mathrm{T}} \approx 3-4\); in peripheral d+Au collisions, \(Z_{\mathrm{T}} \approx 0\).
- The saturation parameter for the excitation dependence is \(Z_{\max} \approx 4\) and the slope parameter \(a \approx 1\).
- The nuclear modification factor \(R = AA/pp/N_{\mathrm{coll}}\) in d+Au collisions rises above unity at high transverse momentum, consistent with data from STAR and PHENIX.

## Context

This work extends the NEXUS model by introducing EPOS, a successor that explicitly treats high parton densities via effective Pomeron-Pomeron interactions. The approach enables a unified description of proton-proton and proton-nucleus collisions at RHIC energies, and the results for the nuclear modification factor in d+Au collisions provide a baseline for understanding cold nuclear matter effects, which is essential before interpreting similar measurements in heavy-ion collisions where quark-gluon plasma formation is expected.
