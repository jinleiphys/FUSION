# Timescales of quantum equilibration, dissipation and fluctuation in nuclear collisions

> Understanding the dynamics of equilibration processes in quantum systems as well as their interplay with dissipation and fluctuation is a major challenge in quantum many-body theory. The timescales of such processes are investigated in collisions of atomic nuclei using fully microscopic approaches. Results from time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) and time-dependent random-phase approximation (TDRPA) calculations are compared for 13 systems over a broad range of energies. The timescale for full mass equilibration ($\sim2\times10^{-20}$s) is found to be much larger than timescales for neutron-to-proton equilibration, kinetic energy and angular momentum dissipations which are on the order of $10^{-21}$s. Fluctuations of mass numbers in the fragments and correlations between their neutron and proton numbers build up within only a few $10^{-21}$s. This indicates that dissipation is basically not impacted by mass equilibration, but is mostly driven by the exchange of nucleons between the fragments.

## Key claim

The paper finds that mass equilibration in nuclear collisions is much slower (∼2×10⁻²⁰ s) than neutron-to-proton equilibration, kinetic energy dissipation, and angular momentum dissipation (all ∼10⁻²¹ s), and that fluctuations build up within a few 10⁻²¹ s; the abstract's claim that "dissipation is basically not impacted by mass equilibration" is conditioned on this separation of timescales, and the authors note that the TDRPA fluctuation results are only available for symmetric systems and that "more studies would be welcome" before drawing definitive conclusions about universality.

## Method

The authors performed fully microscopic time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations to obtain average values of mass asymmetry, neutron-proton asymmetry, total kinetic energy, and orbital angular momentum for 13 collision systems over a broad range of energies. Fluctuations of mass numbers and correlations between neutron and proton numbers in the fragments were computed using the time-dependent random-phase approximation (TDRPA) prescription of Balian and Vénéroni. A normalized observable δX(τ) = (X(τ) − X∞)/(X0 − X∞) was defined as a function of contact time τ between the fragments, with contact defined by a neck density exceeding half the saturation density (∼0.08 fm⁻³).

## Key numbers

- Mass equilibration timescale: ∼2×10⁻²⁰ s (∼20 zs) for full symmetry to be reached on average, based on TDHF calculations of systems including ⁴⁰Ca+²³⁸U, ⁴⁸Ca/⁵⁰Ti+²⁴⁹Bk, and ⁵⁴Cr+¹⁸⁶W at energies near the Coulomb capture barrier VB.
- Neutron-to-proton equilibration timescale: δI(τ) approximately zero for τ ∼ 1 zs and above, in agreement with the experimentally determined rate constant of 3 zs⁻¹ from Jedele et al. (2017).
- Kinetic energy dissipation timescale: full dissipation obtained after typically 1–2 zs, from TDHF calculations of systems including ⁶⁰Ni+⁶⁰Ni, ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca, ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁹Bk, ⁷⁸/⁹²Kr+²⁰⁸Pb, ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁸Ca/⁶⁴Ni, ¹⁷⁶Yb+¹⁷⁶Yb, and ⁵⁸Ni+¹²⁴Sn.
- Angular momentum dissipation timescale: most reactions have dissipated their angular momentum within 1 zs, from TDHF calculations of systems including ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca, ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁸Ca/⁶⁴Ni, ⁴⁰Ca+²⁰⁸Pb, ¹⁷⁶Yb+¹⁷⁶Yb, and ⁷⁸/⁹²Kr+²⁰⁸Pb.
- Fluctuation and correlation buildup timescale: mass fluctuations (σA) and neutron-proton correlations (σNZ) build up within about 3 zs, from TDRPA calculations of symmetric systems ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca, ⁶⁰Ni+⁶⁰Ni, and ¹⁷⁶Yb+¹⁷⁶Yb.

## Context

This work extends previous TDHF and TDRPA studies by providing a systematic comparison of timescales across 13 systems, confirming the separation between slow mass equilibration and fast dissipation/fluctuation processes seen in earlier calculations by Oberacker et al. (2014), Umar et al. (2016), Godbey et al. (2019), Hammerton et al. (2015), Simenel et al. (2011, 2012b), Williams et al. (2018), Wu et al. (2019), and others. The results enable future studies of the interplay between equilibration and dissipation in asymmetric systems using beyond-TDRPA models such as the stochastic mean-field approach, and support the interpretation that multinucleon transfer (rather than mass equilibration) is the primary dissipative mechanism in heavy-ion collisions.
