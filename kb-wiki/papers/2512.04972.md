# Internal superfluid response and torque evolution in the giant glitch of PSR J1718-3718

> We investigate the post-glitch rotational evolution of pulsars by analyzing the 2007 giant glitch of PSR J1718$-$3718 using a vortex creep model that incorporates both inward and outward nonlinear vortex motion, along with a time-varying external torque. A comprehensive fitting framework is developed, constrained by prior knowledge of moment of inertia participation from previous glitch studies. We apply a Markov Chain Monte Carlo approach to quantify uncertainties and parameter correlations. The model reproduces the observed timing data and yields physically consistent values for moment of inertia fractions and creep timescales. Our results indicate that inward creep and a long-term change in external torque dominate the observed increase in spin-down rate, pointing to structural changes within the star-likely triggered by a crustquake that initiated both vortex motion and a change in the moment of inertia. We estimate that the glitch involved approximately $2.4 \times 10^{12}$ inward-moving vortices and $\sim 142$ crustal plates with a typical size of $\sim 0.03$ km. This study demonstrates that detailed post-glitch modeling of sparse timing data can simultaneously constrain internal superfluid dynamics and external torque evolution, providing a quantitative framework to probe the structural properties of neutron star interiors.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the anomalous post-glitch increase in spin-down rate of PSR J1718-3718 can be explained by a combination of inward vortex creep and a long-term change in external torque, likely triggered by a crustquake; this interpretation depends on the assumption that the standard vortex creep model (which only includes outward vortex motion) is insufficient to explain the observed behavior.

## Method

The authors analyzed 17 years of timing data from the Parkes radio telescope for PSR J1718-3718, fitting the post-glitch rotational evolution using a vortex creep model that incorporates both inward and outward nonlinear vortex motion along with a time-varying external torque. They applied a Markov Chain Monte Carlo approach using the emcee package to quantify uncertainties and parameter correlations, with uniform priors and a Gaussian likelihood, and imposed a constraint that the total moment of inertia involved in the glitch does not exceed 7% of the crustal moment of inertia.

## Key numbers

- Glitch fractional change in spin frequency: Delta-nu/nu = 33.293(3) x 10^{-6}
- Fractional change in spin-down rate: Delta-dot-nu/dot-nu = 7.1(3) x 10^{-3}
- Glitch epoch: MJD 54620(240)
- Number of inward-moving vortices: approximately 2.4 x 10^{12}
- Number of crustal plates involved: approximately 142
- Typical plate size: approximately 0.03 km
- Outward creep fractional moment of inertia: log10(I_nl/I) = -2.616 (+0.139, -0.113)
- Inward creep fractional moment of inertia: log10(I_i/I) = -2.156 (+0.476, -0.451)
- Outward creep relaxation timescale: log10(tau_nl) = 2.189 (+0.089, -0.077) days
- Inward creep relaxation timescale: log10(tau_i) = 3.482 (+0.537, -0.392) days
- Minimum stellar moment of inertia change: |Delta I/I| > 1.452 x 10^{-5}
- Energy dissipation due to crust breaking: greater than 2.753 x 10^{41} erg

## Context

This work extends the vortex creep model originally developed by Alpar and collaborators (1984, 1989) to include inward vortex motion, following the approach applied to PSR J1119-6127 by Akbal and Alpar (2015). The study demonstrates that detailed post-glitch modeling of sparse timing data can simultaneously constrain internal superfluid dynamics and external torque evolution, providing a quantitative framework to probe the structural properties of neutron star interiors. The results suggest that the breaking strain of PSR J1718-3718's crust is larger than that inferred from the largest Crab glitch studied by G\"uver and collaborators (2019).
