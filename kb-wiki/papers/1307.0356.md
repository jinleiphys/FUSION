# Equilibration of anisotropic quark-gluon plasma produced by decays of color flux tubes

> A set of kinetic equations is used to study equilibration of the anisotropic quark-gluon plasma produced by decays of color flux tubes possibly created at the very early stages of ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions. The decay rates of the initial color fields are given by the Schwinger formula, and the collision terms are treated in the relaxation-time approximation. By connecting the relaxation time with viscosity we are able to study production and thermalization processes in the plasma characterized by different values of the ratio of the shear viscosity to entropy density, eta/s. For the lowest (KSS) value of this ratio, 4 pi eta/s = 1, and realistic initial conditions for the fields, the system approaches the viscous-hydrodynamics regime within 1-2 fm/c. On the other hand, for larger values of the viscosity, 4 pi eta/s greater or equal to 3, the collisions in the plasma become inefficient to destroy collective phenomena which manifest themselves as oscillations of different plasma parameters. The presence of such oscillations brings in differences between the kinetic and hydrodynamic descriptions, which suggest that the viscous-hydrodynamics approach after 1-2 fm/c is not complete if 4 pi eta/s is greater or equal to 3 and should be extended to include dissipative phenomena connected with color conductivity.

## Key claim

The paper claims that for the lowest (KSS) value of the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio, \(4\pi\bar{\eta}=1\), the anisotropic quark-gluon plasma produced by color flux tube decays approaches the viscous-hydrodynamics regime within 1–2 fm/c, but for larger values (\(4\pi\bar{\eta}\geq 3\)) the plasma exhibits persistent oscillations that make the viscous-hydrodynamic description incomplete, requiring extension to include color conductivity.

## Method

The authors solve a set of boost-invariant kinetic equations for quarks, antiquarks, and gluons, where particle production from the decay of a longitudinal chromoelectric field is given by the Schwinger formula and collision terms are treated in the relaxation-time approximation. The relaxation time is connected to the shear viscosity via \(\tau_{\rm eq}=5\bar{\eta}/T\), and the field evolution is governed by a Maxwell equation including both conductive and displacement currents. The coupled integro-differential equations are solved iteratively for different fixed values of \(4\pi\bar{\eta}\) (1, 3, and 10), with initial field strengths chosen to yield effective temperatures \(T_{\rm max}\sim 300\)–\(500\) MeV.

## Key numbers

- For \(4\pi\bar{\eta}=1\) (the KSS bound), the system approaches the viscous-hydrodynamics regime within 1–2 fm/c.
- For \(4\pi\bar{\eta}\geq 3\), collisions become inefficient to destroy collective oscillations, and the viscous-hydrodynamic description after 1–2 fm/c is not complete.
- The values of \(4\pi\bar{\eta}\) studied are 1, 3, and 10; the range \(1\leq 4\pi\bar{\eta}\leq 3\) is extracted from hydrodynamic analyses of RHIC and LHC data, while \(4\pi\bar{\eta}=10\) is expected from leading-log perturbative results.
- The initial chromoelectric field is set using string tension \(\sigma_q=1\) GeV/fm (\(\sigma_g=3\) GeV/fm), tube area \(\pi r^2=1\) fm\(^2\), and color charge numbers \(k=5\) and \(k=10\).
- The effective temperature of the produced plasma reaches \(T_{\rm max}\sim 300\)–\(500\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier color-flux-tube and transport models (e.g., Bialas (1984), Gyulassy (1986), Elze (1986), Banerjee (1989)) by using a time-dependent relaxation time linked to viscosity and by separately calculating longitudinal and transverse pressures to study the approach to hydrodynamics. It contrasts with viscous-hydrodynamic models (e.g., Baier (2006), Romatschke (2007), Song (2008), Schenke (2010)) by showing that for higher viscosities (\(4\pi\bar{\eta}\geq 3\)) the kinetic description yields oscillations not captured by standard viscous hydrodynamics, suggesting the need for models incorporating color conductivity. The framework also goes beyond the earlier work of Florkowski (2012) by solving the coupled kinetic and Maxwell equations self-consistently.
