# Neutron drip line and the equation of state of nuclear matter

> We investigate how the neutron drip line is related to the density dependence of the symmetry energy, by using a macroscopic nuclear model that allows us to calculate nuclear masses in a way dependent on the equation of state of asymmetric nuclear matter. The neutron drip line obtained from these masses is shown to appreciably shift to a neutron-rich side in a nuclear chart as the density derivative of the symmetry energy increases. Such shift is clearly seen for light nuclei, a feature coming mainly from the surface property of neutron-rich nuclei.

## Key claim

The neutron drip line shifts appreciably to a neutron-rich side as the density derivative of the symmetry energy (parameter L) increases, an effect that is clearer for lighter nuclei and arises mainly from the surface property of neutron-rich nuclei through the density and neutron excess dependence of the surface tension; the authors note that even-odd corrections could be comparable in magnitude to the change in separation energy due to uncertainties in L, implying smoothing would be required to extract EOS information from future empirical data.

## Method

The authors used a macroscopic nuclear model that calculates nuclear masses as a function of the equation of state of asymmetric nuclear matter, with the bulk energy per nucleon expressed in terms of potential energy densities for symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter. They extremized the binding energy with respect to parametrized neutron and proton density distributions for fixed mass number A, then fitted the remaining EOS parameters and gradient coefficient to empirical data for stable nuclei on the smoothed beta stability line. The neutron drip line was drawn by identifying nuclides where one-neutron and two-neutron separation energies become negative, evaluated for various values of the density symmetry coefficient L and incompressibility K0.

## Key numbers

- The density symmetry coefficient L was varied over the range 0 < L < 160 MeV, with two extreme EOS models used: EOS C (L = 146 MeV, K0 = 360 MeV) and EOS G (L = 5.7 MeV, K0 = 180 MeV).
- The saturation density n0 was constrained to 0.14–0.17 fm^{-3}, the saturation energy w0 to -16 ± 1 MeV, the symmetry energy S0 to 25–40 MeV, and the gradient coefficient F0 to 66 ± 6 MeV fm^5.
- A linear relation S0 ≈ B + CL was obtained from fitting, with B ≈ 28 MeV and C ≈ 0.075, nearly independent of K0.
- The rms deviations of calculated masses from measured values were about 3 MeV, comparable to deviations from a Weizsacker-Bethe mass formula.
- The typical neutron excess α along the neutron drip line was of order 0.35–0.4.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous macroscopic nuclear model (Oyamatsu and Iida, 2003) and their demonstration that nuclear size and shape are controlled by the equation of state through the density dependence of the symmetry energy (Oyamatsu and Iida, 2007). It builds on the finding that masses of extremely neutron-rich nuclei depend appreciably on L, understood from the density and isospin dependence of the surface tension (Oyamatsu and Iida, 2010). The results enable experimental constraints on the density dependence of the symmetry energy from future measurements of the neutron drip line at radioactive ion beam facilities, and are relevant to understanding nuclei in neutron star crusts that control thermal and electric transport properties.
