# Measurement of the Spectral Shape of the beta-decay of 137Xe to the Ground State of 137Cs in EXO-200 and Comparison with Theory

> We report on a comparison between the theoretically predicted and experimentally measured spectra of the first-forbidden non-unique $β$-decay transition $^{137}\textrm{Xe}(7/2^-)\to\,^{137}\textrm{Cs}(7/2^+)$. The experimental data were acquired by the EXO-200 experiment during a deployment of an AmBe neutron source. The ultra-low background environment of EXO-200, together with dedicated source deployment and analysis procedures, allowed for collection of a pure sample of the decays, with an estimated signal-to-background ratio of more than 99-to-1 in the energy range from 1075 to 4175 keV. In addition to providing a rare and accurate measurement of the first-forbidden non-unique $β$-decay shape, this work constitutes a novel test of the calculated electron spectral shapes in the context of the reactor antineutrino anomaly and spectral bump.

# Measurement of the Spectral Shape of the beta-decay of 137Xe to the Ground State of 137Cs in EXO-200 and Comparison with Theory

## Key claim
The measured spectral shape of the first-forbidden non-unique beta-decay transition 137Xe(7/2-) to 137Cs(7/2+) agrees well with theoretical predictions, and this transition has no significant dependence on the adopted value of the weak axial coupling g_A or the magnitude of mesonic enhancement effects, making it an ideal test case for validating beta spectra calculations relevant to the reactor antineutrino anomaly and spectral bump.

## Method
The EXO-200 experiment deployed an AmBe neutron source to activate 136Xe via neutron capture, producing 137Xe. The measurement used repeated "activation-decay" cycles where the source was retracted during decay phases to minimize gamma-ray backgrounds, collecting 8.73 hours of livetime. Single-site (SS) events from ground-state decays were selected with energy cuts between 1075 and 4175 keV, and the observed spectrum was compared to Monte Carlo simulations based on theoretical calculations using the shell model code NuShellX@MSU with the jj56pnb effective Hamiltonian. Background contributions from 135Xe, 64Cu, two-neutrino double beta decay, and 40K were subtracted, yielding a final sample of 4526 events with a signal-to-background ratio exceeding 99-to-1.

## Key numbers
- Signal-to-background ratio: more than 99-to-1 in the energy range from 1075 to 4175 keV
- Half-life of 137Xe: 3.818 +/- 0.013 minutes (known value); best-fit from data: 3.81 +/- 0.15 minutes
- Q-value of 137Xe ground-state decay: 4175 keV (used for high energy cut)
- Integrated livetime: 8.73 hours
- Final selected dataset: 4526 events after background subtraction
- Residual background contributions: 22.7(5) events from 64Cu and 0.50(2) events from 135Xe
- Expected low-background events: 43 two-neutrino double beta and 7.8 40K events (approximately 1.1% of all SS events)
- Energy calibration residuals: typically within +/- 0.5%, not exceeding +/- 1%
- Reduced chi-squared of fit between data and theory: shown in Figure 5 (value not explicitly stated in text)
- All normalized residuals within +/- 2 sigma statistical error

## Context
This work extends the limited set of precisely measured first-forbidden beta-decay spectra in the medium-mass A=89-143 region, where only a handful of electron spectra for J+ to J- transitions had been measured with poor precision (Daniel 1968, Booij 1971). The measurement tests the theoretical framework of Hayen et al. (2019a, 2019b), which proposes corrections to the traditional Huber-Mueller model (Mueller 2011) that could explain the reactor antineutrino anomaly and the spectral bump observed by RENO (Ahn 2012), Double Chooz (Abe 2014), and Daya Bay (An 2016). The results validate the calculated spectral shapes for first-forbidden non-unique decays, suggesting that the spectral bump and flux anomaly could be at least partially explained by the exact spectral shapes of abundant first-forbidden non-unique beta decays of fission fragments in nuclear reactors.
