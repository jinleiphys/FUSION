# Spin observables in nucleon-deuteron scattering and three-nucleon forces

> Three-nucleon forces, which compose an up-to-date subject in few-nucleon systems, provide a good account of the triton binding energy and the cross section minimum in proton-deuteron elastic scattering, while do not succeed in explaining spin observables such as the nucleon and deuteron analyzing powers, suggesting serious defects in their spin dependence. We study the spin structure of nucleon-deuteron elastic amplitudes by decomposing them into spin-space tensors and examine effects of three-nucleon forces to each component of the amplitudes obtained by solving the Faddeev equation. Assuming that the spin-scalar amplitudes dominate the others, we derive simple expressions for spin observables in the nucleon-deuteron elastic scattering. The expressions suggest that a particular combination of spin observables in the scattering provides direct information of scalar, vector, or tensor component of the three-nucleon forces. These effects are numerically investigated by the Faddeev calculation.

## Key claim

Three-nucleon forces (3NFs) successfully resolve the underbinding of the triton and the cross-section minimum in proton-deuteron elastic scattering, but they fail to explain spin observables such as nucleon and deuteron analyzing powers; the authors propose that specific linear combinations of spin observables can isolate scalar, vector, or tensor components of the 3NF, thereby enabling targeted tests of the spin-dependent parts of the force.

## Method

The authors decompose the nucleon-deuteron elastic scattering T-matrix into spin-space tensors of rank 0 (scalar), 1 (vector), 2 (tensor), and 3 (third-rank tensor) using the invariant amplitude method. They solve the Faddeev equation in coordinate space with the Argonne V18 two-nucleon force (AV18) and include the Brazil model (BR) 2π-exchange 3NF, a Gaussian central 3NF (GS), and a spin-orbit 3NF (SO). At an incident energy of 3 MeV, they apply a scalar-amplitude-dominance (SAD) approximation, neglecting second-order terms of vector and tensor amplitudes, to derive simple expressions for spin observables, then compare these with exact Faddeev calculations.

## Key numbers

- Triton binding energy: AV18 alone gives 7.51 MeV; AV18+BR gives 8.44 MeV; AV18+GS gives 8.48 MeV (empirical value); AV18+BR+SO gives 8.39 MeV.
- Incident energy for all scattering calculations: \(E_N = 3\) MeV (below deuteron breakup threshold).
- For the GS-3NF, parameters are \(r_G = 1.0\) fm and \(V_0^G = -45\) MeV; for the SO-3NF, parameters are \(\alpha = 1.5\) fm\(^{-1}\) and \(W_0 = -20\) MeV.
- The unpolarized differential cross section in the SAD approximation is \(N_R = |U_1|^2 + |U_3|^2\); differences between full and SAD cross sections are less than 1%.
- The amplitude \(U_3\) (quartet scalar) is much larger than \(U_1\) (doublet scalar); the vector amplitude \(S_3\) (quartet) is much larger than \(S_1\) (doublet) and \(S_2\) (doublet-quartet transition).
- The combination \(A_y + \frac{4}{\sqrt{3}} iT_{11}\) enhances the contribution of the vector amplitude \(S_3\); the combination \(A_y - \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} iT_{11}\) enhances the contribution of \(S_2\).
- The quantities \(-\frac{1}{3}(C_{x,x}+C_{y,y}+C_{z,z}-1)\sigma(\theta)\) and \(\frac{1}{3}(C_{x,x}+C_{y,y}+C_{z,z}+2)\sigma(\theta)\) give \(\frac{1}{6}|U_1|^2\) and \(\frac{1}{6}|U_3|^2\), respectively, in the SAD approximation.

## Context

This work extends the authors’ previous analysis of forward-scattering total cross sections (Ishikawa et al., 2001) to finite scattering angles, using the same spin-space tensor decomposition. It addresses the long-standing discrepancy that 3NFs like the Brazil model (Coelho et al., 1983) improve the cross-section minimum but fail to fix spin observables such as the vector analyzing powers, as noted by several groups (e.g., Sagara et al., 1994; Shimizu et al., 1995; Witala et al., 2002). The proposed linear combinations of observables enable separate experimental tests of the scalar, vector, and tensor components of 3NFs, which could guide the development of more complete three-nucleon force models.
