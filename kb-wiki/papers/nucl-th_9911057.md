# Statistical Theory of Parity Nonconservation in Compound Nuclei

> We present the first application of statistical spectroscopy to study the root-mean-square value of the parity nonconserving (PNC) interaction matrix element M determined experimentally by scattering longitudinally polarized neutrons from compound nuclei. Our effective PNC interaction consists of a standard two-body meson-exchange piece and a doorway term to account for spin-flip excitations. Strength functions are calculated using realistic single-particle energies and a residual strong interaction adjusted to fit the experimental density of states for the targets, ^{238} U for A\sim 230 and ^{104,105,106,108} Pd for A\sim 100. Using the standard Desplanques, Donoghue, and Holstein estimates of the weak PNC meson-nucleon coupling constants, we find that M is about a factor of 3 smaller than the experimental value for ^{238} U and about a factor of 1.7 smaller for Pd. The significance of this result for refining the empirical determination of the weak coupling constants is discussed.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first application of statistical spectroscopy to calculate the root-mean-square parity nonconserving (PNC) interaction matrix element M in compound nuclei, finding that using the standard Desplanques, Donoghue, and Holstein (DDH) estimates of weak meson-nucleon coupling constants yields M values about a factor of 3 smaller than experiment for 238U and about a factor of 1.7 smaller for Pd, indicating that the DDH coupling parameters require further refinement to explain the TRIPLE measurements.

## Method

The authors developed a statistical spectroscopy framework for parity violation in compound nuclei, combining an effective PNC interaction consisting of a standard two-body meson-exchange piece and a doorway term for 0- spin-flip excitations. They calculated strength functions using realistic single-particle energies from a Woods-Saxon potential and a residual strong interaction adjusted to reproduce experimental level densities for 238U (A~230) and 104,105,106,108Pd (A~100). The statistical approach uses bivariate Gaussian strength densities characterized by centroids, variances, and correlation coefficients derived from configuration moments, with the model space chosen to include single-particle orbitals within the spreading width (approximately 2-3 MeV) where chaotic mixing occurs.

## Key numbers

- For 238U (A~230): the calculated M is about a factor of 3 smaller than the experimental value using DDH "best" coupling constants
- For Pd isotopes (A~100): the calculated M is about a factor of 1.7 smaller than the experimental value using DDH "best" coupling constants
- The mean spacing between neutron resonances in the compound nuclei is on the order of 10-100 eV
- The strong spreading width of states is on the order of 2 MeV
- Typical correlation coefficients xi in medium and heavy nuclei are approximately 0.7-0.9
- Woods-Saxon potential parameters used: r0=1.27 fm, a=0.67 fm, V=(-51+33*tau_z*(N-Z)/A) MeV, V_ls=-0.44V

## Context

This work extends statistical spectroscopy methods, previously successful for positive parity operators, to the study of parity violation in heavy compound nuclei, addressing the TRIPLE experimental data from Garvey (1990s). The approach contrasts with nonstatistical shell-model calculations such as those by Haxton (1990s) for light nuclei, where individual matrix elements are calculable but suffer from strong cancellations and model dependence. The results enable future refinement of the weak meson-nucleon coupling constants by providing benchmark calculations that separate statistical mixing within the model space from nonstatistical effects (including 1-hbar-omega collectivity from giant resonances) treated through effective interaction theory.
