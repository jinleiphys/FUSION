# Statistical fluctuations for the fission process on its decent from saddle to scission

> We reconsider the importance of statistical fluctuations for fission dynamics beyond the saddle in the light of recent evaluations of transport coefficients for average motion. The size of these fluctuations are estimated by means of the Kramers-Ingold solution for the inverted oscillator, which allows for an inclusion of quantum effects.

## Key claim

The paper argues that statistical fluctuations in fission dynamics beyond the saddle are much more important than previously recognized, and that quantum effects in the momentum distribution can significantly increase both the average kinetic energy and its variance at scission, especially for large damping.

## Method

The authors use the Kramers-Ingold stationary solution for an inverted oscillator to model the fission path from saddle to scission, with transport coefficients (inertia M, friction γ, stiffness C) treated as constant along the path. They compute the average kinetic energy and its variance at the scission point using analytic expressions derived from this solution, incorporating quantum diffusion coefficients via the fluctuation-dissipation theorem with Drude regularization.

## Key numbers

- The difference in potential energy between saddle and scission is taken as ΔV = 20 MeV.
- The critical temperature Tc below which the quantum description breaks down is of order 0.5 MeV or less, decreasing with increasing damping.
- For T/ħϖ > 0.5, quantum modifications to the saddle-to-scission time can be safely neglected.
- The mean saddle-to-scission time τ₀ lies in the range of 1–2 × 10⁻²¹ s.
- For large damping and large temperatures, the ratio σ/E_kin approaches the limiting value √(2/3).
- Changing the Drude cutoff frequency ω_D by a factor of two modifies final results by less than 30%.
- For η=10, using equipartition to estimate initial kinetic energy overestimates τ₀ by about 50%; for η=1, the overestimate is about 10%.

## Context

This work extends the classic Kramers (1940) transport equation description of fission dynamics to include quantum effects, building on the locally harmonic approximation developed by Hofmann and collaborators (1990s). It contrasts with earlier trajectory calculations that neglect fluctuating forces, showing they can grossly underestimate kinetic energy. The results enable improved interpretation of experiments on fission accompanied by GDR γ-rays (Pauthoe, 1990s) and light particle evaporation (Jolest, 1990s), and suggest that macroscopic estimates of damping (η ≈ 7.5 for ²²⁴Th at T=2 MeV from the wall formula) imply quantum effects must be included.
