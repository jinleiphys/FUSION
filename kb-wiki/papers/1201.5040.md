# Measuring the phase of the scattering amplitude with vortex beams

> We show that colliding vortex beams instead of (approximate) plane waves can lead to a direct measurement of how the overall phase of the plane wave scattering amplitude changes with the scattering angle. Since vortex beams are coherent superpositions of plane waves with different momenta, their scattering amplitude receives contributions from plane wave amplitudes with distinct kinematics. These contributions interfere, leading to the measurement of their phase difference. Although interference exists for any generic wave packet collision, we show that using vortex beams dramatically enhances sensitivity to the phase in comparison with non-vortex beams. Since the overall phase is inaccessible in a plane wave collision, this measurement would be of great importance for a number of topics in hadronic physics, for example, meson production in the resonance region, physics of nucleon resonances, and small angle elastic hadron scattering.

## Key claim

Colliding two coaxial Bessel vortex beams (rather than plane waves) can directly measure how the overall phase of the plane-wave scattering amplitude changes with the scattering angle, because the vortex-beam scattering amplitude is a coherent sum of two plane-wave amplitudes with slightly different initial momenta whose interference reveals their relative phase. The paper shows that using vortex beams dramatically enhances sensitivity to this phase compared to any generic non-vortex wave packet, but the absolute magnitude of the phase remains unmeasurable.

## Method

The authors develop the theory of scattering for two coaxial Bessel vortex beams (pure Bessel states and transverse Bessel wave packets) in a 2→2 process, assuming monochromatic, paraxial beams in pure states. They compute the generalized cross section by integrating the plane-wave S-matrix element weighted by the momentum-space wave functions of the vortex states, which contain delta-functions in transverse momentum. For a given total transverse momentum K, exactly two kinematic configurations of the initial transverse momenta contribute coherently, producing an interference term proportional to the cosine of the sum of the orbital helicity phases and the difference of the plane-wave scattering amplitudes' phases. The analysis uses the paraxial approximation and treats the finite transverse extent of realistic beams via a weight function with finite width.

## Key numbers

- The transverse momentum K of the two initial Bessel states lies in the ring |ϰ₁ − ϰ₂| ≤ |K| ≤ ϰ₁ + ϰ₂, where ϰ₁ and ϰ₂ are the moduli of the transverse momenta of the two beams.
- For pure Bessel states, the interference term contains a factor 1/(2Δ) where Δ = (1/2)ϰ₁ϰ₂ sin(δ₁ + δ₂) is the area of the triangle with sides ϰ₁, ϰ₂, |K|.
- At the end points of the K region (where Δ = 0), the contribution from the inner region is suppressed as 1/log(ϰR) for macroscopic quantization radius R, but for realistic R this logarithm is only ∼50, so the inner region still contributes.
- For transverse Bessel wave packets with width σ, the regularization parameter becomes 1/σ instead of R, and the logarithmic factor log(ϰ/σ) is not large, giving comparable contributions from end points and central region.
- In the illustrative plots, ϰ₁ = 1 and ϰ₂ = 0.5 (arbitrary units), with σ = ϰ₀/50 and σ = ϰ₀/5 shown.

## Context

This work extends the formalism of double-twisted scattering introduced by Ivanov (2011) and Serbo (2011), which showed that vortex-beam collisions can probe observables inaccessible in plane-wave collisions. The proposal directly addresses the long-standing problem of the overall phase ambiguity in hadronic physics, such as in meson photoproduction data analysis and baryonic resonance spectroscopy (e.g., γp → K⁺Λ), where the phase cannot be measured via Coulomb-hadronic interference or partial-wave analysis alone. The method enables future experiments using vortex beams of photons (generated via Compton backscattering of twisted optical photons, as proposed by Serbo (2011)) or protons (created via fork diffraction gratings or vortex transfer from electron beams, as proposed by Ivanov and Karlovets (2011)) to measure the angular dependence of the scattering amplitude's phase, which is crucial for disentangling interfering partial waves in resonance physics and for constraining Pomeron models in high-energy elastic hadron scattering.
