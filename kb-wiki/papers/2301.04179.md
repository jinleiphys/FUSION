# Scattering Amplitude from Quantum Computing with Reduction Formula

> Utilizing the Lehmann-Symanzik-Zimmermann reduction formula, we present a new general framework for computing scattering amplitudes in quantum field theory with quantum computers in a fully nonperturbative way. In this framework, one only has to construct one-particle states of zero momentum, and no wave packets of incoming particles are needed. The framework is able to incorporate scatterings of bound states, and is ideal for scatterings involving a small number of particles. We expect this framework to have particular advantages when applied to exclusive hadron scatterings. As a proof of concept, by simulations on classical hardware, we demonstrate that in the one-flavor Gross-Neveu model, the fermion propagator, the connected fermion four-point function, and the propagator of a fermion-antifermion bound state obtained from our proposed quantum algorithm have the desired pole structure crucial to the implementation of the Lehmann-Symanzik-Zimmermann reduction formula.

## Key claim
The authors propose a quantum computing framework for computing scattering amplitudes nonperturbatively using the Lehmann-Symanzik-Zimmermann (LSZ) reduction formula, which avoids the need to prepare spatially separated wave packets of incoming particles. The framework is presented as ideal for processes with a small number of external particles (e.g., 2-to-2 scatterings) and can incorporate bound states, but the authors note that the computational complexity scales exponentially with the number of external particles \(n\), unlike the polynomial scaling of the Jordan-Lee-Preskill formalism.

## Method
The method uses the LSZ reduction formula to express scattering amplitudes in terms of connected \(n\)-point correlation functions, propagators, and field normalization factors, all evaluated in momentum space. The authors propose a quantum algorithm that first prepares the interacting vacuum and zero-momentum one-particle states using the quantum alternating operator ansatz (QAOA) and a quantum-number-resolving variational quantum eigensolver, then evaluates the required correlation functions via time evolution and Fourier transforms. As a proof of concept, the algorithm is simulated on classical hardware for the (1+1)-dimensional one-flavor Gross-Neveu model, using 14 qubits on seven lattice sites and seven temporal sites, with the temporal spacing equal to the lattice spacing.

## Key numbers
- Quark mass from exact diagonalization: \(m_h a = 1.18\) (from the discretized Hamiltonian of the Gross-Neveu model).
- Bound-state mass from exact diagonalization: \(m_{h_O} a = 1.96\) (quark-antiquark bound state in the same model).
- Bare parameters used in the simulation: \(m_q a = 0.84\) and \(g = 0.40\).
- Number of qubits used in the classical simulation: 14 qubits (seven lattice sites).
- Number of temporal sites used: seven temporal sites.
- Peak of the real part of the quark propagator trace \(\operatorname{Tr}K_\psi(p)\) at \(p^0 a = \pm 1.14\) (corresponding to the quark pole).
- Zero of the imaginary part of the quark propagator trace at \(p^0 a = \pm 1.28\) (close to \(\pm m_h a\)).
- Peak of the real part of the connected four-point function \(G^{\alpha\beta\alpha\beta}_\psi\) at \(k_1^0 a = \pm 1.01\) (quark pole).
- Zero of the imaginary part of the connected four-point function at \(k_1^0 a = \pm 0.87\).
- Peak of the real part of the bound-state propagator trace \(\operatorname{Tr}K_O(p)\) at \(p^0 a = \pm 2.02\) (bound-state pole).
- Zero of the imaginary part of the bound-state propagator trace at \(p^0 a = \pm 1.89\).

## Context
This work extends the quantum-computational approach to scattering amplitudes pioneered by Jordan, Lee, and Preskill (2012, 2014), which required preparing wave packets and adiabatically turning on/off the coupling constant. The proposed LSZ-based framework avoids those requirements and is particularly suited for bound-state scatterings, such as 2-to-2 pion or nucleon scatterings in quantum chromodynamics, where first-principle calculations are currently limited to energies below three-hadron thresholds (Luscher, 1990; Briceno, 2017; Andersen, 2018). The authors note that a convergent result for the full scattering amplitude in the Gross-Neveu model was not achieved in their classical simulation due to limited qubits, but they expect it to be feasible on a quantum computer with more than a hundred qubits.
