# New insights into antikaon-nucleon scattering and the structure of the Lambda(1405)

> We perform a combined analysis of antikaon-nucleon scattering cross sections and the recent SIDDHARTA kaonic hydrogen data in the framework of a coupled-channel Bethe-Salpeter approach at next-to-leading order in the chiral expansion of the effective potential. We find a precise description of the antikaon-proton scattering amplitudes and are able to extract accurate values of the scattering lengths, a0=-1.81^+0.30_-0.28 + i 0.92^+0.29_-0.23 fm, a1=+0.48^+0.12_-0.11 + i 0.87^+0.26_-0.20 fm. We also discuss the two-pole structure of the Lambda(1405).

## Key claim

The paper claims that a precise description of antikaon-nucleon scattering amplitudes and accurate scattering lengths can be obtained using a coupled-channel Bethe-Salpeter approach at next-to-leading order in chiral perturbation theory, but this result is conditional on including the full off-shell dependence of the interaction kernel and a novel procedure to enforce analyticity, which moves unphysical poles away from the real axis without manual intervention.

To preserve analyticity, they minimized a combined chi-squared that included both a data term and a dispersion-relation term that penalizes solutions producing poles on the first Riemann sheet.

## Context

This work extends the Bethe-Salpeter framework previously applied to pion-nucleon s-wave scattering by Bruns (2010) to the antikaon-nucleon system. It improves upon earlier studies by Borasoy (2006) and Ikeda (2011, 2012) by using a Bethe-Salpeter equation without on-shell approximation for intermediate meson-baryon states and by implementing a systematic procedure to enforce analyticity. The inclusion of the precise SIDDHARTA kaonic hydrogen data from Bazzi (2011) allows for much smaller errors on the scattering lengths, particularly for the isospin I=1 amplitude. The analysis reveals that the second pole of the Lambda(1405) has a larger real part than typically obtained in other determinations, which the authors trace to differences in fitting strategies and the treatment of next-to-leading order low-energy constants.
