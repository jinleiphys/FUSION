# Neutron Star Crust Can Support A Large Ellipticity

> Non-axisymmetrical deformations of the crust on rapidly rotating neutron stars are one of the main targets of searches for continuous gravitational waves. The maximum ellipticity, or fractional difference in moments of inertia, that can be supported by deformations of the crust (known as "mountains") provides an important upper limit on the strength of these continuous gravitational wave sources. We use the formalism of Gittins et al 2021, along with a deforming force that acts mainly in the transverse direction, to obtain a maximum ellipticity of 7.4$\times$10$^{-6}$. This is larger than the original results of Gittins et al 2021 but consistent with earlier calculations by Ushomirsky et al 2000. This suggests that rotating neutron stars could be strong sources of continuous gravitational waves.

## Key claim
The maximum ellipticity a neutron star crust can support is at least 7.4×10⁻⁶, which is larger than the recent estimate by Gittins et al. (2021) but consistent with earlier calculations by Ushomirsky et al. (2000), provided the deforming force is carefully chosen to avoid breaking the weak outer crust and instead strain the strong inner crust primarily in the transverse direction.

## Method
The authors used the formalism of Gittins et al. (2021) to solve the perturbed elastic Euler equation and Poisson equation for a 1.4 M☉, 10 km neutron star with an n=1 polytropic equation of state and a shear modulus μ = κρ with κ = 10¹⁶ cm² s⁻². They applied a deforming force density that acts mainly in the transverse direction, with an additional radial component turned on smoothly near the base of the crust (r_c = 9.99 km) to satisfy the boundary condition T₁E(R₂) = 0 at the crust-ocean interface. The force amplitude was adjusted until the crust broke at a single point according to the von-Mises stress criterion with breaking strain σ̄_br = 0.1, and the maximum ellipticity was computed as the absolute difference between the ellipticities of a fluid-solid star and a completely fluid star deformed by the same force.

## Key numbers
- Maximum ellipticity of 7.4×10⁻⁶ for the force f_i = B ρ r ∇_i Y_lm + C Θ(r - r_c) ρ (r - r_c)² ∇_i r Y_lm (Equation 6 in the paper).
- The Ushomirsky et al. (2000) formalism gives a maximum ellipticity of 2.8×10⁻⁶ for the same 1.4 M☉, 10 km neutron star.
- The maximum ellipticity from the Gittins et al. (2021) tidal-like force (f_i = -A ρ ∇_i(r² Y_lm)) is only 2.2×10⁻⁸.
- The maximum ellipticity from the force f_i = B ρ r ∇_i Y_lm (without the radial piece) is 6.9×10⁻⁷.
- The maximum quadrupole moment from the Ushomirsky et al. (2000) approach is Q₂₂^max ≈ 2.2×10³⁹ g cm² for a breaking strain of 0.1.

## Context
This work extends the formalism of Gittins et al. (2021) by demonstrating that their earlier small ellipticity estimates (as low as 10⁻⁸) were due to the specific choice of deforming forces, not a fundamental limitation of the crust. The results reconcile the Gittins et al. (2021) approach with the earlier calculations of Ushomirsky et al. (2000), who obtained 2.8×10⁻⁶ using the Cowling approximation. The authors note that relaxing the Cowling approximation can increase the Ushomirsky et al. (2000) result by 25-200%, and their own result of 7.4×10⁻⁶ is 164% larger than the Cowling-approximation value, confirming consistency. This work enables more optimistic predictions for continuous gravitational wave searches from neutron star mountains, particularly for young pulsars like the Crab.
