# Electron-Ion Collider impact study on the tensor charge of the nucleon

> In this letter we study the impact of the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) on the phenomenological extraction of the tensor charge from a QCD global analysis of single transverse-spin asymmetries (SSAs). We generate EIC pseudo-data for the Collins effect in semi-inclusive deep-inelastic scattering for proton and $^{3\!}He$ beams across multiple center-of-mass energies. We find a significant reduction in the uncertainties for the up, down, and isovector tensor charges that will make their extraction from EIC data on SSAs as precise as current lattice QCD calculations. We also analyze the constraints placed by future data from the proposed SoLID experiment at Jefferson Lab, discuss its important complementary role to the EIC, and present the combined impact from both facilities.

## Key claim

The paper projects that with Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) data on the Collins effect in semi-inclusive deep-inelastic scattering, phenomenological extractions of the up, down, and isovector tensor charges will achieve relative uncertainties of 5% or less, matching the precision of current lattice QCD calculations; however, the central values shift depending on which pseudo-data sets are included, indicating that precision does not guarantee accuracy and that multiple experiments covering a wide kinematic range are needed to minimize parametrization bias.

## Method

The authors performed a QCD global analysis of single transverse-spin asymmetries (SSAs) using the JAM20 framework as a baseline, which includes Collins effect SIDIS and SIA data, Sivers effect SIDIS and Drell-Yan data, and proton-proton \(A_N\) data. They generated EIC pseudo-data for the Collins asymmetry \(A_{UT}^{\sin(\phi_h+\phi_S)}\) using pythiaeRHIC with eic-smear detector simulation for both transversely polarized proton and \(^3\)He beams at multiple center-of-mass energies (29, 45, 63, 85, and 141 GeV), assuming 70% beam polarization and 10 fb\(^{-1}\) integrated luminosity per energy sample. They also generated SoLID pseudo-data for proton and \(^3\)He targets with 8.8 and 11 GeV electron beams, and performed separate re-fits including only pseudo-data above a given \(x_{\rm min}\) to isolate the impact of each kinematic region.

## Key numbers

- The JAM20 baseline extraction gives \(g_T = 0.87(11)\), \(\delta u = 0.72(19)\), \(\delta d = -0.15(16)\) at \(Q^2 = 4\) GeV\(^2\).
- With EIC pseudo-data from both proton and \(^3\)He beams, the extracted tensor charges become \(\delta u = 0.709(15)\), \(\delta d = -0.109(5)\), \(g_T = 0.818(16)\) at \(Q^2 = 4\) GeV\(^2\), with all relative errors \(\lesssim 5\%\).
- With SoLID pseudo-data alone, the extracted values are \(\delta u = 0.68(3)\), \(\delta d = -0.123(8)\), \(g_T = 0.80(3)\) at \(Q^2 = 4\) GeV\(^2\), with relative errors \(\lesssim 7\%\).
- With combined EIC and SoLID pseudo-data, the results are \(\delta u = 0.688(11)\), \(\delta d = -0.123(3)\), \(g_T = 0.811(13)\) at \(Q^2 = 4\) GeV\(^2\), with all relative uncertainties \(\lesssim 3\%\).
- The Pearson correlation coefficient between \(\delta u\) and \(\delta d\) drops from 0.80 for JAM20 to 0.043 when both proton and \(^3\)He EIC data are included.
- The EIC pseudo-data set comprises 8223 points; the SoLID pseudo-data set comprises 1222 points (526 for proton target, 696 for \(^3\)He target).

## Context

This study extends the JAM20 global analysis (Cammarota et al., 2020), which for the first time found agreement between phenomenological SSA extractions and lattice QCD for the tensor charge, but with uncertainties still much larger than lattice calculations (Gupta et al., 2018; Alexandrou et al., 2019). The work demonstrates that EIC data will reduce phenomenological uncertainties to the level of lattice QCD, while SoLID data from Jefferson Lab provides complementary constraints at large \(x\) (\(0.05 \lesssim x \lesssim 0.65\)) where the EIC has limited coverage. The combined analysis enables extractions more precise than current lattice calculations and highlights the need for multiple experiments to avoid parametrization bias and achieve accurate results.
