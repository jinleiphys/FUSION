# Barrier Distributions as a Tool to Investigate Fusion and Fission

> The recent availability of precisely measured fusion cross-sections has enabled the extraction of a representation of the distribution of barriers encountered during fusion. These representations, obtained from a variety of reactions, provide a direct observation of how the structure of the fusing nuclei changes the inter-nuclear potential landscape, thus affecting the fusion probability. Recent experiments showing the effects of static quadrupole and hexadecapole deformation, single-- and double-phonon states, transfer of nucleons between two nuclei, and high lying excited states are reviewed. The application of these concepts to the explanation of the anomalous fission-fragment anisotropies observed following reactions with actinides is discussed.

# Barrier Distributions as a Tool to Investigate Fusion and Fission

## Key claim

The function d²(Eσ)/dE² extracted from high-precision fusion excitation functions provides a representation of the distribution of fusion barrier heights that can distinguish between different coupling mechanisms (e.g., static deformation, vibrational excitations, nucleon transfer), even when the excitation functions themselves appear similar; however, this representation is only a smoothed approximation of the true barrier distribution due to quantum tunnelling effects and the discrete nature of experimental data.

## Method

Fusion excitation functions were measured with high precision (uncertainty ~1%) using experimental setups at the Australian National University. The second derivative d²(Eσ)/dE² was extracted from the measured cross-sections using a point-difference formula, yielding a representation of the barrier distribution. Theoretical coupled-channels calculations were performed and compared to the experimental barrier distributions using an identical numerical differentiation procedure, allowing identification of the specific nuclear structure couplings (rotational deformation, vibrational phonon states, transfer channels) responsible for the observed fusion enhancement.

## Key numbers

- For the ¹⁶O + ¹⁵⁴Sm reaction, experimental data with 10% uncertainty yielded poorly defined barrier distributions, while data with ~1% uncertainty clearly distinguished between different coupling models.
- The ¹⁷O + ¹⁴⁴Sm reaction showed low-energy cross-sections more than four times higher than those for ¹⁶O + ¹⁴⁴Sm, attributed to the neutron stripping channel ¹⁴⁴Sm(¹⁷O,¹⁶O)¹⁴⁵Sm with a ground-state Q-value of +2.6 MeV.
- For ¹⁶O + ¹⁵⁴Sm, the barrier distribution showed sensitivity to hexadecapole deformation β₄, with ¹⁵⁴Sm having positive β₄ and ¹⁸⁶W having negative β₄, producing distinctly different barrier distribution shapes.
- The ¹⁶O + ²⁰⁸Pb reaction required inclusion of 2-phonon octupole excitations (3⁻ ⊗ 3⁻) in coupled-channels calculations to reproduce the experimental barrier distribution, supporting recent spectroscopic evidence for double-phonon states in ²⁰⁸Pb.
- For ¹⁶O + ¹⁴⁴Sm, treating couplings to the octupole vibration of ¹⁶O to all orders (rather than linear coupling approximation) re-established the double-peaked barrier distribution, with a shift of 2 MeV compared to calculations ignoring this coupling.

## Context

This work extends the eigenchannel approximation of Dasso et al. (1982) showing that coupling to intrinsic degrees of freedom replaces the single fusion barrier by a distribution of barriers, and the barrier distribution extraction method proposed by Rowley (1991). The high-precision measurements resolve ambiguities in earlier data (e.g., Stokstad et al.) that had 10% uncertainties and could not distinguish between different coupling models. The technique enables identification of specific nuclear structure effects including static quadrupole and hexadecapole deformation, single- and double-phonon vibrational states, and nucleon transfer channels. The barrier distribution concept is applied to explain anomalous fission-fragment anisotropies observed in reactions with actinide targets (e.g., ¹⁶O + ²³⁸U), where a geometrical model assuming tip collisions produce quasi-fission and side collisions produce fusion-fission successfully reproduces the data. This work enables improved predictions for heavy-element synthesis and population of high-spin states.
