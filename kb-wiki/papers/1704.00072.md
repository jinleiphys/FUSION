# Peeking into the Origins of IceCube Neutrinos: I. Buried Transient TeV Miniburst Rates

> Any interpretation of the astrophysical neutrinos discovered by IceCube must accommodate a variety of multimessenger constraints. We address implications of these neutrinos being produced in transient sources, principally if buried within supernovae so that gamma rays are absorbed by the star. This would alleviate tension with the isotropic Fermi GeV background that >10 TeV neutrinos rival in detected energy flux. We find that IceCube data constrain transient properties, implying buried GeV-TeV electromagnetic emission near or exceeding canonical SN explosion energies of ~10^51 erg, indicative of an origin within superluminous SNe. TeV neutrino bursts with dozens of IceCube events -- which would be of great use for understanding r-process nucleosynthesis and more -- may be just around the corner if they are a primary component of the flux.

# Peeking into the Origins of IceCube Neutrinos: I. Buried Transient TeV Miniburst Rates

## Key claim

The IceCube neutrino flux could originate from transient sources buried within supernovae such that gamma rays are absorbed by the stellar envelope, alleviating tension with the Fermi isotropic gamma-ray background; however, this scenario requires that the buried GeV-TeV electromagnetic emission approaches or exceeds canonical supernova explosion energies of approximately 10^51 erg, pointing toward superluminous supernovae as the likely progenitors.

## Method

The authors scale the cosmic rate of core-collapse supernovae to define a fractional rate f_T of TeV neutrino transients, then apportion the total IceCube neutrino emissivity among these transients to compute per-event neutrino output and expected muon and shower event multiplicities in IceCube. They model neutrino spectra as smoothly-broken power laws (Model 1 with slopes -1 and -2.5, Model 2 with slopes -2 and -2.5, both breaking at 13 TeV) and integrate over cosmic star formation history, using muon cross sections and detector geometry to predict event rates at Earth as a function of distance and transient fraction.

## Key numbers

- Cosmic neutrino emissivity at z=0 for Model 1: approximately 1.6 x 10^37 erg s^-1 Mpc^-3; for Model 2: approximately 6.2 x 10^37 erg s^-1 Mpc^-3
- For f_T = 10^-2, TeV neutrino output per transient: approximately 5 x 10^50 erg; local volume out to 60 Mpc contains approximately 1 such transient per year
- At 60 Mpc with f_T = 10^-2, Model 1 yields approximately 2 muons with E_mu > 1 TeV at detector, approximately 3 muons with E_mu > 0.1 TeV; Model 2 yields approximately 2.5 muons with E_mu > 1 TeV, approximately 5 with E_mu > 0.1 TeV
- Approximately 0.5 muons with E_mu > 10 TeV for each model at 60 Mpc
- Approximately 1 shower in 1 km^3 volume with E_em > 1 TeV, approximately 0.5 with E_em > 10 TeV
- Atmospheric neutrino background rate in 12 deg^2 patch: approximately 15 per year for E_mu > 1 TeV
- Rate of hydrogen-rich superluminous SNe at z ~ 0.2: 1.5 (+1.5/-0.8) x 10^-7 Mpc^-3 yr^-1, giving f_T approximately 10^-3
- Fast-rising bright transients estimated at 4-7% of core-collapse SNe, placing f_T near 10^-1

## Context

This work extends the discussion of IceCube neutrino origins in the context of multimessenger constraints, particularly the tension with the Fermi isotropic gamma-ray background noted by Murase (2013) and Kistler (2015). The authors build on prior buried-jet models from Meszaros (2001), Razzaque (2004), Ando (2005), Horiuchi (2008), Enberg (2008), Bhattacharya (2014), and Tamborra (2015), and on observed supernova rate measurements from Li (2010) and Quimby (2013). The results enable searches for TeV neutrino minibursts in IceCube data, either as muon multiplets without optical association or as coincidences with observed supernovae, and suggest that transient fractions f_T less than approximately 10^-3 may already be excluded by the lack of high-multiplicity bursts in existing IceCube data.
