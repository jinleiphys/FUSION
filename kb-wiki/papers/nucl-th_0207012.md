# Hydrodynamic description of spectra at high transverse mass in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions

> Transverse mass spectra of pions and protons measured in central collisions of heavy ions at the SPS and at RHIC are compared to a hydrodynamic parameterization. While the chemical temperature needed at RHIC is significantly higher compared to SPS, the spectra may be described using kinetic freeze-out parameters which are similar for both beam energies. At RHIC either the temperature or the flow velocity is higher, but the data provide no unambiguous proof for much stronger transverse flow. The contribution of such hydrodynamic emission at high transverse momenta is investigated in detail. It is shown that hydrodynamics may be relevant up to relatively high transverse momenta. The importance of the velocity profile used in this context is highlighted.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a hydrodynamic parameterization can describe transverse mass spectra of pions and protons at both SPS and RHIC energies, but the data do not provide unambiguous proof for much stronger transverse flow at RHIC compared to SPS, as either the temperature or the flow velocity could be higher.

## Method

The author uses a modified version of the Wiedemann-Heinz hydrodynamic parameterization (1996) that includes transverse flow and resonance decays, with a Woods-Saxon spatial distribution replacing the original Gaussian and separate chemical and kinetic freeze-out temperatures. The model is fitted simultaneously to pion and (anti-)proton transverse mass spectra from NA44, NA49, and WA98 at SPS (158 AGeV Pb+Pb) and from PHENIX and STAR at RHIC (130 GeV Au+Au), extracting kinetic temperature, transverse flow velocity, chemical temperature, and baryonic chemical potential.

## Key numbers

- SPS (NA44 best fit): kinetic temperature T_kin = 122.2 ± 1.9 MeV, average transverse flow velocity ⟨β_T⟩ = 0.478 ± 0.004, chemical temperature T_chem = 143.9 ± 0.6 MeV, baryonic chemical potential μ_B = 193.3 ± 0.9 MeV, χ²/ν = 217/82
- SPS (NA49 best fit): T_kin = 75.8 ± 1.9 MeV, ⟨β_T⟩ = 0.550 ± 0.004, T_chem = 133.2 ± 0.5 MeV, μ_B = 184.5 ± 2.0 MeV, χ²/ν = 129/31
- SPS (NA49 with low m_T omitted): T_kin = 108.0 ± 6.6 MeV, ⟨β_T⟩ = 0.489 ± 0.016, T_chem = 136.6 ± 0.9 MeV, μ_B = 184.6 ± 3.3 MeV, χ²/ν = 16/22
- RHIC (PHENIX narrow profile fit A): T_kin = 143.0 ± 6.8 MeV, ⟨β_T⟩ = 0.450 ± 0.016, T_chem = 173.3 ± 2.0 MeV, μ_B = 35.9 ± 4.2 MeV, χ²/ν = 101/57
- RHIC (STAR narrow profile fit B): T_kin = 94.8 ± 7.9 MeV, ⟨β_T⟩ = 0.578 ± 0.012, T_chem = 165.3 ± 2.2 MeV, μ_B = 31.2 ± 2.5 MeV, χ²/ν = 19/36
- RHIC (combined PHENIX+STAR narrow profile): T_kin = 112.3 ± 3.3 MeV, ⟨β_T⟩ = 0.515 ± 0.006, T_chem = 165.0 ± 1.0 MeV, μ_B = 34.5 ± 2.2 MeV, χ²/ν = 206/98
- WA98 neutral pions (12.7% central) fit with fixed T_chem and μ_B from NA44: T_kin = 130.3 MeV, ⟨β_T⟩ = 0.415, χ²/ν = 27/18

## Context

This work extends earlier hydrodynamic parameterizations by Schnedermann et al. (1993), Wiedemann and Heinz (1996), and Tomasik et al. (1999) by introducing separate chemical and kinetic freeze-out temperatures and a Woods-Saxon spatial profile. The paper directly compares SPS data from NA44, NA49, and WA98 with RHIC data from PHENIX and STAR, finding that chemical temperature at RHIC (around 165-173 MeV) is significantly higher than at SPS (around 133-144 MeV), while kinetic freeze-out parameters can be similar. The analysis enables quantitative estimates of the hydrodynamic contribution to high-p_T particle production, which is necessary for interpreting jet quenching measurements at RHIC, as noted in the context of pQCD calculations by the author.
