# The chemical equilibration volume: measuring the degree of thermalization

> We address the issue of the degree of equilibrium achieved in a high energy heavy-ion collision. Specifically, we explore the consequences of incomplete strangeness chemical equilibrium. This is achieved over a volume V of the order of the strangeness correlation length and is assumed to be smaller than the freeze-out volume. Probability distributions of strange hadrons emanating from the system are computed for varying sizes of V and simple experimental observables based on these are proposed. Measurements of such observables may be used to estimate V and as a result the degree of strangeness chemical equilibration achieved. This sets a lower bound on the degree of kinetic equilibrium. We also point out that a determination of two-body correlations or second moments of the distributions are not sufficient for this estimation.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the degree of strangeness chemical equilibration in a heavy-ion collision can be inferred from the size of a "chemical equilibration volume" \(V\), which is assumed to be smaller than the freeze-out volume \(V_f\); the authors propose that measuring probability distributions of strange hadrons, rather than just mean yields or two-body correlations, can provide an estimate of \(V\) and thus a lower bound on the degree of kinetic equilibrium.

## Method

The authors model the fireball as a collection of \(p\) independent domains, each of volume \(V = V_f / p\), within which strangeness is exactly conserved via a canonical ensemble. They compute the partition function for strange particles in a single domain using a recursion relation technique, then derive probability distributions for the number of kaons (and other strange hadrons) emitted from the full system by convolving single-domain probabilities with a second recursion relation. The calculations are performed at fixed temperature \(T = 170\ \text{MeV}\) and baryon chemical potential \(\mu = 0\), using single-particle partition functions for strange hadrons up to strangeness \(\pm 3\).

## Key numbers

*   The freeze-out volume is taken as \(V_f = 400\, V_{\text{nuc}}\), where \(V_{\text{nuc}}\) is the volume of a nucleon.
*   The number of domains \(p\) is varied from 1 to 81, corresponding to domain volumes \(V = V_f / p\) ranging from \(400\, V_{\text{nuc}}\) down to approximately \(4.94\, V_{\text{nuc}}\).
*   The temperature used in all numerical results is \(T = 170\ \text{MeV}\) with \(\mu = 0\).
*   The recursion relation for the canonical partition function \(Z_n\) (net strangeness \(n\)) is given by \(Z_n = \frac{1}{n} [ S_1 Z_{n-1} + 2 S_2 Z_{n-2} + 3 S_3 Z_{n-3} ]\), where \(S_k\) are single-particle partition functions for strangeness \(k\).
*   The mean number of kaons, the probability to produce 20 kaons, and the probability to produce 74 kaons are all normalized to their values for an unpartitioned volume (\(p=1\)) and plotted as functions of the number of domains and of the total volume.

## Context

This work extends the canonical-ensemble treatment of strangeness conservation in small systems, previously applied to proton-proton collisions by Hagedorn (1968) and to heavy-ion collisions by Cleymans et al. (1991) and Hamieh et al. (2000). It introduces a practical recursion-relation method (following Mekjian's approach for intermediate-energy reactions) to compute probability distributions for strange hadrons, which the authors argue are more sensitive to the degree of chemical equilibration than mean yields or second moments. The paper enables future experimental searches for a signal of a quark-gluon plasma, because a large chemical equilibration volume would point toward an efficient strangeness-equilibration mechanism such as a QGP.
