# Search for DCC in relativistic heavy-ion collisions : Possibilities and Limitations

> The experimental observation of disoriented chiral condensate is affected due to various physical and detector related effects. We study and quantify the strength of the experimental signal, ``neutral pion fraction'' within the framework of a simple DCC model, using the analysis methods based on the multi-resolution discrete wavelet technique and by evaluating the signal to background ratio. The scope and limitations of DCC search in heavy-ion collision experiments using various combination of detector systems are investigated.

# Search for DCC in relativistic heavy-ion collisions: Possibilities and Limitations

## Key claim
The experimental observation of disoriented chiral condensate (DCC) is affected by multiple physical and detector-related effects that dilute the signal; the strength of the DCC signal, quantified via the neutral pion fraction and analyzed with discrete wavelet transformation, depends critically on domain size, multiplicity, number of domains, pion decay, detector efficiency and purity, and the availability of transverse momentum information, with the caveat that the study uses a simple DCC model where DCC pions are introduced at freeze-out rather than dynamically.

## Method
The authors used a simple DCC model in which generic particle production was simulated with the VENUS event generator and DCC-type fluctuations were introduced at freeze-out by changing the identity of charged pions to neutral ones and vice versa following the DCC probability distribution P(f) = 1/(2*sqrt(f)). They applied multi-resolution discrete wavelet transformation (DWT) using the D-4 wavelet basis to analyze bin-to-bin fluctuations in the photon fraction across azimuthal bins at resolution scale j_max = 5, and quantified the DCC signal strength via a parameter zeta defined as the relative increase in root-mean-square deviation of the wavelet father function coefficient distribution for DCC-containing event sets compared to normal events. Signal-to-background ratios were evaluated by counting events beyond 2 sigma of the normal distribution peak in the neutral pion fraction distribution.

## Key numbers
- The probability distribution of the neutral pion fraction f inside a DCC domain is P(f) = 1/(2*sqrt(f)), while for normal events the distribution peaks at f = 1/3.
- For a DCC domain of Delta_phi = 90 degrees and Delta_eta = 1, the strength of the DCC signal increases approximately as sqrt(N), where N is the average of mean photon and mean charged particle multiplicity; typical N values are 320 for WA98 at SPS, about 500 for STAR at RHIC, and about 2800 for ALICE at LHC (assuming a pseudorapidity density of about 8000 at mid-rapidity).
- The percentage decrease in DCC signal strength due to pi0 decay is larger for smaller domains; for a domain of Delta_phi = 30 degrees the decrease is approximately 40 percent, while for Delta_phi = 180 degrees it is approximately 15 percent (values read from Fig. 4).
- The photon detector was assumed to have realistic photon counting efficiency of 70 percent +/- 5 percent and purity of 70 percent +/- 5 percent; the charged particle detector efficiency was 90 percent +/- 5 percent.
- For a fixed neutral pion fraction f = 0.33 (generic event), the purity of the photon sample is about 0.74; purity increases with increasing f but does not reach 1 at f = 1 due to charged particles other than pions.
- The statistical error on the strength parameter zeta was found to be about 0.1 for the case when all pions in a domain are DCC pions.
- The probability of DCC formation at SPS has been predicted to be of order 10^{-3}, close to the upper limit set by WA98 data analysis.

## Context
This work extends earlier experimental searches for DCC including the JACEE, UA1, UA5, D0, CDF, Minimax, WA98, and NA49 experiments, all of which reported null results or upper limits on DCC production. The study quantifies how physical effects (multiple DCC domains, pi0 decay, increasing multiplicity from SPS to RHIC to LHC energies) and detector effects (efficiency, purity, charged particle contamination in photon detectors, availability of pT information) impact the observability of the DCC signal. The results provide guidance for ongoing and future DCC searches at RHIC (STAR with PMD, EMCAL, TPC, FTPC; PHENIX) and LHC (ALICE with PMD, FMD, PHOS, TPC), and demonstrate that the DWT-based analysis can detect signals from multiple DCC domains if phase-space separation is maintained, contrary to earlier theoretical expectations that multiple domains would wash out the signal.
