# Simulation studies of the isovector reorientation effect of deuteron scattering on heavy target

> The isovector reorientation (IVR) effect of deuteron scattering on heavy target provides a novel means to probe the nuclear isovector potential, which gives rise to the nuclear symmetry energy. The simulation studies on the experimental measurement of IVR effect using the SAMURAI terminal at RIKEN Nishina center have been performed to demonstrate the feasibility of the experiment. By introducing a well-designed polarimeter to detect the $\mathrm{p}(\vec{\mathrm{d}}, \mathrm{d})\mathrm{p}$ elastic scattering, monitoring of the tensor polarization of the deuteron beam can be implemented. The protons and neutrons produced by the breakup of polarized deuterons scattering off heavy targets are designed to be measured by proton drift chamber (PDC) combined with the SAMURAI magnet and NEBULA detector, respectively. The detector responses are simulated using Geant4 framework, where the events of the deuteron elastic breakup are generated by an Improved Quantum Molecular Dynamics model. The results of reconstructing the deuteron breakup events demonstrate the feasibility of detecting the IVR effect at SAMURAI with both longitudinal and transverse tensor polarized deuteron beams with a polarization degree of approximately 80\%.

# Simulation studies of the isovector reorientation effect of deuteron scattering on heavy target

## Key claim

The simulation demonstrates the feasibility of detecting the isovector reorientation (IVR) effect at the SAMURAI terminal using both longitudinal and transverse tensor polarized deuteron beams with a polarization degree of approximately 80%, provided that a well-designed polarimeter is implemented to monitor the tensor polarization and that the detector setup is optimized for breakup event reconstruction.

## Method

The authors performed Geant4 simulations of polarized deuteron scattering at 190 MeV/u on heavy targets (112Sn, 124Sn, and 208Pb), using an Improved Quantum Molecular Dynamics (ImQMD) model as the event generator for deuteron elastic breakup. The detector system includes a polarimeter for monitoring beam tensor polarization via p(d,d)p elastic scattering, a proton drift chamber (PDC) combined with the SAMURAI magnet for proton detection, and the NEBULA neutron detector array. The target was placed 3 meters upstream of the magnet center to optimize neutron acceptance, and events with impact parameters b = 7–9 fm were selected as the region most sensitive to the IVR effect.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy: 190 MeV/u
- Beam intensity: 1.6 × 10⁻³ pnA (or 10⁷ particles per second)
- Polarization degree: approximately 80% of the theoretical value
- Target thickness: 3 mm, producing angular straggling of about 0.5° for deuterons and 1° for protons
- Polarimeter target: 1000 mg/cm² thick polyethylene (CH2)
- Detector placement: polarimeter proton detectors at θ₁ = 55.9° and θ₂ = 11.3° (lab), each 600 mm from target; deuteron detector at 20.87°, 500 mm from target
- Total counts at polarimeter detectors within 30 minutes: approximately 10⁵ events
- SAMURAI magnetic field: 1.2 T, with 30° configuration
- Target distance from magnet center: 3 meters upstream
- NEBULA acceptance angle: ±12° relative to target position
- PDC placed at 42.5°
- Impact parameter range for IVR sensitivity: b = 7–9 fm
- Tensor polarization monitoring precision: 10%

## Context

This work extends the theoretical proposal of the IVR effect as a probe of the nuclear isovector potential and symmetry energy, originally introduced in PhysRevLett.115.212501 and PhysRevC.101.024603. The simulation addresses two key experimental challenges: monitoring the tensor polarization of the deuteron beam (particularly the previously unexplored longitudinal component p_{z'z'}) and optimizing the detector setup at the SAMURAI terminal at RIKEN Nishina Center. The polarimeter design builds on the method for monitoring p_{y'y'} described by Bieber et al. (2001), while the analyzing power data for deuteron-proton elastic scattering are taken from Sekiguchi et al. The results enable future experimental measurements that could constrain the density dependence of the symmetry energy, complementing constraints from heavy-ion experiments (Tsang et al., 2004; Xiao et al., 2008; Zhang et al., 2017) and neutron star merger observations (LIGO Scientific Collaboration, 2017, 2018).
