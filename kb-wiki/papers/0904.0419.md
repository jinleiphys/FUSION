# How good are the Garvey-Kelson predictions of nuclear masses?

> The Garvey-Kelson relations are used in an iterative process to predict nuclear masses in the neighborhood of nuclei with measured masses. Average errors in the predicted masses for the first three iteration shells are smaller than those obtained with the best nuclear mass models. Their quality is comparable with the Audi-Wapstra extrapolations, offering a simple and reproducible procedure for short range mass predictions. A systematic study of the way the error grows as a function of the iteration and the distance to the known masses region, shows that a correlation exists between the error and the residual neutron-proton interaction, produced mainly by the implicit assumption that $V_{np}$ varies smoothly along the nuclear landscape.

## Key claim

The Garvey-Kelson relations, used iteratively with up to 12 possible estimates per nucleus, produce mass predictions for the first three iteration shells that are comparable to Audi-Wapstra extrapolations and better than any global nuclear mass model, but only under the implicit assumption that the residual neutron-proton interaction varies smoothly across the nuclear landscape, which is not always true and causes systematic errors that grow with iteration number.

## Method

The authors used the two Garvey-Kelson algebraic relations (which connect masses of six neighboring nuclei) in an iterative procedure: starting from experimentally known masses in the AME95 compilation, they predicted masses for nuclei in the AME03 compilation, taking the average of all available estimates (up to 12 per nucleus). Predicted masses were added to the "known" set for subsequent iterations, and the root mean square deviation between predicted and experimental masses was computed at each iteration step. For long-range probes, the procedure was tested on approximately 9000 binding energies from three mass models (Liquid Drop Model, LDMM, and Duflo-Zuker) for nuclei with A > 160.

## Key numbers

* For the AME95-03 probe, 93 nuclei were predicted in the first iteration with a root mean square deviation sigma = 0.3391 MeV (Garvey-Kelson) versus 0.3160 MeV (Audi-Wapstra).
* After three iterations, 198 nuclei were predicted with accumulated root mean square deviation A_sigma = 0.3803 MeV (Garvey-Kelson) versus 0.3693 MeV (Audi-Wapstra).
* After five iterations, 242 nuclei were predicted with A_sigma_mod = 0.3483 MeV (Garvey-Kelson), compared to 242 nuclei with A_sigma_mod = 0.232 MeV reported in prior work (Lunney 2003) after seven iterations.
* After 11 iterations, 340 nuclei were predicted with A_sigma_mod = 0.628 MeV (Garvey-Kelson), compared to 340 nuclei with A_sigma_mod = 0.717 MeV reported in prior work (Lunney 2003) after 21 iterations.
* For the 95-03 test with N >= 8, Z >= 8, the initial subset contained 1760 nuclei and the prediction set contained 389 nuclei; for N >= 28, Z >= 28, the initial subset had 1454 nuclei and the prediction set had 371 nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the use of Garvey-Kelson relations beyond the nucleus-by-nucleus graphical analysis employed in the Atomic Mass Evaluations (Audi and Wapstra 1995, 2003) by implementing a fully iterative procedure that averages up to 12 estimates per nucleus. It directly compares against the Duflo-Zuker model (Duflo 1995), the Bethe-Weizsacker Liquid Drop Model with and without shell corrections (Danchev 2003, Dieperink 2007), and the Audi-Wapstra extrapolations. The study enables systematic short-range mass predictions for very unstable nuclei, particularly those recently measured at GSI, ISOLDE, GANIL, and other facilities, while identifying the need for strategies to correct the systematic errors arising from the non-smooth behavior of the residual neutron-proton interaction.
