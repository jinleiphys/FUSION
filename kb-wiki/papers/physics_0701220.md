# How changing physical constants and violation of local position invariance may occur?

> Light scalar fields very naturally appear in modern cosmological models, affecting such parameters of Standard Model as electromagnetic fine structure constant $α$, dimensionless ratios of electron or quark mass to the QCD scale, $m_{e,q}/Λ_{QCD}$. Cosmological variations of these scalar fields should occur because of drastic changes of matter composition in Universe: the latest such event is rather recent (redshift $z\sim 0.5$), from matter to dark energy domination. In a two-brane model (we use as a pedagogical example) these modifications are due to changing distance to "the second brane", a massive companion of "our brane". Back from extra dimensions, massive bodies (stars or galaxies) can also affect physical constants. They have large scalar charge $Q_d$ proportional to number of particles which produces a Coulomb-like scalar field $ϕ=Q_d/r$. This leads to a variation of the fundamental constants proportional to the gravitational potential, e.g. $δα/ α= k_αδ(GM/ r c^2)$. We compare different manifestations of this effect. The strongest limits $k_α+0.17 k_e= (-3.5\pm 6) * 10^{-7}$ are obtained from the measurements of dependence of atomic frequencies on the distance from Sun (the distance varies due to the ellipticity of the Earth's orbit).

## Key claim

Cosmological variations of fundamental constants arise from light scalar fields that become excited during changes in the Universe's matter composition, with the most recent such event being the transition from matter domination to dark energy domination at redshift \(z\sim 0.5\); the paper uses a two-brane model as a pedagogical example but notes that the specific coupling functions and dynamical mechanisms remain model-dependent and not yet settled.

## Method

The authors derive cosmological evolution equations for scalar fields (dilaton and brane-position scalars) coupled to matter via coupling functions \(A(\phi)\) and \(B(\phi)\), showing that the scalar field behaves as a damped oscillator driven by changes in the equation of state of the Universe. For static massive bodies, they solve the Poisson-like equation \(\Delta \phi = 4\pi G A'(\phi) T\) to obtain a Coulomb-like scalar field \(\phi = Q_d/r\) proportional to the number of particles, leading to variations of fundamental constants proportional to the gravitational potential \(\delta(GM/rc^2)\). They then compare limits from atomic clock experiments that measure half-year variations of frequency ratios due to Earth's elliptical orbit.

## Key numbers

- The half-year variation of the Sun's gravitational potential at Earth due to orbital ellipticity is \(\delta(GM/rc^2) = 3.3 \times 10^{-10}\).
- From mercury ion (\(^{199}\mathrm{Hg}^+\)) and cesium (\(^{133}\mathrm{Cs}\)) clock comparisons: \(\delta \ln(\omega_{\mathrm{Hg}}/\omega_{\mathrm{Cs}}) = (0.7 \pm 1.2) \times 10^{-15}\), yielding the constraint \(k_\alpha + 0.17 k_e = (-3.5 \pm 6) \times 10^{-7}\).
- From hydrogen and cesium hyperfine transition comparisons: \(\delta \ln(\omega_{\mathrm{H}}/\omega_{\mathrm{Cs}}) < 7 \times 10^{-15}\), yielding \(|k_\alpha + 0.13 k_q| < 2.5 \times 10^{-5}\).
- The gravitational potential at the Sun's surface is \(GM/rc^2 \sim 2 \times 10^{-7}\); for white/brown dwarfs it is \(\sim 3 \times 10^{-4}\); for neutron stars it is \(\sim 0.1\).

## Context

This work extends the self-decoupling mechanism of Damour and Polyakov (1994) by applying it to the recent matter-to-dark-energy transition at \(z\sim 0.5\), and connects cosmological scalar field variations to spatial variations near massive bodies. It builds on prior calculations by Barrow and Olive (2006) showing that the last cosmological slide can reconcile Oklo and meteorite limits with quasar absorption claims by Webb et al. (2001, 2002). The paper enables future searches for violations of local position invariance using atomic clocks in space missions (e.g., proposals by Schiller and Maleki) and high-sensitivity transitions in dysprosium, molecules, and thorium-229 nuclei.
