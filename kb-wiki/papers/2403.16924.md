# Tuned electron-nucleus resonance as a tool of producing the 229mTh isomer

> The possibility of refining the energy of the 8.36-eV 229mTh nuclear isomer -- the most likely candidate for the role of a nuclear frequency standard -- by means of resonant optical pumping is discussed. Attention is focused on considering the broadening of the resonance in order to reduce scanning time. The two-photon method proposed exploits the radical broadening of the isomer line due to mixing with the electron transition. This is not burdened with the cross-section reduction, in contrast with internal-conversion-based resonance broadening or intended extra-broadening of the spectral line of a pumping laser. In the case under consideration, according to the calculations, it turns out to be two orders of magnitude more efficient. It is applicable to both ionized and neutral thorium atoms. Realization of the method supposes excitation of the both nucleus and electron shell in the final state.

# Tuned electron-nucleus resonance as a tool of producing the 229mTh isomer

## Key claim

The two-photon resonance method exploiting electron-nucleus mixing (the Warsaw effect) can broaden the isomer line to atomic-scale widths (~10⁻⁸ eV) without reducing the excitation cross section, making it two orders of magnitude more efficient than internal-conversion-based broadening or deliberate laser line broadening for scanning the 229mTh isomer energy. The method is applicable to both neutral and ionized thorium atoms, unlike internal-conversion-based approaches which only work for neutral atoms.

## Method

The author calculates the cross section for two-photon resonant excitation of the 229mTh isomer via a Feynman process where a valence 7s electron absorbs a laser photon and is promoted to a 7p level, while the nucleus simultaneously undergoes a virtual photon exchange with that electron and transitions to the isomeric state. The cross section is derived using the detailed balancing principle and incorporates the Warsaw effect mixing probability β², the radiative width of the atomic 7p-7s transition, and the total width of the 7p level. Numerical parameters are computed using the Dirac-Fock method with the RAINE code, extrapolated from earlier calculations at 3.5 eV to the modern isomer energy of 8.36 eV.

## Key numbers

- Isomer energy: ωₙ = 8.355740(3) eV (from reference BCSH)
- Natural nuclear width: Γₙ = 0.667×10⁻¹⁹ eV (10⁻⁵ Hz)
- Internal conversion coefficient for M1 transition in neutral Th: α(M1) = 0.987×10⁹
- Conversion-broadened width of isomer in neutral atoms: Γₐ = 0.7×10⁻¹⁰ eV (10 kHz)
- Radiative width of 7p₃/₂-7s atomic transition: Γ_γ^(a)(7p₃/₂-7s; ω) = 3.1×10⁻⁶ eV
- Radiative width of 7p₁/₂-7s atomic transition: Γ_γ^(a)(7p₁/₂-7s; ω) = 2.4×10⁻⁶ eV
- Discrete conversion coefficient for 7s-7s transition: α_d(7s-7s; ωₙ) = 1.23×10¹⁰ eV
- Warsaw effect probability on valence 7s electrons: W = 4×10⁻¹²
- Resonance cross section for 7p₃/₂ final state: σ_γ = 6.24×10⁻²¹ cm²
- Photon flux for 1 mW beam on 10⁻⁴ cm² target: J = 5.47×10¹⁸ cm⁻² c⁻¹
- Isomer formation rate: p = 0.034 c⁻¹
- Isomeric atoms formed per 10⁻⁵ s pulse: N_is ≈ 5.4×10⁶
- Width of 7p₃/₂ level: Γ_7p₃/₂ = 3.13×10⁻⁸ eV
- Scan time for 1 eV interval: 11 minutes; for 0.024 eV uncertainty: 30 seconds
- Enhancement factor relative to bare-nucleus cross section: fWQ(Γ_7p/Γ_γ^(n)) = 92

## Context

This work extends the Warsaw effect (mixing of nuclear ground and isomeric states through electron interaction), previously demonstrated in hydrogen-like ions by Zylstra et al., to a practical two-photon scanning method for the 229mTh isomer. It contrasts with the frequency-comb project of Zhang et al., which uses internal-conversion broadening of 9 orders of magnitude but loses the same factor in cross section, and with the four-wave mixing approach of BCSH (2024) which deliberately broadens the laser line. The method resolves the "thorium puzzle" noted by Karpeshin (2021) regarding inconsistencies in multipolarity and cross-section estimates in earlier multi-photon proposals such as Porsev et al. The approach enables rapid determination of the isomer energy to laser-measurement precision and is applicable to both neutral and ionized thorium, making it compatible with nuclear clock projects using 229Th ions.
