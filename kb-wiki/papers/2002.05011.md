# Symmetry reduction of tensor networks in many-body theory I. Automated symbolic evaluation of $SU(2)$ algebra

> The ongoing progress in (nuclear) many-body theory is accompanied by an ever-rising increase in complexity of the underlying formalisms used to solve the stationary Schrödinger equation. The associated working equations at play in state-of-the-art ab initio nuclear many-body methods can be analytically reduced with respect to angular-momentum, i.e. $SU(2)$, quantum numbers whenever they are effectively employed in a symmetry-restricted context. The corresponding procedure constitutes a tedious and error-prone but yet an integral part of the implementation of those many-body frameworks. Indeed, this symmetry reduction is a key step to advance modern simulations to higher accuracy since the use of symmetry-adapted tensors can decrease the computational complexity by orders of magnitude. While attempts have been made in the past to automate the (anti-) commutation rules linked to Fermionic and Bosonic algebras at play in the derivation of the working equations, there is no systematic account to achieve the same goal for their symmetry reduction. In this work, the first version of an automated tool performing graph-theory-based angular-momentum reduction is presented. Taking the symmetry-unrestricted expressions of a generic tensor network as an input, the code provides their angular-momentum-reduced form in an error-safe way in a matter of seconds. Several state-of-the-art many-body methods serve as examples to demonstrate the generality of the approach and to highlight the potential impact on the many-body community.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first version of an automated tool (AMC) that performs angular-momentum reduction of tensor networks using graph-theory-based methods, taking symmetry-unrestricted expressions as input and providing their SU(2)-reduced form in an error-safe way within seconds, though the authors note that at least a minimum level of human input and experience remains necessary to choose the most convenient reduction scheme for each tensor.

## Method

The authors implement a symbolic angular-momentum algebra tool in Python 3 that transforms symmetry-unrestricted tensor networks into angular-momentum-reduced tensor networks by replacing all tensors with their SU(2)-reduced counterparts via the Wigner-Eckart theorem and constraining contraction patterns to reduced quantum numbers. Angular-momentum objects are simplified through identification of subgraphs in a suitably defined network called a Yutsis graph, with the reduction formulated as a transformation of abstract syntax trees that facilitates post-processing into different output formats. The code takes symmetry-unrestricted expressions of a generic tensor network as input and provides their angular-momentum-reduced form, with several state-of-the-art many-body methods serving as examples to demonstrate generality.

## Key numbers

- The SU(2) group is parametrized by three Euler angles with domain D_SU(2) = [0,4π] × [0,π] × [0,2π]
- The (2J+1)-dimensional irreducible representations of SU(2) are labelled by J, with 2J ∈ ℕ, 2M ∈ ℤ, J-M ∈ ℕ, and -J ≤ M ≤ +J
- The code provides angular-momentum-reduced forms "in a matter of seconds" (no specific runtime number given beyond this qualitative statement)

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to automate the derivation of working equations in many-body theory, which previously focused on automating (anti-)commutation rules for Fermionic and Bosonic algebras (e.g., the tensor contraction engine by Hirata (2003) in quantum chemistry) but lacked systematic treatment of symmetry reduction. The authors note that existing software is typically restricted to numerical evaluation of coupling coefficients rather than symbolic manipulation of complex tensor networks, with similar attempts for symbolic simplifications of angular-momentum expressions existing (Wormer, 2006) without formal connection to many-body theory. The tool enables the implementation of advanced many-body frameworks in nuclear structure in an error-free way, supporting methods such as many-body perturbation theory, coupled-cluster theory, self-consistent Green's function theory, and the in-medium similarity renormalization group method.
