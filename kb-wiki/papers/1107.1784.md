# Benchmark calculation of no-core Monte Carlo shell model in light nuclei

> The Monte Carlo shell model is firstly applied to the calculation of the no-core shell model in light nuclei. The results are compared with those of the full configuration interaction. The agreements between them are within a few % at most.

## Key claim

The Monte Carlo shell model (MCSM) is applied for the first time to no-core full configuration interaction (FCI) calculations in light nuclei, and the results agree with exact FCI results to within a few percent at most, provided that energy-variance extrapolation is used for binding energies and radii.

## Method

The authors performed no-core shell model calculations using the MCSM approach, which stochastically samples a small number of importance-truncated basis states to reduce the size of the Hamiltonian matrix. They benchmarked 9 states of light nuclei (from 4He to 12C) using the JISP16 NN interaction, comparing MCSM results with exact FCI results obtained from the MFDn code. The model space was truncated by the number of major shells (N_shell = 2, 3, and 4), and MCSM binding energies and radii were extrapolated using the energy variance with polynomial fits.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy differences between MCSM and FCI are around a few tens of keV at most across all benchmarked states
- Point-particle RMS matter radius differences between MCSM and FCI are roughly of the order of 10^{-3} fm at most
- For 4He (0+), MCSM binding energy at N_shell=4 with hbar-Omega=30 MeV is -28.738 MeV, matching FCI exactly
- For 6Li (1+), MCSM electric quadrupole moment at N_shell=4 is -0.280 e fm^2, compared to FCI value of -0.285 e fm^2
- For 7Li (3/2-), MCSM magnetic dipole moment at N_shell=4 is 3.036 mu_N, compared to FCI value of 2.993 mu_N
- For 12C (0+), MCSM binding energy at N_shell=4 with hbar-Omega=30 MeV is -92.179 MeV (FCI not available for this case)

## Context

This work extends the Monte Carlo shell method (MCSM) of Otsuka and collaborators to the no-core shell model framework, which previously was limited to p-shell nuclei at state-of-the-art computational facilities (as noted by Maris and Vary). The benchmark demonstrates that MCSM can overcome the computational limitations of standard NCSM calculations, enabling predictions for systems like 10B and 12C at N_shell=4 that exceed current FCI capabilities. This enables future ab-initio calculations in larger model spaces and heavier nuclei than previously possible with exact diagonalization methods.
