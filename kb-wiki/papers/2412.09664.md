# Quantum stresses in the hydrogen atom

> Gravitational form factors are often interpreted as providing access to stresses inside hadrons, in particular through Fourier transforms of the form factors $D$ and $\bar{c}$. Some researchers, however, have expressed skepticism of this interpretation. I revisit the question, and argue that it is indeed appropriate to interpret these quantities as stress distributions. I consider the hydrogen atom's ground state as a familiar example, and use the pilot wave interpretation of quantum mechanics to give the distributions a clear meaning. A striking result is that $\bar{c}$ -- rather than $D$ -- quantifies the force law binding the system, which can be understood through Cauchy's first law of motion.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the gravitational form factors $D$ and $\bar{c}$ can indeed be interpreted as stress distributions inside hadrons, contrary to skepticism expressed by Ji and Liu (2021). A key finding is that $\bar{c}$, rather than $D$, quantifies the force law binding the system, which can be understood through Cauchy's first law of motion, but this interpretation relies on adopting the pilot wave (Bohmian) interpretation of quantum mechanics to give the distributions a clear meaning.

## Method

The author calculates the gravitational form factors $A(\bd^2)$, $D(\bd^2)$, and $\bar{c}(\bd^2)$ for the non-relativistic hydrogen atom ground state by evaluating matrix elements of the stress tensor operator between momentum eigenstates. The stress tensor is decomposed into contributions from the electron, proton, and Coulomb field, with the particle stress operator chosen via Weyl quantization to match free-field theory results. The form factors are then Fourier transformed to obtain impact-parameter-space distributions, and the pilot wave interpretation is used to interpret these distributions as literal stresses, with the electron and proton following definite trajectories guided by the wave function.

## Key numbers

- $D(0) = M/\mu - 1$ for the total D-term, which equals approximately 1837 for physical proton and electron masses, and is positive (contradicting the negativity conjecture for mechanical stability)
- $D_a(0) = \mu^2 M / (5 m_a^3)$ for each particle contribution, and $\bar{c}_a(0) = -\alpha^2 \mu^2 / (3 M m_a)$ for each particle
- $D_\gamma(0) = (4/5)(M/\mu - 1/2)$ and $\bar{c}_\gamma(0) = \alpha^2 \mu / (3M)$ for the Coulomb field contribution
- $C(0) = 1/4 > 0$ for the combination $C(\bd^2) = (m_e/4M) D(\bd^2)$, reproducing Ji and Liu's result
- The momentum sum rule $A_e(0) + A_p(0) + A_\gamma(0) = 1$ is satisfied, and the local momentum conservation sum rule $\bar{c}_e(\bd^2) + \bar{c}_p(\bd^2) + \bar{c}_\gamma(\bd^2) = 0$ is also satisfied

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Ji and Liu (2022), who previously calculated the form factor $C(\bd^2)$ for the hydrogen atom and argued that gravitational form factors provide access to momentum current densities rather than forces. The author reproduces Ji and Liu's results in the infinite proton mass limit and agrees that the positive sign of $D(0)$ refutes the negativity conjecture for mechanical stability previously speculated by Polyakov and Schweitzer (2018) and others. By using the pilot wave interpretation, the paper provides a concrete ontology for interpreting stress tensor expectation values as literal forces, enabling future work on the mechanical properties of more complex quantum systems and potentially hadrons.
