# Bose-Einstein correlations and the transition time from QCD plasma to hadrons

## Key claim
The size of the interaction region determined from HBT (Hanbury Brown–Twiss) analyses is increased due to the finite time required to convert quarks and gluons into hadrons; a rough estimate yields an increase of the HBT radius by about 15%, but this result depends on the assumption of a simplified two-dimensional model and applies strictly only when longitudinal momenta of both particles are identical.

## Method
The authors develop a phenomenological two-dimensional model in the transverse plane, assuming uncorrelated particle production. They consider a plasma lump moving with velocity v(x) and assume that hadron formation in the lump rest frame takes a time τ₀, which is Lorentz-boosted in the laboratory frame. The Wigner function formalism is used to compute the HBT correlation function, and the effect of the transition time appears as a shift Δx = v(x)γ(x)τ₀ in the emission point, which modifies the effective momentum difference Q_eff in the Fourier transform. The model is evaluated analytically for Hubble flow and numerically for a hyperbolic flow profile.

## Key numbers
- The enhancement factor for HBT radii under Hubble flow is [1 + μ τ₀], where μ ≈ 0.1/fm is the Hubble flow constant; with τ₀ = 1/m_π, this gives a correction of about 15%.
- The Hubble flow parameter μ ≈ 0.1/fm gives vγ = 1 at |x| = R = 10 fm, consistent with arguments from other work.
- For the alternative flow profile |v(x)|γ(x) = sinh(μ₁ x), the parameter μ₁ = 0.08/fm was used with R = 10 fm and T = 160 MeV; the effect on R_HBT^side was similar to the Hubble flow case within a few percent.
- A classical argument for hadron formation time yields τ₀ ≈ 2π fm ≈ 4.5/m_π, based on a bound-state picture with radius r ≈ 1 fm and orbital velocity v = 1.

## Context
This work extends discussions of formation time effects on HBT measurements that were previously studied in the framework of UrQMD by Graf and collaborators, and builds on Stodolsky’s (year not given in text) analysis of hydrogen atom formation adapted to qq̄ systems. The results show that the transition time from quark-gluon plasma to hadrons increases the HBT radii beyond those predicted by hydrodynamical evolution alone. The authors note that a similar but technically more detailed analysis of time delay in hadron production was performed by Kisiel (year not given in text), focusing on long-lived resonances, and that collisions between produced hadrons (discussed by Heinz and collaborators, year not given in text) may further influence HBT radii but are beyond the scope of this note.
