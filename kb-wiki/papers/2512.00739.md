# A Review on Intense Electromagnetic Fields in Heavy-Ion Collisions: Theoretical Predictions and Experimental Results

> In heavy-ion collisions at relativistic energies, the incident nuclei travel at nearly the speed of light. These collisions deposit kinetic energy into the overlap region and create a high-temperature environment where hadrons ``melt'' into deconfined quarks and gluons. The spectator nucleons, which do not undergo scatterings, generate an ultra-intense electromagnetic field -- on the order of $10^{18}$ Gauss at Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider, and $10^{19}$ Gauss at the Large Hadron Collider. These powerful electromagnetic fields have a significant impact on the produced particles, not only complicating the study of particle interactions but also inducing novel physical phenomena. To explore the nature of these fields and their interactions with deconfined quarks, we provide a detailed overview, encompassing theoretical estimations of their generation and evolution, as well as experimental efforts to detect them. We also provide physical interpretations of the discovered results and discuss potential directions for future investigations.

## Key claim

This review paper synthesizes theoretical predictions and experimental results on ultra-intense electromagnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions, arguing that while classical electrodynamics can reliably estimate the peak magnetic field strength (on the order of 10^18 Gauss at RHIC and 10^19 Gauss at LHC), the temporal evolution of these fields remains an open theoretical question due to complex interplay with the quark-gluon plasma medium, with estimates varying by several orders of magnitude across different models.

## Method

The authors provide a comprehensive overview of theoretical frameworks for electromagnetic field generation using Liénard-Wiechert potentials and Woods-Saxon nuclear distributions, then review experimental measurements of field-induced effects including charge-dependent directed flow, dilepton production in ultra-peripheral collisions, and the chiral magnetic effect. They compare theoretical calculations from multiple models (including HIJING, Finite-Difference-Time-Domain solutions of Maxwell's equations, and Drude model approaches) with experimental data from RHIC and LHC collaborations.

## Key numbers

- Peak magnetic field strength of approximately 10^18 Gauss at RHIC and 10^19 Gauss at LHC, generated by spectator protons in relativistic heavy-ion collisions
- Magnetic field decays to negligible compared with peak value in approximately 10^-24 seconds (1 fm/c) in vacuum
- QGP vorticity of approximately 10^21 s^-1 at RHIC energies, which can prolong magnetic field lifetime
- Electrical conductivity-to-temperature ratio (sigma_el/T) of QGP spans two orders of magnitude, from 0.001 to 0.4 for temperatures from 0.1 to 0.6 GeV
- QGP conductivity estimated from Parton-Hadron-String Dynamics is approximately 500 times larger than that of copper and silver at room temperature
- Directed flow difference Delta dv_1/dy between protons and anti-protons shows negative values in 50-80% centrality range for Au+Au, Ru+Ru, and Zr+Zr collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV, and Au+Au at sqrt(s_NN)=27 GeV, with significances exceeding 5 sigma
- More negative Delta dv_1/dy at sqrt(s_NN)=27 GeV compared to sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV indicates stronger late-stage magnetic field at lower energies
- D^0 and anti-D^0 directed flow splitting in Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=5.02 TeV shows positive Delta dv_1/dy with significance below 3 sigma

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on the chiral magnetic effect by Kharzeev (2007) and Fukushima (2008), and builds upon theoretical calculations of magnetic field generation by Skokov (2009), Deng (2012), and Bzdak (2011). The paper synthesizes recent experimental results from the STAR collaboration (2023) on charge-dependent directed flow, which for the first time observed signals consistent with electromagnetic field effects on the QGP in symmetric collisions. The review enables future investigations into constraining QGP electrical conductivity through experimental magnetometers, resolving discrepancies between lattice QCD results and transport model predictions for conductivity, and understanding the role of magnetic fields in QCD phase transitions and neutron star physics.
