# Synthesis of superheavies: State of affairs and outlooks

> Nuclear reactions leading to formation of new superheavy elements and isotopes are discussed in the paper. ``Cold'' and ``hot'' synthesis, fusion of fission fragments, transfer reactions and reactions with radioactive ion beams are analyzed along with their abilities and limitations. Several most promising reactions are proposed for experimental study.

# Synthesis of superheavies: State of affairs and outlooks

## Key claim

While cold fusion reactions with lead and bismuth targets have produced superheavy elements up to Z=113 and hot fusion with calcium-48 on actinide targets has reached Z=118, further progress toward the predicted island of stability near N=184 requires alternative reaction mechanisms because the available target materials (californium being the heaviest usable actinide) and the sharp decrease of fusion probability with increasing projectile charge limit both approaches.

## Method

The authors used a multi-dimensional Langevin-type dynamical model to calculate the probability of compound nucleus formation, combined with a statistical model for the survival probability of excited compound nuclei against fission and neutron evaporation. They calculated capture cross sections, fusion probabilities, and evaporation residue cross sections for various projectile-target combinations, including cold fusion (lead-208 and bismuth-209 targets), hot fusion (calcium-48 with actinide targets), symmetric fusion of fission fragments, and reactions with radioactive ion beams.

## Key numbers

- Production cross section of element 113 in cold fusion (bismuth-209 target with zinc-70 beam): 0.03 pb
- Production cross section of element 118 in hot fusion (calcium-48 with californium-249): about 1 pb
- Half-life of element 112 from cold fusion (zinc-70 + lead-208): about 1 ms; from hot fusion (calcium-48 + uranium-238): about 34 s
- Predicted 1n evaporation residue cross section for element 114 in germanium-76 + lead-208: 0.06 pb
- Predicted 1n evaporation residue cross section for element 116 in selenium-82 + lead-208: 0.004 pb
- Predicted 1n evaporation residue cross section for element 118 in krypton-86 + lead-208: 0.0005 pb
- Excitation energy of compound nucleus at Bass barrier for calcium-48 + californium-249: 28 MeV
- Fission barrier of compound nucleus from calcium-48 + plutonium-244 (Z=114): 8.9 MeV (shell correction 8.89 MeV)
- Fission barrier of compound nucleus from calcium-48 + curium-248 (Z=116): 8.6 MeV (shell correction 8.58 MeV)
- Excitation energy of compound nucleus at Bass barrier for xenon-136 + xenon-136: 5 MeV
- Experimental upper limit for evaporation residue cross section in xenon-136 + xenon-136: about 2 pb
- Required beam intensity for symmetric fusion of fission fragments: 10^13 particles per second or higher
- Required beam intensity for sulfur-44 + curium-248 to produce element 112 isotopes: no less than 10^12 particles per second
- Required beam intensity for oxygen-22 + curium-248 to detect one decay event per week: 10^8 particles per second

## Context

This work extends the analysis of superheavy element synthesis beyond the established cold fusion approach (Hoffman 2000, Morita) and the hot fusion program with calcium-48 (Oganessian 2006, 2007). The authors provide quantitative predictions that contradict the dinuclear system model (Feng 2007) for element 120 production, and their calculated evaporation residue cross sections for xenon-136 + xenon-136 disagree by three orders of magnitude with the diffusion model (Swiatecki 2005, 2007). The paper enables planning of future experiments at available facilities by identifying the most promising reactions, particularly titanium-50 beams with actinide targets for elements 117, 119, and 120, and radioactive beams such as oxygen-22 for producing neutron-rich transfermium isotopes.
