# Interplay between one-particle and collective degrees of freedom in nuclei

> Some developments of nuclear-structure physics uniquely related to Copenhagen School are sketched based on theoretical considerations versus experimental findings and one-particle versus collective aspects. Based on my personal overview I pick up the following topics; (1) Study of vibration in terms of particle-vibration coupling; (2) One-particle motion in deformed and rotating potentials, and yrast spectroscopy in high-spin physics; (3) Triaxial shape in nuclei: wobbling motion and chiral bands; (4) Nuclear structure of drip line nuclei: in particular, shell-structure (or magic numbers) change and spherical or deformed halo phenomena; (5) shell structure in oblate deformation.

## Key claim

This personal overview argues that the interplay between one-particle and collective degrees of freedom, as developed by the Copenhagen School (Bohr and Mottelson), provides the essential framework for understanding nuclear structure phenomena from particle-vibration coupling through high-spin physics to drip-line nuclei, though the author notes that the selection of topics is based on her own research involvement and personal perspective rather than a comprehensive review.

## Method

The paper presents a qualitative survey of five selected topics in nuclear-structure physics, drawing on theoretical considerations from the Bohr-Mottelson framework and comparing them with experimental findings. For each topic, the author describes the underlying physical concepts (particle-vibration coupling, deformed and rotating mean fields, triaxial shape dynamics, shell-structure changes near drip lines, and oblate deformation) and illustrates them with specific examples from the literature, including detailed comparisons between calculated and observed quantities for the octupole vibration in 208Pb and its septuplet in 209Bi, as well as wobbling excitations in 163Lu.

## Key numbers

- Octupole vibration in 208Pb: vibrational energy 2.614 MeV, vibrational strength B(E3) = 32 BW(E3) (taken from Coulomb excitation experiments)
- Energy splitting of the septuplet in 209Bi: observed 250 keV compared with calculated 350 keV
- Effective E1 charge used in 209Bi calculations: [e^p_eff(E1)]^2 = 0.14 e^2
- Octupole vibration in 146Gd: excitation energy 1.579 MeV, B(E3) = 37 BW(E3)
- Maximum angular momentum nuclei can accommodate: order of 100 ħ for A ≈ 130
- Largest angular momentum reported in discrete line studies before superdeformed band discovery: 46 ħ
- Superdeformed band in 152Dy: extending to I ≈ 60 ħ, axis ratio approximately 2:1, quadrupole deformation β ≈ 0.6
- Triaxial shape parameters for 163Lu TSD bands: γ ≈ +20°, quadrupole deformation ε2 ≈ 0.38 (from ultimate cranker calculations)

## Context

This paper extends the Bohr-Mottelson framework (Nuclear Structure Vol. II, 1975) for understanding nuclear collective and single-particle motion, building on the Nilsson model (1955) for deformed potentials and the cranking model for rotating nuclei. It connects the classic particle-vibration coupling studies of the 1960s-1970s (e.g., octupole vibrations in 208Pb and 209Bi) to modern high-spin physics discoveries such as superdeformed bands (Twin et al., 1986) and wobbling excitations (2001-2002), and opens questions about the definitive experimental identification of chiral bands in triaxial nuclei.
