# Breaking the EOS-Gravity Degeneracy with Masses and Pulsating Frequencies of Neutron Stars

> A thorough understanding of many astrophysical phenomena associated with compact objects requires reliable knowledge about both the equation of state (EOS)of super-dense nuclear matter and the theory of strong-field gravity simultaneously because of the EOS-gravity degeneracy. Currently, variations of the neutron star (NS) mass-radius correlation from using alternative gravity theories are much larger than those from changing the NS matter EOS within known constraints. At least two independent observables are required to break the EOS-gravity degeneracy. Using model EOSs for hybrid stars and a Yukawa-type non-Newtonian gravity, we investigate both the mass-radius correlation and pulsating frequencies of NSs. While the maximum mass of NSs increases, the frequencies of the $f$, $p_1$, $p_2$, and $w_I$ pulsating modes are found to decrease with the increasing strength of the Yukawa-type non-Newtonian gravity, providing a useful reference for future determination simultaneously of both the strong-field gravity and the supranuclear EOS by combining data of x-ray and gravitational wave emissions of neutron stars.

# Breaking the EOS-Gravity Degeneracy with Masses and Pulsating Frequencies of Neutron Stars

## Key claim

The EOS-gravity degeneracy in neutron stars can be broken by simultaneously measuring the stellar mass and one of its pulsating mode frequencies (preferably the f-mode), because the Yukawa-type non-Newtonian gravity decreases all pulsating frequencies while increasing the maximum mass for a given nuclear EOS, but the authors caution that their use of the Fujii ansatz (a Yukawa term added to the Newtonian potential) is a technical choice rather than a physically preferred modified-gravity theory, and that the Yukawa term strictly appears only at the weak-field limit while neutron stars have strong gravitational fields.

## Method

The authors construct hybrid star EOSs using an extended isospin- and momentum-dependent effective interaction (MDI) for the hyperonic phase, the MIT bag model for the quark phase, and Gibbs conditions for the mixed phase, with bag constants B^{1/4}=170 MeV and 180 MeV. They model modified gravity by adding a Yukawa term with strength parameter g^2/μ^2 to the gravitational potential, which effectively stiffens the EOS through an additional pressure term P_UB = (1/2)(g^2/μ^2)ρ^2. They then solve the TOV equations for static neutron stars and compute the frequencies of the f, p1, p2, and w_I pulsating modes using established numerical algorithms from Lee (1983, 1985) and Chandrasekhar (1990), reproducing earlier results within 1%.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass without Yukawa contribution (B^{1/4}=170 MeV): about 1.46 M_⊙
- With g^2/μ^2=50 GeV^{-2} (B^{1/4}=170 MeV): maximum mass 1.97 M_⊙, radius about 12.4 km
- With B^{1/4}=180 MeV: g^2/μ^2=40 GeV^{-2} needed to reach maximum mass consistent with PSR J1614-2230 (1.97±0.04 M_⊙) and PSR J0348+0432 (2.01±0.04 M_⊙)
- Hadron-quark mixed phase density range for B^{1/4}=170 MeV: ρ/ρ_0=1.31 to 6.56; for B^{1/4}=180 MeV: ρ/ρ_0=2.19 to 8.63
- The f-mode frequency decreases significantly with increasing g^2/μ^2 for fixed mass (shown for 1.4 M_⊙ and 1.2 M_⊙)
- The soft-stiff EOS band from Lattimer (2012) and Hebeler et al. (2013) only allows possible gravity variation above about 1.4 M_⊙

## Context

This work extends the study of the EOS-gravity degeneracy first highlighted by DeDeo (2003), who showed that variations in the NS mass-radius correlation from modifying gravity are larger than those from varying the EOS within known constraints. The authors build on their own previous work (Xu et al., 2010; Lin et al., 2011) on hybrid star EOSs and on Krivoruchenko et al. (2009) who showed that global properties of neutron stars can be significantly modified by a Yukawa term while internal structures remain unchanged for g^2/μ^2 below about 200 GeV^{-2}. The paper enables future simultaneous determination of strong-field gravity and supranuclear EOS by combining x-ray and gravitational wave observations, noting that the f-mode frequencies are within reach of advanced gravitational detectors, while the w_I mode requires new high-frequency detectors.
