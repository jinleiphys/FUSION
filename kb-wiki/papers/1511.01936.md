# A new approach to barrier-top fission dynamics

> We proposed a calculational framework for describing induced fission that avoids the Bohr-Wheeler assumption of well-defined fission channels. The building blocks of our approach are configurations that form a discrete, orthogonal basis and can be characterized by both energy and shape. The dynamics is to be determined by interaction matrix elements between the states rather than by a Hill-Wheeler construction of a collective coordinate. Within our approach, several simple limits can be seen: diffusion; quantized conductance; and ordinary decay through channels. The specific proposal for the discrete basis is to use the $K^π$ quantum numbers of the axially symmetric Hartree-Fock approximation to generate the configurations. Fission paths would be determined by hopping from configuration to configuration via the residual interaction. We show as an example the configurations needed to describe a fictitious fission decay $^{32}{\rm S} \rightarrow ^{16}{\rm O} + ^{16}{\rm O}$. We also examine the geometry of the path for fission of $^{236}$U, measuring distances by the number of jumps needed to go to a new $K^π$ partition.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a calculational framework for induced fission that replaces the Bohr-Wheeler assumption of well-defined fission channels with a discrete, orthogonal basis of configurations characterized by energy and shape, where dynamics are determined by interaction matrix elements between states rather than by a Hill-Wheeler collective coordinate construction. The authors hedge that while several simple limits can be seen (diffusion, quantized conductance, ordinary decay through channels), the specific proposal to use $K^\pi$ quantum numbers of the axially symmetric Hartree-Fock approximation to generate configurations remains to be fully implemented for realistic fission calculations.

## Method

The authors construct a discrete basis using mean-field configurations characterized by $K^\pi$ quantum numbers from axially symmetric Hartree-Fock theory, with configurations grouped by shape parameter and energy. Fission dynamics are modeled as hopping between configurations via the residual two-body interaction, with interaction matrix elements calculated using two-body matrix elements and spectator orbital overlaps. The authors demonstrate the approach by examining configurations for a fictitious $^{32}$S $\rightarrow$ $^{16}$O + $^{16}$O fission decay and by mapping the geometry of fission paths for $^{236}$U using the HFBaxial code, measuring distances by the number of pair jumps needed to change $K^\pi$ partitions.

## Key numbers

- For $^{236}$U, the number of pairs to be moved from the first minimum to the second minimum totals 6 pairs, as calculated in the FRLD model by Möller.
- For $^{236}$U, configurations were examined up to quadrupole moment $Q = 140$ bn at the largest deformation.
- The average interaction strength between configurations scales with excitation energy $E$ as $\overline{ \langle \alpha| v | \beta \rangle^2} \sim E^{3/2}/\rho(E)$, with a power-law fit very close to the predicted 3/2-power dependence.
- For the diffusion limit, the diffusion coefficient is given by $D = 2 \pi\rho(E) \overline{ (q_\alpha - q_\beta)^2\langle \alpha | v| \beta\rangle^2}$ where $\rho$ is the level density in a single layer.

## Context

This work extends the discrete-basis approach to fission dynamics as an alternative to the generalized Bohr-Wheeler theory developed by Bjørnholm and Lynn (1980) and the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) framework attempted by the Gogny group (2011). The authors contrast their approach with the Los Alamos group calculations (2013, 2014) that use the Bohr-Wheeler framework with two barriers, and note that statistical and diffusive models from recent studies (2011, 2015) can describe many features of fission final states. The approach enables future exploration of configuration-interaction (CI) interactions from realistic effective Hamiltonians to determine where the diffusive limit can be applied, and provides a conceptual bridge to quantum transport in condensed matter physics.
