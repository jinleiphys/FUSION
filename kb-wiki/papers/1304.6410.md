# Charged elliptic flow at zero charge asymmetry

> The difference between the flow ellipticities of oppositely charged pions, Δv_2=v_2[π-]-v_2[π+], measured recently by STAR collaboration at RHIC shows a linear dependence on the event charge asymmetry A_+-={(N_+-N_-)/ (N_++N_-)}: Δv_2(A_+-)= Δv_2(0)+ r A_+- with a slope r>0 and a non-zero intercept Δv_2(0)>0 of order 10^{-4}. We point out two novel mechanisms which could explain the non-zero value of the charged elliptic flow Δv_2 at zero charge asymmetry A_+-=0, i.e., the non-zero positive intercept Δv_2(0). Both effects are due to the electric fields created by the colliding ions. These fields have quadrupole asymmetry of the magnitude and the sign needed to account for the nonzero intercept Δv_2(0) in the RHIC data. One of the mechanisms also involves chiral magnetic effect. This mechanism, although negligible at RHIC energies, may become important at LHC energies.

# Charged elliptic flow at zero charge asymmetry

## Key claim

The non-zero positive intercept Δv₂(0) in the linear relation between charged pion elliptic flow difference and event charge asymmetry, observed by STAR at RHIC, can be explained by two novel mechanisms involving electric fields created by the colliding ions, rather than by the Chiral Magnetic Wave mechanism that explains the slope. Both mechanisms produce a net positive electric quadrupole moment independent of charge asymmetry, with the direct electric field quadrupole effect dominating at RHIC energies while the magnetically induced effect via the chiral magnetic effect may become important at LHC energies.

## Method

The authors identify two sources of electric quadrupole moment in the quark-gluon plasma created in off-central heavy-ion collisions. The first mechanism arises from the quadrupole pattern of the net electric field from the two colliding ions, which drives currents via conductivity (in the hydrodynamic regime) or via collisionless acceleration (in the short-lived field regime), transporting charge outward perpendicular to the reaction plane. The second mechanism combines the electric and magnetic fields: the P- and CP-odd quantity E·B creates axial charge via the triangle anomaly, and the chiral magnetic effect acting on these axial charges in the presence of the magnetic field induces an electric current that produces a positive electric quadrupole moment. The authors perform order-of-magnitude estimates for both mechanisms using parameters appropriate for RHIC and LHC energies.

## Key numbers

- The intercept Δv₂(0) observed by STAR is of order 10⁻⁴, with the specific value approximately 3 × 10⁻⁴ from the data shown in Fig. 1.
- The direct electric field quadrupole mechanism yields Δv₂ ≈ 10⁻⁴ × (T/400 MeV) × (10³/N_tot) × (200 GeV/√s), matching the observed intercept at RHIC energy √s = 200 GeV.
- The magnetically induced mechanism via chiral magnetic effect yields Δv₂ ≈ 10⁻⁷ × (400 MeV/T)² × (10³/N_tot) at RHIC energies, negligible compared to the direct effect.
- At LHC energies, the magnetically induced effect could reach Δv₂ ≈ 10⁻⁵ × (τ_B/2 fm) × (eB/1 fm⁻²) × (400 MeV/T)² × (10³/N_tot), potentially comparable to the direct effect.
- The electric field strength is estimated as eE ≈ γZe²/(4πR²) with Z ≈ 80, R ≈ 7 fm, and γ = √s/(2 GeV).
- The lifetime of the electric field is τ_E ≈ 2R/γ, approximately 2 fm/c at RHIC energies.
- The mean free time is estimated as τ_free ≈ 1.5/T from lattice QCD conductivity σ = 0.4 eT Σ q_F² ≈ 0.27 eT.
- The charge asymmetry A_± in the STAR data is ≤ 0.04.

## Context

This work extends the Chiral Magnetic Wave (CMW) mechanism proposed by Burnier, Liao, and Shuryak (2011, 2012) which explains the linear slope r in the Δv₂(A_±) relation, but does not account for the non-zero intercept. The authors address the previously unexplained intercept, which Dunlop, Lacey, and others (2011) suggested might arise from initial isospin asymmetry in a quark coalescence model. The two mechanisms identified here provide a natural explanation for the intercept that is closely related to the same electromagnetic fields responsible for the CMW. The work enables quantitative simulations incorporating spatial variations and fluctuations of electromagnetic fields, as called for by Bzdak and Skokov (2011), Deng and Huang (2012), and Bloczynski, Duan, and Shuryak (2012), to produce reliable predictions for both RHIC and LHC energies.
