# Holographic Chiral Magnetic Conductivity

> We present holographic computations of the time-dependent chiral magnetic conductivity in the framework of gauge/gravity correspondence. Chiral magnetic effect is a phenomenon where an electromagnetic current parallel to an applied magnetic field is induced in the presence of a finite axial chemical potential. Motivated by a recent weak-coupling perturbative QCD calculation, our aim is to provide a couple of complementary computations for strongly coupled regime which might be relevant for strongly coupled RHIC plasma. We take two prototypical holographic set-ups for computing chiral magnetic conductivity; the first model is Einstein gravity with U(1)_L X U(1)_R Maxwell theory, and our second set-up is based on the Sakai-Sugimoto model in a deconfined and chiral symmetry restored phase. While the former takes into account full back-reaction while the latter not, the common feature is an important role played by the appropriate 5-dimensional Chern-Simons term corresponding to the 4-dimensional axial anomaly.

## Key claim

The paper provides holographic computations of the frequency-dependent chiral magnetic conductivity in strongly coupled gauge theories, complementing a previous weak-coupling perturbative QCD calculation by Kharzeev and Warringa (2009). The results are obtained in two distinct holographic setups that differ in whether the back-reaction of the axial density on the geometry is included, but both rely on the universal 5-dimensional Chern-Simons term encoding the 4-dimensional axial anomaly.

## Method

The author uses gauge/gravity duality to compute the retarded chiral magnetic conductivity \(\sigma(\omega)\) in two holographic models. The first model is Einstein gravity with a negative cosmological constant coupled to a \(U(1)_L \times U(1)_R\) Maxwell theory, including a 5D Chern-Simons term; an exact AdS-Reissner-Nordstrom black hole with axial charge provides the background. The second model is the Sakai-Sugimoto model in its deconfined, chiral-symmetry-restored phase with one flavor, where the effective 5D gauge theory is a Dirac-Born-Infeld action and back-reaction is neglected. In both cases, a small oscillatory external electromagnetic magnetic field is applied, and the linearized equations of motion are solved in Eddington-Finkelstein coordinates with in-falling boundary conditions at the horizon to extract the induced electromagnetic current from the near-boundary expansion.

## Key numbers

* The 4-dimensional anomaly coefficient relates the 5D Chern-Simons coupling to the effective number of flavors and colors: \(\kappa = -\frac{2 G_5}{3\pi} (N_F^{\text{eff}} N_c)\).
* The axial chemical potential in the first model is given by \(\mu_a = Q / r_H^2\), where \(Q\) is the charge parameter and \(r_H\) the horizon radius.
* The background metric function is \(V(r) = 1 - m/r^4 + 2 Q^2 / (3 r^6)\), with the horizon at the largest root of \(V(r_H)=0\).
* The holographic renormalization prescription for the induced electromagnetic current is \(j_{em}^3 = \frac{e^2}{4\pi G_5} \lim_{r\to\infty} r^2 (A_{em})_3\).
* A constant shift to the chiral magnetic conductivity from the Chern-Simons contribution is \(\Delta \sigma = - e^2 \mu_a / (2\pi^2) (N_F^{\text{eff}} N_c)\), which is minus the zero-frequency value \(\sigma(0)\) obtained in the paper.

## Context

This work extends the static chiral magnetic conductivity computed by Son and Surowka (2009) to finite frequency, motivated by the time-dependent perturbative QCD calculation of Kharzeev and Warringa (2009) for the strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma at RHIC. The holographic approach provides complementary results in the strongly coupled regime, and the frequency dependence is essential for hydrodynamic simulations of the RHIC experiment. The paper also discusses a subsequent observation by Rebhan, Schmitt, and Stricker (2009) regarding the correct identification of holographic currents in the presence of 5D Chern-Simons terms, which leads to a constant shift in the conductivity and raises open questions about the high-frequency behavior and the role of IR boundary contributions.
