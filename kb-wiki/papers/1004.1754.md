# Muon Capture on the Deuteron -- The MuSun Experiment

> We propose to measure the rate \Rd\ for muon capture on the deuteron to better than 1.5% precision. This process is the simplest weak interaction process on a nucleus that can both be calculated and measured to a high degree of precision. The measurement will provide a benchmark result, far more precise than any current experimental information on weak interaction processes in the two-nucleon system. Moreover, it can impact our understanding of fundamental reactions of astrophysical interest, like solar pp fusion and the $ν+d$ reactions observed by the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory. Recent effective field theory calculations have demonstrated, that all these reactions are related by one axial two-body current term, parameterized by a single low-energy constant. Muon capture on the deuteron is a clean and accurate way to determine this constant. Once it is known, the above mentioned astrophysical, as well as other important two-nucleon reactions, will be determined in a model independent way at the same precision as the measured muon capture reaction.

## Key claim

The MuSun experiment aims to measure the muon capture rate on the deuteron to better than 1.5% precision, which would resolve a three-standard-deviation discrepancy between the most precise existing measurement (6.2% uncertainty) and the best recent effective field theory calculation (1% uncertainty). The measurement will determine a single low-energy constant that relates muon capture on the deuteron to astrophysical reactions including solar pp fusion and neutrino-deuteron reactions observed by the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory.

## Method

The experiment uses a cryogenic time projection chamber (TPC) filled with ultrapure deuterium gas at temperatures of 25-35 K and densities of 0.05-0.08 relative to liquid hydrogen density, corresponding to pressures of 4-6.5 bar. The TPC operates without gas amplification as an ionization chamber with a 100-pad readout plane, providing 30-50 keV energy resolution. Muon timing is provided by entrance scintillators and a multiwire proportional chamber, while decay electrons are tracked by two cylindrical multiwire proportional chambers and a scintillator hodoscope barrel inherited from the MuCap experiment. Neutron detectors (DEMON counters with NE213 liquid scintillator) and gamma detectors monitor muon chemistry and impurity capture events.

## Key numbers

- Target precision for the muon capture rate on the deuteron: better than 1.5%
- Existing most precise measurement has 6.2% uncertainty and disagrees with theory by three standard deviations
- Best recent effective field theory calculation has 1% uncertainty
- TPC operating temperature: 25-35 K; density: 0.05-0.08 relative to liquid hydrogen; pressure: 4-6.5 bar
- TPC drift velocity: 0.4 cm/microsecond; drift time from cathode to grid: 25 microseconds; grid to anode: approximately 150 ns
- TPC energy resolution: 30-50 keV; energy threshold: 150-200 keV
- Drift cathode potential: -80 to -100 kV; grid potential: -3 to -5 kV
- Anode pad size: 10x10 mm squared; total number of pads: 100; active area: 100x100 mm squared
- Grid wire diameter: 55 micrometers; wire pitch: 400 micrometers; total of 325 wires with 60 g tension
- Beryllium entrance window: 61 mm diameter, 0.4 mm thickness; maximum stress at 10 bar: 88.7 MPa
- Aluminum pressure vessel side wall thickness: 2.5 mm; maximum stress: 68 MPa
- Muon beam intensity: approximately 10^5 per second; momentum: 30-50 MeV/c; momentum width: 3% FWHM
- Beam purity requirements: pion/muon ratio less than 10^-5; electron/muon ratio less than 20%
- Total requested production beam time: 22 weeks
- Expected isotopic purity: less than 1 ppm protium contamination in deuterium
- Previous MuCap purification achieved: 7 +/- 1 ppb of N2; less than 5 ppb of O2; approximately 10 ppb of H2O
- Fusion neutrons from d-mu-d fusion: monoenergetic at 2.45 MeV
- Capture neutrons from muon-deuteron capture: peaked at 1-3 MeV with energetic component reaching 53 MeV

## Context

This experiment extends the techniques developed for the MuCap experiment (which measured muon capture on the proton) to the deuteron system, using a new cryogenic TPC operating at higher gas densities. The measurement will resolve a three-standard-deviation discrepancy between the most precise existing measurement (6.2% uncertainty) and recent effective field theory calculations by Chen, Ji, and collaborators (2005) and by Ando, Myhrer, and Kubodera (2002). The determination of the axial two-body current low-energy constant will enable model-independent predictions for solar pp fusion and the neutrino-deuteron reactions measured by the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory, as well as other two-nucleon weak interaction processes.
