# Fitted HBT radii versus space-time variances in flow-dominated models

> The inability of otherwise successful dynamical models to reproduce the ``HBT radii'' extracted from two-particle correlations measured at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) is known as the ``RHIC HBT Puzzle.'' Most comparisons between models and experiment exploit the fact that for Gaussian sources the HBT radii agree with certain combinations of the space-time widths of the source which can be directly computed from the emission function, without having to evaluate, at significant expense, the two-particle correlation function. We here study the validity of this approach for realistic emission function models some of which exhibit significant deviations from simple Gaussian behaviour. By Fourier transforming the emission function we compute the 2-particle correlation function and fit it with a Gaussian to partially mimic the procedure used for measured correlation functions. We describe a novel algorithm to perform this Gaussian fit analytically. We find that for realistic hydrodynamic models the HBT radii extracted from this procedure agree better with the data than the values previously extracted from the space-time widths of the emission function. Although serious discrepancies between the calculated and measured HBT radii remain, we show that a more ``apples-to-apples'' comparison of models with data can play an important role in any eventually successful theoretical description of RHIC HBT data.

## Key claim

For realistic hydrodynamic and blast-wave models of heavy-ion collisions, HBT radii extracted by fitting a Gaussian to the computed two-particle correlation function agree better with experimental data than radii previously obtained directly from the space-time variances of the emission function, although serious discrepancies between calculated and measured HBT radii remain.

## Method

The authors compute the two-particle correlation function for non-interacting identical bosons by Fourier transforming model emission functions from a blast-wave parameterization and from hydrodynamic calculations, using a Monte Carlo technique to perform the space-time integrals. They then fit the resulting three-dimensional correlation function with a Gaussian form using a novel analytic algorithm that reduces the multi-parameter fit to solving a set of linear equations for a four-dimensional matrix, mimicking the experimental fitting procedure. The fit parameters are studied as a function of the maximum relative momentum included in the fit (fit-range variation), and the resulting HBT radii are compared to radii calculated directly from the space-time variances of the emission function.

## Key numbers

- Blast-wave model parameters: source radius R = 13.3 fm, temperature T = 97 MeV, maximum transverse flow rapidity ρ₀ = 1.03, average freeze-out time τ = 9 fm/c, emission duration Δτ = 2.83 fm/c
- Correlation function evaluated in three-dimensional bins of width 2.5 MeV/c in each direction (qₒ, qₛ, qₗ)
- For the blast-wave model at K_T = 0, the fitted outward radius Rₒ and longitudinal radius Rₗ are systematically smaller than those from RMS variances, while the sideward radius Rₛ from RMS variances is slightly smaller than from the Gaussian fit
- For the blast-wave model, the fitted correlation strength λ is very close to 1

## Context

This work addresses the "RHIC HBT Puzzle," where dynamical models that otherwise successfully describe RHIC data fail to reproduce measured HBT radii. Previous comparisons between models and experiment often used the short-cut of calculating space-time variances from the emission function, assuming Gaussian sources (Wiedemann and Heinz, 1996; Kolb and Heinz, 2003). The authors show that for realistic hydrodynamic models, the Gaussian-fit procedure yields HBT radii that differ from variance-based radii, with the same basic tendencies as found in the blast-wave model but with larger differences. This enables more meaningful "apples-to-apples" comparisons between models and experimental data, which may help resolve some of the persistent discrepancies.
