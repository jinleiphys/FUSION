# The chemical potential for the inhomogeneous electron liquid in terms of its kinetic and potential parts with special consideration of the surface potential step and BCS-BEC crossover

## Key claim

The difference between the chemical potential and the total energy per particle is exactly the electrostatic potential step at the surface, a relation not previously found in the literature; this identification allows the BCS-BEC crossover to be interpreted as a competing effect between bulk energy and surface potential step, with the sign change of the chemical potential marking the transition.

## Method

The authors combine the quantum-mechanical virial theorem with the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem and the Budd-Vannimenus theorem to derive an exact expression for the chemical potential of a three-dimensional homogeneous electron liquid in terms of average kinetic and potential energies and the surface potential step. They then apply the same thermodynamic relations to an integrable one-dimensional model with delta-function interactions, using the Gaudin integral equation solutions of Wadati and Iida (2007) to compute the ground-state energy, chemical potential, and surface potential step in both the weak-coupling (BCS) and strong-coupling (BEC) limits.

## Key numbers

- For the three-dimensional homogeneous electron liquid, the chemical potential is given by \(N\mu = \frac{5}{3}\langle T\rangle + \frac{4}{3}\langle V\rangle\) (Equation m), where \(\langle T\rangle\) and \(\langle V\rangle\) are the average kinetic and potential energies.
- The surface potential step \(\Delta\Phi\) is related to the bulk energy per particle \(E_b/N\) by the Budd-Vannimenus theorem: \(\Delta\Phi = -\frac{r_s}{3}\frac{\partial (E_b/N)}{\partial r_s}\) (Equation BV), where \(r_s\) is the mean inter-electronic spacing.
- In the one-dimensional BCS regime (weak coupling, \(|\gamma|\ll 1\)), the ground-state energy per particle is \(E_{\rm BCS}/N D^2 = \pi^2/12 \left(1 + 6\gamma/\pi^2 - 3\gamma^2/(2\pi^2)\right) + \mathcal{O}(\gamma^3)\) (Equation 33), where \(D=N/L\) is the particle density and \(\gamma=c/D\) is the dimensionless coupling.
- The chemical potential in the BCS regime is \(\mu_{\rm BCS} = D^2\pi^2/4 \left(1 + 4\gamma/\pi^2 - \gamma^2/(2\pi^2)\right) + \mathcal{O}(\gamma^3)\) (Equation 35).
- The BCS-BEC transition occurs at \(\mu=0\), with the critical coupling approximately \(\gamma_{\rm BCS}^c \approx -1.98\) from the weak-coupling side and \(\gamma_{\rm strong}^c \approx -2.07\) from the strong-coupling side.
- In the strong-coupling (BEC) limit, the scaled ground-state energy is \(E_{\rm strong}/N D^2 = -\frac{1}{4}\gamma^2 + \frac{\pi^2}{12}\left(\frac{\gamma}{2\gamma+1}\right)^2\left(1 + \frac{4\pi^2}{15(2\gamma+1)^3}\right)\) (Equation 18).

## Context

This work extends the early many-body result of March (1972) that combined the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem with the virial theorem for a homogeneous correlated electron liquid, and the Budd-Vannimenus theorem (1973) linking surface potential steps to bulk energy. The authors reinterpret the chemical potential's deviation from the bulk energy as a surface potential step, providing a unified picture for the BCS-BEC crossover in the one-dimensional integrable model of Wadati and Iida (2007). This enables future work on analytically tractable models in higher dimensions where the chemical potential could be expressed solely in terms of the ground-state energy per particle, and suggests the surface potential step as an alternative measure for the BCS-BEC transition.
