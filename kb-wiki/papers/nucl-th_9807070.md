# Isovector Vibrations in Nuclear Matter at Finite Temperature

> We consider the propagation and damping of isovector excitations in heated nuclear matter within the Landau Fermi-liquid theory. Results obtained for nuclear matter are applied to calculate the Giant Dipole Resonance (GDR) at finite temperature in heavy spherical nuclei within Steinwedel and Jensen model. The centroid energy of the GDR slightly decreases with increasing temperature and the width increases as $T^2$ for temperatures $T < 5$ MeV in agreement with recent experimental data for GDR in $^{208}$Pb and $^{120}$Sn. The validity of the method for other Fermi fluids is finally suggested.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the width of the Giant Dipole Resonance (GDR) in heated nuclear matter increases as \(T^2\) for temperatures below 5 MeV, with the centroid energy slightly decreasing, and that this behavior is primarily due to collisional damping from nucleon-nucleon collisions rather than thermal Landau damping, but the authors acknowledge that other contributions (fragmentation width, thermal shape fluctuations, and nucleon-nucleon correlations) are missing and that a combination of models would give better agreement with data.

## Method

The authors used the linearized Landau-Vlasov equation with a collision integral treated in the relaxation time approximation, including memory effects via the Landau prescription, to calculate the isovector response of spin-isospin symmetric nuclear matter at finite temperature. The formalism was applied to finite nuclei using the Steinwedel-Jensen (SJ) model, which describes the GDR as a volume polarization mode with wavenumber \(k = \pi/2R\), where \(R\) is the nuclear radius. The two-body relaxation time \(\tau\) was calculated using nucleon-nucleon scattering cross sections from the Bonn A potential, both with vacuum and in-medium reduced cross sections.

## Key numbers

- The isovector Landau parameter at zero temperature is \(F_0'(0) = 1.33\), derived from the symmetry energy coefficient \(\beta = 28\) MeV using the Weizsäcker mass formula.
- The coefficient \(\alpha^{(-)}\) in the relaxation time formula \(\tau = \hbar\alpha^{(-)} / (T^2 + (\hbar\omega_R/2\pi)^2)\) is 2.3 MeV for vacuum cross sections and 5.5 MeV for in-medium reduced cross sections.
- The nuclear saturation density used is \(\rho_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- The relaxation time parameters \(\kappa_l\) (in MeV\(^2\) fm/c) for \(l=1,2,\infty\) with vacuum cross sections are 503, 491, and 401, respectively; with in-medium cross sections at \(\rho = \rho_0\) they are 1123, 1068, and 1003, respectively.
- The GDR width increases as \(T^2\) for temperatures \(T < 5\) MeV, with the centroid energy slightly decreasing with increasing temperature.

## Context

This work extends the second theoretical approach to the temperature dependence of the GDR width, which attributes the thermal contribution to collisional damping from nucleon-nucleon collisions and Landau spreading, as opposed to the adiabatic coupling to thermal shape deformations proposed by Ormand, Bortignon, and Broglia (1996). The authors build on prior RPA calculations by Barranco and Vinas (1990) and Barranco, Vinas, and collaborators (1991), which found Landau damping too weak to explain the observed width increase, and incorporate memory effects in the collision term following Abrikosov and Khalatnikov (1959). The results enable future combination with thermal shape fluctuation models to better reproduce experimental data, as suggested by a statistical model analysis of \(\gamma\)-spectra from inelastic \(\alpha\)-scattering on \(^{120}\)Sn by Gervais (1997).
