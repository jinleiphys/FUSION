# Thermal Relaxation and Cooling of Quark Stars with a Strangelet Crust

> In this article, we explore the cooling of isolated quark stars. These objects are structured of a homogeneous quark matter core and crusted by matter. To do this, we adopt two kinds of crust: (i) a crust made of purely nuclear matter following the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland (BPS) equation of state (EoS) and (ii) a crust made of nuggets of strange quark matter (strangelets). Both models have the same quark matter core described by the MIT bag model EoS. Our main purpose is to quantify the effects of a strangelet crust on the cooling and relaxation times of these strange stars. We also perform a thorough study of the thermal relaxation of quark stars, in which we have found that objects with a strangelet crust have a significantly different thermal relaxation time. Our study also includes the possible effects of color superconductivity in the quark core.

## Key claim
Quark stars with a strangelet crust cool significantly faster than those with a traditional nuclear (BPS) crust, with thermal relaxation times two orders of magnitude smaller (∼1 year versus ∼100 years), but the observed neutron star cooling data cannot distinguish between the two crust models because the difference manifests mainly in the early thermalization phase of young stars.

## Method
The authors solved the general relativistic thermal balance and transport equations for isolated quark stars, using the MIT bag model equation of state (with m_s = 100 MeV, B^{1/4} = 128.9 MeV, α_s = 0.4) for the homogeneous quark core. Two crust models were compared: a nuclear matter crust following the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland (BPS) equation of state, and a strangelet crust composed of charged strange quark matter nuggets in a neutralizing electron background, as described by Jaikumar (2006) and Alford (2008). The thermal evolution included neutrino emissivities from quark direct Urca, modified Urca, and bremsstrahlung processes, with specific heat and thermal conductivity from the literature, and the Color-Flavor-Locked (CFL) superconducting phase was considered for the quark core with pairing gaps Δ = 0.1, 1.0, and 10 MeV.

## Key numbers
- Maximum mass for both crust models: ∼2.41 M⊙
- For a 1.42 M⊙ star: radius 12.78 km (strangelet crust) vs 13.27 km (BPS crust)
- For a 2.00 M⊙ star: radius 13.77 km (strangelet crust) vs 14.11 km (BPS crust)
- Crust thickness: ∼20 m and mass ∼10⁻⁵ M⊙ for strangelet crust; ∼500 m and mass ∼10⁻⁴ M⊙ for BPS crust
- Transition density between core and crust: ε_tr ∼ 153.76 MeV·fm⁻³
- Thermal relaxation time for strangelet crust: ∼1 year; for BPS crust: ∼100 years (two orders of magnitude difference)
- CFL pairing gaps considered: Δ = 0.1, 1.0, and 10 MeV; cooling saturates for Δ ≥ 10 MeV

## Context
This work extends the study of strange star cooling by Blaschke (2000), Grigorian (2005), and Blaschke (2006) by specifically quantifying the effects of a strangelet crust, as proposed by Jaikumar (2006) and Alford (2006, 2008), on thermal relaxation times. It also builds on the thermal relaxation framework for neutron stars developed by Lattimer (1994) and Gnedin (2001), and on the earlier work of Alcock (1986) and Huang (1997) regarding nuclear crusts on strange stars. The results enable future observational discrimination between crust models if young, thermally relaxing quark stars are detected, and suggest that CFL pairing with Δ ∼ 1–10 MeV is needed to match observed cooling data from Potekhin (2020).
