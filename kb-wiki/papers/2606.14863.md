# Towards a consistent perturbation theory at finite temperature

> The standard approach to perturbation theory for finite-temperature quantum field theories has several issues, including the appearance of ill-defined on-shell contributions in the real-time formulation, and infrared diverges in massless theories. Earlier studies indicate that these issues all stem from the inconsistent thermal generalisation of the Gell-Mann-Low relation, which forms the foundation of perturbation theory in vacuum. This inconsistency arises from the use of free scattering states in the relation, which are known not to exist in interacting thermal theories. In this work, we propose a generalisation of the Gell-Mann-Low relation for scalar theories based on non-perturbative spectral insights, namely that finite-temperature scattering states can be described by damped but stable particle-like excitations, so-called thermoparticles. The perturbative expansion of this generalised relation gives rise to contributions with exactly the same topology as the standard finite-temperature approach, except that now the propagators appearing in this expansion are not those of a free field but of thermoparticles, which depend on the dynamics of the theory. We demonstrate that thermoparticle perturbation theory resolves the known problems of the standard approach. Furthermore, by comparing imaginary-time calculations at two-loop level with numerical lattice simulations of two-point correlation functions in massive $ϕ^{4}$ theory, we explicitly show that this framework gives rise to precise predictions, as in the vacuum case, in stark contrast to the standard approach.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the standard finite-temperature perturbation theory fails because it uses free-field scattering states in the Gell-Mann-Low relation, which are inconsistent with the Narnhofer-Requardt-Thirring theorem; the authors claim that replacing these with "thermoparticle" propagators, which incorporate a thermal damping factor, resolves both the on-shell singularities and infrared divergences, and yields percent-level precision predictions when compared to lattice simulations.

## Method

The authors first review the standard real-time and imaginary-time formalisms, identifying that pinch singularities and infrared divergences arise from the use of free-field propagators with on-shell delta functions. They then construct a generalized Gell-Mann-Low relation based on thermoparticle states, whose spectral functions have a distinguished component proportional to \(\delta(s-m^2)\) multiplied by a thermal damping factor \(\widetilde{D}_{m,\beta}(\vec{u})\) in the Bros-Buchholz representation. The perturbative expansion of this relation yields Feynman diagrams with the same topology as the standard approach, but with thermoparticle propagators instead of free-field propagators. The framework is tested by computing two-point correlation functions in massive \(\phi^4\) theory at two-loop order in the imaginary-time formalism and comparing with numerical lattice simulations.

## Key numbers

* In massive \(\phi^4\) theory, the thermoparticle perturbation theory framework gives rise to "percent-level precision predictions" when comparing two-loop imaginary-time calculations with numerical lattice simulations of two-point correlation functions.
* The standard perturbative approach is observed to "increasingly worsen with temperature for coupling values where vacuum perturbation theory is quantitatively consistent" (Lowdon, 2024).
* Without fitting a \(g^6\)-coefficient to lattice data, the analytic series for the QCD equation of state are "not consistent with the lattice data even at \(T\sim 100\) GeV" (Bresciani, 2025).
* The one-loop spectral function in the standard approach is \(\rho_{(1)}(p_0,\vec{p}) = 2\pi \epsilon(p_0)[\delta(p^2-m^2) - \Delta m_+^2 \delta'(p^2-m^2)]\), where \(\Delta m_+^2\) is the thermal bubble contribution.

## Context

This work extends the non-perturbative spectral analysis of Bros and Buchholz (1992, 1993, 1995, 1996, 2001, 2003), who established the spectral representation for thermal two-point functions and introduced the concept of thermoparticles as damped but stable particle-like excitations. It directly addresses the foundational inconsistency identified by Landsman (1988) regarding the use of free scattering states in the thermal Gell-Mann-Low relation, and the infrared breakdown demonstrated by Weldon (1998, 2001). The framework enables a systematic perturbative expansion at finite temperature that avoids the ad-hoc regularizations and resummations required in the standard approach, and provides a path toward consistent calculations in theories such as QCD where standard thermal perturbation theory fails to match lattice data even at high temperatures.
