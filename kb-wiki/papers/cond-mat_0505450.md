# A New Thermodynamics from Nuclei to Stars III

> Equilibrium statistics of Hamiltonian systems is correctly described by the microcanonical ensemble. Classically this is the manifold of all points in the $N-$body phase space with the given total energy. Due to Boltzmann's principle, $e^S=tr(δ(E-H))$, its geometrical size is related to the entropy $S(E,N,...)$. This definition does not invoke any information theory, no thermodynamic limit, no extensivity, and no homogeneity assumption, as are needed in conventional (canonical) thermo-statistics. Therefore, it describes the equilibrium statistics of extensive as well of non-extensive systems. Due to this fact it is the {\em fundamental} definition of any classical equilibrium statistics. It can address nuclei and astrophysical objects as well. All kind of phase transitions can be distinguished sharply and uniquely for even small systems. It is further shown that the second law is a natural consequence of the statistical nature of thermodynamics which describes all systems with the same -- redundant -- set of few control parameters simultaneously. It has nothing to do with the thermodynamic limit. It even works in systems which are by far {\em larger} than any thermodynamic "limit".

## Key claim

The microcanonical ensemble, based on Boltzmann's principle \(e^S = \operatorname{tr}(\delta(E-H))\), provides the fundamental definition of equilibrium statistics for both extensive and non-extensive systems without requiring the thermodynamic limit, extensivity, or homogeneity assumptions that are necessary in conventional canonical thermo-statistics.

## Method

The paper develops a geometric, microcanonical framework where entropy \(S(E,N,\dots)\) is defined purely from the phase-space volume \(W(E,N,V) = \epsilon_0 \operatorname{tr} \delta(E-H_N)\), and phase transitions are identified by the topology of the Hessian (curvature determinant) of the entropy density \(s(e,n)\). The approach is applied to the Potts-gas model on a finite \(50 \times 50\) lattice, to Monte Carlo simulations of sodium clusters (200, 1000, and 3000 atoms), and to self-gravitating rotating systems, demonstrating that first-order phase transitions correspond to convex intruders in \(S\) and negative heat capacities.

## Key numbers

- For the 2-dimensional Potts-3 lattice gas on a \(50 \times 50\) lattice, the phase diagram shows a region of phase separation (triangle \(AP_mB\)) where the Hessian determinant is negative (\(\lambda_1 > 0, \lambda_2 < 0\)).
- For sodium clusters: transition temperature \(T_{tr} = 940\) K (\(N_0 = 200\)), \(990\) K (\(N_0 = 1000\)), \(1095\) K (\(N_0 = 3000\)), compared to bulk value \(1156\) K.
- Latent heat per atom for sodium clusters: \(q_{lat} = 0.82\) eV (\(N_0 = 200\)), \(0.91\) eV (\(N_0 = 1000\)), \(0.94\) eV (\(N_0 = 3000\)), bulk \(0.923\) eV.
- Surface tension over transition temperature \(\sigma/T_{tr}\) for sodium clusters: \(2.75\) (\(N_0 = 200\)), \(5.68\) (\(N_0 = 1000\)), \(7.07\) (\(N_0 = 3000\)), bulk \(7.41\).
- For a system of 3000 sodium atoms at 1 atm, the fragmentation transition shows a backward swing in the caloric curve \(T(E)\); at \(e^* \sim 0.442\) eV the liquid drop evaporates 329 monomers, 7.876 dimers, and 1.295 quadrimers on average.

## Context

This work extends the microcanonical approach of Gross (2001, 2004) to demonstrate that phase transitions and negative heat capacities are generic features of first-order transitions in both short-range and long-range systems, contradicting the conventional view that such phenomena require the thermodynamic limit or long-range forces. It enables a unified thermodynamic description of finite systems (nuclei, atomic clusters) and non-extensive systems (self-gravitating astrophysical objects), and it provides a derivation of the second law from reversible dynamics without invoking the thermodynamic limit, addressing issues raised by Lebowitz (1999) and Goldstein (2003).
