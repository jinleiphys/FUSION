# Hadron Correlations and Parton Recombination

> Parton recombination has been found to be an extremely useful model to understand hadron production at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider. It is particularly important to explore its connections with hard processes. This article reviews some of the aspects of the quark recombination model and places particular emphasis on hadron correlations.

## Key claim

Parton recombination is a useful model for hadron production at RHIC, but its validity is conditional: it applies primarily at intermediate transverse momenta (up to about 6 GeV/c for baryons and 4 GeV/c for mesons), where thermal quark recombination dominates, while above these momenta perturbative fragmentation takes over.

## Method

The paper reviews the quark recombination model, which postulates that thermalized parton degrees of freedom at the phase transition temperature recombine into hadrons via convolution of parton Wigner functions with hadron Wigner functions, using only valence quarks with constituent masses around 300 MeV. For hadron correlations, the factorization of n-parton Wigner functions into independent single-parton distributions is extended to include small 2-particle correlations via a Gaussian ansatz in azimuthal angle, with the correlation strength parameter c0 assumed much less than 1.

## Key numbers

- Transverse momentum range where recombination dominates: up to about 4 GeV/c for mesons and up to about 6 GeV/c for baryons in central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s)=200 GeV
- Energy loss estimated at up to 14 GeV/fm for a 10 GeV parton
- Correlation amplification factor Q=4 for two mesons, Q=6 for a meson-baryon pair, and Q=9 for a baryon-baryon pair
- Scaling violation variable A=(B-M)/(B+M) shows violations smaller than 5% for higher Fock states
- Kinematic windows used for correlation analysis: trigger particles 2.5 GeV/c <= P_T <= 4.0 GeV/c, associated particles 1.7 GeV/c <= P_T <= 2.5 GeV/c, with |y| < 0.35

## Context

This review extends the recombination model work of Fries, Muller, Nonaka, and Bass (2003) and Hwa and Yang (2002) by focusing on hadron correlations, which are more difficult to describe than single hadron spectra. It addresses the experimental observations from PHENIX and STAR collaborations showing that jet-like correlations at intermediate transverse momenta differ strongly from vacuum expectations, and enables future studies of how higher Fock states and gluon content affect the quark number scaling law for elliptic flow.
