# Weak magnetism for antineutrinos in supernovae

> Weak magnetism increases antineutrino mean free paths in core collapse supernovae. The parity violating interference between axial and vector currents makes antineutrino-nucleon cross sections smaller then those for neutrinos. We calculate simple, exact correction factors to include recoil and weak magnetism in supernova simulations. Weak magnetism may significantly increase the neutrino energy flux. We calculate, in a diffusion approximation, an increase of order 15% in the total energy flux for temperatures near 10 MeV. This should raise the neutrino luminosity. Weak magnetism also changes the emitted spectrum of $ \barν_x$ (with $x=μ$ or $τ$) and $\barν_e$. We estimate that $\barν_x$ will be emitted about 7% hotter than $ν_x$ because $\barν_x$ have longer mean free paths. Likewise weak magnetism may increase the $\barν_e$ temperature by of order 10%. This increase in temperature coupled with the increase in neutrino luminosity should increase the heating in the low density region outside of the neutrino sphere. This, in turn, could be important for the success of an explosion. It is important to check our results with a full simulation that includes Boltzmann neutrino transport and weak magnetism corrections.

## Key claim

Weak magnetism and recoil corrections to neutrino-nucleon cross sections, which are often neglected in supernova simulations, may significantly increase the neutrino energy flux and alter the emitted antineutrino spectra, potentially impacting the success of the explosion mechanism; however, the author emphasizes that these results should be checked with a full simulation that includes Boltzmann neutrino transport.

## Method

The author calculates exact correction factors to all orders in the neutrino energy over nucleon mass (k/M) for neutrino-nucleon scattering cross sections, including recoil, weak magnetism, nucleon form factors, and strange quark contributions. These corrections are then applied within a diffusion approximation to estimate changes in lepton number currents, energy fluxes, and emitted spectra for muon/tau neutrinos and electron antineutrinos in a supernova environment, using a simple Monte Carlo model from Raffelt (2001) for spectral estimates.

## Key numbers

- The total energy flux increases by approximately 15% for temperatures near 10 MeV due to weak magnetism and recoil corrections.
- Muon/tau antineutrinos ($\bar\nu_x$) are emitted about 7% hotter than muon/tau neutrinos ($\nu_x$), almost independent of the choice of optical depth parameter $\tau_0$.
- The electron antineutrino temperature may increase by of order 10% due to weak magnetism.
- For $\bar\nu_e$ charged current interactions, the correction factor $R_{\bar\nu_e} \approx 1 - 7.22e$ (where $e = k/M$), giving a 15% reduction in opacity for a typical neutrino energy of 20 MeV.
- For $\nu_e$, the correction factor $R_{\nu_e} \approx 1 + 1.01e$.
- The chemical potential for muon/tau neutrinos is approximately $\mu_{\nu_x} \approx \delta_c \pi^2 T^2 / (6M)$ with $\delta_c \approx 3.30$ for $\nu-n$ scattering.
- Strange quark contributions with $g_a^s = -0.2$ could reduce opacities by 21% at electron fraction $Y_e = 0.1$ or 15% at $Y_e = 0.2$.
- The muon number (number of $\nu_\mu$ minus $\bar\nu_\mu$) for the hot protoneutron star could be as large as $10^{54}$.

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations by Horowitz (1998, 2000) on weak magnetism effects in supernovae and provides exact correction factors that go beyond the first-order expansions used previously. The paper contrasts with the nonrelativistic calculations of Burrows and Sawyer (1998), which include phase space and medium corrections but no matrix element corrections from weak magnetism, and with the relativistic RPA calculations of Reddy et al. (1999), which include recoil but not weak magnetism. The results enable future supernova simulations to incorporate these free-space corrections exactly and without model dependence, which is important now that accurate Boltzmann neutrino transport algorithms are available from Rampp and Janka (2000).
