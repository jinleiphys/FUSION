# Neutrino interactions in astrophysics and the third neutrino mixing angle theta13

> The third neutrino mixing angle theta13 is the least known one. In this contribution, after a brief discussion of the current efforts to determine the value of this angle better, various astrophysical implications of a non-zero value of theta13 are summarized.

## Key claim

A non-zero value of the third neutrino mixing angle θ₁₃ has significant astrophysical implications, particularly for core-collapse supernova physics and r-process nucleosynthesis, but the exact value remains uncertain and the quoted errors are non-Gaussian when a non-zero value is reported.

## Method

The paper reviews current experimental limits on θ₁₃ from reactor experiments (Chooz, Double Chooz, Daya Bay, RENO) and accelerator experiments (T2K), as well as combined analyses of KamLAND and solar neutrino data including SNO results. It then examines the theoretical implications of a non-zero θ₁₃ for neutrino propagation in supernovae, focusing on matter-enhanced oscillations and neutrino-neutrino interactions described by a Hamiltonian with flavor creation and annihilation operators.

## Key numbers

- Current experimental limit: sin²(2θ₁₃) < 0.19 at 90% C.L. from the Chooz experiment, as reported by the Particle Data Group
- SNO low-threshold analysis gives sin² θ₁₃ < 0.057 at 95% C.L., with a best fit value of 2.00⁺²·⁰⁹₋₁·₆₃ × 10⁻²
- The range 0 ≤ sin² 2θ₁₃ ≤ 0.1 is shown in the analysis of KamLAND and solar neutrino best fit values

## Context

This work extends the discussion of neutrino mixing beyond the two-flavor analyses of KamLAND and solar neutrino data, which showed different best fit values for mass difference and mixing angle (Balantekin 2008). It connects to ongoing experimental efforts (Double Chooz, Daya Bay, RENO, T2K) and theoretical work on collective neutrino oscillations in supernovae (Duan et al. 2008). The paper enables future studies of r-process nucleosynthesis yields, which depend on the electron fraction determined by neutrino interactions, and highlights that uncertainties from θ₁₃ and the two-body axial current counter-term L₁A in SNO data analysis are comparable.
