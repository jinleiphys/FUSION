# Microscopic analysis of induced nuclear fission dynamics

> The dynamics of low-energy induced fission is explored using a consistent microscopic framework that combines the time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) and time-dependent nuclear density functional theory (TDDFT). While the former presents a fully quantum mechanical approach that describes the entire fission process as an adiabatic evolution of collective degrees of freedom, the latter models the dissipative dynamics of the final stage of fission by propagating the nucleons independently toward scission and beyond. By combining the two methods, based on the same nuclear energy density functional and pairing interaction, we perform an illustrative calculation of the charge distribution of yields and total kinetic energy for induced fission of $^{240}$Pu. For the saddle-to-scission phase a set of initial points for the TDDFT evolution is selected along an iso-energy curve beyond the outer fission barrier on the deformation energy surface, and the TDGCM is used to calculate the probability that the collective wave function reaches these points at different times. Fission observables are computed with both methods and compared with available data. The relative merits of including quantum fluctuations (TDGCM) and the one-body dissipation mechanism (TDDFT) are discussed.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that combining the time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) and time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) into a single microscopic framework for induced fission of $^{240}$Pu is actually less than optimal: quantum fluctuations from TDGCM are essential for fission yields, while dissipative effects from TDDFT are crucial for total kinetic energy, but the weak points of each approach cannot be removed even when combined.

## Method
The authors combine two microscopic approaches based on the same relativistic energy density functional PC-PK1 and monopole pairing interaction: the TDGCM with Gaussian overlap approximation (GOA) evolves the collective wave function in a two-dimensional space of quadrupole $\beta_{20}$ and octupole $\beta_{30}$ deformations, while time-dependent covariant DFT (TD(C)DFT) propagates nucleons independently in a three-dimensional lattice space with time-dependent BCS pairing. For the saddle-to-scission phase, initial points for TDDFT evolution are selected along an isoenergy curve beyond the outer fission barrier, and the TDGCM provides the probability that the collective wave function reaches these points at different times (10, 20, and 30 zs). Fission observables are computed with both methods and compared to experimental data for $^{240}$Pu.

## Key numbers
- The TDGCM calculation uses a time step of $\delta t = 5 \times 10^{-4}$ zs and charge distributions are calculated after $6 \times 10^4$ time steps, corresponding to 30 zs.
- The TD(C)DFT calculation uses a mesh spacing of 1.0 fm for all directions with box size $L_x \times L_y \times L_z = 20 \times 20 \times 60$ fm$^3$, and a time step of $6.67 \times 10^{-4}$ zs.
- The TDGCM charge yields show asymmetric peaks at $Z = 41$ and $Z = 53$, one mass unit away from the experimental peaks.
- The TD(C)DFT charge yields from the isoenergy curve 1 MeV below the equilibrium minimum produce most scission events in the intervals $Z = 40$--$42$ and $Z = 52$--$54$.
- The TDGCM total kinetic energies are systematically too large compared to data, while TD(C)DFT slightly underestimates TKE for fragments near the peaks of the charge yield distribution and considerably underestimates TKE for the tails.
- The average energy of the initial TDGCM wave packet $E_{\rm coll}^*$ is chosen approximately 1 MeV above the fission barrier (approximately 8 MeV for $^{240}$Pu), while initial points for TDDFT are on the deformation energy surface 1 or 4 MeV below the energy of the equilibrium minimum.

## Context
This work extends the suggestion by Negele (1978) and Simenel (2014) that fission could be described by combining an adiabatic model for the initial stage with a nonadiabatic method for the saddle-to-scission dynamics, and complements a semiphenomenological approach by Sadhukhan (2016) that used WKB and Langevin equations for spontaneous fission of $^{240}$Pu. The results indicate that quantum fluctuations (included in TDGCM but not TDDFT) are essential for fission yields, while one-body dissipation (included in TDDFT but not TDGCM) is crucial for total kinetic energy, pointing toward the need for a unified microscopic framework that incorporates both effects.
