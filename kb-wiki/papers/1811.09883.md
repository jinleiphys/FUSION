# Comparing a few distributions of transverse momenta in high energy collisions

> Transverse momentum spectra of particles produced in high energy collisions are very important due to their relations to the excitation degree of interacting system. To describe the transverse momentum spectra, one can use more than one probability density functions of transverse momenta, which are simply called the functions or distributions of transverse momenta in some cases. In this paper, a few distributions of transverse momenta in high energy collisions are compared with each other in terms of plots to show some quantitative differences. Meanwhile, in the framework of Tsallis statistics, the distributions of momentum components, transverse momenta, rapidities, and pasudorapidities are obtained according to the analytical and Monte Carlo methods. These analyses are useful to understand carefully different distributions in high energy collisions.

## Key claim

The paper systematically compares several families of transverse momentum distribution functions (Hagedorn, standard, and Boltzmann) with their modified forms, showing that the choice of functional form significantly affects the extracted temperature parameter and that no single form is universally superior; the authors hedge that the modified forms "measure" lower temperatures than the original functions for a given spectrum, but it is "hard to say which one is better."

## Method

The authors compare three families of transverse momentum distributions: the Hagedorn inverse power-law and three modified versions; the simplest standard distribution (with Fermi-Dirac, Maxwell-Boltzmann, and Bose-Einstein statistics) and two modified forms; and the simplest Boltzmann distribution with two modified forms. They then derive distributions of momentum components, transverse momenta, rapidities, and pseudorapidities in the framework of Tsallis statistics using both analytical integration and Monte Carlo methods, assuming isotropic emission in the rest frame. Finally, they fit selected distributions to PHENIX data for pions and protons in Au-Au collisions at 200 GeV.

## Key numbers

- Hagedorn function parameters used for comparison: \(p_1=p_2=p_3=p_4=10\) GeV/\(c\) and \(n_1=n_2=n_3=n_4=5\).
- Rest masses used in modified Hagedorn function: \(m_0=0.139\) GeV/\(c^2\) (pion) and \(m_0=0.938\) GeV/\(c^2\) (proton).
- Temperature parameters for standard and Boltzmann distributions: \(T_1=T_2=T_3=0.2\) GeV, with chemical potential \(\mu=0.1\) GeV and rapidity range \(y_{\min}=-0.5\), \(y_{\max}=0.5\).
- Tsallis statistics calculations use entropy index \(q=1.05\) and temperature \(T=0.15\) GeV for \(p_x\) and \(p_T\) distributions.
- Fits to PHENIX Au-Au 200 GeV data: Eq. (1) uses \(n_1=40, 39, 27, 35\) with \(p_1=10\) GeV/\(c\); Eq. (6) uses \(T_2=0.16, 0.13, 0.29, 0.19\) GeV; Eq. (9) uses \(T_2=0.15, 0.12, 0.29, 0.19\) GeV; Eq. (23) uses \(T=0.14, 0.12, 0.27, 0.19\) GeV with \(q=1.01\).
- Pseudorapidity distribution width from isotropic emission: \(\sigma_\eta \approx 0.91\).

## Context

This work extends the systematic comparison of transverse momentum distribution functions used in high-energy nuclear collisions, building on the Hagedorn function (Hagedorn, 1965) and Tsallis statistics formulations (Tsallis, 1988; Wilk and Wlodarczyk, 2000). The authors show that modified forms of standard distributions yield systematically lower extracted temperatures, and they demonstrate that a superposition of a soft component (from blast-wave or Tsallis models) with a hard power-law component is needed to describe spectra across the full transverse momentum range. This enables more careful extraction of freeze-out parameters in heavy-ion collisions, as applied in the authors' recent works (Wang et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2018) using blast-wave models with Boltzmann-Gibbs and Tsallis statistics.
