# Probing the momentum-dependence of the symmetry potential by the free n/p ratio of preequilibrium emission

> Based on an isospin and momentum-dependent transport model, we studied the effect of momentum-dependent symmetry potential on the free neutron to proton ratio of preequilibrium nucleon emission. It is found that for $^{132}Sn+^{124}Sn$ reaction at 400 MeV/nucleon incident beam energy, free n/p ratio of preequilibrium nucleon emission mainly probe the momentum-dependence of symmetry potential at nucleon momentum around 400 $\sim$ 600 MeV/c. Whereas for 200 MeV/nucleon incident beam energy, this observable mainly probe the momentum-dependence of symmetry potential at nucleon momentum around 200 $\sim$ 400 MeV/c. To probe the symmetry energy/potential using free n/p ratio, not all the details of the momentum-dependence of the symmetry potential are important, the values of symmetry potential at only certain momentum range are crucial for an observable. It is important to input reasonable density- and momentum-dependence of the symmetry potential according to the magnitude of incident beam energy of heavy-ion collisions. The present experimental data on the symmetry potential are not enough for probing the density-dependent symmetry energy. More experimental data (such as nucleon and nuclei's scattering experiments at different nucleonic momenta and densities) on the symmetry potential are therefore needed to pin down the density-dependent symmetry energy.

# Probing the momentum-dependence of the symmetry potential by the free n/p ratio of preequilibrium emission

## Key claim

The free neutron-to-proton (n/p) ratio of preequilibrium nucleon emission in heavy-ion collisions does not probe the momentum-dependent symmetry potential uniformly across all nucleon momenta; instead, it is sensitive only to the symmetry potential within a specific momentum window that depends on the incident beam energy. For the $^{132}Sn+^{124}Sn$ reaction at 400 MeV/nucleon, the observable mainly probes the symmetry potential at nucleon momenta around 400–600 MeV/c, while at 200 MeV/nucleon it probes the 200–400 MeV/c range.

## Method

The authors used the isospin- and momentum-dependent transport model IBUU04 to simulate $^{132}Sn+^{124}Sn$ collisions at 200 and 400 MeV/nucleon incident beam energies with an impact parameter of 1 fm. They decomposed the sensitivity of the preequilibrium free n/p ratio to the symmetry potential in momentum space by selectively turning off the symmetry potential in specific momentum intervals (0–200, 200–400, 400–600, and 600–800 MeV/c) and comparing the resulting n/p ratios to the standard calculation using the full MDI interaction with parameter $x=0$. The relative sensitivity was defined as $|R_0 - R_i| / R_0$, where $R_0$ is the standard n/p ratio and $R_i$ is the ratio obtained with the symmetry potential turned off in the $i$-th momentum region.

## Key numbers

- For $^{132}Sn+^{124}Sn$ at 400 MeV/nucleon beam energy, the maximal relative sensitivity of the free n/p ratio to the symmetry potential occurs in the momentum range 400–600 MeV/c.
- For $^{132}Sn+^{124}Sn$ at 200 MeV/nucleon beam energy, the maximal relative sensitivity occurs in the momentum range 200–400 MeV/c.
- At 200 MeV/nucleon beam energy, the relative sensitivity of the free n/p ratio is about one time larger than that at 400 MeV/nucleon beam energy.
- The experimental constraints on the symmetry potential (shaded region in Fig. 1) are described by $U = a - b E_{kin}$ with $a \simeq 22$–34 MeV and $b \simeq 0.1$–0.2, reaching up to about 450 MeV/c in momentum space.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous decomposition of symmetry energy sensitivity in density space (Liu et al., 2014), which found that the free n/p ratio mainly probes the symmetry energy/potential around $1.5\rho_0$. The present study performs a similar decomposition in momentum space, showing that the observable is sensitive only to specific momentum ranges of the symmetry potential. The results highlight that existing experimental data on the symmetry potential (from Dirac et al., 1979) are insufficient to constrain the density-dependent symmetry energy, and motivate new nucleon-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus scattering experiments at different nucleonic momenta and densities. This work enables more targeted experimental and theoretical efforts to constrain the momentum dependence of the symmetry potential, which is necessary for accurate transport model simulations of heavy-ion collisions at facilities such as NSCL, FRIB, and RIBF.
