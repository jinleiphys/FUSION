# Thermal Conductivity and Thermal Hall Effect in Dense Electron-Ion Plasma

> In this study, we examine thermal conductivity and the thermal Hall effect in electron-ion plasmas relevant to hot neutron stars, white dwarfs, and binary neutron star mergers, focusing on densities found in the outer crusts of neutron stars and the interiors of white dwarfs. We consider plasma consisting of single species of ions, which could be either iron $\isotope[56]{Fe}$ or carbon $\isotope[12]{C}$ nuclei. The temperature range explored is from the melting temperature of the solid $T\sim10^9$~K up to $10^{11}$~K. This covers both degenerate and non-degenerate electron regimes. We find that thermal conductivity increases with density and temperature for which we provide analytical scaling relations valid in different regimes. The impact of magnetic fields on thermal conductivity is also analyzed, showing anisotropy in low-density regions and the presence of the thermal Hall effect characterized by the Righi--Leduc coefficient. The transition from a degenerate to non-degenerate regime is characterized by a minimum ratio of thermal conductivity to temperature, which is analogous to the minimum observed already in the case of electrical conductivity. We provide also formulas fit to our numerical results, which can be used in dissipative magneto-hydrodynamics simulations of warm compact stars.

## Key claim

The paper provides analytical scaling relations and numerical fit formulas for thermal conductivity and the thermal Hall (Righi-Leduc) coefficient in dense electron-ion plasmas, covering both degenerate and non-degenerate regimes. The key condition is that the results apply specifically to single-species ion plasmas (iron-56 or carbon-12) at densities found in outer neutron star crusts and white dwarf interiors, with temperatures from the melting point (approximately 10^9 K) up to 10^11 K.

## Method

The authors solved the Boltzmann transport equation for electrons scattering off a single ion species in a fully ionized plasma, using the relaxation-time approximation with relativistic kinematics. They computed the thermal conductivity tensor components (parallel and perpendicular to the magnetic field) and the Righi-Leduc coefficient across a grid of densities and temperatures spanning the degenerate to non-degenerate electron transition. The numerical results were then fitted to analytical expressions for use in dissipative magneto-hydrodynamics simulations.

## Key numbers

- Temperature range explored: from the melting temperature of the solid (approximately 10^9 K) up to 10^11 K.
- Two ion species considered: iron-56 (56Fe) and carbon-12 (12C).
- Thermal conductivity increases with both density and temperature, with analytical scaling relations provided for different regimes.
- A minimum in the ratio of thermal conductivity to temperature is found at the transition from degenerate to non-degenerate regimes, analogous to the minimum previously observed for electrical conductivity.
- Magnetic fields cause anisotropy in thermal conductivity in low-density regions and produce a thermal Hall effect characterized by the Righi-Leduc coefficient.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of electrical conductivity in dense plasmas (which had already shown a minimum in conductivity-to-temperature ratio) to the thermal conductivity and thermal Hall effect. The results are directly applicable to dissipative magneto-hydrodynamics simulations of warm compact stars, including hot neutron stars, white dwarfs, and binary neutron star merger remnants. The analytical fit formulas provided enable more accurate modeling of heat transport in these astrophysical environments than was previously possible with simpler approximations.
