# Transport properties of nuclear pasta phase with quantum molecular dynamics

> We study the transport properties of nuclear pasta for a wide range of density, temperature and proton fractions, relevant for different astrophysical scenarios adopting a quantum molecular dynamics model. In particular, we estimate the values of shear viscosity as well as electrical and thermal conductivities by calculating the static structure factor $S(q)$ using simulation data. In the density and temperature range where the pasta phase appears, the static structure factor shows irregular behavior. The presence of a slab phase greatly enhances the peak in $S(q)$. However, the effect of irregularities in $S(q)$ on the transport coefficients is not very dramatic. The values of all three transport coefficients are found to have the same orders of magnitude as found in theoretical calculations for the inner crust matter of neutron stars without the pasta phase and therefore, is in contrast to earlier speculations that a pasta layer might be highly resistive, both thermally and electrically.

## Key claim

The authors find that the shear viscosity, electrical conductivity, and thermal conductivity of nuclear pasta phases all have the same orders of magnitude as those calculated for the inner crust matter of neutron stars without pasta, contradicting earlier speculations that a pasta layer might be highly resistive both thermally and electrically; however, this conclusion is qualified by the observation that the effect of irregularities in the static structure factor on the transport coefficients is "not very dramatic" rather than completely absent.

## Method

The authors performed quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations of nuclear matter at sub-saturation densities (0.1 to 0.6 times normal nuclear matter density) for temperatures from 0 to 5 MeV and three proton fractions (0.5, 0.3, and 0.1). They calculated the static structure factor S(q) for protons from the simulation trajectories, then used this S(q) to compute the Coulomb logarithms and from them the shear viscosity, electrical conductivity, and thermal conductivity for degenerate electrons scattering off protons.

## Key numbers

- Density range studied: 0.1 to 0.6 times normal nuclear matter density (rho_0 = 0.165 fm^-3)
- Temperature range studied: 0 to 5 MeV
- Proton fractions studied: 0.5 (symmetric matter), 0.3 (supernova environment), and 0.1 (neutron star crust)
- Number of nucleons per simulation box ranged from 4096 (at 0.1 rho_0) to 24576 (at 0.6 rho_0)
- Box size fixed at 62.47 fm for all densities
- Peak static structure factor S(q_peak) for protons at T=0, Y_p=0.5 ranged from 116.60 (at 0.1 rho_0) down to 30.48 (at 0.6 rho_0)
- Coulomb logarithm for shear viscosity (Lambda_ep^eta) at Y_p=0.5 ranged from 16.61 (at 0.1 rho_0, T=0) down to 0.59 (at 0.6 rho_0, T=5)
- Coulomb logarithm for thermal/electrical conductivity (Lambda_ep^kappa) at Y_p=0.5 ranged from 18.06 (at 0.1 rho_0, T=0) down to 0.62 (at 0.6 rho_0, T=5)
- At rho=0.4 rho_0, Y_p=0.3, T=2 MeV, the proton S(q_peak) reached 350.1 due to slab formation

## Context

This work extends dynamical studies of nuclear pasta transport properties beyond the limited scope of Horowitz (2008) and Horowitz (2015), who estimated shear viscosity and thermal conductivity for only a few densities and a single temperature and proton fraction. The results contradict earlier speculations (referenced as Pons 2013 and others) that pasta layers would be highly resistive. The calculated transport coefficients enable more realistic modeling of neutron star cooling behavior, magnetic field decay, and viscous damping of neutron star oscillations, and the neutron static structure factors provided are relevant for neutrino transport in core-collapse supernovae as studied by Horowitz (2004a, 2004b).
