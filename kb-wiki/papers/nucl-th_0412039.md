# The importance of few-nucleon physics at low energy

> This manuscript originated from the discussion at the workshop on the "Future of Few-body Low Energy Experimental Physics" (FFLEEP), which was held at the University of Trento on December 4-7, 2002 and has been written in its present form on March 19, 2003. It illustrates a selection of theoretical advancements in the nuclear few-body problem, including two- and many-nucleon interactions, the three-nucleon bound and scattering system, the four-body problem, the A-body (A$>$4) problem, and fields of related interest, such as reactions of astrophysical interest and few-neutron systems. Particular attention is called to the contradictory situation one experiences in this field: while theory is currently advancing and has the potential to inspire new experiments, the experimental activity is nevertheless rapidly phasing out. If such a trend will continue, advancements in this area will become critically difficult.

## Key claim

The paper argues that few-nucleon physics at low energy is a flourishing field with accurate theoretical methods and relatively low experimental costs, yet experimental activity is rapidly phasing out, which will make future advancements critically difficult if this trend continues.

## Method

The authors review theoretical advancements in the nuclear few-body problem, covering two- and many-nucleon interactions, three-nucleon bound and scattering systems, the four-body problem, and A-body systems with A greater than 4. They also discuss reactions of astrophysical relevance and few-neutron systems, using selected illustrative examples rather than a comprehensive review to highlight open problems and the need for continued experimental support.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy of $^4$He calculated with CD-Bonn NN potential: 26.26 MeV, compared to experimental value 28.30 MeV (underbinding by approximately 2 MeV)
- Binding energy of $^4$He calculated with AV18 NN potential: 24.1 MeV, compared to experimental value 28.30 MeV
- Binding energy of $^3$H with CD-Bonn + TM' 3N-force: 8.48 MeV, matching the experimental value of 8.48 MeV
- Binding energy of $^4$He with AV18 + Urbana IX 3N-force: 28.50 MeV, compared to experimental value 28.30 MeV
- $nn$-scattering length: approximately -18 fm
- Discrepancy in vector analyzing power $A_y$ for $Nd$ scattering at $E_{lab} \le 2$ MeV: theoretical predictions underpredict data by approximately 30 percent at the maximum of angular distributions, increasing to approximately 40 percent at $E_{lab} = 650$ keV
- Elastic $nd$ scattering data shown at 65 MeV and 135 MeV, and $pd$ elastic scattering at 1 MeV

## Context

This paper extends the long line of work on realistic NN-potentials such as CD-Bonn, Nijmegen I, and AV18, which underbind light nuclei when used alone, and reviews the inclusion of three-nucleon forces like Tucson-Melbourne (TM) and Urbana IX that were adjusted to reproduce the $^3$H binding energy. The work enables future experimental programs at low-energy accelerator facilities to test chiral effective field theory predictions, resolve the $A_y$-puzzle in $Nd$ scattering, and investigate the possible existence of multi-neutron clusters, building on earlier studies of tetra-neutron systems via double charge exchange reactions.
