# Pion loop fluctuations of constituent quarks and baryons

> The implications of the pion (meson) degrees of freedom for baryon properties, taken at the constituent quark and baryon levels are compared. It is shown that there is a dramatic qualitative difference between two approaches.

## Key claim

The paper argues that treating pion (meson) loop fluctuations at the constituent quark level yields a dramatically different and more physically consistent picture of baryon properties than treating them directly at the baryon level, because the pion-quark coupling constant is small enough for perturbative convergence while the pion-baryon coupling is not.

## Method

The authors compare two approaches to incorporating pion degrees of freedom in baryon structure: the traditional baryon-level approach (strong coupling theory, chiral perturbation theory for baryons) and the constituent quark-level approach (chiral constituent quark model). They examine the coupling constants, convergence properties, intermediate state requirements, and the physical origin of key baryon observables such as mass splittings, magnetic moments, and charge radii in both frameworks.

## Key numbers

- The πNN coupling constant is large: g²_πNN/4π ∼ 14, indicating divergent loop expansion at the baryon level
- The πQQ coupling constant is relatively small: g²_πQQ/4π ∼ 0.6–0.7, enabling convergent perturbation theory at the quark level
- The small parameter (m_Q/Λ_χ)² ∼ 0.1 guarantees loop corrections to naive quark model predictions are not large
- Loop corrections to the proton and neutron anomalous magnetic moments are at the level of 5–10%
- The ratio μ_n/μ_p from the quark-level approach comes out only 2% above the empirical ratio 0.68, while the naive quark model prediction of 2/3 is 2% below
- The kaon loop fluctuations of valence u and d constituent quarks induce a strangeness magnetic moment of the proton in the range -0.01 to -0.05

## Context

This work extends the chiral constituent quark model approach developed by Glozman and collaborators, which explains the low-lying baryon spectrum through the short-range part of pion-exchange interaction combined with SU(6) wave functions demanded by the large N_c limit of QCD. It contrasts with traditional hadronic approaches such as the Skyrme model, chiral bag models, and baryon chiral perturbation theory, which suffer from divergent loop expansions and require the full infinite tower of radially excited baryon states as intermediate states to reproduce the short-range meson exchange interaction. The paper enables a consistent treatment of pion cloud effects on baryon observables that preserves the successful SU(6) quark model predictions while adding small, controlled corrections.
