# Do we need dense matter equation of state in curved spacetime for neutron stars?

> Neutron stars are regarded as natural laboratories for the study of dense strong interaction matter. The equation of state (EoS) of dense matter computed in flat spacetime is used to predict the structure of neutron stars by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation. Recently, it has been reported that the curved spacetime effect or specifically gravitational time dilation effect on the EoS of dense matter leads to a significant increase of the maximum mass limit of neutron stars [Phys. Rev. D \textbf{104}, 123005 (2021) and J. Cosmol. Astropart. Phys. 02 (2021) 026]. However, in this work, we show that to study the hydrostatic equilibrium of dense matter within the framework of general relativity and relativistic fluid dynamics, the grand canonical EoS of dense matter, $p(T,μ)$, should be the same as that computed in flat spacetime, otherwise it is not consistent with local thermodynamic relations and energy-momentum conservation of the fluid. The gravitation influences the pressure $p$ only through enhancing the temperature $T$ and the chemical potential $μ$, known as Tolman's law and Klein's law. We rewrite the TOV equation as an alternative version so that the grand canonical EoS computed by using field theoretical methods can be used as a direct input. This may provide a tool to study the grand canonical EoS of dense matter via deep learning.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the grand canonical equation of state (EoS) of dense matter, \(p(T,\mu)\), used to model neutron stars must be the same as that computed in flat spacetime; any explicit dependence of the EoS on the gravitational potential \(\Phi(r)\) is incompatible with local thermodynamic relations and energy-momentum conservation, contrary to recent claims by Hossain (2021) that curved spacetime effects significantly increase the maximum mass limit.

## Method

The authors derive the condition \(\partial p(T,\{\mu_i\}; r)/\partial r = 0\) by comparing the conservation equation \(\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0\) (which yields \(dp/dr = -(p+\varepsilon) d\Phi/dr\)) with the chain rule for \(dp/dr\) expressed in terms of the partial derivatives of \(p(T,\{\mu_i\}; r)\) with respect to \(T\), \(\mu_i\), and \(r\), using the fundamental thermodynamic relation \(\varepsilon = Ts + \sum_i \mu_i n_i - p\). They then rewrite the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation in terms of the baryon chemical potential \(\mu_{\rm B}\) as \(d\mu_i/dr = -G\mu_i (m+4\pi r^3 p)/[r^2(1-2Gm/r)]\), and demonstrate its solution using the Walecka model mean-field approximation for neutron star matter under \(\beta\)-equilibrium and charge neutrality.

## Key numbers

* The most massive observed neutron star, in the binary system PSR J0740+6620, has a mass of \(2.08 \pm 0.07 M_\odot\) (with \(M_\odot\) the solar mass).
* The gravitational potential at the surface, \(\Phi_{\rm s}\), is approximately \(-0.5\) for central baryon densities \(n_{\rm B}^{\rm c} > 1\ {\rm fm}^{-3}\) in the Walecka model.
* The Chandrasekhar limit is given as \(1.4 M_\odot\), and the estimated upper mass limit for neutron stars is \(2.16 M_\odot\).

## Context

This work clarifies a fundamental consistency issue in neutron star modeling, directly contradicting the approach of Hossain (2021) and Hossain (2021) which claimed that gravitational time dilation modifies the EoS itself and raises the maximum mass. The authors show that the standard framework, using a flat-spacetime EoS as input to the TOV equation, is self-consistent when the gravitational redshift of temperature (Tolman's law) and chemical potential (Klein's law) is properly accounted for. This enables the direct use of grand canonical EoS from field-theoretical methods and suggests a pathway for extracting the dense matter EoS via deep learning.
