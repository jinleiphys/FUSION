# Spontaneous double alpha decay: First experimental limit and prospects of investigation

> Nuclear decays with simultaneous emission of two alpha particles are energetically possible for a number of nuclides. Prospects of searching for such kind of decay for nuclides present in the natural isotopic composition of elements are discussed here. The first experimental limit on half-life for 2alpha decay is set for 209Bi as T1/2 > 2.9e20 y at 90% C.L., using the data of work [P. de Marcillac et al., Nature 422 (2003) 876]. Theoretical T1/2 estimations for the process are also given. Using these values, which are on the level of 1e33 y or more, one can conclude that the prospects of experimental observation of 2alpha decay are very pessimistic.

# Spontaneous double alpha decay: First experimental limit and prospects of investigation

## Key claim
The first experimental limit on spontaneous double alpha decay is set for bismuth-209, but the theoretical half-life estimates for all candidate nuclides are on the order of 10^33 years or more, making experimental observation of this decay mode extremely unlikely under current and foreseeable techniques.

## Method
The author analyzed atomic mass data from the 2017 Atomic Mass Evaluation to identify 80 naturally abundant nuclides for which double alpha decay is energetically possible, calculating Q-values and theoretical half-lives using semi-empirical cluster decay formulae for beryllium-8 emission from Poenaru (2002). An experimental limit was derived by re-analyzing data from the 2003 BGO scintillating bolometer measurement by de Marcillac et al. that first observed single alpha decay of bismuth-209, applying the Feldman-Cousins statistical method for zero observed events in the expected energy region.

## Key numbers
- First experimental limit for double alpha decay of bismuth-209: T1/2 > 2.9 × 10^20 years at 90% confidence level, derived from 100 hours of measurement with a 45.7 g Bi4Ge3O12 crystal containing 8.84 × 10^22 bismuth-209 nuclei
- Energy release Q2alpha for bismuth-209: 3292.2 ± 3.5 keV
- Theoretical half-life for bismuth-209 double alpha decay: 4.3 × 10^113 years
- Most promising theoretical half-life among naturally abundant candidates: 1.5 × 10^33 years for protactinium-231 (Q2alpha = 10192.2 ± 2.6 keV)
- Number of isotopes for which double alpha decay is energetically possible: 1459 out of 3436 listed in the 2017 Atomic Mass Evaluation
- Maximum Q2alpha value among all nuclides: 22.7 MeV for copernicium-276

## Context
This work extends the theoretical discussion of double alpha decay first raised by Novikov (1979) and Berezinsky (1988), and provides the first experimental search for this decay mode. The author uses the same experimental data from de Marcillac et al. (Nature, 2003) that demonstrated single alpha decay of bismuth-209 to set the double alpha decay limit. The theoretical estimates follow the cluster decay formalism of Poenaru (2002) for beryllium-8 emission, and the results are consistent within 1-2 orders of magnitude with earlier estimates by Berezinsky (1988) for nuclides such as samarium-148, gadolinium-152, dysprosium-156, platinum-190, and uranium-234. A very recent microscopic calculation by Mercier et al. (2021) using energy density functionals suggests half-lives for polonium-212 and radium-224 that are 13-20 orders of magnitude shorter than the semi-empirical estimates, potentially improving prospects for experimental investigation.
