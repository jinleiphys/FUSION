# Exact solution of the nuclear pairing problem

> In many applications to finite Fermi-systems, the pairing problem has to be treated exactly. We suggest a numerical method of exact solution based on SU(2) quasispin algebras and demonstrate its simplicity and practicality. We show that the treatment of binding energies with the use of the exact pairing and uncorrelated monopole contribution of other residual interactions can serve as an effective alternative to the full shell-model diagonalization in spherical nuclei. A self-consistent combination of the exactly treated pairing and Hartree-Fock method is discussed. Results for Sn isotopes indicate a good agreement with experimental data.

# Exact solution of the nuclear pairing problem

## Key claim
The authors present an exact numerical method for solving the general pairing Hamiltonian using SU(2) quasispin algebras that is fast and practical for realistic nuclear systems, claiming that it makes approximate methods such as BCS unnecessary for many applications. The method is exact only for the pairing part of the Hamiltonian; other residual interactions are treated approximately via a monopole contribution, and the full agreement with experiment depends on this combined treatment.

## Method
The method exploits the SU(2) quasispin algebra for each degenerate single-particle j-level, where the pairing Hamiltonian is rewritten in terms of quasispin operators L⁺ⱼ, L⁻ⱼ, and Lᶻⱼ. The partial seniority sⱼ (number of unpaired particles in each j-shell) becomes a good quantum number, and the Hamiltonian matrix is constructed in the basis of seniority eigenstates |{sⱼ}, {Nⱼ}⟩, with diagonal and off-diagonal matrix elements given by closed-form expressions using angular momentum algebra. Diagonalization is performed separately for each seniority representation, yielding exact eigenstates of the pairing Hamiltonian, and the resulting degenerate states can be further classified by total angular momentum.

## Key numbers
- For ¹¹⁶Sn (half-filled shell), the full many-body space contains 601,080,390 states, of which 272,828 have spin zero; the exact pairing method reduces this to only 420 independent seniority sets, with the largest matrix (s=0) having dimension 110.
- Near the ⁴⁸Ca shell closure, there is a 4.8 MeV gap between f₇/₂ and higher single-particle orbitals; the exact pairing solution shows almost 2 MeV of condensation energy where BCS gives zero pairing energy.
- Ground state energies from the EP plus monopole method for Ca isotopes differ from full shell-model diagonalization using the FPD6 interaction by less than 0.5 MeV.
- Single-particle energies used for Ca isotopes (relative to ⁴⁸Ca): -9.9 MeV (f₇/₂), -5.1 MeV (p₃/₂), -1.6 MeV (f₅/₂), and -3.1 MeV (p₁/₂).
- Single-hole energies for Sn isotopes (based on ¹³¹Sn data): -9.76, -8.98, -7.33, -7.66, and -7.57 MeV for the five valence orbitals.

## Context
This work extends the quasispin formalism originally developed by Racah (1942) and applied in shell-model contexts by Kerman, Lawson, and Macfarlane (1961) and Auerbach (1965). It provides a practical alternative to the BCS method of Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer (1957), which suffers from particle-number violation and a sharp phase transition in small systems, and to the Richardson method (1963) which is formally exact but mathematically complex. The method enables fast exact treatment of pairing in heavy nuclei such as Sn isotopes, and can be combined self-consistently with Hartree-Fock calculations (using the SKX interaction of Brown, 1998) or with monopole contributions from realistic interactions such as FPD6 (Richter et al., 1991) and G-matrices derived from the CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon interaction (Machleidt, Sammarruca, and Song, 1996).
