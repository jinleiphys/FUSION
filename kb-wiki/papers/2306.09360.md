# Strong Interaction Physics at the Luminosity Frontier with 22 GeV Electrons at Jefferson Lab

> This document presents the initial scientific case for upgrading the Continuous Electron Beam Accelerator Facility (CEBAF) at Jefferson Lab (JLab) to 22 GeV. It is the result of a community effort, incorporating insights from a series of workshops conducted between March 2022 and April 2023. With a track record of over 25 years in delivering the world's most intense and precise multi-GeV electron beams, CEBAF's potential for a higher energy upgrade presents a unique opportunity for an innovative nuclear physics program, which seamlessly integrates a rich historical background with a promising future. The proposed physics program encompass a diverse range of investigations centered around the nonperturbative dynamics inherent in hadron structure and the exploration of strongly interacting systems. It builds upon the exceptional capabilities of CEBAF in high-luminosity operations, the availability of existing or planned Hall equipment, and recent advancements in accelerator technology. The proposed program cover various scientific topics, including Hadron Spectroscopy, Partonic Structure and Spin, Hadronization and Transverse Momentum, Spatial Structure, Mechanical Properties, Form Factors and Emergent Hadron Mass, Hadron-Quark Transition, and Nuclear Dynamics at Extreme Conditions, as well as QCD Confinement and Fundamental Symmetries. Each topic highlights the key measurements achievable at a 22 GeV CEBAF accelerator. Furthermore, this document outlines the significant physics outcomes and unique aspects of these programs that distinguish them from other existing or planned facilities. In summary, this document provides an exciting rationale for the energy upgrade of CEBAF to 22 GeV, outlining the transformative scientific potential that lies within reach, and the remarkable opportunities it offers for advancing our understanding of hadron physics and related fundamental phenomena.

## Key claim

The paper presents the scientific case for upgrading the CEBAF accelerator at Jefferson Lab from 12 GeV to 22 GeV, arguing that such a facility would be uniquely capable of addressing non-perturbative QCD dynamics at the luminosity frontier, but the full accelerator design is not yet completed and multiple technical challenges remain, particularly regarding beam extraction methods and the balance between user needs, technical feasibility, and cost.

## Method

The proposed upgrade replaces the highest-energy recirculating arcs (Arc 9 and Arc A) with Fixed Field Alternating Gradient (FFA) arcs using Halbach-style permanent magnets, increasing the number of recirculations through the accelerating cavities from 5 passes to 10 passes while keeping the energy gain per pass unchanged. A new 650 MeV 3-pass recirculating injector based on the existing LERF facility replaces the old 123 MeV injector, and the LINAC focusing optics are reconfigured with triplet cells using 45 Tesla/m quadrupoles scaled with momentum to cover an energy ratio of 1:33. The design supports simultaneous transport of 6 passes with energies spanning a factor of two through the FFA arcs, which use 86 repeating 3.15-meter-long FODO cells with combined function magnets.

## Key numbers

- The proposed upgrade increases CEBAF energy from the present 12 GeV to about 22 GeV
- The new injector energy is 650 MeV (replacing the old 123 MeV injector)
- The FFA arcs consist of 86 repeating cells, each 3.15 meters long
- The beam pipe aperture is about 4 cm
- The LINAC focusing uses quadrupoles with a gradient of 45 Tesla/m
- The design covers an energy ratio of 1:33 in the LINAC optics
- After 10 passes at 21.9 GeV, the normalized horizontal emittance is 49 mm·mrad with a relative energy spread of 0.11%
- After 10.5 passes at 22.9 GeV, the normalized horizontal emittance is 61 mm·mrad with a relative energy spread of 0.12%
- The proposed 22 GeV, 10-pass design promises a total normalized emittance of 81 mm·mrad with a relative energy spread of 1.5 × 10^(-3)
- Beyond 22 GeV, energy loss per electron due to synchrotron radiation reaches 0.9 GeV, comparable to the energy gain per LINAC
- The FFA arc packing factor is increased to about 92% to mitigate synchrotron radiation effects

## Context

This document extends the CEBAF energy upgrade concept beyond the previous 6-to-12 GeV upgrade, building on the successful demonstration of multi-pass FFA beam transport by the CBETA project (Cornell Brookhaven Electron Test Accelerator, Bartnik 2020). The proposed 22 GeV CEBAF would complement high-energy facilities like the Electron-Ion Collider by providing unique capabilities for studying non-perturbative hadron structure at the luminosity frontier, enabling measurements of hadron spectroscopy, partonic structure, hadronization, form factors, and QCD confinement that are not possible at other existing or planned facilities.
