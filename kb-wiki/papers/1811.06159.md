# Understanding the energy resolution of liquid argon neutrino detectors

> Available estimates for the energy resolution of DUNE vary by as much as a factor of four. To address this controversy, and to connect the resolution to the underlying physical processes, we build an independent simulation pipeline for neutrino events in liquid argon, combining the public tools GENIE and FLUKA. Using this pipeline, we first characterize the channels of non-hermeticity of DUNE, including subthreshold particles, charge recombination, and nuclear breakup. Particular attention is paid to the role of neutrons, which are responsible for a large fraction of missing energy in all channels. Next, we determine energy resolution, by quantifying event-to-event stochastic fluctuations in missing energy. This is done for several sets of assumptions about the reconstruction performance, including those available in the literature. The resulting migration matrices, connecting true and reconstructed neutrino energies, are presented. Finally, we quantify the impact of different improvements on the experimental performance. For example, we show that dropping particle identification information degrades the resolution by a factor of two, while omitting charge deposits from de-excitation gammas worsens it by about 25%. In the future, this framework can be used to assess the impact of cross section uncertainties on the oscillation sensitivity.

## Key claim
The energy resolution of DUNE depends critically on event-by-event stochastic fluctuations in missing energy, not just average energy loss; the resolution estimates in the literature vary by a factor of four because different studies make different assumptions about reconstruction performance, particle thresholds, and whether information about event composition is retained.

## Method
The authors built an independent simulation pipeline combining GENIE (version 2.12.8) for primary neutrino-nucleus interactions on argon and FLUKA (version 2011.2x.2) for particle propagation in liquid argon, with propagation thresholds set to 0.05 MeV. They simulated charged-current neutrino and antineutrino events at DUNE-relevant energies (1-4 GeV) within a 12 m × 14.5 m × 58 m detector geometry, characterizing missing energy channels (subthreshold particles, charge recombination, nuclear breakup, and neutrons) and quantifying event-to-event fluctuations to produce migration matrices connecting true and reconstructed neutrino energies under different reconstruction assumptions.

## Key numbers
- Available estimates for DUNE energy resolution vary by as much as a factor of four between different studies.
- Dropping particle identification information degrades the energy resolution by a factor of two.
- Omitting charge deposits from de-excitation gammas worsens the resolution by about 25%.
- For 4 GeV neutrino scattering on argon, the average hadronic energy is 1.6 GeV for neutrinos and 1.0 GeV for antineutrinos.
- On average, 40% of neutrino energy goes into the hadronic system in νN CC scattering, while for antineutrinos the average hadronic energy fraction is only 25%.
- Prompt neutrons account for 19% energy loss from the primary interaction at 4 GeV neutrino energy.
- A 4 GeV muon travels approximately 20 meters in liquid argon (energy loss rate ~2 MeV/cm).
- The radiation length in liquid argon is 14 cm, with critical energy for electrons of 32 MeV; shower maximum occurs at 48 cm for 1 GeV electrons and 68 cm for 3 GeV electrons.
- For 1 GeV electrons, 97% of energy is deposited within 1.5 m from the primary vertex; for 3 GeV, the corresponding distance is 1.7 m.
- The containment fraction for muons at Eν=3 GeV is 2/3.
- Muon energy resolution from contained tracks is ~5%, while for partially contained muons estimates range from 18% to 30%.

## Context
This work addresses the controversy in DUNE energy resolution estimates, which range from ~5% in the total-charge method of Sorel (2014) and De Romeri (2016) to much worse values in the DUNE Conceptual Design Report (Acciarri, 2015) and the Fast Monte Carlo approach. It extends earlier studies of missing energy in primary neutrino interactions by Ankowski (2015) that focused on prompt neutrons and particle thresholds but did not quantify fluctuations. The framework enables future assessment of cross section uncertainties on oscillation sensitivity and can inform experimental priorities by distinguishing improvable effects (simulation volume, thresholds, information retention) from intrinsic limitations of liquid argon detection.
