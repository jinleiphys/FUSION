# Relative contributions of the weak, main and fission-recycling r-process

> There has been a persistent conundrum in attempts to model the nucleosynthesis of heavy elements by rapid neutron capture (the $r$-process). Although the location of the abundance peaks near nuclear mass numbers 130 and 195 identify an environment of rapid neutron capture near closed nuclear shells, the abundances of elements just above and below those peaks are often underproduced by more than an order of magnitude in model calculations. At the same time there is a debate in the literature as to what degree the $r$-process elements are produced in supernovae or the mergers of binary neutron stars. In this paper we propose a novel solution to both problems. We demonstrate that the underproduction of elements above and below the $r$-process peaks characteristic in the main or weak $r$-process events (like magnetohydrodynamic jets or neutrino-driven winds in core-collapse supernovae) can be supplemented via fission fragment distributions from the recycling of material in a neutron-rich environment such as that encountered in neutron star mergers. In this paradigm, the abundance peaks themselves are well reproduced by a moderately neutron rich, main $r$-process environment such as that encountered in the magnetohydrodynamical jets in supernovae supplemented with a high-entropy, weakly neutron rich environment such as that encountered in the neutrino-driven-wind model to produce the lighter $r$-process isotopes. Moreover, we show that the relative contributions to the $r$-process abundances in both the solar-system and metal-poor stars from the weak, main, and fission-recycling environments required by this proposal are consistent with estimates of the relative Galactic event rates of core-collapse supernovae for the weak and main $r$-process and neutron star mergers for the fission-recycling $r$-process.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the persistent underproduction of elements just above and below the r-process abundance peaks near mass numbers 130 and 195 in main or weak r-process models can be resolved by adding a fission-recycling component from neutron-rich environments such as neutron star mergers, and that the relative contributions of the weak, main, and fission-recycling r-processes to solar-system and metal-poor star abundances are consistent with estimated Galactic event rates of core-collapse supernovae and neutron star mergers.

## Method

The authors performed r-process nucleosynthesis simulations using three distinct astrophysical environments: a neutrino-driven wind model from a 1.8 solar mass supernova core for the weak r-process, a magnetohydrodynamic jet model from core-collapse supernovae for the main r-process, and a binary neutron star merger outflow model based on 3D smoothed-particle hydrodynamics simulations for the fission-recycling r-process. They employed a nuclear network code with beta-decay rates, beta-delayed neutron emission probabilities, and fission fragment distributions from a semi-empirical model that can produce single-humped, bimodal, or trimodal fission yields, using the KTUY mass model and a two-center shell model for fission barriers.

## Key numbers

- The best-fit weighting factors for the combined abundance pattern are f_Fission = 0.16 and f_Weak = 4.3, corresponding to approximately 79% weak r-process, 18% main r-process, and 3% fission-recycling r-process contributions.
- The ejected mass from the binary neutron star merger (two 1.0 solar mass neutron stars) is approximately 0.01 solar masses.
- The fission fragment distribution model uses a Gaussian width parameter sigma = 7.0 and prompt neutron loss N_loss = 2, based on measured experiments on actinides.
- Varying the Gaussian width parameter sigma from 4 to 14 changes the rare-earth peak abundance by only +20% to -25%.
- Approximately 90% of the A=160 abundance bump in the fission-recycling model comes from asymmetric fission of more than 200 different parent nuclei, while only about 10% comes from termination of the r-process path at N=212 and Z=111.
- The r-process path in the neutron star merger calculations proceeds to nuclei with A approximately 320 before fission termination.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing debate over r-process sites by addressing the underproduction problem near the A=130 and A=195 abundance peaks that has been present in r-process calculations since the 1970s (Mathews 1985). The paper builds on prior studies of magnetohydrodynamic jet models (Nishimura 2006, 2015; Fujimoto 2007, 2008; Winteler 2012), neutrino-driven wind models (Woosley 1994; Wanajo 2013), and neutron star merger models (Goriely 2011, 2013; Korobkin 2012; Wanajo 2014), and enables a quantitative method to determine the relative contributions of different r-process environments to observed abundances in the solar system and metal-poor stars.
