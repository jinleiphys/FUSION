# On the positivity of Fourier transforms

> Characterizing in a constructive way the set of real functions whose Fourier transforms are positive appears to be yet an open problem. Some sufficient conditions are known but they are far from being exhaustive. We propose two constructive sets of necessary conditions for positivity of the Fourier transforms and test their ability of constraining the positivity domain. One uses analytic continuation and Jensen inequalities and the other deals with Toeplitz determinants and the Bochner theorem. Applications are discussed, including the extension to the two-dimensional Fourier-Bessel transform and the problem of positive reciprocity, i.e. positive functions with positive transforms.

## Key claim

The paper provides two constructive sets of necessary conditions for a real function to have a positive Fourier transform, but explicitly states that a complete constructive characterization of such functions remains an open problem; the conditions are necessary but not sufficient, and the authors test their ability to constrain the positivity domain rather than fully determine it.

## Method

The authors derive two families of necessary inequalities that any function ψ(r) must satisfy if its Fourier transform φ(s) is positive. The first method ("matrix method") applies Bochner's theorem on positive-definite functions to a hierarchy of symmetric Toeplitz matrices built from ψ at equidistant points, requiring all determinants to be positive. The second method ("analytic method") uses analytic continuation of ψ onto the imaginary axis combined with Jensen's inequality for convex functions, yielding lower bounds on ψ(ir) in terms of ψ(0) and the first moment of φ, which is expressed as an integral over ψ itself. Both methods are tested numerically on combinations of harmonic oscillator eigenstates with up to five components.

## Key numbers

- Out of 951 grid points with positive ψ in the 3-component test, 520 correspond to simultaneous ψ>0 and φ>0, while 431 have φ with negative parts.
- The double maximality condition (ψ(0) and ψ₂(0) being maxima) eliminates 271 out of 431 non-positive cases, leaving 160 "rebels" undetected.
- With convolution parameter b=1/2, the cosh bound for ψ_{b2} alone detects 79 out of 160 rebels; for ψ_{b4} alone, 80 detections; combined, 94/160 ≈ 59% detection.
- In the 5-component random test (4388 positive cases, 21988 negative cases), the 3-matrix Bochner test on ten associated functions detects 19653 out of 21988 cases, giving ≈ 89% efficiency.
- The cosh test alone on ten associated functions detects 15887 out of 21988 cases, giving ≈ 72% efficiency.
- Combining 5th order Bochner test on ten associates with convexity bound tests yields ≈ 93% detection (20468 out of 21988).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Giraud and Peschanski, 2014) which used Gaussian times Hermite polynomial combinations to explore the positivity space, and addresses the long-standing open problem of characterizing functions with positive Fourier transforms. Known sufficient conditions such as convexity (Lafforgue) are acknowledged to cover only a limited part of the solution space. The paper provides practical necessary criteria that can be applied directly in ψ-space without numerical Fourier transforms, and extends the analysis to two-dimensional Fourier-Bessel transforms relevant to gluon distribution and dipole scattering in particle physics (Kovchegov). The results enable systematic filtering of candidate functions for positivity, though the complete constructive characterization remains unsolved.
