# Cascading nuclear excitation of \(^{235}\text{U}\) via inelastic electron scattering in laser-irradiated clusters

> Nuclear excitation induced by lasers holds broad application prospects in precision metrology and nuclear energy, such as nuclear batteries and nuclear clocks. In the present work, the nuclear excitation via inelastic electron scattering (NEIES) mechanism in \(^{235}\text{U}\) is investigated by combining the Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater method for theoretical calculations with 2D3V particle-in-cell (PIC) simulations for numerical modeling. The excitation cross-sections of \(^{235}\text{U}\) from the ground state to excited states is evaluated and an efficient indirect excitation scheme for generating \(^{235}\text{U}\) isomers is proposed by first exciting the nuclei from ground-state to high-energy excited states and then decays to the isomeric state. The calculations show that laser-tuned multi-channel NEIES can boost isomeric state accumulation efficiency by eleven orders of magnitude over direct excitation. Additionally, PIC simulations reveal that laser polarization significantly alters high-energy electron distributions. At identical intensity, p-polarized lasers generate seven times more electrons with energies sufficient for exciting $^{235}\text{U}$ to the second excited state (and beyond to the fourth state) compared to s-polarization. This work offers a practical and feasible way for efficient isomeric state generation, supporting experimental tests of laser-driven nuclear excitation in sub-TW lasers.

# Cascading nuclear excitation of \(^{235}\text{U}\) via inelastic electron scattering in laser-irradiated clusters

## Key claim

The authors propose that laser-tuned multi-channel nuclear excitation via inelastic electron scattering (NEIES) can boost isomeric state accumulation efficiency in \(^{235}\text{U}\) by eleven orders of magnitude over direct excitation, but only if the electron energy is tuned to first populate higher-energy excited states (up to the fourth excited state at 81.724 keV) which then cascade decay to the isomeric state, rather than attempting direct ground-to-isomer excitation.

## Method

The authors combined Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater (DHFS) theoretical calculations of electron-nucleus interaction potentials and excitation cross-sections for \(^{235}\text{U}\) with 2D3V particle-in-cell (PIC) simulations using the EPOCH code to model laser-irradiated uranium clusters. They calculated excitation cross-sections for transitions from the ground state to the first four excited states (T01, T021, T031, T041 channels) for neutral \(^{235}\text{U}\) atoms and several ionic states (\(^{235}\text{U}^{7+}\), \(^{235}\text{U}^{30+}\), \(^{235}\text{U}^{92+}\)), then simulated electron energy spectra and density distributions from laser-cluster interactions at intensities of \(1 \times 10^{17}\) and \(1 \times 10^{18}\ \text{W/cm}^2\) with s- and p-polarized pulses.

## Key numbers

- The isomeric state of \(^{235}\text{U}\) has an excitation energy of 76 eV and a half-life of 26 minutes, while the second excited state (13.0339 keV) has a half-life of 0.5 ns and the third excited state (51.6968 keV) has a half-life of 191 ps.
- The fourth excited state lies at 81.724 keV; the total reaction cross-section for all channels reaches a peak at an electron energy of approximately 81 keV.
- Laser-tuned multi-channel NEIES boosts isomeric state accumulation efficiency by eleven orders of magnitude over direct excitation.
- At identical laser intensity, p-polarized lasers generate seven times more electrons with energies sufficient for exciting \(^{235}\text{U}\) to the second excited state (and beyond to the fourth state) compared to s-polarization.
- The simulated \(^{235}\text{U}\) cluster had a radius of 20 nm, number density \(n_0 = 2.35 \times 10^{22}\ \text{cm}^{-3}\), containing approximately \(7.87 \times 10^5\) target atoms.
- The reduced transition probability from the isomeric state to the ground state is \(B(E3) = 0.036\ \text{W.u.}\); all other transitions were set to \(B = 1\ \text{W.u.}\)
- PIC simulations used laser pulse parameters: Gaussian temporal profile with 20 fs FWHM, wavelength 800 nm, and peak intensities of \(1 \times 10^{17}\ \text{W/cm}^2\) and \(1 \times 10^{18}\ \text{W/cm}^2\).

## Context

This work extends previous studies on nuclear excitation via inelastic electron scattering in \(^{235}\text{U}\) that focused on low-energy ranges and direct excitation from the ground state to the isomeric state (PhysRevC.106.064604). It also builds on work by Qi et al. (PhysRevC.110.L051601) showing that laser-matter interactions can generate electrons with energies on the 100 eV scale. The proposed cascading excitation scheme enables experimental tests of laser-driven nuclear excitation using sub-TW lasers, avoiding the need for large accelerator facilities, and could support applications in nuclear batteries and nuclear clocks. The authors note that while \(^{229}\text{Th}\) has been the primary focus for nuclear clock research due to its 8.36 eV transition, it is extremely rare in nature, whereas \(^{235}\text{U}\) offers a more accessible alternative with its long-lived isomeric state.
