# New possibilities for QCD at finite density

> I review the growing theoretical indications that at high densities color SU(3) gauge symmetry is spontaneously broken by the formation of a quark pair condensate. This leads to a rich phase structure for QCD as a function of temperature and chemical potential. I also discuss the prospects for lattice QCD calculations at finite density, including the Glasgow algorithm and imaginary chemical potential.

## Key claim

The paper reviews theoretical indications that at high densities, color SU(3) gauge symmetry is spontaneously broken by quark pair condensation, but notes that these results come from Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model calculations rather than from first-principles QCD, and that lattice QCD calculations at finite density remain extremely difficult due to the sign problem.

## Method

The author reviews mean-field and variational analyses of NJL models of QCD, using either the four-leg instanton vertex or the single-gluon-exchange vertex as the effective interaction, to study quark pair condensation in two-flavor and three-flavor cases. The paper also discusses the Glasgow algorithm for lattice QCD at finite density, which expands the partition function in powers of fugacity using ensembles generated at zero chemical potential, and the alternative approach of using imaginary chemical potential to keep the fermion determinant real and positive.

## Key numbers

* In two-flavor QCD, NJL model calculations indicate BCS-style gaps of order 100 MeV at densities only a few times nuclear density.
* The spin-1 pairing channel for blue quarks in two-flavor QCD gives a parameter-sensitive gap of about 1 keV.
* In the two-flavor phase diagram, the nuclear gas-liquid phase transition line ends at a critical point at T ~ 10 MeV.
* For the Glasgow method on a 2^4 lattice, the onset chemical potential rises towards m_N/3 at very high statistics.
* The phase of the quark determinant drops as exp(-V), requiring statistics ~ exp(V).

## Context

This work extends earlier suggestions by Bailin and Love and by Barrois that QCD at high density might behave like a superconductor via the BCS mechanism, and builds on NJL model studies by Alford, Rajagopal, and Wilczek (1998) and by others. The paper contrasts the Glasgow algorithm approach of Barbour and collaborators with the imaginary chemical potential method tested on the Hubbard model by Alford, Kapustin, and Wilczek (1998), and notes that Hands and Morrison (1998) performed lattice simulations of a Gross-Neveu model without yet seeing unambiguous evidence of quark pairing. The review enables future work on experimental signatures in heavy-ion collisions, possible roles in neutron star physics, and the 2+1 flavor case with a realistic strange quark mass.
