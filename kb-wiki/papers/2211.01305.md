# Modewise Johnson-Lindenstrauss Embeddings for Nuclear Many-Body Theory

> In this work, we explore modewise Johnson-Lindenstrauss embeddings (JLEs) as a tool to reduce the computational cost and memory requirements of nuclear many-body methods. JLEs are randomized projections of high-dimensional data tensors onto low-dimensional subspaces that preserve key structural features. Such embeddings allow for the oblivious and incremental compression of large tensors, e.g., the nuclear Hamiltonian, into significantly smaller random sketches that still allow for the accurate calculation of ground-state energies and other observables. Their oblivious character makes it possible to compress a tensor without knowing in advance exactly what observables one might want to approximate at a later time. This opens the door for the use of tensors that are much too large to store in memory, e.g., complete two-plus three-nucleon Hamiltonians in large, symmetry-unrestricted bases. Such compressed Hamiltonians can be stored and used later on with relative ease. As a first step, we analyze the JLE's impact on the second-order Many-Body Perturbation Theory (MBPT) corrections for nuclear ground-state observables. Numerical experiments for a wide range of closed-shell nuclei, model spaces and state-of-the-art nuclear interactions demonstrate the validity and potential of the proposed approach: We can compress nuclear Hamiltonians hundred- to thousand-fold while only incurring mean relative errors of 1\% or less in ground-state observables. Importantly, we show that JLEs capture the relevant physical information contained in the highly structured Hamiltonian tensor despite their random characteristics. In addition to the significant storage savings, the achieved compressions imply multiple order-of magnitude reductions in computational effort when the compressed Hamiltonians are used in higher-order MBPT or nonperturbative many-body methods.

## Key claim
Modewise Johnson-Lindenstrauss embeddings (JLEs) can compress nuclear Hamiltonians hundred- to thousand-fold while preserving ground-state observables with mean relative errors of 1% or less, but this result is demonstrated only for second-order Many-Body Perturbation Theory (MBPT(2)) corrections in closed-shell nuclei, not yet for higher-order or nonperturbative methods.

## Method
The authors apply modewise JLEs, randomized linear projections of high-dimensional tensors onto low-dimensional subspaces, to the normal-ordered two-body part of nuclear Hamiltonians expressed in a Hartree-Fock single-particle basis. They reformulate the MBPT(2) energy and radius corrections as inner products of low-rank tensors, then replace the full Hamiltonian tensor with a compressed random sketch obtained by multiplying each tensor mode by independent JL embedding matrices. Numerical experiments are performed for closed-shell nuclei across a range of model spaces and using state-of-the-art nuclear interactions, comparing ground-state observables computed from the sketched Hamiltonians to those from the full Hamiltonians.

## Key numbers
- Compression ratios of 100- to 1000-fold are achieved for nuclear Hamiltonians while incurring mean relative errors of 1% or less in ground-state observables (energy and radius).
- The MBPT(2) energy correction for a given nucleus requires storage of O(N_o²N_u²) entries in the optimal case, where N_o is the number of occupied single-particle states and N_u is the number of unoccupied states; the JLE sketches are competitive with this optimal storage requirement.
- The IMSRG(2) method naively scales as O(N⁶), while IMSRG(3) requires O(N⁹) effort, where N is the single-particle basis dimension; the achieved compressions imply multiple order-of-magnitude reductions in computational effort when compressed Hamiltonians are used in such methods.

## Context
This work extends randomized projection techniques from data science and machine learning, specifically the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma (Johnson 1984) and its modewise tensor generalization (Iwen 2021), to nuclear many-body theory. It contrasts with prior approaches that reduce basis size through optimized orbitals, such as natural orbitals from perturbatively corrected one-body density matrices (Tichai 2019, Hoppe 2021, Novario 2020), which require prior knowledge of the Hamiltonian structure. The JLE approach is oblivious (no prior structural assumptions) and enables compression of full two-plus-three-nucleon Hamiltonians that are too large to store in memory, opening the door for use in higher-order MBPT or nonperturbative methods like IMSRG (Hergert 2016, 2017) and Coupled Cluster theory (Hagen 2014).
