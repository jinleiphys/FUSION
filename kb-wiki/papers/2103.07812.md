# The muon abundance in the primordial Universe

> Muon abundance is required for the understanding of several fundamental questions regarding properties of the primordial Universe. In this paper we evaluate the production and decay rates of muons in the cosmic plasma as a function of temperature. This allows us to determine when exactly the muon abundance disappears. When the Universe cools below the temperature $kT_\mathrm{disappear}\approx 4.135$ MeV the muon decay rate overwhelms production rates and muons vanish quasi-instantaneously from the Universe. Interestingly, we show that at $T_\mathrm{disappear}$ the muon number is nearly equal to baryon abundance.

## Key claim

Muons disappear from the primordial Universe when the temperature drops below approximately 4.135 MeV, at which point the muon decay rate exceeds the total production rate; at this disappearance temperature, the muon pair abundance is nearly equal to the baryon abundance, a coincidence the authors highlight.

## Method

The authors computed thermal production rates for muons via electron-positron annihilation and photon-photon fusion, and thermal decay rates for muons and pions, using Boltzmann-limit approximations for particle distributions in the cosmic plasma. They integrated the invariant matrix elements for the production reactions over Mandelstam variables to obtain cross sections, then used these to evaluate the thermal reaction rates per volume as a function of temperature. The muon disappearance temperature was identified as the point where the total muon production rate falls below the muon decay rate, accounting for time dilation in the expanding Universe.

## Key numbers

- Muon disappearance temperature: \(kT_\mathrm{disappear} \approx 4.135\) MeV, where the muon decay rate overwhelms the total production rate.
- At \(T_\mathrm{disappear}\), the muon-to-baryon density ratio is \(n_{\mu^\pm}/n_\mathrm{B} \approx 0.644\).
- The temperature at which muon pair abundance equals baryon abundance is \(T \approx 4.212\) MeV, slightly above the disappearance temperature.
- The Universe expansion time at \(T_\mathrm{disappear}\) is \(1/H = 0.084\) s, which is \(3.8 \times 10^4\) times longer than the muon lifespan \(\tau_\mu = 2.197 \times 10^{-6}\) s.
- The present-day baryon-to-photon ratio is \(B/N_\gamma \approx 0.61 \times 10^{-9}\), used to derive the baryon-to-entropy ratio \(n_\mathrm{B}/s_\gamma \approx 1.7 \times 10^{-10}\).
- The effective entropic degrees of freedom for \(10 > T > 3\) MeV are \(g^s_\ast = 10.75\), accounting for photons, electrons/positrons, and neutrinos.

## Context

This work extends the authors' ongoing study of strangeness freeze-out in the early Universe (Rafelski 2020), motivated by the need to know when muons remain in chemical equilibrium to maintain detailed balance for reactions involving kaon and pion decays into muons. The results enable model building for baryon-antibaryon separation scenarios and strangelet formation in the primordial Universe, and provide a baseline for future work on strangeness abundance evolution. The production cross sections for photon-photon fusion follow Gould (1967), and the thermal rate formalism draws on Kuznetsova (2008) and Letessier (2002).
