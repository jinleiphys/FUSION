# Kinetic Energy Distribution of Fragments for Thermal Neutron-Induced $^{235}$U and $^{239}$Pu Fission Reactions

> Focused on the generation and evolution of vast complementary pairs of the primary fission fragments at scission moment, Dinuclear and Statistical Model (DSM) is proposed. (1) It is assumed that the fissile nucleus elongates along a symmetric coaxis until it breaks into two primary fission fragments. (2) Every complementary pair of the primary fission fragments is approximatively described as two ellipsoids with large deformation at scission moment. (3) The kinetic energy in every complementary pair of the primary fragments is mainly provided by Coulomb repulsion, which is explicitly expressed through strict six-dimensional integrals. (4) Only three phenomenological coefficients are obtained to globally describe the quadrupole deformation parameters of arbitrary primary fragments both for $^{235}$U($n_{th}, f$) and $^{239}$Pu($n_{th}, f$) reactions, on the basis of the common characteristics of the measured data, such as mass and charge distributions, kinetic energy distributions. In the framework of DSM, the explicit average total kinetic energy distribution $\overline{TKE}(A)$ and the average kinetic energy distribution $\overline{KE}(A)$ are consistently represented. The theoretical results in this paper agree well with the experimental data. Furthermore, this model is expected as the reliable approach to generally evaluate the corresponding observebles for thermal neutron-induced fission of actinides.

## Key claim

The Dinuclear and Statistical Model (DSM) can consistently represent the average total kinetic energy distribution $\overline{TKE}(A)$ and average kinetic energy distribution $\overline{KE}(A)$ for thermal neutron-induced fission of $^{235}$U and $^{239}$Pu, but only by assuming that a single "Most Probable Primary Fragment Pair" dominates the contribution to each mass number $A$ and that deformation parameters depend solely on isospin asymmetry via three phenomenological coefficients.

## Method

The fissile nucleus is assumed to elongate along a symmetric coaxis until scission, where each complementary primary fragment pair is approximated as two ellipsoids with large deformation. The kinetic energy is computed from Coulomb repulsion via strict six-dimensional integrals, with the proton density treated as a uniform charged drop with a sharp surface. Deformation parameters for all primary fragments are expressed as $\beta_i = \alpha_i I_i$, where $I_i = (N_i - Z_i)/A_i$ is the isospin asymmetry, and the three phenomenological coefficients $\alpha_i$ are globally fitted to reproduce measured mass, charge, and kinetic energy distributions for both reactions simultaneously.

## Key numbers

- For $^{235}$U($n_{th}, f$), the Most Probable Primary Fragment Pair for mass $A_{L0}=118$ has $\beta_{L0}=1.3445$ and $\alpha_{L0}=6.1020$; for $A_{L0}=82$, $\beta_{L0}=1.0665$ and $\alpha_{L0}=5.4659$.
- For $^{239}$Pu($n_{th}, f$), the Most Probable Primary Fragment Pair for mass $A_{L0}=120$ has $\beta_{L0}=1.3458$ and $\alpha_{L0}=6.2112$; for $A_{L0}=80$, $\beta_{L0}=1.0730$ and $\alpha_{L0}=6.1315$.
- The charge constant $e^2$ used in the Coulomb repulsion integral is 1.44 MeV$\cdot$fm.
- The nuclear radius parameter is $r_0 = 1.226$ fm.
- The critical mass number $A=140$ corresponds to the peaks of measured mass distributions for low energy neutron-induced fission of actinides.

## Context

This work extends the pre-scission configuration (PSC) approach (Manea, 2011) by using two ellipsoids rather than a rotational ellipsoid and a sphere, and by introducing a systematic method to select the Most Probable Primary Fragment Pair based on ground-state half-lives. The DSM aims to provide a reliable evaluation tool for thermal neutron-induced fission observables of actinides, complementing semi-empirical models like GEF (Schmitt, 2018) and machine learning methods (Niu, 2018; Wang, 2019), while avoiding the complexity of fully microscopic models that have not yet been adopted by evaluated nuclear data libraries such as ENDF/B-VIII.0 (Brown, 2018), JEFF-3.3.1, JENDL-4.0u2, and CENDL-3.1 (Ge, 2011).
