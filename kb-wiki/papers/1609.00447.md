# Evidence for a dynamically refracted primary bow in weakly bound $^9$Be rainbow scattering from $^{16}$O

> We present for the first time evidence for the existence of a dynamically refracted primary bow for $^{9}$Be+$^{16}$O scattering. This is demonstrated through the use of coupled channel calculations with an extended double folding potential derived from the density-dependent effective two-body force and precise microscopic cluster wave functions for $^{9}$Be. The calculations reproduce the experimental Airy structure in $^{9}$Be+$^{16}$O scattering well.It is found that coupling of a weakly bound $^{9}$Be nucleus to excited states plays the role of a booster lens, dynamically enhancing the refraction over the {\it static} refraction due to the Luneburg lens mean field potential between the ground states of $^{9}$Be and $^{16}$O.

# Evidence for a dynamically refracted primary bow in weakly bound $^9$Be rainbow scattering from $^{16}$O

## Key claim

The authors present evidence that the primary nuclear rainbow observed in $^9$Be+$^{16}$O scattering is not caused solely by the static mean field potential (Luneburg lens) between ground states, but requires dynamical enhancement of refraction through coupling of the weakly bound $^9$Be nucleus to its excited states, which acts as a "booster lens." This claim is conditional on coupled channel calculations using an extended double folding potential with precise microscopic cluster wave functions for $^9$Be.

## Method

Coupled channel calculations were performed using an extended double folding (EDF) model with a density-dependent effective two-body force (DDM3Y-FR interaction) and microscopic cluster wave functions for $^9$Be from the generator coordinate method. The calculations included the ground band states of $^9$Be ($3/2^-$ at 0.0 MeV, $5/2^-$ at 2.43 MeV, and $7/2^-$ at 6.38 MeV) with reorientations, while a Woods-Saxon volume-type imaginary potential was introduced phenomenologically. The angular distributions were decomposed into farside and nearside components to identify Airy minima and confirm refractive scattering.

## Key numbers

- At $E(^9$Be)=157.7 MeV, the first-order Airy minimum $A1$ is located at $\theta=45^\circ$; the coupled channel calculation reproduces this position, while the single channel calculation (without coupling) places it at $\theta=38^\circ$, showing insufficient attraction.
- At $E(^9$Be)=74.25 MeV ($E(^{16}$O)=132 MeV), the second-order Airy minimum $A2$ is located at $\theta=78^\circ$; the coupled channel calculation reproduces this, while the single channel calculation places it at $\theta=65^\circ$.
- The volume integral per nucleon pair $J_V$ changes by $\Delta J_V = 25$ MeV fm$^3$ at 157.7 MeV and $\Delta J_V = 32$ MeV fm$^3$ at 74.25 MeV when coupling is included, corresponding to about 10% of the refraction being due to dynamical effects.
- The normalization factor $N_R$ for the real double folding potential is 1.00 in the coupled channel calculation at 157.7 MeV and 1.03 at 74.25 MeV, compared to 1.1 in single channel calculations.
- The $\chi^2$ values at 74.25 MeV ($\theta>45^\circ$) are 4.2 for single channel and 2.4 for coupled channel calculations; at 157.7 MeV ($\theta>30^\circ$) they are 8.4 and 6.6, respectively.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of nuclear rainbow scattering in systems such as $^{16}$O+$^{16}$O, $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C, and $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C (Goldberg 1972, Khoa 2007), where rainbows were understood as caused by static mean field potentials. It builds on recent findings by Ohkubo (2014, 2015) showing that a secondary bow in $^{12}$C scattering is dynamically generated by coupling to excited states. The paper also addresses earlier work by Satchler (1983) who interpreted the $^9$Be+$^{16}$O angular distribution as a nuclear rainbow "ghost," and by Khoa (1988) who used M3Y double folding calculations. The results enable future investigation of dynamically refracted rainbows in other weakly bound nuclear systems and confirm that the EDF model with density-dependent forces can describe rainbow phenomena involving weakly bound nuclei.
