# What is the Brightest Source for Dilepton Emissions at RHIC?

> We calculate the dilepton emissions as the decay product of the charm and bottom quarks produced in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC energy. We take into account the next-to-leading-order radiative corrections in perturbative QCD to the heavy quark production from both an initial hard parton-parton scattering and an ideal quark-gluon plasma. We find that the thermal charm decay dominates the dilepton production in the low dilepton mass region ($<2$ GeV), while the heavy quark production from the initial scattering takes over the intermediate and high mass regions ($> 2$ GeV). Our result also indicates the importance of the bottom quark in the high mass region ($>4 $ GeV ) due to its large mass and cascade decay. If the initial scattering produced charm suffers a significant energy loss due to the secondary interaction, the bottom decay constitutes the major background for the thermal dileptons.

# What is the Brightest Source for Dilepton Emissions at RHIC?

## Key claim

Thermal charm decay from an ideal quark-gluon plasma dominates dilepton production in the low mass region (below 2 GeV), while initial hard-scattering heavy quark production dominates above 2 GeV; however, if initial-scattering charm suffers significant energy loss from secondary interactions, bottom decay becomes the major background for thermal dileptons, dominating already above approximately 2 GeV rather than above 4 GeV.

## Method

The authors calculated dilepton (dimuon) production from charm and bottom quark decays in Au+Au collisions at RHIC energy using next-to-leading-order (NLO) perturbative QCD matrix elements for heavy quark production from both initial hard parton-parton scattering and an idealized thermalized quark-gluon plasma. Heavy quark fragmentation was handled with the Peterson fragmentation function, and the subsequent semileptonic cascade decays (including b to c to s) were simulated using the Isajet Monte Carlo program, with NLO parton-level events generated separately to avoid double counting. For the thermal plasma, a Bjorken scaling expansion was assumed with initial temperature 0.55 GeV, and the NLO thermal charm rate was estimated using a Debye screening mass to regularize infrared singularities.

## Key numbers

- Total cross sections for Au+Au at sqrt(s) = 200 GeV/nucleon: sigma_ccbar = 153 microbarns, sigma_bbbar = 1.5 microbarns (using m_c = 1.5 GeV, m_b = 4.7 GeV)
- Choosing a smaller charm mass m_c = 1.2 GeV increases the charm total cross section by a factor of 2
- Peterson fragmentation parameters: epsilon_c = 0.15, epsilon_b = 0.016; normalization constants N = 1.28 (charm) and 0.25 (bottom)
- Thermal charm NLO K-factor ranges from 1.7 to 2.5 as p_T varies from 1 GeV to 10 GeV
- Thermal charm dominates dilepton production for invariant mass below 2 GeV
- Initial-scattering bottom decay dominates for dimuon invariant mass above approximately 4 GeV (or above approximately 2 GeV if charm suffers significant energy loss)
- Initial plasma temperature T_0 = 0.55 GeV, thermalization time tau_0 = 0.7 fm/c, critical temperature T_c = 0.2 GeV, spatial rapidity cutoff eta_max = 3

## Context

This work extends the earlier leading-order calculations of thermal charm production by Muller and collaborators (1995) and the correlated charm decay study by Gavin, McGaughey, Ruuskanen and Vogt (1996), which found charm decay to be the dominant dilepton source. The authors include full NLO corrections to heavy quark production and explicitly compare initial-scattering and thermal sources across a wide mass range. The results enable experimental planning for dilepton measurements at RHIC, particularly for identifying thermal dilepton signals from the quark-gluon plasma and for understanding the role of bottom decays as a background, especially if charm energy loss in the medium is significant as suggested by Shuryak (1996).
