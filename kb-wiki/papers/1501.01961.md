# Neutrino-driven supernova of a low-mass iron-core progenitor boosted by three-dimensional turbulent convection

> We present the first successful simulation of a neutrino-driven supernova explosion in three dimensions (3D), using the Prometheus-Vertex code with an axis-free Yin-Yang grid and a sophisticated treatment of three-flavor, energy-dependent neutrino transport. The progenitor is a nonrotating, zero-metallicity 9.6 Msun star with an iron core. While in spherical symmetry outward shock acceleration sets in later than 300 ms after bounce, a successful explosion starts at ~130 ms postbounce in two dimensions (2D). The 3D model explodes at about the same time but with faster shock expansion than in 2D and a more quickly increasing and roughly 10 percent higher explosion energy of >10^50 erg. The more favorable explosion conditions in 3D are explained by lower temperatures and thus reduced neutrino emission in the cooling layer below the gain radius. This moves the gain radius inward and leads to a bigger mass in the gain layer, whose larger recombination energy boosts the explosion energy in 3D. These differences are caused by less coherent, less massive, and less rapid convective downdrafts associated with postshock convection in 3D. The less violent impact of these accretion downflows in the cooling layer produces less shock heating and therefore diminishes energy losses by neutrino emission. We thus have, for the first time, identified a reduced mass accretion rate, lower infall velocities, and a smaller surface filling factor of convective downdrafts as consequences of 3D postshock turbulence that facilitate neutrino-driven explosions and strengthen them compared to the 2D case.

# Neutrino-driven supernova of a low-mass iron-core progenitor boosted by three-dimensional turbulent convection

## Key claim
The first fully self-consistent three-dimensional (3D) simulation of a neutrino-driven supernova explosion shows that 3D turbulent convection can strengthen the explosion relative to two-dimensional (2D) models, contrary to previous findings for more massive progenitors. The explosion energy in 3D is roughly 10% higher than in 2D, reaching >10^50 erg, due to reduced neutrino energy losses in the cooling layer caused by less coherent and less massive convective downdrafts in 3D.

## Method
The authors performed 1D, 2D, and full 4π 3D simulations of a nonrotating, zero-metallicity 9.6 M⊙ iron-core progenitor using the Prometheus-Vertex code with three-flavor, energy-dependent ray-by-ray-plus neutrino transport and an axis-free Yin-Yang grid. The simulations employed the high-density equation of state of Lattimer and Swesty (1991) with nuclear incompressibility K=220 MeV, a 1D gravity potential with general relativistic corrections, and an angular resolution of 2 degrees with 400-600 radial zones.

## Key numbers
- Explosion energy in 3D at 400 ms postbounce: (0.77 to 1.05) × 10^50 erg, with a growth rate of (0.75 to 1.25) × 10^50 erg/s from the proto-neutron star wind
- Explosion energy in 2D at 400 ms postbounce: (0.67 to 0.92) × 10^50 erg, with similar growth rate as in 3D
- 1D explosion energy: ~2 × 10^49 erg, with only 2.2 × 10^-3 M⊙ in the gain layer at t_pb > 300 ms
- Gain-mass difference between 3D and 2D: ΔM_gain ≈ 1.2 × 10^-3 M⊙ between 100 ms and 200 ms postbounce
- Nuclear recombination energy of the gain-mass difference: (1.6 to 2.0) × 10^49 erg, using e_recomb = (7 to 8.8) MeV/nucleon ≈ (6.76 to 8.49) × 10^18 erg/g
- Shock radius at 400 ms postbounce: ~6000 km in multi-D simulations versus ~2000 km in 1D
- Binding energy of stellar layers ahead of shock at t_pb = 400 ms: -3.5 × 10^48 erg in 1D, -9.5 × 10^47 erg in multi-D
- 2D explosion energy variations with doubled angular resolution and different seed perturbations: ~4%

## Context
This work extends previous 2D studies of the same 9.6 M⊙ progenitor by Mueller (2013) and provides the first successful 3D neutrino-driven supernova simulation with self-consistent neutrino transport. It contradicts earlier findings by Hanke (2012), Couch (2013, 2014), and Takiwaki (2012, 2014) that 3D turbulence disfavors explosions in more massive progenitors (11.2, 20, 27 M⊙), and corrects an erroneous claim by Nordhaus (2010) of a positive 3D effect that was later retracted by Dolence (2013) and Burrows (2012). The results enable future studies of whether the identified 3D effects (reduced mass accretion rate, lower infall velocities, smaller surface filling factor of downdrafts) could also benefit explosions of more massive stars, though current resolution is insufficient to capture fully developed turbulence.
