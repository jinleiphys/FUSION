# Quantum Simulation for High Energy Physics

> It is for the first time that Quantum Simulation for High Energy Physics (HEP) is studied in the U.S. decadal particle-physics community planning, and in fact until recently, this was not considered a mainstream topic in the community. This fact speaks of a remarkable rate of growth of this subfield over the past few years, stimulated by the impressive advancements in Quantum Information Sciences (QIS) and associated technologies over the past decade, and the significant investment in this area by the government and private sectors in the U.S. and other countries. High-energy physicists have quickly identified problems of importance to our understanding of nature at the most fundamental level, from tiniest distances to cosmological extents, that are intractable with classical computers but may benefit from quantum advantage. They have initiated, and continue to carry out, a vigorous program in theory, algorithm, and hardware co-design for simulations of relevance to the HEP mission. This community whitepaper is an attempt to bring this exciting and yet challenging area of research to the spotlight, and to elaborate on what the promises, requirements, challenges, and potential solutions are over the next decade and beyond.

## Key claim

Quantum simulation for high-energy physics (HEP) is now recognized in U.S. decadal particle-physics community planning as a rapidly growing subfield, but the paper hedges that while problems of importance to HEP that are intractable with classical computers may benefit from quantum advantage, it is not yet known what the best theoretical formulations of HEP problems are when coupled with existing quantum algorithms, and whether newly-devised algorithms tailored to field-theory simulations are needed.

## Method

The paper surveys the landscape of HEP problems that are intractable with classical computing due to exponential scaling of Hilbert spaces or sign problems in Monte Carlo methods, and reviews the requirements for mapping quantum field theories onto Hamiltonian simulation frameworks suitable for quantum simulators. It examines the necessary co-design of theory, algorithms, and hardware across subfields including collider physics, neutrino physics, cosmology, and quantum gravity, and discusses the workforce, infrastructure, and institutional collaboration models needed to advance the program.

## Key numbers

- Approximately 85% of the matter in the universe is observed only via gravitational effects, motivating quantum simulation of light dark matter such as axions
- The paper states that quantum computers can compute S-matrix elements using resources that scale only polynomially with the number of lattice sites, demonstrated explicitly for scalar field theory by Jordan (2011)
- Collective neutrino oscillations in core-collapse supernovae and neutron-star mergers require solving a many-body problem whose exact solution has computational cost exponential in the number of neutrinos, while quantum computing can simulate this with polynomial resources

## Context

This community whitepaper extends the work of the U.S. particle-physics community planning process (the "Snowmass" process) to formally include quantum simulation for HEP for the first time. It builds on prior theoretical work by Jordan (2012) on polynomial-resource quantum algorithms for scattering, and on lattice field theory methods that are hampered by sign problems as described by Troyer (2005) and Alexandru (2016). The paper enables a coordinated research program across theory, algorithm development, and hardware co-design, identifying specific target problems in collider physics, neutrino astrophysics, cosmology, and quantum gravity that could see practical quantum advantage in the coming decade.
