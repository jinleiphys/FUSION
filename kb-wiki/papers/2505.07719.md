# Training neural control variates using correlated configurations

> Neural control variates (NCVs) have emerged as a powerful tool for variance reduction in Monte Carlo (MC) simulations, particularly in high-dimensional problems where traditional control variates are difficult to construct analytically. By training neural networks to learn auxiliary functions correlated with the target observable, NCVs can significantly reduce estimator variance while preserving unbiasedness. However, a critical but often overlooked aspect of NCV training is the role of autocorrelated samples generated by Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC). While such samples are typically discarded for error estimation due to their statistical redundancy, they may contain useful information about the structure of the underlying probability distribution that can benefit the training process. In this work, we systematically examine the effect of using correlated configurations in training neural control variates. We demonstrate, both conceptually and numerically, that training on correlated data can improve control variate performance, especially in settings with limited computational resources. Our analysis includes empirical results from $U(1)$ gauge theory and scalar field theory, illustrating when and how autocorrelated samples enhance NCV construction. These findings provide practical guidance for the efficient use of MCMC data in training neural networks.

# Training neural control variates using correlated configurations

## Key claim

Training neural control variates on autocorrelated MCMC samples, which are typically discarded for error estimation, can improve variance reduction compared to training only on decorrelated samples, particularly when computational resources are limited. The improvement depends on the specific system, observable, and network architecture, and is most pronounced when the autocorrelation time of the improved estimator differs from that of the original observable.

## Method

The author trains neural networks to parametrize control variates via Stein's identity, using feedforward networks with CELU activations and ADAM optimization. Training is performed on correlated MCMC samples with varying integrated autocorrelation times (achieved by adjusting Metropolis-Hastings acceptance rates to 0.9–0.95), while testing uses fully decorrelated samples. The method is applied to Wilson loops in 2D U(1) gauge theory at β=5.55 and to zero-momentum two-point correlators in φ⁴ scalar field theory in 2, 3, and 4 dimensions.

## Key numbers

- In U(1) gauge theory at β=5.55, training on 10⁴ correlated samples (with integrated autocorrelation times varied via acceptance rates 0.9–0.95) yields better variance reduction than training on decorrelated samples, for Wilson loops of area A=4 and A=16
- For scalar field theory on a 16×16 lattice (m²=0.1, λ=0.5), training on 10⁴ correlated samples improves error reduction compared to decorrelated training, with loss function showing early stopping needed after a certain number of training steps
- For 3D scalar field theory on a 32×8×8 lattice (m²=0.01, λ=0.1), error reduction improves with more correlated training samples using both a 2-layer×32-node network and a 4-layer×64-node network
- For 4D scalar field theory on a 16×8×8×8 lattice (m²=0.1, λ=0.5), incorporating more correlated samples leads to better variance reduction for a fixed MCMC chain and training budget
- Test sets throughout use 10³ fully decorrelated samples obtained by increasing separation between successive configurations

## Context

This work extends the neural control variate framework developed for lattice field theory by Bedaque (2023) and Lawrence (2024), and addresses a gap in prior work by Bhattacharya (2023) and Bedaque (2023) which assumed independent configurations for training. The findings provide practical guidance for large-scale lattice simulations where independent configurations are scarce (typically ~10³ from HPC resources), suggesting that correlated configurations between measurements can be repurposed for training. The approach may also extend to other neural network-based methods relying on MCMC sampling, including contour deformation, neural network quantum states, and neural network assisted Monte Carlo algorithms.
