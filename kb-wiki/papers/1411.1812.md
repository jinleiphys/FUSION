# Effect of initial fluctuations on the collective flow in intermediate-energy heavy ion collisions

> A systemical analysis of the initial fluctuation effect on the collective flows for Au+Au at 1$A$ GeV has been presented in the framework of Isospin-dependent Quantum Molecular Dynamics model (IQMD), and a special focus on the initial fluctuation effect on the squeeze-out is emphasized. The flows calculated by the participant plane reconstructedby the initial geometry in coordinate space are compared with those calculated by both the ideal reaction plane and event plane methods. It is found that initial fluctuation weakens squeeze-out effect, and somediscrepancies between the flows extracted by the above different plane methods appearwhich indicate that the flows are affected by the evolution of dynamics. In addition, we found that the squeeze-out flow is also proportional to initial eccentricity. Our calculations also qualitatively give the similar trend for the excitation function of the elliptic flow of the FOPI experimental data. Finally we address the nucleon number scaling of the flows for light particles. Even though initial fluctuation decreases the ratio of $v_4/v_2^2$ as well as $v_3/(v_1v_2$) a lot, all fragments to mass number 4 keep the same curve and shows independent of transverse momentum.

## Key claim

Initial fluctuations in the collision geometry weaken the squeeze-out (negative elliptic flow) in intermediate-energy heavy ion collisions, in contrast to relativistic energies where they amplify positive elliptic flow; the effect is most pronounced for higher harmonic flows, which are quenched similarly to viscous damping.

## Method

The authors used the Isospin-dependent Quantum Molecular Dynamics (IQMD) model to simulate Au+Au collisions at 1A GeV, generating events with event-by-event fluctuating initial conditions. They compared collective flow coefficients (v1, v2, v3, v4) calculated with respect to three different reference planes: the ideal reaction plane (the original Px-Pz plane in the model), the participant plane (reconstructed from the initial geometry in coordinate space), and the event plane (reconstructed from final-state momentum space using the standard experimental method). Flows were analyzed as functions of rapidity, transverse four-velocity, centrality, and beam energy, and compared with FOPI experimental data.

## Key numbers

* For Au+Au at 1A GeV, the triangular flow v3 magnitude is limited within ±2% for all three plane methods.
* The maximum compression density is reached near 9 fm/c, almost independent of impact parameter.
* The proportional relation between v2 and participant eccentricity ε2 has a slope of -0.045 for the absolute values.
* The transition energy from positive to negative elliptic flow is around 0.2A GeV, with maximum squeeze-out around 0.5A GeV.
* Before initial fluctuation is considered, v4/v2^2 is about 0.5 and v3/(v1v2) is about 0.6, independent of transverse momentum; after including initial fluctuation, v4/v2^2 drops to about 0.1 and v3/(v1v2) drops to about 0.12.
* The IQMD model uses a Gaussian wave packet width parameter L = 8.66 fm^2, normal nuclear matter density ρ0 = 0.17 fm^-3, and Fermi momentum PF ≈ 268 MeV/c.

## Context

This work extends the study of initial fluctuation effects from relativistic heavy-ion collisions (where they are well established, e.g., by Alver (2010) and Han (2011)) into the intermediate-energy domain for the first time. It contradicts the relativistic-energy finding that initial fluctuation enhances elliptic flow; instead, at 1A GeV the spectator shadowing effect quenches the anisotropy from initial fluctuations. The results enable systematic comparison with FOPI experimental data (FOPI Collaboration, various years) and provide a baseline for future model improvements, as the IQMD calculations underpredict the excitation function of elliptic flow even though the trend is reproduced. The nucleon number scaling of flows for light fragments (up to mass number 4) is found to hold even after including initial fluctuation, extending earlier scaling studies by Yan (various years) and Oh and Ko (various years).
