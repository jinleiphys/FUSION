# Negative moments as the signature of the radial density at small distances

> The present paper proposes a robust evaluation of any radial density at small distances using negative-order radial moments evaluated in momentum space. This evaluation provides a valuable insight into the behavior of a given radial density in the vicinity of $r=0$, and puts strong emphasis on the importance of measuring form factors at large squared four-momentum transfer, a domain essential for the determination of negative order moments. A specific attention is paid to the regularization scheme directly affecting the numerical determination of the radial density's parametrization. The proposed method is applied to non-relativistic study cases of the nucleon electric ($G_{En}, G_{Ep}$), and proton magnetic $G_{Mp}$ form factors. The validation is performed through comparison of the results of the approach to the analytically determined Maclaurin expansion - in the vicinity of $r=0$ - of the radial density function. The method is also applied to the relativistic Dirac form factor $F_1$ of the proton. In such a non-trivial case, the Maclaurin development might not exist for the radial density, rendering the determination from the proposed method extremely important.

## Key claim

Negative-order radial moments, evaluated in momentum space via a regularized integral method, can robustly determine the behavior of any radial density at small distances (near \(r=0\)), even in cases where the Maclaurin expansion of the radial density does not exist, such as for the relativistic Dirac form factor \(F_1\) of the proton.

## Method

The authors express radial moments \((r^\lambda, f_D)\) of a \(D\)-dimensional radial density \(f_D(r)\) in terms of its Fourier transform \(\tilde{f}_D(k)\) using a principal-value regularization at infinite momentum. For negative \(\lambda\), the integral converges at low \(k\) but requires regularization at high \(k\) via an asymptotic expansion of \(\tilde{f}_D(k)\). By scanning \(\lambda\) values below a critical value \(\lambda_c\) and fitting the inverse of the regularized moment integral, they extract \(\lambda_c\) (and thus the exponent \(\beta = \lambda_c + D\) describing the density's behavior as \(r\to 0\)) and the coefficients \(g_j\) of the Maclaurin-like expansion of \(f_D(r)\) near \(r=0\). The method is validated on analytic test functions and applied to non-relativistic nucleon form factors (\(G_{En}, G_{Ep}, G_{Mp}\)) and the relativistic Dirac form factor \(F_1\) of the proton.

## Key numbers

* For the generic test function \(f(r) = \frac{a^{3-b}}{4\pi \Gamma(3-b)} \frac{e^{-a r}}{r^b}\) with \(a = 4\ \text{fm}^{-1}\), the extracted \(\beta\) values range from \(-0.50\) (for \(b = -0.50\)) to \(1.00\) (for \(b = 1.00\)), with corresponding \(g_0\) values from \(3.0649\ \text{fm}^{-3+\beta}\) to \(1.2732\ \text{fm}^{-3+\beta}\).
* For the proton electric form factor \(G_{Ep}\) using the parametrization of Kelly (2004), \(\beta = 0.0000\), \(g_0 = 1.4115\ \text{fm}^{-3}\), \(g_1 = 3.5549\ \text{fm}^{-4}\), and \(g_2 = -42.2277\ \text{fm}^{-5}\).
* For \(G_{Ep}\) using the parametrization of Venkat et al. (2011), \(\beta = 0.0000\), \(g_0 = 1.4370\ \text{fm}^{-3}\), \(g_1 = -0.1215\ \text{fm}^{-4}\), and \(g_2 = 15.8100\ \text{fm}^{-5}\).
* For the proton magnetic form factor \(G_{Mp}\) using the parametrization of Kelly (2004), \(\beta = 0.0000\), \(g_0 = 2.7962\ \text{fm}^{-3}\), \(g_1 = -5.9620\ \text{fm}^{-4}\), and \(g_2 = -32.0702\ \text{fm}^{-5}\).

## Context

This work extends the Integral Method (IM) previously introduced by Hoballah et al. (2021) for extracting radial moments of any real order \(\lambda > -3\) from form factor data. While prior studies focused on even-valued positive moments (e.g., \(<r^2>\) for the charge radius, \(<r^4>\) for surface thickness) obtained from derivatives of form factors at zero momentum transfer, this paper demonstrates that negative-order moments, accessible only at large momentum transfer, are uniquely sensitive to the density near \(r=0\). The method enables reconstruction of the small-\(r\) behavior of nucleon densities without performing an inverse Fourier transform, and is applicable even when the Maclaurin expansion of the density does not exist (as for the relativistic Dirac form factor \(F_1\)), making it a tool for future analyses of high-\(Q^2\) electron scattering data.
