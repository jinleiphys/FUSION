# Gluons and the quark sea at high energies: distributions, polarization, tomography

> This report is based on a ten-week program on "Gluons and the quark sea at high-energies", which took place at the Institute for Nuclear Theory in Seattle in Fall 2010. The principal aim of the program was to develop and sharpen the science case for an Electron-Ion Collider (EIC), a facility that will be able to collide electrons and positrons with polarized protons and with light to heavy nuclei at high energies, offering unprecedented possibilities for in-depth studies of quantum chromodynamics. This report is organized around four major themes: i) the spin and flavor structure of the proton, ii) three-dimensional structure of nucleons and nuclei in momentum and configuration space, iii) QCD matter in nuclei, and iv) Electroweak physics and the search for physics beyond the Standard Model. Beginning with an executive summary, the report contains tables of key measurements, chapter overviews for each of the major scientific themes, and detailed individual contributions on various aspects of the scientific opportunities presented by an EIC.

## Key claim

An Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) with high luminosity and a wide range in $Q^2$ may enable, for the first time, a model-independent extraction of generalized parton distributions (GPDs) from deeply virtual Compton scattering (DVCS) data, provided that systematic uncertainties from detector acceptance and $t$-extrapolation can be controlled.

## Method

The authors performed detailed simulations of DVCS events at an EIC, generating $t$-spectra with an exponential dependence $d\sigma/dt \propto \exp(B t)$ and fitting them to extract the slope parameters $B$ and $C$. They estimated the uncertainty in the impact parameter profile $F(b)$ from extrapolation to large and small $|t|$ by assuming different functional forms for the scattering amplitude (exponential, dipole, modified dipole, modified exponential) and requiring continuity at the boundary of the measured region. They also discussed how polynomiality conditions, dispersion relations, and QCD evolution equations can constrain GPDs from DVCS data.

## Key numbers

- Fitted slope $B = 5.02 \text{ GeV}^{-2}$ with error below $1\%$ from simulated DVCS data at an integrated luminosity of $11.9 \text{ fb}^{-1}$ for $|t| < 1 \text{ GeV}^2$ and $151 \text{ fb}^{-1}$ for $|t| > 1 \text{ GeV}^2$
- Fitted parameters from a quadratic exponential fit: $B = (4.92 \pm 0.10) \text{ GeV}^{-2}$ and $C = (0.079 \pm 0.076) \text{ GeV}^{-4}$, with logarithmic $t$ slope at $|t| = 1.75 \text{ GeV}^2$ of $(5.20 \pm 0.18) \text{ GeV}^{-2}$
- For an exponential cross section with $B = 4 \text{ GeV}^{-2}$, raising $|t|_{\text{max}}$ from $1 \text{ GeV}^2$ to $2 \text{ GeV}^2$ reduces the critical impact parameter $b_{\text{cr}}$ from $\sim 0.25 \text{ fm}$ to $\sim 0.1 \text{ fm}$
- For a dipole scenario with mass $M = 770 \text{ MeV}$, measuring up to $|t|_{\text{max}} = 3.3 \text{ GeV}^2$ gives $b_{\text{cr}} \sim 0.1 \text{ fm}$
- With an exponential slope $B = 6.6 \text{ GeV}^{-2}$, reducing the minimum measured $\Delta_T$ from $300 \text{ MeV}$ to $200 \text{ MeV}$ dramatically improves the extrapolation uncertainty for $b$ above about $1.25 \text{ fm}$
- The predicted large-$b$ behavior from chiral symmetry breaking is $F(b) \propto b^{-1} e^{-\kappa b}$ with $\kappa \approx 2 m_\pi \approx (0.7 \text{ fm})^{-1}$

## Context

This work extends the science case for an Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) developed during a 2010 program at the Institute for Nuclear Theory, building on the parton imaging formalism of Burkardt (2002) and Diehl (2002). The analysis demonstrates that EIC data could resolve ambiguities in heavy-ion collision initial conditions that currently plague interpretations of RHIC results, where the extracted shear viscosity to entropy ratio $\eta/s$ varies by a factor of two depending on whether Glauber-like or color glass condensate (CGC) initial conditions are used (Luzum, 2008). The paper enables future quantitative studies of GPD extraction and connects to open questions about whether the quark-gluon plasma is better described by strongly-coupled AdS/CFT methods or weakly-coupled pQCD.
