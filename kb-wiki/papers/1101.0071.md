# Model for projectile fragmentation: case study for Ni on Ta, Be and Xe on Al

> For projectile fragmentation we work out details of a model whose origin can be traced back to the Bevalac era. The model positions itself between the phenomenological EPAX parametrization and microscopic transport models like "Heavy Ion Phase Space Exploration Model" (HIPSE) and antisymmetrised molecular dynamics(AMD). We apply the model to some recent data of projectile fragmentation of Ni on Ta and Be at beam energy 140 MeV/nucleon and some older data of Xe on Al at beam energy 790 MeV/nucleon. Reasonable values of cross-sections for various composites populated in the reactions are obtained.

# Model for projectile fragmentation: case study for Ni on Ta, Be and Xe on Al

## Key claim
The model, positioned between the phenomenological EPAX parametrization and microscopic transport models (HIPSE and AMD), produces reasonable cross-sections for projectile fragmentation at beam energies from 140 to 790 MeV/nucleon using a single temperature parameter of about 4.25 MeV for all impact parameters, but the authors acknowledge that this single-temperature approximation fails for very peripheral collisions where lower temperatures would be more appropriate.

## Method
The model proceeds in three stages: (1) abrasion using straight-line geometry to calculate the probability distribution of neutron and proton numbers in the projectile-like fragment (PLF) as a function of impact parameter, using a minimal distribution model; (2) thermodynamic breakup of the abraded system at a fixed temperature using the canonical thermodynamic model (CTM) at a freeze-out volume of 3V₀; (3) secondary de-excitation of hot fragments via evaporation of light particles (n, p, d, t, ³He, α) using Weisskopf evaporation theory with Monte Carlo sampling, including fission as a de-excitation channel.

## Key numbers
- Beam energies: 140 MeV/nucleon for Ni on Be and Ta; 790 MeV/nucleon for Xe on Al
- Temperature extracted from fits to summed charge and mass cross-sections: T ≈ 4.25 MeV, tested against T = 3.25 MeV and 5.25 MeV
- Freeze-out volume used in CTM: 3V₀ (three times normal nuclear volume)
- Number of rings for impact parameter integration: M = 20 for Ni on Be, M = 100 for Ni on Ta, M = 200 for Xe on Al
- For ⁴⁸Ca on ⁹Be at 140 MeV/nucleon, the maximum yield of Si isotopes is experimentally at n = 16, while the model gives it at n = 17

## Context
This work extends the Bevalac-era abrasion-ablation approach to projectile fragmentation, providing an implementable alternative to more computationally expensive transport models like HIPSE and AMD. The model successfully reproduces cross-sections for Ni on Ta, a system where transport calculations were prohibitively large (Mocko et al., 2008). The results enable systematic calculations of projectile fragmentation cross-sections for exotic nuclei production, though the authors note that improvements are needed for very peripheral collisions and for better reproduction of isoscaling slopes compared to data from Yao et al. (2009).
