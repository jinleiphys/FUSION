# Medium modifications for light and heavy nuclear clusters in simulations of core collapse supernovae -- Impact on equation of state and weak interactions

> The present article investigates the role of heavy nuclear clusters and weakly bound light nuclear clusters based on a newly developed equation of state for core collapse supernova studies. A novel approach is brought forward for the description of nuclear clusters, taking into account the quasiparticle approach and continuum correlations. It demonstrates that the commonly employed nuclear statistical equilibrium approach, based on non-interacting particles, for the description of light and heavy clusters becomes invalid for warm nuclear matter near the saturation density. This has important consequences for studies of core collapse supernovae. To this end, we implement this nuclear equation of state provided for arbitrary temperature, baryon density and isospin asymmetry, to spherically symmetric core collapse supernova simulations in order to study the impact on the dynamics as well as on the neutrino emission. For the inclusion of a set of weak processes involving light clusters the rate expressions are derived, including medium modifications at the mean field level. A substantial impact from the inclusion of a variety of weak reactions involving light clusters on the post bounce dynamics nor on the neutrino emission could not be found.

# Medium modifications for light and heavy nuclear clusters in simulations of core collapse supernovae

## Key claim

The commonly employed nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) approach based on non-interacting particles becomes invalid for warm nuclear matter near saturation density, and the newly developed generalized relativistic density-functional (gRDF) equation of state provides a more appropriate description; however, the inclusion of a variety of weak reactions involving light clusters does not have a substantial impact on post-bounce dynamics or neutrino emission.

## Method

The authors developed a novel equation of state (gRDF(DD2)) using the generalized relativistic density-functional approach with density-dependent DD2 parametrization, incorporating medium-modified nuclear binding energies for light clusters (Z ≤ 2) and heavy clusters (Z > 2) derived from the quantum-statistical approach, including Pauli blocking and continuum correlations via a virial expansion. They implemented this EOS into the spherically symmetric general relativistic neutrino-radiation hydrodynamics code AGILE-BOLTZTRAN with three-flavor Boltzmann neutrino transport, and performed core collapse supernova simulations launched from an 18 M⊙ progenitor, comparing results with the modified NSE EOS of Hempel and Schaffner-Bielich (2009) using the same DD2 mean-field parametrization.

## Key numbers

- Central density at core bounce for gRDF(DD2): 2.72 × 10^14 g cm^−3, compared to 3.32 × 10^14 g cm^−3 for HS(DD2)
- Saturation density ρ₀ = 2.5 × 10^14 g cm^−3 (equivalent to nucleon number density n₀ = 0.15 fm^−3)
- Temperature range of supernova conditions: T = 0–100 MeV
- Restmass density range of supernova conditions: ρ = 10^0–10^15 g cm^−3
- Neutrino energy bins used in simulations: E_ν ∈ [0.5, 300] MeV with 36 bins
- 6 momentum scattering angles used in neutrino transport: μ = cos θ ∈ {−1, +1}
- Maximum neutron star mass of 2.42 M⊙ predicted by the DD2 parametrization, consistent with pulsar observations
- Neutron star radius of about 13 km for a 1.4 M⊙ neutron star predicted by the model

## Context

This work extends the quantum-statistical approach of Röpke (2020) and the generalized relativistic density-functional model of Typel (2009) and Pais (2016) to develop a full supernova equation of state, contrasting with the modified NSE approach of Hempel and Schaffner-Bielich (2009) which overestimates light cluster abundances at densities above 10^12 g cm^−3. The study enables more reliable predictions of neutrino luminosities and spectra from core collapse supernovae, and demonstrates that the exotic neutron-rich isotopes proposed by Yudin (2019) are strongly suppressed when medium effects are properly accounted for, contrary to what the simple NSE model suggests.
