# Can decay heat measurements tell us something about the Reactor Antineutrino Anomaly?

> Measurements of the decay energy released as a function of time following the thermal neutron induced fission on $^{235}$U and $^{239,241}$Pu were performed in the 1970s at Oak Ridge National Laboratory with the purpose of quantifying possible Loss Of Coolant Accident scenarios. This decay energy, known in technical parlance as decay heat, is mainly composed of two terms, that of the electrons produced together with antineutrinos in the beta-minus decay of the neutron-rich fission products, and that of the gammas produced in the subsequent decay of excited nuclear levels. In this work we study if this extensive set of decay heat measurements can be used to assess the Reactor Antineutrino Anomaly, that is, the approximately 5\% deficit of electron antineutrinos produced by nuclear reactors, first deduced by Mention and collaborators in 2011, and observed by the major reactor antineutrino experiments near nuclear power plants since. With the assistance of nuclear databases, we are able to obtain the ratio of electron spectra under equilibrium conditions for $^{235}$U to $^{239}$Pu, in better agreement with the lower trend recently reported by Kopeikin and collaborators, as well as those for $^{235}$U to $^{241}$Pu and $^{241}$Pu to $^{239}$Pu, which do not agree well with those measured at the Insitut Laue-Langevin in the 1980s. We conclude that a new experimental campaign is needed to measure the electron spectra utilizing a high-resolution and signal-to-noise-ratio electron spectrometer and a highly precise fission normalization procedure.

## Key claim

The Reactor Antineutrino Anomaly (a roughly 5% deficit of electron antineutrinos from reactors) may be mainly due to faulty normalization of the ILL-measured $^{235}$U electron spectrum, but a simple renormalization of that spectrum alone does not resolve the anomaly because it leaves both the deficit at the IBD peak and the 5.5 MeV "bump" unchanged.

## Method

The authors digitized and analyzed electron spectra from 1970s Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL) decay-heat measurements following thermal neutron-induced fission of $^{235}$U, $^{239}$Pu, and $^{241}$Pu. They used summation calculations with nuclear databases (JEFF-3.3 and updated ENDF/B-VIII.0 decay data) to convert the measured spectra from non-equilibrium irradiation conditions to equilibrium spectra, then computed ratios of equilibrium electron spectra for different fissile isotopes. Uncertainties were estimated using a Monte Carlo method for database contributions and a quadratic fit to derive experimental uncertainties from the ORNL data.

## Key numbers

- The ILL group used a $^{207}$Pb thermal neutron capture cross section of 712$\pm$10 mb from the 1981 evaluation; the 2018 evaluation gives 647$\pm$9 mb, which is 9% lower.
- The ORNL $^{235}$U to $^{239}$Pu electron spectra ratio ($R_{59}$) values align with the lower trend reported by Kopeikin et al. (2021), which is approximately 5% lower than the ILL ratio.
- At 3.5-4.5 MeV, where short-irradiation corrections are minimal, the ORNL-derived $R_{59}$ values lie approximately halfway between the ILL and Kurchatov Institute measurements.
- The ORNL short irradiation accounted for 40-65% of the equilibrium spectrum in the 1.5 to 7 MeV energy interval.
- The most reliable energy range for the ORNL results is 1.5 to 5 MeV; above 5 MeV, statistical uncertainties become dominant.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Kopeikin et al. (2021), who measured the $^{235}$U to $^{239}$Pu electron spectra ratio and found it 5% lower than the ILL values, suggesting a $^{235}$U normalization issue. The authors also build on earlier normalization concerns raised by Letourneau and Onillon at the 2018 Applied Antineutrino Physics Workshop, who identified discrepancies in the $^{207}$Pb cross section between JEFF-3.1 and JEFF-3.3. The study enables a new experimental campaign to measure electron spectra with a high-resolution, high-signal-to-noise-ratio spectrometer and precise fission normalization, which would resolve the remaining inconsistencies in the $^{239}$Pu and $^{241}$Pu normalizations and provide definitive antineutrino spectra.
