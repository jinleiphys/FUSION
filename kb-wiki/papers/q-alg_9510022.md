# The Use of Quantum Groups in Nuclear Structure Problems

> Various applications of quantum algebraic techniques in nuclear structure physics, such as the su$_q$(2) rotator model and its extensions, the use of deformed bosons in the description of pairing correlations, and the construction of deformed exactly soluble models (Interacting Boson Model, Moszkowski model) are briefly reviewed. Emphasis is put in the study of the symmetries of the anisotropic quantum harmonic oscillator with rational ratios of frequencies, which underly the structure of superdeformed and hyperdeformed nuclei, the Bloch--Brink $α$-cluster model and possibly the shell structure in deformed atomic clusters.

## Key claim

This review argues that quantum algebraic techniques, particularly the su$_q$(2) rotator model and generalized deformed algebras, can describe rotational spectra, pairing correlations, and the symmetries of the anisotropic quantum harmonic oscillator with rational frequency ratios, but the paper is a brief survey rather than a presentation of new experimental results; the authors state that the deformed algebra approach provides a symmetry framework for superdeformed and hyperdeformed nuclei, the Bloch-Brink alpha-cluster model, and possibly deformed atomic clusters, with the caveat that many of these applications remain under development or are tested only in toy models.

## Method

The paper reviews the application of quantum groups (deformed Lie algebras) to nuclear structure, covering: (1) the su$_q$(2) rotator model where the Hamiltonian is proportional to the second-order Casimir operator of su$_q$(2) and the deformation parameter $\tau$ (with $q=e^{i\tau}$) is linked to the softness parameter of the Variable Moment of Inertia (VMI) model; (2) the use of $q$-deformed bosons for pairing correlations in single-$j$ shells; (3) $q$-deformed versions of the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) and Moszkowski model; and (4) a detailed algebraic construction showing that a generalized deformed u(2) algebra is the symmetry algebra of the two-dimensional anisotropic quantum harmonic oscillator with rational frequency ratios $m:n$ (where $m$ and $n$ are coprime natural numbers), with the structure function derived from the oscillator's creation and annihilation operators.

## Key numbers

- For the 2D anisotropic oscillator with frequency ratio $m=1$, $n=2$, the energy eigenvalues are given by $E = N + (2p-1)/(2m) + (2q-1)/(2n)$, yielding $E = N + 3/4$ for $(p,q)=(1,1)$ and $E = N + 5/4$ for $(p,q)=(1,2)$, with $N=0,1,2,\ldots$.
- For $m=1$, $n=2$, the structure function for $(p,q)=(1,1)$ is $\Phi(E,x) = x(N+1-x)(N+3/2-x)$ with $E = N + 5/4$, and for $(p,q)=(1,2)$ it is $\Phi(E,x) = x(N+1-x)(N+1/2-x)$ with $E = N + 3/4$.
- For $m=1$, $n=3$, the energy eigenvalues are $E = N + 2/3$ (for $q=1$), $E = N + 1$ (for $q=2$), and $E = N + 4/3$ (for $q=3$), with corresponding structure functions given as products of four factors.
- In the $m=1$, $n=2$ case, the "angular momentum" eigenvalues for the $N=1$, $(p,q)=(1,1)$ irrep are $\ell = \pm 1/\sqrt{2}$, and for $(p,q)=(1,2)$ they are $\ell = \pm \sqrt{3/2}$.
- In the $m=1$, $n=2$ case, the $N=2$, $(p,q)=(1,1)$ irrep has angular momentum eigenvalues $\ell = -2, 0, +2$, while the $(p,q)=(1,2)$ irrep has $\ell = -\sqrt{8}, 0, +\sqrt{8}$.
- The degeneracy pattern for the 1:2 frequency ratio is 1, 1, 2, 2, 3, 3, 4, 4, ... (two copies of u(2) degeneracies); for 1:3 it is 1, 1, 1, 2, 2, 2, 3, 3, 3, ... (three copies).

## Context

This review extends earlier work on quantum algebras in nuclear physics, including the su$_q$(2) rotator model introduced by Bonatsos and collaborators (1991-1995) for deformed and superdeformed nuclei, and the use of deformed oscillators for pairing correlations by Bonatsos and Daskaloyannis (1993). The detailed analysis of the anisotropic harmonic oscillator with rational frequency ratios builds on prior studies of superintegrable systems by Fradkin (1965) and the Holt potential and Fokas-Lagerstrom potential treated in Ref. 65 (likely Kalnins, Miller, and Pogosyan, 1990s). The work enables the systematic classification of single-particle levels in superdeformed and hyperdeformed nuclei, alpha-cluster configurations in light nuclei via the Bloch-Brink model, and potentially the shell structure of deformed atomic clusters, though the authors note that a $q$-deformed version of the full IBM is still under development.
