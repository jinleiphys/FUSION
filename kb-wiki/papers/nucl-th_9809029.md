# Antisymmetrized molecular dynamics with quantum branching processes for collisions of heavy nuclei

> Antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) with quantum branching processes is reformulated so that it can be applicable to the collisions of heavy nuclei such as Au + Au multifragmentation reactions. The quantum branching process due to the wave packet diffusion effect is treated as a random term in a Langevin-type equation of motion, whose numerical treatment is much easier than the method of the previous papers. Furthermore a new approximation formula, called the triple-loop approximation, is introduced in order to evaluate the Hamiltonian in the equation of motion with much less computation time than the exact calculation. A calculation is performed for the Au + Au central collisions at 150 MeV/nucleon. The result shows that AMD almost reproduces the copious fragment formation in this reaction.

## Key claim

Antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) with quantum branching processes is reformulated so that it can be applied to heavy-nucleus collisions such as Au + Au multifragmentation, by treating the wave packet diffusion effect as a random term in a Langevin-type equation of motion and introducing the triple-loop approximation to reduce computation time; the paper claims that the model "almost reproduces the copious fragment formation" for Au + Au central collisions at 150 MeV/nucleon, but this is a qualitative statement based on a single demonstrative calculation rather than a quantitative reproduction of experimental data.

## Method

The wave packet diffusion effect, previously treated via a frictional cooling procedure, is reformulated as a Gaussian random fluctuation added to the physical coordinates of each nucleon's wave packet centroid, with the fluctuation variance determined from the Vlasov equation. The fluctuation is converted into a one-body operator that generates a stochastic term in the equation of motion for the AMD wave function parameters, and energy conservation is restored by a cooling term restricted to neighboring packets. A new approximation called the triple-loop approximation is introduced to evaluate the AMD Hamiltonian with computation time proportional to the third power of the mass number instead of the fourth power.

## Key numbers

- The width parameter of the Gaussian wave packets is taken as nu = 0.16 fm^{-2}.
- The delay time for the fluctuation response is tau = 5 fm/c.
- The required CPU time for the original AMD calculation is proportional to the fourth power of the mass number; the triple-loop approximation reduces this to the third power, meaning that for Au + Au reactions the required CPU time is about 600 times that for Ca + Ca reactions in the original method.
- A calculation is performed for Au + Au central collisions at 150 MeV/nucleon.
- The binding energies obtained by the frictional cooling method coincide with experimental data within the precision of 1 MeV/nucleon.
- Clusters are identified by the condition that two packets i and j belong to the same cluster if |Z_i - Z_j| < 1.75.
- The neighborhood N_k for energy conservation is defined as packets within |Z_i - Z_k| < 2.5 that belong to the same cluster as packet k, excluding packet k itself.
- The monopole and quadrupole moments in coordinate and momentum space are included in the constrained quantities when N_k contains more than 15 packets.

## Context

This work extends the AMD-V model previously applied to Ca + Ca reactions at 35 MeV/nucleon (Ono, 1995; Wada, 1997) to heavy systems such as Au + Au, which had not been feasible due to computational cost. The reformulation of the wave packet diffusion as a Langevin-type random term makes the numerical treatment easier than the original frictional cooling method of Ono (1995), and the triple-loop approximation reduces the CPU time scaling from the fourth to the third power of mass number. The approach contrasts with the fluctuation-dissipation method of Ohnishi and Randrup (1997), which introduces correlations among different packets without microscopic justification, whereas the present work derives fluctuations from the Vlasov equation with independent fluctuations for different packets. The model enables microscopic dynamical calculations of multifragmentation for heavy systems like Au + Au, for which experimental data were published by Reisdorf (1997) but no satisfactory microscopic explanation had been given.
