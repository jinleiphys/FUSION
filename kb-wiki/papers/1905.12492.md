# The effect of internal magnetic field on collective flow in heavy ion collisions at intermediate energies

> The properties of nuclear matter under extreme conditions of high temperature, density and isospin-asymmetry have attracted wide attentions in recent years. At present, heavy ion reactions in combination with corresponding model simulations are one of the most important ways to investigate this subject. It is known that a strong magnetic field can be created in heavy ion collisions. However, its effect on the motion of charged particles is usually neglected in previous transport model simulations. In this work, within the Ultra-relativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) model, the temporal evolution and spatial distribution of the internal magnetic field are calculated. The magnetic field strength is found to reach about $eB\approx470$ MeV$^{2}$ ($B\approx8\times10^{16}$ G) for Au+Au collisions at $E_{\text{lab}}$=1 GeV/nucleon with impact parameter of 7 fm. The magnetic field in Cu+Au collisions exhibits somewhat different spatial distribution from that in Au+Au collisions. The magnetic field is found to affect the directed flow of pions at forward and backward rapidities to some extent, dependent of the impact parameter and beam energy while the effect on the elliptic flow is small. This suggests that, because $π$ mesons produced in heavy ion collisions at intermediate energies are considered as a sensitive probe for the nuclear symmetry energy, it is necessary to consider the effect of the internal magnetic field.

## Key claim
The internal magnetic field generated in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies (around 1 GeV/nucleon) has a noticeable effect on pion directed flow at forward and backward rapidities, but the effect on elliptic flow is small; the authors conclude that it is necessary to consider this magnetic field when using pions as a probe of the nuclear symmetry energy, because the magnetic field effect on the pion yield ratio can be comparable in magnitude to the symmetry energy effect.

## Method
The authors implemented the Liénard-Wiechert potential formalism within the Ultra-relativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) transport model to calculate the temporal evolution and spatial distribution of the internal magnetic field produced by all charged particles (both spectators and participants). They used a non-relativistic wave-packet approximation to avoid singularities from point-like particles, and added the resulting Lorentz force to the equations of motion for charged particles. Simulations were performed for Au+Au and Cu+Au collisions at beam energies of 0.4, 0.6, 1.0, and 1.5 GeV/nucleon with impact parameters from 1 to 10 fm, using three different Skyrme interactions (SV-mas08, Skz4, SV-sym34) that give different density dependences of the nuclear symmetry energy.

## Key numbers
- Magnetic field strength reaches about eB ≈ 470 MeV² (B ≈ 8×10¹⁶ G) for Au+Au collisions at Elab = 1 GeV/nucleon with impact parameter b = 7 fm.
- The relativistic correction to the magnetic field calculation is approximately +20% at times t ≲ 20 fm/c when the field is appreciable.
- The change in average transverse momentum ⟨p_x⟩ for pions due to the magnetic field is on the order of 5 MeV/c at forward and backward rapidities in Au+Au at 1 GeV/nucleon with b = 7 fm.
- The integral of the magnetic field over time, ∫₀¹⁰⁰ fm/c -eB_y(0,0,0) dt, shows only weak energy dependence across beam energies from 0.4 to 1.5 GeV/nucleon.

## Context
This work extends the earlier study by Ou and Li (2011) who used the IBUU transport model to investigate internal electromagnetic fields, finding that the magnetic field affected pions at large rapidities but had almost no effect on nucleon observables. The present UrQMD results are qualitatively consistent with those IBUU results but quantitatively different, which the authors attribute to different physical ingredients such as equations of state and two-body interaction cross sections. The paper enables future studies to include magnetic field effects when extracting the nuclear symmetry energy from pion observables, since the magnetic field effect on the π⁻/π⁺ ratio can be on the same order as the symmetry energy effect, potentially leading to wrong conclusions if neglected.
