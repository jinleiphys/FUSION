# Quark deconfinement and the duration of short Gamma Ray Bursts

> We propose a model for short duration gamma-ray bursts (sGRBs) based on the formation of a quark star after the merger of two neutron stars. We assume that the sGRB central engine is a proto-magnetar, which has been previously invoked to explain the plateau-like X-ray emission observed following both long and short GRBs. Here, we show that: i) a few milliseconds after the merger it is possible to form a stable and massive star made in part of quarks; ii) during the early cooling phase of the incompletely formed quark star, the flux of baryons ablated from the surface by neutrinos is large and it does not allow the outflow to achieve a bulk Lorentz factor high enough to produce a GRB; iii) after the quark burning front reaches the stellar surface, baryon ablation ceases and the jet becomes too baryon poor to produce a GRB; iv) however, between these two phases a GRB can be produced over the finite timescale required for the baryon pollution to cease; a characteristic timescale of the order of $\sim 0.1 $ s naturally results from the time the conversion front needs to cover the distance between the rotational pole and the latitude of the last closed magnetic field line; v) we predict a correlation between the luminosity of the sGRB and its duration, consistent with the data; vi) our model also predicts a delay of the order of ten seconds between the time of the merger event and the sGRB, allowing for the possibility of precursor emission and implying that the jet will encounter the dense cocoon formed immediately after the merger.

# Quark deconfinement and the duration of short Gamma Ray Bursts

## Key claim

The duration of prompt emission in short gamma-ray bursts (sGRBs) is set by the finite time required for the quark burning front to travel from the rotational pole to the latitude of the last closed magnetic field line on a newly formed quark star, producing a characteristic timescale of approximately 0.1 s. This mechanism explains why sGRB prompt emission is roughly two orders of magnitude shorter than in long GRBs, despite both classes likely sharing a similar proto-magnetar central engine.

## Method

The authors model the merger of two neutron stars that produces a massive proto-magnetar whose central region undergoes rapid quark deconfinement via thermal nucleation within a few milliseconds, followed by a slower burning front propagating outward through the hadronic outer layers. They compute the neutrino luminosity from both the rapid core burning and the slow outer burning phases, then use this to estimate the mass ablation rate from the stellar surface and the resulting maximum Lorentz factor of the magnetar wind. The duration of the sGRB prompt phase is derived from the time needed for the burning front to traverse the open magnetic field line region near the rotational pole, accounting for the star's rotational deformation and the geometry of the magnetosphere.

## Key numbers

- The rapid quark deconfinement of the central region occurs on a timescale of approximately 3-4 ms.
- The total heat deposited during the rapid burning phase is Q ~ 10^54 erg, with a neutrino diffusion time τ_diff ~ 3-4 s.
- During the first tenth of a second after merger, the mass loss rate reaches values as large as Ṁ ~ 10^{-3} M⊙ s^{-1}.
- The dynamical timescale for clearing baryons from the jet base is t_d ~ 0.01 s.
- The polar radius to equatorial radius ratio for a star rotating at ~1 kHz is R_eq/R_p ~ 1.2-1.4.
- The polar conversion time t_p ~ 10-20 s, giving a pole-to-equator conversion timescale Δt ~ a few seconds.
- The latitudinal extent of the open magnetosphere zone is θ_open ≈ 0.1 R_6 (P/2 ms)^{-1}, yielding a baryon cessation timescale t_c ~ a few 0.1 s.
- The neutrino energy from the merger remnant is approximately 10 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the proto-magnetar model previously applied to long GRBs by Thompson (2004) and Metzger (2010) and to sGRBs by Metzger (2008), Bucciantini (2011), and Rowlinson (2013), which successfully explained the extended emission and plateau phases but could not account for the short duration of sGRB prompt emission. The authors invoke the "two-families" scenario of Drago (2013, 2015) in which the most massive compact stars are quark stars rather than neutron stars, building on earlier work by Haensel (1991) and Dai (1998) showing that neutrinos of a few tens of MeV cannot ablate material from quark star surfaces. The model predicts a correlation between sGRB luminosity and duration consistent with the observational findings of Shahmoradi (2014), and also allows for precursor emission as observed by Troja (2010), with a delay of order ten seconds between merger and the main GRB event.
