# The impact of global nuclear mass model uncertainties on $r$-process abundance predictions

> Rapid neutron capture or `$r$-process' nucleosynthesis may be responsible for half the production of heavy elements above iron on the periodic table. Masses are one of the most important nuclear physics ingredients that go into calculations of $r$-process nucleosynthesis as they enter into the calculations of reaction rates, decay rates, branching ratios and Q-values. We explore the impact of uncertainties in three nuclear mass models on $r$-process abundances by performing global monte carlo simulations. We show that root-mean-square (rms) errors of current mass models are large so that current $r$-process predictions are insufficient in predicting features found in solar residuals and in $r$-process enhanced metal poor stars. We conclude that the reduction of global rms errors below $100$ keV will allow for more robust $r$-process predictions.

## Key claim

Current global nuclear mass model root-mean-square (rms) errors are too large to produce robust r-process abundance predictions; reducing these errors below 100 keV would allow differentiation between mass models and enable accurate predictions, but even at 100 keV some regions (e.g., near the N=82 and Z=50 closed shells) may require still higher accuracy.

## Method

The authors performed global Monte Carlo simulations of a hot r-process wind trajectory (entropy 200 k_B, electron fraction Y_e=0.3, timescale 80 ms) using a reduced reaction network starting at T=2 GK. For each Monte Carlo step, all nuclear masses entering the network were varied using a Gaussian distribution centered on the theoretical mass value with variance equal to the mass model's rms error, and only one-neutron separation energies were recalculated to update photo-dissociation rates. Three mass models were tested: Finite Range Droplet Model (FRDM1995), Duflo Zuker (DZ), and Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov version 17 (HFB-17), each coupled with beta-decay rates from Moller (2003) and neutron capture rates from NONSMOKER (1998).

## Key numbers

- FRDM1995 has an rms error of 649 keV when compared to nuclei with A > 20 in the 2012 Atomic Mass Evaluation.
- DZ has an rms error of 405 keV when compared to the same data set.
- HFB-17 has an rms error of 561 keV when compared to the same data set.
- Reducing mass model rms errors to 100 keV (hypothetical) reduces variability in r-process predictions to less than an order of magnitude in most regions.
- The hot wind trajectory has entropy 200 k_B, electron fraction Y_e=0.3, and timescale 80 ms, producing a main r-process component up to the third (A=195) peak.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous individual sensitivity studies of nuclear masses (Mumpower 2014a, Mumpower 2014b) and their investigations of individual neutron capture rates and beta-decay rates. It reinforces the conclusion from Brett (2012) and the authors' earlier work that mass model rms errors must be reduced to around 100 keV for robust predictions. The new Monte Carlo approach provides a lower bound on the impact of uncertain masses and enables future studies of mass correlations, skewed distributions, and additional astrophysical environments such as neutron star mergers.
