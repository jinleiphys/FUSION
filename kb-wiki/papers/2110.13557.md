# Neutron Stars and Gravitational Waves: the Key Role of Nuclear Equation of State

> Neutron stars are the densest known objects in the universe and an ideal laboratory for the strange physics of super-condensed matter. Theoretical studies in connection with recent observational data of isolated neutron stars, as well as binary neutron stars systems, offer an excellent opportunity to provide robust solutions on the dense nuclear problem. In the present work, we review recent studies concerning the applications of various theoretical nuclear models on a few recent observations of binary neutron stars or neutron-star--black-hole systems. In particular, using a simple and well-established model, we parametrize the stiffness of the equation of state with the help of the speed of sound. Moreover, in comparison to the recent observations of two events by LIGO/VIRGO collaboration, GW170817 and GW190425, we suggest possible robust constraints. We also concentrate our theoretical study on the resent observation of a compact object with mass~$\sim 2.59_{-0.09}^{+0.08}~M_{\odot}$ (GW190814 event), as a component of a system where the main companion was a black hole with mass $\sim 23~M_{\odot}$. There is scientific debate concerning the identification of the low mass component, as it falls into the neutron-star--black-hole mass gap. This is an important issue since understanding the nature of GW190814 event will offer rich information concerning the upper limit of the speed of sound in dense matter and the possible phase transition into other degrees of freedom. We systematically study the tidal deformability of a possible high-mass candidate existing as an individual star or as a component in a binary neutron star system. Finally, we provide some applications of equations of state of hot, dense nuclear matter in hot neutron stars, protoneutron stars, and binary neutron star merger remnants.

## Key claim

The paper reviews how parametrizing the nuclear equation of state through the speed of sound, combined with observational constraints from gravitational wave events GW170817, GW190425, and GW190814, can provide robust constraints on the stiffness of dense nuclear matter and the upper limit of the speed of sound, though the nature of the GW190814 secondary component (whether neutron star or black hole) remains an open debate.

## Method

The authors employ a momentum-dependent effective interaction (MDI) model to construct equations of state for cold and hot nuclear matter, parametrizing the stiffness via the speed of sound in the range between \(c/\sqrt{3}\) and \(c\). They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for nonrotating neutron stars and the stationary axisymmetric metric equations for rapidly rotating configurations, computing tidal deformability and Love numbers. The EoSs are matched to observational data from the LIGO/Virgo events GW170817, GW190425, and GW190814, and to measured neutron star masses from pulsar timing.

## Key numbers

- The GW190814 event involved a compact object with mass \(\sim 2.59_{-0.09}^{+0.08} M_{\odot}\) as a component of a binary system with a black hole of mass \(\sim 23 M_{\odot}\).
- The effective tidal deformability from GW170817 constrains the radius of a \(1.4 M_{\odot}\) neutron star to \(R_{1.4}=12.9_{-0.7}^{+0.8}\) km and the dimensionless tidal deformability to \(\Lambda_{1.4}=616_{-158}^{+273}\).
- Observed maximum neutron star masses include \(1.908 \pm 0.016 M_{\odot}\) (Arzoumanian 2018), \(2.01 \pm 0.04 M_{\odot}\) (Antoniadis 2013), \(2.14^{+0.10}_{-0.09} M_{\odot}\) (Cromartie 2019), and \(2.27^{+0.17}_{-0.15} M_{\odot}\) (Linares 2018).
- The merger rate density of GW190814-like binary systems was estimated as \(7_{-6}^{+16}\) Gpc\(^{-3}\)yr\(^{-1}\).
- The empirical constraint on maximum neutron star mass from population fitting is \(M_{\rm max} \leq 2.6 M_{\odot}\) at 90% confidence (Alsing 2018), while Farr and Chatziionannou (2020) obtained \(M_{\rm max}=2.25_{-0.26}^{+0.81} M_{\odot}\).

## Context

This work extends prior studies on the speed of sound in dense matter, notably the causal bound \(v_s \leq c\) established by Zel'dovich (1962, 1971) and the conformal bound \(v_s = c/\sqrt{3}\) discussed by Bedaque and Steiner (2015). It applies the speed-of-sound parametrization method from Tews (2018) and Margaritis (2020) to recent gravitational wave observations, building on earlier work by Moustakidis (2017) and Reed (2020) on tidal deformability constraints. The results enable future studies to distinguish between neutron star and black hole interpretations of high-mass compact objects like the GW190814 secondary, and to constrain phase transitions in dense matter through combined gravitational wave and electromagnetic observations.
