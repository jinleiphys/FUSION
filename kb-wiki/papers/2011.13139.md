# Deformed Halo of ^{29}_{9}F_{20}

> Using a simple model based on the knowledge of spherical and deformed Woods-Saxon potentials, it is shown that the recent observation of halo phenomena in $^{29}$F can be interpreted as an evidence for the prolate deformation of the ground state of $^{29}$F. The prolate deformation is the result of the shell structure, which is unique in one-neutron resonant levels, in particular near degeneracy of the neutron 1$f_{7/2}$ and 2$p_{3/2}$ resonant levels, together with the strong preference of prolate shape by the proton number $Z$ = 9. On the other hand, in oxygen isotopes spherical shape is so much favored by the proton number $Z$ = 8 that the presence of possible neutron shell-structure may not make the system deformed. Thus, the strong preference of particular shape by the proton numbers 8 and 9, respectively, together with a considerable amount of the energy difference between the neutron $1d_{3/2}$ and $2s_{1/2}$ orbits in oxygen isotopes seems to play an important role in the phenomena of oxygen neutron drip line anomaly, as was suggested by H. Sakurai {\it et al.} in 1999.

## Key claim

The observed halo phenomena in \(^{29}\)F can be interpreted as evidence for prolate deformation of its ground state, with the deformation arising from the unique shell structure of one-neutron resonant levels (near degeneracy of the neutron \(1f_{7/2}\) and \(2p_{3/2}\) resonant levels) combined with the strong preference of prolate shape by the proton number \(Z = 9\).

## Method

The author used a simple model based on spherical and axially-symmetric quadrupole-deformed Woods-Saxon potentials, with parameters taken from Bohr and Mottelson (1969). Neutron one-particle energies as a function of quadrupole deformation were obtained by solving coupled differential equations for radial wave functions in coordinate space with correct asymptotic behavior, with positive-energy resonant states defined via the eigenphase \(\delta_{\Omega}\). The radial dependence of weakly-bound neutron orbits was examined to identify halo effects.

## Key numbers

- The prolate deformation of \(^{29}\)F is estimated to be \(\beta \approx 0.4\) (specifically \(\beta \approx 0.44\) is noted as giving nearly equal Fermi energy to the spherical case for \(N = 20\)).
- The only bound excited state of \(^{29}\)F is observed at \(E_x = 1080 (18)\) keV, reported by Paulauskas et al. (2017).
- The heaviest bound oxygen isotope is \(^{24}_{8}\)O\(_{16}\) (16 neutrons), while fluorine accommodates up to \(^{31}_{9}\)F\(_{22}\) (22 neutrons), an increase of 6 neutrons.
- The heaviest bound neon isotope is \(^{34}_{10}\)Ne\(_{24}\), an increase of only 2 neutrons from fluorine.
- Woods-Saxon potential parameters: diffuseness \(a = 0.67\) fm, radius \(R = r_0 A^{1/3}\) with \(r_0 = 1.27\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the interpretation of deformed halo phenomena previously observed in odd-N nuclei such as \(^{31}\)Ne (Nakamura et al., 2009, 2014), \(^{37}\)Mg (Kobayashi et al., 2014), and \(^{29}\)Ne (Nakamura et al., 2016), where the degeneracy of \(f_{7/2}\) and \(p_{3/2}\) shells and the Jahn-Teller effect play a crucial role. The paper directly addresses the recent experimental observation of a two-neutron halo in \(^{29}\)F by Bagchi et al. (2020) and connects to the oxygen neutron drip line anomaly suggested by Sakurai et al. (1999), arguing that the shape difference between oxygen (spherical, \(Z=8\)) and fluorine (prolate, \(Z=9\)) is a key element. The work contrasts with shell-model calculations by Utsuno et al. (2001) that used harmonic-oscillator wave functions unable to properly describe halo properties, and with tensor-force explanations that may explain the drip line to \(N=20\) but not to \(N=22\).
