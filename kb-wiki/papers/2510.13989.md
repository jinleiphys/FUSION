# Surrogate Models for Linear Response

> Linear response theory is a well-established method in physics and chemistry for exploring excitations of many-body systems. In particular, the quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA) provides a powerful microscopic framework by building excitations on top of the mean-field vacuum; however, its high computational cost limits model calibration and uncertainty quantification studies. Here, we present two complementary QRPA surrogate models and apply them to study response functions of finite nuclei. One is a reduced-order model that exploits the underlying QRPA structure, while the other utilizes the recently developed parametric matrix model algorithm to construct a map between the system's Hamiltonian and observables. Our benchmark applications, the calculation of the electric dipole polarizability of ${}^{180}$Yb and the $β$-decay half-life of ${}^{80}$Ni, show that both emulators can achieve 0.1\%--1\% accuracy while offering a six to seven orders of magnitude speedup compared to state-of-the-art QRPA solvers. These results demonstrate that the developed QRPA emulators are well-positioned to enable Bayesian calibration and large-scale studies of computationally expensive physics models describing the properties of many-body systems.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that two complementary surrogate models for the quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA) can achieve 0.1%–1% accuracy while offering a six to seven orders of magnitude speedup compared to state-of-the-art QRPA solvers, but this performance is benchmarked only for two specific observables (electric dipole polarizability of 180Yb and β-decay half-life of 80Ni) and for variations of only two parameters of the underlying energy density functional at a time.

## Method

The authors constructed two emulators for QRPA calculations of finite nuclei. The first emulator (EM1) is a reduced-order model that approximates the high-dimensional strength function using a small number of Lorentzian peaks, with the reduced QRPA matrix represented as a linear combination of rank-n1 matrices learned from full order model (FOM) evaluations. The second emulator (EM2) uses the Parametric Matrix Model (PMM) algorithm in a purely data-driven setting, with trainable parametric matrices optimized to predict the target observables directly without constructing the intermediate strength function. Both emulators were trained using FOM evaluations from the finite-amplitude method (FAM) QRPA for the deformed nucleus 180Yb and a spherical matrix QRPA solver for 80Ni.

## Key numbers

- Both emulators achieve 0.1%–1% accuracy relative to the full order model.
- The emulators offer a speedup of six to seven orders of magnitude compared to state-of-the-art QRPA solvers.
- The full order model for 180Yb requires approximately 10^4 seconds of wall-clock time per calculation (40 CPUs with 10 GB memory per CPU).
- The full order model for 80Ni requires approximately 10^2 seconds per calculation (56 cores).
- The QRPA matrix dimension Nd is approximately 10^4–10^5 for the deformed nucleus 180Yb and approximately 10^4 for the spherical nucleus 80Ni.
- The strength function for 180Yb was evaluated on the interval ω ∈ [0,40] MeV with a step size of 0.1 MeV and Lorentzian smearing η = 1 MeV.
- The dipole polarizability contour integration used radius γ = 0.1 MeV with 10 discretized points.
- A total of 225 FOM calculations were performed on a regular grid for each system: for 180Yb spanning bTV ∈ [1.0,4.5] fm^2 and dTV ∈ [0.1,2.0]; for 80Ni spanning V0^is ∈ [0.0,2.0] and g0 ∈ [0.0,1.0].

## Context

This work extends the application of surrogate modeling techniques to nuclear QRPA calculations, which are computationally prohibitive for Bayesian model calibration and uncertainty quantification studies. The authors build on prior developments in reduced basis methods (Quarteroni 2015, Hesthaven 2016), the Parametric Matrix Model architecture (Cook 2025), and the finite-amplitude method for QRPA (Nakatsukasa 2007, Kortelainen 2015). The emulators are designed to enable large-scale studies of computationally expensive physics models describing many-body systems, particularly for calibrating nuclear energy density functionals that currently cannot include excited-state properties due to computational cost.
