# The state of matter in simulations of core-collapse supernovae -- Reflections and recent developments

> In this review article we discuss selected developments regarding the role of the equation of state (EOS) in simulations of core-collapse supernovae. There are no first-principle calculations of the state of matter under supernova conditions since a wide range of conditions is covered, in terms of density, temperature and isospin asymmetry. Instead, model EOS are commonly employed in supernova studies. These can be divided into regimes with intrinsically different degrees of freedom: heavy nuclei at low temperatures, inhomogeneous nuclear matter where light and heavy nuclei coexist together with unbound nucleons, and the transition to homogeneous matter at high densities and temperatures. In this article we discuss each of these phases with particular view on their role in supernova simulations.

## Key claim

This review argues that while model equations of state (EOS) are essential for core-collapse supernova simulations because no first-principle calculations exist for the full range of supernova conditions, the choice of EOS and its treatment across different density and temperature regimes significantly affects simulation outcomes, though the impact on the neutrino signal and general supernova evolution may be only on the order of a few percent for variations at supersaturation density.

## Method

The authors review the role of the EOS in core-collapse supernova simulations by dividing the supernova phase diagram into three regimes: heavy nuclei at low temperatures (below approximately 0.5 MeV), inhomogeneous nuclear matter where light and heavy nuclei coexist with unbound nucleons, and homogeneous matter at high densities and temperatures. They discuss spherically symmetric simulations employing accurate three-flavor Boltzmann neutrino transport within a fully self-consistent general relativistic radiation-hydrodynamics framework, comparing different nuclear models including relativistic mean field EOS, non-relativistic EOS, and the thermodynamic bag model for quark matter.

## Key numbers

- Central densities and maximum temperatures in supernova simulations may vary on the order of 10-20% depending on the stellar progenitor
- Normal nuclear matter density is approximately $\rho_0 \simeq 2.5\times10^{14}$ g cm$^{-3}$ ($n_0\simeq0.15$ fm$^{-3}$)
- Temperatures in supernova interiors can reach up to several $10^{11}$ K ($1.16\times10^{10}$ K $\simeq 1$ MeV)
- Nuclear pasta phases exist in density ranges: for $Y_e=0.05$, approximately $8.3\times 10^{13} - 1.3 \times 10^{14}$ g cm$^{-3}$ with melting temperature $T_{\rm melt} \sim 3-5$ MeV; for $Y_e=0.30$, approximately $3.3\times 10^{13} - 2.0 \times 10^{14}$ g cm$^{-3}$ with $T_{\rm melt} \sim 10$ MeV
- The vacuum Q-value for charged-current processes is $Q_0=m_n-m_p=1.2935$ MeV
- Neutron star mass constraint of 2 M$_\odot$ from observations by Antoniadis (2013) and Demorest (2010)
- The PNS deleptonization timescale is on the order of 10-30 seconds

## Context

This review extends and systematizes earlier work on EOS effects in supernova simulations, including studies by Marek (2008), Suwa (2013), and Nagakura (2017) in multi-dimensional frameworks, and by Sumiyoshi (2006), Fischer (2009), OConnor (2011), and Steiner (2013) in spherical symmetry. It builds on chiral effective field theory constraints from Hebeler (2010), Krueger (2013), and others, and on the discovery by Sagert (2008) that a hadron-quark phase transition could trigger supernova explosions. The review enables future work by identifying which EOS features and weak interaction treatments are most critical for accurate supernova modeling, particularly the need for consistent treatment of medium modifications in weak processes and the role of nuclear pasta phases in PNS deleptonization.
