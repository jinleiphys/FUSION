# Decomposition of fluctuating initial conditions and flow harmonics

> Collective flow observed in heavy ion collisions is largely attributed to initial geometrical fluctuations, and it is the hydrodynamic evolution of the system that transforms those initial spatial irregularities into final state momentum anisotropies. Cumulant analysis provides a mathematical tool to decompose those initial fluctuations in terms of radial and azimuthal components. It is usually thought that a specified order of azimuthal cumulant, for the most part, linearly produces flow harmonic of the same order. In this work, we carry out a systematic study on the connection between cumulants and flow harmonics using a hydrodynamic code called NeXSPheRIO. We conduct three types of calculations, by explicitly decomposing the initial conditions into components corresponding to a given eccentricity and studying the out-coming flow through hydrodynamic evolution. It is found that for initial conditions deviating significantly from Gaussian, such as those from NeXuS, the linearity between eccentricities and flow harmonics partially breaks down. Combining with the effect of coupling between cumulants of different orders, it causes the production of extra flow harmonics of higher orders. We argue that these results can be seen as a natural consequence of the non-linear nature of hydrodynamics, and they can be understood intuitively in terms of the peripheral-tube model.

## Key claim

For initial conditions that deviate significantly from Gaussian, such as those from the NeXuS event generator, the linear relationship between eccentricities and flow harmonics partially breaks down, and coupling between cumulants of different orders produces extra flow harmonics of higher orders.

## Method

The authors used the NeXSPheRIO hydrodynamic code to perform three types of calculations on NeXuS initial conditions for 0-5% most central collisions. They explicitly decomposed the initial energy density into Fourier components \(e_n(r,\phi)\), then (1) extracted individual \(e_n\) components and evolved them separately, (2) eliminated specific cumulants from the initial conditions, and (3) randomly rotated eccentricity planes while keeping eccentricity magnitudes fixed, then studied the resulting flow harmonics through hydrodynamic evolution.

## Key numbers

- For central collisions (0-5%), IC composed of \(e_1\) produced sizable \(v_2\) and \(v_3\) in addition to \(v_1\), as shown in event-by-event averages over 150 NeXuS events.
- IC with \(\varepsilon_3 \sim 0\) (much smaller than the original \(\varepsilon_3\)) nevertheless produced \(v_3\) comparable to the original \(v_3\) from the full event.
- In the peripheral-tube model test, tubes placed at radius \(r < 4.0\) fm in 200 \(A\)GeV Au+Au collisions contributed negligibly to collective flow, while tubes near the boundary produced significant flow.
- The non-linear response of \(v_4\) proportional to \(V_2^2\) (coefficient \(w_{4(22)}\)) was found to be a function of centrality and transverse momentum.

## Context

This work extends studies of the connection between initial eccentricities and flow harmonics, following the cumulant analysis framework of Alver and Roland (2010) and the nonlinear response studies of Gardim et al. (2012). It contradicts the assumption of linear hydrodynamic response for realistic event generators like NeXuS, which produce non-Gaussian initial conditions. The results enable further investigation of how much of the observed flow fluctuations come from eccentricity plane correlations versus eccentricity magnitude fluctuations, and support the peripheral-tube model as an alternative to triangularity-based explanations for ridge and shoulder structures in two-particle correlations.
