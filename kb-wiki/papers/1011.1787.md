# Volume-Enclosing Surface Extraction

> In this paper we present a new method, which allows for the construction of triangular isosurfaces from three-dimensional data sets, such as 3D image data and/or numerical simulation data that are based on regularly shaped, cubic lattices. This novel volume-enclosing surface extraction technique, which has been named VESTA, can produce up to six different results due to the nature of the discretized 3D space under consideration. VESTA is neither template-based nor it is necessarily required to operate on 2x2x2 voxel cell neighborhoods only. The surface tiles are determined with a very fast and robust construction technique while potential ambiguities are detected and resolved. Here, we provide an in-depth comparison between VESTA and various versions of the well-known and very popular Marching Cubes algorithm for the very first time. In an application section, we demonstrate the extraction of VESTA isosurfaces for various data sets ranging from computer tomographic scan data to simulation data of relativistic hydrodynamic fireball expansions.

## Key claim

VESTA constructs triangular isosurfaces from 3D data on cubic lattices without using templates, and can produce up to six different surface types depending on the chosen connectivity mode and resolution, while the abstract does not emphasize that the algorithm's ability to avoid holes depends on a consistent global or local choice of turning direction at points of ambiguity.

## Method

The algorithm starts by identifying all boundary faces between active voxels (above threshold) and inactive voxels, recording each face center as a potential surface support point. Voxel face vectors are collected from these boundary faces and grouped into closed vector cycles by connecting them through junctures at face edges, with points of ambiguity resolved by uniformly selecting either a "disconnect" mode (left turns) or a "connect" mode (right turns). The resulting N-cycles (N=3 to 12) are then decomposed into oriented triangles, optionally using an average point for cycles longer than 3 to improve accuracy. A marching variant processes 2x2x2 voxel neighborhoods sequentially using a predefined table of directed vector paths.

## Key numbers

- VESTA can produce up to **six** different types of surfaces: three connectivity modes (disconnect, connect, mixed) each in low resolution (L) or high resolution (H) mode.
- A single isolated active voxel yields **48** initial voxel face vectors, which are processed into **24** VESTA cycle vectors forming **12** edges of an octahedron.
- There are a total of **14** different types of VESTA N-cycles (N=3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 12); N-cycles of length 10 or 11 do not exist.
- The maximum VESTA surface cycle length is **12** (a nonplanar 12-cycle).
- All VESTA surface cycles are confined to a **2x2x2** voxel neighborhood at all times.

## Context

This work extends the DICONEX algorithm (Schlei, 2009) from 2D contour extraction into 3D surface extraction, and provides the first in-depth comparison between VESTA and various versions of the Marching Cubes algorithm (Lorensen and Cline, 1987). VESTA addresses known issues in prior approaches: the original Marching Cubes could generate holes due to overlooked templates (Lohmann, 1998), Marching Tetrahedrons has directional ambiguities (Doi and Koide, 1991; Gueziec and Hummel, 1995), and Marching Lines requires further processing to resolve cell ambiguities (Thirion and Gourdon, 1996; Bloomenthal, 1994). The method enables extraction of isosurfaces from computer tomographic scan data and simulation data of relativistic hydrodynamic fireball expansions, and can be implemented in a memory-efficient marching variant suitable for large datasets.
