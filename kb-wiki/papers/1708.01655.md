# Possible Beyond the Standard Model Physics Motivated by Muonic Puzzles

> Recent measurements of the proton radius using the Lamb shift in muonic hydrogen are troublingly discrepant with values extracted from hydrogen spectroscopy and electron-proton scattering experiments. This discrepancy, which differs by more than five standard deviations, may be a signal of new physics caused by a violation of lepton universality. Another candidate for a new physics signal is the muon anomalous magnetic moment. The measurement at BNL differs from the standard model prediction by at least three standard deviations. Motivated by these two puzzles, first we use polarized lepton-nucleon elastic scattering to search for a new scalar boson, and furthermore we suggest new measurements of the nucleon form factors. Next, we display a method to analyze the beam dump experiments without using approximation on phase space, and we use it to constrain all possible new spin-0 and spin-1 particles, and a variety of other measurements to study the possibility of the new physics. Finally, assuming a new scalar boson can solve the two puzzles simultaneously, we present a general model-independent analysis and constrain the existence of the new physics.

## Key claim

The proton radius discrepancy (greater than five standard deviations) and the muon anomalous magnetic moment discrepancy (at least three standard deviations) could both be explained by a new light scalar boson that couples preferentially to muons and protons, but the scalar boson signal in elastic lepton-nucleon scattering is only potentially observable in narrow kinematic regions where the standard model one-photon-exchange cross section approaches zero.

## Method

The author calculates the differential cross section for elastic lepton-nucleon scattering including one-photon exchange, one-scalar-boson exchange, and their interference, using all possible polarization configurations in a general reference frame. For beam dump experiments, the author develops a method to compute cross sections and particle production numbers without using the Weizsacker-Williams approximation or Monte Carlo simulations, enabling more accurate constraints on new spin-0 and spin-1 particles. A general model-independent analysis is then performed to constrain the mass and couplings of a hypothetical scalar boson that could simultaneously resolve both muonic puzzles.

## Key numbers

- Proton radius from muonic hydrogen Lamb shift: 0.84087(39) fm; CODATA (2014) value: 0.8751(61) fm; discrepancy exceeds five standard deviations
- Muon anomalous magnetic moment discrepancy: Delta a_mu = (287 +/- 80) x 10^{-11} (Davier 2010) or (261 +/- 78) x 10^{-11} (Hagiwara 2011); experimental and standard model uncertainties are 63 x 10^{-11} and approximately 49 x 10^{-11}, respectively
- For a scalar boson mass m_phi = 1 MeV, coupling constraints: epsilon_e less than or similar to 2.3 x 10^{-4}, epsilon_p = epsilon_mu less than or similar to 1.3 x 10^{-3}, epsilon_n less than or similar to 6.7 x 10^{-5}
- The new scalar boson mass is constrained to between about 100 keV and 100 MeV by requiring it to solve both puzzles simultaneously

## Context

This work extends the search for new physics motivated by the proton radius puzzle first highlighted by Pohl (2010) and Antognini (2013) and the muon g-2 discrepancy from the BNL measurement reported by Blum (2013). The analysis builds on constraints from TuckerSmith (2010) and provides a systematic method to explore parameter space for a light scalar boson, identifying unexplored regions in the coupling constant-mass plane for future experiments such as those at JLab, MUSE at PSI, and the J-PARC muon facility.
