# Dyson-Schwinger Equations: An Instrument for Hadron Physics

> Dyson-Schwinger equations furnish a Poincare' covariant approach to hadron physics. They reveal that dynamical chiral symmetry breaking is tied to the long-range behaviour of the strong interaction and make predictions corroborated by modern lattice-QCD simulations. A hallmark in the contemporary use of DSEs is the existence of a nonperturbative, symmetry preserving truncation that enables the proof of exact results. The systematic error associated with the truncation's leading term has been quantified and this underpins an efficacious one-parameter model that is being employed to study meson excited states.

# Dyson-Schwinger Equations: An Instrument for Hadron Physics

## Key claim
Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs) provide a Poincaré covariant framework for hadron physics in which a nonperturbative, symmetry-preserving truncation scheme exists, and the systematic error of its leading (rainbow-ladder) term has been quantified. The framework yields predictions for meson properties that agree with lattice-QCD results, but the long-range behaviour of the light-quark interaction is unknown and must be modelled, restricting model dependence to the infrared domain \(k^2 \lesssim 1\,\text{GeV}^2\).

## Method
The authors solve the DSE for the dressed-quark propagator (the gap equation) and the inhomogeneous Bethe-Salpeter equations for the axial-vector and pseudoscalar vertices, using a systematic dressed-loop expansion of the dressed-quark-gluon vertices. For the gap equation’s kernel, they employ a one-parameter model for the dressed-gluon interaction that combines an infrared Gaussian enhancement with a perturbative ultraviolet term, and they solve the ladder-truncated Bethe-Salpeter equation for pseudoscalar mesons via a Chebyshev decomposition of the amplitude.

## Key numbers
- The infrared domain where model dependence enters is \(k^2 \lesssim 1\,\text{GeV}^2\).
- For the one-parameter interaction model, the fitted observables are approximately constant along the trajectory \(\omega D = (0.72\,\text{GeV})^3\) for \(\omega \in [0.3, 0.5]\,\text{GeV}\), defining a dressed-glue mass-scale \(m_g = 720\,\text{MeV}\).
- Ground-state pion results at current-quark mass \(m_u(1\,\text{GeV}) = m_d(1\,\text{GeV}) = 5.5\,\text{MeV}\): \(f_{\pi_0} = 0.092\,\text{GeV}\), \(m_{\pi_0} = 0.14\,\text{GeV}\), \(\rho_{\pi_0} = (0.81\,\text{GeV})^2\).
- First excited pseudoscalar meson mass (using only the \(i\gamma_5 E\) amplitude): \(m_{\pi_1} = 1.1\,\text{GeV}\), compared to the experimental value \(m_{\pi_1}^{\text{expt.}} = 1.3 \pm 0.1\,\text{GeV}\).
- Estimated leptonic decay constant of the first excited state: \(f_{\pi_{n=1}} \leq 1.5\,\text{MeV}\) at physical current-quark mass.
- In the chiral limit, the leptonic decay constant vanishes for every pseudoscalar meson except the ground-state pion: \(f^0_{\pi_{n\neq 0}} = 0\).
- The dynamically generated dressed-quark mass at zero momentum is \(M(0) \approx 0.5\,\text{GeV}\).
- The \(\rho\)-\(\pi\) mass splitting is present in the chiral limit; the vector meson mass receives a \(\sim 10\%\) correction when going from rainbow-ladder to the fully resummed truncation, and including the first correction (\(n=1\)) yields a vector meson mass within \(\lesssim 1\%\) of the fully resummed result.

## Context
This work extends the DSE approach pioneered in reviews by Roberts and collaborators (review articles from the late 1990s and early 2000s) and builds directly on the symmetry-preserving truncation scheme developed by Bender, Detmold, and colleagues (2002). The one-parameter interaction model of Maris and Tandy (1999–2000) is used to compute ground-state meson properties, and the paper extends this to excited pseudoscalar mesons for the first time. The results agree with quenched lattice-QCD data for the dressed-quark propagator from Bowman and colleagues (2002). The exact relation \(f_{\pi_n} m_{\pi_n}^2 = [m_u(\zeta) + m_d(\zeta)] \rho_{\pi_n}(\zeta)\) generalises the Gell-Mann–Oakes–Renner relation to all pseudoscalar mesons and provides a constraint on models of exotics and hybrids. The framework enables future calculations of meson electroweak form factors, \(\pi\pi\) scattering, and baryon observables.
