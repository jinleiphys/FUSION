# Dissipation coefficients from scalar and fermion quantum field interactions

> Dissipation coefficients are calculated in the adiabatic, near thermal equilibrium regime for a large class of renormalizable interaction configurations involving a two-stage mechanism, where a background scalar field is coupled to heavy intermediate scalar or fermion fields which in turn are coupled to light scalar or fermion radiation fields. These interactions are typical of warm inflation microscopic model building. Two perturbative regimes are shown where well defined approximations for the spectral functions apply. One regime is at high temperature, when the masses of both intermediate and radiation fields are less than the temperature scale and where the poles of the spectral functions dominate. The other regime is at low temperature, when the intermediate field masses are much bigger than the temperature and where the low energy and low three-momentum regime dominate the spectral functions. The dissipation coefficients in these two regimes are derived. However, due to resummation issues for the high temperature case, only phenomenological approximate estimates are provided for the dissipation in this regime. In the low temperature case, higher loop contributions are suppressed and so no resummation is necessary. In addition to inflationary cosmology, the application of our results to cosmological phase transitions is also discussed.

# Dissipation coefficients from scalar and fermion quantum field interactions

## Key claim

The paper derives dissipation coefficients for a background scalar field in the adiabatic, near-thermal-equilibrium regime for two-stage interaction mechanisms (catalyst fields mediating between the background field and light radiation fields), but the high-temperature regime results are only phenomenological approximate estimates due to unresolved resummation issues, whereas the low-temperature regime results are reliable because higher-loop contributions are suppressed.

## Method

The authors compute dissipation coefficients using the closed-time-path (CTP) functional formalism, integrating over intermediate "catalyst" fields (scalars χ or fermions ψ_χ) that couple to both the background scalar field φ and to light radiation fields (scalars σ or fermions ψ_σ). The dissipation coefficient Υ is expressed in terms of spectral functions for the catalyst fields, which are evaluated in two distinct perturbative regimes: the pole approximation (dominating at high temperature where catalyst and radiation masses are both less than T) and the low-momentum approximation (dominating at low temperature where catalyst masses are much larger than T). The spectral functions incorporate one-loop self-energies from the decay processes χ → σσ, χ → ψ_σ ψ̄_σ, and ψ_χ → σ ψ̄_σ.

## Key numbers

- Low-temperature regime (m_χ, m_ψ_χ ≫ T ≫ m_σ, m_ψ_σ): dissipation coefficients scale as a power law in temperature rather than being exponentially suppressed, in contrast to what the pole approximation would predict
- High-temperature regime (m_χ, m_ψ_χ, m_σ, m_ψ_σ ≪ T): only phenomenological approximate estimates are provided due to resummation issues
- Very low-temperature regime (all masses ≫ T): dissipation coefficients are suppressed by Boltzmann exponential factors
- Coupling constraints for perturbative control: g_3² ≪ h_1, h_2 and λ_χ ≪ h_1, h_2 (for scalar interactions); λ_σ ≪ g_3², h_2², h_3² (to keep σ self-interaction subdominant)

## Context

This work extends the two-stage dissipation mechanism first developed for warm inflation model building by Berera and Ramos (2001) and further studied by Moss and Xiong (2006). The paper provides a systematic treatment of both scalar and fermion catalyst fields across multiple temperature regimes, which was previously lacking. The results enable quantitative modeling of dissipative effects in warm inflation and cosmological phase transitions (Grand Unified, electroweak, QCD, chiral) where the order parameter interacts with heavy intermediate fields that decay into light radiation. The authors note that gauge field interactions, required for many applications, are left for future work.
