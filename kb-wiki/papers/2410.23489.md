# Pauli energy contribution to nucleus-nucleus interaction

> The investigation delves into understanding how the Pauli exclusion principle influences the bare potential between atomic nuclei through the application of advanced theoretical methodologies. Specifically, the application of the novel Frozen-Hartree-Fock (DCFHF) technique is employed. The resulting potentials demonstrate a noticeable repulsion at short distances, attributed to the effects of the Pauli exclusion principle. To account for dynamic phenomena, such as nucleon transfer processes, the density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock (DC-TDHF) method is utilized. This approach integrates isovector contributions into the potential, shedding light on their influence on fusion reactions. Notably, the inclusion of isovector effects leads to a reduction or enhancement in the inner part of the potential, suggesting a nuanced role of transfer in the fusion process.

## Key claim

The Pauli exclusion principle contributes a repulsive kinetic-energy component to the nucleus-nucleus interaction that is not fully captured by standard frozen-Hartree-Fock potentials; this Pauli repulsion widens and raises the fusion barrier, and its proton and neutron contributions differ significantly in neutron-rich and asymmetric systems, with dynamical effects (including multinucleon transfer) generally reducing the repulsion near the barrier but increasing it inside.

## Method

The authors computed bare nucleus-nucleus potentials using the density-constrained frozen Hartree-Fock (DCFHF) method, which enforces antisymmetrization between all nucleons while keeping the proton and neutron densities fixed to their ground-state shapes. Dynamical potentials were obtained via the density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock (DC-TDHF) method, which uses the time-evolving densities from TDHF trajectories. The Pauli kinetic energy (PKE) was extracted from the nuclear localization function (NLF) formalism, and its proton and neutron components were separated by comparing DCFHF results with conventional frozen Hartree-Fock (FHF) results that neglect Pauli exchange between different nuclei.

## Key numbers

- For the $^{48}$Ca+$^{48}$Ca system at a separation of $R\approx 9$ fm (inner turning point at roughly 0.9 times the barrier potential $V_B$), the neutron contribution to the Pauli energy significantly surpasses the proton contribution.
- In the $^{16}$O+$^{208}$Pb system inside the barrier, the neutron contribution to the Pauli repulsion is roughly double that of the proton contribution.
- The NLF plots for the $^{40}$Ca+$^{40}$Ca, $^{48}$Ca+$^{48}$Ca, and $^{16}$O+$^{208}$Pb systems were shown at an approximate internuclear distance of 11 fm.
- For the $^{40}$Ca+$^{40}$Ca system ($N=Z$), the proton and neutron contributions to the Pauli energy are almost identical.

## Context

This work extends the study of Pauli repulsion in nucleus-nucleus potentials beyond the earlier frozen-Hartree-Fock approach of Simenel (2017), which first showed that Pauli repulsion widens the barrier and hinders sub-barrier fusion. The authors build on the nuclear localization function formalism developed by Reinhard (2011) and generalized by Li (2020) to include time-odd terms. The results enable future microscopic investigations of pairing and deformation effects on Pauli repulsion (Umar, 2021), and they motivate extending TDHF to sub-barrier energies using imaginary-time methods to study the role of dynamics on Pauli kinetic energy below the Coulomb barrier.
