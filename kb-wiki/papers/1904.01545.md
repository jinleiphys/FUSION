# Merger of compact stars in the two-families scenario

> We analyse the phenomenological implications of the two-families scenario on the merger of compact stars. That scenario is based on the coexistence of both hadronic stars and strange quark stars. After discussing the classification of the possible mergers, we turn to detailed numerical simulations of the merger of two hadronic stars, i.e., "first family" stars in which delta resonances and hyperons are present, and we show results for the threshold mass of such binaries, for the mass dynamically ejected and the mass of the disk surrounding the post-merger object. We compare these results with those obtained within the one-family scenario and we conclude that relevant signatures of the two-families scenario can be suggested, in particular: the possibility of a rapid collapse to a black hole for masses even smaller than the ones associated to GW170817; during the first milliseconds, oscillations of the postmerger remnant at frequencies higher than the ones obtained in the one-family scenario; a large value of the mass dynamically ejected and a small mass of the disk, for binaries of low total mass. Finally, based on a population synthesis analysis, we present estimates of the number of mergers for: two hadronic stars; hadronic star - strange quark star; two strange quark stars. We show that for unequal mass systems and intermediate values of the total mass, the merger of a hadronic star and a strange quark star is very likely (GW170817 has a possible interpretation into this category of mergers). On the other hand, mergers of two strange quark stars are strongly suppressed.

## Key claim
The two-families scenario, where hadronic stars (HSs) and strange quark stars (QSs) coexist, predicts that the merger of two hadronic stars can lead to a prompt collapse to a black hole for total masses even smaller than those associated with GW170817, and that the merger of a hadronic star and a strange quark star is a very likely interpretation for GW170817 itself, provided the system has unequal masses and intermediate total mass.

## Method
The authors performed fully general-relativistic numerical simulations of the merger of two hadronic stars (HSs) using the Einstein Toolkit, employing the SFHo-HD equation of state which includes delta resonances and hyperons. They determined the threshold mass for prompt collapse to a black hole, the dynamically ejected mass, and the disk mass for these binaries. Separately, they used the population synthesis code Startrack to estimate the relative merger rates for the three possible binary types: HS-HS, HS-QS, and QS-QS.

## Key numbers
- The maximum mass of a static hadronic star (HS) in the two-families scenario is $\mtov^H \sim 1.6 M_{\odot}$.
- The maximum mass of a static strange quark star (QS) is $\mtov^Q \sim 2.1 M_{\odot}$.
- The threshold mass for prompt collapse of a HS-HS binary is $\mth \sim 2.5 M_{\odot}$.
- The maximum mass of a supramassive, uniformly rotating Coll-hybrid star (a transient hadron-quark configuration) is $\mshyb \sim 2.6 M_{\odot}$.
- The minimum mass of a strange quark star formed from the conversion of a hadronic star is $\sim (1.35-1.45) M_{\odot}$.
- The condition for quark matter nucleation in hadronic matter is satisfied at a baryon density of (0.9–1) fm$^{-3}$, corresponding to a strangeness fraction $Y_S \sim 0.2-0.3$.

## Context
This work extends the two-families scenario (Drago 2013, Wiktorowicz 2017) by providing the first detailed numerical simulations of HS-HS mergers within that framework, building on a preliminary study (Drago 2017). It contrasts the results with the standard one-family scenario and the twin-stars scenario (Alford 2013, Paschalidis 2017), showing that the two-families scenario can accommodate a smaller radius for a 1.4 $M_{\odot}$ star (down to about 10 km) than the one-family scenario (larger than about 12 km), as constrained by the tidal deformability from GW170817 (Burgio 2018). The work enables future studies of the electromagnetic counterparts (kilonovae and short gamma-ray bursts) from these distinct merger types and provides a framework for interpreting gravitational wave signals from binary compact star mergers.
