# Prediction of Alpha-Decay Half-Lives of Actinide Nuclei Using the DDM3Y Effective Interaction Potential

> The prediction of nuclear half-lives is vital for understanding nuclear stability with significant applications in astrophysics, nuclear energy, and medical physics. This study investigates the $α$-decay half-lives of 154 actinide nuclei in the atomic number range $89 \le Z \le 103$ using the Density-Dependent M3Y (DDM3Y) effective interaction potential. The theoretical framework utilizes a double-folding model where the densities of the $α$-particle and the daughter nucleus are folded to derive the nuclear interaction potential.Theoretical half-lives were calculated using the WKB approximation and compared against experimental data and established semi-empirical models, including the Viola-Seaborg (VSS), CPPM, GLDM, and ELDM frameworks. The DDM3Y model demonstrates a systematically improved agreement with experimental half-lives across the actinide series, effectively capturing the inverse correlation between $Q$-values and decay times. Statistical analysis yielded a standard deviation of 1.76, confirming the reliability of this approach for predicting the stability and decay properties of heavy and new isotopes.

## Key claim

The DDM3Y effective interaction potential, applied within a double-folding model and WKB approximation, yields alpha-decay half-lives for 154 actinide nuclei (Z=89-103) that show systematically improved agreement with experimental data compared to semi-empirical formulas, with a standard deviation of 1.76, though the model shows notable outliers for specific isotopes such as 228Pu, 246Fm, 252No, 213Ac, 227Np, 229Am, and 251No.

## Method

The authors calculated alpha-decay half-lives by constructing the total alpha-nucleus potential as the sum of a double-folded nuclear interaction potential (using DDM3Y effective interaction with Gaussian alpha-particle density and two-parameter Fermi density for daughter nuclei), a Coulomb potential, and a centrifugal term (set to zero for ground-state to ground-state transitions). Barrier penetrability was computed using the WKB approximation, and half-lives were obtained using the decay constant formula with pre-formation probabilities of 0.43 for even-even, 0.35 for odd-A, and 0.18 for odd-odd nuclei. The results were compared against experimental data and predictions from the Viola-Seaborg (VSS), Coulomb and Proximity Potential Model (CPPM), Generalized Liquid Drop Model (GLDM), and Effective Liquid Drop Model (ELDM).

## Key numbers

- Standard deviation of 1.76 for the DDM3Y model across 154 actinide isotopes (Z=89-103), indicating overall agreement with experimental half-lives
- For 209Ac, the DDM3Y model predicted a half-life of 245.87 ms compared to the experimental value of 0.087 s (87 ms), while VSS gave 0.022 s, CPPM gave 0.578 s, GLDM gave 0.033 s, and ELDM gave 0.075 s
- For 238Pu, the DDM3Y model predicted a half-life of 54.2 years compared to the experimental value of 87.7 years, while VSS gave 81.8 years, CPPM gave 1.77 x 10^4 years, GLDM gave 7.93 years, and ELDM gave 241 years
- For 241Am, the DDM3Y model predicted a half-life of 395 years compared to the experimental value of 432.6 years, while VSS gave 141 years, CPPM gave 3.04 x 10^4 years, GLDM gave 3.81 x 10^2 years, and ELDM gave 332 years
- For 244Cm, the DDM3Y model predicted a half-life of 11.18 days compared to the experimental value of 18.11 years, showing a large discrepancy for this isotope

## Context

This work extends the application of the DDM3Y double-folding model, previously used for elastic alpha-particle scattering analyses by Gils (1987) and for alpha-decay half-lives in the range Z=52-110 by Xu (2006), to the specific actinide region Z=89-103. The study provides a systematic comparison against the Viola-Seaborg formula (Viola and Seaborg, 1966), the Coulomb and Proximity Potential Model, the Generalized Liquid Drop Model (Royer, 2000), and the Effective Liquid Drop Model, demonstrating that the DDM3Y approach offers competitive accuracy for predicting half-lives of heavy and potentially new isotopes in the actinide series.
