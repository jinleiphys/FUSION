# The possibility of $^{14}$C cluster as a building block of medium mass nuclei

> The possibility of the $^{14}$C cluster being a basic building block of medium mass nuclei is discussed. Although $α$ cluster structures have been widely discussed in the light $N\approx Z$ mass region, the neutron to proton ratio deviates from unity in the nuclei near $β$-stability line and in neutron-rich nuclei. Thus, more neutron-rich objects with $N>Z$ could become the building blocks of cluster structures in such nuclei. The $^{14}$C nucleus is strongly bound and can be regarded as such a candidate. In addition, the path to the lowest shell-model configuration at short relative distances is closed for the $^{14}$C+$^{14}$C structure contrary to the case of the $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C structure; this allows to keep appreciable separation distance between the $^{14}$C clusters. The recent development of antisymmetrized quasi-cluster model (AQCM) allows us to utilize $jj$-coupling shell model wave function for each cluster in a simplified way. The AQCM results for the $^{14}$C+$^{14}$C structure in $^{28}$Mg are compared with the ones of cranked relativistic mean field (CRMF) calculations. Although theoretical frameworks of these two models are quite different, they give similar results for the nucleonic densities and rotational properties of the structure under investigation. The existence of linear chain three $^{14}$C cluster structure in $^{42}$Ar has also been predicted in AQCM. These results confirm the role of the $^{14}$C cluster as a possible building block of cluster structures in medium mass nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the $^{14}$C nucleus, with its neutron-to-proton ratio of 1.33, can serve as a building block for cluster structures in medium-mass nuclei, particularly where the beta-stability line deviates from $N \approx Z$. The claim is hedged by the observation that the $^{14}$C+$^{14}$C cluster structure in $^{28}$Mg appears only in specific excited configurations (the [0,2] configuration in CRMF) and that the two theoretical models used (AQCM and CRMF) give similar density distributions but differ in the predicted rotational behavior of the cluster structure.

## Method

The authors employed the antisymmetrized quasi-cluster model (AQCM) to describe $^{14}$C clusters, using the Tohsaki interaction for the central nucleon-nucleon force and the G3RS interaction for the spin-orbit part. For $^{28}$Mg, the AQCM results for the $^{14}$C+$^{14}$C structure were compared with cranked relativistic mean field (CRMF) calculations using the NL3* functional. The AQCM was also used to investigate a linear chain of three $^{14}$C clusters in $^{42}$Ar.

## Key numbers

- The optimal distance between two $^{14}$C clusters in $^{28}$Mg is approximately 4 fm, compared to about 2 fm for the $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C system in $^{24}$Mg.
- The $^{14}$C nucleus has a neutron threshold at $E_x = 8.18$ MeV, with no excited state below $E_x = 6$ MeV.
- The moment of inertia of the linear chain three-$^{14}$C cluster structure in $^{42}$Ar is 15.59 MeV$^{-1}$.
- The quadrupole deformation $\beta_2$ of the ground state band [0,0] in $^{28}$Mg is 0.34 at spin $I=0$; the [0,2] configuration (representing the $^{14}$C+$^{14}$C cluster) has a total quadrupole deformation of 0.81 at spin zero, and the [1,2] configuration has 0.89.
- The size parameter $\nu$ was set to 0.17 fm$^{-2}$ for both $^{28}$Mg and $^{42}$Ar calculations.

## Context

This work extends the study of cluster structures beyond the traditional $\alpha$-cluster (N=Z) region into neutron-rich medium-mass nuclei, where the spin-orbit interaction is known to break $\alpha$ clusters (PhysRevC.70.054307, 2004). The paper builds on the AQCM framework developed by Itagaki and collaborators (PhysRevC.94.064324, 2016; PhysRevC.87.054334, 2013) and compares with CRMF calculations previously applied to clusterization in light nuclei up to $^{50}$Ti (RA.16; AA.18). The results suggest that $^{14}$C clusters could be a basic building block for superdeformed bands in $^{28}$Mg and linear chain structures in $^{42}$Ar, motivating future experimental searches for such rotational bands.
