# Modeling GW170817 based on numerical relativity and its implications

> Gravitational-wave observation together with a large number of electromagnetic observations shows that the source of the latest gravitational-wave event, GW170817, detected primarily by advanced LIGO, is the merger of a binary neutron star. We attempt to interpret this observational event based on our results of numerical-relativity simulations performed so far paying particular attention to the optical and infra-red observations. We finally reach a conclusion that this event is described consistently by the presence of a long-lived hypermassive or supramassive neutron star as the merger remnant, because (i) significant contamination by lanthanide elements along our line of sight to this source can be avoided by the strong neutrino irradiation from it and (ii) it could play a crucial role to produce an ejecta component of appreciable mass with fast motion in the post-merger phase. We also point out that (I) the neutron-star equation of state has to be sufficiently stiff (i.e., the maximum mass of cold spherical neutron stars, M_max, has to be appreciably higher than 2M_sun in order that a long-lived massive neutron star can be formed as the merger remnant for the binary systems of GW170817, for which the initial total mass is >~ 2.73M_sun and (II) no detection of relativistic optical counterpart suggests a not-extremely high value of M_max approximately as 2.15-2.25M_sun.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the optical and infrared observations of GW170817 are consistently explained by the presence of a long-lived hypermassive or supramassive neutron star as the merger remnant, but only if the neutron-star equation of state is sufficiently stiff (maximum mass appreciably higher than 2 solar masses) while simultaneously not extremely high (maximum mass approximately 2.15-2.25 solar masses), based on the absence of a relativistic optical counterpart.

## Method

The authors interpreted electromagnetic observations of GW170817 by comparing them with results from their numerical-relativity simulations of binary neutron star mergers, using two representative equations of state (SFHo with maximum mass 2.06 solar masses and DD2 with maximum mass 2.42 solar masses). They performed shear-viscous-radiation hydrodynamics simulations in general relativity for merger remnants, evolving both the massive neutron star and torus in a self-consistent manner with alpha-viscosity parameters of 0.01, 0.02, and 0.04. The simulations included finite-temperature effects of nuclear-matter equation of state, neutrino cooling, and neutrino heating.

## Key numbers

- Total mass of GW170817 binary system constrained to range between approximately 2.73 and 2.78 solar masses for the 90% credible interval
- Chirp mass approximately 1.188 solar masses with 90% credible interval +0.004/-0.002 solar masses
- Luminosity distance to source approximately 40 +8/-14 Mpc
- Inclination angle of binary orbital axis constrained to less than 28 degrees
- For SFHo equation of state (maximum mass 2.06 solar masses): dynamical ejecta mass approximately 0.01 solar masses, average velocity 0.20-0.25c
- For DD2 equation of state (maximum mass 2.42 solar masses): dynamical ejecta mass 0.002-0.005 solar masses depending on mass ratio, average velocity 0.16-0.19c
- Early viscosity-driven ejecta mass approximately 0.01 times (alpha_vis/0.02) solar masses for torus mass of approximately 0.2 solar masses
- Viscous time scale for differential rotation approximately 23 milliseconds times (alpha_vis/0.01)^{-1} times (c_s/(c/3))^{-1} times (R_eq/15 km)^2 times (H/10 km)^{-1}
- For SFHo equation of state: remnant black hole mass approximately 0.96 times total mass, black hole spin approximately 0.7
- For DD2 equation of state with equal masses: torus mass approximately 0.2 solar masses

## Context

This work extends numerical-relativity simulations performed by the authors' group since 1999 (Shibata 1999, Shibata and Uryu 2000) and builds on prior studies of dynamical ejecta properties (Sekiguchi 2015, 2016) and viscosity-driven mass ejection (Fernandez and Metzger 2013, 2014, 2015; Just 2015; Siegel and Metzger 2017). The paper enables constraints on the neutron-star equation of state from multimessenger observations, specifically ruling out soft equations of state where the maximum mass is not much larger than 2 solar masses and the stellar radius is smaller than 11 km, while also suggesting that the maximum mass cannot be extremely high based on the absence of a relativistic optical counterpart.
