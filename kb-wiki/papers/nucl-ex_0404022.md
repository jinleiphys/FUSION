# The Coulomb Dissociation of 8B and a Critical Assessment of the Seattle S17(0) Result

> The Coulomb dissociation of 8B, measured with high precision by the GSI group, is in excellent agreement with the astrophysical cross section factor (S17) measured by the Weizmann group with a 7Be target. The GSI and Weizmann data are in good agreement with the Seattle data at high energies, but at low energies we observe a slight systematic (up to 2sigma) deviation, yet the Seattle group repeatedly rejects the CD method. We show that when plotting the slopes, they mis plotted one CD data point and exclude measured slopes that contradict their claim. Indeed the measured slope is essential to elucidate the d-wave correction to S17(0) that could be as large as 15%, and is the last open question that needs to be resolved before S17(0) can be quoted with an accuracy of 5% or better. We show that this goal has not been achieved (in spite of the strong claim of the Seattle group), since currently there is no agreement among experiments as well as among theoretical models on the value of the slope. In addition, currently there is no theoretical framework within which (for example the Seattle) data can be analyzed and S17(0) extrapolated with the claimed high precision of 2.5%. This (last) issue of the slope and the d-wave correction must be resolved (by future measurements) so as to allow quoting S17(0) with an accuracy of 5% or better.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the Seattle group's claimed high-precision value of the astrophysical S-factor \(S_{17}(0)\) (with an uncertainty of 2.5%) has not been achieved, because there is no agreement among experiments or theoretical models on the slope of \(S_{17}\) at energies above 300 keV, and the d-wave correction to \(S_{17}(0)\) (which could be as large as 15%) remains unresolved.

## Method

The author compares the Coulomb dissociation (CD) data of \(^8\)B from the GSI group with direct capture measurements from the Weizmann, Seattle, Bochum, and Orsay groups, and with older data from Filippone, Vaughn, Parker, and Kavanagh. The slopes \(S' = dS/dE\) extracted from a linear fit to \(S_{17} = a(1 + bE)\) over the range 300–1400 keV are compared across experiments and against theoretical predictions from eleven models, including those of Descouvemont and Baye and Descouvemont and Douford.

## Key numbers

- The d-wave correction to \(S_{17}(0)\) could be as large as 15%, as shown by Filippone (1983).
- The Seattle group claims a precision of 2.5% for \(S_{17}(0)\), but the author argues this is not justified.
- The Seattle group claims a precision of 4.5% for the slope \(b\) measured in direct capture, which the author disputes.
- The GSI CD data and Weizmann direct data yield \(S_{17}(0)\) values of 20.6 eV-b and 18.2 eV-b when fitted with normalization factors of 0.9 and 0.77, respectively, using the Descouvemont and Douford and Typel theories.
- The Seattle data require a normalization factor of 0.73 to match the Descouvemont and Baye theory, which changes the predicted slope.
- The Bochum and Orsay data disagree with the Seattle data by three to five sigma.
- The CD yield varies by no more than 50% over the energy range 200–1300 keV, while the direct cross section varies by a factor of 50 over the same range.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing debate over the value of \(S_{17}(0)\) for the \(^7\)Be(p,\(\gamma\))\(^8\)B reaction, which is critical for predicting the \(^8\)B solar neutrino flux. The author contrasts the high-precision CD data from the GSI group (Iwasa 1999, Schumann 2003) and the direct capture data from the Weizmann group with the Seattle group's result (2003), and notes that the Seattle data disagree with the Bochum (Strieder 2001) and Orsay (Hammache 2001) direct measurements. The paper argues that the slope and d-wave correction must be resolved by future measurements, possibly using \(^7\)Be beams at ISOLDE, before \(S_{17}(0)\) can be quoted with an accuracy of 5% or better.
