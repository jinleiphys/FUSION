# QCD at Finite Baryon Density: Nucleon Droplets and Color Superconductivity

> We use a variational procedure to study finite density QCD in an approximation in which the interaction between quarks is modelled by that induced by instantons. We find that uniform states with conventional chiral symmetry breaking have negative pressure with respect to empty space at all but the lowest densities, and are therefore unstable. This is a precisely defined phenomenon which motivates the basic picture of hadrons assumed in the MIT bag model, with nucleons as droplets of chiral symmetry restored phase. At all densities high enough that the chirally symmetric phase fills space, we find that color symmetry is broken by the formation of a <qq> condensate of quark Cooper pairs. A plausible ordering scheme leads to a substantial gap in a Lorentz scalar channel involving quarks of two colors, and a much smaller gap in an axial vector channel involving quarks of the third color.

# QCD at Finite Baryon Density: Nucleon Droplets and Color Superconductivity

## Key claim

Uniform states with conventional chiral symmetry breaking have negative pressure with respect to empty space at all but the lowest densities, making them mechanically unstable; this instability motivates identifying nucleons as droplets of chirally symmetric phase, analogous to the MIT bag model, but the authors note this picture is not obtained for all parameter choices and fails for "unreasonable" parameter ranges where the model has no physical interpretation.

## Method

The authors use a variational procedure with a four-fermion interaction Hamiltonian modeled on the instanton vertex from QCD, with form factors to mimic asymptotic freedom, for two flavors and three colors of massless quarks. They construct trial wave functions that allow for chiral symmetry breaking via particle-antiparticle pairing and for color superconductivity via quark-quark Cooper pairing, then solve the resulting gap equations and compute energy densities and pressures as functions of quark number density. The coupling constant is fixed by requiring the chiral gap parameter Delta = 400 MeV at zero density, and results are checked for robustness across parameter choices.

## Key numbers

- Chiral gap parameter Delta = 400 MeV at zero density, used to fix the coupling; results verified qualitatively unchanged for 300 MeV < Delta < 500 MeV
- For nu=1 and Lambda=0.6 GeV: critical Fermi momentum p_F(n_c) = 0.212 GeV where chiral gap vanishes; stable zero-pressure Fermi momentum p_F(n_0) = 0.268 GeV; vacuum energy density epsilon(0) = -(0.127 GeV)^4
- For nu=1 and Lambda=0.8 GeV: p_F(n_c) = 0.246 GeV; p_F(n_0) = 0.298 GeV; epsilon(0) = -(0.141 GeV)^4
- For nu=1 and Lambda=1.0 GeV: p_F(n_c) = 0.274 GeV; p_F(n_0) = 0.321 GeV; epsilon(0) = -(0.152 GeV)^4
- For nu=0.5 and Lambda=0.6 GeV: p_F(n_c) = 0.262 GeV; p_F(n_0) = 0.310 GeV; epsilon(0) = -(0.147 GeV)^4
- For nu=0.5 and Lambda=0.8 GeV: p_F(n_c) = 0.298 GeV; p_F(n_0) = 0.340 GeV; epsilon(0) = -(0.161 GeV)^4
- For nu=0.5 and Lambda=1.0 GeV: p_F(n_c) = 0.328 GeV; p_F(n_0) = 0.362 GeV; epsilon(0) = -(0.172 GeV)^4
- Color superconducting gap in the Lorentz scalar channel: approximately 50-150 MeV at density n_0, peaking at 100-200 MeV at somewhat higher density
- Color superconducting gap in the axial vector channel (third color): at most a few keV, five orders of magnitude smaller than the scalar channel gap

## Context

This work extends the tradition of modeling low-energy QCD dynamics with instanton-induced interactions, following the instanton liquid approach and the earlier work of Vaks and Larkin (1960s) and Nambu and Jona-Lasinio (1961). The finding that uniform chiral symmetry broken matter has negative pressure and breaks into droplets parallels independent work by Buballa using a Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model with a different interaction. The paper enables further study of the percolation mechanism for chiral symmetry restoration at finite density (contrasting with the finite-temperature transition), and opens investigation of color superconducting phases with gaps spanning five orders of magnitude, with potential implications for neutron star physics and heavy ion collision signatures.
