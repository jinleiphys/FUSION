# Non-exponential decays and nonextensivity

> We demonstrate that non-exponential decays of unstable systems can be understood as yet another example of nonextensivity encountered in many physical systems and as such can be characterized by the nonextensivity parameter q.

# Non-exponential decays and nonextensivity

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that non-exponential decays of unstable systems can be understood as a manifestation of nonextensivity, characterized by the Tsallis parameter q, provided that the decay width γ fluctuates according to a gamma distribution. The key condition is that the parameter 1/τ of the original exponential distribution fluctuates with a relative variance that directly determines q.

## Method
The authors use a random matrix approach to show that the distribution of decay widths γ for a system of N intercoupled states follows a gamma distribution. Starting from a Gaussian ensemble of random Hermitian matrices, they derive the distribution of eigenvector amplitudes C_i, which leads to the Porter-Thomas distribution for a single channel (ν=1) and a gamma distribution for ν independent channels. They then show that averaging an exponential decay law over this gamma-distributed width yields a Tsallis-type q-exponential function, with the nonextensivity parameter given by q = 1 + 2/ν.

## Key numbers
- The relative variance of the decay width fluctuations is given by ⟨(γ − ⟨γ⟩)²⟩/⟨γ⟩² = 2/ν, where ν is the number of independent channels.
- The nonextensivity parameter q is related to the number of channels by q = 1 + 2/ν.
- For a single channel (ν=1), the width distribution is the Porter-Thomas distribution, with relative variance 2.
- The mean squared amplitude of eigenvector components is ⟨C_i²⟩ = 1/N for large N.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous demonstration (Wilk and Wlodarczyk, 2000) that fluctuations of the parameter 1/τ according to a gamma distribution produce power-law distributions characterized by q. It connects the random matrix approach of nuclear physics, specifically the Porter-Thomas distribution for resonance widths (Porter and Thomas, 1956), to Tsallis nonextensive statistics. The result enables a unified description of non-exponential decays in quantum systems, including compound nucleus resonances and chaotic quantum systems, using the single parameter q to quantify deviations from exponential behavior.
