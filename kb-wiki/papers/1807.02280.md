# System stability and truncation schemes to the Dyson-Schwinger Equations

> With decades of years development, although important progresses have been made by the pioneers of this field, providing a sophisticated truncation scheme is still a great challenge up to now if the Dyson-Schwinger Equations(DSEs) of both quark and gluon propagators (or including even more DSEs) remain after truncation. In this work we view the coupled reminiscent DSEs of the gluon and quark propagators after truncation as a system with feedback. Then studying the stability of this equation array gives useful results. Our calculation shows that the sum of the gluon and ghost loops plays the most important role in keeping this system stable and having reasonable solutions. The quark-gluon vertex plays a relative smaller but also important role. Our method also could give constraints and inspirations on fabricating a more sophisticated model of the quark-gluon vertex.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the coupled Dyson-Schwinger Equations (DSEs) for quark and gluon propagators, after truncation, require a small correction factor to maintain system stability; this factor arises naturally from the diagrams omitted in the truncation scheme, specifically from the full quark-gluon vertex and the gluon-plus-ghost loops.

## Method

The author treats the truncated, coupled DSEs of the quark and gluon propagators as a feedback system and studies its stability by introducing a scalar correction factor η(T,Q) into the relation between the gluon propagator and its self-energy. The correction factor is constrained by requiring stable solutions at finite chemical potential, and its origin is traced by analyzing the response of the quark and gluon self-energies to perturbations of the quark propagator, using Feynman diagrams up to order g⁴ and parameters from the Qin-Chang model and lattice QCD.

## Context

This work addresses the long-standing challenge of constructing truncation schemes for the coupled DSEs of quark and gluon propagators, which has hindered progress beyond the gap equation alone. It extends the approach of Fischer (2003, 2013), which used different quark-gluon vertices in the quark self-energy and quark loops, by providing a stability-based rationale for the required suppression. The method offers constraints for modeling the full quark-gluon vertex, complementing guidance from gauge covariance (Ball and Chiu, 1980) and multiplicative renormalisability (Cornwall and Papavassiliou, 1989; Papavassiliou, 1990; Binosi and Papavassiliou, 2009), and could enable more sophisticated truncation schemes for nonperturbative QCD at finite chemical potential.
