# Neutron skin uncertainties of Skyrme energy density functionals

> Background: Neutron-skin thickness is an excellent indicator of isovector properties of atomic nuclei. As such, it correlates strongly with observables in finite nuclei that depend on neutron-to-proton imbalance and the nuclear symmetry energy that characterizes the equation of state of neutron-rich matter. A rich worldwide experimental program involving studies with rare isotopes, parity violating electron scattering, and astronomical observations is devoted to pinning down the isovector sector of nuclear models. Purpose: We assess the theoretical systematic and statistical uncertainties of neutron-skin thickness and relate them to the equation of state of nuclear matter, and in particular to nuclear symmetry energy parameters. Methods: We use the nuclear superfluid Density Functional Theory with several Skyrme energy density functionals and density dependent pairing. To evaluate statistical errors and their budget, we employ the statistical covariance technique. Results: We find that the errors on neutron skin increase with neutron excess. Statistical errors due to uncertain coupling constants of the density functional are found to be larger than systematic errors, the latter not exceeding 0.06 fm in most neutron-rich nuclei across the nuclear landscape. The single major source of uncertainty is the poorly determined slope L of the symmetry energy that parametrizes its density dependence. Conclusions: To provide essential constraints on the symmetry energy of the nuclear energy density functional, next-generation measurements of neutron skins are required to deliver precision better than 0.06 fm.

## Key claim

The systematic error on neutron-skin thickness across the Skyrme energy density functional family does not exceed 0.06 fm in most neutron-rich nuclei, but the statistical error from uncertain coupling constants is larger and grows with neutron excess; the dominant single source of uncertainty is the poorly determined slope L of the symmetry energy.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using six Skyrme energy density functionals (SkM*, SkP, SLy4, SV-min, UNEDF0, and UNEDF1) with density-dependent zero-range pairing, computing proton and neutron root-mean-square radii for even-even nuclei across the mass table up to Z=120. Systematic error was estimated as the root-mean-square spread of predictions across the six functionals, while statistical error was obtained via least-squares covariance analysis of the parameter uncertainties for UNEDF0 and SV-min.

## Key numbers

- Systematic error Δr_skin^syst does not exceed 0.05 fm even for extremely neutron-rich systems (the model spread across six Skyrme EDFs)
- For 208Pb: statistical error Δr_skin^stat = 0.058 fm (UNEDF0) and 0.037 fm (SV-min); systematic error = 0.013 fm; PREX experimental error = 0.18 fm; planned PREX-II precision = 0.06 fm
- For 48Ca: statistical error Δr_skin^stat = 0.035 fm (UNEDF0) and 0.026 fm (SV-min); systematic error = 0.019 fm; planned CREX precision = 0.02 fm
- Slope of symmetry energy L = 45 ± 40 MeV for UNEDF0 and 45 ± 26 MeV for SV-min; symmetry energy a_sym = 30.5 ± 3.1 MeV (UNEDF0) and 30.7 ± 1.9 MeV (SV-min)
- SkP has L = 19.7 MeV; SV-min has L = 44.8 MeV; UNEDF0 has L = 45.1 MeV

## Context

This work extends the global survey of Erl12 on model uncertainties for drip-line positions and global nuclear properties, now focusing specifically on neutron-skin thickness. The results contradict the expectation that systematic and statistical errors would be comparable (as found for drip-lines in Erl12), instead finding statistical errors dominate. The study provides a benchmark for planned experiments such as PREX-II (targeting 0.06 fm precision on 208Pb) and CREX (targeting 0.02 fm on 48Ca), showing that experimental precision better than 0.06 fm is needed to constrain the symmetry energy slope L. The methodology builds on the covariance analysis techniques developed in Klu09, Kor10, Kor12, and Gao13.
