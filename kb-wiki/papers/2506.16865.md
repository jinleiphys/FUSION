# Photon Emission from Nucleon-Nucleon Bremsstrahlung in Fermi-energy Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The emission of direct (hard and thermal) photons from nucleon-nucleon Bremsstrahlung in heavy-ion collisions at Fermi energies is analyzed. We utilize a photon emission rate based on a quantum-field-theoretical model together with nucleon distribution functions extracted from a coarse-graining method of transport model simulations. The latter accounts for off-equilibrium effects during the early stages of nuclear collisions primarily occurring in the beam direction while the transverse-momentum distributions are amenable to a thermal description. With this setup, we quantify the contributions from first-chance nucleon-nucleon collisions and the subsequent transition to a thermal source to the photon energy spectrum measured from Ca-Ca collisions at 35 A$\cdot$MeV bombarding energy. We find that most of the hard photons are produced in the initial stages of heavy-ion collisions from primordial collisions where nucleons move with the initial collective nuclear motion while the emission from the later stages plays a sub-dominant role. We compare our calculations to experimental measurements of a differential photon-energy spectrum from a collision system of similar size and beam energy, thereby including acceptance cuts as applied in the detectors.

## Key claim

The authors find that most hard photons in Fermi-energy heavy-ion collisions are produced during the initial stages from primordial nucleon-nucleon collisions where nucleons retain their initial collective nuclear motion, while emission from later, more thermalized stages plays a sub-dominant role; this conclusion depends on using a coarse-graining method that accounts for off-equilibrium effects in the beam direction while transverse-momentum distributions remain amenable to a thermal description.

## Method

The authors employed a coarse-graining method applied to Constrained Molecular Dynamics (CoMD) transport simulations of central Ca-Ca collisions at 35 A·MeV, extracting local nucleon distribution functions from a grid of cells with volumes of (2 fm)^3. They combined these distribution functions with a quantum-field-theoretical photon emission rate based on the soft-photon approximation, using parameterized experimental nucleon-nucleon cross sections with energy-dependent hard-sphere scattering radii, and folded the rates over the space-time evolution to compute photon energy spectra including experimental acceptance cuts.

## Key numbers

- Bombarding energy: 35 A·MeV for Ca-Ca collisions (central collisions of ^40Ca+^40Ca)
- Cell volume for coarse-graining grid: (2 fm)^3, with a grid of 8×8×8 cells
- Hard-sphere scattering radius for np cross section parameterized in five energy ranges from 0 to above 116 MeV, with values ranging from 9.04065 T_CM^(-0.354978) fm at low energies to 2.32566 T_CM^(-0.155409) fm at high energies
- Hard-sphere scattering radius for pp cross section parameterized as 7.1873 T_CM^(-0.5267) fm for T_CM < 52 MeV and 0.999341 T_CM^(-0.0286) fm for T_CM ≥ 52 MeV
- pp cross section is approximately 60-70% of the pn cross section for T_CM < 100 MeV
- Comparison made to experimental data from ^36Ar+^98Mo collisions at 37 A·MeV

## Context

This work extends earlier coarse-graining approaches that were applied to ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions (Huovinen et al., 2002) and relativistic energies (Galatyuk et al., 2015; Endres et al., 2015) to the Fermi-energy regime for the first time. It contrasts with earlier calculations using constant hard-sphere scattering radii of 2.1 fm (Bauer et al., 1986) or √3 fm (Cassing et al., 1986), and with transport model implementations using the Bauer cross section in IQMD (Wang et al., 2020), EQMD (Shi et al., 2021), and BUU (Deng et al., 2016) that underestimated high-energy photon yields above 60 MeV. The approach enables a seamless transition from anisotropic initial conditions to nearly thermalized conditions, and the use of Fermi distribution functions accounts for Pauli blocking essential at Fermi energies.
