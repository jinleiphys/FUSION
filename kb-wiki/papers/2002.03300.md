# A New Approach to Mass and Radius of Neutron Stars with Supernova Neutrinos

> Neutron stars are formed in core-collapse supernova explosions, where a large number of neutrinos are emitted. In this paper, supernova neutrino light curves are computed for the cooling phase of protoneutron stars, which lasts a few minutes. In the numerical simulations, 90 models of the phenomenological equation of state with different incompressibilities, symmetry energies, and nucleon effective masses are employed for a comprehensive study. It is found that the cooling timescale is longer for a model with a larger neutron star mass and a smaller neutron star radius. Furthermore, a theoretical expression of the cooling timescale is presented as a function of the mass and radius and it is found to describe the numerical results faithfully. These findings suggest that diagnosing the mass and radius of a newly formed neutron star using its neutrino signal is possible.

# A New Approach to Mass and Radius of Neutron Stars with Supernova Neutrinos

## Key claim
The cooling timescale of a protoneutron star (PNS), measurable from the supernova neutrino signal, depends systematically on both the mass and radius of the final cold neutron star, and can be described by a theoretical formula that fits numerical results faithfully. The authors suggest that combining this timescale with the total neutrino emission energy (binding energy) may allow simultaneous diagnosis of neutron star mass and radius, but note that uncertainties from nucleon effective mass, neutrino opacities, and convection must still be resolved.

## Method
The authors performed quasi-static PNS cooling simulations by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations and multigroup flux-limited diffusion neutrino transport for 90 phenomenological equation-of-state (EOS) models, varying incompressibility (K0 = 220, 245, 270 MeV), symmetry energy parameters (10 combinations of S0, L, S00), and nucleon effective mass (u = 1.0, 0.75, 0.5). Initial conditions were taken from spherically symmetric core-collapse simulations of 15 and 40 solar mass progenitors, with four baryon mass configurations (1.47, 1.62, 1.62, and 1.78 solar masses). The cooling timescale was defined as the maximum e-folding time of the electron antineutrino luminosity.

## Key numbers
- 90 models of the finite-temperature EOS were employed, combining 30 zero-temperature EOS models (3 incompressibilities × 10 symmetry energy sets) with 3 effective mass values.
- Cold neutron star radii range from 11 km to 13 km for typical masses across the EOS models.
- Gravitational masses are approximately 1.33, 1.44, and 1.57 solar masses for baryon masses of 1.47, 1.62, and 1.78 solar masses, respectively.
- The theoretical expression for cooling timescale uses τ* = 37.0 s, 35.2 s, and 33.7 s for effective masses u = 1, 0.75, and 0.5, respectively.
- For the LS220 EOS with baryon mass 1.47 solar masses, the cooling timescale is τ_cool = 21.8 s, and the binding energy is Eb = 0.136 solar masses.
- The symmetry energy at 2n0 is constrained to 46.9 ± 10.1 MeV from GW170817 data.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous systematic study (Nakazato and Suzuki, 2019) by using 90 EOS models instead of a smaller set, and by providing an analytic formula for the cooling timescale as a function of mass and radius. It builds on decades of PNS cooling simulations (Burrows and Lattimer, 1986; Suzuki, 1994; Pons et al., 1999; Roberts et al., 2012) and recent constraints from X-ray bursts (Steiner et al., 2010; Ozel et al., 2010), gravitational wave observations (LIGO/Virgo, 2018), and NICER measurements (2019, 2021). The results enable a new method to infer neutron star mass and radius from future Galactic supernova neutrino detection, complementing existing approaches that use only the total neutrino event number (Sato and Suzuki, 1987; Lattimer and Yahil, 1989).
