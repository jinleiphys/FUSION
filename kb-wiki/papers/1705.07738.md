# Developing diagnostic tools for low-burnup reactor samples

> We test common fluence diagnostics in the regime of very low burnup natural uranium reactor samples. The fluence diagnostics considered are the uranium isotopics ratios $^{235}$U/$^{238}$U and $^{236}$U/$^{235}$U, for which we find simple analytic formulas agree well with full reactor simulation predictions. Both ratios agree reasonably well with one another for fluences in the mid $10^{19}\,\mathrm{n/cm^2}$ range. However, below about $10^{19}\,\mathrm{n/cm^2}$ the concentrations of $^{236}$U are found to be sufficiently low that the measured $^{236}$U/$^{235}$U ratios become unreliable. We also derive and test diagnostics for determining sample cooling times in situations where very low burnup and very long cooling times render many standard diagnostics, such as the $^{241}$Am/$^{241}$Pu ratio, impractical. We find that using several fragment ratios are necessary to detect the presence of systematic errors, such as fractionation.

## Key claim

Simple analytic formulas for the uranium isotopic ratios ²³⁵U/²³⁸U and ²³⁶U/²³⁵U agree well with full reactor simulations for fluence diagnostics in low-burnup natural uranium samples, but the ²³⁶U/²³⁵U ratio becomes unreliable below about 10¹⁹ n/cm² because ²³⁶U concentrations are too low to measure accurately. For cooling time diagnostics, using several fission fragment ratios is necessary to detect systematic errors such as fractionation, since standard diagnostics like ²⁴¹Am/²⁴¹Pu are impractical at very low burnup and very long cooling times.

## Method

The authors tested fluence diagnostics using the uranium isotopic ratios ²³⁵U/²³⁸U and ²³⁶U/²³⁵U, inverting simple analytic depletion equations to derive fluence from measured ratios. For cooling time diagnostics, they identified four "linear" fission fragments (⁸⁵Kr, ¹²⁵Sb, ¹³⁷Cs, ¹⁵⁵Eu) whose production is linear in fluence, derived analytic expressions for their activities, and constructed cooling time diagnostics from ratios of these fragments. They verified the diagnostics against detailed reactor simulations using CINDER08 cross-sections over 70 sample cases spanning ranges of thermal flux, fast flux, cooling time, irradiation time, and shutdown history, then applied the diagnostics to ten archived low-burnup natural uranium reactor samples analyzed by TIMS and gamma spectrometry.

## Key numbers

- The ²³⁵U/²³⁸U fluence diagnostic returned input fluence within ~0.5% for both uranium ratios when using the maximum expected fast flux.
- The ²³⁶U/²³⁵U ratio becomes unreliable below about 10¹⁹ n/cm² due to low ²³⁶U concentrations.
- Theoretical errors for fluence diagnostics: ±0.54% for ²³⁵U/²³⁸U and ±0.24% for ²³⁶U/²³⁵U (overall quadrature sum).
- Theoretical errors for cooling time diagnostics: ±4.02% for ¹³⁷Cs/¹⁵⁵Eu, ±0.63% for ¹³⁷Cs/¹²⁵Sb, and ±3.40% for ¹⁵⁵Eu/¹²⁵Sb (overall quadrature sum).
- Average discrepancy between the two fluence diagnostics in experimental samples was 19.9% for fluences above 10¹⁹ n/cm².
- Average discrepancy between ¹⁵⁵Eu-based cooling time diagnostics and the ¹³⁷Cs/¹²⁵Sb ratio was ~37%.
- The simulation used a thermal flux of 8.5×10¹³ n/cm²/sec with 4 cycles of [5,19] hr ON/OFF periods.
- ¹⁵⁵Eu deviates from the linear approximation by ~1-3% due to its large neutron-capture cross-section.
- Leakage of volatile fission fragments such as ⁸⁵Kr can occur at ≥13% level in PWR fuels.

## Context

This work extends fluence diagnostic methods using uranium isotopic ratios (Boulyga 2002, Boulyga 2006) into the very low burnup regime where standard diagnostics like ²⁴¹Pu/²⁴¹Am (Mayer 2012) and ¹³⁴Cs/¹³⁷Cs (Navarro 2011) become impractical. The new cooling time diagnostics using linear fission fragments enable detection of systematic errors such as fractionation in low-burnup samples, which is critical for nuclear forensics and non-proliferation verification. The approach enables simultaneous determination of fluence and cooling time from a single sample, with the fluence determination used to verify that burnup is low enough to validate the simple analytic expressions for cooling time.
