# B(E2) anomaly and triaxial deformation in the interacting boson model

> The influences of triaxial dynamics on the anomalous $E2$ transitional behaviors in the interacting boson model (IBM) have been comprehensively examined without symmetry restrictions. Specifically, different triaxial schemes in the IBM have been analyzed using a general procedure for deriving triaxial rotor modes based on two distinct approaches. The results indicate that the $B(E2)$ anomaly feature, characterized by $B_{4/2}=B(E2;4_1^+\rightarrow2_1^+)/B(E2;2_1^+\rightarrow0_1^+)<1.0$, can occur in all triaxial schemes due to strong band-mixing effects. This finding is further tested in describing the available data for $^{172}$Pt,~$^{168,170}$Os and $^{166}$W using the model constructed upon the extended consistent-$Q$ formula, providing a simple theoretical explanation of the depressed $B_{4/2}$ values observed in the experiments.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the anomalous B(E2) feature (B4/2 < 1.0) can occur in all triaxial schemes within the interacting boson model due to strong band-mixing effects, but this result depends on the specific construction of the dynamic Hamiltonian and is not guaranteed for every IBM Hamiltonian, as previous work (Wang 2023) showed that the O(6) limit Hamiltonian fails to produce the anomaly.

## Method

The authors constructed triaxial rotor modes in the interacting boson model by separating the Hamiltonian into a static part that yields a triaxial potential at the mean-field level and a dynamic part that generates band-mixing effects, formulated as a scalar polynomial in angular momentum and quadrupole operators. They derived two approaches for evaluating the rotor mode parameters: one using the classical large-N limit of the IBM with coherent states to determine intrinsic deformation, and another using expectation values of Casimir operators from the ground state wave function. Three specific triaxial schemes were examined: an O(6) scalar polynomial with sixth-order terms, a cubic interaction term, and the extended consistent-Q formula, along with dynamic triaxiality from the standard consistent-Q Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

- B4/2 is defined as B(E2;4_1+ -> 2_1+)/B(E2;2_1+ -> 0_1+) and the anomaly corresponds to B4/2 < 1.0
- The asymmetry parameter for the rotor model is defined as k-bar = (2*Gamma_1 - Gamma_3 - Gamma_2)/(Gamma_3 - Gamma_2) with range -1 <= k-bar <= 1
- For the rotor model, symmetric tops with k-bar = +/-1 constantly produce B4/2 > 1.0 for gamma_e from 0 to 60 degrees
- The O(6) scalar polynomial potential includes terms up to sixth order in Q, with the sixth-order term proportional to cos^2(3*gamma)
- The cubic interaction potential includes a term proportional to beta^6[cos^2(3*gamma) - 1]/[7(1+beta^2)^3]
- The consistent-Q Hamiltonian with N=9 yields gamma_eff values ranging from 0 to 60 degrees along the SU(3)-O(6)-anti-SU(3) transition, with the O(6) point (chi=0) at gamma_eff = 30 degrees

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the B(E2) anomaly in the IBM framework, specifically building on the SU(3) image of the triaxial rotor developed by Zhang (2014, 2022) which attributed the anomaly to finite-N triaxial rotor modes. It addresses a question raised by Wang (2023) regarding whether the anomaly can manifest in IBM scenarios different from the SU(3) scheme. The paper also connects to earlier work on triaxial deformation in the IBM by Van Isacker and Chen (1981) and Heyde (1984), and to the extended consistent-Q formula proposed by Fortunato (2011). The findings enable systematic investigation of triaxial effects across different IBM schemes and provide a theoretical explanation for depressed B4/2 values observed experimentally in neutron-deficient nuclei near N=90.
