# Two-body currents at finite momentum transfer and applications to M1 transitions

> We explore the impact of two-body currents (2BCs) at finite momentum transfer with a focus on magnetic dipole properties in $^{48}$Ca and $^{48}$Ti. To this end, we derive a multipole decomposition of 2BCs to fully include the momentum-transfer dependence in $\mathit{ab\,initio}$ calculations. As application, we investigate the effects of 2BCs on the strong M1 transition at 10.23$\,$MeV in $^{48}$Ca using the valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group (VS-IMSRG) with a set of non-implausible interactions as well as the 1.8/2.0 (EM) interaction. Experiments, such as $(e,e')$ and $(γ,n)$, disagree on the magnetic dipole strength $B$(M1) for this transition. Our VS-IMSRG results favor larger $B$(M1) values similar to recent coupled-cluster calculations. However, for this transition there are larger cancellations between the leading pion-in-flight and seagull 2BCs, so that future calculations including higher-order 2BCs are important. For validation of our results, we investigate additional observables in $^{48}$Ca as well as M1 transitions in $^{48}$Ti. For these, our results agree with experiment. Finally, our results show that for medium-mass nuclei 2BC contributions to M1 and Gamow-Teller transitions are, as expected, very different. Therefore, using similar quenching factors for both in phenomenological calculations is not supported from first principles.

# Two-body currents at finite momentum transfer and applications to M1 transitions

## Key claim
Two-body currents (2BCs) have a small effect on the M1 transition at 10.23 MeV in ⁴⁸Ca, enhancing the B(M1) by less than 4%, but with strong cancellations between the leading pion-in-flight and seagull contributions that may be accidental, meaning higher-order 2BCs are needed for a definitive prediction. The VS-IMSRG results favor larger B(M1) values similar to recent coupled-cluster calculations, in agreement with the (γ,n) experiment but above the (e,e') measurement.

## Method
The authors derived a multipole decomposition of leading-order two-body electromagnetic currents in momentum space, enabling ab initio calculations at finite momentum transfer for medium-mass nuclei. They implemented this decomposition in the NuHamil code and performed valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group (VS-IMSRG) calculations for M1 transitions in ⁴⁸Ca and ⁴⁸Ti using a set of 34 non-implausible chiral EFT interactions plus the 1.8/2.0 (EM) interaction, computing transition form factors and B(M1) strengths with and without 2BCs.

## Key numbers
- The 2BC enhancement of B(M1) in ⁴⁸Ca at 10.23 MeV does not exceed 4% for all interactions studied.
- For the 1.8/2.0 (EM) interaction, axial-vector 2BCs reduce the B(GT) in ⁴⁸Ca by more than 40%, while vector 2BCs enhance the B(M1) by less than 2%.
- For non-implausible interactions, the reduction in B(GT) from 2BCs is smaller than 25%, and in some cases the GT strength increases by up to approximately 11%.
- In ⁴⁸Ti, 2BCs enhance B(M1) by between 30% and 65% for the transition to the 3.742 MeV state, and between 6% and 32% for the 7.22 MeV state.
- The correlation coefficient between B(M1) and B(GT) from non-implausible interactions without 2BCs is r = 0.61; with 2BCs it is r = 0.62.
- Up to Q less than approximately 100 MeV, the combined 2BC contribution to the transition form factor in ⁴⁸Ca is about two orders of magnitude smaller than the leading-order 1BC contribution, increasing the form factor by up to 3%.

## Context
This work extends the study of two-body currents in electromagnetic transitions from light nuclei to medium-mass nuclei (⁴⁸Ca and ⁴⁸Ti), building on prior chiral EFT developments by Krebs (2019, 2020) and the multipole decomposition framework of Walecka (1995). The results contradict the phenomenological practice of using similar quenching factors for M1 and Gamow-Teller transitions, as the vector and axial-vector 2BCs behave very differently. The findings are consistent with coupled-cluster calculations by Acharya (2024) for the ⁴⁸Ca M1 transition and with the β-decay quenching studies by Gysbers (2019). The multipole decomposition enables future applications to neutrinoless double-beta decay, muon capture, and neutrino-nucleus scattering in medium-mass and heavy nuclei.
