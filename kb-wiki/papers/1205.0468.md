# Inverse scattering: applications to nuclear physics

> In what follows we first set the context for inverse scattering in nuclear physics with a brief account of inverse problems in general. We then turn to inverse scattering which involves the S-matrix, which connects the interaction potential between two scattering particles with the measured scattering cross section. The term `inverse' is a reference to the fact that instead of determining the scattering S-matrix from the interaction potential between the scattering particles, we do the inverse. That is to say, we calculate the interaction potential from the S-matrix. This review explains how this can now be done reliably, but the emphasis will be upon reasons why one should wish to do this, with an account of some of the ways this can lead to understanding concerning nuclear interactions.

## Key claim

This review argues that inverse scattering methods, particularly the iterative-perturbative (IP) algorithm, can reliably determine local interaction potentials from S-matrix elements in nuclear physics, but the emphasis is on demonstrating why one would want to do this rather than on the formal theory. The author notes that while nuclear inverse problems are formally under-determined and subject to ambiguities, practical implementations with prior information can overcome these limitations in many cases.

## Method

The paper reviews multiple approaches to fixed-energy and fixed-angular-momentum inversion, with detailed treatment of the iterative-perturbative (IP) method that exploits the approximately linear response of S-matrix elements to changes in the scattering potential. The IP method iteratively adjusts a starting reference potential by solving a linearized system using singular value decomposition, and has been extended to handle spin-1/2 projectiles, mixed-case inversion across multiple energies, energy-dependent inversion, parity-dependent potentials, and spin-1 projectiles yielding tensor interactions. The review also covers Newton-Sabatier inversion, semiclassical WKB methods, and the Lipperheide-Fiedeldey method.

## Key numbers

- The IP method minimizes the sum σ² = Σ_l |S_l^tar - S_l^inv|² over N_l partial waves, where N_b ≤ N_l basis functions are used
- For spin-1/2 scattering, the potential takes the form V_cen(r) + iW_cen(r) + 2(l·s)(V_so(r) + iW_so(r))
- The linear response relation δS_l = (i m/ℏ²k) ∫₀^∞ ψ²_l(r) ΔV(r) dr forms the basis of the IP method
- The S-matrix is defined through asymptotic forms: u_l(r) → I_l(r) - S_l O_l(r) for spinless particles, and u_{lj}(r) → I_l(r) - S_{lj} O_l(r) for spin-1/2 projectiles

## Context

This review, commissioned for Scholarpedia, extends the earlier review by Kukulin and Mackintosh (2004) on inverse scattering applications in nuclear physics, and references the comprehensive formal treatment by Chadan and Sabatier (1989). The work enables the extraction of dynamic polarization potentials from coupled-channel calculations, the determination of local equivalents of non-local potentials, and the systematic study of parity-dependent and energy-dependent nuclear interactions. The paper identifies open questions including the discrimination between genuine wavy features in potentials and spurious oscillations arising from under-determination, and the extension of inversion methods to more complex coupled-channel cases.
