# More efficient formulas for efficiency correction of cumulants and effect of using averaged efficiency

> We derive formulas for the efficiency correction of cumulants with many efficiency bins. The derivation of the formulas is simpler than the previously suggested method, but the numerical cost is drastically reduced from the naive method. From analytical and numerical analyses in simple toy models, we show that the use of the averaged efficiency in the efficiency correction can lead to wrong corrected values, which have larger deviation for higher order cumulants. These analyses show the importance of carrying out the efficiency correction without taking the average.

## Key claim

The paper derives simpler and more numerically efficient formulas for efficiency correction of cumulants with many efficiency bins, and demonstrates that using a single averaged efficiency across non-uniform acceptance regions can lead to large deviations in corrected cumulant values, especially for higher-order cumulants, and should therefore be avoided.

## Method

The authors derive relations between cumulants and factorial cumulants for both single-variable and multivariate cases, then connect the factorial cumulants of the true distribution to those of the observed distribution using the binomial model with independent efficiencies per bin. The efficiency correction formulas up to sixth order are obtained by converting true cumulants to factorial cumulants, applying the efficiency scaling to factorial cumulants, and converting back to cumulants of the observed distribution. Analytical calculations and numerical toy model simulations are performed to study the effect of using averaged efficiency versus bin-by-bin efficiency correction.

## Key numbers

- The numerical cost of the naive method grows proportional to \(M^m\), where \(M\) is the number of efficiency bins and \(m\) is the order of the cumulant, making it impractical for large \(M\) and high \(m\)
- The proposed method reduces the numerical cost drastically compared to the naive method, with the number of cumulants not depending on the number of efficiency bins \(M\)
- Explicit formulas for efficiency correction are provided up to sixth order, e.g., \(\langle N^2 \rangle_c = \frac{1}{p^2} \langle n^2 \rangle_c + \left(\frac{1}{p} - \frac{1}{p^2}\right) \langle n \rangle_c\) for the single-bin case
- For the multivariate case, the sixth-order correction formula (Eq. mk_6) contains terms such as \(\langle q_{(6,1)} \rangle_c - 31\langle q_{(6,2)} \rangle_c + 180\langle q_{(6,3)} \rangle_c - 390\langle q_{(6,4)} \rangle_c + 360\langle q_{(6,5)} \rangle_c - 120\langle q_{(6,6)} \rangle_c\)

## Context

This work extends the efficiency correction methods for cumulants previously developed by Kitazawa (2015) and others, where the earlier method based on cumulant expansion had complicated derivations for higher orders and was difficult to apply to sixth order. The paper also addresses the practical problem of non-uniform detector acceptance, showing that the common practice of using a single averaged efficiency (as might be done when bin-by-bin correction is too costly) can produce incorrect results. This enables more reliable higher-order cumulant measurements in heavy-ion collision experiments such as the RHIC Beam Energy Scan Phase II program, which aims to measure sixth-order cumulants to search for QCD critical point signals.
