# Dissecting the Cygnus Region with TeV Gamma Rays and Neutrinos

> Recent Milagro observations of the Cygnus region have revealed both diffuse TeV gamma-ray emission and a bright and extended TeV source, MGRO J2019+37, which seems to lack an obvious counterpart at other wavelengths. Additional study of this curious object also promises to provide important clues concerning one of the Milky Way's most active environments. We point out some of the principal facts involved by following three modes of attack. First, to gain insight into this mysterious source, we consider its relation to known objects in both the Cygnus region and the rest of the Galaxy. Second, we find that a simple hadronic model can easily accommodate Milagro's flux measurement (which is at a single energy), as well as other existing observations spanning nearly seven orders of magnitude in gamma-ray energy. Third, since a hadronic gamma-ray spectrum necessitates an accompanying TeV neutrino flux, we show that IceCube observations may provide the first direct evidence of a Galactic cosmic-ray accelerator.

# Dissecting the Cygnus Region with TeV Gamma Rays and Neutrinos

## Key claim

The unidentified TeV source MGRO J2019+37, discovered by Milagro at 12 TeV, can be explained by a simple hadronic model that fits existing observations across nearly seven orders of magnitude in gamma-ray energy, but only if the source is assumed to be Galactic and if the EGRET source 3EG J2021+3716 is associated with it. The accompanying TeV neutrino flux predicted by this hadronic model should be detectable by IceCube within several years, providing the first direct evidence of a Galactic cosmic-ray accelerator.

## Method

The authors constructed a multi-wavelength gamma-ray spectrum for MGRO J2019+37 by combining the Milagro flux measurement at 12 TeV with EGRET data from the nearby unidentified source 3EG J2021+3716 at MeV-GeV energies, an upper limit from Whipple at 0.3 TeV, and an inferred upper limit from CASA-MIA at 115 TeV. They then fit hadronic models using the Kelner, Aharonian and Bugayov (2006) parametrization for proton-proton scattering, assuming source proton spectra of $E_p^{-2.35}$ and $E_p^{-2}$ with exponential cutoffs, and normalized the models to the Milagro flux. From these gamma-ray spectra, they derived the expected muon neutrino flux and calculated the event rate in IceCube for muons above 1 TeV.

## Key numbers

- Milagro flux for MGRO J2019+37: $E^2 d\Phi/dE = (3.49 \pm 0.47_{\rm stat} \pm 1.05_{\rm sys}) \times 10^{-12}$ TeV cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$ at a median energy of 12 TeV
- Source angular width from Milagro: 2D Gaussian of width $\theta = 0.32^\circ$, implying a physical radius $r \simeq 5 (\theta/0.3^\circ) (\mathcal{D}/1 {\rm kpc})$ pc
- Inferred CASA-MIA upper limit for MGRO J2019+37 region: $E^2 d\Phi/dE = 10^{-12}$ TeV cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$ at 115 TeV (derived from the Cygnus X-3 integral flux limit $\Phi(E>115 {\rm TeV}) < 6.3 \times 10^{-15}$ photons cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$)
- Required cosmic-ray proton energy for the $E_p^{-2.35}$ model: $\mathcal{E}_p \approx 5 \times 10^{50} (1 {\rm cm}^{-3}/n_H) (\mathcal{D}/1 {\rm kpc})^2$ erg
- Source significance in Milagro: $\sim 11 \sigma$
- Angular separation between MGRO J2019+37 and TeV J2032+4130: $\sim 5^\circ$

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the Cygnus region following the Milagro discovery by Abdo (2006) of diffuse TeV emission and the new source MGRO J2019+37, which lacks obvious multi-wavelength counterparts. The authors build on earlier studies of the region by COS B (Hermsen 1977), EGRET (Hunter 1997; Hartman 1999), and HEGRA (Aharonian 2002), which identified TeV J2032+4130. The hadronic modeling approach follows the framework of Kelner, Aharonian and Bugayov (2006) for gamma-ray production from proton-proton scattering, and the neutrino predictions are based on the method of Kistler (2006). The results enable targeted searches by IceCube, VERITAS, MAGIC, and GLAST to determine whether MGRO J2019+37 is a hadronic cosmic-ray accelerator, and they suggest that the Cygnus region may host the first identified Galactic source of high-energy neutrinos.
