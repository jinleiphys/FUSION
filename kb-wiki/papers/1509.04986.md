# Thermal shape fluctuation model study of the giant dipole resonance in $^{152}$Gd

> We have studied the giant dipole resonance (GDR) in the hot and rotating nucleus $^{152}$Gd within the framework of thermal shape fluctuation model (TSFM) built on the microscopic-macroscopic calculations of the free energies with a macroscopic approach for the GDR. Our results for GDR cross sections are in good agreement with the experimental values except for a component peaking around 17 MeV where the data has large uncertainties. Such a component is beyond our description which properly takes care of the splitting of GDR components due to the deformation and Coriolis effects. Around this 17 MeV lies the half maximum in experimental cross sections, and hence the extracted GDR widths and deformations (estimated from these widths) turn out to be overestimated and less reliable. Reproducing these widths with empirical formulae could conceal the information contained in the cross sections. Fully microscopic GDR calculations and a more careful look at the data could be useful to understand the GDR component around 17 MeV. We also discuss the occurrence of $γ$-softness in the free energy surfaces of $^{152}$Gd and its role on GDR.

# Thermal shape fluctuation model study of the giant dipole resonance in $^{152}$Gd

## Key claim

The TSFM calculations reproduce the experimental GDR cross sections for $^{152}$Gd reasonably well, except for a component peaking around 17 MeV where the data have large uncertainties; this component cannot be explained by the macroscopic approach that accounts for deformation and Coriolis splitting, and the extracted GDR widths are therefore overestimated and less reliable.

## Method

The authors employed the thermal shape fluctuation model (TSFM) built on microscopic-macroscopic Nilsson-Strutinsky calculations of free energies, with a macroscopic anisotropic harmonic oscillator model for the GDR including a separable dipole-dipole interaction. GDR cross sections were averaged over quadrupole deformation parameters $\beta$ and $\gamma$ using Boltzmann weights derived from the free energy surfaces, and the GDR full width at half maximum was extracted from the averaged cross sections. Calculations were performed for two beam energies (149 MeV and 185 MeV) and compared with experimental data from the literature.

## Key numbers

- At beam energy $E\sim149$ MeV, experimental GDR widths range from $8.5\pm0.3$ MeV (at $T=1.59$ MeV, $I=27.0\hbar$) to $10.1\pm0.5$ MeV (at $T=1.34$ MeV, $I=55.1\hbar$); TSFM with $\eta=2.3$ gives widths from 7.4 to 8.7 MeV, while $\eta=3.35$ gives widths from 8.7 to 9.8 MeV.
- At beam energy $E\sim185$ MeV, experimental GDR widths range from $9.5\pm0.5$ MeV (at $T=1.91$ MeV, $I=13.3\hbar$) to $11.7\pm0.8$ MeV (at $T=1.64$ MeV, $I=56.5\hbar$); TSFM with $\eta=2.3$ gives widths from 7.4 to 9.1 MeV, while $\eta=3.35$ gives widths from 8.7 to 10.4 MeV.
- The GDR component that cannot be explained peaks around 17 MeV, where the half maximum of experimental cross sections lies.
- The phenomenological scaling formula (PSF) uses a ground state GDR width $\Gamma_0=3.8$ MeV, while the critical temperature formula (CTF) uses $\Gamma_0=5.7$ MeV to reproduce the measured widths.

## Context

This work extends earlier TSFM studies of hot and rotating nuclei by the same group (Arumugam et al., 2005-2014) and addresses a discrepancy noted by Dracoulis et al. (2010) where the phenomenological scaling formula (PSF) of Kusnezov et al. (1998) could not explain the measured GDR widths in $^{152}$Gd with a single value of the reduced width parameter. The results contradict the interpretation of Pandit et al. (2014) that the critical temperature formula (CTF) successfully explains the enhanced widths, arguing instead that the experimental widths are overestimated due to uncertainties in the cross section data around 17 MeV. The study enables future fully microscopic GDR calculations and a re-examination of the experimental data to clarify the origin of the high-energy shoulder.
