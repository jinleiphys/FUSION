# Body Fixed Frame, Rigid Gauge Rotations and Large N Random Fields in QCD

> The "body fixed frame" with respect to local gauge transformations is introduced. Rigid gauge "rotations" in QCD and their \Sch equation are studied for static and dynamic quarks. Possible choices of the rigid gauge field configuration corresponding to a nonvanishing static colormagnetic field in the "body fixed" frame are discussed. A gauge invariant variational equation is derived in this frame. For large number N of colors the rigid gauge field configuration is regarded as random with maximally random probability distribution under constraints on macroscopic--like quantities. For the uniform magnetic field the joint probability distribution of the field components is determined by maximizing the appropriate entropy under the area law constraint for the Wilson loop. In the quark sector the gauge invariance requires the rigid gauge field configuration to appear not only as a background but also as inducing an instantaneous quark-quark interaction. Both are random in the large N limit.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a "body fixed frame" formalism for local gauge transformations in QCD, arguing that rigid gauge rotations can be studied analogously to rotational motion in molecular and nuclear physics, with the key hedge being that the rigid gauge field configuration should be regarded as random with a maximally random probability distribution in the large N limit, rather than as a fixed classical configuration.

## Method

The author defines rigid gauge rotations via the ansatz \(A^i(x,t) = U(x,t)a^i(x)U^+(x,t) + (i/g)U(x,t)\partial^i U^+(x,t)\), where \(a^i(x)\) are time-independent fixed fields. The kinetic energy of these rotations is expressed in terms of a moment of inertia operator, and the Hamiltonian for the rigid gauge rotor is derived. For large N, the rigid gauge field configuration is treated as random, with its joint probability distribution determined by maximizing entropy under constraints on macroscopic-like quantities such as the area law for Wilson loops.

## Key numbers

- The moment of inertia operator is defined as \(I\omega = -(1/4g^2)[d^i,[d^i,\omega]]\), where \(d^i = \partial^i - iga^i(x)\).
- The Hamiltonian of the rigid gauge rotor is \(H_{rot}^A = (1/16g^2)\int d^3x d^3y \, \text{Tr} \, \hat{G}(x) I^{-1}(x,y) \hat{G}(y)\).
- For a static quark-antiquark pair, the Schrödinger equation reduces to an \(N^2 \times N^2\) matrix equation: \(Q_{\alpha\gamma}(x_0)c_{\gamma\beta} + Q^*_{\beta\mu}(y_0)c_{\alpha\mu} - P_{\alpha\beta,\gamma\mu}(x_0,y_0)c_{\gamma\mu} = E c_{\alpha\beta}\).
- For zero colormagnetic field, the propagator is a simple Coulomb; for a uniform field diagonal in color, the propagator behaves asymptotically as a decaying Gaussian.

## Context

This work extends the analogy between gauge rotations and rotational motion in molecular and nuclear physics, building on earlier studies by Lee (1980s), Jaciw (1980s), Simon (1980s), and Dosch (1980s). It connects to the large N master field concept from Witten (1979) and to matrix model results from Migdal (1983), Makeenko (1983), and others. The formalism enables a statistical treatment of the rigid gauge field in the large N limit, with potential applications to understanding confinement through the dual Meissner effect picture of Mandelstam (1976) and to the relationship between confinement and localization in the quark sector.
