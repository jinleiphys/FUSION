# Reply to Comment on "Bare quark matter surfaces of strange stars and $e^+e^-$ emission"

> I reply to A. Mitra's comment on my PRL paper "Bare quark matter surfaces of strange stars and $e^+e^-$ emission". I show that his points of criticism are not correct.

# Reply to Comment on "Bare quark matter surfaces of strange stars and \(e^+e^-\) emission"

## Key claim

The Coulomb barrier at the quark surface of a hot strange star cannot be quenched for a long time, contrary to Mitra's claim, because electrons bound to quark matter by electromagnetic interaction will restore the barrier on a timescale of \(\sim 3\times 10^{-21}\) s even if it is momentarily quenched. However, the thermal energy stored in electrons in the surface layer is only \(\sim 10^{32}\) ergs even at \(10^{11}\) K, so the pair emission cannot be sustained for long without heat transport from the interior.

## Method

The author argues against Mitra's claim of electric field quenching by analyzing the electron distribution in quark matter, where the Fermi energy (\(\varepsilon_{\rm F} \gg mc^2\)) means electrons move at near light speed and can rapidly restore the Coulomb barrier. The thermal energy available for conversion into \(e^+e^-\) pairs is calculated from the electron density, surface area, and temperature in the surface layer. The possibility of convective heat transport from the stellar interior is evaluated using the Rayleigh number criterion, with estimates of thermometric conductivity, kinematic viscosity, and thermal expansion coefficient for quark matter.

## Key numbers

- Electric field at the quark surface: \(E \gg E_{\rm cr} \simeq 1.3 \times 10^{16}\) V cm\(^{-1}\)
- Restoration time for quenched Coulomb barrier: \(\sim 3 \times 10^{-21}\) s, based on electron extension distance \(\Delta R \sim 10^{-10}\) cm above the quark surface
- Potential drop across the Coulomb barrier: \(\varepsilon_{\rm F}/e \simeq 2 \times 10^7\) V
- Thermal energy stored in surface-layer electrons at \(T_{\rm S} = 10^{11}\) K: \(W_e \sim 10^{32}\) ergs, for star radius \(R \simeq 10^6\) cm, electron density \(n_e \simeq 3 \times 10^{34}\) cm\(^{-3}\), and \(\varepsilon_{\rm F} \simeq 20\) MeV
- Diffusion time scale for electrons and photons from interior to surface: \(t_e \sim 10^{12} - 10^{13}\) s
- Convection time scale for thermal energy transport: \(t_c \simeq 0.003\) s, with Rayleigh number \(\tilde{\rm R} \simeq 10^{12} (h/1\,{\rm cm})^3\) for \(\Delta T \simeq T \simeq 10^{11}\) K, \(k \simeq 0.1\) cm\(^2\) s\(^{-1}\), \(\nu \simeq 1\) cm\(^2\) s\(^{-1}\), \(\alpha \simeq 10^{-14}\) K\(^{-1}\), and \(g \simeq 10^{14}\) cm s\(^{-2}\)
- Onset of convective behavior occurs in \(\sim 10^{-5}\) s when cooled layer depth \(h \sim 10^{-3}\) cm

## Context

This reply defends the original claim in Usov (1998) that the Coulomb barrier at bare quark matter surfaces can produce \(e^+e^-\) pairs, against a comment by Mitra (1998) who argued the electric field would be quenched. The analysis builds on the quark matter surface model of Alcock, Farhi, and Olinto (1986) and Kettner, Weber, Weigel, and Glendenning (1995), which established the existence of a strong electric field at the quark surface due to electron binding. The author introduces convective heat transport as a mechanism to maintain high surface temperatures, citing Foster (1965) and Homsy (1973) for convective instability criteria, and Heiselberg and Pethick (1993) for transport coefficients. A detailed calculation of thermal transport and pair luminosity is promised for future work.
