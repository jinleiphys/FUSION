# Virtual Color Superconductivity--Status and Perspective

## Key claim
The paper argues that color superconducting phases may manifest not only at high density (as conventionally assumed) but also at zero or low density as a "virtual" phase of the QCD vacuum, with observable consequences in hadron structure and nucleon properties. The author acknowledges that this perspective contradicts the standard view that such phases only appear above a critical density too high for any system except neutron stars.

## Method
The author reviews his own decade-long program (1989-1999) developing a local finite-density quantum field theory using an 8-component real representation for fermions, which differs from the conventional 4-component representation at finite density. This framework is applied to study diquark condensation (both vector and scalar channels), the competition between color superconducting phases and chiral symmetry breaking, and the effects of statistical blocking. The approach is then used to analyze empirical nucleon data including chiral properties, anapole moments, deep inelastic scattering, and sum rules.

## Key numbers
- The BCS gap at high density is of order $\Delta_{BCS} \sim e^{-c/g}$ with $c$ a constant, and a gap of order a few 100 MeV is guaranteed at not so high density since the natural scale is of order 1 GeV and the coupling is of order 1.
- There exists a critical density $\rho_c$ below which the color superconducting phase ceases to manifest at macroscopic scale; the current estimate of $\rho_c$ is so high that physical effects would only be possible in a neutron star under conventional understanding.

## Context
This work extends the study of color superconductivity from the high-density regime (where the color-flavor locked state involving up, down, and strange quarks is the consensus ground state) to the zero- and low-density regime, which the author began investigating in 1989. The approach contrasts with later commonly adopted models (e.g., those reviewed by Shaf) that contain continuous mixing between color superconducting and chiral symmetry breaking phases, whereas the author's 8-component formalism with a "reality condition" requires quark and mirror quark (not mirror antiquark) to condense. The paper identifies possible experimental signatures in the nucleon anapole moment (recently discovered by the SAMPLE collaboration and not accounted for by conventional electroweak theory), the Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule, and polarized structure functions at small Bjorken x from deep inelastic scattering data.
