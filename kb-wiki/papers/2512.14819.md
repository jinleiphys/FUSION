# The entangling power of non-entangling channels

> There are processes that cannot generate entanglement but may, nevertheless, amplify entanglement already present in a system. Here, we show that a non-entangling operation can increase the Schmidt number of a quantum state only if it can generate entanglement with some non-zero probability. This is in stark contrast to the case where the parties of a quantum network are only able to control their joint state by local operations and classical communication (LOCC). There, being able to apply operations probabilistically (stochastic LOCC) does not increase the Schmidt number. Our findings show that certain non-entangling operations become entangling when selecting on specific measurement outcomes. This naturally leads us to the class of stochastically non-entangling maps, being those that cannot generate entanglement even probabilistically. Intrigued by this finding, we devise a Schmidt number for quantum channels that quantifies whether a channel can generate entanglement probabilistically. Moreover, we show that a channel is non-entangling if and only if its dual map is witness-preserving -- it takes entanglement witnesses to witnesses. Based on this finding, we derive Bell-like inequalities whose violation signals that a process generates entanglement.

# The entangling power of non-entangling channels

## Key claim

A non-entangling quantum channel can increase the Schmidt number of a quantum state only if at least one of its Kraus operators can generate entanglement with non-zero probability when postselection is applied; this contrasts with stochastic LOCC, where probabilistic operations never increase the Schmidt number.

## Method

The authors define the class of stochastically non-entangling maps as those channels that can be decomposed into Kraus operators each of which maps product states to product states, and they prove that such maps cannot increase the Schmidt number of any state. They then construct a convex-roof-based channel Schmidt number that quantifies a channel's ability to generate entanglement probabilistically, and they characterize non-entangling channels via the property that their dual maps preserve entanglement witnesses, from which Bell-like inequalities for detecting entangling processes are derived.

## Context

This work extends the study of non-entangling maps beyond the LOCC and SLOCC paradigms (Bennett et al. 1996, Dür et al. 2000) and builds on prior results showing that some non-entangling maps can increase the Schmidt number (Chitambar and Vianna 2020). The authors introduce stochastically non-entangling maps as a new class that is strictly larger than separable maps but strictly smaller than all non-entangling maps, resolving the question of how probabilistic postselection enables entanglement amplification outside the SLOCC framework. The channel Schmidt number and witness-preserving characterization provide tools for identifying which apparently non-entangling processes harbor latent entangling capabilities, relevant for entanglement distillation and quantum network protocols.
