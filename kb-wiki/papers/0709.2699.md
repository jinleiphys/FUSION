# Validating relativistic models of nuclear structure against theoretical, experimental, and observational constraints

> Relativistic mean-field models of nuclear structure have been enormously successful at reproducing ground-state properties of finite nuclei throughout the periodic table using a handful of accurately calibrated parameters. In this contribution we use powerful theoretical, experimental, and observational constraints -- not employed in the calibration procedure -- to validate two such models: NL3 and FSUGold. It is observed that FSUGold is consistent with all these constraints, except perhaps for a high density equation of state that appears mildly softer than required by astronomical observations. It is argued that incorporating such constrains goes a long way in removing much of the ambiguity left over from the standard calibrating procedure.

## Key claim

FSUGold is consistent with all theoretical, experimental, and observational constraints not used in its calibration, except that its high-density equation of state appears mildly softer than required by current astronomical observations; the NL3 model, while equally successful for ground-state properties, is ruled out by several of these independent constraints.

## Method

The author compared predictions from two relativistic mean-field models (NL3 and FSUGold) against three categories of constraints: (1) the low-density equation of state of pure neutron matter from the universal behavior of dilute Fermi gases with effective-range corrections by Schwenk and Pethick (2005); (2) experimental constraints from heavy-ion collisions, including isoscaling data and flow analyses by Danielewicz (2002) and Shetty (2005); (3) observational constraints from neutron-star masses and radii, specifically the mass of PSR J0751+1807 from Nice (2005) and the mass-radius determination for EXO 0748-676 from Ozel (2006). The models were solved at the mean-field level using the Lagrangian density of Eq. (1), which includes scalar self-interactions (kappa, lambda), a quartic vector self-coupling (zeta), and a mixed isoscalar-isovector coupling (Lambda_v).

## Key numbers

- Incompressibility coefficient of symmetric nuclear matter: K = 230 +/- 10 MeV (from breathing-mode analyses by Agrawal (2003) and Garg (2006))
- NL3: saturation density rho_0 = 0.148 fm^{-3}, binding energy epsilon_0 = -16.24 MeV, incompressibility K = 271 MeV, symmetry energy J = 37.3 MeV, slope of symmetry energy L = 118.4 MeV
- FSUGold: saturation density rho_0 = 0.148 fm^{-3}, binding energy epsilon_0 = -16.30 MeV, incompressibility K = 230 MeV, symmetry energy J = 32.6 MeV, slope of symmetry energy L = 60.5 MeV
- Neutron-star limiting mass: NL3 predicts 2.78 M_sun; FSUGold predicts 1.72 M_sun
- Neutron-skin thickness of 208Pb: NL3 predicts 0.28 fm; FSUGold predicts 0.21 fm
- Mass of PSR J0751+1807: M = 2.1 +/- 0.2 M_sun (Nice, 2005)
- Mass and radius of EXO 0748-676: M >= 2.10 +/- 0.28 M_sun, R >= 13.8 +/- 1.80 km (Ozel, 2006)
- Symmetry energy exponent gamma from isoscaling: gamma = 0.69 (Shetty, 2005; Li, 2005); FSUGold gives gamma = 0.64; NL3 gives gamma = 0.98
- Universal constant for dilute Fermi gases: xi approximately 0.4
- Neutron-neutron scattering length: a_nn = -18.5 fm; effective range: r_e = +2.7 fm

## Context

This work extends the validation of relativistic mean-field models beyond the ground-state properties used in calibration, following the approach of Todd-Rutel and Piekarewicz (2005) who introduced the FSUGold model with additional couplings (zeta and Lambda_v) to address the degeneracy left by standard calibration. The paper directly compares NL3 (Lalazissis, Ring, and collaborators, 1996, 1999) and FSUGold against model-independent low-density constraints from Schwenk and Pethick (2005), heavy-ion flow constraints from Danielewicz (2002), and neutron-star observations from Nice (2005) and Ozel (2006). The results enable future work to incorporate these constraints explicitly into density functional calibration, and highlight that improved neutron-star mass measurements (e.g., from Constellation X) will be essential to pin down the high-density equation of state.
