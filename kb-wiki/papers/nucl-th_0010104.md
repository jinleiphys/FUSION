# Quantum Field Treatment of DCC Dynamics

> A practical quantum-field treatment is developed for systems endowed with an effective mass function depending on both space and time and a schematic application illustrates the quantitative importance of quantum fluctuations in the dynamics of disoriented chiral condensates.

## Key claim

The paper develops a practical quantum-field treatment for systems with a space- and time-dependent effective mass function, and a schematic application shows that quantum fluctuations produce a quantitatively significant quasiparticle density even when the initial state has no thermal excitations (zero temperature), contrary to what classical treatments would predict.

## Method

The author extends the mean-field linear sigma model to non-uniform environments by expanding the field operators in canonical standing-wave modes and solving the field equation of motion numerically for each mode. The time-dependent mass matrix is diagonalized at each time to define an adiabatic quasiparticle basis, and Bogoliubov coefficients are computed to transform between the initial free-particle basis and the instantaneous quasiparticle basis. The spatial and spectral quasiparticle densities arising purely from quantum fluctuations are then evaluated from the Bogoliubov coefficients.

## Key numbers

- The initial mass excess is confined to a region \(|x| \leq 5~\fm\), with the spatial profile smeared by an exponential of width 1 fm.
- The resonance frequency for quasiparticle production is \(\omega_{\rm res} \approx \frac{1}{2}\omega_\sigma = 300~\MeV\), corresponding to a momentum \(k_{\rm res} \approx 266~\MeV/c\).
- The spectral profile shows visible enhancement near this resonance at later times, producing two countermoving beams with flow velocities \(v_{\rm flow} \approx \pm k_{\rm res}/m\).

## Context

This work extends the classical-field simulations of DCC dynamics by Rajagopal (1993) and Asakawa (1995) and the uniform-environment quantum treatments by Boyanovsky (1995), Mottola (1995), and Randrup (1997) to non-uniform, rapidly evolving environments relevant to heavy-ion collisions. The treatment enables quantitative assessment of quantum fluctuation effects on observable signals from chiral dynamics, particularly for scenarios at RHIC, and provides a framework that can be applied to scenarios previously addressed only at the classical level, such as the Bjorken rod model.
