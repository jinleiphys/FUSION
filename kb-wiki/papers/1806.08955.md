# From Nuclei to the Cosmos: Tracing Heavy-Element Production with the Oldest Stars

> Understanding the origin of the elements has been a decades long pursuit, with many open questions still remaining. Old stars found in the Milky Way and its dwarf satellite galaxies can provide answers because they preserve clean elemental patterns of the nucleosynthesis processes that operated some 13 billion years ago. This enables the reconstruction of the chemical evolution of the elements. Here we focus on the astrophysical signatures of heavy neutron-capture elements made in the s-, i- and r-process found in old stars. A highlight is the recently discovered r-process galaxy Reticulum II that was apparently enriched by a neutron star merger. These results show that old stars in dwarf galaxies provide a novel means to constrain the astrophysical site of the r-process, ushering in much needed progress on this major outstanding question. This nuclear astrophysics work complements the many nuclear physics efforts into heavy-element formation, and aligns with recent results on the gravitational wave signature of a neutron star merger.

## Key claim

Old stars in the Milky Way and its dwarf satellite galaxies preserve clean elemental patterns of nucleosynthesis processes from approximately 13 billion years ago, enabling reconstruction of chemical evolution, but the interpretation of these patterns is complicated by the inability to determine how many progenitor stars contributed to the observed abundances, particularly for s-process elements at metallicities [Fe/H] > -2.6.

## Method

The paper reviews spectroscopic abundance analysis of metal-poor stars using high-resolution spectra (resolving power R > 20,000) covering visible wavelengths from ~350 to ~700 nm, with abundances derived from measured strengths of spectral absorption lines using model atmospheres that approximate physical conditions in stellar outer layers. Elemental patterns of neutron-capture elements from the s-process, i-process, and r-process are compared with theoretical nucleosynthesis predictions to identify the astrophysical sites of heavy-element production, with particular focus on stars in dwarf satellite galaxies of the Milky Way.

## Context

This review extends the decades-long pursuit of understanding element origins, building on foundational work by Burbidge (1957) and the stellar archaeology framework established by Beers and Christlieb (2005). The paper connects observational stellar abundance studies with nuclear physics efforts at facilities like the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB), and aligns with results from gravitational wave signatures of neutron star mergers (Fernandez 2016, Thielemann 2017). The discovery of the r-process galaxy Reticulum II, apparently enriched by a neutron star merger, demonstrates that old stars in dwarf galaxies provide a novel means to constrain the astrophysical site of the r-process, enabling progress on this major outstanding question in nuclear astrophysics.
