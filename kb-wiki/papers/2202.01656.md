# Constraints on the merging binary neutron star mass distribution and equation of state based on the incidence of jets in the population

> A relativistic jet has been produced in the single well-localised binary neutron star (BNS) merger detected to date in gravitational waves (GWs), and the local rates of BNS mergers and short gamma-ray bursts are of the same order of magnitude. This suggests that jet formation is not a rare outcome for BNS mergers, and we show that this intuition can be turned into a quantitative constraint: at least about $1/3$ of GW-detected BNS mergers, and at least about $1/5$ of all BNS mergers, should produce a successful jet (90\% credible level). Whether a jet is launched depends on the properties of the merger remnant and of the surrounding accretion disc, which in turn are a function of the progenitor binary masses and equation of state (EoS). The incidence of jets in the population therefore carries information about the binary component mass distribution and EoS. Under the assumption that a jet can only be produced by a black hole remnant surrounded by a non-negligible accretion disc, we show how the jet incidence can be used to place a joint constraint on the space of BNS component mass distributions and EoS. The result points to a broad mass distribution, with particularly strong support for masses in the $1.3-1.6\,\mathrm{M_\odot}$ range. The constraints on the EoS are shallow, but we show how they will tighten as the knowledge on the jet incidence improves. We also discuss how to extend the method to include future BNS mergers, with possibly uncertain jet associations.

## Key claim

At least about 1/3 of GW-detected BNS mergers and at least about 1/5 of all BNS mergers should produce a successful relativistic jet at the 90% credible level, based on the single observed jet-associated event GW170817 and the comparison of local BNS merger and short GRB rates.

## Method

The authors derived posterior probability distributions on the jet incidence (fraction of BNS mergers producing a successful jet) using two approaches: a binomial argument based on the single GW-detected BNS merger GW170817 that produced a jet, and a ratio of the local rate density of beaming-corrected short GRB jets to the local BNS merger rate density from Galactic double neutron star observations (Grunthal 2021). They then modeled the jet incidence under the assumption that a jet can only be produced when the merger remnant collapses to a black hole on a short timescale (HMNS or prompt BH) and is surrounded by an accretion disc of at least 10^-3 solar masses, using fitting formulae from Kruger (2020) for disc mass and ejecta mass and from Zappa (2018) for gravitational wave energy loss.

## Key numbers

- At least 1/3 of GW-detectable BNS mergers should produce a successful jet (90% credible level, uniform prior); corresponding 3-sigma lower limit is 5.2% (uniform prior) or 3.4% (Jeffreys prior)
- At least 1/5 of all BNS mergers should produce a successful jet (90% credible level), with a 3-sigma lower limit of 2.4%
- Local BNS merger rate density from Galactic systems: median 390 Gpc^-3 yr^-1 with 90% credible range 134-693 Gpc^-3 yr^-1 (based on Grunthal 2021)
- Local rate density of GRB170817A-like events: median 342 Gpc^-3 yr^-1 with 90% credible interval 5-2142 Gpc^-3 yr^-1
- The posterior distribution on the total jet incidence f_j,tot follows a power law P(f_j,tot) proportional to f_j,tot^gamma with best-fit gamma = 0.42
- Minimum disc mass for jet launching assumed: 10^-3 solar masses; typical jet energy range: 10^49-10^50 erg, requiring disc mass ~0.01-0.1 solar masses at assumed efficiency eta ~ 10^-3

## Context

This work extends the intuition from Beniamini (2019) and Ghirlanda (2019) that jet formation is common in BNS mergers by providing quantitative Bayesian lower limits on the jet incidence. The method connects the observed jet incidence to the binary component mass distribution and neutron star equation of state through the conditions for black hole remnant formation and accretion disc survival, building on numerical relativity results from Kruger (2020), Zappa (2018), and Bauswein (2021). The framework enables future joint constraints on the mass distribution and EoS as more BNS mergers with jet associations are observed, and can be incorporated into Bayesian hierarchical population studies of BNS mergers.
