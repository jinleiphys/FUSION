# Evolutions in first-order viscous hydrodynamics

> Motivated by the physics of the quark-gluon plasma created in heavy-ion collision experiments, we use holography to study the regime of applicability of various theories of relativistic viscous hydrodynamics. Using the microscopic description provided by holography of a system that relaxes to equilibrium, we obtain initial data with which we perform real-time evolutions in 2+1 dimensional conformal fluids using the first-order viscous relativistic hydrodynamics theory of Bemfica, Disconzi, Noronha and Kovtun (BDNK), BRSSS and ideal hydrodynamics. By initializing the hydrodynamics codes at different times, we can check the constitutive relations and assess the predictive power and accuracy of each of these theories.

## Key claim

Using holography to obtain a first-principles microscopic description of a strongly coupled conformal fluid relaxing to equilibrium, the authors test the regime of applicability of BDNK, BRSSS, and ideal hydrodynamics; they find that all three theories agree with the microscopic evolution only when gradients are small (first and second order corrections below about 2%), and that outside this regime none of the theories provides an accurate description, contrary to the "unreasonable success of hydrodynamics" sometimes claimed for quark-gluon plasma.

## Method

The authors use numerical general relativity to simulate the gravitational collapse of a massless scalar field in 3+1 dimensional anti-de Sitter space, which holographically describes the real-time dynamics of the stress tensor in a strongly coupled conformal field theory. From this microscopic solution, they extract initial data (energy density, fluid velocities, and for BRSSS the dissipative stress tensor) at three different times and use it to initialize independent numerical evolutions of the BDNK, BRSSS, and ideal hydrodynamic equations in 2+1 dimensional Minkowski spacetime. They compare the resulting stress tensor evolutions from each hydrodynamic theory to the holographic solution at an off-center point.

## Key numbers

* The system hydrodynamizes at times around \(t\overline{T} \simeq 0.5\), defined as when the ratios \(T_{xx}^{1st}/T_{xx}^{id}\) and \(T_{xx}^{2nd}/T_{xx}^{id}\) fall below 2% at the representative point \(\{x,y\}\overline{T}\simeq \{0.12,0\}\).
* At the earliest initialization time \(t\overline{T}\simeq 0.019\), second order gradient corrections are as large as 40% compared to ideal terms.
* At the late initialization time \(t\overline{T}\simeq 0.58\), first and second order gradients are both about 1% of the ideal term, and the energy density from all hydrodynamic evolutions differs from the holographic result by less than about 2% across the whole domain for all times beyond that point.
* The BDNK frames used are Frame 1 (\(a_1=10, a_2=10\)) and Frame 2 (\(a_1=6, a_2=4\)), both satisfying the hyperbolicity conditions \(a_2>1\) and \(a_1>4a_2/(a_2-1)\).

## Context

This work extends the study of BDNK hydrodynamics beyond the effectively 1+1 dimensional evolutions of Pandya and Pretorius (2021) to 2+1 dimensional conformal fluids, and goes beyond prior checks of constitutive relations (e.g., Heller and Spalinski (2014)) by using the holographic data to initialize the hydrodynamic codes at different times and compare full evolutions. The results enable a systematic program to test causal viscous hydrodynamic theories against microscopic holographic evolutions, with future extensions to non-conformal and charged theories and to initial data modeling heavy-ion collisions or neutron star mergers.
