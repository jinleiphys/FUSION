# Solving Nuclear Structure Problems with the Adaptive Variational Quantum Algorithm

> We use the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick (LMG) model and the valence-space nuclear shell model to examine the likely performance of variational quantum eigensolvers in nuclear-structure theory. The LMG model exhibits both a phase transition and spontaneous symmetry breaking at the mean-field level in one of the phases, features that characterize collective dynamics in medium-mass and heavy nuclei. We show that with appropriate modifications, the ADAPT-VQE algorithm, a particularly flexible and accurate variational approach, is not troubled by these complications. We treat up to 12 particles and show that the number of quantum operations needed to approach the ground-state energy scales linearly with the number of qubits. We find similar scaling when the algorithm is applied to the nuclear shell model with realistic interactions in the $sd$ and $pf$ shells. Although most of these simulations contain no noise, we use a noise model from real IBM hardware to show that for the LMG model with four particles, weak noise has no effect on the efficiency of the algorithm.

## Key claim

The ADAPT-VQE algorithm, when augmented with symmetry-projection techniques from nuclear-structure theory, achieves linear scaling of quantum operations with qubit number even across a quantum phase transition and in the presence of spontaneous symmetry breaking, contrary to the expectation that such features would cause exponential growth in circuit depth.

## Method

The authors applied the ADAPT-VQE algorithm to the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick (LMG) model for up to 12 particles, using a pool of one- and two-body Pauli operators and comparing several variants: an uncorrelated reference state (method 0), a Hartree-Fock reference state with quasiparticle operators (method HF), and two symmetry-projected versions (HF-PAV and HF-VAP) that restore number-parity symmetry via the projected Hamiltonian \(P_+ H P_+\). They also applied the algorithm to the nuclear shell model in the \(sd\) and \(pf\) valence spaces using the Jordan-Wigner mapping and a pool of two-body fermion excitation operators, with realistic USDB and KB3G interactions. Noisy simulations for the LMG model with four particles used a noise model from the IBM Vigo quantum processor and a custom depolarizing noise model.

## Key numbers

* For the LMG model, the number of pool operators needed to reach within 0.1% of the ground-state energy scales linearly with the number of qubits \(N\) for all methods tested, across coupling values \(y\) from 0.1 to 0.9.
* The phase transition in the LMG model occurs at \(y = 1/3\) for any number of particles \(N\).
* In noisy simulations of the LMG model with \(N=4\) and \(y=0.3\), using 8000 shots, the energy convergence with the real IBM Vigo noise model and a custom model with 10% single-qubit and 20% two-qubit depolarizing error rates was identical to the noiseless simulation.
* A custom noise model with 20% single-qubit and 40% two-qubit depolarizing error rates caused faster accumulation of gradient error as the circuit grew.

## Context

This work extends prior VQE studies on the LMG model by Cervia et al. (2021), who used a symmetry-enforcing ansatz limited to three qubits with CNOT scaling of \(\mathcal{O}(2^N)\), and by Chikaoka (2022), who compared unitary coupled cluster and structure-learning ansatze for up to four qubits and found performance declining with interaction strength. It also builds on the shell-model VQE study by Stetcu et al. (2021), which analyzed encodings and unitary coupled cluster performance in four nuclei with up to six valence nucleons. The results enable near-term quantum simulations of nuclear structure across phase transitions and in realistic shell-model spaces, demonstrating that ADAPT-VQE with symmetry projection provides a universal scheme for both the symmetry-broken and unbroken regimes.
