# Understanding fusion and its suppression for the $^{9}$Be projectile with different targets

> The role of the breakup process and one neutron stripping on the near barrier fusion are investigated for the weakly bound projectile $^{9}$Be on $^{28}$Si, $^{89}$Y, $^{124}$Sn, $^{144}$Sm and $^{208}$Pb targets. Continuum-discretized coupled channels (CDCC) calculations for the breakup with a $^{8}$Be + n model of the $^{9}$Be nucleus and coupled reactions channels (CRC) calculations for the one neutron stripping to several single particle states in the target are performed for these systems. A good description of the experimental fusion cross sections above the Coulomb barrier is obtained from the CDCC-CRC calculations for all the systems. The calculated incomplete fusion probabilities for different target systems are found to be consistent with the systematic behaviour of the complete fusion suppression factors as a function of target atomic mass, obtained from the experimental data.

# Understanding fusion and its suppression for the \(^{9}\)Be projectile with different targets

## Key claim

The complete fusion suppression for the \(^{9}\)Be projectile is approximately independent of target mass across a wide range from light (\(^{28}\)Si) to heavy (\(^{208}\)Pb) targets, contrary to earlier empirical predictions that the incomplete fusion probability should decrease monotonically with target charge. This near-constant behaviour is obtained from CDCC-CRC calculations that include both \(^{8}\)Be + n breakup and one-neutron stripping couplings, and it resolves discrepancies where the \(^{9}\)Be + \(^{89}\)Y and \(^{9}\)Be + \(^{124}\)Sn systems had been found not to follow the earlier systematics of Gomes et al. (2010).

## Method

The authors performed continuum-discretized coupled channels (CDCC) calculations for the \(^{8}\)Be + n breakup of \(^{9}\)Be and coupled reaction channels (CRC) calculations for one-neutron stripping to several single-particle states in each target, using the FRXY.li version of the code FRESCO. The absorption cross sections from CDCC calculations with three choices of short-range imaginary potential (applied to both fragments, only the \(^{8}\)Be-target part, or only the neutron-target part) were used to extract the incomplete fusion cross sections for the neutron and the \(\alpha\) particle separately. The calculations were carried out for \(^{9}\)Be on \(^{28}\)Si, \(^{89}\)Y, \(^{124}\)Sn, \(^{144}\)Sm, and \(^{208}\)Pb targets.

## Key numbers

* The one-neutron stripping reactions have positive Q-values: 6.81 MeV for \(^{28}\)Si, 5.19 MeV for \(^{89}\)Y, 4.07 MeV for \(^{124}\)Sn, 5.09 MeV for \(^{144}\)Sm, and 2.27 MeV for \(^{208}\)Pb.
* The continuum was included up to an energy \(E_{max} \approx 7\) MeV above the \(^{8}\)Be + n breakup threshold.
* Relative angular momentum values up to \(l = 4\) for the neutron-core relative motion were used; inclusion of higher \(l\) values changed the calculated fusion cross section by less than about 2 percent.
* The short-range imaginary potential used Woods-Saxon parameters \(W_0 = 50\) MeV, \(r_0 = 0.9\) fm, and \(a = 0.25\) fm.
* The incomplete fusion probabilities and suppression factors were evaluated at a lab energy of \(1.3 V_b\), where \(V_b\) is the Coulomb barrier for each system.

## Context

This work extends the CDCC-CRC approach previously applied to \(^{9}\)Be + \(^{208}\)Pb by Singh et al. (2012) and to elastic scattering by Sanat et al. (2013) and Parkar et al. (2013), now covering a wide target mass range from light to heavy. It directly addresses the discrepancy noted by Gomes et al. (2010), whose empirical model based on Hinde et al. (2002) predicted that the incomplete fusion probability should decrease with target charge, whereas the data for \(^{9}\)Be + \(^{89}\)Y and \(^{9}\)Be + \(^{124}\)Sn did not follow this trend. The results support the conclusion of Rafiei et al. (2010) that the complete fusion suppression is nearly independent of target atomic number, and they enable future exclusive measurements of incomplete and complete fusion with weakly bound projectiles, especially in the light target mass region, to further test this universal behaviour.
