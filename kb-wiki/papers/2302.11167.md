# Fluctuations of atomic energy levels due to axion dark matter

> The amplitude of the pseudoscalar (axion) or scalar field fluctuates on a time scale of order of million field oscillation periods which is a typical coherence time in the virialized axion galactic dark matter halo model. This causes fluctuations of frequencies of atomic clocks on the same time scale. We show that this effect may be employed to search for the axion and scalar field dark matter with atomic and nuclear clocks. We re-purpose the results of the atomic clocks experiments comparing the variations of frequencies of hyperfine transitions in Rb and Cs atoms as well as in hydrogen atom vs cavity frequency fluctuations, and extract new limits on the axion coupling constant $f_a$ for masses in the range $2.4\times 10^{-17}\text{ eV}\lesssim m \lesssim 10^{-13}\text{ eV}$. We also show that similar energy shifts arise in the second-order perturbation theory with linear in the pseudoscalar field interaction. These shifts may be potentially measured with nuclear clocks based on the low-energy transition in $^{229}$Th nucleus. We propose a procedure which could, in principle, help determine the axion mass if the axion dark matter signal is present in experimental data sets.

# Fluctuations of atomic energy levels due to axion dark matter

## Key claim
The amplitude of the axion (pseudoscalar) or scalar dark matter field fluctuates stochastically on the coherence time scale of approximately one million field oscillation periods, causing measurable fluctuations in atomic clock frequencies. The mean value of the resulting atomic energy level shift is approximately equal to its standard deviation due to the exponential distribution of the shift, which allows extracting limits on the axion coupling constant from existing experimental data without requiring Fourier analysis.

## Method
The authors model the axion dark matter field as either a monochromatic wave with Rayleigh-distributed amplitude and uniformly random phase, or as a wave packet summing over virialized dark matter particles with velocities following the standard halo model distribution. They compute quadratic-in-field energy shifts from first-order perturbation theory arising from the QCD axion-gluon interaction, which induces variations in pion mass and consequently in nuclear properties. They then re-purpose published standard deviations from atomic clock comparison experiments (Rb/Cs and H/Si cavity) to set limits on the axion decay constant by equating the experimental variance to the theoretically expected variance from axion-induced fluctuations, under the condition that integration time per measurement is much larger than the oscillation period but much smaller than the coherence time.

## Key numbers
- Axion mass range probed: $2.4 \times 10^{-17}\text{ eV} \lesssim m \lesssim 10^{-13}\text{ eV}$ from Rb/Cs experiment
- Axion mass range probed: $7.3\times 10^{-16}\text{ eV} < m < 1.9\times 10^{-10}\text{ eV}$ from H/Si cavity experiment
- Limit from Rb/Cs experiment: $f_a > 1.8 \times 10^{9} \text{ GeV} \left(10^{-15}\text{ eV}/m\right)$ using reported standard deviation $\sigma = 3 \times 10^{-15}$ with integration time $t_1 = 864$ s and $N = 100814$ measurements
- Limit from H/Si cavity experiment: $f_a > 5.8 \times 10^{9} \text{ GeV} \left(10^{-15}\text{ eV}/m\right)$ using reported standard deviation $\sigma \approx 3 \times 10^{-15}$ with integration time $t_1 \approx 10.7$ s
- Local dark matter density assumed: $\rho_\text{DM} \approx 0.4\text{ GeV/cm}^3$
- Skewness and kurtosis of the exponential distribution for axion-induced shifts: $S = 2$, $K = 9$
- Coherence time definition: $\tau \sim 10^6 \pi/m$ (approximately $10^6$ oscillation periods)

## Context
This work extends the approach of searching for scalar and pseudoscalar dark matter through atomic clock experiments, building on prior calculations of quadratic-in-field interactions by Arvanitaki (2015), Stadnik (2015), and Kim and Perez (2022). The authors re-purpose data from the Rb/Cs clock comparison experiment (Guena 2012) and the H/Si cavity experiment (Hees 2016) to derive new limits on the axion decay constant in mass ranges not covered by previous lab-based analyses. The proposed binning procedure for identifying axion signals through statistical moments (skewness and kurtosis) is related to the stacking method of Foster (2018) and the network-based correlation approach of Derevianko (2018) and the GNOME collaboration (2021). The results enable future searches using nuclear clocks based on the low-energy isomeric transition in $^{229}$Th, where second-order perturbation theory contributions may become significant.
