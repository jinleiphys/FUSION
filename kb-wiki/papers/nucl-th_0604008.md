# Origins of Bulk Viscosity at RHIC

> A variety of physical phenomena can lead to viscous effects. Several sources of shear and bulk viscosity are reviewed with an emphasis on the bulk viscosity associated with chiral restoration and with chemical non-equilibrium. We show that in a mean-field treatment of the limiting case of a second order phase transition, the bulk viscosity peaks in a singularity at the critical point.

# Origins of Bulk Viscosity at RHIC

## Key claim
The paper shows that in a mean-field treatment of the linear sigma model, the bulk viscosity peaks in a singularity at the critical point for a second-order phase transition, scaling as approximately 1/|T − T_c|, but the authors caution that this singular behavior invalidates the underlying linear-response assumptions of Navier-Stokes hydrodynamics, meaning the large corrections should instead be handled by explicitly evolving the mean field or using Israel-Stewart equations.

## Method
The authors derive Kubo relations for shear and bulk viscosity from linear response theory, then identify five physical sources of viscosity: non-zero mean free paths, non-zero interaction range, classical electric fields, non-equilibrium chemistry, and dynamic mean fields. They focus on the last two sources by calculating the bulk viscosity in the linear sigma model with up and down quarks, where the sigma field couples to quarks to provide their mass. The bulk viscosity is computed from the offset of the sigma field from equilibrium (due to damping Γ = 400 MeV) and from chemical non-equilibrium (with τ_chem = 1 fm/c at density 1 fm⁻³), using thermodynamic derivatives evaluated numerically from the constraint ∂P/∂σ = 0 at fixed temperature.

## Key numbers
- For the linear sigma model with λ² = 40, the critical coupling is g_c = 3.554; for g < g_c the transition is a smooth cross-over, at g = g_c it is second order, and for g > g_c a first-order phase transition occurs with T_c = 172 MeV.
- The bulk viscosity near the critical point scales as B ∼ 1/|T − T_c| in the mean-field treatment.
- The damping width for the sigma field is taken as Γ = 400 MeV.
- The chemical equilibration time is taken as τ_chem = 1 fm/c at a density of one quark per fm³.
- For a Bjorken expansion starting with ε = 8 GeV/fm³ at τ = 1 fm/c, the Navier-Stokes correction to the pressure (B∇·u) becomes comparable to the equilibrium pressure near the critical region for g ≈ g_c.

## Context
This work extends prior studies of viscosity in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC, where experimental observations of large elliptic flow motivated the "perfect liquid" picture with small shear viscosity. The paper contrasts the peaking of bulk viscosity near T_c with the minimum of shear viscosity near T_c predicted by Csernai (2006) for the deconfinement transition. The authors note that the mean-field critical exponents will differ from more realistic quantum calculations, and that the dynamics of the sigma field should be treated explicitly as in Paech (2005) rather than absorbed into viscous coefficients when corrections are large. The results suggest that bulk viscosity effects could help explain HBT measurements showing rapid expansion with sudden disintegration, as discussed by Retiere (2003).
