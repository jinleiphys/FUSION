# Decay of Quasi-Particle in a Quantum Dot: the role of Energy Resolution

> The disintegration of quasiparticle in a quantum dot due to the electron interaction is considered. It was predicted recently that above the energy $\eps^{*} = Δ(g/\ln g)^{1/2}$ each one particle peak in the spectrum is split into many components ($Δ$ and $g$ are the one particle level spacing and conductance). We show that the observed value of $\eps^{*}$ should depend on the experimental resolution $δ\eps$. In the broad region of variation of $δ\eps$ the $\ln g$ should be replaced by $\ln(Δ/ gδ\eps)$. We also give the arguments against the delocalization transition in the Fock space. Most likely the number of satellite peaks grows continuously with energy, being $\sim 1$ at $\eps \sim \eps^{*}$, but remains finite at $\eps > \eps^{*}$. The predicted logarithmic distribution of inter-peak spacings may be used for experimental confirmation of the below-Golden-Rule decay.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the observed threshold energy for quasiparticle disintegration in a quantum dot, previously predicted as \(\eps^{*} = \Delta(g/\ln g)^{1/2}\), actually depends on the experimental energy resolution \(\delta\eps\); in a broad region of \(\delta\eps\), the \(\ln g\) factor should be replaced by \(\ln(\Delta/ g\delta\eps)\). The author also argues against a delocalization transition in Fock space, proposing instead that the number of satellite peaks grows continuously with energy but remains finite.

## Method

The author analyzes the participation ratio (PR) for a quasiparticle excitation in a closed quantum dot, using perturbation theory to sum contributions from mixing with increasingly complex many-particle–many-hole states (3-particle, 5-particle, etc.). The effective matrix elements connecting the initial quasiparticle to higher-generation states are computed, accounting for the experimental resolution \(\delta\eps\) by restricting the integration over energy denominators to values where the effective interaction exceeds \(\delta\eps\). The asymptotic behavior of the series coefficients is estimated by counting independent Feynman diagrams and phase-space factors.

## Key numbers

- The predicted threshold for quasiparticle disintegration without resolution effects is \(\eps^{*} = \Delta(g/\ln g)^{1/2}\), where \(\Delta\) is the single-particle level spacing and \(g \gg 1\) is the dimensionless conductance.
- With finite experimental resolution \(\delta\eps\), the effective threshold becomes \(\eps_c = \Delta\sqrt{g/\ln(\Delta/ g\delta\eps)}\).
- The density of three-particle states (two particles and one hole) is \(\nu_3 = \eps^{2}/(4\Delta^{3})\).
- The variance of two-particle interaction matrix elements is \(\overline{V^{2}} = \Delta^{2}/g^{2}\).
- The first correction to the participation ratio from mixing with three-particle states is \(P_3 = 1 - \sqrt{\pi/2}\,\eps^{2}/(g\Delta^{2})\).
- The effective matrix element connecting generations 1 and 5 is \(\overline{|V_{eff}^{(5)}|} = (2\Delta)/(\pi g^{2})\ln g\).
- With finite resolution, the effective matrix element for generation 5 becomes \(\overline{|V_{eff}^{(5)}(\delta\eps)|} = (2/\pi)(\Delta/g^{2})\ln(\Delta/(g\delta\eps))\).
- The density of final states for generation \(2n+1\) is \(\nu_{2n+1} = \eps^{2n}/(\Delta^{2n+1}) \cdot 1/[(2n)!(n+1)!n!]\).
- The number of diagrams connecting the same initial and final states is \(2^{-n}n!(n+1)!(2n-1)!!\).
- The coefficients \(p_n\) in the series for the participation ratio satisfy \(p_n < (\text{const})^{n}/n!\).

## Context

This work extends the prediction of Altshuler, Gefen, Kamenev, and Levitov (1997) that quasiparticle disintegration in quantum dots occurs at energies much lower than the Golden Rule estimate \(\eps' = \Delta\sqrt{g}\), by showing that the observed threshold depends on experimental resolution. The author argues against the localization-delocalization transition in Fock space proposed by those authors and by Mirlin (1996), instead suggesting a smooth crossover with a finite number of satellite peaks. The predicted logarithmic distribution of inter-peak spacings offers an experimentally accessible signature of below-Golden-Rule decay, potentially distinguishable from Poisson or Wigner-Dyson distributions.
