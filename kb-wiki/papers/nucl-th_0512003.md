# Anatomy of three-body decay III. Energy distributions

> We address the problem of calculating momentum distributions of particles emerging from the three-body decay of a many-body resonance. We show that these distributions are determined by the asymptotics of the coordinate-space complex-energy wave-function of the resonance. We use the hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method where all lengths are proportional to the hyperradius. The structures of the resonances are related to different decay mechanisms. For direct decay all inter-particle distances increase proportional to the hyperradius at intermediate and large distances. Sequential three-body decay proceeds via spatially confined quasi-stationary two-body configurations. Then two particles remain close while the third moves away. The wave function may contain mixtures which produce coherence effects at small distances, but the energy distributions can still be added incoherently. Two-neutron halos are discussed in details and illustrated by the $2^+$ resonance in $^{6}$He. The dynamic evolution of the decay process is discussed.

## Key claim

The momentum distributions of particles from three-body decay are determined solely by the asymptotic (large-distance) behavior of the coordinate-space complex-energy resonance wave function, not by the short-distance structure; the energy distributions from direct and sequential decay channels can be added incoherently even when the wave function contains mixtures that produce coherence effects at small distances.

## Method

The authors use the hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method combined with complex scaling to obtain three-body resonance wave functions. They solve the complex-scaled Faddeev equations as functions of the hyperradius, then extract the asymptotic behavior of the wave function and perform a Fourier transform to momentum space using Zeldovich regularization of the divergent integrals. For decays involving bound or resonant two-body subsystems, the asymptotic wave function is decomposed into separate non-interfering terms representing direct three-body breakup and sequential decay via two-body configurations.

## Key numbers

- The $2^+$ resonance in $^{6}$He is used as the illustrative example for two-neutron halos.
- For nuclear systems with effective two-body range $R_{eff} \simeq 4$ fm, the hyperangular quantum number $K_{max}$ of at least 50 is needed to describe two-body substructures for hyperradius $\rho \simeq 100$ fm.
- For sequential decay via a two-body resonance, the energy distribution of the emitted particle has a Breit-Wigner shape with width equal to the sum of the two-body and three-body widths: $\Gamma_0 + \Gamma_{23}$.
- The most probable energy of particle 1 in sequential decay is $E_1 = E_0 - E_{23}^{(0)}$, where $E_0$ is the three-body resonance energy and $E_{23}^{(0)}$ is the two-body resonance energy.

## Context

This work extends the schematic model of direct and sequential three-body decay mechanisms from Garrido et al. (2005) and the detailed resonance structure investigations from Garrido et al. (2005) to provide a general method for computing relative energy distributions after three-body decay. The approach contrasts with earlier work using saddle-point approximation by Fedorov et al. (2004) and instead employs Zeldovich regularization. The method enables interpretation of kinematically complete measurements now available for many systems (Dan87, Boc89, Kry95, Bai96, Gom01, Gio02, Pfu02, Chr02, Fyn03, Bla03, Zer04, Bla05) and can be generalized to decays involving multiple two-body bound states or resonant subsystems.
