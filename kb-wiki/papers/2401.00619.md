# Collision energy dependence of source sizes for primary and secondary pions at NICA energies

> We study the evolution of the two-pion correlation function parameters with collision energy in the context of relativistic heavy-ion collisions within the NICA energy range. To this end, we perform UrQMD simulations in the cascade mode to produce samples of pions from $5\times 10^6$ Bi+Bi collisions for each of the studied energies. The effects of the quantum-statistical correlations are introduced using the correlation afterburner code CRAB. We fit the correlation function using Gaussian, exponential and symmetric Lévy shapes and show that for all collision energies the latter provides the best fit. We separate the sample into pions coming from primary processes and pions originating from the decay of long-lived resonances, and show that the source size for the latter is significantly larger than for the former. The source size for the secondaries, is similar but in general larger than the size for the whole pion sample. To further characterize the pion source, we also simulate the effects of a non-ideal detector introducing a momentum smearing parameter, representing the minimum pair momentum and thus a maximum source size that can be resolved. The values of the correlation function intercept parameter are therefore modified from the values they attain for the perfect detector case. Using the core-halo picture of the source, we show that the values of the intercept parameter are influenced by the presence of a significant fraction of core pions coming from the decay of long-lived but slow-moving resonances. These findings serve as a benchmark to compare with future Monte Carlo studies that consider an Equation of State and thus allow for a phase transition within the studied energy domain.

## Key claim

The paper shows that for Bi+Bi collisions in the NICA energy range (sqrt(s_NN) = 4.0 to 9.2 GeV), the symmetric Levy shape provides the best fit to the two-pion correlation function compared to Gaussian or exponential forms, but this result is obtained from Monte Carlo simulations without an Equation of State and thus does not include a possible phase transition.

## Method

The authors performed UrQMD simulations in cascade mode to generate 5 million central Bi+Bi collisions at each of four collision energies (sqrt(s_NN) = 4.0, 5.8, 7.7, and 9.2 GeV). Quantum-statistical correlations were added using the CRAB afterburner code, and the resulting two-pion correlation functions were fitted with Gaussian, exponential, and symmetric Levy shapes. The pion sample was separated into primary pions and secondary pions (from long-lived resonance decays), and the effects of finite detector resolution were simulated by introducing a momentum smearing parameter of 10 MeV.

## Key numbers

* At sqrt(s_NN) = 5.8 GeV for the complete pion set, the Levy fit yields R_inv = 8.121 +/- 0.059 fm, lambda = 1.05 +/- 0.006, and alpha = 1.312 +/- 0.015, with chi^2/ndf = 0.835, compared to Gaussian (chi^2/ndf = 38.301) and exponential (chi^2/ndf = 14.849) fits.
* For primary pions at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.8 GeV, the Levy fit gives R_inv = 3.516 +/- 0.014 fm, lambda = 0.982 +/- 0.004, and alpha = 1.863 +/- 0.021.
* For secondary pions at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.8 GeV, the Levy fit gives R_inv = 8.402 +/- 0.065 fm, lambda = 1.066 +/- 0.007, and alpha = 1.31 +/- 0.016.
* With 10 MeV momentum smearing at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.8 GeV, the complete pion sample yields R_inv = 6.238 +/- 0.046 fm, lambda = 0.632 +/- 0.004, and alpha = 1.502 +/- 0.022, representing a 23% reduction in source size and 39% reduction in intercept compared to the ideal case.
* The average space-time interval of freeze-out coordinates for sqrt(s_NN) = 9.2 GeV was Delta = 9.19 fm, which agrees within a few percent with the Levy fit value.

## Context

This work extends femtoscopic studies based on Levy shape fits, previously performed at SPS (Porfy 2023), RHIC (Kincses 2016, 2017; Lokos 2018), and LHC (CMS 2023) energies, into the NICA energy range where the critical end point (CEP) is thought to exist. The results serve as a benchmark for future Monte Carlo studies that include an Equation of State and allow for a phase transition, and the authors note that the core-halo picture (Wiedemann 1996, Csorgo 1994) shows the core contains a significant fraction of secondary pions from long-lived but slow-moving resonances, not just primary particles.
