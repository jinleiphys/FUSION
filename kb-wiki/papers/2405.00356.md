# Nuclear mass predictions with anisotropic kernel ridge regression

> The anisotropic kernel ridge regression (AKRR) approach in nuclear mass predictions is developed by introducing the anisotropic kernel function into the kernel ridge regression (KRR) approach, without introducing new weight parameter or input in the training. A combination of double two-dimensional Gaussian kernel function is adopted, and the corresponding hyperparameters are optimized carefully by cross-validations. The anisotropic kernel shows cross-shape pattern, which highlights the correlations among the isotopes with the same proton number, and that among the isotones with the same neutron number. Significant improvements are achieved by the AKRR approach in both the interpolation and the extrapolation predictions of nuclear masses comparing with the original KRR approach.

## Key claim

The anisotropic kernel ridge regression (AKRR) approach improves nuclear mass predictions over the original isotropic KRR approach by introducing a cross-shaped kernel pattern that strengthens correlations among isotopes (same proton number) and isotones (same neutron number), without adding new weight parameters or inputs to the training.

## Method

The authors replaced the isotropic Gaussian kernel in the standard KRR framework with a combination of double two-dimensional Gaussian kernel functions having different length scales in the neutron and proton directions (σ₁ and σ₂). Hyperparameters were optimized via leave-one-out cross-validation on 2340 nuclei with Z≥8 and N≥8 from the AME2020 database, using the WS4 mass model as the baseline theoretical prediction. The regularization parameter λ was fixed at 0.3, matching the original KRR approach.

## Key numbers

- The AKRR approach achieved a root-mean-square (rms) mass deviation of **141 keV** in leave-one-out cross-validation, compared to **195 keV** for the original KRR approach.
- The global minimum of the AKRR rms deviation surface occurred at hyperparameters **(σ₁=3.3, σ₂=0.7)** (or equivalently σ₁=0.7, σ₂=3.3).
- For medium-mass and heavy nuclei, mass deviations from the AKRR approach were mostly reduced to **within 0.1 MeV**.
- The KRR approach with isotropic kernels achieved rms deviations around **195 keV** at its two minima (σ=0.8 and σ=2.2).

## Context

This work extends the KRR approach for nuclear mass predictions, which was first introduced by Wu (2020) and later refined to include odd-even effects (KRRoe, Wu 2021) and gradient information (GKRR, Wu 2022). The key innovation is replacing the isotropic Gaussian kernel used in all prior KRR-based mass models with an anisotropic kernel, motivated by the physical expectation that a nucleus is more similar to its isotopes and isotones than to nuclei at the same Euclidean distance but different proton/neutron combinations. The approach enables more accurate mass predictions for medium-mass and heavy nuclei, which is relevant for r-process nucleosynthesis studies.
