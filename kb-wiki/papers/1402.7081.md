# Dispersive approach to hadronic light-by-light scattering

> Based on dispersion theory, we present a formalism for a model-independent evaluation of the hadronic light-by-light contribution to the anomalous magnetic moment of the muon. In particular, we comment on the definition of the pion pole in this framework and provide a master formula that relates the effect from pi pi intermediate states to the partial waves for the process gamma^* gamma^* --> pi pi. All contributions are expressed in terms of on-shell form factors and scattering amplitudes, and as such amenable to an experimental determination.

## Key claim

The authors present a dispersive formalism for a model-independent evaluation of the hadronic light-by-light (HLbL) contribution to the anomalous magnetic moment of the muon, based on the assumption that the most important contributions come from single- and double-pion intermediate states, with the explicit caveat that restoring the correct high-energy behavior (e.g., via short-distance constraints) is a direction for future improvements.

## Method

The HLbL tensor is decomposed into contributions from the pion pole, a scalar QED (FsQED) box diagram with vertices dressed by pion vector form factors, and a remainder term from ŽĆŽĆ intermediate states. Dispersion relations are derived for the scalar coefficients of a redundant basis of Lorentz structures, with the s-channel discontinuity expressed in terms of partial-wave helicity amplitudes for the process ╬│*╬│* ŌåÆ ŽĆŽĆ. The master formula relates the ŽĆŽĆ contribution to a╬╝ to these partial waves, which are in principle accessible experimentally.

## Context

This work extends the dispersive approach previously applied to hadronic vacuum polarization (HVP) to the more complicated case of hadronic light-by-light scattering, which has so far been evaluated within hadronic models (e.g., Bijnens (1995), Hayakawa (1995), Knecht (2001)). The formalism enables a model-independent treatment of ŽĆŽĆ intermediate states, settling issues raised by Engel (2012) and Bijnens (2012) concerning the dressing of the pion loop, and provides a framework for future inclusion of higher-mass intermediate states and short-distance constraints.
