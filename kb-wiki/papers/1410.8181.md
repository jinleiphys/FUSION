# On the Search for Inhomogeneous Phases in Fermionic Models

> We revisit the Gross-Neveu model with N fermion flavors in 1+1 dimensions and compute its phase diagram at finite temperature and chemical potential in the large-N limit. To this end, we double the number of fermion degrees of freedom in a specific way which allows us to detect inhomogeneous phases in an efficient manner. We show analytically that this "fermion doubling trick" predicts correctly the position of the boundary between the chirally symmetric phase and the phase with broken chiral symmetry. Most importantly, we find that the emergence of an inhomogeneous ground state is predicted correctly. We critically analyze our approach based on this trick and discuss its applicability to other theories, such as fermionic models in higher dimensions, where it may be used to guide the search for inhomogeneous phases.

# On the Search for Inhomogeneous Phases in Fermionic Models

## Key claim

The authors show that a "fermion doubling trick", treating fermion fields at momenta p−Q and p+Q as independent degrees of freedom, correctly predicts the chiral phase boundary and the emergence of an inhomogeneous ground state in the Gross-Neveu model in 1+1 dimensions, but only under the condition that the chiral phase transition is second order and that the ground-state condensate is well-approximated by a single cosine ansatz.

## Method

The authors compute the effective potential of the Gross-Neveu model with N fermion flavors in 1+1 dimensions at finite temperature and chemical potential in the large-N limit, using a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to introduce an auxiliary scalar field σ. They employ a single-cosine ansatz for the background field, σ̄(x) = M cos(2Qx), and integrate out the fermions after artificially doubling the fermion degrees of freedom by treating ψ(p−Q) and ψ(p+Q) as independent fields. The resulting effective potential is renormalized using the vacuum fermion mass M₀ to set the scale, and the phase diagram is obtained by minimizing the potential with respect to M and Q.

## Key numbers

- The vacuum fermion mass M₀ at vanishing temperature and chemical potential sets the scale for all dimensionful quantities; dimensionless ratios O/M₀ are universal numbers independent of the coupling choice.
- The coupling g² decreases logarithmically with the UV cutoff Λ as g² ∼ 1/ln(Λ), consistent with asymptotic freedom.
- For Q ≠ 0, the spatial average factor I = (1/L)∫ cos²(2Qx) dx equals 1/2, compared to 1 for Q = 0.
- Along the chiral phase boundary, the momentum Q(μ) tends to zero continuously as the Lifshitz point is approached, and for large μ ≫ M₀, Q(μ) ∼ μ.

## Context

This work revisits the Gross-Neveu model in 1+1 dimensions, whose exact phase diagram in the large-N limit was previously solved by Thies (2003) and Schnetz et al. (2004, 2005), revealing a chirally symmetric phase, a homogeneous broken-phase, and an inhomogeneous crystal phase separated by second-order transitions meeting at a Lifshitz point. The fermion doubling trick is presented as a computationally efficient method to search for inhomogeneous phases, potentially applicable to higher-dimensional fermionic models such as Nambu-Jona-Lasinio-type models used in QCD phenomenology, where numerical methods are typically required. The approach correctly reproduces the chiral phase boundary and the Lifshitz point position, but fails to reproduce the boundary between the homogeneous and inhomogeneous broken phases, as the single-cosine ansatz becomes insufficient at low temperatures where the exact ground state is a kink-antikink crystal.
