# Reaction-in-Flight Neutrons as a Test of Stopping Power in Degenerate Plasmas

> We present the first measurements of reaction-in-flight (RIF) neutrons in an inertial confinement fusion system. The experiments were carried out at the National Ignition Facility, using both Low Foot and High Foot drives and cryogenic plastic capsules. In both cases, the high-energy RIF ($E_n>$ 15 MeV) component of the neutron spectrum was found to be about $10^{-4}$ of the total. The majority of the RIF neutrons were produced in the dense cold fuel surrounding the burning hotspot of the capsule and the data are consistent with a compressed cold fuel that is moderately to strongly coupled $(Γ\sim$0.6) and electron degenerate $(θ_\mathrm{Fermi}/θ_e\sim$4). The production of RIF neutrons is controlled by the stopping power in the plasma. Thus, the current RIF measurements provide a unique test of stopping power models in an experimentally unexplored plasma regime. We find that the measured RIF data strongly constrain stopping models in warm dense plasma conditions and some models are ruled out by our analysis of these experiments.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first measurements of reaction-in-flight (RIF) neutrons in an inertial confinement fusion system, finding that the RIF signal constrains stopping power models in warm dense plasma conditions and rules out some models; however, the analysis is conditional on using full 3-D simulations constrained by a broad range of experimental data, and the most accurate measurement (shot N140304) favors the Maynard-Deutsch model over the Li-Petrasso model but does not uniquely identify a single correct model.

## Method

The experiments were performed at the National Ignition Facility using cryogenic plastic capsules with both Low Foot and High Foot drives. RIF neutrons with energies above 15 MeV were detected via neutron activation of thulium foils placed 50 cm from the target, using the threshold reaction $^{169}$Tm$(n,3n)^{167}$Tm; the activated foils were shipped to Los Alamos and assayed using two clover Ge detectors with active NaI Compton suppression to measure the 207.79 keV gamma-ray from $^{167}$Tm decay. The measured $^{167}$Tm/$^{168}$Tm atom ratios were compared to predictions from full 3-D HYDRA simulations and 1-D CPT-Implode simulations that varied the stopping power model, with the simulations constrained by x-ray and neutron imaging data.

## Key numbers

- The high-energy RIF component ($E_n > 15$ MeV) was about $10^{-4}$ of the total neutron spectrum for both Low Foot and High Foot drives.
- For the first RIF observation (shot N130331), the measured average $^{167}$Tm/$^{168}$Tm ratio was $(2.0 \pm 0.74) \times 10^{-5}$ using 2 mm thick foils.
- For the most accurate measurement (shot N140304) using 0.5 mm foils, the measured $^{167}$Tm/$^{168}$Tm ratio was $(1.69 \pm 0.24) \times 10^{-5}$.
- The cold fuel plasma conditions were characterized by a coupling parameter $\Gamma \sim 0.6$ and electron degeneracy ratio $\theta_\mathrm{Fermi}/\theta_e \sim 4$ (with a range of $\Gamma \sim 0.3-1.2$ and $\theta_\mathrm{Fermi}/\theta_e \sim 2.3-5.0$ across the cold fuel).
- For shot N140304, the Maynard-Deutsch model predicted $1.59 \times 10^{-5}$, the Li-Petrasso model predicted $1.16 \times 10^{-5}$, and the Grabowski model predicted $0.89 \times 10^{-5}$ for the $^{167}$Tm/$^{168}$Tm ratio.
- For shot N130331, the Corman-Spitzer model predicted $0.55 \times 10^{-5}$ compared to the measured $(2.0 \pm 0.74) \times 10^{-5}$.
- The cold fuel density was in the range $10^{25}$-$10^{26}$ cm$^{-3}$ and temperature was a fraction of a keV.
- The $^{167}$Tm half-life used for identification was 9.25 days, and the 207.79 keV gamma-ray was emitted 41% of the time.

## Context

This work extends the study of stopping powers from weakly-coupled plasmas, where models such as Brown-Preston-Singleton (BPS) (1991), Maynard-Deutsch (1985), and Li-Petrasso (1993) are known to agree, into the previously unexplored regime of moderately to strongly coupled and degenerate plasmas found in the cold fuel of cryogenic NIF capsules. The measurements rule out the Corman-Spitzer (1972) and Grabowski (1980) models and show that the BPS model breaks down under these conditions. The results enable future direct measurements of RIF spectra by neutron time-of-flight to place even more stringent constraints on energy loss in strongly coupled systems, particularly regarding the effects of plasma degeneracy.
