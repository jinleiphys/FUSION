# Energy density functional in nuclear physics

> Fundamentals of energy density functional in nuclear physics are presented. Much attention is paid to a mathematically rigorous treatment of deriving the energy density functional. The specific features of the density functional used in studying many-nucleon systems, which is quite different from that used in many-electron systems, are also shown. The intended audience are physicists, chemists and mathematicians. In particular those who will start to study the density functional theory are intended.

## Key claim

This pedagogical paper presents a mathematically rigorous derivation of the nuclear energy density functional from the zero-range Skyrme interaction, emphasizing that the density functional formalism for many-nucleon systems differs fundamentally from that used in many-electron systems because the nuclear force is not perfectly known and must be modelled through an effective Hamiltonian whose parameters are determined phenomenologically.

## Method

The authors start from the zero-range Skyrme nucleon-nucleon interaction, which includes central, non-local, tensor, spin-orbit, and density-dependent terms, and expand it in powers of relative momenta up to quadratic order. They then calculate the energy expectation value using Slater determinants of single-particle wave functions, applying the fermionic relation \(P_r P_\sigma P_\tau = -1\) and assuming no isospin mixing, to derive the Hamiltonian density in terms of local densities (particle density, kinetic energy density, spin density, current density, spin-current density, and spin kinetic energy density). The variational principle is applied using Gateaux differentials in infinite-dimensional Hilbert/Banach spaces to obtain the effective interaction in the density functional formalism.

## Key numbers

* The nucleon-nucleon interaction is decomposed into nuclear potential \(V_K\), Coulomb potential \(V_C\), and pairing potential \(V_{\rm pair}\): \(V = V_K + V_C + V_{\rm pair}\).
* The two-body Skyrme interaction contains parameters \(t_0\), \(t_1\), \(t_2\), \(t_e\), \(t_o\) for central, non-local, and tensor terms, plus \(W_0\) for the spin-orbit term and \(t_3\) with exponent \(\alpha\) for the density-dependent term.
* The zero-range interaction is expanded with a low-momentum cut-off up to quadratic terms in \({\bm k}\) and \({\bm k}'\), valid for energies below the Fermi energy.
* The Hamiltonian density is expressed using six local densities: \(\rho({\bm r})\), \(\tau({\bm r})\), \(s_{\mu}({\bm r})\), \(j_{\mu}({\bm r})\), \(J_{\mu\nu}({\bm r})\), and \(T_{\mu}({\bm r})\), where \(\mu,\nu\) index Cartesian coordinates \(x,y,z\).

## Context

This work provides a pedagogical introduction to the nuclear energy density functional formalism, extending the foundational approach of Hohenberg and Kohn (1964) for many-electron systems to the nuclear many-body problem. The derivation follows the Skyrme interaction framework originally developed by Skyrme (1956) and later refined by Vautherin and Brink (1972) and Engel et al. (1975), and it enables systematic construction of effective Hamiltonians for describing both stationary and non-stationary states of atomic nuclei, including studies of nuclear shapes, superheavy elements, and the origin of chemical elements.
