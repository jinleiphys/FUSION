# Critical test of multi-{\it j} supersymmetries from magnetic moment measurements

> Magnetic moment measurements in odd nuclei directly probe the distribution of fermion states and hence provide one of the most critical tests for multi-$j$ supersymmetries in collective nuclei. Due to complexity of calculations and lack of data, such tests have not been performed in the past. Using the Mathematica software, we derive analytic expressions for magnetic moments in the $SO^{(BF)}(6) \times SU^{(F)}(2)$ limit of the $U(6/12)$ supersymmetry and compare the results with recent measurements in $^{195}$Pt.

## Key claim

Magnetic moment (g-factor) measurements provide a more critical test of multi-\(j\) supersymmetry wave functions in collective nuclei than energies, E2 transitions, or transfer reactions because the M1 operator has no free parameters and the g-factors of different orbitals differ significantly; however, the agreement between the parameter-free supersymmetry predictions and the experimental data in \(^{195}\)Pt is remarkable overall, except for the ground state where a significant deviation occurs.

## Method

The authors derived analytic expressions for magnetic moments in the \(SO^{(BF)}(6) \times SU^{(F)}(2)\) limit of the \(U(6/12)\) supersymmetry using Mathematica to evaluate the expectation value of the M1 operator in the boson-fermion product states. The M1 operator was expressed in terms of boson and single-particle angular momentum operators with g-factors \(g_B\) (determined from the even-even partner \(^{194}\)Pt) and \(g_j\) (from single-particle Schmidt values with a quenching factor of 0.6). The resulting formulas were then compared with recent g-factor measurements in \(^{195}\)Pt.

## Key numbers

* The boson g-factor was determined as \(g_B = 0.3\) from the even-even nucleus \(^{194}\)Pt (Stuchbery 1991).
* The spin g-factor for neutrons was taken as \(g_s = -2.3\), using the standard quenching factor of 0.6 relative to the free value.
* For the ground state of \(^{195}\)Pt (0 keV, \(J=1/2\)), the experimental g-factor is 1.22, while the supersymmetry prediction is 0.77.
* For the stretched state at 239 keV (\(J=5/2\)) in the \((7,0,0)\) representation, the experimental g-factor is 0.25(4) versus the theoretical 0.40.
* For the stretched state at 667 keV (\(J=9/2\)) in the \((7,0,0)\) representation, the experimental g-factor is 0.34(4) versus the theoretical 0.35.
* For the stretched state at 130 keV (\(J=5/2\)) in the \((6,1,0)\) representation, the experimental g-factor is 0.36(3) versus the theoretical 0.36.
* For the stretched state at 563 keV (\(J=9/2\)) in the \((6,1,0)\) representation, the experimental g-factor is 0.34(3) versus the theoretical 0.35.

## Context

This work extends the multi-\(j\) supersymmetry framework of Balantekin, Bars, Bijker, and Iachello (1983) by providing the first parameter-free test of the wave functions using magnetic moments, which had previously been deemed too complicated to calculate. Prior tests of the \(U(6/12)\) supersymmetry in \(^{195}\)Pt relied on level energies, E2 transition rates, and one-nucleon transfer reactions (Warner 1982, Sun 1983, Vergnes 1983, Bruce 1985, Mauthofer 1986), but these are less sensitive to single-particle distributions. The results enable future g-factor measurements and similar tests in other nuclei that evidence supersymmetry, and they motivate further numerical studies of two-body M1 operators to resolve the ground-state discrepancy.
