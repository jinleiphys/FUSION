# Directed flow of charged particles within idealized viscous hydrodynamics at energies available at the BNL Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider and at the CERN Large Hadron Collider

> Following the Boz$\dot{\textrm{e}}$k-Wyskiel parametrization tilted initial condition, an alternative way to construct a longitudinal tilted fireball based on the Glauber collision geometry is presented. This longitudinal tilted initial condition combined with the Ideal-CLVisc (3 + 1)D hydrodynamic model, a nonvanishing directed flow coefficient $v_{1}$ in a wide rapidity range is observed. After comparing the model's results with experimentally observed data of directed flow coefficient $v_{1}(η)$ from $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 200 GeV Cu + Cu, Au + Au collisions at RHIC energy to $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 2.76 TeV and $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 5.02 TeV Pb + Pb collisions at the LHC energy. We find that the directed flow measurements in heavy-ion collisions can set strong constraints on the imbalance of forward and backward incoming nuclei and on the magnitude asymmetry of pressure gradients along the $x$ direction.

## Key claim

The authors present an alternative parametrization for constructing a longitudinally tilted fireball initial condition based on Glauber collision geometry, and show that when combined with ideal (3+1)D hydrodynamics, this tilted source produces a nonvanishing directed flow coefficient \(v_{1}\) across a wide rapidity range. The claim is hedged by the fact that the model uses ideal hydrodynamics (no viscosity) and a free parameter \(H_{t}\) that must be tuned to match experimental data, with the directed flow coefficient being sensitive to this parameter.

## Method

The authors modify the wounded-nucleon weight function in the optical Glauber model by adding a term proportional to \(H_{t}[T_{2}(x,y)-T_{1}(x,y)]\tan(\eta_{s}/\eta_{t})\), where \(H_{t}\) is a free parameter controlling the forward-backward imbalance and \(\eta_{t}=8.0\) is a constant. This tilted initial energy density is evolved using the Ideal-CLVisc (3+1)D hydrodynamic model with the Kurganov-Tadmor algorithm on a GPU, using the Wuppertal-Budapest lattice QCD equation of state and an isothermal freeze-out temperature of 137 MeV. The directed flow coefficient \(v_{1}(\eta)\) is computed from the Cooper-Frye formula for charged particles and pions, and compared to experimental data from STAR and ALICE at multiple collision energies.

## Key numbers

- Maximum energy density \(\varepsilon_{0}\) values used: 83.5 GeV/fm\(^{3}\) for Cu+Cu at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200\) GeV; 155.5 GeV/fm\(^{3}\) for Au+Au at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200\) GeV; 465.0 GeV/fm\(^{3}\) for Pb+Pb at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76\) TeV; 580.0 GeV/fm\(^{3}\) for Pb+Pb at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=5.02\) TeV (all at initial proper time \(\tau_{0}=0.2\) fm)
- Tilted parameter \(H_{t}\) values extracted from data: \(H_{t}=1.0\) for Au+Au at 200 GeV; \(H_{t}=0.7\) for Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV (10-20% centrality); \(H_{t}=0.8\) for Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV (30-40% centrality)
- Maximum pressure gradient \(-\partial_{x}P\) in Au+Au at 200 GeV: approximately 16 GeV/fm\(^{4}\) on the \(x>0\) side and 14 GeV/fm\(^{4}\) on the \(x<0\) side at \(\eta_{s}=2.1\)
- Maximum pressure gradient \(-\partial_{x}P\) in Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV: approximately 48 GeV/fm\(^{4}\) on the \(x>0\) side and 40 GeV/fm\(^{4}\) on the \(x<0\) side at \(\eta_{s}=2.1\)
- Inelastic cross sections used: 40 mb for \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200\) GeV; 64 mb for \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76\) TeV; 67 mb for \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=5.02\) TeV
- Hydrodynamic grid: \(N_{x}\times N_{y}\times N_{\eta_{s}}=201\times 201\times 105\) cells run for 1600 time steps

## Context

This work extends the Bozek-Wyskiel parametrization for tilted initial conditions (Bozek 2010, 2011) by providing an alternative Glauber-based construction that introduces a phenomenological parameter \(H_{t}\) to control the forward-backward asymmetry. The results are compared to directed flow measurements from the STAR Collaboration at RHIC (Abelev 2008) and the ALICE Collaboration at the LHC (Abelev 2013, Acharya 2019). The study enables future work incorporating viscous corrections, event-by-event fluctuations (such as the T\(_{\mathrm{R}}\)ENTo-3D model by Ke 2016), magnetic field effects (as studied by Inghirami 2019 and Gursoy 2014, 2018), and hadronic cascade afterburners like UrQMD or SMASH to improve comparisons with experimental data.
