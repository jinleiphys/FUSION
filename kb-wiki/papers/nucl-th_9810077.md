# Reaction dynamics in Pb+Pb at the CERN/SPS: from partonic degrees of freedom to freeze-out

> We analyze the reaction dynamics of central Pb+Pb collisions at 160 GeV/nucleon. First we estimate the energy density pile-up at mid-rapidity and calculate its excitation function: The energy density is decomposed into hadronic and partonic contributions. A detailed analysis of the collision dynamics in the framework of a microscopic transport model shows the importance of partonic degrees of freedom and rescattering of leading (di)quarks in the early phase of the reaction for E > 30 GeV/nucleon. The energy density reaches up to 4 GeV/fm^3, 95% of which are contained in partonic degrees of freedom. It is shown that cells of hadronic matter, after the early reaction phase, can be viewed as nearly chemically equilibrated. This matter never exceeds energy densities of 0.4 GeV/fm^3, i.e. a density above which the notion of separated hadrons loses its meaning. The final reaction stage is analyzed in terms of hadron ratios, freeze-out distributions and a source analysis for final state pions.

# Reaction dynamics in Pb+Pb at the CERN/SPS: from partonic degrees of freedom to freeze-out

## Key claim

Partonic degrees of freedom dominate the early phase of central Pb+Pb collisions at 160 GeV/nucleon, but the authors explicitly state that this does not imply an equilibrated Quark-Gluon-Plasma has been formed, because the "partonic" phase in their UrQMD model is an incoherent superposition of non-interacting partonic constituents that retain their original correlation into hadrons, with deconfinement not implemented.

## Method

The authors used the UrQMD (Ultra-relativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics) microscopic transport model to simulate central Pb+Pb collisions at 160 GeV/nucleon. Energy density was decomposed into "partonic" contributions (from hadrons within their formation time, including leading diquarks and newly produced partons) and "hadronic" contributions (from fully formed hadrons), calculated by summing over Gaussian wave packets of hadrons around mid-rapidity (y_cm ± 1). Thermodynamic quantities (temperature T and baryonic chemical potential μ_B) were extracted by fitting a statistical model to energy, baryon, and strangeness densities in a central cell (2×2×1 fm³).

## Key numbers

- Maximum total energy density at mid-rapidity reaches 4 GeV/fm³ at t ≈ 1 fm/c for central Pb+Pb at 160 GeV/nucleon
- 95% of the energy density at peak is contained in partonic degrees of freedom
- Hadronic matter in the local thermal equilibrium phase never exceeds energy densities of ~0.4 GeV/fm³ (350 MeV/fm³)
- Without rapidity cut, energy densities during early reaction stage can reach 20 GeV/fm³
- At beam energy of 40 GeV/nucleon, more than 90% of the energy density is contained in partonic degrees of freedom at one time during the collision
- Local thermal equilibrium in the central cell is first reached at t ≈ 10 fm/c
- Statistical model fit to final hadron ratios yields T = 140 MeV and μ_B = 210 MeV
- Approximately 80% of final pions stem from resonance decays, 20% from direct string fragmentation
- 20% of all pions scatter elastically after their last inelastic interaction before freeze-out

## Context

This work extends the analysis of relativistic heavy-ion collisions using microscopic transport models, building on the UrQMD framework described in Bass (1998) and related approaches such as Quantum Molecular Dynamics by Peilert (1988), Hartnack (1989), and Sorge (1989). The study provides a detailed decomposition of energy density into partonic and hadronic contributions across beam energies from 2 to 200 GeV/nucleon, addressing the question of whether hadronic transport models remain valid at CERN/SPS energies where Lattice Gauge Theory estimates (Blum 1995, Boyd 1995, Laermann 1996) suggest energy densities exceed the critical density for deconfinement. The results enable future comparisons with parton cascade models (Geiger 1992) and motivate improved treatment of internal hadron dynamics during formation time (Gerland 1998), as well as experimental searches for signatures of the mixed phase regime around 40 GeV/nucleon.
