# Path optimization method for the sign problem

> We propose a path optimization method (POM) to evade the sign problem in the Monte-Carlo calculations for complex actions. Among many approaches to the sign problem, the Lefschetz-thimble path-integral method and the complex Langevin method are promising and extensively discussed. In these methods, real field variables are complexified and the integration manifold is determined by the flow equations or stochastically sampled. When we have singular points of the action or multiple critical points near the original integral surface, however, we have a risk to encounter the residual and global sign problems or the singular drift term problem. One of the ways to avoid the singular points is to optimize the integration path which is designed not to hit the singular points of the Boltzmann weight. By specifying the one-dimensional integration-path as $z=t+if(t) (f\in \mathbb{R})$ and by optimizing $f(t)$ to enhance the average phase factor, we demonstrate that we can avoid the sign problem in a one-variable toy model for which the complex Langevin method is found to fail. In this proceedings, we propose POM and discuss how we can avoid the sign problem in a toy model. We also discuss the possibility to utilize the neural network to optimize the path.

## Key claim

The Path Optimization Method (POM) can evade the sign problem in Monte-Carlo calculations for complex actions by variationally optimizing the integration path to enhance the average phase factor, even in cases where the complex Langevin method fails, provided the optimized path does not hit singular points of the Boltzmann weight.

## Method

The authors parameterize the one-dimensional integration path as \(z(t) = t + i f(t)\) with \(f \in \mathbb{R}\), define a cost function that reflects the seriousness of the sign problem (based on the deviation of the local phase from the global phase), and optimize the path parameters using the steepest descent method to minimize the cost function. For the toy model, integration is performed using the double exponential formula, and expectation values are computed via hybrid Monte-Carlo sampling on the optimized path, with symmetric sampling of \(\pm t\) points based on reflection symmetry of the phase-quenched statistical weight.

## Key numbers

- For the toy model with \(p = 50\) and \(\alpha = 10\), the optimized path avoids the singular point at \(z = -i\alpha\) and goes through two fixed points, where the effective action barrier height is approximately 20 (in units of \(\mathrm{Re}\,S_\mathrm{eff}\)) relative to the minimum at the fixed points.
- The expectation value of \(\mathrm{Re}\,x^2\) for \(p = 50\) agrees with exact results even in the severe sign problem region, including at \(\alpha \simeq 9.95\) and \(\alpha \simeq 10.59\) where the partition function vanishes and the expectation value diverges.
- At \(\alpha = 15\) with \(p = 50\), there is only one relevant thimble and the sign problem is less serious; at \(\alpha = 10\) with \(p = 50\), two thimbles are relevant and both terminate at the singular point.

## Context

This work extends the Lefschetz-thimble method (Witten 2010, Cristoforetti 2012, Fujii 2013) and the complex Langevin method (Parisi 1980, Parisi 1984, Aarts 2009) by providing an alternative variational approach that does not require solving flow equations or finding fixed points in advance. The toy model was introduced by Nishimura (2015) to demonstrate where complex Langevin fails. The method enables future applications to systems with larger degrees of freedom, such as the \(\phi^4\) theory (Mori 2017), and potentially to finite-density QCD, where the sign problem remains a major obstacle.
