# Many-Body Physics: Collective fermionic excitations in quark-gluon plasmas and cold atom systems

> In this talk I discuss collective excitations that carry fermion quantum numbers. Such excitations occur in the quark-gluon plasma and can also be produced in cold atom systems under special conditions.

## Key claim

Collective fermionic excitations (the "plasmino" and the "goldstino") exist in both the quark-gluon plasma and cold atom systems, but their existence depends on specific conditions: in the QGP, the goldstino requires a non-zero difference between the asymptotic thermal masses of bosons and fermions (\(\delta m^2 \neq 0\)), while in cold atoms, the goldstino appears when the chemical potentials of the two fermion species are equal (\(\Delta\mu = 0\)) and the interaction strengths are matched (\(U_{\rm bb} = U_{\rm bf}\)).

## Method

The paper uses the hard thermal loop (HTL) approximation to compute the retarded fermion self-energy in a weakly coupled quark-gluon plasma at high temperature, deriving the dispersion relations of soft (\(p \sim gT\)) and ultra-soft (\(p \ll g^2 T\)) fermionic modes. For cold atom systems, a schematic model on an optical lattice with two fermion species (\(f\), \(F\)) and a bosonic bound state (\(b\)) is introduced, and the supercharge correlator is computed in the random phase approximation (RPA) by resumming boson-fermion bubble graphs.

## Key numbers

- The soft scale in the QGP is \(\kappa \sim gT\), where the expansion parameter \(\gamma_{gT} \sim g\).
- The ultra-soft scale is \(\kappa \sim g^2 T\), where \(\gamma_{g^2 T} \sim 1\) and perturbation theory breaks down.
- The asymptotic thermal mass difference is \(\delta m^2 = m_b^2 - m_f^2 \sim g^2 T^2\).
- The plasmino at zero momentum has poles at \(\omega_\pm = \pm \omega_0\), with \(\omega_0 \sim gT\).
- The goldstino in the cold atom system has a dispersion relation \(\omega = \Delta\mu + \alpha p^2\), becoming massless when \(\Delta\mu = 0\).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical identification of fermionic collective modes in the quark-gluon plasma, originally studied via HTL methods by Blaizot (1993) and others, and connects them to analogous phenomena in cold atom systems proposed by Shi (2009). The goldstino interpretation builds on earlier work by Lebedev (1989) on spontaneous supersymmetry breaking at finite temperature, and the schematic model for the split dispersion relation is inspired by Brown's schematic model for giant resonances in nuclei. The results enable experimental searches for goldstino-like modes in optical lattices and deepen the analogy between QCD matter and condensed matter systems.
