# Entanglement and accidental symmetries in the nucleon-nucleon system

> We study the connection between accidental symmetries in the nuclear interaction and spin entanglement in two-nucleon scattering. Specifically, we incorporate different levels of Wigner $SU(4)$ and Serber symmetries into leading-order potentials derived from chiral effective field theory. We conduct a quantitative analysis by computing the full $S$ matrix, demonstrating that the neutron-proton spin entanglement can be related to the symmetry properties of the interaction and the presence of certain operators and partial waves. Furthermore, we study the order-by-order evolution of the spin entanglement, up to next-to-next-to-leading order in Weinberg power counting, for both neutron-proton and neutron-neutron scattering. Entanglement suppression is not observed in neutron-neutron scattering, which can be attributed to the Pauli principle and the absence of accidental symmetries in this system. We conclude that entanglement is a useful guide for studying the power counting and symmetries in nuclear interactions derived from effective field theories.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that spin entanglement in neutron-proton scattering is directly linked to the breaking of accidental Wigner SU(4) and Serber symmetries in the nuclear interaction, with entanglement suppression occurring only when both short-distance and long-distance Wigner symmetry are simultaneously enforced; the authors do not observe entanglement suppression in neutron-neutron scattering, which they attribute to the Pauli principle and the absence of accidental symmetries in that system.

## Method

The authors constructed five modified versions of the leading-order Weinberg power counting chiral potential by selectively imposing Wigner SU(4) symmetry (equal S-wave contact terms and removal of the tensor operator from one-pion exchange) and Serber symmetry (zeroing odd-parity partial waves). They computed the full S-matrix via partial-wave decomposition of the Lippmann-Schwinger equation, truncating the sum over total angular momentum at J=50, and evaluated the entanglement power of the M-matrix using Monte Carlo integration over initial spin states with 1000 sampling points. The low-energy constants were tuned by least-squares fits to single Nijmegen I phase shifts at specific laboratory energies.

## Key numbers

- Entanglement power vanishes for the fully Wigner- and Serber-symmetric potential V^(1)_(sym,sym) at all momenta and scattering angles
- Entanglement power is highly suppressed for momenta between approximately 50 MeV and 150 MeV for all np potentials studied
- The tensor force from one-pion exchange is identified as the main source of entanglement for relative momenta p ≳ 150 MeV
- Low-energy entanglement (p ≲ 50 MeV) vanishes only when both short-distance Wigner symmetry (equal S-wave contacts) and long-distance Wigner symmetry are implemented
- The M-matrix at very low energies is proportional to the identity when the singlet and triplet scattering lengths are equal, producing zero entanglement
- The cutoff momentum used for regulating all potentials is Λ = 500 MeV
- The sum over total angular momentum J in the M-matrix calculation is truncated at J = 50

## Context

This work extends the earlier finding by Beane (2018) that spin-entanglement suppression in S-wave neutron-proton scattering is connected to Wigner SU(4) symmetry, moving beyond the S-wave approximation to study the full S-matrix across all scattering angles and energies up to 350 MeV. The study also builds on previous work by Calle Cordon (2008, 2009) regarding Wigner SU(4) as a long-distance symmetry for even-parity partial waves, and by Bai (2022, 2023) who studied spin entanglement using phenomenological models and the PWA93 model. The results enable systematic investigation of how accidental symmetries emerge from effective field theory power counting and provide a new diagnostic tool for studying nuclear interactions across different kinematic regimes.
