# Recent open heavy flavor studies for the Electron-Ion Collider

> The future Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) will operate a series of high-luminosity high-energy electron+proton ($e+p$) and electron+nucleus ($\textit{e + A}$) collisions to study several fundamental questions in the high energy and nuclear physics field. Heavy flavor hadron and jet production at the EIC plays an important role in exploring both potential modification on the initial-state nuclear parton distribution functions (nPDFs) and final-state parton propagation and hadronization processes under different nuclear medium conditions. The current design of the EIC ePIC detector has good performance of vertex and track reconstruction, particle identification and energy determination in the pseudorapidity region of $-3.5<η<3.5$, which will enable a series of high precision heavy flavor hadron and jet measurements. Latest simulation studies of the projected nuclear modification factor $R_{eA}$ of heavy flavor jets and heavy flavor hadron inside jets in $e+p$ and $\textit{e + Au}$ collisions at $\sqrt{s} =$ 28.6 GeV and 63.2 GeV as well as the projected statistical accuracy of inclusive and differential charm baryon over meson ratio measurements in $e+p$ collisions will be presented. The impacts of these proposed EIC measurements on constraining the heavy quark propagation properties in cold nuclear medium and exploring the heavy quark hadronization process will be discussed.

# Recent open heavy flavor studies for the Electron-Ion Collider

## Key claim
The future EIC will enable high-precision heavy flavor hadron and jet measurements that can constrain heavy quark energy loss and hadronization in cold nuclear matter, but only if the ePIC detector performance meets the requirements from the EIC yellow report and the projected statistical uncertainties are realized with the assumed integrated luminosities of 10 fb⁻¹ for e+p and 500 pb⁻¹ for e+Au collisions.

## Method
The study uses a standalone simulation framework based on Deeply Inelastic Scattering event generation in PYTHIA8, with parameterized ePIC detector response derived from GEANT4 simulations applied by smearing true particle information. Heavy flavor hadrons (D±, B±, D0, Λc±) and jets are reconstructed using the anti-kT algorithm with cone radius R=1.0, with jet flavor tagging based on reconstructed decay and secondary vertex characteristics. Projected nuclear modification factors ReA and charm baryon over meson ratios are calculated for e+p and e+Au collisions at center-of-mass energies of 28.6 GeV and 63.2 GeV.

## Key numbers
- EIC will operate at center-of-mass energies of 20–141 GeV, with instantaneous luminosity of 10³³⁻³⁴ cm⁻²s⁻¹
- ePIC detector covers pseudorapidity region −3.5 < η < 3.5, with central detector spanning 9.5 m longitudinally by 3.3 m horizontally
- Jets are reconstructed with cone radius R=1.0; majority of reconstructed jets in 63.2 GeV collisions have low pT coverage of 5–20 GeV/c
- Projected light- and charm-jet ReAu measurements achieve great precision with pT < 10 GeV/c in 28.6 GeV collisions; pT coverage extends to pT < 17 GeV/c in 63.2 GeV collisions
- Better than 8% statistical uncertainties can be achieved for reconstructed B± inside jet ReAu measurements with 10 fb⁻¹ e+p and 500 pb⁻¹ e+Au integrated luminosities
- A 25 mrad crossing angle is introduced at IP6 to enhance delivery luminosity and mitigate beam backgrounds

## Context
This work extends the heavy flavor program from RHIC and LHC heavy ion measurements (which explored flavor-dependent energy loss in hot nuclear matter) to the cold nuclear medium environment of the EIC. The inclusive charm baryon over meson ratio results from p+p, p+A, and A+A collisions at RHIC and the LHC (e.g., Λc/D0) challenged the universality of charm quark fragmentation functions, and the EIC measurements aim to extract differential fragmentation functions directly. The projected ReA measurements for heavy flavor hadrons inside jets will discriminate between the parton energy loss model (where hadronization occurs outside the nucleus) and the absorption model (where hadronization occurs inside the nucleus), building on theoretical work by Vitev, Ke, and Kang. These studies will also compensate kinematic coverage gaps from previous HERA results and improve understanding of current and future heavy ion measurements at RHIC and the LHC.
