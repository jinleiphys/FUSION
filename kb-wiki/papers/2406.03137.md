# Strange Dwarfs: a review on the (in)stability

> White dwarfs are the remnants of stars not massive enough to become supernovae. This review explores the concept of strange dwarfs, a unique class of white dwarfs which contain cores of strange quark matter. Strange dwarfs have different sizes, masses, and evolutionary paths with respect to white dwarfs. They might form through the accumulation of normal matter on strange quark stars or by capture of strangelets. The stability of strange dwarfs has been debated, with initial studies suggesting stability, while later analyses indicated potential instability. This review revisits these discussions, focusing on the critical role of boundary conditions between nuclear and quark matter in determining stability. It also offers insights into their formation, structure, and possible detection in the universe.

# Strange Dwarfs: a review on the (in)stability

## Key claim

The apparent contradiction between earlier studies claiming strange dwarfs are stable (Glendenning 1994) and later work claiming they are unstable (Alford 2017) is resolved by recognizing that these studies used different boundary conditions at the interface between nuclear matter and quark matter; the stability of a strange dwarf depends critically on whether the phase transition between hadrons and quarks is treated as slow (quark content of the core remains constant during oscillations) or rapid (quark content can change during oscillations), with the slow case yielding stability and the rapid case yielding instability.

## Method

The authors construct equations of state for strange dwarfs by combining the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland (BPS) equation of state for the nuclear matter envelope with a quark matter equation of state based on the MIT bag model, using a piecewise function with a transition pressure at the interface. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations using two different parameter pairs: fixed transition pressure (allowing the quark baryon number in the core to vary) and fixed quark baryon number in the core (allowing the transition pressure to vary). They then analyze radial oscillations by solving the perturbed fluid and metric equations, applying either slow or rapid boundary conditions at the hadron-quark interface, and compute the eigenvalues of the fundamental radial mode.

## Key numbers

- The Chandrasekhar mass for the BPS equation of state is approximately 1 solar mass, lower than the typical 1.4 solar masses for carbon-oxygen white dwarfs or 1.2 solar masses for oxygen-magnesium-neon white dwarfs.
- The BPS fit ranges in energy density from approximately 7 g/cm³ to approximately 4 × 10¹¹ g/cm³, with the neutron drip density at about 4 × 10¹¹ g/cm³.
- For the slow transition scenario, a strange dwarf with mass approximately 0.02 solar masses, core baryon number approximately 2.69 × 10⁵⁵, and transition density at the neutron drip density shows a stable fundamental mode with ω² = 0.788275 Hz².
- For the rapid transition scenario, the same star configuration shows an unstable fundamental mode with ω² = -1.62785 Hz².
- The maximum mass for strange dwarfs with core baryon number 10⁵⁵ approaches approximately 0.996 solar masses.
- For core baryon numbers greater than approximately 10⁴⁶, the e-folding time of the mechanical instability falls below 1 second, allowing collapse to occur faster than the development of a deflagration (which takes several seconds).
- When the core baryon number exceeds approximately 10⁵², the boundary density deviates from approximately 10⁹ g/cm³ (the typical central density of a white dwarf at the Chandrasekhar mass).

## Context

This review extends the debate between Glendenning (1994), who found strange dwarfs stable, and Alford (2017), who found them unstable, by showing that the discrepancy arises from different assumptions about boundary conditions at the hadron-quark interface, as previously analyzed by Di Clemente (2022). The work builds on the formalism for phase transitions in compact stars developed by Pereira (2017) and Di Clemente (2020), and on the distinction between slow and rapid phase transitions introduced by Thorne. The results enable predictions for accretion-induced collapse scenarios: strange dwarfs with sufficiently large quark cores (B_core > 10⁴⁶) could collapse faster than the nuclear deflagration timescale, potentially explaining the observed absence of accretion-induced collapse events, in contrast to the standard type Ia supernova pathway described by Canal (1990).
