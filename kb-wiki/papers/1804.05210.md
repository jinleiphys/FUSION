# Non-resistive dissipative magnetohydrodynamics from the Boltzmann equation in the 14-moment approximation

> We derive the equations of motion of relativistic, non-resistive, second-order dissipative magnetohydrodynamics from the Boltzmann equation using the method of moments. We assume the fluid to be composed of a single type of point-like particles with vanishing dipole moment or spin, so that the fluid has vanishing magnetization and polarization. In a first approximation, we assume the fluid to be non-resistive, which allows to express the electric field in terms of the magnetic field. We derive equations of motion for the irreducible moments of the deviation of the single-particle distribution function from local thermodynamical equilibrium. We analyze the Navier-Stokes limit of these equations, reproducing previous results for the structure of the first-order transport coefficients. Finally, we truncate the system of equations for the irreducible moments using the 14-moment approximation, deriving the equations of motion of relativistic, non-resistive, second-order dissipative magnetohydrodynamics. We also give expressions for the new transport coefficients appearing due to the coupling of the magnetic field to the dissipative quantities.

## Key claim

The authors derive a causal, second-order dissipative magnetohydrodynamic theory for a non-resistive (infinite electrical conductivity) fluid from the Boltzmann equation using the 14-moment approximation, but they explicitly note that the assumption of infinite conductivity is an idealization inconsistent with the finite mean free path required for the Boltzmann equation's collision term, and they plan to address finite conductivity in a subsequent paper.

## Method

The authors start from the relativistic Boltzmann equation for a single-component gas of spinless, point-like particles undergoing binary elastic collisions, coupled to an external electromagnetic field. They assume infinite electrical conductivity, which forces the electric field four-vector to vanish and allows the electric field to be expressed in terms of the magnetic field. Using the method of moments, they derive equations of motion for the irreducible moments of the deviation of the single-particle distribution function from local thermodynamic equilibrium, then truncate the infinite hierarchy using the 14-moment approximation to obtain closed second-order equations for the dissipative quantities (bulk viscous pressure, particle diffusion current, and shear-stress tensor) in the presence of a magnetic field.

## Key numbers

* The thermal Larmor radius is defined as \(R_T \equiv (\textswab{q} B \beta_0)^{-1}\), where \(\beta_0 = 1/T\) is the inverse temperature, and the condition for neglecting Landau quantization is \(T^2 \gg \textswab{q} B\) (i.e., the thermal wavelength \(\beta_0 \ll R_T\)).
* The dimensionless parameter \(\xi_B \equiv \lambda_{\text{mfp}}/R_T = \textswab{q} B \beta_0 \lambda_{\text{mfp}}\) is introduced to characterize the relative strength of collisional and magnetic effects, with no a priori constraint on its value as long as \(\lambda_{\text{mfp}} \gg \sqrt{\sigma/\pi}\) and \(R_T \gg \beta_0\).
* The electromagnetic energy-momentum tensor in the non-resistive limit is \(T_B^{\mu\nu} = (B^2/2)(u^\mu u^\nu - \Delta^{\mu\nu} - 2 b^\mu b^\nu)\), where \(B^2 = -B^\mu B_\mu\) and \(b^\mu = B^\mu/B\).

## Context

This work extends the Israel-Stewart second-order formalism (Israel 1976, Stewart 1977, Israel 1979) for relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics to include magnetic fields, following the same 14-moment approximation framework developed by Denicol, Niemi, Molnár, and Rischke (2010, 2012). It reproduces previous results for first-order (Navier-Stokes) transport coefficients in a magnetic field, such as those from Huang (2009, 2011), Critelli (2014), Finazzo (2016), and Hernandez (2017). The derivation enables future studies of dissipative magnetohydrodynamic effects in heavy-ion collisions, where large magnetic fields exist (Skokov 2009, Deng 2012, Tuchin 2013, Bzdak 2011), and provides the foundation for a subsequent treatment of finite electrical conductivity.
