# Measurement of the pi^+ meson polarizabilities via the gamma p->gamma pi^+ n reaction

> An experiment on the radiative pi^+ meson photoproduction from the proton (gamma p->gamma pi^+ n) was carried out at the Mainz Microtron MAMI in the kinematic region 537 MeV <E_{gamma}<817 MeV, 140^o<theta_{gamma-gamma'}^cm<180^o. The pi^+ meson polarizabilities have been determined from a comparison of the data with the predictions of two different theoretical models, the first one being based on an effective pole model with pseudoscalar coupling while the second one is based on diagrams describing both resonant and nonresonant contributions. The validity of the models has been verified by comparing the predictions with the present experimental data in the kinematic region where the pion polarizability contribution is negligible (s_1<5 mu^2) and where the difference between the predictions of the two models does not exceed 3%. In the region, where the pion polarizability contribution is substantial (5<s_1/mu^2<15, -12<t/mu^2<-2), the difference (alpha-beta)_{pi^+} of the electric (alpha) and the magnetic (beta) polarizabilities has been determined. As a result we find: (alpha-beta)_{pi^+}=(11.6\pm 1.5_{stat}\pm 3.0_{syst}\pm 0.5_{mod})x10^-4fm^3. This result is at variance with recent calculations in the framework of chiral perturbation theory.

## Key claim

The difference of the electric and magnetic polarizabilities of the charged pion is measured as (α-β)π⁺ = (11.6 ± 1.5_stat ± 3.0_syst ± 0.5_mod) × 10⁻⁴ fm³, a value that disagrees with chiral perturbation theory predictions (which give approximately 4.4–5.4 × 10⁻⁴ fm³) but is obtained only after restricting the analysis to kinematic regions where two different theoretical models differ by less than 3% when the polarizability is set to zero.

## Method

The experiment measured the γp → γπ⁺n reaction at the Mainz Microtron MAMI using a tagged photon beam (537–817 MeV), a liquid hydrogen target, the TAPS BaF₂ photon spectrometer, multi-wire proportional chambers and a forward scintillator detector for π⁺ mesons, and a time-of-flight neutron detector. The cross section was integrated over the final photon angle (140°–180° in the center-of-mass frame) and the Treiman-Yang angle, and the polarizability was extracted by comparing the data to two theoretical models: one using pseudoscalar pion-nucleon coupling with all pole diagrams, and another including nucleon and pion pole diagrams plus contributions from Δ(1232), P₁₁(1440), D₁₃(1520), and S₁₁(1535) resonances.

## Key numbers

- (α-β)π⁺ = (11.6 ± 1.5_stat ± 3.0_syst ± 0.5_mod) × 10⁻⁴ fm³, the central result from averaging over 12 photon energy bins
- Photon energy range: 537 MeV < Eγ < 817 MeV
- Kinematic region for polarizability extraction: 5 < s₁/mπ² < 15 and -12 < t/mπ² < -2
- Model-1 result: (α-β)π⁺ = (12.2 ± 1.6_stat ± 3.3_syst) × 10⁻⁴ fm³
- Model-2 result: (α-β)π⁺ = (11.1 ± 1.4_stat ± 2.8_syst) × 10⁻⁴ fm³
- Systematic error from detection efficiency: ±4% (quadratic sum of 2% from neutron cross sections and 3% from GEANT simulation uncertainty)
- Total systematic error on cross section: ±5%, equivalent to a root-mean-square error of 3%
- Background suppression level: 3% relative to the signal after kinematic cuts
- Number of identified events: approximately 4 × 10⁵ radiative π⁺ photoproduction events
- Missing-mass peak at MX = 139.7 MeV with resolution σ = 20 MeV

## Context

This measurement extends earlier work by the Lebedev Institute (which gave απ⁺ = 20 ± 12 × 10⁻⁴ fm³ with large errors) and by Antipov et al. (who found απ⁻ = -βπ⁻ = 6.8 ± 1.4 ± 1.2 × 10⁻⁴ fm³ from pion scattering off nuclear Coulomb fields). The result contradicts chiral perturbation theory calculations at one-loop (approximately 5.4 × 10⁻⁴ fm³) and two-loop (4.4 ± 1.0 × 10⁻⁴ fm³) order by Burgi, but agrees with dispersion sum rule predictions of approximately 10.3–10.6 × 10⁻⁴ fm³ from Radushkevich and Kashevarov. The measurement enables improved tests of chiral perturbation theory and constrains models of pion internal structure, though the authors note that better neutron detectors would be needed to reduce the dominant systematic uncertainty.
