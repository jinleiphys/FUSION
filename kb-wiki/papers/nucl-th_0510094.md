# Critical Point Symmetries in Nuclei

> Critical Point Symmetries (CPS) appear in regions of the nuclear chart where a rapid change from one symmetry to another is observed. The first CPSs, introduced by F. Iachello, were E(5), which corresponds to the transition from vibrational [U(5)] to gamma-unstable [O(6)] behaviour, and X(5), which represents the change from vibrational [U(5)] to prolate axially deformed [SU(3)] shapes. These CPSs have been obtained as special solutions of the Bohr collective Hamiltonian. More recent special solutions of the same Hamiltonian, to be described here, include Z(5) and Z(4), which correspond to maximally triaxial shapes (the latter with ``frozen'' gamma=30 degrees), as well as X(3), which corresponds to prolate shapes with ``frozen'' gamma=0. CPSs have the advantage of providing predictions which are parameter free (up to overall scale factors) and compare well to experiment. However, their mathematical structure [with the exception of E(5)] needs to be clarified.

## Key claim

Critical Point Symmetries (CPS) provide parameter-free predictions (up to overall scale factors) for nuclei at shape phase transitions, but their mathematical structure, with the exception of E(5), remains to be clarified, as the X(5), Z(5), Z(4), and X(3) solutions are either approximate or lack identified algebraic structures.

## Method

The authors obtain analytical solutions of the Bohr collective Hamiltonian by assuming an infinite square well potential in the β variable and, depending on the model, either allowing γ to vary around a fixed value (Z(5) with γ ≈ 30°, X(5) with γ ≈ 0°) or freezing γ to a constant (Z(4) with γ = 30°, X(3) with γ = 0°). Separation of variables is achieved either exactly (Z(4), X(3)) or approximately (Z(5), X(5)), leading to Bessel function eigenfunctions for the β-equation and, for the γ-dependent models, a harmonic oscillator equation in γ. The resulting spectra and B(E2) transition rates are computed and compared to experimental data in specific mass regions.

## Key numbers

- Z(5) model: energy spectrum given by E(s, n_w, L, n_γ̃) = E₀ + A (x_{s,ν})² + B n_γ̃, where x_{s,ν} are zeros of Bessel functions and ν = √[4L(L+1) − 3α² + 9]/2 = √[L(L+4) + 3n_w(2L−n_w) + 9]/2.
- Z(4) model: ground state band coincides with the spectrum of the E(4) Euclidean algebra in four dimensions, with ν = √[L(L+4) + 3n_w(2L−n_w) + 4]/2; for n_w = 0, ν = L/2 + 1.
- X(3) model: energy levels E_{s,L} = (ħ²/2Bβ_W²) x_{s,ν}², with ν = √[L(L+1)/3 + 1/4]; interband B(E2) rates include, e.g., B(E2; 0₂ → 2₁) = 164.0 normalized to B(E2; 2₁⁺ → 0₁⁺).
- X(3) model: β₁ bands of N=90 isotones (¹⁵⁰Nd, ¹⁵²Sm, ¹⁵⁴Gd, ¹⁵⁶Dy) agree well with X(3) predictions, but their spacing is about half of that predicted by X(5).

## Context

This work extends the original Critical Point Symmetries introduced by Iachello (2001), E(5) for the U(5)-to-O(6) transition and X(5) for the U(5)-to-SU(3) transition, by presenting new analytical solutions of the Bohr Hamiltonian: Z(5) and Z(4) for triaxial shapes with γ ≈ 30° and γ = 30°, respectively, and X(3) for prolate axially symmetric shapes with γ = 0. The Z(4) ground state band is related to the E(4) Euclidean algebra, while X(3) involves only three degrees of freedom. These models enable parameter-free predictions for nuclei at shape phase transitions, with experimental candidates identified in the Pt (Z(5)), Xe (Z(4)), and Os/Pt and N=90 isotone (X(3)) regions. Future work aims to clarify the algebraic structures of X(3) and Z(4) as a step toward understanding X(5) and Z(5), and to extend the approach to octupole and dipole degrees of freedom.
