# A reassessment of nuclear effects in muonic deuterium using pionless effective field theory at N3LO

> We provide a systematic assessment of the order-$α^5$ nuclear contributions to the Lamb shift of muonic deuterium, including the accompanying radiative corrections due to vacuum polarization, up to next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order (N3LO) within the pionless effective field theory (EFT). We also evaluate higher-order corrections due to the single-nucleon structure, which are expected to be the most important corrections beyond N3LO. We find a correlation between the deuteron charge and Friar radii, which can be useful to judge the quality of charge form factor parametrisations. We refine the theoretical description of the $2γ$-exchange contribution, especially in the elastic contribution and the radiative corrections, ameliorating the original discrepancy between theory and experiment in the size of $2γ$-exchange effects. Based on the experimental Lamb shift of muonic deuterium, we obtain the deuteron charge radius, $r_d(μ\text{D})=2.12763(13)_\text{exp}(77)_\text{theory}$~fm, which is consistent with (but less precise than) the value obtained by combining the H-D isotope shift with the muonic hydrogen Lamb shift. The theory uncertainty is evaluated using a Bayesian procedure and is dominated by the truncation of the pionless EFT series.

## Key claim

The paper provides a systematic assessment of order-α⁵ nuclear contributions to the Lamb shift of muonic deuterium using pionless effective field theory (πEFT) at N3LO, finding that the elastic two-photon-exchange contribution deviates significantly from previous data-driven results due to the poor low-momentum behavior of the Abbott et al. (2000) deuteron form factor parametrization, while the inelastic contribution is consistent with recent evaluations.

## Method

The authors calculated the elastic and inelastic two-photon-exchange contributions to the muonic deuterium Lamb shift at N3LO in pionless effective field theory, using previously computed deuteron doubly-virtual Compton scattering amplitudes (Lensky, 2021). They evaluated the forward two-photon-exchange correction through the longitudinal and transverse scalar amplitudes, computed the elastic contribution from the deuteron charge form factor, and determined the inelastic contribution from the non-pole parts of the VVCS amplitudes. Higher-order corrections from single-nucleon structure were evaluated by inserting full nucleon form factors at leading order and using dispersive integrals for nucleon polarizability contributions.

## Key numbers

* Elastic two-photon-exchange contribution to the 2S Lamb shift: E_elastic(2S) = -0.4463(77) meV at N3LO in πEFT
* Inelastic two-photon-exchange contribution: E_inel(2S) = -1.509(16) meV
* Total two-photon-exchange contribution: E_2γ(2S) = -1.752(20) meV
* Hadronic corrections beyond N3LO: E_hadr(2S) = -0.032(6) meV
* Electron vacuum polarization correction to two-photon exchange: E_eVP(2S) = -0.027 meV
* Coulomb distortion contribution: E_Coulomb(2S) = 0.2625(15) meV
* Deuteron charge radius extracted from muonic deuterium: r_d(μD) = 2.12763(13)_exp(77)_theory fm = 2.12763(78) fm
* Deuteron charge radius from H-D isotope shift combined with muonic hydrogen: r_d(μH, iso) = 2.12771(22) fm
* Low-energy constant l_1^{C0_S} = -1.8(4)×10⁻³

## Context

This work extends the systematic treatment of nuclear effects in muonic deuterium using pionless effective field theory, following the N3LO evaluation of deuteron charge form factors and VVCS amplitudes by Lensky (2021). It resolves the original discrepancy between theory and experiment in the two-photon-exchange effects noted by Pohl (2016), primarily by including the electron vacuum polarization correction to two-photon exchange previously identified by Kalinowski (2018). The results enable a consistent extraction of the deuteron charge radius from muonic deuterium that now agrees with the value obtained from combining muonic hydrogen with the hydrogen-deuterium isotope shift (Antognini, 2012), and provide a framework for systematic improvement of theoretical uncertainties using Bayesian methods (Furnstahl, 2015; Perez, 2015).
