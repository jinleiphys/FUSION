# Extended Theory of Finite Fermi Systems (Collective Vibrations in Closed Shell Nuclei)

> We review an extension of Migdal's Theory of Finite Fermi Systems which has been developed and applied to collective vibrations in closed shell nuclei in the past ten years. This microscopic approach is based on a consistent use of the Green function method. Here one considers in a consistent way more complex 1p1h$\otimes$phonon configurations beyond the RPA correlations. Moreover, these configurations are not only included in the excited states but also explicitly in the ground states of nuclei. The method has been applied to the calculation of the strength distribution and transition densities of giant electric and magnetic resonances in stable and unstable magic nuclei. Using these microscopic transition densities, cross sections for inelastic electron and alpha scattering have been calculated and compared with the available experimental data. The method also allows one to extract in a consistent way the magnitude of the strength of the various multipoles in the energy regions in which several multipoles overlap. We compare the microscopic transition densities, the strength distributions and the various multipole strengths with their values extracted phenomenologically.

## Key claim
The extended theory of finite Fermi systems (ETFFS) explicitly includes 1p1h\(\otimes\)phonon configurations beyond RPA correlations in both the excited states and the ground states of closed-shell nuclei, enabling a more realistic description of giant resonance widths and fine structure than standard TFFS or RPA, though the approach remains semi-phenomenological because the effective interaction and single-particle input are parameterized rather than derived self-consistently.

## Method
The authors derive the ETFFS using the Green function method, starting from the Bethe–Salpeter equation in the particle-hole channel. They split the one-particle Green function into a quasiparticle pole part and a smooth remainder, then apply Landau’s renormalization procedure to obtain an equation for the response function that contains only the 1p1h propagator explicitly, with all complex configurations renormalized into the effective particle-hole interaction \(F\) and effective charges. In the extension, they additionally include 1p1h\(\otimes\)phonon configurations explicitly in the response equation and in the ground state, and solve the resulting equations in \(\mathbf{r}\)-space to treat the single-particle continuum.

## Key numbers
* The Landau-Migdal interaction parameters \(f_{in}, f_{ex}, f', g, g'\) are density-dependent; the dimensionless parameters are defined via \(F_l = C_0 f_l\) where \(C_0\) is the inverse density of states at the Fermi surface.
* The compression modulus \(K\) is related to the Landau parameter \(f_0\) by \(K = 6 \frac{\hbar^2 k_F^2}{2m^*}(1+2f_0)\).
* The symmetry energy \(\beta\) is given by \(\beta = \frac{1}{3} \frac{\hbar^2 k_F^2}{2m^*}(1+2f'_0)\).
* The effective mass \(m^*/m = 1 + \frac{2}{3}f_1\).

## Context
This work extends Migdal’s original theory of finite Fermi systems (TFFS) (Migdal, 1967) by explicitly incorporating 1p1h\(\otimes\)phonon configurations, which are only renormalized into effective quantities in the standard TFFS. It builds on earlier extensions that included pairing (Baranger, 1960) and self-consistency effects (Sfkh78, Khs82), and on the use of the \(\mathbf{r}\)-space continuum treatment (Sb75, Sfk76). The ETFFS enables quantitative calculation of giant resonance widths and transition densities for stable and unstable magic nuclei, and provides a framework for analyzing overlapping multipole strengths in broad resonances.
