# Constraining the dense matter equation of state with joint analysis of NICER and LIGO/Virgo measurements

> The NICER collaboration recently published a joint estimate of the mass and the radius of PSR J0030+0451, derived via X-ray pulse-profile modeling. Raaijmakers et al. (2019) explored the implications of this measurement for the dense matter equation of state (EOS) using two parameterizations of the high-density EOS: a piecewise-polytropic model, and a model based on the speed of sound in neutron stars. In this work we obtain further constraints on the EOS following this approach, but we also include information about the tidal deformability of neutron stars from the gravitational wave signal of the compact binary merger GW170817. We compare the constraints on the EOS to those set by the recent measurement of a 2.14 solar mass pulsar, included as a likelihood function approximated by a Gaussian, and find a small increase in information gain. To show the flexibility of our method, we also explore the possibility that GW170817 was a neutron star-black hole merger, which yields weaker constraints on the EOS.

## Key claim

The joint analysis of NICER mass-radius measurements for PSR J0030+0451 and gravitational wave tidal deformability constraints from GW170817 yields posterior equation of state distributions that are broadly consistent with prior constraints from the 2.14 solar mass pulsar alone, with only a small increase in information gain; the final posterior distributions are very similar to the prior when only the high-mass pulsar is included, because GW170817 favors softer EOS while NICER favors stiffer EOS.

## Method

The authors performed Bayesian parameter estimation using two parameterizations of the high-density equation of state: a three-piece polytropic model and a speed-of-sound model, both matched to chiral effective field theory calculations below 1.1 times nuclear saturation density. They combined nuisance-marginalized likelihood functions from the NICER mass-radius posterior of PSR J0030+0451 (from Riley et al. 2019), the LIGO/Virgo posterior on masses and tidal deformabilities from GW170817 (with chirp mass fixed to 1.186 solar masses), and a Gaussian likelihood approximation for the 2.14 solar mass pulsar PSR J0740+6620, using nested sampling to draw weighted posterior samples.

## Key numbers

- PSR J0030+0451 mass: 1.34 (+0.15, -0.16) solar masses and equatorial radius: 12.71 (+1.14, -1.19) km at the 68% credible interval (from Riley et al. 2019)
- PSR J0030+0451 mass: 1.44 (+0.15, -0.14) solar masses and equatorial radius: 13.02 (+1.24, -1.06) km at the 68% credible interval (from Miller et al. 2019)
- GW170817 chirp mass: 1.186 +/- 0.001 solar masses
- Radius constraints from GW170817 alone: 11.9 (+1.4, -1.4) km from spectral EOS analysis and 10.8 (+2.0, -1.7) km for the more massive NS at the 90% credible interval (from Abbott et al. 2018)
- PSR J0740+6620 mass: 2.14 (+0.10, -0.09) solar masses (from Cromartie et al. 2019)
- Log-evidences for the PP model: -1.69 +/- 0.03 (with 2.14 solar mass pulsar), -15.44 +/- 0.02 (adding GW170817), -17.05 +/- 0.03 (adding NICER)
- Log-evidences for the CS model: -2.22 +/- 0.02 (with 2.14 solar mass pulsar), -15.08 +/- 0.02 (adding GW170817), -17.10 +/- 0.03 (adding NICER)
- Bayes factors (PP over CS): 0.70 (2.14 solar mass pulsar only), 1.70 (adding GW170817), 1.05 (adding NICER)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Raaijmakers et al. (2019) by incorporating gravitational wave tidal deformability information from GW170817 alongside the NICER mass-radius measurement of PSR J0030+0451, using both piecewise-polytropic and speed-of-sound parameterizations with nuclear physics priors from chiral effective field theory. The results are compared to constraints from the massive pulsar measurement of Cromartie et al. (2019) and to the spectral EOS analysis of Abbott et al. (2018), showing that the spectral model allows for much stiffer EOS due to lacking nuclear physics constraints around saturation density. The methodology enables future sequential Bayesian updating with larger numbers of neutron star observations, and the authors demonstrate flexibility by exploring the neutron star-black hole merger scenario for GW170817, which yields weaker EOS constraints than the neutron star-neutron star assumption.
