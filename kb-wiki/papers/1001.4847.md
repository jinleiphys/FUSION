# Production of heavy isotopes in transfer reactions by collisions of $^{238}$U+$^{238}$U

> The dynamics of transfer reactions in collisions of two very heavy nuclei $^{238}$U+$^{238}$U is studied within the dinuclear system (DNS) model. Collisions of two actinide nuclei form a super heavy composite system during a very short time, in which a large number of charge and mass transfers may take place. Such reactions have been investigated experimentally as an alternative way for the production of heavy and superheavy nuclei. The role of collision orientation in the production cross sections of heavy nuclides is analyzed systematically. Calculations show that the cross sections decrease drastically with increasing the charged numbers of heavy fragments. The transfer mechanism is favorable to synthesize heavy neutron-rich isotopes, such as nuclei around the subclosure at N=162 from No (Z=102) to Db (Z=105).

# Production of heavy isotopes in transfer reactions by collisions of $^{238}$U+$^{238}$U

## Key claim

The dinuclear system (DNS) model calculations show that low-energy transfer reactions in collisions of $^{238}$U+$^{238}$U are not suitable for synthesizing superheavy nuclei with Z>106 because the cross sections are at the level of 1 pb or below, but the mechanism is favorable for producing heavy neutron-rich isotopes around the subclosure at N=162 from No (Z=102) to Db (Z=105). The abstract's claim of favorable synthesis is qualified by the strong dependence on collision orientation and the drastic decrease of cross sections with increasing atomic number.

## Method

The authors used a dinuclear system (DNS) model that couples nucleon transfer to relative motion by solving a set of microscopically derived master equations distinguishing protons and neutrons, with the distribution probability evolved in the potential energy surface of the DNS. The time evolution of the distribution probability for fragment 1 with proton number Z1 and neutron number N1 and excitation energy E1 was described by master equations including mean transition probabilities for one-nucleon transfer, quasifission rates, and fission rates estimated with the one-dimensional Kramers formula. The cross sections of primary fragments were calculated after the DNS reached relaxation balance, and the survival probabilities of fragments after particle and gamma emission in competition with fission were estimated using a statistical approach.

## Key numbers

- The height of the interaction potential at the touching configuration for nose-nose collisions is 713 MeV; for side-side orientation the value is 813.5 MeV.
- The maximal angular momentum taken in the calculation is Jmax=200, which includes all partial waves where transfer reactions may take place.
- The interaction time in the dissipation process has a value of a few 10^-20 s.
- The local temperature is given by the Fermi-gas expression with level density parameter a=A/12 MeV^-1.
- Cross sections for superheavy nuclei with Z>106 are at the level of 1 pb and even below 1 pb.
- Neutron-rich isotopes of Fm and Md were produced at the larger cross section of 0.1 μb (referenced from Zagrebaev and Greiner, 2007).
- The incident energy used for the main calculations was Ec.m.=900 MeV for orientation studies and Ec.m.=800 MeV for comparison with experimental data.

## Context

This work extends the multi-nucleon transfer reaction approach initially proposed by Zagrebaev (2006) for producing neutron-rich heavy and superheavy nuclei in low-energy collisions of actinide nuclei, and builds on earlier experimental work by Hu (1977) and Schädel (1978) showing that cross sections of heavy fragments decrease rapidly with increasing atomic number. The DNS model results are compared with calculations by Zagrebaev and Greiner (2007, 2008) using multi-dimensional Langevin equations, and with time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations by Golabek (2009) and improved quantum molecular dynamics (ImQMD) model by Tian (2008). The work enables future experimental studies of the influence of shell closure on the production of neutron-rich isotopes, and may help fill the gap of new isotopes between the cold fusion reactions at GSI and the $^{48}$Ca induced reactions at FLNR in Dubna.
