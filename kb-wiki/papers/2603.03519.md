# Microscopic description of cluster radioactivity fission valleys along isotopic and isotonic chains

> Cluster radioactivity has been successfully described as a super-asymmetric fission mode within the microscopic self-consistent Gogny Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov approximation [Phys. Rev. C 84, 044608 (2011)]. For nuclei preserving the neutron-to-proton $N/Z$ ratio of the doubly magic $^{208}$Pb, a cluster radioactivity fission valley has been identified. Such a valley can also be found both in actinides and super-heavy nuclei. In this paper, chains of isotopes and isotones are examined to determine the limits of existence of the cluster radioactivity fission mode. It is shown that the super-asymmetric valley can be found in a wide range of the nuclear chart. Nevertheless, the valley flattens more and more when diverging from the isospin asymmetry of $^{208}$Pb. For neutron-deficient nuclei with $N/Z <$ 1.41, it is found that the valley diminishes before reaching the scission point, and cluster radioactivity can not be observed.

# Microscopic description of cluster radioactivity fission valleys along isotopic and isotonic chains

## Key claim

The super-asymmetric fission valley that describes cluster radioactivity exists in a wide range of heavy and super-heavy nuclei, but only when the neutron-to-proton ratio is not too far from that of $^{208}$Pb; for neutron-deficient nuclei with $N/Z < 1.41$, the valley diminishes before reaching the scission point and cluster radioactivity cannot be observed.

## Method

The authors performed constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using the Gogny D1S energy density functional, with constraints on the axially symmetric quadrupole $\hat{Q}_{20}$ and octupole $\hat{Q}_{30}$ operators. Potential energy surfaces were computed for chains of uranium isotopes ($^{216-252}$U), $N=140$ isotones ($^{220}$Hg to $^{242}$No), copernicium isotopes ($^{268-294}$Cn), and $N=172$ isotones ($^{274}$No to $^{296}$124). The fission paths were determined by following the bottom of the super-asymmetric valley using a single constraint on the octupole moment.

## Key numbers

* The benchmark nucleus $^{232}$U has a ridge height of 9 MeV at $Q_{30}=20$ b$^{3/2}$, separating the super-asymmetric valley from the normal asymmetric valley at $Q_{20}=37$ b.
* The fission barrier in the cluster radioactivity mode in actinides reaches around 25 MeV at the top.
* The super-asymmetric valley disappears at $N/Z \approx 192/90$ for $^{222}$U and $^{242}$No, corresponding to $N/Z \approx 1.41$.
* The most neutron-deficient nuclei where the barrier can be determined are $^{222}$U ($N/Z=1.41$), $^{236}$Cm ($N/Z=1.45$), $^{270}$Cn ($N/Z=1.41$), and $^{290}$Og ($N/Z=1.46$).
* For $^{220}$Hg and $^{242}$No, the height of the ridge is lower than 4 MeV at $Q_{30}=20$ b$^{3/2}$.
* In super-heavy nuclei, the asymmetric part of the fission barrier varies between 1 MeV and 10 MeV in height.
* For the heaviest isotones $^{294}$122 and $^{296}$124, no cluster fission valley was found at the considered octupole deformation.

## Context

This work extends the microscopic description of cluster radioactivity as a super-asymmetric fission mode, first established within the Gogny HFB framework by Warda et al. (2011) in Phys. Rev. C 84, 044608. It builds on prior findings that super-asymmetric fission valleys exist in actinides and super-heavy nuclei such as $^{284}$Cn, where the $N/Z$ ratio matches that of $^{208}$Pb. The study systematically maps the limits of this fission mode across isotopic and isotonic chains, showing that the valley flattens when diverging from the $^{208}$Pb isospin asymmetry. The results enable predictions of which nuclei may exhibit cluster radioactivity and identify the $N/Z \approx 1.41$ line as an upper limit for its possible existence, guiding future experimental searches in both neutron-deficient and neutron-rich regions of the nuclear chart.
