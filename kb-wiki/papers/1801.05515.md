# On the jets emitted by driven Bose-Einstein condensates

> Features of the emission of jets by driven Bose-Einstein condensates, discovered by Clark et al. (Nature 551, 356359), can be understood by drawing analogies with particle physics. In particular, the widening of the $Δϕ=π$ peak in the angular correlation function is due to a dijet acollinearity, which I estimate to be about 5$^{\circ}$ RMS. I also propose new correlation studies using observables commonly used in studies of the quark-gluon plasma.

## Key claim

The widening of the Δϕ=π peak in the angular correlation function of jets from driven Bose-Einstein condensates can be explained by a dijet acollinearity of about 5° RMS, drawing an analogy with intrinsic parton kT in proton-proton collisions.

## Method

The author estimates the dijet acollinearity by applying a method from particle physics (CCOR collaboration, PHENIX collaboration) that uses Gaussian approximations for jet transverse spread and basic trigonometry. The average transverse momentum of atoms relative to the jet axis is extracted from the Δϕ=0 peak width, then combined with the Δϕ=π peak width to compute the average acollinearity angle via Equation (3). A numerical simulation with 1000 draws of acollinearity angles from a Gaussian with 5° standard deviation and jet angular density approximated by a Gaussian with 1.5° standard deviation reproduces the measured correlation function.

## Key numbers

- Dijet acollinearity estimated at about 5° RMS.
- Half-maximum half-width of the Δϕ=0 peak is about 2° for R=8.5 μm and f=2 kHz.
- Width of the Δϕ=π peak is about three times larger than the Δϕ=0 peak.
- Bose-Einstein condensate radius R=8.5 μm and vertical extent 0.5 μm (root-mean-square).
- Polar angle threshold for atoms traversing most of the condensate: arcsin(0.5/8.5) ≈ 3°.
- Integral of the Δϕ=π peak is 70–85% of the Δϕ=0 peak integral.

## Context

This work extends the experimental discovery by Clark et al. (Nature 551, 356–359, 2017) of jet emission from driven Bose-Einstein condensates, offering an explanation for the unexplained broadening of the Δϕ=π peak that the original authors noted required further investigation. The analogy draws on the 1977 work by Feynman et al. on intrinsic parton kT in hadron collisions, and the estimation method follows the CCOR collaboration and PHENIX collaboration approaches. The proposed multi-particle cumulant techniques, inspired by quark-gluon plasma studies (Cumulant1, Cumulant2, Cumulant3), could reveal collective behavior or exotic effects such as vortices and solitons in the condensate system.
