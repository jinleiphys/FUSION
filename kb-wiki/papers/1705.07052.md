# Magnetic monopole mass bounds from heavy ion collisions and neutron stars

> Magnetic monopoles, if they exist, would be produced amply in strong magnetic fields and high temperatures via the thermal Schwinger process. Such circumstances arise in heavy ion collisions and in neutron stars, both of which imply lower bounds on the mass of possible magnetic monopoles. In showing this, we construct the cross section for pair production of magnetic monopoles in heavy ion collisions, which indicates that they are particularly promising for experimental searches such as MoEDAL.

# Magnetic monopole mass bounds from heavy ion collisions and neutron stars

## Key claim

Magnetic monopoles, if they exist, would be produced via the thermal Schwinger process in strong magnetic fields and high temperatures, and comparing this production rate to experimental upper bounds and astrophysical observations yields lower bounds on the monopole mass that are model-independent (applying to both elementary and composite monopoles) and do not rely on perturbation theory, though the bounds depend on the magnetic charge and the approximations assume weak, slowly varying fields.

## Method

The authors use the semiclassical rate of thermal Schwinger pair production for magnetic monopoles, calculated in a companion paper (Gould and Rajantie, 2017), to derive the pair production cross section in heavy ion collisions by integrating the rate over the fireball spacetime volume. For neutron stars, they equate the number density of monopoles produced by thermal Schwinger pair production to the density that would dissipate the magnetic field via acceleration, then compare to observed magnetic field strengths to derive mass bounds.

## Key numbers

- Lower bound on monopole mass from SPS heavy ion collisions (beam energy 160A GeV): $m \gtrsim \left(2.0 + 2.6(g/g_D)^{3/2}\right)$ GeV, where $g_D = 2\pi/e$ is the Dirac charge and the experiment was sensitive only to $g \geq 2g_D$
- Lower bound from magnetars ($B \approx 10^{-4}$ GeV²): $m \gtrsim 0.31$ GeV for $g = g_D$ and $m \gtrsim 0.70$ GeV for $g = 2g_D$, using neutron star radius $R \approx 1.0 \times 10^{20}$ GeV⁻¹ and dynamo time $\tau_D \approx 1.5 \times 10^{24}$ GeV⁻¹ (one second)
- Projected observable mass range for LHC Pb-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5.02$ TeV: up to approximately $(1 + 28(g/g_D)^{3/2})$ GeV, assuming constant field approximation and acceptance of $O(10^{-4})$
- Comparison bound from big bang nucleosynthesis: $m \gtrsim 0.45$ GeV, derived from $m/T_{\mathrm{RH}} \gtrsim 45$ and $T_{\mathrm{BBN}} \approx 10$ MeV
- Magnetic fine structure constant: $\alpha_M \approx 34$
- Magnetic field in SPS lead collisions: $B_{160\mathrm{GeV}} \approx 0.0097$ GeV²
- Temperature in SPS lead collisions: $T_{160\mathrm{GeV}} \approx 0.185$ GeV
- Upper bound on monopole pair production cross section from SPS search (He, 1997): $\sigma_{M\bar{M}} < 1.9$ nb
- Minimum-bias inelastic cross section for SPS experiment: $\sigma^{\mathrm{inel}}_{HI} \approx 6.3$ b

## Context

This work extends the thermal Schwinger pair production calculation of Gould and Rajantie (2017) to derive experimental and astrophysical mass bounds on magnetic monopoles. It contrasts with earlier work by Drukier and Nussinov (1982) which argued for exponential suppression of monopole production in low-entropy collisions; the authors argue that in high-temperature, high-magnetic-field environments this suppression is absent, analogous to sphaleron-induced baryon number violation (Klinkhamer and Manton, 1984; Kuzmin, Rubakov, and Shaposhnikov, 1985). The heavy ion bounds improve upon the model-independent BBN bound of approximately 0.45 GeV (Turner, 1982; Collins and Perry, 1984; Lindblom, 1984), while the neutron star bounds are related to the Parker bound (Parker, 1970; Turner, Parker, and Bogdan, 1982; Adams et al., 1993) but use the production rate rather than flux limits. The results enable future experimental searches at MoEDAL and other LHC detectors, though the authors note that accounting for spacetime dependence of the magnetic field at higher energies requires further theoretical work.
