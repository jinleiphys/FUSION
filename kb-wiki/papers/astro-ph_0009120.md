# Cooling of Hybrid Neutron Stars and Hypothetical Self-bound Objects with Superconducting Quark Cores

> We study the consequences of superconducting quark cores (with color-flavor-locked phase as representative example) for evolution of temperature profiles and the cooling curves in quark-hadron hybrid stars and in hypothetical self-bounded objects having no a hadron shell (quark core neutron stars). The quark gaps are varied from 0 to $Δ_q =50$ MeV. For hybrid stars we find time scales of $1\div5$, $5\div10$ and $50\div100$ years for the formation of a quasistationary temperature distribution in the cases $Δ_q =0$, 0.1 MeV and $\gsim$ 1 MeV, respectively. These time scales are governed by the heat transport within quark cores for large diquark gaps ($Δ\gsim$ 1 MeV) and within the hadron shell for small diquark gaps ($Δ\lsim 0.1$ MeV). For quark core neutron stars we find a time scale $\simeq 300$ years for the formation of a quasistationary temperature distribution in the case $Δ\gsim$ 10 MeV and a very short one for $Δ\lsim$ 1 MeV. If hot young compact objects will be observed they can be interpreted as manifestation of large gap color superconductivity. Depending on the size of the pairing gaps, the compact star takes different paths in the ${lg}T_s $ vs. ${lg} t$ diagram where $T_s$ is the surface temperature. Compared to the corresponding hadronic model which well fits existing data the model for the hybrid neutron star (with a large diquark gap) shows too fast cooling. The same conclusion can be drawn for the corresponding self-bound objects.

## Key claim

The paper finds that for hybrid neutron stars and self-bound quark core neutron stars with large diquark pairing gaps (≥1 MeV), the cooling is too fast compared to existing observational data, which are better fitted by pure hadronic models. However, the authors note that if future observations detect very hot young compact objects, they could be interpreted as evidence for large-gap color superconductivity.

## Method

The authors solved the coupled equations for energy balance and thermal energy transport (including relativistic corrections) to compute the evolution of temperature profiles and surface temperatures for 1.4 solar mass hybrid neutron stars and quark core neutron stars. They varied the diquark pairing gap from 0 to 50 MeV, included contributions from neutrino emissivity, specific heat, and heat conductivity for both quark matter (with color-flavor-locked phase) and hadronic matter, and used a Tsuruta law relation between surface and mantle temperatures.

## Key numbers

- For hybrid stars, quasistationary temperature distribution forms in 1–5 years for Δ_q = 0, 5–10 years for Δ_q = 0.1 MeV, and 50–100 years for Δ_q ≥ 1 MeV
- For quark core neutron stars, quasistationary temperature distribution forms in approximately 300 years for Δ_q ≥ 10 MeV
- Quark direct Urca emissivity: ε_ν^QDU ≈ 9.4 × 10^26 α_s u Y_e^(1/3) ζ_QDU T_9^6 erg cm^(-3) s^(-1)
- Quark specific heat: c_q ≈ 10^21 u^(2/3) ζ_S T_9 erg cm^(-3) K^(-1)
- Hybrid star radius ≈ 10 km, quark core neutron star radius ≈ 9 km, pure hadronic star radius ≈ 13 km (all for 1.4 M_⊙)
- Electron fraction in hybrid star: Y_e ≈ 0.03 at hadron-quark boundary density n_H, Y_e ≈ 8.5 × 10^(-5) at quark core boundary n_Q

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Blaschke et al. (1999) on self-bound quark core neutron stars and by Page et al. (2000) on hybrid neutron stars, which had disregarded heat transport effects within superconducting quark cores. The paper demonstrates that heat transport delays cooling significantly (50–300 years) compared to isothermal approximations, but still finds that large-gap color-flavor-locked phase models cool too fast to fit existing X-ray data from Schaab et al. (1999) and Yakovlev et al. (2000). The results enable future comparisons with observations of young supernova remnants to test for color superconductivity signatures.
