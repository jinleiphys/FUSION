# Probing the neutron star interior and the Equation of State of cold dense matter with the SKA

> With an average density higher than the nuclear density, neutron stars (NS) provide a unique test-ground for nuclear physics, quantum chromodynamics (QCD), and nuclear superfluidity. Determination of the fundamental interactions that govern matter under such extreme conditions is one of the major unsolved problems of modern physics, and -- since it is impossible to replicate these conditions on Earth -- a major scientific motivation for SKA. The most stringent observational constraints come from measurements of NS bulk properties: each model for the microscopic behaviour of matter predicts a specific density-pressure relation (its `Equation of state', EOS). This generates a unique mass-radius relation which predicts a characteristic radius for a large range of masses and a maximum mass above which NS collapse to black holes. It also uniquely predicts other bulk quantities, like maximum spin frequency and moment of inertia. The SKA, in Phase 1 and particularly in Phase 2 will, thanks to the exquisite timing precision enabled by its raw sensitivity, and surveys that dramatically increase the number of sources: 1) Provide many more precise NS mass measurements (high mass NS measurements are particularly important for ruling out EOS models); 2) Allow the measurement of the NS moment of inertia in highly relativistic binaries such as the Double Pulsar; 3) Greatly increase the number of fast-spinning NS, with the potential discovery of spin frequencies above those allowed by some EOS models; 4) Improve our knowledge of new classes of binary pulsars such as black widows and redbacks (which may be massive as a class) through sensitive broad-band radio observations; and 5) Improve our understanding of dense matter superfluidity and the state of matter in the interior through the study of rotational glitches, provided that an ad-hoc campaign is developed.

## Key claim

The Square Kilometer Array (SKA) will enable transformative constraints on the neutron star equation of state (EOS) through precision timing and large-scale surveys, but the most stringent results depend on discovering rare extreme systems (high-mass pulsars, fast rotators above ~1 kHz, and relativistic binaries) rather than on routine measurements of typical sources.

## Method

The paper reviews how pulsar timing measurements of post-Keplerian orbital parameters (periastron precession, orbital decay, Shapiro delay) in binary systems yield neutron star masses, and how the mass-radius relation derived from the EOS connects to observables like maximum spin frequency and moment of inertia. It surveys current observational constraints from masses, spin rates, and glitch activity, then projects the improvements expected from SKA Phase 1 and Phase 2 surveys and timing campaigns, including targeted searches of Fermi gamma-ray sources and globular clusters.

## Key numbers

- PSR J1614-2230 mass: 1.97 ± 0.04 M_sun (Shapiro delay measurement)
- PSR J0348+0432 mass: 2.01 ± 0.03 M_sun (optical spectroscopy of white dwarf companion)
- Current fastest-spinning pulsar: PSR J1748-2446ad at 716 Hz (1.396 ms period), constraining radius of a 1.4 M_sun star to < 15 km
- Nuclear saturation density: ρ_sat = 2.8 × 10^14 g/cm^3; neutron drip density: ~4 × 10^11 g/cm^3; crust-core boundary: ~2 × 10^14 g/cm^3
- Keplerian frequency formula: f_K = C (M/M_sun)^(1/2) (R/10 km)^(-3/2) with C = 1.08 kHz for neutron stars, 1.12 kHz for strange stars
- SKA1 all-sky survey expected to discover ~10,000 pulsars, ~1,800 millisecond pulsars, ~100 double neutron star systems
- Current glitch catalog: ~440 glitches in ~150 pulsars; Crab pulsar shows minimum glitch size Δν = 0.05 μHz
- Lower limit on moment of inertia for PSR J1614-2230: I > 10^45 g cm^2 (from gamma-ray luminosity, not strongly constraining)

## Context

This paper extends the white paper tradition of the SKA science case (Keane 2014, Hessels 2014) by focusing specifically on neutron star interior physics. It builds on the mass measurements of Demorest (2010) and Antoniadis (2013) that established the ~2 M_sun threshold, and on the glitch monitoring programs at Jodrell Bank, Parkes, and Urumqi (Yu 2010, Espinoza 2011).
