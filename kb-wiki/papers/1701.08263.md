# The ideal relativistic fluid limit for a medium with polarization

> We use Lagrangian effective field theory techniques to construct the equations of motion for an ideal relativistic fluid whose constituent degrees of freedom have microscopic polarization. We discuss the meaning of such a system, and argue that it is the first term in the EFT appropriate for describing polarization observables in heavy ion collisions, such as final state particle polarization and chiral magnetic and vortaic effects. We show that this system will generally require non-dissipative dynamics at higher order in gradient than second order, leading to potential stability issues known with such systems. We comment on the significance of this in the light of conjectured lower limits on viscosity.

## Key claim

The paper constructs an effective field theory for an ideal relativistic fluid with microscopic polarization, showing that such a system requires non-dissipative dynamics at higher order in gradient than second order, which leads to potential stability issues. The authors argue that polarization degrees of freedom break the circulation theorem and that the gradient expansion is altered in a specific way when polarization is non-negligible, though they note polarization is expected to be small but not necessarily parametrically smaller than the mean free path.

## Method

The authors use Lagrangian effective field theory techniques, starting from three scalar fields \(\phi^I\) describing Lagrangian coordinates of a perfect fluid with volume-preserving diffeomorphism invariance. They introduce a new variable \(y^{\mu\nu}\) representing the local angular momentum density of a fluid cell, orthogonal to the four-velocity, and impose local equilibrium by requiring collinearity between polarization and vorticity: \(y_{\mu\nu} = \chi(b,\omega^2) \omega_{\mu\nu}\). The effective Lagrangian is constructed as \(F(b \times f(y))\) where \(f(y) = 1 - c y_{\mu\nu} y^{\mu\nu} + \mathcal{O}(y^4)\), and the equations of motion are derived from the Euler-Lagrange equations for a Lagrangian depending on second derivatives of fields.

## Key numbers

* The microscopic density of degrees of freedom is \((gT)^3\) where \(g \sim N_c^2\), giving numerical values of order \(10^{2-3}\) in heavy ion collisions and order \(10^4\) in ultracold atom systems.
* The polarizeability constant \(c\) in the Lagrangian has dimensions such that \(c \sim T_0^2\), where \(T_0\) is the separation of microscopic degrees of freedom.
* In the high temperature limit, net polarization decreases as \(\sim \tanh(\mu_S/T)\) where \(\mu_S\) is the microscopic polarizeability.

## Context

This work extends the Lagrangian formulation of relativistic hydrodynamics (Nicolis et al., 2011; Nicolis et al., 2015) to include polarization degrees of freedom, addressing a gap in the theoretical description of polarization observables in heavy ion collisions. It contrasts with previous treatments such as Israel-Stewart equations derived from the Boltzmann equation (which neglect spin dynamics entirely) and thermodynamic equilibrium calculations with angular momentum (Becattini et al., 2013; Becattini et al., 2015), which assume global rather than local equilibrium. The paper enables future development of dissipative corrections where polarization relaxes to vorticity, and provides a framework for understanding the relationship between vorticity and polarization in strongly coupled systems.
