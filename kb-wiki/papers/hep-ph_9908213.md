# Effects of friction on the chiral symmetry restoration in high energy heavy-ion collisions

> We study the effects of friction on the chiral symmetry restoration which may take place temporarily in high energy heavy ion collisions. The equations of motion with friction are introduced to describe the time evolution of the chiral condensates within the framework of the linear $σ$ model. Four types of friction are used to study how the result is sensitive to the choice of the friction. For the thermalization stage, the time dependent temperature is parameterized so as to simulate the result of the parton-cascade model. It is parameterized according to the one dimensional scaling hydrodynamics for the subsequent cooling stage. The time development of the condensates and the entropy production due to friction are calculated numerically. The time interval in which the chiral symmetry is restored approximately is investigated in detail for four types of friction. It is found that; (i) the maximum temperature must be high enough (not lower than 230 MeV) and the friction must be strong enough in order that the chiral symmetry restoration lasts for a long time (not shorter than 3fm/c); (ii) the ratio of time interval in which chiral symmetry is restored, to the time interval in which the temperature is higher than the critical temperature is typically 0.5 when the friction is strong enough; and (iii) the entropy due to the friction is mainly produced in the early stage of the cooling. The effect of freezeout is discussed briefly.

## Key claim

The paper shows that for chiral symmetry to be approximately restored for a sufficiently long time (at least 3 fm/c) in high-energy heavy-ion collisions, the maximum temperature must be at least 230 MeV and the friction must be strong enough; however, the condition that the temperature exceeds the critical temperature does not by itself guarantee chiral symmetry restoration in a time-dependent process because of finite relaxation time.

## Method

The authors introduce equations of motion with friction terms to describe the time evolution of chiral condensates within the linear sigma model under the massless free particle approximation (MFPA). Four types of friction are used: constant friction (case A), friction with the zero-temperature pion mass (case B), friction with the temperature-dependent mass evaluated at the zero-temperature vacuum (case C), and friction with the temperature-dependent mass evaluated at the temperature-dependent vacuum (case D). The time-dependent temperature is parameterized to simulate the parton-cascade model for the thermalization stage and one-dimensional scaling hydrodynamics for the subsequent cooling stage.

## Key numbers

- The linear sigma model parameters are set to λ = 20, v = 87.4, and H^(1/3) = 119 MeV, generating a pion mass of 135 MeV, sigma mass of 600 MeV, and pion decay constant f_π = 92.5 MeV.
- For case A (constant friction), the condition for the chiral symmetry restoration time τ_r ≥ 3 fm requires T_m > 230 MeV and η > 1 fm^(-1).
- For case B, the condition for τ_r ≥ 3 fm requires T_m ≥ 250 MeV and C_η ≳ 0.1.
- For case C, the condition for τ_r ≥ 3 fm requires T_m ≥ 260 MeV and C_η ≥ 2.
- For case D, the condition for τ_r ≥ 3 fm requires T_m ≥ 220 MeV and C_η ≳ 1.5–2.0.
- The ratio of the time interval in which chiral symmetry is restored to the time interval in which temperature exceeds the critical temperature is typically 0.5 when the friction is strong enough.
- The maximum temperature T_m is estimated as ~620 MeV for Au-Au collisions at RHIC and ~880 MeV at LHC energies, with the proper time τ_m typically about 1 fm.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Ishihara, 1999) where friction was not taken into account and it was found that chiral condensates cannot stay near the origin for a long time. The paper builds on earlier work by Biró and Greiner (who suggested η = 2.2 fm^(-1) at T ~ T_c), Rischke (who calculated η for the broken phase), and Yabu et al. (who obtained η = 0.7 m_π using the Caldeira-Leggett method). The results enable more realistic modeling of disoriented chiral condensate (DCC) formation scenarios, showing that the conventional assumption of chiral symmetry restoration for a sufficiently long time is not automatically guaranteed and requires precise estimation of both the maximum temperature and the friction for the soft mode.
