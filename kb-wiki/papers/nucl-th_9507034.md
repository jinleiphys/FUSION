# Quantum Dissipation

> We address the question of the microscopic origin of dissipation in collective motion of a quantum many--body system in the framework of a parametric random matrix approach to the intrinsic dynamics. We show that the fluctuation--dissipation theorem is generally violated and, moreover, energy diffusion has a markedly non--Gaussian character and the corresponding distribution has very long tails. Such features do not support a Langevin or Fokker--Planck approach to dissipation in collective nuclear motion.

## Key claim

The fluctuation-dissipation theorem is generally violated in the quantum many-body system studied, and the energy diffusion process is markedly non-Gaussian with very long tails, which does not support a Langevin or Fokker-Planck approach to dissipation in collective nuclear motion.

## Method

The authors model the intrinsic dynamics of a quantum many-body system using a parametric random matrix approach, where the Hamiltonian depends on a collective coordinate X(t) that evolves classically at constant velocity V0. They solve the time-dependent Schrödinger equation numerically for a finite system of N=101 levels with GOE spectral fluctuations, and also derive analytic solutions in the adiabatic and diabatic limits for an infinite system with an exponentially increasing level density.

## Key numbers

- For the numerical simulation: N=101 levels, bandwidth N0=21, Gaussian correlation F(x)=exp(-x²/2) with X0=1, and initial condition n_k(0)=δ_{k,51}
- The diffusion constant D(V0²) exhibits quadratic velocity dependence, with D(V0²) = πℏW0V0²/X0² = ℏΓ↓V0²/(2X0²) in the adiabatic limit, where Γ↓=2πW0
- In the diabatic limit, the relation between friction and diffusion is βD = γ(1 + β²κ0²/4), showing violation of the Einstein fluctuation-dissipation theorem
- The spreading width is given by Γ↓=2πW0

## Context

This work extends the parametric random matrix approach introduced by the same authors in earlier work (Bulgac, Dang, Kusnezov, 1995) to study dissipation in collective nuclear motion. It contradicts the long-standing suggestion by Hill and Wheeler (1953) that Landau-Zener transitions are the origin of nuclear dissipation, and also contradicts the classical "wall formula" of Swiatecki (1974) and various phenomenological models based on Langevin or Fokker-Planck equations. The results enable future investigation of whether the intrinsic characteristics identified here have a meaningful classical limit, and suggest that a fully quantum treatment of both fast and slow subsystems may be necessary.
