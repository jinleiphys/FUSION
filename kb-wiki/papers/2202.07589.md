# Theoretical description of pygmy (dipole) resonances

> Stable and unstable nuclei with neutron excess ($N>Z$) show - in the isovector dipole transition strength distribution - a small hump around the neutron emission threshold energy known as Pygmy Dipole Resonance (PDR). One of its main features is the isospin mixing allowing the experimental studies with both isovector and isoscalar probes. Different theoretical approaches and methodologies are used to deduce the characteristics of the PDR. In this Chapter, the various mean-field theories and their extensions, devoted to understand and reproduce the strength distribution of these low-lying dipole states, are summarised. Special attention is dedicated to the calculations of the inelastic cross section, aspect that is particularly important in the investigation with isoscalar probes, such as $α$-particles or $^{17}$O. The relevance of the radial form factors is presented in relation to the inelastic cross-section calculations.

## Key claim

The Pygmy Dipole Resonance (PDR) is characterized by a strong isospin mixing that allows excitation by both isovector and isoscalar probes, but below the neutron emission threshold the PDR states split into two parts: a low-energy region excited by both probe types and a higher-energy region populated only by the isovector interaction. This isospin splitting appears to be a general property of all neutron-excess nuclei investigated to date, though its presence above the neutron emission threshold remains unconfirmed.

## Method

The authors review macroscopic approaches (extensions of the Goldhaber-Teller and Steinwedel-Jensen models to three-fluid systems of protons, neutron core, and neutron valence) and microscopic approaches based on Hartree-Fock plus Random-Phase Approximation (RPA) with Skyrme or Gogny effective interactions. For the microscopic calculations, the RPA equations are solved to obtain transition densities and reduced transition probabilities B(E1) for dipole states, using the isovector operator O^{(IV)}_{1M} and the isoscalar operator O^{(IS)}_{1M} (with the spurious center-of-mass contribution removed). Special attention is given to calculating inelastic cross sections and radial form factors for comparison with experimental data from isoscalar probes such as alpha particles and 17O.

## Key numbers

- The IVGDR energy centroid follows E_x = 31.2 A^{-1/3} + 20.6 A^{-1/6} as a function of mass number A.
- For 68Ni, the PDR peak appears at 11 MeV in the gamma-ray spectrum from relativistic Coulomb excitation on a Au target at 600 MeV/u.
- The ratio of PDR energy to IVGDR energy is expressed as E_PDR/E_IVGDR = C (Z/(Z+N_V))^{1/2} [1 - (R_bar * y)/(20 a^2)], where y = R_n - R_p is the neutron skin thickness, R_bar is the average radius, and a is the diffuseness.
- For 132Sn, RPA calculations with SGII Skyrme interaction show the IVGDR mode around 15 MeV and the ISGDR mode around 30 MeV, with the PDR appearing at lower energy.
- The discrete RPA dipole states are convoluted with a 1-MeV width Lorentzian to produce continuous strength distributions.

## Context

This chapter reviews theoretical approaches to the PDR, extending the macroscopic models of Goldhaber and Teller (1948) and Steinwedel and Jensen (1950) and the microscopic RPA framework of Rowe (2010) and Ring and Schuck (2004). It builds on experimental work by Endres et al. (2010) showing the isospin splitting of the PDR in 124Sn, and by Wieland et al. (2009) and Rossi et al. (2013) on 68Ni. The review enables future work to clarify the collective nature of the PDR, the interplay between isoscalar and isovector contributions, and the behavior of the PDR in strongly deformed nuclei, where existing experimental data are insufficient.
