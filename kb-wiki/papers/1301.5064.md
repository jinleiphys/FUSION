# Tensor hypercontraction: A universal technique for the resolution of matrix elements of local, finite-range $N$-body potentials in many-body quantum problems

> Configuration-space matrix elements of N-body potentials arise naturally and ubiquitously in the Ritz-Galerkin solution of many-body quantum problems. For the common specialization of local, finite-range potentials, we develop the eXact Tensor HyperContraction (X-THC) method, which provides a quantized renormalization of the coordinate-space form of the N-body potential, allowing for a highly separable tensor factorization of the configuration-space matrix elements. This representation allows for substantial computational savings in chemical, atomic, and nuclear physics simulations, particularly with respect to difficult "exchange-like" contractions.

## Key claim

The paper claims that an exact and separable decomposition (X-THC) exists for any local, finite-range N-body potential when the one-particle basis set is of the direct-product polynomial type, enabling a reduction in computational scaling for exchange-like contractions from O(M^(2ND)) to O(M^(ND+1)) without any approximation. The hedge is that this exactness is conditional on the basis being of the polynomial direct-product form, such as Hermite functions, which are common in nuclear physics but not in electronic structure.

## Method

The method combines density fitting (DF) with a Gaussian quadrature (discrete variable representation, DVR) to achieve a "perfect dealiasing renormalization." First, the products of basis functions are exactly expanded in an auxiliary polynomial basis via DF. Then, the three-index overlap integrals are exactly resolved by a Gaussian quadrature grid, which provides a natural decomposition that separates the bra and ket indices. This yields the X-THC factorization where the integral tensor is expressed as a product of collocation matrices X and a quantized renormalized potential matrix Z.

## Key numbers

- The computational scaling for forming a generalized pairing tensor is reduced from O(M^(2ND)) (conventional) to O(M^(ND+1)) (X-THC), where M is the number of basis functions per dimension, N is the number of bodies, and D is the number of spatial dimensions.
- For a one-dimensional (D=1) problem with M+1 Hermite functions, the auxiliary basis consists of 2M+1 Hermite functions, and the Gaussian quadrature uses 2M+1 nodes.
- In the practical demonstration with N=2 and D=1,2,3, X-THC is "several orders of magnitude faster than conventional approaches for the largest M studied."
- For a separable potential with N_w=8 terms, the X-THC scaling is O(N_w M^(ND+1)), compared to O(N_w M^(ND+N)) for conventional approaches.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previously introduced Tensor HyperContraction (THC) method for electronic structure (Hohenstein 2012, Parrish 2012) by proving that an exact version exists for polynomial basis sets. It directly addresses limitations in nuclear density functional theory (DFT), where the zero-range Skyrme pseudopotential (Skyrme 1958) is used for computational savings despite known problems, and the finite-range Gogny pseudopotential (Decharge 1980) still contains problematic density-dependent terms. The X-THC method enables the use of finite-range, density-independent two- and three-body potentials in nuclear DFT with the same computational complexity as zero-range potentials, potentially removing artifacts that cause the collapse of beyond-mean-field methods (Dobaczewski 2007, Duguet 2009, Lacroix 2009, Bender 2009).
