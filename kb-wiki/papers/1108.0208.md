# Renormalization approach to constituent quark models of Quarkonium

> Constituent quark models, while successful, require a great deal of fine tuning of the short distance interactions by introducing phenomenological gluonic form factors which are ultimately designed to accurately reproduce the spectrum. We apply and develop renormalization ideas to reduce the short distance sensitivity and show that, as naively expected, but not explicitly implemented in the models, the physics of binding is entirely linked to the string tension whereas leptonic decays depend more on the gluon exchange potential. We also show how the spectrum of S- and D- $1^{--}$ states is successfully intertwined through the singular tensor interaction.

## Key claim
The authors show that by applying renormalization techniques to remove short-distance regulators in constituent quark models, the binding energies of excited charmonium states become largely insensitive to the short-range part of the potential, with the ground state mass serving as the only input needed from the short-distance region.

## Method
The authors solve the non-relativistic Schrödinger equation for charmonium using the Cornell potential (Coulomb plus linear confinement) and its extensions including tensor and spin-orbit terms from one-gluon exchange. They impose a short-distance cutoff \(r_c\) and fix the ground state (\(J/\psi\)) mass as input, then determine excited state energies by matching logarithmic derivatives of the wave functions at \(r_c\) and taking the limit \(r_c \to 0\). For coupled S- and D-wave channels, they use auxiliary boundary conditions (BC5 and BC6) to determine the D/S asymptotic ratio \(\eta\) and enforce orthogonality between states.

## Context
This work extends the renormalization approach previously applied to nuclear and atomic systems (Ruiz Arriola 2007, Pavon Valderrama 2005) to heavy quarkonium, building on the Cornell potential (Eichten 1979) and the constituent quark model of Vijande (2005). It contradicts the common practice of introducing phenomenological gluonic form factors to regularize short-distance singularities, showing that the ground state mass can be used as a renormalization condition to predict excited states with minimal short-distance sensitivity. The method enables a disentanglement of binding physics (dominated by the string tension) from leptonic decay physics (more dependent on the one-gluon exchange potential), and it provides a framework to assess theoretical uncertainties from short-distance modeling in quarkonium spectroscopy.
