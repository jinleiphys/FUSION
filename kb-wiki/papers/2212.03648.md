# Understanding globular cluster abundances through nuclear reactions

> Globular clusters contain multiple stellar populations, with some previous generation of stars polluting the current stars with heavier elements. Understanding the history of globular clusters is helpful in understanding how galaxies merged and evolved and therefore constraining the site or sites of this historic pollution is a priority. The acceptable temperature and density conditions of these polluting sites depend on critical reaction rates. In this paper, three experimental studies helping to constrain astrophysically important reaction rates are briefly discussed.

# Understanding globular cluster abundances through nuclear reactions

## Key claim

The paper argues that three different experimental approaches, proton inelastic scattering, one-proton transfer reactions, and direct recoil detection, are each necessary to resolve specific nuclear data uncertainties that currently limit our ability to constrain the temperature and density conditions of the polluting sites responsible for abundance anomalies in globular clusters; the authors conclude that two tentative resonances in the 22Ne(p,γ)23Na reaction likely do not exist and should be omitted from future evaluations, but this conclusion depends on the absence of evidence in a single inelastic-scattering experiment.

## Method

The authors performed three separate experiments at the Maier-Leibnitz Laboratory in Garching and at TRIUMF in Vancouver. For 23Na, a 14-MeV proton beam was used with the Q3D spectrograph to measure the 23Na(p,p')23Na* inelastic-scattering reaction, populating states via a compound-nucleus mechanism to search for resonances near Ex = 8862 and 8894 keV. For 31P, a 25-MeV 3He beam was used with the Q3D spectrograph to measure the 30Si(3He,d)31P one-proton transfer reaction, extracting proton spectroscopic factors and proton widths via Distorted-Wave Born Approximation calculations using the code fresco. For 40Ca, a 39K beam from the Off-Line Ion Source at TRIUMF was directed into a windowless hydrogen gas target at three energies corresponding to resonances at Er = 337, 606, and 666 keV, with the DRAGON recoil separator detecting the 40Ca recoils in coincidence with gamma rays from the BGO detector array.

## Key numbers

- The two tentative resonances in 22Ne(p,γ)23Na are located at Er = 65 and 100 keV (Ex = 8862 and 8894 keV, respectively); the higher resonance was ruled out by a LUNA experiment (PhysRevLett.121.172701), while the lower resonance may still increase the reaction rate considerably if it exists.
- For the 30Si(p,γ)31P reaction, the major remaining uncertainty is the unknown spin-parity of the Er = 149-keV resonance (Ex = 7446 keV); if this state has ℓ = 2 it may contribute to the reaction rate, but if ℓ = 3 the contribution is negligibly small.
- The 39K(p,γ)40Ca reaction was measured at three resonance energies: Er = 337, 606, and 666 keV; the observed gamma-ray intensities following proton capture do not follow the expected pattern from a previous study (Kikstra 1990).

## Context

This work extends the ongoing effort to constrain the nuclear reaction rates that govern the sodium-oxygen anticorrelation observed in globular clusters, a phenomenon that points to multiple stellar populations and past pollution events. The 22Ne(p,γ)23Na reaction has been the focus of direct measurements at DRAGON at TRIUMF (Lennarz 2020, Hutcheon 2003) and at LUNA (PhysRevC.95.015806, PhysRevC.94.055804), while the 30Si(p,γ)31P reaction rate was previously identified as a bottleneck in nucleosynthesis flow from magnesium to potassium. The 39K(p,γ)40Ca direct measurement with DRAGON builds on prior identification of astrophysically important resonances (Dermigny 2017) and will be complemented by a pending experiment at iThemba LABS using the AFRODITE array of HPGe clovers to resolve discrepancies in gamma-ray decay patterns.
