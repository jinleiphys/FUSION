# Preparation of excited states for nuclear dynamics on a quantum computer

> We study two different methods to prepare excited states on a quantum computer, a key initial step to study dynamics within linear response theory. The first method uses unitary evolution for a short time $T=\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{1-F})$ to approximate the action of an excitation operator $\hat{O}$ with fidelity $F$ and success probability $P\approx1-F$. The second method probabilistically applies the excitation operator using the Linear Combination of Unitaries (LCU) algorithm. We benchmark these techniques on emulated and real quantum devices, using a toy model for thermal neutron-proton capture. Despite its larger memory footprint, the LCU-based method is efficient even on current generation noisy devices and can be implemented at a lower gate cost than a naive analysis would suggest. These findings show that quantum techniques designed to achieve good asymptotic scaling on fault tolerant quantum devices might also provide practical benefits on devices with limited connectivity and gate fidelity.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Linear Combination of Unitaries (LCU) method for preparing excited states on quantum computers can be practically useful on current noisy devices despite its larger memory footprint, contradicting the expectation that such techniques are only beneficial for fault-tolerant quantum computers. The time-dependent method achieves fidelity F with success probability proportional to the infidelity (1-F), while the LCU method achieves higher success probability whenever the target infidelity is less than 1/6.

## Method

The authors implemented two state preparation protocols on both emulated and real superconducting quantum devices using a toy model for thermal neutron-proton capture. The first method applies short-time unitary evolution U(γ) = exp(-iγO) controlled by an ancilla qubit, followed by projective measurement to select the excited state component. The second method uses the Linear Combination of Unitaries (LCU) algorithm, which decomposes the excitation operator O into a sum of unitaries and employs prepare and select unitaries on an ancilla register to probabilistically apply the exact excitation operator.

## Key numbers

- For the time-dependent method, the time parameter γ must satisfy γ ≤ √(6(1-F_min))/Λ to guarantee a minimum fidelity F_min, where Λ is the 1-norm of the coefficient vector in the LCU decomposition of O
- The success probability for the time-dependent method is bounded by P_s ≤ sin²(√(6(1-F_min))) ≈ 6(1-F_min) for small infidelities
- The LCU-based method achieves success probability P_s^LCU = η²/Λ², where η = ||O|Ψ₀⟩|| is the norm of the excited state
- The LCU method has higher success probability than the time-dependent method whenever the target infidelity satisfies Δ_f ≤ 1/6
- For the simple single-qubit excitation operator O(θ) = cos(θ)X + sin(θ)𝟙, the time evolution operator can be implemented exactly using four CNOT gates, four Z-rotations, and five additional single-qubit gates

## Context

This work extends the state preparation approach proposed in Roggero (2019) for computing exclusive scattering in linear response theory, providing a detailed analysis of efficiency bounds and practical implementation on current quantum hardware. The methods are motivated by the n(p,d)γ reaction relevant for big-bang nucleosynthesis, building on precise classical computations by Park (1998) and Chen (1999). The results enable quantum computation of nuclear reaction dynamics on NISQ devices and suggest that techniques designed for fault-tolerant quantum computers, such as the LCU algorithm from Childs (2012), can provide practical benefits on current noisy devices with limited connectivity and gate fidelity.
