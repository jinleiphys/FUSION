# First-order phase transition from hypernuclear matter to deconfined quark matter obeying new constraints from compact star observations

> We reconsider the problem of the hyperon puzzle and its suggested solution by quark deconfinement within the two-phase approach to hybrid compact stars with recently obtained hadronic and quark matter equations of state. For the hadronic phase we employ the hypernuclear equation of state from the lowest order constrained variational method and the quark matter phase is described by a sufficiently stiff equation of state based on a color superconducting nonlocal Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model with constant (model nlNJLA) and with density-dependent (model nlNJLB) parameters. We study the model dependence of the phase transition obtained by a Maxwell construction. Our study confirms that also with the present set of equations of state quark deconfinement presents a viable solution of the hyperon puzzle even for the new constraint on the lower limit of the maximum mass from PSR J0740+6620. In this work we provide with model nlNJLB for the first time a hybrid star EoS with an intermediate hypernuclear matter phase between the nuclear and color superconducting quark matter phases, for which the maximum mass of the compact star reaches $2.2~M_\odot$, in accordance with most recent constraints. In model nlNJLA such a phase cannot be realised because the phase transition onset is at low densities, before the hyperon threshold density is passed. We discuss possible consequences of the hybrid equation of state for the deconfinement phase transition in symmetric matter as it will be probed in future heavy-ion collisions at FAIR, NICA and corresponding energy scan programs at the CERN and RHIC facilities.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a first-order phase transition from hypernuclear matter to color-superconducting quark matter can solve the hyperon puzzle while satisfying the new lower limit on maximum mass from PSR J0740+6620, but only when the quark matter equation of state includes a density-dependent bag pressure (model nlNJLB); the constant-parameter model (nlNJLA) fails because the deconfinement onset occurs at densities below the hyperon threshold.

## Method

The authors construct hybrid star equations of state using a two-phase approach with a Maxwell construction for the phase transition. The hadronic phase is described by the lowest order constrained variational (LOCV) method with AV18 nucleon-nucleon interactions plus Urbana three-body forces, including non-interacting Lambda and Sigma hyperons under beta-equilibrium and charge neutrality. The quark matter phase is described by a nonlocal Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (nlNJL) model with color superconductivity, using either constant parameters (model nlNJLA) or density-dependent parameters for the vector coupling and confining bag pressure (model nlNJLB). The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations are solved to obtain mass-radius relations for compact stars.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of hybrid star reaches 2.2 solar masses for model nlNJLB, in accordance with most recent constraints
- The hyperon onset density corresponds to a chemical potential of 1063 MeV
- The van der Waals excluded volume density for nucleons is n_VdW = 0.20 ± 0.018 fm^-3, based on nucleon radius r_N = 0.67 ± 0.2 fm
- The density for 50% reduction of the chiral condensate is n_chiSR = 0.256^{+0.050}_{-0.036} fm^-3, corresponding to a chemical potential of 1025 MeV
- The LOCV(Y) hadronic equation of state is considered inapplicable above a chemical potential of about 1050 MeV

## Context

This work extends the two-phase approach to hybrid stars previously studied by Burgio (2002) and Baldo (2003), who suggested quark deconfinement as a solution to the weak hyperon puzzle but could not reach 2 solar masses. It builds on the color-superconducting NJL model of Blaschke (2005) and Klahn (2006), and the density-dependent relativistic meanfield model DD2 with hyperons from Lastowiecki (2011). The paper enables future searches for deconfinement phase transitions in symmetric matter at facilities such as FAIR, NICA, and the energy scan programs at CERN and RHIC.
