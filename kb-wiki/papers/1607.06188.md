# Timescales in heavy ion collisions

> The study of high energy collisions between heavy nuclei is a field unto itself, distinct from nuclear and particle physics. A defining aspect of heavy ion physics is the importance of a bulk, self-interacting system with a rich space-time substructure. I focus on the issue of timescales in heavy ion collisions, starting with proof from low-energy collisions that femtoscopy can, indeed, measure very long timescales. I then discuss the relativistic case, where detailed measurements over three orders of magnitude in energy reveal a timescale increase that might be due to a first-order phase transition. I discuss also consistency in evolution timescales as determined from traditional longitudinal sizes and a novel analysis using shape information.

## Key claim

Femtoscopy can measure very long emission durations in heavy ion collisions, but only when the analysis is performed in the correct rest frame of the emitting source rather than the laboratory frame; previous failures to observe long timescales were due to this frame choice rather than a limitation of the technique itself.

## Method

The authors measured two-proton correlation functions at low relative momentum in Ar+Sc collisions at E/A=80 MeV and re-analyzed Xe+Al data at E/A=31 MeV, selecting longitudinal and transverse correlation functions by cuts on the angle between relative momentum and total momentum defined in the source rest frame rather than the laboratory frame. For relativistic collisions, they compiled the world dataset of pion HBT radii from central heavy ion collisions over three orders of magnitude in energy, comparing the difference R_out^2 - R_side^2 as a function of collision energy. They also extracted evolution timescales from the transverse-mass dependence of the longitudinal HBT radius using the Sinyukov formula, and cross-checked these timescales against spatial anisotropy measurements from non-central collisions.

## Key numbers

- Emission duration of 20-40 fm/c extracted from Ar+Sc collisions at E/A=80 MeV for a spherical Gaussian source with radius r_0=4.5-4.8 fm
- Emission duration of 1300 fm/c extracted from Xe+Al collisions at E/A=31 MeV for a spherical source with radius R=3.5 fm, the longest timescale ever measured with multidimensional intensity interferometry in subatomic physics
- A clear peak in R_out^2 - R_side^2 observed around sqrt(s_NN)=15 GeV in RHIC Beam Energy Scan data, consistent with a first-order phase transition signature
- HBT radii measured over three orders of magnitude in collision energy from sqrt(s_NN)=2.35 GeV to 2760 GeV
- Longitudinal HBT radii from STAR at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV fitted with the Sinyukov formula to extract evolution timescales

## Context

This work extends the femtoscopic technique validated at low energies by Lisa (1993) and Lisa (1994) to relativistic heavy ion collisions, where measurements over three orders of magnitude in energy reveal a possible first-order phase transition signature near sqrt(s_NN)=15 GeV. The results contradict the conclusion of the 2005 review by Lisa (2005) that no burning log signature existed, enabled by new data from the RHIC Beam Energy Scan program and ALICE at the LHC. The extracted evolution timescales from longitudinal HBT radii and the cross-check using spatial anisotropy measurements from non-central collisions provide complementary constraints for hydrodynamic models, which are increasingly needed to address the lower energy range where QCD physics is most relevant.
