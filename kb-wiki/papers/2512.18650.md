# Neural Network Construction of the Equation of State from Relativistic ab initio Calculations

> Constraining the nuclear matter equation of state (EOS) beyond saturation density is a central goal of nuclear physics and astrophysics. While the relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (RBHF) theory, an \textit{ab initio,} non-perturbative nuclear many-body theory starting from realistic interactions, accurately describes nuclear matter properties near the saturation density $ρ_0 \approx 0.16$ fm$^{-3}$, its applicability is currently limited to densities up to $3 ρ_0$, necessitating a reliable extrapolation to higher densities. In this work, we employ supervised machine learning to train thousands of fully connected neural networks on low-density RBHF data. By enforcing thermodynamic consistency and smoothness, we finally select a subset of 264 optimal models. These models employ the Swish activation function, which we identify as the most reliable choice for stable extrapolation after extensive testing and comparison. Using these models to extend the EOS over the full density range, we obtain the nuclear matter symmetry energy and then compute the neutron star mass-radius relation and tidal deformability, which are in a great harmony with current astronomical observations. The corresponding extrapolation uncertainty originates from the combined contributions of both the 264 optimal models and the linear regression on nuclear matter EOS, yielding a symmetry energy of $E\mathrm{_{sym}(5ρ_0)=136.0 \pm 52.8 MeV}$, a pressure of $P(5ρ_0) = 346.3 \pm 97.4 \mathrm{MeV/fm^{3}}$, a maximum neutron star mass of $M\mathrm{_{max}=2.18 \pm 0.18} M_{\odot}$, and a tidal deformability of $Λ_{1.4M_\odot} = 532 \pm 34$. This work establishes a general and data-driven framework to explore dense matter EOS by integrating \textit{ab initio} calculations with modern machine learning techniques.

## Key claim

The authors claim that a neural network trained exclusively on low-density (up to ~0.5 fm$^{-3}$) relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (RBHF) data can reliably extrapolate the nuclear matter equation of state to higher densities relevant for neutron star interiors, but only when the Swish activation function is used and thermodynamic consistency is enforced; the extrapolation is not guaranteed for other activation functions.

## Method

The authors trained 1000 fully connected neural networks (two hidden layers with 10 and 5 neurons) on 458 converged RBHF data points covering densities up to 0.5 fm$^{-3}$ and isospin asymmetries $\alpha \leq 0.7$ (or $\rho \leq 0.45$ fm$^{-3}$ for $\alpha \geq 0.8$). They systematically tested ReLU, Sigmoid, Tanh, and Swish activation functions, selecting Swish as the only one yielding thermodynamically stable extrapolations. From the 1000 networks, 264 were retained by imposing a validation mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) threshold of 1.5% or less, and the ensemble spread provided the uncertainty estimate.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density: $\rho_0 \approx 0.16$ fm$^{-3}$
- RBHF calculations converged up to densities $\rho \approx 0.52-0.57$ fm$^{-3}$, depending on isospin asymmetry
- Training set: 458 data points; validation set: 72 data points
- Minimum validation MAPE achieved: 0.95% (random seed 577)
- 264 neural networks satisfied the validation MAPE threshold of $\leq 1.5\%$
- Symmetry energy at $5\rho_0$: $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(5\rho_0) = 136.0 \pm 52.8$ MeV
- Pressure at $5\rho_0$: $P(5\rho_0) = 346.3 \pm 97.4$ MeV/fm$^3$
- Maximum neutron star mass: $M_{\mathrm{max}} = 2.18 \pm 0.18$ $M_\odot$
- Tidal deformability at $1.4 M_\odot$: $\Lambda_{1.4M_\odot} = 532 \pm 34$

## Context

This work extends the fully self-consistent RBHF framework in the full Dirac space developed by Wang et al. (2021, 2022), which previously could only be performed up to baryon densities of about 0.5 fm$^{-3}$. Unlike prior neural network approaches that mapped EOS directly to astrophysical observables (Fujimoto 2018, 2020; Traversi 2020; Morawski 2020) or incorporated the two-solar-mass constraint during training, this study directly links neural network modeling with ab initio nuclear matter calculations to produce genuine predictions for the high-density sector. The framework enables simultaneous evaluation of fitting and extrapolation capabilities and provides access to microscopic quantities such as the symmetry energy, which previous EOS-to-observable mapping approaches could not offer.
