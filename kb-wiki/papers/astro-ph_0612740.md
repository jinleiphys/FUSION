# Strangelets, Nuclearites, Q-balls--A Brief Overview

> Astrophysical bounds on the properties and abundances of primordial quark nuggets and cosmic ray strangelets are reviewed. New experiments to search for cosmic ray strangelets in lunar soil and from the International Space Station are described. Analogies with baryonic and supersymmetric Q-balls are briefly mentioned, as are prospects for strangelets as ultra-high energy cosmic rays.

# Strangelets, Nuclearites, Q-balls, A Brief Overview

## Key claim

Astrophysical bounds on primordial quark nuggets and cosmic ray strangelets are reviewed, but the existence of absolutely stable strange quark matter remains an open question not yet decided by experiment or observation; the survival of cosmological quark nuggets is unlikely unless a first-order quark-hadron phase transition occurred, which is currently not favored by lattice QCD studies at zero chemical potential.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical bounds on quark nugget survival from the early Universe (surface evaporation, boiling, and Big Bang nucleosynthesis constraints) and estimates strangelet production rates from binary strange star collisions and pulsar surface extraction. It then compares these flux predictions to existing experimental limits from mica track searches and neutron star conversion arguments, and describes two ongoing experiments: the Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer (AMS-02) on the International Space Station and the Lunar Soil Strangelet Search (LSSS) using a tandem accelerator on Apollo 11 lunar soil samples.

## Key numbers

- Primordial quark nugget flux hitting Earth: approximately 6.0 × 10⁵ A⁻¹ ρ₂₄ v₂₅₀ cm⁻² s⁻¹ sr⁻¹, where v₂₅₀ is speed in units of 250 km s⁻¹ and ρ₂₄ is mass density in units of 10⁻²⁴ g cm⁻³
- Big Bang nucleosynthesis constraint: only nuggets with A > A_BBN ≈ 10²² Ω_nug³ f_n³ are allowed, where Ω_nug is the nugget contribution to cosmic density and f_n ≤ 1 is surface penetrability
- Galactic binary strange star collision rate: approximately one collision every 10,000 years, releasing 10⁻¹⁰ to 10⁻⁶ M_⊙ per year
- Expected flux of color-flavor locked strangelets near Earth: approximately 10⁻⁶ A⁻¹·⁴⁷ cm⁻² s⁻¹ sr⁻¹
- Upper flux limit from ancient mica tracks: 8 × 10⁻¹⁹ cm⁻² s⁻¹ sr⁻¹ for nuggets with A > 1.4 × 10¹⁴
- Flux limit from neutron star conversion argument: approximately 4 × 10⁻⁴² v₂₅₀² cm⁻² s⁻¹ sr⁻¹ for nuggets with A < 10³¹
- LSSS sensitivity: 10⁻¹⁷ cm⁻² s⁻¹ sr⁻¹ over a wide mass range, using 15 grams of Apollo 11 lunar soil with effective cosmic ray exposure time of approximately 500 million years
- GZK-cutoff energy for strangelets: E_Total ≈ 10¹⁹ A eV, pushing the cutoff beyond 3 × 10²⁰ eV for large A

## Context

This review extends earlier work on strange quark matter stability by Witten (1984) and subsequent studies of nugget evaporation by Alcock and Farhi (1985) and boiling by Alcock and collaborators (1989). It connects to the broader search for dark matter candidates and ultra-high energy cosmic rays beyond the GZK-cutoff, as discussed by Greisen (1966). The paper enables experimental searches with AMS-02 and LSSS to either detect strangelets or place severe limits on stable strange quark matter, with analogous bounds applicable to baryonic and supersymmetric Q-balls as described by Kusenko at the same workshop.
