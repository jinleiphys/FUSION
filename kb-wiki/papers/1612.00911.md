# Spins in Exotic Nuclei: RI-beam Experiments with Polarized Targets

> Spin-degrees of freedom play a significant role in exotic nuclei. Scattering with polarized protons has potential as a powerful tool to explore the spin effects in nuclei. This lecture note discusses the background, current status, and future prospects of experimental studies with spin polarized targets and radioactive ion beams.

## Key claim

This lecture note argues that spin-polarized targets combined with radioactive ion (RI) beams provide a powerful experimental tool to directly probe spin-dependent interactions in exotic nuclei, particularly the spin-orbit coupling, which is predicted to change significantly in neutron-rich nuclei far from the stability line.

## Method

The paper reviews the historical development of spin-orbit coupling in nuclear physics, from the original shell-model proposal by Mayer (1949) and Haxel, Jensen, and Suess (1949) through modern experiments. It describes the construction and operation of a triplet-dynamic-nuclear-polarization (triplet-DNP) target using a naphthalene crystal doped with pentacene, which achieves proton polarization at low magnetic fields (approximately 0.1 T) and temperatures around 100 K. The target was used in inverse-kinematics scattering experiments at the RIPS facility at RIKEN, where vector analyzing powers for p-6,8He elastic scattering at 71 MeV/u were measured.

## Key numbers

* Proton polarization of approximately 10-20% was achieved in the triplet-DNP target during the p-6,8He scattering experiments at RIKEN, operated at approximately 0.1 T and 100 K.
* A new prototype laser system achieved a proton polarization of approximately 41% at 0.6 T and room temperature in a p-terphenyl-d4 sample doped with pentacene-d14, using a 126-ns pulse width at 1-kHz repetition rate.
* The spin-orbit potential peak amplitudes in 6,8He were found to be 1.3-2 MeV·fm, compared to 3.5-5.5 MeV·fm for stable nuclei.
* The spin-orbit splitting between 1p3/2 and 1p1/2 in 35Si was reduced by 25% compared to that in 37S, as reported by Burgunder (2014).
* The target thickness requirement for RI-beam experiments with a beam intensity of 10^3 s^-1 is greater than 10^20 cm^-2 to achieve a luminosity larger than 10^23 s^-1 cm^-2.

## Context

This lecture note extends the theoretical predictions of Dobaczewski (1994) and Otsuka (2005) regarding changes in spin-orbit coupling in neutron-rich nuclei, and the experimental findings of Schiffer (2004) on energy differences between proton h11/2 and g7/2 orbits in Sn isotopes. The work enables future experimental studies of spin-orbit splitting in exotic nuclei using transfer reactions and resonant scattering with polarized targets, building on the triplet-DNP target technique developed at RIKEN.
