# Chemical and mechanical spinodals a unique liquid-gas instability

> We demonstrate that the instabilities of asymmetric nuclear matter at sub-saturation densities do not present two types of instabilities as usually discussed but a unique one. The associated order parameter is everywhere dominated by the isoscalar density and so the transition is of liquid-gas type even in the so-called chemical instability region.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that asymmetric nuclear matter (ANM) below saturation density does not exhibit two distinct types of instabilities (mechanical and chemical) as previously argued, but only a single instability whose order parameter is everywhere dominated by the isoscalar density, making the transition of liquid-gas type even in the region traditionally called the chemical instability.

## Method

The authors perform a stability analysis of asymmetric nuclear matter by examining the curvature matrix of the free energy density with respect to baryon density and asymmetry density. They compute the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of this matrix for several Skyrme (SLy230a, SGII, SIII) and Gogny (D1, D1S, D1P) effective interactions, and analyze the direction of the unstable eigenvector to determine the nature of the instability. The analysis is linked to the sign of the Landau parameter F_np and the positivity of the symmetry energy curvature F_33.

## Key numbers

- The spinodal region remains almost identical to the zero-temperature one up to a temperature of 5 MeV.
- For recent forces (SLy230b and D1P), the sound velocity in almost all the spinodal region is larger than 0.06c (where c is the speed of light).
- The contour lines of equal imaginary sound velocity are shown at values i0.09c, i0.06c, i0.03c, and 0 (the spinodal border).
- The asymmetry can be varied up to rho_p < 3 rho_n without quantitatively changing the density at which instability appears or the imaginary sound velocity compared to symmetric nuclear matter.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analysis (Mar 2003) and contradicts the earlier interpretation by Muller and Serot (1995) that asymmetric nuclear matter has separate mechanical and chemical instability regions. The paper uses density functional approaches from Skyrme and Gogny effective interactions, including forces such as SLy230a (Chabanat 1997), SGII (Nguyen 1981), SIII (Beiner 1975), D1 (Gogny 1975), D1S (Berger 1991), and D1P (Farine 1999). The results enable a unified understanding of the liquid-gas phase transition in asymmetric nuclear matter and support the fractionation phenomenon where the dense phase becomes more symmetric while the gas phase becomes more asymmetric.
