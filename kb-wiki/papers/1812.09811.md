# Comparing treatments of weak reactions with nuclei in simulations of core-collapse supernovae

> We perform an extensive study of the influence of nuclear weak interactions on core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe), paying particular attention to consistency between nuclear abundances in the equation of state (EOS) and nuclear weak interactions. We compute properties of uniform matter based on the variational method. For inhomogeneous nuclear matter, we take a full ensemble of nuclei into account with various finite-density and thermal effects and directly use the nuclear abundances to compute nuclear weak interaction rates. To quantify the impact of a consistent treatment of nuclear abundances on CCSN dynamics, we carry out spherically symmetric CCSN simulations with full Boltzmann neutrino transport, systematically changing the treatment of weak interactions, EOSs, and progenitor models. We find that the inconsistent treatment of nuclear abundances between the EOS and weak interaction rates weakens the EOS dependence of both the dynamics and neutrino signals. We also test the validity of two artificial prescriptions for weak interactions of light nuclei and find that both prescriptions affect the dynamics. Furthermore, there are differences in neutrino luminosities by ~10% and in average neutrino energies by 0.25-1 MeV from those of the fiducial model. We also find that the neutronization burst neutrino signal depends on the progenitor more strongly than on the EOS, preventing a detection of this signal from constraining the EOS.

# Comparing treatments of weak reactions with nuclei in simulations of core-collapse supernovae

## Key claim

Inconsistent treatment of nuclear abundances between the equation of state (EOS) and weak interaction rates artificially weakens the apparent EOS dependence of both core-collapse supernova dynamics and neutrino signals; when consistency is enforced, the differences between EOS models become more pronounced, and the neutronization burst neutrino signal depends more strongly on the progenitor model than on the EOS, preventing its use as a direct EOS constraint.

## Method

The authors performed spherically symmetric core-collapse supernova simulations with full Boltzmann neutrino transport, using a variational method (VM) EOS that includes a full ensemble of nuclei with finite-density and thermal effects, and a relativistic mean field (FYSS) EOS for comparison. They computed electron and positron capture rates on heavy and light nuclei directly from the nuclear abundances provided by each EOS, and systematically varied the treatment of weak interactions, EOS, and progenitor masses (11.2, 15, 27, and 40 solar masses).

## Key numbers

- Neutrino luminosities differ by approximately 10% between models with consistent versus inconsistent treatment of nuclear abundances.
- Average neutrino energies differ by 0.25–1 MeV from those of the fiducial model (V112).
- The VM EOS predicts a cold neutron star radius for 1.35 solar masses of around 14.5 km (Shen EOS), which is larger than the observational constraint from GW170817 that the radius should be less than 13 km.
- Simulations were run up to 400 ms after bounce for the 11.2 solar mass progenitor and 500 ms for other progenitors.
- The spatial domain covered 0 to 5000 km with 384 radial grid points, and neutrino momentum space used 20 energy bins (0–300 MeV) and 10 angular bins.

## Context

This work extends previous comparisons by Nagakura et al. (2017) and Furusawa et al. (2017) that used a common weak reaction table between different EOS models, by instead computing weak interaction rates consistently with the nuclear abundances in each EOS. It also builds on studies by Sullivan et al. (2016) and Togashi et al. (2018) that investigated electron capture sensitivity under consistent treatment but did not address the inconsistency between EOS and weak rates, nor the role of light nuclei. The results enable more reliable predictions of neutrino signals from core-collapse supernovae and highlight that the neutronization burst cannot be used to constrain the nuclear EOS due to stronger progenitor dependence.
