# Super-Symmetry transformation for excitation processes

> Quantum Mechanics SUper-SYmmetry (QM-SUSY) provides a general framework for studies using phenomenological potentials for nucleons (or clusters) interacting with a core. The SUSY potentials result from the transformation of the mean field potential in order to account for the Pauli blocking of the core orbitals. In this article, we discuss how other potentials (like external probes or residual interactions between the valence nucleons) are affected by the SUSY transformation. We illustrate how the SUSY transformations induce off-diagonal terms in coordinate space that play the essential role on the induced transition probabilities on two examples: the electric operators and Gaussian external fields. We show that excitation operators, doorway states, strength and sum rules are modified.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the standard "internal SUSY approximation", in which only the core-halo potential is SUSY-transformed while external fields and residual interactions are left unchanged, is not equivalent to the exact projection method that enforces Pauli blocking. The authors show that a consistent SUSY framework must also transform excitation operators and two-body interactions, and that this transformation induces off-diagonal terms in coordinate space that modify transition probabilities, doorway states, strength functions, and sum rules.

## Method

The authors develop a formal mapping using pseudo-unitary SUSY operators (both State Removal Potential, SRP, and Phase Equivalent Potential, PEP) that act on the full valence-space Hamiltonian, including external fields and residual interactions. They apply this formalism analytically to the harmonic oscillator potential and numerically to a Wood-Saxon plus surface potential representing the core-halo interaction in \(^{11}\)Be, computing the transformed potentials and the mapping of electric multipole operators and Gaussian external fields.

## Key numbers

- Harmonic oscillator parameters used for illustration: \(V_0 = 50\) MeV, \(\hbar\omega = 10\) MeV; the lowest energy state is at \(-35\) MeV.
- For the harmonic oscillator, the SRP transformation maps the original potential \(v_0^l\) to \(v_0^{l+1} + \hbar\omega\), i.e., the effective angular momentum increases by 1 and the potential is shifted by \(\hbar\omega = 10\) MeV.
- For the \(^{11}\)Be core-halo potential, the bound states are two \(s\)-states at \(-25.0\) MeV and \(-0.5\) MeV, and one \(p\)-state at \(-11\) MeV; the potential parameters are \(v_0 = -44.1\) MeV, \(\alpha = -10.15\) MeV, \(r_0 = 1.27\) fm, and \(a_0 = 0.75\) fm.
- Numerical calculations for the harmonic oscillator used a mesh of 400 points from 0 fm to 20 fm with vanishing boundary conditions.

## Context

This work extends the QM-SUSY framework previously applied to halo nuclei by Ridikas et al. (1996), Capel et al. (2003), and others, who used only the "internal" SUSY approximation for core-halo potentials. It also relates to comparisons between the full projector method and the internal SUSY approximation performed by Thompson et al. (2000) and Descouvemont et al. (2003). The consistent SUSY formalism presented here is mathematically equivalent to the full projection method and provides an exact framework for computing excitation processes and residual interactions in halo nuclei, enabling more accurate calculations of breakup, transfer reactions, and coupled-channel effects.
