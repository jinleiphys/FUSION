# Modeling barrier-top fission dynamics in a discrete-basis formalism

> A configuration-interaction model is presented for the barrier region of induced fission. The configuration space is composed of seniority-zero configurations constructed from self-consistent mean-field wave functions. The Hamiltonian matrix elements between configurations include diabatic and pairing interactions between particles. Other aspects of the Hamiltonian are treated statistically, guided by phenomenological input of compound-nucleus transmission coefficients. In this exploratory study the configuration space is restricted to neutron excitations only. A key observable calculated in the model is the fission-to-capture branching ratio. We find that both pairing and diabatic interactions are important for achieving large branching to the fission channels. In accordance with the transition-state theory of fission, the calculated branching ratio is found to be quite insensitive to the fission decay widths of the pre-scission configurations. However, the barrier-top dynamics appear to be quite different from transition-state theory in that the transport is distributed over many excited configurations at the barrier top.

## Key claim

The paper presents a configuration-interaction (CI) model for barrier-top fission dynamics that, while too simplified for quantitative predictions, is sufficiently realistic to allow qualitative conclusions about transport mechanisms; the authors find that transport is distributed over many excited configurations at the barrier top, which contradicts the transition-state theory assumption that fission proceeds through a small number of internal channels.

## Method

The authors construct a bridge Hamiltonian using seniority-zero configurations built from self-consistent mean-field wave functions along a fission path for 236U, with reference states defined by quadrupole constraint and energies rescaled by a factor of 0.37 to match empirical barrier heights. The Hamiltonian includes pairing interactions (strength G = 0.2 MeV) and diabatic interactions (strength h2 = 1.5 MeV) between configurations, while the compound nucleus and post-barrier states are treated statistically using Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) reservoirs. Reaction probabilities are computed using the trace formula (Datta formula) for the S-matrix, with the full Hamiltonian linking an entrance channel to capture and fission exit channels.

## Key numbers

- The barrier height for 236U calculated with the UNEDF1 Skyrme functional is between 5 and 6 MeV (accepted empirical value), but the HF PES gives barriers much higher than this value
- The scaling parameter f_pes = 0.37 is used to rescale reference state energies to bring the PES closer to empirical values
- The Q6 model uses 6 reference states at deformations q = (18, 22, 26, 29, 33, 37) barns
- The largest Q-block has dimension N_q = 153, with total bridge configuration dimension of 514
- The excitation energy cutoff is E_max = 4 MeV
- The pairing strength within Q-blocks is G = 0.2 MeV, determined by reproducing the first excited 0+ state at E_ex(1) = 0.92 MeV
- The diabatic interaction strength is h2 = 1.5 MeV
- The GOE reservoir dimension is N_GOE = 100 with internal interaction strength v = 0.1 MeV, producing level density rho_0 = 31.8 MeV^-1
- The entrance channel transmission factor for s-wave neutrons at E_n = 1 keV is T_in = 0.02
- The capture width is Gamma_c = 1.25 keV and fission width is Gamma_f = 15 keV
- The baseline branching ratio alpha^-1 = 0.95 at 4.5 MeV, compared to the experimental value of approximately 3 at the physical neutron threshold at 6.5 MeV
- Without diabatic interaction (h2 = 0.0), the branching ratio drops to alpha^-1 = 0.13
- With pairing strength reduced to G = 0.1 MeV, the branching ratio drops to alpha^-1 = 0.37

## Context

This work extends previous simplified models by Bertsch (2022) and Hagino (2020) by introducing a configuration-interaction computational framework for fission barrier dynamics, in contrast to the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) which treats dynamics as a Schrodinger equation of a few collective coordinates. The model contradicts transition-state theory by showing that transport is distributed over many excited configurations at the barrier top rather than through a small number of internal channels. The approach enables future studies of the energy dependence of reaction probabilities and the importance of diabatic interactions, though the authors note that the space must likely be extended to include broken-pair configurations and proton excitations to achieve quantitative agreement with experimental branching ratios.
