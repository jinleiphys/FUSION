# Signatures of quark-hadron phase transitions in general-relativistic neutron-star mergers

> Merging binaries of neutron stars are not only strong sources of gravitational waves, but also have the potential of revealing states of matter at densities and temperatures not accessible in laboratories. A crucial and long-standing question in this context is whether quarks are deconfined as a result of the dramatic increase in density and temperature following the merger. We present the first fully general-relativistic simulations of merging neutron stars including quarks at finite temperatures that can be switched off consistently in the equation of state. Within our approach, we can determine clearly what signatures a quark-hadron phase transition would leave in the gravitational-wave signal. In particular, we show that if the conditions are met for a phase transition to take place at several times nuclear saturation density, they would lead to a post-merger signal considerably different from the one expected from the inspiral, that can only probe the hadronic part of the equations of state, and to an anticipated collapse of the merged object. We also show that the phase transition leads to a very hot and dense quark core that, when it collapses to a black hole, produces a ringdown signal different from the hadronic one. Finally, in analogy with what is done in heavy-ion collisions, we use the evolution of the temperature and density in the merger remnant to illustrate the properties of the phase transition in a QCD phase diagram.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first fully general-relativistic simulations of merging neutron stars that include a finite-temperature equation of state with a first-order phase transition from hadrons to quarks, which can be switched off consistently. The authors show that if the conditions for a phase transition are met at several times nuclear saturation density, the post-merger gravitational-wave signal would be considerably different from the inspiral signal and the merged object would collapse earlier than in the purely hadronic case.

## Method

The authors performed fully general-relativistic hydrodynamical simulations of equal-mass binary neutron star mergers with total masses of 2.8 and 2.9 solar masses, using the Chiral Mean Field (CMF) model for the equation of state. For each mass, they ran two identical simulations: one with the standard CMF equation of state including quarks and a first-order phase transition (CMF_Q), and one with a purely hadronic version in which quarks were suppressed (CMF_H). The simulations used the Frankfurt/IllinoisGRMHD code with a fourth-order accurate finite-difference scheme and a neutrino-leakage scheme, evolving the spacetime with the Z4c formulation of the Einstein equations.

## Key numbers

* Maximum mass of a hadronic star at zero temperature and zero angular momentum: 2.07 solar masses (1.97 solar masses when quarks are included)
* Radius of a reference 1.4 solar mass star: 13.7 km
* Highest numerical grid resolution: approximately 250 meters covering the two stars, with a very-high resolution run at approximately 125 meters
* Quark fraction in the low-mass binary: up to 0.9 locally, representing approximately 15-20 percent of the total baryon mass
* Phase difference in gravitational-wave signal for the low-mass binary: approximately three radians by the time the CMF_Q binary collapses to a black hole
* Relative difference in ringdown frequency for the high-mass binary: less than 25 percent, yielding an overlap of 0.92
* Collapse time difference between reference and very-high resolution simulations: 1.5 percent

## Context

This work extends previous studies that either investigated hyperons (Sekiguchi 2011, Radice 2017), modeled quarks with the simple MIT bag model at zero temperature (Oechslin 2004), or included quarks with temperature effects but without a hadronic component or crust (Bauswein 2010). Unlike those earlier approaches, which used a conformally flat approximation of general relativity and smooth particle hydrodynamics, this paper employs a self-consistent nuclear equation of state with finite-temperature effects and a first-order phase transition, coupled to a fully general-relativistic spacetime evolution. The results enable future work on metastable quark cores, neutrino burst signals from phase transitions, and jumps in post-merger oscillation frequencies.
