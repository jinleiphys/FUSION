# Molecular Dynamics for Dense Matter

> We review a molecular dynamics method for nucleon many-body systems called the quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) and our studies using this method. These studies address the structure and the dynamics of nuclear matter relevant to the neutron star crusts, supernova cores, and heavy-ion collisions. A key advantage of QMD is that we can study dynamical processes of nucleon many-body systems without any assumptions on the nuclear structure. First we focus on the inhomogeneous structures of low-density nuclear matter consisting not only of spherical nuclei but also of nuclear "pasta", i.e., rod-like and slab-like nuclei. We show that the pasta phases can appear in the ground and equilibrium states of nuclear matter without assuming nuclear shape. Next we show our simulation of compression of nuclear matter which corresponds to the collapsing stage of supernovae. With increase of density, a crystalline solid of spherical nuclei change to a triangular lattice of rods by connecting neighboring nuclei. Finally, we discuss the fragment formation in expanding nuclear matter. Our results suggest that a generally accepted scenario based on the liquid-gas phase transition is not plausible at lower temperatures.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that nuclear "pasta" phases (rod-like and slab-like nuclei) can appear in the ground and equilibrium states of low-density nuclear matter without assuming any particular nuclear shape, using quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations that impose no prior structural constraints.

## Method

The authors employ a quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) model where each nucleon is represented by a Gaussian wavepacket with fixed width, and the total wavefunction is a direct product of these single-particle wavefunctions. The equations of motion for the wavepacket centroids are derived from a Hamiltonian that includes kinetic energy, a phenomenological Pauli potential to mimic fermionic properties, a Skyrme-type local potential with Coulomb and symmetry terms, and a momentum-dependent potential fitted to reproduce the real part of the optical potential. To simulate infinite nuclear matter, a cubic cell with periodic boundary conditions is used, and the ground state is obtained by cooling the system via damping equations of motion until the energy reaches a minimum.

## Key numbers

- The Pauli potential parameters are set to \(C_{\rm P}=207\) MeV, \(p_0=120\) MeV, and \(q_0=1.644\) fm to reproduce the kinetic energy of an ideal Fermi gas at zero temperature.
- Three equations of state are prepared with different incompressibilities: Soft (\(K=210\) MeV), Medium (\(K=280\) MeV), and Hard (\(K=380\) MeV), extracted from the curvature of the energy per nucleon at saturation density \(\rho_0 \simeq 0.165\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- The symmetry energy coefficient is taken as \(C_{\rm s}=25\) MeV, yielding a symmetry energy of \(34.6\) MeV for nuclear matter at saturation density.
- The single particle potential is constrained to \(U(0) = -80\) MeV, \(U(p)=0\) at \(E_{\rm lab}=200\) MeV, and \(U(p \rightarrow \infty) = \alpha + \beta = 77\) MeV, with an effective mass \(m^*=0.8\,m\) at \(\rho=\rho_0\).
- The Gaussian wavepacket width is set to \(\lambda = 1.45\) fm for the Medium EOS parameter set.

## Context

This work extends the earlier predictions of nuclear pasta structures by Ravenhall (1983) and Hashimoto (1984), which relied on the Wigner-Seitz approximation that imposes geometrical symmetry and allows only simple structures such as spheres, cylinders, and plates. The QMD approach removes these structural assumptions, enabling the study of dynamical processes such as the compression of nuclear matter corresponding to the collapsing stage of supernovae, where a crystalline solid of spherical nuclei transitions to a triangular lattice of rods. The method also enables simulations of fragment formation in expanding nuclear matter, suggesting that the generally accepted liquid-gas phase transition scenario is not plausible at lower temperatures.
