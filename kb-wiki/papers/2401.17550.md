# Second-order optimisation strategies for neural network quantum states

> The Variational Monte Carlo method has recently seen important advances through the use of neural network quantum states. While more and more sophisticated ansätze have been designed to tackle a wide variety of quantum many-body problems, modest progress has been made on the associated optimisation algorithms. In this work, we revisit the Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature, an optimiser that has been used extensively in a variety of simulations. We suggest improvements on the scaling and the direction of this optimiser, and find that they substantially increase its performance at a negligible additional cost. We also reformulate the Variational Monte Carlo approach in a game theory framework, to propose a novel optimiser based on decision geometry. We find that, on a practical test case for continuous systems, this new optimiser consistently outperforms any of the KFAC improvements in terms of stability, accuracy and speed of convergence. Beyond Variational Monte Carlo, the versatility of this approach suggests that decision geometry could provide a solid foundation for accelerating a broad class of machine learning algorithms.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a novel optimiser based on decision geometry, reformulating Variational Monte Carlo in a game theory framework, consistently outperforms both standard and improved Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature (KFAC) optimisers in terms of stability, accuracy, and speed of convergence for continuous quantum many-body systems, though the improvements to KFAC scaling and direction also substantially increase its performance at negligible additional cost.

## Method

The authors benchmark optimisation strategies on a system of spinless fermions in a one-dimensional harmonic trap with a Gaussian two-body interaction, using a neural network quantum state (NQS) ansatz with permutation-equivariant layers and generalised Slater matrices. They first revisit the Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature (KFAC) optimiser, identifying issues related to the use of energy as a loss function and permutation-equivariant layers, then propose improvements to its scaling and update direction. Separately, they reformulate the Variational Monte Carlo approach using decision geometry from game theory to construct a novel optimiser, and compare all methods across interaction strengths from V0=-20 to V0=20 and particle numbers from A=2 to A=6.

## Key numbers

* The NQS ansatz uses L=2 equivariant layers with width H=64 and one generalised Slater matrix, resulting in approximately 10^4 parameters.
* Monte Carlo sampling uses N_W=4096 walkers with 400 Metropolis-Hastings iterations per epoch for thermalisation.
* Pre-training uses 10^3 epochs with the Adam optimiser before any optimisation comparison.
* The interaction range is fixed at sigma_0=0.5, with interaction strength V_0 varied from -20 to 20 and particle number A from 2 to 6.
* Adam requires between 10^4 to 10^5 epochs to reach 5% accuracy in correlation energy (citing prior work).

## Context

This work extends prior applications of KFAC to Variational Monte Carlo with neural network quantum states, which had been used in challenging cases by Pfau et al. (2020), Spencer et al. (2020), and others, but required ad hoc adjustments to rectify parameter updates. The paper builds on the information geometry foundation of Amari (1983, 2016) and the KFAC algorithm of Martens (2015), and introduces decision geometry following Dawid (2005, 2007) and Gneiting (2007) as an alternative. The new optimiser enables more efficient VMC calculations for continuous many-body systems and suggests broader applicability to machine learning algorithms beyond VMC.
