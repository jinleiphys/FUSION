# Rapidity scan with multistage hydrodynamic and statistical thermal models

> We calibrate a (3+1)-dimensional multistage hybrid framework using the measured pseudo-rapidity distribution of charged particles and rapidity distribution of net protons for central Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=7.7,\,19.6,\,62.4,\,200$ GeV. We then study the thermodynamic properties of the nuclear matter along the beam direction, and the phase diagram regions probed by the hadronization process near the chemical freeze-out. Using the rapidity-dependent thermal yields of identified particles with full rapidity coverage from the hybrid framework, we apply different scenarios of the statistical thermal model to extract the thermodynamic parameters at the freeze-out, with the known system properties from the hybrid model as a closure test. We find significant theoretical uncertainties in the thermal models when applied to regions away from midrapidity. We also propose a thermal model inspired by the hybrid approach that includes thermal smearing and longitudinal flow for the nuclear matter created at low beam energies.

## Key claim

The authors calibrate a (3+1)-dimensional multistage hybrid framework to reproduce measured pseudo-rapidity distributions of charged particles and rapidity distributions of net protons for central Au+Au collisions at four beam energies, then use this framework to test whether statistical thermal models can reliably extract thermodynamic parameters away from midrapidity; they find significant theoretical uncertainties in thermal models when applied to regions away from midrapidity, particularly at low beam energies where boost invariance breaks down.

## Method

The authors construct a (3+1)-dimensional hybrid framework combining parametric initial conditions, the MUSIC hydrodynamic code with shear viscosity and baryon diffusion, the iS3D particlization sampler using the Cooper-Frye prescription on a constant-energy-density freeze-out surface at 0.26 GeV/fm^3, and the UrQMD hadronic afterburner. They calibrate the initial condition parameters (Table I) to reproduce experimental data for central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 7.7, 19.6, 62.4, and 200 GeV. Using the rapidity-dependent identified particle yields from this hybrid model, they apply three statistical thermal model scenarios (single source, discrete source, and continuous source with thermal smearing) to extract rapidity-dependent thermodynamic parameters, comparing the extracted parameters to the known freeze-out properties from the hybrid model as a closure test.

## Key numbers

* Freeze-out energy density: e_fo = 0.26 GeV/fm^3, corresponding to freeze-out temperature around 150 MeV at zero chemical potential
* Proton mass m_p = 938 MeV, much larger than freeze-out temperature T ≈ 160 MeV, justifying Maxwell-Boltzmann approximation for protons
* Thermal smearing widths: protons have Gaussian-like rapidity spread Δy ≈ 0.9, pions Δy ≈ 1.6, kaons Δy ≈ 1.2
* Initial condition parameters for 19.6 GeV: tau_0 = 1.8 fm, s_0 = 5.56, N_B = 0.97
* Initial condition parameters for 200 GeV: tau_0 = 1.0 fm, s_0 = 16.6, N_B = 0.5
* Beam rapidity at 200 GeV: y_b = 5.36
* Off-equilibrium corrections change net proton yields by about 3% for 0-5% Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 19.6 GeV
* Boost-invariance remains a good approximation within |eta_s| ≲ 0.6 at 19.6 GeV

## Context

This work extends the (3+1)-dimensional multistage hydrodynamic modeling of Denicol (2018) and Du (2022) by calibrating to a wider range of Beam Energy Scan energies and performing a systematic closure test of statistical thermal models. The rapidity scan method follows the approach of Karpenko (2018), Brewer (2018), and Begun (2018), but uses a realistic dynamical framework rather than assuming Bjorken flow. The continuous source thermal model builds on the formalism of Becattini (2007) and Cleymans (2007), while the authors propose a new thermal model that incorporates both thermal smearing and longitudinal flow effects inspired by the hybrid approach. This work enables quantitative assessment of which rapidity windows allow reliable extraction of thermodynamic parameters using thermal models, and provides a benchmark for future rapidity-dependent analyses at BES energies.
