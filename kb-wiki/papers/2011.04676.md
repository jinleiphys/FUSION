# Discerning nuclear pairing properties from magnetic dipole excitation

> Pairing correlation of Cooper pair is a fundamental property of multi-fermion interacting systems. For nucleons, two modes of the Cooper-pair coupling may exist, namely of $S_{12}=0$ with $L_{12}=0$ (spin-singlet s-wave) and $S_{12}=1$ with $L_{12}=1$ (spin-triplet p-wave). In nuclear physics, it has been an open question whether the spin-singlet or spin-triplet coupling is dominant, as well as how to measure their role. We investigate a relation between the magnetic-dipole (M1) excitation of nuclei and the pairing modes within the framework of relativistic nuclear energy-density functional (RNEDF). The pairing correlations are taken into account by the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) model in the ground state, and the relativistic quasi-particle random-phase approximation (RQRPA) is employed to describe M1 transitions. We have shown that M1 excitation properties display a sensitivity on the pairing model involved in the calculations. The systematic evaluation of M1 transitions together with the accurate experimental data enables us to discern the pairing properties in finite nuclei.

## Key claim

The authors show that magnetic dipole (M1) excitation properties in open-shell nuclei display a sensitivity to whether spin-singlet or spin-triplet pairing channels dominate, even though different pairing models can reproduce ground-state properties with equivalent quality. The strong attractive pairing in the spin-triplet channel has not been validated by comparison with experimental M1 data.

## Method

The authors employ the relativistic nuclear energy-density functional (RNEDF) framework, using the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) model to determine ground states and the relativistic quasi-particle random-phase approximation (RQRPA) to describe M1 transitions. Two finite-range Gaussian pairing potentials are compared: the D1S interaction, which supports spin-singlet pairing, and a newly introduced S1P (spin-triplet promoting) interaction, which enhances spin-triplet pairing. Calculations are performed for even-even Ca isotopes and several other open-shell nuclei under spherical symmetry.

## Key numbers

- The D1S and S1P pairing models produce two-neutron separation energies and binding energies for Ca isotopes that coincide well and equivalently reproduce empirical data; deviations in charge radii for light Ca isotopes are as expected from mean-field calculations.
- The non-energy-weighted summation m0 values obtained with D1S and S1P show 20-40% difference for open-shell Ca nuclei; the energy-weighted summation m1 values show 10-30% difference.
- For 50Ti, the D1S calculation yields neutron-M1 and proton-M1 peaks at 8.6 and 11 MeV respectively, qualitatively agreeing with experimental two-peak distribution from Crawley (1983) and Sober (1985); the S1P calculation shifts these to 8.7 and 7.5 MeV.
- The error from experimental binding energy per nucleon data is less than 0.1 MeV throughout Ca, Sn, and Pb isotopes for both pairing models.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Oishi 2019, 2020) showing that M1 excitation is connected to the coupled spin components of nuclear Cooper pairs. It contrasts with the generalized energy-weighted summation rule by Hinohara (2019), which predicted independence from pairing interactions, but the authors note their finite-range pairing potentials and spin-dependent M1 operator may not satisfy the conditions for that rule. The results enable systematic evaluation of M1 transitions combined with accurate experimental data to discern whether spin-singlet or spin-triplet pairing dominates in finite nuclei, while noting that further developments are needed to account for nuclear deformation and couplings to higher-order configurations.
