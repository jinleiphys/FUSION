# From the colour glass condensate to filamentation: Systematics of classical Yang-Mills theory

> The non-equilibrium early time evolution of an ultra-relativistic heavy ion collision is often described by classical lattice Yang-Mills theory, starting from the colour glass condensate (CGC) effective theory with an anisotropic energy momentum tensor as initial condition. In this work we investigate the systematics associated with such studies and their dependence on various model parameters (IR, UV cutoffs and the amplitude of quantum fluctuations) which are not yet fixed by experiment. We perform calculations for SU(2) and SU(3), both in a static box and in an expanding geometry. Generally, the dependence on model parameters is found to be much larger than that on technical parameters like the number of colours, boundary conditions or the lattice spacing. In a static box, all setups lead to isotropisation through chromo-Weibel instabilities, which is illustrated by the accompanying filamentation of the energy density. However, the associated time scale depends strongly on the model parameters and in all cases is longer than the phenomenologically expected one. In the expanding system, no isotropisation is observed for any parameter choice. We show how investigations at fixed initial energy density can be used to better constrain some of the model parameters.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the non-equilibrium early-time evolution of classical Yang-Mills theory starting from colour glass condensate initial conditions depends much more strongly on model parameters (IR cutoff, UV cutoff, and amplitude of quantum fluctuations) than on technical parameters like the number of colours, boundary conditions, or lattice spacing; however, the isotropisation time scale in a static box is always longer than the phenomenologically expected one, and in an expanding system no isotropisation is observed for any parameter choice.

## Method

The authors performed classical lattice Yang-Mills simulations for SU(2) and SU(3) gauge groups, using the McLerran-Venugopalan model with Gaussian-distributed colour charges to generate initial conditions. They evolved the field equations in both a static box and an expanding geometry (comoving coordinates), varying the number of longitudinal sheets N_l, the IR cutoff m, the UV cutoff Lambda, and the amplitude of quantum fluctuations Delta, while monitoring the energy density components and the ratio of longitudinal to transverse pressure as measures of isotropisation.

## Key numbers

- The transverse lattice spacing is set by a_perp = 120/(Q N_perp) ≈ 12/N_perp fm, with Q = 2 GeV fixed throughout the paper
- The physical box size corresponds to L = 120/Q ≈ 12 fm, matching the diameter of a gold nucleus with A=197
- For the IR cutoff, m = 0.1 Q ≈ 200 MeV ≈ 1/fm is used as a physically motivated choice corresponding to the proton radius scale
- The saturation scale Q_s is approximately 0.85 Q for m/Q = 0.1 and approximately 1.03 Q for m/Q = 0 at N_l = 30
- Boundary effects from embedding a 400^2 nucleus in larger lattices are at the 5% level
- Discretisation effects become negligible for Q*tau > 0.3 on a 400^2 lattice
- The energy density dependence on N_l saturates for N_l > 30, with faster saturation when m/Q = 0.1 compared to m/Q = 0

## Context

This work extends systematic studies of classical Yang-Mills simulations for heavy-ion collisions, following the framework established by Lappi (2008) for lattice implementation of CGC initial conditions and Fukushima (2008) for the multi-sheet construction. The paper contrasts with earlier work by Romatschke (2005, 2006), Berges (2012, 2013), and Fukushima (2011, 2013) by providing a comprehensive parameter scan showing that model parameter dependence dominates over technical parameter dependence, and by demonstrating that isotropisation fails in expanding geometry for all parameter choices. The results enable future work to constrain model parameters by matching to fixed initial energy density, and highlight the need for a better understanding of the IR cutoff parameter m and its possible anisotropy in the initial geometry.
