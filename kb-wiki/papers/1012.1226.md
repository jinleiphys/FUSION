# Confining but chirally symmetric dense and cold matter

> The possibility for existence of cold, dense chirally symmetric matter with confinement is reviewed. The answer to this question crucially depends on the mechanism of mass generation in QCD and interconnection of confinement and chiral symmetry breaking. This question can be clarified from spectroscopy of hadrons and their axial properties. Almost systematical parity doubling of highly excited hadrons suggests that their mass is not related to chiral symmetry breaking in the vacuum and is approximately chirally symmetric. Then there is a possibility for existence of confining but chirally symmetric matter. We clarify a possible mechanism underlying such a phase at low temperatures and large density. Namely, at large density the Pauli blocking prevents the gap equation to generate a solution with broken chiral symmetry. However, the chirally symmetric part of the quark Green function as well as all color non-singlet quantities are still infrared divergent, meaning that the system is with confinement. A possible phase transition to such a matter is most probably of the first order. This is because there are no chiral partners to the lowest lying hadrons.

# Confining but chirally symmetric dense and cold matter

## Key claim
Cold, dense matter can exist in a phase that is simultaneously confining (color-non-singlet quantities remain infrared divergent) and chirally symmetric (the quark-antiquark condensate vanishes), provided the density is high enough that Pauli blocking prevents the gap equation from generating a broken-chiral-symmetry solution. The phase transition from broken to restored chiral symmetry in this confining context is most probably first order in real QCD, because the lowest-lying hadrons lack chiral partners.

## Method
The author uses a manifestly confining, chirally symmetric model in 3+1 dimensions where the only gluonic interaction is a linear instantaneous Coulomb-type potential, a generalization of the 't Hooft model. Assuming unbroken translational and rotational invariance (a liquid phase), the gap equation for a probe quark above the Fermi momentum is solved with Pauli blocking of levels below the Fermi surface. The quark Green function contains both a chirally breaking part A_p and a chirally symmetric part B_p; above the chiral restoration transition A_p vanishes while B_p remains infrared divergent, signaling confinement.

## Key numbers
- The chiral restoration phase transition occurs at a density where the quark Fermi momentum P_f is sufficiently large that Pauli blocking removes the phase space needed for a nontrivial broken-chiral-symmetry solution of the gap equation.
- Lattice results for N_c=2 suggest that a deconfining transition could occur at densities of order 100 times nuclear matter density (Hands 2010).
- The model exhibits effective chiral restoration in hadrons with large angular momentum J, where centrifugal repulsion cuts off low-momentum components.

## Context
This work extends the concept of quarkyonic matter introduced by McLerran (2007) for large N_c QCD, where confinement persists to arbitrary density, and addresses whether a chirally symmetric subphase can exist within it. The argument builds on the observation of approximate parity doubling in highly excited hadrons (Glozman 2007, 2009) and the pattern of strong decays of excited hadrons (Glozman 2004), which suggest that chiral symmetry breaking is irrelevant for high-lying states. The model used is a 3+1 dimensional generalization of the 't Hooft model and shares features with the Gribov-Zwanziger scenario in Coulomb gauge. The analysis contrasts with non-confining models like the NJL model, where chiral restoration occurs without confinement, and with exactly solvable 1+1 dimensional theories (the 't Hooft model and Gross-Neveu model) where translational invariance is broken and a chiral spiral with inhomogeneous condensate appears. The work enables future lattice and experimental searches for a confining but chirally symmetric phase at densities relevant to neutron star interiors and heavy-ion collisions.
