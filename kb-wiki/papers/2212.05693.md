# A scheme for excitation of thorium-229 nuclei based on the electronic bridge excitaion

> Thorium-229 possesses the lowest nuclear first excited state with an energy of about 8 eV. The extremely narrow linewidth of the nuclear first excited state with the uncertainty of 53 THz prevents the direct laser excitation and the realization of the nuclear clock. We present a proposal using the Coulomb crystal of a linear chain formed by the $^{229}$Th$^{3+}$ ions, the nuclei of $^{229}$Th$^{3+}$ ions in the ion trap are excited by the electronic bridge (EB) process. The 7$P_{1/2}$ state of the thorium-229 nuclear ground state is chosen for the EB excitation. Using the two-level optical Bloch equation under experimental conditions, we calculate that 2 out of 36 prepared thorium ions in the Coulomb crystal can be excited to the nuclear first excited state, and it takes about 2 hours to scan over the uncertainty of 0.22 eV. Taking the advantage of transition enhancement of the EB and the long stability of the Coulomb crystal, the energy uncertainty of the first excited state can be limited to the order of 1 GHz.

# A scheme for excitation of thorium-229 nuclei based on the electronic bridge excitation

## Key claim
The authors propose that using an electronic bridge (EB) excitation process in a Coulomb crystal of trapped 229Th3+ ions, approximately 2 out of 36 ions can be excited to the nuclear isomeric state under resonant conditions, but this result depends on the theoretical prediction that the EB transition linewidth is 40 times larger than the direct nuclear excitation linewidth, and the calculation assumes specific experimental parameters including a 350 nm laser with 250 mW power focused to a 10 μm spot size.

## Method
The authors simulate a linear-chain Coulomb crystal of 36 laser-cooled 229Th3+ ions in a linear Paul trap, prepare the ions in the 7P1/2 electronic state via a three-level pumping scheme using 269 nm and 196 nm lasers, then drive the electronic bridge excitation with a 350 nm laser to transfer population from the nuclear ground state (5/2+[633]) to the isomeric state (3/2+[631]). The time-dependent nuclear excitation probability is calculated using Torrey's solution of the two-level optical Bloch equations, accounting for Doppler broadening at 50 μK temperature and the modified Rabi frequency under the experimental laser linewidth conditions.

## Key numbers
- Isomeric state energy: 8.12 ± 0.11 eV (average from multiple measurements), corresponding to an uncertainty of 53 THz or 0.22 eV
- 2 out of 36 trapped 229Th3+ ions can be excited to the isomeric state under resonant EB excitation after 300 μs irradiation by a 350 nm laser
- Total scan time to cover the 0.22 eV uncertainty range: approximately 5670 seconds (about 2 hours)
- Final energy uncertainty of the isomeric state limited to approximately 1 GHz (4 × 10⁻⁶ eV), dominated by the natural linewidth of the 7P1/2 electronic state
- Rabi frequency for EB excitation: 334 kHz (unmodified), 140 kHz (modified on resonance), 98 kHz (modified at detuning ΓD/2)
- Doppler broadening linewidth for EB excitation at 350 nm: 2.8 MHz at 50 μK ion temperature
- Direct nuclear excitation yields only 0.12 excited nuclei out of 100 ions after 30000 s irradiation, making detection infeasible
- EB transition enhancement factor relative to direct nuclear excitation: 40 times

## Context
This work extends previous theoretical investigations of electronic bridge excitation for 229Th in various charge states, including 229Th+ (Porsev et al.), 229Th2+, 229Th3+ (Bilous et al., Minkov et al.), and 229Th35+ (Karpeshin et al.). The proposal builds on experimental demonstrations of laser cooling and Coulomb crystallization of 229Th3+ ions (Campbell et al., 2021) and aims to improve upon the current isomeric energy uncertainty of 0.22 eV (Kraemer et al., 2022) by using the EB process to achieve 1 GHz precision. Successful implementation would enable the development of a nuclear optical clock and tests of fundamental constant variation, while a null result would constrain the strength of electron-nucleus coupling or indicate inaccuracies in the explored energy range.
