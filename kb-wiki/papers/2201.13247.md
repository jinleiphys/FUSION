# The Sturdiness of the Shell Model: Informal Review

> With shell model codes being able to encompassing larger and larger spaces we find that the percentage occupancy of the leading spaces becomes smaller and smaller. How can the shell model survive in such circumstances? We will not solve this puzzle here but rather will show examples where, with some explanations, the shell model holds fast. We will use nuclear moments as an example.

# The Sturdiness of the Shell Model: Informal Review

## Key claim
The shell model remains remarkably predictive for nuclear moments even when large-scale calculations cause the percentage occupancy of the leading single-particle orbits to become very small. The authors do not resolve this puzzle but demonstrate, using magnetic and quadrupole moments as examples, that simple single-j shell predictions often survive despite the complexity of modern large-space calculations.

## Method
The authors review experimental measurements of quadrupole and magnetic moments across several isotopic chains (Ca isotopes A=41–47, N=28 isotones, odd-N Cd isotopes A=111–129) and compare them to single-j shell model predictions, large-space shell model calculations (including complete pf space, pf+g9/2, and 2p-2h core-breaking), and covariant density functional theory with pairing. They also analyze two-body matrix elements extracted from experimental spectra in the f7/2 and g9/2 shells, computing centroids and standard deviations for even-J (T=1) and odd-J (T=0) states to quantify the isospin dependence of the residual interaction.

## Key numbers
- Free neutron magnetic moment: μn = -1.913 nuclear magnetons; free proton magnetic moment: μp = +2.793 nuclear magnetons.
- For A=51, measured quadrupole moment Q = +0.04 b; for A=49, Q = -0.04 b (nearly equal and opposite, as predicted by single-j shell model for a p3/2 hole vs. particle).
- For Ca isotopes A=41–47, measured magnetic moments deviate from the single-j value of -1.913 μn, lying closer to -1.4 μn (quenching).
- For the N=28 isotones (49Sc, 51V, 53Mn, 55Co), the Schmidt value for f7/2 protons is μ = 5.793 μn; measured moments lie on a straight line with negative slope.
- For 42Sc, empirical two-body matrix elements (MBZE 2006) for J=0 through J=7 range from 0.0000 MeV (J=0) to 3.2420 MeV (J=6), with J=7 at 0.6163 MeV.
- For the f7/2 shell, the deviation between even-J and odd-J centroids ranges from 25.39% (Q.Q interaction) to 92.10% (MBZE 2006 42Sc).
- For the g9/2 shell, the deviation between even-J and odd-J centroids ranges from 20.14% (Q.Q interaction) to 53.67% (Qi et al.).
- Excitation energies of even-J states in 42Ca, 42Sc, and 42Ti are nearly identical (e.g., J=2 at 1.5247, 1.5803, and 1.5546 MeV respectively), demonstrating charge independence of the nuclear force.

## Context
This informal review extends the long tradition of single-j shell model calculations for nuclear moments, referencing the Schmidt moments and the work of Arima and Horie (1960s) on the microscopic explanation of magnetic moment quenching. The authors contrast the success of simple single-j predictions with the need for large-space calculations by Ruiz et al. (for Ca isotopes) and the covariant density functional theory plus pairing approach of Zhao, Zhang, and Meng (for Cd isotopes). The paper also builds on the binding energy formalism of the Israeli group (de-Shalit, Racah, Talmi, Thieberger, Unna) to derive a two-body effective radius operator for isotope shifts, and discusses the work of Ichimura and Yazaki and Mavromatis, Zamick, and Brown on second-order corrections to magnetic moments. The results enable further study of how the shell model remains robust despite configuration mixing, and highlight open questions about intruder states (e.g., the near-zero g factors of 2+ states in 42Ca and 44Ca, explained by Gerace and Green via deformed states).
