# The Future of Nuclear Energy: Facts and Fiction: An update using 2009/2010 Data

> An update of our 2009 study, "The Future of Nuclear Energy, Facts and Fiction" using the 2009 and the available 2010 data, including a critical look at the just published 2009 edition of the Red Book, is presented. Since January 2009, eight reactors with a capacity of 4.9 GWe have been connected to the electric grid and four older reactors, with a combined capacity of 2.64 GWe have been terminated. Furthermore, 27 reactor constructions, dominated by China (18) and Russia (4), have been initiated. The nuclear fission produced electric energy in 2009 followed the slow decline, observed since 2007, with a total production of 2560 TWhe, 41 TWhe (1.6%) less than in 2008 and roughly 100 TWhe less than in the record year 2006. The preliminary data from the first 10 months of 2010 in the OECD countries indicate that nuclear power production in North-America remained at the 2009 levels, while one observes a recovery in Europe with an increase of 2.5% and a strong rise of 5% in the OECD Asia-Pacific area compared to the same period in 2009. Worldwide uranium mining has increased during 2009 by about 7000 tons to almost 51000 tons. Still roughly 18000 tons of the 2010 world uranium requirements need to be provided from the civilian and military reserves. Perhaps the most remarkable new data from the just published 2009 edition of the Red Book, are that (1) the best understood RAR (reasonable assured) and IR (inferred) resources, with a price tag of less than 40 US dollars/Kg, have been inconsistently absorbed in the two to three times higher price categories and (2) uranium mining in Kazakhstan is presented with a short lifetime. The presented mining capacity numbers indicate an uranium extraction peak of 28000 tons during the years 2015-2020, from which it will decline quickly to 14000 tons by 2025 and to only 5000-6000 tons by 2035.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the nuclear energy sector has not entered a "renaissance" and that the slow decline in nuclear electricity generation observed since 2006 continued through 2009, with the caveat that preliminary 2010 data from OECD countries show a stabilization and partial recovery, but that this does not yet constitute a sustained growth trend.

## Method

The author updates a previous 2009 study by compiling and analyzing the latest available data from the IAEA Power Reactor Information System (PRIS), the World Nuclear Association (WNA), the 2009 edition of the NEA/IAEA Red Book, and national mining reports. The analysis covers reactor connections and terminations, electricity generation totals, uranium mining output, and resource estimates, comparing these numbers against past predictions from the same sources.

## Key numbers

- Eight reactors with a combined capacity of 4.9 GWe were connected to the grid since January 2009; four older reactors with a combined capacity of 2.64 GWe were terminated.
- Nuclear electricity generation in 2009 was 2560 TWhe, a decrease of 41 TWhe (1.6%) from 2008 and roughly 100 TWhe less than the record year 2006.
- Preliminary data from the first 10 months of 2010 show an overall increase of 1.9% (+34 TWhe) for OECD countries compared to the same period in 2009, with Europe up 2.5% and OECD Asia-Pacific up 5%.
- Worldwide uranium mining in 2009 reached 50772 tons, an increase of about 7000 tons (15%) from 2008, with Kazakhstan contributing 14020 tons (an increase of about 6000 tons or 63%).
- Roughly 18000 tons of the 2010 world uranium requirements still needed to be provided from civilian and military reserves.
- The Red Book mining capacity numbers indicate a uranium extraction peak of 28000 tons during 2015-2020, declining to 14000 tons by 2025 and to 5000-6000 tons by 2035.

## Context

This paper updates the author's 2009 study "The Future of Nuclear Energy: Facts and Fiction" and extends the critical analysis of official nuclear industry projections, particularly the NEA/IAEA Red Book and WNA forecasts. The work contradicts optimistic "nuclear renaissance" narratives by showing that actual reactor construction and electricity generation have consistently fallen short of predictions made in earlier Red Book editions (e.g., the 1997 Red Book predicted 427-461 GWe capacity by 2010, while actual capacity was about 374.6 GWe). The analysis enables future comparisons between announced plans and realized outcomes, particularly for uranium mining in Kazakhstan and for the construction schedules of new reactors in China, Russia, and India.
