# Improved estimate of electron capture rates on nuclei during stellar core collapse

> Electron captures on nuclei play an important role in the dynamics of the collapsing core of a massive star that leads to a supernova explosion. Recent calculations of these capture rates were based on microscopic models which account for relevant degrees of freedom. Due to computational restrictions such calculations were limited to a modest number of nuclei, mainly in the mass range A=45-110. Recent supernova simulations show that this pool of nuclei, however, omits the very neutron-rich and heavy nuclei which dominate the nuclear composition during the last phase of the collapse before neutrino trapping. Assuming that the composition is given by Nuclear Statistical Equilibrium we present here electron capture rates for collapse conditions derived from individual rates for roughly 2700 individual nuclei. For those nuclei which dominate in the early stage of the collapse, the individual rates are derived within the framework of microscopic models, while for the nuclei which dominate at high densities we have derived the rates based on the Random Phase Approximation with a global parametrization of the single particle occupation numbers. In addition, we have improved previous rate evaluations by properly including screening corrections to the reaction rates into account.

# Improved estimate of electron capture rates on nuclei during stellar core collapse

## Key claim

The authors present electron capture rates for roughly 2700 individual nuclei under core-collapse conditions, extending previous calculations that were limited to nuclei in the mass range A=45-110. The new rates cover the very neutron-rich and heavy nuclei that dominate the nuclear composition during the last phase of collapse before neutrino trapping, using a hybrid approach where microscopic models are applied for early-stage nuclei and a Random Phase Approximation with a global parametrization of single particle occupation numbers is used for high-density nuclei.

## Method

Individual electron capture rates were calculated for approximately 2700 nuclei using three approaches: large-scale shell model diagonalization for nuclei in the mass range A=45-65 (the LMP pool), a hybrid SMMC+RPA model for about 250 nuclei in the mass range A=66-120 and proton number Z=28-45 (the SMMC pool), and a Fermi-Dirac parametrization of occupation numbers combined with RPA for over 2200 additional nuclei with Z=28-70 and N=40-160 (the FD+RPA pool). The rates were then folded with a Nuclear Statistical Equilibrium (NSE) composition to produce average electron capture rates for stellar collapse conditions, including screening corrections that were not properly accounted for in previous evaluations.

## Key numbers

* The SMMC+RPA pool covers about 250 nuclei in the mass range A=66-120 and proton number range Z=28-45
* The FD+RPA pool adds more than 2200 additional nuclei with proton numbers Z=28-70 and neutron numbers N=40-160
* For a 15 solar mass progenitor at density 8.56e9 g/cm^3, the unscreened average electron capture rate is 1.94e0 sec^-1 and the screened rate is 1.38e0 sec^-1
* For a 15 solar mass progenitor at density 1.07e13 g/cm^3, the unscreened average electron capture rate is 5.18e6 sec^-1 and the screened rate is 2.67e6 sec^-1
* For a 25 solar mass progenitor at density 2.22e9 g/cm^3, the unscreened average electron capture rate is 8.99e-2 sec^-1 and the screened rate is 6.65e-2 sec^-1
* For a 25 solar mass progenitor at density 1.11e13 g/cm^3, the unscreened average electron capture rate is 5.59e6 sec^-1 and the screened rate is 2.98e6 sec^-1
* The average mass number of heavy nuclei in the 15 solar mass progenitor increases from 60.2 at early times to 111.3 at the highest density
* The average mass number of heavy nuclei in the 25 solar mass progenitor increases from 56.4 at early times to 84.8 at the highest density

## Context

This work extends the earlier LMSH tabulation (Langanke et al., 2003) which combined the LMP shell model rates (Caurier et al., 1999; Langanke and Martinez-Pinedo, 2000, 2001) with the LMS hybrid model rates (Langanke et al., 2003) for about 80 heavier nuclei. The previous tabulation suffered from a decreasing fraction of nuclear composition covered as collapse continued, with the average rate becoming increasingly uncertain when dominated by a few heavier nuclei. The new tabulation enables more accurate supernova simulations by providing individual rates for the neutron-rich heavy nuclei that dominate at high densities, and by properly including screening corrections. The authors note that a parameterization suggested in Langanke et al. (2003) for missing nuclei was found to be too simple for all collapse conditions (Juodagalvis et al., 2008).
