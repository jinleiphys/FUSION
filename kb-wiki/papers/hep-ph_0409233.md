# Introduction to Nonequilibrium Quantum Field Theory

> There has been substantial progress in recent years in the quantitative understanding of the nonequilibrium time evolution of quantum fields. Important topical applications, in particular in high energy particle physics and cosmology, involve dynamics of quantum fields far away from the ground state or thermal equilibrium. In these cases, standard approaches based on small deviations from equilibrium, or on a sufficient homogeneity in time underlying kinetic descriptions, are not applicable. A particular challenge is to connect the far-from-equilibrium dynamics at early times with the approach to thermal equilibrium at late times. Understanding the ``link'' between the early- and the late-time behavior of quantum fields is crucial for a wide range of phenomena. For the first time questions such as the explosive particle production at the end of the inflationary universe, including the subsequent process of thermalization, can be addressed in quantum field theory from first principles. The progress in this field is based on efficient functional integral techniques, so-called n-particle irreducible effective actions, for which powerful nonperturbative approximation schemes are available. Here we give an introduction to these techniques and show how they can be applied in practice. Though we focus on particle physics and cosmology applications, we emphasize that these techniques can be equally applied to other nonequilibrium phenomena in complex many body systems.

## Key claim

The paper argues that far-from-equilibrium quantum field dynamics, including thermalization, can be described from first principles using n-particle irreducible (nPI) effective actions, which provide nonperturbative approximation schemes that are both non-secular in time and capable of capturing universal late-time behavior, in contrast to standard perturbative or mean-field approaches that fail for such regimes.

## Method

The author constructs generating functionals for connected Green's functions in the presence of linear and quadratic source terms, then obtains the two-particle irreducible (2PI) effective action via Legendre transforms. The equations of motion for the macroscopic field, propagator, and higher n-point functions are derived from stationarity conditions of the nPI effective action, which is truncated according to powers of a small expansion parameter (e.g., coupling constant, loop order, or 1/N) without further expanding the dynamical variables themselves.

## Key numbers

* For the illustrative classical anharmonic oscillator with damping parameter ε = 0.1, the perturbative expansion is only valid for ε t ≪ 1, while the "nPI type" expansion yields a uniform approximation with errors of order ε³ at all times.
* The Boltzmann-type equation for momentum modes y_p shows universal late-time approach to the Bose-Einstein distribution 1/(e^{β(|p| - μ)} - 1), with temperature β⁻¹ and chemical potential μ determined solely by conserved quantities.
* In the loop expansion hierarchy, the 2PI effective action at two-loop order captures the complete self-consistent description, while three-loop order requires at least the 3PI effective action in general.

## Context

This work extends the functional integral techniques of 1PI effective actions to nPI formulations, addressing the failure of standard perturbative and mean-field approximations (such as leading-order large-N expansions) for far-from-equilibrium dynamics. The approach enables first-principles calculations of explosive particle production at the end of inflation and subsequent thermalization, phenomena previously inaccessible to quantum field theory methods. The techniques are equally applicable to heavy-ion collision dynamics and Bose-Einstein condensate evolution, and provide a foundation for deriving effective kinetic descriptions with controlled ranges of validity.
