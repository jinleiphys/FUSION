# Role of Long-Range Correlations on the Quenching of Spectroscopic Factors

> We consider the proton and neutron quasiparticle orbits around the closed-shell 56Ni and 48Ca isotopes. It is found that large model spaces (beyond the capability of shell-model applications) are necessary for predicting the quenchings of spectroscopic factors. The particle-vibration coupling is identified as the principal mechanism. Additional correlations--due to configuration with several particle-hole excitations--are estimated using shell-model calculations and generate an extra reduction which is < ~4% for most quasiparticle states. The theoretical calculations nicely agree with (e,e'p) and heavy ion knock-out experiments. These results open a new path for a microscopic understanding of the shell-model.

## Key claim

Long-range correlations, primarily particle-vibration coupling, are the dominant mechanism for the quenching of absolute spectroscopic factors in medium-mass nuclei, while short-range correlations contribute only a small fraction of the total reduction. The paper finds that large model spaces beyond the capability of standard shell-model applications are necessary to predict this quenching, and that additional correlations from multi-particle-hole configurations generate an extra reduction of less than about 4% for most quasiparticle states.

## Method

The author performed self-consistent Green's function (SCGF) calculations using the Faddeev random phase approximation (FRPA) in a large model space of up to 10 major harmonic oscillator shells, employing the N3LO interaction from chiral perturbation theory with modified tensor monopoles. The self-energy was split into a mean-field part (including short-range correlations via G-matrix resummation) and a part describing long-range correlations through particle-vibration coupling at all orders. Shell-model calculations using the ANTOINE code with up to 6p6h and 8p8h excitations were performed in restricted model spaces to estimate the additional effects of configuration mixing not captured by FRPA.

## Key numbers

- For the neutron 1p3/2 ground state of 57Ni, the full FRPA spectroscopic factor is 0.65, compared to the experimental value of 0.58(11) from knockout reactions
- Short-range correlations alone give spectroscopic factors of 0.95-0.96 for valence orbits around 56Ni, indicating only about 5% quenching from SRC
- The difference between shell-model and FRPA results in the restricted 1p0f space for 56Ni is Delta Z_alpha = -0.03 for the neutron 1p3/2 state
- For the proton 0d3/2 hole state in 47K, the full FRPA plus shell-model correction gives 0.59, agreeing with the experimental (e,e'p) value of 0.56(4)
- The summed 1s1/2 strength in 47K is predicted to be 0.50, compared to the experimental (e,e'p) value of 0.61(5)
- For 48Ca, the final corrected strengths are smaller than pure shell-model ones by a factor of 0.75 for the 0d3/2 peak and 0.70 for the summed 1s1/2 strength

## Context

This work extends earlier self-consistent Green's function calculations by Dickhoff and collaborators (2004) that focused on only one type of correlation, and provides the first consistent calculation of all long-range correlation effects in medium-mass nuclei using a realistic interaction. The results contradict the assumption that the uniform quenching of spectroscopic factors to 60-70% of the independent particle model value observed in stable nuclei originates primarily from short-range correlations, as had been investigated by Polls et al. (1995) and Necula et al. (1998) and confirmed by Jefferson Laboratory experiments (Roh.04, Sub.08). The work enables a new path for deriving single-particle orbits, effective charges, and interactions for the shell model from realistic nuclear interactions, and provides a microscopic understanding of why shell-model calculations can describe relative spectroscopic factors while absolute quenching requires coupling to collective modes at higher energies.
