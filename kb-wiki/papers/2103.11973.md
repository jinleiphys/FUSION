# Direct and sequential four-body recombination rates at low temperatures

> We investigate four-body nuclear reactions in stellar environments contributing to creation of light nuclei, exemplified by $^9$Be and $^{12}$C. The originally assumed process is radiative capture, where nuclear clusters combine into the excited final nucleus and photon emission populates the stable nuclear ground states. Instead, we consider nuclear four-body recombination reactions where a spectator nuclear particle replaces the photon. We first develop the elaborate formalism for both, direct and sequential capture processes, where the decaying three-body resonance is formed without and with population of an intermediate two-body resonance, respectively. To facilitate both calculations and practical applications we parameterize the involved cross sections as done successfully in previous computations of reaction rates. We consider the lowest-lying nuclear states with their dominant contributions at low stellar temperatures. We calculate and compare reaction and production rates for different processes. The direct reaction mechanism dominates by many orders of magnitude at low temperature, where the sequential stepping stones are energetically too expensive to use. At somewhat higher temperatures these two different nuclear four-body mechanisms become comparable. Comparison to radiative three-body capture reveals already formally, but also numerically, that four-body nuclear recombination must dominate for sufficiently high nuclear densities. Numerical values are given for all these rates as function of temperature and density. The relative importance is exhibited.

## Key claim

The paper argues that four-body nuclear recombination reactions (where a spectator particle replaces the photon) can dominate over radiative three-body capture for sufficiently high nuclear densities, but the relative importance of direct versus sequential capture mechanisms depends critically on temperature, with direct capture dominating by many orders of magnitude at low temperatures where sequential stepping stones are energetically too expensive.

## Method

The authors developed formal expressions for four-body recombination reaction rates for both direct capture (three-body resonance formed without intermediate two-body states) and sequential capture (population of an intermediate two-body resonance before forming the three-body resonance). They parameterized the involved cross sections using Breit-Wigner forms for resonant processes and standard S-factor parametrizations for inelastic two-body cross sections, then applied these to the formation of $^9$Be and $^{12}$C induced by protons, neutrons, or alpha particles.

## Key numbers

- For temperatures about 0.01 GK, direct population of the resonance could lead to radiative reaction rates for formation of $^{12}$C or $^9$Be of up to seven orders of magnitude bigger than by the sequential mechanism (from discussion of prior work in Garrido (2011))
- The four-body recombination dominates over radiative capture for densities higher than $10^6$ g/cm$^3$, especially for temperatures below 1 GK (from discussion of prior work in Diez (2010, 2014))
- For neutron densities above $10^7$ g/cm$^3$ and temperatures greater than a few GK, inelastic collisions were estimated to substantially increase the rate of formation of $^{12}$C (from discussion of prior work in Truran (1969))
- For sufficiently low temperatures ($T \lesssim 3\times 10^7$ K), the capture proceeds through the low energy wing of the alpha-alpha resonance (from discussion of prior work in Nomoto (1985) and Langanke (1986))

## Context

This work extends the formalism previously developed for three-body radiative capture in Garrido (2011) to the case of four-body recombination reactions, and builds upon earlier studies by Truran (1969), Morley (1970), and Davids (1971) that suggested particle-induced de-excitation could enhance reaction rates. The paper provides a more complete treatment than the simplified two-body approach used in those earlier works and in the recent paper by Beard (2017), by explicitly accounting for the finite width of resonances and the distinction between direct and sequential capture mechanisms.
