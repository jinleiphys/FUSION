# A Possible Solution to the Tritium Endpoint Problem

> Scalar or right-chiral interaction currents may be expected to produce a neutrino coupled to the electron which is different from, and perhaps even orthogonal to, that coupled to the electron by the standard model weak interaction. We show that, using reasonable parameter values for such additional interactions, it is possible to generate a spectrum which, if analyzed in the manner commonly employed by experimental groups, produces a negative neutrino mass-squared.

## Key claim

Scalar or right-chiral interaction currents beyond the Standard Model can couple the electron to a different linear combination of neutrino mass eigenstates than the Standard Model weak interaction does; using reasonable parameter values for such additional interactions, it is possible to generate a beta spectrum which, when analyzed under the standard single-mass-neutrino assumption, yields a negative neutrino mass-squared.

## Method

The authors introduce an effective low-energy Hamiltonian containing scalar (S), right-chiral (R), and left-chiral (L) four-fermion currents, with the Standard Model L current dominant. They assume three neutrino mass eigenstates and derive a generalized differential spectrum formula that includes interference terms between the Standard Model and new interactions, parameterized by factors epsilon_i and phi_i for each mass eigenstate. They then fit this generalized spectrum with the standard single-neutrino formula to extract an apparent neutrino mass-squared, and examine both differential and integral fitting procedures.

## Key numbers

- The endpoint energy for tritium beta decay is approximately 18.65 keV.
- In the numerical example, the authors assume cos^2(theta_1) = 0.995, f_1 = 0.2/(1.01), and m_2 = 200 m_1, where m_1 is the mass of the dominant eigenstate.
- For this example, the extracted best-fit value yields <m^2>_fit approximately -2 m_1^2 when the cut-off is chosen where the extracted value is most stable.

## Context

This work addresses the long-standing "tritium endpoint problem" in which experiments (ITEP, Zurich, LANL, LLNL, Troitsk, Mainz) consistently found a negative best-fit neutrino mass-squared when analyzing tritium beta decay spectra under the assumption of a single massive neutrino. The paper extends earlier work by Enz (1957) on the influence of the Lorentz nature of the weak interaction on endpoint spectra, and the authors' own more general treatment (Stephenson and Goldman, 1998). It enables experimental groups to fit tritium data using a form that accounts for possible non-Standard Model interactions, potentially resolving the apparent negative mass-squared anomaly without invoking unphysical tachyonic neutrinos.
