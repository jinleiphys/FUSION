# Evolution of strangeness and hyperons in quarkyonic matter

> We study the evolution of matter composition from nuclear to quark densities in the confining regime, by extending an ideal model of Quarkyonic matter, IdylliQ model, to multi-flavor systems including strangeness. The model provides a dual description of quark and baryon occupation probabilities which are determined by minimizing the energy of the system. Saturation of low-momentum quark states drives the formation of quark matter and constrains baryon distributions, inducing statistical repulsion among baryon species. Applying the model to charge-neutral matter composed of neutrons, $Λ_0$, and $Σ_0$ hyperons, we find that, for typical size of baryons, $d$-quark saturation occurs before hyperons appear, delaying their onset and shifting the threshold density from $\sim 2$--$3n_{\rm sat}$ to $\sim 5$--$6n_{\rm sat}$ ($n_{\rm sat} \approx 0.16\,{\rm fm^{-3}}$: nuclear saturation density). After hyperons emerge, low-momentum hyperon states remain only sparsely occupied due to the quark saturation. These features mitigate the hyperon puzzle, in which the appearance of hyperons softens neutron star equations of state significantly by increasing energy density with little pressure increase. Our results highlight the key role of quark saturation in dense baryonic matter and provide new insights into the interplay between quark dynamics and hyperon physics in neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper claims that in charge-neutral quarkyonic matter composed of neutrons, Λ₀, and Σ₀ hyperons, saturation of d-quark states at low momenta delays the onset of hyperons from the conventional threshold density of ∼2–3n_sat to ∼5–6n_sat (n_sat ≈ 0.16 fm⁻³), and that after hyperons emerge, low-momentum hyperon states remain only sparsely occupied due to quark saturation, thereby mitigating the hyperon puzzle.

## Method

The authors extended the IdylliQ (Ideal Dual Quarkyonic) model, which provides a dual description of quark and baryon occupation probabilities determined by energy minimization, to multi-flavor systems including strangeness. They applied the model to charge-neutral matter composed of neutrons, Λ₀, and Σ₀ hyperons, using a specific momentum-space smearing function φ(q) that allows inversion of the duality relation to express baryon distributions in terms of quark distributions. The composition was determined by imposing chemical equilibrium conditions and minimizing the energy density functional for fixed baryon density, with the d-quark saturation constraint enforced through a local relation between baryon and quark occupation probabilities.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: n_sat ≈ 0.16 fm⁻³
- Hyperon threshold density shifts from ∼2–3n_sat to ∼5–6n_sat due to d-quark saturation
- Neutron mass: M_N ≈ 0.94 GeV; hyperon mass: M_Y ≈ 1.12 GeV (Λ₀ and Σ₀ assumed degenerate)
- The new hyperon threshold condition is μ_n = 2M_Y − M_N, which is close to the threshold of the charge-neutral S=−2 hyperon Ξ⁰ (uss)
- The d-quark saturation constraint allows hyperons to occupy low-momentum states only with probability ∼1/N_c³ (where N_c = 3)
- The onset density for Ξ⁰ hyperons is ∼5–6n_sat

## Context

This work extends the IdylliQ model previously developed for isospin-symmetric matter (Fujimoto 2023) to three-flavor systems including strangeness, building on the Quarkyonic matter concept originally proposed by McLerran (2007). The results address the hyperon puzzle, where conventional nuclear matter models predict hyperon onset at ∼2–3n_sat (Glendenning 1991, Schaffner 1995, Balberg 1997), which would soften neutron star equations of state and violate observational constraints (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013). The quark saturation mechanism provides a new perspective on in-medium N-Y repulsion that differs from approaches using lattice-QCD constraints on hyperon-nucleon interactions (Beane 2012, Inoue 2018) or three-body forces (Yamamoto 1988). The framework enables future work incorporating baryon-baryon interactions and structural changes of baryons to achieve a full solution to the hyperon puzzle.
