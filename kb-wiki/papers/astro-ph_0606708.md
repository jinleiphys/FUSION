# Constraining a possible time-variation of the gravitational constant through "gravitochemical heating" of neutron stars

> A hypothetical time-variation of the gravitational constant $G$ would cause neutron star matter to depart from beta equilibrium, due to the changing hydrostatic equilibrium. This forces non-equilibrium beta processes to occur, which release energy that is invested partly in neutrino emission and partly in heating the stellar interior. Eventually, the star arrives at a stationary state in which the temperature remains nearly constant, as the forcing through the change of $G$ is balanced by the ongoing reactions. Comparing the surface temperature of the nearest millisecond pulsar, PSR J0437-4715, inferred from ultraviolet observations, with our predicted stationary temperature, we estimate two upper limits for this variation: (1) $|\dot G/G| < 2 \times 10^{-10}$ yr$^{-1}$, if we allow direct Urca reactions operating in the neutron star core, and (2) $|\dot G/G| < 4 \times 10^{-12}$ yr$^{-1}$, considering only modified Urca reactions. Both results are competitive with those obtained by other methods, with (2) being among the most restrictive.

## Key claim

A time-variation of the gravitational constant \(G\) would cause neutron star matter to depart from beta equilibrium, leading to internal heating and neutrino emission that eventually reaches a stationary state; by comparing the predicted stationary surface temperature of the millisecond pulsar PSR J0437-4715 with its observed ultraviolet emission, the authors derive two upper limits on \(|\dot G/G|\) that depend on which nuclear reactions (direct Urca or modified Urca) are assumed to operate in the neutron star core.

## Method

The authors model the thermal and chemical evolution of a neutron star under a monotonic change in \(G\), using the thermal balance equation and the evolution equation for chemical imbalances \(\eta_{npl} = \mu_n - \mu_p - \mu_l\). They solve for the stationary state (\(\dot T = \dot \eta_{npl} = 0\)) where heating from the changing \(G\) is balanced by neutrino and photon cooling, and compute the stationary surface temperature as a function of \(|\dot G/G|\) for multiple equations of state and stellar masses. They then compare these theoretical temperatures to the blackbody fit of ultraviolet observations of PSR J0437-4715 by Kargaltsev, Pavlov, and Romani (2004) to set upper limits on \(|\dot G/G|\).

## Key numbers

* For the case allowing direct Urca reactions in the neutron star core: \(|\dot G/G| < 2 \times 10^{-10}\) yr\(^{-1}\).
* For the case considering only modified Urca reactions: \(|\dot G/G| < 4 \times 10^{-12}\) yr\(^{-1}\).
* The stationary photon luminosity for modified Urca only: \(L_{\gamma}^{st} \simeq 5 \times 10^{28} \left(|\dot G/G| / 10^{-12}\ \mathrm{yr}^{-1}\right)^{8/7}\) erg s\(^{-1}\).
* The stationary surface temperature for modified Urca only: \(T_{s}^{st} \simeq 7.7 \times 10^4 \left(|\dot G/G| / 10^{-12}\ \mathrm{yr}^{-1}\right)^{2/7}\) K.
* The timescale to reach the stationary state for modified Urca only: \(\tau_{st} \simeq 2 \times 10^8 \left(|\dot G/G| / 10^{-12}\ \mathrm{yr}^{-1}\right)^{-6/7}\) yr.
* The spin-down age of PSR J0437-4715 is \(\tau_\mathrm{sd} \simeq 5 \times 10^{9}\) yr, and the cooling age of its white dwarf companion is \(\tau_\mathrm{WD} \simeq 2.5-5.3 \times 10^{9}\) yr.
* The mass of PSR J0437-4715 is \(M_\mathrm{psr} = 1.58 \pm 0.18 M_{\odot}\).

## Context

This work extends the formalism of "rotochemical heating" developed by Fernandez and Reisenegger (2005), where spin-down compression drives a star out of beta equilibrium, to the case of a changing gravitational constant ("gravitochemical heating"). The method probes timescales of order \(10^8\) yr, placing it in the same class as constraints from binary pulsar masses (Thorsett, 1996) and globular clusters (Innocenti, 1995), and the modified-Urca limit of \(|\dot G/G| < 4 \times 10^{-12}\) yr\(^{-1}\) is among the most restrictive for such timescales. Future improvements could come from a larger sample of neutron stars with measured thermal emission, such as the nearby slow pulsar PSR J0108-1431, where rotochemical heating is negligible and any detected surface temperature would be a clear signature of a change in \(G\).
