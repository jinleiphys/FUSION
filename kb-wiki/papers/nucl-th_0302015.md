# Spinodal decomposition of expanding nuclear matter and multifragmentation

> Density fluctuations of expanding nuclear matter are studied within a mean-field model in which fluctuations are generated by an external stochastic field. Fluctuations develop about a mean one-body phase-space density corresponding to a hydrodinamic motion that describes a slow expansion of the system. A fluctuation-dissipation relation suitable for a uniformly expanding medium is obtained and used to constrain the strength of the stochastic field. The distribution of the liquid domains in the spinodal decomposition is derived. Comparison of the related distribution of the fragment size with experimental data on the nuclear multifragmentation is quite satisfactory.

## Key claim

The authors extend their previous mean-field model of spinodal decomposition (Matera, 2000) to include a time-dependent average density, showing that the distribution of liquid domains formed during the expansion of hot nuclear matter can be treated as a stationary process, and that the resulting fragment size distribution agrees with experimental multifragmentation data when the expansion is slow enough that the condition \(|\Gamma_k| < T\) holds.

## Method

The authors model the excited nuclear system as a sphere of homogeneous nuclear matter undergoing a slow hydrodynamic expansion along an isoentrope, with density and temperature changing according to a scaling law. They introduce a stochastic scalar field as a linear perturbation about the hydrodynamic solution, derive a fluctuation-dissipation relation for the uniformly expanding medium to constrain the stochastic field strength, and solve the linearized Vlasov equation to obtain the time-dependent growth rate \(\Gamma_k(t)\) of density fluctuations. The distribution of liquid domain sizes is then obtained from the Gaussian probability distribution of density fluctuations, with the length scale \(L(t)\) determined from the peak and width of the variance \(\sigma^2_k(t)\).

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature \(T(0) = 6\ \mathrm{MeV}\) and initial density \(\varrho(0) = 0.7\varrho_{eq}\) (where \(\varrho_{eq} = 0.16\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3}\)) are used as parameters compatible with nuclear multifragmentation.
- The initial expansion rate \(\alpha = |\dot\gamma(0)/\gamma(0)|\) is chosen in the range \((1/6 \times 10^{-2} \div 10^{-2})\ c/\mathrm{fm}\), corresponding to peripheral velocities of \((0.017 \div 0.1)c\) for a sphere of radius \(R \simeq 10\ \mathrm{fm}\).
- For \(\alpha = 0.01\ c/\mathrm{fm}\), the condition \(\Gamma_k^{av}(t) < T(t)\) is satisfied up to \(t \lesssim 50\ \mathrm{fm}/c\).
- The effective interaction parameters are \(a = -356.8\ \mathrm{MeV}\), \(b = 303.9\ \mathrm{MeV}\), \(\sigma = 1/6\), giving a saturation binding energy of \(15.75\ \mathrm{MeV}\) and incompressibility modulus of \(201\ \mathrm{MeV}\).

## Context

This work extends the stationary-density model of Matera (2000) by incorporating a time-dependent average density that follows a hydrodynamic expansion, building on earlier Boltzmann-Langevin approaches (Ayik, 1990; Randrup, 1990; Colonna, 1994) and the statistical fluctuation method of Colonna (1998). The results enable comparison with experimental multifragmentation data from reactions where the emitting source undergoes expansion and cooling after initial compression (Assenard, 1999; Frankland, 2001) or initial heating (Beaulieu, 1999; Beaulieu, 2000), and the authors find that slower expansions produce larger density fluctuations at the same point inside the spinodal region, making decomposition easier.
