# The Lorentz Integral Transform and its Inversion

> The Lorentz integral transform method is briefly reviewed. The issue of the inversion of the transform, and in particular its ill-posedness, is addressed. It is pointed out that the mathematical term ill-posed is misleading and merely due to a historical misconception. In this connection standard regularization procedures for the solution of the integral transform problem are presented. In particular a recent one is considered in detail and critical comments on it are provided. In addition a general remark concerning the concept of the Lorentz integral transform as a method with a controlled resolution is made.

## Key claim
The Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method is a well-founded approach for calculating reaction observables without explicit continuum wave functions, and the term "ill-posed" for its inversion is a historical misconception that does not imply any fundamental limitation on the accuracy or reliability of the results.

## Method
The LIT of a response function R(ω) is defined as ℒ(σ_R, σ_I) = ∫ dω R(ω) / [(ω - σ_R)² + σ_I²], and is computed by solving (H - E₀ - σ_R - i σ_I)|Ψ̃⟩ = Θ|0⟩ using bound-state methods. The response R(ω) is then obtained by inverting this integral transform, which is a so-called ill-posed problem. The inversion is performed using regularization procedures, such as expanding the solution as a sum of the first N functions of a complete set, where N serves as the regularization parameter, and fitting the coefficients to the computed LIT.

## Key numbers
- The regularization parameter d for the derivative example is chosen as about [2Δf(x)/f''(x)]^(1/2), yielding an error in the derivative of about [2f''(x)Δf(x)]^(1/2).
- The Fourier transform method uses a cutoff at |k| = k_max, with the approximate solution R(x, k_max) = (1/2π) ∫_{-k_max}^{k_max} [L̃(k)/K̃(k)] e^{-ikx} dk.
- If R(x) ~ x^λ at x→0, then R̃(k) ~ |k|^{-(1+λ)} at |k|→∞.

## Context
This paper reviews and defends the LIT method, which has been applied to inclusive inelastic reactions of nuclei with A=3, 4, 6, and 7 (e.g., 3bgam, 4bgam1, 6bgam, 7bgam) and to exclusive reactions for A=4 (e.g., 4bexgam). It extends prior work by clarifying that the inversion problem is not inherently ill-posed, contrary to claims in a recent paper (GS09), and it criticizes that paper's proposed "novel" inversion method as a known regularization procedure that has not been tested on realistic cases. The paper enables continued use of the LIT approach with confidence in its controlled resolution, where the parameter σ_I can be reduced to resolve finer structures at increased computational cost.
