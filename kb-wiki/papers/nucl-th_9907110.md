# An inversion procedure for coupled-channel scattering: determining the deuteron-nucleus tensor interaction

> We present a practical $S$-matrix to potential inversion procedure for coupled-channel scattering. The inversion technique developed is applied to non-diagonal $S^J_{ll'}$ for spin one projectiles, yielding a tensor interaction $T_{\rm R}$, and is also applicable to spin-1/2 plus spin-1/2 scattering. The method is a generalization of the iterative-perturbative, IP, method. It is tested and evaluated and we investigate the degree of uniqueness of the potential, particularly for cases where there is insufficient information to define the potential uniquely. We examine the potentials which result when the $S$-matrix is generated from a $T_{\rm P}$ interaction. We also develop the generalisation, using established procedures, of IP $S$-matrix-to-potential inversion to direct observable-to-potential inversion. This `direct inversion' procedure is demonstrated to be an efficient method for finding a multi-component potential including a $T_{\rm R}$ interaction fitting multi-energy $σ$, ${\rm i}T_{11}$, $T_{20}$, $T_{21}$ and $T_{22}$ data for the scattering of spin-1 nuclei from spin-zero target. It is applicable to other channel spin 1 cases.

## Key claim

The paper presents a practical generalization of the iterative-perturbative (IP) S-matrix-to-potential inversion method to coupled-channel scattering for spin-1 projectiles, specifically yielding a tensor interaction of the T_R type. The method is tested and evaluated, but the authors caution that the degree of uniqueness of the potential is limited, particularly when there is insufficient information to define the potential uniquely, and that the inversion is restricted to T_R-type tensor forces (not T_P-type).

## Method

The authors generalized the IP inversion method, previously developed for spin-1/2 projectiles, to handle non-diagonal S-matrix elements S^J_{ll'} for spin-1 projectiles scattering from spin-zero targets. The method iteratively adjusts a multi-component potential (central, spin-orbit, and T_R tensor terms, each real and imaginary, with possible parity dependence) by solving linear equations based on the linear response of the S-matrix to small potential changes, using a generalized perturbation expression for coupled channels. The inversion was implemented in the code IMAGO and tested by inverting S-matrices generated from known potentials, including single-energy cases for deuterons on ⁵⁸Ni at 56 MeV and multi-energy cases for deuterons on ⁴He from 8 to 13 MeV.

## Key numbers

- For the ⁵⁸Ni target at 56 MeV deuteron laboratory energy: the inverted potential reproduced the target potential with σ ≈ 10⁻³, where σ² = Σ|S^t_k − S^c_k|² summed over partial waves.
- For the multi-energy d + ⁴He test at 11 energies (8, 8.5, …, 12.5, 13 MeV): the starting potential had σ = 10.552, and the converged inverted potential achieved σ = 0.135.
- For the T_P test case with 30 MeV deuterons on ⁵⁶Fe: the T_R potential had a Woods-Saxon volume form with depth 5 MeV; the real central potential from T_P inversion was reproduced to within a few percent for all r.
- The non-diagonal matrix elements of the T_R operator are given as ⟨J, J−1|T_R|J, J+1⟩ = ⟨J, J+1|T_R|J, J−1⟩ = [J(J+1)]^{1/2}/(2J+1).

## Context

This work extends the IP inversion method (previously developed by the same group for spin-1/2 projectiles, as described in Cooper et al. (various years) and Mackintosh et al. (various years)) to the coupled-channel case of spin-1 projectiles, enabling determination of tensor interactions from non-diagonal S-matrices. The method enables direct observable-to-potential inversion for spin-1 scattering data, which can be applied to analyze the large body of existing polarization data for deuterons and ⁶Li, and to study longstanding puzzles such as the anomalously small real part of the tensor interaction in deuteron-nucleus scattering. The authors note that the folding model predictions for the T_R interaction (Keaton et al., various years; Raynal, various years) are much too strong for heavy nuclei and about three times too strong for a ⁴He target, motivating the need for phenomenological determination via inversion.
