# Limiting Rotation Rate of Neutron Stars from Crust Breaking and Gravitational Waves

> Neutron stars are not observed to spin faster than about half their breakup rate. This limiting rotational frequency may be related to the strength of their crusts. As a star spins up from accretion, centrifugal forces stress the crust. We perform finite-element simulations of rotating neutron stars and find that the crust fails at rotation rates about half the breakup rate. Given uncertainties in microphysics, we have not determined the crust configuration after this failure. Instead, we argue that the crust may fail in an asymmetric way and could produce a configuration with a significant ellipticity (fractional difference in moments of inertia). If the ellipticity is large, a rotating star will radiate gravitational waves that may limit further spin up. These stars may be promising sources for LIGO / VIRGO and next generation gravitational wave detectors.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the maximum rotation rate of neutron stars is determined by crust breaking, which occurs at about half the Keplerian breakup frequency, and that the resulting asymmetric crust failure can produce a significant ellipticity that enables gravitational wave emission to limit further spin-up. The authors explicitly state they have not determined the crust configuration after failure, and the argument depends on the assumption that the crust fails asymmetrically.

## Method
The authors performed finite-element simulations of rotating neutron stars using a Newtonian polytropic equation of state, treating the centrifugal acceleration as a first-order perturbation. They solved the perturbed Euler and Laplace equations to obtain the displacement field, then computed the von-Mises strain throughout the crust to determine the rotation rate at which the maximum strain exceeds the breaking strain. The simulations used a 1.4 solar mass star with radius 10 km, with results scaled to other radii using the Kepler frequency scaling.

## Key numbers
- The crust fails at 0.58 of the Kepler breakup frequency for a breaking strain of 0.1, and at 0.42 of the breakup frequency for a breaking strain of 0.05
- For a 1.4 solar mass star with radius 12 km, the crust fails at 690 Hz assuming a breaking strain of 0.05
- The non-relativistic Kepler frequency for a 12 km star is 1650 Hz; the relativistic Kepler frequency is 1130 Hz
- The maximum ellipticity the crust can support is approximately 10^{-5}
- The ellipticity needed for torque balance between gravitational wave radiation and accretion is approximately 10^{-8}

## Context
This work extends earlier estimates by Fattoyev and Horowitz (2018) that used a simple constant-density neutron star model, and by Franco, Link, and Epstein (2000), by performing more realistic finite-element simulations that resolve the density dependence of crustal strain. The results suggest that crust breaking may explain why no neutron star is observed to spin faster than about 716 Hz (the fastest known pulsar, reported by Hessels in 2006). The paper argues that the resulting asymmetric crust failure could make these stars promising sources for LIGO, VIRGO, and next-generation gravitational wave detectors such as Cosmic Explorer.
