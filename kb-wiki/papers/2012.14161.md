# Prospects for quarkonium studies at the high-luminosity LHC

> Prospects for quarkonium-production studies accessible during the upcoming high-luminosity phases of the CERN Large Hadron Collider operation after 2021 are reviewed. Current experimental and theoretical open issues in the field are assessed together with the potential for future studies in quarkonium-related physics. This will be possible through the exploitation of the huge data samples to be collected in proton-proton, proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions, both in the collider and fixed-target modes. Such investigations include, among others, those of: (i) J/psi and Upsilon produced in association with other hard particles; (ii) chi(c,b) and eta(c,b) down to small transverse momenta; (iii) the constraints brought in by quarkonia on gluon PDFs, nuclear PDFs, TMDs, GPDs and GTMDs, as well as on the low-x parton dynamics; (iv) the gluon Sivers effect in polarised-nucleon collisions; (v) the properties of the quark-gluon plasma produced in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions and of collective partonic effects in general; and (vi) double and triple parton scatterings.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the potential for quarkonium-production studies at the high-luminosity LHC, but the abstract glosses over the fact that many of the projected measurements depend on running scenarios and integrated luminosities that have not yet been endorsed by the experiments, LHC, or CERN, and are reported only indicatively to give order-of-magnitude expectations.

## Method

The paper surveys the experimental and theoretical status of quarkonium physics in proton-proton, proton-nucleus, and nucleus-nucleus collisions, and projects the capabilities of the HL-LHC based on proposed running scenarios from the CERN Yellow Report (Citron 2018). It reviews specific observables including nuclear modification factors (R_AA), elliptic flow (v_2), and ratios of excited-to-ground-state yields, and discusses how these will be improved with larger integrated luminosities. The paper also summarizes recent theoretical developments in semi-classical transport models, open quantum systems, and effective field theories (NRQCD_G, SCET_G) applied to quarkonium production and suppression in the quark-gluon plasma.

## Key numbers

- The projected integrated luminosity for PbPb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.5 TeV in Run 3 (2021) is 2.3 inverse nanobarns over 3 weeks, and in 2022 is 3.9 inverse nanobarns over 5 weeks.
- For the HL-LHC, the expected luminosity for CMS is 10 inverse picobarns of PbPb data and 650 inverse picobarns of pp reference data.
- The binding energies of quarkonium states in vacuum range from 0.05 GeV (psi(2S)) to 1.16 GeV (eta_b), with the QGP peak temperature estimated at 0.385-0.740 GeV in PbPb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV.
- The ratio R_AA[chi_b(1P)] / R_AA[Upsilon(2S)] is predicted to be about 1/3 in central collisions in models with correlated recombination (Yao 2020), while models without it give a ratio larger than unity.
- The energy-loss model overpredicts J/psi suppression by a factor of 2 to 3 at pT > 10 GeV compared to ATLAS data, and predicts R_AA[psi(2S)] / R_AA[J/psi] approximately 1.1, while CMS data show the psi(2S) suppression is 2 to 3 times larger than that of J/psi.
- The expected integrated luminosity for the fixed-target mode in LHCb (SMOG2) is 20 inverse nanobarns in one year of PbAr collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 72 GeV.

## Context

This paper extends the physics case outlined in the CERN Yellow Report (Citron 2018) and builds on measurements from ATLAS (Aaboud 2018), CMS (Sirunyan 2017, 2018), and ALICE (Acharya 2020) at the LHC, as well as earlier RHIC results (Abelev 2012). It enables future discrimination between competing theoretical models of quarkonium suppression and regeneration, including transport models (Du 2017), statistical hadronisation (Andronic 2017), and effective field theory approaches (Makris 2019). The paper also identifies open questions such as the role of correlated recombination, the need for precise measurements of excited states like chi_b(1P) and Upsilon(3S), and the potential to observe quantum effects in quarkonium transport.
