# Review of nonflow estimation methods and uncertainties in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> Collective anisotropic flow, where particles are correlated over the entire event, is a prominent phenomenon in relativistic heavy-ion collisions and is sensitive to the properties of the matter created in those collisions. It is often measured by two- and multi-particle correlations and is therefore contaminated by nonflow, those genuine few-body correlations unrelated to the global event-wise correlations. Many methods have been devised to estimate nonflow contamination with various degrees of successes and difficulties. Here, we review those methods pedagogically, discussing the pros and cons of each method, and give examples of ballpark estimate of nonflow contamination and associated uncertainties in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. We hope such a review of the various nonflow estimation methods in a single place would prove helpful to future researches.

## Key claim
This review paper systematically catalogs and evaluates methods for estimating nonflow contamination in anisotropic flow measurements, but emphasizes that no single method is universally reliable; each carries inherent assumptions that make uncertainty quantification difficult, and the choice of method and baseline significantly affects the resulting flow signal.

## Method
The authors provide a pedagogical review of three main categories of nonflow estimation methods used in relativistic heavy-ion collisions: pseudorapidity gap methods (simple gap, two-subevent, three- and four-subevent), low-multiplicity subtraction methods (inverse multiplicity scaling, near-side jetlike yield-scaled subtraction), and data-driven template fit methods. For each method, they discuss the underlying assumptions, implementation details, and known shortcomings, drawing on examples from RHIC and LHC experiments.

## Key numbers
- In central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV, HIJING simulations show only a modest excess (10-20%) of correlation strengths compared to the 1/N scaling from peripheral collisions would predict
- The measured v2 in peripheral 70-80% Au+Au collisions is approximately 6.9% and in top 5% central collisions approximately 2.4% (from Adams et al. 2004)
- Under 1/N scaling assumptions, nonflow in central collisions from peripheral baseline would give (v2^peri)^2 * (Nch^peri/Nch^cent) approximately 1.7x10^-4, corresponding to 30% nonflow in (v2^cent)^2
- The pp/AuAu ratio of cumulative correlations (NV2) in top 5% central collisions is on average 15%, or 12% after correcting for occupancy-dependent detector efficiency

## Context
This review extends the foundational work on nonflow by Borghini et al. (2000, 2001, 2002) and Ollitrault et al. (2009), and addresses the growing need for systematic nonflow estimation in light of small-system collectivity observations by CMS (2010, 2012), ATLAS (2012), ALICE (2012), and PHENIX (2013, 2018). The paper enables future researchers to select appropriate nonflow estimation methods with awareness of their limitations, particularly for ongoing debates about whether observed anisotropies in small systems (pp, pA, dA) represent genuine collective flow or are dominated by nonflow correlations.
