# Anisotropic Compact Stars in Rastall-Rainbow Gravity

> In this work, we have investigated anisotropic neutron stars in the framework of Rastall-Rainbow gravity. All our calculations were computed using the IU-FSU realistic equation of state (EoS), in which was considered two cases: standard nucleonic composition and the one with the eight lightest baryons. From the neutron star masses and radii obtained we conclude that anisotropic pressure has significant consequences on the structure of stellar objects. In particular, when anisotropy is considered within the general relativity framework, it significantly modifies the maximum stellar mass. On the other hand, when Rastall-Rainbow gravity and anisotropy are simultaneously considered, they provide the best results for mass and radius values, including important astrophysical objects such as the LMXB NGC 6397 and the extremely massive pulsar millisecond MSP J0740 + 6620. Although the expected inclusion of hyperons in the nuclear model reproduces stellar masses smaller than those produced by standard nucleonic matter, we shown that the hyperon puzzle problem can be solved by including anisotropic effects on compact stars in the context of the Rastall-Rainbow gravity.

## Key claim

The paper claims that including anisotropic pressure in neutron star models within the Rastall-Rainbow gravity framework can simultaneously satisfy the observational constraints from the low-mass X-ray binary NGC 6397 and the massive pulsar MSP J0740+6620, and can also resolve the hyperon puzzle (the softening of the equation of state when hyperons are included), but only for specific positive values of the anisotropy parameter beta and specific choices of the Rastall and Rainbow parameters.

## Method

The authors solved modified Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations derived from the Rastall-Rainbow gravity field equations for a static, spherically symmetric, anisotropic fluid. They used the IU-FSU relativistic mean-field equation of state for two compositions: standard nucleonic matter and matter including the eight lightest baryons (hyperons). Anisotropy was introduced via a profile sigma = beta * p_r * (1 - e^{-lambda}), where beta is a constant parameter, and the Rastall parameter lambda and Rainbow function Sigma were varied around values close to unity.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass for nucleonic matter in general relativity (GR) with no anisotropy: 1.94 solar masses, radius 11.22 km.
- Maximum mass for nucleonic matter in GR with anisotropy beta = 1.5: 2.44 solar masses, radius 11.65 km.
- Maximum mass for nucleonic matter in Rastall-Rainbow gravity (Sigma = 1.1, lambda = 0.999) with anisotropy beta = 1.0: 2.50 solar masses, radius 12.48 km.
- Maximum mass for nucleonic matter in Rastall-Rainbow gravity (lambda = 1.001, Sigma = 1.01) with anisotropy beta = 1.5: 2.47 solar masses, radius 11.94 km.
- Maximum mass for hyperonic matter in GR with no anisotropy: 1.56 solar masses, radius 11.70 km.
- Maximum mass for hyperonic matter in GR with anisotropy beta = 2.1: 2.19 solar masses, radius 12.01 km.
- Maximum mass for hyperonic matter in Rastall-Rainbow gravity (Sigma = 0.95, lambda = 0.999) with anisotropy beta = 2.1: 2.08 solar masses, radius 11.25 km.
- Maximum mass for hyperonic matter in Rastall-Rainbow gravity (lambda = 0.999, Sigma = 1.01) with anisotropy beta = 2.1: 2.21 solar masses, radius 11.99 km.
- Radius for the canonical 1.4 solar mass star in GR with nucleonic matter and no anisotropy: 12.55 km.
- Radius for the canonical 1.4 solar mass star in GR with hyperonic matter and no anisotropy: 13.34 km.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study of neutron stars in Rastall-Rainbow gravity (Mota et al., 2019) by adding anisotropic pressure, which was previously studied in GR by Arbanil et al. (2016). The paper addresses the hyperon puzzle, where inclusion of hyperons softens the equation of state and reduces maximum masses below 2 solar masses; earlier attempts to resolve it include adding new degrees of freedom (Lopes et al., 2018) or using braneworld models (Benito et al., 2014). The results enable future work where the energy dependence of the Rainbow functions is treated more realistically rather than as effective parameters.
