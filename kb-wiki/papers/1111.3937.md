# Strangelet dwarfs

> If the surface tension of quark matter is low enough, quark matter is not self bound. At sufficiently low pressure and temperature, it will take the form of a crystal of positively charged strangelets in a neutralizing background of electrons. In this case there will exist, in addition to the usual family of strange stars, a family of low-mass large-radius objects analogous to white dwarfs, which we call "strangelet dwarfs". Using a generic parametrization of the equation of state of quark matter, we calculate the mass-radius relationship of these objects.

## Key claim

If the surface tension of quark matter is below a critical value (of order a few MeV/fm² in typical models), strange matter will not be self-bound at low pressure and temperature but instead forms a crystal of positively charged strangelets in an electron background, giving rise to a new family of low-mass, large-radius stellar objects called "strangelet dwarfs" that are analogous to white dwarfs.

## Method

The authors solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation using an equation of state derived from a Wigner-Seitz cell model of the strangelet crystal, treating each cell as a spherical quark matter lump surrounded by degenerate electrons. They use a Thomas-Fermi approach to solve the Poisson equation for the charge distribution within each cell, with the pressure discontinuity at the quark matter surface balanced by surface tension, and minimize the enthalpy per quark to find the favored cell configuration. The quark matter equation of state is parameterized generically in terms of the quark chemical potential, charge density, charge susceptibility, and surface tension, with the electron gas treated relativistically including mass effects.

## Key numbers

* Critical surface tension for strangelet crystal formation: \(\sigma_{\rm crit} = 0.1325 \, n_Q^2 / \sqrt{4\pi\alpha} \chi_Q^{3/2}\), which for typical models is of order 1 to 10 MeV/fm²
* Quark chemical potential at the vacuum transition: \(\mu_{\rm crit} = 300\) MeV (fixed, must be less than approximately 310 MeV for the strange matter hypothesis)
* Debye screening lengths studied: \(\lambda_D = 4.82\) fm and \(\lambda_D = 6.82\) fm, corresponding to charge susceptibilities \(\chi_Q \approx 0.2 \mu_{\rm crit}^2\) and \(\chi_Q \approx 0.1 \mu_{\rm crit}^2\)
* Charge density parameters studied: \(n_Q = 0.0445\), \(0.0791\), and \(0.124\) fm\(^{-3}\), corresponding to strange quark masses of approximately 150, 200, and 250 MeV in non-interacting quark matter
* Surface tension values studied: \(\sigma = 0.3\), 1.0, 3.0, and 10.0 MeV fm\(^{-2}\), with the softness prefactor \(S\) ranging from 90 to 367 MeV depending on parameters (see Table I)
* Transition to uniform quark matter occurs above pressure \(p \approx 20000\) MeV\(^4\)
* Nonrelativistic electron regime applies when pressure \(\lesssim 0.0003\) MeV\(^4\) (where \(k_{Fe} \lesssim m_e\))

## Context

This work extends the study of strangelet crystal crusts on strange stars (Alford 2008) to the diffuse branch of gravitationally bound strange matter objects, analogous to how white dwarfs are the diffuse branch of nuclear matter stars alongside neutron stars. The authors build on earlier work showing that low surface tension quark matter favors a crystal of strangelets over uniform matter (Jaikumar 2005, Alford 2006), and use a similar Wigner-Seitz cell approach to that employed in studies of mixed phases in neutron star interiors (Maruyama 2007). The predicted strangelet dwarfs could be detected by gravitational lensing surveys such as MOA and OGLE, potentially placing bounds on the existence of low-mass compact objects.
