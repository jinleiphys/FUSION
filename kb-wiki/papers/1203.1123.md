# Too many $X's$, $Y's$ and $Z's$?

> A large number of new states have been reported during the last few years in charmonium spectroscopy above the charmed meson production threshold. They have been called $X's$, $Y's$, and $Z's$. We reflect on the influence of thresholds on heavy meson spectroscopy comparing different flavor sectors and quantum numbers. The validity of a quark-model picture above open-flavor thresholds would severely restrict the number of channels that may lodge meson-meson molecules.

## Key claim

The constituent quark model can accommodate most of the recently reported charmonium and bottomonium states above open-flavor thresholds, but only a very restricted set of quantum numbers can host meson-meson molecules; the X(3872) is the only firmly established state that is difficult to fit into the simple quark-antiquark scheme, while many other claimed states may disappear with better statistics or be explained as threshold cusps.

## Method

The authors compare the ordering of two-meson thresholds (heavy-light + light-heavy versus heavy-heavy + light-light) for hidden-strange, hidden-charm, and hidden-bottom sectors across several JPC quantum numbers, using experimental masses from the PDG. They then perform coupled-channel calculations based on a constituent quark model with one-gluon exchange, confinement, and a chiral potential between light quarks, computing Fredholm determinants to assess whether attractive interactions produce bound states or resonances. The analysis is extended to consider the effect of many-body confining forces and the stability of exotic QQ\bar n\bar n systems.

## Key numbers

- The X(3872) has JPC = 1++ and lies just below the D\bar D* threshold; the D\bar D* and J/ψω thresholds are almost degenerate for these quantum numbers in the charm sector.
- For hidden-strange (Q=s), the (Q\bar Q)-(n\bar n) two-meson mass is larger than the (Q\bar n)-(n\bar Q) mass for all JPC; for hidden-charm and hidden-bottom, the ordering is reversed.
- In the bottom sector, the B\bar B* interaction is more attractive than in charm due to lower kinetic energy, but the coupling to the lower Υω channel destroys any bound state, so no twin of the X(3872) is expected in bottomonium.
- For exotic QQ\bar n\bar n systems, stability increases with heavy quark mass, being much more probable in the bottom sector than in the strange sector.

## Context

This work extends the constituent quark model analysis of Weinstein and Isgur (1990) on light scalar mesons to the heavy flavor sector, and directly addresses the proliferation of XYZ states reported by Belle, BaBar, and other collaborations. The authors argue against hadronic models based on traditional meson theory of nuclear forces or heavy quark symmetry that predict twin states of the X(3872) in bottomonium, as discussed by Tornqvist (1992). The paper enables a critical reassessment of which experimental signals are genuine resonances versus threshold effects, and motivates searches for exotic QQ\bar n\bar n states at facilities such as LHCb and Belle II.
