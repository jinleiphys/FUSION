# Recent thermodynamic results from lattice QCD analyzed within a quasi-particle model

> The thermodynamic behavior of QCD matter at high temperature is currently studied by lattice QCD theory. The main features are the fast rise of the energy density $ε$ around the critical temperature $T_c$ and the large trace anomaly of the energy momentum tensor $< Θ_μ^μ>=ε- 3 P$ which hints at a strongly interacting system. Such features can be accounted for by employing a massive quasi-particle model with a temperature-dependent bag constant. Recent lattice QCD calculations with physical quark masses by the Wuppertal-Budapest group have shown a slower increase of $ε$ and a smaller $<Θ_μ^μ>$ peak with respect to previous results from the hotQCD collaboration. We investigate the implications of such differences from the point of view of a quasi-particle model, also discussing light and strange quark number susceptibilities. Furthermore, we predict the impact of these discrepancies on the temperature-dependence of the transport properties of matter, like the shear and bulk viscosities.

## Key claim

The quasi-particle model (QPM) can reproduce the thermodynamic behavior of QCD matter from both the hotQCD and Wuppertal-Budapest lattice data sets, but the slower rise of energy density and smaller trace anomaly peak in the Wuppertal-Budapest results lead to larger quasi-particle masses and a significantly different temperature dependence of transport coefficients, particularly shear viscosity.

## Method

The authors fit a quasi-particle model with temperature-dependent effective masses for quarks and gluons and a temperature-dependent bag constant to lattice QCD energy density data from both the hotQCD collaboration (using p4 action) and the Wuppertal-Budapest collaboration. The coupling constant g(T) is obtained numerically by solving a differential equation derived from thermodynamic consistency, and the resulting quasi-particle masses and bag constant are then used to compute shear and bulk viscosities via relativistic kinetic theory in the relaxation time approximation, using two different parametrizations for the relaxation time (one with g^2 dependence and one with g^4 dependence matching perturbative QCD).

## Key numbers

- The critical temperature used for the hotQCD fit is T_c = 175 MeV; for the Wuppertal-Budapest fit it is T_c = 155 MeV
- For the g^2 parametrization of relaxation time, the parameterization g^2(T) = 48π^2/[(11N_C-2N_f) ln(λ(T/T_C - T_S/T_C)^2)] yields λ=17.7 and T_S/T_C=1.15 for the hotQCD fit, and λ=2.6 and T_S/T_C=0.57 for the Wuppertal-Budapest fit
- For the g^4 parametrization with μ* = πT, the minimum η/s is approximately 0.68 for the hotQCD fit and 0.66 for the Wuppertal-Budapest fit
- With the g^4 parametrization and a_2=25, the minimum η/s reaches approximately 1/(4π) for both fits
- At T ~ 4 T_c, the hotQCD fit gives 4π η/s ~ 10 while the Wuppertal-Budapest fit gives 4π η/s ~ 4 (using the g^4 parametrization)

## Context

This work extends previous quasi-particle model analyses of lattice QCD thermodynamics (Gorenstein 1995, Peshier 1996, Levai and Heinz 1998) by comparing fits to two different contemporary lattice data sets: the hotQCD collaboration results and the newer Wuppertal-Budapest collaboration results (Borsanyi 2010) which use physical quark masses and continuum extrapolation. The paper enables predictions for transport properties that cannot be directly computed on the lattice, showing that the choice of lattice data significantly affects the temperature dependence of shear and bulk viscosities, with implications for hydrodynamic modeling of heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC energies.
