# Dynamical Quasi-Stationary States in a system with long-range forces

> The Hamiltonian Mean Field model describes a system of N fully-coupled particles showing a second-order phase transition as a function of the energy. The dynamics of the model presents interesting features in a small energy region below the critical point. In particular, when the particles are prepared in a ``water bag'' initial state, the relaxation to equilibrium is very slow. In the transient time the system lives in a dynamical quasi-stationary state and exhibits anomalous (enhanced) diffusion and Lévy walks. In this paper we study temperature and velocity distribution of the quasi-stationary state and we show that the lifetime of such a state increases with N. In particular when the $N\to \infty$ limit is taken before the $t \to \infty$ limit, the results obtained are different from the expected canonical predictions. This scenario seems to confirm a recent conjecture proposed by C.Tsallis.

# Dynamical Quasi-Stationary States in a system with long-range forces

## Key claim

For the Hamiltonian Mean Field model initialized in a "water bag" state at energies just below the critical point, if the thermodynamic limit (N → ∞) is taken before the infinite-time limit (t → ∞), the system never reaches canonical equilibrium but instead remains indefinitely in a quasi-stationary state with a temperature lower than the canonical prediction, supporting the generalized thermostatistics conjecture of C. Tsallis.

## Method

The authors performed microcanonical numerical simulations of N classical particles on a unit circle interacting via the Hamiltonian Mean Field (HMF) model, integrating the equations of motion with a 4th-order symplectic algorithm using time step δt = 0.2 for up to 10⁷ steps. They initialized all particles at θ_i = 0 with a uniform ("water bag") momentum distribution centered at zero, then measured the time evolution of temperature (via average kinetic energy), mean square displacement in angle, and momentum probability distribution functions for system sizes up to N = 25000 at energy densities U = E/N between 0.6 and the critical value U_c = 0.75.

## Key numbers

- Critical energy density: U_c = 0.75, corresponding to critical temperature T_c = 0.5 in the canonical ensemble.
- For U = 0.69 and N = 1000, the anomalous diffusion exponent α = 1.45 ± 0.1 in the quasi-stationary regime.
- For U = 0.69, the walking time distribution exponent μ = 2.14 and trapping time distribution exponent ν = 1.58, yielding α = 1.44 via the Klafter-Zumofen formula.
- The canonical temperature at U = 0.69 is T_can = 0.4757; the quasi-stationary state temperature for N = 20000 lies below this value and saturates around N = 20000.
- Maximum system size simulated: N = 25000.
- Relative energy conservation error: ΔE/E < 10⁻⁵.

## Context

This work extends previous analytical and numerical studies of the HMF model by Latora, Rapisarda, and Ruffo (1998-2000), which showed that the model has an exact canonical solution and exhibits a second-order phase transition. The results contradict the expectation that large-N systems quickly relax to canonical equilibrium; instead, the quasi-stationary state lifetime diverges with N, and the caloric curve shows a backbending that mimics a first-order phase transition rather than the equilibrium second-order transition. The findings support the conjecture of C. Tsallis (proposed at the same conference) that nonextensive systems may require generalized thermostatistics, and open the question of connecting out-of-equilibrium dynamics in long-range interacting systems to both generalized statistics and real experimental systems.
