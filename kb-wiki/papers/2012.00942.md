# Imaginary Time Mean-Field Method for Collective Tunneling

> Background: Quantum tunneling in many-body systems is the subject of many experimental and theoretical studies in fields ranging from cold atoms to nuclear physics. However, theoretical description of quantum tunneling with strongly interacting particles, such as nucleons in atomic nuclei, remains a major challenge in quantum physics. Purpose: An initial-value approach to tunneling accounting for the degrees of freedom of each interacting particle is highly desirable. Methods: Inspired by existing methods to describe instantons with periodic solutions in imaginary time, we investigate the possibility to use an initial value approach to describe tunneling at the mean-field level. Real-time and imaginary-time Hartree dynamics are compared to the exact solution in the case of two particles in a two-well potential. Results: Whereas real-time evolutions exhibit a spurious self-trapping effect preventing tunneling in strongly interacting systems, the imaginary-time-dependent mean-field method predicts tunneling rates in excellent agreement with the exact solution. Conclusions: Being an initial-value method, it could be more suitable than approaches requiring periodic solutions to describe realistic systems such as heavy-ion fusion.

## Key claim

The imaginary-time-dependent mean-field method predicts tunneling rates in excellent agreement with the exact solution for strongly interacting systems, whereas real-time mean-field evolutions exhibit a spurious self-trapping effect that prevents tunneling when the interaction strength exceeds a critical threshold of \(|\mu| = 2\alpha\).

## Method

The authors compare real-time and imaginary-time Hartree dynamics for two distinguishable particles in a two-well potential, where the single-particle Hamiltonian has eigenstates \(\ket{+}\) (ground state, energy 0) and \(\ket{-}\) (excited state, energy \(\alpha\)), and the interaction \(\mu\) acts when both particles occupy the same well. Real-time mean-field equations are derived from the time-dependent Hartree approximation, and imaginary-time equations are obtained via a Wick rotation \(t \rightarrow -i\tau\). Tunneling probabilities are extracted from the imaginary-time mean-field paths by computing the action \(W[T] = -i\int_0^T d\tau \bra{\Psi}\partial_\tau\ket{\Psi}\) and comparing the resulting tunneling rates to the exact analytical solution.

## Key numbers

* The critical interaction strength for the onset of self-trapping in real-time mean-field dynamics is \(|\mu| = 2\alpha\) (with \(\alpha=1\) in the calculations), above which particles become trapped in one well.
* For the exact solution, the tunneling rate is given by \(2\omega_- = \frac{2}{\pi}\Delta E\) where \(\Delta E = \frac{\sqrt{4\alpha^2+\mu^2} - |\mu|}{2}\), which for \(|\mu| \gg \alpha\) asymptotes to \(\alpha^2/|\mu|\).
* The energy splitting \(\Delta E\) in nuclear systems is estimated to range from \(10^{-13}\) MeV for ground-state fission to \(10^{-2}\) MeV near the barrier, based on parameters from Arve (1987).

## Context

This work extends the imaginary-time mean-field approach originally developed by Levit (1980) and Reinhardt (1981) for describing instantons with periodic solutions, but replaces the requirement for periodic bounce solutions with an initial-value method that only requires the initial state \(|\Psi(0)\rangle\). The method is tested against the exact solution of a two-well model, and the authors propose it could be applied to realistic nuclear systems such as heavy-ion fusion, spontaneous fission, and cluster decay, where nucleonic degrees of freedom and dissipation effects are important. The approach contrasts with real-time mean-field methods (e.g., time-dependent Hartree-Fock) which fail for strongly interacting systems due to discrete self-trapping, as previously identified by Eilbeck (1985), Smerzi (1997), and Milburn (1997).
