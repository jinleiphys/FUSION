# Development of particle multiplicity distributions using a general form of the grand canonical partition function and applications to L3 and H1 Data

> Various phenomenological models of particle multiplicity distributions are discussed using a general form of a unified model which is based on the grand canonical partition function and Feynman's path integral approach to statistical processes. These models can be written as special cases of a more general distribution which has three control parameters which are $a$, $x$, $z$. The relation to these parameters to various physical quantities are discussed. A connection of the parameter $a$ with Fisher's critical exponent $τ$ is developed. Using this grand canonical approach, moments, cumulants and combinants are discussed and a physical interpretation of the combinants are given and their behavior connected to the critical exponent $τ$. Various physical phenomena such as hierarchical structure, void scaling relations, KNO scaling features, clan variables, and branching laws are shown in terms of this general approach. Several of these features which were previously developed in terms of the negative binomial distribution are found to be more general. Both hierarchical structure and void scaling relations depend on the Fisher exponent $τ$. Applications of our approach to the charged particle multiplicity distribution in jets of L3 and H1 data are given. It is shown that just looking at the mean and fluctuation of data is not enough to distinguish these distributions or the underlying mechanism. The mean, fluctuation and third cummulant of distribution determine three parameters $x$, $z$, $a$. We find that a generalized random work model fits the data better than the widely used negative binomial model.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a unified three-parameter model (parameters $a$, $x$, $z$) based on the grand canonical partition function can describe various phenomenological multiplicity distributions, and that a generalized random walk model fits L3 and H1 charged particle multiplicity data better than the widely used negative binomial model, but only when the mean, fluctuation, and third cumulant are all used to determine the three parameters.

## Method

The authors construct a general grand canonical partition function with weights $x_k$ for clusters of size $k$, where the $x_k$ are the combinants. They relate the parameter $a$ to Fisher's critical exponent $\tau$ and show that specific choices of $x_k$ (e.g., $x_k = x z^k/k^\tau$) reduce to known distributions such as the negative binomial, geometric, and Lorentz/Catalan models. They then fit the generalized model to charged particle multiplicity distributions in jets from L3 and H1 data, using the mean, fluctuation, and third cumulant to determine the three parameters $x$, $z$, and $a$.

## Key numbers

- The negative binomial distribution has $\kappa_m = (m-1)! \kappa_2^{m-1}$ for its reduced factorial cumulants, where $\kappa_2 = \xi = 1/x$ and $x$ is the negative binomial parameter.
- For a thermal pion model in the high temperature limit, $x_k = (V/\pi^2) T^3 /k^4$, giving $\tau = 4$; in the low temperature limit, $x_k = (V/\lambda_T^3) (e^{-m/T})^k /k^{5/2}$, giving $\tau = 5/2$.
- The Lorentz/Catalan model has $x_k = \frac{1}{k} 2^{-2(k-1)} \binom{2(k-1)}{k-1} x z^k$, and in ancestral variables $x = \beta/4p$, $z = 4p(1-p)$.
- For the Lorentz/Catalan model, the void scaling function is $\chi = (\sqrt{2\xi\bra A\ket+1} - 1)/(\xi\bra A\ket)$.
- For the negative binomial distribution, the void scaling function is $\chi = \ln(1 + \xi\bra A\ket)/(\xi\bra A\ket)$.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies of nuclear multifragmentation using canonical partition functions (Lee and Mekjian, earlier works) to multiparticle production using a grand canonical approach. It unifies several existing phenomenological models (negative binomial, geometric, Lorentz/Catalan) as special cases of a general distribution with parameter $a$ related to Fisher's critical exponent $\tau$. The paper enables future analyses of multiplicity distributions at RHIC and other colliders to distinguish between underlying production mechanisms by using higher-order cumulants beyond the mean and fluctuation, and it connects particle production phenomenology to critical phenomena and cluster physics.
