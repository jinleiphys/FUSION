# Quark and Glue Components of the Proton Spin from Lattice Calculation

> The status of lattice calculations of the quark spin, the quark orbital angular momentum, the glue angular momentum and glue spin in the nucleon is summarized. The quark spin calculation is recently carried out from the anomalous Ward identity with chiral fermions and is found to be small mainly due to the large negative anomaly term which is believed to be the source of the `proton spin crisis'. We also present the first calculation of the glue spin at finite nucleon momenta.

# Quark and Glue Components of the Proton Spin from Lattice Calculation

## Key claim
The total quark spin contribution to the proton spin is small (approximately 25%), primarily due to a large negative anomaly term from the topological charge density, which the authors identify as the source of the "proton spin crisis." The first lattice calculation of glue spin at finite nucleon momenta is presented as a preliminary result, with the caveat that the glue spin operator is frame-dependent and must be extrapolated to the infinite momentum frame for comparison with experimental glue helicity.

## Method
The authors performed lattice QCD calculations using overlap fermions on a 24^3 x 64 lattice with 2+1 flavor dynamical domain-wall configurations at a sea pion mass of 330 MeV. For the quark spin, they employed the anomalous Ward identity (AWI) to compute the flavor-singlet axial-vector current non-perturbatively, separating contributions from the pseudoscalar density (low-frequency modes) and the topological charge density (high-frequency modes). For the glue spin, they constructed the electric field from the overlap Dirac operator and the gauge potential from unsmeared gauge links in Coulomb gauge, calculating matrix elements at nucleon momenta p_z = 0, 460 MeV, and 920 MeV. A quenched lattice calculation was also performed with three valence quark masses extrapolated to the physical pion mass to obtain the full decomposition of proton spin into quark spin, quark orbital angular momentum, and glue angular momentum.

## Key numbers
- Total quark spin fraction of the nucleon spin: 0.25(12) from quenched calculation
- Glue angular momentum fraction of the nucleon spin: 0.28(8) from quenched calculation
- Quark orbital angular momentum fraction of the nucleon spin: 0.47(13) from quenched calculation
- Strange quark spin Δs = -0.026(5) at m_pi = 330 MeV from AWI approach on dynamical configurations
- Corrected total quark spin ΔΣ = 0.30(6) at the unitary point (m_pi = 330 MeV) after applying a 1.8(1) factor for induced pseudoscalar form factor and finite volume effects
- Isovector axial coupling g_A^3 = 1.13(2) from axial-vector current at the unitary point
- Momentum fraction for u quark (connected insertion): 0.413(38) in MSbar at 2 GeV
- Momentum fraction for d quark (connected insertion): 0.150(19) in MSbar at 2 GeV
- Glue momentum fraction: 0.334(55) in MSbar at 2 GeV
- Charm quark gives zero contribution to proton spin within error due to cancellation between pseudoscalar and topological terms

## Context
This work extends lattice calculations of nucleon spin structure that began with quenched approximations (Dong 1995, Fukugita 1994) and continued with dynamical fermions (QCDSF 2011, Engelhardt 2012, Abdel-Rehim 2013, Babich 2010). The small quark spin found here (approximately 25%) is consistent with global analyses of deep inelastic scattering data (deFlorian 2009), while the strange quark spin Δs = -0.026(5) is several times smaller than the global fit value of approximately -0.11 (deFlorian 2009, Leader 2015), raising questions about renormalization procedures. The glue spin calculation represents the first lattice attempt to compute ΔG directly, aiming to complement experimental results from COMPASS, STAR, HERMES, and PHENIX that show evidence of non-zero glue helicity (deFlorian 2014). The approach enables future comparisons between lattice QCD and experimental determinations of the glue helicity distribution, potentially resolving the "Dark Spin" conundrum where known components do not sum to the full proton spin.
