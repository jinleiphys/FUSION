# Towards few-body QCD on a quantum computer

> Quantum computers are promising tools for the simulation of many-body systems, and among those, QCD stands out by its rich phenomenology. Every simulation starts with a codification, and here we succently review a newly developed compact encoding based on the identification between registers and particles; the quantum memory is divided into registers, and to each we associate a Hilbert space of dimension the number of degrees of freedom of the codified particles. In this way we gain an exponential compression over direct encodings for a low number of particles with many degrees of freedom. As an example we apply this encoding on a two-register memory and implement antisymmetrization and exponentiation algorithms.

# Towards few-body QCD on a quantum computer

## Key claim
The paper presents a compact register-particle encoding for quantum computers that achieves exponential compression over direct encodings for systems with few particles but many internal degrees of freedom, and demonstrates its applicability to QCD-inspired few-body problems. The encoding is efficient specifically for low particle numbers with many degrees of freedom, but the authors note that unentangling circuits required for antisymmetrization are currently beyond the capabilities of available quantum hardware.

## Method
The authors divide quantum memory into registers, each representing a single particle with its degrees of freedom (presence/absence, spin, momentum) encoded as binary numbers in dedicated qubits. Creation and annihilation operators are constructed using set, scrap, and control operators acting on individual registers, with step-antisymmetrizers enforcing fermionic statistics. The time evolution operator is implemented via Trotter decomposition of kinetic and momentum-exchange terms, with the resulting exponentials decomposed into basic quantum gates using Qiskit's HamiltonianGate function.

## Key numbers
- The compact encoding uses 5 qubits per particle for discretization a) (1 presence + 1 spin + 3 momentum qubits representing 5 momentum values) and 6 qubits per particle for discretization b) (1 presence + 1 spin + 4 momentum qubits representing 13 momentum values)
- For the two-electron simulation, the kinetic energy coefficient is e_k = |k̄|² E₀ / (2 r_s²) with E₀ = 340 eV and r_s = 30
- The momentum exchange coefficient is λ_q = E₀ / (r_s N_e |q̄|) with N_e = 2
- For discretization a): Trotter interval of 0.055 eV⁻¹, 100 time points, 1000 shots per point; single frequency at 1.79 ± 0.04 eV with period 0.56 ± 0.01 eV⁻¹
- For discretization b): Trotter interval of 0.067 eV⁻¹, 250 frequency points; five resolved peaks at frequencies 0.54 ± 0.06, 0.84 ± 0.06, 1.37 ± 0.06, 3.17 ± 0.06, and 3.71 ± 0.06 eV
- The antisymmetrization circuit without uncomputation has depth of approximately 300 layers; with uncomputation steps the depth increases to around 2000 layers
- The ibm_brisbane chip experiment used 5000 shots with error mitigation

## Context
This work extends the compact encoding scheme introduced by Galvez-Viruet (2024) for quantum simulation of many-body systems, building on the foundational work of Feynman (1982) and Lloyd (1996) on quantum simulation. It addresses the sign problem that plagues classical Monte Carlo simulations of QCD, as discussed by de Forcrand (2010) and Ortiz (2001), which limits calculations of real-time observables and equations of state for neutron star matter. The encoding is contrasted with direct encodings such as Jordan-Wigner (1928) and Bravyi-Kitaev (2002) which require one qubit per fermionic mode. The work enables future extensions to include color and flavor quantum numbers, moving toward full QCD simulations on quantum computers, though the authors note that current hardware limitations prevent implementation of the full unentangling circuits.
