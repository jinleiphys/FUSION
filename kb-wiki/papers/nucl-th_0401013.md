# Supergiant halos as an integral record of natural pionic radioactivity

> In this paper an unified interpretation of the supergiant halos (SGH), discovered by Grady, Walker and Laemmlein, is discussed. So, it is proved that SGH`s can be considered as integral records of the nuclear pionic radioactivity.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that supergiant halos (SGH) discovered by Grady and Walker (1969) and Laemmlein (1960s) can be interpreted as integral records of nuclear pionic radioactivity, rather than as evidence for undiscovered alpha-emitters; this interpretation depends on the condition that the pionic dose from pion emission satisfies the coloration threshold rule for the mineral.

## Method

The authors compare the known characteristic features of supergiant halos (SGH), including their radii, lack of sharp edges, gradual fading of coloration, and presence of fossil fission tracks, with the predicted signatures of pionic radiohalos (PIRH) produced by spontaneous pion emission from a parent nucleus via inclusive reactions such as (A,Z) → π + X. They argue that SGH match the PIRH(-) type, where only negative pions (π⁻) satisfy the dose rule, and that Laemmlein halos with radii around 1–1.5 mm may correspond to SPIRH, a superposition of both π⁻ and π⁺ emission.

## Key numbers

- SGH radii range between 50 μm and 410 μm, with inclusions of radii 20–52 μm.
- The difference in radius between PIRH(-) and SPIRH is about 700–800 μm, in favor of SPIRH.
- Laemmlein halos have dimensions around 1–1.5 mm, potentially identifying them as SPIRH.
- π⁻ yields from pionic radioactivity are expected to be about two orders of magnitude larger than π⁺ yields.

## Context

This work extends the interpretation of radioactive halos beyond conventional alpha-emitters, building on the discovery of supergiant halos by Grady and Walker (1969) and Laemmlein (1960s), which could not be explained by known alpha-decay chains. The authors propose that pionic radioactivity, previously discussed in their own papers (Ion et al., 2004), provides a unified explanation for these anomalous halos, and suggest that future investigation of Laemmlein halos could test the SPIRH hypothesis involving positive pion emission.
