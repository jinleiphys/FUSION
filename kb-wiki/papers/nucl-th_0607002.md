# The Skyrme Interaction in finite nuclei and nuclear matter

> Self-consistent mean-field models are a powerful tool in the investigation of nuclear structure and low-energy dynamics. They are based on effective energy-density functionals, often formulated in terms of effective density-dependent nucleon-nucleon interactions. The free parameters of the functional are adjusted to empirical data. A proper choice of these parameters requires a comprehensive set of constraints covering experimental data on finite nuclei, concerning static as well as dynamical properties, empirical characteristics of nuclear matter, and observational information on nucleosynthesis, neutron stars and supernovae. This work aims at a comprehensive survey of the performance of one of the most successful non-relativistic self-consistent method, the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock model (SHF), with respect to these constraints. A full description of the Skyrme functional is given and its relation to other effective interactions is discussed. The validity of the application of SHF far from stability and in dense environments beyond the nuclear saturation density is critically assessed. The use of SHF in models extended beyond the mean field approximation by including some correlations is discussed. Finally, future prospects for further development of SHF towards a more consistent application of the existing and promisingly newly developing constraints are outlined.

## Key claim

This work provides a comprehensive survey of the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) model's performance against a broad set of constraints from finite nuclei, nuclear matter, and astrophysics, while critically assessing that the model's extrapolative power to nuclei far from stability and to dense environments beyond saturation density is still rather unsatisfactory and lacks credibility.

## Method

The authors review the Skyrme energy-density functional formalism, including its motivation from density-matrix expansion and unitary correlator methods, and detail the composition of the functional from local densities and currents. They survey the performance of SHF across static and dynamical properties of finite nuclei, nuclear matter characteristics, and astrophysical applications, comparing results from multiple Skyrme parameter sets (including SkM*, SkP, SLy6, SkI3, SkI4, and BSk1) against experimental data and discussing limitations from correlations beyond mean field.

## Key numbers

- The Skyrme functional typically leaves about 6 to 12 free parameters which are adjusted phenomenologically.
- Ab initio calculations starting from measured nucleon-nucleon interactions fail to reproduce nuclear binding and radii at a quantitative level, with typical errors of about 5%.
- The density-dependent term in the Skyrme force is motivated by effective forces between nucleons in nuclear environment, with the t3 term providing appropriate saturation.
- Properties of nuclear matter even at densities below 3 n0 (where n0 is nuclear saturation density) offer a sensitive test of Skyrme parameterizations.

## Context

This review extends earlier work on Skyrme interactions by Skyrme (1956, 1959) and the revival by Vautherin and Brink (1970, 1972), and compares SHF with its relativistic cousin, the relativistic mean-field model, and with ab initio approaches such as Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) and Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) models. The work enables future development of more consistent Skyrme parameter sets that simultaneously satisfy extended constraints from finite nuclei, neutron stars, and supernovae, while identifying the major challenge of establishing more reliable extrapolations to the drip-line regions needed for astrophysical applications like understanding the r-process.
