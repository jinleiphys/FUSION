# Dissociation of a Heavy Quarkonium at T<T_c

> We examine three different ways a heavy quarkonium can dissociate at temperatures below the quark-gluon plasma phase transition temperature T_c: spontaneous dissociation, dissociation by thermalization, and dissociation by collision with hadrons. We evaluate the cross sections for the dissociation of J/psi and Upsilon in collision with pion as a function of temperature, using the quark-interchange model of Barnes and Swanson. We also evaluate the dissociation temperatures for various quarkonia, and the fraction of quarkonium lying above the dissociation threshold as a function of temperature, using an interquark potential inferred from lattice gauge calculations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that heavy quarkonium can dissociate below the quark-gluon plasma phase transition temperature \(T_c\) via three distinct mechanisms, but the quantitative results depend on the specific interquark potential and selection rules used; the dissociation temperatures for charmonia are substantially altered when angular-momentum selection rules are invoked, whereas bottomia are less affected.

## Method

The authors used the quark-interchange model of Barnes and Swanson to calculate dissociation cross sections for \(J/\psi\), \(\chi_{c2}\), and \(\Upsilon\) in collision with \(\pi\), \(K\), and \(\rho\) at \(T=0\), employing a nonrelativistic scattering amplitude with relativistic kinematics and taking the mean of the `post' and `prior' formalisms. For finite temperatures, they solved the Schrodinger equation with a temperature-dependent Yukawa-plus-exponential potential inferred from lattice gauge calculations by Karsch et al. (2000), including spin-spin, spin-orbit, and tensor interactions, to obtain quarkonium single-particle energies and dissociation temperatures. They then computed the fraction of quarkonium lying above the dissociation threshold as a function of temperature and evaluated the temperature-dependent dissociation cross sections for \(\pi+J/\psi\) and \(\pi+\Upsilon\) using the same quark-interchange model with temperature-modified wavefunctions and interactions.

## Key numbers

- Dissociation cross section maxima for \(\pi+J/\psi\), \(K+J/\psi\), and \(\pi+\chi_{c2}\) at \(T=0\) are about 1-2 mb.
- Dissociation cross sections for exothermic reactions \(\rho+J/\psi\), \(K+\chi_{c2}\), and \(\rho+\chi_{c2}\) at \(T=0\) decrease from about 6 mb to about 0-2 mb as kinetic energy decreases from 0.05 GeV to 0.2 GeV.
- Maxima of dissociation cross sections for \(\pi\), \(K\), and \(\rho\) on \(\Upsilon\) at \(T=0\) are about 0.1 mb.
- Charmonium dissociation temperatures in units of \(T_c\) (with selection rules): \(\psi'\) at 0.50, \(\chi_{c2}\) at 0.91, \(\chi_{c1}\) at 0.90, \(J/\psi\) at 0.99.
- Bottomium dissociation temperatures in units of \(T_c\) (with selection rules): \(\Upsilon''\) at 0.57, \(\chi_{b2}'\) at 0.82, \(\chi_{b1}'\) at 0.82, \(\Upsilon'\) at 0.96, \(\chi_{b2}\), \(\chi_{b1}\), and \(\Upsilon\) all above 1.0.
- The quantity \(\langle \sigma v \rangle\) for \(\pi+J/\psi\) is about 2 mb at \(T/T_c=0.70\) and rises to about 3 mb at \(T/T_c=0.95\), with \(T_c\) taken as 0.175 GeV.
- Estimated survival probability for heavy quarkonium in central Pb-Pb collisions at 158A GeV is \(S\sim 0.5\); for central Au-Au collisions at RHIC with \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200\) GeV, \(S\sim 0.1\).

## Context

This work extends the study of heavy quarkonium dissociation below \(T_c\) by examining three mechanisms simultaneously, building on the quark-interchange model previously applied to light-quark systems by Barnes and Swanson (1992) and Swanson (1992). It confirms general features of the results of Digal, Petreczky, and Satz (2001) but finds differences in dissociation temperatures due to the choice of potential and the inclusion of angular-momentum selection rules. The temperature-dependent potential is taken from lattice gauge calculations by Karsch et al. (2000). The results enable more realistic estimates of \(J/\psi\) and \(\Upsilon\) survival probabilities in heavy-ion collisions at SPS and RHIC energies, and motivate further microscopic investigations of collisional dissociation in hot hadron gases.
