# Isoscaling in statistical fragment emission in an extended compound nucleus model

> Based on an extended compound nucleus model, isospin effects in statistical fragment emission from excited nuclear systems are investigated. An experimentally observed scaling behavior of the ratio of isotope yields $Y_i(N,Z)$ from two similar emitting sources with different neutron-to-proton ratios is predicted theoretically, i.e., the relationship of $Y_2/Y_1 \propto exp(αN + βZ)$ is demonstrated. The symmetry energy coefficient $C_{sym}$ extracted from the simulation results is $\sim$ 27 MeV which is consistent with realistic theoretical estimates and recent experimental data. The influence of the surface entropy on the isoscaling behavior is discussed in detail. It is found that although the surface entropy increases the numercial values of isoscaling parameters $α$ and $β$, it does not affect the isoscaling behavior qualitatively and has only a minor effect on the extracted symmetry energy coefficient.

# Isoscaling in statistical fragment emission in an extended compound nucleus model

## Key claim

The extended compound nucleus (ECN) model predicts the experimentally observed isoscaling behavior (exponential scaling of isotope yield ratios with neutron number N and proton number Z) for statistical fragment emission from equilibrated nuclear systems, and the symmetry energy coefficient extracted from the simulation is approximately 27 MeV. The surface entropy increases the numerical values of the isoscaling parameters α and β but does not qualitatively affect the isoscaling behavior and has only a minor effect on the extracted symmetry energy coefficient.

## Method

The authors used the Weisskopf formalism to compute fragment emission probabilities from equilibrated compound nuclei, calculating entropies for the equilibrium and saddle-point (touching spheres) configurations within the Fermi gas model. The level density parameter included both volume and surface contributions using the parametrization of Toke and Swiatecki, with volume coefficient α_V = 1/14.6 MeV⁻¹ and surface coefficient α_S = 4/14.6 MeV⁻¹. Calculations were performed for pairs of compound nucleus sources with proton number Z_s = 75 and mass numbers A_s = 165, 175, 185, and 195 at excitation energies from 2 to 7 MeV/nucleon, without accounting for nuclear expansion or sequential decay.

## Key numbers

- Symmetry energy coefficient extracted from the model without surface entropy: C_sym = 27.3 ± 0.1 MeV, consistent with the standard liquid drop model value of 25 MeV and with the experimental value of (27.2 ± 2.2) MeV from 25-MeV/nucleon ⁸⁶Kr + ¹²⁴,¹¹²Sn reactions (Sou, 2004)
- Symmetry energy coefficient extracted including surface entropy effects: C_sym = 27.6 ± 0.1 MeV
- Level density parameter volume coefficient: α_V = 1/14.6 MeV⁻¹; surface coefficient: α_S = 4/14.6 MeV⁻¹
- Surface energy parameters from Moller (1995): α_s = 21.13, κ_s = 2.3; volume parameters: α_v = 15.9937, κ_v = 1.927
- Temperature differences among the four sources when including surface entropy: less than 0.1 MeV for each fixed excitation energy per nucleon

## Context

This work extends the compound nucleus model to higher excitation energies, providing a unified description of statistical emission of light particles and fragments. The ECN model is related to models used in fission studies and is equivalent to conventional compound nucleus models (such as GEMINI by Charity, 1988) at low excitation energy but extends the compound nucleus concept toward high excitations. The isoscaling analysis builds on experimental observations by Tsang (2001, 2002, 2003) and others, and the results are consistent with theoretical estimates from the antisymmetrical molecular dynamics model (Ono, 1999), the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck model (Liu, 2001), the lattice gas model (Ma, 2001; Das, 2001), the expanding evaporating source model (Friedman, 2002), and statistical multifragmentation models (Tsang, 2002, 2003; Botvina, 2002; Souza, 2004). The model enables future studies of nuclear expansion effects on isoscaling parameters and the density dependence of the symmetry energy, as explored recently by Shetty (2007).
