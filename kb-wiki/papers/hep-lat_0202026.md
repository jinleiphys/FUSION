# Finite Density Algorithm in Lattice QCD -- a Canonical Ensemble Approach

> I will review the finite density algorithm for lattice QCD based on finite chemical potential and summarize the associated difficulties. I will propose a canonical ensemble approach which projects out the finite baryon number sector from the fermion determinant. For this algorithm to work, it requires an efficient method for calculating the fermion determinant and a Monte Carlo algorithm which accommodates unbiased estimate of the probability. I shall report on the progress made along this direction with the Padé - Z$_2$ estimator of the determinant and its implementation in the newly developed Noisy Monte Carlo algorithm.

# Finite Density Algorithm in Lattice QCD -- a Canonical Ensemble Approach

## Key claim

The canonical ensemble approach, which projects out a definite nonzero baryon number sector from the fermion determinant via Fourier transformation and performs the projection before the Monte Carlo accept/reject step, can overcome the overlap problem that plagues grand canonical reweighting methods at zero temperature; however, the approach may still suffer from a sign problem at the thermodynamic limit when baryon number N and volume V are very large, though it should work for small N such as 3 or 6 for one or two baryons in a finite volume.

## Method

The author proposes projecting out the finite baryon number sector from the fermion determinant by assigning a U(1) phase factor e^{-iφ} to time-direction links and performing a Fourier transform P_N = (1/2π) ∫₀²π dφ e^{-iφN} det M[φ], combined with a triality projection to restrict to baryon number multiples of 3. The algorithm requires two key components: the Padé-Z₂ method for stochastic estimation of the fermion determinant, which uses a Padé approximant of the logarithm and complex Z₂ noise vectors to estimate Tr log M, and the Noisy Monte Carlo algorithm, which uses two Metropolis accept/reject steps (one for updating gauge variables with fixed noise, one for refreshing noise with fixed gauge variables) to avoid probability-bound violations while maintaining detailed balance.

## Key numbers

- For an 8³ × 12 lattice with Wilson action at β = 6.0 and κ = 0.150, the unimproved Padé-Z₂ estimate of Tr log M₁ with 400 Z₂ noise vectors gave δ₀ = 5.54, while the 11th-order improved estimate reduced the error to δ₁₁ = 0.15, a factor of 37 improvement.
- The 11th-order Padé approximant with expansion point z₀ = 0.1 gave consistent results for log det M₁ (812.33 ± 0.62 with 100 Z₂ noises) compared to the 11th-order Padé with z₀ = 1.0 (812.37 ± 0.62), while 5th-order Padé gave different answers (473 ± 10 for z₀ = 0.1 versus 798 ± 10 for z₀ = 1.0), indicating insufficient accuracy.
- The improved estimate of Tr log M₁ with 50 Z₂ noises and 11th-order subtraction gave 813.54 ± 0.38, consistent with the unimproved estimate using 10,000 Z₂ noises (812.81 ± 1.08).
- For the 8³ × 12 lattice, the fermion matrix dimension N = 73728.

## Context

This work extends the fugacity expansion approach of the Glasgow group (Barbour et al., 1998) and the imaginary chemical potential method (de Forcrand et al., 1990; Wiese, 1987; Akiyama et al., 2000) by proposing a canonical ensemble approach that projects out baryon sectors before Monte Carlo updating, analogous to the variation-after-projection method in nuclear many-body theory (Zeh, 1965; Rouhaninejad and Yoccoz, 1966) which is superior to the variation-before-projection method (Peierls and Yoccoz, 1957). The multi-parameter reweighting approach of Fodor and Katz (2001) works near the finite temperature transition line but is not clearly extendable to zero temperature. The Noisy Monte Carlo algorithm builds on the work of Kennedy and Kuti (1985) by using two Metropolis steps to avoid probability-bound violations. The Padé-Z₂ estimator improves upon the Z₂ noise method of Dong and Liu (1994) and the multiple-mass solver of Glassner et al. (1996). The implementation of the Kentucky Noisy Monte Carlo algorithm with dynamical fermions (Joo et al., 2002) gives correct results compared to standard Hybrid Monte Carlo but currently suffers from low acceptance rates and long autocorrelations.
