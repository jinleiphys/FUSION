# Translating neutron star observations to nuclear symmetry energy via artificial neural networks

> One of the most significant challenges involved in efforts to understand the equation of state of dense neutron-rich matter is the uncertain density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy. Because of its broad impact, pinning down the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy has been a longstanding goal of both nuclear physics and astrophysics. Recent observations of neutron stars, in both electromagnetic and gravitational-wave spectra, have already constrained significantly the nuclear symmetry energy at high densities. Training deep neural networks to learn a computationally efficient representation of the mapping between astrophysical observables of neutron stars, such as masses, radii, and tidal deformabilities, and the nuclear symmetry energy allows its density dependence to be determined reliably and accurately. In this work we use a deep learning approach to determine the nuclear symmetry energy as a function of density directly from observational neutron star data. We show for the first time that artificial neural networks can precisely reconstruct the nuclear symmetry energy from a set of available neutron star observables, such as, masses and radii as those measured by, e.g., the NICER mission, or masses and tidal deformabilities as measured by the LIGO/VIRGO/KAGRA gravitational-wave detectors. These results demonstrate the potential of artificial neural networks to reconstruct the symmetry energy, and the equation of state, directly from neutron star observational data, and emphasize the importance of the deep learning approach in the era of Multi-Messenger Astrophysics.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates for the first time that artificial neural networks can precisely reconstruct the nuclear symmetry energy as a function of density directly from neutron star observational data, such as mass-radius or mass-tidal deformability pairs, but this is presented as a proof-of-concept study that does not yet incorporate observational errors and uncertainties.

## Method

The author adopts an equation of state metamodel parameterized by Taylor expansions around saturation density, fixing the symmetric nuclear matter component and varying only the symmetry energy parameters L and Ksym within ranges of [30.6-86.8] MeV and [-400-100] MeV respectively. For each generated equation of state, the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations are solved to produce mass-radius and mass-tidal deformability sequences, and deep neural networks are trained in a supervised regression framework to map 50 randomly selected points from these sequences (in the 1-2 solar mass range) to the corresponding symmetry energy at 100 density points between approximately 0.5 and 5 times nuclear saturation density.

## Key numbers

- The slope parameter L of the symmetry energy is sampled from the range [30.6-86.8] MeV
- The curvature parameter Ksym of the symmetry energy is sampled from the range [-400-100] MeV
- The symmetry energy at saturation density S0 is fixed at 31.7 MeV
- The symmetric nuclear matter binding energy E0 is fixed at 15.9 MeV
- The symmetric nuclear matter incompressibility K0 is fixed at 240 MeV
- The maximum neutron star mass constraint is set at 2.14 solar masses, based on the most massive pulsar observed to date
- Input sequences consist of 50 randomly selected points from mass-radius or mass-tidal deformability curves in the range of 1-2 solar masses
- Output symmetry energy vectors have dimension 100, covering the density range of approximately 0.5 to 5 times nuclear saturation density
- Training data sets consist of 40,000 independent sequences, with validation and testing sets of approximately 1000 samples each

## Context

This work extends previous efforts to extract the dense matter equation of state from neutron star observations using Bayesian inference (Ozel 2010, Steiner 2010, Raithel 2016) and Gaussian process approaches (Essick 2020), which the author notes are limited by uncertainties in assumed prior distributions. The deep learning approach builds on the author's prior work applying convolutional neural networks to gravitational wave detection (Krastev 2020, Krastev 2021) and on recent studies exploring neural networks for equation of state extraction (Fujimoto 2021, Ferreira 2019, Morawski 2020, Traversi 2020). The results enable future application to realistic multi-messenger astrophysical data from the NICER mission and LIGO/VIRGO/KAGRA gravitational-wave detectors, with the next step being incorporation of observational errors and uncertainties.
