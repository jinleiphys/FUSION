# Neutron Skins: Weak Elastic Scattering and Neutron Stars

> The recently completed PREX-2 campaign - which measured the weak form factor of lead at an optimal momentum transfer - has confirmed that the neutron skin of lead is relatively large and has provided a precise determination of the interior baryon density of a heavy nucleus. In turn, the measured form factor can be related to various nuclear and neutron-star properties. Astrophysical observations by the NICER mission have benefited from improvements in flux, energy resolution, and notably, timing resolution. NICER has the capability to measure pulse profile data, which enables simultaneous mass-radius determinations. During the next decade, measurements in astrophysics, gravitational wave astronomy, and nuclear physics are expected to provide a wealth of more precise data. In this review we provide an overview of the current state of neutron skin measurements and offer insights into the prospects for the future.

## Key claim

The PREX-2 campaign has confirmed that the neutron skin of lead is relatively large, but the extracted neutron skin thickness carries both an experimental uncertainty and a small model (theoretical) uncertainty due to the surface thickness of 208Pb; the cleanest extracted observable, the weak form factor at the average momentum transfer, is quoted as model independent.

## Method

The authors review how parity-violating electron scattering (PVES) experiments PREX-2 and CREX measure the parity-violating asymmetry APV, from which the weak form factor is extracted after accounting for Coulomb distortions via solutions of the Dirac equation with vector and axial-vector potentials. The weak form factor is then related to the neutron skin thickness by solving mean-field equations for doubly-magic nuclei and incorporating single-nucleon electromagnetic and weak form factors. For future measurements, the Mainz Radius EXperiment (MREX) will use the P2 spectrometer at the MESA facility to measure elastic scattering off 208Pb with full azimuthal coverage from fused-silica Cherenkov detectors.

## Key numbers

* Neutron skin thickness of 208Pb: Rn - Rp = 0.278 ± 0.078 (exp) ± 0.012 (theo) fm, from the PREX-2 campaign.
* MREX aims to determine the neutron skin thickness of 208Pb to better than 0.03 fm, about a factor of two improvement over PREX-2 precision.
* MESA will provide a low-energy electron beam with Emax = 155 MeV, intensity I = 150 μA, and polarization P ≥ 85%.
* Preliminary rate estimates suggest a ±0.03 fm determination of the neutron skin thickness is feasible within 1500 hours of running time, assuming a 1% systematic uncertainty in the parity-violating asymmetry.
* The current model uncertainty in the neutron skin determination is 25%, and an independent 10% measurement of the surface thickness would reduce this contribution below the error budget of the APV measurement.
* NICER observations of pulsars PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620 provide simultaneous mass-radius determinations; DINO model predictions of radii near 15 km are inconsistent at the 68% level with NICER data for the low-mass star.
* Third-generation gravitational wave observatories aim to determine stellar radii with a precision of 100 meters.

## Context

This review extends the line of work from the PREX-2 and CREX campaigns (Adhikari 2021, 2022) that measured weak form factors of 208Pb and 48Ca, and connects these laboratory measurements to neutron-star properties constrained by NICER observations (Miller 2019, Riley 2019, Miller 2021, Riley 2021) and gravitational-wave detections such as GW170817 (Abbott 2017). The paper surveys future measurements including MREX at the MESA facility (Becker 2018), which will improve precision on the neutron skin of 208Pb, and next-generation X-ray missions like Athena (Nandra 2013) and third-generation gravitational-wave observatories (Cosmic Explorer, Einstein Telescope) that will further constrain the equation of state of neutron-rich matter.
