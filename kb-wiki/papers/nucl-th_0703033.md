# Large-scale prediction of the parity distribution in the nuclear level density and application to astrophysical reaction rates

> A generalized method to calculate the excitation-energy dependent parity ratio in the nuclear level density is presented, using the assumption of Poisson distributed independent quasi particles combined with BCS occupation numbers. It is found that it is crucial to employ a sufficiently large model space to allow excitations both from low-lying shells and to higher shells beyond a single major shell. Parity ratios are only found to equilibrate above at least 5-10 MeV of excitation energy. Furthermore, an overshooting effect close to major shells is found where the parity opposite to the ground state parity may dominate across a range of several MeV before the parity ratio finally equilibrates. The method is suited for large-scale calculations as needed, for example, in astrophysical applications. Parity distributions were computed for all nuclei from the proton dripline to the neutron dripline and from Ne up to Bi. These results were then used to recalculate astrophysical reaction rates in a Hauser-Feshbach statistical model. Although certain transitions can be considerably enhanced or suppressed, the impact on astrophysically relevant reactions remains limited, mainly due to the thermal population of target states in stellar reaction rates.

## Key claim

The parity distribution in nuclear level densities only equilibrates above excitation energies of at least 5-10 MeV, and an overshooting effect near closed shells can cause the parity opposite to the ground state parity to dominate over a range of several MeV before equilibration; however, despite these strong effects on individual transitions, the overall impact on astrophysical reaction rates remains limited due to thermal population of target states in stellar environments.

## Method

The authors developed a generalized method based on Poisson-distributed independent quasiparticles combined with BCS occupation numbers to compute the excitation-energy-dependent parity ratio in the nuclear level density. Single-particle levels were calculated in a deformed Saxon-Woods potential including all major shells up to 11 ℏω, and the pairing gap was extrapolated to higher nuclear temperatures using a Fermi-type fit to avoid discontinuities from the BCS-to-Fermi-Dirac phase transition. The parity-projected level densities were then used as input to recalculate astrophysical reaction rates within the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model for nuclei from Ne to Bi across the entire proton-to-neutron dripline range.

## Key numbers

- Parity ratios equilibrate only above at least 5-10 MeV of excitation energy, with some nuclei requiring even higher energies.
- The overshooting effect at closed shells can reach 20-60% above the equilibrated value of ρ_s/ρ_g = 1 before finally reaching equilibrium at higher excitation energies.
- For ⁵⁶Fe, inclusion of the 2d₅/₂ orbital to the pf+g₉/₂ shell leads to deviations over 10% in the parity ratio above excitation energies of about 15 MeV.
- For ⁵⁶Fe, accounting for excitations across the full sd+pf+gds shells produces deviations of at least 10% already at E_x ≃ 7 MeV.
- The iteration procedure for achieving consistency between single-particle structure and input level density typically converged by the third iteration step.
- The model space included all major shells up to 11 ℏω oscillator shells.

## Context

This work extends the quasiparticle model proposed by Alhassid et al. (2000) by allowing odd particle numbers, including all shells up to the 11 ℏω oscillator shell, and permitting excitations between all considered shells. It provides a macroscopic-microscopic alternative to time-consuming shell model Monte Carlo calculations (e.g., Langanke et al. 2006, Alhassid et al. 2003) that are difficult to apply to the thousands of nuclei required in astrophysical nucleosynthesis studies. The parity-dependent level densities can be combined with any total level density approach, such as the shifted Fermi-gas model of Rauscher et al. (1997), and the results enable more accurate Hauser-Feshbach calculations of stellar reaction rates for explosive burning processes involving unstable nuclei.
