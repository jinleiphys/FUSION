# Renormalization group equations as 'decoupling' theorems

> We propose a simple derivation of renormalization group equations and Callan-Symanzik equations as decoupling theorems of the structures underlying effective field theories.

## Key claim

Renormalization group equations (RGE) and Callan-Symanzik equations (CSE) can be derived as "decoupling theorems" that express how the canonical scaling contributions from underlying structures (theories at higher or lower energy scales) become anomalous scaling contributions within an effective field theory (EFT), provided one assumes the existence of a well-defined underlying theory from which the EFT is projected.

## Method

The author postulates a projection operator \(\breve{P}_{\texttt{\tiny EFT}}\) that represents the reconstruction of an EFT from a complete underlying theory (UT) via subspace projection, averaging over underlying dynamics, and taking decoupling limits. Starting from the canonical scaling law of a vertex function in the UT (which includes the underlying parameters \(\{\sigma\}\)), the author applies this projection to obtain the EFT scaling law, where the underlying parameters are replaced by agent constants \(\{\bar{c}\}\). The operator \(\sum d_{\bar{c}} \bar{c} \partial_{\bar{c}}\) is then expanded into insertions of EFT operators (kinetic, coupling, and composite) with coefficients identified as anomalous dimensions, yielding the familiar forms of RGE and CSE.

## Key numbers

* The canonical mass dimension of any parameter is denoted \(d_{\cdots}\), and the vertex function scales as \(\Gamma^{(n)}([\lambda p],[\lambda^{d_g} g]; \{ \lambda^{d_{\sigma}} \sigma\}) = \lambda^{d_{\Gamma^{(n)}}} \Gamma^{(n)} ([p],[g];\{\sigma\})\) (Eq. 1).
* The EFT scaling law becomes \(\{\lambda \partial_\lambda + \sum_{O_N} \delta_{O_N} \hat{I}_{O_N} + \sum_g (d_g+\delta_g) g \partial_g + \sum_\phi \delta_\phi \hat{I}_\phi - d_{\Gamma^{(n)}}\} \Gamma^{(n)}([\lambda p],[g];\{\bar{c}\}) = 0\) (Eq. 11).
* For renormalizable theories (no composite operator anomalies), the RGE takes the form \(\{\bar{\mu}\partial_{\bar \mu} - \sum_g \bar{\delta}_g g\partial_g - \sum_\phi n_\phi \frac{\delta_\phi}{2} - \sum_\psi n_\psi \frac{\delta_\psi}{2} \} \Gamma^{(n_\phi,n_\psi)} ([p],[g];\{\bar{\mu};(\bar{c}^i_0)\}) = 0\) (Eq. 18).
* The trace anomaly is given by \(g_{\mu \nu} \Theta^{\mu \nu} = -\sum_g \delta_g g \partial_g \mathcal{L}_{\texttt{\tiny EFT}} + \sum_\phi \delta_\phi \hat{O}_{kinetic}(\phi)\) (Eq. 26).

## Context

This work extends the conceptual understanding of RGE and CSE by deriving them from a postulated underlying theory, rather than from regularization-dependent renormalization procedures. It connects to the BPHZ renormalization program (where the \(\mathcal{R}\) operation is identified as a realization of the projection \(\breve{P}_{\texttt{\tiny EFT}}\)) and to the Appelquist-Carazzone decoupling theorem (1975), which is reinterpreted as a repartitioning of parameters between the EFT and underlying sets when a heavy field is integrated out. The perspective enables a unified treatment of UV, IR, and other divergences within EFT, and suggests future work on non-UV singularities (e.g., nonperturbative QCD dynamics) and factorization approaches.
