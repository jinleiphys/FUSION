# Hadron-nucleon Total Cross Section Fluctuations from Hadron-nucleus Total Cross Sections

> The extent to which information about fluctuations in hadron-nucleon total cross sections in the frozen approximation can be extracted from very high energy hadron-nucleus total cross section measurements for a range of heavy nuclei is discussed. The corrections to the predictions of Glauber theory due to these fluctuations are calculated for several models for the distribution functions, and differences of the order of 50 mb are found for heavy nuclei. The generating function for the moments of the hadron-nucleon cross section distributions can be approximately determined from the derivatives of the hadron-nucleus total cross sections with respect to the nuclear geometric cross section. The argument of the generating function, however, it limited to the maximum value of a dimensionless thickness function obtained at zero impact parameter for the heaviest nuclear targets: about 1.8 for pions and 3.0 for nucleons.

# Hadron-nucleon Total Cross Section Fluctuations from Hadron-nucleus Total Cross Sections

## Key claim
Information about fluctuations in hadron-nucleon total cross sections can be extracted from very high energy hadron-nucleus total cross section measurements for a range of heavy nuclei, but only up to a limited argument of the generating function determined by the maximum dimensionless thickness function for stable nuclei: about 1.8 for pions and 3.0 for nucleons. The generating function for the moments of the hadron-nucleon cross section distribution can be approximately determined from derivatives of hadron-nucleus total cross sections with respect to nuclear geometric cross section, but surface corrections to this relation require further investigation.

## Method
The author applies the frozen approximation and Glauber theory to express hadron-nucleus total cross sections in terms of the generating function of the reduced cross section distribution function, with the argument being a reduced nuclear thickness function t(A,b). Corrections to standard Glauber predictions due to cross section fluctuations are calculated for several model distribution functions (including Poisson-like distributions and those from Blattel et al. (1993)), using both uniform nuclear density and Woods-Saxon nuclear densities with surface thickness parameter a0 = 0.523 fm. The derivative of the dispersive contribution to the total cross section with respect to twice the nuclear geometric cross section is compared to the generating function evaluated at the maximum thickness function for each nucleus.

## Key numbers
- Differences between model predictions for the dispersive contribution to hadron-nucleus total cross sections are of the order of 50 mb for heavy nuclei.
- The maximum dimensionless thickness function t_max for the largest stable nuclei (A=238) is approximately 3.0 for incident nucleons and 1.8 for incident pions.
- For pions on nucleons, the average cross section is approximately 24 mb; for nucleons on nucleons, approximately 40 mb.
- The second moment ratio for nucleons is approximately 1.25 times the square of the average cross section; for pions, approximately 1.4 to 1.5 times the square of the average cross section.
- The nuclear radius parameter r0 is approximately 1.1 fm; the Woods-Saxon surface thickness parameter a0 is approximately 0.523 fm.
- Experimental errors in Ref. [6] (Murthy et al., 1975) are of the same order as the 50 mb differences between models, indicating the need for accuracy better than 10 mb on cross sections exceeding 3000 mb.

## Context
This work extends the cross section distribution function approach of Good and Walker (1960) and the more recent analyses by Blattel, Baym, Frankfurt, Heiselberg, and Strikman (1993), who estimated second and third moments of the distribution for pions and nucleons. The paper addresses whether hadron-nucleus total cross sections can provide additional information beyond what is known from forward diffraction dissociation on nucleons and deuterons. The approach is closely related to the importance of inelastic intermediate states in hadron-nucleus scattering discussed by Jennings and Miller (1995) and confirmed experimentally by Murthy et al. (1975). The results suggest that accurate measurements of total cross sections for hadrons on a range of heavy nuclei at very high energies could discriminate among different cross section distribution function models, but require experimental precision of perhaps less than 10 mb on cross sections exceeding 3000 mb, and further theoretical work on surface corrections to the derivative relation.
