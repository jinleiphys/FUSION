# Transport equations for driven many-body systems

> Transport equations for autonomous driven Fermionic quantum systems are derived with the help of statistical assumptions and of the Markov approximation. The statistical assumptions hold if the system consists of subsystems within which equilibration is sufficiently fast. The Markov approximation holds if the level density in each subsystem is sufficiently smooth in energy. The transport equation describes both, relaxation of occupation probability among subsystems at equal energy that leads to thermalization, and the transport of the system to higher energy caused by the driving force. The laser-nucleus interaction serves as an example for the applicability and flexibility of the approach.

## Key claim

Transport equations for autonomous driven fermionic quantum systems can be justified using statistical assumptions on coupling matrix elements between constituent parts of the system, which take the role of a heat bath, provided that equilibration within each subsystem is sufficiently fast and the level density in each subsystem is sufficiently smooth in energy.

## Method

The author starts from a Hamiltonian consisting of a single-particle part, a two-body residual interaction, and a time-dependent dipole driving force, and assumes that within each class of Slater determinants (defined by spin, parity, particle-hole number, and excitation energy) the eigenstates behave like members of the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) of random matrices. Using the Markov approximation, which neglects energy derivatives of the average level density, the time-dependent Schrödinger equation is averaged over the Gaussian distribution of matrix elements to derive transport equations for mean occupation probabilities. The derivation treats the residual interaction and the dipole interaction separately, with the dipole interaction connecting states differing in energy by the photon energy.

## Key numbers

- The spreading width of the Giant Dipole Resonance (GDR) is given as Γ^↓ ≈ 5 MeV, describing the damping of the GDR due to mixing with eigenstates of the nuclear Hamiltonian.
- The laser photon energy is ℏω₀ ≈ 10 MeV for the laser-nucleus interaction example.
- The condition for the Markov approximation is that the energy interval over which the average level density ρ_α changes significantly is large compared to ℏ times the inverse characteristic time t for relaxation or dipole absorption, expressed as (ℏ^n / t^n) (∂^n ρ_α / ∂E^n) ≪ ρ_α for n = 1, 2, ...
- The spreading width for states in class α at energy E is defined as Γ^↓_{Eα} = 2π Σ_β V²_{αβ} ρ_β(E), where V²_{αβ} is the mean square matrix element of the residual interaction and ρ_β(E) is the average level density.
- The rates for probability transport between classes are R_{α→β} = (2π/ℏ) V²_{αβ} ρ_β and R_{β→α} = (2π/ℏ) V²_{βα} ρ_α, related by detailed balance ρ_α R_{α→β} = ρ_β R_{β→α}.

## Context

This work provides the theoretical justification for transport equations previously used without firm foundation in studies of laser-nucleus interaction by Palffy et al. (2014, 2015) and Kobyakov (2020). The approach extends the statistical treatment of time-independent compound-nucleus scattering cross sections from Agassi et al. (1975) to time-dependent driven systems. The derived transport equations enable theoretical description of multiple dipole excitation sequences in nuclei hit by intense laser pulses (being developed at the Extreme Light Infrastructure and the Gamma Factory at CERN) and in fermionic atoms in oscillating traps, where the enormous level densities make direct solution of the Schrödinger equation impossible.
