# Simulation of symmetric nuclei and the role of Pauli potential in binding energies and radii

> It is shown that the use of a density dependent effective Pauli potential together with a nucleon-nucleon interaction potential plays a crucial role to reproduce not only the binding energies but also the matter root mean square radii of medium mass range spin-isospin saturated nuclei. This study is performed with a semiclassical Monte Carlo many-body simulation within the context of a simplified nucleon-nucleon interaction to focus on the effect of the genuine correlations due to the fermionic nature of nucleons. The procedure obtained is rather robust and it does not depend on the detailed features of the nucleon-nucleon interaction. For nuclei below saturation the density dependence may be represented in terms either of the nucleon number, $A$, or the associated Fermi momenta. When testing the simulation procedure for idealized "infinite" symmetric nuclear matter within the corresponding range of densities, it turns out that finite size effects affect the Pauli potential strength parametrization in systems up to about 120 particles while remaining approximately stable for larger systems.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a density-dependent effective Pauli potential, rather than a constant one, is crucial for reproducing both the binding energies and matter root mean square radii of medium-mass spin-isospin saturated nuclei in a semiclassical Monte Carlo simulation; the abstract's claim of robustness is qualified by the finding that finite-size effects affect the Pauli potential strength parametrization in systems up to about 120 particles.

## Method

The authors performed semiclassical Monte Carlo simulations of symmetric nuclei (Z=N, A=8 to 44) using a simplified square-well nucleon-nucleon (NN) interaction with a repulsive core and an attractive well, plus a Gaussian-form Pauli potential that depends on relative position and momentum. They first used a density-independent Pauli potential fixed to reproduce the binding energy and radius of 16O, then introduced a density-dependent Pauli potential where the strength and range parameters were expressed as functions of the Fermi momentum extracted from quasielastic electron scattering data. The simulations were run at a low temperature of 1 MeV using the Metropolis algorithm, and the statistical averages of binding energies and root mean square radii were calculated after thermalization.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy per nucleon of 16O used as calibration: 7.98 MeV (from nuclear data).
- Matter root mean square radius of 16O used as calibration: 2.72 fm.
- Pauli potential parameters for the density-independent case (SWCPo model): V_P = 41 MeV, q_0 = 1.88 fm, p_0 = 104.96 MeV/c.
- Square-well NN potential parameters: repulsive core strength V_Core = 100 MeV, attractive well depth V_0 = 3 MeV, core width a = 2 fm, well width b = 2 fm.
- Statistical uncertainty of calculated binding energies: at most 5%.
- For 16O, Woods-Saxon fit parameters: central density beta = 0.066 ± 0.002 fm^{-3}, half-density radius alpha = 4.0 ± 0.5 fm, diffuseness gamma = 0.41 ± 0.45 fm.
- For 20Ne, Woods-Saxon fit parameters: beta = 0.068 ± 0.007 fm^{-3}, alpha = 3.97 ± 0.20 fm, gamma = 0.1 ± 0.5 fm.
- Liquid drop model radius parameter r_0 ≈ 1.1 fm.
- For infinite symmetric nuclear matter (SNM) at T=0: incompressibility K ≈ 230 MeV, saturation density ρ_0 = 0.165 fm^{-3}.
- Density range for the studied nuclei: lower bound ρ/ρ_0 = 0.51, upper bound ρ/ρ_0 = 0.8.
- Finite-size effects in SNM simulations stabilize for particle number A ≈ 120.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Perez-Garcia et al., 2007) by introducing a refined spin-dependent NN interaction and evaluating matter radii and density distributions, going beyond binding energies alone. The approach contrasts with fully quantum methods like fermionic molecular dynamics (FMD) and antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD), which are computationally heavy for heavy nuclei, and with Skyrme-type models that use local density dependence. The density-dependent Pauli potential parametrized by Fermi momentum shows similarities to the vector mean field in relativistic mean field models (QHD) and to approaches in chiral dynamics (Chiral). The results enable a practical semiclassical framework for modeling symmetric nuclei that can be extended to study asymmetric systems or hyperonic content.
