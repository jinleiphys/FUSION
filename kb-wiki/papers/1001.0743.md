# Microscopic evaluation of the pairing gap

> We discuss the relevant progress that has been made in the last few years on the microscopic theory of the pairing correlation in nuclei and the open problems that still must be solved in order to reach a satisfactory description and understanding of the nuclear pairing. The similarities and differences with the nuclear matter case are emphasized and described by few illustrative examples. The comparison of calculations of different groups on the same set of nuclei show, besides agreements, also discrepancies that remain to be clarified. The role of the many-body correlations, like screening, that go beyond the BCS scheme, is still uncertain and requires further investigation.

# Microscopic evaluation of the pairing gap

## Key claim

Despite significant progress in microscopic calculations of the pairing gap in nuclei, the role of many-body correlations beyond the BCS scheme (such as screening) remains uncertain, and discrepancies between different groups' calculations on the same set of nuclei persist, indicating that a fully satisfactory ab initio description of nuclear pairing has not yet been achieved.

## Method

The authors review and compare multiple theoretical approaches to computing the pairing gap: BCS calculations with realistic NN potentials (Argonne v14, v18, Paris) renormalized via projection of high-momentum components, the Local Potential Approximation (LPA) method for finite nuclei, and inclusion of induced interactions from surface vibrations within Nuclear Field Theory and quasiparticle RPA. For the nucleus 120Sn, they solve the BCS gap equation in a model subspace using different self-consistent mean-field bases (Saxon-Woods, DF3 energy density functional, and several Skyrme forces including SKP, SKM*, and SLy4) with the separable Paris potential.

## Key numbers

- For 120Sn, the BCS gap obtained with the Saxon-Woods basis and bare mass m*=m is Δ_F = 1.65 MeV, exceeding the experimental value of approximately 1.3-1.4 MeV.
- With the SLy4 Skyrme force (effective mass m* ≈ 0.7m at normal density), the BCS gap for 120Sn is Δ_F = 1.25 MeV, below the experimental value.
- The Milano group found a direct BCS gap of Δ_dir ≈ 0.7 MeV (with Argonne v14 and SLy4 mean field) and Δ_dir = 1.04 MeV in a later calculation, with induced interactions bringing the total to Δ_F = 1.47 MeV.
- Duguet et al. obtained Δ ≈ 1.6 MeV for 120Sn using V_low-k from Argonne v18 with the SLy4 basis.
- Cooper pairs in 120Sn are concentrated at the surface with a size of 2-3 fm, and the total number of Cooper pairs is N_Cp ≈ 10.
- The effective pairing interaction at the surface can exceed the interior value by one order of magnitude.

## Context

This review extends the line of work comparing realistic NN potentials (Argonne v14/v18, Paris) with phenomenological forces like Gogny D1S (Pillet, Sandulescu, Schuck, 2007) and Skyrme forces for pairing calculations in finite nuclei. It directly addresses the contradiction between the Milano group results (Baldo et al., 2004-2008) and those of Duguet et al. (2006-2007) for the BCS gap in 120Sn, showing that the choice of effective mass in the mean field is the critical factor. The paper enables future work by identifying that the screening (induced interaction) problem remains unsolved, with different methods giving different results for the induced contribution, and by demonstrating that the LPA method provides a practical approach for renormalizing the pairing interaction in finite systems.
