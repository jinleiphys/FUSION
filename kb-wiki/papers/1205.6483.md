# Geometrical approach to the proton spin decomposition

> We discuss in detail and from the geometrical point of view the issues of gauge invariance and Lorentz covariance raised by the approach proposed recently by Chen et al. to the proton spin decomposition. We show that the gauge invariance of this approach follows from a mechanism similar to the one used in the famous Stueckelberg trick. Stressing the fact that the Lorentz symmetry does not force the gauge potential to transform as a Lorentz four-vector, we show that the Chen et al. approach is Lorentz covariant provided that one uses the suitable Lorentz transformation law. We also make an attempt to summarize the present situation concerning the proton spin decomposition. We argue that the ongoing debates concern essentially the physical interpretation and are due to the plurality of the adopted pictures. We discuss these different pictures and propose a pragmatic point of view.

## Key claim

The Chen et al. approach to proton spin decomposition is gauge invariant via a mechanism analogous to the Stueckelberg trick, and is Lorentz covariant provided one uses a suitable (non-standard) Lorentz transformation law for the gauge potential, rather than requiring it to transform as a Lorentz four-vector.

## Method

The paper provides a geometrical interpretation of the Chen et al. decomposition, showing that the separation of the gauge potential into pure-gauge and physical parts corresponds to assuming a privileged basis in the internal space, with the pure-gauge part acting as a connection. It demonstrates that this separation introduces an additional Stueckelberg gauge symmetry, and that the Lorentz transformation law of the gauge potential is not forced to be that of a four-vector but can be chosen to prevent mixing of physical and gauge degrees of freedom under Lorentz boosts.

## Key numbers

* About 1/3 of the nucleon spin comes from the quark spin, based on analyses from Ageev (2005), Alexakhin (2006), and Airapetian (2007).
* The gluon spin seems to contribute little to the nucleon spin, based on analyses from Ageev (2005), Boyle (2006), Kiryluk (2005), and Fatemi (2006).

## Context

This work addresses the theoretical controversy surrounding the Chen et al. approach (Chen, 2008; Chen, 2009), which proposed a gauge-invariant decomposition of the proton spin that reduces to the Jaffe-Manohar decomposition (Jaffe, 1989) in a non-abelian generalization of the Coulomb gauge. The paper extends the geometrical understanding of gauge theories to resolve criticisms regarding gauge invariance and Lorentz covariance raised by Tiwari (2008), Ji (2010), and Ji (2009), and it enables a pragmatic framework for interpreting different spin decompositions and accessing orbital angular momentum contributions.
