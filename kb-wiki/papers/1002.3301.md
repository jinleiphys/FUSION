# Proton Decay: Improving the sensitivity through nuclear dynamics?

> The kinematics of the decay of a bound proton is governed by the proton spectral function. We evaluate this quantity in 16O using the information from nuclear physics experiments. It also includes a correlated part. The reliability of this evaluation is sufficient to open the possibility of correlated cuts in the missing mass and momentum variables in order to identify the decay events from the bound protons with a possible increase of the signal to noise ratio.

## Key claim

The kinematics of a bound proton decaying inside an oxygen nucleus can be reliably predicted using the nuclear spectral function, which includes both uncorrelated (shell-model) and correlated (short-range correlation) components. This opens the possibility of applying correlated cuts on missing mass and missing momentum to identify decay events, potentially increasing the signal-to-noise ratio in future proton decay experiments, though the analysis must account for the loss of events where the decay pion is inelastically scattered.

## Method

The authors evaluated the proton spectral function for 16O using two complementary models: one based on a Hartree-Fock Skyrme force for the uncorrelated part combined with a factorized two-body momentum distribution for the correlated part (Ciofi degli Atti model), and another using the Local Density Approximation that combines (e,e'p) data with nuclear matter spectral function calculations (Benhar et al. model). The spectral function describes the probability to find a nucleon with given momentum and removal energy, and was used to map the distribution of decay events in the missing mass versus missing momentum plane.

## Key numbers

- The correlated part of the spectral function represents 20% of the total spectral function in 16O.
- In the first model, the integral of the correlated spectral function over momenta above 300 MeV/c gives 11% of the total.
- The probability to find a nucleon with momentum >= 300 MeV/c in 16O is 0.15 to 0.2, based on the deuteron probability of 3-4% scaled by a factor of ~5.
- Shell energies in the Skyrme model: P1/2 at 12.06 MeV (width ~5 MeV), P3/2 at 18.63 MeV (width ~5 MeV), S1/2 at 37.96 MeV (width ~40 MeV).
- Shell energies in the Benhar et al. model: P1/2 at 12.50 MeV, P3/2 at 18.75 MeV, a second P3/2 at 23.00 MeV, S1/2 at 42.50 MeV.
- The correlated spectral function follows V(P_miss) = 0.78 - 1.78 P_miss^2 (V in GeV^2, P_miss in GeV/c).
- With the cut sqrt(V) >= 800 MeV and k <= 250 MeV/c, the fraction of nuclear events retained is 0.83 (Ciofi model) and 0.80 (Benhar model).
- With a tighter cut sqrt(V) > 900 MeV, approximately 44% of events are removed: 26% (25%) from uncorrelated and 18% (19%) from correlated events in the first (second) model.
- The normalization integral without any cut on V gives N = 0.98; the correlated spectral function normalization in (P_miss, V) space is 0.18 (with 0.02 in negative V region).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Yamazaki and Akaishi (1999), who first pointed out that nuclear correlations produce a broad low-energy tail in the invariant mass spectrum of proton decay, by providing a quantitative spectral function framework tested against (e,e'p) and (p,2p) data. It enables future experiments like Super-Kamiokande to apply correlated cuts on missing mass and momentum to recover decay events that would otherwise be lost due to the broadening from short-range correlations, particularly important as tighter cuts are needed to reject atmospheric neutrino backgrounds.
