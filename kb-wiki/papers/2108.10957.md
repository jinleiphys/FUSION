# Formal Aspects of Quantum Decay

> The Fock-Krylov formalism for the calculation of survival probabilities of unstable states is revisited paying particular attention to the mathematical constraints on the density of states, the Fourier transform of which gives the survival amplitude. We show that it is not possible to construct a density of states corresponding to a purely exponential survival amplitude. he survival probability $P(t)$ and the autocorrelation function of the density of states are shown to form a pair of cosine Fourier transforms. This result is a particular case of the Wiener Khinchin theorem and forces $P(t)$ to be an even function of time which in turn forces the density of states to contain a form factor which vanishes at large energies. Subtle features of the transition regions from the non-exponential to the exponential at small times and the exponential to the power law decay at large times are discussed by expressing $P(t)$ as a function of the number of oscillations, $n$, performed by it. The transition at short times is shown to occur when the survival probability has completed one oscillation. The number of oscillations depend on the properties of the resonant state and a complete description of the evolution of the unstable state is provided by determining the limits on the number of oscillations in each region.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that it is not possible to construct a density of states whose Fourier transform yields a purely exponential survival amplitude, contradicting the possibility of a density of states that would produce the purely exponential decay commonly observed in laboratory experiments. This result is presented as a "reverse statement" of Khalfin's (1958) seminal result using the Paley-Wiener theorem, relying instead on a simple analytic continuation argument.

## Method

The authors revisit the Fock-Krylov formalism, where the survival amplitude is given as a Fourier transform of the density of states (DOS). They perform a contour integration in the complex energy plane to separate the survival amplitude into exponential and non-exponential components, showing that a purely exponential amplitude would require the DOS to vanish along the negative imaginary axis, which forces the DOS to be zero everywhere. They further derive that the survival probability and the autocorrelation function of the DOS form a pair of cosine Fourier transforms, a particular case of the Wiener-Khinchin theorem, and analyze the Taylor expansion of the survival probability around t=0 to establish its evenness.

## Key numbers

- The survival probability Taylor expansion around t=0 is given as P(t) = 1 - t^2 * <(Delta H)^2>_0 + O(t^4), where <(Delta H)^2>_0 is the variance of the Hamiltonian in the initial state.
- For a narrow isolated resonance, the approximate autocorrelation function is R(y) = 8*pi*omega_s * |R(z_s)|^2 / (y^2 + omega_s^2), where omega_s is the imaginary part of the pole and R(z_s) is the residue.
- The second derivative of the survival probability at t=0 is P''(0) = -2*<(Delta H)^2>_0, which is negative.
- The paper references experimental data from a quantum tunneling experiment (shorttexpt) where two parameter sets were found to agree with data at small times: theta = 2 with tau* = 12.55 microseconds, and theta = 3/2 with tau* = 23.15 microseconds.

## Context

This work extends the foundational results of Khalfin (1958) on the non-exponential nature of quantum decay and the Fock-Krylov formalism introduced by Fock and Krylov. The paper provides mathematical constraints on the density of states that must be satisfied for physically meaningful survival probabilities, particularly the necessity of a form factor that vanishes at large energies to ensure finite moments of the Hamiltonian. The results enable more precise determination of critical transition times between quadratic, exponential, and power-law decay regimes, and have implications for experimental searches for non-exponential decay in nuclear and particle physics systems such as those studied by the authors in their previous work (ourpapercriticaltimes).
