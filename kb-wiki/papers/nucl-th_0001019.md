# Comment on Yuan et al

> The work of Yuan et al. (Phys. Rev. C 60 (1999) 045203) is being represented as definitive regarding the non-existence of a particular nonstrange, isoscalar, spin three dibaryon which has been proposed. We comment on this paper and its relation to our work and that of others.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the work of Yuan et al. (1999) should not be considered definitive regarding the non-existence of the proposed nonstrange, isoscalar, spin-three dibaryon \(d^{*}\), because a range of models with differing physics assumptions (quark substructure, meson-exchange, or both) all reproduce known low-energy baryon-baryon scattering data and therefore the existence of the \(d^{*}\) remains an open experimental question.

## Method

The authors compare three classes of theoretical approaches to dibaryon physics: (1) constituent quark models with distorted wavefunctions (their own work and that of others), (2) purely hadronic models with no quark substructure (effective potentials or meson exchanges), and (3) intermediate models that include quark substructure but restrict interactions to meson or gluon exchanges (Yuan et al., Thomas et al., Wilets et al.). They then critique Yuan et al.'s specific objection regarding the nonrelativistic confinement potential used in their own model, explaining that their Hamiltonian is an extended effective matrix element approach rather than a simple potential model.

## Key numbers

- The proposed dibaryon is a nonstrange, isoscalar, spin-three state, denoted \(d^{*}\).
- The authors note that their own nonrelativistic model Hamiltonian is an extended effective matrix element approach, not simply a potential model.
- No specific numerical results (masses, binding energies, cross sections, or precision values) are given in the text for any dibaryon state.

## Context

This comment extends the debate over the existence of the \(d^{*}\) dibaryon, following the work of Yuan et al. (1999) which claimed to rule it out. The authors situate their own approach (Wang and Goldman, prior work) alongside those of Jaffe (for the \(H\) dibaryon), Mulders, Yazaki, and others who have proposed dibaryon states. They argue that experimental searches, such as proton-induced excitation discussed by Wong and possible electron scattering experiments, are needed to distinguish between the competing theoretical pictures, rather than relying on theoretical judgment alone.
