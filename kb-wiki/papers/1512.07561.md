# Jet formation and interference in a thin QCD medium

> In heavy-ion collisions, an abundant production of high-energy QCD jets allows to study how these multiparticle sprays are modified as they pass through the quark-gluon plasma. In order to shed new light on this process, we compute the inclusive two-gluon rate off a hard quark propagating through a color deconfined medium at first order in medium opacity. We explicitly impose an energy ordering of the two emitted gluons, such that the "hard" gluon can be thought of as belonging to the jet substructure while the other is a "soft" emission (which can be collinear or medium-induced). Our analysis focusses on two specific limits that clarify the modification of the additional angle- and formation time-ordering of splittings. In one limit, the formation time of the "hard" gluon is short compared to the "soft" gluon formation time, leading to a probabilistic formula for production of and subsequent radiation off a quark-gluon antenna. In the other limit, the ordering of formation is reverted, which automatically leads to the fact that the jet substructure is resolved by the medium. We observe in this case a characteristic delay: the jet radiates as one color current (quark) up to the formation of the "hard" gluon, at which point we observe the onset of radiation of the new color current (gluon). Our computation supports a picture in which the in-medium jet dynamics are described as a collection of subsequent antennas which are resolved by the medium according to their transverse extent.

## Key claim

The paper computes the double-inclusive gluon emission rate off a hard quark in a thin QCD medium at first order in opacity, showing that when the formation time of the "hard" gluon is longer than that of the "soft" gluon, the jet substructure is resolved by the medium and a characteristic delay appears: the jet radiates as a single color current until the hard gluon forms, after which the new color current (gluon) begins to radiate.

## Method

The authors compute the two-gluon emission rate from a hard quark interacting once with a single scattering center (first order in opacity expansion), imposing strong energy ordering \(p^+ \gg k_H^+ \gg k_S^+\). They work in light-cone gauge with eikonal Feynman rules, model the medium as a fluctuating color field with a single non-vanishing component \(A^-\), and perform all amplitude calculations and squaring symbolically using Mathematica, including 15 real diagrams and 23 virtual (double-Born) diagrams. The analysis focuses on two limits defined by the relative ordering of the formation times of the hard and soft gluons.

## Key numbers

* The energy ordering assumed is \(p^+ \gg k_H^+ \gg k_S^+\), with both gluon energies much larger than the typical medium momentum transfer \(q\) (i.e., \(k_S^+ \gg q\)).
* The medium is modeled at first order in opacity, valid when \(\alpha_s n_0 L \ll 1\) (dilute medium approximation).
* The total rate separates into two color structures: \(C_F^2 C_A \, w_Q(x^+;\mathbf{q}) + C_F C_A^2 \, w_G(x^+;\mathbf{q})\).
* In the limit where the hard gluon formation time is short compared to the soft gluon formation time (\(\tau_H \ll \tau_S\)), the rate reduces to a probabilistic formula for production of and subsequent radiation off a quark-gluon antenna.
* In the opposite limit (\(\tau_S \ll \tau_H\)), the medium resolves the jet substructure, leading to a delayed onset of gluon radiation.

## Context

This work extends the single-gluon emission rate computed by BDMPS-Z (Baier 1997, Zakharov 1997) and the opacity expansion formalism of Wiedemann (2000) and Gyulassy (2000) to the double-gluon case with energy ordering. It complements the numerical analysis of the full rate at first order in opacity by Fickinger (2013) and the dense-medium two-gluon study by Arnold (2015), which considered comparable formation times. The results support the picture of medium resolution proposed by Mehtar-Tani (2010, 2011) and Casalderrey-Solana (2011), where the jet shower is organized into effective emitters according to a medium resolution scale based on transverse separation. This enables a space-time picture of in-medium jet evolution as a collection of subsequent antennas resolved by the medium.
