# Matching the Nonequilibrium Initial Stage of Heavy Ion Collisions to Hydrodynamics with QCD Kinetic Theory

> High-energy nuclear collisions produce a nonequilibrium plasma of quarks and gluons which thermalizes and exhibits hydrodynamic flow. There are currently no practical frameworks to connect the early particle production in classical field simulations to the subsequent hydrodynamic evolution. We build such a framework using nonequilibrium Green's functions, calculated in QCD kinetic theory, to propagate the initial energy-momentum tensor to the hydrodynamic phase. We demonstrate that this approach can be easily incorporated into existing hydrodynamic simulations, leading to stronger constraints on the energy density at early times and the transport properties of the QCD medium. Based on (conformal) scaling properties of the Green's functions, we further obtain pragmatic bounds for the applicability of hydrodynamics in nuclear collisions.

## Key claim

The paper provides the first practical framework to connect early-time classical field simulations of heavy ion collisions to subsequent hydrodynamic evolution, using nonequilibrium Green's functions calculated in QCD kinetic theory. The authors show that the kinetic response to a variety of initial conditions can be smoothly extrapolated to physically relevant couplings by using an appropriate scaling variable, making the approach a practical tool for RHIC and LHC phenomenology.

## Method

The authors use a nonequilibrium linear response formalism where the energy-momentum tensor at each spacetime point is decomposed into a local average background and linearized perturbations. The background evolution and linear response functions are extracted from numerical solutions of the Boltzmann equation with leading-order QCD collision kernels in pure-glue kinetic theory, starting from a microscopic gluon distribution function motivated by classical simulations of early-time dynamics. The resulting Green's functions propagate initial energy and momentum perturbations from an early time tau_ekt to a later time tau_hydro when hydrodynamics becomes applicable, and the computer code (called KoMPoST) is publicly available.

## Context

This work extends the "bottom-up" thermalization scenario of Baier (2000) and the classical Yang-Mills simulations of Berges (2013, 2014) by providing a practical tool to bridge between early-time color glass condensate dynamics and late-time hydrodynamic simulations. The approach builds on the nonequilibrium linear response formalism of Keegan (2016) and is detailed in the companion paper Kurkela (2018). The framework enables stronger constraints on the energy density at early times and transport properties of the QCD medium, and can be used to assess the limits of applicability of hydrodynamic descriptions in small collision systems such as p+p and p+Pb.
