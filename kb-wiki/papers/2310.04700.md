# Importance of physical information on the prediction of heavy-ion fusion cross section with machine learning

> In this work, the Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM), which is a modern decision tree based machine-learning algorithm, is used to study the fusion cross section (CS) of heavy-ion reaction. Several basic quantities (e.g., mass number and proton number of projectile and target) and the CS obtained from phenomenological formula are fed into the LightGBM algorithm to predict the CS. It is found that, on the validation set, the mean absolute error (MAE) which measures the average magnitude of the absolute difference between $log_{10}$ of the predicted CS and experimental CS is 0.129 by only using the basic quantities as the input, this value is smaller than 0.154 obtained from the empirical coupled channel model. MAE can be further reduced to 0.08 by including an physical-informed input feature. The MAE on the test set (it consists of 280 data points from 18 reaction systems that not included in the training set) is about 0.19 and 0.53 by including and excluding the physical-informed feature, respectively. We further verify the LightGBM predictions by comparing the CS of $^{ 40,48}{\rm Ca }$+$^{78}{\rm Ni}$ obtained from the density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock approach. Our study demonstrates the importance of physical information in predicting fusion cross section of heavy-ion reaction with machine learning.

## Key claim
The Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM) can predict heavy-ion fusion cross sections with a mean absolute error (MAE) of 0.129 on the validation set using only basic nuclear quantities, which is already better than the empirical coupled channel (ECC) model's MAE of 0.154; however, the key hedge is that this performance depends critically on including a physical-informed input feature, without which the test set MAE rises to 0.53, compared to 0.19 when the physical feature is included.

## Method
The authors used the LightGBM decision-tree-based machine learning algorithm to predict the logarithm of heavy-ion fusion cross sections. They trained the model on 3635 experimental data points from 220 reaction systems (with projectile charge Z between 12 and 48 and target mass number between 24 and 208), using 18 basic features (mass number, proton number, neutron number, binding energy, fusion Q-value, and separation energies of projectile, target, and compound nucleus) plus optionally one of three physical-informed features: the ECC model prediction, the Wong formula prediction, or the simple quantity Z1Z2/Ec.m.. The model was tested on 280 unseen data points from 18 reaction systems measured in recent experiments, and predictions were also compared with density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock calculations for 40,48Ca+78Ni.

## Key numbers
- On the validation set, the MAE for Mode_BF (basic features only) is 0.129, compared to 0.154 for the ECC model.
- Including the ECC model prediction as a physical-informed feature (Mode_ECC) reduces the validation MAE to 0.068.
- Including the Wong formula prediction (Mode_W) or Z1Z2/Ec.m. (Mode_E) gives a validation MAE of 0.081.
- On the test set of 280 data points from 18 reaction systems, the MAE is 0.19 when including the physical-informed feature and 0.53 when excluding it.
- The Wong formula alone gives a MAE of 2.38 on the validation set.

## Context
This work extends the application of machine learning to nuclear reaction modeling, specifically following the approach of Gao et al. (2021) who used LightGBM for nuclear mass predictions. It improves upon the empirical coupled channel model of Wang et al. (2017) by showing that a decision-tree algorithm can outperform traditional phenomenological models when given basic nuclear features. The study enables future work to use machine learning for predicting fusion cross sections of unmeasured reaction systems, particularly for exotic nuclei relevant to superheavy element synthesis and nuclear astrophysics, and demonstrates that simple physical quantities like Z1Z2/Ec.m. can serve as effective physical-informed features.
