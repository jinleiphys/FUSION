# Model dependence of the neutron-skin thickness on the symmetry energy

> The model dependence in the correlations of the neutron-skin thickness in heavy nuclei with various symmetry energy parameters is analyzed by using several families of systematically varied microscopic mean field models. Such correlations show a varying degree of model dependence once the results for all the different families are combined. Some mean field models associated with similar values of the symmetry energy slope parameter at saturation density $L$, and pertaining to different families, yield a greater-than-expected spread in the neutron-skin thickness of the $^{208}$Pb nucleus. The effective value of the symmetry energy slope parameter $L_{\rm eff}$, determined by using the nucleon density profiles of the finite nucleus and the density derivative $S^\prime(ρ)$ of the symmetry energy starting from about saturation density up to low densities typical of the surface of nuclei, seems to account for the spread in the neutron-skin thickness for the models with similar $L$. The differences in the values of $L_{\rm eff}$ are mainly due to the small differences in the nucleon density distributions of heavy nuclei in the surface region and the behavior of the symmetry energy at subsaturation densities.

## Key claim

The correlation between the neutron-skin thickness in heavy nuclei and the symmetry energy slope parameter L shows a model dependence that is not fully captured by the abstract's description: different mean-field models with similar L values can yield a spread in the neutron-skin thickness of 208Pb of almost 0.05 fm, which is larger than expected from the average linear correlation. This spread can be accounted for by an effective slope parameter Leff that incorporates the density dependence of the symmetry energy at subsaturation densities and the nucleon density profiles of the finite nucleus.

## Method

The authors used five families of systematically varied microscopic mean-field models: three relativistic (FSV, TSV, DDME) and two non-relativistic Skyrme (SAMi-J, KDE0-J). For each family, parameter sets were varied to explore different symmetry energy parameters while maintaining fit quality to experimental binding energies and charge radii within 0.25% and 0.8%, respectively. The neutron-skin thickness was decomposed into bulk and surface contributions using two-parameter Fermi fits to the self-consistent density distributions, following the method of Hasse and Myers (1988). An effective slope parameter Leff was defined by weighting the density derivative of the symmetry energy S'(ρ) with the nucleon density and local isospin asymmetry distributions of the finite nucleus.

## Key numbers

- For 208Pb, the correlation coefficient between J - asym(A) and Δrnp is 0.972, and between J - asym(A) and the bulk part Δrnp^bulk is 0.988.
- For 208Pb, the correlation coefficient between asym(A)/J and Δrnp is 0.965, and between asym(A)/J and Δrnp^bulk is 0.992.
- Using empirical constraints asym(A) = 22.4 ± 0.3 MeV and J = 32.5 ± 2.5 MeV, the predicted Δrnp for 208Pb is 0.197 ± 0.047 fm.
- From this Δrnp value, the extracted symmetry energy slope parameter L for 208Pb is 64 ± 23 MeV.
- The predicted Δrnp for 132Sn, derived from the 208Pb value, is 0.260 ± 0.050 fm.
- Five models from different families with L in the narrow range 62.1–67.0 MeV show a spread in Δrnp of almost 0.05 fm for 208Pb, while the average linear fit gives δ(Δrnp) ≈ 0.002 δL, meaning a 5 MeV change in L corresponds to only about 0.01 fm change in Δrnp.

## Context

This work extends earlier analyses of correlations between neutron-skin thickness and symmetry energy parameters (Brown 2000, Furnstahl 2002, Centelles 2009, Warda 2009) by using systematically varied families of models rather than unbiasedly selected individual models, allowing identification of sources of model dependence. The study complements the analysis of Centelles (2010) which showed deviations from linear correlation in some models. The results enable more reliable extraction of symmetry energy parameters from future high-precision measurements of neutron-skin thickness, such as the proposed PREX follow-up aiming for 0.06 fm accuracy in 208Pb, and provide predictions for 132Sn where no measurement yet exists.
