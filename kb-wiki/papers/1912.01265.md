# Crystallization of the inner crust of a neutron star and the influence of shell effects

> Context. In the cooling process of a non-accreting neutron star, the composition and properties of the crust are thought to be fixed at the finite temperature where nuclear reactions fall out of equilibrium. A lower estimation for this temperature is given by the crystallization temperature, which can be as high as $\approx 7\times 10^9$ K in the inner crust, potentially leading to sizeable differences with respect to the simplifying cold-catalyzed matter hypothesis. Aims. We extend the recent work by Fantina et al. (2019) on the outer crust, to the study of the crystallization of the inner crust and the associated composition in the one-component plasma approximation. Methods. The finite temperature variational equations for non-uniform matter in both the liquid and the solid phases are solved using a compressible liquid-drop approach with parameters optimized on four different microscopic models which cover the present uncertainties in nuclear modeling. Results. We consider separately the effect of the different nuclear ingredients with their associated uncertainties, namely the nuclear equation of state, the surface properties in the presence of a uniform gas of dripped neutrons, and the proton shell effects arising from the ion single-particle structure. Our results suggest that the highest source of model dependence comes from the smooth part of the nuclear functional. Conclusions. We show that shell effects play an important role at the lowest densities close to the outer crust, but the most important physical ingredient to be settled for a quantitative prediction of the inner crust properties is the surface tension at extreme isospin values.

## Key claim

The crystallization temperature in the inner crust can be as high as ≈ 7×10⁹ K, but the most important physical ingredient for quantitative predictions is the surface tension at extreme isospin values, not the nuclear equation of state or shell effects; shell effects are important only at the lowest densities close to the outer crust.

## Method

The finite-temperature variational equations for non-uniform matter in both liquid and solid phases were solved using a compressible liquid-drop (CLD) approach, with surface and curvature parameters optimized to reproduce the crust-core transition properties of four different microscopic functionals (BSk22, BSk24, BSk25, BSk26). Shell effects were added on top of the CLD free energy using the Strutinsky Integral method, and a temperature-dependent factor was introduced to account for the washing out of shell corrections at high temperature, calibrated against TETFSI results from Onsi et al. (2008).

## Key numbers

- Crystallization temperature can be as high as ≈ 7×10⁹ K in the inner crust (from abstract).
- The critical temperature for surface modes is T_c ≈ 1.75×10¹¹ K, well above the crystallization temperature (from text).
- At the highest density close to the crust-core transition, crystallization temperatures differ by up to 35% between different nuclear models (from text).
- The temperature at which shell effects vanish was fixed to k_B T_0 = 1 MeV with x(T_0) = 0.02 (from text).
- For the classical OCP, the mean ion-coupling parameter at crystallization is Γ_m ≈ 175 (from text).
- Symmetry energy values: E_sym = 32 MeV (BSk22), E_sym = 30 MeV (BSk24), E_sym = 29 MeV (BSk25) (from text).

## Context

This work extends the recent study by Fantina et al. (2019) on the crystallization of the outer crust to the inner crust region, using the same one-component plasma approximation. It builds on the ground-state composition calculations of Pearson et al. (2018) and the CLD formalism of Carreau et al. (2019), and compares with the earlier microscopic TETFSI results of Onsi et al. (2008). The results show that the smooth part of the nuclear functional is the dominant source of model dependence, and that simple CLD modeling may be sufficient to study crust properties at crystallization, enabling more reliable predictions for neutron star cooling and crust properties.
