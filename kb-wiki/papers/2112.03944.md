# Quantum Simulation of Chiral Phase Transitions

> The Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model has been widely studied for investigating the chiral phase structure of strongly interacting matter. The study of the thermodynamics of field theories within the framework of Lattice Field Theory is limited by the sign problem, which prevents Monte Carlo evaluation of the functional integral at a finite chemical potential. Using the quantum imaginary time evolution (QITE) algorithm, we construct a quantum simulation for the $(1+1)$ dimensional NJL model at finite temperature and finite chemical potential. We observe consistency among digital quantum simulation, exact diagonalization, and analytical solution, indicating further applications of quantum computing in simulating QCD thermodynamics.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the quantum imaginary time evolution (QITE) algorithm can successfully simulate the chiral phase transition of the (1+1)-dimensional Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model at finite temperature and finite chemical potential, with results consistent across digital quantum simulation, exact diagonalization, and analytical mean-field calculation, but only for a simplified 4-qubit system with specific parameter choices (lattice spacing a=1 MeV⁻¹, bare mass m=100 MeV, coupling g=1).

## Method

The authors discretize the (1+1)-dimensional NJL Hamiltonian on a spatial lattice using staggered fermions to avoid fermion doubling, then apply the Jordan-Wigner transformation to map the fermionic system onto a 4-qubit spin Hamiltonian. They use the QITE algorithm with Suzuki-Trotter decomposition to approximate imaginary time evolution via unitary operations, and employ the QMETTS algorithm to compute thermal expectation values of the chiral condensate ⟨ψ̅ψ⟩ as a function of temperature and chemical potential.

## Key numbers

- Lattice spacing a = 1 MeV⁻¹, bare quark mass m = 100 MeV, dimensionless coupling constant g = 1
- Dynamically generated mass Δm ≡ M-m = 4 MeV at sufficiently low μ²+T², in addition to the bare mass m = 100 MeV
- Imaginary time step Δβ = 0.001 MeV⁻¹ used for the QITE algorithm (with comparison also shown for Δβ = 0.005 MeV⁻¹)
- Chemical potentials studied: μ = 0 MeV, 100 MeV, and 150 MeV
- Temperature/chemical potential range explored: [0 MeV, 300 MeV]
- System uses 4 qubits (n_q=4) with N lattice sites (even number, N/2 position points)

## Context

This work extends the application of quantum computing to finite-temperature field theory, specifically addressing the sign problem that limits lattice QCD simulations at finite chemical potential, a problem noted by Ratti (2021) and Philipsen (2007). The authors build on the QITE algorithm developed by Motta et al. and prior work on quantum simulation of thermal systems by Bauer et al. and Ville et al. (2021). The use of the (1+1)-dimensional NJL/Gross-Neveu model, originally studied by Gross and Neveu (1974) and Thies (2019), provides a testbed where analytical mean-field solutions are available for comparison. This work enables future quantum simulations of QCD thermodynamics on larger qubit systems, potentially avoiding the sign problem that limits classical Monte Carlo approaches.
