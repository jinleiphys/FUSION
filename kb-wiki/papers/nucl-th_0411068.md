# Fluctuations in the level density of a Fermi gas

> We present a theory that accurately describes the counting of excited states of a noninteracting fermionic gas. At high excitation energies the results reproduce Bethe's theory. At low energies oscillatory corrections to the many--body density of states, related to shell effects, are obtained. The fluctuations depend non-trivially on energy and particle number. Universality and connections with Poisson statistics and random matrix theory are established for regular and chaotic single--particle motion.

## Key claim

The paper presents a theory that accurately describes the counting of excited states of a noninteracting fermionic gas, including oscillatory corrections to the many-body density of states that are related to shell effects; the fluctuations depend non-trivially on energy and particle number, and their statistical properties are universal for temperatures much smaller than the energy conjugate to the shortest periodic orbit.

## Method

The authors decompose the single-particle density of states into a smooth Thomas-Fermi term and an oscillatory term expressed as a sum over classical periodic orbits via the semiclassical approximation. They then compute the many-body density of states through an inverse Laplace transform and saddle-point approximation of the grand potential, separating the entropy into smooth and fluctuating parts. The fluctuating part of the entropy is expressed in terms of the temperature-dependent oscillatory grand potential, which is evaluated using periodic orbit theory with a temperature damping factor.

## Key numbers

* For chaotic systems at low temperatures (2π²T ≪ δ), the variance of the entropy fluctuations is ⟨S̃²⟩ = c₄ π² T/δ, where c₄ = 0.0609...
* For chaotic systems at intermediate temperatures (δ ≪ 2π²T ≪ E_c), the variance saturates at ⟨S̃²⟩ = c₃/β, where c₃ = 0.1023... and β = 1 (2) for systems with (without) time-reversal invariance.
* For high temperatures (2π²T ≫ E_c), the fluctuations decay as ⟨S̃²⟩^{1/2} ∝ T^{-1} after an exponential transient.
* The shell correction amplitude grows linearly at low temperatures and decays as T^{-1} when T ≫ E_c/2π².
* The numerical test used approximately 2000 fermions in a two-dimensional rectangular cavity, an integrable system.

## Context

This work extends Bethe's (1936) original theory of the nuclear level density by incorporating oscillatory shell corrections that Bethe's formula omits. It builds on prior work by Bloch (1954), Rosenzweig (1957), and Bohr and Mottelson (1969) on shell corrections and the backshifted Bethe formula, as well as on the semiclassical periodic orbit theory of Gutzwiller (1971) and the temperature-dependent treatment of shell effects by Leboeuf, Monastra, and Relaño (2001). The theory enables more accurate predictions of nuclear level densities for compound nuclear decay rates, evaporation residue yields, and astrophysical thermonuclear rates, with applications to specific nuclear systems to be presented in a separate contribution.
