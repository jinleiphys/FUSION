# Constraints on the Variation of Physical Constants, Equivalence Principle Violation, and a Fifth Force from Atomic Experiments

> The aim of this paper is to derive limits on various forms of ``new physics'' using atomic experimental data. Interactions with dark energy and dark matter fields can lead to space-time variations of fundamental constants, which can be detected through atomic spectroscopy. In this study, we examine the effects of a varying nuclear mass $m_{N}$ and nuclear radius $r_{N}$ on two transition ratios: the comparison of the two-photon transition in atomic hydrogen with the hyperfine transition in $^{133}$Cs based clocks, and the ratio of optical clock frequencies in in Al$^{+}$ and Hg$^{+}$. The sensitivity of these frequency ratios to changes in $m_{N}$ and $r_{N}$ enables us to derive new limits on the variations of the proton mass, quark mass, and the QCD parameter $θ$. Additionally, we consider the scalar field generated by the Yukawa-type interaction of feebly interacting hypothetical scalar particles with Standard Model particles in the presence of massive bodies such as the Sun and Moon. Using the data from the Al$^{+}$/Hg$^{+}$, Yb$^{+}$/Cs and Yb$^{+}$(E2)/Yb$^{+}$(E3) transition frequency ratios, we place constraints on the interaction of the scalar field with photons, nucleons, and electrons for a range of scalar particle masses. We also investigate limits on the Einstein Equivalence Principle (EEP) violating term ($c_{00}$) in the Standard Model Extension (SME) Lagrangian and the dependence of fundamental constants on gravity.

## Key claim

The paper derives new limits on variations of fundamental constants (proton mass, quark mass, QCD parameter θ), equivalence principle violation, and scalar-mediated fifth forces using atomic clock data, but these limits depend on the assumption that only one parameter varies at a time while others are held fixed.

## Method

The authors analyze how variations in nuclear mass m_N and nuclear radius r_N affect two transition frequency ratios: the two-photon transition in atomic hydrogen compared to the hyperfine transition in ¹³³Cs, and the optical clock transition ratio in Al⁺/Hg⁺. They calculate mass shift and field shift coefficients for these transitions, then use experimental drift measurements to extract limits on the variation of hadronic parameters. For the fifth force analysis, they model the scalar field generated by Yukawa-type interactions of hypothetical scalar particles with Standard Model particles in the presence of the Sun and Moon, using frequency ratio data from Al⁺/Hg⁺, Yb⁺/Cs, and Yb⁺(E2)/Yb⁺(E3) transitions.

## Key numbers

- Fractional time variation of the Cs/H frequency ratio: 3.2 (6.3) × 10⁻¹⁵ yr⁻¹ (from Fischer 2004)
- Fractional time variation of the Al⁺/Hg⁺ frequency ratio: -5.3 (7.9) × 10⁻¹⁷ yr⁻¹ (from AlHgdrift)
- Limit on quark mass drift from Cs/H data: dm_q/dt = -8.2 (16) × 10⁻¹⁴ yr⁻¹
- Limit on proton mass drift from Cs/H data: dm_p/dt = -5.3 (10) × 10⁻¹⁵ yr⁻¹
- Limit on internucleon distance drift from Al⁺/Hg⁺ data: dr₀/dt = -1.1 (1.7) × 10⁻¹⁴ yr⁻¹
- Limit on proton mass drift from Al⁺/Hg⁺ nuclear mass variation: dm_p/dt = 1.0 (1.6) × 10⁻¹² yr⁻¹
- Limit on dθ²/dt from Cs/H data: 8.2 (16) × 10⁻¹³ yr⁻¹
- Limit on dθ²/dt from Al⁺/Hg⁺ internucleon distance variation: 1.8 (2.8) × 10⁻¹³ yr⁻¹
- Field shift parameter for the ²S₁/₂ → ²D₅/₂ transition in Hg⁺: 85.5 GHz/fm²
- Total mass shift parameter for Al⁺: -1530 GHz amu

## Context

This work extends the method of Flambaum and Mansour (2023), which used Yb⁺ optical clock transitions to constrain hadron and quark mass variations and the QCD parameter θ, to additional atomic systems (Cs/H and Al⁺/Hg⁺). The limits from Al⁺/Hg⁺ are approximately 44 times weaker than those from Yb⁺, consistent with the difference in experimental precision. The fifth force analysis extends the approach of Leefer (2016) by using newer clock comparison data and comparing constraints to those from the MICROSCOPE mission (which constrained the Eotvos parameter to η = (-1 ± 27) × 10⁻¹⁵ at 2σ). The results enable improved searches for dark matter and dark energy interactions through atomic spectroscopy.
