# Manifest Gauge Invariance for Structure Dependent Radiative Corrections to Processes Involving Atoms and Nuclei

> Radiative corrections to reactions involving atoms or nuclei can become sensitive to the structure of the bound state. Generically, one encounters correlation functions of multiple currents which must satisfy Ward identities. At intermediate steps, however the Ward identities are obscured, and often violated by physically motivated approximation schemes. In this paper we outline a method to construct a representation of the aforementioned correlators that manifests gauge invariance in the limit of a heavy target (i.e., when recoil energy can be neglected). This representation then enables manifestly gauge invariant approximation schemes. Furthermore, the proposed representation naturally separates the largest contributions that dominate scattering amplitudes in the limit of a heavy constituent (e.g., proton) mass. We analyze elastic electron scattering from nuclei in detail, and also discuss radiative corrections to processes mediated by the weak interaction.

# Manifest Gauge Invariance for Structure Dependent Radiative Corrections to Processes Involving Atoms and Nuclei

## Key claim

The paper presents a method to construct a manifestly gauge-invariant representation of multi-current correlation functions that arise in radiative corrections to processes involving atoms and nuclei, under the condition that target recoil energy can be neglected (heavy target limit). The representation ensures that Ward identities remain satisfied even after physically motivated approximation schemes, such as the closure approximation or elastic approximations, are applied to the hadronic tensor.

## Method

The authors decompose the electromagnetic current into charge density and transverse components, then use the continuity equation and the condition that the target Hamiltonian annihilates the ground state to derive identities that separate the hadronic tensor into separately gauge-invariant pieces. For elastic electron scattering, the hadronic tensor \(H_{\mu\nu}(q_1,q_2)\) is rewritten into four terms: a charge-density term, an anticommutator term involving transverse currents and densities, a commutator-plus-seagull term, and a term involving modified currents \(\mathscr{J}_\mu^{(\pm)}\) that are individually conserved (\(q^\mu \mathscr{J}_\mu = 0\)). For weak interaction processes, an analogous decomposition is performed on the correlator \(H_\mu\) involving one electromagnetic current and one weak operator.

## Key numbers

- The \(\langle \rho \rho \rangle\) term in the decomposition begins at \(O(1/M^0)\) in the expansion in the constituent (proton) mass \(M\).
- The \(\{\ldots\}_{\mu\nu}\) and \([\ldots]_{\mu\nu} + S_{\mu\nu}\) terms begin at \(O(1/M^1)\).
- The \(\mathscr{J}\!\!\!\mathscr{J}\) term begins at \(O(1/M^2)\).
- For one-body currents, the seagull vertex is \(S_{\mu\nu}(\vb{q}_1,\vb{q}_2) = -\frac{1}{M} g_{\perp\mu\nu}\rho(\vb{q}_1+\vb{q}_2)\), where \(g_{\perp\mu\nu} = g_{\mu\nu} - v_\mu v_\nu\) and \(v_\mu = (1,\vb{0})\).
- The commutator of the one-body charge density and current operators yields \([\vb{J}_{\rm 1B}(\vb{q}_2),\rho_{\rm 1B}(\vb{q}_1)] = \frac{\vb{q}_1}{M} \rho_{\rm 1B}(\vb{q}_1+\vb{q}_2)\).

## Context

This work extends the long-standing effort to handle radiative corrections to reactions involving composite targets, where "box diagrams" with excited intermediate states introduce sensitivity to nuclear or atomic structure (Bottino 1966, Bethe 1971, Friar 1974). Prior approaches such as the closure approximation (Friar 1974, Jakubassa-Amundsen 2022) and the elastic approximation are shown to violate Ward identities when applied directly to the naive hadronic tensor. The method enables manifestly gauge-invariant improved closure approximations and systematic inclusion of intermediate states, and is relevant for precision applications including nuclear beta decays (Seng 2018, Hardy 2020), parity-violating electron scattering (CREX 2022, PREX 2021), neutrino-nucleus scattering (Lovato 2020, Rocco 2020, Tomalak 2021), and the dispersive correction to electron scattering (Bethe 1971, Friar 1974, Offermann 1991).
