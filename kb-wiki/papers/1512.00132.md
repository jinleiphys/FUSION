# Electron capture and beta-decay rates for sd-shell nuclei in stellar environments relevant to high density O-Ne-Mg cores

> Electron capture and beta-decay rates for nuclear pairs in sd-shell are evaluated at high densities and high temperatures relevant to the final evolution of electron-degenerate O-Ne-Mg cores of stars with the initial masses of 8-10 solar mass. Electron capture induces a rapid contraction of the electron-degenerate O-Ne-Mg core. The outcome of rapid contraction depends on the evolutionary changes in the central density and temperature, which are determined by the competing processes of contraction, cooling, and heating. The fate of the stars are determined by these competitions, whether they end up with electron-capture supernovae or Fe core-collapse supernovae. Since the competing processes are induced by electron capture and beta-decay, the accurate weak rates are crucially important. The rates are obtained for pairs with A=20, 23, 24, 25 and 27 by shell-model calculations in sd-shell with the USDB Hamiltonian. Effects of Coulomb corrections on the rates are evaluated. The rates for pairs with A=23 and 25 are important for nuclear URCA processes that determine the cooling rate of O-Ne-Mg core, while those for pairs with A=20 and 24 are important for the core-contraction and heat generation rates in the core. We provide these nuclear rates at stellar environments in tables with fine enough meshes at various densities and temperatures for the studies of astrophysical processes sensitive to the rates. In particular, the accurate rate tables are crucially important for the final fates of not only O-Ne-Mg cores but also a wider range of stars such as C-O cores of lower mass stars.

## Key claim

Electron capture and beta-decay rates for sd-shell nuclear pairs (A=20, 23, 24, 25, 27) are evaluated using shell-model calculations with the USDB Hamiltonian, and the authors claim that the fine mesh steps (Δlog₁₀(ρYₑ)=0.02, Δlog₁₀T=0.05) are necessary because interpolation from coarser grids can produce errors larger than a factor of 10, particularly when ground-state-to-ground-state transitions are forbidden or excited-state contributions are essential.

## Method

The authors performed shell-model calculations in the sd-shell using the USDB Hamiltonian with an effective axial-vector coupling constant gAᵉᶠᶠ/gA=0.764 to obtain Gamow-Teller (GT) and Fermi transition strengths. Electron capture and beta-decay rates were computed using standard stellar weak-rate formalism including Fermi-Dirac electron distributions, with excited states up to Ex=2 MeV included via thermal population weighting. Coulomb corrections from electron screening and changes in nuclear chemical potential were evaluated using the random phase approximation dielectric function and the formalism of Ichimaru (1993), respectively.

## Key numbers

- URCA density for the ²⁵Na-²⁵Mg pair is found at log₁₀(ρYₑ)=8.77, with temperature dependence Δlog₁₀(ρYₑ) < 0.01 for log₁₀T=8-9.2
- URCA density for the ²³Ne-²³Na pair is found at log₁₀(ρYₑ)=8.92, with temperature dependence Δlog₁₀(ρYₑ) ≈ 0.01 for log₁₀T=8-9.2
- Coulomb corrections shift the URCA density to a higher density region by Δlog₁₀(ρYₑ)=0.04
- The Q-value for ²⁵Na→²⁵Mg is 3.83 MeV; for ²³Ne→²³Na it is 4.38 MeV; for ²⁷Mg→²⁷Al it is 2.61 MeV
- Electron capture Q-values: ²⁴Mg has 5.52 MeV, ²⁰Ne has 7.02 MeV; threshold energies for successive captures: ²⁴Na has 2.47 MeV, ²⁰F has 3.81 MeV
- The calculated B(GT) value for the ²³Na ground-state to ²³Ne ground-state transition is smaller than the experimental value by a factor of 2.67 for USDB and 1.96 for USD
- The calculated B(GT) value for the ²⁵Mg ground-state to ²⁵Na ground-state transition agrees with experiment within 15% (USDB) and 19% (USD)
- Rates are provided for densities log₁₀(ρYₑ)=8.0-11.0 in steps of 0.02 and temperatures log₁₀T=8.0-9.65 in steps of 0.05, plus log₁₀T=7.0-8.0 in steps of 0.20

## Context

This work extends previous calculations by Oda et al. (1994) who used the USD Hamiltonian, by adopting the updated USDB Hamiltonian and including Coulomb corrections from the electron background. The rates for A=23 and 25 pairs are critical for nuclear URCA processes that determine cooling of O-Ne-Mg cores in 8-10 solar mass stars, as previously studied by Suzuki et al. (2013) and Jones et al. (2013). The rates for A=20 and 24 pairs are important for core contraction and heating in later stages leading to electron-capture supernovae. The fine-grid rate tables enable more accurate stellar evolution simulations that can determine whether such stars end as O-Ne-Mg white dwarfs, electron-capture supernovae, or Fe core-collapse supernovae, and are also applicable to C-O cores of lower mass stars and white dwarfs in binary systems.
