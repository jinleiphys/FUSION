# The R-matrix theory

> The different facets of the $R$-matrix method are presented pedagogically in a general framework. Two variants have been developed over the years: $(i)$ The "calculable" $R$-matrix method is a calculational tool to derive scattering properties from the Schrödinger equation in a large variety of physical problems. It was developed rather independently in atomic and nuclear physics with too little mutual influence. $(ii)$ The "phenomenological" $R$-matrix method is a technique to parametrize various types of cross sections. It was mainly (or uniquely) used in nuclear physics. Both directions are explained by starting from the simple problem of scattering by a potential. They are illustrated by simple examples in nuclear and atomic physics. In addition to elastic scattering, the $R$-matrix formalism is applied to transfer and radiative-capture reactions. We also present more recent and more ambitious applications of the theory in nuclear physics.

## Key claim

The R-matrix theory is presented as a dual-purpose framework: a "calculable" method for solving the Schrödinger equation in scattering problems, and a "phenomenological" method for parametrizing cross sections. The review emphasizes that these two variants have developed largely independently in atomic and nuclear physics, with little mutual influence, and aims to bridge that gap by presenting both pedagogically.

## Method

The paper reviews both the calculable and phenomenological R-matrix methods, starting from potential scattering in a single channel and extending to multichannel systems. For the calculable variant, the configuration space is divided into an internal region (where the wave function is expanded over a discrete basis of eigenstates) and an external region (where asymptotic Coulomb or free solutions apply), with matching at the channel radius via the Bloch operator. The phenomenological variant parametrizes the R-matrix as a sum over poles with reduced-width amplitudes and energy denominators, as in Eq. (1.1). The review also covers applications to transfer reactions, radiative capture, and coupled-channel calculations, and provides explicit matrix elements for sine, Gaussian, and Lagrange basis functions in the appendices.

## Key numbers

- The width of a non-overlapping resonance is given by Γ = 2 γ²_{nlJ} P_l, where P_l is the penetration factor (Section 1.2).
- The kinetic-energy matrix element for sine functions (l=0, units of ħ²/2μ) is ⟨φ_i|T_0|φ_j⟩ = (π²/(2a)) (i − 1/2)² δ_{ij} (Appendix C).
- The overlap matrix element for sine functions is ⟨φ_i|φ_j⟩ = (a/2) δ_{ij} (Appendix C).
- For Gaussian basis functions, the overlap matrix element is ⟨φ_i|φ_j⟩ = I_{2l+2}(ν_i+ν_j), where I_k(ν) = γ((k+1)/2, ν a²) / (2 ν^{(k+1)/2}) (Appendix C).
- The Sommerfeld parameter is η = Z₁ Z₂ e²/(ħ v) = sgn(Z₁ Z₂)/(a_B k), with a_B = ħ²/(μ |Z₁ Z₂| e²) (Eqs. 2.2, 2.3).
- The Coulomb phase shift is σ_l = arg Γ(l + 1 + iη) (Eq. 2.6).

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Wigner and Eisenbud (1946, 1947) and the comprehensive review by Lane and Thomas (1958) on the phenomenological R-matrix, while also covering the calculable R-matrix developed by Haglund and Robson (1965) and Buttle (1967). It contrasts the R-matrix approach with the K-matrix method (Humblet, 1990) and notes similarities to the variational K-matrix method (Kato, 1977) and the Gamow-state method (Tolstikhin et al., 1997). The paper enables future applications in nuclear astrophysics (low-energy radiative capture), microscopic cluster calculations, and coupled-channel problems with non-local interactions, and provides a pedagogical entry point for both atomic and nuclear physics communities.
