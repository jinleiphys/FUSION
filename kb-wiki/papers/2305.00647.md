# DT fusion through the $^5$He $3/2+$ "Bretscher state" accounts for $\ge 25\%$ of our existence via nucleosynthesis and for the possibility of fusion energy

> In big bang nucleosynthesis (BBN), the deuterium-tritium (DT) fusion reaction, D(T,n)$α$, enhanced by the 3/2$^+$ resonance, is responsible for 99% of primordial $^4$He. This has been known for decades and has been well documented in the scientific literature. However, following the tradition adopted by authors of learned articles, it was stated in a matter-of-fact manner and not emphasized; for most people, it has remained unknown. This helium became a source for the subsequent creation of $\geq$25% of the carbon and other heavier elements and, thus, a substantial fraction of our human bodies. (To be more precise than $\geq$25% will require future simulation studies on stellar nucleosynthesis.) Also, without this resonance, controlled fusion energy would be beyond reach. For example, for inertial confinement fusion (ICF), laser energy delivery for the National Ignition Facility (NIF) would have to be approximately 70 times larger for ignition. Because the resonance enhances the DT fusion cross section a hundredfold, we propose that the 3/2$^+$ $^5$He excited state be referred to as the "Bretscher state" in honor of the Manhattan Project scientist who discovered it, in analogy with the well-known 7.6 MeV "Hoyle state" in $^{12}$C that allows for the resonant 3$α$ formation.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the resonant enhancement of the deuterium-tritium (DT) fusion reaction by the 3/2+ excited state of 5He is responsible for at least 25% of the carbon and heavier elements that make up the human body, and that without this resonance, controlled fusion energy would be unattainable. The authors hedge that the "at least 25%" figure is a lower bound based on neutron-proton conservation, and that more precise quantification requires future stellar nucleosynthesis simulations.

## Method

The authors synthesize existing results from big bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) calculations by Smith et al. (1993) and Grohs et al. (2016) to trace the dominant nuclear reaction pathways that produced primordial 4He. They then apply a simple conservation argument (neutron and proton number conservation) to relate the primordial 4He mass fraction to the fraction of heavier elements derived from it, and perform inertial confinement fusion (ICF) calculations using the Haines et al. (2017) implosion model to estimate the impact of removing the Bretscher state resonance on laser energy requirements for ignition at the National Ignition Facility (NIF).

## Key numbers

- 99% of primordial 4He was produced via the D(T,n)4He reaction through the 3/2+ resonance (Smith et al. 1993).
- The DT fusion cross section is enhanced a hundredfold by the Bretscher state resonance.
- Without the resonance, the laser energy required for ignition at NIF would be approximately 70 times larger than the current 2 MJ delivered.
- The DT resonance peaks at 65 keV in the center of mass, and the BBN temperature when 4He production peaks is approximately 70 keV.
- A 20% reduction in the DT fusion cross section would cause ICF designs to lose about half of their yield.
- The primordial universe was approximately 25% 4He by mass, with 75% hydrogen (1H).

## Context

This work extends the BBN sensitivity studies of Smith et al. (1993) and the ab initio cross section calculations of Hupin et al. (2019) by explicitly connecting the DT resonance to the production of heavier elements in stars and to the feasibility of ICF. The authors propose naming the 3/2+ 5He excited state the "Bretscher state" in analogy with the Hoyle state in 12C, honoring the Manhattan Project scientist who first identified and characterized the resonance in 1945-46. The paper enables future simulation studies to refine the 25% lower bound on the contribution of BBN-produced helium to stellar nucleosynthesis.
