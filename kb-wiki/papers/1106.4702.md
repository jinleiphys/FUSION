# Concavity Theorems for Energy Surfaces

> Concavity properties prevent the existence of significant landscapes in energy surfaces obtained by strict constrained energy minimizations. The inherent contradiction is due to fluctuations of collective coordinates. A solution to those fluctuations is given.

## Key claim

Strict constrained energy minimizations with fixed collective operators and fixed trial spaces necessarily produce concave energy surfaces with no maxima or saddles, contradicting the intuition of energy landscapes with barriers; this paradox arises because quantum fluctuations of the collective coordinate can become large and invalidate the collective label, but can be overcome by keeping the fluctuation of the collective coordinate constant.

## Method

The authors prove analytically, using both exact diagonalization and constrained Hartree-Fock (HF) theory, that the Legendre transform of a strictly minimized constrained Hamiltonian always yields a concave energy function e(b). They test this concavity theorem numerically with random matrices and with solvable models of N identical fermions in a harmonic trap with a two-body interaction, using Hartree approximations. They then demonstrate that introducing a double constraint involving both B and B², with the second Lagrange multiplier adjusted to keep the fluctuation Δb constant, breaks concavity and allows a faithful reconstruction of the potential barrier.

## Context

This work extends the known concavity property of constrained energy surfaces to a rigorous theorem, contradicting the common assumption that such surfaces can contain saddles and barriers as used in nuclear fission and fusion models (Nix and Swiatecki, 1965; Frisk et al., 1995). The authors show that technical modifications used by Giraud et al. (1975) and Frisk et al. (1995) to stabilize numerical procedures amount to using effective Lagrange multipliers and do not resolve the fundamental concavity issue. The paper enables a new approach where fluctuations of collective coordinates are controlled by adding B² terms with adjustable coefficients, and raises the question of whether existing constrained mean-field solutions in the literature already contain a self-damping mechanism for collective coordinate fluctuations.
