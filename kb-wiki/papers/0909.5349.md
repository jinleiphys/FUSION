# Non-identical particle femtoscopy at sqrt(s_NN)=200 AGeV in hydrodynamics with statistical hadronization

> Non-identical particle femtoscopy probes not only the size of the emitting system, but also the emission asymmetries between particles of different mass, which are intimately related with the collective behavior of matter. We apply the technique to the simulations from the {\tt THERMINATOR}+Lhyquid model of the heavy-ion collisions at sqrt{s_{NN}}=200 AGeV. We present predictions for all common pair combinations and discuss their interpretation. Emphasis is put on extracting unique information about collective behavior of matter from the non-identical particle correlations and understanding the role of particles coming from decays of hadronic resonances. We also present, in detail, the technical aspects of the non-identical particle femtoscopy technique applied to data from the heavy-ion collisions. We list the sources of systematic errors coming from the method itself and the usual assumptions. We describe robust analysis methods and discuss their limitations.

## Key claim

Non-identical particle femtoscopy can directly measure emission asymmetries between particles of different masses, which arise from collective flow in hydrodynamic models; these asymmetries provide a more direct and unambiguous signal of collectivity than the traditional mT scaling of femtoscopic radii, because no mechanism other than collectivity is known to produce such specific emission asymmetries.

## Method

The authors used the THERMINATOR+Lhyquid model, combining ideal-fluid hydrodynamics with Gaussian initial conditions and statistical hadronization at a freeze-out temperature of 150 MeV, to simulate Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV across six centrality classes. They calculated single-particle emission distributions and two-particle correlation functions for pion-kaon, pion-proton, and kaon-proton pairs, then extracted the mean emission points (shifts) and variances (sizes) of the source distributions in the out-side-long coordinate system. A "scrambling" procedure was applied to break the spatial-momentum correlation and test whether the asymmetries vanish without collective flow.

* Central Au+Au: primordial pion-kaon emission asymmetry <x_out^pi> - <x_out^K> = -1.64 fm; pion-proton asymmetry = -2.78 fm; kaon-proton asymmetry = -1.14 fm (Table 2, "flow" row, primordial)
* Central Au+Au: when including resonance decay products ("all"), pion-kaon asymmetry becomes -3.54 fm; pion-proton asymmetry = -4.69 fm; kaon-proton asymmetry = -1.15 fm (Table 2, "flow" row, all)
* Central Au+Au: after scrambling (removing directional correlation), primordial asymmetries become -0.04 fm (pi-K), -0.01 fm (pi-p), 0.03 fm (K-p) (Table 2, "scrambled" row, primordial)

* Gaussian initial condition parameters for 0-5% centrality: a = 2.70 fm, b = 2.93 fm, eccentricity epsilon* = 0.08 (Table 1)

## Context

This work extends the successful simultaneous description of soft observables at RHIC (including pionic femtoscopic radii) achieved by Broniowski et al. (2008) using Gaussian initial conditions and the THERMINATOR model, by now applying non-identical particle femtoscopy to predict emission asymmetries. Previous studies using blast-wave parametrizations (Retiere and Lisa, 2004) had illustrated the connection between collective flow and asymmetries but used simplified emission functions and neglected resonance decays. The results enable direct experimental tests of hydrodynamic collectivity at RHIC and LHC, as the predicted asymmetries cannot be reproduced by alternative explanations such as temperature gradients (Gombeaud et al., 2009) that might mimic mT scaling.
