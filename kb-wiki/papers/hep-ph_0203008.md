# Renormalization in Self-Consistent Approximation schemes at Finite Temperature III: Global Symmetries

> We investigate the symmetry properties for Baym's $Φ$-derivable schemes. We show that in general the solutions of the dynamical equations of motion, derived from approximations of the $Φ$-functional, do not fulfill the Ward-Takahashi identities of the symmetry of the underlying classical action, although the conservation laws for the expectation values of the corresponding Noether currents are fulfilled exactly for the approximation. Further we prove that one can define an effective action functional in terms of the self-consistent propagators which is invariant under the operation of the same symmetry group representation as the classical action. The requirements for this theorem to hold true are the same as for perturbative approximations: The symmetry has to be realized linearly on the fields and it must be free of anomalies, i.e., there should exist a symmetry conserving regularization scheme. In addition, if the theory is renormalizable in Dyson's narrow sense, it can be renormalized with counter terms which do not violate the symmetry.

## Key claim

The paper proves that for any truncated \(\Phi\)-derivable (2PI) approximation scheme, one can construct an effective action functional \(\tilde{\Gamma}[\varphi]\) from the self-consistent propagators that is invariant under the same global symmetry as the classical action, even though the self-consistent solutions of the dynamical equations themselves violate the Ward-Takahashi identities for vertex functions. This holds provided the symmetry is realized linearly on the fields and the theory is free of anomalies, with the additional condition that if the theory is renormalizable in Dyson's narrow sense, it can be renormalized with symmetry-preserving counter terms.

## Method

The authors analyze the symmetry properties of the 2PI (two-particle irreducible) action functional \(\Gamma[\varphi,G]\) for the O(\(N\))-symmetric \(\phi^4\) theory, using the path-integral formalism with local and bilocal sources. They derive generalized Ward-Takahashi identities for the exact functional and then show that for any truncated \(\Phi\)-functional that remains invariant under the symmetry transformation, the effective action \(\tilde{\Gamma}[\varphi] = \Gamma[\varphi,\tilde{G}[\varphi]]\) (where \(\tilde{G}[\varphi]\) solves the Schwinger-Dyson equation at fixed mean field) is symmetric. The external vertex functions, including the self-energy and higher \(n\)-point functions, are then defined by functional derivatives of \(\tilde{\Gamma}\) and satisfy the usual 1PI Ward-Takahashi identities, with the three-point vertex function obtained from a Bethe-Salpeter equation that resums \(t\)- and \(u\)-channel ladders.

## Key numbers

* O(\(N\)) linear \(\sigma\)-model: the number of massless Goldstone bosons in the spontaneously broken phase is \(N-1\), derived from the dimension of the symmetry group O(\(N\)) minus the dimension of the symmetry group of the mean field O(\(N-1\)).
* Superficial degree of divergence for a diagram \(\gamma\) in the \(\phi^4\) theory: \(\delta(\gamma) = 4 - E\), where \(E\) is the number of external legs, meaning only proper \(n\)-point functions with \(n \leq 4\) require renormalization.

## Context

This work extends the renormalization program for \(\Phi\)-derivable schemes developed in the first two papers of this series (van Hees and Knoll, 2001, referred to as I and II) to the question of global symmetries. It addresses the symmetry violations previously observed by Baym and Grinstein in various approximations for the O(\(N\))-model, and by Hohenberg and Martin in the context of superfluid Helium. The construction of the symmetric effective action \(\tilde{\Gamma}[\varphi]\) provides a general method to recover Ward-Takahashi identities for any \(\Phi\)-derivable truncation, enabling symmetry-preserving non-perturbative approximations for vertex functions such as the self-energy and Noether current correlators, with the Hartree approximation serving as the simplest example where the Random-Phase approximation (RPA) emerges as the symmetry-restoring scheme.
