# Formation Of Quark Matter In Neutron Stars

> At very large densities and/or temperatures a quark-hadron phase transition is expected to take place. Simulations of QCD on lattice at zero baryon density indicate that the transition occurs at $T_c \sim 150-170$ MeV. The calculations indicate that transition is likely to be second order or a cross over phenomenon. Although the lattice simulations have not given any indication on when the transition occurs at nonzero baryon density, the transition is expected to occur around the densities of few times nuclear matter density. Also, there is a strong reason to believe that the quark matter formed after the phase transition is in colour superconducting phase. The matter densities in the interior of neutron stars are expected to be several times the nuclear matter density and therefore the neutron star cores may possibly consist of quark matter. One then expects that this quark matter is formed during the collapse of supernova. Starting with the assumption that the quark matter, when formed consists of predominantly u and d quarks, we consider the evolution of strange quarks by weak interactions in the present work. The reaction rates and time required to reach the chemical equilibrium are computed here. Our calculations show that the chemical equilibrium is reached in about $10^{-7}$ seconds. Further more during and immediately after the equilibration process enormous amount of energy is released and copious numbers of neutrinos are produced. We show that for reasonable models of nuclear equations of state the amount of energy released could be as high as $10^{53}$ ergs and as many as $10^{58}$ neutrinos may be emitted during the quark matter formation.

## Key claim

The authors show that if quark matter forms in neutron star cores during supernova collapse, the subsequent chemical equilibration of strange quarks via weak interactions occurs in about \(10^{-7}\) seconds, releasing up to \(10^{53}\) ergs of energy and producing as many as \(10^{58}\) neutrinos, but only under the assumption that the initial quark matter consists predominantly of u and d quarks with negligible strangeness.

## Method

The authors computed weak interaction reaction rates for nonleptonic (\(d+u \leftrightarrow s+u\)) and semi-leptonic processes (e.g., \(d+e^+ \rightarrow u+\bar{\nu}_e\)) using standard V-A theory, integrating over Fermi distributions for quarks and leptons at finite temperature. They then solved coupled rate equations for the time evolution of quark and electron densities, temperature, and neutrino emissivities, assuming thermal equilibrium is maintained by strong and electromagnetic interactions throughout the chemical equilibration. Calculations were performed for two quark matter models: the bag model (u,d masses 0,0 MeV; s mass 150 MeV) and the chiral colour dielectric (CCD) model (u,d masses 100,100 MeV; s mass 210 MeV), for initial baryon densities of \(3-5\) times nuclear matter density and initial temperatures of a few tens of MeV.

## Key numbers

- Chemical equilibrium is reached in about \(10^{-7}\) seconds for both bag and CCD models.
- The temperature of the quark matter rises by 30–40 MeV during equilibration, peaking around \(10^{-8}\) seconds.
- About 0.03 neutrinos per baryon are emitted during chemical equilibration, with per-baryon energy release of about 3 MeV (average neutrino energy \(\sim 100\) MeV).
- By \(10^{-6}\) seconds, about 1 neutrino per baryon is emitted, with average energy larger than 50 MeV.
- For a neutron star with central density of \(7 \times \rho_{NM}\) (where \(\rho_{NM} = 0.17\ \text{fm}^{-3}\)), the quark matter core mass is estimated at 0.5–1.2 solar masses depending on the equation of state (soft: incompressibility K=120 MeV; hard: K=224 MeV).
- Total energy released from a 0.5 solar mass quark core is estimated at \(8 \times 10^{52}\) ergs or more, with total neutrino number \(\sim 10^{57}\) from the core, potentially reaching \(10^{58}\) after diffusion.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of the hadron-quark phase transition in neutron stars (e.g., lattice QCD results indicating a transition temperature of 150–170 MeV at zero baryon density, and earlier estimates of transition energy density around 2 GeV/fm\(^3\) that have been revised downward to 0.6 GeV/fm\(^3\)). The authors build on the expectation that quark matter in neutron star cores should be in a colour-superconducting phase, as proposed in theoretical investigations of attractive quark-quark interactions. The results enable quantitative modeling of the neutrino burst and energy release accompanying quark matter formation in supernovae and proto-neutron stars, with implications for supernova explosion mechanisms and possibly for gamma-ray bursters (e.g., GRB 971214 and GRB 990123) if neutrino energy can be converted to gamma rays.
