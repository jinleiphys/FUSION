# Cross section of the Coulomb excitation of $^{229m}$Th by low energy muons

> The inelastic scattering cross section for muons, $μ^-$, with energies $E$=9--100~eV from the $^{229}$Th nuclei is calculated in the framework of the second order of the perturbation theory for the quantum electrodynamics. The dominant contribution to the excitation of the low energy isomer $^{229m}$Th$(3/2^+,8.19\pm0.12$~eV) comes from the $E2$ multipole. The excitation cross section reaches the value of $10^{-21}$ cm$^2$ in the range $E\approx$10~eV. This is four to five orders of magnitude larger than the electron excitation cross section and enough for efficient excitation of $^{229m}$Th on the muon beam at the next generation of muon colliders.

# Cross section of the Coulomb excitation of $^{229m}$Th by low energy muons

## Key claim
The inelastic scattering cross section for muons exciting the $^{229m}$Th isomer reaches $10^{-21}$ cm$^2$ at $E\approx10$ eV, which is four to five orders of magnitude larger than the electron excitation cross section, but this result depends on the dominance of the $E2$ multipole contribution and assumes that the Born-approximation scaling of $\sigma_{E2}$ with the square of the scattered particle mass survives in the low-energy regime.

## Method
The cross section for the $^{229}$Th$(\mu^-,\mu^-)^{229m}$Th reaction was calculated using second-order perturbation theory in quantum electrodynamics, with the nonrelativistic limit applied for muon kinetic energies $E=9$–100 eV. The Dirac equation was solved for the muon wave functions in a total potential consisting of a uniformly charged nuclear sphere (radius $R_0=1.2A^{1/3}$ fm) and an electron shell potential obtained from density functional theory via self-consistent double integration of the electron density. The muon matrix elements for $E2$ and $M1$ transitions were evaluated numerically, and the total cross section was summed over partial waves.

## Key numbers
- The excitation cross section for $^{229m}$Th by muons reaches $10^{-21}$ cm$^2$ at $E\approx10$ eV.
- The muon cross section is four to five orders of magnitude larger than the electron excitation cross section from Tkalya (2020).
- The muon-to-electron mass ratio is $m_\mu/m_e \approx 206.77$, giving a Born-approximation scaling factor of $\sigma_{E2}(\mu^-)/\sigma_{E2}(e^-) \approx 4\times10^4$.
- The isomeric state energy is taken as $E_{\rm{is}} = 8.19\pm0.12$ eV (Peik 2020) or $8.10\pm0.17$ eV (Seiferle 2019).
- Two values of the reduced $E2$ transition probability are used: $B_{\rm{W.u.}}(E2;3/2^+\rightarrow 5/2^+) = 11.7$ (average of four experimental measurements) and $B_{\rm{W.u.}}(E2;3/2^+\rightarrow 5/2^+) = 27$ (theoretical calculation by Minkov 2017).
- The estimated rate of isomer excitation in a metallic $^{229}$Th target of thickness 1–10 nm ranges from $dN_{\rm{is}}/dt \approx 1$ s$^{-1}$ (pessimistic) to $10^3$ s$^{-1}$ (optimistic), assuming a muon source current of $j_\mu \approx 4\times10^8$ s$^{-1}$ and a slow-muon fraction $\xi \approx 10^{-4}$–$10^{-5}$.
- The muon matrix element for $E2$ excitation reaches a plateau at $r\approx5R_0 \approx 0.007a_B$, while the electron matrix element forms in the region $r\lesssim 0.1a_B$.

## Context
This work extends the proposal by Tkalya (2020) for exciting $^{229m}$Th via inelastic electron scattering, where the electron cross section reached $10^{-25}$ cm$^2$ at $E\approx10$ eV. The muon cross section is predicted to be four to five orders of magnitude larger due to the $E2$ multipole scaling with the square of the scattered particle mass, as derived from the Born-approximation formulas of Alder (1956) for electric excitation and Tkalya (2012) for magnetic excitation. The result enables a new method for producing $^{229m}$Th using slow muon beams at next-generation muon colliders, and it offers a way to extract the reduced $E2$ transition probability $B_{\rm{W.u.}}(E2;5/2^+\rightarrow3/2^+)$ from scattering data, testing nuclear models such as those of Minkov (2017). The non-resonant nature of the excitation means that precise knowledge of the isomeric energy is not required for the experiment.
