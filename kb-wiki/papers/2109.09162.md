# $r$-process nucleosynthesis from compact binary mergers

> The merger of two neutron stars or of a neutron star and a black hole often result in the ejection of a few percents of a solar mass of matter expanding at high speed in space. Being matter coming from the violent disruption of a neutron star, these ejecta are initially very dense, hot and extremely rich in neutrons. The few available protons form heavy nuclei ("seeds") that absorb the more abundant free neutrons, increasing their size. The neutron density is so high that a substantial number of neutron captures occur before the resulting unstable nuclei can decay toward more stable configurations, converting neutrons into protons. Depending mostly on the initial neutron richness, this mechanism leads to the formation of up to half of the heavy elements that we observe in nature and it is called rapid neutron capture process ("$r$-process"). The prediction of the precise composition of the ejecta requires a detailed knowledge of the properties of very exotic nuclei, that have never been produced in a laboratory. Despite having long been a speculative scenario, nowadays several observational evidences point to compact binary mergers as one of the major sites where heavy elements are formed in the Universe. The most striking one was the detection of a kilonova following the merger of a neutron star binary: the light emitted by this astronomical transient is indeed powered by the radioactive decay of freshly synthesized neutron-rich nuclei and testifies the actual nature of compact binary mergers as cosmic forges.

## Key claim

Compact binary mergers are now considered one of the major astrophysical sites for r-process nucleosynthesis, but the precise composition of the ejecta remains uncertain because it requires detailed knowledge of very exotic nuclei that have never been produced in a laboratory.

## Method

The authors review the mechanisms of matter ejection from binary neutron star (BNS) and neutron star-black hole (BHNS) mergers, including dynamical ejecta (tidal tails and shock-heated material) and disk wind ejecta (neutrino-driven, viscosity-driven, spiral-wave, and magnetically-driven winds). They describe how the electron fraction, entropy, and expansion timescale of each ejecta component determine the conditions for rapid neutron capture nucleosynthesis, and they connect these conditions to observational evidence from the kilonova AT2017gfo and metal-poor star abundances.

## Key numbers

- Dynamical ejecta velocities: approximately 0.1-0.3 c for tidal tails, with shock-heated ejecta peaked around 0.2-0.3 c and a high-velocity tail extending up to 0.6-0.8 c
- Dynamical ejecta mass: ranges between approximately 10^-4 and 10^-2 solar masses, depending on binary properties and nuclear equation of state
- Disk mass in BNS mergers: ranges between 10^-3 and 0.3 solar masses
- Disk wind ejecta velocities: approximately 0.034-0.074 c for recombination-driven winds, and approximately 0.1-0.2 c for spiral-wave and magnetically-driven winds
- Electron fraction in dynamical ejecta: 0.05 to 0.15 for tidal components, with shock-heated ejecta reaching up to approximately 0.4 at high latitudes
- Electron fraction in disk wind ejecta: broad distribution between 0.1 and 0.45, with bulk values between 0.2 and 0.3 for short-lived massive neutron star remnants
- Neutrino luminosity at merger: approximately a few times 10^53 erg s^-1
- Typical neutrino mean energies in the decoupling region: approximately 10 MeV for electron neutrinos, 15 MeV for electron antineutrinos, and 20 MeV for muon and tau neutrinos
- Dynamical timescale of merger: approximately 1.23 ms for a typical total binary mass of 2.7 solar masses and neutron star radius of 12 km
- Accretion timescale for disk evolution: approximately 0.76 s for a remnant mass of 2.6 solar masses and disk radius of 100 km
- BHNS dynamical ejecta mass: up to approximately 0.15 solar masses
- BHNS disk mass: up to approximately 0.5 solar masses

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on r-process nucleosynthesis from compact binary mergers, which began with calculations for BHNS systems by Lattimer (1974, 1976) and for BNS systems by Eichler (1989), with the first BNS merger nucleosynthesis modeling by Symbalisty (1982) and Freiburghaus et al. (1999). The paper synthesizes results from modern numerical relativity simulations (e.g., Bernuzzi et al. 2020, Radice et al. 2018) and connects them to the observational breakthrough of the kilonova AT2017gfo associated with GW170817 (Abbott et al. 2017, Smartt et al. 2017, Kasen et al. 2017). The work enables future studies to refine nucleosynthesis yield predictions by incorporating more accurate nuclear physics inputs for exotic neutron-rich nuclei and to interpret abundance patterns observed in metal-poor stars (Sneden et al. 2008).
