# Diffusive dynamics of critical fluctuations near the QCD critical point

> A quantitatively reliable theoretical description of the dynamics of fluctuations in non-equilibrium is indispensable in the experimental search for the QCD critical point by means of ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions. In this work we consider the fluctuations of the net-baryon density which becomes the slow, critical mode near the critical point. Due to net-baryon number conservation the dynamics is described by the fluid dynamical diffusion equation, which we extend to contain a white noise stochastic current. Including nonlinear couplings from the 3d Ising model universality class in the free energy functional, we solve the fully interacting theory in a finite size system. We observe that purely Gaussian white noise generates non-Gaussian fluctuations, but finite size effects and exact net-baryon number conservation lead to significant deviations from the expected behavior in equilibrated systems. In particular the skewness shows a qualitative deviation from infinite volume expectations. With this benchmark established we study the real-time dynamics of the fluctuations. We recover the expected dynamical scaling behavior and observe retardation effects and the impact of critical slowing down near the pseudo-critical temperature.

# Diffusive dynamics of critical fluctuations near the QCD critical point

## Key claim

Purely Gaussian white noise generates non-Gaussian fluctuations (skewness, kurtosis) in the net-baryon density near the QCD critical point, but finite system size and exact net-baryon number conservation cause the skewness to show a qualitative deviation from infinite-volume expectations, specifically, the scaling behavior of variance and higher-order cumulants with correlation length is drastically reduced compared to leading-order predictions.

## Method

The authors solve a stochastic diffusion equation for the net-baryon density, derived from a Ginzburg-Landau free energy functional that includes nonlinear couplings (cubic, quartic, and sextic terms) matched to the 3d Ising model universality class. The dynamics are evolved in one spatial dimension with a white noise stochastic current satisfying fluctuation-dissipation balance, and the system is treated in a finite volume with exact net-baryon number conservation. Equilibrium benchmarks are established at fixed temperature, then real-time dynamics are studied under a cooling temperature profile mimicking heavy-ion collision expansion.

## Key numbers

- Correlation length scale far from criticality: $\xi_0 = 0.48$ fm
- Critical net-baryon density assumed: $n_c = 1/3$ fm$^{-3}$
- Pseudo-critical temperature: $T_c = 0.15$ GeV
- System size: $L = 20$ fm; observation volume: $V \simeq 1.95$ fm; lattice spacing: $\Delta x = L/256$
- Equilibrium variance scaling: $\sigma_V^2 \propto \xi^{1.30 \pm 0.05}$ (reduced from leading-order expectation $\xi^2$)
- Equilibrium kurtosis scaling: $(\kappa\sigma^2)_V \propto \xi^{2.5 \pm 0.1}$ (reduced from leading-order expectation $\xi^5$)
- Skewness described by competing terms: $\propto \xi^{1.47 \pm 0.05}$ and $\propto \xi^{2.40 \pm 0.05}$ (infinite-volume expectation is $\xi^{2.5}$)
- Dynamical critical exponent: $z = 4 \pm 0.1$ (consistent with model B universality class)
- Time to reach $T_c$ in dynamical evolution: $\tau_c = 2.3$ fm/c
- Dynamical variance reaches about 75% of equilibrium value; dynamical kurtosis reaches about 50% of equilibrium value

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of critical fluctuations in QCD by moving beyond equilibrium assumptions used in earlier studies such as Stephanov (2008) and Asakawa (2009), which predicted scaling of cumulants with powers of the correlation length. It builds on the dynamical universality classification of model B by Hohenberg (1977) and incorporates nonlinear couplings from lattice Ising model studies by Tsypin (1994, 1997). The results enable quantitative dynamical predictions for the Beam Energy Scan at RHIC and provide a benchmark for future studies of non-equilibrium effects in heavy-ion collisions, including retardation and critical slowing down that reduce fluctuation signals compared to equilibrium expectations.
