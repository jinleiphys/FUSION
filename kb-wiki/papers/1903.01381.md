# Gluon Radiation from a Classical Point Particle

> We consider an initially at rest colored particle which is struck by an ultra-relativistic nucleus. The particle is treated classically both with respect to its motion and its color charge. The nucleus is treated as a sheet of colored glass within the context of the Color Glass Condensate framework. We compute both the momentum and coordinates of the struck classical particle and the emitted radiation. Our computations generalize the classic electrodynamics computation of the radiation of an accelerated charged particle to include the radiation induced by the charged gluon field. This latter contribution adds to the classic electrodynamics result and produces a gluon rapidity distribution that is roughly constant as a function of rapidity at rapidities far from the fragmentation region of the struck particles. These computations may form the basis of a first principles treatment for the initial conditions for the evolution of matter produced in the fragmentation region of asymptotically high energy collisions.

## Key claim

The paper generalizes the classic electrodynamics computation of radiation from an accelerated charged particle to the non-abelian case of a color-charged particle struck by a sheet of Color Glass Condensate, finding that the gluon radiation includes a contribution from the charged gluon field that adds to the classical electrodynamics-like result and produces a gluon rapidity distribution roughly constant as a function of rapidity far from the fragmentation region of the struck particle, but only within the kinematic region where the saturation momentum of the target is much smaller than that of the projectile and the produced gluon field is large enough to be treated classically but small enough for linearized equations.

## Method

The authors treat a single color-charged particle classically in both motion and color charge, colliding it with an ultra-relativistic nucleus modeled as a sheet of colored glass in the Color Glass Condensate framework. They solve Wong's equations for the particle's trajectory and compute the gluon radiation field by solving the linearized Yang-Mills equations for the fluctuation field around the strong background field of the nucleus, using a gauge where the background field vanishes after the collision and matching boundary conditions at the sheet. The computation separates the radiation into a part analogous to classical electrodynamics from the instantaneous acceleration and an additional part arising from the interaction of the quark's color field with the background gluon field of the nucleus.

## Key numbers

* The saturation momentum scale is given by \( Q_{\rm sat}^2 = \alpha_s N_c \frac{1}{\pi R_A^2} \frac{dN}{dy} \), where \(\alpha_s\) is the strong coupling constant, \(N_c\) the color factor, and \(dN/dy\) the rapidity density of gluons.
* The gluon rapidity distribution for a static quark accelerated to rapidity \(y_p\) is \( \frac{dN}{dyd^2k_T} = \frac{g^2 T_a T_a}{(2\pi)^3} \frac{1}{2k_T^2} \left( \frac{\sinh(y_p)}{\cosh(y-y_p)\cosh(y)} \right)^2 \), with maximum value \(4\tanh^2(y_p/2)\) at \(y = y_p/2\).
* For large \(y_p\) and small \(y\) (target fragmentation region), the distribution grows like \( \left( \frac{2}{1+e^{-2y}} \right)^2 \).
* The final transverse momentum of the struck quark is estimated as \( p_T^2 \sim Q_{\rm sat}^2 \log(1/(\Lambda_{\rm QCD} r)) \), where \(r\) is the transverse separation scale.

## Context

This work extends the phenomenological ideas of Anishetty, Koehler and McLerran (1980) on baryon stopping and compression in the fragmentation region, reformulating them within the Color Glass Condensate framework of McLerran and Venugopalan. It generalizes earlier computations of gluon production in nucleus-nucleus collisions (Kovchegov, 1996; Krasnitz and Venugopalan, 2000) to the asymmetric case of a single particle target, and provides a first-principles basis for initial conditions of matter evolution in the fragmentation region of asymptotically high energy collisions, which could later be extended to the fully physical situation of nucleus-nucleus collisions.
