# Distinguishing Newly Born Strange Stars from Neutron Stars with g-Mode Oscillations

> The gravity-mode (g-mode) eigenfrequencies of newly born strange quark stars (SQSs) and neutron stars (NSs) are studied. It is found that the eigenfrequencies in SQSs are much lower than those in NSs by almost one order of magnitude, since the components of a SQS are all extremely relativistic particles while nucleons in a NS are non-relativistic. We therefore propose that newly born SQSs can be distinguished from the NSs by detecting the eigenfrequencies of the g-mode pulsations of supernovae cores through gravitational radiation by LIGO-class detectors.

## Key claim

The gravity-mode (g-mode) eigenfrequencies of newly born strange quark stars (SQSs) are lower than those of neutron stars (NSs) by almost one order of magnitude, because SQS constituents are all extremely relativistic particles while nucleons in NSs are non-relativistic; this difference provides a means to distinguish the two types of stars via gravitational wave detection by LIGO-class detectors, but the result depends on the assumption that the SQS equation of state is approximately \(p = \rho/3 + \text{const.}\) and that the nonzero strange quark mass is responsible for the nonvanishing frequencies.

## Method

The authors solved the Newtonian linear oscillation equations for a nonrotating, unmagnetized, fluidic star in the Cowling approximation (neglecting perturbations of the gravitational potential). Equilibrium configurations for newly born NSs were taken from 2D hydrodynamic supernova simulations (Dessart 2006), with nucleon interactions described by relativistic mean field theory; for SQSs, the MIT bag model was used with bag constant \(B^{1/4} = 154.5\ \mathrm{MeV}\) and strange quark mass \(m_s = 150\ \mathrm{MeV}\). The Brunt-Vaisala frequency and the eigenfrequencies for \(l=2\) g-modes were computed by numerical integration from the stellar center to the surface, enforcing a vanishing Lagrangian pressure perturbation at the surface.

## Key numbers

- For a newly born NS at \(t = 100\ \mathrm{ms}\) after core bounce, the \(n=1\), \(l=2\) g-mode eigenfrequency is \(717.6\ \mathrm{Hz}\); at \(t = 200\ \mathrm{ms}\) it is \(774.6\ \mathrm{Hz}\); at \(t = 300\ \mathrm{ms}\) it is \(780.3\ \mathrm{Hz}\).
- For a newly born SQS with the same mass as the NS at each time, the corresponding \(n=1\), \(l=2\) eigenfrequencies are \(82.3\ \mathrm{Hz}\) (at \(100\ \mathrm{ms}\)), \(78.0\ \mathrm{Hz}\) (at \(200\ \mathrm{ms}\)), and \(63.1\ \mathrm{Hz}\) (at \(300\ \mathrm{ms}\)).
- For a SQS with increased entropy per baryon (\(1.5S\)), the \(n=1\) frequencies become \(100.7\ \mathrm{Hz}\), \(105.9\ \mathrm{Hz}\), and \(96.1\ \mathrm{Hz}\) at the same three times.
- For a SQS of mass \(1.4\ M_\odot\), the \(n=1\) frequencies are \(100.2\ \mathrm{Hz}\), \(91.4\ \mathrm{Hz}\), and \(73.0\ \mathrm{Hz}\); for a SQS of mass \(1.68\ M_\odot\), they are \(108.8\ \mathrm{Hz}\), \(100.9\ \mathrm{Hz}\), and \(84.5\ \mathrm{Hz}\).

## Context

This work extends the study of g-mode oscillations in supernova cores (Burrows 2006; Ott 2006) to distinguish SQSs from NSs, building on prior proposals such as differences in radial vibration dissipation (Wang 1984), bulk viscosity (Haensel 1989), spin rate (Madsen 1992), and cooling (Schaab 1997). The results enable future gravitational-wave observations by LIGO and VIRGO to potentially identify the composition of newly formed compact objects, and they highlight that the suppression of g-mode frequencies is a generic consequence of relativistic matter, not dependent on the specific quark matter model.
