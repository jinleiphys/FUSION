# The imaginary part of the heavy-quark potential from real-time Yang-Mills dynamics

> We extract the imaginary part of the heavy-quark potential using classical-statistical simulations of real-time Yang-Mills dynamics in classical thermal equilibrium. The $r$-dependence of the imaginary part of the potential is extracted by measuring the temporal decay of Wilson loops of spatial length $r$. We compare our results to continuum expressions obtained using hard thermal loop theory and to semi-analytic lattice perturbation theory calculations using the hard classical loop formalism. We find that, when plotted as a function of $m_D r$, where $m_D$ is the hard classical loop Debye mass, the imaginary part of the heavy-quark potential shows little sensitivity to the lattice spacing at small $m_D r \lesssim 1$ and agrees well with the semi-analytic hard classical loop result. For large quark-antiquark separations, we quantify the magnitude of the non-perturbative long-range corrections to the imaginary part of the heavy-quark potential. We present our results for a wide range of temperatures, lattice spacings, and lattice volumes. This work sets the stage for extracting the imaginary part of the heavy-quark potential in an expanding non-equilibrium Yang Mills plasma.

## Key claim

The imaginary part of the heavy-quark potential extracted from classical-statistical Yang-Mills simulations shows little sensitivity to the lattice spacing at small separations \(m_D r \lesssim 1\) and agrees well with the semi-analytic hard classical loop (HCL) result, but only when plotted as a function of \(m_D r\) using the HCL Debye mass; the agreement is conditional on the choice of scaling variable and the use of classical rather than quantum thermal equilibrium.

## Method

The authors performed classical-statistical simulations of real-time SU(3) Yang-Mills theory on cubic lattices with sizes up to \(252^3\), initializing fields in a quasi-thermal state and allowing them to self-thermalize dynamically. They extracted the imaginary part of the heavy-quark potential by measuring the temporal exponential decay of Wilson loops of spatial length \(r\) in the thermalized system, and compared the results to continuum hard thermal loop (HTL) theory and to semi-analytic lattice-regularized hard classical loop (HCL) calculations. The temperature \(g^2 T\) and Debye mass \(\hat{m}_D\) were determined self-consistently from chromo-electric field correlation functions.

## Key numbers

* For \(\beta = 2N_c/(g^2 T a)\) in the range \(92 \leq \beta \leq 300\), the directly measured Debye mass \(\hat{m}_D\) is approximately linearly related to the HCL Debye mass by \(\hat{m}_D \approx 1.42\; m_{D}^{\text{HCL}}\).
* The asymptotic large-\(\beta\) limit of the HCL fit parameters are \(A_\infty^{\text{HCL}}(\beta \to \infty) = -0.144\) and \(B^{\text{HCL}}(\beta \to \infty) = 1.55\).
* For a coarse lattice spacing \(a=1\), the relative systematic error in the extracted temperature is \(\Delta T/T < 3\%\).
* The continuum HTL result gives an asymptotic large-\(r\) value of the imaginary part as \(-C_F g^2 T / 4\pi\).

## Context

This work extends the earlier classical-statistical study of Laine (2007) from small lattices (\(12^3\) and \(16^3\)) to much larger volumes up to \(252^3\), and from SU(2) to SU(3) gauge theory, enabling a precise reconstruction of the functional form of the imaginary potential over a wider range of distances. The results enable future extraction of the imaginary part of the heavy-quark potential in expanding non-equilibrium Yang-Mills plasmas, where analytic methods are complicated by the chromo-Weibel instability.
