# Temperature dependence of single-particle properties in nuclear and neutron matter in the Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock model

> The understanding of the interaction of nucleons in nuclear and neutron-rich matter at non-zero temperature is important for a variety of applications ranging from heavy-ion collisions to nuclear astrophysics. In this paper we apply the Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock method along with the Bonn B nucleon-nucleon potential to predict single-particle properties in symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter at finite temperature. It is found that temperature effects are generally small but can be significant at low density and momentum.

## Key claim

Temperature effects on single-particle properties in nuclear and neutron matter are generally small for temperatures up to a few tens of MeV, but can be significant at low density and low momentum, with the single-particle potential tending to become slightly more repulsive with increasing temperature.

## Method

The Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) method was extended to finite temperature by replacing the zero-temperature step-function occupation number with the Fermi-Dirac distribution, while keeping the zero-temperature Bethe-Goldstone expansion terms as the dominant contributions. The Bonn B nucleon-nucleon potential was used, and the single-particle potential, chemical potential, effective mass, and entropy were computed self-consistently for symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter at temperatures from 0 to 30 MeV.

## Context

This work extends previous zero-temperature DBHF calculations by the same author (Sammarruca, 2007, 2008, 2009) to finite temperature, and goes beyond the earlier finite-temperature DBHF work of ter Haar and Malfliet (1987). The results are compared with finite-temperature Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculations by Baldo and Ferreira (1999) and with self-consistent Green's function calculations by Rios et al. (2006, 2009). The predictions enable future studies of thermodynamic quantities and in-medium cross sections relevant for heavy-ion collision dynamics and supernova physics.
