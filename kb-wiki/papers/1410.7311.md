# The in-medium heavy quark potential from quenched and dynamical lattice QCD

> We present our latest results for the the complex valued static heavy-quark potential at finite temperature from lattice QCD. The real and imaginary part of the potential are obtained from the position and width of the lowest lying peak in the spectral function of the Wilson line correlator in Coulomb gauge. Spectral information is extracted from Euclidean time data using a novel Bayesian approach different from the Maximum Entropy Method. In order to extract both the real and imaginary part, we generated anisotropic quenched lattices $32^3\times N_τ$ $(β=7.0,ξ=3.5)$ with $N_τ=24,\ldots,96$, corresponding to $839{\rm MeV} \geq T\geq 210 {\rm MeV}$. For the case of a realistic QCD medium with light u, d and s quarks we use isotropic $48^3\times12$ ASQTAD lattices with $m_l=m_s/20$ provided by the HotQCD collaboration, which span $286 {\rm MeV} \geq T\geq 148{\rm MeV}$. We find a clean transition from a confining to a Debye screened real part and observe that its values lie close to the color singlet free energies in Coulomb gauge. The imaginary part, estimated on quenched lattices, is found to be of the same order of magnitude as in hard-thermal loop (HTL) perturbation theory.

## Key claim

The real part of the in-medium static heavy-quark potential lies close to the color singlet free energies in Coulomb gauge at all temperatures investigated, and the imaginary part is of the same order of magnitude as in hard-thermal loop (HTL) perturbation theory, but the imaginary part is estimated only on quenched lattices and not yet on dynamical lattices.

## Method

The complex-valued static heavy-quark potential at finite temperature was extracted from lattice QCD by reconstructing the spectral function of the Wilson line correlator in Coulomb gauge using a novel Bayesian approach different from the Maximum Entropy Method. The real part was obtained from the position of the lowest-lying peak in the spectral function, and the imaginary part from its width, by fitting a skewed Lorentzian shape. Anisotropic quenched lattices \(32^3\times N_\tau\) with \(\beta=7.0,\xi=3.5\) were generated for temperatures from 210 MeV to 839 MeV, and isotropic \(48^3\times12\) ASQTAD lattices with \(m_l=m_s/20\) from the HotQCD collaboration were used for dynamical QCD with light u, d and s quarks at temperatures from 148 MeV to 286 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Quenched lattices: \(32^3\times N_\tau\) with \(N_\tau=24,\ldots,96\), corresponding to temperatures \(839\ \mathrm{MeV} \geq T \geq 210\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (lattice spacing \(a_s=0.039\ \mathrm{fm}\), \(T_c\approx271\ \mathrm{MeV}\)).
- Dynamical lattices: isotropic \(48^3\times12\) ASQTAD lattices with \(m_l=m_s/20\), spanning temperatures \(286\ \mathrm{MeV} \geq T \geq 148\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (\(T_c\approx174\ \mathrm{MeV}\)).
- For quenched lattices, the real part of the potential was extracted at nine temperatures: 839, 629, 503, 419, 360, 315, 280, 252, and 210 MeV.
- For dynamical lattices, the real part was extracted at seven temperatures: 286, 243, 232, 205, 182, 164, and 148 MeV.
- The spectral reconstruction used \(N_\omega=4000\) frequency points for quenched and \(N_\omega=4600\) for dynamical lattices, with \(N_{\mathrm{hr}}=550\) and \(N_{\mathrm{hr}}=1000\) points respectively to resolve the lowest-lying peak.
- The imaginary part on quenched lattices was found to be of the same order of magnitude as leading-order HTL perturbation theory; no numerical value for the imaginary part is quoted in the text.

## Context

This work extends previous lattice studies of the heavy-quark potential by using a novel Bayesian spectral reconstruction method that surpasses the Maximum Entropy Method (Asakawa, 2000) in accuracy and precision, and by extracting both the real and imaginary parts from the spectral function rather than modeling the potential from color singlet free energies or internal energies as done in earlier work (Nadkarni, 1986). The results enable first-principles input for phenomenological models of heavy quarkonium evolution in a quark-gluon plasma, such as those used to interpret bottomonium yields at RHIC and LHC (Brambilla, 2010; Chatrchyan, 2012; Abelev, 2012). The finding that the real part lies close to the color singlet free energies confirms that earlier modeling approaches were reasonable, while the imaginary part provides a non-perturbative check of HTL perturbation theory (Laine, 2007).
