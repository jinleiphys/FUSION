# Chiral Partners in a Chirally Broken World

> The isovector--vector and the isovector--axial-vector current are related by a chiral transformation. These currents can be called chiral partners at the fundamental level. In a world where chiral symmetry was not broken, the corresponding current-current correlators would show the same spectral information. In the real world chiral symmetry is spontaneously broken. A prominent peak -- the rho-meson -- shows up in the vector spectrum (measured in (e^+ e^-)-collisions and tau-decays). On the other hand, in the axial-vector spectrum a broad bump appears -- the a_1-meson (also accessible in tau-decays). It is tempting to call rho and a_1 chiral partners at the hadronic level. Strong indications are brought forward that these ``chiral partners'' do not only differ in mass but even in their nature: The rho-meson appears dominantly as a quark-antiquark state with small modifications from an attractive pion-pion interaction. The a_1-meson, on the other hand, can be understood as a meson-molecule state mainly formed by the attractive interaction between pion and rho-meson. A key issue here is that the meson-meson interactions are fixed by chiral symmetry breaking. It is demonstrated that one can understand the vector and the axial-vector spectrum very well within this interpretation. It is also shown that the opposite cases, namely rho as a pion-pion molecule or a_1 as a quark-antiquark state lead to less satisfying results. Finally speculations on possible in-medium changes of hadron properties are presented.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the rho-meson and a1-meson, often called chiral partners at the hadronic level, differ not only in mass but in their internal nature: the rho-meson is dominantly a quark-antiquark state, while the a1-meson can be understood as a meson-molecule state formed by the attractive interaction between pion and rho-meson. This conclusion is conditional on the choice of physically reasonable renormalization scales (in the GeV range rather than TeV range) for the loop calculations.

## Method
The authors study the vector and axial-vector current-current correlators using a Bethe-Salpeter equation approach with kernels taken from lowest-order chiral interactions. For the vector channel, they analyze the electromagnetic form factor of the pion in the time-like region; for the axial-vector channel, they analyze tau decay to three pions. In each channel, they compare two scenarios: one with only final-state rescattering (meson-meson molecule) and one with an additional preformed resonance (quark-antiquark state), with the strength of final-state interactions fixed by chiral symmetry breaking.

## Key numbers
- The rho-meson appears as a peak at about 770 MeV in the vector spectrum
- The a1-meson appears as a broad bump at about 1250 MeV in the axial-vector spectrum
- For the rho-meson, a scenario with only pion-pion rescattering fails to describe data when renormalization points are set to the pion mass (physically reasonable); only when renormalization points are raised to 1.1 TeV or 10 TeV (physically unreasonable) can a peak be produced
- For the a1-meson, the first scenario (only rescattering) describes the data with one free parameter (the renormalization point) in the GeV range, not the TeV range
- In a simple in-medium model, increasing the rho-meson width by 50 MeV or 100 MeV causes the dynamically generated a1 to also melt

## Context
This work extends the approach of Lutz and Kolomeitsev (2004) who suggested that axial-vector mesons can be dynamically generated from final-state interactions, and of Oset and collaborators who studied the a1 as a dynamically generated state. It contrasts with the traditional view that both rho and a1 are simple quark-antiquark states. The paper enables future studies of in-medium changes of hadron properties and chiral restoration, suggesting that the rho-meson may dissolve in hadronic matter as a precursor to deconfinement.
