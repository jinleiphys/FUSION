# Effective Field Theory for Cold Atoms

> Effective Field Theory (EFT) provides a powerful framework that exploits a separation of scales in physical systems to perform systematically improvable, model-independent calculations. Particularly interesting are few-body systems with short-range interactions and large two-body scattering length. Such systems display remarkable universal features. In systems with more than two particles, a three-body force with limit cycle behavior is required for consistent renormalization already at leading order. We will review this EFT and some of its applications in the physics of cold atoms. Recent extensions of this approach to the four-body system and N-boson droplets in two spatial dimensions will also be discussed.

## Key claim

Effective Field Theory (EFT) provides a systematically improvable, model-independent framework for few-body systems with short-range interactions and large two-body scattering length, but the abstract glosses over the fact that in three spatial dimensions a three-body force with limit cycle behavior is required for consistent renormalization already at leading order, introducing a three-body parameter that cannot be predicted by the EFT and must be determined from a three-body observable.

## Method

The author reviews the EFT for nonrelativistic bosonic atoms with large S-wave scattering length, starting from a Lagrangian with two- and three-body contact interactions. For the three-body system in three dimensions, the atom-dimer scattering amplitude is obtained by solving an integral equation with an ultraviolet cutoff, where the three-body force strength runs on a limit cycle as a function of the cutoff. For the two-dimensional N-boson droplet, the author uses a variational method with a running coupling constant derived from renormalization group analysis, equivalent to a Hartree calculation in the large-N limit.

## Key numbers

- The transcendental number \(s_0 = 1.00624\) governs the limit cycle period of the three-body force in three dimensions
- When the ultraviolet cutoff \(\Lambda\) is increased by a factor of \(\exp(\pi/s_0) \approx 22.7\), the three-body force returns to its original value
- For N-boson droplets in two dimensions with large N, the size ratio \(R_{N+1}/R_N \approx 0.3417\) and the binding energy ratio \(B_{N+1}/B_N \approx 8.567\)
- The three-body ground state energy in two dimensions for zero-range potential is \(B_3^{(0)} = 16.522688(1) B_2\) and the excited state is \(B_3^{(1)} = 1.2704091(1) B_2\)
- The four-body ground state energy in two dimensions for zero-range potential is \(B_4^{(0)} = 197.3(1) B_2\) and the excited state is \(B_4^{(1)} = 25.5(1) B_2\)
- The critical number of bosons in two dimensions beyond which universality is lost is \(N_{\rm crit} \approx 0.931 \ln(R_2/l) + {\cal O}(1)\)

## Context

This work extends the universal predictions for the three-body system first derived by Efimov (1971), confirming the Efimov effect where infinitely many three-body bound states accumulate at threshold as the scattering length goes to infinity. The EFT approach provides a systematic framework that explains scaling relations such as the Phillips line in nuclear physics and the Tjon line correlating triton and alpha particle binding energies. The results enable future applications to halo systems in nuclear physics and coexisting condensates of atoms, dimers, and trimers in atomic physics, as well as Fermi systems with three or more spin states.
