# Neutron-pair structure in the continuum spectrum of $^{26}$O

> The structure of $^{26}$O is currently being investigated on both theoretical and experimental fronts. It is well established that it is unbound and the resonance parameters are fairly well-known. The theoretical analysis may involved two- and three-body interactions, as well as correlations with the continuum spectrum of energy. In order to properly assess the structure of the ground and excited states, it is imperative to include a large single particle representation with the right asymptotic behavior. The purpose of this work is to provide details of the single particle continuum configurations of the ground and excited $0^+$ states. We use a large complex energy single particle basis, formed by resonances and complex energy scattering states, the so called Berggren basis, and a separable interaction, which is convenient to solve in a large model space. Three $0^+$ states were found in the complex energy plane. Changes of the resonant parameters, i.e. energy and width, were analyzed as a function of strength of the residual interaction. It is shown how a subtle difference in the interaction could change the unbound character of $^{26}$O into a Borromean nucleus. Only one of the two excited states can be considered as a candidate for a physical meaningful resonance. The calculated occupation probabilities are in agreement with other theoretical approaches although the calculated half live is three-order of magnitude smaller than the experimental one.

# Neutron-pair structure in the continuum spectrum of $^{26}$O

## Key claim
The authors find that a subtle difference in the residual interaction strength could change the unbound character of $^{26}$O into a Borromean nucleus, but only one of the two excited $0^+$ states can be considered a physically meaningful resonance; the calculated half-life is three orders of magnitude smaller than the experimental value, indicating missing correlations.

## Method
The authors use a three-body shell model Hamiltonian in the Berggren basis (complex-energy single-particle representation including bound states, resonances, and scattering states) to diagonalize the $^{24}$O plus two neutrons system. A Woods-Saxon plus spin-orbit mean-field is fitted to reproduce the $^{25}$O resonance parameters, and a separable residual interaction (surface or volume form factor) is used to solve the dispersion relation for the $0^+$ states, tracking pole trajectories in the complex energy plane as a function of the interaction strength.

## Key numbers
- Ground state energy of $^{26}$O: $E_{\text{gs}} = 0.01842 - i\,0.1635 \times 10^{-6}$ MeV at the physical strength $G_{\text{Exp}} = 6.06$ MeV
- First excited $0^+$ state: $E_{0^+_2} = 1.434 - i\,1.6568$ MeV (discarded as unphysical due to large width)
- Second excited $0^+$ state: $E_{0^+_3} = 3.4560 - i\,0.3317$ MeV (physically meaningful resonance)
- Calculated half-life of ground state: $T_{1/2} = 8.766 \times 10^{-3}$ ps, compared to experimental $4.5 \pm 3$ ps (Kohley 2013)
- Ground state occupation probabilities: $\sum (d_{3/2})^2 = 73.7\%$, $\sum (f_{7/2})^2 = 19.0\%$, $\sum (p_{3/2})^2 = 4.2\%$
- Mean-field parameters: $V_0 = 44.1$ MeV, $V_{so} = 22.84$ MeV fm, diffuseness $a = 0.72$ fm, reduced radius $r_0 = 1.25$ fm

## Context
This work extends the theoretical study of $^{26}$O, an unbound two-neutron emitter beyond the oxygen drip line at $^{24}$O. It builds on prior calculations using the Continuum Shell Model (Volya 2005, 2006), Green function formalism (Hagino 2014, 2016), three-body hyperspherical harmonics (Grigorenko 2015), Gamow Shell Model (Fossez 2017), and ab-initio Gamow Shell Model (Hu 2020). The authors specifically address missing ingredients in these references: the evolution of resonances in the complex energy plane, continuum-continuum contributions up to the $0g$-shell, and a criterion to distinguish physically significant $0^+$ excitations from spurious ones. The results enable future work with more realistic or phenomenological interactions adjusted for the continuum, as suggested by Yaganathen (2017).
