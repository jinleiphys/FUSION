# Investigating event-shape methods in the search for the chiral magnetic effect in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> The Chiral Magnetic Effect (CME) is a phenomenon in which electric charge is separated by a strong magnetic field from local domains of chirality imbalance and parity violation in quantum chromodynamics (QCD). The CME-sensitive observable, charge-dependent three-point azimuthal correlator $Δγ$, is contaminated by a major physics background proportional to the particle's elliptic flow anisotropy $v_2$. Event-shape engineering (ESE) binning events in dynamical fluctuations of $v_2$ and event-shape selection (ESS) binning events in statistical fluctuations of $v_2$ are two methods to search for the CME by projecting $Δγ$ to the measured anisotropy $v_2=0$ intercept. We conduct a systematic study of these two methods using physics models as well as toy model simulations. It is observed that the ESE method fulfills the general premise of measuring the CME but is statistically hungry. It is found that the intercept from the ESS method depends on the details of the event content, such as the mixtures of background-contributing sources, because of statistical fluctuations of intertwining variables used in the method, and is thus not practically useful or clean to measure the CME.

## Key claim

The Event-Shape Selection (ESS) method's intercept, obtained by projecting the charge-dependent correlator Δγ to measured v₂=0, depends on the detailed event content such as mixtures of background-contributing sources due to statistical fluctuations of intertwining variables, making it not practically useful or clean for measuring the Chiral Magnetic Effect (CME), whereas the Event-Shape Engineering (ESE) method fulfills the general premise of measuring the CME but requires significantly larger statistics.

## Method

The authors conducted a systematic study using four physics models (AVFD, AMPT, EPOS4, HYDJET++) and two toy model versions to simulate heavy ion collisions, focusing on the 30-40% centrality range for Au+Au collisions at √s_NN=200 GeV and 27 GeV. They compared the ESE method, which bins events by the elliptic flow vector magnitude q₂ calculated from particles different from the particles of interest (POIs), with the ESS method, which bins events by the normalized pair q̂² calculated from the same POIs used for the physics measurements. The charge-dependent three-point azimuthal correlator Δγ was analyzed as a function of measured v₂ in each event class, and the intercept at v₂=0 was examined for each method.

## Key numbers

- AVFD model simulations used three axial charge density values: n₅/s = 0, 0.1, and 0.2 for Au+Au collisions at √s_NN=200 GeV
- AMPT simulations used a total parton scattering cross section of 3 mb with a hadronic interaction cutoff time of 30 fm/c
- Toy Model I included primordial pions and two resonances (K_S and ρ⁰) with resonance mass generated according to Breit-Wigner distribution, using a 40% v₂ fluctuation about the parameterized mean v₂(pT)
- The magnetic field in AVFD had a typical lifetime comparable to the initial time of hydrodynamic evolution of approximately 0.6 fm/c
- EPOS4 simulations used version 4.0.0 for Au+Au collisions at √s_NN=200 and 27 GeV
- HYDJET++ simulations used version 2.4 for Au+Au collisions at √s_NN=200 and 27 GeV

## Context

This work extends the investigation of event-shape methods for CME searches previously studied by the STAR collaboration (Adamczyk et al., 2013) using the event-shape (ES) method, and by Xu et al. (2023) who proposed the ESS method using AVFD model simulations. The study systematically compares ESE and ESS methods across multiple physics models to elucidate what the projected intercept entails from each method, finding that the ESS method suffers from biases due to statistical fluctuations of intertwining variables that prevent clean CME measurement, while the ESE method is statistically hungry but conceptually sound. This enables future experimental searches to focus on the ESE method despite its statistical requirements, and clarifies that the ESS method's intercept cannot be reliably interpreted as a CME signal.
