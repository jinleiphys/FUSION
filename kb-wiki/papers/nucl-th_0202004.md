# Absorbing Boundary Condition Approach for Breakup Reactions of Halo Nuclei

> Application of the absorbing boundary condition is discussed to analyse breakup reactions of weakly bound nuclei. The key ingredient is an introduction of the absorbing potential outside the physical area which simulates the outgoing boundary condition approximately. The scattering problem is then recasted into the Schroedinger like equation with a source term in the interaction region and with the vanishing boundary condition at the boundary. We demonstrate usefulness of the method taking a few examples. Deuteron breakup reactions are examined comparing present results with those by the continuum-discretized coupled-channel method. We next discuss the breakup reactions of single-neutron halo nucleus, 11Be.

# Absorbing Boundary Condition Approach for Breakup Reactions of Halo Nuclei

## Key claim

The absorbing boundary condition (ABC) approach provides a convenient and flexible description of breakup reactions that avoids introducing discretized continuum channels, but its trade-off is a heavy computational cost to calculate the wave function in real-space, requiring iterative solvers for large sparse linear systems.

## Method

The authors replace the infinitesimal positive imaginary term in the Green's function with a finite, space-dependent absorbing potential placed outside the physical interaction region, recasting the scattering problem into an inhomogeneous Schrödinger-like equation with vanishing boundary conditions. For three-body breakup reactions (projectile = core + neutron on a target), the equation is solved in partial-wave expansion with radial discretization on a grid, using the Bi-CG (Conjugate Gradient) iterative method with diagonal preconditioning to handle the resulting large, sparse, complex symmetric linear system.

## Key numbers

- For deuteron + $^{58}$Ni at $E_d = 80$ MeV: radial grid spacing 0.2 fm for $R$ and 0.5 fm for $r$, radial region up to 50 fm, absorbing potentials placed beyond 25 fm with strengths $W_R = 50$ MeV and $W_r = 20$ MeV; matrix dimension $N_{\text{dim}} = 100,000$ (250 × 100 × 4 channels)
- For $^{11}$Be + $^{12}$C: $^{10}$Be-n binding energy set to 0.504 MeV (2s orbital); radial grid 0.2 fm for $R$ and 0.5 fm for $r$; radial region up to 30 fm ($R$) and 50 fm ($r$); absorbing potential placed 20–30 fm ($R$) and 25–50 fm ($r$); neutron-$^{12}$C angular momenta up to $l=3$; matrix size approximately 240,000
- For $^{10}$Be-$^{12}$C optical potential test at $E_{\text{lab}} = 300$ MeV: Woods-Saxon parameters $V=123$ MeV, $r_V=0.75$ fm, $a_V=0.80$ fm, $W=65$ MeV, $r_W=0.78$ fm, $a_W=0.80$ fm; absorber outside $R=20$ fm with $\Delta R=10$ fm; tested $W_{abs}=20, 100, 2500$ MeV
- Absorber parameter condition: $20 \frac{E^{1/2}}{\Delta R \sqrt{8m}} < W_{abs} < \frac{1}{10} \Delta R \sqrt{8m} E^{3/2}$

## Context

This work extends the absorbing boundary condition approach, originally developed for chemical reactions (Seideman and Miller, 1992), to nuclear breakup reactions of weakly bound halo nuclei. It provides an alternative to the continuum-discretized coupled-channel (CDCC) method (Yahiro et al., 1982; CDCC collaboration), which requires constructing artificial discretized continuum states and delicate convergence checks. The ABC approach reproduces CDCC results for deuteron breakup on $^{58}$Ni at 80 MeV, and enables calculations for $^{11}$Be + $^{12}$C reactions below 50 MeV/A where the eikonal approximation (used in earlier work by Bertsch et al., 1989; Otsuka et al., 1992; and others) becomes inaccurate and substantially underestimates breakup cross sections.
