# EMMI Rapid Reaction Task Force Meeting on 'Quark Matter in Compact Star'

> The recent measurement of two solar mass pulsars has initiated an intense discussion on its impact on our understanding of the high-density matter in the cores of neutron stars. A task force meeting was held from October 7-10, 2013 at the Frankfurt Institute for Advanced Studies to address the presence of quark matter in these massive stars. During this meeting, the recent oservational astrophysical data and heavy-ion data was reviewed. The possibility of pure quark stars, hybrid stars and the nature of the QCD phase transition were discussed and their observational signals delineated.

## Key claim

The meeting concluded that the existence of two-solar-mass pulsars does not rule out quark matter in neutron star cores, but it does require a stiff quark matter equation of state with repulsive interactions to reconcile the high mass constraint with the possibility of a quark phase transition. The key hedge is that while quark matter remains viable, the timing of the phase transition (before or after deleptonization) and the specific model parameters determine whether hybrid stars or pure quark stars can reach the observed masses.

## Method

The task force reviewed observational astrophysical data from pulsar mass measurements (Shapiro delay and white dwarf companion spectroscopy), heavy-ion collision data, and theoretical models of dense QCD matter. They compared equations of state from the bag model, Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, perturbative QCD, and nuclear theory approaches, evaluating their ability to produce compact stars with masses at or above two solar masses. The participants assessed both Maxwell and Gibbs constructions for the hadron-quark phase transition, and considered dynamical scenarios including combustion, deflagration, and detonation processes for converting neutron stars to hybrid or quark stars.

## Key numbers

- Pulsar PSR J1614-2230 mass: 1.97 ± 0.04 solar masses, measured via Shapiro delay with companion white dwarf mass 0.500 ± 0.006 solar masses and binary inclination 89.17° ± 0.02°
- Pulsar PSR J0348+0432 mass: 1.97-2.05 solar masses at 68.27% confidence, or 1.90-2.18 solar masses at 99.73% confidence, from white dwarf companion modeling
- Lower limit on radius of a 1.76 solar mass compact star: R > 11 km within 3-sigma error, from pulse phase-resolved X-ray spectroscopy of PSR J0437-4715
- Hybrid star masses up to 2.1-2.4 solar masses found in NJL model calculations with repulsive vector interactions, with radii between 12 and 13 km
- Kepler period for a 1.45 solar mass strange star: approximately 0.55 to 0.8 milliseconds, compared to about 1 millisecond for neutron stars of the same mass
- Electric field at surface of non-superconducting strange quark stars: approximately 10^18 V/cm; for color-superconducting strange matter: exceeding 10^19 V/cm
- Energy density of electric fields alters strange quark star masses at the 15% level and radii at the 5% level
- Luminosity of e+e- pairs from hot strange star surface at 10^11 K: approximately 3 × 10^51 erg/s
- Energy released in hadron-to-quark phase transition: up to 10^52 erg
- Unburned hadronic material remaining after conversion: approximately 0.5 solar masses

## Context

This meeting report extends the discussion initiated by the Hyperons2012 meeting in Warsaw (November 2012), which focused on hyperon-induced softening of neutron star equations of state. The present task force addresses whether quark matter can coexist with the two-solar-mass constraint from Demorest (2010) and Antoniadis (2013), building on earlier work by Glendenning (1992) on hybrid stars and mixed phases. The findings enable future observational tests using the FAST and SKA telescopes for pulsar mass measurements, advanced LIGO and VIRGO for gravitational wave detection from compact binary mergers, and NICER and LOFT for precision radius measurements of compact stars.
