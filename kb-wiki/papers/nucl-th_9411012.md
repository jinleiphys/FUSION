# Nuclear Multifragmentation Critical Exponents

> We show that the critical exponents of nuclear multi-fragmentation have not been determined conclusively yet.

## Key claim

The critical exponents of nuclear multifragmentation have not been determined conclusively yet, because the analysis used by the EoS collaboration (Gilkes et al., 1994) to extract them from data on 1 A GeV gold on carbon is not sufficient to support the claim, as demonstrated by applying the same analysis to a percolation model with known critical exponents.

## Method

The authors used a percolation model of nuclear fragmentation with a bond breaking parameter p and known critical exponents (beta = 0.41, gamma = 1.8) to simulate the fragmentation of a system with Z = 79 charges, generating 10,000 events for statistics comparable to the experimental data. They then applied the same moment analysis used by the EoS collaboration (Gilkes et al., 1994), plotting ln(Z_max) vs. ln(|m - m_c|) and ln(M_2) vs. ln(|m - m_c|) with a cut at m_c = 26, to extract apparent critical exponents. They also simulated the effect of pre-equilibrium contamination by adding 10 pre-equilibrium particles to a 69-charge equilibrated system and repeating the analysis.

## Key numbers

- In the percolation model, the true critical exponents are beta = 0.41 and gamma = 1.8.
- Using the EoS analysis method on the percolation model with m_c = 26, the extracted value of gamma was approximately 1.2 to 1.4, significantly lower than the true value of 1.8.
- The best fit to the percolation model simulation of ln(Z_max) vs. ln(|m - m_c|) gave an apparent beta = 0.55, compared to the true value of 0.41.
- When 10 pre-equilibrium particles were added to a 69-charge system, the extracted apparent beta became 0.29, which coincidentally matches the value extracted from the experimental data.
- The pre-equilibrium contamination in cascade simulations for the system used by the EoS collaboration (Gilkes et al., 1994) varies from 0 to possibly 15 charges.

## Context

This comment paper directly challenges the conclusions of the EoS collaboration (Gilkes et al., 1994), who claimed to have determined the critical exponents gamma, beta, and tau for finite nuclei from fragmentation of 1 A GeV gold on carbon. The authors use the percolation model of nuclear fragmentation (Bauer, 1985; 1986; 1988) to demonstrate that the analysis method used by the EoS collaboration fails to recover the known critical exponents of the model, and that pre-equilibrium contamination further distorts the extracted values. This work calls for more careful treatment of the relationship between multiplicity and the control parameter, and for experimental removal or modeling of pre-equilibrium particles before critical exponents can be reliably determined.
