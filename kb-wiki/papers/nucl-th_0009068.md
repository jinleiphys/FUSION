# Fourth Order Diffusion Monte Carlo Algorithms for Solving Quantum Many-Body Problems

> By decomposing the important sampled imaginary time Schrödinger evolution operator to fourth order with positive coefficients, we derived a number of distinct fourth order Diffusion Monte Carlo algorithms. These sophisticated algorithms require higher derivatives of the drift velocity and local energy and are more complicated to program. However, they allowed very large time steps to be used, converged faster with lesser correlations, and virtually eliminated the step size error. We demonstrated the effectiveness of these quartic algorithms by solving for the ground state properties of bulk liquid helium.

## Key claim
By factorizing the imaginary-time Schrödinger evolution operator to fourth order with positive coefficients, the authors derived Diffusion Monte Carlo algorithms that allow very large time steps, converge faster with less correlation, and virtually eliminate step-size error, but these algorithms require higher derivatives of the drift velocity and local energy and are more complicated to program.

## Method
The authors decomposed the important-sampled imaginary-time evolution operator \( e^{-\epsilon(T+D+E_L)} \) into products of exponentials of \( T \) (diffusion), \( D \) (drift/continuity), and \( E_L \) (local energy/rate) operators, using Suzuki-style positive-coefficient factorizations that retain double-commutator corrections. The resulting fourth-order Langevin algorithm (for the Fokker-Planck part) was combined with weight updates from the local energy to produce three distinct fourth-order DMC algorithms (DMC4, DMC4a, DMC4b), each requiring solving deterministic drift trajectories via fourth-order Runge-Kutta and sampling non-uniform Gaussian random walks.

## Key numbers
- For the 3-D harmonic oscillator with trial parameter \( \alpha = 1.8 \), the fourth-order algorithm (DMC4) showed quartic convergence in step size \( \epsilon \), verified analytically via Mathematica and by Monte Carlo simulation.
- For the 3-D Morse potential with \( D_e = 50 \), \( r_0 = 1 \), \( \alpha = 10 \), exact ground state energy \( -12.5 \), and variational energy \( -11.1774 \), DMC4 again showed quartic convergence and a nearly flat step-size error curve.
- For bulk liquid helium (Aziz potential, \( \hbar^2/m = 12.12 \, \AA^2 K \)), DMC4 extrapolated to a ground-state energy of \( -7.114(2) \, K \), DMC2a to \( -7.111(2) \, K \), in agreement with Boronat and Casulleras’s second-order DMC result of \( -7.121(10) \, K \).
- DMC4 allowed time steps roughly ten times larger than second-order algorithms (up to \( \epsilon = 0.2 \)), with the radial density distribution \( g(r) \) virtually unchanged at \( \epsilon = 0.1 \) and \( \epsilon = 0.2 \), in excellent agreement with the experimental distribution of Svensson et al.
- The correlation time for the ground-state energy of liquid helium was roughly \( \Delta t \approx 1.5 \) (in imaginary-time units), independent of step size.
- DMC4 required about 5 times the computational time of the second-order algorithm (51 hours on a single Origin 2000 processor); DMC4a took 46 hours; DMC4b took 36 hours but could not stabilize weights beyond \( \epsilon \approx 0.05 \) for liquid helium.

## Context
This work extends the operator-factorization approach of Chin (1990) for second-order DMC algorithms and overcomes Suzuki’s (1991) proof that beyond second order, simple factorizations of \( e^{\epsilon(A+B)} \) require negative coefficients (impossible for diffusion). The authors build on Chin (1997) and Suzuki (1996) positive-coefficient fourth-order factorizations that retain double commutators. The resulting algorithms enable solving for ground-state properties of bulk liquid helium with much larger time steps and reduced correlations, and they open the door to systematic categorization of higher-order DMC factorizations and optimization of commutator placement.
