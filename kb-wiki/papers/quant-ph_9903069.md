# Small Violations of Statistics

> There are two motivations to consider statistics that are neither Bose nor Fermi: (1) to extend the framework of quantum theory and of quantum field theory, and (2) to provide a quantitative measure of possible violations of statistics. After reviewing tests of statistics for various particles, and types of statistics that are neither Bose nor Fermi, I discuss quons, particles characterized by the parameter $q$, which permit a smooth interpolation between Bose and Fermi statistics; $q=1$ gives bosons, $q=-1$ gives fermions. The new result of this talk is work by Robert C. Hilborn and myself that gives a heuristic argument for an extension of conservation of statistics to quons with trilinear couplings of the form $\bar{f}fb$, where $f$ is fermion-like and $b$ is boson-like. We showed that $q_f^2=q_b$. In particular, we related the bound on $q_γ$ for photons to the bound on $q_e$ for electrons, allowing the very precise bound for electrons to be carried over to photons. An extension of our argument suggests that all particles are fermions or bosons to high precision.

## Key claim

Greenberg and Hilborn provide a heuristic argument that for quons (particles characterized by a parameter \(q\) that interpolates between Bose and Fermi statistics) with trilinear couplings of the form \(\bar{f}fb\), the relation \(q_f^2 = q_b\) holds, allowing the very precise bound on violations of Fermi statistics for electrons to be carried over to photons; the argument suggests that all particles are fermions or bosons to high precision, but the derivation is heuristic and requires refinement.

## Method

The authors consider quon fields obeying the algebra \(a_k a_l^\dagger - q a_l^\dagger a_k = \delta_{kl}\) and construct quonic Grassmann numbers as external sources for the photon quon field. They then replace the quonic Grassmann numbers with bilinear products of electron creation and annihilation operators, using the approximate relation \(b(k+p) b(r) \approx q_e b(r) b(k+p)\) on Fock-like states, to derive the connection \(q_\gamma = q_e^2\) between the photon and electron \(q\)-parameters.

## Key numbers

- The bound on violations of Fermi statistics for electrons from the Ramberg-Snow experiment is \(v_F \leq 1.7 \times 10^{-26}\), where \(v_F\) is the coefficient of the anomalous component of the two-electron density matrix.
- The bound on violations of Bose statistics for photons from preliminary results is \(v_B \leq 10 \times 10^{-7}\).
- The bound on violations of Fermi statistics for electrons from atomic spectroscopy is \(10^{-6}\) to \(10^{-8}\).
- The Goldhaber experiment found any violation of the exclusion principle for electrons must be less than \(5\%\).
- The bound on violations of Bose statistics for oxygen nuclei from forbidden bands in the \(O_2\) spectrum is \(v_B \leq 5 \times 10^{-7}\).
- The probability of finding two \(^{16}\text{O}\) nuclei in carbon dioxide in a permutation antisymmetric state is less than \(5 \times 10^{-9}\).

## Context

This work extends the quon formalism developed by Greenberg (1991) and others, which provides the only theory that allows parametrization of small violations of statistics, in contrast to parastatistics (Green, 1953) which gives gross violations and the Ignatiev-Kuzmin model (1987) which was shown by Govorkov to have negative-probability states. The result enables high-precision bounds on violations of Bose statistics for photons to be inferred from the extremely precise electron bounds, though the derivation is heuristic and a refined derivation is needed; the quon theory is consistent for nonrelativistic theories but does not satisfy spacelike commutativity of observables in the relativistic case.
