# Strangeness and Metastable Neutron Stars: What Might have Happened to SN1987A

> Immediately after they are born, neutron stars are characterized by an entropy per baryon of order unity and by the presence of trapped neutrinos. If the only hadrons in the star are nucleons, these effects slightly reduce the maximum mass relative to cold, catalyzed matter. However, if negatively charged particles in the form of hyperons, a kaon condensate, or quarks are also present, these effects result in an increase in the maximum mass of $\sim 0.2{\rm M}_{\odot}$ compared to that of a cold, neutrino-free star. This could lead to the delayed formation of a black hole; such a scenario is consistent with our present knowledge of SN1987A.

## Key claim

If negatively charged strange particles (hyperons, kaon condensate, or quarks) are present in a newborn neutron star, the maximum mass can be about 0.2 solar masses larger when neutrinos are trapped than after they diffuse out, potentially causing a delayed collapse to a black hole; this scenario is consistent with the observed neutrino signal from SN1987A but cannot be confirmed due to limited counting statistics.

## Method

The authors used a relativistic field theoretical model (Walecka model) in which baryons interact via exchange of sigma, rho, and omega mesons, with coupling constants chosen to reproduce nuclear matter properties. They calculated the equation of state and maximum gravitational masses for four compositions (nucleons only, nucleons plus hyperons, nucleons plus kaon condensate, and quark matter) under two conditions: cold neutrino-free matter and hot matter with trapped neutrinos (entropy per baryon S=0 or S=2). The composition was determined by enforcing charge neutrality and beta equilibrium with appropriate chemical potentials for all species.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass for nucleons-only matter without trapped neutrinos: 2.01 solar masses; with trapped neutrinos (S=0): 1.94 solar masses
- Maximum mass for matter with hyperons without trapped neutrinos: 1.54 solar masses; with trapped neutrinos (S=0): 1.77 solar masses (an increase of about 0.2 solar masses)
- Maximum mass for matter with kaon condensate without trapped neutrinos: 1.83 solar masses; with trapped neutrinos (S=0): 1.93 solar masses
- The deleptonization time scale is estimated to be 10-15 seconds
- The universal binding energy relation for neutron stars: B.E. = (0.065 +/- 0.01)(M_B/solar mass)^2 solar masses, where M_B is the baryonic mass
- The estimated gravitational mass of the SN1987A remnant is in the range 1.40-1.56 solar masses (Thielemann et al. and Bethe and Brown)
- The observed range of neutron star gravitational masses is 1.34-1.44 solar masses, with PSR1913+16 at 1.44 solar masses

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of protoneutron star evolution (Burrows and Lattimer, 1986; Keil and Janka, 1995) and the possibility of metastable neutron stars (Prakash et al., 1995). It directly addresses the puzzle of why no neutron star is readily apparent in the SN1987A remnant despite a neutrino signal lasting at least 12 seconds. The paper shows that if strange matter appears, the maximum mass can drop by about 0.2 solar masses during deleptonization, enabling black hole formation on the 10-15 second timescale. This scenario would imply a population of relatively low-mass black holes, one candidate being the compact object in 4U1700-37 (Brown and Weingartner, 1994). Future detections from galactic supernovae in upgraded detectors (SNO, Super Kamiokande) could distinguish this scenario by observing the cessation or changes in the neutrino signal.
