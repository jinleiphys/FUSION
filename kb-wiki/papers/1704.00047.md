# Numerical calculation of the decay widths, the decay constants, and the decay energy spectra of the resonances of the delta-shell potential

> We express the resonant energies of the delta-shell potential in terms of the Lambert $W$ function, and we calculate their decay widths and decay constants. The ensuing numerical results strengthen the interpretation of such decay widths and constants as a way to quantify the coupling between a resonance and the continuum. We calculate explicitly the decay energy spectrum of the resonances of the delta-shell potential, and we show numerically that the lineshape of such spectrum is not the same as, and can be very different from, the Breit-Wigner (Lorentzian) distribution. We argue that the standard Golden Rule cannot describe the interference of two resonances, and we show how to describe such interference by way of the decay energy spectrum of two resonant states.

## Key claim

The paper numerically demonstrates that the decay width \(\overline{\Gamma}\) and decay constant \(\Gamma\) derived from resonant (Gamow) states are not equal to the pole width \(\gr\) and unity respectively, contradicting a result from second-order perturbation theory; the authors argue their formalism is sound because the numerical results show \(\overline{\Gamma} \neq \gr\) and \(\Gamma \neq 1\) for the delta-shell potential.

## Method

The authors solved the transcendental equation for the resonant energies of the delta-shell potential (zero angular momentum) exactly using the Lambert \(W\) function, then computed the decay widths \(\overline{\Gamma}\) and decay constants \(\Gamma\) via integrals involving the Lorentzian factor and the matrix element of the potential between the resonant state and continuum states. They also calculated the decay energy spectrum \(\frac{\mathrm{d}P(E)}{\mathrm{d}E}\) as the normalized differential decay constant and compared its lineshape to the Breit-Wigner (Lorentzian) distribution for several resonances at different potential strengths \(\lambda\).

## Key numbers

- For \(\lambda = 100\), the first resonance has \(\overline{\Gamma} = 0.0002\) (in units of \(\hbar^2/2ma^2\)) and \(\Gamma = 0.0006\), while the pole width \(\gr = 0.0001\) (Table in Appendix, inferred from text context).
- For \(\lambda = 10\), the third resonance shows an asymmetric decay energy spectrum with a small enhancement at the \(E=0\) threshold (Fig. 2 in Appendix).
- For \(\lambda = 100\), the third resonance is "very sharp" with \(\gr \approx 10^{-3}\) (in units of \(\hbar^2/2ma^2\)), and \(\overline{\Gamma}_{\mathrm{sharp}}\) is "very close" to \(\gr\) while the exact \(\overline{\Gamma}\) is not.
- For bound states (\(\lambda = -10, -100\)), \(\overline{\Gamma} = 0\) and \(\Gamma = 1\); for virtual states (\(\lambda = -0.5\)), \(\overline{\Gamma} = 0\) but \(\Gamma \neq 1\).

## Context

This work extends the formalism of de la Madrid (2015) (Ref. [NPA15]) by providing numerical validation for the delta-shell potential, a standard toy model used previously by Mondragon (2000) for exceptional points, by Gaston (2011, 2012, 2013, 2014, 2017) for decay and time evolution studies, and by others for resonance theory. The results contradict a second-order perturbation theory result from Duncan (2012) that would imply \(\overline{\Gamma} = \gr\) and \(\Gamma = 1\); the numerical demonstration resolves this conflict in favor of the Gamow-state formalism. The decay energy spectrum formula enables future fitting of experimental resonant lineshapes beyond the Breit-Wigner approximation, including threshold effects and interference of two resonances.
