# Out of Equilibrium Thermal Field Theories - Finite Time after Switching on the Interaction - Wigner Transforms of Projected Functions

> We study out of equilibrium thermal field theories with switching on the interaction occurring at finite time using the Wigner transforms (in relative space-time) of two-point functions. For two-point functions we define the concept of projected function: it is zero if any of times refers to the time before switching on the interaction, otherwise it depends only on the relative coordinates. This definition includes bare propagators, one-loop self-energies, etc. For the infinite-average-time limit of the Wigner transforms of projected functions we define the analyticity assumptions: (1) The function of energy is analytic above (below) the real axis. (2) The function goes to zero as the absolute value of energy approaches infinity in the upper (lower) semiplane. Without use of the gradient expansion, we obtain the convolution product of projected functions. We sum the Schwinger-Dyson series in closed form. In the calculation of the Keldysh component (both, resummed and single self-energy insertion approximation) contributions appear which are not the Wigner transforms of projected functions, signaling the limitations of the method. In the Feynman diagrams there is no explicit energy conservation at vertices, there is an overall energy-smearing factor taking care of the uncertainty relations. The relation between the theories with the Keldysh time path and with the finite time path enables one to rederive the results, such as the cancellation of pinching, collinear, and infrared singularities, hard thermal loop resummation, etc.

## Key claim

The paper develops a formalism for out-of-equilibrium thermal field theories where the interaction is switched on at a finite time, using Wigner transforms of projected two-point functions; the key hedge is that the method works only as long as the two-point functions involved satisfy specific analyticity assumptions (analytic above or below the real axis and vanishing at infinity in the corresponding half-plane), and that contributions which are not Wigner transforms of projected functions appear in the Keldysh component, signaling limitations of the approach.

## Method

The author defines "projected functions" as two-point functions that are zero if either time coordinate refers to a time before the interaction is switched on (set to \(t_i=0\)), and otherwise depend only on the relative coordinates. The Wigner transform of such a projected function is obtained by applying a projection operator \(P_{X_0}\) (a sinc kernel) to the infinite-average-time limit of the Wigner transform. Without using a gradient expansion, the convolution product of two projected functions is derived in closed form, and the Schwinger-Dyson series is summed for the retarded, advanced, and Keldysh components.

## Key numbers

* The projection operator is defined as \(P_{X_0}(p_0,p'_0) = \frac{1}{\pi} \Theta(X_0) \frac{\sin(2X_0(p_0-p'_0))}{p_0-p'_0}\), and in the limit \(X_0 \to \infty\) it becomes \(\delta(p_0-p'_0)\).
* For a simple pole contribution \(a/(p_0-\bar p_0)\) with \(\operatorname{Im}\bar p_0 < 0\), the projected function at finite \(X_0\) is \(a \frac{1 - e^{-2iX_0(p_0-\bar p_0)\operatorname{sgn}(\operatorname{Im}\bar p_0)}}{p_0-\bar p_0}\), which for large \(X_0\) decays as \(e^{-2X_0|\operatorname{Im}\bar p_0|}\).
* The retarded free scalar propagator at finite \(X_0\) is given by \(G_{R,X_0}^0(p) = G_{R,\infty}^0(p_0) \left(1 - (\cos 2X_0\omega_p - i\frac{p_0}{\omega_p}\sin 2X_0\omega_p) e^{2iX_0(p_0+i\epsilon)}\right)\), where \(\omega_p = (\vec p^2 + m^2)^{1/2}\).
* The convolution product of two projected functions satisfying the analyticity assumptions reduces to \(C_{X_0}(p_0,\vec p) = \int dp_{01} P_{X_0}(p_0,p_{01}) A_{\infty}(p_{01},\vec p) B_{\infty}(p_{01},\vec p)\), i.e., a simple product smeared by the projection operator.

## Context

This work extends the out-of-equilibrium thermal field theory approach of Keldysh (1964) and Schwinger (1961) to the case of a finite switching-on time, addressing a limitation of earlier work by Carrington (1998) and Defu (1998) on hard thermal loop resummation and the cancellation of pinching singularities by Altherr (1993), Niegawa (1994), and others, which assumed infinite time and ignored gradient corrections. The method enables the study of early-time dynamics in systems such as heavy-ion collisions, where the system evolves far from equilibrium and cannot be treated by extrapolation from infinite-time results; it also establishes a relation between finite-time and Keldysh-path theories, allowing rederivation of known results (cancellation of pinching, collinear, and infrared singularities, HTL resummation) under the analyticity assumptions.
