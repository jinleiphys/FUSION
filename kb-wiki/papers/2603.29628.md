# A systematic approach to Covariance matrix formulation in charged particle activation experiments

> This work presents a detailed covariance and correlation matrix analysis for experimentally measured cross sections obtained using the activation technique. Both statistical and systematic contributions to the covariance matrix were explicitly calculated using sensitivity coefficients. The detector efficiency was determined by refitting standard source data with an exponential function, and the associated covariance matrix of the fitted parameters was propagated to estimate the uncertainty in efficiency at the relevant $γ$-ray energy. The cross sections and the corresponding experimental parameters, such as beam flux, target thickness, $γ$-ray intensity, and decay corrections, were taken from previously published measurements and are used here for the purpose of illustrating the covariance formalism. The resulting covariance and correlation matrices provide a comprehensive representation of uncertainties and their interdependencies. This formalism demonstrates the importance of including correlated uncertainties for reliable interpretation and comparison of experimental cross section data.

## Key claim

This work presents a step-by-step Jacobian-based formalism for constructing covariance and correlation matrices in charged-particle activation cross-section measurements, demonstrating that systematic uncertainties from common normalization parameters (detector efficiency, beam flux, target thickness, decay data) introduce non-negligible correlations between data points that are ignored by the traditional quadratic summation of uncertainties.

## Method

The author derives sensitivity coefficients (partial derivatives) for each parameter in the activation cross-section formula (Eq. 2.2), including the decay constant which has a more complex dependence through irradiation, cooling, and counting time terms. The covariance matrix is constructed as the sum of a diagonal statistical component (from counting statistics) and a systematic component computed via the Jacobian propagation formula \(V = J \eta J^T\), assuming input parameters are mutually uncorrelated. Detector efficiency uncertainty is propagated from a three-parameter exponential fit to calibration source data using the fitted parameter covariance matrix. The formalism is illustrated using four previously published cross-section data points for the \(^{144}\mathrm{Sm}(p,\gamma)\) reaction at 2.59–4.11 MeV.

## Key numbers

* Detector efficiency at \(E_\gamma = 893.73\) keV: \(\varepsilon = 0.03127\) with relative uncertainty \(3.59\%\), derived from refitting calibration data with parameters \(\varepsilon_c = 0.002205\), \(\varepsilon_0 = 0.13897\), \(E_0 = 329.427\) and their covariance matrix.
* Cross-section values with total uncertainties: \(129.24 \pm 52.55\ \mu\)b at 4.11 MeV, \(30.00 \pm 12.20\ \mu\)b at 3.68 MeV, \(3.114 \pm 1.265\ \mu\)b at 3.17 MeV, \(0.242 \pm 0.098\ \mu\)b at 2.59 MeV.
* Off-diagonal correlation coefficients between all four energy points are approximately \(3.26\%\)–\(3.27\%\), reflecting the systematic normalization correlations.
* Decay constant sensitivity coefficients \(S_{\lambda,i}\) range from \(-0.36\) to \(-0.90\) for the four data points, calculated using \(\lambda = 1.35\times10^{-6}\ \mathrm{s}^{-1}\).
* Target thickness uncertainty: \(40\%\) relative; beam flux uncertainty: \(5\%\) relative; \(\gamma\)-ray intensity uncertainty: \(4\%\) relative (\(66 \pm 4\%\) for the 893.73 keV line).

## Context

This work extends the covariance methodology emphasized by Smith (1987) and the IAEA/OECD evaluated nuclear data libraries (Chadwick, 2011) to the specific case of charged-particle activation measurements, where normalization parameters are fully correlated across energies. The formalism is illustrated using data from Bar (2024) for the \(^{144}\mathrm{Sm}(p,\gamma)\) reaction, which originally reported cross sections without covariance information. The approach enables proper statistical weighting of experimental data in global model fits and reaction rate calculations for nuclear astrophysics, and can be extended to other activation-based measurements; cases using monitor foils for beam flux determination require additional treatment as described by Otuka (2017).
