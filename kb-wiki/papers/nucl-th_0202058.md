# On "the authentic damping mechanism" of the phonon damping model. II

> This article continues a discussion raised in previous publications (LANL preprint server, nucl-th/0202006 and nucl-th/0202020). I try to convince my opponents that general arguments are not "my case" and may be applied to their model.

## Key claim

The phonon damping model (PDM) in its PDM-1 version does not provide a physically distinct damping mechanism for giant resonances; its apparent success in fitting experimental data is a trivial consequence of using the Breit-Wigner form as an ad hoc input and adjusting three free parameters (position, width, and amplitude) to the observable being described.

## Method

The author re-examines the PDM-1 equations and strength functions, showing that the approximate form \(S_q'(E)\) used in PDM calculations is obtained by expanding the polarization operator near a solution of the secular equation and then extrapolating far from that solution. He repeats PDM calculations for \(^{16}\)O and \(^{18}\)O using the same Hamiltonian and parameters as in Dang et al. (2001), employing a realistic \(1p1h\) spectrum from a Hartree-Fock calculation with SGII Skyrme forces, and compares the exact and approximate strength functions.

## Key numbers

- In \(^{16}\)O, the phonon couples to all \(1p1h\) configurations with an equal strength of \(F_1 = 1.025\) MeV.
- In \(^{18}\)O, the coupling strength for the \([1p_{1/2} 2d_{5/2}]_\nu\) configuration at 8.2 MeV is enhanced to \(F_1' = 40 F_1 = 41\) MeV.
- The exact PDM solutions of the secular equation for \(^{18}\)O yield \(E_{GDR} = -19.7\), 50.6, and 55.0 MeV, very different from the reported value of 22.5 MeV in Dang et al. (2001).
- In the simplified two-level limit, the PDM eigenstates for \(^{18}\)O appear at -19.0 and 49.4 MeV, with phonon strength distributed as 40% and 60%, respectively.

## Context

This paper continues a debate initiated in Ponomarev (2002) and Dang et al. (2002) over whether the PDM-1 version (Dang et al., 2001 and earlier works) provides a physically meaningful damping mechanism for giant resonances. The author argues that the model's fits are not physically informative because they rely on three adjustable parameters and an ad hoc Breit-Wigner shape, and that the model fails to reproduce the correct eigenenergies when the exact secular equation is solved. The work enables a critical reassessment of claims that the PDM describes pygmy dipole resonances in neutron-rich nuclei, and it independently confirms the conclusion of Harakeh et al. (2001) that the PDM overestimates low-energy dipole strength.
