# Electromagnetic transition form factors and Dalitz decays of hyperons

> Dalitz decays of a hyperon resonance to a ground-state hyperon and an electron-positron pair can give access to some information about the composite structure of hyperons. We present expressions for the multi-differential decay rates in terms of general transition form factors for spin-parity combinations J^P = 1/2^+/-, 3/2^+/- of the hyperon resonance. Even if the spin of the initial hyperon resonance is not measured, the self-analyzing weak decay of the "final" ground-state hyperon contains information about the relative phase between combinations of transition form factors. This relative phase is non-vanishing because of the unstable nature of the hyperon resonance. If all form factor combinations in the differential decay formulae are replaced by their respective values at the photon point, one obtains a QED type approximation, which might be interpreted as characterizing hypothetical hyperons with point-like structure. We compare the QED type approximation to a more realistic form factor scenario for the lowest-lying singly-strange hyperon resonances. In this way we explore which accuracy in the measurements of the differential Dalitz decay rates is required in order to distinguish the composite-structure case from the pointlike case. Based on the QED type approximation we obtain as a by-product a rough prediction for the ratio between the Dalitz decay width and the corresponding photon decay width.

## Key claim
The paper provides model-independent expressions for Dalitz decay rates of hyperon resonances in terms of general transition form factors, showing that even without measuring the initial resonance spin, the self-analyzing weak decay of the ground-state hyperon can reveal a relative phase between form factor combinations that is non-zero only because of the unstable nature of the resonance. The authors compare a QED-type approximation (representing point-like hyperons) with a more realistic form factor scenario to determine the experimental accuracy needed to distinguish composite structure from point-like structure.

## Method
The authors constructed constraint-free transition form factors for spin-parity combinations J^P = 1/2^±, 3/2^± of hyperon resonances using a Bardeen-Tung-Tarrach (BTT) formalism, then derived helicity amplitudes and differential decay widths for two-body radiative decays, three-body Dalitz decays (Y* → Y e+e-), and four-body decays including the subsequent weak decay Y → πN. They applied this framework to the specific cases of Λ(1405) with J^P = 1/2^- and Λ(1520) with J^P = 3/2^- decaying to ground-state Λ, comparing a QED-type approximation (form factors set to photon-point values) with a more realistic form factor scenario incorporating transition radii.

## Key numbers
- The radiative decay width for spin-3/2 to spin-1/2 transitions (opposite parity) is given by Γ₂ = e² [(m_Y*+m_Y)²(m_Y*²-m_Y²)/(96π m_Y*³)] × [3|H_-(0)|² + |H_+(0)|²]
- The radiative decay width for spin-1/2 to spin-1/2 transitions (opposite parity) is Γ₂ = e² |F̃_+(0)|² (m_Y*+m_Y)² (m_Y*²-m_Y²)/(8π m_Y*³)
- The kinematical constraint for spin-3/2 opposite-parity transitions requires H_+((m_Y*-m_Y)²) = H_0((m_Y*-m_Y)²) = H_-((m_Y*-m_Y)²)
- The kinematical constraint for spin-1/2 opposite-parity transitions requires F̃_+((m_Y*-m_Y)²) = F̃_0((m_Y*-m_Y)²)
- The lower limit of the dilepton invariant mass squared q² is (2m_e)², where m_e is the electron mass
- The upper limit of q² is (m_Y*-m_Y)² for Dalitz decays

## Context
This work extends the formalism developed by Körner (1976) and Perotti (2018) for e+e- → Y*Y̅ to a different kinematical regime, namely hyperon Dalitz decays. It builds on previous studies of hyperon transitions by Granados (2017), Junker (2019), and Husek (2019) for states like Σ(1385) and Σ⁰. The paper provides a framework for upcoming measurements at GSI/FAIR by HADES and PANDA (Ramstein, 2019), enabling experimental extraction of transition form factor information that can distinguish between quark-model descriptions and hadronic-molecule interpretations of resonances like Λ(1405) and Λ(1520).
