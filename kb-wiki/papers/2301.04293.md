# Chiral uncertainties in ab initio nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering

> The effective interaction between a nucleon and a nucleus is one of the most important ingredients for reaction theories. Theoretical formulations were introduced early by Feshbach and Watson, and efforts of deriving and computing those `optical potentials' in a microscopic fashion have a long tradition. However, only recently the leading order term in the Watson multiple scattering approach could be calculated fully {\it ab initio}, meaning that the same nucleon-nucleon (NN) interaction enters both the structure as well as the reaction pieces on equal footing. This allows the uncertainties from the underlying chiral effective NN interaction to be systematically explored in nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering observables. In this contribution the main ingredients for arriving at the {\it ab initio} leading order of the effective nucleon-nucleus interaction in the Watson approach will be reviewed. Concentrating on one specific chiral NN interaction from the LENPIC collaboration and light nuclei with a 0$^+$ ground state, the leading order nucleon-nucleus interaction is calculated using up to the third chiral order (N2LO) in the nucleon-nucleon potential, and elastic scattering observables are extracted. Then pointwise as well as correlated uncertainty quantification is used for the estimation of the chiral truncation error. Elastic scattering observables for $^4$He, $^{12}$C, and $^{16}$O for between 65 and 200 MeV projectile energy will be analyzed.

## Key claim

The leading-order term of the Watson multiple scattering approach can now be calculated fully *ab initio*, meaning the same chiral nucleon-nucleon (NN) interaction enters both the nuclear structure and reaction calculations on equal footing, allowing systematic exploration of chiral truncation uncertainties in nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering observables for light nuclei with 0+ ground states at projectile energies between 65 and 200 MeV.

## Method

The authors compute the leading-order nucleon-nucleus effective interaction using the spectator expansion of the Watson optical potential, folding chiral NN amplitudes (parameterized as Wolfenstein amplitudes) with nonlocal, translationally-invariant one-body scalar and spin-projected densities obtained from no-core shell model (NCSM) calculations. They use the EKM chiral NN interaction with a semi-local coordinate space regulator of R = 1 fm and a breakdown scale of 600 MeV, calculating elastic scattering observables up to N2LO (third chiral order) for 4He, 12C, and 16O. Pointwise and correlated uncertainty quantification is applied to estimate chiral truncation errors, using an expansion parameter Q = max(p_NA, q)/Lambda_b where p_NA is the center-of-mass momentum in the nucleon-nucleus system and q is the momentum transfer.

## Key numbers

- Projectile energy range: 65 to 200 MeV laboratory kinetic energy
- Breakdown scale of the chiral interaction: Lambda_b = 600 MeV
- Regulator for the EKM chiral NN interaction: R = 1 fm (semi-local coordinate space)
- Expansion parameter defined as Q = (1/600 MeV) * max(M_pi, p_NA, q), where M_pi is the pion mass
- For 4He, nearly converged binding energies and proton radii are obtained, agreeing with Yakubovsky calculations using the same NN potential to within estimated numerical uncertainties
- The KMT prescription agrees with the exact Watson description for elastic proton scattering from 8He at 71 MeV laboratory kinetic energy up to momentum transfers of about 2 fm^-1

## Context

This work extends the long tradition of microscopic optical potential calculations (Feshbach, 1958; Watson, 1953; Kerman, McManus, and Thaler, 1959) by incorporating modern chiral EFT interactions from the LENPIC collaboration and *ab initio* nuclear structure from NCSM and symmetry-adapted NCSM (Launey, 2016; Dytrych, 2020). It enables systematic uncertainty quantification for nucleon-nucleus scattering similar to what has been done for NN scattering (Furnstahl, 2015; Melendez, 2017, 2019) and nucleon-deuteron scattering (Epelbaum, 2019), and provides a foundation for including three-nucleon forces and higher-order terms in the spectator expansion.
