# Neutrino-induced neutron spallation and supernova r-process nucleosynthesis

> In order to explore the consequences of the neutrino irradiation for the supernova r-process nucleosynthesis, we calculate the rates of charged-current and neutral-current neutrino reactions on neutron-rich heavy nuclei, and estimate the average number of neutrons emitted in the resulting spallation. Our results suggest that charged-current $ν_e$ captures can be important in breaking through the waiting-point nuclei at N=50 and 82, while still allowing the formation of abundance peaks. Furthermore, after the r-process freezes out, there appear to be distinctive neutral-current and charged-current postprocessing effects. A subtraction of the neutrino postprocessing effects from the observed solar r-process abundance distribution shows that two mass regions, A=124-126 and 183-187, are inordinately sensitive to neutrino postprocessing effects. This imposes very stringent bounds on the freeze-out radii and dynamic timescales governing the r-process. Moreover, we find that the abundance patterns within these mass windows are entirely consistent with synthesis by neutrino interactions. This provides a strong argument that the r-process must occur in the intense neutrino flux provided by a core-collapse supernova.

## Key claim

The paper argues that neutrino-induced neutron spallation from charged-current and neutral-current reactions on neutron-rich heavy nuclei produces distinctive, observable modifications to the solar r-process abundance pattern, particularly in the mass windows A=124-126 and 183-187, which provides strong evidence that the r-process must occur in the intense neutrino flux of a core-collapse supernova, but the authors hedge that this conclusion depends on the assumption that the observed sensitivity in those mass windows is not an accidental result.

## Method

The authors calculated charged-current νe capture rates on waiting-point nuclei at N=50, 82, and 126 using a schematic model based on experimental systematics for Fermi and Gamow-Teller strength distributions, with the GT strength approximated as a Gaussian centered near the isobaric analog state with a width of 5 MeV. For neutral-current reactions, they employed a generalized Goldhaber-Teller model satisfying the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn sum rule for first-forbidden transitions and used continuum random phase approximation (CRPA) and shell-model calculations as benchmarks for representative nuclei. The subsequent neutron emission from highly-excited daughter nuclei was estimated using statistical Hauser-Feshbach techniques with a neutron evaporation code, and the resulting postprocessing effects were evaluated by subtracting the predicted neutrino-induced modifications from the observed solar r-process abundance distribution.

## Key numbers

- Charged-current νe capture rates on waiting-point nuclei at r=100 km with Lν=10^51 erg/s: average rates of approximately 5.2 s^-1 for N=50, 5.7 s^-1 for N=82, and 8.5 s^-1 for N=126
- Average number of neutrons emitted after each νe capture on waiting-point nuclei: approximately 2-5 neutrons, depending on the shell closure
- For the N=50 peak (A~80), the condition for local β-flow equilibrium to hold within 20% at freeze-out requires (Lν,FO/10^51 erg/s) x (100 km/rFO)^2 ≤ 0.12
- For the N=82 peak, the corresponding limit is 0.83; for the N=126 peak, it is 0.37
- The ratio of neutrino-induced charge flow to β-decay charge flow at N=50 is ≤ 1.2 when τdyn << τν, and at N=82 it can be as high as ≤ 4.0 under the same condition
- The neutral-current cross section for heavy-flavor neutrinos scales approximately as ⟨σνNC⟩ ≈ σ0 x A, with σ0 ≈ 10^-42 cm^2 for ⟨Eν⟩ ≈ 25 MeV
- The two mass regions most sensitive to neutrino postprocessing are A=124-126 and A=183-187

## Context

This work extends the earlier studies of Fuller and Meyer (1995) and McLaughlin and Fuller (1995) by considering the competition between β-decay and charged-current neutrino reactions in the context of a dynamic, expanding bubble, rather than assuming static conditions. It also builds on the earlier estimate of Domogatskii and Nadezhin (1980) for charged-current neutrino spallation yields, but provides a more comprehensive treatment including neutral-current effects. The results impose stringent bounds on freeze-out radii and dynamic timescales in supernova r-process models, particularly the hot bubble model of Woosley et al. (1994), and suggest that explicit incorporation of neutrino reactions into r-process network simulations is needed to understand the observed abundance pattern.
