# Specialists' Meeting on the Nucleon-Nucleus Optical Model up to 200 MeV: Conclusions and Recommendations

## Key claim
The meeting concluded that a satisfactory medium-energy nucleon-nucleus optical potential has not yet been achieved, and that it is time to stop extrapolating low-energy optical-model potentials to medium energies, instead treating them as low-energy boundaries for new developments. The abstract glosses over the fact that the Dirac versus Schrodinger approach remains an open question, with Schrodinger proponents arguing that Dirac agreement may be fortuitous due to less detailed investigations of medium, non-local, and off-shell effects.

## Method
The meeting gathered experimentalists and theorists working on the medium-energy nucleon-nucleus optical model potential to assess present understanding and determine next steps, driven by fundamental physics goals and the need for accurate cross-section libraries for applied programs. Five general approaches were discussed: Dirac and relativistic Schrodinger phenomenological potentials, dispersive potentials, semi-microscopic potentials, microscopic potentials, and coupled-channel potentials. Conclusions and recommendations were drawn from technical presentations, subsequent discussions, and roundtables.

## Key numbers
- Recommended benchmark targets and energies: {40Ca, 208Pb; 100, 200, 300 MeV}
- Proton total reaction cross section measurements needed at accuracies better than 5%
- The JLM folding model was extended upwards to 200 MeV by refitting the imaginary part
- Presentations addressed the model at energies as high as 1 GeV, with applied programs requiring understanding up to 2 GeV
- A calculation showed strong sensitivity of the pi-pi s-wave scattering phase shift (T=0) to small ~2 MeV changes in the pion mass

## Context
This meeting summary extends the work of Finlay et al. on neutron total cross sections, Lhenry on proton total reaction cross sections, and Madland on Dirac phenomenology. It builds on the Jeukene-Lejeune-Mahaux (JLM) folding model extended by Bauge and Delaroche, and the dispersive approach extended to deformed nuclei by Romain and Delaroche. The recommendations enable future development of a global medium-energy nucleon-nucleus optical potential, with the next assessment proposed for 1999 plus or minus 1 year.
