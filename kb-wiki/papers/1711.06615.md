# Neutron skins and neutron stars in the multi-messenger era

> The historical first detection of a binary neutron star merger by the LIGO-Virgo collaboration [B. P. Abbott et al. Phys. Rev. Lett. 119, 161101 (2017)] is providing fundamental new insights into the astrophysical site for the $r$-process and on the nature of dense matter. A set of realistic models of the equation of state (EOS) that yield an accurate description of the properties of finite nuclei, support neutron stars of two solar masses, and provide a Lorentz covariant extrapolation to dense matter are used to confront its predictions against tidal polarizabilities extracted from the gravitational-wave data. Given the sensitivity of the gravitational-wave signal to the underlying EOS, limits on the tidal polarizability inferred from the observation translate into constraints on the neutron-star radius. Based on these constraints, models that predict a stiff symmetry energy, and thus large stellar radii, can be ruled out. Indeed, we deduce an upper limit on the radius of a $1.4\,M_{\odot}$ neutron star of $R_{\star}^{1.4}\!<\!13.76\,{\rm km}$. Given the sensitivity of the neutron-skin thickness of ${}^{208}$Pb to the symmetry energy, albeit at a lower density, we infer a corresponding upper limit of about $R_{\rm skin}^{208}\!\lesssim\!0.25\,{\rm fm}$. However, if the upcoming PREX-II experiment measures a significantly thicker skin, this may be evidence of a softening of the symmetry energy at high densities---likely indicative of a phase transition in the interior of neutron stars.

## Key claim

The gravitational-wave signal from the binary neutron star merger GW170817 imposes an upper limit on the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star of 13.76 km, which translates into an upper limit on the neutron-skin thickness of lead-208 of about 0.25 fm; however, if the upcoming PREX-II experiment measures a significantly thicker skin, this may indicate a softening of the symmetry energy at high densities that could be evidence of a phase transition in neutron star interiors.

## Method

The authors used a set of relativistic mean-field (RMF) models of the equation of state, including the FSUGold2 family and nine other representative RMF models, that accurately describe finite nuclei properties, support two-solar-mass neutron stars, and provide Lorentz covariant extrapolations to dense matter. They computed the dimensionless tidal polarizability for 1.4 solar mass neutron stars from each model and compared these predictions against the 90% confidence limit of Lambda <= 800 extracted from the LIGO-Virgo gravitational-wave data. The correlations between tidal polarizability, neutron star radius, and neutron-skin thickness of lead-208 were then used to translate the gravitational-wave constraints into limits on nuclear observables.

## Key numbers

- Upper limit on the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: R_star^1.4 < 13.76 km, deduced from the Lambda_star^1.4 <= 800 limit from GW170817.
- Upper limit on the neutron-skin thickness of lead-208: R_skin^208 <= 0.25 fm, inferred from the same gravitational-wave constraint.
- Lower limit on the tidal polarizability of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: Lambda_star^1.4 >= 490, derived from the PREX lower bound on R_skin^208 of about 0.15 fm.
- The tidal polarizability of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star falls within the range: 490 <= Lambda_star^1.4 <= 800.
- The PREX collaboration measured R_skin^208 = 0.33 +0.16 -0.18 fm.
- The stiffest model that survives the Lambda_star^1.4 <= 800 constraint (TAMUC-FSUa) gives: R_star^1.4 = 13.6 km, R_skin^208 = 0.25 fm, and L = 82.5 MeV.
- A lower limit on the radius of a 1.6 solar mass neutron star of R_star^1.6 = 10.68 +0.15 -0.04 km was obtained by Bauswein et al. (2017) under the assumption that the merger did not result in prompt collapse.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the first binary neutron star merger detection by the LIGO-Virgo collaboration (Abbott et al. 2017) by connecting gravitational-wave constraints on tidal polarizability to laboratory nuclear observables, specifically the neutron-skin thickness of lead-208 measured by the PREX experiment (Abrahamyan et al. 2012, Horowitz et al. 2012). The results enable future work by providing testable predictions for the upcoming PREX-II experiment at Jefferson Laboratory, which aims to measure the neutron-skin thickness of lead-208 with 0.06 fm sensitivity, and by suggesting that a thick neutron skin combined with small neutron star radii could indicate a phase transition in neutron star interiors.
