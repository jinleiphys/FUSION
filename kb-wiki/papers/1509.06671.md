# Quantum Simulations of Nuclei and Nuclear Pasta with the Multi-resolution Adaptive Numerical Environment for Scientific Simulations

> Neutron star and supernova matter at densities just below the nuclear matter saturation density is expected to form a lattice of exotic shapes. These so-called nuclear pasta phases are caused by Coulomb frustration. Their elastic and transport properties are believed to play an important role for thermal and magnetic field evolution, rotation and oscillation of neutron stars. Furthermore, they can impact neutrino opacities in core-collapse supernovae. In this work, we present proof-of-principle 3D Skyrme Hartree-Fock (SHF) simulations of nuclear pasta with the Multi-resolution ADaptive Numerical Environment for Scientific Simulations (MADNESS). We perform benchmark studies of $^{16} \mathrm{O}$, $^{208} \mathrm{Pb}$ and $^{238} \mathrm{U}$ nuclear ground states and calculate binding energies via 3D SHF simulations. Results are compared with experimentally measured binding energies as well as with theoretically predicted values from an established SHF code. The nuclear pasta simulation is initialized in the so-called waffle geometry as obtained by the Indiana University Molecular Dynamics (IUMD) code. The size of the unit cell is 24\:fm with an average density of about $ρ= 0.05 \:\mathrm{fm}^{-3}$, proton fraction of $Y_p = 0.3$ and temperature of $T=0\:$MeV. Our calculations reproduce the binding energies and shapes of light and heavy nuclei with different geometries. For the pasta simulation, we find that the final geometry is very similar to the initial waffle state. In the present pasta calculations spin-orbit forces are not included but will be added in the future. Within the MADNESS framework, we can successfully perform calculations of inhomogeneous nuclear matter. By using pasta configurations from IUMD it is possible to explore different geometries and test the impact of self-consistent calculations on the latter.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the MADNESS framework can perform self-consistent 3D Skyrme Hartree-Fock simulations of nuclear pasta, but the present calculations do not include spin-orbit forces for the pasta configuration, and the final geometry remains very similar to the initial waffle state rather than evolving to a different shape.

## Method

The authors solve the Skyrme Hartree-Fock equations in 3D using the MADNESS framework, which employs adaptive pseudo-spectral approximations based on multi-wavelet bases with guaranteed precision. They rewrite the Hartree-Fock equations into Lippmann-Schwinger form and solve them iteratively via convolutions with Green's functions for the bound state Helmholtz equation. For nuclear pasta, the simulation is initialized with a waffle geometry obtained from the Indiana University Molecular Dynamics (IUMD) code in a 24 fm unit cell with periodic boundary conditions and the Jellium approximation for electrons.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy per nucleon for $^{16}\mathrm{O}$: -7.288 MeV (M-SHF, free boundary conditions, $\epsilon = 10^{-7}$), compared to -7.288 MeV (Sky3D, 0.25 fm grid)
- Binding energy per nucleon for $^{208}\mathrm{Pb}$: -7.867 MeV (M-SHF, free boundary conditions, $\epsilon = 10^{-7}$), compared to -7.867 MeV (Sky3D, 0.5 fm grid)
- Binding energy per nucleon for $^{238}\mathrm{U}$: -7.894 MeV (M-SHF, free boundary conditions, $\epsilon = 10^{-7}$), compared to -7.894 MeV (Sky3D, 0.5 fm grid)
- Nuclear pasta unit cell size: 24 fm with average density $\rho = 0.05\:\mathrm{fm}^{-3}$, proton fraction $Y_p = 0.3$, and temperature $T = 0\:\mathrm{MeV}$
- Gaussian smoothing parameter $\sigma = 0.25\:\mathrm{fm}$ used for initial iterations

## Context

This work extends previous Skyrme Hartree-Fock studies of nuclear pasta (Magierski 2002, Goegelein 2007, Newton 2009, Pais 2012) by implementing the MADNESS framework for 3D simulations with adaptive resolution. The approach contrasts with molecular dynamics studies (Horowitz 2008, Schneider 2013, 2014) that can simulate larger systems but use schematic two-body potentials rather than self-consistent quantum calculations. The proof-of-principle pasta simulation uses initial configurations from the IUMD code and enables future studies of different pasta geometries with self-consistent calculations including spin-orbit forces.
