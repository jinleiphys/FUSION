# Negative-parity Nucleon Resonance in the QCD Sum Rule

> The negative-parity baryons are studied by a novel approach in the QCD sum rule. It is found that the parity of the ground-state nucleon is determined by the sign of the quark condensate. We predict the mass of negative-parity nucleon.

# Negative-parity Nucleon Resonance in the QCD Sum Rule

## Key claim

The parity of the ground-state nucleon is determined by the sign of the quark condensate, with a negative quark condensate producing a heavier negative-parity nucleon than the positive-parity nucleon. The authors predict the mass of the negative-parity nucleon using a novel separation technique in the QCD sum rule, but the result depends on the choice of the mixing parameter t in the nucleon interpolating current, with t ≈ 0.8 giving the strongest coupling to the negative-parity state.

## Method

The authors use the "old-fashioned" correlation function (defined with a theta function in time) rather than the standard time-ordered correlation function, which allows them to separate positive- and negative-parity baryon contributions in the rest frame. They construct independent Borel sum rules for the A and B spectral functions, where the B term (which is not invariant under chiral transformation) causes the parity splitting. The nucleon interpolating current contains a mixing parameter t, and the authors choose t ≈ 0.8 to maximize coupling to the negative-parity state while maintaining convergence of the operator product expansion up to dimension 6 operators.

## Key numbers

- Predicted negative-parity nucleon mass: approximately 1550 MeV, compared to the observed value of 1535 MeV with width 150 MeV ± 15 MeV
- Quark condensate used: ⟨q̄q⟩ = (−0.25 GeV)³
- Gluon condensate used: ⟨(αs/π) GG⟩ = (0.36 GeV)⁴
- Mixed condensate ratio: m₀² = ⟨q̄ σ·G q⟩ / ⟨q̄q⟩ = (1.0 GeV)², consistent with the standard range 0.5–1.0 GeV²
- Four-quark condensate: ⟨(q̄q)²⟩ = 1.25 ⟨q̄q⟩² (25% enhancement over vacuum saturation hypothesis)
- For t = 0.9, the N⁻ mass is about 1.4 GeV; for t = 0.8 the mass difference is larger
- At t = 1, the masses of N⁺ and N⁻ become equal because odd-dimensional operators (q̄q, q̄σ·Gq) do not contribute at dimension 6 truncation
- For t = 1.05 and 1.1, the N⁻ sum rule has no solution when QCD parameters are adjusted to reproduce the N⁺ mass

## Context

This work extends the QCD sum rule approach to baryons pioneered by Ioffe (1981) and Shifman, Vainshtein, and Zakharov (1979) by developing a technique to separate negative-parity baryon contributions from the sum rule. A similar study by Chung, Dosch, Kremer, and Schall (1982) used a current with derivative operators but did not find Borel stability, yielding only qualitative results. The authors show that the sign of the quark condensate determines which parity state is the ground state, and their predicted N⁻ mass (1535 MeV resonance) agrees well with experimental data. This work enables systematic study of negative-parity baryon resonances within the QCD sum rule framework and clarifies the role of chiral symmetry breaking in baryon parity splitting.
