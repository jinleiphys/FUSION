# Structure of superheavy nuclei

> Properties of transactinide nuclei needed for the interpretation of spectroscopic data and for the extrapolation to the expected "island of stability" of superheavy nuclei are reviewed from a theorist's point of view in the framework of self-consistent mean-field models.

## Key claim

This review argues that predictions for the location of the next spherical shell closures in superheavy nuclei remain inconclusive, with self-consistent mean-field models and macroscopic-microscopic models giving conflicting results; the actual gaps in single-particle spectra rarely exceed 2 MeV, and the decisive criterion for magicity in superheavy nuclei is no longer the size of the gaps themselves but rather the combined shell correction over a broad region of proton and neutron numbers.

## Method

The authors review theoretical predictions for superheavy nuclei using self-consistent mean-field models (Skyrme, Gogny, and relativistic mean-field interactions) and compare them with macroscopic-microscopic models (Nilsson, Folded-Yukawa, Woods-Saxon potentials). They analyze single-particle spectra, shell correction energies, deformation energy surfaces, and Nilsson diagrams for nuclei in the transactinide region, and compare calculated band heads in odd-A nuclei with experimental spectroscopic data for nuclei around Z ≈ 100.

## Key numbers

- The combined shell correction from self-consistent calculations with the Skyrme SLy6 parametrisation takes large values in a region ranging from N ≈ 164 to N ≈ 184 and Z ≈ 114 to Z ≈ 126
- Predictions for gaps in the single-particle spectra of spherical superheavy nuclei rarely exceed 2 MeV
- The nucleus ²⁹²₁₇₂120 shows an oblate minimum at a β₂ value of -0.13 in the deformation energy surface obtained with SLy6
- The effective mass for NL-Z and SkI3 parametrisations is m₀*/m ≈ 0.6, while for SkP it is m₀*/m = 1
- The deformed N=152 gap is not reproduced by self-consistent mean-field models using Skyrme, Gogny, or relativistic mean-field interactions, while the single-particle potentials used in macroscopic-microscopic calculations fairly reproduce the deformed gaps in the A ≈ 250 region

## Context

This review extends the long-standing discussion of the "island of stability" originally predicted by Nilsson (1968) and others to be centred at Z ≈ 114 and N ≈ 184. It contrasts the predictions of macroscopic-microscopic models (which consistently give Z=114, N=184) with self-consistent mean-field models (which predict Z=120 or 126 and N=172 or 184), following earlier comparative work by Bender (1999). The paper enables future efforts to use spectroscopic data from deformed transactinides in the Z ≈ 102 region to empirically constrain spherical single-particle energies and thereby resolve the conflicting predictions for the location of the island of stability.
