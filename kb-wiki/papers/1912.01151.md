# Alpha Decay to Doubly Magic Core in Quartetting Wave Function Approach

> We present a microscopic calculation of $α$-cluster formation in heavy nuclei $^{104}$Te ($α$+$^{100}$Sn), $^{212}$Po ($α$+$^{208}$Pb) and their neighbors $^{102}$Sn, $^{102}$Te, $^{210}$Pb and $^{210}$Po by using the quartetting wave function approach. Improving the local density approximation, the shell structure of the core nucleus is considered, and the center-of-mass (c.o.m.) effective potential for the quartet is obtained self-consistently from the shell model wavefunctions. The $α$-cluster formation and decay probabilities are obtained by solving the bound-state of the c.o.m. motion of the quartet and the scattering state of the formed $α$-cluster in the Gurvitz approach. Striking shell effects on the $α$-cluster formation probabilities are analyzed for magic numbers 50, 82 and 126. The computed $α$-decay half-lives of these special nuclei are compared with the newest experimental data.

## Key claim

The quartetting wave function approach (QWFA), when improved by replacing the Thomas-Fermi local density approximation with explicit shell-model single-particle states for the core nucleus, can reproduce the striking shell effects on α-cluster formation probabilities at magic numbers 50, 82, and 126, and yields α-decay half-lives in agreement with experiment without adjusting the nucleon-nucleon interaction strength per nucleus.

## Method

The authors solve coupled Schrödinger equations for the center-of-mass (c.o.m.) motion and intrinsic motion of a quartet (two protons, two neutrons) moving relative to a heavy core nucleus. The c.o.m. effective potential is constructed self-consistently from Woods-Saxon plus spin-orbit single-particle wavefunctions of the quartet nucleons, joined at a critical density radius to an external potential that includes a double-folded M3Y nuclear interaction, Coulomb potential, and a density-dependent Pauli blocking term. The α-cluster formation probability is obtained by integrating the bound-state c.o.m. wavefunction beyond the critical radius, and the decay width is computed via the Gurvitz two-potential approach using the bound and scattering wavefunctions at a separation radius.

## Key numbers

- **α-cluster preformation probability for ¹⁰⁴Te:** \(P_\alpha = 0.7235\), with a calculated half-life of \(1.479 \times 10^{-8}\) s, consistent with the experimental upper limit \(< 1.8 \times 10^{-8}\) s.
- **α-cluster preformation probability for ²¹²Po:** \(P_\alpha = 0.1045\), with a calculated half-life of \(3.395 \times 10^{-7}\) s, compared to the experimental value \(2.997 \times 10^{-7}\) s.
- **α-cluster preformation probability for ²¹⁰Pb:** \(P_\alpha = 0.0176\), with a calculated half-life of \(1.777 \times 10^{16}\) s, compared to the experimental value \(3.701 \times 10^{16}\) s.
- **α-cluster preformation probability for ²¹⁰Po:** \(P_\alpha = 0.0137\), with a calculated half-life of \(1.060 \times 10^{7}\) s, compared to the experimental value \(1.196 \times 10^{7}\) s.
- **α-cluster preformation probability for ¹⁰²Sn:** \(P_\alpha = 0.0551\) (no experimental decay data available).
- **α-cluster preformation probability for ¹⁰²Te:** \(P_\alpha = 0.3718\) (no experimental decay data available).
- **Critical density for α-cluster formation/dissolution:** \(\rho_c = 0.02917\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- **Critical radii:** \(R_c(^{100}\text{Sn}) = 5.912\) fm; \(R_c(^{208}\text{Pb}) = 7.438\) fm.
- **M3Y interaction strength parameters (same for all nuclei):** \(c = 17692\), \(d = 4980\).

## Context

This work extends the quartetting wave function approach previously applied by Xu et al. (2016, 2017) using a Thomas-Fermi local density approximation, which could not describe shell effects. By incorporating explicit Woods-Saxon shell-model states for the core, the authors reproduce the dramatic change in α-decay half-life from ²¹⁰Po (\(10^7\) s) to ²¹²Po (\(10^{-7}\) s) across the N=126 magic shell, a feature difficult for earlier models by Brown and Hatsukawa. The approach enables future improvements such as including gradient terms in the intrinsic motion equations and accounting for the spatial extension of the α-particle to smooth sharp edges in the effective potential.
