# Strangeness and light fragment production at high baryon density

> We discuss medium effects on light cluster production in the QCD phase diagram within a generalized Beth-Uhlenbeck (GBU) approach by relating Mott transition lines to those for chemical freeze-out. We find that in heavy-ion collisions at highest energies provided by the LHC light cluster abundances should follow the statistical model because of low baryon densities. At low energies in the nuclear fragmentation region, where the freeze-out interferes with the liquid-gas phase transition, selfenergy and Pauli blocking effects are important. At intermediate energies the HADES, FAIR and NICA experiments can give new information. The GBU approach provides new insights to strange hadron production in this energy domain for explaining the "horn" effects.

## Key claim

The generalized Beth-Uhlenbeck (GBU) approach shows that light cluster production in heavy-ion collisions transitions from being strongly affected by medium effects (selfenergy and Pauli blocking) at low energies to following free-streaming quasiparticle behavior at LHC energies, and that a similar transition governs the "horn" effects in strange-to-nonstrange hadron ratios, though the best description of deuteron rapidity distributions at 20 and 30 A GeV is obtained when selfenergy effects are discarded.

## Method

The authors apply a generalized Beth-Uhlenbeck (GBU) approach to compute medium-dependent binding energies of light nuclear clusters (deuterons, tritons, helions, alpha particles) via the Bethe-Salpeter equation, accounting for selfenergy and Pauli blocking effects that produce Mott transitions where binding vanishes. They combine this with a three-fluid hydrodynamics simulation (THESEUS) using a sudden freeze-out scheme, incorporating scalar and vector selfenergies for nucleons in the particle distribution functions on the freeze-out surface, and compare results to NA49 data for deuteron rapidity distributions in Pb+Pb collisions at 20 and 30 A GeV.

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out temperatures range from 39.34 MeV at \(E_{\rm lab}=1.23\) A GeV (\(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.0\) GeV, \(\mu_B=846.0\) MeV) to 165.9 MeV at \(E_{\rm lab}=21298\) A GeV (\(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200\) GeV, \(\mu_B=23.5\) MeV), as given by the Cleymans et al. (2005) empirical relation.
- Baryon densities at freeze-out including light clusters range from 0.0529 fm\(^{-3}\) at \(E_{\rm lab}=1.23\) A GeV to 0.0366 fm\(^{-3}\) at \(E_{\rm lab}=21298\) A GeV; without clusters the densities are lower, e.g., 0.0231 fm\(^{-3}\) at the lowest energy.
- The "horn" in the \(K^+/\pi^+\) ratio is located at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}\sim 8\) GeV, established experimentally by the NA49 collaboration (Afanasiev et al., 2002).
- The ALICE experiment at LHC observes light cluster production consistent with thermal statistical model predictions at \(T_{fo}\sim 156.5\) MeV (Andronic et al., 2017).

## Context

This work extends the quantum statistical freeze-out approach of Roepke et al. (2017) and the empirical freeze-out parameterization of Cleymans et al. (2005) by connecting Mott transition lines for light clusters to chemical freeze-out lines in the QCD phase diagram. It addresses the energy region of the future NICA facility (\(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2-11\) GeV), partly covered by AGS, CERN-SPS, RHIC BES I, and HADES experiments, and aims to explain the "horn" effects in strange particle ratios that have been attributed to a tricritical point in the QCD phase diagram (Andronic et al., 2009; Bugaev et al., 2017). The results enable systematic studies of light (hyper)nuclear cluster production at HADES, BM@N, and MPD to analyze the transition from low to high freeze-out temperatures, and provide a framework for understanding the system-size dependence observed by NA61/SHINE (Podlaski, 2019).
