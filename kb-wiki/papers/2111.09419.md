# Determination of complete experiments using graphs

> This work presents ideas for the determination of complete experiments using graphs, which are based on a recently published, modified form of Moravcsik's theorem. The lucid representation of complete experiments in terms of graphs, which is at the heart of the theorem, leads to a fully automated procedure that can determine complete experiments for in principle any reaction, i.e. for any number of amplitudes $N$. For larger $N$ (i.e. $N \geq 4$), the sets determined according to Moravcsik's theorem turn out to be slightly overcomplete. A new type of directional graph has been proposed recently, which can decrease the length of the complete sets of observables in some of these cases. The presented results are relevant for reactions with larger numbers of spin-amplitudes, which are at the center of interest in forthcoming measurements, such as single-meson electroproduction $(N=6)$, two-meson photoproduction $(N=8)$ or vector-meson photoproduction $(N=12)$.

## Key claim

The paper presents graphical methods for determining complete experiments (minimal sets of polarization observables that uniquely determine spin-amplitudes) based on a modified form of Moravcsik's theorem, but notes that for larger numbers of amplitudes (N >= 4), the sets determined by Moravcsik's theorem are slightly overcomplete, containing more observables than the absolutely minimal number of 2N.

## Method

The authors represent each of N spin-amplitudes as a point in a graph, and each relative phase between amplitudes as a line connecting two points, with solid lines representing cosine-type observables (real parts of bilinear products) and dashed lines representing sine-type observables (imaginary parts). They apply Moravcsik's theorem which states that a graph is fully complete if it is connected, all points have order two (attached to two lines), and the graph has an odd number of dashed lines. For reactions with larger N, they also introduce new "directional" graphs containing single-lined arrows, double-lined arrows denoting crossed selections of observable-pairs, and inner arrows specifying sign conventions for discrete phase-ambiguity formulas.

## Key numbers

- For single-meson photoproduction (N = 4 amplitudes, N^2 = 16 observables): 12 complete sets composed of 10 observables each were found using Moravcsik's theorem, each containing two observables more than the absolutely minimal number of 2N = 8.
- For single-meson photoproduction: using new directional graphs, 60 known complete sets of minimal length 2N = 8 were re-derived.
- For electroproduction (N = 6 amplitudes, N^2 = 36 observables): applying Moravcsik's theorem to each of the (N-1)!/2 = 60 relevant graph-topologies yields 776 complete sets with a minimum length of 13 observables, one more than the absolutely minimal number of 2N = 12.
- For electroproduction: using new directional graphs, 1216 new complete sets of minimal length 2N = 12 were found.
- For two-meson photoproduction (N = 8 amplitudes, N^2 = 64 observables): there are (N-1)!/2 = 2520 connected start-topologies, and Moravcsik-complete sets contain at least 24 observables.
- For vector-meson photoproduction (N = 12 amplitudes): there are (N-1)!/2 = 19958400 connected start-topologies.

## Context

This work extends the original theorem by Moravcsik (1984) and its recent modified form by Wunderlich (2020), applying graphical methods to amplitude-extraction problems in baryon spectroscopy. The methods are relevant for forthcoming measurements of reactions with larger numbers of spin-amplitudes, such as single-meson electroproduction (N=6), two-meson photoproduction (N=8), and vector-meson photoproduction (N=12), and can help plan future measurements at facilities around the world. The new directional graphs proposed by Wunderlich (2021) can reduce the length of complete sets of observables in some cases, bringing them closer to the absolutely minimal number of 2N.
