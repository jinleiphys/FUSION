# Theory @ Hard Probes 2013

> This talk presents an overview of the theoretical contributions at the Hard Probes 2013 conference, held in Stellenbosch, South Africa, in November 2013.

## Key claim

This paper provides an overview of theoretical contributions at the Hard Probes 2013 conference, summarizing the state of the field rather than presenting a single new result. The author notes that understanding proton-nucleus collisions has become a serious challenge in itself, which is "quite bad news if one wishes to use them as a reference" for interpreting nucleus-nucleus collisions.

## Method

The author categorizes the theoretical contributions into four topics: initial state (16 contributions), electromagnetic probes (7 contributions), energy loss (16 contributions), and heavy flavors (11 contributions). For each topic, the paper summarizes recent developments presented at the conference, including improvements to the Color Glass Condensate framework, next-to-leading order calculations of photon and dilepton production rates, refinements to jet quenching models, and lattice QCD studies of heavy quark properties.

## Key numbers

- The jet quenching parameter from lattice calculations using EQCD effective theory gives an estimated value of approximately 6 GeV^2/fm at typical RHIC temperatures.
- For the J/Psi spectral function from lattice QCD, the bound state peak is "almost gone at T = 1.45 T_c".
- The predicted photon v_2 from current models is too small by a factor of approximately 4 compared to data.
- The mean energy lost in a single gluon emission is proportional to alpha_s times omega_c, where omega_c = hat{q} L^2.
- The drag force on a heavy quark in the strong coupling limit is given by d p / dt = (sqrt{lambda} / 2 pi) (pi T)^2 beta / sqrt(1 - beta^2).

## Context

This paper summarizes the theoretical program presented at the Hard Probes 2013 conference, extending work on the Color Glass Condensate framework (including JIMWLK and BK evolution equations), perturbative QCD calculations of photon and dilepton rates (building on Arnold (2001) and Aurenche (2002)), and multiple approaches to jet quenching (BDMPS, GLV, AMY models). The overview enables future work by identifying open questions, particularly the need for better understanding of proton-nucleus collision geometry, improved photon v_2 predictions, and continued development of heavy quark energy loss models that reconcile R_AA and v_2 measurements.
