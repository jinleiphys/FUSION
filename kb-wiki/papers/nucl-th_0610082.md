# Direct Reactions with Exotic Nuclei, Nuclear Structure and Astrophysics

> Intermediate energy Coulomb excitation and dissociation is a useful tool for nuclear structure and astrophysics studies. Low-lying strength in nuclei far from stability was discovered by this method. The effective range theory for low-lying strength in one-neutron halo nuclei is summarized and extended to two-neutron halo nuclei. This is of special interest in view of recent rather accurate experimental results on the low-lying electric dipole strength in $^{11}$Li. Another indirect approach to nuclear astrophysics is the Trojan horse method. It is pointed out that it is a suitable tool to investigate subthreshold resonances.

## Key claim

Intermediate-energy Coulomb excitation and dissociation is a useful tool for nuclear structure and astrophysics studies, but the effective-range theory for low-lying electromagnetic strength in halo nuclei is limited to cases where the halo expansion parameter gamma = qR is small (gamma less than 1), and the method's applicability to two-neutron halo nuclei like 11Li depends on the assumption that the two continuum neutrons are uncorrelated and that the binding energy is shared equally between them.

## Method

The authors summarize and extend effective-range theory for low-lying electric dipole strength in one-neutron halo nuclei, applying it to 11Be using a cutoff radius of 2.78 fm and an inverse bound-state decay length of 0.1486 fm^-1 to extract an asymptotic normalization constant (ANC) from Coulomb dissociation data. They then extend this approach to two-neutron halo nuclei like 11Li by writing the two-neutron halo wave function as a product of single-particle wave functions, assuming the binding energy Eb is shared equally between the two neutrons (each bound by epsilon = Eb/2), and describing the final state as two uncorrelated continuum neutrons. The dipole strength distribution for the two-neutron halo is obtained by folding the single-particle dipole distribution with an overlap term that accounts for the "shake-off" of the remaining neutron after the first is removed by the photon interaction.

## Key numbers

- For 11Be: inverse bound-state decay length q = 0.1486 fm^-1; cutoff radius R = 2.78 fm; extracted ANC C0 = 0.724(8) fm^-1/2; spectroscopic factor C^2S = 0.704(15)
- Halo expansion parameter for 11Be: gamma = qR = 0.4132 (less than 1)
- For the p1/2 channel in 11Be: binding energy 184 keV, corresponding to q = 0.094 fm^-1; with R = 2.78 fm, gamma^2 = 0.068; scattering length a1 = 210 fm^3
- Dimensionless parameters from fit to 11Be data: c^{3/2}_1 = -0.41(86,-20) and c^{1/2}_1 = 2.77(13,-14), corresponding to scattering length a^{1/2}_1 = 456(61,-73) fm^3
- For 11Li: binding energy Eb approximately 300 keV; the two-neutron halo is Borromean (subsystems n-n and 9Li-n are unbound)

## Context

This work extends the effective-range approach previously introduced by Typel and Baur (2004) for one-neutron halo nuclei to two-neutron halo nuclei, motivated by recent experimental results on low-lying electric dipole strength in 11Li from Nakamura and collaborators. The approach is closely related to effective field theories used for the nucleon-nucleon system and halo nuclei (Bertulani and collaborators, 2002), and complements the asymptotic normalization constant (ANC) method of Mukhamedzhanov and collaborators, which assumes the S-factor is entirely determined by the ANC of the bound state wave function. The results enable future comparison with the more detailed three-body theory of Esbensen and Bertsch, and with experimental data from the Coulomb dissociation of 11Li at RIKEN.
