# Realizing the potential of deep neural network for analyzing neutron star observables and dense matter equation of state

> The difficulty in describing the equation of state (EoS) for nuclear matter at densities above the saturation density ($ρ_0$) has led to the emergence of a multitude of models based on different assumptions and techniques. These EoSs, when used to describe a neutron star (NS), lead to differing values of observables. An outstanding goal in astrophysics is to constrain the dense matter EoS by exploiting astrophysical and gravitational wave measurements. Nuclear matter parameters appear as Taylor coefficients in the expansion of the EoS around the saturation density of symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter and provide a physically-motivated representation of the EoS. In this paper, we introduce a deep learning-based methodology to predict key neutron stars observables such as the NS mass, NS radius, and tidal deformability from a set of nuclear matter parameters. Using generated mock data, we confirm that the neural network model is able to accurately capture the underlying physics of finite nuclei and replicate inter-correlations between the symmetry energy slope, its curvature, and the tidal deformability arising from a set of physical constraints. We also test our network with mock data generated by a different class of physics model, which was not part of the training, to explore the limitations of model dependency in the results. We also study the validity of our trained model using Bayesian inference and show that the performance of our model is on par with physics-based models with the added benefit of much lower computational cost.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a feedforward neural network trained on nuclear matter parameters can predict neutron star observables with accuracy comparable to traditional physics-based Skyrme models, but at a fraction of the computational cost; however, they explicitly clarify that they are not claiming neural networks can replace physics-based models, only that such models can help capture some features of the equation of state at much lower computational cost.

## Method

The authors generated a dataset of 2106 samples by drawing nuclear matter parameters from a multivariate Gaussian distribution with a prescribed correlation coefficient of 0.8 between the symmetry energy slope and curvature, then constructing Skyrme equations of state and solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain neutron star observables. They trained a feedforward neural network with two hidden layers of 15 neurons each and ReLU activation functions to map seven nuclear matter parameters (e0, ρ0, K0, Q0, Jsym,0, Lsym,0, Ksym,0) to six neutron star observables (maximum mass, maximum radius, radius at 1.4 solar masses, and tidal deformability at 1.0, 1.4, and 1.8 solar masses). The trained network was then integrated into a Bayesian inference framework and compared against a traditional Skyrme physics model.

## Key numbers

- The training dataset consisted of 2106 filtered samples of nuclear matter parameters and corresponding neutron star observables
- The neural network architecture used 7 input neurons, two hidden layers with 15 neurons each, and 6 output neurons
- Training used an initial learning rate of 1 × 10^{-3} with batch size 16 for up to 40 epochs
- The multivariate Gaussian distribution used a correlation coefficient of 0.8 between Lsym,0 and Ksym,0
- The mean values for nuclear matter parameters included: e0 = -16.0 MeV, ρ0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}, K0 = 230 MeV, Q0 = -300 MeV, Jsym,0 = 32 MeV, Lsym,0 = 60 MeV, Ksym,0 = -100 MeV
- The data split was 70% training (1515 instances), 20% validation (422 instances), and 10% testing (169 instances)

## Context

This work extends the approach of Han (2021) by treating the feedforward neural network as a representation of the equation of state itself, rather than merely an interpolation tool between nuclear matter parameters and neutron star observables. The authors build on the Case-II dataset methodology from Malik (2020) which incorporates finite nuclei properties, addressing the model-dependency issues identified by Carson (2018) and Ghosh (2022). This enables computationally efficient Bayesian inference for neutron star equation of state studies, which the authors note has not been previously employed in nuclear astrophysics literature.
