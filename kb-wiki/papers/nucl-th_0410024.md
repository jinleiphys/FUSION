# Two particle rapidity, transverse momentum, and azimuthal correlations in relativistic nuclear collisions and transverse radial expansion

> At the very first stage of an ultra-relativistic nucleus-nucleus collision new particles are produced in individual nucleon-nucleon collisions. In the transverse plane, all particles from a single $NN$ collision are initially located at the same position. The subsequent transverse radial expansion of the system creates strong position-momentum correlations and leads to characteristic rapidity, transverse momentum, and azimuthal correlations among the produced particles.

# Two particle rapidity, transverse momentum, and azimuthal correlations in relativistic nuclear collisions and transverse radial expansion

## Key claim

Transverse radial expansion in ultra-relativistic nucleus-nucleus collisions creates characteristic two-particle correlations in rapidity, transverse momentum, and azimuthal angle because all particles from a single nucleon-nucleon collision start at the same transverse position and receive on average the same collective push; the correlations are not boost invariant because transverse expansion itself depends on rapidity.

## Method

The author employs a thermal blast-wave model with boost-invariant longitudinal expansion and freeze-out at constant proper time, assuming a uniform matter density within a cylinder and a power-law transverse rapidity flow profile. Two-particle spectra for particles originating from the same nucleon-nucleon collision are computed by integrating over the transverse flow rapidity and boost direction, with a dilution factor that accounts for mixing particles from multiple uncorrelated nucleon-nucleon collisions. Numerical calculations are performed for two different velocity profiles (n=2 and n=0.5) and compared to preliminary STAR data.

## Key numbers

- At ISR energies in the central rapidity region, the mean number of particle pairs in an individual NN collision satisfies $\langle n(n-1) \rangle_{NN} \approx 1.66 \langle n \rangle_{NN}^2$.
- The blast-wave calculations use a freeze-out temperature T = 110 MeV.
- The dilution factor $D_{N_c}$ is given by $D_{N_c} = \frac{\langle n(n-1) \rangle_{NN}}{(N_c-1) \langle n \rangle_{NN}^2 + \langle n(n-1) \rangle_{NN}}$.
- The two-particle $p_t$ correlation input from NN collisions is taken as $\langle \delta p_{t,1} \delta p_{t,2} \rangle / \langle p_t \rangle^2 = 0.011$, about 20% smaller than measured at ISR.
- The narrowing of the charge balance function with centrality is consistent with experimentally observed narrowing of about 15-20% of the balance function width.
- Azimuthal correlations from radial expansion at $\langle \rho_t^2 \rangle \sim 0.3$ are comparable with estimates of non-flow type azimuthal correlation strength.

## Context

This work extends the thermal parameterization approach of Schnedermann, Sollfrank, and Heinz (1993) for single-particle transverse momentum spectra to two-particle correlations, noting that the mean transverse momentum depends mostly on average expansion velocity squared while two-particle $p_t$ correlations are sensitive to the variance in collective expansion velocity. The predicted azimuthal correlations from radial expansion could be a major contributor to non-flow azimuthal correlations in elliptic flow measurements, as discussed in STAR flow analyses (2003). The narrowing of charge balance functions observed by STAR (2003) is explained by the increase in mean $p_t$ rather than rapidity diffusion. The approach opens possibilities for studying system thermalization time, particle diffusion, and for "jet tomography" to infer how deep in the system a hard collision occurred.
