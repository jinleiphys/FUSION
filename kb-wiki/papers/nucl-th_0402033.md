# Adiabatic nucleus-nucleus potential at near-barrier energies from selfconsistent calculations

> Adiabatic fusion potentials, extended well into compound nucleus region, are calculated for a number of reactions within the static Hartree-Fock method with the Skyrme SkM* interaction. The calculated fusion barriers agree quite well with the data, in spite of considerable errors in reaction Q values. This suggests some error cancellation, possibly with the relative kinetic energy term. Calculated berrier heights are consistent with the idea of fusion hindrance in tip collisions. Adiabatic potentials differ considerably from the results of the frozen density approximation. We briefly discuss relative positions and heights of the fusion and fission potentials and speculate on their possible connection with the fusion hindrance for large Zt Zp.

# Adiabatic nucleus-nucleus potential at near-barrier energies from selfconsistent calculations

## Key claim

Adiabatic fusion potentials calculated with the static Hartree-Fock method using the Skyrme SkM* interaction agree reasonably well with experimental fusion barriers, despite large errors in reaction Q values (up to 17 MeV for heavy systems), suggesting error cancellation possibly involving the overestimated relative kinetic energy correction. The calculated barrier heights support the idea that fusion hindrance in tip collisions arises not from barrier height but from the required nuclear rearrangement.

## Method

Static Hartree-Fock calculations were performed for central collisions of target-projectile combinations, using the Skyrme SkM* interaction with time-reversal invariant states and no pairing. The adiabatic nucleus-nucleus potential V(R) was obtained as the HF energy of the dinuclear complex minus the binding energies of the separated fragments, with a subtraction of the asymptotic relative kinetic energy term to normalize the potential to zero at infinity. Calculations were done for both tip and side collisions (symmetry axis parallel or perpendicular to the relative distance vector) for deformed nuclei, using a spatial mesh with spacing 0.69 or 0.77 fm.

## Key numbers

- For the reaction $^{244}$Pu+$^{48}$Ca, the difference in barrier position between tip and side collisions, $R_{tip}-R_{side}$, is about 2 fm.
- For reactions on $^{208}$Pb and actinide targets leading to very heavy nuclei, adiabatic barriers are 10-20 MeV lower than frozen density barriers from Dobrowolski (2003) and Denisov (2002).
- Calculated fusion barriers $B_{cal}$ (rounded to 0.5 MeV) agree with threshold barriers $B_{thre}$ from Wilczynski (2002) to within up to 4 MeV for eight out of nine systems; one system ($^{238}$U+$^{48}$Ca) shows a 6 MeV discrepancy for side collision and 11 MeV for tip collision.
- For $^{208}$Pb+$^{64}$Ni, evaporation residues were detected at $E_{cm}=235.3-238.2$ MeV, at least 6 MeV above the calculated barrier.
- For $^{208}$Pb+$^{70}$Zn, two evaporation residues were produced at $E_{cm}=257.2$ and 259.1 MeV, at least 13 MeV above the calculated barrier.
- For $^{244}$Pu+$^{48}$Ca, three evaporation residue events were observed at $E_{cm}=194.5-202$ MeV, 2-3 MeV above the calculated side collision barrier.
- The SkM* force underestimates binding energies by about 9 MeV for $^{182}$W and $^{244}$Pu, about 14 MeV for $^{256}$No, and an expected 18 MeV for $^{292}$114.
- Reaction Q values are overestimated by up to 5 MeV for lighter compound systems, about 11 MeV for $^{90}$Zr+$^{90}$Zr, about 17 MeV for $^{48}$Ca+$^{208}$Pb, and expected about 13 MeV too large for $^{48}$Ca+$^{244}$Pu.
- For $^{292}$114, the calculated mass-symmetric fission barrier is 15 MeV, compared to an experimental estimate of about 6.5 MeV from fission barrier data.

## Context

This work extends earlier static Hartree-Fock fusion studies by Skalski (2003) with more extensive iterations and improved kinetic energy treatment, and contrasts with frozen density calculations by Denisov (2002) and Dobrowolski (2003) which produce higher barriers and unrealistic repulsive potentials. The adiabatic potentials differ markedly from frozen density results, and the comparison with experimental threshold barriers from Wilczynski (2002) and Bass interaction barriers supports the idea that fusion hindrance in tip collisions, observed experimentally for systems like $^{110}$Pd+$^{110}$Pd (severe hindrance by at least four orders of magnitude) and $^{154}$Sm+$^{60}$Ni, is related to the required nuclear rearrangement rather than barrier height alone. The results enable future multidimensional studies including pairing to better understand the connection between fusion potential and fission barriers for superheavy element synthesis.
