# The Theory of Nuclear Forces: Is the Never-Ending Story Coming to an End?

> I review the recent progress in our understanding of nuclear forces in terms of an effective field theory (EFT) for low-energy QCD and put this progress into historical perspective. This is followed by an assessment of the current status of EFT based nuclear potentials. In concluding, I will summarize some unresolved issues.

## Key claim

Chiral perturbation theory (ChPT) at next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order (N³LO) can produce nucleon-nucleon (NN) potentials that match the precision of phenomenological high-precision potentials, but only if the cutoff procedures are handled carefully; the widely-cited Nijmegen determination of the low-energy constant \(c_3\) from NN data is unreliable due to a harsh short-range cutoff that introduces a huge systematic error.

## Method

The author reviews the historical development of nuclear force models from Yukawa’s pion theory through meson-exchange models to modern chiral effective field theory (EFT). He compares the \(\chi^2\)/datum achieved by NN potentials at successive orders of ChPT (NLO, NNLO, N³LO) against the 1999 Nijmegen NN database, and contrasts the values of the low-energy constant \(c_3\) extracted from different analyses, demonstrating the effect of cutoff choices on phase-shift predictions.

## Key numbers

- The NLO potentials fit the world \(np\) data below 290 MeV with \(\chi^2\)/datum between 67 and 105; NNLO potentials achieve \(\chi^2\)/datum between 12 and 27.
- The Idaho N³LO potential fits the \(np\) data below 290 MeV with \(\chi^2\)/datum = 1.1, comparable to the Argonne \(V_{18}\) potential’s 1.04.
- The best Bochum/Juelich N³LO potential fits the \(np\) data with \(\chi^2\)/datum = 1.7; the worst gives 7.9.
- For \(pp\) data below 290 MeV, the Idaho N³LO potential achieves \(\chi^2\)/datum = 1.5, versus 1.4 for Argonne \(V_{18}\); the best Bochum/Juelich N³LO gives 2.9, the worst 22.3.
- The Nijmegen 2003 \(pp+np\) analysis reports \(c_3 = -4.78 \pm 0.10\) GeV\(^{-1}\); the Idaho and Bochum/Juelich N³LO potentials use \(c_3 \approx -3.3 \pm 0.1\) GeV\(^{-1}\); a cutoff-free analysis of peripheral partial waves yields \(c_3 = -2.7\) GeV\(^{-1}\).

## Context

This paper reviews the transition from phenomenological high-precision NN potentials (Nijmegen, Argonne \(V_{18}\), CD-Bonn, all from the 1990s) to chiral EFT-based potentials that are both theoretically grounded and quantitatively accurate. It extends the work of Ordóñez, Ray, and van Kolck (1994) and Entem and Machleidt (2003) by demonstrating that N³LO is the minimum order needed to meet the precision standards established by the 1990s potentials. The unresolved issues include the need for a 3NF at N³LO (to address the \(A_y\) puzzle in nucleon-deuteron scattering) and the ongoing debate about the consistency of Weinberg power counting.
