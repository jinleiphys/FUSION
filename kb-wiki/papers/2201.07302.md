# Efficient emulation of relativistic heavy ion collisions with transfer learning

> Measurements from the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) and the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) can be used to study the properties of quark-gluon plasma. Systematic constraints on these properties must combine measurements from different collision systems and methodically account for experimental and theoretical uncertainties. Such studies require a vast number of costly numerical simulations. While computationally inexpensive surrogate models ("emulators") can be used to efficiently approximate the predictions of heavy ion simulations across a broad range of model parameters, training a reliable emulator remains a computationally expensive task. We use transfer learning to map the parameter dependencies of one model emulator onto another, leveraging similarities between different simulations of heavy ion collisions. By limiting the need for large numbers of simulations to only one of the emulators, this technique reduces the numerical cost of comprehensive uncertainty quantification when studying multiple collision systems and exploring different models.

## Key claim

Transfer learning can reduce the computational cost of building emulators for heavy ion collision simulations by mapping the parameter dependencies of a source emulator (trained on a large dataset) onto a target system, leveraging similarities between different collision systems or different dynamical evolution models, but the method requires that the source and target share the same parameter space and that the target training data are scarce relative to the source.

## Method

The authors apply a Kennedy-O'Hagan multi-fidelity emulation framework adapted for transfer learning, where the target simulator output \( f_T(\mathbf{x}) \) is modeled as a linear correlation factor \( \rho \) times the source simulator output \( f_S(\mathbf{x}) \) plus a Gaussian process discrepancy function \( \delta(\mathbf{x}) \). They train the source emulator on 473 design points from Pb+Pb collisions at \( \sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}} = 2.76 \) TeV (or from one particlization model), then train the target emulator on a much smaller number \( n \) of design points from Au+Au collisions at \( \sqrt{s_\mathrm{NN}} = 0.2 \) TeV (or from a different particlization model), using maximum likelihood to estimate the correlation coefficient and GP hyperparameters. Performance is evaluated by computing the mean squared error between emulator predictions and full-model test data at 100 held-out design points.

## Key numbers

*   For transfer learning between collision systems (Au+Au at 0.2 TeV as target, Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV as source), the mean squared error (MSE) of the transfer learning emulator trained with \( n = 47 \) target design points is comparable to the MSE of a standard Gaussian process emulator trained with \( n = 473 \) target design points (see Fig. 1, which shows MSE as a function of \( n \)).
*   The source emulator is trained on \( m = 473 \) design points from the source system, while the target emulator uses \( n \) target design points ranging from 1 to 473.
*   For each design point and each particlization model, 2500 simulations were performed with stochastically fluctuating initial conditions.
*   The full-model simulations share a common set of 17 model parameters.

## Context

This work extends the standard practice of using Gaussian process emulators for Bayesian parameter estimation in heavy ion collisions, as done by Bernhard (2018, 2019) and the JETSCAPE collaboration (2020), by introducing transfer learning to reduce the computational burden of training separate emulators for each collision system or each dynamical model. The method enables more efficient global Bayesian inference across multiple collision systems (e.g., Au+Au at RHIC and Pb+Pb at LHC) and multiple particlization models (Grad, Chapman-Enskog, Pratt-Torrieri-Bernhard viscous corrections), which previously required scaling the training data linearly with the number of models. The approach is based on the Kennedy-O'Hagan multi-fidelity model (2000) but applied to different physical systems rather than different fidelities of the same system.
