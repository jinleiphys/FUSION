# How to measure the spreading width for decay of superdeformed nuclei

> A new expression for the branching ratio for the decay via the E1 process in the normal-deformed band of superdeformed nuclei is given within a simple two-level model. Using this expression, the spreading or tunneling width Gamma^downarrow for superdeformed decay can be expressed entirely in terms of experimentally known quantities. We show how to determine the tunneling matrix element V from the measured value of Gamma^downarrow and a statistical model of the energy levels. The accuracy of the two-level approximation is verified by considering the effects of the other normal-deformed states.

## Key claim

The spreading width \(\Gamma^\downarrow\) for superdeformed decay can be expressed entirely in terms of experimentally measurable quantities (the branching ratio \(F_N\) and the decay widths \(\Gamma_S\) and \(\Gamma_N\)), contrary to prior claims that it is a free parameter; however, the extraction requires that the inequality \(F_N < \Gamma_N/(\Gamma_N + \Gamma_S)\) be satisfied, and uncertainties in \(\Gamma_N\) (of order 100%) can make the determination meaningless in some cases.

## Method

The authors derive a new expression for the branching ratio \(F_N\) for E1 decay from a superdeformed (SD) state into a normal-deformed (ND) band using a two-level model with a retarded Green's function approach, yielding \(\Gamma^\downarrow\) as a function of \(F_N\), \(\Gamma_S\), and \(\Gamma_N\). They then use a Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE) statistical model for the ND level spacings to compute the probability distribution of the tunneling matrix element \(V\) from the measured \(\Gamma^\downarrow\) and the average ND level spacing \(D_N\). The accuracy of the two-level approximation is verified by extending the model to include a third (next-nearest neighbor) ND state and numerically comparing the resulting branching ratios.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{152}\)Dy(28): \(F_N = 0.40\), \(\Gamma_S = 10.0\) meV, \(\Gamma_N = 17\) meV, \(D_N = 220\) eV, yielding \(\Gamma^\downarrow = 11\) meV (compared to \(41,000\) meV from prior work).
- For \(^{152}\)Dy(26): \(F_N = 0.81\), \(\Gamma_S = 7.0\) meV, \(\Gamma_N = 17\) meV, \(D_N = 194\) eV, yielding a physically impossible negative \(\Gamma^\downarrow = -40\) meV, indicating violation of the inequality \(F_N < \Gamma_N/(\Gamma_N + \Gamma_S)\).
- For \(^{194}\)Hg(12): \(F_N = 0.40\), \(\Gamma_S = 0.108\) meV, \(\Gamma_N = 21\) meV, \(D_N = 344\) eV, yielding \(\Gamma^\downarrow = 0.072\) meV (compared to 560 meV from prior work).
- For \(^{194}\)Hg(10): \(F_N = 0.97\), \(\Gamma_S = 0.046\) meV, \(\Gamma_N = 20\) meV, \(D_N = 493\) eV, yielding \(\Gamma^\downarrow = 1.6\) meV (compared to 37,000 meV from prior work).
- The average detuning \(\langle|\Delta|\rangle = D_N/4\) for the nearest ND level, and \(\langle|\Delta_2|\rangle = 3D_N/4\) for the next-nearest neighbor.
- In the \(A \approx 190\) mass region, the two-state branching ratio agrees with the three-state result to within \(0.9 \lesssim F_N^{(2)}/F_N^{(3)} \leq 1\); in the \(A \approx 150\) region, \(0.7 < F_N^{(2)}/F_N^{(3)} < 1\).
- The total correction from all ND states in the \(A \approx 190\) region is at most about 23% of the two-state branching ratio.

## Context

This work extends the two-level model of Stafford and Barrett (1999) for superdeformed decay-out, providing a direct experimental determination of the spreading width \(\Gamma^\downarrow\) that contradicts the approach of Vigezzi et al., who used an average spreading width \(\Gamma^\downarrow = 2\pi\langle V^2\rangle/D_N\). The authors show that the values of \(\Gamma^\downarrow\) extracted from the data of Lauritsen et al. (2002) differ by three to five orders of magnitude from those reported in that work. The results enable a statistical determination of the tunneling matrix element \(V\) from measured \(\Gamma^\downarrow\) values, and the two-level approximation is shown to be sufficient for describing the dominant decay-out process, with corrections from additional ND states bounded to about 23% in the \(A \approx 190\) region.
