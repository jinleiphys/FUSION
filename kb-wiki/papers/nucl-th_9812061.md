# Lessons to be learned from the coherent photoproduction of pseudoscalar mesons

> We study the coherent photoproduction of pseudoscalar mesons---particularly of neutral pions---placing special emphasis on the various sources that put into question earlier nonrelativistic-impulse-approximation calculations. These include: final-state interactions, relativistic effects, off-shell ambiguities, and violations to the impulse approximation. We establish that, while distortions play an essential role in the modification of the coherent cross section, the uncertainty in our results due to the various choices of optical-potential models is relatively small (of at most 30%). By far the largest uncertainty emerges from the ambiguity in extending the many on-shell-equivalent representations of the elementary amplitude off the mass shell. Indeed, relativistic impulse-approximation calculations that include the same pionic distortions, the same nuclear-structure model, and two sets of elementary amplitudes that are identical on-shell, lead to variations in the magnitude of the coherent cross section by up to factors of five. Finally, we address qualitatively the assumption of locality implicit in most impulse-approximation treatments, and suggest that the coherent reaction probes---in addition to the nuclear density---the polarization structure of the nucleus.

## Key claim

The paper establishes that the largest uncertainty in calculating the coherent photoproduction cross section of neutral pions arises from the ambiguity in extending on-shell-equivalent representations of the elementary amplitude off the mass shell, rather than from uncertainties in optical-potential models or nuclear structure. Relativistic impulse-approximation calculations using the same pionic distortions, the same nuclear-structure model, and two sets of elementary amplitudes that are identical on-shell produce variations in the magnitude of the coherent cross section by up to factors of five.

## Method

The authors performed relativistic distorted-wave impulse approximation (RDWIA) calculations for coherent neutral-pion photoproduction from 40Ca and 12C, using a mean-field approximation to the Walecka model to generate relativistic ground-state densities. They employed two different on-shell-equivalent parameterizations of the elementary gamma N to pi0 N amplitude (tensor and vector representations) obtained from the VPI phase-shift analysis, and modeled pionic final-state interactions using an optical-potential approach extended from the low-energy work of Carr, Stricker-Bauer, and McManus to the Delta-resonance region.

## Key numbers

- The uncertainty in the coherent cross section due to different choices of optical-potential models is at most 30% for 40Ca at E_gamma = 186 MeV (50 MeV pion).
- For 12C at E_gamma = 173 MeV, the tensor parameterization result is five times larger than the vector prediction.
- At E_gamma = 230 MeV for 40Ca, factors of two or more are observed between vector and tensor representations.
- The distorted-wave calculation at E_gamma = 168 MeV for 40Ca more than doubled the differential cross section at its maximum relative to the plane-wave result, with a shift of about 10 degrees in the position of the minima.
- The vector calculation for 12C at theta_lab = 60 degrees agrees with experimental data if shifted by +25 MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier nonrelativistic impulse-approximation calculations of coherent pion photoproduction by Bofinger (1986), Cekic (1987), Bennhold and Tanabe (1990), and Trykov and Filikhin (1994), which assumed the full nuclear contribution appears only via the matter density. The study contradicts the simple picture that the coherent reaction is insensitive to nuclear-structure effects, showing instead that it probes the polarization structure of the nucleus in addition to the nuclear density. The results enable future work on medium modifications to the Delta-resonance, such as the binding-energy correction of about 40 MeV suggested by Gaarde (1991) and the 15 MeV shift invoked by Peters, Lenske, and Mosel (1998), which cannot be accounted for within the impulse-approximation framework.
