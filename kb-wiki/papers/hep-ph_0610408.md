# Numerical symmetrization of state of identical particles

> Talk presented by O.Utyuzh at XXXVI International Symposium on Multiparticle Dynamics, September 2-8, 2006, Paraty (RJ), Brazil; minor corrections, to be published in Braz. J. Phys. (2007)

## Key claim

The paper clarifies and discusses the authors' previously proposed method of numerical symmetrization for identical particles, arguing that Bose-Einstein correlations can be efficiently modeled by dividing particles into clusters (Elementary Emitting Cells) rather than symmetrizing the full multiparticle wave function, but acknowledges that this approach is "most probably, not very practical one and therefore hardly to be followed."

## Method

The authors propose dividing the initial set of identical secondaries into clusters (Elementary Emitting Cells, EECs) of particles with similar momenta, then symmetrizing only within each cell rather than over all particles in the event. They compare two approximation schemes for cells with many particles: a sequential method that adds particles one by one using two-particle correlations, and a method where all particles in a cell are correlated only with the first particle defining that cell. The results are compared to the exact full symmetrization and to a Monte Carlo generator that selects particles directly from a Bose-Einstein distribution.

## Key numbers

- For typical EECs, the average number of particles per cluster is about \(n_{cl}=2\) particles per cluster, depending on circumstances.
- The Metropolis procedure for symmetrization becomes prohibitively slow for clusters with \(n_{cluster} > 10\) particles.
- The exact maximal probability for \(n_{cl}\) particles all in the same state is \(n_{cl}!\) (e.g., for \(n_{cl}=4\), this is 24).
- The sequential approximation gives a maximal probability of \(2^{\frac{1}{2}n_{cl}(n_{cl}-1)}\) when all particles are in the same state, which exceeds the exact limit for \(n_{cl} > 2\).
- The sequential approximation keeps the width of the two-particle correlation function \(C_2\) the same as the exact method, but differs substantially in \(C_2(\delta P=0)\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier EEC approach (Utyuzh et al., 2006) and the cluster-based symmetrization method of Germans et al. (2001), which modified the original Metropolis procedure of Zajc (1987) by limiting symmetrization to particles in clusters. The paper contrasts its method with the THERMINATOR Monte Carlo generator (Kisiel et al., 2006), which selects particles directly from a Bose-Einstein distribution and is shown to substantially underestimate the correlation function \(C_2\). The authors conclude that no existing Monte Carlo event generator can properly include quantum statistics effects, and advocate building generators from first principles with Bose-Einstein bunching as a basic principle, following the earlier work of MIE (2002).
