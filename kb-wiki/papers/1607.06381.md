# Small systems and regulator dependence in relativistic hydrodynamics

> Consistent theories of hydrodynamics necessarily include nonhydrodynamic modes, which can be viewed as a regulator necessary to ensure causality. Under many circumstances the choice of regulator is not relevant, but this is not always the case. In particular, for sufficiently small systems (such as those arising in pA or pp collisions) such dependence may be inevitable. We address this issue in the context of the modern version of Müller-Israel-Stewart theory of relativistic hydrodynamics. In this case, by demanding that the nonhydrodynamic modes be subdominant, we find that regulator dependence becomes inevitable only for multiplicities $dN/dY$ of the order of a few. This conclusion supports earlier studies based on hydrodynamic simulations of small systems, at the same time providing a simple physical picture of how hydrodynamics can be reliable even in such seemingly extreme conditions.

## Key claim

By demanding that nonhydrodynamic modes be subdominant in the modern (BRSSS) version of Müller-Israel-Stewart theory, regulator dependence becomes inevitable only for final-state charged-particle multiplicities \(dN/dY\) of order a few, supporting the reliability of hydrodynamics for small systems such as those produced in pA and pp collisions.

## Method

The paper analyzes the dispersion relations of linearized perturbations in BRSSS (conformal Müller-Israel-Stewart) theory, comparing the imaginary parts (decay rates) of the hydrodynamic and nonhydrodynamic modes. By finding the wavenumber \(K\) at which these decay rates become comparable, the author derives a condition on the product of system size \(R\) and temperature \(T\) for hydrodynamic modes to dominate. This bound is then translated into an estimate of the minimum entropy per unit rapidity and, via an approximate relation, into a minimum charged-particle multiplicity.

## Key numbers

- The condition for nonhydrodynamic modes to be subdominant is \(R T > 2\pi \sqrt{2 (T\tau_\Pi) (\eta/s)}\), where \(\tau_\Pi\) is the relaxation time, \(\eta/s\) the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio, \(R\) the system size, and \(T\) the temperature.
- Using the minimal causal value \(T\tau_\Pi = 2\eta/s\) and the KSS bound \(\eta/s = 1/4\pi\), the bound simplifies to \(R T > 1\).
- The minimum entropy per unit rapidity below which regulator dependence is inevitable is estimated as \((dS/dY)_{MIN} \approx 25\).
- The corresponding minimum charged-particle multiplicity is \((dN/dY)_{MIN} \approx 3\).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Romatschke (2015) and Habich et al. (2015), which used the size of second-order gradient corrections as a measure of systematic error in small-system hydrodynamics, by instead focusing directly on the decay of nonhydrodynamic modes. The analysis provides a simple analytic inequality supporting the conclusion from hydrodynamic simulations that hydrodynamics can remain reliable down to very small multiplicities. The result is also consistent with AdS/CFT simulations by Chesler (2015, 2016), which found that hydrodynamics approximates the exact energy-momentum tensor down to drop sizes of order \(RT \approx 1\). The paper enables a clear criterion for when BRSSS theory is testing universal hydrodynamics versus its regulator sector, and suggests that for systems violating the bound, alternative hydrodynamic theories with different nonhydrodynamic sectors (such as those proposed by Heller et al., 2014) should be considered.
