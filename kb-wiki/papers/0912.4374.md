# Semiclassical theory for spatial density oscillations in fermionic systems

> We investigate the particle and kinetic-energy densities for a system of $N$ fermions bound in a local (mean-field) potential $V(\bfr)$. We generalize a recently developed semiclassical theory [J. Roccia and M. Brack, Phys. Rev.\ Lett. {\bf 100}, 200408 (2008)], in which the densities are calculated in terms of the closed orbits of the corresponding classical system, to $D>1$ dimensions. We regularize the semiclassical results $(i)$ for the U(1) symmetry breaking occurring for spherical systems at $r=0$ and $(ii)$ near the classical turning points where the Friedel oscillations are predominant and well reproduced by the shortest orbit going from $r$ to the closest turning point and back. For systems with spherical symmetry, we show that there exist two types of oscillations which can be attributed to radial and non-radial orbits, respectively. The semiclassical theory is tested against exact quantum-mechanical calculations for a variety of model potentials. We find a very good overall numerical agreement between semiclassical and exact numerical densities even for moderate particle numbers $N$. Using a "local virial theorem", shown to be valid (except for a small region around the classical turning points) for arbitrary local potentials, we can prove that the Thomas-Fermi functional $τ_{\text{TF}}[ρ]$ reproduces the oscillations in the quantum-mechanical densities to first order in the oscillating parts.

## Key claim

The paper generalizes a previously developed semiclassical closed-orbit theory (Roccia and Brack, 2008) for spatial density oscillations in fermionic systems from one dimension to higher dimensions (D > 1), showing that the semiclassical densities agree well with exact quantum-mechanical results even for moderate particle numbers N, provided the results are regularized at the center of spherical systems and near classical turning points.

## Method

The authors start from the semiclassical Green function (Gutzwiller, 1971) and derive expressions for the oscillating parts of the particle density ρ(r), the kinetic-energy densities τ(r) and τ₁(r), and their average ξ(r) in terms of sums over non-periodic closed orbits (NPOs) of the corresponding classical system at the smooth Fermi energy. They regularize the semiclassical results for spherical systems at r = 0 (where a U(1) symmetry breaking occurs) and near classical turning points (where Friedel oscillations dominate) using techniques from WKB theory. The semiclassical densities are then compared to exact quantum-mechanical calculations for various model potentials including spherical billiards, quartic oscillators, and other smooth potentials.

## Key numbers

- For a 3D spherical billiard with N = 100068 particles, the kinetic-energy density profiles show two types of oscillations: regular short-ranged oscillations and irregular long-ranged oscillations whose wavelength decreases with increasing r (all densities divided by N^(5/3), units ℏ²/2m = R = 1)
- For the nearly chaotic two-dimensional quartic oscillator V(x,y) = ½(x⁴ + y⁴) − κ x² y² with κ = 0.6, the local virial theorem is tested for N = 632 particles (ℏ = m = 1), showing good agreement between δτ(r) and [λ̃ − V(r)] δρ(r) except in the surface region

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical closed-orbit theory for spatial density oscillations first developed by Roccia and Brack (2008) from one-dimensional systems to higher dimensions, and generalizes earlier work on periodic orbit theory for the density of states (Gutzwiller, 1971; Berry and Mount, 1972). The theory is applicable to trapped fermionic gases (motivated by experiments of Jin and collaborators), metallic clusters, and quantum dots, where the self-consistent mean field can be approximated by local potentials or billiard systems. The local virial theorem derived here extends a result previously obtained for isotropic harmonic oscillators by Brack, van Zyl, and collaborators to arbitrary local potentials, and the semiclassical approach enables calculation of spatial density oscillations without full quantum-mechanical diagonalization.
