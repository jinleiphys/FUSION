# On the importance of using exact pairing in the study of pygmy dipole resonance

> The strength functions of giant dipole resonance (GDR) in oxygen $^{18 - 24}$O, calcium $^{50 - 60}$Ca, and tin $^{120 - 130}$Sn isotopes are calculated within the phonon damping model under three approximations: without superfluid pairing, including BCS pairing, and exact pairing gaps. The analysis of the numerical results shows that exact pairing decreases the two-neutron separation energy in light nuclei, but increases it in heavy nuclei as compared to that obtained within the BCS theory. In neutron-rich medium and heavy nuclei, exact pairing significantly enhances the strength located at the low-energy tail of the GDR, which is usually associated with the pygmy dipole resonance. The line shape of the GDR changes significantly with increasing the neutron number within an isotopic chain if the model parameter is kept fixed at the value determined for the stable isotope.

## Key claim
Exact pairing significantly enhances the low-energy E1 strength (pygmy dipole resonance) in neutron-rich medium and heavy nuclei compared to BCS pairing, but the effect is conditional on the choice of the particle-phonon coupling parameter; when the parameter is fixed to the value for the stable isotope, the enhancement increases with neutron number.

## Method
The authors calculated GDR strength functions for oxygen (18-24O), calcium (50-60Ca), and tin (120-130Sn) isotopes using the phonon damping model (PDM) with Woods-Saxon single-particle potentials. They compared three treatments of superfluid pairing: no pairing, BCS pairing with a mean-field gap, and exact pairing obtained by diagonalizing the pairing Hamiltonian to extract an exact pairing gap and exact quasiparticle energies. The GDR strength function was computed from the imaginary part of the phonon Green function, and the PDR strength was quantified as the integrated energy-weighted strength up to 15 MeV (oxygen), 12 MeV (calcium), and 10 MeV (tin).

## Key numbers
- For tin isotopes with exact pairing, the PDR exhausts from around 4.5% to 7% of the total GDR integrated cross section, and this fraction increases with mass number A.
- The GDR width for tin isotopes without pairing is around 5 MeV; for oxygen isotopes without pairing it is between 11 and 14 MeV; for calcium isotopes without pairing it is between 8 and 10 MeV.
- The unperturbed GDR energy for oxygen isotopes was set to 23.7 MeV (experimental value for 18O); for calcium and tin isotopes the empirical formula E_GDR = 31.2 A^{-1/3} + 20.6 A^{-1/6} was used.
- The upper integration limits for PDR strength were E_up = 15 MeV (oxygen), 12 MeV (calcium), and 10 MeV (tin).
- The maximum integration energies for the total GDR sum rule were E_max = 50 MeV (oxygen), 40 MeV (calcium), and 30 MeV (tin).
- The Woods-Saxon potential parameters were V_0 = 51 MeV, k = 0.86, r_0 = 1.27 fm, a = 0.67 fm, and spin-orbit strength lambda = 35.0.

## Context
This work extends the phonon damping model (PDM) previously applied to oxygen and calcium isotopes with BCS pairing (Dang, 2009) by incorporating exact pairing gaps from diagonalization of the pairing Hamiltonian (Volya, 2001). It addresses discrepancies between relativistic RPA predictions of a collective PDR in tin isotopes (Vretenar, 2000; Litvinova, 2008) and QRPA calculations that do not find collective low-energy states (Sarchi, 2005). The results suggest that exact pairing, rather than mean-field BCS pairing, may resolve some of these theoretical disagreements and is important for describing PDR in neutron-rich nuclei near the drip line.
