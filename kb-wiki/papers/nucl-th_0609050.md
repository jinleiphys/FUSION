# The Nuclear Force Problem: Is the Never-Ending Story Coming to an End?

> The attempts to find the right (underlying) theory for the nuclear force have a long and stimulating history. Already in 1953, Hans Bethe stated that "more man-hours have been given to this problem than to any other scientific question in the history of mankind". In search for the nature of the nuclear force, the idea of sub-nuclear particles was created which, eventually, generated the field of particle physics. I will review this productive history of hope, error, and desperation. Finally, I will discuss recent ideas which apply the concept of an effective field theory to low-energy QCD. There are indications that this concept may provide the right framework to properly understand nuclear forces.

# The Nuclear Force Problem: Is the Never-Ending Story Coming to an End?

## Key claim

The effective field theory (EFT) approach to low-energy QCD, specifically chiral perturbation theory (χPT), may provide the correct framework to properly understand nuclear forces, but it is too early to claim the problem is solved; the story is "certainly converging" rather than finished. The approach requires going to next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order (N³LO) to achieve accuracy comparable to phenomenological potentials, and the three-nucleon force (3NF) at that order is still under development.

## Method

The author reviews the historical development of nuclear force theories from Yukawa's meson theory (1935) through one-boson-exchange models, dispersion-theoretic potentials (Paris, Bonn), and quark cluster models, then describes the modern chiral EFT approach. In χPT, the nucleon-nucleon (NN) potential is constructed from contact terms and pion-exchange diagrams ordered by powers of (Q/Λχ) where Λχ ≈ 1 GeV, with the potential then applied in a Lippmann-Schwinger or Schrödinger equation to obtain scattering amplitudes. The regulator function exp[-(p/Λ)^(2n) - (p'/Λ)^(2n)] is applied with Λ ≪ Λχ to enforce the low-momentum validity of the expansion.

## Key numbers

- Λχ ≈ 1 GeV: the chiral symmetry breaking scale that sets the expansion parameter for χPT
- χ²/datum for np data below 290 MeV: 1.10 at N³LO (Λ=500 MeV), compared to 10.1 at NNLO, 36.2 at NLO, and 1.04 for the phenomenological Argonne V18 potential
- χ²/datum for pp data below 290 MeV: 1.50 at N³LO (Λ=500 MeV), compared to 35.4 at NNLO, 80.1 at NLO, and 1.38 for Argonne V18
- Number of contact terms: 2 at order Q⁰, 7 at order Q², and 15 at order Q⁴
- Three-nucleon forces first appear at NNLO (ν=3) and involve two new parameters (D and E) not present in the two-nucleon problem
- Four-nucleon forces first appear at N³LO and are expected to be even weaker than 3NF

## Context

This review extends the historical narrative from Yukawa (1935) through the meson theories of the 1950s, the one-boson-exchange model of the 1960s, and the sophisticated 2π-exchange models (Stony Brook, Paris, Bonn) of the 1970s, to the modern EFT approach pioneered by Weinberg (1979, 1990) and van Kolck. The work by Ordóñez, Ray, and van Kolck (1994) constructed the first NN potential in coordinate space at NNLO; Kaiser, Brockmann, and Weise (1997) gave the first model-independent prediction for peripheral partial waves at NNLO; Epelbaum et al. (1998) developed the first momentum-space NN potential at NNLO; and Entem and Machleidt (2003) presented the first potential at N³LO. The approach enables systematic calculation of two- and many-body forces on the same footing, with first applications in no-core shell model, coupled cluster formalism, and unitary-model-operator approach producing promising results for nuclear structure.
