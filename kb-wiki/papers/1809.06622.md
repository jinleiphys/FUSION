# Reflections on the Origin of the EMC Effect

> In the 35 years since the European Muon Collaboration announced the astonishing result that the valence structure of a nucleus was very different from that of a free nucleon, many explanations have been suggested. The first of the two most promising explanations is based upon the different effects of the strong Lorentz scalar and vector mean fields known to exist in a nucleus on the internal structure of the nucleon-like clusters which occupy shell model states. The second links the effect to the modification of the structure of nucleons involved in short-range correlations, which are far off their mass shell. We explore some of the methods which have been proposed to give complementary information on this puzzle, especially the spin-dependent EMC effect and the isovector EMC effect, both proposed by Cloet, Bentz and Thomas. It is shown that the predictions for the spin dependent EMC effect, in particular, differ substantially within the mean-field and short-range correlation approaches. Hence the measurement of the spin dependent EMC effect at Jefferson Lab should give us a deeper understanding of the origin of the EMC effect and, indeed, of the structure of atomic nuclei.

# Reflections on the Origin of the EMC Effect

## Key claim
The two most promising explanations for the EMC effect are mean-field modification of nucleon structure by the strong Lorentz scalar field in a nucleus (up to half the nucleon mass) and modification of nucleons in short-range correlations that are far off their mass shell; the spin-dependent EMC effect should distinguish between these approaches because mean-field models predict a large effect while short-range correlation models predict little or none.

## Method
The paper reviews and compares predictions from the quark-meson coupling (QMC) model, implemented with both the MIT bag model and the covariant Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, against the short-range correlation (SRC) approach for the spin-dependent and isovector EMC effects. For the SRC case, the author analyzes the depolarization of a valence proton in a tensor-correlated neutron-proton pair using angular momentum algebra with Clebsch-Gordan coefficients for D-wave scattering. The isovector EMC effect is examined through the shift of light-cone momentum from u-quarks to d-quarks induced by the isovector mean field in nuclei with N ≠ Z.

## Key numbers
- The scalar mean field in a nucleus has a strength typically between 300–500 MeV, up to half the nucleon mass.
- A nucleon in a correlated pair with relative momentum 400 MeV/c is off-shell by around 80 MeV.
- The QMC-derived energy density functional reproduces binding energies of nuclei across the periodic table at the 0.3% level, with superheavies at 0.1%.
- For the SRC tensor correlation picture, the polarization of the struck proton after the interaction is of order -10% to -15% instead of +100%.
- In the isovector EMC effect for a nucleus like Pb, the difference in the EMC ratios for u and d quarks is as large as 20%.

## Context
This paper extends the author’s long-standing work on the EMC effect, beginning with the original discovery by the European Muon Collaboration (Aubert 1983, Bodek 1983) and the early pion-field explanation explored with Ericson (1983) and Llewellyn Smith (1983). It contrasts the mean-field QMC approach, developed by Guichon (1987) and later refined by Cloet, Bentz, and Thomas (2005–2012), with the SRC explanation supported by Weinstein (2010) and Hen (2016). The paper identifies upcoming Jefferson Lab measurements of the spin-dependent EMC effect on polarized 7Li as a critical test, and proposes parity-violating electron scattering and charged-current weak interactions to test the isovector EMC effect.
