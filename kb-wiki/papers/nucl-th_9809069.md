# Nuclear Forces and Nuclear Structure

> After a historical review, I present the progress in the field of realistic NN potentials that we have seen in recent years. A new generation of very quantitative (high-quality/high-precision) NN potentials has emerged. These potentials will serve as reliable input for microscopic nuclear structure calculations and will allow for a systematic investigation of off-shell effects. The issue of three-nucleon forces is also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a new generation of "high-precision" nucleon-nucleon (NN) potentials, fitting the world NN data with a χ² per datum near 1.0, now provides a reliable starting point for microscopic nuclear structure calculations, but the abstract glosses over the condition that these potentials differ substantially in their off-shell behavior, particularly in the tensor force, which leads to significantly different predictions for nuclear structure observables like the triton binding energy.

## Method

The author reviews the historical development of meson-theoretic NN potentials, from nonrelativistic one-boson-exchange models through the field-theoretic Bonn full model, then describes the construction of five modern high-precision potentials (CD-Bonn, Nijm-I, Nijm-II, Reid93, and Argonne V18) that fit the pruned Nijmegen database of 4301 pp and np data below 350 MeV. These potentials are compared in terms of their theoretical basis (local vs. nonlocal, meson-exchange vs. phenomenological), their number of free parameters (40–50), and their predictions for deuteron properties and triton binding energy in both nonrelativistic and relativistic calculations.

## Key numbers

- The Nijmegen phase-shift analysis achieved χ²/datum = 0.99 for 4301 data (1787 pp and 2514 np data) below 350 MeV laboratory energy
- The five high-precision potentials fit the pruned Nijmegen database with χ²/datum = 1.03 (CD-Bonn, Nijm-I, Nijm-II, Reid93) and 1.09 (Argonne V18)
- CD-Bonn predicts deuteron D-state probability PD = 4.83%, while the other potentials yield PD = 5.7(1)% (Nijm-I: 5.66%, Nijm-II: 5.64%, Reid93: 5.70%, Argonne V18: 5.76%)
- Deuteron quadrupole moment predictions range from 0.270 fm² (CD-Bonn, Reid93, V18) to 0.272 fm² (Nijm-I), compared to the experimental value of 0.276(3) fm²
- Asymptotic D/S state ratio predictions range from 0.0250 (V18) to 0.0255 (CD-Bonn), compared to the experimental value of 0.0256(4)
- Nonrelativistic triton binding energy predictions: CD-Bonn gives 8.00 MeV, while Nijm-I gives 7.72 MeV, Nijm-II gives 7.62 MeV, Reid93 gives 7.63 MeV, and V18 gives 7.62 MeV (experimental value: 8.48 MeV)
- Relativistic calculation with CD-Bonn gives triton binding energy of 8.2 MeV
- The pre-1990 Bonn full model had 12 parameters and χ²/datum = 1.90 for combined pp and np data, compared to 15 parameters and χ²/datum = 5.12 for Nijmegen (1978), and approximately 60 parameters and χ²/datum = 3.71 for the Paris potential (1980)

## Context

This review extends the line of meson-theoretic NN potentials developed by the Bonn group (Machleidt, Holinde, and Elster, 1987) and contrasts them with the dispersion-theoretic Paris potential (Lacombe et al., 1980) and the Nijmegen potential (Nagels, Rijken, and de Swart, 1978). The new high-precision potentials enable systematic investigation of off-shell effects in microscopic nuclear structure calculations, particularly through the comparison of predictions from the CD-Bonn potential (which retains full nonlocal Feynman amplitudes) against local potentials like Argonne V18 (Wiringa, Stoks, and Schiavilla, 1995) and Reid93 (Stoks et al., 1994). The work also discusses the need for three-nucleon forces to resolve the remaining underbinding of the triton (experimental 8.48 MeV vs. 7.62–8.00 MeV from two-body potentials alone).
