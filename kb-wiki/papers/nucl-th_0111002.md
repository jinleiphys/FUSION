# Semi-classical Characters and Optical Model Description of Heavy Ion Scattering, Direct Reactions, and Fusion at Near-barrier Energies

> An approach is proposed to calculate the direct reaction (DR) and fusion probabilities for heavy ion collisions at near-Coulomb-barrier energies as functions of the distance of closest approach D within the framework of the optical model that introduces two types of imaginary potentials, DR and fusion. The probabilities are calculated by using partial DR and fusion cross sections, together with the classical relations associated with the Coulomb trajectory. Such an approach makes it possible to analyze the data for angular distributions of the inclusive DR cross section, facilitating the determination of the radius parameters of the imaginary DR potential in a less ambiguous manner. Simultaneous $χ^{2}$-analyses are performed of relevant data for the $^{16}$O+$^{208}$Pb system near the Coulomb-barrier energy.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that direct reaction (DR) and fusion probabilities for heavy-ion collisions at near-barrier energies can be calculated within a single optical model framework using two distinct imaginary potentials, and that the sum of elastic, DR, and fusion probabilities stays close to unity (within about 20%), but the calculated DR probability is shifted to smaller distances by about 0.05 fm compared to experimental data unless the radius parameter is adjusted.

## Method

The authors introduce an optical model with two imaginary potentials: a surface-type Woods-Saxon derivative for direct reactions (DR) and a volume-type Woods-Saxon for fusion. They calculate partial-wave DR and fusion cross sections from these potentials, then convert them to probabilities as functions of the distance of closest approach D using classical Coulomb trajectory relations. Simultaneous chi-squared analyses of elastic scattering, inclusive DR angular distributions, and fusion cross section data are performed for the 16O+208Pb system at near-barrier energies to determine the strength parameters of the polarization potentials, which are constrained by the dispersion relation.

## Key numbers

- For the 16O+208Pb system at Elab=90 MeV, the maximum DR probability PD reaches approximately 0.24 at reduced distance d ≈ 1.58 fm, where the elastic probability PE is approximately 0.7
- The sum PE+PD becomes essentially zero (10^-3) at reduced distance d = dc = 1.30 fm, the s-wave Coulomb-barrier top distance
- The interaction distance dI where PE begins to fall from unity is d > 1.65 fm
- The DR radius parameter was adjusted from rD = 1.50 fm to rD = 1.55 fm to improve agreement with PD data, shifting the calculated PD by 0.05 fm
- The DR imaginary potential strength WD(E) has slope 0.015 in the threshold region (73.0 < E ≤ 92.5 MeV), saturating at 0.2925 MeV for E > 92.5 MeV
- The fusion imaginary potential strength WF(E) has slope 0.32 in the threshold region (76.0 < E ≤ 86.0 MeV), saturating at 3.2 MeV for E > 86.0 MeV
- The DR threshold energy E0;D = 73.0 MeV and fusion threshold energy E0;F = 76.0 MeV were extracted from Stelson plots
- The fusion real potential VF(E) shows an energy variation of about 2 MeV over an interval of approximately 10 MeV, while the DR real potential VD changes by only 0.1 MeV over approximately 20 MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous optical model approach (Kim 1990) for the 16O+208Pb system, incorporating revised fusion data from Morton (1999) and adding the capability to calculate DR probabilities for comparison with angular distribution data. The method builds on the semi-classical treatment of heavy-ion reactions described by Bass (1980) and Satchler (1980), and uses the dispersive optical potential formalism of Mahaux, Ngo, and Satchler (1986). The approach enables less ambiguous determination of the DR imaginary potential radius parameters and demonstrates that the threshold anomaly (rapid energy variation) appears in the fusion part of the potential but not in the DR part, which enables future studies to separate these reaction mechanisms more cleanly.
