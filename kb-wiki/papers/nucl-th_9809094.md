# Impressionism and Surrealism in Multiparticle Dynamics

> Invited talk at the CORR98 meeting on Correlations, Mátraháza, June 1998, to appear in the proceedings of the meeting in World Scientific. This talk is dedicated to the memory of Peter Carruthers.

## Key claim

The paper argues that many theoretical analyses of Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) in heavy-ion physics rely on the Wigner function formalism in a "pseudo-hydrodynamical" way that is mathematically unjustified, and that the widespread use of Gaussian parametrizations and the neglect of particle-antiparticle correlations for neutral particles leads to incorrect physical conclusions.

## Method

The author reviews the Wigner function formalism for BEC and derives its domain of validity, showing that the semiclassical approximation requires small momentum gradients across the source and that the formalism is only justified when combined with a full hydrodynamical treatment including an equation of state. He then compares the Wigner approach with the classical current formalism, demonstrating that the latter is more general and avoids violations of quantum mechanical bounds, and analyzes the specific case of photon interferometry where the neglect of same-point emission terms leads to incorrect predictions for the intercept of the correlation function.

## Key numbers

- For a system of charged pions (50% positive, 50% negative), the maximum intercept Max C₂(k,k) is 1.5; for photons, Max C₂(k,k) exceeds this value and can reach 2
- The number of independent parameters needed to characterize BEC within the Wigner formalism is 10, the same as in the current formalism
- For neutral pions the maximum intercept is 3, compared to 2 for charged pions
- The condition for semiclassical validity is Λ/(4π) |dk/dx| ≪ k, where Λ is the de Broglie wavelength

## Context

This invited talk at the CORR98 meeting (Mátraháza, June 1998) extends the author's long-standing work on BEC and multiparticle dynamics, building on the classical current formalism developed in Weiner's group (APW, 1990s) and the Wigner function approach of Carruthers and Zachariasen (1980s). The paper criticizes the pseudo-hydrodynamical analyses common in heavy-ion literature, citing the NA49 experimental paper (1998) as an example of the limits of such approaches, and challenges the results of Slotta and Heinz on photon correlations while defending the earlier work of Neuhauser (1990) and Leo (1990). The work enables a more rigorous treatment of higher-order correlations and photon interferometry for quark-gluon plasma searches.
