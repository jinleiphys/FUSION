# Recent Developments in the Nuclear Many-Body Problem

> The study of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) over the past quarter century has had relatively little impact on the traditional approach to the low-energy nuclear many-body problem. Recent developments are changing this situation. New experimental capabilities and theoretical approaches are opening windows into the richness of many-body phenomena in QCD. A common theme is the use of effective field theory (EFT) methods, which exploit the separation of scales in physical systems. At low energies, effective field theory can explain how existing phenomenology emerges from QCD and how to refine it systematically. More generally, the application of EFT methods to many-body problems promises insight into the analytic structure of observables, the identification of new expansion parameters, and a consistent organization of many-body corrections, with reliable error estimates.

## Key claim

Effective field theory (EFT) methods, which exploit separation of scales, are beginning to bridge the gap between quantum chromodynamics (QCD) and traditional low-energy nuclear many-body phenomenology, but the connection remains incomplete and many gaps remain to be filled before a fully ab initio QCD calculation of heavy nuclei is achieved.

## Method

The paper reviews the application of chiral effective field theory to nuclear many-body problems, including the construction of the most general Lagrangian consistent with QCD symmetries, the declaration of a regularization and renormalization scheme (using a smooth momentum cutoff), and a well-defined power counting that provides small expansion parameters (momentum or pion mass divided by the chiral symmetry breaking scale of about 1 GeV). The author then surveys how these EFT methods are applied across different nuclear regimes: Green's function Monte Carlo for light nuclei up to A=10, shell model approaches with renormalization group methods, and Kohn-Sham density functional theory with naturalness-based power counting for heavy nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The chiral symmetry breaking scale Lambda_chi is approximately 1 GeV, and the pion decay constant f_pi is approximately 100 MeV
- For two massless quark flavors in color superconductivity, the gap Delta is of order 100 MeV (with current uncertainties plus or minus a factor of two), and critical temperatures are of order 50 MeV or 10^12 K
- Green's function Monte Carlo calculations have been pushed through A=10 with an accuracy in binding energies of 1% or better
- For the dilute Fermi gas with hard sphere radius R, the EFT expansion parameter is k_F times R, where k_F is the Fermi momentum
- The impact of color superconductivity on the equation of state is of order (Delta/mu)^2, which at a few percent is much less than the uncertainties in the equation of state

## Context

This review extends the traditional nuclear many-body problem into the broader QCD many-body problem, encompassing the full QCD phase diagram including color superconductivity and small-x physics. The work builds on prior chiral EFT calculations by Epelbaum et al. (2000) for nucleon-nucleon phase shifts, Green's function Monte Carlo results by Carlson et al. (2000) for light nuclei, and shell model renormalization group approaches by Haxton and collaborators (2000). The paper enables future work connecting lattice QCD calculations to nuclear phenomenology through matching procedures, and identifies open questions such as whether the Delta resonance should be included as a Lagrangian degree of freedom and how to correctly interpolate color superconducting phases for real-world quark masses.
