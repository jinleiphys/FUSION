# Coherent gamma photon generation in a Bose-Einstein condensate of $^{135m}$Cs

> We have identified a mechanism of collective nuclear de-excitation in a Bose-Einstein condensate of $^{135}$Cs atoms in their isomeric states, $^{135m}$Cs, suitable for the generation of coherent gamma photons. The process described here does not correspond to single-pass amplification, which cannot occur in atomic systems due to the large shift between absorption and emission lines, nor does it require the large densities associated to standard Dicke super-radiance. It thus overcome the limitations that have been hindering the generation of coherent gamma rays in many systems. Therefore, we propose an approach for generation of coherent gamma rays, which relies on a combination of well established techniques of nuclear and atomic physics, and can be realized with currently available technology.

# Coherent gamma photon generation in a Bose-Einstein condensate of $^{135m}$Cs

## Key claim
The authors identify a mechanism for collective nuclear de-excitation in a Bose-Einstein condensate of $^{135m}$Cs isomers that produces coherent gamma photons, but only under the condition that the BEC's coherence triggers an absolute instability, this is not single-pass amplification (which is impossible due to nuclear recoil) nor standard Dicke super-radiance (which requires unattainable densities). The mechanism relies on coherence transfer from the BEC to the photon field, and the authors claim it can be realized with currently available technology.

## Method
The authors model the $^{135m}$Cs isomer as a two-level system (the M4 transition at 846.1 keV from $J^\pi=19/2^-$ to $11/2^+$) and write the Hamiltonian for the atomic BEC, the photon field, and their interaction. Using the Bogoliubov c-number approximation to replace the excited-state annihilation operator with $\sqrt{N_0}$ (treating the BEC as a pure condensate with condensed fraction unity), they derive coupled Heisenberg equations for the photon and intermediate-state operators. They then solve for the total number of photons emitted in a given mode and identify the condition for exponential growth (absolute instability) as $\hbar^2\Delta_{\mathbf{k}}^2 - 4 n_0 \sum_\zeta |\xi_\zeta(\mathbf{k},0)|^2 < 0$, which defines a frequency interval around the recoil-shifted resonance where collective emission occurs.

## Key numbers
- The M4 gamma transition energy is 846.1 keV, with a half-life of $T_{1/2}=53$ min for the $^{135m}$Cs isomeric state.
- The nuclear recoil energy is approximately 2.8 eV, while the natural linewidth at BEC temperature is $\Gamma \sim 10^{-19}$ eV.
- The density required for standard Dicke super-radiance would be $n_{SR} \geq 10^{29}$ cm$^{-3}$, which is unattainable.
- The internal conversion coefficient is $\alpha_{IC} \approx 0.04$, so internal conversion can be neglected.
- The isomeric shift between $^{135m}$Cs and $^{135}$Cs for the D$_2$ line is approximately 0.8 GHz, roughly $10^2$ times larger than the natural linewidth.
- The critical parameter $\delta_1$ at $\Delta_{\mathbf{k}}=0$ scales as $\sqrt{n_0}$, and the authors estimate a burst of $10^4$–$10^5$ coherent photons for a BEC of similar atom number to stable cesium experiments.
- The BEC temperature is approximately $10^{-7}$ K, with densities in the range $10^{12}$ cm$^{-3} \leq n_0 \leq 10^{14}$ cm$^{-3}$ where the instability condition is satisfied.

## Context
This work extends the concept of collective annihilation from positronium BECs (Armenia et al., 2015) to nuclear gamma-ray emission, proposing a mechanism distinct from single-pass amplification (which is blocked by nuclear recoil) and from standard Dicke super-radiance (Baldwin, 1986), which requires densities $n_{SR} \geq \lambda_0^{-3}$ that are impossible for nuclear transitions. The approach overcomes the two fundamental problems of gamma-ray laser proposals: accumulating a large number of isomeric nuclei and reducing Doppler broadening. The use of $^{135m}$Cs is motivated by the availability of established techniques for producing the isomer via proton-induced fission of actinides, followed by neutralization, laser cooling, and trapping analogous to stable $^{133}$Cs. The work enables experimental searches for coherent gamma emission using existing BEC technology, though the collisional properties of ultra-cold $^{135m}$Cs are not yet known, preventing precise estimates of BEC size and photon burst intensity.
