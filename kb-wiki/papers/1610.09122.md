# Calculation of the astrophysical S-factor S_12 with the Lorentz integral transform

> The LIT approach is tested for the calculation of astrophysical S-factors. As an example the S-factor of the reaction 2H(p,gamma)3He is considered. It is discussed that a sufficiently high density of LIT states at low energies is necessary for a precise determination of S-factors. In particular it is shown that the hyperspherical basis is not very well suited for such a calculation and that a different basis system is much more advantageous. A comparison of LIT results with calculations, where continuum wave functions are explicitly used, shows that the LIT approach leads to reliable results. It is also shown how an error estimate of the LIT inversion can be obtained.

# Calculation of the astrophysical S-factor S_12 with the Lorentz integral transform

## Key claim

The Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method can reliably determine low-energy astrophysical S-factors in the presence of a Coulomb barrier, provided that a sufficiently high density of LIT states is achieved in the low-energy region; the hyperspherical harmonics (HH) basis is not well suited for this purpose, whereas a Jacobi-coordinate basis that explicitly includes the single-particle coordinate yields the necessary state density.

## Method

The authors test the LIT approach by calculating the S-factor for the reaction ²H(p,γ)³He using the central MT-I/III nucleon-nucleon potential in unretarded dipole approximation. They solve the LIT equation via expansion in two different basis systems: a hyperspherical harmonics (HH) basis and a Jacobi-coordinate basis where the pair coordinate and single-particle coordinate are expanded separately using Laguerre polynomials. For validation, they also compute the continuum wave functions explicitly using the Kohn variational principle with HH expansions and compare the resulting photodisintegration cross sections and S-factors.

## Key numbers

- The ³He binding energy with the MT-I/III potential is about 8 MeV (three-body breakup threshold); the two-body breakup threshold (difference of binding energies of ²H and ³He) is at 5.8 MeV.
- For the Jacobi basis, convergence of the LIT in the region just above the two-body breakup threshold with σ_I = 0.125 MeV requires N₂ ≈ 70 basis states for the single-particle coordinate; the final calculation uses N₂ = 79.
- The P-wave phase shifts from the Kohn variational calculation agree with those of the Pisa group to about 0.5% or less.
- Stable inversion results are obtained for N = 11 to N = 18 basis functions in the inversion expansion (Eqs. inv1 and inv2).
- The LIT calculation uses σ_I values ranging from 0.125 MeV (lowest energy interval, 5.7–6.15 MeV) up to 20 MeV (highest interval, 108.05–308.05 MeV).

## Context

This work extends the LIT method, previously applied to various nuclear reactions (Efros 2007, Barnea 2014), to the regime of extremely small low-energy cross sections relevant to stellar nucleosynthesis. It addresses a limitation identified in calculations of the ⁴He isoscalar monopole resonance (Bacca 2013), where the HH basis gave insufficient LIT state density below the three-body breakup threshold. The authors follow the strategy proposed by Leidemann (2015) of using a hybrid basis that explicitly includes the single-particle coordinate. The successful test with the MT-I/III potential enables future application of the LIT method to S-factors involving a higher number of nucleons, complementing ab initio calculations such as those by Marcucci (2005, 2016).
