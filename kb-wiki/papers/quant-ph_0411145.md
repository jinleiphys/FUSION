# Quantum Zeno effect by general measurements

> It was predicted that frequently repeated measurements on an unstable quantum state may alter the decay rate of the state. This is called the quantum Zeno effect (QZE) or the anti-Zeno effect (AZE), depending on whether the decay is suppressed or enhanced. In conventional theories of the QZE and AZE, effects of measurements are simply described by the projection postulate, assuming that each measurement is an instantaneous and ideal one. However, real measurements are not instantaneous and ideal. For the QZE and AZE by such general measurements, interesting and surprising features have recently been revealed, which we review in this article. The results are based on the quantum measurement theory, which is also reviewed briefly. As a typical model, we consider a continuous measurement of the decay of an excited atom by a photodetector that detects a photon emitted from the atom upon decay. This measurement is an indirect negative-result one, for which the curiosity of the QZE and AZE is emphasized. It is shown that the form factor is renormalized as a backaction of the measurement, through which the decay dynamics is modified. In a special case of the flat response, where the detector responds to every photon mode with an identical response time, results of the conventional theories are reproduced qualitatively. However, drastic differences emerge in general cases where the detector responds only to limited photon modes. For example, against predictions of the conventional theories, the QZE or AZE may take place even for states that exactly follow the exponential decay law. We also discuss relation to the cavity quantum electrodynamics.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the conventional predictions of the quantum Zeno effect (QZE) and anti-Zeno effect (AZE), which assume instantaneous ideal measurements described by the projection postulate, are drastically modified under general (realistic) measurements; in particular, the QZE or AZE can occur even for states that exactly follow the exponential decay law, contrary to the conventional expectation that such effects require a quadratic short-time behavior.

## Method

The authors employ the quantum measurement theory to analyze a continuous, indirect, negative-result measurement of an excited atom decaying by photon emission, modeled as a two-level atom coupled to a photon field (Friedrichs model). They treat the joint quantum system of the atom and a photodetector (the measuring apparatus) without invoking the projection postulate, and derive how the form factor of the atom-photon interaction is renormalized as a backaction of the measurement, which modifies the decay dynamics. The analysis considers both the case of flat detector response (responding identically to all photon modes) and general cases where the detector responds only to limited photon modes.

## Key numbers

* The survival probability at very short times follows \( s(t) = 1 - \langle (\Delta \hat{H}_S)^2 \rangle t^2 + \mathcal{O}(t^4) \), where \(\langle (\Delta \hat{H}_S)^2 \rangle\) is the variance of the Hamiltonian in the initial state.
* The jump time \( t_{\rm j} \) is defined as the time scale at which the crossover between the quadratic short-time behavior and the later exponential decay occurs.
* The measurement interval \( \tau_{\rm i} \) is compared to the jump time \( t_{\rm j} \): the QZE (suppression of decay) occurs when \( \tau_{\rm i} / t_{\rm j} \to +0 \), while the AZE (acceleration of decay) can occur for \( \tau_{\rm i} \sim t_{\rm j} \).
* The form factor \( |g_{\mu}|^2 \) is defined by \( |g_{\mu}|^2 = \int d\veck |g_{\veck}|^2 \delta(\eps_{\veck} - \mu) \), and the radiative decay rate from the Fermi golden rule is given by \( \Gamma = 2\pi |g_{\Omega}|^2 \).

## Context

This review extends the foundational work on the QZE by Beskow and Nilsson (1967), Khalfin (1968), and Misra and Sudarshan (1977), which assumed instantaneous ideal measurements via the projection postulate, and the later work on the AZE by Lane (1983) and Kofman and Kurizki (1996). The paper builds on the authors' own recent results (Koshino and Shimizu, 2004; Koshino, 2004) showing that the Zeno effect can occur even for purely exponential decay, and it connects the analysis to cavity quantum electrodynamics (cavity QED) and the concept of motional narrowing. The work enables a more realistic treatment of measurement-induced modifications of decay in quantum optics and other systems where detector response is non-ideal.
