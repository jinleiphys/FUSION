# Pulsar Signal of Deconfinement

## Key claim

A solitary millisecond pulsar that is near the mass limit and undergoes a phase transition (first or second order) to a substantially more compressible phase will exhibit spontaneous spin-up, a blatantly observable signal. The signal is estimated to be "on" for 1/50 of the spin-down era of millisecond pulsars, but the transition must be accompanied by a sufficient softening of the equation of state, and the critical density must be attained in stars very near the mass limit.

## Method

The author computes a sequence of rotating neutron stars in General Relativity with constant baryon number but varying angular velocity, using an equation of state that includes a deconfinement phase transition from hadronic matter (nucleons, hyperons, and leptons) through a mixed phase to pure quark matter. The General Relativistic expression for a rotating star incorporates centrifugal stretching, changes in composition, changes in the space-time metric from rotation, and frame dragging. The time evolution of angular velocity is then computed from the moment of inertia as a function of angular velocity using the magnetic dipole energy-loss formula.

## Key numbers

- The spin-up epoch lasts 2 × 10^7 years, compared to a characteristic spin-down life of 10^9 years for millisecond pulsars, giving an event rate of 1/50.
- The period of PSR 1937+21 is measured as P = 1.5578064487275(3) ms, with a period change rate of approximately 10^-19.
- The pure quark phase appears only when the rotational frequency falls below approximately 1370 rad/s.
- The central density rises by a factor of three and the equatorial radius decreases by 30 percent as the star spins down through the transition.
- The epoch over which the braking index is anomalous is 10^8 years.
- About 25 solitary millisecond pulsars are presently known out of approximately 50 total millisecond pulsars discovered.

## Context

This work extends the analogy of "backbending" observed in high-spin nuclei in the 1970s (Mottelson and Valatin 1960; Johnson and Stephens 1972) to neutron star physics, where a phase change from stiff nuclear matter to compressible quark matter causes a decrease in moment of inertia and consequent spin-up. The phenomenon is contrasted with the normal spin-down behavior of pulsars due to magnetic dipole radiation. The signal enables a direct observational test for deconfinement in dense matter, complementary to searches in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, though the author notes that failure to observe the signal would not prove the deconfined phase does not exist.
