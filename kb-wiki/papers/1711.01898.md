# A long-lived remnant neutron star after GW170817 inferred from its associated kilonova

> The successful joint observation of the gravitational wave event GW170817 and its multi-wavelength electromagnetic counterparts first enables human to witness a definite merger event of two neutron stars (NSs). This historical event confirms the origin of short-duration gamma-ray bursts (GRBs), and in particular, identifies the theoretically-predicted kilonova phenomenon that is powered by radioactive decays of $r$-process heavy elements. However, whether a long-lived remnant NS could be formed during this merger event remains unknown, although such a central engine has been suggested by afterglow observations of some short-duration GRBs. By invoking this long-lived remnant NS, we here propose a model of hybrid energy sources for the kilonova AT2017gfo associated with GW 170817. While the early emission of AT2017gfo is still powered radioactively as usually suggested, its late emission is primarily caused by delayed energy injection from the remnant NS. In our model, only one single opacity is required and an intermediate value of $κ\simeq0.97\,\rm cm^2g^{-1}$ is revealed, which could be naturally provided by lanthanide-rich ejecta that is deeply ionized by the emission from a wind of the NS. These self-consistent results indicate that a long-lived remnant NS, which must own a very stiff equation of state, had been formed during the merger event of GW170817. This provides a very stringent constraint on the strong interaction in nuclear-quark matter. It is further implied that such GW events could provide a probe of the early spin and magnetic evolutions of NSs, e.g., the burying of surface magnetic fields.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the kilonova AT2017gfo associated with GW170817 was powered by hybrid energy sources, radioactive decay for early emission and delayed energy injection from a long-lived remnant neutron star for late emission, rather than by multiple ejecta components with different opacities, and they infer that this remnant neutron star must have a very stiff equation of state and survive for at least about 20 days after the merger.

## Method

The authors modeled the bolometric and multi-filter UVOIR light curves of AT2017gfo using a simplified radiation transfer code with a power-law density distribution for the merger ejecta, where the thermal energy evolution of each mass layer included both radioactive heating and energy injection from a central neutron star wind. They assumed black-body spectra and performed a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) fit with seven free parameters (five ejecta parameters and two neutron star parameters) to the homogenized observational dataset from Villar et al. (2017), adopting a spin-down decay index of α=1 corresponding to gravitational-wave braking rather than magnetic dipole braking.

## Key numbers

- Ejecta mass: M_ej = 0.0298 ± 0.0002 M_⊙ (2.98 ± 0.02 × 10⁻² M_⊙)
- Opacity: κ = 0.97⁺⁰·⁰¹₋₀·₀₂ cm² g⁻¹
- Minimum ejecta velocity: v_min = 0.10⁺⁰·⁰²₋₀·₀₁ c
- Maximum ejecta velocity: v_max = 0.40⁺⁰·⁰⁰₋₀·₀₁ c
- Density power-law index: δ = 1.46⁺⁰·⁰⁴₋₀·₀₅
- Absorbed neutron star wind power: ξ L_md(0) = (1.98 ± 0.03) × 10⁴¹ erg s⁻¹
- Spin-down timescale: t_sd = 1.53⁺⁰·⁰²₋₀·⁰³ × 10⁵ s (about 1.77 days)
- Peak bolometric luminosity of AT2017gfo: L_bol,p ∼ 5 × 10⁴¹ erg s⁻¹ at t_p ∼ 5000 s after GW170817
- Distance to source: D = 40 Mpc

## Context

This work extends the hybrid-energy-source model for kilonovae originally proposed by Yu et al. (2013), where a remnant neutron star provides additional power beyond radioactivity, and applies it to the well-observed event AT2017gfo. The authors argue that their single-opacity model (κ ∼ 0.97 cm² g⁻¹) avoids the need for multiple ejecta components with different opacities as invoked by Villar et al. (2017) and others, and that the inferred long-lived remnant neutron star with a mass of about 2.6 M_⊙ provides a stringent constraint on the neutron star equation of state, favoring stiff EOSs such as MS1, SHT, NL3ωρ, and others that allow a maximum non-rotating mass above ∼2.6 M_⊙. The results also suggest that the surface magnetic field of the remnant neutron star was buried by fallback accretion, suppressing magnetic dipole radiation and allowing gravitational-wave braking to dominate the spin-down, which could explain the "internal plateau + steep decay" structure seen in many short GRB afterglows.
