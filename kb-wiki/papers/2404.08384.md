# High-fidelity Nuclear Coherent Population Transfer via the Mixed-State Inverse Engineering

> Nuclear coherent population transfer (NCPT) plays an important role in the exploration and application of atomic nuclei. How to achieve high-fidelity NCPT remains so far challenging. Here, we investigate the complete population transfer of nuclear states. We first consider a cyclic three-level system, based on the mixed-state inverse engineering scheme by adding additional laser fields in an open three-level nuclear system with spontaneous emission. We find the amplitude of the additional field is related to the ratio of the pump and Stokes field amplitudes. As long as an appropriate additional field is selected, complete transfer can be achieved even when the intensities of the pump and Stokes fields are exceedingly low. The transfer efficiency exhibits excellent robustness with respect to laser peak intensity and pulse delay. We demonstrate the effectiveness through examples such as $^{229}$Th, $^{223}$Ra, $^{113}$Cd, and $^{97}$Tc, which have a long lifetime excited state, as well as $^{187}$Re, $^{172}$Yb, $^{168}$Er and $^{154}$Gd with a short lifetime excited state. Focusing on the case without additional coupling, we further reduce the three-level system to an effective two-level problem. We modify the pump and Stokes pulses by using counterdiabatic driving to implement high-fidelity population transfer. The schemes open up new possibilities for controlling nuclear states.

## Key claim

The paper claims that high-fidelity nuclear coherent population transfer can be achieved via a mixed-state inverse engineering (MIE) scheme even when pump and Stokes field intensities are exceedingly low, provided an appropriate additional laser field is selected; the transfer efficiency is robust to variations in laser peak intensity and pulse delay, and the scheme works for nuclei with both long and short excited-state lifetimes.

## Method

The authors model a cyclic three-level nuclear Lambda-system (ground state, isomeric state, and excited state) interacting with three X-ray laser pulses, including spontaneous emission from the excited state. They apply the mixed-state inverse engineering scheme by solving the Lindblad master equation for the density matrix and using the instantaneous steady state as the evolutionary trajectory to derive the required additional coupling field. For the case without additional coupling, they reduce the three-level system to an effective two-level problem under one-photon resonance and modify the pump and Stokes pulses using counterdiabatic driving to achieve high-fidelity population transfer.

## Key numbers

- For long lifetime excited state nuclei: $^{229}$Th (lifetime 0.172 ns), $^{223}$Ra (0.6 ns), $^{113}$Cd (0.322 ns), and $^{97}$Tc (0.76 ps) achieve nearly perfect NCPT under the MIE scheme.
- For short lifetime excited state nuclei: $^{187}$Re (54 fs), $^{172}$Yb (11 fs), $^{168}$Er (3.5 fs), and $^{154}$Gd (1.54 fs) also achieve high-fidelity population transfer under MIE.
- For $^{97}$Tc, the Stokes laser intensity is set to $I_s = 23.514 I_p$; for $^{168}$Er, $I_s = 0.0703 I_p$.
- The infidelity for both $^{97}$Tc and $^{154}$Gd under the MIE scheme can be kept below $10^{-2}$.
- For the effective two-level shortcut scheme with $^{97}$Tc, the real intensities are $I_p = 1 \times 10^{23}$ W/cm$^2$ and $I_s = 2.3514 \times 10^{24}$ W/cm$^2$.
- For the effective two-level shortcut scheme with $^{154}$Gd, the real intensities are $I_p = 7.8927 \times 10^{21}$ W/cm$^2$ and $I_s = 1.4175 \times 10^{21}$ W/cm$^2$.
- The additional field amplitude is on the order of $10^{15}$ W/cm$^2$ for controlling perfect population transfer when pump and Stokes fields have lower strengths.

## Context

This work extends the mixed-state inverse engineering scheme, previously applied to a single nitrogen-vacancy center and open two-level quantum systems (Wang 2024), to nuclear coherent population transfer in a cyclic three-level system. It contrasts with traditional STIRAP techniques used in nuclear systems such as Lambda-like three-state systems (PhysRevC.87.054609, PhysRevC.94.054601) and tripod systems (PhysRevC.96.044619), which require adiabatic conditions and longer interaction times that lead to decoherence. The results enable fast, robust control of nuclear states for applications including nuclear clocks and quantum information processing, and provide a viable experimental pathway using seeded XFEL sources with photon energy 12.4 keV, bandwidth 10 meV, and pulse duration 0.1 ps.
