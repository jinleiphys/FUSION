# High Density Behaviour of Nuclear Symmetry Energy and High Energy Heavy-Ion Collisions

> High energy heavy-ion collisions are proposed as a novel means to obtain information about the high density ({\rm HD}) behaviour of nuclear symmetry energy. Within an isospin-dependent hadronic transport model using phenomenological equations of state ({\rm EOS}) for dense neutron-rich matter, it is shown that the isospin asymmetry of the HD nuclear matter formed in high energy heavy-ion collisions is determined mainly by the HD behaviour of nuclear symmetry energy. Experimental signatures in several sensitive probes, i.e., $π^-$ to $π^+$ ratio, transverse collective flow and its excitation function as well as neutron-proton differential flow, are investigated. A precursor of the possible isospin separation instability in dense neutron-rich matter is predicted to appear as the local minima in the excitation functions of the transverse flow parameter for both neutrons and protons above the pion production threshold. Because of its {\it qualitative} nature unlike other {\it quantitative} observables, this precursor can be used as a unique signature of the isospin dependence of the nuclear {\rm EOS}. Measurements of these observables will provide the first terrestrial data to constrain stringently the HD behaviour of nuclear symmetry energy and thus also the {\rm EOS} of dense neutron-rich matter. Implications of our findings to neutron star studies are also discussed.

## Key claim

High energy heavy-ion collisions can serve as a novel terrestrial probe of the high density behavior of nuclear symmetry energy, but the predicted signatures depend qualitatively on whether the symmetry energy rises monotonically or falls above about twice normal nuclear matter density. The paper emphasizes that a precursor of the isospin separation instability, a local minimum in the excitation function of transverse flow, is a qualitative rather than quantitative observable, making it a unique signature distinct from other probes.

## Method

The authors employed an isospin-dependent hadronic transport model (IBUU) that solves the phase-space evolution of nucleons, Delta resonances, and pions with explicit isospin degrees of freedom, using test-particle methods. Two representative forms of the symmetry energy were used: one increasing linearly with density (E^a_sym) and one that peaks near 1.5ρ₀ then falls to zero at a critical density u_c=3 (E^b_sym), reflecting the two main classes of many-body predictions. The model was applied to central and mid-central collisions of neutron-rich nuclei such as ¹³²Sn + ¹²⁴Sn at beam energies from 200 to 2000 MeV/nucleon, and several observables were computed including the π⁻/π⁺ ratio, transverse collective flow, and neutron-proton differential flow.

## Key numbers

- The symmetry energy at normal nuclear matter density is taken as E_sym(ρ₀) = 30 MeV, consistent with the range 34 ± 4 MeV from atomic mass data.
- For the E^b_sym form, the critical reduced density is u_c = 3 (i.e., ρ_c = 3ρ₀), at which the symmetry energy crosses zero; the predicted u_c from many-body theories ranges from about 2.7 to 9.
- In central ¹³²Sn + ¹²⁴Sn collisions at impact parameter 1 fm, maximum baryon densities of about 1.7ρ₀ to 3.7ρ₀ are reached for beam energies from 200 to 2000 MeV/nucleon.
- The E^b_sym form leads to about 10-15% higher compression than the E^a_sym form.
- Pion production reduces the high-density n/p ratio by about 15%, while the sensitivity to symmetry energy remains almost unchanged.
- A variation of about 30% in the high-density n/p ratio due to different symmetry energies results in about a 15% change in the final π⁻/π⁺ ratio, giving an approximate response factor of 0.5.
- A local minimum in the excitation function of the transverse flow parameter appears around E_beam/A = 500 MeV for both neutrons and protons with the E^b_sym form; with the E^a_sym form, a local minimum for protons appears around E_beam/A = 300 MeV.
- The maximum population of Δ(1232) resonances is about 2% at 400 MeV/nucleon and about 20% at 2000 MeV/nucleon in central ¹³²Sn + ¹²⁴Sn collisions.

## Context

This work extends the longstanding effort to determine the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy, which has been studied with microscopic and phenomenological models by Wiringa (1988), Brown (2000), and many others, but remains extremely uncertain above normal nuclear density. The paper proposes high energy heavy-ion collisions as a terrestrial complement to astrophysical constraints from neutron star properties, such as those studied by Lattimer (1991) and Prakash (1997). The predicted local minimum in the flow excitation function is analogous to the softening signature associated with quark-gluon plasma formation in ultra-relativistic collisions, and the results enable future experiments at facilities like the Rare Isotope Accelerator (RIA) to constrain the equation of state of dense neutron-rich matter and its implications for neutron star composition and cooling.
