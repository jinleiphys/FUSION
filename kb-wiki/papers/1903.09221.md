# Extreme Gravity and Fundamental Physics

> 14 pages, 2 figures, White Paper submitted to the Astro-2020 (2020 Astronomy and Astrophysics Decadal Survey) by GWIC-3G Science Case Team (GWIC: Gravitational-Wave International Committee); replaced Figure 1 with a revised version that incorporates Gravity IR Flare data point

## Key claim

Future third-generation gravitational-wave observatories (3G) such as the Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer will enable tests of general relativity and searches for new physics in the strong-field regime, but the paper frames this as a high-reward yet uncertain prospect, noting that new physics "could reveal itself" rather than asserting that it will be found.

## Method

The paper is a white paper submitted to the Astro-2020 decadal survey by the GWIC-3G Science Case Team, reviewing the scientific potential of next-generation gravitational-wave detectors. It surveys three broad areas: tests of gravity (including searches for extra fields, graviton mass, Lorentz violations, and parity violations), probes of dark matter (primordial black holes, dark matter accretion and capture by compact objects), and studies of compact objects (black hole spectroscopy, neutron star equation of state, exotic compact objects, and bosonic clouds from superradiance). The methodology is a forward-looking assessment of projected observational capabilities rather than new experimental or theoretical results.

## Key numbers

- Current bound on graviton mass from LIGO: m_g < 7.7 x 10^{-23} eV/c^2, with improvements of two orders of magnitude possible with 3G detectors
- 3G detectors will observe stellar-mass black holes at redshifts of z ~ 10-20
- 3G detectors will improve current measurements of tidal deformability by a factor of approximately 10
- Bosonic clouds from superradiance can have masses up to approximately 10% of the black hole mass, for boson masses of 10^{-21} to 10^{-11} eV
- 3G detectors will observe sources at high redshifts of z ~ 10-20

## Context

This white paper extends the science case established by the first LIGO/Virgo detections (GW150914 and GW170817) and projects the capabilities of proposed 3G observatories beyond the current Advanced LIGO and Virgo era. It builds on prior work by Berti (2018), Yunes (2016), and others on testing general relativity with gravitational waves, and by Cardoso (2017) and Barack (2018) on exotic compact objects. The paper enables a community-wide discussion of priorities for the 2020 Astronomy and Astrophysics Decadal Survey, identifying open questions about the nature of gravity, dark matter, and compact objects that 3G detectors could address.
