# Aspects of univalence in holographic axion models

> Univalent functions are complex, analytic (holomorphic) and injective functions that have been widely discussed in complex analysis. It was recently proposed that the stringent constraints that univalence imposes on the growth of functions combined with sufficient analyticity conditions could be used to derive rigorous lower and upper bounds on hydrodynamic dispersion relation, i.e., on all terms appearing in their convergent series representations. The results are exact bounds on physical quantities such as the diffusivity and the speed of sound. The purpose of this paper is to further explore these ideas, investigate them in concrete holographic examples, and work towards a better intuitive understanding of the role of univalence in physics. More concretely, we study diffusive and sound modes in a family of holographic axion models and offer a set of observations, arguments and tests that support the applicability of univalence methods for bounding physical observables described in terms of effective field theories. Our work provides insight into expected `typical' regions of univalence, comparisons between the tightness of bounds and the corresponding exact values of certain quantities characterizing transport, tests of relations between diffusion and bounds that involve chaotic pole-skipping, as well as tests of a condition that implies the conformal bound on the speed of sound and a complementary condition that implies its violation.

## Key claim

The paper tests whether the recently proposed method of using univalent (complex analytic and injective) functions to derive rigorous bounds on hydrodynamic dispersion relations is generically applicable, by examining concrete holographic axion models; the authors find that while univalence methods can be applied, the bounds are often not tight and the regions of univalence are typically smaller than the disk of convergence of the hydrodynamic series.

## Method

The authors study the hydrodynamic dispersion relations of diffusive and sound modes in two families of holographic axion models: one with explicitly broken translational symmetry (the linear axion model) and one with spontaneously broken translational symmetry (a holographic solid model). They compute the complex spectral curves and dispersion relations numerically from the holographic dual gravity theories, then determine the regions of univalence in the complex momentum plane by checking for zeros of the group velocity (ω'(z) = 0) and applying the Noshiro-Warschawski condition Re f'(z) > 0. They compare the exact values of transport coefficients (diffusivity, speed of sound) with the bounds derived from univalence inequalities, including the growth theorem and the MacGregor (log) bounds, and test relations involving pole-skipping and the conformal bound on the speed of sound.

## Key numbers

- For the linear axion model with explicit breaking parameter m/T = 4.8, the energy diffusion constant is D = 0.208/(2πT) and the univalence radius in the z = q² variable is |z_U| ≈ 0.6/(2πT)², which is smaller than the radius of convergence |z_R| ≈ 1.0/(2πT)².
- For the holographic solid model with spontaneous breaking parameter m/T = 1.0, the longitudinal sound speed is v_s = 0.561, and the univalence radius in the z = q variable is |z_U| ≈ 0.5/(2πT), compared to the radius of convergence |z_R| ≈ 1.0/(2πT).
- The conformal bound on the speed of sound v_s ≤ 1/√3 ≈ 0.577 is satisfied in the holographic solid model for m/T = 1.0 (v_s = 0.561), but the authors also find a complementary condition that implies its violation for larger values of m/T.
- The pole-skipping point for energy diffusion in the linear axion model occurs at ω = -i/(2πT) and q = 0, and the authors test the relation between this point and the univalence bounds.

## Context

This work extends the complex analytic approach to hydrodynamic bounds introduced by Grozdanov (2020) by testing it in concrete holographic models, specifically the holographic axion models reviewed by Baggioli (2021). The paper provides numerical evidence for the qualitative properties of diffusion and sound dispersion relations found in that earlier work, and establishes connections between univalence and the complex spectral curves studied by Grozdanov (2019). The results enable future applications of univalence methods to other effective field theories and provide insight into the typical size of univalence regions and the tightness of the resulting bounds on transport coefficients.
