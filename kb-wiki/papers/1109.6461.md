# Helicity dependent parton distributions

> The helicity dependent parton distributions describe the number density of partons with given longitudinal momentum x and given polarization in a hadron polarized longitudinally with respect to its motion. After the discovery, more than 70 years ago, that the proton is not elementary, the observation of Bjorken scaling in the late 1960s lead to the idea of hadrons containing almost pointlike constituents, the partons. Since then, Deep Inelastic Scattering (DIS) has played a crucial role in our understanding of hadron structure. Through DIS experiments it has been possible to link the partons to the quarks, and to unveil the presence of other pointlike constituents, the gluons, which lead into a dynamical theory of quarks and gluons - quantum chromodynamics (QCD). Polarized DIS, i.e. the collision of a longitudinally polarized lepton beam on a polarized target (either longitudinally or transversely polarized), provides a complementary information regarding the structure of the nucleon. Whereas ordinary DIS probes simply the number density of partons with a fraction x of the momentum of the parent hadron, polarized DIS can partly answer the question as to the number density of partons with given x and given spin polarization in a hadron of definite polarization, either parallel or transverse with respect to the motion of the hadron. In here, the phenomenology associated with DIS off longitudinally polarized targets, and the present theoretical understanding of the dynamics involved, are described.

## Key claim

This review describes the phenomenology and theoretical understanding of deep inelastic scattering (DIS) off longitudinally polarized targets, showing that while quark helicities contribute a significant fraction of the nucleon spin, the precise magnitude and shape of the gluon contribution remains unknown and could still be an important fraction of the total.

## Method

The paper reviews the formalism of polarized DIS, including the spin-dependent structure functions g1 and g2, the parton model description in terms of helicity-dependent parton distributions Δq(x), and the QCD generalization with factorization, evolution, and gluonic contributions. It then surveys experimental results from inclusive and semi-inclusive polarized DIS experiments at SLAC, CERN, DESY, and Jefferson Lab, as well as polarized proton-proton scattering at RHIC, and compares them with predictions from constituent quark models and perturbative QCD analyses.

## Key numbers

- The anomalous magnetic moment of the proton is κp ≈ 1.79 Bohr magnetons (1933 measurement)
- The Bjorken sum rule at leading order: ∫₀¹ dx [g₁ᵖ(x,Q²) - g₁ⁿ(x,Q²)] = gA/6, with gA = 1.2670 ± 0.0035
- The flavor octet axial charge gA⁸ = 0.585 ± 0.025 from hyperon β-decay
- QCD corrections to the Bjorken sum rule up to third order: (gA/6) × {1 - αS/π - 3.583(αS/π)² - 20.215(αS/π)³}
- The Ellis-Jaffe sum rule prediction gA⁰ ≈ gA⁸ ≈ 0.6, contradicted by the EMC measurement giving gA⁰ ≈ 0
- To resolve the spin crisis via gluon helicity alone would require ΔG ≈ 1.7, while observed values give |ΔG| ≈ 0.29
- Valence quarks carry roughly 60% of the nucleon spin, while sea quarks reduce this to about 30-35%

## Context

This review extends the line of work following the 1988 EMC measurement (Ashman et al., 1988) that revealed the "proton spin crisis," contradicting the Ellis-Jaffe sum rule (Ellis and Jaffe, 1974) and the non-relativistic quark model expectation that quarks carry most of the proton spin. The paper discusses how subsequent experiments have confirmed the fundamental Bjorken sum rule (Bjorken, 1970) with QCD corrections (Larin et al., 1997), and how modern analyses combining inclusive DIS, semi-inclusive DIS (SIDIS) from HERMES and COMPASS, and polarized pp scattering at RHIC (De Florian et al., recent) are constraining the helicity-dependent parton distributions. The review enables future work on the remaining open questions: the precise gluon helicity contribution, quark orbital angular momentum (accessible through Generalized Parton Distributions and Ji's sum rule, Ji, 1997), and higher-twist effects at intermediate Q².
