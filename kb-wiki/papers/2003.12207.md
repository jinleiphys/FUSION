# Calibration of Energy Density Functionals with Deformed Nuclei

> Nuclear density functional theory is the prevalent theoretical framework for accurately describing nuclear properties at the scale of the entire chart of nuclides. Given an energy functional and a many-body scheme (e.g., single- or multireference level), the predictive power of the theory depends strongly on how the parameters of the energy functionals have been calibrated with experimental data. Expanded algorithms and computing power have enabled recent optimization protocols to include data in deformed nuclei in order to optimize the coupling constants of the energy functional. The primary motivation of this work is to test the robustness of such protocols with respect to some of the technical and numerical details of the underlying calculations, especially when the calibration explores a large parameter space. To this end, we quantify the effect of these uncertainties on both the optimization and statistical emulation of composite objective functions. We also emphasize that Bayesian calibration can provide better estimates of the theoretical errors used to define objective functions.

## Key claim

The paper tests whether calibration protocols for nuclear energy density functionals that include deformed nuclei are robust to technical and numerical details of the underlying Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations, particularly the choice of initial quadrupole moment used to start self-consistent iterations. The authors find that while approximately 50% of parameter space points have at least one failed HFB calculation among the 79 nuclear configurations, this "noise" has insignificant impact on optimization and Bayesian calibration outcomes.

## Method

The authors performed HFB calculations with the Skyrme UNEDF1 energy density functional using the HFBTHO solver, computing binding energies, proton root-mean-square radii, odd-even staggering energies, and fission isomer excitation energies for 79 nuclear configurations across a 12-dimensional parameter space. They systematically varied the target quadrupole moment used to initialize the HFB iterations, developing three different configurations (C0, C1, C2) based on the median, 12.5th percentile, and 87.5th percentile of final quadrupole moments from a self-consistent iterative scheme. The impact of these changes was quantified on forward model calculations, optimization using the POUNDERS algorithm, and statistical emulation with Gaussian processes for Bayesian calibration.

## Key numbers

- The UNEDF1 dataset includes 115 data points across 4 data types: binding energies, proton RMS radii, odd-even staggering energies, and fission isomer excitation energies
- The parameter space volume studied spans 12 parameters with ranges including: saturation density from 0.155 to 0.165 fm^-3, nuclear matter incompressibility from -200 to -245 MeV, symmetry energy from -28 to -32 MeV, and symmetry energy slope from -20 to -60 MeV
- The self-consistent target quadrupole adjustment converged in 4 iterations with a maximum change in target quadrupole moment of approximately 0.05 barns
- For the initial configuration C0, nonconvergent computations decreased from 1.87% to 1.13% after self-consistent adjustment, while nonphysical results decreased from 4.84% to 0.93%
- For an arbitrary parameter space point, there is approximately a 50% chance that at least one of the 79 HFB calculations fails

## Context

This work extends the UNEDF optimization protocol developed by Kortelainen et al. (2010, 2012, 2014), which previously calibrated energy density functionals primarily using spherical nuclei and nuclear matter properties. The study addresses concerns raised by Bender (2006) and Nikolov (2011) that bulk surface properties, particularly isovector ones, can only be constrained by calculations in very deformed nuclei. The results enable more reliable Bayesian calibration of energy density functionals that include deformed nuclei, addressing the known problem of discontinuities in potential energy surfaces identified by Dubray (2012) in fission calculations.
