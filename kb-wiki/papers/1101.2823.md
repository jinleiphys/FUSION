# Recent Developments in the Theory of Quarkonium

> 7 pages, invited plenary talk at the Xth Nicola Cabibbo International Conference on Heavy Quarks and Leptons, Laboratori Nazionali di Frascati (Italy), 11-15 October, 2010

# Recent Developments in the Theory of Quarkonium

## Key claim

The paper argues that effective field theories (EFTs) such as NRQCD and pNRQCD provide a unifying, model-independent framework for quarkonium physics, but the attribution of the lowest bottomonia and charmonia states to either perturbative or nonperturbative soft regimes remains ambiguous due to lack of direct information on the radius of quarkonium systems.

## Method

The author constructs a hierarchy of effective field theories by systematically integrating out high-energy scales: NRQCD is obtained from QCD by integrating out the heavy-quark mass scale m, and pNRQCD follows from NRQCD by integrating out the soft scale mv. Matching procedures enforce equivalence between QCD and each EFT at a given order in the expansion in v, with the potential emerging as a Wilson coefficient that undergoes renormalization and satisfies renormalization group equations.

## Key numbers

- For bottomonium, v^2 ~ 0.1; for charmonium, v^2 ~ 0.3; for toponium, v^2 ~ 0.01
- Determination of alpha_s at the Upsilon mass: alpha_s(M_Upsilon(1S)) = 0.184 (+0.015 / -0.014), giving alpha_s(M_Z) = 0.119 (+0.006 / -0.005)
- For the zero-flavor case, r_0 * Lambda_MSbar = 0.622 (+0.019 / -0.015) from the NNNLL static energy
- Energy levels calculated at order m * alpha_s^5
- For bottomonium 1S states at LHC, the hierarchy m * alpha_s >> pi T >> m * alpha_s^2 >> m_D applies

## Context

This plenary talk reviews progress made by the International Quarkonium Working Group (QWG, established 2002) and extends the effective field theory framework developed in Brambilla (2004) and Brambilla (2010). The work enables precision calculations of quarkonium spectra, decays, and production, and provides a rigorous definition of the quarkonium potential at finite temperature with applications to heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC. The paper also discusses recent lattice calculations of nonperturbative potentials using the Luscher multi-level algorithm from Koma (2007) and perturbative three-loop results for the static potential from Smirnov (2008) and Anzai (2009).
