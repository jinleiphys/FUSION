# Comparison of dynamical multifragmentation models

> Multifragmentation scenarios, as predicted by antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) or momentum-dependent stochastic mean-field (BGBD) calculations are compared. While in the BGBD case fragment emission is clearly linked to the spinodal decomposition mechanism, i.e. to mean-field instabilities, in AMD many-body correlations have a stronger impact on the fragmentation dynamics and clusters start to appear at earlier times. As a consequence, fragments are formed on shorter time scales in AMD, on about equal footing of light particle pre-equilibrium emission. Conversely, in BGBD pre-equilibrium and fragment emissions happen on different time scales and are related to different mechanisms.

## Key claim

The paper compares two dynamical multifragmentation models, antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) and momentum-dependent stochastic mean-field (BGBD), and finds that while both reproduce some aspects of multifragmentation data, the fragment formation mechanisms differ fundamentally: in BGBD fragmentation is driven by spinodal decomposition (mean-field instabilities) in the low-density phase, whereas in AMD many-body correlations cause clusters to appear already at earlier times, on about equal footing with pre-equilibrium emission.

## Method

Central collisions of ¹¹²Sn + ¹¹²Sn at 50 MeV/nucleon were simulated using two microscopic approaches: the stochastic mean-field model BGBD (Boltzmann-Langevin type with momentum-dependent GBD effective interaction, soft equation of state with compressibility 215 MeV, fluctuations introduced by agitating the one-body density) and the antisymmetrized molecular dynamics model AMD (Slater determinant of Gaussian wave packets with Gogny effective interaction, compressibility 228 MeV, fluctuations introduced by stochastically localizing wave packets during two-nucleon collisions). The authors analyzed one-body observables (radial density profiles, radial collective momentum profiles, number of nucleons in the gas phase) and density variances as functions of time, using 200 BGBD trajectories at impact parameter b = 0.5 fm and 20 AMD events with 0 < b < 1 fm.

## Key numbers

- System: ¹¹²Sn + ¹¹²Sn at 50 MeV/nucleon (central collisions)
- BGBD uses a soft equation of state with compressibility modulus K_NM(ρ₀) = 215 MeV
- AMD uses Gogny effective interaction with incompressibility K_NM(ρ₀) = 228 MeV
- In BGBD, a clear change of slope in the gas-phase nucleon number occurs at around t ≈ 100 fm/c, where fragments start to form
- In AMD, the collective momentum increases less than in BGBD when the system goes from compression toward normal density (t ≈ 70 fm/c), indicating more energy goes into thermal kinetic fluctuations
- Density fluctuations have close values in the two approaches at the high density phase (t ≈ 40 fm/c)
- In BGBD, fluctuations are damped during expansion until low-density values are reached at t ≈ 70 fm/c, then grow again
- In AMD, density fluctuations start to grow already at t ≈ 40 fm/c, without the initial damping seen in BGBD

## Context

This work extends comparisons between molecular dynamics and mean-field approaches for multifragmentation, following earlier studies by Frankland (2001), Moretto (1996), D'Agostino (1999), Chomaz (2004), and the EPJA review (2004). The improved AMD version (Ono, 1999) and the stochastic BGBD model (Joseph model, 1999) are considered closer approaches than earlier molecular dynamics (MD, 1992) and standard mean-field (BNV, 1994) formulations, which gave rather different results. The study enables identification of experimental observables sensitive to whether fragmentation proceeds via spinodal decomposition or via early clustering from many-body correlations, particularly at Fermi energies where one- and two-body effects are equally important.
