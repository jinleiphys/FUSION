# The Neutron Star Crust: Nuclear Physics Input

> A fully self-consistent model of the neutron star inner crust based upon models of the nucleonic equation of state at zero temperature is constructed. The results nearly match those of previous calculations of the inner crust given the same input equation of state. The extent to which the uncertainties in the symmetry energy, the compressibility, and the equation of state of low-density neutron matter affect the composition of the crust are examined. The composition and pressure of the crust is sensitive to the description of low-density neutron matter and the nuclear symmetry energy, and the latter dependence is non-monotonic, giving larger nuclei for moderate symmetry energies and smaller nuclei for more extreme symmetry energies. Future nuclear experiments may help constrain the crust and future astrophysical observations may constrain the nuclear physics input.

## Key claim

The composition and pressure of the neutron star inner crust are sensitive to the description of low-density neutron matter and the nuclear symmetry energy, but the dependence on the symmetry energy is non-monotonic: models with moderate symmetry energies produce larger nuclei than models with either lower or higher symmetry energies, a nuance not captured in the abstract's summary.

## Method

A liquid-drop model of the inner crust was constructed, with bulk energy derived from the equation of state (EOS) of homogeneous nucleonic matter, plus surface and Coulomb contributions. Six free parameters (surface tension, surface symmetry energy, Coulomb correction factor, asymmetry parameter, and two central density parameters) were fit to experimental nuclear masses from Audi (2003) for each input EOS. The crust composition and pressure were then determined by minimizing the total energy density at fixed baryon density over proton number, atomic number, and dripped neutron density, using several EOS models including APR, SLy4, SkM*, NL4Q, and a set of schematic EOSs with varied symmetry energy and compressibility.

## Key numbers

- The neutron drip density defining the inner crust lower boundary is about 4 × 10^11 g/cm^3.
- The baseline schematic model "Sch" has a symmetry energy of 31 MeV at saturation density and a compressibility of 230 MeV.
- The transition density to homogeneous nuclear matter ranges from 0.0333 fm^-3 (NL4QN2) to 0.0676 fm^-3 (Schγ1) across models.
- For the APR EOS, the transition density is 0.0522 fm^-3; for SLy4 it is 0.0669 fm^-3 (compared to 0.076 fm^-3 from Douchin (2001)).
- The RMS deviation of the mass fits ranges from 2.6 MeV (SLy4) to 2.75 MeV (Schγ2).
- The surface tension σ ranges from 1.11 MeV/fm^2 (Schγ2) to 1.20 MeV/fm^2 (SchS28).
- The surface symmetry energy σ_δ ranges from 0.978 (Schγ2) to 2.67 (NL4Q).
- Nuclear size in the inner crust is modified by 50% or more when the low-density neutron matter EOS is changed from NL4Q to NL4QN2.
- At a fixed density of n_B = 0.01 fm^-3, varying the symmetry energy at saturation between 28 and 34 MeV changes the composition non-monotonically.
- The pressure in the crust varies by up to a factor of two due to uncertainty in the symmetry energy.

## Context

This work extends the liquid-drop crust models of Baym (1971) and Lattimer (1985) by systematically varying the nuclear EOS input while ensuring consistency between the description of nuclei and dripped neutrons. It compares results to the microscopic calculations of Douchin (2001) using SLy4 and to Lattimer (2006) using SkM*, finding qualitative agreement but quantitative differences attributable to the mass formula. The results enable future astrophysical observations (e.g., of neutron star crust cooling, giant flares, or moment of inertia) to constrain the nuclear symmetry energy, and motivate experiments such as the PREX neutron skin measurement at Jefferson Lab and heavy-ion collision studies.
