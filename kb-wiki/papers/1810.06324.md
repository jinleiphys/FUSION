# Machine Learning based jet momentum reconstruction in heavy-ion collisions

> The precise reconstruction of jet transverse momenta in heavy-ion collisions is a challenging task. A major obstacle is the large number of (mainly) low-$p_{\rm T}$ particles overlaying the jets. Strong region-to-region fluctuations of this background complicate the jet measurement and lead to significant uncertainties. In this paper, a novel approach to correct jet momenta (or energies) for the underlying background in heavy-ion collisions is introduced. The proposed method makes use of common Machine Learning techniques to estimate the jet transverse momentum based on several parameters, including properties of the jet constituents. Using a toy model and HIJING simulations, the performance of the new method is shown to be superior to the established standard area-based background estimator. The application of the new method to data promises the measurement of jets down to extremely low transverse momenta, unprecedented thus far in data on heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a Machine Learning based approach to correct jet transverse momenta for the heavy-ion background on a jet-by-jet basis, claiming superior performance to the established area-based method in toy model and HIJING simulations, but the abstract's promise of measuring jets down to "extremely low transverse momenta" is hedged by the fact that the method was only tested on simulated data and the authors note that a small residual bias (of order 1 GeV/c) could cause up to approximately 20% deviations in yields at low transverse momentum around 20 GeV/c for pure quark or gluon samples.

## Method

Charged jets were reconstructed using the anti-kT algorithm with resolution parameter R=0.4 in FastJet, using charged particles above pT=0.15 GeV/c. The authors trained three Machine Learning regression algorithms (shallow neural network with three layers of [100,100,50] nodes, random forest with 30 trees, and linear regression) on a toy model where PYTHIA6 Perugia 2011 jets at sqrt(s)=2.76 TeV were embedded in a thermal background with Gaussian multiplicity distribution (mean 1800, width 200). The input features included the uncorrected jet pT, area-based corrected pT, jet shape observables (mass, radial moment, momentum dispersion, LeSub), number of constituents, mean and median constituent pT, and the pT of the ten hardest constituents; the true jet momentum was defined as the raw jet momentum multiplied by the fraction of momentum from PYTHIA particles.

## Key numbers

- Average charged particle transverse momentum density in 0-10% most central Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=2.76 TeV: <rho> ≈ 138.2 GeV/c per unit area, with standard deviation sigma(rho)=18.5 GeV/c
- For jets with 40 ≤ pT_true < 60 GeV/c, the neural network residual width (sigma) was 3.5 GeV/c, compared to 7.8 GeV/c for the area-based method (a factor of approximately two improvement)
- The neural network showed mean residual bias of 0.2 GeV/c for inclusive jets, 1.0 GeV/c for quark jets, and -0.5 GeV/c for gluon jets in the 40-60 GeV/c range
- The random forest and linear regression gave residual widths of 4.0 and 4.2 GeV/c respectively for the same pT range
- The toy model used 5 million events yielding roughly 6.5 million jet samples, with 10% used for training
- HIJING test dataset used 2 million events at sqrt(s_NN)=2.76 TeV with impact parameter b<3.6 fm and pT_hard>5.8, giving mean multiplicity N≈2200 with width 200

## Context

This work extends the standard area-based background subtraction method for heavy-ion jet measurements (Cacciari, 2007) by applying Machine Learning regression to reduce residual fluctuations on a jet-by-jet basis. The approach is motivated by the large background fluctuations in ALICE measurements at the LHC (Abelev, 2012) and builds on the use of ML techniques in high-energy physics (Albertsson, 2018). The method enables potential measurements of jets at lower transverse momenta than currently possible, though the authors note that systematic uncertainties from fragmentation differences (quark vs gluon jets) and the need for realistic training data (e.g., from mixed events or proton-proton data) must be addressed before application to real heavy-ion data.
