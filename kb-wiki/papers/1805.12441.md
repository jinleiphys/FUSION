# Dispersive Corrections to the Born Approximation in Elastic Electron-Nucleus Scattering in the Intermediate Energy Regime

> Measurements of elastic electron scattering data within the past decade have highlighted two-photon exchange contributions as a necessary ingredient in theoretical calculations to precisely evaluate hydrogen elastic scattering cross sections. This correction can modify the cross section at the few percent level. In contrast, dispersive effects can cause significantly larger changes from the Born approximation. The purpose of this experiment is to extract the carbon-12 elastic cross section around the first diffraction minimum, where the Born term contributions to the cross section are small to maximize the sensitivity to dispersive effects. The analysis uses the LEDEX data from the high resolution Jefferson Lab Hall A spectrometers to extract the cross sections near the first diffraction minimum of 12C at beam energies of 362 MeV and 685 MeV. The results are in very good agreement with previous world data, although with less precision. The average deviation from a static nuclear charge distribution expected from linear and quadratic fits indicate a 30.6% contribution of dispersive effects to the cross section at 1 GeV. The magnitude of the dispersive effects near the first diffraction minimum of 12C has been confirmed to be large with a strong energy dependence and could account for a large fraction of the magnitude for the observed quenching of the longitudinal nuclear response. These effects could also be important for nuclei radii extracted from parity-violating asymmetries measured near a diffraction minimum.

## Key claim

The paper confirms that dispersive (two-photon exchange) corrections to the Born approximation in elastic electron-¹²C scattering are large and strongly energy-dependent near the first diffraction minimum, with an average deviation of 30.6% from a static nuclear charge distribution at 1 GeV, but the authors note that this result comes from linear and quadratic fits to world data and that the theoretical prediction of Friar and Rosen (1974), which predicted a constant ~2% effect, fails to reproduce the observed magnitude and energy dependence.

## Method

The experiment used the LEDEX data from Jefferson Lab Hall A’s two high-resolution spectrometers to measure elastic electron-¹²C cross sections at beam energies of 362 MeV and 685 MeV, focusing on kinematic settings near the first diffraction minimum (effective momentum transfer q_eff ≈ 1.84 fm⁻¹). The measured cross sections were compared to static cross sections calculated from a Fourier-Bessel parameterization of the charge density, and the fractional deviation was taken as the dispersive correction. The authors also performed linear and quadratic polynomial fits to world data on the energy dependence of dispersive effects in the first diffraction minimum of ¹²C.

## Key numbers

- Measured elastic cross section at 362 MeV, θ = 61°: (3.26 ± 0.28) × 10⁻⁸ fm²/sr (statistical uncertainty 7.70%, systematic 3.50%)
- Measured elastic cross section at 685 MeV, θ = 30.5°: (2.35 ± 0.11) × 10⁻⁷ fm²/sr (statistical uncertainty 4.24%, systematic 2.40%)
- Ratio of measured to static cross section at 362 MeV: 4.49% deviation
- Ratio of measured to static cross section at 685 MeV: 21.76% deviation
- Average deviation from static charge distribution at 1 GeV from linear and quadratic fits: 30.6%
- Linear fit parameters: p₀ = −6.64 ± 1.13, p₁ = (3.55 ± 0.26) × 10⁻² MeV⁻¹, χ²/ndf = 2.092/6
- Quadratic fit parameters: p₀ = −4.40 ± 4.04, p₁ = (2.36 ± 2.078) × 10⁻² MeV⁻¹, p₂ = (1.30 ± 2.25) × 10⁻⁵ MeV⁻², χ²/ndf = 1.758/5
- Effective momentum transfers in the diffraction minimum: q_eff = 1.82 fm⁻¹ (362 MeV) and 1.81 fm⁻¹ (685 MeV)
- Target thickness: 0.083 ± 0.001 g/cm² of 99.95% pure ¹²C

## Context

This work extends the study of dispersive corrections to elastic electron-nucleus scattering that began with the seminal paper by Sick (1970) and continued through experiments by Offermann et al. (1991) and Gueye et al. (1998, 1999). The results confirm the large, energy-dependent dispersive effects near the first diffraction minimum of ¹²C that were previously observed, and contradict the constant ~2% prediction from the harmonic-oscillator model of Friar and Rosen (1974). The findings suggest that dispersive corrections could account for a large fraction of the observed quenching of the longitudinal nuclear response and may be important for extracting nuclear radii from parity-violating asymmetry measurements near diffraction minima.
