# Integrating by parts at finite density

> Both nonzero temperature and chemical potentials break the Lorentz symmetry present in vacuum quantum field theory by singling out the rest frame of the heat bath. This leads to complications in the application of thermal perturbation theory, including the appearance of novel infrared divergences in loop integrals and an apparent absence of four-dimensional integration-by-parts (IBP) identities, vital for high-order computations. Here, we propose a new strategy that enables the use of IBP techniques in the evaluation of Feynman integrals, in particular vacuum or bubble diagrams, in the limit of vanishing temperature $T$ but nonzero chemical potentials $μ$. The central elements of the new setup include a contour representation for the temporal momentum integral, the use of a small but nonzero $T$ as an IR regulator, and the systematic application of both temporal and spatial differential operators in the generation of linear relations among the loop integrals of interest. The relations we derive contain novel inhomogeneous terms featuring differentiated Fermi-Dirac distribution functions, which severely complicate calculations at nonzero temperature, but are shown to reduce to solvable lower-dimensional objects as $T$ tends to zero. Pedagogical example computations are kept at the one- and two-loop levels, but the application of the new method to higher-order calculations is discussed in some detail.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a new strategy that enables the use of integration-by-parts (IBP) techniques for Feynman integrals at nonzero chemical potentials in the limit of vanishing temperature, despite the breaking of Lorentz symmetry; the method works because the inhomogeneous terms containing differentiated Fermi-Dirac distribution functions, which complicate calculations at nonzero temperature, reduce to solvable lower-dimensional objects as temperature tends to zero.

## Method

The authors work in the imaginary-time formalism of thermal field theory, using a contour representation for the temporal momentum integral and keeping a small but nonzero temperature as an infrared regulator. They systematically apply both temporal and spatial differential operators to generate linear relations among loop integrals, where temporal derivatives produce boundary terms containing differentiated Fermi-Dirac distribution functions. These boundary terms are then evaluated using contour deformations that exploit the delta-sequence behavior of the differentiated distribution functions in the zero-temperature limit, with pedagogical examples worked out at one-loop and two-loop levels.

## Key numbers

- The one-loop fermion bubble integral is defined as $\mathcal{I}_{\alpha}^{s} (\mu,T) \equiv \oint_{p_0}^f p_0^s\,\nFt(p_0)\, I_{\alpha}(p_0)$, where the fermionic contour involves Matsubara frequencies $\omega_n^\rmii{F} = (2n+1)\pi T+i\mu$
- The two-loop fermionic sunset integral is defined as $\mathcal{S}_{\alpha_1 \alpha_2 \alpha_3}^{s_1 s_2}(\mu, T) = \oint_{p_0}^f \oint_{q_0}^b p_0^{s_1} q_0^{s_2} \nFt(p_0) \nFt (q_0)\, S_{\alpha_1 \alpha_2 \alpha_3}(p_0,q_0,p_0-q_0)$
- The derivative of the fermionic distribution function yields a nascent delta distribution: $-\beta n_{\rmii{F}}'[-\beta y] = \frac{\beta}{2}\frac{1}{[\cosh(\beta y) + 1]} \stackrel{T < |y|}{\longrightarrow} \delta_{\T}(y)$
- For a massless fermionic propagator raised to power $\alpha$, the naive residue approach yields the physically correct result only for $1/2 < \alpha \leq 1$; for $\alpha>1$ an explicit divergence occurs at $p = \mu$, $p_0 \sim 0$

## Context

This work extends the contour-integral formulation introduced by Gorda et al. (2022) for handling subtle low-temperature limits in loop calculations with chemical potentials, and generalizes the vacuum IBP technique of Chetyrkin and Tkachov (1981) to finite-density systems where Lorentz symmetry is broken. The method enables high-order perturbative calculations for the equation of state of cold and ultradense quark matter relevant to neutron-star physics, complementing existing four-loop results at high temperature (Kajantie et al., 2002; Vuorinen, 2003; Laine and Schroder, 2006) and high chemical potential (Kurkela et al., 2009, 2016; Gorda et al., 2021).
