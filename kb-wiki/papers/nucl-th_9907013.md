# ISGDR- bulk or surface mode ?

> A polarization and response function for finite systems and temperatures is derived from linearizing the Vlasov equation. Besides the Lindhard response function in local density approximation we obtain an additional contribution due to the surface. This formula is applied to isoscalar giant resonances which we consider as next harmonics to the basic dipole mode. The spurious center of mass motion is subtracted within the polarization. The results based on simple Fermi liquid considerations are in reasonable agreement with experimental data.

# ISGDR – bulk or surface mode?

## Key claim
The isoscalar giant dipole resonance (ISGDR) can be understood within a Fermi liquid model, but surface contributions to the response function are not negligible for reproducing the experimental strength function; the authors derive a polarization formula that includes a surface term beyond the standard Lindhard response in local density approximation.

## Method
The authors linearize the semiclassical Vlasov equation to obtain a polarization function for finite systems, then perform a gradient expansion retaining first-order spatial gradients to capture surface effects. They apply the resulting response function to spherical nuclei using a Skyrme force parameterization (Vautherin and Brink 1972) and the Steinwedel-Jensen model, treating the ISGDR as the next harmonic above the spurious dipole mode and subtracting the center-of-mass contribution from the polarization.

## Key numbers
- Skyrme parameters used: t₀ = −983.4 MeV fm³, t₃ = 13106 MeV fm⁶, x₀ = 0.48, at nuclear saturation density n₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³, with incompressibility K = 318 MeV.
- The spurious dipole mode wave vector is q_sp = π/(2R₀); the ISGDR wave vector is q_isgdr = π/R₀.
- The surface contribution to the polarization is given by Π^surf(q,ω) = −(3/(2 q R₀)) Im Π⁰(q,ω).

## Context
This work extends earlier Fermi-liquid treatments of giant resonances by including surface corrections via a gradient expansion, building on the Lindhard response formalism and the extended Thomas-Fermi approximation (Ring and Schuck 1980). The ISGDR had been experimentally observed (Davis et al. 1997) and previously interpreted as a squeezing mode analogous to sound waves (Harakeh and Dieperink 1981; Gai and Sagawa 1981). The authors’ surface-corrected response function shifts the theoretical strength function toward experimental data from Garg (private communication), and the results support the interpretation of the mode as an isoscalar dipole type. The approach enables future calculations of finite-system response at finite temperature.
