# How degeneracies can obscure interesting physics

> We show how degeneracies, accidental or otherwise, can obscure some interesting physics. We further show how one can get around this problem.

# How degeneracies can obscure interesting physics

## Key claim
Degeneracies (accidental or otherwise) between nuclear states can hide the existence of interaction-independent eigenstates; the authors show that by adding a small isospin-dependent term to remove the degeneracy, the unique state that emerges with any interaction can be recovered.

## Method
The authors perform shell-model matrix diagonalizations for four identical particles in the $g_{9/2}$ shell, first using a quadrupole-quadrupole ($Q\cdot Q$) interaction for two proton holes and two neutron holes in $^{96}$Cd. They then add a $t(1)\cdot t(2)$ interaction (equivalently, shifting the $T=0$ two-body matrix elements by -1.000 MeV for odd-spin states) to lift the degeneracy between $T=0$ and $T=2$ states and reveal the unique interaction-independent eigenstate.

## Key numbers
- Two degenerate $I=4^+$ states in $^{96}$Cd appear at an excitation energy of 3.5284 MeV when using the $Q\cdot Q$ interaction.
- The unique $T=2$, $v=4$ state appears at 6.5285 MeV after the degeneracy is removed.
- The two-body matrix elements for the $Q\cdot Q$ interaction (in MeV, from $J=0$ to $J=9$) are: -1.0000, -0.8788, -0.6516, -0.3465, -0.0152, 0.2879, 0.4849, 0.4849, 0.1818, and -0.5454.

## Context
This work extends the earlier finding by Escuderos and Zamick (2006) that in the $g_{9/2}$ shell with four identical particles, a unique $v=4$ state emerges for $I=4$ or $6$ regardless of the interaction used. The authors previously noted in Zamick (2007) that with a pairing interaction the two $v=4$ states are degenerate, obscuring this uniqueness. The present paper shows that the same problem occurs with a $Q\cdot Q$ interaction in the proton-neutron system ($^{96}$Cd), and demonstrates how to recover the unique state by removing the degeneracy. This enables future calculations to correctly identify interaction-independent eigenstates in mixed systems.
