# Progress in the Development of Global Medium-Energy Nucleon-Nucleus Optical Model Potentials

> Two existing global medium-energy nucleon-nucleus phenomenological optical model potentials are described and compared with experiment and with each other. The first of these employs a Dirac approach (second-order reduction) that is global in projectile energy and projectile isospin and applies to the target nucleus 208-Pb. Here the standard S-V (isoscalar-scalar, isoscalar-vector) model has been extended to include the corresponding isovector components by introduction of a relativistic Lane model. The second of these employs a relativistic equivalent to the Schroedinger equation and is global in projectile energy, projectile isospin, and target (Z,A). Here, particular attention is given to predictions for the integrated scattering observables - neutron total cross sections and proton total reaction cross sections - and their sensitivity to the absorptive parts of the potential. Finally, current work is described and the influence of the nuclear bound state problem (treated in relativistic mean field theory) on the Dirac scattering problem is mentioned.

# Progress in the Development of Global Medium-Energy Nucleon-Nucleus Optical Model Potentials

## Key claim

Two global phenomenological optical model potentials for medium-energy nucleon-nucleus scattering are described and compared, but the paper explicitly states that "a satisfactory global medium-energy nucleon-nucleus optical potential does not yet exist" and that current work continues toward this goal. The Dirac potential is global only for the target nucleus 208-Pb, while the relativistic Schrodinger potential is global in projectile energy, projectile isospin, and target (Z,A) but was constructed by iterative computation without a generalized nonlinear least-squares adjustment algorithm.

## Method

The first potential uses the Dirac equation in the mean field approximation with isoscalar-scalar and isoscalar-vector potentials extended to include isovector components via a relativistic Lane model, applied to nucleon scattering from 208-Pb over 95-300 MeV. The second potential employs a relativistic equivalent to the Schrodinger equation with relativistic kinematics, starting from the Schwandt et al. (1982) proton potential and extending it to target masses 12-208 and energies 50-400 MeV by iteratively adjusting the absorptive potential parameters to reproduce measured total reaction and total cross sections for nine nuclei. Both potentials use symmetrized Woods-Saxon form factors.

## Key numbers

- Dirac potential energy range: 95 to 300 MeV for nucleon + 208-Pb scattering
- Schrodinger potential target mass range: 12 to 208; projectile energy range: 50 to 400 MeV
- For the Dirac logarithmic model on 208-Pb: scalar real strength = -570 -/+ 307*epsilon + 23.1*ln(T) MeV; vector real strength = 532 +/- 235*epsilon - 37.4*ln(T) MeV
- For the Dirac exponential model on 208-Pb: scalar real strength = (-491 -/+ 362*epsilon)*exp(-T/5440) MeV; vector real strength = (399 +/- 287*epsilon)*exp(-T/1686) MeV
- Dirac logarithmic model chi-square/point for combined neutron and proton data: 12.0; exponential model: 12.8
- Dirac logarithmic model chi-square/point for neutron data only: 0.90; exponential model: 0.95
- Schrodinger potential real central strength: V_R = 105.5[1 - 0.1625*ln(T)] -/+ 16.5[(N-Z)/A] - (1/2 + tau_3)(0.4*Z/A^(1/3)) MeV
- Schrodinger potential imaginary diffuseness for T > 140 MeV: a_I = 0.3537 + 0.08451*A^(1/3) - 0.001835*A^(2/3) fm
- Current work targets energy range of (perhaps) 20 MeV to (perhaps) 2000 MeV and target mass range 16 to 209, with a database of over 20000 points

## Context

This work extends the Dirac phenomenology of the S-V (isoscalar-scalar, isoscalar-vector) model by adding isovector components through a relativistic generalization of the Lane model (1962), building on earlier work by Arima (1981) on the "wine-bottle" shape of the effective central potential. The Schrodinger potential extends the proton optical-model potential of Schwandt et al. (1982) to a wider mass and energy range and transforms it to a neutron potential. The paper enables future development of a truly global medium-energy optical potential using both relativistic Schrodinger and Dirac phenomenology with an identical database in a nonlinear least-squares adjustment, and connects to microscopic Dirac approaches using relativistic Hartree theory from recent bound-state work.
