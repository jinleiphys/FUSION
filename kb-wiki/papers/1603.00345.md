# Casimir-Polder interaction of neutrons with metal or dielectric surfaces

> We predict a repulsive Casimir-Polder-type dispersion interaction between a single neutron and a metal or dielectric surface. We consider a scenario where a single neutron is subject to an external magnetic field. Due to its intrinsic magnetic moment, the neutron then forms a magnetisable two-level system which can exchange virtual photons with a nearby surface. The resulting dispersion interaction between a purely magnetic object (neutron) and a purely electric one (surface) is found to be repulsive, in contrast to the typical attractive interaction between electric objects. Its magnitude is considerably smaller than the standard atom--surface Casimir-Polder force due to the magnetic nature of the interaction and the smallness of the electron-to-neutron mass ratio. Nevertheless, we show that it can be comparable to the gravitational potential of the same surface and should be taken into consideration in future neutron interference experiments.

# Casimir-Polder interaction of neutrons with metal or dielectric surfaces

## Key claim

The paper predicts a repulsive Casimir-Polder-type dispersion interaction between a single neutron and a metal or dielectric surface, but only when the neutron is subject to an external magnetic field that lifts the degeneracy of its spin states; the interaction is considerably smaller than the standard atom-surface Casimir-Polder force but can be comparable to the gravitational potential of the same surface and should be considered in future neutron interference experiments.

## Method

The authors model the neutron as a magnetisable two-level system with spin eigenstates split by an external static magnetic field, interacting with the quantised plate-assisted electromagnetic field via its magnetic dipole moment. Using second-order perturbation theory within the framework of macroscopic quantum electrodynamics, they compute the energy shift of the neutron's ground state by evaluating the scattering part of the dyadic Green's tensor for a semi-infinite half-space, considering four different models for the surface's electric response: perfect conductor, plasma model, Drude model, and Drude-Lorentz model.

## Key numbers

- The neutron Casimir-Polder coefficient for a perfect conductor is \(C_3^\mathrm{N} \approx 1.7 \times 10^{-10} C_3^\mathrm{A}\), where \(C_3^\mathrm{A}\) is the atomic van der Waals coefficient, due to the fine-structure constant (\(\alpha \approx 1/137\)) and the electron-to-neutron mass ratio.
- For applied magnetic fields \(B_\mathrm{ext} \lesssim 5\,\mathrm{T}\), the critical distance for the nonretarded limit is \(z_\mathrm{nret} = c/(\gamma_\mathrm{N} B_\mathrm{ext}) \gtrsim 0.32\,\mathrm{m}\), meaning typical experimental distances (nm to \(\mu\)m) lie in the nonretarded regime.
- For a plasma-model mirror, the first-order Casimir-Polder energy shift for the transition \((n,m) = (1,5)\) in gravitational resonance spectroscopy is \(\Delta E_{\mathrm{CP}}(1,5) = 1.7 \times 10^{-14}\,\mathrm{peV}\), below the current detection threshold of \(\Delta E \leq 2 \times 10^{-3}\,\mathrm{peV}\) (Cronenberg 2018) and below a planned sensitivity of \(5 \times 10^{-9}\,\mathrm{peV}\).
- The neutron's static electric polarisability is \(\alpha = (11.8 \pm 1.1) \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{fm}^3\) and magnetic polarisability is \(\beta = (3.7 \pm 1.2) \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{fm}^3\) (Zyla 2020), giving an attractive potential that is several orders of magnitude smaller than the repulsive Casimir-Polder potential.
- For the Drude-Lorentz model of silicon, the plasma frequency is \(\omega_\mathrm{P} = 2.3 \times 10^{16}\,\mathrm{rad/s}\) and the transverse resonance frequency is \(\omega_\mathrm{T} = 7.1 \times 10^{16}\,\mathrm{rad/s}\) (Pirozenko 2008).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of Casimir-Polder interactions to purely magnetic objects, building on earlier studies of spin particles near conducting walls by Babiker and Barton (1972) and on repulsive dispersion forces between magnetisable and polarisable objects (Henkel 2005, Haakh 2009, Bimonte 2009). The authors note that while repulsive Casimir forces have been predicted for mixed electric-magnetic object combinations, they have proven elusive experimentally because the attractive electric-electric force dominates in natural materials; the neutron, being electrically neutral and purely magnetic, avoids this constraint. The results enable future high-precision gravitational resonance spectroscopy experiments with neutrons (Cronenberg 2018, Sedmik 2020) to distinguish between different models of surface electromagnetic response, potentially testing the Drude-plasma debate in Casimir physics, and require that Casimir-Polder contributions be taken into account in short-range gravity experiments.
