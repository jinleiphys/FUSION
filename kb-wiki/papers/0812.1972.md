# Extending the canonical thermodynamic model: inclusion of hypernuclei

> The canonical thermodynamic model has been used frequently to describe the disassembly of hot nuclear matter consisting of neutrons and protons. Such matter is formed in intermediate energy heavy ion collisions. Here we extend the method to include, in addition to neutrons and protons, lambda particles. This allows us to include productions of hypernuclei in intermediate energy heavy ion collisions. We can easily predict average mass number of hypernuclei produced and values of relative cross-sections of different hypernuclei. Computation of absolute cross-section was not attempted at this stage and will require much more detailed work.

# Extending the canonical thermodynamic model: inclusion of hypernuclei

## Key claim

The canonical thermodynamic model (CTM) can be extended from two-component (neutron, proton) systems to three-component systems including Λ particles, enabling predictions of average hypernucleus mass numbers and relative cross-sections; however, absolute cross-section computation was not attempted and would require much more detailed work.

## Method

The canonical partition function for a system with fixed baryon number A, charge Z, and strangeness number H is constructed from the one-particle partition functions ω_{a,z,h} of composites with mass a, charge z, and h Λ particles. A recurrence relation Q_{A,Z,H} = (1/A) Σ a ω_{a,z,h} Q_{A-a,Z-z,H-h} is used to compute the many-particle partition function, from which average multiplicities ⟨n_{a,z,h}⟩ are obtained. For low-mass nuclei, experimental ground-state energies and spin degeneracies are used; for heavier nuclei (h=0 or 1), a liquid-drop formula with temperature-dependent surface tension is employed, and excited-state contributions are included via a multiplicative factor exp(r(T) T a / ε₀).

## Key numbers

- For a system with A=100, Z=40 at T=4 MeV, the average mass number of the hypernucleus formed is about 95, with multiplicity of non-strange composites of 1.24 and average non-strange composite mass about 5 and average charge about 1.7.
- For PLF temperatures in the expected 6 MeV to 10 MeV range, the average mass number of hypernuclei should be in the 20 to 7 range.
- For A=100, Z=40 at T=8 MeV with freeze-out density one-third normal density, the relative population ⟨n_{a,h}⟩ drops off rapidly with a such that population beyond a=40 can be ignored.
- For H=2 in A=100 at T=8 MeV, the number of hypernuclei is doubled compared to H=1, with only very small changes in the number of non-strange composites.
- For the participant zone at freeze-out density 1/6 normal density, A=100, Z=40, and T=18 MeV, the average value of a for h=1 is 2.55.

## Context

This work extends the canonical thermodynamic model (CTM), previously applied to two-component nuclear multifragmentation (Dasgupta, Bhattacharyya), to three-component systems including Λ particles. It closely follows the same physics as Botvina's recent work using the statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) but provides a computationally easier prescription. The approach contrasts with earlier coalescence models for hypernucleus production (Wakai, 1980s) and more recent coalescence calculations (Gaitanos), and enables predictions of hypernucleus yields that can be tested experimentally in intermediate energy heavy ion collisions at 1-2 GeV/A beam energies.
