# Three-Nucleon Forces: Implementation and Applications to Atomic Nuclei and Dense Matter

> Recent advances in nuclear structure theory have significantly enlarged the accessible part of the nuclear landscape via ab initio many-body calculations. These developments open new ways for microscopic studies of light, medium-mass and heavy nuclei as well as nuclear matter and represent an important step toward a systematic and comprehensive understanding of atomic nuclei across the nuclear chart. While remarkable agreement has been found between different many-body methods for a given nuclear Hamiltonian, the comparison with experiment and the understanding of theoretical uncertainties are still important open questions. The observed discrepancies to experiment indicate deficiencies in presently used nuclear interactions and operators. Chiral effective field theory (EFT) allows to systematically derive contributions to nucleon-nucleon (NN), three-nucleon (3N) and higher-body interactions including estimates of theoretical uncertainties. While the treatment of NN interactions in many-body calculations is well established, the calculation of 3N interactions and their incorporation in ab initio frameworks is still a frontier. This work reviews in detail recent and current developments on the derivation and implementation of improved 3N interactions and provides a comprehensive introduction to fundamental methods for their practical calculation and representation. We further give an overview of novel and established methods that facilitate the inclusion and treatment of 3N interactions in ab initio nuclear structure frameworks and present a selection of the latest calculations of atomic nuclei as well as nuclear matter based on state-of-the-art nuclear NN and 3N interactions derived within chiral EFT. Finally, we discuss ongoing efforts, open questions and future directions.

## Key claim

While chiral effective field theory (EFT) allows systematic derivation of nucleon-nucleon (NN), three-nucleon (3N), and higher-body interactions with uncertainty estimates, the calculation of 3N interactions and their incorporation in ab initio frameworks remains a frontier, and no current nuclear interactions can simultaneously predict few- and many-body observables across the full mass range within theoretical uncertainties.

## Method

This review systematically catalogs the derivation and implementation of 3N interactions within chiral EFT, covering the chiral expansion of nuclear forces at different orders (LO through N4LO), regularization schemes, and representation methods for 3N interactions in momentum and coordinate space. It surveys multiple ab initio many-body frameworks including no-core shell model (NCSM), coupled cluster (CC), in-medium similarity renormalization group (IM-SRG), self-consistent Green's function (SCGF), quantum Monte Carlo (QMC), and many-body perturbation theory (MBPT), and discusses renormalization group methods such as similarity renormalization group (SRG) for decoupling low- and high-momentum components.

## Key numbers

* The pion mass m_pi is approximately 135 MeV, setting the typical momentum scale for chiral EFT.
* The breakdown scale Lambda_b of chiral EFT is around 500 MeV, giving an effective expansion parameter Q/Lambda_b of approximately 1/3.
* The radius of an average atomic nucleus is of order a few femtometers (1 fm = 10^-15 m).
* The unit system uses hbar = c = 1 = hbar c = 197.326 MeV fm.
* Ab initio calculations have been extended to mass numbers up to approximately 100 for atomic nuclei, limited by storage of three-body matrix elements.
* The next lightest meson after the pion, the rho meson, has mass m_rho approximately 770 MeV.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on chiral EFT nuclear interactions originally formulated by Weinberg (1990, 1991) and developed by many groups including Epelbaum (2009), Machleidt (2011), and Hammer (2019). It documents the dramatic expansion of ab initio calculations from 2009 to 2018 as illustrated by Hergert (2016), enabled by SRG methods from Bogner (2007, 2010) and improved many-body frameworks. The work enables systematic cross-benchmarks between different many-body methods and provides a foundation for developing improved nuclear interactions that can simultaneously describe light, medium-mass, and heavy nuclei as well as nuclear matter.
