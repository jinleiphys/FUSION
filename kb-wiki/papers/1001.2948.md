# Thermalization through Hagedorn states - the importance of multiparticle collisions

> Quick chemical equilibration times of hadrons within a hadron gas are explained dynamically using Hagedorn states, which drive particles into equilibrium close to the critical temperature. Within this scheme master equations are employed for the chemical equilibration of various hadronic particles like (strange) baryon and antibaryons. A comparison of the Hagedorn model to recent lattice results is made and it is found that for both Tc =176 MeV and Tc=196 MeV, the hadrons can reach chemical equilibrium almost immediately, well before the chemical freeze-out temperatures found in thermal fits for a hadron gas without Hagedorn states.

## Key claim

Hagedorn states (heavy resonances with an exponentially rising mass spectrum) can drive hadrons into chemical equilibrium almost immediately after the phase transition, even for strange and multi-strange baryons, provided the decay widths are sufficiently large; the mechanism works for both Tc = 176 MeV and Tc = 196 MeV, but the final abundance of Omega baryons depends sensitively on the assumed decay width (a 50% reduction prevents full equilibration).

## Method

The authors set up coupled master equations for the time evolution of pions, Hagedorn states, and specific hadron–antihadron pairs (p pbar, K Kbar, Lambda Lambdabar, Omega Omegabar) in an expanding fireball described by a Bjorken expansion with radial flow. The Hagedorn spectrum is taken as an exponential mass distribution with a prefactor, fitted to lattice QCD results for the trace anomaly or energy density at two different critical temperatures, and volume corrections are included to account for repulsive interactions. The rate equations incorporate decay widths for Hagedorn states decaying into pions alone or into pions plus a hadron–antihadron pair, with branching ratios following a Gaussian distribution centered on the average pion multiplicity.

## Key numbers

* Chemical equilibration times for hadrons are on the order of Δτ ≈ 1–3 fm/c.
* For Tc = 196 MeV, the initial time t0 is about 2 fm/c; for Tc = 176 MeV, t0 is about 4 fm/c.
* The initial radius at mid-rapidity is r0 = 7.1 fm for Tc = 196 MeV.
* The total pion number in the 5% most central collisions is ∫ dNπ/dy dy = 874.
* The entropy per pion is taken as Sπ/Nπ ≈ 5.5.
* The average number of pions from a Hagedorn state decaying into pions alone ranges from ⟨n_i⟩ ≈ 3 to 34; when a hadron–antihadron pair is present, ⟨n_i,x⟩ ≈ 2 to 7.
* Decay widths for Hagedorn states range from Γ_i = 250 MeV to 1800 MeV; for the Omega Omegabar channel, Γ_i,ΩΩbar ranges from 0.01 MeV to 4 MeV.
* The final particle ratios at T = 110 MeV match experimental data from STAR and PHENIX within error bars for both Tc values and for all initial conditions tested.

## Context

This work extends earlier suggestions by Braun-Munzinger, Stachel, and Wetterich that an overpopulation of pions and kaons near Tc could drive baryons into equilibrium, and by Greiner and collaborators that multi-mesonic collisions (nπ ↔ X Xbar) can chemically equilibrate antibaryons at SPS energies but are insufficient at RHIC. The Hagedorn-state mechanism provides a dynamical basis for the rapid equilibration observed in thermal fits, relating the critical temperature Tc to the chemical freeze-out temperature Tchem. The results enable future implementation of Hagedorn states in transport approaches such as UrQMD and motivate further study of strange and baryonic Hagedorn states to refine the description of multi-strange baryon production.
