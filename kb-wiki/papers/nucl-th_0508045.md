# High-energy behavior of the nuclear symmetry potential in asymmetric nuclear matter

> Using the relativistic impulse approximation with empirical NN scattering amplitude and the nuclear scalar and vector densities from the relativistic mean-field theory, we evaluate the Dirac optical potential for neutrons and protons in asymmetric nuclear matter. From the resulting Schrö% dinger-equivalent potential, the high energy behavior of the nuclear symmetry potential is studied. We find that the symmetry potential at fixed baryon density is essentially constant once the nucleon kinetic energy is greater than about 500 MeV. Moreover, for such high energy nucleon, the symmetry potential is slightly negative below a baryon density of about $% ρ=0.22$ fm$^{-3}$ and then increases almost linearly to positive values at high densities. Our results thus provide an important constraint on the energy and density dependence of nuclear symmetry potential in asymmetric nuclear matter.

## Key claim

The nuclear symmetry potential at fixed baryon density becomes essentially constant once the nucleon kinetic energy exceeds about 500 MeV, and for such high-energy nucleons the symmetry potential is slightly negative below a baryon density of about 0.22 fm⁻³ and then increases almost linearly to positive values at higher densities. This result is obtained within the relativistic impulse approximation using empirical NN scattering amplitudes and scalar/vector densities from relativistic mean-field theory, and the authors note that the symmetry potential is not very sensitive to the choice of RMF parameter set at low densities and high nucleon energies.

## Method

The authors evaluate the Dirac optical potential for neutrons and protons in asymmetric nuclear matter using the relativistic impulse approximation (RIA) with forward NN scattering amplitudes from McNeil, Ray, and Wallace (1983), which are valid for nucleon kinetic energies above about 300 MeV. The nuclear scalar and vector densities are obtained from relativistic mean-field theory using three parameter sets (NL3, Z271v, and HA). From the resulting Schrödinger-equivalent potential, the nuclear symmetry potential (Lane potential) is extracted as the difference between neutron and proton real SEPs divided by twice the isospin asymmetry.

## Key numbers

- The nuclear symmetry potential at fixed baryon density becomes essentially constant for nucleon kinetic energy greater than about 500 MeV.
- At ρ_B = 0.08 fm⁻³ (about half nuclear saturation density), the symmetry potential starts from about 0 MeV and saturates to about -3.8 ± 0.5 MeV at high energy.
- At ρ_B = 0.16 fm⁻³ (normal nuclear matter density), the symmetry potential starts from about 4.8 MeV and saturates to about -1.8 ± 1.7 MeV at high energy.
- At ρ_B = 0.24 fm⁻³ (about 1.5 times nuclear saturation density), the symmetry potential starts from about 12 MeV and saturates to about 5.3 ± 3.8 MeV at high energy.
- The symmetry potential changes from negative to positive values at a fixed baryon density of about ρ_B = 0.22 fm⁻³ for high-energy nucleons (800 MeV kinetic energy).

## Context

This work extends the relativistic impulse approximation approach of McNeil, Ray, and Wallace (1983) to asymmetric nuclear matter, providing constraints on the high-energy behavior of the nuclear symmetry potential that had been studied by various models including Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (Fuchs et al., 2004) and non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approaches, as well as phenomenological interactions like the MDI interaction (Das et al., 2003; Li et al., 2004). The results are compared with the MDI interaction for different values of the parameter x, showing agreement with x=0 at low densities but divergence at high densities. The findings provide important input for isospin-dependent transport models used in studying heavy-ion collisions with radioactive nuclei at intermediate and high energies, and are useful for extending Lorentz-covariant transport models (Ko, 1987; Mosel, 1992) to include isospin degrees of freedom.
