# Projected Density Matrix Sampling for Lattice Hamiltonians

> Quantum Monte Carlo methods are powerful tools for studying quantum many-body systems but face difficulties in accessing excited states and in treating sign problems. We present a continuous-time path-integral Monte Carlo method for computing the low-lying spectrum of generic quantum Hamiltonians within a projection subspace. The method projects the thermal density matrix onto a subspace spanned by a chosen set of linearly independent states. It is free of Trotter discretization errors and systematically converges to the low-energy states which have finite overlap with the projection subspace as the $β$ parameter increases. While most effective for systems without a sign problem, the method also yields information about low-energy spectra when sign problems are present. We illustrate the approach on two problems. For the sign-free case, we compute the first four low-energy levels in the scaling limit of the one-dimensional Ising model with both transverse and longitudinal fields, demonstrating the flow from the conformal limit to the massive $E_8$ quantum field theory. For the sign-problem case, we apply the method to the frustrated Shastry-Sutherland model and benchmark it against exact diagonalization on small lattices. We also present results for larger systems beyond the lattice sizes accessible to exact diagonalization, while limited to small $β$ where sign problems occur. Our method provides a general route toward quantum Monte Carlo spectroscopy for lattice Hamiltonians.

# Projected Density Matrix Sampling for Lattice Hamiltonians

## Key claim

The authors present a continuous-time path-integral Monte Carlo method that computes low-lying spectra of generic quantum Hamiltonians by projecting the thermal density matrix onto a chosen subspace of linearly independent states; the method systematically converges to low-energy states with finite overlap on the projection subspace as the inverse temperature parameter β increases, and while most effective for sign-problem-free systems, it also yields information about low-energy spectra when sign problems are present.

## Method

The method constructs two matrices: the overlap matrix Z with elements Z_ac = ⟨ψ_a|e^(-βH)|ψ_c⟩ and the energy matrix E with elements E_ac = ⟨ψ_a|H e^(-βH)|ψ_c⟩, both sampled via continuous-time path-integral Monte Carlo without Trotter discretization errors. The generalized eigenvalue problem involving these matrices is then solved to extract projected thermal energies e_a(β) that converge to the true low-energy eigenvalues as β increases. For sign-problem cases, the method is limited to small β where the sign problem is manageable, and a further projection onto a zero-mode-free subspace of Z is employed to avoid numerical instabilities from eigenvalues that become indistinguishable from zero within Monte Carlo errors.

## Key numbers

- For the sign-free one-dimensional Ising model with transverse and longitudinal fields at L=12 and h=0.2, the four largest overlaps between zero-momentum Z-basis states and exact energy eigenstates range from 0.6908 (for the state with all spins up overlapping with |α_0⟩) down to 0.1363 (for the r=4 state overlapping with |α_0⟩).
- For the Shastry-Sutherland model on a 2×2 lattice (L=2), the ground state energy at coupling (J=0, J′=1) is -4.00(0)(1), and at (J=1, J′=0.75) it is -2.50(0)(1).
- For the Shastry-Sutherland model on a 4×4 lattice (L=4), the ground state energy at (J=0, J′=1) is -11.228483(0)(1), and at (J=1, J′=0.75) it is -6.884403(0)(1).
- For the Shastry-Sutherland model on a 4×4 lattice at (J=1, J′=0.5), the ground state energy is -6.000000(0)(1).
- For the Shastry-Sutherland model on a 4×4 lattice at (J=1, J′=0.66), the ground state energy is -6.000000(0)(1).

## Context

This work extends the continuous-time quantum Monte Carlo approach to spectroscopy by introducing a projection-based method that avoids Trotter errors, building on the stochastic series expansion framework of Sandvik (2010). The method is demonstrated on two benchmark systems: the one-dimensional transverse-field Ising model with a longitudinal field, where it captures the flow from the conformal limit to the massive E8 quantum field theory, and the frustrated Shastry-Sutherland model, where it is benchmarked against exact diagonalization on small lattices (L=2 and L=4) and extended to larger systems beyond exact diagonalization reach. The approach enables quantum Monte Carlo spectroscopy for lattice Hamiltonians, providing a route to access excited states in systems where sign problems are absent or mild.
