# Nuclear forces from chiral effective field theory: a primer

> This paper is a write-up of introductory lectures on the modern approach to the nuclear force problem based on chiral effective field theory given at the 2009 Joliot-Curie School, Lacanau, France, 27 September - 3 October 2009.

## Key claim

This pedagogical paper argues that chiral effective field theory (ChPT) provides a systematic and model-independent framework for deriving nuclear forces from QCD's symmetries, overcoming the conceptual deficiencies of phenomenological potentials, but only if the infrared enhancement of reducible diagrams is handled by resummation via a dynamical equation, as originally pointed out by Weinberg (1990, 1991).

## Method

The paper reviews the construction of the most general non-relativistic two-nucleon potential in terms of spin-space and isospin operators, then introduces chiral perturbation theory for pions and single nucleons. It explains how to extend the EFT to few-nucleon systems by iterating the Lippmann-Schwinger equation to resum reducible diagrams, and outlines the derivation of nuclear forces order by order in the chiral expansion.

## Key numbers

* Quark masses in the MS-bar scheme at scale 2 GeV: m_u ≈ 1.5–3.3 MeV, m_d ≈ 3.5–6.0 MeV, given as small compared to typical hadron masses of order 1 GeV.
* Pion mass M_π ≈ 140 MeV versus rho meson mass M_ρ ≈ 770 MeV, illustrating the large mass gap that justifies treating pions as explicit degrees of freedom.
* The Argonne V_18 potential describes proton-proton and neutron-proton scattering data with χ²_per_datum = 1.09 using about 40 adjustable parameters.

## Context

This primer extends the line of work initiated by Weinberg (1990, 1991) on applying chiral effective field theory to nuclear forces, and contrasts with conventional phenomenological potentials such as CD-Bonn 2000 (Machleidt, 2000), Argonne V_18 (Wiringa, 1994), and Nijmegen I/II (Stoks, 1994). It enables systematic improvement of nuclear force calculations with quantifiable theoretical uncertainties and consistent three-nucleon forces, addressing deficiencies highlighted by the Urbana-Argonne group (Pieper, 2001) and Gloeckle (1995).
