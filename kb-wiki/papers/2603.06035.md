# Impact of microscopic structural transitions on particle stability and lifetimes of hot nuclei

> The impact of temperature-induced deformations and shape fluctuations on the particle stability and decay processes has been investigated across the isotopes of hot nuclear systems with $Z = 28$ to $50$, with focus on astrophysically crucial pathways at excitation energies relevant to stellar environments. We perform global finite-temperature analysis using the statistical theory of hot nuclei combined with the triaxially deformed Nilsson Hamiltonian and Strutinsky's prescription, and explore the interplay between deformation, shell quenching, separation energies, and $β$-decay characteristics at finite temperatures. Our results show that around critical temperatures $T_c \approx 1$--$2$ MeV, where the shell quenching effects become predominant, the nuclear deformation reduces and the shape undergoes a transition to the spherical configuration. Our computed neutron and proton separation energies, which usually decrease with increasing temperature, implying the reduced binding in hot nuclei, occasionally show an enhancement in some nuclei at reduced deformation around $T_c$ that shifts the last unbound nucleon to the bound, stabilizing the nucleus by shifting the drip-line boundaries. A few nuclei are found to show one- and two-neutron drip line expansion with temperature. Moreover, the temperature-induced changes in deformation strongly correlate with the marked variations in our calculated $Q_β$ values and lifetimes, underscoring their impact on weak interaction rates. These findings provide insight into the sensitivity of particle stability and weak-interaction observables to thermal effects and may serve as complementary inputs for modeling nuclear processes in hot astrophysical environments.

## Key claim
The paper claims that around critical temperatures T_c ≈ 1–2 MeV, where shell quenching becomes predominant, nuclear deformation reduces and shapes transition to spherical configurations; this occasionally enhances neutron and proton separation energies in some nuclei, shifting the last unbound nucleon to bound and expanding drip-line boundaries, but this effect is not universal and depends on the specific nucleus and its deformation trajectory.

## Method
The authors performed a global finite-temperature analysis using the statistical theory of hot nuclei combined with the triaxially deformed Nilsson Hamiltonian and Strutinsky's macroscopic-microscopic prescription. They minimized the free energy F = E − TS with respect to the Nilsson deformation parameters β₂ and γ to determine the equilibrium shape and deformation of excited nuclei at fixed temperatures T = 0.6–3.0 MeV. Binding energies, separation energies, Qβ values, and β-decay lifetimes were computed for even–even isotopic chains of Z = 28 to 50, covering nuclei from the proton to neutron drip line.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies by Aggarwal and collaborators (Aggarwal 2009, Aggarwal 2014) on temperature-dependent nuclear deformation and binding, and is consistent with finite-temperature QRPA calculations by Minato and Hagino (2009) and FT-PNRQRPA calculations by Ravlic and collaborators (2021), which also predict systematic decreases in β-decay half-lives with increasing temperature. The results are qualitatively consistent with finite-temperature relativistic Hartree–Bogoliubov calculations by Ravlic and collaborators (2023) that reported drip-line expansion with temperature, but the present work explicitly links this expansion to deformation collapse and shell quenching. The findings provide complementary inputs for modeling nuclear processes in hot astrophysical environments, particularly for pre-collapse and early high-temperature stellar stages where β-decay remains relevant before electron-capture-dominated dynamics set in.
