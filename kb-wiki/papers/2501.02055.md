# Parity-doubled nucleons can rapidly cool neutron stars

> In confined hadronic matter, the spontaneous breaking and restoration of chiral symmetry can be described by considering nucleons, $N_{+}(939)$, and excited states of opposite parity, $N_{-}(1535)$. In a cold, dense hadronic phase where chiral symmetry remains spontaneously broken, direct Urca decay processes involving the $N_{-}$ are possible, e.g. $N_- \rightarrow N_+ + e^- + \barν_e$. We show that at low temperature and moderate densities, because the $N_-$ is much heavier than the $N_+$, such cooling dominates over standard $N_+$ direct Urca processes. This provides a strong astrophysical signature of the pattern of chiral symmetry restoration in neutron stars.

## Key claim

In a cold, dense hadronic phase where chiral symmetry remains spontaneously broken, direct Urca decay processes involving the negative-parity nucleon \(N_{-}(1535)\) (e.g., \(N_{-} \rightarrow N_{+} + e^{-} + \bar{\nu}_e\)) can dominate over standard nucleon direct Urca processes at low temperature and moderate densities, because the \(N_{-}\) is much heavier than the \(N_{+}\). This provides a strong astrophysical signature of the pattern of chiral symmetry restoration in neutron stars, though the precise density window and mass range are model-dependent.

## Method

The authors construct a parity-doubled nucleon model using a chirally symmetric Lagrangian with nucleons \(\psi_1\) and \(\psi_2\) coupled to scalar (\(\sigma\)), pseudoscalar (\(\pi\)), and vector (\(\omega, \rho\)) mesons, with a chirally invariant mass term \(m_0\). They compute neutrino emissivities from direct Urca processes involving the \(N_{-}\) and compare them to modified Urca processes and standard direct Urca processes from the IUF relativistic mean-field model. The cooling curves are obtained by solving the heat balance equation using the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation for stellar structure, with a crust equation of state attached at baryon chemical potential \(\mu_B = 952\) MeV.

## Key numbers

- The Fermi sea of \(\neutronMinus\) (negative-parity neutron) forms at baryon number density \(n_B = 2.94\, n_{\text{sat}}\), where \(n_{\text{sat}} = 0.16\ \text{fm}^{-3}\) is nuclear saturation density.
- The \(\neutronMinus \rightarrow \proton + e^{-} + \bar{\nu}_e\) direct Urca process is kinematically allowed only between \(n_B = 2.94\, n_{\text{sat}}\) and \(n_B = 3.25\, n_{\text{sat}}\).
- The onset density for \(\protonMinus\) (negative-parity proton) is \(n_B = 5.31\, n_{\text{sat}}\).
- For a \(2.19\ \text{M}_{\odot}\) star with parity-doubled baryons, the characteristic cooling timescale is 159 days, compared to 298 years for a parity-singlet model (PSM) with only \(N_{+}\).
- For a \(1.95\ \text{M}_{\odot}\) star, the parity-doubled model cools with a characteristic timescale of 179 days, compared to 236 days for the IUF model (which only includes \(N_{+}\) and has a direct Urca threshold at \(n_B = 4.13\, n_{\text{sat}}\)).
- The mass range corresponding to central densities where \(\neutronMinus \rightarrow \proton + e^{-} + \bar{\nu}_e\) is allowed is \(1.83\ \text{M}_{\odot} - 1.97\ \text{M}_{\odot}\) in this model.
- The maximum mass predicted by the model is \(M = 2.19\ \text{M}_{\odot}\) with radius \(R(1.4\ \text{M}_{\odot}) = 12.9\) km.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of parity-doubled models in neutron stars (Dexheimer 2012, Mukherjee 2017, Marczenko 2018) by computing for the first time the direct Urca neutrino emissivity for processes involving the negative-parity nucleon \(N_{-}\). Prior work considered the effect of \(N_{-}\) population on the direct Urca threshold for processes involving only \(N_{+}\) (Dexheimer 2012), noted the lack of a direct Urca threshold for processes involving \(N_{+}\) (Mukherjee 2017), and noted when direct Urca processes involving \(N_{-}\) are kinematically allowed in the chirally symmetric phase (Marczenko 2018). This mechanism may help explain neutron stars that appear to cool more rapidly than by standard mechanisms (Brown 2017, Marino 2024) and could impact neutron star merger dynamics and gravitational-wave signals through bulk viscous effects.
