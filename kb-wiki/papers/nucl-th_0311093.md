# Modelling of compound nucleus formation in fusion of heavy nuclei

> A new model that includes the time-dependent dynamics of the single-particle (s.p.) motion in conjunction with the macroscopic evolution of the system is proposed for describing the compound nucleus (CN) formation in fusion of heavy nuclei. The diabaticity initially keeps the entrance system around its contact configuration, but the gradual transition from the diabatic to the adiabatic potential energy surface (PES) leads to fusion or quasifission. Direct measurements of the probability for CN formation are crucial to discriminate between the current models.

# Modelling of compound nucleus formation in fusion of heavy nuclei

## Key claim
The paper proposes a new model for compound nucleus (CN) formation that, for the first time, combines time-dependent single-particle motion with macroscopic evolution, where the gradual transition from a diabatic to an adiabatic potential energy surface (PES) determines whether fusion or quasifission occurs. The model's predictions for the probability of CN formation agree well with direct experimental measurements for the $^{96}$Zr+$^{124}$Sn reaction, but the author notes that direct measurements are crucial to discriminate between current models.

## Method
The model solves a master equation for population probabilities over a discretized collective coordinate space of shape parameters (elongation $\lambda$ and volume asymmetry $\xi$), using a time-dependent PES that evolves from diabatic to adiabatic. The diabatic PES is calculated using the diabatic two-center shell model (TCSM), while the adiabatic PES uses Strutinsky's macroscopic-microscopic method; the transition between them is governed by a relaxation equation for single-particle occupation numbers with an average relaxation time $\tau$ derived from level widths. The calculations are performed for (near) symmetric central collisions ($l=0$) with an initial excitation energy of 40 MeV at the contact configuration, using a time step of $10^{-23}$ s on a grid ($1\leq\lambda\leq1.8$, $|\xi|\leq0.7$).

## Key numbers
- For $^{90}$Zr+$^{90}$Zr $\rightarrow$ $^{180}$Hg: $P_{CN} = 3\times 10^{-1}$, $t_0 = 5\times 10^{-20}$ s, $E_{CN} = 40$ MeV
- For $^{100}$Mo+$^{100}$Mo $\rightarrow$ $^{200}$Po: $P_{CN} = 1.4\times 10^{-1}$, $t_0 = 4.5\times 10^{-20}$ s, $E_{CN} = 40$ MeV
- For $^{110}$Pd+$^{110}$Pd $\rightarrow$ $^{220}$U: $P_{CN} = 1.7\times 10^{-2}$, $t_0 = 4\times 10^{-20}$ s, $E_{CN} = 40$ MeV
- For $^{100}$Mo+$^{110}$Pd $\rightarrow$ $^{210}$Ra: $P_{CN} = 7.5\times 10^{-2}$, $t_0 = 4.3\times 10^{-20}$ s, $E_{CN} = 40$ MeV
- For $^{96}$Zr+$^{124}$Sn $\rightarrow$ $^{220}$Th: $P_{CN} = 4.5\times 10^{-2}$, $t_0 = 4\times 10^{-20}$ s, $E_{CN} = 40$ MeV, compared to experimental $P_{CN}^{\exp.} = 5\times 10^{-2}$ from Hinde
- The strength constant $\kappa_0$ has a realistic value of $\sim 10^{22}$ s$^{-1}$
- The relaxation parameter $N_{coll} = 3$ is used for the number of two-body collisions per nucleon to establish equilibrium
- The width parameter $\Gamma_0^{-1} = 0.061$ MeV$^{-1}$ is used for half saturation density

## Context
This work extends previous models of CN formation by combining ideas (i) multidimensional collective coordinate space, (ii) master equation dynamics, and (iii) time-dependent single-particle motion, which had previously only been applied separately. Prior approaches include models using adiabatic PES along the radial coordinate (Greiner, Swiatecki, Aritomo, Tokuda, Zagrebaev, Shen, Abe) and the dinuclear system (DNS) model (Volkov) which assumes a sudden PES in the radial coordinate and adiabatic behavior in the mass asymmetry coordinate. The model's predictions for $^{96}$Zr+$^{124}$Sn agree with direct experimental measurements from Hinde, while the fluctuation-dissipation model of Tokuda predicts $P_{CN}$ values about one order of magnitude larger for $^{110}$Pd+$^{110}$Pd and $^{100}$Mo+$^{110}$Pd. The approach enables future combination with models for the initial capture stage and CN survival against fission to calculate evaporation residue cross sections, and a more appropriate TCSM for large mass-asymmetry reactions is being developed for predicting superheavy element production cross sections.
