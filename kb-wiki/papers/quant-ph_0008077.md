# Diffraction of wave packets in space and time

> The phenomenon of wave packet diffraction in space and time is described. It consists in a diffraction pattern whose spatial location progresses with time. The pattern is produced by wave packet quantum scattering off an attractive or repulsive time independent potential. An analytical formula for the pattern at $t\to\infty$ is derived both in one dimension and in three dimensions. The condition for the pattern to exist is developed. The phenomenon is shown numerically and analytically for the Dirac equation in one dimension also. An experiment for the verification of the phenomenon is described and simulated numerically.

# Diffraction of wave packets in space and time

## Key claim

Wave packet scattering off a time-independent potential (attractive or repulsive) produces a persistent diffraction pattern in space that travels with time and does not decay exponentially, unlike the "diffraction in time" phenomenon. The pattern survives only for wave packets whose initial width is smaller than a value related to the potential width; for wider packets the multiple-peak structure merges into a single peak.

## Method

The author derives an analytical formula for the asymptotic (t → ∞) wave function in one dimension using a Gaussian wave packet scattering off a square well, employing the saddle point method to evaluate the reflected wave contribution from low-momentum components. The analysis is extended to the one-dimensional Dirac equation with a scalar potential and to three-dimensional scattering off a spherically symmetric square well, where only the L=0 partial wave contributes at long times. Numerical simulations of the Schrödinger and Dirac equations are performed and compared to the analytical expressions.

## Key numbers

- Initial momentum q₀ = 0.4, mass m = 40, width σ = 0.5, initial position x₀ = -60, well width w = 1, well depth V₀ = 1, at time t = 1.2 × 10⁷ (in units where ħ = c = 1) for the one-dimensional comparison between numerical and analytical results
- Velocity v = q₀/m = 0.01 (nonrelativistic regime) for the one-dimensional numerical example
- Condition for pattern survival: σ << √(w/q₀), derived from the scaling argument k_max ≈ (2w)⁻¹
- For the three-dimensional example: neutron packet of 1 Fermi width, initial velocity v = 0.02 c, well width 10 Fermi, depth 40 MeV, initial position x₀ = -20 Fermi, impact parameter y₀ = 2 Fermi, time t = 5 × 10¹⁴ (in Fermi units)
- For the proposed helium experiment: drop volume 1 cm³, total particle number N = 5 × 10²¹, plate width w = 1 cm, barrier strength V₀ = 4 eV, detector size dx = 1 mm

## Context

This work extends the author's own numerical investigations of one- and two-dimensional wave packet scattering (Kälbermann, 1998; 1999) that showed curious multiple-peak structures. It contrasts with the "diffraction in time" phenomenon of Moshinsky (1952), which arises from a suddenly opened shutter and decays exponentially, and with the "diffraction in space and time" of Zeilinger (1987) using plane waves with time-dependent slits. The present effect persists to infinite time and arises from interference between the incoming packet and the reflected wave originating from momenta k → 0, which corresponds to excitation of a virtual half-bound state at threshold, related to Levinson's theorem (Sassoli de Bianchi, 1994; Dong, 1997). The work enables experimental tests using macroscopic quantum systems such as liquid helium drops and provides a new tool for investigating structure and dynamics of atoms and nuclei through time-dependent wave packet scattering.
