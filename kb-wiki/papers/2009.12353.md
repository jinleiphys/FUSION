# The special point on the hybrid star mass--radius diagram and its multi--messenger implications

> We show the existence and investigate the location of the special point (SP) in which hybrid neutron star mass-radius (M-R) curves have to cross each other when they belong to a class of hybrid equation of state (EoS) constructed with generic constant--speed--of--sound (CSS) quark matter models for which the onset deconfinement is varied. We demonstrate that for a three-parameter CSS model the position of the SP in the M-R diagram is largely independent of the choice of the hadronic EoS, but in dependence on the stiffness of the quark matter EoS it spans a region that we identify. We find that the difference between the maximum mass and the SP mass depends on the mass at the onset of deconfinement so that an upper limit of $0.19~M_\odot$ for this difference is obtained from which a lower limit on the radius of hybrid stars is deduced. Together with a lower limit on the radius of hadronic stars, derived from a class of reasonably soft hadronic EoS including hyperons, we identify a region in the M-R diagram which can be occupied only by hybrid stars. Accordingly, we suggest that a NICER radius measurement on the massive pulsar PSR J0740+6620 in the range of 8.6-11.9 km would indicate that this pulsar is a hybrid neutron star with deconfined quark matter in the inner core.

# The special point on the hybrid star mass–radius diagram and its multi–messenger implications

## Key claim

The special point (SP) is not a strict intersection of all hybrid mass–radius curves but rather a very narrow region to which they converge, so its mass and radius values should be considered approximate. The authors suggest that a NICER radius measurement of PSR J0740+6620 in the range of 8.6–11.9 km would indicate that this pulsar is a hybrid neutron star with deconfined quark matter in the inner core, but this conclusion depends on knowledge of the lower limit for purely hadronic neutron star radii.

## Method

The authors construct hybrid equations of state by coupling a hadronic density-dependent relativistic mean field model (DD2 parametrization with excluded volume effects) to three classes of quark matter models: the constant-speed-of-sound (CSS) model, an ordinary bag model with vector repulsion, and the vBag model which imposes simultaneous chiral symmetry restoration and deconfinement. For each model class, they vary the onset density of deconfinement and solve the Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff equations to obtain mass–radius relations, then locate the special point where these curves converge. They derive an upper limit on the mass difference between the maximum mass and the SP mass as a function of the onset mass, and combine this with observational constraints from PSR J0740+6620 and GW170817.

## Key numbers

- Upper limit on the difference between maximum mass and SP mass: 0.19 solar masses, derived from the fit formula M_max = M_SP + 0.208 solar masses – 0.104 M_onset.
- Lower limit on SP mass for agreement with the 2 solar mass constraint: 1.77 solar masses, based on the 95.4% credibility lower limit of 2.14 – 0.18 solar masses for PSR J0740+6620.
- Suggested radius range for PSR J0740+6620 to be identified as a hybrid star: 8.6–11.9 km.
- For the vBag model, the change in SP mass and radius when varying the hadronic EoS is less than 10%.
- The crossover temperature from lattice QCD is given as 156.5 ± 1.5 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the original observation of a special point in hybrid star mass–radius curves by Yudin (2014) to a wider class of equations of state including vector repulsion and the vBag model. The authors demonstrate that the SP is largely independent of the hadronic EoS choice, in contrast to earlier assumptions. The study enables a phenomenological tool for interpreting multi-messenger observations from NICER and GW170817 to identify hybrid stars with quark matter cores. The authors specifically compare their results to the APR equation of state from Akmal (1998) and to multi-pomeron exchange models from Yamamoto (2017, 2015) that solve the hyperon puzzle.
