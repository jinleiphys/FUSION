# Mathematical formulae for neutron self-shielding properties of media in an isotropic neutron field

> The complexity of the neutron transport phenomenon throws its shadows on every physical system wherever neutron is produced or used. In the current study, an ab initio derivation of the neutron self-shielding factor to solve the problem of the decrease of the neutron flux as it penetrates into a material placed in an isotropic neutron field. We have employed the theory of steady-state neutron transport, starting from Stuart's formula. Simple formulae were derived based on the integral cross-section parameters that could be adopted by the user according to various variables, such as the neutron flux distribution and geometry of the simulation at hand. The concluded formulae of the self-shielding factors comprise an inverted sigmoid function normalized with a weight representing the ratio between the macroscopic total and scattering cross-sections of the medium. The general convex volume geometries are reduced to a set of chord lengths, while the neutron interactions probabilities within the volume are parameterized to the epithermal and thermal neutron energies. The arguments of the inverted-sigmoid function were derived from a simplified version of neutron transport formulation. Accordingly, the obtained general formulae were successful in giving the values of the experimental neutron self-shielding factor for different elements and different geometries.

## Key claim

The authors derive an *ab initio* mathematical formula for the neutron self-shielding factor in an isotropic neutron field, claiming it reproduces experimental values for different elements and geometries. The key hedge is that the derived formula is an approximation that neglects higher-order terms (denoted $\mathcal{R}$) from the velocity distribution, which the authors state have negligible contributions except when relying on the entire range of the Maxwellian thermal neutron distribution.

## Method

Starting from Stuart's formula and Blaauw's reformulation of neutron blackness, the authors derived a closed-form expression for the self-shielding factor $G$ as an inverted sigmoid function normalized by the ratio of macroscopic total to scattering cross-sections ($\Sigma_t/\Sigma_s$). The geometry of convex bodies was reduced to a set of chord lengths derived from the solution of the steady-state neutron diffusion equation with vacuum boundary conditions, yielding average chord lengths ($\bar{\ell}$) for spheres, infinite sheets, cylinders, and boxes. The probability of first interaction ($P_0$) was obtained from the attenuation relation using the average chord length, and the macroscopic cross-sections were computed from element-averaged thermal cross-sections and resonance integrals.

## Key numbers

*   For an infinite sheet of thickness $t$, the average neutron-chord length is $\bar{\ell} = 3t$.
*   For a sphere of radius $R$, the average neutron-chord length is $\bar{\ell} = 3R$.
*   For an infinite wire (cylinder) of radius $R$, the average neutron-chord length is $\bar{\ell} = 3.9191R$.
*   For a finite cylinder of radius $R$ and height $H$, the average neutron-chord length is $\bar{\ell} = \frac{3\pi R H}{2.4048 H + \pi R}$.
*   For a finite disk of radius $R$ and thickness $t$, the average neutron-chord length is $\bar{\ell} = \frac{3\pi R t}{2.4048 t + \pi R}$.
*   The digitization uncertainty of experimental data used for validation was less than 2%.

## Context

This work extends prior empirical and Monte Carlo approaches to neutron self-shielding, specifically those by Fleming (1982), Blaauw (1995, 1996), and the series of studies by Gonalves, Martinho, and Salgado (2001-2006) which provided empirical formulae for limited geometries and elements. The derived analytical formulae aim to replace time-consuming Monte Carlo calculations (Larsen, 2006) and provide a practical tool for routine neutron activation analysis by using only three well-known integral parameters: thermal cross-section, resonance integral, and average chord length. This enables non-specialist users to compute self-shielding factors without detailed spectroscopic data or complex simulations.
