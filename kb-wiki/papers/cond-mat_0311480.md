# Unified description of floppy and rigid rotating Wigner molecules formed in quantum dots

> Restoration of broken circular symmetry is used to explore the characteristics of the ground states and the excitation spectra of rotating Wigner molecules (RWM's) formed in two-dimensional parabolic N-electron quantum dots. In high magnetic fields, the RWM's are floppy rotors with the energies of the magic angular momentum (L) states obeying aL + b/L^{1/2}. Under such fields the ground-state energies (referenced to the kinetic energy in the lowest Landau level) approach the electrostatic energy of N point charges in the classical equilibrium molecular configuration. At zero field and strong interelectron repulsion, the RWM's behave like quasiclassical rigid rotors whose energies vary as L^2. The particular L-dependence in high B is inherent and natural to a floppy rotating WM, and it can be used as a crucial diagnostic tool for resolving the recently posed question whether the composite-fermion or the RWM picture is appropriate for QD's.

# Unified description of floppy and rigid rotating Wigner molecules formed in quantum dots

## Key claim

Rotating Wigner molecules (RWMs) in two-dimensional parabolic quantum dots exhibit a unified behavior across magnetic field regimes: at high magnetic fields they are floppy rotors with energies following aL + b/L^{1/2}, while at zero field and strong interelectron repulsion they behave as quasiclassical rigid rotors with energies varying as L^2. The authors claim this RWM picture can resolve whether composite-fermion or RWM descriptions are appropriate for quantum dots, but note that the high-B limit of ground-state energies approaching the classical electrostatic energy of N point charges is a result "innate to the WM picture" that the composite-fermion approach "cannot conform to."

## Method

The authors use a two-step method: first, broken-symmetry unrestricted Hartree-Fock (UHF) orbitals are approximated by parameter-free displaced Gaussian functions at classical equilibrium positions of N point charges in a parabolic confinement; second, circular symmetry is restored via post-Hartree-Fock projection operators to extract correlated many-body states with good total angular momenta L. The projected energies are computed from overlap and Hamiltonian matrix elements between the UHF determinant and its rotated versions, allowing access to remarkably high angular momenta (L up to 1100 for N=5) that exact diagonalization cannot reach.

## Key numbers

- For N=2 electrons (triplet state), projected (PRJ) and exact total energies become "practically the same" for B > 7 T (parameters: m* = 0.067 m_e, ħω₀ = 3 meV, κ = 12.9)
- Ground-state energies approach classical electrostatic limits: E^st_cl(3) = 14.415 meV for N=3 and E^st_cl(5) = 42.873 meV for N=5 as B → ∞
- Projected ground states reach angular momenta L = 369, 690, and 1100 for N=3, 4, and 5 respectively
- At B=60 T for N=5, the interaction energy V_PRJ(L) follows C_V/L^{1/2} with C_V = 15.388 (in units of e²/κλ); for L=40 (ν=1/4), V_PRJ = 2.5000; for L=190 (ν=1/19), V_PRJ = 1.1216
- At B → ∞ for N=6 in the (1,5) configuration, C_V = 18.787; for L=75 (ν=1/5), V_PRJ = 2.2196; for L=675 (ν=1/45), V_PRJ = 0.7247
- At B=0 and R_W=200 for N=5, projected total energies follow E_PRJ(L) ≈ E_PRJ(0) + C_R L^2 with C_R/C_R^cl ≈ 0.978–0.992 for L = 5–45; E_PRJ(0) = 323.3070 ħω₀
- Classical electrostatic limit formula: E^st_cl(N) = (3/8)(2 R_W)^{2/3} N S_N^{2/3} ħω₀, where S_N = Σ_{j=2}^N (sin[(j-1)π/N])^{-1}
- Addition energy (finite second difference) for N=4 tends to Δ^st_{2,cl} E(4) = 3.446 meV at high B

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier proposal (Yannouleas and Landman, 2002) that rotating Wigner molecules, rather than composite-fermions (Jain, 1989; Hawrylak, 1993), are the fundamental physical entity in strongly correlated quantum dots. It directly challenges the composite-fermion approach to quantum dots by showing that the high-B ground-state energy limit approaches the classical electrostatic energy of point charges, a result the authors argue is incompatible with composite-fermion theory. The method builds on prior studies of static Wigner molecules (Reusch, 2001; Kaestner, 2001; Sun, 2002; Szafran, 2003) by adding the crucial symmetry restoration step that captures rotation. The work enables experimental detection of the RWM picture through the predicted L-dependence of excitation spectra and the constant addition energies at high magnetic fields, which may be measurable in high-mobility samples at low fractional fillings.
