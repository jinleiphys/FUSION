# Baryon Transport in Color Flux Tubes

> Color flux tubes are a standard perspective from which to understand stopping in high-energy collisions. Mechanisms for baryon transport and polarization within a tube are considered here, both in regards to the the transport of baryons from the target and projectile toward mid-rapidity, and in regards to the correlations of baryon-antibaryon pairs created in the tube. The roles of tube merging and gluon radiation with a tube are emphasized.

## Key claim

Baryon number transport in high-energy collisions can be understood through color flux tubes without requiring quarks to travel large distances; instead, baryon number appears to move because quark-antiquark dipoles from the vacuum align in a polarized manner, analogous to electric dipoles in a dielectric, and the exponential falloff of baryon stopping with rapidity loss emerges naturally if the paths are thermally weighted.

## Method

The author develops a heuristic flux-tube model where baryon transport occurs when two simple tubes (each with a quark at one end and an antiquark at the other) merge into one, transferring baryon number to the merging point. A linear response theory is constructed using gauge-invariant correlators based on the cubic Casimir of SU(3) to connect color fields to baryon current and polarization, with Kubo relations derived for both conductivity and polarizability. The formalism is extended to include tubes with intermediate gluonic color charges and higher multiplets that decay to color singlets.

## Key numbers

- For a quark (p=1,q=0): ⟨Q^(2)⟩ = 4/3 and ⟨Q^(3)⟩ = (10/9)g³; for an antiquark (p=0,q=1): ⟨Q^(3)⟩ = -(10/9)g³
- The degeneracy of a (p,q) multiplet is (p+1)(q+1)(p+q+2)/2
- The quadratic Casimir: C^(quadratic) = (p²+q²+3p+3q+pq)/3
- The cubic Casimir: C^(cubic) = (p-q)(2p+q+3)(2q+p+3)/18
- Baryons from incoming nuclei lose roughly one unit of rapidity in the collision
- At LHC energies, the rapidity difference from target/projectile to mid-rapidity is approximately 7 units

## Context

This work extends the flux-tube merging picture of Vance and Pratt (1997), which proposed that baryon transport occurs when two strings from separate quarks merge, with baryon number appearing at the merging point. The approach is contrasted with the baryon-junction hypothesis of Kharzeev (1996), where baryon number is carried by gluonic degrees of freedom rather than quarks. The paper provides a formal linear-response framework connecting color fields to baryon transport, and suggests that baryon-antibaryon pair correlations and the weaker transport of electric charge compared to baryon number could be tested experimentally, though the author notes the analysis is too qualitative to warrant immediate experimental investment.
