# Prevailing Triaxial Shapes in Atomic Nuclei and a Quantum Theory of Rotation of Composite Objects

> In the traditional view, heavy deformed nuclei are like axially-symmetric prolate ellipsoids, rotating about one of the short axes. In the present picture, their shapes may be triaxial. The triaxial shape yields complex rotations, which actually well reproduce experimental data, as confirmed by state-of-the-art Configuration Interaction calculations. Two origins are suggested for the triaxiality: (i) binding-energy gain by the symmetry restoration for triaxial shapes, and (ii) another gain by specific components of the nuclear force, like tensor force and high-multipole (e.g. hexadecupole) central force. While the origin (i) produces basic smaller triaxiality for virtually all deformed nuclei, the origin (ii) produces medium triaxiality for a certain class of nuclei. An example of the former is 154Sm, a typical showcase of axial symmetry but is now suggested to depict a modest yet finite triaxiality. The latter, medium triaxiality, is discussed from various viewpoints for some exemplified nuclei including 166Er, and experimental findings. Many-body structures of the gamma band and the double-gamma band are clarified. Regarding the general features of rotational states of deformed many-body systems including triaxial ones, the well-known J(J+1) rule of rotational excitation energies is discussed, within the quantum mechanical many-body theory, without resorting to the quantization of a rotating classical rigid body. The picture of prevailing triaxial shapes thus emerges, where the empirically known rotational-band pattern appears with good K quantum number, but the internal structure is dfferent from conventional picture a la A. Bohr. The possible relations to Davydov's rigid-triaxial-rotor model are mentioned.

## Key claim

The paper argues that, contrary to the traditional textbook picture of axially-symmetric prolate ellipsoids, a large number of heavy deformed nuclei (mass number A > 150) exhibit substantially triaxial shapes, with the triaxiality arising from two distinct mechanisms: a small triaxiality from symmetry restoration that applies to virtually all deformed nuclei, and a medium triaxiality from specific nuclear force components (tensor force and high-multipole central force) that applies to a certain class of nuclei.

## Method

The authors performed state-of-the-art Configuration Interaction (CI) calculations using the Monte Carlo Shell Model (MCSM) with the Quasiparticle Vacua Shell Model (QVSM) methodology, employing a realistic effective nucleon-nucleon interaction based on the monopole-based-universal interaction (V_MU) with central and tensor force components. The calculations used 8 active proton orbitals and 10 active neutron orbitals, with 28 active protons and 28 active neutrons for the nucleus 166Er, incorporating angular-momentum, parity, and particle-number projections in a variation-after-projection framework.

## Key numbers

- For 166Er, the B(E2; 2+_2 → 0+_1) transition strength is 5.17 ± 0.21 Weisskopf units (W.u.), indicating collective character of the 2+_2 state
- The rotational band energy formula E_x(J^P) = κ J(J+1) is shown to reproduce experimental 4+ and 6+ energies when κ is adjusted to the measured 2+ level energy
- The criterion 3.00 ≤ E_x(4+_1)/E_x(2+_1) ≤ 3.33 (the ideal rotor ratio being 10/3) is used to select strongly deformed nuclei
- 66 nuclei between mass number A=150 and 208 fulfill this rotational criterion with E_x(2+_1) mostly below 0.125 MeV
- Only 9 nuclei in the A=100-132 region and 5 light nuclei (including 24Mg and 42S) meet the same criterion

## Context

This work extends and challenges the traditional picture established by Bohr and Mottelson (1950s-1970s) of axially-symmetric prolate deformation as the dominant shape for heavy deformed nuclei, which has been a textbook item for over 70 years. It also relates to the rigid-triaxial-rotor model of Davydov, whose claim of triaxial shapes is assessed with appreciation while the model itself is evaluated separately. The calculations go beyond earlier many-body approaches such as the pairing-plus-quadrupole model of Kumar and Baranger (1968) and the work of Bes and Sorensen (1969), which advocated preponderance of axially symmetric shapes. The results enable a quantum many-body description of rotational bands classified by K quantum number without resorting to quantization of a classical rigid body, and suggest experimental approaches such as Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions to probe ground-state triaxiality.
