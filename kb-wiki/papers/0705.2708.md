# Neutron Star Interiors and the Equation of State of Superdense Matter

> Neutron stars contain matter in one of the densest forms found in the Universe. This feature, together with the unprecedented progress in observational astrophysics, makes such stars superb astrophysical laboratories for a broad range of exciting physical studies. This paper gives an overview of the phases of dense matter predicted to make their appearance in the cores of neutron stars. Particular emphasis is put on the role of strangeness. Net strangeness is carried by hyperons, K-mesons, H-dibaryons, and strange quark matter, and may leave its mark in the masses, radii, moment of inertia, dragging of local inertial frames, cooling behavior, surface composition, and the spin evolution of neutron stars. These observables play a key role for the exploration of the phase diagram of dense nuclear matter at high baryon number density but low temperature, which is not accessible to relativistic heavy ion collision experiments.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that the cores of neutron stars likely contain exotic forms of superdense matter, including hyperons, meson condensates, H-dibaryons, and strange quark matter, but emphasizes that the actual composition depends sensitively on the still-uncorrected equation of state and on observational constraints such as measured neutron star masses, with the caveat that many predicted phases (e.g., K⁻ condensates) may be preempted by other particles (e.g., Σ⁻ hyperons or quarks) that appear at similar densities.

## Method

The paper surveys theoretical models for the equation of state of neutron star matter, classifying them into Thomas-Fermi, Schrödinger-based (variational, Monte Carlo, Brueckner-Hartree-Fock), relativistic field-theoretical (relativistic mean-field, relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock), Nambu-Jona-Lasinio, and chiral SU(3) quark mean-field approaches. It then applies the Gibbs conditions for phase equilibrium between confined hadronic matter and deconfined quark matter, using the bag model for quark matter and relativistic mean-field models for hadronic matter, to compute particle compositions and mass-radius relations for both non-rotating and rotating neutron stars.

## Key numbers

* Maximum mass of a non-interacting relativistic neutron gas: 0.7 solar masses (M☉), at a central density of 17 times nuclear matter density.
* Maximum mass of an interacting neutron gas: around 3 M☉, but such stars have radii too large to support observed rapid rotation (e.g., PSR J1748-2446ad at 716 Hz).
* Observed neutron star masses: PSR J0751+1807 = 2.1 ± 0.2 M☉; 4U 1636+536 = 2.0 ± 0.1 M☉; Vela X-1 = 1.86 ± 0.16 M☉; Cyg X-2 = 1.78 ± 0.23 M☉; 4U 1700-37 = 2.44 ± 0.27 M☉; 2S0921-630 = 2.0 – 4.3 M☉.
* Theoretical upper limit on neutron star mass (Rhoades-Ruffini): 3.2 M☉ under causality; up to ~5 M☉ if causality is abandoned.
* Nuclear matter saturation density: n₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³; binding energy E/A = –16.0 MeV; incompressibility K ≈ 240 MeV; symmetry energy aₛ = 32.5 MeV.
* Threshold for Λ hyperon appearance (non-interacting estimate): k_Fn ≥ 3 fm⁻¹, corresponding to n ≈ 6 n₀; with interactions, threshold drops to about 2 n₀.
* Critical proton fraction for nucleon direct Urca processes: ~11% (n_p/n > 1/9); with muons, raised to about 0.15.
* K⁻ meson mass in dense matter: m*_K⁻ ≈ 200 MeV at densities ~3 n₀, from the relation m*_K⁻/m_K⁻ ≈ 1 – 0.2 n/n₀ (m_K⁻ vacuum mass = 495 MeV).
* Onset density for H-dibaryon matter: above ~4 n₀.
* Bag constants for strange quark matter: B = 57 MeV/fm³ (B^(1/4) = 145 MeV) gives E/A = 829 MeV; B = 85 MeV/fm³ (B^(1/4) = 160 MeV) gives E/A = 915 MeV.
* Color-superconducting gap for quark matter at astrophysical chemical potentials (μ < 1000 MeV): between 50 and 100 MeV.
* Electric field at strange star surface: ~10¹⁹ V/cm.

## Context

This review extends earlier work on neutron star equations of state summarized in books by Glendenning (1997) and Weber (1999), and in reviews by Heiselberg and Pandharipande (2000), Lattimer and Prakash (2001), and Weber (2005). It specifically addresses the role of strangeness, via hyperons, K⁻ mesons, H-dibaryons, and strange quark matter, which was not emphasized in earlier reviews. The paper enables future observational tests: measurements of neutron star masses near 2 M☉ (e.g., from Nice et al. 2005, Barret et al. 2006) can rule out soft equations of state that predict maximum masses below ~1.5 M☉ (e.g., those with K⁻ condensation from Thorsson et al. 1994), while observations of rapid rotation (e.g., PSR J1748-2446ad at 716 Hz from Hessels et al. 2006) constrain the stiffness of the equation of state. The discussion of color superconductivity (Rajagopal and Wilczek 2001, Alford et al. 2001) opens the possibility of using neutron star cooling, magnetic field evolution, and glitches as probes of the quark matter phase.
