# Nuclear shape transitions, level density, and underlying interactions

> The configuration interaction approach to nuclear structure uses the effective Hamiltonian in a finite orbital space. The various parts of this Hamiltonian and their interplay are responsible for specific features of physics including the shape of the mean field and level density. This interrelation is not sufficiently understood. We intend to study phase transitions between spherical and deformed shapes driven by different parts of the nuclear Hamiltonian and to establish the presence of the collective enhancement of the nuclear level density by varying the shell-model matrix elements. Varying the interaction matrix elements we define, for nuclei in the sd and pf shells, the sectors with spherical and deformed shapes. Using the moments method that does not require the full diagonalization we relate the shape transitions with the corresponding level density. Enhancement of the level density in the low-energy part of the spectrum is observed in clear correlation with a deformation phase transition induced mainly by the matrix elements of single-particle transfer. The single-particle transfer matrix elements in the shell model nuclear Hamiltonian are indeed the carriers of deformation, providing rotational observables and enhanced level densities.

## Key claim

The single-particle transfer matrix elements (the "one unit change" part of the shell-model Hamiltonian) are the primary carriers of nuclear deformation, and their strong presence correlates with an enhancement of the low-energy level density; however, this enhancement is not observed at the exact quantum phase transition point (λ ≈ 0.2) but rather in the deformed regime away from the transition.

## Method

The authors divided the shell-model Hamiltonian into two parts: V₁ containing matrix elements that change subshell occupation by one unit (particle-hole type, Δℓ = 0 or 2) and V₂ containing the remaining matrix elements. They varied the strengths k₁ and k₂ of these parts independently, and also studied a control parameter λ in H = h + (1-λ)V₁ + λV₂, for nuclei in the sd shell (²⁴Mg, ²⁸Si) and pf shell (⁵²Fe). Using the moments method (which avoids full diagonalization) they computed level densities and low-lying observables including yrast energies, quadrupole moments, and B(E2) transition probabilities.

## Context

This work extends the moments method approach previously developed by the authors (PLB11, CPC13, SZ15) and builds on a study by Horoi (unreferenced in text by year) that identified specific pf-shell matrix elements responsible for spherical-to-deformed transitions. The paper contradicts the expectation from IBM studies by Cejnar (unreferenced) and experimental observations by Meyer (unreferenced) that level density enhancement occurs at the phase transition point; instead, the enhancement is found in the deformed regime (k₁=1.0) and drops sharply at the transition. This enables future studies of how specific interaction components control collective behavior and level densities in larger shell-model spaces.
