# Connecting Relativistic Density Functional Theory to Microscopic Calculations

> The development of systematic effective field theories (EFTs) for nuclear forces and advances in solving the nuclear many-body problem have greatly improved our understanding of dense nuclear matter and the structure of finite nuclei. For global nuclear calculations, density functional theories (DFTs) have been developed to reduce the complexity and computational cost required in describing nuclear systems. However, DFT often makes approximations and assumptions about terms included in the functional, which may introduce systematic uncertainties compared to microscopic calculations using EFTs. In this work, we investigate possible avenues of improving nuclear DFT using nonlinear relativistic mean-field (RMF) theory. We explore the impact of RMF model extensions by fitting the nonlinear RMF model to predictions of nuclear matter and selected closed-shell nuclei using four successful chiral EFT Hamiltonians. We find that these model extensions are impactful and important in capturing the physics present within chiral Hamiltonians, particularly for charge radii and neutron skins of closed-shell nuclei. However, there are additional effects that are not captured within the RMF model, particularly within the isoscalar sector of RMF theory. Additional model extensions and the reliability of the nonlinear RMF model are discussed.

## Key claim

By fitting nonlinear relativistic mean-field (RMF) models to predictions from four chiral effective field theory (EFT) Hamiltonians for nuclear matter and closed-shell nuclei, the authors find that model extensions (including a delta meson and tensor couplings) are important for capturing chiral EFT physics in charge radii and neutron skins, but that additional effects in the isoscalar sector remain uncaptured by the RMF model.

## Method

The authors fit nonlinear RMF models, based on the FSUGold-like Lagrangian with scalar self-interactions and omega-rho cross-coupling, to pseudo-data from four chiral EFT Hamiltonians (1.8/2.0 (EM), DeltaNNLO_GO, 1.8/2.0 (EM7.5), and 1.8/2.0 (sim7.5)). They performed fits using the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm and Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling, targeting the energy per particle of symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter at densities 0.10-0.22 fm^-3, plus ground-state energies, charge radii, and neutron skins of closed-shell nuclei from O-16 to Pb-208 computed with the in-medium similarity renormalization group (IMSRG) and many-body perturbation theory (MBPT).

## Key numbers

*   The RMF model extensions (delta meson and tensor couplings) improved reproduction of charge radii for closed-shell nuclei with A > 56 when fit to the DeltaNNLO_GO Hamiltonian.
*   The RMF-EM(7.5) model showed considerably larger relative errors in energies and charge radii compared to fits to the DeltaNNLO_GO and 1.8/2.0 (EM) Hamiltonians, indicating difficulty capturing stronger short-range three-nucleon forces.
*   None of the RMF models fit to chiral EFT predictions lie within the 90% confidence ellipse for the PREX and CREX experimental results for neutron skins of Pb-208 and Ca-48.
*   The scalar meson mass for the RMF-GO model was 523.2300 +/- 1.8179 MeV; for RMF-EM it was 512.0006 +/- 0.0185 MeV; for RMF-EM(7.5) it was 502.1187 +/- 0.2811 MeV; and for RMF-sim(7.5) it was 534.5869 +/- 0.9979 MeV.
*   The omega-rho cross-coupling Lambda_v for RMF-GO was 0.0198 +/- 0.0007; for RMF-EM it was 0.0897 +/- 0.0436.

## Context

This work extends previous studies that compared density functionals with microscopic calculations mainly for neutron and nuclear matter (Goriely 2005, Typel 2018, Marino 2021, Alford 2022) by also fitting to finite-nuclei properties from chiral EFT. It builds on recent efforts to add terms to the Yukawa sector of the RMF Lagrangian (Salinas 2024, Kunjipurayil 2025) and addresses the known difficulty of reconciling PREX and CREX neutron-skin measurements within density functional theory (Reed 2023). The results enable future work to identify missing isoscalar physics in RMF models and suggest that including Fock terms could help resolve remaining discrepancies.
