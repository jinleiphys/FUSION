# Revisiting surface-integral formulations for one-nucleon transfers to bound and resonance states

> One-nucleon transfer reactions, in particular (d,p) reactions, have played a central role in nuclear structure studies for many decades. Present theoretical descriptions of the underlying reaction mechanisms are insufficient for addressing the challenges and opportunities that are opening up with new radioactive beam facilities. We investigate a theoretical approach that was proposed recently to address shortcomings in the description of transfers to resonance states. The method builds on ideas from the very successful R-matrix theory; in particular it uses a similar separation of the parameter space into interior and exterior regions, and introduces a parameterization that can be related to physical observables, which, in principle, makes it possible to extract meaningful spectroscopic information from experiments. We carry out calculations, for a selection of isotopes and energies, to test the usefulness of the new approach.

## Key claim

The paper tests a recently proposed surface-integral formalism for one-nucleon transfer reactions that recasts the reaction amplitude as a surface integral plus remnant interior and exterior terms, finding that while the surface term dominates for bound states at low energies and for resonances at certain radii, it does not fully reproduce the full cross section and shows significant dependence on the chosen separation radius, particularly at higher beam energies.

## Method

Finite-range DWBA calculations were carried out using a modified version of the reaction code Fresco for (d,p) reactions on targets 20O, 48Ca, and 90Zr, with the Reid Soft-Core potential for the deuteron, the Daehnick optical-model potential for deuteron-nucleus interactions, and the Koning-Delaroche potential for proton-nucleus interactions. The surface term was calculated as the difference between prior-interior and post-interior contributions, and the contributions of interior, surface, and exterior terms to the transfer cross section were isolated by setting specific terms to zero and comparing the resulting peak cross sections to the full calculation. For resonance states, bin functions (momentum-averaged continuum wave functions) were used to describe the overlap function.

## Key numbers

- For 90Zr(d,p)91Zr stripping to the ground state at Ed = 11 MeV, the cross-over from interior-dominated to exterior-dominated occurs at much smaller radii in the prior formulation (interior integration from 0 to 2 fm captures over 80% of peak cross section) than in the post formulation (interior integration from 0 to 7-8 fm yields less than 30% of peak cross section)
- For 90Zr(d,p)91Zr, the surface term for bound final states is dominant at radii around 6-8 fm, slightly larger than the rms radii of the overlap functions (4.94 fm for the 5/2+ ground state)
- For the f7/2 resonance in 91Zr at about 1 MeV above the neutron separation threshold of 7.195 MeV, the surface term is dominant at slightly larger radii (7-10 fm) even though the overlap function has an rms value of 11.7 fm
- For 48Ca(d,p)49Ca populating the ground state, the surface term dominates at a = 6.5 fm for Ed = 13 MeV and at a = 7.0 fm for Ed = 19.3 MeV, but shifts to a = 5.25 fm for Ed = 56 MeV where the surface term does not provide a good approximation to the full cross section
- The potential well for calculating one-neutron overlap functions in 91Zr had a radius of 5.53 fm and diffuseness of 0.66 fm; the rms radius of the overlap for the 5/2+ ground state was 4.94 fm
- For 48Ca, the potential VnA had a radius of 4.54 fm and diffuseness of 0.65 fm; the rms radius of the one-neutron overlap function was 4.62 fm

## Context

This work extends the formalism proposed by Mukhamedzhanov (2011) that applies R-matrix concepts (originally developed by Lane and Thomas (1958) for binary reactions) to one-nucleon transfer reactions, specifically addressing shortcomings in describing transfers to resonance states that have been problematic since early work by Huby (1965) and Vincent (1970). The study tests the surface-integral approach for targets across different mass regions (20O, 48Ca, 90Zr) and finds that while the method shows promise for low-energy transfers to bound states and resonances, the surface term alone does not fully reproduce the cross section, and its utility diminishes at higher beam energies. The results motivate further implementation within the Continuum-Discretized Coupled-Channels (CDCC) framework of Austern (1987), where the prior term is identically zero, and provide guidance for extracting spectroscopic information from experiments at radioactive beam facilities.
