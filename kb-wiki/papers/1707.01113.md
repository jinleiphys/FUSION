# Path Integral Monte Carlo study of particles obeying quantum mechanics and classical statistics

> Ultracold atomic systems have been of great research interest in the past, with more recent attention being paid to systems of mixed species. In this work we carry out non-perturbative Path Integral Monte Carlo (PIMC) simulations of N distinguishable particles at finite temperature, which can be thought of as an ultracold atomic system containing N distinct species. We use the PIMC approach to calculate thermodynamic properties of particles interacting via hard-sphere and hard-cavity potentials. The first problem we study is a two-particle system interacting via a hard-sphere and hard-cavity interaction in order to test the effectiveness of two approximations for the thermal density matrix corresponding to these potentials. We then apply the PIMC method to a system of many hard-sphere particles under periodic boundary conditions at varying temperature in order to calculate the energy per particle, pressure, and specific heat of the system. We examine how finite-size effects impact the results of PIMC simulations of hard-sphere particles and when the thermodynamic limit has been reached. Our results provide microscopic benchmarks for a system containing distinguishable particles, which can be thought of as a limiting case for ultracold atomic systems of mixed species.

## Key claim

The paper provides non-perturbative Path Integral Monte Carlo benchmarks for distinguishable particles (quantum boltzmannons) interacting via hard-sphere and hard-cavity potentials, which serve as a limiting case for ultracold atomic systems of mixed species where the number of distinct states equals the number of particles, making quantum statistics irrelevant even at low temperature.

## Method

The authors performed Path Integral Monte Carlo simulations of N distinguishable particles at finite temperature, using the Image Approximation (IA) and Cao-Berne (CB) two-body density matrices for hard-sphere and hard-cavity interactions. For the two-particle system, they compared PIMC results against analytic solutions of the Schrodinger equation with spherical Bessel functions. For the many-body system, they applied the CB density matrix to hard-sphere particles under periodic boundary conditions, calculating energy per particle, pressure, and specific heat while systematically increasing particle number to reach the thermodynamic limit.

## Key numbers

- Two-particle convergence study performed at temperatures T (hbar^2/m sigma^2 k_B)^{-1} = 0.5, 1.0, 1.6, 2.0, 2.5 with hard-cavity radius r_cav/sigma = 6
- Many-body simulations performed at density n sigma^3 = 0.2063, corresponding to Wigner-Seitz radius r_0 = (3/4 pi n)^{1/3} approximately 1.05 sigma
- Many-body simulations conducted at temperatures T (hbar^2/m sigma^2 k_B)^{-1} = 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0 with particle numbers N = 20, 60, 108, 200, 300, 400
- Thermodynamic limit for energy per particle and pressure was found to be in the N = 300-400 range at T (hbar^2/m sigma^2 k_B)^{-1} = 2.0
- For non-interacting gas at T (hbar^2/m sigma^2 k_B)^{-1} = 0.1 and n sigma^3 = 0.2063, E/N approached the equipartition value of 0.15 (hbar^2/m sigma^2) as N increased

## Context

This work extends the study of ultracold atomic mixtures, particularly the many-component gases of Yb and Sr isotopes studied experimentally by groups such as Cazalilla (2014), Taie (2010), and Pagano (2014). The authors provide microscopic benchmarks for the limiting case where each particle is in a distinct quantum state, which is analogous to the 1/N expansion in gauge theories as discussed by Appelquist (1981). The results enable future non-perturbative studies of strongly interacting many-component Fermi gases and can help disentangle the effects of interactions from those of quantum statistics in nuclear physics contexts, where neutrons and protons with two spin states each form a four-species problem.
