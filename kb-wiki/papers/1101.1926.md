# Analyzing the Power Spectrum of the Little Bangs

> In this talk we discuss the analogy between data from heavy-ion collisions and the Cosmic Microwave Background. We identify p_T correlations data as the heavy-ion analogy to the CMB and extract a power-spectrum from the heavy-ion data. We define the ratio of the final state power-spectrum to the initial coordinate-space eccentricity as the transfer-function. From the transfer-function we find that higher $n$ terms are suppressed and we argue that the suppression provides information on length scales like the mean-free-path. We make a rough estimate of the mean-free-path and find that it is larger than estimates based on the centrality dependence of v_2.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the power-spectrum extracted from \(p_T\) correlations in heavy-ion collisions is the heavy-ion analogue of the Cosmic Microwave Background power-spectrum, and that the suppression of higher harmonic modes in the transfer-function (the ratio of final-state power-spectrum to initial coordinate-space eccentricity) can be used to estimate the mean-free-path, though their rough estimate yields a value larger than estimates based on the centrality dependence of \(v_2\).

## Method

The authors took STAR data on \(p_T\) correlations versus relative azimuthal angle and relative rapidity, considered a slice at zero relative rapidity, and performed a Fourier decomposition to obtain the power-spectrum coefficients \(a_n\). They calculated the initial-state participant eccentricity \(\varepsilon_{n,part}^2\) for all harmonics \(n\) using a Monte Carlo Glauber model for central collisions (impact parameter \(b=0\) fm), including the intrinsic deformation of the Au nucleus. They then defined the transfer-function as the ratio \(a_n / \varepsilon_{n,part}^2\) and used the condition that conversion becomes inefficient when the mean-free-path exceeds the length scale \(2\pi\langle R\rangle/n\) to make a crude estimate of the mean-free-path.

## Key numbers

* The power-spectrum \(a_n\) was extracted from STAR \(p_T\) correlations data for the 5% most central Au+Au collisions.
* The participant eccentricity \(\varepsilon_{n,part}^2\) was calculated for central collisions at \(b=0\) fm using a Monte Carlo Glauber model.
* The average radial position of a participant was taken as \(\langle R\rangle = 3\) fm.
* The harmonic beyond which conversion is inefficient was taken as \(n = 6\), yielding a mean-free-path estimate of \(l_{mfp} = 3\) fm.
* The condition for efficient conversion is \(l_{mfp} < 2\pi\langle R\rangle/n\).

## Context

This work extends the analogy between heavy-ion collisions and Big Bang cosmology first explored by Mishra et al., who suggested measuring RMS values of Fourier coefficients \(v_n\) as the power-spectrum, and by Sorensen, who connected higher Fourier components to correlation structures like the ridge and mach cones. The authors introduce the transfer-function as a new tool to extract length scales such as the mean-free-path, and note that their crude estimate (3 fm) is larger than estimates based on the centrality dependence of \(v_2\) from Knudsen fits or from the longitudinal width of \(p_T\) correlations by Gavin and Aziz. This approach challenges models of heavy-ion collisions to correctly describe the conversion of coordinate-space anisotropies at all scales into momentum-space anisotropies.
