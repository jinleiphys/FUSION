# Die kontrollierte Kettenreaktion (The controlled nuclear chain reaction)

## Key claim

The paper presents a compact theoretical description of controlled nuclear chain reactions, arguing that the presence of delayed neutrons (with an average delay of about 13 seconds) makes the chain reaction in a reactor controllable, in contrast to the uncontrolled chain reaction in a nuclear weapon where all fissions are induced by prompt neutrons within about 10⁻⁶ seconds. The point-model description is justified because a local disturbance of neutron density spreads across a reactor of a few meters in size within fractions of a second.

## Method

The author develops the point-model kinetics of a nuclear reactor, starting from the stationary case where neutron production rate P is proportional to the number of thermal neutrons n, defining the generation time Λ = 1/𝒫 and the destruction probability 𝒟 = 1/τ. The reactivity ρ = (𝒫 − 𝒟)/𝒫 is introduced, and the simplified kinetic equations (point reactor equations) are derived for the neutron number n and the delayed neutron precursor concentration C, using a one-group delayed-neutron approximation with average precursor lifetime l = 13 s and delayed neutron fraction β = 0.64% for ²³⁵U. The secular equation for the reciprocal reactor periods ω is solved, yielding the stable reactor period for the delayed supercritical regime 0 < ρ < β.

## Key numbers

- Generation time in commercial thermal reactors: Λ ≈ 10⁻⁵ s to 10⁻⁴ s
- In a 3 GW thermal reactor: approximately 10²⁰ fissions per second, with about 196 MeV (3.14 × 10⁻¹¹ J) released per fission
- Neutron density in a power reactor: about 10⁸ neutrons per cubic centimeter
- Delayed neutron fraction for pure ²³⁵U: β = 0.64% (with modern values favoring β = (0.665 ± 0.021)%)
- Average precursor lifetime: l = 13 s, corresponding to decay constant λ ≈ 0.0773 s⁻¹
- In a stationary reactor, precursor number exceeds neutron number by a factor of about 10³ (C ≈ 10³ n)
- Prompt time constant for 0 ≤ ρ < 0.9β: tₑ,ₚᵣ = Λ/(β − ρ) ≈ 0.01 to 0.1 s
- For a typical light-water reactor over one operating cycle, β decreases from about 0.6% (beginning of cycle) to about 0.5% (end of cycle)
- In the CROCUS research reactor with weakly enriched uranium, effective β can reach 0.78%
- Plutonium-239 has β = 0.21%
- The longest-lived precursor ⁸⁷Br has a lifetime of 80.387 seconds (half-life 55.72 s)

## Context

This lecture note/tutorial extends the classic treatment of reactor kinetics based on the Fermi cycle (neutron cycle) concept and the point reactor model, referencing the original 1939 observation of delayed neutrons by Roberts and the standard delayed neutron data from Keepin. The work enables quantitative understanding of reactor startup from the subcritical source range (where n ∝ −1/ρ), the approach to criticality via critical experiments, and the safe operation of commercial light-water reactors (pressurized and boiling water reactors) where reactivity is controlled by control rods and, in PWRs, by boric acid addition to the coolant. The paper notes that in fast breeder reactors the generation time is significantly shorter (about 10⁻⁶ to 10⁻⁷ s) than in thermal reactors.
