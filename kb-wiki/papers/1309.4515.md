# Dihadron Correlations Relative to the Event Plane in 200 GeV Au+Au Collisions from STAR

> Dihadron correlations with a high-pt trigger particle are analyzed by STAR relative to the event plane in Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV (arXiv:1010.0690v1). The elliptic and quadrangular flow anisotropies are subtracted. The remaining dihadron correlation signals are found to be composed of a near-side peak, which is separated into jet-like and "ridge"-like components, and an away-side correlation structure. The ridge-like structure is found to decrease with the trigger particle azimuthal angle relative to the event plane from in-plane to out-of-plane. The away-side structure is found to evolve from single-peak for in-plane triggers to double-peak for out-of-plane triggers. Is the dihadron correlation signal just a manifestation of the triangular and higher-order harmonic flows? This talk addresses this question, exploring the answers using limiting-case assumptions, and discusses the implications of the data regarding the ridge and the double-peak structure.

# Dihadron Correlations Relative to the Event Plane in 200 GeV Au+Au Collisions from STAR

## Key claim

The observed near-side ridge and away-side double-peak structures in dihadron correlations at large pseudorapidity separation (|Δη| > 0.7) are not entirely explained by triangular flow (v₃) or higher-order harmonic flow backgrounds, but the ridge could be fully accounted for if the elliptic flow (v₂) depends on the trigger particle azimuthal angle relative to the event plane; however, this does not determine whether the ridge originates from flow or nonflow mechanisms.

## Method

Dihadron correlations with a high-pT trigger particle (3 < pT(t) < 4 GeV/c) and associated particles (1 < pT(a) < 2 GeV/c) were measured in minimum-bias 20-60% centrality Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV, with the trigger particle azimuthal angle relative to the event plane (φs) divided into six slices. Elliptic (v₂), quadrangular (v₄), and triangular (v₃) flow backgrounds were subtracted using two-particle cumulant methods with η-gaps of 0.7, and the systematic uncertainties were evaluated by comparing different v₂ estimation methods including four-particle cumulants and two-particle cumulants averaged over the full 2π range. The analysis also examined the effect of φs-dependent v₂ by measuring two-particle cumulants in events selected by trigger particle orientation.

## Key numbers

- Trigger particle pT range: 3 < pT(t) < 4 GeV/c; associated particle pT range: 1 < pT(a) < 2 GeV/c
- Pseudorapidity separation cut: |Δη| > 0.7
- Centrality selection: minimum-bias 20-60% Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV
- The quadrangular flow background parameterization used: v₄{ψ₂} = 1.15 v₂²
- The v₃ measurement used a reference particle pT range of 0.15 < pT < 2 GeV/c with η-gap = 0.7
- The φs-dependent v₂ of associated particles (1.5 < pT(a) < 2 GeV/c) was found to decrease with increasing φs, with the largest value at φs ≈ 0° and smallest at φs ≈ 90°
- The φs-dependent v₄ was smallest at φs = 45° and largest at φs = 0° and 90°
- The v₃ was found to be independent of φs

## Context

This work extends the STAR analysis of dihadron correlations relative to the event plane (arXiv:1010.0690v1) and addresses the question raised by Mishra (2008) and Alver (2010) about whether triangular flow from initial-state geometry fluctuations can explain the observed ridge and double-peak structures. The results are compared to the NeXSPheRIO model calculations by Takahashi, Hama, and Andrade (2009), which suggested that initial energy density fluctuations with subsequent hydrodynamic evolution could generate both a near-side ridge and away-side double-peak correlations. The analysis also responds to the argument by Luzum (2011) that the correlation data at |Δη| > 0.7 can be fitted by Fourier harmonics up to 4th order. The robust persistence of the away-side double-peak structure for out-of-plane triggers, even after extensive flow subtraction, enables further investigation of path-length-dependent jet-medium interactions in the strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma (sQGP).
