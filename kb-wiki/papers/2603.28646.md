# Neural Quantum States in Non-Stabilizer Regimes: Benchmarks with Atomic Nuclei

> As neural networks are known to efficiently represent classes of tensor-network states as well as volume-law-entangled states, identifying which properties determine the representational capabilities of neural quantum states (NQS) remains an open question. We construct NQS representations of ground states of medium-mass atomic nuclei, which typically exhibit significant entanglement and non-stabilizerness, to study their performance in relation to the quantum complexity of the target state. Leveraging a second-quantized formulation of NQS tailored for nuclear-physics applications, we perform calculations in active orbital spaces using a restricted Boltzmann machine (RBM), a prototypical NQS ansatz. For a fixed number of configurations, we find that states with larger non-stabilizerness are systematically harder to learn, as evidenced by reduced accuracy. This finding suggests that non-stabilizerness is a primary factor governing the compression and representational efficiency of RBMs in entangled regimes, and motivates extending these studies to more sophisticated network architectures.

# Neural Quantum States in Non-Stabilizer Regimes: Benchmarks with Atomic Nuclei

## Key claim
For a fixed number of configurations in the restricted Boltzmann machine (RBM) ansatz, nuclear ground states with larger non-stabilizerness (quantum magic) are systematically harder to learn, as evidenced by reduced fidelity and larger energy errors, suggesting that non-stabilizerness is a primary factor governing representational efficiency of RBMs in entangled regimes rather than entanglement alone.

## Method
The authors construct neural quantum state (NQS) representations of ground states of medium-mass atomic nuclei in the sd-shell using a restricted Boltzmann machine (RBM) defined over occupation-number configurations in a second-quantized formulation. They perform calculations within an active orbital space using the USDB nucleon-nucleon interaction, comparing NQS results to exact interacting shell model (ISM) diagonalization. Network parameters are optimized either by direct fidelity maximization against the exact ground state or by variational Monte Carlo (VMC) energy minimization using the stochastic reconfiguration (RMSProp) algorithm, with the hidden-unit density α varied from 1 to 8.

## Key numbers
- For α=1 (Nθ = 1248 parameters), the NQS reproduces nuclear ground states with fidelities >99.6% for Hilbert spaces up to this many configurations; above this threshold fidelity degrades.
- With α=8, sd-shell nuclear wave functions are reproduced within less than 13% error in the worst cases, while relative errors on energies are less than 3.5%.
- For 28Si (the largest sd-shell nucleus), a network with α=8 has a number of parameters only about 10% of the total number of configurations.
- Energy minimization with VMC reproduces energies within 5% accuracy for α=2 and within 3% accuracy for α=8.
- 24Mg, with 28,503 configurations, exhibits the largest non-stabilizerness and is represented with the lowest fidelity among the nuclei studied.
- Even-even nuclei typically possess excitation gaps of 1.5–2 MeV, while problematic odd-A nuclei like 19F, 21Ne, 23Na, and 32P have excitation gaps between 77 and 399 keV.

## Context
This work extends prior studies of non-stabilizerness in nuclear systems by Brokemeier (2024) and investigations of NQS in the Heisenberg model by Sinibaldi (2025), applying second-quantization NQS methods originally developed in quantum chemistry and condensed-matter physics by Choo (2019) to atomic nuclei for the first time. The findings establish a direct connection between representational complexity of neural networks and intrinsic quantum complexity in nuclear wave functions, and motivate extending these studies to more sophisticated architectures such as Vision Transformers (Viteritti, 2022) and exploring basis-optimization techniques (Robin, 2023) to reduce state complexity.
