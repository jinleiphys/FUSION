# Thermodynamical description of hadron-quark phase transition and its implications on compact-star phenomena

> One of the most promising possibilities may be the appearance of quark matter in astrophysical phenomena in the light of recent progress in observations. The mechanism of deconfinement is not well understood, but the thermodynamical aspects of the hadron-quark (HQ) phase transition have been extensively studied in recent years. Then the mixed phase of hadron and quark matter becomes important; the proper treatment is needed to describe the HQ phase transition and derive the equation of state (EOS) for the HQ matter, based on the Gibbs conditions for phase equilibrium. We here adopt a EOS based on the baryon-baryon interactions including hyperons for the hadron phase, while we use rather simple EOS within the MIT bag model in the quark phase. For quark matter we further try to improve the previous EOS by considering other effective models of QCD. One of the interesting consequences may be the appearance of the inhomogeneous structures called "pasta", which are brought about by the surface and the Coulomb interaction effects. We present here a comprehensive review of our recent works about the HQ phase transition in various astrophysical situations: cold catalyzed matter, hot matter and neutrino-trapped matter. We show how the pasta structure becomes unstable by the charge screening of the Coulomb interaction, thermal effect or the neutrino trapping effect. Such inhomogeneous structure may affect astrophysical phenomena through its elasticity or thermal properties. Here we also discuss some implications on supernova explosion, gravitational wave and cooling of compact stars.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the authors' own work on the hadron-quark phase transition under the assumption that the transition is of first order (as suggested by many model studies), and shows that the resulting "pasta" structures in the mixed phase become unstable due to charge screening, thermal effects, or neutrino trapping, which may affect astrophysical phenomena.

## Method

The authors adopt a nonrelativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach with the Argonne V18 nucleon-nucleon potential, Urbana UIX three-body forces, and Nijmegen NSC89 hyperon-nucleon potentials for the hadron phase, while using the MIT bag model (with a bag constant of 100 MeV·fm⁻³ or a density-dependent bag model) for the quark phase. They impose Gibbs conditions for phase equilibrium (including baryon number, charge, and lepton number conservation) and incorporate finite-size effects by solving the Poisson equation and using a surface tension parameter within the Wigner-Seitz approximation to describe inhomogeneous "pasta" structures.

## Key numbers

- Bag constant set to 100 MeV·fm⁻³ for the standard MIT bag model; density-dependent bag model uses B∞ = 50 MeV·fm⁻³, B0 = 400 MeV·fm⁻³, and β = 0.17 with saturation density n0 = 0.17 fm⁻³.
- Surface tension range considered: σ ≈ 10–100 MeV·fm⁻², based on estimates from the MIT bag model for strangelets and lattice gauge simulations at finite temperature.
- Beyond a limiting surface tension of σ ≈ 65 MeV·fm⁻², the pasta structures become mechanically unstable.
- Example density profile shown at baryon density nB = 0.4 fm⁻³, with Wigner-Seitz cell radius RW = 26.7 fm and droplet radius R = 17.3 fm at zero temperature.
- Free energy per baryon compared at temperatures T = 0, 10, 30, 50 MeV for hadron and quark matter with and without neutrino trapping (lepton fraction YL = 0.4).

## Context

This review extends the earlier work of Glendenning (1992) on the Gibbs conditions for multi-component systems in neutron-star matter, and the studies of Heiselberg et al. (1993) and Voskresensky et al. on finite-size effects in the hadron-quark mixed phase. It builds on the explicit calculations of Endo et al. (2005) for pasta structures and the authors' own prior work on kaon condensation and hyperon matter (Maruyama et al., 2006; Yasutake et al., 2009, 2012). The results enable further studies of supernova explosions, gravitational wave emission, and compact-star cooling, with the quark model uncertainty identified as the main ambiguity to be constrained by astrophysical observations.
