# Dissipative dynamics in quasi-fission

> Quasi-fission is the primary reaction mechanism that prevents the formation of superheavy elements in heavy-ion fusion experiments. Employing the time-dependent density functional theory approach we study quasi-fission in the systems $^{40,48}$Ca+$^{238}$U. Results show that for $^{48}$Ca projectiles the quasi-fission is substantially reduced in comparison to the $^{40}$Ca case. This partly explains the success of superheavy element formation with $^{48}$Ca beams. For the first time, we also calculate the repartition of excitation energies of the two fragments in a dynamic microscopic theory. The system is found in quasi-thermal equilibrium only for reactions with $^{40}$Ca. The differences between both systems are interpreted in terms of initial neutron to proton asymmetry of the colliding partners.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the reduced quasi-fission observed with neutron-rich $^{48}$Ca projectiles compared to $^{40}$Ca is due to the longer survival of $^{48}$Ca's magicity during the collision, which reduces dissipation; this claim is hedged by noting that the system formed in $^{48}$Ca+$^{238}$U is out of thermal equilibrium, whereas the $^{40}$Ca+$^{238}$U system reaches quasi-thermal equilibrium.

## Method

The authors performed time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations on a 3D Cartesian grid with no symmetry restrictions using the Skyrme SLy4d energy density functional, including all relevant time-odd terms. They simulated central and near-central collisions of $^{40,48}$Ca+$^{238}$U at various center-of-mass energies, with the $^{238}$U symmetry axis initially oriented at $90^{\circ}$ to the internuclear axis (side collisions). Contact times, fragment masses and charges, total kinetic energy (TKE), and fragment excitation energies were extracted from the TDHF time evolution using a density constraint method.

## Key numbers

- Contact time for $^{40}$Ca+$^{238}$U at $E_{\mathrm{c.m.}}=209$ MeV and $L=20$: $\Delta t = 9.35$ zs, with 66 nucleons transferred to the light fragment.
- Maximum contact time for $^{40}$Ca+$^{238}$U central collisions: 32 zs at c.m. energy $\sim10\%$ above the barrier ($E_{\mathrm{c.m.}}/V_B \approx 1.10$).
- Frozen Hartree-Fock barriers: $V_B=199.13$ MeV for $^{40}$Ca+$^{238}$U and $V_B=196.14$ MeV for $^{48}$Ca+$^{238}$U.
- For $^{40}$Ca+$^{238}$U at 5-10% above the barrier: heavy fragment excitation energy $E^{*(TDHF)}_H\simeq60$ MeV, light fragment excitation energy $E^{*(TDHF)}_L\simeq40$ MeV; statistical equilibrium prediction gives $E^{*(eq)}_H\simeq64$ MeV and $E^{*(eq)}_L\simeq36$ MeV.
- For $^{48}$Ca+$^{238}$U, the quasi-fission region is confined to a narrow energy window $E_{\mathrm{c.m.}}/V_B\simeq1.03-1.04$; above these energies, contact times exceed 35 zs.
- TDHF predictions of TKE for $^{40}$Ca+$^{238}$U central collisions at $E_{c.m.}/V_B=1.0-1.1$ are in excellent agreement with the Viola systematics.

## Context

This work extends TDHF studies of heavy-ion collisions to quasi-fission in superheavy element formation, building on prior TDHF calculations of capture cross-sections for $^{48}$Ca+$^{238}$U by Umar (2010) and earlier quasi-fission studies using statistical and transport models by Adamian (2003), Zagrebaev (2007), and Aritomo (2009). The results explain the experimental success of hot-fusion synthesis of superheavy elements with $^{48}$Ca beams (Oganessian 2010, 2012) and provide the first microscopic calculation of fragment excitation energy repartition in quasi-fission, enabling future studies of dissipation mechanisms and thermal equilibrium in heavy-ion collisions.
