# Extension of the ratio method to low energy

> Background: The ratio method has been proposed as a means to remove the reaction model dependence in the study of halo nuclei. Purpose: Originally, it was developed for higher energies but given the potential interest in applying the method at lower energy, in this work we explore its validity at 20 MeV/nucleon. Method: The ratio method takes the ratio of the breakup angular distribution and the summed angular distribution (which includes elastic, inelastic and breakup) and uses this observable to constrain the features of the original halo wave function. In this work we use the Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel method and the Coulomb-corrected Dynamical Eikonal Approximation for the study. Results: We study the reactions of 11Be on 12C, 40Ca and 208Pb at 20 MeV/nucleon. We compare the various theoretical descriptions and explore the dependence of our result on the core-target interaction. Conclusions: Our study demonstrates that the ratio method is valid at these lower beam energies.

## Key claim

The ratio method, previously validated at higher beam energies (around 70 MeV/nucleon), is shown to be valid at 20 MeV/nucleon for light targets such as carbon, but the adiabatic approximation underlying the method breaks down for heavy targets like lead at this lower energy, making the method best suited for light targets.

## Method

The authors performed Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (CDCC) calculations for the carbon target and Coulomb-corrected Dynamical Eikonal Approximation (CC-DEA) calculations for carbon, calcium, and lead targets, modeling the collision of a one-neutron halo nucleus (11Be) on each target at 20 MeV/nucleon. They computed the ratio of the breakup angular distribution to the summed angular distribution (including elastic, inelastic, and breakup contributions) and compared this ratio to the prediction from the Recoil Excitation and Breakup (REB) model, which expresses the ratio as a form factor depending only on the projectile structure.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy: 20 MeV/nucleon for all reactions studied.
- Breakup cross section for 11Be on carbon at 20 MeV/nucleon: practical measurements likely possible up to 40 degrees in center-of-mass scattering angle.
- For the carbon target, CDCC calculations included partial waves up to l_max = 6, Q_max = 6 multipoles, integration up to R_max = 60 fm, matching at R_asym = 1000 fm, and total angular momentum up to J_max = 20000.
- For CC-DEA calculations at 20 MeV/nucleon: carbon target required N_theta x N_phi = 16 x 31 points on the unit sphere; calcium target required 14 x 27; lead target required 12 x 23.
- CC-DEA radial grid: 600 points extending to 600 fm for carbon and calcium, 800 points extending to 800 fm for lead.
- Impact parameter range: 0-100 fm for carbon, 0-160 fm for calcium and lead, with extrapolation to b_max = 200 fm (carbon) or 300 fm (calcium and lead).
- Breakup considered to a final core-neutron scattering state of E = 125 keV.

## Context

This work extends the ratio method, originally developed for reactions at around 70 MeV/nucleon by Capel et al. (2011, 2013), to lower beam energies suitable for facilities such as SPIRAL 2 and FRIB. The study uses CDCC, which has been benchmarked against exact Faddeev calculations for deuteron collisions by Upadhyay et al. (2012), and the Coulomb-corrected DEA developed by Fukui et al. (2014). The results enable experimental application of the ratio method at lower-energy facilities and suggest that even simple dynamical descriptions may provide accurate ratios due to the method's insensitivity to the reaction mechanism.
