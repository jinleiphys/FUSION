# Neutrino-induced neutron spallation and the site of the r-process

> All of the actinides and roughly half the natural abundance of elements with mass number A > 70 come from the rapid neutron capture process, or the r-process. If the r-process, as suggested by many, occurs deep in a supernova, then it is under the influence of an intense neutrino flux. Here we discuss the effects of both charged-current and neutral-current neutrino interactions on the r-process. We show that the multiple-neutron emission induced by both kinds of neutrino interactions can affect the observed r-process abundance pattern significantly. In particular, we find that five nuclei below the r-process abundance peak at A = 195 may be entirely attributed to the neutrino-induced neutron spallation processes following the r-process freeze-out. Furthermore, the deduced neutrino fluence agrees with the conditions in the recent supernova r-process model. These results strongly argue that the r-process occurs in supernovae. They also provide a sensitive probe for the conditions at the supernova r-process site.

# Neutrino-induced neutron spallation and the site of the r-process

## Key claim
The deficiency of standard r-process calculations in the mass region A=183–187 can be completely remedied by neutrino-induced neutron spallation following r-process freeze-out, provided the neutrino fluence is approximately 0.015 × 10⁴⁷ erg km⁻². This fluence is consistent with conditions in the recent supernova r-process model, but the claim depends on the specific postprocessing neutron emission probabilities derived from both charged-current and neutral-current neutrino interactions.

## Method
The author calculated the effects of charged-current νₑ capture and inelastic neutral-current scattering of νₘ(τ) and ν̄ₘ(τ) on neutron-rich progenitor nuclei near A∼195 produced during the r-process. Neutron emission probabilities after each neutrino interaction were estimated using statistical techniques, with average neutron multiplicities of ∼4 for charged-current and ∼2 for neutral-current interactions. The postprocessing neutron emission probabilities Pₙ were then computed from Poisson distributions of these events, and the final abundance Y_f(A) was obtained by summing progenitor abundances Y_pro(A+n) weighted by Pₙ for a given neutrino fluence ℱ.

## Key numbers
- Average neutrino energies: ⟨E_νₑ⟩ ≈ 11 MeV, ⟨E_ν̄ₑ⟩ ≈ 16 MeV, ⟨E_νₘ(τ)⟩ = ⟨E_ν̄ₘ(τ)⟩ ≈ 25 MeV
- Charged-current νₑ capture rate: ∼8 s⁻¹ at r=100 km from the neutron star for L_ν=10⁵¹ erg s⁻¹ per species
- Total neutral-current scattering rate: ∼12 s⁻¹ at r=100 km for L_ν=10⁵¹ erg s⁻¹ per species
- Average neutron emission: ∼4 neutrons per νₑ capture reaction; ∼2 neutrons per inelastic neutral-current scattering
- Best-fit neutrino fluence: ℱ = 0.015 (in units of 10⁴⁷ erg km⁻²)
- Observed abundances at A=183–187 reproduced within 1σ at this fluence
- Typical neutrino luminosity decay timescale: ∼3 s; r-process dynamic timescale: ∼0.1–1 s
- r-process freeze-out radius: ∼300–1000 km in the supernova model of Woosley et al.

## Context
This work extends standard r-process calculations (Friedel 1995) that greatly underproduce nuclei at A∼182–187 due to the progenitor abundance pattern lacking corresponding nuclei. It contrasts with the approach of Chen (1995), who suggested quenching the strength of the N=82 closed neutron shell to eliminate similar deficiencies below the A∼130 peak. The results provide a sensitive probe for supernova r-process site conditions, as the freeze-out radius and dynamic timescale are now constrained by the postprocessing neutrino fluence. The paper enables future work to test whether neutrino postprocessing can resolve similar deficiencies in other mass regions and to refine the inferred supernova conditions.
