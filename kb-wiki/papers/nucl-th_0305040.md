# Weak Interactions in Atoms and Nuclei: The Standard Model and Beyond

> Studies in nuclear and atomic physics have played an important role in developing our understanding of the Standard Model of electroweak interactions. We review the basic ingredients of the Standard Model, and discuss some key nuclear and atomic physics experiments used in testing these ideas. We also summarize the conceptual issues of the Standard Model that motivate the search for new physics.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that nuclear and atomic physics experiments have been and continue to be essential for testing the Standard Model of electroweak interactions and for motivating searches for physics beyond the Standard Model, though the authors note that the Standard Model remains incomplete and contains 17 independent parameters that must be taken from experiment.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical structure of the electroweak sector of the Standard Model, beginning with the SU(3)_C × SU(2)_L × U(1)_Y gauge group and the Higgs mechanism for spontaneous symmetry breaking. It then surveys key low-energy tests of the Standard Model, including nuclear beta-decay measurements used to extract the CKM matrix element V_ud, and discusses how atomic parity violation experiments probe the weak mixing angle and weak charges of quarks and leptons.

## Key numbers

- The weak scale v is approximately 242 GeV, derived from the relation v^2 = M_W^2(M_Z^2 - M_W^2)/(π α M_Z^2)
- The ratio M_W/M_Z equals cos θ_W, where θ_W is the Weinberg angle (weak mixing angle)
- Weak interaction amplitudes at low energies are suppressed relative to electromagnetic amplitudes by a factor of q^2/M_W^2, where M_W is the W boson mass
- The electroweak sector contains 17 independent parameters: 3 from the gauge sector (g, M_Z, e), 1 from the Higgs sector (M_H), and 13 from the fermion sector (9 lepton and quark masses plus 4 CKM angles and phase)

## Context

This review extends the pedagogical tradition of explaining the Standard Model's electroweak sector through the lens of nuclear and atomic physics, building on the foundational work of Glashow, Weinberg, and Salam (1960s-1970s) and the Higgs mechanism developed by Higgs, Englert, and Brout (1964). The paper enables future work by identifying the precise nuclear and atomic experiments, such as nuclear beta-decay for V_ud extraction and atomic parity violation measurements, that provide the most stringent tests of the Standard Model and the most sensitive probes for new physics beyond it.
