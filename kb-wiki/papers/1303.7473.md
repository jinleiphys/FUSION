# On the Scission Point Configuration of Fisioning Nuclei

> The scission of a nucleus into two fragments is at present the least understood part of the fission process, though the most important for the formation of the observables. To investigate the potential energy landscape at the largest possible deformations, i.e. at the scission point (line, hypersurface), the Strutinsky's optimal shape approach is applied. For the accurate description of the mass-asymmetric nuclear shape at the scission point, it turned out necessary to construct an interpolation between the two sets of constraints for the elongation and mass asymmetry which are applied successfully at small deformations (quadrupole and octupole moments) and for separated fragments (the distance between the centers of mass and the difference of fragments masses). In addition, a constraint on the neck radius was added, what makes it possible to introduce the so called super-short and super-long shapes at the scission point and to consider the contributions to the observable data from different fission modes. The calculated results for the mass distribution of the fission fragment and the Coulomb repulsion energy "immediately after scission" are in a reasonable agreement with experimental data.

## Key claim

The scission point configuration of fissioning nuclei can be described by an interpolation between constraints on quadrupole and octupole moments (suitable at small deformations) and constraints on the distance between centers of mass and mass difference (suitable for separated fragments), with the addition of a neck-radius constraint that enables the introduction of super-short and super-long scission shapes; the calculated mass distributions and Coulomb repulsion energies show only qualitative agreement with experimental data, indicating that full dynamical calculations with shell effects are required for a more precise description.

## Method

The author applies Strutinsky's optimal shape approach, solving the Euler-Lagrange equation for the profile function ρ(z) of an axially symmetric nucleus by minimizing the liquid-drop energy under constraints on volume, elongation, and mass asymmetry. A smoothed Sign-function constraint is introduced as an interpolation between the quadrupole/octupole moment constraints and the distance/mass-difference constraints, with a smoothing width Δz treated as an additional parameter. The neck radius is further incorporated as an independent degree of freedom by adding a constraint on the amount of matter in the neck region, allowing the generation of super-short and super-long scission shapes.

## Key numbers

- The neck radius at the critical deformation (scission point) is approximately equal to (0.25 – 0.30)R₀ for fissility parameters in the range 0.4 ≤ x_LD ≤ 0.9.
- The scission point energy calculated with the R₁₂, δ constraints is (2–5) MeV lower than that calculated with the Q₂, Q₃ constraints.
- The energies of shapes calculated with Δz = 2.0 R₀ are (1–3) MeV lower compared to those calculated with Q₂, Q₃ constraints.
- The nuclear radius parameter used is r₀ = 1.225 fm.
- The collective temperature T_coll used in the scission-point model yield calculation is 1 MeV.
- The octupole deformation contribution to the deformation energy at the touching point of two ¹¹⁸Pd nuclei is of the order 0.5 MeV.
- The peaks of the mass distribution for ²³⁶U are located at fragment masses 95 and 141.

## Context

This work extends the Strutinsky optimal shape approach (Strutinsky, 1960s) from small deformations to the scission point, addressing the long-standing difficulty of describing mass-asymmetric shapes at the largest deformations. The interpolation between Q₂/Q₃ constraints and R₁₂/δ constraints resolves the unphysical discontinuities and unrealistic tip shapes that arise from using either set alone. The introduction of the neck degree of freedom enables the description of multiple fission modes (super-short, standard, super-long) as proposed by Brosa (1990) and observed experimentally by Hübsch (1990) and others. The calculated mass distribution for ²³⁶U agrees with the well-known experimental results of Huizenga (1960s), and the Coulomb repulsion energies show qualitative agreement with the total kinetic energy data of Müller (1970s), Baba (1970s), and Zeyen (1980s). This work enables future dynamical calculations that include shell effects and the full multimodal character of fission.
