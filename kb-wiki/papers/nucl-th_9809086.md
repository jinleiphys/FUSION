# On the Origin of Nuclear Superfluidity

> The induced pairing interaction arising from the exchange of collective surface vibrations among nucleons moving in time reversal states close to the Fermi energy is found to lead to values of the pairing gap which are similar to those experimentally observed.

## Key claim

The induced pairing interaction arising from the exchange of low-lying collective surface vibrations between nucleons in time-reversal states near the Fermi energy produces pairing gaps that account for a large fraction of the empirical values, but not all of them; the calculated gaps are systematically somewhat smaller than the experimental values (e.g., ~1 MeV calculated vs 1.5 MeV empirical for ¹²⁰Sn).

## Method

The authors calculated the induced pairing interaction using random-phase approximation and the particle-vibration coupling model, including vibrational states of multipolarities λ^π = 2⁺, 3⁻, 4⁺, and 5⁻. They solved the state-dependent gap equation (Eq. 2) using the induced interaction matrix elements (Eq. 1) for neutrons and protons in isotopic chains of Ca, Ti, and Sn, and also for the proton-neutron case in ⁴²Sc. The particle-vibration coupling strengths were fixed by experimental transition probabilities of low-lying 2⁺ and 3⁻ vibrational states.

## Key numbers

- For ¹²⁰Sn, the calculated state-dependent pairing gap near the Fermi energy is ~1 MeV, compared to the empirical value of 1.5 MeV from binding-energy differences (Eq. 3).
- For ⁴²Sc, the calculated proton-neutron pairing gap is 1.5 MeV, essentially coinciding with the empirical value of 1.6 MeV.
- The calculations were performed for isotopic chains of ²⁰Ca, ²²Ti, and ⁵⁰Sn, and for ⁴²₂₁Sc.
- The induced interaction includes vibrational states of multipolarities λ^π = 2⁺, 3⁻, 4⁺, and 5⁻.

## Context

This work extends the analogy between nuclear pairing and superconductivity (Bohr, 1969; Bohr and Mottelson, 1975) by showing that the exchange of collective surface vibrations, analogous to phonon exchange in superconductors, can account for a substantial part of nuclear pairing gaps. The results challenge the standard description of nuclear pairing in terms of frequency-independent effective interactions and suggest that the induced interaction mechanism should produce significant proton-neutron pairing correlations. The findings also have implications for understanding the quenching of the pairing gap with increasing angular momentum and temperature.
