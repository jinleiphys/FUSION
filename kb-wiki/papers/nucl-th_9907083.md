# Multiple Scattering Theory for Slow Neutrons (from thermal to ultracold)

> The general theory of neutron scattering is presented, valid for the whole domain of slow neutrons from thermal to ultracold. Particular attention is given to multiple scattering which is the dominant process for ultracold neutrons (UCN). For thermal and cold neutrons, when the multiple scattering in the target can be neglected, the cross section is reduced to the known value. A new expression for inelastic scattering cross section for UCN is proposed. Dynamical processes in the target are taken into account and their influence on inelastic scattering of UCN is analyzed.

## Key claim

The paper presents a general theory of neutron scattering valid for the entire slow-neutron domain from thermal to ultracold, claiming that the standard Born approximation and Fermi pseudopotential approach fail for ultracold neutrons (UCN) because multiple scattering becomes dominant, and that a consistent multiple scattering theory (MST) framework can be reduced to numerically solvable linear equations for neutron amplitudes at nuclear surfaces, provided the short-range and low-energy conditions allow s-wave scattering length approximation.

## Method

The authors start from the exact N+1-body Schrödinger equation for neutron-target interaction and reformulate it into MST equations involving t-matrices and Green functions. They exploit two small parameters, the short range of neutron-nucleus interaction compared to interatomic distances and wavelengths, and the small neutron energy compared to the interaction potential depth, to reduce the many-body problem to a set of linear equations for the neutron wave amplitudes \(\phi^j_\nu\) at the surface of each nucleus, expressed in terms of renormalized scattering lengths and correlation functions of the target nuclei positions. The scattering amplitudes and cross sections are then derived from these amplitudes via asymptotic wave function analysis.

## Key numbers

- Thermal and cold neutron wavelengths: \(0.03\ \text{nm} \leq \lambda \leq 1\ \text{nm}\) (from Introduction).
- Condition for neglecting rescattering: \(n b \lambda^2 \ll 1\), i.e., \(\lambda \ll 100\ \text{nm}\), where \(n \sim 10^{22}\ \text{cm}^{-3}\) is nuclear density and \(b \sim 10^{-12}\ \text{cm}\) is scattering amplitude (from Introduction).
- UCN wavelength: \(\lambda \geq 10\ \text{nm}\) (from Introduction).
- UCN energy: \(\sim 10^{-7}\ \text{eV}\), corresponding to temperature \(\sim 10^{-3}\ \text{K}\) (from Introduction).
- UCN intrusion length (mean free path analog): \(\sim 10\ \text{nm}\) (from Introduction).

## Context

This work extends the standard theory of thermal and cold neutron scattering based on the Fermi pseudopotential and Born approximation, as established by Turchin (1965), Gurevich and Tarasov (1968), and Lovesey (1984), to the ultracold neutron regime where multiple scattering dominates. It addresses the failure of previous MST approximations, such as the weak-coupling, long-mean-free-path, and long-correlation-length assumptions used by Goldberger and Watson (1964) and earlier UCN treatments by Steyerl (1976), for UCN inelastic scattering. The derived equations enable numerical calculation of elastic and inelastic scattering cross sections for UCN, including quasielastic processes relevant to UCN cooling and heating observed by Bondarenko et al. (1998) and Nesvizhevsky et al. (1999), and to condensed matter studies proposed by Golub et al. (1996).
