# Formation of neutron-rich and superheavy elements in astrophysical objects

> We calculate the reaction and the fusion cross-sections of neutron-rich heavy nuclei taking light exotic isotopes as projectiles. Results of neutron-rich Pb and U isotopes are demonstrated as the representative targets and He, B as the projectiles. The Gluaber Model and the Coupled Channel Formalism are used to evaluate the reaction and the fusion cross-sections for the cases considered.Based on the analysis of these cross-sections, we predict the formation of heavy, superheavy and super-superheavy elements through rapid neutron/light nuclei capture r-process of the nucleosynthesis in astrophysical objects.

# Formation of neutron-rich and superheavy elements in astrophysical objects

## Key claim
The authors calculate reaction and fusion cross-sections for neutron-rich heavy nuclei with light exotic projectiles, and based on these cross-sections they predict the formation of heavy, superheavy, and super-superheavy elements through rapid neutron/light nuclei capture r-process in astrophysical objects. The claim is conditional on the existence of a suitable energy range (approximately 30 to 200 MeV incident projectile energy) and a highly neutron-rich, high-temperature environment such as relativistic jets of gamma-ray bursts or supernovae jets near a nascent neutron star.

## Method
The reaction cross-sections were calculated using the Glauber model with effective field theory motivated relativistic mean field (E-RMF) nuclear densities using the G2 parameter set. The fusion cross-sections were estimated using the non-relativistic coupled-channel formalism with the computer code CCFULL, including all orders of coupling. The calculations were performed for neutron-rich Pb and U isotopes as representative targets, with He and B isotopes as projectiles.

## Key numbers
- The reaction and fusion cross-sections are optimum at approximately 30 to 200 MeV of incident projectile energy; beyond this range the cross-sections decrease drastically.
- A star with initial mass of approximately 20 solar masses develops a carbon-oxygen core of approximately 3.3 solar masses, leaving behind a neutron star of approximately 1.4 solar masses, approximately 1.3 solar masses of oxygen, and approximately 0.6 solar masses of heavier elements (Si and Fe group).
- The kinetic energy of a supernova explosion is approximately 10^51 ergs (approximately 6.25 × 10^56 MeV).
- The radius of a neutron star shrinks from approximately 100 km to 10 km over time.
- A representative formation path for the superheavy element ^408X_132 (Z=132, N=276) is given through successive ^20B capture reactions: ^20B+^208Pb → ^228Fr, then ^20B+^228Fr → ^248U, continuing through ^268Bk, ^288No, ^308Bh, ^328X_112, ^348X_117, ^368X_122, ^388X_127, and finally ^408X_132.
- Each ^20B capture increases the proton number Z by 5 units and the mass number A by 20 units.
- A superheavy isotope with Z=122 or 124 and mass number A=292 has been reported in natural Th with an estimated half-life of t_1/2 ≥ 10^8 years (citing Marinov et al., 2008).

## Context
This work extends earlier studies by Satpathy et al. (2008) which claimed that neutron-rich U and Th isotopes are thermally fissile and could release orders of magnitude more energy than ^235U through multi-fragmentation fission in astrophysical objects. The calculations use the Glauber model formalism developed by Glauber (1959) and the coupled-channel approach from Hagino et al. (1999), with nuclear densities from the E-RMF model with G2 parameter set from Tang et al. (1996) and Patra et al. (2001). The work enables predictions for the formation of superheavy elements beyond Z=118 in astrophysical environments such as gamma-ray burst jets and supernovae jets, and suggests that such elements may be synthesizable in laboratory conditions. The authors also reference calculations by Nilsson et al. (1968) suggesting stability of the most stable superheavy elements could be as high as 10^9 years.
