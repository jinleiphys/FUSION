# Translation of collision geometry fluctuations into momentum anisotropies in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We develop a systematic framework for the study of the initial collision geometry fluctuations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions and investigate how they evolve through different stages of the fireball history and translate into final particle momentum anisotropies. We find in our event-by-event analysis that only the few lowest momentum anisotropy parameters survive after the hydrodynamical evolution of the system. The geometry of the produced medium is found to be affected by the pre-equilibrium evolution of the medium and the thermal smearing of the discretized event-by-event initial conditions, both of which tend to smear out the spatial anisotropies. We find such effects to be more prominent for higher moments than for lower moments. The correlations between odd and even spatial anisotropy parameters during the pre-equilibrium expansion are quantitatively studied and found to be small. Our study provides a theoretical foundation for the understanding of initial state fluctuations and the collective expansion dynamics in relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

# Wiki Page: Translation of collision geometry fluctuations into momentum anisotropies in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

The paper develops a systematic framework showing that only the few lowest momentum anisotropy parameters survive after hydrodynamical evolution of the fireball, and that pre-equilibrium free-streaming evolution and thermal smearing of discretized initial conditions both tend to smear out spatial anisotropies, with these effects being more prominent for higher moments than for lower moments. The correlations between odd and even spatial anisotropy parameters during pre-equilibrium expansion are found to be small.

## Method

The authors construct full phase-space initial conditions from a Monte Carlo Glauber model including nucleon position fluctuations and fluctuations from individual nucleon-nucleon collisions, with particle multiplicities sampled from a negative binomial distribution. They simulate the pre-equilibrium phase by solving the free-streaming Boltzmann equation (collision term set to zero) for massless particles, then evolve the system using three-dimensional relativistic ideal hydrodynamics. The spatial anisotropy parameters epsilon_n and final momentum anisotropy coefficients v_n are computed event-by-event for Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear radius R=6.38 fm and diffuseness d=0.535 fm for Au nucleus, with normalization rho_0=0.163/fm^3
- Nucleon-nucleon inelastic cross section sigma_NN=42 mb at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV
- Two-component scaling parameter alpha=0.13 for charged particle multiplicity description
- Negative binomial distribution parameters mu=2.35 and k=1.9 for p+p/p+antip collisions at midrapidity
- Transverse momentum power law parameters b=0.88 and c=4
- Pre-equilibrium evolution time t_0=0.6 fm/c used for ratio studies; t_0=1.2 fm/c used for transformation matrix analysis
- At b=8 fm, diagonal transformation matrix elements show 13% reduction for epsilon_2 and 19% reduction for epsilon_3 after pre-equilibrium free-streaming
- Width of initial v_n distribution found to be 1/sqrt(2N) for all n, with factor sqrt(2) relation between rapidity bins |y|<0.5 and |y|<1

## Context

This work extends event-by-event studies of initial geometry fluctuations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, building on the Monte Carlo Glauber model approach of Miller (2003) and Broniowski (2007), and the triangular flow proposals of Alver (2010). It provides a theoretical foundation connecting initial state fluctuations to final momentum anisotropies, enabling more precise extraction of transport coefficients such as the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio eta/s, which previous work by Romatschke (2007) and Song (2008) showed is sensitive to initial eccentricity uncertainties of 20-30%. The framework enables systematic study of how different harmonic moments propagate through the fireball history, which is needed to understand higher-order flow coefficients measured by Abelev (2007) and Adare (2010).
