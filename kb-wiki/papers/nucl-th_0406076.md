# Dipole states in stable and unstable nuclei

> A nuclear structure model based on linear response theory (i.e., Random Phase Approximation) and which includes pairing correlations and anharmonicities (coupling with collective vibrations), has been implemented in such a way that it can be applied on the same footing to magic as well as open-shell nuclei. As applications, we have chosen to study the dipole excitations both in well-known, stable isotopes like $^{208}$Pb and $^{120}$Sn as well as in the neutron-rich, unstable $^{132}$Sn nucleus, by addressing in the latter case the question about the nature of the low-lying strength. Our results suggest that the model is reliable and predicts in all cases low-lying strength of non collective nature.

# Dipole states in stable and unstable nuclei

## Key claim

The authors implement a self-consistent model (QRPA-PC) that includes pairing correlations and phonon coupling on the same footing for both magic and open-shell nuclei, and find that low-lying dipole strength exists in all studied nuclei but is of non-collective nature. The hedge is that the model is "reliable" and "suggests" this conclusion, but the authors acknowledge that the low-lying strength in $^{132}$Sn is fragmented and at variance with relativistic RPA predictions, meaning experimental data are needed to discriminate.

## Method

The authors start from a Hartree-Fock-BCS (HF-BCS) mean field using a Skyrme force (SIII) in the particle-hole channel and a zero-range density-dependent pairing force in the particle-particle channel, then solve the (Quasi-particle) Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) in configuration space with a 100 MeV cutoff. They extend this by coupling the QRPA states to collective vibrations (phonons of multipolarity 1$^{-}$, 2$^{+}$, 3$^{-}$, and 4$^{+}$ below 30 MeV with at least 5% strength), creating the QRPA-PC model, and apply it to dipole excitations in $^{208}$Pb, $^{120}$Sn, and $^{132}$Sn.

## Key numbers

* For $^{208}$Pb, the RPA centroid energy $E_0 = m_1/m_0$ is 14.9 MeV; after phonon coupling (QRPA-PC) it shifts to 14.4 MeV; the experimental peak energy is 13.46 MeV with width 3.9 MeV, while the QRPA-PC fit gives 13.1 MeV with width 3.7 MeV.
* For $^{120}$Sn, the QRPA centroid energy is 17.3 MeV (SIII) and the QRPA-PC centroid remains 17.1 MeV; the experimental peak energy is 15.4 MeV with width 4.9 MeV, while the QRPA-PC fit gives 15.7 MeV with width 5.3 MeV.
* For $^{132}$Sn, the QRPA centroid energy is 17.0 MeV (SIII) and the QRPA-PC centroid remains 17.0 MeV; the predicted peak energy from QRPA-PC is 15.5 MeV with width 5.8 MeV.
* The total energy-weighted sum rule (EWSR) values are 2448 MeV$\cdot$fm$^2$ ($\kappa = 0.399$) for $^{120}$Sn, 2566 MeV$\cdot$fm$^2$ ($\kappa = 0.397$) for $^{132}$Sn, and 4202 MeV$\cdot$fm$^2$ ($\kappa = 0.408$) for $^{208}$Pb.
* For $^{208}$Pb, the calculated low-lying B(E1) strength is 0.53 e$^2$fm$^2$ below 6 MeV and 1.27 e$^2$fm$^2$ up to 8 MeV, compared to experimental values of 0.52 e$^2$fm$^2$ and 0.80 e$^2$fm$^2$, respectively.
* In $^{132}$Sn, the lowest RPA peak at 8.44 MeV is 78% associated with the 3s$_{1/2} \rightarrow$ 3p$_{3/2}$ transition and absorbs only 0.2% of the EWSR; two higher peaks at 8.61 MeV and 9.53 MeV absorb 0.5% and 0.3% of the EWSR, respectively.

## Context

This work extends the pioneering calculations of NP81 (1981) by using a self-consistent Skyrme interaction and including pairing in a consistent way, improving on the earlier model of NP01 (2001) which dropped the pairing contribution from the residual interaction. The results are compared with experimental photoabsorption cross sections for $^{208}$Pb and $^{120}$Sn, and predictions are made for the unstable neutron-rich nucleus $^{132}$Sn, where the authors find fragmented low-lying strength in contrast to the more collective low-lying states predicted by relativistic RPA studies (Vret, year not given). The work enables future experimental tests to discriminate between non-relativistic and relativistic microscopic models of dipole strength in medium-heavy neutron-rich nuclei.
