# Excitation of $^{229}$Th nuclei in laser plasma: the energy and half-life of the low-lying isomeric state

> The results of experimental studies of the low-energy isomeric state in the $^{229}$Th nucleus are presented. The work is consisted of several stages. During the first stage $^{229}$Th nuclei were excited with the inverse internal conversion to the low-lying isomeric level in plasma that was formed by laser pulse at the $^{229}$Th-containing target surface. Then thorium ions having excited nuclei were extracted from the plasma by an external electrical field and implanted into thin SiO$_2$ film grown on a silicon substrate (that is a dielectric material with about 9 eV band-gap). Gamma decay of isomeric nuclei was registered during the second stage by the general methods of the electron spectroscopy after the photon-electron emission from the silicon substrate. Substitution of the photon registration with the electron one allowed us to increase the desired signal by several orders of magnitude and detect the $^{229}$Th nuclei decay. During the third stage the electron spectra from standard Xe VUV source was obtained that allowed determining the energy of photons. In order to prove that the detected signal is caused by isomeric $^{229}$Th nuclei decay a series of experiments was carried. The analysis of electron spectra gives the following results: the energy of the nuclear transition is $E_{\text{is}}=7.1(^{+0.1}_{-0.2})$~eV, the half-life of the isomeric level in bare nucleus in vacuum is $T_{1/2}=1880\pm170$~s, the reduced probability of the isomeric nuclear transition is $B_{\text{W.u.}}(M1;3/2^+\rightarrow 5/2^+)=(3.3\pm0.3)\times10^{-2}$.

## Key claim

The paper reports the first direct excitation of the low-lying isomeric state in $^{229}$Th nuclei via inverse internal conversion in laser plasma, followed by implantation into a wide-bandgap SiO$_2$ matrix to suppress internal conversion decay. The authors claim an isomeric transition energy of $E_{\text{is}}=7.1(^{+0.1}_{-0.2})$ eV and a half-life of $T_{1/2}=1880\pm170$ s for the bare nucleus in vacuum, though the observed decay in the SiO$_2$ matrix shows non-exponential behavior attributed to the Purcell effect.

## Method

$^{229}$Th nuclei were excited via inverse internal conversion in laser plasma produced by a Nd:YAG laser (1064 nm, 15 ns pulses, $10^9-10^{10}$ W/cm$^2$) ablating a ThO$_2$ target containing 6.8% $^{229}$Th. Thorium ions were extracted by an external electric field (10 kV) and implanted into a 6.5 nm SiO$_2$ film on a silicon substrate, where the wide bandgap (approximately 9 eV) prevents internal conversion decay. Gamma decay of isomeric nuclei was detected indirectly by measuring photoelectrons emitted from the silicon substrate using electron spectroscopy, with the energy scale calibrated against XPS Si2p core-level measurements and a xenon VUV discharge lamp.

## Key numbers

- Isomeric transition energy: $E_{\text{is}} = 7.1(^{+0.1}_{-0.2})$ eV, determined from photoelectron spectra with calibration against a Xe VUV lamp (147 nm, 8.4 eV)
- Half-life of the isomeric level in bare nucleus in vacuum: $T_{1/2} = 1880 \pm 170$ s
- Reduced probability of the isomeric nuclear transition: $B_{\text{W.u.}}(M1;3/2^+ \rightarrow 5/2^+) = (3.3 \pm 0.3) \times 10^{-2}$
- Estimated number of excited nuclei per laser pulse: $N_{\text{is}} \simeq 10^8$, from an excitation probability $\eta_{\text{IIC}} \simeq 10^{-5}$ per nucleus
- Laser pulse energy: 100 mJ, pulse duration: 15 ns, power density: $10^9-10^{10}$ W/cm$^2$
- Target contained 6.8% $^{229}$Th isotope with overall activity approximately 100 kBq

## Context

This work extends the search for the $^{229}$Th isomeric state, previously measured indirectly via gamma-ray cascades by Beck et al. (2007, 2009) giving $E_{\text{is}} = 7.8 \pm 0.5$ eV, and by the direct internal conversion measurements of von der Wense et al. (2016) giving a range of 6.3-18.3 eV. The authors' value of 7.1 eV deviates from Beck's result by 1.5 joint standard deviations but with three times smaller uncertainty. The excitation-implantation method enables direct nuclear excitation without relying on $^{233}$U alpha decay, and the use of electron spectroscopy rather than direct VUV photon detection increases signal by several orders of magnitude. This approach could enable future optical clock applications and nuclear transition-based lasers, as well as studies of the Purcell effect on nuclear decay rates in dielectric environments.
