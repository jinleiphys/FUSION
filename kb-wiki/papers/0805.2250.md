# Density dependence of isospin observables in spinodal decomposition

> Isotopic fluctuations in fragment formation are investigated in a quasi-analytical description of the spinodal decomposition scenario. By exploiting the fluctuation-dissipation relations the covariance matrix of density fluctuations is derived as a function of the wave vector for nuclear matter at given values of density, charge asymmetry, temperature, and of the time that the system spends in the instability region. Then density fluctuations in ordinary space are implemented with a Fourier transform performed in a finite cubic lattice. Inside this box, domains with different density coexist, from which clusters of nucleons eventually emerge. Within our approach, the isotopic distributions are determined by the N/Z ratio of the leading unstable isoscalarlike mode and by isovectorlike fluctuations present in the matter undergoing the spinodal decomposition. Hence the average value of the N/Z ratio of clusters and the width of the relative distribution reflect the properties of the symmetry energy. Generating a large number of events, these calculations allow a careful investigation of the cluster isotopic content as a function of the cluster density. A uniform decrease of the average charge asymmetry and of the width of the isotopic distributions with increasing density is observed. Finally we remark that the results essentially refer to the early break--up of the system.

## Key claim

The isotopic content of clusters formed during spinodal decomposition depends on the density of the domain from which they emerge, with the average N/Z ratio and the width of isotopic distributions decreasing uniformly as cluster density increases, though the results refer essentially to the early break-up of the system.

## Method

The authors use a quasi-analytical linear approximation for density fluctuations in asymmetric nuclear matter initialized inside a finite cubic lattice with periodic boundary conditions, deriving the covariance matrix of fluctuations via fluctuation-dissipation relations as a function of wave vector for given density, charge asymmetry, temperature, and time spent in the instability region. They generate events by Fourier transforming these fluctuations into coordinate space and apply a coalescence algorithm based on neighboring cell densities to identify nucleon clusters, using two different parametrizations of the symmetry energy (a "superstiff" and a "soft" form) with a Skyrme-like effective interaction.

## Key numbers

- Average density chosen: 0.4 times the saturation density (0.16 fm⁻³), with temperature T = 4.5 MeV
- Time spent in instability region: t = 80 fm/c
- Cubic lattice side length: L = 21 fm; primitive cell side length: 3 fm
- Box contains approximately 370 nucleons at the chosen density
- 4 × 10³ events run for each combination of symmetry energy parametrization and global asymmetry
- Average numbers of nucleons in liquid phase: approximately 270; in vapour phase: approximately 100
- For the "superstiff" symmetry energy: d = 19 MeV in the expression S(ρ) = (2d/ρ_eq²) × ρ/(1+ρ/ρ_eq)
- For the "soft" symmetry energy: d₁ = 240.9 MeV·fm³ and d₂ = 819.1 MeV·fm⁶ in S(ρ) = d₁ − d₂ρ
- Symmetric interaction parameters: A = −356.8 MeV, B = 303.9 MeV, σ = 1/6
- Isovector surface term coefficient: D = 40 MeV·fm⁵

## Context

This work extends the formalism developed by Colonna et al. (2005) and Matera et al. (2000) for density fluctuations in spinodal decomposition, improving upon those earlier approaches by enabling event-by-event analysis through direct Fourier expansion in a finite lattice. The results connect to experimental studies of multifragmentation in heavy-ion collisions such as those reported by Tabacaru et al. (2006) and Frankland et al. (2001), and to theoretical investigations of isospin distillation by Muller and Serot (1995), Bao et al. (1997), and Baran et al. (1998). The finding that lighter clusters originate from lower-density domains where isospin distillation is less effective contradicts the trend predicted by statistical model calculations (Botvina et al., 2001; Larochelle et al., 1999), offering a way to distinguish between dynamical and statistical fragmentation mechanisms.
