# A systematic study of giant quadrupole resonances with the subtracted second random--phase approximation: beyond--mean--field centroids and fragmentation

> A systematic analysis of giant quadrupole resonances is performed for several nuclei, from $^{30}$Si to $^{208}$Pb, within the subtracted second random--phase--approximation (SSRPA) model in the framework of the energy--density--functional theory. Centroid energies and widths of the isoscalar giant quadrupole resonances are compared with the corresponding random--phase--approximation (RPA) values. We find lower SSRPA centroid energies compared to the RPA values leading, in general, to a better agreement with the experimental data. As far as the widths are concerned, we observe for both SSRPA and RPA cases a global attenuation of the single--particle Landau damping going from lighter to heavier nuclei and we obtain, systematically, larger widths in the SSRPA model compared to the RPA case. For some selected nuclei for which high--resolution ($p,p'$) experimental data are available, namely $^{40}$Ca, $^{90}$Zr, $^{120}$Sn, and $^{208}$Pb, the theoretical strength distributions are directly compared with the experimental spectra. We observe a significant improvement, with respect to RPA results, in the description of the spreading widths and of the fragmentation of the obtained spectra, due to the coupling between 1 particle-1 hole and 2 particle-2 hole configurations.

## Key claim

The subtracted second random-phase-approximation (SSRPA) model yields systematically lower centroid energies for isoscalar giant quadrupole resonances compared to standard RPA, leading to better agreement with experimental data for most nuclei studied, except for $^{40}$Ca where the SSRPA centroid is slightly underestimated.

## Method

The authors performed fully self-consistent RPA and SSRPA calculations using the SLy4 Skyrme interaction within the energy-density-functional framework for thirteen spherical or near-spherical nuclei from $^{30}$Si to $^{208}$Pb. The SSRPA model includes coupling between 1 particle-1 hole (1p1h) and 2 particle-2 hole (2p2h) configurations, with a subtraction procedure to remove double counting of correlations and ultraviolet divergences from zero-range forces. Centroid energies and widths were extracted by fitting Lorentzian distributions to folded strength distributions, rather than using moment-based formulas, to avoid artifacts from strong fragmentation.

## Key numbers

* SSRPA centroid energies are systematically lower than RPA values; for example, the $^{40}$Ca SSRPA centroid shifts from 16.74 to 16.86 MeV when the 2p2h cutoff is increased from 60 to 70 MeV, a change of less than 1%.
* For $^{90}$Zr, the RPA centroid overestimates the experimental value by more than 1 MeV, while the SSRPA prediction is located at lower energies in better agreement with data.
* SSRPA widths are systematically larger than RPA widths; for $^{40}$Ca the SSRPA width is 2.61 MeV compared to 0.14 MeV in RPA, and for $^{208}$Pb the SSRPA width is 1.21 MeV compared to 0.1 MeV in RPA.
* The 2p2h energy cutoff was set to 60 MeV for medium-mass nuclei ($^{30}$Si through $^{68}$Ni) and 50 MeV for heavy nuclei ($^{90}$Zr through $^{208}$Pb), with stability verified up to 70 MeV for $^{40}$Ca.

## Context

This work extends the SSRPA model, previously applied to the giant dipole resonance in $^{48}$Ca (Vasseur et al., 2017), to a systematic study of isoscalar giant quadrupole resonances across a wide mass range. It improves upon standard RPA calculations by incorporating 2p2h configurations, which are essential for describing spreading widths and fine structure. The results are compared with experimental data from Youngblood et al. (1980s) for centroid energies and with high-resolution ($p,p'$) measurements from iThemba LABS for $^{40}$Ca (Usman et al., 2011), $^{90}$Zr, $^{120}$Sn, and $^{208}$Pb (Shevchenko et al., 2004). The SSRPA model provides a more realistic description of fragmentation than RPA, though the calculated strength distributions do not extend over the full experimental energy range, likely due to missing higher-order correlations and continuum coupling.
