# How many particles do make a fluid? Qualifying collective behavior in expanding ultracold gases

> Collective phenomena in quantum many-body systems are often described in terms of hydrodynamics, an appropriate framework when the involved particle numbers are effectively macroscopic. We propose to use experiments on expanding clouds of few and many interacting cold atoms to investigate the emergence of hydrodynamics as a function of particle number. We consider gases confined in two-dimensional elliptically-deformed traps, and we employ the manifestation of elliptic flow as an indicator of collective behavior. We quantify the response of the gas to the deformation of the trapping potential, and show how such information can be used to establish how many atoms are needed for the system to develop a degree of collectivity comparable to that expected in the hydrodynamic limit. This method permits one, in particular, to exploit observations made in expanding atomic gases to shed light on the apparent hydrodynamic behaviour of mesoscopic systems of quarks and gluons formed in the scattering of light ions in high-energy collider experiments.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that elliptic flow measurements in expanding ultracold atomic clouds can be used to quantify how many interacting particles are needed for a system to develop a degree of collectivity comparable to the hydrodynamic limit, but the authors emphasize that this is a proposed experimental method rather than a completed measurement, and they provide only qualitative expectations for interacting gases while computing exact baselines only for non-interacting quantum gases.

## Method

The authors propose to confine cold atoms in two-dimensional elliptically-deformed harmonic traps with known eccentricity ε₂, release the trap, and measure the asymptotic angular distribution of outgoing atoms. By repeating the experiment many times at fixed trap orientation and fixed particle number N, one obtains a statistical average of cos(2φ) that defines v₂, and the ratio v₂/ε₂ quantifies how efficiently the initial spatial deformation is converted into momentum anisotropy. The method is designed to work for any N, from few to many atoms, by accumulating statistics over repeated runs.

## Key numbers

- The experiment of O'Hara et al. (2002) used a trap aspect ratio λ = 0.035, corresponding to ε₂ = 0.932, and observed a cloud with widths σ_y = 159 μm and σ_x = 375 μm at t = 2 ms.
- From that experiment, the extracted elliptic flow was v₂ = 0.42, yielding v₂/ε₂ = 0.45.
- The authors note that this v₂/ε₂ value is close to that found in model calculations of peripheral ²⁰⁸Pb+²⁰⁸Pb collisions at the Large Hadron Collider, where v₂/ε₂ ≈ 0.3.
- The paper mentions that in high-energy experiments, collective behavior is observed down to systems emitting as few as 20 particles per unit rapidity.

## Context

This work extends the concept of elliptic flow from high-energy nuclear collision physics (where it was pioneered by Ollitrault (1992) and quantified via v₂/ε₂ by Poskanzer and Voloshin (1999)) to the cold atom domain, where the trap geometry is known and controllable. The method enables systematic studies of the transition from few-body to many-body collective behavior, complementing existing cold atom experiments on elliptic flow that have focused on macroscopic particle numbers (O'Hara et al., 2002; Fletcher et al., 2018). The authors aim to connect observations in expanding atomic gases to the puzzle of apparent hydrodynamic behavior in mesoscopic quark-gluon systems formed in light-ion collisions at high-energy colliders.
