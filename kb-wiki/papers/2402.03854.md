# On the calculation and use of effective single-particle energies. The example of the neutron $1d_{3/2}$-$1d_{5/2}$ splitting along $\text{N}=20$ isotones

> The rich phenomenology of quantum many-body systems such as atomic nuclei is complex to interpret. Often, the behaviour (e.g. evolution with the number of constituents) of measurable/observable quantities such as binding or excitation energies can be best understood on the basis of a simplified picture involving auxiliary quantities that are not observable, i.e. whose values vary with parameters that are internal to the theoretical construction (contrarily to measurable/observable quantities). While being useful, the simplified interpretation is thus theoretical-scheme-dependent. This applies, in particular, to the so-called single-nucleon shell structure based on auxiliary effective single-particle energies (ESPEs). In this context, the present work aims at (i) recalling the way to compute ESPEs out of solutions of many-body Schrödinger's equation, (ii) illustrating the use of ESPEs within the frame of state-of-the-art ab initio calculations to interpret the outcome of a recent nuclear experiment and (iii) demonstrating the impact of several alterations to the computation of ESPEs. While the chosen alterations constitute approximations within the ab initio scheme, they are built-in when employing other theoretical constructs at play in nuclear physics. The present considerations are thus meant to empirically illustrate variations that can be expected between ESPEs computed within different (equally valid) theoretical schemes.

## Key claim

Effective single-particle energies (ESPEs) are non-observable quantities whose values depend on the theoretical scheme and resolution scale used to compute them, meaning that different but equally valid theoretical frameworks can yield different ESPEs even when they reproduce the same experimental observables. The paper demonstrates this scheme dependence by computing ESPEs within an ab initio framework and then introducing two alterations that approximate features built into other theoretical approaches.

## Method

The authors perform ab initio self-consistent Gorkov-Green's function (GGF) calculations at the ADC(2) approximation level for the even-even N=20 isotones 34Si, 36S, 38Ar, and 40Ca. They compute the one-nucleon spectral function and construct the Baranger centroid Hamiltonian from its first moment, then diagonalize this Hamiltonian to obtain ESPEs for the neutron 1d5/2 and 1d3/2 orbitals. The sensitivity of the resulting spin-orbit splitting is tested by introducing two alterations to the ESPE definition: restricting the diagonalization to a single major harmonic oscillator shell (mimicking the valence-space shell model) and truncating the energy range of included final states (mimicking the direct-reaction approach).

## Key numbers

- The neutron 1d5/2-1d3/2 ESPE splitting in 36S computed with the ab initio scheme using the sat Hamiltonian is approximately 3.5 MeV
- The same splitting in 40Ca computed with the same scheme is approximately 5.5 MeV
- The direct-reaction approach yields a splitting that increases from 36S to 40Ca, opposite to the global trend that predicts a reduction
- The experimental campaign at iThemba LABS studied the 36S(p,d)35S reaction at proton energy Ep=66 MeV, yielding information on 98 final states in 35S up to an excitation energy of 16 MeV
- Calculations use a spherical harmonic oscillator basis with emax=13 and e3max=16

## Context

This work extends the formal demonstration of ESPE non-observability by Duguet (2012, 2015) to a concrete physics case of recent experimental interest, namely the anomalous reduction of the neutron 1d5/2-1d3/2 spin-orbit splitting along N=20 isotones reported by Jongile (2023). The paper enables future systematic comparisons between ESPEs computed within different theoretical schemes (ab initio, valence-space shell model, direct-reaction approach) and motivates extending the direct-reaction analysis to other N=20 isotones such as 38Ar and 34Si.
