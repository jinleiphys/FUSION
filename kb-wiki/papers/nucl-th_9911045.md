# Studying Phase Transitions in Nuclear Collisions

> In this talk I discuss three main topics concerning the theoretical description and observable signatures of possible phase transitions in nuclear collisions. The first one is related to the multifragmentation of thermalized sources and its connection to a liquid-gas phase transition in finite systems. The second one is dealing with the Coulomb excitation of ultrarelativistic heavy ions resulting in their deep disintegration. The third topic is devoted to the description of a first order phase transition in rapidly expanding matter. The resulting picture is that a strong collective flow of matter will lead to the fragmentation of a metastable phase into droplets. If the transition from quark-gluon plasma to hadron gas is of the first order it will manifest itself by strong nonstatistical fluctuations in observable hadron distributions.

# Studying Phase Transitions in Nuclear Collisions

## Key claim

The paper argues that a first-order phase transition in rapidly expanding matter (such as the quark-gluon plasma to hadron gas transition) will proceed through nonequilibrium fragmentation of the metastable phase into droplets, and that this process will manifest as strong nonstatistical fluctuations in hadron distributions observable on an event-by-event basis, provided the transition is indeed first order.

## Method

The author reviews three distinct theoretical approaches: (1) the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) for describing thermalized nuclear sources undergoing a liquid-gas phase transition, using microcanonical Monte Carlo generation of break-up channels with fragment de-excitation via Fermi break-up and evaporation; (2) the RELDIS code combining equivalent-photon spectra, an intranuclear cascade model, and SMM to simulate electromagnetic dissociation of ultrarelativistic heavy ions; and (3) a dynamical fragmentation model applying Grady's energy-balance prescription to estimate droplet sizes from a first-order chiral phase transition in expanding matter described by the linear sigma-model with constituent quarks.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of normal nuclear matter: ρ₀ ≈ 0.15 fm⁻³
- Critical temperature for liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear matter: T_c ∼ 10 MeV
- Critical temperature from grand canonical SMM: about 7 MeV
- Average neutron multiplicities from electromagnetic dissociation: 4.1 at SPS, 7.2 at RHIC, 8.8 at LHC
- Total electromagnetic dissociation cross sections: 100 b for RHIC, 200 b for LHC
- Predicted ED reaction rate at RHIC luminosity L ≈ 10²⁷ cm⁻²s⁻¹: 10⁵ interactions per second
- Characteristic droplet radius from Grady prescription for slow expansion (H⁻¹ = 20 fm/c): R* = 3.4 fm, giving droplet mass M* ≈ 100 GeV
- Characteristic droplet radius for fast expansion (H⁻¹ = 6 fm/c): R* = 1.5 fm, giving droplet mass M* ≈ 10 GeV
- Power-law exponent for fragment charge distribution near critical point: τ ≈ 2.2
- Temperature at which heat capacity peaks: T ≈ 6 MeV
- Probability to emit more than 20 neutrons in electromagnetic dissociation at RHIC: about 5%

## Context

This talk extends the Statistical Multifragmentation Model developed by Bondorf, Mishustin, and collaborators (1985) and the subsequent work by Gross on statistical approaches to nuclear fragmentation. The analysis of caloric curves and Campi scatter plots builds on experimental work by the ALADIN collaboration (Pochodzalla et al., 1995) and the Bologna group (D'Agostino et al., 1999). The electromagnetic dissociation treatment extends the work of Pshenichnov et al. on RELDIS, while the dynamical fragmentation model applies Grady's (1982) energy-balance method for fluid fragmentation to the chiral phase transition described by the linear sigma-model of Mocsy et al. The paper enables future searches for nonstatistical fluctuations in hadron distributions at RHIC and LHC, and suggests that lessons from the liquid-gas phase transition should guide studies of deconfinement and chiral transitions in relativistic heavy-ion collisions.
