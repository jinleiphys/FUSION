# Reply on Toke's and Schroeder's comment on our paper "Statistical nature of multifragmentation"

> We point out some basic misunderstandings and wrong statements about our microcanonical Metropolis Monte Carlo (MMMC) model of nuclear fragmentation.

## Key claim

The authors defend their microcanonical Metropolis Monte Carlo (MMMC) model against Toke and Schroeder's criticism, arguing that the commenters' objections are based on misunderstandings and that the MMMC model strictly enforces overall energy conservation and equal weighting of all exit quantum states, contrary to the claims made against it.

## Method

The authors clarify that their MMMC model treats every exit quantum state characterized by a complete set of quantum numbers with the same weight, integrating neutron momenta under constraints of fixed total momentum and fixed total kinetic energy within the common center-of-mass system. They distinguish their approach from the Copenhagen Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM), which switches to the canonical ensemble for each selected partition and treats neutron evaporation as a slow secondary process.

## Key numbers

- Excitation energy of 2–3 MeV/nucleon: the threshold at which the multifragmentation channel opens, making the binomial distribution inadequate because it ignores this threshold and violates energy conservation.
- Approximately 400 MeV excitation energy: the energy where the authors' calculations in their original paper begin, which Toke and Schroeder questioned as qualifying as "very small excitation" when fragment production is forbidden by energy conservation.

## Context

This reply addresses a comment by Toke and Schroeder (1998) on the authors' earlier paper (Gross, 1997) concerning the statistical nature of multifragmentation. The authors defend their MMMC model against criticisms about its microcanonical character, citation practices, and treatment of experimental filters, while distinguishing their approach from the Copenhagen SMM model (Bondorf, 1995; Bondorf, 1998). The discussion clarifies the proper treatment of fission fragments and experimental detection filters for interpreting multifragmentation data.
