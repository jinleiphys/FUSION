# Understanding the correlation between elliptic and triangular flow

> The relative correlation between the magnitudes of elliptic flow ($v_2$) and triangular flow ($v_3$) has been accurately measured in nucleus-nucleus collisions at the LHC collider. As a function of the centrality of the collision, it changes sign and varies non-monotonically. We show that this is naturally explained by two combined effects. The first effect is a skewness in initial-state fluctuations, which is quantified by the correlation between the geometry-driven elliptic deformation in the reaction plane and the fluctuation-driven triangularity $\varepsilon_3$. We introduce an intensive measure of this skewness, which is generically of order unity and depends weakly on the system size and centrality. We evaluate its magnitude using Monte Carlo simulations of the initial state, which show that it is sensitive to the nucleon width. The second effect is the fluctuation of impact parameter relative to centrality classifiers used by experiment. The ATLAS collaboration uses two different centrality classifiers, the multiplicity $N_{ch}$ and the transverse energy $E_T$. We fit both sets of results for Pb+Pb collisions up to $\approx 40\%$ centrality with a single parameter, the intensive mixed skewness. Its value inferred from experiment agrees with theoretical expectations.

# Understanding the correlation between elliptic and triangular flow

## Key claim

The observed correlation between elliptic flow ($v_2$) and triangular flow ($v_3$) in Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC is explained by two combined effects: a skewness in initial-state fluctuations (quantified by a correlation between geometry-driven elliptic deformation and fluctuation-driven triangularity) and the fluctuation of impact parameter relative to centrality classifiers used by experiment. The authors show that a single intensive parameter, the mixed skewness, can fit both ATLAS datasets using multiplicity $N_{ch}$ and transverse energy $E_T$ as centrality classifiers up to approximately 40% centrality.

## Method

The authors decompose the normalized symmetric cumulant ${\rm nsc}_{2,3}\{4\}$ into cumulants of initial-state anisotropies $\varepsilon_2$ and $\varepsilon_3$, identifying the leading non-Gaussian contribution as a mixed skewness (third-order cumulant) and a subleading mixed kurtosis (fourth-order cumulant). They define intensive measures $\Gamma_S$ and $\Gamma_K$ that suppress dependence on system size and shape, evaluate these using Monte Carlo simulations with the \trento{} model of initial conditions for different nucleon widths and substructure parameters, and fit the intensive skewness to ATLAS data on ${\rm nsc}_{2,3}\{4\}$ while accounting for impact parameter fluctuations via Bayesian inference from the measured centrality classifier distributions.

## Key numbers

- $\kappa_2 \approx 0.326$ and $\kappa_3 \approx 0.235$: linear hydrodynamic response coefficients for elliptic and triangular flow, respectively, for mid-central Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV per nucleon pair (from Ref. Nijs 2020)
- $\Gamma_S \approx -2.1$: best-fit value of the intensive mixed skewness from a combined fit of ATLAS data for $N_{ch} > 750$ and $E_T > 950$ GeV (encompassing roughly the 40% most central collisions)
- $\Gamma_S \approx -2$: central value of the intensive mixed skewness from the fit, lying in the middle of Monte Carlo simulations
- Relative standard deviation of $N_{ch}$ at $b=0$: 4.5%, used to tune the fluctuation parameter $k$ in \trento{} simulations
- $\gamma = 3.5$: parameter characterizing the variation of $N_{ch}$ and $E_T$ fluctuation width with centrality, used in modeling impact parameter fluctuations

## Context

This work extends the understanding of anisotropic flow correlations beyond the naive expectation that elliptic and triangular flow are uncorrelated, which follows from the central limit theorem applied to initial-state fluctuations. The anti-correlation (negative ${\rm nsc}_{2,3}\{4\}$) seen in hydrodynamic simulations by Gardim (2016), Zhu (2016), and Schenke (2019), and in transport simulations by Nasim (2016) and Li (2021), is here explained through a simple analytic framework without invoking a specific hydrodynamic model. The positive correlation in ultracentral collisions is attributed to impact parameter fluctuations, an effect first studied by Gardim (2016) who noted sensitivity to centrality bin width. The intensive measures of non-Gaussianity build on the work of Giacalone (2020), Alqahtani (2024), and Roubertie (2025). The results enable data-driven extraction of the nucleon width parameter $w$ from experimental correlations, complementing sensitivity found in correlations between transverse momentum and $v_n^2$ by Giacalone (2021).
