# From nuclei to neutron stars: simple binding energy computer modelling in the classroom (part 2)

> We introduce two simple online activities to explore the physics of neutron stars. These provide an introduction to the basic properties of compact objects, like their masses and radii, for secondary school students. The first activity explores the idea of the minimum mass of a neutron star. It is directly linked to the concept of binding energy and follows on from our previous activities. The second activity focuses on the maximum mass of neutron stars using a solvable model of the neutron star interior. The activities are based on spreadsheets, provided as Supplementary Material, and can be easily adapted to different levels, age groups and discussion topics. In particular, these activities can naturally lead towards discussions on extrapolations and limits of theoretical models.

## Key claim

The paper presents two spreadsheet-based educational activities that use the liquid-drop model to extrapolate from nuclear binding energies to neutron star properties, but the authors explicitly acknowledge that the minimum mass prediction (≈0.04 M⊙) is substantially lower than the observed minimum mass (≈1.1 M⊙), and that the constant-density model for the maximum mass activity is "not physical" in its mass-radius dependence.

## Method

The first activity extends the Bethe-Weizsäcker liquid-drop mass formula to a pure neutron system (Z=0) and adds a gravitational binding energy term, then solves for the neutron number N₀ where total binding energy per particle becomes zero to find the minimum mass of a neutron star. The second activity uses the analytical Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff solution for a constant-density star in general relativity, where students input a fixed interior density ρ₀ and radius R to compute pressure profiles and find the limiting compactness C < 8/9 that yields a maximum mass.

## Key numbers

- Minimum neutron star mass: N₀ ≈ 5 × 10⁵⁵ neutrons, corresponding to M_S ≈ 0.04 M⊙ (where M⊙ = 2 × 10³⁰ kg) and radius R₀ ≈ 4 km
- Gravitational force between two protons at nuclear distance: F_g ≈ 10⁻³⁴ N, compared to electromagnetic force F_C ≈ -200 N (36 orders of magnitude difference)
- Gravitational energy of ²⁰⁸Pb: E_g ≈ 4 × 10⁻³³ MeV, compared to typical nuclear binding energies of 1-10 MeV per particle
- Density of minimum-mass object: ρ ≈ 3 × 10¹⁷ kg m⁻³, with corresponding energy density ε = ρc² ≈ 2.7 × 10³⁴ J m⁻³
- For constant-density model with ρ₀ = 10¹⁸ kg m⁻³: limiting radius R_lim ≈ 12 km and limiting mass M_lim ≈ 3.5 M⊙
- Typical neutron star compactness: C ≈ 0.4 for M = 1.4 M⊙ and R ≈ 10 km

## Context

This work extends the Binding Blocks outreach project and the liquid-drop model activities from Part 1 (Rios et al., 2020) into the astrophysical domain of neutron stars, providing educational resources that are scarce for secondary school students compared to undergraduate materials such as Balian (1999), Silbar (2004), and Jackson (2005). The activities enable discussions on model extrapolation versus interpolation, the difference between theoretical and observational limits (since the predicted minimum mass of 0.04 M⊙ is far below the observed minimum of about 1.1 M⊙ from Lattimer (2012) and Ozel (2016)), and connect to recent multimessenger astronomy including gravitational wave detections of binary neutron star mergers (GW170817, GW190425).
