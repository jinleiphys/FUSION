# New analysis method of the halo phenomenon in finite many-fermion systems. First applications to medium-mass atomic nuclei

> A new analysis method to investigate halos in finite many-fermion systems is designed, as existing characterization methods are proven to be incomplete/inaccurate. A decomposition of the internal wave-function of the {$N$-body} system in terms of overlap functions allows a model-independent analysis of medium-range and asymptotic properties of the internal one-body density. The existence of a spatially decorrelated region in the density profile is related to the existence of three typical energy scales in the excitation spectrum of the {$(N-1)$-body} system. A series of model-independent measures, taking the internal density as the only input, are introduced. The new measures allow a quantification of the potential halo in terms of the average number of fermions participating to it and of its impact on the system extension. Those new "halo factors" are validated through simulations and applied to results obtained through energy density functional calculations of medium-mass nuclei. Performing spherical Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations with state-of-the-art Skyrme plus pairing functionals, a collective halo is predicted in drip-line Cr isotopes, whereas no such effect is seen in Sn isotopes.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a new model-independent method to identify and characterize halos in finite many-fermion systems, claiming that existing characterization methods (such as the Helm model) are incomplete or inaccurate; the method is validated through simulations and applied to spherical Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations, predicting a collective halo in drip-line Cr isotopes but no such effect in Sn isotopes, though the authors note that the analysis based solely on root-mean-square radii is insufficient and can be misleading.

## Method

The authors decompose the internal wave-function of the N-body system in terms of overlap functions to analyze medium-range and asymptotic properties of the internal one-body density in a model-independent way. They introduce a series of new measures (halo factors) that take the internal density as the only input, quantifying the potential halo in terms of the average number of fermions participating in it and its impact on the system extension. These measures are validated through toy models and then applied to results from spherical Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations using the Skyrme SLy4 functional with a density-dependent delta interaction pairing scheme (REG-M) for Cr and Sn isotopes.

## Key numbers

- The two-neutron separation energy S2n drops to almost zero at N=50 for Cr isotopes, with the predicted drip-line nucleus being 80Cr
- For Sn isotopes, the predicted drip-line nucleus is 174Sn for the {SLy4+REG-M} parameter set
- The Helm model parameters R0 and sigma are adjusted using the first zero of the realistic form factor at k1 (where z1^1 approximately equals 4.49341)
- The neutron r.m.s. radius shows a significant kink at the N=50 shell closure for Cr isotopes and a weak kink at the N=82 shell closure for Sn isotopes
- The cutoff for the pairing regularization scheme is set at 60 MeV in the single-particle equivalent spectrum

## Context

This work extends previous investigations of halo phenomena in medium-mass nuclei using relativistic and non-relativistic energy density functional methods (Meng 1998, Mizutori 2000, Sandulescu 2003, Geng 2004, Kaushik 2005, Grasso 2006, Terasaki 2006), which relied on r.m.s. radii evolution or the Helm model to characterize halos. The authors demonstrate that the Helm model (originally developed by Helm 1956, Rosen 1957, Raphael 1970) produces non-zero halo parameters even for stable nuclei, making it unreliable. The new method enables model-independent quantification of halo properties and can be extended to odd and deformed systems, potentially constraining isovector density dependencies and pairing strength in low-density regimes for future radioactive beam facilities.
