# Stochastic Methods for Quantum Scattering

> Quantum scattering at zero energy is studied with stochastic methods. A path integral representation for the scattering cross section is developed. It is demonstrated that Monte Carlo simulation can be used to compare effective potentials which are frequently used in multiple scattering with the exact result.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that Monte Carlo simulation can be used to compare effective potentials (frequently used in multiple scattering) with the exact result for quantum scattering at zero energy, but only in the regime where the reference potential is already a good guess; where this is not true, results become worse (e.g., 17% deviation at one test point).

## Method

A path integral representation for the scattering cross section is developed from the density matrix in the low-temperature limit. The method compares the full projectile-target interaction with a reference effective potential by measuring a functional of the sampled paths, using a forward Euler Langevin algorithm for path sampling. The feasibility is demonstrated for potential scattering off a Gaussian potential, using a square well as the reference potential fitted to the first two nonvanishing moments.

## Key numbers

- For the Gaussian potential with strength \(V_0 = -0.5\) (where the first bound state lies at \(V_0 = -0.66\)), the cross section is \(\sigma = 274\).
- At \(\beta = 100\) with \(2.5 \times 10^5\) paths after \(2.5 \times 10^4\) equilibration updates, the stochastic calculation reproduces the exact result within 2% where the reference potential is a good guess.
- At \(V_0 = -0.5\) the stochastic result deviates about 17% from the exact result.
- The path was subdivided into 200 time intervals with \(\Delta\beta = 0.5\); the projectile mass was set to \(m = 1\).

## Context

This work extends stochastic path integral methods, already established for ground-state properties of many-body systems (Negele, year not given), to zero-energy quantum scattering. It addresses shortcomings of standard optical model potentials, such as the repulsive real part needed for kaonic atom spectra (Friedman, year not given) and the inability of optical models to calculate inclusive cross sections. The method enables nonperturbative testing of effective potentials used in multiple scattering (Foldy, year not given) and can be extended to nonzero momentum via the \(\beta\) dependence of observables, though inelastic and inclusive cross sections remain out of reach at this stage.
