# Parameterisations of thermal bomb explosions for core-collapse supernovae and 56Ni production

> Thermal bombs are a widely used method to artificially trigger explosions of core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe) to determine their nucleosynthesis or ejecta and remnant properties. Recently, their use in spherically symmetric (1D) hydrodynamic simulations led to the result that {56,57}Ni and 44Ti are massively underproduced compared to observational estimates for Supernova 1987A, if the explosions are slow, i.e., if the explosion mechanism of CCSNe releases the explosion energy on long timescales. It was concluded that rapid explosions are required to match observed abundances, i.e., the explosion mechanism must provide the CCSN energy nearly instantaneously on timescales of some ten to order 100 ms. This result, if valid, would disfavor the neutrino-heating mechanism, which releases the CCSN energy on timescales of seconds. Here, we demonstrate by 1D hydrodynamic simulations and nucleosynthetic post-processing that these conclusions are a consequence of disregarding the initial collapse of the stellar core in the thermal-bomb modelling before the bomb releases the explosion energy. We demonstrate that the anti-correlation of 56Ni yield and energy-injection timescale vanishes when the initial collapse is included and that it can even be reversed, i.e., more 56Ni is made by slower explosions, when the collapse proceeds to small radii similar to those where neutrino heating takes place in CCSNe. We also show that the 56Ni production in thermal-bomb explosions is sensitive to the chosen mass cut and that a fixed mass layer or fixed volume for the energy deposition cause only secondary differences. Moreover, we propose a most appropriate setup for thermal bombs.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that the claimed anti-correlation between 56Ni yield and energy-injection timescale in thermal-bomb supernova models vanishes when the initial collapse of the stellar core is included before the bomb releases energy, and can even reverse such that slower explosions produce more 56Ni, contradicting earlier conclusions that rapid explosions are required to match observed abundances.

## Method
The authors performed one-dimensional (1D) hydrodynamic simulations using the Prometheus-HOTB code with an Eulerian grid of 2000 zones, applying thermal-bomb energy deposition to four progenitor models with zero-age-main-sequence masses of 12.3, 19.7, 21.0, and 26.6 solar masses from the Sukhbold et al. (2014) progenitor set. They compared uncollapsed models (where the inner boundary radius was kept constant) with collapsed models (where the boundary was first contracted to mimic core collapse), varied the energy-deposition timescale, and post-processed the nucleosynthesis using the SkyNet nuclear network code.

## Key numbers
- Four progenitor models were used with ZAMS masses of 12.3, 19.7, 21.0, and 26.6 solar masses, taken from the Sukhbold et al. (2014) set
- The inner grid boundary was placed at two locations: where Ye=0.48 (deep location, "D" models) and where s/kB=4 (oxygen shell base, "O" models), with corresponding enclosed masses ranging from 1.23 to 1.38 solar masses for the Ye=0.48 location and 1.48 to 1.74 solar masses for the s/kB=4 location
- The target explosion energy was 10^51 erg, determined at t >= 80 seconds after explosion initiation
- The radial grid resolution was Delta r/R_ib = 10^-3 in the innermost cell and Delta r/r < 0.013 everywhere

## Context
This work directly addresses the conflicting results between Sukhbold et al. (2019), who found that 56Ni production decreases with longer energy-deposition timescales in uncollapsed thermal-bomb models, and Young et al. (2007), who reported increased 56Ni production with longer delay times in collapsed models. The authors show that the discrepancy arises from whether the initial core collapse is included, and propose a most appropriate setup for thermal bombs that can serve as an easy-to-implement method for systematic 1D CCSN simulations without detailed neutrino transport, while noting that such approaches cannot capture all aspects of multi-dimensional models, particularly regarding neutrino-processed inner ejecta.
