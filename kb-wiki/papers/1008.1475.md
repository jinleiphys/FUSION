# Equation of state of strongly interacting matter: spectra for thermal particles and intensity correlation of thermal photons

> We find that an equation of state for hot hadronic matter consisting of all baryons having $M < 2$ GeV and all mesons having $M < 1.5$ GeV, along with Hagedorn resonances in thermal and chemical equilibrium, matches rather smoothly with lattice equation of state (p4 action, ${N_τ}=8$) for T up to $\approx 200$ MeV, when corrected for the finite volume of hadrons. Next we construct two equations of state for strongly interacting matter; one, HHL, in which the above is matched to the lattice equation of state at $T=165$ MeV and the other, HHB, where we match it to a bag model equation of state with critical temperature $T_c=165$ MeV. We compare particle spectra, thermal photon spectra and histories of evolution of the quark-gluon plasma produced in the central collision of gold (lead) nuclei at RHIC (LHC) energies, considering ideal hydrodynamical expansion of the system. The particle and thermal photon spectra are seen to differ only marginally, for the two equations of state. The history of evolution shows differences in the evolution of temperature and radial velocity, as one might expect. We calculate intensity interferometry of thermal photons and find it to be quite distinct for the two equations of state, especially for the outward correlation. The longitudinal correlation also shows a dependence on the equation of state, though, to a smaller extent.

# Equation of state of strongly interacting matter: spectra for thermal particles and intensity correlation of thermal photons

## Key claim

A hadron resonance gas including all baryons with mass below 2 GeV and all mesons with mass below 1.5 GeV, supplemented with Hagedorn resonances and corrected for finite hadron volume, matches lattice QCD results (p4 action, N_tau=8) up to approximately 200 MeV; however, the particle spectra and thermal photon spectra from hydrodynamic evolution using either a lattice-matched equation of state (HHL) or a bag model equation of state (HHB) differ only marginally, while the outward intensity correlation of thermal photons shows a clear distinction between the two.

## Method

The authors constructed two equations of state for strongly interacting matter at zero baryonic chemical potential: HHL, which matches a volume-corrected hadron-plus-Hagedorn resonance gas to the lattice QCD equation of state at T=165 MeV, and HHB, which matches the same hadronic description to a bag model equation of state with a first-order phase transition at T_c=165 MeV. They solved ideal relativistic hydrodynamic equations for azimuthally symmetric, longitudinally boost-invariant expansion of the quark-gluon plasma produced in central Au+Au collisions at RHIC (200A GeV) and Pb+Pb collisions at LHC (5.5A TeV), using the Cooper-Frye formula for particle spectra and leading-order photon production rates from quark matter and hadronic matter.

## Key numbers

- Hadronic matter includes all mesons with mass below 1.5 GeV and all baryons with mass below 2.0 GeV, plus Hagedorn states with mass from 2 GeV to 12 GeV using density parameters A=0.5 GeV^(3/2), m_0=0.5 GeV, T_H=0.196 GeV
- Matching temperature for both equations of state: T=165 MeV
- Average initial energy density at RHIC: 80.8 GeV/fm^3 at tau_0=0.2 fm/c
- Average initial energy density at LHC: 718.3 GeV/fm^3 at tau_0=0.1 fm/c
- Freeze-out temperature: T=100 MeV
- Mixed phase energy density range for HHB: 0.4 to 2 GeV/fm^3
- Bag constant used for volume correction: B^(1/4)=340 MeV
- Photon interferometry studied for K_T less than or equal to 2 GeV
- Rapidity density at RHIC: dN_ch/dy approximately 680; at LHC: dN_ch/dy approximately 2040

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of hadron resonance gas models (Raju, 2000s) and Hagedorn states (Noronha, 2000s) by incorporating finite-volume corrections (Kapusta and Olive, 1980s) to achieve agreement with lattice QCD results (Bazavov, 2009). It builds on hydrodynamic calculations of particle spectra and thermal photon production (Srivastava, 2000s) and prior intensity interferometry proposals (Srivastava and collaborators, 1990s; Scott and Miklos, 1990s). The results enable experimental discrimination between a first-order phase transition and a rapid crossover using photon intensity interferometry, addressing the HBT puzzle at RHIC where pion interferometry showed R_out/R_side approximately 1 (puzzle references, 2000s).
