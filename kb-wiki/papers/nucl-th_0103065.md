# Shell stabilization of super- and hyperheavy nuclei without magic gaps

> Quantum stabilization of superheavy elements is quantified in terms of the shell-correction energy. We compute the shell correction using self-consistent nuclear models: the non-relativistic Skyrme-Hartree-Fock approach and the relativistic mean-field model, for a number of parametrizations. All the forces applied predict a broad valley of shell stabilization around Z=120 and N=172-184. We also predict two broad regions of shell stabilization in hyperheavy elements with N approx 258 and N approx 308. Due to the large single-particle level density, shell corrections in the superheavy elements differ markedly from those in lighter nuclei. With increasing proton and neutron numbers, the regions of nuclei stabilized by shell effects become poorly localized in particle number, and the familiar pattern of shells separated by magic gaps is basically gone.

# Shell Stabilization of Super- and Hyperheavy Nuclei Without Magic Gaps

## Key claim

The familiar pattern of sharp shell closures separated by magic gaps disappears for nuclei beyond Z=82 and N=126, replaced by broad valleys of shell stabilization that are poorly localized in particle number; however, the calculations are restricted to spherical symmetry and the shell corrections represent an upper bound, as deformation and exotic topologies could provide additional binding.

## Method

The authors computed shell-correction energies from self-consistent mean-field models using the non-relativistic Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) approach with parametrizations SkP, SLy6, SkI3, and SkI4, and the relativistic mean-field (RMF) model with parametrizations NL3 and NL-Z2. The shell energy was extracted via a Green's function approach to the level density, including single-particle states up to 50 MeV above the Fermi energy, with pairing correlations ignored and all calculations restricted to spherical symmetry.

## Key numbers

- All forces predict a broad valley of shell stabilization around Z=120 and N=172-184.
- Two broad regions of shell stabilization are predicted in hyperheavy elements at N approximately 258 and N approximately 308.
- For nuclei with Z greater than 105, the delay in spontaneous fission half-lives due to shell effects can be as much as 15 orders of magnitude (citing Muenzenberg 1988).
- The half-lives of known isotopes with Z greater than 105 decrease from 0.9 seconds for 263_157_106 to 0.2 milliseconds for 277_165_112.
- The single-particle level density grows approximately as A^(1/3) with increasing mass.

## Context

This work extends prior macroscopic-microscopic studies of shell stabilization in superheavy elements by Moeller and Nix (1994), Moeller and Nix (1992), and Smolanczuk (1997), and follows the earlier self-consistent study by Kruppa et al. (2000) which examined shell energy along selected isotopic and isotonic chains. The results serve as a starting point for subsequent studies of deformed shell effects and fission barriers in superheavy and hyperheavy nuclei, and imply that a range of beam-target combinations may reach shell-stabilized superheavy elements experimentally.
