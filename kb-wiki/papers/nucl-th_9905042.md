# Can Nuclear Decay constant be Modified ?

## Key claim

The lifetime of the first excited state of 133Cs increases by 49.4% when the absorber (CsCl) is placed between two parallel flat plates, compared to the standard value measured in free space. The author interprets this as evidence that nuclear decay constants can be modified by boundary conditions, though the proposed mechanism relies on an ad hoc model of repeated photon reflection and reabsorption.

## Method

The lifetime of the 81 keV transition from the first excited state (5/2+) to the ground state (7/2+) in 133Cs was measured using Mossbauer spectroscopy at 4.2 K. The absorber (CsCl powder) was placed between two gold-coated silicon wafer plates separated by 0.61 mm, while the gamma-ray source (Ba*TiO3) remained in free space. The experimental linewidths with and without plates were compared, and the natural linewidth modified by the plates was extracted by subtracting the known thickness and systematic broadening contributions.

## Key numbers

* Lifetime of 133Cs first excited state with plates: 9.37 ± 0.19 ns, which is 49.4% larger than the standard value of 6.27 ± 0.02 ns
* Modified natural linewidth with plates: Gamma(a) = (0.487 ± 0.010) × 10^(-7) eV
* Experimental linewidth without plates: Gamma_exp = (2.149 ± 0.038) × 10^(-7) eV (0.796 ± 0.014 mm/s)
* Experimental linewidth with plates: Gamma'_exp = (1.909 ± 0.038) × 10^(-7) eV (0.707 ± 0.014 mm/s)
* Standard natural linewidth: Gamma_nat = (0.728 ± 0.002) × 10^(-7) eV
* Absorber thickness parameter without plates: t_a = 2.388 (n = 1.075 × 10^21 cm^(-2))
* Absorber thickness parameter with plates: t'_a = 2.388 (n' = 1.613 × 10^21 cm^(-2))
* Monte Carlo simulation (GEANT3) gave reflection coefficient sigma = 0.00018 for 81 keV gamma rays from the silicon plates

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical investigations by Cheon (1991-1997) on vacuum fluctuation effects on nuclear energy levels in finite spaces, and follows reports that spontaneous emission rates for hydrogen 2P-states, Rydberg atoms (Hulet, 1985), and cyclotron radiation (Gabrielse and Dehmelt, 1985) can be modified by cavity effects. The result contradicts the long-held assumption that nuclear decay constants are invariable quantities unaffected by external electromagnetic fields or boundary conditions. The author proposes that the effect arises from reabsorption of reflected gamma rays by the emitting nucleus, though the 81 keV gamma-ray wavelength (0.015 nm) makes reflection from silicon plates extremely inefficient, and the quantitative model requires an ad hoc number of reflections (n approximately 1540-2200) to match the observed lifetime change.
