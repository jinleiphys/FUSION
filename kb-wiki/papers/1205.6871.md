# The Neutron Star Mass-Radius Relation and the Equation of State of Dense Matter

> The equation of state (EOS) of dense matter has been a long-sought goal of nuclear physics. Equations of state generate unique mass versus radius (M-R) relations for neutron stars, the ultra-dense remnants of stellar evolution. In this work, we determine the neutron star mass-radius relation and, based on recent observations of both transiently accreting and bursting sources, we show that the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star lies between 10.4 and 12.9 km, independent of assumptions about the composition of the core. We show, for the first time, that these constraints remain valid upon removal from our sample of the most extreme transient sources or of the entire set of bursting sources; our constraints also apply even if deconfined quark matter exists in the neutron star core. Our results significantly constrain the dense matter EOS and are, furthermore, consistent with constraints from both heavy-ion collisions and theoretical studies of neutron matter. We predict a relatively weak dependence of the symmetry energy on the density and a value for the neutron skin thickness of lead which is less than 0.20 fm, results that are testable in forthcoming experiments.

# The Neutron Star Mass-Radius Relation and the Equation of State of Dense Matter

## Key claim
The radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star lies between 10.4 and 12.9 km (95% confidence) when all variations in the equation of state model and data interpretation are considered, but this range expands to 9.17-13.92 km under more speculative scenarios such as anomalously small color correction factors or inclusion of additional burst sources. The constraints are claimed to be independent of core composition assumptions, including the possible existence of deconfined quark matter.

## Method
The authors performed a Bayesian analysis using Markov Chain Monte Carlo marginal estimation to determine posterior probability densities for neutron star mass-radius relations. They employed five different equation of state models: two piecewise polytropic models (A and B), a model with uniform priors in pressure at fixed energy densities (C), a model allowing a quark matter phase at high densities (D), and a bare strange quark star model (E). The analysis incorporated eight neutron stars: four photospheric radius expansion (PRE) burst sources and four quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries (qLMXBs), with multiple modifications to the data interpretation including variations in color correction factors and removal of individual sources or source classes.

## Key numbers
- Radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: 11.2 to 12.3 km (95% confidence) for the baseline model
- Global limits encompassing all EOS and data interpretation variations: 10.4 to 12.9 km (95% confidence)
- Under the most extreme assumptions (model C with modification II): radii as small as 9.17 km (95% lower limit)
- Maximum mass constraint used: 1.97 +/- 0.04 solar masses for PSR J1614-2230
- Symmetry energy parameter L: 43.3 to 66.5 MeV (68% confidence), 41.1 to 83.4 MeV (95% confidence)
- Neutron skin thickness of lead-208: less than 0.20 fm
- Highest central energy density of maximum mass star: approximately 1200 MeV/fm^3

## Context
This work extends previous constraints from Ozel et al. (2010), which considered only PRE sources, and Steiner et al. (2010), which used a smaller data set, by demonstrating that the mass-radius constraints remain valid when removing extreme transient sources or all bursting sources. The results are consistent with effective field theory calculations of neutron matter from Hebeler et al. (2010), quantum Monte Carlo calculations from Gandolfi et al. (2012), and heavy-ion collision constraints from Danielewicz et al. (2002). The analysis rules out over one third of the Skyrme models studied by Stone et al. (2003) and covariant field-theoretical models like NL3 from Lalazissis et al. (1997). The predicted neutron skin thickness of lead is testable in forthcoming parity-violating electron scattering experiments.
