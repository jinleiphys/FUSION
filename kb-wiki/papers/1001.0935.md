# Quarkonium above deconfinement as an open quantum system

> Quarkonium at temperatures above deconfinement is modeled as an open quantum system, whose dynamics is determined not just by a potential energy and mass, but also by a drag coefficient which characterizes its interaction with the medium. The reduced density matrix for a heavy particle experiencing dissipative forces is expressed as an integral over paths in imaginary time and evaluated numerically. We demonstrate that dissipation could affect the Euclidean heavy-heavy correlators calculated in lattice simulations at temperatures just above deconfinement.

# Quarkonium above deconfinement as an open quantum system

## Key claim
Dissipative effects from the medium, characterized by a drag coefficient, can change the Euclidean heavy-heavy correlators calculated in lattice simulations at temperatures just above deconfinement, but the paper only identifies this effect qualitatively and does not yet provide a full spectral analysis suitable for direct comparison with lattice data.

## Method
The authors model quarkonium as an open quantum system by applying the Caldeira-Leggett approach to imaginary time, deriving a path-integral expression for the reduced density matrix of a heavy particle interacting with a bath of harmonic oscillators. They isolate divergences through integration by parts to make the functional integral finite and suitable for path-integral Monte Carlo simulation, then compute the Euclidean correlator for the vector channel at T=1.2 T_c using the Cornell potential and a representative drag coefficient.

## Key numbers
- Temperature: T = 1.2 T_c, where T_c is the deconfinement transition temperature.
- Drag coefficient: η = 0.1 GeV^{-2} (representative value chosen for the calculation).
- The effective mass of the heavy quark is given by M_eff = M + (ηβ/π)[log(ηβ/(2πM)) - Ψ(1 + ηβ/(2πM))], where Ψ is the digamma function and β = 1/T.

## Context
This work extends the Brownian motion model for charmonium in heavy-ion collisions introduced by Young and Shuryak (2008), which showed that survival of J/ψ states depends on multiple interactions with the medium and collision time scales. It also addresses newer lattice calculations by Jakovac (2006) and the comparison of correlators with potential models by Mocsy and Petreczky (2007). The paper contrasts with the HTL-based approach of Beraudo et al. (2009) by focusing on strong coupling as suggested by gauge-gravity duality. The next steps required include applying the maximum entropy method to extract spectral functions and incorporating zero-mode contributions for comparison with lattice data.
