# Rescattering of Vector Meson Daughters in High Energy Heavy Ion Collisions

> We consider the role of hadronic rescattering of daughter kaons on the observed mass spectra from $ϕ$ meson decays in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions. A hadronic cascade code (RQMD v2.4) shows that $\sim$26% of all $ϕ$'s decaying to $K^+K^-$ in central Pb+Pb collisions at SPS energies ($E_{beam} = 158 GeV/A$) have a rescattered or absorbed daughter. This significantly affects the reconstructed invariant mass of the pair and shifts $ϕ$ mesons out of the mass peak. Kaon rescattering depletes the low velocity region, hardening and broadening the observed phi $m_t$ and rapidity distributions respectively, relative to the dilepton channel. This effect produces an apparent change in the experimentally determined branching ratio not necessarily related to chiral symmetry restoration. Comparisons to recent experimental measures at CERN energies reveal a possible mechanism to account for the shape of the observed spectra, though not their absolute relative magnitude.

## Key claim

Hadronic rescattering of daughter kaons from phi meson decays in central Pb+Pb collisions at SPS energies depletes the low transverse mass region and broadens the rapidity distribution, producing an apparent change in the experimentally determined branching ratio that is not necessarily related to chiral symmetry restoration; however, this effect alone cannot account for the absolute magnitude of the observed difference between the dilepton and di-kaon spectra.

## Method

The authors used the hadronic cascade code RQMD v2.4 to simulate central Pb+Pb collisions at 158 GeV per nucleon beam energy, tracking all phi mesons that decay to K+K- pairs and following the subsequent rescattering or absorption of the daughter kaons through the hadronic medium. They compared the reconstructed invariant mass, transverse mass, and rapidity distributions of phi mesons whose daughters escaped unscathed versus those whose daughters rescattered or were absorbed, and compared these results to experimental data from the NA50 (dilepton) and NA49 (di-kaon) collaborations at CERN.

## Key numbers

- Approximately 26% of all phi mesons decaying to K+K- in central Pb+Pb collisions at SPS energies have a rescattered or absorbed daughter
- Approximately 17% of decaying phi mesons have at least one daughter kaon that rescatters, and a separate (non-orthogonal) 17% have at least one daughter that is absorbed
- The invariant mass peak of phi mesons with non-rescattered daughters has a width of approximately 4 MeV
- The inverse slope of the transverse mass distribution for all phi mesons in RQMD is T = 220 ± 3 MeV, while for phi mesons observed through the kaon decay channel it is T = 242 ± 4 MeV
- The survival probability of both daughter kaons approaches approximately 60% at the lowest transverse mass
- The freeze-out volume for kaons corresponds to longitudinal proper time tau ≤ 36.5 fm and radial position r ≤ 20 fm
- NA50 reports an inverse slope of T = 218 ± 10 MeV for phi → mu+mu-, while NA49 finds T = 305 ± 15 MeV for phi → K+K-
- RQMD predicts a 17 MeV difference in slope between the two channels, much smaller than the observed difference
- The gaussian fit to the RQMD rapidity distribution for measured phi → K+K- gives sigma = 0.96 ± 0.1 and amplitude A = 2.45 ± 0.46, compared to the experimental fit of sigma = 1.22 ± 0.17 and A = 2.43 ± 0.15

## Context

This work extends studies by Lissauer and Shuryak (1991) and Seibert and Gale (1995) on the viability of the di-kaon channel for studying chiral symmetry restoration, by explicitly considering the previously neglected effect of hadronic rescattering of daughter kaons on the experimentally measured branching ratio. The results contradict the interpretation that differences between the dilepton and di-kaon phi meson spectra necessarily indicate chiral symmetry restoration, and instead suggest that measuring the ratio of the transverse mass spectra from both channels over the same kinematic range could help determine the time the hadronic system spends in the dense phase before freeze-out, as discussed by Stock (1999) and the WA98 Collaboration (1999).
