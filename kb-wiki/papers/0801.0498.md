# S-matrix approach to equation of state of nuclear matter

> We calculate the equation of state of nuclear matter based on the general analysis of the grand canonical partition function in the $S$-matrix framework. In addition to the low mass stable particles and their two-body scattering channels considered earlier, the calculation includes systematically all the higher mass particles and their exited states as well as the scattering channels formed by any number of these species. We estimate the latter contribution by resonances in all the channels. The resulting model-independent virial series for pressure gets substantial contribution from the heavy particles and the channels containing them. The series converges for larger values of baryon density than found earlier.

# S-matrix approach to equation of state of nuclear matter

## Key claim

The virial series for pressure of nuclear matter, derived from the complete S-matrix formulation of statistical mechanics, converges for larger baryon densities than found in earlier virial calculations (Horowitz and Schwenk, 2005) because it systematically includes all higher-mass nuclei, their excited states, and scattering channels formed by any number of these species, with heavy-channel contributions approximated by resonances.

## Method

The authors start from the Dashen-Ma-Bernstein S-matrix formulation of the grand canonical partition function for nuclear matter, treating protons and neutrons as elementary species. They decompose the partition function into contributions from stable single-particle states (all nuclei in ground and excited states) and from multiparticle scattering channels; the latter are divided into light channels (two-body NN, N-alpha, and alpha-alpha scattering, evaluated using experimental phase shifts) and heavy channels (containing at least one nucleus with A >= 8), where the S-matrix elements are approximated by narrow resonances near threshold, yielding additional ideal-gas terms. The level density of excited states and resonances is taken from the Fermi gas model with empirical parameter a = A/8 MeV^{-1}, and binding energies for approximately 9000 nuclei up to Z=135 are taken from Myers (1977).

## Key numbers

- The level density parameter for excited states of nuclei is taken as a = A/8 MeV^{-1}.
- The lower integration limit for excited states is E_0 = 2 MeV; the upper limit for excited states is E_s = 8 MeV; the resonance region extends to E_r = 12 MeV.
- The effective binding energy per nucleon for convergence estimates is b = 8.5 MeV for moderately large nuclei.
- The virial series converges for fugacities ze < (1/2) exp(-beta b), which is half the radius of convergence estimated without excited states.
- For fugacities and densities considered, nuclei heavier than A = 15 do not play any significant role.
- The contribution from light scattering channels is about an order of magnitude smaller than that from the ideal gas terms.

## Context

This work extends the Beth-Uhlenbeck approach to the second virial coefficient and the Dashen-Ma-Bernstein (1969) complete S-matrix formulation of statistical mechanics to nuclear matter, going beyond the calculation of Horowitz and Schwenk (2005) who included only the alpha particle as a heavy species alongside protons, neutrons, and their two-body scattering channels. The results are compared to the microscopic variational calculation of Pandharipande (1979) at T = 10 MeV. The approach enables calculation of the equation of state at moderate temperatures (2-10 MeV) up to higher densities than previously possible in a model-independent way, and can be extended to asymmetric systems, to include pions at higher temperatures, and to calculate relative abundances of nuclei and energy density.
