# Dark Matter Studies Entrain Nuclear Physics

> We review theoretically well-motivated dark-matter candidates, and pathways to their discovery, in the light of recent results from collider physics, astrophysics, and cosmology. Taken in aggregate, these encourage broader thinking in regards to possible dark-matter candidates --- dark-matter need not be made of "WIMPs," i.e., elementary particles with weak-scale masses and interactions. Facilities dedicated to nuclear physics are well-poised to investigate certain non-WIMP models. In parallel to this, developments in observational cosmology permit probes of the relativistic energy density at early epochs and thus provide new ways to constrain dark-matter models, provided nuclear physics inputs are sufficiently well-known. The emerging confluence of accelerator, astrophysical, and cosmological constraints permit searches for dark-matter candidates in a greater range of masses and interaction strengths than heretofore possible.

## Key claim

The paper argues that recent experimental results from collider physics, astrophysics, and cosmology encourage broader thinking about dark-matter candidates beyond the standard WIMP (Weakly Interacting Massive Particle) paradigm, with nuclear physics facilities well-positioned to investigate certain non-WIMP models, though the authors note that dark matter may not be comprised of particles at all.

## Method

The authors review theoretically well-motivated dark-matter candidates including supersymmetric models, hidden sector models with internal gauge symmetries, asymmetric dark-matter models, and sterile neutrino models, selecting topics that connect to facilities and expertise in nuclear physics. They examine how cosmological constraints from the cosmic microwave background, big-bang nucleosynthesis, and large-scale structure can probe dark-matter properties, and discuss how terrestrial measurements of neutron lifetime and nuclear reaction cross sections are essential for interpreting these cosmological tests.

## Key numbers

- Dark matter comprises approximately 23 percent of the energy density of the universe today, with a precision of a couple of percent
- The baryon rest mass contribution to closure from WMAP9 CMB data yields Omega_b = 0.0463 ± 0.0024
- The neutron lifetime assessment shifted from tau_n = 885.7 ± 0.8 s to tau_n = 880.1 ± 1.1 s, yielding a reduction in the He-4/H abundance of O(0.001)
- The baryon-to-photon ratio from CMB is eta approximately 6.11 × 10^{-10}, with Planck promising ±0.74% precision
- Standard model physics predicts N_eff approximately 3.046(1) for the relativistic energy density parameter
- South Pole Telescope reports N_eff = 3.71 ± 0.35, WMAP9 reports N_eff = 3.84 ± 0.40, Atacama Cosmology Telescope reports N_eff = 2.78 ± 0.55
- A nonresonantly produced sterile neutrino candidate must have mass M > 12.1 keV at Bayesian 95% confidence interval from Lyman-alpha constraints
- Cold dark-matter particle mass must exceed O(2 GeV) to avoid closing the Universe under standard thermal relic assumptions
- The curvature parameter Omega_k = -0.001 ± 0.012 from CMB data alone

## Context

This review extends the extensive literature on WIMP dark-matter models, noting recent reviews by Bertone (2004), Gaitskell (2004), Jaeckel (2010), and Feng (2010), while responding to the 2012 discovery of a Higgs-like particle at 125 GeV and strengthening limits on superpartner masses. The paper connects nuclear physics facilities like JLab and MAMI to hidden sector dark-matter searches, and discusses how precision cosmology from Planck and future CMB polarization experiments will enable new constraints on dark-matter models, provided nuclear physics inputs such as the neutron lifetime and BBN reaction cross sections are sufficiently well-known.
