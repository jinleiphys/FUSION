# Application of the microscopic optical potential of chiral effective field theory in astrophysical neutron-capture reactions

> A state-of-the-art microscopic global nucleon-nucleus optical potential has been developed by Whitehead, Lim, and Holt (WLH) within the framework of many-body perturbation theory, incorporating realistic nuclear interactions derived from chiral effective field theory. Given its potentially greater predictive power for reactions involving exotic isotopes, we apply it to the calculations of astrophysical neutron-capture reactions for the first time, which are particularly important to the nucleosynthesis of elements heavier than iron. It is found that this potential provides a good description of experimental known neutron-capture cross sections and Maxwellian-averaged cross sections. For unstable neutron-rich nuclei, we comprehensively calculate the neutron-capture reaction rates for all nuclei with $26\leq Z\leq84$, located between the valley of stability and the neutron drip line, using the backward-forward Monte Carlo method with the $f_{rms}$ deviation as the $χ^2$ estimator. The results reveal a noticeable separation in the uncertainty of rates around an isospin asymmetry of 0.28 under the constraint $f_{rms} \leq 1.56$. This highlights the critical role of isospin dependence in optical potentials and suggests that future developments of the WLH potential may pay special attention to the isospin dependence.

## Key claim

The microscopic optical potential of Whitehead, Lim, and Holt (WLH), derived from chiral effective field theory without fitting to nucleon-nucleus scattering data, provides a good description of experimental neutron-capture cross sections and Maxwellian-averaged cross sections, though not with greater accuracy than the phenomenological Koning-Delaroche (KD) potential; a noticeable separation in the uncertainty of calculated reaction rates occurs around an isospin asymmetry of 0.28 under the constraint f_rms ≤ 1.56.

## Method

The authors applied the WLH microscopic global nucleon-nucleus optical potential within the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model using the reaction code TALYS, sampling 400 parameter sets from a multivariate normal distribution derived from five chiral interactions. They compared calculated neutron-capture cross sections and Maxwellian-averaged cross sections (MACS) at 30 keV against experimental data from the KADoNiS database for 221 nuclei with 26 ≤ Z ≤ 83, using the f_rms deviation as a χ² estimator. For neutron-rich nuclei, they employed the backward-forward Monte Carlo method to propagate parameter uncertainties and calculated neutron-capture rates for all nuclei with 26 ≤ Z ≤ 84 between the valley of stability and the neutron drip line.

## Key numbers

- For 221 nuclei with 26 ≤ Z ≤ 83, the MACS values calculated using the WLH potential at 30 keV generally deviate from KADoNiS experimental data within a factor of two.
- The f_rms deviations for the 400 sampled parameter sets reach up to 2.16, with most values below 1.74.
- Under the constraint f_rms ≤ 1.56, the uncertainty range of the WLH potential effectively encompasses the theoretical results of the KD potential.
- For 150Sn, differences in reaction rates reach up to five orders of magnitude across different f_rms_crit values (1.50, 1.56, and 1.74).
- A noticeable separation in uncertainty occurs around an isospin asymmetry δ = (N-Z)/A of 0.28: below this value the uncertainty range remains within one order of magnitude, above it mostly exceeds three orders of magnitude.
- Nearly half of the parameter sets have f'_rms deviations below 2.1 with respect to 298 experimental neutron strength functions.

## Context

This work extends the application of the microscopic global optical potential developed by Whitehead, Lim, and Holt (Whitehead 2021) from elastic scattering, charge-exchange reactions, and transfer reactions to astrophysical neutron-capture reactions for the first time. The results are compared with the widely used phenomenological potential of Koning and Delaroche (Koning 2003), showing consistency in magnitude and trend. The observed isospin dependence corroborates earlier suggestions by Goriely (2007) about the impact of the isovector imaginary component of the optical potential on neutron capture for neutron-rich nuclei, and the work enables future systematic studies of neutron-capture rates for r-process nucleosynthesis calculations, particularly for nuclei far from stability where experimental data are unavailable.
