# Spectator matter fragmentation in Au+Au reactions: Phase space analysis

> Clusterization in phase space has been analyzed for peripheral Au+Au reactions at 1000 AMeV using simulated annealing clusterization algorithm (SACA). We investigate how these fragments are correlated in phase space and compare our model calculations with minimum spanning tree (MST) method. Our theoretical study highlights the importance of binding energy criterion in recognizing the fragment structure. MST method however, fails to break-up the spectator matter effectively and thus under-estimates the multiplicity of intermediate mass fragments (IMFs).

## Key claim

The paper argues that the simulated annealing clusterization algorithm (SACA), which applies a binding energy criterion, is superior to the minimum spanning tree (MST) method for correctly identifying intermediate mass fragments from spectator matter break-up in peripheral Au+Au collisions, but only for the single simulated event at one impact parameter and energy.

## Method

The phase space of nucleons was recorded at different time steps using quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) transport theory for a single event of 197Au+197Au collision at 1000 AMeV with impact parameter b=8 fm, using a soft equation of state (K=200 MeV) and energy-dependent nucleon-nucleon cross section. The nucleon phase space was then clusterized using both the minimum spanning tree (MST) method and the simulated annealing clusterization algorithm (SACA), which applies a binding energy cut of -4.0 MeV per nucleon for fragments with three or more nucleons.

## Key numbers

- Incident energy: 1000 AMeV for 197Au+197Au collision
- Impact parameter: b = 8 fm
- Binding energy cut: E_bind = 4.0 MeV per nucleon for fragments with N_f ≥ 3; E_bind = 0 for N_f < 3
- Intermediate mass fragment (IMF) definition: 5 ≤ A ≤ 65
- SACA analysis performed at 60 fm/c; MST analysis performed at 200 fm/c
- MST method produced only a single cluster of mass A = 5 in the backward hemisphere

## Context

This work extends the earlier development of the SACA algorithm by Puri and collaborators (2000-2010) for cluster recognition in heavy-ion collisions, which had previously reproduced the universal "rise and fall" pattern of intermediate mass fragment multiplicities. The study directly compares SACA with the simpler minimum spanning tree (MST) method and finds that MST underestimates fragment multiplicities compared to ALADiN experimental data. The results suggest that the binding energy criterion is essential for correctly identifying spectator matter fragments, but the single-event simulation limits the statistical significance of the conclusions.
