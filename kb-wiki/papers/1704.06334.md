# Assessing theoretical uncertainties in fission barriers of superheavy nuclei

> Theoretical uncertainties in the predictions of inner fission barrier heights in superheavy elements have been investigated in a systematic way for a set of state-of-the-art covariant energy density functionals which represent major classes of the functionals used in covariant density functional theory. They differ in basic model assumptions and fitting protocols. Both systematic and statistical uncertainties have been quantified where the former turn out to be larger. Systematic uncertainties are substantial in superheavy elements and their behavior as a function of proton and neutron numbers contains a large random component. The benchmarking of the functionals to the experimental data on fission barriers in the actinides allows to reduce the systematic theoretical uncertainties for the inner fission barriers of unknown superheavy elements. However, even then they on average increase on moving away from the region where benchmarking has been performed. In addition, a comparison with the results of non-relativistic approaches is performed in order to define full systematic theoretical uncertainties over the state-of-the-art models. Even for the models benchmarked in the actinides, the difference in the inner fission barrier height of some superheavy elements reaches $5-6$ MeV. This uncertainty in the fission barrier heights will translate into huge (many tens of the orders of magnitude) uncertainties in the spontaneous fission half-lives.

## Key claim

Even after benchmarking covariant density functionals against experimental fission barriers in the actinides, the difference in predicted inner fission barrier heights for some superheavy elements reaches 5-6 MeV, which translates into uncertainties of many tens of orders of magnitude in spontaneous fission half-lives.

## Method

The authors performed axially symmetric relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations for even-even nuclei from Z=96 to Z=126 and from the proton drip line up to N=196, using five covariant energy density functionals (NL3*, DD-ME2, DD-MEδ, DD-PC1, and PC-PK1) that represent the major classes of functionals in covariant density functional theory. Systematic uncertainties were quantified as the spread between the highest and lowest predicted inner fission barrier heights across the functional set, while statistical uncertainties were evaluated for the ²⁹⁶112 nucleus using 500 randomly selected functional variations from a set defined by a χ² condition. Triaxial RHB calculations were additionally performed for selected Z=112-120 nuclei to study saddle point energies.

## Key numbers

- The spread in inner fission barrier heights across all five functionals reaches 3.5 to 5.5 MeV in the Z=112-120, N=170-186 region
- After benchmarking to actinide data (using only NL3*, DD-PC1, and PC-PK1), theoretical uncertainties for N≤180 nuclei are typically reduced to less than 2 MeV, with isolated nuclei around Z=110, N~164 and Z~110, N~176 showing uncertainties up to 4 and 5.5 MeV respectively
- For the ²⁹⁶112 nucleus, the statistical uncertainty (standard deviation σ_E) at the inner saddle is close to 0.7 MeV, while at the superdeformed minimum and outer saddle it is close to 0.5 MeV
- In the actinides (Z≤100, N≤164), the spreads in inner fission barrier heights are typically smaller than 2.5 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous systematic studies of theoretical uncertainties in ground state properties within covariant density functional theory by Agbemava, Afanasjev, and collaborators (2013-2016) to excited states, specifically fission barriers in superheavy nuclei. It builds on earlier triaxial RMF+BCS calculations of fission barriers in Z=112-120 nuclei using NL3* by Afanasjev et al. (2012) and provides the first systematic analysis of both systematic and statistical uncertainties for fission barriers within the CDFT framework. The results enable identification of which aspects of many-body physics can be predicted with higher confidence and indicate where next-generation energy density functionals need improvement, particularly regarding the large random component in systematic uncertainties as a function of proton and neutron numbers.
