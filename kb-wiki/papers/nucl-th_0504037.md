# Geometrical aspects of isoscaling

> The property of isoscaling in nuclear fragmentation is studied using a simple bond percolation model with ``isospin'' added as an extra degree of freedom. It is shown analytically, first, that isoscaling is expected to exist in such a simple model with the only assumption of fair sampling with homogeneous probabilities. Second, numerical percolations of hundreds of thousands of grids of different sizes and with different $N$ to $Z$ ratios confirm this prediction with remarkable agreement. It is thus concluded that isoscaling emerges from the simple assumption of fair sampling with homogeneous probabilities, a requirement which, if put in the nomenclature of the minimum information theory, translates simply into the existence of equiprobable configurations in maximum entropy states.

## Key claim

Isoscaling emerges from the simple assumption of fair sampling with homogeneous probabilities in a bond percolation model, without requiring quantum effects, thermodynamic equilibrium, or specific nuclear interactions; the isoscaling property itself is a geometric-statistical effect, and only the absolute values of the parameters alpha and beta carry information about physical effects such as the asymmetry term in the equation of state.

## Method

The authors used a three-dimensional bond percolation model with isospin added as an extra degree of freedom, where nodes on a cubic lattice represent nucleons and bonds between nearest neighbors are broken with a homogeneous probability b. They derived analytic expressions for the isoscaling ratio R_21 in the infinite-size limit, then performed numerical percolations of hundreds of thousands of grids of sizes 5x5x5, 6x6x6, and 7x7x7 with different fixed proton numbers or fixed proton/neutron occupancy probabilities, using a bond breaking probability b=0.31.

## Key numbers

- For a 6x6x6 grid (A=216) with fixed Z=108 and a 7x7x7 grid (A=343) with Z=108: alpha = 0.315, beta = -0.463, C = 1.5883, from 200,000 percolations of each grid.
- For 6x6x6 grids with p1=0.5, q1=0.5 and p2=0.33, q2=0.67: alpha = 0.29267, beta = -0.41552, from 200,000 configurations.
- For 5x5x5 grids with the same probabilities (p1=0.5, q1=0.5 and p2=0.33, q2=0.67): isoscaling was also observed.
- For 6x6x6 grids with p1=0.5, q1=0.5 and p2=0.42, q2=0.58: alpha = 0.14842, beta = -0.17435, from 200,000 configurations.

## Context

This work extends the findings of Dorso et al. (2005) that isoscaling exists in purely classical systems out of equilibrium, by showing it arises in an even simpler bond percolation model with no dynamics or interactions. It contradicts the implicit assumption in earlier studies by Tsang et al. (2001), Botvina et al., and others that isoscaling necessarily reflects the symmetry term of the nuclear binding energy or specific thermodynamic conditions; instead, the authors argue that the isoscaling property itself is a minimum-information, maximum-entropy effect, and only the absolute values of alpha and beta contain physical information. This enables future work to focus on extracting meaningful physics from the isoscaling parameters rather than from the existence of the scaling relation itself.
