# Realistic shell-model calculations and exotic nuclei

> This paper presents a short overview of the shell-model approach with realistic effective interactions to the study of exotic nuclei. We first give a sketch of the current state of the art of the theoretical framework of this approach, focusing on the main ingredients and most relevant recent advances. Then, we present some selected results for neutron-rich nuclei in various mass regions, namely oxygen isotopes, $N=40$ isotones, and nuclei around $^{132}$Sn, to show the merit as well as the limits of these calculations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that realistic shell-model calculations using effective interactions derived from free nucleon-nucleon potentials can successfully describe many properties of neutron-rich exotic nuclei, but the approach has clear limits: it fails to reproduce ground-state energies of oxygen isotopes when using theoretical single-particle energies, likely due to the omission of three-body forces, while results for heavier regions like N=40 isotones and nuclei around 132Sn are generally accurate without needing explicit three-body terms.

## Method

The authors derive shell-model effective Hamiltonians from bare nucleon-nucleon potentials (CD-Bonn and N3LOW) using a perturbative approach based on the Q-box folded-diagram expansion, including diagrams up to second or third order. The effective interactions are constructed for a two-valence-particle nucleus and then applied to systems with more valence particles, using either theoretical or experimental single-particle energies. Calculations are performed for oxygen isotopes in the sd shell with 16O as core, for N=40 isotones with 48Ca as core using two different model spaces, and for nuclei around 132Sn with 132Sn as closed core.

## Key numbers

- For oxygen isotopes, the calculated ground-state energies overestimate experimental values; an upshift of 427 keV to the calculated single-particle spectrum (to reproduce the experimental ground-state energy of 17O relative to 16O) brings the curve closer to experiment and shows the correct slope from N=16 onward.
- For N=40 isotones, the inclusion of the neutron 1d5/2 orbital in the model space is essential for quantitative agreement with experimental B(E2) values: for 64Cr and 66Fe, model space (I) significantly underestimates the B(E2) while model space (II) does not.
- For 68Ni, only 0.05 neutrons occupy the 1d5/2 orbital in model space (II), indicating its minor role in that nucleus.
- For the (νf7/2)2 configuration in the 132Sn region, the J=0+ matrix element is about -0.6 MeV, compared to -1 MeV or less for the (νh11/2)-2 and (πg7/2)2 configurations.
- Calculated B(E2; 2+1 → 0+1) values for Sn and Te isotopes: 130Sn 1.4 W.u. (expt 1.2(3)), 134Sn 1.6 W.u. (expt 1.4(2)), 132Te 7.8 W.u. (expt 10(1)), 134Te 4.9 W.u. (expt 5.6(6)), 136Te 9.9 W.u. (expt 5.9(9)).
- The odd-even staggering of binding energies shows a drop of about 0.5 MeV in the observed OES for Sn when crossing N=82.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous realistic shell-model calculations for neutron-rich nuclei (Coraggio 2007, 2011, 2013, 2014) and provides a unified overview of results across three mass regions. The oxygen isotope results are compared with calculations by Otsuka (2010), Hagen (2012), and Hergert (2013) that suggest three-body forces explain the neutron drip line at N=16. The N=40 results connect to experimental and theoretical work on collectivity by Lenzi (2010), Rother (2011), Baugher (2012), and Crawford (2013). The 132Sn region results build on studies by Terasaki (2002) and Shimizu (2004) regarding the role of pairing forces. The paper enables future work on clarifying the role of three-body forces in the one-body effective Hamiltonian and on extending calculations to more valence neutrons in the 132Sn region where third-order Q-box effects may become important.
