# Transport coefficients of two-flavor quark matter from the Kubo formalism

> The transport coefficients of quark matter at non-zero chemical potential and temperature are computed within the two-flavor Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model. We apply the Kubo formalism to obtain the thermal ($κ$) and electrical ($σ$) conductivities as well as an update of the shear viscosity ($η$) by evaluating the corresponding equilibrium two-point correlation functions to leading order in the $1/N_c$ expansion. The Dirac structure of the self-energies and spectral functions is taken into account as these are evaluated from the meson-exchange Fock diagrams for on-mass-shell quarks. We find that the thermal and electrical conductivities are decreasing functions of temperature and density above the Mott temperature $T_{\rm M}$ of dissolution of mesons into quarks, the main contributions being generated by the temporal and vector components of the spectral functions. The coefficients show a universal dependence on the ratio $T/T_{\rm M}$ for different densities, i.e., the results differ by a chemical-potential dependent constant. We also show that the Wiedemann-Franz law for the ratio $σ/κ$ does not hold. The ratio $η/s $, where $s$ is the entropy density, is of order of unity (or larger) close to the Mott temperature and, as the temperature increases, approaches the AdS/CFT bound $1/4π$. It is also conjectured that the ratio $κT/c_V $, with $c_V$ being the specific heat, is bounded from below by $1/18$.

## Key claim

The thermal and electrical conductivities of two-flavor quark matter, computed within the Nambu–Jona-Lasinio model using the Kubo formalism, are decreasing functions of temperature and density above the Mott temperature \(T_{\rm M}\), and show a universal dependence on the ratio \(T/T_{\rm M}\) that differs only by a chemical-potential-dependent constant; the Wiedemann-Franz law does not hold, and the ratio \(\eta/s\) approaches the AdS/CFT bound \(1/4\pi\) as temperature increases.

## Method

The authors applied the Kubo-Zubarev formalism to compute the thermal conductivity (\(\kappa\)), electrical conductivity (\(\sigma\)), and shear viscosity (\(\eta\)) of two-flavor quark matter described by the Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model. The equilibrium two-point correlation functions were evaluated to leading order in the \(1/N_c\) expansion, which reduces the calculation to a single-loop diagram with fully dressed quark propagators. The Dirac structure of the quark self-energies and spectral functions was taken into account, with self-energies obtained from meson-exchange Fock diagrams for on-mass-shell quarks. The final expressions for \(\kappa\) and \(\sigma\) were derived in terms of the Lorentz components of the quark spectral function, and the shear viscosity was updated using the same formalism.

## Key numbers

- The NJL model parameters used: current quark mass \(m_0 = 5.5\) MeV, effective four-fermion coupling \(G = 10.1\) GeV\(^{-2}\), and ultraviolet cutoff \(\Lambda = 650\) MeV.
- The ratio \(\eta/s\) is of order unity (or larger) close to the Mott temperature \(T_{\rm M}\) and approaches the AdS/CFT bound \(1/4\pi\) as temperature increases.
- It is conjectured that the ratio \(\kappa T / c_V\) is bounded from below by \(1/18\).
- The electrical conductivity \(\sigma\) is proportional to \(\frac{40 N_c \alpha}{27 \pi^2}\) times an integral over the spectral function, where \(\alpha = 1/137\) is the fine-structure constant.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of transport coefficients in quark matter using the NJL model and the Kubo formalism, particularly the shear viscosity calculations by Iwasaki et al. (2008), Aouissat et al. (2008), and Lang et al. (2014, 2015). It provides the first computation of thermal and electrical conductivities within this framework, including the full Dirac structure of the spectral functions. The results are relevant for hydrodynamical modeling of the quark-gluon plasma in heavy-ion collisions and for astrophysical applications in compact stars, though the authors note that extension to the dense, cold regime would require accounting for nonzero isospin, color superconductivity, and leptons.
