# Sequential bottomonium production at high temperatures

> Bottomonium production in heavy ion collisions is modified compared with any simple extrapolation from elementary collisions. This modification is most likely caused by the presence of a deconfined system of quarks and gluons for times of several fm/c. In such a medium, bottomonium can be destroyed, but the constituent bottom quarks will likely stay spatially correlated due to small mean free paths in this system. With these facts in mind, we describe bottomonium formation with a coupled set of equations. A rate equation describes the destruction of $Υ(1S)$ particles, while a Langevin equation describes how the bottom quarks stay correlated for a sufficiently long time so that recombination into bottomonia is possible. We show that within this approach it is possible to understand the magnitude of $Υ(1S)$ suppression in heavy ion collisions and the larger suppression of the $Υ(2S)$ state, implying that the reduction in the ratio of $Υ(1S)/Υ(2S)$ yield in heavy ion collision does not necessarily correspond to sequential melting picture.

# Sequential bottomonium production at high temperatures

## Key claim

The observed suppression of $\Upsilon(2S)$ relative to $\Upsilon(1S)$ in heavy ion collisions can be explained by a sequential pattern of bottomonium *formation* according to bound state size, rather than requiring the sequential melting picture; the authors show this using a coupled rate equation for $\Upsilon(1S)$ destruction and Langevin dynamics for correlated $b\bar b$ pairs, but note their study is simplified to a static medium and ignores quantum effects.

## Method

The authors model bottomonium production by separating initially produced $b\bar b$ pairs into three energy bins: pairs with center-of-mass energy below 9.3 GeV form $\Upsilon$ instantly, pairs above 9.5 GeV become open beauty, and pairs in the intermediate bin (9.3–9.5 GeV) are treated as correlated pairs evolving via Langevin dynamics with a screened Cornell potential. A rate equation with thermal width $\Gamma$ describes the dissociation of $\Upsilon(1S)$ in the deconfined medium, with feed-down from dissociated $\Upsilon(1S)$ adding to the correlated pair population; the evolution is studied in a static medium at constant temperature.

## Key numbers

- Bottom quark mass set to $M_b = 4.5$ GeV in PYTHIA 8.1 simulations.
- Energy bins for $b\bar b$ pairs: $E < 9.3$ GeV (instant $\Upsilon$ formation), $9.3 < E < 9.5$ GeV (correlated pairs), $E > 9.5$ GeV (open beauty).
- Thermal width for $\Upsilon(1S)$ dissociation: $\Gamma = 40$ MeV (weak coupling estimate) and $\Gamma = 200$ MeV (larger hadronic width used also at $T=200$ MeV).
- Diffusion constant chosen as $2\pi T D = 1.5$ (strong coupling regime).
- Screening radius set to $r_{scr}(T) = 0.8/T$ based on lattice QCD.
- At $T=200$ MeV with the potential present, the fraction of correlated $b\bar b$ pairs decreases only by a factor of two after 10 fm of medium lifetime.
- The peak in the distribution of correlated $b\bar b$ pairs as a function of relative distance has a width of about 0.25 fm, comparable to the $\Upsilon(1S)$ size.

## Context

This work extends the Langevin-based model for charmonium production developed by Young et al. (2008, 2009, 2011) to the bottomonium sector, coupling it with a rate equation for the tightly bound $\Upsilon(1S)$ state. It contrasts with the sequential melting interpretation of CMS data (Chatrchyan et al., 2011) by proposing that the larger suppression of $\Upsilon(2S)$ relative to $\Upsilon(1S)$ arises from a sequential formation pattern where correlated pairs preferentially form the smaller ground state. The authors note their static-medium calculation can be extended to expanding realistic media as done for charmonium by Young et al. (2011), but identify several open theoretical issues including quantum effects, non-perturbative dynamics near the transition temperature, and matching of the bottom quark mass.
