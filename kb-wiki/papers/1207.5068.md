# Quasiclassical molecular dynamics for the dilute Fermi gas at unitarity

> We study the dilute Fermi gas at unitarity using molecular dynamics with an effective quantum potential constructed to reproduce the quantum two-body density matrix at unitarity. Results for the equation of state, the pair correlation function and the shear viscosity are presented. These quantities are well understood in the dilute, high temperature, limit. Using molecular dynamics we determine higher order corrections in the diluteness parameter $nλ^3$, where $n$ is the density and $λ$ is the thermal de Broglie wave length. In the case of the contact density, which parameterizes the short distance behavior of the correlation function, we find that the results of molecular dynamics interpolates between the truncated second and third order virial expansion, and are in excellent agreement with existing T-matrix calculations. For the shear viscosity we reproduce the expected scaling behavior at high temperature, $η\sim 1/λ^3$, and we determine the leading density dependent correction to this result.

# Quasiclassical molecular dynamics for the dilute Fermi gas at unitarity

## Key claim

The authors introduce a quasiclassical molecular dynamics method that uses an effective two-body quantum potential to study the dilute Fermi gas at unitarity, but they note that genuine quantum many-body effects such as pairing and superfluidity are not included because the method is truncated at two-body interactions. The method exactly reproduces the second virial coefficient and the pair correlation function in the dilute limit, and the authors claim that the molecular dynamics results interpolate between the truncated second and third order virial expansion for the contact density.

## Method

The authors construct an effective classical two-body potential from the logarithm of the two-particle Slater sum at unitarity, using the limit of an attractive square well with zero range and s-wave phase shift fixed to pi/2. They then perform classical molecular dynamics simulations of a two-component Fermi gas with equal numbers of spin-up and spin-down particles, using the effective potential for opposite-spin interactions and a separate potential derived from free fermion statistics for like-spin interactions. The pressure is computed using the virial theorem, and the shear viscosity is extracted from the Green-Kubo relation using the stress tensor correlation function averaged over five independent components.

## Key numbers

- The second virial coefficient is given as b2 = 3/(4*sqrt(2)), and the third virial coefficient is b3 = -0.29095295.
- The molecular dynamics results follow the second order virial expansion for n*lambda^3 less than or approximately equal to 0.2.
- The leading density-dependent correction to the shear viscosity is fit as c2 approximately 0.32, using the form eta = (eta0/lambda^3) * (1 + c2 * n*lambda^3).
- The high temperature limit of the shear viscosity from the classical interaction is given as eta/(hbar*n) = (75*sqrt(2)*pi)/(8*(5+pi)*n*lambda^3), while the exact quantum result is eta/(hbar*n) = (15*sqrt(2)*pi)/(16*n*lambda^3), a factor of approximately 1.2 difference.
- The contact density in the high temperature limit scales as C = (32*pi^2*n_down*n_up)/(m*T).
- The molecular dynamics simulations were performed with N=108 atoms (54 per spin state) for production runs of 2x10^5 simulation time units, with a time step of 0.001 and a potential cutoff l0* = 0.05.

## Context

This work extends the quasiclassical molecular dynamics approach previously used for strongly correlated Coulomb plasmas (Golubnychiy 2001, Bonitz 2003) to the unitary Fermi gas, where the effective potential is of purely quantum mechanical origin rather than a correction to classical Coulomb behavior. The method provides an alternative to quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) approaches (Carlson 2003, Astrakharchik 2004, Bulgac 2005) that cannot easily compute real-time response functions and transport coefficients, and to diagrammatic methods (Enss 2010, Guo 2011) where the required diagrams are not a priori clear. The results enable future inclusion of three-body effective interactions to extend the range of applicability beyond n*lambda^3 approximately 0.5-1.0, and provide benchmark calculations for experimental measurements of shear viscosity in cold atomic gases near unitarity.
