# Probing the QCD phase structure using event-by-event fluctuations

> Heavy-ion collisions at relativistic energies probe matter at extreme conditions of temperatures and energy densities. The study of event-by-event fluctuations of experimental observables is crucial to probe the QCD phase transition, locate the critical point, and learn about the associated critical phenomena. At the critical point, all thermodynamic quantities behave anomalously. Fluctuation measurements provide access to thermodynamic response functions. We discuss the methods for obtaining the isothermal compressibility using particle multiplicity fluctuation, and specific heat using fluctuations in mean transverse momentum, temperature, and energy. Lattice QCD calculations have predicted non-monotonic behavior in the higher-order cumulants of conserved quantities at the critical point. Fluctuations in the multiplicity of charged to neutral particles have been measured to understand the formation of domains of disoriented chiral condensates. We review the recent fluctuation results as a function of collision centrality and energy from experiments at SPS, RHIC, and LHC. In addition, we propose to map the temperature fluctuations in eta-phi plane to probe local fluctuations of temperature and energy density.

## Key claim

Event-by-event fluctuations of experimental observables in heavy-ion collisions can probe the QCD phase transition and locate the critical point, but the non-monotonic behavior in the net-proton kurtosis times variance product (with 3.0 sigma significance) is the only signature currently observed that is most likely compatible with theoretical predictions of the critical point, while other conserved quantity cumulants show smooth variation within present uncertainties.

## Method

The paper reviews methods for extracting thermodynamic response functions from event-by-event fluctuations: isothermal compressibility from particle multiplicity fluctuations using the grand canonical ensemble relation, and specific heat from fluctuations in mean transverse momentum and effective temperature. Higher-order cumulants (mean, variance, skewness, kurtosis) of net-charge, net-proton, net-kaon, and net-Lambda distributions are measured experimentally by the STAR and ALICE Collaborations as functions of collision centrality and energy, with products of moments constructed to cancel volume dependence. Off-diagonal cumulants between conserved quantities (charge, baryon number, strangeness) are also measured to probe flavor-carrying susceptibilities.

## Key numbers

- Isothermal compressibility from hadron resonance gas calculations shows a sharp increase around sqrt(s_NN) approximately 20 GeV for central collisions
- Specific heat shows a sharp rise below sqrt(s_NN) = 62.4 GeV for central collisions
- Net-charge fluctuation measure D shows diffusion parameter sigma_f = 0.41 +/- 0.05 for central Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV
- Net-proton kurtosis times variance shows non-monotonic variation with beam energy at 3.0 sigma significance for central Au-Au collisions, not explained by hadron resonance gas or UrQMD model
- Net-kaon cumulant ratios C_p,k change sign from negative at 200 GeV to positive around 19.6 GeV for most central Au-Au collisions
- WA98 experiment at CERN SPS set 90% confidence level upper limit on DCC production per central event in 158 AGeV Pb-Pb collisions

## Context

This review extends the experimental program that began more than three decades ago at Bevelac, Berkeley and covers four generations of experiments at BNL and CERN, including the RHIC Beam Energy Scan program designed to probe the critical point by varying collision energy at close intervals. The work connects lattice QCD calculations (which predict non-monotonic behavior in higher-order cumulants at the critical point) to experimental measurements from SPS, RHIC, and LHC. The proposed mapping of temperature fluctuations in the eta-phi plane aims to enable inference of thermodynamic conditions at different stages of QGP evolution by comparing measured fluctuations with hydrodynamic calculations.
