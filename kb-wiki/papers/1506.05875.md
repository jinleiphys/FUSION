# Relativistic and Nuclear Medium Effects on the Coulomb Sum Rule

> In light of the forthcoming high precision quasielastic electron scattering data from Jefferson Lab, it is timely for the various approaches to nuclear structure to make robust predictions for the associated response functions. With this in mind, we focus here on the longitudinal response function and the corresponding Coulomb sum rule for isospin-symmetric nuclear matter at various baryon densities. Using a quantum field-theoretic quark-level approach which preserves the symmetries of quantum chromodynamics, as well as exhibiting dynamical chiral symmetry breaking and quark confinement, we find a dramatic quenching of the Coulomb sum rule for momentum transfers $\left|\mathbf{q}\right| \gtrsim 0.5\,$GeV. The main driver of this effect lies in changes to the proton Dirac form factor induced by the nuclear medium. Such a dramatic quenching of the Coulomb sum rule was not seen in a recent quantum Monte Carlo calculation for carbon, suggesting that the Jefferson Lab data may well shed new light on the explicit role of QCD in nuclei.

## Key claim

Using a quark-level approach based on the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, the authors predict a dramatic quenching of the Coulomb sum rule for momentum transfers |q| greater than about 0.5 GeV, driven primarily by medium-induced changes to the proton Dirac form factor. This quenching is significantly larger than that seen in a recent quantum Monte Carlo calculation for carbon, suggesting that forthcoming Jefferson Lab data may reveal explicit QCD effects in nuclei.

## Method

The authors compute the longitudinal response function and Coulomb sum rule for isospin-symmetric nuclear matter using a quantum field-theoretic quark-level approach based on the NJL model, which preserves QCD symmetries and exhibits dynamical chiral symmetry breaking and quark confinement. Nucleon electromagnetic form factors are calculated in the nuclear medium by solving the Poincare covariant Faddeev equation with in-medium quark propagators, where the dressed quark mass at a given density is determined from the minimum of the effective potential for nuclear matter. The longitudinal response function is obtained from the imaginary part of the longitudinal polarization, including random phase approximation (RPA) correlations mediated by sigma, omega and rho meson exchange, with all vertices evaluated using the in-medium nucleon form factors.

## Key numbers

- At nuclear matter saturation density (rho_B = 0.16 fm^-3), the proton magnetic moment increases by 7% relative to its free value, while the neutron anomalous magnetic moment decreases by 5%.
- At saturation density, the proton Dirac and charge radii each increase by about 8%, while the Pauli and magnetic radii grow by half that amount; the neutron charge radius decreases in magnitude by 4%.
- For |q| approximately 1 GeV, the Coulomb sum rule using free nucleon currents gives S_L approximately 0.82, representing relativistic corrections of roughly 20% relative to the non-relativistic expectation of unity.
- Using the in-medium nucleon current at saturation density (rho_B = 0.16 fm^-3) produces an additional 30% reduction in the Coulomb sum rule at |q| approximately 1 GeV compared to the free current result.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous NJL-based studies of the EMC effect (Cloet 2005, 2006, 2009, 2012) and in-medium nucleon form factors (Cloet 2014) to the longitudinal response function and Coulomb sum rule. The predicted dramatic quenching contrasts with the Green function Monte Carlo calculation for carbon by Lovato (2013), which assumes unmodified nucleon properties and finds at most modest quenching. The results are consistent with earlier calculations by Saito (1999) and Horikawa (2005) that also self-consistently modify bound nucleon properties, and with experimental data on various nuclei from Zghiche (1993) and Morgenstern (2001). The forthcoming Jefferson Lab quasielastic scattering measurements (E05-110) on multiple nuclear targets should resolve whether this quenching is observed, thereby testing whether QCD effects explicitly modify nucleon structure in the nuclear medium.
