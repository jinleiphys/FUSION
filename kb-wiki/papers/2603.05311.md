# Exploring $T_{ΥΥ}$ tetraquark candidates in a coupled-channels formalism

> We investigate the spectrum of $T_{ΥΥ}$ tetraquark candidates within a coupled-channels framework. The analysis includes all $L\leq2$ combinations of $Υ(1S)$, $Υ(2S)$, $η_b(1S)$, and $η_b(2S)$ in the $J^P = 0^\pm, 1^\pm, 2^\pm$ sectors. The meson-meson interaction is derived from an underlying constituent quark model through the resonating group method, and the properties of the states are obtained from poles of the scattering matrix. We find a rich spectrum of resonant, and virtual, states distributed between the $η_b(1S)η_b(1S)$ and $Υ(2S)Υ(2S)$ thresholds. The pattern of poles exhibits approximate heavy-quark spin symmetry multiplets. Several states are dominated by a single channel and can be associated with threshold-driven structures, while higher-mass resonances show sizable mixing among channels involving radially excited bottomonia. The predicted widths range from tens to several hundred MeV. Branching ratios indicate that many states couple predominantly to final states with at least one excited bottomonium, whereas only a subset of the spectrum is expected to be visible in the $η_b(1S)η_b(1S)$, $η_b(1S)Υ(1S)$ and $Υ(1S)Υ(1S)$ channels. These results provide quantitative guidance for experimental searches of fully heavy tetraquarks and offer a test of coupled-channel dynamics and heavy-quark spin symmetry in the $bb\bar b\bar b$ sector.

## Key claim
The paper claims that fully bottom tetraquark ($bb\bar b\bar b$) candidates can be generated dynamically from coupled-channel interactions of physical color-singlet bottomonia, without invoking explicit compact diquark-antidiquark or color-octet configurations. The hedge is that these states are not loosely bound molecules but rather compact meson-meson configurations arising from short-range quark rearrangement, and the theoretical uncertainty is estimated by varying the potential strength by $\pm10\%$.

## Method
The authors employ a constituent quark model (CQM) with one-gluon exchange and color confinement, using the resonating group method (RGM) to derive the meson-meson interaction from underlying quark dynamics. They solve the coupled-channel Lippmann-Schwinger equation for all $L\leq2$ combinations of $\Upsilon(1S)$, $\Upsilon(2S)$, $\eta_b(1S)$, and $\eta_b(2S)$ in the $J^P = 0^\pm, 1^\pm, 2^\pm$ sectors, extracting resonant and virtual states as poles of the scattering matrix in the complex energy plane.

## Key numbers
- Three degenerate poles in $0^{--}$, $1^{--}$, and $2^{--}$ sectors at mass $19.546_{-3}^{+2}$ GeV with widths $311-312$ MeV, dominated by $\eta_b(1S)\Upsilon(2S)$ and $\eta_b(2S)\Upsilon(1S)$ components.
- A $0^{++}$ state at $18.894_{-3}^{+2}$ GeV with width $165_{-15}^{+17}$ MeV, dominated by $\eta_b(1S)\eta_b(1S)$ (70% wavefunction composition).
- A $1^{+-}$ state at $18.948_{-2}^{+1}$ GeV with width $148_{-12}^{+14}$ MeV, almost entirely $\eta_b(1S)\Upsilon(1S)$ (98.2% wavefunction composition).
- A $2^{++}$ state at $19.005_{-3}^{+2}$ GeV with width $176_{-12}^{+14}$ MeV, dominated by $\Upsilon(1S)\Upsilon(1S)$ (96.7% wavefunction composition).
- A virtual $2^{++}$ state at $20.040 \pm 1$ GeV with width $38 \pm 6$ MeV, dominated by $\Upsilon(2S)\Upsilon(2S)$ (92.2% wavefunction composition).
- Predicted widths range from tens to several hundred MeV, with the broadest resonance being a $1^{+-}$ state at $19.882_{-6}^{+7}$ GeV with width $551_{-25}^{+27}$ MeV.

## Context
This work extends the coupled-channels framework previously applied to the fully charmed sector by Ortega et al. (2023) to the fully bottom sector, providing a direct and consistent comparison. It is complementary to compact tetraquark approaches such as those by Karliner and Rosner (2016) and Wu et al. (2016), and addresses the question of whether structures predicted in the literature can be interpreted as threshold-driven states emerging from coupled-channel dynamics. The results provide quantitative guidance for experimental searches at the LHC, where fully bottom tetraquark searches in the $\Upsilon(1S)\Upsilon(1S)$ spectrum have so far yielded no significant signals.
